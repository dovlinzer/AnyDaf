# Sotah 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## All Languages: Sotah, Vi…

### Sotah, Viduy, Shema

The daf opens in the middle of a discussion about which ritual declarations may be recited in any language. Already mentioned are *sotah* — not the writing of the *parsha* but the reciting of it — along with *viduy maaser* and *shema*. All three derive from the *Torah*, and the Mishnah has established that each may be performed in any language.

### Tefillah & Rachamim

*Tefillah* is now addressed. The Gemara states: *rachamei hi* — it is supplication to God, direct communication with God — *vechol heicha deba'i matzlei*, and so one may pray however one needs to speak. Because *tefillah* is at its core a matter of personal address to the Almighty, it is most appropriate that it be expressed in whatever language allows a person to be most articulate.

### Avot vs. Korbanot

This is a significant point, because the word *rachamei* carries a precise conceptual weight in the Gemara. It stands in contrast to the classic debate in Berakhot over whether *tefillah* is *keneged avot* or *keneged korbanot* — whether it corresponds structurally to the *korbanot*, with all their prescribed forms and regulations, or whether it corresponds to the way Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov prayed, which involved no fixed text, no fixed time, and was very much of the heart. The Gemara's conclusion in Berakhot is that at its core *tefillah* is *keneged avot*, though the structure and timing of the *korbanot* have been layered upon it. When the Gemara wants to invoke that core dimension — that *tefillah* is fundamentally personal communication — it uses the phrase *rachamei ninhu*. And it is precisely because of that core that *tefillah* may, in principle, be offered in any language.

### Rav Yehuda & Aramaic

The Gemara immediately challenges this. *Vetefillah bechol lashon?* Is it truly the case that *Shemoneh Esrei* may be said in any language? Does not Rav Yehuda say *leolam al yishal adam tzarachav bilshon Aramit* — one should never make a personal request in Aramaic? And does not Rabbi Yochanan add that whoever makes their request in Aramaic, *ein malachei hasharet nizkakim lo* — the ministering angels will not attend to them?

### Angels & Aramaic

The reason given is *lefi she'ein malachei hasharet makirim bilshon Aramit* — they do not recognize Aramaic. This is a striking idea: that there is something a heavenly being would fail to recognize. Some *Rishonim* qualify this, suggesting it applies specifically to Aramaic rather than to all non-Hebrew languages, and that the issue is not literal non-recognition but a kind of disapproval — the angels regard Aramaic as a corruption of Hebrew, being itself a Semitic language. In any event, the principle is that one should not recite *tefillot* in Aramaic because the angels serve as the bearers of prayer, and they will not convey what is said in that language.

### Individual vs. Communal

The Gemara resolves the tension: *lo kashya — ha beyachid ha betzibur*. The question depends on whether one is praying privately or communally. When one prays as part of a community, God's presence is found directly among the congregation, and there is no need for angelic intermediaries. When one prays privately, the angels are needed to carry the prayer upward, and in that context the Aramaic restriction applies.

One can also understand this on a more conceptual level that extends beyond the specific issue of Aramaic. When one recites the fixed Hebrew text, even if it somewhat constrains one's ability to express oneself with full personal feeling, what is achieved is connection — to the tradition, to the weight and depth of the words themselves, and to a sense of community through a shared text. When one is already praying *betzibur*, that sense of communal connection and *mesorah* is present regardless, and it therefore becomes permissible to pray in another language. But when one is praying alone, the Hebrew text may itself serve as the vehicle that connects the individual to something larger than the private moment.

### Women & Vernacular

This distinction generated discussion among the *Rishonim* because in practice, women — and others not fluent in Hebrew — would recite *Shemoneh Esrei* even when praying privately. This led some *Rishonim* to refine the distinction: the relevant factor is not whether one is literally praying among others, but whether one is reciting a communal prayer as opposed to a wholly personal, self-composed text. If it is a communal prayer like *Shemoneh Esrei*, it may be recited in translation even by someone praying alone — perhaps because God hears the communal prayer wherever it is said, or because one praying *Shemoneh Esrei* at home is, in some sense, joining the larger community offering that same prayer. It may also be that when one composes a personal prayer in one's own words, using one's own language is the most natural approach. In any case, the *Rishonim* ultimately tend to limit Rav Yehuda's restriction specifically to Aramaic, so that once Aramaic ceased to be the vernacular, the practical import of the restriction largely disappeared.

### Angels & Languages

The Gemara now turns to the underlying premise and challenges it directly. *V'ei malachei hasharet makiri belashon Aramit* — is it truly the case that the angels do not know Aramaic? A *baraita* is cited: Yochanan the *Kohen Gadol* heard a *bas kol* from the *Kodesh Hakodashim* — Rashi explains this occurred while he was performing the *avodah* on Yom Kippur — and the voice proclaimed *nitzchu talyaya de'azlu le'agacha kerava le'Antyocha*, that the young men of the *Chashmonaim* who went to battle against the Seleucids were victorious. A second story follows: Shimon HaTzaddik heard a *bas kol* from the *Kodesh Hakodashim* declaring *batlis avidata de'amar sanay le'aysay al heichala* — the campaign that the enemy had planned against the *Beis Hamikdash* has been nullified — and that a certain Greek ruler had been killed and his decrees annulled. The time was recorded, and it was found to correspond exactly to the moment those events occurred. In both cases, *uvelashon Aramit hayah* — the *bas kol* was delivered in Aramaic. Since a *bas kol* presumably passes through an angel, this seems to demonstrate that the angels do know Aramaic.

The Gemara offers two responses. First: *bas kol shani* — a *bas kol* is different, *de'le'ishtamuei avida*, because its very purpose is to be heard and understood by people, so perhaps a special angel delivers such proclamations specifically in the people's language. Second: *ve'i bayit eima Gavriel hava* — perhaps it was Gavriel who was speaking, and Gavriel is the one angel who knows all languages, *de'amar mar ba Gavriel velimdo shivim lashon* — as the master taught, Gavriel came and taught Yosef all seventy languages. These two possibilities resolve the challenge, and the restriction on Aramaic prayer stands as stated.

## Birchat Hamazon

### Hamazon Source

The Mishnah next establishes that *birchat hamazon* may also be recited in any language. This requires discussion because *birchat hamazon* is clearly grounded in a *pasuk* in the *Torah*: *ve'achalta vesavata uverachta et Hashem Elokecha* — you shall eat and be satisfied and bless the Lord your God. The fact that it derives from a *Torah* verse might lead one to think that Hebrew is required.

### Rashi vs. Tosafot

Rashi reads the Gemara as simply noting the *pasuk* to indicate that there is no fixed text — since the verse just says "bless," without specifying any particular wording, there is no reason to require Hebrew. On Rashi's reading, the Gemara is not answering a question but simply observing that of course it can be in any language, since there is nothing to translate. Tosafot, however, records a different *girsa* of the Gemara — one that begins with the question *mena hanei milei*, from where do we know this — and argues that Rashi's text has been altered. On Tosafot's reading, the Gemara does need to derive that *birchat hamazon* may be said in any language, because the presence of the word *uverachta* might have led us to think — by comparison to other *brachot* such as *birchat kohanim* that must be said in Hebrew — that here too Hebrew is required. The *pasuk* must therefore be cited to show that it may be said in any language. This is a genuinely striking point: even when there is no fixed textual formula to translate, there is still reason to think that perhaps Hebrew specifically is required — and a *pasuk* is needed to dispel that assumption.

### Hamazon & God

What is the proof from the verse? Tosafot explains: *uverachta et Hashem* — since the *bracha* and the praise are directed *klapei haShechinah*, toward God directly, one may bless in any language one chooses. When the communication is purely between the person and God, with no ritual dimension addressed to others, the language barrier dissolves. This is, notably, quite parallel to the justification for *tefillah* as *rachamei* — direct address to God removes the requirement of a specific sacred tongue. Yet, as Tosafot makes clear, even in such a case the principle needed to be explicitly derived, since there remains an independent reason to require Hebrew even when no prescribed text exists.

## Holy Tongue Required

### Oaths in Any Language

The Mishnah also includes *shevuat ha'eidut* — the oath administered to a witness who denies knowing relevant testimony — and derives its permissibility in any language from the verse *v'nefesh ki techeta v'shama kol alah*, since it says *bechol lashon she'hi shoma'at*, in whatever language she hears. The need for a *pasuk* here, too, indicates that even for something not purely ritualistic, there is reason to suppose Hebrew might be required. *Shevuat hapikadon* — the oath regarding a withheld object — is derived from *shevuat ha'eidut* through a *gezeirah shavah* on the word *techeta* appearing in both contexts, and similarly may be administered in any language. The Mishnah's list of things permitted in all languages is thus complete; the *Rishonim* note that many other *brachot* and recitations are of course also said in other languages, but these are the specific items singled out by the Mishnah.

### Lashon Hakodesh List

The Mishnah then turns to the contrasting category: *v'eilu ne'emarim belashon hakodesh* — the following may only be said in *lashon hakodesh*: *mikra bikkurim*, *chalitzah*, *brachot v'klalot*, and others enumerated in the Mishnah.

### Mikra Bikkurim

The Gemara addresses each in turn. For *mikra bikkurim*, the Mishnah derives the requirement from the verse *v'anita v'amarta lifnei Hashem Elokecha* — you shall respond and say before the Lord your God. The double verb *v'anita v'amarta* — respond and say — also appears in the passage of the Levites at *Har Gerizim* and *Har Eival*, where it is written *v'anu halevi'im v'amru el kol ish Yisrael kol ram*. By means of *gezeirah shavah* on the term *aniyah*, we learn: just as there by the *brachot v'klalot* it is in *lashon hakodesh*, so too here by *mikra bikkurim* it must be in *lashon hakodesh*. This is a notable ruling, because one might have thought that *mikra bikkurim* — which is an expression of personal gratitude to God — should be in the language that most allows the individual to speak from the heart. Nevertheless, the *gezeirah shavah* establishes that it requires Hebrew. Perhaps because the declaration in the *Beis Hamikdash* is a public ritual, heard by all present, its formal and communal character calls for the sacred tongue.

### Brachot v'Klalot Source

The Gemara then asks: how do we know that the *brachot v'klalot* of the Levites were themselves in *lashon hakodesh*? The answer comes through another *gezeirah shavah*: the verse by the Levites uses the expression *kol ram* — a loud voice — and separately, in the passage of *matan Torah*, it says *Moshe yedaber vehaElokim ya'anenu b'kol* — Moshe would speak and God would answer him with a voice. Just as there, when God spoke, it was clearly in Hebrew, so too the *kol ram* of the Levites must be in *lashon hakodesh*.

### Chalitzah & Kacha

For *chalitzah*, the Mishnah presents two approaches. The *rabbanan* learn from the double verb *v'ansa v'amra* — she shall respond and say — the same pattern of *aniyah v'amirah* that signals *lashon hakodesh*. Rabbi Yehuda, however, does not require this derivation for *chalitzah* because he learns it from the word *kacha* in the verse *kacha ye'aseh* — so shall it be done. Rabbi Yehuda reads *kacha* rather than the simpler *ko* as indicating two things: the declaration must be in Hebrew, and furthermore, it is specifically the physical acts of *chalitzah* — not the verbal declaration — that are indispensable for the *chalitzah* to be valid. The *rabbanan*, who do not read the elongated *kacha* as teaching two separate points, use it only to establish that the acts are essential, and derive the requirement of Hebrew from *v'ansa v'amra* instead.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Method

The Gemara asks: if Rabbi Yehuda uses *kacha* for both points and does not use *v'ansa v'amra* by *chalitzah*, what does he do with that phrase? The answer is that he needs it to teach regarding the Levites that the *brachot v'klalot* are in *lashon hakodesh*. Rabbi Yehuda's entire chain of derivation thus runs differently from that of the *rabbanan*: he learns that *chalitzah* must be in Hebrew from *kacha*, then uses *chalitzah* to teach that *brachot v'klalot* are in Hebrew, and from there applies the *gezeirah shavah* to *mikra bikkurim*. The Gemara asks why Rabbi Yehuda does not simply learn the Levites' *brachot v'klalot* from the *gezeirah shavah* on *kol* connecting to Moshe and God's voice, as the first approach had done. The answer is that the *gezeirah shavah* on *aniyah* is the tradition he received, while the *kol-kol* connection is not his tradition — at a certain point one cannot ask why one *gezeirah shavah* was chosen over another, as these are matters of received tradition.

### Baraita Rule

A *baraita* is then cited in support of Rabbi Yehuda's view: *kol makom she'ne'emar ko, kacha, aniyah, v'amirah, einu ela lashon hakodesh* — wherever the *Torah* uses the expressions *ko*, *kacha*, *aniyah*, or *amirah*, the requirement is that it be in *lashon hakodesh*. The Baraita applies this to three instances: *ko tevarachu et bnei Yisrael* — referring to *birchat kohanim*; *kacha d'chalitzah* — the *kacha* of *chalitzah*; and *aniyah v'amirah d'Levi'im* — the *aniyah v'amirah* of the Levites by *brachot v'klalot*. From that last anchor point, the derivation extends further to *mikra bikkurim*.

## Har Gerizim & Eival

### Location Description

The Mishnah speaks of the ceremony on *Har Gerizim* and *Har Eival*, and the Gemara now examines a *baraita* addressing the precise location of those mountains as described in the verses. The *baraita* cites the verse *halo hemah b'ever haYarden*, behold they are across the Jordan. The phrase is then parsed: *me'ever l'Yarden u'l'halan divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — on the other side of the Jordan, and further beyond, according to Rabbi Yehuda. On this reading, *ever haYarden* denotes the east bank, and the mountains are described as being at a substantial distance from it. *Acharei derech mevo hashemesh* — after the way of the coming of the sun — is read as *mekom she'chamah zorachat*, the place where the sun rises, i.e., from the east. This is admittedly not the simple *peshat* of the verse, but the intent is to emphasize that the mountains are located far from the eastern side of the Jordan.

Continuing: *b'eretz ha'Kna'ani hayoshev ba'aravah* — in the land of the Canaanites who dwell in the valley. This, according to the *baraita*, refers to *Har Gerizim* and *Har Eival*, where the *Kuti'im* (Samaritans) dwell in the current era. *Mul haGilgal* — opposite Gilgal, *samuch l'Gilgal* — near Gilgal, because these mountains are located well to the west of the Jordan, in the direction of Gilgal. *Etzel elonei Moreh* — near *elonei Moreh*, which is identified as *Shechem*. The *baraita* then cites the parallel verse from the story of Avraham — *v'ya'avor Avram ba'aretz ad mekom Shechem, Alon Moreh* — to establish by *gezeirah shavah* that *Alon Moreh* here too means *Shechem*.

A second *baraita* is cited, in which Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Yose recounts that with this very passage he demonstrated to the Samaritans that their *sifrei Torah* were falsified. The Samaritans, who rejected the oral tradition, claimed that *Elon Moreh* by *Har Gerizim* and *Har Eival* referred to *Shechem* — the location they held sacred. Rabbi Elazar responded: *zieftem Toratechem v'lo alissem b'yedchem klum* — you have falsified your Torah and gained nothing thereby — for we too agree that *Elon Moreh* is *Shechem*. But we know this through a *gezeirah shavah* from the verse about Avraham. You, who reject the oral tradition, have no such derivation — where did you learn it from? The precise logic of this exchange requires closer examination; the Gemara's point seems to turn on something beyond the simple identification of the name, though exactly what that additional element is remains somewhat opaque in the text.

### Rabbi Elazar

A different approach to the verse is now presented. Rabbi Elazar says: *halo hem b'ever haYarden — samoch l'Yarden*. The word *ever* does not mean a far distance but rather immediately near. The mountains are located just on the other side of the Jordan — immediately to the west, as soon as one crosses. This contrasts with Rabbi Yehuda's reading of the verse as pointing to a location far from the eastern bank. Rabbi Elazar supports his reading: one cannot interpret the verse as pointing far away from the Jordan, because another verse says *v'haya b'ovrechem et haYarden* — it will be when you cross the Jordan — implying that the erection of the stones and the ceremony of *brachot v'klalot* follow immediately. The phrase *b'yom asher tavru* — on the day that you cross — reinforces that this was to take place immediately upon crossing.

On Rabbi Elazar's reading, *acharei derech mevo hashemesh* means toward the setting of the sun — i.e., to the west — which coheres naturally with a location just across the Jordan on the western bank.

### Problems & Responses

His reading, however, presents difficulties. The verse describes the location as *b'eretz ha'Kna'ani*, yet the territory immediately west of the Jordan at the crossing point is the land of the *Chivi*, not the *Kena'ani*. Furthermore, the verse says *hayoshev ba'aravah* — who dwells in the valley — but the *Chivi'im* dwell among mountains and hills, not in a valley. And *mul haGilgal* — opposite Gilgal — is problematic because Gilgal was itself some distance from the actual crossing point, so one could not truly say that the crossing spot faces Gilgal.

### Second Direction

These difficulties lead to the interpretation of Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov: *lo ba hakatuv ela l'harot lahem derech bishniya k'derech sheher'a lahem berishona* — the verse from *mul haGilgal* onward is not describing the location of *Har Gerizim* and *Har Eival* at all. Rather, it is providing directional guidance for entering the land. When they left Egypt, the *Bnei Yisrael* were guided by the *ananei kavod*; now, entering the land a second time without those clouds, the Torah provides geographical markers to orient their movement. The mention of the valley, Gilgal, and the land of the *Kena'ani* are thus waypoints for the journey into the land — not descriptors of where the ceremony took place. Rashi adds that following this path through the valley, past Gilgal, and through the land of the *Kena'ani* ensures that they penetrate deep into the land rather than remaining near the Jordan crossing.

## Jordan Crossing

### Aron Crosses First

A *baraita* describes how the *Bnei Yisrael* actually crossed the Jordan. On a normal day of travel through the wilderness, the order was: two of the tribal camps would march first, then the *aron* would follow behind the first two *degalim*. But on the day of the crossing, *nasa techila* — the *aron* went first. This was necessary because the *aron* carried by the Levites entering the water is what caused the waters to stop flowing. The verse is cited: *hinei aron habrit adon kol ha'aretz over lifneichem baYarden* — the *aron habrit* of the master of the whole world passes before you into the Jordan.

### Three Times

Ordinarily it was the *Levi'im* who carried the *aron*, but on the day of the crossing, *nas'u hakohanim* — it was the *kohanim* who carried it. The verse confirms: *vehaya kenach kapot raglei hakohanim nosei aron Hashem* — when the feet of the *kohanim* bearing the *aron of Hashem* came to rest in the Jordan. A *baraita* adds: Rabbi Yosei says there were three occasions on which the *kohanim* specifically carried the *aron* — when they crossed the Jordan, when they circled Yericho, and when the *aron* was returned to its permanent place when Shlomo brought it into the *Kodesh Hakodashim*.

### Waters Back Up

As soon as the feet of the *kohanim* touched the water, *chazaru hamayim le'achoreihem* — the waters turned backward. Unlike the splitting of the *Yam Suf*, where a sea was divided, here the Jordan was a rushing river; the oncoming water met resistance and could no longer flow forward, while the water already below drained away, leaving dry ground. The verse is cited: *vaya'amdu hamayim hayordim milmala, kamu ned echad* — the waters descending from above stood, forming a single wall.

### 12 Mil Dimensions

How high did this wall of water rise? Rabbi Yehuda says: *shneim asar mil al shneim asar mil, keneged machaneh Yisrael* — 12 *mil* by 12 *mil*, corresponding to the size of the camp of Israel. The width of the Jordan itself was approximately 50 *amot* (which corresponds closely to the river's present width of about 60 feet). The dimensions of 12 *mil* by 12 *mil* therefore describe not the width of the wall across the river, but the extent to which the water backed up — 12 *mil* in depth along the river and 12 *mil* in height. A *mil* is 2,000 *amot*, approximately a kilometer, so 12 *mil* represents roughly 12 kilometers. The camp of Israel was understood to occupy a square of 12 *mil* by 12 *mil* when they traveled.

### Water Speed

Rabbi Eliezer ben Shimon challenges Rabbi Yehuda's calculation. *Adam kal o mayim kalim* — which moves faster, a person or flowing water? *Hevei omer mayim kalim* — clearly the water is faster. If the wall was only 12 *mil* high, and water moves faster than people, then by the time the *Bnei Yisrael* finished crossing — which took as long as it would take to traverse 12 *mil* — the rushing water from upstream would have risen far higher than 12 *mil* and would have simply crashed over that wall and drowned them. *Im ken bayim mayim veshotef otam* — if so, the waters would have come and swept them away.

### 300 Mil Height

The answer: *ela melamed shehayu mayim nikdashim ve'olim kippin al gabei kippin* — rather, the waters were sanctified and piled up in layers upon layers, *yoter mishlosh meot mil* — more than 300 *mil* in height. This reflects the assumption that water travels approximately 25 times faster than people walk, and so in the time it took the nation to cross, the backed-up water rose to more than 300 *mil* high.

### Kings' Response

The miraculous scale of the event was such that *ad she'ra'u otam kol malchei mizrach uma'arav* — until all the kings of the East and the West saw it. The verse in Sefer Yehoshua is cited: when all the kings of the Amorites on the western side of the Jordan heard how God had dried up the waters of the Jordan before the *Bnei Yisrael* until they had passed through, *vayimas levavam* — their hearts melted — and *velo hayah bahem od ruach mipnei bnei Yisrael* — they no longer had any spirit to stand against the *Bnei Yisrael*.

The Gemara adds that even before the crossing of the Jordan, Rachav had told Yehoshua's messengers about the earlier miracle of the *Yam Suf*: *ki shamanu et asher hovish Hashem et mei Yam Suf* — we heard how God dried up the waters of the *Yam Suf* — and *venishma vayimas levavenu velo kamah od* — and we heard and our hearts melted and no spirit remained. The parallel phrasing between Rachav's account of the *Yam Suf* and the verse describing the reaction of the kings to the Jordan crossing underscores that the miracles at both bodies of water formed a unified arc of Divine intervention that broke the resolve of *Bnei Yisrael*'s enemies.
