# Bava Metzia 85 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Elazar: Background

### Magistrate & Afflictions

The daf begins at the bottom of 84b, in the midst of an extended narrative concerning Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon. By way of review: Rabbi Elazar had served as a magistrate for the Roman authorities, apprehending thieves — a role that drew sharp rabbinic criticism. Although he was ultimately reassured that a particular act he feared had been rash was not in fact a mistake, he remained troubled. As a form of personal *kaparah*, he voluntarily accepted physical afflictions upon himself each night. His wife, distressed by this self-imposed suffering, initially separated from him. When he died, he instructed those around him not to bury him immediately, fearing that the rabbis — still aggrieved over the magistrate episode — would not treat his body with proper honor. He was accordingly left lying in an upper chamber.

### Body Preserved & Burial

Rabbi Elazar's body remained in that upper chamber for somewhere between eighteen and twenty-two years, entirely preserved, without any sign of decomposition. When the time finally came for burial, he was brought to the cave where his father, Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, was interred. A serpent was guarding the entrance to the cave. Those present called out: "Open up so that the father may receive the son," and the serpent opened its mouth and allowed him in. This remarkable detail closes the preceding narrative and sets the stage for what follows.

## Rabbi's Proposal

### Widow's Response

Following Rabbi Elazar's death, Rabbi — the *nasi* — sent word to his widow proposing marriage. She refused, replying with a striking image: *kli she'nishtamesh bo kodesh yishtamesh bo chol?* — "Should a vessel that was used for something holy now be used for something mundane?" The implication was clear: her late husband's holiness was of an altogether different order than Rabbi's.

### Weapons & Pouch

She reinforced her refusal with a proverb current in *Eretz Yisrael*: in a place where the master of the house hangs up his weapons on a particular peg, should the shepherd's pouch be hung on the same peg? The contrast she drew was between the master of the house — Rabbi Elazar — and the shepherd — Rabbi himself.

### Good Deeds Debate

Rabbi replied, sending word back to her: he was prepared to concede that Rabbi Elazar may have been greater than him in *Torah*, but surely not in *maasim tovim*. As *nasi*, Rabbi provided for the entire Jewish people — how could Rabbi Elazar be considered his superior in good deeds? This is a striking claim: Rabbi's understanding of *maasim tovim* was grounded in communal leadership and material sustenance for *Klal Yisrael*.

### Suffering & Merit

The widow's final reply was pointed: regarding Torah, she said, she had not known that Rabbi Elazar surpassed him — but Rabbi himself had just admitted it. As for *maasim tovim*, that too she knew: *de'akibel lei yissurei* — Rabbi Elazar had accepted *yissurim* upon himself. One might pause here and ask whether *yissurim* — personal, physical suffering — truly constitutes *maasim tovim* in the conventional sense of benefiting others. Indeed, his afflictions had at times prevented him from attending the *beit midrash*. The widow's formulation is interesting and not entirely self-evident, but the *gemara* will return to this question in a different register.

## Young Rabbi's Elevation

### Teacher Hierarchy

The *gemara* now asks what the basis was for the common knowledge that Rabbi Elazar was greater than Rabbi in *Torah*. The answer lies in a story from their shared youth. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha were seated on stools — the position of teachers — in the *beit midrash*, while Rabbi (then still simply the young Yehudah, son of the *nasi*) and Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon sat on the ground before them, as students.

### Students Challenge Teach…

As the session proceeded, the two younger men sitting on the ground were asking difficult questions and providing sharp answers — *makshu u'mfarku* — demonstrating that they were, intellectually speaking, the equals if not the superiors of those seated above them. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha took note: "We are drinking from their waters, yet they are sitting on the ground?" They arranged for stools to be brought for the two younger scholars and elevated them to equal footing.

### The Dove Speech

At this point, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel — who was Rabbi's father — said: *pireda achas yesh li beinechem* — "I have one dove among you," referring to his son. *Ve'atem mevakshim le'avda mimeni?* — "And you want to make him lost to me?" Rashi explains that the concern was *ayin hara*: elevating these young men to the stools would attract the evil eye, inviting jealousy and the potential harm that accompanies it. To protect Rabbi, they brought him back down from the stool. It is worth noting the resonance of the phrase: the "dove" language echoes an earlier version of the Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai story, in which Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai appeared in a dream and referred to his buried son as "my dove."

### Korcha's Challenge

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha then objected: *mi she'yesh lo av yichye, mi she'ein lo av yamut?* — "Should the one who has a father live, and the one who has no father die?" In other words, only Rabbi receives the protection of being demoted, because he has his father interceding on his behalf? Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai had already died, leaving Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon without that protection. Should Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon be exposed to *ayin hara* on Rabbi's account? In response, they also brought Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon back down to sit on the ground.

### Elazar's Resentment

Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon became despondent — *chalash da'atei*. The pattern of *chalash da'atei* echoes the later story of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish, where similar dynamics of intellectual rivalry and perceived slights play out. Here, Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon understood that he was being demoted only because they had to demote Rabbi, as if the two were equivalents. *Kachashivtu lei kavasi* — "You consider him my equal?" He knew full well that he was Rabbi's superior in *Torah*.

### Support Withdrawn

Until that moment, whenever Rabbi would pose a question or articulate a position from his place on the ground, Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon would back him up and support what he said. From this point forward, however, whenever Rabbi said "I have something to answer" or "I have a response," Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon would preempt him: *kach ve'chach yesh lach le'hashiv?* — "That is what you have to say?" He would immediately supply the answer himself, making Rabbi's contribution appear trivial — *HashTa hikfitinu teshuvot chavilot she'ein bahem mamash* — "Now you are surrounding us with bundles of responses that have no substance." He was, in effect, constantly working to undermine Rabbi's standing.

This is notable for how it inverts the dynamic that Rabbi Yochanan would later lament when he lost Reish Lakish. Rabbi Yochanan did not want a mere supporter who would simply agree with him; he wanted a challenger who would sharpen his thinking. Here, however, Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon's "challenges" were not constructive refinements — a teacher sometimes rephrases a student's question to strengthen it — but rather deliberate acts of undermining. The support that had preceded them was genuinely helpful; what replaced it was not. The result was that *chalash da'atei de'Rebbi* — now it was Rabbi who became despondent, feeling made to appear foolish. But the point of the story, for purposes of the *gemara*'s argument, is established: Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon was clearly the greater *talmid chacham*.

### Father's Comfort

Rabbi went and told his father, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, what had happened. His father comforted him: *beni al yeira lecha, she'hu ari ben ari* — "My son, do not let it trouble you. He is a lion the son of a lion." Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, the author of the *Zohar*, was his father — an extraordinary lineage. *Ve'ata ari ben shual* — "And you are a lion, but the son of a fox." In other words, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel was acknowledging that he himself was not so great in *Torah*. By absorbing the implied slight — accepting the characterization of himself as a "fox" — he deflected the insult away from Rabbi so that Rabbi would not feel personally diminished. It is this act that the *gemara* now identifies as one of three exemplary instances of *anavah*.

## Three Humble Sages

### The List

The *gemara* transitions with *ve'hainu de'amar Rebbi* — "and this is what Rabbi said." Rabbi declared: *shelosha anvesanim hen* — there are three truly modest people. They are: *abba*, his father Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel; *Bnei Beteira*; and Yonatan ben Shaul. It is worth pausing on the irony: a later source, the *mishnah* at the end of *Sotah*, states that when Rabbi died there was no more *anavah* in the world. And yet here Rabbi himself is listing those he considers the models of *anavah*, without including himself. One might argue that this very act of not including himself is itself a form of *anavah* — or, more likely, the *gemara* simply reflects how Rabbi genuinely understood the virtue.

### Shimon's Humility

The case of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel has already been explained: he took the insult upon himself, describing himself as a fox, so that his son Rabbi would not feel that it was he who was being called the inferior one. The self-deprecation was deliberate and generous.

### Beteira's Recognition

The *Bnei Beteira* were the leading authorities at the time of the Second *Beit HaMikdash*. A well-known episode: when the question arose whether the *korban pesach* could be brought on *Shabbat* when *erev pesach* fell on *Shabbat*, no one knew the answer. Someone recalled that there was a scholar who had come up from *Bavel*, a student of Shemaya and Avtalyon — *Hillel HaBavli shemo*. Hillel proceeded to demonstrate from *kal vachomer* and numerous other logical arguments that the *korban pesach* overrides *Shabbat*. (An interesting variant in the *Tosefta* records that despite all his arguments, they did not accept his reasoning — until he said, *kach mekubalni mi'Shemaya ve'Avtalyon*, "this is the tradition I received from Shemaya and Avtalyon," at which point they immediately accepted him. The *Tosefta* version reflects a broader tension in *Torah shebe'al peh* between received *mesorah* and rational derivation.) The outcome in any case was that *hoshivuhu barosh* — they installed Hillel at the head — *ve'minnuhu l'nasi aleihem*, making him the *nasi*, initiating the *nesiut* that descended through to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and ultimately to Rabbi himself.

The *Bnei Beteira* are credited with *anavah* for willingly ceding their authority and position to Hillel upon recognizing his greatness.

### Yonatan's Acceptance

Yonatan ben Shaul earned his place on the list because he said to David: *ve'ata timloch al Yisrael ve'ani eheyeh lecha lemishneh* — "You will be king over Israel and I will be your second." He accepted his subordinate role voluntarily, acknowledging David's kingship.

### Gemara's Questions

The *gemara* immediately presses: *mi'mai* — on what basis do we know these were truly *anavim* rather than simply pragmatic actors? Perhaps Yonatan ben Shaul simply saw that the entire world was following David and made the sensible calculation — *dechaza d'agdi alma batar Dovid* — better to align with the one who is clearly going to prevail than to resist the tide. Similarly, perhaps the *Bnei Beteira* merely saw that Hillel was greater than they — *dechazu l'Hillel de'adif minaihu* — and acted out of self-interest.

Rashi makes this point explicit regarding the *Bnei Beteira*: they were embarrassed — *boshim badavar* — because questions were being put to them in the *beit midrash* that they could not answer, while Hillel could. Better to step down voluntarily than to be publicly humiliated by one's own inadequacy. That is not *anavah*; that is self-protection.

Yet the *gemara*'s framing leaves open a more generous reading. Perhaps *anavah* does not require a false or artificially low self-assessment. Perhaps it consists simply in the honest recognition of others' superiority and the willingness to act accordingly — not concealing one's own abilities, but not insisting on primacy in the face of clear evidence to the contrary. Rashi's reading, though, suggests that what distinguishes genuine *anavah* from mere pragmatism is the absence of any external compulsion. In Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's case, nothing forced him to say what he said — and therefore he alone, of the three, is clearly an *anav*.

## Comparing Afflictions

### Rabbi Embraces Suffering

The *gemara* now states: *amar Rebbi, chavivin yissurin* — Rabbi declared that *yissurim* are beloved. He then proceeded to accept upon himself thirteen years of *yissurin*. One might wonder: why did Rabbi simply accept the widow's characterization? Why, merely because she said that *yissurim* made Rabbi Elazar greater, should he treat that as authoritative? Rashi addresses this directly. Rashi explains that Rabbi's conclusion did not rest on what the widow said but on direct evidence: *shara shehoyilu yissurin she'Rebbe Elazar shelo shalta bo rimmah* — the *yissurim* had visibly accomplished something, namely that the body was preserved for between eighteen and twenty-two years without decomposition. Rabbi saw in that preservation tangible proof of the extraordinary power of *yissurim*, and perhaps noted a certain poetic logic: the body that was afflicted in life was the body that was preserved in death.

### Nature of Afflictions

Rabbi accepted thirteen years of *yissurin* — the significance of the number thirteen is not explained. *Shita b'tzamarta* — six years with *tzamarta*, which Rashi understands as a urinary infection — possibly kidney stones — and *shiva b'tzaparna* — seven years with *tzaparna*, which Rashi identifies as a disease of the mouth, perhaps something like thrush. An alternate tradition reverses the durations: seven years with the urinary condition and six with the oral disease.

### Animal Feeding Timing

The *gemara* offers a striking detail illustrating the intensity of Rabbi's pain. *Ahuroirah* — his stableman, the keeper of his horses — was, according to a *midrash* cited by *Tosafot*, so wealthy from the dung of Rabbi's horses that he was richer than Shvor Malka, the Persian king. These were enormous, powerful animals: when the stableman threw fodder to them, their neighing could be heard from three *milei* — approximately three kilometers. The stableman would deliberately time the feeding of the animals to coincide with the moment Rabbi had to go relieve himself, so that the sound of the horses would drown out whatever sounds Rabbi made in his pain.

### Pain's Intensity

Even so, the *gemara* records, *ma'avar lei kalyei l'kalyei de'Rebbi* — Rabbi's own cries overpowered even the sound of the horses. *Ve'sham'u lehu nchutei yama* — sailors at sea could hear him screaming. The image is almost overwhelming.

### Love vs. Deed

And yet, with all of this, the *gemara* concludes: *yissurei de'Rebbe Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon adifi mi'd'Rebbi* — Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon's *yissurim* were of a higher order than Rabbi's. The distinction the *gemara* draws is precise. Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon's *yissurin*: *me'ahava bo u'me'ahava ozlu* — they came from love and they departed from love. They were religiously motivated from the beginning — he invited them out of a spirit of religious devotion — and they left when he set them aside in order to go and learn in the *beit midrash*, equally a religious act. By contrast, Rabbi's: *adai masei bo v'adai masei ozlu* — they came as a consequence of an action and they departed as a consequence of an action. The *gemara* will now explain what this means.

## The Calf Story

### Calf Seeks Refuge

*D'hu egla d'havei kamamtilei l'shchita* — there was a calf that was being led to *shchita*. It broke away and ran to hide itself in the skirts of Rabbi's cloak, weeping. Rabbi's response was unsparing: *zil, lekach notzarta* — "Go. This is what you were created for." He sent it away and showed it no compassion.

### Heaven's Decree

From above, the response was immediate: *ho'il velo kamracham, leisu alei yissurin* — since he did not show compassion, let him be afflicted with *yissurin*, so that he may come to understand what pain means and thereby develop the capacity to empathize with others' suffering.

### Rabbi's Repentance

The *yissurin* departed through a different act. One day, Rabbi's maidservant was sweeping the house and came upon a litter of newborn weasels. She began sweeping them away. Rabbi intervened: *shabkinhu* — "Leave them alone." *Ksiv verachamov al kol ma'asav* — "It is written, 'His compassion is over all His creatures.'" Rabbi had changed. He had become genuinely compassionate, even toward creatures considerably less appealing than a frightened calf. From above: *ho'il umracham, nerachem alei* — "Since he shows compassion, let us show compassion on him."

A question presents itself: if Rabbi's *yissurin* departed through an act of compassion — through *rachamim*, through what one might call *ahavah* — why does the *gemara* still distinguish his *yissurin* unfavorably from those of Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon, whose *yissurin* departed *me'ahava*? The answer seems to lie in the overall religious orientation of the experience. Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon related to his *yissurim* in an explicitly religious framework throughout — he invited them religiously and released them for religious reasons. Rabbi, at least according to this account, was not relating to the experience in that same religious mode. He was ill, and he happened to perform a compassionate act. The act was beautiful, but the frame was different.

### Blessings During Sufferi…

A further dimension of the *yissurin* narrative: *kulhu sheinei yissurei de'Rebbi Elazar, lo shachiv inish belo zimnei* — all the years that Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon was suffering his afflictions, no one died before their proper time. The suffering of the righteous appears to have conferred a kind of protection on the community around him. Similarly, *kulhu sheinei yissurei de'Rebbi, lo itzterich alma lemitra* — all the years of Rabbi's afflictions, the world did not need rain. This is, admittedly, a slightly ambiguous blessing. Rain, after all, is generally understood as a gift, and its withholding evokes the curse of Eliyahu. The *gemara* addresses this: *Rabbah bar bar Shila* teaches that the day of rain is as difficult as the day of judgment — as necessary as both may be, neither is exactly pleasant. And *Ameimar* adds: were rain not needed to sustain the world, the rabbis would simply pray for it to stop altogether.

The blessing was not drought, however. *Afilu hachi* — even though it did not rain, the ground was miraculously saturated. *Ki havei akri pugla mi'mishra da, hava kai birei mala maya* — when you would pull a radish from its place in the ground, the hole it left behind would fill with water. The earth itself was so full of moisture that no external rain was necessary.

## Rabbi Elazar's Son

### The Son's Downfall

Rabbi traveled to the village of Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon. He asked the people there: *yesh lo ben le'oto tzadik* — "Did that righteous man leave behind a son?" The answer was yes — but the son had gone badly astray. *Ve'chol zonah she'niskeret bishnayim, socherato bishmona* — any prostitute who rented herself out for two would pay him eight. He was beautiful and had become a male prostitute. The contrast with his father is stark and raises obvious theological discomfort: how could the son of such a *tzadik* have descended so low?

### Ordination & Placement

Rabbi took immediate and decisive action. *Atyei asmechei beRebbi* — first, he gave the son *semicha*, ordination. This is remarkable: granting *semicha* to someone living as a prostitute. The logic seems to be one of transformative expectation — invest him with the dignity and responsibility of the title, and he may rise to meet it. Rabbi then placed him under the tutelage of *Rabbi Shimon ben Isi ben Lekunya*, who was the young man's maternal uncle.

### Initial Resistance

Every day the son would say he wanted to go back to his village — *le'kiryati ana azil* — back to his former life. The response was: How can you? They announced you as a *chacham*, they draped a golden cloak over you in the investiture ceremony — *ve'gulta de'dahava parsu alach* — and they proclaimed you a *Rabbi*. You wish to simply go home? The son was moved. He took an oath — *mumi, azova da* — and committed to staying and learning.

### Success Story

In time, the son grew and was sent to Rabbi's *mesivta*. Rabbi heard a student's voice in discussion and recognized it immediately: *ha'kala damei le'kala de'Rabbi Elazar berabi Shimon* — "That voice sounds like the voice of Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon." He was told: it is his son. The transformation was complete. The son — who, as we now learn, was named Rabbi Yosi — had become a genuine *talmid chacham*.

### Proverbs Citation

Rabbi applied to this outcome the verse: *pri tzadik etz chayim, ve'lokeach nefashot chacham* — "The fruit of the righteous is a tree of life, and the one who takes souls is wise" (Mishlei). *Pri tzadik etz chayim* — that is Rabbi Yosi ben Rabbi Elazar, the fruit of the righteous father who became a tree of life in his own right. *Ve'lokeach nefashot chacham* — that is Rabbi Shimon ben Isi ben Lekunya, who took this young man's soul under his wing and nurtured it. And of course Rabbi orchestrated the entire enterprise.

### Cave Refusal

When Rabbi Yosi died, they brought him to the family cave — the cave where Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai and Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon were buried — to be interred alongside his father and grandfather. The same scene repeated itself: the serpent (*achna*) was coiled at the entrance, blocking the way. As before, they called out: *p'tach picha ve'yikanes ben etzel aviv* — "Open your mouth and let the son come to the father." But this time, *lo p'tach lehu* — the serpent did not open.

### Heaven's Explanation

The bystanders drew an obvious conclusion: *she'zeh gadol mi'zeh* — the father must have been greater than the son, and therefore the cave would not accept the son as an equal. But then *yatzta bat kol ve'amrah* — a heavenly voice issued forth and said: it is not because the father was greater. *Ella zeh hayah betza'ar me'arah ve'zeh lo hayah betza'ar me'arah* — the father had suffered in the cave. This is the cave of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai: everyone knows the story of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai hiding in the cave for years, fleeing Roman persecution, enduring extraordinary deprivation. Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon had been there with him. Rabbi Yosi had not shared that suffering. The cave was therefore more receptive to one who had suffered within it. The son, despite his eventual greatness, had to be buried elsewhere. The entire arc of the story is here brought into relief: father and son shared the cave in life and in death; the grandson did not.

It is worth noting, as was observed before the class: the irony is palpable. Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai fled to the cave because of his contempt for the Roman government. His son Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon later served as a magistrate in the employ of that same government. And yet even Rabbi Elazar's willingness to compromise with Rome could not sever the bond with his father's suffering — the cave accepted him precisely because he had shared in it.

## Rabbi Tarfon's Grandson

### Grandson's Deeds

Rabbi traveled to the village of Rabbi Tarfon, who had passed away. He asked: *yesh lo ben le'oto tzadik* — "Did that righteous man leave behind a son?" The phrase *she'hayah mekapeach et banav* is appended: Rabbi Tarfon was known for his frequent oath — *akpeach et banai* — something like "let me bury my children" or "on the lives of my children," used as an emphatic affirmation. The people replied: no sons survived — which itself hangs in the air given his habit of invoking his children in oaths. There was, however, a grandson — *ben bat yesh lo*, the son of a daughter. And this grandson too had gone off the *derech*: the same description as before — *ve'chol zonah she'niskeret bishnayim socherato bishmona*.

### Marriage Incentive

Rabbi adopted a different strategy here than with the son of Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon. Rather than immediately granting *semicha*, he offered an incentive: *ei hadrat bach, yehavina lach barti* — "If you do *teshuvah*, I will give you my daughter in marriage." The parallel to Rabbi Yochanan's offer to Reish Lakish is unmistakable — "do *teshuvah* and I will give you my sister" — and it is surely deliberate. *Hadar bieh* — the grandson did *teshuvah*.

### Marriage Dispute

The *gemara* records a dispute about what happened next. *Ika de'amri nasbah ve'geirsah* — some say he married Rabbi's daughter and then divorced her. *Ika de'amri lo nasbah klal* — others say he refused to marry her at all. Why? *Kedai shelo yomru bishvil zeh chazar zeh* — so that people would not say he had done *teshuvah* only in order to marry the daughter. This position is noteworthy: once he had genuinely undergone the process of *teshuvah* and become a *talmid chacham*, he did not wish the original motivation to cast a shadow over his transformation. Though there is a certain awkwardness in the version where he married and then divorced — it raises obvious concerns about the woman involved — both versions reflect the same underlying concern for authenticity.

## Teaching Torah Merit

### Academy Reward

The *gemara* now asks: *lama lei kulei hai* — why did Rabbi exert himself to this degree for the wayward descendants of great *tzaddikim*? The answer comes in a chain of transmission: Rav Yehuda citing Rav, and others citing Rabbi Chiya bar Abba citing Rabbi Yochanan, and still others citing Shmuel bar Nachmani citing Rabbi Yonatan: *kol hamelamed et ben chaveiro Torah, zoche ve'yoshev be'yeshiva shel maalah* — whoever teaches his friend's son Torah will merit sitting in the heavenly *yeshiva*. The proof text is from *Yirmiyahu*: *im tashiv ashivacha lefanai ta'amod* — "If you will bring them back (*tashiv*), I will return you (*ashivacha*) to stand before Me." The verbal root *shuv* connects *teshuvah*, *yeshiva*, and *yoshev* — to return, to the academy, to sitting — an interconnection the *gemara* exploits as a textual anchor for the teaching.

### Annulling Decrees

A further principle follows: *ve'chol hamelamed et ben am ha'aretz Torah* — whoever teaches the son of an *am ha'aretz* Torah — *afilu Hakadosh Baruch Hu gozer gezeirah, mevatla bishvilo* — even if the Holy One Blessed Be He has issued a decree, He will annul it for the sake of such a person. The scale of the two cases differs markedly. Teaching a friend's son Torah is an act of loyalty and care for those close to you — a natural extension of human relationship. Teaching the son of an *am ha'aretz*, however, is a reversal of the seemingly natural order of things. If someone can accomplish that, he has in a sense changed the course of nature — and for such a person, God is prepared to change the course of nature in turn. The proof text: *ve'im totzi yakar mi'zolel, ke'fi tihyeh* — "If you bring out something precious from what is degraded, you will be as My mouth." What you say will be fulfilled.

### Generational Torah

Rabbi Parnach in the name of Rabbi Yochanan adds: *kol she'hu talmid chacham u'beno talmid chacham u'ven beno talmid chacham, shuv ein Torah poseket mi'zaro le'olam* — if you are a *talmid chacham*, and your son is a *talmid chacham*, and your grandson is, the *Torah* will never cease from your descendants. The *mesorah* has been so thoroughly embedded that it becomes self-sustaining. The proof text is the promise of *va'ani zot beriti otam amar Hashem, lo yamushu mipicha u'mipi zarecha u'mipi zera zarecha, amar Hashem me'ata ve'ad olam* — "This is My covenant with them, says God: the Torah will not depart from your mouth, nor from the mouth of your children, nor the children of your children, says God, from now and forever."

The *gemara* notes the unusual placement of *amar Hashem* within the verse. Why does it interrupt? *Amar Hakadosh Baruch Hu ani areiv lach be'davar zeh* — God is positioning Himself as the guarantor. Once the three generations have been established — *mipicha u'mipi zarecha ve'zera zarecha* — God personally guarantees that it will continue *me'ata ve'ad olam*.

The *Maharsha* cautions against a potential misreading: this does not mean that descendants of great *talmidei chachamim* need not exert effort themselves. *Ein haTorah yerushah lach* — the *Torah* is not simply an inheritance that arrives without labor. The *Maharsha* explains that God as guarantor functions as a guarantor of a loan functions: the loan still must be repaid, but the guarantor ensures it will be. The *Torah* must still be worked for — but God will ensure the conditions that make that work possible and successful.

Rabbi Yirmiyah adds a final dimension: *mikan ve'eilach Torah mechazeret al achsanya shela* — from that point on, once three generations have been established, the *Torah* returns to its lodging place. The *Maharsha* highlights the word *mechazeret* as distinct from *chozeret*: *mechazeret* suggests active searching, as though the *Torah* is going out looking for its home. This too implies that it does not happen automatically — the *Torah* seeks, it is not simply deposited. But the family has become a home to which it is naturally drawn.

## Fasting for Merit

### Yosef's First Fast

Rav Yosef sat forty fasts. In a dream, he was shown the verse *lo yamushu mipicha* — the Torah will not depart from your mouth. He had achieved the first level of the promise.

### Generational Fasts

He sat another forty fasts. This time, *lo yamushu mipicha u'mipi zarecha* — the promise extended to his children. And then a third set of fasts — some say forty, some say a hundred — after which: *lo yamushu mipicha u'mipi zarecha u'mipi zera zarecha*. The grandchildren were included.

### Torah's Lodging

Upon achieving this third generation of promise, Rav Yosef said: *mikan ve'eilach lo tzrichina* — I need nothing further. *Torah mechazeret al achsanya shela* — the *Torah* will find its way back to its home. The three-generation threshold had been crossed; the foundation was secure.

### Zeira's Forgetting

When Rav Zeira went up to *Eretz Yisrael*, he too sat a hundred fasts — but for a different purpose: *dilishitkach gemara bavla minei* — that he might forget his Babylonian *gemara*, *ki heichi dilo nitredei* — so that it should not distract him.

### Learning Styles

What does it mean that Babylonian learning would "distract" him? Rashi explains: in *Eretz Yisrael*, the scholars — including Rabbi Yochanan and the other *amoraim* of that generation — were *nochin zeh la'zeh*, pleasant with one another, not given to constant confrontation and challenge. Their approach was closer to *pshat*, to the plain meaning of texts, establishing the tradition without the elaborate superstructure of challenges and resolutions, distinctions and counter-distinctions, that characterize the *Bavli*. The *Talmud Eretz Yisrael* tends to stay closer to the original language of *mishnah* and *braisa*; the *Talmud Bavli* builds an elaborate dialectical architecture on top of those foundations. Rashi notes this not as a simple compliment to either tradition but as a difference in character. If Rav Zeira arrived in *Eretz Yisrael* with his mind steeped in Babylonian analytical habits, those habits might pull him away from the mode of learning prevalent there.

### Pilpul Critique

The *Maharsha* seizes on this context to level a pointed critique at the *pilpul* of his own day. He distinguishes between *pilpulim* that genuinely sharpen understanding — *ravcha shmaita*, that open up the *sugia* and illuminate it — and a different kind of argumentation: *pilpul shel hevel*, empty dialectics, in which the goal is not to arrive at truth but to display rhetorical skill. He writes: if someone could construct a more impressive-sounding argument even for a position that is complete nonsense, he was praised — *harei zeh meshubach*. And *vechol echad mechaven lefasel divrei chaveiro* — every participant was simply trying to disprove what the others said, with no genuine commitment to *halacha le'ma'aseh* or to *pshat*. This, the *Maharsha* suggests, is what the *gemara* has in mind when it criticizes the *Babylonian* approach — not the depth of Babylonian learning per se, but its potential degeneracy into performance rather than truth-seeking.

### Second Fast

Rav Zeira fasted another hundred fasts *delo lishkach Rebbi Elazar bishinei* — that Rabbi Elazar (possibly Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon, though the chronology is not verified) should not die while he, Rav Zeira, was alive, *ve'nafki lei milei de'tzibura* — since in that event Rav Zeira would be thrust into communal responsibilities he wished to avoid.

### Fire Resistance

He then fasted yet another hundred fasts *de'lo lishlot bei nura de'Gehinnom* — that the fire of *Gehinnom* should have no power over him after death.

### Evil Eye Burns

To test whether his fasts had achieved this protection, Rav Zeira would conduct a monthly self-examination. *Shagar tanura, salik ve'yasiv begoavei* — he would heat the oven and sit inside it, *ve'lo hava shalta bei nura* — and the fire had no effect on him. One day, however, *yahavu bei rabbanan eina* — the rabbis gave him the evil eye. Who is this person, sitting in ovens? *Ve'itcharchu shokeih* — and his legs were burned. As a result, he acquired the nickname *katin charich shakeih* — the short one with the burned legs. The *Maharsha* notes that the name *Zeira* may itself derive from *ze'ira*, the Aramaic for "small," consistent with this epithet.

## Land's Destruction

### Unanswerable Question

The *gemara* introduces a famous and haunting *pasuk*: *mi ha'ish ha'chacham ve'yaven et zot, va'asher dibber pi Hashem eilav ve'yagiddah, al mah avdah ha'aretz* — "Who is the wise person who will understand this, and to whom has God spoken, that he may tell it — for what reason was the land destroyed?" *Davar zeh amru chachamim ve'lo peirashuhu* — the rabbis were asked and could not explain it. *Amru nevi'im ve'lo peirashuhu* — the prophets were asked and could not explain it. *Ad she'peirasho HaKadosh Baruch Hu be'atzmo* — until the Holy One Blessed Be He explained it Himself: *al ozvam et Torati asher natati lifneihem* — because they abandoned My Torah that I placed before them.

### Missing Blessings

And yet even this divine explanation requires interpretation. *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav: shelo barchu batorah techilah* — they did not recite the *birchot haTorah* before learning. Rashi explains the connection through the word *lifneihem* — "that I placed before them." A *berachah* is recited *before* performing a *mitzvah*. The absence of *birchot haTorah* indicates something about their relationship to Torah learning itself.

### Blessings & Gifts

Rashi sharpens the analysis: *vekivan de'lo mevarchi, galia da'ataihu she'eina matanah chashuvah lahem* — the failure to recite a *berachah* reveals that they did not treasure the Torah as a gift. A *berachah* expresses the value one places on what one receives. If you receive the Torah but treat it as something you simply take up out of intellectual habit or personal inclination — rather than as a divine gift of extraordinary worth — you have fundamentally misunderstood its nature. It is also worth noting the linguistic connection that brings this discussion here: the language of *lo yamushu mipicha u'mipi zarecha u'mipi zera zarecha* that we have been discussing is precisely the language of *birchot haTorah* — *shelo yamushu ha'Torah mipi'nu u'mipi zarei'nu ve'zera zarei'nu*. The two are bound together: genuine continuity of Torah through the generations, and the *berachah* that expresses why it is worth continuing.

## Humility & Torah

### Humility's Reward

*Amar Reish Lakish*: the verse *katan ve'gadol sham hu, ve'eved chofshi me'adonav* — "small and great are there, and a slave is free from his master." This would seem obvious — of course both the small and the great are present in the afterlife. The deeper meaning: *kol hamaktin atzmo al divrei Torah be'Olam HaZeh, na'aseh gadol le'Olam HaBa* — whoever makes himself small over Torah in this world becomes great in the World to Come. That is the *katan ve'gadol* — small in this world, great in the next.

### Wisdom's Rest

*Amar Rav Chama*: *be'lev navon tanuach chochmah, u've'kerev kesilim tivade'a* — "in the heart of a wise person wisdom rests, but in the midst of fools it is made known." *Zeh talmid chacham ben talmid chacham* — a *talmid chacham* who is the son of a *talmid chacham*: the *Torah* rests peacefully and quietly in his heart. He does not need to announce it or display it; he is secure in it. *Tivade'a*, by contrast — *zeh talmid chacham ben am ha'aretz* — the first-generation scholar, who has accomplished something genuinely remarkable in rising from an unlearned family, tends toward ostentation. It is like new wealth: it announces itself. The restraint and confidence that come with Torah having been one's inheritance for generations is a different thing altogether.

### Coin Parable

*Amar Ulla*: that is what people say — *astira be'galgina, kish kish karya* — a single coin in an empty pitcher rattles and rings constantly. But a pitcher filled with coins makes no sound. One coin: endless noise. Many coins: silence. The more you have, the quieter you are.

### Slavery to Freedom

*Ve'chol ha'masim atzmo ke'eved al divrei Torah be'Olam HaZeh* — whoever makes himself like a slave to Torah in this world, fully accepting its yoke and subjecting himself to its demands, *na'aseh chofshi le'Olam HaBa* — becomes truly free in the World to Come. The paradox is deliberate: it is not freedom from Torah that constitutes ultimate freedom, but the condition of having fully submitted to it in this world.

## Rabbi Chiya's Cave

### Missing Cave

Reish Lakish was going through *Tzfat*, marking the caves of the rabbis — cataloguing their burial places. *Ki m'ta le'me'arta de'Rabbi Chiya, i'almah minei* — when he arrived at where Rabbi Chiya's cave should have been, he could not find it. It was hidden from him.

### Despondency

*Chalash da'atei* — once again the phrase, as throughout this *daf*, *chalash da'atei*. Reish Lakish became despondent. He said before God: *lo pilpalti Torah ke'moso?* — Did I not have the same sharpness, the same analytical power, the same *pilpul* in Torah as Rabbi Chiya? Was I not his equal in that regard?

### Heaven's Response

*Yatzas bat kol ve'amrah lo* — a heavenly voice went out and answered him: *Torah ke'moso pilpalta* — yes, you were his equal in Torah analysis, in *pilpul*, in sharpness. But — *Torah ke'moso lo ribatzta* — you did not spread Torah like he did. It is not enough to be a brilliant *talmid chacham* within the walls of the *beit midrash*. What Rabbi Chiya did was bring the Torah outward, to the people — *ribbui Torah*, the spreading and proliferation of Torah learning in the world. That is what earned him the distinction that his cave remained hidden, beyond what could be catalogued and marked by even the greatest of scholars.

### Chiya's Power

The *gemara* here introduces what will be the subject of the next portion of the *daf*: a demonstration of how Rabbi Chiya actually spread Torah and why that work was of such extraordinary consequence. The power of Rabbi Chiya to disseminate Torah broadly — not merely to learn it at the highest level, but to ensure it reached those who might otherwise have been left without it — is precisely what the heavenly voice identified as the quality that set him apart. That story will be taken up in the next shiur.
