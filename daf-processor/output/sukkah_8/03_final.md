# Sukkah 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Abaye's Tannaim

### Intro: Dirat Keva

Yesterday's discussion introduced a theme that lies at the heart of the laws of *sukkah*: Abaye collected a series of *tannaim* — various positions scattered across different *mishnayot* and *braitot* — all of whom, from his perspective, make greater demands of the *sukkah* that reflect an understanding of it as a *dirat keva*, a more permanent structure. This tension runs through the entire tractate. On one hand, the *sukkah* is by definition temporary — that is precisely what distinguishes it from one's house. On the other hand, the *sukkah* is a substitute for one's house, and so perhaps it must be more house-like. Abaye identified a range of *tannaitic* positions, each with its own unique reasoning, but all of them interpretable as rooted in the concept of the *sukkah* as demanding some greater degree of permanence or structure.

One point is worth flagging at the outset: even among those who hold that a *sukkah* must be more *keva*, the *schach* remains irreducibly temporary. As *Tosafot* noted at the very beginning of the tractate, one may have permanent walls but never a permanent roof. Whatever degree of house-like permanence the *tannaim* require, everyone presumably agrees that the *schach* is what fundamentally distinguishes the *sukkah* from a house. With that in mind, we turn to Abaye's list itself, picking up from the discussion on 7b.

### Rabbi's Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי — דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: כׇּל סוּכָּה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת עַל אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi states this opinion, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Any *sukka* that does not have** an area of **four cubits by four cubits is unfit.** These are the dimensions of a permanent residence.

The first *tanna* in Abaye's collection is Rabbi himself. As we learn in a *braita*: *Rabbi omer kol sukkah she-ein ba arba al arba amot pesulah* — any *sukkah* that does not contain four by four *amot* is invalid. Rabbi's requirement is not the standard minimum of seven *tefachim* by seven *tefachim* sufficient to hold one's head, most of one's body (*rosho ve-rubo*), and a small table (*shulchano*). He demands something considerably larger and more substantial. This, in Abaye's reading, reflects a vision of the *sukkah* as requiring a more serious, house-like size.

### Rabbi Yoshiya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה — הָא דַּאֲמַרַן.
>
> **Translation:** The fact that **Rabbi Yoshiya** holds that a *sukka* must be a permanent residence can be seen from **that which we stated,** that the walls must also be impermeable by sunlight like the walls of a permanent residence.

Rabbi Yoshiya, as discussed previously, requires that the walls themselves provide shade. A house with transparent walls is not a house. If the *sukkah* is to approximate a more permanent dwelling, its walls must function like real walls — providing actual shade. In this way, Rabbi Yoshiya's demand parallels that of Rabbi Shimon, who requires fuller wall coverage, in that both insist the walls serve a genuinely house-like function, not merely a nominal halachic one.

### Rabbi Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה דִּתְנַן סוּכָּה שֶׁהִיא גְּבוֹהָה לְמַעְלָה מֵעֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה פְּסוּלָה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַכְשִׁיר
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda** also holds that a *sukka* must be a permanent residence, **as we learned** in the mishna: **A *sukka* that is more than twenty cubits high is unfit; Rabbi Yehuda deems it fit.** As explained above, in constructing a *sukka* more than twenty cubits high, one cannot render his residence a temporary residence; rather, he must construct a sturdy permanent residence.

Rabbi Yehuda presents a slightly different case. As the *Mishnah* records: *sukkah she-hi gevohah lemala me-esrim amah pesulah ve-Rabbi Yehuda machshir* — a *sukkah* higher than twenty *amot* is invalid, but Rabbi Yehuda permits it. This is worth noting: Rabbi Yehuda does not require a *sukkah* to be higher than twenty *amot*, he merely allows it. He is thus the least demanding of the *tannaim* in Abaye's collection, and in a sense his position sits somewhat differently from the others — they impose additional requirements, whereas he removes a restriction. Nevertheless, Abaye includes him because the willingness to validate a grand, imposing structure reflects an openness to the *sukkah* as a more permanent and substantial dwelling.

### Rabbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן — דְּתַנְיָא: שְׁתַּיִם כְּהִלְכָתָן וּשְׁלִישִׁית אֲפִילּוּ טֶפַח, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: שָׁלֹשׁ כְּהִלְכָתָן וּרְבִיעִית אֲפִילּוּ טֶפַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon** agrees, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The dimensions of a *sukka* are **two** walls **in the standard sense,** and **a third** wall that measures **even a handbreadth; Rabbi Shimon says: Three** of the walls must be walls **in the standard sense, and a fourth** wall is required that measures **even a handbreadth.** Apparently, a *sukka* must be surrounded on four sides like a permanent residence.

Rabbi Shimon's position concerns the number of walls required. In the *braita*: *be-rishona u-shtayim ve-hashlishit afilu tefach* — the standard opinion holds that two full walls plus a third measuring even a *tefach* suffice. *Rabbi Shimon omer: gimel ke-hilchatan ve-dalet afilu tefach* — Rabbi Shimon requires three complete walls plus a fourth measuring even a *tefach*. Like Rabbi Yoshiya's demand that the walls provide shade, this requirement insists that the walls play a more genuinely enclosing, house-like role. Whether the emphasis is on full enclosure or on the walls' shading function, both *tannaim* push the *sukkah* in the direction of a real structure.

### Rabban Gamliel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל — דְּתַנְיָא: הָעוֹשֶׂה סוּכָּתוֹ בְּרֹאשׁ הָעֲגָלָה אוֹ בְּרֹאשׁ הַסְּפִינָה, רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל פּוֹסֵל, וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מַכְשִׁיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Gamliel** holds that a *sukka* must be a permanent residence, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who establishes his *sukka* atop a wagon or atop a boat, Rabban Gamliel deems it unfit;** a mobile structure is not a permanent residence. **Rabbi Akiva deems it fit.** Apparently, Rabban Gamliel requires that a *sukka* be a permanent residence.

The *braita* records a dispute between Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Akiva: *ha-oseh sukkato be-rosh ha-agalah o be-rosh ha-sefinah* — one who makes his *sukkah* on top of a wagon or on top of a ship. *Rabban Gamliel posel ve-Rabbi Akiva machshir* — Rabban Gamliel invalidates it and Rabbi Akiva validates it. The reason for Rabban Gamliel's position is structural instability. *Tosafot* notes that the case of a ship is particularly problematic because the winds at sea are far stronger than on dry land, and such a structure may not be able to withstand them even if it would stand in port. The wagon too, bouncing along the road, lacks the stability of a fixed structure. Something whose continued integrity is precarious does not satisfy Rabban Gamliel's standard. In Abaye's framework, this is a demand for stability and permanence, even if a different dimension of it than the other *tannaim* emphasize.

### Beit Shammai/Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי — דִּתְנַן: מִי שֶׁהָיָה רֹאשׁוֹ וְרוּבּוֹ בַּסּוּכָּה וְשׁוּלְחָנוֹ בְּתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי פּוֹסְלִין, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַכְשִׁירִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Beit Shammai** agree, **as we learned** in a mishna: In the case of **one whose head and most of his** body **were in the *sukka* and his table was in the house, Beit Shammai deem** the *sukka* **unfit,** since a small *sukka* is unfit for use and one cannot fulfill the mitzva of *sukka* with it. **And Beit Hillel deem it fit.** Apparently, Beit Shammai require that the *sukka* be similar to a permanent structure.

The *Mishnah*'s dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel concerns whether a *sukkah* must accommodate one's table as well as one's body: *mi she-haya rosho ve-rubo ba-sukkah ve-shulchano be-toch ha-bayit Beit Shammai poslin ve-Beit Hillel machshirin* — if a person's head and most of his body are in the *sukkah* but his table remains in the house, Beit Shammai invalidates it and Beit Hillel validates it. The gap between these positions may not seem dramatic, but it is real. Even if the *sukkah* only needs to be a *dirat arai*, a temporary dwelling, it still needs to be a *dirah* — a place where one actually lives. If one's table is in the house, one will constantly be running back inside; one is never truly dwelling in the *sukkah*. Abaye places Beit Shammai in this collection, though it should be noted that conceptually Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai are not far apart — they differ only in degree. And since we *pasken* like Beit Hillel, that does not necessarily mean we rule that the *sukkah* is a *dirat keva*, as Beit Shammai represents the most minimal of these demands.

### Rabbi Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר — דִּתְנַן: הָעוֹשֶׂה סוּכָּתוֹ כְּמִין צְרִיף, אוֹ שֶׁסְּמָכָהּ לְכוֹתֶל, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר פּוֹסֵל לְפִי שֶׁאֵין לָהּ גַּג, וַחֲכָמִים מַכְשִׁירִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer** holds that a *sukka* must be a permanent residence, **as we learned** in a mishna: In the case of **one who establishes his *sukka* like a type of circular hut** whose walls slope down from the center and has no roof, **or** one **who rested** the *sukka* **against the wall,** taking long branches and placing one end on the ground and leaning the other end against the wall, establishing a structure with no roof, **Rabbi Eliezer deems it unfit because it does not have a roof, and the Rabbis deem it fit.** A permanent residence has a roof.

Rabbi Eliezer's position, recorded in the *Mishnah*, concerns the shape of the *sukkah*: *ha-oseh sukkato k'min tzrif* — one who makes his *sukkah* like a teepee — *o she-samcha l'kotel* — or leans it against a wall (a lean-to). *Rashi* adds that the category may also include something like a structure where the roof flows continuously from the walls without any clear demarcation — perhaps like an igloo — where there is no structurally distinguishable line between wall and roof. *Rabbi Eliezer posel lefi she-ein la gag* — Rabbi Eliezer invalidates such a *sukkah* because it has no real roof, even if there may be a *halachic* roof present. *Va-chachamim machshirin* — the *Rabbanan* validate it. In Abaye's reading, the demand for a real, distinguishable roof and walls is another dimension of requiring the *sukkah* to be more house-like.

### Acherim: Corners

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲחֵרִים — דְּתַנְיָא, אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: סוּכָּה הָעֲשׂוּיָה כְּשׁוֹבָךְ — פְּסוּלָה, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין לָהּ זָוִיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** ***Aḥerim*** agree, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that ***Aḥerim* say: A *sukka* built** in a circular shape **like a dovecote is unfit, because it does not have corners,** and a permanent residence is one with corners.

The final position in Abaye's collection is that of *acherim*, typically understood as Rabbi Meir: *acherim omrim sukkah asuya k'shuvach pesulah lefi she-ein la zaviyot* — a circular *sukkah* is invalid because it has no corners. Who, after all, lives in a circular house? A *sukkah* without corners lacks the defining architectural feature of a normal human dwelling, and *acherim* therefore disqualify it, even if it has a proper roof and adequate dimensions.

Taken together, all of these positions — requiring greater size, functional walls, structural stability, a distinguishable roof, complete wall coverage, and proper corners — can be read as different articulations of the same underlying orientation: a *sukkah* must be more house-like. The specific *halachic* translation differs from *tanna* to *tanna*, but the common direction is clear.

### Gemara Principle

It is worth noting a general principle that is not uncommon in *halachic* literature: when a *Gemara* has an *amora* collect multiple *tannaim* and declare that they all share a common *shittah*, there is a tendency to understand this as a move to bracket that *shittah* out of normative *halacha*. Assembling them under one heading signals, in some readings, that we do not *pasken* like them. The *Gemara* does not say this explicitly here, but awareness of this pattern is valuable context for evaluating Abaye's collection.

## Circular Sukkah Math

### Rabbi Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: סוּכָּה הָעֲשׂוּיָה כְּכִבְשָׁן, אִם יֵשׁ בְּהֶקֵּיפָהּ כְּדֵי לֵישֵׁב בָּהּ עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה בְּנֵי אָדָם — כְּשֵׁרָה, וְאִם לָאו — פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** With regard to **a *sukka*** that is **shaped like a furnace** and is completely round, **if its circumference has sufficient space for twenty-four people to sit in it, it is fit, and if not, it is unfit.**

Having just discussed the disqualification of a circular *sukkah* by *acherim*, the *Gemara* pivots to explore a different question: what size must a circular *sukkah* be in order to be valid? Rabbi Yochanan states: *sukkah asuya k'kivshan* — a *sukkah* made like a furnace, meaning a circular *sukkah* — *im yesh be-hekeifa k'dei le-sheiv ba esrim ve-arba bnei adam, kesherah* — if its circumference is sufficient to seat twenty-four people, it is valid; if not, it is invalid.

### Four-by-Four Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן — כְּרַבִּי, דְּאָמַר: כׇּל סוּכָּה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת עַל אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion did Rabbi Yoḥanan rule that the *sukka* must be so expansive? The Gemara answers: It is undoubtedly **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who said: Any *sukka* that does not have** an area of **four cubits by four cubits is unfit.** Since he requires the *sukka* with the largest minimum dimensions, Rabbi Yoḥanan must hold in accordance with his opinion.

The *Gemara* immediately raises a question. We *pasken* that the minimum *sukkah* size is seven *tefachim* by seven *tefachim*, but Rabbi Yochanan's number — twenty-four people — already implies we are operating under a larger minimum. The *Gemara* therefore assumes: *k'man? k'Rebbi, de-amar kol sukkah she-ein ba arba amot al arba amot pesulah* — we are following Rabbi's opinion that a *sukkah* must be four by four *amot*.

Now the problem becomes quantitative. If a person occupies one *amah* of circumference space, and we need a circle whose internal diameter is four *amot*, then the circumference of that circle should only be twelve — since the *Gemara* estimates *pi* at three, and three times four is twelve. Where, then, does Rabbi Yochanan's twenty-four come from? The *Gemara* considers this step by step.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדִי, גַּבְרָא בְּאַמְּתָא יָתֵיב, כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ בְּהֶקֵּיפוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים יֵשׁ בּוֹ רוֹחַב טֶפַח, בִּתְרֵיסַר סַגִּי?
>
> **Translation:** However, even if he holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, the question arises: **Now, since** when **a person sits,** he occupies one **cubit** of space, the circumference required by Rabbi Yoḥanan for the *sukka* is twenty-four cubits. However, mathematically, for **every three handbreadths circumference** in a circle, **there is a diameter of** approximately one **handbreadth.** Consequently, rather than requiring a *sukka* that holds twenty-four people, a *sukka* that holds merely **twelve people** should **suffice,** since a *sukka* with a circumference of twelve cubits has a diameter of approximately four. In that case, why does Rabbi Yoḥanan require the *sukka* to have double the necessary circumference?

It begins: *kama meruba yoser al ha-igul? reviya* — how much more is the circumference of the square than the circumference of the circle inscribed in it? A quarter more. This is the *Gemara*'s first calculation: if a circle has a diameter of one unit, its circumference is three (using *pi* = 3); the square that contains it has a circumference of four. So the square's circumference exceeds the circle's by one-quarter. If a four-by-four square has a circumference of sixteen, then the circle inside it has a circumference of twelve — which is exactly the *Gemara*'s opening figure.

### Square in Circle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּעִיגּוּלָא, אֲבָל בְּרִיבּוּעָא — בָּעֲיָא טְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This applies only in** the middle of **the circle** that has a circumference of twelve cubits, as the diameter of the circle is four cubits; **but in** order for a **square** inscribed within a circle to have a perimeter of sixteen cubits, the circle **requires** a circumference that is **more** than twelve cubits.

But here the *Gemara* corrects itself: *hanei milei be-igula de-nafik mi-go ribu'a* — that calculation applies when the circle fits inside the square. What we actually need is the reverse: a square that fits inside the circle. The *sukkah* is circular, and we must ensure that a four-by-four square can fit within it. *Aval ribu'a de-nafik mi-go igul ba-ei tefei* — for a square inscribed in a circle, the circle must be larger still. A circle whose circumference equals the perimeter of the surrounding square is not the same as a circle large enough to contain that square inside it.

### Diagonal Calculation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי כַּמָּה מְרוּבָּע יוֹתֵר עַל הָעִיגּוּל — רְבִיעַ, בְּשִׁיתְּסַר סַגִּי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: **Now,** by **how much is** the perimeter of **a square** inscribing a circle **greater than the** circumference of that **circle?** It is greater by **one quarter** of the perimeter of the square. If that is the case, a circle **with** a circumference of **sixteen** cubits is **sufficient.** Why, then, does Rabbi Yoḥanan require a circumference of twenty-four cubits?

The key is the diagonal of the square. To inscribe a four-by-four square within a circle, the diameter of that circle must equal the diagonal of the square. By the Pythagorean theorem — *a* squared plus *b* squared equals *c* squared — where *a* and *b* are both four, the diagonal is four times the square root of two. This diagonal becomes the diameter of the required circle, and the circumference of that circle is three (our estimate of *pi*) times this diameter — three times four times the square root of two.

### 1.4 Diagonal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּעִיגּוּל דְּנָפֵיק מִגּוֹ רִיבּוּעָא, אֲבָל רִיבּוּעָא דְּנָפֵיק מִגּוֹ עִיגּוּלָא — בָּעֵינַן טְפֵי, מִשּׁוּם מוּרְשָׁא דְקַרְנָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This** statement with regard to the ratio of the perimeter of a square to the circumference of a circle **applies to a circle inscribed in a square, but** in the case of **a square circumscribed by a circle,** the circle **requires a greater** circumference **due to the projection of the corners** of the square. In order to ensure that a square whose sides are four cubits each fits neatly into a circle, the circumference of the circle must be greater than sixteen cubits.

The *Gemara* addresses the square root of two by means of a well-known approximation: *kol amsa be-ribu'a, amsa ve-trei chumshai be-alakhsona* — any unit square has a diagonal of one and two-fifths, that is, 1.4. This is the *Gemara*'s estimate of the square root of two. It is, in fact, a quite good approximation: the actual value is approximately 1.414.

### 16.8 vs 24

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי כׇּל אַמְּתָא בְּרִיבּוּעָא אַמְּתָא וּתְרֵי חוּמְשֵׁי בַּאֲלַכְסוֹנָא, בְּשִׁיבְסַר נְכֵי חוּמְשָׁא סַגִּיא? לָא דָּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara calculates precisely how much greater the circumference must be in order to circumscribe the four-by-four-cubit square. **Now,** in **every square** whose sides each measure one **cubit** its **diagonal measures one and two-fifths cubits,** and in a circle that circumscribes a square, the diagonal of the square is the diameter of the circle. In this case, the circumscribed square measures four by four cubits; therefore, the diagonal of the square, which is the diameter of the circle, measures five and three-fifths cubits. Since the Gemara calculates the circumference of the circle as three times its diameter, a circular *sukka* with a circumference of **seventeen** cubits **minus one-fifth** of a cubit should be **sufficient.** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yoḥanan **was not precise** and rounded the dimensions of the circular *sukka* to a number higher than the absolute minimum.

With this in hand, we can now calculate. The diagonal of the four-by-four square is four times 1.4, which is 5.6. That is the diameter of the circle. The circumference of that circle is three times 5.6, which is 16.8. The *Gemara* confirms this: *bishivsar naki chumsha sagya* — seventeen minus a fifth, meaning 16.8, should be sufficient. So why did Rabbi Yochanan say twenty-four?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימוֹר דְּאָמְרִינַן לָא דָּק פּוּרְתָּא, טוּבָא מִי אָמְרִינַן לָא דָּק?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara wonders: **Say that we say** that the Sage was **not precise** when the difference between the number cited and the precise number is **slight;** however, when the difference is **great, do we say** the Sage was **not precise?** After all, Rabbi Yoḥanan stated that the minimum measure is twenty-four cubits, a difference of more than seven cubits.

The *Gemara* tries to invoke the principle of *lo dak* — imprecision, rounding — but immediately rejects it in this context. One can accept a minor rounding; going from 16.8 to 24 is not a minor imprecision. Moreover, if we say 24 is sufficient when the true requirement is 16.8, we are being lenient, not stringent. *Eimar de-amrinan lo dak pursa — tuva mi amrinan?* — We can say he rounded a little, but can we say he rounded that much? And *lekula mi amrinan lo dak* — we do not invoke imprecision in the lenient direction.

### People Sitting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר קַשִּׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: מִי סָבְרַתְּ גַּבְרָא בְּאַמְּתָא יָתֵיב, תְּלָתָא גַּבְרֵי בְּתַרְתֵּי אַמְּתָא יָתְבִי. כַּמָּה הָווּ לְהוּ — שִׁיתְּסַר, אֲנַן שִׁיבְסַר נְכֵי חוּמְשָׁא בָּעֵינַן! לָא דָּק.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Keshisha,** i.e., the elder, **son of Rav Ḥisda, said to Rav Ashi: Do you hold** that when **a man sits, he sits** and occupies one **cubit,** and consequently a *sukka* that seats twenty-four people must have a circumference of twenty-four cubits? In fact, **three people sit** and occupy **two cubits.** The Gemara asks: **How many** cubits **are there** in the *sukka* required by Rabbi Yoḥanan? There are **sixteen** cubits. But **we require** a *sukka* with a circumference of **seventeen** cubits **minus one-fifth,** as calculated above. The Gemara answers: **He was not precise** and rounded the figure down to the lower whole number; actually, the required circumference is four-fifths of a cubit larger.

At this point Mar Kashisha son of Rav Chisda offers a solution to Rav Ashi: *mi savrat gavra be-amta yasiv? tlata gavri be-trei amata yasvin* — who said one person occupies one *amah*? Three people sit in every two *amot*. On this assumption, a circumference of 16.8 *amot* would accommodate not 16.8 people but rather 16.8 times one and a half, which is 25.2 people — close to 24. Working backwards, the *Gemara* then calculates that 24 people at three per two *amot* corresponds to a circumference of 16 *amot*. We needed 16.8 — so 24 people gets us a circumference of 16. That is still a *kula*, still less than needed. And indeed: *eimar de-amrinan lo dak — lekula mi amrinan lo dak?* Even in this new framework we are still rounding leniently.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימוֹר דְּאָמְרִינַן לָא דָּק לְחוּמְרָא, לְקוּלָּא מִי אָמְרִינַן לָא דָּק?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this explanation: **Say that we say** that the Sage was **not precise** when the result is a **stringency,** e.g., he required a *sukka* whose dimensions are greater than the minimum required dimensions; however, when the result is a **leniency, do we say** the Sage **was not precise?** In that case, the lack of precision will lead to establishing a *sukka* whose dimensions are smaller than the minimum requirement.

The *Gemara* then presents another approach, attributed to Rav Assi: *le-olam gavra be-amah yatvei* — actually, one person does occupy one *amah*, as originally assumed. But *Rabbi Yochanan mekom gavrei lo kachshiv* — Rabbi Yochanan is not counting the space that the people sit in as interior space. The people are sitting on the outside of the circle, surrounding the *sukkah*. When we measure twenty-four people seated around the outside of the *sukkah*, the diameter we calculate is the outer circle — including the bodies of the people. Subtracting one *amah* on each side (since each person occupies one *amah*), the inner diameter of the *sukkah* itself is 5.6 plus 2, that is 7.6 *amot* in diameter. This is being imprecise — rounding the inner diameter up slightly to 6 *amot* — but now the rounding is *lechumra*, in the stringent direction. An inner diameter of 6 *amot* yields a circumference of 18. We needed 16.8. We are demanding slightly more than necessary, and that kind of imprecision is entirely acceptable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַסִּי לְרַב אָשֵׁי: לְעוֹלָם גַּבְרָא בְּאַמְּתָא יָתֵיב, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מְקוֹם גַּבְרֵי לָא קָחָשֵׁיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Asi said to Rav Ashi: Actually, a man sits** and occupies **one cubit, and Rabbi Yoḥanan is not factoring the space** that the **men** occupy in his calculation. In other words, to this point, the assumption has been that Rabbi Yoḥanan calculated the circumference of the *sukka* required to seat twenty-four people. Actually, he merely calculated the circumference of the inner circle formed by the twenty-four people seated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּמָּה הָווּ לְהוּ — תַּמְנֵי סְרֵי, בְּשִׁיבְסַר נְכֵי חוּמְשָׁא סַגִּיא! הַיְינוּ דְּלָא דָּק וּלְחוּמְרָא לָא דָּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **How many** cubits **are there** in the circumference of the inner circle formed by a circle of twenty-four people? There are **eighteen** cubits. Based on the principle that for every three cubits of circumference there is one cubit of diameter, the diameter of a circle whose outer circumference surrounds twenty-four people is eight cubits. To calculate the circumference of the inner circle, subtract from the diameter the space occupied by two people, each sitting at one end of the diameter. The result is a diameter of six cubits. Based on the above principle, a circle with a diameter of six cubits will have a circumference of eighteen cubits. However, a circumference of **seventeen** cubits **minus one-fifth** of a cubit should be **sufficient.** The Gemara answers: **This is** the case **where he was not precise, and** in this case **he is not precise** when the result is a **stringency,** as instead of sixteen and four-fifths, Rabbi Yoḥanan required eighteen cubits.

The *Gemara* confirms: *haynu lo dak ve-lechumra lo dak* — this is the acceptable form of imprecision, rounding up for stringency.

### Area Ratio

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּנַן דְּקֵיסָרִי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ דַּיָּינֵי דְקֵיסָרִי אָמְרִי: עִיגּוּלָא דְּנָפֵיק מִגּוֹ רִיבּוּעָא — רִבְעָא,
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages of Caesarea, and some say** that it was **the judges of Caesarea, said** that Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement could be explained using a different calculation: The circumference of **a circle inscribed in a square** is **one**-**quarter** less than the perimeter of the square,

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִיבּוּעָא דְּנָפֵיק מִגּוֹ עִיגּוּלָא — פַּלְגָא. וְלָא הִיא, דְּהָא קָחָזֵינַן דְּלָא הָוֵי כּוּלֵּי הַאי.
>
> **Translation:** while the perimeter of **a square circumscribed by a circle** is smaller than the circumference of that circle by **half,** i.e., if one adds half the perimeter of the square to the perimeter of the square, that is equal to the circumference of its circumscribing circle. Therefore, a circle with a circumference of twenty-four cubits would circumscribe a square with a perimeter of sixteen cubits, as prescribed by Rabbi Yoḥanan. The Gemara notes: **And** that **is not** the case, **as we see that** the circumference of the circumscribing circle **is not that much.** The actual circumference is closer to seventeen cubits.

*Rabbanan de-Kesari*, and some say *Dayanei de-Kesari* (the judges of Caesarea), offer a different statement: *igula de-nafik mi-go ribu'a, riv'a* — a circle inscribed in a square is a quarter less, that is, the ratio of circumference is 3 to 4, as discussed. But *ribu'a de-nafik mi-go igula, palga* — a square inscribed in a circle is related by a half. The *Gemara* rejects this geometrically: *ve-lo hi — de-kachazeina de-la havi kulei hai* — we see that the ratio is not that large.

However, *Tosafot* and the *Ravad* observe that the *Dayanei de-Kesari* may in fact be correct — and indeed have provided the clearest explanation of Rabbi Yochanan's twenty-four — but they are speaking about area, not circumference. If Rabbi Yochanan intended his twenty-four as a measure of area rather than circumference, the problem resolves elegantly. A four-by-four square has an area of sixteen. What is the area of the circle circumscribed around it?

If the square is two by two, its diagonal — and hence the circle's diameter — is two times the square root of two, giving a radius of the square root of two. The area of the circle is *pi* times *r* squared: *pi* times two, which using *pi* = 3 gives an area of six. The area of the square is four. The ratio is 4 to 6, or 2 to 3. Scaling up to a four-by-four square with area sixteen, the enclosing circle has an area of twenty-four. This is precisely the 2-to-3 ratio the *Dayanei de-Kesari* described, and it perfectly explains Rabbi Yochanan's number.

The *Gemara* apparently understood Rabbi Yochanan to be speaking about circumference, and therefore found the *Dayanei de-Kesari*'s ratio too extreme to be geometrically accurate in that context. But if one understands the statement as referring to area, the ratio is exact, and the explanation is strikingly simple: a four-by-four square has an area of sixteen, and the circle that contains it has an area of twenty-four.

### Tosafot Geometry

*Tosafot* appends two remarkable geometric demonstrations to this *sugya*. The first, visible in the illustration at the bottom-left of amud 1, demonstrates that 1.4 is a slightly imprecise estimate for the square root of two. Consider a ten-by-ten square, subdivided into a grid of one hundred units. Connecting the midpoints of each side creates an inner square rotated forty-five degrees; this inner square contains exactly half the area of the outer square, so its area is fifty. Now, each quadrant of the outer square is a five-by-five square. The diagonal of a five-by-five square, at the ratio of 1.4, would be five times 1.4, which is seven. A square with side seven has area forty-nine — not fifty. The one-unit discrepancy demonstrates the slight inaccuracy of the 1.4 estimate. The actual square root of two is approximately 1.414, and the demonstration makes the imprecision vivid.

*Tosafot*'s second illustration anticipates, in a striking way, the integral calculus — centuries before its formal development. To determine the area of a circle, *Tosafot* imagines the circle as composed of an infinite number of concentric rings, or equivalently, infinite radial line segments. Cutting all these segments at their midpoints and laying the two halves out flat produces a triangle. The base of the triangle is the circumference of the circle; the height is the radius. The area of that triangle — one-half times base times height, or one-half times circumference times radius — gives the area of the circle. *Tosafot* realizes this geometrically by folding and rearranging the segments into a rectangle, from which the formula for the area of a circle can be derived.

## Smelter's Sukkot

### Inner/Outer Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: שְׁתֵּי סוּכּוֹת שֶׁל יוֹצְרִים זוֹ לִפְנִים מִזּוֹ — הַפְּנִימִית אֵינָהּ סוּכָּה, וְחַיֶּיבֶת בִּמְזוּזָה. וְהַחִיצוֹנָה סוּכָּה, וּפְטוּרָה מִן הַמְּזוּזָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Levi said in the name of Rabbi Meir:** With regard to **two craftsmen's booths, one within the other,** as potters would build two booths, an inner one used as living quarters and an outer one for plying their craft and selling their wares, **the inner one is not** fit for fulfillment of the mitzva of ***sukka*,** since the potter resides there year-round and it is not evident during the Festival that he is residing there for the sake of the mitzva of *sukka*. **And** since it a permanent residence, **it is** also **obligated in** the mitzva of ***mezuza*. And the outer** booth **is** fit for fulfillment of the mitzva of ***sukka*,** since he does not reside there year-round, and when he resides there during the Festival it is evident that he is doing so for the sake of the mitzva. Since it is not designated as a year-round residence, but rather serves as an entrance to his residence and a passage for merchants and merchandise, it is not considered a residence **and is not obligated in** the mitzva of ***mezuza*.**

The *Gemara* now turns to a different scenario. Rav Huna in the name of Rabbi Meir states: *shtei sukkot shel tzaltarim zu lifnim mi-zu* — if there are two *sukkot* of smelters, one inner and one outer — *ha-penimit eina sukkah* — the inner one cannot serve as a *sukkah* — *de-chayevet be-mezuzah* — because it is obligated in a *mezuzah*. The inner structure is where the smelter actually lives throughout the year; it is his house. Even if it is structurally built like a *sukkah* — perhaps with temporary walls and proper *schach* — it is where he resides, and therefore it cannot be used as his *sukkah* for the festival. *Ve-ha-chitzonah sukkah* — the outer structure, his shop, may serve as a *sukkah* — *u-peturah min ha-mezuzah* — and it is ordinarily exempt from a *mezuzah*, since no one lives there year-round.

This is a striking teaching. Structurally, the inner dwelling may be perfectly constructed. But the *Gemara* makes clear that the validity of a *sukkah* depends not only on structure but on usage: if a space functions as your permanent home throughout the year, you cannot fulfill the *mitzvah* there. The outer room, which is normally a shop and not a dwelling, is precisely the right type of space.

### Gatehouse Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי? תִּהְוֵי חִיצוֹנָה כְּבֵית שַׁעַר הַפְּנִימִית, וְתִתְחַיֵּיב בִּמְזוּזָה! מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא קְבִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why** is the outer booth exempt from the mitzva of *mezuza*? **Let the outer** booth **be** considered **like a gatehouse of the inner** booth **and** therefore **be obligated in** the mitzva of ***mezuza*.** The Gemara answers: It is exempt **because** even the inner booth **is not** a **permanent** residence. It requires a *mezuza* because the potter resides there year-round; however, that alone does not render it a full-fledged residence that would obligate one to affix a *mezuza* to the outer booth as its gatehouse.

The *Gemara* questions the exemption of the outer structure from *mezuzah*: *de-havei chitzoniyah ke-veit shaar ha-penimit ve-titchayev be-mezuzah* — let the outer room be considered a gatehouse for the inner one, and therefore also obligated in a *mezuzah*. An antechamber that leads into a house requires a *mezuzah* alongside the house it serves; why would the outer shop not function as such an antechamber?

### Temporary Status

The *Gemara* rejects this: *mishum de-lo kvia* — because these structures are not sufficiently fixed. As *Rashi* explains, the concept of a *beit shaar*, a gatehouse obligated in *mezuzah*, applies to substantial, distinguished houses that naturally have entranceways serving them. A structure that is sufficiently temporary in character — even if someone lives in it — does not rise to the level of requiring a proper antechamber. The outer shop, being even less substantial, cannot be considered the antechamber to an inner structure that is itself impermanent. Consequently, it requires no *mezuzah*.

The broader upshot of this *sugya* is significant: even structural conformity to *sukkah* requirements is insufficient if the space is one's year-round residence. The identity of a *sukkah* is shaped by how it is used, not only by how it is built.

## Builder Intent

### Ganbakh Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: גַּנְבַּ״‎ךְ: סוּכַּת גּוֹיִם, סוּכַּת נָשִׁים, סוּכַּת בְּהֵמָה, סוּכַּת כּוּתִים סוּכָּה מִכׇּל מָקוֹם — כְּשֵׁרָה, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁתְּהֵא מְסוּכֶּכֶת כְּהִלְכָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** The booths represented by the mnemonic: ***Gimmel*, *nun*, *beit*, *kaf*,** which stands for **a booth of gentiles [*goyim*], a booth of women [*nashim*], a booth of domesticated animals [*behema*], a booth of Samaritans [*Kutim*], a booth of any sort,** each is **fit** for use as a *sukka*, **provided it is roofed in the standard sense.** None of them is disqualified due to the one who constructed it or the purpose for which it was constructed.

The *Gemara* now turns to a question about who may build a *sukkah* and what intentions are required. A *braita* is cited introducing the mnemonic *Ganbakh*: *sukkot goyim* — a *sukkah* built by or for non-Jews; *sukkot nashim* — built for women; *sukkot behemah* — built for animals; *sukkot kutim* — built by or for Samaritans. *Sukkah mikol makom kesherah* — any such *sukkah* is valid — *u-vilvad she-tehei mesukakhat ke-hilkhatah* — as long as it has proper *schach*. The identity of the builder, the intended beneficiary, and the purpose for which it was built are all irrelevant. No intention to fulfill the *mitzvah* of *sukkah* is required of the builder. No Jewish builder is required. No Jewish beneficiary is required. What matters is whether the structure meets the technical requirements.

### Shade Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״כְּהִלְכָתָהּ״? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: וְהוּא שֶׁעֲשָׂאָהּ לְצֵל סוּכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the term: **In the standard sense? Rav Ḥisda said** that it means: **And** provided **that one established** the booth **to** provide **shade of a *sukka*** from its roofing, it may be used to fulfill the mitzva of *sukka*.

The *Gemara* immediately asks: *mai ke-hilkhatah* — what does "proper *schach*" mean in this context? Surely the *schach* must always comply with basic requirements — this seems to add nothing. Rav Chisda explains: *vehu she-asah le-tzeil* — it must have been constructed for the purpose of shade. This is a critical and precise point. We do not require that the *schach* be placed with the intent to fulfill the *mitzvah* of *sukkah*. We do not require that the *schach* be placed for the benefit of Jews, or for any religiously significant purpose. But the *schach* must have been put in place for shade — it must function as a covering intended to provide shade. This is not merely a technical requirement about materials; it is a minimal purposive requirement that the covering be understood as a covering. Something that happens to be overhead but was placed there for an entirely different purpose — not as a roof, not to provide shade — does not have the identity of *schach*. The concept of *schach* is, at its core, a shade-providing covering, and without that purpose present at the time of its installation, the designation does not attach.

### Rakbash Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִכׇּל מָקוֹם״ לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי: סוּכַּת רַקְבַּ״‎שׁ. דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: סוּכַּת רַקְבַּ״‎שׁ: סוּכַּת רוֹעִים, סוּכַּת קַיָּיצִים סוּכַּת בּוּרְגָּנִין סוּכַּת שׁוֹמְרֵי פֵירוֹת, סוּכָּה מִכׇּל מָקוֹם — כְּשֵׁרָה, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁתְּהֵא מְסוּכֶּכֶת כְּהִלְכָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What does** the phrase: A booth **of any sort,** come **to include?** What other booths are included in this generalization? The Gemara answers: It **comes to include** the **booths** listed in another *baraita* with the mnemonic: ***Reish*, *kuf*, *beit*, *shin*, as the Sages taught: The booth** known by the mnemonic ***reish*, *kuf*, *beit*, *shin*,** which stands for **the booth of shepherds [*ro'im*], the booth of fig driers [*kayyatzim*], the booth of guards of fields [*burganin*], the booth of the guards of produce [*shomerei peirot*], a booth of any sort,** each is **fit, provided it is roofed in the standard sense.**

The *braita* continues with a second list, organized around the mnemonic *Rakbash*: *sukkot ro-im* — *sukkot* built by shepherds tending their flocks; *sukkot kayatzim* — built by people watching produce drying in the fields; *sukkot burganin* — built by city watchmen; *sukkot shomrei peirot* — built by fruit-watchers. All of these are structures built by working people who move around and erect temporary shelters in the course of their work. *Sukkah mikol makom kesherah* — any such *sukkah* is valid — *u-vilvad she-tehei mesukakhat ke-hilkhatah* — as long as the *schach* is proper. And again, Rav Chisda: *vehu she-asah le-tzeil* — it was made for shade.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״כְּהִלְכָתָהּ״? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: וְהוּא שֶׁעֲשָׂאָהּ לְצֵל סוּכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks again: **What** is the meaning of the term: **In the standard sense? Rav Ḥisda said** that it means: **And** provided **that one established** the booth **to** provide **shade of a *sukka*,** it may be used to fulfill the mitzva of *sukka*.

### List Ordering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי תַּנָּא דְּגַנְבַּ״‎ךְ, אַלִּימָא לֵיהּ גַּנְבַּ״‎ךְ מִשּׁוּם דִּקְבִיעִי, וְקָא תָּנֵא ״מִכׇּל מָקוֹם״ לְאֵתוֹיֵי רַקְבַּ״‎שׁ, דְּלָא קְבִיעִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **This *tanna* who** taught and detailed the *halakhot* of booths **of *gimmel*, *nun*, *beit*, *kaf*** did so because the fitness of the booths of ***gimmel*, *nun*, *beit*, *kaf*** for use in fulfilling the mitzva of *sukka* is **powerful** and more obvious **to him because they are permanent** structures, even though their builders are not obligated in the mitzva. **And he taught:** Booths of **any sort, to include** the booths of ***reish*, *kuf*, *beit*, *shin*, which,** although they **are** seasonal and **not permanent** structures, may still be used to fulfill the mitzva of *sukka*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי תַּנָּא דְּרַקְבַּ״‎שׁ, אַלִּימָא לֵיהּ רַקְבַּ״‎שׁ, דִּבְנֵי חִיּוּבָא נִינְהוּ, וְתָנֵא ״מִכׇּל מָקוֹם״ לְאֵתוֹיֵי גַּנְבַּ״‎ךְ, דְּלָאו בְּנֵי חִיּוּבָא נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And that** other ***tanna*** who taught and detailed the *halakhot* of booths **of *reish*, *kuf*, *beit*, *shin*** did so because the fitness of the booths of ***reish*, *kuf*, *beit*, *shin*** for use in fulfilling the mitzva of *sukka* is **powerful** and more obvious **to him** because those who constructed the booths are **obligated** in the mitzva of *sukka*. **And he taught:** Booths of **any sort, to include** the booths of ***gimmel*, *nun*, *beit*, *kaf*, which,** although those who constructed them are **not obligated** in the mitzva, may still be used to fulfill the mitzva of *sukka*.

The *Gemara* observes that each *braita* contains a catchall phrase — *sukkah mikol makom* — and asks what that phrase comes to include. In the *Ganbakh* list, *sukkah mikol makom* comes to include even the *Rakbash* cases. In the *Rakbash* list, *sukkah mikol makom* comes to include even the *Ganbakh* cases. What accounts for each *tanna*'s starting point?

The *tanna* who leads with *Ganbakh* regards those cases as the more prominent or more obvious: *de-kfiei* — they tend to be relatively fixed structures. Non-Jews building *sukkot* for non-Jews, or permanent-looking shelters built for animals or Samaritans, are substantial enough that one might never have doubted their validity — and yet the *tanna* includes them, and then extends even further to include the *Rakbash* cases, which are definitively impermanent and portable.

The *tanna* who leads with *Rakbash* regards those cases differently: *alima leih Rakbash de-vnei chiyyuvah ninhu* — the *Rakbash* builders are themselves liable in *mitzvot*, they are Jewish men building shelters in the course of their labor. That is perhaps the clearer category to start with. The *chiddush* is that even the *Ganbakh* cases — structures built by or for those not obligated in the *mitzvah* — are valid.

### Conclusion

The two lists thus approach the question from opposite default assumptions, but they converge on the same *halachic* conclusion: a *sukkah* need not be built by someone obligated in the *mitzvah*, need not be built for the sake of *sukkot*, and need not even be built for Jewish occupants. The singular non-negotiable requirement is that the *schach* must have been placed there for the purpose of shade. That purpose — shade — is the essential identity of *schach*, and it alone is what determines whether the covering qualifies. Everything else is secondary.
