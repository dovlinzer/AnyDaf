# Shabbat 75 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Cooking: Food & Non-Food

### Kneading & Baking Org.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַלָּשׁ וְהָאוֹפֶה. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: שְׁבַק תַּנָּא דִידַן בִּישּׁוּל סַמְמָנִין דַּהֲוָה בְּמִשְׁכָּן וּנְקַט אוֹפֶה?! תַּנָּא דִידַן סִידּוּרָא דְפַת נָקֵט.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna, among those liable for performing primary categories of labor: **And one who kneads and one who bakes. Rav Pappa said: Our *tanna* left** out the labor of **cooking** the **spices** for dye, **which was** performed **in the Tabernacle, and included** the labor of **baking,** which was not performed in the construction of the Tabernacle. If, as stated above, all the primary categories of labor were derived from the labors in the Tabernacle, why did the *tanna* omit cooking? The Gemara answers: **Our *tanna* cited the sequence** of preparing **bread,** which was the underlying principle behind his organization of the primary categories of labor. He opened with plowing and concluded with the preparation of bread.

The daf begins on 74b, five lines from the bottom, with the words *ve-halash ve-ha'ofeh* — kneading and baking. The Gemara opens with a question posed by Pappa: why does our *tanna* ignore the *bishul simmanim* — the cooking of the various plant-based materials from which dyes were extracted — which did in fact occur in the *Mishkan*, and instead record *ofeh*, baking? The answer is *tanna didan siddura de-pas nakat*: our *tanna* is organizing the *melachot* according to the process of bread-making. As previously discussed, this may be merely an organizational principle — the conventional understanding — or it may reflect a distinct conceptual framework for understanding the *melachot*, as the *Yereim* understands it.

### Peg in Oven Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר רַב עַוִּירָא: הַאי מַאן דִּשְׁדָא סִיכְּתָא לְאַתּוּנָא — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם מְבַשֵּׁל. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא לְשָׁרוֹרֵי מָנָה קָא מִיכַּוֵין, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּמִירְפָּא רָפֵי וַהֲדַר קָמֵיט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Rav Avira said: One who places a peg into an oven** to dry **is liable for** performing the labor of **cooking.** The Gemara asks: That is **obvious.** The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that **he intends to strengthen the utensil,** as ultimately, the peg is hardened in the oven, in contrast to cooking in which the fire softens the item being cooked. Therefore, **he teaches us that** initially the wood **is softened** in the oven, **and** only **afterward it is hardened.**

Rav Aha bar Rav Avira teaches: one who throws a moist peg into an oven is liable for *bishul* — cooking. The Gemara initially asks *pshita* — is this not obvious? Yet the moment we are no longer speaking about food, nothing is self-evident with respect to *bishul*. The Gemara therefore explains the underlying concern: one might have thought the person's intention is *lishuruyei maneh* — to harden the peg, which one does by placing it in the oven and evaporating the moisture. The Gemara's response is *de-mirpa rafi* — first the peg becomes softer — *ve-hadar kamit* — and only afterward does it harden. The precise science may be uncertain, but what matters is the Gemara's conceptual point: *bishul* applies to non-foodstuffs, and the key definition it focuses on is the use of heat to induce softening. By contrast, when it comes to food, the focus is on heat altering taste — whether it renders something edible, whether it improves flavor, and related questions that are themselves subject to debate. When the object is not food, the operative definition shifts to heat-induced softening, mirroring what happens in the normal cooking process.

### Pitch Liquefying

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: הַאי מַאן דְּאַרְתַּח כּוּפְרָא — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם מְבַשֵּׁל. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא כֵּיוָן דַּהֲדַר וְאִיקִּישׁ אֵימָא לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Huna said: One who boils pitch is liable for** performing the labor of **cooking.** The Gemara asks: That is **obvious.** The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that **since it proceeds to harden** afterward, **say** that it is **not considered** cooking. One might think that since the pitch was hard before it was cooked and will ultimately be hard after it is cooked, boiling pitch is not considered cooking. Therefore, **he teaches us** that even a temporary change is considered cooking.

Rava bar Rav Huna adds: one who heats up pitch — liquefying it — is likewise liable for *bishul*. Again the Gemara asks *pshita*, and again there is a genuine concern: *keivan de-hadar ve-ikushi* — since once the pitch cools it will re-harden, one might have thought this does not qualify as cooking. The Gemara rules that it does. The question arises: is the *hava amina* rooted in the problem of *eino mitkayem* — something that does not last? When one heats water it is cooked even though it will cool, and perhaps the same logic applies here: the transformation lasts long enough to serve its function. If one wants liquefied pitch to pour onto a surface, the fact that it will later re-harden is irrelevant — a meaningful transformation has occurred in the interim. In any event, the Gemara treats this as *bishul* without hesitation.

### Cooking Non-Food Items

Two further points emerge from these cases. First, *bishul* is not limited to food. Second, it is not limited to *gidulei karka* — things that grow from the ground. Some other *melachot* are so limited, but one may certainly cook meat and be liable. Pitch, which derives from some petroleum-like substance, is also included. The heat-induced transformation of an object is the core definition of *bishul*, irrespective of what category of object is involved.

## Multiple Melachot Examples

### Barrel & Oven

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הַאי מַאן דַּעֲבַד חָבִיתָא — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם שֶׁבַע חַטָּאוֹת. תַּנּוּרָא — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם שְׁמוֹנֶה חַטָּאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: One who** unwittingly **crafted an** earthenware **barrel** on Shabbat **is liable to** bring **seven sin-offerings:** He crumbles the lumps of dirt; which is (1) grinding; (2) selects the stones from the dirt; (3) kneads the mortar; (4) cuts the mortar into pieces of a suitable size; (5) builds the mold; (6) kindles the fire, and then fires the earthenware vessel, which is (7) baking (*ge'onim*). One who crafts **an oven is liable for eight sin-offerings,** since in addition to those seven labors, he spreads another layer of mortar to finish the job, performing the prohibited labor of (8) smoothing.

With the discussion of *bishul* essentially concluded, the Gemara moves into a series of cases that function almost as puzzles: how many distinct *melachot* can one transgress within a single production process? Rava teaches: one who makes a pottery barrel — *chavita* — is liable for seven *sin offerings*, having transgressed seven distinct *melachot*. One who makes an oven — *tanura* — which is similar pottery but is subsequently glazed and finished, is liable for eight. The Gemara does not enumerate the categories itself; for that one must consult Rashi.

### Rashi's Barrel List

Rashi's enumeration of the seven *melachot* in making a pottery barrel runs approximately seven lines in the standard text. First, grinding up the earth to be used for the pottery — this is *tochen*. One might wonder whether pulverizing clay is truly comparable to the paradigmatic *tochen* of grinding wheat into fine powder, or whether breaking up earth into small pieces is categorically different. Yet Rashi elsewhere rules that mincing beets constitutes *tochen*, so grinding dirt follows. Second, selecting out the stones from the earth — *borer*, selecting. Third, sifting the earth through a sieve — one wants clean, fine material for the plaster or pottery. Fourth, mixing the earth with water to create a moldable clay — this is *losh*, kneading, analogous to adding water to flour to create a homogeneous mass. Fifth, smoothing over the surface — *memareiach*, smoothing, which in the leather context refers to spreading a substance evenly. Sixth, making the fire — *mavir*. Seventh, placing it in the fire — *mevashel*, cooking. One might note a tension: the Gemara's earlier discussion emphasized that *mevashel* requires the object to first become soft, yet fired pottery only hardens. Rashi does not pause to address this. Note also that Rashi does not count digging the hole from which the dirt was extracted, because that constitutes *melacha she'einah tzerichah legufah* — a destructive act whose byproduct alone is desired. Others offer variant lists.

### Makeh BeFatish

The additional eighth *melacha* for the oven is explained by Rashi as follows: after removing the vessel from the kiln, one applies a plaster coating. Now *memareiach* — smoothing — has already been counted. Rashi therefore classifies this final application as *makeh b'fatish* — the last finishing act performed actively and deliberately. A crucial distinction emerges: when an object is merely passively completed by being left in the oven, that passive completion does not constitute *makeh b'fatish*. Only an active, deliberate final act — such as manually applying plaster to the finished oven — qualifies. This raises a general question about *makeh b'fatish b'ochlin*: if *makeh b'fatish* is the generic last act of completion, would finishing a food item also trigger it? The question is noted but not here resolved.

### Boneh & Vessels

There is a noteworthy dispute between Rashi and Tosafot — joined by the *Geonim* — about whether one of the acts in this process should be counted as *boneh*, building. Rashi holds *ein binyan bikeilim* — there is no category of building applied to vessels. Tosafot disagrees, arguing that *ein binyan bikeilim* is not an absolute principle. It means that reassembling a disassembled vessel does not constitute *binyan*, but creating a vessel from raw materials entirely from scratch does constitute *boneh* and the prohibition applies even to vessels. This disagreement has practical *halachic* implications that recur later in the tractate.

### Basket: 11 & 13

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַאי מַאן דַּעֲבַד חַלְּתָא — חַיָּיב אַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה חַטָּאוֹת. וְאִי חַיְּיטֵיהּ לְפוּמֵּיהּ — חַיָּיב שְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה חַטָּאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: One who** unwittingly **crafts a receptacle** from reeds on Shabbat **is liable to** bring **eleven sin-offerings.** In pruning the reeds, he performed both (1) reaping and (2) planting, as he stimulates growth of the remaining reeds. He (3) gathers the reeds; (4) selects them; (5) smooths and levels them; cuts them into small pieces, which is (6) grinding; and (7) cuts them to a particular measurement. When he begins weaving the reeds, he performs the labors of (8) stretching the warp; (9) constructing two meshes; and (10) weaving. Crafting the object as a whole constitutes (11) building (*ge'onim*). **And if he sews the mouth** of the receptacle, **he is liable to** bring **thirteen sin-offerings** with the added labors of (12) sewing and (13) tying.

Abaye then extends the exercise: one who makes a reed wicker basket — *chalsa* — is liable for eleven *sin offerings*. If one adds a lip around its opening, the total rises to thirteen. The question, as before, is how to enumerate the *melachot* involved.

### Rashi Basket Breakdown

Rashi's analysis of the eleven *melachot* in basket-making proceeds as follows. First, one prunes branches from a tree. Rashi notes that since one also intends to prune the tree — benefiting it — this yields two *melachot* simultaneously: pruning as a form of planting (*zore'a*), and uprooting what is detached from its place of growth — *kotzer*. That is two. Third, gathering the fallen branches into a pile — *me'ameir*, gathering. Fourth, selecting the good ones — *borer*. Fifth, smoothing the sticks to be used — *memachek*. That is five. Sixth, Rashi says one breaks the sticks into two or three pieces — and classifies this as *tochen*. This is admittedly striking, since *tochen* conventionally involves reducing something to tiny particles, and breaking sticks into a few pieces does not obviously fit that description. Rashi's precise reasoning here is not fully elaborated, and this classification does not appear to play a major role in the later *poskim*'s treatment of the parameters of *tochen*. Seventh, cutting the sticks to particular sizes — *mechatech*, cutting to a precise measure. That is seven. Then come three acts associated with weaving on a loom: setting up the frame — *meisach*; weaving — *oreig*; and the third loom-related act as well. That yields ten. Finally, trimming the edges upon completion — *makeh b'fatish*. That is eleven.

The additional two *melachot* that bring the total to thirteen when one adds the lip are *tofer* — sewing — and *kosher* — knotting, since the lip requires some stitching and knotting to attach. The interesting *chidush* in Rashi's list, as noted, is the application of *tochen* to the breaking of sticks — a classification that is difficult to square with the standard parameters of that *melacha*, and one that the *rishonim* do not appear to have leveraged significantly in their practical discussions.

## Wool from Live Animal

### Three Offerings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַטּוֹוֶה צֶמֶר שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי בְּהֵמָה בְּשַׁבָּת — חַיָּיב שָׁלֹשׁ חַטָּאוֹת: אַחַת מִשּׁוּם גּוֹזֵז, וְאַחַת מִשּׁוּם מְנַפֵּץ, וְאַחַת מִשּׁוּם טוֹוֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One who** unwittingly **spins wool** still attached **on the back of** an **animal on Shabbat is liable to** bring **three sin-offerings: One due to shearing,** since, in the process, some of the wool is detached from the sheep; **and one due to combing** the wool; **and one due to spinning.**

Rabbah bar bar Chana cites Rabbi Yochanan: one who spins wool directly off the back of a live sheep on *Shabbat* — a technique in which the animal is brought indoors and the wool is spun directly from it without prior shearing — is liable for three *sin offerings*: one for *gozez* (shearing), one for *menapeitz* (combing, presumably accomplished through the pulling and twisting involved in the process), and one for *toveh* (spinning). Even though all three acts are performed as a single continuous process, each constitutes a distinct *melacha*.

### Rav Kahana: No Derech

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר: אֵין דֶּרֶךְ גְּזִיזָה בְּכָךְ, וְאֵין דֶּרֶךְ מְנַפֵּץ בְּכָךְ, וְאֵין דֶּרֶךְ טְוִויָּה בְּכָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said: This is not** a typical **manner of shearing, and this is not** a typical **manner of combing, and this is not** a typical **manner of spinning.**

Rav Kahana disagrees: *ein derech gezizah b'kach v'ein derech menapeitz b'kach v'ein derech tveiyah b'kach* — this is not the normal way of shearing, not the normal way of combing, and not the normal way of spinning. Accordingly, one is exempt from all three. This reflects the principle that liability attaches only when an act is performed in its normal manner — *derech* — and performing all three in a single unconventional process disqualifies each of them.

### Wise Women's Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה: שָׁטוּף בָּעִזִּים, וְטָווּי בָּעִזִּים, אַלְמָא טְוִויָּה עַל גַּבֵּי בְּהֵמָה שְׁמָהּ טְוִויָּה!
>
> **Translation:** **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* **in the name** of **Rabbi Neḥemya** that the verse in the context of the work of the Tabernacle: "And all the women whose hearts lifted them with wisdom spun the goats" (Exodus 35:26) means that **they washed** the hair **on the goats, and they spun** it into threads **on the goats** themselves without first shearing the hair? **Apparently, spinning on the back of an animal is considered** a typical manner of **spinning.**

The Gemara challenges Rav Kahana from a *baraita* cited in the name of Rabbi Nechemia. The verse states *ve-chol ishah chachmat lev beyadeiha tavu* — all wise-hearted women spun with their hands. Rabbi Nechemia explains what made them so remarkable: they drove the goats into the water to wash the wool, then spun the fiber directly off the goats. *Alma tveiyah gabei behemah shemah tveiyah* — we see that spinning directly from a live animal is called spinning.

### Wisdom Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָכְמָה יְתֵירָה שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Extraordinary wisdom is different.** Although certain individuals are capable of spinning wool that way, the typical person is not capable of performing that feat.

The Gemara resolves the challenge: *chochma yesirah shani* — that case involved exceptional wisdom, a special technique that only particularly skilled women could execute. For ordinary people, spinning directly from a live animal is not the normal manner of performing any of these three *melachot*. One might ask whether those exceptional women themselves would be liable — and perhaps for them, since this constitutes their normal *derech*, the liability would apply. Tosafot addresses this: even if one is personally capable of performing the act in this unusual way, *batlah da'ato eitzel kol adam* — one's personal practice is nullified against the standard practice of people generally, and so it is still not considered a normal manner of performance. A further point, raised by a student and worth noting, is that even though we derive the *melachot* from the *Mishkan*, the parameters of what constitutes a normal *derech* are applied as general standards — not simply based on what was done in the *Mishkan* itself.

## Feather Processing

### Three-Part Process

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַתּוֹלֵשׁ אֶת הַכָּנָף וְהַקּוֹטְמוֹ וְהַמּוֹרְטוֹ — חַיָּיב שָׁלֹשׁ חַטָּאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *Tosefta*: **One who** unwittingly **plucks** a large feather from **the wing** of a bird on Shabbat, **and who snips** the tip of the feather, **and who pulls out** the thin threads that comprise the feather **is liable to** bring **three sin-offerings.**

The Gemara introduces a *baraita*: one who plucks a feather from a bird, snips it, and strips the feathers from the quill is liable for three *sin offerings*. The question of whether the bird must be alive is an interesting one — *gezizah* involves cutting something off a living creature, and it is debatable whether a dead animal would be included. Rashi clarifies that *kanaf* here refers to the feathers of the wing.

### Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: תּוֹלֵשׁ — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם גּוֹזֵז, קוֹטֵם — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם מְחַתֵּךְ, מְמָרֵט — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם מְמַחֵק.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said** in explanation: One who **plucks** the wing **is liable due to** the labor of **shearing.** One who **snips** the tip of the feather **is liable due to cutting.** And one who **pulls out** the threads **is liable due to smoothing.**

Reish Lakish identifies the three categories. Plucking the feather — *tolesh* — is liable because of *gozez*, shearing. This is not a significant novelty, since shearing refers to cutting something from a living creature for the sake of the detached material. Snipping the tip — *kotem* — is liable because of *mechatech*, cutting to a precise size. *Mechatech* requires that one care about producing a specific size or shape. Rashi explains that one is not merely cutting off the sharp lower tip; rather, one is cutting off the upper tip of the feather, which can be used for down — but only a specific portion of the tip yields usable material. One therefore cuts at a precise location to obtain a precise quantity. Rashi alternatively suggests that since the feather will ultimately serve as a quill, the cut is made at a particular point to produce a usable writing instrument of the right dimensions. Either way, this is *mechatech*. Stripping the feathers from the quill — *memaret* — is liable because of *memachek*, smoothing.

### Memachek Parameters

This last ruling is striking and important. Smoothing in its primary context — leather-making — involves a desire that the smoothed surface be smooth. Here, however, one stripping feathers from a quill has no particular interest in the smoothness of the result; one simply wants to remove the feathers. And yet the Gemara rules it is *memachek*. Unless, that is, one intends to use the quill as a writing instrument, in which case smoothness of the barrel is indeed desired. This ambiguity goes to the heart of *memachek*: must one specifically desire a smooth result, or does the act of smoothing itself suffice regardless of intent? This has significant practical consequences. If one applies a poultice or salve to a wound, spreading it flat across the surface, one does not necessarily want the salve itself to form a smooth layer — one merely wants to apply it. Nevertheless, as we will see, the Gemara rules such spreading constitutes *memachek*. One might ask whether two *melachot* can be violated simultaneously: if shaving, for example, is *gozez* because one cuts from a living body, might it simultaneously be *memachek* because one desires the skin smooth? This is indeed analogous to the present case, where stripping feathers is both an act of removal and an act of smoothing.

### Modern Applications

These questions bear directly on contemporary practice. *Sicha* — applying oil to the skin — is prohibited on *Yom Kippur* but not on *Shabbat*; oil of standard viscosity applied to the skin raises no *Shabbat* concern. The question arises regarding thicker substances: if a cream or ointment thicker than oil is spread smooth across the skin, does that constitute *memachek*? The practical guidance that follows from this is that one should dab such substances on rather than spread them smooth — applying the cream in multiple spots and allowing movement or pressure to distribute it, rather than deliberately smoothing it into an even layer. The same considerations arise with liquid soap — whose viscosity is arguably no greater than oil, and which one does not intend to leave as a thin smooth coating — and with toothpaste, where the question is whether one is genuinely trying to create a smooth spread or merely applying an agent to all surfaces. These remain live questions in *memachek*'s parameters.

## Knotting & Unknottin (II)

### Tent Stakes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְשִׁירָה בַּמִּשְׁכָּן הֵיכָא הֲוַאי? אָמַר רָבָא: שֶׁכֵּן קוֹשְׁרִין בְּיִתְדוֹת אֹהָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Where was** there **tying in the Tabernacle? Rava said: They tied** the **tents** of the Tabernacle **to the pegs.**

The Gemara turns to the *melachot* of *kosher* (knotting) and *matir* (unknotting), which will be treated in detail in a later chapter. Here it poses a preliminary question: where did knotting occur in the *Mishkan*? Rava answers: *shekein koshrim biyatdot ohalim* — they would tie down the curtains of the *Mishkan* to the tent stakes. Rashi reads this as referring to *yesodot* — the stakes to which the *yeriyot*, the surrounding curtains of the *Chatzer Ohel Moed*, were fastened.

### Temporary Knot Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קוֹשְׁרִים?! הָהוּא קוֹשֵׁר עַל מְנָת לְהַתִּיר הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** And is that considered performance of the labor of **tying? That was tying** a knot **in order to untie** it. When the children of Israel departed from an encampment, they dismantled the Tabernacle, which involved untying all of the knots. One is not liable for tying a temporary knot on Shabbat.

The Gemara objects: those knots were tied *al m'nat lehatir* — for the purpose of being undone, since the *Mishkan* was periodically disassembled when the camp traveled. This is a significant conceptual point. In other contexts, an object that eventually returns to its prior state — such as liquefied pitch that re-hardens — is not disqualified as non-lasting. Yet the Gemara treats this knot as categorically *al m'nat lehatir*. This suggests that the concept of permanence as it applies to *kesher* (knotting) may be stricter than *eino mitkayem* as applied to other *melachot*. A knot tied even for an indefinitely long period — such as for the entire duration of the encampment, which could last fifty years — might still be considered temporary if there is awareness that it will ultimately be untied. The Gemara elsewhere does note that since the Israelites were entirely dependent on divine instruction, they were prepared to remain in place indefinitely, which would confer permanence. That argument would have worked here as well, yet the Gemara presses to find a different example — suggesting it is interested in locating a case of permanent knotting on its own terms.

### Broken String Knots

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שֶׁכֵּן אוֹרְגֵי יְרִיעוֹת שֶׁנִּפְסְקָה לָהֶן נִימָא קוֹשְׁרִים אוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said: As the weavers of curtains** for the Tabernacle, **when a thread would rip, they would tie it.**

Abaye proposes: *shekein orgei yeriyot* — those who wove the *Mishkan* curtains. *Shenifska lahem nima* — if a thread broke during weaving, they would knot it together. This supplies an example of permanent knotting in the *Mishkan* context. Rava then asks: fine, now we have *kosher* — but what about *matir*, unknotting? If two broken threads each had to be knotted, and the two knots wound up directly adjacent to one another in the weave, the fabric would look flawed. One solution would be to undo one of the knots and re-knot differently. But the Gemara rejects this on aesthetic grounds: *hashta lifnei melech basar vadam ein osin kein* — before an earthly king one would not leave such an obvious flaw in fine work; *lifnei melech malchei hamlachim* — before the King of Kings, certainly not. Rashi suggests that in practice they would simply pull the thread out entirely and restart, rather than leave adjacent knots. The Gemara accordingly dismisses this as a source for *matir*.

### Snail Traps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי עִילַּאי, שֶׁכֵּן צָדֵי חִלָּזוֹן קוֹשְׁרִין וּמַתִּירִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said, and some say** that **Rav Elai** said: **The trappers of *ḥilazon*,** whose blood was used in the Tabernacle as a dye, **tie and untie** their nets.

Rava — or according to some, Rav Ilai — proposes the final answer: *shekein tzedei chilazon koshri'in umatirin* — those who set nets to trap the *chilazon* snails, from whose blood the *techelet* dye was produced, would regularly knot and unknot the nets in the course of adjusting them. This supplies both *kosher* and *matir* as genuine *Mishkan*-connected activities. The Gemara is thus willing to trace a *melacha* through a chain of processes — trapping snails, extracting dye, producing the blue thread — several stages removed from the final product. One might also observe that the snails were found by the sea while the *Mishkan* was in the desert, though perhaps this activity occurred before the Israelites departed, near the *Yam Suf*. The deeper point is that the Gemara is prepared to count a *melacha* rooted in an imperfect, problem-contingent part of the process — not only the ideal, flawless execution. There may even be a *mussar* dimension to this: the Torah acknowledges that even sacred work does not always go perfectly, and the responses to those imperfections are themselves part of the process.

### Knot for Permanence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַתּוֹפֵר שְׁתֵּי תְּפִירוֹת. וְהָא לָא קָיְימָא! אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְהוּא שֶׁקְּשָׁרָן.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna, among those liable for performing primary categories of labor: **And one who sews two stitches.** The Gemara asks: **That does not endure;** two stitches will unravel immediately. A prohibited labor whose result is temporary is not considered a prohibited labor. **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: That** has the legal status of a prohibited labor only in a case **where,** after sewing the stitches, **he tied them.** He tied a knot at each of the two ends of the thread so that the stitches would not unravel.

The Gemara then addresses *tofer* — sewing — in this context: *vehatofer shtei tefirot*, making two stitches. The Gemara asks: *vahalo kayma* — two stitches alone will just pull out; they are *eino mitkayem*, not lasting. Rabbah bar bar Chana cites Rabbi Yochanan: *vehu she-ksharan* — the ruling applies where one tied a knot at the end of the stitches, preventing them from pulling loose. One might ask whether that terminal knot itself constitutes an act of *kosher*. Perhaps it does; perhaps it depends on whether it is a double, professional knot. In any case, one is liable for *tofer* by virtue of the stitches, which the terminal knot renders permanent. This raises a further question: is the *melacha* of stitching dependent on an act performed after the stitching itself? Until the knot is tied, the stitches are *eino mitkayem*. One might argue that an act performed subsequently to the *melacha* can retroactively render the prior act a completed *melacha* — an interesting principle in its own right.

## Tearing for Sewing

### Moth Hole Repair

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַקּוֹרֵעַ עַל מְנָת לִתְפּוֹר. קְרִיעָה בַּמִּשְׁכָּן מִי הֲוָה? רַבָּה וְרַבִּי זֵירָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: שֶׁכֵּן יְרִיעָה שֶׁנָּפַל בָּהּ דַּרְנָא, קוֹרְעִין בָּהּ וְתוֹפְרִין אוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna, among those liable for performing primary categories of labor: **One who tears in order to sew two stitches.** The Gemara asks: **Was there tearing in** the construction of **the Tabernacle?** The Gemara answers that it was **Rabba and Rabbi Zeira who both said** the following explanation: **As, when a curtain had a worm** which made a tear **in it, they would tear** the curtain further to lengthen the tear, **and** that enabled them to then **sew it** in a manner that obscured the tear.

The Gemara asks where *keria* — tearing — occurred in the *Mishkan*, given the general principle that destruction is only a *melacha* when performed for a constructive purpose. Rabba bar Rav Zeira and Rav Zeira — both of them — answer: *shekein yeriyah shenafal bah darna* — a curtain of the *Mishkan* into which a moth hole had fallen. One would tear away the damaged area around the hole and then resew the gap with a patch in its place. This again illustrates the Gemara's recurring pattern of locating *melachot* in the corrective responses to imperfection within the *Mishkan* process.

## Tightening Threads

### Tightening Threads

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זוּטְרָא בַּר טוֹבִיָּה אָמַר רַב: הַמּוֹתֵחַ חוּט שֶׁל תְּפִירָה בְּשַׁבָּת — חַיָּיב חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zutra bar Toviya said** that **Rav said: One who tightens the thread of a stitch on Shabbat is liable to** bring **a sin-offering.** If two parts of a garment that were sewn together begin to separate, and one pulls the thread to reattach them, it is tantamount to having sewn them.

Rav Zutra bar Tuvia cites Rav: one who stretches and tightens a loose thread of existing stitching on *Shabbat* — *hamoteiach chut shel tefira beShabbat* — is liable for *chatas*. One did not perform the original sewing; one simply found a thread that had become slack and pulled it taut, re-securing the attachment. A practical instance: tightening the knots of one's *tzitzit*, or pulling taut a thread by which a button is loosely attached. In both cases, no new stitches are made, yet the act of tightening constitutes *tofer*. The ruling would presumably apply only where the tightening produces a result that is *kiyama* — lasting — for a meaningful period. If the thread immediately loosens again, it may be *eino mitkayem* and the liability would not apply. But where the act does secure the thread for a significant duration, even without new stitching, it is a form of *tefira*.

One might ask more broadly whether *tefira* is confined to stitching or encompasses other methods of joining two pieces together — gluing, for example. Whether gluing would constitute *tofer* or perhaps *kosher* is a question that the Gemara here leaves open.

At this point the Gemara inserts two additional statements attributed to Rav Zutra bar Tuvia in the name of Rav that are not directly relevant to the discussion of *Shabbat melachot*. One concerns the prohibition against learning even a single matter from a *magush* — which Rav identifies as a blasphemer or idolator rather than a practitioner of witchcraft, as proven by the fact that one is permitted to study witchcraft *lidrosha* (for the sake of understanding and teaching), whereas Rav's blanket prohibition would be inexplicable were the *magush* merely a witch. The second concerns the requirement to calculate *tekufot u'mazalot* — the astronomical seasons and constellations — and the condemnation of one who possesses the knowledge to do so but neglects to do it. Rabbi Yochanan derives from the verse *ki hi chochmaschem u'vinaschem le'einei ha'amim* that computing *tekufot u'mazalot* is a positive obligation — the knowledge that even the nations will recognize as genuine wisdom — and Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani adds that it leads to the appreciation of divine creation. The *Smag* codifies this as *halacha*, and the *Rambam*, while not specifically emphasizing *tekufot u'mazalot*, does include the study of natural sciences under the *mitzvah* of *ahavat Hashem*, since contemplating the wisdom of creation leads to both love and awe of God.

## Chilazon/Snail Trapping

### Trapping & Squeezing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַצָּד חִלָּזוֹן וְהַפּוֹצְעוֹ — אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם. שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: פְּצִיעָה בִּכְלַל דִּישָׁה. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵין פְּצִיעָה בִּכְלַל דִּישָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *Tosefta*: **One who traps a *ḥilazon* and breaks its** shell to remove its blood for the dye **is liable to** bring **only one** sin-offering. He is not liable for breaking the shell. **Rabbi Yehuda says: He is liable to** bring **two,** for performing the prohibited labors of trapping and for threshing, **as Rabbi Yehuda would say: The breaking of a *ḥilazon* is included** in the primary category of **threshing,** as its objective is to extract the matter that he desires from the shell that he does not. The Rabbis **said to him: Breaking** the shell **is not included** in the primary category of **threshing.**

The Gemara cites a *baraita*: one who traps the *chilazon* snail and squeezes its blood — *hatzad chilazon vehapotzo* — is liable for only one *sin offering*, for *tzeida* (trapping). Rabbi Yehuda says: two, because *ptzi'ah bichlal disha* — the squeezing is included in the category of *disha*, threshing, which in this context means extracting something from its natural container. The Rabbis respond: *ein ptzi'ah bichlal disha* — squeezing blood from a snail is not *disha*.

### Disha Limited

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן — קָסָבְרִי אֵין דִּישָׁה אֶלָּא לְגִדּוּלֵי קַרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: What is the rationale** for the opinion **of the Rabbis? They hold: Threshing** applies **only to** produce **that grows** from **the ground.**

Rava explains the Rabbis' position: *kasavri ein disha ela b'gidulei karka* — *disha* applies only to agricultural products, things that grow from the ground. A sea creature such as the *chilazon* is categorically excluded.

### Mefarek Question

This ruling carries significant implications. The Gemara elsewhere categorizes milking as *mefarek* — extracting. Some authorities argue that if *disha* is limited to *gidulei karka*, then milking — which involves a land animal rather than an agricultural product — would be only *d'rabbanan* on a biblical level. Others maintain that milking is an exception or that land animals, which subsist on and are rooted in the earth, might be included within a broader conception of *gidulei karka*. Tosafot raises this possibility but rejects it. A third, counterintuitive position found in some *rishonim* and *poskim* holds that the limitations on the *avot* do not necessarily apply to the *toldot* — so that even if *disha* is restricted to *gidulei karka*, the *tolada* of *mefarek* might not carry the same restriction. This is an admittedly strange position, since one would expect a *tolada* to be at least as limited as its *av*, but it is noted. The practical stakes are high: the question of whether a woman expressing milk on *Shabbat* is a *d'oraita* or *d'rabbanan* concern turns in part on this very issue.

The *Yerushalmi*, as cited by Tosafot, raises an additional question: whether trapping snails constitutes *tzeida* at all. If the *chilazon* is a snail — as is now generally accepted — it does not flee. One goes and scoops them up. Does locating and extracting an immobile creature constitute *tzad*? This is a genuine question in the parameters of trapping that the *Yerushalmi* leaves open.

### Live Squeezing Preferred

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיחַיַּיב נָמֵי מִשּׁוּם נְטִילַת נְשָׁמָה! אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֶׁפְּצָעוֹ מֵת. רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא שֶׁפְּצָעוֹ חַי, מִתְעַסֵּק הוּא אֵצֶל נְטִילַת נְשָׁמָה.
>
> **Translation:** Even if extracting blood is not considered threshing, **let him be liable for taking a life as well. Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** This is referring to a case **where he broke its** shell after it was **dead. Rava said: Even** if you **say that he broke it** when it was **alive,** he is exempt. Since he had no intention of killing the *ḥilazon*, he is considered as one who **is acting unawares with regard to taking a life.**

Rava raises a further question: why is one not also liable for *netilat neshama* — taking a life — when squeezing the snail? Rabbi Yochanan answers: *shepetza'o meit* — it was already dead when squeezed. Rava counters: even if it was alive, *mit'asek hu eitzel netilat neshama* — the killing is incidental to the purpose of the act. Tosafot notes that *mit'asek* is not the precisely correct term here; what is meant is *davar she'eino mitkavein* — an unintended consequence of an otherwise permitted act. Yet Abaye and Rava have jointly established the principle *modeh Rabbi Shimon b'psik reisha v'lo yamut* — even Rabbi Shimon, who permits unintended acts, concedes liability when the forbidden result is inevitable, as when one cuts off an animal's head and claims not to intend its death. Squeezing a snail until its blood is fully extracted would seem equally inevitable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַויְיהוּ: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בִּ״פְסִיק רֵישֵׁיהּ וְלֹא יָמוּת״! שָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דְּכַמָּה דְּאִית בֵּיהּ נְשָׁמָה טְפֵי נִיחָא לֵיהּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלֵיצִיל צִיבְעֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Didn't Abaye and Rava both say** that **Rabbi Shimon,** who rules that an unintentional act is permitted, **agrees** that **in** a case of: **Cut off its head and will it not die,** one is liable? One who performs an action that will inevitably result in a prohibited labor cannot claim that he did not intend for his action to lead to that result. Lack of intention is only a valid claim when the result is merely possible, not inevitable. Since one who extracts blood from a *ḥilazon* inevitably takes its life, how can Rava claim that his action is unintentional? The Gemara answers: **Here it is different, as the longer** the *ḥilazon* **lives, the better** it is **for** the trapper, **so that its dye will become clear.** Dye extracted from a live *ḥilazon* is a higher quality than that which is extracted from a dead one. Rabbi Shimon agrees that one who performs an action with inevitable consequences is liable only in a case where the consequences are not contrary to his interests. Since he prefers that the *ḥilazon* remain alive as long as possible, he is not liable for the inevitable consequences.

The Gemara resolves the difficulty: *d'kama d'it beih neshama tefei nicha leih* — as long as the snail remains alive, one prefers that it stay alive, *ki heichi d'litzil tziv'eih* — so that the color of the blood will be clearer and of higher quality. It emerges that the blood extracted from a live snail is superior to that taken from a dead one. One therefore actively does not want the snail to die during the process. This converts the situation into a *psik reisha d'lo nicha lei* — an unavoidable consequence that the actor specifically does not desire — and the ruling is that one is exempt in such a case.

### Psik Reisha Parameters

This ruling occasions a notable dispute between Rashi and Tosafot. Rashi holds that the exemption of *psik reisha d'lo nicha lei* applies only when one actively does not want the result. If one is merely indifferent — *lo ichpat lei* — one is still liable. Tosafot vigorously disagrees, marshaling evidence from other *sugyot* to argue that indifference and active non-desire are legally equivalent. The *halacha* follows Tosafot: once one is indifferent to the unintended result, one does not incur biblical liability, regardless of whether one affirmatively does not want it or simply does not care. The three positions — actively desiring the result, being indifferent, and specifically not wanting it — thus collapse into two for practical *halachic* purposes: one either desires the outcome or one does not, and absence of desire (whether through indifference or active preference against) suffices for exemption in a *psik reisha* context.

## Slaughtering Analysis

### Rava vs. Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשּׁוֹחֲטוֹ. שׁוֹחֵט מִשּׁוּם מַאי חַיָּיב? רַב אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם צוֹבֵעַ. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם נְטִילַת נְשָׁמָה.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna, among those liable for performing primary categories of labor: **And one who slaughters** an animal on Shabbat. The Gemara asks: As there was no slaughter necessary for construction of the Tabernacle, **one who slaughters** an animal, **due to what** prohibited labor is he **liable? Rav said:** He is liable **due to dyeing,** as in the course of the slaughter the hide is dyed with blood. **And Shmuel said:** He is liable **due to taking a life.**

The Gemara proceeds to *shechita* — slaughtering. The question posed is not about whether *shechita* is a *melacha* (the *Mishnah* plainly lists it), but rather what conceptual category underlies the liability: *shochet mishum mai chayav* — for which *melacha* is one liable when slaughtering? Rava says: *mishum tzovei'a* — for coloring, since the blood colors the skin of the animal's neck at the point of slaughter. Shmuel says: *mishum netilat neshama* — for taking life, which is the natural category for slaughter.

### Staining for Commerce

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם צוֹבֵעַ אִין, מִשּׁוּם נְטִילַת נְשָׁמָה לָא? אֵימָא: אַף מִשּׁוּם צוֹבֵעַ. אָמַר רַב: מִילְּתָא דַּאֲמַרִי, אֵימָא בַּהּ מִילְּתָא, דְּלָא לֵיתוּ דָּרֵי בָּתְרָאֵי וְלִיחֲכוּ עֲלַי: צוֹבֵעַ בְּמַאי נִיחָא לֵיהּ — נִיחָא דְּלִיתַּוַּוס בֵּית הַשְּׁחִיטָה דְּמָא, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִיחְזוּהּ אִינָשֵׁי וְלֵיתוּ לִיזְבְּנוּ מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara wonders: Is that to say according to Rav, that **due to dyeing, yes,** he is liable; **due to taking a life, no,** he is exempt? Rather, emend Rav's statement and **say:** He is liable **due to dyeing as well.** And **Rav said: I will say something** as an explanation **with regard to the statement I said,** so that **later generations will not come and laugh at me: In what** sense **is dyeing** a **desired** consequence **for him? It is desired that the area of the slaughter will be inundated** with **blood, so that people will see it** freshly dyed **and come to purchase** fresh meat **from him.** Therefore, the one slaughtering the animal also wants its neck dyed.

The Gemara initially appears to press Rava: according to you, there is liability for *tzovei'a* — but no liability for *netilat neshama*? Of course there is liability for taking life as well. Rava clarifies: one incurs a *double chiyuv* — both *netilat neshama* and *tzovei'a*. But Rava feels compelled to explain himself lest future generations mock the *tzovei'a* claim as absurd. *B'mai nicha lei* — why would one actually want the coloring? The answer is *nicha lei d'litnef beit hash'chita dama* — one wants the site of the slaughter to be stained deeply with blood, *ki heichi d'lichazei inshi v'leitu v'lizbnu mineih* — so that passersby will see the fresh blood and know the meat was recently slaughtered, thereby attracting customers. This commercial desirability of the bloodstained neck demonstrates that the coloring is actively desired, making it a direct act of *tzovei'a* with full *kavanah*.

### Rabbeinu Tam

Tosafot raises the question of what constitutes *netilat neshama* when one wounds without killing — as the Gemara implies one is liable for wounding as well. One possibility is that injury is a stage on the path toward death. Rabbeinu Tam advances his famous position: the common denominator of all the cases in which liability arises for *netilat neshama* is the extraction of blood. *Ki hadam hu hanefesh* — the blood represents the soul, and therefore taking blood is a form of taking life. This is why wounding — where blood flows — also falls under *netilat neshama*, while killing without bloodshed, though certainly *netilat neshama* in a plain sense, lacks this additional dimension. On Rabbeinu Tam's reading there are thus effectively two sub-categories: killing an animal outright, and extracting blood through wounding. This framework also bears on the question of why squeezing the *chilazon* does not generate liability for *netilat neshama* — a question Tosafot addresses at some length.

## Tanning & Salting

### Salting & Working

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַמּוֹלְחוֹ וְהַמְעַבְּדוֹ. הַיְינוּ מוֹלְחַ וְהַיְינוּ מְעַבֵּד!
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna, among those liable for performing primary categories of labor: **And one who salts it and one who tans it.** The Gemara asks: The prohibited labor of **salting is the same as** the prohibited labor of **tanning,** i.e., salting is a stage in the tanning process.

The Gemara asks: the *Mishnah* lists both *moleach* (salting) and *me'aved* (working/processing) as separate *melachot*, yet salting is simply part of the process of working leather. Why enumerate them separately?

### Sartat Added

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: אַפֵּיק חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ וְעַיֵּיל שִׂירְטוּט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish both said: Remove one of them and replace** it with **drafting.**

Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish both answer: *afik chad mineihu* — remove one of those two, *ve-ayyel sirtut* — and insert *sirtut*, line-making, which was absent from the list. The process of making leather or other craftwork includes scoring lines as a guide for cutting or writing — *sirtut* — and this had been omitted. Adding it and removing the redundant *moleach* preserves the total at thirty-nine. This exchange reveals something important: the count of thirty-nine is still, at this stage, somewhat fluid. The categories are still crystallizing, and the Rabbis are prepared to reconfigure the list to arrive at the correct total.

### Salting Meat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: הַאי מַאן דְּמָלַח בִּישְׂרָא — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם מְעַבֵּד. רָבָא אָמַר: אֵין עִיבּוּד בָּאוֹכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Huna said: One who salts meat** on Shabbat to preserve it **is liable due to** the labor of **tanning. Rava said: There is no tanning** with regard to **food.**

Since *moleach* has been absorbed into *me'aved*, one might think that salting only generates liability when applied to leather or similar materials undergoing processing. The Gemara immediately applies *me'aved* to food. Rava bar Rav Huna rules: *hai man demalach bisra chayav mishum me'aved* — one who salts meat is liable for *me'aved*. Rava, however, contests this: *ein ibud be'ochlin* — there is no *ibud* (working/processing) when it comes to foods. *Ibud* is a category that applies to materials being transformed — leather, wood, and similar objects. Food is food; applying *me'aved* to foodstuffs is fundamentally excluded. This parallels another exclusion: *tzovei'a* similarly does not apply to foods in many opinions.

### Transformative Salting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: וַאֲפִילּוּ רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא לָא אָמַר אֶלָּא דְּקָא בָּעֵי לֵיהּ לְאוֹרְחָא, אֲבָל לְבֵיתָא — לָא מְשַׁוֵּי אִינִישׁ מֵיכְלֵיהּ עֵץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: And even Rabba bar Rav Huna said** it falls into the category of tanning **only when he needs to** pack the meat for **a trip** and salts it thoroughly. **However, to** eat in **the house, a person does not render his food** inedible, tantamount to a piece of **wood.** In that case, he certainly would not salt the meat to a degree that would approximate tanning.

Rav Ashi qualifies the position of Rava bar Rav Huna: even that ruling was only intended for a case *deka'ba'i lei le'orcha* — where one is salting for a long journey, making something akin to preserved dried meat or beef jerky. In that case, one genuinely transforms the food — extracting all moisture, hardening it, rendering it something fundamentally different from what it was — analogous to what the processing of leather achieves. That is the kind of transformation that *me'aved* is meant to capture. *Aval leveisa* — but if one is salting for home use a few days hence, *lo matzi inish demashvei michlei etz* — people do not turn their food into wood. Home salting is not sufficiently transformative to qualify. The opposing view holds that no matter how transformative the salting, food is categorically excluded from *ibud*. On that view, heavily preserved salted food still cannot trigger *me'aved*. The two positions thus diverge over whether the exclusion of *ochlin* from *ibud* is functional (depending on the degree of transformation) or categorical.

## Scraping, Cutting & Writing

### Pillar Smoothing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַמְמַחֲקוֹ וְהַמְחַתְּכוֹ. אָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר חֲנִינָא: הַשָּׁף בֵּין הָעַמּוּדִים בְּשַׁבָּת — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם מְמַחֵק.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna, among those liable for performing primary categories of labor: **And one who smooths it and one who cuts it. Rabbi Aḥa bar Ḥanina said: One who rubs** the hide **between the pillars on Shabbat,** i.e., places the skin between pillars made for that purpose (Rav Hai Gaon) and rubs it between them, **is liable due to** the labor of **smoothing.**

The Gemara turns to *memachek* (smoothing), *mechatech* (cutting to size), and related *melachot*. Rav Ami bar Chanina rules: one who rubs against pillars — the columns of a portico — on *Shabbat* is liable for *misa* for *memachek*. Rashi explains that one is smoothing the floor beneath by dragging one's body along it. Tosafot rejects this reading — presumably because that would more naturally be *boneh* than *memachek* — and instead explains that one is literally rubbing leather against the stone pillars, a direct application of the leather-smoothing process.

### Three Finishing Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: שְׁלֹשָׁה דְבָרִים סָח לִי רַב אָשֵׁי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: הַמְגָרֵר רָאשֵׁי כְּלוֹנְסוֹת בְּשַׁבָּת — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם מְחַתֵּךְ, הַמְמָרֵחַ רְטִיָּה בְּשַׁבָּת — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם מְמַחֵק, וְהַמְסַתֵּת אֶת הָאֶבֶן בְּשַׁבָּת — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם מַכֶּה בְּפַטִּישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥiyya bar Abba said: Rav Ashi told me three statements in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: One who planes the tops of posts on Shabbat** to make them even **is liable due to** the labor of **cutting,** due to his insistence that they all be equal. **One who spreads a bandage** onto a wound **on Shabbat is liable due to** the labor of **smoothing. And one who chisels a stone on Shabbat is liable due to** the labor of **striking** a blow **with a hammer,** as he thereby completes work on the stone.

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba cites three rulings in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi. First: one who whittles the tip of a pole on *Shabbat* — shaping it to a point — is liable for *mechatech*. Although one smooths incidentally in the process, the dominant act is a precise cut to produce a specific shape — namely, a point — and *mechatech* is the governing category, not *memachek*. This illustrates a general principle: when an act can be classified under a more specific category, that category takes precedence over a broader one.

### Salve Smoothing

Second: one who spreads a *ritya* — a medicinal salve or poultice — on a wound on *Shabbat* is liable for *memachek*. This is the critical ruling for a range of practical applications. One might have thought *memachek* requires an intention that the surface itself become smooth — and here one merely wants to apply the salve therapeutically. One does not care whether it lies flat. Nevertheless, the act of spreading it smooth constitutes *memachek*. This is the basis for the practical guidance that one should dab such substances on rather than spread them, relying on subsequent movement to distribute the material — whether salve on a wound, cream on skin, or toothpaste on teeth.

### Stone Finishing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן קִיסְמָא אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: הַצָּר צוּרָה בִּכְלִי וְהַמְנַפֵּחַ בִּכְלִי זְכוּכִית — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם מַכֶּה בְּפַטִּישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Kisma said** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: One who engraves a figure onto an** earthenware **vessel and one who blows** in order to craft **a glass vessel is liable due to** the labor of **striking** a blow **with a hammer.**

Third: one who makes the finishing indentation around the edge of a stone on *Shabbat* is liable for *makeh b'fatish*. This might appear to be *memachek*, since one is smoothing a surface, yet the Gemara classifies it as *makeh b'fatish* — the last finishing act that completes the object. Similarly, one who makes a final decorative incision in a vessel (*hatziyar tziyura b'chli*) or who blows a final adjustment into a glass vessel (*hamnafeiach b'chli zchuchit*) is liable for *makeh b'fatish*. The principle is broad: any last act that brings an object to its finished state is *makeh b'fatish*, which is why the *amora'im* later summarize — *kol midi d'it bei gmar melacha chayav mishum makeh b'fatish* — anything that constitutes the final act of completion is *makeh b'fatish*. The literature on the precise parameters of this principle is extensive.

### Thread Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הַאי מַאן דְּשָׁקֵיל אַקּוּפֵי מִגְּלִימֵי — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם מַכֶּה בְּפַטִּישׁ. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי דְּקָפֵיד עֲלַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said: One who removes** protruding, irregular **threads from a cloak is liable due to** the labor of **striking** a blow **with a hammer. And that applies** only **if he is particular about them** and would not wear the garment until all protruding threads are removed.

Rabbi Yehuda rules: one who removes a hanging thread from a garment — the kind of loose end left after the garment is otherwise finished — is liable for *makeh b'fatish*, since it is the last act of completion. This applies only *d'kapeid alayhu* — if one actually cares about that thread. If one is indifferent, even though removing it technically finishes the garment, the absence of concern presumably means the garment was never considered incomplete. The converse question — whether removing threads sewn in by manufacturers for packaging purposes, such as the stitching that keeps jacket pockets closed, constitutes *makeh b'fatish* — is probably inapplicable, since the garment was already finished before those threads were added; *makeh b'fatish* is the last stage of the original production process, not the undoing of something added afterward.

### Writing & Erasing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַכּוֹתֵב שְׁתֵּי אוֹתִיּוֹת. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כָּתַב אוֹת אַחַת גְּדוֹלָה וְיֵשׁ בִּמְקוֹמָהּ לִכְתּוֹב שְׁתַּיִם — פָּטוּר. מָחַק אוֹת גְּדוֹלָה, וְיֵשׁ בִּמְקוֹמָהּ לִכְתּוֹב שְׁתַּיִם — חַיָּיב. אָמַר רַבִּי מְנַחֵם בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: זֶה חוֹמֶר בַּמּוֹחֵק מִבַּכּוֹתֵב.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna, among those liable for performing primary categories of labor: **And one who writes two letters. The Sages taught: One who wrote one large letter, and in its space there is** room **to write two, is exempt,** as he wrote only one letter. However, **one who erased** one **large letter, and in its space there is** room **to write two, is liable. Rav Menaḥem, son of Rabbi Yosei, said: And that is a** greater **stricture with regard to erasing than with regard to writing.** Although greater stringency is usually accorded to creative acts, here the destructive act of erasing is more stringent. Although he erased only one letter, he made room for two, which is the essence of the prohibited labor.

The *baraita* establishes an instructive asymmetry between writing and erasing. If one writes a single very large letter that occupies the space where two letters could have been written, one is exempt — only one letter was written, and the minimum for liability is two letters. However, if one erases a large letter and the resulting blank space is sufficient to accommodate two letters, one is liable. The act of erasing is not measured by how many letters were erased but by how much space is cleared for writing. Rabbi Menachem the son of Rabbi Yosi notes: *zeh chomer b'mochek mib'koteiv* — this is a case where erasing is more stringent than writing.

## 39 Melachot & Disputes

### Avot Melachot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֵלּוּ אֲבוֹת מְלָאכוֹת״. ״אֵלּוּ״ — לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, דִּמְחַיֵּיב עַל תּוֹלָדָה בִּמְקוֹם אָב.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna concludes: **These are the primary categories of labor.** The Gemara explains that the emphasis on the word **these,** indicating these and no others, comes **to exclude** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** who **renders one liable for** the performance of **a subcategory** of prohibited labor when performed **together with a primary category** under which it is subsumed. Rabbi Eliezer deems one who performs two prohibited labors, a primary category and its subcategory, liable to bring two sin-offerings. In his opinion, one who unwittingly performed all the labors in one lapse of awareness would be liable to bring more than thirty-nine sin-offerings.

The *Mishnah* concludes: *eilu avot melachot* — these are the primary categories of *melacha*. The word *eilu* — these — is precise and exclusive: *le'apukei mid'Rabbi Eliezer* — to exclude the position of Rabbi Eliezer, who rules *d'mechayev al tolada b'makom av* — that if one performs both an *av* and its *tolada*, one incurs liability for each separately. According to Rabbi Eliezer, the number of *chatatot* one can incur is theoretically unlimited, since every *tolada* is distinct from its *av*. The *Mishnah*'s formulation — *these are* the *avot melachot* — excludes this position and establishes that only the thirty-nine primary categories generate distinct liability.

### Rabbi Yehuda Adds 2

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״חָסֵר אַחַת״ — לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מוֹסִיף אֶת הַשּׁוֹבֵט וְהַמְדַקְדֵּק. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: שׁוֹבֵט הֲרֵי הוּא בִּכְלַל מֵיסֵךְ, מְדַקְדֵּק הֲרֵי הוּא בִּכְלַל אוֹרֵג.
>
> **Translation:** The repetition of the count at the end serves **to exclude** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda. As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda** added **lining up** the threads of the warp **and beating** the threads of the woof to the list of primary categories of labor. **They said to him: Lining up** is a subcategory **subsumed under** the primary category of **stretching** the threads of the warp within the loom, since both involve arranging the threads of the warp. **Beating is subsumed under** the primary category of **weaving.** The mishna teaches that there are no more than thirty-nine primary categories of labor.

The number thirty-nine is stated both at the beginning and at the end of the *Mishnah*. The repetition of the count at the end serves to exclude Rabbi Yehuda, who — as the *baraita* teaches — adds two additional categories: *shoveit*, a method of beating threads into the loom during setup, and *maddek*, a method of beating threads smooth during weaving.

### Refutation

The Rabbis respond to Rabbi Yehuda: *shoveit harei hu bichlal meisach* — *shoveit* is already included within *meisach*, the act of setting up the warp on the loom, since it is part of that preparatory process. *Maddek harei hu bichlal oreig* — *maddek* is already included within *oreig*, weaving, since it is part of the act of weaving itself. These are not independent categories but constituent elements of processes already enumerated. The total therefore remains thirty-nine, and the discussion will continue in the following session.
