# Berakhot 58 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Idolatry & Blessing

### Uprooted Idolatry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּעֶקְרָה מִמֶּנּוּ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָרוֹאֶה מַרְקוּלִיס, אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ … שֶׁנָּתַן אֶרֶךְ אַפַּיִם לְעוֹבְרֵי רְצוֹנוֹ״. מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּעֶקְרָה מִמֶּנּוּ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ … שֶׁעָקַר עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה מֵאַרְצֵנוּ. וּכְשֵׁם שֶׁנֶּעֶקְרָה מִמָּקוֹם זֶה כֵּן תֵּעָקֵר מִכׇּל מְקוֹמוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְהָשֵׁב לֵב עוֹבְדֵיהֶם לְעׇבְדֶךָ״. וּבְחוּץ לָאָרֶץ אֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר ״וְהָשֵׁב לֵב עוֹבְדֵיהֶם לְעׇבְדֶךָ״ מִפְּנֵי שֶׁרוּבָּהּ גּוֹיִים. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: אַף בְּחוּץ לָאָרֶץ צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר כֵּן, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁעֲתִידִים לְהִתְגַּיֵּיר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אָז אֶהְפֹּךְ אֶל עַמִּים שָׂפָה בְרוּרָה״.
>
> **Translation:** [From the preceding daf] One who sees **a place from which idolatry was eradicated** should recite the blessing: Blessed…Who eradicated idolatry from our land. On this topic **the Sages taught** in the *Tosefta*: **One who sees** the idol called **Mercury [*Markulis*] recites: Blessed…who has shown patience to those who violate His will,** as each day new rocks would be thrown upon the pile constructed in Mercury's honor (*Tosafot*). One who sees **a place from which idolatry was eradicated should recite: Blessed…Who eradicated idolatry from our land. And just as it was eradicated from this place, so too may it be eradicated from all places of Israel, and restore the hearts of their worshippers to worship You. Outside of Eretz** Yisrael, **one need not recite: And restore the hearts of their worshippers to worship You, since it is predominantly populated by gentiles. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: Even outside of Eretz** Yisrael **one is required to recite that** formula **because** in the end of days all nations **will convert, as it is stated: "For then will I turn to the peoples a pure language,** that they may all call upon the Name of the Lord, to serve Him with one consent" (Zephaniah 3:9).

The daf opens at the very end of 57b, approximately twelve lines from the bottom, with the *mishnah*'s discussion of *berachos* recited upon encountering particular places and phenomena. The first case is *makom shene'ekar mimenu avodas kochavim* — a place from which idolatry has been uprooted. Upon seeing such a place, one recites a *bracha* in the formulation of *she'akar akum me'artzenu*, which uprooted idolatry from our land. The language *me'artzenu* suggests that this *bracha* applies specifically in *Eretz Yisrael*.

The *Tanna'im* elaborate in a *baraita*: one says not only that God uprooted it from this place, but adds the petition *ukeshem shene'ekrah mimmakom zeh ken te'aker mikkol mekomos shel Yisrael* — that as idolatry has been uprooted from this place, so may it be uprooted from all places where the Jewish people dwell — and *vehashev lev ovdeihem la'avodasecha*, that the hearts of those who worship idols be returned to Your service. Outside of *Eretz Yisrael*, however, the *Gemara* states one need not add this final phrase — *ein tzarich lomar vehashev lev ovdeihem la'avodasecha* — because the majority there are idolaters, and there is no expectation that they will turn to God.

### Markulis Blessing

Before addressing *makom shene'ekar*, the *baraita* introduces the blessing upon seeing *Markulis* — a form of idol worshipped by travelers, consisting of pyramidal piles of stone to which passersby would add stones. Upon seeing it, one recites *baruch shenatan erech apayim le'ovrei retzono*: blessed is He who extends His anger — holds back His wrath — against those who transgress His will.

This *bracha* is somewhat difficult to categorize. It is not quite a standard *birchas hashevach*, nor is it simply a *dayan ha'emes* formulation pronounced over *midas puraniyos*. Rather, it responds to a genuine theological challenge: if we believe in God, how does one account for the prevalence of idolatry throughout the world? The *bracha* provides religious articulation to that challenge — an acknowledgment that God, in His patience, tolerates those who violate His will. Presumably, according to the *Rishonim* who hold that these *berachos* are recited with *shem u'malchus*, this one is included.

One student raised a beautiful point: the *bracha* does not merely explain God's tolerance of idolatry as passive forbearance, but carries with it a certain recognition that even a worldview as ultimately misguided as that of *Markulis*-worship contains an ethical foundation — otherwise God's patience toward it would not require articulation. In the Jewish framework, this *bracha* grants a kind of qualified acknowledgment of what is present without endorsing it.

### Universal vs. Local

The restriction against reciting *vehashev lev ovdeihem la'avodasecha* outside of *Eretz Yisrael* sits in some tension with the universalist theology expressed prominently in the *davening* of *Rosh Hashanah* and *Yom Kippur* — the petition of *u'vchen ten pachdecha al kol hama'asim*, calling for all of God's creatures to acknowledge Him. It seems inconsistent to pray universally for the recognition of God throughout the world while simultaneously declining to say the very same thing upon seeing an idol outside of Israel.

### R. Shimon ben Elazar

It is precisely this tension that *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* addresses. He rules — and the *Rambam* accepts his position as *halacha* — that even *b'chutz la'aretz* one does recite the petition for the return of idolaters to God's service, *mipnei she'atidim lehisgayer* — because they are destined ultimately to convert. The word *lehisgayer* is notable: it frames the future acknowledgment not merely as intellectual acceptance of monotheism, but as actual conversion. One could have argued that non-Jews need only accept *Sheva Mitzvos Bnei Noach* and recognize the one true God — a genuine acknowledgment that would not technically be called *lehisgayer*. The *Gemara* here, however, frames the universal eschatological hope in these more expansive terms, drawing support from the verse *az efpoch el amim safa berura* — then I will turn to the nations one clear language — understood to refer to calling all of them in the name of God.

### Tosafot's Resolution

A practical question arises from all of this: why do we not recite the *bracha* of *shenatan erech apayim l'ovrei ritzono* today upon seeing churches or other non-Jewish houses of worship? *Tosafot* addresses this directly. Their answer is that this *bracha* is only appropriate for a new or novel phenomenon. Just as one recites a *bracha* upon seeing the Mediterranean Sea only if one has not seen it in the past thirty days, so too this *bracha* is not recited if the presence of idolatry is a constant fixture of one's surroundings. *Tosafot* suggests that the reason *Markulis* specifically serves as the paradigm case is that it was constantly being built and rebuilt — each new arrangement of stones constituted a genuinely new phenomenon, and thus each encounter could legitimately trigger the *bracha*. When idolatry is ambient and unchanging, there is no trigger.

This reasoning also explains why we do not make the *bracha* today. One student raised the intriguing mirror question: what about a church that has been converted into a mosque or even a synagogue? *Tosafot* himself does not address this directly, offering instead the different rationale above, and the broader evolution of *halachic* attitudes toward Christianity — which changed significantly even within the period of the *Rishonim* — would itself bear on whether the *bracha* would ever have been applicable to begin with.

## Babylon's Destruction

### Five Blessings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרַשׁ רַב הַמְנוּנָא: הָרוֹאֶה בָּבֶל הָרְשָׁעָה צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵךְ חָמֵשׁ בְּרָכוֹת. רָאָה בָּבֶל, אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ … שֶׁהֶחֱרִיב בָּבֶל הָרְשָׁעָה״. רָאָה בֵּיתוֹ שֶׁל נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר, אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ … שֶׁהֶחֱרִיב בֵּיתוֹ שֶׁל נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר הָרָשָׁע״. רָאָה גּוֹב שֶׁל אֲרָיוֹת אוֹ כִּבְשַׁן הָאֵשׁ, אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ … שֶׁעָשָׂה נִסִּים לַאֲבוֹתֵינוּ בַּמָּקוֹם הַזֶּה״. רָאָה מַרְקוּלִיס, אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ … שֶׁנָּתַן אֶרֶךְ אַפַּיִם לְעוֹבְרֵי רְצוֹנוֹ״. רָאָה מָקוֹם שֶׁנּוֹטְלִין מִמֶּנּוּ עָפָר, אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ … אוֹמֵר וְעוֹשֶׂה גּוֹזֵר וּמְקַיֵּים״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara goes on to discuss special blessings instituted by the Sages to be recited upon seeing extraordinary sights. **Rav Hamnuna taught: One who sees the wicked Babylonia must recite five blessings.** The Gemara elaborates:  **One who saw** the ruins of **Babylonia, recites: Blessed…Who destroyed the wicked Babylonia.** **One who saw** the ruins of **Nebuchadnezzar's house, recites: Blessed…Who destroyed the house of wicked Nebuchadnezzar.** **One who saw the lion's den** into which Daniel was thrown (see Daniel ch. 6) **or the furnace** into which Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah **were thrown** (see Daniel ch. 3), **recites: Blessed…Who performed miracles for our ancestors in this place.** **One who saw Mercury, recites: Blessed…Who has shown patience to those who violate His will.** **One who saw a place from which earth is taken,** as over the generations earth was taken from certain places and used as fertilizer or for construction in the surrounding areas, **recites: Blessed…Who speaks and acts, decrees and fulfills.**

The *Gemara* transitions from blessings over idolatry to blessings over the destruction of a wicked empire. *Dadrash Hamuna* — Hamuna expounded — that one who sees Babylon is obligated to recite five distinct *berachos*. This is remarkable in itself: these are not *berachos* found in the *mishnah*, yet the *Amoraim* are composing new ones, apparently guided by their own religious instinct for what experiences call for formal blessing.

The first *bracha* upon merely seeing Babylon is *baruch shehechariv Bavel harishia* — blessed is He who destroyed the wicked Babylon. The association of Babylon with *rishia* — wickedness — persists even in the Amoraic period, because it was Babylon that destroyed the *Beis HaMikdash*.

### Nebuchadnezzar

The second *bracha* is upon seeing the ruined palace of *Nevuchadnetzar*: *baruch shehechariv beito shel Nevuchadnetzar harasha* — blessed is He who destroyed the house of Nebuchadnezzar the wicked.

### Miracles of Old

Third, upon seeing the lions' den into which Daniel was thrown, or the fiery furnace into which Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah were cast, one recites *baruch she'asa nissim la'avoteinu bamakom hazeh* — blessed is He who performed miracles for our forefathers in this place — the standard formulation found in the *mishnah* for sites of miracles.

### Dust of Babylon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא כִּי הֲוָה חָזֵי חֲמָרֵי דְּשָׁקְלִי עַפְרָא, טָרֵיף לְהוּ יְדָא עַל גַּבַּיְיהוּ, וְאָמַר: רְהוּטוּ צַדִּיקֵי לְמֶעְבַּד רְעוּתָא דְמָרַיְיכוּ. מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבִינָא כִּי הֲוָה מָטֵי לְבָבֶל הֲוָה שָׁקֵיל עַפְרָא בְּסוּדָרֵיהּ וְשָׁדֵי לְבַרָּא, לְקַיֵּים מַה שֶּׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וְטֵאטֵאתִיהָ בְּמַטְאֲטֵא הַשְׁמֵד״. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אֲנָא, הָא דְּרַב הַמְנוּנָא לָא שְׁמִיעַ לִי, אֶלָּא מִדַּעְתַּאי בָּרֵיכְתִּינְהוּ לְכוּלְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **when Rava would see donkeys carrying earth** from Babylonia, **he would slap their backs with his hand and say** to them: **Run, righteous ones, and fulfill the will of your Master. When Mar, son of Ravina, would arrive in Babylonia he would take earth in his kerchief and throw it outside, to fulfill that which is said: "And I will sweep it with the broom of destruction"** (Isaiah 14:23). **Rav Ashi said: I never heard the** statement of **Rav Hamnuna,** that one who sees Babylonia the wicked must recite five blessings. **However, based on my independent reasoning, I recited all of the blessings.**

The fourth and fifth *berachos* are more unusual. *Rav Hamnuna* recites *baruch shenatan erech apayim le'ovrei retzono* — the same formulation used for *Markulis* — upon seeing Babylon itself, reflecting on God's patience even toward this empire of wickedness. He also recites *baruch omer ve'oseh gozer umekayem* — blessed is He who speaks and does, who decrees and fulfills — upon seeing a place from which people take dust.

*Rashi* offers two explanations of this last detail. One is that there was a curse preventing animals from leaving Babylon unless burdened with its dust. The other is that the land had become so desolate that it could no longer sustain growth and was used only as a source of raw material — its very earth mined and transported elsewhere. The land turned to dust represents the fulfillment of the divine decree against Babylon, hence *gozer umekayem*.

The *Gemara* then provides two vivid illustrations. *Rava*, upon seeing donkeys laden with Babylonian dust, would slap their backs and call out to them: *rehatu tzaddiki rehatu* — run, righteous ones, run — to do the will of your Master. The dust-carrying donkeys, in *Rava*'s eyes, were unwitting instruments of the prophetic curse. *Mar* the son of *Ravina*, when entering the ruins of Babylon, would scoop up dust in his handkerchief and cast it outside the city limits, fulfilling the verse *vetatetiha bematateh hashmad* — I will sweep it with the broom of destruction.

It is worth noting that by the time of *Rav Hamnuna*, a second-generation *Amora* in Babylon, the city of Babylon itself — as distinct from the broader country of Babylonia — had already been abandoned and lay in ruins. The practical implications of these *berachos* thus applied specifically to the ruined capital, not to the entire region.

### Rav Ashi's Version

*Rav Ashi* reports that he had never actually heard the ruling of *Rav Hamnuna* regarding these five *berachos*, and that he composed the identical five *berachos* entirely on his own initiative — *mada'ati berachinhu lechulehu* — arriving at precisely the same formulations independently. This is a striking detail: separated by roughly a century, two authorities composing *berachos* without knowledge of each other reached identical conclusions.

### Curse Paradox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר: נִתְקַלְּלָה בָּבֶל — נִתְקַלְּלוּ שְׁכֵנֶיהָ. נִתְקַלְּלָה שׁוֹמְרוֹן — נִתְבָּרְכוּ שְׁכֵנֶיהָ: נִתְקַלְּלָה בָּבֶל — נִתְקַלְּלוּ שְׁכֵנֶיהָ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְשַׂמְתִּיהָ לְמוֹרַשׁ קִפֹּד וְאַגְמֵי מָיִם״. נִתְקַלְּלָה שׁוֹמְרוֹן נִתְבָּרְכוּ שְׁכֵנֶיהָ — דִּכְתִיב: ״וְשַׂמְתִּי שֹׁמְרוֹן לְעִי הַשָּׂדֶה לְמַטָּעֵי כָרֶם וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to Babylonia, the Gemara cites what **Rabbi Yirmeya ben Elazar said:** When **Babylonia was cursed, its neighbors were cursed** along with it. When **Samaria was cursed, its neighbors were blessed.** When **Babylonia was cursed its neighbors were cursed** along with it, **as it is written: "I will also make it a possession for the bittern,** a wading bird, **and pools of water"** (Isaiah 14:23); not only will it be destroyed, but the site will become a habitat for destructive, environmentally harmful creatures. When **Samaria was cursed,** however, **its neighbors were blessed, as it is written: "Therefore I will make Samaria a heap in the field, a place for the planting of vineyards"** (Micah 1:6); although destroyed, it will serve a beneficial purpose.

*Rabbi Yirmiyahu ben Elazar* observes a paradox in the nature of these curses. When Babylon was cursed, its neighbors were cursed as well — *nitkalela Bavel nitkalelu shecheneha* — drawn from the verse *vesamtiha lemorash kipod ve'agmei mayim*, which portrays the destruction as so total and the wasteland so complete that all surrounding lands suffer too. As the saying goes, *oy lerasha oy lishcheno* — woe to the wicked, woe to his neighbor.

By contrast, when *Shomron* was cursed, its neighbors were blessed — *nitkalela Shomron nitbarechu shecheneha* — because the verse describes *Shomron*'s destruction as its transformation into a field suitable for vineyard planting: *vesamti Shomron le'ei hasadeh lematayei karem*. Even in destruction, the land becomes productive and beneficial to those around it. The homiletical point is that the Jewish people, even when punished, leave a blessing behind for their neighbors.

## Masses of People

### Masses of Israel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: הָרוֹאֶה אוּכְלוּסֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ … חֲכַם הָרָזִים״. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָרוֹאֶה אוּכְלוּסֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ … חֲכַם הָרָזִים״ — שֶׁאֵין דַּעְתָּם דּוֹמָה זֶה לָזֶה, וְאֵין פַּרְצוּפֵיהֶן דּוֹמִים זֶה לָזֶה. בֶּן זוֹמָא רָאָה אוּכְלוּסָא עַל גַּב מַעֲלָה בְּהַר הַבַּיִת, אָמַר: ״בָּרוּךְ … חֲכַם הָרָזִים וּבָרוּךְ … שֶׁבָּרָא כׇּל אֵלּוּ לְשַׁמְּשֵׁנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Hamnuna said: One who sees multitudes of Israel,** six hundred thousand Jews, **recites: Blessed…Who knows all secrets.** **The Sages taught** in a *Tosefta*: **One who sees multitudes of Israel recites: Blessed…Who knows all secrets.** Why is this? He sees a whole nation **whose minds are unlike each other and whose faces are unlike each other,** and He Who knows all secrets, God, knows what is in each of their hearts. The Gemara relates: **Ben Zoma once saw a multitude [*okhlosa*] of Israel** while standing **on a stair on the Temple Mount.** He immediately **recited: Blessed…Who knows all secrets and Blessed…Who created all these to serve me.**

*Rav Hamnuna* then introduces a *bracha* for seeing large gatherings of Jews: *baruch chacham harazim* — blessed is He who is wise to secrets. A *baraita* explains: *she'ein da'atam domeh zeh l'zeh v'ein partzufeihem domim zeh l'zeh* — no two people think alike and no two people look alike. God alone knows the inner world of every individual.

There is something particularly beautiful and deliberate about locating this *bracha* at the moment of seeing a *mass* of people. When two or three individuals stand before us, we naturally perceive each as distinct. But when confronted with hundreds of thousands, there is a powerful tendency toward abstraction — the crowd becomes a number, the individuals disappear into the aggregate. It is precisely at this moment that the *bracha* insists on the opposite: each person before you is irreducibly unique, created *b'tzelem Elokim*, and God perceives the interiority of every single one. The *bracha* thus functions not only as praise of God's omniscience, but as a moral corrective — a reminder never to lose the face of the individual, especially when surrounded by multitudes.

A question naturally arises: if the *bracha* reflects the genuine uniqueness of every human being, why does it not apply equally upon seeing large gatherings of non-Jews? According to the text, seeing a *uchlosa* of non-Jews generates a very different response — the verse *boshah imchem*, your mother is shamed. This asymmetry is striking, and presumably the *Gemara* has in mind specifically idolaters rather than non-Jews in general, though the context makes this only partially clear. *Rav Hamnuna*'s *bracha* on *uchlosa* of Israel, however, stands as a profound statement about individual dignity even within mass experience.

A further structural question arises regarding whether any of these *berachos* require *shem u'malchus*. Some *Rishonim* hold that all the *berachos* in the ninth *perek* are recited without *shem u'malchus*, pointing among other things to the fact that the response to seeing non-Jewish multitudes is a *pasuk* rather than a formal *bracha*. Others maintain that the *berachos* enumerated explicitly — including *chacham harazim* — do require *shem u'malchus*.

### Ben Zoma Vision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא הָיָה אוֹמֵר: כַּמָּה יְגִיעוֹת יָגַע אָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן עַד שֶׁמָּצָא פַּת לֶאֱכוֹל: חָרַשׁ, וְזָרַע, וְקָצַר, וְעִמֵּר, וְדָשׁ, וְזָרָה, וּבָרַר, וְטָחַן, וְהִרְקִיד, וְלָשׁ, וְאָפָה, וְאַחַר כָּךְ אָכַל. וַאֲנִי מַשְׁכִּים וּמוֹצֵא כׇּל אֵלּוּ מְתוּקָּנִין לְפָנַי. וְכַמָּה יְגִיעוֹת יָגַע אָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן עַד שֶׁמָּצָא בֶּגֶד לִלְבּוֹשׁ: גָּזַז, וְלִבֵּן, וְנִפֵּץ, וְטָוָה, וְאָרַג, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מָצָא בֶּגֶד לִלְבּוֹשׁ, וַאֲנִי מַשְׁכִּים וּמוֹצֵא כׇּל אֵלֶּה מְתוּקָּנִים לְפָנַי. כׇּל אוּמּוֹת שׁוֹקְדוֹת וּבָאוֹת לְפֶתַח בֵּיתִי, וַאֲנִי מַשְׁכִּים וּמוֹצֵא כׇּל אֵלּוּ לְפָנַי.
>
> **Translation:** Explaining his custom, **he would say: How much effort did Adam the first** man **exert before he found bread to eat:** He **plowed, sowed, reaped, sheaved, threshed, winnowed** in the wind, **separated** the grain from the chaff, **ground** the grain into flour, **sifted, kneaded, and baked and** only **thereafter he ate. And I,** on the other hand, **wake up and find all of these prepared for me.** Human society employs a division of labor, and each individual benefits from the service of the entire world. Similarly, **how much effort did Adam the first** man **exert before he found a garment to wear?** He **sheared, laundered, combed, spun and wove, and** only **thereafter he found a garment to wear. And I,** on the other hand, **wake up and find all of these prepared for me.** Members of **all nations,** merchants and craftsmen, **diligently come to the entrance of my home, and I wake up and find all of these before me.**

The *Gemara* recalls that *Ben Zoma* once stood on a step on *Har HaBayis* and saw a *uchlosa* of people. He recited *baruch chacham harazim* — the *bracha* of God's knowledge of every individual's secrets — and added *u'varuch shebara kol eilu l'shameshni*: blessed is He who created all of these people to serve me.

This second clause is arresting. *Ben Zoma* himself had been mentioned the previous daf as someone whose face is a sign of imminent wisdom — *yitzapeh l'chochmah* — and the declaration that all these people exist *l'shameshni*, to serve me, carries an unmistakable elitist resonance. It will be worth observing what he intends.

### Adam's Labor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא הָיָה אוֹמֵר: אוֹרֵחַ טוֹב מַהוּ אוֹמֵר? — כַּמָּה טְרָחוֹת טָרַח בַּעַל הַבַּיִת בִּשְׁבִילִי, כַּמָּה בָּשָׂר הֵבִיא לְפָנַי, כַּמָּה יַיִן הֵבִיא לְפָנַי, כַּמָּה גְּלוּסְקָאוֹת הֵבִיא לְפָנַי, וְכׇל מַה שֶּׁטָּרַח — לֹא טָרַח אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִילִי. אֲבָל אוֹרֵחַ רַע מַהוּ אוֹמֵר? — מַה טּוֹרַח טָרַח בַּעַל הַבַּיִת זֶה? פַּת אַחַת אָכַלְתִּי, חֲתִיכָה אַחַת אָכַלְתִּי, כּוֹס אֶחָד שָׁתִיתִי, כׇּל טוֹרַח שֶׁטָּרַח בַּעַל הַבַּיִת זֶה — לֹא טָרַח אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל אִשְׁתּוֹ וּבָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** Ben Zoma **would say: A good guest, what does he say? How much effort did the host expend on my behalf, how much meat did** the host **bring before me. How much wine did he bring before me. How many loaves [*geluskaot*] did he bring before me. All the** effort **that he expended, he expended only for me. However, a bad guest, what does he say? What effort did the host expend? I ate** only **one** piece of **bread, I ate** only **one piece of meat** and **I drank** only **one cup** of wine. **All the effort that the home owner expended he only expended on behalf of his wife and children.**

*Ben Zoma* develops his thought. He says: how much effort did *Adam HaRishon* have to expend before he could eat a piece of bread? He had to plow, sow, reap, heap, thresh, winnow, separate chaff from grain, grind, sift, knead, and bake — and only then could he eat. But I, *Ben Zoma* says, *mashkim u'motzei kol eilu metukanim l'fanai* — I wake up and find all of this already prepared before me.

Similarly with clothing: *Adam HaRishon* had to shear, whiten, comb, spin, and weave before he had a garment. But I wake up and the craftspeople have already brought their wares to my door — *kol ummaniyot shokdot u'baot lepetach beiti* — and everything is already prepared.

On one reading, the *bishvili* — for my sake — in this passage echoes the *l'shameshni* of the previous statement and suggests that *Ben Zoma* views himself as the pinnacle of creation, the one for whose benefit all others labor. This is not entirely unlike the approach of the *Rambam* in *Moreh Nevuchim*, who argues that the world requires the broad mass of craftsmen and farmers precisely so that the rare philosophical theologian, who alone genuinely comprehends truth and cleaves to God, has the material conditions necessary to pursue that understanding. A deeply platonic hierarchy underlies this view.

### Guest Teachings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל אוֹרֵחַ טוֹב מַהוּ אוֹמֵר? — ״זְכֹר כִּי תַשְׂגִּיא פׇעֳלוֹ אֲשֶׁר שֹׁרְרוּ אֲנָשִׁים״. עַל אוֹרֵחַ רַע כְּתִיב: ״לָכֵן יְרֵאוּהוּ אֲנָשִׁים וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to a good guest, what does he say? "Remember that you magnify his work, whereof men have sung"** (Job 36:24); he praises and acknowledges those who helped him. **With regard to a bad guest it is written: "Men do therefore fear him;** he regards not any who are wise of heart" (Job 37:24).

Yet the very next teaching of *Ben Zoma* reframes everything through the lens of *hakarat hatov*. He says: what does a good guest say? *Kamah trachot tarach ba'al habayit bishvili* — how much effort did the host go to for my sake? Look at all the meat he brought, the wine, the pastries — *kol mah shetarach lo tarach ella bishvili* — all of it was done for me. And what does a bad guest say? What effort did he really make? He only gave me one loaf, one slice, one cup — and all of it was for his family anyway, not for me.

The contrast is illuminating. In both cases, the guest uses the word *bishvili* — for my sake — but the meaning and effect are entirely different. The good guest says *bishvili* in order to feel the full weight of gratitude; the bad guest uses the same language to minimize and dismiss. *Ben Zoma*'s teaching is that the appropriate perception of reality is the one that generates *hakarat hatov*. One should understand all that has been provided to oneself — bread, clothing, the labor of countless strangers — as having been done *for you*, not because you are the ontological center of the universe, but because that perception alone produces the depth of appreciation that is the proper human response to receiving good.

This reading retroactively illuminates *Ben Zoma*'s statement about *l'shameshni*. It is not primarily a claim about cosmic hierarchy but about gratitude: when you see all these people, recognize that their labor reaches you, that you are the beneficiary, and feel the full force of that debt.

The *pasuk* cited for the good guest — *zachor ki tasgi poalo asher shoraru anashim*, remember that his deeds are great and people sing his praises — reinforces that the good guest responds to hospitality with active acknowledgment and praise. The bad guest receives only a darkening of the verse.

### Yishai & Masses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהָאִישׁ בִּימֵי שָׁאוּל זָקֵן בָּא בַאֲנָשִׁים״. אָמַר רָבָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב זְבִיד, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא: זֶה יִשַׁי אֲבִי דָּוִד שֶׁיָּצָא בְּאוּכְלוּסָא, וְנִכְנַס בְּאוּכְלוּסָא, וְדָרַשׁ בְּאוּכְלוּסָא. אָמַר עוּלָּא: נְקִיטִינַן אֵין אוּכְלוּסָא בְּבָבֶל. תָּנָא: אֵין אוּכְלוּסָא פְּחוּתָה מִשִּׁשִּׁים רִבּוֹא.
>
> **Translation:** On the topic of multitudes, the Gemara cites another verse: **"And the man in the days of Saul was old, and came among men"** (I Samuel 17:12). **Rava, and some say Rav Zevid, and some say Rav Oshaya, said: This** refers to **Yishai, father of David, who** always **went out with multitudes, and entered with multitudes, and taught Torah with multitudes. Ulla said: We hold there is no multitude in Babylonia.** The Sage **taught: A multitude is no fewer than six hundred thousand** people.

The *Gemara* returns to the theme of *uchlosa*. The verse *vayehi bimei Shaul zaken ba ba'anashim* — and it came to pass in the days of Saul, an old man came with men — is interpreted by *Rava*, or alternatively by *Rav Zvid* or *Rav Oshia*, as referring to *Yishai avi Dovid* — Jesse, the father of David. Wherever Jesse went, *sheyatza be'uchlosa venichnas be'uchlosa* — he would emerge and enter with a great multitude, and whenever he delivered a *drasha*, it was before a mass gathering. The connection to *uchlosa* brings the passage back to the question of what constitutes a significant *uchlosa* of Israel.

### 600,000 Threshold

*Ulla* states: *naktinan ein uchlosa be'Bavel* — we hold the position that *uchlosa* does not apply in Babylon. Even a very large gathering there would not carry the halachic or spiritual significance of *uchlosa*. The implication is that *uchlosa* is not merely about number but about representing *Klal Yisrael* as such — and that representational quality apparently inheres only to gatherings in *Eretz Yisrael* or to gatherings with the character of the full community of Israel.

This is confirmed by a *tanna* who defines the threshold: *ein uchlosa pachot mi-shishim ribo* — *uchlosa* requires no fewer than 600,000. The number is not coincidental; it corresponds to those who left Egypt, and thus to the paradigmatic count of *Klal Yisrael* as a whole.

## Wise & Royal

### Jewish Wisdom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָרוֹאֶה חַכְמֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, אוֹמֵר ״בָּרוּךְ … שֶׁחָלַק מֵחׇכְמָתוֹ לִירֵאָיו״. חַכְמֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם, אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ … שֶׁנָּתַן מֵחׇכְמָתוֹ לְבָשָׂר וָדָם״. הָרוֹאֶה מַלְכֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ … שֶׁחָלַק מִכְּבוֹדוֹ לִירֵאָיו״. מַלְכֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם, אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ … שֶׁנָּתַן מִכְּבוֹדוֹ לְבָשָׂר וָדָם״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: One who sees the Sages of Israel recites: Blessed…Who has shared of His wisdom with those who revere Him.** One who sees **Sages of the nations of the world recites: Blessed…Who has given of His wisdom to flesh and blood. One who sees kings of Israel recites: Blessed…Who has shared of His glory with those who revere Him.** One who sees **kings of the** other **nations of the world recites: Blessed…Who has given of His glory to flesh and blood.**

A *Tanna'itic* source establishes the *bracha* upon seeing *chachmei Yisrael*: *baruch shecholak mechachmato lireiaiv* — blessed is He who gave of His wisdom to those who fear Him. The formulation *cholak* — shared, partitioned — implies that the sage's wisdom is in some sense a portion of divine wisdom itself.

## Masses of People…

### Non-Jewish Masses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: הָרוֹאֶה אוּכְלוּסֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ … חֲכַם הָרָזִים״. אוּכְלוּסֵי גּוֹיִם, אוֹמֵר: ״בּוֹשָׁה אִמְּכֶם וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Hamnuna said: One who sees multitudes of Israel,** six hundred thousand Jews, **recites: Blessed…Who knows all secrets.** One who sees **multitudes of gentiles recites: "Your mother shall be sore ashamed,** she who bore you shall be confounded; behold, the hindermost of the nations shall be a wilderness, a dry land, and a desert" (Jeremiah 50:12).

As noted in the earlier discussion of *uchlosa*, seeing a mass gathering of non-Jews does not generate the *bracha* of *chacham harazim*, but instead the recitation of the verse *boshah imchem* — your mother is shamed. This stands in pointed contrast to the *bracha* over masses of Israel. Whether this reflects the specific context of idolaters or is a broader statement about non-Jewish multitudes remains a matter of reading.

## Wise & Royal…

### Gentile Wisdom

By contrast with the *bracha* over Jewish sages, the *bracha* upon seeing wise non-Jews — even idolaters — is *baruch shenatan mechachmato livriyotav*: blessed is He who gave of His wisdom to His creatures. Two distinctions from the corresponding blessing over Jewish sages are notable. First, *natan* — gave — rather than *cholak* — shared — suggesting perhaps a more external bestowal rather than a partitioning of divine wisdom itself. Second, *livriyotav* — to His creatures — rather than *lireiaiv* — to those who fear Him. The acknowledgment is genuine and important: wisdom among non-Jews is real, it comes from God, and it deserves recognition. One does not say *lireiaiv* — for that would imply a relationship of *yirat Shamayim* — but the wisdom itself is recognized as a divine gift.

### Jewish Kings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְעוֹלָם יִשְׁתַּדֵּל אָדָם לָרוּץ לִקְרַאת מַלְכֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל. וְלֹא לִקְרַאת מַלְכֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל בִּלְבַד, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ לִקְרַאת מַלְכֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם, שֶׁאִם יִזְכֶּה — יַבְחִין בֵּין מַלְכֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל לְמַלְכֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One should always strive to run toward kings of Israel** to greet them. **And not only** should he run **toward kings of Israel, but also toward kings of the nations of the world, so that if he will be privileged** to witnesses the glory of the Messiah (Rashi) and the World-to-Come, **he will distinguish between the kings of Israel and the kings of the nations of the world.**

Upon seeing *malchei Yisrael* one recites *baruch shecholak mikvodo lireiaiv* — blessed is He who shared His honor with those who fear Him. The *kavod* of kingship is understood as a reflection or echo of divine honor. Here again *cholak* is used, suggesting that the honor granted to a Jewish king is in some sense a genuine portion of divine honor, not merely an external gift.

### Gentile Kings

Upon seeing *malchei ovdei kochavim* one recites *baruch shenatan mikvodo livriyotav* — blessed is He who gave of His honor to flesh and blood. Some *Rishonim* have the text *levasar vadam* rather than *livriyotav*. Again *natan* replaces *cholak* and *livriyotav* replaces *lireiaiv*, parallel to the distinction between Jewish and non-Jewish wisdom.

An important *halachic* question follows from these blessings: does one recite them upon seeing contemporary heads of state — presidents or constitutional monarchs? The general *maskana* is that one does not recite the *bracha* with *shem u'malchus*, because modern rulers lack both the absolute authority and the ceremonial *kavod* of ancient kings. The *kavod* of ancient kingship — the mounted retinue, the trumpets, the prostrating crowds — is not replicated in modern democratic governance or even in constitutional monarchy, where one may have the glory without the power or the power without the glory. The *bracha* may be recited but without *shem u'malchus*.

### Greet All Kings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שֵׁשֶׁת סַגִּי נְהוֹר הֲוָה. הֲווֹ קָאָזְלִי כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לְקַבּוֹלֵי אַפֵּי מַלְכָּא, וְקָם אֲזַל בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת. אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ הָהוּא מִינָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חַצְבֵי לְנַהְרָא, כַּגְנֵי לְיָיא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תָּא חֲזִי דְּיָדַעְנָא טְפֵי מִינָּךְ. חֲלַף גּוּנְדָּא קַמַּיְיתָא. כִּי קָא אָוְושָׁא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִינָא: אֲתָא מַלְכָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: לָא קָאָתֵי. חֲלַף גּוּנְדָּא תִּנְיָינָא. כִּי קָא אָוְושָׁא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִינָא: הַשְׁתָּא קָא אָתֵי מַלְכָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: לָא קָא אָתֵי מַלְכָּא. חָלֵיף תְּלִיתַאי. כִּי קָא שָׁתְקָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: וַדַּאי הַשְׁתָּא אָתֵי מַלְכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Sheshet was blind. Everyone was going to greet the king and Rav Sheshet stood up and went along with them. This heretic found him** there and **said to him:** The intact **jugs** go **to the river, where do the broken** jugs **go?** Why is a blind person going to see the king? Rav Sheshet **said to him: Come see that I know more than you** do. **The first troop passed,** and **when the noise grew louder, this heretic said to him: The king is coming. Rav Sheshet said to him:** The king **is not coming. The second troop passed,** and **when the noise grew louder, this heretic said to him: Now the king is coming. Rav Sheshet said to him: The king is not coming. The third** troop **passed,** and **when there was silence, Rav Sheshet said to him: Certainly now the king is coming.**

*Rav Yochanan* teaches: one should always endeavor to go out and greet kings — not only Jewish kings, but even *malchei ovdei kochavim*. The reason is: *she'im yizkeh*, if one merits it — *Rashi* understands this as living to see the era of *Mashiach* — one will then be able to distinguish and truly appreciate the vastly greater honor accorded to the king *Mashiach* by comparison with the honor one has previously witnessed given to non-Jewish kings.

### Rav Sheshet Blind

*Rav Sheshet* was blind. When the entire community went out to greet a passing king, he went with them. A certain *min* — heretic — mocked him: *chatzvi l'nahara* — why bring broken vessels to a river? What use is a blind man at a royal procession? *Rav Sheshet* replied: *ta d'yadana tafi minach* — come, I know more than you.

### Heretic's Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִינָא: מְנָא לָךְ הָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּמַלְכוּתָא דְאַרְעָא כְּעֵין מַלְכוּתָא דִרְקִיעָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״צֵא וְעָמַדְתָּ בָהָר לִפְנֵי ה׳ וְהִנֵּה ה׳ עֹבֵר וְרוּחַ גְּדוֹלָה וְחָזָק מְפָרֵק הָרִים וּמְשַׁבֵּר סְלָעִים לִפְנֵי ה׳ לֹא בָרוּחַ ה׳ וְאַחַר הָרוּחַ רַעַשׁ לֹא בָרַעַשׁ ה׳. וְאַחַר הָרַעַשׁ אֵשׁ לֹא בָאֵשׁ ה׳ וְאַחַר הָאֵשׁ קוֹל דְּמָמָה דַקָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **This heretic said to him: How do you know this?** Rav Sheshet **said to him: Royalty on earth is like royalty in the heavens, as it is written** with regard to God's revelation to Elijah the Prophet on Mount Horeb:  **"And He said: Go forth, and stand upon the mount before the Lord.** **And, behold, the Lord passed by, and a great and strong wind rent the mountains, and broke in pieces the rocks before the Lord;** **but the Lord was not in the wind;** **and after the wind an earthquake;** **but the Lord was not in the earthquake;** **and after the earthquake a fire;** **but the Lord was not in the fire;** **and after the fire a still small voice.** And it was so, when Elijah heard it, that he wrapped his face in his mantle and went out, and stood in the entrance of the cave" (I Kings 19:11–13). God's revelation was specifically at the moment of silence.

As the royal procession passed, three successive groups went by. At the sound of the first group — the noise and fanfare — the *min* announced that the king was arriving. *Rav Sheshet* said: he has not yet come. The second group passed with great noise, and again the *min* said: now the king comes. Again *Rav Sheshet* said: he has not yet come. Then the third group passed — and there was silence. *Rav Sheshet* said: now the king is certainly coming.

The *min* asked: how do you know? *Rav Sheshet* answered: *d'malchuta d'ara k'ein malchuta d'rakia* — because the earthly kingship is an echo of the heavenly kingship. And what do we know of the divine presence? The *pasuk* of *Eliyahu* at the mountain: *v'ruach gedolah v'chazak mefarek harim* — a great and powerful wind breaking the mountains — *lo baruach Hashem*, God is not in the wind. After the wind, a great earthquake — *lo bara'ash Hashem*, not in the earthquake. After the earthquake, fire — *lo ba'esh Hashem*, not in the fire. And after the fire: *kol demamah dakah* — a still, soft voice. The divine presence comes in silence. So too the king: when all the fanfare has passed and silence falls, then the king appears.

### Rav Sheshet Blesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא מַלְכָּא, פָּתַח רַב שֵׁשֶׁת וְקָא מְבָרֵךְ לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִינָא: לְמַאן דְּלָא חָזֵית לֵיהּ קָא מְבָרְכַתְּ? וּמַאי הֲוִי עֲלֵיהּ דְּהָהוּא מִינָא? אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: חַבְרוֹהִי כַּחְלִינְהוּ לְעֵינֵיהּ, וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: רַב שֵׁשֶׁת נָתַן עֵינָיו בּוֹ, וְנַעֲשָׂה גַּל שֶׁל עֲצָמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **When the king came, Rav Sheshet began to bless him. The heretic** mockingly **said to him: Do you bless someone you do not see?** The Gemara asks: **And what ultimately happened to this heretic? Some say** that **his friends gouged out his eyes, and some say** that **Rav Sheshet fixed his gaze upon him, and** the heretic **became a pile of bones.**

When the king arrived, *Rav Sheshet* recited the *bracha* — *asher natan mikvodo l'basar vadam*. The *min* challenged him: you are making a *bracha* for someone you did not see? *Rav Sheshet* was apparently able to sense the royal *kavod* without seeing it; the *bracha* does not require visual perception. This raises broader *halachic* questions about blessings recited by blind people — *pokeach ivrim*, *yotzer hameorot*, and those connected to visual experiences of nature — though those involve additional technical considerations.

What became of the *min*? The *Gemara* gives two versions. Some say his friends — sardonically — *kachlinu le'einei*, applied mascara to his eyes, a euphemism for blinding him. Others say *Rav Sheshet nasa einav bo ve'asa gal shel atzamos* — he cast his eyes upon him and turned him into a pile of bones, the classic formula for a sage's *ayin hara*. There is a beautiful irony: *Rav Sheshet*, who could not see, directed his *ayin hara* more effectively than a sighted man could.

### Kingship Echo

The theme of *malchusa d'ar'a k'ein malchusa d'rakia* — earthly kingship as echo of heavenly kingship — is central not only to the story of *Rav Sheshet* but to the entire complex of blessings over kings. The *bracha* *shenatan mikvodo l'vasar vadam* is not merely a polite acknowledgment of human authority; it is a theological claim that legitimate temporal power derives from and points toward divine sovereignty.

## Rabbinic Authority

### Rav Shila Trial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שֵׁילָא נַגְּדֵיהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דִּבְעַל גּוֹיָה. אֲזַל אֲכַל בֵּיהּ קוּרְצֵי בֵּי מַלְכָּא, אֲמַר: אִיכָּא חַד גַּבְרָא בִּיהוּדָאֵי דְּקָא דָּיֵין דִּינָא בְּלָא הַרְמָנָא דְמַלְכָּא. שַׁדַּר עֲלֵיהּ פְּרִיסְתְּקָא. כִּי אֲתָא אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: מַאי טַעְמָא נַגֵּדְתֵּיהּ לְהַאי? אֲמַר לְהוּ: דְּבָא עַל חֲמָרְתָא. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: אִית לְךָ סָהֲדִי? אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִין. אֲתָא אֵלִיָּהוּ אִדְּמִי לֵיהּ כְּאִינִישׁ, וְאַסְהֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** As for the connection between divine and earthly royalty, the Gemara cites another story: **Rabbi Sheila** ordered that **a man who had relations with a gentile woman be flogged.** That man **went to inform the king and said: There is one man among the Jews who renders judgment without the king's authority [*harmana*].** The king **sent a messenger [*peristaka*] for** Rabbi Sheila to bring him to trial. **When** Rabbi Sheila **came, they said to him: Why did you** order **flogging for this man? He said to them:** Because **he had relations with a female donkey.** According to Persian law this was an extremely heinous crime, so **they said to him: Do you have witnesses** that he did so? **He replied: Yes,** and **Elijah the prophet came** and **appeared as a person and testified.**

*Rav Shila* administered lashes to a man who had relations with a non-Jewish woman. The man went and *achal bei kurtzei bei malka* — slandered him before the royal court — reporting that a certain Jew was exercising judicial authority without royal permission. The king dispatched officers to bring *Rav Shila* before them.

When asked why he had administered lashes, *Rav Shila* told the officers that the man had had relations with a donkey. They asked whether he had witnesses. *Rav Shila* said yes — and *Eliyahu* appeared, disguised as an ordinary person, and testified accordingly. The officers concluded that this warranted the death penalty. *Rav Shila* immediately responded that from the day Israel went into exile, *leit lan reshu lemikta* — we do not have authority to execute — thus distancing himself from any claim to capital jurisdiction, the very overreach of which he had been accused.

### Elijah's Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד דִּמְעַיְּינִי בֵּיהּ בְּדִינָא, פְּתַח רַבִּי שֵׁילָא וַאֲמַר: ״לְךָ ה׳ הַגְּדֻלָּה וְהַגְּבוּרָה וְגוֹ׳״. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: מַאי קָאָמְרַתְּ? אֲמַר לְהוּ, הָכִי קָאָמֵינָא: ״בְּרִיךְ רַחֲמָנָא דְּיָהֵיב מַלְכוּתָא בְּאַרְעָא כְּעֵין מַלְכוּתָא דִרְקִיעָא, וִיהַב לְכוּ שׁוּלְטָנָא וְרָחֲמִי דִּינָא״. אֲמַרוּ: חַבִּיבָא עֲלֵיהּ יְקָרָא דְמַלְכוּתָא כּוּלֵּי הַאי! יָהֲבִי לֵיהּ קוּלְפָא אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: דּוּן דִּינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **As they considered the sentence,** Rabbi Sheila praised God for saving him from danger: **"Yours, O Lord, is the greatness, power,** glory, triumph, and majesty; for all that is in heaven and on earth is Yours; Yours is the kingdom, O Lord, and You are exalted as head above all" (I Chronicles 29:11). **They asked him: What did you say? He told them: This is what I said: Blessed is the Merciful One who grants kingdom on earth** that is a **microcosm of the kingdom in heaven, and granted you dominion and love of justice. They said to him:** Indeed, the **honor of royalty is so dear to you. They gave him a staff** to symbolize his license to sit in judgment and **said to him: Judge.**

The officers were impressed. *Rav Shila* then opened the verse *lecha Hashem hagedulah vehagevurah* — to You God is the greatness and the power. Asked what he was saying, he explained: I am praising God *deyahav malchuta be'ar'a ke'ein malchuta derakia* — who gave kingship on earth in the image of heavenly kingship — and I am acknowledging that your people, lovers of justice, are His emissaries. The officers responded: *chaviva lach yekara de'malchuta* — the honor of kingship is clearly dear to you. They gave him a rod — a symbol of judicial authority — and appointed him to render judgment.

### Justice via Rod

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הֲוָה נָפֵיק, אֲמַר לֵיהּ הַהוּא גַּבְרָא: עָבֵיד רַחֲמָנָא נִיסָּא לְשַׁקָּרֵי הָכִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רָשָׁע, לָאו חֲמָרֵי אִיקְּרוּ? דִּכְתִיב: ״אֲשֶׁר בְּשַׂר חֲמוֹרִים בְּשָׂרָם״. חַזְיֵיהּ דְּקָאָזֵיל לְמֵימְרָא לְהוּ דִּקְרִינְהוּ חֲמָרֵי, אֲמַר: הַאי רוֹדֵף הוּא. וְהַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה: אִם בָּא לְהׇרְגְּךָ — הַשְׁכֵּם לְהׇרְגוֹ. מַחְיֵיהּ בְּקוּלְפָא וְקַטְלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **As he was leaving, that man said to** Rabbi Sheila: **Does God perform such miracles for liars? He replied: Scoundrel! Aren't** gentiles **called donkeys? As it is written: "Whose flesh is as the flesh of donkeys"** (Ezekiel 23:20). Rabbi Sheila **saw that he was going to tell** the Persian authorities **that he called them donkeys. He said:** This man has the legal status of **a pursuer.** He seeks to have me killed. **And the Torah said: If one comes to kill you, kill him first. He struck him with the staff and killed him.**

As *Rav Shila* was leaving, the man he had lashed came to him and complained: *avad Rachmana nisa le'shakrei* — God performed miracles for liars? You lied to them. *Rav Shila* answered: *lav chamarei karinhu* — they are called donkeys — *de'ketiv asher besar chamorim besaram*, the verse itself says "whose flesh is as the flesh of donkeys." The disparagement is severe, and the self-censorship of the original *goy* to *Mitzrit* in many manuscripts now becomes entirely comprehensible.

Seeing that the man was about to return to the officers and report what *Rav Shila* had said, *Rav Shila* declared: *hai rodef hu* — this man is a *rodef*, pursuer — and invoked the principle *ha'ba le'horgecha hashkem le'horgo*: if someone is coming to kill you, act preemptively. He struck him with the rod and killed him.

The structural parallel to the *Rav Sheshet* story is deliberate: in both cases, someone mocks or undermines a sage, and in both cases the outcome is their death — one through *ayin hara*, one more directly. The narrative point seems clear: do not oppose or undermine rabbinic authority; God will vindicate the rabbis.

One student observed that the phrase *chaviva alei yekara de'malchuta* — the honor of kingship is dear to me — resonates with *hakarat hatov*, the gratitude and appreciation for legitimate authority that runs through the entire sugya. The recognition of earthly authority as a gift from divine sovereignty is not merely political accommodation but an expression of genuine gratitude for the order that makes communal life possible.

### Greatness Midrash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: הוֹאִיל וְאִתְעֲבִיד לִי נִיסָּא בְּהַאי קְרָא, דָּרֵישְׁנָא לֵיהּ: ״לְךָ ה׳ הַגְּדֻלָּה״ — זוֹ מַעֲשֵׂה בְרֵאשִׁית, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״עֹשֶׂה גְדֹלוֹת עַד אֵין חֵקֶר״. ״וְהַגְּבוּרָה״ — זוֹ יְצִיאַת מִצְרַיִם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּרְא יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת הַיָּד הַגְּדֹלָה וְגוֹ׳״. ״וְהַתִּפְאֶרֶת״ — זוֹ חַמָּה וּלְבָנָה שֶׁעָמְדוּ לוֹ לִיהוֹשֻׁעַ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּדֹּם הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ וְיָרֵחַ עָמָד וְגוֹ׳״. ״וְהַנֵּצַח״ — זוֹ מַפַּלְתָּהּ שֶׁל רוֹמִי. וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְיֵז נִצְחָם עַל בְּגָדַי וְגוֹ׳״. ״וְהַהוֹד״ — זוֹ מִלְחֶמֶת נַחֲלֵי אַרְנוֹן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עַל כֵּן יֵאָמַר בְּסֵפֶר מִלְחֲמֹת ה׳ אֶת וָהֵב בְּסוּפָה וְגוֹ׳״. ״כִּי כֹל בַּשָּׁמַיִם וּבָאָרֶץ״ — זוֹ מִלְחֶמֶת סִיסְרָא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִן שָׁמַיִם נִלְחָמוּ הַכּוֹכָבִים מִמְּסִלּוֹתָם וְגוֹ׳״. ״לְךָ ה׳ הַמַּמְלָכָה״ — זוֹ מִלְחֶמֶת עֲמָלֵק, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״כִּי יָד עַל כֵּס יָהּ״. ״וְהַמִּתְנַשֵּׂא״ — זוֹ מִלְחֶמֶת גּוֹג וּמָגוֹג, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״הִנְנִי אֵלֶיךָ גּוֹג נְשִׂיא רֹאשׁ מֶשֶׁךְ וְתֻבָל״. ״לְכֹל לְרֹאשׁ״ — אָמַר רַב חָנָן בַּר רָבָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֲפִילּוּ רֵישׁ גַּרְגִּיתָא מִן שְׁמַיָּא מַנּוּ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Sheila **said: Since a miracle was performed on my behalf with this verse** that I cited, **I will interpret it homiletically: Yours, O Lord, is the greatness; that is the act of creation, and so it says: "Who does great things past finding out"** (Job 9:10);  **And the power; that is the exodus from Egypt, as it is stated: "And Israel saw the great work** which the Lord did to the **Egyptians"** (Exodus 14:31); **And the glory; that is the sun and the moon that stood still for Joshua, as it is stated: "And the sun stood still, and the moon stayed,** until the nation had avenged themselves of their enemies" (Joshua 10:13); **And the triumph; that is the downfall of Rome, and so it says** describing the downfall of Edom, whom the Sages identified as the forefather of Rome: **"Their lifeblood is dashed against My garments** and I have stained all My raiment" (Isaiah 63:3); **And the majesty; this is the war of the valleys of Arnon, as it is stated: "Wherefore it is said in the book of the Wars of the Lord: Vahev in Sufa,** and the valleys of Arnon" (Numbers 21:14); **For all that is in the heaven and in the earth is Yours; this is the war of Sisera, as it is stated: "They fought from heaven, the stars in their courses** fought against Sisera" (Judges 5:20). **Yours is the kingdom, O Lord; this is the war of Amalek, and so it says:** "And he said: **The hand upon the throne of the Lord:** the Lord will have war with Amalek from generation to generation" (Exodus 17:16), as then God will sit on His throne. **And you are exalted; this is the war of Gog and Magog, and so it says: "I am against you, O Gog, the chief prince of Meshekh and Tubal"** (Ezekiel 38:3); and: **As head above all; Rav Ḥanan bar Rava said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** All leadership and authority, **even** the most insignificant, the **one responsible for distributing water, is appointed by heaven.**

*Rav Shila* then expounds the verse he had cited — *lecha Hashem hagedulah vehagevurah* — offering a grand historical sweep through which each attribute corresponds to a world-historical event. *Hagedulah* — greatness — corresponds to *ma'aseh bereshit*, the creation of the world, as the verse says: *oseh gedolot ad ein cheker*, He performs great things without limit. *Hagevurah* — power — corresponds to the Exodus from Egypt: *vayar Yisrael et hayad hagedulah asher asah Hashem beMitzrayim*. *Hatiferet* — glory — corresponds to the miracle of the sun and moon standing still for Yehoshua: *vayidom hashemesh veyareach amad*. *Hanetzach* — triumph — corresponds to the fall of Rome, drawing on the play between *netzach* and the word *nitzam*, their splattered lifeblood. *Hahod* — glory — corresponds to the war of the wadi of Arnon. *Ki chol bashamayim uva'aretz* — all that is in heaven and earth — corresponds to the war of Sisra, when *min hashamayim nilchamu kochavim mimesilotam*, the stars fought from their courses. *Lecha Hashem hamamlachah* — to You God is kingship — corresponds to the war against Amalek, for the verse says *ki yad al kes yah*, the hand upon the throne of God. *Vehamitnase* — and the exalted one — corresponds to the war of Gog and Magog, the eschatological battle, drawing on the word *nasi* in *hineni elecha Gog nasi*.

### Authority Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַתְנִיתָא תַּנָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: ״לְךָ ה׳ הַגְּדֻלָּה״ — זוֹ קְרִיעַת יַם סוּף. ״וְהַגְּבוּרָה״ — זוֹ מַכַּת בְּכוֹרוֹת. ״וְהַתִּפְאֶרֶת״ — זוֹ מַתַּן תּוֹרָה. ״וְהַנֵּצַח״ — זוֹ יְרוּשָׁלַיִם. ״וְהַהוֹד״ — זוֹ בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught in a *baraita* in the name of Rabbi Akiva:**  **Yours, O Lord, is the greatness; this is the splitting of the Red Sea;** **the power; this is the plague of the firstborn;** **the glory; this is the giving of the Torah;** **the triumph; this is Jerusalem;**  and **the majesty; this is the Temple.**

A parallel teaching is attributed in a *baraita* to *Rabbi Akiva*, who provides a more straightforward chronological sequence without extensive proof-texts: *hagedulah* is the splitting of the sea; *hagevurah* is the smiting of the firstborn; *hatiferet* is the giving of the Torah; *hanetzach* is Jerusalem; *hahod* is the *Beis HaMikdash*.

*Rav Chana bar Rav* said in the name of *Rav Yochanan*: even a *resh gargita* — the person in charge of drawing water from the well — holds his authority *min shamaya* — from heaven. Even the most minor functionary in any position of communal authority receives that mandate from the heavenly kingship. This is of a piece with the *bracha* over kings: *malchusa d'ar'a* in all its gradations reflects *malchusa d'rakia*.

A *baraita* in the name of *Rabbi Akiva* reinforces this: every clause of *lecha Hashem hagedulah* maps to a divine act in history, from creation to the messianic culmination.

## Houses & Cemeteries

### Jewish Homes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָרוֹאֶה בָּתֵּי יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּיִשּׁוּבָן, אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ … מַצִּיב גְּבוּל אַלְמָנָה״. בְּחוּרְבָּנָן, אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ דַּיַּין הָאֱמֶת״. בָּתֵּי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם בְּיִשּׁוּבָן, אוֹמֵר: ״בֵּית גֵּאִים יִסַּח ה׳״. בְּחוּרְבָּנָן, אוֹמֵר: ״אֵל נְקָמוֹת ה׳ אֵל נְקָמוֹת הוֹפִיעַ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: One who sees the houses of Israel inhabited** and tranquil **recites: Blessed…Who establishes the border of the widow.** One who sees them **in ruins he recites: Blessed…the true Judge.** One who sees **the houses of the nations of the world inhabited recites: "The Lord will destroy the house of the proud,** but He will establish the border of the widow" (Proverbs 15:25). **And** if he sees them **in ruins he recites: "God of vengeance, Lord, God of vengeance, shine forth"** (Psalms 94:1).

The *Gemara* now turns to *berachos* over houses. Upon seeing *batei Yisrael beyishuvan* — houses of Israel in their settled state — one recites *baruch matziv gevul almanah* — blessed is He who establishes the border of the widow. The *almanah* here is *Eretz Yisrael* herself, bereaved of her people, and the restored Jewish settlement reestablishes her boundaries. *Rashi* explains this in the context of the *yishuv* of the Second Temple period: seeing Jews reestablishing homes in the land.

The question of whether this *bracha* applies today is not simple. *Tosafot*'s principle that a constant, ambient phenomenon does not trigger a *bracha* would seem to apply. Yet surely during the waves of *aliyah* that rebuilt Jewish settlement in the land — the first *aliyah*, the second *aliyah*, and the subsequent reconstruction — this *bracha* would have been entirely appropriate upon witnessing the emergence of new Jewish communities where there had been none.

Upon seeing *batei Yisrael be'churbanam* — their ruins — one recites *baruch dayan ha'emes*, the standard acknowledgment of divine justice in the face of tragedy.

### Non-Jewish Homes

Upon seeing *batei ovdei kochavim* — non-Jewish homes — in their settled state, the *Gemara* says one recites *beit ge'im yisach Hashem* — the house of the haughty God will destroy — framing the flourishing of non-Jewish settlement as an occasion for a verse expressing hope for its eventual overthrow.

The contrast with the earlier *bracha* upon seeing *Markulis* is striking almost to the point of paradox. When one sees the idol itself — the very object of idolatrous worship — the *bracha* is one of patient divine tolerance: *shenatan erech apayim le'ovrei retzono*. But when one sees merely the *houses* of idolaters, the response is a verse calling for their destruction. The tolerance expressed toward the artifact of idolatry seems greater than that expressed toward the homes of its practitioners. One possible resolution is that the texts derive from different sources — one a *tanna'itic* teaching, another possibly a *Tosefta* — and represent different contexts or authors. Another possibility is that some manuscripts read *batei avoda zara* — actual houses of worship — rather than homes, which would bring the contrast into sharper relief, though even then the asymmetry remains surprising.

Upon seeing non-Jewish homes in their destruction, one recites *El nekamos Hashem El nekamos hofia* — God of vengeance, God of vengeance, appear — acknowledging that the destruction represents divine justice at work.

### Rav Chisda Mourns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא וְרַב חִסְדָּא הֲווֹ קָא אָזְלִי בְּאוֹרְחָא. כִּי מְטוֹ אַפִּתְחָא דְּבֵי רַב חָנָא בַּר חֲנִילַאי נְגַד רַב חִסְדָּא וְאִתְּנַח. אֲמַר לֵיהּ עוּלָּא: אַמַּאי קָא מִתְּנַחַתְּ? וְהָאָמַר רַב: אֲנָחָה שׁוֹבֶרֶת חֲצִי גּוּפוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַתָּה בֶן אָדָם הֵאָנַח בְּשִׁבְרוֹן מׇתְנַיִם וְגוֹ׳״. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר אַף כׇּל גּוּפוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָיָה כִּי יֹאמְרוּ אֵלֶיךָ עַל מָה אַתָּה נֶאֱנָח וְאָמַרְתָּ אֶל שְׁמוּעָה כִּי בָאָה וְנָמֵס כׇּל לֵב וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Ulla and Rav Ḥisda were once walking along the road when they came upon the doorway of the house of Rav Ḥana bar Ḥanilai. Rav Ḥisda groaned and sighed. Ulla asked him: Why are you sighing? Didn't Rav say: Sighing breaks half of one's body? As it is stated: "Sigh, therefore, you son of man; with the breaking of your loins"** (Ezekiel 21:11); sighing breaks a person down to his loins. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that sighing breaks **even** one's **entire body, as it is stated: "And it shall be, when they say unto you: Why are you sighing? That you shall say: Because of the tidings, for it comes; and every heart shall melt,** and all hands shall be slack, and every spirit shall be faint, and all knees shall drip with water" (Ezekiel 21:12).

The *Gemara* then turns to a poignant narrative. *Ulla* and *Rav Chisda* were traveling together and passed the door of the former home of *Rav Chana bar Chanilai*, which had fallen into ruin. *Rav Chisda* let out a long, heavy sigh. *Ulla* challenged him: why such a sigh? Doesn't *Rav* teach that *anacha shoveret chatzi gufo shel adam* — a deep sigh breaks half of a person's body — drawing from the verse *ha'anach be'shivron motnayim*, sigh with the breaking of the loins, the lower half of the body? And *Rabbi Yochanan* says it destroys the whole body, from the verse *ve'hayah ki yomru eilecha al mah atah ne'enach* — why do you sigh? — *ve'namas kol lev*, and every heart melted.

*Rav Chisda* answered: how could I not sigh? The house that once had sixty bakers working by day and sixty by night, baking for all who needed — whose master never removed his hand from his pocket, so that a poor person from a noble family would never have to stand and wait and be embarrassed while *Rav Chana* reached for his wallet — whose four doors were open to the four directions of the earth so that anyone could enter from any direction, hungry, and leave satisfied — that house also cast wheat and barley outside its windows during years of famine, so that those too embarrassed to ask for charity in daylight could retrieve it at night without anyone seeing them. That house has now fallen into a mound. *Ve'lo esnach* — and I should not sigh?

### Consolation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן — מִיּוֹם שֶׁחָרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ נִגְזְרָה גְּזֵירָה עַל בָּתֵּיהֶן שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים שֶׁיֶּחֶרְבוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּאׇזְנָי ה׳ צְבָאוֹת אִם לֹא בָּתִּים רַבִּים לְשַׁמָּה יִהְיוּ גְּדֹלִים וְטוֹבִים מֵאֵין יוֹשֵׁב״. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עָתִיד הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְהַחֲזִירָן לְיִשּׁוּבָן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שִׁיר הַמַּעֲלוֹת לְדָוִד הַבֹּטְחִים בַּה׳ כְּהַר צִיּוֹן״. מָה הַר צִיּוֹן עָתִיד הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְהַחֲזִירוֹ לְיִשּׁוּבוֹ, אַף בָּתֵּיהֶן שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים עָתִיד הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְהַחֲזִירָן לְיִשּׁוּבָן. חַזְיֵיהּ דְּלָא מְיַישְּׁבָא דַּעְתֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דַּיּוֹ לְעֶבֶד שֶׁיְּהֵא כְּרַבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Ulla **said to** Rav Ḥisda: You have nothing about which to sigh, as **Rabbi Yoḥanan said as follows: From the day that the Temple was destroyed a decree was issued upon the houses of the righteous that they would be destroyed, as it is stated: "Of a truth many houses shall be desolate, even great and fair, without inhabitant"** (Isaiah 5:9). **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** In the **future,** in the end of days, **the Holy One, Blessed be He, will restore them to their** original locations and their **inhabited state, as it is stated: "A Song of Ascents of David. They who trust in the Lord are as Mount Zion,** which cannot be moved but abides forever" (Psalms 125:1). From this verse one may infer that **just as in the future the Holy One, Blessed be He, will restore Mount Zion to its inhabited state, so too, in the future the Holy One, Blessed be He, will restore the houses of the righteous to their inhabited state,** so you have no reason to sigh. **Seeing that he was still not satisfied,** Ulla **said to him: It is enough for a servant to be like his master.** Since God leaves His home, the Holy Temple, in ruins, one should not be distraught over the destruction of the houses of the righteous.

*Ulla* offered consolation through a teaching of *Rabbi Yochanan*: *miyom shecharav Beis HaMikdash nigzera gezeira al bateihen shel tzaddikim sheyecharavu* — from the day the *Beis HaMikdash* was destroyed, a decree was issued that the houses of the righteous shall be destroyed. The verse is cited: *im lo vatim rabim leshama yihyu gedolim vetovim me'ein yoshev* — truly, great and good houses shall become desolate with no inhabitant. The very greatness and goodness of the house draws it into the destruction decreed in the wake of the *Churban*.

But *Rabbi Yochanan* also taught: *atid HaKadosh Baruch Hu lehachziran leyishuvam* — God will eventually restore them to their settled state — drawing from the verse *haboteichim baHashem ke'har tzion*, those who trust in God are like Mount Zion: *mehar tzion atid HaKadosh Baruch Hu lehachziro leyishuvo*, just as Mount Zion will be restored, so will the houses of the righteous.

Yet even this consolation did not settle *Rav Chisda*'s heart. *Ulla* then offered a second, more austere comfort: *dayo le'eved she'yehei k'rabo* — it is enough for the servant to be like his master. The *Beis HaMikdash* itself is destroyed; it would be unseemly for the houses of the righteous to stand in full glory while God's own house lies in ruins. The destruction of *tzaddikim*'s homes is not punishment but alignment — they share in the condition of the *Shechinah* in exile. The echo of *malchusa d'rakia* is felt here too: *ar'a demalchuta deshmaya* — the earthly condition of the heavenly kingship — even in its broken state, maps to something above.

### Jewish Graves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָרוֹאֶה קִבְרֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ … אֲשֶׁר יָצַר אֶתְכֶם בַּדִּין, וְזָן אֶתְכֶם בַּדִּין, וְכִלְכֵּל אֶתְכֶם בַּדִּין, וְאָסַף אֶתְכֶם בַּדִּין, וְעָתִיד לַהֲקִימְכֶם בַּדִּין״. מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבִינָא מְסַיֵּים בַּהּ, מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: ״וְיוֹדֵעַ מִסְפַּר כּוּלְּכֶם, וְהוּא עָתִיד לְהַחְיוֹתְכֶם וּלְקַיֵּים אֶתְכֶם. בָּרוּךְ … מְחַיֵּה הַמֵּתִים״. קִבְרֵי גוֹיִם, אוֹמֵר: ״בּוֹשָׁה אִמְּכֶם וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One who sees graves of Israel recites:**  **Blessed…Who formed you in judgment,** **and Who nourished you in judgment,** **and Who sustained you in judgment,** **and collected** your soul **in judgment,** **and in the future will raise you** from the dead **in judgment.** And **Mar, son of Ravina, concludes** the formula of this blessing **in the name of Rav Naḥman:** **And Who knows the number of you all,** **and Who in the future will restore you to life and sustain you.** **Blessed…Who revives the dead.** One who sees **graves of gentiles recites: "Your mother shall be sore ashamed,** she who bore you shall be confounded; behold, the hindermost of the nations shall be a wilderness, a dry land, and a desert" (Jeremiah 50:12).

Upon seeing *kivrei Yisrael* — Jewish graves — one recites an extended *bracha*: *baruch asher yetzar etchem bedin ve'zan etchem bedin vechilkel etchem bedin ve'asaf etchem bedin* — blessed is He who created you with justice, sustained you with justice, provided for you with justice, and gathered you in with justice. The same divine justice that governs life also governs death; this is a form of *dayan ha'emes* but situated within the full arc of a human life. *Mar* the son of *Ravina* adds a closing formulation in the name of *Rav Nachman*: *veyodeia mispar kulchem* — He knows the count of all of you, none forgotten — *vehu atid lehachayotchem ulekayem etchem*, and He will revive and establish you, concluding *baruch mechayei hameitim*. This full *bracha*, which includes *shem u'malchus* in the formulation of *mechayei hameitim*, is one that is actually practiced — it is long enough to warrant a formal *siyum*. The *nusach* of this *bracha* is found on this very *daf*, which has served at least one occasion as a practical reference when the text was not otherwise available.

### Non-Jewish Graves

Upon seeing *kivrei ovdei kochavim* one recites *boshah imchem* — your mother is shamed — the same verse cited earlier in response to non-Jewish masses.

### Friend After Time

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: הָרוֹאֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ … שֶׁהֶחֱיָינוּ וְקִיְּימָנוּ וְהִגִּיעָנוּ לַזְּמַן הַזֶּה״. לְאַחַר שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ, אוֹמֵר: ״בָּרוּךְ … מְחַיֵּה הַמֵּתִים״. אָמַר רַב: אֵין הַמֵּת מִשְׁתַּכֵּחַ מִן הַלֵּב אֶלָּא לְאַחַר שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נִשְׁכַּחְתִּי כְּמֵת מִלֵּב הָיִיתִי כִּכְלִי אוֹבֵד״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: One who sees his friend after thirty days** have passed since last seeing him **recites: Blessed…Who has given us life, sustained us and brought us to this time.** One who sees his friend **after twelve months recites: Blessed…Who revives the dead.** As **Rav said: A dead person is only forgotten from the heart after twelve months** have elapsed, **as it is stated: "I am forgotten as a dead man out of mind; I am like a lost vessel"** (Psalms 31:13), and with regard to the laws of lost objects, it is human nature to despair of recovering a lost object after twelve months (see *Bava Metzia* 28a).

*Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* teaches: one who sees a friend after thirty days recites *baruch shehecheyanu vekiyemanu vehigiyanu lazman hazeh* — the standard *shehecheyanu* blessing. *Tosafot* qualifies that this applies specifically to a friend one is genuinely happy to see. After twelve months of separation, one recites *baruch mechayei hameitim* — for who knows what may happen to a person in a year? The implication is that after twelve months without contact, a reunion carries something of the character of revival.

The practical question of whether these *berachos* are recited today is genuine. In an era of telephones, email, and near-constant contact, the conditions that generate both the joy of reunion and the concern for someone's welfare after extended separation are significantly attenuated. Most people today do not recite these *berachos*. *Rav Yoel Bin-Nun* has argued against the tendency — which he associates with an overly Litvish caution — to suppress every occasion for religious expression out of fear of *bracha levatala*. He is known to have recited *shehecheyanu* with *shem u'malchus* upon reunion with someone he had not seen for a long time.

*Rav* adds a related observation: a dead person is not forgotten from the heart until after twelve months — *ein hameit mishtakeach min halev ela achar shneim asar chodesh* — drawing from the verse *nishkachti kemeit milev hayiti kechli oved*, I have been forgotten like a dead person, like a lost vessel. The *Gemara* in *Bava Metzia* similarly holds that announcements for lost objects cease after a year; the psychological logic is the same. It is this fading from active memory that makes a reunion after twelve months feel like encountering someone returned from the dead, and thus warrants *mechayei hameitim*.

## Bar Chanina Encounter

### Greeting Blessings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא וְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הֲווֹ קָאָזְלִי בְּאוֹרְחָא. פְּגַעוּ בֵּיהּ בְּרַב חֲנִינָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: בַּהֲדֵי דַּחֲזֵינָךְ בָּרֵיכִינַן עֲלָךְ תַּרְתֵּי: ״בָּרוּךְ … אֲשֶׁר חָלַק מֵחׇכְמָתוֹ לִירֵאָיו״ וְ״שֶׁהֶחֱיָינוּ״. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֲנָא נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזִתִינְכוּ חַשֵּׁבְתִּינְכוּ עִלָּוַאי כְּשִׁיתִּין רִבְּווֹן בֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל, וּבָרֵיכְנָא עֲלַיְיכוּ תְּלָתָא: הָנָךְ תַּרְתֵּי, וּ״בָרוּךְ … חֲכַם הָרָזִים״. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: חֲכִימַתְּ כּוּלֵּי הַאי! יָהֲבִי בֵּיהּ עֵינַיְיהוּ, וּשְׁכֵיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Pappa and Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, were once walking along the road when they met Rav Ḥanina the son of Rav Ika. They said to him: When we saw you we recited two blessings over** meeting **you: Blessed…Who has shared of His wisdom to those who revere Him,** and: **Who has given us life…**as they had not seen him in over a month. **He said to them: I too, once I saw you, considered you in my eyes as** equivalent to **six hundred thousand** of the **house of Israel, and I recited three blessings over you. I recited those two** that you recited, as well as: **Blessed…Who knows all secrets,** which is the blessing recited upon seeing six hundred thousand Israelites. **They said to him: Are you all that clever? They fixed their gaze upon him and he died.**

The *Gemara* closes with a story that beautifully weaves together multiple threads of the entire discussion. *Rav Pappa* and *Rav Huna* the son of *Rav Yehoshua* were traveling together when they encountered *Bar Chanina* the son of *Rav Ika* from a distance. As they approached, they told him: upon seeing you from afar, we already recited two *berachos* for you — *baruch shechalak meichochmato lireiaiv*, that God shared His wisdom with those who fear Him, because you are such a distinguished Torah scholar; and *shehecheyanu*, because we had not seen you in so long.

### Response & Eye

*Bar Chanina* responded: I, upon seeing you, recited three *berachos*. Those two — *shehecheyanu* and *natan meichochmato lireiaiv* — and additionally *baruch chacham harazim*, the *bracha* recited upon a *uchlosa* of 600,000 Jews, because seeing you was of such significance to me that it was as though I had encountered all of *Klal Yisrael* gathered together.

*Amru lei* — they said to him — *chakimat kulei hai*, you are so clever! Perhaps it was too clever by half. *Yahavi beih einaiyu veshachiv* — they gave him the evil eye and he died.

The story is the perfect coda: it gathers the *bracha* of *shehecheyanu* on reunion, the *bracha* of wisdom on scholars, and the *bracha* of *chacham harazim* on the 600,000. And it ends, as both the *Rav Sheshet* and the *Rav Shila* episodes ended, with an *ayin hara* — reminding us, after a full day of elaborate and beautiful *berachos*, that the power attending those who wield such blessings is not to be trifled with.
