# Zevachim 78 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blood Mixtures

### Mishnah Introduction

The *mishna* at the bottom of 77b introduces a discussion of what happens when the blood of a sacrifice becomes mixed with other liquids — a topic that connects directly to the broader *sugya* of *bitul*, the halachic nullification of forbidden or otherwise problematic substances that occurs when they are mixed into a larger quantity of a permissible substance. The classic analogy from the laws of *kashrut* is a drop of *chazir* soup that falls into one's chicken soup. But in the context of our *mishna*, the situation is reversed: rather than wanting the admixture to lose its identity, we want it to retain its identity, because the blood of a *korban* must be present and detectable in order to be placed on the *mizbeach*.

### Dam b'Mayim

The *mishna* rules: *dam shenitarev bemayim* — if blood of a sacrifice becomes mixed with water — *im yesh bo mareh dam kasher*, if the mixture still has the appearance of blood, the blood is considered present and may be placed on the altar. The precise meaning of "appearance of blood" is somewhat ambiguous: does it mean that the blood is detectable through the reddish hue it lends the water, or that the mixture as a whole must actually resemble blood? The former reading seems more plausible given the context, though the *mishna* leaves it incompletely defined.

What is particularly striking here is the criterion employed. When it comes to food, the operative standard is *ta'am* — taste. If a forbidden substance can still be tasted in the mixture, it is considered present; if it cannot be tasted, it is *batel*. The principle of *ta'am ke-ikar*, that the taste of a forbidden thing counts like the thing itself, is well established. Yet here, in the domain of blood placed on the altar, the *mishna* applies a different standard altogether: *mareh*, appearance. The reason for this distinction is rooted in the nature of the act. Sight is our primary mode of perceiving and categorizing the world. When the question is whether something is present before us to be offered or used — as with blood on the altar or water for a *mikvah* — we rely on how it looks, not how it tastes. Only when dealing with foodstuffs, where the act of eating is central, does the sense of taste become the relevant criterion. This is an important general principle: appearance governs presence in the domain of ritual use; taste governs presence in the domain of consumption.

### Dam b'Yayin

The *mishna* continues: *nitarev beyayin* — if the sacrificial blood becomes mixed with wine. Here the difficulty is immediate. Wine is red and dark, and the blood can no longer be detected by sight within it. Yet the *mishna* rules: *ro'in oto ke'ilu hu mayim*, you look at the wine as if it were water. The blood is then assessed hypothetically: had this been water, would the blood have been visible? If yes, the blood is considered still present and may be placed on the altar.

This ruling is striking in two respects. First, it employs the legal fiction of *ro'in* — treating one substance as though it were something else — in order to reach a *lenient* result. In the normal laws of *kashrut*, the analogous device is used to be strict. If a drop of *treif* chicken soup falls into kosher chicken soup, the rabbis say: had this been *chazir*, it could be tasted — and therefore even though it cannot be tasted now, it is still considered present, rendering the mixture forbidden. That is a rabbinic stringency, imposed because the principle of *min b'mino* — like-with-like mixtures — would ordinarily permit *bitul* by majority at the biblical level. Here the *mishna* applies the same logical structure — "had this been a different medium, it would be detectable" — but arrives at a leniency: the blood remains present and the *korban* is valid.

The deeper implication is significant. This suggests that the concept of treating *min b'mino* as *eino mino* — looking at identical substances as though they were different — may have a biblical foundation, not merely a rabbinic one. If we can use *ro'in* to be lenient at the level of *korbanot*, it implies that the technique is grounded in something more fundamental than a rabbinic decree of strictness.

### Dam b'Dam

The *mishna* extends the same logic to the case of blood mixed with blood: *nitarav bedam behemah bedam chulin* — if the sacrificial blood becomes mixed with the blood of a non-sanctified animal, or *bedam hachayah*, the blood of a wild animal. Here too, one cannot visually distinguish the two, and the *mishna* rules *ro'ehu ke'ilu hu mayim*: you regard the mixture as if it were water, and assess whether the sacrificial blood would have been detectable under those conditions. If yes, it may be placed on the altar.

Logically, if we permit this analysis for blood-and-wine mixtures, we must permit it for blood-and-blood mixtures as well. The principle of *ro'in* applies regardless of the medium.

### Rabbi Yehudah's Din

At the top of 78a, Rabbi Yehudah introduces a further ruling: *ein dam mevatel dam* — blood does not nullify blood. It is important to understand the direction of this ruling. The *chachamim* hold that even by *dam b'dam* mixtures, there is a point at which the sacrificial blood becomes *batel* — when the proportion of non-sacrificial blood is so great that, hypothetically viewing the mixture as water, the sacrificial blood would no longer be visible. Rabbi Yehudah disagrees. He holds that since both components are genuinely blood — not merely similar in appearance, but identical in substance — there can be no *bitul* at all. The logic is that *bitul* requires the predominance of one type of thing over another. When you have wine and blood, you can say there is so much wine that there is no longer any blood here. But when you have blood and blood, you cannot say there is so much blood that there is no longer any blood — it is all blood, and therefore the sacrificial blood remains perpetually present.

This ruling of Rabbi Yehudah is, paradoxically, a leniency in the laws of *korbanot*, even though Rabbi Yehudah is characteristically stringent in the laws of *kashrut*. The underlying principle is the same in both cases — *min b'mino lo batel*, that like-with-like cannot nullify — but the practical consequence differs by context. In *kashrut*, this prevents a forbidden substance from becoming *batel*; in *korbanot*, it ensures the sacrificial blood is never considered absent.

Two additional cases complete the *mishna*. If the blood becomes mixed with *dam psulim*, the blood of an invalid sacrifice such as a *ba'al mum*, the whole mixture must be poured into the drain, *yishafech la'amah*, even though Rabbi Yehudah would otherwise hold the sacrificial blood to be ever-present. The blood of invalid sacrifices may not go on the altar under any circumstances. Similarly, *bedam hatamtzit* — blood that drains from an animal after the initial spurting, which is not halachically considered life-blood even if from a *korban* — the ruling is likewise to drain it away. Rabbi Eliezer is lenient regarding the *hatamtzit* case, and both the *mishna* and the *Gemara* note that *im lo nimlach venatan kasher*: if one did not inquire and went ahead and placed the blood on the altar, the *korban* is valid after the fact, since the principle that the sacrificial blood is not *batel* was not violated. The rabbis' restriction on placing such blood on the altar is thus a purely procedural concern, not a statement about the blood's presence.

## Chozer ve'Ni'ur

### Water vs. Blood

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that the ruling of the *mishna* — that blood mixed with water is valid if it still has the appearance of blood — applies specifically to the scenario *shenaflo mayim le-toch dam*: water falling into the blood. In that case, even if water is progressively added until the blood is barely visible, the blood is never *batel* so long as it retains some appearance of blood. But *aval nafal dam le-toch mayim* — if it was the blood that fell into the water, drop by drop — a different analysis applies. Each successive drop falls into a body of water so large that the drop is invisible and thus *batel*. When after many drops the accumulated blood finally renders the water visibly reddish, does the blood "come back"? Rabbi Yochanan holds that since each individual drop was *batel rishon rishon* as it fell in, those drops remain *batel*, and the eventual visible redness does not retroactively restore them.

It is important to note that this distinction does not conflict with the *mishna*, which is simply discussing what happens when a mixture of blood and water exists at a given moment, without specifying how the admixture came about. The *mishna*'s *pshat* is that all the liquid was mixed at once, in which case there is no question of sequence. Rabbi Yochanan is addressing a separate scenario of incremental addition.

### Reawakening Principle

This brings us to the principle of *chozer ve-ni'ur* — does nullification reverse when proportions shift? In general halacha, the answer is yes. If drops of *chazir* soup fall one by one into kosher chicken soup and are each initially *batel* due to the large proportion of kosher liquid, but accumulate until the *chazir* becomes detectable, the mixture becomes forbidden. The *chazir* "reawakens" once it reaches detectable proportions. This is the ordinary rule of *chozer ve-ni'ur*.

Why then does Rabbi Yochanan hold that by sacrificial blood, the *bitul* is not reversed when the mixture becomes reddish again? The answer lies in the principle of *dichuy*: once something in the domain of *korbanot* has been "pushed away" — rendered unfit — it cannot revert to fitness. The drops of blood that were *batel*, were in a state in which they could not be offered on the altar. They are *nidcha*. Even if the proportions later shift and the blood becomes visible again, the *dichuy* is irreversible in the context of sacrifices.

### Dichuy Exception

Rav Papa adds an important qualification: *ule-inyan kisui eino ken* — by the *mitzvah* of *kisui hadam*, covering the blood after slaughter, the same principle does not apply. If blood drips progressively into a puddle of water and the first drops are *batel* and invisible, but eventually enough blood accumulates to be visible, one may still perform *kisui hadam* on the mixture. Even though the first drops were temporarily *batel* and in a state where *kisui* could not have been performed on them, this does not constitute *dichuy* in the relevant sense. The reason is *she-ein dichuy be-mitzvos* — the concept of *dichuy* applies only to sacrifices, not to *mitzvot* generally. In the realm of *mitzvot*, there is no principle that once an object was temporarily unfit for a *mitzvah* it cannot later be used. Similarly, in the laws of *kashrut*, if drops of *chazir* soup accumulate until the *chazir* becomes detectable, the mixture becomes forbidden — the *chazir* has "reawakened" — because *dichuy* does not apply there either. The sole exception is *korbanot*, where *dichuy* is operative and once blood has been *batel* and *nidcha* it cannot be restored.

## Mixed Prohibitions

### Piggul/Notar/Tamei

Reish Lakish introduces a striking ruling: if one mixes together *piggul* (sacrificial meat rendered invalid through improper intention), *notar* (sacrificial meat left past its time), and *tamei* (ritually impure meat) — three distinct *issurim*, each carrying the penalty of *karet* — and then eats from the mixture, one is *patur*, exempt. The reason given is *ee efshar she-lo yarbeh min al chaveiro ve-yvatlenu*: it is impossible that one type of forbidden substance does not predominate over another and thereby nullify it. Since the three are roughly equal, whatever *issur* one focuses on, two-thirds of the mixture is not that particular *issur*, and two-thirds nullifies the one-third. The same logic applies to each of the three in turn, so none of them can be established as having been eaten in the requisite amount.

### Three Inferences

The *Gemara* derives three distinct principles — *shema mina telat* — from Reish Lakish's ruling.

The first and most fundamental: *issurin mevatlin zeh et zeh* — prohibitions can nullify one another. One does not need *heter*, permitted substance, to nullify *issur*. The standard paradigm of *bitul* imagines a large quantity of permitted material overwhelming a small amount of forbidden material. Reish Lakish tells us this is not a necessary condition. The analytical structure is simpler: we have *neveilah* and non-*neveilah*; since two-thirds of the mixture is non-*neveilah*, the *neveilah* is *batel*. The "non-*neveilah*" does not need to be kosher — it can itself be another *issur*. This is a significant *chiddush*.

The second inference: *noten ta'am berov lav de-oraita* — even if a substance imparts taste, as long as it is a minority it is nullified at the biblical level. This is the *Gemara*'s initial reading of the case. However, as we will see, the *Gemara* will walk this back: since *piggul*, *notar*, and *tamei* are all varieties of the same meat, they all taste identical, and the case is one of *min b'mino* where taste is irrelevant and majority alone governs. The real *chiddush* remains the first one.

The third inference: *hatra'at safek lo shemah hatra'ah* — a doubtful warning does not constitute a valid *hatra'ah* for purposes of lashes. This is where Rashi and Tosafot invest considerable effort in interpretation, because the case as described does not immediately present an obvious scenario of doubtful warning.

### Rashi's Approach

The way Rashi explains the *hatra'at safek* inference does not actually require three separate forbidden items — even two suffice. Imagine one places *piggul* and *notar* simultaneously into one's mouth and begins chewing. The question of which *issur* one is liable for depends on which occupies the majority of the chewed mass at any given moment. In the overlap — the region where the two are being masticated together — one of the two will be more prevalent and will nullify the other, meaning there is a full *kezayis* of the majority *issur* but not of the minority one. Since we do not know which is the majority in that overlap, we cannot specify for which *issur* a warning should have been issued. The warning is therefore inherently uncertain — a *hatra'at safek* — and does not count.

One might object, as Tosafot does, that here we know perfectly well the person is committing a punishable offense — we simply do not know which of the two *issurim* to pin on him. This is different from the classic case of *hatra'at safek* where there is genuine doubt about whether an *aveirah* is occurring at all. Tosafot therefore offers alternative explanations for the *Gemara*'s third inference. Leaving Tosafot's approach aside, the preferred reading is somewhat more metaphorical: just as one who commits an *aveirah* but cannot be warned for it escapes punishment because the *aveirah* cannot be definitively attributed to a specific act, here too the inability to attribute a specific *issur* to the person eating the mixture precludes liability.

## Taste vs. Majority

### Flour Mixture

Rava challenges the *Gemara*'s second inference — that majority alone suffices even when taste is imparted — from a *mishna* elsewhere. If one makes dough from a mixture of wheat flour and rice flour, *im yesh bah ta'am dagan chayeves be-challah*: if the wheat taste is still detectable, the dough is obligated in *challah* as though it were genuine bread, even if the majority of the mixture is rice flour. This demonstrates that taste, not majority, determines the presence of the wheat. How then can Reish Lakish imply that majority alone suffices?

The *Gemara* initially deflects: this rule requiring *challah* even from a rice-majority dough is only *miderabbanan* — a rabbinic stringency. Biblically, the wheat is *batel*. But the *Gemara* immediately challenges this from the *seifa* of that same *mishna*, which states that *adam yotzei bo yedei chovaso be-Pesach*: one may use this dough for *matzah* on Pesach. If the obligation of *matzah* is fulfilled, the wheat cannot be considered *batel* at the biblical level — the dough is genuinely considered wheat-based bread. This confirms that taste establishes biblical presence, and *ta'am ke-ikar de-oraita* is a genuine Torah principle.

### Min b'Mino Principle

The *Gemara* resolves the tension with a clean distinction: *min b'sheino mino beta'ama, min b'mino beruba* — when unlike substances are mixed, taste determines presence; when like substances are mixed, majority alone determines presence. Wheat flour and rice flour are unlike in taste; therefore the detectability of the wheat taste is the operative criterion. *Piggul*, *notar*, and *tamei* are all meat, identical in taste; therefore only majority governs, and taste adds nothing. There is, in truth, only a single underlying rule: a substance is present if it can be detected, where "detected" means tasted when the substances are unlike, and simply counted by proportion when they are alike. The apparent tension between Reish Lakish's case and the flour mixture dissolves entirely under this analysis.

This also retrospectively corrects the *Gemara*'s second inference. The ruling of Reish Lakish is not a *chiddush* that majority overrides taste even when taste is imparted. It is simply the ordinary rule for *min b'mino* mixtures, where taste is inherently irrelevant. The genuine *chiddush* remains the first inference: *issurim mevatlin zeh et zeh*.

## Ro'in Concept

### Ro'in Lenient Use

The *Gemara* now returns to examine the *ro'in* device employed by our *mishna* — the instruction to look at the wine mixed with blood as though it were water. This is a case where the *mishna* applies *ro'in* leniently: because the blood and wine look alike, one might expect *bitul* to operate, and yet the *mishna* says we treat the wine as water and assess whether the blood would still have been visible under those alternate conditions. If it would have been, the blood remains present and the *korban* is valid.

### Min b'Mino Tension

This raises an immediate difficulty. The *Gemara* has just established that for *min b'mino* mixtures, majority rules. But our *mishna* seems to say that even when two liquids look alike — blood in wine — we do not simply apply *bitul* by majority. Instead we apply *ro'in*, treating them as though they were unlike. Moreover, we do this to be *lenient*, not strict. If the *mishna* permits *ro'in* to be lenient — holding the blood present even when it cannot be seen — it would seem to imply that *ro'in* is a robust, generally applicable device. And if we use it to be lenient, surely we should use it to be strict as well — meaning that whenever two like substances are mixed, we should treat them as unlike and ask whether the forbidden substance would be detectable in a different medium.

The *Gemara* raises this challenge explicitly: *mai lav ro'in oto le-yayin k'ilu hu mayim* — does this not mean we look at the wine as if it were water, and treat the blood as still present? If so, the principle of *ro'in* works even to be lenient, and presumably also to be strict, which would seem to undermine the entire framework of *min b'mino* going by majority. The *Gemara* attempts to deflect by proposing the opposite reading — *lo*, we look at the blood as if it were water and it is immediately *batel* — but this reading is rejected as clearly contrary to the *mishna*'s *pshat*. The *Gemara* notes that if this were the *mishna*'s meaning, it would simply say the blood is *batel* without the language of *ro'in* at all. Furthermore, a *baraita* is cited in which Rabbi Yehudah explicitly uses *ro'in* in a way that preserves presence, confirming that *ro'in* is a device for holding something present, not for *bitul*.

The *Gemara* therefore concludes: *tannai hi* — this is a dispute between *Tannaim*. Whether *ro'in* is a valid operative principle — in any direction — is itself debated.

### White Wine Baraita

The *baraita* just referenced addresses the following case. A bucket contains white wine or milk. The bucket is immersed in a *mikvah* for purification. For the *tevilah* to be valid, the water of the *mikvah* must reach the interior of the vessel. But the vessel is filled with white wine or milk, which are indistinguishable in color from water. The question is whether the *mikvah* water that enters the vessel is considered the predominant substance.

The first opinion rules *holchin achar ha-rov* — we follow the majority. If the majority of the liquid inside the vessel is *mikvah* water, it is treated as ordinary water and the *tevilah* is valid. Since white wine and water look identical, there is no detectable difference, and majority alone governs — consistent with the *min b'mino* rule.

Rabbi Yehudah disagrees: *ro'in oto k'ilu yayin adom*, we look at the wine as though it were red wine. *Im dehah marehu kasher* — if the color of the mixture fades sufficiently (as if diluted red wine loses its color), the *tevilah* is valid; *ve-im lav pasul* — if not, it is invalid. Rabbi Yehudah here employs *ro'in* to be *strict*. Rather than accepting that the white wine is undetectable and simply applying majority, he asks: had the wine been red, would it still be detectable? If yes, we treat it as still present, and the *tevilah* has not been achieved.

This use of *ro'in* to be strict is familiar from the laws of *kashrut*: had this been a different (detectable) kind of forbidden substance, it would be present, and therefore we treat it as present now. The *chiddush* in our *mishna* was that *ro'in* is applied in the opposite direction, to be lenient. What the *Gemara* now says is that this is a dispute: Rabbi Yehudah holds of *ro'in* as a general operative principle — he applies it here to be strict. The *Gemara* accordingly proposes that our *mishna*, which applies *ro'in* to be lenient, also reflects Rabbi Yehudah's position, since *ro'in* for Rabbi Yehudah is a general tool, not limited to one direction. The *chachamim*, who reject *ro'in* in the *baraita* and simply follow majority, would presumably also reject *ro'in* as a leniency.

## Ro'in Debate

### Baraita Sources

A further *baraita* is cited to develop Rabbi Yehudah's position. Rabbi Yehudah says in the name of Rabban Gamliel: *ein dam mevatel dam* — blood does not nullify blood; *ein rok mevatel rok* — spit does not nullify spit (as in the case of the spit of a *zav* mixed with that of a *tahor* person); *ve-ein mei raglayim mevatlin mei raglayim* — urine does not nullify urine (as in the case of a *zav*'s urine mixed with a *tahor* person's urine). The principle articulated is *min b'mino lo batel* — like-with-like is never nullified. This is attributed to Rabbi Yehudah's rebbe, Rabban Gamliel.

### Abaye's Resolution

The *Gemara* observes that within the relevant *mishnah* about the immersion of vessels, there appears to be an internal tension. One clause seems to reflect the *ro'in* principle of Rabbi Yehudah, while another clause — regarding *mei chatat* mixed with *mikvah* water — rules that majority suffices. Abaye resolves this: *ha didei ha de-rabbei* — one ruling reflects Rabbi Yehudah's own position, and the other reflects the position of his rebbe, Rabban Gamliel. The *ro'in* device belongs to the rebbe's tradition; the ruling that majority suffices is Rabbi Yehudah's own view.

### Rashi's Explanation

Rashi explains the relationship between Rabbi Yehudah's *ro'in* and his rebbe's *min b'mino lo batel* as follows. When the *baraita* teaches that blood, spit, and urine do not nullify their like, that reflects the genuine case of *min b'mino* — identical substances — where there can be no *bitul* at all. When Rabbi Yehudah applies *ro'in* in the case of white wine and water, or in the case of blood mixed with wine, those are technically *eino mino* cases — the substances are genuinely different, and *bitul* would ordinarily be possible. But Rabbi Yehudah, coming from a tradition that fundamentally resists *bitul*, applies the same resistance even in cases that merely *look* like *min b'mino* — because the visual similarity makes them behave analogously. Since all these liquids appear uniform, Rabbi Yehudah imports the *min b'mino lo batel* logic of his rebbe's tradition and treats them accordingly — asking not "is the minority detectable now?" but "would it be detectable in a different medium?" And this resistance to *bitul* applies regardless of whether the result is a stringency or a leniency: Rabbi Yehudah resists *bitul* as a general posture, and the practical consequence depends on the context. In *kashrut*, resisting *bitul* means the forbidden substance remains present; in *korbanot*, resisting *bitul* means the sacrificial blood remains present and usable.

By contrast, where Rabbi Yehudah does allow majority to govern — the case of *mei chatat* — this represents his own position, independent of his rebbe's tradition, applicable to a case where the circumstances genuinely warrant it.

### Rava's Alternative

Rava offers a different resolution that does not require invoking the rebbe-versus-own-position framework. He says: *bidli she-tocho tahor ve-gabbo tamei askinan* — we are dealing with a bucket that is *tamei* only on the outside and *tahor* on the inside. This arises because *tum'at mashkin*, the *tum'ah* of liquids, is of rabbinic status and can in certain circumstances cause a vessel to become *tamei* only on its external surface, not internally. Since the *mikvah* water needs to reach only the exterior of the vessel to effect *tevilah*, technically no *mikvah* water need enter the interior at all. Why then does the *mishna* require a majority of *mikvah* water inside? Because the rabbis were concerned — *dilma chayis aleihu ve-lo matbil lei* — that one might be reluctant to fully submerge a vessel filled with precious *mei chatat* water, and might not immerse it completely. To counter this tendency, the rabbis imposed the requirement that *mikvah* water enter and predominate inside the bucket. But since this is only a rabbinic precaution, the *Gemara* concludes: *ve-keivan de-ika ribuya lo tzarich* — once the requirement to introduce *mikvah* water inside is operative, majority suffices. Since technically no *mikvah* water is needed inside at all, there is no reason to require more than a majority once the rabbis imposed the obligation.

According to Rava, then, Rabbi Yehudah's position throughout is consistently one of *ro'in*: he always views like substances through the lens of their differences in order to resist *bitul*. The reason *ro'in* is not applied to the *mei chatat* case has nothing to do with whose tradition is being invoked, but rather with the technical halachic fact that the inside of the vessel need not be immersed at all. Since the *mikvah* water's entry into the vessel is only a rabbinic safeguard rather than a genuine requirement, Rabbi Yehudah does not deploy *ro'in* to strengthen it further.
