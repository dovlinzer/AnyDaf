# Arakhin 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Vital Organ Representati…

### Kevodo Chiddush

We pick up near the bottom of 4b, in the middle of the *drasha* concerning *tumtum* and *androgynous* — those of indeterminate gender — and their status in *arachin*. The *gemara* had established that they can be *maarich* (the subject who vows a valuation) but not *ne'erach* (the object of such a valuation). The question arose: why would one have thought to exclude them even from a simple *neder* (vow of market value)? After all, a person can vow the market value of a tree, and the tree's gender is plainly irrelevant.

The *gemara*'s answer is that the *chiddush* here is something more subtle. From the *parsha* of *arachin* we learn that even if you do not explicitly mention the whole person — even if you say only their heart, their lungs, or some other vital organ, referred to as *kevodo* — that designation is understood to represent the entire person. This principle is derived from *arachin* and extended to apply in cases of *neder* as well: if you vow to pay the value of someone's heart, you owe the full value of that person. One might have thought that the concept of *kevodo* — a vital organ representing the whole — is limited to those who fall within the formal category of *arachin*, thereby excluding a *tumtum* and *androgynous*. This *drasha* teaches otherwise: the principle that a vital organ represents the entirety applies to everyone when reckoning market value, not only to those formally within the *arachin* framework.

### Braita Contradiction

With that established, the *gemara* turns directly to challenge the principle itself. The preceding *amud* quoted an extensive *braita* stating that if someone says "the head of this slave" or "the head of this donkey I am donating to the *Beit HaMikdash*," it does not represent the whole — rather, it is as though the *Beit HaMikdash* and the donor are partners in the animal, each owning half. So which is it? Does a vital organ, when donated, represent the entirety or only a partial share?

The *gemara* sharpens the contradiction. One might think the distinction turns on whether the subject is formally within *arachin* — animals are not, humans are. But the *gemara* rejects this framing immediately: even by a slave, who *is* within the category of *arachin* (and for whom, therefore, the principle of *kevodo* should clearly apply), this same *braita* says that "the head of this slave" creates only a partnership, not full ownership by the *Beit HaMikdash*. The problem therefore is not about who falls within *arachin*; it is a direct contradiction between the principle of *kevodo* and the *braita*'s rule of partial ownership.

### Direct Cases Resolved

The *gemara* offers its first resolution: *lo kashya* — there is no difficulty. The distinction is between *kodshei mizbe'ach* (sanctity for the altar) and *kodshei bedek ha-bayit* (sanctity for the Temple treasury). Intuitively, one might have expected the reverse resolution: a *korban* is defined entirely by its identity as an offering, so it is either everything or nothing, and therefore a vital organ sanctified for a *korban* should represent the whole. Property and ownership, by contrast, can be divided, so one might have expected that designating a vital organ for the *Beit HaMikdash* treasury creates only partial ownership. The *gemara*, however, says the opposite. When designated as a *korban* for the altar, the vital organ represents only a part, yielding a partnership. When donated directly to the Temple treasury, the vital organ represents the whole.

The *gemara* does not leave the altar-side of this resolution unchallenged. It focuses on the *braita*'s case of a cow: if you say "the head of this cow is *hekdesh*," it literally means the head — because people actually sell the head of a cow separately in the market, so the word "head" here is meant literally, not as a representative designation for the whole animal. The *gemara* asks: but if we are saying that by a *korban* the head yields only a partnership, why is the effect limited to literally just the head? If the logic of *korban* sanctity is that it is all-or-nothing in nature, then designating a vital organ for a *korban* should cause the sanctity to spread throughout the entire animal — *tifshot kedusha bekulah*.

To demonstrate this, the *gemara* cites a *braita* (found on 89a): if someone says "the leg of this animal is an *olah*," one might think this makes the entire animal an *olah*. The verse teaches otherwise: *kol asher yiten mimenu la-Hashem yiheyeh kodesh* — only that which you give from it is holy, not the entirety. So a non-vital organ does not extend its sanctity to the whole. But what is done with an animal that now has one leg consecrated as an *olah*? The animal cannot simply be freed to non-sacred use, since the verse says *yiheyeh* — it retains its status. The resolution offered: sell the animal to someone who needs to bring an *olah* — say, a fellow who already has an *olah* obligation. He acquires the animal, sanctifies it in its entirety as an *olah*, and brings it. He pays the seller for everything except the leg, since the leg was not the seller's to sell; the money received for the rest is non-sacred. This is the position of Rabbi Meir.

Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon read the verse differently: *kol asher yiten mimenu la-Hashem yiheyeh kodesh* means that even a partial designation causes the whole to be sanctified. An *olah* is definitional — like a state that either fully obtains or does not. You cannot be partially an *olah*, just as one cannot be partially in a certain condition. Either the animal is a *korban* or it is not.

The *gemara* then synthesizes: even according to the view that partial designation does not spread to the whole, that is only when the designated limb is a non-vital organ — the leg. But if it is a vital organ, *davar she-neshamah teluyah bo*, all agree that the sanctity spreads to the entire animal. So we are back to the same difficulty: by a direct *korban*, a vital organ sanctifies the whole; by a direct donation to the *Beit HaMikdash*, a vital organ represents the whole. Where, then, does the *braita* fit — the one that says designating a vital organ yields only a partnership?

### Hybrid Category

The *gemara* provides the resolution: *lo kashya* — the distinction is not between *mizbe'ach* and *bedek ha-bayit*, but between *kedushat ha-guf* (intrinsic sanctity, where the thing itself is used) and *kedushat damim* (value sanctity, where the thing is sold and the proceeds are used). These pairs are often assumed to be synonymous — *kedushat mizbe'ach* with *kedushat ha-guf*, and *kedushat bedek ha-bayit* with *kedushat damim* — but they do not in fact coincide perfectly.

*Kedushat ha-guf* means the thing itself is being used, whether as a *korban* or as some direct donation like wood for the altar or a vessel for Temple service. *Kedushat damim* means the thing is sold and the money is used — whether donated to the Temple treasury or designated toward purchasing a *korban*. A third category emerges: *kedushat damim le-mizbe'ach* — I sanctify this animal (or this car, or this slave) such that the proceeds of its sale will go to fund a *korban*. This is neither pure *kedushat ha-guf* (the thing itself becomes a *korban*) nor pure *kedushat bedek ha-bayit* (a straight donation to the treasury). It is a hybrid.

The *gemara*'s answer: when the donation is direct to the Temple treasury, a vital organ represents the whole. When it is a direct *korban*, a vital organ represents the whole. But when it is this hybrid middle category — the value of the vital organ designated toward a *korban* — that is the one case in which the *braita* applies: you and the *Beit HaMikdash* are partners, and the vital organ does not represent the entirety. The logic of this middle category is admittedly difficult to articulate clearly. The *gemara* itself does not offer a conceptual explanation for why this intermediate position behaves differently from each of its two pure analogues.

### Blemished Animal Test

Abaye challenges Rava, who had articulated the above position. Rava himself had elsewhere ruled that if someone tries to sanctify a male, unblemished animal for its *damim* — meaning the proceeds should go toward an *olah* — the animal automatically receives an upgrade: it is treated as sanctified directly as a *korban* with *kedushat ha-guf*, since that is where it is headed anyway. If so, there is no middle category to speak of: whenever you try to sanctify a fit animal for the proceeds to go toward a *korban*, it immediately becomes an outright *korban*. You cannot inhabit the hybrid space at all.

The *gemara* offers a preliminary answer: the distinction is between sanctifying the whole animal and sanctifying only a single organ. If you designate only a vital organ toward a *korban*, we will not perform the double translation — first treating the organ as representing the whole, and then upgrading the whole from *kedushat damim* to *kedushat ha-guf*. We perform only one translation at a time. But Abaye pushes back: Rava himself asked in *Temurah* whether sanctifying a vital organ for its *damim* toward a *korban* undergoes the automatic upgrade. He raised it as an open question, not as a settled ruling.

The *gemara* then supplies the decisive answer: the automatic upgrade applies only to an unblemished animal, *betam*, one that is itself fit to become a *korban*. Here, in the cases we are discussing — a donkey, a slave, or a blemished animal — there can be no upgrade, since the thing cannot itself become a *korban*. The *braita* that established the partnership rule was precisely using such examples: the case of the donkey, the cow, the slave. In those cases, where the animal or person cannot be directly sanctified as a *korban*, and where you are therefore necessarily in the hybrid space of *kedushat damim le-mizbe'ach*, the vital organ does not represent the whole, and the result is a partnership.

This also clarifies the geometry somewhat: a direct donation to the *Beit HaMikdash* — even if it is just a check — gives the full value of the vital organ as the whole person's value. A direct *korban* sanctification, where the vital organ triggers *kedushat ha-guf*, gives the whole animal. But when neither upgrade is possible — you have something that cannot be a *korban* itself, and you want the *damim* to go toward a *korban* — you are in this middle space where only a partnership results.

### Unresolved Hybrid Cases

Before concluding, the *gemara* raises Rava's question (from *Temurah*) in its original form: *meirosh li le-gabei mizbe'ach mahu* — if I say "the value of my head toward the altar," do we reckon the full value of the person (*niddon bi-khvodo*) or only a partial value? Rava had noted the tension: in cases of pure monetary valuation (*damim*), we find that *kevodo* always applies, the organ representing the whole. But in cases directly for the *mizbe'ach*, we have never found *kevodo* applying in the hybrid sense. The question was left as *teiku* — unresolved. The *gemara* now clarifies: that question was asked before this *braita* was brought to bear. Now that the *braita* has been cited, we know the answer: this is precisely the hybrid case, and the result is a partnership, not full valuation.

The *gemara* then presents two further unresolved hybrid cases. Rava (or Rabbah — there is a textual question about the name) asks: *erchi alai le-gabei mizbe'ach mahu* — if someone says "my *erech* upon me toward the altar," what is the law? The word *erech* invokes the Torah's fixed valuation system, which was instituted for donations to the Temple treasury. What happens when someone tries to use that system but directs it toward a *korban*? Rava frames the specific question in terms of *haseig yad* — the Torah's sliding scale provision for *arachin*, allowing someone who cannot afford the fixed value to pay a reduced amount. Does this hybrid — *erech* directed toward the *mizbe'ach* — carry the sliding scale rules of *arachin*? Or, since contributions to the *mizbe'ach* are never on a sliding scale, do we apply the rule of a *korban* and require the exact fixed amount? *Teiku* — no answer.

Finally, Rav Ashi asks: *hikdish sdei achuza le-gabei mizbe'ach* — if someone sanctifies his *sdei achuza* (inherited ancestral field, whose redemption is governed by the Torah's fixed rules in the *parsha*) not for the Temple treasury but with the proceeds designated for *korbanot*, what is the law? Does he get the benefit of the Torah's special fixed redemption formula (*beit zera chomer se'orim ba-chamishim shekel kesef*), since this is after all a *sdei achuza*? Or, since contributions to the *mizbe'ach* are always redeemed at market value, does that rule apply instead? *Teiku* — no answer.

These unresolved cases are an almost natural outgrowth of the *gemara*'s recognition that *kedushat damim le-mizbe'ach* exists as a category at all. Once the *gemara* allows that one can sanctify something for its monetary value to be used toward a *korban* — rather than only sanctifying the thing directly — it opens the door to these hybrid constructs, combining the rules of one system (*arachin*, *sdei achuza*) with the destination of another (the *mizbe'ach*). The *gemara* leaves these configurations without resolution.

## Arachin Categories

### Less Than Month Old

With the complex *sugya* of vital organs set aside, the *gemara* returns to more straightforward questions of *erchin*. The next *mishna* states: *pachot mi-ven chodesh nidar aval lo ne'erach* — an infant less than one month old does not fall within the Torah's *arachin* framework (which begins at one month old with fixed valuations), but such an infant is *nidar*: if someone says "I will pay the market value of this infant," he is obligated to pay whatever the market value is.

### Erech Reinterpretation

A *braita* introduces a complication: what if you used the wrong word? You said *ha-maareich pachot mi-ven chodesh* — you used the word *erech*, the technical term for the Torah's fixed valuation, in reference to an infant less than one month old for whom no fixed *erech* exists. Rabbi Meir says: *noten damav* — you pay the market value. The Rabbis say: *lo amar klum* — you said nothing; there is no *erech* for this infant, so your statement is legally void and you owe nothing.

The *gemara* asks: what is the logic of the dispute? Rabbi Meir holds *ein adam motzi devarav le-vatala* — a person does not utter meaningless statements. Therefore, even if you said *erech*, since you presumably know that there is no *erech* for an infant under one month, what you really intended was market value (*damim*). The Rabbis hold that people do sometimes say things without halakhic content, and we interpret your words as literally as possible: you said *erech*, there is no *erech* here, you owe nothing.

Tosafot notes an important dimension to Rabbi Meir's position. One might think that Rabbi Meir is simply reinterpreting your words as a policy matter. But Tosafot emphasizes that this is connected to the broader principle operative in *arachin* and *hafla'ah* more generally — we care about your *da'at*, your underlying intent, not merely the words spoken. Rabbi Meir is therefore prepared to interpret the word *erech* contextually, giving it the meaning of *damim*, because we are attentive to what you were trying to achieve. Tosafot further notes that this is easier to apply here than in a case of outright error. If someone intended to say one thing and said something completely different — a complete mismatch between thought and speech — the statement is void entirely. But here it is not a complete error; it is a question of contextual meaning. You said *erech* in a context where *erech* cannot operate, and Rabbi Meir holds that in such a case we translate the term into the closest operative equivalent: *damim*.

### Vessel Erech

The *gemara* then asks: *ke-man azla ha de-amar Rav Gidel amar Rav* — who does the following ruling follow? Rav, citing Rav Gidel, taught: *ha-omer erech kli alai noten damav* — if someone says "the *erech* of this vessel is upon me," he pays its market value. A vessel does not appear anywhere in the Torah's *arachin* charts — the charts are for persons. So one might say that "the *erech* of my car" is legally meaningless, yielding zero. Rav says no: he pays market value. This clearly follows Rabbi Meir.

But the *gemara* asks: is this obvious? Perhaps even the Rabbis would agree here. The Rabbis might hold that by an infant under one month, the person genuinely made a mistake — he knew the word *erech* has a technical meaning and honestly thought that the charts extended to infants under a month. Given that error, his statement can be voided. But by a vessel, no one could seriously think that the Torah's fixed valuation charts apply to a car. Therefore, the Rabbis would agree that in this case, the person certainly knew there is no *erech* for a vessel and intended to obligate himself in the market value. The *gemara* rejects this: no, the Rabbis maintain consistently that we go by what you literally said, and if *erech* has no technical application, you said nothing. It is Rabbi Meir alone who makes the principled claim that *ein adam motzi devarav le-vatala* and interprets the term contextually to mean market value, whether by an infant or by a vessel.

### Ani's Animal

The *gemara* proceeds to a further case. *Ke-man azla ha de-amar Rav Gidel amar Rav* — the ruling that if someone says "I am sanctifying my fellow's animal," he must pay its value to the *Beit HaMikdash*. One cannot sanctify another person's animal — it is not his to sanctify. Yet Rav says the statement obligates him in the animal's market value. This clearly follows Rabbi Meir.

The *gemara* asks: didn't we already know this from Rav's earlier ruling about a vessel? Why do we need an additional ruling about another's animal? The *gemara* answers: one might have thought those cases are different. In the vessel case, the person knows with certainty that there is no *erech* for a vessel and consciously substituted market value for the inapplicable term. In the animal case, there is a more plausible alternative interpretation: the person may be saying, "if I can persuade the owner to sell it to me, I will sanctify it and bring it as a *korban*." We might therefore not interpret his statement as an immediate monetary obligation to the *Beit HaMikdash* but as a contingent intention to purchase and then sanctify. The *chiddush* is that Rabbi Meir goes even further: he interprets the statement to create a present, binding obligation to pay the animal's value to *hekdesh*, even though this is a significant stretch from the words "I am sanctifying another's animal." Rabbi Meir's principle that statements must be given halakhic meaning is applied even here, where the reinterpretation is not a simple contextual translation but a substantial extension of what was said.

### Alai Requirement

Rav Ashi qualifies the ruling: *ve-hu de-amar alai* — the rule that one who sanctifies another's animal must pay its value applies only if he included the word *alai* ("upon me"). Rashi explains the relevant formulation as *behemat chaveiro zu alai* — "my fellow's animal is upon me." The word *alai* creates a hook: even if we cannot give the statement the meaning of a direct sanctification (since the animal is not his), we can read *alai* as expressing a personal obligation. Without *alai*, however — if he simply said *harei zu* ("behold, this animal is a *korban*") — that is too far a stretch. A declaration that this specific animal is a *korban*, with no language of personal obligation, cannot be reinterpreted to mean a monetary obligation to pay its market value. Thus even Rabbi Meir's expansive principle has its limits: the initial statement must contain some linguistic foothold on which the reinterpretation can rest.

To summarize the arc of this *sugya*: Rabbi Meir's principle begins with the straightforward case of *erech* misapplied to an infant — a simple contextual reinterpretation of a technical term. It extends to a vessel — a slightly greater stretch since vessels are entirely absent from the *arachin* framework. It extends further to another's animal, where the reinterpretation moves from the vocabulary of sanctification to an obligation in monetary value. And it is qualified at last by Rav Ashi's requirement that the word *alai* be present to anchor the reinterpretation to something actually expressed.

## Non-Jews in Arachin

### Mishnah Dispute

The next *mishna* takes up the status of non-Jews (*ovdei kochavim*) in *arachin*. The *parsha* of *arachin* opens with the words *dabeir el Bnei Yisrael* — speak to the Children of Israel — which could suggest the entire system is limited to Jews. But separately, the *parsha* uses the word *ish* (a man, any man), which is inclusive. The question is how to distribute these two signals.

Rabbi Meir rules: a non-Jew is *ne'erach* — he can appear on the valuation charts as the object of an *erech* — but he is not *maarich*, meaning he cannot himself obligate in an *erech*. Rabbi Yehuda rules the reverse: a non-Jew is *maarich* — he can undertake the obligation — but he is not *ne'erach*, meaning he cannot be the object of an *erech*.

### Agreement Zone

Both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda agree, however, that *she-nodrim ve-nidrim* — non-Jews can obligate themselves through regular *nedarim* (vows of market value) and can be the object of such vows as well. The entire dispute is confined to the technical category of *erech* with its fixed Torah charts. Outside that narrow framework — for ordinary donations, market-value vows, and the like — non-Jews participate fully on both sides.

### Meir Analogy

The *braita* elaborates each position. Rabbi Meir explains why he allows the non-Jew to be *ne'erach* but not *maarich*: *mipnei she-ribah kasuv ba-ne'erachim yoseir mi-ba-maarichin* — the Torah's charts include a wider class of people as objects than as subjects. Specifically: a *cheireish*, *shoteh*, and *katan* — a deaf-mute, a mentally ill person, and a minor — all appear on the charts as objects (*ne'erachim*) but cannot themselves be subjects (*maarichin*), since they lack the requisite legal competence. Since the category of *ne'erach* is broader, we extend it to include non-Jews; since the category of *maarich* is narrower, we exclude non-Jews from it.

### Yehuda Analogy

Rabbi Yehuda makes the opposite argument: *mipnei she-ribah kasuv ba-maarichin yoseir mi-ba-ne'erachim* — there are people who can be subjects without appearing on the charts. Specifically: a *tumtum* and *androgynous* — persons of indeterminate gender — can be *maarichin* (they can obligate themselves in an *erech*) but are not *ne'erachim* (they do not appear on the charts, since the charts require determinate gender). Therefore the category of *maarich* is broader, and we extend it to non-Jews; the charts are more restrictive, and we exclude non-Jews from them. Rabbi Yehuda reasons that the Torah's fixed valuation charts are formal and exclusive — they exclude those of indeterminate gender, and by the same logic they exclude non-Jews — whereas the capacity to make an *erech* obligation is more open, extending to those of indeterminate gender and therefore to non-Jews as well.

### Rava Evaluation

Rava offers a striking evaluation: *hilchata de-Rabbi Meir mistabra, teimeh lo mistabra* — the legal ruling of Rabbi Meir (that non-Jews are *ne'erachim* but not *maarichin*) is correct, but his reasoning is unpersuasive. And conversely: *teimeh de-Rabbi Yehuda mistabra, ve-hilchesa lo mistabra* — Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning is more logically compelling, but his ruling does not follow.

Why is Rabbi Meir's reasoning weak? Because his comparison to *cheireish*, *shoteh*, and *katan* is inapt. Those individuals are excluded from being *maarichin* because they lack *da'at* — the legal and cognitive capacity to make binding decisions. A non-Jew possesses full *da'at*; the analogy therefore does not hold. Why is Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning stronger? Because his comparison to *tumtum* and *androgynous* is apt — these are persons of full competence who nonetheless appear on one side of the *arachin* framework and not the other, demonstrating that the Torah can allocate these categories asymmetrically without reference to cognitive capacity.

### Ezra-Nehemiah

Yet Rava rules like Rabbi Meir's *psak* — that non-Jews cannot be *maarichin* — on entirely different grounds. A verse from *Ezra-Nechemia*: *lo lachem va-lanu livnot beit Elokeinu* — "it is not for you and for us to build the house of our God." When the non-Jews of the land offered to participate in rebuilding the *Beit HaMikdash*, Nechemia refused, out of concern about the influence they might exert over the project. This demonstrates that there is some principled reservation about non-Jewish contributions to the *Beit HaMikdash*, even if the reservation is not absolute.

As Tosafot notes, this reservation is not total: the *mishna* itself states that non-Jews can make *nedarim* and donate market value to the *Beit HaMikdash*. But where the Torah is excluding non-Jews from *some* category of involvement in the *arachin* system, the *Ezra-Nechemia* story gives us reason to locate that exclusion on the giving side — they cannot be *maarichin* — rather than on the receiving side. This reasoning, external to the internal logic of the *arachin* charts, is what actually supports Rabbi Meir's *psak*, not his analogy to *cheireish*, *shoteh*, and *katan*. We will continue with this discussion on the following *daf*.
