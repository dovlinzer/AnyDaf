# Arakhin 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kedusha Rishona/Shniya

### Cities Listed in Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: לָמָּה מָנוּ חֲכָמִים אֶת אֵלּוּ, שֶׁכְּשֶׁעָלוּ בְּנֵי הַגּוֹלָה מָצְאוּ אֵלּוּ וְקִידְּשׁוּם, אֲבָל רִאשׁוֹנוֹת בָּטְלוּ מִשֶּׁבָּטְלָה קְדוּשַּׁת הָאָרֶץ. קָסָבַר: קְדוּשָּׁה רִאשׁוֹנָה קִידְּשָׁה לִשְׁעָתָהּ, וְלֹא קִידְּשָׁה לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei,** says: **Why did the Sages count** specifically **these** cities as those walled since the days of Joshua, son of Nun? They counted them **because when the exiles ascended** to Eretz Yisrael from Babylonia, **they discovered these** cities **and sanctified them; but** the sanctity of the **first** walled cities **was nullified when the sanctity of the land was nullified** and the Jewish people were exiled. The Gemara notes: Apparently, Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, **holds** that the **initial consecration** of Eretz Yisrael in the days of Joshua **consecrated** it **for its time,** until the exile, **but did not consecrate** Eretz Yisrael **forever.**

Today's daf is 33a, the penultimate daf of the masechta. We pick up, however, at the very bottom of 32b. The *mishna* had listed the cities governed by the laws of *batei arei chomah* — houses in walled cities — and those cities were primarily ones mentioned in *Ezra Nechemiah*. This raises an obvious question: what about all the cities from the time of *Yehoshua*? What is the explanation for this restricted list?

### Rabbi Ishmael's Braitas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: אָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: וְכִי אֵלּוּ בִּלְבַד הָיוּ? וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״שִׁשִּׁים עִיר כׇּל חֶבֶל אַרְגּוֹב... כׇּל אֵלֶּה עָרִים בְּצוּרוֹת״! אֶלָּא לָמָּה מָנוּ חֲכָמִים אֶת אֵלּוּ? שֶׁכְּשֶׁעָלוּ בְּנֵי הַגּוֹלָה מָצְאוּ אֵלּוּ וְקִידְּשׁוּם. קִידְּשׁוּם? הָא אָמְרִינַן דְּלָא צְרִיךְ לְקַדּוֹשִׁינְהוּ! אֶלָּא מְנָאוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But raise a contradiction** from another *baraita*: **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, said: And were these** cities enumerated in the mishna the **only** walled cities from the days of Joshua? **But isn't it already stated:** "There was not a city that we took not from them; **sixty cities, all the region of Argob…all these cities were fortified with high walls,** gates, and bars" (Deuteronomy 3:4–5)? **Rather, why did the Sages** specifically **count these** cities? They counted them **because when the exiles ascended** from Babylonia **they discovered these and sanctified them.** The Gemara interjects: Can the *baraita* really mean that they **sanctified them? But we say** later in the same *baraita* that **it is not necessary to sanctify** them. **Rather,** the *baraita* means that **they** found these cities and **counted them** in the mishna.

The *gemara* had presented two *braitos* in the name of Rabbi Ishmael b'Rabbi Yose. The first holds that the *mishna*'s list is merely a sample, and of course the full roster from the time of *Yehoshua* is included. The second holds that the list is essentially complete — or nearly so — because the operative framework derives from the time of *Ezra*, not *Yehoshua*.

### Kedusha Frameworks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא אֵלּוּ בִּלְבַד, אֶלָּא כֹּל שֶׁתַּעֲלֶה לְךָ מָסוֹרֶת בְּיָדְךָ מֵאֲבוֹתֶיךָ שֶׁמּוּקֶּפֶת חוֹמָה מִימוֹת יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן — כׇּל מִצְוֹת הַלָּלוּ נוֹהֲגוֹת בָּהּ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁקְּדוּשָּׁה רִאשׁוֹנָה קִידְּשָׁה לִשְׁעָתָהּ וְקִידְּשָׁה לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And not only these; rather,** with regard to **any** city for **which you receive a tradition from your ancestors that it is surrounded by a wall from the days of Joshua, son of Nun, all these mitzvot** of walled cities **are observed in it, due to** the fact that the **initial consecration** of Eretz Yisrael **consecrated** it **for its time and consecrated** it **forever.** Evidently, Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, holds that the initial consecration of Eretz Yisrael is eternal.

These two positions reflect a fundamental *machlokes*. Do we go by the first *kedusha* of *Eretz Yisrael* from the time of *Yehoshua* — *kedusha rishona kidsha l'sha'ata v'kidsha l'atid lavo*, meaning the first sanctification was effective both for its time and for all future time — or do we say that *kedusha* lapsed at the destruction of the *Beis Hamikdash* and we go by the second *kedusha* established at the time of *Ezra*, following the position that *kedusha rishona lo kidsha l'atid lavo*?

### Sanctity of Place

Beyond its importance as a general *machlokes*, what is fascinating here is that this dispute is being translated not merely into terms of soil and agricultural sanctity, but into issues of *makom* — the sanctity of place itself. The *gemara* speaks of cities being formally sanctified as *arei chomah*, which bears some resemblance to the *kedusha* of *Yerushalayim* and *kedushas hamikdash*. This is a striking dimension of the concept.

### Megilla Parallels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: תְּרֵי תַּנָּאֵי וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בַּר יוֹסֵי אַמְרַהּ, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בַּר יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: ״אֲשֶׁר לוֹא חוֹמָה״ — אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ עַכְשָׁיו וְהָיָה לוֹ קוֹדֶם לָכֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **If you wish, say** that this is a dispute between **two *tanna'im*, and** they disagree **with regard to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yishmael,** son of Rabbi Yosei. **And if you wish, say** instead that **one** of the *baraitot*, specifically the second one, was actually **said** by **Rabbi Elazar bar Yosei. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Elazar bar Yosei says:** Since the verse states: **"Which has [*lo*] a wall,"** with *lo* written with an *alef*, according to which the verse may also be taken to mean: Which does not have a wall, this indicates that **even if** a city **does not have** a wall **now, but it had** a wall **before,** in the era of Joshua, son of Nun, it retains its status as a walled city.

This also has significant bearing on *Megilla*. Why does *masechta Megilla* connect the status of *mukaf chomah* to *mukaf chomah mi'yemos Yehoshua bin Nun*? One explanation is that *Chazal* generally prefer to employ prefabricated *halakhic* models — if a *halakha* needs to be articulated, it is conceptually cleaner to apply an existing model, even if it is not a perfect fit. A similar phenomenon appears at the beginning of *Gittin*, which uses the geographic definition of *Eretz Yisrael* established for *trumos u'maasros* when discussing the transmission of a *get*, even though that particular definition is not strictly germane to the *gittin* question.

### Megilla & Purim

There is, however, a deeper dimension to the *Megilla* connection that deserves attention — and since *Purim* is approaching, it bears mention. The *gemara* in *Megilla* suggests that tying *megilla* reading to cities *mukaf chomah* from the time of *Yehoshua* is a way of giving *kavod* to *Eretz Yisrael*. The whole problem of *megilla* is that it commemorates a *nes* that occurred *b'chutz la'aretz*. Perhaps, then, the choice of connecting *megilla* to *mukaf chomah mi'yemos Yehoshua* is not only about the historical period of *Yehoshua* per se, but about drawing on the model of the *kedusha* of *batei arei chomah* — finding, at the very moment when everything is centered on exile and *chutz la'aretz*, a way to anchor the celebration in the *kedusha* of *Eretz Yisrael*. Of course, this is complicated by the fact that the law also applies to cities in *chutz la'aretz* that were *mukaf chomah mi'yemos Yehoshua bin Nun*, such as *Shushan* — but the connection is nonetheless striking.

## Reinheritance Question

### Pesach Inheritance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹמֵר: ״וֶהֱבִיאֲךָ ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ אֶל הָאָרֶץ אֲשֶׁר יָרְשׁוּ אֲבוֹתֶיךָ וִירִשְׁתָּהּ״, מַקִּישׁ יְרוּשָּׁתְךָ לִירוּשַּׁת אֲבוֹתֶיךָ — מָה יְרוּשַּׁת אֲבוֹתֶיךָ בְּחִידּוּשׁ כׇּל דְּבָרִים הַלָּלוּ, אַף יְרוּשָּׁתְךָ בְּחִידּוּשׁ כׇּל דְּבָרִים הַלָּלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** so it **says** with regard to the return of the Jews from exile: **"And the Lord your God will bring you into the land that your fathers possessed, and you shall possess it"** (Deuteronomy 30:5). The verse **compares your possession to the possession of your fathers: Just as the possession of your fathers** came **with the renewal of all these matters,** i.e., the Sabbatical Year and the Jubilee Year, and *terumot* and tithes, **so too your possession** comes **with the renewal of all these matters,** as the initial consecration was nullified.

The *gemara* now challenges the position that everything follows the *kedusha* from the time of *Yehoshua*. A verse in *Devarim* states: *asher yarshu avosecha v'yirashta* — the land that your fathers inherited, you will inherit. This seems to imply that the return from exile constitutes a new act of inheriting the land — in other words, a resanctification. How can one maintain that everything flows from the original *kedusha* of *Yehoshua* if the verse describes a fresh inheritance upon return?

### Repossession Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ? דִּבְעוֹ רַחֲמֵי עַל יֵצֶר דַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וּבַטְּלֵיהּ, וְאַגֵּין זְכוּתָא עֲלַיְיהוּ כִּי סוּכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** the *tanna* who maintains **the other** opinion, that the initial consecration of Eretz Yisrael is eternal, how does he interpret the verse in Nehemiah? The Gemara answers that when the verse states: "For since the days of Joshua," this is not referring to actual *sukkot*; rather, the verse means **that** Ezra **prayed for mercy with regard to** the evil **inclination of idol worship and nullified it, and the merit** of his prayer **protected them like a *sukka*.** 

The *gemara* resolves this with a careful reading: *kivan d'yarshu avosecha yarashta at* — once your fathers inherited it, you have already inherited it. The verse does not describe a new inheritance at all. Rather, returning from exile means repossessing the inheritance of your forefathers. You are not *reinheriting*; you are reclaiming what was already yours. The question of whether the operative *kedusha* is the *kedusha rishona* or *kedusha shniya* is precisely the question of which framework governs that repossession.

## Shemita/Yovel Status

### Yovlos Nullified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מָנוּ שְׁמִיטִּין וְיוֹבְלוֹת? הַשְׁתָּא מִשֶּׁגָּלוּ שֵׁבֶט רְאוּבֵן וְשֵׁבֶט גָּד וַחֲצִי שֵׁבֶט מְנַשֶּׁה בָּטְלוּ יוֹבְלוֹת, עֶזְרָא דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ ״כׇּל הַקָּהָל כְּאֶחָד אַרְבַּע רִבּוֹא אַלְפַּיִם וְשֵׁשׁ מֵאוֹת וְשִׁשִּׁים״ הֲוָה מָנֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* cited earlier teaches that the Jews began counting the Jubilee Year upon their return from exile. The Gemara asks: **But did they count Sabbatical** Years **and Jubilee** Years in the days of Ezra? **Now,** if **from** the time **that the tribe of Reuben and the tribe of Gad and half the tribe of Manasseh were exiled** (see I Chronicles 5:26) the counting of **Jubilee** Years was **nullified,** despite the fact that a majority of Jews lived in Eretz Yisrael, then in the time of **Ezra,** about **which it is written: "The whole congregation together was 42,360"** (Ezra 2:64), **would** they have **counted** Jubilee Years?

The *gemara* now asks: *u'mi manu shmitin v'yovlos* — is it really true that they counted *shmitos* and *yovlos* during the second *Beis Hamikdash*? According to the *braita* that holds they resanctified the land in the time of *Ezra* and restarted the *shemita* and *yovel* cycle, the implication is that such counting was indeed in effect during the second *Beis Hamikdash*. But this requires scrutiny.

The *gemara* notes — as was discussed on the previous daf — that already when the tribes of *Reuven*, *Gad*, and half of *Menashe* were exiled by *Sancherev*, even before the exile of the remaining tribes of *Eretz Yisrael* proper, the *yovlos* were nullified: *batlu yovlos*.

### Kol Yoshveha Aleha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: מִשֶּׁגָּלוּ שֵׁבֶט רְאוּבֵן וְשֵׁבֶט גָד וַחֲצִי שֵׁבֶט הַמְנַשֶּׁה בָּטְלוּ יוֹבְלוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּקְרָאתֶם דְּרוֹר בָּאָרֶץ לְכׇל יֹשְׁבֶיהָ״, בִּזְמַן שֶׁכֹּל יוֹשְׁבֶיהָ עָלֶיהָ, וְלֹא בִּזְמַן שֶׁגָּלוּ מִקְצָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **From** the time **that the tribe of Reuben and the tribe of Gad and half the tribe of Manasseh were exiled,** the counting of **Jubilee** Years was **nullified, as it is stated: "And you shall proclaim liberty throughout the land to all its inhabitants;** it shall be a Jubilee for you" (Leviticus 25:10), indicating that the *halakhot* of the Jubilee Year apply only **when all its inhabitants are in** Eretz Yisrael, **and not when some of them have been exiled.**

The *gemara* proceeds to the *drasha* that underlies this principle. The verse states: *u'krasem dror ba'aretz l'chol yoshveha* — you shall proclaim liberty throughout the land to all its inhabitants. The operative phrase is *l'chol yoshveha*: *yovel* applies only *b'zman she'kol yoshveha aleha* — when all its inhabitants are upon it. This means when the Jewish people as a whole, each tribe in its proper ancestral portion, fully possesses the land. If even a fraction of the tribes has been exiled, *yovel* is no longer *noheig*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל הָיוּ עָלֶיהָ וְהֵן מְעוֹרָבִים, שֵׁבֶט בִּנְיָמִין בִּיהוּדָה וְשֵׁבֶט יְהוּדָה בְּבִנְיָמִין, יְהֵא יוֹבֵל נוֹהֵג? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לְכׇל יֹשְׁבֶיהָ״ — בִּזְמַן שֶׁיּוֹשְׁבֶיהָ כְּתִיקּוּנָן, וְלֹא בִּזְמַן שֶׁהֵן מְעוֹרָבִין!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought that if all the Jews **were** living **in** Eretz Yisrael, **but they are intermingled,** e.g., **the tribe of Benjamin** is living **in** the portion of the tribe of **Judah, and the tribe of Judah in** the portion of the tribe of **Benjamin,** that the **Jubilee** Year **should be in effect.** Therefore, **the verse states: "To all its inhabitants,"** which teaches that the Jubilee Year applies only **when its inhabitants** are living **according to their** proper **arrangment, and not when they are intermingled.** How, then, could those who returned from exile have counted the Jubilee Years?

A further *she'ela* arises: perhaps if all twelve tribes were present but intermixed — not dwelling in their ancestral tribal allotments — *yovel* would still apply? The verse therefore adds *yoshveha*, indicating that they must dwell upon it as they should, *k'tikunan*, each in the proper place. *Yovel* requires not merely the presence of all tribes but their proper, ordered settlement.

### Ezra's 40,000

Given this standard, the *gemara* asks how *yovel* could have been instituted at the time of *Ezra*. The verse records that *kol ha'kahal k'echad* — the entire community that made the ascent from *Bavel* — numbered forty-two thousand six hundred and sixty persons. If *yovel* was already nullified by the loss of the Trans-Jordan tribes, and if even a partial exile suffices to suspend it, how could forty thousand returnees — representing at most two tribes, *Yehuda* and *Binyamin* — have reinstated *yovel*?

### Yovel for Shemita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מָנוּ יוֹבְלוֹת לְקַדֵּשׁ שְׁמִיטִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: They counted Jubilee** Years in order **to sanctify Sabbatical** Years. That is, at the end of every seven cycles of the Sabbatical Year they would count the fiftieth year as a Jubilee Year, so that the next Sabbatical cycle would begin in its proper time, in the fifty-first year. Nevertheless, the *halakhot* of the Jubilee Year were not in effect.

Rav Papa bar Yitzchak resolves this: *manu yovlos l'kadesh shemita* — they did not practice *yovel*, but they designated *yovel* years in order to sanctify *shemita*. *Shemita* does not require the presence of all twelve tribes; it requires a valid *kedusha* of the land and a properly calibrated agricultural cycle. So while the practices of *yovel* were not observed, the *beis din* — the same central authority that sets *Rosh Chodesh* and *ibbur* — designated the fiftieth year as the *yovel* year. This was a calendrical act, not a substantive one. The year was named and counted as *yovel* without the associated *yovel* practices being observed.

This framework requires clarification on two levels. First, can *shemita* exist at all without *yovel*? Second, if the *yovel* year is not designated, does the cycle collapse because one ends up with forty-nine-year cycles rather than fifty-year cycles, throwing off the entire *shemita* count? The *gemara* here seems to be working from the premise that you need *yovel* at least to maintain the integrity of the calendar — the fifty-year cycle — in order for *shemita* to remain properly aligned.

The conceptual structure being proposed is thus: there can be a formal designation of a *yovel* year by the *beis din* without the actual *yovel* practices, similar to how the *beis din* sets *Rosh Chodesh* without any agricultural consequence. This designation has *halakhic* significance as a calendrical act even when the substantive laws of *yovel* are not operative.

## 50-Year vs. 49-Year

### Rabbis' 50-Year

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְרַבָּנַן דְּאָמְרִי: שְׁנַת חֲמִשִּׁים אֵינָהּ מִן הַמִּנְיָן, אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּאָמַר: שְׁנַת חֲמִשִּׁים עוֹלָה לְכָאן וּלְכָאן, לְמָה לִי? בִּשְׁמִיטִּין סַגִּיא! הָא וַדַּאי דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **This works out well according to the Rabbis, who say** that the **fiftieth year is not** included **in the counting** of the cycle of Sabbatical Years. It was therefore necessary to count the Jubilee Year. **But according to Rabbi Yehuda, who says** that the **fiftieth year is counted for here and for there,** i.e., it is both the fiftieth year of the previous cycle and the first year of the subsequent cycle, **why do I** need for them to count the Jubilee Years? If the Jubilee Year is not in effect, then it is **enough to** count the **Sabbatical** Years. The Gemara explains: **That** *baraita* **is certainly not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.**

The *gemara* refines Rav Papa's answer by distinguishing between the positions of the *rabbanan* and Rabbi Yehuda on the structure of the *yovel* cycle. The *rabbanan* hold that *shenat chamishim eina min haminyan* — the fiftieth year does not count as year one of the next cycle. There is thus a genuine fifty-year cycle: forty-nine working years, then the *yovel* year, then the new cycle begins with year one. Under this view, the designation of the *yovel* year matters enormously for the calendar, because without it one would count seven-year *shemita* cycles within an incorrect framework.

### Rabbi Yehuda 49-Year

Rabbi Yehuda holds that *shenat chamishim ola lechan u'lechan* — the fiftieth year counts as both the last year of the old cycle and the first year of the new one. There is therefore no break year between cycles; the cycle is effectively forty-nine years. Under Rabbi Yehuda's view, the designation of a *yovel* year has no calendrical significance, since the count proceeds on seven-year cycles regardless. The *gemara* therefore challenges Rav Papa's answer: according to Rabbi Yehuda, why would anyone bother designating *yovel* years if they do not practice *yovel* and the *yovel* designation has no effect on the calendar? Could they not simply observe *shemita* without any reference to *yovel*?

### Shemita Independence

The *gemara* responds that Rav Papa's answer is indeed not consistent with Rabbi Yehuda's view. According to Rabbi Yehuda, if one is not practicing *yovel*, there is no reason to designate the year at all — the seven-year *shemita* cycles proceed independently. The resolution the *gemara* offers is simply that Rav Papa's position aligns with the *rabbanan* and not with Rabbi Yehuda.

### Shemita Today

This discussion yields an important implication: the *gemara*'s treatment here strongly suggests that *shemita* does not require *yovel* to be operative. Under the *rabbanan*, the only reason to designate a *yovel* year is calendrical — to maintain fifty-year cycles. But the practice of *shemita* itself does not appear to require *yovel*. Under Rabbi Yehuda there is not even a calendrical reason needed, and *shemita* proceeds entirely independently.

This has direct bearing on the question of whether *shemita* today is *d'Oraisa* or *d'rabbanan*. *Tosafos*, based on a *gemara* in *Gittin* discussing *Hillel*'s *takana* of *pruzbul*, raises the question of how *Hillel* could enact a *takana* that circumvents even a nominal *mitzvah min haTorah*. The *gemara* there seems to suggest that the relevant obligations were already only rabbinic in force. Some *Rishonim* read that discussion to mean that without operative *yovel*, *shemita* — or at least certain aspects of it such as the nullification of debts — does not apply *d'Oraisa*. Others read the *gemara* differently, limiting the rabbinic character specifically to *shmiyas kesafim*, the release of monetary debts, which is conceptually most parallel to *yovel*'s land-reversion provisions, while holding that the agricultural restrictions of *shemita* retain biblical force. This is a question with direct practical relevance — Rav Kook's *heter mechira* rested in part on the assumption that *shemita* is only *d'rabbanan* today.

One further point: this *gemara* implies that during the second *Beis Hamikdash* they designated *yovel* years and therefore observed fifty-year cycles. Yet the *Rambam* states that today we operate on forty-nine-year cycles. The question of exactly when and how this shift occurred — from the fifty-year framework of the second *Beis Hamikdash* period to the forty-nine-year framework of today — is itself an open question, compounded by the *Rambam*'s famous reliance on *mesorah* from the time of the *Geonim* to establish the current *shemita* year. The *Rishonim* are not fully in agreement on when exactly the year falls.

## Yovel in First Temple

### Jeremiah & Slaves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא מָנוּ שְׁמִיטִּין וְיוֹבְלוֹת? וְהָכְתִיב: ״מִקֵּץ שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים תְּשַׁלְּחוּ אִישׁ אֶת אָחִיו הָעִבְרִי אֲשֶׁר יִמָּכֵר לְךָ״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did they not count Sabbatical** Years **and Jubilee** Years once the tribes of Reuben and Gad and half the tribe of Manasseh were exiled? **But isn't it written** that Jeremiah, who lived many years later, said: **"At the end of seven years you shall let go every man his brother that is a Hebrew, that has been sold unto you,** and has served you six years, you shall let him go free from you; but your fathers did not listen unto Me, nor inclined their ear" (Jeremiah 34:14)?

The *gemara* now asks a different question: can one really say that *shmitin v'yovlos* were not kept even toward the end of the first *Beis Hamikdash* period? A verse from *Yirmiyahu* — speaking at the end of the first *Beis Hamikdash* — records him exhorting the people: *tishalchu ish et achiv ha'ivri asher yimacher lach* — send free the Hebrew slave. The formulation in *Yirmiyahu* is *miketz sheva shanim*, at the end of seven years.

### Six vs. Seven Years

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָוֵינַן בְּהוּ: ״מִקֵּץ שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים״? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וַעֲבָדְךָ שֵׁשׁ שָׁנִים״! וְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: שֵׁשׁ לַנִּמְכָּר, וְשֶׁבַע לַנִּרְצָע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And we discussed this** verse: Why does it state: **"At the end of seven years** you shall let go every man"? **But isn't it written** in the same verse: **"And has served you six years"? And Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says:** The verse is referring to two separate individuals. The phrase "and has served you **six** years" is referring **to** a typical Hebrew slave who **was sold,** who goes free after six years (see Exodus 21:2), **and** the phrase "at the end of **seven** years" is referring **to** a slave whose ear **was pierced** with an awl to extend his tenure. Such an individual remains a slave until the Jubilee Year, even if that is the following year, i.e., the end of the seventh year. If so, the verse indicates that the Jubilee Year was in effect in the times of Jeremiah, even though the tribes of Reuben, Gad, and half the tribe of Manasseh were exiled.

This raises an immediate textual difficulty. The verse elsewhere states *v'avadcha shesh shanim* — the slave works six years and is freed at the beginning of the seventh. What then does *Yirmiyahu* mean by commanding release at the end of seven? Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves this: *shesh l'nimkar v'sheva l'nirtza* — six years applies to the ordinary case of a sold slave, while seven years applies to the slave who had his ear pierced and thus extended his service beyond the initial six. The earliest the *yovel* could free such a slave is year seven of his service. *Yirmiyahu* is thus referring to the case of the *nirtza* slave who is freed at *yovel*, which could fall in year seven. Reading the verse this way implies that *yovel* was operative during *Yirmiyahu*'s time — long after *Sancherev*'s exile of the Trans-Jordan tribes.

### Sarcastic Rebuke

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא בְּתוֹכָחָה כְּתִיב, וְקָאָמַר נָבִיא: הֲשִׁלַּחְתֶּם? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וַיִּשְׁמְעוּ וַיְשַׁלֵּחוּ״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **That** verse was not stated by Jeremiah in the form of a command; rather, it **is written in** the form of **reproof.** That is, the **prophet** Jeremiah is **saying** to the Jewish people: **Did you send** free the pierced slaves when the Jubilee Year was observed? The Gemara asks: **But isn't it written** there: **"And they listened and let them go"** (Jeremiah 34:10)? Evidently, the entire passage is referring to matters occurring in the present.

The *gemara* deflects this implication by proposing that *hahu b'tochecha ksiv* — that verse is written as *mussar*, as a rebuke. *Yirmiyahu* was not issuing a practical directive about what the people should currently be doing. Rather, he was sarcastically reproaching them, asking whether they had ever fulfilled this *mitzvah* in the past. The exhortation was about their prior failures, not a command for the present.

### They Listened

The *gemara* immediately challenges this reading: *v'ha ksiv vayishmeu vayishallachu* — but the verse says they listened to him and actually released the slaves. This demonstrates that *Yirmiyahu* was speaking about something they could and did act upon in the present, not merely lamenting the past. The rebuke interpretation cannot be sustained in light of their actual response.

### 12 Tribes Restored

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: יִרְמְיָה הֶחְזִירָן, וְיֹאשִׁיָּה בֶּן אָמוֹן מָלַךְ עֲלֵיהֶן. וּמְנָא לַן דַּהֲדוּר? דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי הַמּוֹכֵר אֶל הַמִּמְכָּר לֹא יָשׁוּב״. אֶפְשָׁר יוֹבֵל בָּטֵל, וְנָבִיא מִתְנַבֵּא עָלָיו שֶׁיִּבְטַל? אֶלָּא מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהֶחְזִירָן יִרְמְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** Indeed, the Jubilee Year was not in effect once the tribes of Reuben, Gad, and half the tribe of Manasseh were exiled. But **Jeremiah brought back** all the exiled tribes, **and Josiah, son of Amon, ruled over them. And from where do we** derive **that** Jeremiah **brought them back? As it is written: "For the seller shall not return to that which he has sold"** (Ezekiel 7:13). Ezekiel prophesied that there will come a time when fields will not be returned to the owners in the Jubilee Year. Now, is it **possible** that the **Jubilee** Year had already been **nullified and** yet **the prophet is prophesying that it will be annulled** in the future? **Rather,** this **teaches that Jeremiah brought back** the exiled tribes.

The *gemara* therefore accepts that *yovel* was indeed *noheig* at the end of the first *Beis Hamikdash* period and turns to reconcile this with the earlier principle that *yovel* requires *kol yoshveha aleha*. The answer, offered in the name of Rav Nachman, is both bold and striking: *Yirmiyah hechziran v'Yoshiyahu ben Amon malach aleihem* — *Yirmiyahu* undertook a special mission and gathered representatives of all twelve tribes, bringing them back to settle in their ancestral lands, and *Yoshiyahu* ruled as king over all twelve tribes.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָלַן דְּיֹאשִׁיָּה מָלַךְ עֲלֵיהֶן? דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר מָה הַצִּיּוּן הַלָּז אֲשֶׁר אֲנִי רוֹאֶה, וַיֹּאמְרוּ אֵלָיו אַנְשֵׁי הָעִיר הַקֶּבֶר אִישׁ הָאֱלֹהִים אֲשֶׁר בָּא מִיהוּדָה וַיִּקְרָא אֶת הַדְּבָרִים עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ בֵּית אֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where do we** derive that **Josiah ruled over** all **the** ten tribes exiled by Assyria? **As it is written:** "And as Josiah turned himself, then he spied the sepulchres…**then he said: What monument is that which I see? And the men of the city told him: It is the tomb of the man of God who came from Judah and proclaimed** these **things** that you have done **against the altar of Bethel"** (II Kings 23:16–17).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מָה טִיבוֹ שֶׁל יֹאשִׁיָּהוּ בְּבֵית אֵל? אֶלָּא כְּשֶׁהֶחְזִירָן יִרְמְיָהוּ, יֹאשִׁיָּהוּ מָלַךְ עֲלֵיהֶם. רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״גַּם יְהוּדָה שָׁת קָצִיר לָךְ בְּשׁוּבִי שְׁבוּת עַמִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Now, what connection does Josiah,** king of Judah, **have with the altar at Bethel,** a city in the kingdom of Israel? **Rather,** this indicates that **when Jeremiah brought back** the ten tribes, **Josiah ruled over them. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** The fact that the tribes returned may be derived **from here: "Also, O Judah, there is a harvest appointed for you, when I would return the captivity of My people"** (Hosea 6:11). That is, when the ten tribes will return from their captivity, a king of Judah will rule over them.

Some *Rishonim* hold that one does not need a majority of each tribe — a small representative group from each tribe is sufficient. The proof that *Yirmiyahu* accomplished this comes from *Yechezkel*, who, writing after the *churban*, says: *ki hamocher el hamimkar lo yashuv* — the seller will no longer return to what he sold. This implies that before the *churban*, sellers did return to their ancestral land — that *yovel* was operative. As the *gemara* notes, it would be strange for a *navi* to lament the passing of something that had already been nullified since the time of *Sancherev*. Therefore, *yovel* must have been reinstated, which establishes that *Yirmiyahu* must have restored representatives of all twelve tribes.

Evidence that *Yoshiyahu* ruled over those beyond *Yehuda* and *Binyamin* comes from a verse recording that *Yoshiyahu* traveled through *Beis El* — a city belonging to the kingdom of *Yisrael*, not *Yehuda*. His presence there would be inexplicable unless he had governance over those territories. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak adds a further proof from *Hoshea*: *gam Yehuda shat katzir lach b'shuvi shevut ami* — the *drasha* reading *katzir* as *katzin*, a ruler, indicating that *Yehuda* would exercise governance over *Yisrael*.

This leaves an outstanding question regarding the second *Beis Hamikdash*. The *gemara* has now resolved the apparent *yovel* at the end of the first *Beis Hamikdash* period through *Yirmiyahu*'s restoration of tribal representatives. But the *gemara* also makes clear that during the second *Beis Hamikdash*, *yovel* was not practiced. This creates a difficulty for *Hillel*'s *takana* regarding the walled city house — a *takana* apparently responding to a practical situation of sellers going into hiding to avoid having to return the house. *Tosafos* addresses this, suggesting that perhaps even in the time of the second *Beis Hamikdash* there were small representatives of all twelve tribes: *mikol shevet v'shevet chavru miktatan*. This answer is in some tension with the *gemara* itself, which emphasized the small number of returnees with *Ezra* and the impossibility of *yovel* under those conditions. Some *Rishonim* accordingly propose that *Hillel*'s *takana* addressed a *d'rabbanan yovel* — a rabbinic-level framework without *d'Oraisa* force — that was operative during the second *Beis Hamikdash* period.

## Unwalled City Houses

### Hybrid Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בָּתֵּי הַחֲצֵרִים נוֹתְנִין לָהֶם כֹּחַ יָפֶה שֶׁבְּבָתֵּי עָרֵי חוֹמָה וְכֹחַ יָפֶה שֶׁבַּשָּׂדוֹת, וְנִגְאָלִין מִיָּד וְכׇל שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ כְּבָתִּים, וְיוֹצְאִין בַּיּוֹבֵל וּבְגִרְעוֹן כֶּסֶף כְּשָׂדוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA With regard to the **houses of the** unwalled **courtyards** mentioned in the Torah (see Leviticus 25:31), i.e., houses in villages that are not surrounded by walls, **one accords them the exceptional provisions that** apply **to houses of walled cities and the exceptional provisions that** apply **to fields.** Therefore, **they are redeemed immediately and** for **the entire twelve months** following the sale, **like** in **the** sale of **houses** of walled cities, and not like fields, which may be redeemed only after two years. **And they leave** the possession of the buyer **during the Jubilee** Year **or with** a per annum **deduction** from the **money** of the sale price, **like the** sale of **fields.** By contrast, houses of walled cities become the possession of the buyer in perpetuity after one year, and if they are redeemed within the year, one pays the full sale price.

We turn now to the second major topic of the *daf*: the status of houses in unwalled cities, *batei hachatzeirim*. One might have assumed that such houses enjoy neither the privileges of *batei arei chomah* nor the full protections of agricultural fields — or perhaps that they simply follow the rules of fields. The *mishna* surprises us: *nosnim lahem kol ha'yafeh she'b'vatei arei chomah v'kol ha'yafeh she'b'sadot* — we give them every favorable feature of houses in walled cities and every favorable feature of fields.

Concretely, this means: the house in the unwalled city can be redeemed immediately (*nigalim miyad*), like a house in a walled city. It may be redeemed at any point during the twelve-month period, *k'vatim*, as houses are redeemed. And it goes out at *yovel* and is redeemable at proportional cost during the remaining years, *k'sadot*, like fields. The only period during which the seller cannot force redemption is year two — the gap between the two categories, as we will clarify below. So the seller retains the right to force redemption during year one, and again from year three onward, and also recovers the property at *yovel*. It is a remarkable consolidation of privileges.

Conceptually, this can be understood as the Torah's analog to a housing initiative: a house in a walled city is expensive urban real estate, perhaps more transitory in character, more commercial, and therefore subject to more limited protections. But a house in a village outside the metropolis is connected to one's farmland and ancestral inheritance — the very concept of *nachalah* the Torah is so insistent upon. One can sell one's field and still live somewhere, but giving up a village home is more existentially significant. Hence the Torah provides even broader protections for these houses than for either category alone.

### Sedeh Achuza

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״עַל שְׂדֵה הָאָרֶץ יֵחָשֵׁב״ — הִקִּישׁוֹ הַכָּתוּב לִשְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה, מָה שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה יוֹצֵא בַּיּוֹבֵל וּבְגִרְעוֹן כֶּסֶף, אַף בָּתֵּי הַחֲצֵרִים יוֹצְאִין בַּיּוֹבֵל וּבְגִרְעוֹן כֶּסֶף.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA **The Sages taught:** The verse states: "But the houses of the courtyards that have no wall round about them **shall be reckoned with the fields of the country;** they may be redeemed, and they shall go out in the Jubilee" (Leviticus 25:31). **The verse juxtaposed** houses of unwalled courtyards **to an ancestral field: Just as an ancestral field leaves** the possession of the buyer **in the Jubilee** Year **or with** a per annum **deduction** from the **money** of the sale price, **so too, houses of the** unwalled **courtyards leave** the possession of the buyer **in the Jubilee** Year **or with the** per annum **deduction** from the **money** of the sale price.

The *gemara* traces the derivation. The verse says *al sedei ha'aretz yeichashev* — it shall be reckoned as a field of the land — which alone would suggest treating the unwalled city house simply like a *sadeh*. The *gemara* reads this as a *hekesh* to *sedeh achuzah*: just as *sedeh achuzah* goes out at *yovel* and may be redeemed proportionally during the remaining years, so too *batei chatzerim* go out at *yovel* and are subject to proportional redemption. So far this tracks the simple *pshat* of the verses.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מָה שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה אֵינָהּ נִגְאֶלֶת בְּפָחוֹת מִשְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים, אַף בָּתֵּי הַחֲצֵרִים אֵינָם נִגְאָלִים פָּחוֹת מִשְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״גְּאוּלָּה תִּהְיֶה לוֹ״ — מִיָּד. הוֹאִיל וְנָתַתָּ לָהֶם כֹּחַ יָפֶה שֶׁבַּשָּׂדוֹת וְכֹחַ יָפֶה שֶׁבַּבָּתִּים, יָכוֹל לֹא יֵצְאוּ בַּיּוֹבֵל? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּבַיּוֹבֵל יֵצֵא״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **If** one derives the *halakha* from an ancestral field, then **just as an ancestral field may not be redeemed less than two years** after its sale, **so too, houses of the** unwalled **courtyards may not be redeemed less than two years** after their sale. Therefore, **the verse states: "They may be redeemed,"** indicating that they may be redeemed **immediately.** The *baraita* continues: **Since you have accorded** houses of the unwalled courtyards **the exceptional provisions that** apply **to fields and the exceptional provisions that** apply **to the houses** of walled cities, one **might** have thought that **they do not leave** the possession of the buyer **in the Jubilee** Year. Therefore, **the verse states: "And they shall go out in the Jubilee."**

But the *gemara* then asks: perhaps like *sadeh achuzah*, these houses cannot be redeemed within two years? To this, the verse says *geulah tihyeh lo* — he has a right of redemption — which the *mishna* reads as affirming all redemption rights, including the right to redeem immediately, *miyad*, as one redeems a house. And conversely, once we have granted the seller all the favorable features of both categories, one might have supposed that the property should perhaps not revert at *yovel* — the verse therefore explicitly states *v'vayovel yetzei*, affirming that *yovel* reversion applies.

### Hekdesh Redemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְמַקְדִּישׁ בַּיִת בְּבָתֵּי הַחֲצֵרִים, וּגְאָלוֹ אַחֵר מִיַּד הֶקְדֵּשׁ, וּפָגַע בּוֹ יוֹבֵל בְּשָׁנָה שְׁנִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *tanna* of the *baraita* **saying** in the last clause, i.e., why might one think that such houses do not leave the possession of the buyer in the Jubilee Year? After all, ancestral fields leave the possession of the buyer in the Jubilee Year, and the *tanna* teaches that the exceptional provisions of an ancestral field apply to houses in unwalled courtyards. **Rav Huna said:** The verse **is necessary only for** the case of **one who consecrates a house among the houses of the** unwalled **courtyards, and another redeemed it from the possession of** the **Temple** treasury, **and the Jubilee** Year **arrived in** the **second year** after its redemption.

The *gemara* then asks a pointed question: *mai ka'amar* — what was the point of that last concern? If we have already established through the *hekesh* to *sedeh achuzah* that it goes out at *yovel*, why was there any *hava amina* that it would not?

### Sanctified Houses

Rav Huna answers: *lo nitzrecha ela b'makdish bayis b'vatei chatzerim* — the teaching is necessary specifically for the case where someone *sanctifies* his house in an unwalled city — rendering it *hekdesh* — and someone else (*acher*) redeems it from *hekdesh*. Now *yovel* arrives in year two. The dilemma is sharp: into which category do we place this?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי מְדַמֵּית לֵיהּ? אִי לְבָתֵּי עָרֵי חוֹמָה מְדַמֵּית לֵיהּ — אִיחֲלֵיט לֵיהּ לְלוֹקֵחַ, אִי לִשְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה מְדַמֵּית — לְכֹהֲנִים נָפְקָא, לְהָכִי אִצְטְרִיךְ ״וּבְיֹבֵל יָצָא״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna elaborates: **To what will you compare** this case? **If you compare** this house **to houses of walled cities** that were consecrated and redeemed by another, then the house belongs **to the buyer in perpetuity** by the end of the first year (see 31b). **If you compare** it **to an ancestral field** that was consecrated and redeemed by another, **it leaves to** the possession of **the priests** in the Jubilee Year (see 25a). **For this** reason it **was necessary** for the verse to state: **"And they shall go out in the Jubilee,"** to teach that houses in unwalled courtyards which were consecrated and redeemed by another are returned to their owners in the Jubilee Year.

If we treat it like a house in a walled city, then after the first year has passed the buyer (*lokeiach*) gets it permanently — *chalit lei lokeiach*. If we treat it like *sedeh achuzah*, then when *yovel* hits it goes to the *kohanim* — *nafka l'Kohanim* — not back to the original owner. Either way, the original owner would not recover his house. The verse *v'vayovel yetzei* therefore comes to teach that even in this scenario, sitting between the two categories, the property returns to the original owner. This is a privilege that transcends both frameworks.

### Levite Redemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב זְעֵירָא: מַאי אִירְיָא גְּאָלוֹ אַחֵר? אֲפִילּוּ לֹא גְּאָלוֹ נָמֵי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמְרוּ הֶקְדֵּשׁ יוֹצֵא בְּלֹא פִּדְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zeira objects to this: Why** is it necessary to interpret this verse as referring **specifically** to a case where **another redeemed** the house from the Temple treasury? **Even** if another **did not redeem it,** the house should **also** be returned to its owner in the Jubilee Year, as the verse states simply: "And they shall go out in the Jubilee." **Abaye said to** Rav Zeira: An unredeemed house is not returned to its owners in the Jubilee Year, so **that** people **will not say** that **consecrated** property can **leave** the possession of the Temple treasury **without redemption.**

The *gemara* then asks: *menalan* — from where do we know that *hekdesh* does not simply revert to the owner without a redemption payment? The proof comes from the laws of *Levi'im*. A *Levi* has enhanced rights when it comes to selling his property — *geulat olam tihyeh l'Levi'im*, he may always redeem — yet when he sanctifies his property as *hekdesh* and it returns to him, he has a diminished right — *hura kocho b'hekdesho* — because *hekdesh* does not exit without a *pidyon*. If even a *Levi*, with all his expanded rights in property transactions, must give money to transfer the *kedusha* when his sanctified property is returned to him, then certainly an ordinary *Yisrael* — whose general rights are more limited — must do the same. The principle: *hekdesh yeitzei b'lo pidyon* — *hekdesh* does not exit without redemption.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָלַן? מִבֶּן לֵוִי — מָה בֶּן לֵוִי שֶׁיִּפָּה כֹּחוֹ בְּמִמְכָּרוֹ, הוֹרַע כֹּחוֹ בְּהֶקְדֵּשׁוֹ; יִשְׂרָאֵל — שֶׁהוֹרַע כֹּחוֹ בְּמִמְכָּרוֹ, אֵין דִּין שֶׁהוֹרַע כֹּחוֹ בְּהֶקְדֵּשׁוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: And **from where do we** derive that consecrated property does not leave the possession of the Temple treasury without redemption? We derive it **from** the *halakha* with regard to **a Levite:** And **what,** if with regard to **a Levite, whose power is enhanced with regard to his sale,** as a Levite may always redeem a field or house that he sold (see mishna in 33b), **his power is diminished with regard to his consecration,** as a field that he consecrated does not leave the possession of the Temple treasury until he redeems it; then with regard to **an Israelite, whose power is diminished with regard to his sale,** since if he sells an ancestral field he may not redeem it for the first two years, and he may redeem a house of a walled city only within one year of the sale, **is it not logical that his power is diminished with regard to his consecration** and that if he consecrated a house of an unwalled courtyard he must redeem it from the Temple treasury before it enters his possession?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתָם מְנָלַן? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְיָצָא מִמְכָּר״, שׁוֹמֵעַ אֲנִי אֲפִילּוּ עֲבָדָיו מִטַּלְטְלָיו וּשְׁטָרָיו, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בַּיִת וְעִיר אֲחֻזָּתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And there,** with regard to a Levite, **from where do we** derive that his consecrated property does not leave the possession of the Temple treasury without redemption? **As it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: "And if a man purchases from the Levites, the house that was sold in the city of his possession shall go out during the Jubilee Year" (Leviticus 25:33). From the phrase **"that was sold…shall go out" I** would **derive** that any item sold by a Levite returns to him in the Jubilee Year, **even his slaves, movable property, and promissory notes.** Therefore, **the verse states: "House…in the city of his possession,"** which teaches that these items do not return to the Levite without redemption.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״מִמְכַּר״? מִמְכָּריוֹ יוֹצֵא בְּחִנָּם, וְאֵין הֶקְדֵּשׁ יוֹצֵא בְּחִנָּם, אֶלָּא בְּפִדְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **What** is the meaning when **the verse states: "That was sold"?** The verse teaches that a house or field **that was sold** by a Levite **leaves** the possession of the buyer and enters the Levite's possession **for free in the Jubilee Year, but the consecration** of a Levite **does not leave for free in the Jubilee Year; rather,** it leaves only **with redemption.**

The *gemara* then traces the derivation by the *Levi'im* themselves from the verse *va'asher yiga'al min ha'Levi'im v'yatza mimkar bayis v'ir achuzato ba'yovel* — what he redeems from the *Levi'im* goes out at *yovel*. One might have thought *yatza mimkar* means all property a *Levi* sold — including slaves, movable property, and documents. The verse therefore specifies *bayis*, house, limiting the category to landed property. And the formulation *mimkaro*, from his selling — things he sold to another person — exit at *yovel* automatically, but things he *sanctified* do not exit automatically. For those, redemption money is required: *mimkaro yotzei b'chinam v'ein hekdesh yotzei b'chinam*.

### Hekdesh Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: הַכֹּל הָיוּ בִּכְלַל ״וְנָתַן הַכֶּסֶף וְקָם לוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** Rav Huna, who says that a house in an unwalled courtyard that was consecrated and subsequently redeemed by another returns to the original owner in the Jubilee Year, **disagrees with Rabbi Oshaya. As Rabbi Oshaya says: All** items **were included in** the command: **And he will give the money and it will be assured to him** (see Leviticus 27:19), i.e., one who redeems an item from the Temple treasury becomes its owner in all regards.

When *Abaye* expressed this principle — that we do not want *hekdesh* to appear to exit without some formal transfer — he framed it in terms of appearances, *shelo yomru hekdesh yeitzei b'lo pidyon*. Based on the verse, however, it is not merely a matter of appearances. It is a substantive rule: the *kedusha* of *hekdesh* must be formally transferred through *pidyon*. No property exits *hekdesh* without that mechanism.

### Hekdesh Stays

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁפָּרַט הַכָּתוּב בִּשְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה ״וְהָיָה הַשָּׂדֶה בְּצֵאתוֹ בַיֹּבֵל קֹדֶשׁ לַה׳״ — שָׂדֶה הוּא דִּפְרַק לֵיהּ וְנָפְקָא לְכֹהֲנִים, אֲבָל הָנָךְ כִּדְקָיְימִי קָיְימִי.
>
> **Translation:** **When the verse specified with regard to an ancestral field: "But the field, when it goes out in the Jubilee, shall be holy unto the Lord,** as a field dedicated; his ancestral field shall be for the priest" (Leviticus 27:21), one derives that **it is** only with regard to **a field that** one can **redeem it and** yet **it leaves** the possession of the Temple treasury to enter the possession of **the priests** in the Jubilee Year. **But** with regard to **these** houses in unwalled courtyards that were consecrated and redeemed by another, **they remain in their current state,** i.e., in the possession of the one who redeemed them.

The *gemara* notes that Rav Huna's analysis argues with the position of Rabbi Yehoshua. Rabbi Yehoshua holds: *kulam hayu b'chlal v'nasan ha'kesef v'kam lo* — the general rule is that one gives money to acquire from *hekdesh* and thereby obtains it. The verse by *sedeh achuzah* — *ve'hayetah ha'sadeh b'tzeitah ba'yovel kodesh la'Hashem ke'sedeh ha'cherem* — specifies that it goes to the *kohanim* only because *David* had already redeemed it from *hekdesh* first. *Sadeh hu de'prakei v'nafka l'Kohanim* — it is specifically the field that was redeemed by someone else and thereby exits *hekdesh* that goes to the *kohanim*. But property still sitting in the possession of *hekdesh* — *hanach k'dakai kaimi* — stays exactly as it was. *Hekdesh* retains it indefinitely until someone redeems it. *Yovel* does not dislodge property from *hekdesh* at all; it simply remains there until a *pidyon* occurs.

### Second Year Gap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּבַיּוֹבֵל יֵצֵא״, לְמָה לִי? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְמוֹכֵר בַּיִת בְּבָתֵּי הַחֲצֵרִים, וּפָגַע בּוֹ יוֹבֵל בְּשָׁנָה שְׁנִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: According to Rabbi Oshaya, **why do I** need the phrase **"and they shall go out in the Jubilee"? Rav Pappa said: It is necessary only for** the case of **one who sells a house among the houses of the** unwalled **courtyards and the Jubilee** Year **arrived in** the **second year.** The verse indicates that the house is nevertheless returned to the seller.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי מְדַמֵּית לֵיהּ? אִי לְבָתֵּי עָרֵי חוֹמָה מְדַמֵּית לֵיהּ — אִיחֲלֵיט לֵיהּ לְלוֹקֵחַ, אִי לִשְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה מְדַמֵּית לֵיהּ — הַשְׁלָמָה בָּעֵי, לְהָכִי אִצְטְרִיךְ ״וּבַיּוֹבֵל יֵצֵא״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa elaborates: **To what will you compare** this case? **If you compare** this case **to** one of the **houses of walled cities** that was sold, then the house belongs **to the buyer in perpetuity** at the end of the first year. And **if you compare it to** the case of **an ancestral field** that was sold, then **it requires completion** of another year after the Jubilee Year, as taught in a *baraita* cited earlier (29b): If the buyer consumed an ancestral field's produce for one year before the Jubilee Year, he completes another year after the Jubilee Year. **For this** reason **it was necessary** for the verse to state: **"And they shall go out in the Jubilee,"** to teach that in such a case the house of an unwalled courtyard returns to the possession of the original owner.

Rav Papa then applies this principle to the unwalled city house scenario. The teaching *v'vayovel yetzei* is necessary precisely for the case where someone sells his house in an unwalled city and *yovel* arrives in the second year — the gap between the two categories. *L'mai medamit lei* — into which framework does it fall?

If it is treated like *batei arei chomah*, then after the first year has passed, the buyer gets it permanently even at *yovel*. If it is treated like *sedeh achuzah*, then the seller cannot recover it — the buyer is entitled to make up any year that fell within *yovel*, *hashlamah ba'ei*. The verse *v'vayovel yetzei* therefore teaches that neither framework applies in this year-two gap: the property returns to the seller, and the buyer receives no right of extension. A *braita* is cited in support of Rav Huna's position, and the *gemara* notes it constitutes a contradiction to Rabbi Yehoshua's view: if someone sanctifies a house in an unwalled city and *yovel* arrives without redemption, *chozer l'be'alim ba'yovel* — it returns to the owner. This contradicts Rabbi Yehoshua's position that *hekdesh* remains in *hekdesh* until actively redeemed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא, וּתְיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ בַּיִת בְּבָתֵּי הַחֲצֵרִים — הֲרֵי זֶה גּוֹאֵל מִיָּד וְגוֹאֲלוֹ לְעוֹלָם; גְּאָלוֹ אַחֵר מִיַּד הֶקְדֵּשׁ, הִגִּיעַ יוֹבֵל וְלֹא נִגְאַל — חוֹזֵר לַבְּעָלִים בַּיּוֹבֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Huna, and** this *baraita* is **a conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Rabbi Oshaya:** With regard to **one who consecrates a house among the houses of** unwalled **courtyards, this** individual **may redeem** the house **immediately and he may redeem it always.** If **another** individual **redeemed it from the possession of the Temple** treasury, and the **Jubilee** Year **arrived and it was not redeemed** by the original owner, the house **returns to the owner in the Jubilee** Year.

## Unwalled City Criteria

### Two/Two Minimum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְאֵלּוּ הֵן בָּתֵּי הַחֲצֵרִים — שְׁנֵי חֲצֵרִים שֶׁל שְׁנֵי בָתִּים, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמּוּקֶּפֶת חוֹמָה מִימוֹת יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן, הֲרֵי הֵן כְּבָתֵּי חֲצֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA **And these are the houses of the** unwalled **courtyards** whose *halakha* was taught in the previous mishna: Any city in which there are **two courtyards** each containing **two houses, although it is surrounded by a wall from the era of Joshua, son of Nun, their** halakhic status **is like that of the houses of** the unwalled **courtyards.**

Having established the laws of *batei hachatzeirim*, the *gemara* turns to their definition. The *mishna* states: *shnei chatzeiros shel shnei batim* — even if a settlement has a wall, if it consists of only two streets with two houses each, it does not qualify as a walled city — *harei hen k'vatei chatzeirim*. It retains the status of an unwalled settlement regardless of its physical wall.

### Size Over Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִמַּשְׁמַע שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״בָּתֵּי הַחֲצֵרִים״ אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם חוֹמָה? מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אֲשֶׁר אֵין לָהֶם חוֹמָה״? אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶם חוֹמָה כְּמִי שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם חוֹמָה. וְכַמָּה? בָּתִּים — שְׁנַיִם, חֲצֵירוֹת — שְׁנַיִם, שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת שֶׁל שְׁנֵי בָתִּים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA ** The Sages taught: By inference from** that **which is stated: "The houses of the courtyards"** (Leviticus 25:31), **do I not know that they have no wall** around them? If so, **what** is the meaning when **the verse states: "Which have no wall"?** It means that there are certain places that **even though they have a wall,** they are considered **like** places **that do not have a wall. And how many,** i.e., what is considered such a place? The term **"houses"** indicates a minimum of **two,** and the term **"courtyards"** likewise indicates a minimum of **two.** Therefore, if a city has only **two courtyards** each containing **two houses,** its houses are considered like the houses of the unwalled courtyards.

The *braita* derives this from the verse's phrasing. The verse says *batei hachatzeirim* — houses of the unwalled cities — and then adds the apparently redundant phrase *asher ein lahem chomah saviv* — which do not have a wall around. One would have assumed from context that these cities lack walls. The redundancy therefore teaches: *af al pi she'yesh lahem chomah k'mi she'ein lahem chomah* — even if they have a wall, they are treated as though they have none. The *drasha* then identifies two houses (*batim*) and two courtyards or streets (*chatzerot*) as the minimal threshold below which even a walled city does not count.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא בַּיִת וְחָצֵר! אִם כֵּן לִיכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״חֲצֵרִים״, וְכִי תֵּימָא אִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״חֲצֵרִים״ — חָצֵר בְּלֹא בַּיִת מַשְׁמַע, הַהוּא קַרְפֵּף אִיקְּרִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But** you can **say** that the phrase: Houses of the courtyards, means one **house and** one **courtyard,** i.e., one house in each courtyard. Consequently, if each courtyard contains two houses, the city should be considered like a walled city. The Gemara explains: **If so, let the Merciful One write: "Courtyards,"** without mentioning houses, as a courtyard must contain at least one house. **And if you would say: If the Merciful One had written** only: **"Courtyards,"** then one might mistakenly have concluded that the verse **indicates a courtyard without a house** and that if the courtyard contains a house then the city is considered walled, one could not have arrived at that conclusion, since **such** an area **is called an enclosure [*karpef* ],** not a courtyard.

This ruling applies even to a settlement that is *mukaf chomah mi'yemos Yehoshua bin Nun* — walled from the time of *Yehoshua*. The physical wall is simply not determinative if the settlement does not meet the minimum population and structural threshold. The *gemara* also asks: could the minimum be even lower — one house and one street? The derivation from *batim chatzerot* (both in the plural, with *batim* adding a count beyond what *chatzerot* alone would imply) establishes that two houses per two streets is the minimum, and anything below that, regardless of walls, is treated as *batei chatzerim*.

### Historical Status

The question of whether an existing wall might elevate a small settlement's status if it later grows is implicitly addressed: the status is fixed based on the historical condition at the time of *Yehoshua* or *Ezra*, as one follows based on the earlier *machlokes*. Subsequent development does not alter the classification.

### Megilla & Purim

A final point: the *gemara* notes that *af al pi she'ein lo achshav* — even if the settlement no longer has [the wall] now — the determinative factor is what it had at the time of *Yehoshua* or *Ezra*. The classification is entirely backward-looking. This has obvious resonance with the laws of *megilla*, where the question of which cities read on the fifteenth versus the fourteenth of *Adar* similarly turns on their ancient walled status — a point the *gemara* notes is apposite precisely as *Purim* approaches.

## Levite Inheritance

### Israelite Inherits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁיָּרַשׁ אֲבִי אִמּוֹ לֵוִי — אֵינוֹ גּוֹאֵל כַּסֵּדֶר הַזֶּה. וְכֵן לֵוִי שֶׁיָּרַשׁ אֶת אֲבִי אִמּוֹ יִשְׂרָאֵל — אֵינוֹ גּוֹאֵל כַּסֵּדֶר הַזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA **An Israelite who inherited** a house in a walled city from **his mother's father** who was **a Levite does not redeem** the house **in accordance with this procedure** delineated in the previous *mishnayot*; rather, if he sold the inherited house, he may redeem it always, like a Levite. **And likewise, a Levite who inherited** a house in a walled city from **his mother's father** who was **an Israelite does not redeem** the house **in accordance with this procedure** delineated in the previous *mishnayot*.

The *mishna* now addresses the interaction of Levitical status with Levitical land. The first case: a *Yisrael* who inherits from his maternal grandfather, a *Levi*, now holds Levitical land but is not himself a *Levi*. The *mishna* states *eino go'el k'seder hazeh* — he does not redeem according to the [special Levitical] arrangement.

### Levite Inherits

The converse: a *Levi* who inherits from his maternal grandfather, a *Yisrael*, now holds non-Levitical land but is himself a *Levi*. Again, *eino go'el k'seder hazeh* — he does not redeem according to the Levitical arrangement.

### Rabbi's Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי בָתֵּי עָרֵי הַלְוִיִּם הִוא אֲחוּזָּתָם״, עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא לֵוִי וְעָרֵי הַלְוִיִּם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין דְּבָרִים הַלָּלוּ אֲמוּרִים אֶלָּא בְּעָרֵי הַלְוִיִּם.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna provides the source for these *halakhot*: **As it is stated:** "And if a man purchases from the Levites, the house that was sold in the city of his possession shall go out during the Jubilee Year; **as the houses of the cities of the Levites are their possession** among the children of Israel" (Leviticus 25:33). The verse indicates that the ability to always redeem the house of a Levite does not apply **unless** the one selling the house **will be a Levite and** the house is located in **the cities of the Levites.** This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **And the Rabbis say: These matters are stated only** with regard to a house **in the cities of the Levites,** even if the owner was not a Levite.

As the *gemara* will demonstrate, these two rulings appear to contradict the *mishna*'s conclusion, which establishes a two-criteria requirement. The *gemara* therefore rereads *eino go'el k'seder hazeh* as *eino go'el ella k'seder hazeh* — he redeems only in the manner of an ordinary *Yisrael*, not with Levitical privileges. The double negation is resolved into a positive limitation: with only one of the two criteria, one is treated as a *Yisrael*.

### Mamzer Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְאֶלָּא כְּמַאן? כְּבֶן לֵוִי. וַהֲדַר תָּנֵי: ״עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא לֵוִי וְעָרֵי הַלְוִיִּם״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA The mishna teaches that according to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, if an Israelite sold a house in a walled city that he inherited from his mother's father who was a Levite, he does not redeem it in accordance with the procedure delineated in the previous *mishnayot*. The Gemara asks: **Rather, like whom** does he redeem it? He redeems it **like a Levite. But** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **then teaches** that the ability to always redeem the house of a Levite does not apply **unless** the one selling the house **will be a Levite and** the house is located in **the cities of the Levites.** If so, an Israelite who inherited a house in a walled city from his mother's father who is a Levite should redeem it in the manner of an Israelite.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא: אֵינוֹ גּוֹאֵל אֶלָּא כַּסֵּדֶר הַזֶּה, עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא לֵוִי וְעָרֵי הַלְוִיִּם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Say** that the mishna means that an Israelite who inherited a house in a walled city from his mother's father who was a Levite **redeems** that house **only in accordance with this procedure** delineated in the previous *mishnayot*, i.e., he has only one year to redeem it, after which the house becomes the possession of the buyer in perpetuity. And this is always the *halakha* **unless** the one selling the house **will be a Levite and** the house is located **in the cities of the Levites,** in which case he can always redeem it. This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא עָרֵי הַלְוִיִּם — דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי בָּתֵּי עָרֵי הַלְוִיִּם״, אֶלָּא לֵוִי מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״וַאֲשֶׁר יִגְאַל מִן הַלְוִיִּם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** the house must be from **the cities of the Levites, as it is written: "As the houses of the cities of the Levites** are their possession" (Leviticus 25:33). **But from where do we** derive that the one selling the house must also be **a Levite** for these *halakhot* to apply? The Gemara responds: **As it is written** in the same verse: **"And if a man purchases from the Levites**…shall go out during the Jubilee Year." The verse is referring specifically to a Levite who sells his house.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: ״וַאֲשֶׁר יִגְאַל מִן הַלְוִיִּם״ — יָכוֹל לֵוִי מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל יִגְאַל, שֶׁזֶּה יִפָּה כֹּחוֹ וְזֶה הוֹרַע כֹּחוֹ, אֲבָל לֵוִי מִלֵּוִי לֹא, שֶׁזֶּה יִפָּה כֹּחוֹ וְזֶה יִפָּה כֹּחוֹ — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מִן הַלְוִיִּם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds that **it is** likewise **taught** in a *baraita*: Given that the verse already states: "The Levites shall have a perpetual right of redemption" (Leviticus 25:32), why does the next verse state: **"And if a man purchases from the Levites**…shall go out during the Jubilee Year"?It is because one **might** have thought: **A Levite may** always **redeem from an Israelite** the house that he sold, **as this** Levite's **power was enhanced,** in that he can redeem always, **and that** Israelite's **power was diminished,** as he may redeem only within one year. **But a Levite may not** always redeem **from** another **Levite, as this** one's **power was enhanced and that** one's **power was** equally **enhanced.** Rather, the Levite may redeem only within one year of the sale. Therefore, **the verse states:** "And if a man purchases **from the Levites,"** which literally means: And if a man redeems from the Levites, which teaches that even a Levite who redeems a house purchased by another Levite can always redeem the house.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִן הַלְוִיִּם״ — וְלֹא כׇּל הַלְוִיִּם, פְּרָט לְבֶן לֵוִי מַמְזֵר וְנָתִין.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: When the verse states: **"From the Levites,"** this teaches that some Levites may always redeem their houses, **but not all the Levites. Excluded is a Levite** who is **a son born from an incestuous or adulterous relationship [*mamzer*], or a Gibeonite,** i.e., his mother is from the Gibeonites, as these are disqualified from entering the congregation of the Jewish people (see *Yevamot* 78a). If such a Levite inherited from his father and sold a house from the cities of the Levites, he does not redeem it as a Levite.

The *drasha* of *min ha'Levi'im v'lo kol ha'Levi'im* — from the *Levi'im* but not all *Levi'im* — excludes the *ben Levi mamzer* and *netin*. This is a notable ruling: a *mamzer* who is a *Levi* retains his *Levi* status in most halakhic contexts — he receives the second *aliya*, he receives *ma'aser rishon* — but here, the perpetual redemption right of *geulat olam* is denied to him. The rationale is that the special Levitical right is keyed to the full identity of membership in the *shevet*, and a *mamzer*, whose lineage is compromised in the halakhic sense, does not possess that full identity.

### Chachamim's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין דְּבָרִים הַלָּלוּ אֲמוּרִים אֶלָּא בְּעָרֵי הַלְוִיִּם. אֲבָל עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא לֵוִי — לָא אָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And the Rabbis say: These matters are stated only** with regard to a house **in the cities of the Levites,** even if the owner was not a Levite. The Gemara explains: **But** according to the Rabbis, **we do not say** that the ability to always redeem the house of a Levite does not apply **unless** the one selling the house **will be a Levite.** Rather, the Rabbis hold that anyone who inherits a portion in a Levite city may redeem that portion as a Levite, even if he himself is not a Levite.

The *Chachamim* disagree with Rabbi's position: *ein devarim halalu amurim ella b'arei Levi'im af al pi she'eino Levi* — the special redemption rights follow the land, not the person. So long as the property in question is Levitical land — land within the cities of the *Levi'im* — the perpetual redemption right applies, regardless of whether the current owner is personally a *Levi*. The land-type alone determines the *halakha*.

## Levite City Structure

### City Layout

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין עוֹשִׂין שָׂדֶה מִגְרָשׁ, וְלֹא מִגְרָשׁ שָׂדֶה, וְלֹא מִגְרָשׁ עִיר, וְלֹא עִיר מִגְרָשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The Levites received two thousand cubits surrounding their cities, one thousand cubits of empty lots and one thousand cubits for fields and vineyards. **One may neither render a field** an empty **lot nor** an empty **lot a field.** Similarly, one may neither incorporate an empty **lot** into **a city nor** render part of **a city** an empty **lot.**

The *mishna* establishes that one may not convert a *migrash* into a field, nor a field into a *migrash*, nor extend the city into the *migrash*, nor rezone part of the city as *migrash*. The Torah describes the structure of Levitical cities as having the city itself, surrounded by a *migrash* — a thousand-*amah* radius of open, park-like space — and beyond that another thousand-*amah* radius of planting fields. This layout must be preserved.

### Rabbi Elazar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? בְּעָרֵי הַלְוִיִּם, אֲבָל בְּעָרֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל עוֹשִׂין שָׂדֶה מִגְרָשׁ, וְלֹא מִגְרָשׁ שָׂדֶה, מִגְרָשׁ עִיר, וְלֹא עִיר מִגְרָשׁ, שֶׁלֹּא יַחְרִיבוּ אֶת עָרֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל. הַכֹּהֲנִים וְהַלְוִיִּם מוֹכְרִין לְעוֹלָם, וְגוֹאֲלִין לְעוֹלָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״גְּאוּלַּת עוֹלָם תִּהְיֶה לַלְוִיִּם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said: In what** case **is this statement said?** It applies **in the cities of the Levites. But in the cities of the Israelites one may render a field** an empty **lot but not** an empty **lot a field,** and one may incorporate an empty **lot** into **a city but not** render part of **a city** an empty **lot,** in order to ensure **that they will not** thereby **destroy the cities of Israel. The priests and the Levites may sell** their fields and houses **always and may redeem** them **always, as it is stated: "The Levites shall have a perpetual right of redemption"** (Leviticus 25:32). Priests are also members of the tribe of Levi.

Rabbi Elazar ben Menachem clarifies that these restrictions apply only to *arei haLevi'im* — the cities of the *Levi'im* — where the Torah explicitly prescribes the city's layout. For cities of ordinary *Yisrael*, the rules are flexible: *osim sadeh migrash o migrash sadeh* — one may convert a field to park space or park space to field, and the city may expand outward into what was previously *migrash*.

### No Destruction

However, even for non-Levitical cities, one restriction remains: *v'lo ir migrash* — one may not demolish part of the city and rezone it as open park space. The reason given is *shelo yachrivu et arei Yisrael* — lest they destroy the cities of *Yisrael*. The *mishna* thus preserves at minimum a prohibition against the deliberate dismantling of established settlements. One might ask: if the city has been abandoned anyway, does it matter whether the structures stand empty or are repurposed as open ground? The *halakha* nonetheless maintains that the act of demolishing and converting is prohibited, imposing a floor beneath which urban degradation cannot be deliberately accelerated.

### Geulat Olam

The *mishna* then states: *hakohanim v'haLevi'im mocherim l'olam u'goalim l'olam* — *kohanim* and *Levi'im* may sell at all times and redeem at all times. This formalizes what was implied throughout the prior discussion: the perpetual right of redemption applies to all Levitical property, without the one-year limit applicable to walled city houses or the two-year limit applicable to fields. The source is *geulat olam tihyeh la'Levi'im*.

### Lo Yimacher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: ״בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? בְּעָרֵי הַלְוִיִּם, אֲבָל בְּעָרֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל עוֹשִׂין״ וְכוּ׳. דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִיהָא בְּדִלְוִיִּם לָא מְשַׁנֵּינַן, מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Elazar said: In what** case **is this statement said?** It is **in the cities of the Levites. But in the cities of the Israelites** one may render a field an empty lot but not an empty lot a field, and one may incorporate an empty lot into a city but not render part of a city an empty lot, in order to ensure that they will not thereby destroy the cities of Israel. The Gemara asks: **In any event, everyone** agrees that **in** the cities **of the Levites one may not change** a field into an empty lot or an empty lot into a city. **From where are these matters** derived?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וּשְׂדֵה מִגְרַשׁ עָרֵיהֶם לֹא יִמָּכֵר״. מַאי ״לֹא יִמָּכֵר״? אִילֵּימָא לֹא יִמָּכֵר כְּלָל, וְהָא מִדִּכְתִיב: ״גְּאֻלַּת עוֹלָם תִּהְיֶה לַלְוִיִּם״, מִכְּלָל דִּמְזַבְּנִי. אֶלָּא מַאי ״לֹא יִמָּכֵר״? לֹא יְשַׁנֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said: As the verse states: "But the fields of the open land about their cities may not be sold"** (Leviticus 25:34). **What** is the meaning of the phrase **"may not be sold"? If we say** it means that such fields **may not be sold at all,** that cannot be correct, as **from** the fact **that it is written: "The Levites shall have a perpetual right of redemption"** (Leviticus 25:32), one learns **by inference that** their fields **may be sold. Rather, what** is the meaning of the phrase **"may not be sold"?** It means that a field, an empty lot, or a city **may not be changed** from its current status.

The *gemara* closes by addressing the verse's phrase *u'sdeh migrash areihem lo yimacher* — the field of their *migrash* shall not be sold. The question arises: does *lo yimacher* mean a literal prohibition on selling? If so, it would contradict the principle of *geulat olam*, which presupposes the possibility of sale.

The *gemara* resolves this: *lo yimacher* does not mean they cannot sell. Rather, it means *lo yeshaneh* — the structure may not be *changed*. *Levi'im* may sell their property and their buyers may hold it — and the *Levi'im* always retain the right to redeem it. But the underlying structure of the Levitical city — its spatial layout of city, *migrash*, and fields — must remain intact. The prohibition is not on the transaction, but on structural alteration. Sales occur; redemptions occur; but the essential character of the *arei haLevi'im* endures unchanged.
