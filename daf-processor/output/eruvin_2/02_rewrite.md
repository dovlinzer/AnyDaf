# Eruvin 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eruvin Overview

### Scope

We begin today a new *masechta*: *Masechet Eruvin*, which will occupy us for approximately three months. Its primary focus is on two types of *eiruv*.

### Two Types

It is worth noting at the outset that what we commonly refer to as the *eiruv* — the beams and wires that define an outdoor space as a *reshus hayachid* by creating substitute walls — is technically called a *tzuras hapesach*, not an *eiruv* at all. The term *eiruv* properly refers to something else entirely. When multiple houses open into a shared courtyard, the residents must join together through a loaf of bread — *eiruvei chatzeiros* — commingling their ownership so that the entire space is treated as a single property. Similarly, when multiple courtyards open into an alleyway, the residents of those courtyards join together through *shitufei mevoos*. These are the two primary forms of *eiruv*.

### Bread & Ownership

The *eiruv*, then, is fundamentally an act of commingling: the word *eiruv* is related to *arev*, to intermingle. Food — specifically bread — serves as the vehicle by which distinct owners define themselves as sharing one property.

### Distance Extension

The second dimension of *Masechet Eruvin* concerns walking distance. By Torah law, one may walk no more than two thousand *amos* beyond a city on *Shabbos*. However, if one designates a point outside the city as one's place of departure for *Shabbos* — again through food — one gains additional distance in that direction while forfeiting it in the opposite direction. Here too, food serves to define where a person is considered to be present.

### Shabbos Parallel

It is striking that *Masechet Shabbos* opens in a similar vein, with a focus on bread and food — specifically the act of giving bread to a poor person — as the defining gesture of presence and transaction. Both tractates, it seems, center food as the primary vehicle for establishing legal categories of place and personhood.

### Walls Focus

Our *Mishnah*, however, does not begin with the joining of ownership. It begins instead with the structural question: how does one create walls — or their legal equivalent — where real walls do not exist?

## Mavoi Structure

### Mavoi Definition

The *Mishnah* opens with the *mavoi*, the alleyway. To understand the *mavoi*, one must first visualize the spatial arrangement it presupposes. Multiple houses open into a shared enclosed yard — a *chatzer*. The walls of those houses, packed closely together, effectively form the walls of the courtyard itself.

### Chatzer vs Mavoi

The *chatzer* is the courtyard surrounded by houses on all sides. The *mavoi* is the next level up: multiple *chatzeros* opening into a shared alleyway. If one imagines a row of such courtyards flanking a thoroughfare, that thoroughfare is the *mavoi*.

### Open vs Closed

A *mavoi mefulash* — an alleyway open at both ends — functions as a street and could potentially constitute a *reshus harabim*. But if the *mavoi* dead-ends at a *chatzer*, it is not a through-passage, and therefore not a *reshus harabim*. Indeed, if the surrounding houses and courtyards form tight walls on three sides, the *mavoi* may itself constitute a *reshus hayachid* biblically.

### Ownership Q&A

This raises a question worth pausing over: does the definition of *reshus hayachid* depend on ownership? The answer, biblically, is no. *Reshus hayachid* is defined purely by physical structure — a space enclosed by three walls — without regard to who owns it. Ownership plays no role whatsoever in the biblical definitions of *reshus hayachid* or *reshus harabim*. *Reshus harabim* is defined by function: a major gathering place or principal thoroughfare. *Reshus hayachid* is defined by enclosure: three walls.

### Rabbinical Unification

At the rabbinic level, however, the presence of multiple owners sharing a single space was felt to undermine the experiential quality of a unified private domain. The rabbis therefore required that the various owners unify their space through *eiruvei chatzeiros* and *shitufei mevoos* — treating it as though it belonged to a single owner — in order to permit carrying. This rabbinic requirement of commingled ownership is entirely separate from the biblical structural definition.

### Fourth Wall Need

Even structurally, three walls alone are insufficient at the rabbinic level. The open fourth side of the *mavoi* requires some marker: either a *lechi*, a vertical post, or a *kora*, a horizontal beam. Conceptually, what this marker accomplishes is to define the fourth opening as a *doorway* rather than a gap in the wall. A doorway is part of a structure; a gap is a breach. The *lechi* or *kora* transforms the open end of the *mavoi* from a break into a threshold, and that is sufficient — rabbinically — to complete the enclosure.

## Height & Width

### Twenty Cubit Height

The *Mishnah* states: if the *kora* — the horizontal crossbeam — is placed higher than twenty *amos* above the ground, it must be lowered. *Rabbi Yehuda* disagrees and holds that no such reduction is required.

### Ten Cubit Width

Likewise, if the opening of the *mavoi* is wider than ten *amos* — roughly fifteen feet — it must be reduced. The fourth side can only be treated as a doorway up to a certain width.

### Doorway Concept

The conceptual basis is straightforward: a doorway has intuitive parameters. Beyond a certain width, an opening no longer reads as a doorway; it looks like a breach, a gap in the wall. The threshold of ten *amos* is where the *Mishnah* draws that line.

### Tzuras Hapesach

There is, however, an important exception. If one erects a *tzuras hapesach* — a complete doorframe structure consisting of two vertical posts and a horizontal beam spanning between them — even an opening wider than ten *amos* is acceptable. The *tzuras hapesach*, by virtue of constituting the full architecture of a doorway, defines even a wider span as an opening rather than a breach.

### Modern Practice

This is precisely how contemporary *eruvin* are constructed. Vertical posts are affixed against telephone poles, positioned directly beneath the telephone or electric wire running between them. The wire, running directly overhead, functions as the horizontal beam. Together, the posts and wire create a *tzuras hapesach* across an otherwise open gap. The posts need not actually touch the wire — they need only be positioned directly beneath it.

## Sukkah Comparison

### Language Difference

The *Gemara* observes that *Masechet Sukkah* opens with a parallel *Mishnah*: a *sukkah* higher than twenty *amos* is *pesulah* — invalid. *Rabbi Yehuda* there says it is valid. The language and the dispute appear nearly identical to our *Mishnah*. Yet there is a notable difference in formulation: *Sukkah* uses the term *pesulah*, declaring the *sukkah* disqualified, while our *Mishnah* uses *takanta* — stating the remedy, telling one to lower the beam.

### Rashi: Pesulah

Why the difference? The *Gemara's* first answer: *sukkah* is a biblical obligation, and the *Mishnah* is informing us that a *sukkah* built in this way is disqualified. The institution of *sukkah* pre-exists the *Mishnah*; everyone knows what a *sukkah* is, and the *Mishnah* simply pronounces it *pasul*. By contrast, the *mavoi* with its crossbeam is a rabbinic institution. One cannot declare something *pasul* before first establishing what the requirements are. *Rashi* explains that since the *Mishnah* has not yet told us that a crossbeam is required at all, it cannot simply say the crossbeam is *pasul* — it must instead describe how to fix the situation.

### Tosafot Question

*Tosafot*, in their very first comment on *Masechet Eruvin*, press a pointed question on this: why does the *Mishnah* not simply begin by telling us what makes a *mavoi* valid — a *lechi* or a *kora* — and only then note that if one places the crossbeam higher than twenty *amos*, it must be lowered? Beginning with the disqualification before establishing the basic requirement is a strange pedagogical choice. *Tosafot* suggests that the parallel structure with *Masechet Sukkah* may itself be the explanation for why the *Mishnah* is organized this way — though that, of course, merely pushes the question back to *Sukkah*. The broader point is that the *Mishnah's* organizational logic does not always proceed from first principles — a feature of an oral tradition not necessarily designed for the uninitiated reader.

### Terseness Reason

The *Gemara* offers a second answer as well. Even for biblical matters, one could in principle state the remedy rather than the disqualification. The reason *Sukkah* uses *pesulah* is simply terseness: the *Mishnah* in *Sukkah* lists multiple ways a *sukkah* can be invalid — too tall, insufficient shade, fewer than three walls, too short — and rather than specifying the remedy for each scenario separately, it collapses them all into a single declaration of *pesulah*. In the case of the *mavoi*, only one scenario is being addressed, so it is possible to specify the remedy directly.

## Heichal & Ulam

### R. Yehuda Ulam

We now arrive at the more substantive question underlying the *Mishnah's* parameters. The *Gemara* establishes that the requirements of twenty *amos* in height and ten *amos* in width are not arbitrary — they derive from a specific architectural precedent.

### Chachamim Heichal

*Rav Yehuda* said in the name of *Rav*: the *Chachamim* derived the parameters of a valid doorway from the opening of the *Heichal* — the inner chamber of the *Beit Hamikdash*, corresponding to the *Mishkan's* central sanctuary, which housed the *menorah*, the *shulchan*, and the golden *mizbe'ach*. The opening of the *Heichal* was, as the *Mishnah* elsewhere records, twenty *amos* high and ten *amos* wide — precisely the maximum parameters in our *Mishnah*. *Rabbi Yehuda*, by contrast, derived the parameters from the opening of the *Ulam*, the vestibule that preceded the *Heichal*. The *Ulam* did not exist in the *Mishkan*; it was added by *Shlomo* in the time of the *Beit Hamikdash* and extended beyond the width of the *Heichal*, forming a wider hall at the entrance. The opening of the *Ulam* was forty *amos* high and twenty *amos* wide — exactly double the dimensions of the *Heichal*.

### Sanctity Divide

The dispute turns on a question of sanctity. The *Gemara* explains that both positions derive from the verse *veshachatu petach ohel moed* — one should slaughter [the offering] at the opening of the *ohel moed*. The operative word is *petach*, doorway. The question is: which opening in the *Beit Hamikdash* corresponds to the *petach ohel moed* of the *Mishkan*?

The *Chachamim* hold that the sanctity of the *Heichal* and the sanctity of the *Ulam* are distinct. The *Ulam*, on their view, does not carry the same sanctity as the *Heichal*; only the *Heichal* is the *ohel moed*. Therefore, only the opening of the *Heichal* qualifies as the *petach ohel moed*, and it is from those dimensions — twenty by ten — that the parameters of a valid doorway are derived.

### Verse Dispute

Both positions, then, are rooted in the same verse. The dispute is entirely about which opening in the *Beit Hamikdash* the phrase *petach ohel moed* refers to when transplanted from the context of the *Mishkan*.

### United Sanctity

*Rabbi Yehuda* holds that the *Heichal* and the *Ulam* share a single, unified sanctity. Since the *Ulam* is part of the sanctity of the *Heichal*, its opening equally qualifies as the *petach ohel moed*. He cites the phrase *el petach ulam habayis* — at the opening of the *ulam* of the house — as evidence that the word *petach* applies to the *Ulam* as well.

*Tosafot* note, with characteristic precision, that this verse does not appear anywhere in *Tanach* as cited. It is, as the *Ri* explains, a composite of two separate *pesukim*. The *Gemara* nonetheless proceeds to analyze it as quoted. The *Rabbanan* respond: had the verse said merely *petach ulam*, one might accept that the *Ulam's* opening is called a *petach*. But because the verse says *petach ulam habayis*, the word *petach* here refers not to the doorway itself but to the house that opens into the *ulam* — a relational description, not a designation of doorway status. *Rabbi Yehuda's* proof is therefore deflected.

## Mishkan-Mikdash

### Application Question

An underlying question runs through this entire discussion: how can laws written about the *Mishkan* be applied to the *Beit Hamikdash*? The *Mishkan* was a significantly smaller structure. The verse *veshachatu petach ohel moed* was said in the context of the *Mishkan* in the wilderness. How does one legitimately apply it to the *Beit Hamikdash*?

### Doors Open Rule

This question has practical legal stakes. *Rav Yehuda* said in the name of *Shmuel*: if one slaughtered *shlamim* before the doors of the *Heichal* were opened in the morning, those *shlamim* are invalid, as it is stated: *veshachatu petach ohel moed* — one must slaughter at the opening of the *ohel moed*. From this verse we learn not only *where* the slaughter must take place but *when*: the doors must be open, and slaughter performed while they are closed is invalid. The application of this ruling to the *Heichal* of the *Beit Hamikdash* depends entirely on the correspondence between the *Mishkan* and the *Mikdash*. *Tosafot* note a further implication: given that the *tamid* must in any case be the first offering brought each morning, and it must be slaughtered with the doors open, one might wonder what independent work this ruling does. *Tosafot* suggest it addresses cases where an offering was slaughtered before the *tamid* was brought, or where the doors were closed mid-day — establishing that the invalidity is not limited to the morning sequence but applies whenever the doors are closed.

### Name Exchange

The *Gemara* establishes the correspondence through a mutual exchange of names: the *Mikdash* is called *Mishkan* in certain verses, and the *Mishkan* is called *Mikdash* in others. Once this equivalence is demonstrated in both directions, laws written in the context of one may be applied to the other.

### Textual Sources

The *Gemara* provides the textual sources. The *Mikdash* is called *Mishkan* from the verse in *Bechukosai*: *venasati mishkani besochechem* — I will place My *mishkan* among you. Since the *Mishkan* already existed at the time this verse was spoken, it must refer to the future *Beit Hamikdash*, which is therefore called a *mishkan*. Conversely, the *Mishkan* is called *Mikdash* from the verse *veasу li mikdash veshachanti besocham* — they shall make for Me a *mikdash* and I will dwell among them. That verse, describing the construction of the *Mishkan*, uses the name *mikdash*. With both names established in both directions, the legal correspondence between the two institutions is grounded.

## Alternative Sources

### Courtyard Option

The *Gemara* now asks: given all of the above, why derive the parameters of a valid doorway from the opening of the *Heichal* or *Ulam* at all? Why not derive them instead from the opening of the courtyard of the *Mishkan*? The courtyard of the *Mishkan* was an open-air enclosure — a hundred *amos* long and fifty *amos* wide, the fifty referring to the open space in front of the *Mishkan* itself, with the *Mishkan* occupying thirty *amos* at the back and twenty *amos* behind it. The curtains surrounding the courtyard were five *amos* high. As for the opening: fifteen *amos* of curtain on one side, fifteen on the other — leaving an opening of twenty *amos*.

### Gate vs Door

The *Gemara* rejects this source: the opening of the *chatzer* is explicitly called a *shaar*, a gate. Gates are categorically different from doorways — they can be larger than ordinary *pesachim*. One cannot derive the parameters of a standard doorway from a gate.

### Height Reread

The *Gemara* offers an alternative reading as well. When the Torah states fifteen *amos* of curtain on each side, this does not necessarily refer to the width of the curtains — it may refer to their height. The curtains were fifteen *amos* tall — measured, according to this reading, from the top of the *mizbe'ach* upward, not from the ground — while the basic height stated elsewhere as five *amos* refers to a different measurement. On this reading, we do not even know the actual width of the courtyard's opening, and therefore cannot derive any standard from it.

### Petach Source

Both the *Chachamim* and *Rabbi Yehuda*, the *Gemara* concludes, derived their parameters specifically from a *petach* — an ordinary doorway — and not from a *shaar*. The courtyard opening, whether understood as a gate or as a measurement of uncertain dimension, cannot serve as the relevant source.

## Heichal Basis Q

### Doors Question

The *Gemara* now turns a critical eye on the derivation from the *Heichal* itself. If the *Chachamim* are truly basing themselves on the *Heichal's* opening, why do we not require doors? The *Heichal* had doors. Yet the *Mishnah* teaches that a *mavoi* is made permissible with a *lechi* or a *kora* — the most minimal of markers — with no mention of doors being required.

### Privacy, Not Status

The *Gemara* responds: the *Heichal* would have been considered a doorway even without its doors. The doors served the purpose of *tzniusa* — privacy — not to confer doorway status. Without the doors, the opening of the *Heichal* would still have been called a *petach*. This allows the *Chachamim* to derive the parameters of a doorway from the *Heichal* without requiring that all doorways have actual doors.

### Width Exception Q

But the *Gemara* presses a second challenge. If we are learning from the *Heichal*, and the *Heichal* had a complete *tzuras hapesach* — a full doorframe — and was nonetheless only ten *amos* wide, then how can our *Mishnah* allow an opening wider than ten *amos* simply by adding a *tzuras hapesach*? The *Heichal* itself had a *tzuras hapesach* and was still limited to ten. What, then, does adding a *tzuras hapesach* accomplish beyond ten *amos*?

### According to Rav

The *Gemara's* answer is pointed: these difficulties apply only according to *Rav* — it was *Rav* who said that the *Chachamim* derived the parameters from the opening of the *Heichal*. But it is possible that neither the *Chachamim* nor *Rabbi Yehuda* base themselves on the *Heichal* or *Ulam* at all. The entire derivation from the *Beit Hamikdash* may be only *Rav's* reading of the *Chachamim's* position, and not the position itself.

## Rav's Correction

### Recitation

This leads to a striking narrative. *Rav Yehuda bar Chiya* was reciting the *Mishnah* to *Chiya bar Rav* in the presence of *Rav* himself. He recited the *Mishnah* as stating that if a *mavoi* has a *tzuras hapesach*, even if it is wider than ten *amos*, one does not need to reduce it.

### Rav's Objection

*Rav* objected immediately. The *Mishnah* should be read differently: even with a *tzuras hapesach*, one must still reduce the opening to less than ten *amos*. According to *Rav*, the *tzuras hapesach* does not permit an opening wider than ten *amos* — it only addresses situations where the basic structural requirement is borderline, not where the width itself exceeds the maximum.

### Implication

The implication follows directly from *Rav's* position. If the *Chachamim* derived the parameters of a valid doorway from the *Heichal*, and the *Heichal* was exactly ten *amos* wide — even with a full *tzuras hapesach* — then no *tzuras hapesach* can authorize an opening wider than ten *amos*. The *Heichal's* doorway had the most complete doorframe possible, and it was still ten *amos*. A *tzuras hapesach* elsewhere cannot exceed that standard.

### Modern Eruv Impact

The practical implications for contemporary *eruvin* are significant. Modern *eruvin* are built with *tzuras hapesach* structures spanning distances far greater than ten *amos* — often spanning entire city blocks. According to *Rav's* position, this would not be valid: a *tzuras hapesach* cannot make permissible an opening wider than ten *amos*. According to the view that the *Chachamim* and *Rabbi Yehuda* are not deriving from the *Heichal* at all — that the parameters are independent rabbinic determinations — there would be no such limitation, and a *tzuras hapesach* could indeed validate a much wider gap. The question of which position is adopted has direct bearing on the validity of contemporary *eruvin* constructions, and we will continue to explore this as we proceed.
