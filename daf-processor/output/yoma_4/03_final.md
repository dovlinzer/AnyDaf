# Yoma 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Two Sources Debate

### Yochanan's Miluim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שִׁבְעַת יָמִים קוֹדֶם יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים מַפְרִישִׁין כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל מִבֵּיתוֹ לְלִשְׁכַּת פַּרְהֶדְרִין, וּשְׁנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים מִתַּלְמִידָיו שֶׁל מֹשֶׁה מוֹסְרִין לוֹ כׇּל שִׁבְעָה, כְּדֵי לְחַנְּכוֹ בָּעֲבוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA Seven days prior to Yom Kippur, the Sages would remove the High Priest from his house to the Chamber of *Parhedrin*, a chamber in the Temple complex. And two Torah scholars from among the students of Moses transmit the teachings to him all seven days in order to train him in the Temple service.

The fourth *daf* of *Yoma* opens with a question that is both halachic and deeply conceptual: what is the source for the seven-day separation of the *kohen gadol* prior to *Yom Kippur*, and by extension, the analogous separation of the *kohen* who performs the *parah adumah*? Until this point in the *sugya*, the dominant position has been that of Rabbi Yochanan, who derives the practice from the *miluim* — the inauguration of the Tabernacle — on the basis of the verse *ka'asher asah bayom hazeh tzivah Hashem la'asot lechaper aleichem*. The comparison to the *miluim* is in many respects natural and compelling: both involve a preparation for a specific *avodah* in the *Beit Hamikdash*, and the Gemara had already characterized the relationship as *techilah bamakom* — a sense of initiating or consecrating the space and its service. The *korban* of the *kohen gadol* is central, and the entire structure focuses on the person who must perform the *avodah* and what is required to make him capable of performing it.

As for the *parah adumah*, there is a second tradition in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that excludes it from this model entirely: the *parah adumah* is, on this view, only a rabbinic matter related to *tumah and taharah*, and does not belong to the fundamental framework derived from the *miluim*. The alternative tradition that includes the *parah adumah* within the model strains to identify a common denominator, but that tension is acknowledged.

### Lakish's Sinai Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מֵהֵיכָא קָא יָלְפַתְּ לַהּ — מִמִּלּוּאִים, אִי מָה מִלּוּאִים כׇּל הַכָּתוּב בָּהֶן מְעַכֵּב בָּהֶן, אַף הָכָא נָמֵי — כׇּל הַכָּתוּב בָּהֶן מְעַכֵּב בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: From where did you derive this? You derived it from the inauguration. If so, just as with regard to the inauguration, failure to perform all the details that are written in its regard invalidates it, so too here, with regard to Yom Kippur, failure to perform all the details that are written in its regard invalidates the Yom Kippur service.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: ״וּמַתְקִינִין לוֹ כֹּהֵן אַחֵר״, וְלָא קָתָנֵי ״מַפְרִישִׁין״!
>
> **Translation:** But didn't we learn in the mishna: And they would designate another priest in his stead, and it is not taught that with regard to the designated priest: Seven days before Yom Kippur they remove him from his house, although ultimately he may perform the Yom Kippur service? Apparently, failure to sequester the priest does not invalidate the service.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֶלָּא מָר מֵהֵיכָא יָלֵיף לַהּ? אָמַר: מִסִּינַי, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּשְׁכּוֹן כְּבוֹד ה׳ עַל הַר סִינַי וַיְכַסֵּהוּ הֶעָנָן שֵׁשֶׁת יָמִים וַיִּקְרָא אֶל מֹשֶׁה בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי״, מִכְּדֵי כְּתִיב: ״וַיִּקְרָא אֶל מֹשֶׁה בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי״, מַאי ״שֵׁשֶׁת יָמִים״? זֶה בָּנָה אָב: שֶׁכׇּל הַנִּכְנָס בְּמַחֲנֵה שְׁכִינָה, טָעוּן פְּרִישַׁת שִׁשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan said to Reish Lakish: Rather, from where do you, Master, derive the *halakha* of sequestering before Yom Kippur? Reish Lakish said to him: I derive it from Sinai, as it is written: "And the glory of the Lord abode upon Mount Sinai and the cloud covered him six days, and He called to Moses on the seventh day from the midst of the cloud" (Exodus 24:16). Now, since it states: "And He called to Moses on the seventh day," what is derived from the previous explicit mention of six days? These six days are mentioned as a paradigm, from which a general principle is derived that anyone who enters the camp of the Divine Presence, the site of the revelation at Mount Sinai, or the place where the Divine Presence rests, the Holy of Holies, requires prior sequestering for six days of sanctification.

Reish Lakish disputes the *miluim* derivation on a pointed logical ground: if the seven-day separation were truly modeled on the *miluim*, it would be indispensable, and yet we know that a substitute *kohen* — the understudy — would not require the same preparation even *bedi'avad*. This demonstrates, in Reish Lakish's view, that the practice cannot be grounded in the *miluim* framework at all. Instead, he grounds the requirement in Moshe's experience at Har Sinai, reading the relevant verse to mean that Moshe was enveloped in the cloud for six days before ascending to receive the Torah — a period of preparation before his encounter with God. The *kohen gadol* similarly requires such preparation, though not for seven days: the seventh day is added as a precaution against *tumah* and is not part of the fundamental model. Likewise, the *parah adumah* would not be grounded in this model; its seven-day separation would be a *tumah* concern rather than a reflection of the core principle.

This distinction is conceptually significant. Reish Lakish's model shifts the entire frame of reference: the preparation is not about enabling an *avodah*, not about the ritual mechanics of what the *kohen gadol* must do on *Yom Kippur*, but about the encounter with God itself. Just as Moshe needed six days of preparation before standing in the divine presence, the *kohen gadol* needs preparation before entering the *Kodesh Hakodashim* — before stepping into *Machaneh Shechinah* in its most precise and exalted sense, not merely the *Beit Hamikdash* in general but the innermost chamber where the divine presence is concentrated. And thinking about how this framework applies to us, it places the emphasis on the inner preparation for encountering God rather than on the proper execution of the Temple service.

### Water vs. Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: בִּשְׁלָמָא לְדִידִי דְּיָלֵיפְנָא מִמִּלּוּאִים, הַיְינוּ דְּתַנְיָא: זֶה וְזֶה מַזִּין עָלָיו כׇּל שִׁבְעָה מִכׇּל חַטָּאוֹת שֶׁהָיוּ שָׁם, דַּהֲוַאי נָמֵי הַזָּאָה בְּמִלּוּאִים. אֶלָּא לְדִידָךְ דְּיָלְפַתְּ מִסִּינַי, הַזָּאָה בְּסִינַי מִי הֲוַאי?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan said to Reish Lakish: Granted, according to my opinion, that I derive the *halakha* of sequestering from the inauguration, that explains that which is taught in the *baraita*: With regard to both this priest engaged in the burning of the red heifer and that High Priest prior to Yom Kippur, one sprinkles upon him for all seven days the purification water mixed with ashes from all the previous red heifer sin-offerings that were safeguarded there in the Temple. The reason for this practice is that there was also sprinkling during the inauguration. However, according to your opinion, that you derive it from Sinai, was there in fact sprinkling at Sinai? According to your opinion, why are the priests sprinkled?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וּלְטַעְמָיךְ מִי נִיחָא? בְּמִלּוּאִים דָּם, הָכָא מַיִם! הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא, דְּתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: נִכְנְסוּ מַיִם תַּחַת דָּם. אֶלָּא לְדִידָךְ, הַזָּאָה בְּסִינַי מִי הֲוַאי?
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish said to him: And according to your reasoning, does it work out well? At the inauguration, the sprinkling was with blood; here, the sprinkling was with water. Rabbi Yoḥanan answered: That is not difficult, as Rabbi Ḥiyya taught: Water replaced blood, but both have the status of sprinkling. However, according to your reasoning, at Sinai, was there sprinkling at all?

The Gemara at the top of *daf dalet amud aleph* records Rabbi Yochanan's response to Reish Lakish. Rabbi Yochanan says: *bishlama lidi* — it works well according to me, since I derive the seven-day separation from the *miluim*, for that fits precisely with a *baraita* we will encounter shortly: *zeh v'zeh mazzin alav kol shiva mikol chatatot shehayu sham* — both the *kohen* preparing for the *parah adumah* and the *kohen gadol* preparing for *Yom Kippur* would have the ashes of the *parah adumah* sprinkled upon them every single day throughout the seven-day period, drawing from all the various red heifers whose ashes were stored. Rashi explains that they would preserve the ashes of earlier *parot adumot* rather than wait until one supply was entirely exhausted, and indeed cites a tradition that the ashes of Moshe's original *parah* never ran out entirely. The sprinkling was not merely a functional act of achieving *taharah*; if that were all, one would sprinkle only on the third and seventh days. Sprinkling every single day points to a deeper ritual dimension. And what is the parallel? The *miluim* itself involved some act of sprinkling — with blood — every one of the seven days. The daily sprinkling in the preparation for *Yom Kippur* is thus analogous to the daily sprinkling throughout the *miluim*.

Rabbi Yochanan presses his point: *ela ledidach* — but according to you, Reish Lakish, *haza b'Sinai mi havi* — where does any preparatory sprinkling exist at Har Sinai? This speaks strongly in favor of the *miluim* model. Reish Lakish responds: *ultamich mi nicha* — but wait, why is it good even according to you? In the *miluim* the sprinkling used blood; here we use water — the waters of the *parah adumah*. These are not the same thing. The Gemara immediately deflects this: *ha lo kashya*, for Rav Chiyya teaches in a *baraita* that *nichnesu mayim tachat dam* — water entered in place of blood. We acknowledge the substitution and do not claim identity. The reason is clear: there are no fixed *korbanot* in the days leading up to *Yom Kippur* from which blood could be taken, and using the blood of a *korban* for sprinkling without explicit Torah mandate would be a misuse of the offering. Therefore, the only meaningful sprinkling available is that of the *parah adumah*, and we apply the model as best the circumstances allow. But the point stands against Reish Lakish: *ela ledidach, haza b'Sinai mi havi* — according to your model, there is no basis for the sprinkling at all.

### Initiation vs. Encounter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַעֲלָה בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish said to him: The Sages merely established a higher standard, and this sprinkling is not a requirement.

Reish Lakish's response to the challenge is *maala b'alma* — a qualitative enhancement, something added once the core practice is already in place. His position is that the fundamental requirement — analogous to Moshe's preparation at Har Sinai — calls only for six days, with a seventh added for *tumah* concerns. The sprinkling is similarly an elaboration: once the practice of separation exists, the ritual is enriched with additional acts that deepen the sense of preparation and heighten the solemnity of what is being built toward. Rashi suggests that the sprinkling functions as a form of preparation for entering *Machaneh Shechinah* — not merely to achieve *taharah*, but to create and sustain a daily ritual of readiness. Each day of sprinkling intensifies the cumulative experience of buildup, of the *kohen gadol*'s consciousness that he is approaching something extraordinary, that he is preparing to enter into the most direct form of divine encounter available to any human being. Reish Lakish is entirely willing to concede that certain features of the seven-day separation — the *tumah* considerations, the sprinkling — do not flow organically from his model. But at the core, he insists: conceptually, this is about standing before God, about the preparation for that encounter, not about a *Beit Hamikdash* ritual that enables the *avodah* in the manner Rabbi Yochanan envisions.

The two models thus represent genuinely different conceptual frameworks. For Rabbi Yochanan, the axis of the preparation is the *avodah* — the *kohen gadol* must be prepared to perform the service, and the seven-day separation, like the *miluim*, is a process of enabling and initiating that service. For Reish Lakish, the axis is the *encounter* — the *kohen gadol* must be prepared to enter into the divine presence, and everything in the seven days is oriented toward that.

## Textual Derivations

### Baraita on Miluim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ. תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״בְּזֹאת יָבֹא אַהֲרֹן אֶל הַקֹּדֶשׁ״, בְּמַה שֶּׁאָמוּר בָּעִנְיָן, מַאי הִיא — בְּעִנְיָן דְּמִלּוּאִים. וּמָה אָמוּר בְּעִנְיָן דְּמִלּוּאִים — אַהֲרֹן פֵּירַשׁ שִׁבְעָה וְשִׁמֵּשׁ יוֹם אֶחָד, וּמֹשֶׁה מָסַר לוֹ כׇּל שִׁבְעָה כְּדֵי לְחַנְּכוֹ בָּעֲבוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** A *baraita* was taught in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan that the sequestering is derived from the inauguration; and a *baraita* was taught in accordance with the opinion of Reish Lakish that it is derived from Sinai. A *baraita* was taught in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan: It was stated with regard to the inauguration: "With this Aaron will come into the Sanctuary, with a young bull for a sin-offering and a ram for a burnt-offering" (Leviticus 16:3). To what is the term: With this, referring? It is referring to that which is stated in the matter. What is the matter? It is the matter of the inauguration. In the manner that the priest was prepared for the inauguration, so too is he prepared for Yom Kippur. And what is stated in the matter of the inauguration? It is that Aaron the priest withdrew for seven days and served one day, and Moses transmitted the Torah guidelines to him all seven days in order to train him in the Sanctuary service.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף לְדוֹרוֹת כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל פּוֹרֵשׁ שִׁבְעָה וּמְשַׁמֵּשׁ יוֹם אֶחָד, וּשְׁנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים מִתַּלְמִידָיו שֶׁל מֹשֶׁה — לְאַפּוֹקֵי צַדּוּקִין, מוֹסְרִין לוֹ כׇּל שִׁבְעָה כְּדֵי לְחַנְּכוֹ בָּעֲבוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** And throughout the generations as well, the High Priest withdraws seven days prior to Yom Kippur and serves one day. And two Torah scholars from among the students of Moses, to the exclusion of Sadducees, who are not students of Moses, transmit the Torah guidelines to him all seven days in order to train him in the Sanctuary service.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: שִׁבְעַת יָמִים קוֹדֶם יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים מַפְרִישִׁין כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל מִבֵּיתוֹ לְלִשְׁכַּת פַּרְהֶדְרִין. וּכְשֵׁם שֶׁמַּפְרִישִׁין כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, כָּךְ מַפְרִישִׁין כֹּהֵן הַשּׂוֹרֵף אֶת הַפָּרָה לַלִּשְׁכָּה שֶׁעַל פְּנֵי הַבִּירָה צָפוֹנָה מִזְרָחָה. וְאֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה מַזִּין עָלָיו כׇּל שִׁבְעָה מִכׇּל חַטָּאוֹת שֶׁהָיוּ שָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** From there the Sages said in the mishna: Seven days prior to Yom Kippur the Sages would remove the High Priest, who performs the entire Yom Kippur service, from his house to the Chamber of *Parhedrin*; and just as the Sages would remove the High Priest, so do they remove the priest who burns the heifer, from his house to the chamber that was before the *bira* at the northeast corner of the courtyard on the Temple Mount. And with regard to both this priest whom the Sages sequester prior to Yom Kippur and that priest whom the Sages sequester prior to engaging in the burning of the heifer, one sprinkles upon him, for all seven days of sequestering, the purification water with ashes from all the previous red heifer sin-offerings that were safeguarded there in the Temple.

The Gemara now brings a *baraita* in support of each position. For Rabbi Yochanan: *tanya k'vateih d'Rabbi Yochanan* — the derivation begins from the verse *u-vezot yavo Aharon el hakodesh*, "with this Aaron shall enter into the sanctuary." The word *bezot* — in the feminine — does not straightforwardly match *par ben bakar*, a bull, and *ayil*, a ram, both masculine. Rashi explains that the feminine *bezot* points to some additional process — a *perishah*, a separation — that must accompany Aaron's entry into the *Kodesh Hakodashim*. And what process? *Be-inyan miluim* — what we find in the *miluim*. What does the *miluim* tell us? *Aharon piresh shivah ve-shimesh yom echad* — Aaron separated for seven days and then served on the eighth. Furthermore, *u-Moshe masar lo kol shivah* — Moshe served as the effective *kohen* throughout the seven days of the *miluim*, *kedei le-chanecho ba-avodah* — in order to prepare Aaron for the *avodah*. The preparation was not a passive retreat but an active training process.

*Ve-af le-dorot* — and so too for all generations: the *kohen gadol* separates for seven days and serves on the day of *Yom Kippur*. And *shene talmidei chachamim mi-talmidav shel Moshe* — two *talmidei chachamim* drawn from the disciples of Moshe serve as his companions throughout the seven days, *mosrin lo kol shivah, kedei le-chanecho ba-avodah* — transmitting to him the entire sequence of the *avodah*, preparing him as Moshe prepared Aaron. The *baraita* concludes with a reference to the passage in the *Mishnah* that the *kohen gadol* is separated from his house seven days before *Yom Kippur* to the *Lishkat Parhedrin*.

### Tzedukim & Sages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תֹּאמַר: בְּמִלּוּאִים דָּם, הָכָא מַיִם, אָמַרְתָּ: נִכְנְסוּ מַיִם תַּחַת דָּם. וְאוֹמֵר ״כַּאֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה בַּיּוֹם הַזֶּה צִוָּה ה׳ לַעֲשׂוֹת לְכַפֵּר עֲלֵיכֶם״. ״לַעֲשׂוֹת״ — אֵלּוּ מַעֲשֵׂה פָרָה, ״לְכַפֵּר״ — אֵלּוּ מַעֲשֵׂה יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** And if you say that at the inauguration the sprinkling was with blood, and here the sprinkling was with water, you said: Water replaced blood. And it says in the verse: "As has been done this day, so the Lord has commanded to do, to make atonement for you" (Leviticus 8:34). To do, these are the actions performed in the burning of the red heifer; to make atonement, these are the actions performed on Yom Kippur. This *baraita*, then, is proof for the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan.

The *baraita*'s specification that the two sages represent *talmidei Moshe* carries a pointed implication: *le-afokei Tzedukim* — to exclude the *Sadducees*. The two sages must be representatives of the authentic *mesorah*, the true tradition, as opposed to what the *baraita* regards as a perverted or false tradition. They stand in for Moshe — the transmitter of the unbroken chain — and in doing so they validate the *kohen gadol*'s service as continuous with that original chain of transmission. Each day they would walk the animals before the *kohen gadol*, instruct him, and rehearse the *avodah* with him, precisely paralleling what Moshe did for Aaron throughout the *miluim*. The entire structure is reconstructed: a substitute Moshe in the form of two *talmidei chachamim*, a successor-Aaron in the form of the *kohen gadol*, and a seven-day preparation that mirrors the *miluim* almost exactly.

The *baraita* then adds, *u-cheshem she-mafrishim kohen gadol kach mafrishim kohen ha-soref et ha-parah* — just as one separates the *kohen gadol*, so does one separate the *kohen* who burns the *parah adumah*. Rashi notes that this addition sits uneasily within the elegant model because it doesn't fit the *miluim* parallel. The *miluim* analogy was constructed specifically for the *kohen gadol* on *Yom Kippur*; the *parah adumah* is a different matter entirely, which is why, as noted earlier, one tradition attributed to Rabbi Yochanan excluded it. According to Rashi, this inclusion is therefore best understood as an appended rabbinic extension — not the *ikkar* of what the verse teaches — and the *parah adumah*'s seven-day separation is in a different category from the *kohen gadol*'s.

### Bezot Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי ״בְּזֹאת״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְגוּפֵיהּ, בְּפַר בֶּן בָּקָר לְחַטָּאת וְאַיִל לְעוֹלָה! אָמְרִי: אִי לְקׇרְבָּן לְחוֹדֵיהּ, לֵימָא קְרָא ״בְּזֶה״ אוֹ ״בְּאֵלֶּה״, מַאי ״בָּזֹאת״ — שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** But the term: With this [*bezot*], is required for the meaning of the verse itself; the priest is required to bring a young bull for a sin-offering and a ram for a burnt-offering. The Sages say in response: If the term comes to teach only with regard to the offerings, let the verse say: With this [*bezeh*], in the masculine, referring to the bull, or: With these [*be'elleh*], referring to the bull and the ram. What, then, may be derived from the use of the feminine term ***bezot*,** which refers to neither the bull nor the ram? Learn from it two conclusions; one with regard to the offerings and one with regard to sequestering.

A potential objection now arises: the verse *bezot yavo Aharon el hakodesh* contains the word *bezot* — but *bezot* is needed simply for its straightforward meaning, to specify the *korban* through which Aaron enters! How can you use it to imply an entire seven-day preparatory process? The Gemara answers that if the Torah had intended only to specify the *korban*, it would have written *bezeh* or *be-eileh* — forms that match the masculine *par* and *ayil*. The use of the feminine *bezot* signals that something additional is implied: *mashma mi-na tarti* — the language teaches two things at once. The *korban* is specified, and simultaneously a further preparatory process is required. The grammatical anomaly is the opening through which the *drasha* enters.

### Future Generations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְאוֹמֵר״? וְכִי תֵּימָא: יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים קַמָּא הוּא דְּבָעֵי פְּרִישָׁה, כִּדְאַשְׁכְּחַן בְּמִלּוּאִים אֲבָל בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים דְּעָלְמָא לָא. אִי נָמֵי: כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל קַמָּא הוּא דְּבָעֵי פְּרִישָׁה, אֲבָל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל בְּעָלְמָא לָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: ״כַּאֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה וְכוּ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** What is the meaning of the term: And it says? Why does the *baraita* cite an additional proof from another verse? Why wasn't the first proof sufficient? And if you say that it is on the first Yom Kippur when Aaron performed the service that the High Priest requires sequestering, as we find in the inauguration when the priests were sequestered before being consecrated as priests, but on Yom Kippur in general, no, subsequent High Priests do not require sequestering; or alternatively, if you say: It is the first High Priest who requires sequestering, as did all the priests during the inauguration, but subsequent High Priests in general, no, they do not require sequestering before Yom Kippur; then come and hear that which it says in the verse: "As has been done** this day, so the Lord has commanded to do," meaning that this is a mitzva for all generations.

Even with *bezot* doing double duty, there remains a further hermeneutic need, and this is where *ka'asher asah bayom hazeh* becomes essential. One might have read *bezot yavo Aharon* as applying only to the first time Aaron entered the *Kodesh Hakodashim* — a one-time initiatory act, analogous to the *miluim* which, after all, was performed only once to inaugurate the *kohanim* and the *Mishkan*. Indeed, the entire *parasha* of *Acharei Mot* is addressed to Aaron rather than to future generations, which the Vilna Gaon pointedly observes: the parasha does not even mention *Yom Kippur* explicitly until its very end. The primary framing is *bezot yavo Aharon* — here is what Aaron must do if he wishes to enter. The annual *Yom Kippur* observance is appended at the conclusion. One might therefore have concluded that the seven-day separation was required only for Aaron himself, as a personal initiation, or only for the first *Yom Kippur* — the first entry into the *Kodesh Hakodashim* — as an inauguration of that space.

The verse *ka'asher asah bayom hazeh tzivah Hashem la'asot lechaper aleichem* closes this interpretive opening: *la'asot* — to do this in future generations. This is what the Gemara means by *tashma* — "come and hear": the two possible limitations (first *kohen gadol* only, or first *Yom Kippur* only) are foreclosed by this verse, which commands that the practice continue *le-dorot*. And it is worth noting that these two limitations correspond precisely to the two ideas the Gemara had already raised in the closing lines of the previous *daf*: the uniqueness of Aaron as a person and the inauguration of the *Kodesh Hakodashim* as a space. Both were potential grounds for restricting the requirement to a single historical moment, and both are overridden by *ka'asher asah bayom hazeh*.

This also illuminates something important for Reish Lakish's position: the entire framing of *bezot yavo Aharon* — not a general rule but a focused account of Aaron's access to God — resonates deeply with the idea that the preparation is fundamentally about the encounter, not merely about the mechanics of service. Even within Rabbi Yochanan's framework, the *parasha*'s structural emphasis on Aaron as an individual approaching God supports what Reish Lakish highlights.

### Asmachta Framework

Before moving on, it is important to pause at a significant qualification introduced by Tosafot. The discussion has proceeded as though the seven-day separation and its associated practices — especially the daily sprinkling — are grounded in a genuine *d'Oraita* requirement. Tosafot challenges this directly: *delav le-gamrei drasha gemura hi* — this is not truly a complete Torah-level derivation. The substitution of water for blood is only possible, Tosafot argues, because the entire derivation from the *miluim* is an *asmachta b'alma* — a rabbinic practice that finds textual support in the Torah without being strictly commanded by it.

Moreover, Tosafot notes — citing Rashi's commentary on the *Chumash* — that the verse *ka'asher asah bayom hazeh* in the *miluim* passage most naturally refers not to the eighth day but to the first day: Moshe told the people on day one of the *miluim*, "continue this practice for the next seven days." On this reading, the verse is not establishing a future Yom Kippur obligation at all; its *peshat* is internal to the *miluim* narrative itself. *V'keivan d'asmachta b'alma hu* — since it is only *asmachta*, we need not be troubled by the substitution of water for blood, for the entire structure is a rabbinic framework that models itself on the *miluim* without claiming to replicate it with full halakhic precision.

The more interesting question, however, is not whether to classify this as *d'Oraita* or *d'Rabbanan*, but what the underlying conceptual model is. And for Rabbi Yochanan, that model is unambiguously the *miluim*: the initiation and ongoing annual preparation of the *kohen gadol* for his unique service, with all the attending ritual elaboration that concept entails.

## Chronology Dispute

### Moshe in Cloud

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מֹשֶׁה עָלָה בֶּעָנָן, וְנִתְכַּסָּה בֶּעָנָן, וְנִתְקַדֵּשׁ בֶּעָנָן, כְּדֵי לְקַבֵּל תּוֹרָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל בִּקְדוּשָּׁה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּשְׁכּוֹן כְּבוֹד ה׳ עַל הַר סִינַי״ — זֶה הָיָה מַעֲשֶׂה אַחַר עֲשֶׂרֶת הַדִּבְּרוֹת, שֶׁהָיוּ תְּחִלָּה לְאַרְבָּעִים יוֹם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי.
>
> **Translation:** And a *baraita* was taught in accordance with the opinion of Reish Lakish that sequestering is derived from Sinai: Moses ascended in the cloud, and was covered in the cloud, and was sanctified in the cloud, in order to receive the Torah for the Jewish people in sanctity, as it is stated: "And the glory of the Lord abode upon Mount Sinai and the cloud covered him six days, and He called to Moses on the seventh day from the midst of the cloud" (Exodus 24:16). This was an incident that occurred after the revelation of the Ten Commandments to the Jewish people, and these six days were the beginning of the forty days that Moses was on the mountain (see Exodus 24:18); this is the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili.

The Gemara now brings a *baraita* supporting Reish Lakish: *Moshe ala be-anan ve-niskasa be-anan ve-niskadesh be-anan kedai le-kabel Torah le-Yisrael bi-kedusha* — Moshe ascended into the cloud, was covered by the cloud, and was sanctified by the cloud, in order to receive the Torah for Israel in a state of holiness. The verse cited is *vayishkon kevod Hashem al Har Sinai* — the cloud of God rested on Har Sinai. The *baraita* adds: *zeh hayah ma'aseh achar Aseret HaDibrot she-hayu techilah le-arba'im yom* — this event occurred after the Ten Commandments had been given, and it initiated the forty days that Moshe would spend atop Har Sinai. This is the view of Rabbi Yose HaGelili.

To appreciate why this reading is at all contested, it is necessary to understand the structure of the *pesukim* at the end of *Parashat Mishpatim*. What is not always appreciated is that *parashat Mishpatim* is a direct continuation of the *Aseret HaDibrot* — the civil laws are an elaboration of what was communicated at Sinai. Throughout the entire *parasha*, Moshe is still on the mountain in the same encounter. At the very end of *Mishpatim*, the narrative shifts: God tells Moshe to come back up with Aaron, Nadav, Avihu, and seventy elders. Moshe descends, reports everything to the people — *vayavo Moshe vayisaper la-am et kol divrei Hashem ve-et kol ha-mishpatim* — the people respond with *na'aseh*, a covenant is enacted, the *dam habrit* is sprinkled on the people. Then the elders ascend with Moshe for that brief, extraordinary vision of God. Then comes the command: *aleh eilai ha-harah ve-heyeh sham* — come up and be there, and I will give you the *luchos*. Moshe and Yehoshua rise; to the elders he says, stay here. And then: *vayishkon kevod Hashem al Har Sinai vayichaseihu he-anan sheshet yamim vayikra el Moshe bayom ha-shevi'i* — the cloud covered the mountain for six days and God called to Moshe on the seventh — *vayavo Moshe be-toch he-anan vaya'al el ha-har vayehi Moshe ba-har arba'im yom ve-arba'im lailah*.

The *peshat* of these *pesukim* is strikingly clear: after *matan Torah*, after the *dam habrit*, after the vision of the elders, God calls Moshe up for a forty-day stay, and there is a six-day period of the cloud covering the mountain before God calls to Moshe on the seventh day. This is exactly Reish Lakish's position, and Rabbi Yose HaGelili reads it accordingly: those six days were preparation, those forty days were Moshe's time with God, and it is this model that underlies the *kohen gadol*'s seven-day separation.

### Yose: After Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי סָבַר לַהּ כְּתַנָּא קַמָּא, דְּאָמַר בְּשִׁשָּׁה בַּחוֹדֶשׁ נִיתְּנָה תּוֹרָה, הִלְכָּךְ זֶה הָיָה מַעֲשֶׂה אַחַר עֲשֶׂרֶת הַדִּבְּרוֹת. ״וַיִּשְׁכּוֹן כְּבוֹד ה׳ עַל הַר סִינַי וַיְכַסֵּהוּ הֶעָנָן שֵׁשֶׁת יָמִים״ — לְמֹשֶׁה, ״וַיִּקְרָא אֶל מֹשֶׁה בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי״ — לְקַבּוֹלֵי שְׁאָר תּוֹרָה. דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ ״וַיִּשְׁכּוֹן כְּבוֹד ה׳״ מֵרֹאשׁ חוֹדֶשׁ, ״וַיְכַסֵּהוּ הֶעָנָן לָהָר וַיִּקְרָא אֶל מֹשֶׁה בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי״ לְקַבּוֹלֵי עֲשֶׂרֶת הַדִּבְּרוֹת, הָא קַבִּילוּ לְהוּ מִשִּׁשָּׁה, וְהָא אִסְתַּלַּק עָנָן מִשִּׁשָּׁה!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yosei HaGelili holds in accordance with the opinion of the first *tanna* in the *baraita*, who said that it was on the sixth of the month that the Torah was given; therefore, this incident occurred after the revelation of the Ten Commandments. That is why he explains the verse "And the glory of the Lord abode on Mount Sinai and the cloud covered him for six days" to mean that the cloud covered Moses, and He called to Moses on the seventh day to receive the rest of the Torah. As, should it enter your mind to interpret the verse as follows: "And the glory of the Lord abode" from the New Moon of Sivan; "And the cloud covered it," the mountain; "And He called to Moses on the seventh day," to receive only the Ten Commandments; didn't they already receive the Ten Commandments on the sixth of the month, and the cloud that was on the mountain already departed on the sixth of the month?

Rabbi Yose HaGelili's reading is thus precisely aligned with what one would call the *peshat* of the *pesukim*. The cloud covered Har Sinai, Moshe was enveloped in it for six days, and on the seventh God called him — *le-kabalei she'ar Torah* — to receive the remainder of the Torah. The forty days of Moshe on the mountain began on that seventh day, after the *Aseret HaDibrot* had already been given. This is Rabbi Yose HaGelili's position: *shene'emar vayishkon kevod Hashem al Har Sinai* — that *parasha* at the end of *Mishpatim* is an event chronologically subsequent to the *Aseret HaDibrot*, not a recap of events preceding them.

### Akiva: Before Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּאָמַר: בְּשִׁבְעָה בַּחוֹדֶשׁ נִיתְּנָה תּוֹרָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הַיְינוּ דְּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּשִׁיתְּסַר בְּתַמּוּז נִשְׁתַּבְּרוּ הַלּוּחוֹת: עֶשְׂרִין וְאַרְבְּעָה דְּסִיוָן, וְשִׁיתְּסַר דְּתַמּוּז, מָלוּ לְהוּ אַרְבְּעִין יוֹמִין דַּהֲוָה בָּהָר, וּבְשִׁיתְּסַר בְּתַמּוּז נְחֵית וַאֲתָא וְתַבְרִינְהוּ לְלוּחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** And Rabbi Akiva holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, who said that on the seventh of the month the Torah was given to the Jewish people. That is why Moses was summoned on the seventh of the month immediately after the revelation of the Ten Commandments. The Gemara asks: Granted, according to the opinion of Rabbi Akiva that the Torah was given on the seventh of Sivan and Moses then proceeded to climb the mountain and remain there for forty days, that explains the calculation that you find: On the seventeenth of Tammuz the tablets were shattered, according to the standard tradition. How so? Calculate twenty-four days until the end of Sivan and sixteen days of Tammuz; they total the forty days that he was on the mountain. On the seventeenth of Tammuz he descended from the mountain and came and shattered the tablets.

Rabbi Akiva disagrees in a striking way. He reads *vayishkon kevod Hashem* not as the cloud covering Moshe but as the cloud covering the mountain — *vayichaseihu he-anan le-har*, the cloud covered the mountain, not Moshe. And this entire passage, in his reading, is not a narrative of events following the *Aseret HaDibrot* but a summary-recap of everything from *Rosh Chodesh* — the first of Sivan, when Israel arrived at Har Sinai. The six days of the cloud were the six days leading up to the *Aseret HaDibrot*, and the call to Moshe on the seventh day was the call immediately preceding the giving of the Torah.

Why does Rabbi Akiva take this unusual reading? One reason emerges from the text of *Parashat Yitro* itself: there, God calls to Moshe just before the *Aseret HaDibrot* — *vayikra Hashem el Moshe rosh ha-har vaya'al Moshe* — and the content of that call is to warn the people to stand back. At the end of *Mishpatim*, God again calls Moshe — *vayikra el Moshe bayom ha-shevi'i mitoch he-anan* — but without explicit content. Rabbi Akiva understands this second *vayikra* as a summation of the same event narrated in *Yitro*, with the entire closing paragraph of *Mishpatim* functioning as a retrospective account, not a new one. The cloud covered the mountain from *Rosh Chodesh*, the *Aseret HaDibrot* were given on the seventh day, and then Moshe entered the cloud and ascended for forty days — all of this is retrospectively summarized.

But this creates a problem for Reish Lakish's model. If the six days of cloud-covering preceded the *Aseret HaDibrot* rather than following them, and if the cloud covered the mountain rather than Moshe personally, then there is no six-day personal preparation of Moshe — and Reish Lakish's derivation loses its textual basis. Moreover, as was already noted, Moshe was going back and forth throughout the *Sinai* narrative; a continuous six-day preparation simply cannot be fitted into that picture if the events took place before *matan Torah*.

### Vayikra Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הַיְינוּ דְּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּשִׁיתְּסַר בְּתַמּוּז נִשְׁתַּבְּרוּ הַלּוּחוֹת
>
> **Translation:** Granted, according to the opinion of Rabbi Akiva that the Torah was given on the seventh of Sivan and Moses then proceeded to climb the mountain and remain there for forty days, that explains the calculation that you find: On the seventeenth of Tammuz the tablets were shattered.

What then does Rabbi Akiva do with the *vayikra el Moshe bayom ha-shevi'i*? If Moshe and all of Israel were standing there together at that moment, the calling is not to summon Moshe into a cloud or to draw him up the mountain for a forty-day stay — those events came later. So what was the *vayikra* for? This question needs resolution.

### Honor Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֹשֶׁה וְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל עוֹמְדִין, וְלֹא בָּא הַכָּתוּב אֶלָּא לַחְלֹק כָּבוֹד לְמֹשֶׁה.
>
> **Translation:** Moses and all of the Jewish people were standing and listening and the verse comes only to accord deference to Moses.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: לֹא בָּא הַכָּתוּב אֶלָּא לְמָרֵק אֲכִילָה וּשְׁתִיָּה שֶׁבְּמֵעָיו, לְשׂוּמוֹ כְּמַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Natan says: Moses was in fact called to enter the cloud; however, his entrance was not for the purpose of sequestering and purifying him, rather, the verse comes only to cleanse the food and drink that was in his intestines, to render him like the ministering angels who require neither food nor drink.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מַתְיָא בֶּן חָרָשׁ אוֹמֵר: לֹא בָּא הַכָּתוּב אֶלָּא לְאַיֵּים עָלָיו, כְּדֵי שֶׁתְּהֵא תּוֹרָה נִיתֶּנֶת בְּאֵימָה, בִּרְתֵת וּבְזִיעַ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עִבְדוּ אֶת ה׳ בְּיִרְאָה וְגִילוּ בִּרְעָדָה״. מַאי ״וְגִילוּ בִּרְעָדָה״? אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר מַתְנָה אָמַר רַב: בִּמְקוֹם גִּילָה שָׁם תְּהֵא רְעָדָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Matya ben Ḥarash says: The verse calling Moses into the cloud comes only to intimidate him, to instill in him a sense of awe of the Creator, so that the Torah would be delivered with reverence, with quaking and with trembling, as it is stated: "Serve the Lord with awe, and rejoice with trembling" (Psalms 2:11). Apropos the end of the verse, the Gemara asks: What is the meaning of "and rejoice with trembling"? Joy and trembling seem contradictory. Rav Adda bar Mattana said that Rav said: Where there is the joy of fulfilling a mitzva, there will be the trembling of the awe of Heaven there.

The Gemara records the answer: *velo ba ha-kasuv ela le-chalek kavod le-Moshe* — the verse came only to give honor to Moshe. It was a public singling out of Moshe before all of Israel at the moment of *matan Torah* — a calling of his name before the assembled nation, to distinguish him and accord him recognition as the one through whom the Torah was being transmitted. This is, of course, the same explanation given by Rabbi Elazar: *Moshe ve-chulan omdin velo ba ha-kasuv ela le-chalek lo kavod le-Moshe*. The Gemara notes that Rabbi Akiva's position supports Rabbi Elazar's formulation, and the two are now aligned.

A question immediately arises: if the divine voice that called Moshe was heard only by Moshe and not by the assembled people — *moshe shama ve-chulan lo shamu* — how does calling his name give him public honor? The Gemara later provides two resolutions. The first: *ha b'Sinai ha b'Ohel Moed* — at Sinai, it was a one-time event in which everyone heard; from the *Ohel Moed* onward, God spoke to Moshe privately. The second: *ha b'kriya ha b'dibur* — even at *Ohel Moed*, one might distinguish between the initial calling of Moshe's name, which was heard by all, and the content of the subsequent divine speech, which only Moshe heard. Each day, in this reading, the nation could hear God call Moshe's name — a public declaration of his unique status — even if the message that followed was exclusively between God and Moshe.

## Six Days Meaning

### Natan: Purification

The Gemara records several explanations of the six-day preparation, offered by different *tanna'im*. Rabbi Natan says: *velo ba ha-kasuv ela le-morek achilah ve-shtiyah she-bi-m'av la-asomo ke-malakhei ha-sharet* — the text came only to purge Moshe's physical digestive substance, to make him like the ministering angels. Rashi reads this as directly connected to the period in the cloud: the six days served to cleanse Moshe of his corporeality, to strip away as much physical substance as possible so that he could sustain forty days without eating or drinking — *lechem lo achal u-mayim lo shatiti*. The preparation for the divine encounter required diminishing the body's claim on the person, approximating the angelic state that continuous divine proximity demands. Notably, Rashi observes that this explanation is specific to Moshe and cannot straightforwardly be generalized to the *kohen gadol* on *Yom Kippur*, for we do not require the *kohen gadol* to abstain from food throughout his seven-day preparation.

### Matya: Fear & Awe

Rabbi Matya ben Cheresh offers a different explanation: *velo ba ha-kasuv ela le-ayem alav* — the text came only to strike fear upon him. *Kedei she-tehei Torah nitenet be-eimah be-ret u-ve-ze'ah* — so that the Torah should be given in awe, trembling, and perspiration. The Maharsha notes that this explanation and Rabbi Natan's, placed as they are immediately after Rabbi Akiva's position in the *baraita*, are best read as explanations not of the six days themselves but of the *vayikra el Moshe* — what was the function of that act of calling? One answer was to give honor. Another, Rabbi Natan's, is that even a single divine call was sufficient to purge Moshe's physicality. Rabbi Matya ben Cheresh's answer is that it was to instill fear in Moshe's heart before he received the Torah.

### Rejoicing + Fear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עִבְדוּ אֶת ה׳ בְּיִרְאָה וְגִילוּ בִּרְעָדָה״. מַאי ״וְגִילוּ בִּרְעָדָה״? אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר מַתְנָה אָמַר רַב: בִּמְקוֹם גִּילָה שָׁם תְּהֵא רְעָדָה.
>
> **Translation:** as it is stated: "Serve the Lord with awe, and rejoice with trembling" (Psalms 2:11). What is the meaning of "and rejoice with trembling"? Joy and trembling seem contradictory. Rav Adda bar Mattana said that Rav said: Where there is the joy of fulfilling a mitzva, there will be the trembling of the awe of Heaven there.

The *baraita* supports this with the verse *ivdu et Hashem be-yirah ve-gilu bi-ra'adah* — serve God with fear and rejoice in trembling. The Gemara asks: *mai ve-gilu bi-ra'adah*? What does it mean to rejoice in trembling? Rav Adda bar Matna said in the name of Rav: *bi-mekom gilah sham tehei ra'adah* — at the very place of rejoicing, there too must be trembling. The reception of Torah is a supreme elevation — and it is precisely that elevation that must be accompanied by the profoundest awe. The two do not cancel each other; they coexist, each intensifying the other.

This balance resonates powerfully with the *Yom Kippur* context. The death of the *shenei benei Aharon* at the threshold of *Kodesh Hakodashim* stands as the paradigm of what happens when one approaches the divine presence without the proper disposition of fear. *Bezot yavo Aharon* — with this, and only this rigorous preparation, may Aaron enter. The *vayikra*, the trembling that accompanied it, and the entire preparatory process serve to hold together the exaltation of encounter with the sobriety of standing before the living God.

Rashi reads these explanations as connected to the position of Rabbi Yose HaGelili, where there is a genuine six-day preparatory period in the cloud. The Maharsha, however, suggests they may equally fit Rabbi Akiva's reading, in which case they explain the significance of the single act of *vayikra el Moshe* rather than of an extended period of preparation. On either reading, the conceptual message is the same: the encounter with the divine demands a total reorientation of the person — physical, emotional, and spiritual.

## Soloveitchik Analysis

### Lakish Recap

At this point it is worth pausing to draw together the two conceptual models as they have emerged across the *sugya*, with the help of Rav Soloveitchik's analysis in Rav Aharon Lichtenstein's work on *Maseches Yoma* — a *daf*-by-*daf* presentation of Rav Soloveitchik's *chiddushim* on the *masechta*, of which the first extended treatment addresses precisely this debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish. Reish Lakish's model, Rav Soloveitchik suggests, is in a certain sense the more straightforwardly intuitive one. It is not about the *avodat Yom HaKippurim* as such; it is about the God encounter itself — the act of entering the *Kodesh Hakodashim* and standing in the immediate divine presence — and it is about creating the state of *kedusha* that makes such an encounter possible. The *kohen gadol* must prepare himself as Moshe prepared himself before ascending into the cloud.

### Yochanan Deepened

Rabbi Yochanan's model requires more unpacking. On the surface, one might say it is simply about enabling the *avodah* — a preparatory ritual that allows the service to proceed. But Rav Soloveitchik argues for something richer: the seven-day separation, modeled on the *miluim*, does not merely prepare the *kohen gadol* to perform the service. It elevates his status, reaffirming and in a sense reconstituting him as the *kohen gadol* fit for this unique service. Just as the *miluim* established the *kohanim* in their priestly identity — transforming Aaron from a private individual into the *kohen gadol* — the annual seven-day preparation repeats this process on a yearly basis. It is not a rehearsal; it is a renewal of his very standing as the *kohen gadol* appropriate for *Yom Kippur*. This is why, on Rabbi Yochanan's model, the *baraita*'s derivation from *bezot yavo Aharon* is so apt: the verse is not primarily about logistics but about identity and access — *bezot*, through this process, Aaron may enter.

### Aaron Representation

Rav Soloveitchik extends this further. The Vilna Gaon had already noted that *Parashat Acharei Mot* is addressed to Aaron, not generically to future *kohanim* or to the nation — and the annual observance is added only at the *parasha*'s conclusion. This framing suggests that to enter the *Kodesh Hakodashim*, even in future generations, the *kohen gadol* must in some sense enter as Aaron — must be constituted as Aaron's representative and successor in that role. The seven-day preparation, modeled on the *miluim* through which Aaron was first established, re-enacts that establishment each year. And this is where the *shenei talmidei chachamim* — the two sages who attend the *kohen gadol* throughout the seven days — take on their full significance: they are substitute Moshe figures, standing in for the one who first prepared Aaron. The result is a recreation of the *miluim* relationship: a substitute Moshe prepares a substitute Aaron to enter the *Kodesh Hakodashim* as Aaron's authorized successor. *Bezot yavo Aharon* — and by extension, *bezot* will every *kohen gadol* who is truly constituted as Aaron's representative enter.

### Rituals Essential

This framework also illuminates why the preparation cannot be performed for the substitute *kohen* — the understudy who stands ready in case the primary *kohen gadol* becomes disqualified. One might think: if the preparation is so important, why not prepare the understudy as well? Rav Soloveitchik's answer is that the process of elevation can only build upon an existing foundation. The seven-day preparation does not create a *kohen gadol* from scratch; it elevates a *kohen gadol* into his *Yom Kippur* identity. The substitute has no such foundation — he does not carry the baseline status of *kohen gadol* — and therefore the process of elevation cannot be applied to him. *Bedi'avad*, he may perform the service if necessary, but there is no mechanism for giving him the preparation that properly belongs to his role, because that preparation presupposes the role already exists.

Finally, and more broadly, all of the surrounding practices — the daily sprinkling, the two attending sages, the provisions made for the understudy — are not secondary concerns or administrative details. They are part of the comprehensive structure of *miluim*-modeled preparation. If the entire purpose is to make the *kohen gadol ra'uy la-avodah* — genuinely fit to perform the service — then everything that ensures he will be able to do so is, at its root, derived from that same conceptual source. The *shnei talmidei chachamim* teaching him the *avodah* each day, the sprinkling with the ashes of the *parah adumah*, the provisions for *taharah* — all of these are components of the single *miluim*-derived process that culminates in the *kohen gadol*'s service on *Yom Kippur*.

## Calendar Dispute

### Sixth vs Seventh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא? בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי, דְּתַנְיָא: בְּשִׁשָּׁה בַּחוֹדֶשׁ נִיתְּנָה תּוֹרָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: בְּשִׁבְעָה בּוֹ. מַאן דְּאָמַר בְּשִׁשָּׁה: בְּשִׁשָּׁה נִיתְּנָה, וּבְשִׁבְעָה עָלָה (דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּקְרָא אֶל מֹשֶׁה בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי״). מַאן דְּאָמַר בְּשִׁבְעָה: בְּשִׁבְעָה נִיתְּנָה, וּבְשִׁבְעָה עָלָה, [דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּקְרָא אֶל מֹשֶׁה בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי״].
>
> **Translation:** With regard to what do Rabbi Yosei HaGelili and Rabbi Akiva disagree? The Gemara explains that their dispute is parallel to the dispute between these other *tanna'im*, as it was taught in a *baraita*: On the sixth day of the month of Sivan, the Torah, the Ten Commandments, was given to the Jewish people. Rabbi Yosei says: It was on the seventh day of the month. According to the one who said that it was on the sixth, the Torah was given on the sixth, which is the day of the revelation of the Ten Commandments, and on the seventh day of the month Moses ascended the mountain, as it is written: "And He called to Moses on the seventh day" (Exodus 24:16). According to the one who said that the Torah was given on the seventh of the month, it was given on the seventh and Moses ascended on the seventh, as it is written: "And he called to Moses on the seventh day."

The Gemara turns to identify the underlying disagreement between Rabbi Yose HaGelili and Rabbi Akiva, locating it in a broader dispute among *tanna'im*: *b'shishah b'chodesh nitnah Torah le-Yisrael* — the Torah was given on the sixth of Sivan; *Rabbi Yosi omer b'shivah bo* — Rabbi Yosi says on the seventh. This calendrical dispute is the engine that drives the different readings of the *pesukim* in *Mishpatim*.

### Yose's Logic

If the Torah was given on the sixth of Sivan, then the seventh day at the end of *Parashat Mishpatim* — *vayikra el Moshe bayom ha-shevi'i* — cannot refer to the seventh day of Israel's encampment at Sinai, for the Torah had already been given on the sixth. One cannot, on this reading, place the call to Moshe on the seventh day before *matan Torah* — the sixth was *matan Torah*, the seventh has to be afterward. Therefore, Rabbi Yose HaGelili must read the closing *parasha* of *Mishpatim* as an event subsequent to the *Aseret HaDibrot*, in which Moshe was enveloped in the cloud for six days and called on the seventh to begin his forty-day ascent.

### Akiva's Logic

Rabbi Akiva, holding that Torah was given on the seventh of Sivan, has no difficulty reading the closing *parasha* of *Mishpatim* as a recap from the beginning. The cloud settled on the mountain from *Rosh Chodesh*; there were six days until the seventh, when God called to Moshe at the moment of *matan Torah*; and then afterward Moshe entered the cloud and went up for forty days. *Vayikra el Moshe bayom ha-shevi'i* refers to the seventh of Sivan, the very day the Torah was given — not to a post-*Sinai* preparatory period. On this reading, *mishkan kevod Hashem* covered the mountain, not Moshe himself.

### Forty Days Math

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הַיְינוּ דְּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּשִׁיתְּסַר בְּתַמּוּז נִשְׁתַּבְּרוּ הַלּוּחוֹת: עֶשְׂרִין וְאַרְבְּעָה דְּסִיוָן, וְשִׁיתְּסַר דְּתַמּוּז, מָלוּ לְהוּ אַרְבְּעִין יוֹמִין דַּהֲוָה בָּהָר, וּבְשִׁיתְּסַר בְּתַמּוּז נְחֵית וַאֲתָא וְתַבְרִינְהוּ לְלוּחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Granted, according to the opinion of Rabbi Akiva that the Torah was given on the seventh of Sivan and Moses then proceeded to climb the mountain and remain there for forty days, that explains the calculation that you find: On the seventeenth of Tammuz the tablets were shattered, according to the standard tradition. How so? Calculate twenty-four days until the end of Sivan and sixteen days of Tammuz; they total the forty days that he was on the mountain. On the seventeenth of Tammuz he descended from the mountain and came and shattered the tablets.

The Gemara then works through the chronological arithmetic. According to Rabbi Akiva, who holds Torah was given on the seventh, Moshe began his forty-day ascent on the seventh of Sivan. Counting from the seventh: twenty-four days remain in Sivan, plus sixteen days of Tamuz — that is forty days. Moshe came down on the seventeenth of Tamuz and broke the *luchos*. The *cheshbon* works precisely, and *Shivah Asar b'Tamuz* as the day the *luchos* were broken is confirmed.

### Yose's Inclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, דְּאָמַר: שִׁשָּׁה דִפְרִישָׁה וְאַרְבְּעִין דְּהַר, עַד עֶשְׂרִין וּתְלָת בְּתַמּוּז לָא אִתַּבּוּר לוּחוֹת! אָמַר לְךָ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי: אַרְבְּעִין דְּהַר בַּהֲדֵי שִׁשָּׁה דִפְרִישָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** However, according to Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, who said: There were six days of sequestering after the Torah was given and an additional forty days that Moses was on the mountain, the tablets were not shattered until the twenty-third of Tammuz, contrary to the standard tradition. Rabbi Yosei HaGelili could have said to you: The forty days that Moses was on the mountain include the six days of sequestering.

According to Rabbi Yose HaGelili, however, who holds Torah was given on the sixth, the forty days began on the seventh — the day after *matan Torah* — and six additional days of preparation preceded even that. Adding six days of preparation plus forty days in the cloud to the sixth of Sivan pushes the descent past the seventeenth of Tamuz, arriving instead at the twenty-third. The Gemara presses: how does Rabbi Yose HaGelili maintain that the *luchos* were broken on the seventeenth?

Rabbi Yose HaGelili's answer is remarkable: *arba'im de-tura be-hadi shisha de-prisha* — the forty days on the mountain included the six days of preparation. The six days of cloud-covering were not a separate period that preceded the forty days; they were the first six of the forty days themselves. Only from the seventh day onward was Moshe actively in God's presence receiving Torah; the first six days were embedded within the forty, serving as the preparation that opened the encounter. This means Moshe spent only thirty-four days in direct divine communication — but the forty days is counted from the beginning of his ascent into the cloud, preparation included. There is something quietly profound in this: the journey to stand before God is itself part of the standing before God.

## Cloud Entry Problem

### Honor, Not Call

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: ״וַיִּקְרָא אֶל מֹשֶׁה״ — מֹשֶׁה וְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל עוֹמְדִין, מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: ״וַיִּקְרָא אֶל מֹשֶׁה״ — מֹשֶׁה וְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל עוֹמְדִין, וְלֹא בָּא הַכָּתוּב אֶלָּא לַחְלֹק לוֹ כָּבוֹד לְמֹשֶׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Master said in that *baraita* cited above that when the Torah says: "And He called to Moses," it means that Moses and all of the Jewish people were standing and listening. The Gemara suggests that this supports the opinion of Rabbi Elazar, as Rabbi Elazar said that when the Torah says: "And He called to Moses," it means that Moses and all of the Jewish people were standing and listening and the verse comes only to accord deference to Moses.

The final section of the *daf* takes up what Rabbi Elazar had already stated and what Rabbi Akiva's position implied: *vayikra el Moshe bayom ha-shevi'i* was not to summon Moshe upward but *le-chalek kavod le-Moshe* — to give him honor before the assembled people. The Gemara confirms that this ruling supports Rabbi Elazar's formulation.

### Cloud Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״קוֹל לוֹ״, ״קוֹל אֵלָיו״ — מֹשֶׁה שָׁמַע וְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל לֹא שָׁמְעוּ! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — בְּסִינַי, הָא — בְּאֹהֶל מוֹעֵד. וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — בִּקְרִיאָה, הָא — בְּדִבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: The Torah states: "And when Moses went into the Tent of Meeting that He might speak with him, then he heard the voice speaking unto him from above the Ark cover that was upon the Ark of the Testimony, from between the two cherubs; and He spoke unto him" (Numbers 7:89). The Torah could have said: He heard the voice speaking to him; however, instead the verse said: He heard the voice speaking unto him, indicating that the voice reached him alone. Moses alone heard God's voice and all of the Jewish people did not hear it. The Gemara answers: This is not difficult. This case, where everyone heard God's voice, was at Sinai. That case, where Moses alone heard God's voice, was at the Tent of Meeting. Or if you wish, say instead an alternative resolution. This is not difficult; when God addressed Moses by calling to him, everyone heard; that which God subsequently communicated by speaking, Moses alone heard.

But now a sharp textual contradiction emerges. At the end of *Sefer Shemot*, the Torah states: *ve-lo yachol Moshe lavo el Ohel Moed* — Moshe could not enter the *Ohel Moed* because of the *anan*. The divine presence in the cloud was so concentrated that Moshe could not penetrate it. Yet the closing verse of *Parashat Mishpatim* states: *vayavo Moshe be-toch he-anan* — Moshe came into the midst of the cloud at Har Sinai. What is it? Can Moshe enter the cloud or not?

Before resolving that contradiction, the Gemara addresses the challenge to Rabbi Elazar's claim that the *vayikra* was *le-chalek kavod le-Moshe*. If God's voice, when addressed to Moshe, was heard only by Moshe — *Moshe shama ve-chulan lo shamu* — how does calling his name confer public honor? This principle is derived from the *pasuk* in *Sefer Bamidbar*: *u-ve-vo Moshe el Ohel Moed lishmo'a et ha-kol midaber eilav* — *midaber eilav*, directed toward him alone, meaning only Moshe heard. If the divine calling was private, it cannot publicly distinguish Moshe.

### Sinai vs Ohel

The Gemara's first resolution: *lo kashya, ha b'Sinai ha b'Ohel Moed* — the two cases are different. At Har Sinai, the calling was a single extraordinary event that all of Israel heard; subsequently, at the *Ohel Moed*, the divine speech was exclusively private. Sinai was a unique communal revelation in which the calling of Moshe's name was a public act — *lechalek kavod le-Moshe* before the whole nation. The ongoing private communications from the *Ohel Moed* are a different mode entirely.

An alternative resolution is also offered: *ha b'kriya ha b'dibur* — even at the *Ohel Moed*, one can distinguish between the calling — *kriya*, the address by name — and the content of the speech — *dibur*. The calling of the name might always have been audible to those present; only the substance of what God said was restricted to Moshe. On this reading, it was always publicly known that God was calling Moshe, even if the content of divine speech was not shared. This is a striking possibility: the nation perpetually witnesses God naming Moshe, confirming his unique relationship with God, even as the specific words of Torah pass between them privately.

### Divine Force

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְלַמֵּד שֶׁתְּפָסוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְמֹשֶׁה וֶהֱבִיאוֹ בֶּעָנָן. דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנָא: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״בְּתוֹךְ״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״בְּתוֹךְ״ — ״וְיָבוֹאוּ בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּתוֹךְ הַיָּם״. מָה לְהַלָּן שְׁבִיל, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהַמַּיִם לָהֶם חוֹמָה״, אַף כָּאן שְׁבִיל.
>
> **Translation:** This teaches that the Holy One, Blessed be He, grabbed Moses and brought him into the cloud since he could not enter on his own. The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: There is a verbal analogy that resolves this contradiction. It is stated here: "And Moses came into the cloud," and it is stated below, in another verse: "And the children of Israel went into the sea on dry land" (Exodus 14:22); Just as below, there was a path within the sea, as it is written: "And the water was a wall for them" (Exodus 14:22), here too, there was a path through the cloud, but Moses did not actually enter the cloud.

Returning to the core contradiction — Moshe cannot enter the *anan* at the *Ohel Moed*, yet he entered the *anan* at Har Sinai — the Gemara resolves it through a remarkable teaching: *melamed she-tefaso Hakadosh Baruch Hu le-Moshe ve-hivi'o ba-anan* — God took hold of Moshe and brought him into the cloud. Moshe did not himself penetrate the divine presence; God drew him in. This is not Moshe crossing a threshold under his own power, but God extending himself to bring Moshe inside. The encounter, in other words, is entirely on God's initiative — God makes it possible, God enables the entry, God guarantees the path.

The school of Rabbi Yishmael reinforces this with a *gezeirah shavah*: it says *be-toch* here at Sinai, and it says *be-toch* elsewhere — *vayavo'u benei Yisrael be-toch ha-yam* — the people went into the *midst* of the sea, where the waters formed walls: *ha-mayim lahem chomah*. As there a path was made through what would otherwise be impassable, so here: God opened a path for Moshe through the cloud. Moshe was not in the full substance of the divine cloud; there was a channel, a *shvil*, through which he could pass without being consumed.

The *daf* closes, then, on precisely this tension — the impossibility and the possibility of the divine-human encounter held together. Moshe cannot enter the *anan*; God brings him in. The path exists, but only because God creates it. The *kohen gadol* prepares for seven days, goes through every stage of the process, and yet the entry into the *Kodesh Hakodashim* is only possible because God permits it and sustains it. That is the final image *daf dalet* leaves us with: the encounter with God is utterly beyond human capacity to engineer, and yet it is made real through the combination of human preparation — rigorous, sustained, ritually elaborate — and divine accommodation. *Bi-mekom gilah sham tehei ra'adah* — the rejoicing and the trembling belong together, at Sinai and on *Yom Kippur* alike.
