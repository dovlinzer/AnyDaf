# Nazir 63 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nazir Discovers Tumah

### Opening Topic

The mishnah at the top of daf 63 introduces a substantially new topic. The preceding discussion concerned which categories of persons are subject to the laws of *nezirut* — concluding, for instance, that a Canaanite slave (*eved*) is included while non-Jews are not. Now the mishnah turns to an entirely different question: the case of a *nazir she-gilach ve-noda lo she-hu tamei* — a *nazir* who completed his thirty-day term, brought his *korbanot*, shaved, and only then discovered that at some point during his *nezirut* he had been *tamei*. Perhaps someone has just informed him that the house he entered a week earlier contained a corpse. He now confronts the realization that his *nezirut* was retroactively compromised, that he brought his *korbanot* in a state of *tumah*, and that seemingly his entire *nezirut* was invalidated.

### Tumah Yeduah vs. Tehom

The mishnah distinguishes between two categories of *tumah*. If it was *tumah yeduah* — a known *tumah*, meaning one that was known to others even if not to the *nazir* himself — then *soter*, his entire *nezirut* is retroactively demolished. He must begin the thirty days again, bring new *korbanot*, and shave again, just as if he had discovered the *tumah* at the moment it occurred. However — and here lies the *chiddush* of the mishnah — if it is *tumat ha-tehom*, *tumah* of the depths, a particular category of hidden *tumah*, then *eino soter*: his *nezirut* is not invalidated, and he has fulfilled his obligation through his *korbanot* and his shaving.

### Tumat Tehom Exception

The concept of *tumat ha-tehom* is at first striking, because in both cases the *nazir* was, in fact, *tamei* in hindsight. In neither scenario did he know about it at the time, and yet only one category — *tumah yeduah* — destroys his *nezirut* while the other does not. The *chiddush* of the mishnah, then, is this exception: a certain class of hidden *tumah*, if discovered only after the *nezirut* is complete, does not retroactively undermine it.

### Before/After Shaving

The mishnah adds a critical qualification: *im ad she-lo gilach, bein kach u-vein kach soter* — if he discovered the *tumah* before he shaved, then regardless of whether it was *tumat ha-tehom* or *tumah yeduah*, his *nezirut* is destroyed and he must begin again. The *chiddush* of *tumat ha-tehom* applies only when he discovers the *tumah* after he has finished — that is, after he has shaved. The mishnah's emphasis on shaving (*gilach*) as the operative endpoint raises a question about the structure of the rule.

### When Does Nezirut End

The mishnah's emphasis on *gilach* as the critical boundary invites a question: does this formulation assume that *nezirut* is not complete until the *nazir* has shaved? This would align with the position of Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that *tiglachat me'akevet* — the shaving is indispensable and the *nezirut* is not concluded until all the *korbanot* and the shaving have been performed. By contrast, the Rabbis hold that *keivan she-nizrak alecha echad min ha-damim yatza* — once one blood-offering has been sprinkled on the *nazir*'s behalf, his *nezirut* is concluded. One might therefore wonder whether the mishnah's reference to shaving is specifically tied to Rabbi Eliezer's position, or whether the rule of *tumat ha-tehom* independently creates a requirement to wait until shaving. The *gemara* will resolve this: the operative principle is whether the *nezirut* is over, whatever that point is determined to be. If we adopt Rabbi Eliezer's view, then "before he shaved" is simply the way of saying "before his *nezirut* concluded." If we adopt the Rabbis' view, then safety comes once he has brought his first *korban* — the shaving is simply the marker of completion under Rabbi Eliezer's framework, not an independent requirement of *tumat ha-tehom*.

## Cave Example

### Immersion Scenario

The mishnah proceeds to illustrate the concept of *tumat ha-tehom* with a concrete case. *Yarad litbol be-mearah* — he descended to immerse himself in a cave, and afterward it was discovered that there was a *met*, a corpse, *tzaf al pi ha-mearah*, at the mouth or opening of the cave. The precise topography here requires some interpretation. Based on Tosafot, it appears that the body was located at or near the entrance of the cave, in the same body of water in which the *nazir* immersed, so that if the body was there at the time of his immersion, he would have been beneath the same roof (*ohel*) as the corpse. The question is whether the body was there when he immersed — he did not see it, but it has now been discovered.

### Floating Meaning

The word *tzaf*, ordinarily translated as "floating," generates some puzzlement in context. What does it mean for a corpse to be floating at the mouth of a cave? The most coherent reading, aided by Tosafot, is that the body was floating in the water at the entrance — that is, the body of water in the cave extended to the mouth, and the corpse was found floating in that same water. If the corpse was there when the *nazir* immersed, he would have been in *ohel* relationship with it — beneath the same enclosed roof of the cave. The question of whether he was *tamei* turns on whether the corpse was actually present during the immersion.

### Floating vs. Buried

The mishnah then draws a distinction that is the heart of the *tumat ha-tehom* rule. *Nimtza tzaf* — if the corpse was found floating, exposed to the open air, then *tamei*: this is not *tumat ha-tehom*, and the *nazir*'s *nezirut* is destroyed. *Nimtza meshuka be-karka ha-mearah* — if the corpse was found buried in the floor of the cave, and the *nazir* went down *le-hakir*, to cool himself, then *tahor*: this is *tumat ha-tehom*, and his *nezirut* stands. The core distinction is between *exposed* and *buried*. Something that was out in the open — even if neither the *nazir* nor anyone else happened to see it — is categorically not *tumat ha-tehom*. Something buried beneath the ground is. Neither scenario involved the *nazir* knowing about the *tumah* at the time, yet only the buried case qualifies.

### Tosafot Explanation

One might ask why the mishnah switches its language mid-case — using *tovel* (immersing) in the floating scenario and *le-hakir* (cooling oneself) in the buried scenario — since the reason one entered the cave would seem irrelevant to the *halakha*. Tosafot explains that the switch is deliberate and reflects a double *chiddush*. When a person enters water for the purpose of *tevila*, he is being scrupulous about his *tahara* and presumably attentive to any potential source of *tumah*; yet even such a careful person is considered to have encountered *tumat ha-tehom* if the body was buried — while still being considered *tamei* if the body was exposed, even though he missed it. In the reverse direction, a person who entered simply to cool off is presumably less vigilant; yet even for such a person, if the body was buried, it is *tumat ha-tehom*. Both directions of the switch carry a *chiddush*.

### Burial + Unknowable

The mishnah focuses on the distinction between buried and exposed, but it leaves implicit a second requirement that the *gemara* will make explicit: the *tumah* must not only be buried but must also have been entirely unknown — *ein echad makirah be-sof ha-olam*, not known to anyone anywhere in the world. If a body is buried but the local community is aware of the grave — even if the *nazir* himself did not know — that is not *tumat ha-tehom*. A *beit ha-pras*, for example, would not qualify, since people are generally aware of it. The mishnah does not spell this out, but the *gemara* will.

## Purification Case

### Tumah During Purification

The mishnah introduces a further limitation on *tumat ha-tehom*. *Yarad litaher mi-tumat met* — if he descended to immerse not simply as a general act of purification but specifically to complete his purification from a prior *tumat met*: he had already been in contact with a corpse, undergone the sprinkling of the ashes of the *parah adumah*, and was now going to the *mikveh* to finalize his *tahara*. During this immersion, it is subsequently discovered that there was a buried, unknown corpse — the very type of buried, unknowable *tumah* that in the earlier case would have qualified as *tumat ha-tehom*. Nevertheless, the mishnah rules: *tamei*, his *nezirut* is *soter*. The principle of *tumat ha-tehom* does not apply here.

### Chazakah Principle

The mishnah explains the ruling with the principle: *shechazkat tamei tamei, shechazkat tahor tahor, she-raglayim la-davar* — the presumptive status (*chazaka*) of a *tamei* person is *tamei*, and the presumptive status of a *tahor* person is *tahor*, and there is a *raglayim la-davar*. The operative logic is *chazaka*: this person entered the *mikveh* with the legal status of *tamei*. His status was already *tamei*, and the discovery of a buried corpse would have, if anything, extended or compounded that *tumah*. Since his *chazaka* was *tamei*, we do not apply the benefit of *tumat ha-tehom* to spare him — his starting status was already compromised. The *tumat ha-tehom* exception was designed for a person who began from a *chazaka* of *tahor* and encountered a wholly hidden *tumah*; it does not extend to one whose legal status was already *tamei*.

### Chazakah vs. Evidence

It is worth distinguishing between *chazaka* and what the mishnah calls *raglayim la-davar*, which the mishnah uses in conjunction with the *chazaka* principle. *Chazaka* is not a probabilistic claim. It does not assert that a person is *more likely* to have retained a certain status; it is a legal operating principle by which, for *halachic* purposes, we treat a person's status as unchanged until we have affirmative information otherwise. Whether I touched a *tamei* object or not is a factual matter about which *chazaka* says nothing in terms of likelihood; it simply instructs the *halacha* to presume status quo. *Raglayim la-davar*, by contrast, normally denotes circumstantial evidence — factual grounds that give some support to a conclusion. Used here alongside *chazaka*, the phrase perhaps means that the *chazaka* itself gives the status "legs," a kind of permanence and solidity, rather than referring to independent circumstantial evidence. Alternatively, *raglayim la-davar* might be used loosely. The distinction between these two modes of legal reasoning — presumptive status versus circumstantial support — is worth bearing in mind throughout the discussion.

One might also observe that the mishnah's phrase *tamei* vs. *tahor* in describing the outcomes of these cases is itself somewhat imprecise. As the Rambam's language makes clear, the real question in all of these cases is *soter* vs. *lo soter* — whether the *nezirut* is retroactively invalidated. Even when the mishnah says *tahor* in the *tumat ha-tehom* case, the *nazir* is not literally *tahor*: he still must go through the process of becoming *tahor* (sprinkling of *parah adumah* ashes, *mikveh*, etc.). What the *halakha* means is that his *nezirut* and his *korbanot* were not destroyed. The Rambam consistently frames these cases in terms of *soter* and *eino soter*, and that is the more precise formulation.

Regarding the ultimate source of *tumat ha-tehom*, one might anticipate that such a counterintuitive principle — that a person retroactively known to have been *tamei* is nonetheless treated as having fulfilled his *nezirut* — must derive from a specific *pasuk*, and the *gemara* will indeed explore textual sources. But it is worth noting now that the mishnah itself offers no derivation, and the *gemara* will ultimately conclude that *tumat ha-tehom* is a *halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, or at minimum a very ancient tradition (*gemara gemiri lah*) whose source cannot be traced through standard *drashah*.

## Sources Drashah

### Pesuk Nazir

The *gemara* asks *mina hanei mili* — from where do we derive this extraordinary rule? Rabbi Elazar cites a *pasuk* from the laws of *nazir*: *ve-chi yamut met alav be-feta pitom* — "and if a dead body dies upon him suddenly" (Numbers 6:9). The phrase *alav* — "upon him," meaning in his clear awareness — is read to indicate *be-mechuveret lo*, that which is manifest to him. When the death is sudden and plain (*pitom*), the *nazir* is *tamei*. The implication is that when the *tumah* was *not* manifest to him — not sudden, not in his direct awareness — a different rule applies, creating the category we are calling *tumat ha-tehom*.

### Pesuk Pesach

Resh Lakish derives the principle from a different *pasuk*, one concerning the *korban Pesach*: *ki yihyeh tamei la-nefesh o be-derech rechoka* — "if a man is *tamei* for a dead body or on a far road" (Numbers 9:10), which describes when a person brings *Pesach Sheni*. The key word Resh Lakish focuses on is *derech*, a road or path. *Mah derech be-galuy* — just as a road is exposed and open to public view, *af kol be-galuy* — so too any *tumah* that qualifies under this verse must be "out in the open." By implication, the criterion of *tumat ha-tehom* for *Pesach* is that the *tumah* was *not* in the open — it was covered and buried. Thus from this *pasuk* we derive the "exposed vs. buried" element of the rule.

### Combining Sources

Together, these two *pesukim* could in principle yield both criteria of *tumat ha-tehom*: from *alav* ("upon him") we derive the requirement of unknownness — the *tumah* must not have been in the awareness of the person or, as we will see, of anyone; and from *derech* ("road") we derive the requirement of being covered and buried, not exposed. One might thus synthesize the two and construct the full definition of *tumat ha-tehom* from these verses. However, the *gemara* raises a difficulty. The *baraita* on *tumat ha-tehom* requires not merely that the *nazir* was unaware, but that *ein echad makirah be-sof ha-olam* — not a single person in the world knew of the *tumah*, even someone at the farthest end of the earth. If one uses the *pasuk* of *alav* as the source, one might conclude only that *the nazir himself* was unaware, and that if anyone at the far end of the world happened to know, it would still be *tumat ha-tehom*, since it was still not "upon him." The *gemara* observes that the *pasuk* of *derech* might support the notion that the *tumah* must be unknown to all — since a path implies public awareness — but the derivation is strained. Ultimately, the *gemara* concludes that these *pesukim* cannot satisfactorily yield both criteria.

### Baraita Dead Body

The *gemara* quotes a *baraita* that articulates the critical case: *ha-motze met mushkav le-rochbah shel derech* — if one finds that a corpse was laid out spanning the full width of a road. Here there is no *safek* about whether the traveler passed over the *met*: since the corpse covered the entire width of the path, anyone who walked that road definitively passed over it and was under *ohel* with it. For ordinary *tumah* purposes, *be-tumat ha-met* — the person is *tamei*. But *benazir ve-oseh Pesach tahor* — for the *nazir* and the one performing the *Pesach* offering, the *baraita* declares them *tahor*, meaning their obligations were not invalidated.

This is a striking case because it is not a *safek* — it is a certainty, in hindsight, that the person was *tamei*. Yet *tumat ha-tehom* saves him for purposes of *nezirut* and *Pesach*. This will be important later when the *gemara* considers whether *tumat ha-tehom* requires an element of doubt (*safek*) or operates even in cases of retrospective certainty.

Given the difficulties in deriving *tumat ha-tehom* from *pesukim*, the *gemara* concludes: *tumat ha-tehom gemara gemiri lah* — it is a received tradition. The Rambam characterizes it as *davar zeh halakha mi-pi ha-kabalah*. Whether *gemara gemiri lah* is precisely synonymous with *halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* is a matter of some discussion. Rashi in several places treats them as identical. The Netziv, drawing on the Rambam and other *rishonim*, suggests that *halakhata gemiri lah* may refer to an ancient, enshrined tradition of very early *batei din* whose source is no longer known — not necessarily a tradition transmitted orally from Sinai in the technical sense, though it carries full *de-oraita* authority. The phrase *halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* is itself sometimes used loosely in *tannaitic* literature, as the gemara's preference for the Aramaic formulation here may simply reflect linguistic register rather than a substantive distinction.

As for the practical scope of *tumat ha-tehom*: the *halakha* applies specifically to *nazir* and *oseh Pesach*, not to *terumah* or other categories of *tahara*. If someone ate *terumah* and subsequently discovered he had been subject to a *tumat ha-tehom*, he does not benefit from any exemption — he violated the prohibition, albeit *be-shogeg*. The *halakha* of *tumat ha-tehom* is a specific leniency carved out for *nezirut* and *Pesach*.

## Rami's Question

### Timing Question

Having established that *tumat ha-tehom* was defined as a *halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, and that the mishnah's emphasis on shaving reflects the position of Rabbi Eliezer, the *gemara* turns to a question posed by Rami bar Chama. This is distinct from the *tumat ha-tehom* context — Rami bar Chama is asking about an ordinary *tumah yeduah*. His question: *nitma be-toch melot ve-noda lo le-achar melot mahu* — if the *nazir* became *tamei* during his thirty days, but the discovery occurred only after the thirty days were complete, what is the *halakha*? Does the timing of discovery matter? Specifically: *batar yedi'ah azlinan* — do we follow the moment of discovery? And since the discovery was after the thirty days, is it treated more leniently, analogously to a case where *tumah* occurred after the thirty days?

To frame the question precisely: Rabbi Eliezer holds that *tumah* occurring *after* the thirty-day term does not destroy the entire *nezirut* — the *nazir* need not restart thirty days, but merely waits seven days to become *tahor* before bringing his *korbanot* (*soter shiva*). The question Rami bar Chama raises is whether *tumah* that occurred *during* the thirty days but was only *discovered* afterward might be treated similarly, on the grounds that the discovery came late — or whether the factual reality that *tumah* occurred during the *nezirut* is determinative, regardless of when it was discovered.

### Rava First Answer

Rava responds by directing attention to the mishnah: *ta shema, ad she-lo gilach, bein kach u-vein kach soter* — even in a case of *tumat ha-tehom*, if he discovered the *tumah* before he shaved, the *nezirut* is destroyed either way. The force of this proof: the phrase *bein kach u-vein kach* ("either way") implies that the mishnah is addressing a case where the discovery came after the thirty days but before the shaving — since if he discovered it *during* the thirty days, the ruling would be obvious and not worth stating. Therefore, the mishnah teaches: even when discovery occurs after thirty days have elapsed, if it was before shaving (and thus, per Rabbi Eliezer, before *nezirut* was concluded), the *nezirut* is *soter*. This would appear to resolve Rami bar Chama's question: we do not follow the moment of discovery; we follow the moment of *tumah*.

### All vs. Seven Days

The *gemara* does not rest here, however. Even granting that it is *soter*, a further question emerges: *kulo soter o shiv'ah soter mahu* — does the *tumah* destroy the entire thirty days, requiring the *nazir* to recommence from the beginning, or does it only obligate a seven-day waiting period (*soter shiva*)? This question becomes interesting specifically in the context of Rabbi Eliezer. The Rabbis hold that *tumah* requiring a restart is always thirty days; under their view, whether the *tumah* occurred during or after the term, the outcome is thirty days. But Rabbi Eliezer's position is more nuanced: he holds that *tumah* occurring after thirty days is *soter shiva* only. The mishnah under discussion is, as established, the position of Rabbi Eliezer. So now: under Rabbi Eliezer, if the *nazir* became *tamei* during the thirty days but discovered it only afterward — is that treated like *tumah* after thirty days (*soter shiva*), or like a standard mid-*nezirut* *tumah* (*soter hakol*)?

The *gemara* raises the possibility that *soter shiva* might apply here, reasoning that since the *yedi'ah* came after *melot*, perhaps Rabbi Eliezer would treat it similarly to *tumah* occurring after the term. Conversely, since the *tumah* itself occurred during the thirty days, perhaps it destroys the full *nezirut* even for Rabbi Eliezer.

### Mishnah Conclusion

The *gemara* concludes by observing that the mishnah employs the language *bein kach u-vein kach soter* — "either way it is *soter*" — without distinguishing between the case of becoming *tamei* during the term and discovering it during the term, on the one hand, and becoming *tamei* during the term and discovering it afterward, on the other. Tosafot, the Rosh, and the pseudo-Rosh on this *daf* all read the *gemara*'s conclusion as follows: since Rabbi Eliezer rules that *tumah* after thirty days is *soter shiva*, and since the mishnah draws no distinction between that case and the case of *tumah* during thirty days discovered afterward, the *gemara* equates them — and the conclusion is that even *tumah* occurring *during* the thirty days, if discovered only afterward, is *soter shiva* under Rabbi Eliezer's view. This reading is, as must be acknowledged, not immediately transparent from the *gemara*'s language. The argument is that since the mishnah does not differentiate, we infer that the case of late discovery is treated identically to the post-term *tumah* scenario — which Rabbi Eliezer rules is *soter shiva* rather than *soter shloshim*. This is a striking conclusion, and the reading requires some care.

## Full Baraita

### Road Span Case

The *gemara* now quotes the full *baraita* on *tumat ha-tehom*. The first case: *ha-motzi met mutal le-rochbah shel derech* — a corpse discovered lying the full width of a road, buried and unknown (*tumat ha-tehom*). The ruling: *le-trumah tamei* — for *terumah* purposes the person is *tamei*; *u-venazir u-ve-oseh Pesach tahor* — but for *nazir* and *oseh Pesach*, the principle of *tumat ha-tehom* applies and his obligations were fulfilled. The use of *tahor* here is somewhat loose, as noted above: he is not literally *tahor* and must still undergo the purification process; the meaning is that his *nezirut* and *Pesach* obligation are not invalidated.

### Escape Route

The *baraita* immediately qualifies this ruling: *bameh devarim amurim she-ein lo makom la-avor* — when is this true? When there was no space to pass around the body — the corpse genuinely spanned the entire width, leaving no room for the traveler to have avoided it. *Aval yesh lo makom la-avor* — but if there was a small strip of the path, even a foot's width at the edge, that the corpse did not reach, such that a person walking that extreme edge would have avoided the *met*, then even for *terumah* the person is *tahor*.

### Public Domain

The reason is the principle of *safek tumah be-reshut ha-rabim*: a public road is a *reshut ha-rabim*, and a *safek* about *tumah* in a *reshut ha-rabim* is ruled *tahor*. Even if the probability of having walked in that narrow strip is very small — 5% of the road's width was clear, 95% covered — the existence of any possibility that one avoided the corpse is sufficient in a *reshut ha-rabim* to declare the person *tahor* for all purposes, including *terumah*.

### Body Condition

The *baraita* adds a further qualification: *bameh devarim amurim she-matza'o shalem* — when is the ruling about a clear path being sufficient to be *tahor* applicable? Only when the body was whole. *Aval meshubar o mefurak* — if the body was broken apart or dismembered, even if there was a clear path, *chayshinan bein perakim avar* — we are concerned that the traveler may have woven through the gaps between the limbs, which means he could have passed through the space occupied by the body without walking over any single part. The logic is that a dismembered body presents multiple gaps, and it is possible that the traveler threaded his way through them rather than walking over any portion. In such a case, even the possibility of having avoided the corpse does not save him, because we are concerned that he may in fact have passed between the limbs and thus been in *ohel* with the body through the gaps.

### In Coffin

However: *u-ve-kever* — if all the limbs, even if dismembered, were enclosed in a coffin that spanned the full width of the path, then *afilu nishbar u-meforak tamei*, even if dismembered, the person is *tamei*. The reason is *mipnei she-ha-kever mitztarfo* — the coffin unites all the contents as a single legal unit. One may recall that a coffin (*kever*) itself has the status of the *met* it contains; everything within the coffin is treated as a single entity. Therefore there are no "gaps" through which the traveler could have passed — the entire coffin is the *met*, and one cannot weave between its contents.

### Walking vs. Carrying

*Bameh devarim amurim* — all the above leniencies concerning the possibility of having avoided the body apply *be-mahalech be-raglav*, when the traveler was walking on foot. *Aval ta'un o rachuv* — if he was carrying a burden or riding on an animal, then *tamei* even where there might have been a narrow escape route. The reason: *lefi she-ha-mahalech be-raglav efshar lo she-lo yiga ve-shelo yasit ve-shelo yahil* — a person walking on his feet can be precise enough in his movements to potentially avoid touching, shifting, or creating an *ohel* over the corpse. But *ee efshar she-lo yiga ve-shelo yasit ve-shelo yahil* — a person burdened with a load or mounted on an animal cannot control his movements to the same degree; he will inevitably have leaned, swayed, or extended over the space in a way that creates *tumah*. The *halakha* here is calibrated to circumstantial reality: not every claimed scenario is evaluated individually, but a general assessment is made that a mounted or burdened traveler cannot achieve the precision of avoidance that a careful walker might.

### Definition Criteria

After all these cases, the *baraita* provides the formal definition: *ve-eizehu tumat ha-tehom, kol she-ein makkirin otah be-sof ha-olam* — *tumat ha-tehom* is any *tumah* that is unknown to anyone, even at the far end of the world. *Makkira echad be-sof ha-olam, ein zo tumat ha-tehom* — if even one person anywhere knew of it, it does not qualify. This is the unknowability criterion.

### Straw vs. Crevices

The burial criterion is also specified precisely. *Hayah tamun be-teven o bi-tzrorot* — if the body was buried under straw or under pebbles, *harei zu tumat ha-tehom* — this is *tumat ha-tehom*. The *chiddush* is that even covering as light as straw is sufficient to qualify as "buried" for this purpose. On the other hand, *be-yamim u-va-afelah u-venekikei ha-sla'im* — if it was in water, in darkness, or in a crevice of a rock, *ein zo tumat ha-tehom* — these do not qualify as "buried." The apparent paradox is that a body in a rock crevice — which might be very difficult to see — is considered exposed, while a body under straw is considered buried. The best understanding is that "buried" in this context means covered *in a manner that the ground conceals it*, whereas a crevice, even a deep one, is part of the open landscape and thus "exposed" in the relevant legal sense. The precise limits of this distinction are not fully spelled out by the *baraita*.

### Met Only

Finally, the *baraita* closes: *ve-lo amru tumat ha-tehom ella met bilvad* — the entire principle of *tumat ha-tehom* applies only to *tumat met*, the *tumah* of a corpse. This makes sense: *tumat ha-tehom* is the specific exception carved out for *nazir* and *oseh Pesach*, and the relevant *tumah* for both of those contexts is *tumat met*. Other forms of *tumah* (such as *tumah* from a *sheretz*, etc.) are not subject to this exception.

## Floating Objects

### Sheretz Floating

The *gemara* uses the case in the mishnah — which involved a *met* that was described as *tzaf*, floating — as an occasion to examine an entirely separate *halakha* regarding floating and *safek tumah*. The principle to be established: a floating *tumah* — not related to *tumat ha-tehom*, but a floating *sheretz* (one of the eight creeping creatures whose carcass conveys *tumah*) — creates a *safek* that is treated more leniently than a standard *safek tumah be-reshut ha-yachid*. The *baraita* states: *safek tumah safa, bein bi-kelim bein bi-karka, tehorah* — a doubt about *tumah* involving a floating object, whether floating in a vessel or floating in the ground, is ruled *tahor* according to the *tanna kamma*, even in a *reshut ha-yachid*, where ordinarily a *safek tumah* would be ruled *tamei*.

### On Earth Drashah

The basis for this ruling: Rabbi Yitzchak bar Avdimi derives it from a *pasuk*. The verse states *be-khol ha-sheretz ha-shoretz* — "all creeping things that creep," which would imply that contact with any *sheretz* anywhere renders one *tamei*. But the verse also says *al ha-aretz* — "on the ground." Rabbi Yitzchak bar Avdimi reconciles these by distinguishing between *vadai maga'o* and *safek maga'o*. If one *certainly* touched the *sheretz*, one is *tamei* wherever the *sheretz* is — on the ground, in water, floating anywhere. But if there is a *safek* whether one touched it, then *al ha-aretz* — one is only *tamei* when the *sheretz* was on the ground. If it was floating, and there is a *safek* about the contact, one is *tahor*.

### Floating Tahor

The logic, on reflection, is that floating objects are in some sense less "present" in a space than objects resting on the ground. The principle of *safek tumah be-reshut ha-yachid tamei* operates on the premise that the *tumah* source is fixed and present in a private domain; the *safek* is purely about whether contact occurred. But a floating object is not anchored — it is less definitively "there" — and the verse *al ha-aretz* limits the *safek tumah* rule to cases where the *tumah* source is resting on solid ground. In a *reshut ha-rabim*, this distinction is academic since *safek tumah be-reshut ha-rabim* is always *tahor*; the ruling matters only in a *reshut ha-yachid*, where the floating exemption overrides what would otherwise be a presumption of *tumah*.

### Shimon Vessels

Rabbi Shimon disagrees with the *tanna kamma* in one respect: *bi-kelim tamei, bi-karka tahor* — if the *sheretz* was floating in a vessel, a *safek* about contact renders one *tamei*; if floating in the ground (i.e., in a body of water naturally in the ground), one is *tahor*. Ulla explains Rabbi Shimon's source: the verse *akh mayan u-vor mikveh mayim yihyeh tahor* — "but a spring, a pit, or a collection of water shall be *tahor*" — establishes that water naturally contained in the ground has a special status of *tahara*. But the verse also states *yitma* — implying that in other contexts *tumah* does apply. Rabbi Shimon reads this as distinguishing between floating in a natural body of water in the ground (*tahor* for a *safek*) versus floating in a vessel (*tamei* for a *safek*). Tosafot raises the question of whether the same principle would apply to an object flying through the air rather than floating in water — if a *sheretz* was thrown and one is uncertain whether it struck him, does the "not on the ground" exemption similarly apply? The question is raised but not resolved.
