# Hullin 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rashi vs. Rabbeinu Tam

### Rav Papa's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: הֶחְלִיד בְּמִיעוּת סִימָנִים מַהוּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa raises a dilemma:** If one **concealed** the knife **in** cutting **the minority of the *simanim*** and cut the majority of the *simanim* in the standard manner, **what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The present daf, 31b, brings to a close a series of discussions concerning the *psulim* of *shechita* — the various ways in which the act of slaughter can be rendered invalid. Among those discussed in the preceding material was *chalada*, the defect of "burrowing," which occurs when the knife does not begin cutting from the outside but instead penetrates between the *simanim* (the trachea and esophagus) and cuts in the wrong direction, or begins from the interior. On this note, the *gemara* closes with a brief but significant question posed by Rav Papa: *ki chalda bemiut simanim mah* — what is the *halacha* if the act of *chalada* occurs only in the minority of the *simanim*? The *gemara* leaves this unresolved with the word *teyku*.

On its surface, this seems unremarkable. But the question opens onto one of the most important debates between the Rishonim, one to which we have referred in earlier sessions and which now demands careful examination.

### Rashi's View


According to Rashi, the question of *chalada bemiut simanim* is really a manifestation of a much broader conceptual problem that runs through several passages in this *masechta*. To understand it, consider the following scenario: in the case of a *behema*, one has already cut the first *siman* and sixty percent of the second — which constitutes the *rov*, the majority. At the point where *rov* has been accomplished, the *shechita* is in principle complete. *rubo kekulo* — the majority counts as the whole. But now the *shochet* goes ahead and finishes the remaining forty percent, and does so improperly — through *chalada*, or through *shehiya* (a pause in cutting), or through *hagrama* (cutting in the wrong location). What is the *halacha*?

For Rashi, this question is not unique to *chalada*. He explicitly ties it to earlier discussions in the *gemara* about *hagrama* and to a question that appears on the following daf concerning *shehiya bemiut batra* — a pause that occurs in the final minority of the cutting. In all these cases, Rashi identifies the same underlying conceptual question: once the *rov* has been accomplished, does the act of *shechita* effectively end? Or does the halachic act continue, such that if one goes ahead and completes the remaining portion incorrectly, that completion becomes retroactively incorporated into the act of *shechita* and can invalidate it?

Rashi frames this as a genuine dispute about the meaning of *rubo kekulo*. Is *rov* simply the minimum threshold required — after which *halacha* is satisfied and anything further is legally immaterial? Or does *rubo kekulo* mean that the *rov* is treated *as if* the whole were done — meaning that in principle, the act encompasses the full one hundred percent, and if the remaining portion is completed with a *psul*, that *psul* is part of the act and invalidates it? Since the *gemara* resolves none of this — leaving it as *teyku* — Rashi rules that we must be stringent.

### Rashi's Ruling


This stringency has practical implications that Rashi himself spells out in his commentary to the following daf. Suppose a *shochet* has already completed *rov* of the *simanim* and the animal is clearly dying, but it is taking time and suffering. A natural instinct would be to complete the *shechita* to hasten the animal's death. Rashi says: do not do it. Going back and completing the remaining minority of the *simanim* now — after a pause — would constitute *shehiya bemiut batra*, a *shehiya* in the final minority, and since that final minority may be part of the act of *shechita*, the *shehiya* could invalidate the entire *shechita*. If one wishes to end the animal's suffering, Rashi says to accomplish this by other means — severing the neck from behind, for example — but not by continuing the *shechita* itself.

### Rabbeinu Tam


*Tosafot* cites Rabbeinu Tam, who rejects Rashi's understanding entirely. According to Rabbeinu Tam, once the *rov* of the *simanim* has been accomplished, the act of *shechita* is complete. Anything that happens thereafter — any further cutting, any further contact of the knife with the *simanim* — is simply not part of the *shechita* act. It cannot affect what has already validly occurred. The problem of *shehiya bemiut batra*, or any other *psul* in the final minority, is thus a non-issue according to Rabbeinu Tam.

How, then, does Rabbeinu Tam read Rav Papa's question? He interprets *chalada bemiut simanim* as a different scenario entirely — not about completing the remainder after *rov*, but about a knife that penetrates *into* a *siman* (rather than between the two *simanim*) and cuts with only a *miut* of that *siman* lying beneath the blade. Is that considered *chalada* or not? That is the question according to Rabbeinu Tam, and it is entirely distinct from Rashi's formulation.

The debate thus comes into sharp focus. Rashi holds that *rubo kekulo* is a legal equivalence — the *rov* stands in for the whole, meaning the whole act remains in play, and completing it improperly can invalidate. Rabbeinu Tam holds that *rubo kekulo* means *rov* is simply sufficient — *halacha* requires only *rov*, and once achieved, the act ends there. What follows is legally irrelevant. This is one of the central conceptual disputes about the meaning of *rubo kekulo* in all of *halacha*, and it emerges here from a single ambiguous line of *gemara*.

## Multiple Heads & Knife

### Two Heads & Two People

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹחֵט שְׁנֵי רָאשִׁין כְּאֶחָד – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה. שְׁנַיִם אוֹחֲזִים בְּסַכִּין וְשׁוֹחֲטִים, אֲפִילּוּ אֶחָד לְמַעְלָה וְאֶחָד לְמַטָּה – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who slaughters** by cutting **two** animals' **heads simultaneously, his slaughter is valid.** If **two** people **are grasping a knife and slaughtering** one animal, **even** if each is holding a knife and slaughtering **one above and one below,** with each one slaughtering at a different point in the neck, their **slaughter is valid.**

The *gemara* turns to the next *mishna*, which addresses several related scenarios. *hashocheit bishnei roshim ke-echad* — one who slaughters two animal heads simultaneously. If the knife is long enough and the animals are positioned properly, one can slaughter in a single motion without applying pressure to either neck, and the *shechita* is valid. Similarly, *shnayim ochazim basakin veshochtim* — two people holding opposite ends of a knife together, as if using a saw, and slaughtering — *shechitatan kesheira*, it is valid. This is true even if one person is on one side of the neck and the other is on the other side — *afilu echad lemala ve'echad lemata* — or even if they are using two separate knives, one at each position.

### Head Severed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִתִּיז אֶת הָרֹאשׁ בְּבַת אַחַת – פְּסוּלָה. הָיָה שׁוֹחֵט וְהִתִּיז אֶת הָרֹאשׁ בְּבַת אַחַת, אִם יֵשׁ בַּסַּכִּין מְלֹא צַוָּאר – כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** If **one decapitated** the animal **in one** motion and did not slaughter the animal in the standard manner of drawing the knife back and forth, the slaughter is **not valid.** In a case where **one was** in the process of **slaughtering** the animal in the standard manner **and he decapitated** the animal **in one** motion, **if** the length **of the knife** is equivalent to **the** breadth of the animal's **entire neck,** the slaughter is **valid.**

The *mishna* then addresses a more complex case: *hitiz et harosh bebat achat* — if the head was entirely severed in one motion. The presumptive concern is *drisa*, the *psul* of applying pressure. If the entire head is chopped off in one stroke, we suspect that this occurred through force and pressure rather than through the proper horizontal cutting motion. Therefore, under ordinary circumstances, such a *shechita* is *pesulah*.

However, the *mishna* provides an exception: *haya shocheit vehitiz et harosh bebat achat, im yeish basakin mlo tzavar, kesheira* — if one was actively slaughtering with a horizontal cutting motion and the head was severed, if the knife had the length of a neck (*mlo tzavar*), it is valid.

The *gemara*'s explanation of this phrase is that the knife must measure the length of the animal's neck *plus* an additional neck length — that is, double the length of the neck. The logic is as follows: in a normal *shechita*, the knife travels back and forth, meaning the neck is effectively cut in two passes. A knife of double length, even in a single forward motion, covers the equivalent of two passes across the neck, making it possible to cut cleanly without pressure. If the knife achieves that double coverage through a single horizontal motion, we are not concerned that the severing of the head is evidence of *drisa*.

### Back and Forth Motion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? בִּזְמַן שֶׁהוֹלִיךְ וְלֹא הֵבִיא, אוֹ הֵבִיא וְלֹא הוֹלִיךְ, אֲבָל אִם הוֹלִיךְ וְהֵבִיא, אֲפִילּוּ כׇּל שֶׁהוּא, אֲפִילּוּ בְּאִיזְמֵל – כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **In what** case **is this statement,** that one must be concerned about the length of the knife, **said?** It is **when one drew** the knife **back and did not draw** it **forth, or drew it forth and did not draw it back; but** if **he drew it back and forth, even** if the knife was of **any length, even** if he slaughtered **with a scalpel [*be'izemel*],** the slaughter is **valid.**

The *mishna* then introduces the contrasting case: *im holich veheivi* — if the *shochet* was moving the knife back and forth — *afilu kol shehu afilu be'izmel kesheira* — even the tiniest blade, even an *izmel* (which Rashi identifies as a kind of scalpel or awl), is valid. This is a striking *chiddush*. The requirement for a double-length knife applies only when the *shochet* is performing a single directional motion. When cutting back and forth, even a blade of minimal width suffices, since the back-and-forth motion itself generates sufficient cutting across the *simanim* without recourse to pressure.

This point is worth pausing over. In principle, a *shochet* could perform *shechita* with a blade so small that it requires many back-and-forth passes to sever the *simanim* entirely. As long as no pressure is being applied, this would appear to be valid *shechita*. The concern about *drisa* is not triggered by the repetition of motion, only by the application of force or by evidence — such as the head being severed in a single motion with an insufficiently long blade — that force was applied.

### Source: Shachut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״חֵץ שָׁחוּט לְשׁוֹנָם מִרְמָה דִבֵּר״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna stated: If one decapitated the animal in one motion, the slaughter is not valid. The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived? **Shmuel said:** It is derived from a verse, **as the verse states: "Their tongue is a sharpened [*shaḥut*] arrow, it speaks deceit"** (Jeremiah 9:7). Just as an arrow is propelled by drawing back the bowstring, so too, slaughter [*sheḥita*] must be performed by drawing the knife across the animal's neck and not by pressing the knife or striking the neck with the knife.

The *gemara* asks: where is the textual source for the principle that *shechita* must be accomplished through the sharpness of the blade — through horizontal cutting — rather than through pressure? The answer is attributed to Shmuel, who derives it from the verse *chetz shachut leshonam mirma diber* (Jeremiah 9:7): their tongue is like a *shachut* arrow — an arrow that cuts by moving horizontally through the air by the sharpness of its edge, not by stabbing or pressing. The *tana debe-rabi yishmael* further derives it from the word *veshachat* in the Torah itself: *ein veshachat ela umashach* — *shachut* means to draw horizontally. Additional support comes from the verse *zahav shachut* — spun gold drawn into threads — which likewise conveys the image of a horizontal drawing motion.

The *gemara* notes that one might have thought *zahav shachut* is not derived from the same root as *shechita* — perhaps the *shin* is not part of the verbal root and the word simply means gold shaped into threads. Therefore the verse about the arrow (*chetz shachut*) is also cited, to confirm the association with horizontal cutting.

### Arrow Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא הֲוָה בָּדֵיק לֵיהּ גִּירָא לְרַבִּי יוֹנָה בַּר תַּחְלִיפָא, וְשָׁחַט בֵּהּ עוֹפָא בַּהֲדֵי דְּפָרַח. וְדִילְמָא עָבִיד חֲלָדָה? חֲזִינַן גִּידְפֵי דְּמִיפַּרְמִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rava would examine the arrow for Rabbi Yona bar Taḥlifa** to ensure that there were no notches in it. **And** Rava shot the arrow and **slaughtered a bird with it as it was flying.** The Gemara challenges: **And perhaps** when the arrow cut the bird's neck **it performed** an inverted slaughter, with the arrow **concealed** in the neck, and cut the *simanim* from back to front. The Gemara responds: **We see that the feathers** at the front of the neck were **unraveled** by the arrow, a clear indication that slaughter was performed from the front of the neck.

The *gemara* now narrates a striking story. Rava would inspect the arrows of Rabi Yona bar Tachlifa — a hunter — and Rabi Yona bar Tachlifa would use these arrows to kill birds *bahadi deperach*, as they were flying. The edge of the arrow, presumably checked by Rava to be free of nicks and to have the appropriate sharpness, would cut through the neck of the bird in flight.

The *gemara* immediately raises the concern: *udilma avad lei chalada* — perhaps the arrow burrowed through the neck, entering point-first rather than cutting with its edge? The *gemara* answers: *chazinan gidfei de'miframmei* — we can inspect the neck feathers and see that they were cut from the outside in, confirming that the edge of the arrow, and not the point, performed the cutting.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא בָּעֵי כִּסּוּי, וְכִי תֵּימָא דִּמְכַסּוּ לֵיהּ, וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב: הַשּׁוֹחֵט צָרִיךְ שֶׁיִּתֵּן עָפָר לְמַטָּה וְעָפָר לְמַעְלָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְכִסָּהוּ בֶּעָפָר״, ״עָפָר״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר אֶלָּא ״בֶּעָפָר״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהַשּׁוֹחֵט צָרִיךְ שֶׁיִּתֵּן עָפָר לְמַטָּה וְעָפָר לְמַעְלָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes another difficulty encountered in the slaughter of a flying bird with an arrow. **But doesn't** the bird's blood **require covering** with earth? **And if you would say that** Rabbi Yona bar Taḥlifa **covers** the blood, **but doesn't Rabbi Zeira say** that **Rav says:** In fulfilling the mitzva of covering the blood, **one who slaughters** an undomesticated animal or bird **must place earth beneath** the blood **and earth above** it, **as it is stated:** "He shall spill its blood, **and cover it in earth"** (Leviticus 17:13). **It is not stated:** Cover it with **earth, but rather "in earth**." This **teaches that one who slaughters must place earth beneath** the blood **and earth above** the blood, so that the blood will be within the earth.

The *gemara* then raises the question of *kisui ha-dam*. Since this is a bird (*of*), the blood must be covered. *ve'amar rabi zeira amar rav*: one who slaughters must place dirt below the blood and dirt above it, as derived from the verse *vechisahu be'afar* — "and he shall cover it in the dirt" — the preposition *be* indicating that dirt must be beneath as well as above. The solution offered for Rabi Yona bar Tachlifa's practice is that he would spread dirt across the entire valley where he hunted, so that wherever the bird fell, its blood would land on the prepared earth and could be covered from above.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּמַזְמֵין לֵיהּ לְעָפָר דְּכוּלַּהּ פִּתְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers **that** Rabbi Yona bar Taḥlifa would **designate for himself** the **earth of the entire valley [*patka*]** before shooting the arrow. That earth would serve as the layer of earth beneath the blood and he would proceed to cover the blood with another layer of earth.

The discussion in the shiur naturally raises the question of whether the arrow constitutes a sufficient act of *shechita* given that the hunter is no longer holding it when it cuts the bird. This leads directly into the subject of *koach gavra*, which will be addressed explicitly in the next *mishna*. Theoretically, the *gemara* will clarify, valid *shechita* does not require the *shochet* to be physically manipulating the knife at the moment of cutting — it requires that the act issue from human energy (*koach gavra*), not that the human hand be in contact throughout. An arrow released from a bow, like a knife released from the hand, still carries the energy of the one who propelled it.

This also occasions a broader discussion about how far *koach gavra* can be extended in principle. If one's energy is communicated through a mechanical chain — pedaling a bicycle that drives a machine — that seems clearly to qualify. If one presses a button and the energy that performs the act travels through electrical wires, the question becomes considerably more complex. The degree of separation between the human actor and the act itself is not always easy to measure, and such questions recur throughout *halacha* wherever the concept of *koach gavra* is operative.

## Knife Fell

### Knife Falls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נָפְלָה סַכִּין וְשָׁחֲטָה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁשָּׁחֲטָה כְּדַרְכָּהּ – פְּסוּלָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְזָבַחְתָּ … וְאָכַלְתָּ״, מַה שֶּׁאַתָּה זוֹבֵחַ אַתָּה אוֹכֵל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **a knife fell and slaughtered** an animal, **although** the knife **slaughtered** the animal **in the standard manner,** the slaughter is **not valid, as it is stated: "And you shall slaughter…and you shall eat"** (Deuteronomy 27:7), from which it is derived: **That which you slaughter you may eat,** and that which was slaughtered on its own, you may not eat.

The following *mishna* states: *nafla sakin veshachata* — a knife fell and in the process performed *shechita*. Even though it slaughtered the animal correctly — *af al pi sheshachata kedarka* — the *shechita* is *pesulah*. The *mishna* derives this from the verse *vezavachta ve'achalta*: "you shall slaughter and you shall eat" — what *you* slaughter, *you* may eat. The act of *shechita* must be a human act, not merely an event that happens to cut the *simanim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ טַעְמָא דְּנָפְלָה, הָא הִפִּילָהּ הוּא – כְּשֵׁרָה, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא מִיכַּוֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that if a knife fell and slaughtered an animal the slaughter is not valid. The Gemara notes: **The reason** the slaughter is not valid is **that** the knife **fell. But** by inference, if **one dropped** the knife the slaughter is **valid, and** that is the *halakha* **even though** when dropping the knife he **did not intend** to slaughter the animal.

The *gemara* extracts from the *mishna* an implied leniency: *ta'ama denafla* — the *mishna's* reason is that the knife fell on its own; *ha hipila*, if the person caused it to fall — even by simply releasing it — the *shechita* is valid. This establishes that *koach gavra* does not demand that the knife be actively held at the moment of cutting; it is sufficient that the cutting is the product of the human's energy, even if communicated indirectly.

The *gemara* further notes: *ve'af al gav dela michavein* — and this is so even though the person had no intention for the *shechita* to occur. This introduces a second, independent question: does *shechita* require *kavana*, intent?

### Throwing Knife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תַּנָּא דְּלָא בָּעֵינַן כַּוָּונָה לִשְׁחִיטָה? אָמַר רָבָא: רַבִּי נָתָן הִיא, דְּתָנֵי אוֹשַׁעְיָא זְעֵירָא דְּמִן חַבְרַיָּא: זָרַק סַכִּין לְנוֹעֳצָהּ בַּכּוֹתֶל, וְהָלְכָה וְשָׁחֲטָה כְּדַרְכָּהּ – רַבִּי נָתָן מַכְשִׁיר, וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹסְלִים. הוּא תָּנֵי לַהּ, וְהוּא אָמַר לַהּ: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי נָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who is the *tanna*** who holds **that we do not require intent for slaughter? Rava said:** It **is Rabbi Natan, as Oshaya, the youngest of the company** of Sages, **taught** a *baraita*: If **one threw a knife to embed it in the wall and** in the course of its flight the knife **went and slaughtered** an animal **in its** proper **manner, Rabbi Natan deems** the slaughter **valid and the Rabbis deem** the slaughter **not valid.** Oshaya **teaches** the *baraita* **and he says** about **it:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Natan** that there is no need for intent to perform a valid act of slaughter.

The *gemara* cites a *baraita* taught by the younger Oshaya: *zarak sakin linoatza bakotel* — if one threw a knife at a wall, intending to stick it there, and the knife passed by an animal and slaughtered it *kedarka* — properly — *rabbi natan machshir*, Rabbi Natan declares the *shechita* valid. *vechachamim poslin*, the Sages invalidate it.

Here the situation is clearly one of *koach gavra* — the knife was propelled by human energy — but there is obviously no intention whatsoever to perform *shechita*. Rabbi Natan holds that *shechita* does not require intention. The Sages hold that it does. The *gemara* reports: *halacha ke-Rabbi Natan* — we rule like Rabbi Natan. *shechita* of *chullin* does not require *kavana*.

### Two Criteria

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אַמְרַהּ רָבָא חֲדָא זִימְנָא, דִּתְנַן: וְכוּלָּן שֶׁשָּׁחֲטוּ וַאֲחֵרִים רוֹאִים אוֹתָן – שְׁחִיטָתָן כְּשֵׁרָה. וְאָמְרִינַן: מַאן תַּנָּא דְּלָא בָּעֵי כַּוָּונָה לִשְׁחִיטָה? וְאָמַר רָבָא: רַבִּי נָתָן הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't Rava** already **say it one time** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Natan? **As we learned** in a mishna (2a): **And** with regard to **any of them,** a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor, **who slaughtered** an animal **and others see** and supervise **them, their slaughter is valid** even though they are incapable of intent. **And we said: Who is the *tanna*** who holds **that we do not require intent for slaughter? And Rava said:** It **is Rabbi Natan.**

The *gemara* reflects on the analytical structure underlying these two *mishnayot*. Normally, for an act to be halachically operative, we presume two criteria: first, it must be a human act — it must issue from human energy (*koach gavra*); second, it must be intentional — if it is entirely incidental, it is treated as *mitasek*, something that simply happened without the person's agency. Rabbi Natan's ruling is striking precisely because it disaggregates these two criteria. *koach gavra* is required; *kavana* is not.

### Both Mishnayot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְועִינַן הָתָם – מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא מִיכַּוֵּין לְשׁוּם חֲתִיכָה בְּעוֹלָם, אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּלָא קָא מִיכַּוֵּין – אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָכָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּקָאָתֵי מִכֹּחַ בֶּן דַּעַת, אֲבָל הָתָם דְּלָא קָאָתֵי מִכֹּחַ בֶּן דַּעַת – אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Both statements **are necessary. As had** Rava **taught us** his statement **there** with regard to a deaf-mute, an imbecile, and a minor, one would have thought that the slaughter is valid **due to** the fact **that** although the individual lacks intent to slaughter the animal, **he intends** his action **for the sake of cutting in general. But here,** with regard to throwing a knife at the wall, **where he does not intend** to cut at all, **say no,** the slaughter is not valid. **And had** Rava **taught us** his statement **here** with regard to throwing the knife, one would have thought that the slaughter is valid is **due to** the fact **that** it **comes due to** the action **of a mentally competent person. But there,** with regard to slaughter by a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor, **where** the slaughter **does not come due to** the action **of a mentally competent person, say** that the slaughter is **not** valid. Therefore, it is **necessary** for Rava to teach both cases.

One might ask: given that Rabbi Natan's principle is stated explicitly in the *baraita* about the thrown knife, why did Rava find it necessary to identify Rabbi Natan's view in connection with the earlier *mishna* about the *cheresh, shoteh, ve-katan* (the deaf-mute, the insane person, and the minor) as well? The *gemara* answers: *tzricha* — both statements are necessary.

Had Rava only cited the earlier *mishna*, one might have explained the permissibility of the *cheresh, shoteh, ve-katan* on narrow grounds: those individuals are, in some sense, *intending to cut*. As *tosafot* notes, they are aware that they are cutting the trachea and esophagus. Their lack of *kavana* is therefore narrow — they may not have the capacity to intend the halachic implications of *shechita*, but they have some intention. In that case, the *chiddush* of the earlier *mishna* is not about *kavana* at all, but about whether *shechita* requires an actor who has full halachic standing (*ben da'at*). One might then have thought that a person of full standing throwing a knife with no intention at all would not suffice, since there is truly zero *kavana*. The *baraita* of Rabbi Natan is therefore needed to teach that even in that case, *shechita* is valid.

Conversely, had Rava only cited the *baraita* about throwing the knife, one might have concluded that *kavana* is not required but *koach gavra* must come from a halachically competent adult. The earlier *mishna*, which validates the *shechita* of a minor or deaf-mute when observed by others, adds the complementary *chiddush*: the act need not issue from someone with full halachic standing. Together, the two teachings establish that *shechita* of *chullin* requires *koach gavra* — but neither *kavana* nor a fully competent actor.

## Intent Required?

### Nidda Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: נִדָּה שֶׁנֶּאֶנְסָה וְטָבְלָה, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: טְהוֹרָה לְבֵיתָהּ, וַאֲסוּרָה לֶאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָה. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אַף לְבֵיתָהּ לֹא טָהֲרָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna is now cited as proof in an amoraic dispute. **It was stated:** With regard to **a menstruating woman who,** after the menstrual flow ended, **was compelled** against her will **and immersed** in a ritual bath, **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: She is ritually pure vis-à-vis her house,** i.e., it is permitted for her to engage in intercourse with her husband, **but it is prohibited for her to partake of *teruma*** because the immersion is not considered valid for that purpose. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: She was not purified even vis-à-vis her house.**

The *gemara* now broadens the discussion from *shechita* to the general question of when *halacha* requires *kavana* and when it does not. The springboard is a case involving a *nidda* (a woman in the state of ritual impurity): *nidda shene'ensa vetavla* — a *nidda* who was compelled and immersed in a *mikveh* without intent. Is this a valid *tevilah*?

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *tehora leveisa* — she is permitted to her husband; *ve-asura leochel bitruma* — but she may not eat *teruma*. The distinction rests on the principle, established elsewhere, that immersion for the purpose of eating *kodshim* or *teruma* requires specific intent. Without that intent, the *tevilah* does not achieve purification for those purposes. But for the lesser status — her relationship with her husband, which is a matter of *chullin* — no such intent is needed.

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *af leveisa lo tehora* — she is not even permitted to her husband. For Rabbi Yochanan, the entire framework of *tumah ve-tahara* requires intent, and one cannot split the package.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן, לְרַב דְּאָמַר: טְהוֹרָה לְבֵיתָהּ וַאֲסוּרָה לֶאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָה – עֲוֹן כָּרֵת (הוּתְּרָה) [הִתַּרְתָּ], אִיסּוּר מִיתָה מִיבַּעְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Rav Naḥman: According to** the opinion of **Rav, who says** that **she is ritually pure vis-à-vis her house but it is prohibited for her to partake of *teruma*,** it is difficult. With regard to **a transgression** punishable **by *karet*,** i.e., intercourse with a menstruating woman, **she was rendered permitted** by immersion against her will; with regard to partaking of *teruma*, **a prohibition** punishable by **death** at the hand of Heaven, which is a lesser punishment, **is** it **necessary** to say that it is permitted for her through immersion against her will? Why then does Rav deem it prohibited for her to partake of *teruma*?

Rava challenged Rav's position before Rav Nachman: if this *tevilah* suffices to lift the *karet* prohibition (the severe prohibition of a man being with a woman who is a *nidda*), why would it not lift the lesser prohibition associated with eating *teruma*, which carries only the penalty of death by divine hands (*mita*) rather than *karet*? Rav Nachman responded: the distinction is not about the relative severity of the prohibition. *ba'alah chullin hu* — her husband belongs to the sphere of *chullin*, of lay matters. *uvechullin lo ba'ei kavana* — when it comes to *chullin*, intent is not required. *teruma*, by contrast, belongs to the sphere of sanctified obligations, where intent is required.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בַּעְלָהּ חוּלִּין הוּא, וְחוּלִּין לָא בָּעֵי כַּוָּונָה. וּמְנָא תֵּימְרָא? דִּתְנַן: גַּל שֶׁנִּתְלַשׁ וּבוֹ אַרְבָּעִים סְאָה וְנָפַל עַל הָאָדָם וְעַל הַכֵּלִים – טְהוֹרִין. מַאי לָאו אָדָם דּוּמְיָא דְּכֵלִים? מָה כֵּלִים דְּלָא מִיכַּוְּונִי, אַף אָדָם נָמֵי לָא בָּעֵי כַּוָּונָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman **said to him:** The halakhic status of **her husband is non-sacred, and non-sacred** items **do not require intent** for purification. **And from where do you say** so? It is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Mikvaot* 5:6): In the case of **a wave that was detached** from the sea, **and in it** were **forty *se'a*** of water, **and** that wave **fell on** an impure **person or on** impure **vessels, they are ritually pure. What, is it not** that **a person** is **similar to vessels? Just as vessels do not intend** to be purified and they are purified by the wave, **so too, a person does not require intent** in order to be purified.

This formulation — *chullin lo ba'ei kavana* — becomes the operative principle. When a halachic act serves to change a status in the domain of ordinary, non-sanctified matters, that act does not require *kavana*. The requirement of *kavana* is limited to the domain of *kodshim* — of sanctified objects and elevated halachic categories — and even there it may be rabbinic in origin.

### Wave Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא בְּיוֹשֵׁב וּמְצַפֶּה עָסְקִינַן, אֵימָתַי יִתָּלֵשׁ הַגַּל,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that proof: **From where** is there proof that this is the meaning of the mishna? **Perhaps we are dealing with** the case of one who **sits** near the water **and waits** to determine **when the wave will be detached,** which is tantamount to having intent to immerse,

The *gemara* attempts to prove from this case that *tevilah* without intent is valid: just as the vessels have no intention when the water strikes them, so too the person's immersion should be valid without intent — and thus *chullin* does not require *kavana*.

### Intent Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵלִים דּוּמְיָא דְּאָדָם, מָה אָדָם דְּבָעֵינַן כַּוָּונָה, אַף כֵּלִים נָמֵי דְּקָא מְכַוֵּין לְהוּ אָדָם! וְכִי תֵּימָא, בְּיוֹשֵׁב וּמְצַפֶּה, מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: לִיגְזַר מִשּׁוּם חַרְדָּלִית שֶׁל גְּשָׁמִים, אִי נָמֵי לִיגְזַר רָאשִׁין אַטּוּ כִּיפִּין, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא גָּזְרִינַן!
>
> **Translation:** **and vessels** are **similar to a person: Just as** for **a person, we require** his **intent** for purification, **so too** for **vessels,** they are purified only in a case **where a person intends for them** to be purified. **And if you would say:** If the mishna is referring **to** the case of one who **sits and waits** to determine when the wave will be detached, **what** is the purpose **of stating** this *halakha*? It is obvious and introduces no novel element. The Gemara responds that there is a novel element in this *halakha*. **Lest you say: Let us issue a decree** that a detached wave does not effect purification **due to** the concern that otherwise, one would receive the mistaken impression that one is purified in **a cascade [*ḥardalit*] of rainwater** containing forty *se'a*. The *halakha* is that rainwater purifies only when pooled in one place. **Alternatively, let us issue a decree** that **the edges** of the waves, which are in contact with the ground, are ineffective in purifying people and vessels standing on the ground **due to** the concern that otherwise one would receive the mistaken impression that vessels are purified even if one pushes them upward into the **arc** of the waves while the water remains airborne. Therefore, the *tanna* **teaches us that we do not issue** either of those **decrees.**

The *gemara* deflects this argument: *dilma beyoshev umetzappeh askinan* — perhaps the *mishna* is speaking of a person who is sitting and waiting for the wave, anticipating its arrival, effectively having *kavana* in advance. And if the person had intent, perhaps the vessels are analogous — they were placed there intentionally by someone who wanted the wave to strike them. If so, the *mishna* would not prove anything about the absence of *kavana*.

The *gemara* then asks: if both the person and the vessels had intent, what is the *mishna's chiddush*? The answer is that the *mishna* is teaching that one need not be concerned about confusing this act with immersion in the arc (*kif*) of a wave rather than in its head (*rosh*). The head of a wave — the part that strikes the ground — constitutes a valid *mikveh*; the arc of the wave, suspended in midair, does not, because *she'ein matbilin ba'avir* — one cannot perform *tevilah* in midair. A separate *mishna* teaches: *matbilin berashim ve'ein matbilin bekifin*. The *chiddush* of the wave *mishna* may thus be entirely about this distinction, leaving the question of *kavana* unresolved.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא תֵּימְרָא דְּלָא מַטְבְּלִינַן בְּכִיפִּין? דִּתְנַן: מַטְבִּילִין בְּרָאשִׁים, וְאֵין מַטְבִּילִין בְּכִיפִּין, שֶׁאֵין מַטְבִּילִין בָּאֲוִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where do you say that we do not immerse in the arcs** of waves? **As we learned** in a *baraita*: **One may immerse in the edges** of waves, **but one may not immerse in** their **arcs, as one may not immerse in air.** Immersion may be performed only on the ground.

A subtle additional point deserves mention. Even if one were to posit that the person in the wave case had *kavana* — sitting and waiting for the wave — this raises a conceptual difficulty about how *kavana* functions. Normally, *kavana* accompanies an intentional *act*: I lower myself into the *mikveh* with intent; I move the knife with intent. Here, the person performs no action — the wave acts upon them. Even if one stipulates that the person's waiting constitutes an act of some kind, the extension of *kavana* to this setting — particularly for the vessels, which are inanimate objects placed passively on the shore — is conceptually strained. The *gemara* does not press this point further, but it is a genuine difficulty.

### Fruit Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא חוּלִּין דְּלָא בָּעֵי כַּוָּונָה מִיהָא מְנָלַן? דִּתְנַן: פֵּירוֹת שֶׁנָּפְלוּ לְתוֹךְ אַמַּת הַמַּיִם, וּפָשַׁט מִי שֶׁיָּדָיו טְמֵאוֹת וּנְטָלָן – יָדָיו טְהוֹרוֹת, וּפֵירוֹת אֵינָן בְּ״כִי יוּתַּן״. וְאִם בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיּוּדְחוּ יָדָיו – טְהוֹרוֹת, וּפֵירוֹת בְּ״כִי יוּתַּן״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara again asks: **But in any event, from where do we** derive **that non-sacred** items **do not require intent?** The Gemara answers: It is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Makhshirin* 4:7): Produce becomes susceptible to ritual impurity only if it is dampened by one of seven liquids and its owner was amenable to its dampening. This is derived from the verse: "But when water is placed on the seed, and some of their carcass shall fall on it, it is impure to you" (Leviticus 11:38). If **produce fell into a stream, and one whose hands were ritually impure extended** his hands **and took** the produce from the water channel, **his hands are ritually pure** through immersion in the stream, **and** this **produce is not in** the category of: "But **when** water **is placed."** The produce is not susceptible to ritual impurity because the owner did not intend that his hands become wet. **But if** he placed his hands into the stream **so that** his hands **would be rinsed** and purified, **his hands are ritually pure, and the produce is in** the category of: "But **when** water **is placed."** Since he was amenable to the dampening of his hands, the water on his hands renders the produce susceptible to ritual impurity.

The *gemara* therefore seeks a cleaner proof and cites another *mishna*: *peiros shenafalu toch ammat ha-mayim* — fruit that fell into a water channel. A person who was *tamei* stuck his hands into the water to retrieve the fruit: *yadav tehoros* — his hands are *tahor* — *upeiros einam beki yutan* — and the fruit is not rendered susceptible to *tumah* through the water.

The logic of the *mishna* is as follows: the hands, having been submerged in water, are purified — yet the person had no intention to purify his hands; he merely wanted to retrieve the fruit. The fruit, meanwhile, is not rendered susceptible to *tumah* because there was no desire (*ratzon*) for the water to be on the fruit — contact with water only renders produce susceptible to *tumah* if the owner desired that contact (*ki yutan*). In this case there was no such desire.

The *mishna* then provides a contrasting case: if the person wanted his hands to be cleaned, then *yadav tehoros* — his hands are *tahor* for the obvious reason — *upeiros beki yutan* — and now the fruit is also susceptible, because his desire to have his hands washed constitutes sufficient desire for the water, which was in contact with the fruit.

The proof for present purposes is the first case: the person's hands were purified without any intention to purify them. This demonstrates, at least in the domain of *netilat yadayim*, that a halachic change in status can occur without *kavana*.

### Rav Nachman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: טָבַל לְחוּלִּין וְהוּחְזַק לְחוּלִּין – אָסוּר לְמַעֲשֵׂר. הוּחְזַק – אִין, לֹא הוּחְזַק – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised an objection to Rav Naḥman** from a mishna (*Ḥagiga* 18b): If **one immersed for** the purpose of eating **non-sacred** food **and assumed the presumptive status** of ritual purity **for non-sacred** food, it **is prohibited** for him to partake of second-**tithe** produce. The Gemara infers: If **one assumed the presumptive status** of ritual purity with regard to non-sacred food, **yes,** it is permitted for him to eat non-sacred food; **if he did not assume the presumptive status,** he may **not** eat non-sacred food. This indicates that even when immersing in order to partake of non-sacred food, one must intend to assume the presumptive status of ritual purity.

Rava challenged Rav Nachman with a seemingly contradictory teaching: *taval lechullin vehuchzak lechullin asur lema'aser* — if a person immersed in a *mikveh* intending the *tevilah* to work for *chullin* and was established for *chullin*, he is forbidden to eat *maaser*. This seems to imply that only by virtue of having intent does the *tevilah* count for *chullin* — *huchzak in, lo huchzak lo* — with establishment it works, without it does not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהוּחְזַק לְחוּלִּין – אָסוּר לְמַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman rejects the proof from the mishna and says that no intent is required to assume the presumptive status of ritual purity in order to eat non-sacred food. Rather, **this** is what the mishna **is saying: Although he assumes the presumptive status** of ritual purity **for non-sacred** food, it **is prohibited** for him to partake of second-**tithe** produce.

Rav Nachman reinterpreted the teaching: *af al pi shehuchzak lechullin asur lema'aser* — the point is that even if one intended the *tevilah* for *chullin* and was established for *chullin*, one still cannot eat *maaser sheni*. It does not mean that intent is a prerequisite for *chullin*; it means that having established oneself for *chullin* does not automatically extend to *maaser sheni*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: טָבַל וְלֹא הוּחְזַק – כְּאִילּוּ לֹא טָבַל. מַאי לָאו: כְּאִילּוּ לֹא טָבַל כְּלָל?
>
> **Translation:** Rava **raised an objection to** Rav Naḥman from that same mishna: If **one immersed without** intent to **assume the presumptive status** of ritual purity, it is **as though he did not immerse. What, is** the meaning of the mishna **not** that it is **as though he did not immerse at all?**

Rava pressed further with another formulation: *taval velo huchzak ke'ilu lo taval* — if one immersed without being established for a particular level, it is as if one did not immerse at all. Rav Nachman explained: *ke'ilu lo taval lema'aser* — it is as if one did not immerse with respect to *maaser*, but *aval taval lechullin* — it does work for *chullin*. Rava, thinking this answer was evasive, went and searched and found an explicit *baraita*: *taval velo huchzak muttar lechullin ve'asur lema'aser* — one who immersed without establishing intent is permitted for *chullin* and forbidden for *maaser*. This confirms Rav Nachman's position explicitly: *tevilah* without intent is valid for *chullin*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, כְּאִילּוּ לֹא טָבַל לְמַעֲשֵׂר, אֲבָל טָבַל לְחוּלִּין. הוּא סָבַר: דַּיחוֹיֵי קָא מְדַחֵי לֵיהּ. נְפַק דָּק וְאַשְׁכַּח, דְּתַנְיָא: טָבַל וְלֹא הוּחְזַק – מוּתָּר לַחוּלִּין וְאָסוּר לַמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman rejects that proof as well. **No,** it means that if he immersed without intent it is **as though he did not immerse to** partake of second-**tithe** produce, **but** in that case, **he immersed for non-sacred** food, for which no intent is necessary. The Gemara comments: Rava **believed** that Rav Naḥman **was** merely **putting him off** with his claim that the formulation of the mishna does not conclusively support his objection; he believed that Rav Naḥman was not stating the real meaning of the mishna. Rava then **went out, examined** the sources, **and discovered that it is taught** in a *baraita* explicitly in accordance with the opinion of Rav Naḥman: If one **immersed and** had **no** intent to **assume the presumptive status** of ritual purity, it **is permitted** for him **to** eat **non-sacred** food, **but** it **is prohibited** for him **to** partake of second-**tithe** produce.

### Rabbi Yonatan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: לֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מֵהָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: Let us say that** there **will be a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan from this** *baraita*. This *baraita* states that immersion without intent is effective for non-sacred items, while Rabbi Yoḥanan said (31a) that if a woman who is impure due to menstruation immerses without intent she is forbidden to her husband, who is non-sacred.

At this point, Abaye asked Rav Yosef whether this *baraita* constitutes a definitive refutation of Rabbi Yochanan's position that *kavana* is required even for *chullin*. Rav Yosef responded that Rabbi Yochanan could be understood as ruling in accordance with Rabbi Yonatan ben Yosef.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הוּא דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי יוֹנָתָן בֶּן יוֹסֵף.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef **said to him:** Indeed, the *baraita* is contrary to his opinion, but **Rabbi Yoḥanan is saying** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yonatan ben Yosef.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן בֶּן יוֹסֵף אוֹמֵר: ״וְכוּבַּס״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״שֵׁנִית״? מַקִּישׁ תִּכְבּוֹסֶת שְׁנִיָּה לְתִכְבּוֹסֶת רִאשׁוֹנָה: מָה תִּכְבּוֹסֶת רִאשׁוֹנָה לְדַעַת, אַף תִּכְבּוֹסֶת שְׁנִיָּה לְדַעַת.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse dealing with the purification of a leprous garment, which must be laundered, quarantined for a week, and then immersed in a ritual bath: "And the garment…that you shall wash and the leprosy departed from them, and it shall be washed a second time and shall be pure" (Leviticus 13:58). **Rabbi Yonatan ben Yosef says:** It would have been sufficient for the verse to simply state: **And it shall be washed** and shall be pure. For **what** purpose does **the verse state: "A second time"?** The Torah **juxtaposes the second washing,** the immersion, **with the first washing,** the laundering. **Just as the first washing is** performed **with intent,** as it is written: "And the priest shall command that they wash the garment that has the leprous mark, and he shall quarantine it seven days more" (Leviticus 13:54), **so too, the second washing,** the immersion in a ritual bath, must be performed **with intent.**

A *baraita* records Rabbi Yonatan ben Yosef's position: in analyzing the double use of the washing requirement (*tichabbeis*) for garments afflicted with *tzara'at*, he derives through a *hekesh* (analogical derivation) that just as the first washing is performed with the intent of the *kohen* who commands it, so too the second washing — the purification — requires intent. And this is true even though we are not dealing with *kodshim*. Thus Rabbi Yonatan ben Yosef explicitly holds that *kavana* is required for *tahara* in a context of *chullin*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מָה לְהַלָּן בָּעֵינַן דַּעַת כֹּהֵן, אַף כָּאן בָּעֵינַן דַּעַת כֹּהֵן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְטָהֵר״, מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **If** so, based on the same juxtaposition, perhaps derive: **Just as there,** with regard to the first washing, **we require the intent of a priest,** who commands to wash the garment, **so too here,** with regard to the second washing, **we require the intent of a priest.** Therefore, **the verse states: "And shall be pure"** (Leviticus 13:58), indicating that there is purity **in any case** where there is intent, even without a command from a priest. In the opinion of Rabbi Yonatan ben Yosef, even the immersion of the non-sacred garment must be performed with intent. Rav Yosef states that Rabbi Yoḥanan bases his statement on Rabbi Yonatan ben Yosef's opinion.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי: וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כִּסְתַם מִשְׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shimi bar Ashi objects to** the association of the statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan with the opinion of Rabbi Yonatan ben Yosef: **Did Rabbi Yoḥanan say that** immersion of a non-sacred garment requires intent? **But doesn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say** that **the *halakha* is in accordance with an unattributed mishna?**

The *gemara* then questions whether Rabbi Yochanan could really have held this position, given that Rabbi Yochanan himself ruled that *halacha* follows every *stam mishna* (an anonymous *mishna* representing the majority view). And the *stam mishna* about the knife that fell — from which we derived that dropping a knife intentionally constitutes valid *shechita* even without *kavana* — appears to operate as a *stam mishna* ruling against the requirement of intent.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְנַן: נָפְלָה סַכִּין וְשָׁחֲטָה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁשָּׁחֲטָה כְּדַרְכָּהּ – פְּסוּלָה. וְהָוֵינַן בָּהּ: טַעְמָא דְּנָפְלָה, הָא הִפִּילָהּ הוּא – כְּשֵׁרָה, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא מִיכַּוֵּין. וְאָמְרִינַן: מַאן תַּנָּא דְּלָא בָּעֵי כַּוָּונָה לִשְׁחִיטָה? אָמַר רָבָא: רַבִּי נָתָן הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **And we learned** in the mishna: If **a knife fell and slaughtered** an animal, **although** the knife **slaughtered** the animal **in the standard manner,** the slaughter is **not valid. And we discussed it: The reason** the slaughter is not valid is **that** the knife **fell. But** by inference, if **one dropped** the knife the slaughter is **valid, and** that is the ruling **even though** when dropping the knife he **did not intend** to slaughter the animal. **And we say: Who is the *tanna*** who holds **that we do not require intent for slaughter? Rava said:** It **is Rabbi Natan.** Based on his principle that the *halakha* is in accordance with an unattributed mishna, Rabbi Yoḥanan should rule in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Natan cited in the unattributed mishna, and not in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yonatan ben Yosef.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁחִיטָה, אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן בֶּן יוֹסֵף [מוֹדֵי], מִדְּגַלִּי רַחֲמָנָא מִתְעַסֵּק בְּקָדָשִׁים פָּסוּל – מִכְּלָל דְּחוּלִּין לָא בָּעֵינַן כַּוָּונָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **With regard to slaughter, even Rabbi Yonatan ben Yosef** concedes that intent is not necessary. He learns this **from the fact that the Merciful One revealed** that if **one acts unawares** in performing the slaughter **of sacrificial** animals, with no intent to slaughter, the offering **is disqualified.** This is derived (13a) from the verse: "You shall slaughter it to your will" (Leviticus 19:5). **By inference,** conclude that with regard to the slaughter **of non-sacred** animals **we do not require intent.**

The resolution is that *shechita* is a special case — *bishechita afilu Rabbi Yonatan ben Yosef* would agree that *kavana* is not required, even according to the opinion that otherwise mandates intent for *chullin*. The proof is derived from the Torah's own language regarding *shechitat kodshim*: the Torah explicitly states that one who acts as *mitasek* — who inadvertently performs what would otherwise constitute *shechitat kodshim* without realizing it — has not performed a valid *shechitat kodshim*. The *gemara* draws the inference: *michlal delachullin lo ba'inan kavana* — the very fact that the Torah singles out *kodshim* as requiring intent implies, by contrast, that for *chullin*, *kavana* is not required.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, נְהִי דְּלָא בָּעֵינַן כַּוָּונָה לִזְבִיחָה, לַחֲתִיכָה בָּעֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** While on this subject the Gemara clarifies: **And the Rabbis** who disagree with Rabbi Natan and hold that slaughter of non-sacred animals requires intent would say: **Although we do not require intent to slaughter** non-sacred animals, **we require** intent **to cut** the neck of the animal. Throwing the knife down is not sufficient.

Thus the conclusion is nuanced and carefully calibrated. By *shechitat chullin*, everyone agrees — even Rabbi Yonatan ben Yosef — that *kavana* is not required. The broader question, whether *chullin* generally requires *kavana* for acts that change halachic status (such as *tevilah*), is where the debate lies. We will continue from this point in the next session.
