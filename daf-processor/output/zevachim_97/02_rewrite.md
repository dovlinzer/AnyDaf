# Zevachim 97 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Merika U'Shtifa

### Opening & Mishna

Daf 97 opens by returning to the *mishna* from the bottom of 96b, which concerns the laws of cooking sacrificial meat in a pot and the subsequent obligations of *merika u'shtifa* — the ritual rinsing and scalding required of vessels used for *kodashim*. The *mishna* presents a dispute between Rabbi Tarfon and the *chakhamim* regarding when this process must be performed, followed by a description of what *merika u'shtifa* actually entails. The *mishna* then turns to the case of the *hashpud v'ha'askala* — the spit and the grill — and states that these require *hagala*, kashering by immersion in boiling water.

### Rabbi Tarfon

Rabbi Tarfon's position is that if a pot was used to cook a *korban* at the start of a *regel*, it may continue to be used throughout the entire *regel* without requiring the daily kashering procedure. Normally, one would expect that by the following morning, when the meat cooked in the pot would have become *notar*, the vessel would require kashering. Rabbi Tarfon suspends this obligation for the duration of the festival. The *gemara* asks: what is his reasoning?

The verse states, *u'fanita baboker v'halachta l'ohalecha* — "and you shall turn in the morning and go to your tent" (Deuteronomy 16:7), referring to the *regel*. From this, the *gemara* derives: *hakosev asao l'kulan boker echad* — Scripture has made all of the festival days into a single morning. Rashi explains that since on the first day of *yom tov* one cannot return home — one's "tent" is not within permissible walking distance — the verse must be referring to *chol hamoed*. Because there are only two mornings during *chol hamoed* on which one could go home, Rabbi Tarfon somehow derives that the entire *regel* is treated as a single day. The precise logical step is admittedly difficult to reconstruct, and *Tosafot* works to clarify the argument.

### Piggul & Notar Challenge

The *gemara* challenges whether we are truly prepared to follow this reasoning to its logical conclusion. Rav Achadevoi bar Ami objects: *v'chi ein piggul baregel v'ein notar baregel?* — is there really no *piggul* on a *regel* and no *notar* on a *regel*? If the entire festival is treated as one halakhic day, would slaughtering a *korban* on one day of the *regel* with intent to eat it three days later not constitute *piggul*, since they would be considered the same day? And would meat not become *notar* throughout the festival?

One might respond — and the *gemara* entertains this — that perhaps Rabbi Tarfon indeed holds this position. But a *beraita* forecloses that reading: *lo amar Rabbi Tarfon ela zo bilvad* — Rabbi Tarfon only said his ruling with respect to the *merika* and *shtifa*, the kashering of the vessel. He did not apply the "single day" principle to the actual eating of the meat or to the laws of *piggul* and *notar*.

### Daily Kashering Theory

How, then, does the vessel escape the daily obligation of *merika u'shtifa* without invoking the "all one day" principle? The *gemara* answers with a ruling of Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha: *kol yom vayom naase giul lachaveiro* — each day kashers the vessel for the next day.

### Shelamim Kashering

The mechanism works as follows. During the *regel*, the overwhelming majority of offerings brought are *shelamim*, which carry a two-day eating period. When a *chatas* is cooked in a pot — say at ten in the morning — and a *shelamim* is subsequently cooked in that same pot — say at eleven — the cooking of the *shelamim* itself constitutes a *hagala*, kashering the pot of the absorbed *chatas* taste. This is not the formal biblical ritual of *merika u'shtifa*, but a natural kashering through the subsequent cooking. By the next morning there is therefore no *notar* embedded in the walls of the pot, and no obligation of *merika u'shtifa* arises.

The same dynamic continues: the *shelamim* from Monday remains in the pot's walls with one day remaining before it would become *notar*, but Tuesday's *shelamim* kashers the pot of Monday's *shelamim*, and so on. As long as there is a continuous stream of *shelamim* being cooked — which is guaranteed during the *regel* — the pot is effectively being kashered each day by the next day's cooking. The critical condition is that a *shelamim* must be cooked in the pot before the day's *chatas* reaches its *notar* deadline. Rashi notes either that care is taken to ensure this sequence, or that the sheer volume of *shelamim* during the *regel* makes it a reliable assumption.

On a normal day in the *Beit Hamikdash*, when only a handful of *shelamim* are brought, this assumption cannot be made, and the obligation of *merika u'shtifa* must be addressed directly.

### Chakhamim's View

The *chakhamim* disagree with Rabbi Tarfon: *ad z'man achila* — you wait until the time of eating. Some textual versions read *kol z'man achila*, meaning for as long as it remains within the permitted eating period, no kashering is required; it is only once the eating window has closed — once the absorbed meat would have become *notar* — that the obligation begins.

The *gemara* asks what this phrase means practically: *amar Rav Nachman amar Rabbah bar Avuha*, one waits out the full time of eating, and only afterward performs *merika u'shtifa*. The question arises whether it would be preferable to kasher the pot before the meat becomes *notar*, as a preventive measure. *Tosafot* addresses this, and his basic conclusion is that not only is there no obligation to kasher early, one should not do so — the Torah mandates this ritual specifically once the time of eating has expired.

The scriptural basis is derived by Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Abba bar Yossi ben Abba. The Torah places *u'morak v'shutaf* — the rinsing and the washing — in immediate proximity to *kol zachar bakohanim yochal osah* — all male *kohanim* shall eat of it. Since the rinsing is linked to the eating, the obligation applies *after* the eating period, not during it.

### Merika & Shtifa Procedure

The *mishna* describes the procedure itself: *merika k'merikat hakos u'shtifa k'shtifat hakos* — rinsing like the rinsing of a cup, and scrubbing like the scrubbing of a cup. Rashi understands *merika* as the interior washing and *shtifa* as the exterior. According to the *mishna* and the position attributed to Rabbi (Rebbi), both of these are done with cold water: *merika u'shtifa b'tzonen*.

### Hot Water Debate

A dispute in the *gemara* complicates this picture. Rebbi holds that *merika u'shtifa* are both performed with cold water. The *chakhamim* hold that *merika* is performed with hot water and *shtifa* with cold water.

The reasoning of the *chakhamim* is rooted in an analogy to kashering the vessels of non-Jews: since the pot has absorbed problematic taste, it must be kashered. There must be boiling water involved. Rebbi responds: *hagalah lo kamina* — I am not talking about the kashering itself, which is entirely taken for granted. What the Torah describes is an additional ritual performed *after* the kashering: *ki kamina l'merika u'shtifa d'basar hagalah*. On this reading, the two-step ritual described in the verse — interior rinsing and exterior rinsing — is a distinct religious requirement layered on top of the mandatory kashering, and both are performed with cold water.

The *chakhamim* counter that if two identical washings were intended, the Torah would have used the same verb twice, either *morak morak* or *shutaf shutaf*. The use of two different verbs — *u'morak v'shutaf* — signals that the two acts differ in nature, one being the *hagala* with hot water and the other being the ritual rinse with cold water. Rebbi replies that different verbs are used precisely to distinguish the interior washing from the exterior one — not to indicate a difference in temperature. Had the Torah written the same verb twice, one might have thought it meant two interior washings or two exterior washings. The variation in verbs conveys the distinction between inner and outer washing, while leaving the kashering itself unmentioned because it goes without saying.

*Tosafot* notes that this ritual, performed specifically in the *Beit Hamikdash*, became the model for the general practice of kashering. Accordingly, the *minhag* is to kasher with water, to fill the entire vessel, and to perform the rinsing in a manner that reflects the procedure described here — even where strictly speaking the *halacha* of *ta'am* absorption might not require it.

### Grill & Hagala

The *gemara* notably does not address the case of the grill and the spit in depth — the *mishna* states they require *hagala*, and the natural question is why they do not require *libun*, kashering through direct fire. This question is taken up in tractate Avodah Zarah. One of the answers offered there is that the vessel initially absorbed the *korban* taste while the meat was still *heter* — *heisera bala* — it absorbed permissible matter. When something absorbs taste while the material was still permitted, the stringency of *libun chamur* may not be required, and *hagala* suffices. This principle has practical relevance beyond the *Beit Hamikdash*: a frying pan used for *chametz* before Pesach absorbed the chametz when it was still *heter*, and there is accordingly a basis to say that *hagala* — or at minimum *libun kal*, heating until a paper tissue would singe upon contact — may suffice for Pesach kashering, as distinct from a vessel used for *treifus*. This is not a ruling, but the connection to the *mishna's* choice of *hagala* for the grill is worth noting.

## Mixed Korbanot Taste

### Mishna Overview

The second *mishna* of the daf shifts from the ritual of the vessel to a classic *kashrus* question: what happens when a pot has been used for one type of *korban* and is then used to cook another? The *mishna* addresses two scenarios: cooking *kodashim* together with *chullin* in the same vessel, and cooking *kodshei kodashim* together with *kodashim kalim* — specifically, a *chatas* and a *shelamim*.

### Taste Determination

The *mishna* establishes a rule of taste determination: *im yesh bahen benosein ta'am* — if there is detectable taste in the second thing cooked that derives from what was absorbed in the walls of the pot from the first. When such taste is present, the *mishna* infers that the weightier item was cooked first and its absorbed taste transferred into the lighter item cooked afterward. The *mishna* relies on tasting rather than numerical computation, though in practice later authorities resolved the question through the framework of *bitul b'shishim*, nullification in sixty.

### Status Transfer

When taste transfer is confirmed — *harei hakalim ne'echalin k'chamurim shebahen* — the lighter item must be eaten with the restrictions applicable to the weightier one. *Chullin* that absorbed the taste of a *shelamim* must be eaten within Jerusalem and within the appropriate time frame, as if it were *shelamim*. A *shelamim* that absorbed the taste of a *chatas* may only be eaten by male *kohanim* within the *Beit Hamikdash*, in the manner of a *chatas*. The principle extends across all relevant dimensions: where it may be eaten, by whom, and within what timeframe.

A subtler question concerns *tumah*. If an ordinary piece of meat has absorbed *shelamim* taste, is it treated as *shelamim* with respect to *tumah* as well? That is, does contact with something *tamei* invalidate it as it would invalidate actual *shelamim*? This question is addressed shortly in the *gemara*.

### No Merika & No Touch

The *mishna* then states two apparently counterintuitive rules: *ve'einam te'unim merika u'shtifa* — no *merika u'shtifa* is required for the pot — and *ve'einam poslim bemaga* — the food does not become invalid through contact with something *tamei*. These statements invite scrutiny. Reading the *mishna* at face value, one might think it is saying that even when the lighter item takes on the status of the weightier one, the pot has been effectively kashered by the cooking and no further ritual is required, and that the *tumah* laws of *kodashim* do not extend to this derived status. But the *gemara* complicates this reading through a *chisorei mechasra* — a textual emendation explaining that the *mishna* is missing key words.

The full reading is: *if there is taste* — the lighter item is treated as the weightier, the pot requires *merika u'shtifa*, and touching something *tamei* to it *does* invalidate it. *If there is no taste* — the lighter item is not treated as the weightier, the pot does not require *merika u'shtifa* on account of the weightier item, and there is no *tumah* problem. The *gemara* thus smooths the rough edges of the *mishna* and resolves both apparent surprises: the *tumah* question is straightforwardly answered, and the absence of *merika u'shtifa* applies only when there is no taste.

### Abaye & Rava

The *gemara* then poses a further question. Even granting the full reading, if a *shelamim* is cooked in a *chatas* pot and there is no taste, one understands that the pot has been effectively kashered of the *chatas* taste and no *merika u'shtifa* is required on *chatas* grounds. But the *shelamim* itself was just cooked in the pot — shouldn't *merika u'shtifa* now be required on *shelamim* grounds? Two Amoraim respond differently.

Abaye says: the *mishna*'s statement that no *merika u'shtifa* is required refers specifically to the *chatas* dimension. As a *chatas* pot, no *merika u'shtifa* is needed, because the *chatas* taste was neutralized. The pot has now become simply a *shelamim* pot, and a fresh deadline begins — one will need to address *merika u'shtifa* after the *shelamim* eating window closes.

### Rava & Rabbi Shimon

Rava offers a more elegant answer: *ha manni Rabbi Shimon hi* — the *mishna* follows the position of Rabbi Shimon, who holds that the specific ritual of *merika u'shtifa* only applies to a *chatas* and *kodshei kodashim*, not to *kodashim kalim*. Accordingly, once the cooking of the *shelamim* in the *chatas* pot has effectively constituted a *hagala* and removed the *chatas* taste, and since the *mishna* follows Rabbi Shimon, no *merika u'shtifa* is required for *shelamim* at all. The pot may still require the standard *hagala* at the appropriate time, but the distinctive ritual of *merika u'shtifa* does not apply.

### Two Scenarios

The *gemara* points out that Rava's reading explains why the *mishna* presented two distinct scenarios — *kodashim* cooked with *chullin*, and *kodshei kodashim* cooked with *kodashim kalim* — rather than just one. For Rava, the second scenario is necessary to convey the novel ruling that we follow Rabbi Shimon and that *shelamim* does not require *merika u'shtifa*.

For Abaye, however, both scenarios are also necessary — *tzericha*. Had the *mishna* only taught the *kodashim* and *chullin* case, one might have thought that it is specifically *chullin* that eradicates the *kodashim* absorption when there is no taste, because *chullin* is so categorically different — *delav minaiyhu* — that the contrast is stark enough to neutralize what came before. Conversely, had only the *kodshei kodashim* and *kodashim kalim* case been taught, one might have thought it is specifically *kodashim* that can neutralize other *kodashim*, because they share the same weighty category and can therefore absorb and override one another. Each scenario is needed to prevent a mistaken inference in the other direction.

## Absorption & Status

### Yikdash Principle

The *mishna* then introduces the broader principle of *yikdash*: *kol asher yiga* — anything that touches the flesh of a *chatas* becomes sanctified. The *gemara* notes that the Torah articulates this principle twice — once regarding the flesh of the *chatas* and once regarding the *mincha* — and from this repeated formulation a general rule is derived: when absorbed taste transfers from one *korban* to another item, the receiving item takes on the status of the source.

### Ta'am Ke'Ikkar

This is the principle of *ta'am ke'ikkar* — taste is equivalent to the thing itself. A drop of *neveilah* falling into a *cholent* renders the entire *cholent treif*. A drop of chicken broth falling into boiling milk creates *basar b'chalav*. In the domain of *kodashim*, the principle may operate even more stringently. In ordinary *kashrus* situations, the question is whether one has consumed the actual forbidden substance or merely its taste-equivalent — a question relevant to whether one has eaten a *kezayit* of *neveilah* or merely a tenth thereof. In *kodashim*, it is possible that the absorbed flavor confers the full identity of the source on the receiving item, not merely a proportional fraction.

### Absorption Required

The *gemara* is careful to limit the scope of *yikdash*: *yachol afilu shelo bala?* — perhaps mere touching, even without absorption, confers sanctified status? The answer is no. The verse specifies *bibsarah* — in its flesh — indicating that the transfer of status occurs only when there has been genuine absorption. The *chatas* must have penetrated into the *basar* of the receiving item. Reading *bibsarah* as referring to the flesh of the receiving item rather than the source, the *gemara* requires physical ingestion of the source material into the body of the recipient.

### Point of Contact

Once absorption is established as the operative mechanism, a further limitation follows: *yachol naga bemiktzas chatichah yiheye kulo pasul?* — if a *korban* touches only part of a piece of meat, does the entire piece become invalid? The verse emphasizes *yiga* — the point of contact — indicating that only the locus of absorption is affected. If one can cut away the area where absorption occurred, the remainder remains permissible: *chotech es makom shebalah* — cut away the place that absorbed, and the rest is fine. When the food is not being heated over a fire, absorption is localized; it does not spread through the entire piece. If it is being cooked over fire, the heat spreads the absorbed material throughout, but in the absence of fire, surgical removal of the affected area is sufficient.

### Flesh Only

The principle of *yikdash* applies only to the flesh of the *korban* — *bibsarah*, not *bigidin*, not *b'atzamot*, not *b'karnayim*, not *b'tlafin* — not sinews, bones, horns, or hooves. These parts carry no special halakhic status and are not capable of transferring taste in any practically relevant way. Only the flesh of the sacrificial animal is the vehicle for status transfer.

### Invalid Korban Transfer

The *gemara* now examines the flip side of *yikdash*: what happens when the source *korban* was itself invalid? The verse says *yikdash*, the receiving item becomes sanctified — *lihyot kamoah*, to take on the same status as the source. If the source was invalid, *im psulah hi tipaseil* — the receiving item also becomes invalid, because it now contains absorbed material from an invalid *korban*. If the source was valid but of a higher sanctity than the receiving item, *hi tocheil k'chamur shebah* — it must be eaten in the manner of the more stringent item.

Importantly, the mechanism here is physical, not the transitive transfer characteristic of *tumah*. In *tumah*, A makes B *tamei* and the *tumah* moves from A to B in diminished degree. Here the situation is different: little pieces of A have physically penetrated into B. If A was *pasul*, then B now contains fragments of *pasul* material within it, and that is why B cannot be eaten. B is not itself *tamei* in the *tumah* sense — it simply has embedded within it matter that may not be consumed.

### Positive Mitzvah Challenge

This framework generates a striking question. Suppose A is a *mincha* that became *tamei* through contact with something impure. It was absorbed into B, a valid *mincha*. Now 98% of B is entirely valid *kodesh* with a positive commandment to eat it. Why not invoke the principle of *aseh docheh lo ta'aseh* — a positive commandment overrides a negative prohibition? The positive *mitzvah* to eat the valid *mincha* is directly in tension with the prohibition against eating the *tamei* A-material embedded in B. Since there is no way to eat B without consuming the fragments of A within it, the conflict is irresolvable and direct. Should the *aseh* override the *lo ta'aseh*?

### Ein Aseh Docheh

Rava answers: *ein aseh docheh lo ta'aseh shebamikdash* — the principle that a positive commandment overrides a negative prohibition does not apply in the *Beit Hamikdash*. The proof is from the prohibition against breaking a bone of the *korban pesach*: *ve'etzem lo tishberu vo*. Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya rules that this prohibition applies whether or not the bone contains marrow. One might ask: if the bone has marrow in it and one needs to access it in order to eat the *korban*, why not say the positive commandment to eat the *korban pesach* overrides the prohibition of breaking the bone? From the fact that Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya rules categorically that breaking the bone is forbidden in all circumstances, the *gemara* infers that *aseh docheh lo ta'aseh* does not operate in the *Beit Hamikdash*.

*Tosafot* notes that the *pesach* case is also somewhat different structurally — the *lo ta'aseh* of breaking the bone is violated before the *aseh* of eating is fulfilled, and furthermore there exist workarounds (e.g., placing the bone near a fire to let it open without breaking). Here, by contrast, the conflict is truly simultaneous and unavoidable. Nevertheless, Rava's answer holds: even under these optimal conditions for the *aseh* to override, it does not do so in the context of *mikdash* law.

### Yikdash as Aseh

Rav Ashi offers an alternative answer that is conceptually distinct. *Yikdash aseh hu* — the Torah's instruction that the receiving item takes on the status of the source is itself a positive commandment. Therefore, eating B as if it were ordinary *mincha* does not merely conflict with the prohibition against eating *tamei* matter; it also conflicts with the positive commandment to treat B as if it were A. *Ve'ein aseh docheh lo ta'aseh ve'aseh* — a positive commandment cannot override a negative prohibition *and* another positive commandment simultaneously. Even were one to accept that *aseh docheh lo ta'aseh* applies in the *Beit Hamikdash*, it would not help here because the positive commandment of *yikdash* stands in the way as well.

## Vezos HaTorah

### Umbrella Principle

The *gemara* now confronts a structural question: the Torah explicitly states *yikdash* only in the context of the *chatas* and the *mincha*. How do we know that the principle of status transfer through absorption applies to all other *korbanot* as well? Shmuel in the name of Rabbi Elazar finds the answer in the verse at the end of *Parshat Tzav*: *vezos hatorah la'olah v'la'mincha v'la'chatas v'la'asham v'la'milu'im u'lezevach ha'shelamim* — this is the law of the *olah*, the *mincha*, the *chatas*, the *asham*, the *miluim*, and the *zevach ha'shelamim*. This verse gathers all the *korbanot* under a single legislative umbrella, and the *gemara* proceeds to derive from it that legal principles established in connection with any one of these offerings can be extended to all the others.

### Olah & Kli

From the *olah*: *mah olah te'unah keli af kol te'unah keli* — just as the *olah* requires a *kli* (vessel) for *shechita*, so too all other *korbanot* require a *kli*. The *gemara* quickly establishes that this cannot refer to the bowl in which blood is caught — that is already known from the *shalmei tzibur* brought at Mount Sinai, where the verse states *vayasem ba'aganot*. The reference must therefore be to the *shechita* knife itself.

### Kli Sharet Debate

A significant dispute between Rashi and *Tosafot* concerns what kind of *kli* the knife must be. Rashi holds that we are deriving that the *shechita* knife must be a *kli sharet*, a sanctified Temple vessel. *Tosafot* disagrees: the requirement is that the knife be a fashioned implement — *kli* in the sense of something man-made — as opposed to using a naturally occurring sharp edge, such as a reed. On *Tosafot's* reading, the knife must be crafted, but it need not be a consecrated *kli sharet*. This is a striking debate given that *shechita* does not require a *kohen* and the *shochet* does not even need to stand within the *Beit Hamikdash* — only the animal must be inside. Whether the knife must therefore be consecrated as a *kli sharet* or merely be a fashioned implement is the point of contention.

### Akedat Yitzchak

The source within the *olah* context for the *kli* requirement is derived from the *Akeidat Yitzchak*. The verse states *vayikach es ha'machelet* — and he took the knife. The word *machelet* appears only here in the Torah to describe a knife used for *shechita*, and the emphasis placed on the taking of the knife — rather than simply on the act of slaughtering — teaches that a specific implement is required. *V'hasam ola hu* — and there it was an *olah*, as the verse states *vaya'alehu l'ola tachas bno*, referring to the ram offered in Yitzchak's place.

There is something deeply charged about this derivation. The *gemara* is locating the paradigm for the *shechita* knife used in the *Beit Hamikdash* in the scene of the *Akeidah*. The word *machelet* itself — related to *achila*, eating — evokes something of the horror of that moment. At the same time, the *gemara* subtly shifts its proof-text: rather than deriving from the command given to Avraham regarding Yitzchak (*v'ha'alehu sham l'ola*), it derives from the ram that was ultimately offered in his place (*vaya'alehu l'ola tachas bno*). This may reflect a purposeful choice: the model for sacrificial *shechita* is grounded in the animal that was brought as the substitute, not in the near-sacrifice of Yitzchak himself. The association nonetheless brings to the surface the deeper theological resonance of animal sacrifice as a replacement for something more primal — the idea that the *mizbe'ach* was consecrated by the *Akeidah*, and that every subsequent *shechita* in the *Beit Hamikdash* carries within it the shadow of that founding moment.

### Mincha & Male Kohanim

From the *mincha*: *mah mincha eina ne'echeles ela l'zichrei kehuna af kol einan ne'echalin ela l'zichrei kehuna* — just as a *mincha* may only be eaten by male *kohanim*, so too all other *korbanot* listed may only be eaten by male *kohanim*.

The *gemara* questions the novelty of this derivation. For a *chatas* and an *asham*, the rule that only male *kohanim* may eat it is stated explicitly: *kol zachar bakohanim yochlenah*. For *shalmei tzibur*, the rule derives from the verse in *Parshat Korach* (*b'kodesh hakodashim tochalnenu kol zachar yochal oso*), which the *gemara* reads as teaching that *shalmei tzibur* are eaten only by male *kohanim*. If each offering has its own source, what new information does the *mincha* verse contribute?

The *gemara* answers: *tanna'ei hi* — it is a dispute among *tanna'im* as to which verse serves as the source. Some derive the rule regarding *shalmei tzibur* from *Parshat Korach*; others derive it here from *Parshat Tzav* via the *mincha*. The substantive *halacha* is undisputed; only the exegetical source differs.

### Chatas & Absorption

From the *chatas*: *mah chatas miskadeshes b'valu'a af kol miskadeshes b'valu'a* — just as a *chatas* confers its status upon something that absorbs it, so too any *korban* that is absorbed into another item causes that item to take on the status of the *korban*. This is the very principle that has been under discussion throughout the *sugya*, and the verse *vezos hatorah* is the mechanism by which it is extended beyond the *chatas* and *mincha*, where the Torah states it explicitly, to all the *korbanot* listed in that verse.

### Asham & Fetuses

From the *asham*: *mah asham ein shapir v'shilya kadosh bo af kol ein shapir v'shilya kadosh bo* — just as an *asham* does not cause a fetus or placenta to become sanctified, so too with all other *korbanot*. The *asham* is male, so it obviously never has a fetus. But the *gemara* uses its absence as a halakhic model: since no fetus ever acquired sanctified status by virtue of an *asham*, we derive that fetuses of other *korbanot* — female animals that may be pregnant — do not become sanctified as a result of the mother's consecration.

This derivation relies on a hermeneutic principle: *efshar mi'she'ee efshar* — one may derive a rule about a possible case from an impossible one. Even though the situation of a fetus in an *asham* is impossible by definition, the Torah's implicit treatment of the *asham* as having no sanctified fetus allows us to project that principle onto cases where the scenario is actually possible. The underlying substantive position is *vladot kodashim b'havayasan hen kedoshim* — fetuses of *kodashim* are sanctified only at birth, not in the womb. Accordingly, whatever the mother's status, the fetus does not independently acquire the ritual status of the *korban* while still in utero.

### Miluim & Notar

From the *miluim*: *mah miluim mostroseihem bisreifah ve'ein baalei chayim bemostroseihem* — the meat left over from the *miluim* offerings is burned, and yet living animals are not treated as *notar* when associated with those offerings. The *gemara* is addressing a conceptual question: if one consecrated an additional animal as a supplement or backup to a *korban* — or consecrated a replacement after the original was lost, and the replacement was ultimately offered while the original was subsequently found — one might think the surviving animal should be treated as *notar*, analogous to leftover meat, and therefore burned.

The derivation from *miluim* teaches otherwise. In the *miluim* context, the Torah speaks of leftover meat being burned but never addresses the case of a living backup animal — because the scenario did not arise. The implicit message is that *notar* as a category does not apply to living animals. This is again an application of *efshar mi'she'ee efshar*: a case that never arose in the *miluim* context implicitly teaches a general principle, and *af kol mostroseihem bisreifah ve'ein baalei chayim bemostroseihem* — leftover meat is burned throughout, but living animal supplements do not become *notar*.

### Shelamim & Piggul

From the *zevach ha'shelamim*: *mah shelamim mapgilin u'mitpagrin af kol mapgilin u'mitpagrin* — just as *shelamim* is subject to the laws of *piggul* and can cause *piggul*, so too all other *korbanot* are governed by the laws of *piggul*. The Torah's explicit treatment of *piggul* is in the context of *shelamim*, where an improper intent during the sacrificial service — intending to eat the meat beyond its designated time — renders the offering *piggul*, carrying the severe penalty of *karet*. Through the umbrella verse of *vezos hatorah*, this principle is extended: the concept of *piggul* — both as a disqualifying intent that one may have during the service and as the resulting invalid status of the offering — applies across all the *korbanot* enumerated.

With this final derivation, the *gemara* completes its survey of the *vezos hatorah* principle. The verse at the conclusion of *Parshat Tzav* does not merely serve as a poetic coda summarizing the *korbanot*. It is a substantive legal instrument, extending specific *halachot* known from individual *korbanot* — the *kli* requirement from *olah*, the restriction to male *kohanim* from *mincha*, absorption and status transfer from *chatas*, non-sanctification of fetuses from *asham*, inapplicability of *notar* to living animals from *miluim*, and *piggul* from *shelamim* — to the full range of sacrificial offerings.
