# Shabbat 54 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nursing Father

### Story Setup

The daf begins toward the bottom of 53b, with a *baraisa* introduced by *tanu rabbanan*: *ma'aseh b'echad she-meisah ishto v'henichah ben l'yanek v'lo hayah lo sechar yenikah litten* — a certain man whose wife died left behind a child who still needed to nurse, and he lacked the means to hire a wet nurse. *V'na'aseh lo neis v'niftachu lo dadin k'shnei dadei ishah v'heinik et b'no* — a miracle occurred, and he developed breasts like a woman's and nursed his son.

### Rav Yosef's View

Rav Yosef responds to this story with wonder: *bo re'eh kamah gadol adam zeh she-na'aseh lo neis kazeh* — come and see how great this person is, that such an extraordinary miracle occurred to transform the normal workings of nature on his behalf.

### Abaye's Counter

Abaye demurs sharply: *adarabbah, kamah garua adam zeh she-nishtanu lo sidrei bereishit* — on the contrary, how lowly is this person that the order of creation had to be altered for his sake.

### Theology Debate

This exchange opens a genuinely striking theological question. On one level, the debate turns on how one should regard obvious supernatural intervention in the present era. One view celebrates such a miracle as a mark of divine favor; the opposing view holds that in our time, God works within nature rather than by overriding it, and that a miracle of this magnitude — the transformation of a man's body across biological lines — is cause for concern rather than admiration. The issue may also be more personal: that a man's body underwent something akin to a female physiological change, which itself could be the source of Abaye's discomfort. The two framings — one broadly theological, one specific to this individual — need not be mutually exclusive.

### Earning Livelihood

Rav Yehudah draws his own conclusion from the story: *bo re'eh kamah kashin mezonotav shel adam she-nishtanu lo sidrei bereishit* — come and see how difficult it is for a person to earn a livelihood, such that rather than God arranging for this man to find work, the order of creation itself had to change so he could feed his child. The miracle, on this reading, is not a sign of greatness but a testament to the extraordinary difficulty of *parnasah*. Other passages in the *gemara* similarly acknowledge how grueling it can be for people to find sustenance, and this observation fits within a broader Talmudic awareness of the economic hardships faced even in relatively stable periods.

### Rav Nachman's Point

Rav Nachman adds a corroborating observation: *teida de-misrachei shineitav v'lo ivru mezonot* — one can see that miracles of other kinds occur, and yet *mezonot*, adequate sustenance, still does not come. Whatever miraculous things may happen in a person's life, the difficulty of *parnasah* remains. Easier, it seems, for other types of miracles to occur than for God to simply arrange someone's livelihood.

### Scientific Evidence

It is worth noting that modern science offers some context for the physiological dimension of this story. *Scientific American* has reported that males can indeed lactate, possessing all the necessary biological equipment, and that the relevant hormone release can sometimes be triggered by severe undernourishment. The *gemara* itself emphasizes that this man had no money and could not find work — conditions consistent with starvation. At least one ancient literary parallel appears in Tolstoy's *Anna Karenina*, where an Englishman aboard a ship nurses a child. The convergence of the Talmudic account with this scientific possibility does not resolve the theological debate, but it adds an interesting dimension to a story that might otherwise seem purely legendary.

## Disabled Woman

### Narrative Setup

A second *baraisa* follows: *ma'aseh b'adam echad shenasah isha gidemet* — a certain man married a woman who was missing an arm. *Velo hikkir bah ad yom motah* — and he did not come to know this about her until the day she died.

### Woman's Modesty

Rav's initial response is admiration for the woman: *bo re'ei kama tzenuah isha zu she-lo hikkir bah ba'alah* — see how modest this woman was, that her own husband never discovered her condition throughout their entire life together.

### Husband's Modesty

Rabbi Chiyya challenges this framing. *Zu drakah bekach* — this is simply her way; women in general incline toward modesty, and a woman with something to conceal has additional reason to be careful. The true *chiddush*, Rabbi Chiyya argues, lies elsewhere: *ella kama tzenua adam zeh she-lo hikkir b'ishto* — how modest is this husband, who never came to see his wife fully. He followed her lead, or perhaps he himself was sufficiently restrained in his own conduct that he simply never sought to see her completely.

### Marital Context

This *gemara* sits in some tension with other passages that discuss marital intimacy. There are *gemarot* elsewhere that suggest the biblical obligation of *onah* entails sleeping together without clothing — not merely the act of intimacy itself. Reconciling those teachings with the apparent praise here for a husband who never looked at his wife carefully enough to notice a missing arm requires careful attention to the range of Talmudic attitudes toward nakedness and modesty within marriage.

### Functional Achievement

One student offered a reading that the *gemara* itself may not foreground but is nonetheless worth considering: perhaps *shelo hikkir bah* carries a broader connotation — not only that he never perceived her physical condition, but that she managed her household so fully and competently with one arm that he never had occasion to notice anything wanting. On this reading, the story's praise is for the woman's remarkable capability as much as for either party's physical modesty. The *gemara*'s framing of *tzenuah* may not easily accommodate this interpretation, but it is a thought-provoking alternative lens.

## Animal Garments

### Rams & Levuvin

The *gemara* now returns to the *halachic* discussion of the *mishnah* and what animals may or may not wear on *Shabbat*. The *mishnah* states: *zacharim yotzin belevuvin* — rams may go out with *levuvin*.

### Levuvin Definition

*Mai levuvin?* — what are *levuvin*? Rav Huna says: *totari*, which Rashi explains as tying the rams together in pairs — *shemechabrim otam bekesher shenayim shenayim shello yivrachu* — so that they are paired off and cannot flee. The etymological support: *mah mashma dehai levuvin lishna dekaruvei hu* — where do we see that *levuvin* denotes bringing together? From the verse *libavtini achoti kallah* (Song of Songs), where *levuv* means to draw close. So the rams go out tied one to another.

### Wolf Protection

Ulla offers a different reading. *Levuvin* refers to a leather binding placed *kneged libam* — over the heart of the ram — *kedei shelo yiplu aleihem ze'evim* — so that wolves will not attack them. This is a protective covering at the chest.

### Why Rams Only

The *gemara* immediately asks: *ze'evim adikarim nafli, anekevot lo nafli?* — do wolves attack rams but not ewes? Several explanations are offered in turn. First: *mishum demasgei bereish idra* — the rams walk at the head of the flock and are the first target. But then why don't the wolves simply come around to the back? Next: *mishum deshmeini* — because the rams are fatter and more appealing. But surely there are fat ewes too? Finally, and apparently conclusively: *mishum dezakfi chotmaihu* — because the rams hold their heads high and look around as they walk, which Rashi understands as a kind of provocation to the wolves, who perceive the raised-head posture as a challenge.

### Copulation Control

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers a third reading of *levuvin*: *or shekoshrim lahem tachat zachrutam kedei shelo ya'alu al hanekevot* — a binding placed beneath the rams' genitals to prevent them from copulating with the ewes. This interpretation is supported by the parallel structure of the *mishnah*, which goes on to discuss bindings for the female sheep as well, all of which relate to copulation.

### Shchuzot Definition

The *mishnah* states that ewes may go out *shuchuzot*. *Mai shuchuzot?* — what does this mean? *She'ochzim ha'alyah shelahem lemala kedei sheya'alu aleihem zecharim* — one holds the fat tail upward so that the rams can mount them. So here, unlike the case of the rams, the binding is intended to *enable* copulation rather than prevent it. The *gemara* notes the structural parallelism: *reisha kedei shelo ya'alu al hanekevot, seifa kedei sheya'alu aleihem zecharim* — the first clause prevents copulation, the second facilitates it.

### Shchuzot Etymology

*Mai mashma dehai shuchuzot lishna degiluy hu?* — where does *shuchuzot* suggest exposure? The *gemara* cites the verse in Proverbs describing a woman *shit zonah unetzurat lev* — dressed as a prostitute and of a calculating heart. Somehow *shuchuzot* derives from this language of exposure, particularly sexual exposure.

### Kevulot Definition

The *mishnah* also permits ewes to go out *kevulot*. This is the opposite arrangement: *shemechablin alyah shelahem lemata kedei shelo ya'alu aleihem hazcharim* — one binds the fat tail down to prevent the rams from mounting. Sometimes the farmer wants copulation and sometimes not, depending on the season or breeding plans.

### Kevulot Etymology

*Mai mashma dehai kavul lishna dela avid peiri hu?* — where does *kavul* suggest non-productivity? The *gemara* turns to the story of Hiram, who helped Shlomo build the *Beit HaMikdash* and received certain cities in return. He was displeased: *ma hearim haele asher natata li achi?* — what are these cities you gave me, my brother? — and called the land *eretz kavul* (I Kings 9:13). The *gemara* explores what *kavul* means here.

Rav Huna suggests: *shehayu bah bnei adam shemechubalin bakesef uvazahav* — the inhabitants were so encumbered with gold and silver. Rava objects: if people were laden with wealth, why was Hiram displeased? Rav Huna explains: *keivan de'atirei umifankei lo avdei avidta* — since they were so rich and pampered, they refused to work or be conscripted for the king's projects.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers a different account: *eretz chumtin hayeta* — it was cracked, muddy land into which one's legs would sink up to the ankle. *Ve'amri inshi ara mechavla* — and people would say it was a land of *kavul*, *delo avda peiri* — that bore no fruit. This idiom of *eretz kavul dело avda peiri* — unfruitful land — is then the connection: binding the ewe's fat tail down renders her, so to speak, unfruitful, preventing the rams from ascending.

### Conceptual Analysis

At this point it is worth pausing over the conceptual difficulty these cases present. The discussion throughout this chapter has generally operated on a distinction between what is protective of the animal — and therefore not a burden — and what serves the owner's interests through the animal's body, which is more like a *masoi*, a load. Yet here, according to the *tanna kamma*, a ram going out with a binding to prevent copulation, or a ewe going out with her tail tied up or down to enable or prevent copulation, is permitted. None of this protects the animal's health or prevents it from running away; it serves entirely the economic or breeding interests of the owner.

One might try to frame this as owner interest without active use of the animal — the owner is not riding it or carrying goods on it, merely arranging its reproductive capacity. But even ornamentation, which is similarly passive, is treated as a *masoi* in other contexts. The more coherent resolution may be that the operative distinction is not function at all, but *norm*: what is regularly and customarily worn by animals of a given type at a given time is not a burden, and what exceeds that norm is. Rabbi Yossi, who disagrees and forbids all of these, at least makes the analysis consistent: if none of it is protective in the immediate physical sense, none of it should be permitted.

### Kvunot Definition

The *mishnah* permits ewes to go out *kvunot* as well. *Mai kvunot? Shemechabnin otam lemila* — one covers the lamb's wool from birth onward to protect it. The *gemara* connects this to the appearance of *se'eis*, one of the *tzara'at* markings described as *ketzemer lavan* — like white wool. Rav Bibi bar Abaye explains: *ketzemer naki ben yomo she-mechabnin oso le-mila* — like the clean wool of a one-day-old lamb that has been kept covered from birth in order to protect and preserve the quality of its fleece.

This too is clearly in the owner's financial interest — protecting the wool's value — rather than the animal's health. Rabbi Yossi's exception permitting *kvunot* while forbidding everything else is perhaps the most difficult element of his position to understand, since protecting wool for commercial purposes seems at least as removed from the animal's welfare as preventing copulation. One possible explanation is that because the covering is there from the moment of birth, it is treated as simply part of the animal's natural state — as close to being clothing as anything can be.

## Goat Udders

### Goat Binding

The *mishnah* states: *ha-izim yotzot tzerurot* — goats may go out with their udders bound. Rabbi Yossi disagrees and holds that this is prohibited, except for the *kvunot* case of wool protection.

### Dry vs. Catch

Rabbi Yehudah draws a distinction within the permitted category: binding the udder *le-yavesh* — to dry up the milk — is permitted, while binding it *le-chalav* — to catch the milk — is not. The practical difference between these may be that a binding tight enough to suppress lactation will not slip off, while one designed to catch milk might be loose and could fall, creating a carrying concern. Alternatively, suppressing the milk may be seen as serving the animal — if it is no longer nursing, continued lactation is painful — whereas catching the milk serves only the owner's interest in collecting it.

### Rav's Ruling

Rav rules in accordance with Rabbi Yehudah's distinction: *le-yavesh mutar, lo le-chalav* — binding to dry up the milk is permitted; binding to catch it is not.

### Shmuel's Ruling

Shmuel rules in accordance with Rabbi Yossi: both are forbidden, with the sole exception of the wool protection case.

### Baraita Problem

There is a *baraisa* that reflects Rabbi Yehudah's position directly: *izim yotzot tzerurot le-yavesh aval lo le-chalav* — goats may go out with bound udders to dry up the milk but not to catch it. *Mishum Rabbi Yehudah ben Beseira amru kach halacha* — in principle, this is the law.

### Ben Beseira

However, Rabbi Yehudah ben Beseira adds a crucial practical qualification: *aval mi mafis?* — but who can distinguish? If one person goes out with their goat's udder bound to suppress lactation and another goes out with it bound to catch milk, an observer cannot tell the difference. Since people will not be able to distinguish and will conclude that it is always permitted, we must forbid the entire practice. *Umitoch she-ein mekirin echad ze ve-echad ze assur* — because they cannot be told apart, both are forbidden.

### Final Ruling

*Amar Shmuel, ve-amri lah amar Rav Yehudah amar Shmuel, halacha ke-Rabbi Yehuda ben Beseira* — Shmuel rules in accordance with Rabbi Yehudah ben Beseira: the entire practice is forbidden. A competing tradition (*ki asa Ravin amar Rabbi Yehudah halacha ke-tanna kamma*) holds that the *tanna kamma*, who permits all the bindings, is the operative ruling. The *gemara* does not resolve the dispute at length, but the tendency of the discussion seems to favor the stricter position of Rabbi Yehudah ben Beseira, which Shmuel endorses.

## Prohibited Items

### Metulteles

The *mishnah* now turns to items that animals may *not* wear on *Shabbat*. *Lo yetzei hagamal bemetulteles* — the camel may not go out with a *metulteles*. Rashi explains the *metulteles* as a kind of cushion or cloth placed beneath the camel's tail to protect it from the chafing of the strap that holds a load in place. Because its entire purpose is functional during the carrying of burdens, it is itself considered a burden when worn.

### Double-Bound OK

A *baraita* clarifies: *lo yetzei hagamal bemetulteles hakeshurah lo biznavo* — it may not go out with the *metulteles* bound only to the tail. *Aval yotzei hu bemetulteles hakeshurah biznavo uvechotarto* — but it may go out with a *metulteles* bound both at the tail and at the hump. Rashi reads the underlying concern here not as the object being a burden in the conceptual sense, but as a concern that it will fall off — and a loose object that falls may then be carried. If it is bound in two places, at the tail and the hump, it will not slip off, and therefore it is permitted.

### Placenta Binding

Rabbah bar Rav Huna adds a related ruling: *yotzei gamal bemetulteles hakeshurah lo beshalyata* — the camel may also go out with the *metulteles* bound to its *shalyata*, its placenta or afterbirth. Apparently animals do not always immediately expel the afterbirth, and because the connection to the afterbirth makes it painful for the camel to try to dislodge the covering, there is no risk that it will fall off and need to be carried. Here too Rashi's logic is that stability — certainty that the object will remain on the animal — is the decisive factor.

### Akud

The *mishnah* also forbids the camel from going out *akud* or *ragul*. Rav Yehudah explains: *akud* means *akeidas yad varegel* — binding a foreleg to a hindleg, one side to the other, *ke-Yitzchak ben Avraham* — as Yitzchak was bound at the *Akeidah*.

### Ragul

*Ragul* means *shelo yachof yado al gabei tzvaro v'yikshor* — one takes a single leg, bends it upward, and ties the ankle toward the top of the thigh, so the animal walks on three legs and cannot run. Presumably this was done to prevent the animal from escaping, but it is considered a burden — perhaps because it is painful, or because it is seen as an extreme and unnecessary measure as compared to a simple tether.

### Textual Fix

The *gemara* raises a challenge from a *baraisa* that seems to define *akud* as binding the two forelegs together and the two hindlegs together separately — a distinct configuration from the hand-and-foot binding Rav Yehudah describes. The *gemara* resolves this by identifying a different *tanna* whose position Rav Yehudah follows: *akud akeidas yad v'regel ke-Yitzchak ben Avraham, ragul shelo ya'akof yado al gabei tzvaro v'yikshor* — one hand bound to one foot, and *ragul* as the single leg bent up. This *tanna* does not include the hands-together-feet-together configuration at all.

### Permitted vs. Forbidden

The *Shulchan Aruch* rules accordingly. The practical upshot is that binding the two forelegs together and the two hindlegs together separately — a more symmetrical and perhaps less painful restraint — is considered a normal and acceptable method of preventing the animal from wandering, and does not constitute a burden. Binding one hand to one foot (*akud*) or bending a single leg up (*ragul*) both exceed this norm, and are therefore forbidden on *Shabbat*.

## Commerce & Shatnez

### Mishnah Rules

The *mishnah* continues: *lo yikshor gemalim zeh bezeh veyimshoch* — do not tie camels together in a line and walk them. However, *aval machnis havalim letoch yado veyimshoch* — one may hold multiple separate ropes in hand and lead them that way, as long as they are not tied to one another. And: *uvilvad shelo yichroch* — one must not wind the ropes around one's hand.

### Market Appearance

*Mai ta'ama?* — what is the reason for the prohibition against walking camels in a line? *Mishum d'meichzi k'man d'azil l'chinga* — it looks like one is heading to the market. Walking a line of camels in a row is a quintessentially commercial, weekday activity. This is less a concern about *marris ayin* in the technical sense of others suspecting a violation, and more a direct *Shabbat* concern: the atmosphere and character of *Shabbat* is undermined when one engages in activities that look and feel like commerce. It is not a *Shabbat*-appropriate experience to create in the public space.

### Shatnez Problem

As for the prohibition on wrapping the ropes around one's hand: Rav Ashi explains that the concern is *kilayim de-chavalim* — the ropes may be a mixture of wool and linen, and wrapping and knotting them together could create a *shaatnez* problem.

### Baraita Objection

The *gemara* challenges this: *ha-tokef tekifa achat eino chibur* — a *baraita* teaches that even a single stitch joining wool and linen garments is not sufficient to constitute *shaatnez*. If one stitch doesn't create *shaatnez*, how does wrapping ropes around one's hand do so?

### Resolution

The *gemara* maintains that the concern is indeed *kilayim de-chavalim*, but refines the formulation: *uvilvad shelo yichroch veyikshor* — one must not *wrap and tie* the ropes together. If one twists them together and then knots them, one has created at minimum a category of rabbinic *shaatnez*, even if the biblical prohibition might require something more elaborate such as intertwining the individual strands. The *gemara* treats this level of wrapping-and-knotting as sufficient to require avoidance, without specifying whether it is biblical or rabbinic.

### Camel Walking

The question arises — prompted in the course of the discussion — how one can walk a camel on *Shabbat* at all while holding a rope or lead, without this constituting *carrying*. The answer appears to follow from the earlier analysis: once an item is considered a non-burden for the animal — because it is normal and customary for the animal to wear it — then the owner holding the lead attached to that item is not carrying. An analogy: if someone is wearing a large hat and another person holds the brim to keep it from blowing off, that second person is not *carrying* the hat, because the hat is on someone's body. The collar and lead, being acceptable items for the animal to bear, are extensions of the animal itself rather than independent objects being carried.

### Rope Distance

*Amar Shmuel: uvilvad shelo yetze chevel mi-tachat yado tefach* — one must ensure that the rope does not extend more than a *tefach* beyond one's hand at the far end (away from the camel). Rashi explains that if a length of rope hangs beyond the hand, it will appear as though one is simply holding a piece of rope rather than leading an animal, and may look like carrying.

### Tefach Dispute

The school of Shmuel (*tana de-bei Shmuel*) teaches that the threshold is actually two *tefachim*, not one. Abaye resolves the discrepancy: Shmuel's ruling of one *tefach* is a practical guidance for actual conduct — advising people to be more conservative in practice — while the theoretical limit is indeed two *tefachim*. A second rule applies to the rope between the hand and the camel: *uvilvad sheyagbi'ah min hakarka tefach* — it must be held at least a *tefach* above the ground. If the lead drags along the ground, it no longer looks like one is walking an animal; it looks like one is dragging a rope, which would appear to be carrying.

## Species-Specific

### Donkey

*Ein chamor yotzei bemardaat bizman she'einah keshurah lo* — the donkey may not go out with a saddle blanket if it was not tied to it before *Shabbat*. *Velo bezug af al pi shehu pakuk* — and not with a bell, even if the bell has been stopped so that it makes no sound. *Velo besulam shebetzavaro* — and not with a ladder-like device placed along the length of its neck. *Velo biretzuah sheberaglav* — and not with a strap on its legs.

### Bell Reason

*Mishum demichzei keman de'azal lechinga* — the bell is prohibited because even a stopped, silent bell gives the appearance that the donkey is being taken to market. This is the same concern as with the line of camels: it is a visual marker of commercial weekday activity that undermines the character of *Shabbat*. Tosafot notes that one might instead have argued that the bell is simply an ornament that does not protect the animal and is therefore a *masoi*. But Tosafot resolves this by suggesting that the bell is woven into the halter itself, and the halter as a whole is not a burden — the prohibition is therefore specifically because it *looks* like one is going to market, not because the bell itself is a carried object.

### Ladder Purpose

Regarding the ladder-like device on the neck — *sulum shebetzavaro* — Rav Huna explains: *leheicha d'it leh maka delo hadar hayech bah* — if the animal has a wound on its neck, this device prevents it from turning its head and scraping at the wound. Despite this clearly medical purpose, it is still considered a burden. As with several of the items in this *mishnah*, items applied for health purposes that are nonetheless external to the animal's normal state of dress appear to be treated as *masoi*.

### Leg Strap

The strap placed on the donkey's legs, *retzuah sheberaglav*, is used to correct a gait problem — Rashi explains it addresses something akin to knock-knees — so that the animal walks properly and does not worsen its condition. Again, a therapeutic purpose, and again prohibited.

### Chickens

*V'ein hatarnagolim yotzin bechutin* — chickens may not go out with identifying strings. *D'avdi leh simana ki heichi d'lo lichlefu* — these strings are placed on them so that owners can identify whose chickens are whose, preventing them from being confused or switched. And *velo biretzuah sheberagleihem* — nor may they go out with straps on their legs. *D'avdi leh ki heichi d'lo livru mani* — the straps are placed on chicken's legs to limit the range of their kicking and thereby prevent them from breaking things around the house. This is clearly for the protection of the owner's property rather than the animal, and it is not permitted.

### Rams

*V'ein hazcharim yotzin ba'agalah shetachas ha'alyah* — rams may not go out with little wagons beneath their fat tails. *Ki heichi d'lo lachmatan ayata* — so that the fat tail should not scrape along the ground and become injured. This is arguably a protective purpose, but the wagon is nonetheless forbidden — perhaps because it is a distinctly external object, perhaps because it exceeds any ordinary norm for what a ram would wear.

### Sheep

*V'ein harachelim yotzot chanunot* — female sheep may not go out *chanunot*. The *gemara* will explore what this means through several competing explanations.

### Chanunot Options

The first attempt at a definition comes from Rav Acha bar Ulla, sitting before Rav Chisda: *beshaa shegozezin otah, tomnim lah azak beshemen u'manichin lah al padachtah kedei shelo titztanen* — when the sheep is shorn, one takes a cloth soaked in oil and places it on the animal's forehead so that it does not catch a chill. Rav Chisda rejects this: *im ken asita mar ukva* — you have turned the sheep into Mar Ukva. That is, putting an oil compress on the forehead of a sheep is the kind of refined treatment one gives to very distinguished people, not animals.

The second attempt comes from Rav Pappa bar Shmuel: *beshaa shekarat leiled, tomnin lah shnei azakim shel shemen u'manichin lah echad al padekha v'echad al recham kedei shetitchamem* — during labor, one places two oil-soaked cloths, one on the ewe's forehead and one on her womb, to provide warmth. Rav Nachman objects similarly: *amar lei im kein asita yalta* — now you have made the sheep like Yalta (Rav Nachman's wife). The treatment you describe is what one does for a woman in childbirth, not for a ewe.

### Chanun Tree

Rav Huna offers the authoritative explanation: *eitz echad yesh b'charkhei hayam v'chanun shemo* — there is a tree found in the islands called *chanun*, from which *chanunot* takes its name. *Umevi'im kisam u'manichin lah b'chotmah kedei shetit'atesh v'yiplu darnei rasha* — one takes a splinter from this tree and inserts it into the animal's nostrils so that it sneezes, dislodging the lice or parasites living in its head.

### Female Only

The *gemara* asks: *i hachi, zecharim nami* — if this is the purpose, why is it only for the female sheep? *Keivan d'menagchi zecharim bahadadi, mimeila nafli* — because the rams butt their heads against one another, the impact naturally dislodges the parasites. Female sheep, who do not engage in this behavior, require the *chanun* splinter instead. Still, they may not go out with it on *Shabbat*.

### Compassion View

Shimon the *Nazir* offers an alternative identification, naming a different type of wood — *kisam d'ritma*, a splinter from the broom tree — rather than the *chanun* tree. The *gemara* notes the terminological difficulty this creates: according to Rav Huna, the term *chanunot* is readily explained as derived from the *chanun* tree. But according to the other Rabbis who identify a different wood, what does *chanunot* mean? The answer: *d'avidnan lehu milta d'merachminan aleihu* — because one does something out of compassion, from the root *chanun*, to relieve the animal's discomfort whether during shearing or during birth. The name reflects the act of *chanan*, showing mercy, even if the tree involved is not the *chanun* tree.

### Calf

*V'ein ha'eigel yotzei b'gimon* — the calf may not go out with a *gimon*. Rav Huna explains: *bar nira* — it is a small training yoke placed on a young calf to accustom it to bearing a yoke while it is still pliable, since animals that grow up without yokes are difficult or impossible to train later in life.

### Gimon Etymology

*Mai mashma d'hai gimon lishna d'michaf?* — where does the word *gimon* suggest bending down? *D'chtiv halachof k'agmon rosho* (Isaiah 58:5) — will he bow his head like an *agmon*? Here the verse uses *agmon* to mean something that bends, which Ibn Ezra understands as a fishhook; others translate it as bulrush. In any case, *gimon* derives from the idea of bending the neck down under a yoke. The *gimon* is extraneous to the calf in its natural state and is therefore a burden.

### Cow

*V'lo parah b'or hakupar* — the cow may not go out with hedgehog leather on its udder. *D'avdi lah ki heichi d'lo lamtziuha yali* — this leather is placed over the udder to prevent rodents or other small animals from nursing from the cow. Unlike the earlier cases of binding to suppress or catch milk, which at least some opinions permitted, this device is seen as entirely serving the owner's protective interest rather than anything relating to the animal itself, and it is forbidden.

### Strap Between

*V'lo b'retzu'ah shebein karneiha* — and not with a strap between its horns. An earlier *gemara* records a dispute: Rav holds that the strap is forbidden whether it is placed *l'noy* — for ornamentation — or *l'shamer* — to protect the cow. Even a protective purpose does not render it acceptable; for a cow, which requires no elaborate protection, such a strap is excessive. Shmuel, by contrast, holds: *l'noy assur, l'shamer mutar* — ornamentation is a burden, but a strap placed there for the cow's protection is permitted.

## Elazar's Cow

### Neighbor's Cow

*Parato shel Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya hayata yotzah biretzuah shebein karneha* — the cow of Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah would go out with a strap between its horns, and the Sages were not pleased.

### Attribution

The *gemara* immediately asks: *chada para havi lei?* — did he have only one cow? Rav Yehudah in the name of Rav teaches that Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah would tithe twelve thousand calves from his herd every year. Clearly he was not a one-cow owner.

### Neighbor's Ownership

*Lo shelo hayeta* — the *baraisa* teaches that it was not actually his cow. *Ella she'shchenato hayeta* — it was his neighbor's cow.

### Protest Duty

*Umitoch shelo mikha bah, nikret al shemo* — because he did not protest or prevent his neighbor from doing this, it is called his. He bore responsibility for conduct within his sphere of influence and failed to exercise it, and so the transgression is attributed to him.

### Household

This leads to a sweeping principle taught by Rav, Rabbi Chanina, Rabbi Yochanan, and Rav Chaviva — with a parenthetical noting that throughout *Seder Moed*, this group is sometimes cited with Rabbi Yonatan in place of Rabbi Yochanan — *kol mi she'efshar limkhot le'anshey beito v'lo mikha, nitpas al anshey beito*: anyone who could have prevented the people of his household from sinning and did not protest is held accountable for their sins.

### Scope Expansion

The principle extends outward: *be'anshey iro* — if one could have stopped the people of one's city and did not, one is accountable for the city. *V'kol ha'olam kullo* — and if one could have stopped the entire world from sinning, one bears responsibility for the world's transgressions as well.

### Authority

*Amar Pappa: vehani devei reish galuta nitpasu al kulei alma* — Rav Pappa applies this principle directly: the household of the *Reish Galuta*, the Exilarch, wielded sufficient power and authority over the Jewish community that when anyone in the community did wrong, they bore a particular responsibility — and if they failed to intervene, they were held accountable for the sins of the entire world. The teaching ends here, and the *gemara* will continue in this vein in the next daf.
