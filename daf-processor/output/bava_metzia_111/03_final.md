# Bava Metzia 111 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hiring via Agent

### Opening & Bal Talin

The Gemara on this daf continues the theme of *bal talin* — the prohibition against withholding wages from workers — which was introduced in the previous Mishnah. The discussion ranges from technical loopholes in liability, to the precise timing of wage obligations, to the multiple Torah prohibitions simultaneously violated by a delinquent employer, and ultimately to the scope of these obligations across different categories of workers, animals, and tools.

### Indirect Hiring

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ צֵא שְׂכוֹר לִי פּוֹעֲלִים – שְׁנֵיהֶן אֵין עוֹבְרִין מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תָּלִין״. זֶה, לְפִי שֶׁלֹּא שְׂכָרָן,
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** Concerning one **who says to another: Go out** and **hire workers for me, both of them do not violate the prohibition of delaying** payment of wages if they fail to pay immediately. **This** one, the employer, is exempt **because he did not hire them** himself, and strictly speaking they are not his hired workers.

The Gemara opens by citing a Baraita: *ha'omer l'chaveiro tzei sechor li po'alim* — if one instructs a friend to go hire workers on his behalf, *shneihem ein ovrim mishum bal talin*, neither of them has violated *bal talin*. The reasoning is twofold: the *ba'al habayit* did not himself hire the workers (*zeh lefi shelo secharan*), and the agent who did hire them is not liable because the work was not performed on his behalf (*vezeh lefi she'ein pe'ulato etzlo*). The hired workers are, so to speak, disconnected from both parties with respect to the formal obligation. The logic is structurally similar to the principle of agency in other areas of Halakha — including *melacha* performed on *Shabbos* through a proxy — where the fragmentation of a transaction between two parties creates a significant loophole.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזֶה, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין פְּעוּלָּתוֹ אֶצְלוֹ. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ שְׂכַרְכֶם עָלַי – שְׂכָרוֹ עָלָיו הוּא, דְּתַנְיָא: הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַפּוֹעֵל לַעֲשׂוֹת בְּשֶׁלּוֹ וְהֶרְאָהוּ בְּשֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ שְׂכָרוֹ מִשָּׁלֵם, וְחוֹזֵר וְנוֹטֵל מִבַּעַל הַבַּיִת מַה שֶּׁהֶהֱנָה אוֹתוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **And that** one, the middleman, is exempt **because his work is not** performed **for him.** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of this case? **If** the middleman **said to them: Your wages** are incumbent **upon me, his wages are** indeed **upon him,** as the one who hired the workers bears full responsibility. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who hires a laborer to perform** work **in his own** field, **and** the employer inadvertently **showed** the laborer a field **belonging to another** in which he should work, the employer **must give** the laborer **his full wages; and** in addition, the employer **goes back and takes from the owner** of the field in which he worked the value of **the benefit** that owner **received from** the laborer. The employer is entitled to claim from the owner of the field the profit that owner gained from the work, but not the entire wages of the laborer. This indicates that one who says: Your wage is incumbent upon me, is responsible for the arrangement.

The Gemara then clarifies what case the Baraita actually addresses. If the agent had said to the workers *scharchem alai* — that he personally would be paying their wages — then the liability would rest on him. This is established from another Baraita: if one hires a worker ostensibly for his own field and then directs them to work in someone else's field, he must pay the worker his full wages (*nosein lo scharo misheleim*) and then go claim reimbursement from the actual *ba'al habayit* for the benefit that person received — *mah shehe'enato*. Notably, this implies a legal claim against that third party even without any prior agreement, which raises the interesting question of whether a person can truly be bound to pay for a benefit conferred on them without their consent — a principle resembling *yored* into another's property.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ: שְׂכַרְכֶם עַל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **No,** it is **necessary** to state this *halakha* **where** the middleman **said to them:** The obligation to pay **your wages** is incumbent **upon the employer,** in which case they share responsibility for the payment and neither violates the prohibition.

The Baraita the Gemara actually needs to teach, however, is the case where the agent said explicitly to the workers: *scharchem al ba'al habayit* — your wages are the obligation of the householder. Even in this case, the *aveira* of *bal talin* evaporates, because the person who structured the payment arrangement is not the person who actually owes the wages. One might ask: since *shlucho shel adam kimo'to* — an agent's actions are legally attributed to the principal — why does this loophole work at all? It is a genuine question, and one the Rishonim presumably address in greater depth. The answer lies in the specificity of the verse underlying *bal talin*: *lo talin pe'ulat sachir itcha ad boker* — "you shall not withhold the wages of a hired worker who is with you." The obligation is person-specific, requiring both that one hired the worker and that the work was done for one's benefit.

### Practical Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְהוּדָה בַּר מָרִימָר אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְשַׁמָּעֵיהּ: זִיל אֱגוֹר לִי פּוֹעֲלִים, וְאֵימָא לְהוּ: שְׂכַרְכֶם עַל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת, מָרִימָר וּמַר זוּטְרָא אָגְרִי לַהֲדָדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Yehuda bar Mareimar** would **say to his attendant: Go hire workers for me and say to them: Your wages are upon the employer.** Yehuda bar Mareimar instructed the attendant to do this in order to avoid violating the prohibition of delaying payment of wages. **Mareimar and Mar Zutra would hire** workers **for each other** for the same reason.

The Gemara records that Yehuda bar Mareimar would instruct his servant (*shamash*) — *zil ager li po'alim* — to go hire workers for him, and would direct the servant to tell the workers that their wages were the liability of the *ba'al habayit*. Similarly, Mareimar and Mar Zutra would hire workers for each other, each acting as the other's agent, thereby using this loophole to avoid personal exposure to the *aveira* of *bal talin*. This is not presented as a problematic evasion — it is straightforwardly a legitimate legal structure employed by major *Amoraim* who wished to be scrupulous in avoiding even potential violations. Of course, none of this eliminates the underlying financial obligation to pay workers promptly and in full; it addresses only the specific religious prohibition of *bal talin*.

## Wage Payment Timing

### Marketplace Workers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: הָנֵי שׁוּקָאֵי דְּסוּרָא לָא עָבְרִי מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תָּלִין״, מִידָּע יָדְעִי דְּעַל יוֹמָא דְשׁוּקָא סְמִיכִי, אֲבָל מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תְּשַׁהֶא״ וַדַּאי עוֹבֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Huna said: Those marketplace workers of Sura do not violate the prohibition** by Torah law **of delaying** payment of wages, in the event that they do not pay their employees immediately. This is because everyone **knows that they rely on the market day** to earn their money, and the employees are aware that they will not be paid on the same day that they worked. **But** he **certainly violates the prohibition** by rabbinic law **of: Do not delay,** if he withholds payment any later than the market day.

Rabba bar Rav Huna states that the marketplace workers of Sura (*hanei shokai deSura*) do not violate *bal talin* — *lo avrei mishum bal talin* — because *meyedai de'al yoma deshuka smichei*: the workers understand that payment will only come on market days, Mondays and Thursdays, when their employers actually have cash on hand from selling their wares. Since the workers enter the arrangement with this understanding, the normal timing of the *mitzvah* is adjusted to fit the acknowledged terms of employment. However, Rabba bar Rav Huna adds, *aval mishum bal tishe'ha vadai over* — if the employer then withholds wages beyond what is necessary, he does violate the broader prohibition against prolonged withholding, whether that is understood as a rabbinic enactment or derived from the verse in Mishlei. Concretely, if the worker is paid on Thursday for work done the previous Monday, that is permissible given the understood arrangement; but if the employer had funds on Monday and chose not to pay until Thursday, that would constitute *bal tishe'ha*.

### Rav vs Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׂכִיר שָׁעוֹת גּוֹבֶה כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה וְכׇל הַיּוֹם. אָמַר רַב: שְׂכִיר שָׁעוֹת דְּיוֹם – גּוֹבֶה כׇּל הַיּוֹם, שְׂכִיר שָׁעוֹת דְּלַיְלָה – גּוֹבֶה כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: שְׂכִיר שָׁעוֹת דְּיוֹם – גּוֹבֶה כׇּל הַיּוֹם, וּשְׂכִיר שָׁעוֹת דְּלַיְלָה – גּוֹבֶה כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה וְכׇל הַיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **an hourly laborer collects** his wages **all night and all day. Rav says: An hourly laborer** who worked **by day collects** his wages **all** that **day,** while **an hourly laborer** who worked **by night collects** his wages **all** that **night. And Shmuel says: An hourly laborer** who worked **by day** indeed **collects** his wages **all** that **day, but an hourly laborer by night collects** his wages **all** that **night and all** the following **day.**

The Mishnah's phrase *sachir sha'ot goveh kol halaila vechol hayom* — an hourly worker may collect all night and all day — generates a dispute between Rav and Shmuel. Rav holds: *sachir sha'ot deyom goveh kol hayom* — a daytime hourly worker's wages are collectable throughout the day, i.e., until sunset; *sachir sha'ot delaila goveh kol halaila* — a nighttime hourly worker's wages are collectable throughout the night, i.e., until sunrise. Shmuel agrees regarding the daytime worker — *sachir sha'ot deyom goveh kol hayom* — but holds that a nighttime hourly worker may collect *kol halaila vechol hayom*, throughout the night and the entire following day, until the next sunset.

The question is pointed: why should a nighttime worker be entitled to a longer window of payment under Shmuel's view? Shmuel's position appears to extend the employer's deadline to pay — which might seem more lenient toward the employer — but the Gemara frames this as an interpretation of the relevant *psukim* and how they are applied, rather than as a straightforwardly intuitive rule. One practical rationale might be that one cannot reasonably be expected to collect wages in the middle of the night, or that an employer cannot be disturbed at that hour, so the window effectively runs until the end of business hours the following day.

### Rav's Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: שְׂכִיר שָׁעוֹת גּוֹבֶה כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה וְכׇל הַיּוֹם, תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב! אָמַר לְךָ רַב: לִצְדָדִין קָתָנֵי. שְׂכִיר שָׁעוֹת דְּיוֹם – גּוֹבֶה כׇּל הַיּוֹם, שְׂכִיר שָׁעוֹת דְּלַיְלָה – גּוֹבֶה כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: **An hourly laborer collects** his wages **all night and all day.** This is apparently **a conclusive refutation of Rav.** The Gemara answers: **Rav** could have **said to you** that **he teaches** the mishna **disjunctively** in the following manner: **An hourly laborer by day collects** his wages **all day,** while **an hourly laborer by night collects** his wages **all night.**

The Mishnah's language appears to contradict Rav, since the phrase *goveh kol halaila vechol hayom* suggests that a single worker can collect both all night and all day. Rav responds: *letzdadin katani* — the Mishnah speaks to each case separately. The daytime worker collects throughout the day, and the nighttime worker collects throughout the night; the Mishnah is not saying the same worker collects across both periods.

### Extended Terms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הָיָה שְׂכִיר שַׁבָּת, שְׂכִיר חֹדֶשׁ, שְׂכִיר שָׁנָה, שְׂכִיר שָׁבוּעַ, יוֹצֵא בַּיּוֹם – גּוֹבֶה כׇּל הַיּוֹם. יוֹצֵא בַּלַּיְלָה – גּוֹבֶה כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה וְכׇל הַיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: If **he was a weekly laborer, a monthly laborer, a yearly laborer,** or **a laborer** for a Sabbatical **cycle** of seven years, if he **left** upon the completion of his work **in the day, he collects** his wages **all day;** if he **left at night, he collects** his wages **all night and all day.** This indicates that one who finishes his work at night can be paid throughout the following day as well.

A further Baraita challenges Rav: *haya sachir shabbat, sachir chodesh, sachir shana, sachir shavua* — whether one was hired by the week, month, year, or septennate — *yotze bayom goveh kol hayom, yotze balayla goveh kol halaila*, if the term concluded during the day the employer must pay by sunset, if during the night then by sunrise. Yet the Baraita continues *ve-chol hayom* — "and throughout the day" — which seems to contradict Rav by extending the nighttime worker's window into the following day. Rav responds: *tanai hi* — this is actually a dispute among *Tannaim*, and his disagreement with Shmuel mirrors a pre-existing Tannaitic disagreement. A Baraita confirms this:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַיָא: שְׂכִיר שָׁעוֹת דְּיוֹם – גּוֹבֶה כׇּל הַיּוֹם, שְׂכִיר שָׁעוֹת דְּלַיְלָה – גּוֹבֶה כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: שְׂכִיר שָׁעוֹת דְּיוֹם – גּוֹבֶה כׇּל הַיּוֹם, שְׂכִיר שָׁעוֹת דְּלַיְלָה – גּוֹבֶה כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה וְכׇל הַיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **An hourly laborer by day collects** his wages **all day,** while **an hourly laborer by night collects** his wages **all night;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Shimon says: An hourly laborer by day collects** his wages **all day,** while **an hourly laborer by night collects** his wages **all night and all day.**

The *machlokes* between Rav and Shmuel is thus a reflection of the *machlokes* between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon, grounded in competing *drashot* on the relevant *psukim*. The broader point emerges here: the *mitzvah* of *bal talin* is not coextensive with the ethical and financial obligation to pay workers. There are cases where one can be technically exempt from *bal talin* while still being absolutely obligated to pay — and conversely, the specific rules of timing within *bal talin* sometimes appear almost formal, even arbitrary in their structure. The *shokai deSura* example illustrates that it is the terms of the understood agreement, not a mechanical clock, that determine when the obligation kicks in.

## Five Prohibitions

### Chamesh Shemot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: כׇּל הַכּוֹבֵשׁ שְׂכַר שָׂכִיר, עוֹבֵר בַּחֲמִשָּׁה שֵׁמוֹת הַלָּלוּ וַעֲשֵׂה. מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תַּעֲשֹׁק אֶת רֵעֲךָ״, וּמִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תִּגְזֹל״, וּמִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תַּעֲשֹׁק שָׂכִיר עָנִי״, וּמִשּׁוּום ״בַּל תָּלִין״, וּמִשּׁוּם ״בְּיוֹמוֹ תִּתֵּן שְׂכָרוֹ״, וּמִשּׁוּום ״לֹא תָבֹא עָלָיו הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues. **From here** the Sages **stated: Anyone who withholds the wages of a hired laborer violates these five** negative **prohibitions and** one **positive mitzva.** He **violates** the prohibition **of: "Do not oppress your neighbor"** (Leviticus 19:13), **and** the prohibition **of: "Do not steal"** (Leviticus 19:13), **and** the **prohibition of: "You should not oppress a hired laborer who is poor"** (Deuteronomy 24:14), **and the prohibition of delaying** payment of wages (Leviticus 19:13), **and** he has not fulfilled the positive mitzva **of: "On the same day you shall give him his wages"** (Deuteronomy 24:15), **and** he has violated the prohibition **of: "The sun shall not set upon him"** (Deuteronomy 24:15).

The Gemara now draws a broader conclusion from this whole discussion: *kol hakovesh sachar sachir over b-chamisha shemot* — whoever withholds a hired worker's wages violates *chamesh shemot*, five names, meaning five *lo ta'asei* and one *mitzvat asei*, for a total of six prohibitions. These are: *mishum bal ta'ashok et reacha* (do not cheat your friend), *mishum bal tigzol* (do not steal), *mishum bal ta'ashok sachir ani* (do not cheat a poor hired laborer), *mishum bal talin* (do not withhold wages overnight for a day worker), *mishum beyomo titein sacharo* (pay on the same day — the positive commandment), and *mishum lo tavo alav hashemesh* (do not let the sun set on unpaid wages for a nighttime worker).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי דְּאִיכָּא בִּימָמָא – לֵיכָּא בְּלֵילְיָא, דְּאִיכָּא בְּלֵילְיָא – לֵיכָּא בִּימָמָא? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: שֵׁם שְׂכִירוּת בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But these five prohibitions do not all take effect at the same time, since **those that are** applicable **by day are not** in effect **by night,** while those **that are** applicable **by night are not** relevant **by day.** How can he be in violation of them all? **Rav Ḥisda said:** It means **merely** that **the general concept** of withholding the **wages** of a hired laborer includes all these prohibitions and one positive mitzva.

The Gemara notes that *bal talin* and *beyomo titein sacharo* do not apply simultaneously in every case: *hanei de-ika beyomama leika belayla, de-ika belayla leika beyomama* — one applies to day workers and the other to night workers. Rav Chisda therefore clarifies: *shem sechirut be'alma* — the list describes all the prohibitions associated with the general category of hiring workers; not every individual case will trigger all six simultaneously.

### Oshek vs Gezel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיזֶה הוּא עוֹשֶׁק וְאֵיזֶהוּ גָּזֵל? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: ״לֵךְ וָשׁוּב״, ״לֵךְ וָשׁוּב״ – זֶה הוּא עוֹשֶׁק. ״יֵשׁ לְךָ בְּיָדִי וְאֵינִי נוֹתֵן לְךָ״ – זֶה הוּא גָּזֵל.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **What is** defined as **oppression and what is** defined as **stealing,** and what is the difference between them? **Rav Ḥisda said:** If he told him: **Go and return, go and return** (see Proverbs 3:28), avoiding paying him while saying that he will pay him at some point, **this is oppression.** If he says to him: **You have** money owed to you **in my possession but I will not give** it **to you, this is stealing.**

This leads to a fundamental question: what is the difference between *oshek* (cheating or oppression) and *gezel* (stealing)? Rav Chisda explains: *lech vashuv lech vashuv* — if one repeatedly tells a worker to come back later and keeps putting him off, that is *oshek*; *yesh lacha biyadi ve'eini noten lacha* — if one openly tells the worker "I have your money and I'm not giving it to you," that is *gezel*.

### Oshek/Gezel Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אֵיזֶהוּ עוֹשֶׁק שֶׁחִיְּיבָה עָלָיו תּוֹרָה קׇרְבָּן – דּוּמְיָא דְּפִקָּדוֹן, דְּקָא כָפַר לֵיהּ מָמוֹנָא! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: ״נְתַתִּיו לָךְ״ – זֶהוּ עוֹשֶׁק, ״יֵשׁ לְךָ בְּיָדִי וְאֵינִי נוֹתֵן לָךְ״ – זֶה הוּא גָּזֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet objects to this** from a *baraita*: **What is** the type of **oppression for which the Torah obligated him** to bring **an offering?** It is **similar to** the case of one who had been entrusted with money as **a deposit, where he** then **denies** that he accepted it, thereby keeping **the money.** This contradicts Rav Ḥisda's claim that oppression is referring to one who admits that he owes him. **Rather, Rav Sheshet said** that the difference is as follows: If he said to him: **I gave it to you, this is** defined as **oppression.** If he tells him: **You have** money owed to you **in my possession but I am not giving** it **to you, this is** defined as **stealing.**

Rav Sheshet challenges Rav Chisda's formulation. Both *oshek* and *gezel* appear in the Torah in the context of the *korban asham* for monetary violations, alongside *pikadon*, and the *halacha* requires that they all share a common structure: the offender must have actively denied the claim (*dekafar lei mamona*), in parallel to the denial involved in a *pikadon* case. Under Rav Chisda's definition, telling someone "come back tomorrow" does not involve a denial — so it cannot be the operative case for the Torah's *oshek*. Rav Sheshet therefore offers a different distinction: *natati lach* — "I already paid you" — is *oshek*; *yesh lacha biyadi ve'eini noten lacha* — "I have it and won't give it" — is *gezel*. In Rav Sheshet's formulation, both involve a denial, but the nature of the denial differs.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֵיזֶה הוּא גָּזֵל שֶׁחִיְּיבָה עָלָיו תּוֹרָה קׇרְבָּן, דּוּמְיָא דְּפִקָּדוֹן בָּעֵינַן, דְּקָא כָפַר לֵיהּ מָמוֹנָא! אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: ״לֹא שְׂכַרְתִּיךָ מֵעוֹלָם״ – זֶה הוּא עוֹשֶׁק, ״נְתַתִּיו לָךְ״ – זֶה הוּא גָּזֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye objects to this: What is** the type of **stealing for which the Torah obligated him** to bring **an offering? We require** it to be **similar to** the case of one who had been entrusted with money as **a deposit, where he** then **denies** that he accepted it, thereby keeping **the money.** That is unlike the example of stealing given by Rav Ḥisda and Rav Sheshet, where the party withholding the money concedes that he owes it. **Rather, Abaye said:** If he said to him: **I never hired you, this is oppression;** if he claimed: **I gave it to you, this is stealing.**

Abaye then challenges Rav Sheshet: the same objection applies to *gezel* — *gezel* also requires a denial similar to *pikadon* to trigger the *korban asham* liability. *Be'inan dekafar lei mamona* — you need a denial. Abaye therefore proposes: *lo secharticha me'olam* — "I never hired you in the first place" — is *oshek*, since you are denying the employment relationship itself; *netati lacha* — "I already paid you your wages" — is *gezel*, since you are denying an obligation that you in fact incurred.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת, מַאי שְׁנָא עוֹשֶׁק דְּקַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ, וּמַאי שְׁנָא גָּזֵל דְּלָא קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ? אָמַר לָךְ: גָּזֵל – דְּגַזְלֵיהּ וַהֲדַר כַּפְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to Rav Sheshet, what is different** about **oppression that he raised a difficulty** against Rav Ḥisda concerning it, **and what is different** about **stealing that he did not raise a difficulty,** although Abaye's question was similar to his. The Gemara explains: Rav Sheshet could have **said to you: Stealing** means **that** he first **stole from him** by stating that he will not give him the money, **and later denied** owing it.

The further give-and-take examines why Rav Sheshet only demanded the *pikadon*-style denial for *oshek* and not for *gezel*. His answer is that *gezel* involves a two-stage act: one first takes the object wrongfully (*gezel degazlei*) and then denies it (*vehadar kafrei*) — the act of taking is primary, and the denial is secondary.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ עוֹשֶׁק נָמֵי דַּהֲדַר כַּפְרֵיהּ! הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא: בִּשְׁלָמָא הָתָם כְּתִיב: ״אוֹ בְגָזֵל״ – מִכְּלָל דְּאוֹדִי לֵיהּ מֵעִיקָּרָא. אֲבָל גַּבֵּי עוֹשֶׁק מִי כְּתִיב ״אוֹ בְעוֹשֶׁק״? ״אוֹ עָשַׁק״ כְּתִיב, שֶׁעֲשָׁקוֹ כְּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If so,** then **even** with regard to **oppression as well,** the case can be **that** he first conceded that he owes the wages **and** then **later denied it.** Why does Rav Sheshet say that the case must be where the employer said to the laborer: I gave it to you? The Gemara responds: **How can** these cases **be compared? Granted, there it is written:** "And if he deals falsely with his neighbor in a matter of deposit, or of pledge, **or of robbery"** (Leviticus 5:21), which **by inference** indicates **that he admitted to him at the outset. But with regard to oppression is it written: Or by oppression? It is written: "Or he oppressed,"** which does not refer back to his previous denial but is referring to the actual sin, indicating **that he had already oppressed him.**

The Gemara then asks: but isn't *oshek* the same? Rav Sheshet responds by pointing to the linguistic form of the verse: by *gezel* the Torah uses a nominal form (*o begezel*, "the stolen object"), implying prior acknowledgment that the object existed and was taken; by *oshek* the Torah uses an active verbal form (*o ashak*, "he oppressed"), implying that the act of cheating is itself the operative moment. This textual distinction is what allows Rav Sheshet to treat the two differently.

### Rava's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: זֶה הוּא עוֹשֶׁק זֶהוּ גָּזֵל. וְלָמָּה חִלְּקָן הַכָּתוּב – לַעֲבוֹר עָלָיו בִּשְׁנֵי לָאוִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** There is no need for such an artificial distinction, as **oppression is the same as stealing,** and no practical difference exists between the two categories. **And why,** then, **did the verse divide them** into two categories? It did this **so that he will violate two prohibitions,** stealing and oppression.

Rava cuts through the debate entirely: *zehu oshek zehu gezel* — *oshek* and *gezel* are the same thing. Why then does the Torah mention them twice using different words? *Velama chalkan hakatuv la'avor alav bishnei lavin* — the Torah split them in order to create a double prohibition. One who withholds a worker's wages violates both simultaneously. This is structurally parallel to *bal yera'eh* and *bal yimatze* — the two prohibitions against owning *chametz* on *Pesach*, which overlap entirely in content but create separate *lavin*. According to Rava, there is no meaningful legal distinction between *oshek* and *gezel*: any act of withholding or denying what rightfully belongs to another person constitutes both simultaneously. This means that the distinction Abaye and Rav Sheshet were laboring to construct — which type of denial corresponds to which term — is irrelevant, since both terms cover any denial of liability. Whether the question concerns the *korban asham* specifically is a separate matter; Rava likely would still agree that a *korban asham* requires a *pikadon*-style denial, but holds that as a general matter, the two categories are fungible.

## Animals & Tools

### Rental Obligations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֶחָד שְׂכַר אָדָם וְאֶחָד שְׂכַר בְּהֵמָה וְאֶחָד שְׂכַר כֵּלִים – יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם ״בְּיוֹמוֹ תִּתֵּן שְׂכָרוֹ״, וְיֵשׁ בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם ״לֹא תָלִין פְּעֻלַּת שָׂכִיר אִתְּךָ עַד בֹּקֶר״. אֵימָתַי – בִּזְמַן שֶׁתְּבָעוֹ. לֹא תְּבָעוֹ – אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר עָלָיו. הִמְחָהוּ אֵצֶל חֶנְוָנִי אוֹ אֵצֶל שׁוּלְחָנִי – אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Whether** referring to **a person's wages** that he receives **or the renting of an animal or the renting of utensils,** are all **subject to** the prohibition **of: "On the same day you shall give him his wages"** (Deuteronomy 24:15), **and** are **subject to** the prohibition **of: "The wages of a hired laborer shall not remain with you all night until the morning"** (Leviticus 19:13). **When** does he transgress these prohibitions? He transgresses them **when** the one owed the money **claimed** the payment from **him.** If he **did not claim** his payment from **him** the other **does not transgress** the prohibitions. **If** the one who owes the money **transferred his** payment by leaving instructions **with a storekeeper or with a money changer** to pay him, **he does not transgress** the prohibitions.

The next Mishnah extends the discussion of *bal talin* to additional cases: *echad sachar adam ve-echad sachar behema ve-echad sachar kelim yesh bo mishum beyomo titein sacharo* — whether one hires a person, rents an animal, or rents tools, all of these carry the obligation of *beyomo titein sacharo*. One must pay on time for rental of inanimate objects just as for human labor. Similarly, *veyesh bo mishum lo talin pe'ulat sachir itcha ad boker* — the prohibition against withholding overnight applies equally across all three categories.

### Conditions for Exemption

The Mishnah qualifies this: *eimatai? bizman sheta'avo* — when does the obligation kick in? Only when the worker or owner makes a claim. If the worker never makes a claim for his wages — *lo teva'o* — then the employer has not technically violated the *mitzvah*, since the obligation is triggered by the demand. If the employer arranges credit for the worker with a *chenvani* (shopkeeper) or *shulchani* (money-changer) — *hamamcheihu etzel chenvani ve'etzel shulchani eino over alav* — so that the worker can purchase goods against his anticipated wages, this also exempts the employer from *bal talin*. The arrangement functions like a paycheck advance: as long as the worker has practical access to the value of his wages, the employer is not in violation.

### Worker's Oath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׂכִיר בִּזְמַנּוֹ – נִשְׁבָּע וְנוֹטֵל. עָבַר זְמַנּוֹ – אֵינוֹ נִשְׁבָּע וְנוֹטֵל. אִם יֵשׁ עֵדִים שֶׁתְּבָעוֹ בִּזְמַנּוֹ – הֲרֵי זֶה נִשְׁבָּע וְנוֹטֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna discusses other related *halakhot*: If **a hired laborer** requests payment **at** the proper **time** and the employer claims he already paid him, the laborer **takes an oath** that he did not receive his wages **and** then **takes** the wages from the employer. If **the time had passed,** he does **not take an oath and take** the wages. **If there are witnesses** who testify **that he claimed** the money from **him at** the proper **time, he takes an oath and takes** the money.

*Sachir bizmano nishba venotel* — a worker claiming wages within the appropriate time period may take a rabbinic oath and collect his wages on that basis. Once the time period has passed — *avar zemano, eino nishba venotel* — the worker can no longer use the oath to collect. However, if there are witnesses that he had already made a claim during the relevant period — *im yesh eidim sheta'avo, harei zeh nishba venotel* — he retains the ability to collect via oath even later. The worker's credibility to take the oath is thus grounded either in the timeliness of his claim or in witnessed prior demand.

## Ger Toshav Status

### Ger Toshav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם ״בְּיוֹמוֹ תִּתֵּן שְׂכָרוֹ״, וְאֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם ״לֹא תָלִין פְּעוּלַּת שָׂכִיר אִתְּךָ עַד בֹּקֶר״.
>
> **Translation:** One who hires **a gentile who resides in Eretz Yisrael and observes the seven Noahide mitzvot [*ger toshav*] is subject to** the prohibition **of: "On the same day you shall give him his wages," but is not subject to** the negative mitzva **of: "The wages of a hired laborer shall not remain with you all night until the morning."**

The Mishnah addresses the *ger toshav* — the resident alien, a non-Jew who has accepted certain obligations (presumably the *sheva mitzvot benei Noach*) and thereby acquires a recognized legal status within the Jewish legal system. The Mishnah states: *yesh bo mishum beyomo titein sacharo* — if one hires a *ger toshav*, the positive obligation to pay on time applies; *ve-ein bo mishum lo talin pe'ulat sachir itcha ad boker* — but the negative prohibition of withholding wages overnight does not apply. The reason, as will emerge from the *drashot*, has to do with which *psukim* use the word *re'acha* (your friend) and which do not.

### Attribution Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַנִּי מַתְנִיתִין? לָא תַּנָּא קַמָּא דְּ״מֵאַחֶיךָ״, וְלָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. מַאי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא:
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Whose** opinion is expressed in **the mishna?** It is **not** that of **the first *tanna* of** the *baraita*, who interprets the phrase: **"From your brothers"** (Deuteronomy 24:14), **and** it is **not Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda.** The Gemara clarifies: **What is** this *baraita* that is referred to here? The Gemara explains: **As it is taught** in a *baraita*:

The Gemara asks: *mani matnitin?* — whose position does this Mishnah represent? It cannot be the *Tanna Kamma* (*lo tanna kamma de-me'akhikha*) and it cannot be Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda. The *Tanna Kamma* excludes the *ger toshav* entirely from both obligations based on the word *me'achicha* (from your brother), which limits the protections to fellow Jews. If the Mishnah were the *Tanna Kamma*, the inclusion of the *ger toshav* even for *beyomo titein sacharo* would be a contradiction. And if the Mishnah were Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda, the inclusion of animals and tools would be a contradiction, since Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda holds that *behema ve-keilim* are governed only by *bal ta'ashok*, not by the timing-based prohibitions.

### Baraita Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מֵאַחֶיךָ״ – פְּרָט לַאֲחֵרִים. ״גֵּרְךָ״ – זֶה גֵּר צֶדֶק. ״בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ״ – זֶה אוֹכֵל נְבֵילוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: "You shall not oppress a hired laborer who is poor and needy, whether he be from your brothers or from your stranger that is in your land within your gates" (Deuteronomy 24:14), which is interpreted as follows: The term **"from your brothers"** serves **to exclude others,** i.e., gentiles, who are not your brothers. As for the term **"your stranger," this** is referring to **a righteous convert.** As for the term **"within your gates," this** is referring to a *ger toshav* who lives in Eretz Yisrael and **eats unslaughtered animal carcasses** because he has not accepted Judaism upon himself.

A Baraita is cited to lay out the three Tannaitic positions. The *Tanna Kamma* reads: *me'achicha, perat le-acherim* — "from your brother," excluding non-Jews.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לִי אֶלָּא שְׂכַר אָדָם, מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת בְּהֵמָה וְכֵלִים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּאַרְצְךָ״ – כׇּל שֶׁבְּאַרְצְךָ. וְכוּלָּן עוֹבְרִים בְּכׇל הַשֵּׁמוֹת הַלָּלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **I have** derived **only** that the prohibitions of delaying wages apply to **the hire of people. From where** do I know **to include** payment for the rental of **animals and utensils** in the prohibition of delaying wages? **The verse states: "In your land,"** which includes **all that is in your land. And** in **all of** the above cases of delaying payment they **transgress all of these prohibitions** which apply to delaying payment.

*Gerkha* — the word "your stranger" — excludes the *ger toshav* specifically, meaning the one who is *okhel nevelot* (permitted to eat non-kosher meat), which is the Talmudic characterization of the *ger toshav*'s status. From where do we know to extend the obligation to animals and tools? *Talmud lomar be'artzakha* — "in your land," meaning anything within the domain of your property or your land is included: *kol she-be'artzakha*. And the Baraita concludes: *vekhulan overim bekhol ha-shemot ha-lalu* — all of these categories, humans, animals, and tools, violate all the relevant prohibitions when withheld without pay. This is the source for the Mishnah's opening ruling.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: אֶחָד שְׂכַר אָדָם, וְאֶחָד שְׂכַר בְּהֵמָה וְאֶחָד שְׂכַר כֵּלִים – יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם ״בְּיוֹמוֹ תִתֵּן שְׂכָרוֹ״, וְיֵשׁ בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תָּלִין פְּעֻלַּת שָׂכִיר״. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם ״בְּיוֹמוֹ תִּתֵּן שְׂכָרוֹ״, וְאֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם ״לֹא תָלִין״. בְּהֵמָה וְכֵלִים אֵין בָּהֶן אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תַּעֲשֹׁק״ בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **From here** the Sages **stated: Whether** referring to **the hire of a person or the rental of an animal or the rental of utensils,** all are **subject to** the prohibition **of: "On the same day you shall give him his wages"** (Deuteronomy 24:15), **and they are** likewise **subject to the prohibition of delaying** the payment of wages of a **hired laborer** (Leviticus 19:13). **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says:** One who hires **a *ger toshav* is subject to** the prohibition **of: "On the same day you shall give him his wages," but is not subject to the prohibition of delaying** payment of wages, and the payment of rent of **an animal or vessels is included only in** the prohibition **of: "Do not oppress" alone.**

The parenthetical discussion of the *chenvani* arrangement — where the employer sets up credit at a shopkeeper or money-changer for the worker — naturally raises the question of whether such credit is an adequate substitute for cash wages. The *shulchani* arrangement is simpler, since one can simply go and exchange for cash. The *chenvani* arrangement is more restrictive, since it is limited to purchases at that particular store. The Halakha does not appear to limit what categories of goods the worker may purchase, and the *shulchani* option makes cash available directly.

## Tannaim Dispute

### Tanna Kamma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַנִּי? אִי תַּנָּא קַמָּא דְּ״מֵאַחֶיךָ״ – קַשְׁיָא גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב. אִי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי – קַשְׁיָא בְּהֵמָה וְכֵלִים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to its initial question: In accordance with **whose** opinion is the mishna? **If** it is in accordance with the opinion of **the first *tanna* of** the *baraita*, who interprets the verse: **"From your brothers,"** the *halakha* of **a *ger toshav*** is **difficult,** as he equates a *ger toshav* with a Jew, unlike the mishna. **If** it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** son of Rabbi Yehuda, the *halakha* of the rental payment of **animals and vessels** is **difficult,** as Rabbi Yosei maintains they are not included in any of the prohibitions except for: Do not oppress.

The *Tanna Kamma*'s position is that the *ger toshav* is excluded from *both* obligations — *beyomo titein sacharo* and *lo talin* — by means of a *gezeira shava* of *sachir sachir*. The word *sachir* appears in the context where the verse says *me'achicha*, "from your brother," limiting the scope of the hired worker to whom these laws apply; the *Tanna Kamma* uses the verbal analogy to extend that exclusion across all instances of the word *sachir* in the relevant *psukim*, thereby removing the *ger toshav* from the protections of both the positive and negative commandments.

### Rabbi Yosei Position

Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda does not accept the *gezeira shava* of *sachir sachir* at all. His position is nuanced: a *ger toshav* retains the obligation of *beyomo titein sacharo* (the positive commandment to pay on time), but not *lo talin* (the negative prohibition). Conversely, for *behema ve-keilim*, only *bal ta'ashok* applies — not *beyomo titein sacharo* or *bal talin*. Since he does not employ the *sachir sachir* verbal analogy, his distinctions require separate derivation.

### Rabbi Yishmael School

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הַאי תַּנָּא – תַּנָּא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הוּא, דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אֶחָד שְׂכַר אָדָם, וְאֶחָד שְׂכַר בְּהֵמָה וְאֶחָד שְׂכַר כֵּלִים – יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם ״בְּיוֹמוֹ תִתֵּן שְׂכָרוֹ״ וּמִשּׁוּם ״לֹא תָלִין״. גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב – יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם ״בְּיוֹמוֹ תִתֵּן שְׂכָרוֹ״, וְאֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תָּלִין״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: This *tanna*** of the mishna **is a *tanna* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael, as the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: Whether** in the case of **the hire of a person, the rental of an animal, or the rental of vessels,** all of these payments are **subject to** the mitzva **of: "On the same day you shall give him his wages," and the prohibition of delaying** payment of wages. **A *ger toshav*** is **subject to** the mitzva **of: "On the same day you shall give him his wages," but** he **is not subject to the prohibition of delaying** payment of wages.

Rava resolves the attribution question: *amar Rava, hai tanna, tanna de-vei Rebbi Yishmael hee* — the Mishnah follows the school of Rabbi Yishmael. It is worth noting that while most of our Mishnayot reflect the tradition of Rabbi Akiva's students, the school of Rabbi Yishmael developed its own distinct methodology of *drashot* and *halachic* derivation. A *Tanna de-vei Rebbi Yishmael* is often cited anonymously and occupies a somewhat distinctive position within the Tannaitic corpus. The Baraita from Rabbi Yishmael's school states explicitly: humans, animals, and tools all carry *beyomo titein sacharo* and *lo talin*; but a *ger toshav* has *beyomo titein sacharo* and not *lo talin*.

## Exclusions Logic

### Ki Ani Hu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא ״דְּמֵאַחֶיךָ״ – גָּמַר ״שָׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָא גָּמַר ״שָׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״.
>
> **Translation:** Until this point, the Gemara has discussed the source of the ruling of the first *tanna*. It now analyzes the reasons behind the different opinions. **What is the reason of the first *tanna* of** the *baraita*, who interprets the verse: **"From your brothers"?** He **derives** it by verbal analogy comparing the words: "You shall not oppress **a hired laborer,"** and the verse: "The wages of a **hired laborer** shall not remain with you all night until the morning." Just as the former verse includes a Jew, a *ger toshav*, the rental of an animal, and the rental of utensils, so too, the latter verse includes all of the above. **And Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, does not derive** this verbal analogy of: **"Hired laborer"** and **"hired laborer."**

The question now is: what is the *Tanna de-vei Rebbi Yishmael*'s source for his two-part position — including *behema ve-keilim* while excluding the *ger toshav* from *bal talin*? The Gemara works through this carefully. The *Tanna de-vei Rebbi Yishmael* does not use the *sachir sachir* verbal analogy. Instead, he derives the basic scope of *bal talin* from the verse *velo talin pe'ulat sachir itcha ad boker* — "do not withhold the work of a hired laborer who is with you until morning." From the phrase *kol she-pe'ulato itcha* — anyone whose work is with you — he derives a broad inclusion: any worker whose labor is performed on your behalf is covered.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְהִי דְּלָא גָּמַר ״שָׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״, בְּהֵמָה וְכֵלִים מִשּׁוּם ״בְּיוֹמוֹ תִתֵּן שְׂכָרוֹ״ נָמֵי נִיחַיַּיב? תָּנֵי רַבִּי חֲנַנְיָא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְלֹא תָבֹא עָלָיו הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ כִּי עָנִי הוּא״ – מִי שֶׁהֵן בָּאִין לִידֵי עֲנִיּוּת וַעֲשִׁירוּת, יָצְאוּ בְּהֵמָה וְכֵלִים שֶׁאֵינָן בָּאִין לִידֵי עֲנִיּוּת וַעֲשִׁירוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **Although** Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, does **not derive** the verbal analogy of the words: **"Hired laborer"** and **"hired laborer,"** one **should** still **also be liable** in the case of **animals or vessels due to** the injunction **of: "On the same day you shall give him his wages."** From where does he derive that such items are not included in this prohibition? **Rabbi Ḥananya teaches** in a *baraita* that **the verse states:** "On his day you shall give him his wages, **and the sun shall not set upon him, for he is poor"** (Deuteronomy 24:15). This verse clearly is referring to **one who** can **enter into** a state of **poverty and wealth,** which **excludes animals and vessels, which cannot enter into** a state of **poverty and wealth.**

Rabbi Chananya draws on a different element of the verse: *velo tavo alav hashemesh ki ani hu* — "and the sun shall not set on him, for he is poor." The phrase *ki ani hu* specifies that the prohibition addresses someone capable of experiencing poverty: *mi she-hein ba'in lidei aniyut va-ashirut* — entities that can be poor or rich. Animals and tools cannot become poor; therefore the verse's reasoning excludes them from the literal ambit of *ki ani hu*. Moreover, the owner of an animal available for hire presumably already possesses some assets, giving him a degree of financial stability that the verse's poverty-concern does not obviously reach.

### Priority Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא קַמָּא, הַאי ״כִּי עָנִי הוּא״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי: לְהַקְדִּים עָנִי לְעָשִׁיר. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: הָהוּא ״מִלֹּא תַעֲשֹׁק שָׂכִיר עָנִי וְאֶבְיוֹן״ נָפְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **the first *tanna*,** who does not address **this** verse of: **"For he is poor," what** does he **do with it?** The Gemara answers: **That** verse is **necessary to give precedence to a poor person over a wealthy person** if the employer does not have enough money to pay all his workers. **And** how does **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda,** derive that *halakha*? In his opinion **that** *halakha* **is derived from: "You shall not oppress a hired laborer who is poor and needy"** (Deuteronomy 24:14).

The *Tanna Kamma* uses *ki ani hu* differently — not to exclude animals and tools, but to establish a priority rule: if an employer has two workers to pay, one poor and one wealthy, he must pay the poor worker first. This derives from the verse's explicit identification of the worker's poverty as the rationale for the obligation's urgency.

Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda, meanwhile, derives this same priority principle from a different verse — *lo ta'ashok sachir ani va-evyon* — "do not oppress a poor and destitute hired worker." He therefore has no need to use *ki ani hu* for the priority rule, freeing the *Tanna Kamma* to use it for that purpose while using *ki ani hu* for the exclusion of animals and tools.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא קַמָּא: חַד לְהַקְדִּים עָנִי לְעָשִׁיר, וְחַד לְהַקְדִּים עָנִי לְאֶבְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if so, why does **the first *tanna*** require another verse? The Gemara explains: He maintains that **one** verse serves **to give a poor person precedence over a wealthy person,** while the other **one** serves **to give a poor person precedence over a destitute person,** i.e., a complete pauper who owns nothing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן אֶבְיוֹן – מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא כְּסִיף לְמִתְבְּעֵיהּ. אֲבָל עָשִׁיר, דִּכְסִיף לְמִתְבְּעֵיהּ – אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן עָשִׁיר – מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא צְרִיךְ לֵיהּ, אֲבָל אֶבְיוֹן דִּצְרִיךְ לֵיהּ – אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And** both verses are **necessary, as had** the Torah **taught us** only that a poor person comes before one who is **destitute** one could have said that this is **because** a destitute person is **not ashamed to demand** his money; he is so needy he is not embarrassed to ask for money. **But** with regard to **a wealthy person, who is ashamed to demand** his wages, one might **say** that a poor person does **not** receive precedence over him. **And** conversely, **had** the Torah **taught us** only that this *halakha* applies to **a wealthy person** one could have said that it is **because** he **does not need** his wages right away, **but** with regard to **a destitute person, who does need it** immediately, **say** that it does **not** apply. It was therefore **necessary** for both verses to be stated.

The *Tanna Kamma* also extends the priority principle by reading *ani* and *evyon* as two distinct degrees of poverty. The Gemara then explains why both are needed (*tzricha*): one might have thought the very poor (*evyon*) need not be explicitly protected because they are unashamed to make their wage claims loudly — *de-lo kasif le-mitba* — and therefore already at lower risk of being exploited through delay. The wealthy worker (*ashir*), by contrast, may be embarrassed to press his claim — *kasif le-mitba* — and might therefore need explicit protection. And one might have thought the wealthy worker can be passed over because he does not urgently need the money — *de-lo tzarikh lei*. Each case requires explicit derivation.

This discussion surfaces a genuine psychological observation embedded in the *halacha*: the worker who most urgently needs the money may in fact be less likely to advocate for himself due to internalized social norms around dignity and deference, while the wealthier worker may actually be more comfortable pressing his claim — yet also less urgently in need. The *Tanna Kamma* addresses both poles. The very poor worker (*evyon*) is unashamed to demand his wages and is urgently in need; the merely poor (*ani*) may have somewhat more dignity to maintain. Each deserves protection on different grounds, making both derivations necessary.

## Reconciliation

### Apparent Contradictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא דִּידַן: מָה נַפְשָׁךְ? אִי יָלֵיף ״שָׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״ – אֲפִילּוּ גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב נָמֵי, אִי לָא יָלֵיף ״שָׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״ – בְּהֵמָה וְכֵלִים מְנָא לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to the ruling of **the *tanna* of our** mishna, **whichever way you** look at it, it requires clarification. **If he derives** the verbal analogy of the words: **"Hired laborer"** and **"hired laborer,"** then **even a *ger toshav*** should be included. **If** he does **not derive** the verbal analogy of the words: **"Hired laborer"** and **"hired laborer," from where does he** derive that this *halakha* applies **to animals and vessels?**

The Gemara now presses on the internal consistency of the *Tanna de-vei Rebbi Yishmael*'s position: *uman nafshakh* — whichever way you look at it, there is a difficulty. If he learns the *gezeira shava sachir sachir*, he should include the *ger toshav* in *bal talin* alongside animals and tools. If he does not learn the *gezeira shava*, then his basis for including animals and tools is unclear. How does he hold both positions simultaneously?

### Peulato Itcha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם לָא יָלֵיף ״שָׂכִיר״ ״שָׂכִיר״, וְשָׁאנֵי הָתָם דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״וְלֹא תָלִין פְּעֻלַּת שָׂכִיר אִתְּךָ עַד בֹּקֶר״: כֹּל שֶׁפְּעוּלָּתוֹ אִתְּךָ. אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב נָמֵי! אָמַר קְרָא ״רֵעֲךָ״ – רֵעֲךָ, וְלֹא גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** he does **not derive** the analogy of: **"Hired laborer"** and **"hired laborer," and there it is different, as the verse states: "The wages of [*pe'ulat*] a hired laborer shall not remain with you all night until the morning"** (Leviticus 19:13). This verse is referring to **any** case **where its work [*pe'ulato*] is with you,** which includes animals and vessels. The Gemara asks: **If so,** then **even a *ger toshav*** should be included, as he too performs work for you. The Gemara responds: The initial section of **the verse states: "Your neighbor,"** which refers to **your neighbor** who is Jewish, **and not a *ger toshav*,** who is not called a neighbor.

The Gemara resolves this: *le'olam lo yalif sachir sachir* — the *Tanna de-vei Rebbi Yishmael* never employs the *sachir sachir* verbal analogy. Instead, from *velo talin pe'ulat sachir itcha ad boker*, he derives through the phrase *pe'ulato itcha* — "whose work is with you" — that the obligation extends to any rental or hire arrangement where work or productive use is performed on your behalf, encompassing *behema ve-keilim*.

### Recha Exclusion

Then, from the word *re'acha* that appears in the relevant verse — *re'acha*, your friend, referring to a fellow Jew — he excludes the *ger toshav* from the negative prohibition of *lo talin*. The *ger toshav* is not fully within the category of *re'acha*, and therefore, while many obligations apply to dealings with him, the *lo ta'asei* of *bal talin* is not among them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ בְּהֵמָה וְכֵלִים נָמֵי! הָא כְּתִיב ״אִתְּךָ״. מָה רָאִיתָ לְרַבּוֹת בְּהֵמָה וְכֵלִים וּלְהוֹצִיא גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב? מִסְתַּבְּרָא בְּהֵמָה וְכֵלִים הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְרַבּוֹת – שֶׁכֵּן יֶשְׁנָן בִּכְלַל מָמוֹן רֵעֲךָ, גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב אֵינוֹ בִּכְלַל מָמוֹן רֵעֲךָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** then **even animals and utensils** should not be included, as they too are not called: Your neighbor. The Gemara replies: It is **written: "With you,"** which includes all items that work with you. The Gemara asks: **What did you see** to decide **to include animals and utensils, and to exclude a *ger toshav*?** The Gemara answers: **It stands to reason** that **he should include animals and utensils, as they are** at least **included in** the category of **your neighbor's money,** whereas **a *ger toshav* is not included in your neighbor's money.**

Yet *behema ve-keilim*, though inanimate, are included — not because they themselves are *re'acha*, but because they fall under *mamon recha*, the property of one's friend. The rented animal or borrowed tools belong to a fellow Jew, and therefore the obligation to pay promptly for their use falls under the same penumbra of *re'acha* that generates the *bal talin* prohibition. The *ger toshav* himself, by contrast, is not within that penumbra: *eino bikhlal mamon re'acha*. The result is a principled, if subtle, distinction: it is not the entity performing the work that is decisive, but whose property is at stake and whose interests the *pasuk*'s language is designed to protect.

This means that the *lo ta'asei* of *bal talin* does not apply when a *ger toshav* is hired, but the *mitzvat asei* of *beyomo titein sacharo* still does. The practical difference is primarily one of formal category — *asei* versus *lav*, and the accompanying difference in severity — rather than a practical permission to delay payment. The *ger toshav* must still be paid on time; the violation, when it occurs, is assessed differently.

## Amaleki Exclusion

### Tanna Kamma Recha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא קַמָּא דְּ״מֵאַחֶיךָ״ [וְר׳ יוֹסֵי בְּר׳ יְהוּדָה], הַאי ״רֵעֲךָ״ מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״רֵעֲךָ״ וְלֹא גּוֹי. גּוֹי מֵ״אַחֶיךָ״ נָפְקָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the first *tanna* of** the *baraita*, who interprets: **"From your brothers," what does he do with this** verse: **"Your neighbor"?** The Gemara explains: **That** verse **is necessary for him for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **"Your neighbor," and not a gentile.** The Gemara challenges: The exclusion of **a gentile** is **derived from: "Your brothers,"** and no additional verses are necessary for this purpose.

If the *Tanna Kamma* has already excluded the *ger toshav* from *bal talin* through the *gezeira shava* of *sachir sachir* — derived from the word *me'achicha* — what then does he do with the word *re'acha*? He has no need of *re'acha* for the *ger toshav* exclusion, since that is handled elsewhere. So what additional work does *re'acha* perform in his system?

### Amaleki Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַד לְמִשְׁרֵא עוֹשְׁקוֹ, וְחַד לְמִשְׁרֵא גְּזֵלוֹ, וְקָא סָבַר גֶּזֶל גּוֹי מוּתָּר. וּצְרִיכִי: דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן גְּזֵלוֹ – מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא טָרַח בֵּיהּ, אֲבָל עוֹשְׁקוֹ, דְּטָרַח בֵּיהּ – אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן עוֹשְׁקוֹ – מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אֲתָא לִידֵיהּ, אֲבָל גְּזֵלוֹ, דַּאֲתָא לִידֵיהּ – אֵימָא לָא. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **One** verse serves **to permit** one who **oppresses him, and** the other **one** serves **to permit stealing from him, and** both are **necessary. Because had** the Torah **taught us** this *halakha* only with regard to **stealing from him,** one could have said that this is **because** the gentile **did not toil for him, but** one who **oppresses him, where he** has **toiled for him,** you might **say** that he is **not** permitted to oppress him. **And** conversely, **had** the Torah **taught us** only that the practice of he who **oppresses him** is permitted, it might have been said that this is **because** the money has **not** yet **reached his hand, but** with regard to **stealing from him,** when he takes money **that** has already **reached his hand, say** that this *halakha* does **not** apply. Therefore, both cases are **necessary.**

The Gemara answers: *re'acha ve-lo Amaleki* — "your friend, but not an Amalekite." The *Tanna Kamma* uses *re'acha* to teach that an *Amaleki* does not receive the protections of *lo talin*. This is a striking and unusual *drasha*, one that appears rarely if at all elsewhere. The *Amaleki* presents a specific textual problem: because Amalek descended from Esau, he would technically be included in *me'achicha* — "from your brother" — since Esau was Yaakov's brother, making Amalekites genealogical relatives of Israel. Without an additional exclusion, the word *me'achicha* might include them; therefore *re'acha* is used to exclude them specifically.

There is an obvious practical question: is there ever a realistic scenario in which a Jew hires an *Amaleki* as a laborer? The institution of fighting Amalek is a rule of war authorized by the *beit din* and the king, not an individual obligation to harm every person of Amalekite descent on sight. Within the ordinary labor market, however unlikely, the *halacha* addresses the case. The *drasha* thus serves primarily to close a logical gap in the textual analysis rather than to establish a widely applicable rule.

### Oshek/Gezel Drasha

The verse further supplies a double exclusion — *re'acha* used twice, once for *oshek* and once for *gezel* — *chad le-mishra oshko ve-chad le-mishra gozlo*. One application authorizes, in the formal Halakhic sense of the *beit din*'s jurisdiction, the non-application of the *oshek* prohibition regarding an *Amaleki* (or, in broader readings, a non-Jew), and the other authorizes the non-application of the *gezel* prohibition. The Gemara explains why both are needed (*tzricha*): one might have thought that while stealing from a non-Jew is tolerated within the *halachic* jurisdictional framework (*de-lo tarach bei* — he didn't work hard to earn it, or *de-ati lidei* — it already came into his possession), cheating him out of wages he earned through labor would still be prohibited, or vice versa. Both cases therefore require explicit derivation.

### Meiri Principle

This is precisely the context in which the *Meiri*'s well-known principle becomes relevant. The *Meiri* holds that the formal permissions — where they exist within the *halachic* system — apply only to members of societies without the rule of law, not to peoples who govern themselves by ethical norms and legal structures. Members of civilized societies committed to law and order (*geder ha-dat*) are entitled to the same protections as Jews within the *halachic* system. This principle significantly softens the practical import of these *drashot*, which may in any case reflect a censored or euphemistic text (often *akum* or *nochri* in other manuscripts, or *Notzri* in some readings), rather than the specific category of Amalek per se. The broader jurisdictional understanding of these passages — that *halacha* governs obligations between Jews before a Jewish court, while non-Jews have recourse to their own legal systems — also serves to contextualize the scope of these rules.

## Rabbi Yosei Sources

### Lo Talin Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הַאי ״לֹא תָלִין פְּעֻלַּת שָׂכִיר אִתְּךָ עַד בֹּקֶר״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַב אַסִּי. דְּאָמַר רַב אַסִּי: אֲפִילּוּ לֹא שְׂכָרוֹ אֶלָּא לִבְצוֹר לוֹ אֶשְׁכּוֹל אֶחָד שֶׁל עֲנָבִים – עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תָּלִין״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what** does **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, do with this** verse: **"The wages of a hired laborer shall not remain with you all night until the morning"?** The Gemara answers: It **is necessary for him for that which Rav Asi** teaches, **as Rav Asi says: Even** if one **hired** the laborer **to harvest only one cluster of grapes for him,** one **violates the prohibition of delaying** payment of wages.

The Gemara turns finally to Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda's derivation. He does not use the *sachir sachir* verbal analogy; he derives his distinctions from the verse *lo talin pe'ulat sachir* directly. From that verse, with its various limiting words and expansions, he derives his particular set of inclusions and exclusions: the *ger toshav* is included in *beyomo titein sacharo* but not *lo talin*, and *behema ve-keilim* are restricted to *bal ta'ashok* alone.

### Rav Assi Principle

But there is one further use Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda makes of the verse *lo talin pe'ulat sachir itcha ad boker*: he uses it as the source for Rav Assi's principle. Rav Assi teaches — *afilu lo sacharo ela livtzor lo eshkol echad shel anavim over mishum bal talin* — even if one hired a worker only to pick a single cluster of grapes, the prohibition of *bal talin* applies in full. The obligation is not proportional to the scope of the task; even the most minimal engagement triggers the same *mitzvah*.

### Work Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ: מִ״וְאֵלָיו הוּא נֹשֵׂא אֶת נַפְשׁוֹ״ נָפְקָא, דָּבָר הַמּוֹסֵר נַפְשׁוֹ עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** from where does **the other** Sage, i.e., the first *tanna*, derive this *halakha*? The Gemara answers: He **derives** it **from** the phrase: **"For he sets his soul upon it"** (Deuteronomy 24:15). This indicates that one is liable for delaying the payment of wages due for any work; as a laborer obligates himself to perform the work, it is **something for which he gives his soul.**

The conceptual grounding for Rav Assi's principle is expressed in the verse itself: *ve'elav hu nosei et nafsho* — "and to it [the wage] he carries his soul." Any task into which a person invests himself — *davar ha-moser nafsho alav* — any work that one genuinely puts oneself into, however brief, generates the full set of obligations on the employer's side. The investment of one's effort and livelihood into a task, no matter how small, is sufficient to invoke the Torah's full protection of the worker's wages.

Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda derives this same principle from a different verse — *lo ta'ashok sachir ani va-evyon* — "do not oppress a poor and destitute hired worker." He therefore has no need to use *ki ani hu* for the priority rule, freeing the *Tanna Kamma* to use it for that purpose while using *ki ani hu* for the exclusion of animals and tools.
