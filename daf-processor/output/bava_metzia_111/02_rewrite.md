# Bava Metzia 111 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hiring via Agent

### Opening & Bal Talin

The Gemara on this daf continues the theme of *bal talin* — the prohibition against withholding wages from workers — which was introduced in the previous Mishnah. The discussion ranges from technical loopholes in liability, to the precise timing of wage obligations, to the multiple Torah prohibitions simultaneously violated by a delinquent employer, and ultimately to the scope of these obligations across different categories of workers, animals, and tools.

### Indirect Hiring

The Gemara opens by citing a Baraita: *ha'omer l'chaveiro tzei sechor li po'alim* — if one instructs a friend to go hire workers on his behalf, *shneihem ein ovrim mishum bal talin*, neither of them has violated *bal talin*. The reasoning is twofold: the *ba'al habayit* did not himself hire the workers (*zeh lefi shelo secharan*), and the agent who did hire them is not liable because the work was not performed on his behalf (*vezeh lefi she'ein pe'ulato etzlo*). The hired workers are, so to speak, disconnected from both parties with respect to the formal obligation. The logic is structurally similar to the principle of agency in other areas of Halakha — including *melacha* performed on *Shabbos* through a proxy — where the fragmentation of a transaction between two parties creates a significant loophole.

The Gemara then clarifies what case the Baraita actually addresses. If the agent had said to the workers *scharchem alai* — that he personally would be paying their wages — then the liability would rest on him. This is established from another Baraita: if one hires a worker ostensibly for his own field and then directs them to work in someone else's field, he must pay the worker his full wages (*nosein lo scharo misheleim*) and then go claim reimbursement from the actual *ba'al habayit* for the benefit that person received — *mah shehe'enato*. Notably, this implies a legal claim against that third party even without any prior agreement, which raises the interesting question of whether a person can truly be bound to pay for a benefit conferred on them without their consent — a principle resembling *yored* into another's property.

The Baraita the Gemara actually needs to teach, however, is the case where the agent said explicitly to the workers: *scharchem al ba'al habayit* — your wages are the obligation of the householder. Even in this case, the *aveira* of *bal talin* evaporates, because the person who structured the payment arrangement is not the person who actually owes the wages. One might ask: since *shlucho shel adam kimo'to* — an agent's actions are legally attributed to the principal — why does this loophole work at all? It is a genuine question, and one the Rishonim presumably address in greater depth. The answer lies in the specificity of the verse underlying *bal talin*: *lo talin pe'ulat sachir itcha ad boker* — "you shall not withhold the wages of a hired worker who is with you." The obligation is person-specific, requiring both that one hired the worker and that the work was done for one's benefit.

### Practical Examples

The Gemara records that Yehuda bar Mareimar would instruct his servant (*shamash*) — *zil ager li po'alim* — to go hire workers for him, and would direct the servant to tell the workers that their wages were the liability of the *ba'al habayit*. Similarly, Mareimar and Mar Zutra would hire workers for each other, each acting as the other's agent, thereby using this loophole to avoid personal exposure to the *aveira* of *bal talin*. This is not presented as a problematic evasion — it is straightforwardly a legitimate legal structure employed by major *Amoraim* who wished to be scrupulous in avoiding even potential violations. Of course, none of this eliminates the underlying financial obligation to pay workers promptly and in full; it addresses only the specific religious prohibition of *bal talin*.

## Wage Payment Timing

### Marketplace Workers

Rabba bar Rav Huna states that the marketplace workers of Sura (*hanei shokai deSura*) do not violate *bal talin* — *lo avrei mishum bal talin* — because *meyedai de'al yoma deshuka smichei*: the workers understand that payment will only come on market days, Mondays and Thursdays, when their employers actually have cash on hand from selling their wares. Since the workers enter the arrangement with this understanding, the normal timing of the *mitzvah* is adjusted to fit the acknowledged terms of employment. However, Rabba bar Rav Huna adds, *aval mishum bal tishe'ha vadai over* — if the employer then withholds wages beyond what is necessary, he does violate the broader prohibition against prolonged withholding, whether that is understood as a rabbinic enactment or derived from the verse in Mishlei. Concretely, if the worker is paid on Thursday for work done the previous Monday, that is permissible given the understood arrangement; but if the employer had funds on Monday and chose not to pay until Thursday, that would constitute *bal tishe'ha*.

### Rav vs Shmuel

The Mishnah's phrase *sachir sha'ot goveh kol halaila vechol hayom* — an hourly worker may collect all night and all day — generates a dispute between Rav and Shmuel. Rav holds: *sachir sha'ot deyom goveh kol hayom* — a daytime hourly worker's wages are collectable throughout the day, i.e., until sunset; *sachir sha'ot delaila goveh kol halaila* — a nighttime hourly worker's wages are collectable throughout the night, i.e., until sunrise. Shmuel agrees regarding the daytime worker — *sachir sha'ot deyom goveh kol hayom* — but holds that a nighttime hourly worker may collect *kol halaila vechol hayom*, throughout the night and the entire following day, until the next sunset.

The question is pointed: why should a nighttime worker be entitled to a longer window of payment under Shmuel's view? Shmuel's position appears to extend the employer's deadline to pay — which might seem more lenient toward the employer — but the Gemara frames this as an interpretation of the relevant *psukim* and how they are applied, rather than as a straightforwardly intuitive rule. One practical rationale might be that one cannot reasonably be expected to collect wages in the middle of the night, or that an employer cannot be disturbed at that hour, so the window effectively runs until the end of business hours the following day.

### Rav's Defense

The Mishnah's language appears to contradict Rav, since the phrase *goveh kol halaila vechol hayom* suggests that a single worker can collect both all night and all day. Rav responds: *letzdadin katani* — the Mishnah speaks to each case separately. The daytime worker collects throughout the day, and the nighttime worker collects throughout the night; the Mishnah is not saying the same worker collects across both periods.

### Extended Terms

A further Baraita challenges Rav: *haya sachir shabbat, sachir chodesh, sachir shana, sachir shavua* — whether one was hired by the week, month, year, or septennate — *yotze bayom goveh kol hayom, yotze balayla goveh kol halaila*, if the term concluded during the day the employer must pay by sunset, if during the night then by sunrise. Yet the Baraita continues *ve-chol hayom* — "and throughout the day" — which seems to contradict Rav by extending the nighttime worker's window into the following day. Rav responds: *tanai hi* — this is actually a dispute among *Tannaim*, and his disagreement with Shmuel mirrors a pre-existing Tannaitic disagreement. A Baraita confirms this: *sachir sha'ot deyom goveh kol hayom, sachir sha'ot delaila goveh kol halaila* — these are the words of Rabbi Yehuda, whose position matches Rav's; *Rabbi Shimon omer, sachir sha'ot deyom goveh kol hayom, sachir sha'ot delaila goveh kol halaila vechol hayom* — Rabbi Shimon extends the nighttime worker's window, matching Shmuel. The *machlokes* between Rav and Shmuel is thus a reflection of the *machlokes* between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon, grounded in competing *drashot* on the relevant *psukim*.

A broader point emerges here: the *mitzvah* of *bal talin* is not coextensive with the ethical and financial obligation to pay workers. There are cases where one can be technically exempt from *bal talin* while still being absolutely obligated to pay — and conversely, the specific rules of timing within *bal talin* sometimes appear almost formal, even arbitrary in their structure. The *shokai deSura* example illustrates that it is the terms of the understood agreement, not a mechanical clock, that determine when the obligation kicks in.

## Five Prohibitions

### Chamesh Shemot

The Gemara now draws a broader conclusion from this whole discussion: *kol hakovesh sachar sachir over b-chamisha shemot* — whoever withholds a hired worker's wages violates *chamesh shemot*, five names, meaning five *lo ta'asei* and one *mitzvat asei*, for a total of six prohibitions. These are: *mishum bal ta'ashok et reacha* (do not cheat your friend), *mishum bal tigzol* (do not steal), *mishum bal ta'ashok sachir ani* (do not cheat a poor hired laborer), *mishum bal talin* (do not withhold wages overnight for a day worker), *mishum beyomo titein sacharo* (pay on the same day — the positive commandment), and *mishum lo tavo alav hashemesh* (do not let the sun set on unpaid wages for a nighttime worker). The Gemara notes that *bal talin* and *beyomo titein sacharo* do not apply simultaneously in every case: *hanei de-ika beyomama leika belayla, de-ika belayla leika beyomama* — one applies to day workers and the other to night workers. Rav Chisda therefore clarifies: *shem sechirut be'alma* — the list describes all the prohibitions associated with the general category of hiring workers; not every individual case will trigger all six simultaneously.

### Oshek vs Gezel

This leads to a fundamental question: what is the difference between *oshek* (cheating or oppression) and *gezel* (stealing)? Rav Chisda explains: *lech vashuv lech vashuv* — if one repeatedly tells a worker to come back later and keeps putting him off, that is *oshek*; *yesh lacha biyadi ve'eini noten lacha* — if one openly tells the worker "I have your money and I'm not giving it to you," that is *gezel*.

### Oshek/Gezel Debate

Rav Sheshet challenges Rav Chisda's formulation. Both *oshek* and *gezel* appear in the Torah in the context of the *korban asham* for monetary violations, alongside *pikadon*, and the *halacha* requires that they all share a common structure: the offender must have actively denied the claim (*dekafar lei mamona*), in parallel to the denial involved in a *pikadon* case. Under Rav Chisda's definition, telling someone "come back tomorrow" does not involve a denial — so it cannot be the operative case for the Torah's *oshek*. Rav Sheshet therefore offers a different distinction: *natati lach* — "I already paid you" — is *oshek*; *yesh lacha biyadi ve'eini noten lacha* — "I have it and won't give it" — is *gezel*. In Rav Sheshet's formulation, both involve a denial, but the nature of the denial differs.

Abaye then challenges Rav Sheshet: the same objection applies to *gezel* — *gezel* also requires a denial similar to *pikadon* to trigger the *korban asham* liability. *Be'inan dekafar lei mamona* — you need a denial. Abaye therefore proposes: *lo secharticha me'olam* — "I never hired you in the first place" — is *oshek*, since you are denying the employment relationship itself; *netati lacha* — "I already paid you your wages" — is *gezel*, since you are denying an obligation that you in fact incurred.

The further give-and-take examines why Rav Sheshet only demanded the *pikadon*-style denial for *oshek* and not for *gezel*. His answer is that *gezel* involves a two-stage act: one first takes the object wrongfully (*gezel degazlei*) and then denies it (*vehadar kafrei*) — the act of taking is primary, and the denial is secondary. The Gemara then asks: but isn't *oshek* the same? Rav Sheshet responds by pointing to the linguistic form of the verse: by *gezel* the Torah uses a nominal form (*o begezel*, "the stolen object"), implying prior acknowledgment that the object existed and was taken; by *oshek* the Torah uses an active verbal form (*o ashak*, "he oppressed"), implying that the act of cheating is itself the operative moment. This textual distinction is what allows Rav Sheshet to treat the two differently.

### Rava's Position

Rava cuts through the debate entirely: *zehu oshek zehu gezel* — *oshek* and *gezel* are the same thing. Why then does the Torah mention them twice using different words? *Velama chalkan hakatuv la'avor alav bishnei lavin* — the Torah split them in order to create a double prohibition. One who withholds a worker's wages violates both simultaneously. This is structurally parallel to *bal yera'eh* and *bal yimatze* — the two prohibitions against owning *chametz* on *Pesach*, which overlap entirely in content but create separate *lavin*. According to Rava, there is no meaningful legal distinction between *oshek* and *gezel*: any act of withholding or denying what rightfully belongs to another person constitutes both simultaneously. This means that the distinction Abaye and Rav Sheshet were laboring to construct — which type of denial corresponds to which term — is irrelevant, since both terms cover any denial of liability. Whether the question concerns the *korban asham* specifically is a separate matter; Rava likely would still agree that a *korban asham* requires a *pikadon*-style denial, but holds that as a general matter, the two categories are fungible.

## Animals & Tools

### Rental Obligations

The next Mishnah extends the discussion of *bal talin* to additional cases: *echad sachar adam ve-echad sachar behema ve-echad sachar kelim yesh bo mishum beyomo titein sacharo* — whether one hires a person, rents an animal, or rents tools, all of these carry the obligation of *beyomo titein sacharo*. One must pay on time for rental of inanimate objects just as for human labor. Similarly, *veyesh bo mishum lo talin pe'ulat sachir itcha ad boker* — the prohibition against withholding overnight applies equally across all three categories.

### Conditions for Exemption

The Mishnah qualifies this: *eimatai? bizman sheta'avo* — when does the obligation kick in? Only when the worker or owner makes a claim. If the worker never makes a claim for his wages — *lo teva'o* — then the employer has not technically violated the *mitzvah*, since the obligation is triggered by the demand. If the employer arranges credit for the worker with a *chenvani* (shopkeeper) or *shulchani* (money-changer) — *hamamcheihu etzel chenvani ve'etzel shulchani eino over alav* — so that the worker can purchase goods against his anticipated wages, this also exempts the employer from *bal talin*. The arrangement functions like a paycheck advance: as long as the worker has practical access to the value of his wages, the employer is not in violation.

### Worker's Oath

*Sachir bizmano nishba venotel* — a worker claiming wages within the appropriate time period may take a rabbinic oath and collect his wages on that basis. Once the time period has passed — *avar zemano, eino nishba venotel* — the worker can no longer use the oath to collect. However, if there are witnesses that he had already made a claim during the relevant period — *im yesh eidim sheta'avo, harei zeh nishba venotel* — he retains the ability to collect via oath even later. The worker's credibility to take the oath is thus grounded either in the timeliness of his claim or in witnessed prior demand.

## Ger Toshav Status

### Ger Toshav

The Mishnah addresses the *ger toshav* — the resident alien, a non-Jew who has accepted certain obligations (presumably the *sheva mitzvot benei Noach*) and thereby acquires a recognized legal status within the Jewish legal system. The Mishnah states: *yesh bo mishum beyomo titein sacharo* — if one hires a *ger toshav*, the positive obligation to pay on time applies; *ve-ein bo mishum lo talin pe'ulat sachir itcha ad boker* — but the negative prohibition of withholding wages overnight does not apply. The reason, as will emerge from the *drashot*, has to do with which *psukim* use the word *re'acha* (your friend) and which do not.

### Attribution Question

The Gemara asks: *mani matnitin?* — whose position does this Mishnah represent? It cannot be the *Tanna Kamma* (*lo tanna kamma de-me'akhikha*) and it cannot be Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda. The *Tanna Kamma* excludes the *ger toshav* entirely from both obligations based on the word *me'achicha* (from your brother), which limits the protections to fellow Jews. If the Mishnah were the *Tanna Kamma*, the inclusion of the *ger toshav* even for *beyomo titein sacharo* would be a contradiction. And if the Mishnah were Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda, the inclusion of animals and tools would be a contradiction, since Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda holds that *behema ve-keilim* are governed only by *bal ta'ashok*, not by the timing-based prohibitions.

### Baraita Sources

A Baraita is cited to lay out the three Tannaitic positions. The *Tanna Kamma* reads: *me'achicha, perat le-acherim* — "from your brother," excluding non-Jews. *Gerkha* — the word "your stranger" — excludes the *ger toshav* specifically, meaning the one who is *okhel nevelot* (permitted to eat non-kosher meat), which is the Talmudic characterization of the *ger toshav*'s status. From where do we know to extend the obligation to animals and tools? *Talmud lomar be'artzakha* — "in your land," meaning anything within the domain of your property or your land is included: *kol she-be'artzakha*. And the Baraita concludes: *vekhulan overim bekhol ha-shemot ha-lalu* — all of these categories, humans, animals, and tools, violate all the relevant prohibitions when withheld without pay. This is the source for the Mishnah's opening ruling.

The parenthetical discussion of the *chenvani* arrangement — where the employer sets up credit at a shopkeeper or money-changer for the worker — naturally raises the question of whether such credit is an adequate substitute for cash wages. The *shulchani* arrangement is simpler, since one can simply go and exchange for cash. The *chenvani* arrangement is more restrictive, since it is limited to purchases at that particular store. The Halakha does not appear to limit what categories of goods the worker may purchase, and the *shulchani* option makes cash available directly.

## Tannaim Dispute

### Tanna Kamma

The *Tanna Kamma*'s position is that the *ger toshav* is excluded from *both* obligations — *beyomo titein sacharo* and *lo talin* — by means of a *gezeira shava* of *sachir sachir*. The word *sachir* appears in the context where the verse says *me'achicha*, "from your brother," limiting the scope of the hired worker to whom these laws apply; the *Tanna Kamma* uses the verbal analogy to extend that exclusion across all instances of the word *sachir* in the relevant *psukim*, thereby removing the *ger toshav* from the protections of both the positive and negative commandments.

### Rabbi Yosei Position

Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda does not accept the *gezeira shava* of *sachir sachir* at all. His position is nuanced: a *ger toshav* retains the obligation of *beyomo titein sacharo* (the positive commandment to pay on time), but not *lo talin* (the negative prohibition). Conversely, for *behema ve-keilim*, only *bal ta'ashok* applies — not *beyomo titein sacharo* or *bal talin*. Since he does not employ the *sachir sachir* verbal analogy, his distinctions require separate derivation.

### Rabbi Yishmael School

Rava resolves the attribution question: *amar Rava, hai tanna, tanna de-vei Rebbi Yishmael hee* — the Mishnah follows the school of Rabbi Yishmael. It is worth noting that while most of our Mishnayot reflect the tradition of Rabbi Akiva's students, the school of Rabbi Yishmael developed its own distinct methodology of *drashot* and *halachic* derivation. A *Tanna de-vei Rebbi Yishmael* is often cited anonymously and occupies a somewhat distinctive position within the Tannaitic corpus. The Baraita from Rabbi Yishmael's school states explicitly: humans, animals, and tools all carry *beyomo titein sacharo* and *lo talin*; but a *ger toshav* has *beyomo titein sacharo* and not *lo talin*.

## Exclusions Logic

### Ki Ani Hu

The question now is: what is the *Tanna de-vei Rebbi Yishmael*'s source for his two-part position — including *behema ve-keilim* while excluding the *ger toshav* from *bal talin*? The Gemara works through this carefully. The *Tanna de-vei Rebbi Yishmael* does not use the *sachir sachir* verbal analogy. Instead, he derives the basic scope of *bal talin* from the verse *velo talin pe'ulat sachir itcha ad boker* — "do not withhold the work of a hired laborer who is with you until morning." From the phrase *kol she-pe'ulato itcha* — anyone whose work is with you — he derives a broad inclusion: any worker whose labor is performed on your behalf is covered.

### Rabbi Chananya

Rabbi Chananya draws on a different element of the verse: *velo tavo alav hashemesh ki ani hu* — "and the sun shall not set on him, for he is poor." The phrase *ki ani hu* specifies that the prohibition addresses someone capable of experiencing poverty: *mi she-hein ba'in lidei aniyut va-ashirut* — entities that can be poor or rich. Animals and tools cannot become poor; therefore the verse's reasoning excludes them from the literal ambit of *ki ani hu*. Moreover, the owner of an animal available for hire presumably already possesses some assets, giving him a degree of financial stability that the verse's poverty-concern does not obviously reach.

### Priority Principle

The *Tanna Kamma* uses *ki ani hu* differently — not to exclude animals and tools, but to establish a priority rule: if an employer has two workers to pay, one poor and one wealthy, he must pay the poor worker first. This derives from the verse's explicit identification of the worker's poverty as the rationale for the obligation's urgency.

Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda, meanwhile, derives this same priority principle from a different verse — *lo ta'ashok sachir ani va-evyon* — "do not oppress a poor and destitute hired worker." He therefore has no need to use *ki ani hu* for the priority rule, freeing the *Tanna Kamma* to use it for that purpose while using *ki ani hu* for the exclusion of animals and tools.

The *Tanna Kamma* also extends the priority principle by reading *ani* and *evyon* as two distinct degrees of poverty. The Gemara then explains why both are needed (*tzricha*): one might have thought the very poor (*evyon*) need not be explicitly protected because they are unashamed to make their wage claims loudly — *de-lo kasif le-mitba* — and therefore already at lower risk of being exploited through delay. The wealthy worker (*ashir*), by contrast, may be embarrassed to press his claim — *kasif le-mitba* — and might therefore need explicit protection. And one might have thought the wealthy worker can be passed over because he does not urgently need the money — *de-lo tzarikh lei*. Each case requires explicit derivation.

### Psychology of Payment

This discussion surfaces a genuine psychological observation embedded in the *halacha*: the worker who most urgently needs the money may in fact be less likely to advocate for himself due to internalized social norms around dignity and deference, while the wealthier worker may actually be more comfortable pressing his claim — yet also less urgently in need. The *Tanna Kamma* addresses both poles. The very poor worker (*evyon*) is unashamed to demand his wages and is urgently in need; the merely poor (*ani*) may have somewhat more dignity to maintain. Each deserves protection on different grounds, making both derivations necessary.

## Reconciliation

### Apparent Contradictions

The Gemara now presses on the internal consistency of the *Tanna de-vei Rebbi Yishmael*'s position: *uman nafshakh* — whichever way you look at it, there is a difficulty. If he learns the *gezeira shava sachir sachir*, he should include the *ger toshav* in *bal talin* alongside animals and tools. If he does not learn the *gezeira shava*, then his basis for including animals and tools is unclear. How does he hold both positions simultaneously?

### Peulato Itcha

The Gemara resolves this: *le'olam lo yalif sachir sachir* — the *Tanna de-vei Rebbi Yishmael* never employs the *sachir sachir* verbal analogy. Instead, from *velo talin pe'ulat sachir itcha ad boker*, he derives through the phrase *pe'ulato itcha* — "whose work is with you" — that the obligation extends to any rental or hire arrangement where work or productive use is performed on your behalf, encompassing *behema ve-keilim*.

### Recha Exclusion

Then, from the word *re'acha* that appears in the relevant verse — *re'acha*, your friend, referring to a fellow Jew — he excludes the *ger toshav* from the negative prohibition of *lo talin*. The *ger toshav* is not fully within the category of *re'acha*, and therefore, while many obligations apply to dealings with him, the *lo ta'asei* of *bal talin* is not among them.

### Mamon Recha

Yet *behema ve-keilim*, though inanimate, are included — not because they themselves are *re'acha*, but because they fall under *mamon recha*, the property of one's friend. The rented animal or borrowed tools belong to a fellow Jew, and therefore the obligation to pay promptly for their use falls under the same penumbra of *re'acha* that generates the *bal talin* prohibition. The *ger toshav* himself, by contrast, is not within that penumbra: *eino bikhlal mamon re'acha*. The result is a principled, if subtle, distinction: it is not the entity performing the work that is decisive, but whose property is at stake and whose interests the *pasuk*'s language is designed to protect.

This means that the *lo ta'asei* of *bal talin* does not apply when a *ger toshav* is hired, but the *mitzvat asei* of *beyomo titein sacharo* still does. The practical difference is primarily one of formal category — *asei* versus *lav*, and the accompanying difference in severity — rather than a practical permission to delay payment. The *ger toshav* must still be paid on time; the violation, when it occurs, is assessed differently.

## Amaleki Exclusion

### Tanna Kamma Recha

If the *Tanna Kamma* has already excluded the *ger toshav* from *bal talin* through the *gezeira shava* of *sachir sachir* — derived from the word *me'achicha* — what then does he do with the word *re'acha*? He has no need of *re'acha* for the *ger toshav* exclusion, since that is handled elsewhere. So what additional work does *re'acha* perform in his system?

### Amaleki Exclusion

The Gemara answers: *re'acha ve-lo Amaleki* — "your friend, but not an Amalekite." The *Tanna Kamma* uses *re'acha* to teach that an *Amaleki* does not receive the protections of *lo talin*. This is a striking and unusual *drasha*, one that appears rarely if at all elsewhere. The *Amaleki* presents a specific textual problem: because Amalek descended from Esau, he would technically be included in *me'achicha* — "from your brother" — since Esau was Yaakov's brother, making Amalekites genealogical relatives of Israel. Without an additional exclusion, the word *me'achicha* might include them; therefore *re'acha* is used to exclude them specifically.

There is an obvious practical question: is there ever a realistic scenario in which a Jew hires an *Amaleki* as a laborer? The institution of fighting Amalek is a rule of war authorized by the *beit din* and the king, not an individual obligation to harm every person of Amalekite descent on sight. Within the ordinary labor market, however unlikely, the *halacha* addresses the case. The *drasha* thus serves primarily to close a logical gap in the textual analysis rather than to establish a widely applicable rule.

### Oshek/Gezel Drasha

The verse further supplies a double exclusion — *re'acha* used twice, once for *oshek* and once for *gezel* — *chad le-mishra oshko ve-chad le-mishra gozlo*. One application authorizes, in the formal Halakhic sense of the *beit din*'s jurisdiction, the non-application of the *oshek* prohibition regarding an *Amaleki* (or, in broader readings, a non-Jew), and the other authorizes the non-application of the *gezel* prohibition. The Gemara explains why both are needed: one might have thought that while stealing from a non-Jew is tolerated within the *halachic* jurisdictional framework (*de-lo tarach bei* — he didn't work hard to earn it, or *de-ati lidei* — it already came into his possession), cheating him out of wages he earned through labor would still be prohibited, or vice versa. Both cases therefore require explicit derivation.

### Meiri Principle

This is precisely the context in which the *Meiri*'s well-known principle becomes relevant. The *Meiri* holds that the formal permissions — where they exist within the *halachic* system — apply only to members of societies without the rule of law, not to peoples who govern themselves by ethical norms and legal structures. Members of civilized societies committed to law and order (*geder ha-dat*) are entitled to the same protections as Jews within the *halachic* system. This principle significantly softens the practical import of these *drashot*, which may in any case reflect a censored or euphemistic text (often *akum* or *nochri* in other manuscripts, or *Notzri* in some readings), rather than the specific category of Amalek per se. The broader jurisdictional understanding of these passages — that *halacha* governs obligations between Jews before a Jewish court, while non-Jews have recourse to their own legal systems — also serves to contextualize the scope of these rules.

## Rabbi Yosei Sources

### Lo Talin Source

The Gemara turns finally to Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda's derivation. He does not use the *sachir sachir* verbal analogy; he derives his distinctions from the verse *lo talin pe'ulat sachir* directly. From that verse, with its various limiting words and expansions, he derives his particular set of inclusions and exclusions: the *ger toshav* is included in *beyomo titein sacharo* but not *lo talin*, and *behema ve-keilim* are restricted to *bal ta'ashok* alone.

### Rav Assi Principle

But there is one further use Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda makes of the verse *lo talin pe'ulat sachir itcha ad boker*: he uses it as the source for Rav Assi's principle. Rav Assi teaches — *afilu lo sacharo ela livtzor lo eshkol echad shel anavim over mishum bal talin* — even if one hired a worker only to pick a single cluster of grapes, the prohibition of *bal talin* applies in full. The obligation is not proportional to the scope of the task; even the most minimal engagement triggers the same *mitzvah*. Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda derives this from the verse, and Rav Assi's formulation preserves that derivation in later Amoraic teaching.

### Work Principle

The conceptual grounding for Rav Assi's principle is expressed in the verse itself: *ve'elav hu nosei et nafsho* — "and to it [the wage] he carries his soul." Any task into which a person invests himself — *davar ha-moser nafsho alav* — any work that one genuinely puts oneself into, however brief, generates the full set of obligations on the employer's side. The investment of one's effort and livelihood into a task, no matter how small, is sufficient to invoke the Torah's full protection of the worker's wages.
