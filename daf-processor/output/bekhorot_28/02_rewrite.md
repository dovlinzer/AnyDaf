# Bekhorot 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bechor's First Year

### Intro & Overview

The daf opens three lines down from the top, with the word *ve-idach*, picking up a discussion that had been underway regarding the *mishna*'s ruling that a *bechor* must be brought within the first year — or, if it is a *ba'al mum*, eaten within the first year. As the *Gemara* in Rosh Hashanah makes clear, this does not mean the animal becomes *pasul* after the first year. Unlike other animals where the validity of the *korban* depends on the animal's age, the requirement here governs the obligation of the owners: they must bring it within the first year. This distinction prompted *Tosafos* to raise the question of whether *bal te'acher* — the prohibition against postponing one's *korbanos* for a year — would already cover this case. *Tosafos* resolves the difficulty by noting that, depending on how the calendar falls, one might not have three *regalim* occur within the animal's first year, and since *bal te'acher* is defined by the passage of three *regalim*, the independent obligation of the first year is not redundant. The essential point, however, remains: this rule is about the owner's obligation, not the animal's validity.

### Obligation vs. Validity

The *Gemara* derives this obligation from the *pasuk* of *shanah b'shanah* — "year by year." The ensuing discussion turns to what each side does with additional *pesukim*, since *shanah b'shanah* had already been used to teach that the *bechor* may be eaten for two days and the intervening night.

### Shlamim vs. Todah

The *Gemara* raises the question of why the eating period is two days and a night rather than one day and a night, as with a *todah*. One view holds that the comparison is to *shlamim*. Another position counters that the phrase *yiheyeh lach* — "it shall be yours" — implies something additional, and therefore the eating period extends like *shlamim* rather than *todah*. The opposing view responds that *yiheyeh lach* might not be teaching the duration of eating at all. Instead, it may be coming to teach us that even when the *bechor* develops a blemish, it is still given to the *kohen* — a novel principle that the *Gemara* notes is not found anywhere else in the *Torah*: *she-lo matzinu b'chol ha-Torah kulah*.

### Blemished Bechor Status

*Tosafos* explains why this phrase is deployed here with particular emphasis. Normally when we say "we haven't found this anywhere else in the Torah," it simply means the *pasuk* is teaching something new. But here the phrase is meant to convey something genuinely exceptional. The novelty is this: a *ba'al mum* — a blemished *bechor* — is eaten outside the *Beis HaMikdash*, without being redeemed, while retaining its *kedushah*, and is still given to the *kohen*. This is highly unusual; the only parallel is *ma'aser*. Because this *chiddush* is so fundamental, the *Gemara* argues that *yiheyeh lach* is needed to teach it, and cannot simultaneously be used to derive the two-day eating period.

The *Gemara* then presents the counter-response: the verse *u-vesaram yiheyeh lach* uses the plural form — "their flesh shall be yours" — which implies *echad tam v'echad ba'al mum*, whether unblemished or blemished, the *kohanim* receive it. From the plural *u-vesaram* alone one can derive that a blemished *bechor* is given to the *kohen* without redemption, thus freeing *yiheyeh lach* for another purpose. The reply to this is that *u-vesaram* simply refers to all *bechoros* of the Jewish people collectively, not to two distinct categories of blemished and unblemished animals.

### Two Days & Night

The discussion settles several foundational *halachos* of *bechor*. First, it must be brought within the first year, measured from the animal's birth rather than by the calendar year, and if brought after a year it remains *kosher*. Second, even with a blemish, the *kohen* eats it without redemption, outside the *Beis HaMikdash*, while its *kedushah* remains intact. Third, it is eaten for two days and the intervening night.

## Blemish & Extensions

### Mishna's Ambiguity

The *mishna* then addresses what happens when a *bechor* develops a blemish, and the question of how much time the owner retains. The *mishna* states: *nolad lo mum b'soch shnaso rashai l'kaymo kol shneim asar chodesh* — if the *bechor* develops a blemish during the first year, one may keep it for the full twelve months. The *mishna* then adds that afterwards one may keep it for thirty days. The precise meaning of this is ambiguous, and the *Gemara* sets out to clarify it.

### Two Readings

The *Gemara* presents two possible readings. The first reading: if the blemish develops during the year, one has the full twelve months — *and* an additional thirty days after the year ends. The second reading: if the blemish develops during the year, one has only until the end of the twelve months and no more. If the blemish develops after the year — meaning one had no choice but to wait — then one has thirty days from that point, and no more.

The logic strongly favors the second reading. If the blemish appears early in the year, say in the first month, there is no obvious rationale for granting an additional thirty days on top of the full year. The sensible principle is: one has until the end of the year, *or* thirty days from when the blemish developed, whichever comes later. The *Gemara* then introduces a *baraisa* to explore these readings further.

### Modern Practice

The *baraisa* introduces the modern context: *bechor b'zman hazeh* — nowadays, when the *bechor* cannot be brought as a *korban*. In this situation one has no choice but to wait for the animal to develop a blemish. Since the animal must wait for a *mum* in any case, the clock cannot begin running until one has both a blemish and the opportunity to present it to an expert for inspection.

### Waiting for Blemish

The *baraisa* states: *ad shelo nira lehar'oso lechacham* — before the animal has developed a blemish and there is someone available to inspect it — *rashai l'kaymo shtayim veshalosh shanim*, one may hold onto it for two, three years. *Tosafos* points out that this would be almost tautological if it simply meant the animal has no blemish yet, since there is nothing to do with it in any case. Rather, *Tosafos* understands the phrase to mean that even when a blemish has appeared, if no qualified expert is immediately available, one is not required to travel great distances or expend significant effort to find one. One may wait however long it takes — two or three years if necessary — until an expert becomes accessible. This is an important ruling: the obligation does not require extraordinary measures to arrange inspection.

### Hashavas Aveida

Once a blemish has developed and an expert is available, the *baraisa* distinguishes between two scenarios. If the blemish developed during the year: *rashai l'kaymo kol yud-bet chodesh*, one has until the end of the twelve months. If after the year: *eino rashai l'kaymo afilu yom echad*, technically one should not even have a single day, since the year has already passed. Nevertheless, *mipnei hashavas aveida leba'alim* — because of concern for returning loss to the owners — the rabbis granted thirty additional days.

### Rashi vs. Tosafot

*Rashi* and *Tosafos* differ on what *hashavas aveida* means in this context. *Rashi* understands it as a case where no *kohen* is available to receive the animal. If one were required to *shecht* it immediately with no *kohen* available, the meat would rot and the *kohanim* would receive nothing. The thirty days therefore allow time to locate a *kohen*, protecting the *kohen*'s interests.

*Tosafos* describes a less extreme scenario. The *mishna* already provides that the *yisrael* gives the animal to the *kohen* after a month or two, so the *kohen* is presumably already in possession. The *hashavas aveida* concern for *Tosafos* is simply that the *kohen* may not need thirty pounds of meat at that very moment. Requiring immediate *shechita* would force him to absorb a needless loss. Granting thirty days allows the *kohen* to arrange the sale or consumption of the meat in a way that minimizes waste. This is accordingly a lower threshold — the *kohen* could *shecht* it right away, but the thirty days give him the opportunity to make the most of it.

### 15-Day Proof

The *Gemara* then introduces a proof case to resolve the ambiguity. A *baraisa* states: *nolad lo mum b'chamisha asar yom b'toch shnaso* — if the blemish developed with fifteen days remaining in the year — *mashlimin lo chamisha asar yom achar shnaso*, one receives those fifteen days plus an additional fifteen days after the year, completing the thirty. This case makes the principle clear. If the blemish had appeared early in the year, granting a year and thirty days would be excessive. If it appeared after the year, thirty days are granted from that point. If it appeared with fifteen days remaining, one receives fifteen days plus fifteen more. The operative rule is thus: thirty days from when the blemish developed, *or* until the end of the year, whichever comes later. *Shma mina* — this is the conclusion.

### Rabbi Eliezer

The *Gemara* notes that this ruling supports *Rabbi Eliezer*, who said: *notnin lo shloshim yom mishaa shenolad bo mum* — thirty days are given from the time the blemish develops. This formulation means that if the thirty days extends past the end of the year, one benefits from those days; if the year ends first, the year is the limit. This is precisely the principle established by the *baraisa*.

### Alt. Rabbi Eliezer

However, there is an alternative tradition of *Rabbi Eliezer*'s position. This version states: how do we know that if the blemish developed during the year, one receives thirty days *after* the year — i.e., a full year plus thirty days regardless? *Rabbi Eliezer* derives it from the phrase *shanah b'shanah*: "year by year." He asks, what constitutes a halachically significant "year" in some contexts? Thirty days — since we sometimes treat thirty days of a year as symbolically representing the entire year. Therefore *shanah b'shanah* implies two years, with the second "year" consisting of at least thirty days, granting everyone fundamentally a year and a month.

The *Gemara* challenges this position with the *baraisa*: if the blemish developed fifteen days before the end of the year, one receives *mashlimin* — the remaining fifteen days are completed up to thirty, not an entirely new thirty-day period. *Hashlama in, mi-hava lo* — making up the thirty days, yes; granting an additional thirty on top of a full year, no. This constitutes a *teyuvta* — a decisive refutation — of *Rabbi Eliezer*'s second formulation.

The practical upshot is this: nowadays, since the animal cannot be offered as a *korban*, one waits for a blemish to appear. Once it does, and an expert is accessible, the time allotted is until the end of the year or at least thirty days, whichever comes later — granted by the rabbis as a concession to prevent undue loss.

## Pre-Shechita Issues

### Before vs. After

The *Gemara* turns to the next *mishna*, which concerns what happens when a *bechor* is *shechted* before its blemish is inspected by a qualified expert. The *mishna* presents two positions. *Rabbi Yehuda* permits relying on an inspection conducted after the *shechita*: if the blemish is then determined to be genuine, the animal may be eaten. *Rabbi Meir* forbids it: *ho'il v'nishchat shelo al pi mumche assur* — since it was *shechted* without the ruling of an expert, it is forbidden regardless of what the post-*shechita* inspection reveals.

### Non-Expert Ruling

The *mishna* then presents a separate case: *mi she'eino mumche shera'ah es habechor v'nishchat al piv* — someone who is not a qualified expert examined the *bechor* and it was *shechted* on the basis of that examination. In such a case, *harei zeh yikaver* — the animal must be buried — and the person who gave the ruling must compensate the owner from his own assets for the loss caused by his unauthorized ruling. The *Gemara* notes that the instruction to bury it applies regardless of what a subsequent inspection might reveal. This is because the *Gemara* reads this ruling as consistent with *Rabbi Meir*'s position that *shechita* on the basis of an unauthorized ruling is entirely illegitimate.

### Eye vs. Body Blemish

*Rabba bar bar Chana* offers a key clarification. Regarding *dukin she'ba'ayin* — cataracts of the eye — *chulei alma lo pligi*, everyone agrees it is forbidden to rely on a post-*shechita* inspection, because such blemishes *mishtanin* — they change at or around the moment of death. A ruling made after *shechita* therefore cannot tell us what the eye looked like before. By contrast, *mumim she'beguf* — blemishes on the body — do not change at the moment of death, and a post-*shechita* inspection reliably reflects the animal's pre-*shechita* condition. The debate between *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Yehuda* therefore concerns only this latter category.

### Rabbi Meir's Gezeirah

*Rabbi Meir* holds: *gazrinan mumim she'beguf atu dokin she'ba'ayin* — we make a *gezeira* that if we permit reliance on post-*shechita* inspection for body blemishes, it will lead people to do the same in cases of eye cataracts, where it is invalid. Therefore *Rabbi Meir* prohibits even the legitimate cases.

### Rabbi Yehuda View

*Rabbi Yehuda* disagrees: *lo gazrinan mumim she'beguf atu dokin she'ba'ayin* — we do not extend the prohibition from one category to the other. Since body blemishes provide a reliable basis for ruling, we permit the post-*shechita* inspection in those cases without concern that it will be confused with the eye-blemish scenario.

One might ask why *Rabbi Meir* does not frame his concern differently — namely, as a *knas* against the irresponsible behavior of *shechting* without prior inspection, rather than as a *gezeira* against confusion between cases. Indeed, allowing someone to salvage the situation after the fact by inspecting post-*shechita* could be seen as encouraging the bad behavior of not seeking inspection beforehand. Yet the *Gemara* frames *Rabbi Meir*'s rationale as a *gezeira* about the slippery slope from body blemishes to eye blemishes, not as a penalty for procedural negligence.

### Slippery Slope

This distinction carries significant practical weight. The *Gemara* elsewhere establishes that *halacha k'Rabbi Meir b'gezeirotav* — we rule like *Rabbi Meir* when he makes a *gezeira*.

### Knas Analysis

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* notes that the language of the *mishna* — *ho'il v'nishchat shelo al pi mumche assur* — suggests that what is driving *Rabbi Meir* is the violation of procedure: the animal was *shechted* without an expert ruling. This language implies a *knas*, a fine for wrongdoing, rather than a *gezeira* designed to prevent confusion between cases. *Tosafos* draws a sharp distinction between the two: *b'gezeirotav velo b'knasav* — we rule like *Rabbi Meir* when he makes a *gezeira*, but not when he imposes a *knas*. Since the *Gemara* here frames the ruling as a *knas*, *Tosafos* concludes that we do not rule like *Rabbi Meir* in this instance. The question of whether *Rabbi Meir* is making a *gezeira* or a *knas* is therefore not only a matter of conceptual clarity but has direct implications for the *halacha*.

### Variability Question

The *Gemara* then raises a further question: when we say that *dokin she'ba'ayin* change after death, do we mean all such blemishes change, or only some?

### Witness Testimony

The practical stakes of this question concern the reliability of witness testimony. If all *dokin she'ba'ayin* change, then witnesses who testify that the blemish looked exactly the same before *shechita* as after must be lying — *shakrinei hinhu* — since we know that change is universal. In that case, a *chacham* could never accurately assess the pre-*shechita* state of the eye, and no reliable ruling could be made even with witness support.

### Some Change, Some Don't

However, if some change and some do not, then witness testimony can be trusted in the cases where no change occurred, and the *chacham* can issue a valid ruling based on what he observes combined with what the witnesses attest.

### Rabbi Yoshiya Demo

*Rabba bar bar Chana* reports: *sach li Rabbi Yoshiya demin Osha* — *Rabbi Yoshiya* of *Osha* told me: *bo va'arecha be-dukin she'ein mishtanin* — come, let me show you cases of *dokin* where the blemish does not change. The *Gemara* infers from this — *miklal de'ika demishtanei ve'ika delo mishtanei* — that some change and some do not, since he was pointing out specific cases as examples rather than demonstrating a universal phenomenon. This resolves the question: witnesses can be trusted, and *chacham* rulings can be made reliably in those cases where the blemish is known not to change.

This passage touches on a broader and genuinely striking question about the relationship between *halacha* and empirically observed facts. The *Gemara*'s willingness to declare witnesses unreliable — *lachushei sahadi* — if their testimony contradicts the received understanding of how *dokin she'ba'ayin* behave reflects a general disposition toward authoritative tradition over direct observation. This same issue surfaces in the *Rashba*'s *teshuvah* on the question of *treifos*: if witnesses attest that an animal survived a condition the *Gemara* classifies as fatal, does that revise the list of *treifos*? The *Rashba* holds that such testimony cannot be accepted, because accepting facts that contradict the statements of *Chazal* would undermine the authority of received tradition. This approach is not unique to *halacha*; the pre-modern world broadly privileged authoritative texts over empirical evidence — Galen's anatomical descriptions, for example, were presumed correct even when dissection seemed to suggest otherwise.

A related nuance also surfaces here: the combination of expert assessment and witness testimony. The *chacham* observes the blemish directly after *shechita*, while the witnesses attest that no change occurred. The expert's ruling depends not only on his own observation but on the reliability of testimony he cannot independently verify. This interplay of expertise and testimony — the expert sees what is present now, the witnesses supply the historical fact of continuity — raises interesting questions about the structure of authoritative rulings that will recur throughout the discussion that follows.

## Judicial Liability

### Civil Errors

The *Gemara* now broadens its discussion to the larger question of judicial malpractice. The *mishna* presents the paradigm case: *dan et hadin zika et hachayav ve-chiyav et hazakai* — a judge who ruled that a liable party was exempt, or that an exempt party was liable. Similarly, he may have declared *tamei* what was *tahor*, or *tahor* what was *tamei*. In all such cases, *mah she'asa asuy* — what has been done is done. The ruling is not reversed. Nevertheless, *ve-yeshalem mibeito* — the judge must compensate the injured party from his own assets.

## Compensation Rates

### Quarter vs. Half

A *baraisa* specifies that compensation is not at the full value of the animal. Rather, one pays a quarter of the value for *behema daka* — small grazing animals — and half for *behema gasa* — cattle.

### Not Full Loss

The reason compensation falls short of the full loss is that the *bechor* situation involves inherent uncertainty. Nowadays the animal can only be *shechted* once it develops a *mum*, and it is possible the animal never develops a *mum* at all, or develops one only in old age after significant investment. The actual loss is therefore not equivalent to the full current value of the animal, which is why liability is capped at a fraction.

### Value Difference

*Rav Papa* explains the disparity between the two fractions by noting that cattle represent a far greater absolute loss than small animals — *zeh hefsed merubeh ve-zeh hefsed mu'at*. The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: if the only difference is the absolute magnitude of the loss, one should simply pay half of the relevant amount in each case, and the proportion should be identical. Merely pointing to the difference in absolute value does not explain why the rate itself — a quarter versus a half — differs.

### Grazing Discouragement

*Rav Huna bar Manoach* in the name of *Rav Acha* the son of *Rav Ika* offers the definitive answer: *mishum gezeirat megadlei behema daka nagu bah* — the lower rate for grazing animals reflects the rabbinic prohibition against raising small livestock in settled areas of *Eretz Yisrael*. Grazing animals strip vegetation entirely and can seriously damage the land when kept in one place. Because the institution of raising such animals is disapproved of, the compensation rate is reduced accordingly.

This explanation, however, contains an apparent difficulty: the person who suffers from the lower compensation is not the owner who raised the disapproved animals, but rather the *kohen* — who may well have received the animal legitimately. It is the *chacham* who pays less, and it is the *kohen* whose recovery is reduced. The person who bore no wrongdoing absorbs the consequence of a disapproved practice by someone else.

### Rashi Alt. View

This difficulty may explain why *Rashi* offers an alternative explanation. Rather than grounding the lower rate in the *gezeira* against raising small livestock, *Rashi* argues that the lower rate reflects the lower return on investment that *behema daka* yields in any case. By the time the animal eventually develops a blemish after years of maintenance and expense, the net benefit to the *kohen* would have been considerably less than its current market value suggests. Accordingly, the actual loss the *kohen* sustains is genuinely smaller for grazing animals than for cattle, making the lower proportion fair as a reflection of realistic diminished return. This reading also sidesteps the problem raised by the *gezeira* explanation, since it focuses on what the *kohen* would actually have received rather than on penalizing a practice for which he bears no responsibility.

## Grama & Garmi

### Garmi Definition

This brings the *Gemara* to the general principles of causation in damages. The well-known principle is *grama be-nizikin patur* — indirect causation in damages is exempt from liability. But there is a category identified with *Rabbi Meir* called *garmi*, which occupies an intermediate position: more direct than ordinary *grama*, though still not a fully physical act of damage in the classic sense. *Garmi* is distinguished from *mazik* — direct physical damage — on one end, and from remote *grama* on the other. The *Ramban* devoted an entire *kuntres* to analyzing the *garmi* cases in the *Gemara*, precisely because the line between categories is difficult to draw with precision.

## Judicial Liability…

### Purity Rulings

Returning to the *mishna*, the *Gemara* addresses the cases of *tima et ha-tahor ve-tiher et ha-tamei* — declaring something *tamei* that was *tahor*, or *tahor* that was *tamei*.

### What's Done Done

*Mah she'asa asuy* — what has been done is done — is straightforward in the civil case: money that has changed hands cannot be clawed back. But in the *tumah* and *tahara* cases, the phrase requires clarification. If a ruling was simply wrong, one would expect to reverse it. The *Gemara* will explain below how concrete physical actions were taken that make the ruling's effects irreversible.

### Mumche Exemption

However, the *mishna* qualifies the liability rule with a crucial exception: *im haya mumche le-veit din patur mi-leshalem* — if the judge was a court-authorized expert, he is exempt from paying. The *mumche le-veit din* status — which involves licensing, community recognition, and demonstrable expertise, as discussed in *Sanhedrin* — carries with it exemption from financial liability. Two possible rationales present themselves. The first is purely pragmatic: without such protection, no one would be willing to accept the responsibility of rendering rulings. The second, and perhaps more analytically compelling, rationale is that an expert of such recognized standing who makes an error is presumed to have committed not a negligent mistake but an *oness* — an unavoidable, unforeseeable occurrence for which liability is inappropriate.

## Grama & Garmi…

### Taking & Handing

The *Gemara* asks whether the *mishna*'s liability cases even constitute *garmi* or *mazik*, and *Rav Elazar* in the name of *Rav* explains. In the case of *mechayev et ha-zakkai* — obligating an exempt party — the *Gemara* posits a scenario of direct physical action: *kegon she-natal ve-natan be-yad* — the judge himself physically took the money from *Reuven* and handed it to *Shimon*. In this scenario, the judge is a direct *mazik* and liability is clear.

### Collateral Example

For the case of *zika et ha-chayav* — exempting the liable party — physical action is harder to construct, since simply declaring someone exempt involves no physical act. *Ravina* provides the scenario: *Reuven* held *Shimon*'s jewelry as collateral, worth the amount of the disputed debt. The judge ruled *Reuven* exempt, and to demonstrate the finality of his ruling, physically took the collateral from *Shimon*'s possession and returned it to *Reuven*. Since *Reuven* was not in fact exempt, the judge took something *Shimon* had legal rights to and removed it from his control. That is a direct physical damage, and the judge must compensate.

### Mixing Grain

In the case of *tima et ha-tahor* — declaring *tahor* things *tamei* — the direct action is constructed as follows: the judge takes a *sheretz* and physically throws it onto the *terumah* to prove his point, thereby actively making *tahor* produce *tamei*. This explains both the physical causation and the irreversibility signified by *mah she'asa asuy*.

### Mixing Proof

For the reverse case, *tiher et ha-tamei* — declaring *tamei* things *tahor* — the scenario is: *she'irvev im peirotav*, the judge mixes the questionable grain with the *kohen*'s *tahor terumah* to prove his ruling that it is *tahor*. When it turns out the grain was *tamei*, the entire mixture is now compromised. This explains both the physical action and the resulting loss: the *kohen* loses his *tahor terumah* because it has been irreversibly mixed with *tamei* produce.

### Ruling Binding?

This also illuminates the phrase *mah she'asa asuy* in the *tumah* context. The case of *tiher et ha-tamei* is particularly useful, because in that case alone it is not obvious what loss was caused by the ruling as such — the judge declared something *tahor*, and one might ask what financial harm that produces. The mixing scenario creates the loss and demonstrates the irreversibility. But it also raises a deeper question: does the judge's ruling have independent halachic force? If a *rav* rules something is *tahor*, does it actually become *tahor* for halachic purposes, such that another *rav* cannot simply overrule it? There is direct discussion in the *Gemara* about one rabbi overruling another in a *halacha* ruling. In the case of *tiher et ha-tamei*, however, the ruling cannot actually render something *tahor* if it is truly *tamei*, which is precisely why the mixing scenario is necessary to explain the mechanism of the loss.

## Judicial Liability……

### Rabbi Tarfon Story

The *Gemara* now presents the famous story of *Rabbi Tarfon*. A cow was brought before him that was missing its uterus — whether congenitally or surgically removed was unclear. *Rabbi Tarfon* ruled it was a *treifa* and ordered it fed to the dogs, since one may not eat a *treifa*. The case was then brought before the sages at *Yavneh*, who ruled that an animal lacking a uterus is in fact not a *treifa* at all.

### Todos Evidence

The basis for the sages' ruling was the testimony of *Todos the doctor*, who stated: *ein parah ve-chazirah yotzet me-Alexandria shel Mitzrayim she-ein chotchin ha-em shelah bishvil she-lo teled* — no cow or pig leaves Alexandria of Egypt without its uterus having been surgically removed, so that the animals cannot breed outside the city, thereby preserving the Alexandrians' monopoly on their specialized breeds. Since these animals were known to survive the procedure and live productively, it was proven that the absence of a uterus is not a fatal defect. Notably, while the *Gemara* earlier was inclined to dismiss the testimony of witnesses when their account conflicted with received knowledge, here expert factual testimony is accepted and it reshapes the *halachic* ruling.

When *Rabbi Tarfon* heard the sages' ruling, he exclaimed: *chamorkha Tarfon* — there goes your donkey, *Tarfon* — implying that he now expected to have to sell his most valuable asset to compensate for the loss his erroneous ruling had caused.

### Akiva Response

*Rabbi Akiva* responded immediately: *Tarfon, atah mumcheh le-veit din, ve-chol ha-mumcheh le-veit din patur mi-leshalem* — you are a court-authorized expert, and any expert authorized by the court is exempt from liability. *Rabbi Tarfon*'s exemption from paying is therefore clear. What is striking, however, is that *Rabbi Tarfon* himself did not know this rule, and had to be reminded of his own status by *Rabbi Akiva*. There is something quite ironic in this: the same *Rabbi Tarfon* who possessed *mumche* status and was protected by it was unaware that his status carried this exemption. This raises a genuine question about what *mumche* status actually requires — it evidently signifies recognized expertise and communal authorization, but it does not require encyclopedic knowledge of every *halacha*, even *halachos* directly pertinent to the *mumche*'s own position.

## Error Categories

### Black-Letter Error

The *Gemara* adds that *Rabbi Tarfon* might in any case have been exempt on an entirely different and seemingly opposite basis. The principle of *toeh b'dvar mishna* holds that if a judge makes an error in a matter of obvious, black-letter law — something that is explicitly and clearly stated — the ruling is not considered a binding ruling at all. A *mishna* in *Chullin* lists a missing uterus among the conditions that do *not* make an animal a *treifa*, which means *Rabbi Tarfon*'s error was precisely this kind of blatant mistake.

### Judgment Error

By contrast, *toeh b'shikul hadaas* — an error in judgment — refers to a case where reasonable arguments exist on both sides, the general practice runs one way, but one could understand how a judge might have concluded otherwise. Such a ruling, even if wrong, is still binding as a ruling.

### Void & Exempt

The distinction carries a decisive practical consequence. A *toeh b'dvar mishna* ruling is treated as a non-ruling: *chozer*, it is reversed. And since it never constituted a valid ruling, no liability attaches to it. A *toeh b'shikul hadaas* ruling, by contrast, remains binding despite being wrong — *mah she'asa asuy* — and the judge must compensate for the resulting loss.

The rationale is subtle and worth pausing over. Why should a *wrong* ruling remain binding? Because although the outcome was incorrect, the process fell within the reasonable bounds of legitimate judicial reasoning. It is analogous to an appellate court's standard of review: not every error is reversed. Since the ruling was within bounds, it stands. But since it was in the end wrong, the judge bears financial responsibility for the harm it caused.

### Obvious Error

In *Rabbi Tarfon*'s case, however, the *mishna* in *Chullin* explicitly includes the missing uterus among non-*treifa* conditions. His error was not in judgment but in failing to apply a clear and explicit ruling. *Toeh b'dvar mishna* therefore applies, the ruling is void, and there is no liability.

### Both Extremes

The *Gemara* notes the remarkable paradox: *Rabbi Tarfon* is exempt from liability either because he is a *mumche* — one extreme of expertise — or because his error was so blatant as to constitute no ruling at all — the opposite extreme. Both routes lead to the same result.

### Indirect Causation

But a further complication arises. Even granting that it was not a binding ruling, *Rabbi Tarfon*'s statement still caused the animal to be fed to the dogs. (*Rashi* clarifies that *Rabbi Tarfon* did not literally feed it himself; rather, it was fed to the dogs on the basis of his ruling.) But if his pronouncement was technically a non-ruling, why did the owner's act of feeding it to the dogs flow from *Rabbi Tarfon*'s responsibility at all? The answer has to do with the structure of *grama*: if the statement is formally a ruling, one can argue that it directly caused the resulting action — the owner had no choice but to comply. If it was not a ruling, the causal chain becomes more attenuated. The owner acted on something that was not formally binding; the causal connection now looks more like indirect *grama*, for which the *Gemara* generally does not impose liability. This is a genuinely difficult point: the formalization of a statement as a ruling is what elevates the causal connection from *grama* to something more direct, yet the very same formalization also triggers the question of whether it was a legitimate ruling in the first place.

### Canonization

The *Gemara*'s discussion of *toeh b'dvar mishna* also raised a broader question among the *rishonim*: what texts and opinions are sufficiently authoritative that contradicting them constitutes a *dvar mishna*-level error? Are the opinions of the *Geonim* in this category, such that ruling against them would be *toeh b'dvar mishna*? This question led to extended discussion among the *rishonim* about which texts become canonized within *halacha* to the degree that they carry that status, and to what extent everything written after the *mishna* and *Gemara* falls into the category of *shikul hadaas* — reasoned judgment that may legitimately be contested.
