# Berakhot 41 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Non-Ground Foods

### Intro: Vinegar & Locusts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְעַל דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין גִּדּוּלוֹ מִן הָאָרֶץ אוֹמֵר ״שֶׁהַכֹּל נִהְיֶה בִּדְבָרוֹ״. עַל הַחוֹמֶץ וְעַל הַנּוֹבְלוֹת וְעַל הַגּוֹבַאי, אוֹמֵר ״שֶׁהַכֹּל נִהְיֶה בִּדְבָרוֹ״. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁהוּא מִין קְלָלָה אֵין מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And over** a food **item whose growth is not from the ground, one recites: By whose word all things came to be.** And **over vinegar,** wine that fermented and spoiled, **and over *novelot*,** dates that spoiled, **and over locusts, one recites: By whose word all things came to be. Rabbi Yehuda says:** Over **any** food item **that is a type** resulting from a **curse, one does not recite a blessing over it** at all. None of the items listed exist under normal conditions, and they come about as the result of a curse.

The *mishna* on daf 40b closes with the category of foods that do not grow from the ground — having already addressed *adama*, *ha-etz*, *borei peri ha-gafen*, and *ha-motzi*, the *mishna* now turns to what lies outside all those categories. The ruling is: *ve-al davar she-ein gidulo min ha-aretz* — on something that does not grow from the ground — *omer she-hakol nihyah bi-dvaro*. The examples given are vinegar (*chometz*), *novlot* (a kind of spoiled or damaged date, to be examined below), and *govai* (a kosher species of locust). On all of these, one recites *shehakol*.

### R. Yehuda: Min Klala

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁהוּא מִין קְלָלָה אֵין מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** Over **any** food item **that is a type** resulting from a **curse, one does not recite a blessing over it** at all.

On this the *mishna* records the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda: *kol she-hu min ha-klala ein mevarchin alav* — anything that is an expression of curse, one does not recite a blessing over it. Locusts, which devastate crops and represent a curse upon the world, fall into this category. Vinegar, at least in antiquity, was typically not the product of deliberate effort but of wine gone bad. These are, in Rabbi Yehuda's view, unworthy of a *bracha*.

This raises two distinct questions. The first is theological. We have already learned elsewhere the principle *ke-shem she-mevarchin al ha-tov kach mevarchin al ha-ra* — just as one blesses over the good, so one blesses over the bad — which would seem to suggest that even unfortunate circumstances call for acknowledgment of God's sovereignty. The second question is more structural: we generally hold that one may not derive benefit without a preceding *bracha*, yet Rabbi Yehuda seems to hold that where the item is an expression of curse, the Torah's *bracha* is not warranted, and therefore deriving benefit from it incurs no obligation. Both tensions are striking and the *gemara* does not fully resolve them here, moving on instead to practical *halacha*.

### Seven Species Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ לְפָנָיו מִינִין הַרְבֵּה, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִם יֵשׁ בֵּינֵיהֶן מִין שִׁבְעָה — עָלָיו הוּא מְבָרֵךְ. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מְבָרֵךְ עַל אֵיזֶה מֵהֶן שֶׁיִּרְצֶה.
>
> **Translation:** On a different note: If there were **many types** of food **before him,** over which food should he recite a blessing first? **Rabbi Yehuda says: If there is one of the seven species** for which Eretz Yisrael was praised **among them, he recites** the first **blessing over it. And the Rabbis say: He recites a blessing over whichever of them he wants.**

The *gemara* next records a dispute regarding the case where *minim harbe* — many different types of food — are set before a person. Rabbi Yehuda rules: *im yesh beineihem mi-min shiva alav hu mevarech* — if one of the *shivat ha-minim* is among them, the *bracha* is made on that item first. The *chachamim* disagree: *mevarech al eizeh mehem she-yirtze* — one may recite the blessing on whichever one wishes. The earlier discussion in the *gemara* (where choice was placed at the bottom of any hierarchy of preference) seemed to favor Rabbi Yehuda's formulation. How this plays out will become clearer below.

### Braita: Shehakol List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: עַל דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין גִּדּוּלוֹ מִן הָאָרֶץ, כְּגוֹן בְּשַׂר בְּהֵמוֹת חַיּוֹת וְעוֹפוֹת וְדָגִים אוֹמֵר ״שֶׁהַכֹּל נִהְיֶה בִּדְבָרוֹ״. עַל הֶחָלָב וְעַל הַבֵּיצִים וְעַל הַגְּבִינָה אוֹמֵר ״שֶׁהַכֹּל״. עַל הַפַּת שֶׁעִפְּשָׁה, וְעַל הַיַּיִן שֶׁהִקְרִים, וְעַל הַתַּבְשִׁיל שֶׁעִבֵּר צוּרָתוֹ אוֹמֵר ״שֶׁהַכֹּל״. עַל הַמֶּלַח וְעַל הַזָּמִית וְעַל כְּמֵהִין וּפִטְרִיּוֹת אוֹמֵר ״שֶׁהַכֹּל״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught: Over a** food **item whose growth is not from the earth, for example, meat from domesticated animals, non-domesticated animals, and fowl and fish, one recites: By whose word all things came to be.** So too, **over milk, and over eggs, and over cheese, one recites:** By whose word **all things** came to be. This is not only true with regard to items that come from animals, but **over moldy bread, and over wine that fermented slightly, and over a cooked dish that spoiled, one recites: By whose word all things came to be,** because the designated blessing is inappropriate for food that is partially spoiled. Similarly, **over salt and over brine, and over truffles and mushrooms, one recites: By whose word all things came to be.**

A *braita* is now cited that enumerates the categories requiring *shehakol*. These include: meat of domesticated animals, wild animals, birds, and fish (*besar behemot chayot ve-ofot ve-dagim*); dairy products and animal byproducts such as milk, eggs, and cheese (*chalav, beitzim, gevina*); foods that have spoiled — moldy bread (*pat she-i-efsha*), wine that has developed a surface film (*yayin she-hikrim*), or a cooked dish whose form has been ruined (*tavshil she-avar tzurato*); and also salt, brine (*zamit*), and mushrooms and truffles (*kmehin u-fitriyot*). On all of these one recites *shehakol*.

This *braita* stands in evident tension with Rabbi Yehuda's position: it explicitly rules that spoiled and deteriorated foods — bread gone moldy, wine partially turned — do receive a *bracha*, namely *shehakol*. The question of whether the concern is about lack of taste, or whether it is about the theological impropriety of blessing over what has gone bad, remains open.

### Fungi: Root Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דִּכְמֵהִין וּפִטְרִיּוֹת לָאו גִּדּוּלֵי קַרְקַע נִינְהוּ? וְהָתַנְיָא הַנּוֹדֵר מִפֵּירוֹת הָאָרֶץ — אָסוּר בְּפֵירוֹת הָאָרֶץ, וּמוּתָּר בִּכְמֵהִין וּפִטְרִיּוֹת, וְאִם אָמַר ״כׇּל גִּדּוּלֵי קַרְקַע עָלַי״ — אָסוּר אַף בִּכְמֵהִין וּפִטְרִיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is this to say** that **truffles and mushrooms are not** items **that grow from the ground? Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **One who vows** not to eat **from the fruit of the earth is forbidden** to eat all **fruit of the earth;** however, he **is permitted** to eat **truffles and mushrooms.** And **if he said: All** items **that grow from the ground** are forbidden **to me, he is forbidden** to eat **even truffles and mushrooms.** Apparently, truffles and mushrooms are items that grow from the ground.

The *gemara* pauses on the case of *kmehin u-fitriyot* — mushrooms and truffles — and asks: *lemeimra d'khmeina u-fitrya la gidulei karka ninhu?* Do we mean to say that fungi do not grow from the ground? They clearly emerge from the ground's surface — perhaps one should recite *borei pri ha-adama*? The *gemara* adduces a *baraita* about vows: one who vows to abstain from *peirot ha-aretz* (fruits of the ground) is forbidden to eat fruit but permitted to eat mushrooms and truffles. However, if he vows to abstain from *kol gidulei karka* — all things that grow from the ground — he is forbidden even from mushrooms and truffles. So they are considered *gidulei karka* in some sense. Why then is *borei pri ha-adama* not recited over them?

### Abaye: No Root Draw

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִירְבָּא רָבוּ מֵאַרְעָא, מֵינָק לָא יָנְקִי מֵאַרְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** With regard **to growth, they grow from the earth,** but with regard **to sustenance, they do not draw sustenance from the earth.**

Abaye resolves the difficulty: *mirba ravvu me-ara* — yes, they grow off the ground — *minka la yanki me-ara* — but they do not draw their nutrients from within the ground. Unlike true ground produce, fungi draw on already-decomposed organic matter; they have no root system that processes inorganic nutrients from the soil. They grow from the ground without nursing from it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״עַל דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין גִּדּוּלוֹ מִן הָאָרֶץ״ קָתָנֵי! תְּנִי: עַל דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין יוֹנֵק מִן הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Why is that distinction significant? **Wasn't it taught: Over** a food **item whose growth is not from the ground** one recites the blessing: By whose word all things came to be? Even according to Abaye, mushrooms grow from the ground. The Gemara answers: **Emend** the *baraita* to read: **Over** a food **item that does not draw sustenance from the ground,** one recites: By whose word all things came to be. Consequently, even over mushrooms one recites: By whose word all things came to be.

The *gemara* then re-reads the *mishna*'s language accordingly: rather than *al davar she-ein gidulo min ha-aretz* (something that does not grow from the ground), the correct reading is *al davar she-ein yonek min ha-aretz* — something that does not draw its nourishment from the ground. The text was imprecise; the principle is about the mechanism of nourishment, not merely the location of growth.

One might raise the question of hydroponics — vegetables grown in water rather than soil, from a species that would ordinarily grow from the ground. In this particular case, the plant is not drawing from the soil at all. The widespread practice is to recite *adama* on such produce, though the question deserves fuller analysis in its own right.

### Novlot Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַל הַנּוֹבְלוֹת: מַאי נוֹבְלוֹת? רַבִּי זֵירָא וְרַבִּי אִילְעָא. חַד אָמַר: בּוּשְׁלֵי כַמְרָא. וְחַד אָמַר: תַּמְרֵי דְזִיקָא.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that **over *novelot*** one recites: By whose word all things came to be. The Gemara asks: **What** are ***novelot*?** The Gemara responds that the *amora'im* **Rabbi Zeira and Rabbi Il'a** disputed this. **One said** that the term refers to dates that, due to extreme conditions, were **burned by the heat** of the sun and ripened prematurely. **And one said** that they are **dates** that fell from the tree because **of the wind.**

The *gemara* turns to the case of *novlot*, the spoiled dates mentioned in the *mishna*. Two *amoraim* from the school of Rav Zeira and Rabbi Ila dispute the term: one says *bushlei chamra* — dates that have been "cooked" by excessive exposure to the sun while still on the tree; the other says *tamrei d'zika* — dates blown off the tree by the wind.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁהוּא מִין קְלָלָה אֵין מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בּוּשְׁלֵי כַמְרָא, הַיְינוּ דְּקָרֵי לֵיהּ ״מִין קְלָלָה״, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר תַּמְרֵי דְזִיקָא — מַאי מִין קְלָלָה?
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** later in the mishna that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** Over **any** food item **that is a type** resulting from a **curse, one does not recite a blessing over it** at all. **Granted, according to the one who said** that *novelot* are dates **burned by the heat** of the sun, **that is** the reason **that** he **considers them a type of curse; however, according to the one who said** that *novelot* are **dates that** fell because **of the wind, what** is the reason that it is considered a **type of curse?** Dates that fell from the tree are no worse than other dates.

The *gemara* tests each interpretation against Rabbi Yehuda's ruling that things which are an expression of curse receive no *bracha*. According to the view that these are sun-scorched dates, the description fits: the fruit has been ruined by the elements, a form of natural curse, and it also explains why only *shehakol* (and not *borei pri ha-etz*) is recited — the dates have undergone *ishtani l'reiusa*, a change for the worse. According to the view that they are merely wind-fallen dates, however, it is difficult to call them a curse — they are fully edible, simply fallen early from the tree.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְּׁאָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara reconciles: Rabbi Yehuda's statement was **about the rest,** the vinegar and locusts, not about the *novelot*.

The *gemara* proposes *pashra*: perhaps Rabbi Yehuda's *min klala* language refers only to the other items in the *mishna*'s list (the vinegar and the locusts), not to the *novelot*. This resolution is not entirely satisfying.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בּוּשְׁלֵי כַמְרָא, הַיְינוּ דִּמְבָרְכִינַן עֲלַיְיהוּ ״שֶׁהַכֹּל״. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר תַּמְרֵי דְזִיקָא, ״שֶׁהַכֹּל״?! ״בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הָעֵץ״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְבָרוֹכֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** that the Gemara raised the question differently: **Granted, according to the one who said** that *novelot* are dates **burned by the heat** of the sun, **that is** the reason **that we recite over them: By whose word all things came to be,** as they are of inferior quality. **However, to the one who said** that *novelot* are **dates that** fell because **of the wind,** should we recite over them: By whose word all things came to be? **We should recite: Who creates fruit of the tree.**

An alternative formulation reverses the difficulty: according to the view that they are sun-cooked dates, it is perfectly intelligible that the *mishna* would prescribe *shehakol* rather than *borei pri ha-etz* — the dates have been ruined and are *ishtani l'reiusa*. But according to the view that they are merely wind-blown and intact, why would one recite anything less than *borei pri ha-etz*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, בְּנוֹבְלוֹת סְתָמָא כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּבוּשְׁלֵי כַמְרָא נִינְהוּ. כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּנוֹבְלוֹת תְּמָרָה. דִּתְנַן: הַקַּלִּים שֶׁבַּדְּמַאי: הַשִּׁיתִין, וְהָרִימִּין, וְהָעוּזְרָדִין, בְּנוֹת שׁוּחַ, וּבְנוֹת שִׁקְמָה, וְגוּפְנִין, וְנִצְפָּה, וְנוֹבְלוֹת תְּמָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the conclusion is, **with regard to *novelot* unmodified, everyone agrees that they are** dates that were **burned by the heat** of the sun. **When they argue, it** is **with regard to** those dates known as ***novelot temara*, as we learned** in a mishna concerning the laws of doubtfully tithed produce [*demai*]: Although, under normal circumstances, fruits that come into one's possession by means of an *am ha'aretz* must be tithed due to concern lest the *am ha'aretz* failed to do so, the following fruits of inferior quality are **lenient with regard to *demai*** and one need not tithe them: ***Shittin*, *rimin*, *uzradin*, *benot shuaḥ*, *benot shikma*, *gufnin*, *nitzpa*, and *novelot temara*.**

The *gemara* concludes: the unqualified term *novelot* in the *mishna* refers to dates that have been sun-scorched (*bushlei chamra*). The distinct phrase *novelot temarah*, found elsewhere, may refer to wind-fallen dates. This leads the *gemara* into the *mishna* of *Demai*.

### Demai Categories

The phrase *novelot temarah* appears in the *mishna* of tractate *Demai*, in a section listing *hakalin she-be-demai* — the lenient categories of *demai*. *Demai* arises when one purchases produce from *amei ha-aretz* who are not meticulous about separating *terumot u-ma'asrot*. While one may assume *teruma* has been separated (since its proportion is negligibly small and *amei ha-aretz* generally would not neglect it), *ma'asrot* remain a concern. The *mishna* in *Demai* lists a set of fruits so commercially insignificant that one may assume even *amei ha-aretz* would not have objected to separating *ma'asrot* from them — and therefore the consumer need not worry about *demai* with respect to these items.

### Fruit Identifications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁיתִין, אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִין תְּאֵנִים. רִימִּין — כַּנְדֵי. הָעוּזְרָדִין — טוּלְשֵׁי. בְּנוֹת שׁוּחַ, אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: תְּאֵינֵי חִיוָּרָתָא. בְּנוֹת שִׁקְמָה, אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: דּוּבְלֵי. גּוּפְנִין — שִׁילְהֵי גּוּפְנֵי. נִצְפָּה — פִּרְחָה. נוֹבְלוֹת תְּמָרָה — רַבִּי אִילְעָא וְרַבִּי זֵירָא, חַד אָמַר: בּוּשְׁלֵי כַמְרָא, וְחַד אָמַר: תַּמְרֵי דְזִיקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara identifies these plants. ***Shittin*, Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** They are **a type** of **figs. *Rimin*** are **lote. *Uzradin*** are **crabapples. *Benot shuaḥ*, Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** They are **white figs. *Benot shikma*, Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** They are **the fruit of the sycamore tree. *Gufnin*** are **the last grapes** which remain on the tree at the end of the season. ***Nitzpa*** are the **fruit of the caper-bush. *Novelot temara*, Rabbi Il'a and Rabbi Zeira** disagreed. **One said** that they are dates **burned by the heat** of the sun, **and one said** that they are **dates** that fell because **of the wind.**

The *mishna* in *Demai* lists: *hashitin*, *harimin*, *ha-ozradin*, *bnot shuach*, *bnot shikma*, *gofnin*, *nitzpa*, and *novlot temarah*. The *gemara*, with the assistance of Rabbah bar bar Chana citing Rabbi Yochanan, identifies each: *shitin* are a variety of unripe or inferior figs (*min te'einim*); *rimin* are jujube berries (fruit of the *ziziphus lotus*); *ozradin* are crabapples; *bnot shuach* are white figs (*te'anei chivarta*); *bnot shikma* are the fruit of the sycamore tree (*duvli*), not normally consumed; *gofnin* are end-of-season grapes of inferior quality (*shilei gofnei*); *nitzpa* is the fruit of the caper bush — our familiar friend from earlier discussions; and *novlot temarah* is the case now under examination.

### Fallen Dates Hekker

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר תַּמְרֵי דְזִיקָא, הַיְינוּ דְּהָכָא קָרֵי לַהּ ״נוֹבְלוֹת״ סְתָמָא, וְהָתָם קָרֵי לַהּ ״תְּמָרָה״, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בּוּשְׁלֵי כַמְרָא, נִיתְנֵי אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי ״נוֹבְלוֹת תְּמָרָה״, אוֹ אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי ״נוֹבְלוֹת״ סְתָמָא. קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, according to the one who said that** *novelot temara* are **dates** felled **by the wind, that is why here,** when our mishna speaks of ruined dates, **it calls them *novelot*, unmodified and there,** when it speaks of those that fell because of the wind, **it calls them** *novelot **temara*****. However, according to the one who said that** *novelot temara* are dates **burned by the heat** of the sun, **it should have taught** in **this** mishna here **and that,** the mishna in tractate *Demai*, ***novelot temara*; or** taught in **this and that *novelot*, unmodified.** The use of different terms indicates that the *mishnayot* are discussing different items. No answer was found to this question, and the Gemara notes that indeed, according to the one who said that *novelot temara* are dates burned by the heat of the sun, **it is difficult.**

The dispute between sun-cooked and wind-blown dates reasserts itself in the context of the *Demai* *mishna*. According to the view that *novelot temarah* means wind-blown dates (*tamrei d'zika*), one can understand why the *mishna* calls them *novelot temarah* with the qualifier — they are intact dates that have merely fallen, so the qualifying word marks their lesser status. According to the view that they are sun-cooked, there is less reason to use different terminology in the two sources. The *gemara* concedes this as a *kashya* but does not press further.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — שֶׁעֲשָׂאָן גּוֹרֶן. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב: הַלֶּקֶט וְהַשִּׁכְחָה וְהַפֵּאָה שֶׁעֲשָׂאָן גּוֹרֶן — הוּקְבְּעוּ לְמַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **With what are we dealing here?** With a case where he gathered the dates that fell because of the wind and **made them** into a pile, like **a pile of threshed grain,** signifying that the produce is a finished product. **As Rabbi Yitzḥak said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov:** Even gifts to the poor such as **gleanings, forgotten sheaves, and** produce of the **corners,** which are normally exempt from tithes, if a poor person gathered them and **made them into a pile of threshed grain,** by rabbinic law **they were rendered** obligated in **tithes.** In that case, only *demai* would be exempt from tithes.

A separate question is posed: if these are indeed wind-blown dates — intact, perfectly edible — why are they listed among lenient *demai* categories? They are, after all, fit for market. The *gemara* answers: *hefker ninhu* — they are presumed abandoned. Dates that fall naturally from the tree are generally assumed by their owners to be of no value to them; people commonly expect others to pick them up, and the owner would in retrospect have acquiesced. This *de facto* abandonment of wind-fallen dates is closely related to the broader *Bava Metzia* discussion of *ye'ush she-lo mi-da'at* — whether unwitting renunciation, before the owner is even aware of a loss, constitutes legally effective *ye'ush*.

However, the *gemara* adds, if the owner explicitly gathered these fallen dates together and processed them as he would normal produce — taking them to a threshing area and collecting them — this changes matters: *she-asaan goren*. Drawing on Rabbi Yitzchak citing Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov, the *gemara* rules that *leket*, *shikcha*, and *pe'ah* which a poor person has gathered and processed like a normal crop are rendered *hukbe'u le-ma'aser* — they become obligated in *ma'aser* rabbinically, because through his actions the owner has effectively treated them like his regular agricultural output.

## Blessing Priority

### Same Bracha Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: מַחֲלוֹקֶת בְּשֶׁבִּרְכוֹתֵיהֶן שָׁווֹת, דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר מִין שִׁבְעָה עָדִיף, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי מִין חָבִיב עָדִיף. אֲבָל בְּשֶׁאֵין בִּרְכוֹתֵיהֶן שָׁווֹת — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מְבָרֵךְ עַל זֶה, וְחוֹזֵר וּמְבָרֵךְ עַל זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna cited a dispute with regard to the order in which one is supposed to recite the blessings when **there were many types** of food **before him.** Rabbi Yehuda says: If there is one of the seven species for which Eretz Yisrael was praised among them, he recites the first blessing over it. And the Rabbis say: He recites a blessing over whichever of them he wants. **Ulla said:** This **dispute** is specifically in a case **where the blessings** to be recited over each type of food **are the same,** as in that case **Rabbi Yehuda holds: The type of the seven** species **takes precedence, and the Rabbis hold: The preferred type takes precedence,** and a blessing is recited over it first. **However,** when **their blessings are not the same, everyone agrees that one must recite a blessing over this** type of food **and then recite another blessing over that,** ensuring that the appropriate blessing is recited over each type of food.

The *gemara* now returns to the dispute about prioritizing *brachot* when multiple foods are present. Ulla frames the parameters: *machlokes she-be-virchoteihem shavot* — the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the *chachamim* applies only when the foods share the same *bracha*, such as two fruits both requiring *borei pri ha-etz*. When a single *bracha* must represent the entire group, the question of which food takes pride of place becomes meaningful. Rabbi Yehuda holds *min shiva adif* — a member of the *shivat ha-minim* takes precedence; the *rabbanan* hold *min chaviv adif* — whichever food one personally prefers takes precedence.

### Different Brachot

When the foods carry *different* brachot, Ulla continues, *divrei ha-kol mevarech al ze ve-chozer u-mevarech al ze* — everyone agrees one makes each *bracha* separately, and the order does not matter. This seems straightforward: a carrot and an apple carry different *brachot* (*adama* and *ha-etz* respectively), so one simply recites each in turn, with no priority question.

But one might object: did we not learn several days ago that certain *brachot* take precedence over others — that *borei pri ha-adama* precedes *shehakol*, and that more specific *brachot* take precedence over generic ones? The resolution, following Rashi as explained by Tosafot, is that the earlier discussion concerned the case of *shehakol* competing with a specific *bracha* — there, *shehakol* is so generic that the specific *bracha* clearly prevails. But when two specific *brachot* confront each other — *borei pri ha-etz* and *borei pri ha-adama* — both are specific. Although *borei pri ha-adama* can cover *borei pri ha-etz* retroactively (*be-di'avad*), the two categories nonetheless have independent standing. Each governs its own domain, and when they are truly separate eating occasions, everyone can agree that the order is free.

### Radish & Olive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הָיוּ לְפָנָיו צְנוֹן וְזַיִת — מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַצְּנוֹן וּפוֹטֵר אֶת הַזַּיִת!
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara raises an objection** to this based on what was taught in a *baraita*: If **a radish and an olive were before him, he recites a blessing over the radish and exempts the olive** from the requirement of a blessing, although their blessings are different.

The *gemara* raises an objection from a *baraita*: *hayu lefanav tzonon ve-zayit* — one had before him a radish and an olive. The radish requires *borei pri ha-adama*; the olive, a member of the *shivat ha-minim*, requires *borei pri ha-etz*. The *baraita* rules: *mevarech al ha-tzonon u-poter et ha-zayit* — one recites the *bracha* on the radish and thereby exempts the olive. This is striking on multiple counts. First, it implies that the *rabbanan* allow — or even recommend — reciting *borei pri ha-adama* and covering *borei pri ha-etz* under it, apparently *le-chatchila*. Second, it passes over the olive entirely despite it being from the *shivat ha-minim*, which seems to contradict Rabbi Yehuda's position.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַזַּיִת, שֶׁהַזַּיִת מִמִּין שִׁבְעָה. לֵית לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הָא דִּתְנַן: כׇּל שֶׁהוּא עִיקָּר וְעִמּוֹ טְפֵלָה — מְבָרֵךְ עַל הָעִיקָּר וּפוֹטֵר אֶת הַטְּפֵלָה? וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלֵית לֵיהּ, וְהָתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִם מֵחֲמַת צְנוֹן בָּא הַזַּיִת — מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַצְּנוֹן וּפוֹטֵר אֶת הַזַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues and asks: **If so, say the latter clause** of the *baraita* where **Rabbi Yehuda says: One recites a blessing over the olive, as the olive is a type of the seven** species. Does **Rabbi Yehuda not accept that** principle **which we learned** in a mishna: **Any** food **that is primary, and** is eaten **with** food that is **secondary, one recites a blessing over the primary** food, and that blessing **exempts the secondary** from the requirement to recite a blessing before eating it? **And if you say: Indeed, he does not hold** that the primary food exempts the secondary, **wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says: If** it is **due to the radish** that **the olive comes, one recites a blessing over the radish and exempts the olive.** If so, the dispute whether to recite a blessing over the radish or the olive must be in a case where the radish is not primary. This is an apparent contradiction of Ulla's statement.

Adding to the difficulty, the end of that same *baraita* records Rabbi Yehuda's view: *mevarech al ha-zayit she-zayit mi-min shiva* — one makes the *bracha* on the olive, because the olive is from the *shivat ha-minim*. The *gemara* then asks: does Rabbi Yehuda not accept the principle of *ikkar ve-tafel* — that when one food is primary and another accompanies it in a secondary capacity, the *bracha* on the primary exempts the secondary? The *baraita*'s own conclusion states as much: *im machmat tzonon ba ha-zayit* — if the olive is being eaten because of the radish (i.e., to flavor or accompany the radish), *mevarech al ha-tzonon u-poter et ha-zayit* — even Rabbi Yehuda agrees the *bracha* is made on the radish. So Rabbi Yehuda does accept *ikkar ve-tafel*; his disagreement is only about the case where neither is secondary to the other.

### Ikkar v'Tafel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל שֶׁהוּא עִיקָּר וְעִמּוֹ טְפֵלָה — מְבָרֵךְ עַל הָעִיקָּר וּפוֹטֵר אֶת הַטְּפֵלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Any** food **that is primary, and** is eaten **with** food that is **secondary, one recites a blessing over the primary** food, and that blessing **exempts the secondary** from the requirement to recite a blessing before eating it.

This establishes a clear principle: *kol she-hu ikkar ve-imo tefela mevarech al ha-ikkar u-foter et ha-tefela* — when one food is primary and another accompanies it in a subordinate role, the *bracha* on the primary exempts the secondary. The secondary food is present not as a food in its own right but to serve the primary — to enhance its taste, cleanse the palate, or provide a vehicle for eating it.

### Gemara Rewrite

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם בִּצְנוֹן עִיקָּר עָסְקִינַן, וְכִי פְּלִיגִי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבָּנַן — בְּמִילְּתָא אַחֲרִיתִי פְּלִיגִי, וְחַסּוֹרֵי מְחַסְּרָא, וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: הָיוּ לְפָנָיו צְנוֹן וְזַיִת — מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַצְּנוֹן וּפוֹטֵר אֶת הַזַּיִת. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — כְּשֶׁהַצְּנוֹן עִיקָּר, אֲבָל אֵין הַצְּנוֹן עִיקָּר — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מְבָרֵךְ עַל זֶה וְחוֹזֵר וּמְבָרֵךְ עַל זֶה. וּשְׁנֵי מִינִין בְּעָלְמָא שֶׁבִּרְכוֹתֵיהֶן שָׁווֹת — מְבָרֵךְ עַל אֵיזֶה מֵהֶן שֶׁיִּרְצֶה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מְבָרֵךְ עַל הַזַּיִת, שֶׁהַזַּיִת מִמִּין שִׁבְעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Actually, we are dealing with** a case where the **radish is** the **primary** component of the meal, **and when Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis disagree, it is with regard to a different case that they disagree, and** this *baraita* **is incomplete and it teaches the following:** If **a radish and an olive were before him, he recites a blessing over the radish and exempts the olive. In what** circumstances **does this apply?** Specifically **when the radish is primary, but when the radish is not primary, everyone agrees that one recites** the appropriate **blessing over this** one **and then he again recites** the appropriate **blessing over that** one. However, **in general,** if **two types of food whose blessings are identical** were before him, **he recites a blessing over whichever of them that he wants. Rabbi Yehuda says: He recites a blessing over the olive, as the olive is a type of the seven** species.

The *gemara* is now confronted with the full force of the objection. The *baraita* appeared to set up a case where radish and olive are competing on equal terms — neither is *ikkar* and neither is *tafel* — and the *rabbanan* rule that one may recite *borei pri ha-adama* and have it cover *borei pri ha-etz*. That would flatly contradict the earlier conclusion that different *brachot* each have their own independent sphere.

The *gemara* therefore undertakes a radical rewriting of the *baraita*: *ve-chasurei mechsera ve-hachi katani* — the text is incomplete and should be read as follows. The opening case of radish and olive — *mevarech al ha-tzonon u-foter et ha-zayit* — refers specifically to the case where the radish is the *ikkar*, and everyone agrees. The *baraita* then continues: *bameh devarim amurim? ke-she-tzonon ikkar* — this applies when the radish is primary. *Aval im ein ha-tzonon ikkar* — but when it is not primary, then *divrei ha-kol mevarech al ze ve-chozer u-mevarech al ze* — everyone agrees one makes separate *brachot*, each in its own domain, and may choose whichever order one wishes. The disputed case, where Rabbi Yehuda and the *rabbanan* actually disagree, is recast as one involving two foods with the *same* bracha (*borei pri ha-etz*), where the question is which one represents the group.

This is, as the *gemara* itself acknowledges, a substantial rewrite. The *baraita* as written spoke quite explicitly of radish and olive as competing contenders for a single *bracha*. The *gemara* essentially removes that competition from the text and replaces it with a different dispute — one about priority within the *shivat ha-minim* when a single *bracha* must represent the group.

### Rav Ami Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא. חַד אָמַר: מַחֲלוֹקֶת בְּשֶׁבִּרְכוֹתֵיהֶן שָׁווֹת, דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר מִין שִׁבְעָה עָדִיף, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי מִין חָבִיב עָדִיף. אֲבָל בְּשֶׁאֵין בִּרְכוֹתֵיהֶן שָׁווֹת — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מְבָרֵךְ עַל זֶה וְחוֹזֵר וּמְבָרֵךְ עַל זֶה. וְחַד אָמַר: אַף בְּשֶׁאֵין בִּרְכוֹתֵיהֶן שָׁווֹת נָמֵי מַחֲלוֹקֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa disagreed with regard to** this subject. **One said:** The **dispute** is **in** a case **where their blessings are identical, as Rabbi Yehuda held:** A **type of the seven species takes precedence** and the blessing is recited over it first. **And the Rabbis held: The preferred type takes precedence,** and a blessing is recited over it first; **however, when their blessings are not identical, everyone agrees that one recites** the appropriate **blessing over this** one **and then he again recites** the appropriate **blessing over that** one. **And one said: Even when their blessings are not identical,** there is **also a dispute.**

The *gemara* now records a direct dispute between Rav Ami and Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha. One holds that the *machloket* between Rabbi Yehuda and the *rabbanan* applies only when the *brachot* are equal (*shevichoseihem shavot*) — and when the *brachot* differ, everyone agrees each is recited separately. The other holds that the *machloket* applies *even* when the *brachot* differ (*af be-she-ein birchoteihem shavot nami machloket*) — and the debate extends even to the question of whether *borei pri ha-adama* may *le-chatchila* be used to cover a food requiring *borei pri ha-etz*.

### Pasuk Order Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בְּשֶׁבִּרְכוֹתֵיהֶן שָׁווֹת מַחֲלוֹקֶת — שַׁפִּיר, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בְּשֶׁאֵין בִּרְכוֹתֵיהֶן שָׁווֹת פְּלִיגִי, בְּמַאי פְּלִיגִי! אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: לְהַקְדִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses this: **Granted, according to the one who said** that the **dispute** is **in** a case **where their blessings are identical, it** works out **well. However, according to the one who says** that **they disagree in** a case **where their blessings are not identical,** then **about what do they disagree?** One must recite two blessings in any case. **Rabbi Yirmeya said:** They disagree with regard to which blessing **precedes** the other.

The *gemara* then asks: according to the view that the dispute extends to different *brachot*, what exactly are they arguing about? If each food will in any case receive its own *bracha*, what difference does the debate make? Rabbi Yirmiyah answers: *le-hakdim* — they are arguing about which one comes first. The basis for this is a principle stated by Rav Yosef (and some say Rabbi Yitzchak): *kol ha-mukdam ba-pasuk ze mukdam le-vracha* — whatever appears earlier in the *pasuk* receives the earlier *bracha*.

### Seven Species List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי יִצְחָק: כׇּל הַמּוּקְדָּם בְּפָסוּק זֶה, מוּקְדָּם לִבְרָכָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֶרֶץ חִטָּה וּשְׂעוֹרָה וְגֶפֶן וּתְאֵנָה וְרִמּוֹן אֶרֶץ זֵית שֶׁמֶן וּדְבָשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rav Yosef, and some say Rabbi Yitzḥak, said: Each** food whose significance is manifest in that it **precedes** the others **in this verse,** which sings the praises of Eretz Yisrael, **takes precedence** over the others **in terms of blessing as well, as it is stated: "A land of wheat and barley, vines, figs and pomegranates, a land of olive oil and honey"** (Deuteronomy 8:8).

The *pasuk* in question is: *eretz chita u-se'ora ve-gefen u-te'ena ve-rimon, eretz zayit shemen u-dvash* (Deuteronomy 8:8). This verse lists the seven species in a specific order: wheat, barley, grape, fig, pomegranate — then a second *eretz* — olive oil, and (date) honey. According to Rabbi Yirmiyah's application of this principle, when two species from this list are before a person and each requires a separate *bracha*, the one that appears earlier in the verse is recited first. Rabbi Yehuda holds that this ordering principle governs even when the *brachot* differ; the *rabbanan* may concede the ordering principle absent a *chaviv* factor, but allow personal preference to override it.

Tosafot rules accordingly: *borei pri ha-etz* precedes *borei pri ha-adama* even when the *brachot* differ, following Rabbi Yehuda whose position we adopt as *halacha*. The question of how *chaviv* — personal preference or immediate eating intention — factors into this ordering remains a live one in later *poskim*.

## Shiurim Standards

### R. Chanin: Sizes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי חָנָן. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חָנָן: כׇּל הַפָּסוּק כּוּלּוֹ לְשִׁיעוּרִין נֶאֱמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: And this opinion **disagrees** with the opinion **of Rabbi Ḥanan. As Rabbi Ḥanan said: The entire verse was stated for** the purpose of teaching **measures** with regard to different *halakhot* in the Torah.

The *gemara* notes that the use of this *pasuk* as a source for *bracha* priority is challenged by Rabbi Chanin. Rabbi Chanin says: *kol ha-pasuk kulo le-shiurin ne'emar* — the entire verse was stated with respect to halachic *shiurim*, standard measurements, not as a source for the precedence of *brachot*. The seven species serve as the referents for various halachic quantities, each species providing the defining measure for a distinct area of law.

### Wheat: Eat Time

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״חִטָּה״, דִּתְנַן: הַנִּכְנָס לַבַּיִת הַמְנוּגָּע, וְכֵלָיו עַל כְּתֵפָיו, וְסַנְדָּלָיו וְטַבְּעוֹתָיו בְּיָדָיו — הוּא וָהֵן טְמֵאִין מִיָּד. הָיָה לָבוּשׁ כֵּלָיו, וְסַנְדָּלָיו בְּרַגְלָיו, וְטַבְּעוֹתָיו בְּאֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו — הוּא טָמֵא מִיָּד, וְהֵן טְהוֹרִין עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁהֶא בִּכְדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס. פַּת חִטִּין וְלֹא פַּת שְׂעוֹרִין, מֵיסֵב וְאוֹכְלָן בְּלִפְתָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Wheat** was mentioned as the basis for calculating the time required for one to become ritually impure by entering a house afflicted with leprosy, **as we learned** in a mishna: **One who enters a house afflicted** with leprosy of the house (see Leviticus 14:33–53) **with his clothes** resting **on his shoulders, and his sandals and his rings are in his hands,** both **he and they,** the clothes, sandals, and rings, **immediately** become **ritually impure.** However, if **he was dressed in his clothes, and his sandals were on his feet and his rings on his fingers, he immediately becomes ritually impure** upon entering the house, **but they,** the clothes, sandals, and rings, **remain pure until he stays** in the house **long enough to eat half a loaf** of bread. This calculation is made with **wheat bread,** which takes less time to eat, **and not** with **barley bread,** and with one who is **reclining and eating** the bread **with a relish,** which hastens the eating. There is, then, a Torah measurement that is connected specifically to wheat.

*Ne'emar chita* — wheat is mentioned, and from this we learn the standard of *achilat pras*, the time it takes to eat a half-loaf, which is operative in the laws of *tuma*. As the *mishna* teaches: one who enters a *bayit ha-menuga* (a house afflicted with leprosy) while wearing his clothes — with sandals on his feet and rings on his fingers — becomes *tamei* immediately, but his garments only acquire *tuma* if he remains long enough to eat a *pras*. The time of *achilat pras* is measured with reference to wheat bread (*pas chitin*), not barley bread — Rashi explains that wheat bread is eaten somewhat more readily. Furthermore, the standard assumes one is eating while reclining (*meisev*) and eating the bread with an accompaniment (*u-v'liftan*), as would be normal.

### Barley: Bone Size

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שְׂעוֹרָה״, דִּתְנַן: עֶצֶם כִּשְׂעוֹרָה מְטַמֵּא בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא, וְאֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא בְּאֹהֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **Barley** is also used as the basis for a measure, **as we learned** in a mishna: **A bone** from a corpse that is the size **of** a grain of **barley imparts ritual impurity through contact and by being carried,** but **it does not impart impurity** by means **of a tent,** i.e., if the bone was inside a house, it does not defile all of the articles in the house.

*Se'ora* — barley — provides the standard for bone *tuma*. The *mishna* teaches: *etzem keshorah* — a bone the size of a barley grain causes *tuma* by contact and by carrying, though not *tuma* in a tent (*tumas ohel*), which requires a much more substantial portion of the skeletal structure.

### Wine: Reviit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״גֶּפֶן״, כְּדֵי רְבִיעִית יַיִן לְנָזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The halakhic measure determined by **vines** is **the quantity of a quarter *log* of wine for a Nazirite** and not the wine equivalent of a quarter *log* of water, which is a slightly different measure.

*Gefen* — the grape — provides the standard of *reviit ya'yin le-nazir*: a *nazir* (nazirite) transgresses the prohibition against wine only when he drinks a *reviit* (approximately three and a half ounces). The choice of wine as the referent here is somewhat puzzling, since it is the very substance being measured for the *nazir*'s prohibition. Both Rashi and Tosafot grapple with this. Tosafot suggests that the issue has to do with surface tension — that wine displacement and water displacement yield different results when measuring volume, and the relevant standard must therefore be calibrated to wine. Rashi approaches the matter from the angle of density — wine contains more substance per unit of volume than water — though the precise halachic application of that density difference is not immediately obvious, since *reviit* is a volume measurement. Both explanations strain somewhat, and this remains a point of difficulty.

### Fig: Grogeret

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״תְּאֵנָה״, כִּגְרוֹגֶרֶת לְהוֹצָאַת שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Figs** serve as the basis for the measure of **a dried fig-bulk,** typically the smallest unit of food for which someone will be held liable **for carrying out on Shabbat** from one domain to another.

*Te'ena* — the fig — provides the standard called *grogeret*, the size of a dried fig, which is the minimum quantity for which carrying on *Shabbat* constitutes a transgression. *Kegrogeret le-hotza'at Shabbat* — the dried fig size is the standard amount for *Shabbat* carrying.

### Pomegranate: Hole Size

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״רִמּוֹן״, כְּדִתְנַן: כׇּל כְּלֵי בַּעֲלֵי בָתִּים שִׁיעוּרָן כְּרִמּוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Pomegranates** teach us a particular measurement as well, **as we learned** in a mishna: **All** ritually impure wooden **utensils belonging to** ordinary **homeowners,** as an impure vessel loses its status as a vessel and consequently its impurity when it can no longer be used, if they have holes **the size of pomegranates.**

*Rimon* — the pomegranate — provides the standard for holes in vessels and their *tuma* status. A homeowner's vessel, which is not sold commercially, retains its status as a vessel — and thus its susceptibility to *tuma* — even with a hole, as long as the hole is smaller than a pomegranate. The reasoning is practical: a commercial vessel would be removed from sale once any hole appeared; a homeowner's vessel would continue to be used for progressively coarser goods — first liquid, then grain, then fruit — until the hole grew too large even for pomegranates to be stored without falling through. Only at that point does the object cease to function as a vessel and lose its susceptibility to *tuma*. *Kol klei ba'alei batim shiuram ke-rimonim*.

### Kezayis Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֶרֶץ זֵית שֶׁמֶן״, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אֶרֶץ שֶׁכָּל שִׁיעוּרֶיהָ כְּזֵיתִים. כׇּל שִׁיעוּרֶיהָ סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! וְהָא אִיכָּא הָנָךְ דַּאֲמַרַן! אֶלָּא אֶרֶץ שֶׁרוֹב שִׁיעוּרֶיהָ כְּזֵיתִים.
>
> **Translation:** **A land of olive oil: Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said** that the verse should be expounded as follows: **A land, all of whose measures are** the size of **olives.** The Gemara poses a question: **Can it enter your mind** that it is a land **all of whose measures** are the size of olives? **Aren't there those** measures **that we mentioned** above, which are not the size of olives? **Rather, say: A land, most of whose measures are** the size of **olives,** as most of the measures relating to forbidden foods and other matters are the size of olives.

*Zayit shemen* — the olive — provides what Rav Yosef states is the overwhelmingly dominant halachic standard: *eretz she-rov shiureha ke-zaysim* — a land most of whose measures are the *kezayis*. How much *matzah* must one eat? A *kezayis*. How much of a forbidden substance constitutes a transgression? A *kezayit*. How much food must one consume to be obligated in *birkat ha-mazon*? A *kezayis*. The *gemara* qualifies the claim slightly — not literally *all* measurements, since as we have just seen, some use other standards — but the *kezayis* is correctly described as the central and most prevalent halachic standard of food quantity.

### Date Honey

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״דְּבָשׁ״, כְּכוֹתֶבֶת הַגַּסָּה בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Honey,** i.e., dates from which date honey is extracted, also alludes to a measurement. With regard to Yom Kippur, one is only liable if he eats the equivalent **of a large date on Yom Kippur.**

*U-dvash* — honey, understood here as date honey rather than bee honey — provides the standard for *Yom Kippur*. The amount of food that, if consumed on *Yom Kippur*, constitutes a transgression of the *inuy* (affliction) prohibition is not a *kezayis* but rather *ke-kosevet ha-gasa* — like a large date. The rationale is that on *Yom Kippur* the relevant threshold is not mere eating but genuine abatement of the fast's discomfort, and a *kezayis* alone is insufficient for that purpose; a fuller, more satiating quantity is required.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידָךְ — הָנֵי שִׁיעוּרִין בְּהֶדְיָא מִי כְּתִיבִי? אֶלָּא מִדְּרַבָּנַן, וּקְרָא אַסְמַכְתָּא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what will **the other** *amora*, who interpreted the verse as referring to the *halakhot* of precedence in blessings, say with regard to this midrash? The Gemara responds: **Are these measures written explicitly** in the Torah? **Rather,** they are **by rabbinic** law, **and the verse** is **a mere support,** an allusion to these measures.

The *gemara* then addresses the objection: if the *pasuk* is being used as the source for these *shiurim*, what is left for Rabbi Yirmiyah's use of it as a basis for *bracha* priority? The answer is that the *shiurim* themselves are not explicitly written anywhere — they are not derived directly from the text of the *pasuk* as a matter of *derasha*. Rather, they are either *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* (as an alternative reading in the *gemara* suggests with the word *hilchasa*) or derive from rabbinic tradition, and the *pasuk* serves merely as an *asmakhta* — a textual peg for what was already known. The *pasuk*, therefore, remains available to Rabbi Yirmiyah as a source for priority in *brachot*.

## Blessing Priority (cont.)

### Dates & Pomegranates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא וְרַב הַמְנוּנָא הֲווֹ יָתְבִי בִּסְעוֹדְתָּא. אַיְיתוֹ לְקַמַּיְיהוּ תַּמְרֵי וְרִמּוֹנֵי. שְׁקַל רַב הַמְנוּנָא, בָּרֵיךְ אַתַּמְרֵי בְּרֵישָׁא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא: לָא סָבַר לַהּ מָר לְהָא דְּאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי יִצְחָק: כׇּל הַמּוּקְדָּם בְּפָסוּק זֶה קוֹדֵם לִבְרָכָה?
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the *halakhot* of precedence in blessings, the Gemara relates: **Rav Ḥisda and Rav Hamnuna were sitting at a meal. They brought dates and pomegranates before them. Rav Hamnuna took and recited a blessing over the dates first. Rav Ḥisda said to him:** Does **the Master not hold that** *halakha* **which Rav Yosef, and some say Rabbi Yitzḥak, said: Each** food that **precedes** the others **in this verse, precedes** the others **in terms of blessing as well?** The pomegranate precedes the date in that verse.

A practical illustration of the priority principle appears immediately. Rav Chisda and Rav Hamnuna were sitting together at a meal when dates and pomegranates were brought before them. Rav Hamnuna took the dates and recited the *bracha* on them first. Rav Chisda challenged him: does the master not hold of the principle stated by Rav Yosef — or some say Rabbi Yitzchak — that *kol ha-mukdam ba-pasuk ze mukdam le-vracha*, whatever comes earlier in the *pasuk* gets the earlier *bracha*? The pomegranate (*rimon*) is the fifth species in the first *eretz*, while honey (*devash*) — understood as date honey — is the second species mentioned after the second *eretz*. So the pomegranate, appearing earlier in the verse overall, should take precedence.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זֶה שֵׁנִי לְ״אֶרֶץ״, וְזֶה חֲמִישִׁי לְ״אֶרֶץ״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאן יָהֵיב לַן נִגְרֵי דְפַרְזְלָא וּנְשַׁמְּעִינָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Hamnuna **said to him: This,** the date, **is** mentioned **second to** the word **land,** in the verse: "A land of olive oil and honey," just after the olive, **and this,** the pomegranate, is **fifth to** the word **land.** Rav Ḥisda **said to him** admiringly: **Who will give us iron legs that we may serve you** and constantly hear from you novel ideas.

Rav Hamnuna replied: *ze sheni la-aretz ve-ze chamishi la-aretz* — this one (the date honey) is second relative to the second *eretz*, while that one (the pomegranate) is fifth relative to the first *eretz*. When the verse restarts with a second *eretz*, the count resets; therefore, date honey as the second item from the second *eretz* precedes the pomegranate as the fifth item from the first *eretz*.

Rav Chisda's response was enthusiastic: *man yahav lan nagdi de-farzla ve-nishme'inach* — would that we had iron legs so that we could always follow you and attend to your teachings! This ruling is the accepted *halacha*: the two *eretz* groupings are counted separately, and proximity to one's respective *eretz* determines priority within and between the groups, with the first *eretz* group taking overall precedence when competing against the second.

Tosafot adds: when a food from the first *eretz* competes with a food from the second *eretz* for priority, the first *eretz* wins.

## Foods in Meals

### Mid-Meal Fruit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הֵבִיאוּ לִפְנֵיהֶם תְּאֵנִים וַעֲנָבִים בְּתוֹךְ הַסְּעוּדָה, אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: טְעוּנִים בְּרָכָה לִפְנֵיהֶם וְאֵין טְעוּנִים בְּרָכָה לְאַחֲרֵיהֶם. וְכֵן אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: טְעוּנִים בְּרָכָה לִפְנֵיהֶם וְאֵין טְעוּנִים בְּרָכָה לְאַחֲרֵיהֶם. וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: טְעוּנִין בְּרָכָה בֵּין לִפְנֵיהֶם בֵּין לְאַחֲרֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated:** If **they brought figs and grapes before them during a meal,** what blessings need to be recited? **Rav Huna said: They require a blessing before** eating **them, and do not require a blessing after** eating **them,** as Grace after Meals exempts them. **And so too, Rav Naḥman said: They require a blessing before** eating **them, and do not require a blessing after** eating **them. And Rav Sheshet said: They require a blessing both before** eating **them and after** eating **them,** even if he ate them during the meal,

The *gemara* now introduces one of the most practically significant discussions of the *daf*: what are the *bracha* obligations for foods eaten during and after a meal of bread? The case presented is figs and grapes brought in the middle of the meal (*betoch ha-seudah*). One can easily visualize the setting — a Roman-style reclined meal in which small dishes of fruit are brought between courses, perhaps to cleanse the palate.

### Huna & Nachman

Both Rav Huna and Rav Nachman rule identically: *te'unim bracha lifneihem ve-ein te'unim bracha le-achareihem* — these mid-meal fruits require a *bracha* before eating them but not after. The reasoning operates on two axes. Before: the figs and grapes are not considered a staple part of the meal and are not understood as accompanying the bread in a *tafel* capacity; they therefore require a separate *bracha* before. After: even though they may not be *zan* (sustaining) in the strict sense, *birkat ha-mazon* with its formulation of *ha-zan et ha-kol* (Who nourishes all) covers all food consumed during the meal, and no separate *bracha achrona* is required.

### Rav Sheshet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁאֵין לְךָ דָּבָר שֶׁטָּעוּן בְּרָכָה לְפָנָיו וְאֵין טָעוּן בְּרָכָה לְאַחְרָיו אֶלָּא פַּת הַבָּאָה בְּכִסָנִין בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** as you have nothing which requires a blessing before** eating **it and does not require a blessing after** eating **it,** because it is exempted by Grace after Meals, **except bread,** a sweetened and spiced pastry, **that comes as dessert,** as it, too, is a type of bread.

Rav Sheshet disagrees and takes a stricter position: *te'unim bracha bein lifneihem bein le-achareihem* — these mid-meal fruits require a *bracha* both before and after. In his view, there is no category of food that receives a *bracha* before but is then covered by *birkat ha-mazon* without its own *bracha achrona* — with one exception to be noted momentarily. The implication is that fruit eaten mid-meal, not being genuinely *zan*, does not fall within *birkat ha-mazon*'s scope.

### Rashi: Flavored Bread

Rav Sheshet's exception is *pas ha-ba'ah be-kisnin* — a form of bread-like food whose exact definition is the subject of major dispute among the *rishonim*, with significant contemporary application to things like pastries, pizza, and *bourekas*. In brief: it is bread in which some element — sweetening, spicing, filling — renders it more of a flavored or dessert item than a true *lechem*. Rashi's view is that *pas ha-ba'ah be-kisnin* is the one case in which a *bracha* is recited before (the *bracha* of *mezonot*) without a corresponding *bracha achrona*, because *birkat ha-mazon* covers it. On Rashi's reading, the broader principle is that *birkat ha-mazon* covers anything eaten in the meal that is in some sense associated with the bread — the bread functions as the organizing center, and what accompanies it, including flavored bread-like foods, is absorbed into its *bracha* sphere at the end.

Tosafot and virtually all other *rishonim* reject this interpretation and do not rule accordingly. The upshot of Rashi's position, were it followed, would be that a substantially wider range of foods eaten during a meal would require separate *brachot* before — any food that is not conceptually linked to the bread.

### R. Chiya: Full Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא: פַּת פּוֹטֶרֶת כׇּל מִינֵי מַאֲכָל, וְיַיִן פּוֹטֵר כׇּל מִינֵי מַשְׁקִים.
>
> **Translation:** The statements of both Rav Huna and Rav Sheshet **disagree with** the opinion **of Rabbi Ḥiyya, as Rabbi Ḥiyya said: Bread exempts all the types of food** that one eats after it, **and wine exempts all types of drinks** that one drinks after it, and one need not recite a blessing either before or after eating them.

The *gemara* notes that the view of both Rav Huna and Rav Nachman — requiring a *bracha* before but not after for mid-meal fruit — stands in tension with the position of Rabbi Chiya. Rabbi Chiya states: *pas poter kol minei maachal, ve-yayin poter kol minei mashke* — bread exempts all types of food, and wine exempts all types of drink. Rabbi Chiya holds that any food consumed in the context of a bread meal is covered by *ha-motzi*, requiring no additional *bracha* before or after. Similarly, wine drunk in the context of a meal at which *borei peri ha-gafen* was already recited requires no further *bracha* — a practically important ruling with implications for *kiddush* and for other drinks served alongside wine.

### Rav Papa Halacha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הִלְכְתָא דְּבָרִים הַבָּאִים מֵחֲמַת הַסְּעוּדָה בְּתוֹךְ הַסְּעוּדָה — אֵין טְעוּנִים בְּרָכָה לֹא לִפְנֵיהֶם וְלֹא לְאַחֲרֵיהֶם. וְשֶׁלֹּא מֵחֲמַת הַסְּעוּדָה בְּתוֹךְ הַסְּעוּדָה — טְעוּנִים בְּרָכָה לִפְנֵיהֶם וְאֵין טְעוּנִים בְּרָכָה לְאַחֲרֵיהֶם. לְאַחַר הַסְּעוּדָה — טְעוּנִים בְּרָכָה בֵּין לִפְנֵיהֶם בֵּין לְאַחֲרֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** Summarizing, **Rav Pappa said** that **the *halakha*** is: Food **items that come due to the meal,** which are eaten together with the bread as part of the meal, **during the meal, neither require a blessing before** eating **them nor after** eating **them,** as they are considered secondary to the bread. **And** food items like fruit, **that do not come due to the meal,** as part of the meal, but may be brought **during the meal, require a blessing before** eating **them and do not require a blessing after** eating **them.** If they come **after the meal, they require a blessing both before** eating **them and after** eating **them.**

Rav Papa rules definitively on the three categories: *devarim ha-ba'im machmat ha-seudah betoch ha-seudah* — things that come as part of the meal and are eaten during it — *ein te'unim bracha lo lifneihem ve-lo le-achareihem*. They require no *bracha* before or after; *ha-motzi* covered them at the start and *birkat ha-mazon* covers them at the end. This is the ordinary case: meat, vegetables, potatoes — whatever fills the plate of the meal. *Ve-shelo machmat ha-seudah betoch ha-seudah* — things not considered *machmat ha-seudah* but eaten during the meal — *te'unim bracha lifneihem ve-ein te'unim bracha le-achareihem*. They require a *bracha* before but *birkat ha-mazon* covers them after. *Le-achar ha-seudah* — after the meal — *te'unim bracha bein lifneihem u-vein le-achareihem*. Once the meal is concluded, any food eaten requires both a *bracha* before and a *bracha achrona* after.

### Machmas Haseuda

The decisive question is what *machmat ha-seudah* means — and here Rashi and Tosafot part ways fundamentally. Rashi holds that *machmat ha-seudah* means something directly associated with the bread: foods that one eats together with, alongside, or as a vehicle for the bread. On this reading, a dish of oatmeal eaten in the middle of a bread meal — not functioning as a bread-accompaniment — would be *shelo machmat ha-seudah* and would require its own *bracha* before. Rashi's conception is essentially bread-centric: *ha-motzi* covers what is in the bread's orbit; everything else stands outside it.

Tosafot rejects this decisively. According to Tosafot, *machmat ha-seudah* does not require any relationship to bread specifically. Rather, it refers to anything that performs a central function of the *seudah* — the meal itself — which is to say, anything that substantively fills and satiates. What falls *outside* this category is food eaten primarily for its flavor or pleasure rather than to fill: appetizers consumed for their taste before the meal truly begins, or desserts consumed after the main substance of the meal is complete.

### Tosafot vs Rashi

On Tosafot's reading, the defining criterion is functional: does this food do the work of a meal — does it fill? If yes, it is *machmat ha-seudah* and is covered by *ha-motzi* without further *bracha*. If it is present for its taste, its pleasurable qualities, its role as a *hors d'oeuvre* or a dessert, then it is *shelo machmat ha-seudah* and requires its own *bracha* before, though *birkat ha-mazon* still covers it at the end. This is the accepted *halacha*.

### Modern Meal Courses

Applying this framework to contemporary dining involves genuine difficulty. The categories of appetizer, main course, and dessert exist in modern meals as they did in pre-modern ones, but the lines are not always clear. A grapefruit served before the meal functions as an appetizer; is it *machmat ha-seudah* by virtue of being part of the meal's structure, or *shelo machmat ha-seudah* because it is not filling? Many hold that appetizers of this type do require a *bracha* before. Dessert, where the meal's sustaining purpose has been fulfilled and what follows is purely for pleasure, more clearly falls into the *shelo machmat ha-seudah* category — though in practice many do not recite a separate *bracha* on dessert. Some authorities have ruled broadly that in modern Western meals, where the courses are less sharply distinguished than in European or medieval meals, more of the meal's components can be treated as *machmat ha-seudah*.

What the *halacha* does not do is draw the line according to the bread specifically. A filling grain dish — even oatmeal, even rice — is *machmat ha-seudah* on Tosafot's view and requires no separate *bracha*, despite having no connection to bread. And a fruit dessert, however familiar a part of the meal it may feel, requires a *bracha* before because it is not there to fill. The abstract criterion — filling versus flavoring — must be applied to each meal's structure as it actually presents itself, and where the line falls will sometimes depend on the specific meal and occasion.
