# Niddah 50 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Judge vs. Witness

### Mishnah Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַכָּשֵׁר לָדוּן — כָּשֵׁר לְהָעִיד, וְיֵשׁ שֶׁכָּשֵׁר לְהָעִיד וְאֵינוֹ כָּשֵׁר לָדוּן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Any** person **who is fit to adjudicate** a case and serve as a judge **is fit to testify** as a witness, **and there are** those **who are fit to testify but are not fit to adjudicate.**

The daf begins with a *mishnah* found two lines from the bottom of 49b, part of a series of *mishnayot* structured around the logical form: everything that is A is B, but there are things that are B that are not A. The *mishnah* under discussion states: *kol hakasher ladun kasher leha'id, veyesh shekasher leha'id ve'eino kasher ladun* — anyone who is qualified to serve as a judge is qualified to serve as a witness, but there are those who are qualified as witnesses yet cannot serve as judges.

### One-Eyed Persons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְאֵתוֹיֵי סוֹמֵא בְּאַחַת מֵעֵינָיו. וּמַנִּי?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is added** by this statement, that some people are fit to testify but not to adjudicate? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** This serves **to add one who is blind in one of his eyes. And** in accordance with **whose** opinion is this ruling?

The *gemara* then asks its characteristic question: *le'atuyei mai* — what case is this coming to include? It is worth pausing on the phrasing. *Le'atuyei mai* normally functions as a secondary inquiry, introduced when a *mishnah's* main point is clear and one is asking what additional case is being swept in. Here, though, it really amounts to asking what the *mishnah's* central referent is — what is the paradigmatic case of someone who can be a witness but not a judge?

Rabbi Yochanan answers: *le'atuyei suma be'achat me'einav* — the case of someone blind in one eye. The ruling is immediately striking. Someone blind in both eyes is obviously disqualified as a witness, since the verse uses the language *o ra'ah*, requiring sight; and beyond the formal textual exclusion, one can appreciate the substantive concern that a person without sight may not accurately perceive the events about which he testifies, given how much of human sensory experience flows through vision. But a person with sight in one eye is presumed sufficient for testimony — itself something of a *chidush*, since one might have thought that the absence of depth perception or full peripheral vision introduces enough uncertainty to disqualify.

What makes the ruling counterintuitive is the asymmetry it produces. One would naturally assume that a judge, who is not directly witnessing the events but evaluating the claims of others before him, requires *less* of a visual capacity than a witness. And yet we are told the reverse: one-eyed vision suffices for testimony but disqualifies for adjudication.

### Rabbi Meir Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עַל פִּיהֶם יִהְיֶה כׇּל רִיב וְכׇל נָגַע״? וְכִי מָה עִנְיָן רִיבִים אֵצֶל נְגָעִים? מַקִּישׁ רִיבִים לִנְגָעִים: מָה נְגָעִים בַּיּוֹם, דִּכְתִיב ״וּבְיוֹם הֵרָאוֹת בּוֹ״, אַף רִיבִים בַּיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir would say: What** is the meaning when **the verse states: "According to their word shall every dispute and every leprous mark be"** (Deuteronomy 21:5)? **What do disputes have to do with leprous marks?** The verse **juxtaposes disputes to leprous marks,** to teach that **just as leprous marks** are viewed by a priest only **in the daytime, as it is written** with regard to leprous marks: **"And on the day when** raw flesh **appears in him** he shall be impure" (Leviticus 13:14), **so too, disputes** are adjudicated only **in the daytime.**

The *gemara* then asks: *umani* — whose opinion does this *mishnah* reflect? And the answer is: this is Rabbi Meir.

The *gemara* explains Rabbi Meir's position by citing a *baraita*: Rabbi Meir would ask, what is the meaning of the verse that juxtaposes *kol riv vechol nega* — all disputes and all lesions — under the authority of the *kohanim*? What is the point of placing legal judgment alongside the inspection of *tzara'at*? Rabbi Meir's answer is that the verse creates a formal linkage — *makish rivim lenegaim* — and from this *hekesh* he derives reciprocal requirements that apply to both domains.

### Leprosy Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה נְגָעִים שֶׁלֹּא בְּסוֹמֵא, דִּכְתִיב: ״לְכׇל מַרְאֵה עֵינֵי הַכֹּהֵן״, אַף רִיבִים שֶׁלֹּא בְּסוֹמֵא. וּמַקִּישׁ נְגָעִים לְרִיבִים, מָה רִיבִים שֶׁלֹּא בִּקְרוֹבִים, אַף נְגָעִים שֶׁלֹּא בִּקְרוֹבִים. אִי מָה רִיבִים בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה, אַף נְגָעִים בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה? וְדִין הוּא: מָמוֹנוֹ בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה, גּוּפוֹ לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְהוּבָא אֶל אַהֲרֹן הַכֹּהֵן אוֹ אֶל אַחַד מִבָּנָיו הַכֹּהֲנִים״, הָא לָמַדְתָּ שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ כֹּהֵן אֶחָד רוֹאֶה אֶת הַנְּגָעִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And just as leprous marks** are seen by priests who can see, but **not by blind** priests, **as it is written: "As far as appears to the priest"** (Leviticus 13:12), which teaches that they must be viewed by priests who can see with both eyes, **so too, disputes are not** adjudicated **by blind judges,** even if they are blind in only one eye. **And** the verse **juxtaposes leprous marks to disputes** to teach that **just as disputes may not** be judged **by relatives** of the litigants, **so too, leprous marks may not** be viewed **by** a priest who is **a relative** of the afflicted party. **If** these two *halakhot* are compared, one can also say that **just as disputes** are judged specifically **by three** judges, **so too, leprous marks** must be viewed **by three** priests. **And this** suggestion is supported by **a logical inference:** If a case that only involves **one's money** is adjudicated **by three** judges, **all the more so** is it **not** clear that leprous marks, which afflict the person **himself,** should be viewed by three priests? To counter this notion, **the verse states: "And he shall be brought to Aaron the priest or to one of his sons, the priests"** (Leviticus 13:2). **You** have **learned** from this **that even one priest may view leprous marks.**

The derivation proceeds in two directions. From *nega'im* to *rivim*: just as the inspection of *tzara'at* spots can only be performed during the day — as the verse states *uveyom hira'ot bo*, "on the day that the living flesh appears" — so too court cases may only be conducted during the day. This is established *halacha*. And just as the inspection of *nega'im* cannot be performed by someone blind even in one eye — as the verse states *lechol marei einei hakohen*, to the full visual field of the *kohen's* eyes — so too a judge must have full sight in both eyes.

The linkage is then run in the opposite direction as well: just as a judge cannot be a relative of the litigant, so too a relative cannot inspect the *nega'im* of his kinsman. Rabbi Meir thus introduces a formal disqualification of relatives from examining *tzara'at*, even though the standard *mishnah* in *Nega'im* seems to say the opposite — *kol nega'im adam ro'eh chutz mi'negei atzmo*, a person may inspect any *nega'im* except his own, implying that relatives may inspect each other's. This *mishnah* has a famous double meaning: one can see anyone else's faults (*nega'im*) but not one's own.

The *baraita* then raises a further inference: just as monetary cases require a court of three, perhaps *nega'im* inspection should also require three? The argument seems compelling — if the taking of someone's money requires three judges, surely the determination that someone is a *metzora*, which affects the person's very body, should require no less. The verse itself, however, forecloses this: *vehuva el Aharon hakohen o el achad mibanav hakohanim* — he shall be brought to Aharon or to one of his sons, explicitly one *kohen*. Hence even a single *kohen* suffices for *nega'im* inspection.

At this point, a broader observation is worth noting. The verse being expounded assigns these roles to the *kohanim*, yet the *gemara* is applying it to rabbinic judges. This reflects a historical transition: in the biblical period and through much of the Second Temple era, the *kohanim* served as the religious-legal authorities of the people, as expressed in *Vezot HaBerachah* — *yoru mishpatecha leYa'akov veToratecha leYisrael* — and as confirmed in the opening of *Shoftim*, where the verse directs litigants to the *kohanim haleviyim* at the appointed place. But as the Second Temple period drew to a close, and certainly after its destruction, that authority shifted to the *talmidei chachamim*. The *gemara* is thus invoking a verse that formally speaks of *kohanim*, while tacitly recognizing that the rabbinic *beit din* has inherited that judicial function. The *kohanim* retained the formal role in examining *tzara'at* — assisted, when necessary, by a *talmid chacham* who would instruct them in the relevant *halacha* before the *kohen* rendered the formal ruling — while the rabbis took over the function of sitting in judgment.

One might ask whether similar reasoning would apply to a *kohen* or judge who is colorblind — after all, *lechol marei* implies a full range of visual capacity. The *gemara* does not appear to press the analogy that far in either domain. The case of myopia and its halachic implications for *nega'im* inspection and *dayyanut* similarly remains without explicit treatment in the *sugya*, though the question is a natural one. What the *gemara* does establish is the formal rule under Rabbi Meir's reading: full binocular sight is required for a judge, even as one-eyed sight suffices for a witness.

### Blind Judge Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא סַמְיָא דַּהֲוָה בְּשִׁבָבוּתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דַּהֲוָה קָדָיֵין דִּינָא, וְלָא קָאָמַר לֵיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי. הֵיכִי עָבֵיד הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כִּסְתַם מִשְׁנָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain blind person who was** living **in the neighborhood of Rabbi Yoḥanan who would render judgments, and** Rabbi Yoḥanan **did not say anything to him.** The Gemara asks: **How could** Rabbi Yoḥanan **do this? But didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan** himself **say** that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the ruling of **an unattributed mishna?**

The *gemara* then recounts a striking story. There was a blind person — by context, blind in one eye — in the neighborhood of Rabbi Yochanan, who was actually sitting and adjudicating cases. Rabbi Yochanan did not intervene or object. The *gemara* is puzzled: Rabbi Yochanan himself had stated that this very *mishnah* — *kol hakasher ladun kasher leha'id* — comes to teach that a one-eyed person cannot be a judge. How, then, could he allow this practice to continue in his own town, where he was clearly the religious authority?

### Two Mishnayot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְנַן: כׇּל הַכָּשֵׁר לָדוּן — כָּשֵׁר לְהָעִיד, וְיֵשׁ כָּשֵׁר לְהָעִיד וְאֵין כָּשֵׁר לָדוּן, וְאָמְרִינַן: לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְאֵתוֹיֵי סוֹמֵא בְּאַחַת מֵעֵינָיו. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סְתָמָא אַחֲרִינָא אַשְׁכַּח, דִּתְנַן: דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת דָּנִין בַּיּוֹם, וְגוֹמְרִין בַּלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And we learned** in the mishna: **Any** person **who is fit to adjudicate** a case and serve as a judge **is fit to testify** as a witness, **and there are** those who are **fit to testify but** are **not fit to adjudicate. And we said: What is added** by this *halakha*, **and Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** It serves **to add one who is blind in one of his eyes,** as he is fit to testify but unfit to judge. Since Rabbi Yoḥanan evidently maintains that one who is blind even in one of his eyes is not fit to judge, why did he not admonish this judge? The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Yoḥanan found another unattributed mishna** which indicates a different conclusion. **As we learned** in the mishna in tractate *Sanhedrin* (32a): In cases of **monetary law,** the court **judges during the daytime, and concludes** the deliberations and issues their ruling even **at night.** This is not the *halakha* with regard to leprous marks, which may not be viewed at night at all. Accordingly, one does not compare judging cases of monetary law to viewing leprous marks. Since this comparison was the source for disqualifying a blind judge, one cannot derive from it that a blind judge is disqualified.

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty: *Rabbi Yochanan stama achrina ashkach* — Rabbi Yochanan found another *stam mishnah* to rely on that permits it. The principle is that *halacha* follows a *stam mishnah* — an anonymous, unattributed ruling — because such rulings carry the weight of the majority. But when two *stam mishnayot* appear to conflict, one must determine which to follow.

The competing *mishnah* comes from *Sanhedrin*, which states that *dinei mamonot* — monetary cases — may begin during the day and be concluded at night. This is relevant because the first inference Rabbi Meir drew from the *hekesh* to *nega'im* was that court proceedings must take place during the day, just as *nega'im* inspection can only occur in daylight. If the *mishnah* in *Sanhedrin* permits concluding proceedings at night, it demonstrates that the link to *nega'im* is not applied rigorously. And if the link is not rigorously applied, there is no basis to extend the further inference — that just as *nega'im* requires full binocular sight, so too must judges. The counter-proof may not be airtight, but neither was the original *mishnah* explicit about one-eyed persons. Rabbi Yochanan therefore has sufficient grounds to rely on the *Sanhedrin* *mishnah* as reflecting a non-Rabbi Meir position.

### Context Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי אוּלְמֵיהּ דְּהַאי סְתָמָא מֵהַאי סְתָמָא? אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: סְתָמָא דְּרַבִּים עֲדִיף, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מִשּׁוּם דְּקָתָנֵי לַהּ גַּבֵּי הִלְכְתָא דְּדִינֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** in **what** manner is **the strength of that unattributed** mishna in tractate *Sanhedrin* greater **than** the strength of **this unattributed** mishna here? Why did Rabbi Yoḥanan accept the ruling of that mishna? The Gemara explains: **If you wish, say** that **an unattributed** mishna that records the opinion **of many** Sages, as in *Sanhedrin*, is **preferable,** whereas the mishna here was established as in accordance with the individual opinion of Rabbi Meir. **And if you wish, say** instead that it is **because** the mishna in *Sanhedrin* **teaches this** *halakha* **in the context of the *halakhot* of judges.** Since that chapter is the primary source for all of the *halakhot* of court matters, its rulings are of greater weight.

The *gemara* then asks: *umai umada hai stama mehai stama* — what makes one *stam mishnah* superior to the other? Two answers are offered. First, we know our *mishnah* reflects Rabbi Meir's individual position. When another *stam mishnah* is identified, the second one is backed by the majority who disagree with Rabbi Meir, and we follow the majority. Second — and this is a significant methodological principle — *mishum detani lah gabi hilchesa dedayani*: we prefer the ruling taught in the context where it naturally belongs.

This second principle deserves emphasis. When there is a conflict between two *sugyot* or two *mishnayot*, there is a general inclination — widely invoked by the *Rishonim* — to follow the discussion that appears in its proper halachic context over one that arrives at the same topic only by digression. The *mishnah* in *Sanhedrin* is directly addressing the laws of judges and *dayyanut*; it is there precisely because that is where such laws belong. Our *mishnah* in *Niddah*, whatever its reason for appearing here, is addressing the topic of judges only tangentially, as part of a broader series. When these two conflict, the *Sanhedrin* ruling, being in its natural home, carries greater authoritative weight.

An additional possibility — that the blind *dayan* had been accepted by the litigants as binding arbitration, which would sidestep all formal requirements — was considered but not adopted by the *gemara*.

The upshot of this entire discussion is a possible inversion of what one might have expected. *Tosafot* notes that according to the *gemara's* conclusion — that we do not follow Rabbi Meir's link to *nega'im* — it is possible that the very reverse of Rabbi Meir's ruling holds. By Rabbi Meir: one-eyed witness valid, one-eyed judge invalid. According to the Rabbis: a witness who is blind in both eyes is still excluded by the verse *o ra'ah*, but a judge has no such explicit textual disqualification, and since we do not apply the *nega'im* link, a judge may perhaps even be blind in both eyes. The very characteristic that disqualifies for testimony — total blindness — may not disqualify for adjudication.

## Women as Judges

### Women Judges?

*Tosafot* opens a separate discussion arising from the same *mishnah* with the question: can a woman serve as a judge?

### Deborah & Verse

*Tosafot* takes it as a starting premise that a woman could indeed be a judge, drawing on two sources. The first is the explicit case of Deborah, of whom the verse states *vehi shofta et Yisrael* — she judged Israel. While the plain sense of the *Shoftim* narratives is arguably that the *shofetim* were political leaders rather than sitting *dayyanim*, the *gemara* tends to read the *shofetim* as judges in the narrow technical sense as well, as reflected in the phrase *keShmuel bedoro kYiftach bedoro, hashofet asher yihye bayamim hahem*. The second source is a *drasha* in *Bava Kama* on the verse *asher tasim lifneihem* — "that you shall place before them" — from which the *gemara* derives that the laws of the Torah apply to men and women equally: *hishvah hakatuv isha le'ish lechol dinim shebaTorah*. If women are subject to the civil laws as litigants, the same verse may extend to women as judges before whom those laws are placed.

### Witness Problem

This leads immediately to a difficulty. If a woman can be a judge, she falls under the rule of the *mishnah* itself: *kol hakasher ladun kasher leha'id* — anyone who can judge can testify. But women are formally disqualified as witnesses in the most rigorous *halachic* sense, as stated explicitly in the *mishnah* in the first chapter.

### Answer 1: Men Only

*Tosafot's* first resolution is to reread the *mishnah* as implicitly gender-specific: *kol ish hakasher ladun kasher leha'id* — the rule that every qualified judge is a qualified witness applies only to men. Women occupy a different category entirely: a woman might be a valid judge without being a valid witness, and the general equivalence principle does not apply to her.

### Alt Answers

*Tosafot* then offers two alternative approaches. The first: although women may be judged under the civil laws, they are not included in the category of *dayyanim* — the verse *asher tasim lifneihem* cannot be pressed to yield that inference, and the case of Deborah was a special dispensation, a *gezeirat hakatuv* reflecting a unique divine authorization rather than a general halachic rule. The second alternative: Deborah did not actually adjudicate cases in the formal sense at all. Rather, *lo hayta dana ela melamedet lahem et hadinim* — she would teach everyone the relevant laws, and the men would then rule on the basis of what she had taught. On this reading, *shafta* means not that she rendered formal judgments but that she was the authoritative teacher of law — what we might call a *poseket* or *Rosh Yeshiva* — while the formal court proceedings remained in the hands of male judges. This parallels the arrangement noted earlier regarding the blind *talmid chacham* who could advise the judges without formally sitting on the *beit din*.

The two positions that emerge from *Tosafot* and the broader discussion among the *Rishonim* are thus: first, that women are indeed eligible as judges, with the equivalence principle restricted to men; and second, that women cannot serve as judges, with Deborah explained either as a unique exception or as a teacher rather than an adjudicator. This is the classical debate among the *Rishonim* on the question.

It is also worth noting that the conclusion of the *gemara* regarding blind judges creates an additional exception to the general principle independent of the women's question. Even setting aside the debate about women, *Tosafot* observes that one who is blind in both eyes might be a valid judge under the Rabbis' view, yet cannot be a witness — so the equivalence principle has exceptions on both ends of the debate.

## Terumot & Ma'aserot

### Ma'aserot Tamei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֹּל שֶׁחַיָּיב בַּמַּעַשְׂרוֹת — מִטַּמְּאִין טוּמְאַת אֳוכָלִין, וְיֵשׁ שֶׁמִּטַּמְּאִין טוּמְאַת אֳוכָלִין וְאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בַּמַּעַשְׂרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Any** food from **which** one is **obligated to** separate **tithes becomes impure with the ritual impurity of food; and there is** food **that becomes impure with the ritual impurity of food but** from **which one is not obligated to** separate **tithes.**

The next *mishnah* introduces a new pair in the series: *kol she'chayav be'ma'asrot metamei tum'at ochlin* — anything obligated in *ma'aserot* can also become *tamei* as food. And, conversely, there are things that can become *tamei* as food but are not obligated in *ma'aserot*.

### Meat, Fish, Eggs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי בָּשָׂר וְדָגִים וּבֵיצִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is added** by the last clause of this mishna? The Gemara answers: This serves **to add meat, fish, and eggs.** Although they are subject to the ritual impurity of food, one is not obligated to separate tithes from them.

The *gemara* asks its standard *le'atuyei mai* and answers immediately: *le'atuyei basar ve'dagim ve'beitzim* — meat, fish, and eggs. These are clearly foods, and can become *tamei* as such, but they do not grow from the ground and are therefore not subject to *ma'aserot*. The *gemara* itself seems almost impatient with this: *peshita* — obvious.

### Pe'ah Stricter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֹּל שֶׁחַיָּיב בַּפֵּאָה — חַיָּיב בַּמַּעַשְׂרוֹת, וְיֵשׁ שֶׁחַיָּיב בַּמַּעַשְׂרוֹת וְאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בַּפֵּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **any** produce from **which** one is **obligated to** designate **produce in the corner of the field given to the poor [*pe'a*],** as commanded in the Torah (see Leviticus 19:9, 23:22), one is **obligated to** separate **tithes** from it; **and there is** produce from **which** one is **obligated to** separate **tithes but** from **which one is not obligated to** designate ***pe'a*.**

The *mishnah* then continues with the next pair: *kol she'chayav be'Pe'ah chayav be'ma'asrot* — anything obligated in *pe'ah* is also obligated in *ma'aserot*. But there are things obligated in *ma'aserot* that are not obligated in *pe'ah*. The structure implies that *pe'ah* has more stringent criteria than *ma'aserot*.

### Five Criteria

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי תְּאֵנָה וְיָרָק, שֶׁאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בַּפֵּאָה. דִּתְנַן: כְּלָל אָמְרוּ בַּפֵּאָה — כֹּל שֶׁהוּא אוֹכֶל, וְנִשְׁמָר, וְגִידּוּלוֹ מִן הָאָרֶץ, וּלְקִיטָתוֹ כְּאֶחָד, וּמַכְנִיסוֹ לְקִיּוּם — חַיָּיב בַּפֵּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is added** by the last clause of the mishna? The Gemara answers: It serves **to add figs and vegetables,** for **which** one is **not obligated to** designate ***pe'a*,** although the obligation of tithes does apply to them. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Pe'a* 1:4): The Sages **stated a principle with regard to** the *halakhot* of ***pe'a*:** With regard to **anything that is food, and is protected, and** that **grows from the earth, and is gathered as one,** i.e., there is one fixed time for gathering it, **and** that one **brings in** to store **for preservation,** its owner **is obligated to** designate ***pe'a*.**

The *gemara* cites the first *mishnah* in *Pe'ah* to establish the full set of criteria: *kol she'hu ochel* — it must be a food; *venishmar* — it must be guarded, meaning it is not *hefker*, not abandoned to all comers; *vegidulo min ha'aretz* — it must grow from the ground; *velekitato ke'echad* — it must be harvested all at once, not piecemeal over time; and *umachniso l'kiyum* — it must be storable for an extended period, not perishable. These five criteria must all be met for a crop to be obligated in *pe'ah*.

The *mishnah* in *Pe'ah* then specifies what each criterion excludes. *Ochel* excludes plants grown for dye — even if they fill an entire field, they are not food. *Venishmar* excludes *hefker* produce. *Vegidulo min ha'aretz* excludes fungi and mushrooms — though they are found in soil, they are not genuinely *grown* from the ground. *Velekitato ke'echad* excludes figs — figs ripen progressively and are harvested at various intervals rather than all at once. *Umachniso l'kiyum* excludes vegetables — they are perishable and cannot be stored for extended periods.

### Ma'aserot Criteria

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹכֵל — לְמַעוֹטֵי סְפִיחֵי סְטֵיס וְקוֹצָה, וְנִשְׁמָר — לְמַעוֹטֵי הֶפְקֵר, וְגִידּוּלוֹ מִן הָאָרֶץ — לְמַעוֹטֵי כְּמֵהִים וּפִטְרִיּוֹת, וּלְקִיטָתוֹ כְּאֶחָד — לְמַעוֹטֵי תְּאֵנָה, וּמַכְנִיסוֹ לְקִיּוּם — לְמַעוֹטֵי יָרָק. וְאִילּוּ גַּבֵּי מַעֲשֵׂר תְּנַן: כֹּל שֶׁהוּא אוֹכֶל, וְנִשְׁמָר, וְגִידּוּלוֹ מִן הָאָרֶץ — חַיָּיב בַּמַּעַשְׂרוֹת, וְאִילּוּ לְקִיטָתוֹ כְּאֶחָד, וּמַכְנִיסוֹ לְקִיּוּם — לָא קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes each criterion of the mishna. The clause: **Anything that is food,** serves **to exclude** the ***sefiḥin*,** produce that grew without being intentionally planted, **of woad [*setim*] and safflower [*vekotza*].** These plants are used as dyes rather than for food. The clause: **And is protected,** serves **to exclude ownerless** crops, which no one protects. The clause: **And grows from the earth,** serves **to exclude truffles and mushrooms,** which do not draw sustenance from the ground. The clause: **And is gathered as one,** serves **to exclude** the **fig** tree, whose fruit is gathered over an extended period, as the figs do not all ripen at the same time. Finally, the clause: **And** that **one brings in** to storage **for preservation,** serves **to exclude vegetables,** which cannot be stored for lengthy periods. **And yet** in the case **of tithe, we learned** in a mishna (*Ma'asrot* 1:1) with regard to the *halakhot* of tithes: **Anything that is food, and is protected, and grows from the earth is obligated in tithes. And whereas** some of the conditions overlap, the following criteria **are not taught** with regard to tithes: **Gathered as one, and that one brings in** to storage **for preservation.** Evidently, the obligation of tithes applies to fig trees and vegetables, despite the fact that the obligation of *pe'a* does not apply.

The first *mishnah* in *Ma'asrot* then applies only three of the five criteria: *kol she'hu ochel, venishmar, vegidulo min ha'aretz* — food, guarded, grown from the ground. The final two criteria — *lelkitato ke'echad* and *umachniso l'kiyum* — are absent from the *ma'aserot* list entirely. The *gemara* presents these two *mishnayot* side by side to demonstrate this disparity with elegant economy.

### Figs & Veggies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם הָיוּ בָּהֶם שׁוּמִים וּבְצָלִים — חַיָּיבִין, דִּתְנַן: מַלְבְּנוֹת בְּצָלִים שֶׁבֵּין הַיָּרָק, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: פֵּאָה מִכׇּל אַחַת וְאֶחָת, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מֵאַחַת עַל הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that **if** these vegetables that are exempt from *pe'a* **contained garlic and onions,** which are stored for an extended period of time, then they are **obligated** in *pe'a* as well. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Pe'a* 3:4): If one has **garden beds of onions that are between the vegetables, Rabbi Yosei says** that one leaves a separate ***pe'a* from each and every one** of the beds. **And the Rabbis say** one leaves *pe'a* **from one** garden bed **for all** of them.

The practical consequence follows immediately. Figs, excluded from *pe'ah* because they are not harvested all at once, and vegetables, excluded because they are perishable, are both obligated in *ma'aserot*, since those two criteria do not apply there. These are the cases that fulfill the *mishnah's* claim that there are things obligated in *ma'aserot* but not in *pe'ah*.

### Rashi: Grain Model

Two explanations are offered for why *pe'ah* requires the additional criteria. Rashi explains that *pe'ah* is modeled after grain — grain is the paradigmatic crop, it is harvested all at once, and it is storable. The Torah's own idiom, *dagan tirosh veyitzhar* — grain, wine, and oil — reflects this model, and *pe'ah* follows grain as its prototype.

### Tosafot: Poor

*Tosafot* presses a question against Rashi's answer: if the model for *pe'ah* is grain, that same grain-model should apply to *ma'aserot* as well — why don't the same two additional criteria apply there? *Tosafot* proposes a different and illuminating rationale. With *pe'ah*, the poor must physically hover nearby and wait for the owner to finish harvesting so they can collect the corner of the field. With *ma'aserot*, by contrast, there is no such hovering — the owner processes the produce, separates the tithes, and distributes them later; the poor are not waiting on the spot.

*Tosafot* therefore argues that the *Chachamim* deliberately excluded figs and vegetables from *pe'ah* out of concern for the poor themselves. Figs ripen gradually; if the poor were to stand around waiting for the owner to complete his fig harvest — a process spread over weeks — they would be forgoing better opportunities to collect *pe'ah* elsewhere. The *Chachamim* did not want to impose this burden on the poor. Vegetables present a similar logic: the quantity and value of vegetable *pe'ah* is modest, *eino shaveh ela me'at*, and if the poor were to wait until evening for an owner to finish harvesting vegetables, they would lose more than they gained. This is admittedly a paternalistic rationale, but *Tosafot* accepts that legislation for society sometimes requires anticipating that people will not always act in their own best interest.

### Biblical Fruits

Crucially, both Rashi and *Tosafot* agree on a further foundational point: none of these obligations — neither figs nor vegetables in *ma'aserot* nor their *pe'ah* equivalents — are biblical. The biblical obligation of *ma'aserot* and *pe'ah* is limited to *dagan tirosh veyitzhar* — grain, wine, and oil. All extension of these obligations to other produce is rabbinic in origin. There is some debate among the *Rishonim* about whether fruit other than grapes and olives carries a biblical *ma'aserot* obligation, but Rashi and *Tosafot* in this *sugya* hold that it does not. Vegetables are universally agreed to be rabbinic.

### Garlic & Onions

Within the category of vegetables, the *gemara* notes one important exception. *Im hayu bahem shumim uvetzalim* — if there were garlic and onions among the vegetables — *chayavin* — they are in fact obligated in *pe'ah*, since garlic and onions satisfy the criterion of *machniso l'kiyum*: they can be stored for extended periods, unlike most vegetables. The *mishnah* in *Pe'ah* records a debate between Rabbi Yosei and the *Chachamim* about whether alternating rows of onions between other vegetable rows are treated as one field or many for the purpose of *pe'ah* — *pe'ah mikol achat ve'achat* versus *me'achat al hakol* — but both agree that onions are in fact obligated.

## Dead Birds & Intent

### Grass Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חַנָּה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עוּלְשִׁין שֶׁזְּרָעָן מִתְּחִילָּה לִבְהֵמָה, וְנִמְלַךְ עֲלֵיהֶן לָאָדָם — צְרִיכוֹת מַחְשָׁבָה לִכְשֶׁיִּתָּלְשׁוּ, קָסָבַר מַחְשֶׁבֶת חִבּוּר לֹא שְׁמָהּ מַחְשָׁבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In the case of **endives that were initially planted** to be fed **to animals, and** later the owner **reconsidered their** designation and decided to use them **for human** consumption, **they require intent** to be used for human consumption **once they are detached** from the ground, in order for them to be susceptible to ritual impurity. Rabbi Yoḥanan rules in this manner because he **holds** that **intent** to designate produce while it is **attached** to the ground **is not considered intent.**

The *gemara* cites a ruling of Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *ulshin shezara'an mitechilah livheimah* — if one planted a certain type of grass (something akin to alfalfa) with the initial intention of using it as animal feed, and then reconsidered while it was still growing — *ve'nimlach aleihem le'adam* — and decided to harvest it for human consumption instead, does this new intention change the plant's status, rendering it food for purposes of becoming *tamei*?

The ruling is: *tzrichah machshavah lecsheyintaleshu* — the intention to repurpose it as human food takes effect only once it has been uprooted, not while it is still growing. The principle underlying this is *machshevet chibur lo shemah machshavah* — intention regarding something still attached to the ground is not considered meaningful intention. The logic is straightforward: since produce cannot become *tamei* while it is attached to the ground, its identity and status as food cannot be defined while it is in that state either. Only after separation from the ground does intention become operative.

### Dead Bird Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא, שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר דְּבָרִים נֶאֶמְרוּ בְּנִבְלַת עוֹף טָהוֹר, וְזֶה אֶחָד מֵהֶן: צְרִיכָה מַחְשָׁבָה וְאֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה הֶכְשֵׁר. אַלְמָא מַחְשֶׁבֶת חַיִּים לֹא שְׁמָהּ מַחְשָׁבָה, הָכָא נָמֵי מַחְשֶׁבֶת חִבּוּר לֹא שְׁמָהּ מַחְשָׁבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: We learn** in a mishna (*Teharot* 1:1) **as well: Thirteen matters were stated with regard to the carcass of a kosher bird, and this is one of them:** In order for such a carcass to be susceptible to impurity and to be capable of imparting impurity to food through contact, it **requires** a person's **intention** to eat it, **but it does not require** exposure to liquid to be **rendered susceptible** to impurity. Even if one had intent to eat the bird while it was still alive, intent is still required after it became a carcass for it to transmit impurity. Rava concludes: **Evidently, intent** that the bird be eaten while the bird is **alive is not considered intent. Here too, intent** to designate produce while it is **attached** to the ground **is not considered intent.**

Rava adduces a parallel from a *mishnah* in *Taharot* about the *neveilat of tahor* — the carcass of a kosher bird. This *mishnah* lists thirteen laws that govern such a carcass, and one of them is: *tzrichah machshavah ve'einah tzrichah hechsheir* — it requires intention to be treated as food in order to convey *tumah* by contact, but it does not require the preliminary preparation (*hechsheir*, i.e., contact with liquid) that normally renders food susceptible to *tumah*.

This *halacha* is itself striking. Normally, for food to become *tamei* it must first be made susceptible through contact with liquid, and then an external *tamei* object must touch it. The *neveilat of tahor* bypasses both requirements: it does not need *hechsheir* because it is itself a source of *tumah* when swallowed, meaning it essentially already has its own *tumah* built in, becoming *tamei* as a *rishon* without needing contact with anything else. But this elevated status activates only once the carcass is designated as human food — without that intention, it is inert.

Now, what does this prove for the case of the grass? Rava argues: *alma machshevet chayim lo shemah machshavah* — intention formed while the bird was alive carries no weight. Presumably the chicken wandering in one's yard was always thought of as future food — one intended to *shecht* it eventually. Yet the *mishnah* says that once it dies, a new act of designation is required. This demonstrates that the prior intention, formed while the bird was alive, does not persist or count. By analogy, intention formed while something is attached to the ground also does not carry forward.

### Wild Bird

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר: הָכָא בְּגוֹזָל שֶׁנָּפַל מִן הָרוּם עָסְקִינַן, דְּלָא הֲוָה קַמַּן דְּלַחְשׁוֹב עֲלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said:** Rava's proof is inconclusive, as **here we are dealing with a young bird that fell from a height, where** the bird **was not before us** prior to it becoming a carcass, **so that** one could have had **intent that it** is food. Consequently, it requires intent afterward for it to impart impurity, but had there been intent while it was still alive, that would have sufficed.

Rabbi Zeira objects to Rava's proof: the *mishnah* about the *neveilat of tahor* may be describing a case of a wild bird that fell from the sky — *gozal shenafal min ha'agam* — where there was never any prior intention at all, simply because the bird was not under anyone's control. In such a case, there was no *machshavah mechayim* to begin with. Rabbi Zeira is not conceding that prior intention fails to carry over; he is suggesting the *mishnah* is describing a scenario where there was no prior intention to begin with, and therefore the *mishnah* tells us you need *machshavah* after death — but it leaves open the question of whether *machshavah* when alive would have sufficed.

### Feral Chicken

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: תַּרְנְגוֹלֶת שֶׁבְּיַבְנֶה, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תַּרְנְגוֹל בָּרָא הֲוָה. אַחִיכוּ עֲלֵיהּ: תַּרְנְגוֹל בָּרָא עוֹף טָמֵא הוּא, וְעוֹף טָמֵא מִי קָמְטַמֵּא? אָמַר לְהוּ אַבָּיֵי: גַּבְרָא רַבָּה אֲמַר מִילְּתָא, לָא תְּחִיכוּ עֲלֵיהּ, בְּתַרְנְגוֹלֶת שֶׁמָּרְדָה, וּמַאי ״בָּרָא״? דְּאִיבְּרַאי מִמָּרַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rabbi Zeira: **What is there to say** about the case of the **chicken in Yavne?** In that instance the Sages deemed the chicken impure due to intent only after it became a carcass, despite the fact that it was present before them while it was alive. This apparently indicates that intent which occurred while the bird was alive is not considered intent. Rabbi Zeira **said to** Abaye: That **was** actually **a wild chicken,** which was not before them while it was alive, and therefore there was no intent that it should be food while it was alive. Those who heard this comment **laughed at** Rabbi Zeira's interpretation: **A wild chicken is a non-kosher bird, and does a non-kosher bird impart impurity?** Abaye **said to them: A great man has stated a matter; do not laugh at him.** Rabbi Zeira means that this is referring **to a chicken that rebelled** against its owner, ran away to the wild, and raised its kosher chicks there. One of those chicks subsequently returned from the wild. Consequently, it had not been present before the Sages while it was alive. **And what** did Rabbi Zeira mean when he mentioned **a wild [*bara*]** chicken? He meant one **that was created [*de'ivrai*] from** a chicken **that rebelled.**

Abaye responds by invoking the forthcoming case of the chicken in *Yavneh*: if we are going to say that wild birds are the paradigmatic case, how do we explain that case, which clearly involves a *tarnegol* — a domesticated chicken? One cannot dismiss the precedent so easily.

Rabbi Zeira's answer is then worked out: *tarnigol habar hava* — the bird in question was a wild chicken. Abaye objects that a wild chicken (*tarnigol habar*) is a non-kosher species, and the laws of *neveilat of tahor* apply specifically to the carcass of a *tahor* bird. The *gemara* therefore clarifies: *be-tarnigola de-marda* — it is not a different species but rather a domesticated chicken that had escaped and become feral, thus coming under neither its owner's control nor his prior intention. As Abaye cautions: *gavra rabba amar milsa, lo tichachei lei* — a great person said something; do not mock the answer. It is a perfectly valid scenario: the chicken broke free, became wild, and was no longer subject to any *machshavah mechayim*.

Rashi raises a residual question: if the chicken was once in the owner's yard and he had thought of it as food then, why does that prior designation not persist after it goes feral? Rashi himself resolves this by suggesting the case involves chicks born to the feral chicken after it escaped — they are of a kosher species but were never under the owner's control or intention at any point.

### Swamp Chickens

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: תַּרְנְגוֹלְתָּא דְּאַגְמָא הֲוַאי. רַב פָּפָּא לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: תַּרְנְגוֹלָא דְּאַגְמָא אֲסִיר, תַּרְנְגוֹלְתָּא דְּאַגְמָא שַׁרְיָא. וְסִימָנָיךְ — ״עַמּוֹנִי וְלֹא עַמּוֹנִית״. דָּרַשׁ מָרִימָר: תַּרְנְגוֹלְתָּא דְּאַגְמָא אֲסִירָא. חַזְיוּהָ רַבָּנַן דְּדָרְסָה וְאָכְלָה, וְהַיְינוּ גֵּירוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa stated** an alternative interpretation: **It was a marsh [*de'agma*] hen.** Since no one lives in a marsh, there was no opportunity for intent while it was alive, and therefore intent was necessary afterward. The Gemara adds: **Rav Pappa** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning** in this regard, **as Rav Pappa said:** The animal called **the marsh rooster is prohibited,** as it is a non-kosher bird, whereas **the marsh hen is** kosher and **permitted.** **And your mnemonic** to remember which animal is permitted and which is prohibited is the well-known statement of the Sages with regard to the verse: "An Ammonite or a Moabite shall not enter into the assembly of the Lord" (Deuteronomy 23:4). **An Ammonite** man is unfit to enter the assembly **but not an Ammonite woman.** Here too, the animal with a female name is permitted, whereas the one with a male name is prohibited. **Mareimar taught: The marsh hen is prohibited,** contrary to the opinion of Rav Pappa, as **the Sages saw that it mauled and ate** its prey. **And this is** the ***geiruta*,** a non-kosher bird.

Rav Pappa offers yet another explanation of the case: *tarnigolta de-agma hava* — it was a species of swamp chickens, wild but kosher, and therefore a suitable case for *neveilat of tahor*. This accords with Rav Pappa's own position: *tarnigola de-agma assur, tarnigolta de-agma sharya* — the swamp rooster is forbidden but the swamp hen is permissible. The mnemonic offered is *amoni ve'lo amonit* — the male Ammonite is forbidden, not the female.

### Mareimar Dispute

*Tosafot* initially considers whether within a single species the male might display non-kosher signs (*simanim*) while the female does not — making it a genuine case of one gender being kosher and the other not within the same species. But *Tosafot* ultimately rejects this: at the level of halachic species identification, what matters is not the *simanim* alone but the identification of the species itself as one the Torah intended to prohibit. *Tosafot* concludes that the "swamp roosters" and "swamp hens" are in fact two entirely distinct species that happen to share evocative names — much as the name "ladybug" does not imply the species has only female members.

Mareimar disagrees with Rav Pappa altogether and rules that even the swamp hens are forbidden.

### Non-Kosher Signs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַזְיוּהָ רַבָּנַן דְּדָרְסָה וְאָכְלָה, וְהַיְינוּ גֵּירוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **the Sages saw that it mauled and ate** its prey. **And this is** the ***geiruta*,** a non-kosher bird.

The basis for Mareimar's ruling is empirical: the rabbis observed that these birds *darsa ve'achla* — they trampled their food and ate. Trampling is a classic indicator of a non-kosher bird. Rashi offers two interpretations of what *derisah* entails: either the bird holds food down with its talon while eating part of it, or it picks up food with its claws and eats in that manner. Either way, using the claw in the act of eating is the characteristic behavior (*darsa*) that marks a bird as non-kosher. This swamp species, whatever its appearance, exhibited that behavior and was therefore classified as *tamei*.

## Yavneh Chicken

### Kal V'Chomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: גּוֹזָל שֶׁנָּפַל לַגַּת, וְחִשַּׁב עָלָיו לְהַעֲלוֹתוֹ לְכוּתִי — טָמֵא, לְכֶלֶב — טָהוֹר. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי אוֹמֵר: אַף לְכֶלֶב — טָמֵא. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי: קַל וָחוֹמֶר, אִם מְטַמְּאָה טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה שֶׁלֹּא בְּמַחְשָׁבָה — לֹא יְטַמֵּא טוּמְאָה קַלָּה שֶׁלֹּא בְּמַחְשָׁבָה?!
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the case of a young kosher bird that fell and died, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **A young** kosher **bird that fell into a winepress** and died there, **where** the owner **intended to draw it up** from the press **for a Samaritan** to eat, is **ritually impure,** like any carcass of a kosher bird. If he intended to draw it up from the press **for a dog** to eat, it is **ritually pure. Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri says: Even** if he intended to draw it up from the press **for a dog** to eat, it is **impure,** as the bird does not require intent for it to be impure. **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri says** in explanation of his opinion: This *halakha* can be derived by the following ***a fortiori*** inference: **If** the carcass of a kosher bird **transmits severe** ritual **impurity,** i.e., it renders one's garments impure when an olive-bulk of it is in the throat, **without thought,** i.e., even if no one had intent that a person should eat it, should it **not transmit a lenient impurity** of food, by touch alone, likewise **without thought?**

The *gemara* now turns to the *tannaic* dispute introduced by the case of the bird in the wine vat. A *baraita* records: if a bird fell into a vat (*gat*), died, and the finder decided to give it to a non-Jew for food — *tamei*. Designating it as food for a non-Jew suffices, since the requirement is only that it be considered human food, not Jewish food specifically. If the finder decided to give it to a dog — *tahor*, since dog food does not qualify. Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri dissents and holds that even designation for a dog renders it *tamei* — but as the *gemara* will explain, his reasoning is not that animal food suffices; rather, in his view, no designation is required at all.

Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri then presses a *kal va'chomer*: if swallowing this bird creates a heavy *tumah* — it becomes an *av hatumah* — without any intention whatsoever (one who swallows it accidentally is still *tamei*), then surely it should at least produce the lighter *tumah* of a *rishon* even without intention. The heavier *tumah* requires no *machshavah*; why should the lighter require more?

### Two Tumah Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לֹא, אִם אָמַרְתָּ בְּטוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה, שֶׁכֵּן אֵינָהּ יוֹרֶדֶת לְכָךְ, תֹּאמַר בְּטוּמְאָה קַלָּה, שֶׁכֵּן יוֹרֶדֶת לְכָךְ!
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to** Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri: **No,** this is not a valid *a fortiori* inference. **If you said** that there is no requirement of intent **with regard to severe ritual impurity, that** is because severe ritual impurity **does not assume** its status **with that** requirement of thought, i.e., intent is not relevant to that type of impurity. **Shall you** also **say** that there is no requirement of thought **with regard to lenient impurity, which does assume** its status **with that** requirement of thought? The Gemara will soon explain the precise meaning of this concept of assuming its status with the requirement of thought.

The *Chachamim* respond: the two forms of *tumah* are not structurally equivalent. The heavy *tumah* produced by swallowing does not operate through the bird's identity as food — it is a distinct category of *tumah* rooted in the act of consumption itself, not in the object's status as *ochel*. Therefore, intention to treat it as food is irrelevant. But the lighter *tumah* — the *rishon* status it acquires by contact when designated as food — is entirely predicated on its identity as food. *Sheken yardah lach* — that category depends on the *ochel* status. Without designation as food, there is nothing to become *tamei* through contact.

### Yavneh Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן: תַּרְנְגוֹלֶת שֶׁבְּיַבְנֶה תּוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁיּוֹרֶדֶת לְכָךְ, וְטִמְּאוּהָ שֶׁלֹּא בְּמַחְשָׁבָה. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מִשָּׁם רְאָיָה? כּוּתִים הָיוּ שָׁם, וְחִשְּׁבוּ עָלֶיהָ לַאֲכִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri **said to** the Rabbis: The case of the **chicken in Yavne can prove** that the question of whether or not intent is required does not depend on that factor. The case in Yavne involved an item **that does assume** its status **with that** requirement of thought, **and** yet the Sages **declared it impure without intent.** The Rabbis **said to** Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri: Will you cite **proof from there?** In that case **there were Samaritans there, and** the people in Yavne **intended that it be eaten** by the Samaritans.

Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri then invokes the chicken in *Yavneh*: there was a case there where a bird was treated as *tamei* even though no one had explicitly designated it as food — *timuah shelo bemachshavah*. If the *Chachamim* are correct that *machshavah* is required, how was that chicken ruled *tamei*?

The *Chachamim* deflect this proof entirely: *Kutim hayu sham* — there were non-Jews in *Yavneh* at the time, and they had intended to eat the chicken. The chicken was not *tamei* without *machshavah*; it was *tamei precisely because* there was *machshavah* — that of the non-Jewish residents who had designated it as their food. Non-Jewish intention to eat something is sufficient to render it food for these purposes.

### City vs. Village

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא בִּכְרַכִּים, לְמָה לַהּ מַחְשָׁבָה? וְהָתְנַן: נִבְלַת בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה בְּכׇל מָקוֹם, וְנִבְלַת עוֹף טָהוֹר וְהַחֵלֶב בַּכְּרַכִּים — אֵין צְרִיכִין לֹא מַחְשָׁבָה וְלֹא הֶכְשֵׁר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the case of a young kosher bird that fell into a winepress: **What** type of situation **are we dealing with? If we say** that we are dealing **with cities,** where there are many people available to consume all sorts of food, including the carcass of a kosher bird, **why does it** require **intent? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Okatzin* 3:3): **An unslaughtered carcass of a kosher animal in any location,** whether the population is large or small, **and the carcass of a kosher bird or the fat** of a kosher animal found **in cities [*bakerakim*], require neither intent** for human consumption **nor** contact with liquid for them to be rendered **susceptible** to impurity?

The *gemara* then steps back to ask a structural question about the entire debate: *be'mai askinan* — where is this taking place, in a city or in a village? The distinction matters because the *mishnah* in *Taharot* draws a sharp geographic line. In cities, certain things that might not normally be considered food are routinely eaten by the urban population and therefore require no *machshavah* — they are automatically classified as food. In villages, the same items are not normally consumed and therefore do require explicit *machshavah* to achieve food status.

The *mishnah* enumerates specific cases. The carcass of a kosher animal (*neveilat behemah tehorah*) requires no *machshavah* anywhere — both city and village populations will eat it. The carcass of a kosher bird (*neveilat of tahor*) and forbidden fat (*chelev*) in cities require no *machshavah* — urban populations are presumed to eat such things. The carcass of a kosher bird in villages, and the carcass of a non-kosher animal everywhere, require *machshavah* — these are not foods that populations normally consume, and explicit designation is required.

### Vat Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בִּכְפָרִים, וּמִי אִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר דְּלָא בָּעֲיָא מַחְשָׁבָה? וְהָתְנַן: נִבְלַת בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה בְּכׇל מָקוֹם, וְנִבְלַת עוֹף טָהוֹר בַּכְּפָרִים — צְרִיכָה מַחְשָׁבָה וְאֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה הֶכְשֵׁר! אָמַר רַבִּי זְעֵירָא בַּר חֲנִינָא: לְעוֹלָם בִּכְרַךְ, וְגִתּוֹ מֵאֲסַתּוּ, וַעֲשָׂאַתּוּ כִּכְפָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it must be referring **to villages,** where the population is small and there are not many people who would eat the carcass. **But** this too is difficult: **Is there anyone who said that intent is not required** in the case of a carcass of a non-kosher bird for the impurity of food? **Didn't we learn** in the beginning of that same mishna: **A carcass of a non-kosher animal** found **in any location, and an unslaughtered carcass of a kosher bird in the villages** both **require intent** to consume them, **but they do not require** contact with a liquid to become **susceptible** to ritual impurity? **Rabbi Zeira bar Ḥanina says: Actually,** the *baraita* is referring to a case that occurred **in a city,** and even so intent is required. The reason is that the young bird fell into a winepress, **and** the **winepress rendered it disgusting and** thereby **rendered it like** the carcass of a kosher bird in **a village,** where there are few people who would eat it.

Rabbi Zeira bar Chanina resolves this: *le'olam bekrach* — the case of the *Yavneh* chicken is indeed set in a city. Normally in a city, no *machshavah* would be needed. But here, *degito me'asto va'asao kekfar* — the fact that the bird had fallen into the vat made it repellent to potential consumers, effectively downgrading it to the status of a village case where *machshavah* would ordinarily be required.

The dispute between Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri and the *Chachamim* thus hinges on whether the city's general presumption of edibility overrides the off-putting circumstances of the particular case. Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri holds that since we are in a city, the general rule of automatic food status applies regardless of the vat — no *machshavah* is ever needed for a bird in a city, even under these conditions. The *Chachamim* hold that the vat has rendered this specific bird unappetizing enough that it no longer carries the city's default presumption, and *machshavah* is therefore required. The *gemara* breaks here, and this analysis will continue on the following daf.
