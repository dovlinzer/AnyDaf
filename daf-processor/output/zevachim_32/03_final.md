# Zevachim 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Psulim & Shechita

### Kol Hapsulim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַפְּסוּלִין שֶׁשָּׁחֲטוּ – שְׁחִיטָתָן כְּשֵׁרָה, שֶׁהַשְּׁחִיטָה כְּשֵׁרָה בְּזָרִים וּבְנָשִׁים וּבַעֲבָדִים וּבִטְמֵאִים. וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִהְיוּ טְמֵאִים נוֹגְעִין בַּבָּשָׂר. לְפִיכָךְ הֵן פּוֹסְלִין בְּמַחְשָׁבָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **all** those **who are unfit** for Temple service **who slaughtered** an offering, **their slaughter is valid, as the slaughter** of an offering **is valid** *ab initio* when performed even **by non-priests, by women, by** Canaanite **slaves, and by ritually impure** individuals. **And** this is the *halakha* **even with** regard to **offerings of the most sacred order, provided that the ritually impure will not touch the flesh** of the slaughtered animal, thereby rendering it impure. **Therefore,** these unfit individuals can **disqualify** the offering **with** prohibited **intent,** e.g., if one of them intended to partake of the offering beyond its designated time or outside its designated area.

The new *perek* opens with a sweeping declaration: *kol happsulim sheshachtu shechitatan kesherah* — any invalid person who performs the act of *shechita* has done so validly. The category of *pasul* here includes a non-*Kohen*, an *onen*, a *tamei* person, and others of similarly compromised status. The reason is fundamental: *shechita* requires no particular personal qualification. Even for *kodshei kodashim*, the holiest class of sacrifices, *shechita* is *kasher* performed by non-*Kohanim*, by women, by slaves, and by those in a state of *tumah* — *bezarim uvenashim uvavadim uvitmeim*, even *bekodshei kodashim*. The single operative restriction is *uvilvad shelo yiheyu tameim nogim babasar*: if the *shochet* is *tamei*, he must take care not to touch the meat and thereby transmit impurity to it.

### Machshava of Shochet

A direct consequence of this ruling concerns the role of intent. Because the act of *shechita* performed by a *pasul* person is itself legitimate, the *machshava* — the operative thought — of the person actually wielding the knife is legally meaningful. If any of these *psulim* performs *shechita* while entertaining a *piggul* thought, their thought has real halachic effect. The corollary is equally important: the thought of the owner is legally irrelevant to *shechita*. It is not ownership but the identity of the one performing the *avoda* that determines whose *machshava* counts.

### Shechita Fundamental

This principle flows directly from *shechita*'s status as one of the four foundational acts — *shechita*, *kabbalat dam*, *holachah*, and *zerikah* — that together render a *korban kasher*. For three of those four acts, a *Kohen* specifically must perform them, and so only a *Kohen*'s intent is operative. But *shechita* belongs to whoever does it. The act is not tethered to *kehunah* and is not conditioned on the performer being the owner. Therefore, whoever performs the *shechita* — *Kohen* or non-*Kohen*, owner or stranger — it is that person's *machshava* that matters.

### Tosafot on Women

*Tosafot* raises two important observations here. First, *Tosafot* emphasizes that women may perform *shechita* on *korbanot* even *lechatchila*, rejecting any *chumra* to the contrary suggested by a work called *Hilchot Eretz Yisrael*. Second, *Tosafot* addresses the practical difficulty posed by a *tamei shochet*. A *tamei* person, depending on the nature of the *tumah*, may be required to stand outside *Ezrat Nashim*. If so, how could such a person perform *shechita* at all? The answer lies in the fact — learned earlier in the first *perek* — that the *shochet* need not stand inside the *Beit Hamikdash* at all, since *shechita* is *kasher* performed by a non-*Kohen*. The *behema* stands on one side of the boundary line and the *tamei shochet* stands on the other side, cutting across with a sufficiently long knife. This works as long as the *tamei*'s *tumah* only requires exclusion from the *Azara* proper. If the *tumah* is of such severity as to require standing outside *Ezrat Nashim* as well, the practical arrangement becomes considerably more difficult to envision.

## Kabbalat Dam Rules

### Vechulan Shekiblu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלָּן שֶׁקִּיבְּלוּ אֶת הַדָּם חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ וְחוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ – אִם יֵשׁ דַּם הַנֶּפֶשׁ, יַחְזוֹר הַכָּשֵׁר וִיקַבֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to **all of them,** in a case **where they collected the blood** with the intent to offer it **beyond its** designated **time or outside its** designated **area, if there is blood of the soul** that remains in the animal, **the** priest **fit** for Temple service **should again collect** the blood and sprinkle it on the altar.

The *Mishnah* continues: *vechulan shekiblu et hadam chutz lizmano vechutz limkomo* — all of these *psulim*, if they received the blood with a *machshava* of wrong time or wrong place, they do not render the *korban* *piggul* and they do not actively invalidate it. This is the key structural principle: because their act has no positive validity, their improper *machshava* equally lacks effect. They cannot make *piggul* and they cannot positively disqualify. The *korban* remains in an undetermined state — not rendered valid, but not actively ruined.

### Chutz Lizmano

From this principle follows a practical remedy: if the blood was received by a *pasul* person with an intent of *chutz lizmano* or *chutz limkomo*, the *korban* is not yet lost. *Im yesh dam hanefesh* — if the animal's lifeblood is still flowing from the neck — *yachazor hakasher veyekabel*: a *kasher* person should return and receive the remaining blood. If all the blood was already caught by the *pasul* person and none remains, then nothing further can be done. The *korban* cannot be rendered valid because no *kasher* person performed the *kabbalah*, but equally it was not affirmatively disqualified. The critical point is that nothing was accomplished — neither for good nor for ill — by the *pasul*'s act of reception.

### Psul No Active Stigma

This brings into sharp relief an important structural *halacha*: a *pasul*'s performance of an *avoda* act does not constitute an active *psul*. It is not that the act is done and then damages the *korban*; rather, the act simply fails to accomplish what a valid performance would have accomplished. There is no affirmative disqualification. Therefore, wherever there is an opportunity to redo the act validly — wherever more blood remains, wherever a *kasher* person can step in — one should do so. The contrast is instructive: if an animal was slaughtered incorrectly, there is no possibility of a second *shechita*. But if blood was caught by a *pasul*, that failure to constitute valid *kabbalah* is not a one-way door. If more blood is available, a *kasher* person may catch it and the *korban* may yet be saved. This same logic extends, at least in principle, to *zerikah* — throwing the blood — though the *Gemara* will examine whether that final act carries distinct complications.

## Nitenat Locations

### Kibel Kasher Natan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קִיבֵּל הַכָּשֵׁר וְנָתַן לַפָּסוּל – יַחְזִיר לַכָּשֵׁר. קִיבֵּל בִּימִינוֹ וְנָתַן לִשְׂמֹאלוֹ – יַחְזִיר לִימִינוֹ. קִיבֵּל בִּכְלִי קֹדֶשׁ וְנָתַן לִכְלִי חוֹל – יַחְזִיר לִכְלֵי קֹדֶשׁ. נִשְׁפַּךְ מִן הַכְּלִי עַל הָרִצְפָּה וַאֲסָפוֹ – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** If **the** priest **fit** for Temple service **collected** the blood in a vessel **and gave** the vessel **to an unfit** person, that person **should return** it **to the fit** priest. If the priest **collected** the blood in a vessel **in his right** hand **and moved** it **to his left** hand, **he should return** it **to his right** hand. If the priest **collected** the blood **in a sacred vessel and placed it in a non-sacred vessel, he should return** the blood **to a sacred vessel.** If the blood **spilled from the vessel onto the floor and he gathered it** from the floor, it is **valid.**

The *Mishnah* next addresses a case of handoff: *kibel hakasher venatan lapasul* — a *kasher Kohen* received the blood and then transferred it to a *pasul Kohen* for transport. The ruling: *yachzir l'kasher*, return it to the *kasher* person and redo the *holachah*. The same underlying logic applies — the *pasul*'s carrying did not validate the *holachah*, but it also did not destroy anything. (This connects to the broader *Gemara*ic debate about *holachah shelo b'regel* and what constitutes valid transport, but the simple force of the *Mishnah* is clear: no harm was done, so redo it.) This section thus reinforces the general rule: a *pasul*'s involvement is merely the absence of a valid step, not the presence of an invalidating act.

### Biyemino Bikli Kodesh

The *Mishnah* continues through a series of similar cases, each governed by the same principle. If blood was received in the right hand and transferred to the left hand — an invalid mode of transport — it should be returned to the right hand; this did not invalidate. If blood was caught in a *kli kodesh*, a sanctified vessel, and then poured into an unsanctified vessel (*kli chol*), from which it cannot validly be transported, the solution is equally straightforward: return it to the *kli kodesh*. It was not invalidated. Similarly, if blood spilled from the vessel onto the floor after having been properly caught from the neck of the animal — the *kabbalah* itself was performed correctly — one may gather it back up and the blood remains *kasher*. In none of these cases did the deviation constitute an affirmative act of disqualification. They simply represent valid acts yet to be completed, and wherever a correction is possible, it should be made.

### Shelo Bimkomo Rashi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְתָנוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי הַכֶּבֶשׁ; שֶׁלֹּא כְּנֶגֶד הַיְסוֹד; נָתַן אֶת הַנִּיתָּנִין לְמַטָּה – לְמַעְלָן; וְאֶת הַנִּיתָּנִין לְמַעְלָן – לְמַטָּה; וְאֶת הַנִּיתָּנִין בִּפְנִים – בַּחוּץ; וְאֶת הַנִּיתָּנִין בַּחוּץ – בִּפְנִים; אִם יֵשׁ דַּם הַנֶּפֶשׁ – יַחְזוֹר הַכָּשֵׁר וִיקַבֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** If an unfit person **placed** the blood **upon the ramp** or on the wall of the altar **that** is **not opposite the base** of the altar, or if **he placed** the blood **that is** to be **placed below** the red line **above** the red line, **or** if he placed the blood **that is** to be **placed above** the red line **below** the red line, **or** if he placed the blood **that is** to be **placed inside** the Sanctuary **outside** the Sanctuary **or the** blood **that is** to be **placed outside** the Sanctuary **inside** the Sanctuary, then **if there is blood of the soul** that remains in the animal, **the** priest **fit** for Temple service **should again collect** the blood and sprinkle it on the altar.

The *Mishnah* then addresses what is perhaps the most significant set of cases: blood placed on the wrong location. The scenarios include: placing the blood on the *kevesh* (the ramp) rather than on the altar walls themselves; placing it at a corner of the altar where there is no projecting *yesod* (base) below, which is not a legitimate location; placing blood that was supposed to go below the *chut hasikra* (the dividing line on the altar) above it, or vice versa; and placing blood designated for the inner altar on the outer altar, or the reverse. In all these cases, if *dam hanefesh* remains, a *kasher* person should redo the *zerikah*.

This last ruling is the most striking *chiddush* of the *Mishnah*, because here we are dealing with the final act of the *avoda*. A separate Talmudic question — *zarika osa shirayim*, whether the act of *zerikah* itself renders any remaining blood as *shiyarei hadam* (remnant blood that may no longer be used for a redo) — bears directly on this case. The *Mishnah* nonetheless permits a redo, and Rashi presents two distinct interpretive pathways for understanding why.

### Rashi Derech Rishona

Rashi's first reading operates from the assumption that a *pasul* person who performs *zerikah* does *make* the blood into *shiyarim* — that is, even an invalid *Kohen*'s performance of the final act brings the *korban* to a point of no return if done in the right location. Given that premise, the fact that the *Mishnah* permits a redo must be teaching something else: *shelo bimkomo lav kimkomo dami* — placing the blood in the wrong location on the altar is not equivalent to placing it in the right location. Since it is as though nothing was done at all, the blood is not rendered into *shiyarim*, no terminal act has been completed, and therefore a *kasher* person may collect the remaining blood and redo the *zerikah* properly.

Under this reading, the distinction between a valid and invalid location on the altar is doing significant legal work. If a *pasul Kohen* threw the blood on the correct spot, that would end the matter — no further act is possible, and the *korban* is lost. But throw it on the wrong spot, even if you are a *pasul*, and it is as though no *zerikah* occurred at all; the process remains open.

### Rashi Derech Shniya

Rashi's second reading proceeds from the opposite premise: *shelo bimkomo kimkomo dami* — wrong-location *zerikah* is halachically equivalent to right-location *zerikah*. If that is true, then we cannot explain the *Mishnah*'s permission for a redo by virtue of the wrong location. Instead, the *Mishnah* must be teaching the converse: *ein pasul ose shirayim* — a *pasul* person's *zerikah*, even when performed in the correct location, does not render the blood into *shiyarim*. The *pasul*'s act accomplishes nothing, not even the terminal transformation of the remaining blood into remnant. Therefore, even after a *pasul* has thrown the blood — even onto the right place on the altar — if blood remains, a *kasher Kohen* may still perform a valid *zerikah*.

These two readings of the *Mishnah*'s end correspond to two distinct questions: whether location or personal status is the operative factor generating the permit for a redo. Under Rashi's first reading, location is the key; under the second, personal status is. And each reading implies a different *halacha* for the case not addressed by the *Mishnah* — either a *kasher Kohen* throwing blood in the wrong place, or a *pasul* throwing blood in the right place.

## Gemara: Non-Kohen

### Shachtu Bedieved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ ״שָׁחֲטוּ״ – דִּיעֲבַד אִין, לְכַתְּחִלָּה לָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara infers from the use of the past tense in the mishna: With regard to all those who are unfit for Temple service who **slaughtered,** that **after the fact, yes,** the slaughter is valid. But ***ab initio*, no,** those who are unfit may not slaughter an offering.

The *Gemara* now takes up the language of the *Mishnah* itself. The *Mishnah* says *shachtu* — they slaughtered — implying *b'diavad in, lechatchila lo*: one who is *pasul* may have slaughtered and the *shechita* stands, but such a person should not do so *lechatchila* in the first place. Yet this seemingly *b'diavad* framing sits in tension with other sources.

### Midrash on Shechita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: ״וְשָׁחַט״ – שְׁחִיטָה בְּזָר כְּשֵׁרָה, שֶׁהַשְּׁחִיטָה כְּשֵׁרָה בְּזָרִים וּבְנָשִׁים וּבַעֲבָדִים וּבִטְמֵאִים. וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים. אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בַּכֹּהֲנִים?
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from what was taught in the halakhic midrash *Torat Kohanim*: The verse states with regard to a burnt offering: **"And he shall slaughter** the bull before the Lord" (Leviticus 1:5). The fact that the verse does not stipulate that a priest must slaughter the offering teaches that **slaughter is valid** if performed **by a non-priest,** as stated in the mishna: **As the** sacrificial rite of **slaughter is valid** when performed **by non-priests, by women, by** Canaanite **slaves, and by ritually impure** individuals, **and even with** regard to **offerings of the most sacred order. Or** perhaps the term "and he shall slaughter" in the verse is referring **only** to slaughter **by priests?**

The *Gemara* raises a *midrash halacha* from a *braita* that reads the relevant verse closely. The *pasuk* says *veshachat et ben habakar* — and he shall slaughter the ox — without specifying *veShachat haKohen*. The *Kohanim* are only introduced in the very next phrase: *vehikrivu bnei Aharon haKohanim et hadam* — the sons of Aharon the *Kohanim* shall bring the blood close. The Torah is thus deliberately careful to associate the *Kohanim* with the blood and all subsequent acts, but not with the *shechita* itself. The *braita* spells this out: *mikabbalah va'eilach mitzvat kehunah* — from the receiving of the blood onward, that is the *mitzvat kehunah*. The *shechita* is thereby taught to be *kesherah b'chol adam* — valid performed by any person. One might have thought otherwise, since the *pasuk* elsewhere says *vetishmeru et kehunatchem lechol dvar hamizbeach* — you shall guard the *kehunah* for all matters of the altar — suggesting the *Kohen* must perform even *shechita*. But the precise juxtaposition of *veshachat* (without specifying *Kohen*) followed immediately by *vehikrivu bnei Aharon haKohanim* (specifying *Kohanim* only from the blood onward) forecloses that reading.

### Havu Lei Hu Hadin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתָּ: וְכִי מֵאַיִן בָּאתָה – מִכְּלָל שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַתָּה וּבָנֶיךָ אִתְּךָ תִּשְׁמְרוּ אֶת כְּהֻנַּתְכֶם לְכׇל דְּבַר הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״; יָכוֹל אַף בִּשְׁחִיטָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְשָׁחַט אֶת בֶּן הַבָּקָר לִפְנֵי ה׳ וְהִקְרִיבוּ וְגוֹ׳״ – מִקַּבָּלָה וְאֵילָךְ מִצְוַת כְּהוּנָּה; לִימֵּד עַל הַשְּׁחִיטָה שֶׁכְּשֵׁרָה בְּכׇל אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **You** can **say** in response: **From where did you come** to the idea that the verse is referring only to a priest? It is **from the fact that it is stated** to Aaron: **"And you and your sons with you shall keep your priesthood in everything that pertains to the altar"** (Numbers 18:7). One **might** have thought that this is the *halakha* **even with** regard to **slaughter,** that only priests are fit to slaughter an offering. Therefore, **the verse states: "And he shall slaughter the bull before the Lord, and** Aaron's sons, the priests, **shall present [*vehikrivu*]** the blood" (Leviticus 1:5), from which it is inferred that specifically **from** the **collection** of the blood **and onward** it is **a mitzva** exclusively **of the priesthood.** This **teaches about the** rite of **slaughter that it is valid** *ab initio* if performed **by any person.** This contradicts the inference from the mishna that slaughter of an offering by a non-priest is valid only after the fact.

The language of this *braita* — *shechita b'zarim uvnashim* — makes no qualification at all; these persons may perform *shechita lechatchila* with no hesitation. Why then did the *Mishnah* adopt the *b'diavad* formulation? The *Gemara* answers: *hu hadin d'afilu lechatchila nami* — the same *din* applies even *lechatchila*. The *b'diavad* formulation in the *Mishnah* is not a restriction but a stylistic artifact.

### Tamei Gezeira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא הַדִּין דַּאֲפִילּוּ לְכַתְּחִילָּה נָמֵי; וּמִשּׁוּם דְּקָא בָּעֵי לְמִיתְנָא טְמֵאִים – דִּלְכַתְּחִלָּה לָא, גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יִגְּעוּ בַּבָּשָׂר; תְּנָא: ״שֶׁשָּׁחֲטוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The same is true that even *ab initio* as well** it is permitted for those unfit for Temple service to slaughter an offering, **but because** the mishna **wants to teach** with regard to the **ritually impure that** they may **not** slaughter an offering ***ab initio*** due to a rabbinic **decree lest they touch the flesh** and render it ritually impure, therefore the mishna **taught** this *halakha* with the expression in the past tense: **Who slaughtered,** i.e., after the fact.

The reason for the *b'diavad* framing is practical: the *Mishnah* needed to include the case of a *tamei* person, and for a *tamei shochet*, *lechatchila* is indeed inadvisable — not because the *shechita* itself is deficient, but because of a Rabbinic concern. The restriction is a *gezeira shema yigu b'vasar*: there is concern the *tamei* person may come to inadvertently touch the meat and transmit *tumah* to it. As a precautionary measure, we say a *tamei* person should not perform *shechita lechatchila*. The *shechita* itself remains fully and unconditionally *kasher*.

### Tosafot Tamei Concern

*Tosafot* offers a noteworthy observation: one might have expected the concern about a *tamei shochet* to be the possibility of inadvertently stepping over the boundary line and entering the *Azara*. *Tosafot* dismisses this. A person who knows they are *tamei* is, precisely because of that awareness, on heightened guard about the boundary. The danger of crossing a line is actually greater for someone who does not perceive themselves as restricted. The analogy is apt: the risk of confusion between *kasher* and non-*kasher* food is greater when everything at the table appears permissible than when one item is clearly forbidden. Awareness of *tumah* status prevents inadvertent boundary crossing. The only genuine concern, therefore, is the possibility of physical contact with the meat.

## Shochet Location

### Utamei Dieved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְטָמֵא דִּיעֲבַד שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי?! וּרְמִינְהוּ: ״וְסָמַךְ... וְשָׁחַט״ – מָה סְמִיכָה בִּטְהוֹרִים, אַף שְׁחִיטָה בִּטְהוֹרִים! מִדְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** in the case of **a ritually impure** individual who performed the slaughter, is it **permitted** even **after the fact? And** one can **raise a contradiction** from what is taught in the halakhic midrash: The Torah states two consecutive directives: **"And he shall place** his hand upon the head of the burnt offering…**And he shall slaughter** the bull before the Lord" (Leviticus 1:4–5). This juxtaposition indicates that both directives are referring to the same individual, which teaches that **just as placing hands** on the head of an offering may be performed only **by ritually pure** individuals, **so too,** the **slaughter** may be performed only **by ritually pure** individuals. The Gemara answers: **By rabbinic law,** an impure person is prohibited from slaughtering an offering lest he touch the flesh of the offering, but by Torah law the slaughter is valid.

Having established that even a *tamei* person's *shechita* is unimpeachable *b'diavad*, the *Gemara* now raises a sharper challenge. The prior discussion assumed that the *shochet* need not be physically situated within the *Azara* — only the animal must be there. But if a *shochet* must in fact stand in the *Azara*, then a *tamei* person could not perform *shechita* even *b'diavad*, since entry into the *Azara* in a state of *tumah* is a categorical prohibition.

### Samach V'Shachat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא סְמִיכָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״לִפְנֵי ה׳״; שְׁחִיטָה נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב: ״לִפְנֵי ה׳״! אֶפְשָׁר דְּעָבֵיד סַכִּין אֲרוּכָּה וְשָׁחֵיט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **placing hands** that it can be performed only by those who are ritually pure even by Torah law? It is **that it is written: "Before the Lord,"** which is referring to the Temple courtyard, and an impure person may not enter the Temple courtyard. With regard to **slaughter as well, isn't it written: "Before the Lord"** (Leviticus 1:5)? How can a ritually impure person enter the Temple courtyard to slaughter an offering? The Gemara answers: It is **possible,** in a case **where he fashions a long knife,** stands outside the Temple courtyard, **and slaughters** the offering that is inside the courtyard.

The challenge is mounted from a *braita* that juxtaposes *smicha* and *shechita*: *vSamach* — he shall lean [his hand on the animal] — and *vShachat* — and he shall slaughter. One *Tanna* reads this juxtaposition as a *hekesh*: just as *smicha* requires the person to be *tahor*, so does *shechita* require *tahor* status. But why would we ever have thought *smicha* requires *tahor* status in the first place? The *braita* points to the *pasuk*'s use of the phrase *lifnei Hashem* — the *someich* must perform the *smicha* in the presence of God, meaning within the *Azara*. And a *tamei* person cannot enter the *Azara*. But the *pasuk* equally places the word *lifnei Hashem* in immediate proximity to *vShachat* — and the *Gemara* observes that *lifnei Hashem* is actually more closely juxtaposed to the act of *shechita* than to *smicha*.

### Efshar Sakina Aruka

The *Gemara* resolves the difficulty by arguing that *lifnei Hashem* refers not to the person performing the act but to the animal being slaughtered. The animal must be *lifnei Hashem*, within the *Azara*; the *shochet* may stand outside. This is practically realizable: *efshar d'avid sakina arucha v'shachit* — one can simply use a long knife and slaughter from beyond the boundary. Similarly, for *smicha*, if *lifnei Hashem* refers only to the animal, the *someich* could extend his arms across the line and press his hands upon the animal's head. The *Gemara* considers this possibility but notes that it depends on the question of *bia b'miktzat* — whether extending part of one's body across the threshold constitutes a prohibited entry for a *tamei* person.

### Bia Bimiktzat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְמִיכָה נָמֵי – אֶפְשָׁר דִּמְעַיֵּיל יְדֵיהּ וְסָמֵיךְ. קָסָבַר בִּיאָה בְּמִקְצָת שְׁמָהּ בִּיאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to **placing hands also, it is possible,** in a case **where he inserts his hands** into the Temple courtyard **and places** them on the head of the offering. The Gemara answers: The *tanna* **holds** that **a partial entry,** in which only a part of the body enters the Temple courtyard, **is called entry.** Therefore, an impure individual may not insert even part of his body, such as his hands, into the Temple courtyard.

This is the crux of a major debate. The question of *bia b'miktzat shma bia* — whether partial-body entry into a sacred precinct constitutes halachically cognizable entry — is not merely a technicality. If *bia b'miktzat shma bia*, then a *tamei* person who extends his hands across the line into the *Azara* has transgressed as fully as if he had walked in entirely. Under that view, *smicha* would be impossible for a *tamei* person, since pressing one's hands onto an animal standing within the *Azara* necessarily requires that the hands cross the boundary. *Shechita*, by contrast, requires no entry at all — the long knife crosses, but no part of the person's body does.

### Shimon HaTeimani

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מַנִּי – שִׁמְעוֹן הַתִּימְנִי הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְשָׁחַט אֶת בֶּן הַבָּקָר לִפְנֵי ה׳״ – וְלֹא הַשּׁוֹחֵט לִפְנֵי ה׳. שִׁמְעוֹן הַתִּימְנִי אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן שֶׁיִּהְיוּ יָדָיו שֶׁל שׁוֹחֵט לִפְנִים מִן הַנִּשְׁחָט? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְשָׁחַט אֶת בֶּן הַבָּקָר לִפְנֵי ה׳״ – שׁוֹחֵט אֶת בֶּן הַבָּקָר יְהֵא לִפְנֵי ה׳.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this? It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Shimon HaTimni,** who holds that the one who slaughters an offering must stand inside the Temple courtyard. This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*, that when the verse states: **"And he shall slaughter the bull before the Lord"** (Leviticus 1:5), it teaches that the bull must be inside the Temple courtyard, **but the slaughterer** need **not** be **"before the Lord."** Therefore, he may stand outside the Temple courtyard and slaughter the offering that is inside. **Shimon HaTimni says: From where** is it derived **that the hands of** the **slaughterer must be** farther **inside** the Temple courtyard **than the** animal that is **slaughtered? The verse states: "And he shall slaughter the bull before the Lord,"** which teaches that the **slaughterer of the bull must be before the Lord.**

Against the dominant assumption that only the animal need be within the *Azara* for *shechita*, the *Gemara* presents the opinion of *Shimon HaTeimani*, who reads the *pasuk* differently. The verse *vShachat et ben habakar lifnei Hashem* — he shall slaughter the ox before God — is read by the majority as meaning the *nishchat*, the animal being slaughtered, must be *lifnei Hashem*. But *Shimon HaTeimani* holds that *vShachat* — the *shochet* — must be *lifnei Hashem*. The *shochet* himself must stand in the *Azara*. This reading is intuitive on the face of it: *shechita* is an act of *avoda*, and one naturally expects the performer of an *avoda* to be standing within the sacred precinct. The difficulty, of course, is that *shechita* does not require a *Kohen* at all — and once the personal status requirement is lifted, it becomes less obvious why a locational requirement should persist. *Shimon HaTeimani* nonetheless maintains it. Under his view, a *tamei* person cannot perform *shechita* even *b'diavad* because a *tamei* person cannot be present in the *Azara*.

## Bia Bimiktzat Ulah

### Tamei Yad Lokei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: טָמֵא שֶׁהִכְנִיס יָדוֹ לִפְנִים – לוֹקֶה. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּכׇל קֹדֶשׁ לֹא תִגָּע וְגוֹ׳״ – מַקִּישׁ בִּיאָה לִנְגִיעָה; מָה נְגִיעָה בְּמִקְצָת שְׁמָהּ נְגִיעָה, אַף בִּיאָה בְּמִקְצָת שְׁמָהּ בִּיאָה.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the question of whether partial entry into the Temple courtyard is considered entry, **Ulla says** that **Reish Lakish says: A ritually impure** individual **who inserted his hand inside** the Temple courtyard **is flogged** for transgressing the prohibition of entering the Temple while impure, **as it is stated** with regard to the impurity of a woman who gave birth: **"Every consecrated item she shall not touch,** and to the Temple she shall not come, until the days of her purification are complete" (Leviticus 12:4). The verse **juxtaposes entry** into the courtyard **to touching** a sacred item. **Just as touching with** even **a part** of the body **is considered touching, so too, entry with part** of the body **is considered entry.**

*Ulla* transmits in the name of *Reish Lakish* a striking ruling: *tamei shehichnis yado lifnim lokei* — a *tamei* person who inserts his hand into the *Azara* receives *malkot* (lashes). This is a full-fledged violation, not a mere rabbinic concern.

### Mikyash Bia Negiah

The source is a *hekesh* between *negi'ah* (touching) and *bia* (entry). The verse, in the context of a woman who has given birth, states *bechol kodesh lo tiga ve'el hamikdash lo tavo* — she shall not touch any holy thing and shall not enter the *Mikdash* until her days of purification are complete. The *Gemara* derives: just as *negi'ah* with a mere part of one's body — a finger, a hand — constitutes full halachic touching, so too *bia b'miktzat shma bia* — entry with a part of the body constitutes entry. Extending a hand across the boundary of the *Azara* is entry. Hence, lashes.

### Metzora Chamishi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא לְעוּלָּא: מְצוֹרָע שֶׁחָל שְׁמִינִי שֶׁלּוֹ לִהְיוֹת בְּעֶרֶב הַפֶּסַח, וְרָאָה קֶרִי בּוֹ בַּיּוֹם, וְטָבַל –
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hoshaya raised an objection to Ulla** from that which was taught in a *baraita*: There is the case of **a leper whose eighth** day, on which he becomes ritually pure from his leprosy and performs the final stages of his purification process, **occurs on Passover eve,** such that it would be possible to bring his offerings and be fit to partake of the Paschal offering that evening. **And he experienced a seminal emission on that day** before he performed the final stages of his purification process, rendering him ritually impure and prohibited from entering the Temple. **And he** then **immersed** himself in a ritual bath. The question is whether he may come to the gate of the Temple courtyard, which is necessary to complete his purification process and terminate his status as a leper.

*Rav Oshaya* challenges this ruling from the case of a *metzora* whose eighth day falls on *erev Pesach*.

## Metzora Baal Keri

### Yom Shmini Metzora

The case is as follows. A *metzora* reaches his eighth day of purification — the day on which he must appear at the *Beit Hamikdash* to bring his *korban* and complete his *taharah* process — and this day falls on *erev Pesach*. Under ordinary circumstances this presents no difficulty: he comes to the *Mikdash*, completes the eighth-day procedure, and is fully *tahor* in time to eat the *korban Pesach* that night. But this particular individual is also a *baal keri* — he has had a seminal emission. Now his situation is considerably more complicated.

### Machane Shechina Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין טְבוּל יוֹם אַחֵר נִכְנָס, זֶה נִכְנָס; מוּטָב יָבוֹא עֲשֵׂה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ כָּרֵת, וְיִדְחֶה עֲשֵׂה שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages said: Although** any **other** individual who was ritually impure **who immersed that day** and is waiting for nightfall for the purification process to be completed **may not enter** the Temple mount until nightfall, **this** one **may enter.** The reason is that **it is better** for **a positive mitzva that has** a punishment of ***karet*,** i.e., the bringing of the Paschal offering, to **come and override a positive mitzva for which there is no** liability to be punished with ***karet*,** i.e., the mitzva to remove individuals who are ritually impure from the Temple courtyard, than have the positive mitzva that has a punishment of *karet* not be performed.

The *Gemara* recalls the *pasuk*: *vayishalchu min hamachane kol tzaruah vechol zav vechol tamei lanefesh* — three categories of *tumah*, three separate zones of exclusion. *Tzaruah* is excluded from all three *machanot* — he must dwell entirely alone outside the camp. *Zav* — and this category includes *zavah*, *niddah*, and *baal keri*, all those who have *tumah yotzei migufo* — is excluded from *machaneh Leviyah* (the *Har HaBayit*) and *machaneh Shechina* (the *Azara* and *Ezrat Nashim*) but may be present in *machaneh Yisrael* (the rest of *Yerushalayim*). *Tamei lanefesh*, meaning *tamei meis* — along with those *tamei* from external sources such as *sheretz* or *neveilah* — is excluded only from *machaneh Shechina* itself.

Our *metzora* on his eighth day has completed the seven-day purification and may now enter as far as the *Har HaBayit*. What bars him from the *Azara* is not his *tzaraat* status — that he has effectively worked through — but only the fact that he has not yet completed the eighth-day procedure itself. He is a *mechusar kipurim*, lacking only the final *korban*. He stands at the *Nicanor Gate*, on the boundary between *Ezrat Nashim* and the *Azara*, and the *Kohen* applies the blood from the *korban metzora* to his thumbs. This physical procedure cannot be delegated; the *metzora* must be personally present.

His problem is that he is also a *baal keri*. As a *baal keri*, even if he immerses in a *mikvah*, he is a *tvul yom* — one who has immersed but whose *taharah* is not complete until nightfall. The question is whether a *tvul yom* of a *baal keri* retains a prohibition against entering *Har HaBayit* and *Ezrat Nashim* until nightfall.

### Tvul Yom Pesach

His problem is that he is also a *baal keri*. As a *baal keri*, even if he immerses in a *mikvah*, he is a *tvul yom* — one who has immersed but whose *taharah* is not complete until nightfall. The question is whether a *tvul yom* of a *baal keri* retains a prohibition against entering *Har HaBayit* and *Ezrat Nashim* until nightfall.

### Rabbi Yochanan Tvul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה – אֲפִילּוּ עֲשֵׂה אֵין בּוֹ. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּעֲמֹד יְהוֹשָׁפָט בִּקְהַל יְהוּדָה וִירוּשָׁלִַם בְּבֵית ה׳ לִפְנֵי הֶחָצֵר הַחֲדָשָׁה״ – מַאי ״חָצֵר הַחֲדָשָׁה״? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֶׁחִידְּשׁוּ בָּהּ דְּבָרִים (הַרְבֵּה), וְאָמְרוּ: טְבוּל יוֹם אַל יִכָּנֵס לְמַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: By Torah law, there is not even a positive mitzva** that restricts from entering the Temple courtyard one who has immersed that day and will become pure only upon nightfall, **as it is stated: "And Jehoshaphat stood in the congregation of Judea and Jerusalem, in the House of the Lord, before the new courtyard"** (II Chronicles 20:5). **What** is the meaning of **"the new courtyard"? Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** This is referring to the place **in which they innovated many matters and said** that **one who immersed that day may not enter the camp of the Levites,** which in Jerusalem includes the entire Temple Mount. This indicates that the prohibition is of rabbinic origin, and by Torah law it is permitted for him to enter.

*Rabbi Yochanan* goes further. He holds that *d'var Torah afilu asei ein bah* — even as a matter of Torah law, there is no positive *mitzvah* preventing a *tvul yom* from being in *machaneh Leviyah*. A *tvul yom* is, from the Torah's perspective, effectively *tahor* for purposes of *Har HaBayit* and *Ezrat Nashim*. His proof: the verse *vayaamod Yehoshaphat bikhal Yehudah viYerushalayim b'veis Hashem lifnei hahatzer hahadashah* — he stood in the House of God before the new courtyard. *Rabbi Yochanan* understands *hatzer hadashah* as *Ezrat Nashim*, and the "newness" refers to a rabbinic innovation: *sh'hiddeshu bah devarim*, they newly decreed that a *tvul yom* should not enter *machaneh Leviyah*. This shows that prior to the rabbinic decree, a *tvul yom* was permitted in this zone. The biblical prohibitions — both the positive *asei* and the negative *lo ta'asei* — apply only to a *tamei* person who has not yet immersed. Once immersion occurs, the Torah considers the person effectively *tahor* for these zones, and any remaining restriction is purely rabbinic. For *korban Pesach*, that rabbinic restriction is set aside.

### Amar Eli Bia Bonanot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּיאָה בְּמִקְצָת שְׁמָהּ בִּיאָה, הֵיכִי מְעַיֵּיל יְדֵיהּ בִּבְהוֹנוֹת? אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי עֲשֵׂה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ כָּרֵת הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Hoshaya concludes his objection: **And if you say** that **partial entry** into the courtyard **is considered entry, how** does the leper who immersed himself that day **insert his hands** into the courtyard **so that** his right **thumb and big toe** will become purified? In this situation, both **this** mitzva to eat of the Paschal offering **and that** mitzva for an impure person to not enter the courtyard **are** each **a positive mitzva for which there is** liability to be punished with ***karet*,** as someone who immersed himself that day who enters the Temple courtyard is liable to be punished with *karet*.

But now the *Gemara* resurfaces the problem that *Rav Oshaya* was building toward. Even if we accept that this *metzora-tvul yom* is permitted in *Ezrat Nashim* — either because the *asei* is overridden or because there is no *asei* at all — a separate and serious difficulty remains. The *metzora* must have the blood from his *korban* applied to his thumbs. He stands on the *Ezrat Nashim* side of the *Nicanor Gate*. His thumbs must cross into the *Azara* proper. Everyone agrees that until his eighth-day procedure is complete, a *metzora* may not enter the *Azara* proper. So the only way his thumbs can be in the *Azara* is if he reaches them across the boundary line. That is precisely *bia b'miktzat*. If *bia b'miktzat shma bia*, how is this permitted? And all the more so for someone who is also a *baal keri*: even after immersing, he is not permitted in the *Azara* itself, and sticking his thumbs into the *Azara* would constitute *bia b'miktzat* — which on *Ulla* and *Reish Lakish*'s account would incur *malkot*.

## Metzora Hutrah

### Houil Vetuter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִטּוּנָךְ – שָׁאנֵי מְצוֹרָע, הוֹאִיל וְהוּתַּר לְצָרַעְתּוֹ הוּתַּר לְקִירְיוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Ulla **said to** Rabbi Hoshaya: A proof to my opinion can be brought **from your** own **burden,** i.e., from the question you asked. **A leper is different,** as the Torah permitted him to insert his right thumb and big toe into the courtyard in order to become purified despite the fact that he has not yet brought an atonement offering to complete the purification process, and **since he was permitted** to partially enter the courtyard **for** purification from **his leprosy, he was** also **permitted** with regard to his impurity due **to his seminal emission.** Nevertheless, a partial entry into the courtyard is in fact considered an entry.

*Reish Lakish* responds: *matuncha* — the very challenge you pose is my answer. *Shani metzora, ho'il v'hutar l'tzara'ato hutar l'keryo* — the *metzora* case is unique, because the Torah itself, by prescribing the thumb-application as the mechanism of the *metzora*'s *taharah*, implicitly permits the *metzora* to extend his thumbs. There is an inherent *heter* embedded in the *taharah* procedure for *metzora*. Once that *heter* exists for the *metzora*'s status, it extends as well to the lighter *tumah* status of *baal keri* that the same individual carries. Since the dispensation already applies, and the *baal keri* status is the lesser *tumah*, the dispensation encompasses that as well.

### Rav Yosef Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, קָסָבַר עוּלָּא: רוּבָּן זָבִים וְנַעֲשׂוּ טְמֵאֵי מֵתִים; הוֹאִיל וְהוּתְּרוּ לְטוּמְאָתָן – הוּתְּרוּ לְזִיבָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** Based on Ulla's previous comment, **Rav Yosef said: Ulla holds** that if **most of** the Jewish people were ***zavim*** on Passover eve, and were therefore prohibited from sacrificing the Paschal offering while impure, **and** then **they became ritually impure due to a corpse,** which allows them to sacrifice the Paschal offering despite their impurity as they are a majority of the Jewish people, **since they are permitted** to sacrifice the offering **with regard to their impurity** due to a corpse, **they are** also **permitted with regard to their *ziva*.**

*Rav Yosef* seizes on the logic of *Ulla*'s position to draw a broader inference. Since *Ulla* holds that a dispensation granted for one *tumah* extends to a lesser *tumah* in the same individual, *Ulla* must hold the following: if the majority of the *tzibur* consists of *zavim*, and those same people become *temei meitim*, then since they would receive a dispensation to bring the *korban Pesach* as *tamei meis* — under the principle of *tumah hutrah b'tzibur* — that dispensation would extend to cover their *zivah* status as well.

### Abaye Hazirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִי דָּמֵי?! טוּמְאָה אִישְׁתְּרַאי, זִיבָה לָא אִישְׁתְּרַאי!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: Are** the cases **comparable?** With regard to a leper who experienced a seminal emission, he was already permitted to partially enter the courtyard despite his initial impurity. Therefore, the subsequent impurity does not prohibit him from entering. By contrast, the *zavim* were originally prevented from sacrificing the Paschal offering. Therefore, even if he would be **permitted** to enter the courtyard despite becoming **impure** due to a corpse, he would **not** be **permitted** to enter due to the ***ziva*** that was previously present.

*Abaye* objects: *mi dami?* The comparison does not hold. By the *metzora*, the dispensation came first — the *metzora* had the *heter* for his thumbs before he acquired the *baal keri* status. Here you have the sequence reversed: they were *zavim* first, which status is not covered by *tumah hutrah b'tzibur*, and only then did they become *temei meitim*. You cannot say that an override granted for a subsequent *tumah* reaches back to cover a pre-existing and heavier *tumah* that was never individually subject to an override.

### Dilma Hachi Kamart

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, דִּלְמָא הָכִי קָאָמַר מָר: רוּבָּן טְמֵאֵי מֵתִים וְנַעֲשׂוּ זָבִים; הוֹאִיל וְהוּתְּרוּ לְטוּמְאָתָן – הוּתְּרוּ לְזִיבָתָן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, Abaye **said to him: Perhaps this** is what **the Master is saying:** If **most of** the Jewish people were initially **ritually impure due to a corpse, and they** subsequently **became *zavim*, since they are permitted with regard to their impurity** due to a corpse, **they are permitted with regard to their *ziva*** as well. Rav Yosef **said to him: Yes,** that was what I meant.

*Abaye* then reconsiders and offers a corrected version of *Rav Yosef*'s inference: perhaps the *chiddush* works with the reverse order. The majority of the *tzibur* is *temei meitim* — already subject to *tumah hutrah b'tzibur*, the dispensation applies. Then they become *zavim*. Now the question is whether the already-active dispensation continues to cover them even in light of the new *zivah* status. This mirrors the *metzora* case: dispensation first, additional *tumah* second. *Rav Yosef* accepts the correction.

### Lo Dami Hutrah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי לָא דָּמֵי; מְצוֹרָע – הֶיתֵּירָא הוּא, דְּחוּיָה הִיא, לְהָא אִידְּחַאי לְהָא לָא אִידְּחַאי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But still,** this is **not comparable** to the case of Ulla, since with regard to **a leper, it is** with total **permission** that he is allowed partial entry into the courtyard. And **since it was permitted** with regard to one impurity, **it was permitted** with regard to a second impurity. But the **ritual impurity** with regard to the sacrificing of the Paschal offering **is** only **overridden,** and there is not a total permission. Perhaps **with regard to this,** i.e., ritual impurity due to a corpse, **it is overridden,** but **with regard to that,** i.e., the impurity of *ziva*, **it is not overridden.**

Even so, the *Gemara* challenges the analogy. There is a fundamental difference between *metzora* and *tumah b'tzibur*. By *metzora*, the Torah prescribed the thumb-application as part of the *taharah* procedure itself. The implication is that for a *metzora*, the *bia b'miktzat* is not merely tolerated or overridden — it is simply not a problem at all. This is the distinction between *hutrah* and *dchuya*. *Hutrah* — the Torah's complete dispensation — means that no prohibition was ever in force here to begin with: sticking the thumbs in is simply part of what the *metzora* does, and the Torah never considered it a violation. *Dchuya* — the "pushing aside" model — means that there is a genuine prohibition, but it is overridden by a countervailing value. *Tumah b'tzibur* operates in the *dchuya* framework: the *tumah* is a real problem that is being overridden by the communal necessity of the *korban Pesach*. Under *dchuya*, since we are acknowledging a real problem and consciously overriding it, we are limited in how far that override reaches. It can cover *tamei meis*, which is the specific *tumah* the dispensation was articulated for. It cannot automatically sweep in *zivah*, which was never individually covered.

### Rava Adderaba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אַדְּרַבָּה, אִיפְּכָא מִסְתַּבְּרָא! מְצוֹרָע – הֶיתֵּירָא הוּא, לְהָא אִישְׁתְּרַאי וּלְהָא לָא אִישְׁתְּרַאי; טוּמְאָה – דְּחוּיָה הוּא, מָה לִי חַד דִּחוּיָא מָה לִי שְׁתֵּי דְּחִיּוֹת?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: On the contrary, the opposite is** more **reasonable.** With regard to **a leper, it is** with specific **permission** that he was allowed by the Torah to insert his right thumb and big toe into the courtyard in order to become purified despite his ritual impurity, which was not granted for other impurities. Therefore, perhaps only **with regard to this** impurity **it is permitted, but with regard to that** impurity, i.e., due to a seminal emission, **it is not permitted.** But with regard to **ritual impurity,** since **it is overridden** in the case of ritual impurity due to a corpse, **what** difference does it make **to me** if there is **one** impurity that is **overridden,** and **what** difference does it make **to me** if there are **two** impurities that are **overridden?**

*Rava* disputes this reasoning directly. *Adderaba ipcha mistabra* — the reverse logic is more compelling. By *metzora*, if the correct understanding is *hutrah* — that the Torah simply never considered the thumb-extension to be any kind of violation — then what we have is a narrow, specific, self-contained dispensation. The Torah said: for a *metzora*, this is fine. That says nothing about any other situation. Apply it to *baal keri*? We never heard that. There is no principle of override in force that could logically extend. *Lehah ishtarei, lehah lo ishtarei* — to this it applies, to that it does not.

By contrast, under *dchuya* — the *tumah b'tzibur* model — we have articulated an affirmative principle: the communal obligation of *korban Pesach* is weighty enough to override *tumah*. That is a principle, not a narrow exception. And once you have established that such overrides are possible, *ma li chada dchuya ma li shtei dchuyos* — what difference does it make whether we are overriding one type of *tumah* or two? The power of override, once invoked, can reach further. *Hutrah* creates a specific carve-out. *Dchuya* creates a principle that can be extended. *Rava*'s logic is that the *dchuya* framework is therefore actually broader in its potential application than the *hutrah* framework — a counterintuitive but compelling argument.

### Metzora vs. Baal Keri

The conclusion that emerges from this discussion is that the question of how the *metzora* who is also a *baal keri* can extend his thumbs into the *Azara* admits of two resolutions. Under one view, *bia b'miktzat* is not a violation to begin with — *bia b'miktzat lo shma bia* — and there is therefore no problem to solve. Under the other view, *bia b'miktzat shma bia* is indeed a real violation, and the special *hutrah* that the Torah grants the *metzora* for his thumb-application extends to cover even the *baal keri* status that the same person carries simultaneously. What cannot work, according to *Rava*, is an explanation based on *dchuya* — for a *dchuya*-based dispensation, being inherently broader in principle, would only reinforce the thumb-extension, not limit it. It is therefore *hutrah* or nothing.

## Mikhlal Shecheihem

### Common Ground

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דְּתַרְוַיְיהוּ סְבִירָא לְהוּ: טוּמְאָה דְּחוּיָה הִיא בְּצִבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: It can be derived **by inference that both** Abaye and Rava **hold** that **impurity is overridden in** cases involving the **public** and is not entirely permitted.

Despite their disagreement over which framework is more logically expansive, both *Reish Lakish* and *Rava* share two foundational commitments. First, both agree that the *metzora*'s thumb-application is a case of *hutrah* — the Torah's complete dispensation, not a situation of overriding a prohibition. Second, both agree that *tumah b'tzibur* operates under the framework of *dchuya* — the communal *tumah* is a real problem that is being overridden, not a situation that the Torah simply defined as non-problematic. These two points of agreement — *metzora hutrah, tumah dchuya* — are *michlal*, established by inference from their very debate, and they set the stage for the next discussion.
