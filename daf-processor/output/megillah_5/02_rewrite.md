# Megillah 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Reading Dates & Tannaic…

### Month vs. Day-of-Week Or…

The Gemara on this daf opens by picking up in the middle of 4b, roughly ten lines down from the top, with the doubled word *keitzad keitzad* — "how so, how so?" — as the Mishnah begins working through the variations in reading dates. The Mishnah establishes that when *Purim* falls on a Monday, both the *kfarim* (villages) and the *ayarot gedolot* (large cities) simply read on that day. There is no complexity when the fourteenth of Adar falls on a Monday or Thursday, since those are already the designated market days on which the villages come in to town. The scheduling problem only arises when the fourteenth does not fall on one of those days.

The Gemara then raises a subtle structural question. The beginning of the Mishnah organized its discussion by the order of the days of the month — the 12th, 13th, and 14th — yet when the Mishnah proceeds to give its specific examples, it shifts to the day of the week: does *Purim* fall on a Monday? On a Tuesday? On a Wednesday? Why abandon the day-of-month ordering? The Gemara answers: *aidi demitpachei lei nakat sidura deyoma* — because if you were to organize the examples by day of the month, you would be forced to work backwards through the week, producing a counterintuitive and non-logical sequence. It is far cleaner to name the day of the week on which the fourteenth falls and let the reader do the arithmetic forward, rather than presenting the arithmetic in reverse.

### Friday Scenario Dispute

The Gemara focuses on the Friday scenario: *chal lihyot b'erev Shabbat* — when the fourteenth falls on a Friday. In this case the Mishnah states that the *ayarot gedolot* and the *mukafot choma* (walled cities) both read on Friday — the 14th — and the villages read on Thursday, since the walled cities cannot read on *Shabbat* and the villages move to the market day. The Gemara then asks: *matnitin mani* — whose position is reflected in this Mishnah? It answers: *ei Rabbi ei Rabbi Yosi* — it could be either Rabbi (Judah ha-Nasi) or Rabbi Yosi, since both of their positions turn out to be consistent with the Mishnah's ruling, but a *braita* reveals that the matter is in fact disputed among *Tannaim*.

The *braita* presents the position of the *Tanna Kamma*: when the fourteenth falls on a Friday, both the *kfarim* and the *ayarot gedolot* advance their reading to Thursday, and the *mukafot choma* read on Friday itself. The underlying logic is that the Torah states *achen haprazim b'arei haprazot*, contrasting open cities with walled cities — the two categories must always read on different days, their times must remain distinct. Since the walled cities cannot read on *Shabbat*, they are pushed to Friday, and once Friday is occupied by the walled cities, the large cities can no longer remain on Friday and must retreat to Thursday.

### Three Positions

Rabbi then disagrees: *lo yidachu ha-ayarot mimkomam* — the large cities do not get pushed from their fixed place. His view is that the walled and unwalled cities may both read on Friday, the 14th. That is indeed the position of our Mishnah.

Rabbi Yosi introduces a third possibility. In his *braita*, when the fourteenth falls on a Friday, it is the *mukafin* and the *kfarim* together who advance to Thursday — the walled cities and the villages both go back — while the *ayarot gedolot* read on Friday. His reasoning is that while he agrees the walled cities and unwalled cities must remain distinct, the walled cities cannot precede the unwalled ones. To have the walled cities read on the 14th before the unwalled read on the 15th would be perverse, since the entire logic of the walled cities celebrating later is that their miracle continued an extra day. To place them earlier would undermine the very content the calendar is meant to commemorate.

The Gemara then carefully articulates the competing principles at stake for each position.

### Competing Principles

For the *Tanna Kamma*, the Gemara cites the verse *b'chol shana v'shana* — "every year" — and draws the inference that just as in every normal year the unwalled cities precede the walled cities, so too here the unwalled cities must read before the walled. If the walled are pushed to Friday, the unwalled must go to Thursday. The Gemara raises the counterargument: perhaps *b'chol shana v'shana* means instead that just as in every normal year the unwalled cities are not moved from their fixed date, so here too we should not move the 14th? The Gemara responds: *shani hacha d'lo efshar* — this case is different because it is not possible to maintain all principles simultaneously; the priority of keeping the 14th and 15th distinct overrides the preference for keeping the 14th in place.

For Rabbi, the Gemara likewise cites *b'chol shana v'shana* to mean: just as normally we do not displace the unwalled cities from their date, here too — *lo yidachu mimkoman* — they remain on the 14th. And if that results in the walled and the unwalled reading on the same day, let it be. The Gemara raises the competing inference — perhaps we should insist they read on different days — and again answers *shani hacha d'lo efshar*: one cannot satisfy every principle, and Rabbi's priority is the integrity of the 14th.

The Gemara then works through Rabbi Yosi's position in parallel: his *b'chol shana v'shana* principle insists that the walled cities never precede the unwalled, even if they wind up on the same day, so the walled cities go all the way back to Thursday. The Gemara again probes whether perhaps we should insist on distinct days, and again receives the same answer — *shani hacha d'lo efshar* — something must give.

The Gemara then establishes, parenthetically, that Rabbi himself is not categorically opposed to the villagers reading on a Thursday in the *Shabbat* scenario. A *braita* records that when the fourteenth falls on *Shabbat*, Rabbi says: *ho'il v'nidchu ayaros mimkoman, yidchu leyom ha-kenisah* — since the unwalled cities are already being displaced from *Shabbat*, push them all the way back to Thursday so that everyone among the unwalled reads on the same day. The Gemara thus clarifies that Rabbi's reluctance to push the large cities back to Thursday in the Friday case was precisely because there the 14th is a perfectly valid reading day and he would not move them unnecessarily. But when the 14th itself falls on *Shabbat* and movement is unavoidable, Rabbi is willing and even prefers consolidating the displaced groups onto Thursday. The Gemara notes that a teaching of Rav Huna — that when *Purim* falls on *Shabbat*, *hakol nidchin leyom ha-kenisah*, everyone who is being displaced is displaced to Thursday — goes according to Rabbi, since it is consistent with his principle of consolidation once displacement becomes unavoidable.

### Multiple Purim Days

One of the most conceptually striking features of this Masechet is how *Chazal* have institutionalized the idea of different groups celebrating *Purim* on different days. The Megilla itself only records in passing that *Shushan* had an additional day and designates the 14th for the *perazim*; it never explicitly mandates separate days for the *mukafim*. Yet the Mishnah and these *braitot* not only preserve the distinction between 14th and 15th but expand it, creating a range of possible reading dates spanning the 11th through the 15th.

This raises a genuine question: is the fragmentation of the communal celebration not troubling? The unity of a shared liturgical calendar is one of the features that binds a people. The difference between Eastern Orthodox and Western Christian practice is precisely its divergent calendar of holidays. Two opinions among these *Tannaim* actually insist on preserving the distinctness of the 14th and 15th even at the cost of displacing one group, suggesting they regard the integrity of the two-day distinction as paramount.

And yet our Mishnah — the ruling of Rabbi — is willing to have the walled and unwalled read on the same day in certain circumstances, and indeed when *Purim* falls on *Shabbat*, Rabbi prefers to collapse the multiple displaced populations onto a single Thursday. It seems that for Rabbi the proliferation of separate days is not a value in itself. One might observe that a full range of possible reading dates from the 11th through the 15th actually softens the symbolic division: it is not that there are simply two peoples with two festivals, but rather a complex calendrical system in which any given community may find itself on one of many possible days. Whether the multiplication of dates reinforces or ameliorates the division is a question the sugya leaves genuinely open.

## No Reading on Shabbat

### Rava's Carrying Concern

With the ground cleared, the Gemara makes explicit what all the foregoing discussions assume: *megilla b'Shabbos lo karinan* — we do not read the Megilla on *Shabbat*. The Gemara asks: *mai ta'ama* — what is the reason? The reason is never stated in the Tannaitic sources and must be supplied by the Amoraim.

Rava provides the famous explanation: *hakol chayavin b'krias Megilla* — everyone is obligated to read the Megilla — *v'ein hakol baki'in b'mikra Megilla* — but not everyone knows how to read it themselves. Therefore, *gezira shema yitlena b'yado v'yeilech etzel baki lilmod v'ya'avirenu arba amos b'reshus ha-rabbim* — the Rabbis decreed a safeguard lest someone take his Megilla in hand, walk over to a neighbor who knows how to read it, and in the process carry it four *amot* in the public domain.

### Shofar, Lulav, & Carrying

It is important to recognize that the next line — *v'hainu ta'ama d'shofar v'hainu ta'ama d'lulav* — is introduced not by Rava but by the *stam* of the Gemara. The anonymous editorial voice extends Rava's carrying rationale to *shofar* and *lulav*, two other observances that are similarly not performed on *Shabbat*. But when we studied Masechet Sukkah and Masechet Rosh Hashanah, we encountered other possible reasons for those prohibitions. By *shofar*, for instance, the Gemara elsewhere cites the distinction between *zichron teruah* and *yom teruah*, suggesting a more intrinsic connection to *Shabbat* observance rather than merely a carrying concern. The *Yerushalmi* does not necessarily accept that the carrying rationale is the exclusive or even the primary reason for those prohibitions. All we have from Tannaitic sources is the prohibition itself; the attribution of the carrying rationale to all three — Megilla, shofar, and lulav — stems entirely from Rava's statement about the Megilla, which the *stam* then extrapolates.

### Rav Yosef's Reason

Rav Yosef offers a substantially different rationale, one with no reference to carrying at all: *mipnei she-eineihem shel aniyim nesuot b'mikra Megilla* — because the eyes of the poor are directed toward the reading of the Megilla. When the Megilla is read publicly, the passage *matanot l'evyonim* is recited, and the poor gather in the synagogue in anticipation of receiving gifts. Were the reading to take place on *Shabbat*, when giving and receiving money is prohibited, the poor would come with hope and leave with nothing — a state of acute despair at the very moment the Megilla is being read. Rav Yosef's reason thus focuses on the social reality of *matanot l'evyonim* and the experience of the impoverished members of the community.

This is a structurally different type of reason. It implies nothing about *shofar* and *lulav* — those observances have no corresponding collection for the poor — and so one might say that according to Rav Yosef, the Megilla has its own reason distinct from those other cases.

Tosafot, troubled by the apparent conflict between Rava's and Rav Yosef's explanations, argues that Rav Yosef does not actually dispute Rava's carrying rationale but merely adds an additional reason. His argument is that since there is no parallel reason available for *shofar* and *lulav* beyond carrying, carrying must remain in play; for the Megilla Rav Yosef simply identifies a second independent rationale. But this reading is not obviously correct. The plain sense of the Gemara is that Rav Yosef offers an alternative, and it is the *stam* alone that extended Rava to *shofar* and *lulav*; Rav Yosef may well hold that those cases have their own reasons entirely.

### Gifts & Early Reading

A *braita* is then cited that confirms and elaborates on Rav Yosef's position: even though the Rabbis said the *kfarim* advance their reading to the market day, they also collect and distribute *matanot l'evyonim* on that same day — *govin bo bayom u'machalkin bo bayom* — because the eyes of the poor are directed toward the reading of the Megilla. The coupling is deliberate: *matanot l'evyonim* follows the Megilla reading wherever it goes. The poor gather on account of the reading, and so the distribution must happen on the day of the reading, not on the actual fourteenth.

Not only that — the *braita* insists on same-day distribution, not merely same-day collection. One might have thought: collect early while the communal gathering is happening, but hold the funds and distribute them on the real *Purim* date. No. The *braita* requires distribution *bo bayom* as well, precisely because the poor are expecting it at the moment of the reading.

### Seudah & Mishloach Manot

The *braita* then adds: *simcha einah noheges ela bizmanah* — rejoicing, meaning the *Seudas Purim*, is only observed at its proper time. The festive meal does not follow the Megilla reading to an early date; it remains fixed on the actual 14th or 15th.

This creates a clear division of the four *mitzvot* of *Purim*. The *mikra megillah* moves early; *matanot l'evyonim* travels with the Megilla. The *seudah* stays on the real date. That leaves *mishloach manot*. The *mishloach manot* is understood to be food sent in connection with the *seudah* — it is a way of sharing in the festive meal — and so *mishloach manot* goes with the *seudah*, not with the Megilla, and both remain on the real date.

However, the case of *Purim meshulash* in *Yerushalayim*, where the fifteenth falls on *Shabbat*, introduces a further wrinkle. There the Megilla is read on Friday, the *seudah* is on *Shabbat*, and *mishloach manot* — given the concern about carrying on *Shabbat* — is done on Sunday. So while in the village scenario *mishloach manot* and the *seudah* remain together on the actual *Purim* date, in the *Shabbat* case the two can be separated by the practical constraints of *Shabbat* observance.

## Private vs. Public Readi…

### Rav's Position

The Gemara now turns to a question that emerges naturally from the centrality of *pirsumei nisa* to *mikra megillah*: if publicizing the miracle is so essential — sufficient to include even those normally exempt from time-bound commandments, as we saw earlier in the Masechet — does one need to read the Megilla specifically before a *tzibur*, or can an individual fulfill the obligation alone?

Rav states: *megilla bizmanah korin otah afilu beyachid, shelo bizmanah ba-asarah* — if reading on the right time, even an individual may read it; if reading off the right time — meaning on the early dates, such as the village's Monday or Thursday reading — you need ten people.

The distinction draws on the idea that when the reading is *bizmanah*, meaning on the actual designated day, the entire population is involved in the observance, so even an individual reading participates in a broader communal act. Rashi explains this explicitly: *mitokh she-chovah bo bayom al kol yachid v'yachid korin otah afilu beyachid, de-hakol korin bo v'ika pirsum hanes* — since the obligation is universal on that day and everyone is reading, even a private reading partakes of the overall *pirsum ha-nes*. When it is *shelo bizmanah*, by contrast, the population is split: some are reading early, others will read on the actual date. There is no universal participation, and so the *pirsumei nisa* must be secured explicitly through a quorum.

One should also note that the question of whether "ten" here means specifically ten men — the standard *minyan* — or whether it invokes a broader concept of *rabbim* (many people) is not entirely settled. The *mitzvah* of *pirsumei nisa* is not a *davar sheb'kedushah* requiring a formal *minyan*; the Maharam MiRothenburg, for instance, rules that *b'rabbim* for the purposes of *kiddush Hashem* is not defined by ten men. The same ambiguity may apply here.

### Rav Assi Stricter

Rav Assi takes a stricter position: *bein bizmanah bein shelo bizmanah ba-asarah* — whether on the right time or not, you always need ten. For Rav Assi, the essential character of *mikra megillah* is *pirsumei nisa*, and *pirsumei nisa* always requires a communal setting. There is no scenario in which a private reading fully instantiates the *mitzvah*; the publicization of the miracle is, by definition, a public act.

This is somewhat analogous to the question of *pirsumei nisa* by *Chanukah* candles — whether one can fulfill the obligation with a purely internal household publicization, or whether the miracle must be broadcast outward to others. Rav Assi takes the maximalist position: the miracle always requires publicization to *rabbim*.

### Rav & Rav Assi Reconciled

The Gemara then reports a practical episode: there was a case in which the Megilla was read *bizmanah*, and although Rav himself had stated that *bizmanah* allows individual reading, he was nonetheless concerned for the position of Rav Assi and insisted on reading *b'rabbim*. In practice, then, Rav leaned toward the stricter standard.

The Gemara then challenges this: would Rav really say that even *bizmanah* requires ten people? It cites a teaching in Rav's name — *Purim she-chal lihyos b'Shabbat, erev Shabbat zmanam* — that when *Purim* falls on *Shabbat*, Friday becomes their time. The Gemara initially reads this as implying a halachic distinction: Friday has the status of *zmanah*, and therefore it can be read *bizmanah* even as an individual. But this reading would only make sense if Rav held that *bizmanah* and *shelo bizmanah* carry different requirements — exactly his original position.

### Purim on Shabbat Case

The case of *Purim* falling on *Shabbat* introduces an important conceptual refinement. When the entire date shifts — not just the villagers reading early while the town reads on the real date, but the entire population displaced from *Shabbat* to Friday — the Friday reading takes on the character of *zmanah*. In the village scenario, the population is genuinely split: some read on Monday or Thursday, others on the actual date. That is a real *shelo bizmanah* for those who read early. But when *Purim* falls on *Shabbat* and everyone, everywhere is reading on Friday, Friday has functionally become the day, and its character is more like *zmanah* than *shelo bizmanah*.

### Rav Consistent with Rav…

The Gemara ultimately resolves the apparent contradiction differently. It concludes that *le-inyan mikra megillah ba-asarah* — when it comes to reading the Megilla with ten people — Rav may in fact hold like Rav Assi and always require ten. The statement *erev Shabbat zmanam* is not intended to convey a halachic distinction for the purpose of whether ten are required; rather, it is a statement directed against the position of Rabbi. Rabbi, as we saw, holds that since the reading is being displaced from *Shabbat* regardless, it might as well be pushed all the way back to Thursday. *Erev Shabbat zmanam* rejects that: Friday — not Thursday — is the appropriate day, and the statement underscores that Friday is the proper substitute, not to teach anything about the *bizmanah/shelo bizmanah* distinction for the purposes of requiring ten people.

In the end, the Gemara reads Rav as consistent with Rav Assi: even *bizmanah*, one should read with ten. Rashi and Tosafot both note, however, that from another Gemara it is clear that one can technically fulfill the obligation *biyachid*, and the practical *halacha* does not require ten. What Rav Assi's position establishes is not that a private reading is entirely invalid, but that it represents a significantly diminished — almost *bedi'eved* — fulfillment. Tosafot even raises a view that when reading alone one might not be entitled to recite the *bracha* of *she-asa nissim*, since the *pirsum ha-nes* component, which arguably gives rise to that blessing, is so attenuated. Even if one fulfills the minimum obligation, the reading without a *tzibur* may lack the core character of the *mitzvah*.

This has practical implications. If someone must choose between a rushed private reading in the morning and a full reading with a *minyan* at a less convenient time, the consideration is not merely the usual calculation of *b'rov am hadrat melech* — performing commandments with a larger community for the sake of divine honor. Here the very substance of the *mitzvah*, its *pirsumei nisa* dimension, is at stake when reading alone.

## Big Cities & Villages

### Ten Batlanim Definition

The Mishnah defines *ir gedolah* — a big city — as any settlement that has *asarah batlanim* — ten men who are available to be present in the synagogue at all times and are supported by the community for that purpose. Rashi explains: *beteilim mi-milachtam v'nizonim mishel tzibur kedei lihyot metzuyim b'tefillah b'veit ha-knesset* — they are free from their regular work and are sustained from communal funds so that they can always be present for prayer in the synagogue. Anything below this threshold is classified as a *kfar*, a village, and its inhabitants would need to advance their reading to the market day.

### Early Not Late

The Mishnah then states the general rule for the Megilla: *b'eilu amru makdimin v'lo ma'acharin* — regarding these cases, the Rabbis said you advance the reading and never delay it. The reading of the Megilla on the 14th or 15th may be moved to an earlier date under the relevant circumstances; it never gets pushed to a later date. The Gemara cites Rabbi Abba bar Shmuel who grounds this in the verse *v'lo ya'avor* — it shall not pass. The deadline is fixed; the reading cannot be performed after the designated time. Moving it earlier, under the appropriate conditions, is permitted; moving it later is never permitted.

The same passage of the Gemara also records two additional statements from Rabbi Abba bar Shmuel. One establishes that years are counted by months, not days — derived from *l'chodshei ha-shana*, "according to the months of the year" — meaning that if someone takes a vow for a year, we measure the year by the calendar date one year later, not by counting 354 or 365 individual days. The other establishes that months are counted by days, not hours — derived from *ad chodesh yamim*, "until the month of days" — meaning a month expires on the same date the following month, not after precisely 29 days and 12 hours. Rashi illustrates with the example of one who vows not to eat something for a year beginning on the third of *Sivan*: the year concludes on the second of *Sivan* the following year, not by counting out the precise number of days.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Caveat

Rabbi Yehuda adds a crucial qualification to the village's early reading: *eimatai* — when is it true that the villagers read early? *Makom she-nichnasim b'sheini u'v'chamishi* — only in a place where the villagers actually come into town on Mondays and Thursdays. If this particular set of villages does not in practice come to the town on those days — *lo b'sheini v'lo b'chamishi* — then we do not allow them to read early. *Ein korin otah ela bizmanah* — they read it only at its proper time.

The entire institution of the early village reading was predicated on a practical reality: the villagers gathered in the town on market days to hear the Torah read, and the Rabbis arranged for the Megilla to be read at that same gathering so as not to burden the villages with a separate trip. If the villages in question do not actually gather in town on those days, the rationale dissolves and the concession does not apply.

### Other Times Not Moveable

The Mishnah then contrasts the Megilla with several other observances: *zman atzei kohanim v'ha-am* — the day designated for bringing wood to the *Beit ha-Mikdash* — *Tisha B'Av*, *Chagiga*, and *Hakhel*. For all of these, the rule is the reverse: *ma'acharin v'lo makdimin* — they may be postponed but never advanced. If these observances fall on *Shabbat*, or cannot be performed on their designated day for another reason, they move to a later date, never to an earlier one.

## Postponement vs. Advance…

### Tisha B'Av Recoil

The Gemara explains the rationale for each case. Why does *Tisha B'Av* get postponed rather than advanced? *Akdumei puranuta lo makdemi* — one does not rush to bring bad things forward. *Tisha B'Av* marks catastrophe and mourning. We naturally recoil from it; we would never choose to fast and mourn a day early. The very character of the day as an occasion of communal suffering means that the human instinct is to push it away, not to draw it closer.

### Obligation & Timing

For *Chagiga* and *Hakhel*, the reason is different: *mishum di-kdem lehu zman chiyuva* — because the obligation has not yet come into force before the designated time. The *Chagiga* offering may only be brought during the *chag*; one cannot bring it before the *chag* begins because the obligation does not yet exist. Similarly, *Hakhel* takes place at the outset of the *Shmita* cycle, on the first day of *Chol ha-Moed Sukkot* of the *Shmita* year; if that day falls on *Shabbat*, the assembly is postponed to the following day. The obligation cannot precede its own inception point. One cannot discharge an obligation that has not yet arisen, and therefore advancement is structurally impossible, while postponement — doing it after the time has already arrived — remains valid.

Rashi extends this logic to *atzei kohanim* as well, though the reasoning is somewhat circular: we assume that if something cannot be done on its fixed day, it can be done later but not earlier, because the obligation is tied to the arrival of its time rather than preceding it.

### Rava on Chagiga

A *tanna* teaches: *chagiga v'chol zman chagiga ma'acharin* — the *Chagiga* and the entire period of *Chagiga* can be postponed. The Gemara asks: what does "the entire period of *Chagiga*" mean? The Gemara first explores a more complex reading proposed by Rav Oshaya: perhaps it refers to two distinct scenarios — the case where the *Chagiga* itself falls on *Shabbat* and must be postponed, and the case of the *olat re'iyah*, which even on a normal *Yom Tov* cannot be brought on the first day of *Yom Tov* (since it is not an *ochel nefesh* activity as would be required to permit *melacha* on *Yom Tov*) and must always be postponed. The Gemara notes that this second reading follows the position of *Beit Shammai*, who hold that *olot* may not be brought on *Yom Tov* at all, while *Beit Hillel* permit it.

Rava then offers a simpler and more elegant reading: *chagiga v'chol zman chagiga ma'acharin* — the *Chagiga* can be postponed for the entire duration of the *zman chagiga*, meaning the full length of the *regel*, but no later. If the first day of *Yom Tov* falls on *Shabbat* and the *Chagiga* cannot be brought, it may be brought on any subsequent day of the *chag*. The Mishnah confirms: *mi shelo chag b'yom tov rishon shel chag, chogeg v'holech kol ha-regel kulo u'v'yom tov ha-acharon shel chag* — one who did not bring the *Chagiga* on the first day may bring it throughout the entire *regel* including the last day of *Yom Tov*. But *avar ha-regel v'lo chag, eino chayav b'achrayuso* — if the entire *regel* passed without bringing it, the obligation lapses and cannot be discharged afterward.

Rava adds one further point: even for *Shavuot*, which is a single day, the same principle of postponement applies. If *Shavuot* falls on *Shabbat*, the *Chagiga* can be brought on Sunday. The Mishnah confirms: *modin Beit Shammai u-Veit Hillel d'im chal Atzeret lihyot b'Shabbat she-yom tivoach achar Shabbat* — both *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* agree that if *Shavuot* falls on *Shabbat*, the day of slaughter (i.e., bringing the *Chagiga*) is the following Sunday. The six days following *Shavuot* retain a connection to the *chag* analogous to the *Chol ha-Moed* days of the other *Chagim*, preserving the window for bringing the *Chagiga*.

## Rabbi & Tisha B'Av

### Rabbi's Actions

The Gemara records a remarkable tradition in the name of Rabbi Elazar, who said it in the name of Rabbi Chanina: Rabbi planted a sapling on *Purim* and bathed publicly in *Kronah shel Tzipori* — in the wagon-district of *Tzipori* — on the 17th of *Tammuz*. The planting on *Purim* speaks to the question of whether *Purim* carries a prohibition against *melacha*, a topic the Gemara will address in the following daf. The public bathing on the 17th of *Tammuz* was a deliberate demonstration: although that date is a fast day, it carries none of the additional *inuyim* — afflictions such as prohibitions on bathing, anointing, and wearing leather shoes — that apply on *Tisha B'Av* and *Yom Kippur*.

### Uproot Tisha B'Av

The *braita* then adds the explosive detail: *vi-bikeish la'akor Tisha B'Av v'lo hodu lo* — Rabbi sought to uproot *Tisha B'Av* entirely, and the Rabbis did not agree. The statement is stunning. How could any authority seek to abolish *Tisha B'Av*, and on what grounds?

### Correction: Shabbat Case

Rabbi Abba bar Zavda corrects the record before Rabbi Elazar: *lo kach hayah ma'aseh*, Rabbi, that is not what happened. *Ela Tisha B'Av she-chal lihyot b'Shabbat havah* — it was a specific year in which *Tisha B'Av* fell on *Shabbat*, so the fast was postponed to the tenth of *Av*. In that year, Rabbi argued: *ho'il v'nidcha yidcha* — since we are already not observing *Tisha B'Av* on its correct day, we might as well waive it entirely for that year. His reasoning was not an attempt to permanently abolish the fast, but a one-time application of the principle that once a day is displaced from its proper time, its observance loses the specific grounding that makes it obligatory. The Rabbis did not accept this reasoning and insisted on observing the fast on the tenth.

### Two Witnesses Better

Rabbi Elazar then applies a verse to the exchange: *tovim ha-shnayim min ha-echad* — two are better than one. Without Rabbi Abba bar Zavda's correction, the original report would have portrayed Rabbi as having attempted a wholesale abolition of *Tisha B'Av*. With his clarification, the picture is far more limited and legally coherent. The statement is thus both a compliment to Rabbi Abba bar Zavda and a reflection on the importance of accurate transmission: a single tradition, even when preserved by a reliable authority, benefits from being checked against additional witnesses.
