# Bava Batra 91 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hoarding & Prices

### Personal Storage

The Gemara opens on 91b, in the midst of a broader discussion concerning the manipulation of produce prices and the prohibition against hoarding. *Amar Rav*: *oseh adam et kavo otzar* — a person may make his *kav*, his personal measure of produce, into a storehouse. The Gemara carves out a clear distinction between two kinds of holding back produce from the market. On one hand, cornering the market — buying up all available supply in order to drive prices up — is forbidden. On the other hand, retaining one's own grain, the produce of one's own fields, while waiting for a more favorable time to sell, is entirely permissible. One is simply maximizing returns on one's own produce, not engineering artificial scarcity. A parallel reading permits even purchasing produce from the marketplace, provided it is done to meet one's own household needs rather than to speculate.

A *tanya nami hachi* — a parallel *baraita* — supports Rav's ruling: *ein otzrin peirot devarim sheyesh bahem chayei nefesh*, one may not hoard produce that constitutes basic sustenance needs. The *baraita* then draws the limiting principle: *bameh devarim amurim bilokeach min hashuk* — this restriction applies when one is buying from the marketplace, effectively cornering available supply. But *b'machnis mishelo mutar* — holding onto one's own produce is permitted. This confirms Rav's ruling.

### Necessities vs. Luxuries

The *baraita* specifies which categories of produce fall under the prohibition: *kegon yeinot shemanim veslatot* — wine, oils, and flour. It is worth noting that wine is classified here as a basic necessity, which reflects its ubiquity as a beverage in the ancient world. By contrast, *tavlin* — spices such as *kamon* (cumin) and *pilpelin* (peppers) — are considered luxury items, and hoarding them is *mutar*. The distinction between necessity and luxury has practical halachic weight, though one might observe that the line is not entirely self-evident: a world without spice is a world of considerable blandness, and yet the Rabbis drew the boundary here.

### Shmitta Storage

The *baraita* continues with an important exception: *umutar l'adam l'otzar peirot b'Eretz Yisrael gimel shanim erev sheviit vesheviit umotzei sheviit* — one is permitted to store produce for the three years of the *shemitta* cycle. The logic is straightforward: grain grows in the sixth year and must sustain the household through that year, through the *shemitta* year itself when no planting occurs, and into the eighth year until newly planted crops become available. This three-year storage window is explicitly sanctioned.

A deeper question lurks here: does this permission extend to buying from the marketplace with the intention of reselling during the *shemitta* period at a profit — essentially engaging in precisely the kind of speculative hoarding ordinarily forbidden? The answer is not simple. On one hand, the person is holding back supply when prices are low and releasing it when scarcity is high. On the other hand, they are performing a genuine public service, making essential produce available to those who failed to plan adequately — an echo, in miniature, of Yosef's strategy in Mitzrayim. Certainly the permission to store for one's own needs is clear; whether commercial intent is also covered remains an open question, and some authorities do extend the permission that far.

### Carobs in Famine

At the other extreme: *afilu kav charuvin lo ya'atzor b'shnei batzoret* — in years of famine, one should not hoard even a *kav* of carobs, for even so small a purchase brings a curse upon market prices. In a time of drought and scarcity, when collective anxiety about produce availability is acute, even a slightly larger-than-normal purchase can trigger market disruption disproportionate to its actual size.

This ruling is admittedly puzzling. Precisely in a time of famine, one's instinct is to secure a reserve, to protect oneself against further scarcity. The Gemara's concern appears to be about market psychology and the cascading effect even minor hoarding can have when anxiety is already running high. Perhaps the prohibition is somewhat narrowed to carobs specifically, since they are not a primary necessity — but the precise balance between self-protection and communal responsibility in a time of crisis is left somewhat open.

### Three-Year Storage

The practical application of the *shemitta* storage rule is illustrated through a brief anecdote. *Amar lei Rav Yosef bar Rav Chanina le-Fuga shamayei*: Rav Yosef bar Rav Chanina instructed his attendant Fuga to go out and store produce for three years — *erev shevi'it, shevi'it, umotzei shevi'it* — exactly as the *baraita* had taught. This serves as a concrete demonstration that the ruling was taken seriously and acted upon in practice.

## Export Restrictions

### No Export Staples

The Gemara transitions to a new *baraita*: *ein motzi'in peirot me-Eretz Yisrael* — one may not export produce from Eretz Yisrael, specifically *devarim sheyesh bahem chayei nefesh* — items essential to basic sustenance: *kegon yeinot shemanim veslatot*, wine, oil, and flour, as enumerated before. The Rabbis enacted a range of *takkanot* designed to protect the welfare of the population living in Eretz Yisrael, and preventing the export of basic foodstuffs was central among them.

It is worth pausing to situate the earlier discussion about hoarding. Much of what preceded this passage is naturally understood in the context of Eretz Yisrael, or at minimum in any community where the Jewish population is the majority — for these communal regulations to have their intended effect, they must govern the entire local market. Where Jews constitute a minority, the market is too heavily influenced by external forces for such restrictions to be meaningful. But the explicit focus on Eretz Yisrael as a distinct category now comes to the fore.

### Wine Exception

*Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira* dissented with respect to wine, permitting its export: *matir b'yayin mipnei shemam'it et hatifla* — because wine reduces frivolousness, or in other words, he regarded excessive wine in society as a social ill. Since he did not classify wine as a genuine necessity — indeed, he viewed it as something of a hazard — he saw no reason to restrict its export.

### Inter-Province Trade

The *baraita* extends the export prohibition beyond the borders of Eretz Yisrael proper: *kach ein motzi'in me-Eretz Yisrael le-Suria* — one likewise may not export these staples to Syria. Syria — the surrounding lands conquered by David and later by the Hasmoneans — occupies an intermediate status in halacha, sometimes referred to as *kibbush yachid*, conquest by an individual, with consequent uncertainty about whether it carries the full sanctity of Eretz Yisrael. One explanation holds that David conquered these territories before completing the conquest of the lands explicitly promised within the covenantal borders, leaving Syria in a hybrid status: for some purposes it is treated like Eretz Yisrael, for others it is not. Here, it is treated as *not* Eretz Yisrael — and yet the export prohibition still applies, because the goal is to strengthen the *ikar yishuv*, the primary Jewish settlement within the covenantal borders.

*Ve-Rabbi matir me-hiparchia le-hiparchia* — Rabbi permits trade from one province to another. The *Rashbam* explains this as referring specifically to neighboring provinces, geographically contiguous regions, so that even trade from Eretz Yisrael to Syria is permitted as long as the regions share a border.

### No Markup on Staples

At the top of 91a, the Gemara introduces a further restriction: *ein mishtakrin b'Eretz Yisrael bidvarim sheyesh bahem chayei nefesh* — one may not profit through commercial trade in staple goods in Eretz Yisrael, once again specifying wine, oil, and flour. The practical question is: how does commerce function at all if profit is prohibited? The *Rashbam* explains that the intent is to eliminate intermediaries — the goal is for buyers to purchase directly from farmers or producers, without layers of wholesalers and retailers each adding their margin. Every additional middleman drives prices up, and the Rabbis wanted the lowest possible prices for basic necessities.

The Gemara then notes that *amru alav al Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah* — it was reported of Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah that he himself would act as a middleman and profit from wine and oil. Two explanations are offered for why he did not violate the prohibition. With respect to wine, he held like Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira, who did not classify wine as a basic necessity. With respect to oil: *b'atrei d'Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah shechiach mishcha* — in his locale, oil was abundant, and therefore the usual concern about middlemen restricting access and inflating prices simply did not apply. Where supply is plentiful and prices are naturally low, an intermediary does not cause the harm that the regulation is designed to prevent.

### Middlemen Limits

A separate *baraita* addresses eggs: *ein mishtakrin pa'amayim b'veitzim* — one may not make double profits from eggs. *Mari bar Mari* reports that Rav and Shmuel disputed the precise meaning of *pa'amayim*. One holds that a single middleman may not double the price, i.e., resell at twice the cost. The other holds that the restriction is on two successive middlemen — a wholesaler followed by a retailer — each taking a cut, so that by the time the product reaches the consumer the price has been raised twice over.

The *Rashbam* notes that eggs are not quite in the category of *chayei nefesh* in the fullest sense, and collecting and transporting them involves real effort and fragility. The Rabbis therefore permitted some profit margin — more than with wine, oil, and flour — but they placed a ceiling on it, either on the size of the markup or the number of intermediaries permitted.

## Price Fluctuations

### Price Drop Alarm

The Gemara records: *matri'in al haprakmatya va'afilu b'Shabbat* — one sounds the alarm for communal prayer on account of a drastic drop in the price of produce, and this may be done even on *Shabbat*, when ordinarily one does not pray for personal needs. This is striking: the conventional concern has been about prices rising too high, restricting access to food. Yet a dramatic price *drop* is treated as equally serious, warranting emergency communal response even on *Shabbat*. The worry is that a precipitous collapse in prices signals the onset of a broader economic crisis — deflation, depression, the unraveling of entire industries — and the potential suffering this brings is grave enough to override the normal *Shabbat* restrictions on petitionary prayer.

### Critical Commodities

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan*: the emergency response applies specifically to *klei fishtan b'Bavel v'yayin v'shemen b'Eretz Yisrael* — linen goods in Babylonia, and wine and oil in Eretz Yisrael. These are the commodities that serve as the economic backbone of their respective regions. When the price of linen collapses in Babylonia, or wine and oil collapse in Eretz Yisrael, entire sectors of the population — farmers, craftsmen, traders — are imperiled. This is therefore the category of economic disruption severe enough to mobilize communal prayer.

## Emigration Rules

### Price Thresholds

The Gemara now transitions from commercial regulation to the question of emigration: *ein yotzin me'Eretz Yisrael lechutz la'aretz* — one does not leave Eretz Yisrael for *chutz la'aretz*, even temporarily, *ela im ken amdu satayim b'sela* — unless two *se'ah* of grain has risen to a *sela*, roughly double the ordinary price. Only in the face of such severe inflation is departure permitted. The connection to the story of Elimelech, Naomi, and their sons — who left Eretz Yisrael during famine — is already implicit and will be made explicit shortly.

### Availability Priority

*Amar Rabbi Shimon eimatai bizman she'eino motzei likach* — Rabbi Shimon qualifies: when is departure permitted? When produce cannot be found to purchase at all. But *aval bizman shemotzei likach afilu amda se'ah b'sela lo yeitzei* — if produce can be obtained, even if it costs a full *sela* per *se'ah*, one may not leave. The operative criterion, in Rabbi Shimon's view, is availability, not merely price.

### Tosafot Challenge

*Tosafot* immediately raises a logical difficulty: *eimatai bizman she'eino motzei likach — teima im eino motzei likach afilu elef se'ah b'sela mai ya'avid keivan she'eino motzei likach* — if the criterion is unavailability, then price is irrelevant. If there is simply nothing to buy, the price at which nothing is being sold is beside the point. *Tosafot*'s question is sharp: Rabbi Shimon seems to be offering availability as the key factor, yet the original *baraita* framed the threshold in terms of price. The resolution must lie somewhere between the two: Rabbi Shimon is not describing literal total unavailability, but rather a condition of extreme difficulty of access combined with prohibitive price — a situation where the grain, even if technically obtainable, is effectively beyond reach. The two criteria must be read in tandem, not as alternatives, for otherwise Rabbi Shimon's formulation would render the price threshold meaningless.

The underlying principle, however, is unambiguous: leaving Eretz Yisrael for *chutz la'aretz* even in hard times is treated with the utmost seriousness, and the bar for permissibility is set very high.

## Elimelech Story

### Leadership Departure

*Vechen hayah Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai omer* — Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai similarly taught: *Elimelech, Machlon, veChilyon gedolei hador hayu ufarnasei hador hayu* — Elimelech, Machlon, and Chilyon were the great ones of their generation, men of wealth and leadership who sustained their community. Elimelech apparently held a position of *shofet* or something close to it. *Mipnei mah ne'anshu* — why were they punished, why did they all die? *Mipnei sheyatzu me'aretz lechutz la'aretz* — because they left Eretz Yisrael for *chutz la'aretz*. This reading is not far-fetched: the *pesukim* themselves say they left because of the famine, and they died; and when Naomi returned, the verse says *ki pakad Hashem et amo* — God had remembered His people. The implicit critique is devastating: while these men gave up and left, those who remained in Eretz Yisrael were remembered by God and found redemption. The men who departed found only death.

### Naomi's Return

When Naomi returned, the city stirred: *vatehom kol ha'ir aleihen vatomeranah hazot Naomi* — the whole city was astounded at them and said, "Is this Naomi?"

### Wordplay Critique

*Amar Rabbi Yitzchak*: the word *hazot* contains a wordplay. By shifting the *heh* to a *chet*, one reads *chazitem* — "have you seen?" The popular interpretation, Rabbi Yitzchak suggests, was: "Have you seen this Naomi, who went from Eretz Yisrael to *chutz la'aretz* — see what became of her?" *Mah alta lah* — what did it get her? While those who stayed were redeemed, she buried her husband and her sons. It is a pointed communal commentary, embedded in the very word with which the people of Bethlehem greeted her return.

### Ruth & Boaz

*Ve'amar Rabbi Yitzchak amar Rabbi Yehoshua*: on the very day that Ruth the Moabite came to Eretz Yisrael — even before she had converted — Boaz's wife died. The Gemara then cites a folk saying: *ad d'lo shachiv shichva kaima bego beiteih* — even before the one on the deathbed has passed, the next occupant of the household has already been established. God arranges the succession. The stage was set for Ruth to fill the place of Boaz's wife before that place was even empty. This also, incidentally, places Boaz in a state of bereavement and loss precisely at the moment of Ruth's arrival — a detail that colors the entire story of their subsequent relationship.

## Boaz Genealogy

### Boaz = Avtzan

*Amar Rabbah bar Rav Huna amar Rav: Avtzan zeh Boaz* — Avtzan, one of the *shoftim* of Bnei Yisrael, is to be identified with Boaz. *Mai k'mashma lan* — what is the practical significance of this identification? The Gemara answers: it connects to another statement of Rabbah bar Rav Huna himself.

### 120 Feasts

*D'amar Rabbah bar Rav Huna amar Rav: me'ah v'esrim mishta'ot asah Boaz l'vanav* — Boaz made 120 wedding feasts for his children. The proof-text is drawn from the *pesukim* about Avtzan: *vayihyu lo shloshim banim ushloshim banot shilach hachutza ushloshim banot hevi l'vanav min hachutz* — he had 30 sons and 30 daughters; the 30 daughters he sent out in marriage, and 30 daughters-in-law he brought in for his sons. *Vayishpot et Yisrael sheva shanim* — and he judged Israel for seven years. For each of the 60 children, Boaz made two celebrations: *echad b'veit aviv v'echad b'veit chamiv* — one in the father's house, corresponding to the *eirusin*, and one in the father-in-law's house, corresponding to the *nisuin*. That yields 120 feasts in total.

### Manoach Excluded

*U'vechulan lo ziman et Manoach* — and to none of these 120 feasts did he invite Manoach. At that time, Manoach was childless — *ishto akara v'lo yaladah*, his wife was barren and had borne no children. Boaz's reasoning: *amar kudna akara b'mai para li* — this sterile mule, how can he repay me? The social expectation was reciprocal: you invite someone to your child's wedding feast, and they invite you to theirs. Since Manoach had no children, there was no prospect of reciprocation.

The *Maharsha* is troubled by this, since Boaz is ordinarily regarded as a *tzaddik*, and the phrase "sterile mule" is hardly charitable. The *Maharsha* suggests that Boaz was in fact motivated by a concern for Manoach's dignity: he did not wish to place Manoach in the socially awkward position of being obligated to a host he could never repay. However one reads Boaz's motive, the outcome is the same — Manoach received no invitation.

### One > Sixty

*Tana: v'chulam metu b'chayav* — all of Boaz's 60 children died during his lifetime. And yet ultimately, through his marriage to Ruth, he became the ancestor of David HaMelech. The folk saying cited is: *b'chayecha d'yaladat shitin shitin lama li — ichbi v'olid chad d'mishitin zariz* — if you have 60 children, who needs 60? Better to have one more who will outshine all 60.

The *Rashbam* clarifies the point: according to his reading, the 60 children had not yet died by the time Boaz married Ruth, yet even with 60 living children Boaz felt the obligation to continue having children — consistent with the principle derived elsewhere in *Gemara biYevamot* that one should never rest content with the children one already has, since one can never know which child will ultimately flourish and carry forward the family's legacy. The child born of Ruth surpassed all the rest, leading to the royal line of David. Some read the final proverb differently: the "one" who surpasses the 60 of Boaz refers not to Obed son of Ruth but to Shimshon son of Manoach — the very man excluded from all the wedding feasts — and the statement becomes a particularly pointed critique of Boaz's conduct.

### Nachshon Lineage

*Amar Rav Chanan bar Rava amar Rav*: Elimelech, Salmo (Boaz's father), Ploni Almoni (the unnamed kinsman-redeemer of the Book of Ruth), and Naomi's father — *kulam benei Nachshon ben Aminadav hein* — all were descendants of Nachshon ben Aminadav. Given that Boaz and Naomi's family were related and both from the tribe of Yehuda, the connection to the line of Nachshon is coherent. The Gemara notes that Ploni Almoni is treated here not as an anonymous placeholder but as an actual name — the *goel* who declined to redeem Ruth was himself of this lineage. The genealogical assertion unifies all the central figures of the Book of Ruth under a single ancestral line.

*Mai kamashma lan* — why does it matter to establish this lineage? *She'afilu mi sheyeish lo zechut avot ein omedet lo b'sha'at yetzi'ah me'eretz lechutz la'aretz* — even a person who possesses the merit of illustrious ancestors will not find that merit standing in his defense at the moment of departing from Eretz Yisrael to *chutz la'aretz*. Even Elimelech, descended from the great Nachshon ben Aminadav, was not protected by that *zechut* when he abandoned his people and his land in their time of need.

## Biblical Mothers

### Mothers' Names

*Ve'amar Rabi Chanan bar Rava amar Rav* introduces a list of mothers whose names are not recorded in *Tanakh*. The mother of Avraham was *Amatalai bat Karnebo*. The mother of Haman was *Amatalai bat Orvati* — the same first name, *Amatalai*, in both cases. The mnemonic offered is *tamei tamei tahor tahor*: Haman is impure, and *Orvati* echoes *orev*, a raven, a bird associated with impurity — the two impure ones go together; Avraham is pure, and *Karnebo* suggests *karim*, sheep — the two pure ones go together. The mother of David was *Nitzevet bat Adael*. The mother of Shimshon — always referred to in *Tanakh* simply as *eshet Manoach* — was *Tzalponis*. Additionally, Shimshon's sister (whose existence is not mentioned in the *pesukim*) is named *Nashyan*. This is acknowledged as a puzzling collection of traditions: where do these names come from?

### Anti-Heretic Purpose

*Lemai nafka mina* — what is the practical application of knowing these names? *Leteshuvat haminim* — to respond to heretics. When heretics challenge Jewish tradition by pointing to figures in *Tanakh* whose mothers are unnamed, the response is that the oral tradition preserves those names, even where the written text is silent.

## Abraham Captivity

### Three to Bar Mitzvah

*Amar Rabi Chanan bar Rava amar Rav: eser shanim nechbash Avraham Avinu* — Avraham Avinu was held in captivity for ten years. The *Maharsha* explains this in light of the *midrashic* tradition: Avraham recognized God at the age of three and began to oppose idolatry; the powers of the time — his father, Nimrod — subjected him to a ten-year period of captivity and judgment before casting him into the fiery furnace of *Ur Kasdim*. This would place the imprisonment from age three until his *bar mitzvah*, the age at which the broader society considered a person legally accountable for their actions.

### Geography Variants

The ten years of captivity were divided between two locations: *shalosh b'Kuta v'sheva b'Kardu* — three years in *Kuta* and seven in *Kardu*. *Rav Dimi mi-Neharde'a* reverses the order: three in *Kardu* and seven in *Kuta*. *Rav Chisda* adds a geographical identification: *Ibra Ze'ira de-Kuta zehu Ur Kasdim* — a place called the small *Ibra* of *Kuta* is to be identified with the biblical *Ur Kasdim*, the location where, according to the *midrash*, Avraham was thrown into the fire. The name *Ibra* may carry an echo of *Ivri*, reflecting the connection to Avraham's identity as a Hebrew.

## Divine Providence

### Nations Mourn

*V'amar Rav Chana bar Rava amar Rav*: on the day that Avraham Avinu died, *amdu kol gedolei umos ha'olam b'shura* — all the great ones of the nations of the world stood in a line to offer *nichum aveilim*, and they said: *oy lo l'olam she'avad manhigo v'oy l'sfina she'avad kabarnita* — woe to the world that has lost its leader, and woe to the ship that has lost its captain. This is fitting for Avraham: the *pesukim* record his reputation among the surrounding nations, and his universal proclamation of God's name, *vayikra b'shem Hashem*, left an indelible mark.

### Leader & Captain

The *Maharsha* asks why two separate images are used — the leader (*manhigo*) and the captain of a ship (*kabarnita*). He explains that they correspond to Avraham's two great contributions to humanity. The first is the recognition of God's existence: just as one who sees the world's rotation must infer a Mover, Avraham grasped that *yesh l'olam manhig* — the world has a Director. The proclamation *oy l'olam she'avad manhigo* thus echoes both the loss of Avraham and his life's central teaching: there is a Leader over the world. The second contribution is *tzedek u'mishpat* — justice and righteousness — which God says Avraham will teach his children: *la'asot tzedakah umishpat*. A ship's captain navigates by keeping to a straight course, and Avraham taught humanity how to navigate life with moral clarity, keeping to the straight and narrow. Both images are thus deeply apt.

### Local Authority

The Gemara then cites a further statement: *afilu resh gargasusa mishmaya mukmi lei* — even the supervisor of a communal well, who determines which families draw water on which day, holds his position by divine appointment. The proof-text is *v'hamisnasei l'chol l'rosh* — God is exalted above all heads, but the verse also implies that God raises up every head, every position of authority, however modest. The *Maharsha* notes two layers of meaning: it is a statement of *hashgacha pratis* — that even the most local, seemingly insignificant official is placed there by divine will — and simultaneously a moral charge, reminding every person in a position of authority that they were appointed by God and must conduct themselves accordingly, with justice and integrity.

## Famine Debate

### Prayer Duty

Returning now to the story of Elimelech — after a series of digressions about Boaz, genealogies, Avraham's captivity, and divine providence — the Gemara records a dissenting voice. *Amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha: chas v'shalom she'afilu matzu subim lo yatzu* — God forbid that Elimelech, Machlon, and Chilyon left because of famine. Even had they found nothing but bran to eat, they never would have abandoned Eretz Yisrael. This is a striking reinterpretation: while everyone else stayed, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha insists that true leaders of Elimelech's caliber would have remained under any circumstances.

Then why were they punished? *V'ela mimma ne'anshu she'haya lahem levakesh rachamim al doram v'lo bikshu* — they were punished because they failed to pray for their generation. As men of leadership and means, their obligation was not merely to sustain themselves but to intercede on behalf of their people. Whatever compelled their departure, they abandoned their spiritual responsibility to their community. The proof-text: *b'za'akecha yatzilucha kivutzayich* — through your outcry, you will save those gathered around you. A leader has an obligation to cry out to God on behalf of those in their care.

### Economic Conditions

*Amar Rabba bar bar Chana amar Rabbi Yochanan: lo shanu ela maot b'zol u'ferot b'yoker* — the restriction on leaving Eretz Yisrael discussed earlier applies specifically when money is cheap and produce is expensive. This is the classic inflation scenario: currency is plentiful, but food is scarce and costly. Under those conditions, the prohibition on emigration applies in full.

### Depression Worse

*Aval maot b'yoker afilu amdu arba se'in b'sela yotzin* — but if money is scarce, even if four *se'ah* can be purchased for a *sela* — prices are low — one may leave. A condition of depression, where currency itself is nearly unobtainable, is deemed more dire than inflation. Why? Because Rabbi Yochanan draws on personal memory.

## Nostalgic Memories

### Tiberias Famine

*Nahirna kad havu kayamin arba se'in b'sela v'havu nefishi nefichei chafan b'Tverya* — I remember, says Rabbi Yochanan, when four *se'ah* could be bought for a *sela*, and yet in Tiberias there were many who were bloated from famine. *Mide'leit issar* — because no one could lay their hands on even a single small coin. Cheap produce means nothing if no one has money. The condition of monetary scarcity — economic depression — produces greater suffering than high prices, because at least high prices can be met by someone who has resources. When money has dried up entirely, even a market full of affordable goods is inaccessible.

### Abundant Carobs

Rabbi Yochanan then pivots to a memory of abundance: *nahirna kad hava batza yenuka charuva v'hava nagid chuta de'duvsha al terein derauhi* — I remember when a child would crack open a carob, and the sweet juice would drip down both of his arms. The carob, ordinarily an unremarkable and rather dry fruit, was so plump and moist in that era of blessing that its juices ran freely. *V'amar Elazar: nahirna kad hava natil urva bisra v'nagid chuta de'mishcha me'reish shura v'ad le'ara* — and Elazar adds: I remember when a raven would pick up a piece of meat and set it down on top of a wall, and the fat would drip all the way from the top of the wall to the ground. These are images of a world overflowing with natural abundance — a time when the divine blessing was palpably present in the physical world.

### Righteous Youth

*V'amar Rabbi Yochanan: nahirna kad havu metayelin talya v'talyata* — I remember when a young man and a young woman would stroll together *b'shuka* — in the marketplace — already sixteen or seventeen years old and physically mature, and *v'lo havu chatan* — they would not sin. These were days not only of material abundance but of moral elevation. Young people, despite the natural pull of youthful desire, conducted themselves with complete propriety. The world, Rabbi Yochanan implies, was a different place — beautiful in its produce and beautiful in its people.

## Authority Warnings

### Concession Falls

*V'amar Rabbi Yochanan: nahirna kad havu amrin bei midrasha* — I remember what they used to say in the *beit midrash*, a teaching about the dangers of entanglement with non-Jewish authority. *Demodei lehoun naflu bideihoun* — whoever concedes to them will fall into their hands. The *Maharsha* explains that this refers not necessarily to acknowledging the validity of a position, but to fawning and flattery — the sycophantic cultivation of those in power. The underlying message is the classic rabbinic counsel of maintaining distance from governmental authority. Whoever seeks favor through flattery ultimately becomes dependent and, in the end, vulnerable.

### Reliance Loses

*V'dimtrachitz aleihon diley dilhon* — and whoever places their reliance upon them rather than upon God: what is his will become theirs. In the end, those who stake their security on the goodwill of earthly authorities rather than on divine protection will find that their possessions slip away and end up in those very authorities' hands. These were teachings preserved in the *beit midrash* — warnings not merely of a pragmatic nature but rooted in a theological conviction about where true security lies.
