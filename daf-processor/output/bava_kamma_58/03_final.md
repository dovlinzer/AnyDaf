# Bava Kamma 58 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hana'ah vs. Nezek

### Nafela L'Gina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפְלָה לְגִינָּה וְנֶהֱנֵית – מְשַׁלֶּמֶת מַה שֶּׁנֶּהֱנֵית. אָמַר רַב: בְּנֶחְבְּטָה;
>
> **Translation:** If the animal **fell into a garden and derives benefit,** the owner **pays** for **the benefit that** it **derives. Rav says:** This is referring to a case **where** the vegetables softened the impact of **striking** the ground, and the owner pays for this benefit of the animal being saved from injury.

The daf opens by returning to a core *Bava Kamma* concern: an animal that enters another's field and causes damage. The *mishnah* uses the phrase *nafela legina v'nehenis* — a sheep fell into a garden and derived benefit — and concludes *meshalemes mah she'nehenis*: the owner pays only for the benefit received, not for the *nezek*.

The underlying principle is the concept of *hana'ah*. Even if the animal's entry into the field would otherwise constitute *shen* or *regel* — categories of actionable damage — the fact that the animal fell in as an *ones* (force majeure) exempts the owner from liability for *nezek*. Nevertheless, a concrete transfer of value has occurred: the animal benefited from the owner's property. That benefit, as it were, remains "held" by the animal's owner, and must be returned. The logic parallels a case where money was transferred from one person's bank account to another's inadvertently — no theft occurred, no *aveidah* was created, but the value must be restored. So too here: the owner pays *mah she'nehenis*, no more.

### Cushioning Fall

On this, *Rav* says a single word: *benachbeta* — the case concerns an animal whose fall was cushioned by the fruit. The *hana'ah* in this instance is not a meal consumed but rather the physical cushioning of the animal's impact. Assessing that *hana'ah* is genuinely difficult: the fruit may have been worth ten dollars, yet it may have prevented the animal's death, in which case the benefit received far exceeds the market value of the fruit. The Gemara does not resolve precisely how one would calculate *hana'ah* in such a case.

### Eating Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אָכְלָה – אֲפִילּוּ מַה שֶּׁנֶּהֱנֵית אֵינָהּ מְשַׁלֶּמֶת. לֵימָא רַב לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דַּאֲמַר רַב: הָיָה לַהּ שֶׁלֹּא תֹּאכַל?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: This statement of Rav indicates that the owner pays only for what occurred while the animal landed, **but if** the animal then **ate** from the garden, the owner **is not** required to **pay even** for **the benefit that** the animal **derives. Shall we say that Rav** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning? As Rav says** that in a case where one placed his produce in the courtyard of another without permission, and an animal belonging to the owner of the courtyard ate the produce and was sickened thereby, the owner of the produce is not liable, as he can claim: The animal **should not have eaten** it. Similarly, here the owner of the animal can say: This animal fell through no fault of mine, and so it is not my fault that it ate.

What follows is striking. The Gemara introduces: *aval achla afilu mah she'nehenis eino meshalemess* — but if the animal ate, it does not pay even *mah she'nehenis*. This is puzzling in the extreme. The animal fell in as an *ones*, which explains the exemption from *nezek*. But why should the owner be exempt from paying for the meal? The animal ate someone else's food and derived clear, quantifiable benefit. Why is there no obligation to restore even the *hana'ah*?

### Hayah Lah Shelo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! אֵימוֹר דְּאָמַר רַב הָתָם ״הָיָה לַהּ שֶׁלֹּא תֹּאכַל״ – הֵיכָא דְּאִיתַּזְקָא הִיא, דְּמָצֵי אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָרֵיהּ דְּפֵירֵי: ״לָא מְשַׁלַּמְנָא, הָיָה לַהּ שֶׁלֹּא תֹּאכַל״; לְאַזּוֹקֵי הִיא אַחֲרִינֵי, דִּפְטִירָה לְשַׁלּוֹמֵי, מִי אָמַר?!
>
> **Translation:** The Sages of the Gemara **say: How can** these cases **be compared?** One could **say that Rav said there** that the animal **should not have eaten** the produce. This claim is relevant **where** the animal **itself was injured** by eating another's produce, **because the owner of the produce can say: I will not pay,** because the animal **should not have eaten.** This is a claim to exempt the owner of the produce from paying the owner of the animal. **Did** Rav **say** that **when** the animal **causes damage to another's** produce, the owner can **exempt** himself **from paying** the owner of the produce by stating this claim?

The Gemara attempts to connect this ruling to Rav's position elsewhere: *d'amar Rav hayah lah shelo tochal* — the animal should not have eaten so much. This refers to a case in which Reuven brought fruit into Shimon's domain without permission, and Shimon's cow ate it and suffered a stomach ache. There, Rav says Reuven need not pay for the cow's injury, reasoning that the cow should not have eaten to excess and thereby harmed itself. The Gemara suggests that perhaps the rule about the eating animal also follows this logic — *hayah lah shelo tochal*, it should not have eaten, and therefore the owner is exempt.

But this reasoning is immediately counterintuitive. *Hayah lah shelo tochal* means the animal brought harm upon itself by overeating — the point being that the damage was self-inflicted. To apply that principle here, where the animal is consuming someone *else's* property and causing *them* a loss, inverts the logic entirely. If the animal should not have eaten, that is all the more reason to require payment to the one whose food was consumed. The Gemara senses this immediately.

### One Word Only

A further complication: as a sharp reader points out, Rav said only one word — *benachbeta*. The formulation *aval achla afilu mah she'nehenis* is the Gemara's own inference, not Rav's ruling. This observation is significant because once we recognize that Rav limited his statement to *benachbeta*, the framing of the entire discussion shifts. The word *benachbeta* is not meant to be *davka* — it is not limiting the *mishnah*'s ruling to cases of cushioning only. Rather, it highlights the *chiddush* of that case.

### Lo Mibaya Achla

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לָא מִבַּעְיָא״ קָאָמַר: לָא מִבַּעְיָא אָכְלָה – דִּמְשַׁלֶּמֶת מַה שֶּׁנֶּהֱנֵית; אֲבָל נֶחְבְּטָה – אֵימָא מַבְרִיחַ אֲרִי מִנִּכְסֵי חֲבֵירוֹ הוּא, וּמַה שֶּׁנֶּהֱנֵית נָמֵי לָא מְשַׁלֵּם; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** one cannot make such an inference from Rav's statement. Rav **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary,** and this is how to understand his statement: **It is not necessary** to state that if the animal fell into the garden and **ate** from its produce, **that** the owner **pays** for **the benefit that** it **derives. But** if the produce softened the blow of **striking** the ground and thereby the animal avoided injury, one might **say** that the owner of the animal should not pay, on the grounds that the owner of the garden may be viewed, analogously, to one who **repels a lion from another's property.** In such a case, although the latter benefited from his action, he is not obligated to pay for it. Similarly in this case, one might think that the owner of the animal does **not pay even** for **the benefit that** the animal **derived.** For this reason Rav **teaches us** that the owner of the animal must pay for this benefit as well.

The Gemara now resolves the apparent paradox with the principle of *lo mibaya ka'amar* — the *mishnah* is stating its ruling by way of the less obvious case. It goes without saying (*lo mibaya*) that if the animal fell as an *ones* and then ate, *meshalemes mah she'nehenis* — the owner pays for the benefit of the meal. That is the classic *mah she'nehenis* case: no liability as a *mazik*, but an obligation to restore the value of a meal received. The *chiddush* is specifically about *benachbeta* — that even in the case of cushioning, where the *hana'ah* is not a consumed meal but a prevented injury, one still pays *mah she'nehenis*. That is what Rav came to teach with his single word.

## Mavriach Ari Debate

### Lion Chase Hypothetical

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא הָכִי נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **say** that **this is indeed** the *halakha*, and the owner of the animal should be exempt for paying for the benefit of his animal not being injured?

Having established that one pays *mah she'nehenis* even in the *benachbeta* case, the Gemara asks: why? After all, the animal's fall cushioned by the fruit presents an unusual configuration — the owner's property passively intervened to prevent harm to another's property. This raises a comparison to the classic case of *mavriach ari minichsei chaveiro*: chasing a lion away from a friend's field. Is such a benefactor entitled to reimbursement for the value of the benefit conferred?

### Mitzvah Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַבְרִיחַ אֲרִי מִנִּכְסֵי חֲבֵירוֹ – מִדַּעְתּוֹ הוּא, הַאי – לָאו מִדַּעְתּוֹ. אִי נָמֵי, מַבְרִיחַ אֲרִי מִנִּכְסֵי חֲבֵירוֹ – לֵית לֵיהּ פְּסֵידָא, הַאי – אִית לֵיהּ פְּסֵידָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: One who **repels a lion from another's property** does so **with intent,** knowing that he would be ineligible for payment. By contrast, **this** owner of the garden did **not** act **with intent** and would have preferred for the incident not to have happened. **Alternatively,** one could say that one who **repels a lion from another's property does not** thereby **have any loss** himself. By contrast, **this** owner of the garden **has a loss,** in that his produce is damaged.

The Gemara's initial concern is this: performing a *mitzvah* does not entitle one to payment. A doctor or a rabbi, strictly speaking, is not permitted to charge for services that constitute the performance of a *mitzvah*; whatever compensation is permitted operates under the rubric of *sechar batala* — opportunity cost, payment for what one could have been doing otherwise, not for the *mitzvah* act itself. If chasing the lion was a *mitzvah*, perhaps no reimbursement is warranted. The benefited party might simply say: a good deed is its own reward.

This framing generates a tension. On one hand, if the benefactor performed a *mitzvah*, he cannot collect. On the other hand — and this is a distinct conceptual point — there is a question whether there was any *hana'ah* at all in the technical sense. The person whose property was saved has not gained additional value; his property simply was not lost. Preventing a loss and conferring a benefit are different things. If the *hana'ah* concept requires that value be transferred from one party to another, then merely preventing destruction may not qualify as *hana'ah* at all.

### Mida'ato Distinction

The Gemara draws a key distinction. *Mavriach ari minichsei chaveiro mi-da'ato hu* — when a person consciously acts to chase the lion, he does so of his own volition. In that case, the act is framed as a *mitzvah*, and the conscious choice to perform it carries an implicit understanding that one is acting selflessly, without expectation of payment. *Hai lav mi-da'ato hu* — in the *benachbeta* case, the person is not acting at all. His property acted on its own. There was no conscious decision, no *mitzvah* framing, and therefore no implicit waiver of the right to reimbursement. The irony is notable: the less deliberate the act, the stronger the claim to compensation.

This distinction carries a further implication. When the owner's property passively cushioned the animal's fall, the situation is not framed as *mavriach ari* — it is simply: I suffered a loss because of your animal, and I am entitled to recover something. The absence of conscious agency removes the *mitzvah* overlay entirely.

### Loss-Based Rationale

The Gemara offers a second rationale: *mavriach ari minichsei chaveiro leit lei pesida* — when a person consciously chases the lion, he incurs no loss. He cannot collect payment for his personal service. But *hai it lei pesida* — in the *benachbeta* case, there is an actual loss. And where there is a loss, the party who suffered it is entitled to at least recover expenses.

Importantly, this does not mean full *nezek* compensation. One cannot be paid for personal service and cannot receive more than the *hana'ah* conferred. The entitlement is likely limited to the lesser of the actual loss and the *hana'ah* received by the other party — which, practically speaking, in most such cases will be equivalent. If $10 worth of fruit was destroyed while saving a sheep worth considerably more, the *mah she'nehenis* is at least $10, and the owner of the fruit can recover that amount. The illustration is apt: if in saving someone's property one ruins a suit in a puddle, one cannot charge for the *mitzvah* of saving the property, but one is entitled to recover the cleaning bill — to the extent that it overlaps with the *hana'ah* conferred.

### Hefsed & Psida

The Gemara's analysis thus yields a coherent framework. The *mavriach ari* who acts consciously and incurs no loss cannot collect anything — neither for service nor for *hana'ah*. The owner of property that passively intervenes and sustained a loss occupies a different position: because there was no conscious *mitzvah* act and because there was genuine *hefsed*, the owner can recover, but only to the extent of the *hana'ah* conferred — not as full *nezek*. The entitlement is recovery of loss, bounded by *hana'ah*, not payment for service.

This framework implicates numerous other *sugyot* throughout *Shas* — including the case of the barrel of wine spilled to collect another's honey — which *Tosafot* addresses at length. The present Gemara only touches the surface of those broader questions about conscious intervention, selfless acts, and the limits of compensation for benefit conferred.

## Ones & Pshiah

### Slipping in Urine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי נָפַל? רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר: שֶׁהוּחְלְקָה בְּמֵימֵי רַגְלֶיהָ, רָבָא אָמַר: שֶׁדְּחָפַתָּה חֲבֶרְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **How did** the animal **fall?** In which case does this *halakha* apply? **Rav Kahana says: It slipped on its** own **urine. Rava says: Another** animal belonging to the same owner **pushed it,** causing it to fall there.

Having established the framework of *mah she'nehenis* and *ones*, the Gemara turns to the definitional question: what exactly constitutes *ones* when an animal falls into a field? *Rav Kahana* says: *she'huchleka b'meymei ragleha* — it slipped on its own urine. That is clearly *ones*.

### Single File Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר שֶׁדְּחָפַתָּה חֲבֶרְתָּהּ – כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן שֶׁהוּחְלְקָה בְּמֵימֵי רַגְלֶיהָ. וּמַאן דְּאָמַר שֶׁהוּחְלְקָה בְּמֵימֵי רַגְלֶיהָ – אֲבָל דְּחָפַתָּה חֲבֶרְתָּהּ, פָּשְׁעָה, וּמְשַׁלֶּמֶת מַה שֶּׁהִזִּיקָה; דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִיבְּעִי לָךְ עַבּוֹרֵי חֲדָא חֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: Rava, **the one who says that** the mishna is referring to a case where **another** animal **pushed it,** in which case the owner pays only for the benefit that the animal derived and is exempt from paying for the damage caused, holds that **all the more so** this *halakha* would apply in a case **where it slipped on its urine,** which is beyond the owner's control. **But** Rav Kahana, **the one who says** that the mishna is referring to a case **where** the animal **slipped on its urine,** holds that if, **however, another** animal **pushed it,** this indicates that the owner **was negligent** and should have prevented this happening. Therefore, in such a case, the owner **pays** for **what** his animal **damaged, since** the owner of the garden **can say to** the owner of the animal: **You should have led** your animals **across one by one,** so that they would not be able to push each other.

*Rava* says the *ones* case is where *dchaftah chavertah* — a fellow animal from the same herd pushed it down. According to the one who holds that being pushed by a fellow animal constitutes *ones*, being slipped in its own urine is certainly *ones* as well — it is the easier case. But according to the one who limits *ones* to slipping in urine, being pushed by a fellow animal may actually constitute *pshiah* on the owner's part. Why? *D'amar lei* — because the owner of the field can say: *iba'ei lach le'avorei chada chada* — you should have led them through single file. If you are walking your animals past a slope or ledge from which they could fall into someone's property, basic responsible ownership requires that they pass one by one.

### Cliff Case Limitation

*Tosafot* introduces an important limitation. The cases of *ones* described by Rav Kahana and Rava — slipping in urine or being pushed by a fellow animal — apply only where the animal could not have descended into the field on its own. If the path adjacent to the field is a cliff that the animal could not have walked down independently, and it fell in, that is *ones*. But if the animal was capable of walking down into the field on its own, then even a fall constitutes *pshiah*, because the relevant negligence is *techilato b'pshiah* — the owner was negligent from the outset in walking the animal where it could freely enter another's domain.

This raises a pointed question: must an animal owner refrain from walking his flock near any field into which they could stray? The *Riva*, cited by the *Gra* in a marginal note, pushes back strongly. He rules that walking one's sheep past another's yard does not constitute *pshiah*. As he puts it: *v'chi ochezna biznava v'yeilech?* — is the owner supposed to hold the sheep by the tail wherever he walks? The requirement cannot be so sweeping. What the Gemara identifies as negligent — not going single file, not attending to an animal's known propensity to stray — is distinguishable from the ordinary act of shepherding a flock through the landscape.

### Row-to-Row Movement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּאוֹתָהּ עֲרוּגָה, אֲבָל מֵעֲרוּגָה לַעֲרוּגָה – מְשַׁלֶּמֶת מַה שֶּׁהִזִּיקָה. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ מֵעֲרוּגָה לַעֲרוּגָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ – עַד שֶׁתֵּצֵא וְתַחֲזוֹר לְדַעַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana says: They taught only** that the owner pays for the benefit that it derives **in the same garden bed** into which it fell, **but** if it went **from** one **garden bed to** another **garden bed** and ate from that one, the owner **pays** for **what it damaged. And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Even** if the animal goes **from** one **garden bed to** another **garden bed** and eats, **and even** if the animal continues going from one bed to another and eating for **the entire day,** the owner pays only for the benefit that the animal derived and not for what it damaged, **unless it leaves** the garden entirely **and returns with** the owner's **knowledge.**

Returning to the Gemara proper, *Rav Kahana* addresses the duration of the *ones* status once the animal has fallen in. *Lo shanu ela b'osah arugah* — the *ones* classification applies only while the animal remains within the same row or garden patch into which it fell. Once the animal moves from one *arugah* to another — *aval me'arugah l'arugah meshalemet mah shehizikah* — the owner's *ones* is exhausted and he is now a *poshea*. The logic is that sufficient time and opportunity to intervene have passed; at the point of moving between patches, we define the owner as negligent for having failed to act.

### No Time Limit

*Rav Yochanan* disagrees. Even moving from one patch to another does not automatically convert the *ones* to *pshiah*. *V'afilu kol hayom kulo* — even if the animal was eating the entire day, the owner remains exempt *ad sheteitzei v'tachzor l'daas* — until it leaves and returns with knowledge. The Gemara declines to set any objective time threshold. Rather, the question is entirely case-by-case: the owner is not liable until he is aware of what is happening and has a genuine opportunity to intervene. Six hours of eating, if the owner was distant and ignorant, still falls within the *ones* category.

### Heightened Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא תֵּימָא עַד שֶׁתֵּצֵא לְדַעַת וְתַחֲזוֹר לְדַעַת, אֶלָּא כֵּיוָן שֶׁיָּצְתָה לְדַעַת – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁחָזְרָה שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת. מַאי טַעְמָא? דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: כֵּיוָן דְּיָלְפָא, כֹּל אֵימַת דְּמִשְׁתַּמְטָא – לְהָתָם רָהֲטָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said,** explaining Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement: **Do not say** this means: **Unless** the animal **leaves with** the owner's **knowledge and returns with** his **knowledge; rather, once it leaves** the garden **with** the owner's **knowledge, even if it returned without** his **knowledge,** the owner is liable to pay for what it damaged. **What is the reason** for this? It is **that** the owner of the garden can **say to** the owner of the animal: **Since** the animal **has** now **learned** that the garden is there with food to eat, **every time it strays it will run to there.**

*Rav Papa* adds a further dimension. Even within *Rav Yochanan*'s framework, once the owner knew that his animal had been in the field and had left, he is liable even if it subsequently re-enters without his knowledge — *af al pi shechazrah shelo l'daas*. The reasoning is *keivan d'yalfa* — since the animal has now demonstrated a habit of going to eat in that field, the owner bears a heightened level of *shmira* going forward. Every time the animal breaks away, it is likely to run back to that field. Knowing this, the owner is negligent if he does not take precautions — up to and including keeping the animal restrained — to prevent recurrence.

### Rashi's Interpretation

*Rashi* interprets the term *l'daas* throughout this discussion not as literal knowledge alone, but as a proxy for negligence: one can be aware and still be an *ones* (if one is distant and cannot intervene), and one can be ignorant and still be a *poshea* (if the ignorance itself reflects negligence). The operative question is always whether the owner was acting responsibly given what he knew and what he could have done.

### Birthing in Field

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָרְדָה כְּדַרְכָּהּ וְהִזִּיקָה – מְשַׁלֶּמֶת מַה שֶּׁהִזִּיקָה. בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: יָרְדָה כְּדַרְכָּהּ וְהִזִּיקָה בְּמֵי לֵידָה, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: If the animal **descended** into the garden **in its usual manner and caused damage** there, the owner **pays** for **what it damaged. Rabbi Yirmeya asks:** If the animal **descended in its usual manner and** gave birth to a calf there and **damaged** the produce **with amniotic fluid, what is** the *halakha*? Does the owner of the animal have to pay for this damage?

*Rabbi Yirmiyah* poses a related question. Suppose the animal descends normally — without *ones* — into another's field, and rather than eating, it gives birth there, and the birthing fluids ruin the owner's produce. This would presumably be *regel*. Is the owner liable?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר תְּחִלָּתוֹ בִּפְשִׁיעָה וְסוֹפוֹ בְּאוֹנֶס חַיָּיב, לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ; כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ – אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר תְּחִלָּתוֹ בִּפְשִׁיעָה וְסוֹפוֹ בְּאוֹנֶס פָּטוּר; מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies Rabbi Yirmeya's question: **Do not raise the dilemma in accordance with** the opinion of **the one who says** that one is **liable** in a case of damage that is **initially through negligence and ultimately by accident,** since in this case the owner was negligent in allowing the animal to enter another person's courtyard, although the actual causing of the damage was ultimately by accident. **When should you raise this dilemma?** Raise it **in accordance with** the opinion **of the one who says** that one is **exempt** in a case that is **initially through negligence and ultimately by accident. What** is the *halakha* in this case?

The question is framed in terms of the dispute about *techilato b'pshiah v'sofo b'ones*: where an act begins with negligence but ends in something unexpected. According to the view that *techilato b'pshiah v'sofo b'ones* renders one *chayav*, the case presents no difficulty — the owner was negligent in allowing the animal into the field, and liability extends to all damage that resulted. The question arises according to the view that such a combination normally exempts. Does the fact that the animal was about to give birth count as part of the same act of negligence, such that the owner should have anticipated and prevented it?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי אָמְרִינַן: כֵּיוָן דִּתְחִלָּתוֹ בִּפְשִׁיעָה וְסוֹפוֹ בְּאוֹנֶס – פָּטוּר; אוֹ דִלְמָא, הָכָא כּוּלַּהּ בִּפְשִׁיעָה הוּא – דְּכֵיוָן דְּקָא חָזֵי דִּקְרִיבָה לַהּ לְמֵילַד, אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְנָטוֹרַהּ וּלְאִסְתַּמּוֹרֵי בְּגַוַּהּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The two sides of the question are as follows: **Do we say** that, **since** this case is **initially through negligence and ultimately by accident,** he is **exempt** from liability? **Or perhaps here,** in this case, **it is entirely** due to his **negligence; since he saw that** the animal **was close to giving birth, he should have safeguarded it** adequately **and taken care of it,** and he bears responsibility for failing to do so. The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The Gemara answers: *keivan d'chazey d'karva l'meylad* — since the owner knew the animal was close to giving birth, *ibaei leh l'naturei* — he was obligated to guard it against going into the field precisely for this reason. Birthing is a foreseeable consequence of the negligence; it is not an independent *ones* but part of the same nexus of negligence. The Gemara leaves this as *teiku* — unresolved.

## Shishim Standard

### Ubiyeir B'Sdei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד מְשַׁלֶּמֶת מַה שֶּׁהִזִּיקָה וְכוּ׳. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי?
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **How does** the court appraise the value of the damage when the owner **pays** for **what it damaged?** The court appraises a large piece of land with an area required for sowing one se'a of seed [*beit se'a*] in that field, including the garden bed in which the damage took place, it appraises how much it was worth before the animal damaged it and how much is it worth now, and the owner must pay the difference. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived?

The Gemara now turns to how one calculates *mah shehizikah* — the payment for damage. The established principle is that one does not simply pay the market value of the fruit consumed. Rather, one assesses the depreciation of the field: how much was the field worth before, and how much is it worth now? The source for this is the *pasuk*: *ubiyeir bisdei acheir* — it consumed in another's field.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וּבִעֵר בִּשְׂדֵה אַחֵר״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁשָּׁמִין עַל גַּב שָׂדֶה אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mattana says: As the verse states: "And it feed in another field [*uvi'er bisde aḥer*]"** (Exodus 22:4). This **teaches that** the court **appraises** the damage **relative to another field,** i.e., relative to the damaged field as a whole and not an appraisal of only the specific garden bed that was damaged.

*Rav Masna* derives from this verse that assessment is based on the field rather than the fruit: *melamed sheshamin al gav sadeh acheir* — we assess against the backdrop of another field.

### Acheir Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי ״וּבִעֵר בִּשְׂדֵה אַחֵר״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְאַפּוֹקֵי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But **this** phrase: **"*Uvi'er bisde aḥer*,"** can be understood as meaning: "And it feed in another's field," and accordingly, **is necessary** to teach that the owner is not liable unless it was a field with an owner, **to exclude** damage caused by an animal in **the public domain,** for which the owner is not liable.

The Gemara challenges this: surely the phrase *ubiyeir bisdei acheir* is needed to establish the basic *halacha* that liability for *shen* applies only in a private domain — *la'apukei reshus harabim* — and not in the public domain. The word *acheir* (another's) is doing that work. How can it simultaneously teach the assessment standard?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״וּבִעֵר בִּשְׂדֵה חֲבֵירוֹ״, אִי נָמֵי ״שְׂדֵה אַחֵר״! מַאי ״בִּשְׂדֵה אַחֵר״? שֶׁשָּׁמִין עַל גַּב שָׂדֶה אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If so,** if this was the sole intention of the verse, **let the Merciful One write** in the Torah: **And it feed in a field belonging to another [*uvi'er bisde ḥaveiro*], or alternatively,** let it write: And it consume **another field [*sedeh aḥer*]." What** is conveyed by the particular expression: **"In another field [*bisde aḥer*]"?** It is to teach that the court **appraises** the damage **relative to another field.**

The Gemara responds: if the verse meant only to exclude the public domain, it could have said *bisdei chaveiro* — in a friend's field. The specific word *acheir* — another, implying a standardized model — signals something additional: *sheshamin al gav sadeh acheir*, we assess based on a standardized other field. Furthermore, there are two verses that address this: the verse describing the act of damage (*ubiyeir*) and the verse describing payment (*meitav sadeihu umeitav karmo yeshalem*). From their combination, the Gemara derives *tarti* — two things: first, liability is limited to damage in a private domain; second, the assessment is conducted based on the framework of another field.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא כּוּלֵּיהּ לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא; לְאַפּוֹקֵי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** **But** why not **say** that this verse **comes entirely for this** purpose, i.e., to teach that the court appraises the damage relative to another field? And in that case, **from where do we** derive the **exclusion** of liability for damage by Eating in **the public domain?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, לִכְתְּבֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא גַּבֵּי תַשְׁלוּמִין – ״מֵיטַב שָׂדֵהוּ וּמֵיטַב כַּרְמוֹ יְשַׁלֵּם בִּשְׂדֵה אַחֵר״! לְמָה לִי דְּכַתְבֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא גַּבֵּי ״וּבִעֵר״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If** it is **so** that the verse was referring solely to the method of appraising the damage, **the Merciful One should have written this** in the Torah **in the context of payment,** as follows: **His best-quality field and the best quality of his vineyard he shall pay in another field** (see Exodus 22:4), thereby adding the term: In another field, and, by extension, the directive concerning how the damage is appraised, to the verse discussing payment. **Why do I** need **the Merciful One to write it in** the context of the act of damaging, in the verse: **"And it feed** in another field"? **Conclude two** conclusions **from it:** The verse is referring to both the place where the damage occurred and the method by which the damage is appraised.

The conceptual point underlying this discussion is one that *Rav Lichtenstein* once developed around the letter *beit*: the question is whether *bisdei acheir* means the damage occurred *in* another's field (a locative reading), or whether it means the animal *consumed another's field* (an objective reading, as in *ubiyeir es sadeh acheir*). If the field itself is the object of the damage, then the natural unit of assessment is the field — not the individual fruits consumed. This reading appears consistent with the parallel verse: *meitav sadeihu umeitav karmo yeshalem* — the *pasuk* speaks of paying for the *field* and the *vineyard*, not for the *fruit*.

### Seah B'Shishim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי שָׁיְימִינַן? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: סְאָה בְּשִׁשִּׁים סְאִין. רַבִּי יַנַּאי אָמַר: תַּרְקַב בְּשִׁשִּׁים תַּרְקַבִּים. חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר: קֶלַח בְּשִׁשִּׁים קְלָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **How do we,** the court, **appraise** the value of the damage? **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina says:** The court appraises the value of an area required for sowing one ***se'a*** of seed [*beit se'a*] **relative to** an area required for sowing **sixty *se'a*** of seed, and according to this calculation determines the value of the damage. **Rabbi Yannai says:** The court appraises each ***tarkav*,** equivalent to half a *beit se'a*, **relative to** an area of **sixty *tarkav*. Ḥizkiyya says:** The court appraises the value of each **stalk** eaten **relative to sixty stalks.**

*Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina* gives the operative standard: *seah b'shishim se'in* — for every *seah* consumed, one assesses it within a field of sixty *se'ah*. This is the *shishim* standard, analogous to the principle of *bittul* applied elsewhere in *halacha*.

The practical import is this: the same *seah* of grain consumed will produce a very different damage calculation depending on the size of the field it was part of. If someone has a two-*seah* field and half of it was consumed, the depreciation is enormous. If someone has a thousand-*seah* field and one *seah* was consumed, the depreciation is trivial. Rather than allowing the size of each individual farmer's field to determine the award — which would be both unpredictable and potentially unjust — the *shishim* standard creates a uniform benchmark: assess the damage as if it occurred within a sixty-*seah* field.

This is the *chiddush* of *sadeh acheir* — not merely that the field is the unit of assessment rather than the fruit, but that a standardized, hypothetical field size is used for every case.

### Per-Acre Pricing

*Rabbi Yannai* states: *tirkav b'shishim tirkavim* — a *tirkav* (half a *seah*) is assessed among sixty *tirkavim*, which corresponds to a thirty-*seah* field. Superficially, this seems identical to Rabbi Yose's position. But *Rashi* explains the difference: the per-acre cost of a field varies with its size. A smaller field commands a higher per-acre price because small fields are scarcer and more specialized. By assessing a *tirkav* within sixty *tirkavim* — a smaller field than sixty *se'ah* — Rabbi Yannai applies a higher per-acre valuation, which produces a larger award for the *nizak*. This represents a more stringent position for the damager.

### Alternative Standards

*Chizkiyah* departs from the field-based model entirely: *kelach b'shishim kelachim* — one stalk assessed among sixty stalks. Rather than asking how much the field depreciated, Chizkiyah assesses the damage to the fruit directly: what is the value of sixty stalks compared to fifty-nine stalks? This is a radical departure, reintroducing the fruit itself as the unit of measurement. Chizkiyah still uses the *shishim* multiplier, but he applies it to the crop rather than to the field.

### Kav Prohibitions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין שָׁמִין קַב – מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּשְׁבִּיחוֹ, וְלֹא בֵּית כּוֹר – מִפְּנֵי שֶׁפּוֹגְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** When appraising the damage, the court **does not appraise** it based on an area of a *beit **kav*****, because** doing so **enhances his** position, **and** they also do **not** appraise it relative to an area of **a *beit kor*,** equivalent to the area in which one can plant thirty *se'a* of seed, **because this weakens his** position.

A *baraita* is cited: *ein shamin kav mipnei shemashbichah v'lo beit kor mipnei shepogmah* — one does not assess by a *kav* because that is too favorable to the *nizak*, and not by a *beit kor* because that is too harmful to the *nizak*. The Gemara is initially puzzled by the wording, noting the asymmetry between *kav* (a unit of measure) and *beit kor* (a unit of field size). After reanalysis, the *baraita* is reread as: one does not assess a *kav* of damage on its own (*bifnei atzmo*) — because assessing the raw value of a *kav* of grain inflates the award unfairly — and one does not assess a *kav* within a *beit kor* — because the per-acre depreciation of such a large field would be negligibly small, short-changing the *nizak*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֵין שָׁמִין קַב בְּשִׁשִּׁים קַבִּים – מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּשְׁבִּיחַ מַזִּיק, וְלֹא כּוֹר בְּשִׁשִּׁים כּוֹרִין – מִפְּנֵי שֶׁפּוֹגֵם מַזִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** this *baraita* **saying? Rav Pappa said: This** is what the *baraita* is **saying:** The court **does not appraise** the value of one ***kav* relative to** an area of **sixty *kav*,** which, being too large for an individual but too small for a trader, is always sold in the market at a lower price, **because that** would **enhance** the position of **the one liable for damage.** Conversely, the court does **not** appraise the value of **a *kor* relative to** an area of **sixty *kor*,** an area so large that it is purchased only by a person with a specific need and therefore for a high price, **because that** would **weaken** the position of **the one liable for damage.**

### Fair Middle Ground

*Rav Papa* synthesizes the *baraita* with the prior discussion: the answer is *b'shishim* — the sixty-fold standard is precisely the middle path that avoids both extremes. Assessing the damage in terms of the fruit itself overpays the *nizak*; assessing it within a field too large makes it vanishingly small. A standardized field of sixty times the damaged area represents a calibrated and fair benchmark.

## Adam Hamazik

### Palm Tree Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּקַץ קַשְׁבָּא מֵחַבְרֵיהּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְדִידִי חֲזֵי לִי, וּתְלָתָא תָּאלָתָא בְּקִינָּא הֲווֹ קָיְימִי, וַהֲווֹ שָׁווּ מְאָה זוּזֵי. זִיל הַב לֵיהּ תְּלָתִין וּתְלָתָא וְתִילְתָּא. אָמַר: גַּבֵּי רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא דְּדָאֵין דִּינָא דְּפָרְסָאָה, לְמָה לִי? אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּשִׁשִּׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who cut down a date palm [*kashba*]** belonging **to another.** The latter **came** with the perpetrator for arbitration **before the Exilarch.** The Exilarch **said to** the perpetrator: **I personally saw** that place where the date palm was planted, and it actually contained **three date palms [*talata*] standing** together **in a cluster,** growing out of a single root, **and they were worth** altogether **one hundred dinars.** Consequently, since you, the perpetrator, cut down one of the three, **go** and **give him thirty-three and one-third** dinars, one third of the total value. The perpetrator rejected this ruling and **said: Why do I** need to be judged by **the Exilarch, who rules according to Persian law? He came before Rav Naḥman** for judgment in the same case, who **said to him:** The court appraises the damage **in** relation to an area **sixty** times greater than the damage caused. This amount is much less than thirty-three and one-third dinars.

A practical case is now presented. A person cut down a palm tree belonging to his neighbor. The matter came before the *Reish Galuta*, who told the parties: I personally saw that plot — there were three palm trees growing from a single base (*kina*), the entire cluster was worth 100 *zuz*, you cut down one of the three, so pay thirty-three and a third *zuz*. The defendant protested: this is Persian law, not *halacha*. He brought the case to Rav Nachman.

### Rav Nachman Position

*Rav Nachman* ruled: assess it *b'shishim* — apply the *shishim* standard. Rather than simply dividing the value of the palm cluster by three, one should determine how much the loss of one palm tree depreciates the value of a standardized larger grove of palm trees assessed at sixty times the unit.

### Rava Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִם אָמְרוּ בְּנִזְקֵי מָמוֹנוֹ, יֹאמְרוּ בְּנִזְקֵי גוּפוֹ?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Rav Naḥman: **If** the Sages **said** that the court appraises **damage** caused **by one's property,** such as his animal, relative to an area sixty times greater, **would** they also **say** that the court appraises damage relative to an area sixty times greater even **for** direct **damage** caused by **one's body?**

*Rava* challenged Rav Nachman: *im amru b'nizkei mamon yomru b'nizkei gufo?* — just because this principle applies to damage caused by an animal (*shen*) does not mean it applies when a person personally causes damage. The *shishim* standard was derived from the verse *ubiyeir bisdei acheir*, which speaks specifically to animal damage. Perhaps it is a special *halacha* for *shen*, and when *adam hamazik* — a person acting intentionally or negligently — destroys property, we assess the damage differently, without the *shishim* overlay.

### Vineyard Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרָבָא: בְּנִזְקֵי גוּפוֹ מַאי דַּעְתָּיךְ – דְּתַנְיָא: הַמַּבְכִּיר כַּרְמוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ סְמָדַר – רוֹאִין אוֹתוֹ כַּמָּה הָיָה יָפֶה קוֹדֶם לָכֵן, וְכַמָּה הוּא יָפֶה לְאַחַר מִכָּאן; וְאִילּוּ בְּשִׁשִּׁים לָא קָתָנֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rava: With regard to damage** caused by **one's body, what is your opinion?** Are you basing your opinion on the following, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If one **destroys the vineyard of another** while the grapes are **budding [*semadar*],** the court **views how much** the vineyard **was worth before** he destroyed it, **and how much it is worth afterward.** Abaye states the inference: **Whereas,** the method of appraising one part in **sixty is not taught.** Is the basis of your ruling the fact that in this *baraita* that discusses damage caused directly by a person, the method of appraising one part in sixty is not mentioned?

*Abaye* responds to Rava's challenge: what do you suppose the law would be by personal damage? A *baraita* states that if someone destroys another's vineyard when the grapes were *smadar* (unripe), one assesses *kamah hayta yafa kodem lachen v'kamah hi yafa l'achar mikan* — how much was the vineyard worth before and how much is it worth after. That language describes a field-depreciation model, not assessment of the fruit directly. And crucially, *v'ilu b'shishim lo katani* — the *baraita* does not explicitly say *shishim*. Does that mean *shishim* does not apply here?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַטּוּ גַּבֵּי בְהֶמְתּוֹ נָמֵי מִי לָא תַּנְיָא כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא?! דְּתַנְיָא: קָטְמָה נְטִיעָה – רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר, גּוֹזְרֵי גְזֵירוֹת שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם אוֹמְרִים: נְטִיעָה בַּת שְׁנָתָהּ – שְׁתֵּי כֶּסֶף, בַּת שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים – אַרְבָּעָה כֶּסֶף. אָכְלָה חֲזִיז – רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: נִידּוֹן בַּמְשׁוּיָּיר שֶׁבּוֹ, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: רוֹאִין אוֹתָהּ כַּמָּה הָיְתָה יָפָה, וְכַמָּה הִיא יָפָה.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye continued: **Is that to say** that **with regard to** damage caused by **an animal it is not taught** in a mishna or *baraita* without mentioning the method of appraising one part in sixty **like this case?** But this is not so, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If an animal **broke down a sapling** that had not yet borne fruit, **Rabbi Yosei says: Those who issue decrees in Jerusalem say** that the damages are determined based on a fixed formula: If the **sapling** was **in its first year,** the owner of the animal pays **two** pieces of **silver;** if the sapling was **two years old,** he pays **four** pieces of **silver.** If the animal **ate unripe blades of grain** used for pasture, **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says: It is judged according to what remains of it,** i.e., the court waits until the rest of the field ripens and then appraises the value of what was previously eaten. **And the Rabbis say:** The court **views how much** the field **was worth** before he destroyed it, **and how much it is worth** now.

The *baraita* presents a spectrum of views on how to assess damage to unripe fruit. *Rabbi Yossi HaGlili* holds that one looks at *mishuyar sheba* — the value at harvest: what would the crop have been worth when brought to market? *Rashi* explains this as assessing future value — the full ripened worth of the produce — which may seem unfair but reflects the loss of a realized agricultural investment. The *chachamim* disagree: *ro'in osah kamah hayta yafa v'kamah hi yafa* — one looks at the actual current value of the vineyard before and after the damage. Assessment is based on present market value of the field, not projected fruit value.

A parallel dispute concerns *smadar* — unripe grapes. *Rabbi Yehoshua* says one looks at the grapes as if they were already ripe and ready for harvest. The *chachamim* again apply the field-depreciation model. These positions span a range: raw fruit value, projected ripe value, current field value — each representing a distinct theory of what was actually damaged and how that damage should be measured.

### Maturity Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָכְלָה סְמָדַר – רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: רוֹאִין אוֹתָן כְּאִילּוּ הֵן עֲנָבִים עוֹמְדוֹת לִיבָּצֵר, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: רוֹאִין כַּמָּה הָיְתָה יָפָה וְכַמָּה הִיא יָפָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בִּזְמַן שֶׁאָכְלָה לוּלְבֵי גְפָנִים וְיִחוּרֵי תְאֵנִים, אֲבָל אָכְלָה פַּגִּים אוֹ בוֹסֶר – רוֹאִין אוֹתָן כְּאִילּוּ עֲנָבִים עוֹמְדוֹת לִיבָּצֵר.
>
> **Translation:** If the animal **ate** the grapes while they were in the **budding** stage, **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** The court **views** the grapes that were damaged **as if they were grapes about to be picked,** and appraise the damage based on this. **And the Rabbis say:** The court **views how much** the vineyard **was worth** before the animal ate the grapes, **and how much it is worth** now. **Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Shimon: In what** case **is this statement,** that the court appraises the vineyard or group of trees that were damaged, **said?** It is **when** the animal **ate young branches [*lulevei gefanim*] of vines or shoots of fig** trees, **but** where **it ate unripe figs or unripe grapes,** the court **views them** and appraises them **as if** they were **grapes ready to be picked.**

*Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda* in the name of *Rabbi Shimon ben Nedra* introduces a maturity-based distinction. If the animal ate *lulavei gefanim v'chorei te'einim* — the earliest stage, before blossoming — then the *chachamim*'s field-depreciation standard applies. But if it ate *pagim o boseir* — fruit that has already begun to develop, even if still unripe — then *ro'in osan k'ilu hen anavim omados l'hibatzeir*: one assesses as if the grapes were fully ripe and ready for market. Once development has begun, even partial maturity is treated as full ripeness for purposes of assessment.

### Shishim Applies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: רוֹאִין אוֹתָן כַּמָּה הָיְתָה יָפָה, וְכַמָּה הִיא יָפָה – וְלָא קָתָנֵי בְּשִׁשִּׁים!
>
> **Translation:** Abaye continues: **In any event, it teaches** that **the Rabbis say:** The court **views how much** the vineyard **was worth** before the animal ate the produce **and how much it is worth** now, **and** it **does not teach** that the court appraises the damage relative to an area **sixty** times greater.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי אִית לָךְ לְמֵימַר – בְּשִׁשִּׁים; הָכָא נָמֵי בְּשִׁשִּׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, what have you to say?** You must say that the wording employed by the *baraita* is to be understood to mean that the court appraises the damage **relative to** an area **sixty** times greater, so **here too,** in the *baraita* dealing with one who himself causes damage, the wording is to be understood to mean that the damage is valued **relative to** an area **sixty** times greater.

*Abaye* now draws the inference. The *baraita* about animal damage — which describes the field-depreciation model without explicitly mentioning *shishim* — is universally understood to imply *shishim* as the operative standard. We read *shishim* into it even without explicit textual support because we know that is how field depreciation is calculated. By the same token, when the *baraita* about personal damage (*adam hamazik*) describes a field-depreciation model, *shishim* should equally be implied there as well. *Hacha nami b'shishim* — here too, by personal damage, the *shishim* standard applies.

The debate between Rav Nachman and Rava — and the analysis contributed by Abaye — thus produces a live question: is the *shishim* standard a *halacha* specific to *shen* and animal damage, derived from the particular verse of *ubiyeir bisdei acheir*, or is it a general principle of how field depreciation is assessed whenever a field is the unit of damage, applicable equally to *adam hamazik*? The Gemara will continue to develop this question on the following *daf*.
