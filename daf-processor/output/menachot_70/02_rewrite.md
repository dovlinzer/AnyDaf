# Menachot 70 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Stalk Uprooted & Replant…

### R. Shimon ben Pazi's Case

The sugya opens with a question posed by Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi, found seven lines above where the lines widen on the page — twelve lines from the bottom. The case runs as follows: *shibbolet she-hevi'ah shlish kodem ha-Omer ve-akrah u-sh'talah le-achar ha-Omer* — a stalk of grain that had reached one-third ripeness before the *Omer* was brought, was then uprooted, and was replanted after the *Omer*.

Why does one-third ripeness matter? Because reaching *shlish* — a third of full development — is the threshold at which grain acquires the status of *peri*, actual fruit or produce, and becomes subject to the obligations of *terumot* and *ma'asrot*. Before that point the grain is merely fodder, lacking full halachic grain-status. Once it crosses that threshold, it is considered grain in the halachic sense.

The scenario, then, is this: a stalk reached one-third ripeness while still in the ground. At that stage it has the full status of grain — and therefore the *Omer* offering, when it arrives, renders it permitted. The grain is then uprooted and, after the *Omer* has come and gone, replanted as a stalk. Now it continues to grow. Had this stalk simply remained in the ground throughout, everything about it would be permitted by virtue of the *Omer*. And had someone planted entirely new seeds after the *Omer*, what grew from those seeds would be *chadash* — new grain forbidden until the following year's *Omer*. This case sits precisely between those two poles. The stalk itself is not new seed; it is the very thing that had been in the ground before the *Omer*. Yet it was removed and replanted. To what degree is the subsequent growth a continuation of what was already permitted, and to what degree is it a new planting — making the growth *chadash*?

The *Gemara* assumes as its starting point that the new growth is indeed considered a new planting, so that what grows afresh is, at least in principle, *chadash*. The question is then framed precisely: *batar ikar azlinan* — do we go by the *ikar*, the foundation? The base stalk was already rendered permissible by the *Omer*; perhaps the new growth is subordinate to that foundation and inherits its permitted status. Or do we instead go *batar tosefet* — after the addition that grows subsequently — treating the new growth as possessing its own distinct status, such that the stalk is a mixture of the permitted original grain and forbidden new growth, requiring one to wait until *ha-Omer ha-ba*, the following year's *Omer*?

### Orlah & Grafting

The *Gemara* attempts to resolve the question by invoking a ruling cited by Rabbi Abbahu in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *yaldah she-sivchah bi-zekeinah* — a young tree intertwined with an old one. This refers to the laws of *orlah*: fruit growing from a tree in its first three years is *orlah* and forbidden, while an old, established tree bears permitted fruit. The *Gemara* here understands "intertwined" to mean grafted — a branch from a young tree (whose fruit would be *orlah*) has been grafted onto an old tree, and some fruit was already growing on the branch at the time of grafting. That initial fruit is *orlah*. After grafting, the branch continues to grow while connected to the old tree, so the additional growth derives from the old, permitted tree. Yet the ruling is: *afilu hosif be-matayim assur* — even if the new growth is two hundred times the original forbidden amount, the entire thing is forbidden.

This is striking. Normally, *orlah* that falls into a mixture of non-*orlah* is *batel* — nullified — in a ratio of one to two hundred. Yet here, even when the permitted growth dwarfs the original *orlah* by a factor of two hundred, it is not nullified. The reason, apparently, is that since the *ikar* — the foundation upon which all subsequent growth rests — was forbidden, the later growth is treated as an extension of that forbidden base rather than as something independent that could nullify it.

Tosafot note that this understanding of the case derives from the Yerushalmi, which envisages the branches becoming intertwined before the young tree takes root, such that it is regarded as a mere extension of the old tree. Our *Gemara*, however, understands the case as a graft, even though the standard term for grafting (*hirkiv*) is not used here.

The inference the *Gemara* draws is the following: if the *ikar* principle is applied stringently in the *orlah* case — so that a forbidden foundation contaminates even a two-hundredfold permitted addition — perhaps the same principle should apply leniently in the case of the replanted stalk. There, the foundation is permitted grain (already sanctioned by the *Omer*), and the new growth is the forbidden addition. Perhaps the new growth should be considered subordinate to the permitted base.

### Kilayim & Onion Case

A parallel ruling, cited by Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan, reinforces the point. *Batzal she-shatalo be-kerem* — an onion that was planted in a vineyard creates a problem of *kilayim*, specifically *kilei ha-kerem*, the forbidden mixture of vegetables with a vineyard. If the vineyard was then completely removed, the onion remains but its initial growth occurred under *kilayim* conditions. Now, with the vineyard gone, the onion continues to grow. The ruling: *afilu hosif be-matayim assur* — even if the onion now grows to two hundred times its original size, the entire mass is forbidden. Normally *kilayim*, like *orlah*, is nullified at one in two hundred; yet here no nullification occurs, because the subsequent growth of *heter* built upon a foundation of *issur* takes on the status of the *issur* and cannot create *bittul*.

The *Gemara* proposes drawing the inverse inference for the replanted stalk: just as a forbidden *ikar* renders forbidden even a massive addition of *heter*, so too a permitted *ikar* should render permitted even a significant addition of *chadash*.

### Foundation Principle Lim…

The *Gemara* deflects this reasoning. *Hi gufah ka-miba'ya lei* — that very case was precisely the question Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi was asking. He wanted to know whether one could learn from the *orlah* and *kilayim* rulings. The *Gemara* then articulates two possible readings of those earlier rulings: either it is perfectly clear to the Rabbis that one always goes *batar ikar* regardless of whether that yields stringency or leniency — *lo shenah le-kula ve-lo shenah le-chumra* — and the inference to our case is straightforward; or perhaps the Rabbis actually had a doubt about the *ikar* principle and adopted it only in cases where it produced a stringency, *le-chumra*, but would not apply it to produce a leniency, *le-kula*. The question remains unresolved — *teiku*.

This is a genuinely interesting result. One might instinctively feel that the *ikar* principle and the rules of *bittul* are simply different conceptual frameworks: the question of whether something growing in the ground is defined by its origin or by its current state seems categorically distinct from the question of whether a small forbidden quantity is nullified in a larger permitted mixture. The *Gemara* is asking whether these cases are governed by a single unified principle or whether the *ikar* concept operates only directionally — only to be stringent. The answer, for now, is that we do not know.

## Ma'aser & Replanting

### Rabbah's Ma'aser Q

A related series of questions follows. Rabbah (the *girsa* here is debated) asks: what would be the analogous case in the domain of *ma'aser* rather than *chadash*? The scenario: you harvested your grain, took *ma'aser* — giving a tenth to the Levite — so that the remaining ninety percent is now exempt and permitted. Instead of replanting seeds (which would give rise to a straightforwardly new crop, fully obligated in *terumot u-ma'asrot*), you replanted the stalks themselves. More grain grew. The question operates on a premise: assume, following the unresolved question above, that we do not go leniently *batar ikar* — the new growth is not subsumed into the status of the old base and instead has its own independent status, rendering it obligated in *ma'aser*. Fine. But then the opposite extreme presents itself: if we take the new growth's status seriously as something genuinely new and independent, does the status of newness redefine even the old base? Must one now take *ma'aser* off of one hundred percent of what is harvested — including the original stalks from which *ma'aser* was already taken — or only off the fifty percent (or whatever proportion) that is truly new?

The structure of the question, then, involves three possibilities: the new growth is absorbed into the old base and requires nothing; the new growth is obligated on its own but the old base retains its exempt status; or the new growth, if it triggers the stricter ruling, redefines even the old base, making everything obligated again.

### Abaye's Distinction

Abaye responds to Rabbah's question by asking: *mai shana mi-kol chitei u-sa'arei de'alma?* — how is this different from any wheat or barley in the world? When you replant seeds and harvest a crop, you take *terumot u-ma'asrot* over the entire yield. Rabbah replies that the case is categorically different: *davar she-zaro kaleh lo miba'ya li* — when the seed disintegrates underground, giving rise to something entirely new, there is no question that everything harvested is fresh and fully obligated. The question arises only *davar she-ein zaro kaleh* — when what you replanted does not disintegrate. You replanted the stalks; when you come to harvest, the original stalks are still physically present, identifiable as the old base that had already been tithed. Does the continued growth redefine them, or not?

The *Gemara* then attempts to resolve this from a ruling of Rav Yitzchak in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *litra batzal* — a liter's worth of onions from which *terumot u-ma'asrot* had been taken was then replanted to produce more. The ruling: *mit'aser lefi kulo* — when you harvest, you take *ma'aser* from the total quantity, not from the total minus the original liter. The old base, even though it had already been tithed, is swept into the new tithing obligation once new growth occurs on top of it.

This seems to answer Rabbah's question squarely — the new growth redefines even the old base. But the *Gemara* rejects the comparison: *hatam hainu zri'atah* — there, that is the normal method by which onions are planted. Onions are grown by planting bulbs, and when one plants bulbs and harvests a larger yield, everything harvested is treated as new produce and fully obligated, regardless of whether the original bulb remains intact. This is simply how onion cultivation works, and halachically it is treated the same as planting seeds: the entire harvest is new. *Hacha lav hainu zri'atah* — here, however, replanting a grain stalk is not the standard method of growing grain. Because it is an unusual mode of planting, one cannot assume that the old base is simply redefined as new. The question of what happens to the original base in such an unusual case remains open.

### Flower Pot Analogy

Rabbi Chanina bar Minyumi poses a related question to Abaye: *atiz she-eino nakuv mahu?* — what is the law regarding a flowerpot without a hole? The *Gemara* is well-acquainted with the distinction between *atiz nakuv* — a flowerpot with a hole, which is halachically connected to the ground and subject to biblical obligations of *terumot u-ma'asrot* — and *atiz she-eino nakuv* — a flowerpot without a hole, which lacks direct physical connection to the ground and is exempt from those obligations at the biblical level, though rabbinically it retains some status of planting.

The *Gemara* first dismisses the question as trivially obvious — if there is no hole, there is no hole — but then recognizes what the real question must be: *chazar ve-nakvo ka-amart* — the case is one where it started without a hole and then someone drilled a hole in it. Some growth occurred while there was no hole, during which the plant was not biblically obligated; then a hole was made, connecting it to the ground, so that subsequent growth is fully obligated. The question mirrors the one about replanted stalks: does making the hole redefine the status of the growth that had already occurred while there was no hole?

The *Gemara* distinguishes this case from Rabbah's question on a different axis: *hacha chada zri'ah hi* — here it is one single planting throughout, and the question is merely whether a change in the pot's connectivity to the ground (the hole being made) redefines the existing growth. *Hatam shtei zri'ot ninhu* — there, in the case of the replanted stalk, there are two distinct plantings: the original planting before the *Omer* and the replanting afterward. These may be governed by different considerations.

Rashi's understanding of the distinction is that in the one-planting case, where nothing was physically uprooted and replanted, the old growth stands separate from the new and is not redefined by the subsequent change. The *Shitta Mekubetzes* reads it in the opposite direction: once the hole is made and the plant is now drawing from the ground, even the earlier growth is considered as though it were growing from the ground all along. The upshot, in either reading, is that the two cases — flowerpot and replanted stalk — may yield different halachic conclusions, and neither question is resolved by the other.

## Terumah on Growing Stalks

### R. Abahu's Terumah Q

Rabbi Abahu raises yet another variation on these themes. *Shibbolet she-marcha ba-keri u-shatalah* — a stalk of grain that had been smoothed on the pile (*mericha ba-keri*) and was then replanted. The *mericha* — smoothing of the grain heap — is the act that renders grain fully obligated in *terumot u-ma'asrot* and makes it *tevel*: forbidden to eat until the appropriate portions have been set aside. So this stalk, at the time of replanting, was already at the stage of full *chiyuv*. It was *tevel* in the fullest sense — forbidden to eat, but possessing the latent capacity to become *terumah* once designated. Now the stalk is replanted and it grows. Can it still become *terumah* while it is growing?

Normally, the only route to *terumah* is through harvested, processed, pile-ready grain. Growing grain cannot become *terumah*. But this grain has already passed through that entire process. The question is whether the act of replanting strips away the prior status — reverting the stalk to the condition of something *mechubar*, attached to the ground and therefore outside the category of things that can be *terumah* — or whether the prior status persists through the replanting, leaving open the theoretical possibility that it could be designated *terumah* even while growing.

### Abaye's Terumah Answer

Abaye's initial inclination is toward the latter option: since the grain was fully smoothed and rendered *tevel* before replanting, and since *tevel* is precisely the status from which *terumah* emerges through designation, perhaps even now — while growing — one could designate it as *terumah*. The replanting, on this view, does not sever the prior status.

### Objection to Terumah

The Rabbis immediately challenge Abaye: *im ken matzinu terumah be-mechubar le-karka* — if what you are saying is correct, then you have created the phenomenon of *terumah* on something attached to the ground. But the established principle is *lo matzinu terumah be-mechubar le-karka* — there is no *terumah* on something attached to the ground. How can you allow this replanted grain to become *terumah* while it is still growing?

### Eating Off the Stalk

Abaye clarifies his position. The statement that there is no *terumah be-mechubar* does not mean that the grain literally cannot have the status of *terumah* while growing. What it means is that there can be no *chiyuv* — no liability — for eating it while it is still attached. The laws of *terumah* impose on a non-*kohen* who eats *terumah* either *mitah bi-ydei Shamayim* if done deliberately, or repayment plus a *chomesh* if done inadvertently — but these penalties attach only to an act of *achilah*, an act of eating. Why would eating growing *terumah* not constitute such an act?

Two sub-cases: *de-ei talash ve-achal* — if you plucked the grain from the stalk and then ate it, then at the moment of eating it is no longer *mechubar*, it is *talush*, detached, so the scenario of eating it while attached never arose. *Ve-ei gachin ve-achal* — if you bent over and ate it directly from the stalk without first plucking it, then *batla da'ato etzel kol adam* — your manner of eating is so deviant from normal human behavior that it cannot be counted as a proper act of eating at all. No reasonable person eats grain by lowering their mouth to the stalk; such behavior is not recognized by halacha as *achilah*.

This is a genuinely notable point. Usually when the *Gemara* determines that something does not count as normal eating, the reason involves the character of the food itself — it is disgusting, or unfit, or was consumed in a grossly excessive quantity. Here, the food itself is perfectly fit to eat; it is only the manner of consumption that is bizarre. The *Gemara* is saying that the *mode* of eating, not just the *object* of eating, can be abnormal enough to disqualify the act from the category of *achilah*. That said, one might wonder: if a person stood on their head and ate grain, we would presumably still call it *achilah* even though that too is not normal. The distinguishing factor, as the *Gemara* will make clearer, is that when the object itself is *mechubar* — still attached — that feature of attachment is what makes the act categorically abnormal, not merely quirky.

Importantly, a search of the *Masoret ha-Shas* — that remarkable cross-reference apparatus that covers the entire *Gemara* — reveals a striking admission. On the statement that "there is no *terumah be-mechubar le-karka*," the *Masoret ha-Shas* notes: *likotzer da'ati lo yadati letzayen mekomo be-mishnah o ve-braita she-ein meforash davar zeh* — because of my limited knowledge, I cannot locate this statement in any *mishnah* or *baraita*. This is a remarkable concession from the person who cross-referenced the entire *Gemara*. What it really means is that there is no such explicit *mishnah* or *baraita*. Perhaps the *Gemara* is not saying that a *mishnah* explicitly states this principle, but rather that the phenomenon simply never occurs — *lo matzinu* in the sense of "we have never encountered it," implying it would be anomalous for Abaye to be inventing it now.

### Egg in Bird Analogy

The *Gemara* now introduces a parallel case, described as written in the notebook of *Ilfa* — *pinkusa de-Ilfa*. The case involves *beitzai nivlas of tahora* — eggs found in a kosher bird that was not slaughtered properly (*neveilah*). The halachic category of *nivlas of tahora* — a kosher species improperly slaughtered — conveys *tum'ah* specifically through the act of swallowing: one who swallows it becomes *tamei*, together with the garments being worn at that moment (*metamei begadim be-veit ha-beli'ah*). This is a consumption-triggered *tum'ah*, making it critical to define what counts as an act of eating or swallowing.

The case: an egg that was partially laid at the moment the bird died — *miktzatam bachutz u-miktzatam bifnim* — half outside the bird's body, half still inside. The ruling: the half that is inside (*mibifnim*) causes *tum'ah* through swallowing, while the half outside (*mibachutz*) does not. The inside portion is treated as part of the bird; eating it is like eating the *neveilah* itself. The outside portion has left the bird and is no longer considered meat.

The *Gemara* understands this to mean not merely where the egg was at the moment of death, but where it is at the moment of consumption: if you eat the egg while it is still inside the bird, the act of consumption counts as eating *neveilah*; if you removed the egg and then ate it, it does not.

A question naturally arises — one that students raise: how does one eat half of an egg while it is inside a bird? One answer suggested by the *Gemara*'s framing is that you ate the whole egg, but halachically we only attribute to you the eating of the half that was inside at the relevant moment. The *shiur* for *tum'ah* purposes counts only that portion. The *Gemara* then asks why eating the inside portion while it is still within the bird is considered a normal act of eating at all. Who eats an egg while it is still inside a chicken? Should that not also be classified as *batla da'ato etzel kol adam* — an act so bizarre as to be disqualified from the category of *achilah*?

### Attached Objects Not Eat…

The *Gemara* draws the distinction: when an object is *talush* — fully detached, like an egg that has come out of the bird — *avid inish de-achli hachi*, people can and do eat such things in various ways, even unconventional ones. The egg is a normal food object in a normal state, and how you choose to consume it does not redefine the act. But *mechubar la-karka* — or here, something still physically connected to and enclosed within a living body — *lav avid inish de-achli* — no person normally eats things in that manner. The attachment itself redefines the act, not merely the quirky posture of the eater. This is the conceptual key: it is the state of the *object* — being attached — that renders the act of eating categorically abnormal, whereas eccentricities in the manner of eating a detached, normal object do not undermine the act's status as *achilah*.

This distinction clarifies Abaye's earlier ruling as well. When growing *terumah* is eaten directly off the stalk, it is the *mechubar* status of the grain — not merely the unusual posture of the consumer — that prevents it from being considered a proper act of eating. Hence no *chiyuv* for *mitah* or *chomesh* arises, even if in theory the grain has the status of *terumah*.

The *Gemara* closes this sequence with a ruling of Rav Tiviumi bar Kisna in the name of Shmuel: *ha-zore'a kilayim be-atiz she-eino nakuv assur* — one who plants *kilayim* in a flowerpot without a hole transgresses a prohibition. Abaye responds: if you were telling us that such a person receives *makat mardut* — rabbinic lashes — that would be a significant ruling, since it would affirm that a flowerpot without a hole has rabbinic status equivalent to actual ground, strong enough to trigger rabbinic punishment. But if all you are telling me is that it is merely forbidden, what are you adding? We already know from an established teaching that an *atiz she-eino nakuv* has at least rabbinic status as planting: for if one takes *terumah* from produce grown in an unholed pot to cover produce grown in a holed pot, the *terumah* taken is technically valid *terumah* (because the unholed pot has rabbinic status), yet it does not cover the obligation of the holed-pot produce (because the difference in biblical status makes them incompatible), and so one must return and take *terumah* again. The fact that the first designation had any status at all proves the rabbinic standing of the unholed pot. Shmuel's statement adds nothing to that already known principle.

## Five Grains Identified

### Five Grains Mishnah

The sugya now turns to the *mishnah* that defines the five halachic grains and their shared legal properties. The *mishnah* lists: wheat (*chitim*) and barley (*se'orim*), which are familiar, and then three others — *kusmin*, *shibolet shual*, and *shifon*. These five together constitute the *chameshet ha-minim*, the five species of grain that are the foundation of a broad range of halachic categories.

The *mishnah* states: *harei elu chayavin be-challah* — these are obligated in *challah*, the dough-portion separated when making bread. Moreover, *u-mitztarfim zeh im zeh* — they combine with one another. Even if one's dough is made from a mixture of wheat flour, barley flour, and spelt flour, all the components combine toward the *shiur* — the minimum quantity — that obligates one in *challah*. You do not need a full qualifying quantity of each individual grain.

The *mishnah* then states: *ve-asurin be-chadash mi-lifnei ha-Pesach* — all five grains are forbidden as *chadash*, new grain, before the *Omer* is brought on the sixteenth of Nisan. This is precisely why the topic is relevant in Menachot: the *Omer* is an offering of barley, but its effect — and the prohibition of *chadash* — extends to all five grain species, not to barley alone. Rice, fruit, and other produce are entirely unaffected; only these five grains are subject to the *chadash* prohibition.

The *mishnah* further specifies: *u-miktzar mi-lifnei ha-Omer* — not only is it forbidden to *eat* the new grain, it is forbidden to *cut* or harvest any of these grains until after the *Omer* has been harvested. One may pick fruit, harvest rice, but one may not cut any of the five grain species until the *Omer* itself is cut.

The mechanism by which the *Omer* permits the grain is also articulated: *hishrishu kodem la-Omer, ha-Omer matiran* — if the grain has taken root by the time the *Omer* is brought, then the *Omer* renders it permitted, even if it continues to grow thereafter. *Ve-im lav, asurin ad she-yavo ha-Omer ha-ba* — if the grain had not yet taken root when the *Omer* was brought, then this year's *Omer* has no effect on it, and it must wait for the following year's *Omer*. This resolves a practical question: in a field of wheat, how does one determine whether a given planting had taken root by the time of the *Omer*? The answer lies not in physically examining the roots, but in calculating from the time of planting: if the grain was planted two weeks before the *Omer* (with a competing view of one month), it is considered to have taken root. The determination is thus chronological, not observational — which is the only practical way to make such an assessment across an entire field.

### Grain Categorization

A *baraita* is cited that categorizes the three lesser-known grains: *kusmin* is *min chitin* — a type of wheat — while *shibolet shual* and *shifon* are *min seorin* — types of barley. This taxonomic classification matters practically, because a *mishnah* in tractate Challah specifies that only grains of the same type combine toward the *shiur* of *challah*. Wheat-type grains combine with one another; barley-type grains combine with one another; but wheat-type and barley-type flours mixed together do not combine toward a single *shiur* of *challah*. Knowing whether *kusmin* belongs in the wheat column or the barley column (and it seems to straddle both) thus determines when a mixed-flour dough achieves the requisite *shiur*.

### Rashi's Identifications

The *Gemara* presents the Aramaic identifications of the three grains. *Kusmin* is identified as *gulba*; *shifon* as *dishra*; and *shibolet shual* is rendered literally — *shiboleta de-tala*, the stalk of the fox (*shual*), which is simply an Aramaic translation of the Hebrew name.

Rashi provides more interpretively useful identifications. For *kusmin*, Rashi says *aspilta* — spelt. For *shifon*, Rashi says *sigla* — the precise word is obscure, but it is standardly identified as rye. For *shibolet shual*, Rashi says *avina*, and *avina* is actually the scientific term used for oats. On the basis of these identifications by Rashi — and the Soncino translation that followed them — the standard received understanding became that the three lesser-known grains are spelt, oats, and rye. This identification has the force of *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* in the popular consciousness.

### Feliks's Challenge

Professor Yehuda Feliks, the great scholar of flora and fauna in the *Mishnah* and *Tanakh*, author of *HaChai VeHaTzomeach BaMishnah* and *HaChai VeHaTzomeach BaMikra*, raised a pointed challenge specifically to the identification of *shibolet shual* with oats. Professor Feliks argued that *shibolet shual* was in fact a particular variety of barley — a species consistent with the *Gemara*'s own classification of it as *min seorin*, a type of barley. He found it impossible that *shibolet shual* could be oats, for two reasons: first, oats lack gluten, and he understood the capacity to ferment and become *chametz* — which the *Gemara* treats as a defining property of the five grains — to be bound up with the presence of gluten; second, oats were not known in the regions where *Chazal* lived during the period of the *Mishnah* and *Gemara*.

Professor Feliks relates that his identification had practical halachic consequences. After he published his conclusions, Rav Shlomo Zalman Auerbach — the preeminent *posek* of his generation — began reciting *she-hakol* over his morning oatmeal rather than *borei minei mezonot*, on the grounds that if oats is not one of the five grain species, oatmeal does not merit the *mezonot* blessing. This practice came to the attention of Rav Yosef Shalom Elyashiv, who reportedly reproved Rav Shlomo Zalman sharply: one cannot overturn hundreds of years of Jewish practice and the consensus of the *Rishonim*, who all identified *shibolet shual* with oats, on the basis of a single academic scholar's opinion.

### Canonical Interpretation

This exchange crystallizes a fundamental question about the nature of *Torah she-ba'al peh*. As one interlocutor observes, virtually all the *Rishonim* who provided identifications did translate *shibolet shual* as *avina*. All the *Rishonim* who weighed in on the matter agreed. Whatever the grain may have been in some original historical-botanical sense — and Professor Feliks may well be right that oats were unknown in Eretz Yisrael during the *talmudic* period — the *Rishonim*'s canonized interpretation becomes *halacha*. The nature of *Torah she-ba'al peh* is not solely about what was originally meant by a source; it is about what becomes the authoritative, canonized interpretation. When all the *Rishonim* say *avina*, that designation becomes the halacha, regardless of whether it accurately reflects what *Chazal* originally had in mind.

There is also a practical dimension worth noting: precisely because oats lacks gluten, it provides a grain option for those who suffer from celiac disease. Oat *matzah* and oat products fulfilling the obligation of the five grains become available to populations who cannot tolerate wheat, barley, spelt, or rye. Whatever the botanical history, the halachic identification of oats as one of the five grains serves an important practical function.

A similar question applies to *kusmin* — spelt. Professor Feliks argued it was more likely *emmer wheat* or *farro* rather than what we call spelt today. But again, the received tradition identifies it as spelt, and that is what it remains in practice.

## Rice & Millet Excluded

### Gezeirah Shavah

Having established which grains are included among the five, the *Gemara* asks why rice and millet are excluded. Reish Lakish answers: *atya lechem lechem mi-matzah* — a *gezeirah shavah* is drawn from the word *lechem* appearing both in the obligation of *challah* (*ve-haya be-achalchem mi-lechem ha-aretz*) and in the context of *matzah* (*lechem oni*, bread of affliction). Just as the *lechem* of *matzah* refers to something that can ferment and become *chametz*, so too the *lechem* of *challah* — and by extension *chadash* — refers only to grains that can undergo genuine fermentation.

### Fermentation vs. Rot

The defining characteristic of the five grains, then, is not merely that they grow in the ground or that they can be ground into flour, but that they ferment — *baim lidei chimutz*. Rice and millet do not ferment in the sense that wheat and barley do; instead, *she-ein bahen lidei chimutz ela lidei sirchon* — rather than fermenting, they rot. Fermentation is a specific biological process distinct from simple spoilage, and the *Gemara* treats the capacity for genuine fermentation as the criterion that places a grain within the five halachic species. Rice and millet lack this capacity and are therefore excluded.

This distinction is not arbitrary: rice has no gluten, millet has no gluten, and neither will rise when moistened with water. They will spoil and smell, but they will not produce the characteristic rise and sour that defines *chametz*. Wheat, barley, spelt, oats, and rye share this capacity, which is why they alone constitute the halachic five.

### Fermentation Principle

The operative principle, then, is: *devarim ha-baim lidei chimutz, adam yotzei bahen yedei chovato ba-Pesach* — things that can ferment can be used for the *mitzvah* of *matzah* and fall within the prohibition of *chametz*. *Devarim she-ein bahen lidei chimutz, yatzu elu* — things that cannot ferment are excluded from *chametz*, *matzah*, and *lechem* in the halachic sense. Rice and millet are explicitly in this excluded category. This is the *Gemara*'s formal grounding for why the five grains are five and not seven or more.

## Grain Combinations

### Baraisa: Combine Together

A *baraisa* teaches that *ha-teru'ah ve-ha-kemachim ve-ha-batzekot mitztarfim zeh im zeh* — the whole grain kernels, the flour, and the dough, even when representing different stages of processing and different grain species among the five, all combine with one another for various halachic purposes. The *Gemara* then asks: for what *halacha* exactly? It cannot be *challah*, since *challah* is taken only from dough, not from whole grain kernels or loose flour. What then is the purpose of stating that kernels and flour and dough all combine?

### Rav Kahana: Chadash

Rav Kahana answers: *le-inyan chadash* — for the prohibition of *chadash*. If one ate a *kezayit* composed of a mixture of new grain in various states — some whole kernels, some flour, some dough — from different grain species, all of it combines toward the *kezayit* that makes one liable for transgressing the *chadash* prohibition and subject to lashes.

### Rav Yosef: Chametz

Rav Yosef answers: *le-inyan chametz be-Pesach* — for the prohibition of *chametz* on Pesach. Different grains and different states of processing combine to make a *kezayit* of *chametz* for which one is liable. Rashi notes that even whole grain can ferment: if water is added to kernels and they crack, fermentation can occur. Therefore the combination of whole grains, flour, and dough is entirely relevant to the *chametz* prohibition.

### Rava: Tumah of Food

Rav Pappa suggests the combination applies to *ma'aser sheni* — but this answer is recognized as weak, since *ma'aser sheni* applies to all produce, not specifically to grain, and there is no reason grain in different states would need a special rule about combining. Rava offers the correct answer: *le-inyan tum'at ochlin* — they combine for the purposes of *tum'at ochlin*, the *tum'ah* of food.

### Food & Beitzah

*Tum'at ochlin* requires that the object be considered food and that it reach the minimum quantity of a *kebeitzah* — an egg's volume. Foods of different types and states can combine toward this measure. What Rava's answer teaches is that whole kernels, flour, and dough — even from different grain species among the five — all combine toward the *kebeitzah* required for *tum'at ochlin* to apply. They are all food, and food combines.

The *Gemara* explains more precisely: *sikyaya de-tvu'ah ve-kamchim dumya de-batzakot* — grain and flour are analogous to dough in that they are all genuine food. Just as dough is *ochla be-einiah* — real, recognizable food in its own right — so too are whole grain and flour. And since they are food, they combine for *tum'ah* purposes.

### Barley Hulls Issue

But a complication arises. If grain and flour combine for *tum'ah*, one might think that means barley in any state — including with its hulls — combines. The *Gemara* draws a distinction: wheat, even with its hull or outer covering intact, combines — because the hull of wheat is edible, and therefore the whole thing counts as food. But barley with its hull (*she-einah klufah*) does not combine, because the hull of barley is inedible. Moreover, Rashi goes further: the presence of inedible hulls does not merely render the hull itself irrelevant — the hulls create a *chatzitzah*, a separation, between one food item and another, so that even the edible grain inside cannot be counted together with other food items toward the *kebeitzah*.

### Seeds With Hulls

The *baraita* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael, however, seems to contradict this: the verse says *al kol zera zerua asher yizarei* — regarding any seed that is sown — implying that seeds are susceptible to *tum'ah* in the state in which they are sown. And *chitah be-klipatah u-se'orah be-klipatah* — wheat with its hull and barley with its hull — that is how one takes them out to plant. So even barley with its hull should be considered food and susceptible to *tum'ah*.

### Moist vs. Dry Hulls

The *Gemara* resolves the contradiction: *ha be-lachot, ha be-yaveshot* — one case refers to moist hulls, the other to dry hulls. When the hulls are moist, they are edible; edible hulls are part of the food, and the entire grain including hull combines for *tum'ah* purposes. When the hulls are dry, they are inedible; inedible hulls do not count as food and, as Rashi explains, they prevent even the inner grain from combining with other food toward the *kebeitzah*. The *baraita* that permits barley-with-hull to combine refers to moist conditions; the ruling that barley-with-hull does not combine refers to dry, hardened hulls that no one would eat.

## Chadash All Five Grains

### Lechem Prohibition

The final point of the sugya brings together the threads concerning *chadash* and the five grains. The *mishnah* stated that all five grains are forbidden as *chadash* before the *Omer*. The *Gemara* asks: *mi-nayin mili* — what is the source for extending the *chadash* prohibition to all five grains and not only to barley, from which the *Omer* itself is brought?

Reish Lakish answers: *atya lechem lechem mi-matzah* — the same *gezeirah shavah* of *lechem lechem* from *matzah* that was used earlier to define the five grains for *challah* purposes applies here as well. The Torah states: *lo tochal alav chametz, shivat yamim tochal alav matzot lechem oni* — you shall not eat *chametz* on it, for seven days you shall eat *matzot*, bread of affliction. From this the school of Rabbi Yishmael derives: *devarim ha-baim lidei chimutz, adam yotzei bahen yedei chovato ba-Pesach* — things that can ferment into *chametz* are the things from which one can fulfill the obligation of *matzah*, and those same things are subject to the prohibition of *chadash*. Since the word *lechem* in the verse about not eating *chadash* refers to things that can become bread — and bread is defined as things that ferment — the prohibition of *chadash* extends to all five grains and none other. Rice and millet, which do not ferment, are excluded from *chadash* for the same reason they are excluded from *challah* and from *chametz* and *matzah*: they simply cannot become bread in the halachic sense of that word.
