# Bava Batra 155 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shtar & Shchiv Meira

### Shtar Concession

The daf opens in the midst of a highly complex sugya concerning the status of an ambiguous gift document and the competing claims that arise when the donor later walks around in good health. The core dispute is between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim*: when a person gives a gift and then recovers, was that gift made as a *shchiv meira* — a deathbed gift that reverts if the donor survives — or was it an ordinary gift made in full health? That question itself, however, is not where the Gemara focuses its attention. The deeper question is: what precisely is the nature of this disagreement?

To set the stage: when a *shtar* arrives without specific language identifying it as a healthy-person's gift or a deathbed gift, and the donor concedes that he wrote it but claims he was a *shchiv meira* at the time, what weight does that claim carry? One position holds that once the donor concedes to the authenticity of the document — *modeh bishtar shekatavo* — the *shtar* requires no further *kiyum* (formal validation by witnesses), and therefore the counter-claim that it was a *shchiv meira* gets no traction. The opposing position holds that until the *shtar* is actually *mekuyam*, the document remains vulnerable to challenge, and since a forgery claim would have been believed, a claim of *shchiv meira* is similarly entertained.

### Kiyum Debate

Rav Huna and Rabbi Yochanan are presented as interpreting this dispute in terms of precisely this *kiyum* question. Under their reading, Rabbi Meir holds that *modeh bishtar shekatavo ein tzarich lekaymo* — once the donor concedes the document was written, no *kiyum* is required, and the *shtar* stands on iron footing. The *chachamim*, by contrast, invoke *hamotzi mechaveiro alav hara'aya*: since the *shtar* lacks *kiyum*, a competing claim of *shchiv meira* introduces enough uncertainty that the burden of proof rests on the claimant seeking to enforce the gift.

### Minors Case

Before this reading can settle, the Gemara raises a challenge from the famous case of minors selling land. In that case, heirs came forward claiming that their brother had been a minor at the time he sold property, and they demanded that the sale be voided. The buyers held a *shtar*. Why, the Gemara asks, were the buyers assumed to hold the property? If anything, the heirs' claim that the brother was a minor should have carried weight — unless the *shtar* was so thoroughly valid that it could not be dislodged.

### Reish Lakish Challenge

Reish Lakish presses this point directly against Rabbi Yochanan. He cites a tradition reported in Rabbi Yochanan's name — *yafe iru bnei mishpacha*, that the family members made a legitimate complaint and were believed. If that is so, asks Reish Lakish, how does it square with Rabbi Yochanan's position that *modeh bishtar shekatavo ein tzarich lekaymo*? If the *shtar* is unassailable once the signer concedes its authenticity, why should a claim that the brother was a minor gain any foothold against it? The reason such claims are entertained at all must be precisely because the *shtar* lacks *kiyum* and can therefore be destabilized. But that is Reish Lakish's position, not Rabbi Yochanan's. The challenge thus exposes an apparent inconsistency within Rabbi Yochanan's own views.

### Rabbi Yochanan Denial

Rabbi Yochanan responds to Reish Lakish with a flat denial: *zo Elazar amra* — that ruling was said by my student Elazar, not by me. Rabbi Yochanan disavows any association with the position that the heirs' claim was believed, insisting that as far as he was concerned, the buyers held the property on the strength of a fully valid *shtar* and no counter-claim of minority could undermine it.

### Rav Zeira Objects

Rav Zeira finds this response insufficient. He is willing to allow that Rabbi Yochanan might distance himself from the opinion of Elazar, his student. But can Rabbi Yochanan really deny affiliation with a position held by Rabbi Yannai, his own teacher? For Rabbi Yannai says in the name of Rebbi: *modeh bishtar shekatavo tzarich lekaymo* — when one concedes a *shtar* was written, it still requires *kiyum*. This is the direct opposite of what Rabbi Yochanan claimed. Moreover, Rabbi Yochanan himself, in a recorded exchange with Rabbi Yannai, appears to have endorsed this very requirement, reading the *chachamim*'s phrase *hamotzi mechaveiro alav hara'aya* as meaning that the only valid *ra'aya* is *kiyum hashtar* — that is, the *shtar* must be formally validated, and until it is, counter-claims can be heard.

This creates a stark internal contradiction within Rabbi Yochanan's recorded positions. Yesterday — in the preceding sugya — he was presented as holding *ein tzarich lekaymo*, the *shtar* needs no further validation. Here, in his exchange with Rabbi Yannai, he appears to hold *tzarich lekaymo*. How can these be reconciled?

### Position Reversal

The Gemara resolves this by citing a tradition in the name of Rabbeinu Yosef, who quotes Rav Yehuda quoting Shmuel: *zu divrei chachamim* — the requirement of *kiyum* is actually the position of the *chachamim*, not Rabbi Meir. We must reverse the attributions entirely. What we had previously understood as the *chachamim*'s position — that *hamotzi mechaveiro alav hara'aya* means the *shtar* can be challenged without *kiyum* — is now reassigned to Rabbi Meir. The *chachamim*, in this reversed reading, are the ones who say *ein tzarich lekaymo*, while Rabbi Meir says *tzarich lekaymo*.

This reversal allows Rabbi Yochanan to be consistently placed on one side of the debate. He rules like the *chachamim* — *ein tzarich lekaymo* — but now that position belongs to the *chachamim*, not to Rabbi Meir, so there is no tension with his apparent endorsement of *kiyum* in the other context. Every parallel source must accordingly be flipped. A *baraita* that records Rabbi Meir saying *ein ne'emanim lepaslo* — the witnesses cannot invalidate the *shtar* — and the *chachamim* saying *ne'emanim* — they are believed — seems to preserve the old attribution. The Gemara answers: *ipoch* — switch it there too.

The Gemara then raises whether the challenge Rabbi Yochanan posed to Reish Lakish from the story of the minor brother also needs to be reversed. The answer is no — that exchange need not be inverted, because it can be read consistently under the new framework.

### Story Resolution

Under the reversed framework, Rabbi Yochanan now occupies the position that *tzarich lekaymo* — the *shtar* must be validated — is the operative rule, but he rules *ein tzarich lekaymo* as the law, aligning himself with the *chachamim* in their new assignment. The case of the minor brother is then re-read accordingly. In this reading, the property was assumed to belong to the buyers — the *lekuchot* — from the outset, because their *shtar* did not require *kiyum* and therefore the burden of proof lay with the heirs. That is why the heirs sought to exhume the body: they were the ones who needed to prove their claim.

Rabbi Yochanan can then say: *bishlama ledidi d'amina ra'aya b'kiyum hashtar* — according to me, the proof required is *kiyum hashtar*, and once the *shtar* stands, the buyers' possession is presumed valid. The heirs must disprove it. The counter-claim that the brother was a minor is in any case treated as inherently weak, because there is a *chazaka* that witnesses do not sign a *shtar* unless the parties to the transaction are legally competent. The *shchiv meira* scenario, by contrast, is entirely plausible for a properly witnessed *shtar* — a person who is gravely ill can still execute a valid document. That is why a *shchiv meira* claim retains some traction while a minority claim against a *shtar* does not. Two approaches thus coexist in the Gemara for why the buyers held the property under both versions of the debate: one grounded in the iron validity of an uncontested *shtar*, and the other grounded in the inherent implausibility of the minority claim regardless of *kiyum*.

## Minor Selling Land

### Age Question

Having worked through the tangled dispute over *shtar* and *shchiv meira*, the Gemara now pivots to the independent but closely related question introduced by the story of the minor brother: *katan me'eimatai mocher b'nichsei aviv* — from what age may a minor sell in his father's property?

The instinctive answer might be: once he reaches *bar mitzvah*, thirteen years and a day. But the Gemara is about to introduce a different framework entirely — one in which *chazal* imposed an additional layer of protection specifically around the sale of inherited land, requiring a higher age of maturity before such transactions are recognized as valid.

Why specifically land, and why specifically inherited property? Several threads of reasoning converge here. Land represents the core of a family's patrimony, its fundamental continuity from one generation to the next. The Torah's entire framework of inheritance is oriented around land. One who squanders inherited land before reaching genuine maturity is not merely making a poor business decision; he is severing something of lasting significance. Moreover — and this is a distinct but related point — there is a well-known human tendency to treat inherited wealth with less care than earned wealth. What comes without effort can depart without regret. These considerations together explain why *chazal* would impose a higher threshold for the sale of *nichsei aviv* than for other legal transactions.

A further nuance concerns the scope of this rule. *Tosafot* raises the question of whether *nichsei aviv* is strictly limited to inherited property or whether it extends to land this young person himself purchased. The more compelling reading, *Tosafot* suggests, is that the limitation applies specifically to inherited property. It would be paradoxical to allow a seventeen-year-old to purchase land and then hold that very transaction void because he cannot yet sell land. What he acquired through his own dealings he may dispose of as well. The additional safeguard is reserved for what he received.

### 18 or 20

The specific age requirement is the subject of an Amoraic dispute. Rava says in the name of Rav Nachman: *ben shmoneh esrei* — eighteen years old. Rav Huna bar Chinena says in the name of Rav Nachman: *miben esrim* — twenty years old. Both are thus attributed to Rav Nachman, with the Amoraim disagreeing over what Rav Nachman actually held.

### Benei Brak Story

Rabbi Zeira challenges this debate from a *ma'aseh* — the very case of Benei Brak already discussed in the context of the *shtar* dispute. There, when someone sold his father's property and then died, the family came forward claiming he had been a minor at the time of the sale. They asked Rabbi Akiva whether they were permitted to exhume the body and examine it for signs of puberty. Rabbi Akiva ruled that this was forbidden — both because of the prohibition against desecrating a corpse, and because the physical signs may change after death, rendering the examination meaningless even if permitted.

The significance of this story for the age debate is the following: there is no way to determine from a body's examination whether a person was eighteen or twenty. Age in years cannot be read from a corpse. The only thing physical examination could reveal is the presence or absence of pubic hair — the biological markers of puberty. This means the case must involve a person whose age in years was not in question, but whose physical maturity was. Even at eighteen or twenty, if the person had not yet developed the requisite *simanei* gidlut, he would be considered a *katan* for purposes of this *halacha*. The examination was therefore aimed at determining biological adulthood, not chronological age.

Rabbi Zeira's challenge is directed specifically at the opinion of twenty: if the age is twenty, and everyone agrees this person was twenty, then even without pubic hair he would be halachically an adult — for the Mishnah rules that one who is twenty without two pubic hairs is treated as a *saris* and is considered a full adult. Why then would there be any point in examining the body?

### Saris Exception

This is where the Gemara introduces a qualification. Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak says in the name of Rav: *vehu shenoldu bo simanei saris* — the rule that a twenty-year-old without pubic hair is deemed a *saris* applies only when that person also displays specific bodily signs of being a *saris*, that is, indicators of genuine infertility. Without those additional markers, the mere absence of pubic hair at age twenty does not automatically confer adult status; the person may still be considered a *katan*.

Rava adds that this reading is also supported by a precise analysis of the Mishnah's language. The Mishnah does not say *hu saris* — he is a *saris* — but rather *hu hasaris* — he is the *saris* — implying that a specific, pre-existing condition of being a *saris* must be demonstrable. The definite article signals that we are not assigning the status of *saris* by default, but recognizing it where it already manifestly exists.

The practical consequence for the Benei Brak story is clear: the examination was meaningful precisely because it might reveal the absence of pubic hair without any indicators of *saris* status. In that case, even a twenty-year-old would remain a *katan*, and the sale would be void.

### Middle Age Rule

This raises a further question: if the absence of pubic hair with no indicators of *saris* status leaves a person in a state of perpetual minority, is there any point at which such a person is considered an adult regardless? Rav Chiya rules: *ad rov shenotav* — until the majority of his expected lifespan. Since the verse implies a lifespan of seventy years, the midpoint is thirty-five or thirty-six. Once this person crosses into the second half of his life without ever having developed pubic hair and without indicators of *saris* status, he is nonetheless treated as an adult.

### Medical Advice

Rav Chiya also offers a practical note: when people would come before him who were already into their later years and still had not developed pubic hair, he would assess their physical condition and advise accordingly. If the person was thin, he would tell them to gain weight. If the person was overweight, he would tell them to lose weight. The reasoning is that the appearance of these *simanei* maturity can sometimes be triggered by changes in body weight — in one direction or another — and what appears to be a physiological absence may in fact be a reversible condition. The implication is that Rav Chiya was attempting, where possible, to help such individuals attain recognized adult status before the midpoint of life, rather than simply waiting for the calendar to resolve the question.

### Within vs After

The Gemara next poses the question of *toch zman* — what is the status of a person who acts within, rather than after, the required year? The question is whether *toch zman*, acting during the eighteenth year (or the twentieth year), is treated *kilfnei zman* — as if before the relevant age — or *k'achar zman* — as if already past it.

Rava says in the name of Rav Nachman: *toch zman kilfnei zman* — acting within the eighteenth year is treated as not yet having reached eighteen. Rava bar Rav Hila says in the name of Rav Nachman: *toch zman k'achar zman* — acting within the eighteenth year counts as eighteen. Both again attribute their positions to Rav Nachman, reflecting an internal dispute about what he actually ruled.

The Gemara notes that Rava's position — *toch zman kilfnei zman* — was not stated explicitly but rather inferred from an incident. Someone acting within his eighteenth year went and sold property. The case came before Rava, who declared the sale invalid. Observers assumed this was because Rava held *toch zman kilfnei zman*. The Gemara cautions, however: *velo hi* — this inference may be wrong.

### Irresponsibility Test

The actual reason Rava voided the sale, the Gemara suggests, may have had nothing to do with the *toch zman* question. Rava may have genuinely held *toch zman k'achar zman* but voided the sale for a different reason: *shtuta yeseira chaza bei* — he observed extreme foolishness in the seller. The specific behavior cited is that this person had been freeing his slaves, which in the context of a slave-owning ancient economy was understood as economically irrational and irresponsible behavior with one's property.

This introduces a second axis of analysis. Beyond the formal age thresholds of eighteen or twenty, the Gemara recognizes a category of *shututa yeseira* — exceptional irresponsibility — that can disqualify a sale even when the seller meets the chronological requirements. The protective principle behind the age requirement is the prevention of foolish squandering of an inherited estate. If a person, even upon reaching the requisite age, demonstrates a pattern of behavior that reveals an incapacity for responsible judgment, that purpose is not served by rubber-stamping the transaction.

Rava's ruling of invalidity in that case, then, was not necessarily a ruling on *toch zman* at all. It may simply have been the application of this *shututa yeseira* principle to an individual who had demonstrated manifestly irresponsible conduct.

### Acumen Exception

The Gemara then records the query sent by Giddal bar Minyashi to Rava. The question posed is: *tinokes bat arba'asara shana v'yom echad* — a girl of fourteen years and one day — who *yoda'as b'tiv masa umatan*, who is knowledgeable and sophisticated in business matters. Is her sale of her father's property valid?

The case thus presents the mirror image of *shututa yeseira*. There, someone meeting the formal age requirement but exhibiting extreme irresponsibility loses the protection of the rule. Here, someone below the formal age requirement but exhibiting exceptional business acumen raises the question of whether the protective restriction should be lifted in the other direction.

The ages chosen — fourteen — are not incidental. This is a girl who is *bat mitzvah* by one day, so she is a full halachic adult for all other purposes. Yet for the purpose of selling *nichsei aviv*, she is below the required threshold. The question is whether demonstrated business sophistication can substitute for chronological age.

The follow-up questions in the Gemara press the hypothetical further: why frame the case around a fourteen-year-old girl rather than a boy, and why fourteen rather than twelve — the age of *bat mitzvah*? The Gemara deflects both with *ma'aseh she'hayah kach hayah* — the case was as it was. The specifics were dictated by the actual situation at hand. Nevertheless, the principle implied is clear: if the concern is protection of an inexperienced minor from foolish decisions about inherited land, then a genuinely sophisticated businessman or businesswoman operating even below the formal age might argue for recognition.

### Giddal Story

The response to Giddal bar Minyashi's query is direct: *im yoda'as b'tiv masa umatan, mikchah mekach umimkarah mimkar* — if she truly understands the nature of commercial transactions, her purchases are purchases and her sales are sales. The acumen exception is recognized.

The Gemara then relates a story involving Giddal bar Minyashi himself that illuminates the practical application of these categories. A person below twenty had gone and sold his father's property. The case came before Rava. His relatives counseled him on how to appear foolish before Rava in order to void the sale — specifically, they told him to eat dates and spit the pits out in Rava's presence, presenting himself as uncouth and lacking in judgment.

He did so, and Rava ruled the sale invalid: *zvini lav zvini* — the sale was no sale.

When the court was about to formalize this ruling in a written document, the buyers saw an opportunity. They instructed the seller to go to Rava and complain: how can you charge a *zuz* for writing this document, when you can buy an entire *Megillat Esther* for a *zuz*? Their goal was to demonstrate that the seller was capable of making a reasoned comparison and articulating a substantive complaint — in other words, that he was not as foolish as his behavior had suggested.

He went and made exactly this argument to Rava. Rava reversed his ruling: *zvini zvini* — the sale is valid after all.

The relatives, incensed, told Rava that the buyers had coached him. Rava was unmoved: *misbar v'savar* — the fact that someone can follow and internalize an explanation, and then act on it, is itself evidence of intelligence. *Mida yad-i* — this shows that he understands. As for the pit-spitting performance: *chutzpa yeseirata hu d'hava bei* — he was not foolish, he was audacious. He was willing to behave boorishly in front of a great authority to achieve his ends. That is a sign of chutzpah, not stupidity.

### Three Categories

The story thus maps onto the three-tier framework that emerges from this entire sugya. At the lower extreme, a person exhibiting *shututa yeseira* — genuine, extreme foolishness — cannot sell even after reaching the formal age. At the upper extreme, a person who is *yodea b'tiv masa umatan* — sophisticated in commercial dealings — may sell even before reaching the formal age. In between is the normal population, for whom the formal age thresholds of eighteen or twenty govern.

*Tosafot* reads the Giddal bar Minyashi story as testing the *shututa yeseira* threshold. Was this young man so irresponsible that his sale was void even assuming he met the age requirement? Rava's ultimate conclusion was that he was not in the *shututa yeseira* category — he was merely bold, not genuinely incompetent. The sale therefore reverted to the normal rule, and since the normal rule for Rava is eighteen (not twenty), and the seller was under twenty but presumably over eighteen, the sale was valid. This is the source from which we derive, inferentially, that Rava's position on the threshold age is eighteen — a position that, as the Gemara has noted, was not stated explicitly by Rava but only arrived at through this *michlala*.

The *Rashbam*, by contrast, reads the story as testing whether the seller qualified for the *yodea b'tiv masa umatan* exception. *Tosafot* finds this reading difficult: a young man who can be coached to complain about the cost of a legal document and manipulated in both directions by competing parties is hardly the paradigm of commercial sophistication that the exception envisions.

### Testimony Rights

The Gemara closes with a ruling by Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua: *ule'edus eduso edus* — even a minor below the required age of eighteen or twenty, who cannot sell his father's land, may nonetheless testify regarding a sale.

This ruling carries a striking conceptual implication. One might have supposed that if a person lacks the legal capacity to engage in a transaction, he also lacks the capacity to understand and testify about it. The Gemara implicitly rejects this assumption by drawing a sharp distinction between judgment and comprehension. The age restriction on selling *nichsei aviv* is not grounded in a claim that the person below eighteen or twenty fails to understand what is happening around him. It is grounded in a concern about susceptibility to manipulation, impulsivity, and poor judgment in the exercise of commercial discretion. Testifying about what one witnessed requires comprehension, not the same quality of judgment required for executing consequential transactions. Therefore, halachic capacity for testimony is not impaired by the same considerations that restrict the sale.

Mar Zutra adds a further distinction that will serve as the launching point for the continuation of this discussion: *lo amaran ela b'metaltalei aval b'mekarkei lo* — this capacity to testify applies to movable property, but not to land. The question of whether a minor below the qualifying age may testify specifically about real estate transactions introduces a new dimension to the analysis, connecting the rules of testimony back to the original protective concern about *karka* — the inherited land that lies at the heart of this entire sugya.
