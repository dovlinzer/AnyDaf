# Sanhedrin 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kohen Gadol & Yibbum

### Perek Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹלֵץ וְחוֹלְצִין לְאִשְׁתּוֹ וּמְיַבְּמִין אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ. אֲבָל הוּא אֵינוֹ מְיַבֵּם, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא אָסוּר בְּאַלְמָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *kohen gadol* **performs *ḥalitza*** with his brother's widow **and** his brother **performs *ḥalitza*** with his wife, **and others may perform levirate marriage with his wife. But he himself does not perform levirate marriage,** as **he is prohibited** from marrying **a widow.**

We begin the second *perek* of Masechet Sanhedrin, on 19b, starting three lines down from the word *kapasik vetani*. The *perek* is broadly concerned with the special laws governing the dignity and personal status of the *kohen gadol* and the *melech*. The *mishnah* established that the *kohen gadol* does not perform *yibbum*. More precisely, the *mishnah* states: *kohen gadol dan vedannin oto, meid umeidim oto, choletz vecholtzin le-ishto umeyabmin et ishto* — the *kohen gadol* is judged and judges, testifies and others testify against him, performs *chalitzah* and others perform *chalitzah* for his wife, and others may perform *yibbum* with his wife. *Aval hu eino meyabbem* — but he himself does not perform *yibbum* if his brother dies without children. The reason given is *mipnei shehu assur be-almanah*: one of the prohibitions incumbent upon the *kohen gadol* is that he may not marry a widow, and once his brother dies, the sister-in-law becomes an *almanah*, making *yibbum* impossible for him.

### Kohen Gadol Status

The *kohen gadol* bears two distinct laws concerning his personal status in marriage. First, he is prohibited from marrying an *almanah* — a widow. All *kohanim* are prohibited from marrying a divorcee, and the *kohen gadol* bears the additional prohibition against marrying a widow. Second, stated in the form of a positive commandment, the Torah requires him to marry a *betulah*: *betulah yikach*. These two obligations — the *lo ta'aseh* of *almanah lo yikach* and the *aseh* of *betulah yikach* — combine to create the complex halachic tension that the *gemara* proceeds to analyze.

### Erusin vs Nisu'in

The *gemara* opens with the observation that the *mishnah* made no distinction between a brother who had reached the stage of *erusin* — the initiation of marriage, equivalent to *kiddushin*, where the marriage has not yet been consummated — and the stage of *nisu'in*, where the marriage is fully completed. The *mishnah* categorically prohibits the *kohen gadol* from performing *yibbum* in both cases.

## Erusin Nisu'in Debate

### Aseh vs Lo Taseh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא מִן הַנִּישּׂוּאִין, הָוֵי עֲשֵׂה וְלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה — וְאֵין אֲשֵׂה דוֹחֶה לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה וַעֲשֵׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted,** it is understood that he does not consummate levirate marriage with a widow **from marriage,** since **there is** both **a positive mitzva** and **also a prohibition: and a positive mitzva does not override a prohibition and a positive mitzva.**

The *gemara* explains why the categorical ruling is understandable in the case of *nisu'in*: *bishlama min ha-nisu'in havi aseh velo ta'aseh* — in the case of *nisu'in*, we have both an *aseh* and a *lo ta'aseh* arrayed against the *mitzvah* of *yibbum*. The *mitzvah* of *yibbum* is itself an *aseh*, but the *kohen gadol* faces the *lo ta'aseh* of *almanah lo yikach* and the *aseh* of *betulah yikach* working against him. *Ve-ein aseh docheh lo ta'aseh va-aseh* — the general principle is that a positive commandment does not override the combination of a *lo ta'aseh* and an *aseh*. So in the case of *nisu'in*, the *kohen gadol* faces two prohibitions standing against one *mitzvah*, and the case for prohibiting *yibbum* is clear.

### Aseh Docheh Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִן הָאֵירוּסִין אַמַּאי? יָבֹא עֲשֵׂה וְיִדְחֶה לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה!
>
> **Translation:** **But** as for the ruling that he does not consummate levirate marriage with a widow **from betrothal, why** not? The **positive mitzva** to consummate levirate marriage should **come and override the prohibition.**

The difficulty arises with *erusin*. At the stage of *erusin*, we presume the woman is still a *betulah* — she has not had relations with her husband. If we remove the *aseh* of *betulah yikach* from the equation, what remains is simply an *aseh* of *yibbum* against a *lo ta'aseh* of *almanah lo yikach*. And there is a well-established principle — derived from *psukim* in Yevamos — that *aseh docheh lo ta'aseh*, a positive commandment overrides a negative one. If so, the *mishnah* should have drawn a distinction: in the case of *erusin*, the *kohen gadol* should be permitted to perform *yibbum*, since the *mitzvah* of *yibbum* as an *aseh* would override the *lo ta'aseh* alone. Why does the *mishnah* state the prohibition categorically?

### Gzerah Biah Rishonah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּזֵירָה בִּיאָה רִאשׁוֹנָה, אַטּוּ בִּיאָה שְׁנִיָּה. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: אִם קָדְמוּ וּבָעֲלוּ בִּיאָה רִאשׁוֹנָה – קָנוּ, וְאָסוּר לְקַיְּימָן בְּבִיאָה שְׁנִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The **first** act of **intercourse** is prohibited by rabbinic **decree due to** the likelihood of **a second** act of **intercourse.** The Gemara comments: **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: **If** the High Priest or one whose *yevama* is forbidden to him **went ahead and engaged in a first** act of **intercourse** with her, **he acquired** her as a wife, **but it is prohibited to retain** that woman as a wife **for a second** act of **intercourse.**

The *gemara* answers: *gzeira biah rishonah, atu biah shniyah*. There is in fact no biblical prohibition preventing the *kohen gadol* from performing *yibbum* with a *yevamah* from *erusin* — the prohibition is rabbinic. The *chachamim* were concerned that if the first act of *biah* — which is itself the *mitzvah* of *yibbum*, as the *psukim* make clear — were permitted, the couple would continue with a second *biah*. The second *biah* is not a *mitzvah*, and therefore the considerations of *betulah yikach* and *almanah lo yikach* reassert themselves in full force. At that point the *kohen gadol* faces both the *aseh* and the *lo ta'aseh* against him, and the *aseh* of *yibbum* cannot overcome that combination. In order to prevent the *kohen gadol* from arriving at that forbidden second *biah*, the *chachamim* issued a *gzeira* prohibiting even the first *biah*.

The *gemara* notes that a *baraita* supports this position: *im kadmu u-va'alu biah rishonah, kanu* — if the *kohen gadol* went ahead and violated the *gzeira* and had the first *biah*, the marriage takes effect (*kanu*), but they are thereafter prohibited from continuing with a second *biah* and the relationship must be dissolved.

## Funeral Procession

### Rav Meir Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וּמִן הַמִּקְדָּשׁ לֹא יֵצֵא״ – לֹא יֵצֵא עִמָּהֶן, אֲבָל יוֹצֵא הוּא אַחֲרֵיהֶן. כֵּיצַד? הֵן נִכְסִין וְהוּא נִגְלֶה, הֵן נִיגְלִין וְהוּא נִכְסֶה. וְיוֹצֵא עַד פֶּתַח שַׁעַר הָעִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse concerning the High Priest, which states: **"And from the Temple he shall not emerge"** (Leviticus 21:12), means: **He shall not emerge with them** as they escort the bier, **but he emerges after them. How so?** Once **they are concealed** from sight by turning onto another street, **he is revealed** on the street they departed, and when **they are revealed,** then **he is concealed. And he emerges** with them **until the entrance** of the gate of the city.

The *gemara* now turns to the *mishnah*'s discussion of how the *kohen gadol* conducts himself in relation to a funeral procession. The *mishnah* records a debate between Rav Meir and Rav Yehudah. Rav Meir holds that when the *kohen gadol* has a death in his family — *meis lo meis* — he does not follow the funeral procession directly. Instead, the two processionals alternate their visibility: *hen nichsin v'hu nigleh, hen niglin v'hu nichseh* — when the funeral party is hidden from view, he appears, and when they emerge, he withdraws. The two processionals thus remain perpetually separate. Nevertheless, he does accompany the procession up to the gate of the city: *v'yotzei imahen ad pesach shaar ha-ir*.

### Rav Yehudah Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁפִּיר קָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda is saying well,** and his statement is consistent with the straightforward meaning of the verse: "And from the Temple he shall not emerge" (Leviticus 21:12).

Rav Yehudah takes a stricter position: *eino yotzei min ha-Mikdash* — the *kohen gadol* may not leave the Temple precinct at all. The *gemara* quotes the *baraita* which presents the *pasuk* underlying Rav Yehudah's ruling: *u-min ha-Mikdash lo yeitzei* — "and from the Temple he shall not depart" (in *Parshas Emor*, in the laws of *aveilus* for the *kohen gadol*). The *baraita* itself, however, reads the *pasuk* in the manner of Rav Meir: *lo yeitze imahen* — he shall not go out *with them*, but *aval yotzei hu achareihem* — he may follow behind them. *Keitzad? Hen nichsin v'hu nigleh, hen niglin v'hu nichseh* — the same alternating arrangement described in the *mishnah*.

### Rav Meir Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רַבִּי מֵאִיר: אִי הָכִי, לְבֵיתוֹ נָמֵי לָא! אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: ״מִן הַמִּקְדָּשׁ לֹא יֵצֵא״ – מִקְּדוּשָּׁתוֹ לֹא יֵצֵא. וְכֵיוָן דְּאִית לֵיהּ הֶכֵּירָא, לָא אָתֵי לְמִינְגַּע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Rabbi Meir** could have **said to you: If so,** that you understand the verse so narrowly, he should **not** go out **to his house as well** but should be required to stay in the Temple. **Rather, this** is what **it is saying: "And from the Temple [*hamikdash*] he shall not emerge"** means: **From his sanctity [*mikedushato*] he shall not emerge** by contracting ritual impurity, **and since he has a distinctive indicator** in that he does not walk together with those accompanying the bier, **he will not come to touch** the bier and contract impurity.

How then does Rav Meir read *u-min ha-Mikdash lo yeitzei*? *Ela hachi ka'amar: min ha-Mikdash lo yetzei — mikedushato lo yetzei* — the *pasuk* means not that he may not physically leave the Temple building, but that he must not depart from his state of sanctity. The concern the *pasuk* addresses is *tum'at meit*. The *kohen gadol* is prohibited from contracting corpse *tumah*, and the *pasuk* is warning him to guard his sanctity in that regard. *V'keivan de-is lei hekera* — and since he has a *hekera*, a visible act of distinction that separates him from the funeral party, that is sufficient. When a person is publicly and visibly acting in a manner distinct from the procession, he will be reminded to remain on guard and *lo ati leminga* — he will not come to touch anything that is *tamei meis*.

### Yehudah Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אַגַּב מְרָרֵיהּ, דִּילְמָא מִקְּרֵי וְאָתֵי וְנָגַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how would **Rabbi Yehuda** respond? The Gemara explains: There is still cause for concern that **on account of his bitterness** due to the death of his loved one, **perhaps it will happen that he comes and touches** the bier.

Rav Yehudah's counter-position is that this *hekera* is inadequate: *agav merira dilma mikrei ve-ati venaga* — in the midst of grief, a person's judgment is diminished, and he may come to inadvertently touch something that causes *tum'at meit*. The visible separation of the two processionals is therefore not a sufficient safeguard. Rav Yehudah thus requires the *kohen gadol* to remain within the Temple entirely.

## Nichimat Avelim

### Temple Officials

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁהוּא עוֹבֵר בַּשּׁוּרָה לְנַחֵם אֶת אֲחֵרִים – סְגָן וּמָשׁוּחַ שֶׁעָבַר בִּימִינוֹ, וְרֹאשׁ בֵּית אָב וַאֲבֵלִים וְכׇל הָעָם מִשְּׂמֹאלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **When** the High Priest **passes by in the line to console others, the deputy** High Priest **and** the **former anointed** High Priest, who had served temporarily and then stepped down, are **on his right. And the head of the patrilineal family** appointed over the priestly watch performing the sacrificial rites that day in the Temple; **and the mourners; and all the people** are **on his left.**

A *baraita* now describes the physical arrangements for *nichum avelim* involving the *kohen gadol*. *Tanu rabanan: keshehu over ba-shura lenachem et acherim* — when the *kohen gadol* passes along the *shura*, the line of comfort extended to mourners, the ceremonial attendants accompanying him are: the *sgan* — the assistant to the *kohen gadol* — and the *mashuach she'avar*, both positioned to his right. Rashi explains that the *mashuach she'avar* is a former *kohen gadol* who was temporarily removed from service due to some *psul* — a disqualification — and who was replaced for the interim, but who retains his status and returns to his service when the *psul* passes. To the left of the *kohen gadol* stands the *rosh beis av* — the head of the mourning family — and then *vechol ha'am mismol*, all the people arrayed to the left.

### Kohen Comforts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְשֶׁהוּא עוֹמֵד בַּשּׁוּרָה וּמִתְנַחֵם מֵאֲחֵרִים – סְגָן מִימִינוֹ, וְרֹאשׁ בֵּית אָב וְכׇל הָעָם מִשְּׂמֹאלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And when he is standing in the line** among the other mourners **and is consoled by others, the deputy** High Priest is **on his right, and the head of the patrilineal family and all the people** are **on his left.**

The physical arrangement thus places the *kohen gadol* at the center, with the *sgan* and *mashuach she'avar* to his right and the mourning family and community to his left.

### Kohen Mourns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל מָשׁוּחַ שֶׁעָבַר לָא אָתֵי גַּבֵּיהּ. מַאי טַעְמָא? חָלְשָׁא דַּעְתֵּיהּ. סָבַר: קָא חָדֵי בִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: **But** the **previously anointed one does not come before him. What is the reason?** The High Priest **will become distraught. He will think: He is happy about me** in my bereaved state.

The *baraita* then turns to the reverse situation: *keshehu omed ba-shura u-misnachem me-acherim* — when the *kohen gadol* himself is the mourner and receives comfort from others. Here the *sgan* is to his right, and to his left stands the *rosh beis av* and all the people. A careful reader will notice that someone is conspicuously absent from this list: the *mashuach she'avar*, who was present in the previous scenario, does not appear when the *kohen gadol* is the mourner.

### Mashuach Concern

The *gemara* raises the question: *aval mashuach she'avar lo asi gabei?* — does the *mashuach she'avar* not come to visit the *kohen gadol* when he is in mourning? The answer given is: *chalsha date-ih, savar kachadi bi* — the *kohen gadol's* spirit would be weakened by his presence; he would think that the *mashuach she'avar* is inwardly pleased at his misfortune. The logic is that the *mashuach she'avar* was displaced from the position of *kohen gadol* due to his temporary *psul*, and during that interval he fills the role. If something befalls the current *kohen gadol*, the *mashuach she'avar* steps in. There is thus a structural tension between the two — not necessarily personal animosity, but an objective conflict of interests — and the *kohen gadol* in his grief might naturally suspect the *mashuach she'avar* of *schadenfreude*. To protect the *kohen gadol* from this weakening of spirit, the *chachamim* ruled that the *mashuach she'avar* does not attend the mourning house of the *kohen gadol*.

## Pappa's Inferences

### Sgan Mamona

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מֵהָא מַתְנִיתָא תְּלָת. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הַיְינוּ סְגַן הַיְינוּ מְמוּנֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Learn from it, from this *baraita*, three** matters. **Learn from it** that **the deputy** High Priest **is** the same as the **appointed** person, as the *baraita* is referring to the deputy High Priest in the same function described by the mishna as the appointed one.

Rav Pappa states: *amar Pappa, shma mina tlat* — from this *baraita* we derive three things. First: *hainu sgan hainu mamona* — the *sgan* is the same figure referred to elsewhere as the *mamona*. Another *baraita* uses the term *mamona* for this official, and Rav Pappa confirms that the two terms refer to the same person.

### Original Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: אֲבֵלִים עוֹמְדִין וְכׇל הָעָם עוֹבְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And learn from it** that the way of consoling in a line is that **the mourners stand and all the people pass by** and console them.

Second: *avelim omdim ve-chol ha'am ovrim* — we learn from here that the original procedure for *nichum avelim* was for the mourners to stand and the community to pass by them.

### Positioning Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: אֲבֵלִים לִשְׂמֹאל הַמְנַחֲמִין הֵן עוֹמְדִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And learn from it** that the custom is that the **mourners stand to the left of the consolers.**

Third: *avelim li-smol ha-menachamim hen omdim* — we learn that the mourners are positioned to the left of those providing comfort, and those providing comfort are the ones standing.

## Historical Development

### Two Families Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ אֲבֵלִים עוֹמְדִין, וְכׇל הָעָם עוֹבְרִין. וְהָיוּ שְׁתֵּי מִשְׁפָּחוֹת בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם מִתְגָּרוֹת זוֹ בָּזוֹ, זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת: ״אֲנִי עוֹבֶרֶת תְּחִלָּה״, וְזֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת: ״אֲנִי עוֹבֶרֶת תְּחִלָּה״. הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁיְּהֵא הָעָם עוֹמְדִין וַאֲבֵלִים עוֹבְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Initially the mourners would stand, and all the people would pass by** one after another and console them. **And there were two families in Jerusalem who would fight with each other,** as **this** one **would say: We pass by first** because we are more distinguished and important, **and that** one **would say: We pass by first.** Consequently, **they decreed that the people should stand and** the **mourners pass by,** and disputes would be avoided.

A *baraita* now provides the historical context for why the procedure was changed. *Tanu rabbanan: be-rishona hayu avelim omdim ve-chol ha'am ovrim* — originally, the mourners stood and the community passed by. *Ve-hayu shtei mishpachot bi-Yerushalayim mitgarot zu ba-zu* — but there were two families in Jerusalem who would quarrel with one another. *Zot omeret ani overet techila, zot omeret ani overet techila* — each one would insist that she had the right to pass by the mourners first, each claiming priority of honor.

### Takana Reversal

To eliminate this conflict, the *chachamim* instituted a *takana*: *hitkinu she-yihiyu ha-am omdim ve-avelim ovrim* — they reversed the procedure, making the community stand and the mourners pass by. Even though this reversal is, in some sense, not the ideal arrangement for the procedures of *halachos aveilus*, the values of *kavod ha-briyos* and the prevention of dispute were deemed sufficient grounds to alter the practice. The awareness that *shalom* is built into the historical origins of this reversal is something worth carrying in mind.

## Rabbi Yosi Takanos

### Shura Restoration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר אַבָּא: הֶחְזִיר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אֶת הַדָּבָר לְיוֹשְׁנוֹ בְּצִיפּוֹרִי, שֶׁיִּהְיוּ אֲבֵלִים עוֹמְדִין וְכׇל הָעָם עוֹבְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Abba says: Rabbi Yosei returned the matter to its former** custom **in Tzippori** his city, **that the mourners would stand and all the people would pass.**

Rabbi Bar Abba reports: *hechzir Rabbi Yosi et ha-davar le-yoshno be-Tzipori* — Rabbi Yosi restored the original procedure in Tzipori, instituting that the mourners would stand and the community would pass by, returning the *shura* to its original form.

### Child Safety Takana

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָמֵי בַּר אַבָּא: הִתְקִין רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּצִיפּוֹרִי שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא אִשָּׁה מְהַלֶּכֶת בַּשּׁוּק וּבְנָהּ אַחֲרֶיהָ, מִשּׁוּם מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁהָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rami bar Abba says: Rabbi Yosei instituted** an ordinance **in Tzippori that a woman should not walk in the market and** have **her son** following **behind her;** rather, he should walk in front of her, **because of an incident that happened** in which bandits abducted a child and assaulted the mother when she came searching for him in his place of captivity.

Rabbi Yosi enacted a second *takana* in Tzipori: *shelo tehei isha mehalacḥet ba-shuk u-vnah achareha* — a woman should not walk through the marketplace with her child walking behind her. The reason: *mishum ma'aseh she-hayah* — because of an incident that occurred. Rashi supplies the details of that incident: kidnappers seized the child from behind his mother. When she turned around and did not see him, she began to cry out. One of the kidnappers approached her and said, come, I will show you where he is. When she followed him in, she was assaulted. The *takana* thus requires that the child walk close to the mother rather than trailing behind, to prevent the exploitation of that physical separation.

### Bathroom Yichud

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָמֵי בַּר אַבָּא: הִתְקִין רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּצִיפּוֹרִי שֶׁיִּהְיוּ נָשִׁים מְסַפְּרוֹת בְּבֵית הַכִּסֵּא, מִשּׁוּם יִיחוּד.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rami bar Abba says: Rabbi Yosei instituted** an ordinance **in Tzippori that women should converse in the bathroom, because of** the restrictions on women being **secluded** with men.

Rabbi Yosi's third *takana* in Tzipori: *sheyihiyu nashim mesaprot be-veit ha-kisse mishum yichud* — women should speak aloud to one another while in the bathroom, on account of the laws of *yichud*. The underlying concern, as Rashi explains, is that a man might approach and, hearing no sound indicating who is present, enter and create a situation of *yichud*.

The Talmud cites the *mishnah*: *lo yityached adam im shtei nashim* — a man may not have *yichud* even with two women. So the remedy is for the two women to speak to each other, thereby alerting any approaching man that two women are present and thus removing the concern of *yichud*.

There is a notable analytical tension here concerning the time element of *yichud*. Ordinarily, *yichud* requires the elapse of a duration equivalent to *zman biah* — perhaps five or six minutes — not merely the existence of a situation. According to Rashi's framing, however, there is no mention of any such time threshold; the setting itself appears to constitute the problem. The Yad Ramah addresses this difficulty and takes a different approach: he understands the women's speaking aloud not as a *halachic* fix for *yichud* in the technical sense, but as a practical deterrent — the noise creates an impression that many people are present, and anyone with harmful intent will be dissuaded. For Rashi, the concern appears to be the *halachic* category of *yichud* itself; for the Yad Ramah, it functions more as an anti-assault protection.

## Shura Requirements

### Ten Person Minyan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מְנַשְּׁיָא בַּר עוּת: שְׁאֵילִית אֶת רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה רַבָּה בְּבֵית עָלְמִין דְּהוּצָל, וְאָמַר לִי: אֵין שׁוּרָה פְּחוּתָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה בְּנֵי אָדָם, וְאֵין אֲבֵלִים מִן הַמִּנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Menashya bar Ute says: I asked** a question of **Rabbi Yoshiya the Great in the cemetery of Huzal, and he said** this *halakha* **to me: There is no line** for consoling mourners with **fewer than ten people, and** the **mourners are not** included in the **count.**

Rav Menashya bar Shlub reports: Rav Yosef asked Rav Yoshiya Rabbah this question at the cemetery in Hutzal, and he was told: *ein shura pechuta me-asara bnei adam* — the *shura* of *nichum avelim* requires a minimum of ten people. *Ve-ein avelim min ha-minyan* — and the mourners themselves do not count toward the ten. This is intuitive: the entire purpose of the *shura* is for the community to gather around the mourners and comfort them. The mourners are the center of the ritual, not participants who constitute it.

### Flexible Arrangement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּין שֶׁאֲבֵלִים עוֹמְדִין וְכׇל הָעָם עוֹבְרִין, בֵּין שֶׁאֲבֵלִים עוֹבְרִין וְכׇל הָעָם עוֹמְדִין.
>
> **Translation:** This minimum number of consolers applies **whether the mourners stand and all the people pass by, or the mourners pass by and all the people stand.**

The same authority ruled: *bein she-avelim omdim ve-chol ha-am ovrim, bein she-avelim ovrim ve-chol ha-am omdim* — whether the mourners stand and the community passes by, or the mourners pass and the community stands, both arrangements are acceptable. The essential requirement is the gathering of ten, not the specific physical choreography.

## Comfort Formula

### Formula Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁהוּא מִתְנַחֵם כּוּ׳. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: כִּי מְנַחֵם הוּא אַחֲרִינֵי, הֵיכִי אָמַר לְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: And **when he is consoled** by others in his mourning, all the people say to him: We are your atonement. And he says to them: May you be blessed from Heaven. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **When** the High Priest **consoles others, what** should **he say to them?**

The *mishnah* described what is said to the *kohen gadol* when he is in mourning, but did not address what the *kohen gadol* himself says when he performs *nichum avelim* for others. The *gemara* raises this question: *ki menachem hu achrinei, heicha amar lehu?* — when the *kohen gadol* comforts others, what does he say to them?

### Titnachwmu Formula

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: וְהוּא אוֹמֵר תִּתְנַחֲמוּ. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא כִּי מְנַחֲמִי אַחֲרִינֵי לְדִידֵיהּ, אָמַר לְהוּ אִיהוּ תִּתְנַחֲמוּ? נְחָשָׁא קָא רָמֵי לְהוּ! אֶלָּא, כִּי מְנַחֵם לְאַחֲרִינֵי אֲמַר לְהוּ תִּתְנַחֲמוּ. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** an answer from a *baraita*: **And he says: May you be consoled.** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** in which he says this? **If we say that when others console him** in his mourning **he says to them: May you be consoled,** this does not make sense, because **he** would be **throwing a curse at them** by saying that they too will need to be consoled. **Rather,** it must mean: **When he consoles others, he says to them: May you be consoled. Learn from** the *baraita* that this is what he says to console others.

The *gemara* offers: *ta shema, ve-hu omer titnachamu* — come and hear, for he says *titnachamu*, "you shall be comforted." The *gemara* then pauses to clarify: *heichi dami?* — in what case is this said?

The *gemara* considers the possibility that *titnachamu* is said when others are comforting him and he responds. But this reading is rejected immediately: *nechasha ka-rami lehu?* — if he is the mourner and others are comforting him, and he responds with "you shall be comforted," that sounds like a curse. It would be as if a mourner, upon receiving comfort, turns to the comforters and wishes upon them that they too should need to be comforted. This cannot be the intended reading.

### Comforter Context

The *gemara* therefore concludes: *ela ki menachem le-achrinei, amar lehu titnachamu* — rather, it is when the *kohen gadol* is himself the active comforter of others that he pronounces the phrase *titnachamu*. *Shma mina* — and from this we derive the formula.

One observation worth noting: the word *titnachamu* is in the plural. This is structurally analogous to the standard formula in use today — *ha-Makom yenachem etchem* — which is likewise phrased in the plural even when addressing a single mourner. The formula *titnachamu* similarly uses the plural regardless of the number of mourners present, suggesting that the conventional use of a *lashon rabbim* in the comforting formula may have deep roots in this very practice.
