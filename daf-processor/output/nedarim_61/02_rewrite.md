# Nedarim 61 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Harvest Vows

### Intro: Harvest Terms

The mishna at the center of today's daf concerns vows that employ agricultural time-markers. If someone says *ad hakatzir* — until the time of the grain harvest — or *ad habatzir* — until the grape harvest — or *ad hamasik* — until the time of the olive pressing — the rule is *aino assur ela ad sheyagia*: the person is forbidden only until that time arrives, meaning until it begins. This in itself is not a novel ruling; it follows directly from what was established earlier regarding *ad hapesach*. The *chidush* of this mishna lies in what comes next.

The mishna introduces its *klal* before its *prat*, which is somewhat unusual. The general principle is stated as follows: *kol shezmano kavua* — anything that has a fixed time — if one says *ad sheyagia*, one is forbidden until that time arrives. But if one says *ad sheyehei*, until it "becomes," the term is interpreted to mean until it is over — *assur ad sheyeitzei*. This is the rule established for Pesach in the previous mishna. The expression *ad sheyehei* points to when the event becomes a *haya*, a thing of the past; only then is the prohibition lifted.

The contrast — and the *chidush* — is the second half: *vechol she'ein zmano kavua*, anything that does not have a fixed time. In such cases, whether one says *ad sheyehei* or *ad sheyagia*, the result is the same: *aino assur ela ad sheyagia*, one is forbidden only until the season begins. The practical illustration given is *kayitz* — the gathering in of summer fruit. Whether one says *ad hakayitz* or *ad sheyehei hakayitz*, the vow expires when people begin bringing the fruit in their baskets, *ad sheyatchilu ha'am lehachnis bekalkalot*. If, however, one explicitly says *ad sheya'avor hakayitz* — until the *kayitz* passes — then the end point is *ad sheyekaplu hamiktzo'ot*, until the harvesting knives are folded and put away.

### Fixed vs. Unfixed

The conceptual core of this mishna is the distinction between *zmano kavua* and *ein zmano kavua*. The instinctive reading of *ein zmano kavua* is that one does not know when the season begins — every year the harvest starts at a different time, and so the phrase *ad sheyehei* cannot be pointing to a known endpoint. On this reading, when one says "until it happens" about something whose beginning is uncertain, the phrase naturally denotes the onset: it hasn't happened yet, and when it happens — meaning when it starts — that is when the vow expires. By contrast, when one says *ad sheyehei hapesach*, since everyone knows precisely when Pesach begins, "until it happens" cannot mean until it starts — that is already known. It must therefore point forward to when Pesach will have already occurred, will have become a thing of the past. This reading gives the principle considerable internal logic.

### Ein Zmano Kavua

The Ran, however, offers a different explanation of *ein zmano kavua*. He understands it to mean not that the beginning is uncertain, but that the *duration* is uncertain — one does not know when it ends. On this basis, he argues that we are disinclined to interpret *ad sheyehei* as meaning "until it is over," because when the endpoint is itself indeterminate, such a reading would leave the vow without any concrete terminus. We therefore fall back on the concrete beginning point rather than an open-ended conclusion.

One might find this somewhat puzzling: after all, the beginning of the harvest is also variable from year to year, so the asymmetry is not immediately obvious. But part of the Ran's point may be that the beginning of the harvest is in fact a milestone — a recognizable moment when people go out to the fields with their baskets — while the end tapers off gradually and is never marked by a clear, collective act of cessation. The onset has a definite social character; the conclusion does not. So while both are uncertain in the abstract, the beginning is in practice more determinate than the end, and *ad sheyehei* therefore tracks the beginning rather than the indeterminate close.

### Kayitz & Fruit

The Gemara elaborates on exactly what type of fruit defines the beginning of the *kayitz* season. A *tana* teaches: *kol kalkalah sheamru kol kalkalah shel teenim* — when the mishna speaks of people bringing in the baskets, it refers specifically to baskets of figs, not grapes. The reason, as a *braita* explains, is that *kayitz* as a word connotes hand-picking, and figs are picked by hand while grapes are cut with a knife. A *braita* further states: if someone takes a vow *miperot hakayitz* — from the fruits of the *kayitz* — he is forbidden only from figs.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagrees: *anavim bekhlal teenim* — grapes are included in the same category as figs. His reasoning is that when grapes become very ripe and the vine's supporting tendrils dry out, they too can be picked by hand — *ki mirdedu miktetzim beyado* — and therefore they are not categorically excluded from the definition of *kayitz*.

It should be noted that in the agricultural context of the Land of Israel, figs, dates, olives, and grapes were the primary produce. That the term *kayitz* attaches specifically to hand-picked fruits in this setting reflects the cultural and linguistic context in which these vows were made.

### End of Kayitz

The mishna states that the end of the *kayitz* season is marked by the folding away of the harvesting knives: *ad sheyachpilu hamiktzaot*. A *tana* specifies that the operative threshold is *rov hamiktzaot* — most of the knives, not all. This raises an immediate difficulty. If most of the knives in a given area have been put away, but a particular field-owner has not yet finished his harvest and his workers are still active, on what basis may anyone enter that field and take fruit?

The Tosefta states: *hukplu rov hamiktzaot mutarot mishum gezel* — once most of the knives have been folded away, anything remaining in the field is no longer prohibited as theft. The reasoning is that the owner has effectively abandoned the remaining produce — he has been *mafkir* it — and it is therefore also exempt from *ma'aser*. But this logic applies cleanly only when one can say with confidence that this particular owner has relinquished his claim. The use of *rov* introduces genuine ambiguity: how does one know that this specific field falls within the majority whose owners have finished harvesting? The apparent answer embedded in the rule is that the communal practice — the fact that most workers in the region have put their tools away — effectively forces a social reality in which even those who have not formally concluded simply cannot any longer harvest. The rule seems to impose a collective endpoint even on individual holdouts, perhaps on the theory that once the season is effectively over for the region, the remaining fruit is treated as abandoned. This tension will be explored through the stories that follow.

## Rov Hamaktzaot

### Huflu Rov Rule

The halacha of *huflu rov hamaktzaot* has clear social-welfare dimensions. Its promulgation appears to have been driven in part by the rabbis themselves, who actively demonstrated its application. The field-owners, it emerges from the stories below, were not always sympathetic to the rule — some may not have fully accepted it, or may have accepted it in principle while resenting its application in practice.

### Rabbi's Story

The first story: Rabbi and Rabbi Yossi bar Rabbi Yehuda came to a certain place — *iklu l'hahu atra* — at the time when most of the knives had been folded away. Rabbi began eating from the fruit of the trees they passed. Rabbi Yossi bar Rabbi Yehuda did not eat. The owner of the field arrived and said to them: why are the rabbis not eating? The knives have been put away — *hukplu rov hamaktzaot* — go right ahead. And even so, Rabbi Yossi bar Rabbi Yehuda did not eat. The Gemara explains: *kasavar mishum sanyus milta hu deka'amar hai gavra* — he suspected that the field-owner was not sincere, that he was saying this out of spite or resentment rather than genuine permission. This is entirely plausible given what we saw above: if the field-owner genuinely felt that *rov hamaktzaot* obligated him to yield his fruit, he would not have said it with evident bitterness. Rabbi Yossi bar Rabbi Yehuda read the social cue correctly.

### Rav Chama Story

The second story: Rav Chama bar Rav Chanina came to a certain place at the same time — *bizman shehukplu rov hamaktzaot* — and he was eating. He gave some to his attendant, but the attendant would not eat. Rav Chama told him: eat. This is what Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yossi transmitted in the name of his father Rabbi Yossi — that once most of the knives have been put away, it is permitted. The attendant apparently felt uncertain about the halacha or its application; Rav Chama had to actively instruct him and cite the ruling with rabbinic authority. The point seems to be that the rabbis were not merely themselves aware of this rule but felt it important enough to actively promote, even to their own household staff. There is something almost like a public-welfare function being performed: the rule exists, and the rabbis are demonstrating it by personal example and explicit teaching.

### Rabbi Tarfon Story

The third story is the most famous and most ethically complex. A certain man found Rabbi Tarfon in his field at the time when most of the knives had been put away, eating from the produce. The man put Rabbi Tarfon into a sack, hoisted him up, and was dragging him toward the river to throw him in — evidently under the impression that he had finally caught the thief who had been stealing from his vineyard all year. Rabbi Tarfon, from inside the sack, called out: *oi lo l'Tarfon sheze horgo* — woe to Tarfon, for this man is going to kill him. Upon hearing the name, the man dropped the sack and fled.

The rest of Rabbi Tarfon's life was shadowed by anguish over this incident. As Rabbi Abahu reports in the name of Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel: *kol yamav shel oto tzaddik haya mitzta'er al davar ze* — that righteous man was distressed about this matter for the rest of his days. His stated reason was: *oi li shenishtamashti b'kitra shel Torah* — woe to me that I used the crown of Torah. He felt he had no right to invoke his identity as Rabbi Tarfon to extricate himself, that some other means should have been found.

### Kitra Shel Torah

The Gemara buttresses this with a principle stated by Rabbah bar Bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *kol hamishtamesh b'kitra shel Torah ne'ekar min ha'olam* — anyone who uses the crown of Torah for his own purposes is uprooted from the world. The proof is a *kal vachomer* from Belshatzar. Belshatzar used the sacred vessels of the Beit Hamikdash — yet those vessels, the Gemara notes, had already been de-sanctified, as derived from the verse *u'va'u vah pritzim v'chililuha*: once the Beit Hamikdash was breached, its vessels became *klei chol*. The principle *kivan she'partzu na'asu chol* — once they have been broken into, they become ordinary — is a significant halachic principle with implications in many areas, including the status of synagogues that have been seized. Nevertheless, even though the vessels Belshatzar used had already been de-sanctified, he was killed for it that very night — *babeliyo kteil Belshatzar*. The *kal vachomer*: if the Torah itself — which can *never* be de-sanctified, which is *shechai v'kayam l'olam* — is misused, *al achat kama v'kama*, how much more severe the consequence.

The Gemara then raises a difficulty: given that Rabbi Tarfon was eating *during* the permissible period of *huflu rov hamaktzaot*, why was the man attacking him in the first place? The Gemara answers: *mishum dehu havei ganvi lei anveizi kol shatah* — that man had been having his grapes stolen throughout the year by someone, and when he stumbled upon Rabbi Tarfon, he was convinced he had finally caught his thief. The legality of *huflu rov hamaktzaot* was therefore irrelevant from the man's perspective — he thought he was dealing with a year-round criminal.

This sharpens the question of Rabbi Tarfon's anguish. If the man believed Rabbi Tarfon was a common thief and would not have been persuaded by a simple offer of payment or an invocation of the halachic rule, what alternative did Rabbi Tarfon have? The Gemara answers: *mishum de'Rabbi Tarfon ashir gadol havei, havei lei le-fayaso bi-damei* — Rabbi Tarfon was a very wealthy man. He could have offered substantial compensation, enough to satisfy the man for whatever had been stolen throughout the year. He was not compelled to identify himself as a great Torah sage. That, the Gemara concludes, is what he should have tried first, and his failure to explore that avenue — reaching instead for the announcement of his identity — is what caused him lasting remorse.

## Status & Identity

### Learning Motivation

A *braita* is now cited that addresses the proper motivation for Torah study, grounding the broader discussion in the *pasuk*: *le-ahavah et Hashem Elokecha lishmoa be-kolo u-le-davkah bo* — to love God, to hear His voice, to cleave to Him. A person should not say: I will study Torah so that people will call me a *chacham*. Nor: I will study Mishnah so that they will call me *rebbi*. Nor: I will study so that I will become an elder who sits in the *yeshivah* — though this motivation is somewhat different, since it may be more about the pleasure of learning than the honor it brings. Rather, one should *lemod me-ahavah* — learn out of love. And: *ve-sof ha-kavod lavo* — in the end, the honor will come. As the *pesukim* say: *koshrem al etzbe'otecha kotvem al luach libecha* — bind them to your fingers, inscribe them on the tablet of your heart — and *deracheiha darchei no'am*, its ways are ways of pleasantness, and *etz chayim hi lamachazikim bah*, it is a tree of life for those who hold fast to it.

The teaching does not deny the well-known principle that one should always learn even *shelo lishmah*, since *mitoch shelo lishmah ba lishmah* — from learning for ulterior motives, one arrives at learning for its own sake. But the ideal, clearly, is *lishmah* — learning for love of God and devotion to the Torah itself, not for the status it confers.

### Elazar's Teaching

Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok adds: *aseh devarim leshem pa'alam* — do things for the sake of their Maker — *ve-daber bahem lishmam* — and speak of them for their own sake. *Al ta'asem atarah lehitgadel bahem* — do not make them a crown for self-aggrandizement — *ve-al ta'asem kardom lihyot oder bahem* — and do not make them an agricultural tool, a spade to dig with. The same *kal vachomer* from Belshatzar follows: Belshatzar used vessels that had already been de-sanctified and was destroyed. One who uses the crown of Torah — which is never de-sanctified — *al achat kama v'kama*.

### Rava's Position

Against this background, Rava introduces a seemingly divergent position: *shari leih le-inish le-odoei nafshieh be-atra de-la yad'ei leih* — it is permitted for a person, and specifically for a *tzurba meirabanan*, to make himself known in a place where people do not know who he is. The proof-text is the verse in which *Ovadyah* identified himself to *Eliyahu*, saying *ve-avdecha yere et Hashem mi-ne'urav* — your servant has feared God since his youth. Ovadyah was in a situation where his identity and history were directly relevant, and he made them known. So too, Rava holds, a Torah scholar who arrives in an unknown town may identify himself rather than remaining anonymous. The line between appropriate self-identification and misuse of the Torah's crown is real but not always obvious. If a scholar comes to an inn and no one knows who he is, he may let it be known. This seems qualitatively different from exploiting one's status to override a just claim against oneself — but exactly where the line falls is not immediately clear from Rava's formulation.

### Known vs. Unknown

The Gemara itself then raises the tension with the Rabbi Tarfon story: but Rabbi Tarfon also identified himself in a place where the man did not know him — and he was anguished about it for the rest of his life. The Gemara's response returns to the argument already established: *inash gadol hayah va-hava leih le-payusei bi-dmei* — he was a great and wealthy man and could have offered compensation. But this still leaves Rava's position somewhat open-ended. Is it only permissible to identify oneself when there is no alternative available? That seems like an overly narrow reading of Rava's ruling, which appears to permit self-identification more broadly.

Rava himself then addresses an apparent internal contradiction in his position. The verse says *yehalelucha zar velo picha* — let others praise you, not your own mouth. How does that square with permitting self-identification? The Gemara resolves this cleanly: *ha b'atra d'yad'i lei ha b'atra d'lo yad'i lei* — in a place where people know who you are, you need not say anything; others will establish your identity, and the information will spread on its own. But in a place where no one knows you, you may speak on your own behalf. Rava does not appear to feel fully constrained by the Gemara's treatment of the Rabbi Tarfon narrative; his position seems more permissive — that a Torah scholar is genuinely entitled to acknowledgment and to the honor that properly accrues to his station, and that making this known is not the same as *mishtamesh b'kitra shel Torah*.

### Ran's View

The Ran resolves the tension with a principled distinction. He explains: the problem with what Rabbi Tarfon did was not that he identified himself per se. In the Ran's reading, the man, upon hearing the name Rabbi Tarfon, did not let him go out of a conviction that he was innocent — he let him go because he was unwilling to harm so prominent a Torah figure, even though he still believed Rabbi Tarfon had been stealing from him all year. That is *mishtamesh b'kitra shel Torah*: using Torah status to escape the *consequences* of something one is actually being held responsible for, regardless of whether that responsibility is just or unjust.

The Ran then draws the critical line: *liftor atzmo b'chvod Torah bamah she'eino chayav bo* — to use the honor of Torah to exempt oneself from something one is genuinely not obligated to do — that is permitted. And the examples that follow in the Gemara are all cases of legitimate entitlement. A *talmid chacham* may say he will not pay the poll tax — *lo yehavna karga* — because Torah scholars are genuinely exempt from that tax. He may say his case should be heard first at the court — *sharo li tigrai bereisha* — because that is an established right of Torah scholars, derived from *u-vnei David kohanim hayu*: just as a *kohen* receives priority — *kohen notel b'rosh* — derived from *v'kidashto* and developed by the school of Rabbi Yishmael to mean priority in reading Torah, in leading *Birkat Hamazon*, and in receiving the first and finest portion — so too does a *talmid chacham* receive priority. These are not instances of misusing the crown; they are the crown's proper exercise.

The Ran thus formulates the principle as follows: a *talmid chacham* may use his status for the benefits it genuinely entitles him to, just as *kohanim* and *leviyim* are entitled to *terumah* and *ma'aser*. What he may not do is use that status to get out of something that is legitimately being claimed against him — to escape accountability under cover of Torah honor. That is the authentic misuse. This reading has an elegant coherence, though it inevitably leaves open the further question of precisely what the entitlements of a *talmid chacham* are — a question the Gemara does not fully resolve but whose general contours are suggested by the examples cited.

## Tax Exemptions

### Poll Tax Exempt

Having articulated the principle through the Ran's framework, the Gemara now turns explicitly to the material benefits to which *talmidei chachamim* are entitled. Rava states: *shari lei l'tzurba meirabanan l'meimar lo yehavna karga* — a Torah scholar may declare that he will not pay the poll tax. The scriptural basis is the verse from Ezra: *minda blo vahalach lo shalit lemirmei aleihon* — it is not permitted to impose upon them. Rav Yehuda identifies each term: *minda zu manat hamelech* — a tribute to the king; *blo zu kesef gulgaltah* — the poll tax, literally a head tax; *vehalach zu arnona* — a third category, variously interpreted. The Ran understands *arnona* as some form of obligation to provision the king and his armies when they pass through. Tosafot, drawing on Latin etymology, understands it as corvée — forced labor. The JPS translation renders the three terms as tribute, head tax, and land tax.

This exemption has ancient and widespread precedent. Religious authorities and institutions were routinely exempted from taxation by ruling powers throughout history — from the account in the Yosef narrative, through the Persian edict in Ezra, through medieval European practice in which the Church enjoyed tax-exempt status. The practical dynamic often involved negotiation: the Jewish community would establish before the governing authority that these individuals served a religious function, and the exemption would follow from the recognized status of the religious class.

### Servant of Flame

Rava adds a further ruling: *shari lei l'tzurba meirabanan l'meimra avda d'nura ana* — it is permitted for a Torah scholar to say "I am a servant of the flame," in the context of the Zoroastrian Babylonian environment where a sacred, maintained flame was central to religious worship. By saying this, one would be understood by the tax authorities as belonging to or serving the Zoroastrian priestly class, whose members were also exempt from the poll tax. The result: *lo yavina acharga* — I will not pay the poll tax. The Gemara explains: *mai ta'ama l'salukei aria d'minei ka'amar* — he is only saying this to escape the lion pressing him for money, to avoid a payment he is in principle not obligated to make.

This is a striking ruling. The declaration creates the impression in the minds of the tax collectors that the speaker is affiliated with *avodat zarah*. This is not a small or casual misrepresentation. Relevant to the broader halachic literature is the question of whether such ambiguous speech is permissible in more extreme circumstances — such as to conceal one's Jewish identity during persecution — and this Gemara has been cited in that context.

### Ran's Justification

The Ran addresses the obvious difficulty. He offers two lines of reasoning. First, *de-mucha milsa de-amar hachi le-ashtomei mikarga* — it is transparent that the speaker is only saying this to evade the tax; the statement is not a sincere confession of religious identity, and its instrumental character is evident. But evident to whom? If it is evident to the non-Jewish tax collectors, why would the stratagem work? And if it is only evident to Jewish observers, the first justification is weaker than it appears.

The Ran therefore adds: a scholar does not become suspect of *avodat kochavim* simply because tax collectors take his ambiguous statement at face value. The scholar knows he is referring to God, who is described as *eish ochlah* — a consuming fire. The phrase *avda d'nura* is technically true of every servant of God. The gap between what the speaker intends and what the hearer understands is what permits the statement. Nevertheless, the Ran also notes that the ruling is specifically about a *talmid chacham* who is in principle exempt from the tax — he is not cheating the system, only asserting an exemption through an indirect and ambiguous formulation. Whether someone who is not a *talmid chacham* could use such language to evade a tax obligation they genuinely bear is another question entirely and is not resolved here.

## Rav Ashi Forest

### Rav Ashi Case

The Gemara now introduces a case that moves from the question of tax evasion to the broader parameters of *lifnei iver* — the prohibition on placing a stumbling block before the blind, which extends to facilitating another's sin. Rav Ashi had a forest — *havah lei hahu appa* — and he sold it to *bei nura*, the house of fire, that is, to Zoroastrians, presumably the priests or the institution associated with the sacred flame. Ravina challenged him: *haika lifnei iver lo titein michshol* — does this not violate the prohibition of *lifnei iver*? You are providing them with the very material they will use in their religious worship, which constitutes *avodat zarah* — and facilitating a Noahide in committing one of the seven prohibitions of *bnei Noach* is itself forbidden.

### Rav Ashi Defense

Rav Ashi responded: *rov etzim le-haskata ninhu* — most wood is purchased for heating and cooking, not for religious ritual purposes. Even if the buyers are Zoroastrians whose religion centers on a sacred fire, one cannot assume that every piece of wood sold to them will be used for religious worship rather than for ordinary domestic needs. Since the majority of wood is used for mundane purposes, Rav Ashi held that he bore no halachic responsibility for how this particular batch would be used.

The question of where exactly the sale was made — whether to a private buyer who happened to be Zoroastrian or to the religious institution itself — is worth noting. *Bei nura* as a designation suggests the institutional context, which might strengthen Ravina's concern. But even so, Rav Ashi maintained that *rov etzim le-haskata ninhu* provides sufficient cover.

### Ashkenazi Context

This Gemara is directly relevant to a well-documented feature of medieval Ashkenazi Jewish economic life: Jewish merchants commonly sold goods to the Church and to Christian clergy, including clearly religious objects such as chalices, liturgical vestments, and other ritual items. How could this be squared with *lifnei iver* and the prohibition on facilitating *avodat zarah*? Several justifications were developed in the *rishonim* to address this reality.

One approach, which developed in the period of the Tosafot, was to argue that Christians are not truly sincere in their religious practice — that it is not genuine *avodat zarah* in the full sense. This is of course historically implausible, and the argument becomes even weaker when applied to sales made directly to clergy and to the institutional Church rather than to lay individuals. A later development, associated with the period of the Rema and drawing on a Tosafot, was the position that Christianity constitutes *avodat zarah* for Jews but not for non-Jews — a theologically controversial and legally significant distinction. Another argument was that since the Church could obtain the same goods from any number of other suppliers, the Jewish merchant's contribution was not the determining cause of the sin. As noted, however, this reasoning is difficult to sustain: the fact that someone else could supply the forbidden item does not generally exempt one from *lifnei iver*, and this Gemara itself seems to assume otherwise, since wood was presumably available from other sources and the concern for *lifnei iver* was raised nonetheless.

### Tosafot Ruling

The Tosafot on this passage address the limits of the *rov etzim* principle directly. Tosafot states: *heicha de'ika lifnei iver* — in cases where *lifnei iver* genuinely applies — *she'ein lahem heter* — where there is no permissible use — the prohibition is real. Tosafot then derives: *u-mi-kan yesh lomar she'assur lehalvot ma'ot le-tzorech binyan avodat kochavim* — it is forbidden to lend money for the construction of a church — *o le-tzorech tachshiteha* — or for its ornamental furnishings. *Ve-chol sheken limkor lahem meshamshei ve-divrei tiflut* — and certainly one may not sell them actual implements of worship and what the Tosafot calls *divrei tiflut* (a pointed play on *tefillot*) — such as *gvi'im* (chalices), *machtot* (censers), and *sefarim* (books, meaning liturgical texts). Tosafot concludes: *veha-mone'a yatzliach* — one who is able to refrain will be rewarded. The phrasing is telling: Tosafot acknowledges that the practice was widespread and that calling for complete abstention was aspirational rather than enforceable — but the halachic position is unambiguous. Selling these items is *assur*.

## Geographic Vows

### Mountain vs. Valley

The final mishna of the daf addresses how the terms of a vow are interpreted when the person's location at the time of the vow differs from the location where the vow's term will be fulfilled. The mishna establishes: *hakol lefi mekom nidro* — everything follows the place of the vow. *Im hayah bahar bahar, im hayah bebikah bebikah* — if one was on a mountain, we follow the mountain; if in a valley, we follow the valley. The different elevations experience different agricultural rhythms, and the *neder* tracks the environmental context in which it was formulated. A *braita* applies this specifically to *kayitz*: if one vowed *ad hakayitz* while in the Galil, and then descended to the valleys, even though the *kayitz* season has arrived in the valleys, the prohibition continues — *assur ad sheyagia kayitz ba-Galil* — until the *kayitz* comes in the Galil.

### Second Rain

The mishna continues: *ad hageshamim*, until the rains — or *ad sheyehu geshamim*, until the rains occur — is interpreted as *ad shetered reviah shniyah*, until the second rainfall descends. The second rain is treated as the onset of the rainy season proper, the point at which one can say the rains have genuinely arrived.

### Rain Season

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel qualifies this: *ad sheyagia zmanah shel reviah* — until the time of the second rain arrives, even if it has not actually fallen. This is a realistic concession to meteorological variability: in a dry year, the rains may be significantly delayed, and a vow "until the rains" should not remain binding indefinitely on the theoretical possibility that rain may still come.

### When Rain Ends

The mishna then addresses the other end of the rainy season: *ad sheyifseku geshamim* — until the rains end. Rabbi Meir holds: *ad sheyetzei Nissan kulo* — until the end of Nissan. Even though Pesach marks the practical end of the rainy season, rains can continue intermittently into Nissan, and Rabbi Meir calibrates the terminus at the end of that month. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *ad sheya'avor ha-Pesach* — until Pesach has passed. We stop reciting *mashiv haruach umorid hageshem* before the end of Pesach, but actual rain can extend beyond that point; Rabbi Yehuda nonetheless sets the end of the rainy season at the conclusion of Pesach.

### Galil to Valley

The *braita* cited in the Gemara makes the geographic principle concrete. A person who vowed *ad hakayitz baGalil* — until the *kayitz* in the Galil — and then descended to the valleys remains bound by his vow even when the *kayitz* arrives in the valleys, until *sheyagia kayitz baGalil*. One might equally ask the reverse: if someone vowed while in a valley and later went to the mountains where the *kayitz* comes later, would we apply stringency or leniency? The mishna's general principle — *hakol lefi mekom nidro* — would suggest that the place of the vow determines the reference point in both directions.

### Singular/Plural

Rabbi Zeira introduces a linguistic distinction that resolves the *machloket* between the *tanna kamma* and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel regarding the rain terms. The dispute, Rabbi Zeira explains, applies specifically when one says *ad hageshamim* — the plural form, referring to the rains. In that case, there is a question of whether the term means the actual rains must fall (*reviah shniyah*) or whether the rainy season's arrival is sufficient (Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel). But if one says *ad hageshem* — the singular, "the rain" — Rabbi Zeira holds that everyone would agree: *ad zman geshamim ka'amar* — this refers to the season of rain, not to an actual rainfall event. The singular form, in Rabbi Zeira's reading, evokes the concept of the rainy season more abstractly, and there is no dispute about its meaning. The plural *geshamim* is more ambiguous — it could be pointing to actual precipitation events — and that ambiguity is what generates the disagreement between the *tanna kamma* and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.
