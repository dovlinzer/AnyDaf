# Nazir 40 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shiurim: Hairs & Implements

### Rav Chisda: Shiurim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לִלְקוֹת — בְּאַחַת, לְעַכֵּב — בִּשְׁתַּיִם, לִסְתּוֹר — אֵינוֹ סוֹתֵר אֶלָּא בְּרוֹב רֹאשׁוֹ וּבְתַעַר.
>
> **Translation:** § Concerning a nazirite who shaves his hair, **Rav Ḥisda says:** With regard **to flogging,** a nazirite who shaved his hair is flogged **for** shaving even **one** hair; with regard **to invalidation,** i.e., the ritual shaving of his hair at the end of his naziriteship, the shaving is **invalidated** if he failed **to** remove **two** hairs; and as **for negating** his naziriteship, shaving during his term **negates** it **only if** he shaved **most of his head. And** this applies only if he removed his hair **with a razor.**

The daf opens in the middle of the discussion concerning the prohibition against a *nazir* cutting his hair. The Torah states *ta'ar lo ya'avor al rosho*—a razor shall not pass over his head—and the preceding sugya examined whether this prohibition encompasses implements other than a literal razor. By way of summary: one *beraita* derived from the *pasuk* of *kadosh yihyeh gadel perah se'ar rosho*, a *mitzvat aseh*, that even methods falling short of a razor are included in some form of prohibition. Tosafot understood this to cover even a trim that is not *ke'ein ta'ar*—not close to the root—constituting a violation of the *aseh* but not of the *lo ta'aseh*. A second *beraita* extended the *lo ta'aseh* to anything resembling a razor, reading *lo ya'avor al rosho* as encompassing any *ke'ein ta'ar*. Rabbi Yonatan, by contrast, held that only a literal razor triggers any biblical prohibition at all; anything else is entirely exempt. Rebbe read *ta'ar* literally, yet the Gemara's conclusion is that even Rebbe would agree that something resembling a razor entails a *lo ta'aseh*, while using a razor itself produces two *lavin*—*ta'ar* and *lo ya'avor*.

With that framework in place, the Gemara turns from the question of which implements are prohibited to the question of how many hairs must be involved for various legal consequences. Rav Chisda teaches: to incur lashes (*lalkot*), even cutting a single hair suffices—*ta'ar lo ya'avor al rosho* does not require removing all of one's hair. To prevent fulfillment of the *mitzvah* (*le'akev*)—that is, when a *nazir* shaves at the conclusion of his *nezirut* in order to bring his *korbanot*—he must remove virtually all his hair, and leaving two hairs means the *mitzvah* has not been fulfilled, whereas leaving only one hair is close enough that it counts as having shaved the entirety. Tosafot notes that these *shiurim* are *halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*: they are transmitted measures without a derivable textual reason, though there is a certain internal logic to each.

### Soter: Rov Rosho

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּתַעַר — אִין, בְּמִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא — לָא? וְהָקָתָנֵי: מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת אֶת כׇּל הַמַּעֲבִירִין! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: כְּעֵין תַּעַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: This indicates that according to the opinion of Rav Ḥisda, a nazirite who shaved **with a razor, yes,** he negates thirty days, whereas if he did so **with anything else, no,** he does not negate days. **But isn't it taught: From where** is it derived **to include all** implements **that remove** hair, i.e., that a nazirite is liable if he uses any of them? The verse states: "Shall not come upon his head," meaning in any manner. This indicates that a nazirite must add days to his term of naziriteship even if he shaved with an implement other than a razor. **Rather, say** that Rav Ḥisda meant: **In the manner of a razor.** That is, the nazirite negates his naziriteship only if he removes his hair as one does with a razor, without leaving any part of it.

A third category is *listor*—interfering with the count of the *nezirut*. As discussed previously, the Rambam holds that shaving obligates the *nazir* to wait thirty days, while Tosafot holds that the *nazir* must ensure thirty days of growth before the conclusion of his *nezirut*. Taking Tosafot's approach, the question of *soter* is essentially whether a given cutting disrupts the growth record sufficiently to require additional days. The Gemara teaches: *eino soter ela be-rov rosho*—it only constitutes *soter* if the majority of the head is shaved. This makes sense within Tosafot's framework: if only a minority of hairs were removed, the majority still carries the requisite thirty days of growth, and the shaving of the minority does not undermine the *nezirut* as a whole.

### Soter: Razors Only?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: נָזִיר שֶׁתָּלַשׁ, מֵירַט, סִיפְסֵף כׇּל שֶׁהוּא — אֵינוֹ סוֹתֵר אֶלָּא בְּרוֹב רֹאשׁוֹ וּבְתַעַר. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁשְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת מְעַכְּבוֹת בּוֹ, כָּךְ שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת סוֹתְרוֹת בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **This** opinion **is also taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 4:3): With regard to **a nazirite who tore out, uprooted,** or **pulled out any amount** of hair, this **negates** days of his naziriteship **only if** he shaved **most of his head, and** only if he did so **in** the manner of **a razor. Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Shimon: Just as** leaving **two hairs invalidates his** shaving of ritual purity at the end of his term, and the act is considered incomplete, **so too,** leaving a mere **two hairs negates** days of **his** naziriteship, if he shaved during his term.

The word *u-ve-ta'ar*—"and with a razor"—appears at the end of the *beraita*. The question arises: to which of the three cases does this word attach? It clearly applies to the *mitzvah* of shaving at the conclusion of *nezirut*, and presumably applies as well to the *lo ta'aseh*. But what of *soter*? Is it only when the *nazir* shaves with a razor that his count is disrupted, or does even shaving by other means—a depilatory, for example—produce *soter*? Tosafot argues that the razor requirement attaches only to the prohibition that results in lashes, not to *soter*. However, the plain sense of the *beraita* appears to condition all three categories on the razor.

### Gemara Challenges

The Gemara challenges the implication that only a razor can produce a violation: *be-ta'ar in, be-midi achrini lo?*—only with a razor and with nothing else? The Gemara then cites a *beraita*: *minyan le-rabbot et kol ha-ma'avirin*—how do we know to include anything that removes the hair from the prohibition of *lo ya'avor al rosho*?

### Ke'ein Ta'ar

The Gemara resolves: *ela eima ke'ein ta'ar*—rather, read it as anything resembling a razor. This is the category invoked by the earlier *beraitot* when discussing *tolesh*, *miret*, *sifsif*, and similar methods. Tosafot understands all of these prior discussions as converging on this point: the *lav* extends to anything functionally analogous to a razor, while a mere trim not at the root would be an *aseh* only.

### Second Braita

The Gemara then cites a second *beraita*: *ve-tanya nami hachi u-ve-ta'ar—tolesh, miret, ve-sifsif kol she-hu*, and if a *nazir* pulled out, plucked, or otherwise removed even the smallest amount of hair—even one hair—he is *chayav*, and *eino soter ela be-rov rosho*: it only constitutes *soter* with the majority of his head. The word *u-ve-ta'ar* here appears attached to the condition of *soter*, which would suggest on the plain reading that *soter* requires specifically a razor. Tosafot reads otherwise, but acknowledges the plain sense favors the other interpretation. Notably, the plain sense of this *beraita* establishes that the threshold for transgression is lower than the threshold for *soter*: one hair suffices to transgress, while *soter* requires the majority of the head—and perhaps a razor as well.

### R. Shimon ben Yehuda

Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda, citing Rabbi Shimon, disputes the majority-of-the-head requirement for *soter*. He reasons: just as leaving two hairs prevents the *nazir* from being considered to have fulfilled the *mitzvah* of shaving—because two hairs still represent meaningful remaining hair—so too does removing two hairs constitute something significant enough to be *soter*, without requiring the majority of the head. The analogy is admittedly strained: leaving two hairs means the *mitzvah* is not yet complete because almost everything remains, whereas Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda's conclusion is that removing two hairs is a significant act. Yet this is the position he maintains.

## Three Shavings: Torah

### Three Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: שְׁלֹשָׁה מְגַלְּחִין, וְתִגְלַחְתָּן מִצְוָה: נָזִיר, וּמְצוֹרָע, וּלְוִיִּם. וְכוּלָּן שֶׁגִּילְּחוּ שֶׁלֹּא בְּתַעַר, אוֹ שֶׁשִּׁיְּירוּ שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת — לֹא עָשׂוּ וְלֹא כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** § **We learned** in a mishna **there** (*Nega'im* 14:4): **Three** types of people must **shave** their hair, **and their shaving** is **a mitzva: A nazirite; and a leper,** as it is stated: "And he who is to be cleansed shall wash his clothes, and shave off all his hair" (Leviticus 14:8–9). **And** the third category is **Levites,** when they were first sanctified for their service, as it is stated: "And this you shall do to them to cleanse them: Sprinkle the water of purification upon them, and let them cause a razor to pass over all their flesh" (Numbers 8:7). **And** with regard to **all of them, if they shaved with** an implement **other than a razor, or if they left two hairs** uncut, **they have done nothing,** i.e., they have not fulfilled their obligation.

The Gemara now transitions to examine the three instances in the Torah where shaving is a *mitzvah*: the *nazir*, the *metzora*, and the *levi'im*. Each warrants separate examination. By the *levi'im*, the obligation is most explicit: *ve-he'eviru ta'ar al kol besaram*—they passed a razor over their entire body. By the *nazir*, the shaving at conclusion is expressed as *ve-gilach ha-nazir*, without an explicit mention of a razor at that point, though the earlier prohibition of *ta'ar lo ya'avor al rosho ad melot ha-yamim* implies that the razor is the implement used at the conclusion. By the *metzora*, the Torah states *ve-hayah ba-yom ha-shevi'i yigaleach et kol se'aro—et rosho ve-et zekano ve-et gabot einav ve-et kol se'aro yigaleach*—all of his hair, including beard and eyebrows, but without any mention of a razor.

### Mishnah: All Three

The Gemara introduces a Mishnah from *Nega'im*: *shlosha megalchin ve-tiglachtan mitzvah*—three categories of persons shave and their shaving is a *mitzvah*: a *nazir*, a *metzora*, and the *levi'im*. The Mishnah continues: *ve-chulan she-gilchu she-lo be-ta'ar o she-shiyaru shtei se'arot lo asu ve-lo klum*—if any of them shaved without a razor or left two hairs remaining, they accomplished nothing. From this Mishnah we derive two halakhot: first, all three require a razor; second, leaving two hairs is enough to invalidate the shaving.

### Why Mention Mitzvah?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: שְׁלֹשָׁה מְגַלְּחִין וְתִגְלַחְתָּן מִצְוָה. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא מִשּׁוּם עַבּוֹרֵי שֵׂעָר הוּא, וַאֲפִילּוּ סָךְ נָשָׁא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this mishna. **The Master said** there: **Three** types of people must **shave** and cut their hair, **and their shaving** is **a mitzva.** The Gemara asks: This **is obvious;** after all, doesn't the Torah command all three to shave? The Gemara answers: The statement of the mishna is necessary, **lest you say** that the mitzva **is due to** and aimed at the **removal of hair, and** therefore **even one who applied a depilatory [*nasha*]** to remove his hair has fulfilled his obligation. The mishna therefore **teaches us that** this is **not** so, as the mitzva must be performed by shaving.

The Gemara asks: *peshita*—is it not obvious that the shaving of these three is a *mitzvah*? Why state it explicitly? The Gemara answers: one might have thought that the essential requirement is merely the *end result*—that the person be rendered hairless—such that even applying a depilatory lotion (*nasha*) would suffice. The Mishnah therefore teaches that it is not the end result that matters but the act itself, and that act must constitute proper shaving. The distinction between *act* and *result* is fundamental here: one might have supposed the Torah's concern is simply that hair be absent, but the Mishnah establishes that the act of shaving is itself the *mitzvah*.

### Nazir: Ta'ar Explicit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי: וְכוּלָּן שֶׁגִּילְּחוּ שֶׁלֹּא בְּתַעַר. בִּשְׁלָמָא גַּבֵּי נָזִיר, כְּתִיב: ״תַּעַר לֹא יַעֲבוֹר עַל רֹאשׁוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna further **teaches: And** with regard to **all of them, if they shaved with** an implement **other than a razor,** they have done nothing. The Gemara asks: **Granted, with regard to a nazirite** the source of this *halakha* is clear, as **it is written: "A razor shall not come upon his head"** (Numbers 6:5), which indicates that when he does shave he must do so with a razor.

By the *nazir*, the Gemara notes: *bishlama gabei Nazir ketiv ta'ar lo ya'avor al rosho*—the Torah explicitly mentions a razor in the context of the *nazir*'s prohibition, and from this we derive that the *mitzvah* of shaving at the conclusion is also with a razor.

### Levi'im: Ta'ar Explicit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְגַבֵּי לְוִיִּם כְּתִיב: ״וְהֶעֱבִירוּ תַעַר עַל כׇּל בְּשָׂרָם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** similarly, **with regard to** the **Levites it is written: "And let them cause a razor to pass over all their flesh"** (Numbers 8:7).

By the *levi'im*, the derivation is even more straightforward: *ve-gabei Levi'im ketiv ve-he'eviru ta'ar al kol besaram*—the Torah explicitly states they passed a razor over their entire body.

### Metzora: Ta'ar Source?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מְצוֹרָע בְּתַעַר, מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** **However, from where do we** derive that **a leper** must be shaved **with a razor** for his ritual purification?

By contrast, by the *metzora*, the Torah never mentions *ta'ar* at all. *Ela metzora be-ta'ar me-nalan*—how do we know the *metzora* uses a razor? This is the question that will occupy much of the remainder of the sugya. It is worth noting that the *beraita* cited earlier in the tractate, which we studied previously, simply took it for granted that a *metzora* uses a razor and therefore ruled that you cannot learn the *nazir*'s razor requirement from the *metzora* since the *metzora* is a stricter case, covering the whole body. The Gemara now probes where that assumption came from.

## Tzad Hashaveh: Logic

### From Levi'im?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא, תֵּיתֵי מִלְּוִיִּם: מָה לְוִיִּם שֶׁכֵּן טְעוּנִין תִּגְלַחַת, וְאֵין תִּגְלַחְתָּן אֶלָּא בְּתַעַר — אַף אֲנִי אָבִיא אֶת הַמְצוֹרָע שֶׁהוּא טָעוּן תִּגְלַחַת, וְאֵין תִּגְלַחְתּוֹ אֶלָּא בְּתַעַר.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that this *halakha* **is derived from** the case of the **Levites,** as, **just as Levites require shaving and their shaving is only with a razor, so too, I will bring** the case of **a leper, who requires shaving and** say that **his shaving** can likewise be performed **only with a razor,**

The Gemara first attempts to derive the *metzora*'s razor requirement from the *levi'im* by analogy: just as *levi'im* are required to shave and that shaving requires a razor, so too the *metzora* who is required to shave should also use a razor.

### Levi'im Distinct

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לִלְוִיִּם שֶׁכֵּן טְעוּנִין תְּנוּפָה בְּגוּפָם, תֹּאמַר בִּמְצוֹרָע דְּלָא!
>
> **Translation:** then this comparison **can be refuted. What** is unique **about Levites** is **that** they have an extra stringency, in that **they require waving of their bodies,** i.e., Aaron was required to pick up and wave the bodies of the Levites as part of the ritual of their sanctification (see Numbers 8:11). Will **you say** the same **with regard to a leper, who** does **not** require waving?

The Gemara immediately refutes this attempt: *mah Levi'im she-kein te'unim tenufa be-gufam*—the *levi'im* undergo the unique rite of having their own bodies waved (*tenufa*), a distinctive element of their initiation that marks a considerably more demanding ritual context. Since *metzora* involves no such *tenufa*, the analogy fails.

### From Nazir?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא תֵּיתֵי מִנָּזִיר: מָה לְנָזִיר שֶׁכֵּן קׇרְבָּנוֹ טָעוּן לֶחֶם, תֹּאמַר בִּמְצוֹרָע דְּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the *halakha* that a leper must use a razor is **derived from** the case of **a nazirite,** who does not require waving either. However, this comparison can also be refuted: **What** is unique **about the nazirite** is **that his offering requires bread. Will you say** the same **with regard to a leper,** who does **not** require bread as part of his purification process?

The Gemara then attempts to learn from the *nazir*: if even a *nazir*, whose shaving obligation covers only his head, requires a razor, then surely a *metzora*, whose obligation covers the entire body and is therefore seemingly more stringent, should certainly require a razor as well. This appears to be a strong *kal va-chomer*.

### Nazir Distinct

The Gemara refutes this as well: *mah le-Nazir she-kein korbano ta'un lechem*—the *nazir*'s *korban* includes bread (*lechem*, i.e., the *matzah* accompaniment), which is a feature absent from the *metzora*'s *korban*. While this distinction may seem tangential to the question of shaving, the *nazir*'s *korban* is in some respect more demanding, which means the cases are not sufficiently analogous to permit a direct derivation.

### Common Denominator

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵחֲדָא לָא אָתְיָא, תֵּיתֵי מִתַּרְוֵיהוֹן. מֵהֵי תֵּיתֵי? תֵּיתֵי מִלְּוִיִּם, מָה לִלְוִיִּם שֶׁכֵּן טְעוּנִין תְּנוּפָה בְּגוּפָם. נָזִיר יוֹכִיחַ, מָה לְנָזִיר שֶׁכֵּן קׇרְבָּנוֹ טָעוּן לֶחֶם. לְוִיִּם יוֹכִיחוּ. וְחָזַר הַדִּין, לֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה, וְלֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה. הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶם שֶׁהֵן טְעוּנִין תִּגְלַחַת, וְתִגְלַחְתָּן בְּתַעַר. אַף אֲנִי אָבִיא אֶת הַמְצוֹרָע שֶׁהוּא טָעוּן תִּגְלַחַת, וְתִגְלַחְתּוֹ בְּתַעַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** clearly the *halakha* of a leper is **not derived** from either **one** of the above cases, that of the Levites or the nazirite. Therefore, **let it be derived from the two of them.** The Gemara elaborates: As stated previously, **from which** single case **can it be derived?** If you say it **can be derived from** the **Levites, what** is unique **about Levites** is **that they require waving of their bodies.** This suggestion can be countered by saying that the case of **a nazirite proves** that this stringency is not the decisive factor leading to the requirement of a razor, as a nazirite is not waved and yet he must be shaved with a razor. And if you respond: **What** is unique **about the nazirite** is **that his offering requires bread,** one can similarly argue that the **Levites prove** that this stringency does not lead to the *halakha* of shaving with a razor, as the offering of the Levites does not require bread and nevertheless they must be shaved with a razor. **And** in this manner **the derivation has reverted** to its starting point. However, at this stage the *halakha* is derived from a combination of the two sources: **The aspect of this** case **is not like the aspect of that** case, **and the aspect of that is not like the aspect of this;** each case has its own special features. **The common denominator is that they require shaving and their shaving is with a razor.** Therefore, **I will also bring** the case of **the leper, who requires shaving, and** say that **his shaving** must be **with a razor.**

The Gemara then attempts a *tzad ha-shaveh*—a derivation from the common denominator of both cases: both the *nazir* and the *levi'im* require shaving and both require a razor; therefore, any obligation to shave should require a razor, and the *metzora* should be included. The argument is structured as the classic back-and-forth of Talmudic derivation: begin with *levi'im*, note that their *tenufa* distinguishes them, invoke *nazir* to show that *tenufa* is not the critical factor since *nazir* also uses a razor without *tenufa*, note that *nazir* has bread in his *korban* which *levi'im* do not, and conclude that *lo re'i zeh ke-re'i zeh*—neither case is exactly like the other. The *tzad ha-shaveh*—the common denominator—is that *sh'neihem te'unim tiglachat ve-tiglachtan be-ta'ar*: both require shaving and both require a razor. Apply this to the *metzora*, who also requires shaving, and conclude that his shaving too must be with a razor.

### Metzora: Sliding Scale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא מִבַּרְנִישׁ לְרַב אָשֵׁי, וְלִיפְרוֹךְ: מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן, שֶׁכֵּן אֵין קׇרְבָּנוֹ בְּדַלּוּת. תֹּאמַר בִּמְצוֹרָע שֶׁקׇּרְבָּנוֹ בְּדַלּוּת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava of Barnish said to Rav Ashi: And let us refute** this derivation in the following manner: **What** is the common denominator between the Levites and the nazirite? **Their common denominator is that the offering** in each case does not include a level **of poverty.** In both cases the offering is fixed, i.e., a poor person does not have the option of bringing a less expensive offering due to his financial straits. Will **you say** the same **with regard to the leper, whose offering** includes a level **of poverty,** as a poor person can bring turtledoves instead of sheep (Leviticus 14:21–22)? Since the Torah was more lenient in the case of a leper than the cases of a nazirite and the Levites, it could also be that the *halakha* is also lenient with regard to his shaving, by not demanding the use of a razor. Consequently, there is no proof that a leper is obligated to shave with a razor.

Rava of Barnish raises an objection to Rav Ashi: even this *tzad ha-shaveh* can be refuted. What do both *nazir* and *levi'im* share that the *metzora* does not? *She-kein ein korbanam be-dalut*—their *korbanot* have no sliding scale. A poor *nazir* and a poor *levi* must still bring the same *korban* as a wealthy one. A *metzora*, however, may bring birds if he cannot afford the standard *korban*—there is a *korban* adjusted for poverty. This leniency in the *korban* of a *metzora* arguably indicates a more flexible or lenient framework for the *metzora*'s obligations, and one might therefore extend that flexibility to the shaving requirement as well, permitting non-razor alternatives.

This is a striking argument in the Gemara, because the connection between the sliding-scale *korban* and the implement used for shaving seems logically remote. One might raise a far more compelling distinction: the shavings of the *nazir* and the *levi'im* were performed in the context of the *Beit ha-Mikdash*, whereas the *metzora*'s shaving is not—a distinction that would seem directly relevant to the stringency of the ritual. Nevertheless, this is the objection the Gemara itself advances.

### Cannot Learn Metzora

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא בַּר מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא לְרָבָא: הַאי תַּנָּא, מֵעִיקָּרָא אָמַר: לְלׇמְדוֹ מִמְּצוֹרָע אִי אֶפְשָׁר, שֶׁאֵין דָּנִין קַל מֵחָמוּר לְהַחְמִיר עָלָיו. וַהֲדַר אָמַר: נֵילַף מִדִּינָא, וּמִדִּינָא נָמֵי לָא יָלֵיף?
>
> **Translation:** In continuation of this discussion, **Rava bar Mesharshiyya said to Rava: This *tanna* initially said,** with regard to the obligation of a nazirite to shave with a razor (39b): **It is impossible to learn** this requirement **from** the *halakha* that **a leper** must use a razor, **as one does not derive** a *halakha* in **a lenient** case **from** the *halakha* in a more **stringent** one in a manner that would cause one **to be stringent** in the more lenient case. This indicates that it is obvious to the *tanna* that a leper himself must shave with a razor. **And he then said: Let us derive** by means **of an inference** that a leper must use a razor, **and** ultimately **he did not derive** it **from an inference** by analogy **either,** due to Rava of Barnish's objection. What, then, is the source for the *halakha* that a leper must use a razor?

The conclusion at this stage is that the *metzora*'s razor requirement cannot be derived from the *nazir*, from the *levi'im*, or from their common denominator. The earlier *beraita* had assumed it by *metzora* and could not apply it to *nazir*; today's *beraita* assumes it by *nazir* and cannot derive it for *metzora*. Rava bar Masharsheya explains to Rava that these two *beraitot* are not in conflict—they reflect different positions: the first follows the *Rabbanan* and the second follows Rabbi Eliezer. Understanding how those two positions differ is what the Gemara now turns to.

## Rabbanan: Razor Req'd

### Peah Zakenim Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָהוּא — אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן. הָא — אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. דִּתְנַן: וְאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיְּלַקְּטֶנּוּ בְּתַעַר. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ לִיקְּטוֹ בְּמַלְקֵט וּבְרָהִיטְנֵי — חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to** Rava bar Mesharshiyya: **That** *baraita*, which states that one cannot derive the *halakha* from the case of a leper, which indicates that it is evident that a leper must shave with a razor, is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis.** They derive the *halakha* of a leper's shaving from the prohibition against destroying one's beard. Conversely, **this** source, which attempted to derive the shaving of a leper from that of a nazirite and the Levites, is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** who does not derive the *halakha* of a leper's shaving from the prohibition against destroying one's beard. Rabbi Eliezer must therefore derive this *halakha* by analogy from the cases of a nazirite and the Levites. This is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Makkot* 20a): **And** one is **liable** for destroying his beard **only if he removes it with a razor. Rabbi Eliezer says: Even** if **he removed it with** small **tweezers or a plane [*rehitni*] he is liable.** Rabbi Eliezer maintains that one violates the prohibition even by destroying his beard with means other than a razor.

The Gemara introduces the Mishnah from *Makkot*: *ve-eino chayav ad she-yelaktenu be-ta'ar*—one is not liable until he shaves the beard with a razor. This is the standard prohibition against shaving the beard, *pe'at ha-zakan*. The *Rabbanan* hold that this prohibition is triggered only by a razor; plucking with tweezers (*malket* and *rehitni*) does not incur liability. Rabbi Eliezer disagrees, holding that even plucking triggers liability.

### Source: Rosho

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּרַבָּנַן? דְּתַנְיָא: ״זְקָנוֹ״ מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וּפְאַת זְקָנָם לֹא יְגַלֵּחוּ״, יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ מְצוֹרָע כֵּן — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״זְקָנוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason of the Rabbis;** how do they derive from this *halakha* that a leper must shave with a razor? **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that the verse states with regard to the shaving of a leper: "He shall shave all his hair off his head and his beard" (Leviticus 14:9). Since the verse states: "All his hair," **what** is the meaning when **the verse states: "His beard"?** It is **because it is stated** with regard to priests: **"Neither shall they shave off the corners of their beards"** (Leviticus 21:5). One **might** have thought that **the same** should **also** apply to **a leper,** i.e. that a leper who was a priest cannot shave his beard, since the prohibition encompasses both a *lo ta'aseh* (from *peat zekanam*) and a *mitzvat aseh* (from *kedoshim tihyu la'Eloheihem*, as Tosafot explains, which encompasses the specific *kedushah* obligations of *kohanim*). Since a *kohen* faces both a *lo ta'aseh* and an *aseh* prohibiting beard-shaving, the rule of *ein aseh docheh lo ta'aseh va-aseh* would prevent the *aseh* of *metzora*-shaving from overriding this combination. Therefore, the explicit mention of *zekano* tells us that even a *kohen metzora* must and may shave his beard.

The *Rabbanan*'s position is derived from a *beraita* that reads two *pesukim* in conjunction. The verse by *kohanim* states *u-fe'at zekanam lo yegalechu*—they shall not shave the corners of their beards. The verse by *Yisrael* states *ve-lo tashchit et pe'at zekanecha*. The word *yegalechu* points to an act of shaving with a blade; the word *tashchit* points to destruction at the root. Either verse alone would be insufficient: *yegalechu* alone might have included even scissors; *tashchit* alone might have included even plucking to the root. Taking the intersection—what satisfies both *yegalechu* and *tashchit*—yields the answer: *eizeh giluach she-yesh bo hashchata, hevei omer zeh ta'ar*—what shaving involves destruction? That is a razor.

### Kohen Metzora: Why?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָלַן דִּבְתַעַר? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וּפְאַת זְקָנָם לֹא יְגַלֵּחוּ״, יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ גִּילְּחוֹ בְּמִסְפָּרַיִם יְהֵא חַיָּיב, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְלֹא תַשְׁחִית״.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where do we** derive **that** this shaving of a leper must be performed **with a razor?** It is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*, with regard to the prohibition against a priest shaving his beard in the verse **"Neither shall they shave off the corners of their beards"** (Leviticus 21:5): One **might** have thought that a priest should **be liable even if he shaved** his beard **with scissors. The verse states,** in the general prohibition issued to all Jewish men: **"Neither shall you destroy** the corners of your beard" (Leviticus 19:27). This teaches that one is liable only if he shaves in a destructive manner, by uprooting the hairs entirely, which excludes the use of scissors.

Given the *Rabbanan*'s position that the only prohibited implement is a razor, the Gemara now connects this back to the *metzora*. The Torah in the context of the *metzora* states not merely *rosho* but explicitly *zekano*—his beard. Since the Torah already says *kol se'aro*, all of his hair, why mention *zekano* separately? The Gemara explains: *lefi she-ne'emar u-fe'at zekanam lo yegalechu*—because there is a prohibition against shaving the beard, one might have thought a *kohen* who is a *metzora* cannot shave his beard, since the prohibition encompasses both a *lo ta'aseh* (from *peat zekanam*) and a *mitzvat aseh* (from *kedoshim tihyu la'Eloheihem*, as Tosafot explains, which encompasses the specific *kedushah* obligations of *kohanim*). Since a *kohen* faces both a *lo ta'aseh* and an *aseh* prohibiting beard-shaving, the rule of *ein aseh docheh lo ta'aseh va-aseh* would prevent the *aseh* of *metzora*-shaving from overriding this combination. Therefore, the explicit mention of *zekano* tells us that even a *kohen metzora* must and may shave his beard.

### Yegalechu & Tashchit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לִיקְּטוֹ בְּמַלְקֵט וּבְרָהִיטְנֵי יְהֵא חַיָּיב, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּפְאַת זְקָנָם לֹא יְגַלֵּחוּ״. הָא כֵּיצַד? אֵיזֶהוּ גִּילּוּחַ שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ הַשְׁחָתָה — הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זֶה תַּעַר.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that **even** if **he removed it with tweezers or planes he** should **be liable. The verse states: "Neither shall they shave off the corners of their beards"** (Leviticus 21:5), which indicates that the priests are liable only for removing their beards in a manner of shaving. **How so? What is** the manner of **shaving that involves destruction? You must say this is** shaving with **a razor.** The Rabbis learn from here that the implement forbidden to a priest is the same one that must be used for the shaving of a leper, namely a razor.

The *beraita* then establishes the derivation of the razor requirement for the prohibition of beard-shaving by working through two stages. First: *yachol afilu gilach be-misparayim yehei chayav?*—one might think even scissors would be prohibited, since the verse says *lo yegalechu*. Therefore, *talmud lomar lo tashchit*: it has to involve *hashchata*, destruction at the root, which scissors do not achieve. Second: *yachol yikto be-malket u-ve-rehitni yehei chayav?*—one might then think even tweezers, which destroy at the root. Therefore, *talmud lomar u-fe'at zekanam lo yegalechu*: it must be an act of *giluach*, shaving. What shaving involves destruction? A razor. This convergence of *giluach* and *hashchata* is the *Rabbanan*'s source that the prohibition of beard-shaving is limited to a *ta'ar*.

### Metzora: Implicitly Ta'ar

Now the connection to *metzora* becomes clear. The *Rabbanan* have established that the only prohibited implement for beard-shaving is a razor. The Torah then needs to explicitly permit a *kohen metzora* to shave his beard. That permission—derived from the word *zekano*—only makes sense if the shaving of a *metzora* is done with a razor. If the *metzora*'s shaving could be accomplished with tweezers or some other non-razor implement, there would be no conflict with the *kohen*'s prohibition, since the *kohen*'s prohibition is razor-specific. The Torah would have had no need to explicitly permit it. The very fact that the Torah grants explicit permission proves that the *metzora*'s shaving is with a razor—because that is the only scenario in which permission was needed.

### Not Mere Permission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? דִּילְמָא לְעוֹלָם אֲפִילּוּ לִיקְּטוֹ בְּמַלְקֵט וּבְרָהִיטְנֵי נָמֵי מִצְוָה קָעָבֵיד, וְהָא קָאָתֵי לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן דַּאֲפִילּוּ בְּתַעַר לָא מִיחַיַּיב עֲלֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where** do we know that this is correct? **Perhaps** if a leper **actually removed it with tweezers or a plane he also performs the mitzva** and thereby fulfills his obligation, **and this** verse **is coming to teach us that even** if the leper shaved **with a razor he is not liable for** destroying his beard. In other words, one might have thought that a leper is prohibited from shaving with a razor, and the verse teaches that this is not correct. If so, there is no proof from here that the shaving of a leper must be performed with a razor.

The Gemara anticipates a counterargument: perhaps the Torah is not establishing an obligation to use a razor, but merely a permission—telling us that even if a *kohen metzora* wishes to use a razor, he may do so, even though it would otherwise be prohibited. On this reading, the *mitzvah* itself could still be fulfilled with a non-razor implement, and the *pasuk* simply extends a leniency for those who prefer the razor.

### No Alternatives Exist

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: אִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ כִּי עָבֵיד נָמֵי בְּמַלְקֵט וּבְרָהִיטְנֵי שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי, לִישְׁתּוֹק קְרָא מִינֵּיהּ, וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא: וּמָה גַּבֵּי נָזִיר דְּאִיסּוּרָא קָא עָבֵיד, אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי מִחַיַּיב, הָכָא דְּמִצְוָה — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** **They say** in response: **If it should enter your mind that if** a leper **performs** his shaving **with tweezers or a plane too,** it is **well** and he has performed the mitzva, then **let the verse be silent** and refrain **from** the extra phrase, "his beard." **And** I would **say** the following: **And just as with regard to a nazirite, who performs a transgression** by shaving his hair during his naziriteship and who, **even so,** is **deemed liable** for removing hair without the use of a razor, **here** too, in the case of a leper, **where** his shaving is **a mitzva,** does it **not** follow **all the more so** that he should be permitted to shave with any implement?

The Gemara rejects this interpretation in two ways. The first argument—that a *nazir*'s shaving already demonstrates we can override prohibitions when a *mitzvah* of shaving is present, so the *pasuk* could have been teaching us something else—is structurally complex. The stronger and clearer answer, articulated by Reish Lakish's principle, is this:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ, אִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ כִּי עָבֵיד בְּמַלְקֵט וּבְרָהִיטְנֵי — מִצְוָה קָא עָבֵיד, מִדְּלָא כְּתִיב ״תַּעַר״, כְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה מוֹצֵא עֲשֵׂה וְלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה, אִם אַתָּה יָכוֹל לְקַיֵּים אֶת שְׁנֵיהֶם — מוּטָב, וְאִם לָאו — יָבֹא עֲשֵׂה וְיִדְחֶה אֶת לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, if it should enter your mind** that **when he performs** his shaving **with tweezers or a plane he performs a mitzva, from** the fact that the word **razor is not written** he should not be allowed to use a razor at all, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Reish Lakish, who says** a principle in this regard. The Gemara cites the relevant principle: **Any place where you find a positive** mitzva **and a prohibition** that clash with one another, **if you can** find some way **to fulfill both,** that is **preferable; and if that is not** possible, the **positive** mitzva **will come and override the prohibition.** In this case the leper can fulfill his duty with tweezers or a plane, and therefore it should be prohibited for him to shave with a razor. Since it was necessary for the verse to say that the positive mitzva for a leper to shave overrides the prohibition against destroying one's beard, this proves that the mitzva can be performed only with a razor.

*kol makom she-atah motzei aseh ve-lo ta'aseh, im atah yakhol le-kayeim sheneihem mutav*—wherever an *aseh* confronts a *lo ta'aseh*, if you can fulfill both without conflict, you must do so. If the *metzora*'s *mitzvah* could be fulfilled with a non-razor implement, then the Torah would never have authorized overriding the prohibitions for a *kohen metzora* by using a razor—it would have directed him to use tweezers instead, thereby avoiding the conflict entirely. The very fact that the Torah found it necessary to permit the use of a razor—overriding the prohibition—demonstrates conclusively that there is no alternative: the *metzora*'s shaving must be with a razor and nothing else. This is the foundation upon which the earlier *beraita* assumed the *metzora* requires a *ta'ar*: it followed the *Rabbanan*, who locate the beard-shaving prohibition exclusively in the razor, and therefore could derive from the Torah's explicit permission for a *kohen metzora* that the *mitzvah* must be performed with that very implement.

## R. Eliezer: Non-Razors

### R. Eliezer: Plucking Too

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מַאי טַעְמָא? יָלֵיף מֵ״רֹאשׁוֹ״. דְּתַנְיָא: ״רֹאשׁוֹ״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר גַּבֵּי נָזִיר ״תַּעַר לֹא יַעֲבוֹר עַל רֹאשׁוֹ״, יָכוֹל אַף נָזִיר מְצוֹרָע כֵּן — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״רֹאשׁוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi Eliezer, what is** his **reason?** From where does he derive the *halakha* that a leper must use a razor? He **derives** it **from** the fact that the verse states with regard to a leper: "He shall shave all his hair off **his head** and his beard" (Leviticus 14:9). **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Why does the verse state: "His head,"** if it already stated: "All his hair"? It is **because it is stated with regard to a nazirite: "No razor shall come upon his head"** (Numbers 6:5), and therefore one **might** have thought that **the same** should **also** apply to **a nazirite** who is **a leper,** i.e., it should be prohibited for him to shave his head even for the purification ritual for his leprosy. Therefore, **the verse states: "His head,"** to teach that a nazirite who is a leper must shave his head with a razor. Being that a nazirite is prohibited only to use a razor, if a leper could fulfill his obligation to remove his hair using other implements, a nazirite who is a leper would not be permitted to use a razor. Therefore, it can be inferred from the *halakha* of a nazirite who is a leper that the only way for a leper to remove his hair is with a razor.

Rabbi Eliezer's position, as stated in the Mishnah in *Makkot*, is that one transgresses the prohibition of beard-shaving even through *malket* and *rehitni*—tweezers and related plucking implements—without using a razor. The prohibition for Rabbi Eliezer is not limited to the *ta'ar*.

### Source: Rosho

Rabbi Eliezer derives his understanding of the *metzora*'s obligation from the word *rosho* in the verse about the *metzora*: *yigaleach et kol se'aro et rosho*. Since the Torah already said *kol se'aro*, all of his hair, the explicit mention of *rosho* must come to teach something additional—specifically, that even the *nazir* who becomes a *metzora* must shave, though we will return to this below.

### Nazir Metzora: Why?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא לְעוֹלָם אֲפִילּוּ לִיקְּטוֹ בְּמַלְקֵט וּבְרָהִיטְנֵי — מִצְוָה קָעָבֵיד. וְכִי תֵּימָא: תַּעַר לְמָה לִי — לְמֵימְרָא דַּאֲפִילּוּ בְּתַעַר. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא, הוֹאִיל וְגַבֵּי נָזִיר כִּי עָבֵיד בְּתַעַר מִיחַיַּיב, גַּבֵּי מְצוֹרָע נָמֵי לִיחַיַּיב, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: And **from where** do you know that a leper must shave his head with a razor? **Perhaps even** if he **actually removed it with tweezers or a plane he performs a mitzva. And if you would say:** If so, **why do I** need the verse to teach me that he uses **a razor,** through the derivation of the superfluous term "his hair," as above, one can answer: **That is to say that** he is permitted to shave **even with a razor.** As, **it might enter your mind to say** that **since with regard to a nazirite, when** he **performs** the act of shaving **with a razor** he is **liable** for transgressing the prohibition, **with regard to** a nazirite who is **a leper** as well, he should **also be liable** even if he shaved for his leprosy. The verse therefore **teaches us** that this is **not** the case, but it does not teach that a leper must use a razor.

The parallel question for Rabbi Eliezer is: why does the Torah need to explicitly permit the shaving of a *nazir* who becomes a *metzora*? The word *rosho* in the context of the *metzora* is being used to teach that even a *nazir*, who is prohibited from having a razor pass over his head, must nonetheless shave when he becomes a *metzora*. For Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that the *nazir*'s prohibition includes not only a razor but also plucking and similar methods—since the *aseh* of *kadosh yihyeh gadel perah se'ar rosho* encompasses all forms of hair removal—it would be impossible to suggest that the *nazir metzora* could simply use tweezers and thereby avoid the conflict. Rabbi Eliezer must therefore derive from *rosho* that the shaving overrides even those broader prohibitions.

### Eliezer Cannot Assume

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ כִּי עָבֵיד בְּמַלְקֵט וּבְרָהִיטְנֵי מִצְוָה קָעָבֵיד, מִדְּלָא כְּתַב ״תַּעַר״, כְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **If it should enter your mind** that **when** a leper **performs** his hair removal **with tweezers or a plane he performs a mitzva,** then **from** the fact that the verse **did not** explicitly **write: Razor,** it can be inferred that he is not allowed to use one, **in accordance with** the aforementioned principle of **Reish Lakish** that one may not violate a prohibition, even for the sake of a mitzva, if it is possible to perform the mitzva in a different manner. Rather, the verse must be coming to teach that the mitzva of the shaving of a leper can be fulfilled only with a razor.

This is precisely why Rabbi Eliezer cannot employ the reasoning the *Rabbanan* use to establish the *metzora*'s razor requirement. The *Rabbanan*'s logic was: the Torah had to permit the *kohen metzora* to use a razor; the only beard-shaving prohibition is razor-specific; therefore, the *metzora*'s shaving must be with a razor. But for Rabbi Eliezer, the prohibition of beard-shaving extends beyond the razor to include plucking. Consequently, even if the *metzora*'s shaving could be done with tweezers, there would still be a conflict—and the Torah would still need to authorize it. Rabbi Eliezer therefore cannot infer from the Torah's permission that the *metzora* must use a razor; the permission is required regardless. He cannot start from the assumption that the *metzora* uses a razor, which is precisely why the *beraita* that follows Rabbi Eliezer's position begins instead from the assumption that the *nazir* uses a razor and attempts—ultimately without success—to derive the *metzora*'s razor requirement by other means. The full resolution of that difficulty is left for the following daf.
