# Eruvin 97 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tefillin New vs. Old

### New Tefillin Concern

Today's daf is 97, and we pick up at the very bottom of 96b. The *mishnah* had been discussing the case of finding new *tefillin* on *Shabbat*, and the ruling that one cannot bring them home — the concern being that new *tefillin* might not actually be *tefillin* at all, but rather some form of amulet. "New," as will become clear shortly, means that the knots have never been tied on them, or at least it is visibly apparent that no knots were ever formed.

The *gemara* raises an obvious challenge: we have an entire discussion elsewhere about finding blue strings of *techeilet* in the marketplace, and the ruling there is that if they were cut at the right length and prepared in the right way, we can assume they were done correctly. Why should the case of *tefillin* be any different?

### Torach/Effort Debate

To this, the *gemara* answers — *ella amar Rava* — that the underlying question of whether we say "nobody would go to such an effort unless this were the real thing" (*torach*) is actually itself a *tannaitic* dispute. As the *baraita* teaches: *hamotzei tefillin machnisam zug zug, echad ish v'echad isha, echad chadashot v'echad yeshanot* — if you find *tefillin* you may bring them in one pair at a time, whether men's or women's, and whether new or old. That is the position of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehudah, however, forbids the new but permits the old.

So we see that Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehudah are debating precisely this principle. A new *tefillin* is a black square leather box — it has the form. Rabbi Meir holds that nobody would go to the effort of producing such an object if it were not genuine *tefillin*, and therefore we do not suspect it is an amulet. Rabbi Yehudah remains concerned that it might be, and the debate is precisely over whether the effort invested in producing such an object is itself proof of authenticity: *mar savar torach inish, u'mar savar lo torach inish* — one holds a person would go to such effort even for a counterfeit, the other holds they would not. This also resolves the earlier question about the blue *tzitzit* strands — that position, that we assume proper preparation from the appearance of proper preparation, is the view of Rabbi Meir.

### Definitions of Old/New

At this point the *gemara* introduces a teaching that fundamentally shifts the entire analysis. The father of Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak — that is, Rav Yitzchak, the grandfather of Shmuel — teaches a *baraita* that defines old and new *tefillin* in concrete terms: *elu hen yeshanot, kol she-yesh ba-hen retsuot u'mekusharot* — old *tefillin* are those that have straps, and the straps have been knotted. *Chadashot* — new ones — are those that may have straps but the straps have not yet been knotted.

### Wearing Problem

This teaching transforms the analysis entirely. The *gemara* now says: *de-kulei alma lo torach inish* — everybody agrees in principle that nobody would construct these *tefillin* boxes if they were not genuine. The concern about new *tefillin* is therefore not that they might be amulets. Rather, the problem with new *tefillin* is: how can you wear them home on *Shabbat* if no knot has been tied? The *dalet* on the head *tefillin* keeps it in place; the loop on the *shel yad* allows it to be tightened. Without those knots, there is no way to wear the *tefillin* home, and tying a knot on *Shabbat* is itself a violation. The *amulet* concern was never the real issue — the question is one of practical wearability on *Shabbat*.

## Knot vs. Bow

### Bow Forbidden

The immediate question this raises is: if the only problem is the absence of a knot, why not simply make a bow? After all, one ties one's shoes with a bow rather than a knot. So could one not wear the *tefillin* home by fastening them with a bow?

*Amar Rav Chisda*: *zos omeres aniva psulah b'tefillin* — this demonstrates that a bow is invalid for *tefillin*. A bow cannot substitute for a knot in fulfilling the *mitzvah* of wearing *tefillin*. The permission to wear *tefillin* on *Shabbat* — even when *Shabbat* is generally not *zman tefillin* — is based on the fact that wearing them constitutes a normal mode of dress, *derech malbush*. But just as one may not wear *tefillin* on a part of the body where they are not normally worn, because that would not constitute *derech malbush*, similarly, if the normal mode of wearing *tefillin* requires a knot, then wearing them with a bow is not *derech malbush*, and such wearing would not be valid either for the *mitzvah* or as a legitimate mode of carrying on *Shabbat*.

### Bow = Shabbat Knot

*Abaye* then says: *Rav Yehudah l'taamei*, Rabbi Yehudah goes according to his own reasoning, for Rabbi Yehudah holds that a bow in the laws of *Shabbat* counts as a proper knot — *aniva kshira maalya hi*. Therefore, making a bow is not a solution to the problem. One cannot bind the *tefillin* onto one's arm, *Shabbat* has begun, one cannot make a knot — and the proposed alternative of a bow is equally problematic for Rabbi Yehudah, since for him a bow is halachically equivalent to a knot with respect to *Shabbat*.

This also perfectly explains the structure of the debate between Rabbi Yehudah and Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Meir presumably holds that a bow does *not* constitute a *Shabbat* violation, and therefore one could wear new *tefillin* home by means of a bow. Rabbi Yehudah holds the bow is itself a *Shabbat* problem, so no solution exists, and new *tefillin* without knots cannot be worn home at all.

The *gemara* then draws out the implication: *taama d'aniva kshira maalya hi* — the reason Rabbi Yehudah prohibits it is precisely because a bow equals a knot for *Shabbat*. But *ha lav hachi aniv lehu* — if not for the *Shabbat* concern, using a bow would have been a viable solution in principle. This brings the *gemara* back to whether a bow could ever serve as a legitimate knot for *tefillin*.

### Knot Form

Rav Yehudah son of Rav Shmuel bar Shilas says in the name of Rav: *kesher shel tefillin halacha l'Moshe miSinai hu* — the knot of *tefillin* is a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*. And Rav Nachman adds: *v'noyehem l'var* — their beauty must be on the outside. That is, when one forms the knot at the back of the head in the shape of a *dalet*, or the knot on the *shel yad* in the shape of a *yud*, the shape must be visible and exposed so that one who looks at it can see it.

This generates two distinct questions: first, what constitutes a valid knot — can a bow qualify? And second, what must the knot look like? The *gemara* now presses the point: according to Rabbi Meir, who permits wearing new *tefillin* home on *Shabbat*, he must be permitting some form of bow since one cannot make a full knot. But how could a bow ever be a legitimate knot for *tefillin*, given that it would not form the required shape of a *dalet* or a *yud*?

### Bow Solution

The *gemara* answers: *d'aniv lehu k'ein kshira dideyhu* — one makes the bow so that it resembles the required knot, shaping it to look like a *dalet* or like a *yud*. Whether such a bow would fully constitute fulfillment of the *mitzvah* of *tefillin* on a regular weekday remains ambiguous in the *gemara's* formulation. But at minimum, it means the *tefillin* look normal enough that wearing them on *Shabbat* would be considered a legitimate mode of dress — not something bizarre or unprecedented — and therefore would not constitute carrying but rather wearing.

This is the *gemara's* answer for Rabbi Meir's position: one makes a bow, which for Rabbi Meir is not a *Shabbat* violation, and one shapes it to resemble the required knot form. For Rabbi Yehudah, in theory such a bow would be possible in terms of appearance, but the *Shabbat* problem with the bow prevents it.

On the question of what exactly constitutes a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* with respect to the knot: Rashi holds that all three letters are included — the *shin* on the *tefillin* (though it is not quite a knot in the conventional sense), the *dalet*, and the *yud*. Tosafos, in the first line, takes issue with this and argues that the *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* applies only to the *shin* itself. The *dalet* and *yud*, by Tosafos' reading, are derived from the structure of the knots and straps, not from the *Sinaitic* tradition as such. In either case, the *gemara* is clear that the knots must visibly resemble the *dalet* and *yud*, and the solution of a bow shaped accordingly is what enables wearing on *Shabbat* according to Rabbi Meir.

A further question naturally arises: beyond the knot and the letters, *tefillin* are typically wound around the arm seven times and then around the finger. If one were to wrap the *tefillin* around the arm only once, would an observer even recognize it as wearing *tefillin*? The principle seems to be that there is a range of tolerance — what counts as a legitimate wearing of *tefillin* need not be perfectly identical to the standard weekday practice, but at a certain point it ceases to be recognizable as wearing them at all. The precise limits of that range are not spelled out in the *gemara*.

## Buying Tefillin

### Two-One Check

*Amar Rav Chisda amar Rav*: if one purchases *tefillin* from someone who is not a *mumcheh* — not an expert with proven credentials — one must verify their *kashrus* before relying on them. The term *mumcheh* here does not simply mean "skilled"; it means someone whose reliability has been established and certified. In the scenario the *gemara* envisions, a wholesaler collects *tefillin* from various *sofrim* and sells them to retailers. Since this wholesaler's reliability is not independently established, the buyer must conduct spot checks. The rule: *bodek shtayim shel yad v'achas shel rosh*, or *shtayim shel rosh v'achas shel yad* — check two of one type and one of the other type, for a total of three.

### Why Two-One?

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: *ma nafshach* — make up your mind. If this wholesaler purchased all his *tefillin* from a single supplier, then why not simply check three of the same type? The principle of *chazaka* — that three instances establish a presumption — would apply here, and if all the *tefillin* come from one *sofer*, the type distinction should not matter. Conversely, if the wholesaler buys from multiple suppliers — two or three different *sofrim* — then checking three pieces total is clearly insufficient; every individual pair would require its own verification, since each might have come from a different, unvalidated source. Under neither scenario does the "two of one and one of the other" formula make intuitive sense.

### One Supplier

The *gemara* resolves this: *le'olam michad gavra zavnin* — indeed, the wholesaler purchases from a single supplier. But the purpose of the mixed sampling is this: one needs to ensure that this *sofer* is validated, *mitmachei*, with respect to both *shel yad* and *shel rosh*. A *sofer* might write a beautiful *ksav* yet make a systematic error specific to one type. For example, he might not arrange the *parshiyos* in the *shel rosh* in the correct order, or he might fail to make the proper separation between the *parshiyos* in the *shel yad*. Therefore, three samples are sufficient to establish a *chazaka* of reliability — but among those three, one must ensure that both types have been examined. It is not enough to check three *shel yad* pieces and extrapolate to the *shel rosh*.

The logic is thus: three samples from a single supplier establishes reliability, but the sample must cover both types, so as to validate the supplier's practice with respect to each. That is why two of one and one of the other suffices — not three of the same, and not three of each.

### Rav Kahana

A *baraita* of Rav Kahana then states: *b'tefillin bodek shtayim shel yad u'shtayim shel rosh* — check two of the *shel yad* and two of the *shel rosh*, for a total of four. This does not fit neatly into either a "total of three" framework or a "three of each type" framework.

### Rebbi Position

The *gemara* identifies this position as that of *Rebbi*: *ha mani Rebbi hi, d'amar bitrei zimnei hvei chazakah* — Rebbi holds that a *chazaka* is established after two instances, not three. This is the well-known dispute between Rebbi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel regarding how many occurrences are required to establish a presumption. Here, applied to spot-checking, Rebbi holds that two samples from each type suffice to establish confidence in the supplier's practice for that type — hence two of *shel yad* and two of *shel rosh*.

### Multiple Bags

The *gemara* then turns to a *seifa* in that same *baraita*: *v'chein batzeves hashaini, v'chein batzeves hashlishi* — and similarly for the second group and the third group. This seems to say that after verifying two bags, one can extrapolate to all remaining bags. But this contradicts Rebbi's own principle: if two instances establish a *chazaka*, then after verifying two bags, one should already be able to stop.

The *gemara* resolves: *modeh Rebbi bitzvatim d'mitrei tlata gavra zavnin* — Rebbi concedes that when it comes to separate bags, each bag comes from a different supplier, and therefore no cross-bag extrapolation is possible. The principle of two establishing a *chazaka* applies within a single bag from a single source; it cannot bridge across multiple bags sourced from multiple suppliers. The mention of a third bag in the *baraita* is therefore not to say "after three bags you are done" — rather, it is *lafukei michizkei*, to negate the normal *chazaka* rule: do not think that verifying two bags allows you to extrapolate to all subsequent bags. Each bag requires its own inspection. As the *gemara* confirms: *ein hachi nami* — indeed, even a fourth and fifth bag must be checked.

This discussion has practical resonance well beyond *tefillin*, touching on how *halakha* treats sampling in contexts where large quantities of goods are examined — such as the checking of produce for insects, where a similar question arises about how many units from a given batch must be inspected.

## Bundles/Bags

### Bundle Types

The *gemara* now returns to the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* rules that if one finds *tefillin* in bundles — *tzfashim* or *krichot* — one waits until nightfall and brings them home after *Shabbat*. The *gemara* first asks what distinguishes *tzfashim* from *krichot*. *Amar Rav Yehudah amar Rav*: they are essentially the same thing in different configurations. *Tzfashim* — *zuzei zuzei* — are paired sets of *tefillin*, a *shel yad* and *shel rosh* wrapped together as pairs, multiple such pairs collected together. *Krichot* — *de'krichu tuvah* — are bundles where all the *tefillin* are wrapped together without being separated into pairs. The functional difference between them is limited; they simply describe different physical arrangements.

### Single vs. Multiple Trips

The *gemara* then asks why one cannot simply carry the *tefillin* home pair by pair, wearing each pair in turn for the duration of the trip, and making multiple trips until all are safely brought in.

### Completion Test

*Amar Rav Yitzchak berei de'Rav Yehudah*: his father Rav Yehudah explained to him the following principle — *kol she'ilu machnisan zug zug ve'chlot kodem shkiat hachamah, machnisan zug zug*. If the number of *tefillin* is small enough that making individual trips, one pair at a time, could realistically be completed before the end of *Shabbat*, then one should indeed make all those trips. But if the quantity is so large that even after dozens of trips there will still be pairs remaining when *Shabbat* ends, then one should not begin the process at all — *machshich aleihem u'mivian*, one waits there until nightfall and brings them all home at once.

The logic: if one cannot in any case remove all of them within *Shabbat*, then leaving some behind is unavoidable regardless. In that case, it is better not to engage in the piecemeal rescue at all, and simply remain with the *tefillin* until *Shabbat* ends. This reasoning, of course, assumes that waiting there is a viable option — the *mishnah's* next case will address the scenario where it is not.

## Danger Scenarios

### Cover & Leave

When waiting is not possible — *u'v'sakana michasan ve'holech* — if there is genuine danger, one covers the *tefillin* and leaves. The scenario envisioned is one where remaining to guard them through nightfall poses a risk, whether from attack, theft, or another threatening circumstance. In such a case, if wearing one pair home is possible, one would presumably do so; the rest are covered and left. The question of what happens to the *tefillin* left momentarily unguarded during the back-and-forth of individual trips is a genuinely difficult one — *Chazal* appear to have exercised a degree of practical leniency here, perhaps because an item visibly in the process of being tended to is treated somewhat differently by passersby. More importantly, the character of *tefillin* as *kisvei kodesh* means the effort to rescue as many as possible is obligatory, on the model of a *sefer Torah* in a burning synagogue: one saves what one can.

### Small Trips

The *gemara* now introduces a competing solution from a *baraita*: *besakanah molichan pachos pachos me'arba amot* — in a case of danger, one may carry them in increments of less than four *amot* at a time. If one can recall the *mishnah* at the end of *Masechet Shabbat*, this is the same technique described there for a person caught outside the city with belongings at nightfall. The suggestion is that rather than leaving the *tefillin* behind and simply covering them, one could take the entire bundle and inch it home in multiple segments, each less than four *amot*, thereby avoiding a biblical violation while still rescuing all of the *tefillin*. Why, then, does the *mishnah* say simply "cover them and leave"?

### Two Danger Types

*Amar Rav lo kashya* — Rav resolves the apparent contradiction: these two rulings address two entirely different types of danger. *Ha besakanas nochri, ha besakanas listin* — one ruling pertains to danger from non-Jews, the other to danger from robbers.

### Rashi's View

Rashi initially understands the distinction as follows. When the danger is from non-Jews who will mistreat the *tefillin* — desecrate their *kedushah* — one cannot simply cover and leave, since an enemy who finds them will handle them disrespectfully. However, when the danger is from Jewish robbers who are motivated by the monetary value of the *tefillin*, one need not carry them at all using the less-than-four-*amot* method, since if the robbers find them they will at least treat the *tefillin* with appropriate reverence, being Jews themselves who recognize their sanctity. The less-than-four-*amot* method is therefore unnecessary for the *kedushah* concern, but one should not carry them openly due to the personal danger of being attacked for their monetary value. Rashi's comment on *besakanas listin* — that Jewish robbers *dekatlei lei mishum mamona*, would kill for money, but *vetfillin lo mevazu lehu*, would not disrespect the *tefillin* — is a striking formulation.

### Revised: Edict

Rashi himself, however, ultimately rejects this reading in light of the continuation of the *gemara*, and offers a revised understanding. *Sakanas nochri* refers to a *sha'as hashmad* — a period of persecution in which Jews are forbidden by decree from wearing or handling *tefillin*. In such a circumstance, one cannot wear the *tefillin* or be found in possession of them at all; the only recourse is to cover them and leave. *Sakanas listin*, by contrast, refers to the risk that the *tefillin* will be stolen by robbers who covet them for their value — there is no edict, no prohibition on being found with them. In that case, the concern is the risk of being physically attacked while carrying them, but not of being caught with them by an authority. Therefore, the solution is to carry them in increments of less than four *amot*, avoiding a biblical violation while keeping them in one's possession.

The distinction is thus clean: if the danger is that *tefillin* cannot be found on one's person, cover and leave. If the danger is merely the risk of robbery, carry in small increments.

## Tanna Kamma vs. R. Shimon

### Quiet Approach

*Abaye* then presses on the *mishnah*: having established that the *mishnah* speaks of a case of *sakanas ovdei gilulim* — danger from non-Jews enforcing a prohibition — how does Rabbi Shimon's position fit in? Rabbi Shimon says *nosein l'chaveiro v'chaveiro l'chaveiro* — pass the *tefillin* from person to person in a daisy chain. But in a case where the non-Jews are enforcing a decree against *tefillin*, such a large public operation would only attract attention: *kol shekein d'avsha milsa* — it would make the matter far more conspicuous.

The *gemara* therefore concludes: *chasuri mechasra v'hachi kasani* — the *mishnah* is to be read as if a distinction is embedded within it. *Bameh devarim amurim besakanas ovdei kochavim* — when there is a non-Jewish edict, cover and leave. *Aval besakanas listim* — but when the danger is merely robbery, carry in small trips of less than four *amot*. And it is on *this* case — the robbery case — that Rabbi Shimon disagrees and advocates the daisy chain method instead.

### Daisy Chain

So the debate between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Shimon applies specifically to the robbery scenario, where one may handle and carry the *tefillin*. The *tanna kamma* says: carry in segments of less than four *amot*, doing so quietly and individually. Rabbi Shimon says: use the daisy chain — *nosein l'chaveiro v'chaveiro l'chaveiro* — passing the *tefillin* from person to person down a line of people.

### Philosophy

The *gemara* articulates the underlying logic of each position. *Mar savar pachos m'arba amot adif* — the *tanna kamma* holds that individual small trips are preferable, because *d'ei amart nosein l'chaveiro v'chaveiro l'chaveiro, avsha milsa d'shabbos* — the daisy chain creates a large public commotion on *Shabbat*, which is in itself disrespectful of the day's character, setting aside any concern about non-Jews. Better to handle the matter quietly.

Rabbi Shimon holds the opposite: *nosein l'chaveiro adif* — the daisy chain is preferable, because *d'ei amart molichan pachos pachos m'arba amot, zimnin d'lav adateih* — if you rely on a person to carry in segments shorter than four *amot*, there will be moments of inattention where one takes a few extra steps without thinking, and one will come to carry four *amot* in *reshus harabim* — a biblical violation.

This is a conceptually rich debate, touching on what one might describe as the tension between the quality of *Shabbat* as a public experience and the protection of the individual from personal transgression. The *tanna kamma* is willing to accept a modest risk of inadvertent personal violation if it preserves the texture of *Shabbat* for the community. Rabbi Shimon prioritizes eliminating the risk of individual transgression even at the cost of some disruption to the public experience of *Shabbat*. This debate also resonates with a larger dispute among the *rishonim* — both Rambam and Ramban maintain that there is a biblical *mitzvah* of *tishbos*, of resting on *Shabbat*, that extends beyond the thirty-nine *melachos*. Ramban understands *tishbos* to mean that *Shabbat* must be experienced as a day of genuine rest and appropriate quality, independent of technical violation of the *melachos*. Rambam understands *tishbos* as a prohibition on activities that might lead one *to* transgress a *melachah*. The *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Shimon can be read as anticipating precisely this dispute.

## Newborn Child

### Scenario

The *mishnah* then introduces an analogous case involving a child. *Bno mai ba'ei hasam* — what is a child doing out in the field? A *baraita* of the school of Menashya clarifies: *be-she-yeldato imo ba-sadeh* — the child was born in the field just before *Shabbat*, and there was no opportunity to bring the newborn home before *Shabbat* began.

The *mishnah* extends the same daisy-chain method to the case of the child: pass the infant from person to person to bring it home. The *gemara* notes: *u'mai afilu hen me'ah* — even if a hundred people are needed to form the chain, *de-af al gav de-kashya lei yada* — even though the repeated handoffs are uncomfortable and unpleasant for the infant — *afilu hachi, hadi* — even so, this is the preferred approach.

This is a striking formulation. The *gemara* elsewhere is unequivocal that *pikuach nefesh* overrides virtually all of *halachah*. One must therefore assume that the *gemara* is working with the understanding that in this particular scenario, the daisy-chain method does not increase any actual risk to the newborn — the difficulty is described as discomfort, not danger — and that if bringing the child home via this method would in any way endanger it, the *pikuach nefesh* principle would obviously take precedence and one would simply carry the child home directly.

### Barrel Analogy

Rabbi Yehudah then extends the principle: *nosein adam chavit* — Rabbi Yehudah says one can do the same daisy-chain method with a barrel. Before even reaching the *halachic* questions about *tchum*, one might ask: since when may one use this technique for a barrel, which is simply a monetary object? The case of *tefillin* involves *kisvei kodesh*; the case of a child involves life. A barrel of wine or oil raises a different kind of question. The answer may lie in the precedent of the *mishnah* at the end of *Masechet Shabbat*, where the *gemara* permitted the less-than-four-*amot* technique for personal belongings in situations of significant distress.

### Tchum Problem

The *chakhamim* object to Rabbi Yehudah's barrel case on a different ground entirely: *ha'behema v'hakeilim k'raglei ha'bealim* — animals and vessels are subject to their owner's *tchum*. If the barrel would need to be transported beyond the owner's *tchum* to reach safety, that is a separate and independent *halachic* problem. How does Rabbi Yehudah address the *tchum* issue?

## Water & Tchum

### Pour Method

*Amar Reish Lakish mishum Levi Saba*: the scenario being described is not one in which anyone physically moves the barrel. Rather, *d'meara m'chavis l'chavis* — one pours from barrel to barrel. Everyone along the line has their own barrel, and the liquid is transferred by pouring, each person pouring into the next person's barrel. This way, no barrel crosses the *tchum* — only the liquid moves. The question then becomes whether the liquid itself has a *tchum* issue.

### Water Insignificant

*Rabbi Yehuda l'taamei d'amar mayim ein bahen mamash* — Rabbi Yehudah applies his own principle that water lacks legal substance. The source: a *baraita* teaches that Rabbi Yehudah rules that water and salt are *beteilim* — nullified — in dough and therefore do not have the laws of *tchumin*. The water borrowed from a neighbor to make *challah* does not impose the neighbor's *tchum* on the dough, because Rabbi Yehudah holds that water absorbed into dough is insignificant. If water lacks *tchum* relevance, then moving it raises no *tchum* problem.

### Water in Pot

The *gemara* immediately challenges this extrapolation. *Eima d'shamas lei l'Rabbi Yehudah heicha d'belian b'isa* — Rabbi Yehudah said water is insignificant specifically when it is absorbed into dough and has become invisible as an independent substance. But *aval heicha d'isneihu b'einihu* — when the water exists in its own state, as free liquid, has Rabbi Yehudah ever said it lacks *tchum* significance?

The *gemara* proceeds to demonstrate the opposite: *hashta b'kdeira amar Rabbi Yehudah lo betili* — Rabbi Yehudah explicitly says that water in a pot — the liquid component of a stew — is *not* nullified and *does* carry *tchum* laws. The same *baraita* cited above continues: *v'ein betilim b'kdeira, mipnei rotva* — water and salt are not nullified in a pot, because the sauce retains its independent character. If water in a pot has *tchum* significance for Rabbi Yehudah, then surely water in its free state does as well. The Reish Lakish / water-insignificance approach therefore cannot stand.

### Rava: Drawn

*Ela amar Rava*: the case must be understood differently. *Hacha b'chavis she'kansah shevisa u'mayim she'lo kanu shevisa askinan* — the barrel acquired its *shvisa* — established its resting place — at the onset of *Shabbat*, but the water did not. Rashi explains: the water was drawn from a river on *Shabbat* itself, or it is rainwater collected on *Shabbat*. Water drawn from a flowing river during *Shabbat* never had an established location at the onset of *Shabbat* and therefore was never subject to a *tchum* boundary.

Once the water has no *tchum*, the barrel — which is merely the container for the water — is secondary to it. *D'vateil hachavis l'gabei mayim* — the barrel is subordinate to the water it contains, just as the *gemara* rules in related contexts: *hamotzi chai b'mita patur af al hamita* — if one carries a living person on a stretcher, one is exempt not only regarding the person — since *chai nosei es atzmo*, a living person bears his own weight — but also regarding the stretcher itself, since the stretcher is subordinate to its contents. Similarly, *hamotzi ochlin pachos mikishiur b'kli, patur af al hakli, mipnei shehakli tafel lo* — if one carries food below the minimum quantity in a vessel, one is exempt even for the vessel, since it is secondary to the food. The same principle applies here: the barrel is secondary to the water, and if the water has no *tchum*, neither does the barrel.

### Caravan Need

*Masiv Rav Yosef*: a *baraita* states — *b'shayara, nosein mayim v'chavis l'chaveiro v'chaveiro l'chaveiro* — in a caravan, one passes the water and the barrel from person to person. *B'shayara in, shelo b'shayara lo* — in a caravan yes, otherwise no. This implies the permission is limited to a caravan context, whereas the *mishnah* appears to speak without that restriction.

*Ela amar Rav Yosef*: read the *mishnah* too as referring to a caravan. And this resolves the *tchum* problem not through the technical mechanism of water drawn on *Shabbat*, but through *pikuach nefesh*: in a caravan without access to water, the need for water is a genuine life-threatening emergency. Rabbi Yehudah therefore sets aside the *tchum* concern entirely in such a circumstance.

### Two Cases

*Abaye* then draws a careful distinction between the two scenarios. In the caravan case: *b'shayara afilu chavis shekansah shvisa u'mayim shekanu shvisa* — even if the barrel and the water both established their *shvisa* at the onset of *Shabbat*, the *tchum* concern is overridden because of the life-threatening need for water in the caravan context. Outside the caravan: *shelo b'shayara, chavis shekansah shvisa u'mayim shelo kanu shvisa* — the case is specifically where the water did not establish a *shvisa*, i.e., it was drawn on *Shabbat* and therefore never acquired a *tchum* boundary. Abaye's contribution is thus to maintain both cases as distinct: the caravan case is governed by *pikuach nefesh* and dispenses with the *tchum* question entirely; the non-caravan case is the one Rava described, where the water was drawn on *Shabbat* and technically lacks a *tchum*.

### Ownerless

*Rav Ashi amar*: the case involves an ownerless barrel and ownerless water — *chavis d'hefker askinan u'mayim d'hefker askinan*. Ownerless objects are not subject to their owner's *tchum*, since they have no owner. The objection that *chakhamim* raise regarding *tchum* — *v'man amru lah, Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri hi* — belongs to Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri, who holds that even ownerless objects acquire their own *shvisa*, defined by 2000 *amah* from wherever they happened to be at the onset of *Shabbat*, and are therefore not entirely free of *tchum* concerns. The *chakhamim's* objection — *lo yehalechu elu yoser mi'keilim sheyesh lahem baalim* — is therefore not to say these objects are limited by their owner's *tchum*, but rather to say that ownerless objects are no more permissive than owned objects; they too carry *tchum* limitations, just derived differently.

## Fallen Scroll

### Scenario

The *mishnah* now turns to a case that continues the theme of special dispensations for *kisvei kodesh*. *Haya kore b'sefer al ha-iskopa* — one was reading from a scroll on the porch, and *v'nisgalgal ha-sefer mi-yado* — the scroll rolled out of one's hand and began to fall, leaving half of the scroll hanging over the edge of the balcony, with the *reshus hayachid* above and the *reshus harabim* below. Can one roll it back up?

### Porch Case

In the *iskopa* case — reading from a porch or covered area — *v'gololo etzlo* — one may always roll the scroll back up toward oneself. No restriction applies.

### Within Ten

On a roof: *ad she-lo higia l'asara tfachim, gololo etzlo* — as long as the hanging portion has not descended into the ten-*tefach* airspace of *reshus harabim*, one may roll it back up. Above ten *tefachim* from the ground, the airspace is a *makom p'tor* — an exempt domain — not a *reshus harabim*. Rolling the scroll up from there does not constitute removing it from *reshus harabim*, and the action is therefore permitted.

### Beyond Ten

Once one end of the scroll is hanging within the ten *tefachim* of *reshus harabim* — *mishe-higia l'asara tfachim* — one may no longer roll it up. The scroll is now effectively present in *reshus harabim*, and rolling it up would constitute an act of transferring from *reshus harabim* to *reshus hayachid*. What does one do instead? *Hofcho al ha-ksav* — one turns the script-side of the scroll toward the wall, so that the text is at least somewhat protected from the elements. One is still holding one end of the scroll; the turning is possible without rolling it up.

The difference between the *iskopa* and the roof cases — why the porch carries no limitation while the roof must distinguish between within and beyond ten *tefachim* — is a point the *gemara* addresses separately.

### R. Yehudah

Rabbi Yehudah extends the permission: *afilu eino mesulak min ha-aretz ela k'mlo machat* — even if the hanging end is only a needle's width above the ground — a millimeter — as long as it is not actually resting on the ground of *reshus harabim*, one may roll it back up. Even within the ten-*tefach* zone, the hair's breadth of elevation is sufficient.

### R. Shimon

Rabbi Shimon goes further still: *afilu ba-aretz, gololo etzlo* — even if one end of the scroll is actually resting on the ground of *reshus harabim*, one may roll it up. One end remains in one's hand in *reshus hayachid*, and even though the other end is touching the ground of *reshus harabim*, the scroll may be retrieved.

### Principle

Rabbi Shimon grounds this ruling in a general principle: *she-ein l'cha davar mishum shvus omed bifnei kisvei kodesh* — there is no rabbinic prohibition that may stand in the way of protecting *kisvei kodesh*. While the earlier sections of this *daf* involved techniques and workarounds that did not require formally overriding any rabbinic decree, here — at least according to Rabbi Shimon's most expansive position — we are explicitly told that rabbinic restrictions yield before the imperative to protect holy scriptures. The biblical prohibition of *Shabbat* is not abrogated; but rabbinic enactments do not prevail when *kisvei kodesh* are at stake. We will continue from there.
