# Sotah 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mincha Process

### Frankincense Transfer

The daf begins at the bottom of 14b, where the Gemara resumes its account of the process of bringing the *mincha* of the *sotah*. Before returning specifically to that ritual, the Gemara completes its citation of a *braita* that describes the standard procedure for bringing any *mincha* — useful background for the discussion that follows.

The *braita* states: *umalket et levonata venotnah al gabav* — the *kohen* gathers the *levona* (frankincense) from the original vessel in which the *mincha* was presented and transfers it to the separate *kli sharet* (sacred vessel) into which the *kometz* (handful) has already been placed. The verse cited is *ve-et kol ha-levona asher al ha-mincha* — "and all the frankincense that is upon the *mincha*" — which he then offers upon the *mizbeach*.

### Handful Offering

The top of 15a states: *umaale umaktir bichli sharet* — he brings it up and offers it in the *kli sharet*. This formulation raises a question: is he actually burning up the *kli sharet* itself? Rashi notes a different *girsa* of the text: *maktirah* cannot be read as "he has already burnt it," since the next line states that he first salts it. Rather, the reading should be *maale bichli sharet lehaktirou* — he brings it up in the *kli sharet* for the purpose of offering it, which both clarifies that the *kli sharet* is not itself being consumed and that the burning has not yet taken place. First, he salts it — *umolcha* — in fulfillment of the verse *vechol korban minchatcha bamelach timlach*, that all *korbanot* must be salted. Only then does he place it on the fire: *venotnah al gabei ha-ishim*. Afterward, *karev ha-kometz sheyarayim ne'echalin* — once the handful has been offered, the remainder (*sheyarayim*) becomes permissible to be eaten.

### Handful Timing

A *tannaitic* debate addresses the precise moment at which the *kometz* is considered to have been offered, thereby permitting the remainder. Rabbi Chanina holds that it is once the fire has taken hold: *mishe-tishlot bo ha-ur*. Rabbi Yochanan holds a stricter standard: *mishe-tatzit ha-ur berubo* — once the fire has caught the majority of it. This is a more advanced stage, requiring that the fire has not merely ignited the *kometz* but has actually taken hold of most of it.

### Mixing Ingredients

The *braita* continues: the *kohanim* may then mix into the remainder wine, oil, and honey — anything they wish — provided they do not allow it to become *hametz*. The reason: the verse says *lemoshcha* — the remainder is to be treated in a manner befitting dignity, *kederech shehamelachim ochlim*, in the way that kings eat. One might have thought that mixing in additional foodstuffs diminishes the sanctity of the *korban*; the *braita* teaches that on the contrary, eating in a dignified, refined manner is not only permitted but appropriate. The sole limitation is that one may not allow the remainder to become leavened.

### Leavening Prohibition

The source for this prohibition is the verse *lo teafeh hametz chelkam* — do not make even their portion into *hametz*. Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish derives from the word *chelkam* that even the priests' portion — the remainder left after the *kometz* is offered — may not be allowed to become *hametz*. One might wonder how leavening is even possible once a *mincha* has already been cooked or placed on fire, a question that belongs more properly to Masechet Menachot, where an entire chapter is devoted to the *issur* of *hametz* in the context of *menachot*. Different *menachot* involve different modes of preparation — some are placed directly on coals, some are made on a flat pan (*machavat*) or deep pan (*meracheshet*), and there is even a *minchat ma'afeh tanur* baked in an oven — which is part of why Masechet Menachot is so extensive.

## Menachot Comparison

### Sinner's Mincha

Having completed the background *braita*, the Gemara now returns to the *minchat sotah* and opens with the statement from the Mishnah that all *menachot* require oil and frankincense, with the exception of the *mincha* of the *sotah*. The Gemara immediately challenges this: *vehaika minchat chotei* — what about the *minchat chotei*, the meal offering of a sinner? At the end of Parashat Vayikra, a category of *korbanot* called *oleh veyored* (variable offerings, scaled to one's means) provides that for certain specified sins, a person of limited means may bring a *mincha* in lieu of an animal *chatat*. That offering — the *minchat chotei* — is also explicitly stated in the Torah to require neither oil nor frankincense: *lo yasim aleha shemen velo yitein aleha levonah*, followed by *ki chatati* — because it is a sin offering. So the *sotah*'s *mincha* is not unique in lacking oil and frankincense.

### Three Qualities

The Gemara resolves this by reframing the Mishnah's claim. *Hachi ka'amar* — what the Mishnah is actually saying is not that all other *menachot* have both oil and frankincense, but rather that all other *menachot* possess at least one of three positive qualities: they contain *shemen* (oil), *levona* (frankincense), or they are made from refined wheat flour (*soles*) that comes from wheat (*chitin*). Every *mincha*, without exception, has at least one of these distinguishing positive characteristics — though some have only one, while others have all three.

### Sota Mincha Qualities

To illustrate: the *minchat chotei*, the sinner's meal offering, lacks oil and frankincense, yet it is made from refined wheat flour (*soles min ha-chitin*) — one redeeming quality remains. The *minchat ha-omer*, though it comes from barley rather than wheat, still requires oil and frankincense and is prepared from refined barley flour (*geres*) — it retains two of the three positive qualities. By contrast, *ve-zo* — the *sotah*'s *mincha* — has none: it has no oil, no frankincense, it is made from barley (*se'orim*) rather than wheat, and it is made from coarsely ground flour (*kemach*) rather than refined flour (*soles*) — that is, whole grain with the bran still present, the opposite of *soles*. It is thus uniquely deficient across all three dimensions, a point the Gemara emphasizes as expressive of its degraded symbolic character. And yet, as an aside, the *kohanim* are still obligated to eat it — they receive no prize for that particular assignment.

## Sota Mincha Reasoning

### Rabbi Shimon's View

A *braita* is cited presenting Rabbi Shimon's analysis of the underlying logic. He argues that by strict reasoning, the *minchat chotei* — the standard sinner's meal offering — *should* have required oil and frankincense: *she-lo yehei chotei niskar* — so that the sinner should not benefit by having a less costly or less elaborate offering. Why should he be let off easy? Yet the Torah does not impose this additional expense. Why? *She-lo yehei korbano mehurdar* — because it is more important that the offering not be beautiful or embellished, since its symbolic function — communicating the gravity of sin — outweighs the concern about the sinner's financial ease. The symbolic message takes precedence over the punitive economic logic.

### Sin Offering Parallel

Rabbi Shimon extends this reasoning. He argues that by the same logic, a *chatat* for a standard sin (conventionally referred to as a *chatat cheilev*, since the passage establishing the *chatat* in the Torah is juxtaposed to the prohibition of eating forbidden fat) *should* have required *nesachim* (libations — the accompanying wine, oil, and flour offerings that come with many animal sacrifices). Rashi notes that the juxtaposition is not incidental: the laws of *chatat* in the Torah are written directly adjacent to the passage about *cheilev* and *dam* being offered on the *mizbeach*, making *chatat cheilev* a natural reference point. The same logic applies: the sinner should not benefit from the absence of *nesachim*. But they are not required — *she-lo yehei korbano mehurdar* — so that the offering not be embellished.

Importantly, Rabbi Shimon is here giving a *ta'ama d'kra* — a reason-based interpretation of the Torah's ruling. The question raised is whether he is merely *describing* the known law by offering an explanation, or whether he is actually *deriving* the law from his interpretive principle and using it to determine halacha in cases we do not yet know. The first possibility is significant because Rabbi Shimon is generally one who *darshinan ta'ama d'kra* — who applies the reason of a verse to determine legal conclusions in other cases. The Gemara will test which of these readings is correct.

### Metzora Exception

Rabbi Shimon continues: the *chatat* and *asham* of a *metzora* (a person afflicted with *tzara'at*) *do* bring *nesachim* — and this is because they are not brought on account of sin. The *metzora*'s purification offerings are not *kaparah* for transgression but rather part of a *taharah* process. Rashi notes that this is not derived by Rabbi Shimon alone from logical inference — the Gemara elsewhere (Masechet Menachot, chapter *Shtei Middot*) actually has a specific *drasha* to include the *metzora*'s offerings in the requirement of *nesachim*. So according to Rashi, Rabbi Shimon's statement here is *descriptive*: he knows the law and is explaining *why* these offerings are the exception.

The Gemara then objects: is it really true that the *metzora*'s offerings have nothing to do with sin? Does not the Gemara elsewhere (Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani citing Rabbi Yonatan) state that *nega'im* come for seven sins? The Gemara answers: there, the *nega* itself serves as the atonement for the sin — *hu d'ichpar lei*. By the time the person brings the *korban*, it is not to atone for sin but simply to be reinstated in the world of *kodshim*: *l'ishtarei b'kodshim hu d'ka-mayti*. The *korban* is purely a part of the *taharah* process, not an act of *kaparah* for the original transgression.

### Nazirite Challenge

The Gemara presses further: according to Rabbi Shimon's logic, a *chatat* of a *nazir* should also require *nesachim*, since it too is not brought on account of sin. We know that in practice a *nazir*'s *chatat* does not have *nesachim* — so the challenge is: does this refute Rabbi Shimon's explanatory principle entirely, or does it merely reveal that we are working with established *halacha* and testing whether his explanation is consistent with the data?

This is a subtle and important question about the nature of the argument. If the Gemara is actually using Rabbi Shimon's principle to determine what the law *should* be, it would be proposing a real change in *halacha*. But that seems not to be the case — the Gemara appears to know the *halacha* and is asking whether Rabbi Shimon's explanation is coherent given the data. Either way, the challenge stands: the *chatat nazir* does not bring *nesachim*, yet according to Rabbi Shimon's framework it should, since it is not brought for sin.

Tosafot notes a more fundamental formulation available: the underlying rule is simply that only a *korban* that can be brought *beneder uvenidava* — as a freewill offering — brings *nesachim*. An *olah* and *shelamim* fall into that category and therefore always bring *nesachim*, even when obligatory (as in *musaf* offerings). A *chatat* and *asham*, which are by nature always obligatory and never voluntary, do not bring *nesachim* — with the single exception of the *metzora*'s *chatat* and *asham*, which the Torah explicitly associates with *nesachim*. This is the real rule. Rabbi Shimon is offering a *ta'ama d'kra* — an explanatory rationale — for why the rule works out the way it does, not a derivation of the rule itself.

### Nazirite Resolution

The Gemara resolves the challenge: *savar lah keRabbi Eliezer haKappar* — Rabbi Shimon holds in accordance with Rabbi Eliezer haKappar, who maintains that a *nazir* is also considered a sinner. Therefore, even the *chatat* of a *nazir* is brought in the context of sin, and the data remains consistent with Rabbi Shimon's explanation.

Tosafot raises a difficulty by noting that other passages in the Gemara discuss the extent of Rabbi Eliezer haKappar's position. Some *Gemarot* suggest that only a *nazir tamei* — one who became ritually impure and thereby failed to complete his *nezirut* — is considered a sinner by Rabbi Eliezer haKappar, because through impurity he undermined his commitment. Other *Gemarot* suggest that even a *nazir tahor* — one who completed his *nezirut* without any impurity — is considered a sinner simply because he denied himself the permitted pleasure of wine; even without regret, there is an element of improper self-denial. The present Gemara, Tosafot observes, seems to require that even the *nazir tahor* be considered a sinner according to Rabbi Eliezer haKappar — because it is specifically the *nazir tahor* whose *chatat* is an animal offering of the type that could theoretically require *nesachim*. The *nazir tamei* brings a different type of sacrifice. So the challenge and its resolution only cohere if Rabbi Eliezer haKappar's category of "sinner" encompasses the *nazir tahor*.

## Rabban Gamliel

### Animal-Like Actions

The Gemara now introduces a *braita* in which Rabban Gamliel offers an independent explanation for why the *sotah*'s *mincha* is made from barley. He addresses the sages — referred to here as *sofrim* — with a certain formality, saying *hanichu li ve'edreshenu kemin chomer* — "leave me and I will expound it beautifully." The phrase *kemin chomer* is interpreted by Rashi as something golden and beautiful, like a fine clasp; Tosafot suggests it may mean a pearl or jewel. Either way, Rabban Gamliel signals that he has a particularly elegant formulation to offer. The dynamic is intriguing: one wonders whether the designation *sofrim* here reflects a distinction between those whose specialty is *halacha* — Rabban Gamliel's primary domain — and those whose specialty is *drash* and symbolic interpretation, prompting his somewhat pointed request to be allowed to speak. A similar dynamic appears elsewhere in the literature, as when Rabbi Akiva is told that his area is the hardest legal tractates like *nega'im* and *ohalot*, not *aggadah*.

Rabban Gamliel's explanation: *kesheim shema'aseha ma'aseh beheimah kach korbanah ma'achal beheimah* — just as her actions were like those of an animal — following her desires without human moral restraint — so too her offering is the food of an animal. This is, of course, a deeply dehumanizing framing, and, as has been noted throughout these pages, it presumes her guilt.

### Wealth Objection

The preceding explanation, attributed to Rabbi Meir, was that since she fed her lover the delicacies of the world (*ma'adanei olam*), her offering is the food of animals (*ma'achal beheimah*). Rabban Gamliel's objection to Rabbi Meir is that this reasoning works only for a wealthy woman who could actually feed her lover delicacies. What about a poor woman? The explanation simply does not account for all cases.

### Universal Explanation

Rabban Gamliel's explanation, by contrast, applies universally. It does not depend on what she fed her lover or what her financial means were. Regardless of her economic situation, the characterization of her actions as animal-like — as driven by appetite rather than moral agency — is the same. Therefore, the offering that symbolizes those actions is uniformly the food of animals.

## Water Ritual

### Vessel and Water

The Gemara now presents the next Mishnah, which describes the preparation of the bitter waters. The *kohen* would bring a *pili shel cheres* — a pottery vial or vessel — and place into it *chatzi log mayim min ha-kiyor*, half a *log* of water from the *kiyor* (the laver in the Temple). A half-*log* is approximately double a *revi'it*, corresponding to something like six or seven ounces — roughly the size of a standard cup.

### Quarter-Log Measure

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees, holding that only a *revi'it* of water is required. His reasoning will be explained later: since Rabbi Yehuda holds that less text was written on the scroll that is erased into the water (*keshem shemi'at bichtav*), less water is needed to dissolve it.

### Heichal Procedure

The *kohen* would then enter the *Heichal* — the inner sanctum of the Temple, where the *Shulchan* and *Menorah* were located — and turn to his right. This rightward turn reflects the general principle in the Temple that all turns are made to the right, the direction associated with primacy and strength. (The same principle governs, for instance, the direction in which the *Sefer Torah* is carried around, which results in a counter-clockwise procession, since one always moves to one's right. Similarly, the *kallah* circles the *chatan* in this direction.)

### Marble Tile

At a specific location in the *Heichal*, there was a space of one cubit by one cubit (*ama al ama*) covered by a marble tile (*tavla shel shayish* — *tavla* being a Latin-derived term). Set into this tile was an iron ring, allowing the tile to be lifted.

### Retrieving Dirt

The *kohen* would lift the tile — presumably recessed into the floor so as not to be a hazard — and retrieve dirt from the natural ground beneath it.

### Dirt in Water

He would then place this dirt into the water, enough so that it was visible floating on the surface: *kedei sheyeira'eh al hamayim*. The verse cited is: *min ha'afar asher yihyeh bekarka hamishkan yikach hakohen venatan el ha-mayim* — "from the dust that shall be on the floor of the *Mishkan*, the *kohen* shall take and place into the water."

## New Vessel Debate

### New Vessel Required

The Gemara cites a *tanna* who states that the pottery vessel must be new — *pili shel cheres chadasha* — in the view of Rabbi Yishmael. His reasoning is a *gezerah shavah*: he derives *kli kli* — from the shared term "vessel" — connecting the *sotah* ritual to the *metzora* ritual, where the Torah also specifies *kli cheres*. Just as by the *metzora* the vessel must be new, so too here.

### Metzora Comparison

How do we know that the *metzora*'s vessel must be new? The verse states: *veshachat et hatzippor ha'achat el kli cheres al mayim chayim* — the bird is slaughtered over a pottery vessel containing living water. The Gemara derives: just as the *mayim chayim* (living water — water drawn directly from a natural spring, never processed or used) has never been worked with, so too the vessel must never have been used — *kli shelo na'asta bo melacha*.

This generates a further question: if we are connecting the *sotah* ritual to the *metzora* ritual through this *gezerah shavah*, should we also require *mayim chayim* for the *sotah*? The Gemara notes that Rabbi Yishmael himself holds this position — he rules that the water of the *kiyor* comes from an underground spring and therefore qualifies as *mayim chayim*. The *Chachamim*, however, hold that the water of the *kiyor* need not be *mayim chayim* and can be drawn from other collected sources.

The Gemara then challenges the derivation itself: *ika l'mifrach* — one can challenge the extension from *metzora* to *sotah*. The *metzora* ritual involves additional requirements — cedar wood, hyssop, and crimson thread (*etz erez, ezov, ushni tola'at*) — which impose multiple demands on the ritual. Perhaps those additional requirements justify the further demand that the vessel be new. By the *sotah*, where no such parallel demands exist, we might not extend that conclusion.

### Blackening Invalidation

Rava resolves the matter in a way that applies to the *Chachamim*'s position — that the vessel need not be entirely new. He states: *lo shanu* — the teaching that the vessel need not be new applies only when it has not become blackened. *Aval nitachmu panav, psulin* — if its surface has blackened from use, the vessel is invalid.

### Unchanged Vessel

The reason: *dumya d'mayim* — the vessel must parallel the water. Just as the water must be *shelo nishtanu* — unchanged from its natural state — so too the vessel must be *shelo nishtaneh* — unchanged from its original condition. A vessel that has blackened through use has undergone a fundamental transformation and therefore no longer meets this standard.

Tosafot raises a difficulty here, noting that another Gemara in Avoda Zara makes a similar comparison — *kemayim, shelo nishtaneh* — in the context of *bishul akum*, yet that application does not map cleanly onto all the parameters of that law. Tosafot therefore concludes that the derivation here is an *asmachta* — a rabbinic enactment with scriptural support — and that the requirement that the vessel be in its unchanged condition is *derabbanan*, not *d'oraita*. By implication, Tosafot is similarly suggesting that *bishul akum* is rabbinic in nature, though that is not his primary point here.

## Nidcha Question

### Refiring Question

Rava poses a question: if the vessel had become blackened — and is therefore invalid — and was then refired in a kiln until it became white again, what is the law? *Mi amrinan kivan de'idchu idchu* — do we say that once it was pushed away (*nidcheh*) from eligibility, it can never return, even after being restored? This is the principle of *dichui* — that once an object has been rejected for sacred use, it cannot be reaccepted even if the defect is remedied. Or do we say *kivan de'hadar hadar* — since it has returned to a fit state, it is once again valid? Alternatively, one might argue that since the blackening was a transitory condition that has now been reversed, it was perhaps never a true *dichui* in the first place — perhaps comparable to a *mum over* (a passing blemish) that does not permanently disqualify.

### Metzora Items Invalid

To shed light on this question, the Gemara cites Rabbi Eliezer: the cedar, hyssop, and crimson string used in the *metzora* ritual — if they were used to form a strap to carry a pack on one's back — are *psulin*, invalid for subsequent use in the ritual. Presumably the concern is that they were bent or otherwise altered.

### Permanent Damage

The Gemara attempts to derive from this that even if such items could theoretically be straightened out, they remain invalid. It responds: *hatam de'iklaf iklufei* — there, the bending may have caused actual peeling or permanent structural damage that cannot truly be undone. Therefore the *pesul* is permanent not because of *dichui* per se, but because the physical object cannot be fully restored. This response limits the case's applicability to the *nidcha* question about the vessel: since the vessel *can* be fully restored through refiring, perhaps it does return to valid status.

### Melacha Question

Tosafot raises a difficulty with the Gemara's framing. In a *Tosefta* to Nega'im (chapter *Ein Metzora*), Rabbi Eliezer himself states that the cedar, hyssop, and crimson thread used in the *metzora* ritual must not have had *melacha* performed with them. This suggests that the invalidation in that case is not primarily about the bending at all, but about the prohibition of *melacha* with these items — yet the Gemara focuses entirely on the bending. Tosafot does not resolve this difficulty, but it highlights a real tension: if *melacha* is the operative concern, the Gemara's analysis of whether the bending is correctable seems beside the point.

The question also connects to the broader discussion of *dichui* in contexts beyond *korbanot*. The classic principle of *nidcheh* applies most clearly to sanctified *korbanot*. It becomes less clear how it applies to objects used in rituals associated with the Temple but not themselves sanctified — like the items for the *metzora* or the vessel for the *sotah*. There is also a distinct question about *bigdei kehunah*: Tosafot notes that a Gemara in Zevachim holds that priestly garments which became soiled or torn invalidate the Temple service if worn in that state, yet if laundered they are restored to valid status. Priestly garments are sanctified objects, yet a correctable defect does not permanently disqualify them. One might contrast this with the blackened vessel, where the restoration requires refiring — perhaps a more drastic intervention — and ask whether that more fundamental transformation constitutes permanent *dichui*. These various permutations — *korban*, *mitzvah* object, Temple ritual object that is not a *korban*, and sanctified garments — each raise distinct questions about whether and how the concept of *dichui* applies.

## Dirt Sources

### Mishkan Ground

The Gemara turns to a *braita* analyzing the verse: *min ha'afar asher yihyeh bekarka haMishkan yikach hakohen venatan el ha-mayim*. One might have thought that the *kohen* could simply bring dirt from outside and carry it in. The *Talmud lomar* — the teaching that counters this — is *bekarka haMishkan*: the dirt must come from the floor of the *Mishkan* itself, not from an external source. On the other hand, one might have thought that *bekarka haMishkan* requires the *kohen* to actually dig up the floor with a shovel — and this too is rejected. *Asher yihyeh* — "that shall be" — indicates dirt that simply happens to be on the floor, not dirt that must be actively excavated. The practical upshot: *yesh sham havei, ein sham ten sham* — if there is already loose dirt there, use it; if not, pre-lay some dirt on the floor so that it will be available when the time comes.

### Alternative Source

A second *braita* takes an entirely different approach. Reading *asher yihyeh* as the operative phrase rather than *bekarka haMishkan*, this opinion holds that any dirt will do — dirt can even be brought from outside. The word *bekarka haMishkan* is then available to teach something else entirely.

### Multiple Locations

According to this reading, *bekarka haMishkan* serves to include other locations beyond the original *Mishkan* in the wilderness. Issi ben Yehuda teaches: the verse comes to include the *karka* of Shilo, Nov, Givon, and even *Beis Olamim* — the permanent Temple. Dirt from the floors of any of these sacred locations would be valid for the *sotah* ritual.

### Nov Givon Problem

Rashi is deeply troubled by the inclusion of Nov and Givon and therefore maintains a different *girsa* of the text, excluding them and limiting the *ribbuy* to *Beis Olamim*. His reasoning: Shilo requires no *ribbuy* at all, since it was the actual *Mishkan* relocated from the wilderness — obviously it is included in the plain meaning of the verse. Nov and Givon, by contrast, had a diminished status: they functioned as a *bamah*, a high place, without the *Aron* and without the full status of a central sanctuary, and crucially, the *sotah* ritual would not have been performed there — it was not a place where individual communal rituals of this kind were conducted. Therefore Rashi considers it a corruption of the text to include Nov and Givon, and restricts the expansion to *Beis Olamim* alone.

Tosafot disagrees with Rashi's emendation of the *girsa* and defends the inclusion of Nov and Givon on the grounds that Issi ben Yehuda may follow the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda (cited in chapter *Ba Siman*) that individual ritual offerings performed at the *Ohel Mo'ed* in the wilderness could also be performed at the *Ohel Mo'ed* at Gilgal — suggesting there is a position according to which individual rituals like *sotah* could indeed be performed at Nov and Givon. To emend the text entirely on the basis of a difficulty, Tosafot says, is going too far.

### Kal V'Chomer

Issi ben Menachem holds that no *ribbuy* is needed to include *Beis Olamim*. A *kal v'chomer* suffices: if, regarding the lesser *tumah* of ordinary ritual impurity, the Torah makes no distinction between the *Mishkan* and the permanent *Beit HaMikdash* — one may not enter either while *tamei* — then certainly the weightier moral *tumah* of *eshet ish* (suspected adultery, referred to in the Torah as *venitme'ah*) should equally apply to the *Beit HaMikdash*. Therefore *Beis Olamim* requires no special *ribbuy*. What, then, does *bekarka haMishkan* teach according to Issi ben Menachem? Simply that one cannot bring dirt from a basket — it must come from the floor, as explained earlier.

This *kal v'chomer* is, frankly, a striking and puzzling one. The comparison being drawn is between two very different senses of *tumah*. The *tumah* of ordinary ritual impurity is a specific halachic status governed by the laws of entering sacred precincts. The *tumah* of *eshet ish* is a moral and spiritual designation — the Torah says *venitme'ah*, meaning she has become defiled through her actions — but this never generates a *halacha* that a known adulteress is forbidden to enter the *Beit HaMikdash*. Furthermore, the *kal v'chomer* itself runs in a counterintuitive direction: if ordinary *tumah* is kept *out* of the Temple, then the weighty *tumah* of *eshet ish* should... be processed inside it? The inference does not flow in an obvious way. Nevertheless, the analogy between these two distinct domains of *tumah* — the halachic-ritual and the moral-spiritual — is a fascinating one, and the *kal v'chomer*, however structurally unusual, serves to ground the *sotah* ritual's connection to the permanent *Beit HaMikdash* in a principle rather than a mere *ribbuy* of the text.
