# Nedarim 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Opening & Brit Milah

### Daf Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּתְעַסֵּק בַּמָּלוֹן תְּחִלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וַיְהִי בַּדֶּרֶךְ בַּמָּלוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** **Because he was occupied with lodging first** and did not immediately perform the mitzva of circumcision, **as it is stated: "And it came to pass on the way at the lodging-place"** (Exodus 4:24).

We open today on daf 32a, completing the third *perek* before transitioning into the fourth. As we will see, this is not merely the next chapter but represents a genuine category shift from the issues we have been dealing with. We pick up two lines from the top of the page with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.

### Brit Milah Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: לֹא לְמֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ בִּקֵּשׁ שָׂטָן לַהֲרוֹג, אֶלָּא לְאוֹתוֹ תִּינוֹק, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי חֲתַן דָּמִים אַתָּה לִי״, צֵא וּרְאֵה מִי קָרוּי חָתָן — הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר זֶה הַתִּינוֹק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: It was not Moses our teacher** that **Satan wanted to kill, but rather, that infant** who was not circumcised, **as it is stated: "Surely a bridegroom of blood are you to me"** (Exodus 4:25). **Go out and see: Who** does it make sense would be the one that **is called the bridegroom** in this instance? **You must say this is the infant,** since he is the one who entered the covenant of Abraham by means of the circumcision.

The *Gemara* here is focusing entirely on the *aggadata* appended to the *Mishna* concerning the importance of *brit milah*. The *Mishna* entered this territory because it discussed whether the terms *mulim* and *arelim* refer to the physical reality of who has or has not undergone *brit milah*, or whether they refer to Jews and non-Jews respectively. The *Mishna* determined the latter — these are identity categories, not anatomical descriptions. Accordingly, the *Gemara* proceeds with a series of *aggadot* about the supreme importance of *brit milah*. It is also worth noting that the *Gemara* in *Avodah Zarah* discusses this *Mishna* in the context of whether a woman can serve as a *mohelet*, again underscoring that *nimolim* is not purely about physical reality but about who is considered within the *brit* — an issue of identity. And of course, *ve-havai Arami hu* — one who is not within the *brit* is, by definition, outside.

### Physical vs. Conceptual

This placement at the close of the third *perek* of *Nedarim* is fascinatingly apt. *Nedarim* is a tractate about words creating reality, and *milah* itself also means word — so there is already something poetic here. The *aggadata* emphasizes how *brit milah* is about changing the physical reality of one's body, which stands in striking contrast to the phenomenon of *nedarim*, which changes the identity of things through words rather than through any alteration of the physical world.

### Milah vs. Neder

Yet the dialectic is not so simple, because returning to the beginning of the *Mishna*, who is considered *nimol* and who is considered *arel* is not determined purely by physical reality but also by the categories and words we use. There is thus an interesting tension throughout these first three *perakim* between the physical world of *issur ve-heter* — of things that create identity through bodily change — and the conceptual world shaped by thoughts and words, which is the world of *nedarim*. There may be no clean resolution, but the tension itself is illuminating.

## Tzipporah's Circumcision

### Moshe's Delay

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בַּר בִּיזְנָא: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁנִּתְרַשֵּׁל מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ מִן הַמִּילָה, בָּאוּ ״אַף״ וְ״חֵימָה״ וּבְלָעוּהוּ, וְלֹא שִׁיְּירוּ מִמֶּנּוּ אֶלָּא רַגְלָיו. מִיָּד ״וַתִּקַּח צִפֹּרָה צֹר וַתִּכְרֹת אֶת עׇרְלַת בְּנָהּ״, מִיָּד ״וַיִּרֶף מִמֶּנּוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda bar Bizna taught: At the time that Moses our teacher was negligent about the circumcision,** the destructive angels named **Af,** meaning anger, **and Ḥeima,** meaning wrath, **came and swallowed him, and only his legs were left** outside. **Immediately, "Zipporah took a flint, and cut off the foreskin of her son"** (Exodus 4:25), and **immediately "He let him alone"** (Exodus 4:26).

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel teaches: it was not Moshe Rabbeinu whose life was at risk. The verse says that Moshe tarried and should have seized the first opportunity to perform the *brit milah* rather than first settling into the inn. The risk, however, fell not on Moshe but, as Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel teaches, *ella le-oto tinnok* — upon the infant.

### Chatan Damim

The proof text is *ki chatan damim atah li* — "for you are a bridegroom of blood to me." The argument runs: go and see who is called a *chatan* here — it must be the infant. Admittedly, this is difficult, since if Tzipporah is the speaker, one would more naturally expect her *chatan* to be Moshe rather than her son.

### Rosh & Ran

The *Rosh*, in the left-hand column, addresses why the infant is called a *chatan*: *she-mitchatnin oto be-mitzvah rishonah* — one marries him off, as it were, through his first *mitzvah*, initiating him into the world of *mitzvot*. The *Ran* similarly explains *she-na'asah chatan al yedei brit* — he becomes a *chatan* by means of the *brit*. Both explanations have a somewhat circular quality — the infant is called *chatan* because through the *brit* one makes him a *chatan* — but there they stand. What is genuinely fascinating, however, is the underlying theological question: if it is the infant who was at risk, this is less a punishment of Moshe than a statement about the vulnerability of someone who deserves a *brit milah* and does not yet have one — someone who is, in a sense, not yet fully part of the Jewish nation, not yet within the *brit*.

### Blood Symbolism

In the way we actually perform a *brit milah*, the idea of *dam* — blood — is central to the ritual. There is the concept of *hatafat dam brit* for a conversion candidate who lacks a foreskin. The focus on *metzitzah b'peh* likewise reflects an intense ritual concern with blood. There is even a tradition that when a *brit milah* falls on *Rosh Hashanah*, the *ba'al tokei'a* who is also the *mohel* should not wipe the blood from his lips before sounding the *shofar*, so that the blood of the *brit milah* and the *korban* of Yitzchak are joined together.

This begins to tie the blood of *brit milah* to the blood of sacrifices. The entire episode of Tzipporah is, in fact, a *parasha* that foregrounds blood — blood as protection, blood as saving someone vulnerable from death. This clearly foreshadows what will happen in Egypt: the blood of the *Korban Pesach* saves those in the house from the *mashchit*. Here, too, something comes to destroy, and blood is what saves — presumably the infant. The blood of *brit milah* functions as a salvific, protective force, but this is unique to this *parasha*. Everywhere else in the *Torah*, *brit milah* is described in terms of removing the foreskin or making the covenant — *bayom ha-shmini yimol b'sar orlato*, *yimol lachem kol basar* — without any mention of blood. Only here does blood appear so prominently, tapping into the world of *korbanot*, which can itself be understood as a sublimation of child sacrifice: instead of sacrificing the child, you cut off the foreskin, shed some blood, and the child lives.

## Af & Cheima Angels

### Destructive Angels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה בִּיקֵּשׁ מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ לְהוֹרְגָן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הֶרֶף מֵאַף וַעֲזֹב חֵמָה״. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: לְחֵימָה הֲרָגוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״חֵמָה אֵין לִי״. וְהָכְתִיב: ״כִּי יָגֹרְתִּי מִפְּנֵי הָאַף וְהַחֵמָה״! תְּרֵי חֵימָה הֲווֹ. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: גּוּנְדָּא דְחֵימָה.
>
> **Translation:** **At that moment, Moses our teacher wanted to kill them, as it is stated: "Cease from anger [*af* ] and forsake wrath [*ḥeima*]"** (Psalms 37:8), which indicates that he wanted to harm them. **And there are those who say: He killed** the angel named **Ḥeima, as it is stated: "Wrath is not in me"** (Isaiah 27:4). The Gemara asks: How is it possible to say that he killed Ḥeima? **Isn't it written** that Moses himself said much later: **"For I was in dread of the anger and wrath"** (Deuteronomy 9:19)? The Gemara answers: **There are two** types of **wrath. And if you wish, say** that **the army of Ḥeima** remained but not the angel itself.

Rabbi Yehuda bar Bizna expounds: at the moment that Moshe Rabbeinu was tardy regarding the *brit milah*, two destructive angels called *Af* and *Cheima* — wrath and rage — came and swallowed him up. The phrase *vayevakesh hamito* is here being translated into these personified destructive forces. *Ve-lo shayru mimenu ela raglav* — the only part of his body they did not swallow was his legs, though the *Ran* notes this is a euphemism for his penis.

### Tzipporah Acts

*Miyad vatikkach Tzipporah tzor* — immediately Tzipporah grasped what was happening and picked up a flint. *Vatikrot et orlat b'nah* — she circumcised her son, and *vayeiref mimenu* — the angels released Moshe at once. One might wonder how Tzipporah intuited the connection. Perhaps she tried many things and this was the one that worked.

### Divine Wrath

*Be-ota sha'ah bikesh Moshe Rabbeinu lehorgan* — at that moment Moshe wished to destroy those two angels, as the verse is read: *heref me-af va-azov cheimah* — release *Af* and abandon *Cheima* — understood as Moshe wishing to seize and eliminate them. God, however, indicated that the idea of divine anger is a necessary part of the world. The old question of why evil forces exist in a good world underlies this passage: to some degree these forces are necessary, even if the question remains whether they need to be quite as destructive as they are.

### Cheima Destroyed?

*Ve-yesh omrim le-Cheima harag* — some say Moshe actually did succeed in destroying *Cheima*, as implied by the phrase *Cheima ein li* — I no longer have *Cheima*. But then a later verse quotes Moshe himself saying *ki agorti mipnei ha-af ve-ha-cheima* — I was fearful of *Af* and *Cheima* — suggesting *Cheima* still existed at the end of his career. The *Gemara* resolves this: *trei Cheima havu* — there were two types of *Cheima*. Moshe destroyed one but not the other. Alternatively, *Gundat de-Cheima* — the verse refers not to *Cheima* itself but to the forces and retinue of *Cheima*, which Moshe could not eliminate even after destroying *Cheima* proper.

### Yetzer Hara

This episode calls to mind the *midrashim* that relate how, in the time of the Second Temple, the *Anshei Knesset Ha-Gedolah* convened and succeeded in destroying the *yetzer hara* of *avodah zarah*. The same theological question animates both traditions: if God gave us a good world, why do destructive forces persist? The answer seems to be that to some degree they are necessary — but the question of whether they must be as powerful and pervasive as they are remains open.

## Brit Milah Greatness

### Avraham's Mitzvot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: גְּדוֹלָה מִילָה, שֶׁאֵין לָךְ מִי שֶׁנִּתְעַסֵּק בְּמִצְוֹת כְּאַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ, וְלֹא נִקְרָא תָּמִים אֶלָּא עַל שֵׁם מִילָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הִתְהַלֵּךְ לְפָנַי וֶהְיֵה תָמִים״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְאֶתְּנָה בְרִיתִי בֵּינִי וּבֵינֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Great is** the mitzva of **circumcision, for there is no one who was engaged in mitzvot like Abraham our Patriarch, and** yet **he was called wholehearted only due to** the mitzva of **circumcision, as it is stated: "Walk before Me and you should be wholehearted"** (Genesis 17:1), **and it is written** in the next verse: **"And I will make My covenant between Me and you"** (Genesis 17:2), and Abraham was then commanded with regard to circumcision. This indicates that he was not called wholehearted until he performed circumcision.

A *baraita* is introduced: *Rebbi omer: gedola milah she-ein lecha mi she-nit'asek be-mitzvot ke-Avraham Avinu* — great is *brit milah*, for there was no one who was as devoted to *mitzvot* as Avraham Avinu — and yet he was not called *tamim*, whole and complete, except on account of *brit milah*. *She-ne'emar: hithalech lefanai ve-heyeh tamim* — walk before Me and be complete — followed immediately by *ve-etna briti beini u-veinecha* — and I will establish My covenant between Me and you. The verse from *brit milah* follows directly.

This raises a reflection worth pausing over. Avraham symbolizes, in one sense, Jewish identity as an ethnic or familial category — he predates the full system of *taryag mitzvot*, and all who descend from him are part of the Jewish family. This is the language of *Bnei Yaakov* to Shechem: *ki lo nuchal latet et achotenu le-ish asher lo orlah ki cherpah hi lanu* — it is shameful to give our sister to one who is uncircumcised. Here *brit milah* is pure ethnic-familial identity. By contrast, in the episode of Moshe, *brit milah* is connected to *Moshe Rabbeinu* and the world of *mitzvot* proper. These are two distinct models of what *brit milah* symbolizes, and they have implications for how one understands *geirus* as well.

Yet the *baraita* here, even while invoking Avraham, frames the greatness of *brit milah* squarely in the language of *mitzvot*: no one was as devoted in performing *mitzvot* as Avraham Avinu — not in the technical, commanded sense, but in the sense of alacrity and zeal in doing everything God asked — and still he was not *tamim* without *brit milah*. No matter how many *mitzvot* a person performs, this is the one that makes him, at least speaking of men, complete.

### Avraham Complete

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: גְּדוֹלָה מִילָה, שֶׁשְּׁקוּלָה כְּנֶגֶד כׇּל הַמִּצְוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי עַל פִּי הַדְּבָרִים הָאֵלֶּה וְגוֹ׳״, דָּבָר אַחֵר: גְּדוֹלָה מִילָה, שֶׁאִילְמָלֵא מִילָה לֹא נִתְקַיְּימוּ שָׁמַיִם וָאָרֶץ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִם לֹא בְּרִיתִי יוֹמָם וָלָיְלָה וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** so **great is** the mitzva of **circumcision that it is equal to all the mitzvot of the Torah, as it is stated** at the giving of the Torah: **"For according to these words** I have made a covenant with you and with Israel" (Exodus 34:27), and "covenant" refers to circumcision. **Alternatively,** so **great is** the mitzva of **circumcision that if not for circumcision heaven and earth would not have been established, as it is stated: "If My covenant be not with day and night,** I would not have appointed the ordinances of heaven and earth" (Jeremiah 33:25), and the covenant that exists day and night is the covenant of circumcision, as it is always found on the person's body.

*Davar acher*: great is *brit milah*, for without it Avraham was not *tamim* before God. The *pasuk* makes this explicit: the command to walk before God and be whole precedes and connects directly to the commandment of *brit milah*.

### Equal to All

*Davar acher*: *gedola milah she-shkula keneged kol ha-mitzvot she-ba-Torah* — great is *brit milah*, for it is of equal weight to all the other *mitzvot* in the *Torah*. The proof text is *ki al pi ha-devarim ha-eleh karati itcha brit ve-et Yisrael* — because of these matters I have established a covenant with you and with Israel. Since all *mitzvot* are part of a *brit*, and *brit milah* is itself a *brit*, it is therefore equal to all of them combined. One hears in such formulations an echo of the pressures of the Hellenistic period, when Greeks viewed *brit milah* as a disfiguring of the body and many Jews reversed their circumcision in order to compete in athletic contests in the nude. In that climate, declaring *brit milah* equal in weight to all other *mitzvot* was a powerful statement about whether one stands within or outside the Jewish people.

### Sustains World

*Davar acher*: *gedola milah she-ilmalei milah lo nitkaymu shamayim va-aretz* — great is *brit milah*, for were it not for *brit milah*, heaven and earth would not be sustained. The proof text: *im lo briti yomam va-layla chukot shamayim va-aretz lo samti* — if not for My covenant, day and night, I would not have established the laws of heaven and earth.

### vs. Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: גְּדוֹלָה תּוֹרָה, שֶׁאִילְמָלֵא תּוֹרָה לֹא נִתְקַיְּימוּ שָׁמַיִם וָאָרֶץ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִם לֹא בְרִיתִי יוֹמָם וָלָיְלָה חֻקּוֹת שָׁמַיִם וָאָרֶץ לֹא שָׂמְתִּי וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** this statement **disagrees** with the words **of Rabbi Eliezer, for Rabbi Eliezer said: Great is the Torah, for if not for Torah, heaven and earth would not have been established, as it is stated: "If My covenant be not with day and night,** I would not have appointed the ordinances of heaven and earth" (Jeremiah 33:25). According to Rabbi Eliezer, the covenant that exists day and night is the Torah, as it says: "You should contemplate it day and night" (Joshua 1:8).

*U-fliga de-Rabbi Eliezer* — this position argues with Rabbi Eliezer, who taught: *gedola Torah she-ilmalei Torah lo nitkaymu shamayim va-aretz* — great is *Torah*, for were it not for *Torah*, heaven and earth would not be sustained — citing the very same verse. Both readings draw on the same *pasuk* but identify its referent differently: one says *brit milah*, the other says *Torah*.

### Elitism Debate

This disagreement is itself quite fascinating from a sociological perspective. The claim that *Torah* keeps the world going feeds into a certain kind of elitism: historically, only a very small percentage of people could truly say they devoted themselves to *talmud Torah* in the deep sense. If it is *Torah* that sustains the world, then it is the *talmidei chachamim* who sustain the world. If it is *brit milah*, it excludes fifty percent of the population, but fully and equally includes the other fifty percent. There is a meaningful difference between a populist and an elitist framing embedded in this dispute. An additional dimension worth noting: *brit milah* is something done *to* a person — the infant has no consciousness of it, no intention. *Nedarim*, by contrast, is all intention. The most important things are sometimes entirely outside one's control.

## Avraham Confused

### Avraham Trembles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְאַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ ״הִתְהַלֵּךְ לְפָנַי וֶהְיֵה תָמִים״ אֲחָזַתּוּ רְעָדָה. אָמַר: שֶׁמָּא יֵשׁ בִּי דָּבָר מְגוּנֶּה. כֵּיוָן שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ: ״וְאֶתְּנָה בְרִיתִי בֵּינִי וּבֵינֶךָ״, נִתְקָרְרָה דַּעְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: At the time that the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Abraham our Patriarch: "Walk before Me and you should be wholehearted"** (Genesis 17:1), a sensation of **trembling seized him** and **he said: Perhaps there is something disgraceful about me** due to a transgression that I committed, and therefore I cannot be called complete. **When** God **said to him: "And I will make My covenant between Me and you"** (Genesis 17:2), **his mind was set at ease,** since he understood that the removal of the foreskin that he was now commanded to do was the reason he had not yet achieved completion.

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: at the moment that the Holy One Blessed be He said to Avraham Avinu *hithalech lefanai ve-heyeh tamim* — walk before Me and be complete — *ahazato ra'adah* — he was seized with trembling.

### Self-Doubt

Avraham said to himself: *shema yesh bi davar meguneh* — perhaps there is something unseemly, something disgraceful within me, that God must tell me to now become whole and complete? Is there some character deficiency I need to correct?

### God Clarifies

Then God said to him *ve-etna briti beini u-veinecha* — and I will establish My covenant between Me and you — and *nitkarrera da'ato* — Avraham's mind was cooled, calmed. He understood: it is not that his character is incomplete. It is something about his physical body, a *mitzvah* God has not yet commanded him. The incompleteness was not moral but physical — and the *brit milah* was the remedy.

## Mazal Discussion

### Avraham's Mazal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיּוֹצֵא אֹתוֹ הַחוּצָה״, אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, הִסְתַּכַּלְתִּי בַּמַּזָּל שֶׁלִּי וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא בֵּן אֶחָד. אָמַר לוֹ: צֵא מֵאִיצְטַגְנִינוּת שֶׁלְּךָ, אֵין מַזָּל לְיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expounds the verse **"and He brought him outside"** (Genesis 15:5): Abraham **said before Him: Master of the Universe, I looked at my constellation and** according to it **I** will **have only one son,** and a son has already been born to me, i.e., Ishmael. **He said to him: Emerge from your astrology** because **there is no constellation for the Jewish people,** as they are not subject to the influence of astrology.

*Vayotze oto ha-chutza* — God took him outside. This passage is actually two *perakim* earlier in the *Torah* than the *brit milah* narrative. Avraham said before God: *Ribono Shel Olam*, I have looked into my *mazal* — my astrological sign — and one *girsa* reads *ein li ela ben echad* — I have only one son — though this does not fit, since this episode precedes the birth of Yishmael. The preferable *girsa* is *ve-eini ra'ui l'holid ben* — I am not suited to have children — which makes sense given Avraham's advanced age and lack of offspring until that point.

### No Mazal Israel

God responded: *tze me-istagninus shelcha* — leave your astrology — *ein mazal le-Yisrael* — there is no *mazal* for Israel. The Jewish people are *lemala min ha-mazal*, above and beyond the forces of the astrological constellations.

### Name Change

Rashi cites this teaching on the *Torah* and concludes: *Avram eino molid, Avraham molid* — Avram does not have children, that is what you see in your *mazal*, but Avraham — when your name changes — will have children. This is not merely a legal loophole. In the same *parasha* where God commands *brit milah*, God adds the letter *heh* to Avraham's name.

### Transcendence

This, I would suggest, is the deeper point connecting this passage to the *brit milah* discussion. The Greeks criticized *brit milah* as disfiguring what God gave you — the body is perfect, why mutilate it? But the message of *brit milah* is the opposite: we are not subservient to the forces of nature. We can change the course of things, rise above what the natural order would dictate. *Ein mazal le-Yisrael* — the commandment of *brit milah* is itself an act of transcendence over the natural order. You transform yourself, you change the course of nature, and with your new name you become capable of things that seemed naturally impossible.

## Tamim Character

### Reciprocal Wholehearted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: כׇּל הַמְתַמֵּים עַצְמוֹ — הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מִתַּמֵּים עִמּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עִם חָסִיד תִּתְחַסָּד עִם גִּבּוֹר תָּמִים תִּתַּמָּם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: Anyone who conducts himself with wholeheartedness, the Holy One, Blessed be He, treats him with wholeheartedness, as it is stated: "With the devout You act devoutly, and with the one who is strong in his wholeheartedness You act wholeheartedly"** (II Samuel 22:26).

Rabbi Yitzchak said: *kol ha-matmim atzmo ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu matmim imo* — whoever conducts himself with wholeheartedness, God deals with him wholeheartedly. The proof text: *im chasid titchasad, im gibbor tamim titamam* — with the righteous You act righteously, with the strong in his wholeheartedness You deal wholeheartedly.

### Success Follows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי הוֹשַׁעְיָא: כׇּל הַמְתַמֵּים עַצְמוֹ — שָׁעָה עוֹמֶדֶת לוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הִתְהַלֵּךְ לְפָנַי וֶהְיֵה תָמִים״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיִיתָ לְאַב הֲמוֹן גּוֹיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Hoshaya said: Anyone who acts wholeheartedly, time will stand for him,** i.e., he will be successful, **as it is stated: "Walk before Me and you should be wholehearted"** (Genesis 17:1), **and it is written: "And you shall be the father of a multitude of nations"** (Genesis 17:4).

Rabbi Oshaya said: *kol ha-matmim atzmo sha'ah omedet lo* — whoever makes himself *tamim* will have great success. The proof text: *hithalech lefanai ve-heyeh tamim* — followed by *ve-hayita le-av hamon goyim* — and you will become the father of a multitude of nations.

### Physical to Internal

A subtle but important shift has occurred here. We have been speaking about the supreme greatness of *brit milah*, and how Avraham was not complete until he made this physical change in his body. Now we are saying that *tamim* — complete, whole, wholehearted — is a character trait, not a reference to *brit milah* per se. It is about how you conduct yourself in your inner life. The message of the *pasuk* has been extended from the physical act to the internal disposition. One might say the physical act is the external expression and the character is the internal: the external-internal relationship. But this is nonetheless a *chiddush* — it is not obvious, and the *Gemara* makes a point of it.

### Divination Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי: כֹּל הַמְנַחֵשׁ — לוֹ נַחַשׁ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי לֹא נַחַשׁ בְּיַעֲקֹב״. וְהָא בְּלָמֶד אָלֶף כְּתִיב! אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם מִדָּה כְּנֶגֶד מִדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: Anyone who divines,** i.e., he guesses and looks for signs about the future, **the sign** will injure **him, as it is stated: "For there is to him [*lo*] divination with Jacob"** (Numbers 23:23). The Gemara asks: **But it is written** *lo* **with** the letters ***lamed alef*,** meaning "no divination," as opposed to with the letters *lamed vav*, meaning "there is to him divination." The straightforward meaning of the verse is that there is no divination with regard to Jacob. **Rather,** the reason that he will be injured is not based on the verse but rather **due to** the concept of **measure for measure:** Since he attempts to tell his fortune, it injures him.

The opposite of *tamim* is illustrated next. *Kol ha-menachesh lo nachash* — whoever practices divination will have the snake come back at him. *Tamim* means having pure, simple faith in God — not trying to beat the system, not trying to use magical devices to divine the future. Divination (*nachash*) reflects an anxiety-driven attempt to secure oneself against the unknown rather than trusting in God. The proof text cited is *ki lo nachash be-Yaakov* — there is no divination in Yaakov — though the *Gemara* notes the word is spelled *lo* with a *lamed-alef*, meaning "there is none," not *lo* with a *vav*, meaning "to him." The teaching is therefore grounded not in a textual reading but in the principle of *mida ke-neged mida*: if you try to circumvent God's way and figure things out through divination, it will come back to harm you. Simple faith is the best protection against anxiety about the future.

### Pure Faith Reward

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי אַהֲבָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא: כׇּל אָדָם שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְנַחֵשׁ, מַכְנִיסִין אוֹתוֹ בִּמְחִיצָה שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת אֵין יְכוֹלִין לִיכָּנֵס בְּתוֹכָהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי לֹא נַחַשׁ בְּיַעֲקֹב וְלֹא קֶסֶם בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Ahava, son of Rabbi Zeira, teaches: Any person who does not divine** his future **is brought inside a partition** close to God to a place **that even the ministering angels cannot enter inside, as it is stated: "For there is no divination with Jacob, neither is there any enchantment with Israel,** now it is said to Jacob and Israel what has God wrought" (Numbers 23:23). In other words, matters are revealed to Israel that even the angels do not know, since Israel is closer to God than the angels.

Ahava bar Rabbi Zeira taught: *kol adam she-eino menachesh machnisin oto be-mechitza she-afilu malachei ha-sharet ein yecholim likanes betochah* — whoever does not practice divination is brought into an inner chamber so close to God that even the ministering angels cannot enter there. The proof text: *ki lo nachash be-Yaakov ve-lo kesem be-Yisrael* — there is no divination in Yaakov and no witchcraft in Israel — and the end of the verse: *ka-et ye'amer le-Yaakov u-le-Yisrael mah pa'al El* — now it will be told to Yaakov and to Israel what God has done. If you have pure faith in how God acts in the world and what God will do, you will know better than the angels what God is going to do. Actively trying to divine God's plans causes you to forfeit that closeness; simply trusting God elevates you beyond the angels.

## Avraham Punished

### Egypt Slavery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִפְּנֵי מָה נֶעֱנַשׁ אַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ וְנִשְׁתַּעְבְּדוּ בָּנָיו לְמִצְרַיִם מָאתַיִם וְעֶשֶׂר שָׁנִים — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁעָשָׂה אַנְגַּרְיָיא בְּתַלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיָּרֶק אֶת חֲנִיכָיו יְלִידֵי בֵיתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu said** that **Rabbi Elazar said: For what reason was Abraham our Patriarch punished and his children enslaved to Egypt** for **210 years? Because he made a draft [*angarya*] of Torah scholars, as it is stated: "He led forth his trained men, born in his house"** (Genesis 14:14). These trained men that he took to war were actually his disciples, who were Torah scholars.

Rabbi Abbahu said in the name of Rabbi Elazar: why was Avraham Avinu punished and his descendants enslaved in Egypt for 210 years?

### Drafting Issue

The first explanation: *mipnei she-asah angarya be-talmidei chachamim* — because he conscripted *talmidei chachamim* into his army. The verse: *vayarek et chanichav yelidei beito* — he armed his *chanichav*, those trained in his household. The word *chanich* is understood, based on the *Ran*, as *le-chanech ba-Torah* — those initiated into *Torah* study — the *Torah* of Shem and Ever, where Yaakov later studied, the Torah inherited from Noach. This *Gemara* has been cited extensively in *Charedi* circles in Israel as a basis for exempting *yeshiva* students from military service: Avraham drafted *talmidei chachamim*, and for that his descendants were enslaved in Egypt. It is one of the more consequential applications of *aggadata* in contemporary policy debates.

### Exceeded Attributes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהִפְרִיז עַל מִדּוֹתָיו שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בַּמָּה אֵדַע כִּי אִירָשֶׁנָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said: Because he greatly examined [*hifriz*] the characteristics of the Holy One, Blessed be He, as it is stated: "Whereby shall I know that I shall inherit it?"** (Genesis 15:8).

Shmuel offers a second, more textually direct explanation: *she-hifriz al middotav shel ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu* — he went too far regarding God's attributes, exceeded what was appropriate in challenging God. The verse: *bama eida ki irashenah* — by what shall I know that I will inherit the land of Israel? Rather than simply trusting in God's explicit promise, Avraham pushed further and demanded proof.

### Faith vs. Demand

This connects directly to the earlier *tamim* discussion. The theme of *tamim* is: have faith, trust that things will work out, and certainly when God explicitly promises you something, do not say *bama eida* — how will I know? That is the antithesis of the pure trust we described. Avraham, the one time he failed to embody *tamim* and demanded proof rather than accepting God's word, was punished accordingly. The drafting of *talmidei chachamim* is something of a digression from the central thematic thread; it is Shmuel's explanation that ties most naturally into the surrounding discussion.

## Lost Souls

### Separated People

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: שֶׁהִפְרִישׁ בְּנֵי אָדָם מִלְּהִכָּנֵס תַּחַת כַּנְפֵי הַשְּׁכִינָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תֶּן לִי הַנֶּפֶשׁ וְהָרְכֻשׁ קַח לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** He was punished **because he distanced people from entering under the wings of the Divine Presence, as it is stated** that the king of Sodom said to him: **"Give me the people and take the goods to yourself"** (Genesis 14:21), but Abraham refused to take any goods either. If he had not listened to the king of Sodom and had allowed the people to remain with him, he would have brought the prisoners under the wings of the Divine Presence.

Rabbi Yochanan said: the problem was that Avraham separated people from coming under the wings of the *Shechina*. He had an opportunity to bring people into the covenant, under God's protection, and he let it slip away.

### Melech Sedom

The verse: *ten li ha-nefesh ve-ha-rechush kach lach* — the King of Sodom said to Avram: give me the souls, the people you recaptured, and you may keep the spoils. Avram declined the booty — but he should have done the reverse: keep the people, let Sodom have the money. He had an opportunity to bring these people into his community, to serve God, and he lost them. He refused everything — the money and the people — when he should have insisted on keeping the people and surrendered only the spoils.

### Geirus Today

This teaching lends itself to applications as pointed as those from the *talmidei chachamim* passage, but in a very different direction. One could observe that when there are large populations — such as halachically non-Jewish *olim* — who could be brought under the wings of the *Shechina* through a proactive approach to *geirus*, and instead impossibly high standards and communal inaction mean they are left outside, the parallel to Avraham's lost opportunity is uncomfortable. The *aggadata* of the Talmud contains a multiplicity of voices, and one must be honest about the fact that different communities selectively cite those that support their agenda. Both the *talmidei chachamim* passage and this one are genuine voices in the tradition.

## Gematria Teachings

### Eliezer = 318

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שְׁמֹנָה עָשָׂר וּשְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת״, אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי בַּר אַבָּא: אֱלִיעֶזֶר כְּנֶגֶד כּוּלָּם. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אֱלִיעֶזֶר הוּא, דְּחוּשְׁבָּנֵיהּ הָכִי הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Torah states that he took **"eighteen and three hundred"** (Genesis 14:14) men to war. **Rabbi Ami bar Abba said: Eliezer** was **equivalent** to **all of them. There are** those **who say:** Only **Eliezer is** referred to here, **as the numerical value** of the letters of his name **is this** amount, i.e., 318.

The *Gemara* continues with the episode of Avraham arming his household to pursue Lot's captors: *shmona asar u-shlosh me'ot* — 318. Rabbi Ammi bar Abba says: *Eliezer kineged kulam* — Eliezer was equivalent to all 318 in his capacity to wage war. Some say further: the 318 did not literally mean 318 people at all — it was just Eliezer alone. The proof: the *gematria* of *Eliezer* is 318. The *reish* is 300, the *lamed* and *ayin* together are 100, and the *yud-zayin-alef* are 18, totaling 318.

### Age Three

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי בַּר אַבָּא: בֵּן שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים הִכִּיר אַבְרָהָם אֶת בּוֹרְאוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עֵקֶב אֲשֶׁר שָׁמַע אַבְרָהָם בְּקֹלִי״ — חוּשְׁבָּנֵיהּ מְאָה וְשִׁבְעִין וּתְרֵין.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ami bar Abba said: Abraham recognized his Creator at the age of three years, as it is stated: "Because [*ekev*] Abraham hearkened to My voice"** (Genesis 26:5). **The numerical value** of the letters of the word *ekev* is **172,** indicating that he observed the *halakha* for this many years. If Abraham lived until 175 then his first recognition of the Creator must have been at the age of three.

Rabbi Ammi bar Abba further taught: *ben gimel shanim hikir Avraham et boro* — Avraham recognized his Creator at the age of three. The proof: *ekev asher shama Avraham be-koli* — because Avraham listened to My voice. The *gematria* of *ekev* is 172. Avraham lived to 175. Therefore Avraham listened to God's voice for 172 of his years — meaning he recognized God at age three.

This is the occasion for a notable dispute between the *Rambam* and the *Ra'avad*. In the opening of *Mishneh Torah*, the *Rambam* describes Avraham coming to know God through philosophical inquiry and suggests Avraham became aware of God at the age of 40. The *Ra'avad* quotes this very *Gemara* in objection: the age was three, not 40. The disagreement reflects a deeper divide: the *Rambam* understood knowing God as an intellectual achievement requiring mature philosophical reasoning, and therefore could not accept the notion that a three-year-old *knew* God in any meaningful sense — he therefore preferred a different tradition, apparently from a *Midrash*, which gave the age as 40. The *Ra'avad*, more mystical in orientation, had no difficulty accepting that a three-year-old could intuit or experience God in a manner that counts as *recognition*. The *Rambam* effectively discounts the *aggadic* tradition of age three by privileging the philosophical model.

### Satan = 364

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָמֵי בַּר אַבָּא: ״הַשָּׂטָן״ בְּחוּשְׁבָּנֵיהּ תְּלָת מְאָה וְשִׁיתִּים וְאַרְבְּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rami bar Abba said:** The letters of the term **the Satan [*haSatan*] in numerical value** is **364,** which equals the number of days of the year, except for Yom Kippur, during which he has no power.

Rami bar Abba said: the *gematria* of *ha-Satan* — *shin-tet-nun*, along with the definite article *heh* — is 364. There are 365 days in the solar year. This means Satan has no power over Israel for one day of the year — which is Yom Kippur. The *gematria* works by the solar calendar even though our calendar is lunar; the point is the numerical symbolism, not calendrical precision.

### 248 Limbs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: שְׁמֹנָה עָשָׂר וּשְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת״, אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר אַבָּא: כְּתִיב ״אַבְרָם״, וּכְתִיב ״אַבְרָהָם״. בַּתְּחִלָּה הִמְלִיכוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עַל מָאתַיִם וְאַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה אֵבָרִים, וּלְבַסּוֹף הִמְלִיכוֹ עַל מָאתַיִם וְאַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁמוֹנֶה אֵבָרִים,
>
> **Translation:** **And Rami bar Abba said: It is written "Abram," and** after he was commanded to perform circumcision **it is written "Abraham"** (Genesis 17:5). **Initially the Holy One, Blessed be He, enthroned him** as ruler **over 243 limbs,** which is the numerical equivalent of the letters of the word Abram. **And in the end,** after he was circumcised, **He enthroned him** as ruler **over 248 limbs,** which is the numerical equivalent of the letters of the word Abraham.

Rabbi Ammi bar Abba said: one verse says *Avram* and another says *Avraham* — what is the significance of the name change? Initially, God gave Avraham dominion over 243 of his limbs. A person has 248 limbs, and the *gematria* of *Avram* is 243. Ultimately, God gave him dominion over all 248 — *u-le-vasof himlicho al matayim ve-arba'im u-shmonah* — the addition of the *heh* in the name *Avraham* accounting for the five additional limbs.

### Five Control Limbs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵלּוּ הֵן: שְׁתֵּי עֵינַיִם, וּשְׁתֵּי אׇזְנַיִם, וְרֹאשׁ הַגְּוִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** **These are** the additional limbs: **Two eyes, and two ears, and the tip of the sex organ.** Following his circumcision, he had total control over them, and they performed only according to his will.

Which were the five limbs over which he did not originally have dominion? *Shte einayim* — his two eyes, *u-shte oznayim* — his two ears, and *ve-rosh ha-geviyah* — the tip of his penis. The connection to *brit milah* is obvious. These are the organs of perception and desire: what we see and hear can lead us astray — *lo tasuru acharei levavechem ve-acharei eineichem* — and the sexual drive is what compels one to pursue what the eyes and ears have stirred. Avraham, through *brit milah*, gained mastery over those organs. One can try to stop up one's ears or close one's eyes, but the drives of perception and desire are not always within voluntary control. The *brit milah* represents symbolic — and perhaps more than symbolic — mastery over them.

### Control Drives

The *Rambam* in fact follows precisely this reading of *brit milah*: its purpose is to curb the sexual appetite. The *Rambam* states explicitly that *brit milah* decreases sexual pleasure, and that this is its intended function — we are meant to control and master our sexual drive rather than be controlled by it. This view has obvious contemporary resonance in debates about whether circumcision increases or decreases sexual pleasure, but regardless of the empirical question, the *Gemara* here establishes that *symbolically*, what *brit milah* is about is gaining control and mastery over one's drives rather than letting them dominate. And the insight of one of the students is well-taken: *brit milah* is done *to* the infant — he is passive — and perhaps the result is mastery over precisely those things in life over which one is passive. You cannot always control what enters your eyes and ears; what you can control is how you respond. The organs of reception become subject to your will.

## City Parable

### Siege Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָמֵי בַּר אַבָּא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״עִיר קְטַנָּה וַאֲנָשִׁים וְגוֹ׳״. ״עִיר קְטַנָּה״ — זֶה הַגּוּף, ״וַאֲנָשִׁים בָּהּ מְעַט״ — אֵלּוּ אֵבָרִים, ״וּבָא אֵלֶיהָ מֶלֶךְ גָּדוֹל וְסָבַב אֹתָהּ״ — זֶה יֵצֶר הָרָע, ״וּבָנָה עָלֶיהָ מְצוֹדִים וַחֲרָמִים״ — אֵלּוּ עֲוֹנוֹת,
>
> **Translation:** **And Rami bar Abba said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "There was a little city and** few **men** in it, and there came a great king against it, and besieged it, and built great bulwarks against it. Now there was found in it a man poor and wise, and he by his wisdom delivered the city; yet no man remembered that same poor man" (Ecclesiastes 9:14–15)? **"A little city," this** is referring to **the body; "and few men in it," this** is referring to **the limbs; "and there came a great king against it and besieged it," this** is referring to **the evil inclination; "and built great bulwarks against it," these** are **sins.**

Rami bar Abba expounds on a verse in *Kohelet*: *ir ketana* — a small city, *va-anashim bo me'at* — with few people — and a great king comes and lays siege to it, surrounding it. *Ir ketana zeh ha-guf* — the small city is the body. *Va-anashim ba me'at elu evarim* — the few inhabitants are the limbs.

### Interpretation

Avraham said to himself: *shema yesh bi davar meguneh* — perhaps there is something unseemly within me. *Uva eleha melech gadol* — and a great king comes to besiege it: *zeh yetzer hara* — this is the *yetzer hara*. The small, weak body is besieged by the powerful forces of lust and drive seeking to take control.

### Sins Traps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּבָנָה עָלֶיהָ מְצוֹדִים וַחֲרָמִים״ — אֵלּוּ עֲוֹנוֹת,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expounds on the next section of the verse: **"And built great bulwarks against it," these** are **sins.**

*U-banah aleha metzudim ve-charamim* — and he builds against it catapults and traps: *elu avonot* — these are the sins. The *yetzer hara* lays sins as pitfalls and snares for you to fall into. The siege is not only the initial assault but the accumulated network of temptations that progressively encircle the person.

### Yetzer Hatov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּמָצָא בָהּ אִישׁ מִסְכֵּן וְחָכָם״ — זֶה יֵצֶר טוֹב, ״וּמִלַּט הוּא אֶת הָעִיר בְּחׇכְמָתוֹ״ — זוֹ תְּשׁוּבָה וּמַעֲשִׂים טוֹבִים. ״וְאָדָם לֹא זָכַר אֶת הָאִישׁ הַמִּסְכֵּן הַהוּא״ — דְּבִשְׁעַת יֵצֶר הָרָע לֵית דְּמִדְּכַר לֵיהּ לְיֵצֶר טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expounds on the next section of the verse: **"Now there was found in it a man poor and wise," this** is referring to **the good inclination; "and he by his wisdom delivered the city," this** is referring to **repentance and good deeds** that are caused by the good inclination. **"Yet no man remembered that same poor man"** means **that when the evil inclination** overcomes the good inclination **no one remembers the good inclination.**

*U-matza vah ish misken ve-chacham* — and there was found in the city a poor but wise man: *zeh yetzer tov* — this is the *yetzer hatov*, that small quiet voice of conscience. *U-milat et ha-ir be-chochmato* — and he saved the city with his wisdom. And what is that wisdom? *Zu teshuvah u-ma'asim tovim* — repentance and good deeds.

### Teshuvah Future

The word *teshuvah* is striking here because it normally refers to repairing past sins — you have already transgressed, and now you repent. But in this *mashal*, the *teshuvah* is described as *saving* the city, that is, protecting it from future conquest. The resolution: when the *Gemara* says the sins are traps, it means not merely that you might sin, but that once you have committed some sins, those past sins become traps for the future. The *yetzer hara* whispers: you have already sinned, you are not a *tzaddik*, why try to live up to a higher standard? Past sins become an argument for future capitulation. And what saves you? The *yetzer hatov* saying: you can do *teshuvah*, you can actually live differently. So *teshuvah* functions here not only as a remedy for the past but as a protection against being snared by the past into further sin in the future.

### Forgotten Helper

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאָדָם לֹא זָכַר אֶת הָאִישׁ הַמִּסְכֵּן הַהוּא״ — דְּבִשְׁעַת יֵצֶר הָרָע לֵית דְּמִדְּכַר לֵיהּ לְיֵצֶר טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **"Yet no man remembered that same poor man"** means **that when the evil inclination** overcomes the good inclination **no one remembers the good inclination.**

*Ve-adam lo zachar et ha-ish ha-misken ha-hu* — but no one remembered that poor man. The city was saved, but not through a visible act of heroism — through quiet wisdom — and so the man was forgotten. *De-visha'at yetzer hara leit de-midkar leh le-yetzer ha-tov* — when your lusts and drives are seizing control of you, it becomes very difficult to remember the voice of conscience, to recall how it saved you before. The power of desire crowds out the memory of the quieter voice that once protected you.

### Wisdom Strength

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַחׇכְמָה תָּעֹז לֶחָכָם מֵעֲשָׂרָה שַׁלִּיטִים״. ״הַחׇכְמָה תָּעֹז לֶחָכָם״ — זוֹ תְּשׁוּבָה וּמַעֲשִׂים טוֹבִים, ״מֵעֲשָׂרָה שַׁלִּיטִים״ — שְׁתֵּי עֵינַיִם, וּשְׁתֵּי אׇזְנַיִם, וּשְׁתֵּי יָדַיִם, וּשְׁתֵּי רַגְלַיִם, וְרֹאשׁ הַגְּוִיָּיה, וּפֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interprets the following verse in a similar homiletical manner: **"Wisdom is a stronghold to the wise man more than ten rulers** that are in a city" (Ecclesiastes 7:19). **"Wisdom is a stronghold to the wise man," this** is referring to **repentance and good deeds. "More than ten rulers,"** these are **the two eyes, and two ears, and two hands, and two legs, and the tip of the sex organ, and the mouth,** which are the limbs that are used by a person to interact with the world.

*Ha-chochmah ta'oz la-chacham me-asarah shalitim* — wisdom gives strength to the wise man more than ten shields. *Zo teshuvah u-ma'asim tovim* — again, repentance and good deeds. And the ten shields that wisdom surpasses are identified as: *shte einayim* — two eyes, *u-shte oznayim* — two ears, *u-shte yadayim* — two hands (the sense of touch, which also leads astray), *u-shte raglayim* — two legs, which physically bring a person to places of sin, *ve-rosh ha-geviyah* — the tip of the penis, and *ve-ha-peh* — the mouth, through which one talks, arouses appetite through speech, and violates *lashon hara*. These are the ten things that will lead a person astray — and the *yetzer hatov*, the wisdom of repentance and good deeds, is stronger than all ten.

### Ten Temptations

The thematic arc here is important. We have moved from *brit milah* as the source of mastery over the five organs — the eyes, ears, and tip of the penis — to the *yetzer hatov* as the inner force that gives strength over all ten instruments of temptation. Just as we observed the shift from physical *tamim* to *tamim* as a character trait, here too the *Gemara* moves from the physical *brit milah* giving control over drives to the developed inner conscience of the *yetzer hatov* providing protection. *Brit milah* is the foundational physical reality; *yetzer hatov* is the internalized extension of that same principle.

## Kohenim Origin

### Shem Kohen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זְכַרְיָה מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: בִּיקֵּשׁ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְהוֹצִיא כְּהוּנָּה מִשֵּׁם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהוּא כֹהֵן לְאֵל עֶלְיוֹן״. כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִקְדִּים בִּרְכַּת אַבְרָהָם לְבִרְכַּת הַמָּקוֹם — הוֹצִיאָהּ מֵאַבְרָהָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zekharya said in the name of Rabbi Yishmael: The Holy One, Blessed be He, wanted the priesthood to emerge from Shem,** so that his children would be priests, **as it is stated:** "And Melchizedek king of Salem brought forth bread and wine; **and he was priest of God the Most High"** (Genesis 14:18). **Once** Melchizedek, traditionally identified as Shem, **placed the blessing of Abraham before the blessing of the Omnipresent,** He had the priesthood **emerge from Abraham** in particular, and not from any other descendant of Shem.

Rabbi Zechariah said in the name of Rabbi Yishmael: God originally intended to bring the *kehunah* — the priesthood — from Shem. Not from within Bnei Yisrael, not from among any particular Jewish tribe, but from the descendants of Shem son of Noach. They would be the *kohanim* of the world for all future generations.

### Malki-Tzedek

The proof: *ve-hu kohen le-El elyon* — and he was a *kohen* to the Most High God — referring to Malki-Tzedek, king of Shalem, whom *Chazal* identify as Shem son of Noach. This establishes that Shem already served as priest, and his descendants were slated to continue in that role.

### Universalism

One should pause to appreciate the striking universalist dimension of this idea. Our conventional framework is that the Jewish people are chosen, and within the Jewish people the *kohanim* occupy the highest rung of sacred service. But this *Gemara* envisions an alternative reality: the category of *kohanim* would have been *non-Jews* — descendants of Shem, but not Jews — serving in a role of highest holiness, perhaps even in the Temple. It is a genuinely remarkable universalist vision, at least in potential.

### Blessing Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְבָרְכֵהוּ וַיֹּאמַר בָּרוּךְ אַבְרָם לְאֵל עֶלְיוֹן קֹנֵה שָׁמַיִם וָאָרֶץ וּבָרוּךְ אֵל עֶלְיוֹן״. אָמַר לוֹ אַבְרָהָם: וְכִי מַקְדִּימִין בִּרְכַּת עֶבֶד לְבִרְכַּת קוֹנוֹ? מִיָּד נְתָנָהּ לְאַבְרָהָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נְאֻם ה׳ לַאדֹנִי שֵׁב לִימִינִי עַד אָשִׁית אֹיְבֶיךָ הֲדֹם לְרַגְלֶיךָ״, וּבָתְרֵיהּ כְּתִיב: ״נִשְׁבַּע ה׳ וְלֹא יִנָּחֵם אַתָּה כֹהֵן לְעוֹלָם עַל דִּבְרָתִי מַלְכִּי צֶדֶק״. עַל דִּיבּוּרוֹ שֶׁל מַלְכִּי צֶדֶק.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is stated: "And he blessed him and said: Blessed be Abram of God Most High, Maker of heaven and earth, and blessed be God the Most High"** (Genesis 14:19–20). **Abraham said to him: And does one place the blessing of the servant before the blessing of his master?** You should have blessed God first. **Immediately** the Holy One, Blessed be He, **gave** the priesthood **to Abraham, as it is stated: "The Lord says to my lord: Sit at My right hand, until I make your enemies your footstool"** (Psalms 110:1), **and afterward it is written: "The Lord has sworn, and will not repent: you shall be a priest forever, because you are a king of righteousness [*al divrati malki tzedek*]"** (Psalms 110:4), which is explained homiletically to mean: **Due to the** improper **words [*divrati*] of Melchizedek,** the offspring of Abraham shall be priests of God forever.

But *keivan she-hikdim birkat Avraham le-birkat ha-Makom* — since Malki-Tzedek placed the blessing of Avraham before the blessing of God — *hotzi'ah me-Avraham* — God transferred the *kehunah* to Avraham's line. The verse: *va-yevarechehu va-yomar baruch Avraham le-El elyon koneh shamayim va-aretz, u-varuch El elyon* — blessed is Avraham to the Most High God, owner of heaven and earth, and blessed is the Most High God.

### Avraham Correct

Avraham immediately objected: *ve-chi makdimin birkat eved le-birkat kono?* — does one give the blessing of the slave before the blessing of his master? You blessed me before you blessed God. *Miyad nitnah le-Avraham* — immediately the *kehunah* was given to Avraham. The proof text: *ne'um Hashem la-adoni* — God said to my master — where *adoni* refers to Avraham — *shev li-ymini* — sit at My right — *ad ashit oyvecha hadom le-raglecha* — until I make your enemies your footstool. And then the verse continues: *nishba Hashem ve-lo yinachem, atah kohen le-olam al dibrati Malki-Tzedek* — God has sworn and will not relent: you are a priest forever, because of (*al dibrati*) Malki-Tzedek.

### Words Matter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב ״וְהוּא כֹהֵן לְאֵל עֶלְיוֹן״: הוּא כֹּהֵן, וְאֵין זַרְעוֹ כֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And this is as it is written: "And he was priest of God the Most High"** (Genesis 14:18), which emphasizes that **he,** Melchizedek, **is a priest, but his children** will **not** be **priests.**

What does *al dibrati Malki-Tzedek* mean? The *Gemara* reads it as *al diburo shel Malki-Tzedek* — because of what Malki-Tzedek *said*, because of how he misspoke. *Ve-hu kohen, hu kohen, ve-ein zaro kohen* — he was a *kohen*, but his descendants are not *kohanim*. His improper speech cost his line the *kehunah* for all time.

### Words Final

And this is a remarkable note on which to conclude the extended *aggadata* of these three *perakim*. Throughout this entire *sugya*, a profound tension has run: the physical reality of *brit milah* versus the world of character and inner traits. Are you *tamim* because of the physical *brit*, or because of the integrity of your inner life? Does *brit milah* give you mastery over your drives, or does your *yetzer hatov*? And through all of this we are in *Masechet Nedarim* — the tractate of words. So what ultimately determines who is the *kohen*? Words. How *Malki-Tzedek* used words — whether properly or improperly — determined the entire future of the sacred service. In the end, the physical realities in which we live and the words that shape our realities are both indispensable. One might frame this entire *sugya* in contemporary terms as a tension between a behaviorist and a psychodynamic orientation: does a person change through behavior and physical action, or through the inner transformation of the self? The Talmud holds both in tension without resolving them — and perhaps that is the point.

## Fourth Chapter

### Scope Focus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין בֵּין הַמּוּדָּר הֲנָאָה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ לַמּוּדָּר הֵימֶנּוּ מַאֲכָל אֶלָּא דְּרִיסַת הָרֶגֶל וְכֵלִים שֶׁאֵין עוֹשִׂין בָּהֶם אוֹכֶל נֶפֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The difference between one for whom benefit from another is forbidden by vow [*hamuddar hana'a meḥaveiro*] and one for whom** benefit **from his food is forbidden by vow** concerns **only setting foot** on the other person's property **and** borrowing from that person **utensils that one does not use** in **preparation of food** but for other purposes. Those two benefits are forbidden to the former but permitted to the latter.

We now turn to the fourth *perek*, which, as noted at the outset, is not merely the next chapter but a genuine category shift. The first three *perakim* addressed how *nedarim* are created: what words and intentions are required, what population is included when one uses particular formulations, and so forth. The fourth *perek* assumes the *neder* is already in force — it is valid and binding — and asks: what is the scope of the restriction? What exactly is forbidden and what is permitted?

### Pleasure vs. Food

The *Mishna* states: *ein bein ha-mudar hana'ah me-chaveiro le-mudar heimenu ma'achal* — there is no difference between one who is forbidden to derive any benefit (*hana'ah*) from his fellow and one who is forbidden only to derive food benefit (*hana'at ma'achal*). This seems counterintuitive: surely there is a vast difference between being forbidden all benefit and being forbidden only food-related benefit — there are countless types of benefit that have nothing to do with food.

### Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמּוּדָּר מַאֲכָל מֵחֲבֵירוֹ לֹא יַשְׁאִילֶנּוּ נָפָה וּכְבָרָה וְרֵיחַיִם וְתַנּוּר, אֲבָל מַשְׁאִיל לוֹ חָלוּק וְטַבַּעַת וְטַלִּית וּנְזָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, with regard to **one for whom** benefit **from another's food is forbidden by vow,** that person **may not lend him** utensils used in the preparation of food, e.g., **a sieve, or a strainer, or a millstone, or an oven. However, he may lend him a garment, or a finger ring, or a cloak, or nose rings,** as these are not used in the preparation of food. However, he may not lend them to one for whom benefit from him is forbidden by vow.

The *Mishna* goes on to enumerate the only differences: *ella drisat ha-regel* — the right to walk through someone's yard. If one is forbidden all benefit, one cannot cross through the person's property. But if the prohibition is limited to food benefit, one may traverse the yard. Similarly: *ve-ha-keilim she-ein osim bahem ochel nefesh* — vessels not used for food purposes. These too fall outside the scope of a food benefit prohibition.

### Money & Food

The *Gemara* will explain why these are virtually the only differences. When someone says *hana'at ma'achal* is forbidden, the correct interpretation is: you are forbidden from any benefit that can translate into money, because anything worth any money at all could potentially become food — if it is worth even a small amount, one could go and purchase something to eat. Therefore, the scope of a food benefit prohibition is nearly as broad as a general benefit prohibition.

### No Charge Items

The only benefits that escape this broad reading are those that have no monetary value associated with them — things for which no one would normally pay. Cutting through someone's yard is an example of such a benefit: one *could* charge for it, but ordinarily no one does, so it carries no dollar value and therefore cannot translate into food.

### Vessels vs. Clothing

What about *keilim* — vessels? Here a further distinction applies. If one would normally rent out a particular item — say, a rake — then borrowing it constitutes a monetizable benefit, and the person under a food benefit prohibition cannot borrow it. But if one normally lends such items freely as an act of neighborliness, without charge, then borrowing it is not a benefit that could translate into money, and it is permitted. However — and this is the key exception — even if one normally lends out food vessels without charge, those vessels remain forbidden to the person under a food benefit prohibition, because they relate directly to food preparation. By contrast, non-food items such as clothing — a shirt, a ring, a cloak, earrings — may be borrowed freely, since they bear no connection to food even when their monetary value might otherwise render them relevant. This distinction between food-related vessels and non-food items like clothing will be developed further in the discussion that follows.
