# Sanhedrin 81 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Two Sins, Stringent Punishment

### Intro: Two Violations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב בִּשְׁתֵּי מִיתוֹת בֵּית דִּין – נִידּוֹן בַּחֲמוּרָה. עָבַר עֲבֵירָה שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב שְׁתֵּי מִיתוֹת – נִידּוֹן בַּחֲמוּרָה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: נִידּוֹן בַּזִּיקָּה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה שֶׁבָּאָה עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who is liable** to be executed **with two** different **court**-imposed **death** penalties, as he violated two different capital transgressions, **is sentenced to the** more **severe** form of execution. If one **violated** one **transgression** for **which he is liable** to receive **two death** penalties, e.g., if one engaged in intercourse with his mother-in-law, who is also a married woman, **he is sentenced to the** more **severe** form of execution. **Rabbi Yosei says: He is sentenced** to the form of execution that he is liable to receive due **to the first relationship that came upon him,** i.e., if she was his mother-in-law before she was married, he is executed by burning; if she was married before she was his mother-in-law, he is punished by strangulation.

The *mishna* opens with a case of *mi shenischayev shtei misos beis din* — a person who has incurred two separate death penalties from among the four modes of execution discussed throughout this tractate. The rule is *nidonin b'chamura*: he is executed by the more stringent of the two methods. The underlying logic is straightforward — the fact that he is also liable for a lighter execution should not, counterintuitively, reduce his punishment.

### Single Act, Two Violations

A second case in the *mishna* appears at first glance to be identical, but involves a single *aveira* carrying two separate capital liabilities simultaneously. The *gemara* will elaborate on the classic example: a man who has relations with his mother who is also a married woman — generating both a prohibition of *isha uvita* and a prohibition of *eshet ish*. The *tanna kamma* rules the same as in the first case: *nidon b'chamura*, he receives the more stringent execution. Rabbi Yosi, however, disagrees. He holds *nidon b'zika rishona sheba lo* — the punishment is determined by the first *zika*, the first legal status this woman held in relation to him. The *gemara* will work out the precise implications of this position.

### Sequential Sins Obvious

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! אֶלָּא, אִיתְּגוֹרֵי אִיתְּגוּר?
>
> **Translation:** **Isn't this obvious? Rather,** would you say that **he would profit** from the fact that he violated a second capital transgression and would receive a more lenient punishment?

The *gemara* opens with an objection: *pshita* — is this not obvious? Of course the weightier punishment applies; should a person be rewarded for having committed two *aveiros* by receiving only the lighter execution?

### Rava's Case: Judgment Passed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָכִי בְּמַי עָסְקִינַן? כְּגוֹן שֶׁעָבַר עֲבֵירָה קַלָּה, וְנִגְמַר דִּינוֹ עַל עֲבֵירָה קַלָּה, וְחָזַר וְעָבַר עֲבֵירָה חֲמוּרָה. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: כֵּיוָן דְּנִגְמַר דִּינוֹ לַעֲבֵירָה קַלָּה, הַאי גַּבְרָא קְטִילָא הוּא. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where he violated** a relatively **minor transgression** punishable by a less severe form of execution, **and his verdict was issued for the minor transgression, and he then violated a major transgression** punishable by a more severe form of execution. It could **enter your mind to say: Once his verdict was finalized for the minor transgression,** the legal status of **this** individual **is** that of **a dead man,** and anything that he does thereafter is insignificant. To counter this, the *tanna* **teaches us** that he is liable to be punished for the subsequent transgression, and if it is punishable by a more severe form of execution, he is executed with that form of execution.

Rava clarifies that the *mishna* is addressing a less obvious case. The scenario is one of sequence: a person sinned and received a judgment — *nigmar dino* — for a lighter offense, such as *chenek* (strangulation). Then, after that judgment had already been rendered, he went ahead and committed an additional, graver sin, one deserving of a weightier execution such as *sreifa* (burning) or *sayif* (the sword). The *havah amina* — the initial assumption one might have entertained — is that since his judgment has already been passed, *hai gavra ktila hu*, he is already considered as a man condemned to die. One might therefore think that the subsequent, graver transgression carries no legal consequence beyond the existing judgment. The *mishna* teaches otherwise.

### New Judgment Passed

The *gemara* therefore establishes that a new court proceeding is conducted and a new judgment is passed to execute him by the graver method. The *mishna* thus comes to exclude the mistaken assumption that the prior judgment renders the condemned man legally inert with respect to any further capital liability.

## Ezekiel Evidence

### Ezekiel 18 Citation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִנֵּיהּ אֲבוּהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף בַּר חָמָא מֵרַבָּה בַּר נָתָן: מְנַָא הָא מִילְּתָא דַאֲמוּר רַבָּנַן, מִי שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב שְׁתֵּי מִיתוֹת בֵּית דִּין נִידּוֹן בַּחֲמוּרָה? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהוֹלִיד בֵּן פָּרִיץ שֹׁפֵךְ דָּם [וְגוֹ׳] אֶל הֶהָרִים אָכַל וְאֶת אֵשֶׁת רֵעֵהוּ טִמֵּא וְאֶל הַגִּלּוּלִים נָשָׂא עֵינָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** **The father of Rav Yosef bar Ḥama asked of Rabba bar Natan: From where is this matter that the Sages stated** derived: **One who is liable** to be executed with **two** different **court**-imposed **death** penalties **is sentenced to** the more **severe** form of execution? Rabba bar Natan answered: It is derived from a verse, **as it is written: "And he begets a violent son, a shedder of blood,** who does to a brother any of these. And he had not done any of these, for **he has eaten upon the mountains and defiled his neighbor's wife,** has wronged the poor and needy, has taken by robbery, does not return collateral, **and has lifted his eyes toward the idols,** has committed abomination" (Ezekiel 18:10–12).

The *gemara* then raises a question: where is the scriptural basis for the rule that one liable for two death penalties is judged by the more stringent? The text cites Ezekiel 18, which describes a wayward son who commits multiple offenses: he sheds blood (*shofech dam*), eats upon the mountains (*el he-harim achal*), defiles his fellow's wife (*es eshes re'eihu tima*), oppresses the poor (*ani v'evyon honah*), steals and does not restore (*gezeilos gazal chavol lo yashiv*), and lifts his eyes to the idols (*el hagilulim nasa einav to'evah asah*). At the conclusion of this catalogue, the verse declares *mot yumat damav bo* — he shall surely die, his blood is upon him. The phrase *damav bo* is a recognized marker for execution by *skilah* (stoning).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהוֹלִיד בֵּן פָּרִיץ שֹׁפֵךְ דָּם״ – בְּסַיִיף, ״אֶת אֵשֶׁת רֵעֵהוּ טִמֵּא״ – זוֹ אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ בְּחֶנֶק, ״וְאֶל הַגִּלּוּלִים נָשָׂא עֵינָיו״ – זוֹ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בִּסְקִילָה. וּכְתִיב: ״מוֹת יוּמָת דָּמָיו בּוֹ יִהְיֶה״ – בִּסְקִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** This is interpreted: **"And he begets a violent son, a shedder of blood,"** this is one who is liable to be executed by beheading **with a sword; "defiled his neighbor's wife," this is** engaging in intercourse with **a married woman,** for which one is liable to be executed **by strangulation; "lifted his eyes toward the idols," this is idol worship,** for which one is liable to be executed **by stoning. And it is written** thereafter: **"He shall be put to death, his blood shall be upon himself"** (Ezekiel 18:13), which means that he is executed **by stoning.** It appears that one who violates several capital transgressions is executed with the most severe form of execution for which he is liable.

The *gemara* analyzes the offenses listed. Bloodshed (*shofech dam*) deserves *sayif*. Defiling his fellow's wife — adultery, *eshet ish* — carries *chenek*. Looking toward the abominations (*avodah zarah*) carries *skilah*. Yet the verse concludes that he receives *skilah*, the most severe. This demonstrates that even when a person has already incurred a lighter capital liability, he is nonetheless judged by the graver punishment that any subsequent transgression merits.

### Rav Pappa's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: אֵימָא כּוּלְּהוּ בִּסְקִילָה. ״וְהוֹלִיד בֵּן פָּרִיץ שֹׁפֵךְ דָּם״ – זֶה בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה דְּבִסְקִילָה, ״אֵשֶׁת רֵעֵהוּ טִמֵּא״ – זוֹ נַעֲרָה הַמְאוֹרָסָה דְּבִסְקִילָה, ״וְאֶל הַגִּלּוּלִים נָשָׂא עֵינָיו״ – זוֹ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה דְּבִסְקִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak objects to this: Say that all of** the transgressions listed in the verse are punishable **by stoning.** How? **"And he begets a violent son, a shedder of blood," this is a wayward and rebellious son, who** is executed **by stoning; "defiled his neighbor's wife," this is** engaging in intercourse with **a betrothed young woman,** for **which** one is executed **by stoning; "lifted his eyes toward the idols," this is idol worship,** for **which** one is executed **by stoning.**

Rav Pappa bar Yitzchak challenges this reading entirely. He argues that all the offenses enumerated in the passage can be read as deserving *skilah*, making the verse prove nothing about the principle of applying the more stringent penalty across different categories of execution. His reinterpretation: *shofech dam* refers to *ben sorer u-moreh*, the wayward and rebellious son, who is judged *al shem sofo* — on account of what he will eventually become — and whose execution is indeed by *skilah*. The defilement of *eshet re'eihu* refers specifically to *na'arah ha-me'orasah*, the betrothed maiden, whose seducer is also executed by *skilah*. And *avodah zarah* is of course *skilah* as well. Accordingly, the verse may simply be listing multiple offenses all of which independently carry *skilah*, and the conclusion *mot yumat damav bo* tells us nothing about graded punishments.

### Rebuttal: Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן יְחֶזְקֵאל? דִּלְמָא תּוֹרָה קָא מַהְדַּר. אִם כֵּן, אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְאַהְדּוֹרַהּ כִּי הֵיכִי דְּאַהְדְּרַהּ מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If so,** according to that understanding of the verse, **what is Ezekiel teaching us?** According to the first understanding, a new *halakha* is derived from here; but according to the interpretation of Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak there is nothing novel. The Gemara rejects this contention: **Perhaps** it is the *halakhot* of the **Torah** that Ezekiel **is reiterating,** admonishing the people to fulfill the directives of the Torah. The Gemara answers: **If** it is **so** that this was Ezekiel's intention, **it** would **have been necessary for him to reiterate** the *halakhot* **in the manner that Moses our teacher reviewed it,** employing the same language. The fact that Ezekiel diverged from Moses' formulation indicates that he sought to teach a new *halakha*.

The *gemara* responds: if that reading were correct, *mai kamashma lan Yechezkel?* — what exactly is Ezekiel teaching us? If all the offenses equally carry *skilah*, the prophet has said nothing new. One might rejoin that Ezekiel is simply repeating and reviewing existing Torah law. But the *gemara* rejects this as well: if that were his purpose, *ibei leh le-mehadder ke-di-mehadder Moshe Rabbeinu* — he should have done so in the manner of Moshe Rabbeinu, who reviewed the commandments in *Mishneh Torah* with clarity and expansion. Ezekiel's subtle and indirect formulation makes no sense if he is merely rehearsing what the Torah already states more plainly. It follows that Ezekiel must be coming to teach us something new — specifically, the principle of our *mishna*: that when multiple capital liabilities are incurred, the graver execution is applied.

## Ezekiel Interpretation

### Rav Acha's Derasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַב אַחָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״אֶל הֶהָרִים לֹא אָכָל״? שֶׁלֹּא אָכַל בִּזְכוּת אֲבוֹתָיו. ״וְעֵינָיו לֹא נָשָׂא אֶל גִּלּוּלֵי בֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל״? שֶׁלֹּא הָלַךְ בְּקוֹמָה זְקוּפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a homiletic interpretation that **Rav Aḥa, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, taught: What** is the meaning of that **which is written:** "He did not eat upon the mountains, nor did he lift his eyes toward the idols of the house of Israel, nor did he defile his neighbor's wife, nor did he approach a menstruating woman" (Ezekiel 18:6)? This cannot be understood literally, as refraining from performing these transgressions is not sufficient to characterize an individual as righteous. **"He did not eat upon the mountains"** is an allusion to the fact that **he did not eat due to the merit of his fathers;** rather, he relies on his own merit and righteousness. **"Nor did he lift his eyes toward the idols of the house of Israel"** is a reference to one who distances himself from anything similar to idol worship, to the extent **that he did not walk with upright stature,** typical of a haughty person, as haughtiness is typical of idolaters.

Rav Acha b'Rebi Chanina turns to a related passage in Ezekiel — the positive counterpart to the one just discussed. A few verses earlier, the prophet describes the righteous individual: *el he-harim lo akhal* — he did not eat upon the mountains — *ve-einav lo nasa el gillulei beis Yisrael* — he did not look to the abominations. He did not defile his fellow's wife, and he did not approach a *niddah*. The passage concludes: *tzaddik hu chaya yichye* — he is righteous, he shall surely live. The *gemara* observes that these formulations cannot merely mean the person passively avoided grave sins; they must describe positive virtues.

*El he-harim lo akhal* — he did not eat upon the mountains — is interpreted as *she-lo akhal bi-zechut avotav*: he did not rely upon the merit of his forefathers for his sustenance and reward. His righteousness is his own; the mountains, a common metaphor for those from whom one descends, are not the source of his spiritual provision.

The phrase *ve-einav lo nasa el gillulei beis Yisrael* — he did not lift his eyes to the abominations — is read as *she-lo halach bi-komah zekufah*, he did not walk with an upright, arrogant bearing that effectively displaces the divine presence. Humility is thus linked, by way of the verse's idiom, to the rejection of *avodah zarah*.

### Not Eating Mountains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאֶת אֵשֶׁת רֵעֵהוּ לֹא טִמֵּא״ – שֶׁלֹּא יָרַד לְאוּמָּנוּת חֲבֵירוֹ, ״וְאֶל אִשָּׁה נִדָּה לֹא יִקְרָב״ – שֶׁלֹּא נֶהֱנָה מִקּוּפָּה שֶׁל צְדָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **"Nor did he defile his neighbor's wife"** is an allusion to the fact **that he did not infringe upon another's trade,** thereby compromising his livelihood. **"Nor did he approach a menstruating woman"** is an allusion to the fact **that he did not benefit from** the **charity fund** even if he was needy.

The defilement of one's fellow's wife (*es eshes re'ehu lo timme*) is read as *she-lo yarad le-ommanuto shel chaveiro* — he did not encroach upon his fellow's livelihood. The wife, as an emblem of one's entire domestic and personal life, serves here as a metaphor for professional livelihood. Finally, *ve-el ishah niddah lo yikrav* — he did not draw near to a *niddah* — is interpreted as *she-lo ne'enah mikuppah shel tzedakah*: even if poor, he declined to benefit from the communal charity fund, seeking a particular kind of purity and wholeness.

### Virtues: Humility

### Rabban Gamliel Weeps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְתִיב: ״צַדִּיק הוּא חָיֹה יִחְיֶה״. כְּשֶׁהָיָה רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל מַגִּיעַ לְמִקְרָא הַזֶּה, הָיָה בּוֹכֶה. אֲמַר: מַאן דְּעָבֵיד לְכוּלְּהוּ הוּא דְּחָיֵי, בַּחֲדָא מִינַּיְיהוּ – לָא?
>
> **Translation:** **And it is written** with regard to one who refrains from these actions: **"He is a righteous man, he shall live"** (Ezekiel 18:9). The Gemara relates: **When Rabban Gamliel would reach this verse:** "And has kept all My statutes, and has performed them, he shall live" (Ezekiel 18:19), **he would cry and say: It is one who performs all of them, who lives;** one who performs **one of them, no,** he shall not live.

When Rabban Gamliel reached this verse, he would weep. His concern: *man de'avid le-kulhu hu de-chaya* — is it really only one who fulfills all of these qualities who will live? *Be-chada mineyhu lo?* — and one who possesses only some of them, is that insufficient? The standard implied by the verse, read this way, seemed impossibly exacting.

### Rabbi Akiva Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה: ״אַל תִּטַּמְּאוּ בְּכׇל אֵלֶּה״, הָכִי נָמֵי בְּכוּלְּהוּ – אִין, בַּחֲדָא מִינַּיְיהוּ – לָא? אֶלָּא: בְּאַחַת מִכׇּל אֵלֶּה, הָכִי נָמֵי: בְּאַחַת מִכׇּל אֵלֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said to** Rabban Gamliel in an attempt to console him: **If that is so,** that the term "all" is understood in that manner, with regard to that which is written concerning the forbidden relatives enumerated in the Torah: **"Do not become defiled with all of these"** (Leviticus 18:24), would you also say that there **too,** one who is **in** violation of **all of** the prohibitions, **yes,** he is punished, but one who is **in** violation of **one of them, no,** he is not punished? **Rather,** clearly, "all of these" means **in** violation of **one of all these;** therefore, here **too,** the verse in Ezekiel: "And has kept all My statutes," means that **in** fulfillment of **one of all these** one merits eternal life.

Rabbi Akiva responded by invoking a parallel verse. In the laws of *arayos*, the Torah concludes: *al titame'u be-chol eleh* — do not defile yourselves with all of these. One does not read that verse to mean that violating all of them simultaneously is forbidden while any single transgression would be permitted. Rather, the intent is: *be-achat mikol eleh* — even one is forbidden. So too here: the positive formulation in Ezekiel means that even one of these virtues, even a single good deed, is sufficient to warrant the blessing of life. Rabban Gamliel's tears were therefore unfounded, and the verse does not set an impossible threshold.

## One Sin, Two Penalties

### Mother-in-Law Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָבַר עֲבֵירָה שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב שְׁתֵּי מִיתוֹת – נִידּוֹן בַּחֲמוּרָה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: נִידּוֹן בַּזִּיקָּה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה שֶׁבָּאָה עָלָיו. חֲמוֹתוֹ וְנַעֲשֵׂית אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ – נִידּוֹן בַּחֲמוֹתוֹ. אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ וְנַעֲשֵׂית חֲמוֹתוֹ – נִידּוֹן בְּאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If one **violated** one **transgression** for which he is liable to receive two death penalties, he is sentenced to the more severe form of execution. Rabbi Yosei says: He is sentenced to the form of execution that he is liable to receive due to the first relationship that came upon him. **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **How,** i.e., in what circumstances, does **Rabbi Yosei say** that he is **sentenced to the first relationship that came upon him?** If this woman was a widow or a divorcée when she became **his mother-in-law, and** then **she became a married woman** and he engaged in intercourse with her, **he is sentenced** to execution by burning for engaging in intercourse **with his mother-in-law.** If before he married her daughter she was **a married woman, and** then **she became his mother-in-law,** and he engaged in intercourse with her, **he is sentenced** to execution by strangulation for engaging in intercourse **with a married woman.**

The *gemara* now turns to the substantive analysis underlying Rabbi Yosi's position in the *mishna*. A *beraita* is cited explaining what Rabbi Yosi means when he says one is judged according to the first *zika*.

### MIL Becomes Married

The first scenario: a man's *chamot* — his mother-in-law — was never a married woman during any period relevant to him. From the time he became obligated in *mitzvos*, she had already been widowed or was otherwise single. The first and only relevant prohibition she held for him was as his wife's mother, which carries the penalty of *sreifa*. She subsequently married, becoming an *eshet ish* as well. Rabbi Yosi rules *nidon be-chamoto*: he is judged as one who violated the mother-in-law prohibition, and the punishment is *sreifa*. The *issur* of *eshet ish*, which carries the lighter penalty of *chenek*, does not displace the first status.

### Married Becomes MIL

The second scenario presents the *chiddush*. This woman had always been a married woman — *eshet ish* — during the relevant period of this man's life. She was therefore primarily forbidden to him as *eshet ish*, carrying *chenek*. He subsequently married her daughter, so she became additionally forbidden as his *chamot*. Rabbi Yosi rules *nidon be-eshet ish*: he is judged only by the first status, *eshet ish*, and receives the lighter penalty of *chenek* — not *sreifa*. This is the application of *ein issur chal al issur*: once a prohibition has attached to a person or object, a subsequent prohibition of the same or even greater severity does not take legal effect. Rabbi Yosi holds that the first status defines the relationship.

### Rav Adda Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה לְרָבָא: חֲמוֹתוֹ וְנַעֲשֵׂית אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, נִידּוֹן בַּחֲמוֹתוֹ? לִידּוֹן נָמֵי אַאִיסּוּר אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ! דְּהָא אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּאִיסּוּר מוֹסִיף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava said to Rava:** If this woman was a widow or a divorcée when she became **his mother-in-law, and** then **she became a married woman** and he engaged in intercourse with her, **is he sentenced** to execution by burning for engaging in intercourse **with his mother-in-law? Why is this the *halakha*? Let him be sentenced for** violating **the prohibition of a married woman as well, as Rabbi Abbahu says: Rabbi Yosei concedes in** the case of **an expanded prohibition.** Rabbi Yosei holds that generally, a prohibition does not take effect where another prohibition already exists. Nevertheless, when the second transgression is an expanded prohibition, i.e., one that incorporates additional people into the list of those for whom the items or individuals are forbidden, Rabbi Yosei concedes that since the second prohibition applies to others, it takes effect concerning the person included in the first prohibition as well. Here too, the prohibition of intercourse with a married woman is expanded, as it applies to everyone and is therefore more comprehensive than the prohibition of intercourse with his mother-in-law, which applies only to her sons-in-law. Therefore, according to Rabbi Yosei, he should be punished also for violating the prohibition of intercourse with a married woman.

Rav Adda bar Ahava raises a challenge to Rava. In the first scenario — where she was initially the *chamot* and subsequently became a married woman — why not say that the status of *eshet ish* does in fact take effect, making it a case of *issur mosif*, an expanding prohibition? This would be consistent with the principle, transmitted by Rabbi Abbahu, that even Rabbi Yosi concedes the rule of *ein issur chal al issur* does not apply where the second prohibition is an *issur mosif* — a prohibition that broadens the scope of those forbidden. When she was only the *chamot*, she was forbidden exclusively to this one man. When she became a married woman, she became forbidden to every man in the world other than her husband. The second status thus expands the universe of those to whom she is prohibited, which is precisely the definition of *issur mosif*.

### Rabbi Abbahu Teaching

Rabbi Abbahu's teaching is that Rabbi Yosi would concede in a case of *issur mosif* that the second prohibition does take effect. If so, in the first scenario the man would be liable both as one who violated his *chamot* and as one who violated an *eshet ish*. The question is whether this affects his punishment.

### Response: Irrelevant Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַדָּא בְּרִי, בִּתְרֵי קְטָלֵי קָטְלַתְּ לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: Adda my son,** will **you kill him with two death penalties?** Rabbi Yosei concedes that the prohibition of engaging in intercourse with a married woman takes effect in addition to the prohibition of engaging in intercourse with his mother-in-law. If he had violated the transgression unwittingly, he would have been liable to bring two sin-offerings. But if he violated the transgression intentionally, it is impossible to execute him by strangulation after executing him by burning.

The response is pointed: *Adda brei, Adda, masya bisrei katlei katlas lei?* — Do you want to kill him twice? Yes, the second status takes effect, and yes, she is also forbidden to him as an *eshet ish* — but this is entirely irrelevant to the question of execution. Since we are speaking of intentional transgression (*be-meizid*), the man is already subject to *sreifa* on account of the *chamot* prohibition. The lighter *chenek* of *eshet ish* adds nothing to his sentence. As both Rashi and Tosafos note, the question becomes meaningful only in the case of unintentional transgression (*be-shogeg*). There, if the second status takes effect as an *issur mosif*, the man would be required to bring two *korbanos* rather than one. But in the realm of capital punishment, the superimposition of a lesser *issur* upon a greater one does not alter the outcome.

## Repeat Offender

### Mishna: Kippah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁלָּקָה וְשָׁנָה, בֵּית דִּין מַכְנִיסִין אוֹתוֹ לַכִּיפָּה, וּמַאֲכִילִין אוֹתוֹ שְׂעוֹרִין עַד שֶׁכְּרֵיסוֹ מִתְבַּקַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who was flogged** for violating a prohibition **and** then **repeated** the violation and was flogged again assumes the status of a forewarned transgressor. The **court places him into the vaulted chamber [*lakippa*] and feeds him barley** bread **until his belly ruptures** due to the low-quality food, and he dies.

The next *mishna* introduces a famous institution: *mi she-lakah ve-shanah beis din machnisin oso la-kippah* — one who received lashes and repeated the same offense is confined to the *kippah* by the court.

### Kippah: Barley Death

The *mishna* specifies: *u-ma'achilin oso se'orin ad she-kreiso misbaka'as* — he is fed barley until his stomach bursts. The *Bavli*'s understanding of this is that the diet administered in the *kippah* literally brings about the offender's death. The mechanism involves first reducing his stomach through meager rations, and then feeding him barley which absorbs water and expands, causing the stomach to rupture. The *Yerushalmi*, by contrast, reads this passage more moderately, as simply describing a prison regime of unpleasant but non-lethal meager rations. The difference is significant: on the *Bavli*'s reading, the *kippah* is a covert form of capital punishment; on the *Yerushalmi*'s reading, it is an institution of imprisonment.

### Justification Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מִשּׁוּם דְּלָקָה וְשָׁנָה, בֵּית דִּין כּוֹנְסִין אוֹתוֹ לַכִּיפָּה? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: הָכָא בְּמַלְקִיּוֹת שֶׁל כָּרֵיתוֹת עָסְקִינַן, דְּגַבְרָא בַּר קְטָלָא הוּא, וְקָרוֹבֵי הוּא דְּלָא מְיקָרַב קְטָלֵיהּ. וְכֵיוָן דְּקָא מְוַותַּר לַהּ לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ, מְקָרְבִינַן לֵיהּ לִקְטָלֵיהּ עִילָּוֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Due to** the fact **that he was flogged and repeated** the violation does the **court place him into the vaulted chamber** for the duration of his life and advance his death? **Rabbi Yirmeya says** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says: Here, we are dealing with lashes** administered for violations of prohibitions punishable **by excision from the World-to-Come [*karet*]. As** when one violates such a prohibition, **the man is** essentially **liable to** be punished with **death** at the hand of Heaven, **but his death is not advanced** by the court, as it is administered by the heavenly court. **And** in this case, **since he surrenders his life** through his repeated transgressions, **we advance his execution upon him** by placing him in the vaulted chamber.

The *gemara* asks what justifies this extra-legal measure at all. Simply repeating a sin twice — even if one received lashes for it — is hardly a basis, anywhere in the Torah, for confinement and indirect execution. What authorizes the court to act in this way?

### Kares Justification

Rabbi Yirmiyah, citing Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish (Reish Lakish), clarifies: *hacha be-malkiyos shel krisos askinan* — we are not speaking of any sin that carries lashes, but specifically of a sin for which the *karet* penalty applies. *Karet* is a divinely imposed excision — a form of heavenly death — but when witnesses give warning (*hasra'ah*), the earthly penalty of lashes is applied instead. Since the offender is intrinsically one who deserves divine death, *de-gavra bar katla hu*, the court is granted the authority to bring about that death in this manner. Moreover, *u-karuvi hu de-la mikrav* — he has displayed utter indifference to his own soul and life by repeatedly committing sins that carry *karet*, and so the court, as it were, helps actualize the death he has already invited upon himself: *makrivin lei es katlei iluyei*.

### One vs. Multiple Kares

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב לְרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר תַּחְלִיפָא: תָּא אַסְבְּרָא לָךְ, בְּמַלְקִיּוֹת שֶׁל כָּרֵת אַחַת, אֲבָל שֶׁל שְׁתַּיִם וְשֶׁל שָׁלֹשׁ כָּרֵיתוֹת – אִיסּוּרֵי הוּא דְּקָא טָעֵים, וְלָא מְוַותַּר כּוּלֵּי הַאי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ya'akov said to Rabbi Yirmeya bar Taḥlifa: Come and I will explain to you** the circumstances of this *halakha*. It is in a case where he was flogged **with lashes for** repeatedly violating **one** prohibition punishable with ***karet*** that he is considered to have surrendered his life. **But** if he received lashes **for** violating **two or for** violating **three** different prohibitions punishable with ***karet*,** he is not placed in the vaulted chamber, as **he** merely **is tasting** the taste of **prohibition** and wants to enjoy the different experiences, **and he is not** thereby **surrendering** his life **to that extent.**

Rav Yaakov refines the principle further. The rule applies only when the person repeatedly committed the same *karet* offense — *be-malkus shel kares achas*, sins of one specific *karet* category. If, however, the person committed two or three different *karet* transgressions — eating *chametz* on Pesach, eating blood, eating *chelev* — this does not trigger the *kippah* confinement. The reason: in such a case, *issurei hu de-ka tayeim*, he is sampling various forbidden pleasures. He is driven by a variety of different appetites. This does not demonstrate the same pattern of complete recklessness or self-destructive abandon as does repeatedly violating the same prohibition. The pathology is different, and accordingly the legal response is different.

## Hazaka: Two or Three

### Mishna: Two Times

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁלָּקָה וְשָׁנָה. שָׁנָה אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא שִׁילֵּשׁ. לֵימָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל? דְּאִי רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, הָא אָמַר: עַד תְּלָת זִימְנֵי לָא הָוְיָא חֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **One who was flogged** for violating a prohibition **and** then **repeated** the violation, the court places him into the vaulted chamber. The Gemara infers: He is placed in the vaulted chamber if **he repeated** the violation, doing it a second time, **even if he did not** again **repeat** the violation, doing it **a third** time. **Let us say** that **the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, as if** it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, doesn't he say: Presumptive status is not** established **until** the action is performed **three times?** In his opinion, only after violating the prohibition three times would one assume the status of a forewarned transgressor.

The *mishna* holds that a pattern is established after *two* instances — the offender received lashes once, repeated the offense, and on the third commission of the sin is confined to the *kippah*. The *gemara* challenges this: is the *mishna* not inconsistent with the position of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who holds that a *hazaka* — an established behavioral pattern — requires three occurrences, not two?

This debate has a well-known context elsewhere: a woman whose successive husbands die in sequence. The concern is whether there is a causal relationship between her and their deaths. The question is at what point we regard this as an established pattern: after two husbands (the position of Rabbi) or after three (the position of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel). The *gemara* frames the present debate as a parallel dispute.

The *gemara* suggests that the *mishna* may simply be following Rabbi's view that two instances suffice to establish a *hazaka*, and would therefore not accord with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.

### Baraita: Two Lashes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, קָסָבַר עֲבֵירוֹת מַחֲזִיקוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina** rejects that proof and **says: Even** if **you say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel,** the *tanna* **holds** that **transgressions,** not lashes, **establish** the **presumptive status** of a transgressor. Therefore, the mishna that teaches: One who was flogged for violating a prohibition and then repeated the violation and was flogged, is placed in the vaulted chamber only after he violated the prohibition a third time, is in accordance with the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.

A *beraita* specifies: on the first and second commission of the sin the offender receives lashes, and on the third commission he is confined to the *kippah*.

### Abba Shaul: Three Times

Abba Shaul disagrees: even on the third commission he receives lashes; it is only on the fourth commission that he is confined to the *kippah*.

### Lashes vs. Sins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: עָבַר עֲבֵירָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ מַלְקוֹת, פַּעַם רִאשׁוֹנָה וּשְׁנִיָּה מַלְקִין אוֹתוֹ, וּשְׁלִישִׁית כּוֹנְסִין אוֹתוֹ לַכִּיפָּה. אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: אַף בַּשְּׁלִישִׁית מַלְקִין אוֹתוֹ, בָּרְבִיעִית כּוֹנְסִין אוֹתוֹ לַכִּיפָּה. מַאי לָאו דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מַלְקִיּוֹת מַחְזִיקוֹת, וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי וְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל קָמִיפַּלְגִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: If **one performed a transgression that has lashes** as its punishment, the **first and the second time,** the court **flogs him; and** the **third** time, the court **places him in the vaulted chamber. Abba Shaul says: Even the third** time the court **flogs him;** and the **fourth** time, the court **places him in the vaulted chamber. What, is it not that everyone** agrees that **lashes establish** the **presumptive status** of a transgressor, **and** it is **concerning the dispute of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel,** whether presumptive status is established after two or three times, that the Rabbis and Abba Shaul **disagree?**

At first glance, this debate appears easily explicable as a replay of the dispute between Rabbi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: the *tanna kamma* requires two instances before the pattern is established (following Rabbi), while Abba Shaul requires three (following Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel). The *gemara* considers this interpretation — that what establishes the pattern is the repeated defiance of lashes (*malkus machzikos*). According to this reading, the *tanna kamma* says that defying lashes twice — by sinning on a third occasion — establishes the pattern, while Abba Shaul says one must defy lashes three times before the *kippah* is imposed.

### Sins Count

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אִית לְהוּ דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי: דְּמָר סָבַר עֲבֵירוֹת מַחְזִיקוֹת, וּמָר סָבַר מַלְקִיּוֹת מַחְזִיקוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that parallel. **No,** it can be explained that **everyone,** the Rabbis and Abba Shaul, **agrees with** the opinion **of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** that presumptive status is established after three times, **and here it is with regard to this** matter **that they disagree: That** one **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds: Transgressions establish** the **presumptive status** of a transgressor, and after he is flogged twice and performs the transgression a third time, the court places him in the vaulted chamber; **and** one **Sage, Abba Shaul, holds: Lashes establish** the **presumptive status** of a transgressor, and it is only after he performs the third transgression and is flogged for that transgression that he assumes the status of a forewarned transgressor, and when he performs the transgression a fourth time, he is placed in the vaulted chamber.

Ravina, however, resists this framing and insists that all parties in fact hold like Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, that three instances are required. The real dispute, he argues, is not about how many times lashes must be defied, but about what is being counted. The *tanna kamma* holds *aveiros machzikos* — it is the sins themselves that establish the pattern. The person sinned once, sinned again, and sinned a third time; three sins have now been committed and the *kippah* is imposed. Abba Shaul holds *malkus machzikos* — it is the defiance of lashes that establishes the pattern. One must have defied lashes three times, meaning the sin must have been committed four times (each of the first three followed by lashes, and then the fourth sin triggering the *kippah*). On Ravina's reading, no one in this *sugya* dissents from Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's requirement of three instances; they differ only on whether those three instances are three sins or three acts of defying the lash.

## Warnings Without Lashes

### No Lashes Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָדְתַנְיָא: הִתְרוּ בּוֹ וְשָׁתַק, הִתְרוּ בּוֹ וְהִרְכִּין רֹאשׁוֹ – פַּעַם רִאשׁוֹנָה וּשְׁנִיָּה מַתְרִין בּוֹ, שְׁלִישִׁית – כּוֹנְסִין אוֹתוֹ לַכִּיפָּה. אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: אַף בַּשְּׁלִישִׁית מַתְרִין בּוֹ, בִּרְבִיעִית – כּוֹנְסִין אוֹתוֹ לַכִּיפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions that interpretation of the dispute. **And that which is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to a case where witnesses **forewarned him** that he was about to violate a prohibition punishable by lashes **and he was silent** and did not acknowledge that he accepted the forewarning, and likewise a case where the witnesses **forewarned him and he bowed his head** but did not explicitly acknowledge that he accepted the forewarning, the **first and second time they forewarn him** the court does not flog him, because he did not accept the forewarning. If this scenario occurs **a third** time, the court **places him in the vaulted chamber. Abba Shaul says: Even the third** time the witnesses **forewarn him** the court does not flog him; **and the fourth** time, the court **places him in the vaulted chamber.**

The *gemara* now confronts a case where lashes were never actually administered. Witnesses were present and warnings (*hasra'ah*) were given — but the offender never verbally acknowledged the warning (*af al pi chen*) or otherwise responded in the technically required manner that would permit lashes to be administered. In one variant, he nodded his head (*hirkin rosho*) rather than explicitly accepting the warning. The result is that his willfulness was apparent but lashes could not formally be given.

### TK: Third Warning

The *tanna kamma* rules: the first and second times, the court gives him the warning; on the third occasion, he is confined to the *kippah*. This is a striking *chiddush*: even without lashes having been administered, the *kippah* is imposed. Three warnings suffice to establish the pattern.

### AS: Kippah Warning

Abba Shaul disagrees. He maintains that to impose *kippah* confinement, the court must first warn the offender explicitly that the consequence of another violation will be confinement in the *kippah* itself. He cannot issue this warning until the offender has already established himself as a habitual violator. Accordingly, three violations must first occur to establish the *hazaka*; only then may the court warn him regarding the *kippah*. A fourth violation after that warning triggers the actual confinement.

### Kippah Requires Warning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתָם, מַלְקוֹת לֵיכָּא. בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? אָמַר רָבִינָא: בְּכִיפָּה צְרִיכָה הַתְרָאָה קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And there,** in the case in the *baraita*, **there are no lashes,** as he did not explicitly acknowledge acceptance of the forewarning. The reference is to a case where one violated the transgression several times, and if their dispute is not with regard to the number of times required to establish presumptive status, **with regard to what** matter **do they disagree? Ravina says:** It is **with regard to** whether placing an individual into **a vaulted chamber requires forewarning** that **they disagree.** The Rabbis hold that once he disregarded the forewarning three times, the court places him in a vaulted chamber. Abba Shaul holds that after he is forewarned three times that he will be flogged if he performs the transgression, he requires additional forewarning, before the fourth time, that he will be placed in a vaulted chamber if he performs the transgression again.

This raises an obvious question: since lashes are not in play at all in this scenario, what is Abba Shaul's reasoning for requiring a fourth instance? If the pattern is established by the sins themselves (as the *tanna kamma* holds), three sins should suffice. Why does Abba Shaul demand a fourth?

Ravina explains that the underlying dispute is whether *kippah* itself requires prior *hatra'ah* — a formal warning specifically directed at the *kippah* penalty. The *tanna kamma* holds that no such specific warning is needed; the general warnings already given suffice, and once the pattern of three violations is established, the *kippah* follows. Abba Shaul holds that *kippah* does require its own prior *hatra'ah*. Since this warning cannot be issued until the *hazaka* of three violations has already been established, the *kippah* can only be imposed after a fourth violation — the one following the warning. Tosafos notes the difficulty in explaining precisely why Abba Shaul cannot issue the *kippah* warning earlier, and the question is left without a fully satisfying resolution.

## Kippah Structure

### Height of Kippah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי כִּיפָּה? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: מְלֹא קוֹמָתוֹ. וְהֵיכָא רְמִיזָא? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: ״תְּמוֹתֵת רָשָׁע רָעָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **And what** is the nature of this **vaulted chamber? Rav Yehuda says:** It is a small chamber that is **the full height of** the transgressor, and it does not allow him to move in it. The Gemara asks: **And where** is this punishment **intimated? Reish Lakish said** that it is intimated in the verse: **"Evil shall kill the wicked"** (Psalms 34:22). Ultimately, an evildoer finds his demise through his evil, and the result is this harsh form of death.

Having established the principle and mechanism of the *kippah*, the *gemara* briefly describes its physical structure. Rav Yehuda teaches: *malei komaso* — the *kippah* was precisely as high as the person confined within it. It was a solitary enclosure affording no room beyond the prisoner's own stature, a form of total confinement.

### Scripture Hint

The *gemara* asks where there is a scriptural allusion to the legitimacy of this institution. Reish Lakish finds a hint in the verse *timosis rasha ra'ah* — the evil of a wicked person will bring about his death. This supports the *gemara*'s earlier reasoning: since the *kippah* is understood as a mechanism of bringing about death for one who is intrinsically deserving of it by virtue of his repeated *karet* violations, there is biblical warrant for arranging the circumstances of that death.

### Know Not Your Time

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״כִּי [גַּם] לֹא יֵדַע הָאָדָם אֶת עִתּוֹ כַּדָּגִים שֶׁנֶּאֱחָזִים בִּמְצוֹדָה רָעָה״? מַאי ״מְצוֹדָה רָעָה״? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: חַכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a related statement. **And Reish Lakish says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "For man also knows not his time, like fish snared in an evil net"** (Ecclesiastes 9:12)? **What** is the nature of this **evil net? Reish Lakish says:** It is **a hook;** although it is small and the fish is much larger, the fish cannot escape it.

A second teaching of Reish Lakish is appended, connected by the theme of death. He cites the verse: *ki gam lo yeda ha'adam es ito* — a person does not know his time — *kedagim shenechazim be-metzudah ra'ah*, like fish caught in an evil net. The *gemara* asks what *metzudah ra'ah* refers to. Reish Lakish answers: *chikah* — a fishing hook. The image is striking: a tiny instrument capable of ensnaring a massive creature. One never knows what seemingly minor thing may prove fatal. With this rather sobering reflection, the discussion of the *kippah* concludes.

## Murderer, No Witnesses

### Murderer Confined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַהוֹרֵג נֶפֶשׁ שֶׁלֹּא בְּעֵדִים, מַכְנִיסִין אוֹתוֹ לַכִּיפָּה וּמַאֲכִילִין אוֹתוֹ לֶחֶם צָר וּמַיִם לָחַץ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who kills a person not in** the presence of **witnesses** and it is impossible to judge him in court, the court **places him into a vaulted chamber and feeds him sparing bread and scant water** (see Isaiah 30:20).

The next *mishna* introduces a second application of *kippah* confinement: *ha-horeg nefesh she-lo be-eidin* — one who kills without the kind of testimony legally sufficient for court-ordered execution. Such a person is confined to the *kippah* and given *lechem tzar u-mayim lachatz* — meager rations of bread and water.

### Evidence Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא יָדְעִינַן? אָמַר רַב: בְּעֵדוּת מְיוּחֶדֶת. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: שֶׁלֹּא בְּהַתְרָאָה. וְרַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר אֲבִימִי: כְּגוֹן דְּאִיתַּכְחוּשׁ בִּבְדִיקוֹת וְלָא אִיתַּכְחוּשׁ בַּחֲקִירוֹת, כְּדִתְנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה וּבָדַק בֶּן זַכַּאי בְּעוּקְצֵי תְאֵנִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: If there are no witnesses, **from where do we know** that he killed a person and is liable to be punished? **Rav says:** The *tanna* of the mishna is speaking **about** a case of **disjointed testimony,** where the witnesses were not together and they witnessed the murder from different vantage points. The court cannot convict a person for committing a murder based on that type of testimony even though it is clear that the witnesses are telling the truth. **And Shmuel says:** The *tanna* of the mishna is speaking about a case **where** the witnesses testified that they witnessed the murder but there was **no forewarning,** and therefore the court cannot convict him. **And Rav Ḥisda** says that **Avimi says:** The *tanna* of the mishna is speaking of a case **where** the witnesses **contradicted** each other **in the examinations** that involve matters peripheral to the murder **but did not contradict** each other **in the interrogations,** which are integral to the murder, i.e., time and place. Therefore, it is clear to the court that the accused is guilty and consequently they place him in the vaulted chamber. **As we learned** in a mishna (40a): There was **an incident and ben Zakkai examined** the witnesses **with regard to the stems of figs** on the fig tree beneath which the murder took place. Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai questioned the witnesses about the thickness of the stems in order to determine whether they would contradict each other in this peripheral detail in order to save the accused (see 41a).

The *gemara* asks: if there were no proper witnesses, how do we know he committed the murder? Three scenarios are suggested. Rav says: *be-eidus meyuechedes* — there were two witnesses but they observed the act from separate vantage points and did not see one another, failing the technical requirement of joint testimony necessary for a capital conviction. Shmuel says: *she-lo be-hasra'ah* — witnesses were present but did not issue the formal warning (*hasra'ah*) required to render the act judicially actionable. Rav Chisda, citing Avimi, says: *de-iskachish be-bedikos ve-lo iskachish be-chakiros* — the witnesses contradicted themselves on peripheral examination questions (*bedikos*) but remained consistent on the essential substantive questions (*chakiros*). The contradiction on incidental matters was technically sufficient to disqualify their testimony for capital purposes, yet the underlying evidence of guilt remained compelling. A precedent for such rigorous cross-examination is cited: the case of Ben Zakkai, who pressed witnesses even on the stems of figs — trivial details — precisely to probe the reliability of testimony.

### Why Murderers

One might ask why the *kippah* is specifically applied to murderers in this context — rather than, say, to someone who desecrated Shabbos without a valid *hasra'ah*. Rav Chisda's framing makes the answer clear: this is not a formal legal proceeding but a societal response to a genuine and demonstrated danger. The entire structure of the *kippah* for the murderer operates outside the formal rules of Torah adjudication, precisely because those rules — essential as they are — have in this case produced an outcome incompatible with protecting society. The murderer is known; only a technical deficiency in the legal process has prevented formal execution.

### Combined Mishnayos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאֲכִילִין אוֹתוֹ לֶחֶם צָר וּמַיִם לָחַץ. מַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא דְּקָתָנֵי: נוֹתְנִין לוֹ לֶחֶם צָר וּמַיִם לָחַץ, וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָתָם דְּקָתָנֵי: מַאֲכִילִין אוֹתוֹ שְׂעוֹרִין עַד שֶׁכְּרֵיסוֹ מִתְבַּקַּעַת? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי נוֹתְנִין לוֹ לֶחֶם צָר וּמַיִם לָחַץ עַד שֶׁיּוּקְטַן מַעְיָינוֹ, וַהֲדַר מַאֲכִילִין אוֹתוֹ שְׂעוֹרִין עַד שֶׁכְּרֵיסוֹ מִתְבַּקַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And feeds him sparing bread and scant water.** The Gemara asks: **What is different** in the mishna **here that** the *tanna* **teaches** that the court **gives him sparing bread and scant water** in the vaulted chamber, **and what is different** in the previous mishna **there that** the *tanna* **teaches** that the court **feeds him barley** bread **until his belly ruptures;** are these two different punishments? **Rav Sheshet says:** Both **this and that** are one punishment; first, the court **gives him sparing bread and scant water until his intestines contract** due to his starvation diet, **and then** the court **feeds him barley** bread that expands in his innards **until his belly ruptures.**

A textual question arises from the contrast between the two *mishnayos*. The *mishna* of the repeat offender specifies *se'orin* — barley — and *ad she-kreiso misbaka'as*, until the stomach bursts. The present *mishna* specifies *lechem tzar u-mayim lachatz* — sparse bread and pressed water. Are these contradictory regimens?

The *gemara* resolves the tension by reading the two *mishnayos* as describing a single combined process. The murderer is first given meager rations of bread and water, which cause his stomach to contract and shrink. Then barley is introduced, which, absorbing liquid, expands and causes the stomach to rupture — *kreiso misbaka'as*. The two *mishnayos* describe sequential stages of the same fatal regimen. Since the rupture is the mechanism of death on the *Bavli*'s reading, it applies to the murderer as well — there is no reason the murderer should be subject to a less severe outcome than the *karet* recidivist. The combination of the two *mishnayos* resolves the apparent contradiction and produces a unified account.

## Zealotry Kna'im

### Three Zealot Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַגּוֹנֵב אֶת הַקַּסְוָה, וְהַמְקַלֵּל בְּקוֹסֵם, וְהַבּוֹעֵל אֲרַמִּית – קַנָּאִין פּוֹגְעִין בּוֹ. כֹּהֵן שֶׁשִּׁמֵּשׁ בְּטוּמְאָה – אֵין אֶחָיו הַכֹּהֲנִים מְבִיאִין אוֹתוֹ לְבֵית דִּין, אֶלָּא פִּרְחֵי כְהוּנָּה מוֹצִיאִין אוֹתוֹ חוּץ לָעֲזָרָה וּמְפַצְיעִין אֶת מוֹחוֹ בִּגְזִירִין. זָר שֶׁשִּׁמֵּשׁ בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ – רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: בְּחֶנֶק, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who steals a *kasva*, and one who curses with a sorcerer, and one who engages in intercourse with an Aramean woman, zealots strike him** and kill him. Although the Torah does not say that one who performs one of these actions is liable to be executed, it is permitted for anyone who zealously takes the vengeance of the Lord to do so. In the case of **a priest who performed** the Temple **service in** a state of **ritual impurity, his priestly brethren do not bring him to court** for judgment; **rather, the young men of the priesthood remove him from the Temple courtyard and pierce his skull with pieces of wood.** In the case of **a non-priest who performed the service in the Temple, Rabbi Akiva says:** His execution is **by strangulation, and the Rabbis say:** He is not executed with a court-imposed death penalty; rather, he is liable to receive death **at the hand of Heaven.**

The final *mishna* of the daf introduces the institution of *kana'im pogim bo* — zealots who may strike without formal court process. Three cases are enumerated. First: one who steals the *kasva*, a vessel of the *beis hamikdash*. Second: one who curses God through a *kosem*, a prognosticator. Third — and most famous — *ha-ba al ha-aramis*, a man who has intercourse with a non-Jewish woman. In all three cases, *kana'im pogim bo*: a zealot may act and kill the offender in the moment, without judicial authorization. The court neither prosecutes these cases through formal channels nor imposes extra-legal punishment; it is specifically the domain of private zealous action.

A further case is appended, though it operates somewhat differently: a *kohen* who performs the Temple service while *tamei* (*kohen she-shimesh be-tumah*). His fellow *kohanim* do not bring him before *beis din* — apparently because no formal capital penalty applies — but the young *kohanim* (*pirchei kehunah*) take him outside the *azarah* and break open his skull with logs. And the case of a *zar* — a non-*kohen* — who performs Temple service: Rabbi Akiva holds this is punishable by *chenek* through the court; the Sages hold it is *bidei shamayim*, a heavenly punishment.

### Kasva Temple Vessel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי קַסְוָה? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: כְּלִי שָׁרֵת. וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְאֶת קְשׂוֹת הַנָּסֶךְ״. וְהֵיכָא רְמִיזָא? ״וְלֹא יָבֹאוּ לִרְאוֹת כְּבַלַּע אֶת הַקֹּדֶשׁ וָמֵתוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What** is the ***kasva*** mentioned in the mishna? **Rav Yehuda says:** It is a term used to describe any **service vessel** utilized in the Temple. **And likewise, it is stated: "And the jars [*kesot*] with which to pour libations"** (Numbers 4:7). The Gemara asks: **And where is it intimated** that one who steals a service vessel is liable to be killed by zealots? The Gemara answers that it is intimated in the verse: **"But they shall not come to see the sacred items as they are being covered [*kevala*], lest they die"** (Numbers 4:20). The Gemara interprets the term *kevala* as one who ingests, i.e., who takes (see Job 20:15), and the verse thereby teaches that one who steals a service vessel is liable to be killed.

Rav Yehuda identifies the *kasva* as a *kli shares* — a sacred Temple vessel. The term appears in a verse describing the components of the *shulchan*, referring to the boards or supports associated with it. The *gemara* seeks a scriptural hint for the death penalty attached to stealing such a vessel: *ve-lo yavo'u lir'os ke-vala es ha-kodesh ve-meisu* — they shall not come to see as the holy things are being swallowed up, lest they die. The phrase *ke-vala es ha-kodesh* — consuming or taking possession of the sacred — is read as a basis for treating the theft of sacred vessels as a capital-adjacent offense. Rashi notes, however, that this remains only a *remez*, a hint, and does not constitute a formal derivation. Moreover, even if there is some basis for a death penalty, the further inference — that private zealots rather than the court are the appropriate agents — is not derived from this verse and remains unexplained by the *gemara*.

### Prognosticator Curse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַמְקַלֵּל בְּקוֹסֵם. תָּנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: יַכֶּה קוֹסֵם אֶת קוֹסְמוֹ. רַבָּנַן, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבָּה בַּר מָרִי, אָמְרִי: יַכֵּהוּ קוֹסֵם לוֹ וּלְקוֹנוֹ וּלְמַקְנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And one who curses with a sorcerer** is among those liable to be killed by zealots. **Rav Yosef teaches** that the reference is to one who says: **Let the sorcerer strike his sorcerer,** meaning one who curses God in the name of idol worship. He does not explicitly say God's name, but he believes that a sorcerer can curse the sorcerer who provided him with his powers, i.e., the Divine Presence. **The Rabbis, and some say** that it was **Rabba, son of Mari, say** that the reference is to one who says: **Let the sorcerer strike him,** i.e., the individual with whom he is quarreling, **and his Creator and his Provider.** Since he curses God, it is permitted for zealots to kill him.

The second case, *ha-mekallel be-kosem*, is explained by a *beraita* taught by Rav Yosef. The offense involves invoking a *kosem* — a prognosticator or diviner — as a vehicle for cursing God. The formulation described is: *yakei kosem es kosmo* — may the *kosem* strike his own [i.e., the one who gave him his power of divination], treating the *kosem* as a divine or semi-divine figure capable of acting against God. A parallel formulation cited by the Sages includes: *yakehu kosem lo u-le-kono u-le-maknehu* — may the *kosem* strike this person, and his God, and his Creator — adding both a curse against another person and an invocation against the Creator. While not technically *mekallel ha-shem* in its formal sense — which requires using God's name to curse God — this language approximates blasphemy closely enough to fall into this category.

The *gemara* does not here explain the basis for the rule of *kana'im pogim bo* in this case — any more than it resolved that question for the theft of the *kasva*. The underlying rationale, presumably some notion of protecting the sanctity of God and the Temple, is left implicit. The *gemara* similarly does not bridge the gap between recognizing an offense as serious and authorizing private zealous killing rather than formal judicial response. These are questions the *gemara* leaves open, and we will continue from here tomorrow.
