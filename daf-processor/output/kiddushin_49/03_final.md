# Kiddushin 49 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Object Misrepresentation

### Mishnah Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּכוֹס זֶה שֶׁל יַיִן״ וְנִמְצָא שֶׁל דְּבַשׁ, ״שֶׁל דְּבַשׁ״ וְנִמְצָא שֶׁל יַיִן, ״בְּדִינָר זֶה שֶׁל כֶּסֶף״ וְנִמְצָא שֶׁל זָהָב, ״שֶׁל זָהָב״ וְנִמְצָא שֶׁל כֶּסֶף, ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֲנִי עָשִׁיר״ וְנִמְצָא עָנִי, ״עָנִי״ וְנִמְצָא עָשִׁיר – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אִם הִטְעָהּ לְשֶׁבַח, מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If a man said to a woman: **Be betrothed to me with this cup of wine, and it was found** to be a cup **of honey;** or if he said: With this cup **of honey, and it was found** to be a cup **of wine;** or if he said: **With this dinar** made **of silver, and it was found** to be made **of gold;** or if he said: With this dinar made **of gold, and it was found** to be made **of silver;** or if he said: **On the condition that I am wealthy, and he was found** to be **poor;** or if he said: On the condition that I am **poor, and he was found** to be **wealthy, she is not betrothed** in any of these cases. **Rabbi Shimon says: If he misled her to** her **advantage** by giving her something better than what he stated, or if his status was greater than he claimed, she is **betrothed.**

The daf begins in the middle of 48b, continuing the discussion of topics relating to the act of *kiddushin*. The *Mishnah* presents a series of cases in which the object or condition of *kiddushin* turns out to be different from what was specified. The *Mishnah* says: *hiskadshi li be-cos ze shel yayin ve-nimtza shel devash* — if a man says to a woman, "Marry me with this goblet of wine," and it turns out to be filled with honey; or conversely, he said honey and it turned out to be wine. Similarly, *bedinar ze shel kesef ve-nimtza shel zahav* — with this silver *dinar* and it is found to be gold; or he said gold and it was found to be silver. And finally, *al menat she-ani ashir ve-nimtza ani* — on the condition that I am wealthy and he turns out to be poor; or he said poor and turns out to be wealthy. In all of these cases, the *Mishnah* rules *eina mekudeshet* — the *kiddushin* is not valid.

This applies even in the cases where, by any objective measure, the woman received something better than what was promised — gold instead of silver, a wealthy husband instead of a poor one. The reasoning is that the condition was not met, regardless of whether the deviation was for the better or for the worse.

### Conditions vs. Objects

The *Mishnah* presents two analytically distinct categories of misrepresentation. The first involves the object given to effect the *kiddushin* — the goblet or the *dinar* — where the specific item turned out to be different from what was named. The second involves the *al menat* formulation, an explicit conditional stipulation about the man's personal status or circumstances. When a man says *al menat she-ani ashir*, "on the condition that I am wealthy," and it emerges that he is not, that is more obviously problematic: he stated a condition, and the condition was not satisfied. Yet the *Mishnah* treats the object cases similarly. Even when the woman received something of greater value than what was specified, the *kiddushin* is invalid because it was different from what was stated.

### R. Shimon: Leshevach

Rabbi Shimon disputes this ruling in cases where the deviation is *leshevach* — an improvement. *Rabbi Shimon omer: im hita'a leshevach, mekudeshet* — if she was misled and it turned out to be better, the *kiddushin* is valid. This applies both to the object of *kiddushin* (the coin or goblet) and to the personal condition (*al menat*). The *Gemara* will now examine each category in turn.

## Goblet Definition

### Three Baraitot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּכוֹס זֶה״, תָּנֵי חֲדָא: בּוֹ וּבַמֶּה שֶׁבְּתוֹכוֹ, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: בּוֹ וְלֹא בַּמֶּה שֶׁבְּתוֹכוֹ. וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: בַּמֶּה שֶׁבְּתוֹכוֹ וְלֹא בּוֹ. וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא – בְּמַיָּא, הָא – בְּחַמְרָא, הָא – בְּצִיהֲרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** With regard to one who says to a woman: **Be betrothed to me with this cup,** without further specification, it **is taught** in **one** *baraita* (*Tosefta* 2:3) that the betrothal is effected **with** the cup **and** also **with its contents. And** it **is taught** in **another** *baraita* that the betrothal is effected **with** the cup **but not with its contents. And** it **is taught** in yet **another** *baraita* that the betrothal is effected **with its contents but not with it.** The Gemara comments: **And** this is **not difficult:** The *baraitot* do not contradict one another since **this** one is stated **with regard to** a cup of **water, this** one is stated **with regard to** a cup of **wine,** and **this** one is stated **with regard to** a cup of **oil.**

Before addressing the substantive debate, the *Gemara* first establishes what is included when one says "with this goblet." Three *baraitot* are cited, and they appear to contradict one another. The first holds *bo u-ma she-besocho* — the goblet and what is inside it. The second holds *bo ve-lo ma she-besocho* — the goblet alone, not its contents. The third holds *be-ma she-besocho ve-lo bo* — the contents alone, not the goblet itself. The *Gemara* resolves this by saying the three *baraitot* are not in dispute; they address three different circumstances: *ha be-maya, ha be-chamra, ha be-tziara* — one refers to water, one to wine, and one to fish brine or a similar preserved substance.

### Rashi's Cases

Rashi explains the three cases as follows. If someone says "this goblet of water," the water is entirely inconsequential — no one gives a gift of water. The focus must therefore be on the goblet itself. If someone says "this goblet of wine," the wine is meaningful, but Rashi notes that in common usage people say "here is a glass of wine," and the recipient drinks the wine and returns the glass. Just as one who offers a guest a glass of wine does not intend to give away the vessel, so too here — the gift is the wine, not the goblet. If, however, someone says "this jar of fish brine" (*tziara*), the analogy shifts: one who gives a jar of preserves does not expect the recipient to empty it and return the container. The item comes together with what it is stored in, and therefore in that case the reference is to both.

## Wine/Vinegar Debate

### Gemara's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם הִטְעָהּ לְשֶׁבַח הֲרֵי זוֹ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. וְלֵית לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן יַיִן וְנִמְצָא חוֹמֶץ, חוֹמֶץ וְנִמְצָא יַיִן – שְׁנֵיהֶם יְכוֹלִין לַחֲזוֹר בָּהֶם. אַלְמָא, אִיכָּא דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ בְּחַלָּא וְאִיכָּא דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ בְּחַמְרָא. הָכָא נָמֵי: אִיכָּא נִיחָא לֵיהּ בְּכַסְפָּא וְלָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ בְּדַהֲבָא!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that Rabbi Shimon says: **If he misled her to** her **advantage,** she is **betrothed.** The Gemara asks: **But doesn't Rabbi Shimon accept** the statement of the mishna (*Bava Batra* 83a) that if one sells **wine and it was found** to be **vinegar,** or if he sold **vinegar and it was found** to be **wine, both** the buyer and the seller **can retract from** the sale? Although wine is more valuable than vinegar, the seller is not considered to have defrauded the buyer, in which case only the buyer could retract. **Apparently,** this is because **there is** one **for whom** it is **preferable** to have **vinegar and there is** one **for whom** it is **preferable** to have **wine.** Consequently, receiving wine instead of vinegar is not objectively better. If so, **here too, there is** one **for whom** it is **preferable** to have **silver and** it is **not preferable for him** to have **gold.** Why does Rabbi Shimon say that she is betrothed if he gave her gold instead of silver?

The *Gemara* now focuses on Rabbi Shimon's position regarding the object of *kiddushin* and raises a pointed question. *Ve-lait lei le-Rabbi Shimon yayin ve-nimtza chametz, chametz ve-nimtza yayin, shneihem yecholim lachzor bahem?* — Does Rabbi Shimon not hold that if someone goes to a storekeeper and asks to purchase wine and receives vinegar, or asks for vinegar and receives wine, either party may retract? This is a standard principle: even if one receives something of greater value than what was requested — wine instead of vinegar, which is cheaper — either party can undo the transaction, because the buyer's desire was for a specific item, not simply for maximum value. Someone might need vinegar for a recipe and have no use for wine. The item's identity matters independently of its monetary worth.

If that is true, asks the *Gemara*, then even according to Rabbi Shimon, if a man says "marry me with this silver *dinar*" and it turns out to be gold, she should not be *mekudeshet* — because perhaps she wanted silver specifically. The *Gemara* thus presses: *ika de-nicha lei be-chaspa ve-lo nicha lei be-dahava* — there are those who prefer silver and not gold, just as some prefer vinegar and not wine. How then can Rabbi Shimon validate the *kiddushin* when the object is different from what was stated, even if the substitute is of greater worth?

### Tosafot Distinction

*Tosafot* offers a distinction. When it comes to objects, there is a genuine basis for preferring one type over another regardless of value — a craftsman making jewelry might specifically want silver, not gold. The case of money as a pure monetary instrument — where the only consideration is value — is different. If, for instance, a man said "marry me with a check for one hundred *dinars*" and gave a check for two hundred, that would obviously be valid even according to the *Rabbanan*, since there is no reason to prefer less money over more. The debate in the *Mishnah* arises precisely because the specific material of the coin — silver versus gold — may carry independent significance beyond mere monetary value, just as wine and vinegar do.

The *Gemara* nevertheless presses this question conceptually, observing that even Rabbi Shimon should be troubled by the parallel to the storekeeper case. There is something slightly counterintuitive in the *Gemara's* assumption that a woman accepting *kiddushin* is analogous to a buyer at a shop — she is not acquiring the object for its own sake in the way a customer purchases vinegar for a recipe. Yet the *Gemara* frames the two cases as structurally parallel, and that framing drives the subsequent analysis.

## Abaye's Shaliach

### Woman's Shaliach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי: אַשְׁכַּחְתֵּיהּ לְאַבָּיֵי דְּיָתֵיב וְקָמַסְבַּר לֵיהּ לִבְרֵיהּ: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁאָמַר לִשְׁלוּחוֹ: ״הַלְוֵינִי דִּינָר שֶׁל כֶּסֶף, וְלֵךְ וְקַדֵּשׁ לִי אִשָּׁה פְּלוֹנִית״ וְהָלַךְ וְהִלְוָה שֶׁל זָהָב. מָר סָבַר: קְפֵידָא. וּמָר סָבַר: מַרְאֶה מָקוֹם הוּא לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shimi bar Ashi says: I found Abaye sitting and explaining** this mishna **to his son: With what are we dealing here?** With a case **where one said to his agent: Lend me a dinar** made **of silver and go and betroth for me such and such a woman** with it, **and he went and lent** him a dinar made **of gold** and betrothed her with that. One **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that he is **particular** in his requirement that a silver dinar be used, and consequently she is not betrothed. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon, **holds** that **he is** merely **indicating** his position **to him.** He stated silver only as a guideline, but does not care if it is gold.

The *Gemara* cites Rav Shimi bar Ashi, who reports that he found Abaye explaining this *Mishnah* and Rabbi Shimon's position to his son. Abaye's resolution is as follows. The case in the *Mishnah* is not one in which the man handed an object directly to the woman and it turned out to be different from what he stated. In that straightforward scenario, all would agree that the *kiddushin* is invalid — even Rabbi Shimon — because there is no basis to assume she would accept something other than what was specified. Rather, the *Mishnah* is dealing with a case in which the man appointed a *shaliach*: *amar lei le-shlucho, haveini dinar shel kesef, ve-lech ve-kadesh li et isha plonit* — "bring me a silver *dinar* and go betroth this woman on my behalf." The *shaliach* then went and used a gold *dinar* instead. In that scenario, the woman received exactly what she received — she had full *da'at*. The question is whether the man's original instruction to the *shaliach* is understood as a precise specification (*kefida*) or merely as a general direction (*mareh makom*).

### Makpid vs. Mareh Makom

The debate between the *Rabbanan* and Rabbi Shimon is framed as follows: *mar savar kefida, u-mar savar mareh makom hu lo* — one holds that when the man specified silver, he was *makpid*, exacting, and therefore the *shaliach* exceeded his authority by substituting gold; the other holds that specifying silver was merely *mareh makom* — showing the *shaliach* the direction, indicating that silver is sufficient, while not excluding the possibility of giving something more. According to the view of *mareh makom*, since the *shaliach* gave more than the minimum required, the *kiddushin* stands.

### Language Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי״? ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לוֹ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! הִטְעָהּ לְשֶׁבַח? הִטְעָהוּ לְשֶׁבַח מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! ״נִמְצָא״ מֵעִיקָּרָא נָמֵי דְּזָהָב הֲוָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If so,** if the case involves an agent, why does the mishna state: **Be betrothed to me?** The mishna **should have** stated: **Be betrothed to him.** Furthermore, why does it say: **He misled her to** her **advantage?** The mishna **should have** stated: **He misled him to** his **advantage,** as the agent did not mislead the woman but the one who designated him. Similarly, there is a difficulty with regard to the wording: If it was **found** to be made of gold, as according to this explanation, **from the outset,** when the agent gave it to the woman, **it was also** known to be made **of gold.** This fact was not discovered later.

The *Gemara* objects that this reading cannot be correct. If the scenario involves the man's *shaliach*, the *Mishnah* should have used the language *hiskadshi lo* — "be married to him" — referring to the man who sent the *shaliach*. The *Mishnah* instead uses *hiskadshi li* — "marry me" — which is the language of the man speaking directly. Moreover, if it says "it turned out to be gold" (*nimtza shel zahav*), this implies it was always gold and never misrepresented — the *shaliach* simply chose to use gold. These linguistic difficulties show that Abaye's reading cannot be the correct interpretation of the *Mishnah's* language.

## Rava's Reading

### Woman Appoints

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: אֲנִי וַאֲרִי שֶׁבַּחֲבוּרָה (תַּרְגֵּימְנָא) [תַּרְגֵּימְנוּהָ] וּמַנּוּ – רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין, הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁאָמְרָה הִיא לִשְׁלוּחָהּ ״צֵא וְקַבֵּל לִי קִדּוּשַׁי מִפְּלוֹנִי שֶׁאָמַר לִי הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּדִינָר שֶׁל כֶּסֶף״ וְהָלַךְ וְנָתַן לוֹ דִּינָר שֶׁל זָהָב, מָר סָבַר: קְפֵידָא, וּמָר סָבַר: מַרְאֶה מָקוֹם הִיא לוֹ, וּמַאי ״נִמְצָא״ – דְּקָא צַיִיר בִּבְלִיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava says: I and the lion of the group explained it,** and the Gemara interjects: **And who is** the lion of the group? It is **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin: With what are we dealing here?** With a case **where she said to her agent: Go and accept my betrothal for me from so-and-so, who said to me: Be betrothed to me with a dinar** made **of silver, and** the prospective husband **went and gave** the agent **a dinar** made **of gold.** One **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that she is **particular** about becoming betrothed with a silver dinar rather than a gold one. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon, **holds** that **she is** merely **indicating** her position **to him** but does not care what the agent receives from the man. **And what** is the meaning of: It was **found,** as it was evident from the outset that it was gold? The mishna is referring to a case **where** the coin **was wrapped in a cloth,** and the agent was unaware that he was receiving a different dinar from what she had requested.

Rava, together with Rabbi Hiyya bar Abbin — whom the *Gemara* identifies as the *arya she-bachevraya*, the lion of the group — offers an alternative reading. *Hacha be-mai askinan:* the scenario involves a *shaliach* appointed not by the man but by the woman. The man had previously approached the woman and said, "Marry me with a silver *dinar*." She told him she needed to think about it. She then appointed a *shaliach* and said to him: *tzei ve-kabel li kiddushin mi-ploni she-amar li hiskadshi li be-dinar shel kesef* — "Go and accept *kiddushin* on my behalf from that man who told me to be betrothed to him with a silver *dinar*." The *shaliach* went, and the man gave him a gold *dinar* instead. The *nimtza* — "it turned out to be" — refers to the wrapping (*d'katzir be-vavlisa*): the coin was wrapped up, and when it was unwrapped it was discovered to be gold.

### Identification Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר כּוּלְּהוּ סְבִירָא לְהוּ מַרְאֶה מָקוֹם הוּא לוֹ. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן – הָא דַּאֲמַרַן, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל – דִּתְנַן:
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** With regard to **Rabbi Shimon, and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, and Rabbi Elazar, they all hold** that when one instructs an agent in such a manner **he is** merely **indicating** his position **to him,** as opposed to expressing an insistence on certain details. If the agent makes insignificant changes to the instructions the agency is still fulfilled. **Rabbi Shimon** holds this, as seen in **this** mishna **that we** just **said. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** holds this, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Bava Batra* 160a):

This reading preserves the *Mishnah's* language — the woman appointed the *shaliach*, and the language *hiskadshi li* reflects the man's original words that she is quoting to the *shaliach*. The debate between the *Rabbanan* and Rabbi Shimon now concerns the woman's intent when she gave instructions to her *shaliach*. Did she mean that the *shaliach* must accept specifically a silver *dinar* (*kefida*)? Or was she simply identifying which man she was referring to — "the man who offered me a silver *dinar*" being her way of pointing out the specific individual (*mareh makom*)? If the latter, then the silver was merely her way of directing the *shaliach* to the right man, and if that man chose to give gold, there is no reason to think she would object. Just as one might say "the man in the purple suit said he would betroth me" — and if the *shaliach* finds him in a different suit, that is obviously immaterial — perhaps mentioning silver was similarly just a way of identifying who was being discussed. Rabbi Shimon holds this reading; the *Rabbanan* hold that she was *makpid* on the silver itself.

One might ask why her *da'at* should be more flexible simply because a *shaliach* was involved. The *Gemara* does not fully resolve this, but the implication seems to be that when a person empowers a *shaliach* to act in a matter, there is a degree of latitude granted that would not exist in a direct transaction. The specific designation given to the *shaliach* may function more as identification than as a binding specification.

## Three Tannaim

### Abaye Reports

The *Gemara* now introduces three *Tanna'im* who share the principle of *mareh makom* — that when one gives instructions to a *shaliach*, the specification may be understood as pointing a general direction rather than imposing a strict requirement. These three are Rabban Shimon, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, and Rabbi Elazar. The concept of *mareh makom* is not invented for our *Mishnah*; rather, the *Gemara* borrows it from a recognized dispute about the latitude of a *shaliach*. The introduction of this language into the analysis of our *Mishnah* signals that the *Gemara* understands it to be an application of this broader principle.

### Get Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גֵּט פָּשׁוּט, עֵדָיו מִתּוֹכוֹ. מְקוּשָּׁר, עֵדָיו מֵאֲחוֹרָיו. פָּשׁוּט שֶׁכָּתְבוּ עֵדָיו מֵאֲחוֹרָיו, וּמְקוּשָּׁר שֶׁכָּתְבוּ עֵדָיו מִתּוֹכוֹ – שְׁנֵיהֶם פְּסוּלִים. רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: מְקוּשָּׁר שֶׁכָּתְבוּ עֵדָיו מִתּוֹכוֹ – כָּשֵׁר, שֶׁיָּכוֹל לַעֲשׂוֹתוֹ פָּשׁוּט. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: הַכֹּל כְּמִנְהַג הַמְּדִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** In **an ordinary document, its witnesses** are to sign **inside it,** i.e., on the written side of the paper. In a folded and **tied** document, **its witnesses** are to sign **on the back of it.** With regard to **an ordinary** document **whose witnesses wrote** their signatures **on the back of it, or a tied** document **whose witnesses wrote** their signatures **inside of it, both of these** are **not valid. Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel says: A tied** document **whose witnesses wrote** their signatures **inside of it** is **valid, because one can transform it into an ordinary** document by untying it. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Everything** is **in accordance with regional custom.** If an ordinary document is generally used and one wrote a bound one, or vice versa, the document is invalid.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's position emerges from a *Mishnah* dealing with *gittin*. There are two types of *get*: a *get pashut*, which is a flat document with the witnesses signing at the bottom, and a *get mukushar*, which is a folded and sewn document, traditionally associated with the special concern for a *kohen* who cannot remarry a divorcée and therefore should not divorce hastily. To slow down the process, the *get mukushar* required writing on every other line, folding the blank line over the written line and sewing it shut, repeating this for each line — a laborious process — with the witnesses signing on the back of those folds. The *Mishnah* in *Gittin* establishes that a *pashut get* has witnesses signing inside (at the bottom) and a *mukushar get* has witnesses signing outside (on the back). If these are switched — a *pashut* with witnesses on the back, or a *mukushar* with witnesses inside — both are invalid according to Rabbi Hanina. Rabbi Hanina ben Gamliel says a *mukushar* with witnesses inside is valid because it can simply not be sewn, and then it would function as a *pashut*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says *hakol ke-minhag ha-medina* — everything follows the practice of the region.

### Get Decision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָוֵינַן בַּהּ: וְתַנָּא קַמָּא לֵית לֵיהּ מִנְהַג הַמְּדִינָה? וְאָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: בְּאַתְרָא דִּנְהִיגִי בְּפָשׁוּט וַעֲבַד לֵיהּ מְקוּשָּׁר, אִי נָמֵי: בְּאַתְרָא דִּנְהִיגִי בִּמְקוּשָּׁר וַעֲבַד לֵיהּ פָּשׁוּט – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּוַדַּאי קְפִידָא. כִּי פְּלִיגִי, בְּאַתְרָא דִּנְהִיגִי בֵּין בְּפָשׁוּט בֵּין בִּמְקוּשָּׁר, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״עֲבֵיד לִי פָּשׁוּט״, וַאֲזַל וַעֲבַד לֵיהּ מְקוּשָּׁר. מָר סָבַר: קְפֵידָא, וּמָר סָבַר: מַרְאֶה מָקוֹם הוּא לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And we discussed it: And does the first *tanna* not accept** that one should follow the **regional custom?** It is not reasonable that he should take issue with such a basic concept. **And Rav Ashi says** that they have a dispute in a case where one instructed a scribe to write a document for him: If they are **in a place where the custom is** to write **an ordinary** document, **and he made a tied one for him; alternatively,** if they are **in a place where the custom is** to write **a tied** document, **and he made an ordinary** one **for him;** in both of these cases, **everyone agrees that** he was **certainly particular** in his instructions to the agent that he should follow the regional custom, and if the latter deviated from the custom the document is invalid. The situation **in which they disagree** is where they are **in a place where the custom is** to use **either an ordinary** document **or a tied** one, **and** the one requesting the document **said to** the scribe: **Make an ordinary** document **for me, and** the scribe **went and made a tied** document **for him.** In such a case, one **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that the one requesting the document was **particular** about wanting an ordinary document, and since the scribe wrote a tied document, it is considered to have been written without his consent. **And** one **Sage,** Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, **holds** that the one requesting the document was merely **indicating** his **position to** the scribe, stating that if the scribe wanted to save himself the trouble of writing a tied document there would no objection.

The *Gemara* asks: does this principle not say anything new? We know the proper form for each type. Rav Ashi clarifies: the statement *hakol ke-minhag ha-medina* applies in a specific case — where a man instructed a *shaliach* to write one type of *get*, and the *shaliach* wrote the other. The debate between *kefida* and *mareh makom* applies only when both types of *get* are in common use in that locale. If the region uses only one type and the *shaliach* wrote the other, all agree this is *kefida* and the *shaliach* exceeded his authority. *Ki pligi* — the dispute arises when both *pashut* and *mukushar* are accepted in that place — *be-atra d'nahigi bein be-pashut bein be-mukushar* — and the man said to write a *pashut*, and the *shaliach* wrote a *mukushar*. One opinion (*kefida*) holds the *shaliach* deviated from the instruction; Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's view (*mareh makom*) holds that since both are locally acceptable, the man was merely pointing to the simpler option, and if the *shaliach* chose the more involved form, he acted within the scope of the mandate.

## Shaliach Conclusion

### Limits of Doctrine

The *Gemara* establishes that the principle of *mareh makom* applies specifically when the *shaliach* does *more* than what was asked — he chose the more elaborate form, the more valuable coin, the more difficult option. If the *shaliach* does *less* than was asked, or switches laterally to something of equal or different character, all agree the principal is *makpid*. The only space for the debate between *kefida* and *mareh makom* is when the *shaliach* exceeds the instruction in a way that could plausibly be understood as fulfilling the original intent more amply. This sets the parameters within which Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's ruling and Rabbi Shimon's ruling in our *Mishnah* operate.

## Three Tannaim (continued)

### R. Elazar Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר – דִּתְנַן: הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁאָמְרָה: ״הִתְקַבֵּל לִי גִּיטִּי מִמָּקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״, וְקִבֵּל לָהּ גִּיטָּהּ מִמָּקוֹם אַחֵר – פָּסוּל, וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מַכְשִׁיר. אַלְמָא קָסָבַר: מַרְאָה מָקוֹם הִיא לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar** also holds that when one instructs an agent in such a manner he is merely indicating his position to him, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Gittin* 65a): If there was **a woman who said** to her agent: **Receive my bill of divorce for me** from my husband **in such and such a place, and he received her bill of divorce for her elsewhere, it is invalid. And Rabbi Elazar deems it valid. Apparently, he holds** that **she is** merely **indicating a place to him** where he can receive the bill of divorce, but she does not insist that he accept it in that particular spot.

The third of the three *Tanna'im* cited by Abaye is Rabbi Elazar, whose position derives from a *Mishnah* dealing with *gittin* as well. The *Mishnah* states: *ha-ishah she-amrah, hitkabel li giti mi-makom ploni* — a woman said to a *shaliach*, "Go and accept my *get* on my behalf from such-and-such a location" — that is, from a specific place where her husband agreed to meet. *Ve-kiblah et gitah mi-makom acher* — and the *shaliach* went and accepted the *get* at a different location. The *tanna kamma* holds this is *pasul* — invalid — because her instruction specified a particular place, and accepting it elsewhere is a deviation. *Ve-Rabbi Elazar machshir* — Rabbi Elazar validates it. *Alma kasavar mareh makom hu lo* — Rabbi Elazar holds that by naming the location, the woman was merely pointing the *shaliach* toward where her husband would be, not requiring that the exchange happen at precisely that street corner. It is worth noting that this is likely the case where the phrase *mareh makom* originated in its literal sense — showing the place — before being applied metaphorically to other cases of general direction-giving. Unlike the *get* and *kiddushin* cases above, where one option was more valuable than the other, here the question is simply a different location — neither easier nor harder, just different. Yet Rabbi Elazar's principle of *mareh makom* encompasses even this: the instruction was directional, not constitutive. By analogy, when the woman in our *Mishnah* says "the man who offered me a silver *dinar*," Rabbi Shimon applies the same logic — she was identifying the man, not mandating the metal.

## Conditions Analysis

### Ulla's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: מַחֲלוֹקֶת בְּשֶׁבַח מָמוֹן, אֲבָל בְּשֶׁבַח יוּחֲסִין, דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. מַאי טַעְמָא – ״מְסָאנָא דְּרַב מִכַּרְעַאי לָא בָּעֵינָא״. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, אִם הִטְעָהּ לִשְׁבַח יוּחֲסִים אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** § **Ulla says:** The **dispute** in the mishna between the first *tanna* and Rabbi Shimon is only where he misled her **with enhanced monetary** value, i.e., he gave her something worth more than the item he had stipulated. **But** where he misled her **with enhanced lineage,** so that she was under the impression that his genealogy was less impressive than it in fact is, **everyone agrees** that **she is not betrothed. What is the reason** for this? A woman says: **I do not desire a shoe that is larger than my foot.** She does not wish to marry a man whose social standing is far greater than her own. **This is also taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 2:6): **Rabbi Shimon concedes** that **if he misled her with enhanced lineage, she is not betrothed.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the second part of the *Mishnah* — the *al menat* cases of rich and poor — without formal announcement. Ulla states: *machloket be-shvach mamon* — the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the *Rabbanan* is limited to the case where the man's financial situation turns out to be better than stated, i.e., he is rich rather than poor. Here the *Gemara* does not invoke the wine-and-vinegar argument against Rabbi Shimon. The logic is more intuitive: on the assumption that a woman would generally prefer a wealthy husband, Rabbi Shimon holds that if the *al menat* condition fails because the man is *more* than he represented, the *kiddushin* is valid. The *tanna kamma's* objection in this case may simply be formalistic — the condition was stated and not met — rather than based on any genuine concern that she preferred poverty.

However, Ulla continues: *aval be-shvach yichusin, divrei hakol eina mekudeshet* — but if it turns out that the man has higher *yichus* — better lineage or personal status — than he represented, all agree she is not *mekudeshet*. Why? *Masana de-rav mi-kara'i lo ba'ina* — "a shoe bigger than my foot, I do not want." The woman could say: I am not capable of filling that role. A man of higher status — a *kohen* when she expected a *Yisrael*, or a *Yisrael* when she expected a *mamzer* — imposes expectations and social obligations she never agreed to. She may genuinely not want to be the wife of someone whose status she cannot match or whose position creates discomfort or tension within the marriage. Better *yichus* is not straightforwardly *leshevach* from the woman's perspective, and therefore even Rabbi Shimon agrees the *kiddushin* does not stand.

### Baraita Agreement

A *baraita* confirms this: *modeh Rabbi Shimon she-im hita'ah le-shvach yichusin she-eina mekudeshet* — Rabbi Shimon himself concedes that if the misrepresentation was an improvement in personal status, she is not *mekudeshet*. This is not a case of Rabbi Shimon being overruled; it is his own stated position. The distinction he draws is between objective material improvement — greater wealth — and the more complex category of status, where individual preference and social dynamics render the calculus uncertain.

## Rav Ashi Inference

### Silence Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מַתְנִיתִין נָמֵי דַּיְקָא. דְּקָתָנֵי: ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֲנִי כֹּהֵן״ וְנִמְצָא לֵוִי, ״לֵוִי״ וְנִמְצָא כֹּהֵן, נָתִין וְנִמְצָא מַמְזֵר, ״מַמְזֵר״ וְנִמְצָא נָתִין. וְלָא פְּלִיג רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says:** The wording of **the mishna is also precise, as** the following mishna (49b) **teaches:** If one betroths a woman and states that the betrothal is: **On the condition that I am a priest, and he was found** to be **a Levite;** or: On the condition that I am **a Levite, and he was found** to be **a priest;** or: On the condition that I am **a Gibeonite,** a people prohibited by rabbinic law from marrying into the congregation, i.e., from marrying a Jew of fit lineage, **and he was found** to be **a *mamzer*,** who is prohibited by Torah law from marrying into the congregation; or: On the condition that I am **a *mamzer*, and he was found** to be **a Gibeonite,** in all of these cases she is not betrothed. **And Rabbi Shimon does not disagree** with these rulings. This indicates that if one misled a woman with regard to his lineage, Rabbi Shimon concedes that she is not betrothed.

Rav Ashi argues that this conclusion — that Rabbi Shimon's disagreement is limited to the case of wealth and does not extend to *yichus* — can be inferred from the language of the *Mishnah* itself. The *Mishnah* that immediately follows our *Mishnah* deals with cases such as *al menat she-ani kohen ve-nimtza levi, levi ve-nimtza kohen, natin ve-nimtza mamzer, mamzer ve-nimtza natin* — conditions about tribal and legal status — and rules *eina mekudeshet*. Notably, Rabbi Shimon does not voice any disagreement in that *Mishnah*. Rav Ashi argues: *ve-lo paligi Rabbi Shimon* — Rabbi Shimon's silence indicates that he agrees with the ruling in those cases. Since he expressly disagreed in the case of wealth earlier, his failure to speak up when the *Mishnah* discusses *kohen*, *levi*, and *mamzer* demonstrates that he concedes those cases entirely.

### Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי: אֶלָּא דְּקָתָנֵי: ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בַּת אוֹ שִׁפְחָה מְגוּדֶּלֶת״ וְאֵין לוֹ, עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֵין לוֹ וְיֵשׁ לוֹ, דִּשְׁבַח מָמוֹן הוּא, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלָא פְּלִיג?!
>
> **Translation:** **Mar bar Rav Ashi objects to this** inference: **But** what about **that** which **is taught** in the same mishna: If one betroths a woman and states that the betrothal is: **On the condition that I have a grown daughter or maidservant, and he does not have** one; or if one betroths a woman **on the condition that he does not have** a grown daughter or maidservant **and he does have** one, the latter of **which is** an issue of **enhanced monetary** value, as the difference between one who has a maidservant and one who does not impacts how hard the woman will have to work in the home; in these cases will you **also** say **that** Rabbi Shimon **does not disagree** simply because the mishna does not mention his opinion in that case?

Mar bar Rav Ashi challenges this inference. He notes that the later *Mishnah* also includes a case of *al menat she-yesh li bat o shifcha megudelet* — on the condition that I have an older daughter or maidservant capable of helping in the household — and again Rabbi Shimon does not disagree. But this is surely a case of financial benefit, not *yichus*. If Rabbi Shimon only fails to disagree regarding *yichus*, we would have expected him to reassert his position regarding the daughter or maidservant. His silence there undermines the inference from his silence in the *kohen*/*levi* cases. One could argue, says Mar bar Rav Ashi, that Rabbi Shimon simply didn't repeat his position because *he already stated it* regarding wealth, and *hu ha-din le-seifa* — the same ruling applies to all the cases of wealth, including the daughter and maidservant. If so, his silence in the *kohen*/*levi* cases is equally uninformative.

### Gemara Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא? הָתָם, אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי דִּשְׁבַח מָמוֹן, פְּלִיג בְּרֵישָׁא וְהוּא הַדִּין בְּסֵיפָא. הָכָא, דִּשְׁבַח יוּחֲסִים הוּא, אִם אִיתָא דִּפְלִיג, נִתְנֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** where **both this** case **and that** case involve an inaccuracy **of enhanced monetary** value, it is possible that **he disagreed in the first clause and the same is true in the last clause,** and the mishna did not need to restate his opinion. But **here,** where it **is** a case **of enhanced lineage,** which is a different issue, **if it is so that** Rabbi Shimon **disagrees, let him teach** that explicitly. The fact that no dispute is recorded in the case of enhanced lineage is proof that he concedes in that case.

The *Gemara* responds: *hatam idi ve-idi shvach mamon* — both the case of wealth and the case of having a daughter or maidservant belong to the same category of material benefit. It is entirely natural for Rabbi Shimon to remain silent in the later *Mishnah* regarding those cases, since he already stated his position — having a helpful household member falls under the same principle as financial wealth, and the ruling is identical. But the case of *kohen* and *levi* is *shvach yichusin* — a fundamentally different category. Were Rabbi Shimon to disagree there as well, he would have needed to say so explicitly. *Ei ita de-palig litni* — if he disagreed, he should have said it. His silence in that qualitatively distinct context is therefore meaningful, and Rav Ashi's inference stands.

### Megudelet Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָכָא נָמֵי שְׁבַח יוּחֲסִים, מִי סָבְרַתְּ מַאי מְגוּדֶּלֶת – גְּדוֹלָה מַמָּשׁ? מַאי מְגוּדֶּלֶת – גַּדֶּלֶת, דְּאָמְרָה הִיא: לָא נִיחָא לִי דְּשָׁקְלָה מִילֵּי מִינַּאי וְאָזְלָא נָדְיָא קַמֵּי שִׁיבָבוֹתַיי.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** instead: **Here too,** the issue of a daughter or maidservant involves **enhanced lineage,** not enhanced monetary value. His statement should be understood differently. **Do you maintain** that **what** is the meaning of his statement that he has **a grown** daughter or maidservant; that she is **actually grown** up, so that she can be of help to his wife? That is not the meaning of his statement. Rather, **what** is the meaning of: **Grown?** That she **grows** and plaits hair, i.e., he has a daughter or maidservant who is a hairdresser. Why might the potential bride view this as a drawback? **Because she** can **say:** It is **not satisfactory for me** to live in the house with a hairdresser, **as she will take words** she hears **from me and will go pass** them **before my neighbors,** meaning she will gossip about me to others. This concern is more akin to a matter of lineage than a matter of monetary value.

The *Gemara* then offers an alternative answer (*iba'it eima*) that approaches the matter differently. One might reconsider what *megudelet* means. The simple reading takes it to mean a grown (*gedolah*) daughter or maidservant — an adult who can be useful around the house, which is indeed a material benefit analogous to wealth. But the *Gemara* asks: *mai megudelet?* What if *megudelet* does not mean simply an adult? *Tosafot* explains — and this reading is the more compelling one — that *megudelet* means a hairdresser. And why would having a hairdresser in the house be a problem rather than a benefit? Because *lo nicha li de-shakeila mili minai ve-azla ve-ganya kammai shivvutei* — the wife can say: I do not want her to take my personal matters and go spread them among the neighbors. A hairdresser, by the nature of her work, talks while she works and shares what she hears. Rashi's version of this concern is that an important woman (*gedolah* in the sense of prominent) would feel comfortable circulating socially and carrying information. Either way, having such a person in the household is not straightforwardly a benefit for the wife — it introduces the risk of her private affairs becoming public. In this reading, the *megudelet* case is analogous to *yichus*, not to wealth: it is something that may appear to be an improvement but which the woman may legitimately not want. This explains why Rabbi Shimon does not disagree there either.

## Personal Traits

### Reader Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֲנִי קַרְיָינָא״, כֵּיוָן שֶׁקָּרָא שְׁלֹשָׁה פְּסוּקִים בְּבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר עַד שֶׁיִּקְרָא וִיתַרְגֵּם. יְתַרְגֵּם מִדַּעְתֵּיהּ?! וְהָתַנְיָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הַמְתַרְגֵּם פָּסוּק כְּצוּרָתוֹ – הֲרֵי זֶה בַּדַּאי. וְהַמּוֹסִיף עָלָיו – הֲרֵי זֶה מְחָרֵף וּמְגַדֵּף. אֶלָּא מַאי תַּרְגּוּם – תַּרְגּוּם דִּידַן.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** If one said to a woman: Be betrothed to me **on the condition that I am literate** with regard to the Torah, **once he has read three verses in the synagogue she is betrothed. Rabbi Yehuda says** that she is not betrothed **until he reads and translates** the verses. The Gemara asks: Does Rabbi Yehuda mean that **one translates** according to **his own understanding? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Megilla* 3:21) that **Rabbi Yehuda says: One who translates a verse literally is a liar,** since he distorts the meaning of the text, **and** conversely, **one who adds** his own translation **is** tantamount to one who **curses and blasphemes** God? **Rather,** to **which translation** is Rabbi Yehuda referring? He is referring to **our** accepted **translation.**

Having addressed the *al menat* cases involving social status and wealth, the *Gemara* now turns to conditions involving personal traits and scholarly achievement. A *baraita* discusses each in turn. *Al menat she-ani kraina* — on the condition that I am a reader. The minimum that satisfies this condition is *keivan she-kara shelosha pesukim be-veit ha-knesset* — if he has received an *aliyah* and read three *pesukim* in the synagogue, the condition is met and she is *mekudeshet*. Reading three verses publicly demonstrates basic literacy; a high level of scholarship is not implied by the word *kraina* used without further qualification.

Rabbi Yehudah, however, says *ad she-yikra ve-yitargem* — he must be able to read and also do *Targum*, the Aramaic translation. The *Gemara* asks: does this mean he can translate freely in his own words? Surely not — the *Gemara* recalls that Rabbi Yehudah himself ruled that one who translates a verse *ke-tsurato*, in an overly literal fashion, is a liar (*badai*), and one who adds his own embellishments to the accepted translation is a blasphemer. The authorized *Targum* is the traditional *Masorah*-based translation; to deviate from it in either direction is a distortion of the Torah's meaning. The *Gemara* resolves this: *ela mai targum — targum didan*, the *Targum* referred to is the accepted traditional one, understood to be *Targum Onkelos*.

### Learner Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי דַּאֲמַר לַהּ: ״קַרְיָינָא״, אֲבָל אָמַר לַהּ: ״קָרָא אֲנָא״ – עַד דְּקָרֵי אוֹרָיְיתָא נְבִיאֵי וּכְתוּבֵי בְּדִיּוּקָא. ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֲנִי שׁוֹנֶה״, חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר: הֲלָכוֹת, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And this statement applies** only **if he said to her: I am literate, but** if **he said to her: I am a reader,** this indicates that he is an expert in the reading of the Torah, and she is not betrothed **unless** he knows how **to read the Torah, the Prophets, and the Writings with precision.** The Gemara discusses a similar case: If one said to a woman: Be betrothed to me **on the condition that I study [*shoneh*], Ḥizkiyya says** it means that he studies ***halakhot*, and Rabbi Yoḥanan says** it means that he studies **Torah,** i.e., the written Torah.

*Al menat she-ani shoneh* — on the condition that I am a learner. Here, *shoneh* has the connotation of *Mishnah*, the core oral tradition. Hezekiah says the condition is met if one knows *halachot* — basic legal rulings. Rabbi Yochanan says *Torah*, meaning *Midrash Torah*, i.e., the *midreshei halacha* — the halachic midrashim (*Sifra*, *Sifrei*, *Mechilta*) that derive law exegetically from Scripture. A *baraita* confirms this, with Rabbi Meir specifying *halachot* and Rabbi Yehudah specifying *Midrash*. The *Gemara* reconciles Rabbi Yochanan's position with Rabbi Yehudah's — *mai Torah, Midrash Torah* — what he meant by "Torah" is the *midrashic* exposition of the Torah's laws.

### Student Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: אֵיזוֹ הִיא מִשְׁנָה? רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: הֲלָכוֹת, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מִדְרָשׁ! מַאי תּוֹרָה – מִדְרַשׁ תּוֹרָה. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי דַּאֲמַר לַהּ: ״תָּנֵינָא״, אֲבָל אֲמַר לַהּ: ״תַּנָּא אֲנָא״, עַד דְּתָנֵי הִילְכְתָא, סִפְרָא, וְסִיפְרֵי, וְתוֹסֶפְתָּא. ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֲנִי תַּלְמִיד״ – אֵין אוֹמְרִים כְּשִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן עַזַּאי וּכְשִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן זוֹמָא, אֶלָּא, כֹּל שֶׁשּׁוֹאֲלִין אוֹתוֹ בְּכׇל מָקוֹם דָּבָר אֶחָד בְּתַלְמוּדוֹ וְאוֹמְרוֹ, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּמַסֶּכְתָּא דְכַלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to Rabbi Yoḥanan from a *baraita*: **What is** the meaning of: **Mishna? Rabbi Meir says *halakhot*, Rabbi Yehuda says homiletics.** Neither of them, however, says that it refers to the written Torah. **What** is the meaning of: **Torah,** that Rabbi Yoḥanan said? It is **homiletic** interpretation **of the Torah. And this statement applies** only **if he said to her: I study [*taneina*]. But** if **he says to her: I am a *tanna* [*tanna ana*],** she is not betrothed **unless he studies *halakha*,** i.e., Mishna, ***Sifra* and *Sifrei*, and *Tosefta*.** If a man says to a woman: Be betrothed to me **on the condition that I am a student** of Torah, **one does not say** that he must be a student who is scholarly **like Shimon ben Azzai or like Shimon ben Zoma,** who were called students despite their great knowledge, as they were never ordained. **Rather,** it means **anyone who,** when **he is asked one matter in any topic of his studies,** responds appropriately **and can say** what he has learned, **and** this suffices **even** if his statement was **in** the **tractate of *Kalla*.**

### Sage Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֲנִי חָכָם״ – אֵין אוֹמְרִים כְּחַכְמֵי יַבְנֶה, כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וַחֲבֵירָיו, אֶלָּא, כֹּל שֶׁשּׁוֹאֲלִים אוֹתוֹ דְּבַר חׇכְמָה בְּכׇל מָקוֹם וְאוֹמְרָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, if a man says to a woman: Be betrothed to me **on the condition that I am a scholar, one does not say** that he must be **like the scholars of Yavne, like Rabbi Akiva and his colleagues. Rather,** it is referring to **anyone who,** when **he is asked** about **a matter of wisdom on any topic** related to the Torah, responds appropriately **and can say** what he has learned.

## Moral Traits

### Valiant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֲנִי גִּבּוֹר״ – אֵין אוֹמְרִים כְּאַבְנֵר בֶּן נֵר וּכְיוֹאָב בֶּן צְרוּיָה, אֶלָּא כֹּל שֶׁחֲבֵירָיו מִתְיָרְאִים מִמֶּנּוּ מִפְּנֵי גְבוּרָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If a man says to a woman: Be betrothed to me **on the condition that I am strong, one does not say** that he must be as strong **as Abner ben Ner,** King Saul's cousin and general, **or** as strong **as Joab ben Zeruiah,** King David's nephew and general. **Rather,** it means **anyone of whom others are afraid due to his strength.**

*Al menat she-ani gibbor* — on the condition that I am valorous. The standard is not the level of Avner ben Ner or Yoav ben Tzeruyah, the great military commanders. Rather, *kol she-chaverav mis-yar'im heimenu mipnei gevurato* — anyone whose friends are afraid of him because of his physical strength and formidability. Even if it later turns out that in absolute terms he is not a strong man, if his social circle regards him as someone not to be crossed, the condition is considered met.

### Rich

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֲנִי עָשִׁיר״ – אֵין אוֹמְרִים כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן חַרְסוֹם וּכְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה, אֶלָּא כֹּל שֶׁבְּנֵי עִירוֹ מְכַבְּדִים אוֹתוֹ מִפְּנֵי עוֹשְׁרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If a man says to a woman: Be betrothed to me **on the condition that I am wealthy, one does not say** he must be as wealthy **as Rabbi Elazar ben Ḥarsom or** as wealthy **as Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, but** rather it can refer to **anyone who is honored by the members of his town due to his wealth.**

*Al menat she-ani ashir* — on the condition that I am wealthy. Again, not the standard of Rabbi Elazar ben Charsom or Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah, men of legendary wealth. Rather, *kol she-bnei iro mechabdim oto mipnei oshro* — anyone whom the people of his town honor and respect on account of his wealth. Relative standing in one's social context defines *ashir* for purposes of this condition.

### Righteous

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֲנִי צַדִּיק״ – אֲפִילּוּ רָשָׁע גָּמוּר – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, שֶׁמָּא הִרְהֵר תְּשׁוּבָה בְּדַעְתּוֹ. ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֲנִי רָשָׁע״ – אֲפִילּוּ צַדִּיק גָּמוּר – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, שֶׁמָּא הִרְהֵר דְּבַר עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בְּדַעְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If one says to a woman: Be betrothed to me **on the condition that I am** a **righteous man,** then **even if** he was **a completely wicked man she is betrothed,** as **perhaps** in the meantime **he had thoughts of repentance in his mind** and is now righteous. Similarly, if one says to a woman: Be betrothed to me **on the condition that I am a wicked man,** then **even** if he was **a completely righteous man she is betrothed,** as **perhaps he had thoughts of idol worship in his mind,** a serious sin that would earn him the label of wicked.

*Al menat she-ani tzaddik* — on the condition that I am righteous. *Afilu rasha gamur, mekudeshet* — even if the man was known to be a complete *rasha*, she is *mekudeshet*. Why? *Shema hirhir teshuvah be-da'ato* — perhaps at that very moment, he had a thought of repentance. This is the first case in this series that introduces the word *shema* — "perhaps." The implication of the *poskim* is therefore that she is *safek mekudeshet* — doubtfully betrothed — not definitively *mekudeshet* as in the other cases, where a minimum threshold was simply defined. Here the status rests on a possibility.

This passage is the basis for Rav Soloveitchik's discussion in *Al Ha-Teshuvah*. He notes that full *teshuvah* — which absolves the sin — requires *viduy peh*, verbal confession; *charatah*, genuine remorse; *kabbalah le-atid*, commitment to the future; and *meratze chaveiro*, reconciliation with the wronged party. But the *Gemara* here is addressing something different: not the mechanics of atonement but the transformation of personal identity. A person can undergo a sudden, profound inner transformation — going from *rasha* to *tzaddik* in an instant — even without any external process. The man did not say "on the condition that I have done *teshuvah*"; he said "on the condition that I am a *tzaddik*." Perhaps, at the moment of his betrothal — finding the right woman, sensing a turning point in his life — he has become, at that moment, who he claimed to be.

*Al menat she-ani rasha* — on the condition that I am wicked. *Afilu tzaddik gamur, mekudeshet* — even if the man was known to be a complete *tzaddik* his entire life, she is *mekudeshet*. *Shema hirhir devar avodah zarah be-da'ato* — perhaps at that moment, he had a thought of idol worship. A person who has been wholly righteous throughout his life is not immune to a sudden internal turn. As the principle goes, *al ta'amin be-atzmecha ad yom mosecha* — do not trust yourself until the day of your death. The soul's moral condition at any given instant cannot be assumed from its history. This case, like the previous one, yields only *safek mekudeshet*.

## Agadic Homily

### Distribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲשָׂרָה קַבִּים חׇכְמָה יָרְדוּ לָעוֹלָם, תִּשְׁעָה נָטְלָה אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל וְאֶחָד כׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ. עֲשָׂרָה קַבִּים יוֹפִי יָרְדוּ לָעוֹלָם, תִּשְׁעָה נָטְלָה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם וְאֶחָד כׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ. עֲשָׂרָה קַבִּים עֲשִׁירוּת יָרְדוּ לָעוֹלָם, תִּשְׁעָה נָטְלָה רוֹמִי וְאֶחָד כׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ. עֲשָׂרָה קַבִּים עֲנִיּוּת יָרְדוּ לָעוֹלָם, תִּשְׁעָה נָטְלָה בָּבֶל וְאֶחָד כׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ. עֲשָׂרָה קַבִּים גַּסּוּת יָרְדוּ לָעוֹלָם, תִּשְׁעָה נָטְלָה עֵילָם וְאֶחָד כׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the discussion with regard to various attributes, the Gemara cites a related statement: **Ten *kav*** of **wisdom descended to the world; Eretz Yisrael took nine** of them **and all** the rest of **the world** took **one. Ten *kav*** of **beauty descended to the world; Jerusalem took nine and all** the rest of **the world in its entirety** took **one. Ten *kav*** of **wealth descended to the world; Rome took nine and all** the rest of **the world in its entirety** took **one. Ten *kav*** of **poverty descended to the world; Babylonia took nine and all** the rest of **the world in its entirety** took **one. Ten *kav*** of **arrogance descended to the world; Eilam took nine and all** the rest of **the world in its entirety** took **one.**

Having traversed the territory of rich and poor, wise and foolish, righteous and wicked, the *Gemara* closes this sugya with an *aggadic* passage distributing the qualities discussed throughout the world. Ten measures of *chochmah* — wisdom — came down to the world: nine were taken by *Eretz Yisrael* and one by the entire rest of the world. Ten measures of *yofi* — beauty — came down: nine were taken by *Yerushalayim* and one by the rest of the world. Ten measures of *ashirut* — wealth — came down: nine were taken by the early Romans (*Roma'im kadmonim*) and one by the rest of the world. Ten measures of *aniyut* — poverty — came down: nine were taken by *Bavel*, where the *talmidei chachamim* lived in great poverty, and one by the rest of the world. Ten measures of *gasut* — haughtiness — came down: nine were taken by *Elam* and one by the rest of the world.

### Elam's Haughtiness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְגַסּוּת לְבָבֶל לָא נְחִית? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וָאֶשָּׂא עֵינַי וָאֵרֶא וְהִנֵּה שְׁתַּיִם נָשִׁים יוֹצְאוֹת וְרוּחַ בְּכַנְפֵיהֶם וְלָהֵנָּה כְנָפַיִם כְּכַנְפֵי הַחֲסִידָה וְתִשְׁנֶה אֶת הָאֵיפָה בֵּין הָאָרֶץ וּבֵין הַשָּׁמָיִם. וָאֹמַר אֶל הַמַּלְאָךְ הַדֹּבֵר בִּי אָנָה הֵמָּה מוֹלִכוֹת אֶת הָאֵיפָה. וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלַי לִבְנוֹת לָהּ בַּיִת בְּאֶרֶץ שִׁנְעָר״. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: זוֹ חֲנוּפָּה וְגַסּוּת הָרוּחַ שֶׁיָּרְדוּ לְבָבֶל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But did arrogance not descend to Babylonia? But isn't it written: "Then I lifted my eyes and saw, and behold there came forth two women, and the wind was in their wings, for they had wings like the wings of a stork. And they lifted up the measure between the earth and the heaven. Then I said to the angel that spoke with me: 'To where do they take the measure?' And he said to me: 'To build her a house in the land of Shinar'"** (Zechariah 5:9–11). **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This** refers to **flattery and arrogance that descended to Babylonia,** i.e., Shinar. This indicates that arrogance reached Babylonia as well.

The *Gemara* objects: was haughtiness not a trait of *Bavel*? The verse in Zechariah describes two winged women carrying an *eifah* — a large measure — between heaven and earth, and the angel explains they are bringing it to build a house in the land of *Shinar*. Rabbi Yochanan explains: *zu chanufah ve-gasut ha-ruach she-yardu le-Bavel* — this represents flattery and haughtiness, which descended to *Bavel*. The *Gemara* reconciles: yes, it came down to *Bavel*, but then *ishtarvovei hu de-ishtarvov le-hasam* — it rolled and made its way to *Elam*. The house was never built in *Bavel*; it was carried onward. The language *livnot lah bayit* — "to build her a house" — without the house ever having been completed confirms the inference.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין, לְהָכָא נְחִית, וְאִשְׁתַּרְבּוֹבֵי הוּא דְּאִשְׁתַּרְבּוּב לְהָתָם. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי דְּקָתָנֵי: ״לִבְנוֹת לָהּ בַּיִת״, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ. אִינִי?! וְהָאָמַר מָר: סִימָן לְגַסּוּת – עֲנִיּוּת, וַעֲנִיּוּת בְּבָבֶל הוּא דְּאִיכָּא! מַאי עֲנִיּוּת – עֲנִיּוּת דְּתוֹרָה. דִּכְתִיב: ״אָחוֹת לָנוּ קְטַנָּה וְשָׁדַיִם אֵין לָהּ״, וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: זוֹ עֵילָם, שֶׁזָּכְתָה לִלְמוֹד, וְלֹא זָכְתָה לְלַמֵּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Yes, it descended to here,** to Babylonia, **and it made its way to there,** to Eilam. The language of the verse **is also precise, as it teaches: "To build her a house,"** which indicates that the original intention was to build a house in Babylonia, but it was not built there. The Gemara comments: **Conclude from it** that arrogance did not remain in Babylonia. But this raises a further question: **Is that so? But didn't the Master say: A sign of arrogance** is **poverty, and there is poverty in Babylonia,** and not in Eilam. The Gemara answers: To **what** kind of **poverty** is this referring? It is **poverty with regard to Torah,** which was characteristic of Eilam. **As it is written: "We have a little sister, and she has no breasts"** (Song of Songs 8:8), **and Rabbi Yoḥanan said: This** refers to **Eilam, whose** inhabitants **merited to learn but did not merit to teach.** They did not produce Torah scholars capable of imparting their wisdom to others.

But this raises a further question: does haughtiness not belong to *Bavel* after all? The principle *siman le-gasut aniyut* — poverty is a sign of haughtiness — would locate haughtiness in *Bavel*, which we just said took nine measures of poverty. The *Gemara* resolves: the poverty meant here is *aniyut de-Torah* — poverty in Torah. When a person is haughty, he is self-satisfied with his learning and never genuinely applies himself; he does not give himself over to teaching others or to the self-sacrifice that real *Torah* mastery demands. This is the *aniyut* associated with *gasut*. The proof is the verse applied by Rabbi Yochanan to *Elam*: *achot lanu ketanah ve-shadayim ein lah* — "we have a young sister and she has no breasts." This refers to Elam: *she-zacheta lilmod ve-lo zacheta lelamed* — those who merited to study but never merited to teach, because their haughtiness precluded them from attaining the depth and humility that genuine transmission of Torah requires.

> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to its list of endowments of various groups: **Ten *kav*** of **strength descended to the world; the Persians took nine** and the rest of the world took one. **Ten *kav*** of **lice descended to the world; Media took nine** and the rest of the world took one. **Ten *kav*** of **witchcraft descended to the world; Egypt took nine** and the rest of the world took one. **Ten *kav*** of **plagues descended to the world; pigs,** which carry disease, **took nine** and the rest of the world took one. **Ten *kav*** of **licentiousness descended to the world; Arabia took nine** and the rest of the world took one. **Ten *kav*** of **brazenness descended to the world; Meishan,** near Babylonia, **took nine** and the rest of the world took one. **Ten *kav*** of **conversation descended to the world; women took nine** and the rest of the world took one. **Ten *kav*** of **drunkenness descended to the world; the Kushites took nine** and the rest of the world took one. **Ten *kav*** of **sleep descended to the world; slaves took nine and all** the rest of **the world in its entirety took one.**

The *Gemara* continues the catalogue. Ten measures of strength (*gevurah*) came down: nine were taken by the Persians. Ten measures of lice (*kinim*) came down: nine were taken by the Medes. Ten measures of *nega'im* — leprous afflictions — came down: nine were taken by pigs. Ten measures of licentiousness (*znut*) came down: nine were taken by Arabia. Ten measures of brazenness (*azut*) came down: nine were taken by Meshan. Ten measures of idle chatter (*sicha*) came down: nine were taken by women. Ten measures of drunkenness (*shikhrut*) came down: nine were taken by the Ethiopians. Ten measures of sleep (*shena*) came down: nine were taken by slaves, and one by the rest of the world — and here the *Gemara* concludes the discussion.
