# Bava Batra 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Courtyard Division & Mavoy

### Courtyard Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַכְסַנְיָא – לְפִי בְּנֵי אָדָם מִתְחַלֶּקֶת, אוֹ לְפִי פְתָחִים מִתְחַלֶּקֶת? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְפִי בְּנֵי אָדָם מִתְחַלֶּקֶת. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: זֶבֶל שֶׁבֶּחָצֵר – מִתְחַלֶּקֶת לְפִי פְתָחִים. אַכְסַנְיָא – לְפִי בְּנֵי אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna further asked: When a king issues **a billeting** order to the residents of a courtyard, obligating them to house his soldiers in their homes, is the burden **divided according to** the number of **people** in each household, **or** is it **divided according to** the number of **entrances** that each house has opening into the courtyard? Rabbi Ami **said to him: It is divided according to the** number of **people** in each household. The Gemara comments: **That** opinion **is also taught** in a *baraita*: **The dung in a courtyard** which the residents wish to use **is divided** among them **according to** the number of **entrances** that open into the courtyard, whereas **billeting** is divided **according to** the number of **people** in each household.

The daf begins by picking up from the bottom of 11b, continuing the discussion of how a *chatzer* is divided. The principle established there was division *lefi ptacheha* — according to the doors — meaning that while the overall division follows the number of inhabitants, each doorway nonetheless receives a designated space in front of it. This framework governs how the shared space of a *chatzer* is apportioned among its residents.

### Shift to Mavoy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: אֶחָד מִבְּנֵי מָבוֹי שֶׁבִּקֵּשׁ לִסְתּוֹם כְּנֶגֶד פִּתְחוֹ – בְּנֵי מָבוֹי מְעַכְּבִין עָלָיו, שֶׁמַּרְבֶּה עֲלֵיהֶן אֶת הַדֶּרֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna says:** If **one of the residents of an alleyway wishes to close off** an area **adjacent to his entrance,** i.e., to build a structure in the alleyway adjacent to the entrance to his courtyard, **the residents of the alleyway can prevent him** from doing so. This is because **it will increase the way for them,** since they will have to circumvent the structure when going to and from their homes.

A *baraita* then enters the discussion and shifts the focus from the *chatzer* itself to the *mavoy* — the private alley into which the courtyards open. The previous amud addressed questions about courtyards that open into a *mavoy*, including whether a back courtyard can assert rights to its own space within it — a claim the *baraita* generally rejected. The present discussion continues with other provisions of that same *baraita*.

## Sealed Door Rights

### Sealed Door Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה סָתוּם וּבִקֵּשׁ לְפוֹתְחוֹ – אֵין בְּנֵי מָבוֹי מְעַכְּבִין עָלָיו. אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא פָּרַץ אֶת פַּצִּימָיו, אֲבָל פָּרַץ אֶת פַּצִּימָיו – בְּנֵי מָבוֹי מְעַכְּבִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** If **there had been** an entrance there beforehand that is now **sealed and he wishes to open it, the residents of the** other **alleyway cannot prevent him** from doing so. **Rava says: This was taught only when he did not break its doorposts,** i.e., when the doorposts remained intact even after the entrance was sealed. **But if he** sealed the entrance and **broke its doorposts,** thereby demonstrating that the entrance had been completely negated, **the residents of the** other **alleyway can prevent him** from opening a new entrance.

The *baraita* states: *im haya satum uvikesh liftocho, ain bnei mavoy me'akvin alav* — if a back door into the *mavoy* had been sealed and the owner wishes to reopen it, the residents of the *mavoy* cannot prevent him from doing so.

### Paratz Ptzimav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּיִת סָתוּם – יֵשׁ לוֹ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת; פָּרַץ אֶת פַּצִּימָיו – אֵין לוֹ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **A house** that has **a sealed** entrance still **has** the **four cubits** adjoining that entrance because the entrance can be reopened. If **one broke its doorposts** and sealed the entrance, the entrance is completely negated, and **it does not have** the **four cubits** adjoining it.

Rava qualifies this ruling: *lo shanu ela shelo paratz ptzimav* — this applies only where the owner has not destroyed the doorframe. The distinction is critical. If one seals a door by filling the opening with wood or other material, the doorframe remains intact, and the essential structure of a functional door is preserved. But if one destroys the frame itself — filling the opening with bricks and dismantling the frame entirely — one has effectively renounced any right to use that space as a door, and in such a case the *bnei mavoy* may indeed prevent the owner from reopening it.

### Four Amot Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קֶבֶר שֶׁפִּתְחוֹ סָתוּם – אֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא כׇּל סְבִיבָיו; פָּרַץ אֶת פַּצִּימָיו, וּסְתָמוֹ – מְטַמֵּא כׇּל סְבִיבָיו. בַּיִת סָתוּם – אֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא כׇּל סְבִיבָיו; פָּרַץ אֶת פַּצִּימָיו – מְטַמֵּא כׇּל סְבִיבָיו.
>
> **Translation:** There is a similar distinction with regard to the *halakhot* of ritual impurity. There is a *halakha* that a house in which there is a corpse transmits ritual impurity only through its doorways. The *baraita* continues: **A grave whose entrance is sealed does not render all its surroundings ritually impure;** the ritual impurity extends only to the area opposite the entrance. But if **one broke its doorposts and sealed it,** it is no longer considered an entrance, and the grave **renders all its surroundings ritually impure,** because impurity that has no egress bursts from all sides. Similarly, **a house** in which there is a corpse that has **a sealed** entrance **does not render all its surroundings ritually impure.** But if **one broke its doorposts,** it is no longer considered an entrance, and the corpse **renders all of its surroundings ritually impure.**

Abaye offers a *baraita* in support of Rava's distinction: *bayit satum, yesh lo arba amot; paratz ptzimav, ein lo arba amot* — a sealed house retains its four *amot* in the courtyard apportionment, but a house whose doorframe has been destroyed does not. This *chidush* is striking. It is not merely that one retains the right to reopen a sealed door; it goes further and treats even the currently sealed door as a functionally active one, entitling the space before it to its full four *amot* in a division. This makes the principle even more far-reaching than the original ruling about reopening.

### Dead Body Tumah

The *baraita* continues with a parallel application in the realm of *tumah*: *bayit satum, eino metamei kol svivav* — a sealed house containing a dead body does not render everything around it impure. Although the door is closed (and even sealed), since the doorframe has not been destroyed, the house is not treated as a *kever* — a coffin — that would spread *tumah* to its sides and surroundings. The halakhic reasoning is that one assumes a door will be opened and the body removed; accordingly, the house is not viewed as a hermetically sealed coffin.

Rashi and Tosafot engage both relevant principles here — the laws of *kever*, which render the sides and even the surrounding four *amot* impure, and *sof tumah latzet*, the principle concerning the ultimate egress of *tumah*. The practical ruling, however, depends on the same distinction: *paratz ptzimav, metamei kol svivav* — once the doorframe is destroyed, the house acquires the status of a coffin and renders everything around it impure.

## Mevoot Hamefulashos

### Inter-City Alleys

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מְבוֹאוֹת הַמְפוּלָּשׁוֹת לְעִיר אַחֶרֶת, וּבִקְּשׁוּ בְּנֵי הָעִיר לְסוֹתְמָן – בְּנֵי אוֹתָהּ הָעִיר מְעַכְּבִין עֲלֵיהֶן. לָא מִיבְּעֵי כִּי לֵיכָּא דַּרְכָּא אַחֲרִינָא – דִּמְעַכְּבִי, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ כִּי אִיכָּא דַּרְכָּא אַחֲרִינָא, נָמֵי מְעַכְּבִי –
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to **alleyways that are open to another city,** and through which one would ordinarily travel to reach that other city, if **the residents of the city** in which the alleyways are located **wished to block them** off, **the residents of the city** into which the alleyways open **can prevent them** from doing so, because they have a right to reach their city via those routes. The Gemara explains: **It is not necessary** to state **that they can prevent** them from blocking the alleyways **when there is no alternative route** to reach their town, **but they can prevent** them from blocking the alleyways **even when there is an alternative route.**

Rabbah bar bar Chana says in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: if *mevoot hamefulashot* — alleys open at both ends and connecting to another town — and the residents of the town seek to seal them off, *bnei osah ha'ir me'akvin aleihem* — the residents of that other town can prevent them from doing so.

### Mefulash Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם דְּרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, דְּאָמַר: מֶצֶר שֶׁהֶחֱזִיקוּ בּוֹ רַבִּים, אָסוּר לְקַלְקְלוֹ – כִּדְרַב גִּידֵּל, דְּאָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל: רַבִּים שֶׁבֵּרְרוּ דֶּרֶךְ לְעַצְמָן – מַה שֶּׁבֵּרְרוּ, בֵּרְרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** This is **due to** the reasoning that **Rav Yehuda** says that **Rav says. As Rav says:** One is **prohibited from ruining a path that the public has established** as a public thoroughfare, i.e., steps may not be taken to prevent people from using it. This is in accordance with the statement **of Rav Giddel, as Rav Giddel says:** If **the public has chosen a route for itself** and they walk on it, **what they have chosen is chosen,** and it cannot be taken away from them.

To understand this ruling, it is worth clarifying the distinction between a *mavoi satum* and a *mavoi mefulash*. A *mavoi satum* is a dead-end private alley; a *mavoi mefulash* is one open at both ends and functioning almost like a *reshut harabim*, though not precisely so. A typical configuration might be: the *reshut harabim* runs along the main thoroughfare; branching off it are *mevo'ot satumim* (dead-end alleys) into which individual *chatzeirot* open. A *mavoi mefulash*, by contrast, cuts through from one *reshut harabim* to another, or in this case from one town to the next.

The case under discussion involves a *mavoi* that begins in one locality and provides access to another town. The residents of the first locality wish to reassert their rights over this street and seal it off. The *baraita* rules that the residents of the other town can block this. Even if, as the Gemara goes on to say, those residents have an alternative route — *afilu ki ika darcha achrina nami me'akev* — they can still object. The reason is the ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: *metzar she'hecheziku vo rabim asur l'kalkelo* — a path over which the public has established use may not be destroyed.

Tosafot clarifies an important limitation: *hainu k'she'hecheziku birshut* — this applies only where the public's use was originally permitted. If the owner had objected from the outset and the public was using the path without permission, the owner retains the full right to exclude them, and the Gemara in Bava Kama confirms this: one may take up a hoe and physically block unauthorized entry. But where the owner initially permitted use and the public established its presence through that permitted access, that right cannot subsequently be revoked.

### Established Access Rights

The contrast with the ordinary law of *chazaka* is worth noting. In the standard case of *chazaka*, one who has been using another's property must produce a claim of purchase — mere use without objection is insufficient to establish a right. Here, with *rabim*, the rule is different. The public does not need to claim that it purchased rights; the very fact of established, permitted use suffices. This is a distinctive type of *chazkat shimush*, one that confers rights simply through the pattern of use.

This principle is striking in its breadth. As the Gemara later underscores, the right of the *rabim* is not limited to use of the alley as a thoroughfare. It extends to any pattern of use the public has established — including simply being present in the space.

### Doors in Public Alleys

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב עָנָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מְבוֹאוֹת הַמְפוּלָּשִׁין לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וּבִקְּשׁוּ בְּנֵי מְבוֹאוֹת לְהַעֲמִיד לָהֶן דְּלָתוֹת – בְּנֵי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים מְעַכְּבִין עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Anan says** that **Shmuel says:** With regard to **alleyways that open onto a public thoroughfare,** if **the residents of the alleyways wished to put up doors** at the entrance to their alleyways, **the people** who use **the public thoroughfare can prevent them** from doing so.

Rav Anana says in the name of Shmuel: *mevo'ot ha'mefulashim l'reshut ha'rabim* — alleys that open at both ends onto a *reshut harabim* — if the *bnei mevo'ot* wish to install doors across the entrance to their alley, the *bnei reshut ha'rabim* can prevent them. Here the concern is not that the alley serves as a shortcut — apparently it does not — but rather that the *reshut harabim* has in practice spilled over into the mouth of the alley, and the installation of doors would cut off that use.

One might have thought that the objection was grounded in Rabbi Zeira's ruling in the name of Rav Nachman: *arba amot ha'semuchot la'reshut harabim ke'reshut harabim damyan* — the four *amot* adjacent to the *reshut harabim* are treated as part of the *reshut harabim* itself, functioning as a kind of sidewalk or buffer zone. On that theory, the *nafka mina* would be precise: doors set inward beyond four *amot* would be permitted. But the Gemara rejects this: that four-*amot* principle applies in the context of *tumah* law; here the concern is practical. Since the *reshut harabim* is at times crowded, people spill over into the alley, and that pattern of spillover — however far it extends — is itself the established use that cannot be blocked.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְבוּר מִינַּהּ – הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, כִּדְרַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת הַסְּמוּכוֹת לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, כִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים דָּמְיָין; וְלָא הִיא, הָתָם – לְעִנְיַן טוּמְאָה, אֲבָל הָכָא – זִימְנִין דְּדָחֲקִי בְּנֵי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְעָיְילִי טוּבָא.
>
> **Translation:** Some Sages **understood from this** that **this statement** applies specifically to the area **within four cubits** of the public thoroughfare, **in accordance with** the statement **that Rabbi Zeira** says that **Rav Naḥman says, as Rabbi Zeira says** that **Rav Naḥman says: The four cubits** in an alleyway **that are adjacent to the public thoroughfare are considered like the public thoroughfare** itself. Consequently, this area has the *halakha* of a public thoroughfare. **But** that **is not so. There,** the ruling of Rav Naḥman was stated **with regard to the issue of ritual impurity,** with regard to which only the first four cubits of the alleyway are considered like the public thoroughfare. **But here,** with regard to doors set up at the entrance to the alleyway, **sometimes the public thoroughfare becomes crowded with people and they enter far** into the alleyway, even farther than four cubits.

### Four Amot Spillover

The practical upshot is that the question of where doors may be installed depends on the actual extent of the public's established use. If the *rabim* have never practically entered beyond a certain distance inside the alley, a door installed at that point would presumably be permitted. The four-*amot* figure is not the operative boundary here; what matters is the demonstrated pattern of use.

## Forced Land Division

### Nine Kavin Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא אֶת הַשָּׂדֶה – עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּהּ תִּשְׁעָה קַבִּין לָזֶה, וְתִשְׁעָה קַבִּין לָזֶה כּוּ׳. וְלָא פְּלִיגִי; מָר כִּי אַתְרֵיהּ, וּמָר כִּי אַתְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And** the court does **not** divide a jointly owned **field unless there is** space **in it** to plant **nine *kav*** of seed **for this one and nine *kav*** of seed **for that one.** Rabbi Yehuda says: The court does not divide a field unless there is space in it to plant nine half-*kav* of seed for this one and nine half-*kav* of seed for that one. The Gemara comments: **And** they **do not disagree** with regard to the fundamental *halakha*, as this **Sage** ruled **in accordance with** the custom of **his locale, and** that **Sage** ruled **in accordance with** the custom of **his locale.** In Rabbi Yehuda's locale, even a smaller parcel of land was considered a viable field.

The Gemara now turns to *din chaluka* — the forced division of property — and establishes the governing minimum: one cannot compel a co-owner to divide a field unless each party will receive at least *tisha kavim* (nine *kav*) of land. A *kav* works out to somewhere between 100 and 150 square meters, making nine *kavin* roughly 900 square meters, or the dimensions of a football field — not a vast tract, but meaningful as a threshold of agricultural viability.

The Gemara adds: *v'lo peligi, mar ki atra u'mar ki atra* — the authorities are not in dispute; each speaks according to local conditions. In fertile soil, a smaller plot is genuinely viable; in poor soil, one requires more land to sustain meaningful agricultural activity. The minimum, in other words, is relative to what a plot of that size can realistically produce in its particular context.

### One Day's Plowing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּבָבֶל מַאי? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: בֵּי רָדוּ יוֹמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: The mishna was taught in Eretz Yisrael; **what** practice should be followed **in Babylonia? Rav Yosef said:** In Babylonia, a parcel of land the size of which is **the area of a day's plowing** is considered a field; if each of the parties will receive less than that, the field should not be divided.

In Babylonia, Rav Yosef translates this standard as *bei radya yoma* — the amount of land one can plow in a day. A full day's plowing establishes the threshold of viability. The practical significance of this measure is tied to the ability to hire workers: a parcel that only provides a fraction of a day's work cannot attract paid laborers, and without that capacity it cannot be farmed in a sustainable way.

### Plowing Seasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״בֵּי רָדוּ יוֹמָא״? אִי יוֹמָא זַרְעָא – תְּרֵי יוֹמָא כְּרָבָא לָא הָוֵי; אִי יוֹמָא כְּרָבָא – יוֹמָא דְּזַרְעָא לָא הָוֵי! אִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא יוֹמָא דִּכְרָבָא – דְּכָרֵיב וְתָנֵי; וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא יוֹמָא דְּזַרְעָא – בְּהָדוֹרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is meant by a parcel of land the size of which is **the area of a day's plowing? If** it means **a day's** plowing in **the planting** season, i.e., the winter, when it is easy to plow, since the earth has already been turned over at the end of the summer, the field will **not** require **two** full **days** of plowing in **the plowing** season, i.e., at the end of the summer, when it is more difficult to plow, since the earth is hard and dry. In that case, he will have to pay his summer plowman two days' wages for less than two days of work. And **if** it means **a day's** plowing in the **plowing** season, the field will **not** require a full **day** of plowing in the **planting** season. In that case, he will have to pay his winter plowman a full day's wages for less than a full day of work. The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** it is referring to **a day's** plowing in the **plowing** season, and the field will still require a full day of plowing in the planting season **since he plows** once before he sows the seeds **and** then he **repeats** the plowing after the seeds are sown. **And if you wish, say** instead that it is referring to **a day's** plowing in the **planting** season, and the field will in fact require two full days of plowing in the plowing season if it is **rocky ground,** on which plowing takes longer.

The Gemara probes what exactly constitutes "one day's plowing," since the speed of plowing varies considerably by season. Early plowing, before seeding, is done on harder, unworked ground and takes longer; plowing at seeding time, when the ground has been previously worked and softened, goes faster. The Gemara explores two resolutions. One: take the measure as one day's worth of plowing during plowing season, and during seeding season simply plow twice — the double plowing is agronomically beneficial for the seeds and will constitute a full day's labor. The other: take the measure as one day's worth of plowing at seeding time, and on particularly rocky terrain (*bahadurei*), which offers extra resistance, that same area will in fact require two days during plowing season.

The Gemara acknowledges these as theoretical constructs (*heichi timtzei*). The underlying principle is clear regardless of the precise mechanics: the parcel must be large enough to be genuinely worth cultivating — worth investing labor and hiring workers for. If the specifics of the terrain or the season make it unviable to engage workers for a full day's work, the plot fails the minimum threshold and *din chaluka* cannot be applied.

### Water Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּוְולָא – אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: בֵּי דָּאלוּ יוֹמָא. פַּרְדֵּסָא – אָמַר אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: בַּת שְׁלֹשֶׁת קַבִּין.
>
> **Translation:** In connection with this discussion, the Gemara clarifies the conditions under which **a cistern,** from which its joint owners draw their water, is divided. **Rav Naḥman said:** It should be divided only if each party will receive **the volume of** water needed for **a day's irrigation** work. As for **an orchard, Shmuel's father says:** It should be divided only if each party will receive **an area** large enough to plant **three *kav*,** one-third of the measure required for a field.

The Gemara addresses the case of a *davla* — a shared irrigation source, whether a well, a cistern, or a canal — that the partners wish to divide. How one physically divides a shared water source is not entirely clear, and even Rashi's description — whether the reference is to a well or to a shared canal — leaves the mechanics somewhat opaque. It may refer to dividing the resource not physically but temporally: assigning each party exclusive use on alternating days.

Rav Nachman rules: *kedei diluyo yoma* — there must be enough water on each party's "share" to sustain a full day's worth of drawing and irrigation. Rashi explains this in terms of hiring labor: if after the division each party's access to water suffices to keep workers productively occupied for a full day, the division is viable. But if the split would leave one party with only a half-day's worth of water, that portion is too small to sustain a hired workforce, and the division cannot be compelled.

### Orchard Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי, הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ: ״מְנָת בַּכֶּרֶם אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לְךָ״ – סוֹמְכוֹס אוֹמֵר: לֹא יִפְחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה קַבִּין. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אֵין אֵלּוּ אֶלָּא דִּבְרֵי נְבִיאוּת. בְּבָבֶל מַאי? אָמַר רָבָא בַּר קִסְנָא: תְּלָת אַצְיָאתָה בְּנֵי תְּרֵיסַר גּוּפְנֵי – כִּי הֵיכִי דְּרָפֵיק גַּבְרָא בְּיוֹמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **That** opinion **is also taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who says to another: I am selling you part of a vineyard,** without specifying how much of the vineyard, **Sumakhos says: He may not** give him **less than an area** large enough to plant **three *kav*. Rabbi Yosei said: These are nothing other than words of prophecy,** i.e., I do not see the logic behind this statement, and it is as if based on prophecy and a heavenly decree, as the seller did not mention any area, but rather spoke in the most general of terms: Part of a vineyard. The Gemara asks: **What** is the measure with regard to this matter **in Babylonia? Rava bar Kisna said: Three rows [*atzyata*] of twelve vines, which is** the area **a person can hoe in** a single **day.**

Abaye reports in the name of Shmuel: the minimum viable unit for an orchard (*pardisa*) is three *kavin* — not nine. A *baraita* supports this in the context of a sale: if one says to a friend, "I am selling you a *menat b'karem* — a portion of my vineyard," without specifying the size, Sumchus rules that the portion cannot be less than three *kavin*, since that is the minimum that constitutes a meaningful agricultural unit of this type.

Rabbi Yosi responds sharply: *ein elu ela divrei nevi'os* — these are nothing but words of prophecy. As Tosafot clarifies, this is emphatically not a compliment. *Lo l'shavach ka'amar* — he is not praising Sumchus. Rather, he means: *kelomar she-omer belo ta'am* — you are saying something without textual basis or logical argument. The enterprise of *Torah she-ba'al peh* proceeds through text, proof, and reasoned argument, not intuition or arbitrary assertion. To say something without evidence is to speak as though from divine inspiration — and that is no methodology for halakha.

It is worth noting the distinction. When the minimum is applied in the context of *din chaluka* — forced division — it is an assessment of objective viability based on facts about the land, the community, and agricultural practice. That is legitimate halakhic reasoning. But when a document simply says "a portion of my vineyard" and one interprets that to mean the *din chaluka* minimum, that interpretive move — importing the forced-division standard into a contractual context — is what Rabbi Yosi dismisses as *divrei nevi'os*. There are other contextual factors in a sale, including price paid and surrounding circumstances, that make it far from obvious that the bare minimum for forced division is what the parties intended.

In Babylonia, Rava bar Kasna offers the local standard for an orchard: three units of *atzi'ata*, each consisting of twelve vines — thirty-six vines in total.

### Prophecy to Sages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבְדִּימִי דְּמִן חֵיפָה: מִיּוֹם שֶׁחָרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, נִיטְּלָה נְבוּאָה מִן הַנְּבִיאִים וְנִיתְּנָה לַחֲכָמִים. אַטּוּ חָכָם לָאו נָבִיא הוּא? הָכִי קָאָמַר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנִּיטְּלָה מִן הַנְּבִיאִים, מִן הַחֲכָמִים לֹא נִיטְּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** § In connection with Rabbi Yosei's statement that Sumakhos's words are nothing but words of prophecy, the Gemara reports that **Rabbi Avdimi from Haifa says: From the day that the Temple was destroyed prophecy was taken from the prophets and given to the Sages.** The Gemara expresses astonishment: **Is that to say** that **a Sage is not** fit to be **a prophet?** Rabbi Avdimi seems to say that these are two distinct categories of people. The Gemara explains: **This is what** Rabbi Avdimi **is saying: Even though** prophecy **was taken from the prophets, it was not taken from the Sages.**

The mention of *divrei nevi'os* occasions a significant digression. But before turning to it, the Gemara provides an additional gloss for context: the minimum viable area for the *pardisa* division in Babylonian terms is also connected to *bei yoma* — the amount of land one person can weed in a day. This is consistent with the general principle that the minimum must correspond to a genuinely productive unit of labor.

On this foundation, Rav Avdimi of Haifa introduces a sweeping statement: *miyom shecharav Beit HaMikdash, nitlah nevuah min ha'nevi'im ve'nitnah la'chachamim* — from the day the *Beit HaMikdash* was destroyed, prophecy was taken from the *nevi'im* and given to the *chachamim*. One might read this as describing a paradigm shift — that the channel through which Israel accesses divine guidance moved from the prophetic institution to the activity of *Torah she-ba'al peh*, to the *chachamim* and their interpretive engagement with text. That is one reading. But the Gemara proceeds to take the statement more literally.

## Chacham vs. Navi

### Chacham Prophecy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: וְחָכָם עָדִיף מִנָּבִיא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנָבִא לְבַב חׇכְמָה״ – מִי נִתְלֶה בְּמִי? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: קָטָן נִתְלֶה בַּגָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar said: And a Sage is greater than a prophet, as it is stated: "And a prophet has a heart of wisdom"** (Psalms 90:12), i.e., he is wise. When comparisons are drawn, **who is compared to whom? You must say** that **the lesser is compared to the greater.** Here too, prophecy is compared to wisdom, thus indicating that wisdom is greater than prophecy.

Ameimar immediately challenges: *atu chacham lav navi hu?* — what do you mean? Does this imply that *chachamim* did not previously possess *nevuah*? Did they not always have it? He resolves the tension: *af al pi shenitlah min ha'nevi'im, min ha'chachamim lo nitlah* — even though it was taken from the *nevi'im*, it was never taken from the *chachamim*. They always had it, and they still have it. The statement of Rav Avdimi is thus reread as an affirmation of continuity rather than change: the prophetic institution lost its role, but the *chachamim* — who always possessed *nevuah* — carried on without interruption.

This is a remarkable interpretive reversal. A statement that seemed to describe rupture is recast as describing something more like permanence.

Ameimar then adds a further claim: *chacham adif minavi* — a *chacham* is superior to a *navi*. His proof text: *ve'navi levav chochmah* — "and a prophet has a heart of wisdom." If the prophet is described as having wisdom, *mi nitlah bemi* — who is described in terms of whom? *Hevei omer katan nitlah ba'gadol* — the lesser is attached to the greater. Since the prophet is characterized by wisdom, wisdom is the more fundamental quality.

This pulls back toward discontinuity — or at least differentiation. The *chacham* and the *navi* represent distinct modes of religious engagement, and the *chacham*'s mode is superior. This resonates deeply with the Gemara's understanding of how halakha operates: even if Eliyahu HaNavi were to arrive and pronounce a ruling, we would not accept it on the basis of prophetic authority. Halakha proceeds through text and reasoned argument — *Torah she-ba'al peh* — not through prophetic pronouncement. In the domain of *psak*, the *chacham* not only replaces the *navi* but surpasses him, because the method of active human engagement with the divine text is both the proper channel and the superior one.

This also makes *ein elu ela divrei nevi'os* more pointed. Invoking prophecy in a halakhic discussion is not an appeal to a higher authority; it is an abdication of the enterprise. The *navi* may be a conduit for divine will, but the *chacham* — through the rigorous activity of *Torah she-ba'al peh* — is where halakha actually lives.

### Parallel Discoveries

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: תִּדַּע, דְּאָמַר גַּבְרָא רַבָּה מִילְּתָא, וּמִתְאַמְרָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּגַבְרָא רַבָּה אַחֲרִינָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ. אֲמַר רָבָא: וּמַאי קוּשְׁיָא? וְדִילְמָא תַּרְוַיְיהוּ בְּנֵי חַד מַזָּלָא נִינְהוּ! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: תִּדַּע, דְּאָמַר גַּבְרָא רַבָּה מִילְּתָא, וּמִתְאַמְרָא
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Know** that this is so, that the Sages still enjoy the prophetic gift, **as a great man makes a statement** with regard to a point of *halakha* **and** the same statement **is** then **cited in the name of a different great man in accordance with his** statement, indicating that the Sages makes their statements by way of prophecy. **Rava** disagreed and **said: And what is the difficulty** with explaining this? **Perhaps they were born under the same constellation,** and since they are similar in their traits, they reach the same conclusions. **Rather, Rava said: Know** that this is so, **as a great man makes a statement and** the same statement **is** then **cited**

Abaye brings evidence that *chachamim* still possess real *nevuah*: *de-amar gavra rabba milta u-mitamra mi-shmei de-gavra rabba achrina kavateih* — a great scholar articulates an original insight, and it subsequently emerges that another great scholar, independently and at a distance, said the same thing. The phenomenon of two scholars in different places arriving simultaneously at the same original idea, with no contact or shared influence, suggests a form of divine inspiration — something beyond coincidence.

The parallel with the simultaneous independent discovery of calculus by Newton and Leibniz, in entirely different notational systems and without any evident communication, is a striking modern analogue. Historians remain puzzled by it. Abaye would see in it confirmation of his point.

### Mazal Theory

Rava objects: *dilma tarvaihu bnei chad mazla ninhu* — perhaps they are both governed by the same astrological influence, and their shared intellectual output reflects shared *mazal* rather than shared divine inspiration. This, notably, is a naturalistic explanation for what appeared to be a supernatural phenomenon. The concurrence of insight does not require *nevuah*; it can be explained by the mechanics of *mazal* acting on scholars of similar temperament and circumstance.

### Tradition Discovery

Rava therefore proposes a sharper case: a great scholar articulates an insight and it turns out to have been stated *mi-shmei de-Rabbi Akiva bar Yosef* — in the name of Rabbi Akiva, centuries earlier. Here the *mazal* argument cannot apply: no one would suggest that a contemporary scholar shares his astrological configuration with Rabbi Akiva. If independent discovery across that temporal distance is possible, it must reflect some form of genuine divine inspiration.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא בַּר יוֹסֵף כְּווֹתֵיהּ. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: וּמַאי קוּשְׁיָא? דִּלְמָא לְהָא מִילְּתָא בַּר מַזָּלֵיהּ הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **in the name of** the well-known *tanna* **Rabbi Akiva bar Yosef in accordance with his** statement. It certainly cannot be maintained that the first Sage is similar in his nature to the illustrious Rabbi Akiva, so he must have arrived at his statement through prophecy. **Rav Ashi said: And what is the difficulty** with explaining this? **Perhaps** they were born under **the same constellation,** and **with regard to this issue** the first Sage has the same understanding as Rabbi Akiva.

### Luck vs. Prophecy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: תִּדַּע, דְּאָמַר גַּבְרָא רַבָּה מִילְּתָא, וּמִתְאַמְרָא הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי כְּווֹתֵיהּ. וְדִלְמָא כְּסוֹמֵא בַּאֲרוּבָּה! וְלָאו טַעַם יְהֵיב?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ashi said: Know** that this is so, **as a great man makes a statement and** the same statement **is** then **cited as a *halakha*** transmitted **to Moses from Sinai in accordance with his** statement. The Sage makes a statement that corresponds to words pronounced in Heaven, which, without prophecy, is beyond human capability. The Gemara states: **But perhaps** he arrived at this idea by chance, without the assistance of prophecy, **like a blind man** who makes his way **through a skylight.** A blind man cannot deliberately find a skylight; therefore, his finding it occurs by chance. The Gemara answers: **But does** the Sage **not offer a reason** for his statement? The fact that he demonstrates an understanding of the issue indicates that he does not arrive at his idea by chance, but rather by prophecy.

Rav Ashi objects even to this: *dilma le-ha milta bar mazlei hu* — perhaps regarding this specific matter, the scholar happens to share a *mazal* with Rabbi Akiva, even if not generally. The *mazal* theory proves difficult to dislodge. Rav Ashi therefore proposes a still stronger case: a scholar articulates an insight and it emerges that it corresponds to a *Halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* — a tradition from Moshe at Sinai that was not derivable from any text and that no one would claim could be reached by shared *mazal* with Moshe Rabbeinu. Here, the argument concludes, there must be genuine divine inspiration at work.

The Gemara counters: *dilma ke-suma be-arubba* — perhaps this is like a blind man groping in the darkness. Even a stopped clock is right twice a day. One can arrive at a correct conclusion through sheer luck, without prophecy. To this, the Gemara responds: *v'lav ta'am yahiv?* — but the scholar provides a reasoned explanation for his conclusion! If it were mere luck, a coherent argument could not accompany it.

And yet this very response creates a new tension: if the scholar has a reasoned argument, then why invoke *nevuah* at all? Perhaps the argument itself, rather than divine inspiration, accounts for the correct result. The Gemara leaves suspended the interplay among *nevuah*, *mazal*, and rational argumentation as competing explanations for insight — without resolving which is primary.

### Fools & Children Prophecy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִיּוֹם שֶׁחָרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, נִיטְּלָה נְבוּאָה מִן הַנְּבִיאִים וְנִיתְּנָה לַשּׁוֹטִים וְלַתִּינוֹקוֹת. לַשּׁוֹטִים – מַאי הִיא? כִּי הָא דְּמָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי – דַּהֲוָה קָאֵי בְּרִסְתְּקָא דְמָחוֹזָא, שַׁמְעֵיהּ לְהָהוּא שׁוֹטֶה דְּקָאָמַר: רֵישׁ מְתִיבְתָּא דְּמָלֵיךְ בְּמָתָא ״מַחְסֵיָא״ – ״טַבְיוֹמֵי״ חָתֵים. אֲמַר: מַאן חָתֵים ״טַבְיוֹמֵי״ בְּרַבָּנַן – אֲנָא; שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לְדִידִי קָיְימָא לִי שַׁעְתָּא. קָם אֲתָא. אַדַּאֲתָא אִימְּנוֹ רַבָּנַן לְאוֹתֹבֵיהּ לְרַב אַחָא מִדִּפְתִּי בְּרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: From the day that the Temple was destroyed, prophecy was taken from the prophets and given to imbeciles and children.** The Gemara explains: In **what** way was prophecy given **to imbeciles?** It was **like this** incident involving **Mar bar Rav Ashi, who was standing in the street [*beristeka*] of Meḥoza** when **he heard a certain imbecile say: The head of the yeshiva who will be appointed in Mata Meḥasya signs** his name **Tavyumei.** Mar bar Rav Ashi **said** to himself: **Who among the Sages signs** his name **Tavyumei?** Nobody but **me. Conclude from** the statement by the imbecile **that my hour has arrived,** and I will reap success in this matter. **He arose and went** to Mata Meḥasya. **By the time he arrived, the Sages** had already **decided to appoint Rav Aḥa of Difti as the head** of the yeshiva.

Rabbi Yochanan offers a counterpoint to the entire thesis that *nevuah* now belongs to the *chachamim*: *mi-yom shecharav Beit HaMikdash, nitlah nevuah min ha-nevi'im ve-nitnah le-shotim u-le-tinokot* — from the day the *Beit HaMikdash* was destroyed, prophecy was taken from the *nevi'im* and given to the mentally ill and to children. Rather than the *chachamim*, it is precisely those at the margins of rational society — those unconditioned by systematic thought — who now receive flashes of prophetic insight. The *chacham*, on this reading, operates through a wholly different faculty: rigorous, rational engagement, not prophetic inspiration.

## Prophecy Examples

### Mad Prophecy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דְּשָׁמְעִי דַּאֲתָא, שַׁדּוּר זוּגָא דְּרַבָּנַן לְגַבֵּיהּ לְאִימְּלוֹכֵי בֵּיהּ. עַכְּבֵיהּ. הֲדַר שַׁדּוּר זוּגָא דְּרַבָּנַן אַחֲרִינָא, עַכְּבֵיהּ גַּבֵּיהּ. עַד דִּמְלוֹ בֵּי עַשְׂרָה. כֵּיוָן דִּמְלוֹ בֵּי עַשְׂרָה, פְּתַח הוּא וּתְנָא וּדְרַשׁ. לְפִי שֶׁאֵין פּוֹתְחִין בְּכַלָּה פָּחוֹת מֵעֲשָׂרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **As soon as** the Sages **heard that** Mar bar Rav Ashi **had arrived,** they determined not to proceed with their appointment without the approval of an important figure such as him. **They sent a pair of Sages to him to consult with him,** and **he detained them. They again sent a pair of Sages** to him, and **he detained them** as well. This continued **until they completed** a quorum **of ten** Sages. **Once they reached ten** men, Mar bar Rav Ashi **opened** his lecture, **taught, and expounded.** He did not speak earlier **because one should not open a lecture during *kalla*,** the gatherings for Torah study during the months of Elul and Adar, when **less than ten** men are present. He was then appointed as head of the yeshiva.

The Gemara asks: what is an example of *nevuah* among the *shotim*? The case of Mar bar Rav Ashi. He was standing in the *ristaka* — the marketplace or main boulevard — of Mechuza, when he overheard a certain *shoteh* say: the *reish metivta* — the head of the academy — who will reign in Mata Mechasya signs his name Tivyomei.

Mar bar Rav Ashi took note: among the rabbis, who signs his name Tivyomei? *Ana* — that would be me. *Shema mina le-didi kai le-sha'ata* — if that is what this person is saying, it must be that now is my moment.

### Self-Fulfilling Rise

Mar bar Rav Ashi rose and set out for the yeshiva. In the meantime, the rabbis had already voted and selected Rav Akha mi-Difti as the next *rosh yeshiva*. But when word reached them that Mar bar Rav Ashi was on his way, they sent two rabbis to consult him about the succession. He detained them. They sent another pair; he detained them too. This continued until ten rabbis had been sent and remained with him. Once there were ten rabbis present, *patach hu ve-tana ve-darash* — he opened and taught and expounded. Since a *kalla* — a formal rabbinic discourse — may not be opened with fewer than ten people, and since only a *rosh yeshiva* opens such a discourse, Mar bar Rav Ashi had effectively installed himself in the position. By the time the situation was clear, the matter was settled.

The interplay between prophecy and human agency here is rich. The *shoteh*'s words were the catalyst: without them, Mar bar Rav Ashi would not have set out for Mechuza, and without his arrival, the rabbis would never have consulted him. Yet what followed was entirely the product of deliberate, strategic choices. The *nevuah* functioned as an inspiration that set events in motion, but the outcome was shaped by the choices of the people involved. This is a form of self-fulfilling prophecy — one hears a prophetic word, acts on it as such, and in so doing brings it about.

### Rav Akha's Lament

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָרֵי רַב אַחָא אַנַּפְשֵׁיהּ: כׇּל הַמְּרִיעִין לוֹ – לֹא בִּמְהֵרָה מְטִיבִין לוֹ, וְכׇל הַמְּטִיבִין לוֹ – לֹא בִּמְהֵרָה מְרִיעִין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Understanding that he had been passed over for the position, **Rav Aḥa** of Difti **read about himself** the rabbinic aphorism: **Anyone who is treated poorly will not soon be treated well; and anyone who is treated well will not soon be treated poorly.** Rav Aḥa understood that he had lost the chance to be appointed, whereas Mar bar Rav Ashi had the good fortune to be appointed, and would remain in his position.

Rav Akha mi-Difti, now displaced from the position that had been voted for him, said about himself: *kol de-mari'in lo, lo bimheira metivin lo; ve-khol de-metivin lo, lo bimheira mari'in lo* — one who suffers misfortune will not quickly recover from it, and one who has good fortune will not quickly lose it. For Rav Akha, this meant: this opportunity has slipped away, and there is no reason to expect it to return soon.

Tosafot records a poignant detail. In another source, Rav Akha himself uses stronger language: *le-olam ein metivin lo* — never, not merely not quickly. Tosafot resolves the discrepancy in the most human of ways: *mitechila amar lo bimheira metivin lo she-haya metzapeh she-yachaziru lo gedulato* — initially, Rav Akha still hoped that his prominence might be restored, perhaps after some years. He therefore said: it will not come back quickly. *U'keshera'ah shelo hecheziru, shuv amar l'olam ein metivin lo* — but when years passed and his position was not restored, he gave up hope entirely and said: it will never return. That is very sad.

One might also ask how much of this was truly *divrei nevuah* and how much was Mar bar Rav Ashi's own doing. The *shoteh*'s words were the spark; but Mar bar Rav Ashi was very much an active participant in making the prophecy come true. The *nevuah* opened a door; he walked through it with great deliberateness.

### Child Prophecy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּנוֹקֹת – מַאי הִיא? כִּי הָא דְּבַת רַב חִסְדָּא – הֲוָה יָתְבָה בְּכַנְפֵיהּ דַּאֲבוּהָ, הֲווֹ יָתְבִי קַמֵּיהּ רָבָא וְרָמֵי בַּר חָמָא. אֲמַר לַהּ: מַאן מִינַּיְיהוּ בָּעֵית? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: תַּרְוַיְיהוּ. אָמַר רָבָא: וַאֲנָא בָּתְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** And in **what** way was prophecy given **to children?** It was **like this** incident involving the **daughter of Rav Ḥisda,** who when she was a child **was sitting on her father's lap** while he sat and learned. **Rava and Rami bar Ḥama were sitting before him.** Rav Ḥisda jokingly **said to** his daughter: **Which of them would you want** as a husband? **She said:** I want **both of them. Rava said: And I** will be **last.** And this is what happened; first she married Rami bar Ḥama, and when he died she married Rava.

For the *tinokot*, the Gemara cites the daughter of Rav Chisda. She was sitting on her father's lap when both Rava and Rami bar Chama were seated before Rav Chisda learning. Rav Chisda turned to his young daughter and asked: which of these two would you like to marry? She answered: *tarvaihu* — both of them. Rava said: *v'ani batra* — I will be the second. Why would he want to be second? Because the first husband would die. And so it came to pass: she first married Rami bar Chama; after his death, she married Rava.

Here again, the story raises questions about the line between prophecy and the social circumstances that shape outcomes. Rav Chisda's daughter voiced a wish; Rava heard it and oriented his expectations accordingly. The *nevuah* — if it is to be called that — lay somewhere in the young girl's spontaneous expression, which she could not have consciously intended as a prediction.

## Hunger & Wine

### Two Hearts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבְדִּימִי דְּמִן חֵיפָה: קוֹדֶם שֶׁיֹּאכַל אָדָם וְיִשְׁתֶּה, יֵשׁ לוֹ שְׁתֵּי לְבָבוֹת; לְאַחַר שֶׁאוֹכֵל וְשׁוֹתֶה – אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא לֵב אֶחָד. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִישׁ נָבוּב יִלָּבֵב״ – וּכְתִיב: ״נְבוּב לֻחֹת״, וּמְתַרְגְּמִינַן: חֲלִיל לוּחִין.
>
> **Translation:** Having already cited one statement of Rabbi Avdimi from Haifa, the Gemara cites another statement in his name: **Rabbi Avdimi from Haifa says: Before a person eats and drinks he has two hearts,** meaning his heart is unsettled because he is distracted by hunger. But **after he eats and drinks he has only one heart, as it is stated: "A hollow [*nevuv*] man is two-hearted"** (Job 11:12). How is it indicated that "*nevuv*" means hungry? **As it is written** concerning the altar: **"*Nevuv luḥot*"** (Exodus 27:8), **which we translate** into Aramaic as: **Hollow with planks,** meaning that a hollow person, i.e., one who has not yet eaten, is two-hearted.

Rav Dimi of Haifa — having already been cited in connection with prophecy and the sages — is now quoted on an entirely different matter, though it is his name that occasions the transition. *Kodem she-yochal adam ve-yishteh, yesh lo shtei levavot* — before a person eats and drinks, he has two hearts. That is, a hungry person cannot focus: part of his mind is perpetually drawn toward his hunger, leaving him distracted. *Ve-achar she-ochal ve-shoteh, ein lo ela lev echad* — after eating and drinking, he has but one heart, capable of full concentration. The proof text: *ish navov yilavev* — a hollow person will have two hearts, with *navov* parsed as empty, hollow, and *yilavev* read as a doubled *lev*, two hearts. The word *navov* is connected to *nivuv luchot* — the hollow of the tablets — through the Targum's rendering *chalil luchin*, reinforcing the association between hollowness and the doubled heart.

### Wine Opens Heart

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: הָרָגִיל בְּיַיִן, אֲפִילּוּ לִבּוֹ אָטוּם כִּבְתוּלָה – יַיִן מְפַקְּחוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְתִירוֹשׁ יְנוֹבֵב בְּתֻלוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss the meaning of *nevuv*, **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, says:** With regard to **one who is accustomed to wine, although his heart,** i.e., his mind, **is closed like a virgin, wine opens it, as it is stated: "And new wine opens [*yenovev*] the virgins"** (Zechariah 9:17). The word *yenovev* is used here in the sense of clearing out a space: Even if one's heart and mind are closed, wine will open them to understanding.

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua adds: *ha-ragil be-yayin, afilu libbo atum ke-vetulah, yayin mefakcho* — one who is accustomed to wine: even if his heart is sealed up like a virgin, wine will open it. The proof text: *ve-tirosh yenovev betulot* — new wine will hollow out (or open up) virgins, with *yenovev* again echoing the *navov/nivuv* root, carrying the sense of opening, unlocking, making receptive.

## Bechor Division Rights

### Bechor Adjacent Portions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: פְּשִׁיטָא, חֵלֶק בְּכוֹר וְחֵלֶק פָּשׁוּט – יָהֲבִינַן לֵיהּ אַחַד מִצְרָא. יָבָם – מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara resumes its discussion of the division of property. **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, says:** It is **obvious** that if a person inherits **a portion** of his father's estate because he is the **firstborn, and** he also inherits **a portion** of that estate as **an ordinary** son, like the rest of his brothers, **he is given** his two portions **along one boundary,** so that they are adjacent to one another and form a single property. The Gemara asks: **What** is the *halakha* with regard to **a *yavam*,** a man whose brother died without children, who is obligated by Torah law to marry his deceased brother's widow or grant her *ḥalitza*? If he marries his brother's widow, the *halakha* dictates that he receive his brother's portion of their father's estate in addition to his own. Does he too receive the two portions along one boundary?

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua rules: *peshita, chelek bechor ve-chelek pashut yavinan leh achad metzra* — it is obvious that when dividing an ancestral estate, we give the *bechor* his double portion on one shared boundary. The *bechor* receives two portions in the inheritance — his standard portion as a son and the additional portion that is his right as firstborn — and he is entitled to have both portions adjacent to each other, forming one continuous tract of land rather than two separate plots.

### Yavam Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הִיא – הִיא; מַאי טַעְמָא? ״בְּכוֹר״ קַרְיֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא. רָבָא אָמַר, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהָיָה הַבְּכוֹר״ – הֲוָיָיתוֹ כִּבְכוֹר, וְאֵין חֲלוּקָּתוֹ כִּבְכוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: This** case **is** equal to **that** case. **What is the reason** for this? **The Merciful One calls** the *yavam* **"firstborn"** (see *Yevamot* 24a) and therefore he is treated like a firstborn in all regards. He receives the two portions of his father's estate as a single parcel of land. But **Rava said: The verse states: "And it shall be, the firstborn"** (Deuteronomy 25:6). With regard to **his being,** i.e., his inheritance itself, he **is like a firstborn; but** as for **the distribution** of the estate, he **is not like a firstborn,** and the brothers are not obligated to give him two adjacent portions.

The question then arises: what of a *yavam* — a man who performs *yibum* with his deceased brother's widow and thereby acquires his brother's share of the inheritance? He now holds two portions: his own original share in the estate and his brother's share that came to him through *yibum*. Can he demand that these two portions be given to him adjacent to each other, as a *bechor* would receive his two portions together?

Abaye rules: *hi hi* — it is the same case. The *yavam* is entitled to the same adjacency right as the *bechor*. His reasoning: the Torah itself calls the child of *yibum* a *bechor* — *ve-hayah ha-bechor asher teled yakum al shem achiv ha-met* — and from this the Gemara derives that the *yavam* who performs *yibum* is himself treated as a *bechor*. Just as the *bechor* receives his extra portion adjacent to his base portion, so too the *yavam* receives his brother's portion adjacent to his own.

Rava disagrees: *amar kra ve-hayah ha-bechor — havayah ke-bechor ve-ein chalukato ke-bechor* — the verse establishes that the *yavam* has the *identity* of a *bechor*, but does not extend to giving him the property-division rights of a *bechor*. The *yavam* is like a *bechor* in name; the entitlement to adjacent portions does not follow.

## Middat Sdom

### Adjacent Plot Request

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דִּזְבַן אַרְעָא אַמִּצְרָא דְּבֵי נְשֵׁיהּ. כִּי קָא פָּלְגוּ, אֲמַר לְהוּ: פְּלִיגוּ לִי אַמִּצְרַאי. אָמַר רַבָּה: כְּגוֹן זֶה – כּוֹפִין עַל מִדַּת סְדוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** It is reported that **a certain person bought land along the boundary of his father's property.** After some time the father died. **When they** came to **divide** the estate, this person **said to** his brothers: **Give me my portion** of the estate **along my boundary. Rabba said: In** a case **such as this,** the court **compels** people to refrain from **conduct** characteristic **of Sodom.** The court forces a person to waive his legal rights in order to prevent him from acting in a manner characteristic of the wicked city of Sodom. Since it makes no difference to the brothers which portion they receive since the parcels of land must be of equal value, whereas it matters to this brother that the area he receives should be adjacent to the land he already bought, the court forces the others to give this brother his portion along his boundary.

A further case: a man purchased a plot of land on the boundary of land belonging to his father-in-law's house (*bei nasha* — the household of the women, referring either to his wife's family or specifically to the family estate that will pass through the daughters). When the daughters come to divide the estate, the son-in-law — who stands to inherit his wife's portion — asks that his wife's share be allocated adjacent to the land he himself owns, so that his holdings form one continuous parcel.

### Sdom Accommodation

Rava rules: *kigon zeh, kofin al middat Sdom* — in a case like this, one compels accommodation under the principle of *middat Sdom*. *Middat Sdom* is the posture of *sheli sheli ve-shelcha shelcha* — what is mine is mine and what is yours is yours — an unwillingness to do anything for another even at no cost to oneself. *Kofin al middat Sdom* means that where the accommodation costs the other party nothing — where the plots are of equal value and any arrangement is equivalent — the party who would benefit can compel the other to comply. Since all portions are equivalent, there is no loss to the co-heirs in allocating the wife's portion adjacent to the son-in-law's land, and they cannot simply refuse out of indifference.

### Premium for Service

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: אָמְרִי לֵיהּ אֲחֵי, מְעַלִּינַן לֵיהּ עִלּוּיָא כִּי נִכְסֵי דְּבֵי בַּר מָרִיּוֹן! וְהִלְכְתָא כְּרַב יוֹסֵף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef objects to this,** saying this is not a case involving conduct characteristic of Sodom, since the brothers can explain their refusal to grant the request. **The brothers** can **say to him: We assess this** field that you want for yourself **as** particularly **valuable, like the property of the house of bar Maryon.** The brothers can claim that the portion he wants is more desirable than the others, and for that reason they do not want to give it to him. The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Yosef,** and the brothers can refuse the request.

Rav Yosef objects: the co-heirs — the sisters or other siblings — can say to the son-in-law: *maalinen leh illuya ke-nichsei de-bar Maryon* — we will raise the price on you as if it were the property of Bar Maryon, a byword for enormous wealth. That is, we will charge you a premium for the accommodation. By demanding an adjacent plot, you are effectively asking us to forgo the opportunity to extract payment from you for that convenience. We are not obligated to simply provide it gratis. The fact that it does not cost us anything in terms of the land's value does not mean we have no right to charge for the preference.

The Gemara rules: *hilchasa ke-Rav Yosef* — the halakha follows Rav Yosef. The discussion of how this principle applies in further cases — including scenarios involving two parcels on two boundaries — is left to the following daf.
