# Sotah 41 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## High Priest's Blessing

### High Priest Intro

The *mishna* on 40b opens a new topic: having concluded the extended discussion of *birchas kohanim*, the *mishna* turns to other rituals that must be performed specifically in Hebrew. The first of these is *birchas kohen gadol* — and by introducing it this way, the *mishna* uses the occasion to explore how these ceremonies were actually conducted. Some of the rituals described in this section of *Sotah* are primarily historical in character, like the *brachos u'klallos*; others, like *birchas kohanim*, remain practically operative. *Birchas kohen gadol* falls somewhere in between — it was fully operative when the *beis hamikdash* stood, and the *mishna* treats it as an opportunity to reconstruct what it looked like.

The context is *yom hakippurim*. After all the *korbanos* have been brought and the *sa'ir* has been sent *l'azazel*, the *kohen gadol* and the assembly conduct a *keriat hatorah* in the *azarah*. This occurs at the conclusion of the *avodas hayom*, when the bulk of the unique service has been completed. It is worth noting that the *mishna* in *Yoma* records an interesting variant: the reading could be performed either in the white *bigdei kehuna* of *yom kippur* or in a special cloak prepared for this service — a detail that raises its own questions about how fully this reading was incorporated into the structure of the *avoda* proper.

### Torah Passing Ritual

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בִּרְכוֹת כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל כֵּיצַד? חַזַּן הַכְּנֶסֶת נוֹטֵל סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה וְנוֹתְנָהּ לוֹ לְרֹאשׁ הַכְּנֶסֶת, וְרֹאשׁ הַכְּנֶסֶת נוֹתְנָהּ לַסְּגָן, וְהַסְּגָן נוֹתְנָהּ לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל,
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **How** are **the blessings of the High Priest** recited on Yom Kippur? **The synagogue attendant takes a Torah scroll and gives it to the head of the synagogue** that stands on the Temple Mount, **and the head of the synagogue gives it to the deputy** High Priest, **and the deputy** High Priest **gives it to the High Priest.**

The *mishna* describes the ceremony of transferring the *sefer torah* in detail. The *chazzan haknesset* — the attendant of the synagogue who handles all the practical affairs of the *knesset*, whom *Rashi* describes as the *shammash* who bears responsibility for all the workings of the synagogue, bringing things in and out, preparing everything — takes the *sefer torah* and gives it to the *rosh haknesset*. The *rosh haknesset* is the one who makes the decisions: who reads the *haftarah*, who leads the prayers. *Rashi* captures this distinction sharply: *al piv nechtachin divrei haknesset* — on his word all matters of the *knesset* are decided. The roles correspond, roughly, to what we today combine in the *gabbai*. The *rosh haknesset* then passes the *sefer torah* to the *segan*, the deputy *kohen gadol*, who in turn gives it to the *kohen gadol* himself.

One might pause here to ask: which *knesset* is this? Are we speaking of a synagogue on *har habayit*? Was there some formal communal institution in *yerushalayim* that the *chazzan haknesset* represented? This is genuinely unclear. What is evident is that the ritual is synagogue-like in character — a ceremonial reading of the *torah* — yet the precise identity of the *knesset* in question remains uncertain.

### Reading Sections

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל עוֹמֵד וּמְקַבֵּל, וְקוֹרֵא ״אַחֲרֵי מוֹת״, וְ״אַךְ בֶּעָשׂוֹר״. וְגוֹלֵל אֶת הַתּוֹרָה וּמַנִּיחָהּ בְּחֵיקוֹ, וְאוֹמֵר: יָתֵר מִמַּה שֶּׁקָּרִיתִי לִפְנֵיכֶם כָּתוּב כָּאן. ״וּבֶעָשׂוֹר״ שֶׁבְּחוֹמֶשׁ הַפְּקוּדִים קוֹרֵא עַל פֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And the High Priest stands; and receives** the Torah scroll; **and reads** the Torah portion beginning with the verse: **"After the death"** (Leviticus 16:1–34), **and** the portion beginning with the verse: **"But on the tenth"** (Leviticus 23:26–32); **and furls the Torah** scroll; **and places it on his bosom; and says: More than what I have read before you is written here. He** then **reads by heart** the portion beginning with: **"And on the tenth," from the book of Numbers** (see 29:7–11).

Once the *sefer torah* reaches him, the *kohen gadol* stands and receives it, and reads from *acharei mos* — the *avodas yom hakippurim* — and from *ach ba'asor*, the section in *Emor* in *Vayikra* describing the *musafin* of *yom kippur*, drawn from the cycle of *musvei yom tov*. After reading these sections, he rolls the *sefer torah* closed, holds it against his chest, and declares: *yoser mi-mah she-karasi lifneichem kasuv kan* — more than what I have read before you is written here. He is signaling that there is an additional section he will recite, but which he will not read from the scroll. That additional section — *u'va'asur sheb'chumash hapekudim* — is the corresponding passage in *Pinchas* in *Bamidbar*, which also describes the *musaf hayom* of *yom kippur*. He recites this second passage by heart, *al peh*, rather than reading it inside the scroll.

The reason he does not simply scroll to *Pinchas* will be addressed by the *gemara*, but the statement itself requires explanation. Why does he feel compelled to announce that more is written here than he read? Because he then proceeds to recite something without reading from the *sefer torah*, one might suspect that the text he is reciting is simply not found in it. To dispel any suspicion that there is some deficiency in the *sefer torah*, he preemptively declares that everything is indeed written — he is simply not reading it from inside. This logic, as we will see, connects directly to the broader *halachic* principle of *tircha d'tzibura*.

After completing both readings, the *kohen gadol* recites eight *brachos*. The *mishna* lists them: on the *torah*, on the *avoda*, on the *hoda'ah* (thanksgiving), on *mechilas avon* (forgiveness of sin), on the *mikdash*, on *yisrael*, on the *kohanim*, and on *yerushalayim* — with a final element described as *v'hashar tefillah*, a general supplication.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְבָרֵךְ עָלֶיהָ שְׁמֹנֶה בְּרָכוֹת: עַל הַתּוֹרָה, וְעַל הָעֲבוֹדָה, וְעַל הַהוֹדָיָיה, וְעַל מְחִילַת הֶעָוֹן, וְעַל הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, וְעַל יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְעַל הַכֹּהֲנִים, וְעַל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם, וְהַשְּׁאָר תְּפִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** after the reading the High Priest **recites** the following **eight blessings:** A blessing **concerning the Torah, and concerning the** Temple **service, and concerning thanksgiving, and concerning forgiveness for iniquity, and concerning the Temple, and concerning the Jewish people, and concerning the priests, and concerning Jerusalem, and the rest of the prayer.**

What is striking about these *brachos* is that they are not merely *birchos hatorah* in the ordinary sense. Rather, the completion of the *keriat hatorah* is being used as a framework for reciting *brachos* over the entirety of the *avoda* that has just culminated. The *al ha'avoda* is, according to *Rashi*, the *retzei* — the prayer asking God to accept the service — though *Rashi* notes in *Yoma* that the concluding formula cannot be the *hamachazir shechinaso l'tziyon* of our *shemoneh esrei*, since that does not fit the context of an *avoda* just completed. Instead, it may correspond to the *retzei* found in the context of *birchas kohanim*: *she'otcha levadcha b'yirah na'avod*, which carries precisely the sense that the *avoda* is being performed right now. *Rashi* adds the principle: *achar zevichah hoda'ah* — after the sacrifice one gives thanks — which is why the *al ha'avoda* leads naturally into the *al ha'hoda'ah*, corresponding to *modim* of the *shemoneh esrei*.

The structure thus mirrors the *shemoneh esrei* in a loose way: *retzei*, *modim*, and then the hope that the *avoda* will effect *mechilas avon* — corresponding to the *atah bechartanu* type of blessing in which God is described as *melech mochel v'solei'ach la'avonot*. This is followed by *birchos* on the *mikdash*, on *yisrael*, and so on. *Rashi*'s formulations for some of these are suggestive: *baruch asher bachar baMikdash*, *baruch habocher b'amo Yisrael*. The shape of the *davening* is recognizable, even though one is entering it, so to speak, from the middle. What is notable is that this is not fundamentally about the *keriat hatorah* at all — it is using the reading of the *torah* as the occasion to offer a comprehensive liturgical response to everything that has just happened on *yom kippur*.

## Honor Display

### Tosafot's Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ חוֹלְקִין כָּבוֹד לְתַלְמִיד בִּמְקוֹם הָרַב.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara suggests: **You can learn from** the fact that the head of the synagogue and the deputy High Priest receive the Torah scroll before the High Priest that **honor may be given to a student in the presence of the teacher.** Although the High Priest is considered everyone's teacher and master, honor was nevertheless extended to other individuals in his presence without fear of impugning the High Priest's honor.

The *gemara* draws an inference from the chain of transmission: since the *sefer torah* passed from *chazzan* to *rosh haknesset* to *segan* before reaching the *kohen gadol*, one might conclude that we practice *cholkin kavod l'talmid bimkom harav* — we honor the lesser figure even in the presence of the greater one. But this reading, while initially plausible, does not survive scrutiny.

*Tosafot* sharpens the problem considerably. The obvious inference — that all these intermediate stages are simply meant to heighten the honor of the *kohen gadol* by approaching him through multiple steps — is exactly right. The question is more specific: granted that you want to demonstrate the *kohen gadol*'s elevated status by passing the *sefer torah* upward through the hierarchy, why was it necessary to include the *rosh haknesset* at all? Why not go from the *chazzan* directly to the *segan*? Skipping the *rosh haknesset* would still produce an ascending chain — *chazzan* to *segan* to *kohen gadol* — which would equally serve to highlight how high the *kohen gadol* stands. The *rosh haknesset* is, after all, considerably lower in the hierarchy than the *segan* or the *kohen gadol*. Who is slighted by bypassing him?

*Tosafot* answers its own question: even the *segan* would be slightly dishonored if the *sefer torah* arrived in his hands having come directly from someone two levels below him rather than from the person immediately beneath him in the hierarchy. But *Tosafot* then presses further — and this is where the genuine insight lies — why should we care? If the entire enterprise is about the honor of the *kohen gadol*, let the *segan* be slighted. What is wrong with going from *chazzan* to *segan*, bypassing the *rosh haknesset*, and then from *segan* to *kohen gadol*?

### Abaye's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כּוּלַּהּ מִשּׁוּם כְּבוֹדוֹ דְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** A proof may not be adduced from here. Usually one does not show honor for a student in the presence of the teacher, but here the **entire** process **is for the honor of the High Priest.** The passing of the Torah scroll to people of increasing importance demonstrates that the High Priest is considered the most important of all those present.

Abaye's answer is: *kulo mishum kevodo d'kohen gadol* — the entire protocol, including every intermediate stage, is entirely for the sake of the *kohen gadol*'s honor. This is not a concession to the lesser figures in the middle. Rather, having every step proceed in careful, orderly sequence — one rung at a time — itself amplifies the honor of the person at the top. To bypass any intermediate figure, even if it would only slightly reduce the honor of those bypassed, would diminish the cumulative effect of the ascent. Every stage of the ceremony increases the *kavod* of the *kohen gadol*, not merely the final transfer.

This reading represents a significant refinement over the simpler understanding. *Rashi* might have read the chain as a natural courtesy to the intermediate figures. *Tosafot*, and through them Abaye's principle, establishes something more precise: the staged ascent is not a balance between honoring multiple parties but a single coherent act of honoring one supreme authority. Even a subtle disruption to the continuity of that ascent — even bypassing the *rosh haknesset* — would undermine the message that the entire ceremony is designed to convey.

## Torah vs Haftarah

### Jump Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקוֹרֵא ״אַחֲרֵי מוֹת״ וְ״אַךְ בֶּעָשׂוֹר״. ורְמִינְהִי: מְדַלְּגִין בְּנָבִיא וְאֵין מְדַלְּגִין בַּתּוֹרָה!
>
> **Translation:** § It is taught in the mishna that the High Priest receives the Torah scroll **and reads** the Torah portion beginning with the verse: **"After the death"** (Leviticus 16:1), **and** the portion beginning with the verse: **"But on the tenth"** (Leviticus 23:26). Since these two portions are not adjacent to each other, the High Priest skips the section between the two portions. The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a mishna (*Megilla* 24a): **One may skip** sections when reading the *haftara* **in the Prophets, but one may not skip** sections when reading **in the Torah.**

After establishing that the *kohen gadol* reads from *acharei mos* and then from *ach ba'asor*, the *gemara* raises a contradiction. There is a principle that one may jump (*medalgin*) in the *navi* — that is, when reading the *haftarah* one may skip from one passage to another — but one may not jump in the *torah*. Yet here the *kohen gadol* is apparently jumping from one section of the *torah* to another. How is this permitted?

### Translator Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — בִּכְדֵי שֶׁיִּפְסוֹק הַתּוּרְגְּמָן, כָּאן — בִּכְדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יִפְסוֹק הַתּוּרְגְּמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** This is **not difficult. There,** in the mishna in tractate *Megilla* that teaches that one may not skip, the intention is that one should not skip if the sections are so far apart from one another that the delay caused by doing so will be **of such length that the translator** who recites the Aramaic translation **will conclude** his translation before the next section is reached. However, in the case of the mishna **here,** it is permitted to skip verses because the two passages are in close proximity to one another. The delay caused is **of such** short **length that the translator will** still **not conclude** his translation.

Abaye offers a first resolution: *lo kashya* — there is no contradiction. The distinction depends on whether the skip can be completed before the *meturgeman* — the translator — finishes rendering the preceding passage. If the reader can reach the next *pasuk* before the translator concludes, there is no pause, no moment of waiting, and the jump is permissible. If not, there is a pause, and that is *tircha d'tzibura*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא עֲלַהּ קָתָנֵי: מְדַלְּגִין בְּנָבִיא וְאֵין מְדַלְּגִין בַּתּוֹרָה. וְעַד כַּמָּה מְדַלְּגִין — עַד כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יִפְסוֹק הַתּוּרְגְּמָן. מִכְלָל דְּבַתּוֹרָה כְּלָל כְּלָל לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this resolution: **But isn't it taught about** this mishna in a *baraita*: **One may skip** sections when reading **in the Prophets, but one may not skip** sections when reading **in the Torah. And how much may one skip** from one passage to another **in the Prophets?** One may skip when the section skipped is **of such** short **length that** when the furling of the scroll is completed **the translator will** still **not have concluded** his translation. **By inference,** when reading **in the Torah** one may **not** skip **at all.**

But the *gemara* then cites a *braita* that complicates this resolution. The *braita* teaches: one may jump in *navi*, one may not jump in *torah* — and then specifies that even in *navi*, jumping is permitted only up to the point where the translator has not yet finished. This implies that even in *navi*, you cannot create a pause. But the *braita* is also saying that in *torah*, even if you can reach the next *pasuk* before the translator finishes, you still cannot jump. Timing alone, then, cannot be the full criterion. So Abaye offers a second, more refined resolution.

### Theme Continuity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּעִנְיָן אֶחָד, כָּאן בִּשְׁנֵי עִנְיָינִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said** another explanation: This is **not difficult.** In the mishna **here,** it is permitted to skip because both passages pertain **to a single topic. There,** in the mishna in tractate *Megilla* that teaches that one may not skip, the *halakha* is referring to a case where the passages pertain **to two** different **topics.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: מְדַלְּגִין בַּתּוֹרָה בְּעִנְיָן אֶחָד, וּבְנָבִיא בִּשְׁנֵי עִנְיָינִין. וְכָאן וְכָאן בִּכְדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יִפְסוֹק הַתּוּרְגְּמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One may skip** sections when reading **in the Torah** when both sections pertain **to a single topic, and in the Prophets** one may skip from one section to another even if they pertain **to two** different **topics. And** both **here and there,** one may skip only **when** the section skipped is **of such** short **length that** when the furling of the scroll is completed **the translator will** still **not have concluded** his translation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מְדַלְּגִין מִנָּבִיא לְנָבִיא. וּבְנָבִיא שֶׁל שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר מְדַלְּגִין, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יְדַלֵּג מִסּוֹף הַסֵּפֶר לִתְחִילָּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And one may not skip from** one book of the **Prophets to** another book of the **Prophets,** even if both pertain to the same topic. **But among** the books **of the Twelve Prophets one may skip, provided that one does not skip from the end of the book to its beginning.** Rather, if one wishes to read from several of the Twelve Prophets, he must read the passages in the order that they are written.

*Lo kashya* — there is no difficulty. The real distinction is between jumping within a single theme (*inyan echad*) and jumping between different themes (*shnei inyanim*). In the *torah*: you may jump if you are staying within the same subject matter and can complete the jump before the translator finishes. In the *navi*: you may jump even between different themes, but still only if you can do so before the translator finishes. And within the *navi*, you may not jump from one *sefer* of *navi* to another — with the exception of *trei asar*, the twelve minor prophets, which form a single *sefer*. Even there, however, one may not go backwards within the *sefer*, as that would create confusion for the listeners.

### Listener Focus

The *gemara* does not explicitly state in the Sefaria text the discussion of why you cannot jump between themes in the *torah*. This represents pure analysis of the previously cited passages. *Rashi* provides a beautifully precise explanation for why you cannot jump between themes in the *torah* even with adequate timing. When a person's mind is engaged with one subject, it does not immediately disengage and redirect itself toward a new theme. The listener cannot easily switch attention. In the context of reading the *torah* — where the purpose is not merely ritual but genuine instruction in the *mitzvos* — this matters enormously. *Rashi* puts it sharply: *she'ein halev shehu nimshach achar inyan echad mmaher latzet mimenu lishmoa lehavin divrei inyan echad* — a heart drawn along one line of thought does not quickly exit it to absorb a different subject.

And the stakes are high. The *torah* is the source of the *mitzvos* that people are obligated to perform. Reading the *torah* publicly is an opportunity for people to hear and internalize what is required of them. If a listener is shifting between topics and cannot follow properly, a *pasuk* goes ununderstood, it passes over him — *over alav v'eino mekayma* — and he will not fulfill it. In this light, it is better not to read the passage at all than to read it in a way that guarantees the listener will not absorb it. Switching themes mid-reading ensures exactly that failure.

The *navi*, by contrast, does not carry the same weight of obligatory practice. Missing a nuance or losing the thread in the *haftarah* does not produce the same halakhic consequences. Hence the asymmetry: in *torah*, thematic continuity is a precondition; in *navi*, it is not required.

## High Priest's Blessing…

### No Scrolling Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְגוֹלֵל אֶת הַתּוֹרָה וּמַנִּיחָהּ בְּחֵיקוֹ וְכׇל כָּךְ לָמָּה — שֶׁלֹּא לְהוֹצִיא לַעַז עַל סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה. ״וּבֶעָשׂוֹר שֶׁבְּחוֹמֶשׁ הַפְּקוּדִים קוֹרֵא עַל פֶּה״. וְלִיכְרְכֵיהּ לְסֵפֶר וְלִיקְרֵי! אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין גּוֹלְלִין סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה בַּצִּבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § It is taught in the mishna: **And he furls the Torah** scroll, **and places it on his bosom,** and says: More than what I have read before you is written here. The Gemara comments: **And why** must he say **all of this?** It is **so as to not cast aspersions on** the **Torah scroll,** because people might think the portion that he read by heart is not written there. It is stated in the mishna that **he reads by heart** the portion beginning with: **"And on the tenth," from the book of Numbers** (29:7–11). The Gemara asks: **But let him furl the** Torah **scroll** to that portion **and read** it from the text. **Rav Huna bar Yehuda says** that **Rav Sheshet says:** This is not done **because one may not furl a Torah scroll in public,** out of respect for the congregation.

Having established the framework for jumping in the *torah*, the *gemara* returns to the earlier question: why does the *kohen gadol* recite the passage from *pinchas* by heart rather than reading it inside? The *gemara* phrases the question with apparent surprise — *velirkacha lesifra vlikrei*, why not just scroll and read? This is puzzling because the issue seems already to have been addressed. *Rashi* in fact explains that what was happening is that while the *kohen gadol* was scrolling from *acharei mos* toward the next section, the *meturgeman* was translating the last *pasuk* — they were working simultaneously so there would be no perceived pause. It is *pasuk* by *pasuk*, not section by section, that the translation proceeds, and the scrolling and translating overlap.

But a further question is articulated explicitly: *v'ha'amar Rav Huna bar Yehuda amar Rav Sheshet* — because *ein golin sefer torah b'tzibur*, one does not scroll the *sefer torah* before the public. The act of visibly scrolling while the congregation sits and waits is itself a form of *tircha d'tzibura*, not merely the passage of time but the spectacle of delay. Even if time is being used productively — even if the translator is speaking — the visual fact of a pause in the reading, with the scroll being moved, is unseemly. It is not the absolute quantity of time that constitutes *tircha* but rather the felt sense of waiting and hesitation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלַיְתֵי סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה אַחֲרִינָא וְלִיקְרֵי! רַב הוּנָא בַּר יְהוּדָה אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם פְּגָמוֹ שֶׁל רִאשׁוֹן. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין מְבָרְכִין בְּרָכָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **let them bring another Torah scroll** that has previously been furled to that portion **and read** from it? **Rav Huna bar Yehuda says:** People might then mistakenly think that the second scroll was brought **due to a flaw** that was found **in** the **first** scroll. **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says:** Another scroll is not brought **because** then the High Priest will need to recite an additional blessing over it, and **one may not recite a blessing that is unnecessary.**

One might then ask: why not simply bring a second *sefer torah* opened to *pinchas*? *Rav Huna bar Yehuda* addresses this as well: *mishum pigmo shel rishon* — because it raises the appearance of a defect in the first *sefer torah*. If the *kohen gadol* sets aside one scroll and takes up another in the middle of his reading, the onlookers will wonder why. There must be something wrong with the first one. *Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish* adds an additional reason: bringing a second *sefer torah* within the same *aliyah* would necessitate an additional and superfluous *bracha* — *bracha she'eina tzricha*. The *bracha* attaches to the *cheftza*, the *sefer torah* itself, and to bring a second one in the midst of the same reading implies an additional *bracha* over it, which is unnecessary and therefore improper.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי חָיְשִׁינַן לִפְגָמָא? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא: רֹאשׁ חֹדֶשׁ טֵבֵת שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בְּשַׁבָּת מֵבִיא שָׁלֹשׁ תּוֹרוֹת וְקוֹרֵא: אַחַת מֵעִנְיָינוֹ שֶׁל יוֹם, וְאַחַת שֶׁל רֹאשׁ חֹדֶשׁ, וְאַחַת בְּשֶׁל חֲנוּכָּה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions Rav Huna bar Yehuda's answer: **But are we** really **concerned that** people will think that there is **a flaw** in the first scroll? **But didn't Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa say:** When **the New Moon of Tevet,** which always occurs during Hanukkah, **occurs on Shabbat, one brings three Torah** scrolls. **And he reads from one** scroll **the topic of the day,** i.e., the weekly portion; **and** from **one** scroll the portion **of the New Moon; and** from **one** scroll the portion **of Hanukkah.** It is apparent from the statement of Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa that many Torah scrolls may be used, and there is no concern that people will mistakenly think that one or more has a flaw.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּלָתָא גַּבְרֵי בִּתְלָתָא סִיפְרֵי — לֵיכָּא פְּגָמָא, חַד גַּבְרָא בִּתְרֵי סִיפְרֵי — אִיכָּא פְּגָמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: When **three men** read **from three scrolls, there is no** concern that people will think that one of the scrolls has **a flaw,** since people assume that it is befitting for each individual to read from his own scroll. However, when **one man** reads **from two** different Torah **scrolls, there is** a concern that people will think that the first scroll has **a flaw,** and they will not realize that this was done only to avoid forcing the community to wait while the scroll is furled.

The *gemara* then asks whether this concern about *pigmo shel rishon* is really coherent, since we know from *Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha* that on a *shabbat* that coincides with *rosh chodesh* and *chanukah*, three *sifrei torah* are brought out simultaneously. How is that not *pigmo*? The answer: *tlat gavrei b'tlat sifei leika pgama* — when three different people read from three different scrolls, there is no implication that any one scroll is defective, since each person has his own scroll for his own *aliyah*. But *chad gavra b'trei sifei ika pgama* — when the same person, in the same *aliyah*, sets aside one scroll and picks up another, the contrast is inescapable. Why was the first one abandoned? That generates the suspicion of a defect. The solution, then, is to recite the passage from *pinchas* entirely from memory, with the statement *yoser mi-mah she-karasi kasuv kan* serving to assure the congregation that the omission of that passage from the reading was a matter of protocol, not of deficiency.

### Eight Blessings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְבָרֵךְ עָלֶיהָ שְׁמֹנֶה בְּרָכוֹת כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: [מְבָרְכִין] עַל הַתּוֹרָה כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁמְּבָרְכִין בְּבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת, וְעַל הָעֲבוֹדָה, וְעַל הַהוֹדָאָה, וְעַל מְחִילַת עָוֹן — כְּתִיקְנָן. עַל הַמִּקְדָּשׁ בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ, וְעַל הַכֹּהֲנִים בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן, עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן, וְעַל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § It is taught in the mishna: **And** after the reading the High Priest **recites eight blessings. The Sages taught** that these are the eight blessings: **He recites a blessing concerning the Torah in the** usual **way one recites** a blessing in **the synagogue, and** he recites the three blessings **concerning the** Temple **service, and concerning thanksgiving, and concerning forgiveness for iniquity,** and all are recited **in accordance with their established forms** in the *Amida* prayer. He recites the blessing **concerning the Temple in and of itself,** the blessing **concerning the priests in and of itself,** the blessing **concerning the Jewish people in and of itself, and** the blessing **concerning Jerusalem in and of itself.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְּׁאָר תְּפִלָּה. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַשְּׁאָר תְּפִלָּה תְּחִנָּה רִנָּה וּבַקָּשָׁה שֶׁעַמְּךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל צְרִיכִין לִיוּוֹשֵׁעַ. וְחוֹתֵם בְּשׁוֹמֵעַ תְּפִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the end of the mishna, which states: **And the rest of the prayer, the Sages taught: The** text of **the rest of the prayer** is as follows: **A supplication, a song, and a request that Your people, Israel, are in need of redemption. And he concludes** the blessing **with:** Blessed are You, Lord, **the One Who hears prayer.**

The *mishna* lists eight *brachos* recited by the *kohen gadol* following the reading. A *braita* elaborates: the *bracha al hatorah* follows the form used in the synagogue — *asher natan lanu torat emet*. The *brachos al ha'avoda*, *al ha'hoda'ah*, and *al mechilas ha'avon* follow their established formulations familiar from the *shemoneh esrei*. Then come separate *brachos* for the *mikdash*, for *yisrael*, for the *kohanim*, and for *yerushalayim*, each as a discrete *bracha* unto itself. The *braita* concludes: *v'hashar tefillah* — a final petition, described as *techina*, *rina*, and *bakasha*, for whatever *yisrael* needs, concluding with the *chatimah* of *shomea tefillah*.

The count raises a question. If each of the seven named categories constitutes one *bracha*, and *v'hashar tefillah* is an eighth, the tally reaches eight. But the *braita*'s description of that final element as a *bracha* containing *techina*, *rina*, and *bakasha* does not clearly resolve whether it is to be counted separately or understood as subsumed within one of the seven. *Rashi*'s explanation here does not fully clarify the matter, and a parallel difficulty arises from noting that some textual traditions omit the *bracha* for *yerushalayim*, apparently following a *girsa* in *Yoma* and the *Yerushalmi* that does not list it, while *Rashi* does mention *yerushalayim* but omits the *kohanim* as a separate category.

What is clear in any case is that the *brachos* as a whole are not oriented toward the *torah* reading alone. They are a comprehensive liturgical response to the entire *yom kippur* experience: acknowledging the *avoda* that has just been performed, giving thanks for it, praying that it has effected atonement, and invoking God's connection to His *mikdash*, His people, His city. The *keriat hatorah* provides the occasion; the *brachos* are the substance of what the occasion demands. There is something deeply interesting about how this draws the whole congregation into participation in what was otherwise entirely the *kohen gadol*'s private service.

## King's Reading

### Hakhel Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ פָּרָשַׁת הַמֶּלֶךְ כֵּיצַד? מוֹצָאֵי יוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁל חַג, בַּשְּׁמִינִי בְּמוֹצָאֵי שְׁבִיעִית, עוֹשִׂין לוֹ בִּימָה שֶׁל עֵץ בָּעֲזָרָה וְהוּא יוֹשֵׁב עָלֶיהָ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִקֵּץ שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים בְּמֹעֵד וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **How** is **the portion of** the Torah that is read by **the king** recited at the assembly, when all the Jewish people would assemble? At **the conclusion of the first day of the festival** of *Sukkot*, **on the eighth,** after **the conclusion of the Sabbatical Year, they make a wooden platform for** the king **in the** Temple **courtyard, and he sits on it, as it is stated: "At the end of every seven years, in the Festival** of the Sabbatical Year" (Deuteronomy 31:10).

The next *mishna* introduces *parshas hamelech* — the reading of *hakhel*. The *hakhel* ceremony, described in *devarim* as occurring at the conclusion of the *shmita* year, gathers all of *klal yisrael* for a public reading of the *torah*. The *torah* itself says *tikra es hatorah hazos* without specifying that it is the king who reads, yet the *mishna* takes for granted that the king performs the reading. The ceremony takes place *b'motzei yom tov harishon shel chag* — the *gemara* will shortly clarify that this means not the second day of *succos* but rather the eighth year, *bishminis*, following the *shmita*.

### King's Platform

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַזַּן הַכְּנֶסֶת נוֹטֵל סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה וְנוֹתְנָהּ לְרֹאשׁ הַכְּנֶסֶת, וְרֹאשׁ הַכְּנֶסֶת נוֹתְנָהּ לַסְּגָן, וְהַסְּגָן נוֹתְנָהּ לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, וְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל נוֹתְנָהּ לַמֶּלֶךְ. וְהַמֶּלֶךְ עוֹמֵד וּמְקַבֵּל, וְקוֹרֵא יוֹשֵׁב.
>
> **Translation:** **The synagogue attendant takes a Torah scroll and gives it to the head of the synagogue** that stands on the Temple Mount. **And the head of the synagogue gives it to the deputy** High Priest, **and the deputy** High Priest **gives it to the High Priest, and the High priest gives it to the king. And the king stands, and receives** the Torah scroll, **and reads** from it while **sitting.**

A large wooden platform — *bima shel etz* — is constructed in the *azarah*. As with the *kohen gadol*'s reading, the *gemara* will confirm that *azarah* here means the *ezrat nashim*, which is the only space large enough to accommodate the full assembly of *klal yisrael*. The king sits upon this platform. The significance of this detail will become apparent shortly, since kings who are not of *malchei beit david* are not permitted to sit in the *azarah* proper; the *ezrat nashim* resolves this difficulty.

The chain of transmission of the *sefer torah* is identical to what was described for the *kohen gadol*: from *chazzan haknesset* to *rosh haknesset* to *segan* to *kohen gadol*, and then from the *kohen gadol* to the king. The king stands to receive the scroll and then sits to read it — *v'hamelech omed u'mekabel, v'korei yoshev* — which is the prerogative of *malchei beit david* in the *azarah*.

### Agrippas's Stand

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַגְרִיפַּס הַמֶּלֶךְ עָמַד וְקִבֵּל וְקָרָא עוֹמֵד, וְשִׁבְּחוּהוּ חֲכָמִים. וּכְשֶׁהִגִּיעַ לְ״לֹא תוּכַל לָתֵת עָלֶיךָ אִישׁ נׇכְרִי״, זָלְגוּ עֵינָיו דְּמָעוֹת. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אַל תִּתְיָרֵא אַגְרִיפַּס, אָחִינוּ אָתָּה, אָחִינוּ אָתָּה!
>
> **Translation:** **King Agrippa arose, and received** the Torah scroll, **and read** from it while **standing, and the Sages praised him** for this. **And when** Agrippa **arrived at** the verse in the portion read by the king that states: **"You may not appoint a foreigner over you"** (Deuteronomy 17:15), **tears flowed from his eyes,** because he was a descendant of the house of Herod and was not of Jewish origin. The entire nation **said to him: Fear not, Agrippa. You are our brother, you are our brother.**

Agrippas the king — the last king of *yisrael* before the *churban*, a descendant of Herod — stood both to receive the *sefer torah* and to read from it, declining to exercise his privilege of sitting while reading. The sages praised him for this. He chose to show honor to the *torah* by standing, setting aside his royal prerogative.

But when Agrippas reached the passage *lo suchal laseis alecha ish nochri* — you may not set a foreigner over you — his eyes filled with tears. He recognized that this *pasuk* disqualified him: he was descended from Herod, who was of servile and non-Jewish origin, and he therefore could not claim fully Jewish lineage on his father's side. The assembled people called out to him: *al tisra Agrippas, achinu atah, achinu atah* — do not fear, Agrippa, you are our brother. *Rashi* explains that they said this because his *mother* was Jewish — *imo mi'yisrael* — which they held sufficient to regard him as one of them.

### King's Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקוֹרֵא מִתְּחִילַּת ״אֵלֶּה הַדְּבָרִים״, עַד ״שְׁמַע״, וּ״שְׁמַע״, ״וְהָיָה אִם שָׁמֹעַ״, ״עַשֵּׂר תְּעַשֵּׂר״, ״כִּי תְכַלֶּה לַעְשֵׂר״, וּפָרָשַׁת הַמֶּלֶךְ, וּבְרָכוֹת וּקְלָלוֹת, עַד שֶׁגּוֹמֵר כׇּל הַפָּרָשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the king **reads from the beginning of** Deuteronomy, from the verse that states: **"And these are the words"** (Deuteronomy 1:1), **until** the words: **"Hear,** O Israel" (Deuteronomy 6:4). **And** he then reads the sections beginning with: **"Hear,** O Israel" (Deuteronomy 6:4–9), **"And it shall come to pass, if you shall hearken"** (Deuteronomy 11:13–21), **"You shall tithe"** (Deuteronomy 14:22–29), **"When you have made an end of the tithing"** (Deuteronomy 26:12–15), **and the passage concerning the** appointment of **a king** (Deuteronomy 17:14–20), **and the blessings and curses** (Deuteronomy 28), **until he finishes the entire portion.**

The sections read at *hakhel* are drawn from *sefer devarim*. The king begins from the opening of *devarim* — understood as *mishneh hatorah hazos* — reading through to *shema*. He then reads the *shema* itself, which *Rashi* identifies as the declaration of *kabalat malchus shamayim*. He continues with *v'haya im shamoa*, *Rashi* noting that this constitutes *kabalat ol mitzvos* — again, our familiar structure from *keriat shema*. He then reads *aser t'aser* and *ki tichaleh l'aser*, passages concerning *ma'aser* and the gifts to the *aniyim*, because this is the time of *zman ha'asif* — the harvest season of *succos* — and these obligations are particularly timely. *Rashi* explains that the passage *parshat hamelech* intervenes between *aser t'aser* and *ki tichaleh*, but it was read together with the surrounding material so as not to break up the *ma'aser* reading, with *parshat hamelech* read immediately afterward. Finally, he reads the *brachos u'klallos* from *ki tavo* and continues through to the end of the entire *parsha*. *Rashi* identifies the *birchos u'klallos* as the acceptance of the *bris* of the *torah*, the covenant — *kabalat britot shel Torah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּרָכוֹת שֶׁכֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל מְבָרֵךְ אוֹתָן — הַמֶּלֶךְ מְבָרֵךְ אוֹתָן, אֶלָּא שֶׁנּוֹתֵן שֶׁל רְגָלִים תַּחַת מְחִילַת הֶעָוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The same **blessings that the High Priest recites** on Yom Kippur, **the king recites** at this ceremony, **but he delivers** a blessing **concerning the Festivals in place of** the blessing concerning **forgiveness for iniquity.**

After the reading, the king recites the same eight *brachos* as the *kohen gadol*, with one modification: instead of *al mechilas ha'avon* — which is appropriate to *yom kippur* — he substitutes a *bracha* for the *regalim*, concluding with *mekadesh yisrael v'hazmanim*, which fits the *succos* setting.

The translator, *Rashi* notes, does not accompany the king's reading. There is no *meturgeman* when the king reads. This creates an apparent difficulty regarding the jumping between passages in the *torah* — if there is no translator, there is no framework within which to evaluate whether a skip is made before the translator finishes, since there is no translator at all. *Rashi* resolves this by saying that the absence of a translator means the constraint of *birkat she'ein tzricha* and *tircha* operate differently, though this explanation is acknowledged to be somewhat difficult to fully work out.

### Hakhel Day

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בַּשְּׁמִינִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? אֵימָא: בַּשְּׁמִינִית.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna states that the assembly takes place on the eighth. The Gemara asks: Does it **enter your mind** that the assembly takes place **on the eighth** day of the festival of *Sukkot*? The mishna clearly states that the ceremony takes place at the conclusion of the first day of the Festival. Rather, **say** that it takes place **during the eighth** year of the Sabbatical cycle.

The *gemara* then asks: if the *mishna* says *motzei yom tov harishon shel chag* — after the first day of *yom tov* of *succos* — does this mean *hakhel* is done on the second day of *succos*? *Bishmini salka dach'tacha*? The *gemara* resolves this: *ela eima bishminis* — no, it means in the eighth year, the year following *shmita*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל הָנֵי לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The verse describes in detail when the assembly takes place: "At the end of every seven years, in the Festival of the Sabbatical Year, in the festival of *Sukkot*, when all Israel comes to appear before the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 31:10–11). The Gemara asks: **And why do I** need **all these** details?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״מִקֵּץ״ — הֲוָה אָמֵינָא נִימְנוֹ מֵהַשְׁתָּא, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא מִתְרְמֵי בִּשְׁמִיטָּה, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״שְׁמִטָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** All of these details **are necessary, as, if the Merciful One had written** only **"at the end** of every seven years" (Deuteronomy 31:10), **I would have said** that **we count from now,** i.e., from when this was said, **and** that the tally of years begins from the fortieth year in the wilderness, **even though** by this calculation the assembly **would not occur in the Sabbatical Year.** Therefore, **the Merciful One writes: "The Sabbatical Year."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״שְׁמִטָּה״ — הֲוָה אָמֵינָא בְּסוֹף שְׁמִיטָּה, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״בְּמֹעֵד״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if the Merciful One had written** only the phrase: At the end of every seven years of the **"Sabbatical Year," I would have said** that it takes place **at the end of the Sabbatical Year.** Therefore, **the Merciful One writes: "In the Festival,"** and the first Festival following the Sabbatical Year is in the month of Tishrei.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי כְּתַב ״בְּמֹעֵד״ — הֲוָה אָמֵינָא מֵרֵישׁ שַׁתָּא, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״בְּחַג הַסֻּכּוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the Torah **had written** only: "At the end of every seven years **in the Festival** of the Sabbatical Year," **I would have said** that it takes place **on** the festival of **Rosh Hashanah,** which is on the first day of Tishrei. Therefore, **the Merciful One** also **writes: "In the festival of *Sukkot*."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״בְּחַג הַסֻּכּוֹת״ — הֲוָה אָמֵינָא אֲפִילּוּ יוֹם טוֹב אַחֲרוֹן, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״בְּבוֹא כׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל״. מֵאַתְחַלְתָּא דְּמוֹעֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **And if the Merciful One had written** only: **"In the festival of *Sukkot*," I would have said** that it could refer **even** to **the last** day of the **Festival.** Therefore, **the Merciful One** also **writes: "When all Israel comes"** (Deuteronomy 31:11), implying that the assembly takes place **at the beginning of the Festival,** when the entire Jewish people comes to Jerusalem.

The *gemara* then asks why all the different temporal designations in the *pasuk* are necessary — *miketz sheva shanim*, *hashmita*, *b'moed*, *b'chag hasuccos*, *b'vo kol yisrael*. It answers that each one is required to exclude a different mistaken inference. *Miketz sheva shanim* alone might have been taken to mean seven years from the time *moshe* gave the *torah*, not the end of the *shmita* cycle. *Hashmita* alone might have meant the very end of the *shmita* year, rather than the beginning of the following year's *yom tov*. *B'moed* is necessary to tell you it is a *yom tov*, excluding *rosh hashanah*. *B'chag hasuccos* specifies which *yom tov*. And *b'vo kol yisrael* tells you it is at the beginning of the *chag* when they are arriving, not the last day.

## Honor Display…

### King's Progression

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחַזַּן הַכְּנֶסֶת נוֹטֵל סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה וְנוֹתְנוֹ לְרֹאשׁ הַכְּנֶסֶת. שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ חוֹלְקִין כָּבוֹד לְתַלְמִיד בִּמְקוֹם הָרַב! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כּוּלָּהּ מִשּׁוּם כְּבוֹדוֹ דְּמֶלֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** § It is taught in the mishna: **And the synagogue attendant takes a Torah scroll and gives it to the head of the synagogue,** until it is eventually passed to the king. The Gemara suggests: **You can learn from** the fact that all of these dignitaries receive the Torah scroll before the king that **honor may be given to a student in the presence of the teacher. Abaye said:** A proof may not be adduced from here, as the **entire** process **is for the honor of the king,** to show that he is removed from ordinary people by many ranks.

The *gemara* repeats its earlier observation in the context of the king's ceremony: the chain of transmission from *chazzan haknesset* to *rosh haknesset* to *segan* to *kohen gadol* to king again prompts the inference *shema mina cholkin kavod l'talmid b'makom harav* — and again Abaye supplies the same answer: *kula mishum kevod hamelech*. All the intermediate stages exist solely to magnify the honor of the king. The principle applies identically in both contexts: a staged, orderly ascent through every level of the hierarchy, without skipping, is itself the most powerful expression of honor for the person at the top.

## Kingly Honor

### King's Honor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַמֶּלֶךְ עוֹמֵד וּמְקַבֵּל וְקוֹרֵא יוֹשֵׁב. אַגְרִיפַּס הַמֶּלֶךְ עָמַד וְקִיבֵּל וְקָרָא עוֹמֵד. עוֹמֵד מִכְּלָל דְּיוֹשֵׁב? וְהָאָמַר מָר: אֵין יְשִׁיבָה בַּעֲזָרָה אֶלָּא לְמַלְכֵי בֵּית דָּוִד בִּלְבַד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיָּבֹא הַמֶּלֶךְ דָּוִד וַיֵּשֶׁב לִפְנֵי ה׳ וַיֹּאמֶר וְגוֹ׳״! כִּדְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בְּעֶזְרַת נָשִׁים, הָכָא נָמֵי בְּעֶזְרַת נָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** It is taught in the mishna: **And the king stands, and receives** the Torah scroll, **and reads** from it while **sitting. King Agrippa arose, and received** the Torah scroll, **and read** from it while **standing.** The Gemara asks: **By inference,** until that point he had been **sitting. But didn't the Master say** (*Tosefta*, *Sanhedrin* 4:4) that **sitting in the** Temple **courtyard** is permitted **only for kings from the house of David, as it is stated: "Then King David went in, and sat before the Lord; and he said:** Who am I?" (II Samuel 7:18). The Gemara answers: **As Rav Ḥisda said** in a similar context: This took place not in the Israelite courtyard, where the prohibition against sitting applies, but **in the women's courtyard. Here too,** the assembly was **in the women's courtyard.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשִׁבְּחוּהוּ חֲכָמִים. שִׁבְּחוּהוּ, מִכְּלָל דְּשַׁפִּיר עֲבַד? הָאָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר נָשִׂיא שֶׁמָּחַל עַל כְּבוֹדוֹ כְּבוֹדוֹ מָחוּל, מֶלֶךְ שֶׁמָּחַל עַל כְּבוֹדוֹ — אֵין כְּבוֹדוֹ מָחוּל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שׂוֹם תָּשִׂים עָלֶיךָ מֶלֶךְ״, שֶׁתְּהֵא אֵימָתוֹ עָלֶיךָ!
>
> **Translation:** It is stated in the mishna that King Agrippa read from the Torah while standing, **and the Sages praised him** for this. The Gemara asks: **From the fact** that **they praised him,** can it be concluded **that he acted appropriately? Didn't Rav Ashi say: Even according to the one who says** with regard to **a *Nasi* who relinquished** the **honor** due **him, his honor is relinquished,** i.e., he may do so, with regard to **a king who relinquished** the **honor** due **him, his honor is not relinquished, as it is stated: "You shall place a king over you"** (Deuteronomy 17:15). This is interpreted to mean **that his awe shall be upon you.** The Torah establishes that awe is an essential component of kingship, and it is not the prerogative of the king to relinquish it.

Having established that Agrippas stood to read and was praised for it, the *gemara* raises a question: the sages praised him — *shivchuhu*, implying that it was well done — but how? A king is forbidden to waive his honor. The principle is stated explicitly: *melech she-machal al kevodo ein kevodo machul* — a king who relinquishes his honor has not succeeded in relinquishing it. The basis for this principle is the verse *som tasim alecha melech she-tehei imato alecha* — set a king over you, that his fear should be upon you. This is not merely an expectation or a convention; it is a legal requirement. The people need the institution of monarchy to carry its full weight of awe, and the king has no authority to unilaterally diminish that weight. By contrast, a *nasi* or any other officeholder who waives his honor — even the opinion that this is permitted applies to them, according to *amar Avatya*: *nasi she-machal al kevodo kevodo machul*. But the king is categorically different.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִצְוָה שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Since he relinquished his honor for the sake of **a mitzva,** this situation **is different** and does not dishonor him.

Then comes the answer: *mitzvah shani* — for the sake of a *mitzvah*, it is different. When a *mitzvah* is at stake, the king may set aside the protocol of his honor.

### Mitzvah Exception

*Tosafot* raises a powerful objection. The principle *mitzvah shani* — that a *mitzvah* overrides the prohibition of a king compromising his honor — cannot be unlimited. There are cases in the *gemara* where a king is said to be *makhal al kevodo* in the context of a *mitzvah*, such as honoring guests (*hachnosas orchim*) or honoring a bride and groom (*chosson v'kallah*), and yet the ruling there appears to be that even so, *ein kevodo machul*. If *mitzvah shani* applied universally, how could that be?

*Tosafot* distinguishes between types of *mitzvos*. When the king stands to read the *torah* out of honor for the *torah* itself — which is honor for the *shechina* — he is, in effect, saying: my honor means nothing in the presence of God's honor. The *kavod* he is yielding is entirely subordinated to and absorbed by a higher *kavod*: *kavod shamayim*. Similarly, in cases such as whether a king may perform *chalitzah* or *yibbum*, the king does what he does in order to fulfill *mitzvas hamelech* — God's command — which is, in a direct sense, subjugating himself to God's authority.

### Interpersonal Mitzvot

By contrast, when the *mitzvah* in question is *bein adam l'chaveiro* — honoring a *kallah* or rising before a *talmid chakham* — what is brought to the forefront is not God's honor but the other person's honor. Even though there is a *mitzvah* from God to perform these acts, the relational dynamic of the act itself is human-to-human. In that context, the king is effectively trading his honor for theirs. That is not *mitzvah shani* in the relevant sense; it more closely resembles the king simply being *makhal al kevodo* in favor of another human being.

*Tosafot* adds a further dimension: in such cases, the other party — the *kallah*, the *talmid chakham* — themselves bear an obligation to honor the king. There is therefore a countervailing *mitzvah* on the other side: the obligation *upon them* to give the king honor. The king cannot waive what is not solely his to waive; others are bound to honor him, and that obligation is not erased by the king's willingness to forgo it.

## Agrippas & Flattery

### The Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְשֶׁהִגִּיעַ לְ״לֹא תוּכַל לָתֵת״. תָּנָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי נָתָן: בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה נִתְחַיְּיבוּ שׂוֹנְאֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל כְּלָיָיה — שֶׁהֶחֱנִיפוּ לוֹ לְאַגְרִיפַּס.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **And when** Agrippa **arrived at** the verse: **"You may not appoint** a foreigner over you" (Deuteronomy 17:15), tears flowed from his eyes because he was a descendant of the house of Herod and was not of Jewish origin. The entire nation said to him: You are our brother. It is **taught in the name of Rabbi Natan: At that moment the enemies of the Jewish people,** a euphemism for the Jewish people, **were sentenced to destruction for flattering Agrippa.**

When Agrippas reached the passage *lo suchal laseis alecha ish nochri*, he wept. A *tanna* taught in the name of *Rabbi Natan*: on that day, the enemies of *yisrael* — the *gemara*'s euphemism for *yisrael* itself — became deserving of destruction, because they flattered him: *she'hechnifu lo l'Agrippas*. They called out *achinu atah, achinu atah* — and by doing so, they assured him that he was fit, that there was no problem, when in fact there was.

### Genealogy Req.

The question immediately arises: what exactly was wrong? If his mother was Jewish — *imo mi'yisrael* — is he not halakhically Jewish? And if he is Jewish, what precisely is the deficiency that the *pasuk* is pointing to? *Rashi* says that regardless of his mother's status, his descent from non-Jewish ancestry made him *unseemly* for kingship, and the sages were wrong to reassure him.

*Tosafot* goes considerably further. Normally, we follow the rule that for various positions of authority that are restricted to those with proper Jewish lineage, *imo mi'yisrael* — a Jewish mother — is sufficient to satisfy the requirement. But *Tosafot* argues that the office of king is uniquely stringent. The verse itself uses the phrase *mi'kerev acheicha tasim alecha melech* — from *among your brothers* you shall set a king over you — and *Tosafot* reads this as requiring that the king be *mikerev*, fully from within, on *both* sides: *me'aviv u'me'imo mi'yisrael*. The *parsha* of the king thus sets a stricter standard than the general rule about *serarah* and Jewish lineage. Agrippas's father was not Jewish, and therefore, regardless of his mother, he did not meet the *torah*'s requirement for kingship.

### Flattery Defined

If *Tosafot*'s reading is correct, then Agrippas was not merely of questionable lineage — he was legally disqualified by the *torah* from holding the kingship. His kingship was *b'zroa she'lo k'din torah* — established by force and in violation of *din torah*. The people who called out *achinu atah* were not merely being tactful or kind; they were actively affirming that he was fulfilling a *mitzvah* when in fact he was violating a *toraitic* prohibition. *V'chizku b'kach* — they strengthened him in this course of action.

This is the real nature of *chanufah* as the *gemara* will go on to discuss. *Chanufah* is not simply flattery in the ordinary sense of the word — empty praise or unwarranted compliments. It is the act of telling someone who is doing something in violation of the *torah* that what they are doing is perfectly fine and legitimate, and thereby reinforcing them in their transgression. The sages did not just say something kind to Agrippa; they *machzik*-ed him, they gave him the stamp of approval for conduct that the *torah* forbade. That distinction — between flattery as pleasant speech and *chanufah* as endorsement of wrongdoing — will carry the discussion forward into the next *daf*.
