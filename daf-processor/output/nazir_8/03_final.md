# Nazir 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Default & Scope

### Stam Nezirut Intro

The eighth daf of Nazir continues the discussion of *stam nezirut* — the default, unqualified acceptance of *nezirut* — which carries a duration of thirty days. The previous daf dealt with complicating cases: what happens when someone says something like "from here, a large one" or "from here until the end of the world"? In those cases, the rule is that since no particular quantity, time, or unit was specified, the vow is interpreted as a single, standard *nezirut* of thirty days. The contrast is with a case where the person does specify time — as in the earlier *mishnah* where someone says "I am a *nazir* for thirty days and one hour." Since there is no such thing as an hour-long *nezirut*, that statement is interpreted to mean thirty-one days. The organizing principle, then, is threefold: if someone speaks abstractly (big, far, great), that does not translate into additional time or units; if someone specifies time, that redefines duration; and if someone specifies units, that generates multiple *neziruyot*.

### Singular vs. Plural

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם וְשָׁעָה אַחַת״ — נָזִיר שְׁלֹשִׁים וְאֶחָד יוֹם, שֶׁאֵין נְזִירוּת לְשָׁעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: One who says: **I am hereby a nazirite** for **thirty days and one hour,** becomes **a nazirite** for **thirty-one days, as there is no naziriteship for hours** but only for full days.

One subtle point worth noting is that the *mishnah* employs the feminine form in the phrase *hareini nazir achat gedolah*, which is grammatically curious. The *nazir* is masculine, yet *achat gedolah* is feminine, suggesting the underlying referent is *nezirut achat* — one unit of *nezirut* — rather than a masculine *nazir gadol*. This linguistic observation anticipates a theme the *gemara* will develop at length: the difference between using the word *nazir* in the singular versus using the word *nezirut* or *neziruyot*, and whether that grammatical distinction carries legal weight.

## Fill of Box

### Hair/Dust/Sand

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר כִּשְׂעַר רֹאשִׁי״, וְ״כַעֲפַר הָאָרֶץ״, וּ״כְחוֹל הַיָּם״ — הֲרֵי זֶה נְזִיר עוֹלָם, וּמְגַלֵּחַ אַחַת לִשְׁלשִׁים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one says: **I am hereby a nazirite like the hair of my head, or: Like the dust of the earth, or: Like the sand of the sea, he is a nazirite forever.** He has accepted a separate term of naziriteship for every hair or particle of dust or sand, which in practice means that he will be a nazirite forever. **And** he **shaves** his hair **once** every **thirty days.**

The *mishnah* opens with a series of cases: *hareini nazir kisa'ar roshi* — "I am a *nazir* like the hairs of my head"; *ukhe'afar ha'aretz* — "or like the dust of the earth"; *ukhechol hayam* — "or like the sand of the sea." The ruling is *harei zeh nazir olam* — this person is a *nazir* forever. This does not mean a *nazir olam* in the technical sense of an uninterrupted, permanent *nezirut*, but rather that the person has accepted upon himself an effectively infinite number of discrete *neziruyot* in sequence. Because hairs, dust, and grains of sand are all discrete countable units, the vow is interpreted as: "I accept upon myself as many separate *neziruyot* as the number of hairs on my head" — or grains of sand, or specks of dust. Since that number is functionally infinite, the person will forever be cycling through thirty-day *neziruyot*, each ending with the full complement of *korbanot* and shaving, followed immediately by the next.

This must be distinguished from a true *nazir olam*, who accepts a single uninterrupted *nezirut* with no end date, and who is permitted to trim his hair periodically — some hold once a week, some once a month, some once a year — precisely because otherwise the hair would become unmanageable. The person who says "like the hairs of my head" is a *nazir le'olam* in a colloquial sense: he will be a *nazir* forever as a practical matter, cycling through one thirty-day term after another, but he completes each one with a full shaving and *korbanot*. He is not in the legal category of *nazir olam*.

### Rabbi's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אֵין זֶה מְגַלֵּחַ אַחַת לִשְׁלשִׁים יוֹם. וְאֵיזֶהוּ מְגַלֵּחַ אַחַת לִשְׁלשִׁים? הָאוֹמֵר: ״הֲרֵי עָלַי נְזִירוּת כִּשְׂעַר רֹאשִׁי״, וְ״כַעֲפַר הָאָרֶץ״, וּ״כְחוֹל הַיָּם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: This** nazirite **does not shave** his hair **once** every **thirty days,** as he has accepted upon himself one long term of naziriteship lasting for as many days as there are hairs or particles of dust or sand. **And who is** the nazirite who **shaves** his hair **once** every **thirty** days? One **who says: It is hereby** incumbent **upon me** to observe **naziriteships like the hair of my head, or: Like the dust of the earth, or: Like the sand of the sea.** Since he used the plural term naziriteships, it is clear that he is accepting distinct terms of naziriteship.

*Rebbi* disagrees with the *tanna kamma*. He says: *ein zeh megale'ach achat lishloshim yom* — this person does not shave every thirty days. *Rebbi*'s position is that since the speaker used the word *nazir* in the singular — not *neziruyot* in the plural — he did not accept multiple units upon himself. The reference to hairs on his head does not translate into a specific number of *neziruyot*, because units only become significant if the speaker has framed the vow in terms of multiple units. If he used the singular *nazir*, then no matter how vast the comparison, he is simply one *nazir* for thirty days. The number of hairs on his head tells us only that he felt the vow was enormously significant — it does not redefine the scope.

*Rebbi* then clarifies what *would* mean multiple *neziruyot* with shaving every thirty days: *ha'omer harei alai neziruyot kisa'ar roshi* — "upon me are *neziruyot* like the hairs of my head." Only the use of the plural *neziruyot* transforms the comparison into a multiplicity of units. Without that plural, the singular *nazir* limits the vow to one thirty-day term.

### House/Basket Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר מְלֹא הַבַּיִת״, אוֹ ״מְלֹא הַקּוּפָּה״ — בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ, אִם אָמַר: אַחַת גְּדוֹלָה נָזַרְתִּי — נָזִיר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם. וְאִם אָמַר: סְתָם נָזַרְתִּי — רוֹאִין אֶת הַקּוּפָּה כְּאִילּוּ הִיא מְלֵאָה חַרְדָּל, וְנָזִיר כׇּל יָמָיו.
>
> **Translation:** If one says: **I am hereby a nazirite** in accordance with **the capacity of the house, or: The capacity of the basket, one checks** with **him** what he had in mind. **If he said:** My intention was to **take a nazirite vow** for **one long** term of naziriteship, he is **a nazirite** for only **thirty days,** in accordance with the ruling of the mishna that the words long or short are of no account when used in a nazirite vow (7a). **And if he said: I took a nazirite vow without specification,** it is assumed that he meant to accept upon himself terms of naziriteship corresponding to the number of items that fit into the basket, and the smallest items normally placed in baskets are used for this evaluation. Consequently, one **views the basket as though** it were **full of mustard** seeds, which are extremely small, **and he is a nazirite** for **his entire life.**

The *mishnah* then presents the case of *hareini nazir melo habayis o melo hakupa* — "I am a *nazir* like the fill of the house" or "like the fill of this basket." Here, the rule is *bodkin oso* — we examine him, we ask him what he meant. If he says *achas gedolah nazarti* — "I accepted one large *nezirut*" — meaning the comparison to the fill of the house was meant only to convey magnitude, not to specify a number of units, then he is simply a *nazir* for thirty days. He did not give us time, he did not give us units, he just expressed that it felt like a very large thing. Fine — one *nezirut*.

But if he says *stam nazarti* — "I declared *nezirut* without any particular intention" — then the words themselves must be interpreted. The *mishnah* rules: *ro'in es hakupa ke'ilu hi mele'ah chardal* — we view the basket as if it were filled with mustard seeds, and the person is a *nazir* for all the days of his life, since the number of mustard seeds that fill a basket is functionally uncountable. This is striking. The very premise requires examination: why does saying "like the fill of the box" translate into a number of discrete units at all? The person used the word *nazir* in the singular. And even granting that the phrase somehow implies units, why interpret it as the most numerous possible units — mustard seeds — rather than the fewest, such as watermelons or gourds?

### Distance Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר מִכָּאן עַד מָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״ — אוֹמְדִין כַּמָּה יָמִים מִכָּאן עַד מָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי, אִם פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם — נָזִיר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם. וְאִם לָאו — נָזִיר כְּמִנְיַן הַיָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** If one says: **I am hereby a nazirite from here until such and such a place, one estimates how many days** it takes to walk **from here until such and such a place. If** it is **less than thirty days,** he is **a nazirite** for **thirty days,** since this is the minimum term of naziriteship. **And if not,** i.e., if it takes more than thirty days to walk that distance, he is **a nazirite in accordance with the number of days** it takes to walk to that place.

This was discussed earlier in the context of the *mishnah*'s statement that saying "from here to the end of the world" does not lengthen the *nezirut*. The question there was: if "from here to Chicago" translates into a duration, why does "from here to the end of the world" not do the same? Tosafot raises the possibility that people do have a fixed assumption about the distance to the end of the world, which would make it no different from any other measurable journey. Rabbah's resolution was that this case applies when the person is already on the trip — when someone is traveling and declares *nezirut* for the duration of the journey, the distance naturally converts into time. Tosafot adds another dimension: the person may be accepting *nezirut* as a *neder* seeking divine protection for the journey, in which case "until I get there" is the natural scope.

### Solar Year

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר כְּמִנְיַן יְמוֹת הַחַמָּה״ — מוֹנֶה נְזִירוֹת כְּמִנְיַן יְמוֹת הַחַמָּה. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מַעֲשֶׂה הָיָה, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִשְׁלִים — מֵת.
>
> **Translation:** If one says: **I am hereby a nazirite in accordance with the number of days** in **a solar** year, he **counts** 365 consecutive **naziriteships, in accordance with the number of days** in **a solar** year. **Rabbi Yehuda said: There was an incident** where someone took this vow and observed 365 consecutive terms of naziriteship. **Once he completed** all these terms of naziriteship, **he died.**

A parallel case: *hareini nazir keminyan yemot hachama* — "I am a *nazir* like the number of days of the solar year." The *mishnah* rules *moneh neziros keminyan yemot hachama* — the person must fulfill 365 *neziruyot*, one after another. Critically, the formula is "the *number* of days of the solar year," not "for the *duration* of a solar year." The word *keminyan* — "like the number of" — signals that we are counting discrete units, not measuring a span of time. Accordingly, we count 365 separate thirty-day *neziruyot*, each requiring its own completion and *korbanot*. At thirty days per *nezirut*, that amounts to approximately thirty years.

*Rabbi Yehudah* reports that there was an actual such case, and by the time the man finished all 365 *neziruyot*, he had died. This anecdote is cited not merely as a curiosity but as proof of the legal ruling: we do not interpret "the number of days of the year" as one year-long *nezirut*, but as 365 discrete *neziruyot*. The man spent the better part of his life fulfilling them.

### Mustard vs. Watermelon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ רוֹאִין אֶת הַקּוּפָּה כְּאִילּוּ מְלֵאָה חַרְדָּל וְנָזִיר כׇּל יָמָיו. וְאַמַּאי? וְלִיחְזְיַהּ כְּאִילּוּ מְלֵאָה קִישּׁוּאִין וְדִלּוּעִין, וְתִיהְוֵי לֵיהּ תַּקַּנְתָּא!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna taught that if one said: I am hereby a nazirite in accordance with the capacity of the basket, and he did not specifically intend to accept one term of naziriteship, one **views the basket as though** it were **full of mustard** seeds, **and he is a nazirite** for **his entire life.** The Gemara asks: **But why** does one view the basket as though it were full of mustard seeds? **Let us view it as though** it were **full of cucumbers or gourds,** which are much larger. The basket would consequently hold fewer of them, **and there would be a remedy for him,** i.e., he would be able to complete his terms of naziriteship and resume living as a non-nazirite.

The *gemara* addresses the obvious question raised by the fill-of-the-basket ruling. It asks: *b'mai* — why mustard seeds? *Delachzei ke'ilu mele'ah kishuin vedilu'in* — why not view the basket as filled with cucumbers and gourds? Tosafot sharpens the question: why not view it as filled with dough? A single mass of dough, no matter how large the container, could constitute one piece filling the entire basket, which would yield a single *nezirut* of thirty days. The *gemara* is pressing the question of whether ambiguous language should be resolved strictly or leniently.

Tosafot notes that there is a *mishnah* in *Taharos* that states *safek nezirus lehakeil* — in cases of doubt regarding *nezirut*, we rule leniently. That principle would point toward interpreting the basket as filled with the fewest, largest units possible. The *gemara* then reveals that this is in fact a dispute between *Rebbi Shimon* and *Rabbi Yehudah*, and that the stringent interpretation of mustard seeds reflects the position of *Rebbi Shimon*.

## Shimon vs. Yehudah

### Safek Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: בְּמַחֲלוֹקֶת שְׁנוּיָה. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּאָמַר: אָדָם מַכְנִיס אֶת עַצְמוֹ לְדָבָר שֶׁסְּפֵיקוֹ חָמוּר מִוַּדַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** In response to this question, **Ḥizkiyya said:** This issue **is taught** as **a dispute** between *tanna'im*, **and** the mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who said: A person places himself in a state where** the resulting **uncertainty is more stringent than** if there were **certainty,** i.e., an individual willingly accepts conditions that are ambiguous although this may cause him to have to keep more stringent *halakhot* if the uncertainty is not clarified.

The *gemara* introduces the broader principle: what do we do when there is doubt about *nezirut*? *Rebbi Shimon* holds *safek nezirus lehachmir* — in cases of doubt, we rule strictly. *Rabbi Yehudah* holds *safek nezirus lehakeil* — in cases of doubt, we rule leniently. This dispute is directly cited by the *gemara* as the backdrop for the fill-of-the-basket case. *Rebbi Shimon* is strict and therefore interprets the basket as filled with mustard seeds, yielding an uncountable number of *neziruyot*. *Rabbi Yehudah* is lenient and would presumably interpret it more narrowly.

It should be noted that the two cases under this debate are not entirely parallel. One is a question of language: how do we interpret ambiguous words? The other is a question of whether a condition was actually met. These are conceptually distinct — one concerns the meaning of the vow, the other concerns whether the vow took effect at all. The *gemara* treats them together, but the difference matters, as we will see in *Rabbi Yochanan*'s subsequent clarification.

### Conditional Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר עַל מְנָת שֶׁיְּהֵא בִּכְרִי זֶה מֵאָה כּוֹר״, וְהָלַךְ וּמְצָאוֹ שֶׁנִּגְנַב אוֹ שֶׁאָבַד — רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹסֵר, שֶׁסְּפֵק נְזִירוּת לְהַחְמִיר.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to one who says: **I am hereby a nazirite on the condition that this pile** of grain **will be** found to contain at least **one hundred *kor*, and he went** to measure the pile **and found that it was stolen or lost,** making it impossible to determine whether it contained one hundred *kor*, **Rabbi Shimon prohibits** him to drink wine or cut his hair, **as** he holds that in a case of **uncertain naziriteship** one is required **to** act **stringently.** Similarly, in the case in the mishna, since it is not known whether one intended to accept naziriteship according to the number of mustard seeds in the basket or according to the number of gourds there, he must act stringently.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַתִּיר, שֶׁסְּפֵק נְזִירוּת לְהָקֵל.
>
> **Translation:** Conversely, **Rabbi Yehuda permits** him to drink wine or cut his hair, **as** he holds that in a case of **uncertain naziriteship** one is permitted **to** act **leniently.** The naziriteship does not take effect, since the pile might have contained less than one hundred *kor*.

A *baraita* is introduced: *hareini nazir al m'nas sheyehei becheri zeh me'ah kor* — "I am a *nazir* on the condition that there will be in this pile of grain one hundred *kor*." The person then went to measure the pile and found it gone — either stolen or lost. The question is whether the *nezirut* took effect. *Rebbi Shimon* rules that the person is forbidden — *safek nezirus lehachmir*, we treat the uncertainty strictly and assume the *nezirut* applies. *Rabbi Yehudah* rules that the person is permitted — *safek nezirus lehakeil*, and since we do not know whether the condition was met, the *nezirut* does not take effect.

One way to frame *Rabbi Yehudah*'s position: when the person said "on condition that there are one hundred *kor* in the pile," what he really meant is "on condition that we *find* one hundred *kor* in the pile." Since one went and the pile was gone, one never found one hundred *kor* — regardless of whether the hundred *kor* were there before being stolen. On that reading, the condition was demonstrably not met. But the *gemara*'s broader framing is that the dispute is simply about whether uncertainty triggers leniency or stringency.

### Safek Worse Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָדָם מַכְנִיס אֶת עַצְמוֹ לְדָבָר שֶׁסְּפֵיקוֹ חָמוּר מִוַּדַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** A person places himself in a state where the resulting **uncertainty is more stringent than** if there were **certainty.**

The *gemara* then introduces a striking and counterintuitive claim: *adam machnis es atzmo ledavar shesefeko chamur mivado* — a person can put himself into a situation where the *safek* (doubt) is actually more stringent than the *vadai* (certainty). How is being a *safek nazir* worse than being a definite *nazir*?

The answer: if one is a definite *nazir*, one completes thirty days, brings the *korbanot* — *olah*, *shelamim*, *chatat*, and *asham* — and is released. But if one is a *safek nazir*, one must observe the prohibitions of *nezirut* since one might be a *nazir*. Yet at the end of thirty days, one cannot bring the *korbanot* that complete and terminate the *nezirut*, because those *korbanot* — particularly the *chatat* and *asham* — may only be brought by a definite *nazir*. They cannot be brought as voluntary offerings by someone who may not be a *nazir* at all. The result is that the *safek nazir* is trapped: obligated to maintain the restrictions indefinitely because the *nezirut* has not been formally closed, yet unable to close it because doing so requires *korbanot* one cannot bring under uncertainty.

### Solution Strategy

There is, however, a way out of this trap. The *safek nazir* can make the following declaration: "If I am already a *nazir* from my earlier statement, I am doing nothing now. But if I am not yet a *nazir*, then I hereby accept *nezirut* upon myself right now." This conditional statement converts the ambiguous situation into a *vadai* either way. If the first acceptance worked, nothing changes. If it did not, the new declaration is unconditional and definitive. Either way the person is now a certain *nazir*, can complete thirty days, bring the *korbanot*, and be released.

### Tamei Scenario

Tosafot notes, however, that even this solution does not always work, and that is precisely why *safek* remains worse than *vadai* in principle. Consider the following scenario: the person is a *safek nazir* and is about to recite the conditional formula to resolve his status — but before he can do so, he becomes *tamei* (ritually impure). Now the problem compounds. If he was already a *nazir* before becoming *tamei*, he brings the *korbanot* of a *nazir tamei*, which include a *chatat* ha-of and other specific offerings, and he restarts his *nezirut* from the beginning. But if he was not yet a *nazir* when he became *tamei*, and he now accepts *nezirut* upon himself, he does *not* bring the *korbanot* of a *nazir tamei* — because those are only for someone who was already a *nazir* when the impurity occurred. The conditional formula therefore does not resolve the problem in this scenario; one remains stuck in genuine uncertainty with no clean exit. This demonstrates that being a *safek nazir* can be structurally worse than being a *vadai nazir*, even accounting for the partial solution.

The *gemara*'s conclusion is clear: *Rebbi Shimon* is fully aware that his stringency places the person in a worse position than the stringency of a definite obligation — and he is willing to accept that consequence, because his view is that whenever there is doubt in *nezirut*, we go strict. *Rabbi Yehudah* disagrees and refuses to impose *nezirut* under uncertainty.

## Yehudah Position

### Status Quo Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הָתָם לָא נָחֵית לֵיהּ לִנְזִירוּת. הָכָא נָחֵית לֵיהּ לִנְזִירוּת, בְּמַאי לְסַלּוֹקֵיהּ מִינֵּיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: You** can **even say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda** because the cases in the mishna and in the *baraita* are not comparable. **There,** in the case discussed in the *baraita*, **he does not** necessarily even **enter** a state of **naziriteship,** as it is unclear whether the pile contained a *kor* of grain. Consequently, he retains his previous status and is not considered a nazirite. Conversely, in the case discussed **here** in the mishna, **he** certainly **enters** a state of **naziriteship,** since he undoubtedly vowed to be a nazirite for some period of time. Consequently, **how** is it possible **to remove** the state of naziriteship **from him** when it is uncertain when his terms end? Therefore, even Rabbi Yehuda would agree that he remains a nazirite indefinitely.

*Rabbi Yochanan* intervenes with a crucial distinction: *afilu teima Rabbi Yehudah* — one can say even *Rabbi Yehudah* would agree with the strict ruling in the fill-of-the-basket case. The key is *hasam lo nachis lei l'nezirus, hacha nachis lei l'nezirus* — there, in the conditional case of the pile of grain, the person never definitively entered into *nezirut* in the first place. Since we do not know whether the condition was met, we do not know whether *nezirut* ever began. *Rabbi Yehudah*'s leniency operates precisely in this space: the person's current status is non-*nazir*, and without certainty that the condition was fulfilled, we preserve that status quo. We do not impose *nezirut* on the basis of doubt.

But in the fill-of-the-basket case, the person has certainly entered *nezirut* — at minimum, even the most lenient interpretation (one watermelon) means he became a *nazir*. Once *nezirut* has definitely begun, *Rabbi Yehudah* might agree that we do not allow doubt to terminate or limit it. The question is no longer whether *nezirut* begins; it has already begun. The question is whether it continues and extends. And on that question, perhaps even *Rabbi Yehudah* would say: *b'mai islukey minei?* — what force would remove him from his *nezirut*? Since he is already a *nazir*, doubt does not extricate him.

### First Ends Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּאי לָא? לִיחְזְיַהּ לְקוּפָּה כְּאִילּוּ מְלֵאָה קִישּׁוּאִין וְדִלּוּעִין, וְתִיהְוֵי לֵיהּ תַּקַּנְתָּא. הָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתִּין, נְזִירוּת הוּא דְּקַבֵּיל עִילָּוֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why** is it **not** possible to remove from him the status of naziriteship? **Let us view the basket as though** it were **full of cucumbers or gourds,** as he has accepted at least as many terms of naziriteship as the number of cucumbers or gourds that can fit in the basket, **and** in this way **there will be a remedy for him. It entered our minds** to say that **it is** distinct **naziriteships that he accepted upon himself,** and so once he has completed the minimal number of naziriteships, he should no longer be considered a nazirite unless it can be determined that he accepted more than this number of terms of naziriteship.

Tosafot complicates *Rabbi Yochanan*'s proposed distinction. One might think that after the first thirty-day *nezirut* is completed, the person has fully exited *nazir* status — and therefore when the question arises whether a second *nezirut* begins, we are back to the same situation as the conditional case: the person is currently not a *nazir*, and doubt should not create one. In other words, Tosafot is pointing out that *Rabbi Yochanan*'s logic — that the person already entered *nezirut* — applies only during the first term. Once that term ends, the person's status reverts to non-*nazir*, and the rationale for stringency dissolves.

### Second Term Start

This leads to the *gemara*'s sharper formulation of the difficulty. After the first thirty days conclude, the person's first *nezirut* is definitively complete. The question becomes: does a second *nezirut* begin? At this moment, the person is not a *nazir*. If *Rabbi Yehudah* holds that we do not impose *nezirut* under doubt when the person's status quo is non-*nazir*, then at the end of each thirty-day cycle, he should be permitted to stop — because the question of whether the next term begins is itself uncertain, and the status quo is not-*nazir*. The *gemara* raises this as a genuine challenge to *Rabbi Yochanan*'s proposed reconciliation.

The *gemara*'s response is that perhaps the entire fill-of-the-basket case is treated as one *nezirut* whose length is uncertain — rather than multiple *neziruyot* of uncertain number. If *Rabbi Yehudah* holds like *Rebbi* (as the *gemara* will now develop), then saying *nazir* in the singular with a vague quantifier might mean one very long *nezirut* of indefinite duration, not thousands of discrete separate *neziruyot*. In that framing, the question is never "does the next *nezirut* begin?" but rather "when does this one end?" — and since it has already begun, the uncertainty about its endpoint keeps it running, consistent with *Rabbi Yehudah*'s logic.

## Yehudah = Rabbi?

### Singular Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי. דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אֵין זֶה מְגַלֵּחַ אַחַת לִשְׁלשִׁים יוֹם. וְאֵיזֶהוּ שֶׁמְּגַלֵּח אַחַת לִשְׁלשִׁים יוֹם? הָאוֹמֵר ״[הֲרֵי] עָלַי נְזִירוֹת כִּשְׂעַר רֹאשִׁי״ וְ״כַעֲפַר הָאָרֶץ״ וּ״כְחוֹל הַיָּם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is not possible, **as Rabbi Yehuda holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **as we learned** in the mishna (8a) with regard to one who vows: I am hereby a nazirite like the hair of my head, **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: This** nazirite **does not shave** his hair **once** every **thirty days,** as he is considered to have accepted one long term of naziriteship. **And who is** the nazirite **who** has accepted distinct terms of naziriteship and therefore **shaves** his hair **once** every **thirty days?** One **who says: It is** incumbent **upon me** to observe **naziriteships like the hair of my head, or: Like the dust of the earth, or: Like the sand of the sea.** Since Rabbi Yehuda agrees with Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, he holds that one who uses a formulation similar to that used in the first case in the mishna and says: I am hereby a nazirite according to the capacity of the basket, has accepted one long term of naziriteship.

To establish whether *Rabbi Yehudah* could hold like *Rebbi*, the *gemara* must first determine whether *Rabbi Yehudah* actually agrees with *Rebbi*'s core principle. *Rebbi*'s ruling, as stated in the *mishnah*, is that using the word *nazir* in the singular — even combined with "like the hairs of my head" — means only one *nezirut* of thirty days, because the plural *neziruyot* is required to generate multiple units. The *gemara* proposes that perhaps *Rabbi Yehudah* holds this same view, which would explain how our *mishnah* (the fill-of-the-basket case with its stringent ruling) could be attributed to *Rabbi Yehudah*: he agrees it is one *nezirut*, but since it definitely began and we don't know when it ends, it continues indefinitely.

### 365 Days Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מִי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי? וְהָתְנַן: הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר כְּמִנְיַן יְמוֹת הַחַמָּה וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מַעֲשֶׂה הָיָה, וְכֵיוָן שֶׁהִשְׁלִים — מֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does Rabbi Yehuda hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi? **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that if one says: **I am hereby a nazirite in accordance with the number of days** in **a solar** year, he counts 365 consecutive naziriteships, in accordance with the number of days in a solar year. **Rabbi Yehuda said: There was an incident** where someone took this vow and observed 365 consecutive terms of naziriteship. **Once he completed** all these terms of naziriteship, **he died.**

The *gemara* immediately challenges this hypothesis. It cites the very case discussed in the *mishnah*: *hareini nazir keminyan yemot hachama* — "I am a *nazir* like the number of days of the solar year." The *mishnah* rules that the person must fulfill 365 *neziruyot*. And *Rabbi Yehudah* himself endorses this ruling by citing the story of the man who died before completing all 365. The verb *hishlim* — "completed" or "finished them all" — implies a plurality of obligations the man had to discharge one by one. Clearly, *Rabbi Yehudah* holds that when you say *nazir* in the singular combined with "like the number of days of the year," you mean 365 *discrete* *neziruyot*, not one long one.

This directly contradicts the hypothesis that *Rabbi Yehudah* holds like *Rebbi*, since *Rebbi* would say: you used the word *nazir* in the singular, therefore you mean one *nezirut*. *Rabbi Yehudah*, by contrast, treats the reference to number as generating multiple units even when the word *nazir* is in the singular. So on the surface, *Rabbi Yehudah* does not hold like *Rebbi*.

### Number Difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִנְיָן״ שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: This does not prove that Rabbi Yehuda does not agree with Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, since one who specifies: Like the **number, is different,** as even Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi would agree that he is referring to distinct terms of naziriteship rather than one long term of naziriteship.

The *gemara* responds: *minyan shani* — the use of the word *minyan* (number, quantity) is different. When someone explicitly says *keminyan* — "like the *number* of" — everyone may agree that this generates discrete units, regardless of whether the word *nazir* or *neziruyot* was used. The word *keminyan* is inherently about counting, about discrete enumeration, and its presence in the formula is sufficient to signal that the person intends multiple units. This is not the same as saying *nazir* in the singular combined with a vague comparison like "like the hairs of my head" — which contains no explicit number language.

To verify whether *Rebbi* himself holds this distinction, the *gemara* introduces a *baraita*. The *baraita* presents the case of *hareini nazir keminyan yemot hachama* — just as in the *mishnah* — and the *tanna kamma* rules 365 *neziruyot*, and a parallel case of *keminyan yemot halevana* (like the number of days of the lunar month), yielding 29 or 30 *neziruyot*. *Rebbi* then says: *ad sheyomar neziruyot alai keminyan yemot hachama* — the person must use the plural *neziruyot* combined with the number language before we count multiple units. According to *Rebbi*, even the phrase "like the *number* of days of the year" is insufficient if the word used is the singular *nazir*. Only the plural *neziruyot* creates multiple units, regardless of what else is said.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי שָׁאנֵי לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי מִנְיָן? וְהָתַנְיָא: ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר כְּמִנְיַן יְמוֹת הַחַמָּה״ — מוֹנֶה נְזִירוֹת כְּמִנְיַן יְמוֹת הַחַמָּה. ״כִּימֵי הַלְּבָנָה״ — מוֹנֶה כִּימֵי הַלְּבָנָה. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר ״נְזִירוֹת עָלַי כְּמִנְיַן יְמוֹת הַחַמָּה וּכְמִנְיַן יְמוֹת הַלְּבָנָה״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is** the case of one who says: Like the **number, different** according **to Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that if one says: **I am hereby a nazirite like the number of days** in **a solar** year, **he counts naziriteships** corresponding **to the number of days** in **a solar** year. If one says: **Like the days** in **a lunar** year, i.e., a year comprised of twelve lunar months, **he counts** terms of naziriteship corresponding **to the days** in **a lunar** year. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** He is assumed to have accepted one term of naziriteship **until he says:** It is incumbent **upon me** to observe **naziriteships like the number of days** in **a solar** year, **or: Like the number of days** in **a lunar** year. This demonstrates that if one did not explicitly use the plural term naziriteships, he is assumed to have accepted a single term of naziriteship even if he said: Like the number.

This proves that *Rebbi* does not distinguish between cases with and without *minyan* language. For *Rebbi*, the singular *nazir* always means one — full stop. Therefore, the *gemara*'s attempt to reconcile *Rabbi Yehudah* and *Rebbi* by appealing to the *minyan* distinction runs into trouble: that distinction may be *Rabbi Yehudah*'s but is not *Rebbi*'s.

### Figs/Paths Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד: מִי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי? וְהָא תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר מִנְיַן הִילְקְטֵי קַיִץ״, וּ״מִנְיַן שְׁבִלֵי שְׁמִיטָּה״ — מוֹנֶה נְזִירוֹת כְּמִנְיַן הִילְקְטֵי קַיִץ וּכְמִנְיַן שְׁבִלֵי שְׁמִיטָּה!
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, does** Rabbi Yehuda **hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** If one says: **I am hereby a nazirite** like **the number of piles [*helketei*] of figs [*kayitz*]** left to dry, **or:** Like **the number of sheaves of the Sabbatical Year,** he must **count** as many **naziriteships as the number of piles of figs or as the number of sheaves of the Sabbatical Year?** This proves that Rabbi Yehuda does not hold that the individual accepts only one long term of naziriteship.

A further *baraita* is brought to confirm *Rabbi Yehudah*'s independent position. *Rabbi Yehudah* himself says in a *baraita*: *hareini nazir minyan helktei kayitz* — "I am a *nazir* according to the number of mounds of drying figs," or *minyan shvilei shemitah* — "the number of paths cut through the field during *shemitah*." In both cases, *Rabbi Yehudah* rules *moneh neziros keminyan helktei kayitz, keminyan shvilei shemitah* — the person must fulfill that many *neziruyot*. This confirms that *Rabbi Yehudah* holds that when *minyan* language is used, even with the singular *nazir*, the result is multiple discrete *neziruyot*. He clearly does not hold like *Rebbi*, who would require the plural *neziruyot* in any event.

## Yehudah = Rabbi?…

### Partial Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר לַהּ כְּוָתֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא וּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא. סָבַר לַהּ כְּוָתֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא — נְזִירוּת קַבֵּיל עִילָּוֵיהּ. וּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא — דְּאִילּוּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה שָׁנֵי לֵיהּ מוֹנֶה, וְרַבִּי לָא שָׁנֵי לֵיהּ מוֹנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Yehuda holds in accordance with** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's opinion **in one** case **and disagrees with him in one** case. **He holds in accordance with his** opinion **in one** case, as follows: If one says: I am hereby a nazirite like the hairs of my head, he has **accepted upon himself** one term of **naziriteship** for as many days as the number of hairs on his head. **And he disagrees with him in one** case, **as** according to **Rabbi Yehuda,** the case of one who **counts** is **different** from the previous case, and so one who states: Like the number of hairs of my head, is considered to have accepted many distinct terms of naziriteship. However, according to **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, one who **counts** is **not different.** He is assumed to have accepted a single term of naziriteship unless he explicitly uses the plural term naziriteships.

The *gemara* resolves the tension with a nuanced formulation: *Rabbi Yehudah savar lah kavatei bichada upaliga alei bichada* — *Rabbi Yehudah* agrees with *Rebbi* in one respect and disagrees in another. The *gemara* has now established that *Rabbi Yehudah*'s position is a middle ground between *Rebbi* and the *chachamim*, and the specifics of that middle ground are worked out in the following section.

## Middle Position

### Alone = One

*Rabbi Yehudah* agrees with *Rebbi* on the following: if someone says the word *nazir* in the singular — without any *minyan* language, without any explicit quantifier — then even if he adds a comparison that involves vast quantities (like the hairs of my head, like the sand of the sea), that comparison does not transform the vow into multiple *neziruyot*. The singular *nazir* without a number word attached to it means one *nezirut*. On this point, *Rabbi Yehudah* concurs with *Rebbi*: the word *nazir* alone, in the singular, limits the vow to one term. The vast comparison expresses only the subjective weight of the acceptance, not the number of units.

### Plus Minyan = Many

Where *Rabbi Yehudah* disagrees with *Rebbi* is when *minyan* language — explicit number-counting — is introduced. When someone says *hareini nazir keminyan yemot hachama*, or *keminyan helktei kayitz*, the word *keminyan* is an explicit quantifier that converts the vow into discrete units. For *Rabbi Yehudah*, that number language overrides the singular form of *nazir* and generates multiple *neziruyot* equal to the stated count. For *Rebbi*, by contrast, even this does not suffice — only the plural form *neziruyot* creates multiple terms, and nothing else.

This middle position elegantly accounts for the *baraita*ic rulings cited in *Rabbi Yehudah*'s name. In every case where he rules multiple *neziruyot*, explicit *minyan* language was present. In cases without *minyan* language — the vague fill-of-the-basket case, the hairs-on-the-head case without the number word — *Rabbi Yehudah* would agree with *Rebbi* that the singular *nazir* means one term.

This resolution also clarifies *Rabbi Yochanan*'s original argument. The fill-of-the-basket case, where the *mishnah* rules that *stam* (no declared intent) means an uncountable number of *neziruyot*, uses the construction *hareini nazir melo hakupa* — the word *nazir* in the singular, with no explicit *keminyan*. If *Rabbi Yehudah* agrees with *Rebbi* in such cases that the singular *nazir* means one, then the fill-of-the-basket case could indeed go like *Rabbi Yehudah* — interpreted as one indefinitely long *nezirut* rather than multiple discrete ones. The stringency is not about multiplying *neziruyot* but about the ambiguous endpoint of a single, already-begun *nezirut*.

One further observation complicates the picture, raised toward the end of the discussion. The grammatical point is well taken that *nezirut* in the singular is itself an abstract noun — it is not simply the singular of *neziruyot*. If *nezirut* denotes an abstract, endless state, then *Rebbi*'s use of *neziruyot* to denote multiple units is linguistically precise: *neziruyot* as a true plural of a count noun implies discrete enumerable instances, while *nezirut* as an abstract noun implies a single undifferentiated state. This grammatical substructure runs beneath the entire *sugya* and lends precision to what might otherwise seem like a merely formal distinction.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר וְאַחַת״ — מוֹנֶה שְׁתַּיִם. ״וְעוֹד״ — מוֹנֶה שָׁלֹשׁ. ״וְשׁוּב״ — מוֹנֶה אַרְבַּע. פְּשִׁיטָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: ״וְשׁוּב״ כִּי כּוּלְּהוּ, וְהָוְיָא לֵיהּ שֵׁית, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If one said: **I am hereby a nazirite and one, he counts two** terms of naziriteship, since he first took a nazirite vow and then accepted an additional term of naziriteship. If one said: I am hereby a nazirite and one **and more, he counts three** terms of naziriteship. If he added to that statement: **And again, he counts four.** The Gemara asks: **Isn't** this last *halakha* **obvious?** The Gemara answers: It is necessary to teach this *halakha*. **Lest you say** that the words **and again** are referring to terms of naziriteship **equal** in number to **all the** terms he has already accepted, **and he has** therefore accepted **six** terms of naziriteship altogether, the *baraita* therefore **teaches us that** this is **not** the correct interpretation of his statement.

The *baraita* toward the end of the chapter summarizes by presenting the full range of cases — *hareini nazir* plus one (*v'achat*), plus more (*v'od*), plus again (*v'shuv*) — yielding two, three, and four *neziruyot* respectively. The *gemara* briefly entertains whether *v'shuv* could be interpreted as retroactively doubling everything that came before, potentially reaching six. It concludes that *v'shuv* simply means one additional unit, so the total is four.

## Closing Greek Terms

### Hen/Digon/etc.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר״ — סוֹמְכוֹס אוֹמֵר: ״הֵן״ — אַחַת, ״דִּיגוֹן״ — שְׁתַּיִם, ״טְרִיגוֹן״ — שָׁלֹשׁ, ״טֶטְרָגוֹן״ — אַרְבַּע, ״פּוֹנְטִיגוֹן״ — חָמֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If one said: **I am hereby a nazirite, Sumakhos says:** If he then added the word ***hen*,** which means one in Greek, he has accepted **one** term of naziriteship. If one said: ***Digon*,** which means two sides in Hebraicized Greek, he must observe **two** terms of naziriteship. If one said: ***Trigon*,** which means triangle in Hebraicized Greek, then he has accepted three terms of naziriteship. If he said: ***Tetragon*,** quadrilateral in Hebraicized Greek, then he has accepted **four** terms, and if he said: ***Puntigon*,** pentagon in Hebraicized Greek, then he has accepted **five** terms of naziriteship.

The *perek* concludes with a *baraita* attributed to Sumchos, a *tanna* whose name reflects Hellenistic cultural influence. Sumchos enumerates Greek numerical terms as they apply to *nezirut*: *hen* (one) means one *nezirut*; *digon* (two) means two; *trigon* (three) means three; *tetragon* (four) means four; and *pentagon* (five) means five. If someone declares *nezirut* using these Greek number-words, the *halacha* recognizes them as valid specifications of quantity, and the person must fulfill the corresponding number of *neziruyot*.

The terms *trigon*, *tetragon*, and *pentagon* are cognates of the Greek words for three-sided, four-sided, and five-sided figures — the same roots as "triangle," "tetragon," and "pentagon" in English. The word *digon* is less immediately obvious but refers to a two-sided figure. These terms appear in the Talmud as evidence that Greek numerical and geometric vocabulary had penetrated the everyday speech of Jews in the Talmudic period, sufficiently so that *halacha* had to address vows made using that vocabulary.

### House Shapes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בֵּית עִגּוּל, דִּיגוֹן, טְרִיגוֹן, פּוֹנְטִיגוֹן — אֵינוֹ מִטַּמֵּא בִּנְגָעִים, טֶטְרָגוֹן — מִטַּמֵּא בִּנְגָעִים. מַאי טַעְמָא? לְמַטָּה הוּא אוֹמֵר ״קִיר״ ״קִירוֹת״. לְמַעְלָה הוּא אוֹמֵר ״קִיר״ ״קִירוֹת״. הֲרֵי כָּאן אַרְבַּע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another *baraita* dealing with these same expressions: **The Sages taught: A round house, a two-sided** house, **a triangle-**shaped house, and **a pentagon-**shaped house **cannot become impure with leprosy,** whereas a house shaped like **a quadrilateral** can **become impure with leprosy. What is the reason** for this? In the verse **below it states,** when referring to **a wall,** the plural term **walls:** "And, behold, if the plague has spread in the walls of the house" (Leviticus 14:39). Additionally, in the verse **above it states,** when referring to **a wall,** the plural term **walls:** "And behold, if the plague be in the walls of the house" (Leviticus 14:37). These plural terms are unnecessary, and it is therefore understood that **there are four** walls mentioned **here** in order to indicate that a house can become impure through leprosy only if it has four sides.

A second *baraita* follows, also employing these geometric terms, this time in the context of *nega'im* — the laws of *tzara'at* affecting houses. The *baraita* states: *bayis agul* — a circular house — or a house shaped as a *digon*, *trigon*, or *pentagon* does not become *tamei* with *nega'im*. Only a *tetragon* — a four-sided, rectangular house — can become *tamei*. The reason derives from the verse's repeated use of the word *kir* (wall): the Torah mentions walls multiple times in describing a house afflicted with *nega'im*, and the *gemara* derives from these repetitions that four walls are required. A house without four distinct walls — whether round, triangular, or pentagonal — falls outside the category of house subject to *tum'at nega'im* altogether.

With this, the *perek* concludes: *hadran alach kol kinuyei*.
