# Kiddushin 79 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kohein & Forbidden Women

### Chalalah vs. Zonah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר ״בְּנִי זֶה מַמְזֵר״ – אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן. וַאֲפִילּוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם מוֹדִים עַל הָעוּבָּר שֶׁבְּמֵעֶיהָ מַמְזֵר הוּא – אֵינָם נֶאֱמָנִים. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמָנִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who says: This son of mine** is **a *mamzer*,** e.g., if he claims that the son was born to one forbidden to him by a prohibition that carries the punishment of *karet*, **he is not deemed credible** to render him a *mamzer*. **And even** if **both of them,** the father and the mother, **admit that a fetus in her womb is a *mamzer*, they are not deemed credible. Rabbi Yehuda says: They are deemed credible.**

Today's daf is 79, and we begin where we left off at the very bottom of 78a, dealing with the issue of the women who are forbidden to a *kohein*. Yesterday's discussion established several critical distinctions: first, the difference between the definition of a *chalalah* and a *zonah*; and second, the crucial question of whether the prohibition is one of marriage or of sexual relations.

The *gemara* makes clear regarding the *kohein gadol* that the prohibition of *lo yikach* is violated by marriage followed by sex, while according to Abaye marriage alone constitutes that prohibition. The prohibition of *lo yechalel* is triggered by sex alone. In the case of *machzir grushaso* — which is unrelated to *kehunah* specifically — it is marriage culminating in sex that constitutes the violation.

### Kohein Hedyot Dispute

A question the *gemara* never directly resolves is what applies to a normal *kohein hedyot*. The Ravad and Rashi hold that a *kohein hedyot* is governed by the same rules as the *kohein gadol*, and that there is a prohibition of sexual relations alone. The Rambam, by contrast, holds that for a *kohein hedyot* the prohibition is marriage alone — there is no freestanding prohibition of sexual relations.

This distinction carries significant practical weight. When a *kohein* and a *giyores* or a *kohein* and a *gerushah* wish to marry, and no avenue exists to establish that the woman was not truly a *gerushah* or that the *kohein* is not truly a *kohein*, the Rambam's position offers a meaningful, if incomplete, consideration: according to him, without marriage they would not be in violation of the core prohibition — though the children would still be *chalalim*. This is because when a *kohein* has sexual relations with a woman forbidden to him as a *kohein*, the woman becomes a *chalalah* and the children become *chalalim*, and that consequence applies even to a *kohein hedyot*. So while not a full resolution, it is at least noteworthy that even as the children bear the status of *chalalim*, the Rambam's position forecloses a prohibition absent marriage.

### Giyores Status

We then turned to the prohibition of a *giyores*, which is nowhere stated explicitly in the *Torah*. Accordingly, the *gemara* derives it from the *pasuk* in Yechezkel that states the *kohanim* may only marry *besulos mi-zera beit yisrael* — virgins of Jewish descent. According to several *rishonim*, this derivation renders the prohibition *d'rabbanan* or *divrei sofrim*, not *d'oraisa*. According to others, the *pasuk* in Yechezkel reveals the *Torah*'s own definition of *zonah*, thereby making the prohibition *d'oraisa* — though, as noted, it is somewhat paradoxical to categorize a convert as a *zonah*, since that category seems linked to the concept of sexual activity.

### Giyores Positions

Four tannaitic positions emerge. Rabbi Yehuda holds that the disqualification stems from a kind of stigmatic status attached to having originated as a non-Jew, and this can even be transmitted paternally — such that the daughter of a male *ger* is also *pesulah* to a *kohein*. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov holds that if one parent is a born Jew there is no problem, but if both parents are *geirim*, the disqualification passes down. Rabbi Yossi holds that the prohibition attaches only to the woman who actually converted herself — a child of *geirim* is not disqualified. Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, the most lenient position, holds that it is only a problem if the woman converted at the age of three or older; under that age it is not a problem.

### De'oraisa/Derabbanan

The *nafka mina* between the *d'oraisa* and *d'rabbanan* positions is significant in multiple directions. If the prohibition is only *d'rabbanan*, many *poskim* hold that children of such a union between a *kohein* and a *giyores* would not be *chalalim*, since a rabbinic category of *chalal* has no clear precedent (though one might note the interesting question raised by a *chalutzah*'s child). On the other hand, if the prohibition is *d'rabbanan*, it rests entirely and absolutely on the *pasuk* in Yechezkel concerning Jewish descent — end of story. Whereas if we understand the *pasuk* as elucidating the *Torah*'s category of *zonah*, then it becomes somewhat linked to a concept of sexual activity, which potentially opens different analytical avenues. Indeed, a Rashi in Yevamot appears to suggest — against everything else we have said — that a *giyores* is only problematic if she actually had sexual relations as a non-Jewish woman.

### Modern Applications

This framework raises interesting contemporary questions that some *poskim* have begun to explore, albeit without formal written responsa. Consider a child adopted as an infant into a *frum* Jewish home and converted at a very young age — where the entire premise that being raised as a non-Jew correlates with premarital sexual activity seems inapplicable. Could such a case be distinguished within the *zonah* framework? However, our *gemara* itself offers no support for an approach grounded in concern about actual sexual behavior. All the rules are derived from the *pasuk* in Yechezkel, and the very fact that even a woman who converted *in utero* — having been conceived as a non-Jew when her pregnant mother converted — is still categorized as forbidden to a *kohein* according to many *rishonim* (on the basis of the phrase *nizre'ah b'yisrael*, meaning conceived as a Jew), leaves no room in our *sugya* for that line of reasoning. Still, the *rishonim* who frame the prohibition as ultimately implicating the *zonah* category do introduce at least a conceptual linkage to sexual conduct, and that is at least worth noting as one explores the outer parameters.

## Yechezkel Pasuk

### Pasuk Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי קְרָא, רֵישָׁא בְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל וְסֵיפָא בְּכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין.
>
> **Translation:** Concerning **this verse** from Ezekiel, **the first clause** addresses a case **with a High Priest,** as it states that he may not marry a widow, **and the latter clause** addresses a case **with a common priest,** as it states that he may marry a widow. Is that a reasonable manner in which to interpret a verse? Rava **said to him: Yes,** that is the correct interpretation of the verse.

We now turn to the final line of 78a and the opening of 78b. Rav Nachman poses a challenge to Rava: the *pasuk* in Yechezkel appears to address two different categories of *kohanim* simultaneously — its opening addresses the *kohein gadol* (prohibiting marriage with a widow, an *almanah*), while its conclusion, *ki im besulos mi-zera beit yisrael*, is the source we have been using for the prohibition applicable to a *kohein hedyot* regarding a *giyores*. How can a single *pasuk* address two different groups of people?

### Shmuel Precedent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְתַב קְרָא הָכִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְנֵר אֱלֹהִים טֶרֶם יִכְבֶּה וּשְׁמוּאֵל שֹׁכֵב בְּהֵיכַל ה׳״ – וַהֲלֹא אֵין יְשִׁיבָה בַּעֲזָרָה אֶלָּא לְמַלְכֵי בֵית דָּוִד בִּלְבַד! אֶלָּא: ״נֵר אֱלֹהִים טֶרֶם יִכְבֶּה״ – בְּהֵיכַל ה׳, ״וּשְׁמוּאֵל שֹׁכֵב״ – בִּמְקוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman proceeded to ask: **But** can **a verse be written** in **this way,** addressing two different subjects without any demarcation? Rava **said to him: Yes,** and there is a similar case, **as it is written: "And the lamp of God was not yet gone out, and Samuel was laid down to sleep in the Temple of the Lord"** (I Samuel 3:3). If read literally, this verse is puzzling. It apparently states that Samuel was lying in the Sanctuary, **but there is** a right to **sit in the courtyard** of the Temple **only for the kings of the house of David alone.** How then could Samuel be laid down to sleep in the Temple? **Rather,** the verse should be understood as follows: **"And the lamp of God was not yet gone out" in the Temple of the Lord, "and Samuel was laid down" in his place** outside the Temple.

The *gemara* responds affirmatively: yes, a *pasuk* can be written in this fashion, with one part addressing one group and another part addressing another. As precedent, the *gemara* cites the story of Shmuel, where the verse reads: *ve-ner Elokim terem yichbe ve-Shmuel shochev be-heichal Hashem* — "the lamp of God had not yet been extinguished, and Shmuel was lying in the sanctuary of God." But this is problematic, since no one is permitted to sit — let alone lie down — in the *azarah*, the Temple courtyard, except for kings of the house of David. The *gemara* therefore reconfigures the verse: the phrase *be-heichal Hashem* is detached from the second clause and reattached to the first — the lamp of God had not yet been extinguished *in the sanctuary of God*, and Shmuel was lying in his own place, outside the *azarah*. This demonstrates that a *pasuk* can be divided, with its internal elements redistributed, to resolve a textual difficulty.

### Almana Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהָאַלְמָנָה אֲשֶׁר תִּהְיֶה אַלְמָנָה מִכֹּהֵן יִקָּחוּ״ – ״מִכֹּהֵן״ – אִין, מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל לָא?! הָכִי קָאָמַר: ״מִכֹּהֵן יִקָּחוּ״ – מִשְּׁאָר כֹּהֲנִים יִקָּחוּ. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: ״מִכֹּהֵן יִקָּחוּ״ – מִשְּׁאָר כֹּהֲנִים יִקָּחוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Since the Gemara quoted the verse in Ezekiel, it proceeds to discuss it further. The continuation of that verse states: **"And a widow who is the widow of a priest, they may take"** (Ezekiel 44:22). Does this mean that a priest may marry the widow **of a priest, yes,** but the widow **of an Israelite** he **may not** marry? This is counter to the *halakha* that allows a priest to marry any widow. Rather, **this is** what the verse **is saying:** "And a widow who is the widow **of a priest they may take,"** meaning that **of the rest of the priests, they may take.** In other words, it is permitted for all other priests, i.e., common priests, to marry widows, and only the High Priest may not marry them. **That is also taught** in a *baraita*: **"Of a priest they may take"** means **of the rest of the priests, they may take.**

Applying this principle to the Yechezkel *pasuk*, the *gemara* reads: *ve-ha'almana asher tihye almana mi-kohein yikchu* — a widow who is a widow from a *kohein* they may marry. This immediately raises a question: does this mean a *kohein hedyot* may marry the widow of a *kohein* but not the widow of a *yisrael*? The *gemara* clarifies: *mikohein yikchu* means *mi-she'ar kohanim yikchu* — all the other *kohanim* (the *kohanim hedyot*) may marry an *almanah*. The structure of the *pasuk* is therefore: the opening prohibits the *kohein gadol* from marrying an *almanah* or a *gerushah*; the middle section — *ki im besulos mi-zera beit yisrael* — applies the requirement of Jewish lineage to the *kohein hedyot*; and the final clause permits all other *kohanim* to marry a widow.

This is precisely why there were those who sought to suppress Sefer Yechezkel — because its plain meaning contradicts the *Torah*, and we are reading it in a way designed to reconcile the contradiction.

### Widow-Daughter Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מִן הַמַּשִּׂיאִים לַכְּהוּנָּה יִקָּחוּ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: בַּת גֵּר זָכָר כְּבַת חָלָל זָכָר, כֹּל שֶׁאַתָּה נוֹשֵׂא בִּתּוֹ – אַתָּה נוֹשֵׂא אַלְמְנָתוֹ, וְכֹל שֶׁאִי אַתָּה נוֹשֵׂא בִּתּוֹ – אִי אַתָּה נוֹשֵׂא אַלְמְנָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda states** a different explanation of the verse: A widow who is the widow **of one** whose daughters are **fit to marry into the priesthood, they may take.** The Gemara clarifies: **Rabbi Yehuda** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning** above, **as he says: The daughter of a male convert is like the daughter of a male *ḥalal*,** and he ruled that **anyone whose daughter you may marry, you may** likewise **marry his widow; anyone whose daughter you may not marry,** e.g., a male convert, **you may not marry his widow** either. The verse therefore states that a priest may marry the widow only of one whose daughter is permitted to marry into the priesthood.

A *baraita* is then cited in support of this reading: *mikohein yikchu, mi-she'ar kohanim yikchu*. Rabbi Yehuda, however, reads the phrase differently: *min ha-masi'im li-khunah yikchu* — a *kohein hedyot* may marry a widow from men who are eligible to marry their daughters to *kohanim*. The class of men who cannot give their daughters to a *kohein* includes a *ger* — this follows Rabbi Yehuda's own position that the daughter of a male *ger* is like the daughter of a *chalal* and is *pesulah* to a *kohein*. Accordingly, the widow of a *ger* also may not marry a *kohein*, since his wife too assumes a disqualified status through him. The operative principle stated explicitly in the *baraita* is: *kol she-atah nosei bito atah nosei almanato* — whoever's daughter you may marry, you may marry his widow; whoever's daughter you may not marry, you may not marry his widow. This is how we reconcile the Yechezkel *pasuk* with the *Torah*'s law, and from this *pasuk* we derive the prohibition of a *kohein* marrying a *giyores*.

## Giyores Ruling

### Halacha k'Rabbi Yossi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אַף גֵּר שֶׁנָּשָׂא גִּיּוֹרֶת. אָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּעוּלָּא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. וּמִיּוֹם שֶׁחָרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ נָהֲגוּ כֹּהֲנִים סִילְסוּל בְּעַצְמָן כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Yosei says: Even** if there was a male **convert who married** a female **convert,** his daughter is fit to marry into the priesthood. **Rav Hamnuna says in the name of Ulla:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, and similarly, Rabba bar bar Ḥana says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei. And from the day the Temple was destroyed, the priests were accustomed** to act with **a higher standard for themselves, in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov,** and they would marry the daughter of a convert only if one parent was born Jewish.

The *gemara* now states the definitive ruling. Rav Hamnuna in the name of Ulla declares: *halacha ke-Rabbi Yose* — we rule like Rabbi Yossi. A child of converts, even of two converts, is therefore not disqualified; the prohibition attaches only to the woman who herself converted. Rabbah bar bar Chana similarly declares: *halacha ke-Rabbi Yose*.

### Post-Destruction Practice

That said, the *gemara* immediately qualifies: *u-mi-yom she-charav Beit ha-Mikdash nahagu kohanim silsul be-atzman ke-Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* — from the day the *Beit ha-Mikdash* was destroyed, the *kohanim* adopted a higher level of stringency for themselves in the matter of lineage, and they conducted themselves in practice like Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov. It is striking that they adopted this elevated standard specifically after the destruction rather than during the period of Temple service. One might have expected greater stringency when they were actively serving — but perhaps this represents a defiant affirmation of *kedushah* in the absence of the *Mikdash*: we have lost the Temple, but we will not allow our sanctity to diminish.

### Prospective/Retroactive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, אָמַר לִי הוּנָא: בָּא לִימָּלֵךְ – מוֹרִים לוֹ כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב, נָשָׂא – אֵין מוֹצִיאִים אוֹתָהּ מִמֶּנּוּ, כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says:** Rav **Huna said to me:** If a priest **comes to consult** with us as to whether or not he should marry the daughter of two converts, **we instruct him** not to, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov.** But if **he married** her, **we do not remove her from him, in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** as the *halakha* follows his opinion, as stated above.

Rav Nachman further refines the practical ruling: if someone comes to ask a *she'elah* before the fact, one rules like Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov — a *kohein* should not marry the daughter of two converts. But *be-di'avad*, once the couple is already married, one rules like Rabbi Yossi — the *kiddushin* is valid and we do not require them to separate. These are among the more challenging cases in *halacha* today, and they arise with some regularity.

## Mamzer Claims

### Father Not Believed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״וַאֲפִילּוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם״? לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא אִיהוּ, דְּלָא קִים לֵיהּ, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ אִיהִי דְּקִים לַהּ – לָא מְהֵימְנָא. וְלָא מִיבַּעְיָא הֵיכָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ חֲזָקָה דְכַשְׁרוּת, דְּלָא מְהֵימְנִי, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ עוּבָּר נָמֵי, דְּלֵית לֵיהּ חֲזָקָה דְכַשְׁרוּת לָא מְהֵימְנִי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What is** the added novelty indicated by the phrase: **And even** if **both of them?** What is taught there that is not stated in the clause that taught that a father is not deemed credible to state that his son is a *mamzer*? The Gemara answers: The *tanna* **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary. It is not necessary** to state this *halakha* with regard to **his** claim, **when it is not clear to him,** i.e., a father can never be sure that a child is his. **Rather,** the *halakha* is the same **even** with regard to **her, when it is clear** to her who the father is, and yet **she is** nevertheless **not deemed credible. And** additionally, **it is not necessary** to state **when** the child **has a presumption of unflawed** lineage, as he was assumed to be of unflawed lineage before his parents came to testify, **that they are not deemed credible. Rather, even** in the case of **a fetus, who does not have a presumption of unflawed** lineage, as he has no status with regard to lineage until he is born, the parents **are** likewise **not deemed credible.**

The *gemara* elaborates: this is partly *ein adam meisim atzmo rasha* and partly the principle that a single witness — an *ed echad* — is insufficient for matters of personal status. A claim of *mamzerut* constitutes a *davar she-be-ervah*, which requires no fewer than two witnesses. Even someone who is epistemically positioned to know — as the mother certainly is — cannot establish the status through her testimony alone, both because she is a single witness and because she is a woman.

### Legal Basis

The *mishnah* states that even if both the mother and the father agree and jointly declare the child to be a *mamzer*, they are still not believed. This is a *chiddush*. The *mishnah* goes further: *ve-afilu sheneihem al ubar she-be-me'eha* — even if they make this declaration while the child is still in the womb, they are not believed. The *gemara* explains the structure of this ruling: *lo mibaya* he is not believed — for how does a father reliably know enough to establish that he is not the biological father? But even she, who clearly does know who the father is, is not believed, because it remains a *davar she-be-ervah* requiring two witnesses, and she is but one. And not only in a case where the child has been born with an established presumption of legitimacy — *chazakah de-kashrut* — but even where the child is still in utero and no such presumption has yet attached, they are still not believed.

### Rabbi Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר נֶאֱמָנִים. כִּדְתַנְיָא: ״יַכִּיר״ – יַכִּירֶנּוּ לַאֲחֵרִים. מִכָּאן אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: נֶאֱמָן אָדָם לוֹמַר: ״זֶה בְּנִי בְּכוֹר״. וּכְשֵׁם שֶׁנֶּאֱמָן אָדָם לוֹמַר: ״זֶה בְּנִי בְּכוֹר״, כָּךְ נֶאֱמָן אָדָם לוֹמַר ״זֶה בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה״ וְ״זֶה בֶּן חֲלוּצָה״. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Yehuda says: They are deemed credible.** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: Expounding the verse: **"He shall acknowledge** the firstborn" (Deuteronomy 21:17), the Sages said: **He,** the father, **shall acknowledge him to others.** In other words, he is deemed credible to tell others that this is his firstborn. **From here, Rabbi Yehuda said** that **a person is deemed credible to say: This is my firstborn son. And just as a person is deemed credible to say: This is my firstborn son, so too, a person is deemed credible to say** about his son: **This is a son of a divorced woman, or: This is a son of a *ḥalutza*. And the Rabbis say:** As far as these latter claims are concerned, **he is not deemed credible.** He is deemed credible only to state which son is his firstborn.

Rabbi Yehuda dissents and holds that the father *is* believed. The source is the word *yakir* in the *pasuk*: *ki et ha-bechor ben ha-snuah yakir latet lo pi shenayim* — the father shall "recognize" his firstborn son to give him a double portion. The *gemara* interprets: *yakirenu le-acheirim* — he may identify him to others; even if the world does not know who the firstborn son is, the father is believed to declare it. From here Rabbi Yehuda extends: just as the father is believed to identify his firstborn son, so too is he believed to say of his son, *zeh ben gerushah* or *zeh ben chalutzah* — this son's mother was a *gerushah* or a *chalutzah*, making the child a *chalal*. And by extension, Rabbi Yehuda would apparently grant similar credibility to a declaration of *mamzerut* — though the *gemara* acknowledges that claiming *mamzerut* by attributing paternity to another man is a considerably larger *chiddush*, since the father is then not even testifying about his own child. The *chachamim* reject Rabbi Yehuda's extension: they grant credibility to the father only for the purpose of *bechor* recognition, and no further.

### Migo Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק לְרָבָא: בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב ״יַכִּיר״, אֶלָּא לְרַבָּנַן, ״יַכִּיר״ לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said to Rava: Granted,** according **to Rabbi Yehuda, this is** the meaning of **that which is written: "He shall acknowledge,"** as he derives from these words that a father is deemed credible to attest to the identity of his sons. **But** according **to the Rabbis** in the mishna, **why do I** need the phrase **"he shall acknowledge"?**

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak then raises a sharp question: the position of Rabbi Yehuda is well-grounded — the word *yakir* constitutes a *chiddush* that the father is believed to identify the status of his child. But according to the *rabbanan*, who accept the father's testimony only for purposes of firstborn recognition, why does a *pasuk* need to tell us that? It should be self-evident from a *migo* argument: since the father, if he wished, could simply give this child a double portion as a gift, he should be believed to declare him a *bechor* and thereby grant him a double portion through inheritance, since he could have achieved the same result without any declaration at all.

## Firstborn/Migo

### Future Earnings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּצְרִיךְ הֶכֵּירָא. לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? לָתֶת לוֹ פִּי שְׁנַיִם? פְּשִׁיטָא, לְמָה לִי קְרָא? מִיגּוֹ דְּאִי בָּעֵי מִיתְּבַהּ לֵיהּ מַתָּנָה, מִי לָא יָהֲבִי לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is necessary for a case **when he requires identification,** if the identity of the firstborn is unknown. In such a situation the verse teaches that the father is deemed credible. The Gemara asks: **With regard to what *halakha*** is this relevant? Does it refer to the requirement: **"By giving him a double portion"** (Deuteronomy 21:17), i.e., that the firstborn receives a double share of the inheritance? **Isn't** it **obvious** that he is deemed credible with regard to this *halakha*? **Why do I** need **a verse** to teach this? His claim is supported by the fact that **he could have made a more advantageous claim [*miggo*]: If he desired to give him** a double portion as **a gift** while he was alive, **would** the court **not give it to him?** Since the father could have given a double portion to the firstborn without having to testify that he is his firstborn, it cannot be that the verse is teaching us that he is deemed credible with regard to the *halakha* that a firstborn receives a double portion of inheritance.

The *gemara* resolves the *migo* argument as follows: a gift can only operate on property the father currently possesses. But the declaration of *bechor* takes effect on everything the father will earn over the remainder of his life — potentially decades of future accumulation. A gift given today could not capture future property; only the *pasuk* of *yakir* empowers a statement made years in advance to govern all subsequent acquisitions. Thus the *pasuk* is necessary.

### Goses Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּנְכָסִים שֶׁנָּפְלוּ לְאַחַר מִיכֵּן. וּלְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר אָדָם מַקְנֶה דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בָּא לָעוֹלָם, ״יַכִּיר״ לְמָה לִי? שֶׁנָּפְלוּ לוֹ כְּשֶׁהוּא גּוֹסֵס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The verse is referring **to property that came** into his possession **at a later point,** which he was unable to give to his son at the time that he came to testify. The verse teaches that the son whom the father states is the firstborn will receive a double portion even of that property. The Gemara asks: **And according to Rabbi Meir, who says** that **a person can transfer an entity that has not** yet **come into the world,** which would mean that a person can give away his property even before it enters his possession, **why do I** need the verse: **"He shall acknowledge"?** The Gemara answers: It is referring to a case **where property came into** the father's possession **when he was moribund,** at which point he cannot transfer ownership of any of his property to others. It is with regard to such property that the verse states: "He shall acknowledge," to teach that the son whom the father states is the firstborn will receive a double portion even of that property.

The *gemara* then notes a further complication: according to Rabbi Meir, who holds that *adam makneh davar she-lo ba l'olam* — a person can transfer ownership over things not yet in existence — why would a *pasuk* be needed even for future earnings? One could simply give a gift today that takes effect on all future property. The *gemara* answers: *she-naflu ki-she-hu goses* — because the declaration covers even property that falls to the father when he is in his death throes (*goses*). A gift designated to take effect on property received at the moment of *goses* would not be effective, and therefore the *pasuk* is still necessary.

This introduces a remarkable dispute between Rashi and Tosafot. Rashi understands that a *goses* — a person in the final throes of death, as distinct from a *shichiv mera* who is merely bedridden and gravely ill — has lost a certain dimension of legal agency. Though killing a *goses* remains murder, and they retain full personhood, Rashi treats them as analogous in some respects to a minor (*katan*): present as a human being but lacking full legal standing to execute transactions. Tosafot vigorously disputes this on the basis of other *gemarot*, and argues instead that the issue is purely practical: a *goses* typically can no longer speak. Therefore, even if one accepts Rabbi Meir's principle that *adam makneh davar she-lo ba l'olam*, such a transaction would require the capacity to perform the act at the time it takes effect — and a *goses* who cannot speak or signal cannot physically complete the transaction at that moment. The legal power is present, according to Tosafot, but the physical capacity is absent. This is a meaningful distinction: one might have thought that legal authority is sufficient even without physical capacity at the moment of effect, but Tosafot holds that *makneh davar she-lo ba l'olam* requires that one be physically able to execute the act at the time it becomes operative.

## Shaliach Priority

### Father's Override

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁנָּתַן רְשׁוּת לִשְׁלוּחוֹ לְקַדֵּשׁ אֶת בִּתּוֹ, וְהָלַךְ הוּא וְקִדְּשָׁהּ, אִם שֶׁלּוֹ קָדְמוּ – קִידּוּשָׁיו קִידּוּשִׁין, וְאִם שֶׁל שְׁלוּחוֹ קָדְמוּ – קִידּוּשָׁיו קִידּוּשִׁין, וְאִם אֵינוֹ יָדוּעַ
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In a case of **one who authorized his agent to betroth his daughter** to a man that the agent would deem fit, **and** the father **went and betrothed her** to someone else, **if his** betrothal **preceded** that of the agent, **his betrothal is** a valid **betrothal; and if** the betrothal **of his agent preceded** his own, the **betrothal** of the agent **is** a valid **betrothal. And if it is not known** whose betrothal came first,

The *mishnah* now takes up the law of agency in *kiddushin*: *mi she-natan reshus li-shlucho le-kadesh et bito* — a father who appoints a *shaliach* to betroth his daughter, and then himself goes and betroths her. If the father acted first, his act negates the *shlichus* and his *kiddushin* is binding. If the *shaliach* acted first, the *shlichus* was still operative at that time and the *shaliach*'s *kiddushin* is binding and the father's is not.

### Uncertainty Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** – שְׁנֵיהֶם נוֹתְנִים גֵּט. וְאִם רָצוּ – אֶחָד נוֹתֵן גֵּט, וְאֶחָד כּוֹנֵס.
>
> **Translation:** **both** men who might have betrothed her **give** her **a bill of divorce** in order to render her permitted to marry someone else. **And if they wish** and agree between them, **one gives a bill of divorce and the other** marries her.

If it is unknown who acted first, both men must give a *get*, since either might be the true husband. And if they agree to a practical resolution, one gives a *get* and the other may then marry her — because either the husband has given the *get* and the other is now contracting a new marriage, or the non-husband has given an extraneous *get* and the actual husband is now marrying her. (It is worth noting parenthetically, as one student observed, that the option of one giving a *get* and the other marrying her would not be available if the man in question is a *kohein*, who may not marry a woman who has received a *get*.)

### Woman's Override

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁנָּתְנָה רְשׁוּת לִשְׁלוּחָהּ לְקַדְּשָׁהּ, וְהָלְכָה וְקִדְּשָׁהּ אֶת עַצְמָהּ, אִם שֶׁלָּהּ קָדְמוּ – קִדּוּשֶׁיהָ קִידּוּשִׁין, וְאִם שֶׁל שְׁלוּחָהּ קָדְמוּ – קִידּוּשָׁיו קִידּוּשִׁין. וְאִם אֵינָן יוֹדְעִין – שְׁנֵיהֶם נוֹתְנִים לָהּ גֵּט, וְאִם רָצוּ – אֶחָד נוֹתֵן לָהּ גֵּט וְאֶחָד כּוֹנֵס.
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly,** with regard to **a woman who authorized her agent to betroth her and** she then **went and betrothed herself** to someone else, **if her** own betrothal **preceded** that of the agent, **her betrothal is** a valid **betrothal, and if** that **of her agent preceded** hers, the agent's **betrothal is** a valid **betrothal. And if they do not know** whose betrothal came first, **both** men who may have betrothed her **give her a bill of divorce. And if they wish, one may give a bill of divorce and the other marries** her.

The *mishnah* then presents the parallel case: a woman who appoints a *shaliach* to accept *kiddushin* on her behalf, and then herself goes and accepts *kiddushin* directly. If she acted first, her *kiddushin* is binding. A potential objection presents itself: by actively seeking to betroth herself before her *shaliach* has acted, has she not implicitly revoked the *shlichus*? The *mishnah* answers no — she has not revoked the *shaliach* merely by pursuing her own *kiddushin*, and until she actually succeeds in her own act, the *shaliach* retains authority. If the *shaliach* acted first, his act is binding even after she has already been trying to arrange her own *kiddushin*, and her subsequent act is too late.

### Father's Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן גַּבֵּי דִידֵיהּ, מִשּׁוּם דְּגַבְרָא קִים לֵיהּ בְּיוּחֲסִין, אֲבָל אִיתְּתָא דְּלָא קִים לַהּ בְּיוּחֲסִין, אֵימָא לָא נִיהְווֹ קִידּוּשֶׁיהָ קִידּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **And** it is **necessary** to state this *halakha* with regard to one who betroths his daughter as well as with regard to a woman who betroths herself. **As had** the mishna **taught us** this *halakha* only **with regard to** the father **himself,** one might say **that** it is **because a man is expert with regard to lineage,** and his decision to betroth his daughter on his own after having appointed an agent must have been due to the superior lineage of the man that he found willing to marry her. **But** with regard to **a woman, who is not expert with regard to lineage,** one might **say her betrothal should not be** considered a valid **betrothal,** as she still intends for her agent to be able to accept betrothal for her from a man with superior lineage.

The *gemara* explains why we need to state both cases: had we only stated the father's case, one might have thought the reason the father's act prevails over the *shaliach*'s (when the father acts first) is because *gavra kim leh be-yuchsin* — a father knows who is *meyuchas* for his daughter, and when he finds such a man and betroths his daughter himself, he is acting with genuine conviction, thereby definitively negating the *shlichus*. One might then have thought that in the woman's case, since her male *shaliach* presumably knows pedigree better than she does, her own act would never truly override the *shaliach* — she might be understood as acting only as a backup in case the *shaliach* fails, and she would always defer to whatever match her *shaliach* secures.

### Woman's Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן גַּבֵּי דִידַהּ, מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיתְּתָא דָּיְיקָא וּמִינַּסְבָא, אֲבָל אִיהוּ אֵימָא לָא אִיכְפַּת לֵיהּ, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** conversely, **had** the mishna **taught us** this *halakha* only **with regard to** the woman **herself,** one might say **that** it is **because a woman is particular** about whom **she marries,** i.e., she is careful when selecting a husband for herself. Therefore, she would have chosen the partner she deems most suitable and would not want the agent to act on her behalf after she had found someone on her own. **But** with regard to the father, one might **say** that **he does not care** to the same extent, and he may authorize the agent to act in any case. Therefore, it is **necessary** for the mishna to include both cases.

Conversely, had we only stated the woman's case, one might have explained the rule by noting that *itata daika u-minsava* — a woman pays careful attention to whom she is marrying, and when she acts herself it reflects a genuine and definitive choice. One might then have concluded that the father, who will not have to live with his daughter's husband and may not particularly care which eligible man she marries, is perhaps also acting only as a backup, not intending to definitively override the *shaliach*. The *mishnah* therefore teaches both cases to make clear that each acts definitively when acting first, for the reasons specific to each.

## Bogeres Status

### Scenario Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: קִידְּשָׁהּ אָבִיהָ בַּדֶּרֶךְ, וְקִידְּשָׁה עַצְמָהּ בָּעִיר, וַהֲרֵי הִיא בּוֹגֶרֶת. רַב אָמַר: הֲרֵי הִיא בּוֹגֶרֶת לְפָנֵינוּ. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: חָיְישִׁינַן לְקִידּוּשֵׁי שְׁנֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** that the *amora'im* discussed the following question: If **her father betrothed her on the way,** when he was traveling, **and** in the meantime she **betrothed herself in the city, and she is** now **a grown woman, Rav said: She is a grown woman at present,** and her father cannot currently betroth her. Therefore, even if he did betroth her at some point, his betrothal is assumed not to have taken effect. **And Shmuel said: We are concerned with regard to the betrothal of both of them.**

The *gemara* now introduces a major dispute between Rav and Shmuel that will occupy the remainder of the *daf*. The case: a father betrothed his daughter while traveling on the road, and she betrothed herself in the city. We now observe her and find she is a *bogeres* — a fully mature young woman past the *naarah* stage. The critical question is: what was her status at the time of the respective acts of *kiddushin*? If she was a *naarah* when they acted, the father had legal authority over her *kiddushin* and his act is binding; if she was already a *bogeres*, she is legally independent and her act is binding, while the father's is not.

### Rav Position

Rav holds: *harei hi bogeres lefaneinu* — she is a *bogeres* before us now. We therefore assume she was a *bogeres* at the time the acts of *kiddushin* were performed, which means the father had no authority, and her *kiddushin* is the binding one.

### Shmuel Position

Shmuel holds: *chaishinan le-kiddushei sheneihem* — we are concerned about the validity of both betrothals. She began her life as a *ketana* and a *naarah*; we know she is now a *bogeres*, but we cannot determine at which point during the relevant period the transition occurred. Therefore, we do not know whose *kiddushin* was binding, and she requires a *get* from both men.

### Six-Month Window

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימַת? אִילֵּימָא בְּתוֹךְ שִׁשָּׁה, בְּהָא נֵימָא רַב הֲרֵי הִיא בּוֹגֶרֶת לְפָנֵינוּ? הַשְׁתָּה הוּא דְּבָגְרָה. אֶלָּא לְאַחַר שִׁשָּׁה, בְּהָא נֵימָא שְׁמוּאֵל: חָיְישִׁינַן לְקִידּוּשֵׁי שְׁנֵיהֶם? וְהָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין בֵּין נְעָרוֹת לְבַגְרוּת אֶלָּא שִׁשָּׁה חֳדָשִׁים בִּלְבַד!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the case: **When** did the father's betrothal occur? **If we say** it occurred **during the six** months when the daughter was no longer a minor and had the status of a young woman, **in that** case would **Rav say: She is a grown woman at present?** It is reasonable to say that **it is** only **now** that **she has reached her majority,** i.e., assumed the status of a grown woman, and in that case her father's betrothal should take effect, since a father can betroth his daughter when she is a young woman. Rav would not rule that the father's betrothal is disregarded. **Rather,** perhaps it occurred **after** the **six** months had elapsed since she became a young woman. **In that** case would **Shmuel say: We are concerned with regard to the betrothal of both of them? But doesn't Shmuel say: The difference between** the status of **a young woman and** the status of **a grown woman is six months alone,** after which the girl has the status of a grown woman, which means her father can no longer betroth her?

The *gemara* presses on the precise timing. If these acts occurred during the six-month window following her attainment of *naarah* status (marked by the appearance of two pubic hairs), how could Rav rule that she was already a *bogeres*? We generally presume that physical *bogrut* does not occur during those first six months. And if the acts occurred after the six months, why would Shmuel treat it as a *safek* — given that Shmuel himself rules that the maximum interval between *naarah* and *bogeres* is six months and no more? Once six months have passed, she is definitively a *bogeres*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּקַדֵּישׁ בְּהָהוּא יוֹמָא דְּמַשְׁלֵים שִׁשָּׁה. רַב אָמַר: הֲרֵי הִיא בּוֹגֶרֶת לְפָנֵינוּ, מִדְּהַשְׁתָּא בּוֹגֶרֶת, בְּצַפְרָא נָמֵי בּוֹגֶרֶת. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אֲמַר: הַשְׁתָּא הוּא דְּאַיְיתַי סִימָנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** in a case **where he betrothed** her **on the day that** these **six** months **were completed. Rav said: She is a grown woman at present.** There is therefore a presumption **that since** she is **now a grown woman, she** was **also a grown woman** when her father betrothed her **in the morning. And Shmuel said:** Perhaps **it is** only **now that her signs** indicating puberty **came,** but she might have still been a young woman when her father betrothed her.

The *gemara* resolves: *lo tzricha d-kadish be-hahu yoma d'mashlim shisha* — the case is that both acts of *kiddushin* occurred on the very day the six months were completing, the day on which *bogrut* is expected to arrive. By the end of that day, Shmuel agrees she is definitely a *bogeres*; but within that day, the precise moment of transition is unknown. The father may have acted in the morning, before maturity arrived; she may have acted later, or vice versa. Rav holds that since she is a *bogeres* at the end of that day, we assume she was a *bogeres* at the beginning of the day — at the time the father acted — and the father's *kiddushin* was therefore invalid. Shmuel holds that we assume the signs of maturity arrived only at the moment we observe them, and that until then she retained her prior status of *naarah*, which means the father's *kiddushin* may well have been binding.

## Mikvah/Chavit

### Mikvah Validity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל, מַאי שְׁנָא מִמִּקְוֶה? דִּתְנַן: מִקְוֶה שֶׁנִּמְדַּד וְנִמְצָא חָסֵר – כׇּל טְהָרוֹת שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל גַּבָּיו לְמַפְרֵעַ, בֵּין בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד בֵּין בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – טְמֵאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Shmuel,** in **what** way **is** this case **different from** the case of a **ritual bath? As we learned** in a mishna (*Mikvaot* 2:2): With regard to **a ritual bath** that was known to have contained the requisite forty *se'a* of water, **which was** then **measured and found deficient** in its quantity of water, what is the *halakha*? **All pure items whose** purification **had been performed in it,** i.e., any impure items that had been purified through immersion in this ritual bath, **retroactive** to when the ritual bath was last measured, **whether** this ritual bath is found **in the private domain or in the public domain, are impure.** This indicates that the current situation, i.e., the deficient ritual bath, is presumed to extend back until when it was known with certainty to have contained the requisite amount of water. In this case too, the woman should be considered a grown woman at the time of the first betrothal, and the second betrothal should not take effect.

The *gemara* now links this dispute to a classic tannaitic debate about a *mikvah* that was measured and found to be lacking — containing less than forty *se'ah*. We know it started with forty *se'ah*, but it has now been found deficient. The question is: what is the status of all the dunkings (*tevilos*) that relied on this *mikvah* in the past? The *baraita* states that all purifications performed upon it retroactively are *tamei* — whether they occurred in a *reshut ha-yachid* or in a *reshut ha-rabim*. Ordinarily, a *safek* of *tumah* in a *reshut ha-rabim* is ruled *tahor*, but here the ruling is *vadai tamei*, suggesting a definitive retroactive determination of invalidity consistent with Rav's approach.

### Tarti Le're'usa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר: הַעֲמֵד טָמֵא עַל חֶזְקָתוֹ וְאֵימַר לֹא טָבַל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** in the case of a ritual bath, it **is different, since it can be said: Maintain an impure** item **in its presumptive status and say** that **it was not** properly **immersed.** There, the presumptive status of the item being ritually impure is supported by the current state of the ritual bath, which is now deficient.

The *gemara* probes how Shmuel can be reconciled with this ruling. The answer lies in the principle of *tarti le-rei'usa* — two negative factors versus one. In the *mikvah* case, you have two competing characterizations of the *mikvah*: its past status as full and valid, and its present status as deficient. Those two statuses face off and in some sense neutralize each other — the past known validity and the present known deficiency cancel each other out. What then determines the outcome? The independent *chazakah* of the person or vessel: they were *tamei* before immersion and that prior status reasserts itself in the absence of a proven valid *mikvah*. Here there are thus two factors working against the person's claim of *taharah*: the *mikvah*'s current deficiency (neutralizing its past validity) and the person's own prior *tumah*. That is *tarti le-rei'usa* — two adverse factors — in contrast to the *bogeres* case, where there is only one adverse factor (*chada le-rei'usa*): her current *bogeres* status faces off against her prior *naarah* status, but there is no independent *chazakah* of any other party to break the tie, leaving it as a genuine *safek*.

### Chavit Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל, מַאי שְׁנָא מֵחָבִית? דְּתַנְיָא: הָיָה בּוֹדֵק אֶת הֶחָבִית לְהַפְרִישׁ עָלֶיהָ תְּרוּמָה וְהוֹלֵךְ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִמְצְאֵת חוֹמֶץ – כׇּל שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים וַדַּאי, מִיכָּן וְאֵילָךְ – סָפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses another question: **And** according to **Shmuel,** in **what** way **is** this case **different from** the case of **a barrel? As it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Terumot* 2:8): If **one would inspect** the contents of **a barrel** to see if it still contained enough wine **to continually** mentally **separate *teruma* from it** to exempt other untithed wine he had, until all the wine in that barrel would be *teruma* and would be given to a priest, **and afterward** the contents of the barrel **were found** to be **vinegar,** which cannot be set aside as *teruma* for untithed wine, then **all three days** after he had last inspected it is **definitely** viewed as having been wine, and any untithed wine for which *teruma* was separated during those days is tithed. **From then onward,** more than three days after the previous inspection, it is **uncertain** as to whether it had already turned to vinegar, and any untithed wine for which *teruma* was separated during those days is not tithed.

A further analogy is introduced: the case of a *chavit* — a wine vessel. Someone had a cask of wine and was systematically designating portions of it as *terumah* for wine he was pressing. Later, the wine in the cask was found to have turned to vinegar (*chometz*). When did the transformation occur? Any *terumah* designated while the wine was already *chometz* is invalid, since *terumah* must be wine. The *baraita* states that the first three days after checking are definitely one status and thereafter it is a *safek* — with a tannaitic dispute about which way the three days cut. For our purposes, the key point is that this is ruled a *safek*, not *vadai* — in contrast to the *mikvah* case. And yet one might have thought it should be *vadai* by analogy to the *mikvah*, because the grain was definitely *tevel* (untithed) — just as the person was definitely *tamei* before the *mikvah*, the grain was definitely *tevel* before the wine became *terumah*. Why then is the *chavit* case only a *safek*?

### Rabbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָמֵינַן חָבִית אַמִּקְוֶה: מַאי שְׁנָא דְּהָכָא וַדַּאי וּמַאי שְׁנָא דְּהָכָא סָפֵק? וְאָמַר רַב חֲנִינָא מִסּוּרְיָא: מַאן תְּנָא חָבִית – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּגַבֵּי מִקְוֶה נָמֵי סְפֵיקָא מְשַׁוֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** Before stating the difficulty with Shmuel's opinion, the Gemara first clarifies the *baraita*. **And we raised a contradiction between** the *halakha* of the **barrel** and that **of** the **ritual bath: What is different** in **that here,** with regard to the ritual bath, the *halakha* is that the items are **definitely** impure, **and what is different** in **that there,** with regard to the barrel, it is only **uncertain** whether the produce remains untithed? In both cases the situation at present, the ritual bath being deficient and the contents of the barrel having turned to vinegar, should lead to a definite conclusion. The Gemara continues its analysis of the *baraita*: **And Rav Ḥanina from Syria says: Who** is the *tanna* that **taught** the *halakha* of the **barrel? It is Rabbi Shimon, who with regard to a ritual bath also considers** it **as** a matter of **uncertainty** rather than as definitively impure. Therefore, there is no contradiction between the *baraita* and the mishna.

The *gemara* answers that the ruling of *safek* in the *chavit* case reflects the view of Rabbi Shimon, and Rabbi Shimon applies the same logic to the *mikvah*: *d-gabbei mikvah nami safeka mashvi lei* — he also treats the *mikvah* case as a *safek*, not *vadai*. The *baraita* that treats the *mikvah* as *vadai tamei* — even in a *reshut ha-rabim* — reflects the view of the *rabbanan*, not Rabbi Shimon. Rabbi Shimon holds *safek tumah bi-reshut ha-yachid* is *safek*, and in *reshut ha-rabim* it is *tahor*. For the *rabbanan*, the *mikvah* case is *vadai* (driven by the *chazakah* of *tumah*), and consistently the *chavit* case would also be *vadai* (driven by the *chazakah* of *tevel*). For Rabbi Shimon, both cases are *safek*. The two cases are in fact structurally parallel — same rule applies to both — and the apparent contradiction between them dissolves once we recognize that different tannaitic authorities govern each ruling. In the *bogeres* case, there is no independent *chazakah* of a third element to tip the balance, and therefore regardless of whose view one follows, it remains a *safek* — hence Shmuel's ruling of *chaishinan le-kiddushei sheneihem*.

## Mi Motzi Ruling

### Deathbed Gift Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי מוֹצִיא מִיַּד מִי?
>
> **Translation:** The *halakha* is that a moribund person who transfers all of his property to others without leaving anything for himself can retract his bequest upon regaining his health. This is due to the assumption that one would give away all his possessions only if he believes he is going to die, and the bequest was therefore made in error. By contrast, if a healthy person gave all his property to others, he cannot later retract his bequest. The *tanna'im* discuss the case of one who gave away all his property and later, seeking to retrieve his property, claims that he was moribund at the time, while the recipients of the bequest maintain that he was healthy. **Who** is able to **remove** the property **from the possession of whom?**

The *gemara* introduces another analogy: *mi motzi mi-yad mi* — who takes from whom? The case is a person who gives away all his property as a deathbed gift (*matnat shechiv mera*) and then recovers. Such a gift is ordinarily void — it was given in anticipation of death, and since he recovered, the gift lapses. Now he is walking around healthy and claims he gave the gift from his deathbed and is therefore entitled to reclaim the property. The recipients counter that he was healthy when he gave it, and the gift stands. Who bears the burden of proof?

### Rabbi Yaakov View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא מוֹצִיא מִיָּדָם בְּלֹא רְאָיָה, וְהֵן אֵין מוֹצִיאִים מִיָּדוֹ בְּלֹא רְאָיָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב. רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: אִם בָּרִיא הוּא – עָלָיו לְהָבִיא רְאָיָה שֶׁהָיָה שְׁכִיב מְרַע, וְאִם שְׁכִיב מְרַע הוּא – עֲלֵיהֶם לְהָבִיא רְאָיָה שֶׁבָּרִיא הָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **He** is able to **remove** his property held by others **from their possession without** needing to bring **proof** that he was moribund at the time, **and they may not remove** the property **from his possession,** if he still is in possession of the property, **without proof** that he was healthy. This is **the statement of Rabbi Ya'akov. Rabbi Natan says: If he is** currently **healthy,** the obligation **is upon him to bring proof that he was on his deathbed** at the time of the bequest, **and if he is** now **on his deathbed,** the obligation **is upon them to bring proof that he was healthy** at the time of the bequest.

Rabbi Yaakov says: *hu motzi mi-yadam be-lo ra'ayah* — he can take the property back from them without proof, and they cannot take it from him without proof. The underlying reasoning: the property began under his ownership and retains a *chazakah* of being his. They must prove their entitlement; he benefits from the status quo.

Rabbi Natan says the opposite: *barri hu alav le-havi ra'ayah* — if he is now healthy, the burden is on him to prove he was on his deathbed. His current healthy status serves as evidence of his past healthy status, and the recipients accordingly are presumed to have received a valid gift. Conversely, if he is now on his deathbed (*shechiv mera*), then they bear the burden of proving he was healthy when he gave the gift.

### Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא רַב דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי נָתָן וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב? אָמַר לָךְ רַב: אֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי אֲפִילּוּ כְּרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב. עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב הָתָם דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר הַעֲמֵד מָמוֹן עַל חֶזְקָתוֹ, אֲבָל הָכָא מִי נֵימָא הַעֲמֵד גּוּף עַל חֶזְקָתוֹ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that **Rav spoke in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Natan** that one's current status is paramount, **and Shmuel spoke in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ya'akov,** who holds that the question of whether he was moribund remains a matter of uncertainty, and they may not claim the property from him even if he is now healthy. The Gemara rejects this: **Rav** could have **said to you: I spoke even in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ya'akov,** as **Rabbi Ya'akov states** his opinion **only there,** because **it can be said** that the principle of: **Leave** the **property in its** previous **status,** applies, so the property should remain where it is. **But here,** with regard to the question of when a young woman became a grown woman, **shall we say: Establish the body** according **to its previous status?** It is obvious that her body was due to mature.

The *gemara* considers: does this dispute map onto the dispute of Rav and Shmuel? Perhaps Rav follows Rabbi Natan — current status projects backward — and Shmuel follows Rabbi Yaakov — you do not project current status backward, and the property retains its *chazakah* of belonging to the original owner.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי אֲפִילּוּ לְרַבִּי נָתָן. עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי נָתָן הָתָם דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא בְּחֶזְקַת בְּרִיאִים קָיְימִי, מַאן דְּקָא מַפֵּיק נַפְשֵׁיהּ מֵחֲזָקָה – (הָוֵי) עֲלֵיהּ לְאֵיתוֹיֵי רְאָיָה, אֲבָל הָכָא מִי קָא מַפְּקָא נַפְשַׁהּ מֵחֲזָקָה דְּקַמֵּיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel** could have **said: I spoke even in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Natan,** since **Rabbi Natan states** his opinion that the current state is paramount **only there, as everyone has a presumptive status of being healthy.** Consequently, if **a person removes himself from** this **presumption** by claiming he was moribund, the obligation falls **upon him to bring proof. But here, does** she **remove herself from a presumption that was present?** There is no presumption that she was a grown woman in the morning.

The *gemara* rejects this mapping. Rav can say: *ana d'amri afilu ke-Rabbi Yaakov* — I can rule like Rav even while agreeing with Rabbi Yaakov. Rabbi Yaakov ignores his current healthy status because the property has an independent *chazakah* of belonging to him — that *chazakah* tips the balance. But in the *bogeres* case, her body was actively in the process of changing; there is no fixed *chazakah* of the body remaining as it was, precisely because transformation was underway. Therefore Rav is entitled to say she was already a *bogeres* in the morning, even while agreeing with Rabbi Yaakov that one does not blindly project current status backward when an independent *chazakah* of property exists. Shmuel, symmetrically, can say: *ana d'amri afilu ke-Rabbi Natan* — I can follow Shmuel even while agreeing with Rabbi Natan. Rabbi Natan projects current healthy status backward because *chulei alma be-chezkat bri'im kaimei* — the world is presumptively healthy; if you claim you were on your deathbed, that is an extraordinary claim that requires proof. But in the *bogeres* case, it is not at all extraordinary that she was a *naarah* yesterday — that is precisely what she was — and the question is simply when within that day the transition occurred. That is a genuine *safek*, not analogous to an implausible claim of terminal illness. The two disputes therefore do not map onto each other.

## Baraita Reconcile

### Dual Baraitas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that it is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im:***

The *gemara* now confronts the fact that two *baraitas* appear to reflect this very dispute: one states she is definitively a *bogeres* (*harei hi bogeres lefaneinu*) — the father's *kiddushin* is invalid — and another states *chaishinan le-kiddushei sheneihem* — both *kiddushin* are in doubt and she needs a *get* from both men.

### Harmonization Try

The first attempted resolution: perhaps one *baraita* follows Rav and the other follows Shmuel, and there is simply a *machlokes* between the *tannaim* that mirrors the *machlokes* between the *amoraim*.

### Claim Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, אִידִי וְאִידִי כִּשְׁמוּאֵל – כָּאן בְּמַכְחַשְׁתּוֹ, כָּאן בְּשֶׁאֵין מַכְחַשְׁתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No;** it is possible that both **this and that are in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel. Here,** in this *baraita*, it is referring to a case **where she denies his** right to betroth her, as she claims that she was a grown woman before his betrothal. **There,** it is referring to a situation **where she does not deny his** right to betroth her, and therefore it is uncertain which betrothal takes effect.

The *gemara* proposes an alternative: both *baraitas* actually follow Shmuel, and the difference lies in whether the woman herself makes a claim. When she affirmatively claims that she became a *bogeres* earlier in the day — *mekacheshes* — even Shmuel might concede that we credit her claim, and thus her *kiddushin* is binding. When she makes no such claim, Shmuel maintains that it is a *safek* and both men must give a *get*. Under this reading, Rav and Shmuel are not in fundamental disagreement — Rav addresses the case where she makes a claim, Shmuel addresses the case where she does not.

### Rav Yosef Incident

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵימָא מִדְּמַתְנִיתָא לָא פְּלִיגִי, אָמוֹרָאֵי נָמֵי לָא פְּלִיגִי! וְתִסְבְּרָא? הָא רַב יוֹסֵף בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב מְנַשְּׁיָא מִדְּוִויל עֲבַד עוֹבָדָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב, וְאִיקְּפִיד שְׁמוּאֵל וְאָמַר: כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא כָּיְילִי לֵיהּ בְּקַבָּא זוּטָא וְהַאי מִדְּרַבָּנַן כָּיְילִי לֵיהּ בְּקַבָּא רַבָּה? וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ לָא פְּלִיגִי, אַמַּאי קָא מִקְּפֵיד?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, **let us** also **say** that **since the *baraitot* do not disagree, the** opinions of the ***amora'im*,** Rav and Shmuel, **do not disagree** either. One is referring to a case where she denies her father's right to betroth her, while the other is referring to a case where she does not. The Gemara rejects this: **And** how can **you understand it that way? But Rav Yosef, son of Rav Menashya of Dabil, performed an action** in this case **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav** and did not take the father's betrothal into account, **and Shmuel took offense** at this **and said** sarcastically: Is wisdom **measured out to everyone in a small measure, and measured out to this** member **of the Sages in a large measure?** He was angered that Rav Yosef had the audacity to rule against him in this case. **Now, if it enters your mind** that **they did not disagree** at all, **why did** Shmuel **take offense?**

The *gemara* then demonstrates that Rav and Shmuel do in fact disagree. Rav Yosef son of Rav Menashya of Devil ruled in practice like Rav — he treated her as definitively a *bogeres*, invalidating the father's *kiddushin* and recognizing hers. Shmuel became furious.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּילְמָא כִּי עֲבַד עוֹבָדָא, בְּמַכְחַשְׁתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Perhaps when** Rav Yosef **performed an action,** he did so in an instance **where she denied** her father's right to betroth her, in which case even Shmuel would agree that the father's betrothal does not take effect. Rather, it must be that Rav and Shmuel did disagree, and Rav Yosef must have ruled in the case of a woman who did not deny her father's right to betroth her, which explains why Shmuel took exception to his ruling.

### Shmuel Rebuke

Shmuel's rebuke is pointed: *kulei alma kailei leih be-kava zuta, ve-hai mi-derabbanan kailei leih be-kava raba* — everyone else measures out their wisdom in a small measure, and this student measured his out in a large one? The implicit message: normal rabbinic authorities, confronted with this uncertain situation, would recognize the genuine *safek* and treat it accordingly — requiring *gittin* from both men. To rule definitively that she was a *bogeres* in the morning and dismiss the father's *kiddushin* out of hand requires a level of halachic mastery and confidence that this student did not possess. Shmuel was teaching him that the appropriate posture for a jurist of his standing was caution and the recognition of *safek*, not bold retroactive determination. The very fact that Shmuel erupted demonstrates that he and Rav genuinely disagreed, since if they agreed in the case of a woman making a claim, Shmuel would have had no basis for anger if that was indeed the scenario in question.

### Final Halacha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר זוּטְרָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי, הָכִי אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: הִילְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל. וְרַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: הִילְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב. וְהִילְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra said to Rav Ashi: This is what Ameimar said: The *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Shmuel. And Rav Ashi said: The *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav.** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav.**

The *gemara* closes with competing reports: Mar Zutra tells Rav Ashi that the *halacha* follows Shmuel; Rav Ashi himself says the *halacha* follows Rav — and the *gemara*'s final declaration is *ve-hilcheta ke-vatei de-Rav*, we rule like Rav. As a practical matter, then, we treat her as definitively a *bogeres* and do not require *gittin* from both men. However, the *gemara*'s closing lines are somewhat unclear in terms of their precise scope — there are questions of *girsa*, of exactly how to read the final passage — and it is possible the ruling applies specifically when she makes an affirmative claim of earlier maturity, which would provide even stronger grounds for ruling like Rav. The broader ruling, that we follow Rav even without such a claim, is the most likely reading of our text as it stands.
