# Gittin 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Perek Overview

### Masechet Framework

Daf 19 opens at the *mishna*, and it is worth pausing to appreciate how the *perakim* of *Gittin* have been structured and how the transition from the first *perek* to the second unfolds. The *masechet* begins in a somewhat indirect manner, organizing itself around the *takkana* of *bifanai nichtav ubifanai nechtam* — the requirement that the *shaliach* who delivers a *get* declare that it was written and signed in his presence. That framework dominates the opening *perek*, and it resurfaces at the beginning of the second *perek* as well, addressing variations on the same theme. But one can also read this structure as a way of approaching the central themes of the *masechet* obliquely — entering through a side door, as it were, before arriving at the heart of the matter.

One important thread that emerged throughout the first *perek* was a series of parallels between a *get* of an *isha* and a *shtar shikhrur* of an *eved*. The *bifanai nichtav* formulation applies to both, and this parallel itself underscores something significant: this is not merely any *shtar*. It is a document that changes personal status. That may be precisely why testifying about the *nichtav* — the writing itself — carries special weight: there are particular criteria governing the act of writing that do not apply to ordinary documents.

### Ketivat Haget Focus

Having established that broader framework, the second *perek* uses *bifanai nichtav ubifanai nechtam* as a bridge into the core concerns of *ketivat haget*. Where the first *perek* assembled a somewhat varied collection of topics — *arkha'ot*, *shtar shikhrur*, *shtar matana*, and the like — the second *perek* begins to focus squarely on the *get* itself and on what the Torah requires of its writing.

### Dating Requirements

The preceding *mishna* addressed the case of *nichtav hayom venechtam balaila* — what if the *ketiva* and the *chatima* occurred on different days? This was a natural transition from the *bifanai nichtav ubifanai nechtam* framework and led the *gemara* to explore the requirement to include a date in a *get* (*lamma hitkinu zeman begittin?*) as well as the particular *psul* of a *get mukdam* — a *get* bearing an earlier date than its actual execution. That discussion, emerging organically from the *nichtav-nechtam* framework, brought us to a central requirement of *ketivat haget*. Now, the current *mishna* moves even more concretely into the physical dimensions of that writing: what materials may be used, and on what surfaces may a *get* be written?

## Writing Materials

### Permitted Inks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בַּכֹּל כּוֹתְבִים – בִּדְיוֹ, בְּסַם, בְּסִיקְרָא, וּבְקוֹמוֹס, וּבְקַנְקַנְתּוֹם, וּבְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁהוּא שֶׁל קַיָּימָא. אֵין כּוֹתְבִין לֹא בְּמַשְׁקִין, וְלֹא בְּמֵי פֵירוֹת, וְלֹא בְּכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ מִתְקַיֵּים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may write** a bill of divorce **with any** material that can be used for writing: **With *deyo*, with paint [*sam*], with *sikra*, with *komos*, with *kankantom* or with anything that** produces **permanent** writing. However, **one may not write with** other **liquids, nor with fruit juice, nor with anything** that does **not** produce **permanent** writing.

The *mishna* opens with the principle *bekhol kotvin* — one may write with everything — and then proceeds to enumerate: *bediyo* (ink), *besam*, *besikra*, *uvekomus*, *uvekhankantom*, and *uvekhol davar shehu kayyama* — with any substance that endures. These are all permitted writing materials for a *get*.

### Permanence Definition

There is an alternative *girsa* of the *mishna* which reads *bedik khol davar shehu roshem* — anything that leaves a mark — rather than *shehu kayyama*. The distinction is worth pondering. *Roshem* suggests an impression or mark, whereas *kayyama* emphasizes lasting permanence. One might ask whether carving or etching would be included under *roshem*, but the *mishna* does not address that case directly, and the *gemara* will have more to say about the question of permanence.

### Prohibited Liquids

The *mishna* then states: *ein kotvin lo bemashkin velo bemei peirot velo bekhol davar she'eino mitkayyem* — one may not write a *get* with liquids, fruit juices, or any substance that does not endure. The contrast with the permitted list is deliberate: *shehu kayyama* versus *she'eino mitkayyem*. This raises a natural question: precisely how long must something last? Is there a defined *shiur* of permanence? Does it need to resist erasure, or is it enough that it not evaporate immediately? The case of a *ketuba* whose ink has faded over decades, or of writing that disappears within moments of application — these represent very different points on the spectrum. The problem with *mashkin* and *mei peirot* appears to be that they are essentially ephemeral — they do not leave a genuinely lasting *roshem*. The word *roshem* itself may be instructive here: even if a substance does not endure for years, it should at minimum leave a visible, distinguishable mark that does not vanish instantaneously. The question of a pencil is interesting in this regard: a pencil certainly leaves a mark and does not fade on its own, though it can be erased. The *mishna* does not mention resistance to erasure as a criterion — that is a separate concern related to *davar sheyachol lehizdayef* — and so a pencil, or something like it, might not be disqualified on the grounds of *she'eino mitkayyem* alone.

### Permitted Surfaces

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל הַכֹּל כּוֹתְבִים – עַל הֶעָלֶה שֶׁל זַיִת; וְעַל הַקֶּרֶן שֶׁל פָּרָה – וְנוֹתֵן לָהּ אֶת הַפָּרָה; עַל יָד שֶׁל עֶבֶד – וְנוֹתֵן לָהּ אֶת הָעֶבֶד. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: אֵין כּוֹתְבִין לֹא עַל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ רוּחַ חַיִּים, וְלֹא עַל הָאוֹכָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, with regard to the document itself, **one may write on anything,** even **on an olive leaf, or on the horn of a cow. And** the latter is valid if **he gives her the** entire **cow.** Likewise, one may write a bill of divorce **on the hand of a slave, and** that is valid if **he gives her the slave. Rabbi Yosei HaGelili** disagrees and **says: One may not write** a bill of divorce **on any living thing, nor** may it be written **on food.**

Turning to the surface, the *mishna* states *al hakol kotevin* — one may write on anything. The examples given are striking: *al ha'aleh shel zayit* — on an olive leaf — and *al hakeren shel parah* — on the horn of a cow. In the latter case, the *mishna* adds *venoten lo et haparah*: one must give her the cow itself along with the *get*. We will see in the *gemara* why this is so. Similarly, *al yad shel eved venoten lo et ha'eved* — one may write on the arm of a slave, but then the slave himself must be given to her. The operative rule is *vekhatvo venatano* — the *get* must be written and then delivered as a single act; if separating the document from its surface requires an additional act after the writing, complications arise, as the *gemara* will clarify.

### Rabbi Yose HaGalili

*Rabbi Yose HaGelili* disputes part of this ruling: *ein kotevin lo al davar she-yesh bo ruach chayim velo al ochlim* — one should not write a *get* on a living creature, and not on food. We will see his reasoning in the *gemara*, along with the question of whether his position is grounded in a *de'oraita* concern or a rabbinic one.

It is worth noting one structural asymmetry in the *mishna*: the requirement of permanence is stated explicitly for the substance used to write *with*, but not for the surface written *on*. Yet an olive leaf is hardly a permanent object. This tension points back to the question of what *mitkayyem* really demands: perhaps the concern is only with substances that are entirely ephemeral — that evaporate or vanish as soon as they are applied — rather than with materials that will eventually deteriorate under normal conditions.

## Ink Analysis

### Dyo & Sam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּיוֹ – דְּיוֹתָא. סַם – סַמָּא. סִיקְרָא – אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: סְקַרְתָּא שְׁמַהּ. קוֹמוֹס – קוֹמָא. קַנְקַנְתּוֹם – אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חַרְתָּא דְאוּשְׁכָּפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara begins by explaining the terms used in the mishna: ***Deyo*** refers to what is called ***deyota*,** ink, in Aramaic. ***Sam*** refers to ***samma*,** arsenic. With regard to ***sikra*, Rabba bar bar Ḥana said: Its name** in Aramaic is ***sikreta*,** red dye, derived from minium, which is also known as red lead. ***Komos*,** tree resin, is known as ***kuma*** in Aramaic. With regard to ***kankantom*, Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Shmuel said:** This is the **black** substance **used by cobblers,** iron sulfate.

The *gemara* now turns to identify the materials listed in the *mishna*. *Dyo* is straightforwardly *dyuta* — ink. *Sam* is more elusive. Rashi identifies it as orpiment; the Steinsaltz commentary says arsenic; another tradition describes it as ink derived from a grass or root. Whatever the precise referent, it belongs to the category of plant- or mineral-derived colorants used as writing substances.

### Sikra & Kankantom

*Sikra*, according to *Rabbah bar Bar Hana*, is *sikrastah* — a transliteration that essentially concedes the difficulty of rendering the word in another language, illustrating the fundamental challenge of translation when no ready equivalent exists. Rashi understands *sikra* as a reddish substance, and that characterization will be important momentarily. *Kankantom*, as *Rabbah bar Bar Hana* reports in the name of Shmuel, is *khartsa d'ushkhasei* — the blacking used by shoemakers. The Steinsaltz commentary identifies it as copper sulfate, though it is likely iron sulfate.

### Kumos

*Kumos* is identified as *kuma* — gum arabic.

### Permanent Materials

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁהוּא מִתְקַיֵּים וְכוּ׳: לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי הָא דְתָנֵי רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: כְּתָבוֹ בְּמֵי טַרְיָא וְאַפְצָא – כָּשֵׁר. תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: כְּתָבוֹ בַּאֲבָר, בִּשְׁחוֹר וּבְשִׁיחוֹר – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught that one is allowed to write a bill of divorce **with anything** that produces **permanent** writing. The Gemara asks: This statement is meant **to add what?** The Gemara answers: **To add what Rabbi Ḥanina taught:** If **he wrote it with *mei teriya* or** water in which **gallnuts [*aftza*]** were soaked, then it is **valid,** as the writing is permanent. **Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches:** If **he wrote it with lead, with coals, or with black paint** then it is **valid.**

Having listed these substances, the *mishna* concludes with the broader category of *bekhol davar shehu mitkayem* — anything that endures. The *gemara* asks: *le'atuyei mai* — what does this phrase come to include beyond the specific items already enumerated? The answer draws on a teaching of Rabbi Chiyya: if one wrote a *get* with *mei tarya*, it is *kasher*. Rashi explains *mei tarya* as water in which a fruit has been soaked — not fruit juice squeezed directly from the fruit, but rather a liquid in which some dye-bearing plant material has been steeped, producing something closer to an infusion or rudimentary ink. Rashi identifies the relevant substance with *afatzim* — gallnuts — a material that, as we will see later in the *gemara*, plays a significant role in the preparation of *kelafim* as well.

A separate *braita* cites Rabbi Chiyya teaching that if a *get* was written with *avar* (lead), with *shachor* (which Rashi understands as coals or charcoal), or with this shoemaker's dye, it is *kasher*. These may seem obvious, as all three are clearly permanent substances, but their explicit enumeration serves a purpose that will become clearer as the *gemara* proceeds.

## Writing Over Writing

### Dyeing on Sikra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הַמַּעֲבִיר דְּיוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי סִיקְרָא, בַּשַּׁבָּת; רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ – חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם: אַחַת מִשּׁוּם כּוֹתֵב, וְאַחַת מִשּׁוּם מוֹחֵק. דְּיוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי דְּיוֹ, סִיקְרָא עַל גַּבֵּי סִיקְרָא – פָּטוּר. סִיקְרָא עַל גַּבֵּי דְּיוֹ – אָמְרִי לַהּ: חַיָּיב, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** that there was a related discussion about different types of writing: In the case of **one who passes ink over** letters written in **red dye on Shabbat, Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish both say** that he is **liable** to bring **two** sin-offerings for this: **One due to** violating the prohibition against **writing** on Shabbat, because he is now writing letters in ink, **and one due to** violating the prohibition against **erasing** on Shabbat, because as he writes he erases what is written in red dye. They also agree in the case of **ink** written **on top of ink,** or **red dye on top of red dye,** that according to everyone he is **exempt,** because he changed nothing with his writing. However, if he wrote with **red dye on top of ink,** then **some say** that he is **liable and some say** that he is **exempt.**

The *gemara* now introduces a conceptually rich discussion about inking over existing writing, using the laws of *Shabbat* as a comparative framework. The case is *ha-madyo al gabei sikra be-Shabbat*: someone on *Shabbat* applies black ink (*dyo*) on top of writing that had already been done in *sikra* — the reddish, permanent but less visually distinct substance. Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish both agree: *chayav shtayim* — one incurs two violations.

### Kotev vs Mochek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי לַהּ חַיָּיב – מוֹחֵק הוּא. אָמְרִי לַהּ פָּטוּר – מְקַלְקֵל הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains these opinions: **Some say** that he is **liable** because **it is erasing,** as he erases the higher-quality, original writing. **Some say** that he is **exempt** because **he is destroying** and one who acts destructively is exempt, as he has not performed planned, constructive labor on Shabbat.

The first violation is *mishum kotev* — writing. Even though the letters were already there in red, covering them with black ink constitutes a new and improved *ketiva*: black letters on white parchment are more crisp and legible than red ones, and so something qualitatively new has been produced. The second violation is *mishum mochek* — erasing. This is the striking part. The intuition might be that erasing means physically removing something; covering it up seems different. Yet the *gemara* holds that by applying black over red, one has in effect caused the red letters to disappear — and that counts as *mochek*.

This may be compared to the well-known discussion about lettering on cake on *Shabbat*. The *Maharach Or Zarua* discouraged eating it on account of *mochek*, though Rabbi Akiva Eiger called this position extreme. One objection is that eating the letters is *mitkalkel* — a destructive, not constructive, act — and also *eino mitkaven*. But one crucial difference is worth noting: eating the letters on a cake is a form of destruction, whereas inking over red letters with black is arguably an act of construction that simultaneously renders the red invisible. The classic *mochek* is *al menat lichtov* — erasing in order to write — because erasing makes the parchment beneath available for new writing. Here, by covering red with black, one is doing something analogous: the space that held red letters now holds black ones, and the surface has been put to productive use. That is the logic of the double liability.

*Tosafot* engages this question directly. In explaining why the *sikra-on-dyo* case (discussed below) might constitute *mochek al menat lichtov*, *Tosafot* argues that applying a red underlay actually improves the subsequent black layer — *ketiva elyona reshuma nikeret tefei mima'aseh tachtona levada* — the upper black writing will be even more legible when written over a red underlayer than if applied to bare parchment. This is a creative explanation, though its technical accuracy may be questioned.

### Red on Black

The case of *sikra al gabei deyo* — red applied over black — is more ambiguous. Here the *gemara* records a dispute: *amrei lah chayav ve'amrei lah patur*. Those who say *chayav* argue that this constitutes *mochek*: the black letters beneath have been rendered invisible. Those who say *patur* counter that this is *mekalkel* — a destructive act with no constructive purpose. The logic of *mochek* requires not merely that letters be made to disappear, but that their disappearance renders the parchment more usable for subsequent writing. When one covers black with red, the result is worse — red ink on top of black leaves the surface no more usable than before. The parchment has not become a blank slate ready for new writing; it now has red ink sitting atop black ink. One might respond that if the person *prefers* red, then covering black with red could be considered constructive — and in that case perhaps *chayav mishum kotev* would also apply — but the *gemara* leaves the matter as a dispute.

### Ink on Ink

The cases of *deyo al gabei deyo* and *sikra al gabei sikra* are more straightforward: if one adds fresh ink of the same color over existing ink of the same color, one is *patur*. Nothing qualitatively new has been produced. The letters were already there, the color is unchanged, and merely refreshing the same ink atop itself does not constitute a new act of *ketiva*. (If the underlying ink were fading, the analysis might differ — in that case one could argue that something genuinely new and improved has resulted — but the case under discussion assumes clearly legible, intact original writing.)

## Writing Over Writing…

### Pre-inked Signatures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מֵרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עֵדִים שֶׁאֵין יוֹדְעִים לַחְתּוֹם; מַהוּ שֶׁיִּכְתְּבוּ לָהֶם בְּסִיקְרָא, וְיַחְתְּמוּ כְּתָב עֶלְיוֹן? כְּתָב, אוֹ אֵינוֹ כְּתָב? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵינוֹ כְּתָב.
>
> **Translation:** This dispute pertains to the *halakhot* of Shabbat. With regard to the signing of documents, **Reish Lakish raised a dilemma before Rabbi Yoḥanan:** If there are **witnesses who do not know how to sign** their names, **what is** the *halakha*: May their names **be written for them with red dye,** and afterward they **will sign** on top of it in ink? The question is: Is **the upper writing,** which was not directly on the document but on top of other ink, considered to be **writing, or is it not writing? He said to him: It is not writing.** Consequently, this may not be done for witnesses who do not know how to sign.

With this *Shabbat* framework in view, the *gemara* transitions to a related but distinct question: what happens when witnesses who cannot sign their own names are offered pre-written letters in *sikra* over which they then trace in black ink? Does the result count as a valid *chatima*? The preceding discussion about *kotev* and *mochek* by *Shabbat* is the backdrop against which this question now unfolds.

## Signature Over Writing

### Witnesses Cannot Sign

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וַהֲלֹא לִימַּדְתָּנוּ רַבֵּינוּ, כְּתָב עֶלְיוֹן – כְּתָב לְעִנְיַן שַׁבָּת! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְכִי מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאָנוּ מְדַמִּין נַעֲשֶׂה מַעֲשֶׂה?!
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish **said to him: Didn't our master,** i.e., Rabbi Yoḥanan, **teach us** that the **upper writing** is considered to be **writing with regard to** the *halakhot* of **Shabbat?** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: And because we compare** the *halakhot* pertaining to bills of divorce to the *halakhot* of Shabbat, **shall we perform an action** and teach that a bill of divorce may be written in this manner?

Reish Lakish poses the question to Rabbi Yochanan: *edim she'ein yode'im lachtom* — witnesses who do not know how to sign — may one pre-write their names for them in *sikra* and have them sign over it with black ink? Is the resulting *chatima* valid?

Rabbi Yochanan responds: *ktav elyon eino ktav* — the upper layer of writing is not considered writing. The names were already present in *sikra*; tracing over them in ink does not constitute a genuine act of signing.

Reish Lakish presses back: but did our master not teach us that *ktav elyon ktav le'inyan Shabbat* — that by *Shabbat*, writing over existing writing is considered genuine *ketiva* and one is *chayav*? If it counts as writing for *Shabbat*, should it not count as writing for the *chatima* of a *get* as well?

### Analogy Limitation

Rabbi Yochanan's reply is striking: *veki mipnei she'anu m'damim na'aseh ma'aseh* — just because we can draw an analogy, does that mean we should act upon it? This is a remarkable methodological statement. The phrase *mipnei she'anu m'damim na'aseh ma'aseh* appears elsewhere in *halachic* literature as an expression of reluctance to translate a compelling argument into an actual ruling. Poskim sometimes construct an entire line of reasoning and then pull back with precisely this formulation — as if to say: the argument may be internally coherent, but I am not willing to cross the threshold from analysis to practical decision on its basis alone. But it is worth distinguishing two different contexts in which this reluctance can operate. In some cases, a *posek* builds an argument entirely within a single area of *halacha* and then hesitates to follow his own reasoning to its conclusion — that is a kind of general caution about the limits of one's own analysis. Here, however, something more specific is being said: we are making an analogy *across* two different areas of *halacha* — *hilkhot Shabbat* and *hilkhot gittin* — and Rabbi Yochanan is questioning whether the criteria operative in one domain can simply be imported into the other.

### Different Halakhic Areas

What might explain a difference in the definition of *ketiva* between *Shabbat* and the *chatima* of a *get*? One possible approach concerns the conceptual structure of *hilkhot Shabbat*. The framework of *avot* and *toldot* in *Shabbat* and the concept of *melekhet machshevet* together suggest that what matters in many cases is the *result* achieved rather than the precise mechanical process by which it was achieved. If the end result is that something of significance has been produced — if *nitkayma machshavto* — one may be *chayav*, regardless of the particular path taken. On this reading, inking over red letters to produce crisp black letters is a meaningful transformation: a new *cheftza d'ksav* has been created, even if the process was not a conventional act of writing from scratch. But for the *chatima* of witnesses on a *get* — or on any *shtar* — the question may be whether the witnesses performed a genuine *ma'aseh ketiva*. For a signature to be their signature, perhaps it must emerge from an act of writing something that was not already there, rather than from the act of filling in letters pre-formed by someone else. Writing on top of something that is already written may achieve a new result for the purposes of *Shabbat* while still not constituting a valid act of *chatima*. The *gemara* here leaves this distinction suggestive rather than fully resolved, noting that a more extended discussion of the *Shabbat* dimension will follow on the next *daf*.

## Illiterate Witnesses

### Stencil Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: עֵדִים שֶׁאֵין יוֹדְעִים לַחְתּוֹם; רַב אָמַר: מְקָרְעִין לָהֶם נְיָיר חָלָק, וּמְמַלְּאִים אֶת הַקְּרָעִים דְּיוֹ. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: בַּאֲבָר.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** that there was a dispute with regard to the following question: What should be done for **witnesses who do not know** how **to sign? Rav says: One tears blank paper for them,** meaning that a stencil of their names is fashioned from blank paper and placed on the bill of divorce. The witnesses then **fill in the gaps** with **ink** so that their names appear on the document. **And Shmuel says:** One first writes their names on the document **with lead,** and the witnesses write over those letters.

The *gemara* now presents the practical solutions developed by the Amoraim for witnesses who cannot sign their names. Rav's approach: *mekarim lahem niyar chalak u-memalim et ha-keraim b'dyo* — one prepares for them a stencil of blank paper and they fill the cutout letters with ink. *Tosafot* understands this straightforwardly as a physical stencil. Rashi offers a different reading: one makes an indentation in the *klaf* itself and the witnesses fill the indentation with ink.

### Lead Underwriting

Shmuel's approach is different: *be-avar* — one pre-writes the letters for them in lead, and they write over it. This is similar in structure to the *sikra* case discussed above, but lead poses an even more pronounced difficulty.

### Lead Writing Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּאֲבָר סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! וְהָתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: כְּתָבוֹ בַּאֲבָר, בִּשְׁחוֹר וּבְשִׁיחוֹר – כָּשֵׁר! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא – בַּאֲבָרָא, הָא – בְּמַיָּא דַאֲבָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Would it **enter your mind** to say that they actually write for them **with lead? But didn't Rabbi Ḥiyya teach:** If **he wrote it with lead, with coals, or** with **black paint,** then it is **valid?** It can be seen from this *baraita* that writing with lead is considered to be a valid form of writing, and, as Rabbi Yoḥanan taught, if one writes on top of other writing then the second writing is not accepted for the signing of documents. The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult: This,** that Shmuel allowed the witnesses to sign on top of lead writing, applies only when the first writing was done **with lead** itself, which is not considered to be writing on its own. **That,** Rabbi Ḥiyya's statement that it is valid for writing a bill of divorce, applies only when the first writing was done **using lead water,** i.e., water colored black with lead.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: *be-avar salka da'atach?* A *braita* of Rabbi Chiyya already established that writing with *avar* is *kasher* for a *get* — which means it constitutes valid *ksav*. If so, the letters are already written; how can the witnesses write over them and call it their *chatima*?

### Avar vs Mei Avara

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty by distinguishing between *avara* — actual lead rubbed directly onto the *klaf* — and *maya de-avara* — a liquid solution in which lead has been dissolved or processed, functioning as an ink. Rashi explains the distinction in terms that are illuminating. Lead rubbed onto parchment, like a pencil on paper, does not penetrate the surface; it remains on top and can be wiped away. A liquid solution, on the other hand, is absorbed into the *klaf* and becomes part of it, making it genuinely permanent. Rashi says explicitly: *be-avar leshafshef be-chatikha shel avar al ha-klaf umashchirah eino ksav* — rubbing an actual piece of lead across the parchment does not constitute *ksav*. The *maya de-avara*, by contrast, is *ksav*. This is a significant *chiddush*: something that leaves a visible mark — that is clearly *roshem* — and that does not disappear spontaneously, is nevertheless not considered *ksav* because it is not absorbed into the surface and is therefore erasable. This pushes back against the earlier reading of the *mishna* that did not seem to require deep permanence, and raises again the question of how precisely *mitkayyem* is to be defined.

### Gallnut Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר: בְּמֵי מֵילִין. וְהָתָנֵי רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: כְּתָבוֹ בְּמֵי טַרְיָא וְאַפְצָא – כָּשֵׁר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu said:** A solution would be to write the names for these witnesses **with gall water** [***mei milin***] and have them trace their names with ink. The Gemara challenges: **But didn't Rabbi Ḥanina teach** that if **he wrote** a bill of divorce **with *mei teriya* or** water in which **gallnuts** were soaked it is **valid?** As this is so, any additional writing over the writing in gall water should not be accepted as a valid signature.

Rav Abba proposes yet another approach: *be-mei milin* — using gallnut juice as the underlayer over which the witnesses sign. Rashi identifies *mei milin* with ground gallnuts soaked in water — an infusion that, in other contexts, functions as genuine ink. This creates an immediate problem: a *braita* of Rav Chanina teaches that writing a *get* with *mei tarya va-afatza* (gallnut water) is *kasher*, meaning it counts as valid *ksav*. If so, how can the *mei milin* underlayer be permissible?

### Gallnut Worked Klaf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דַּאֲפִיץ, הָא דְּלָא אֲפִיץ; שֶׁאֵין מֵי מֵילִין עַל גַּבֵּי מֵי מֵילִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. This** suggestion of Rabbi Abbahu, that the document be prepared with the names of the witnesses written with gall water, refers to a case where the parchment was processed **with gallnuts. That** statement of Rabbi Ḥanina, that gall water may be used on its own to write a bill of divorce, refers to a case where the parchment was **not** processed **with gallnuts.** The reason for the difference is **because gall water is not** permanent when applied **on top of gall water,** i.e., on parchment that was processed with gallnuts. Therefore, if the parchment was processed using gallnuts, the writing of the names of the witnesses, done with gall water, will not be permanent, and the witnesses may sign their names on top of that writing. When the *baraita* states that witnesses may sign their names with gall water, it is referring to a case where the parchment was not processed with gallnuts, so the gall water signature will be permanent.

The *gemara* resolves this with the distinction *ha de-afitz ha de-lo afitz* — it depends on whether the *klaf* was prepared (*afitz*) with gallnut juice. *Tosafot* explains this well: if the *klaf* itself was treated with a gallnut solution during its preparation, and one then writes on it with a gallnut-based ink, the ink will not leave a visible, distinguishable mark — *she'ein mei milin al gabei mei milin* — because the same substance has already saturated the surface. In this case, the underlayer effectively does not exist as a discernible *ksav*, and the witnesses' writing over it constitutes a genuine *chatima*. On a *klaf* that was not treated with gallnuts, the same gallnut solution would leave a permanent and visible mark, and writing over it would face the same problem as the *sikra* case.

This resolution also sheds light on the broader structure of the discussion. The *gemara* is consistent in assuming that whether a substance constitutes *ksav* for purposes of *get* writing is also the operative criterion for whether witnesses can write over an underlayer. That is itself a kind of *dimui* — an analogical application — but one the *gemara* accepts without hesitation. What the *gemara* refuses to accept is the analogical import of *Shabbat* liability into the definition of valid *chatima*. The distinction between these two analogical moves is itself instructive: the definition of *ksav* for *ketivat haget* presumably sets the threshold for the entire document, including the *chatima*, whereas *Shabbat* operates under a different conceptual architecture and different standards of what constitutes a meaningful act.

## Get vs Other Documents

### Saliva Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: בְּרוֹק. וְכֵן אוֹרִי לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְפָפָּא תּוֹרָאָה: בְּרוֹק. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּגִיטִּין, אֲבָל בִּשְׁטָרוֹת – לָא,
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** A solution would be to write the names for these witnesses **with saliva** and have them trace their names with ink. **And so Rav Pappa instructed Pappa the ox herder,** who did not know how to sign his name, that the court should write his name **with saliva** and he should trace it with ink. The Gemara comments: **This matter,** the leniency to have the witnesses trace their names, applies only **to bills of divorce,** as there is a concern that witnesses will not be found, and the woman will be unable to remarry. **However, for** financial **documents,** the Sages did **not** allow this, and one must instead find witnesses who know how to sign their names.

Rav Pappa's practical solution for illiterate witnesses is the most striking of all: *be-rok* — using saliva. He demonstrated this method to a man called Pappa the ox-driver, showing him how to moisten the surface with spit and have the witnesses sign over it.

### Gets Only

Immediately the *gemara* qualifies: *vehanei milei be-gitten* — all of these accommodations apply only to *gitten*, not to other documents. *Aval bi-shtaros lo.*

### Agunah vs Fraud

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּהָהוּא דַּעֲבַד עוֹבָדָא בִּשְׁאָר שְׁטָרוֹת, וְנַגְּדֵיהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא.
>
> **Translation:** It is told **that** there was **a certain person who performed an action** and allowed witnesses to trace their names **in** a case of **other documents,** which were not bills of divorce, and **Rav Kahana** ordered that **he be flogged** for doing so.

Rashi articulates the reason: *be-gitten mishum agunah ke-dilekamman* — for *gitten*, these leniencies are justified because of the *agunah* problem. The scenario is familiar: a husband is about to depart on a sea voyage, there are witnesses present but they do not know how to sign, and without a *get* the woman risks being left an *agunah* — perhaps forever unable to remarry. Even a *yibbum* situation is treated as an *agunah* case if obtaining the *get* would forestall the need to locate an unwilling *yavam*. In such circumstances the rabbis were willing to accommodate witnesses who could not sign in the ordinary manner. For monetary documents, however, no such urgency exists — one simply waits until proper witnesses are available. More importantly, allowing unrecognizable or stenciled signatures in monetary matters would open the door to catastrophic fraud: anyone could produce a *shtar* with block-letter signatures and claim it was signed by illiterate witnesses.

### Rav Kahana's Lashes

The seriousness with which this was treated is illustrated by a vivid episode: a man who applied these methods of assisted signing to non-*get* documents was brought before Rav Kahana, who administered *malkot* — lashes. Rav Kahana apparently felt that the integrity of the institution of *shtarot* was so fundamental, and the risk of abuse so acute, that even without a specific Torah prohibition, the use of these techniques in monetary documents warranted corporal punishment. This is not a case of *akhal cheilev* — a classic Torah transgression — but Rav Kahana evidently regarded it as a threat to the entire structure of commercial documentation that required the strongest possible deterrent.

## Literacy Requirements

### Tanna Kamma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב: עֵדִים שֶׁאֵין יוֹדְעִין לַחְתּוֹם, מְקָרְעִין לָהֶן נְיָיר חָלָק וּמְמַלְּאִים אֶת הַקְּרָעִים דְּיוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav:** In the case of **witnesses who do not know** how **to sign, one tears a blank** piece of **paper for them,** and they **fill** in **the gaps** with **ink.**

A *braita* consistent with Rav's position is cited: *eidim she'ein yod'im likhtom* — if witnesses do not know how to sign — *mekarim lahem niyar halak u-memalim et ha-keraim dyo* — one prepares a stencil and they fill it with ink. A further expansion: if the witnesses do not even know how to *read* the document, one may read it aloud for them (*korin lifneihem ve-khosmim*) and then have them sign with the stencil. The *tanna kamma* permits both accommodations — reading aloud by others and stenciled signatures — with no stated limitation to *gitten* specifically.

### Rabban Shimon Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּגִיטֵּי נָשִׁים; אֲבָל בְּשִׁחְרוּרֵי עֲבָדִים וּשְׁאָר כׇּל הַשְּׁטָרוֹת, אִם יוֹדְעִין לִקְרוֹת וְלַחְתּוֹם – חוֹתְמִין, וְאִם לָאו – אֵין חוֹתְמִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said: In what** case **is this statement said? For bills of divorce. However, for** bills **of manumission and** for **all other documents, if** the witnesses **know** how **to read and** how **to sign,** then **they sign, and if** they do **not** know how to read and sign **they do not sign.**

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagrees with the scope of this leniency. He agrees that both accommodations — reading aloud and stenciled signatures — are permitted, but limits them to *gittei nashim*. For *shichrurei avadim* (emancipation documents for slaves, which stand at the intersection of *mammonot* and personal status) and all other *shtarot*, he rules: if the witnesses know how to read and sign, they may sign; if not, they may not sign at all.

### Agunah Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל? שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל עֲגוּנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says: What is the reason of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel,** who permitted this only for bills of divorce? He holds that there is reason to be lenient with bills of divorce, **so that the daughters of Israel should not be deserted.** Requiring literate witnesses for bills of divorce could lead to a scenario in which a husband wishes to travel and wants to give his wife a bill of divorce in case he shall not return, but if he does not find literate witnesses he may leave without divorcing her, leaving her unable to remarry.

Rav Elazar explains Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's reasoning: *shelo yehu bnot yisrael agunot* — so that Jewish women should not be left as *agunot*. The formulation focuses on what makes *gitten* exceptional rather than on what makes other *shtarot* consequential, though both considerations could have been stated.

### Accepted Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל. וְרַב גַּמָּדָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא אָמַר: אֵין הֲלָכָה. וְאֶלָּא כְּמַאן, כְּרַבָּנַן?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: The *halakha* is in accordance with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. And Rav Gamda said in the name of Rava: The *halakha* is not** in accordance with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. The Gemara asks: **Rather, in accordance with whose** opinion is the *halakha*? Is it **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** and it is permitted to do this for any document?

Rava rules: *halacha ke-Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — the law follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's distinction. This is the accepted position.

### Rav Gamda Rejected

Rav Gamda reports in Rava's name a contradictory ruling: *ein halacha* — we do not follow Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, apparently allowing these leniencies for all *shtarot*. But the *gemara* rejects this position immediately by invoking Rav Kahana's episode. If Rav Kahana administered lashes to someone who used these methods with ordinary documents, it is clear that the institution was considered inviolable. It is inconceivable that Rav Gamda intended to permit what Rav Kahana saw fit to punish with such severity.

## Reading Verification

### Reading Allowed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הָהוּא דַּעֲבַד עוֹבָדָא בִּשְׁאָר שְׁטָרוֹת, וְנַגְּדֵיהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא! תַּרְגְּמַהּ אַקְּרִיאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But wasn't** there **a certain person who performed an action,** and allowed witnesses to trace their names **in** a case of **other documents** that were not bills of divorce, and **Rav Kahana** ordered that **he be flogged** for doing so? The Gemara answers: Rav Gamda **interpreted** the statement of Rava only **with regard to reading,** meaning that the *halakha* is in accordance with the Rabbis and not Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel only with regard to the question of whether other documents may be read to the witnesses, but not with regard to whether they may sign other documents by means of a stencil.

The *gemara* now resolves the apparent contradiction in Rav Gamda's ruling with the clarification *targema akria* — his statement was addressed only to the *kria* dimension, the reading aloud of a document. When Rav Gamda said the leniency applies to all *shtarot*, he meant only that one may have a document read aloud before signing it; he was not permitting stenciled or unrecognizable signatures in monetary documents. The *chatima* itself must still be the witness's own handwriting. The question now becomes how far the *akria* leniency extends and what safeguards must be in place.

### Trusted Readers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יְהוּדָה מִיצְטַעַר קָרֵי וְחָתֵים. אֲמַר לֵיהּ עוּלָּא: לָא צְרִיכַתְּ, דְּהָא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מָרָא דְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל – קָרוּ קַמֵּיהּ וְחָתֵים, וְרַב נַחְמָן – קָרוּ קַמֵּיהּ סָפְרֵי דַּיָּינֵי וְחָתֵים. וְדַוְוקָא רַב נַחְמָן וְסָפְרֵי דַּיָּינֵי, דְּאִית לְהוּ אֵימְתָא, אֲבָל רַב נַחְמָן וְסָפְרֵי אַחֲרִינֵי, סָפְרֵי דַּיָּינֵי וְאִינִישׁ אַחֲרִינָא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** It is told that **Rav Yehuda** was nearly blind in his old age. He could barely read and he **took great pains** to **read and sign** documents as a witness or a judge. **Ulla said to him: It is not necessary for you** to do this, **as** the court scribes **would read in the presence of Rabbi Elazar the master,** i.e., the halakhic authority **of Eretz Yisrael, and he would sign; and the court scribes would read** documents **before Rav Naḥman and he would sign;** and you may do the same. The Gemara notes: **And** this was done **specifically** in a case like that of **Rav Naḥman and the court scribes, as they had fear** of him because he was a great man and a judge to whom they were subservient. Therefore, there was no concern that they would not read it correctly. **However,** for **Rav Naḥman and other scribes,** or **the court scribes and another person, no;** the document may not be read to the witness, as the scribes may read it incorrectly to him.

Two stories illustrate how senior Amoraim handled this. Rav Yehuda, apparently as his eyesight declined, would strain and squint to read documents himself before signing — suggesting he held that even having someone else read a document aloud is not fully reliable, and one should read it independently if at all possible. Ulla challenged this position, pointing out that Rav Elazar — considered *marei de'eretz yisrael*, the master authority of the Land of Israel, renowned in particular for his expertise in matters of *ketamim* and careful visual examination — would regularly have others read documents aloud before signing them. Similarly, Rav Nachman had the scribes of the *dayanim* read documents before him and then signed.

### Trusted Scribes

But the *gemara* immediately pulls back: *vedavka Rav Nachman vesafrey dayanei* — it was specifically Rav Nachman together with the judges' own scribes, *de'it lehu eimta* — because they were afraid of him and would not misrepresent what was written. With other scribes, or with other individuals, even Rav Nachman's presence would not have been sufficient to guarantee accuracy.

### Deception Concern

This qualification reveals how seriously the *gemara* takes the potential for abuse even in the apparently benign case of having a document read aloud. After all, the document is sitting right there — in theory, one could always take it to someone else to verify. But in practice, witnesses typically do not do that: they hear what is read to them, they trust the reader, and they sign. A dishonest reader can therefore misrepresent the content of a document with remarkable ease. The example is not difficult to imagine: a lender presents a document to the witnesses, reads it as stating that a borrower owes him money, when in fact it says the reverse. The witnesses sign, the document is right there for all to see, and yet they have been deceived. Because of this acute risk, the *gemara* restricts the *akria* leniency even for *shtarot* to circumstances where the reader can be trusted with near-absolute certainty — effectively limiting it to someone in a formal, accountable relationship with the signatory, like the judges' own scribes who feared the consequences of deceiving Rav Nachman.

## Foreign Languages

### Persian Documents

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא, כִּי הֲוָה אָתֵי לְקַמֵּיהּ שְׁטָרָא פָּרְסָאָה דַּעֲבִיד בְּעַרְכָּאוֹת שֶׁל גּוֹיִם, מַקְרֵי לְהוּ לִשְׁנֵי גוֹיִם זֶה שֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי זֶה בְּמֵסִיחַ לְפִי תּוּמּוֹ, וּמַגְבֵּי בֵּיהּ מִמְּשַׁעְבְּדִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara recounts the behavior of another *amora* who had documents read to him: **When documents** that were written in **Persian** and **that were produced in gentile courts came before Rav Pappa,** who did not know how to read Persian, he would **have it be read by two gentiles, each one not in the presence of the other** and in a way that each one would **speak offhandedly,** without knowing that they were giving testimony. Once he clarified what was written in the document **he would collect** payment **with it** even **from liened** property that had been sold, as he held that such a document is entirely valid with regard to monetary law.

The *gemara* now turns to the case of documents in a foreign language. Rav Pappa would sometimes receive Persian *shtarot* that had been produced in non-Jewish courts (*arka'ot shel goyim*). Recall that an early *mishna* already established that documents executed in non-Jewish courts are valid as evidence that a transaction took place, even if they do not satisfy the full technical requirements of a Jewish *shtar*. But how could Rav Pappa know what was written in a document he could not read?

### Double Translation

His solution: *makrey lehu lishney kutim zeh shelo bifney zeh* — he would have two non-Jews each translate the document independently, not in each other's presence. If their translations matched, and they were *bemasiach lepi tumo* — casual, uninvolved individuals who had no prior knowledge of the case — there was no plausible explanation for the agreement other than that both were accurately rendering what the document actually said. Two strangers, unaware of the particulars of the dispute, could not coincidentally produce identical mistranslations.

### Liens Recognized

Rav Pappa's confidence in this method was such that he would collect even from *meshabedi* — property that was subject to a lien — on the basis of such a translated document. The concept of *meshabedi* is significant: a lien on a debtor's property attaches, and binds even subsequent purchasers of that property, only when there is a *shtar* that constitutes a matter of public record. Rav Pappa was willing to treat a properly verified Persian document as satisfying this standard — a striking endorsement of the method's reliability.

### Jewish Persian Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אָמַר לִי רַב הוּנָא בַּר נָתָן, הָכִי אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: הַאי שְׁטָרָא פָּרְסָאָה דַּחֲתִימִי עֲלֵיהּ סָהֲדִי יִשְׂרָאֵל, מַגְבֵּינַן בֵּיהּ מִמְּשַׁעְבְּדִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** that **Rav Huna bar Natan said to me** that **so said Ameimar:** With regard to **this Persian document, on which Jews are signed as witnesses,** the court is able to **collect** payment **with it,** even **from liened** property that had been sold.

Rav Ashi reports a related ruling in the name of Ameimar, via Rav Huna bar Natan: a Persian *shtar* written by Jews and bearing Jewish witnesses is *kasher* and supports collection from *meshabedi*. This is distinct from the *arka'ot* case — there we were dealing with documents from non-Jewish courts; here the document is Jewish in origin, executed by Jewish parties with Jewish witnesses, but written in Persian.

### Forgery Prevention

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא לָא יָדְעִי לְמִיקְרֵי! בִּדְיָדְעִי. וְהָא בָּעֵינַן כְּתָב שֶׁאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְהִזְדַּיֵּיף, וְלֵיכָּא! בְּדַאֲפִיצָן. וְהָא בָּעֵינַן ״צָרִיךְ לַחֲזוֹר מֵעִנְיָנוֹ שֶׁל שְׁטָר בְּשִׁיטָה אַחֲרוֹנָה״, וְלֵיכָּא! בִּדְמַהְדַּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But they don't know how to read,** as most Jews did not read Persian. The Gemara answers: Ameimar's statement applies **when they know** how to read Persian. The Gemara questions how the court can rely upon such a document: **But don't we require** all legal documents to be written in **a writing that cannot be forged; and it is not** so in documents produced by Persians, as the Persians were not particular about this when writing their legal documents. The Gemara answers: His statement applies **in** a case where the paper of the documents was processed **with gall.** Consequently, it is not possible to forge the writing. **But we require** a document **to recap the** essential **topic of the document in its last line; and it is not** so in the case of Persian documents. The Gemara answers: Ameimar's statement applies **in** a case **where** the document **recapped** the essential topic of the document in the final line.

The *gemara* raises an objection: does this not require *ketav she'eino yakhol lehizdayef* — writing that cannot be forged or altered? Documents produced outside the formal Jewish legal system might be written with erasable materials, allowing subsequent alteration of amounts or names.

### Gallnut Protection

The *gemara* answers: *bi-defitzna* — we are speaking of a *klaf* that was treated with gallnut (*afitz*), which prevents erasure. The gallnut preparation bonds with the parchment in such a way that ink written on it cannot be erased without leaving visible evidence of tampering. In such a case, even a document written in Persian by a community less familiar with the full technical requirements of Jewish document law is protected against the specific vulnerability of forgery.

A further objection: a valid *shtar* must repeat the essence of its content in the final line (*tzakich lachzor me-inyano shel shtar be-shitah achronah*). Does such a Persian document satisfy this requirement? The *gemara* answers that in the case under discussion, *ahadreih* — it did repeat the key content in the final line. So the document is entirely congruent with a proper Jewish *shtar* in every respect except its language.

### Any Language Valid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – דְּכׇל לָשׁוֹן כָּשֵׁר?! תְּנֵינָא: גֵּט שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ עִבְרִית וְעֵדָיו יְוָנִית, יְוָנִית וְעֵדָיו עִבְרִית – כָּשֵׁר! אִי מֵהַהִיא, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּגִיטִּין, אֲבָל בִּשְׁאָר שְׁטָרוֹת – לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if Ameimar's statement applies only when all these conditions are met, then **what is he teaching us, that** a document that is properly written in **any language** is **valid? We** already **learned** in a mishna (87b): In the case of **a bill of divorce that he wrote** in **Hebrew and its witnesses** signed in **Greek,** or one that he wrote in **Greek and its witnesses** signed in **Hebrew,** it is **valid.** If this is written in the mishna, Ameimar's statement would not simply repeat it. The Gemara answers: This cannot serve as a clear proof, because **if** the only source for this *halakha* were **from that** mishna then **I would say: This statement** applies only **for bills of divorce,** where the Sages were lenient so that a woman would not be unable to remarry. **However, for other documents, no.** Consequently, Ameimar **teaches us** that other types of documents are valid if they are written in other languages as well.

This prompts the *gemara* to ask: *ela mai ka mashma lan* — what then is the ruling actually teaching us? The answer: *de-khol lashon kasher* — a *shtar* may be written in any language. But is that not already established by the *mishna* concerning a *get* written in Hebrew with Greek witnesses, or in Greek with Hebrew witnesses, which is *kasher*? The *gemara* responds: from that *mishna* alone, one might have concluded *hanei milei be-gittin* — that the latitude for different languages is a feature of *gitten* specifically, where the rabbis are more lenient across a range of dimensions, but that *shtarot* might require Hebrew or Aramaic. The ruling of Ameimar therefore adds *ka mashma lan* — that in *shtarot* as well, language is not a determinative criterion; what matters is that the document meet all the substantive requirements of a valid *shtar*.

To summarize the conclusions that emerge from this entire discussion: for *gitten*, the rabbis permitted both having the document read aloud to the witnesses and the use of stenciled or assisted signatures. For *shtarot*, stenciled signatures are absolutely prohibited — the risk of fraud is too great, as demonstrated by Rav Kahana's willingness to administer lashes. Having a document read aloud before signing is permitted in *shtarot*, but only in circumstances where the reliability of the reader can be established with near-certainty. And the language in which a document is written — whether Hebrew, Aramaic, Persian, or Greek — does not in itself determine the document's validity, provided all other substantive requirements are met.
