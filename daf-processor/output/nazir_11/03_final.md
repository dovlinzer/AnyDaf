# Nazir 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Partial Nezirus Intent

### Beis Hillel/Shammai

Daf 11 opens by returning to the terrain of ordinary *nezirus* after the extended discussion of unusual formulations — including the debate of *Beis Hillel*, *Beis Shammai*, and *Rebbi Yehuda*'s interpretation of *Beis Shammai* — regarding what it means to declare oneself a *nazir* from something that is not itself one of the prohibited categories of *nezirus*. The question there was whether such a declaration constitutes full *nezirus*, no *nezirus* at all, or merely a prohibition on the specific item named. With that backdrop in place, the *Mishnah* now turns to more standard cases.

### Cup of Wine Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מָזְגוּ לוֹ אֶת הַכּוֹס, וְאָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר מִמֶּנּוּ״ — הֲרֵי זֶה נָזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **they poured one a cup** of wine **and he said: I am hereby a nazirite** and therefore will refrain **from it, he is a** full-fledged **nazirite** who must observe all the *halakhot* of naziriteship.

The first *Mishnah* on this daf presents a person who is offered a cup of wine — *mazgu lo es hakos* — and declares, *hareini nazir mimenu*, "I am a *nazir* from it." The word *mimenu* is somewhat ambiguous: it could mean "from wine" generically, or it could mean "from this particular cup." One might therefore have thought that the person was merely declining this specific serving rather than genuinely accepting *nezirus*. Nevertheless, the ruling is that such a person is a *nazir*.

### Partial = Full

This ruling reflects a principle established in an earlier *Mishnah*: accepting *nezirus* with respect to any one component of *nezirus* — wine, haircuts, or *tumas meis* — constitutes full *nezirus*. The position of the *Chachamim* is that *nezirus* is a package deal; if you accept any piece of it, you have accepted all of it. Since this declaration concerns wine, which is a genuine element of *nezirus*, even *Beis Hillel* agrees that the person becomes a *nazir* in full.

It is worth noting that had the person said *hareini nazir min hakos hazeh* — "I am a *nazir* from this cup" — the result might well have been different, since that formulation makes explicit that the reference is to a particular vessel rather than to wine as such. The formulation *mimenu* is general enough to be read as referring to wine, which is why the ruling applies.

## Drunk Woman Story

### Woman's Declaration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאִשָּׁה אַחַת שֶׁהָיְתָה שִׁיכּוֹרָה, וּמָזְגוּ לָהּ אֶת הַכּוֹס, וְאָמְרָה: ״הֲרֵינִי נְזִירָה מִמֶּנּוּ״. אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: לֹא נִתְכַּוְּונָה זוֹ אֶלָּא לוֹמַר ״הֲרֵי הוּא עָלַי קׇרְבָּן״.
>
> **Translation:** **An incident** occurred **with regard to a certain woman who was intoxicated** from wine, **and they poured a cup for her and she said: I am hereby a nazirite** and therefore will refrain **from it. The Sages said: This** woman **did not intend** to accept naziriteship **but** rather, meant **to say: It is hereby** forbidden **to me** as **an offering.** She vowed against deriving benefit from that cup alone, since she did not want to drink any more.

The *Mishnah* then presents a contrasting case: *maaseh be-isha achas she-haysah shikorah* — there was a woman who was drunk, and people were offering her another cup of wine. She declared, *hareini nezirah mimenu*, "I am a *nezirah* from it" — the very same formulation used in the previous case.

### Limited Intent

Despite the identical language, the *Chachamim* rule that she did not become a *nezirah*. Their reasoning is that *lo nis-kavnah zo ela lomar harei hu alai k'korban* — she merely intended to forbid this specific cup to herself as a *neder*, as a *konam*. She was not accepting *nezirus* from wine generally; she meant only to refuse this particular cup.

### Contradiction Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַעֲשֶׂה לִסְתּוֹר?! אָמְרַתְּ רֵישָׁא — הֲרֵי זֶה נָזִיר, וַהֲדַר תָּנֵי: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאִשָּׁה אַחַת, אַלְמָא בְּהַאי הוּא דְּאָסוּר, הָא יֵינָא אַחֲרִינָא — שְׁרֵי!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: Was **an incident** cited **to contradict** the previous ruling? **You said in the first clause** of the mishna that if one said that he is hereby a nazirite and therefore will refrain from a cup that was poured for him, then **he is a nazirite. And** the *tanna* **then teaches: An incident** occurred **with regard to a certain woman** who said: I am hereby a nazirite and therefore will refrain from it, which the Sages interpreted as a vow rendering the cup forbidden to her like an offering. **Apparently it is** only **with regard to this** cup of wine **that is forbidden** to her; **consequently,** any **other wine is permitted,** and she is not a nazirite.

This immediately raises a sharp question: why is the context of the second case any different from the first? In both instances, the identical phrase *mimenu* was used. The *Gemara* presses this directly: *maaseh listor*? Is the story being brought in order to contradict the ruling just stated? You declared at the outset that *harei zeh nazir*, and then you tell us of a case where the same words yield the opposite result?

Before examining the *Gemara*'s resolution, it is worth pausing on a broader question raised by this passage. One might invoke the principle of *perushan le-hakel*, familiar from *Nedarim*, according to which ambiguous *nedarim* are read leniently. If *nezirus* is a species of *neder*, should the same principle not apply here — permitting the person to explain what he or she actually meant? The *Gemara* throughout this tractate has largely not pursued that avenue; rather than asking the declarant what was intended, the discussion proceeds to determine meaning objectively from context and language. The principle of *piv v'libo shavin* — that words and intent should align — is one the *Gemara* is aware of, but its actual deployment in these *sugyos* is more limited than one might expect. The question of when subjective intent can override the plain import of words, and when we say *ein piv v'libo shavin* renders a declaration non-binding, will resurface explicitly later in this *daf*. For now, the *Gemara* proceeds by asking not what she meant to say, but what, objectively, her situation tells us about what she was saying.

### Chasorei Mechasra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: מָזְגוּ לוֹ אֶת הַכּוֹס, וְאָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר מִמֶּנּוּ״ — הֲרֵי זֶה נָזִיר. וְאִם שִׁיכּוֹר הוּא, וְאָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר מִמֶּנּוּ״ — אֵינוֹ נָזִיר. מַאי טַעְמָא — כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי קׇרְבָּן״ הוּא, וְכִי תֵּימָא: לֵימָא הָכִי? סָבַר: מַיְיתִין לִי אַחֲרִינָא וּמְצַעֲרִן לִי, אֵימָא לְהוּ הָא מִילְּתָא דִּפְסִיקָא לְהוּ. וּמַעֲשֶׂה נָמֵי בְּאִשָּׁה אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The mishna **is incomplete and is teaching the following:** If **they poured a cup** of wine **for one and he said: I am hereby a nazirite** and therefore will refrain **from it, he is a nazirite. And if he is intoxicated** and they pour a cup of wine for him **and he said: I am hereby a nazirite** and therefore will refrain **from it, he is not a nazirite. What is the reason** for this? **He is** considered to be **like one who said:** This cup **is** forbidden **to me** like **an offering.** The Gemara asks: **And if you would say** that this was his meaning, **let him say so** explicitly; why would he say: I am hereby a nazirite and therefore will refrain from it? The reason is because **he maintains:** If I specify this cup, **they will bring me another** cup **and aggravate me** with it. It is better that **I say to them this statement, which is definitive to them,** and they will understand that I do not want to drink any more wine. The mishna continues: **And an incident also** occurred **with regard to a certain woman** who was intoxicated and took this vow, and the Sages explained her statement accordingly.

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction with the technique of *chasorei mechasra vehachi katani* — the *Mishnah* is missing a clause and must be read as though it contained an explanatory transition. The missing clause establishes that when an ordinary person, in an ordinary social setting, is offered a cup of wine and says *hareini nazir mimenu*, that person is a *nazir*; but if the person was drunk at the time and made the same declaration, the result is different.

## Drunk Woman Logic

### Strong Language Tactic

The *Gemara* then explains why the drunk person's declaration is treated differently: *keman de'amar harei alai korban hu* — it is as if the person said this cup is forbidden to me like a *korban*. But one might ask: if that is what the person meant, why not simply say those words? The *Gemara* answers: *savar meitina achrina umitza'aru li* — the drunk person is thinking that if he or she says merely "this cup is forbidden to me," those around will simply continue offering wine. The person wants to communicate something categorical and absolute, something that will make clear that no further pressure should be applied. By invoking the language of a *nazir*, the person is deploying a rhetorical tactic: *eima lehu hamiltha*, saying something that signals total abstention, *depaska lehu*, cutting off the attempt entirely.

### Social Pressure

*Tosafos* elaborates on this reasoning helpfully. The passage describes a social reality: *derech b'nei adam l'haftzir b'shikor she'yishteh* — it is common practice to pressure a drunk person to keep drinking. People find it amusing to encourage further intoxication, and the drunk person, who genuinely does not wish to drink more, cannot make that refusal stick by merely declining a particular cup. The only language powerful enough to end the pressure is the language of *nezirus*, which conveys an absolute and apparently binding commitment. *Tosafos* concludes: *aval k'she'eino shikor ein derech l'haftziro* — but when a person is not drunk, there is no social pressure of this kind. If a sober person declines a cup of wine and no one is pressing the matter, there is no need to invoke the language of *nezirus* merely to get people to back off.

### Sober Contrast

This contrast explains the ruling in the first part of the *Mishnah*. When a sober person, who is under no special pressure to drink, uses the language of *nezirus* in response to being offered a cup of wine, that person is genuinely invoking the concept. There is no ulterior communicative purpose being served. The heavy word carries its actual weight, and since the person is declaring *nezirus* from wine — a genuine component of *nezirus* — the full status applies. *Tosafos* states this explicitly: *v'l'kach omer d'nazir mikulehu* — when you use the strong word *nazir* without any social pressure compelling you toward theatrical language, you really mean to be a *nazir* from wine, and being a *nazir* from wine pulls along the rest.

## Conditions on Torah

### Conditional Declaration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֱהֵא שׁוֹתֶה יַיִן וּמִיטַּמֵּא לְמֵתִים״ — הֲרֵי זֶה נָזִיר, וְאָסוּר בְּכוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** If one says: **I am hereby a nazirite on the condition that I will** be allowed to **drink wine and may become** ritually **impure from corpses,** i.e., he wishes to be a nazirite only with respect to the growth of his hair, **he is a** full-fledged **nazirite and is prohibited from** engaging in **all of the** behaviors forbidden to a nazirite, including consuming products of the vine and contracting impurity from a corpse.

The next *Mishnah* presents a new category of case. A person declares: *hareini nazir al mnas she'ehei shoteh yayin umitamei lameitim* — "I will be a *nazir* on the condition that I may still drink wine" or "on the condition that I may still become *tamei* through contact with the dead."

### Conditions Void

The ruling is unambiguous: *harei zeh nazir v'assur bekulan* — this person is a *nazir* and is forbidden in all the relevant respects. None of the conditions are honored.

There are two ways to understand why. The first approach, and the one the *Gemara* will confirm in the name of Ravina, is that the person has accepted full *nezirus* and then attempted to attach conditions to a Torah-mandated status. This triggers the principle of *matneh al mah shekatuv ba-Torah*: any stipulation that contradicts what the Torah requires is void. The person has genuinely accepted the *nezirus*; we simply discard the conditions. The second approach reads *al mnas* not as "with a condition upon" the whole of *nezirus* but as equivalent to *chutz* — "except for." Under this reading, the person was attempting to accept partial *nezirus*, which brings us back to the earlier debate about whether partial acceptance constitutes full *nezirus*. The *Gemara* will return to both of these frameworks.

## Mistaken Understanding

### Unaware of Wine Ban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״יוֹדֵעַ אֲנִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ נְזִירוּת, אֲבָל אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁהַנָּזִיר אָסוּר בְּיַיִן״ — הֲרֵי זֶה אָסוּר, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** If one stated a vow of naziriteship and then said: **I know that there is naziriteship, but I do not know that a nazirite is prohibited from wine, he is prohibited** in all the prohibitions of naziriteship. **But Rabbi Shimon permits** him, since he holds that naziriteship takes effect only if the person accepts all the relevant prohibitions.

The *Mishnah* then introduces two further cases involving mistaken understanding. In the first, the person declares *nezirus* but states: *yodea ani sheyesh nezirot aval eini yodea shehanazir assur beyayin* — "I know that the institution of *nezirus* exists, but I did not know that a *nazir* is forbidden wine." Perhaps, he imagined, *nezirus* is simply about growing one's hair long and nothing more.

### Chachamim vs. Shimon

The ruling of the *Chachamim* is *harei zeh assur* — this person is bound as a *nazir*. Ignorance of one of the *halachos* does not void the acceptance. *Rabbi Shimon*, however, rules that the person is not a *nazir* at all.

### Expected Dispensation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״יוֹדֵעַ אֲנִי שֶׁהַנָּזִיר אָסוּר בְּיַיִן, אֲבָל סָבוּר הָיִיתִי שֶׁחֲכָמִים מַתִּירִין לִי מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵין אֲנִי יָכוֹל לִחְיוֹת אֶלָּא בְּיַיִן״, אוֹ ״מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֲנִי קוֹבֵר אֶת הַמֵּתִים״ — הֲרֵי זֶה מוּתָּר, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹסֵר.
>
> **Translation:** If one said: **I know that a nazirite is prohibited from wine, but I thought that the Sages** would **permit me** to drink wine **because I cannot live without wine, or:** I thought that the Sages would allow me to contract impurity from corpses **because I bury the dead, he is permitted** and the vow of naziriteship does not take effect, **but Rabbi Shimon prohibits** him.

The third case involves a different kind of mistake. Here the person knew full well that a *nazir* is forbidden wine, but declares: *savur hayiti she-Chachamim matirin li* — "I assumed that the rabbis would grant me a dispensation," either because he is someone who cannot function without wine, or because his livelihood as a gravedigger requires him to become *tamei* regularly.

### Positions Reversed

Remarkably, the positions of the *Chachamim* and *Rabbi Shimon* are now reversed. In this third case, the *Chachamim* rule *muttar* — this person is not bound as a *nazir* — while *Rabbi Shimon* rules *assur*. And lest there be any confusion: *muttar* here means the person does not become a *nazir* at all. A partial *nezirus* is not an option except in the case of *Shimshon*.

This reversal is genuinely striking, and the *Gemara* devotes considerable attention to explaining it.

## Partial Acceptance L (II)

### Partial Nezirus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְלִפְלוֹג נָמֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרֵישָׁא! אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: חָלוּק הָיָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אַף בְּרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: In the second case of the mishna, where one states that he did not know that the prohibitions of naziriteship include wine, Rabbi Shimon says that the naziriteship does not take effect because he did not include all of the prohibitions in his vow. The Gemara therefore asks: **Let Rabbi Shimon disagree** with the first *tanna* **in the first clause** in the mishna **as well,** where one said: I am hereby a nazirite on the condition that I may drink wine. **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said:** In fact, **Rabbi Shimon disagrees even** with the first *tanna* **in the first clause** of the mishna, and the mishna's statement: Rabbi Shimon permits him, is referring to both clauses.

Before addressing the reversal directly, the *Gemara* first asks why there is apparently no debate regarding the first case — the person who says *hareini nazir al mnas she'ehei shoteh yayin*. If *Rabbi Shimon* holds that when a person did not realize wine was included, the *nezirus* is not binding — because the person was in effect only accepting a partial *nezirus* — why does *Rabbi Shimon* not say the same when the person explicitly stipulates that wine should be excluded?

The answer given by *Rebbi Yehoshua ben Levi* is that *Rabbi Shimon* in fact does dispute that first case as well; it simply was not recorded separately in the *Mishnah*.

## Conditions on Torah (II)

### Matneh al Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: בְּרֵישָׁא לָא פְּלִיג רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, מַאי טַעְמָא — מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ מַתְנֶה עַל מַה שֶּׁכָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה, וְכׇל הַמַּתְנֶה עַל מַה שֶּׁכָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה — תְּנָאוֹ בָּטֵל. וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמַר לָךְ: [הַאי] ״עַל מְנָת״ — כְּחוּץ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Rabbi Shimon does not disagree** with the first *tanna* **in the first clause** of the mishna. **What is the reason** for that? It is **because one was stipulating counter to that which is written in the Torah** by attempting to limit an explicit Torah law, **and** with regard to **anyone who stipulates counter to that which is written in the Torah, his stipulation is void.** The statement: I am hereby a nazirite, remains valid, and he is therefore a nazirite in all regards. Conversely, **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi** could have **said to you: This** phrase: **On the condition,** does not mean he accepts upon himself all the *halakhot* of naziriteship and then adds a stipulation counter to that which is written in the Torah; rather, it is **considered like** he said: **Apart** from. It is as though he stated from the outset that he will be a nazirite apart from one particular aspect. According to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, Rabbi Shimon holds that since he is not trying to stipulate alternative *halakhot* for full naziriteship but is instead accepting a partial naziriteship, his vow takes effect.

Ravina, however, takes a different position: *Rabbi Shimon* genuinely concedes in the first case. The reason is *matneh al mah shekatuv ba-Torah*: the person has accepted the full *nezirus* and then attempted to impose conditions on a Torah-defined status. The conditions are simply voided, and the full *nezirus* stands. This is distinct from the second case, where the person never understood or accepted the full package in the first place. As *Tosafos* puts it: *d'k'sh'amar hareini nazir chal alav nezirus l'chol mili* — when you say *hareini nazir*, full *nezirus* takes effect immediately on all its components; *v'ki amar al mnas matil tnai al mah shekatuv ba-Torah u'vatel ha-tnai* — and when you then say "on condition," you are attempting to make a stipulation against what the Torah prescribes, and that stipulation is void.

By contrast, in the second case, what the person was internally accepting was a *nezirus* that did not include the wine prohibition — because that is all they knew *nezirus* to be. That is not a case of full acceptance plus a void condition; it is a case of genuinely partial acceptance. And the question of what partial acceptance amounts to is the subject of the ongoing debate.

## Partial Acceptance L (II)

### Partial Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרָבִינָא: אָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֱהֵא שׁוֹתֶה יַיִן וּמִיטַּמֵּא לְמֵתִים״ — הֲרֵי זֶה נָזִיר וְאָסוּר בְּכוּלָּן, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מַתְנֶה עַל מַה שֶּׁכָּתוּב בְּתוֹרָה, וְכׇל הַמַּתְנֶה עַל מַה שֶּׁכָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה — תְּנָאוֹ בָּטֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: It **is taught** in the *Tosefta* (*Nazir* 2:2) **in accordance with** the opinion **of Ravina.** If one **said: I am hereby a nazirite on the condition that I will be allowed to drink wine and may become** ritually **impure from corpses, he is a nazirite and is prohibited from all of them because he stipulates counter to that which is written in the Torah, and** with regard to **anyone who stipulates counter to that which is written in the Torah, his stipulation is void.**

With that foundation in place, the *Gemara* turns to explaining why *Rabbi Shimon* and the *Chachamim* reverse their positions between the second and third cases. The key is to appreciate that the second case — where the person did not know wine was prohibited — is a case of partial *nezirus*. In the person's own conception, *nezirus* meant only the hair-related practices; in accepting *nezirus*, that is all they were accepting.

This brings us back to the debate that was introduced in an earlier *Mishnah*. If a person says *hareini nazir min ha-yayin*, or any formulation indicating acceptance of only one component of *nezirus*, the *Chachamim* hold that accepting any piece of *nezirus* constitutes accepting the whole thing — it is a package deal. *Rabbi Shimon*, by contrast, holds *ad sheyidor mikulan* — unless you explicitly accept all components, you have accepted nothing; a partial package counts as nothing.

So in the second case, the person was in their mind accepting partial *nezirus*. The *Chachamim* say partial acceptance equals full *nezirus*, and therefore the person is bound. *Rabbi Shimon* says partial acceptance equals nothing, and therefore the person goes free.

### Hitur Request

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״יוֹדֵעַ אֲנִי שֶׁהַנָּזִיר אָסוּר בְּיַיִן״. וְהָאָמְרַתְּ רֵישָׁא אָסוּר, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר! אֵימָא נָמֵי: הֲרֵי זֶה אָסוּר, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם לָא תֵּיפוֹךְ. הָתָם
>
> **Translation:** § The final clause of the mishna teaches that if one says: **I know that a nazirite is prohibited from** drinking **wine** but I thought that the Sages would permit me to drink, the Rabbis say that the vow is void, but Rabbi Shimon disagrees. The Gemara asks: **But didn't you say in the earlier clause** of the mishna that with regard to one who says: But I did not know that a nazirite is prohibited from wine, the first *tanna* holds that he is **prohibited and Rabbi Shimon permits** him? What is the difference between the two cases? The Gemara answers: Emend the text and **say** in the final clause **as well: He is prohibited, and Rabbi Shimon permits** him. **And if you wish, say** instead: **Actually, do not reverse** the opinions, and leave the text as it is. Instead, distinguish between the two cases, as **there**

Now the third case. Here the person knew exactly what *nezirus* entailed. What they believed was that they would receive a *heter* — a dispensation — because of their particular circumstances. The *Gemara*'s most elegant explanation is that this case involves not the process of *kabbalah* — acceptance — but the process of *hatara* — dissolution. The person in fact accepted full *nezirus*, and now, in effect, is coming before the *Chachamim* and seeking to be released from one component of it: the prohibition on *tumas meis*, because they are a gravedigger.

The question then becomes: if someone seeks *hatara* — dissolution of a *neder* — on the basis of *charata* regarding only one element of the *neder*, does that partial *charata* dissolve the entire *neder*, or must *charata* attach to the whole?

### Parallel Processes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישָׁא, כְּגוֹן דִּנְזַר מֵחֲדָא, לְרַבָּנַן דְּאָמְרִי דַּאֲפִילּוּ לֹא נָזַר אֶלָּא מֵאַחַת מֵהֶן — הָוֵי נָזִיר, וְאָסוּר. לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָמַר עַד שֶׁיַּזִּיר מִכּוּלָּם — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** in **the earlier clause,** where one said: I did not know that a nazirite is prohibited from drinking wine, that is a case **where he vowed naziriteship** with regard to only **one** of the prohibitions. The difference of opinion is as follows: **According to the Rabbis, who say that even if he vowed naziriteship** with regard to **only one of the** prohibitions of naziriteship **he is** nevertheless **a nazirite,** in that case **he is prohibited** by all of them, despite not knowing about the prohibition against consuming wine. **According to Rabbi Shimon** who **said** he is not a nazirite **unless he takes a vow of naziriteship** with regard to **all of them,** he is **permitted.**

This explanation makes sense at the level of process rather than end-state. One might wonder: if the *Chachamim* say that accepting the wine component alone makes one a *nazir* for everything, why should expressing *charata* over the *tumas meis* component dissolve the wine component as well? Shouldn't the wine component remain in force, and a *nazir* who is still obligated on wine is still a *nazir* in full? The answer is that we are looking not at what formal obligations happen to remain, but at the act itself: the act of *kabbalah* can be partial and still be valid, and the act of *hatara* can likewise be partial and still be valid, because the two processes are structurally analogous.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֵיפָא דִּנְדַר מִכּוּלְּהוּ, וְאִיתְּשִׁיל מֵחֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** Conversely, **the last clause** addresses one who **vowed** naziriteship with regard to **all of them,** thereby accepting upon himself all the *halakhot* of naziriteship, **and** now wishes **to request** dissolution **of one** of the prohibitions, thinking that a halakhic authority can dissolve one aspect of his naziriteship.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבָּנַן דְּאָמְרִי אֲפִילּוּ לֹא נָזַר אֶלָּא מֵאַחַת מֵהֶן הָוֵי נָזִיר, כִּי מִתְּשִׁיל מֵחֲדָא מִינַּיְיהוּ — אִישְׁתְּרִי. לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָמַר עַד שֶׁיַּזִּיר מִכּוּלָּם, כִּי מִתְּשִׁיל נָמֵי מֵהָהוּא, עַד דְּמִתְּשִׁיל מִכּוּלְּהוּ. מִשּׁוּם הָכִי קָתָנֵי: וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹסֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **According to the Rabbis, who say that even if he vowed naziriteship** with regard to **only one of the** prohibitions of naziriteship **he is** nevertheless **a nazirite,** in that case too, **when he requests** of a halakhic authority to dissolve **one of them, he is permitted** to engage in all of the behaviors forbidden to a nazirite. Just as the acceptance of one part of naziriteship causes one to be bound by all the *halakhot* of naziriteship, dissolution of one element of naziriteship nullifies the entire naziriteship. **According to Rabbi Shimon, who said** that he is not a nazirite **unless he takes a vow of naziriteship** with regard to **all of them, when he requests** dissolution **of that** prohibition, none of the prohibitions are permitted **until he requests** dissolution **of all of** the prohibitions of naziriteship. **Due to this** the mishna **teaches: And Rabbi Shimon prohibits** him.

## Alt Explanations

### Nidrei Onesim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: בְּנִדְרֵי אוֹנָסִין קָא מִיפַּלְגִי, וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא דִּשְׁמוּאֵל וְרַב אַסִּי. דִּתְנַן: אַרְבָּעָה נְדָרִים הִתִּירוּ חֲכָמִים: נִדְרֵי זֵירוּזִין. נִדְרֵי הֲבַאי. נִדְרֵי שְׁגָגוֹת. נִדְרֵי אוֹנָסִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: The Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon **disagree with regard to vows** impeded by **circumstances beyond one's control, and** their disagreement is **in the dispute between Shmuel and Rav Asi. As we learned** in a mishna (*Nedarim* 20b): **The Sages dissolved four** types of **vows** without the requirement of a request to a halakhic authority. The first category is **vows of exhortation,** where one employs a vow to urge another to perform some action. The second category is **vows of exaggeration [*havai*],** where one utters a vow that is dependent upon some outlandish claim, such as: I should be bound by a vow if I did not see a square snake. Since he knows that there are no square snakes, it is evident that he is not serious in taking his vow. The third category is **vows that are unintentional,** where one vows for a particular reason and later discovers he was mistaken with regard to the facts; and the fourth is **vows** whose fulfillment are impeded by **circumstances beyond one's control.**

The *Gemara* offers an alternative framework for explaining the third case: the dispute concerns *nidrei onsin* — coerced or circumstantially compelled vows. The *Mishnah* in *Nedarim* records four categories of *nedarim* that the *Chachamim* released: *nidrei ziruzin* (vows of encouragement or haggling), *nidrei havai* (exaggerated or nonsensical vows), *nidrei shegagot* (vows made under mistaken beliefs about conditions), and *nidrei onsin*. A *neder ones* is illustrated by a case where one says "my possessions shall be forbidden to you if you do not come to my celebration," and the other person does not come — but only because of circumstances beyond their control, such as an unavoidable delay. The vow-maker never intended the prohibition to take effect under those conditions.

The *Gemara* applies this framework to the third case: the person who accepted *nezirus* thinking they would receive a dispensation is, in some sense, in a position analogous to *nidrei onsin* — they would never have accepted *nezirus* had they known it would interfere with their livelihood as a gravedigger, and that interference is a kind of *ones*.

### Hatara Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: אַרְבָּעָה נְדָרִים הַלָּלוּ, צְרִיכִין שְׁאֵלָה לַחֲכָמִים. כִּי אַמְרִיתַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אָמַר לִי: תַּנָּא קָתָנֵי ״הִתִּירוּ חֲכָמִים״, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ צְרִיכִין שְׁאֵלָה לַחֲכָמִים?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates the dispute between Shmuel and Rav Asi: **And Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav Asi said: These four vows require a request** made **to the halakhic authorities** to dissolve them, and the vows are not dissolved unless one does so. Rav Yehuda related: **When I stated this** *halakha* of Rav Asi **before Shmuel, he said to me: The *tanna* teaches** that **the Sages dissolved** them, **and you say they require a request** made **to the halakhic authorities?** Shmuel holds that they are dissolved automatically.

The debate between the *Chachamim* and *Rabbi Shimon* in this framework tracks the dispute between *Shmuel* and *Rav Assi*. *Rav Yehuda* cites *Rav Assi* to the effect that even these four categories of *nedarim*, though fundamentally non-binding, still require a formal process of *hatarat nedarim* — the *Chachamim* should formally pronounce *muttar lach* — and this is *derabbanan*. When this position was reported before *Shmuel*, he responded: the *Mishnah* says *hatiru Chachamim* — the *Chachamim* released them — meaning they are simply not binding; no formal process is required. The *Chachamim* in the *Mishnah* on this *daf* follow *Shmuel*: the *neder* is simply not binding, hence *muttar*. *Rabbi Shimon* follows *Rav Assi*: the *neder* is not binding in a fundamental sense, but one must still go through a process of dissolution — meaning it is *assur* in the sense that it is presently binding until formally undone.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּנַן סָבְרִי כִּשְׁמוּאֵל. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן כְּרַב אַסִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its explanation: The dispute between the Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon in the last clause of the mishna is also with regard to this *halakha*. This case is referring to a kind of a vow impeded by circumstances beyond one's control, as he claims that he has no choice but to drink wine or contract impurity from corpses. **The Rabbis hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel,** and therefore the vow is dissolved automatically, without the need to request dissolution from a halakhic authority; **and Rabbi Shimon holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Asi** that one must make a request to a halakhic authority to dissolve the vow, and he remains a nazirite until he does so.

### Ein Piv V'libo

*Tosafos* offers a further alternative. Looking at a line in *Tosafos* that begins with the word *nami*, *Tosafos* argues that the third case is not really a matter of *nidrei onsin* at all. Rather, it is a case of *ein piv v'libo shavin* — the person's words do not match their inner intent. The person said *hareini nazir*, but their mental state at the time of declaration was that *nezirus* would not require them to give up their gravedigging work. What they said and what they intended diverged fundamentally.

The debate then maps onto the same *Amoraic* dispute about *ein piv v'libo shavin*: does this situation require no formal process at all, because the declaration was never truly binding, or does it require going through *hatarat nedarim* even though the *neder* was fundamentally defective? One position says the *neder* is simply not binding and no process is needed; the other says it is presently binding until a formal *hatara* is performed, if only to avoid trivializing the whole institution of *nedarim*. When we say *assur* in this context, we mean not that it is permanently binding, but that it requires formal dissolution — as in the story of a son who returned from camp troubled by a vow he had made, where the *halachic* response of *muttar lach, muttar lach, muttar lach* provided the needed closure, whether for his psychological ease or to preserve the gravity of the institution.

## Korbanot Responsibility

### Dual Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר וְעָלַי לְגַלֵּחַ נָזִיר״, וְשָׁמַע חֲבֵירוֹ וְאָמַר ״וַאֲנִי וְעָלַי לְגַלֵּחַ נָזִיר״. אִם הָיוּ פִּקְחִים — מְגַלְּחִין זֶה אֶת זֶה. וְאִם לָאו — מְגַלְּחִין נְזִירִים אֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one says: **I am hereby a nazirite and it is** incumbent **upon me to shave a nazirite,** meaning he will also pay for the offerings that a nazirite brings when he cuts his hair; **and another heard and said: And I** too am a nazirite **and it is** incumbent **upon me to shave a nazirite,** the other is also a nazirite and is obligated to pay for the offerings of a nazirite. **If they were perspicacious** and wish to limit their expenses, **they shave each other.** They may each pay for the other's offerings, so that their additional vows will not cost them anything. **And if not,** if this arrangement did not occur to them and each brought his own offerings, **they shave other nazirites,** i.e., they must pay for the offerings of other nazirites.

The final *Mishnah* of the *daf* introduces a new scenario. A person declares: *hareini nazir v'alai l'galeach nazir* — "I am a *nazir*, and I accept upon myself the responsibility to shave another *nazir*," which means taking on the financial obligation to bring the *korbanot* for another *nazir* at the conclusion of that person's *nezirus*.

### Mutual Help

The *Mishnah* then describes the optimal practical arrangement. If both parties are clever — *im hayu pikchin* — they will each bring the other's *korbanot*. This way, each person fulfills only one set of *korbanot* rather than two, and neither needs to search for a stranger whose *korbanot* they have obligated themselves to bring. If they cannot arrange this mutual arrangement, each must go and find another *nazir* whose *korbanot* they can bring. The *Mishnah* notes that the second person declared *v'ani v'alai l'galeach nazir* — "and me, and I also take responsibility to shave a *nazir*."

### V'ani Coverage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: שָׁמַע חֲבֵירוֹ וְאָמַר ״וַאֲנִי״, מַהוּ? ״וַאֲנִי״ — אַכּוּלֵּיהּ דִּיבּוּרָא מַשְׁמַע, אוֹ דִלְמָא אַפַּלְגֵיהּ דְּדִיבּוּרָא מַשְׁמַע. אִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר אַפַּלְגֵיהּ דְּדִיבּוּרָא מַשְׁמַע: אַרֵישָׁא, אוֹ אַסֵּיפָא?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages. If one said: I am hereby a nazirite and it is incumbent upon me to shave a nazirite as well, and **another heard and said** only: **And I, what is** the *halakha*? Do we say that: **And I, indicates** acceptance **of the entire statement** of the first one, which would mean he is both a nazirite and must shave a nazirite, **or perhaps it indicates** acceptance **of** only **half the statement** of the first speaker? **If you say it indicates** acceptance **of** only **half the statement,** does it indicate acceptance **of the first clause,** i.e., I am hereby a nazirite, **or the last clause,** i.e., it is incumbent upon me to shave a nazirite?

This formulation raises a question for the *Gemara*: if the second person had said only *v'ani* — "and me" — without the additional clause *v'alai l'galeach nazir*, would that abbreviated statement have covered both obligations, the *nezirus* itself and the responsibility to bring another's *korbanot*? Or does *v'ani* alone cover only part of what the first person said?

## Partial Statement Analys…

### Full vs. Partial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וַאֲנִי וְעָלַי לְגַלֵּחַ נָזִיר״, אִם הָיוּ פִּקְחִים מְגַלְּחִין זֶה אֶת זֶה. מִדְּקָאָמַר ״וַאֲנִי וְעָלַי״ — שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ וַאֲנִי אַפַּלְגֵיהּ דְּדִיבּוּרָא אָמְרִי. אִין, אַפַּלְגֵיהּ דְּדִיבּוּרָא מַשְׁמַע, מִיהוּ: אַרֵישָׁא, אוֹ אַסֵּיפָא? מִינָּה, מִדְּקָאָמַר ״וְעָלַי לְגַלֵּחַ״, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: ״וַאֲנִי״ — עַל תְּחִילַּת דִּיבּוּרָא מַשְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the mishna. If a second person said: **And I, and it is** incumbent **upon me to shave a nazirite,** then **if they are perspicacious they shave each other. From** the fact **that it says: And I, and it is** incumbent **upon me,** you can **learn from this** that: **And I, indicates** acceptance **of** only **half the statement,** as otherwise he would not need to add the second part. The Sages **say** in response to this attempted proof: **Yes,** this proves that it **indicates** acceptance **of** only **half the statement. However,** the other question still has not been answered: Is he referring **to the first** part of the original statement **or to the last** part? The Gemara answers: This can be learned **from** the mishna **itself. From** the fact **that it says: And it is** incumbent **upon me to shave** a nazirite, one can **learn from this** that: **And I, indicates** acceptance **of the beginning of the statement** to become a nazirite, which is why one has to add that he must also pay for the offerings of a nazirite.

The question the *Gemara* formulates is precise: does *v'ani* go on *kulei dibura* — covering the full statement made by the first person — or only *palga d'dibura* — covering part of it? And if only part, which part: the *reisha*, the first element of the declaration (the *nezirus* itself), or the *seifa*, the concluding element (the responsibility to bring another's *korbanot*)?

### Text Analysis

### V'ani Omnibus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְרָבָא: מִמַּאי דְּהָכִי? לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ ״וַאֲנִי״ — אַכּוּלֵּיהּ דְּדִיבּוּרָא, וְאִי מִשּׁוּם ״וְעָלַי״, מַאי קָאָמַר — ״וְעָלַי״ בְּהָא מִילְּתָא. דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא ״הֲרֵי עָלַי לְגַלֵּחַ חֲצִי נָזִיר״ וְשָׁמַע חֲבֵירוֹ וְאָמַר ״וַאֲנִי עָלַי לְגַלֵּחַ חֲצִי נָזִיר״, הָתָם מִי אִיכָּא תַּרְתֵּין מִילֵּי? אֶלָּא, מַאי קָאָמַר: ״עָלַי״ בְּהָא מִילְּתָא, הָכָא נָמֵי, כִּי קָאָמַר ״עָלַי״ — בְּהָא מִילְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said to Rava: From where** do you know **that** it **is so** that the words: And I, are referring to the first part of the statement? **Actually, I will say to you** that: **And I,** is referring **to the entire statement, and if** you claim otherwise **because** he added: **And it is** incumbent **upon me,** the explanation of **what he is saying** is: **And it is** incumbent **on me** to do **this matter.** He was merely clarifying what his intention was when he said: And I. **As, if you do not say so,** then with regard to **that which is taught in the last clause,** in the following mishna, that if one said: **It is** incumbent **upon me to shave half a nazirite, and another heard** that **and said: And I, it is** incumbent **upon me to shave half a nazirite,** then **there, are there two statements?** That mishna is addressing only a single vow. **Rather, what is he saying** by using the words: **It is** incumbent **upon me?** He is referring **to that** whole **matter; here too,** in this mishna, **when he says: And it is** incumbent **upon me,** he is referring **to that** whole **matter.**

*Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua* challenges this proof before Rava. He argues that *v'ani* could in fact cover everything — both the *nezirus* and the obligation to bring another's *korbanot*. If so, why does the *Mishnah* have the second person also say *v'alai l'galeach*? The answer is that this added phrase is not adding halachic content; it is simply explanatory — *alai be-ha milta*, "upon me, this thing," meaning the speaker is spelling out what *v'ani* already implied. The *Mishnah* gives us a full sentence not because the sentence was required to create the halachic effect, but for the sake of clarity.

In support of this reading, *Rav Huna* points to the *Mishnah* that follows in the text: *harei alai l'galeach chatzi nazir*, where a person accepts responsibility to bring the *korbanot* for half of another *nazir*'s obligations. There too, the second person says a full sentence — *v'ani alai l'galeach chatzi nazir* — even though in that case only one thing is at stake, namely the responsibility for another's *korbanot*, with no personal *nezirus* involved. If *v'ani* were truly insufficient and required supplementation, why does the *Mishnah* not simply record that the second person said *v'ani* in a case where there is only one thing to echo? The fact that the *Mishnah* still has that person say a full sentence suggests that the full sentence is the *Mishnah*'s way of giving an example of what was said, not a halachic requirement. And if so, in our case as well, *v'ani* alone would have sufficed.

### Rava's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא רֵישָׁא צְרִיכָא סֵיפָא לָא צְרִיכָא — תָּנֵי סֵיפָא דְּלָא צְרִיכָא מִשּׁוּם רֵישָׁא דִּצְרִיכָא. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ רֵישָׁא לָא צְרִיכָא סֵיפָא לָא צְרִיכָא, תָּנֵי רֵישָׁא דְּלָא צְרִיכָא וְתָנֵי סֵיפָא דְּלָא צְרִיכָא?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: How can** these cases **be compared? Granted, if you say** that in **the first clause** it **is necessary** to teach both parts of the statement and in **the latter clause** it **is not necessary** to teach both parts of the statement, then it can be said that **he taught the unnecessary latter clause due to the necessary first clause,** as it is typical for a mishna to phrase both of its sections in the same style. **But if you say** that it **is not necessary** to teach both parts of the statement in **the first clause** and it is also **not necessary** to teach both parts of the statement in **the latter clause,** as one has accepted the other's entire statement by saying: And I, would the *tanna* **teach an unnecessary first clause and teach an unnecessary latter clause?** Since the addition of: And it is incumbent upon me, is not required in the latter mishna, it must be necessary in this mishna, so the inference of Rava is correct.

Rava rejects this argument. His reasoning is that the two *Mishnayos* can be read together in a way that avoids making both of their full-sentence formulations superfluous. If in the first *Mishnah* — our case — *v'ani* was insufficient and the additional clause was genuinely necessary, then we can account for the second *Mishnah*'s full sentence by saying: *tani seifa d'lo tzricha mishum reisha d'tzricha* — the *seifa* includes a clause that was not technically required, but it does so in order to parallel the *reisha* where the clause was required. This is an acceptable halachic literary technique: one case is formulated in full because it needed to be, and the parallel case adopts the same formulation for consistency.

### Supplementation Needed

But Rava argues that the reverse reading creates an insupportable result. If *v'ani* already covers everything, then in both *Mishnayos* the additional phrases are purely superfluous. Having two independent cases each containing a legally unnecessary clause — purely for stylistic reasons — is not how a *halacha* text operates. One can accept superfluity in the *seifa* where it mirrors necessary language in the *reisha*, but one cannot accept gratuitous superfluity in both. Therefore Rava concludes: *v'ani* alone, without supplementation, is insufficient to create the full halachic effect of both obligations. The first *Mishnah* required both clauses because both clauses do real work: *v'ani* picks up the *nezirus*, and *v'alai l'galeach* adds the obligation to bring the other's *korbanot*. Here the *daf* concludes.
