# Hullin 77 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nishbar Ha-etzem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא נִשְׁבַּר הָעֶצֶם וְיָצָא לַחוּץ, דְּאִישְׁתְּקִיל קוּרְטִיתָא מִינֵּיהּ, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי, שַׁהֲיֵיהּ תְּלָתָא רִיגְלֵי.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain** case in **which a bone** in an animal's leg **broke and protruded outward.** This bone was mostly covered with flesh and skin, but **a small piece [*kurtita*]** of the bone **had been removed from it.** The case **came before Abaye,** who **delayed** his response until **three** pilgrimage **Festivals** had passed, when the Sages gathered together and he could ask them.

### Bone Break Covered

Daf 77a picks up several lines into the page, at the word *yachid*, near the end of the line, with the topic of *nishbar ha-etzem* — the broken bone. This case was introduced in the *mishnah*: if the leg bone of an animal broke and the limb was left dangling, the halachic status of that limb depends on the nature of the break. Under ordinary circumstances, a break high enough up the leg renders the animal a *treifah*, or at least prohibits the consumption of that dangling limb even after *shechitah* of the animal. However, the *mishnah* establishes an exception: if the break is covered by the surrounding flesh, the break is not halachically significant. The Gemara now continues probing exactly how to define adequate coverage.

### Bone Chipped Off

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר מַתְנָא: זִיל קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף בַּר חָמָא, דַּחֲרִיפָא סַכִּינֵיהּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ, אֲמַר: מִכְּדֵי ״נִשְׁבַּר הָעֶצֶם וְיָצָא לַחוּץ״ תְּנַן, מָה לִי נְפַל, מָה לִי אִיתֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Mattana said to** the owner of the animal: **Go before Rava, son of Rav Yosef bar Ḥama, whose knife is sharp,** i.e., he has insight into halakhic matters and decides matters quickly, and ask him to decide your case. The owner **came before** Rava to seek his opinion. Rava **said** to him: **Since we learned** in the *baraita* (76b): If **the bone broke and protruded outward,** if skin and flesh cover a majority of the bone the animal is permitted, **what** difference is there **to me** if the bone **fell** out, and **what** difference is there **to me** if **it is in** its place? In either case, the animal is permitted.

The Gemara introduces a specific incident: *hahu nishbar ha-etzem ve-yatza lachutz* — there was a case where a bone broke and protruded outward — and *ve-ishtakeil korta minei* — a small fragment of the bone had actually chipped off. This case differs from what the *mishnah* addressed directly, which spoke of a break, not of a bone fragment falling away. Nevertheless, the flesh apparently still covered the area. The question was whether that additional factor — the chipped fragment — undermined the protection of the flesh covering.

### Abaye & Rava

This case came *ata lekamei de-Abaye* — before Abaye — who *shehayah telasa riglei*, held it over for three *regalim*, apparently hoping that the gatherings of scholars at festival times would yield an answer. There is something arresting about this: what happened to the meat in the interim? Presumably it was salted and preserved. And there is also something to note about the fact that in Babylonia, the *regalim* had ceased to be pilgrimage occasions, yet the gatherings they occasioned still served as opportunities for broader rabbinic consultation.

Eventually, *Rav Adda bar Matna* said — and Rashi notes that he was speaking to the owner of the animal, not to Abaye — *zel lekamei de-Rava breih de-Rav Yosef bar Chama*, go before Rava son of Rav Yosef bar Chama, *de-charifa sakinah*, whose knife is sharp — a metaphor for Rava's penetrating analytical ability — since Abaye had not been able to resolve the matter. One might ask whether it is proper to seek a second ruling when a first posek has not yet decided; since Abaye rendered no *psak*, however, there was no ruling to be bound by, and the questioner was free to seek another opinion.

### Mishnah Analysis

The owner went before Rava, who said: *michdi, nishbar ha-etzem ve-yatza lachutz tenan* — our *mishnah*, or at least the *baraita*, says simply that the bone broke and protruded, and the question is whether the break is covered by flesh. *Mah li nafal mah li issa* — what difference does it make whether a fragment of bone broke off or not? The *mishnah* draws no such distinction. The implication is clear: as long as the flesh covers the area of the break, the outcome is the same regardless of whether a splinter of bone has fallen away. This is, admittedly, a somewhat less pyrotechnic resolution than one might expect given Rava's reputation for sharp analysis — the argument amounts simply to pointing out that the *mishnah* does not distinguish. Yet Rava found it sufficient, and the ruling stands: even when a piece of the bone has chipped off, if the flesh surrounds the break, that coverage is effective.

## Flesh Coverage Variants

### Ravina's Questions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרָבָא: מִתְלַקֵּט מַהוּ? מִתְרוֹסֵס מַהוּ? מִתְמַסְמֵס מַהוּ? הֵיכִי דָּמֵי מִתְמַסְמֵס? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: כֹּל שֶׁהָרוֹפֵא קוֹדְרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to a case where flesh covers the majority of a broken bone, **Ravina said to Rava:** If the flesh was torn in pieces and spread over the area, and if **gathered** it would constitute a majority, **what is** the *halakha*? Similarly, if the flesh was **pulverized** and thin, **what is** the *halakha*? If it was **decomposed, what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of this case in which the flesh is **decomposed? Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said:** This is referring to **any** kind of flesh **that the doctor cuts away [*kodro*]** and removes to enable the surrounding area to heal.

The Gemara then turns to a series of questions posed by Ravina to Rava. These questions are no longer about whether a bone fragment has broken off, but about the nature and condition of the flesh itself that is supposedly covering the break.

*Mislaket mahu* — what if the flesh covering the break is discontinuous, meaning it surrounds the break in scattered patches with gaps in between, rather than in one contiguous area? The *mishnah* does not say the coverage must be uninterrupted, but one might think that only contiguous flesh provides meaningful protection.

*Mishrosos mahu* — what if the flesh is thinned? It may surround the break, but it has worn down to only a very thin layer rather than full-thickness flesh.

*Mismasas mahu* — what if the flesh is rotting away, though still in place? The Gemara clarifies this case: *heichi dami mismasas*? What does this mean precisely? *Kol she'arofe kodro* — flesh that a doctor would excise, that is, flesh so far gone that a physician would remove it as necrotic tissue. Does such flesh still count as coverage?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: נִיקַּב מַהוּ? נִקְלַף מַהוּ? נִסְדַּק מַהוּ? נִיטַּל שְׁלִישׁ הַתַּחְתּוֹן מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If the flesh that covers the bone **was perforated, what is** the *halakha*? Likewise, if the flesh **was peeled** off the bone, **what is** the *halakha*? If it **was cracked, what is** the *halakha*? If **the bottom third** of the width of the flesh, i.e., the part that is adjacent to the bone, **was removed, what is** the *halakha*?

*Nikev mahu* — what if there are holes in the flesh? Even granting that the majority of the flesh is present and surrounding the break, the flesh is perforated. Rashi notes that this may refer even to a hole that has not removed any flesh, simply a perforation.

*Niklaf mahu* — what if the flesh has been peeled away from the bone, so that it sits above the break without adhering to the bone itself? This case is intuitively more significant: flesh resting loosely atop the break, with no actual adhesion to the bone, seems to offer far less structural support.

*Nisdak mahu* — what if the flesh is cracked? This is similar to the question of holes: the flesh is present but fissured, and one might worry that such cracks will propagate and eventually cause the flesh to detach entirely.

*Nitosh tlisha tachton mahu* — what if the bottom third of the flesh has been removed? This is more severe than the *niklaf* case: not only is the flesh unattached to the bone, but a portion of its thickness has been excised, leaving the flesh sitting atop the break with neither full thickness nor adhesion.

To none of these questions does the Gemara provide a direct answer.

### Skin Adheres to Bone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עוֹר הֲרֵי הוּא כְּבָשָׂר, דִּלְמָא דִּקְנָה מַשְׁכָּא דִּידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear, as Ulla says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Skin is like flesh.** This would appear to indicate that any covering is sufficient. The Gemara refutes this proof: **Perhaps** Rabbi Yoḥanan was referring to a specific case where there was never flesh on the bone, but only skin, e.g., adjacent to the knee, where **the skin holds its own** place close to the bone. This ruling may not apply in an area where there was flesh. Perhaps in such a place the bone must be covered by flesh that is still healthy.

The Gemara then introduces a possible resolution by analogy, citing a ruling of Ulla in the name of Rabbi Yochanan from a prior discussion: *or harei hu kevasar* — skin counts as flesh. That is, if skin covers the break even where there is no overlying flesh, that skin coverage is sufficient. This was established yesterday, and now the Gemara asks whether this ruling sheds light on our questions.

If skin alone — which is thinner and lighter than flesh, and which, at certain points on an animal's body, lies directly against the bone — constitutes adequate coverage, might that not tell us something about cases where flesh is present but compromised, whether thinned, cracked, or peeled away?

The Gemara rejects the analogy. There is a fundamental difference: *dilma dekana mashka didei* — perhaps the reason skin counts is that in the places where skin bears directly on the bone, it is naturally adhering to the bone, it has effectively grown onto it. This is true of the skin over a knee, a shin, an ankle, or a nose — anywhere that one can feel the bone directly through the skin, because there is no intervening fat or muscle. In such places, the skin is *mamash* upon the bone, directly and naturally. But our questions concern cases where flesh is present and presumably was once fully in place, but has been disturbed: peeled back, thinned, cracked, or allowed to rot. That is a different situation from naturally adherent skin. The *or kevasar* principle, therefore, does not resolve the questions about compromised flesh.

### Ring-Shaped Hole

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: כִּי הֲוֵינַן בֵּי רַב פַּפֵּי, אִיבַּעְיָא לַן: נִקְדַּר כְּמִין טַבַּעַת, מַהוּ? וּפְשַׁטְנָא מֵהָא דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: דָּבָר זֶה שָׁאַלְתִּי לַחֲכָמִים וְלָרוֹפְאִים, וְאָמְרוּ: מְסָרְטוֹ בְּעֶצֶם וּמַעֲלֶה אֲרוּכָה, אֲבָל פַּרְזְלָא מִזְרָף זָרֵיף. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: וְהוּא דִּקְנָה גַּרְמָא דִּידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: While we were** studying **in Rav Pappi's study hall, we raised a dilemma:** If the flesh and skin were **cut in the shape of a ring** around the break, and yet most of the circumference of the bone is surrounded by flesh, **what is** the *halakha*? **And we resolved** this dilemma **from this** statement that **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: I asked** about **this matter to the Sages and to the doctors,** what to do when a bone breaks and the surrounding flesh has been cut away, **and they said: One makes an incision in it with** a sharp piece of **bone** to help the blood flow and then congeal, **and** in this manner the wound **will heal.** The Gemara notes: **But** one should not make the incision with **an iron** implement, **as it will cause inflammation. Rav Pappa said: And this** advice should be implemented only in a case where one can see that **the bone is holding** firmly onto **its** flesh, as only in such a case will the flesh heal.

Rav Ashi reports that when he was in the academy of Rav Papi, a further question arose: *nikdar k'min tabaat mahu* — what is the status of a ring-shaped hole in the flesh, a hole of significant size? Rashi notes that even in this scenario we are still assuming the majority of the break is covered; the question is whether a large ring-shaped perforation, while leaving much of the flesh intact, nonetheless so fundamentally disrupts the coverage that it can no longer be said to protect the break.

The answer came from a statement of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav, who said: *davar zeh sha'alti lachachamim u'larofim* — I asked both sages and physicians about this. The question was whether flesh with such a hole can heal. Their answer: *mesarto b'etzem u'ma'ala arucha* — if you scratch the area with a bone and cause it to bleed, the resulting scabbing and regeneration can actually heal the wound and fill the hole over time. This is a mechanism of healing. By contrast, using a metal knife to attempt the same thing — *aval parzla, mizraz zarif* — would actually cause incisions and be counterproductive. The implication of this ruling is that since even a large ring-shaped hole can, under appropriate treatment, heal and regenerate, the flesh in such a case is still considered to constitute coverage of the break.

### Flesh Adhesion

Rav Papa adds a crucial qualification: *v'hu dekana garma didei* — this applies only as long as the flesh is still adhering to the bone. If the flesh has separated from the bone and is merely sitting atop it without cleaving to it, it would not carry the same protective effect. Rashi adds that one should also look for signs that the flesh is in the process of scabbing and healing around the area of the hole.

## Dangling Limb

### Limb Status

At this point the Gemara steps back to orient us within the broader structure of the *perek*. The entire discussion of *nishbar ha-etzem* — the broken bone and the coverage of flesh — arose because the *mishnah* in this *perek* is concerned with the status of dangling limbs. The *perek* as a whole addresses the halachic status of a fetus inside an animal when the mother is *shechted*, and what happens to limbs growing out of the fetus or hanging from it. The *mishnah* introduces the parallel case of a dangling limb of the mother animal itself: if an animal's leg bone has broken and the limb is hanging, and you then *shecht* the animal, the dangling limb cannot be eaten. However, if the break is covered with flesh, that counteracts the problem.

It is this last clause — the flesh covering — that has occupied the Gemara's attention, leading to all the cases just discussed: what counts as covering, how much, under what conditions, and what happens when the flesh is discontinuous, thinned, rotted, perforated, or peeled.

The *mishnah* also raised what appears to be a new category of *treifah*: if the hind leg breaks high enough up, the animal is rendered a *treifah*. This is striking because it does not appear explicitly in the chapter of *eilu treifos*. Notably, the *mishnah* itself does not use the word *treifah* but rather *pesulah* — disqualified — and yet the Gemara treats it as a *treifah*, a category of invalidation with broad consequences. The entire treatment of flesh coverage is bound up in trying to determine when such a break is halachically neutralized by the presence of covering flesh — and Rashi's commentary here, one should acknowledge, is especially extensive and essential. Without Rashi — and in places even with Rashi — the graphic anatomical detail of this passage calls for visual aids that the ancient commentators, needless to say, did not supply.

## Shilya Emerging

### Placenta Alone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַבְּהֵמָה וּמָצָא בָּהּ שִׁלְיָא – נֶפֶשׁ הַיָּפֶה תֹּאכְלֶנָּה, וְאֵינָהּ מְטַמְּאָה לֹא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין וְלֹא טוּמְאַת נְבֵלוֹת. חִישֵּׁב עָלֶיהָ – מְטַמְּאָה טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין, אֲבָל לֹא טוּמְאַת נְבֵלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who slaughters an animal and finds a placenta in its** womb, one with **a hearty soul [*nefesh hayafa*],** i.e., who is not repulsed by it, **may eat it,** as its consumption was permitted by virtue of the slaughter of the mother. Nevertheless, since generally speaking, people do not consume such placentas, it is not regarded as food **and** so it **cannot become impure** with **the ritual impurity of food** even were it to come into contact with a source of impurity. **And** furthermore, it does **not** impart **the ritual impurity of animal carcasses** as it was permitted by virtue of the slaughter of the mother. But if **one intended to** eat **it,** one thereby elevated it to the status of food, and the placenta **becomes impure** with **the ritual impurity of food** if it comes into contact with a source of impurity. **But** even so, it still does **not** impart the **ritual impurity of animal carcasses.**

The final *mishnah* of the *perek* opens: *hashochet et habeheimah umatzah bah shilya* — one who slaughters an animal and finds inside it a *shilya*. The word *shilya* is commonly translated as placenta, though there is a significant question — to which we shall return — as to whether it might more precisely refer to the amniotic sac. Either way, the scenario is that you find this *shilya* without any visible fetus. The animal has apparently miscarried or dissolved the fetus prior to *shechitah*, and all that remains is this remnant. What is its status?

### Placenta Impurity

The *mishnah* rules: *nefesh hayafa tochalennah* — the finicky may decline, but the non-squeamish person may eat it, since the *shechitah* of the mother renders everything inside her permissible. Then the *mishnah* adds: *ve'eino mitamei lo tumat ochlin velo tumat neveilot* — it does not cause *tumah* of foodstuff, and it does not cause *tumah* of a *neveilah*.

The logic of these two rules: it does not cause *tumat ochlin* because it is not normally eaten, and an item must be normally consumed food to become susceptible to or transmit *tumah* as food. It does not cause *tumat neveilah* because the *neveilah tumah* attaches to the actual flesh of a carcass, not to organs that are not meat — just as bones, teeth, and horns of a dead animal do not carry *tumat neveilah*, neither does the *shilya*, which is not meat.

If, however, *chishav alav* — one planned to eat it — *metamei tumat ochlin aval lo tumat neveilah*: by intending to eat it, one has reclassified it as food and it can now receive and transmit *tumat ochlin*. But it can never become a *neveilah*, because that status is reserved for actual meat, which this is not.

### Placenta Emerges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁלְיָא שֶׁיָּצְתָה מִקְצָתָהּ – אֲסוּרָה בַּאֲכִילָה, סִימַן וָלָד בָּאִשָּׁה וְסִימַן וָלָד בַּבְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **a placenta, part of which emerged** from the womb before the mother was slaughtered, its **consumption** is **prohibited** even after the mother animal is slaughtered because the emergence of the placenta is **an indication of a fetus in a woman and an indication of a fetus in an animal.** Accordingly, there is a concern that the head of the fetus might have emerged in that part of the placenta, thereby rendering the fetus as having been born, a status that precludes it from being permitted by the slaughter of its mother. Since the offspring is prohibited, its placenta is likewise prohibited.

*Shilya sheyatza miktzatah asurah be'achilah* — if part of the *shilya* has emerged from the womb, it is forbidden to eat. The reason is that the *shilya* is *siman vlad ba'ishah ve-siman vlad ba-behemah* — its emergence is a sign of birth for a woman and for an animal alike. Since there is no *shilya* without a fetus having existed, the emergence of the *shilya* raises the concern that the dissolved fetus is coming out with it.

This is a genuinely striking ruling. One might have assumed that a dissolved fetus is simply biological matter — like a discharge — with no ongoing halachic status. Yet the *mishnah* treats the dissolved fetus as though it were a stillbirth. If an actual stillborn calf had emerged head-first, it would be considered born, and since it is dead and *shechitah* is impossible, it could not be eaten. The same logic applies here: if the dissolved fetus is treated as having been born, and born in a state that precludes *shechitah*, then the flesh is forbidden.

One can raise the conceptual difficulty: if the fetus was viable and the head emerged, we say it has been born. That is coherent when dealing with an actual formed animal. But why should dissolved material carry that same status? The Gemara's answer is that the logic of *siman vlad* — the sign of birth — extends even to this dissolved state, and the *mishnah*'s ruling follows accordingly.

Moreover, once the head of the fetus has emerged, even the portion remaining inside the womb cannot be eaten — the whole fetus is treated as born. The *mishnah* here applies the same principle to the *shilya*: when any part of it emerges, the entire *shilya* is forbidden, not merely the emerged portion.

There is an extended discussion of the precise meaning of *shilya* versus *shapir*. Artscroll translates *shilya* as the amniotic sac rather than the placenta — the reverse of the common assumption, which holds *shapir* to be the amniotic sac and *shilya* the placenta. It is worth noting that the *sugya* coheres considerably more naturally if *shilya* denotes the amniotic sac: the concern that the dissolved fetus is "coming out with it" is much more intuitive if the sac is what is emerging, since the fetus would have been contained within it. The Steinsaltz commentary at this point describes the *shilya* as a large, blood-vessel-filled mass attached to the uterine wall within which the fetus is washed — language that supports the amniotic sac reading. This is a matter that warrants further investigation in other contexts.

### Sacred Placenta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְבַכֶּרֶת שֶׁהִפִּילָה שִׁלְיָא – יַשְׁלִיכֶנָּה לִכְלָבִים, וּבַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין תִּקָּבֵר, וְאֵין קוֹבְרִין אוֹתָהּ בְּפָרָשַׁת דְּרָכִים, וְאֵין תּוֹלִין אוֹתָהּ בְּאִילָן, מִפְּנֵי דַּרְכֵי הָאֱמוֹרִי.
>
> **Translation:** If **an animal that was giving birth to its firstborn expelled a placenta, one may cast it to the dogs,** and one does not need to be concerned that the placenta came from a male fetus that has the consecrated status of a firstborn. **But in** the case of **sacrificial** animals the placenta **must be buried,** because it came from a fetus that is assumed to have been sacred. The mishna adds: **But one may neither bury it at an intersection, nor may one hang it on a tree,** superstitious rites intended to prevent the animal from miscarrying again, **due to** the prohibition against following **the ways of the Amorite,** which prohibits Jews from practicing the superstitious rites observed by gentiles.

*U'vimukdashim titkaver* — if this *shilya* was expelled from a sanctified animal, it must be buried. The reasoning is that whatever was inside — dissolved or not — would have borne the sanctity of a *korban*, since offspring of sanctified animals, male or female, carry that sanctity. Since we treat the emergence of the *shilya* as a birth, the dissolved contents retain their sacred status, and the *shilya* must therefore be buried rather than discarded. This is not because it is a discharge, but because we treat it as a birth — the same logic that makes it forbidden to eat when it emerges, and that makes it subject to *kedushah* when it comes from a sanctified animal.

### Firstborn Miscarriage

*Ha-mevakeret she-hipila shilya yashlichenah la-klavim* — if an animal that has never yet given birth miscarries and produces only a *shilya*, you may simply give it to the dogs. This is because the dissolved contents might or might not have been a firstborn male with the sanctity of a *bechor*. The Gemara will explain this further: since only a male firstborn carries *kedushat bechor*, and since there is a small subset of male firstborns that nonetheless do not carry that *kedushah* — the *nidmeh* case, to be discussed — the probability that this dissolved *shilya* contains a valid *bechor* is technically less than fifty percent. By *rov*, therefore, one need not treat it as a *bechor*.

## Siman Vlad

### Textual Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כֹּל (בהמה) [בַּבְּהֵמָה] תֹּאכֵלוּ״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַשִּׁלְיָא. יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ יָצְתָה מִקְצָתָהּ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹתָהּ״ – אוֹתָהּ וְלֹא שִׁלְיָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the ruling that a placenta is permitted by virtue of the mother's slaughter, the Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived? **As the Sages taught:** "And **every** animal that parts the hoof, and has the hooves wholly cloven in two, and chews the cud, **of the animals, it you may eat"** (Deuteronomy 14:6). The verse is the source of the *halakha* that a fetus is permitted by virtue of the mother's slaughter (see 69a). The word "every" at the beginning of the verse serves **to include the placenta** as well as the fetus. One **might** have thought that a placenta is permitted **even if part of it emerged** from the womb before slaughter. Therefore, **the verse states: "It** you may eat," i.e., you may eat **it,** the slaughtered animal, **but not its placenta,** if it partially emerged.

*Mena hani milei* — where is the scriptural source for the permissibility of eating the *shilya*? A *baraita* derives it from the verse *kol behema tochlu* — you may eat any animal — which is read to include the *shilya* found inside a slaughtered animal. The *baraita* then raises the possibility that even if part of it has emerged it should still be permitted — *yachol afilu yatza miktzatah* — and the verse responds *otah velo shilyatah*: "it" — the animal itself — but not its *shilya* once the *shilya* has partially emerged.

### No Placenta Alone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדִי אֵין שִׁלְיָא בְּלֹא וָלָד, לְמָה לִי קְרָא? קְרָא אַסְמַכְתָּא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **Now** the assumption is that there is **no placenta without a fetus.** Therefore, if part of the placenta emerged, there is a concern that the head of the fetus might have emerged in that part of the placenta, thereby rendering the fetus as having been born. Such a status would preclude it from being permitted by the slaughter of its mother. **Why do I** need a verse to teach this? The Gemara explains: The **verse is a mere support** for the *halakha*, but not the source for it.

The Gemara then questions why we need a verse at all for the prohibition once it has partially emerged: *ein shilya belo vlad* — there is no *shilya* without a fetus. If a *shilya* is present, a fetus certainly existed at some point, and if the *shilya* is emerging, the dissolved fetus is presumably coming with it. The answer is: *kra asmachta be'alma* — the verse is only an *asmachta*, a scriptural support; the reasoning is logically derivable even without it. This is somewhat surprising even under the amniotic sac reading: one might still have thought that dissolved material, however it got that way, has no ongoing halachic status and requires a verse to tell us otherwise. Yet the Gemara insists the reasoning is self-evident.

### Impurity Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵינָהּ מְטַמְּאָה. בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר נַפָּחָא: עוֹר חֲמוֹר שֶׁשְּׁלָקוֹ מַהוּ? לְמַאי? אִי לְטוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין – תְּנֵינָא, אִי לְטוּמְאַת נְבֵלוֹת – תְּנֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **And** a placenta found inside a slaughtered animal **cannot become impure** with the ritual impurity of food and does not impart the ritual impurity of animal carcasses. In a related discussion, **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Nappaḥa raises a dilemma: A donkey hide that one cooked** and it became softened, **what is** its halakhic status? The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** issue did he raise this dilemma? **If** it was **with regard to the ritual impurity of food, we** already **learn** this *halakha* in a *baraita*, and **if** you say it was **with regard to the ritual impurity of animal carcasses, we** already **learn** that *halakha* as well in another *baraita*.

Regarding the *tumah* rules, the Gemara notes that all of this — neither *tumat ochlin* nor *tumat neveilah* — is explicitly taught in a *baraita*: *ha'or ve-hashilya ein mitammin tumat ochlin* — the skin and the *shilya* do not cause *tumat ochlin* because they are not normally eaten. However, *or sheshalko* — skin that has been boiled — and *hashilya shechishav aleha* — a *shilya* that one has planned to eat — *mitammin tumat ochlin*. So planning to eat it elevates it to the status of food for purposes of *tumah*. As for *tumat neveilah*, a separate *baraita* specifies *benevelatah* — by touching the carcass itself — *velo be'orah velo be'atzmot velo begidim velo bekarnayim velo be'talafayim*: not the skin, not the bones, not the sinews, not the horns, not the hooves. The *shilya* is not flesh, and therefore it does not transmit *tumat neveilah*.

### Boiled Skin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין, דְּתַנְיָא: הָעוֹר וְהַשִּׁלְיָא – אֵין מִטַּמְּאִין טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין, עוֹר שֶׁשְּׁלָקוֹ, וְהַשִּׁלְיָא שֶׁחִישֵּׁב עָלֶיהָ – מְטַמְּאִין טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: With regard to the **ritual impurity of food,** it is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **The hide and the placenta** of an animal, which people do not typically eat, **cannot become impure** with the **ritual impurity of foods.** But **a hide that one cooked** until it became edible **and a placenta that one intended** to eat **can become impure** with the **impurity of foods.**

Rabbi Yitzchak bar Nafcha raises a question: *or chamor sheshalko mahu* — what is the status of the boiled skin of a donkey? Has it been rendered edible, and thus susceptible to *tumat ochlin*? The Gemara responds that both aspects of the question — *tumat ochlin* and *tumat neveilah* — are explicitly addressed in prior *baraitot* and require no new ruling.

The real question, the Gemara concludes, is this: even assuming that boiling other animal skins might render them sufficiently edible to qualify as food, perhaps the skin of a donkey is so inherently repulsive — *de-ma'is* — that no amount of cooking would cause a person to plan to eat it. And if it is so far beyond the range of normal human consumption, even a subjective intention to eat it may not be sufficient to reclassify it as food. This leads to a broader principle implicit in the *mishnah*'s phrase *chishav alav*: a subjective intention is meaningful when it operates within the range of what some people actually do. If an item is so unusual that it falls entirely outside the normal distribution of human eating behavior, subjective intent may be legally insufficient to reclassify it as food.

## Eleazar vs Yochanan

### Eleazar: Intertwined Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁלְיָא שֶׁיָּצְתָה. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין עִמָּהּ וָלָד, אֲבָל יֵשׁ עִמָּהּ וָלָד – אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְוָלָד אַחֵר. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: בֵּין אֵין עִמָּהּ וָלָד בֵּין יֵשׁ עִמָּהּ וָלָד – חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְוָלָד אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: With regard to **a placenta,** part of **which emerged** from the womb before the mother was slaughtered, its consumption is prohibited, as there is a concern that the head of the fetus might have emerged in that part of the placenta. Commenting on this mishna, **Rabbi Elazar says:** The Sages **taught** this *halakha* **only** in a case **where there was no fetus** found **in** the mother's womb. **But if there was a fetus** with its head and the majority of its body **in** the womb, **one** need **not be concerned for** the existence of **another fetus** that might have been in the placenta. Therefore, the consumption of the placenta is permitted. **But Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Whether there was no fetus** found **in** the womb, or **whether there was a fetus, one** needs to **be concerned for** the existence of **another fetus** in the placenta, and therefore its consumption is prohibited.

Rabbi Eleazar addresses the case of a *shilya* that has partially emerged: *lo shanu ela she'ein imah vlad* — we are concerned about a dissolved fetus only when there is no visible fetus present. But if there is an actual fetus still in the womb — *yesh imah vlad* — *ein chosheshin le-vlad acher*: you need not be concerned that there is yet another dissolved fetus accompanying this *shilya*. Seeing one fetus and one *shilya*, you assume they correspond to each other.

### Yochanan: Fetus Present

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *bein ein imah vlad bein yesh imah vlad, chosheshin le-vlad acher* — whether or not a fetus is present, you must be concerned that there is another dissolved fetus accompanying the *shilya*. The presence of a visible fetus does not alleviate the concern about an additional dissolved one.

### Eleazar Stricter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: לְחוּמְרָא אַמְרַהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר! אֶלָּא, אִי אִתְּמַר הָכִי אִתְּמַר, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵינָהּ קְשׁוּרָה בַּוָּלָד, אֲבָל קְשׁוּרָה בַּוָּלָד – אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְוָלָד אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** According to the way the Gemara records the dispute, Rabbi Yoḥanan rules stringently and Rabbi Elazar rules leniently. The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But doesn't Rabbi Yirmeya say** concerning this dispute: **Rabbi Elazar said** an explanation of the mishna that presents **a stringency?** **Rather, if it was stated, it was stated like this: Rabbi Elazar says:** The ruling of the mishna is that the placenta is prohibited even if a fetus is found inside the womb. But the Sages **taught** this **only** in a case **where** the placenta **is not attached to** the **fetus** found inside. Consequently, one must be concerned with the possibility that the placenta came from another fetus that already left the womb, and it is therefore prohibited. **But if it is attached to** the **fetus** found inside, **there is no concern for** the possibility that the placenta came from **another fetus,** and therefore it is permitted.

Rabbi Yirmiyah corrects the framing of Rabbi Eleazar's position. The way the statements were initially presented, Rabbi Eleazar appeared to be lenient relative to Rabbi Yochanan. But Rabbi Yirmiyah says: *l'chumra amar Rabbi Eleazar* — Rabbi Eleazar's statement was intended as a strict ruling, not a lenient one. The correct version of Rabbi Eleazar's position is this: *lo shanu ella she'eina kshura be-vlad* — we are always concerned about another dissolved fetus, the sole exception being when the *shilya* is literally intertwined (*kshura*) with a present fetus. Only when the *shilya* and the fetus are physically bound together can you assume they belong to the same birth. Under all other circumstances — even when a fetus is present but not intertwined — Rabbi Eleazar remains concerned that the *shilya* belongs to a different, dissolved fetus.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אָנוּ אֵין לָנוּ אֶלָּא שִׁלְיָא בְּלֹא וָלָד, אֲבָל יֵשׁ עִמָּהּ וָלָד, בֵּין קְשׁוּרָה בַּוָּלָד בֵּין אֵין קְשׁוּרָה בַּוָּלָד – אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְוָלָד אַחֵר, וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: לְחוּמְרָא אַמְרַהּ ר׳ אֶלְעָזָר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In my understanding of the mishna, **we have** the ruling that the placenta is prohibited **only** in a case where there is **a placenta without** any **fetus** being found. **But if a fetus was** found **with it,** then **whether it is attached to the fetus** or **whether it is not attached to the fetus, there is no concern for** the existence of **another fetus.** The Gemara confirms: **And this** understanding of the two opinions **is** in line with that which **Rabbi Yirmeya says: Rabbi Eleazar said** an explanation of the mishna that presents **a stringency.**

Rabbi Yochanan, by contrast, holds: *anu ein lanu ella shilya belo vlad* — we are only concerned when there is no fetus present at all. If any fetus is present, whether the *shilya* is intertwined with it or not, *ein chosheshin le-vlad acher*: there is no concern about another dissolved fetus. So Rabbi Eleazar is in fact the stricter opinion: the only exception he allows is the case of physical intertwining, whereas Rabbi Yochanan is satisfied by the mere presence of any fetus.

## Deformed Fetus

### Animal-Like Fetus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הַמַּפֶּלֶת מִין בְּהֵמָה חַיָּה וָעוֹף, וְשִׁלְיָא עִמָּהֶן, בִּזְמַן שֶׁקְּשׁוּרָה בָּהֶן – אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְוָלָד אַחֵר, אֵינָהּ קְשׁוּרָה בָּהֶן – הֲרֵינִי מֵטִיל עָלֶיהָ חוֹמֶר שְׁנֵי וְלָדוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar:** The periods of purity and impurity observed by a woman after giving birth apply only after delivering a fetus with a human form. With regard to a woman **who expels** a fetus that has the form of **a type of domesticated animal, undomesticated animal, or bird, and** there is **a placenta with them,** the *halakha* is as follows: **When** the placenta is **attached to them, there is no concern for** the existence of **another fetus** that may have had a human form. But if the placenta is **not attached to them,** it is possible there was another fetus that had a human form and so **I** must **impose upon** the woman **the severity of two** types of **births,** i.e., both the longer period of impurity observed after delivering a female, and the shorter period of purity observed after delivering a male.

A *baraita* is cited in support of Rabbi Eleazar's position. *Ha-mapelet min behema chaya ve'of* — if a woman miscarries and the fetus resembles some kind of animal, wild beast, or bird, is this a birth that causes *tumat leidah*? The *Chachamim* hold it does not; Rashi notes we rule like the *Chachamim*. The fetus, being deformed beyond recognition as human, does not make the woman *tamei* with *tumat leidah*.

However, *ve-shilya imahen* — there is a *shilya* accompanying it. Perhaps the *shilya* indicates a second fetus, one that dissolved, and that second fetus was human and constitutes a birth — which would make the woman *tamei*. The *baraita* rules: *bizman shekshura bahen, ein chosheshin le-vlad acher* — if the *shilya* is intertwined with this deformed fetus, we do not suspect another dissolved fetus, and the woman is not *tamei*. *Eina kshura bahen* — if it is not intertwined, we cannot confidently attribute the *shilya* to this deformed fetus, and we must therefore be concerned about another, dissolved birth.

### Two Births

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמַר: שֶׁמָּא נִימּוֹחַ שָׁפִיר שֶׁל שִׁלְיָא, שֶׁמָּא נִימּוֹחָה שִׁלְיָתוֹ שֶׁל שָׁפִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The reason is **that I** can **say:** Perhaps the placenta that was found did not come from the fetus that was found, but from a fetus with a human form. To say this one must be concerned with the possibility that **perhaps** the **fetus of** the **placenta** that was found had a human form, and that fetus **dissolved,** and **perhaps** the **placenta of** the **fetus** that was found **dissolved.**

In that case of non-intertwining, the *baraita* rules: *hareini matil aleha chomer shnei vladot* — I impose upon her the stringencies of two births. How can there be two stringencies from a single ambiguous situation? The answer lies in the structure of *tumat leidah*: giving birth carries both a stringency and a leniency. The stringency is the period of *tumah* — one week for a male birth, two weeks for a female. The leniency is that during the subsequent period of *zivah*, bleeding does not create additional *tumah*. When there is doubt about whether any birth occurred, both stringencies apply simultaneously: she is immediately *tamei* for the maximum period — two weeks, as if she gave birth to a female — because the *shilya* might indicate a dissolved female birth. And if she bleeds later, she remains *tamei* for that as well, because perhaps no birth occurred at all, and any further bleeding is not shielded by the *leniency* of post-birth days.

The *baraita*'s structure follows Rabbi Eleazar precisely: the only exemption from concern about an additional dissolved fetus is *kshura* — physical intertwining. This confirms and supports Rabbi Eleazar's strict position.

The *baraita* introduces the distinction between *shilya* and *shapir*: *shema nimoch shapir shel shilya* — perhaps the *shapir* of the *shilya* dissolved. Artscroll renders this as "the embryo of the *shapir* of the *shilya*," though the more conventional reading is that the *shapir* is the embryonic sac itself. In either case, the mechanism is the same: when you have one *shilya* and one deformed fetus, and they are not intertwined, the possibility exists that the counterpart of each has dissolved — the fetus's *shilya* dissolved, and the *shilya*'s fetus dissolved — leaving you with one of each, but from two different births. This entire discussion continues to support the reading of *shilya* as the amniotic sac rather than the placenta, a matter that warrants further research.

## Bechor Status

### Males < 50%

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְבַכֶּרֶת שֶׁהִפִּילָה שִׁלְיָא – יַשְׁלִיכֶנָּה לִכְלָבִים, וּבַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין תִּקָּבֵר, וְאֵין קוֹבְרִים אוֹתָהּ בְּפָרָשַׁת דְּרָכִים, וְאֵין תּוֹלִין אוֹתָהּ בְּאִילָן, מִפְּנֵי דַּרְכֵי הָאֱמוֹרִי.
>
> **Translation:** If **an animal that was giving birth to its firstborn expelled a placenta, one may cast it to the dogs,** and one does not need to be concerned that the placenta came from a male fetus that has the consecrated status of a firstborn. **But in** the case of **sacrificial** animals the placenta **must be buried,** because it came from a fetus that is assumed to have been sacred. The mishna adds: **But one may neither bury it at an intersection, nor may one hang it on a tree,** superstitious rites intended to prevent the animal from miscarrying again, **due to** the prohibition against following **the ways of the Amorite,** which prohibits Jews from practicing the superstitious rites observed by gentiles.

The *mishnah* rules that when a *mevakeret* — an animal giving birth for the first time — miscarries and produces only a *shilya*, *yashlichenah la-klavim*: you may give it to the dogs, without concern that it is a *bechor* with *kedushah*. The Gemara asks *mai ta'ama* — what is the reason?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אִיקָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אַמֵּי: רוֹב בְּהֵמוֹת יוֹלְדוֹת דָּבָר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בִּבְכוֹרָה, וּמִיעוּט בְּהֵמוֹת דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ בִּבְכוֹרָה, וּמַאי נִיהוּ? נִדְמֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ika, son of Rav Ami, said: The majority of domesticated animals give birth to something that** can be **consecrated** with **firstborn status,** i.e., an animal with the same form as its mother, **but a minority of animals** give birth to **something that cannot** be consecrated with **firstborn status, and what is this?** It is an animal that **resembles** a species other than that of its mother.

Rav Ika son of Rav Ami explains: *rov behemot yoldot sabar hakadosh bivchora u'mi'ut behema davar she'eino kadosh bivchora* — most births do produce animals with *kedushat bechor*, but a minority do not, even among males. What is that minority? *Nidmeh* — a counterfeit birth.

### Counterfeit Birth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל הַיּוֹלְדוֹת יוֹלְדוֹת מֶחֱצָה זְכָרִים וּמֶחֱצָה נְקֵבוֹת, סְמוֹךְ מִיעוּטָא דְּנִדְמֶה לְמֶחֱצָה דִּנְקֵבוֹת, וְהָווּ לְהוּ זְכָרִים מִיעוּטָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** furthermore, with regard to **all gestating** animals, when they **give birth, half** of their offspring are **male and half** are **female.** Therefore, one can **combine the minority** of animals that **resemble** another species **with the half** that are **females, and** arrive at the conclusion that **male** offspring that resemble their species, which are the only offspring that can be consecrated with firstborn status, **are the minority.** Accordingly, one does not need to be concerned that the placenta found was from an animal consecrated with the status of a firstborn.

*Nidmeh* refers to a case where an animal gives birth to offspring that resembles a different species — a sheep that gives birth to something looking like a goat, or a goat giving birth to something resembling a sheep. Rashi formulates it as: *rachel she'yalda min ez o ez she'yalda domeh le-rachel*. In such cases, even if the offspring is male and is literally the firstborn of its mother, it does not carry *kedushat bechor* because it does not appear to be the right species. The Gemara clarifies that Rashi does not claim the offspring actually belongs to a different species, only that it looks different — and that appearance is sufficient to deny *kedushat bechor*.

Given that all births are approximately 50% male and 50% female, and that among the males there is a small additional percentage that are *nidmeh* and thus lack *kedushat bechor*, the probability that any given dissolved *shilya* contains a valid *bechor* is technically less than fifty percent. By applying *rov*, the *mishnah* rules that you need not treat the dissolved *shilya* as a *bechor*, and you may discard it. This is a striking application of *rov*: the margin of difference from 50% is minuscule — perhaps 0.1% — yet it is treated as sufficient to resolve a question of *d'oraita* sanctity. The willingness of the *halacha* to apply *rov* even where no other resolution is available, and where the margin is this slim, is remarkable.

One might raise a further nuance: what about a *tumtum* or *androgynus*, an animal of indeterminate or dual sex? If such a case is categorized not as a *min bifnei atzmo* — a third independent category — but simply as a male or female whose sex is unknown, it would fit into the existing 50/50 framework and not add to the *mi'ut* that tips the scale. The question is left open.

### Sacred Burial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַמּוּקְדָּשִׁים תִּקָּבֵר. מַאי טַעְמָא? רוּבָּא בַּר מִיקְדָּשׁ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** § The continuation of the mishna states: **But in** the case of **sacrificial** animals the placenta **must be buried,** because it came from a fetus that is assumed to have been sacred. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** to assume this? The Gemara answers: It is because the **majority** of the offspring of sacrificial animals **are fit to be sacred,** as, in contrast to firstborn status, both female and male offspring of sacrificial animals can be sacred. Therefore, one must be concerned that the offspring, and its placenta, were sacred.

*U'vimukdashim titkaver* — if the *shilya* came from a sanctified animal, it must be buried. *Mai ta'ama? Rubo d'mikdashu* — the vast majority of what is born from sanctified animals carries *kedushah*, male or female, because sex is irrelevant to the sanctity of offspring of *mikdash* animals. Only the rare *nidmeh* case would lack it. Therefore, the overwhelming probability — far exceeding 50% — is that this dissolved *shilya* carries *kedushah*, and it must be buried accordingly.

## Burial Restrictions

### Health vs Sorcery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין קוֹבְרִים אוֹתָהּ בְּפָרָשַׁת דְּרָכִים, וְאֵין תּוֹלִין אוֹתָהּ בְּאִילָן, מִפְּנֵי דַּרְכֵי הָאֱמוֹרִי. אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַויְיהוּ: כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ רְפוּאָה – אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם דַּרְכֵי הָאֱמוֹרִי, אֵין בּוֹ רְפוּאָה – יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם דַּרְכֵי הָאֱמוֹרִי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna adds: **But one may neither bury it** at an intersection, nor may one hang it on a tree, due to the prohibition against following the ways of the Amorite. The Gemara cites **Abaye and Rava, who both said: Anything that has** an apparently effective **medicinal** purpose or any other logical reason behind it **is not subject to** the prohibition against following **the ways of the Amorite.** But if **it does not have** an apparently effective **medicinal** purpose **it is subject to** the prohibition against following **the ways of the Amorite.**

The *mishnah* concludes: *ve'ein kovrin otah be-parashat drachim* — one does not bury this *shilya* at the crossroads — *ve'ein tolin otah be'ilan* — nor does one hang it from a tree — *mipnei darchei ha-emori* — because of the ways of the Amorites. These were apparently practices aimed at warding off evil spirits or providing some kind of protection, and they are prohibited under the broader prohibition of *u'vechukoteihem lo telechu*.

The Gemara records that Abaye and Rava both say: *kol davar she-yesh bo refuah, ein bo mishum darchei emori* — anything that serves a health purpose is not prohibited as *darchei emori*. *Ein bo mishum refuah, yesh bo mishum darchei emori* — if it has no health rationale, it is prohibited as the ways of the Amorites.

### Tree Remedies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: אִילָן שֶׁמַּשִּׁיר פֵּירוֹתָיו, סוֹקְרוֹ בְּסִיקְרָא וְטוֹעֲנוֹ בַּאֲבָנִים. בִּשְׁלָמָא טוֹעֲנוֹ בַּאֲבָנִים – כִּי הֵיכִי דְּנִיכְחוֹשׁ חֵילֵיהּ, אֶלָּא סוֹקְרוֹ בְּסִיקְרָא אַמַּאי? כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִיחְזְיוּהּ אִינָשֵׁי וְלִיבְעֻי רַחֲמֵי עִילָּוֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If there is **a tree that sheds its fruit** prematurely, **one may paint it with red paint and load it with stones,** even though this is the practice of the Amorites? The Gemara explains the difficulty: **Granted,** it is permitted **to load it with stones,** as one does so for a logical reason, **in order that** the tree's **strength will lessen.** It is possible that the tree shed its fruits prematurely due to excessive blossoming. It taxes the tree to sustain these blossoms, and this may render the tree incapable of sustaining the fruits that subsequently grow from the blossoms. Stones were used to weaken the tree during blossoming, thereby reducing the number of blossoms that it needed to nourish. **But** with regard to **painting it with red paint, for what** benefit is it performed that makes it permitted despite the fact that this was the practice of the Amorites? The Gemara explains: One does so **in order that people will see** the tree **and pray for it.**

The Gemara illustrates this from a *baraita*: if a tree is dropping its fruit, one may *sokro be-sikra* — paint it with red paint — and *ve-touno ba-avanim* — or weigh down its branches with stones. These are not *darchei emori*. The Gemara asks: the stones make obvious rational sense — *ki heikhi di-nikhish cheileih* — it weakens the tree's vigor, presumably counteracting whatever physiological excess was causing premature fruit drop. But what about painting the tree with red paint? That seems to have no rational medical mechanism.

### Prayer & Healing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתַנְיָא: ״וְטָמֵא טָמֵא יִקְרָא״ – צָרִיךְ לְהוֹדִיעַ לָרַבִּים, וְרַבִּים מְבַקְּשִׁים עָלָיו רַחֲמִים. וְכֵן מִי שֶׁאֵירַע בּוֹ דָּבָר – צָרִיךְ לְהוֹדִיעַ לָרַבִּים, וְרַבִּים מְבַקְּשִׁים עָלָיו רַחֲמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: It is derived from the verse: **"And he will cry: Impure, impure"** (Leviticus 13:45), that a leper must publicize the fact that he is ritually impure. **He must announce** his pain **to the masses, and the masses will pray for mercy on his behalf. And likewise, one to whom** any unfortunate **matter happens must announce** it **to the masses, and** then **the masses will pray for mercy on his behalf.**

The Gemara answers: *ki heikhi delikhzu inshi ve-liv'u aleih rakhamei* — so that people will see it and pray for it. Prayer is not superstition. Painting the tree draws people's attention and prompts *tefillah*, which is a legitimate and efficacious intervention. A *baraita* supports this from the case of the *metzora*, who must call out *tamei tamei* — *tzarich lehodi'a le-rabim, ve-rabim mevakshim alav rakhamim* — he must publicize his condition so that people will pray for him. And the *baraita* extends this: anyone who has suffered a tragedy or hardship should make it known — *ve-rabim mevakshim alav rakhamim* — so that the community will pray on his behalf. This is not merely psychological counsel; it is presented as a direct mechanism of *refuah*.

### Rashi's Distinction

How, then, does one draw the line between *yesh bo refuah* and the forbidden *darchei emori*? Based on the Gemara's examples alone, the natural reading would be that the line tracks rational explicability: something with a comprehensible physical mechanism, or with a direct connection to prayer, is permitted; something that is simply superstition — warding off spirits with no coherent rationale — is *darchei emori*.

Rashi, however, frames the distinction differently. He gives as an example of permitted *refuah*: a drink or a potion — *mashkeh otam* — or even *lachisha*, an incantation whispered over a wound. According to Rashi, incantations directed at healing are not *darchei emori*. What is forbidden? *She'osin shelo al ha-choli* — acts performed not in response to an illness, but simply to ward off evil spirits generally, such as burying the *shilya* at a crossroads or hanging it from a tree. According to Rashi, the operative distinction is not method but purpose: if the goal is *refuah* — treating an actual sickness — then even incantations fall within the permitted range. If the goal is generalized spirit-warding without any specific illness being addressed, that is *darchei emori*.

This is a somewhat more expansive position than the Gemara's examples might suggest on their face, and it is worth noting the tension: the Gemara's language of *yesh bo refuah* might naturally be read as "has a health basis," which aligns with rational medical treatment, whereas Rashi reads *yesh bo* as "is performed for the purpose of health," which admits a broader class of folk remedies and incantations. The Gemara itself, with its examples of painting trees to prompt prayer and of the *metzora* announcing his *tumah* to solicit *tefillah*, may point to a middle position: what is required is not strictly scientific mechanism, but some coherent connection — whether physical or theological — between the act and the hoped-for result. Acts that have no such connection and merely invoke supernatural forces arbitrarily are the *darchei emori* that the Torah prohibits.
