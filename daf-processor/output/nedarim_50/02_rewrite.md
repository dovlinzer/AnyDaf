# Nedarim 50 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Yehuda's Radiance

### Three audiences respond

The daf opens by returning briefly to the series of three vignettes concerning Rabbi Yehuda's luminous complexion — described by the Aramaic word *tzahov*, which might suggest a yellowish tinge but almost certainly connotes a radiant, glowing countenance. Three different interlocutors posed the same question to him: his fellow *rabbanan*, a wealthy woman, and a *Tzeduki* (Sadducee). Each received a response calibrated to the questioner — more expansive and measured for some, brisk and dismissive for the heretic.

### Labor brings honor

From this point the Gemara moves forward. We are told that Rabbi Yehuda, when he would go to the *beit midrash*, would carry a barrel or large jug on his shoulders — *shakil gufa al katfeih* — and he would declare, *gedola melacha she-mechabedet et be'aleha*: labor is a great thing because it brings honor to those who perform it. At first glance this seems puzzling. He is, after all, simply transporting something to sit on, a kind of personal shtender. It is not immediately obvious why this constitutes a significant act of *melacha* of the sort that confers great *kavod*. Nevertheless, this was Rabbi Yehuda's lived demonstration of the principle that honorable labor dignifies the worker, however modest the task.

### Shared cloak blessing

The narrative continues with Rabbi Yehuda's wife, who went and acquired wool, *avda glima*, and fashioned a cloak. The arrangement was that when she would go out to the marketplace she wore it, and when Rabbi Yehuda would go to pray he wore it — *michasya umatzli*. On its surface, this passage conveys the remarkable austerity in which Rabbi Yehuda and his wife lived: a single cloak shared between husband and wife. When Rabbi Yehuda would wrap himself in it, he would recite a *bracha*: *Baruch she'atani me'il* — Blessed is God who has wrapped me in this cloak. Two points deserve attention here. First, this is a different *nusach ha-bracha* from the standard blessing recited upon wearing new garments. Second, the Rosh in Berachot cites this passage as proof that the threshold for reciting *Shehecheyanu* upon a new garment — or *Malbish Arumim* — is fundamentally subjective, determined by what brings genuine enjoyment and significance to the individual. For Rabbi Yehuda, who lived in poverty, this simple homespun cloak brought him real joy, and that was sufficient grounds for the blessing, regardless of how others might appraise its value.

### Unisex garment law

The shared cloak also raises a pointed *halachic* question concerning *beged ish u-veged isha* — the prohibition against men wearing women's garments and vice versa. How could Rabbi Yehuda and his wife both wear the same cloak? The implication appears to be that a garment made without specific intention for either a man or a woman — a genuinely unisex article — is permitted for both to wear, and this episode serves as evidence for that position.

## Yehuda's treasure

### Fast day refusal

The Gemara then introduces another story. *Zimna chada gazar Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel ta'anita* — one time Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who served as *nasi* of the *Sanhedrin*, decreed a fast day, presumably on account of drought, as was standard practice familiar from Masechet Ta'anit. Rabbi Yehuda did not appear at the gathering. We know from Ta'anit that the public fast involved bringing the *Sefer Torah* into the town square and conducting communal prayer there, partly to accommodate greater numbers and partly to lend the occasion the character of public supplication. Rabbi Yehuda's absence was noted.

### Refuses garment gift

The explanation offered was *leit lei kisuya* — he has no garment, no *ksut*, with which to appear. It is worth pausing on the terminology: *ksut* here refers to the outer wrap or cloak, as opposed to *beged*, which denotes more of an inner tunic. The distinction has relevance beyond this narrative — there are those who argue that the obligation of *tzitzit* as formulated in the *pasuk* in Devarim (*al arba kanfot ksutecha asher tichaseh bah*) attaches specifically to the outer wrap rather than to a smaller undergarment, which bears on the question of whether a *tallit katan* fulfills the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit* in the same sense as a *tallit gadol*, though this remains a matter of debate.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, himself of the wealthy *nasi* family — a family whose considerable resources, and whose occasional insensitivity to the poverty of *talmidei chachamim*, are documented elsewhere in *Shas*, most notably in the well-known episodes in Berachot where Rabban Gamliel embarrassed Rabbi Yehoshua until the extent of that sage's poverty became apparent — sent Rabbi Yehuda a new shirt. Rabbi Yehuda refused it. *Shadar lei glima velo kibel.* The refusal appears to reflect a kind of pride, or perhaps a principled self-sufficiency: his wife's cloak, however modest, was enough for him. He had said a *bracha* over it.

### Gold discovery

The story reaches its surprising conclusion at the top of daf 50a. Rabbi Yehuda lifted the mat he was sitting on and said to the messenger: *chazi mai ika* — look and see what is here. The *mefaresh* explains: beneath the mat, a miracle had occurred, and the entire space was filled with gold. *Na'asah li nes ve-nitmale kol oto makom zehuvim.* He was not, in other words, impoverished in any material sense. He simply chose not to avail himself of wealth. As the Gemara puts it: *lo nicha lei l'ithenuyei be-hadein alma* — it was not agreeable to him to derive *hana'ah* from this world. The refusal of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's shirt thus was not born of helplessness but of deliberate spiritual detachment from material comfort.

## Rabbi Akiva marriage

### Father's vow

The Gemara now transitions to the famous story of Rabbi Akiva's marriage — a story that appears as well in Ketubot 62b, and it would be worth comparing the two versions for their differences. *Rabbi Akiva ikadsha lei barteh de-Kalba Savua* — Rabbi Akiva married the daughter of Kalba Savua. The name *Kalba Savua* means "the satisfied dog," which is an unusual epithet. Some *mefarshim* suggest that people would come to him as hungry as a dog and leave fully satisfied — *savua* — because he was extraordinarily generous and wealthy. Whether this is the actual etymology or a folk explanation, it situates Kalba Savua as a man of great means.

When Kalba Savua learned of the marriage, he was furious. Rabbi Akiva at this point was poor, illiterate, and a simple shepherd — hardly what he envisioned for his daughter. He therefore made a *neder hana'ah* from all his property: Rabbi Akiva and perhaps his daughter as well would derive no *hana'ah* from his estate. The story thus arrives in Masechet Nedarim organically, though it is in substance an *agadata* about the *tanna'im*. The theme of *hana'ah* also resonates with the preceding episode: Rabbi Yehuda chose not to take *hana'ah* from this world; here, Kalba Savua weaponizes the *neder* to cut off *hana'ah* as a punitive measure.

### Straw house poverty

The young couple went to live together in a *bei tivna* — a straw house — sleeping on straw, *hava ganu be-itivna*. There, Rabbi Akiva would remove straw from her hair. There is a dispute among the *rishonim* here: the *mefaresh* understands that she would remove straw from his hair, but the Ran reads it as Rabbi Akiva removing the straw from his wife's hair — a small act of tenderness and care even within extreme deprivation, the two of them still tending to one another. Rabbi Akiva said to her: *ei hava lei ramina lach Yerushalayim de-dahava* — if I had the means, I would give you a *Yerushalayim shel zahav*, the golden Jerusalem circlet mentioned in the Mishnah in Shabbat — a tiara of gold, possibly engraved with the forms of Jerusalem's buildings or the Temple, a cherished piece of women's jewelry of that era. That this is the aspiration Rabbi Akiva articulates, even from within a straw house, speaks to his devotion to his wife.

### Elijah straw story

Then Elijah appears — as he must in any properly told *aggadic* tale — disguised as an ordinary person, knocking at the door and asking for a small amount of straw, because his wife had just given birth and they had nothing. Rabbi Akiva turns to his wife and says: *chazi gavra de-afilu tivna la it lei* — look, here is a person who does not even have straw. We, who have so little, still have something that another person lacks entirely. This moment of generosity and perspective from within utter poverty seems to be the catalyst. His wife immediately says to him: *zil havi be-rav* — go, study Torah.

What connects the Elijah episode to her instruction to go learn? Perhaps she recognized in that instinctive act of generosity — in his immediate concern for someone even poorer than himself — a greatness of character that demanded cultivation through Torah. It is also worth noting that in other sources, most prominently in *Avot de-Rabbi Natan*, the account of Rabbi Akiva's awakening to Torah study includes the celebrated image of water wearing away a stone: Rabbi Akiva, at the age of forty and still illiterate, observed water dripping onto rock, and reasoned that if soft water could penetrate hard stone, Torah could surely penetrate his heart. Here in our Gemara, it is his wife's instruction that sends him on his way.

### Twelve years learning

*Azal tartesrei shenei kamei de-Rabbi Eliezer ve-Rabbi Yehoshua* — he went for twelve years to study before Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua. Rabbi Eliezer here is Rabbi Eliezer ben Hurcanus, and Rabbi Yehoshua is Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananiah — two of the greatest *tanna'im* of their generation, known for different intellectual strengths, and themselves frequently in dispute with one another. It is notable that Rabbi Akiva studied under both simultaneously.

### Wife permits departure

After twelve years, Rabbi Akiva returned home. As he approached, he overheard a conversation from behind the house: a *rasha* — a wicked man — was saying to his wife that her father had been right, that she had married someone unsuitable, that her husband had been gone for twelve years and left her as *armalut chayot* — a living widow. Her response was striking: she said she wished that he would go and study for another twelve years.

Rabbi Akiva overheard this. *Ho'il ve-yahavat li reshu ahadar le-achorei* — since she has given me permission, I will turn back now and return to study. And he did. He did not stop to greet her. This troubles everyone who encounters the story. How could he not at least pause, spend even a short time at home, see how she was faring? The story is, in some sense, a parable — not necessarily a literal biographical record — but the message it may be conveying concerns the value of *siyum* without interruption, the irreplaceable quality of unbroken continuity in learning. Twenty-four consecutive years are not simply the arithmetic sum of two twelve-year periods with a break between them. What Rabbi Akiva represents, taken to an extreme, is the model of complete and undivided focus. The *rasha*'s observation that she was a *armalut chayita* is not, the Gemara acknowledges implicitly, without some force; but the story insists that this was her choice, that she understood and permitted it. How she sustained herself materially during this period, the text does not address, and these are genuinely difficult questions to sit with.

## Akiva returns

### Twenty-four thousand

*Hadar azal hava tartesrei shenei achranita* — he went back for another twelve years. *Ata be-esrin ve-arba alfin zugei talmidei* — he returned with twenty-four thousand pairs of students. The number is not incidental: one thousand students for each of the twenty-four years he spent in study. Everyone came out to greet him, *nafku kulei alma le-apei*, and she too rose to go out to meet him.

### Wicked man challenge

The same *rasha* from before appeared and said to her: *ve-at le-heicha* — and where are you going, as if to say, you really think you are actually his wife? She responded: *yodea tzaddik nefesh behemto* — a righteous person knows the soul of his animal. The saying is cryptic. One reading: just as a *tzaddik* can understand even the inner life of his animal, how much more certainly does he know me, his wife. Another reading, more self-deprecating: I have been living like a *behema*, with almost nothing, but even so the *tzaddik* is aware of that. There is something unsettling about the comparison, and the text does not resolve it cleanly.

### Wife deserves credit

When the crowd gathered around Rabbi Akiva and his wife was being pushed aside by the *rabbanan* pressing close, Rabbi Akiva said: *hanichu lah* — leave her be. *Sheli ve-shelachem shelah hu* — what is mine, and what is all of yours, belongs to her. Everything his students owed to him, he owed to her. The acknowledgment, rendered in a single sentence before the assembled thousands, is one of the more remarkable moments of public recognition in the Talmudic corpus. It comes at genuine cost to her — the sacrifice of a normal marriage, of family life, of presence — and Rabbi Akiva does not minimize that cost. The story of course raises larger questions about the tension between total commitment to *talmud Torah* and the obligations of married life, questions that have not become easier in subsequent generations.

## Six riches sources

### Vow annulment

*Shama Kalba Savua* — Kalba Savua heard of Rabbi Akiva's prominence. Twenty-four years earlier he had made a *neder* to ensure his son-in-law derived nothing from his property. Now that son-in-law had become the Rabbi Akiva known throughout *Shas* and the *Mishnayot*. *Ata ve-isha'il al nidrei ve-ishtarei* — he came and asked to have his vow annulled, and it was annulled.

This raises an important *halachic* difficulty. A *mishna* later in the tractate, at daf 64, rules *ein potchin be-nolad* — one cannot use a newly arisen circumstance as the *petach*, the opening, to undo a *neder*. If the rationale for annulling Kalba Savua's vow is that Rabbi Akiva turned out to be a great Torah scholar, that would seem to be precisely a *nolad* — a new fact that had not existed at the time of the vow. The resolution, apparently, is that the potential was always there. The greatness was *not* new; it was latent from the beginning. Kalba Savua could have known, and in some sense should have known, even at the time he made the vow, that this man had within him what was needed to become who he became. This is itself a striking claim: that Rabbi Akiva's genius and spiritual capacity were present even when he was an illiterate shepherd — that his wife saw what his father-in-law could not. The doubled expression *ishtarei ishtarei* may suggest something about the annulment's completeness or the emphasis with which it was granted; there is a suggestion from the Hagahos Ya'avetz that one instance of the word should perhaps be deleted, and the Ran and ArtScroll translations may reflect a reading where the annulment required more than a single process.

### From Kalba Savua

*Min shita milei i'atar Rabbi Akiva* — from six sources Rabbi Akiva became wealthy. The first is *min Kalba Savua*: once the *neder* was dissolved, his now proud father-in-law supported him generously. There is something almost ironic in this: Kalba Savua had always, it seems, wanted a great rabbinic son-in-law; he simply failed to see the potential standing before him. His daughter saw what he could not.

### Shipwreck treasure

The second source: *min ila de-sfinta* — from the mast of a ship. The Gemara explains that every ship contains a hidden cavity in the mast where valuables are concealed. One time, Rabbi Akiva was walking along the beach — *inshuya al kef yama* — and he had previously paid four *zuz* to some sailors to bring him back whatever they might find. They found nothing except a *gavza*, a chest or coffer, washed up on the shore, which they brought to him without opening. It was found to be full of gold coins. The sailors are presented as notably honorable — they brought back exactly what they found, without helping themselves to the contents.

One might ask whether there is any obligation to return such found property. The question is in fact live. The doctrine of *ye'ush* — renunciation of ownership through despair of recovery — would arguably apply once a ship sinks to the sea floor; the original owner, having given up hope of recovery, loses legal title, and the finder acquires the object. What the modern law of salvage looks like, and how far back the practical insistence on retained ownership of sunken vessels extends, is a separate question: the history of maritime insurance, going back to the origins of Lloyd's of London, reflects a consistent expectation that ownership of lost cargo does not simply evaporate. Whether the full conditions for *ye'ush* are met in a given case, and what a *posek* would say about underwater salvage today, are questions that merit their own careful *teshuvot*.

### Arabian traders

The third source involves a ship belonging to traders identified as *Sarukita* people — understood here as Arabian merchants — whose ship was tossed about at sea, *tavat sfintayeh*. The entire commercial cargo was placed into a chest, and that same chest was later found and brought to Rabbi Akiva. The narratives about shipwrecks and sea-chests cluster together and likely reflect both independent traditions about Rabbi Akiva's wealth and the special association of maritime fortune with the blessing of Zevulun — *sefunei tmunei chol*, things hidden in the sand and sea that become a source of riches.

### Matronita's gift

The fourth source is *min Matronita* — from a wealthy Roman noblewoman. The Ran quotes the full story: Rabbi Akiva and his students found themselves in need of funds and went to borrow from this *matronita*. She agreed, but stipulated an unusual condition: God and the sea would serve as *arevim*, as guarantors. If Rabbi Akiva could not repay her, she would look to them. A time for repayment was fixed. When the date arrived, Rabbi Akiva was ill — the Rosh notes explicitly that he fell sick at precisely the time payment was due — and could not pay. The *matronita* went down to the beach and prayed: You know that Rabbi Akiva is sick and cannot repay. You are the guarantor; I am holding You to it. Immediately, the daughter of Caesar was struck with a sudden fit — *nishtatit bito shel Keisar* — and she seized a chest full of precious stones and gold coins and threw it into the sea. The waves carried it to the exact spot where the *matronita* was sitting. She took it and went home.

Later, when Rabbi Akiva recovered, he came to repay the loan in full. She told him: *hazarti etzel ha-arev* — I went to the guarantor, and he paid it all, and more. She returned to Rabbi Akiva the surplus beyond what was owed him, and from this he became wealthy. The narrative is remarkable not only as a story of miraculous divine payment but as a demonstration of Torah's claim that *tzaddikim* operate within a framework where God's involvement in human affairs is direct and responsive.

### Turnusrufus wife

The fifth source is *min ishto shel Turnusrufus* — from the wife of Turnusrufus, the notorious Roman general, one of the great *resha'im* of the Talmudic period, and — as is noted — the very man responsible for Rabbi Akiva's eventual martyrdom. The Ran quotes this story as well: Turnusrufus came home troubled, and his wife asked what was wrong. He said that Rabbi Akiva was constantly taunting him publicly. She said: the God of these people hates licentiousness — give me permission and I will cause him to stumble. She went to seduce Rabbi Akiva. When Rabbi Akiva first saw her, he did three things: he spat, he wept, and he laughed. She asked him to explain. He said he would explain two but not the third. He spat because she came from a *tipa sruha* — a putrid drop, as the *mishna* says of every human being. He wept because such beauty would in the end be consigned to the earth and come to nothing. The laugh he did not explain at the time — but the Ran notes that he had seen through *Ruach Ha-Kodesh* that she would eventually convert and become his wife. Rabbi Akiva resisted the seduction, and through some further sequence of events, derived wealth from the encounter. The end of this story — including her conversion, their marriage, and what that says about Rabbi Akiva's later life — would require tracing across multiple *sugyot* and raises genuinely difficult questions about how the various biographical traditions about Rabbi Akiva fit together.

## Monkey pearls

### Pearl-finding monkey

The sixth source involves *Rav Gamda*, who similarly paid four *zuz* to sailors to bring him whatever they could find. They found nothing except a monkey, *kofa*, which they brought to him. The monkey ran off and began digging — *chafru batrei* — and was found to have been sitting atop a cache of pearls, *riva al marganita*. The honorable sailors brought all of the pearls back to him. Thus: a monkey led to discovery of precious gems, and this constitutes the sixth and final source of Rabbi Akiva's wealth.

### Torah vessel debate

Now the Gemara introduces a new episode. The daughter of Caesar says to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananiah: *Torah mefo'arah bi-chli mecho'ar* — such glorious Torah dwelling in such an ugly vessel. Rabbi Yehoshua was famously unbeautiful in appearance, and the Caesar's daughter poses the challenge with an almost philosophical directness: why should something so luminous be housed in such undistinguished physical form? That the daughter of a Roman emperor would regard the Torah as *mefo'arah* — magnificent — is itself noteworthy and the Gemara does not explain it further.

### Wine vessel analogy

Rabbi Yehoshua responded with a question: in your father's house, what do you store your wine in? She said: in earthenware jars, *manei de-pachra*. He said: everyone stores wine in earthenware — and even the imperial household does the same? Surely you should be storing it in vessels of silver and gold. She went and transferred the wine into silver and gold vessels — *ramat chamra be-manei de-chaspa ve-dahava* — and it went bad, *ve-sari*. Rabbi Yehoshua said: *af Oraita kein* — the Torah is just like this. Throughout *Shas*, Torah is compared to wine — something that begins well and deepens and improves with age. The *mishna* at the end of Kiddushin makes the parallel point that unlike physical crafts, which deteriorate as a person ages and loses strength and fine motor capacity, *talmud Torah* is a discipline in which one grows sharper and wiser with years of sustained effort.

She pressed further: but there are people who are both handsome and learned — *ve-ha'ika shapiran ve-gmiri*? He replied: if they were as ugly as I am, *havu gmiri tfei* — they would be even greater scholars.

## Shmuel criticism

### Judgment questioned

A woman came before Rav Yehuda in Nehardea for a legal judgment, *de-atai le-kamei de-Rav Yehuda min Nehardea le-dina*, and was found liable — *ve-it-haivat min dina*. She then challenged Rav Yehuda by asking whether his teacher Shmuel would have ruled the same way. Shmuel was the *rosh yeshiva* of Nehardea, so a litigant in Nehardea invoking Shmuel's name carried a local resonance. Rav Yehuda had studied under both Rav and Shmuel.

### Physical criticism

When Rav Yehuda asked whether she had known Shmuel, she answered in the affirmative — and then described not his *Torah* or his method of judgment, but his physical appearance: short, obese, dark-complexioned, with prominent teeth.

### Excommunication death

Rav Yehuda said to her: *u-ve-vizui ka atit* — did you come here to disgrace him? The parallel elsewhere in *Shas* is instructive: when an Amora traveling from Bavel to Eretz Yisrael was asked about the great teacher of his yeshiva and referred to Rav by the name *Abba Aricha* rather than his honorific, he was rebuked for failing to appreciate and properly name his master. This woman had presumably seen Shmuel in the streets of Nehardea but had no perception of who he truly was — his depth of *Torah*, his authority as a judge and legal decisor. Her challenge to Rav Yehuda's ruling rested not on a substantive legal objection but on a dismissive, superficial impression of the teacher from whom that ruling ultimately derived. Rav Yehuda placed the woman in *nidui* — excommunication — and the Gemara reports starkly: *paka u-mita* — she exploded and died.

## Medicinal eggs

### Turmaita definition

The Gemara returns to material from the Mishnah concerning the *beita turmaita*. The Mishnah had stated that even one who takes a vow not to eat *tavshil* — a cooked dish — may still eat *beita turmaita*. The Gemara now asks: *mai beita turmaita*? Shmuel explains that it is an egg prepared through an extraordinarily laborious process: it is placed alternately a thousand times in hot water and a thousand times in cold water — *alfa zimnei be-maya chamimei ve-alfa zimnei be-maya kerirei* — until it shrinks to very small size, *ad de-mizutra*, small enough to swallow whole. The reason it does not qualify as a *tavshil* may be that it is not something eaten with bread in the ordinary sense, or more precisely because it functions as a medicine rather than food. Its medicinal purpose is explicit: if one has intestinal trouble, the egg will absorb it on the way down and carry it out on the way up — or rather, on the way through. Because of the extraordinary difficulty of preparation, a skilled slave capable of making it was worth a thousand *dinarei*.

### Shmuel's medical use

The Gemara notes that Shmuel himself would use the *kulcha* vegetable — a different plant — for the same diagnostic and therapeutic purposes, testing himself with it to assess his own medical condition. He pressed this practice to such lengths that the members of his household were tearing their hair out with worry over how much pain he was enduring in the process.

## Fig jam dispute

### Jam comparison

The Gemara then discusses a case from the Mishnah in Ma'asrot concerning *kluvsin* — a variety of figs from which a kind of *laftei*, a thick jam or spread, is made. One who vows not to eat *bana'ot sheva* (another variety) may not eat *kluvsin*, and vice versa, because these are distinct species.

### Rabbi's disinterest

A legal dispute is brought before Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. A man had given his slave to another person on the representation that the slave could prepare a thousand kinds of fig jam. The slave produced only eight hundred kinds. The aggrieved party brought the matter to Rabbi for adjudication. Rabbi's reaction is memorable: *avoteinu amru nashinu tova, anu afilu be-eineinu lo ra'inu* — our ancestors said they had forgotten good things, and even by our own eyes we haven't seen them. The point being that Rabbi, a man of considerable wealth and standing, had absolutely no frame of reference for these eight hundred, let alone a thousand, varieties of fig jam. The entire dispute seemed to him bewildering in its premises. Why was anyone litigating over two hundred missing varieties of fig jam?

### Wedding feast

*Rabbi avid lei hilula le-Rabbi Shimon be-Rabbi* — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi made a wedding feast for his son Rabbi Shimon. The scale was prodigious: the *beit ganana*, the garden house or wedding canopy, alone cost 24,000 *ribvan dinarei* — an enormous figure. The celebration was lavish. And yet — *ve-lo azmanei le-Bar Kappara* — he did not invite Bar Kappara.

### Bar Kappara's rebuke

Bar Kappara's response was pointed. He said: *im le-overei r'tzono kach, le-osei r'tzono al achat kama ve-kama* — if this is the kind of feast put on for those who merely transgress God's will, how much more so will it be for those who fulfill it. Then, after Rabbi invited him in response to this, Bar Kappara sharpened the point: *le-osei r'tzono ba-olam ha-zeh kach, le-olam ha-ba al achat kama ve-kama* — if those who do God's will in this world receive such celebration, how much more extraordinary will be the reward of *olam ha-ba*. The Gemara breaks off here, and we resume from this point in the next session.
