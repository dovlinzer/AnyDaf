# Pesachim 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Collateral & Pesa…

### Welcome & Overview

Today's material constitutes a somewhat standalone *sugya* — a self-contained legal unit — that spans nearly the entirety of the *daf*. By way of introduction, it is worth noting that this discussion is moderately complex, dealing with the intersection of financial law and the laws of *chametz* over *Pesach*. Those around the table will inevitably be a collaborative resource as we make our way through.

### Scope & Framework

The material runs from the *mishna* at the bottom of 30b through the *mishna* at the bottom of 31b, with the bulk of 31a constituting an elaborate discussion of the relationship between creditors, debtors, and collateral. The central question is: when is a creditor considered to have possession of the collateral placed against a loan, and when can the default presumptions governing that relationship be altered by the specific language used at the time of the loan? Along the way, the *sugya* examines implications for cases involving inheritance, all of which ultimately serves to clarify the positions articulated in the *mishna*.

### Mishnah: Two Cases

The *mishna* at the bottom of 30b presents two cases. The first: a *nochri* who lends money to a Jew and takes the Jew's *chametz* as collateral — *achar haPesach mutar behana'ah*, after *Pesach* that *chametz* is permitted to the Jew to derive benefit from. The essential inference is that the *chametz* must have been sufficiently considered in the possession of the *nochri* — the creditor — already from the moment the loan was made, such that the Jew is not considered to have violated *bal yera'eh u'bal yimatze* over *Pesach*.

The second case is the converse: a Jew who lends money to a *nochri* and takes the *nochri*'s *chametz* as collateral — *achar haPesach asur behana'ah*, after *Pesach* that *chametz* is prohibited for the Jew to derive benefit from. The operative principle is the same: if the creditor has sufficient claim on the collateral to be considered its possessor, then the Jew who holds the *nochri*'s *chametz* as collateral is treated as if that *chametz* was in his domain over *Pesach*, rendering it *chametz she'avar alav haPesach*.

By way of background, the rabbis enacted a decree that *chametz she'avar alav haPesach* — *chametz* over which *Pesach* has passed — is prohibited not merely for eating but even for deriving any benefit, in accordance with the view of Rabbi Shimon. This prohibition serves in some sense to add teeth to the biblical prohibitions of *bal yera'eh u'bal yimatze* even after the fact.

## Abaye vs. Rava

### Lemafrea vs. Mikan

Three lines from the bottom of 30b, the *Gemara* introduces an *itmara* — a transmitted Amoraic dispute: *ba'al chov: Abaye amar lemafrea hu goveh, veRava amar mikan ulehaba hu goveh*. That is, with respect to a creditor's acquisition of collateral: Abaye holds that the creditor acquires the collateral *lemafrea* — retroactively — meaning the item is considered to have been in the creditor's possession all along from the inception of the loan. Rava holds that the creditor acquires *mikan ulehaba* — prospectively — meaning the collateral only officially passes into the creditor's possession at the moment the borrower defaults and repayment becomes impossible.

### Scope of Dispute

The *Gemara* proceeds to clarify the precise circumstances in which this dispute applies. Where the *borrower* is the one who took action with the collateral — consecrating it (*hikdish* it) to the *Beit HaMikdash* or selling it — all agree (*kulei alma lo pligi*) that the lender may come and seize it from whoever now holds it, or redeem it. In the case of *hekdesh*, the *Gemara* notes in passing (citing the *mishna* in *Arachin*) that the lender would add a nominal *dinar* and use that to redeem the item — out of a degree of deference to sanctified property not accorded to an ordinary third-party buyer.

The dispute between Abaye and Rava applies specifically where it is the *lender* — not the borrower — who sold or consecrated the collateral item. In that situation alone does the question of when acquisition takes effect become critical.

### Svara: Both Sides

The *Gemara* articulates the internal reasoning (*svara*) of each position. Abaye reasons: *keivan demata zamnei velo parei, iglai milta lemafrea deme'ikara bireshutei hava ka'ei* — once the time for repayment arrived and the borrower did not pay, it was retroactively revealed that from the very beginning the collateral had been in the lender's possession. The lender therefore acted entirely properly in selling it or consecrating it.

Rava reasons: *keivan de'ilu hava lei zuzei hava mesalek lehu bizuzei, ishtakhakh dehashta ka kaneh* — given that throughout the entire duration of the loan, had the borrower possessed the funds he would have been able to repay and reclaim the collateral, it follows that until the moment of actual default, the collateral was never truly the lender's. It is only now, when it becomes definitively clear that repayment is impossible, that the collateral passes into the lender's possession. Up to that point, the theoretical possibility of repayment precluded any firm claim.

## Reuven & Shimon

### The Sale Setup

At this point the *Gemara* raises a challenge from a case cited by Rami bar Chama: *Reuven she'machar sadeh leShimon b'achrayut*. Reuven sold a field to Shimon *b'achrayut* — with a warranty. *Achrayut* here means that Reuven stipulated that if his own creditor were to come and seize the field from Shimon on account of a debt Reuven owed, Reuven would repay Shimon whatever Shimon was required to pay. As Rashi explains, this is the sort of provision a buyer might insist upon when acquiring property from a seller who carries outstanding debts.

At the same time, *vezakfan alav bemilveh* — Shimon did not actually have the funds to pay for the field at the time of purchase. He therefore wrote Reuven a promissory note obligating himself to pay the price of the field, effectively seller-financing the transaction. The field passed to Shimon, and Reuven held a note for the purchase price.

### Creditor Arrives

Reuven then dies. His creditor arrives and seeks to seize the field from Shimon on account of Reuven's outstanding debt. Shimon, rather than surrendering the field, pays off Reuven's creditor directly in money, thereby preventing the seizure.

The heirs of Reuven now approach Shimon and argue: our father left behind a debt — the purchase price of the field — which you owe us. Moreover, they contend, *metaltlei d'yatmi leba'al chov lo mishtabdei* — the personal property (in this case, the monetary debt owed to the estate) that orphans inherit cannot be subjugated to their father's creditor. Consequently, Shimon finds himself in the position of having paid Reuven's creditor once with money, and now being compelled to pay the value of the field a second time to Reuven's heirs. He has, in effect, been made to pay twice.

### Rava's Solution

Rava intervenes with a practical suggestion: *ee pike'ach Shimon, magbei lehu ara vehadar gabei lah minaihu* — if Shimon were shrewd, he would give the field to Reuven's heirs as payment of his debt to them, and then turn around and reclaim the field from them in order to pay Reuven's creditor.

The mechanism for this is a principle cited in the name of Rav Nachman: *yetomim shegavu karka bechovot avihem, ba'al chov chozer vegoveh otah mehen* — when orphans have collected land in satisfaction of their father's debts, the father's creditor may come back and collect that same land from them. By this route, Shimon would surrender the field to the heirs (satisfying his debt to them) and then recover the field from the heirs via the *achrayut* stipulation in order to pay the creditor — ending up with the loss of only the field rather than twice the field's value.

### Challenge to Rava

The *Gemara* now presses on Rava's own position. *Ei amart bishlama lemafrea hu goveh* — had this been Abaye's position, Rav Nachman's ruling would be perfectly coherent: since the lender acquires retroactively, the field is considered as though it was in the creditor's possession already during Reuven's lifetime, and therefore the heirs who received it are holding something that was already effectively the creditor's. *Ela ei amart mikan ulehaba hu goveh, amai chozer vegoveh otah mehen* — but if you hold that the creditor only acquires from the time of default onward, how can the creditor reach back and collect from the heirs? The heirs, at the time they received the field, appear to have acquired it as a fresh transaction; *havei keman dezavin yatmi nichsei dami* — it is as though the orphans themselves purchased the property, and if they had purchased property, it would not be subjugated to their father's creditor. This is a pointed difficulty for Rava.

### Stipulation resolves

The *Gemara* resolves the difficulty: *shani hatam*, this case is different, *de'amar lehu: ki heichi demishtabdina leh le'avuchon, mishtabdina nami leba'al chov de'avuchon* — because Shimon explicitly said to the heirs: just as I am obligated to your father, so too am I obligated to your father's creditor. This explicit stipulation creates a chain of obligation that operates independently of the general rule of *mikan ulehaba*.

The basis for this is the principle of Rabbi Natan, cited in a *braita*: *minayin lenoseh bechavero maneh vechavero bechavero maneh, shemotzi'in mizeh venotnin lazeh* — how do we know that when A is owed a *maneh* by B, and B is owed a *maneh* by C, we may extract from C and give to A? The *pasuk* teaches: *venatan la'asher asham lo* — he shall give to the one to whom he is indebted. This creates a recognized chain of debt through which an original creditor may reach a later obligor, provided the requisite stipulation was made. The present case thus works for Rava not because of the general principle of retroactive acquisition, but because of this specific and explicit undertaking that created a direct link between Shimon and Reuven's creditor.

## Back to Mishnah

### Problem for Rava

Having worked through the implications of Rava's position in the case of Reuven and Shimon, the *Gemara* returns to the *mishna* and challenges Rava directly. The *mishna* states that when a *nochri* loans to a Jew with *chametz* as collateral, the *chametz* is *mutar behana'ah* after *Pesach* — implying that the *chametz* was considered in the *nochri*'s possession during *Pesach*. *Ei amart bishlama lemafrea hu goveh* — for Abaye, this is perfectly understandable: the creditor acquired retroactively, and so the *chametz* was in the *nochri*'s domain throughout *Pesach*. *Ela ei amart mikan ulehaba hu goveh, amai mutar behana'ah?* — but for Rava, how can this be permitted? *Bireshuta deYisrael hava ka'ei* — during *Pesach* the *chametz* was in the Jew's possession, rendering it *chametz she'avar alav haPesach*.

### Okimta: Deposit

The *Gemara* resolves this with an *okimta*: *hacha bemai askinan? Keshehirhino etzlo* — the case in the *mishna* must be one where the Jew physically deposited the *chametz* with the *nochri*. Even if Rava holds that in general the creditor does not acquire the collateral until default, in this particular situation the Jew transferred the *chametz* into the *nochri*'s physical domain. That act of deposit, even without a formal *kinyan*, is sufficient to remove the item from the Jew's *reshut* in a meaningful enough sense that the Jew does not violate *bal yera'eh u'bal yimatze*, and the *chametz* is therefore *mutar behana'ah* after *Pesach*.

As Rashi explains, the rationale combines the formal collateral arrangement (*torat mashkon*) with the principle that *ba'al chov koneh mashkon* — a creditor acquires the collateral. The practical upshot is that when the item is physically in the lender's domain, even under Rava's view, the combination of physical transfer and the collateral relationship is sufficient to render it not in the Jew's possession for *chametz* purposes. The prohibition of *lo yera'eh lecha velo yimatze lecha* is understood as requiring presence in one's domain (*reshut*), not merely legal ownership — and here the *chametz* is genuinely out of the Jew's *reshut*, even if ownership remains technically ambiguous until default.

It is worth noting that this entire prohibition — *chametz she'avar alav haPesach* — is *derabbanan*. The rabbis, who enacted the decree, have the authority to delineate precisely under what circumstances it applies and under what circumstances it does not, including calibrating it according to the degree of physical and legal control a Jew exercises over the *chametz* in question.

## Tannaim: Meir & T.K.

### Braita: Jew→Nokhri

Having established the positions of Abaye and Rava, the *Gemara* attempts to trace a Tannaic precedent for this debate: *leima ketannai*. A *braita* is cited: *Yisrael shehilvah lenochri al chametzo, le'achar haPesach, eino over* — a Jew who loans to a *nochri* with the *nochri*'s *chametz* as collateral, after *Pesach*, does not transgress *chametz she'avar alav haPesach*, according to the *Tanna Kamma*. But *mishum Rabbi Meir amru over* — Rabbi Meir holds that the Jew does transgress.

### Explain via A/R?

One might naturally propose that this Tannaic dispute maps directly onto the Abaye/Rava debate: the *Tanna Kamma*, who says the Jew does not transgress, holds *mikan ulehaba* — the creditor only acquires upon default, and so during *Pesach* the *chametz* remained in the *nochri*'s possession; Rabbi Meir holds *lemafrea* — retroactively the Jew already owned the collateral throughout *Pesach*. However, the *Gemara* questions this alignment: *vetisbra?* Can this really be so? *Eima seifa* — we must look at the second half of the *braita*.

### Braita: Nokhri→Jew

The *seifa* states: *aval nochri shehilvah leYisrael al chametzo, le'achar haPesach, divrei hakol over* — but when a *nochri* loans to a Jew with the Jew's *chametz* as collateral, everyone agrees the Jew transgresses. This is a problem for a simple mapping onto Abaye and Rava. If the *Tanna Kamma* in the *reisha* holds *mikan ulehaba* (creditor does not acquire until default), and Rabbi Meir holds *lemafrea* (creditor acquires retroactively), then in the *seifa* — where it is now a *nochri* lending to a Jew — the *Tanna Kamma* should say the *nochri* acquires the collateral retroactively (freeing the Jew from the prohibition) and Rabbi Meir should say *mikan ulehaba* (keeping it in the Jew's *reshut*). The *seifa* should present reversed positions. Instead, both agree the Jew transgresses — meaning the alignment with Abaye and Rava is not straightforward, and a different explanatory framework is needed.

## Rabbi Yitzchak

### Biblical Derivation

The alternative framework is introduced through the position of Rabbi Yitzchak. The *Gemara* asks: *minayin leba'al chov shekoneh mashkon* — from where do we know at all that a creditor acquires the collateral? Rabbi Yitzchak derives it from the *pasuk* in *Devarim* (24): *hashev tashiv lo et ha'avot kevo hashemesh veshachav besalmato uverachecha* — you must return the collateral garment to the borrower at night so he can sleep in it and bless you — *ulecha tihyeh tzedakah lifnei Hashem Elokecha* — and this shall be an act of charity before God. The innovative interpretation of Rabbi Yitzchak is: *im eino koneh mashkon, tzedakah minayin?* — if the garment truly belongs to the borrower all along, how is returning it to him an act of *tzedakah*? One only performs *tzedakah* by giving something of one's own. The very fact that the *Torah* calls the return of the collateral *tzedakah* proves that the creditor actually owns the collateral — *mikan leba'al chov shekoneh mashkon*.

### T.K.: Jew-Jew only

The *Tanna Kamma* accepts Rabbi Yitzchak's principle but limits its application: *hani milei Yisrael miYisrael hu dekarina beih ulecha tihyeh tzedakah* — this principle of *ba'al chov koneh mashkon* applies only where both parties are Jewish, because only in a Jew-to-Jew loan does the concept of *tzedakah* as commanded in the *Torah* apply. The *mitzvah* of *tzedakah* in this context is specifically to one's Jewish brother (*achicha*). Therefore, in a Jew-to-*nochri* loan, where there is no operative *mitzvah* of *tzedakah* from Jew to non-Jew, the derivation of *ba'al chov koneh mashkon* falls away, and the Jew does not acquire the *nochri*'s collateral.

### R. Meir: Kal Vachomer

Rabbi Meir disagrees, applying a *kal vachomer*: if a Jew acquires collateral from another Jew (*Yisrael miYisrael kani*), then in a Jew-to-*nochri* loan, *lo kol sheken* — how much more so should the Jew acquire the *nochri*'s collateral. The reasoning of the *kal vachomer* is not made fully explicit, but the direction is clear: Rabbi Meir sees the Jew-Jew acquisition as the baseline, and the Jew-*nochri* case as stronger rather than weaker.

### Nokhri never acquires

This framework resolves the *seifa* of the *braita*. When a *nochri* lends to a Jew — *aval nochri shehilvah leYisrael al chametzo, divrei hakol over* — everyone agrees the Jew transgresses. The reason is: *vadai nochri miYisrael lo kani* — it is certain that a *nochri* taking collateral from a Jew never acquires that collateral at all. There is no *pasuk* of *ulecha tihyeh tzedakah* applying to a *nochri* creditor, and there is no *kal vachomer* that would extend acquisition to the *nochri* side. Therefore, even if the Jew deposited the *chametz* with the *nochri*, the *nochri* never truly becomes its owner, and the *chametz* remains legally in the Jew's domain throughout *Pesach* — a fact about which both *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Meir agree. The debate between them pertains only to the Jew-to-*nochri* direction.

## Me'achshav Principle

### Okimta: Stipulation

Having established that a *nochri* lending to a Jew never acquires the collateral, the *Gemara* must now revisit the *mishna*, which states that when a *nochri* loans to a Jew with *chametz* as collateral, the *chametz* is *mutar behana'ah* after *Pesach*. But we have just concluded that a *nochri* never acquires the collateral — so how can the *chametz* be *mutar*? If it remained the Jew's throughout *Pesach*, it should be *chametz she'avar alav haPesach*.

The *Gemara* answers with a new *okimta*: *lo kashya — ha de'amar leih me'achshav, ha delo amar leih me'achshav* — there is no contradiction. The case in the *mishna* where the *chametz* is *mutar* must be one where the *nochri* explicitly said to the Jew *me'achshav* — from now, this is yours — explicitly stipulating at the time of the loan that the *chametz* is transferred into the Jew's possession immediately. In such a case, even under the framework of Rabbi Yitzchak and the *Tanna Kamma*, the explicit declaration overrides the default rule and does cause the *chametz* to transfer. The case where no such stipulation was made follows the default rule and the *chametz* remains the *nochri*'s (or more precisely, remains not-the-Jew's).

### Braita: Deposit

In support of the distinction introduced by the *me'achshav* stipulation, the *Gemara* cites a *braita*: *nochri shehirhin patporni eitzel Yisrael, eino over* — a *nochri* who deposits a *patporni* (a significant or fancy loaf of bread — presumably used as an example of collateral worth pledging) with a Jew does not cause the Jew to transgress *chametz she'avar alav haPesach*. *Ve'im amar lo higaticha, over* — but if the *nochri* said to the Jew *higaticha* — "it has arrived in your possession," meaning it is now yours — then the Jew does transgress.

### Inference: Words matter

The *Gemara* draws the obvious inference: *mai shna reisha umai shna seifa?* — what is the difference between the first clause and the second clause? The only difference is that in the second clause, the *nochri* explicitly declared *higaticha* — a statement of immediate transfer equivalent to *me'achshav*. This confirms that explicit verbal stipulation at the time of collateral transfer has legal effect, changing the ownership status of the pledged item. *Shma mina* — this may be derived from here.

## Stores & Presumption

### Jewish store

The *sugya* concludes with a *braita* establishing practical presumptions of ownership where chametz is found after *Pesach* in ambiguous circumstances. The first case: a store belonging to a Jew, with inventory belonging to a Jew, into which non-Jewish workers enter and exit. If *chametz* is found there after *Pesach* — *asur behana'ah*, it is prohibited to derive benefit from it, and certainly prohibited for eating. The presumption is that any *chametz* found there was the Jew's *chametz* that was not disposed of before *Pesach*, rather than the lunch brought in by a non-Jewish worker. The non-Jewish workers' occasional presence does not create sufficient doubt to override the presumption that the contents of a Jewish-owned store with Jewish inventory belong to the Jewish owner.

### Non-Jewish store

The converse case: a store belonging to a *nochri*, with inventory belonging to a *nochri*, into which Jewish workers enter and exit. If *chametz* is found there after *Pesach* — *mutar be'achilah*, it is permitted even for eating, and certainly permitted for benefit. Here the presumption runs the other way: the contents of a *nochri*-owned store with *nochri* inventory are presumed to belong to the *nochri*, and the Jewish workers' entry does not raise sufficient suspicion that the *chametz* found there was brought in and left by a Jewish worker — which would itself involve a transgression. We follow the default presumption of the store's ownership, and the *chametz* is therefore treated as the *nochri*'s throughout *Pesach*, rendering it entirely permissible after *Pesach*.
