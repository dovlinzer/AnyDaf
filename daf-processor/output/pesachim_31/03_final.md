# Pesachim 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Collateral & Pesach

### Welcome & Overview

Today's material constitutes a somewhat standalone *sugya* — a self-contained legal unit — that spans nearly the entirety of the *daf*. By way of introduction, it is worth noting that this discussion is moderately complex, dealing with the intersection of financial law and the laws of *chametz* over *Pesach*. Those around the table will inevitably be a collaborative resource as we make our way through.

### Scope & Framework

The material runs from the *mishna* at the bottom of 30b through the *mishna* at the bottom of 31b, with the bulk of 31a constituting an elaborate discussion of the relationship between creditors, debtors, and collateral. The central question is: when is a creditor considered to have possession of the collateral placed against a loan, and when can the default presumptions governing that relationship be altered by the specific language used at the time of the loan? Along the way, the *sugya* examines implications for cases involving inheritance, all of which ultimately serves to clarify the positions articulated in the *mishna*.

### Mishnah: Two Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ גּוֹי שֶׁהִלְוָה אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל עַל חֲמֵצוֹ, אַחַר הַפֶּסַח — מוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה. וְיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁהִלְוָה אֶת גּוֹי עַל חֲמֵצוֹ, אַחַר הַפֶּסַח — אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **a gentile lent money to a Jew,** and the Jew gave him **leavened bread as** collateral until after Passover, and **after Passover** the gentile retains this leavened bread in lieu of payment, then **one is permitted to** derive **benefit from** this leavened bread. Since the leavened bread was retained by the gentile based on the transfer that took place prior to Passover, the leavened bread is considered to have belonged to the gentile during Passover. Whereas if **a Jew lent money to a gentile,** and **leavened bread** was given as collateral during Passover in the same manner as in the previous case, then **after Passover it is forbidden to** derive **benefit** from this leavened bread. Since this leavened bread was considered to be in the Jew's property during Passover, it is forbidden to derive benefit from it afterward.

The *mishna* at the bottom of 30b presents two cases. The first: a *nochri* who lends money to a Jew and takes the Jew's *chametz* as collateral — *achar haPesach mutar behana'ah*, after *Pesach* that *chametz* is permitted to the Jew to derive benefit from. The essential inference is that the *chametz* must have been sufficiently considered in the possession of the *nochri* — the creditor — already from the moment the loan was made, such that the Jew is not considered to have violated *bal yera'eh u'bal yimatze* over *Pesach*.

The second case is the converse: a Jew who lends money to a *nochri* and takes the *nochri*'s *chametz* as collateral — *achar haPesach asur behana'ah*, after *Pesach* that *chametz* is prohibited for the Jew to derive benefit from. The operative principle is the same: if the creditor has sufficient claim on the collateral to be considered its possessor, then the Jew who holds the *nochri*'s *chametz* as collateral is treated as if that *chametz* was in his domain over *Pesach*, rendering it *chametz she'avar alav haPesach*.

By way of background, the rabbis enacted a decree that *chametz she'avar alav haPesach* — *chametz* over which *Pesach* has passed — is prohibited not merely for eating but even for deriving any benefit, in accordance with the view of Rabbi Shimon. This prohibition serves in some sense to add teeth to the biblical prohibitions of *bal yera'eh u'bal yimatze* even after the fact.

## Abaye vs. Rava

### Lemafrea vs. Mikan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיתְּמַר: בַּעַל חוֹב, אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: לְמַפְרֵעַ הוּא גּוֹבֶה. וְרָבָא אָמַר: מִכָּאן וּלְהַבָּא הוּא גּוֹבֶה. כׇּל הֵיכָא דְּאַקְדֵּישׁ לֹוֶה וְזַבֵּין לֹוֶה — כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּאָתֵי מַלְוֶה וְטָרֵיף,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **It was stated** that the *amora'im* disagree about the rights of a **creditor** with regard to collateral. **Abaye said: He retroactively collects** the property. In a case where the creditor had a lien on the property of the debtor and the debtor defaults on the loan, it is considered as if the creditor acquired rights to the collateral at the time of the loan and not at the time of collection. **And Rava said:** This is not the case, but rather, **he acquires** the collateral **from that point forward,** and has rights to the collateral only from the time that the loan was due. This dispute has ramifications for the status of the property, and actions undertaken with regard to it, during the intervening period. The Gemara sets several limitations on the scope of this dispute: **Anywhere that the debtor consecrated or sold** the field that was serving as collateral, **everyone agrees** that **the creditor can come and seize** this property and override the sale, because its status as collateral preceded its sale.

Three lines from the bottom of 30b, the *Gemara* introduces an *itmara* — a transmitted Amoraic dispute: *ba'al chov: Abaye amar lemafrea hu goveh, veRava amar mikan ulehaba hu goveh*. That is, with respect to a creditor's acquisition of collateral: Abaye holds that the creditor acquires the collateral *lemafrea* — retroactively — meaning the item is considered to have been in the creditor's possession all along from the inception of the loan. Rava holds that the creditor acquires *mikan ulehaba* — prospectively — meaning the collateral only officially passes into the creditor's possession at the moment the borrower defaults and repayment becomes impossible.

### Scope of Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָתֵי מַלְוֶה וּפָרֵיק. דִּתְנַן: מוֹסִיף עוֹד דִּינָר וּפוֹדֶה אֶת הַנְּכָסִים הָאֵלּוּ. כִּי פְּלִיגִי דְּזַבֵּין מַלְוֶה, וְקַדֵּישׁ מַלְוֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Or** if the debtor consecrated the property, the creditor can **come and redeem** it through a symbolic payment to the Temple. **As we learned** in a mishna: The creditor may **add an additional dinar** to the amount of the loan **and redeem this property.** Although strictly speaking he need not redeem it, this payment was instituted so that it would not appear as though property were removed from the consecrated Temple jurisdiction without a payment. **When they disagree** is in a case **when the creditor sold or consecrated** the property in the interim between the giving of the collateral and the time the loan was due.

The *Gemara* proceeds to clarify the precise circumstances in which this dispute applies. Where the *borrower* is the one who took action with the collateral — consecrating it (*hikdish* it) to the *Beit HaMikdash* or selling it — all agree (*kulei alma lo pligi*) that the lender may come and seize it from whoever now holds it, or redeem it. In the case of *hekdesh*, the *Gemara* notes in passing (citing the *mishna* in *Arachin*) that the lender would add a nominal *dinar* and use that to redeem the item — out of a degree of deference to sanctified property not accorded to an ordinary third-party buyer.

The dispute between Abaye and Rava applies specifically where it is the *lender* — not the borrower — who sold or consecrated the collateral item. In that situation alone does the question of when acquisition takes effect become critical.

### Svara: Both Sides

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר, לְמַפְרֵעַ הוּא גּוֹבֶה: כֵּיוָן דִּמְטָא זִמְנֵיהּ וְלָא פַּרְעֵיהּ, אִיגַּלַּאי מִילְּתָא לְמַפְרֵעַ דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא בִּרְשׁוּתֵיהּ הֲוָה קָאֵי, וְשַׁפִּיר אַקְדֵּישׁ, וְשַׁפִּיר זַבֵּין. וְרָבָא אָמַר, מִכָּאן וּלְהַבָּא הוּא גּוֹבֶה: כֵּיוָן דְּאִילּוּ הֲווֹ לֵיהּ זוּזֵי, הֲוָה מְסַלֵּיק לְהוּ בְּזוּזֵי — אִישְׁתְּכַח דְּהַשְׁתָּא קָא קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: He retroactively acquires** the collateral. **Since the time arrived and he did not repay** his loan, **it has become clear retroactively that it was in** the creditor's **jurisdiction at the outset.** Therefore, **he did well to consecrate or sell** it. However, **Rava said: He collects it from that point forward, since if the** borrower **had money he would remove the** creditor's lien **with** this **money** and the lender would not acquire the property. **It is found** that the creditor **acquires** the property **now,** at the time when the loan is due, and consequently he did not have the right to consecrate or sell it before this time.

The *Gemara* articulates the internal reasoning (*svara*) of each position. Abaye reasons: *keivan demata zamnei velo parei, iglai milta lemafrea deme'ikara bireshutei hava ka'ei* — once the time for repayment arrived and the borrower did not pay, it was retroactively revealed that from the very beginning the collateral had been in the lender's possession. The lender therefore acted entirely properly in selling it or consecrating it.

Rava reasons: *keivan de'ilu hava lei zuzei hava mesalek lehu bizuzei, ishtakhakh dehashta ka kaneh* — given that throughout the entire duration of the loan, had the borrower possessed the funds he would have been able to repay and reclaim the collateral, it follows that until the moment of actual default, the collateral was never truly the lender's. It is only now, when it becomes definitively clear that repayment is impossible, that the collateral passes into the lender's possession. Up to that point, the theoretical possibility of repayment precluded any firm claim.

## Reuven & Shimon

### The Sale Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רָבָא הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: רְאוּבֵן שֶׁמָּכַר שָׂדֶה לְשִׁמְעוֹן בְּאַחְרָיוּת וּזְקָפָן עָלָיו בְּמִלְוֶה, וּמֵת רְאוּבֵן, וַאֲתָא בַּעַל חוֹב דִּרְאוּבֵן וְטָרֵיף לֵיהּ מִשִּׁמְעוֹן, וַאֲתָא שִׁמְעוֹן וּפַיְּיסֵיהּ בְּזוּזֵי —
>
> **Translation:** Before bringing proofs for either side of this dispute, the Gemara attempts to clarify Rava's position. **Did Rava** actually **say this?** A statement he makes in a different context appears to contradict the one made in his name here. **But didn't Rami bar Ḥama say: Reuven sold a field to Shimon with a guarantee** that if the field is repossessed, Reuven will compensate Shimon for his loss. Shimon did not pay for the purchase, and instead **set up** the value of the field **as a loan.** In the meantime, **Reuven died and a creditor of Reuven's came** to collect for a loan that Reuven had taken before he sold the field, as Reuven had no other land remaining, and he **seized** the land **from Shimon,** since it was mortgaged to this loan. **And Shimon went and appeased** the creditor **with money** so the creditor would allow him to keep this field.

At this point the *Gemara* raises a challenge from a case cited by Rami bar Chama: *Reuven she'machar sadeh leShimon b'achrayut*. Reuven sold a field to Shimon *b'achrayut* — with a warranty. *Achrayut* here means that Reuven stipulated that if his own creditor were to come and seize the field from Shimon on account of a debt Reuven owed, Reuven would repay Shimon whatever Shimon was required to pay. As Rashi explains, this is the sort of provision a buyer might insist upon when acquiring property from a seller who carries outstanding debts.

At the same time, *vezakfan alav bemilveh* — Shimon did not actually have the funds to pay for the field at the time of purchase. He therefore wrote Reuven a promissory note obligating himself to pay the price of the field, effectively seller-financing the transaction. The field passed to Shimon, and Reuven held a note for the purchase price.

### Creditor Arrives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּינָא הוּא דְּאָתוּ בְּנֵי רְאוּבֵן וְאָמְרִי לֵיהּ לְשִׁמְעוֹן: אֲנַן, מִטַּלְטְלֵי שְׁבַק אֲבוּן גַּבָּךְ, וּמִטַּלְטְלֵי דְיַתְמֵי לְבַעַל חוֹב לָא מִשְׁתַּעְבְּדִי.
>
> **Translation:** **By right, Reuven's sons** can **come and say to Shimon** that he must pay them the money that he owes for the field. And they are not required to pay Shimon if he demands compensation for the repossession of his field. They can say that **our father left us movable property in your hands,** i.e., the money you owe us for the field, **and,** as a general rule, **movable property that has been left to orphans is not mortgaged to a creditor.** The orphans can claim that the field belongs to Shimon, and as there is no land left for the orphans, there is no way for Shimon to recover the compensation that he is owed. The money he owes Reuven is considered movable property, and therefore he cannot recover his losses from these funds.

Reuven then dies. His creditor arrives and seeks to seize the field from Shimon on account of Reuven's outstanding debt. Shimon, rather than surrendering the field, pays off Reuven's creditor directly in money, thereby preventing the seizure.

The heirs of Reuven now approach Shimon and argue: our father left behind a debt — the purchase price of the field — which you owe us. Moreover, they contend, *metaltlei d'yatmi leba'al chov lo mishtabdei* — the personal property (in this case, the monetary debt owed to the estate) that orphans inherit cannot be subjugated to their father's creditor. Consequently, Shimon finds himself in the position of having paid Reuven's creditor once with money, and now being compelled to pay the value of the field a second time to Reuven's heirs. He has, in effect, been made to pay twice.

### Rava's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: אִי פִּיקֵּחַ שִׁמְעוֹן — מַגְבֵּי לְהוּ אַרְעָא, וַהֲדַר גָּבֵי לַהּ מִינַּיְיהוּ. דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: יְתוֹמִים שֶׁגָּבוּ קַרְקַע בְּחוֹבַת אֲבִיהֶם — בַּעַל חוֹב חוֹזֵר וְגוֹבֶה אוֹתָהּ מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava said** with regard to this case: **If Shimon is clever he will pay them** what he owes with **real estate** and not with money. Since they now have real estate received from their father's estate, Shimon **can then collect** the field **from them** as compensation for the original field that Reuven sold to Shimon. **As Rav Naḥman said:** When **orphans collect real estate for a debt owed to their father** from one person, another **creditor can come and seize this** land **from them** in order to repay the father's debt.

Rava intervenes with a practical suggestion: *ee pike'ach Shimon, magbei lehu ara vehadar gabei lah minaiyu* — if Shimon were shrewd, he would give the field to Reuven's heirs as payment of his debt to them, and then turn around and reclaim the field from them in order to pay Reuven's creditor.

The mechanism for this is a principle cited in the name of Rav Nachman: *yetomim shegavu karka bechovot avihem, ba'al chov chozer vegoveh otah mehen* — when orphans have collected land in satisfaction of their father's debts, the father's creditor may come back and collect that same land from them. By this route, Shimon would surrender the field to the heirs (satisfying his debt to them) and then recover the field from the heirs via the *achrayut* stipulation in order to pay the creditor — ending up with the loss of only the field rather than twice the field's value.

### Challenge to Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא "לְמַפְרֵעַ הוּא גּוֹבֶה", אַמְּטוּ לְהָכִי חוֹזֵר וְגוֹבֶה אוֹתָהּ מֵהֶן — דִּכְמַאן דְּגָבוּ מֵחַיִּים דַּאֲבוּהוֹן דָּמֵי. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ "מִכָּאן וּלְהַבָּא הוּא גּוֹבֶה", אַמַּאי חוֹזֵר וְגוֹבֶה אוֹתָהּ מֵהֶן? הָא הָוֵי כְּמַאן דִּזְבֵין יַתְמֵי נִכְסֵי דָּמֵי, וְאִילּוּ קָנֵי יַתְמֵי נִכְסֵי, מִי קָא מִשְׁתַּעְבְּדִי לְבַעַל חוֹב?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara applies this discussion to our original case. **Granted, if you say** that a creditor **collects retroactively,** and the field is considered as though it belonged to the creditor from the time of the loan, **due to this** reason, **he can then collect** the money **from them, because it is considered as though he collected it during their father's lifetime.** Because the field given by Shimon to repay his debt retroactively belonged to Reuven from the time that Shimon agreed to pay for the field, therefore Shimon can now claim this land from Reuven's heirs. **However, if you say** that **he collects** it **from this point forward, why can he then collect** this land **from them? It is as though the orphans purchased this property. And if orphans buy property, does** it become **indebted to a creditor** of their father? Only property that belonged to the father can be seized in order to pay back his debt, and therefore Rava's statement in the case appears to contradict his statement with regard to the transfer of ownership of collateral.

The *Gemara* now presses on Rava's own position. *Ei amart bishlama lemafrea hu goveh* — had this been Abaye's position, Rav Nachman's ruling would be perfectly coherent: since the lender acquires retroactively, the field is considered as though it was in the creditor's possession already during Reuven's lifetime, and therefore the heirs who received it are holding something that was already effectively the creditor's. *Ela ei amart mikan ulehaba hu goveh, amai chozer vegoveh otah mehen* — but if you hold that the creditor only acquires from the time of default onward, how can the creditor reach back and collect from the heirs? The heirs, at the time they received the field, appear to have acquired it as a fresh transaction; *havei keman dezavin yatmi nichsei dami* — it is as though the orphans themselves purchased the property, and if they had purchased property, it would not be subjugated to their father's creditor. This is a pointed difficulty for Rava.

### Stipulation resolves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר לְהוּ: כִּי הֵיכִי דְּמִשְׁתַּעְבַּדְנָא לֵיהּ לַאֲבוּכוֹן, מִשְׁתַּעְבַּדְנָא נָמֵי לְבַעַל חוֹב דַּאֲבוּכוֹן — מִדְּרַבִּי נָתָן. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לַנּוֹשֶׁה בַּחֲבֵירוֹ מָנֶה וַחֲבֵירוֹ בַּחֲבֵירוֹ, שֶׁמּוֹצִיאִין מִזֶּה וְנוֹתְנִין לָזֶה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "וְנָתַן לַאֲשֶׁר אָשַׁם לוֹ".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resolves this contradiction: **It is different there,** in this case, **as** Shimon could have **said to** Reuven's children that **just as I am indebted to your father, so too, I am indebted to your father's creditor.** And this principle can be learned **from** the statement **of Rabbi Natan** that one who lends to one person and borrows from another can be considered as a middleman between his creditor and his borrower. **As it was taught** in a *baraita*, **Rabbi Natan says: From where** is it derived **that when one lends one hundred** dinar **[*maneh*] to his fellow, and that fellow** lends a similar sum **to** a third **fellow, that we take** the money **from this one,** the second debtor, **and give it to that one,** the first creditor, without going through the middleman, who is both the first debtor and the second creditor? **The verse states: "And he shall give it to him whom he has wronged"** (Numbers 5:7), which indicates that the loan should be repaid to the creditor to whom the money is ultimately owed. Therefore, payment is made to the original creditor regardless of the issue of retroactive acquisition of the collateral.

The *Gemara* resolves the difficulty: *shani hatam*, this case is different, *de'amar lehu: ki heichi demishtabdina leh le'avuchon, mishtabdina nami leba'al chov de'avuchon* — because Shimon explicitly said to the heirs: just as I am obligated to your father, so too am I obligated to your father's creditor. This explicit stipulation creates a chain of obligation that operates independently of the general rule of *mikan ulehaba*.

The basis for this is the principle of Rabbi Natan, cited in a *braita*: *minayin lenoseh bechavero maneh vechavero bechavero maneh, shemotzi'in mizeh venotnin lazeh* — how do we know that when A is owed a *maneh* by B, and B is owed a *maneh* by C, we may extract from C and give to A? The *pasuk* teaches: *venatan la'asher asham lo* — he shall give to the one to whom he is indebted. This creates a recognized chain of debt through which an original creditor may reach a later obligor, provided the requisite stipulation was made. The present case thus works for Rava not because of the general principle of retroactive acquisition, but because of this specific and explicit undertaking that created a direct link between Shimon and Reuven's creditor.

## Back to Mishnah

### Problem for Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: גּוֹי שֶׁהִלְוָה אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל עַל חֲמֵצוֹ — אַחַר הַפֶּסַח מוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא "לְמַפְרֵעַ הוּא גּוֹבֶה" — אַמְּטוּ לְהָכִי מוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to bring proofs for the two sides of the question of retroactive acquisition. **We learned** in the mishna: If **a gentile lent money to a Jew,** and the Jew gave him **leavened bread as** collateral during Passover, and **after Passover** the gentile attained this leavened bread in lieu of payment, then **one is permitted to** derive **benefit from** the leavened bread. The Gemara attempts to clarify this position: **Granted, if you say** that **he retroactively collects** this property, and the leavened bread was acquired retroactively by the gentile, **it is due to this** that **one is permitted to** derive **benefit from it** after Passover.

Having worked through the implications of Rava's position in the case of Reuven and Shimon, the *Gemara* returns to the *mishna* and challenges Rava directly. The *mishna* states that when a *nochri* loans to a Jew with *chametz* as collateral, the *chametz* is *mutar behana'ah* after *Pesach* — implying that the *chametz* was considered in the *nochri*'s possession during *Pesach*. *Ei amart bishlama lemafrea hu goveh* — for Abaye, this is perfectly understandable: the creditor acquired retroactively, and so the *chametz* was in the *nochri*'s domain throughout *Pesach*. *Ela ei amart mikan ulehaba hu goveh, amai mutar behana'ah?* — but for Rava, how can this be permitted? *Bireshuta deYisrael hava ka'ei* — during *Pesach* the *chametz* was in the Jew's possession, rendering it *chametz she'avar alav haPesach*.

### Okimta: Deposit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ "מִכָּאן וּלְהַבָּא הוּא גּוֹבֶה", אַמַּאי מוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה? בִּרְשׁוּתָא דְיִשְׂרָאֵל הֲוָה קָאֵי! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — כְּשֶׁהִרְהִינוֹ אֶצְלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **But if you say** that **he collects it from this point forward,** then **why** should **one be permitted to** derive **benefit from** this leavened bread? The leavened bread **was in the possession of a Jew** during Passover, and therefore it should be forbidden. The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** With a case where **he deposited [*hirhin*]** the leavened bread **with** the gentile in his home, and since the leavened bread was in the gentile's possession during Passover, it is considered as if it belonged to the gentile, provided the gentile ultimately retains ownership of the leavened bread when the Jew defaults on the loan.

The *Gemara* resolves this with an *okimta*: *hacha bemai askinan? Keshehirhino etzlo* — the case in the *mishna* must be one where the Jew physically deposited the *chametz* with the *nochri*. Even if Rava holds that in general the creditor does not acquire the collateral until default, in this particular situation the Jew transferred the *chametz* into the *nochri*'s physical domain. That act of deposit, even without a formal *kinyan*, is sufficient to remove the item from the Jew's *reshut* in a meaningful enough sense that the Jew does not violate *bal yera'eh u'bal yimatze*, and the *chametz* is therefore *mutar behana'ah* after *Pesach*.

As Rashi explains, the rationale combines the formal collateral arrangement (*torat mashkon*) with the principle that *ba'al chov koneh mashkon* — a creditor acquires the collateral. The practical upshot is that when the item is physically in the lender's domain, even under Rava's view, the combination of physical transfer and the collateral relationship is sufficient to render it not in the Jew's possession for *chametz* purposes. The prohibition of *lo yera'eh lecha velo yimatze lecha* is understood as requiring presence in one's domain (*reshut*), not merely legal ownership — and here the *chametz* is genuinely out of the Jew's *reshut*, even if ownership remains technically ambiguous until default.

It is worth noting that this entire prohibition — *chametz she'avar alav haPesach* — is *derabbanan*. The rabbis, who enacted the decree, have the authority to delineate precisely under what circumstances it applies and under what circumstances it does not, including calibrating it according to the degree of physical and legal control a Jew exercises over the *chametz* in question.

## Tannaim: Meir & T.K.

### Braita: Jew→Nokhri

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁהִלְוָה לְגוֹי עַל חֲמֵצוֹ — לְאַחַר הַפֶּסַח אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר. מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר אָמְרוּ: עוֹבֵר. מַאי לָאו, בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: דְּמָר סָבַר לְמַפְרֵעַ הוּא גּוֹבֶה, וּמָר סָבַר מִכָּאן וּלְהַבָּא הוּא גּוֹבֶה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that this dispute is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.** As it was taught: If **a Jew lends money to a gentile** and the gentile gives him **leavened bread as** collateral, **then** the Jew **does not commit any transgression after Passover. They said in the name of Rabbi Meir that he commits a transgression. What, isn't it that they disagree about this, that one Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **holds** that the Jew transgresses by owning leavened bread **because he retroactively collects** the leavened bread and has therefore owned it during Passover, **and one Sage,** who states that the Jew does not commit a transgression, **holds** that **he collects it from this point forward,** and therefore it was not considered to be in his possession during Passover?

Having established the positions of Abaye and Rava, the *Gemara* attempts to trace a Tannaic precedent for this debate: *leima ketannai*. A *braita* is cited: *Yisrael shehilvah lenochri al chametzo, le'achar haPesach, eino over* — a Jew who loans to a *nochri* with the *nochri*'s *chametz* as collateral, after *Pesach*, does not transgress *chametz she'avar alav haPesach*, according to the *Tanna Kamma*. But *mishum Rabbi Meir amru over* — Rabbi Meir holds that the Jew does transgress.

### Explain via A/R?

One might naturally propose that this Tannaic dispute maps directly onto the Abaye/Rava debate: the *Tanna Kamma*, who says the Jew does not transgress, holds *mikan ulehaba* — the creditor only acquires upon default, and so during *Pesach* the *chametz* remained in the *nochri*'s possession; Rabbi Meir holds *lemafrea* — retroactively the Jew already owned the collateral throughout *Pesach*. However, the *Gemara* questions this alignment: *vetisbra?* Can this really be so? *Eima seifa* — we must look at the second half of the *braita*.

### Braita: Nokhri→Jew

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרָא? אֵימָא סֵיפָא: אֲבָל גּוֹי שֶׁהִלְוָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל עַל חֲמֵצוֹ — לְאַחַר הַפֶּסַח דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל עוֹבֵר. וְהָא אִיפְּכָא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ: לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הָתָם "אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר" — הָכָא עוֹבֵר. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הָתָם "עוֹבֵר" — הָכָא אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this explanation: **And** how can **you understand** it that way? **Say the latter clause** of that *baraita*: **However, if a gentile lends** money **to a Jew with leavened bread** as collateral, then **after Passover everyone agrees that he commits a transgression. However,** if the preceding explanation is correct, **it needed** to state **the opposite** of what it said in the first clause, as the case in the latter clause is the reverse of that in the first clause. Namely, **according to the one who said there,** in the first clause of the *baraita*, that **he does not commit a transgression,** he should say that **here,** in this case, **he does commit a transgression. And according to the one who said there,** in the first clause of the *baraita*, that **he does commit a transgression,** he should say that **here,** in the latter clause, **he does not commit a transgression.** For if the creditor retroactively acquires the collateral, then it is as though it belonged to a gentile during Passover, and therefore the Jew would not have committed a transgression by owning it.

The *seifa* states: *aval nochri shehilvah leYisrael al chametzo, le'achar haPesach, divrei hakol over* — but when a *nochri* loans to a Jew with the Jew's *chametz* as collateral, everyone agrees the Jew transgresses. This is a problem for a simple mapping onto Abaye and Rava. If the *Tanna Kamma* in the *reisha* holds *mikan ulehaba* (creditor does not acquire until default), and Rabbi Meir holds *lemafrea* (creditor acquires retroactively), then in the *seifa* — where it is now a *nochri* lending to a Jew — the *Tanna Kamma* should say the *nochri* acquires the collateral retroactively (freeing the Jew from the prohibition) and Rabbi Meir should say *mikan ulehaba* (keeping it in the Jew's *reshut*). The *seifa* should present reversed positions. Instead, both agree the Jew transgresses — meaning the alignment with Abaye and Rava is not straightforward, and a different explanatory framework is needed.

## Rabbi Yitzchak

### Biblical Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — כְּגוֹן שֶׁהִרְהִינוֹ אֶצְלוֹ, וְקָמִיפַּלְגִי בִּדְרַבִּי יִצְחָק. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִנַּיִן לְבַעַל חוֹב שֶׁקּוֹנֶה מַשְׁכּוֹן? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וּלְךָ תִּהְיֶה צְדָקָה". אִם אֵינוֹ קוֹנֶה מַשְׁכּוֹן — צְדָקָה מִנַּיִן? מִכָּאן לְבַעַל חוֹב שֶׁקּוֹנֶה מַשְׁכּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, with what are we dealing here?** With a situation **where he,** the gentile debtor, **deposited** the leavened bread that was serving as collateral **with him,** the Jewish creditor, **and they disagree with regard to** the statement **of Rabbi Yitzḥak. As Rabbi Yitzḥak said: From where** is it derived **that a creditor acquires collateral** given to him, and is considered its owner so long as the item is in his possession? **As it is stated:** "You shall surely return the pledge to him when the sun goes down, that he may sleep in his garment, and bless you; **and it shall be a righteousness for you** before the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 24:13). Rabbi Yitzḥak infers: **If** the creditor **does not acquire the collateral,** then **from where is the righteousness** involved in returning it? In this case, the creditor would not be giving up anything of his own. **From here** it is learned **that a creditor acquires the collateral.**

The alternative framework is introduced through the position of Rabbi Yitzchak. The *Gemara* asks: *minayin leba'al chov shekoneh mashkon* — from where do we know at all that a creditor acquires the collateral? Rabbi Yitzchak derives it from the *pasuk* in *Devarim* (24): *hashev tashiv lo et ha'avot kevo hashemesh veshachav besalmato uverachecha* — you must return the collateral garment to the borrower at night so he can sleep in it and bless you — *ulecha tihyeh tzedakah lifnei Hashem Elokecha* — and this shall be an act of charity before God. The innovative interpretation of Rabbi Yitzchak is: *im eino koneh mashkon, tzedakah minayin?* — if the garment truly belongs to the borrower all along, how is returning it to him an act of *tzedakah*? One only performs *tzedakah* by giving something of one's own. The very fact that the *Torah* calls the return of the collateral *tzedakah* proves that the creditor actually owns the collateral — *mikan leba'al chov shekoneh mashkon*.

### T.K.: Jew-Jew only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: הָנֵי מִילֵּי יִשְׂרָאֵל מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל הוּא, דְּקָרֵינָא בֵּיהּ "וּלְךָ תִּהְיֶה צְדָקָה", אֲבָל יִשְׂרָאֵל מִגּוֹי — לָא קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara applies this principle to the explanation of the *baraita*: **The first *tanna* holds** that **this applies only when a Jew** takes collateral **from a** fellow **Jew, such that I would read** and apply the verse **"It shall be righteousness for you"** and establish that the collateral becomes the property of the lender. **However,** the verse does not speak about the case where **a Jew** takes collateral **from a gentile,** and therefore **he does not acquire** the collateral; it still belongs to the gentile. Therefore, when a Jew has leavened bread as collateral from a gentile to whom he lent money on Passover, he does not violate any prohibition, as the collateral still belongs to the gentile.

The *Tanna Kamma* accepts Rabbi Yitzchak's principle but limits its application: *hani milei Yisrael miYisrael hu dekarina beih ulecha tihyeh tzedakah* — this principle of *ba'al chov koneh mashkon* applies only where both parties are Jewish, because only in a Jew-to-Jew loan does the concept of *tzedakah* as commanded in the *Torah* apply. The *mitzvah* of *tzedakah* in this context is specifically to one's Jewish brother (*achicha*). Therefore, in a Jew-to-*nochri* loan, where there is no operative *mitzvah* of *tzedakah* from Jew to non-Jew, the derivation of *ba'al chov koneh mashkon* falls away, and the Jew does not acquire the *nochri*'s collateral.

### R. Meir: Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר, קַל וָחוֹמֶר: יִשְׂרָאֵל מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל קָנֵי, יִשְׂרָאֵל מִגּוֹי לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?! אֲבָל גּוֹי שֶׁהִלְוָה אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל עַל חֲמֵצוֹ — אַחַר הַפֶּסַח דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל עוֹבֵר. הָתָם וַדַּאי גּוֹי מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל לָא קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** However, **Rabbi Meir holds** that one can derive **an *a fortiori* inference:** If **a Jew acquires** collateral **from** another **Jew, all the more so** is it **not** clear that he will he acquire collateral **from a gentile? However,** with regard to **a gentile who lends money to a Jew with leavened bread as** collateral, **everyone agrees** that **he transgresses** this prohibition after Passover. **There,** in that case, **the gentile certainly does not acquire** collateral **from the Jew,** and such a transaction could be completed only via the usual modes of acquisition.

Rabbi Meir disagrees, applying a *kal vachomer*: if a Jew acquires collateral from another Jew (*Yisrael miYisrael kani*), then in a Jew-to-*nochri* loan, *lo kol sheken* — how much more so should the Jew acquire the *nochri*'s collateral. The reasoning of the *kal vachomer* is not made fully explicit, but the direction is clear: Rabbi Meir sees the Jew-Jew acquisition as the baseline, and the Jew-*nochri* case as stronger rather than weaker.

### Nokhri never acquires

This framework resolves the *seifa* of the *braita*. When a *nochri* lends to a Jew — *aval nochri shehilvah leYisrael al chametzo, divrei hakol over* — everyone agrees the Jew transgresses. The reason is: *vadai nochri miYisrael lo kani* — it is certain that a *nochri* taking collateral from a Jew never acquires that collateral at all. There is no *pasuk* of *ulecha tihyeh tzedakah* applying to a *nochri* creditor, and there is no *kal vachomer* that would extend acquisition to the *nochri* side. Therefore, even if the Jew deposited the *chametz* with the *nochri*, the *nochri* never truly becomes its owner, and the *chametz* remains legally in the Jew's domain throughout *Pesach* — a fact about which both *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Meir agree. The debate between them pertains only to the Jew-to-*nochri* direction.

## Me'achshav Principle

### Okimta: Stipulation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — כְּגוֹן שֶׁהִרְהִינוֹ אֶצְלוֹ, וְקָמִיפַּלְגִי בִּדְרַבִּי יִצְחָק. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִנַּיִן לְבַעַל חוֹב שֶׁקּוֹנֶה מַשְׁכּוֹן? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וּלְךָ תִּהְיֶה צְדָקָה". אִם אֵינוֹ קוֹנֶה מַשְׁכּוֹן — צְדָקָה מִנַּיִן? מִכָּאן לְבַעַל חוֹב שֶׁקּוֹנֶה מַשְׁכּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, with what are we dealing here?** With a situation **where he,** the gentile debtor, **deposited** the leavened bread that was serving as collateral **with him,** the Jewish creditor, **and they disagree with regard to** the statement **of Rabbi Yitzḥak. As Rabbi Yitzḥak said: From where** is it derived **that a creditor acquires collateral** given to him, and is considered its owner so long as the item is in his possession? **As it is stated:** "You shall surely return the pledge to him when the sun goes down, that he may sleep in his garment, and bless you; **and it shall be a righteousness for you** before the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 24:13). Rabbi Yitzḥak infers: **If** the creditor **does not acquire the collateral,** then **from where is the righteousness** involved in returning it? In this case, the creditor would not be giving up anything of his own. **From here** it is learned **that a creditor acquires the collateral.**

Having established that a *nochri* lending to a Jew never acquires the collateral, the *Gemara* must now revisit the *mishna*, which states that when a *nochri* loans to a Jew with *chametz* as collateral, the *chametz* is *mutar behana'ah* after *Pesach*. But we have just concluded that a *nochri* never acquires the collateral — so how can the *chametz* be *mutar*? If it remained the Jew's throughout *Pesach*, it should be *chametz she'avar alav haPesach*.

The *Gemara* answers with a new *okimta*: *lo kashya — ha de'amar leih me'achshav, ha delo amar leih me'achshav* — there is no contradiction. The case in the *mishna* where the *chametz* is *mutar* must be one where the *nochri* explicitly said to the Jew *me'achshav* — from now, this is yours — explicitly stipulating at the time of the loan that the *chametz* is transferred into the Jew's possession immediately. In such a case, even under the framework of Rabbi Yitzchak and the *Tanna Kamma*, the explicit declaration overrides the default rule and does cause the *chametz* to transfer. The case where no such stipulation was made follows the default rule and the *chametz* remains the *nochri*'s (or more precisely, remains not-the-Jew's).

### Braita: Deposit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: גּוֹי שֶׁהִלְוָה יִשְׂרָאֵל עַל חֲמֵצוֹ — אַחַר הַפֶּסַח מוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה. נְהִי נָמֵי דְּהִרְהִינוֹ אֶצְלוֹ, הָא אָמְרַתְּ גּוֹי מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל לָא קָנֵי? לָא קַשְׁיָא הָא — דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ "מֵעַכְשָׁיו", הָא — דְּלָא אָמַר לֵיהּ "מֵעַכְשָׁיו".
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: If **a gentile lent money to a Jew,** and the Jew gave him **leavened bread as** collateral until after Passover, and **after Passover** he retains this leavened bread in lieu of payment, then **one is permitted to** derive **benefit from** this leavened bread. **Even granted** that this is referring to a case where **he deposited** the leavened bread **with him,** the gentile, **didn't you say** that **a gentile does not acquire** collateral **from a Jew,** and if this is the case, then why is it permissible to derive benefit from this leavened bread? According to the previously stated principle, this leavened bread remains Jewish property. The Gemara resolves this question: **This is not difficult,** for **this** case, where the gentile acquires the leavened bread and therefore it is permitted to derive benefit from it, is **when** the gentile **said to him** that if he does not repay his loan, then the collateral will be acquired **from now,** i.e., from the time of the loan. **And that** case, where it is forbidden to derive benefit from the leavened bread, is **when he did not say** that it would be acquired **from now** by the gentile.

In support of the distinction introduced by the *me'achshav* stipulation, the *Gemara* cites a *braita*: *nochri shehirhin patporni eitzel Yisrael, eino over* — a *nochri* who deposits a *patporni* (a significant or fancy loaf of bread — presumably used as an example of collateral worth pledging) with a Jew does not cause the Jew to transgress *chametz she'avar alav haPesach*. *Ve'im amar lo higaticha, over* — but if the *nochri* said to the Jew *higaticha* — "it has arrived in your possession," meaning it is now yours — then the Jew does transgress.

### Inference: Words matter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ומְנָא תֵּימְרָא דְּשָׁנֵי לֵיהּ בֵּין הֵיכָא דְּאָמַר "מֵעַכְשָׁיו" וּבֵין הֵיכָא דְּלָא אָמַר "מֵעַכְשָׁיו", דְּתַנְיָא: גּוֹי שֶׁהִרְהִין פַּת פּוּרְנִי אֵצֶל יִשְׂרָאֵל — אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר, וְאִם אָמַר לוֹ "הִגַּעְתִּיךָ" — עוֹבֵר. מַאי שְׁנָא רֵישָׁא וּמַאי שְׁנָא סֵיפָא? אֶלָּא לָאו, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: שָׁאנֵי הֵיכָא דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ "מֵעַכְשָׁיו" לְהֵיכָא דְּלָא אָמַר לֵיהּ "מֵעַכְשָׁיו". שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **From where do you say that he distinguishes between** a case **where he said** that the collateral would be acquired **from now and** a case **where he did not say** that it would be acquired **from now? As it was taught** in a *baraita*: If **a gentile deposited with a Jew bread baked in an oven [*purni*]** as collateral for a loan, then **he,** the Jew, **does not transgress** the prohibition it shall not be seen and the prohibition it shall not be found. **However, if he said to him: I have made them yours** from now if I do not repay my loan, then it is considered as though the bread belonged to the Jew, and **he transgresses** this prohibition. If one assumes that there is no difference between a case where the debtor says: From now, and a case where he doesn't say: From now, then **what is different in the first clause** of the *baraita* **and what is different in the latter clause? Rather,** must **one not conclude from it that there is a difference between** a case **where he says to him** that he will acquire it **from now and** a case **where he does not say to him** that he will acquire it **from now?** The Gemara concurs: Indeed, **conclude from it** that this is the case.

The *Gemara* draws the obvious inference: *mai shna reisha umai shna seifa?* — what is the difference between the first clause and the second clause? The only difference is that in the second clause, the *nochri* explicitly declared *higaticha* — a statement of immediate transfer equivalent to *me'achshav*. This confirms that explicit verbal stipulation at the time of collateral transfer has legal effect, changing the ownership status of the pledged item. *Shma mina* — this may be derived from here.

## Stores & Presumption

### Jewish store

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חֲנוּת שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל וּמְלַאי שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל, וּפוֹעֲלֵי גוֹיִם נִכְנָסִין לְשָׁם — חָמֵץ שֶׁנִּמְצָא שָׁם אַחַר הַפֶּסַח אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר בַּאֲכִילָה. חֲנוּת שֶׁל גּוֹי וּמְלַאי שֶׁל גּוֹי וּפוֹעֲלֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל נִכְנָסִין וְיוֹצְאִים לְשָׁם — חָמֵץ שֶׁנִּמְצָא שָׁם אַחַר הַפֶּסַח מוּתָּר בַּאֲכִילָה, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר בַּהֲנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in the *Tosefta*: With regard to the case of **a store** owned by **a Jew and** whose **contents belong to the Jew, and gentile workers would enter there** periodically, then **it is forbidden to** derive **benefit from the leavened bread that is found there after Passover, and needless to say,** it is forbidden **to eat** this leavened bread, for it is presumed to belong to the Jewish owner. Conversely, if **a store** is owned by **a gentile and its contents belong to the gentile, and Jewish workers enter and exit** the store, then it can be presumed that **the leavened bread that is found there after Passover** belonged to the gentile, and therefore one is **permitted to eat it** after Passover. And **needless to say,** it is permitted **to** derive **benefit from** this leavened bread.

The *sugya* concludes with a *braita* establishing practical presumptions of ownership where chametz is found after *Pesach* in ambiguous circumstances. The first case: a store belonging to a Jew, with inventory belonging to a Jew, into which non-Jewish workers enter and exit. If *chametz* is found there after *Pesach* — *asur behana'ah*, it is prohibited to derive benefit from it, and certainly prohibited for eating. The presumption is that any *chametz* found there was the Jew's *chametz* that was not disposed of before *Pesach*, rather than the lunch brought in by a non-Jewish worker. The non-Jewish workers' occasional presence does not create sufficient doubt to override the presumption that the contents of a Jewish-owned store with Jewish inventory belong to the Jewish owner.

### Non-Jewish store

The converse case: a store belonging to a *nochri*, with inventory belonging to a *nochri*, into which Jewish workers enter and exit. If *chametz* is found there after *Pesach* — *mutar be'achilah*, it is permitted even for eating, and certainly permitted for benefit. Here the presumption runs the other way: the contents of a *nochri*-owned store with *nochri* inventory are presumed to belong to the *nochri*, and the Jewish workers' entry does not raise sufficient suspicion that the *chametz* found there was brought in and left by a Jewish worker — which would itself involve a transgression. We follow the default presumption of the store's ownership, and the *chametz* is therefore treated as the *nochri*'s throughout *Pesach*, rendering it entirely permissible after *Pesach*.
