# Shevuot 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shvuat Ha'edut Basics

### Oath Language & God's Name

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִן לַעֲשׂוֹת אָלָה שֶׁאֵין עִמָּהּ שְׁבוּעָה כְּאָלָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ עִמָּהּ שְׁבוּעָה, וּשְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין עִמָּהּ אָלָה כִּשְׁבוּעָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ עִמָּהּ אָלָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְשָׁמְעָה קוֹל אָלָה״ – וְשָׁמְעָה אָלָה, וְשָׁמְעָה קוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where** is it derived **to render an *ala* with which** the word **oath is not** written **like an *ala* with which** the word **oath is** written **and an oath with which** the word ***ala* is not** written **like an oath with which** the word ***ala* is** written? **The verse states: "And he hears the voice of an *ala*"** (Leviticus 5:1). The phrase "the voice of an *ala*" is unnecessary, as it would have been sufficient to write: And he heard an *ala*. It is interpreted as though it were written: **And he heard an *ala* and he heard a voice.**

We begin at the bottom of 35b, just before the lines widen and the double dots mark the new sugya. The topic at hand is *shvuat ha'edut*: what language is required, and what constitutes a valid *shvua*? The *Mishnah* had mentioned formulas such as *mashbia ani aleichem* and *metzaveh ani aleichem*, which might have suggested that no explicit *shvua* language is needed at all. The *Gemara*, however, clarifies that these phrases are shorthand for *metzaveh ani aleichem bishvua* — some keyword of *shvua* is indeed required. This was established earlier in the tractate when treating *shvuat bitui* as well: there are synonyms that function as *shvua* language, but some form of oath terminology is necessary.

The *Mishnah* further teaches that one must invoke God's name — though not necessarily the *shem Havayah* (the four-letter name *Yud-Kei-Vav-Kei*). Any of the divine names will suffice: *Aleph-Daled-Nun-Yod*, *Tzevaot*, and so forth. This requirement opens an extended *Gemara* discussion about which names of God are sufficiently sacred to trigger the prohibition against erasure, and related questions. But its relevance to our *sugya* is fundamental: it is here, finally, that the *Gemara* directly addresses the question raised as far back as the beginning of the *masechet* — must God's name form part of a *shvua*, whether implicitly or explicitly?

### Sotah Parallels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: מִנַּיִן לְ״אָלָה״ שֶׁהִיא שְׁבוּעָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיָּבֵא אֹתוֹ בְּאָלָה וְגוֹ׳״; וּכְתִיב: ״וְגַם בַּמֶּלֶךְ נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר מָרָד אֲשֶׁר הִשְׁבִּיעוֹ בֵּאלֹהִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu says: From where** is it derived **with regard to *ala* that it is an oath?** It is derived **as it is stated:** "And he took from the seed of the monarchy…**and brought it into an *ala*"** (Ezekiel 17:13); **and it is stated** with regard to Zedekiah, who was from the seed of the monarchy: **"And he also rebelled against Nebuchadnezzar who had administered an oath to him by God"** (II Chronicles 36:13). This indicates that the *ala* is an oath.

The *Gemara* begins with a challenge. The *Mishnah* states that one is *chayav* with any *kinnui* — any euphemistic or substitute name of God. But a *Baraita* raises the following: by the *Sotah*, the verse reads *veyiten Hashem otach le'alah velishvua* ("may God make you a curse and an oath"). Yet another verse had already stated *vehishbia hakohen et ha'ishah bishvuat ha'alah* ("the *kohen* adjured the woman with the oath of the curse"). Why then does the text repeat the words *alah* and *shvua*, and even invoke God's name explicitly?

The answer given is that the repetition in the *Sotah* passage comes to teach us something about *shvuat ha'edut*. In the passage governing *shvuat ha'edut*, the Torah says only *veshama kol alah* — "and he will hear the sound of a curse." Notice that the word *shvua* never appears there at all. The *Gemara* therefore constructs a link: *ne'emar kan alah vene'emar lehalan alah* — the word *alah* appears here (in *shvuat ha'edut*) and there (in *Sotah*). Just as by *Sotah* the *alah* operates within a *shvuat ha'alah* — a combined oath-curse — so too here the *alah* is a form of *shvua*. And furthermore: just as by *Sotah* the formulation invokes *shem Hashem*, so too by *shvuat ha'edut* one must invoke God's name. These are the two foundational components of *shvuat ha'edut*, and neither one appears explicitly in the verse; both are derived from the connection to *Sotah*.

## Sotah Derivation

### Rabbi Chanina bar Idi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר אִידִי, הָא רַבָּנַן. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר אִידִי אוֹמֵר: הוֹאִיל וְאָמְרָה תּוֹרָה ״הִשָּׁבַע״ וְ״אַל תִּשָּׁבַע״, ״קַלֵּל״ וְ״אַל תְּקַלֵּל״ – מָה ״הִשָּׁבַע״ בַּשֵּׁם, אַף ״לֹא תִּשָּׁבַע״ בַּשֵּׁם; מָה ״קַלֵּל״ בַּשֵּׁם, אַף ״לֹא תְּקַלֵּל״ בַּשֵּׁם.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** This is **not difficult. This** *baraita* is the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥanina bar Idi,** and **that** mishna is the opinion of **the Rabbis, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Ḥanina bar Idi says: Since the Torah says** in some cases: **Take an oath, and** in some cases: **Do not take an oath;** and it says in some cases: **Curse, and** in some cases: **Do not curse, just as** when the Torah says: **Take an oath,** it is **in** the **name** of God, **so too,** when the Torah states: **Do not take an oath,** it is **in the name** of God. And **just as** when the Torah states: **Curse,** it is **in** the **name** of God, **so too,** when the Torah says: **Do not curse,** it is **in** the **name** of God.

The resolution to the tension between the *Baraita* (which seems to require the explicit *shem Havayah*) and the *Mishnah* (which permits any *kinnui*) is provided by Abaye: *ha Rabbi Chanina bar Idi, ha Rabbanan* — this is a dispute between *Tannaim*. Rabbi Chanina bar Idi is the author of the position requiring *shem Havayah*, while the anonymous *Rabbanan* are the authors of the *Mishnah*'s ruling permitting a *kinnui*.

A *Baraita* is then cited that elaborates Rabbi Chanina bar Idi's position. His argument is structured around a series of parallels: since the Torah sometimes commands the taking of an oath (as in *shvuat Hashem tihiyeh bein shneihem*) and sometimes prohibits it (*lo tishvu bishmi lashaker*), and similarly sometimes commands the pronouncement of a curse (as by *Sotah*, *veyiten Hashem otach le'alah velishvua*) and sometimes prohibits cursing (*lo tekalel cheresh*, *Elohim lo tekalel*), the internal logic follows: just as the mandated oath is with God's name, so too the prohibited oath is with God's name; and just as cursing with God's name is sometimes required (by *Sotah*: *yitein Hashem otach le'alah*), so too the prohibition against cursing invokes God's name. The invocation of God in the oath context, and in the curse context, is treated as symmetric. 

It is worth noting — and this is somewhat ironic — that the explicit prohibition against a false *shvua* is stated with God's name more directly than the affirmative requirement to take one: *lo tishvu bishmi lashaker* and *lo tisa et shem Hashem Elokecha lashav* are quite explicit. But the structure of Rabbi Chanina bar Idi's argument works in both directions. The key point is that his derivation from *Sotah* is what commits him to requiring *shem Havayah* specifically — and that is why the *Mishnah*'s permission of a *kinnui* reflects a different view.

### Rabbis' Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, אִי גְּמִירִי גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה – נִיבְעֵי שֵׁם הַמְיוּחָד! אִי לָא גְּמִירִי גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה – ״אָלָה״ דִּשְׁבוּעָה הִיא מְנָא לְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the Rabbis** say: **If they derive** an oath of testimony from *sota* by means of **a verbal analogy, let us require** that both an oath of testimony and the curse will be specifically in **the ineffable name** of God. **If they do not derive** an oath of testimony from *sota* by means of **a verbal analogy, from where do they** derive **that** the instance of the word ***ala* that** is written with regard to an oath of testimony **is an oath?**

The *Gemara* now turns to the *Rabbanan* and presses the question: if the *Rabbanan* accept the *gzerah shava* connecting *shvuat ha'edut* to *Sotah*, then they should arrive at the same conclusion as Rabbi Chanina bar Idi — that the *shem ha-meyuchad* is required. But if they do not accept the *gzerah shava*, then they have no source at all to tell them that the *alah* in *veshama kol alah* is even a *shvua*. The verse says only "curse," not oath. So make up your mind: either accept the *Sotah* link with its full consequences (including *shem Havayah*), or abandon the *Sotah* link entirely — in which case where does the requirement of *shvua* come from at all?

### Word Meaning vs. Gzerah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפְקָא לְהוּ מִדְּתַנְיָא: ״אָלָה״ – אֵין ״אָלָה״ אֶלָּא לְשׁוֹן שְׁבוּעָה, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְהִשְׁבִּיעַ הַכֹּהֵן אֶת הָאִשָּׁה בִּשְׁבוּעַת הָאָלָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **They derive it from that which is taught** in a *baraita*: It is written with regard to an oath of testimony: "And he hears the voice of **an *ala*"** (Leviticus 5:1); ***ala* is nothing other than an expression** meaning **oath. And likewise it says: "And the priest shall administer to the woman with the oath of cursing [*ha'ala*]"** (Numbers 5:21).

The *Gemara* answers: *nafka lehu mide-tanya* — the *Rabbanan* derive their position from a *Baraita*, and their method is distinct from a *gzerah shava*. They do not make a formal hermeneutical link to *Sotah*; rather, they look at the word *alah* as biblical scholars examining usage. They ask: what does the word *alah* mean? Looking at the *Sotah* passage, where it says *shvuat ha'alah*, they observe that *alah* appears in the context of an oath. From this they conclude — not via *gzerah shava* but simply as a matter of semantic understanding — that *alah* is *lashon shvua*, oath-language.

The *Baraita* makes this explicit: *ein alah ela lashon shevuah* — the word *alah* simply means *shvua*. And the proof: *vehen hu omer vehishbia hakohen et ha'ishah bishvuat ha'alah* — the *kohen* adjures the woman with the *shvuat ha'alah*. This is not a *gzerah shava* importing all the laws of one passage into another; it is merely reading the word *alah* in its context and discerning its meaning. Because this is semantic derivation rather than formal analogy, the *Rabbanan* do not also import the requirement of *shem ha-meyuchad*. The connection to *Sotah* tells them only what the word means — not that all conditions of the *Sotah* passage apply.

This raises an immediate and striking question: if the *Rabbanan* do not learn from *Sotah* through *gzerah shava* and do not import *shem Havayah*, then how do they know that any reference to God is required at all? The *Mishnah* after all permits a *kinnui* — *bimi she-shmo rachum*, *bimi she-shmo chanun* — but it still requires some invocation of the divine. Where does even that minimal requirement come from?

No *Rishon* directly addresses this question, and the *Gemara* itself notably does not ask it. That silence is itself significant. The implication seems to be that a *shvua*, by its very definition, is an act of calling upon God as witness. God's name is always implicit or explicit in a *shvua* — that is what the word *shvua* means. Once we have established via semantic analysis that *alah* is *lashon shvua*, we have also imported the inherent structure of a *shvua*, which necessarily involves some invocation of God. There is no need for an explicit verse to establish this, because it follows from the nature of the act itself. This is a theme that runs back to the very beginning of the *masechet*: the *Gemara* has consistently treated reference to God as intrinsic to *shvua*-language, never pausing to derive it as if it were some additional external requirement.

This is precisely where the Rambam and the Ra'avad diverge — a dispute mentioned earlier in the tractate and relevant here. The Rambam holds explicitly that no consequences of a *shvua* — no *korban*, no *lav* — apply without some reference to God's name; it need not be *shem Havayah*, but God must be invoked. The Ra'avad disagrees and holds that even without invoking God, a *shvua* can function analogously to a *neder*: it creates a binding status, and violating it would transgress *lo yachel dvaro* — not *lo tishvu bishmi lashaker* or *lo tisa et shem Hashem Elokecha lashav*, since those are specifically about *shem HaShem* — but a general obligation of not profaning one's word. The Ra'avad does concede, however, that in cases like *shvuat ha'edut* and *shvuat pikadon*, the relevant prohibitions are specifically tied to God's name and do require its invocation.

For the *Rabbanan* of our *Mishnah*, then: they require *shvua* and implicitly some reference to God — not through any explicit verse, but because that is what a *shvua* is — but they do not require *shem Havayah* specifically, since they reject the full *gzerah shava* to *Sotah*.

## Alah vs. Shvua

### Veshama Kol Alah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: ״אָרוּר״ – בּוֹ נִידּוּי, בּוֹ קְלָלָה, בּוֹ שְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara proceeds to define a related term. It is **taught** with regard to the term ***arur*:** There is an element of **ostracism within it,** there is an element of **curse within it,** and there is an element of **oath within it.**

Having established where each view derives its requirements, the *Gemara* turns to a deeper question embedded in the text. The *Baraita* had said that *alah* means *shvua*, and it cited the *Sotah* verse *shvuat ha'alah* as evidence. But the *Gemara* now objects: *hatam shevuat ha'alah ketiv* — by *Sotah* it says the *shvua* of the *alah*. That means they are combined: it is a curse that occurs within the context of an oath. That does not demonstrate that the word *alah* by itself means *shvua*; it demonstrates that the two are joined. So how does one know that an *alah* without a *shvua* constitutes a *shvuat ha'edut*, or that a *shvua* without an *alah* constitutes one?

The *Gemara* answers with a more careful reading of the verse *veshama kol alah*. The word *kol* — "sound" or "voice" — is the key. Had the Torah wished to say only "he heard a curse," it could have said *veshama alah*. The additional word *kol* allows for a double reading: *veshama alah* — he heard a curse; *veshama kol* — he heard a voice, i.e., a *shvua* (since a *shvua* is something spoken and heard). The verse is thereby read as addressing two separate possibilities: either an *alah* alone, or a *kol* — a spoken oath — alone. Either one is sufficient to create liability for *shvuat ha'edut*.

This is a significant halakhic point. All along the discussion has used the word *shvua*, but now the *Gemara* is making explicit what was implicit in the *Mishnah*: a *shvua* is not strictly necessary for *shvuat ha'edut*. An *alah* — a pure curse, without any oath — can also suffice. If someone says to a witness, "May God curse you and bring plague on your house if you know testimony and do not come to testify," and the witness falsely denies knowing any testimony, that person brings a *korban*. This is the *Mishnah*'s case of *yakecha Hashem Elokeinu v'chen yakecha Elokeim* — "may God smite you" — which the *Mishnah* itself identifies as the *alah haketuvah baTorah*. One need not combine *shvua* and *alah*; either suffices on its own.

### Rabbi Abahu's Approach

The text above contains Rabbi Abahu's independent source, cited earlier. Rabbi Abahu then provides an independent source for the equivalence of *alah* and *shvua*: *vayavei oto be'alah* — he brought him into the curse — and a parallel verse that describes the same act using the language of adjuration. The two verses, describing a single event, use the words *alah* and *shvua* interchangeably. This is a clean derivation: one verse uses *alah*, the other uses *shvua* for the same act, demonstrating that sometimes the word *alah* functions as *shvua*.

### Arur as Shvua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בּוֹ נִידּוּי – דִּכְתִיב: ״אוֹרוּ מֵרוֹז אָמַר מַלְאַךְ ה׳, אֹרוּ אָרוֹר יֹשְׁבֶיהָ״, וְאָמַר עוּלָּא: בְּאַרְבַּע מְאָה שִׁיפּוּרֵי שַׁמְּתֵיהּ בָּרָק לְמֵרוֹז.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: There is an element of **ostracism within it, as it is written** in the song of Deborah: **"Curse [*oru*] Meroz, said the angel of God; cursed with a curse [*oru aror*] are its inhabitants"** (Judges 5:23). **And Ulla says: With** blasts from **four hundred *shofarot*, Barak ostracized** the city of **Meroz,** indicating that the term *arur* has the connotation of ostracism.

A *Baraita* is then cited establishing that the word *arur* — "cursed be" — likewise has multiple valences depending on context. *Bo niduy* — in some contexts it means excommunication, as in *oru Meroz*, where Ulla teaches that Barak placed Meroz under *cherem* with four hundred *shofarot*. *Bo klala* — in other contexts it plainly means a curse, as in *ve'eileh ya'amdu al ha'klala* and *arur ha'ish asher ya'aseh pesel u'maseicha* at Har Gerizim and Har Eival. *Bo shevuah* — and in other contexts it means an oath.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בּוֹ קְלָלָה – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאֵלֶּה יַעַמְדוּ עַל הַקְּלָלָה״, וּכְתִיב: ״אָרוּר הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יַעֲשֶׂה פֶסֶל וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** There is an element of **curse within it, as it is written** with regard to the ceremony at Mount Gerizim and Mount Ebal: **"And these shall stand for the curse"** (Deuteronomy 27:13), **and it is written: "Cursed [*arur*] be the man who fashions a graven image,** an abomination to the Lord, the work of the hands of the craftsman, and sets it up in secret. And all the people shall answer and say: Amen" (Deuteronomy 27:15).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בּוֹ שְׁבוּעָה – דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּשְׁבַּע יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בָּעֵת הַהִיא לֵאמֹר, אָרוּר הָאִישׁ לִפְנֵי ה׳ וְגוֹ׳״. וְדִלְמָא תַּרְתֵּי עֲבַד לְהוּ – אַשְׁבְּעִינְהוּ וְלַיְיטִינְהוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** There is an element of **oath within it, as it is written: "And Joshua administered an oath at that time saying: *Arur* be the man before God** who shall arise and rebuild this city, Jericho" (Joshua 6:26). The Gemara challenges: **But perhaps** Joshua **performed two** actions **to** the people; **he administered an oath to them and he cursed them,** and the term *arur* relates to the curse, not to the oath.

## Curse-Oath Equivalence

### Joshua's Arur Oath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵהָכָא: ״וְאִישׁ יִשְׂרָאֵל נִגַּשׂ בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא, וַיֹּאֶל שָׁאוּל אֶת הָעָם לֵאמֹר, אָרוּר הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יֹאכַל וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״וִיהוֹנָתָן לֹא שָׁמַע בְּהַשְׁבִּיעַ אָבִיו אֶת הָעָם״. וְדִלְמָא הָכָא נָמֵי תַּרְתֵּי עֲבַד לְהוּ – אַשְׁבְּעִינְהוּ וְלַיְיטִינְהוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the fact that there is an element of oath in the term *arur* is derived **from here: "And the men of Israel were distressed on that day, and Saul administered an oath [*vayyoel*] to the people, saying: *Arur* is the man who eats** bread until the evening and I will be avenged on my enemies" (I Samuel 14:24). **And it is written: "But Jonathan did not hear when his father administered the oath to the people"** (I Samuel 14:27). The Gemara challenges: **But perhaps, here too,** Saul **performed two** actions **to** the people; **he administered an oath to them and he cursed them.**

The proof that *arur* can mean *shvua* is drawn from the verse *vayashba Yehoshua ba'eit hahi leimor arur ha'ish lifnei Hashem* — Yehoshua imposed an oath on the people saying "cursed be the man before God." The verb *vayashba* tells us this was an oath, yet the content of what was said was the curse-formula *arur ha'ish*. So the word *arur* in that context constituted the swearing.

### Saul & Yonatan

A parallel case is then adduced from the story of Shaul and Yehonatan. One verse states *vayo'el Shaul et ha'am leimor arur ha'ish asher yochal* — Shaul made the people take an *alah* (note: the very verb for *alah* is used), saying "cursed be the man who eats anything today." A subsequent verse refers back to this act with the language of adjuration: *behishbia aviv et ha'am* — when his father adjured the people. Yehonatan, who had not heard Shaul's pronouncement and had eaten from the honey, bears the verse's testimony that what Shaul said was an oath.

### Curse-Oath Unity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי כְּתִיב ״וְאָרוּר״?! הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָתֵית לְהָכִי, הָתָם נָמֵי לָא כְּתִיב ״וְאָרוּר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Is it written** in that context ***ve'arur*,** with a conjunctional prefix, which would indicate that *arur* is independent of the oath that was administered? *Arur* is written without a prefix, indicating that it is an intrinsic part of the oath. The Gemara notes: **Now that you have arrived at this** insight, **there too,** in the context of Joshua, ***ve'arur*** with a conjunctional prefix **is not written,** indicating that *arur* is an intrinsic part of the oath.

The *Gemara* raises the obvious objection to both cases: *ve'dilma tarti avad lehu* — perhaps in each instance both were done? Perhaps Yehoshua (or Shaul) both made them take an oath and cursed them, but the word *arur* itself does not mean *shvua*? The *Gemara* answers: *miksiv ve'arur* — the verse does not say "and cursed." There is no conjunction indicating two separate acts. The single act described was the saying of *arur*, and this was identified as an oath. Then the *Gemara* applies the same logic to both cases simultaneously: *hashta de'atit lehachi, hatam nami lo ketiv ve'arur* — now that you have established this from Shaul, the same is true of Yehoshua: there too it says simply *arur*, not "and cursed," meaning the oath and the curse are one and the same act.

This is a rich conceptual point that the *Gemara* is working out, and it is worth pausing over the underlying structure. What is the *shvua* part and what is the *alah* part? In a typical *shvuat ha'alah*, they are distinct layers: the *shvua* is the declarative swearing in God's name — "I swear by God that I know no testimony" — and the *alah* is the conditional imprecation — "and if I am lying, may such-and-such befall me." These two elements are normally bundled together. What the *Gemara* is showing is that when the *alah* element is pronounced with sufficient weight and in the right context — particularly when it is delivered as a conditional curse directed at another person's behavior — it is as if both elements are present. Saying *arur ha'ish asher yochal* in the context of a public declaration by a leader implicitly carries the force of a sworn oath. That is why the later verse can describe the same act as adjuration. And this is also the significance of the phrase *veshama kol alah*: either a *shvua* or an *alah* independently carries this combined force in the context of *shvuat ha'edut*.

## God's Name Requirement

### Shvuat Ha'alah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: ״אָמֵן״ – בּוֹ שְׁבוּעָה, בּוֹ קַבָּלַת דְּבָרִים, בּוֹ הַאֲמָנַת דְּבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says** with regard to the term **amen:** There is an element of **oath within it,** there is an element of **acceptance of the statement** and agreement **within it,** and there is an element of **confirmation of the statement,** i.e., that he believes and prays that the statement will be fulfilled, **within it.**

The *Gemara* now engages a puzzling verse in the *Sotah* passage: *yitein Hashem otach le'alah velishvua betoch amech* — "may God make you a curse and an oath among your people." The question this raises is: how can a person become an oath? The simple reading would be that people will use her as an exemplar in oaths — "may what befell that woman befall me if I am lying." But there is another, surprising way to read the verse: the word *shvua* itself is sometimes used to mean a curse, not just an oath. This is the reverse of what we have been proving — rather than showing that *alah* means *shvua*, this verse suggests *shvua* can mean *klala*.

The *Gemara* does not pursue this direction, however, because it would undermine the careful distinction being maintained. The point of the entire *sugya* is that these two concepts, though deeply intertwined and often expressed through the same vocabulary, represent distinct phenomena: the sworn statement and the conditional imprecation. That they are often inseparable in practice — and that the verse sometimes uses one word to gesture at both — is precisely what makes the analysis subtle.

### Implicit God's Name

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בּוֹ שְׁבוּעָה – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאָמְרָה הָאִשָּׁה אָמֵן אָמֵן״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: There is an element of **oath within it, as it is written:** "And the priest shall administer an oath to the woman…**and the woman shall say: Amen, amen"** (Numbers 5:21–22). "Amen" is the oath that the woman takes.

Rabbi Yose bar Chanina then turns to the word *amen*. He teaches that *amen* has three distinct functions: *bo shvuah* — it can constitute acceptance of a *shvua* imposed on oneself, as in *ve'amra ha'ishah amen amen* by the *Sotah*, who accepts the oath by responding *amen*. *Bo kabbalat devarim* — it can constitute acceptance of an obligation upon oneself, as in *arur asher lo yakim et divrei hatorah hazot la'asot otam, ve'amar kol ha'am amen* — the people accept the curse upon themselves. *Bo emanut devarim* — it can express affirmation of a belief, as in *vayomer Yirmiyahu amen, ken ya'aseh Hashem* — affirming confidence in the truth of what was said. The function of *amen* is entirely context-dependent.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בּוֹ קַבָּלַת דְּבָרִים – דִּכְתִיב: ״אָרוּר אֲשֶׁר לֹא יָקִים אֶת דִּבְרֵי הַתּוֹרָה הַזֹּאת לַעֲשׂוֹת אוֹתָם, וְאָמַר כׇּל הָעָם אָמֵן״.
>
> **Translation:** There is an element of **acceptance of the statement within it, as it is written: "Cursed is he who shall not confirm the matters of this Torah to perform them; and all the people shall say: Amen"** (Deuteronomy 27:26), expressing their agreement to fulfill all the matters of the Torah.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בּוֹ הַאֲמָנַת דְּבָרִים – דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר יִרְמְיָה [הַנָּבִיא] (אֶל חֲנַנְיָהוּ), אָמֵן כֵּן יַעֲשֶׂה ה׳, יָקֵם ה׳ אֶת דְּבָרֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** There is an element of **confirmation of the statement within it, as it is written: "And Jeremiah the prophet said: Amen, may the Lord do so; may the Lord uphold your statement"** (Jeremiah 28:6).

Rabbi Eliezer then adds a striking teaching: *lav shvuah* — saying "no" is an oath; *hen shvuah* — saying "yes" is an oath. The proof for *lav*: God said *velo yihyeh od hamayim lemabul* — "the waters will no longer be a flood" — and a subsequent verse refers back to this as a sworn oath (*asher nishbati*). So God's simple statement "I will not do this again" was treated as an oath. By the same logic, an affirmative statement is also an oath.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: ״לָאו״ – שְׁבוּעָה, ״הֵן״ – שְׁבוּעָה. בִּשְׁלָמָא ״לָאו״ שְׁבוּעָה – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְלֹא יִהְיֶה עוֹד הַמַּיִם לְמַבּוּל״, וּכְתִיב: ״כִּי מֵי נֹחַ זֹאת לִי אֲשֶׁר נִשְׁבַּעְתִּי״. אֶלָּא ״הֵן״ שְׁבוּעָה – מְנָא לַן? סְבָרָא הוּא – מִדְּ״לָאו״ שְׁבוּעָה, ״הֵן״ נָמֵי שְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Elazar says: No,** or any negative expression, can be **an oath,** and **yes,** or any positive expression, can be **an oath.** The Gemara notes: **Granted** that **no** can be **an oath, as it is written: "And the waters shall no more become a flood"** (Genesis 9:15). **And it is written** with regard to that negative commitment: **"As this is as the waters of Noah unto Me; as I have taken an oath** that the waters of Noah shall no more pass over the earth" (Isaiah 54:9). **But from where do we** derive the fact that **yes** can be **an oath?** The Gemara answers: **It is** based on **logical reasoning; from** the fact **that no** can be **an oath, yes too** can be **an oath.**

Rava qualifies this significantly: this applies only when the word is doubled — *lav lav* or *hen hen*. This emerges from the *Noach* passage itself, where two separate negative statements appear (*velo yikaret kol basar od mimei hamabul; velo yihyeh od mayim lemabul*), suggesting that a single statement is not sufficient and doubling is required.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וְהוּא דְּאָמַר ״לָאו״ ״לָאו״ תְּרֵי זִימְנֵי, וְהוּא דְּאָמַר ״הֵן״ ״הֵן״ תְּרֵי זִימְנֵי; דִּכְתִיב: ״וְלֹא יִכָּרֵת כׇּל בָּשָׂר עוֹד מִמֵּי הַמַּבּוּל״, ״וְלֹא יִהְיֶה עוֹד הַמַּיִם לְמַבּוּל״; וּמִדְּ״לָאו״ תְּרֵי זִימְנֵי, ״הֵן״ נָמֵי תְּרֵי זִימְנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: And** a negative expression **is** an oath only in a case **where one said no, no,** stating the term **two times, or** it is in a case **where one said yes, yes,** stating the term **two times, as it is written: "All flesh shall not be excised any more by floodwaters"** (Genesis 9:11), and it is again written: **"And the waters shall no more become a flood"** (Genesis 9:15). **And from** the fact **that no** is an oath only when stated **two times, yes, too,** is an oath only when stated **two times.**

*Tosafot* substantially downplays this ruling, explaining that *lav lav* or *hen hen* are not literally a *shvua* in the full halakhic sense, but carry a weight of quasi-promise — one should be careful, and there is some measure of consequence, but it is not a *shvua mamash*. The Rambam and Ra'avad take the *Gemara* more seriously. The Rambam explains the cases carefully: if someone explicitly had a *shvua* imposed upon them and responds *hen* — yes — that is like saying *amen* and is fully binding. Even without a *shvua* explicitly imposed, if one says *hen hen* invoking God's name, that constitutes a *shvua*, because the two components of a *shvua* — oath-language and God's name — are present. *Hen hen* serves as the technical term for the *shvua*, and the invocation of God's name satisfies the second requirement. For the Rambam, one cannot create a *shvua* by merely saying "yes, yes" without any reference to God; but if God's name is invoked together with the doubled affirmation, it is a genuine *shvua*.

The *Gemara* then introduces a dispute about cursing with a *kinnui*. A *Baraita* cites the verse *ish ish ki yekalel Elohav* — "any man who curses his God" — and asks what this adds, since the death penalty for cursing God was already stated in the verse *venoikev shem Hashem mot yumat*, which specifies the explicit utterance of the divine name. The answer given is that this additional verse extends the prohibition to the *kinuyim*, the substitute names of God. This is Rabbi Meir's position: one is *chayav misa* even for cursing God using a *kinnui*. The *Chachamim* disagree: the death penalty applies only to *shem ha-meyuchad*, and the additional verse establishes a *lav* — a negative prohibition — for cursing with a *kinnui*, without the death penalty.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְקַלֵּל בְּכוּלָּן – חַיָּיב, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר, וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **One who curses** God **employing any of** these names or appellations of God is **liable** to be executed; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis deem** him **exempt.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אִישׁ [אִישׁ] כִּי יְקַלֵּל אֱלֹהָיו וְנָשָׂא חֶטְאוֹ״ – מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״וְנֹקֵב שֵׁם ה׳ מוֹת יוּמָת״! יָכוֹל לֹא יְהֵא חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל שֵׁם הַמְיוּחָד בִּלְבַד, מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַכִּינּוּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִישׁ אִישׁ כִּי יְקַלֵּל אֱלֹהָיו וְגוֹ׳״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: עַל שֵׁם מְיוּחָד בְּמִיתָה, וְעַל הַכִּינּוּיִין בְּאַזְהָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** that it is written: **"Each and every man who shall curse his God shall bear his sin"** (Leviticus 24:15). **Why** must **the verse state** this? **Wasn't it already stated: "And he who blasphemes the name of the Lord shall be put to death"** (Leviticus 24:16)? One **might** have thought that **one would be liable only for** cursing **the ineffable name** of God **alone. From where** is it derived **to include** liability for one who curses **the appellations** of God? It is derived as **the verse states: "Each and every man who shall curse his God,"** indicating that one is liable **in any case,** even for cursing an appellation. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. The Rabbis say: For** cursing **the ineffable name** of God, one is liable to be executed **with** a court-imposed **death** penalty, as it is stated explicitly in the verse. **And for** cursing one of the **appellations** of God, one is liable **for** violating **a prohibition,** but he is not liable to be executed.

Rabbi Menachem bar Yosi extends this logic to the case of cursing a parent. The verse that describes the death penalty for cursing God is read as doubly purposeful: not only does it remind us about God-cursing, but the emphasis on "the name" (*shem*) comes to teach us that cursing a parent also requires explicit use of God's name to incur the death penalty.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַמְקַלֵּל אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ וְכוּ׳. מַאן חֲכָמִים?
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And one who curses his father and his mother** employing any of these names or appellations of God is liable to be executed; this is the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis deem him exempt. The Gemara asks: **Who are the Rabbis** whose opinion is cited here?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מְנַחֵם בַּר יוֹסֵי. דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי מְנַחֵם בַּר יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: ״בְּנׇקְבוֹ שֵׁם יוּמָת״ – מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״שֵׁם״? לִימֵּד עַל הַמְקַלֵּל אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ, שֶׁאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיְּקַלְּלֵם בַּשֵּׁם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is **Rabbi Menaḥem bar Yosei, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Menaḥem bar Yosei says:** It is stated with regard to one who blasphemes God: **"When he blasphemes a name, he shall be put to death"** (Leviticus 24:16). **Why** must **the verse state "a name,"** when it is stated at the beginning of the verse: "And he who blasphemes the name of the Lord shall be put to death"? This extraneous word "name" **taught with regard to one who curses his father or his mother that he is not liable** to be executed through stoning **until he curses them in the name** of God.

Rabbi Yannai adds that cursing another person or even oneself is prohibited according to everyone — not the death penalty, but a *lav* — even without using God's name. The prohibition against cursing another derives from *lo tekalel cheresh*; the prohibition against cursing oneself derives from *rak hishamer lecha ushemor nafshecha me'od*. This is a striking reading: the verse "guard yourself greatly," whose plain sense concerns remembering Sinai, is read as a prohibition against self-endangerment, and cursing oneself is treated as a form of self-endangerment — reflecting the *Gemara*'s view that a curse carries real efficacy.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַמְקַלֵּל עַצְמוֹ וַחֲבֵירוֹ כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: וְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **One who curses himself or another** employing any of these names or appellations of God violates a prohibition. **Rabbi Yannai says: And everyone agrees** that this is the *halakha*. Even the Rabbis, who hold that one who blasphemes God or curses his parents is liable only if he employs the Tetragrammaton, agree here that one is liable to receive lashes when he curses employing an appellation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַצְמוֹ – דִּכְתִיב: ״רַק הִשָּׁמֶר לְךָ וּשְׁמֹר נַפְשְׁךָ מְאֹד״ – כִּדְרַבִּי אָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא, דְּאָמַר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״הִשָּׁמֶר״, ״פֶּן״ וְ״אַל״ – אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה. וַחֲבֵירוֹ – דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא תְקַלֵּל חֵרֵשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to cite sources for these prohibitions. The prohibition against cursing **oneself** is derived **as it is written: "Only observe for yourself, and keep your soul diligently"** (Deuteronomy 4:9). This is **in accordance with** that **which Rabbi Avin says that Rabbi Ile'a says: Everywhere** in the Torah **that** the terms **observe, lest, or do not are stated, it is nothing other than a prohibition.** One who curses himself does not keep, i.e., take care of, himself and consequently violates the prohibition. **And** cursing **another** is derived **as it is written: "Do not curse the deaf"** (Leviticus 19:14), which applies to others just as it does to one who is deaf.

## Euphemizing Curses

### Rav Kahana's Critique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״יַכְּךָ ה׳ אֱלֹהִים״, וְכֵן ״יַכְּכָה אֱלֹהִים״ – זוֹ הִיא אָלָה הַכְּתוּבָה בַּתּוֹרָה. יָתֵיב רַב כָּהֲנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה, וְיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר הָא מַתְנִיתִין כְּדִתְנַן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כַּנֵּה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if one says: **The Lord God shall strike you** (see Deuteronomy 28:22), **and likewise** if one says: **God shall strike you** if you do not come to testify, **that is a curse that is written in the Torah.** The Gemara relates: **Rav Kahana sat before Rav Yehuda, and he sat and stated the mishna** verbatim **as we learned** it. Rav Yehuda **said to him: Employ a euphemism** and formulate it in the third person rather than the second person: God shall strike him instead of you, so that it will not sound as though you are cursing your teacher.

A practical vignette now illustrates the above with a case of improper enunciation. Rav Kahana was sitting before Rav Yehuda, who was teaching the *Mishnah*. When Rav Yehuda came to the case of *yakecha Hashem Elokeinu* — "may God smite you" — as an example of an *alah*, he read it in the second person, directly addressing those present. Rav Kahana rebuked him: *kanei* — you should say it differently. One should not explicitly invoke a curse in the second person when teaching, as though directing the imprecation at the people in front of you. One should euphemize: say "may God curse him," in the third person, not "you."

### Third Person Usage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב הָהוּא מֵרַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא, וְיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר: ״גַּם אֵל יִתׇּצְךָ לָנֶצַח, יַחְתְּךָ וְיִסָּחֲךָ מֵאֹהֶל, וְשֵׁרֶשְׁךָ מֵאֶרֶץ חַיִּים סֶלָה״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כַּנֵּה.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, the Gemara relates: **A certain one of the Sages sat before Rav Kahana, and he sat and said** the verse: **"God will likewise break you forever; He will take you up and pluck you from the tent, and uproot you from the land of the living, Selah"** (Psalms 52:7). Rav Kahana **said to him: Employ a euphemism** and formulate it in the third person rather than the second person, so that it will not sound as though you are cursing your teacher.

The *Gemara* then symmetrically records that a rabbinic student was sitting before Rav Kahana and committed the same error — reciting the verse *gam El yitatzcha lanetzach, yichtcha veyisacha me'ohel, vesharshacha me'eretz chayim selah* ("God should destroy you forever, cut you down, and uproot you from the land of the living") in the second person. Rav Kahana corrected the student with the same instruction: *amar lei kanei*. One must euphemize.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּרְתֵּי לְמָה לִי? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי מַתְנִיתִין, אֲבָל בִּקְרָאֵי אֵימָא לָא מְכַנֵּינַן; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **two** incidents to relate the same concept? The Gemara answers: It was necessary to relate the second incident as well. **Lest you say** that **this statement** applies only to **the mishna,** as in the Oral Torah, where the content, not the formulation, is significant, emending the text in the interest of euphemism is permitted; **but with regard to verses** in the Bible, where the formulation, i.e., each word, is significant, **say** that **we do not employ a euphemism.** Therefore, the Gemara **teaches us** that it is permitted to employ a euphemism even when reciting verses.

The *Gemara* then asks: why do we need both stories? The answer is that one might have thought that only the *Mishnah*'s rabbinic teaching requires euphemism — perhaps a biblical verse, being God's direct word, must be quoted precisely as written. The second incident teaches that even when quoting scripture, one must phrase it in a way that does not imply the curse is being directed at those present.

## Blessing vs. Curse

### Blessing Formula

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אַל יַכְּךָ״, וִ״יבָרֶכְךָ״, וְ״יֵיטִיב לָךְ״ – רַבִּי מֵאִיר מְחַיֵּיב, וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if one says to the witnesses: God **shall not strike you, or:** God **shall bless you, or:** God **shall benefit you** if you come and testify, **Rabbi Meir deems** him **liable,** as one may infer from that statement that if he fails to testify God will strike him, or will not bless or benefit him. **And the Rabbis deem** him **exempt** because the curse is not explicitly stated.

The *Gemara* now introduces a case where a person administers neither an explicit curse nor an oath, but instead a conditional blessing: "May God bless you if you testify about what you know." No explicit *klala* was pronounced, and no *shvua* formula was used — only a positive formulation. If the witness thereafter falsely denies knowing any testimony, has a valid *shvuat ha'edut* occurred?

### Rabbi Meir Position

Rabbi Meir holds that such a formulation creates *chiyuv* and the person would be obligated to bring a *korban*. His reasoning rests on the principle of *miklal lav atah shome'a hen umiklal hen atah shome'a lav* — from the negative you can infer the positive, and from the positive you can infer the negative. Saying "God will bless you if you testify" implies "God will curse you if you do not testify." In the ordinary language of human communication, this is indeed how such statements are understood, even if formal propositional logic would not strictly license the inference.

### Rabbis Position

The *Chachamim* exempt such a person. They do not accept the inference from one direction to the other as halakhically operative here. Since the curse was never stated — even implicitly, in a legally cognizable way — no *shvuat ha'edut* was created.

## Rabbi Meir Bilateral

### Monetary Stipulations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא לֵית לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר מִכְּלָל לָאו אַתָּה שׁוֹמֵעַ הֵן! אֵיפוֹךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't** it so that **Rabbi Meir does not accept** the principle: **From a negative** statement **you** can **infer a positive** statement, and in an agreement, he requires the parties to explicitly state both the positive and the negative stipulations? The Gemara says: **Reverse** the attribution of the opinions and say that it is the Rabbis who hold that the witness is liable and it is Rabbi Meir who deems him exempt.

The *Gemara* now asks: does Rabbi Meir's position here not contradict his well-known general rule? In the laws of stipulations (*tenai*), Rabbi Meir consistently requires that any condition be stated in both directions — *im ken yihyeh* and *im lo yihyeh*, both the positive and the negative. If P then Q, and if not P then not Q — both must be stated for the condition to be halakhically binding. This is the rule of *tenai kaful*, the doubled condition. If Rabbi Meir normally insists on explicit bilateral formulation, why does he accept here the inference from the positive blessing to the negative curse?

### Prohibition Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַבִּי יִצְחָק, תְּנָא כְּדִתְנַן. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הַשְׁתָּא דַּאֲנַן תְּנַן הָכִי, וְכִי אֲתָא רַבִּי יִצְחָק תָּנֵי הָכִי, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ דַּוְקָא תְּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **When Rabbi Yitzḥak came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he taught** the mishna **as we learned** it and did not reverse the attribution of the opinions. **Rav Yosef said: Now that we have learned** the mishna in **this** formulation, **and when he came, Rabbi Yitzḥak taught** the mishna in **this** formulation, **conclude from it** that the formulation that **we learned** in the mishna was taught **specifically** in that manner and that is the correct formulation.

The text of the *Mishnah* has Rabbi Meir's name and the *Chachamim*'s name switched, so that it is actually the *Chachamim* who say one direction suffices and Rabbi Meir who says both directions are required. When Rav Yitzchak came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he taught a *Baraita* that assigned the positions as our *Mishnah* does — with Rabbi Meir saying one direction suffices. Rav Yosef argues from this that since both our local *Mishnah* and the *Baraita* brought by Rav Yitzchak assign Rabbi Meir the position that one direction suffices, we cannot simply reverse the *Mishnah*'s text.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא קַשְׁיָא! כִּי לֵית לֵיהּ – בְּמָמוֹנָא; אֲבָל בְּאִיסּוּרָא אִית לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But** the question remains **difficult,** as Rabbi Meir does not accept the principle: From a negative statement you can infer a positive statement. The Gemara answers: **When** Rabbi Meir **does not accept** that principle, it is only **in** cases involving **monetary matters; but in** cases involving **ritual matters,** e.g., an oath of testimony discussed in the mishna, **he accepts** the principle, and the witness is liable even in that case.

The *Gemara* therefore distinguishes: *ki leit lei be'mamona, aval be'issura it lei* — Rabbi Meir's requirement of bilateral formulation applies in monetary matters; in cases of prohibition (*issur*), one direction is sufficient even for Rabbi Meir. This would explain why, by *shvuat ha'edut*, which involves a prohibition, Rabbi Meir is satisfied with the blessing alone implying the curse.

### Sotah Mixed Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי סוֹטָה – דְּאִיסּוּרָא הוּא,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But *sota* is** a case involving **ritual,** and not monetary **matters,** and the verse states only what will befall the *sota* if she did not commit adultery: "And if you did not go astray to defilement, while under your husband, you shall be absolved [*hinnaki*]" (Numbers 5:19), and the verse does not state what will befall the woman if she committed adultery.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי תַּנְחוּם בַּר חֲכִינַאי: ״הִנָּקִי״ כְּתִיב; טַעְמָא דִּכְתִיב ״הִנָּקִי״, הָא לָאו הָכִי – מִכְּלַל לָאו אַתָּה שׁוֹמֵעַ הֵן לָא אָמְרִינַן!
>
> **Translation:** A difficulty is raised: According to Rabbi Meir, who does not accept the principle: From a negative statement you can infer a positive statement, both the positive and the negative eventualities should have been written in the verse. **And Rabbi Tanḥum bar Ḥakhinai says** to resolve the difficulty: ***Hinnaki* is written,** meaning: You shall be absolved. But since it is written without the letter *yod*, it is interpreted as though *ḥinnaki* is written, meaning: You shall be strangulated, which is the eventuality if she committed adultery. The Gemara concludes: **The reason** that this verse is not difficult is **that *hinnaki* is written** and *ḥinnaki* is interpreted; **but** if **that** were **not** the case, **we do not say: From a negative** statement **you** can **infer a positive** statement. Apparently, even in ritual matters, Rabbi Meir does not accept the principle.

The *Gemara* then revisits *Sotah*, which had previously served as a test case for Rabbi Meir's *tenai kaful* rule. In the *Gemara* that deals specifically with Rabbi Meir's bilateral requirement, the *Sotah* passage posed a challenge: the verse says "if you have not sinned, you will be cleansed" — only one direction. The defense offered was *hinaki ksiv* — reading *hinaki* as *khinaki*, "you will be strangulated," so that the verse implicitly contains both directions. The *Gemara* here notes that this defense implies: *ha lav hachi* — had it not been for this reading, Rabbi Meir would have had to concede that *Sotah* requires only one direction. But if *Sotah* is a case of *issur* and Rabbi Meir permits one direction for *issur*, why did *Sotah* ever pose a problem for him?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְעוֹלָם תֵּיפוֹךְ; כִּי לֵית לֵיהּ – בְּמָמוֹנָא, בְּאִיסּוּרָא אִית לֵיהּ; וְשָׁאנֵי סוֹטָה דְּאִיסּוּרָא דְּאִית בֵּיהּ מָמוֹנָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, actually reverse** the attribution of the opinions in the mishna and say that Rabbi Meir deems the witness exempt from liability for an oath of testimony. **When** Rabbi Meir **does not accept** that principle, it is only **in** cases involving **monetary matters; but in** cases involving **ritual matters, he accepts** the principle. That is the reason he does not dispute the *halakha* in the *baraita* with regard to a priest intoxicated with wine or with overgrown hair on his head. **And** the reason that he does not accept the principle: From a negative statement you can infer a positive statement, in the case of *sota* is that ***sota* is different because it is a ritual matter in which there are** ramifications involving **monetary matters,** i.e., payment of the marriage contract. The same is true with regard to an oath of testimony in the mishna; although it is a ritual matter, it is a ritual matter with ramifications involving monetary matters.

The *Gemara* resolves this: *ushani issura de'Sotah de'issura de'is lei mamona nami* — *Sotah* is not a pure *issur* case; it contains a *mamon* component as well. The *mamon* element of *Sotah* is the exemption from paying the *ketubbah* — the *Sotah*'s guilt or innocence bears on monetary consequences to the husband. Because there is a monetary dimension, Rabbi Meir applies his bilateral requirement even in the *Sotah* context.

By the same token, *shvuat ha'edut* is also not a pure *issur* case. The entire context of *shvuat ha'edut* is monetary: someone is summoning a witness to testify in a financial dispute, and the witness's testimony — or false denial — affects monetary outcomes. Because *shvuat ha'edut* has this *mamon* component, Rabbi Meir requires that both directions be stated: the blessing and the curse must each be pronounced explicitly. The *Chachamim*, who hold that one direction always implies the other, exempt a person who heard only the blessing and then falsely denied knowledge of testimony.

The final alignment is therefore as follows: Rabbi Meir requires both the blessing and the curse to be stated, because *shvuat ha'edut* is a *mamon*-connected matter, and in *mamon* matters he always demands the bilateral formulation. The *Chachamim* hold that one direction suffices, and the unstated direction is legally implied. *Shetuyei yayin* and similar pure *issur* cases are not problematic for Rabbi Meir because everyone agrees that single-direction formulation is operative there, regardless of Rabbi Meir's general rule.
