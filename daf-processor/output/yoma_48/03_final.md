# Yoma 48 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Stuff Between Finger (II)

### Kemitzah Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, בָּעֵי רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן עוּזָּאָה: בֵּין הַבֵּינַיִם שֶׁל מְלֹא קוּמְצוֹ, מַהוּ? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: דְּגַוַּאי לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ — דְּוַדַּאי קוֹמֶץ הוּא. דְּבָרַאי לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ — דְּוַדַּאי שִׁירַיִם הוּא. כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ דְּבֵינֵי בֵּינֵי מַאי. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲדַר פַּשְׁטַהּ יְהוֹשֻׁעַ [בֶּן] עוּזָּאָה: בֵּין הַבֵּינַיִם סָפֵק נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Uza'a raised a dilemma: What** is the *halakha* with regard to the flour that remains **between** the fingers **of his handfuls?** Is this flour considered to be part of his handful, or is it considered part of the remainder of the meal-offering? The Gemara elaborates: **Rav Pappa said** that with regard to the flour **that is on the inside,** toward the hand, **do not raise the dilemma, as it** is **certainly** part of **the handful.** Likewise, with regard to the flour **that is on the outside, do not raise the dilemma, as it** is **certainly** part of the **remainder** of the flour, like the rest of the meal-offering. Rather, **let the dilemma be raised** with regard to the flour **between** the inside and the outside. **What is** the status of this flour? **Rav Yoḥanan said: Yehoshua ben Uza'a then resolved** his own dilemma: The flour that is **between is doubtful,** and should be treated as questionable in status.

The sugya on this daf opens by returning to a question that had been raised earlier regarding *kemitzah* — the act of taking a handful from a *mincha*. Specifically, the question concerned the status of the flour or other material that becomes lodged between the fingers during the *kemitzah*: is it considered part of the *kometz*, or not? The *Tosafot* understood the conclusion of that earlier discussion to be that the matter remains objectively unresolved — a *safek* — and that the practical question of how to handle such material when offering it follows from that uncertainty.

### Chafinah Between Fingers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (בֵּין הַבֵּינַיִים, פָּשַׁט, וּמָחַץ, וּפִזֵּר, וְחִשֵּׁב, בַּחֲפִינַת חֲבֵירוֹ — סִימָן). בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: בֵּין הַבֵּינַיִם שֶׁל מְלֹא חׇפְנָיו מַהוּ? מַאי קָא מִבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ? אִי גָּמַר ״מְלֹא״ ״מְלֹא״ מֵהָתָם — הַיְינוּ הָךְ! רַב פָּפָּא הָכִי בָּעֵי לֵיהּ: ״מְלֹא חׇפְנָיו וְהֵבִיא״ בָּעֵינַן — וְהָא אִיכָּא, אוֹ דִילְמָא: ״וְלָקַח״ ״וְהֵבִיא״ בָּעֵינַן — וְהָא לֵיכָּא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § ***Bein habeinayim*, *pashat*, *umaḥatz*, *ufizer*, *veḥishev*, *baḥafinat*, *ḥaveiro*:** This is **a mnemonic** for the ensuing questions of Rav Pappa. **Rav Pappa raised a dilemma: What is** the *halakha* with regard to the **in-between** incense **of his handfuls?** The Gemara asks: **What is his dilemma? If he derives** the verbal analogy between **"full"** and **"full"** from his handfuls **there,** the case of the meal-offering handful, **this** case **is** the same as **that** one, and therefore the incense between his fingers is invalid. If Rav Pappa does not accept this verbal analogy, what is the basis of his dilemma? The Gemara explains that **Rav Pappa raised the dilemma** as follows: When the verse states: "And **his hands full** of sweet incense beaten small, **and bring** it within the veil" (Leviticus 16:12), does it mean that **we** merely **require** him to bring his handfuls, and **that is** fulfilled here, as in practice the High Priest brought full handfuls? **Or perhaps we require** that he must fulfill the following two commands in a similar manner: **"And he shall take…and he shall bring"** (Leviticus 16:12)? In other words, just as he must have the intention to take the incense, so too, he must intend to bring all of it. **And that** requirement **is not** fulfilled here, as he did not intend to bring the extra incense between his fingers. No answer was found for this question, and the Gemara concludes: **Let it stand** unresolved.

The *gemara* now continues, after the two dots marking the resumption, with the words *ba'i Rav Papa*. Having discussed the material between the fingers in the context of *kemitzah*, Rav Papa raises a parallel question regarding *chafinah* — the double-handed scooping performed with the *ketoret* on Yom Kippur: *beini beini d'melo chofnov mahu* — what is the status of the *ketoret* that gets lodged between the fingers during *melo chofnov*, the filling of both hands? The mechanics of *chafinah* make this somewhat harder to imagine as a practical occurrence, but it is a genuine theoretical question.

### Identical or Distinct Q

The *gemara* immediately interrogates the precise nature of Rav Papa's question: *mai kamibaia lei* — what exactly is he asking? If the question is *i gmar melo melo mehatam* — whether we derive *melo chofnov* in *chafinah* from *melo kumtzo* in *kemitzah* — then *hainu hach*, this is simply the same question raised earlier. The *gemara* had previously asked whether you need an actual act of *chafinah* or whether it suffices to have merely the correct measure. If *chafinah* is derived from *kemitzah*, the answer to one question determines the answer to the other. So is that what Rav Papa is asking? The *gemara* says no.

### Verb Analysis

*Rav Papa hachi ba'i* — Rav Papa's question is distinct. He is focused on a particular aspect of the relevant *pasuk*: *kach lecha esh me'al hamizbe'ach u'melo chofnov ketoret samim daka v'hevi mibeit laparochet*. The *pasuk* contains both the verb *v'lakach* — taking — and the verb *v'hevi* — bringing. The *gemara* had previously established that *v'hevi* applies to both the coals and the *ketoret*, teaching that both must be brought in a single act of conveyance. Now the question is whether *v'lakach* also extends to cover *melo chofnov*.

The distinction is significant. If *v'lakach* applies to *melo chofnov*, then the material between the fingers was never the subject of a formal act of *lekichah* — taking. It simply ended up there inadvertently, without having been actively taken. If, on the other hand, *v'lakach* does not extend to the *chafinah* and the operative requirement is only *melo chofnov v'hevi* — the correct measure brought in — then whatever finds its way into the space between the fingers is subsumed within the *melo chofnov* and is legitimately included.

### Rav Papa Clarifies

The *gemara* thus frames the precise question as follows. *Hachi kamibaia lei: melo chofnov v'hevi beinun v'ika* — perhaps only the *v'hevi* is operative here, such that all that is required is that the *melo chofnov* be brought, and what happens to be present in the hands is included accordingly. *O dilma v'lakach v'hevi beinun* — or perhaps one requires both the act of taking and the act of bringing, and the material between the fingers, never having been formally taken, is excluded: *v'ha lika*. The *gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teiku*.

One might note that the *gemara* is assuming throughout that the material between the fingers falls within the category of *melo chofnov* at all. One could have argued that it is by definition external to *melo chofnov*. But the *gemara* appears to presuppose that conceptually it counts as part of the handful — the only question being whether the absence of a formal *lekichah* disqualifies it.

## Variations in Acts

### Kemitzah Normal Way

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: פְּשִׁיטָא לִי מְלֹא קוּמְצוֹ כִּדְקָמְצִי אִינָשֵׁי. בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: קָמַץ בְּרָאשֵׁי אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו, מַהוּ? מִלְּמַטָּה לְמַעְלָה, מַהוּ? מִן הַצְּדָדִין, מַהוּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** It is **obvious to me** that the act of **his handfuls** of a meal-offering should be performed *ab initio* in the manner that **people** usually **take a handful,** with their fingertips pointed downward. However, **Rav Pappa raised a dilemma: What is** the *halakha* if **he took a handful with his fingertips,** i.e., if he first put his palm into the vessel and filled his palm with flour by extending his fingers and drawing them in? Does this invalidate the taking of the handful or not? Furthermore, **what is** the *halakha* if he took the handful with the back of his hand placed **downward** in the vessel, and with his fingers collected the flour **upward** into his palm? Lastly, **what is** the *halakha* if he took the flour **from the sides,** by passing the back of his hand to and fro over the flour in the vessel with his fingers spread apart, so that the flour collects in his palm? As no answers were found for these dilemmas, the Gemara states: **Let it stand** unresolved.

Rav Papa now shifts to questions about variations in the formal execution of *kemitzah* and *chafinah* themselves. He begins: *pishita li* — it is obvious to me that *melo kumtzo* means the *kemitzah* should be performed in the normal manner in which people scoop. *Rashi* understands the standard method to be a downward-scooping motion into the palm. But what about variations?

### Kemitzah Variations

*Ba'ei Rav Papa*: If one performed *kemitzah b'rashei etzbaotav* — using only the tips of the fingers, such that the palm faces downward and the flour is scooped upward into the palm — what is the ruling? What if done *milmata l'mala* — from below upward, meaning with the back of the hand facing down and scooping from below, in the manner of a shovel? *Rashi* identifies this as in fact the most natural scooping motion for many people. And what if performed *min hatzedadim* — from the sides? *Rashi* explains this to mean placing the hand flat and drawing the flour over the edge of the palm rather than scooping downward into it. In all of these cases: *mahu* — what is the ruling?

### Teiku on Kemitzah

The *gemara* answers *teiku* across all variations. The underlying question may be understood in two ways. One is that the *gemara* presupposes that a formally defined act is required and is asking what falls within the acceptable range of that act. The other framing, more relevant in the parallel discussion of *chafinah*, concerns how much the act must be ritualized and precisely defined: perhaps once an act of *kemitzah* is required, it carries with it formal parameters, and any departure from those parameters is problematic.

## Stuff Between Finger (II)

### Teiku

As already noted in the previous segment, the *gemara* concludes with *teiku* on the question of the material between the fingers in *chafinah*. The question of whether *v'lakach v'hevi* are both operative, or only *melo chofnov v'hevi*, remains unresolved.

### Two Interpretations

There are two ways to understand what the underlying concern is. One reading focuses on the naturalness of the act: the act of *lekichah* captures only what is intentionally taken, and incidental inclusion between the fingers falls outside of that. A second reading emphasizes inadvertent inclusion: the act of scooping with both hands may produce material that is not genuinely part of *melo chofnov* at all, having entered not through the scooping motion but passively. These two framings lead to overlapping but somewhat distinct analyses of what would be required to resolve the question.

## Variations in Acts (II)

### Chafinah Normal Way

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: פְּשִׁיטָא לִי ״מְלֹא חׇפְנָיו״ כִּדְחָפְנִי אִינָשֵׁי. בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: חָפַן בְּרָאשֵׁי אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו, מַהוּ? מִלְּמַטָּה לְמַעְלָה, מַהוּ? מִן הַצַּד, מַהוּ? חָפַן בְּזוֹ וּבָזוֹ וְקֵרְבָן זוֹ אֵצֶל זוֹ, מַהוּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, **Rav Pappa said:** It is **obvious to me** that when the Torah states: **"His hands full"** (Leviticus 16:12), it means in the manner that **people** usually **fill their hands,** by placing the backs of their hands into the vessel and bringing their hands together. However, **Rav Pappa raised a dilemma: What is** the *halakha* if the High Priest **scooped a handful with his fingertips?** Again, **what is** the *halakha* if he took a handful **from downward** to **upward? What is** the *halakha* if he took a handful **from the side?** And **what is** the *halakha* if **he scooped a handful with this** hand **and with this** hand **and brought them together?** These questions are not answered either, and the Gemara states: **Let it stand** unresolved.

Rav Papa now turns to the same type of questions regarding *chafinah*. *Pishita li* — it is obvious to him that *melo chofnov* refers to the normal manner in which people scoop with both hands. *Rashi* describes this as bringing both hands together at an angle, scooping the *ketoret* up simultaneously into both palms. But the variations raise questions.

### Chafinah Variations

*Ba'ei Rav Papa*: What if one scooped with the tips of the fingers — *b'rashei etzbaotav* — with the palms facing downward and the flour gathered upward? What if performed *milmata l'mala*, with the backs of the hands downward, scooping from below at a downward angle? What if performed *min hatzad*, drawing the material over the edges of both hands rather than scooping actively? And since *chafinah* involves two hands, an additional question arises that has no parallel in *kemitzah*: what if one performed *chafinah b'zo u'v'zo v'kervan zu etzel zu* — scooping first with one hand, then with the other, each gathering one hand's worth independently, and only then bringing the two together? Rather than performing the double-handed scooping in a single integrated motion, the two hands acted sequentially and were joined afterward.

### Teiku on Chafinah

The *gemara* answers *teiku* on all of these variations. It is worth noting that these questions take for granted that a formal act of *chafinah* is required — a point that was itself left unresolved earlier, where the *gemara* entertained the possibility that *melo chofnov* is only a measure and not a required act. Here Rav Papa assumes the act is necessary and asks how precisely it must conform to the normal method. The questions about *chafinah* are thus parallel to those about *kemitzah*, even if the underlying nature of *chafinah* as a ritual act remains, as we shall see, of a somewhat different character.

## Kometz into Vessel

### Kometz on Wall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: דַּבְּקֵיהּ לְקוֹמֶץ בְּדוּפְנֵיהּ דְּמָנָא, מַאי? תּוֹךְ כְּלִי בָּעֵינַן — וְהָא אִיכָּא, אוֹ דִילְמָא: הַנָּחָה בְּתוֹכוֹ כְּתִקְנוֹ בָּעֵינַן — וְהָא לֵיכָּא. תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa: What** is the *halakha* in a case where **he stuck the handful** of flour **onto the side of the vessel?** After the flour of a meal-offering has been separated, it must be placed in a vessel for burning, an action that sanctifies the flour. Rav Pappa inquires as to what the *halakha* is if the priest places the flour on the sides, instead of on the bottom of the vessel. The Gemara clarifies the two sides of the dilemma: Do **we require** the handful to be **inside the vessel, and that is** the case here? **Or perhaps we require** the handful to be **placed properly inside** the vessel, **and that is not** fulfilled in this instance. No answer is found for this question, and the Gemara concludes: **Let it stand** unresolved.

Rav Papa asks a further question, now concerning the *kometz* from the *mincha* and its placement into the *kli sharet*. As part of the *mincha* service, after performing the *kemitzah*, the *kohen* must take the *kometz* and place it into a service vessel. This act parallels *kabalas dam* — the catching of blood in a vessel after *shechitah*. Just as the blood must be received into a vessel to be sanctified for the *avodah*, so too the *kometz* must be placed into a *kli sharet*. The question is what constitutes valid placement.

*Ba'ei Rav Papa: dveka l'kometz b'dofnei demana* — what if the *kohen* pressed the *kometz* against the inner wall of the vessel rather than letting it fall to the floor? The *kometz* is technically inside the vessel, but it is adhering to the wall rather than resting at the base. *Mai* — what is the ruling?

### Two Answers

Two possibilities are articulated. *Tocho beinun v'ika* — perhaps all that is required is that the *kometz* enter the interior space of the vessel, in which case sticking it to the wall is perfectly valid, since it is plainly within the interior. *O dilma hanacha betocho ke'tikno beinun v'hacha leika* — or perhaps one requires placement in the normal manner, meaning that the *kometz* must rest on the floor of the vessel as is standard, and here, with the *kometz* adhering to the wall, that requirement is not met.

This question reflects a broader uncertainty about the nature of the requirement. Is the operative concern the act of placing — and any departure from the standard placement is deficient? Or is it the function of the *kli sharet* — the capacity of the vessel to sanctify what is contained within it? If the latter, one must ask what mechanism the *kli sharet* employs: does it sanctify things that rest within it, or things that are within its airspace? These are questions discussed in other *gemoros*. *Rashi* on the previous *amud* had employed the language of *nitkaven* — intentionality — in the context of the *chafinah* question, but there the concern was about the word *v'lakach*. In the present context, the *gemara* frames the question more in terms of whether the act was performed in the standard manner.

Practically, one might wonder what would be done if a *kohen* had placed the *kometz* against the wall and the validity was uncertain: could he simply remove it and replace it at the base? The *gemara* does not resolve this.

### Tosafot: Kli Sharet

*Tosafot* cite a relevant *gemara* from *Zevachim* 88: *amar Shmuel, kli sharet ein mekadeshin ela mibifnim* — a *kli sharet* sanctifies only from within. This raises the precise question of what constitutes *bifnim*. If sanctification requires the item to be within the interior of the vessel in a meaningful sense, one must determine whether adhesion to the inner wall satisfies that criterion or whether it requires the item to rest within the hollow space in the standard manner. This possibility — that the question is about the nature of the *kli sharet*'s function rather than only about the form of the act — is a lens through which the question can be read.

### Inverted Vessel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי: אַפְכֵיהּ לְמָנָא וְדַבְּקֵיהּ לְקוֹמֶץ בְּאַרְעִיתֵיהּ דְמָנָא, מַהוּ? הַנָּחָה בְּתוֹכוֹ בָּעֵינַן — וְהָא אִיכָּא, אוֹ דִילְמָא הַנָּחָה כְּתִקְנוֹ בָּעִינַן — וְלֵיכָּא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar bar Rav Ashi raised** a similar **dilemma: What** is the *halakha* if the priest **overturned the vessel and stuck** the **handful to** an indentation in **the underside of the vessel?** Do **we require** the handful to be **inside** the vessel, **and that** requirement **is** fulfilled here; **or perhaps we require** it to be **placed properly** in the vessel, **and that is not** the case here? With regard to this question as well, the Gemara states: **Let it stand** unresolved.

*Ba'ei Mar Bar Rav Ashi*. Carrying forward Rav Papa's approach of posing these speculative questions, Mar Bar Rav Ashi asks: *afcheih lemana* — what if one inverted the vessel, turning it upside down, *v'davkia l'kometz b'ar'isa* — and stuck the *kometz* to what had been the floor of the vessel? The *kometz* is stuck to what was originally the base, but the vessel is now inverted so that it no longer functions as a container in any standard sense. *Mahu* — what is the ruling?

### Two Readings

Two interpretations of this case are possible. One reading, which the lecturer adopts as primary, takes *ar'isa* to mean that the *kometz* is literally stuck to the floor of the vessel, but the floor is now facing downward. The *kometz* is affixed to the underside of the inverted vessel. On this reading, the item is clearly not within *toch* — the interior — of the vessel in any meaningful sense, since the vessel is inverted and not functioning as a container. The question of whether *hanacha betocho beinun v'ika* — whether placement within the interior satisfies the requirement — becomes pressing, since on this reading the interior requirement is simply not met.

*Tosafot*, however, read the case differently. They understand the base of the vessel to be curved, such that when the vessel is inverted, its concave base creates a hollow on the underside. On this reading, the *kometz* placed within that curved hollow might be considered to be within a space analogous to the interior of the vessel, and the question is whether such a space counts as *toch*. This reading of *Tosafot* is based on another *gemara* and preserves the possibility that the item could still be considered inside some form of *toch*. The *gemara* does not resolve the question.

## Chafinah Volume

### Smooth or Heaping

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: מְלֹא חׇפְנָיו שֶׁאָמְרוּ — מְחוּקוֹת, אוֹ גְדוּשׁוֹת? אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא לְרַב אָשֵׁי, תָּא שְׁמַע: מְלֹא חׇפְנָיו שֶׁאָמְרוּ — לֹא מְחוּקוֹת, וְלֹא גְּדוּשׁוֹת, אֶלָּא טְפוּפוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Pappa raised a dilemma:** Should **the handfuls to which** the Sages **referred** be **smoothed** over **or** slightly **overflowing? Rabbi Abba said to Rav Ashi: Come** and **hear** an explicit statement in a *baraita*: **The handfuls to which** the Sages **referred** should be **neither smoothed** over **nor overflowing, but full,** without any flour spilling out.

*Ba'ei Rav Papa*: *melo chofnov she'amru — mechukakot o gedushos* — when the *halacha* speaks of *melo chofnov*, is that measure to be understood as smoothed out or heaping? Regarding the *kometz* of a *mincha*, the *kemitzah* is *machuk* — smoothed with the thumb and the small finger to an even, level measure. What is the status of *melo chofnov* for the *ketoret*? Does one smooth out the top of the double-handfuls? That would seem physically difficult, since both hands are occupied. Does one heap it? Or is the measure something else?

### T'fufot Answer

*Amar lei Rav Ashi l'Rav Papa: ta shema*. Rav Ashi directs Rav Papa to a *braita*: *melo chofnov she'amru — lo mechukakot v'lo gedushos ella t'fufot*. The prescribed measure is neither smoothed out nor heaping, but *t'fufot* — with a slight, gentle curvature at the top. It is not a leveled-off measure, which may in any case be physically impossible without a free hand to smooth it, and it is not a heaping measure, which would exceed *melo chofnov*. Rather, it has a gentle rounded curve at the top. The *braita* thus resolves Rav Papa's question.

## Blood Reception Rules

### Blood Spill Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: נִשְׁפַּךְ הַדָּם עַל הָרִצְפָּה וַאֲסָפוֹ פָּסוּל. מִן הַכְּלִי עַל הָרִצְפָּה וַאֲסָפוֹ — כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** § **We learned** in a mishna **there,** in *Zevaḥim* 32a: If **the blood** of the sacrificial animal **spilled on the floor** instead of being collected directly into a vessel, **and** a priest **collected it** from there into a vessel, **it is disqualified,** as it was not collected properly. Conversely, if the blood spilled **from the vessel onto the floor,** after it was collected properly, **and** a priest **collected it** and put it back in the vessel, **it is valid.**

The *gemara* now introduces the *mishna* from which the rules about spillage before and after reception are derived, which will serve as the paradigm for the *chafinah* discussion that follows. The *mishna* states: *nishpach hadam al haritzpa v'asfo pasul* — if after *shechitah* the blood fell directly to the ground and was then scooped up and placed in the vessel, it is invalid. The blood must be caught from the animal directly into the vessel. However, *min hakli al haritzpa v'asfo kasher* — if the blood was properly received in the vessel and subsequently spilled onto the ground, and was then gathered and placed back in the vessel, it is *kasher*. Once the blood has been properly received in the vessel and sanctified therein, a subsequent spill and recovery does not disqualify it.

### Dam Me'hapar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְלָקַח מִדַּם הַפָּר״, מִדַּם הַנֶּפֶשׁ, וְלֹא מִדַּם הָעוֹר, וְלֹא מִדַּם הַתַּמְצִית. ״מִדַּם הַפָּר״, דָּם מֵהַפָּר יְקַבְּלֶנּוּ, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ ״מִדַּם הַפָּר״ — ״מִדָּם״, וַאֲפִילּוּ מִקְצָת דָּם, וְהָאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הַמְקַבֵּל, צָרִיךְ שֶׁיְּקַבֵּל אֶת כׇּל דָּמוֹ שֶׁל פַּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֵת כׇּל דַּם הַפָּר יִשְׁפּוֹךְ אֶל יְסוֹד מִזְבַּח״. אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: מַאי ״מִדַּם הַפָּר״ — דָּם מֵהַפָּר יְקַבְּלֶנּוּ. וְקָסָבַר: גּוֹרְעִין וּמוֹסִיפִין וְדוֹרְשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **As the Sages taught** in a halakhic midrash: **"And** the anointed priest **shall take from the blood of the bull"** (Leviticus 4:5); this means that the priest shall take **from the blood of the soul,** i.e., the bull's blood that flows from the place of slaughter as the animal dies, **and not from the blood of the skin,** which bleeds out when the skin is cut before the slaughter, **nor from the blood squeezed** from an animal after the initial spurt. The *baraita* interprets the phrase **"from the blood of the bull,"** as though these words were written in a different order: **Blood from the bull,** i.e., the priest shall **receive it** directly. **For if it should enter your mind** that the letter *mem*, which means "from" in the phrase **"from the blood of the bull,"** is limiting **and** indicates that **even** if the priest received **some of the blood,** his action is acceptable, **didn't Rav Yehuda say: He who receives** the blood **must receive all of the blood of** the **bull, as it is stated: "And all the blood of the bull he shall pour out on the base of the altar"** (Leviticus 4:7)? This verse emphasizes that the priest must pour all of the bull's blood, which is possible only if he has collected all of it. **Rather, learn from this** that **what is** the meaning of the phrase: **"From the blood of the bull"?** It means that the priest must **receive** the **blood** directly **from the bull. And** this Sage **maintains** that the Sages **subtract and add and interpret homiletically,** i.e., one may take a letter from one word, insert it into a second word, and explain the phrase in that manner.

The *gemara* asks: *mina hani milei* — what is the source for these rules? *Detanu rabanan*: the verse states *v'lakach midam hapar* — the *kohen* shall take from the blood of the ox. *Midam hapar* means *midam hanefesh* — from the lifeblood, the forceful flow that gushes from the severed arteries following *shechitah*. Not *midam ha'or* — not the blood seeping from the skin, from subcutaneous capillaries, from a superficial cut. And not *midam hatamtzit* — not the residual blood that drains out slowly after the main flow has ceased. The blood used for the *avodah* must be the lifeblood proper.

### Hermeneutic Analysis

The *gemara* then moves the *mem* from *midam hapar* to derive a second rule. Reading it as *dam me'hapar yekabelenu* — the blood must be caught directly from the ox — teaches that the blood cannot first hit the floor and be subsequently scooped up. It must travel straight from the animal into the vessel.

One might have argued that the *mem* in *midam hapar* should be read in its natural sense as *from*, meaning only *some* of the blood — not all of it — need be caught. But this reading is rejected.

### All Blood Required

*V'amar Rav Yehuda*: the *kohen* receiving the blood must catch all of the blood — *kol damo shel par* — as derived from the verse *v'chol dam hapar yishpoch el yesod hamizbe'ach*, which speaks of pouring all the blood. This requirement is *lechatchila*; *bedieved*, if not all the blood was caught, it may still be valid. But since the *lechatchila* obligation to catch all the blood is established from elsewhere, the *mem* of *midam* is not needed to limit the obligation to only *some* of the blood. Therefore, the *mem* is available to be repositioned.

### Hermeneutic Rules

The *gemara* spells out the mechanics of this derivation: *gurin u'mosifin v'dorshin* — one may subtract a letter from one location, add it to another location, and then perform the *derasha*. Here, the *mem* is removed from *midam* and appended to *hapar*, yielding *dam me'hapar*, from which it is derived that the blood must come directly from the animal.

## Blood Rules to Ketoret

### Chafinah Scatter Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: נִתְפַּזֵּר הַקְּטוֹרֶת מִמְּלוֹא חׇפְנָיו, מַהוּ? יָדוֹ כְּצַוַּאר בְּהֵמָה דָּמֵי — וּפְסוּלָה, אוֹ דִילְמָא כִּכְלִי שָׁרֵת דָּמֵי — וְלָא פְּסוּלָה? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Pappa raised a dilemma** based on the above ruling: **What** is the *halakha* if **the incense from his handfuls scattered?** Is **his hand considered like the neck of the animal, and** the incense is **disqualified? Or perhaps** his hand **is considered like a vessel used in the Temple service, and** if the incense fell from his hand **it is not disqualified.** No answer was found for this question either, and the Gemara again concludes: **Let it stand** unresolved.

*Ba'ei Rav Papa*: Having established the paradigm of the blood spill, Rav Papa now applies it to *chafinah*. *Nitpazer ha-ktoret mi-melo chofnov mahu* — if, in the course of transferring the *ketoret* from the *chafinah* into the *kaf* (the ladle), some of the *ketoret* scattered and fell to the ground, what is the ruling? Can it be gathered from the ground and added back? The situation is precisely parallel to the blood falling to the ground before proper reception: the *chafinah* corresponds to *shechitah*, and the placing of the *ketoret* into the *kaf* corresponds to *kabalas dam* in the vessel. So does the same rule apply — that what hit the ground before being received in the vessel is disqualified?

### Hand as Throat

*Yado k'tzavar behema dami u'psula* — one possible answer is that the *kohen*'s hand, which holds the *ketoret*, is like the throat of the animal. Just as the blood, once it leaves the throat, must flow directly into the vessel and cannot first hit the ground, so too the *ketoret*, once it leaves the hands, must go directly into the *kaf*. What scattered to the ground was never properly received and is therefore *pasul*.

### Hand as Kli Sharet

*O dilma k'kli sharet dami v'lo psula* — or perhaps the *kohen*'s hand is to be regarded as a *kli sharet*. The *kohen*'s body is sanctified, and in many contexts we objectify the *kohen*'s body as functioning like a vessel of *kedusha*. If so, the *ketoret* was already received into the *kli sharet* — the hands — at the moment of the *chafinah*. The transfer from the hands into the *kaf* is not analogous to catching the blood from the animal. The relevant reception already occurred, and the subsequent scatter would be equivalent to blood that was properly received in a vessel and then spilled — which, as the *mishna* established, is *kasher*.

### Teiku

The *gemara* concludes with *teiku*. The *Rambam*, in his ruling on this matter, takes the stringent position and treats it as a *safek*, ruling that the *ketoret* should not be offered but that if it was, it is valid *bedi'eved*. Nevertheless, there are strong conceptual reasons, as the *gemara*'s analysis itself reflects, to distinguish this case from the blood paradigm.

### Why Chafinah Not Kemitzah

The *Tosafot Yeshanim* note a striking feature of Rav Papa's question: he raises the scatter question only with respect to *chafinah* and not with respect to *kemitzah*. Why? If we are concerned about whether the *ketoret* must go directly from the hands into the *kaf*, one would expect the same question to arise regarding the *kometz* from a *mincha* going into its vessel.

### Analogy Difference

The *Tosafot Yeshanim* explain: *peshita de-gabei kometz havei k'tzavar behema* — it is self-evident that the *kometz* must go straight into the vessel, just as the blood must go straight from the animal's throat. The analogy is direct and admits no question.

### Four Avodot Model

Why is the analogy so direct? *Lefi she-yesh arba avodot ba-mincha ke-neged arba avodot shel dam*. As demonstrated in *Menachot*, the four stages of the *mincha* service parallel the four stages of an animal *korban*: *kemitzah* corresponds to *shechitah*, the placing of the *kometz* into the vessel corresponds to *kabalas dam*, the conveying of the vessel to the *mizbe'ach* corresponds to *holacha*, and the burning of the *kometz* corresponds to *zerikah*. Because *kemitzah* is formally parallel to *shechitah*, and placing the *kometz* in the vessel is formally parallel to *kabalas dam*, the blood rules apply directly. There is no question.

By contrast, *aval b'ketoret mibaiya lei* — the question does arise regarding the *ketoret*. *Lefi she-ein kli l'chafinah ela mishum d'lo efshar* — the *ketoret* is placed into the *kaf* not because there is a formal ritual requirement of *kabbalah bi-kli*, but only because physically one cannot simultaneously hold the *ketoret* in both hands, carry the fire pan, and bring everything into the *Kodesh Kodashim*. The placement in the *kaf* is a practical necessity, not a formal *avodah* step. *Delo dami l'kemitzah* — the *chafinah* is not parallel to *shechitah* in the same structural way, and the placing of the *ketoret* into the *kaf* is not parallel to *kabalas dam*.

### Leftover Status

*Tosafot* add a further distinction. When *kemitzah* is performed, the leftover — the *shirayim* — is of an entirely different character from the *kometz*: the *kometz* goes to the *mizbe'ach* and the *shirayim* are eaten. This mirrors *shechitah*, where the blood goes to the *mizbe'ach* and the meat is eaten. The two portions are categorically distinct. With *ketoret*, by contrast, the portion that is offered and the portion that is left behind are not categorically distinct in the same way. The leftover *ketoret* from one day is simply offered on another day. There is no structural separation between *kometz* and *shirayim* as there is in both the blood and the *mincha* contexts. This further confirms that *chafinah* does not occupy the same structural role as *kemitzah* and *shechitah*, and therefore the blood rules do not apply with the same self-evidence.

This analysis illuminates why the entire trajectory of Rav Papa's questions is so significant. At one end, the *gemara* entertained the possibility that no act of *chafinah* is required at all — perhaps only the measure matters. Working through the questions, we assumed an act is required but found it to be of a fundamentally different quality than *shechitah* or *kemitzah*. And now, at the far end of the trajectory, the *Rambam* rules that *chafinah* is a full *avodah* and *machshava* invalidates. The conceptual journey from "perhaps no act is needed" to "it is a complete *avodah* subject to *pigul*" is considerable. The connections running throughout — *yad kohen k'tzavar behema*, the parallel to *kaparas korbanot* — reinforce the theme the *gemara* has been developing of the *ketoret* as something that *mechaper* in the manner of a *korban*.

## Thought in Chafinah

### Rambam Rules

Before turning to Rav Papa's question, it is worth noting how the *Rambam* rules on the matter. The *Rambam* states: *chafinat ha-ketoret avoda* — the act of *chafinah* is an *avodah*, and *ha-machshava poselet bo* — a *machshava* invalidates it. This ruling takes a clear position on what the *gemara* is about to leave unresolved. As will become apparent, the *gemara*'s attempted proof that *machshava* is operative in *chafinah* is ultimately incomplete, which makes the *Rambam*'s confident ruling all the more striking.

### Pigul in Chafinah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: חִישֵּׁב בַּחֲפִינַת קְטוֹרֶת, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן יָלֵיף ״מְלֹא״ ״מְלֹא״ מִמִּנְחָה, מָה הָתָם מַהְנְיָא בַּהּ מַחְשָׁבָה, הָכָא נָמֵי מַהְנְיָא בַּהּ מַחְשָׁבָה, אוֹ לֹא?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Pappa raised** another **dilemma: What** is the *halakha* if the High Priest **thought** a disqualifying thought **during the taking of the handful of the incense,** e.g., if he intended to burn it after its appropriate time? Does this thought invalidate the rite or not? **Do we say** that this *halakha* **is derived** by means of a verbal analogy of **"handfuls"** and **"handfuls," from** the case of **a meal-offering,** as follows: **Just as there,** with regard to the meal-offering, **thought is effective** to invalidate **it, so too** here, with regard to taking a handful of incense, **thought is effective** to invalidate **it? Or** should the two cases **not** be compared?

*Ba'ei Rav Papa: chishev b'chafinat ketoret* — if a *kohen* had an improper thought (*machshava*) while performing the *chafinah* of the *ketoret* — for instance, intending to offer it at a wrong time, on the eleventh of *Tishrei* — *mahu*? Is it invalidated? The thought of intending to perform a *korban* at the wrong time is the classic *machshevet pigul*, which is operative only during the performance of an *avodah*. The question, then, is whether *chafinah* is an *avodah* such that *machshava* is effective during it.

### Learn or Not

*Mi amrinan yalif melo melo mi-mincha*: do we apply the *gezeirah shavah* of *melo melo* and derive *chafinah* from *mincha*, where we know the *kemitzah* is like *shechitah* and *machshava* is operative? *Ma hatam mahanya bah machshava hacha nami mahanya bah machshava* — just as there a *machshava* is effective, so too here? *O lo* — or perhaps not, since the arguments developed above suggest that *chafinah* does not occupy the same structural role as *kemitzah*, and may not therefore be a full *avodah* subject to invalidation by *machshava*.

### Tevul Yom Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי לְרַב פָּפָּא: תָּא שְׁמַע: הוֹסִיף רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא (הַקּוֹמֶץ) וְהַקְּטוֹרֶת, וְהַלְּבוֹנָה, וְהַגֶּחָלִים, שֶׁאִם נָגַע טְבוּל יוֹם בְּמִקְצָתָן — פָּסַל אֶת כּוּלָּן. קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ: מִדְּפָסַל טְבוּל יוֹם — פָּסְלָה נָמֵי לִינָה. וּמִדְּלִינָה פָּסְלָה — פָּסְלָה נָמֵי מַחְשָׁבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shimi bar Ashi said to Rav Pappa: Come** and **hear** a resolution to your dilemma: **Rabbi Akiva added the handful** of fine flour **and the incense, and the frankincense, and the coals** that are collected in a vessel, to the ruling of the Sages **that if one who immersed himself during the day touched part of them, he disqualifies all of them.** Due to the respect in which sacred objects are held, these objects are treated as one solid unit. This is so despite the fact that its parts are not really attached to each other but are separate small segments and therefore, logically, one who immersed himself during the day should disqualify only those parts of the item with which he came into direct contact. The Gemara explains: **It enters your mind** that **from** the fact that **one who immersed himself during the day disqualifies** these items by touch, therefore **leaving** them after their permitted time **likewise disqualifies** them; **and from** the fact that **leaving** them after their time **disqualifies** them, therefore **thought likewise disqualifies** them. Consequently, as incense is similar to flour with regard to ritual impurity, it is also disqualified by the priest's improper thought.

*Amar lei Rav Ashi bar Ashi l'Rav Papa: ta shema*. Rav Ashi bar Ashi offers a proof. *Hosif Rabbi Akiva* — Rabbi Akiva added to the list of items that, if a *tevul yom* touches part of them, all of them are invalidated: *ha-solet*, the flour of a *mincha*; *ha-ketoret*, the incense — both the daily *ketoret* and that of Yom Kippur; *ha-levona*, the frankincense associated with the *lechem hapanim*; and *ha-gechalim*, the coals. The rule is *shenaga tevul yom b'miktzatan pasal et kullan* — if a *tevul yom* touches part of any of these, the entire quantity in the vessel is invalidated. A *tevul yom* has the status of *sheni l'tumah*; ordinarily, one might have supposed that only the individual piece touched would be affected, while the remainder, as physically separate objects, would remain *tahor*.

### Kli Sharet Unity

The reason all of them are invalidated is that a *kli sharet* unifies everything contained within it. When a *kli sharet* sanctifies the contents through *kedushat ha-guf* — intrinsic sanctification — everything within the vessel is treated as a single unified entity. A *tevul yom* touching any part therefore affects the whole.

### Time Matters Logic

*Kasalka datach*: the *gemara* builds on this as follows. *Mi-depasal tevul yom pasla nami lina* — since a *tevul yom* invalidates these items, so too *lina* — overnight — invalidates them. The connection is that both *tevul yom* and *lina* are consequences of the same underlying reality: the *kli sharet* elevated the items to *kedushat ha-guf*, and things with *kedushat ha-guf* that remain overnight become invalid. If the *kli sharet* operates this way — creating a unified *kedusha* — then *lina* is also effective. And since *lina* means time is operative, *machshava* — intention relating to time — is also operative.

It should be noted that the word *kasalka datach* here is unusual. Ordinarily this phrase introduces an assumption that will subsequently be challenged. Here, the *gemara* does not challenge it, but simply proceeds with the assumption. Similarly, *veha'tanya*, which typically introduces a challenge, sometimes functions as a support.

### Proof Gap

However, a fundamental problem with the proof must be acknowledged. The entire argument establishes that *lina* and *machshava* are operative on the *ketoret* once it is in the *kli sharet*. But the original question was whether *chafinah* is an *avodah* at all — whether a *machshava* entertained during the *chafinah* is effective. The proof addresses whether *machshava* relating to time can invalidate these items; it does not prove that the act of *chafinah* is itself an *avodah* during which such a *machshava* would operate. The *gemara* takes for granted, as *kasalka datach*, that *chafinah* is an *avodah* — but that is precisely the point that needs to be proven. This is why the *Rambam*, in ruling that *machshava* is operative, begins by asserting as a premise *chafinat ha-ketoret avoda hi* — the act of *chafinah* is an *avodah*. He recognizes that this must be stated as a premise, because the *gemara*'s proof does not establish it.

## Thought in Coals

### Pigul in Coals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: חִישֵּׁב בַּחֲתִיַּית גֶּחָלִים, מַהוּ? מַכְשִׁירֵי מִצְוָה כְּמִצְוָה דָּמוּ, אוֹ לָא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Pappa raised** another **dilemma:** **What** is the *halakha* if he **thought** invalidating thoughts during **the raking of the coals?** Does this thought invalidate the incense? The Gemara elaborates: The question here is whether **actions that facilitate** the performance of **a mitzva are considered like the mitzva** itself. If so, merely raking the coals, which facilitates the mitzva of the incense, is like burning the incense itself; therefore, an improper thought would disqualify the incense. **Or** perhaps actions that facilitate the performance of a mitzva are **not** considered part of the mitzva itself. No answer was found for this question either, and the Gemara once again concludes: **Let it stand** unresolved.

*Ba'ei Rav Papa: chishev b'chatiyat gechalim mahu* — if a *kohen* entertained an improper thought while scooping the coals, what is the ruling? *Rashi* presents two versions of what the improper thought might be: either one intends to perform the *ketoret* at the wrong time, or one intends to use the coals themselves at the wrong time. The question is whether the scooping of the coals is an *avodah* in its own right or at least an *avodah* as part of the *ketoret* service.

### Preparatory Like Main

*Machshirei mitzvah k'mitzvah dami* — perhaps preparatory acts to a *mitzvah* are treated as the *mitzvah* itself. The scooping of the coals is preparatory to the burning of the *ketoret*. If the *chafinah* of the *ketoret* is an *avodah*, perhaps this preparatory act is similarly elevated to the status of an *avodah*, and a *machshava* during it is therefore effective.

This principle — *machshirei mitzvah k'mitzvah dami* — has broad applications beyond the present context. It bears on questions such as when a *berachah* is recited for the preparation of an object used in a *mitzvah*, whether *hiddur mitzvah* applies to preparatory acts, and similar questions. But the answers are not uniform across contexts, and one cannot simply assume the same ruling applies everywhere.

### Teiku

*Teiku* — the question remains unresolved. The *gemara* does not know whether the scooping of the coals, as a *machshir* to the *ketoret* service, is elevated to the status of an *avodah* such that *machshava* during it is operative.

This unresolved question reflects the remarkable conceptual arc traversed across this daf. We began with the possibility that the *chafinah* might not require any formal act at all — only the correct measure. We proceeded through questions about the precise form the act must take, how rigidly it is defined, whether the analogy to blood reception governs, and ultimately whether it constitutes a full *avodah* subject to *pigul* machshava. And now we extend the question one step further, to whether even the preparatory act of coal-scooping shares in that status.

## Left Hand Holacha

### Left Hand Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הוֹלָכָה בִּשְׂמֹאל, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת, תְּנֵיתוּהָ: נָטַל אֶת הַמַּחְתָּה בִּימִינוֹ וְאֶת הַכַּף בִּשְׂמֹאלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Sages **raised a dilemma before Rav Sheshet: What** is the *halakha* with regard to **carrying** the blood in one's **left** hand? Is this action valid, or is carrying, like receiving and sprinkling the blood, an act that must be performed with the right hand? Rav Sheshet **said to them: We** already **learned it;** there is an answer to this question from the mishna: **He took the coal pan in his right** hand **and the spoon in his left** hand. This proves that although the spoon is carried in the left hand to the place of the service, the rite is valid.

The *gemara* now takes up a question that was raised earlier in the *mishna* but whose analysis was deferred: *holacha b'smol mah* — what is the ruling regarding the conveyance of items into the *Kodesh Kodashim* when done with the left hand? The issue of *holacha* is of particular theoretical interest. On the one hand, *holacha* is one of the four *avodot* of a *korban*, and certain disqualifications — such as being performed by a *pasul kohen* or being accompanied by a *pigul* thought — apply to it. On the other hand, the *gemara* elsewhere refers to *holacha* as an *avodah she-efshar l'vatla* — an *avodah* that can theoretically be dispensed with, since if the *shechitah* was performed adjacent to the *mizbe'ach* no *holacha* is necessary. *Holacha* is, at its core, simply conveyance from point A to point B. How much does it carry the full formal weight of a ritual *avodah*?

*Amar lei Rav Sheshet: tanituha* — we have already taught this in our *mishna*: *natol et hamachtah b'ymino v'et ha-kaf b'smolo* — the *kohen* takes the fire pan in his right hand and the *kaf* containing the *ketoret* in his left hand. The *kaf* is carried into the *Kodesh Kodashim* in the left hand. Since we have been drawing the analogy throughout this sugya between *chafinah* and *shechitah*, placing the *ketoret* in the *kaf* and *kabalas dam*, and bringing the *ketoret* into the *Kodesh Kodashim* and *holacha* of the blood to the *mizbe'ach* — this answer assumes that the *holacha* of the *ketoret* is parallel to *holacha* of *korbanot*. If the *ketoret*'s conveyance can be performed with the left hand, presumably ordinary *holacha* can as well.

### Olah Limb Precedent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִפְשׁוֹט לְהוּ מֵהָא דִּתְנַן: הָרֶגֶל שֶׁל יָמִין בִּשְׂמֹאל וּבֵית עוֹרָהּ לַחוּץ! אִי מֵהָתָם, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי הוֹלָכָה דְּלָא מְעַכְּבָא כַּפָּרָה, אֲבָל הוֹלָכָה דִּמְעַכְּבָא כַּפָּרָה — לָא, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let us resolve** this dilemma **for them from that which we learned** in a mishna: The priest who is privileged to carry the head and the leg of the daily offering to the ramp carried **the right leg** in his **left** hand, **with its** entire **hide** facing **outward** and the place of the slaughter on the neck facing the priest. This mishna also proves that carrying with the left hand is acceptable. The Gemara rejects this contention: **If** the proof is **from there, I would have said: That applies** only to a type of **carrying that does not invalidate atonement,** as even if the limbs are not carried up to the altar, atonement is nevertheless achieved through the sprinkling of the blood. The rite is valid even if the limbs of the daily offering are not burned at all. **However, with regard to** the type of **carrying that does invalidate atonement,** e.g., carrying the blood to the altar, **no,** perhaps it must be done specifically with the right hand. Rav Sheshet therefore **teaches us** from the mishna that although carrying the spoon is necessary for the mitzva, the rite is nevertheless valid if it is carried in the left hand.

The *gemara* asks: why not derive the permissibility of left-hand *holacha* from a different source? *Ha-regel she-ha-yamin b'smolo l'veit ha-ora l'chutz* — when the *kohen* carries the limbs of the *olah* to the *mizbe'ach*, the right hind leg is carried in his left hand. This would appear to demonstrate that *holacha* can be performed with the left hand.

### Essential vs. Non-Essent…

The *gemara* rejects this as a proof. *Im mei-hatam hava amina hani mili holacha d'la me'akva kapara* — from that source alone, one would conclude only that a *holacha* which is not essential to the atonement can be performed with the left hand. The burning of the limbs of an *olah* on the *mizbe'ach* is not *me'akva* — not necessary for *kapara*. The *kapara* of the *korban* is achieved without the burning of individual limbs.

### Me'akva Restriction

*Aval holacha d'me'akva kapara* — but a *holacha* that is essential to the atonement — such as the carrying of the blood to the *mizbe'ach*, the carrying of the *kometz* to the altar, or the bringing of the *ketoret* into the *Kodesh Kodashim* — one might have thought that such a *holacha* cannot be performed with the left hand, precisely because of its essential role. *Ka'mashma lan* — the *mishna* therefore teaches us that even *holacha d'me'akva kapara* can be performed with the left hand.

This point is striking in light of everything discussed across the daf. We began by questioning whether *chafinah* even required a formal act. We ended by using the *chafinah*'s conveyance into the *Kodesh Kodashim* — which is performed with the left hand — as the proof that even essential *holacha* may be done with the left hand. The *gemara* is not merely saying that the *ketoret*'s *holacha* is like *korbanot*; it is arguing in reverse — that because the *ketoret*'s *holacha* permits the left hand, the more essential forms of *holacha* in *korbanot* also permit it. The argument flows in both directions, and the full analogy between the *ketoret* service and the animal *korban* service is assumed to be operative throughout.
