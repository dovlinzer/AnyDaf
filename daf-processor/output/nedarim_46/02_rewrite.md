# Nedarim 46 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shutfin Mishnah Review

### Intro & Overview

Today's shiur covers *Nedarim* 46, the beginning of *Perek HaShutfin*. We begin by reviewing the *mishnah* that was introduced at the end of the previous *daf* before proceeding into the *gemara's* analysis. The *perek* concerns partners — *shutfin* — who hold joint ownership of a *chatzer*, perhaps a shared courtyard between two adjoining buildings or a communal hallway in a larger structure, and who have taken *nedarim* affecting their ability to derive *hana'ah* from one another or from the jointly owned space.

### Both Partners Forbidden

The *mishnah* opens: *hashutfin shenadu hana'ah zeh mizeh, asurim lehikanes lachatzer* — if two business partners have each taken a *neder* against deriving benefit from the other, both are prohibited from entering the jointly owned *chatzer*. Since each of them has partial ownership of that space, and since neither can receive *hana'ah* from the other's portion, the entire *chatzer* becomes inaccessible to both.

*Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov* disagrees: *zeh nichnas letoch shelo vezeh nichnas letoch shelo* — each one may enter into his own part. This ruling rests on the principle of *yesh breira*, the concept of retroactive clarification of the terms of an agreement. Even though the *chatzer* was held in undifferentiated joint ownership, *Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov* holds that it can become clear after the fact which portion belongs to which partner. Wherever a person walks at any given moment, he may claim retroactively that this was the portion designated for his use — so long as the *neder* against the other's property is not violated. On this view, each partner can navigate the entire *chatzer* by invoking *breira* to designate his current location as his own.

In addition to the prohibition on entering the *chatzer*, the *mishnah* states that *ushneihem asurim leha'amid reichaim vetannur ulegadel tarnigolim* — both are forbidden to place a millstone or an oven in the *chatzer* or to raise chickens there. Objects like a *reichaim* or a *tannur* are heavy, stationary, and permanently occupy a fixed portion of the space. Chickens, by contrast, range freely and effectively commandeer the entire *chatzer*. Both types of use therefore implicate the other partner's property in a way that triggers the prohibition.

One might ask: what meaningful *hana'ah* is at stake from merely walking through a communal *chatzer*? The *Ran* addresses this point based on the *gemara* in *Bava Batra*. Partners in a jointly owned *chatzer* each hold a right to protest certain encroachments — most notably the permanent installation of structures like the *tannur* and *reichaim*. The *Ran* suggests that the right not to exercise that veto — that is, refraining from lodging a protest — itself constitutes a form of *hana'ah*. It is the forbearance of the other partner, his tacit consent to one's use of the common space, that carries halachic weight.

### One Partner Forbidden

The *mishnah* then addresses a case where only one of the two partners has a *neder*: *haya echad mehem mudar hana'ah mechaveiro, lo yikanes lachatzer* — if one of them is *mudar hana'ah* from the other, he may not enter the *chatzer*. There is an important ambiguity here about how to read this case. One reading is that *Reuven* forbade *Shimon* from deriving benefit from his (*Reuven's*) property — that is, *Reuven* imposed the *neder* on *Shimon*. A second reading is that *Reuven* himself took a *neder* not to derive benefit from *Shimon*. This distinction matters greatly in light of the principle *ein adam oser davar she'eino shelo* — a person cannot render forbidden something that does not belong to him. If *Reuven* attempts to forbid *Shimon* from the *chatzer* despite the fact that *Shimon* holds partial ownership in it, the application of that principle becomes complicated. Some *Rishonim* therefore prefer the reading that it is *Reuven* who has taken the *neder* upon himself. Others hold that even the first reading is valid, because partial joint ownership creates sufficient intermingling of interests to allow one partner to restrict the other's access.

*Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov* again permits: *yachol hu lomar lo letoch sheli ani nichnas ve'eini nichnas letoch shelcha* — he can always say, "I am entering my portion, not yours." The *mishnah* adds: *vekofin et hanoder limkor et chelko* — the *beit din* compels the *noder* to sell his share. This detail provides important support for reading the *mishnah* as the first case — where *Reuven* imposed the *neder* on *Shimon*. If the situation were merely that *Reuven* forbade himself from *Shimon's* property, it would be difficult to understand why *beit din* would force *Reuven* to divest his own holding. The compulsion to sell suggests that *Reuven* is the one who has created an unbearable situation for *Shimon*, and it is *Reuven's* portion that must be removed from the equation. Both a textual and a conceptual argument thus exist on either side of this interpretive dispute.

### Third Party Forbidden

The *mishnah* moves to a third scenario: *haya echad min hashuk mudar be'echad mehen hana'ah, harei zeh lo yikanes lachatzer* — a third party from outside the *chatzer*, from the *shuk*, has taken a *neder* not to derive *hana'ah* from one of the partners. He may not enter the *chatzer* because every corner of it is jointly owned by both partners, and he cannot enter any portion without encountering the one from whom he is *mudar*.

*Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov* permits: *letoch shel chaverecha ani nichnas ve'eini nichnas letoch shelcha* — whenever this third party enters, he can invoke the principle that he is entering the other partner's portion, not the forbidden one's. Here again, *Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov* applies the same logic of retroactive designation.

This case raises the question of how a third party from outside acquires any right of access to the *chatzer* to begin with — given *ein adam oser davar she'eino shelo*, one cannot forbid what isn't his, and conversely, one does not ordinarily have rights in another's property. The *Ran* cites the *Rashba* and the *Ra'ah* on this point. The *Rashba* argues that the case applies *davka* when the outsider is entering *letzorekh hashutafut* — specifically in connection with business he is transacting with one of the partners. It is only because of an existing relationship with one of the residents that the *chatzer* is relevant to him at all; without such a relationship, his entry would be mere trespass and the *neder* would have no operative subject matter. The *Ra'avad* disagrees and permits more broadly, though the *Ran* finds the *Ra'avad's* reasoning unconvincing.

### Bathhouse/Press Case

The fourth case in the *mishnah*: *hamudar hana'ah mechaveiro veyesh lo merchatz uveit habad muskarin ba'ir* — a person is *mudar hana'ah* from another, and that other person owns a bathhouse or an olive press that are leased out in the city. *Im yesh lo bahen tefisat yad, assur* — if the owner retains *tefisat yad* in them, it is forbidden. *Ein lo bahen tefisat yad, mutar* — if he does not, it is permitted.

Understanding who is *mudar* from whom requires careful reading. The *mishnah* states *hamudar hana'ah mechaveiro veyesh lo merchatz* — the one who owns the *merchatz* appears to be the person from whom the *mudar* is forbidden. To draw the picture: the owner of the *merchatz* — call him *Reuven* — has leased it to *Shimon*. A third party, *Levi*, is *mudar hana'ah* from *Reuven*. The question is whether *Levi* using the bathhouse constitutes receiving *hana'ah* from *Reuven*. This depends on the nature of *Reuven's* lease arrangement with *Shimon*. If *Reuven* leases on a percentage basis — receiving a share of the proceeds — then every additional customer, including *Levi*, directly increases *Reuven's* income. In that case *Levi* is forbidden from using the *merchatz* because his patronage benefits *Reuven*. If, however, *Shimon* pays *Reuven* a flat fee regardless of how many customers use the bathhouse, then *Levi's* use generates no *hana'ah* for *Reuven*, and *Levi* is permitted.

The concept of *tefisat yad* thus points to a residual ownership interest expressed through ongoing financial stake — a percentage of the proceeds that keeps *Reuven* invested in the day-to-day success of the enterprise. The *gemara* at 46b will develop this point further, so a fuller treatment is reserved for the discussion of *tefisat yad* below.

### House/Field Ownership

The *mishnah* concludes with two related formulations. *Ha'omer lachaveiro konam leveitecha she'ani nichnas* — if a person says "your house is *konam* that I should not enter it" — or *vesadcha she'ani loke'ach* — "your field that I will not buy" — then *met o shemecharah le'acher, mutar*: if the original owner dies or sells it to another, the *neder* is dissolved. This is because the formulation was *beitecha* or *sadcha* — **your** house, **your** field — making the prohibition contingent on the object's ownership by that particular person. Once ownership changes, the operative condition no longer obtains.

By contrast: *konam bayit zeh she'ani nichnas* — "this house that I should not enter" — or *sadeh zo she'ani loke'ach* — "this field that I will not acquire" — *met o shemecharo le'acher, assur*: even after the owner dies or sells, the prohibition remains. Here the *neder* was directed at the object itself, not at the ownership relationship. The *mudar* would need to appear before a *beit din* to have the *neder* dissolved; there is no other mechanism by which he could ever enter that field.

This distinction invites the further question of what happens if the house is demolished and rebuilt. If the formulation was *beitecha*, it is plausible that rebuilding on the same property would preserve the force of the *neder*, since it is still "your house" on that site — though a completely new structure might challenge even that reading. If the formulation was *bayit zeh*, the rebuilt structure is arguably not *zeh*, that same physical building, and the *neder* would not apply to it. This parallels the well-known philosophical puzzle about the Ship of Theseus — at what point does incremental replacement of components change the identity of an object?

The *Rambam* in *Hilchot Nedarim*, *Perek* 8, *Halacha* 2, addresses an analogous case: a person who vows not to marry a certain woman because her father is wicked, or not to enter a house because there is a dangerous dog inside. If the father repents or the dog dies — *metu o she'asu teshuva, harei zeh mutar* — the *neder* lapses, because the original prohibition was implicitly conditional on the continued existence of the harmful circumstance. It is as if the person stipulated at the outset: this *neder* applies only so long as the problem remains.

The *Mishneh Lamelech* notes a further question raised by *Rabbi Eliyahu ben Chaim* (the *Ranach*): if the father did *teshuva* and then returned to his former ways, does the *neder* re-activate? The *Ranach* holds that it does — likening it to the house being rebuilt. The *Mishneh Lamelech* disagrees, arguing that the lapse of the *neder* upon the first *teshuva* was complete, and the subsequent relapse is a new development that cannot retroactively restore the old prohibition. The underlying question — whether a person who sins again after *teshuva* has retroactively invalidated that *teshuva*, or whether each episode is treated as a fresh transgression by a genuinely renewed individual — is itself a profound topic that runs through the literature on *teshuva* and is discussed in other contexts across *Shas*.

## Bidirectional vs Unidire…

### Bidirectional Question

The *gemara* opens with a formal *ibaya*: *benadru pligi?* — is the dispute in the *mishnah* about a case where each partner took a *neder* upon himself not to derive benefit from the other (*nadru*, they vowed), or is it about a case of *hediru ze et ze* — where each one imposed a *neder* on the other, forbidding the other from his property?

These are importantly different situations. In the *nadru* case, *Reuven* says "I will not derive benefit from *Shimon*" and *Shimon* says "I will not derive benefit from *Reuven*." In the *hediru* case, *Reuven* says "*Shimon*, you are forbidden from deriving benefit from my portion," and *Shimon* says "*Reuven*, you are forbidden from deriving benefit from my portion." The question the *gemara* poses: *mi amrinan binadru hu depligi aval behediru ze et ze modu lei Rabbanan leRabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* — do we say that the dispute exists only in the *nadru* case, but in the *hediru ze et ze* case the *Rabbanan* concede to *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov*?

Why would the *Rabbanan* concede in the *hediru* case? *Deki anusim damu* — because in that case each partner is *anus*, acting under duress. *Reuven* did not choose to make himself forbidden from the *chatzer*; *Shimon* unilaterally imposed that restriction on him. You cannot tell a person he is barred from his own home because his neighbor — who shares ownership of the entryway — chose to forbid him. The *ones* situation is foundational: the entire arrangement of joint ownership is now being weaponized against someone who had no say in it. This gives the *Rabbanan* grounds, perhaps, to agree with *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* that the partners must at minimum retain access to their own portions.

Alternatively, *o dilma afilu behediru ze et ze pligi Rabbanan* — perhaps the *Rabbanan* dispute *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* even in the *hediru ze et ze* case, and the fact of *ones* provides no relief. This is the *gemara's* open question.

### Forced Sale as Proof

The *gemara* attempts to resolve this question from the *mishnah's* second case — the unidirectional *neder*. The language of the *mishnah* is *haya echad mehem mudar hana'ah mechaveiro* — one of them was *mudar hana'ah* from his partner. The *gemara* suggests: *tani nadur* — read it not as *mudar* (imposed upon him) but as *nadur* (he vowed himself). That is, perhaps the *mishnah's* second case involves a person who voluntarily took upon himself a *neder* not to benefit from his partner.

However, the *gemara* immediately notes: *hachi nami mistabra detani seifa vekofin et hanoder limkor chelko* — the stronger reading is supported by the end of that clause, which says *beit din* compels the *noder* to sell his portion. If it were a case where *Reuven* voluntarily forbade himself from *Shimon's* property, why would we compel *Reuven* to sell his own share? He made a personal choice about his own conduct; there is no obvious reason to force a divestiture. The compulsion to sell makes sense only if *Reuven* is the one who imposed the restriction on *Shimon* — because then *Reuven* has created an impossible situation for *Shimon*, and the *beit din* acts to remedy it by removing *Reuven's* share from the equation.

This reading then implies that the *mishnah's* second case is precisely the *hediru* scenario — and the *Rabbanan* there still disagree with *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* despite the *ones* dimension. That would suggest the *ones* argument does not persuade the *Rabbanan*.

### Ones Paradox

But this produces a difficulty. If the *mishnah's* second case is one of *ones* — if *Shimon* was involuntarily subjected to *Reuven's* restriction — then why does the *gemara* need to force *Reuven* to sell? The *gemara* asks: *amai kofin oto, ha menanas anis* — if he is *anus*, why are we compelling him to divest his property? The very premise of *ones* is that the situation is against his will and not of his choosing — so it seems unjust to then impose the additional burden of forced sale on him.

The answer implied by the *gemara* is that the *ones* argument cuts the other way: precisely because the situation is one of *ones*, precisely because the partnership agreement has been rendered unworkable against *Shimon's* will, the *beit din* must intervene. The only equitable resolution is to compel *Reuven* — who created this situation by imposing the *neder* — to sell his portion and thus restore *Shimon's* ability to function. It is not a punishment of the *mudar* but rather the logical remedy when the structure of the joint ownership has been undermined. With this the *gemara* transitions to the debate between *Rabba* and *Rav Yosef* about whether the physical size of the *chatzer* affects the analysis.

## Courtyard Size Debate

### Rabba: Small = Permitted

*Amar Zeiri: machloket sheyesh bah kedei chalukah* — Rabba's position (cited here in the name of *Zeiri*) is that the dispute between the *Rabbanan* and *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* applies specifically to a *chatzer* that is large enough to be subdivided — *yesh bah kedei chalukah*. In such a case, the *Rabbanan* hold that even though each partner theoretically has his own portion, the undivided joint ownership renders entry by either forbidden partner problematic. *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* disagrees and permits entry on the basis of *zeh nichnas letoch shelo vezeh nichnas letoch shelo*.

But in a *chatzer* that is too small to be meaningfully subdivided — *ein bah kedei chalukah* — *divrei hakol mutar*: everyone agrees, including the *Rabbanan*, that entry must be permitted. The reason is that in such a small space the "private" portions of the two partners would necessarily overlap; there is no way to allocate each of them a distinct private zone. Given this physical reality, the logic of *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* becomes irresistible: each partner's use of any part of the *chatzer* simply is use of his own portion, because the space cannot be divided otherwise. The *Rabbanan* therefore concede to *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* when the *chatzer* is too small to divide.

### Yosef: BK Analogy

*Rav Yosef* challenges *Rabba's* position with a pointed analogy: *harei beit haknesset dechimi she'ein bo kedei chalukah dami* — consider the case of a *beit knesset*. A *beit knesset* functions like a *chatzer* that cannot be divided: its communal purpose makes it inherently indivisible regardless of its physical size. No one can claim a personal section of the synagogue the way a householder might claim a corner of his courtyard.

The *gemara* in *Megillah* explores the corporate character of *beit knesset* ownership. For a large city, the *beit knesset* was built with the broader public — including travelers and visitors — in mind, and therefore its ownership is so diffuse that the local residents cannot even sell it. For a small town, the *beit knesset* belongs more exclusively to its residents, who function as its shareholders. In either case, the space cannot be portioned out to individuals.

Yet the *mishnah* later rules that when two residents of the same city take *nedarim* against one another, *shneihem asurim bidvar shel otah ha'ir* — both are forbidden from communal assets of that city, including the *beit knesset*. The *beit knesset* thus parallels the small *chatzer* (which *Rabba* says should be *mutar*), yet the *mishnah* treats it as *assur*. If *Rabba* were correct that a space which cannot be divided defaults to *mutar*, the *beit knesset* case should also be *mutar*. The fact that it is *assur* defeats *Rabba's* position.

### Yosef: Size Distinction

*Rav Yosef* therefore proposes the inverse of *Rabba's* framework: *machloket she'ein bah kedei chalukah, aval yesh bah kedei chalukah divrei hakol assur* — the dispute between *Rabbanan* and *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* applies to the *small* courtyard that cannot be divided. There, *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* maintains that each partner can navigate by invoking *zeh nichnas letoch shelo*, while the *Rabbanan* hold that even this small space is entirely *assur*. In the *large* courtyard that is capable of division, however, everyone agrees it is *assur* — even *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* concedes that when the space is large enough to be genuinely subdivided, one cannot plausibly claim that wherever one happens to walk is automatically one's own designated portion. The discreteness of potential private zones undermines the blanket *breira* argument.

This is a striking reversal. *Rabba* had argued that the small *chatzer* is the easy case where all agree it is *mutar*; *Rav Yosef* argues it is the hard case where the dispute lies. The large *chatzer*, rather than being the contested scenario, is actually the one where both authorities agree the entire space is *assur*. The *beit knesset* analogy supports *Rav Yosef*: the synagogue, as an inherently indivisible communal space, is precisely the paradigm case where the dispute plays out — and the *mishnah* rules it *assur*, consistent with the *Rabbanan's* position in the small-*chatzer* dispute.

### Halacha Ruling

*Amar Rav Huna: halacha keRabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* — Rav Huna rules that the *halacha* follows *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov*. *Vechein amar Rabbi Elazar: halacha keRabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* — Rabbi Elazar likewise rules accordingly. The operative principle of *zeh nichnas letoch shelo vezeh nichnas letoch shelo* is therefore accepted as *halacha*, and *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's* invocation of *yesh breira* in this context becomes one of the recognized sources for applying *breira* in practice.

## Breira & Ones Concepts

### How Breira Works

With the *halacha* established, it is worth clarifying precisely how *breira* functions in this context. The principle of *yesh breira* holds that an ambiguous designation can be retroactively clarified by subsequent events. Here, the joint ownership agreement for the *chatzer* never specified which spatial portions belonged to which partner. When *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* rules *zeh nichnas letoch shelo*, he means that wherever a partner is standing at any given moment, that location is retroactively designated as the portion that was his — it was always going to be his portion; the current act of use simply brings that latent designation into focus. The *breira* is not a new legal act but a clarification of something that was, in a sense, always implicit in the original partnership.

This variety of *breira* is arguably more tractable than other applications of the concept precisely because there was a pre-existing purchase agreement undergirding the entire arrangement. Unlike cases of *breira* involving *terumah* or *tithes*, where the status of a particular quantity was indeterminate from the outset, here the two partners genuinely own the *chatzer* together. The task is not creating a new status but identifying what the existing ownership already implied.

### Why Ones Justifies Breira

The *ones* dimension reinforces the availability of *breira* in this context. The entire structure of the joint partnership was premised on mutual benefit and shared use. When one partner imposes a *neder* that forbids the other from accessing any part of the *chatzer*, that act fundamentally undermines the operating premise of the arrangement — not by agreement but by unilateral imposition. The partner who finds himself suddenly barred is *anus*; the situation is not of his choosing. This involuntary character of the restriction is precisely what makes it necessary to invoke *breira*: the original partnership agreement is being coercively subverted, and the retroactive designation of each partner's portion is the mechanism by which the equity of that original arrangement can be at least partially preserved.

### Partnership Assumptions

The concept of fifty-percent joint ownership is less straightforward than it initially appears. When two partners own a *chatzer* together, what exactly does each hold? Does each own fifty percent of every square *amah* simultaneously — a kind of gray undifferentiated co-ownership throughout the space? Or does the arrangement carry an implicit understanding that certain zones are more naturally associated with one partner, particularly the area directly in front of each partner's dwelling? Does "fifty percent" mean fifty percent of the space at all times, or does it contemplate alternating exclusive use — each partner enjoying full access on designated days?

These questions recur throughout *Shas* in contexts involving jointly owned animals, jointly owned tools, and jointly owned slaves. The fundamental ambiguity of what shared ownership operationally means is precisely the gap that *breira* is invoked to fill. In the context of the *chatzer*, the practical implication is that whatever portion a partner is actually using at a given moment may be understood as the portion that was always notionally his — at least when the alternative would leave him with no viable access to property he co-owns.

## Tefisat Yad Analysis

### Rav Nachman: 25% Thresho…

The *gemara* now returns to the *tefisat yad* case from the *mishnah* and asks: *vekamah tefisat yad* — how large must the owner's percentage stake be in order to constitute *tefisat yad* and thereby render use of the bathhouse or press *assur* to the *mudar*?

*Amar Rav Nachman: lemechtza, leshlish, uleravia* — Rav Nachman rules that *tefisat yad* applies when the lessor retains a profit share of one-half, one-third, or one-quarter of the proceeds. *Aval babatzir lo* — but if the percentage falls below a quarter, it does not constitute *tefisat yad*. On this view, only profit-sharing arrangements at or above the twenty-five percent threshold preserve sufficient residual ownership interest to make the *mudar's* use of the facility a prohibited *hana'ah* for the lessor.

### Abaye: All Percentages C…

*Abaye* disagrees: *afilu babatzir assur* — even a percentage below one-quarter creates *tefisat yad* and renders use *assur*. Abaye's logic is straightforward: if the arrangement is percentage-based at all, then each additional customer who uses the bathhouse or press directly increases the lessor's income. Even at ten percent, one more person using the *merchatz* means the original owner earns ten percent more from that visit. The lessor thus has a real and direct financial stake in every individual use of the facility, and the *mudar's* patronage therefore constitutes *hana'ah* flowing to the *mudar-mimenu*. Abaye's position appears to be the logically stronger one — if Rav Nachman accepts that a quarter-share creates *tefisat yad*, it is difficult to see why a tenth-share would not, since the mechanism is identical.

### Percentage vs Fixed Rate

The *gemara* resolves the apparent tension by identifying the case in which *tefisat yad* definitively does not apply: *heichi dami dishru, hamikabel betaska* — the permitted case is where the lessee pays a fixed fee, a *taska*. A *taska* is a flat annual payment to the lessor, independent of how many customers use the facility or how much revenue the business generates. When the arrangement is structured as a *taska*, the number of people who use the *merchatz* has no effect whatsoever on the lessor's income; he has already received his fixed payment. The *mudar's* patronage therefore generates no *hana'ah* for him, and use is permitted.

*Tefisat yad* is thus defined precisely as a percentage-of-proceeds arrangement — any arrangement in which the lessor's income is tied to the ongoing use and revenue of the facility. A *taska* (fixed rate) excludes *tefisat yad* entirely. Both Rav Nachman and Abaye agree on this structural point; their dispute concerns only the minimum percentage threshold at which a profit-sharing arrangement triggers *tefisat yad*.

### Market Practice Standard

The *gemara* invokes the phrase *machtela shilash verivea* — a standard formulation well-known from elsewhere in *Shas* (it appears, for instance, in the context of *geneiva* in *Bava Kamma*) — to indicate that the relevant percentages are not technically precise cutoffs but rather reflect common market practice for this type of transaction in this locality. The fractions of half, third, and quarter are not arbitrary; they represent the standard terms at which these leasing arrangements were conventionally structured in the marketplace. *Tefisat yad* is therefore defined not by an abstract numerical floor but by reference to market norms: whatever share of proceeds is typical for this kind of arrangement in this time and place defines the threshold. Below those conventional market rates, the arrangement is so attenuated that it no longer reflects genuine ongoing ownership interest; at or above those rates, the lessor's stake in the enterprise remains real and operative.
