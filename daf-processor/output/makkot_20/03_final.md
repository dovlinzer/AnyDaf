# Makkot 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Maaser Sheni Entry

### Rav Assi's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי מֵאֵימָתַי חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו – מִשֶּׁרָאָה פְּנֵי הַחוֹמָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ תֹאכְלֶנּוּ (שָׁנָה בְשָׁנָה)״, וּכְתִיב ״(כִּי) לֹא תוּכַל לֶאֱכֹל בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ״, כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּקָרֵינַן בֵּיהּ ״לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ תֹאכְלֶנּוּ״ – קָרֵינַן בֵּיהּ ״לֹא תוּכַל לֶאֱכוֹל בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ״. וְכֹל הֵיכָא דְּלָא קָרֵינַן בֵּיהּ ״לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ תֹאכְלֶנּוּ״ – לָא קָרֵינַן בֵּיהּ ״לֹא תוּכַל לֶאֱכוֹל בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to **second-tithe** produce, **from when is one liable** to receive lashes **for** eating **it** outside the walls of Jerusalem? It is **from when** the produce **entered within the wall** of Jerusalem. **What is the reason** for this? It is derived **as the verse states** with regard to second-tithe produce: **"Before the Lord your God you shall eat it"** (Deuteronomy 12:18), **and it is written** in the previous verse: **"You may not eat within your gates"** (Deuteronomy 12:17), from which it is derived: **Anywhere that we read concerning it: "Before the Lord your God you shall eat it," we read concerning it** the prohibition: **"You may not eat within your gates,"** i.e., it may not be eaten outside the walls of Jerusalem; **and anywhere that we do not read concerning it: "Before the Lord your God you shall eat it,"** as the produce remained outside the walls, **we do not read concerning it** the prohibition: **"You may not eat within your gates."**

The daf picks up on 19b, where the Gemara is engaged with a list of prohibitions that carry *malkos*, including the eating of *maaser sheni* outside of Yerushalayim, *kodashim kalim* consumed outside of Yerushalayim, and similar cases. Following that broader discussion, the Gemara now focuses specifically on *maaser sheni*.

Rav Assi said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *maaser sheni me'imatai chayavin alav?* — from when is one liable for *maaser sheni*? The intuitive answer seems obvious: when you eat it outside of Yerushalayim. But that is precisely the *chiddush* — that answer is not correct. The rule is *misheyira pnei hachomah*: the *maaser sheni* must first have been brought inside the walls of Yerushalayim, and only then, if it is taken back out and eaten outside, does one incur *malkos*. If the *maaser sheni* was separated but never brought into Yerushalayim, there is a violation of a positive commandment but not the *lav* that carries *malkos*.

### Walls Requirement

The reasoning behind this requirement follows from a careful reading of the relevant *pesukim*. The positive commandment is *lifnei Hashem Elokecha tochalennu* — you shall eat it before Hashem your God. The *lav* is *lo tuchal le'echol bish'arecha* — you may not eat it in your gates — referring to *maaser deganecha tiroshecha veyitzharecha*. The Gemara reads these two verses in conjunction: wherever it is appropriate to say *lifnei Hashem Elokecha tochalennu* — wherever the produce has reached the stage where it is fit to be eaten as *maaser sheni* before Hashem — that is precisely the context in which the Torah then says *lo tuchal le'echol bish'arecha*.

### Pesukim Analysis

The logic is that *maaser sheni* is unlike other things that must be consumed in Yerushalayim, which typically acquire their sanctified status *within* Yerushalayim. *Maaser sheni* begins outside. The Torah's positive command — eat it before Hashem — becomes operative only once it has been present inside Yerushalayim, having been fit to be eaten in that sacred context. Only then does the accompanying *lav* — do not eat it in your gates — become applicable. Before it has ever entered Yerushalayim and been in that state of readiness, *lo karina bei lo tuchal le'echol bish'arecha*: the prohibitory verse simply does not apply, and therefore there is no *lav* to carry *malkos*.

## Tosefta Fig Case

### Fig Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: כֹּהֵן שֶׁעָלְתָה בְּיָדוֹ תְּאֵנָה שֶׁל טֶבֶל, אָמַר: תְּאֵנָה זוֹ, תְּרוּמָתָהּ בְּעוּקְצָהּ, מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן בִּצְפוֹנָהּ, וּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי לִדְרוֹמָהּ. וְהִיא שְׁנַת מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי וְהוּא בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, אוֹ מַעְשַׂר עָנִי וְהוּא בִּגְבוּלִין, אֲכָלָהּ –
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara raises an objection** from that which **Rabbi Yosei says:** With regard to **a priest that a fig of untithed produce came into his possession,** and he seeks to separate *terumot* and tithes from it, **he says: This fig,** I designate **its *teruma* at its stem,** I designate its **first tithe at its north** side, **and** I designate its **second tithe at its south** side; **and it is a year** when **second tithe** is separated, i.e., the first, second, fourth, or fifth years of the Sabbatical Year cycle, **and he is in Jerusalem,** when he recites this formula. **Or** he says that poor man's tithe shall be designated at its south side if it is a year of **poor man's tithe,** i.e., the third or sixth years of the Sabbatical Year cycle, **and he is** located anywhere **in the outlying areas,** not in Jerusalem. In those cases, if **he ate** the fig,

A challenge is now raised from a *Tosefta*. Rabbi Yosi describes the following scenario: a *kohen* holds a fig that is *tevel* — produce from which the required separations have not yet been made. The *kohen* declares: the tip of this fig (by the stem) is designated as *terumah*; the northern portion is *maaser rishon*; and the southern portion is *maaser sheni*. He has been making his designations verbally, giving the whole fig a structure of separated gifts, though without having actually physically removed anything. The case is set in a *maaser sheni* year and he is standing in Yerushalayim — or, in the alternate version, it is a *maaser ani* year and he is outside.

### Tosefta Implications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לוֹקֶה אַחַת. וְזָר שֶׁאֲכָלָהּ – לוֹקֶה שְׁתַּיִם. שֶׁאִילּוּ בַּתְּחִלָּה אֲכָלָהּ – אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה אֶלָּא אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **he is flogged** with **one** set of lashes, because he ate it without separating *teruma* of the tithe from the first tithe and is consequently liable for eating untithed produce. Had he separated *teruma* of the tithe, it would have been permitted for him to eat the entire fig, as it is permitted for a priest to partake of both *teruma gedola* and *teruma* of the tithe. **And a non-priest who eats** the fig **is flogged** with **two** sets of lashes, for both eating untithed produce and eating the *teruma* therein, which is forbidden for all non-priests. The *baraita* notes: **As if** the non-priest **ate** the fig **initially,** before separating *teruma* and tithes, **he is flogged** with **only one** set of lashes, for eating untithed produce. Once he separated the *teruma* and tithes, he is flogged for eating *teruma* as well.

A further dimension emerges. *Vezar she'achlah lokeh shtayim* — if a non-*kohen* were to eat this fig, he receives two sets of *malkos*: one for eating *tevel* and one for eating *terumah*. And here the *Tosefta* introduces a striking irony: *she'ilu betchilah achlah eino lokeh ela al achat* — had the non-*kohen* eaten the fig at the very outset, before any designations were made at all, he would have received only one set of *malkos*, since the whole thing was undifferentiated *tevel*. But now that the *kohen* has made his declarations, there is both *terumah* and *tevel* of *maaser rishon* present, yielding an additional prohibition.

### Gemara Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּאִיתֵיהּ בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, הָא בִּגְבוּלִין – לוֹקֶה שָׁלֹשׁ, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָאו רוֹאֶה פְּנֵי חוֹמָה! דְּעַיְּילַי[הּ] וְאַפְּקַי[הּ].
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers from the statement in the *baraita*: And it is a year when second tithe is separated and he is in Jerusalem, that **the reason** he is flogged with two sets of lashes **is that he is** currently **in Jerusalem,** where it is permitted to partake of second-tithe produce. **But** if he ate the fig **in the outlying areas,** outside of Jerusalem, and he designated second tithe in the fig, **he is flogged** with **three** sets of lashes. It is apparent **that even if** this second-tithe produce **has not entered within the wall** of Jerusalem, one is liable to receive lashes for its consumption. This contradicts Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion, as he holds that one is liable to receive lashes for eating second-tithe produce only if it entered Jerusalem. The Gemara answers: The case in the *baraita* is one **where they took** the fig **into** Jerusalem **and** then **took it out** of Jerusalem. That is why he is liable to receive a third set of lashes.

The Gemara turns to the implications of the *Tosefta* for our original rule. The fact that the *Tosefta* sets its case in Yerushalayim — and on that basis the *maaser sheni* does not generate *malkos* — implies, *ha bigvulin lokeh shalosh*: had the same situation occurred outside of Yerushalayim in a *maaser sheni* year, the *kohen* would have received *malkos* for the *maaser sheni* as well. But this directly contradicts Rav Assi's teaching, for the *maaser sheni* in this case had never been brought inside the walls. It appears that eating *maaser sheni* outside of Yerushalayim generates *malkos* even without prior entry into the city.

The Gemara responds with what it acknowledges is a somewhat forced *ukiimta*: *d'oil v'afkei* — the *Tosefta* is speaking of a case where the produce had been brought into Yerushalayim and then taken back out. In the *Tosefta*'s scenario, the *kohen* is in Yerushalayim and therefore receives no *malkos* for the *maaser sheni*. The implicit contrast is not with a case where the produce was never in Yerushalayim, but with a case where it was in Yerushalayim and then brought back out — in which case he *would* receive an additional set of *malkos*.

But the Gemara immediately presses: *ee hachi mai l'meimra?* — if that is all the *Tosefta* is telling us, what is the *chiddush*? That eating *maaser sheni* outside after having brought it in and taken it out is forbidden is entirely obvious. There must be something more novel here.

## Matnot Status

### Solution Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן דְּעַיְּילִינְהוּ בְּטִיבְלַיְיהוּ, וְקָסָבַר: מַתָּנוֹת שֶׁלֹּא הוּרְמוּ – כְּמִי שֶׁהוּרְמוּ דָּמְיָין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, what** is the purpose **of stating** this *halakha*? If the fig entered and exited Jerusalem there is no novel element in the fact that one is liable to receive lashes. The Gemara answers: There is a novel element in this *halakha*, as **what are we dealing with here?** We are dealing with a case **where one took** figs into the city while **they** had the status of **untithed produce,** before *terumot* and tithes were separated, **and** Rabbi Yosei **holds** that the status of **gifts that were not** yet **separated** from the produce is **like** that of gifts **that were** already **separated.** The novel element is that although second tithe was not separated in practice, all the *halakhot* of second tithe are in effect with regard to the portion that he intends to separate as second tithe. Therefore, if after taking the untithed produce into Jerusalem he took it outside of Jerusalem, and ate it, he is liable to receive lashes.

The Gemara answers: *hocha b'mai askinan kegon d'oiliuhu bitveilaihu* — the case under discussion is one where the fig was brought into Yerushalayim while still in its *tevel* state, before any separations had been made at all. And the position being expressed is *d'kasavar matnos shelo hurmu k'mi shehurmu damyan* — that gifts which have not yet been separated are treated as if they have already been separated.

The *chiddush* now becomes clear. The *kohen* had the fig in hand, brought it into Yerushalayim before performing any *hafrasha*, and then either performed the separations in Yerushalayim — in which case the *maaser sheni* is in Yerushalayim and he eats it there without *malkos* — or brought it back out first and then made his declarations. In the latter scenario, the *Tosefta* teaches that he would incur an additional set of *malkos* for the *maaser sheni*, because the fact that the *tevel* fig was brought into Yerushalayim is already considered as if the *maaser sheni* component was brought into Yerushalayim. Since that portion was conceptually present when the fig entered the city, it counts as having been brought in and brought back out.

### Matnot Principle

The novel principle at work here is that *matnos shelo hurmu k'mi shehurmu damyan* — the priestly and levitical gifts that have not yet been physically separated are nevertheless regarded, for certain legal purposes, as if they are already separately present within the produce. When the undivided fig enters Yerushalayim, the *maaser sheni* "within" it is already treated as having entered, even though no designation has yet occurred. This is what transforms an otherwise straightforward case into a meaningful *chiddush* in the *Tosefta*.

### Reb Yosi Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי מַתָּנוֹת שֶׁלֹּא הוּרְמוּ כְּמִי שֶׁהוּרְמוּ דָּמֵי? וְהָתַנְיָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל עַל פֵּירוֹת שֶׁלֹּא נִגְמְרָה מְלַאכְתָּן וְעָבְרוּ בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם – שֶׁיִּפָּדֶה מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי שֶׁלָּהֶן וְיֵאָכֵל בְּכׇל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** does **Rabbi Yosei hold** that the status of **gifts that were not** yet **separated** from the produce is **like** that of gifts **that were** already **separated? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Yosei: Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not disagree with regard to produce whose labor was not completed** and the obligation to tithe had consequently not yet taken effect, **and** which at that stage **passed through Jerusalem, that its second-tithe** produce **may be redeemed and may be eaten anywhere.** As long as the obligation to tithe has not taken effect, the sanctity of Jerusalem does not affect the produce.

A difficulty now arises. This *Tosefta* is attributed to Rabbi Yosi. Does Rabbi Yosi in fact hold that unseparated gifts are treated as if already separated? A *Baraita* quoted in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda citing Rabbi Yosi suggests the opposite: *lo necheluku Beis Shammai u'Beis Hillel al peiros shelo nigmera melachtan v'avru b'Yerushalayim sheyifteru maaser sheini shelahem v'yochlu b'chol makom* — Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel did not dispute that if produce which had not yet reached the stage of *gmar melacha* — the completion of processing that renders it liable in *maaser* — was brought into Yerushalayim and taken back out, the *maaser sheni* separated from it afterward may still be redeemed and eaten anywhere. In that case, everyone agrees there is no *klitas mechitzos*, no status-affixing by the walls. The debate between Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel concerned only produce that *had* reached *gmar melacha* and was therefore *tevel* proper, which was then brought in and out of Yerushalayim.

## Mechitzot Distinction

### Beis Shammai Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַל מָה נֶחְלְקוּ – עַל פֵּירוֹת שֶׁנִּגְמְרָה מְלַאכְתָּן וְעָבְרוּ בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, שֶׁבֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: יַחְזִיר מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי שֶׁלָּהֶם וְיֵאָכֵל בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: יִפָּדֶה וְיֵאָכֵל בְּכׇל מָקוֹם. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מַתָּנוֹת שֶׁלֹּא הוּרְמוּ כְּמִי שֶׁהוּרְמוּ דָּמְיָין – הָא קְלָטוּהוּ מְחִיצוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to what** case **did they disagree?** It is **with regard to produce whose labor was** already **completed and it passed through Jerusalem** before *terumot* and tithes were separated from it, **as Beit Shammai say:** Since one is obligated to separate second tithe from this produce, and the produce was in Jerusalem, **he must bring its second-tithe** produce **back** to Jerusalem, **and it shall be eaten** it **in Jerusalem; and Beit Hillel say:** The produce **may be redeemed and eaten anywhere. And if it enters your mind** to say the status of **gifts that were not** yet **separated** from the produce is **like** that of gifts **that were** already **separated,** how can Beit Hillel hold that the second-tithe produce may be redeemed; **wasn't** the produce **admitted** by **the walls** of Jerusalem? Therefore, even though second tithe was not yet separated, it can no longer be redeemed. Apparently, Rabbi Yosei, who certainly holds in accordance with the opinion of Beit Hillel, holds that the status of gifts that were not yet separated from the produce is not like that of gifts that were already separated.

The dispute between Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel, as presented in this *Baraita* from Rabbi Yosi, concerns the case of *peiros* that completed their *gmar melacha* — they are fully *tevel* and liable in *maaser* — and were then brought into Yerushalayim and subsequently taken back out while still unseparated. The question: once you later separate the *maaser sheni*, can it be redeemed and eaten anywhere? Or has the *maaser sheni* already had its status fixed — *klitas mechitzos* — by virtue of having been present within the walls of Yerushalayim, even while still embedded in the *tevel*?

Beis Shammai holds: *yachzir maaser sheni l'sham v'yeiachel b'Yerushalayim* — you must bring the *maaser sheni* back to Yerushalayim and eat it there. In their view, the *maaser sheni*'s status was fixed when the *tevel* fig passed through the walls — *matnos shelo hurmu k'mi shehurmu damyan* — and the *maaser sheni* is now as if it genuinely entered Yerushalayim. It cannot subsequently be redeemed. Beis Hillel, however, maintains *yipdeh v'yeiachel b'chol makom* — it may be redeemed and eaten anywhere. Beis Hillel does not treat the unseparated gift as legally present for the purpose of *klitas mechitzos*.

This seems to create an internal contradiction within Rabbi Yosi's own position. The *Tosefta* (attributed to Rabbi Yosi) appears to invoke the principle that unseparated gifts are treated as already present — yielding *malkos* when the fig is brought in and taken out — yet the *Baraita* (also from Rabbi Yosi) attributes that very principle to Beis Shammai, while Beis Hillel (the accepted *halakha*) rejects it. How can the *Tosefta* rest on a principle that we rule against?

### Rabbah's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: מְחִיצָה לֶאֱכוֹל דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, מְחִיצָה לִקְלוֹט – דְּרַבָּנַן. וְכִי גְזוּר רַבָּנַן – כִּי אִיתֵיהּ בְּעֵינֵיהּ, בְּטִבְלֵיהּ לָא גְּזוּר רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said:** The *halakha* of the **wall with regard to eating** second-tithe produce, i.e., that second-tithe produce may not be eaten outside the wall of Jerusalem, is **by Torah law;** the *halakha* of the **wall with regard to the admitting** of second-tithe produce, i.e., that once the produce enters within the wall of Jerusalem, it may not be redeemed or taken beyond the wall, is **by rabbinic law. And when the Sages issued the decree** that one may not remove from Jerusalem second-tithe produce that entered within the walls, they did so **when** the tithe **is in its unadulterated form** after it was separated; but in a case where it is **in its** status of **untithed produce, the Sages did not issue a decree.**

Rabbah resolves the tension with a foundational distinction: *mechitzah l'echol d'Oraisa, mechitzah liklot d'Rabanan*. The requirement that *maaser sheni* be eaten within the walls of Yerushalayim is a Biblical obligation. The rule that once *maaser sheni* enters Yerushalayim its status is fixed and it can no longer be redeemed — *klitas mechitzos* — is a Rabbinic enactment. These are two legally distinct applications of the walls of Yerushalayim.

### Lashes vs. Redemption

Given this distinction, the Gemara can now differentiate the two cases. With respect to *malkos* — which is grounded in the Biblical prohibition *lo tuchal le'echol bish'arecha* — we apply the *d'Oraisa* standard. Even when the *maaser sheni* is not yet separated, we treat the unseparated gift as present: *metivlo shelo huram k'mi shehuram dami*. Since the *tevel* fig entered Yerushalayim, the *maaser sheni* within it is regarded as having entered, rendering it fit for its required context of consumption. Taking it back out and eating it therefore violates the Biblical *lav* and carries *malkos*.

With respect to the Rabbinic question of whether the *maaser sheni* can still be redeemed after being brought in and taken out as *tevel* — which is the dispute between Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel — the Rabbinic enactment of *klitas mechitzos* is limited to cases where the *maaser sheni* was actually separated before entering Yerushalayim. *V'ki gazur Rabanan ki isa b'einei b'tivlo lo gazur Rabanan* — when the Rabbis enacted this restriction, they restricted it to cases where the *maaser sheni* was genuinely present, not merely present in potential. Beis Hillel accordingly rules that the *maaser sheni* may still be redeemed, and this ruling does not contradict the *d'Oraisa* position that carries *malkos* in the Biblical domain.

## Ravina Clarifies

### Stick Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: כְּגוֹן דְּנָקֵיט לֵיהּ בְּקַנְיָא. וְתִפְשׁוֹט בַּעְיָא דְּרַב פָּפָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said:** Rabbi Yosei is referring to the case of second-tithe produce that was already separated and brought into Jerusalem, and the novel element of his statement is that he is referring to a fig that was not physically taken into Jerusalem; rather, it is a case **where one is holding** the fig **on a reed** outside Jerusalem, while he is standing inside Jerusalem. **And resolve the dilemma of Rav Pappa** (19b) and conclude that if one is standing inside Jerusalem and holding second tithe outside the city on a reed, it is as though the produce entered the city.

Ravina offers an alternative reading of the *Tosefta* case, returning to close the discussion of the original question. He proposes: *kegon d'nakit lei bekanya* — the scenario is one in which the produce was connected to the outside by a stick. The person was standing inside Yerushalayim, but the produce itself was in a bag located just outside the walls, physically connected to him by a stick.

### Partial Presence

This connects to a question raised earlier by Rav Pappa. In that scenario, the produce was never fully inside Yerushalayim in any conventional sense, yet the person's own presence inside the city, combined with this physical connection, creates an ambiguous status — something halfway between being inside and outside. According to Ravina, the *Tosefta*'s *chiddush* is precisely this: that even such a case of partial or indirect presence would be treated as the *maaser sheni* having been "brought in" to Yerushalayim and then "taken out," such that eating it outside would generate *malkos*. This is not at all obvious, and therefore the *Tosefta* is saying something genuinely novel — that even this attenuated form of presence within the walls suffices to trigger the Biblical consequence. What does *not* happen, and what the Gemara consistently rejects across both readings, is that *malkos* applies to *maaser sheni* which was always, entirely outside of Yerushalayim from start to finish.

## Mourning Prohibitions

### Korach Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַקּוֹרֵחַ קׇרְחָה בְּרֹאשׁוֹ וְהַמַּקִּיף פְּאַת רֹאשׁוֹ, וְהַמַּשְׁחִית פְּאַת זְקָנוֹ, וְהַשּׂוֹרֵט שְׂרִיטָה אַחַת עַל הַמֵּת – חַיָּיב. שָׂרַט שְׂרִיטָה אַחַת עַל חֲמִשָּׁה מֵתִים, אוֹ חָמֵשׁ שְׂרִיטוֹת עַל מֵת אֶחָד – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who creates a bald spot upon his head, and one who rounds the edge of his head** by shaving the hair adjacent to the ear, **and one who mars the edge of his beard, and one who cuts one incision** in a display of mourning **over the dead,** are all **liable** to receive lashes. If **he cut one incision over five dead** people, **or five incisions over one dead** person, he is **liable** to receive lashes **for each and every one.**

The Gemara now moves to the next *Mishnah*, which introduces several prohibitions relating to mourning practices. The *Mishnah* opens with *hakoreiach karcha berosho* — one who makes a bald spot on his head as an act of mourning over the dead — and immediately invokes the *pasuk* from *Emor* addressed to the *Kohanim*: *lo yikchu korcha berosham u'feas zkhanam lo yegaleichu*. Two separate types of appearance-altering practices are grouped together: making a bald patch as an expression of grief, and shaving the corners of the beard.

### Peyot Beard Terms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל הָרֹאשׁ – שְׁתַּיִם, אַחַת מִכָּאן וְאַחַת מִכָּאן, עַל הַזָּקָן – שְׁתַּיִם מִכָּאן וּשְׁתַּיִם מִכָּאן, וְאַחַת מִלְּמַטָּה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אִם נִיטְּלוּ כּוּלָּן כְּאַחַת – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. וְאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיִּטְּלֶנּוּ בְּתַעַר. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ לִקְּטוֹ בְּמַלְקֵט אוֹ בִּרְהִיטָנִי – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **For** rounding the edges of his **head,** one is liable to receive **two** sets of lashes, **one from here,** the hair adjacent to one ear, **and one from there,** the hair adjacent to the other ear. **For** marring the edges of his **beard** there are **two** edges **from here,** on one side of his face, **and two from there,** on the other side, **and one from below,** on his chin. **Rabbi Eliezer says: If** he **removed** the hair on **all** the edges of his beard **in one** action, **he is liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes for all of them. **And one is liable** for marring the edges of his beard **only if he removes** the hair **with a razor. Rabbi Eliezer says: Even** if **he removed** the hair **with tweezers [*malket*] or with a plane [*rehitni*],** he is **liable** to receive lashes.

The *Mishnah* uses different verbs for the two prohibitions involving hair on the head and the beard, and those distinctions are legally meaningful. For the head, the term is *makif* — one who "rounds off" or "encircles" the corner of the head. For the beard, the term is *mashchis* — one who "destroys" the corner of the beard. These terms are not interchangeable. The *pasuk* for the beard reads *lo sashchis es peas zkhancha*, using the language of *hashchasa*, destruction. The Gemara understands *hashchasa* by the beard to require the use of a *ta'ar* — a straight-edge razor that cuts the hair completely flush against the skin. A shaver or clipper that operates with a scissor-like mechanism — where the hairs pass through a guard and are cut by a blade against a shield — is what the Gemara calls *misparayim k'ein ta'ar*: scissors operating like a razor. Such an instrument does not constitute *hashchasa* by the beard and would not incur Biblical *malkos*.

For the *peyos* of the head, by contrast, the operative term in the *pasuk* is *lo takifu*, framed around the act of "rounding" rather than "destroying." There, the scissor-motion standard is more disputed, and the accepted position is that even a *misparayim k'ein ta'ar* raises concern, making its use on the *peyos* problematic in a way that it is not for the beard.

### Sritat Incisions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשּׂוֹרֵט שְׂרִיטָה אַחַת וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְשֶׂרֶט״ – יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ שָׂרַט עַל בֵּיתוֹ שֶׁנָּפַל וְעַל סְפִינָתוֹ שֶׁטָּבְעָה בַּיָּם? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לְנֶפֶשׁ״ – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל הַמֵּת בִּלְבַד. וּמִנַּיִן לַמְשָׂרֵט חָמֵשׁ שְׂרִיטוֹת עַל מֵת אֶחָד שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְשֶׂרֶט״ – לְחַיֵּיב עַל כׇּל שְׂרִיטָה וּשְׂרִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And one who cuts one incision** in a display of mourning **over the dead** is flogged. **The Sages taught** a halakhic midrash on the verse: "And an incision for the soul you shall not place in your flesh" (Leviticus 19:28). Had the verse stated only: **"And an incision,"** one **might** have thought that this prohibition applies to any incision cut in sorrow, **even** if he **cut** an incision in sorrow **over his house that collapsed, or over his ship that sunk at sea.** Therefore, **the verse states: "For the soul,"** from which it is derived that **he is liable** for an incision in mourning **only for the dead alone. And from where** is it derived **that one who cuts five incisions** in mourning **over one dead** person **is liable for each and every** incision? **The verse states: "And an incision" to render** him **liable for each and every incision.**

The *Mishnah* then addresses *sritat* — incisions made on the skin as a sign of grief over the dead. The *pasuk* is *v'saret lanefesh lo sitnu bivsarchem*. Even a single incision made for a deceased person renders one liable to *malkos*. The *Mishnah* then presents a striking case: *soreit srita achas al chamisha meisim* — one who makes a single incision as an expression of mourning for five different deceased relatives simultaneously. Even though it is a single physical act, the *Mishnah* teaches that one receives separate *malkos* for each *meis*. Each dead person for whom the act is performed imparts a distinct identity to the incision, making it as if one incised separately for each. The same logic applies in reverse: *chamisha sritos al meis echad* — five separate incisions all performed for a single deceased person — yields separate *malkos* for each incision, provided there was a separate *hasra'ah* before each one.

### Peyot Locations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל הָרֹאשׁ – שְׁתַּיִם, אַחַת מִכָּאן וְאַחַת מִכָּאן, עַל הַזָּקָן – שְׁתַּיִם מִכָּאן וּשְׁתַּיִם מִכָּאן, וְאַחַת מִלְּמַטָּה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אִם נִיטְּלוּ כּוּלָּן כְּאַחַת – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **For** rounding the edges of his **head,** one is liable to receive **two** sets of lashes, **one from here,** the hair adjacent to one ear, **and one from there,** the hair adjacent to the other ear. **For** marring the edges of his **beard** there are **two** edges **from here,** on one side of his face, **and two from there,** on the other side, **and one from below,** on his chin. **Rabbi Eliezer says: If** he **removed** the hair on **all** the edges of his beard **in one** action, **he is liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes for all of them.

The *Mishnah* enumerates the locations of the *peyos*: on the head, there are two — one on each side, at the area where the sideburn meets the hairline. On the beard, there are five: two on each side — corresponding roughly to the upper cheek and the lower cheek — and one at the bottom at the chin. Rabbi Eliezer, however, holds: *im nitlu kulan ke'achas eino chayav ela achas* — if all five locations on the beard were shaved in a single act, one is liable for only one set of *malkos*. In Rabbi Eliezer's view, the act of shaving the beard is a single unified prohibition, regardless of how many of the five designated spots are affected. According to the *Chachamim*, each of the five locations is a distinct prohibition, and if all five are removed — even in a single sweeping act — one incurs five separate sets of *malkos*, assuming the requisite warnings were given.

Rabbi Eliezer adds a further stringency: *v'eino chayav ad sheyitlenu beta'ar* — for the standard view, one is only liable for the beard if a straight-edge razor is used. But *Rabbi Eliezer omer afilu likto bemelaket o berahitni chayav* — Rabbi Eliezer holds one is liable even for using tweezers or a similar depilatory instrument. In Rabbi Eliezer's view, the prohibition is not limited to an act of cutting with a razor; any act of removal from the designated locations of the beard suffices.

## Korcha Analysis

### Korcha Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לֹא יִקְרְחוּ״, יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ קָרַח אַרְבַּע וְחָמֵשׁ קְרִיחוֹת לֹא יְהֵא חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״קׇרְחָה״ – לְחַיֵּיב עַל כׇּל קׇרְחָה וְקׇרְחָה. ״בְּרֹאשָׁם״ מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״לֹא תִתְגֹּדְדוּ וְלֹא תָשִׂימוּ קׇרְחָה בֵּין עֵינֵיכֶם לָמֵת״, יָכוֹל לֹא יְהֵא חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל בֵּין הָעֵינַיִם בִּלְבַד, מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת כָּל הָרֹאשׁ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בְּרֹאשָׁם״ – לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל הָרֹאשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught:** From the verse concerning priests: **"They shall not create** a bald spot upon their heads" (Leviticus 21:5), one **might** have thought that **even** if **he created four or five bald spots** in one action, **he will be liable to** receive **only one** set of lashes. Therefore, **the verse states: "Bald spot,"** in the singular, **to** render one **liable** to receive lashes **for each and every bald** spot. The verse continues: **"Upon their heads"; what** is the meaning when **the verse states** that term? **Since it is stated: "You shall neither lacerate yourselves, nor place a bald spot between your eyes for the dead"** (Deuteronomy 14:1), one **might** have thought **that one would be liable only for** placing a bald spot **between the eyes. From where** is it derived **that** the prohibition **includes** placing a bald spot on **the entire head? The verse states: "Upon their heads," to include the entire head.**

The Gemara begins its analysis with the prohibition of *korcha* — making a bald spot on the head for the dead. A *Baraita* raises the question: perhaps even if one makes four or five bald spots, he is only liable once, on the grounds that it is all part of a single act? The *Baraita* rejects this, deriving from the repeated use of the word *korcha* — *lo yikrechu korcha* by the *Kohanim* and *lo sasimu korcha* by *Yisrael* — that one is *chayav al kol korcha v'korcha*, liable separately for each and every bald spot made.

### Biblical Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא בַּכֹּהֲנִים, שֶׁרִיבָּה בָּהֶן הַכָּתוּב מִצְוֹת יְתֵירוֹת, יִשְׂרָאֵל מִנַּיִן?
>
> **Translation:** **And I have** derived this prohibition **only with regard to priests,** for **whom the Torah established numerous additional mitzvot,** since the verse "They shall not create a bald spot" is written in the context of the *halakhot* of priests. **From where** do I derive that **a non-priest** is also liable for violating that prohibition?

The *Baraita* then asks about the phrase *berosham* in the *pasuk* for *Kohanim* — why does it say "on their heads" when the broader principle presumably applies? Because by *Yisrael* the *pasuk* specifies *bein eineichem* — between your eyes — one might think only a bald spot made precisely between the eyes generates liability. The *Baraita* therefore uses *berosham* to teach that the prohibition extends to the entire head, not only to the space between the eyes. This raises an asymmetry: perhaps the fuller prohibition — applying to the whole head — belongs to *Kohanim* alone, given that they carry additional obligations? One might then confine the *Yisrael* prohibition to *bein einayim* only.

### Unifying Verses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״קׇרְחָה״ וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״קׇרְחָה״, מָה לְהַלָּן חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל קׇרְחָה וְקׇרְחָה, וְחַיָּיב עַל הָרֹאשׁ כְּבֵין הָעֵינַיִם – אַף כָּאן חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל קׇרְחָה וְקׇרְחָה, וְחַיָּיב עַל הָרֹאשׁ כְּבֵין הָעֵינַיִם. וּמָה לְהַלָּן עַל מֵת – אַף כָּאן עַל מֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **"Bald spot" is stated here** (Deuteronomy 14:1) with regard to non-priests, **and "bald spot" is stated there,** with regard to priests; **just as there,** with regard to priests, one is **liable for each and every bald spot** that he creates, **and is liable for** a bald spot created anywhere on his **head as** he is for a bald spot created **between the eyes, so too here,** with regard to a non-priest, one is **liable for each and every bald spot** that he creates, **and is liable** for a bald spot created anywhere on his **head as** he is for a bald spot created **between the eyes. And just as there,** with regard to non-priests, the prohibition is stated with regard to creating a bald spot as an expression of mourning **over the dead; so too here,** with regard to priests, the prohibition is stated with regard to creating a bald spot as an expression of mourning **over the dead.**

To resolve this, the Gemara employs a *gezeira shava*: the word *korcha* appears both in the *pasuk* addressing *Kohanim* (*lo yikrechu korcha*) and in the *pasuk* addressing *Yisrael* (*lo sasimu korcha*). Through this *korcha-korcha* linkage, everything derived from one verse applies to the other: just as by *Kohanim* one is liable for each individual bald spot and the prohibition covers the entire head, so too by *Yisrael* — *chayav al kol korcha v'korcha v'chayav al harosh k'vein einayim*. And critically, the *gezeira shava* also imports the limitation found by *Yisrael*: just as by *Yisrael* the prohibition is *al hameis* — for the dead — so too by *Kohanim* the prohibition is *al hameis*. The *pasuk* for *Kohanim* does not explicitly mention mourning for a deceased person, but via the *gezeira shava* it is unified with the *Yisrael* prohibition under the single framework of mourning. The phrase *bein eineichem* in the *Yisrael* verse, meanwhile, is not taken as a strict limitation; the *berosham* of the *Kohanim* verse — the whole head — overrides it, and *bein eineichem* is understood as a non-exclusive reference rather than a definitional constraint.

## Multiple Lashes Rule

### Hasra'ah Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי אַרְבַּע וְחָמֵשׁ קְרִיחוֹת הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה, וּבְחָמֵשׁ הַתְרָאוֹת – פְּשִׁיטָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to **these four or five bald spots** created over one dead person, for which one is liable to receive lashes for each and every spot, **what are the circumstances? If we say** that he created those bald spots **one after the other and with five** separate **forewarnings,** one preceding each action, it is **obvious** that he is liable for each and every bald spot; in that case he is one who violates the same prohibition several times and is punished for each violation for which he was forewarned.

Having established that one is liable separately for each bald spot, the Gemara now asks: what exactly is the scenario in which making four or five bald spots results in four or five sets of *malkos*? The question is not trivial, because the requirements for *malkos* demand not only a prohibited act but a valid *hasra'ah* — a warning given immediately before the act — and each separate set of *malkos* requires its own prior warning.

### Nazirite Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בַּחֲדָא הַתְרָאָה, מִי מִחַיַּיב? וְהָתְנַן: נָזִיר שֶׁהָיָה שׁוֹתֶה יַיִן כׇּל הַיּוֹם – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״אַל תִּשְׁתֶּה״, ״אַל תִּשְׁתֶּה״, וְהוּא שׁוֹתֶה – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּסָךְ חֲמֵשׁ אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו נָשָׁא וְאוֹתְבִינְהוּ בְּבַת אַחַת, דְּהָוְיָא לֵיהּ, הַתְרָאָה לְכׇל חֲדָא וַחֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** say that he created five bald spots **with one forewarning; is one liable** in that case? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (21a): **A nazirite who was drinking wine all day is liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes; if onlookers **said to him: Do not drink, do not drink,** forewarning him several times, **and he drinks** after each forewarning, he is **liable** to receive lashes **for each and every** drink. The Gemara answers: **No,** this *halakha* **is necessary only** with regard to one **who,** after one forewarning, **smeared his five fingers** in a **depilatory** agent **[*nasha*] and** then **placed** his fingers **simultaneously** on his hair, thereby creating five bald spots, **as** in that case, **it is** tantamount to **a forewarning for each and every** bald spot that he created. The novel element is that each bald spot is an independent transgression.

The Gemara illustrates the principle by reference to the *Mishnah* concerning a *Nazir*: *Nazir shehayah shoseh yayin kol hayom eino chayav ela achas* — a *Nazir* who drinks wine the entire day receives only one set of *malkos*. Even if the drinking is not continuous — even if he pauses, takes a walk, resumes later — as long as there is only a single *hasra'ah*, only one set of *malkos* applies. But if the witnesses said to him "do not drink" and he drank, and then they said again "do not drink" and he drank again, *chayav al kol achas v'achas* — he is liable for each separately. The *hasra'ah* must immediately precede the specific act; it does not carry forward to subsequent acts.

This principle creates a problem for the case of multiple bald spots. If the person was warned once and then proceeded to make five bald spots — whether in rapid succession or in a single sweeping gesture — the single *hasra'ah* would seem to apply only to the first act. Why should a special *pasuk* of *korcha v'korcha* be needed to establish multiple liability if the *hasra'ah* requirement would in any case limit liability to one?

### Five Fingers Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּסָךְ חֲמֵשׁ אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו נָשָׁא וְאוֹתְבִינְהוּ בְּבַת אַחַת, דְּהָוְיָא לֵיהּ, הַתְרָאָה לְכׇל חֲדָא וַחֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** this *halakha* **is necessary only** with regard to one **who,** after one forewarning, **smeared his five fingers** in a **depilatory** agent **[*nasha*] and** then **placed** his fingers **simultaneously** on his hair, thereby creating five bald spots, **as** in that case, **it is** tantamount to **a forewarning for each and every** bald spot that he created.

Rav Huna resolves this: *d'shach chamish etzba'otav nasha* — the case is where one dipped all five fingers into a depilatory substance and then, in a single simultaneous act, placed all five fingers on five different spots on the head. In this scenario, *d'havei leh hasra'ah l'chol chada v'chada* — the single *hasra'ah* functions as the warning for all five acts, because all five acts were performed simultaneously in one motion. The *hasra'ah* is not "used up" by the first act and then unavailable for the subsequent ones; since all five happened at once, the single warning stands immediately before each one of them simultaneously. The *chiddush* of the verse *korcha v'korcha* is precisely that this single simultaneous act is conceptualized as five distinct violations — one for each bald spot created — and therefore yields five sets of *malkos*.

This is a significant illustration of how *hasra'ah* interacts with complex acts. A single continuous act of drinking, even if it spans a full day, is one act and yields one set of *malkos*. Multiple separate acts, even clearly distinct ones, require separate *hasra'os* to generate separate *malkos*. But a single simultaneous act that by its nature touches five prohibited locations in one moment is treated as five acts for purposes of *malkos*, with the single *hasra'ah* covering all five simultaneously.

## Korcha Shiur

### Size Opinions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה שִׁיעוּר קׇרְחָה? רַב הוּנָא אוֹמֵר: כְּדֵי שֶׁיֵּרָאֶה מֵרֹאשׁוֹ. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: כִּגְרִיס. כְּתַנָּאֵי: כַּמָּה שִׁיעוּר קׇרְחָה – כִּגְרִיס, אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: כְּדֵי שֶׁיֵּרָאֶה מֵרֹאשׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **And how much is the measure of a bald spot** for which one is liable? **Rav Huna says:** It must be of **sufficient** size so **that** bald skin **will be visible on his head. Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon:** It is the size of **a Cilician bean [*kigeris*].** The Gemara comments: This amoraic dispute is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*: And how much is the measure of a bald spot?** It is the size of **a Cilician bean. *Aḥerim* say:** It must be of **sufficient** size so **that** bald skin **will be visible on his head.**

The Gemara turns to the question of minimum size: *v'kamah shiur korcha?* — what is the smallest bald spot for which one is liable? Rav Huna says *kedei sheyeira merosho* — enough to be visible from the head, that is, an observable patch of baldness. Rabbi Yochanan says in the name of Rabbi Elazar son of Rabbi Shimon: *k'gris* — the size of a *gris*, a type of bean. A *Baraita* records these as a *Tannaitic* dispute. Rabbi Yehuda bar Chaviva then reports that in fact three *Tannaim* debate the matter: one says *k'gris*, one says *kedei sheyeira merosho*, and one says *k'shtei searot* — just two hairs. Some versions substitute *k'adasha* — the size of a lentil — for the two-hairs opinion, yielding a three-way dispute between a bean, a lentil, and visible-from-the-head.

### Tzara'at Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה בַּר חֲבִיבָא: פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ תְּלָתָא תַּנָּאֵי, חַד אוֹמֵר: כִּגְרִיס, וְחַד אוֹמֵר: כְּדֵי שֶׁיֵּרָאֶה מֵרֹאשׁוֹ, וְחַד אוֹמֵר: כִּשְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת. וְאִיכָּא דְּמַפֵּיק שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת וּמְעַיֵּיל בְּכַעֲדָשָׁה. וְסִימָנָךְ: בַּהֶרֶת – כִּגְרִיס, וּמִחְיָה – בְּכַעֲדָשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda bar Ḥaviva said** that there are **three *tanna'im*** who **disagree concerning** this matter. **One says:** A spot the size of **a Cilician bean; and one says:** A spot of **sufficient** size so **that** bald skin **will be visible on his head; and one says:** A spot that is the measure **of two hairs. And there is one who removed** the opinion that it is the measure of **two hairs, and inserted** an opinion that it is the size **of a lentil. And your mnemonic** to remember the different opinions is from a mishna with regard to the *halakhot* of leprosy (*Nega'im* 6:5): The measure of impurity for **a snow-white leprous mark [*baheret*]** is **a Cilician bean, and** the measure of impurity for the **raw** flesh that grows within the *baheret* is **a lentil.**

A mnemonic device is offered to help remember the relevant *shiurim*: *v'simancha b'bahares k'gris u'michyah k'adasha* — recall the laws of *tzara'at*. The white patch of *tzara'at* must be at least the size of a *gris* to be a *nega*, and the area of *michyah* — the patch of healed skin within a *nega* that is halakhically significant — must be the size of an *adasha*, a lentil. Since these are familiar units in the context of bodily marks, they serve as natural reference points for the comparable *shiurim* of *korcha*.

### Hair Shiur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: הַנּוֹטֵל מְלֹא פִּי הַזּוּג בַּשַּׁבָּת – חַיָּיב. וְכַמָּה מְלֹא פִּי הַזּוּג? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: שְׁתַּיִם. וְהָתַנְיָא: לַקׇּרְחָה שְׁתַּיִם! אֵימָא: וְכֵן לַקׇּרְחָה שְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** In a related *halakha*, the Sages **taught: One who removes a scissorsful [*melo pi hazug*]** of hair from his head **on Shabbat is liable. And how much is a scissorsful? Rav Yehuda says: Two** hairs. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **For a bald spot** the measure is **two** hairs, from which it may be inferred that the measure with regard to labor on Shabbat is different than for a bald spot. The Gemara answers: Emend the *baraita* and **say: And likewise with regard to a bald spot** the measure is **two** hairs, indicating that the measure for Shabbat and the measure for a bald spot are the same.

The Gemara then turns to the *shiur* for hair removal as it applies to *shabbat*, which has bearing on *korcha* as well. A *Baraita* teaches: *hanoteil kimlo pi hazug b'shabbat chayav* — one who removes as much hair as fills the mouth of scissors on *shabbat* is liable. Rav Yehuda explains that the measure is two hairs. A variant *Baraita* appears to contrast the *shabbat* standard from the *korcha* standard by stating them separately — *lekorcha shtayim* — but this is resolved by reading both as expressing the same measurement: the fill of the mouth of scissors equals two hairs, and two hairs is likewise the standard for *korcha*. Rabbi Eliezer disagrees: *achas* — even one hair is sufficient to make one liable.

### White Hair Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: הַנּוֹטֵל מְלֹא פִּי הַזּוּג בַּשַּׁבָּת חַיָּיב, וְכַמָּה מְלֹא פִּי הַזּוּג – שְׁתַּיִם. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אַחַת. וּמוֹדִים חֲכָמִים לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בִּמְלַקֵּט לְבָנוֹת מִתּוֹךְ שְׁחוֹרוֹת, אֲפִילּוּ אַחַת – שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב. וְדָבָר זֶה אֲפִילּוּ בְּחוֹל אָסוּר, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא יִלְבַּשׁ גֶּבֶר שִׂמְלַת אִשָּׁה״.
>
> **Translation:** **That is also taught** in a *baraita*: **One who removes a scissorsful** of hair **on Shabbat is liable. And how much is a scissorsful?** It is **two** hairs. **Rabbi Eliezer says: One** hair. **And the Rabbis concede to Rabbi Eliezer in** the case of **one who removes white** hairs **from black** ones, **even** if he removes **one** hair, **that he is liable.** Since his intent was to remove that particular hair, its removal constitutes a complete action. The Gemara adds: **And** for a man, **that matter is prohibited even during the week, due to** the fact **that it is stated: "Neither shall a man don a woman's garment"** (Deuteronomy 22:5). Removal of white hairs for the purposes of beautification is characteristic of women, and it is prohibited for a man to perform those actions.

An important exception is noted: *umodim chachamim l'Rabbi Eliezer b'melaket levanot mitoch shachorot afilu achas shehu chayav* — the *Chachamim* concede to Rabbi Eliezer that if someone plucks a white hair from among black hairs, even a single hair renders one liable. The reason is that the act is qualitatively significant: by removing that single white hair, the person has made his entire head of hair appear uniformly black. The significance is not quantitative but functional — the appearance of the person is meaningfully altered by even one hair. In the ordinary case of *korcha*, one hair makes no perceptible difference; two hairs at least constitute something that can be identified as a minimal act. But here, the single white hair does the entire work of changing the appearance, and therefore even one hair crosses the threshold.

### Grooming Lo Yilbash

The Gemara adds that this prohibition against plucking white hairs from among black ones is not limited to *shabbat* but applies even on weekdays: *vedavar zeh afilu b'chol assur mishum shene'emar lo yilbash gever simlat isha* — because a man may not wear the garment of a woman. The verse *lo yilbash gever simlat isha* is understood to extend beyond literal cross-dressing to encompass any grooming practice that is characteristically feminine — that is, practices by which a woman attends to her appearance. A man who plucks white hairs to make his hair appear uniformly black is engaging in the kind of appearance-management that is associated with women's grooming, and this falls within the prohibition of *simlat isha*. The definition here is notably broad: the end result is not a feminine appearance as such — the man simply has black hair — but the *practice* is one associated with feminine self-presentation, and that is sufficient. The Gemara elsewhere discusses the case of a man arranging his appearance in a mirror, which *Rishonim* note is highly contextually dependent; what constitutes a characteristically feminine practice is culturally variable, and many *Rishonim* limit the application of this prohibition accordingly to practices that remain genuinely gender-specific in the relevant society.
