# Makkot 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lav Nitak L'Asei Intro

### Definition & Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁקְּדָמוֹ עֲשֵׂה – לוֹקִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to **any prohibition that** has **a positive mitzva** that **preceded it,** everyone agrees that **one is flogged for its** violation, as it is not classified as a prohibition that entails fulfillment of a positive mitzva.

The daf opens with a principle that is conceptually richer and less technically complicated than much of what precedes it in the third *perek*: the *lav she'nitak l'asei*, a negative prohibition that is connected to a positive commandment in such a way that the *asei* functions as a corrective or remedy, thereby exempting the transgressor from lashes. The governing assumption is that when an *asei* is positioned in the Torah as the means of fixing a violation of a *lav*, the person who performs that remedial act is not subject to *malkus*. One frequently cited example is the prohibition against stealing coupled with the verse *v'heshiv es ha'gezeilah* — return the stolen object. Though in that case the exemption from lashes rests also on a broader principle that monetary transgressions do not incur lashes, the conceptual framework is similar.

### Mother Bird Case

The cleaner, more paradigmatic case is the prohibition of taking the mother bird: *lo tikach ha'eim al habanim* — do not take the mother with her young. The Torah then instructs *shalei'ach t'shaleich es ha'eim* — send the mother bird away. This is the classic instance of *lav she'nitak l'asei*, and the structure is significant: the *asei* appears after the *lav* and can be read as the corrective response to a transgression already committed. Note that the act of sending away the mother bird is not exclusively remedial — one could perform it even without having transgressed, simply by sending the mother away before taking the young. Read on its own terms, the verse might simply mean: rather than taking the mother with her chicks, send her away. But because the *asei* follows the *lav* in the text, the Talmud reads it also as: if you did transgress, here is what you must do to fix it. The same physical act serves both purposes. And precisely because it is read as a corrective mechanism, the *lav* is understood as subject to repair, and the transgressor is therefore not given lashes.

### Asei Precedes Lav

The statement transmitted by Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan introduces a critical qualification: *kol lo ta'aseh she'kidmo asei, lokin alav* — any negative commandment that was preceded by a positive commandment, one receives lashes for its violation. This is a departure from what one might assume, and its logic runs as follows. When the *asei* comes after the *lav*, it is naturally read as a repair mechanism — "don't do this, but if you did, here is how to fix it." When, however, the *asei* precedes the *lav*, the *asei* cannot serve as a remedy for the transgression, because it appeared in the Torah before the transgression was even defined. In such cases, the *asei* is simply an independent positive obligation, not a corrective one. Rabbi Yochanan's example by analogy would be *Shabbos*: the verse *bayom hashvi'i tishbos* commands rest on the seventh day and appears before the negative prohibition against *melacha*. No one reads that *asei* as a remedy for violating *Shabbos* — it is plainly a reinforcing obligation, not a fix. And therefore, if the *asei* came first, the *lav* is not undermined by it, and one who transgresses receives lashes.

## Tamei Entry Case

### Tamei Precedent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אָמַרְתָּ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: לָא. אָמַר רַבָּה: הָאֱלֹהִים! אַמְרַהּ, וּכְתִיבָא וּתְנֵינָא: כְּתִיבָא – "וִישַׁלְּחוּ מִן הַמַּחֲנֶה [וְגוֹ׳]" "וְלֹא יְטַמְּאוּ אֶת מַחֲנֵיהֶם". תְּנֵינָא – הַבָּא לַמִּקְדָּשׁ טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **They said to** Rabbi Yoḥanan: **Did you say** this *halakha*? Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to them: No. Rabba** bar bar Ḥana **said** in the form of an oath: **By God, he said it, and** this *halakha* **is written** in the Torah **and we learn** it in the mishna. **It is written: "And they shall send from the camp** any man who is leprous, and any *zav*…**and they shall not render their camp impure"** (Numbers 5:2–4). There is a prohibition against rendering the camp impure, and there is a positive mitzva to send them out of the camp. Since the positive mitzva precedes and is independent of the prohibition, one is flogged for the violation of that prohibition, as **we learn** in the mishna among those liable to receive lashes: **One who entered the Temple** while ritually **impure.**

When Rabbah bar bar Chana was asked whether he had actually transmitted this ruling in Rabbi Yochanan's name, he initially disavowed it. Rava responded forcefully — *Ha-elohim amarta!* — insisting that he had indeed said it, and moreover that the position is well-grounded in both a *pasuk* and a *mishnah* working in concert.

### Gemara Support

The scriptural support: the Torah writes *veshilchu min hamachaneh kol tzaru'a vechol zav vechol tamei lanafesh* — send out from the camp all who are impure. That is the positive commandment, and it appears first. Only afterward does the Torah state *velo yetame'u es machanehem* — they shall not defile the camp. That is the negative prohibition. The *asei* of leaving the camp precedes the *lav* of defiling it.

Now one might have thought: if a *tamei* person entered the *Beis Hamikdash*, perhaps he could remedy the transgression by simply leaving. After all, there is an *asei* connected here — the obligation to depart from the camp when impure. But the *mishnah* rules explicitly that one who enters the *Beis Hamikdash* in a state of *tumah* receives lashes. This demonstrates that the positive commandment to leave does not serve as a corrective that exempts from lashes — and the reason, consistent with Rabbi Yochanan's principle, is that the *asei* appeared before the *lav*. It is an additional independent obligation, not a mechanism of repair. The *pasuk* and the *mishnah* together, then, confirm Rabbi Yochanan's position rather than undermining it.

### Retraction Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי טַעְמָא קָא הָדַר בֵּיהּ? מִשּׁוּם דְּקַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ אוֹנֶס. דְּתַנְיָא: אוֹנֵס שֶׁגֵּירַשׁ, אִם יִשְׂרָאֵל הוּא – מַחְזִיר וְאֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה. אִם כֹּהֵן הוּא – לוֹקֶה וְאֵינוֹ מַחְזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Rather, what is the reason he retracted** his statement and claimed he did not say it? The Gemara answers: It is **due to** the fact **that** the *halakha* concerning one who **rapes** a virgin young woman, who is required by Torah law to marry her and for whom it is prohibited to divorce her as long as he lives, **is difficult for him, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a rapist who divorced** the woman he raped, **if he is a non-priest, he remarries** her, **and he is not flogged** for violating the prohibition: "He may not send her away all his days" (Deuteronomy 22:29). **If he is a priest,** he is **flogged** for violating the prohibition, **and he does not remarry** her because it is prohibited for him to marry a divorcée.

If the position is so well-grounded, why did Rabbah bar bar Chana retract it in the first place? The Gemara explains: *mishum dekashya lei ones* — because of a difficulty posed by the case of rape. The Torah instructs that the man who has violated a woman must take her as his wife — *lo tihyeh le'ishah* — and may never divorce her: *lo yuchal leshalcha kol yamav*. Here the *asei* of taking her as a wife comes before the *lav* of divorcing her. And yet, as we will see, the *halacha* treats remarriage as a remedy that exempts him from lashes if he did divorce her. That apparent counterexample is what led Rabbah bar bar Chana to withdraw. The Gemara will work to resolve this tension.

## Rape & Divorce Case

### Rape Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם יִשְׂרָאֵל הוּא – מַחְזִיר וְאֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה, אַמַּאי? לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁקְּדָמוֹ עֲשֵׂה הוּא, וְלִילְקֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **If he is a non-priest, he remarries** her, **and he is not flogged. Why? It is a prohibition that** has **a positive mitzva** which **preceded it,** as the positive mitzva: "And she shall be his wife" (Deuteronomy 22:29), precedes the prohibition "He may not send her away." **But let him be flogged,** since he violated the prohibition by divorcing her. Apparently, one is not flogged even if the positive mitzva precedes the prohibition.

A *beraisa* teaches the details of this case. If a man who raped a woman subsequently divorces her, what is his status with respect to lashes? The *asei* of *lo tihyeh le'ishah* — she shall be his wife — comes first in the verse, before the *lav* of *lo yuchal leshalcha*. According to Rabbi Yochanan's principle, one would expect this to be a case where the preceding *asei* does not remedy the transgression, and lashes should follow.

### Remarriage Exemption

Yet the *beraisa* rules: *im Yisrael hu, machazir ve'eino lokeh* — if he is a non-*kohen*, he remarries her and is thereby exempt from lashes, because the remarriage constitutes a fix. If, however, he is a *kohen*, he cannot remarry her — she is now a *gerushah* and forbidden to him — and so he receives lashes without any possibility of remedy. The case of the *yisrael* thus appears to contradict Rabbi Yochanan directly: here the *asei* precedes the *lav* and yet functions as a corrective. This is the *kushya* that drove Rabbah bar bar Chana to retract.

The Gemara acknowledges the difficulty and seeks to explain how, within Rabbi Yochanan's framework, the case of the rapist can be read as an exception — or more precisely, why the *asei* of marrying her can here be understood as a fix even though it preceded the *lav*.

### Ulla Superfluous Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: לֹא יֵאָמֵר "לוֹ תִּהְיֶה לְאִשָּׁה", בְּאוֹנֵס, וְלִיגְמַר מִמּוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע: וּמָה מוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע שֶׁלֹּא עָשָׂה מַעֲשֶׂה – אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא "וְלוֹ תִהְיֶה לְאִשָּׁה", אוֹנֵס לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla says** in response: The positive mitzva in the case of a rapist is not a mitzva that precedes the prohibition. Rather, it follows the prohibition and rectifies it, as it is referring to a case of one who married his victim and then divorced her. **Let** the verse **not state: "And she shall be his wife," in** the case of **a rapist, and let us derive** it by means of an *a fortiori* inference **from** the case of **one who defames** his bride, claiming that she was not a virgin when he consummated the marriage: **If** in the case of **a defamer, who did not perform an action** but sinned through speech, **the Merciful One states: "And she shall be his wife"** (Deuteronomy 22:19), then in the case of **a rapist,** who performed an action, **is it not all the more so** that he is obligated to take her as a wife?
>
> **Lama ne'emar? Im eino inyan lefanav tenehu inyan le'acharav, she'im geirash yachzir.**
>
> **Translation:** Ulla continues: **Why,** then, **is** the mitzva **stated** in the case of the rapist? **If it is not** relevant to the **matter of before** the rapist marries the victim, **apply it** to the **matter of after** the rapist marries the victim, teaching **that if he divorced** her **he must remarry** her. Although in the verse it appears prior to the prohibition "He may not send her away," it actually serves to rectify that prohibition.

Ulla proposes a resolution rooted in the principle of *im eino inyan* — redirecting a superfluous verse to teach something else. His argument begins: the Torah did not need to state the obligation of marriage in the case of the *oneis*, the rapist. Why not? Because we could have derived it through a *kal vachomer* from the case of the *motzi shem ra*, the man who falsely reports that his wife was not a virgin at marriage. In that case too, the Torah states *lo tihyeh le'ishah lo yuchal l'shalchah kol yamav* — she shall remain his wife and he may never divorce her. The *motzi shem ra* did not commit any physical act against the woman; his offense was entirely verbal. And yet the Torah imposed upon him the obligation to remain married to her. If even the slanderer — who committed no physical act — has this *mitzvah*, then certainly the rapist, who committed a far more serious physical offense, should have it as well. Thus the verse's statement of the marriage obligation in the rape case is superfluous. Since it is superfluous, the principle of *im eino inyan l'fanav, taneihu inyan l'acharyav* applies: redirect it to teach something for after the fact — namely, that if he divorced her, he must remarry her, and that act of remarriage functions as a repair of the transgression.

## Rape vs. False Report

### Challenge to Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי, אוֹנֵס מִמּוֹצִיא שֵׁם רָע לָא גָּמַר, דְּאִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְמוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע, שֶׁכֵּן לוֹקֶה וּמְשַׁלֵּם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But still,** this affords no proof, as **one cannot derive** the case of **a rapist from** the case of **a defamer, as** the *a fortiori* inference **can be refuted: What** is notable **about** the case of **a defamer,** which is more stringent than the case of a rapist? It is notable **in that he is flogged and** is liable to **pay** retribution, which is contrary to the principle that in general, two punishments are not administered for one transgression.

The Gemara objects to Ulla's *kal vachomer*. One cannot derive the marriage obligation of the rapist from that of the *motzi shem ra* so easily, because the *motzi shem ra* case carries exceptional punishments: the Torah imposes both lashes *and* a monetary fine — *v'yisru oso v'anshu oso mei'ah kesef* — a combination normally not applied in a single case. The severity of the *motzi shem ra's* punishment may reflect the unique heinousness of his act, making it an atypical case from which one cannot extrapolate to others. So the *kal vachomer* fails: perhaps the obligation to remain married is specific to the *motzi shem ra* on account of the exceptional punishment, and cannot be extended to the *oneis*.

### Redundancy Resolved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: לֹא יֵאָמֵר "לוֹ תִהְיֶה לְאִשָּׁה" בְּמוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע, וְלִיגְמַר מֵאוֹנֵס: וּמָה אוֹנֵס שֶׁאֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה וּמְשַׁלֵּם, אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא "וְלוֹ תִהְיֶה לְאִשָּׁה", מוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? וְלָמָּה נֶאֱמַר? אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לַמּוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע – תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לָאוֹנֵס, אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְפָנָיו – תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לְאַחֲרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Rather, let** the verse **not state: "And she shall be his wife," in** the case of the **defamer, and let us derive** it by means of an *a fortiori* inference **from** the case of **a rapist: And if** in the case of **a rapist, who is not** both **flogged and** liable to **pay** restitution, but is liable to receive only one punishment, **the Merciful One states: "And she shall be his wife,"** in the case of **a defamer,** who is flogged and liable to pay retribution, **is it not all the more so** that he is obligated to take her as a wife? **And why,** then, **is it stated** in the case of the defamer? Rather, **if** the mitzva **is not** relevant to the **matter of** the **defamer, apply it** to the **matter of** the **rapist, and if** the mitzva **is not** relevant to the **matter of before** the rapist marries the victim, **apply it** to the **matter of after** the rapist marries the victim, teaching that if he divorced her he must remarry her. Although in the verse it appears prior to the prohibition "He may not send her away," it actually serves to rectify that prohibition.

The Gemara then reverses the direction: rather than deriving the *oneis* from the *motzi shem ra*, why not learn the *motzi shem ra* from the *oneis*? One would reason: the *oneis* does not incur the doubled punishment of lashes and a fine — he is not an exceptional case in that sense. And yet the Torah requires him to remain married to the woman he violated. A fortiori, the *motzi shem ra*, whose punishment is far heavier, should certainly be required to remain married. If so, the statement of the marriage obligation in the *motzi shem ra* passage is the superfluous one. And being superfluous, we apply *im eino inyan l'fanav, taneihu inyan l'acharyav*: it is not needed to establish the obligation at the outset in the *motzi shem ra* case — that follows from the *oneis* — but rather to teach that if he transgresses and divorces her, he must remarry her as a corrective.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: לֹא יֵאָמֵר "לוֹ תִהְיֶה לְאִשָּׁה" בְּמוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע, שֶׁהֲרֵי אִשְׁתּוֹ הִיא. לָמָּה נֶאֱמַר? אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לַמּוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע – תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לָאוֹנֵס, וְאִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְפָנָיו – תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לְאַחֲרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Rather, let** the verse **not state: "And she shall be his wife," in** the case of the **defamer** because the expression is redundant, **as she is** already **his wife.** It would have been sufficient for the verse to state that it is prohibited for him to send her away. **Why,** then, **is it stated** in the case of the defamer? Rather, **if** the mitzva **is not** relevant to the **matter of** the **defamer, apply it** to the **matter of** the **rapist, and if** the mitzva **is not** relevant to the **matter of before** the rapist marries the victim, **apply it** to the **matter of after** the rapist marries the victim, teaching that if he divorced her he must remarry her. Although the positive mitzva appears in the verse prior to the prohibition "He may not send her away," it actually serves to rectify that prohibition.

But here too the Gemara objects: you still cannot learn one case from the other without challenge, because each case possesses a characteristic that the other lacks. The *oneis* is weightier in that he committed an actual physical act — *asah ma'aseh* — against the woman. The *motzi shem ra* is weightier in that his punishment is uniquely severe. Each case has a dimension of gravity absent from the other, so neither can be simply derived from the other by *kal vachomer* or analogy, and therefore each requires an independent textual source. Neither verse is in fact superfluous, and Ulla's argument collapses.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בִּנְגִיעָה – מִי אִיכָּא נְטִילַת נְשָׁמָה? אֶלָּא בַּאֲכִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The verse **juxtaposes** the matter of a ritually impure person eating **sacrificial** food **to** the matter of entering **the Temple** while ritually impure. **Just as** entering **the Temple** is **a matter** that entails a punishment **that involves the taking of a life,** i.e., *karet*, **so too, every matter** in that verse entails a punishment **that involves the taking of a life.** The Gemara explains: **And if** the prohibition is **with regard to touching** sacrificial food, **is there** a punishment that entails **the taking of a life? Rather,** the prohibition is **with regard to eating.**

At this point the Gemara observes what seems in retrospect almost obvious, and notes wryly that it took some time to arrive at: in the case of the *motzi shem ra*, the verse need not have stated *lo tihyeh le'ishah* — she shall be his wife — because they are already married. The entire premise of that case is an existing marriage in which the husband falsely accuses his wife of pre-marital infidelity. There is therefore no need to instruct him to take her as a wife; she already is. All the Torah needed to say was that he may not divorce her. The statement that she shall be his wife is therefore unambiguously superfluous in the *motzi shem ra* case — not because of any *kal vachomer*, but simply because there is no marriage to establish.

This being so, the redundant phrase in the *motzi shem ra* passage teaches: *im eino inyan l'fanav, taneihu inyan l'acharav* — since it is not needed to establish a marriage obligation at the outset, it is redirected to teach that even after he transgresses and divorces her, remarrying her constitutes a remedy and exempts him from lashes.

But this still leaves the original problem unresolved. We have now established that in the *motzi shem ra* case the *asei* of remarrying serves as a corrective after the fact. But by the *oneis*, the *asei* of taking her as a wife appears before the *lav* of not divorcing. According to Rabbi Yochanan, a preceding *asei* is not a fix. How then do we know that in the *oneis* case, remarriage exempts him from lashes?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא, וְאִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְפָנָיו דְּמוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע – תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לְאַחֲרָיו דִּידֵיהּ, דְּלָא לָקֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions that derivation: **And say** instead: **And if** the mitzva **is not** relevant to the **matter of** the **defamer before** he marries his bride, as they are married, **apply it** to the **matter of** the defamer **himself after** they are married, teaching that if he divorced her and violated the prohibition against divorcing her, he is obligated to remarry her, and it teaches **that he is not flogged,** as the positive mitzva rectifies the prohibition. But in the case of a rapist, the mitzva is to marry her after the rape but there is no mitzva to marry her after he divorces her, and nevertheless, he is not flogged.

The Gemara attempts to say: learn the *oneis* from the *motzi shem ra* — since we see that in the *motzi shem ra* case the *asei* of marrying functions as a corrective, apply the same logic to the *oneis*. But the Gemara immediately concedes that this learning cannot stand, since we have already established that neither case can be derived from the other: *mah l'motzi shem ra sheken lo asah ma'aseh* — the *motzi shem ra* committed no physical act, and perhaps the Torah grants him a corrective precisely because his offense was less severe, whereas the *oneis*, having committed an act of physical violation, may not be granted that lenience.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין הָכִי נָמֵי, וְאָתֵי אוֹנֵס וְגָמַר מִינֵּיהּ. בְּמַאי גָּמַר מִינֵּיהּ? אִי בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר אִי בְּ״מָה מָצִינוּ״ – אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ כִּדְפָרְכִינַן: מָה לְמוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע, שֶׁכֵּן לֹא עָשָׂה מַעֲשֶׂה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Yes, it is indeed so;** the *halakha* is derived with regard to the defamer himself, teaching that if he divorces his bride he is obligated to remarry her. **And** the case of the **rapist comes and is derived from** the case of the defamer, and the same *halakha* applies there as well. The Gemara asks: **With what** derivation is the case of a rapist **derived from** the case of a defamer? **If** it is **by means of an *a fortiori*** inference or **if** it is **by means of** the inductive hermeneutical principle: **What do we find,** those derivations **can be refuted, as we refuted** the derivations earlier in the Gemara: **What** is notable **about** the case of **a defamer?** It is notable **in that he did not perform an action.**

The Gemara acknowledges: *ein hachi nami* — indeed, the *motzi shem ra* verse teaches its own case, that if he divorces her he can remarry to fix it, but this tells us nothing about the *oneis*. The *oneis* case, where the *asei* precedes the *lav*, remains problematic.

## Kol Yamav Solution

### Kol Yamav Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: כׇּל יָמָיו בַּעֲמוֹד וְהַחְזֵר. וְכֵן כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל יָמָיו בַּעֲמוֹד וְהַחְזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava says:** From where is it derived that a rapist must remarry his victim if he divorced her because the positive mitzva rectifies the prohibition "He may not send her away"? It is derived from the superfluous phrase: "All his days" (Deuteronomy 22:29), indicating that **all his days,** even after he marries and divorces her, he is obligated **to arise and remarry** her and is consequently not liable to receive lashes. The Gemara adds: **And likewise, when Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: All his days** he is obligated **to arise and remarry** her.

Rava resolves the difficulty differently, setting aside entirely the comparison to the *motzi shem ra*. He focuses instead on the phrase *kol yamav* — all his days — in the verse *lo yuchal leshalcha kol yamav*. Why does the Torah need to say *kol yamav*? The prohibition of divorcing her is already stated; the phrase *kol yamav* is additional. Rava says: *kol yamav be'amod vehachazir* — the phrase *kol yamav* is teaching that the obligation of maintaining the marriage extends across his entire life, and if he divorces her, he must remarry her. This phrase, being superfluous on a plain reading of the prohibition, is what teaches that remarriage functions as a corrective in the *oneis* case — and does so without relying on any comparison to the *motzi shem ra*.

### Two Readings

There are, however, two distinct ways to understand how *kol yamav* achieves this result, and they lead to meaningfully different conceptual frameworks.

The first reading, associated with Rashi, keeps *kol yamav* attached to the negative prohibition itself. On this reading, the transgression of divorcing her is not defined as the mere act of delivering a *get*. Rather, the prohibition is violated only when he has divorced her *permanently* — for all his days. If he divorces her and subsequently remarries her, he has not in fact transgressed the *lav*, because the divorce did not endure for *kol yamav*. On this reading, the *asei* of marrying is entirely beside the point as a remedial mechanism; the man who remarries her never transgressed in the first place. The exemption from lashes is not because the *asei* fixed the *lav*, but because the *lav* was never fully violated.

The second reading, which Tosafot and Rashi in another context prefer, attaches *kol yamav* to the *asei* of *lo tihyeh le'ishah* — she shall be his wife for all his days. On this reading, the *asei* of marriage is not merely an obligation that precedes the *lav* in the text; it extends forward across his entire life and functions as an ongoing corrective. Even if he divorced her, the *asei* is telling him: make sure she remains your wife for all your days, which means that remarrying her after a divorce is itself the fulfillment of the *asei* — and that fulfillment exempts him from lashes.

The practical difference between these two readings is considerable. On the first reading — Rashi's primary interpretation — the man who divorces and does not remarry has not technically transgressed *so long as* she remains available for remarriage. He can always say he intends to remarry her. A court could not give him lashes while remarriage remains possible, because he has not yet divorced her *kol yamav*. It is only when remarriage becomes permanently impossible — for instance, if she marries someone else or dies — that he has transgressed. On the second reading, the question is whether the *asei* has been fulfilled or not, and a different framework for assessing when lashes are due applies. This connects directly to the broader debate between the *bitlo* and *kiymo* models, to which the Gemara now turns.

## Rav Papa Challenge

### Ox Paradigm

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְרָבָא: וְהָא לָא דָּמֵי לָאוֵיהּ לְלָאו דַּחֲסִימָה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתַב בֵּיהּ רַחֲמָנָא עֲשֵׂה יַתִּירָא מִגְרָע גָּרַע?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Rava** that a question arises with regard to Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement that one is flogged for violating a prohibition preceded by a positive mitzva: **But isn't its prohibition dissimilar to the prohibition of muzzling** an ox while it is threshing grain (see Deuteronomy 25:4), which is the paradigm for all prohibitions for whose violation one is flogged, as no positive mitzva is stated in conjunction with the prohibition of muzzling? Rava **said to him:** Is it reasonable to say that **due to** the fact **that the Merciful One wrote in its** regard **an additional positive mitzva,** the stringency of the prohibition **lessens,** so that no lashes are administered?

Rav Papa challenges Rabbi Yochanan's principle from a different angle. The foundational paradigm for *malkus* is derived from the juxtaposition of the passage mandating lashes — *vehaya im bin hakot harasha, vehipilo hashofet vehikahu lefanav* — with the prohibition of *lo tachsom shor bedisho*, do not muzzle an ox while it threshes. That case establishes the model: a negative prohibition, transgressed through an active physical deed, with no *asei* connected to it whatsoever. Rav Papa's question: even granting Rabbi Yochanan's point that when the *asei* precedes the *lav* it is not a corrective but merely an independent additional obligation — still, is such a *lav* truly comparable to the paradigmatic case of muzzling the ox? The ox case has no *asei* at all. The case where an *asei* precedes the *lav* does have an *asei*, even if that *asei* does not function as a fix. Should the mere presence of an *asei* not be sufficient to distinguish this case from the paradigm and exempt the transgressor from lashes, even without the *asei* serving as a remedy?

### Obligation Not Fix

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, לָאו שֶׁנִּיתָּק לַעֲשֵׂה נָמֵי, לֵימָא: מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתַב בֵּיהּ רַחֲמָנָא עֲשֵׂה יַתִּירָא מִגְרָע גָּרַע? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָהוּא לְנַתּוֹקֵי לָאו הוּא דַּאֲתָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa objected to this claim: **If so,** with regard to **a prohibition that entails** fulfillment **of a positive mitzva as well, let us say:** Is it reasonable to say that **due to** the fact **that the Merciful One wrote in its** regard **an additional positive mitzva,** the stringency of the prohibition **lessens,** so that no lashes are administered? Rava **said to him:** In **that** case, the transgressor is not flogged because the mitzva **comes to sever the prohibition** from the punishment of lashes.

Rabba responds: *mishum dechtav bei Rachmana asei yetira migra gara?* — does the fact that the Torah wrote an additional *asei* thereby weaken the *lav*? Once we have conceptually established that the *asei* which precedes the *lav* is not a corrective but an independent additional obligation, its presence does not diminish the *lav*. If anything, having both a positive obligation and a negative prohibition makes the entire matter weightier, not lighter. The comparison to the paradigm of muzzling the ox is not made arbitrarily on the basis of formal similarity alone; what matters is whether the *lav* is in some way weakened. A preceding *asei* that carries no remedial function does not weaken the *lav* — it reinforces the overall stringency of the obligation.

Rav Papa then presses further: if a non-remedial *asei* does not weaken the *lav*, why should a remedial *asei* — the classic case of *lav she'nitak la'asei*, where the *asei* comes after and is meant to fix — exempt from lashes? Should we not say the same thing: just because there is an *asei*, it does not weaken the *lav*?

Rabba's response is decisive. Here the word *nitak* — detach, undermine — is operative, and crucially, it refers not to what happens to the *asei* but to what happens to the *lav*. When the *asei* follows the *lav* in order to serve as a corrective, the *asei* is there to *natek* the *lav* — to dislodge, neutralize, or undermine the *lav* itself. The *lav* is not simply accompanied by an additional obligation; rather, the *asei* is positioned in the text precisely to attenuate the force of the *lav*. That is a qualitative difference: the *lav* is intrinsically weakened by the corrective *asei* that trails it. By contrast, when the *asei* precedes the *lav*, it adds an obligation; it does not undermine the *lav*. These are categorically distinct cases. The absence of lashes in the case of *lav she'nitak la'asei* is therefore not arbitrary — it flows from the structural fact that the *asei* is designed to neutralize the transgression. And when the *asei* precedes, no such neutralization is at work, and lashes apply.

This is also the basis for the exemption from lashes in the case of a *lav she'ein bo ma'aseh* — a passive transgression. Just as a passive act is categorically weaker than an active one and therefore falls outside the paradigm of muzzling the ox, so too a *lav* accompanied by a subsequent corrective *asei* is categorically weaker — the *asei* has disrupted the *lav* — and lashes similarly do not apply.

## Bitla vs. Kiyma

### Tana Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל מִצְוַת לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ קוּם עֲשֵׂה, קִיֵּים עֲשֵׂה שֶׁבָּהּ – פָּטוּר, בִּיטֵּל עֲשֵׂה שֶׁבָּהּ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** What is the dispute to which the Gemara is alluding? **The *tanna* taught** a *baraita* **before Rabbi Yoḥanan:** With regard to **any prohibition that entails** a command to **arise** and **perform** a mitzva, if **he fulfilled the positive** mitzva **that** is entailed **therein,** he is **exempt** from lashes, and if he **nullified the positive mitzva that** is entailed **therein,** he is **liable** to receive lashes.

A *tana* taught before Rabbi Yochanan the following formulation: *kol mitzvat lo ta'aseh she'yesh ba kum aseh* — any negative commandment that is accompanied by a positive commandment positioned to remedy it — *kiyyem aseh she'bah, patur; bitel aseh she'bah, chayav*. If one fulfilled the *asei* — sent away the mother bird — one is exempt. If one negated the *asei* — killed the mother bird, rendering its fulfillment impossible — one is liable.

### Excluded Middle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי קָא אָמְרַתְּ? קִיֵּים – פָּטוּר, לֹא קִיֵּים – חַיָּיב, בִּיטֵּל – חַיָּיב, לֹא בִּיטֵּל – פָּטוּר! תְּנִי: בִּיטְּלוֹ וְלֹא בִּיטְּלוֹ. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אוֹמֵר: קִיְּימוֹ וְלֹא קִיְּימוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** the *tanna*: **What** is it that **you are saying?** The *baraita* that you recited is self-contradictory, as based on the first clause: If **he fulfilled** the mitzva he is **exempt,** apparently, if **he did not fulfill** the mitzva he is **liable.** Yet based on the latter clause: If **he nullified** the mitzva, he is **liable,** apparently, if **he did not nullify** the mitzva he is **exempt,** even though he failed to fulfill the mitzva. Rather, **teach:** If he **nullified** the mitzva, he is liable to receive lashes, **and** if **he did not nullify** the mitzva, he is exempt from lashes. **And Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says:** The formulation of the *baraita* must be consistent; therefore, teach: If **he fulfilled** the mitzva, he is exempt, **and** if **he did not fulfill** the mitzva he is liable.

The difficulty with this formulation is immediate and striking. The *tana* has articulated two poles — fulfillment and negation — but has said nothing about the vast middle ground between them. In the case of the mother bird: what is the *halacha* for the person who took the mother bird, did not send her away, but also did not kill her? He simply walked away. He has neither fulfilled the *asei* nor actively negated it. The *tana's* formulation leaves this case entirely unaddressed.

Rabbi Yochanan identifies the problem precisely. He poses it as a choice: are you saying *kiyymo patur, lo kiyymo chayav* — if you fulfilled it you are exempt, if you did not fulfill it you are liable? In that case, the framework is *kiyma versus lo kiyma*, and the excluded middle is covered by the "not fulfilled" side: a person who merely walked away without sending the mother bird free has not fulfilled the *asei* and would be liable. This is the more intuitive reading: you transgressed the *lav*, you can remedy it by doing the *asei*, but if you have not done so, you remain liable. Alternatively, are you saying *bitlo chayav, lo bitlo patur* — if you actively negated the *asei* you are liable, if you did not actively negate it you are exempt? In that case, someone who merely walked away — who neither sent the bird away nor killed it — would not be liable at all, because he did not take the affirmative step of negating the *asei*.

These are not equivalent positions, and the *tana's* formulation, as stated, does not cleanly commit to either. Rabbi Yochanan insists: choose one or the other. Present it as a question of fulfillment (*kiyma*) versus non-fulfillment, or as a question of active negation (*bitla*) versus non-negation. As formulated, the *beraisa* creates an irresolvable excluded middle.

The two positions have very different practical implications. Under the *kiymo* model, the person who walks away from the bird without sending it free is liable to lashes — he transgressed the *lav* and failed to fulfill the remedial *asei*. Under the *bitlo* model, the same person is exempt — he has not actively negated the *asei*, and as long as the *asei* remains theoretically performable, he incurs no lashes. On the *bitlo* model, lashes are extraordinarily difficult to impose in almost any real case, because short of actively rendering the *asei* impossible to perform, the transgressor remains technically exempt.

### RY vs. RL Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִידֵּי הוּא טַעְמָא אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן? הָאָמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְתַנָּא, תְּנִי: בִּטְּלוֹ – חַיָּיב, וְלֹא בִּטְּלוֹ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The only reason** Rava stated his explanation, that all his days he is obligated to arise and remarry her and that is why the rapist is not flogged even though it is a prohibition preceded by a positive mitzva, **is to** explain the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who said that for any prohibition that has a positive mitzva which preceded it, everyone agrees that one is flogged for its violation. **Didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say to** the ***tanna*** who would recite the *mishnayot* and *baraitot* in the study hall: **Teach** that if he **nullified** the mitzva, he is **liable** to receive lashes, and **if he did not nullify** the mitzva, he is **exempt** from lashes? According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, it works out well.

Rabbi Yochanan resolves the ambiguity by instructing the *tana* to reformulate: *tani bitlo v'lo bitlo* — teach it in terms of active negation and its absence. You are only *chayav* if you actively negated the *asei*; anything short of that renders you exempt. Resh Lakish, by contrast, insists on the *kiymo* formulation: *kiymo v'lo kiymo* — if you fulfilled the *asei* you are exempt; if you have not yet fulfilled it, you are liable.

## Hasra'at Safek Root

### Warning Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? בְּהַתְרָאַת סָפֵק קָא מִיפַּלְגִי, מָר סָבַר: הַתְרָאַת סָפֵק שְׁמָהּ הַתְרָאָה,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: **With regard to what** matter **do they disagree? They disagree with regard to uncertain forewarning,** i.e., forewarning concerning a transgression with regard to which it will not be clarified whether or not his action will render him liable to receive lashes. One **Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **holds: Uncertain forewarning is characterized as forewarning;** therefore, even if it is unclear whether the action that the transgressor is about to perform will render him liable to receive lashes, he can be forewarned, and if he violates the prohibition in a manner that will render him liable, he is flogged.

The Gemara probes the root of this dispute. What underlying principle generates the disagreement between Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish? The answer: *be'hasra'at safek kamiflagi* — they disagree about the validity of doubtful warning, *hasra'at safek*. To understand why this matters, consider the moment at which the warning is administered in the mother bird case. There are witnesses who see a man approaching a nest and they warn him: do not take the mother bird; if you do, you will receive lashes. Is that a *hasra'at vadai* — a definite warning, meaning a warning that his act will definitely constitute a transgression? Or is it a *hasra'at safek* — a doubtful warning, meaning a warning about an act that may or may not ultimately constitute a transgression?

### Active Completion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָר סָבַר: הַתְרָאַת סָפֵק לֹא שְׁמָהּ הַתְרָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** one **Sage,** Reish Lakish, **holds: Uncertain forewarning is not characterized as forewarning.** According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, the rapist is forewarned when he is about to divorce his wife, even though there is uncertainty whether he will nullify the mitzva. If he nullifies the mitzva, e.g., if he vows that deriving benefit from his ex-wife is forbidden to him, thereby ensuring that he cannot remarry her, he is flogged. Reish Lakish holds that if one's liability to receive lashes was dependent upon the nullification of the mitzva, the rapist would never be flogged, as in that case the forewarning would of necessity be uncertain. Therefore, he explains that the criterion for determining whether one is flogged is whether he fulfilled the mitzva immediately. Violating the prohibition renders him liable to receive lashes; he may then choose to be flogged or to fulfill the mitzva. Therefore, when he is forewarned that he will be flogged if he divorces her, it is not uncertain forewarning.

Under the *bitlo* model, the act of taking the mother bird is inherently incomplete as a transgression. The transgression is not fully constituted until the person has also actively negated the *asei* — say, by killing the mother bird. At the moment of taking the bird, the person has done the first half of the transgression but not the second. The warning at that moment is therefore not definitive — the witnesses cannot say with certainty that this act will result in full liability. The act is a *hasra'at safek* because it remains possible that the person will send the bird away and thereby avoid liability entirely.

### RY Safek Valid

This is the precise axis of the dispute. Rabbi Yochanan holds that *hasra'at safek shema hasra'ah* — a warning even in a case where it is not yet certain that a full transgression will occur is nonetheless a valid warning. Because Rabbi Yochanan accepts *hasra'at safek*, he can hold the *bitlo* position: the transgression is conceptually understood in two stages, and the warning at the first stage is still valid. Resh Lakish, on the other hand, holds *hasra'at safek lo shema hasra'ah* — a doubtful warning does not qualify as a valid warning. Therefore, for Resh Lakish, the only way to give a person valid lashes with a proper warning is to take the *kiymo* approach: hold that the act of taking the bird is itself the complete transgression, the warning is unambiguously definitive, and the subsequent fulfillment of the *asei* functions as a post-hoc remedy. Under this model, the moment of taking is the moment of full transgression, the warning is *vadai*, and lashes are properly grounded.

## Loaf of Bread Case

### Bread Oath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָזְדוּ לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ, דְּאִיתְּמַר: "שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאוֹכַל כִּכָּר זֶה הַיּוֹם", וְעָבַר הַיּוֹם וְלֹא אֲכָלָהּ, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה,
>
> **Translation:** **And they follow their** standard lines of **reasoning, as it was stated** that Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish disagreed in a case where one said: On my **oath I will eat this loaf today, and the day passed and he did not eat it. Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish both say: He is not flogged** for taking a false oath. They disagree with regard to the reason that he is not flogged. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: He is not flogged,**
>
> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵי לָאו שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה, וְכׇל לָאו שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה – אֵין לוֹקִין עָלָיו. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה, מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵי הַתְרָאַת סָפֵק, וְכׇל הַתְרָאַת סָפֵק – לֹא שְׁמָהּ הַתְרָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **because it is a prohibition that does not involve an action.** He violates the oath by failing to perform an action, rather than by performing an action, **and** the principle is: With regard to **any prohibition that does not involve an action, one is not flogged for its** violation. **Reish Lakish says: He is not flogged, because** the forewarning in this case **is an uncertain forewarning.** One cannot properly forewarn him before he takes the oath, because as long as time remains in the day he can still eat the loaf at a later time and fulfill the oath; **and any uncertain forewarning is not characterized as forewarning.**

The Gemara confirms that Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish carry their respective positions consistently into a related case: one who takes an oath that he will eat a loaf of bread today, and the day passes without his having done so. Both agree that he does not receive lashes — but they disagree about the reason.

### Passive Exempt

Rabbi Yochanan says he is exempt *mishum d'havei lav she'ein bo ma'aseh* — because this is a passive transgression. He did nothing; the day simply elapsed. The principle that a *lav she'ein bo ma'aseh*, a prohibition violated through inaction rather than action, does not incur lashes, is the operative rule here. For Rabbi Yochanan, this is a straightforward application of that exemption. Rabbi Yochanan is not troubled by *hasra'at safek* as a category — he accepts it — so the problem with the bread case is not the nature of the warning but the nature of the act.

### RL Safek Invalid

Resh Lakish locates the exemption elsewhere: *mishum d'havei hasra'at safek* — because the warning in such a case is a doubtful warning. When one warns a person at midday that he must eat the bread or face lashes, the person has not yet transgressed, and it is uncertain whether he will. The warning is not definitive. Since Resh Lakish holds that *hasra'at safek lo shema hasra'ah*, the warning is invalid, and lashes cannot be administered without a proper warning. For Resh Lakish, the passive character of the transgression is not the operative concern — the problem is that *hasra'at vadai* could never be properly administered in such a case.

These two explanations are rooted in the same underlying disagreement. Rabbi Yochanan, who accepts *hasra'at safek* and therefore can embrace the *bitlo* model, locates the exemption in the passive nature of the act. Resh Lakish, who requires *hasra'at vadai* and therefore must hold the *kiymo* model in the *lav she'nitak la'asei* context, locates the exemption in the invalidity of the warning. Both agree on the outcome in the bread case, but their reasoning reflects two coherent and divergent ways of understanding when a negative prohibition is truly violated and when the machinery of *malkus* may legitimately be set in motion.
