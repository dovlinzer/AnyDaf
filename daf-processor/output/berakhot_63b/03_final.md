# Berakhot 63b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Masechet Structure

### Sanctity Daily Life

We conclude today with the final section of Masechet Berachot, picking up at 63b, ten lines from the top, at the *tanu rabbanan*. Before turning to the text itself, it is worth pausing to consider what Masechet Berachot as a whole has accomplished. The tractate opens with the world of the *mikdash* — when the *kohanim* eat their *terumah*, how they enter and conduct themselves in that sacred space — and then proceeds to build a framework for sanctity in post-*mikdash* daily life. The *tefillah*, the *berachot* we recite before eating, the liturgical structures we inhabit each day: so much of this is rabbinic in origin, yet the entire enterprise is oriented toward the same goal of *kedushah*, of recognizing and sanctifying God within the natural order of things.

### Parallel Structures

It is instructive to notice that the tractate closes in a way that mirrors how it opened. Just as the beginning of the *masechet* drew us into the world of the *mikdash* through the laws of *terumah* and the *kohanim*, the end returns us there — through a discussion of entering and greeting, of using God's name in the act of meeting one's fellow, as exemplified by Boaz in the field. The closing *pasuk* of the *mishnah*, *et la'asot lashem heferu toratecha*, is a striking way to conclude a tractate on *berachot*. One might ask: what does this have to do with greeting one's friend with *shem hashem*? Perhaps there is a sense in which invoking God's name in so mundane a context represents a kind of *heferu toratecha* — a departure from the more structured *kedushah* one would associate with Torah law. And yet the *mishnah* affirms this practice as vital, even necessary. That in itself could be understood as a statement about all of Masechet Berachot: so much of what we do is rabbinic, a realignment of how *kedushah* is generated, and yet how critical that realignment is in our post-*mikdash* reality.

### Post-Destruction Halakha

One detail of the tractate's architecture that should not pass without remark: just as the opening chapters dealt extensively with laws governing the body's most private acts, the closing pages of the *masechet* returned to those same concerns. The *halakhah* governs even there — and that too is a statement about bringing *kedushah* into every dimension of daily life. The parallel is not accidental. With this structural observation in mind, we turn to the *gemara* itself, which now offers a series of *drashot* that shape the conclusion of the *masechet* in a very particular direction — away from *berachot* and liturgy, and toward *Torah* and *talmud Torah* as the defining center of *kedushah* in the post-*mikdash* world.

## Kermi Yavneh

### Yavneh Transition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן כְּשֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ רַבּוֹתֵינוּ לַכֶּרֶם בְּיַבְנֶה, הָיוּ שָׁם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי. פָּתְחוּ כּוּלָּם בִּכְבוֹד אַכְסַנְיָא וְדָרְשׁוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: When our Rabbis,** the Sages of the Mishna, **entered the vineyard,** the academy, **in Yavne, Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Yosei, Rabbi Neḥemya, and Rabbi Eliezer, son of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, were there** presiding over the Sages. **They all began** to speak **in honor of** their **hosts,** the local population hosting them and their students as guests, **and they taught.**

*Keshenichnesu rabboteinu bekerem beYavneh* — when our rabbis entered the vineyard of Yavneh. This is that foundational moment of transition: the destruction of the *beit hamikdash* and the establishment of a new center for Jewish life. Yavneh becomes the replacement for Yerushalayim. It is the new center, and it is a *Torah* center — the center of a post-*mikdash* Judaism. Present there were Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Yosi, Rabbi Nechemya, Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua, and Rabbi Yosi HaGlili.

### Honor to Host

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּתַח רַבִּי יְהוּדָה רֹאשׁ הַמְדַבְּרִים בְּכָל מָקוֹם בִּכְבוֹד תּוֹרָה, וְדָרַשׁ:
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda, head of the speakers in every place, opened** his speech **in honor of Torah, and taught:**

*Potchu kulam bikhvod achsanya* — they all opened with honor to the host. This is the important principle of *poteach bikhvod achsanya*: when you rise to speak, whether to deliver a *dvar Torah* or to address a gathering, you first thank the host. The *gemara* then tells us that Rabbi Yehuda opened, as he was always the first to speak in any assembly — *rosh hamedabrim bekhol makom*, the one who had the right to open meetings in every place. That right had its origins in a famous episode: Rabbi Yehuda had once opened a gathering by praising the Roman government and its public works, while other rabbis chose rather less complimentary things to say about Rome, and suffered the consequences. As a result, Rabbi Yehuda was granted by the Roman authorities the privilege of always opening meetings with words of thanks to the host. Here, though, rather than praising Rome, Rabbi Yehuda opens with *bikhvod Torah*, in honor of Torah — appropriate for a convocation whose entire purpose was Torah.

### Tent of Meeting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמֹשֶׁה יִקַּח אֶת הָאֹהֶל וְנָטָה לוֹ מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״. וַהֲלֹא דְּבָרִים קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה אֲרוֹן ה׳ שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה מְרוּחָק אֶלָּא שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר מִיל אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה ״וְהָיָה כׇּל מְבַקֵּשׁ ה׳ יֵצֵא אֶל אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״ — תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁהוֹלְכִים מֵעִיר לָעִיר וּמִמְּדִינָה לִמְדִינָה לִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה, עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** It is stated: **"Now Moses used to take the tent and pitch it outside the camp,** far off from the camp; and he called it the Tent of Meeting. And it came to pass, that every seeker of God went out unto the Tent of Meeting, which was outside the camp" (Exodus 33:7). He said: **Isn't this an *a fortiori* inference? Just as the Torah says** of **the ark of God, which was only twelve *mil* from the camp: "Every seeker of God went out unto the Tent of Meeting"; all the more so** should **Torah scholars,** who wander great distances and **go from city to city and country to country to study Torah,** be called seekers of God.

Rabbi Yehuda expounds: *u'Moshe yikach et haohel v'nata lo michutz lamachaneh* — Moshe took the tent and pitched it outside the camp. This took place in the aftermath of the *chet haegel*. After the plague, with the people still under severe divine displeasure, God declared *rega echad eleh bekirbecha v'chiliticha* — He could not dwell in their midst. So Moshe took his own tent, positioned it outside the camp, called it the *ohel mo'ed*, and anyone who wished to seek God would go out to it. The *amud he'anan* would descend there, and God would speak with Moshe. Only later, after Moshe prayed and God forgave the people, was it possible to build the *mishkan* proper and fulfill the promise of *veshachanti betocham*.

This "tent of meeting" outside the camp — lowercase, as it were, a precursor to the formal *ohel mo'ed* — was where God's presence temporarily resided while the people were in a state of estrangement. A natural question arises regarding the *aron*: where was it during this period? According to the simple reading of the Torah, the *aron* had not yet been built, since it was only constructed after Moshe descended the second time with the new tablets. The *gemara* apparently imagines that some version of the *aron*, perhaps housing the *shivrei luchot*, was present in this temporary *ohel mo'ed* as well.

The *kal vachomer* Rabbi Yehuda now constructs is this: *u'mah aron shelo hayah meruchak ela shtem esrei mil* — the *aron* was only twelve *mil* outside the camp, since the Israelite encampment itself was twelve *mil* wide. And yet the Torah says *vehayah kol mevakesh Hashem yetzei el ohel mo'ed* — anyone who sought God would go out to the *ohel mo'ed*. Traveling twelve *mil* to seek God was enough to earn one the title *mevakesh Hashem*. How much more so, then, *talmidei chachamim sheholchim me'ir l'ir u'mimedina limedina lilmod Torah* — Torah scholars who travel from city to city and from country to country to learn Torah — are they called *mevakshei Hashem*.

### Moshe Divine Encounter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְדִבֶּר ה׳ אֶל מֹשֶׁה פָּנִים אֶל פָּנִים״, אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְמֹשֶׁה: מֹשֶׁה, אֲנִי וְאַתָּה נַסְבִּיר פָּנִים בַּהֲלָכָה. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי כָּךְ אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְמֹשֶׁה: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאֲנִי הִסְבַּרְתִּי לְךָ פָּנִים, כָּךְ אַתָּה הַסְבֵּר פָּנִים לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, וְהַחֲזֵר הָאֹהֶל לִמְקוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It is stated: **"And the Lord spoke unto Moses, face to face"** (Exodus 33:11). **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Moses: Moses, you and I will show cheerful faces in** the study of ***halakha*** to those who come to study. **Some say** that **the Holy One, Blessed be He, told Moses: Just as I showed you a cheerful face, so too you will show Israel a cheerful face and restore the tent to its place** in the camp.

The *gemara* now cites an additional teaching embedded in this same narrative. It says *vayedaber Hashem el Moshe panim el panim* — God spoke with Moshe face to face. Rabbi Yitzchak teaches that God said to Moshe: *ani ve'atah nasber panim bahalakhah* — you and I, let us work through the *halakhah* together. The learning of *halakhah*, the process of *talmud Torah*, is here presented as a direct encounter with God. In a world where the *mikdash* no longer stands and that encounter can no longer occur through the *avodah*, it is the engagement with Torah that constitutes the meeting of *panim el panim* with the divine. A second version adds: just as I have explained the Torah to you, *atah hasber panim leYisrael* — you must in turn explain it to Israel.

### Tent Returns Camp

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְשָׁב אֶל הַמַּחֲנֶה״ וְגוֹ׳, אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְמֹשֶׁה: עַכְשָׁיו יֹאמְרוּ הָרַב בְּכַעַס וְתַלְמִיד בְּכַעַס, יִשְׂרָאֵל מַה תְּהֵא עֲלֵיהֶם? אִם אַתָּה מַחֲזִיר הָאֹהֶל לִמְקוֹמוֹ — מוּטָב, וְאִם לָאו — יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן תַּלְמִידְךָ מְשָׁרֵת תַּחְתֶּיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** It is said: **"And he would return into the camp;** but his minister, Joshua bin-Nun, a young man, departed not out of the Tent" (Exodus 33:11). **Rabbi Abbahu said: The Holy One, Blessed be He, told Moses: Now,** they will say: **The Master,** God, **is angry and** the **student,** Moses, is also **angry, and what will happen to Israel?** Rather, you must restore the tent to its place among the people. **If you restore the tent to its place, fine; and if not, Joshua bin-Nun, your student,** will **serve** as Israel's leader **in your place.**

This brings us to the consequence: *vehechazir haohel limkomo* — the tent must be returned to its place. Rabbi Abbahu teaches that God said to Moshe: if both teacher and student remain outside the camp while Israel is abandoned, the people will say — the master is angry and the disciple is angry; what will become of Israel? *Im atah machazir ohel limkomo mutav* — if you return the tent, good. *Ve'im lav* — and if you do not, then *Yehoshua bin Nun talmidcha mesharet tachtecha* — your student Yehoshua will serve in your place. And so Moshe returned to the camp.

The implicit message is not difficult to hear. The *beit hamikdash* has been destroyed; the tent has been removed outside the camp. But the rabbis who gather at Yavneh understand themselves as heirs to Moshe's role. The tent must be brought back. The Torah must be brought to the people. The destruction does not dissolve the obligation; if anything, it intensifies it.

### Joshua Successor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וְשָׁב אֶל הַמַּחֲנֶה״. אָמַר רָבָא: אַף עַל פִּי כֵן, לֹא יָצָא הַדָּבָר לְבַטָּלָה. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמְשָׁרְתוֹ יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן נַעַר לֹא יָמִישׁ מִתּוֹךְ הָאֹהֶל״.
>
> **Translation:** **And that is what is written: "And he would return into the camp;** but his minister, Joshua bin-Nun, a young man, departed not out of the Tent." **Rava said: Nevertheless,** though Moses obeyed and restored the tent, **the statement** written with regard to the role of Joshua **was not uttered for naught.** Joshua bin-Nun remained as deputy to Moses, and ultimately served in his place, as it is stated: **"But his minister, Joshua bin-Nun, a young man, departed not out of the Tent."**

Rava observes: *af al pi ken lo yatza davar levatala* — even so, the divine promise was not made in vain. *Mesharto Yehoshua bin Nun naar lo yamish mitoch haohel* — Yehoshua remained within the tent, and in the end he became the one to transmit Torah to the people. The promise found its fulfillment. Here again, in a passage ostensibly about the transition from Moshe to Yehoshua, one senses the deeper resonance for the rabbis gathered at Yavneh: even if the primary leader hesitates or withdraws, the tradition will find its bearer. The people will not be abandoned.

## Torah Daily Har Sinai

### Daily Torah Study

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד פָּתַח רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בִּכְבוֹד תּוֹרָה וְדָרַשׁ: ״הַסְכֵּת וּשְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל הַיּוֹם הַזֶּה נִהְיֵיתָ לְעָם״, וְכִי אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם נִתְּנָה תּוֹרָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל? וַהֲלֹא אוֹתוֹ יוֹם סוֹף אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה הָיָה! אֶלָּא לְלַמֶּדְךָ שֶׁחֲבִיבָה תּוֹרָה עַל לוֹמְדֶיהָ בְּכָל יוֹם וָיוֹם כַּיּוֹם שֶׁנִּתְּנָה מֵהַר סִינַי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehuda again began** to speak **in honor of Torah and taught:** When Moses took leave of Israel on his last day in this world, he said: **"Keep silence [*hasket*] and hear, Israel; this day you have become a people** unto the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 27:9). This is surprising: **Was the Torah given to Israel on that day? Wasn't that day at the end of forty years** since the Torah was given? **Rather,** it comes **to teach that each and every day the Torah is as dear to those who study it, as** it was **on the day it was given from Mount Sinai.**

*Ve'od patach Rabbi Yehuda bichvod Torah* — Rabbi Yehuda again opened in honor of Torah and expounded. The verse from Devarim reads: *hasket u'shema Yisrael, hayom hazeh nihyeita le'am Hashem Elokecha* — be attentive and listen, Israel; today you have become a nation to Hashem your God. The obvious difficulty: was it on that day that the Torah was given to Israel? Clearly not — that day was the end of forty years of wandering. *Ela lelamedcha shechaviva Torah al lomedeiha bechol yom vayom keyom shenitna meHar Sinai* — rather, this teaches that Torah is as dear to those who study it as if it were given each day anew from Har Sinai. Elsewhere the *gemara* expresses this as: *bechol yom yihyu be'eineicha kachadashim* — each day the words of Torah should be in your eyes as something new.

The point is not merely an aspiration toward freshness or enthusiasm. It is a theological claim: when Torah is studied properly, it constitutes a re-standing at Har Sinai, a renewed acceptance of Torah, a new encounter with the divine. In the absence of the *mikdash*, *talmud Torah* is the mode of that encounter.

### Missing Shema

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי תַּנְחוּם בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא אִישׁ כְּפַר עַכּוֹ: תֵּדַע — שֶׁהֲרֵי אָדָם קוֹרֵא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע שַׁחֲרִית וְעַרְבִית, וְעֶרֶב אֶחָד אֵינוֹ קוֹרֵא — דּוֹמֶה כְּמִי שֶׁלֹּא קָרָא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע מֵעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Tanḥum, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya, of the village of Akko, said: Know** that the Torah is indeed beloved, **as one who recites *Shema*, morning and evening,** for his entire life, and **does not recite it one evening, it is as if he never recited *Shema*.** He cannot compensate for what he missed.

Rav Tanchum son of Rav Chiya, a man of Kefar Akko, illustrates how seriously this weight should be felt: *she'adam korei keri'at shema shacharit ve'arvit, ve'erev echad eino korei* — a person recites *keri'at shema* morning and evening every day of his life, and one evening he fails to recite it. *Domeh kemi shelo kara keri'at shema me'olam* — he feels as though he has never recited *shema* in his life. The point is not a claim about his actual standing, but about the experiential weight of the loss. Missing that single opportunity feels devastating — not merely because of the *mitzvah* of *keri'at shema* as such, but because of what *shema* represents in this context: an act of *talmud Torah*, a *kabbalat Torah* from Har Sinai that was lost that one evening. And here the end of the *masechet* is in striking conversation with its beginning, which also associated *keri'at shema* with *talmud Torah* — though that was not how it began. The closing pages of the *gemara* absorb even the *mitzvah* of *shema* into the column of *talmud Torah*.

## Torah Learning Methods

### Learning Groups

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הַסְכֵּת״ — עֲשׂוּ כִּתּוֹת כִּתּוֹת וְעִסְקוּ בַּתּוֹרָה. לְפִי שֶׁאֵין הַתּוֹרָה נִקְנֵית אֶלָּא בַּחֲבוּרָה. כִּדְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״חֶרֶב אֶל הַבַּדִּים וְנֹאָלוּ״ — חֶרֶב עַל שׂוֹנְאֵיהֶם שֶׁל תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁיּוֹשְׁבִים בַּד בְּבַד וְעוֹסְקִים בַּתּוֹרָה. וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁמִּטַּפְּשִׁים, כְּתִיב הָכָא ״וְנֹאָלוּ״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם ״אֲשֶׁר נוֹאַלְנוּ״. וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁחוֹטְאִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וַאֲשֶׁר חָטָאנוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interprets the word ***hasket*** in this verse homiletically, as an acronym of the words *as*, make, and *kat*, group. **Form [*asu*] many groups [*kitot*] and study Torah, for the Torah is only acquired** through study **in a group.** This is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina; as Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "A sword is upon the boasters [*habaddim*], and they shall become fools [*noalu*]"** (Jeremiah 50:36)? This verse can be interpreted homiletically: **A sword upon the enemies of Torah scholars,** a euphemism for the Torah scholars themselves, **who sit alone [*bad bevad*] and study Torah. And furthermore,** those who study alone **grow foolish, as it is written here, *noalu*, and elsewhere it is written** that after Miriam was afflicted with leprosy, Aaron told Moses: **"For that we have done foolishly [*noalnu*]"** (Numbers 12:11). **And furthermore, they sin** due to that ignorance, **as** at the end of that same verse **it is stated:** "For that we have done foolishly, **and for that we have sinned."**

The *gemara* now turns to a series of practical teachings about how Torah is acquired, all organized around additional readings of the word *hasket* from the same verse. The first: *asu kitot kitot ve'aseku baTorah* — form groups and engage in Torah. The letter *heh* of *hasket* is read as *has*, while *ket* points to *kitah*, a group. *Lefi she'ein haTorah nikneit ela bechabura* — Torah is only acquired in community. There is something profoundly democratic about this. The process of *talmud Torah* is one of conversation, of hearing other minds engage the material, of reaching the bottom of things together. One might contrast this with the *mikdash*, which was anything but democratic: access was structured by lineage, by *tumah* and *taharah*, by physical wholeness. The *korbanot*, with the notable exception of the *korban tamid* and the *korban pesach*, were largely private affairs. Torah study is different in kind — it belongs to everyone, and it is acquired through collective engagement.

### Against Lone Study

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא מֵהָכָא: ״נוֹאֲלוּ שָׂרֵי צֹעַן״.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** instead that it is derived **from here: "The princes of Tzoan are become fools [*noalu*]"** (Isaiah 19:13).

Rabbi Yose bar Hanina pushes this further by finding a *remez* in the verse *herev el habaddim ve-noalu* — a sword against the *baddim*, and they will be foolish. The *gemara* reads this as: *herev el soneihem shel talmidei chachamim* — a sword figuratively against Torah scholars *she-yoshvim bad be-vad ve-oskim baTorah* — who sit alone and study in isolation. Not only is this the wrong approach; it is actively harmful. *Ela she-mitapshin* — they will become foolish. The *gezerah shavah* on the word *noalu* confirms: the same term used for foolishness in the verse about the officers of Tzo'an applies here. *Ve-lo od ella she-chotim* — and not only foolish, but sinful. Isolated study diminishes rather than enhances one's Torah.

### Self-Sacrifice Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״הַסְכֵּת וּשְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל״ — כַּתְּתוּ עַצְמְכֶם עַל דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה. כִּדְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה מִתְקַיְּימִין אֶלָּא בְּמִי שֶׁמֵּמִית עַצְמוֹ עָלֶיהָ — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״זֹאת הַתּוֹרָה אָדָם כִּי יָמוּת בְּאֹהֶל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers **an alternative** explanation of this verse: **"Keep silence [*hasket*] and hear, Israel"; break [*kattetu*] yourselves over words of the Torah.** This is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Reish Lakish, as Reish Lakish said: From where** is it derived **that matters of Torah are only retained by one who kills himself over it? As it is stated: "This is the Torah: When one dies in a tent"** (Numbers 19:14); true Torah study demands the total devotion of one who is willing to dedicate his life in the tent of Torah.

Another reading of *hasket*: *katetu atzmechem al divrei Torah* — grind yourselves over the words of Torah. Resh Lakish teaches: *minayin she-divrei Torah mitkaymin ela be-mi she-memit atzmo aleiha* — how do we know that Torah endures only in one who is willing to give his life for it? *Zot haTorah adam ki yamut be-ohel* — this is the Torah: a person who dies in the tent. This is, of course, figurative — though one recalls the *drasha* of Rabbi Akiva from just a few pages earlier, where the willingness to die for Torah found something close to literal expression.

### Listen Then Analyze

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״הַסְכֵּת וּשְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל״ — הַס, וְאַחַר כָּךְ כַּתֵּת. כִּדְרָבָא, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: לְעוֹלָם יִלְמוֹד אָדָם תּוֹרָה, וְאַחַר כָּךְ יֶהֱגֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers yet another **alternative explanation** of this verse: **"Keep silence [*hasket*] and hear, Israel";** first **be silent [*has*]** and listen and **then** study intensively in order to **analyze [*kattet*]** and clarify the details. This is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rava, as Rava said: One must always study Torah** and gain expertise in it, **and** only **then analyze** and delve into it.

A further reading of *hasket*: *has ve-achar koteitz* — first silence, then grinding; first listen, then analyze. Do not rush to ask questions. First absorb information, receive what is being transmitted, and only afterward begin to probe and analyze. This principle is affirmed by Rava: *le'olam yilmad adam Torah ve-achar kach yehgeh* — a person should always first learn Torah and only afterward contemplate it. This was particularly critical in an era of *Torah she-ba'al peh*, when enormous bodies of material had to be memorized orally before one possessed the raw material to reason from.

### Butter Torah Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״כִּי מִיץ חָלָב יוֹצִיא חֶמְאָה וּמִיץ אַף יוֹצִיא דָם וּמִיץ אַפַּיִם יוֹצִיא רִיב״?
>
> **Translation:** **In the school of Rabbi Yannai they said: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "For the churning of milk brings forth curd, and the wringing of the nose [*af*] brings forth blood, so the forcing of wrath [*appayim*] brings forth strife"** (Proverbs 30:33)?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמִי אַתָּה מוֹצֵא חֶמְאָה שֶׁל תּוֹרָה — בְּמִי שֶׁמֵּקִיא חָלָב שֶׁיָּנַק מִשְׁדֵּי אִמּוֹ עָלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the beginning of the verse: For the churning of milk brings forth curd; **in whom do you find the cream of Torah? With one who spits out the milk that he nursed from his mother's breasts over it;** one who struggles with all his might to study Torah.

The school of Rabbi Yannai expounds the verse *ki mitz chalav yotzi chemah, u-mitz af yotzi dam, u-mitz apayim yotzi riv* — the pressing of milk yields butter; the pressing of anger yields blood; the pressing of the face yields strife. *Bemi atah motzei chemah shel Torah* — where do you find the choice butter of Torah? *Bemi she-maki chalav she-yanak mi-shadei imo aleiha* — in one who sacrifices so completely that they spit out even the milk nursed from their mother's breast. Total self-giving — blood, sweat, and tears, in the idiom of our own day.

### Accepting Rebuke

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּמִיץ אַף יוֹצִיא דָם״ — כׇּל תַּלְמִיד שֶׁכּוֹעֵס עָלָיו רַבּוֹ פַּעַם רִאשׁוֹנָה וְשׁוֹתֵק — זוֹכֶה לְהַבְחִין בֵּין דָּם טָמֵא לְדָם טָהוֹר. ״וּמִיץ אַפַּיִם יוֹצִיא רִיב״ — כׇּל תַּלְמִיד שֶׁכּוֹעֵס עָלָיו רַבּוֹ פַּעַם רִאשׁוֹנָה וּשְׁנִיָּה וְשׁוֹתֵק — זוֹכֶה לְהַבְחִין בֵּין דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת לְדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת. דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: הָרוֹצֶה שֶׁיִּתְחַכֵּם — יַעֲסוֹק בְּדִינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת, שֶׁאֵין לָךְ מִקְצוֹעַ בַּתּוֹרָה יוֹתֵר מֵהֶן, שֶׁהֵן כְּמַעְיָן נוֹבֵעַ.
>
> **Translation:** As for: **And the wringing of the nose brings forth blood, any student whose rabbi is angry [*af*] with him the first time and he is silent** and does not react, **will merit** to be able **to distinguish between blood that is ritually impure and blood that is ritually pure.** With regard to: **And the forcing of wrath [*appayim*] brings forth strife; any student whose rabbi is angry with him for the first and second times,** *appayim* being the plural of *af*, **and he is silent, merits to distinguish between monetary cases,** strife, **and capital cases,** as that is the highest level of learning. **As we learned** in a mishna: **Rabbi Yishmael says: One who seeks to become wise should engage in monetary laws, as there is no greater discipline in Torah, as they are like a flowing well** in which innovations constantly spring forth.

*U-mitz af yotzi dam*: any student whose teacher becomes angry with him the first time and remains silent — *zocheh lehavchin bein dam tamei le-dam tahor* — merits to rule on questions of blood, *dam tamei* and *dam tahor*, and thus attains greatness in Torah. The pressing of anger, absorbed in silence, produces mastery. *U-mitz apayim yotzi riv*: if the teacher grows angry twice — *af* being singular, *apayim* being plural — and the student remains silent on both occasions, *zocheh lehavchin bein dinei mamonot le-dinei nefashot* — he merits the capacity to adjudicate between civil monetary cases and capital cases, the highest levels of *halakhic* knowledge. Rabbi Yishmael affirms the particular importance of *dinei mamonot*: *harotze she-yitchakem ya'asok b'dinei mamonot* — one who wishes to become wise should devote himself to monetary law, *she-ein lecha miktzoa baTorah yoter mehen* — for there is no branch of Torah more demanding or more like a flowing wellspring.

### Pride Torah Sacrifice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״אִם נָבַלְתָּ בְהִתְנַשֵּׂא וְאִם זַמּוֹתָ יָד לְפֶה״ — כׇּל הַמְנַבֵּל עַצְמוֹ עַל דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה — סוֹפוֹ לְהִתְנַשֵּׂא. וְאִם זָמַם — יָד לְפֶה.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "If you have done foolishly in lifting up yourself, or if you have planned devices [*zamota*], lay your hand over your mouth"** (Proverbs 30:32)? **Anyone who abases himself over matters of Torah,** asking questions despite the shame he feels for his ignorance, **will ultimately be exalted. And if he muzzles [*zamam*] himself** due to embarrassment, he will end up with his **hand over** his **mouth,** unable to answer.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Lachmani expounds: *im navalta behitnasei ve'im zamota yad lapeh* — if you have degraded yourself in the act of lifting yourself up, and if you have sealed your mouth, your hand will be placed over your mouth. *Kol hemenabel atzmo al divrei Torah* — whoever is willing to degrade himself over Torah, meaning to accept rebuke without flinching, to ask questions that may make him look foolish, to sacrifice his pride rather than stand on it — *sofo lehitnasei* — in the end he will be lifted up to Torah greatness. Conversely, if *zamota yad lapeh*, if he seals his mouth and refuses to ask, then he will eventually be forced to place his hand over his mouth in silence when others ask him questions he cannot answer.

One cannot help but notice that this sustained emphasis on self-sacrifice carries an undertone that resonates with *korbanot*. The power of sacrifice in the *mikdash*, after all, was precisely the giving over of what is most dear: the animal representing one's labor, even symbolically one's own life, offered wholly to God. Here, in the *beit midrash* that has replaced the *mikdash*, the same dynamic appears: sacrificing one's blood and sweat and pride in the act of *talmud Torah* is the new form of that total self-offering to God.

## Honoring Hosts

### Nechemia Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּתַח רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה בִּכְבוֹד אַכְסַנְיָא וְדָרַשׁ: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״וַיֹּאמֶר שָׁאוּל אֶל הַקֵּינִי לְכוּ סֻּרוּ רְדוּ מִתּוֹךְ עֲמָלֵקִי פֶּן אוֹסִפְךָ עִמּוֹ וְאַתָּה עָשִׂיתָה חֶסֶד עִם כׇּל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״? וַהֲלֹא דְּבָרִים קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה יִתְרוֹ שֶׁלֹּא קֵרַב אֶת מֹשֶׁה אֶלָּא לִכְבוֹד עַצְמוֹ — כָּךְ, הַמְאָרֵחַ תַּלְמִיד חָכָם בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, וּמַאֲכִילוֹ וּמַשְׁקֵהוּ וּמְהַנֵּהוּ מִנְּכָסָיו — עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the homilies offered by the Sages in the vineyard of Yavne. **Rabbi Neḥemya began** to speak **in honor of the hosts and taught: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "And Saul said unto the Kenites: Go, depart, get you down from among the Amalekites lest I destroy you with them, for you showed kindness to all the Children of Israel when they came up out of Egypt"** (I Samuel 15:6)? **Isn't this an *a fortiori* inference: Just as Jethro,** the forbearer of the Kenite tribe, who **only befriended Moses for his own honor,** is treated **in this way** and rewarded that his merit would protect his descendants; **all the more so** should **one who hosts a Torah scholar in his home, providing him with food and drink and availing him of his possessions,** be rewarded with that protection.

Having concluded Rabbi Yehuda's *drashot* on the honor of Torah and the methods of Torah learning, the *gemara* returns to the other theme of the gathering's opening: *poteach bikhvod achsanya*, honoring the host. Rabbi Nechemya expounds the verse *vayomer Shaul el haKeini, lechu suru redu mitoch Amaleki* — Saul said to the Kenite: go, withdraw from the midst of Amalek, lest I destroy you along with them, *v'ata asita chesed im kol Bnei Yisrael* — for you showed kindness to all the children of Israel when they came out of Egypt. The Kenites are descendants of Yitro, who welcomed Moshe.

The *kal vachomer* follows: *u'mah Yisro shelo kirev et Moshe ela likhvod atzmo* — Yitro welcomed Moshe only for his own honor (he was, after all, receiving his son-in-law, and it reflected well upon him). Even so, his descendants received this significant protection. *Hamareiach talmid chacham betoch beito u-ma'achilo u-mashkehu u-mehanehu minechasav* — how much more so one who genuinely hosts a Torah scholar in his home, feeds him, gives him drink, and allows him to benefit from his property — *al achas kama vekama* — how much greater will that person's reward be.

This statement is, of course, functionally an encouragement of the behavior being described. But in context — at a convocation in Yavneh where Torah sages have gathered for an extended period and people of the community are presumably providing for their needs — this is explicitly *bikhvod achsanya*: an expression of gratitude, framed as a statement about the reward that accrues to those doing exactly what the hosts are doing.

### Yosi Non-Jewish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּתַח רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בִּכְבוֹד אַכְסַנְיָא וְדָרַשׁ: ״לֹא תְתַעֵב אֲדֹמִי כִּי אָחִיךָ הוּא לֹא תְתַעֵב מִצְרִי כִּי גֵר הָיִיתָ בְאַרְצוֹ״, וַהֲלֹא דְּבָרִים קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה מִצְרִיִּים שֶׁלֹּא קֵרְבוּ אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶלָּא לְצוֹרֶךְ עַצְמָן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאִם יָדַעְתָּ וְיֶשׁ בָּם אַנְשֵׁי חַיִל וְשַׂמְתָּם שָׂרֵי מִקְנֶה עַל אֲשֶׁר לִי״ — כָּךְ, הַמְאָרֵחַ תַּלְמִיד חָכָם בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ וּמַאֲכִילוֹ וּמַשְׁקֵהוּ וּמְהַנֵּהוּ מִנְּכָסָיו — עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei began** to speak **in honor of the hosts, and taught:** It is said: **"You shall not abhor an Edomite, for he is your brother; you shall not abhor an Egyptian, because you were a stranger in his land"** (Deuteronomy 23:8). **Isn't this an *a fortiori* inference: Just as the Egyptians, who only befriended Israel,** even when they hosted them, **for their own benefit,** as Pharaoh said to Joseph, **as it is stated: "And if you know any able men among them, then make them rulers over my cattle"** (Genesis 47:6), are treated **this way, all the more so** should **one who hosts a Torah scholar in his home, providing him with food and drink and availing him of his possessions** without concern for personal gain, be treated this way.

Rabbi Yosi similarly opens with the honor of the host and expounds: *lo tisa'ev Adomi ki achicha hu; lo tisa'ev Mitzri ki ger hayita be'artzo* — do not despise the Edomite, for he is your brother; do not despise the Egyptian, for you were a sojourner in his land. The *kal vachomer*: *u'mah Mitzriyim shelo kirevu et Yisrael ela litzorech atzmam* — the Egyptians welcomed Israel only for their own benefit, as Pharaoh sought capable men to oversee his flocks — *kach*, and even so, they were granted this enduring protection. One who hosts a Torah scholar in his home and feeds and supports him — how much more will that person be rewarded.

### Oved Edom Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּתַח רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי בִּכְבוֹד אַכְסַנְיָא וְדָרַשׁ: ״וַיְבָרֶךְ ה׳ אֶת עֹבֵד אֱדֹם הַגִּתִּי בַּעֲבוּר אֲרוֹן הָאֱלֹהִים״, וַהֲלֹא דְּבָרִים קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה אָרוֹן שֶׁלֹּא אָכַל וְשָׁתָה, אֶלָּא כִּבֵּד וְרִבֵּץ לְפָנָיו — כָּךְ, הַמְאָרֵחַ תַּלְמִיד חָכָם בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, וּמַאֲכִילוֹ וּמַשְׁקֵהוּ וּמְהַנֵּהוּ מִנְּכָסָיו — עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer, son of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, began** to speak **in honor of the hosts, and taught:** It is stated: **"The Lord has blessed the house of Oved-edom…because of the ark of God"** (II Samuel 6:12). **Isn't this an *a fortiori* inference: Just as in reward for honoring the ark, which neither ate nor drank, but before which** Oved-edom simply **swept and sprinkled** water to settle the dust, he was treated **this way** and merited a blessing, **all the more so** should **one who hosts a Torah scholar in his home, providing him with food and drink and availing him of his possessions** without concern for his personal gain, be rewarded with such a blessing.

Rabbi Elazar son of Rabbi Yosi HaGlili offers a still more compelling example: *vayivarech Hashem et Oved Edom ba'avur aron haElokim* — God blessed Oved Edom on account of the *aron* of God that was in his house, kept there prior to its being brought up to Yerushalayim. Oved Edom was a non-Jew. The *kal vachomer* is elegant: *u'mah aron shelo achal veshata ela kibed veribetz lefanav* — what did Oved Edom have to do for the *aron*? He did not have to feed it or give it drink; he simply swept and kept the space clean. And for that, God blessed him abundantly. *Hamareiach talmid chacham betoch beito* — how much more so one who hosts a Torah scholar and actively provides for him.

What gives this particular *drasha* its special power is that it ties together both major themes of the passage. It is simultaneously an expression of *bikhvod achsanya* — here is the extraordinary reward for hosting Torah sages — and an identification of the Torah scholar with the *aron* itself. The honor shown to the *aron* as the vessel of Torah and divine presence is now shown to the *talmid chacham*; and the reward for hosting the *talmid chacham* exceeds even that given to Oved Edom, because the *talmid chacham* requires active care and nourishment. This is Torah and *talmud Torah* replacing the *mikdash* as the center of *kedushah*: the living bearer of Torah occupies the place once held by the sacred vessel.

### Sextuplets Reward

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הִיא בְּרָכָה שֶׁבֵּרְכוֹ? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה בַּר זְבִידָא: זוֹ חָמוֹת וּשְׁמוֹנֶה כַּלּוֹתֶיהָ שֶׁיָּלְדוּ שִׁשָּׁה שִׁשָּׁה בְּכָרֵס אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** that **blessing with which** Oved-edom **was blessed? Rav Yehuda bar Zevida said: This is Ḥamot and her eight daughters-in-law, each of whom bore six in a single womb,**

What precisely was the blessing given to Oved Edom? Rav Yehuda bar Zvida teaches: *zu chamot ushmona kaloteha* — his mother-in-law and his eight daughters-in-law, *sheyaldu shisha shisha beveten achat* — each one gave birth to sextuplets in a single pregnancy. This is derived from the verse *pe'ulati hashmini*, which gives the name of his eighth son, and from *ki beracho Elokim* — for God blessed him — and from the count in Chronicles of sixty-two descendants of Oved Edom. The arithmetic: nine women (his wife and eight daughters-in-law) each bearing six children yields fifty-four, plus his eight sons gives sixty-two.

## Leadership Order

### Pushing Hour

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין הַלֵּוִי: כׇּל הַדּוֹחֵק אֶת הַשָּׁעָה — שָׁעָה דּוֹחַקְתּוֹ. וְכׇל הַנִּדְחֶה מִפְּנֵי הַשָּׁעָה — שָׁעָה נִדְחֵת מִפָּנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Avin HaLevi said: If one forces the moment** and attempts to take advantage of an undeserved opportunity, the **moment forces him** and he is pushed aside. **If one yields to the moment** and relinquishes an opportunity that presents itself, the **moment yields to him.**

Rabbi Avin HaLevi now offers several teachings, beginning with a principle about timing and leadership: *kol hadochek et hashaah shaah dochakto* — whoever pushes the hour, the hour pushes him aside. *Vekol hanidcheh mipnei shaah shaah nidcheit mipanav* — whoever allows himself to be set aside because of the demands of the moment, the moment in the end yields to him; he rises to greatness.

### Sinai vs Mountains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּרַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף. דְּרַב יוֹסֵף סִינַי וְרַבָּה עוֹקֵר הָרִים, אִצְטְרִיכָא לְהוּ שַׁעְתָּא. שְׁלַחוּ לְהָתָם: סִינַי וְעוֹקֵר הָרִים, אֵיזֶה מֵהֶם קוֹדֵם? שְׁלַחוּ לְהוּ: סִינַי קוֹדֵם, שֶׁהַכֹּל צְרִיכִין לְמָרֵי חִטַּיָּא. אַף עַל פִּי כֵן לֹא קִבֵּל עָלָיו רַב יוֹסֵף, דְּאָמְרִי לֵיהּ כַּלְדָּאֵי: מָלְכַתְּ תַּרְתֵּין שְׁנִין.
>
> **Translation:** This may be derived **from** an incident involving **Rabba and Rav Yosef, as Rav Yosef** was **Sinai,** extremely erudite, **and Rabba** was **one who uproots mountains,** extremely sharp. **The moment arrived when they were needed;** one of them was to be chosen as head of the yeshiva. **They sent** the following question **there,** to the Sages of Eretz Yisrael: **Which takes precedence, Sinai or one who uproots mountains? They sent to them** in response: **Sinai takes precedence,** for **everyone needs the owner of the wheat,** one who is expert in the sources. **Nevertheless, Rav Yosef did not accept** the appointment, **as the Chaldean** astrologers **told him: You will preside** as head of the yeshiva for **two years.**

This principle is illustrated from the relationship between Rabbah and Rav Yosef. *De-Rav Yosef Sinai ve-Rabbah okeir harim* — Rav Yosef was like Har Sinai: he possessed comprehensive mastery of transmitted Torah, of *mesorah*, of the facts. Rabbah was the uprooter of mountains: he could analyze, reason, and apply critical method to the received material. These represent two distinct types of Torah mastery — the first, broad and thorough knowledge of what has been transmitted; the second, the analytical power to derive and generate from that knowledge. This maps, one notes, directly onto the principle stated earlier: first listen and absorb, then analyze.

### Sinai Precedence

When a *rosh yeshiva* was needed, they sent a question to Eretz Yisrael: *Sinai ve-okeir harim eizeh mehem kodem* — which takes precedence? The answer came back: *Sinai kodem, shehakol tzerichin lemarei chtaya* — Sinai comes first, because everyone requires the one who owns the grain. You cannot grind wheat you do not have; you cannot analyze Torah material you have not first received and mastered. For the purposes of leading the *yeshiva*, the one who possesses comprehensive transmitted knowledge must come first. That said, this judgment pertains specifically to the question of institutional leadership, not necessarily to where any individual should direct his own learning energies — those are separate questions.

### Yosef Refusal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְלַךְ רַבָּה עֶשְׂרִין וְתַרְתֵּין שְׁנִין, מְלַךְ רַב יוֹסֵף תַּרְתֵּין שְׁנִין וּפַלְגָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba presided** as head of the yeshiva for **twenty-two years.** After he died, **Rav Yosef presided** for **two and a half years.** Though he did not take advantage of the opportunity that presented itself, he ultimately fulfilled that for which he was destined.

Even so — *afilu hachi lo kabil aleih Rav Yosef* — Rav Yosef refused to accept the position. *De-amri leh Kalda'ei* — Chaldean seers had told him *maloch tartin shnin* — he would lead for only two years before dying. Unwilling to accept that fate, he declined the post.

### Predicted Fate

The result: *malach Rabbah esrin ve-tarmin shnin* — Rabba served as *rosh yeshiva* for twenty-two years. *U-levasof malach Rav Yosef tartin shnin u-palga* — and in the end Rav Yosef served for two and a half years before he died. So the prediction of the seers was ultimately fulfilled, whether or not Rav Yosef intended to circumvent it. One notes, not without irony, that Rav Yosef — the *Sinai*, the master of received knowledge — later became ill and forgot his Torah. Whether that illness preceded or followed his time as *rosh yeshiva* is unclear; it is a pointed observation nonetheless.

### Yosef Humility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּל הָנָךְ שְׁנֵי דִּמְלַךְ רַבָּה, אֲפִילּוּ אוּמָּנָא לְבֵיתֵיהּ לָא קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **all those years that Rabba presided,** Rav Yosef **did not even call a bloodletter to his home.** Rav Yosef did not assume even the slightest air of authority, and would go to seek out the bloodletter rather than call upon the bloodletter to accommodate him.

Moreover, *ve-chol hanach shnei de-malach Rabbah afilu umna leveiti lo kara* — throughout all twenty-two years that Rabbah led, Rav Yosef did not so much as summon a physician for a house call. He refused any display of status or authority that might in any way compromise Rabbah's standing. The principle is illustrated in its full force: stepping aside is not merely a passive act; it demands ongoing active self-effacement.

## Rabbi Avin Statements

### Parting Peace

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין הַלֵּוִי: הַנִּפְטָר מֵחֲבֵרוֹ אַל יֹאמַר לוֹ ״לֵךְ בְּשָׁלוֹם״, אֶלָּא ״לֵךְ לְשָׁלוֹם״, שֶׁהֲרֵי יִתְרוֹ שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ לְמֹשֶׁה ״לֵךְ לְשָׁלוֹם״, עָלָה וְהִצְלִיחַ. דָּוִד שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ לְאַבְשָׁלוֹם ״לֵךְ בְּשָׁלוֹם״, הָלַךְ וְנִתְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Avin HaLevi said: One who takes leave from another should not say to him: Go in peace, but rather,** he should say: **Go to peace. As we see** that, on the one hand, **Jethro said to Moses: "Go to peace"** (Exodus 4:18), and **Moses ascended and was successful.** On the other hand, **David said to** his son, **Absalom: "Go in peace"** (II Samuel 15:9), and Absalom **went and was** ultimately **hanged.**

Rabbi Avin HaLevi teaches: *haniftar mechavero al yomar lo lech beshalom ela lech l'shalom* — one who takes leave of a living person should not say *lech beshalom*, go with peace, but rather *lech l'shalom*, go toward peace. This is derived from Yitro, who said *lech l'shalom* to Moshe — and Moshe went and succeeded. David, by contrast, said to Avshalom *lech beshalom* — go with peace — and Avshalom went and was hanged. The implicit logic: we never fully arrive at peace in this life; life is always a process of striving *toward* peace, toward something not yet achieved. To say *beshalom*, with peace, implies a completeness that belongs only to the dead.

### Beam Owner

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין הַלֵּוִי: מִכָּאן לְבַעַל הַקּוֹרָה שֶׁיִּכָּנֵס בְּעׇבְיָהּ שֶׁל קוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Avin HaLevi said: From here** the general principle is derived: **One who owns a beam should approach** carrying **the thick portion of the beam.** A builder, carrying a beam in order to affix it in a building, must calibrate it and measure carefully so that the thick part of the beam fits in its place; so too must Jacob, who fathered and raised the children who would become the people of Israel, continue to pray for them and complete the task of nation-building.

An additional teaching from Rabbi Avin HaLevi: *mikan le-ba'al hakorah she-yikanes be-ovya shel korah* — the owner of the beam is the one who must enter under the thickness of the beam; the one who is responsible must bear the full weight of the burden. This is the explanation for why the verse invokes the *Elohei Yaakov* — the God of Jacob — rather than the God of Abraham or Isaac. It was Yaakov who raised the twelve sons from whom all of *Bnei Yisrael* descend; *Avraham veYitzchak* did not transmit their legacy to all their children. Yaakov bears the full responsibility of the family and so he is the one who must carry that weight — and it is therefore the *Elohei Yaakov* who comes to our protection when we are in distress.

### Meal Divine Presence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין הַלֵּוִי: כׇּל הַנֶּהֱנֶה מִסְּעוּדָה שֶׁתַּלְמִיד חָכָם שָׁרוּי בְּתוֹכָהּ, כְּאִילּוּ נֶהֱנֶה מִזִּיו שְׁכִינָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיָּבֹא אַהֲרֹן וְכֹל זִקְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל לֶאֱכׇל לֶחֶם עִם חֹתֵן מֹשֶׁה לִפְנֵי הָאֱלֹהִים״, וְכִי לִפְנֵי אֱלֹהִים אָכְלוּ? וַהֲלֹא לִפְנֵי מֹשֶׁה אָכְלוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Avin HaLevi said: One who partakes of a meal at which a Torah scholar is present, it is as if he enjoyed the radiant splendor of the Divine Presence, as it is stated: "And Aaron came, and all the elders of Israel, to eat bread with Moses' father-in-law before God"** (Exodus 18:12). **Did they** actually **eat before God? Didn't they eat before Moses?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לוֹמַר לָךְ כׇּל הַנֶּהֱנֶה מִסְּעוּדָה שֶׁתַּלְמִיד חָכָם שָׁרוּי בְּתוֹכָהּ — כְּאִילּוּ נֶהֱנֶה מִזִּיו שְׁכִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** this verse comes **to tell you** that **one who partakes in a meal at which a Torah scholar is present, it is as if he enjoyed the radiant splendor of the Divine Presence.**

*Kol haneheneh mis'udah she-talmid chacham sharuy betochah* — anyone who benefits from a meal at which a *talmid chacham* is present — *ke'ilu neheneh miziv hashchinah* — it is as though he has benefited from the radiance of the *shekhinah*. The proof text: *vayavo Aharon ve-chol ziknei Yisrael le'echol lechem im choten Moshe lifnei haElohim* — Aharon and all the elders of Israel came to eat bread with Moshe's father-in-law before God. But did they literally eat before God? Did they not eat before Moshe? The *gemara* teaches: *ella lomar lach*, this comes to tell you that anyone who benefits from a meal at which a *talmid chacham* is present is as one who has benefited from the *ziv hashchinah*. The connection between hosting a Torah scholar at one's table and experiencing the divine presence is thus made explicit — and the act of eating together with the *talmid chacham* is assimilated to the eating of *korbanot* in the *mikdash*, now replaced by this new form of sacred encounter.

### Yitro Avshalom

One notices that the same *potach bikhvod achsanya* also appears here in Rabbi Avin's teaching on leave-taking. Yitro saying *lech l'shalom* to Moshe — the same Yitro whose hospitality was praised throughout this section — yielded success; David's *lech beshalom* to Avshalom brought catastrophe. Two blessings, two outcomes, separated by a single letter.

## Final Vision

### Synagogue to Study

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי בַּר חִיָּיא: הַיּוֹצֵא מִבֵּית הַכְּנֶסֶת וְנִכְנָס לְבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ וְעוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה — זוֹכֶה וּמְקַבֵּל פְּנֵי שְׁכִינָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״יֵלְכוּ מֵחַיִל אֶל חָיִל יֵרָאֶה אֶל אֱלֹהִים בְּצִיּוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Levi bar Ḥiyya said: One who leaves the synagogue and** immediately **enters the study hall and engages in Torah** study, **is privileged to receive the Divine Presence, as it is stated: "They go from strength to strength, every one of them appears before God in Zion"** (Psalms 84:8); those who go from a place of prayer to a place of Torah study are privileged to receive a divine revelation in Zion.

Rabbi Levi bar Chiya teaches: *hayotze mibeit hakeneset ve-nichnas le-veit hamidrash ve-osek baTorah* — one who leaves the synagogue and enters the study hall and engages in Torah — *zocheh u-mekabel pnei shekhinah* — merits to receive the divine presence — *shenemar yelchu mechayil el chayil yera'eh el Elohim beTziyon* — as it is written: they go from strength to strength, they will see God in Zion.

This statement is the culmination of everything the *gemara* has been building toward. The *tefillah* matters; *davening* is the beginning of the day's sacred work. But it is the first step, not the final destination. When you leave the synagogue and go to the *beit midrash* and immerse yourself in *talmud Torah*, that is when the encounter with God takes place — that is when *yera'eh el Elohim beTziyon* is fulfilled. And the *Tziyon* in question is no longer only the geographic Tziyon; it is the *beit midrash*, the new center of *kedushah* in the post-*mikdash* world.

### Torah Eternal Growth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר רַב: תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים אֵין לָהֶם מְנוּחָה לֹא בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה וְלֹא בָּעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יֵלְכוּ מֵחַיִל אֶל חָיִל יֵרָאֶה אֶל אֱלֹהִים בְּצִיּוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to that same verse, **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Ashi said** that **Rav said: Torah scholars have rest neither in this world nor in the World-to-Come,** as in both worlds they are constantly progressing, **as it is stated: "They go from strength to strength, every one of them appears before God in Zion."**

Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi said in the name of Rav: *talmidei chachamim ein lahem menuchah* — Torah scholars have no rest, *lo ba'olam hazeh ve-lo ba'olam haba* — neither in this world nor in the next. *Yelchu mechayil el chayil* — they go from strength to strength, always pressing forward, always reaching toward something not yet grasped. The *gemara* thus affirms that *talmud Torah* is not a destination but a process — a process of perpetual growth that does not end even with death.

### Peace World Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים מַרְבִּים שָׁלוֹם בָּעוֹלָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְכׇל בָּנַיִךְ לִמּוּדֵי ה׳ וְרַב שְׁלוֹם בָּנָיִךְ״. אַל תִּקְרֵי ״בָּנָיִךְ״ אֶלָּא ״בּוֹנָיִךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said** that **Rabbi Ḥanina said: Torah scholars increase peace in the world, as it is said: "And all your children [*banayikh*] shall be taught of the Lord, and great shall be the peace of your children"** (Isaiah 54:13). If all the children of Israel are taught of the Lord, there will be peace for all. The Sages interpreted this verse homiletically: **Do not read your children [*banayikh*], but your builders [*bonayikh*].** Torah scholars are those who build peace for their generation.

Rabbi Elazar said in the name of Rabbi Chanina: *talmidei chachamim marbim shalom ba'olam* — Torah scholars increase peace in the world — *she-nemar ve-chol banayich limudei Hashem ve-rav shalom banayich* — as it says: all your children are taught of Hashem, and great is the peace of your children. *Al tikrei banayich ella bonayich* — do not read "your children" but "your builders." Torah scholars are the builders of the world, and what they build is *shalom*.

### Ending Shalom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שָׁלוֹם רָב לְאֹהֲבֵי תוֹרָתֶךָ וְאֵין לָמוֹ מִכְשׁוֹל״. ״יְהִי שָׁלוֹם בְּחֵילֵךְ שַׁלְוָה בְּאַרְמְנוֹתָיִךְ״. ״לְמַעַן אַחַי וְרֵעָי אֲדַבְּרָה נָּא שָׁלוֹם בָּךְ. לְמַעַן בֵּית ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ אֲבַקְשָׁה טוֹב לָךְ״. ״ה׳ עֹז לְעַמּוֹ יִתֵּן ה׳ יְבָרֵךְ אֶת עַמּוֹ בַשָּׁלוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** As it is stated: **"Those who love Your Torah have great peace; there is no stumbling block for them"** (Psalms 119:165); and **"May there be peace within your walls, prosperity within your palaces"** (Psalms 122:7), because: **"For the sake of my brothers and friends, I shall say: Peace be within you. For the sake of the House of the Lord, our God, I will seek your good"** (Psalms 122:8–9), and **"May the Lord give strength to His people; the Lord will bless His people with peace"** (Psalms 29:11).

The passage closes with a cascade of verses centered on *shalom*: *shalom rav le'ohavei toratecha ve'ein lamo michshol; yehi shalom becheileich; shalva be'armenotayich; lema'an achai vere'ai adabra na shalom bach; lema'an beit Hashem Elokeinu avaksha tov lach; Hashem oz le'amo yiten Hashem yevarech et amo bashalom*. That the *masechet* ends with *shalom* is fitting: the *berachah* of *shemoneh esrei* ends with *shalom*; *birkat kohanim* ends with *shalom*; numerous *berachot* throughout the tractate conclude with *shalom*. But here every mention of *shalom* flows from *talmidei chachamim* and from *Torah*.

The contrast with the *mishnah*'s conclusion is instructive and not accidental. The *mishnah* ended with Boaz in the field greeting his workers with *shem hashem* — bringing *kedushah* into the everyday through liturgical greeting, through *berachot*, through the sanctification of the mundane. The *gemara* ends differently. Its final vision is of Yavneh as the new *Tziyon*, of the *beit midrash* as the new *mikdash*, of *talmud Torah* as the encounter with God that the *avodah* once provided, and of Torah scholars as the builders of peace in a world that has lost its center. *Torah*, not merely *tefillah*; *talmud Torah*, not merely liturgy — this is where the *gemara* ultimately stakes its claim about what sustains *kedushah* in the aftermath of destruction.

*Hadran alach Berakhot ve-slika lach masechet.*
