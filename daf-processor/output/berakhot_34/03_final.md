# Berakhot 34 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Modim & Dualism

### Modim Dualism

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ (הָאוֹמֵר: ״יְבָרְכוּךָ טוֹבִים״ — הֲרֵי זֶה דַּרְכֵי מִינוּת). הָעוֹבֵר לִפְנֵי הַתֵּיבָה וְטָעָה — יַעֲבוֹר אַחֵר תַּחְתָּיו, וְלֹא יְהֵא סָרְבָן בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה. מֵהֵיכָן הוּא מַתְחִיל? מִתְּחִלַּת הַבְּרָכָה שֶׁטָּעָה זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **(If one says: "May the good bless You," this is a path of heresy.) One who is passing before the ark,** as prayer leader, **and erred, another should** immediately **pass in his place, and at that moment,** this replacement **should not refuse** in the interest of courtesy. The *Amida* prayer was interrupted and he should replace him as quickly as possible. **From where does** the replacement **commence? From the beginning of the blessing in which** the former **had erred.**

Berakhot 34 is the final daf of this *perek* and of the first half of Masechet Berakhot, which has dealt throughout with *Shema* and *Shemoneh Esrei*. Yesterday's discussion concluded with the problem of saying *Modim Modim* — repeating the word *modim* twice — which raises the concern of dualism, as though one were acknowledging two divine powers. A *Tosafot* at the top of 34a is both generally relevant and particularly apt for this time of year.

*Tosafot* raises the question of *amar psuka psuka vekafla* — whether it is worse to repeat each word or each *pasuk* — and then, a few lines below, addresses a related practice: *ve-otam bnei adam she-omrim beis o gimmel pe'amim Shema Yisrael be-Yom HaKippurim*, those people who actually repeat *Shema Yisrael Hashem Elokeinu Hashem Echad* multiple times. This apparently occurred in certain contexts — perhaps when the *Sifrei Torah* are taken out — though the precise occasion is not entirely clear. *Tosafot* rules, following *Rashi*, that *meshaskin otam*, one silences them, since *Rashi* understands the entire problem to be the repetition of the *pasuk* itself. Even according to the reading of *Rabbenu Chananel*, where one would not technically silence them, the practice is still *meguna*, unseemly and to be looked down upon.

*Tosafot* then considers a possible defense: *shema lo chashiv ela kshe-koreh Krias Shema be-onatah*, perhaps the concern applies only in the context of the *mitzvah* of *Krias Shema*, where the focus is *kabalas ol malchus shamayim* — accepting the yoke of heaven — and where the sensitivity to any appearance of dualism is therefore heightened. Outside that specific *mitzvah* context, repeating a *pasuk* might be less problematic. Even so, *Tosafot* concludes: *u-mikol makom tov shelo lomar* — in any case, the safe course is not to repeat.

However, *Tosafot* explicitly defends the practice of saying *Hashem Hu HaElokim* seven times on *Yom Kippur* and *Yom HaArava* — that is, *Hoshana Rabba*. The explanation is *keneged zayin reki'im*, corresponding to the seven heavens, praising God who dwells above all seven. Moreover, *Hashem Hu HaElokim* appears twice in the same *pasuk* in the narrative of Elijah, lending it further scriptural grounding. So *Hashem Hu HaElokim Hashem Hu HaElokim* is a *minhag kasher*, a legitimate practice — while repeating *Shema Yisrael* is not. The distinction *Tosafot* draws is important: the concern about repeating *psukim* is not an absolute prohibition against all repetition, but one that is tied specifically to the dualism concern, and perhaps even more narrowly to the *mitzvah* context of *Krias Shema* itself.

A related question — about repeating individual words within a *pasuk* due to the *niggun* — touches less on dualism concerns and more on whether doing so constitutes a *hefsek* in the *davening* or a corruption of the *pasuk*. That is a separate discussion left for another occasion.

### Yevarchucha Tovim

The first line of the *mishna* on 34a appears in parentheses: *ha-omer yevarchucha tovim harei zeh derech haminus* — one who says "let the good bless You" is following the way of heresy, often associated with certain Hebrew Christian practices. This line is bracketed because it does not appear in our standard text of the *mishna* and is absent from the *Yerushalmi* as well. It does, however, appear in the parallel *mishna* in Masechet Megillah, where the passage about *al kan tzipor yagiu rachamecha* — "Your compassion extends to the bird's nest" — raises related concerns about *ta'amei ha-mitzvos* and the proper framing of divine attributes. That *mishna* in Megillah includes this additional line. The underlying concern with *yevarchucha tovim* may again be a form of dualism — as though only the good praise the good God, implying a separate evil divinity receives the praise of the wicked.

## Shliach Tzibur Error

### Error Replacement

After the parenthetical, the *mishna* moves to a new topic: *ha-over lifnei hateiva veta'ah* — someone who leads the *davening* and makes an error. The *mishna* rules *ya'avor acher tachtav*, another person should take his place — even mid-*Shemoneh Esrei*. This could apply in the case where *meshaskin oto*, where we feel it necessary to remove the person; but as *Rashi* and *Tosafot* explain, it may simply be that the *shliach tzibbur* became so confused that he is incapable of continuing, and someone else must take over. *Shemoneh Esrei* mid-*tefillah* and all.

The *mishna* adds: *v'lo yehei sarvan b'ota sha'ah* — when you are asked at that moment of crisis to step in and replace someone, do not stage a show of modesty by saying "No, no, find someone else." The *tzibbur* is embarrassed; it is inappropriate for the *tzibbur* to be waiting without the *Shemoneh Esrei* continuing. If the *gabbai* calls on you, accept.

### Restart Point

*Me'heichan hu matchil?* From where does the replacement begin? *Mi-techilat ha-brachah she-ta'ah* — from the beginning of the *brachah* in which the original *shliach tzibbur* erred or became so confused he could not continue. If he was in the middle of *Atah Chonen*, the replacement begins from *Atah Chonen*; if he faltered in *Shomea Tefillah*, the replacement starts there. One does not go all the way to the beginning of *Shemoneh Esrei*, nor does one resume from the precise word at which the previous leader stopped. The unit is the *brachah*.

This reading — that the case is one of confusion rather than textual error per se — is how *Rashi* and *Tosafot* understand the context, since the *mishna* uses the word *ta'ah*, which normally implies error, not inability to continue. The contextual reading is nonetheless well-founded given what follows.

### Amen Distraction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָעוֹבֵר לִפְנֵי הַתֵּיבָה לֹא יַעֲנֶה ״אָמֵן״ אַחַר הַכֹּהֲנִים, מִפְּנֵי הַטֵּרוּף. וְאִם אֵין שָׁם כֹּהֵן אֶלָּא הוּא — לֹא יִשָּׂא אֶת כַּפָּיו. וְאִם הַבְטָחָתוֹ שֶׁהוּא נוֹשֵׂא אֶת כַּפָּיו, וְחוֹזֵר לִתְפִלָּתוֹ — רַשַּׁאי.
>
> **Translation:** **One who passes before the ark should not respond amen after the** blessing of the **priests, because of** potential **confusion.** Since the mishna is describing a situation in which he was praying without a prayer book, responding amen would interrupt the order of the prayer and potentially lead him to begin a different blessing. For this reason, even **if there is no priest other than** the communal prayer leader, **he does not lift his hands** to bless the people, lest he become confused. **And,** however, **if he is certain that he can lift his hands and resume his prayer** without becoming confused, **he is permitted** to recite the blessing.

The *mishna* then addresses a distinct ruling about the *shliach tzibbur*: *ha-over lifnei hateiva lo ya'aneh amen achar ha-kohanim mipnei ha-tiruf* — the *shliach tzibbur* should not answer *amen* after the *kohanim* during *Birchas Kohanim*, because of the concern of *tiruf*, distraction or confusion. It is difficult to imagine how answering *amen* could so thoroughly confuse a person, but the concern is that if he is not *davening* from a *siddur*, answering *amen* shifts his mental focus, and he risks being unable to return smoothly to *Sim Shalom* after *Birchas Kohanim* is complete. If he does have a *siddur* in front of him, the concern presumably does not apply to the same degree — a point the *mishna* will address shortly.

It is worth pausing on the broader assumption embedded here. The *mishna* takes for granted that *Birchas Kohanim* is performed daily — which is the current *minhag Sefardim* and the practice assumed throughout the *Gemara* and all the *mishnayos*. *Tosafot* and the *Rama* must therefore defend the *minhag Ashkenaz* of limiting *Birchas Kohanim* to the *regalim*. Their basic answer — the *Rama*'s in particular — is that *Birchas Kohanim* is associated with *simcha* and the joy of blessing *am Yisrael be-ahava*, and that given the ongoing persecution and economic hardship of Jewish life in Ashkenazic lands, the sense of *simcha* required was absent except on *yom tov*, which itself carries the added association of *simchat ha-regel* and the original context of the priestly blessing in the *Beis HaMikdash*. This reasoning is not in the *Gemara*; the *Gemara* assumes daily *Birchas Kohanim*. What *Tosafot* is doing — and this is characteristic of the *Tosafists* broadly — is not advocating a change of practice, but reconciling an established grassroots reality with the *Gemara*'s expectations.

A parallel can be noted: the *Ashkenazim* similarly do not recite *Shehecheyanu* at a *bris milah*, while *Sefardim* do. The answer given is along similar lines — that the infant's pain makes it inappropriate. These patterns of constriction in expressions of joy likely reflect the lived reality of centuries of *Ashkenazi* life under conditions of oppression more severe than those generally experienced under Islamic rule. That historical asymmetry appears to have had genuine liturgical consequences.

The *mishna* also takes for granted that if other *kohanim* are present, the *shliach tzibbur* who is a *kohen* does not participate in *Birchas Kohanim* — because it would distract him. This despite the fact that other *Gemaros* indicate that a *kohen* who is present and does not *duchen* violates the obligation implied by *koh tevarchu et Benei Yisrael*. Evidently the *mishna* accepts that when both roles cannot be fulfilled simultaneously without distraction, the *shliach tzibbur* is excused from *Birchas Kohanim*. *Tosafot* on the question of whether the *shliach tzibbur* calls out *Kohanim* or prompts each word — *yevarechecha* and so on — explores the extent to which his role in *Birchas Kohanim* is itself a potential source of distraction or interruption, arriving at several positions on the matter.

### Sole Kohen Exception

*V'im ein sham kohen ela hu* — if the *shliach tzibbur* is the only *kohen* present, the *mishna* rules *lo yissa kapav*: he should not *duchen* at all. So great is the concern that he will lose his focus that *Birchas Kohanim* is simply skipped entirely rather than risk the *shliach tzibbur* becoming so confused he cannot continue. *Im havtachato she-hu nosei et kapav ve-chozer li-tefillato* — only if he is personally confident in his ability to complete *Birchas Kohanim* and return seamlessly to his role as *shliach tzibbur* is he permitted to do so. The closing cases thus illuminate what the *mishna*'s concern throughout this passage has been: that someone becomes so confused they cannot continue — which in turn explains *Rashi* and *Tosafot*'s reading of the earlier cases.

## Refusal Protocol

### Three Asks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָעוֹבֵר לִפְנֵי הַתֵּיבָה — צָרִיךְ לְסָרֵב. וְאִם אֵינוֹ מְסָרֵב — דּוֹמֶה לְתַבְשִׁיל שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מֶלַח. וְאִם מְסָרֵב יוֹתֵר מִדַּאי — דּוֹמֶה לְתַבְשִׁיל שֶׁהִקְדִּיחַתּוּ מֶלַח. כֵּיצַד הוּא עוֹשֶׂה: פַּעַם רִאשׁוֹנָה — יְסָרֵב, שְׁנִיָּה — מְהַבְהֵב, שְׁלִישִׁית — פּוֹשֵׁט אֶת רַגְלָיו וְיוֹרֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** One who is approached **to pass before the ark** to serve as prayer leader, for the sake of propriety **should refuse,** to avoid creating the impression that he is too eager. **And if he does not refuse,** but jumps at the opportunity, **he is like cooked food without salt,** which is to say that he acts in bad taste. **However, if he refuses too much** this is similarly inappropriate, as **he is like cooked food that was ruined by** too much **salt.** So **how should he act?** The appropriate conduct when approached to serve as communal prayer leader is as follows: When approached **the first time, one should refuse; the second** time, one should **vacillate** like a wick that has just begun to catch a flame but is not yet burning; and **the third** time, **he should stretch his legs and descend** before the ark.

A *baraita* is introduced: *over lifnei hateiva tzarich lesarev* — one who is asked to lead the *davening* must initially refuse. "No, no, don't pick me, ask someone else, I'm not suited for it." *V'im eino mesarev* — if he does not refuse and says, "Great, you want me as *shliach tzibbur*? Wonderful!" — *domeh le-tavshil she-ein bo melach*, he is like a stew with no salt. There is a tastelessness to such eagerness, a lack of refinement and *derech eretz*. One should not be the first to rush toward a position of *kavod*.

However, *v'im mesarev yoter midai* — if he refuses excessively, genuinely and repeatedly declining when the *tzibbur* actually needs him — *domeh le-tavshil she-hiktikhato melach*, he is like a stew that has been ruined by too much salt. At least the tasteless stew is still edible; the over-salted one is not. If the *tzibbur* needs you, that need must ultimately be met.

*Keitzad hu oseh?* So what is the proper procedure? *Pa'am rishonah yesarev* — the first time you are asked, you decline. *Sheniyah mehavhev* — the second time, you begin to stir, to indicate some openness. *Shlishit poshet et raglav ve-tiyored* — the third time you extend your legs and go down to lead. The *Meiri* understands *mehavhev* not as a verbal "maybe" but as a physical gesture of readiness — you signal that you are prepared to go if asked again.

In practical terms, in a *minyan* where people know the procedure, this three-step dance can become formalized and somewhat pro forma — the *gabbai* asks, you decline, he asks again, you gesture openness, he asks a third time, you accept. If the situation is genuinely urgent, however, as the *mishna* above makes clear, that ritual is set aside entirely: you simply go.

### Derech Eretz

This protocol reflects a broader *derech eretz* principle: a person of quality does not present themselves as eager for positions of honor. The initial refusal is not insincere — it is an expression of genuine humility and an acknowledgment that perhaps someone else would be more suitable. But that same *derech eretz* demands that the refusal not be carried to the point of dysfunction. As with many expressions of courtesy, the procedure is understood by all parties involved and serves a real social and spiritual function even when its mechanics are known to everyone — much as expressions of gratitude remain meaningful even when their reciprocation is predictable.

### Three Moderations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה רוּבָּן — קָשֶׁה, וּמִיעוּטָן — יָפֶה, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: שְׂאוֹר, וּמֶלַח, וְסָרְבָנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** There are **three** things that **are harmful in excess but are beneficial when used sparingly.** They are: **Leavening** in dough, **salt** in a cooked dish **and refusal** for the sake of propriety.

A second *baraita* is then cited as a general principle: *shlosha ruban kasha u-miutan yafe* — three things where an abundance is harmful but a small amount is pleasant and appropriate. They are *se'or*, *melach*, and *sarvanut* — sourdough, salt, and refusal. A small amount of sourdough makes good bread; too much ruins it. A little salt enhances flavor; too much makes food inedible. And refusal: a modest initial reluctance is *derech eretz*, an expression of proper character — but too much refusal prevents the *tzibbur*'s needs from being met.

## Errors & Returns

### Rav Huna Units

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: טָעָה בְּשָׁלֹשׁ רִאשׁוֹנוֹת — חוֹזֵר לָרֹאשׁ. בְּאֶמְצָעִיּוֹת — חוֹזֵר לְ״אַתָּה חוֹנֵן״. בְּאַחֲרוֹנוֹת — חוֹזֵר לָ״עֲבוֹדָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said:** One who **erred in** any of **the first three** blessings **he must return to the beginning** of the *Amida* prayer because the first three blessing comprise a single entity. Likewise, if one **erred in** any of **the** thirteen **middle** blessings, **he returns to** the blessing of: **You grace** humanity, the first of the middle blessings. If one erred **in** any of the three **final** blessings, **he must return to the** blessing of **Temple service,** which is the first of the final blessings.

*Amar Rav Huna*: if someone made an error in the first three *brachot* of *Shemoneh Esrei* — for example, failing to say *mashiv ha-ruach u-morid ha-geshem* and already having completed the second *brachah*, making it impossible to insert it — *chozer l'rosh*, one must return to the very beginning of *Shemoneh Esrei*. The first three *brachot* constitute a single indivisible unit. Any error requiring a correction means the entire unit must be redone.

*U-v'emtza'iyot* — for the middle *brachot*, Rav Huna holds that they too form one large unit, from *Atah Chonen* through *Shomea Tefillah*, and any mistake requiring a return means going all the way back to *Atah Chonen*. *U-v'acharonah* — for the final three, *chozer l'Avodah*, one returns to *Retzei*. So Rav Huna's scheme presents three discrete indivisible units; an error in any one sends you back to the beginning of that unit.

### Rav Assi Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב אַסִּי אָמַר: אֶמְצָעִיּוֹת אֵין לָהֶן סֵדֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Asi** disputes one aspect of Rav Huna's opinion, as **he said: The middle** blessings **have no** set **order.** If one erred in any of them he may insert it at whatever point he becomes aware of his error.

*Rav Assi amar: emtza'iyot ein lahem seder* — there is no fixed order to the middle *brachot*. According to *Rashi*'s reading, this means the middle *brachot* may even be said out of order. If one realizes mid-*tefillah* that one recited *v'tein tal u-matar* when one should have said *v'tein berachah*, and the error is discovered moments later — say, during *Al HaTzaddikim* — one may recite the correct *brachah* of *Barechenu* right there. There is no ordering constraint on the middle *brachot*. This sounds strange, but the underlying principle does have practical application: the *brachot* of *sheva brachot* under the *chuppah*, for instance, are ruled not to be *me'akev* in their order — a very important *halachah* to know should someone accidentally recite them out of sequence. Similarly, if a *ba'al kri'ah* makes the *berachah* after the *aliyah* first, the *halachah* is not invalidated.

### Tosafot Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מֵהֵיכָן הוּא חוֹזֵר — מִתְּחִלַּת הַבְּרָכָה שֶׁטָּעָה זֶה. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְרַב הוּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet raised an objection** based on a *baraita*: **From where does he** commence **repetition** of the *Amida* prayer? He commences **from the beginning of the blessing in which** the former **had erred.** If so, this is **a conclusive refutation of Rav Huna's** opinion, as Rav Huna said that if one erred in one of the middle blessings, he returns to the beginning of the middle blessings, not to the beginning of that particular blessing.

The *Gemara* then challenges both opinions from our *mishna*, which states *mi-techilat ha-brachah she-ta'ah* — you return to the beginning of the *brachah* in which you erred. *Rashi*'s version of the text includes *tiyuvta de-Rav Assi*, a refutation of Rav Assi, since the *mishna* implies you go back to a specific *brachah* and not that you can make it up wherever you are. Rav Huna's position is defended, albeit with some strain: when the *mishna* says "go back to the beginning of the *brachah*," it means *Atah Chonen*, because Rav Huna considers all the middle *brachot* to be one single *brachah*. This is admittedly a stretch.

*Tosafot* disagrees with *Rashi*'s *girsa* entirely. In *Tosafot*'s reading, the *Gemara* never actually refutes Rav Assi. What Rav Assi means by *ein lahem seder* is not that you may make up a missed *brachah* wherever you happen to be, but rather that you are not locked into a single indivisible unit — you may return specifically to the *brachah* where the error occurred, rather than being forced back to *Atah Chonen*. There *is* an ordering of sorts; it just operates at the level of the individual *brachah*. This is the practice: if you made a mistake in a particular *brachah* requiring a return, you go back to that *brachah*. This is how we rule — and it is *Tosafot*'s reading of Rav Assi, not the two-unit extremes of the *Gemara*'s stated positions according to *Rashi*.

### Yamim Noraim Adds

This discussion of errors and return has direct practical significance for the *Yamim Noraim*. Several *Tosafot* in Masechet Berakhot assume that even the later Geonic additions — *zochrenu l'chayim*, *katvenu l'chayim*, *mi chamocha*, and the like — carry the same obligation of return if forgotten. The *Gemara* itself contains *HaMelech HaKadosh* and *HaMelech HaMishpat*; it was the *Geonim* who added the *zochrenu* insertions. We rule that if those later additions are forgotten, one continues, since they were added after the fact. There is indeed a question why such additions are permitted at all in the first three and last three *brachot*, given what we are about to learn — but *Tosafot*'s approach, characteristically embracing accepted communal practice, treats even these Geonic additions as obligatory in the sense that forgetting them requires return.

At the end of the relevant *Tosafot*, beginning with the word *v'heicha*, Tosafot states: if one erred in the first three *brachot* — for example, one made an incorrect *chatimah*, or failed to say *zochrenu l'chayim* in *Birchas Avot* between *Rosh Hashanah* and *Yom Kippur*, or omitted *mi chamocha* in the second *brachah*, or failed to say *HaMelech HaKadosh* — *chozer l'rishonah*, one returns to the very beginning of *Shemoneh Esrei*. According to *Tosafot*, then, forgetting *zochrenu l'chayim* requires starting over entirely. We do not rule this way in practice. But the structure that emerges from *Tosafot*'s position is clear: for the first three, return to the beginning; for the last three, return to *Retzei*; for the middle, return to the specific *brachah* where the error occurred — and this applies even to the Geonic insertions.

## Personal Requests

### No Personal Requests

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: לְעוֹלָם אַל יִשְׁאַל אָדָם צְרָכָיו לֹא בְּשָׁלֹשׁ רִאשׁוֹנוֹת, וְלֹא בְּשָׁלֹשׁ אַחֲרוֹנוֹת, אֶלָּא בְּאֶמְצָעִיּוֹת. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: רִאשׁוֹנוֹת — דּוֹמֶה לְעֶבֶד שֶׁמְּסַדֵּר שֶׁבַח לִפְנֵי רַבּוֹ. אֶמְצָעִיּוֹת — דּוֹמֶה לְעֶבֶד שֶׁמְבַקֵּשׁ פְּרָס מֵרַבּוֹ. אַחֲרוֹנוֹת — דּוֹמֶה לְעֶבֶד שֶׁקִּבֵּל פְּרָס מֵרַבּוֹ, וְנִפְטָר וְהוֹלֵךְ לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said:** There is an additional distinction between the various sections of the *Amida* prayer: **One must never request his** own **needs in the first three or in the last three** blessings; **rather,** he should do so **in the middle** blessings. **As Rabbi Ḥanina said:** During the **first** three blessings, he **is like a servant who arranges praise before his master;** during the **middle** blessings, he **is like a servant who requests a reward from his master;** during the **final** three blessings, one **is like a servant who** already **received a reward from his master and is taking his leave and departing.**

*Amar Rav Yehuda: le'olam al yishal adam tzorachav lo b'shalosh rishonot v'lo b'shalosh acharonot* — a person should never insert personal requests in the first three or the last three *brachot* of *Shemoneh Esrei*. Personal needs, when inserted, belong only *b'emtza'iyot*, in the middle *brachot*. This is because the middle *brachot* are structurally the *brachot* of *bakashot* — petitions — while the opening and closing *brachot* serve different functions, as the *Gemara* proceeds to explain.

### Master Parable

*Kasavar Rabbi Chanina*: the first three *brachot* are *domeh l'eved she-mesader shevach lifnei rabo* — like a servant setting out praises before his master. One approaches God first with praise. And indeed the first three *brachot* — *Magen Avraham*, *Mechayei Meitim*, *HaMelech HaKadosh* — are not explicitly petitionary, though they carry implicit dimensions of hope and desire. Their primary register is declaration: who God is and what God does.

The middle *brachot* are *domeh l'eved she-mevakesh pras mi-rabo* — like a servant asking for a portion from his master. A *pras* here likely means simply a portion, something to sustain oneself; the image is of genuine supplication. And the final *brachot* are *domeh l'eved she-kibel pras mi-rabo ve-niftar ve-holech lo* — like a servant who has received what he sought and now takes his leave of the master.

This last description is striking. The *Gemara* does not say the servant *thanks* the master — it says he *asks his leave*. And if one examines the final *brachot* of *Shemoneh Esrei*, they do not map easily onto pure *hoda'ah*. *Sim Shalom* is a petition for peace; *Retzei* asks God to accept our prayer and restore His presence to the *Beis HaMikdash*; *Ya'aleh v'Yavo* is embedded in *Retzei* and connected to the *avodah*. *Modim* itself is more genuinely one of thanksgiving, and *ha-tov shimcha u-lecha na'eh l'hodos* is an expression of gratitude. But the *brachah* as a whole resists the characterization of simple thanksgiving.

Perhaps it is more accurate to say the closing *brachot* function as an act of taking leave — *shalom aleichem*, in reverse, a gracious departure — rather than as explicit *hoda'ah*. The *Gemara* itself, interestingly, does not use the word *hoda'ah* at this juncture; it says only that the servant received his *pras* and departs. This may be intentional: the closing section is not so much about thanks as it is about completing the encounter appropriately, acknowledging the presence one has been in, and seeking *shalom* as one withdraws. The fit with *Retzei*, which asks God to accept the *avodah* and restore the *Beis HaMikdash*, remains somewhat difficult to fully account for within this model — not entirely unlike *Shomea Tefillah*, which also asks for acceptance of prayer, raising the question of how the two differ.

There is also a structural observation worth noting: the fact that *Retzei*, which alludes to the *korbanot* and the *Beis HaMikdash*, is placed outside the core middle section is itself suggestive. The entire *Shemoneh Esrei*, as structured, contains no explicit mention of *korbanot*. We open with *Hashem sefatai tiftach* and proceed through praise and petition. Even *Retzei*, which gestures toward *tefillah k'neged korbanot*, is positioned in the concluding section rather than at the center, reinforcing its role as closing frame rather than core petition.

### Communal Needs

*Tosafot* addresses the prohibition on personal requests in the first three and last three *brachot*, citing *Rabbenu Chananel* and *Rabbeinu Hai Gaon*: *davka l'yachid aval tzorchei tzibbur sho'alim* — the restriction applies only to an individual's personal needs; communal needs may be inserted. This is offered as the justification for the *minhag* of saying *zochrenu l'chayim* and similar insertions, as well as for the *krovot* — the liturgical poems inserted into *Shemoneh Esrei* in *Ashkenaz* — during the *regalim*.

*Tosafot* notes that the final *brachot* of *Shemoneh Esrei* are fundamentally about communal rather than personal concerns. Indeed, if one examines the *brachot*, while all are phrased in the plural, some of the middle *brachot* — requests for livelihood, healing, intelligence — can be understood as essentially personal even when phrased communally. By contrast, *Hashiva Shofteinu*, *Tzemach David*, and other *brachot* in the latter part of the middle section are clearly national in orientation: restoring of judges, the Davidic dynasty, the return to the land. The emphasis that the closing *brachot* are more *tzarchei tzibbur* than the middle ones is a genuinely interesting observation.

But this was also the center of a major controversy between *Geonei Bavel* and *Geonei Eretz Yisrael*, and subsequently between *Sefardim* and *Ashkenazim*: whether *piyuttim* and *krovot* could be inserted into fixed sections of *davening*, including the first three and last three *brachot*. In *Bavel*, the answer was essentially no — *tefillah kevuah* is fixed and cannot be expanded with poetic additions. *Sefardim*, influenced heavily by the *minhag* rooted in *Bavel*, largely adopted this position. In *Eretz Yisrael*, where the *piyuttim* were being composed, the culture of liturgical creativity was vibrant and the view was that *Yamim Tovim* demanded something more textured and responsive to the character of the day — something to give the *tefillah* its proper power and flavor. *Ashkenaz* followed *Eretz Yisrael* in this regard. Ruth Langer's work *To Worship God Properly* explores this debate in depth.

*Tosafot*'s citation of *tzarchei tzibbur* as the justification is a one-word defense of an entire *minhag* — characteristic of the *Tosafist* embrace of accepted practice. Even the *zochrenu* insertions, now universally accepted, were at some stage contested in the Geonic period. What became universally accepted eventually, while *krovot* never crossed over into universal acceptance, reflects the complex history of how local liturgical practice solidifies into *minhag* and ultimately into *halachah*.

## Prayer Length Signs

### Long vs. Short

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּתַלְמִיד אֶחָד שֶׁיָּרַד לִפְנֵי הַתֵּיבָה בִּפְנֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, וְהָיָה מַאֲרִיךְ יוֹתֵר מִדַּאי. אָמְרוּ לוֹ תַּלְמִידָיו: כַּמָּה אָרְכָן הוּא זֶה! אָמַר לָהֶם: כְּלוּם מַאֲרִיךְ יוֹתֵר מִמֹּשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ, דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״אֵת אַרְבָּעִים הַיּוֹם וְאֶת אַרְבָּעִים הַלַּיְלָה וְגוֹ׳״?!
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** There was **an incident where one student descended** to serve as prayer leader **before the ark in the presence of Rabbi Eliezer, and he was excessively prolonging his prayer. His students** complained and **said to him: How long-winded he is. He said to them: Is** this student **prolonging** his prayer any more **than Moses our teacher** did? **As about** Moses **it is written:** "And I prostrated myself before the Lord **for the forty days and forty nights** that I prostrated myself" (Deuteronomy 9:25). There is no limit to the duration of a prayer.

A *baraita* presents two contrasting stories, both occurring before *Rabbi Eliezer*. In the first, a student was leading the *davening* and was going on at great length — *ma'arikh yoter midai*. The students complained: "Rabbenu, how lengthy this one is!" *Rabbi Eliezer* responded: does he go longer than *Moshe Rabbeinu*? — *d'ksiv: va-etpallel lifnei Hashem arba'im yom ve-arba'im layla*, "I prayed before God forty days and forty nights" — referring to *Moshe*'s prayer after the *Chet Ha'egel*, pleading for the forgiveness of *Bnei Yisrael*.

In the second story, a different student led and was cutting things very short — *mekatzer yoter midai*. The students again complained. *Rabbi Eliezer* replied: is he shorter than *Moshe Rabbeinu*? — *d'ksiv: Eil na r'fa na lah*, five words, which *Moshe* said in prayer for *Miriam*.

The obvious question is what *ma'arikh* and *mekatzer* mean in these stories. Does the student go long by adding personal compositions and elaborations to the text, the text being fluid in that era? Or does he simply *chazzan* with extensive musical flourish and take a long time? Likely the former: the period of the *Gemara* was one in which the exact wording of *Shemoneh Esrei* was still somewhat fluid, and a *shliach tzibbur* might frame each *brachah* in more or less expansive language. This reading — that the length and brevity refer to the actual textual content — is significant. It means that the use of *Moshe*'s extremes as models is itself a statement: there is no single fixed proper length for *tefillah*. A *shliach tzibbur* leading in a period of relative textual latitude could legitimately go quite long or quite short, provided he maintained a sincere connection. What is also notable is the tension with the later reality: once a printed *siddur* became universal, the sense of latitude effectively disappeared. The printed word fixed what had once been fluid, in ways that are worth reflecting upon.

The *Gemara* then adds: *amar Rabbi Yaakov amar Rav Chisda: kol ha-mevakesh rachamim al chaveiro ein tzarich l'hazkir shemo* — one who prays for a sick friend need not mention their name. The source is *Eil na r'fa na lah* — *Moshe* prayed for *Miriam* without saying her name; God knows whom we mean. This is a practically important ruling: if you do not know a person's Hebrew name, or do not know who the person is beyond "someone who is ill," your *tefillah* is still valid.

### Smooth Flow Sign

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוּב מַעֲשֶׂה בְּתַלְמִיד אֶחָד שֶׁיָּרַד לִפְנֵי הַתֵּיבָה בִּפְנֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, וְהָיָה מְקַצֵּר יוֹתֵר מִדַּאי. אָמְרוּ לוֹ תַּלְמִידָיו: כַּמָּה קַצְרָן הוּא זֶה! אָמַר לָהֶם: כְּלוּם מְקַצֵּר יוֹתֵר מִמֹּשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״אֵל נָא רְפָא נָא לָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** There was **again an incident where one student descended** to serve as prayer leader **before the ark in the presence of Rabbi Eliezer, and he was excessively abbreviating his prayer. His students** protested **and said to him: How brief is his** prayer. **He said to them: Is he abbreviating** his prayer **any more than Moses our teacher** did? **As it is written** with regard to the prayer Moses recited imploring God to cure Miriam of her leprosy: "And Moses cried out to the Lord, saying: **'Please, God, heal her, please'"** (Numbers 12:13). This student's prayer was certainly no briefer than the few words recited by Moses.

*Amar Rabbi Yitzchak bar Nachmani*: the sign that a prayer has been accepted is whether the words flow smoothly from one's lips — a theme we will encounter again in a later story about *Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa*. The *psukim* cited in support — *borei niv sefatayim, shalom shalom la-rachok ve-la-karov* — are read as saying: if the *niv sefatayim*, the speech of the lips, is clear and healthy, then *shalom shalom*, peace in abundance. The facility of speech in *tefillah* is taken as a communicative signal of reception, not merely a personal characteristic.

## Bowing Positions

### Bow Locations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵלּוּ בְּרָכוֹת שֶׁאָדָם שׁוֹחֶה בָּהֶן: בְּ״אָבוֹת״, תְּחִלָּה וָסוֹף. בַּ״הוֹדָאָה״, תְּחִלָּה וָסוֹף. וְאִם בָּא לָשׁוּחַ בְּסוֹף כׇּל בְּרָכָה וּבְרָכָה, וּבִתְחִלַּת כׇּל בְּרָכָה וּבְרָכָה — מְלַמְּדִין אוֹתוֹ שֶׁלֹּא יִשְׁחֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *Tosefta*: **These** are the **blessings** in the *Amida* prayer **in which a person bows: In** the first blessing, the blessing of the **Patriarchs,** one bows **at the beginning and the end; in** the blessing of **thanksgiving,** one bows **at the beginning and the end; and if one seeks to bow at the end of each and every blessing and at the beginning of each and every blessing, they teach him not to bow** so as not to go beyond the ordinance instituted by the Sages.

A *baraita* enumerates the specific *brachot* in which one bows: *ba-Avot techilah v'sof u-v'hoda'ah techilah v'sof* — in *Avot* at the beginning and end, and in *Modim* at the beginning and end. The mechanics: at the word *Baruch* one begins to bend, at *Atah* one is fully bent, and at God's name one rises. These four points — beginning and end of *Avot*, beginning and end of *Modim* — are the prescribed locations of bowing for an ordinary person.

*V'im ba lishuach b'sof kol berachah u'verachah u'v-techilat kol berachah u'verachah* — if someone wishes to bow at the beginning and end of every *brachah*, *melam-din oto she-lo yishcheh*, one instructs him not to do so. Bowing everywhere undermines the distinctive significance of bowing where it is prescribed. Uniformity eliminates meaning.

### Special Status Bowing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן פַּזִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי מִשּׁוּם בַּר קַפָּרָא: הֶדְיוֹט, כְּמוֹ שֶׁאָמַרְנוּ. כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, תְּחִלַּת כׇּל בְּרָכָה וּבְרָכָה. הַמֶּלֶךְ — כֵּיוָן שֶׁכָּרַע שׁוּב אֵינוֹ זוֹקֵף, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי כְּכַלּוֹת שְׁלֹמֹה לְהִתְפַּלֵּל וְגוֹ׳ קָם מִלִּפְנֵי מִזְבַּח ה׳ מִכְּרֹעַ עַל בִּרְכָּיו״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi said** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said in the name of** the *tanna* **bar Kappara:** An **ordinary** person, conducts himself **as we said;** he bows at the beginning and the end of the blessings of Patriarchs and thanksgiving; **a High Priest** bows at **the beginning of each and every blessing; the king, once he has bowed** at the beginning of the first blessing, **does not rise** until he concludes the entire prayer, **as it is stated: "And it was that when Solomon finished praying** all of his prayer to the Lord, **he rose from before the altar of the Lord, from kneeling upon his knees** with his hands spread forth toward the heavens" (I Kings 8:54).

*Amar Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi mishum Bar Kappara*: for an ordinary person, the rule is as above. A *kohen gadol* bows at the end of every *brachah*. The reasoning is that those in positions of great power and status must go further to demonstrate that even in the highest human roles, one stands before God in humility. The *kohen gadol*'s enhanced bowing requirements reflect the fact that power can corrupt and distort one's sense of proportion; the act of repeatedly bowing throughout *Shemoneh Esrei* instills a corrective sense of human limitation.

The *Gemara* then records a second version attributed to *Rabbi Yitzchak bar Nachmani* in the name of *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* in which the *kohen gadol* bows at the beginning of every *brachah*, and the king does something more dramatic still: *keivan she-kara shuv eino zokef* — once the king bows at the outset of *Shemoneh Esrei*, he remains bowed for the entire *tefillah*. The source is *Shlomo HaMelech* at the inauguration of the *Beis HaMikdash*: *vayehi kechalot Shlomo lehitpalel kam milifnei mizbe'ach Hashem mikroa al birkav* — when *Shlomo* completed his prayer, he rose from kneeling. The implication is that he had been on his knees for the entire prayer. Hence the king, whose position is most prone to distorting one's sense of humanity, must bow the entire time.

### Kida vs. Hishtachavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: קִידָּה — עַל אַפַּיִם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַתִּקֹּד בַּת שֶׁבַע אַפַּיִם אֶרֶץ״. כְּרִיעָה — עַל בִּרְכַּיִם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״מִכְּרֹעַ עַל בִּרְכָּיו״. הִשְׁתַּחֲוָאָה — זוֹ פִּשּׁוּט יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הֲבוֹא נָבוֹא אֲנִי וְאִמְּךָ וְאַחֶיךָ לְהִשְׁתַּחֲוֹת לְךָ אָרְצָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The term ***kidda*** means bowing **upon** one's **face,** with his face toward the ground, **as it is stated: "Then Bathsheba bowed [*vatikod*] with her face to the ground"** (I Kings 1:31). ***Keria*** means bowing **upon one's knees, as** regarding Solomon **it is stated:** He finished praying and **"he rose from before the altar of the Lord, from kneeling [*mikkeroa*] upon his knees."** Finally, ***hishtaḥava'a*, that is** bowing with **one's hands and legs spread** in total submission, **as it is stated** in Jacob's question to Joseph in response to his dream: **"Shall we, I and your mother and your brothers, come and bow down [*lehishtaḥavot*] to you to the ground?"** (Genesis 37:10).

The *baraita* distinguishes between the forms of prostration mentioned in *Tanach*. *Kida al apayim* — bowing on one's face — is derived from *vatiked Bat Sheva apayim artzah*, Bat Sheva with her face toward the ground. The simple meaning of *al apayim* suggests the face actually touching the ground, though how precisely that is accomplished without also going to one's knees is debated. Some say it means simply bowing one's head forward. *Keri'ah* — bending on the knees — is derived from *mikroa al birkav*, which is explicitly on the knees.

*Hishtachavah* — full prostration — means *pishut yadayim v'raglayim*, extending both hands and legs, lying fully stretched out on the ground, or at minimum fully splayed with the whole body extended rather than merely the face. The two can combine: *vayikdu vayishtachavu* — first the face touches the ground (*kida*), and then full prostration (*hishtachavah*) follows. The phrase *havo navo ani v'imcha v'achicha lehishtachavot lecha artzah* — will we come and prostrate on the ground? — reinforces the image of full prostration spread out on the earth.

### Stone Floor Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא: חֲזֵינָא לְהוּ לְאַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּמַצְלוּ אַצְלוֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥiyya, son of Rav Huna, said: I saw Abaye and Rava, who would lean** their heads and not actually prostrate themselves on the ground.

*Amar Rav Chiyya brei d'Rav Huna: chazina lehu l'Abaye v'Rava de-matzlu atzluyei* — *Abaye* and *Rava* were observed leaning on their side rather than fully prostrating. The context, as *Rabbeinu Nissim* explains with reference to the *Yerushalmi*, relates to the prohibition derived from *v'even maskit lo titenu b'artzechem lehishtachavot aleha* — the prohibition against prostrating on a stone floor outside the *Beis HaMikdash*. Outside the Temple, one may not perform full prostration directly on stone.

This is the actual reason behind the practice — familiar from *Yom Kippur* and *Rosh Hashanah* — of placing a paper or cloth beneath one's knees and face before *nefilat apayim*. It is not, as one might imagine, simply concern for one's suit. The halachic concern is that face and knees should not contact a stone floor. Whether carpeting suffices — as carpeting is attached to the floor and might be considered subsumed within it — is a point of discussion, but many authorities are lenient with carpeting while still supporting the practice of placing something down as an added precaution.

The context in which *Abaye* and *Rava* were leaning rather than fully prostrating appears to be a fast day — *Rav Nisim* understands it as a public *ta'anit* — when the desire for a more dramatic physical expression of prayer was present, but the stone floor made full prostration impermissible, leading to the lateral lean as a compromise. This also opens an interesting broader question: how extensive was *keri'ah* — bending to the knees — during *Shemoneh Esrei* in earlier periods? We associate prostration on the knees with Islamic prayer and assume Jews simply bow at the spine. But perhaps for some people in earlier times, *keri'ah* in *Shemoneh Esrei* was more extensive than what we practice today.

### Modim Bowing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי חֲדָא: הַכּוֹרֵעַ בַּהוֹדָאָה — הֲרֵי זֶה מְשׁוּבָּח. וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: הֲרֵי זֶה מְגוּנֶּה!
>
> **Translation:** **One** *baraita* **taught: One who bows in** the blessing of **thanksgiving, it is praiseworthy. And it was taught in another** *baraita*: One who bows in the blessing of thanksgiving, **it is reprehensible.** These *baraitot* are contradictory.

*Tani chada*: a *baraita* says that one who bows at *Modim* is *meshubach*, praiseworthy. *V'tani idach*: another *baraita* says it is *meguneh*, unseemly. *Lo kashya: ha b'techilah ha b'sof* — the two *baraisos* refer to the beginning and end respectively: bowing at one is praiseworthy, bowing at the other is not. Which end is problematic is not explicitly stated, but the *Gemara* applies this to *Rava*'s practice.

*Rava kara b'hoda'ah techilah v'sof* — *Rava* bowed at both the beginning and end of *Modim*. The rabbis questioned him: given that one *baraita* calls such bowing *meguneh*, why does he do it? *Rava* replied: *chazina lei l'Rav Nachman de-kara* — he observed *Rav Nachman* bowing at the beginning and end of *Modim*, and similarly *chazina lei l'Rav Sheshet de-kara hachi* — he observed *Rav Sheshet* doing the same. This is a striking and recurring motif in the *Gemara*: learning *halachah* from direct observation of a teacher's practice.

The *Gemara* responds: but does the *baraita* not say *hakoreia b'hoda'ah harei ze meguneh*? The resolution: *hahu b'hoda'ah she-ba-Hallel* — that *baraita* refers not to *Shemoneh Esrei* but to the *Hodu l'Hashem ki tov* of *Hallel*. *Rashi* identifies this as *Hodu l'Hashem ki tov* specifically. There, bowing is inappropriate — perhaps because *Hallel* is more in the register of joyful praise, and bowing there would strike an incongruous note. At *Modim* in *Shemoneh Esrei*, where the spirit of awe and acknowledgment of God's sovereignty is dominant, bowing is entirely appropriate and, at both points, praiseworthy.

A further version of the *baraita* is cited — *hakoreia b'hoda'ah u-v'hoda'ah shel Hallel harei ze meguneh* — which suggests the prohibition covers both regular *hoda'ah* and *hoda'ah* of *Hallel*. The *Gemara* resolves: *ki tanya hahi b'hoda'ah de-Birkat Hamazon* — that refers to the *hoda'ah* in *Birkat HaMazon*, presumably *nodeh lecha Hashem Elokeinu*. So the full structure that emerges is: bowing in *Modim* of *Shemoneh Esrei*, at beginning and end, is praiseworthy; bowing in *Hoda'ah* of *Hallel* or *Birkat HaMazon* is unseemly.

## Error Significance

### Bad Sign Error

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמִּתְפַּלֵּל וְטָעָה — סִימָן רַע לוֹ. וְאִם שְׁלִיחַ צִבּוּר הוּא — סִימָן רַע לְשׁוֹלְחָיו, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁשְּׁלוּחוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם כְּמוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who prays and** realizes that he **erred** in his prayer, it is **a bad omen for him;** it indicates to him that his prayer was not accepted. **And if he** who erred **is the communal prayer leader,** it is **a bad omen for those who sent him, because a person's agent** has legal status **equivalent to his own.**

The *mishna* states: *ha-mitpalel v'ta'ah siman ra lo* — someone who is *davening* and makes an error, it is a bad sign for that person. *V'im shliach tzibbur hu — siman ra l'sholchav* — if the *shliach tzibbur* makes an error, it is a bad sign not just for himself but for those who sent him — the *tzibbur* he represents.

The *Gemara* asks: on which *brachah* does an error qualify as a bad sign? Is it any error anywhere in *Shemoneh Esrei*, or is there a more specific locus? *Amar Rabbi Chiyya amar Rav Safra mishum chad d'Bei Rebbi*: only an error in *Avot*, the first *brachah*, constitutes a bad sign. Other errors are less significant as omens.

### Representative Principle

The basis for the bad sign in the case of the *shliach tzibbur* is articulated through a legal principle that appears here in an unusual context: *mipnei she-shlucho shel adam k'moto* — because a person's *shaliach* is like the person themselves. In *halachah* literature, *shlucho shel adam k'moto* is a foundational and frequently invoked principle governing agency in *kiddushin*, commercial transactions, and many other domains. But its appearance in the *mishna* is exclusively in the context of the *shliach tzibbur*.

This is arresting. The principle in the *mishna* is not being deployed in a legal-transactional sense. It is making a statement about the nature of communal prayer itself: the *shliach tzibbur* is genuinely the representative of the *tzibbur* before God. He brings the *tzibbur*'s prayers upward. If he makes an error — if his *tefillah* does not flow — it reflects that the gates of prayer are closed, not necessarily to him personally, but to those he represents. One sees a trace of this in the extended *tefillah* that a *chazzan* recites before *Shemoneh Esrei* on *Yamim Noraim*: declaring that he is representing the congregation and does not feel himself worthy. For most of us today, that sense of representation has faded — the *chazzan* is experienced more as a facilitator of communal singing than as an emissary before God. But the *Rishonim*, when they discuss who should serve as *shliach tzibbur*, reflect a profound understanding that this person is genuinely carrying the *tzibbur*'s prayers upward, and that his errors are the *tzibbur*'s bad omen.

### Chanina Prayer Flow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ עָלָיו עַל רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן דּוֹסָא שֶׁהָיָה מִתְפַּלֵּל עַל הַחוֹלִים וְאוֹמֵר זֶה חַי וְזֶה מֵת. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מִנַּיִן אַתָּה יוֹדֵעַ? אָמַר לָהֶם: אִם שְׁגוּרָה תְּפִלָּתִי בְּפִי — יוֹדֵעַ אֲנִי שֶׁהוּא מְקוּבָּל, וְאִם לָאו — יוֹדֵעַ אֲנִי שֶׁהוּא מְטוֹרָף.
>
> **Translation:** **It was said of Rabbi Ḥanina ben Dosa that he would pray on** behalf of **the sick and** immediately after his prayer he would **say: This** one shall recover from his illness and **live and this** one shall **die.** When **they said to him: From where do you know? He said to them: If my prayer is fluent in my mouth** as I recite it and there are no errors, **I know that** my prayer **is accepted. And if not, I know that** my prayer **is rejected.**

*Amru alav al Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa: she-haya mitpalel al ha-cholim ve-omer zeh chay ve-zeh met* — it was said of *Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa* that he would pray for the sick and afterward say: this one will live, and this one will die. When asked how he knew, he replied: *im shegura tefilati befi* — if my prayer flows smoothly from my lips, *yodea ani she-hu mekubal*, I know it was accepted; *v'im lav*, if not, *yodea ani she-hu meturaf*, I know it was rejected — thrown back, unaccepted.

*Rashi* suggests that *Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa* may have been composing his own prayers in his own words, not reciting a fixed text, which would mean that the smoothness or faltering of the words was a more immediate reflection of his spiritual connection in that moment. The practical significance: how one's *tefillah* unfolds — whether it flows or stumbles — can be read as a sign of its reception. This is admittedly a difficult idea for many modern sensibilities, which tend toward skepticism about such omens. But the *Gemara* presents it with full seriousness: the phenomenon of *tefillah* involves a real connection to God, and the character of that connection can, in certain circumstances, be read in the quality and ease of the prayer itself.

There is an analogy to the *Ba'al Toke'a* on *Rosh Hashanah*: if he has great difficulty producing the *teki'ot*, some take this as a troubling omen. The difference the *Gemara* points to here is that it is not merely a reflection of the individual's skill or state of mind but rather a sign that the *sha'ar ha-tefillah*, the gate of prayer, is or is not open.

### Kavana Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַבָּרַיְיתָא: הַמִּתְפַּלֵּל צָרִיךְ שֶׁיְּכַוֵּין אֶת לִבּוֹ בְּכוּלָּן, וְאִם אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְכַוֵּין בְּכוּלָּן — יְכַוֵּין אֶת לִבּוֹ בְּאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Some teach** that **this** statement was made **on a *baraita*** referring to another topic. It was taught in a *baraita*: **One who prays must focus his heart in all** of the blessings. **And if he is unable to focus** his heart **in all of them, he should focus his heart** at least **in one.**

The *Gemara* connects the question of *ta'ah* with the topic of *kavana*. The *baraita* states: *ha-mitpalel tzarich she-yechaven et libo be-kulam* — when one prays, one must have *kavana* for all the *brachot*. *V'im eino yachol lechaven be-kulam*, if one cannot maintain *kavana* throughout, *yechaven et libo be-achat* — it is sufficient to have *kavana* for one of them. That one *brachah* is identified, again by *Rabbi Chiyya* in the name of *Rav Safra*, as *Avot*. This is the *halachic* ruling: if one has *kavana* for *Avot*, one is *yotzei*, even if the rest of *Shemoneh Esrei* was said more automatically.

*Tosafot* connects this to the principle *le'olam ya'amod adam da'ato: im yachol lechaven yitpalel, v'im lav al yitpalel* — that one should assess whether one can have *kavana* and only *daven* if one can. *Tosafot* understands this as referring to *kavana* for *Avot* specifically: if you can have *kavana* for that *brachah*, proceed; if not, the concern is more serious. The *Rambam*, however, takes a different approach. As *Rav Chaim* explains, the *Rambam* distinguishes between two dimensions of *kavana*. The first — *kavana* to know the meaning of the words — is required minimally for *Avot*; without it there, one is not *yotzei*. The second is a different kind of *kavana* entirely: *da lifnei mi atah omed*, awareness throughout that one stands in the presence of God. According to the *Rambam*, this awareness is required for the entire *Shemoneh Esrei*, and its absence at any point is a genuine failing, even if it does not invalidate one's fulfillment of the formal obligation.

## World to Come

### Teshuvah Merit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הַנְּבִיאִים כּוּלָּן לֹא נִתְנַבְּאוּ אֶלָּא לַמַּשִּׂיא בִּתּוֹ לְתַלְמִיד חָכָם, וְלָעוֹשֶׂה פְּרַקְמַטְיָא לְתַלְמִיד חָכָם, וְלַמְהַנֶּה תַּלְמִיד חָכָם מִנְּכָסָיו. אֲבָל תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים עַצְמָן — ״עַיִן לֹא רָאָתָה אֱלֹהִים זוּלָתְךָ יַעֲשֶׂה לִמְחַכֵּה לוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** with regard to the reward of the righteous: **All the prophets only prophesied** in their prophecies of consolation, **with regard to one who** values wisdom and therefore **marries his daughter to a Torah scholar and to one who conducts business [*perakmatya*] on behalf of a Torah scholar as well as to one who** utilizes his wealth **to benefit a Torah scholar** in some other way. **However,** the prophets did not describe the extent of the reward for **Torah scholars themselves,** whose reward is not quantifiable as it is stated: "And from of old they have not heard, they have not lent an ear, **no eye has seen it, God, aside from You, who will do for those who await Him"** (Isaiah 64:3).

*Amar Rav Chiyya bar Abba amar Rabbi Yochanan: kol ha-neviim kulam lo nitnab'u ela le-massi bito le-talmid chacham* — all the blessings spoken by all the prophets were said only for the benefit of someone who gives his daughter in marriage to a *talmid chacham*, or engages in commerce in partnership with a *talmid chacham* (*oseh prakmatya le-talmid chacham*), or sustains a *talmid chacham* from his property. The entire sweep of prophetic blessing is reserved for those who are connected to — and who support — the *talmidei chachamim*.

*Aval talmidei chachamim atzmam* — the sages themselves receive something categorically different and beyond: *ayin lo ra'ata Elokim zulatcha ya'aseh li-mchakeh lo* — no eye has seen, God, other than You, what You will do for those who wait for You. What awaits those who dedicate themselves entirely to *Torah* is beyond any prophetic imagery, beyond what has ever been seen or described.

### Mashiach Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הַנְּבִיאִים כּוּלָּן לֹא נִתְנַבְּאוּ אֶלָּא לִימוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ, אֲבָל לְעוֹלָם הַבָּא, ״עַיִן לֹא רָאָתָה אֱלֹהִים זוּלָתְךָ״. וּפְלִיגָא דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין בֵּין הָעוֹלָם הַזֶּה לִימוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ אֶלָּא שִׁעְבּוּד מַלְכוּיוֹת בִּלְבַד. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי לֹא יֶחְדַּל אֶבְיוֹן מִקֶּרֶב הָאָרֶץ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: All the prophets only prophesied** with regard to the change in world order in the end of days **with regard to the days of the Messiah. However, with regard to the World-to-Come,** which exists on a higher level, it is stated: **"No eye has seen it, God, aside from You."** **And** the Gemara notes that this statement **disagrees with** the opinion of **Shmuel, as Shmuel said: The only** difference **between this world and the days of the Messiah is** with regard to **servitude to** foreign **kingdoms alone.** While in the days of the Messiah, Israel will be independent and free from enslavement to foreign powers, the world order will remain otherwise unchanged, **as it is stated: "For the poor shall not cease from the land"** (Deuteronomy 15:11), which indicates that the ways of the world are set and unchanging.

A second statement in the same name: *kol ha-neviim kulam lo nitnab'u ela li-ymot ha-mashiach* — all prophetic blessings relate only to the messianic era. *Aval Olam Haba — ayin lo ra'ata*, the world to come is beyond all prophetic description. This raises the question of what *Olam Haba* means. While the term is commonly understood today through the lens of the *Rambam* as the fate of the soul after death, the earlier and more literal meaning in the *Gemara* is the transformed state of the world that follows the messianic period: the era of *techiyat ha-meitim* and the complete renewal of existence.

*U-pligi de-Shmuel* — this position is in tension with *Shmuel*'s well-known ruling: *ein bein ha-olam hazeh li-ymot ha-mashiach ella shibud malchuyot bilvad* — the only difference between the present world and messianic times is that Israel will no longer be subjugated to foreign powers. No miraculous transformation, no prophetic bounty. *Ki lo yechdal evyon mi-kerev ha-aretz* — the poor will never cease from the land, even in messianic times.

The footnote in the text marks *Shmuel*'s position, because the *Rambam* rules like *Shmuel* and does so with significance. Where the *Gemara* presents *Shmuel*'s view as a single minority opinion, the *Rambam* in his writings cites it as *amru chachamim*, as if it were the rabbinic consensus. This was a deliberate theological and polemical move: the *Rambam* was addressing communities that were dangerously preoccupied with miracles and messianic drama, and he wished to redirect their focus away from supernatural expectation and toward law and reason. By presenting *Shmuel*'s position as consensus, he normalized a demythologized vision of the messianic era.

### Teshuvah Heights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הַנְּבִיאִים כּוּלָּן לֹא נִתְנַבְּאוּ אֶלָּא לְבַעֲלֵי תְשׁוּבָה, אֲבָל צַדִּיקִים גְּמוּרִים ״עַיִן לֹא רָאָתָה אֱלֹהִים זוּלָתְךָ״. וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: מָקוֹם שֶׁבַּעֲלֵי תְשׁוּבָה עוֹמְדִין — צַדִּיקִים גְּמוּרִים אֵינָם עוֹמְדִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שָׁלוֹם שָׁלוֹם לָרָחוֹק וְלַקָּרוֹב״. ״לָרָחוֹק״ בְּרֵישָׁא, וַהֲדַר ״לַקָּרוֹב״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: All the prophets only prophesied** their prophecies of consolation **with regard to penitents but with regard to the full-fledged righteous** it is stated: **"No eye has seen it, God, aside from You."** **And** the Gemara notes that **this** statement **disagrees with** the opinion of **Rabbi Abbahu** who holds that penitents are superior to the righteous. **As Rabbi Abbahu said: In the place where penitents stand, even the full-fledged righteous do not stand, as it is stated: "Peace, peace upon him who is far and him who is near."** Peace and greeting is extended first to **him who is far,** the penitent, **and** only **thereafter** is peace extended to **him who is near,** the full-fledged righteous.

A third statement in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: *kol ha-neviim kulam lo nitnab'u ela li-va'alei teshuvah* — all prophetic blessings are reserved for *ba'alei teshuvah*. *Aval tzaddikim gemurim — ayin lo ra'ata*, those who have never sinned receive something beyond prophetic description.

*U-pligi de-Rabbi Abbahu* — this contradicts *Rabbi Abbahu*, who holds: *makom she-ba'alei teshuvah omdim tzaddikim gemurim einam omdim* — the place where *ba'alei teshuvah* stand, the completely righteous cannot stand. The *ba'alei teshuvah* occupy a higher level precisely because they overcame sin and turned back. The *pasuk* is read: *shalom shalom la-rachok ve-la-karov* — peace to the distant one *first*, and then to the near one. The distant one — one who was far from God — is greeted first and with greater emphasis.

*Rabbi Yochanan* responds by interpreting the *pasuk* differently: *rachok* refers to someone who was distant from sin from the beginning — the *tzaddik gamur* who never came close to transgression. *Karov* refers to someone who was close to sin and moved away. So *Rabbi Yochanan* actually reads the *pasuk* as elevating the righteous over the penitent — the reverse of *Rabbi Abbahu*'s reading.

### Hidden Rewards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אַיִן לוֹא רָאָתָה? אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: זֶה יַיִן הַמְשׁוּמָּר בַּעֲנָבָיו מִשֵּׁשֶׁת יְמֵי בְּרֵאשִׁית. רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: זֶה עֵדֶן, שֶׁלֹּא שָׁלְטָה בּוֹ עֵין כׇּל בְּרִיָּה. שֶׁמָּא תֹּאמַר: אָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן הֵיכָן הָיָה? בַּגָּן. וְשֶׁמָּא תֹּאמַר: הוּא גַּן, הוּא עֵדֶן, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְנָהָר יוֹצֵא מֵעֵדֶן לְהַשְׁקוֹת אֶת הַגָּן״, גַּן לְחוּד וְעֵדֶן לְחוּד.
>
> **Translation:** Earlier, Rabbi Yoḥanan said that there is a reward referred to in the verse: "No eye has seen it." The Gemara asks: **What is** this reward about which it is said: **"No eye has seen it"? Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: That is the wine that has been preserved in its grapes since the six days of creation** and which no eye has ever seen. **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said: That is Eden, which no creature's eye has** ever **surveyed.** **Lest you will say: Where was Adam the first** man? Wasn't he there and didn't he survey Eden? The Gemara responds: Adam was only **in the Garden** of Eden, not in Eden itself. **And lest you will say: It is** the **Garden and it is Eden;** two names describing the same place. That is not the case, as **the verse states: "And a river went out from Eden to water the Garden"** (Genesis 2:10). Obviously, the **Garden** exists **on its own and Eden** exists **on its own.**

*Mai ayin lo ra'ata?* — what is the concrete referent of the *pasuk* about what no eye has seen? *Amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: zeh yayin ha-meshummar ba-anavav mi-sheshet yemei bereishit* — wine that has been preserved inside the grapes since the six days of creation, never extracted, never seen. The juice has remained sealed within those original grapes. *Ayin lo ra'ata*, no eye has ever seen it.

*Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani* disagrees: *zeh Eden* — it refers to the Garden of Eden, Eden itself, which no creature has ever seen. *Adam HaRishon* was in *Gan* — the garden — but not in *Eden*. The proof: *venahar yotze me-Eden lehashkot et ha-Gan* — a river came out of Eden to water the garden. *Gan lechud ve-Eden lechud* — the garden and Eden are distinct places. Even *Adam HaRishon* never saw Eden; it remains entirely unseen, the ultimate hidden reward.

## Chanina Stories

### Gamliel Son

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁחָלָה בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל. שִׁגֵּר שְׁנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים אֵצֶל רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן דּוֹסָא לְבַקֵּשׁ עָלָיו רַחֲמִים. כֵּיוָן שֶׁרָאָה אוֹתָם, עָלָה לָעֲלִיָּיה, וּבִקֵּשׁ עָלָיו רַחֲמִים. בִּירִידָתוֹ אָמַר לָהֶם: לְכוּ, שֶׁחֲלָצַתּוּ חַמָּה. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: וְכִי נָבִיא אַתָּה?! אָמַר לָהֶן: לֹא נָבִיא אָנֹכִי וְלֹא בֶן נָבִיא אָנֹכִי, אֶלָּא כָּךְ מְקּוּבְּלַנִי: אִם שְׁגוּרָה תְּפִלָּתִי בְּפִי — יוֹדֵעַ אֲנִי שֶׁהוּא מְקוּבָּל, וְאִם לָאו — יוֹדֵעַ אֲנִי שֶׁהוּא מְטוֹרָף. יָשְׁבוּ וְכָתְבוּ וְכִוְּונוּ אוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה. וּכְשֶׁבָּאוּ אֵצֶל רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, אָמַר לָהֶן: הָעֲבוֹדָה, לֹא חִסַּרְתֶּם וְלֹא הוֹתַרְתֶּם, אֶלָּא כָּךְ הָיָה מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה חֲלָצַתּוּ חַמָּה וְשָׁאַל לָנוּ מַיִם לִשְׁתּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** There was **an incident where Rabban Gamliel's son fell ill.** Rabban Gamliel **dispatched two scholars to Rabbi Ḥanina ben Dosa to pray for mercy** and healing **on his behalf. When** Rabbi Ḥanina ben Dosa **saw them** approaching, **he went up to the attic** on the roof of his house **and prayed for mercy on his behalf. Upon his descent, he said to** the messengers: You may **go** and return to Rabban Gamliel, **as the fever has already left** his son and he has been healed. The messengers **asked him:** How do you know? **Are you a prophet? He replied to them: I am neither a prophet nor son of a prophet** (see Amos 7:14), **but I have received** a tradition with regard to **this** indication: **If my prayer is fluent in my mouth** as I recite it and there are no errors, **I know that** my prayer **is accepted. And if not, I know that** my prayer **is rejected.** The Gemara relates that these messengers **sat and wrote and approximated that** precise **moment** when Rabbi Ḥanina ben Dosa told them this. **When they came before Rabban Gamliel** and related all that had happened and showed him what they had written, Rabban Gamliel **said to them:** I swear **by the Temple service** that in the time you wrote **you were neither earlier or later; rather, this is how the event** transpired: Precisely at that moment **his fever broke and he asked us for water to drink.**

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai*: had I pressed my head between my knees all day long, *lo hayu mashgichim alav* — they would not have paid attention to me in heaven. The contrast is arresting: the greatest *talmid chacham* of his generation, yet his prayers would not have been accepted where *Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa*'s were — and in only a few minutes at that. His wife pressed him: *vechi Chanina gadol mimcha?* Is he greater than you? He replied: *lav* — no. But *hu domeh ke-eved lifnei ha-Melech, va-ani domeh ke-sar lifnei ha-Melech* — he is like a servant before the king who is always present, always in close proximity; I am like an officer who has audiences only a few times a year.

### Zakkai Son

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשׁוּב מַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן דּוֹסָא שֶׁהָלַךְ לִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה אֵצֶל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי, וְחָלָה בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי. אָמַר לוֹ: חֲנִינָא בְּנִי, בַּקֵּשׁ עָלָיו רַחֲמִים וְיִחְיֶה. הִנִּיחַ רֹאשׁוֹ בֵּין בִּרְכָּיו וּבִקֵּשׁ עָלָיו רַחֲמִים, וְחָיָה. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי: אִלְמָלֵי הֵטִיחַ בֶּן זַכַּאי אֶת רֹאשׁוֹ בֵּין בִּרְכָּיו כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ — לֹא הָיוּ מַשְׁגִּיחִים עָלָיו. אָמְרָה לוֹ אִשְׁתּוֹ: וְכִי חֲנִינָא גָּדוֹל מִמְּךָ? אָמַר לָהּ: לָאו, אֶלָּא הוּא דּוֹמֶה כְּעֶבֶד לִפְנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ, וַאֲנִי דּוֹמֶה כְּשַׂר לִפְנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** there was **another incident** involving **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Dosa, who went to study Torah before Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Zakkai, and Rabbi Yoḥanan's son fell ill. He said to him: Ḥanina, my son, pray for mercy on behalf of** my son **so that he will live. Rabbi Ḥanina ben Dosa placed his head between his knees** in order to meditate **and prayed for mercy upon his behalf, and** Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Zakkai's son **lived. Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Zakkai said** about himself: **Had ben Zakkai stuck his head between my knees throughout the entire day, they would not have paid attention to me in heaven. His wife said to him: And is Ḥanina greater than you? He replied to her: No, but** his prayer is better received than my own because **he is like a servant before the King,** and as such he is able to enter before the King and make various requests at all times. **I,** on the other hand, **am like a minister before the King,** and I can enter only when invited and can make requests only with regard to especially significant matters.

A further story: *Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa* had gone to study *Torah* from *Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai* — which itself establishes the hierarchy of *Torah* scholarship, *Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai* being the greater *talmid chacham*. While there, *Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai*'s son fell ill. *Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai* asked him: *Chanina beni, bakesh alav rachamim ve-yichyeh* — Chanina, my son, pray for my son and he will live. *Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa hiniach rosho bein birkav*, he placed his head between his knees and prayed, and the boy lived.

### Servant Officer

The intimacy of the servant — constant, familiar, unremarkable in its access — is paradoxically more effective for certain purposes than the formal access of the officer. The *Gemara* here makes a genuine distinction between two human types: the *talmid chacham* who excels in *Torah* and the individual who excels in *tefillah* and proximity to God. They are not the same. *Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai* does not claim that *Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa* is his superior in *Torah* — he is quite explicitly his student. But in the mode of *tefillah*, in the intimacy of standing before God, the *Gemara* recognizes a different kind of greatness altogether.

## Prayer Setting

### Windows Jerusalem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אַל יִתְפַּלֵּל אָדָם אֶלָּא בְּבַיִת שֶׁיֵּשׁ שָׁם חַלּוֹנוֹת. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְכַוִּין פְּתִיחָן לֵיהּ בְּעִלִּיתֵהּ (לָקֳבֵל) [נֶגֶד] יְרוּשְׁלֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** on the topic of prayer, **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One may only pray in a house with windows,** as then he can see the heavens and focus his heart, **as it is stated** with regard to Daniel's prayer: "In his attic **there were open windows** facing Jerusalem" (Daniel 6:11).

*Amar Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba amar Rabbi Yochanan: al yitpalel adam ela be-vayit she-yesh bo chalonot* — one should pray only in a house with windows. The source is *Daniel*, whose windows in his upper chamber were open toward Jerusalem. *Rashi* explains that windows allow one to look outward toward the heavens, and this supports *kavana* — the awareness of standing before God.

### Chutzpah Behaviors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: חֲצִיף עֲלַי מַאן דִּמְצַלֵּי בְּבַקְתָּא. וְאָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: חֲצִיף עֲלַי מַאן דִּמְפָרֵשׁ חִטְאֵיהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אַשְׁרֵי נְשׂוּי פֶּשַׁע כְּסוּי חֲטָאָה״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the appropriate place to pray, **Rav Kahana said: I consider impudent one who prays in a field.** Employing parallel language, **Rav Kahana also said: I consider impudent one who specifies his transgression, as it is stated: "Happy is he whose iniquity is forgiven, whose transgression is covered over"** (Psalms 32:1); one who conceals his transgressions indicates that he is ashamed of them, and due to his shame he will be forgiven.

*Amar Rav Kahana: chatzifa alai man de-matzli be-vikta* — it is like *chutzpah* to me when someone *davens* in a valley, which is presumably a place of traffic and distraction. Prayer requires a setting conducive to *kavana*, and praying where distractions are abundant reflects a certain carelessness about the act itself.

*V'amar Rav Kahana: chatzifa alai man de-mifaresh chatei* — it is also *chutzpah* to one who publicly announces and elaborates on all of his past sins. The *pasuk* is cited: *ashrei nesui pesha kisui chata'ah* — blessed is the one whose transgression is borne and whose sin is covered. The ideal is the covering and moving past of sin, not its public recitation and display. And with these final words of *Rav Kahana*, the first half of Masechet Berakhot concludes.

*Hadran alach.*
