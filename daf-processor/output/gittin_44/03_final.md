# Gittin 44 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Slaves: Status & Property

### Tikun olam & Slaves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹכֵר עַבְדּוֹ לַגּוֹיִם אוֹ לְחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ – יָצָא בֶּן חוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In a case of **one who sells his slave to gentiles, or** even **to** a Jew **outside of Eretz** Yisrael, the slave **is emancipated.** Since the slave, who is partially obligated in the fulfillment of mitzvot, would be restricted in his ability to fulfill them in his new situation, either because he would be under the authority of a gentile or because he will no longer be in Eretz Yisrael, the Sages penalized his original owner that he should become a freeman, so that if he succeeds in escaping his new owner, he is a full-fledged freeman.

The daf we take up today continues the *Gemara*'s sustained engagement with the laws governing slaves, which has emerged as a central preoccupation of the *tikun olam* enactments of *Gittin*. What makes this discussion particularly striking is the explicit and recurring comparison between the slave and the woman — a comparison that is not merely implicit but structurally built into the halacha itself. The *get shichrur*, the writ of manumission that frees a slave, is derived through a *gzera shava* of *la la* from the *get* that divorces a woman, and the level of the slave's obligation in *mitzvot* is likewise derived from this same verbal analogy.

At first glance, this parallel might seem to cast the woman in an unflattering light — as though she, like the slave, is a form of property being released. But the *Gemara* repeatedly uses the comparison to draw a careful contrast, and it is that contrast which animates much of this *sugya*.

### Issura & Mamona

The key analytical distinction is this: by a woman, there is only *issura* — a dimension of personal status. The laws governing her relationship to her husband are entirely about who she is and to whom she belongs in terms of personal, halachic standing. By the slave, however, there are two dimensions operating simultaneously: *issura*, personal status, and *mamona*, property ownership. The slave is both a person with a halachic identity and an item of property with a monetary value that can be bought, sold, pledged, and inherited.

A great deal of the *sugyot* in this chapter have explored precisely what happens when one of these two dimensions is altered without the other — when a master, for example, renounces monetary ownership (*mafkir*) of the slave without yet executing a formal act of manumission. The *Gemara* is consistent: changing the *mamona* dimension alone does not change the *issura*. The status change requires its own act, its own writ. This dual structure is what makes the slave's situation legally unique, and it is what makes the *tikun olam* concerns here far more complex than anything we encounter in the laws of divorce.

## Sale to Non-Jews

### Mishna: Slave Freed

The *mishna* at the opening of our discussion states: *ha-mokher avdo le-ovdei kokhavim o le-chutz la-aretz yatza ben chorin* — if someone sells his slave to a non-Jew or outside the Land of Israel, the slave goes free. The language sounds automatic: *yatza ben chorin*, he goes free, as though the sale itself operates the manumission. We will see that the *baraita* qualifies this considerably, but the broad principle is established here.

### Rationale: Mitzvot

The underlying rationale is that a slave is obligated in *mitzvot* to a degree comparable to that of a woman — again, the *la la gzera shava* at work. He is obligated in all negative commandments and in all positive commandments that are not time-bound. This partial but real level of *kedusha yisrael*, as it were, means that placing him under the dominion of a non-Jew creates a serious and ongoing violation. The non-Jew will compel him to work on *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*, will feed him food that is not *kosher*, and will in every practical sense prevent him from observing the *mitzvot* to which he is obligated.

The case of selling to *chutz la-aretz* is a lesser concern — the slave does not lose his obligation in all *mitzvot*, only in those that apply specifically in *Eretz Yisrael* — but it remains a genuine halachic problem. Notably, the comparison to the woman surfaces again here: there is an extensive halachic discussion about whether a husband can compel his wife to move to *chutz la-aretz*, and the answer is that he generally cannot force her to give up life in *Eretz Yisrael*. The parallel, though imperfect, reflects a shared underlying concern about the religious consequences of displacement.

### Jewish Presence

There is an additional dimension to the prohibition that goes beyond the individual slave's ability to observe *mitzvot*. *Chazal* were deeply invested in maintaining Jewish presence in the Land of Israel and issued broad *takanot* designed to prevent Jews living in Israel from transferring land, slaves, and cattle to non-Jews. All of these — land, animals, slaves — were understood as elements of an established, rooted presence in the land, and their transfer to non-Jewish ownership was seen as erosion of that presence. This communal concern does not displace the *mitzvot* rationale, which remains primary and most acute in the case of sale to a non-Jew, but it operates alongside it, especially in the case of sale to *chutz la-aretz*.

## Baraita: Writ Needed

### Get Shichrur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמּוֹכֵר עַבְדּוֹ לַגּוֹיִם – יָצָא לְחֵירוּת, וְצָרִיךְ גֵּט שִׁחְרוּר מֵרַבּוֹ רִאשׁוֹן. אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – שֶׁלֹּא כָּתַב עָלָיו אוֹנוֹ, אֲבָל כָּתַב עָלָיו אוֹנוֹ – זֶהוּ שִׁחְרוּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** (*Tosefta*, *Avoda Zara* 3:16): In a case of **one who sells his slave to gentiles,** the slave **is emancipated, but** nevertheless **requires a bill of manumission from his first master. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said: In what** case **is this statement said? When** the master **did not write a document for him, but** if **he wrote a document for him,** then **this is his emancipation** and he does not require a bill of manumission.

The *baraita* immediately clarifies that the *mishna*'s language of *yatza ben chorin* is not as automatic as it sounds: *ha-mokher avdo le-oved kokhavim yatza le-cheirus v-tzarich get shichrur me-rabo rishon* — he has gone out to freedom, but he requires a writ of manumission from his first master. The first master is the one who must act, since the non-Jew who now holds him cannot effect a change in the slave's halachic personal status. A non-Jew cannot write a *get shichrur* that alters the slave's *issura* dimension.

### Rabban Shimon

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* adds a nuance: *bameh devarim amurim ke-shelo katav alav ono* — when is this requirement for a formal writ applicable? When no document has already been written. *Aval katav alav ono zehu shichruro* — if a document has been written for him, that document itself constitutes his freedom. The question then becomes: what exactly is this *ono*, this document?

### Form of Writ

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״אוֹנוֹ״? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת, דִּכְתַב לֵיהּ הָכִי: לִכְשֶׁתִּבְרַח מִמֶּנּוּ, אֵין לִי עֵסֶק בָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the nature of this **document? Rav Sheshet said: He writes to him like this: When you will escape from** the gentile, **I have no business with you.** Even though this is not an explicit bill of manumission, it is sufficient for him to be considered a freeman.

*Rav Sheshet* explains its content: the master writes to the slave before the sale the following language — *likshe-tivrach mimeni ein li esek bach* — if you ever manage to escape from the one to whom I am selling you, I make no claim upon you. Formally, this language sounds more like *hefker* — a renunciation of ownership — than like a declaration of *shichrur*, a change of personal status. And yet, when it is committed to writing and delivered to the slave as a writ, it can function as his *shtar shichrur*.

*Tosafot* raises the obvious difficulty: the writ cannot take effect at the moment of writing, because if the slave were freed at that moment, the subsequent sale to the non-Jew would be invalid — you cannot sell a free man. The document must therefore be conditional, taking effect only when the slave escapes. The phrase *likshe-tivrach mimeni* is exactly this: the freedom is triggered by the condition of escape.

### Hefker vs Shichrur

This brings us to a conceptually important distinction. *Hefker* and *shichrur* are not the same thing, even though both involve the master relinquishing his hold over the slave. *Hefker* addresses only the *mamona* dimension — the master renounces monetary ownership. But the *issura* dimension, the slave's personal status, is not altered by *hefker* alone. *Shichrur* — effected through the formal act of delivering a *shtar* — addresses the status dimension directly. What *Rav Sheshet* is telling us is that the language of the document need not explicitly say *harei atah ben chorin*; it can articulate the *mamona* dimension in the form of a renunciation, but because it is being delivered through the ritual act of *shtar* delivery, it effects the change of status as well. It is the formal act, the giving of the *shtar*, that carries the halachic weight, not merely the content of the declaration.

## Tosafot: Labor Only

### Non-Jew Labor Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לָוָה עָלָיו מִן הַגּוֹי, כֵּיוָן שֶׁעָשָׂה לוֹ גּוֹי נִמוּסוֹ – יָצָא לְחֵירוּת. מַאי נִמוּסוֹ? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר יְהוּדָה: נַשְׁקִי.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** If **he borrows from a gentile on the basis of** the slave, i.e., using the slave as collateral so that the creditor can collect the slave in payment of the debt in the event that the debtor defaults, then **once** the **gentile behaves with** the slave according to **his law [*nimmuso*],** the slave **is emancipated,** just like a slave who is sold to a gentile. The Gemara asks: **What** is defined as: **His law?** The Gemara answers: **Rav Huna bar Yehuda said:** He places **a seal [*nashkei*]** upon him.

*Tosafot* addresses a deeper structural problem: how can the first master's writ take effect at all once the slave is under the control of the non-Jew and entirely outside the master's reach? The answer is grounded in an earlier principle established in the *Gemara*: *lo hem konin mikem* — non-Jews cannot acquire slaves from Jews in the full sense of ownership. More precisely, a non-Jew can only acquire *maasei yadayim*, the labor and work product of the slave, but cannot acquire the slave's *guf*, his body and person, as property.

### Master Owns Body

The practical consequence of this principle is far-reaching. Because the non-Jew acquires only the slave's labor and not his person, the original Jewish master retains full ownership of the slave as property — ownership of his *guf*. And since he retains that ownership, he retains the standing and the capacity to free the slave. The sale to the non-Jew transfers only one layer of the slave's dual status: the *mamona* dimension of his labor is transferred, but the *mamona* dimension of his body — and with it the *issura* dimension entirely — remains with the original master. It is precisely this retained ownership that allows the first master to write a *get shichrur* that has legal effect even after the sale has been completed.

## Loan & Collateral

### Nimuso Definition

The *Gemara* now moves to a related but distinct scenario governed by a new *baraita*: *laveh alav min ha-oved kokhavim* — you borrowed money from a non-Jew with your slave as collateral. The keyword here is *alav*: you borrowed specifically *against* the slave, designating him explicitly as the surety. This is crucially different from simply borrowing money and having the non-Jew later choose to collect against your slave. When you designate the slave as the collateral, you have in effect chosen to put him in harm's way, and the *Gemara* treats that as analogous to a sale.

The question the *baraita* poses is: at what point does the non-Jew's relationship with the slave rise to the level that triggers the obligation to free him? The *baraita*'s answer is: *keivan she-asah lo oved kokhavim nimuso* — once the non-Jew does for him his *nimuso*. The term *nomos*, from which *nimuso* derives, means law or custom, but what specific act or event does it describe?

### Nimuso = Seal

*Rav Huna bar Yehuda* proposes that *nimuso* refers to a *nashki* — some kind of seal, mark, or perhaps even a physical brand that the non-Jew places on the slave to indicate ownership. Once the non-Jew has marked the slave as his, the slave is considered effectively in his possession, and the obligation to free him is triggered.

### Sheshet's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הָאֲרִיסִין, וְהַחֲכִירוֹת, וַאֲרִיסֵי בָּתֵּי אָבוֹת, וְגוֹי שֶׁמִּשְׁכֵּן שָׂדֵהוּ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל; אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁעָשָׂה לוֹ נִמוּסוֹ – פְּטוּרָה מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet raises an objection** to this based on a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Terumot* 2:11): If there was a field owned by a gentile, but there were Jewish **sharecroppers, or** Jewish **tenant farmers, or** Jewish **family sharecroppers,** i.e., an entire family of sharecroppers who work a field generation after generation; **or** in the case of **a gentile who mortgaged his field to a Jew,** then **even though** the gentile **acted for** the Jew **based on his,** the Jew's, **law,** the field is **exempt from tithes,** because the field belongs to a gentile. It is not considered as if it were transferred to the Jew.

*Rav Sheshet* objects forcefully: the same word *nimuso* appears in the context of a field taken as collateral — *oved kokhavim she-mishkan sadeh le-Yisrael* — where a non-Jew has pledged his field to a Jew as surety for a loan. In that case as well, the *baraita* speaks of the *nimuso* occurring. But you do not place a seal on a field. A field cannot be branded or marked the way a slave can. Therefore, *nimuso* cannot mean a physical seal or mark; it must refer to something else that applies equally to slaves and to fields.

### Nimuso = Time

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ נַשְׁקִי, שָׂדֶה בַּת נַשְׁקִי הִיא? אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: זְמַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if it enters your mind** that the phrase: His law, means **a seal, is a field able to be sealed? Rather, Rav Sheshet says:** The expression: He acted for him based on his law, means **time.** In other words, a deadline was set and if the debt was not paid by the given date the slave would automatically be transferred to the possession of the gentile.

*Rav Sheshet* therefore redefines *nimuso* as *zman* — the time for repayment has arrived. When the loan comes due and the borrower has not repaid, the non-Jew is now legally positioned to take possession of the collateral. In the slave's case, this means the non-Jew can now claim the slave, and at that moment the obligation to free the slave is triggered. In the field case, the parallel language in the *baraita* refers to the same *zman* arriving — the time for collection — which is why the same term is used for both.

## Body vs Field

### Difficulty Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָא זְמַן אַזְּמַן! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דִּמְטָא זִמְנֵיהּ, הָא דְּלָא מְטָא זִמְנֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If that is so, there is a **difficulty** with regard to the issue of **time** in the case of the slave **and** the issue of **time** in the case of the field. In the case of the slave the *halakha* is that after the set time, he leaves the debtor's authority and is emancipated, while in the case of the field its produce does not become obligated to be tithed like the produce of a Jew's field after that set time. The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult,** since the following distinction can be made: **This,** the case of the slave who is emancipated, is referring to **when his time** to be transferred **has arrived,** and **this,** the case of the field, is referring to where **its time** to be transferred **has not** yet **arrived.**

This resolution, however, generates a new difficulty. If *nimuso* means the *zman* has arrived in both cases, why does the arrival of the *zman* trigger the obligation to free the slave but not create an obligation regarding the field? The *Gemara* formulates this as *kashya zman a-zman*: the same moment in time is being described, yet the legal consequences differ. The *Gemara* proposes that perhaps in the slave case we are speaking of a moment when the *zman* has arrived, while in the field case the *zman* has not yet arrived — but this reading forces the same word to describe opposite temporal states in the same *baraita*, which is unsatisfying, and the *Gemara* rejects it.

### Tosafot: Slave In House

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא גַּבֵּי עֶבֶד, דִּמְטָא זִמְנֵיהּ צְרִיכָא לְמֵימַר?! אֶלָּא אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי דְּלָא מְטָא זִמְנֵיהּ, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא לְגוּפָא, וְהָא לְפֵירָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But with regard to a slave whose time to be transferred has arrived,** does it **need to be said** that he is emancipated? Isn't it obvious that once he is transferred to the authority of the gentile, he is emancipated, just as in the case of a sale? **Rather,** the Gemara offers a different explanation: **This** case **and that** case are referring to **when his time** to be transferred **has not** yet **arrived, and** it is **not difficult: This,** the case of the slave who is emancipated, is with regard **to** the slave **himself,** as the slave himself is to be transferred to the gentile, **and that** case is with regard **to** the **produce.** In other words, the Jewish creditor has the rights to the produce of the field, but he does not take possession of the actual field. Therefore, it remains exempt from tithes.

*Tosafot* offers the more coherent resolution. In both cases, the *zman* has not yet arrived — meaning the loan is not yet due. But the two situations differ structurally. When a slave is designated as collateral, the non-Jew typically takes physical possession of the slave before the loan comes due. The slave is already in the non-Jew's house, already under his daily control, already being forced to work on *Shabbat* and eat *treif* and violate *halacha* in every practical dimension of his life. The *guf* of the slave — his body, his person — has already been taken into the non-Jew's possession, even though the *zman* for formal collection has not yet arrived.

### Field: Fruit Only

By contrast, a field never moves. When a Jew holds a non-Jew's field as collateral, the Jew may be present on the field, farming it, enjoying its produce — its *peira* — but the *karka* itself, the land, remains exactly where it is. We say the Jew has possession only of the *peira*, the fruit, not of the *guf*, the body, of the field. Since the field itself has not been transferred into anyone's physical possession, there is no parallel to the slave situation.

### Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: בְּשֶׁלָּוָה עַל מְנָת לְמַשְׁכְּנוֹ, וְלֹא מִשְׁכְּנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: Even if the time for the slave or the field to be taken as collateral had arrived, there is something novel about this **in** a case **where he borrowed on the condition that** the creditors **collect** from **it,** i.e., the slave or field, **but they did not** yet **collect** from **it.** Since the field had not yet been collected from the gentile by the Jew as payment of the debt, it remains exempt from tithes, but the mere fact that the Jew agreed to have his slave be collected suffices for the rabbinic penalty to take effect, and the slave is emancipated.

The operative distinction, then, is whether the *guf* has been physically transferred into the non-Jew's domain. Once the slave is living in the non-Jew's house and being subjected daily to violations of *halacha*, the obligation to free him is triggered — regardless of whether the formal *zman* of the loan has arrived. The ongoing nature of the violation, the daily compelled breach of *mitzvot*, is what creates the urgency and triggers the *takana*.

## Seizure vs Forced Sale

### Debt Seizure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: גְּבָאוֹ בְּחוֹבוֹ אוֹ שֶׁלְּקָחוֹ סִיקָרִיקוֹן, לֹ יָצָא לְחֵירוּת. וּבְחוֹבוֹ לֹא?!
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** (*Tosefta*, *Avoda Zara* 3:16): If a gentile **collected** a slave **for** payment of **his debt, or** the slave **was taken by a Sicarius,** i.e., one who would use violence and intimidation to force people to give them their property, then **he is not emancipated.** The Gemara asks: **And** is it so that if a gentile collected a slave **for** payment of **his debt,** the Sages did not institute a penalty and the slave is **not** emancipated?

A further *baraita* introduces two new scenarios that test the boundaries of when the master's choice is implicated. The first: *gevaho be-chovo* — if the non-Jew seized the slave as payment of a general debt, without the master having specifically designated the slave as collateral. In this case, *lo yatza le-cheirut* — the slave does not go free, and the master is not required to redeem and free him. The non-Jew chose to collect against the slave on his own initiative; the master did not choose to expose the slave to non-Jewish ownership. The *Gemara* reinforces this by comparing it to the case of grain seized from a threshing floor for taxes: if the seizure was in payment of a legitimate debt (*bechovo*), the owner has effectively benefited because his debt was discharged, making it akin to a voluntary transaction. But in that case *chamishtershi lei* — the benefit flowed to the owner — whereas in the slave case, even if the debt was discharged, the master did not specifically choose to submit the slave to non-Jewish possession, so we do not impute that choice to him.

The *Gemara* also notes the case of *sikrikon* — a strongman who forces the sale — in which the slave likewise does not go free, because the master was coerced without any meaningful choice or opportunity for alternatives.

### Forced Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא – אָמַר רַב: הַמּוֹכֵר עַבְדּוֹ לְפַרְהַנְגְּ גּוֹי – יָצָא לְחֵירוּת. מַאי הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֶעְבַּד? הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְפַיֵּיס, וְלֹא פִּיֵּיס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses **the** matter **itself. Rav says: One who sells his slave to a gentile** government **official,** then the slave **is emancipated.** The Gemara asks: **What could he have done;** the gentile official forced him to agree to the sale. The Gemara answers: **He should have appeased** the official in some other way, **and he did not appease** him.

The second scenario: *parhang* — a term understood, following *Rashi*, as a kind of strongman or blackmailer, and according to *Tosafot* a Persian term for someone who forces a sale but does pay for the slave. If someone forces you to sell your slave to him, the *Gemara* rules that *yatza le-cheirut* — the slave does go free. This is at first glance surprising: you were forced, so why are you held responsible?

### Responsibility

The *Gemara* answers: *havah lei le-fayusei ve-lo piyes* — you had the option of appeasing the person, of offering him alternative property, of paying him off or negotiating your way out of the situation. The assumption built into the ruling is that you were not entirely without options, and your failure to pursue those options makes you, at least partially, the agent of the slave's transfer into non-Jewish hands. Because you could have prevented it and did not, you are treated as though you chose it.

### No Liability

The contrast is clear: where the seizure was entirely beyond the master's control — through sheer military force or the like, where no negotiation or substitution was possible — the master is not held liable, and the obligation to redeem and free the slave does not arise. The threshold is meaningful choice and meaningful opportunity to act otherwise. When that opportunity existed and was not used, the master bears responsibility.

## Conditional Scenarios

### Thirty-Day Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: מְכָרוֹ לִשְׁלשִׁים יוֹם, מַהוּ? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַב: הַמּוֹכֵר עַבְדּוֹ לְפַרְהַנְגְּ גּוֹי – יָצָא לְחֵירוּת! הָתָם בְּפַרְהַנְגְּ גּוֹי שֶׁאֵינָהּ חוֹזֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma:** If **he sold** the slave to a gentile **for thirty days,** then **what is** the *halakha*; is this considered to be a sale and he is emancipated as a result, or is it not a sale? The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof, **as Rav says:** With regard to **one who sells his slave to a gentile** government **official,** the slave **is emancipated.** The assumption is that he would be sold to the official in order to work for a limited amount of time or perform a specific task, yet he is emancipated. The Gemara answers: **There,** he was sold **to a gentile** government **official, as** this sale **is not reversed.** No proof can be brought from here with regard to the *halakha* of a sale that is in effect for a limited duration.

*Rabbi Yirmiyah* poses a series of penetrating questions about cases that fall at the edges of these categories. The first: *machro li-shloshim yom mahu* — what if you sold the slave to a non-Jew for only thirty days, with the understanding that the slave returns to you afterward? The slave will be compelled to violate *halacha* for thirty days, but it is not a permanent sale. Are you required, when the slave returns at the end of the thirty days, to then free him? The *Gemara* attempts to resolve this from the case of the *parhang*, reasoning that in that scenario the non-Jew presumably intends eventually to resell the slave. But the *Gemara* rejects this inference because the *parhang* case involves a sale with no anticipated return (*she-eino chozer*), which is not comparable to an explicitly time-limited transaction. The question therefore remains unresolved.

### No Work Clause

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְכָרוֹ חוּץ מִמְּלַאכְתּוֹ, מַהוּ? חוּץ מִן הַמִּצְוֹת, מַהוּ? חוּץ מִשַּׁבָּתוֹת וְיָמִים טוֹבִים, מַהוּ? לְגֵר תּוֹשָׁב, לְיִשְׂרָאֵל מְשׁוּמָּד, מַהוּ? לְכוּתִי, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yirmeya asks several questions with regard to the extent of the application of this penalty: If **he sold** the slave to a gentile **aside from his labor,** i.e., the gentile will own the slave but he will still perform labor for the Jewish master, **what is** the *halakha*? If he sold him to a gentile **aside from the mitzvot,** i.e., he stipulated that the slave would be able to continue observing the mitzvot, **what is** the *halakha*? If he sold him **aside from *Shabbatot* and Festivals, what is** the *halakha*? If he sold him **to a gentile who resides in Eretz Yisrael and observes the seven Noahide mitzvot [*ger toshav*],** or **to a Jewish apostate, what is** the *halakha*? If he sold him **to a Samaritan, what is** the *halakha*?

A second question: *chutz mi-melachto mahu* — what if you sell the slave to a non-Jew on the explicit condition that the non-Jew does not put him to work? *Rashi* explains the realistic context: the non-Jew might want the slave not for labor but to have him breed with the non-Jew's own female slave, producing additional slaves. In such a case, the slave might not be compelled to violate *Shabbat* through labor. Does the condition change the master's obligation?

### Mitzvot Clause

A third question: *chutz min ha-mitzvos mahu* — what if you sell the slave on the explicit condition that he is permitted to observe his religious obligations? Or more specifically, *chutz mi-Shabbasos ve-yamim tovim* — on the condition that he is given *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* off? If all of this is written into the contract, does that now exempt the master from the obligation to redeem and free him?

### Mixed Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשׁוֹט מִיהָא חֲדָא: גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב הֲרֵי הוּא כְּגוֹי. כּוּתִי וְיִשְׂרָאֵל מְשׁוּמָּד – אָמְרִי לַהּ כְּגוֹי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ כְּיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **You can resolve at least one** of these questions, as it was taught: **A *ger toshav* is like a gentile.** With regard to a **Samaritan and a Jewish apostate, some say** they are **like gentiles and some say** they are **like Jews.**

*Rabbi Yirmiyah* then extends the inquiry to questions of the buyer's identity: *le-ger toshav o le-Yisrael mumar mahu* — what if the sale is to a *ger toshav*, a non-Jew living in the Land of Israel who observes the *sheva mitzvos b-nei Noach*, or to a *Yisrael mumar*, a Jew who is formally Jewish but entirely non-observant? And *le-Kuti mahu* — what about a Samaritan, whose status as Jew or non-Jew is itself debated?

These questions cut to the heart of a broader jurisprudential tension: do we apply formal categories — Jew versus non-Jew — regardless of the contextual reality of whether the slave will actually be able to observe *halacha*? Or do we take into account the practical circumstances? A *ger toshav* might in practice respect the slave's religious life far more than a non-observant Jew would. Does that practical reality count?

### Status Determinations

The *Gemara* partially resolves one of these questions: *ger toshav harei hu ke-oved kokhavim* — a *ger toshav* is treated like a non-Jew for our purposes. His observance of the Noachide laws does not give him the status of a Jew, and the formal rule applies. As for *Kuti ve-Yisrael mumar*, the *Gemara* records a dispute: *amri lei ke-ovdei kokhavim ve-amri lei ke-Yisrael* — some say they are treated like non-Jews, some say like Jews. That debate remains unresolved, as do the questions about the conditional sales and the clauses regarding *Shabbat* observance. The *Gemara* leaves *Rabbi Yirmiyah*'s questions as *teiku* — unresolved — reflecting the genuine difficulty of adjudicating between formal status and contextual reality.

## Accepting Compensation

### Money Instead

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַבִּי אַמֵּי: עֶבֶד שֶׁהִפִּיל עַצְמוֹ לִגְיָיסוֹת, וְאֵין רַבּוֹ יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיאוֹ – לֹא בְּדִינֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְלֹא בְּדִינֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם, מַהוּ שֶׁיִּטּוֹל אֶת דָּמָיו?
>
> **Translation:** **They raised a dilemma before Rabbi Ami:** If **a slave** fled from his master and **gave himself over to a** foreign **army** to serve as a solider, **and his master cannot remove him, neither through Jewish law nor through the laws of the nations of the world, what is** the *halakha*? Is it permitted **for** the master **to** at least **take his value** from the army, or would this be considered as if he is selling the slave?

The *Gemara* now takes up a practical scenario of a different kind. A non-Jew has taken your slave without your consent — through force or coercion — and while you cannot recover the slave himself, the non-Jew offers you money in compensation. May you accept it? On the surface, accepting money might seem tantamount to ratifying the sale and abandoning your obligation to redeem and free the slave. But if recovery is genuinely impossible, is it permitted to at least salvage the monetary value?

### Investigation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה לְרַבִּי זְרִיקָא: פּוֹק עַיֵּין בִּמְכִילָתָיךְ. נְפַק דָּק וְאַשְׁכַּח, דְּתַנְיָא: הַמּוֹכֵר בֵּיתוֹ לְגוֹי – דָּמָיו אֲסוּרִין. וְגוֹי שֶׁאָנַס בֵּיתוֹ שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְאֵין בְּעָלָיו יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיאוֹ – לֹא בְּדִינֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְלֹא בְּדִינֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם, מוּתָּר לִיטּוֹל אֶת דָּמָיו, וְכוֹתֵב וּמַעֲלֶה בְּעַרְכָּאוֹת שֶׁלָּהֶן – מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כְּמַצִּיל מִיָּדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya said to Rabbi Zerika: Go out and examine your *mishnayot*** to find an answer. **He went out, examined, and discovered** an answer, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Avoda Zara* 6:2): **One who sells his house** in Eretz Yisrael **to a gentile, the monies** received from the sale of the house **are forbidden** to him. **And** if there was **a gentile who seized a Jew's house** by force **and its owner cannot remove it,** i.e., get it back, **neither through Jewish law nor through the laws of the nations of the world,** then **he is permitted to take** the house's **value** from the gentile, **and he** may even **write** a document **and register** the sale **in their courts, because he is like one who rescues** the money **from their possession.** Although it is prohibited for a Jew to sell his house in Eretz Yisrael to a gentile, if it was taken from him by force he is permitted to take payment for it. Similarly, if the slave cannot be retrieved from a gentile, it should be permitted for him to take money in return.

*Rabbi Yirmiyah* instructs *Rabbi Zerika* to investigate the *tannaic* sources — his *michilta*, his collections of teachings — because *Rabbi Ami* has been asked this question and needs the relevant source material to render a ruling.

### House Analogy

*Rabbi Zerika* finds the following *baraita*: if someone sells his house to a non-Jew — which is itself prohibited, both because of the biblical *lo techonem* prohibition against giving non-Jews a foothold in the land and because of the broader rabbinic *takanot* about maintaining Jewish presence in *Eretz Yisrael* — the proceeds of such a sale are forbidden as *issur hana'ah*. However, if a non-Jew forcibly seized the house and *ein ba-alav yakhol le-hotzio lo be-dinei Yisrael ve-lo be-dinei ovdei kokhavim* — the owner cannot recover it through any legal means, neither in Jewish courts nor in non-Jewish courts — then *mutar litol dameihah*, the owner may collect monetary compensation. Furthermore, *ve-chotev u-ma'aleh be-arka'ot shelahem*, he may even register the transaction in non-Jewish civil courts to ensure the money is legally enforceable — because *mipnei she-hu ke-matzil mi-yadam*, this is considered salvaging what remains from their hands, not voluntarily selling to them.

### Slippery Slope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי בַּיִת – דְּכֵיוָן דְּלָא סַגִּי לֵיהּ בְּלֹא בַּיִת, לָא אָתֵי לְזַבּוֹנֵיהּ; אֲבָל עַבְדָּא – דְּסַגִּי לֵיהּ בְּלָא עַבְדָּא, אָתֵי לְזַבּוֹנֵיהּ – אוֹ לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this comparison: **But perhaps this matter applies** only to a **house, that since it is not sufficient,** i.e., not possible, **for him** to live **without a house, he would not sell it** willingly. Therefore, there is no reason to penalize him when it is taken by force. **But** with regard to **a slave, as it is sufficient for him** to live **without a slave,** there is a concern that **he will** also **come to sell him** willingly, and therefore there should be a penalty in this case as well. **Or** it is possible that this distinction is **not** made.

The *Gemara* raises the question of whether this precedent from the house can be applied to the slave. Perhaps they are not comparable. With regard to a house, people do not typically sell their homes freely; the loss of a house is a severe and unusual circumstance, and therefore permitting the monetary collection after the fact will not create a *slippery slope* toward people engineering such situations in order to profit. But a slave is more expendable — *sagi lei be-lo eved* — people can live without a slave, and if we allow the owner to simply collect money and walk away, it might encourage masters to arrange such forced seizures deliberately.

### Rabbi Ami Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לְהוּ רַבִּי אַמֵּי: ״מִינַּי אַמֵּי בַּר נָתָן, תּוֹרָה יוֹצְאָה לְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל: עֶבֶד שֶׁהִפִּיל עַצְמוֹ לִגְיָיסוֹת, וְאֵין רַבּוֹ יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיאוֹ – לֹא בְּדִינֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְלֹא בְּדִינֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם, מוּתָּר לִיטּוֹל אֶת דָּמָיו, וְכוֹתֵב וּמַעֲלֶה בְּעַרְכָּאוֹת שֶׁל גּוֹיִם – מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כְּמַצִּיל מִיָּדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami sent** the following message **to** the other Sages: **From me, Ami bar Natan, Torah emerges to all of Israel:** If **a slave** fled his master and **gave himself to a** foreign **army** to serve as a solider, **and his master cannot remove him, neither through Jewish law nor through the laws of the nations of the world,** then he is **permitted to take** the slave's **value, and he writes** a deed of sale **and registers** this transaction **in gentile courts, because he is like one who rescues** the money **from their possession.**

Nevertheless, *Rabbi Ami* concludes that the house precedent is sufficiently analogous and issues a formal ruling — framed with the weight of a public proclamation, *mini Ami bar Natan Torah yotza le-khol Yisrael* — that from him the following teaching goes out to all of Israel: a slave who has fallen into the hands of soldiers (*geisot*), and whose master cannot recover him through any legal means, whether in Jewish courts or in non-Jewish courts — *mutar litol dameihav ve-chotev u-ma'aleh be-arka'ot shel ovdei kokhavim mipnei she-hu ke-matzil mi-yadam* — the master may collect the compensation and may register the arrangement in non-Jewish civil courts, because this is simply salvaging what can be salvaged. The formal prohibition on selling to non-Jews is not violated when the transaction is already a fait accompli that cannot be undone.

## Redemption Penalty

### Price Multiple

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: הַמּוֹכֵר עַבְדּוֹ לְגוֹי – קוֹנְסִים אוֹתוֹ עַד מֵאָה בְּדָמָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** With regard to **one who sells his slave to a gentile,** even though he can no longer enslave him, **he is penalized** and is forced to redeem the slave from the gentile for **up to one hundred** times the slave's **value.**

*Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* now introduces the mechanism by which the entire rabbinic system discourages the sale of slaves to non-Jews in the first place: *ha-mokher avdo le-ovdei kokhavim kohnsin oto ad me'ah be-dameihav* — if you sell your slave to a non-Jew, the court compels you to buy him back at up to one hundred times his value. The obligation to free the slave is not merely moral; it is backed by an extraordinary financial penalty designed to make the transgression so costly that no rational master would undertake it. Whether this penalty was ever successfully enforced in practice is a separate question, but as a statement of halachic principle it is remarkable.

### Precise or Approximate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּוְקָא, אוֹ לָאו דַּוְקָא? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הַמּוֹכֵר בְּהֵמָה גַּסָּה לְגוֹי – קוֹנְסִים אוֹתוֹ עַד עֲשָׂרָה בְּדָמֶיהָ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is this amount stated **specifically or not specifically?** Perhaps this number is an exaggeration? The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** an answer from **that** which **Reish Lakish says: One who sells a large domesticated animal to a gentile, he is penalized** and is forced to purchase the animal back from the gentile for **up to ten** times **its value.** It can be seen here that one who violates an ordinance of the Sages by engaging in a prohibited sale must pay up to only ten times the item's value to purchase it back, and the same would presumably apply to the case of the slave.

The *Gemara* immediately asks whether the figure of one hundred is *davka* — a precise upper limit — or merely an approximation, an expression of "a very great multiple."

### Animal Comparison

*Reish Lakish* provides a parallel ruling: *ha-mokher behemah gasah le-ovdei kokhavim kohnsin oto ad asarah be-dameihah* — if you sell a large animal to a non-Jew, which is similarly prohibited (the *Gemara* in *Avodah Zarah* explains this is because animals can be worked on *Shabbat*, and there are also considerations of maintaining Jewish presence in the land), you are fined up to ten times the animal's value. Since the slave *baraita* says one hundred and the animal *baraita* says ten, these cannot both be approximations meaning the same thing. The numbers must be *davka*, and they differ.

### Slave vs Animal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא שָׁאנֵי עֶבֶד, דְּכֹל יוֹמָא וְיוֹמָא מַפְקַע לֵיהּ מִמִּצְווֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **But perhaps a slave is different, as each and every day** the owner **releases him from** the fulfillment of **mitzvot** by selling him to a gentile, so there may be a greater penalty as a result.

The *Gemara* then wrestles with the direction of the reasoning. One version of *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*'s statement has the slave at ten times and the animal at one hundred times — the mirror image of the other version. The *Gemara* considers whether the slave's penalty might be lower because the slave eventually must be freed rather than returned (*de-lo hadar lei*), meaning the master recovers nothing at the end of the redemption. But this reasoning is immediately questioned: if the animal *is* returned to the master upon redemption, why must the master pay up to one hundred times its value just to get it back? That seems disproportionate to the single unit of value gained from the return.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא עֶבֶד מִילְּתָא דְלָא שְׁכִיחָא, וּמִלְּתָא דְלָא שְׁכִיחָא לָא גָּזְרִי בַּהּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara offers a different distinction: The sale of **a slave** is **an uncommon matter, and the Sages did not decree** with regard to **an uncommon matter.** Therefore, one cannot compare the penalty in the case of selling a slave to the penalty in the case of selling an animal.

The *Gemara* ultimately resolves the matter differently: *eved milta de-lo shechicha* — selling a slave to a non-Jew is a rare transgression. *U-milta de-lo shechicha lo gazru bah Rabbanan* — the rabbis do not need to impose an extremely heavy fine on something that rarely occurs. Hence the slave's fine is ten times. The animal is one hundred times precisely because animal sales to non-Jews were more common and required a more powerful deterrent.

## Heir Liability

### Question Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה מֵרַבִּי אַסִּי: מָכַר עַבְדּוֹ וָמֵת, מַהוּ שֶׁיִּקְנְסוּ אֶת בְּנוֹ אַחֲרָיו? אִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר: ״צָרַם אוֹזֶן בְּכוֹר, וָמֵת – קָנְסוּ בְּנוֹ אַחֲרָיו״ – מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיסּוּרָא דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הִיא, אֲבָל הָכָא אִיסּוּרָא דְרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raised a dilemma before Rabbi Asi:** If one **sold his slave** to a gentile **and died, what is** the *halakha*: **Is his son penalized after him?** Is the son also required to redeem the slave, or does the penalty apply only to the seller? The Gemara compares this to other penalties assessed by the Sages. **If you say,** in accordance with the opinion that holds that if one **slit** the **ear** of a **firstborn** animal and by doing so intentionally blemishes it so that it may be eaten, **and** then that person **died,** then **his son is penalized after him** and his son may not slaughter and eat it, perhaps this is **because it is a prohibition by Torah law. Here, however,** with regard to the sale of a slave, it is **a prohibition** only **by rabbinic** law and perhaps the son is not penalized.

*Rabbi Yirmiyah* poses the final major question of the *sugya* to *Rabbi Assi*: *machar avdo u-met mahu she-yiknasu et b-no acharav* — if a master sold his slave to a non-Jew and then died, are his sons obligated to buy the slave back — at the inflated price — and free him? Or does the penalty die with the transgressor?

### Tension

The question pulls in two directions. On the one hand, the rabbinic fine is a form of monetary punishment directed at the person who violated the prohibition. Fines of this kind are inherently personal — they target the sinner, not his estate or his heirs. On the other hand, the slave's situation is not resolved by the master's death. He continues, every single day, to be compelled to violate *halacha* while under non-Jewish ownership. The ongoing violation makes the children's inaction look like their own failure to remedy an existing wrong.

### Bechor Comparison

The *Gemara* examines analogous cases. One is *tzaram ozen bechor u-met* — a *kohen* who deliberately inflicted a blemish on a *bechor* animal in order to avoid the obligation of bringing it to Jerusalem as a sacrifice, thereby violating the biblical prohibition of *kol mum lo yihiyeh bo*, and who was therefore penalized by being forbidden to eat from that animal. If this *kohen* then died, was the fine imposed on his sons as well — *kohnsu b-no acharav*? The consideration is that this was an *issura de-oraisa*, a biblical violation. Perhaps the severity of the biblical transgression justifies extending the penalty to the next generation. But that would not necessarily help us with our case, where the prohibition against selling a slave to a non-Jew is *issura de-rabbanan* — of rabbinic origin.

### Chol HaMoed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר: ״כִּוֵּון מְלַאכְתּוֹ בַּמּוֹעֵד, וָמֵת – לֹא קָנְסוּ בְּנוֹ אַחֲרָיו״ – מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא עֲבַד אִיסּוּרָא, הָכָא מַאי? לְדִידֵיהּ קְנַסוּ רַבָּנַן – וְהָא לֵיתֵיהּ; אוֹ דִילְמָא לְמָמוֹנֵיהּ קְנַסוּ רַבָּנַן – וְהָא אִיתֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that there is a different comparison: The *halakha* is that while there are types of labor that one is permitted to perform on the intermediate days of Passover and *Sukkot*, one may not intentionally schedule the labor to be performed at those times. One who does so is penalized and must forfeit the profits of that labor. The *halakha* is that if one **planned** to perform **his labor on** the intermediate days of **the Festival, and he died,** then **his child is not penalized after him, because** the son **did not perform a prohibited** act. **Here, what** is the *halakha*? Did **the Sages penalize** only **him, and he is no longer** alive, **or perhaps the Sages penalized his property,** by saying that he should lose it, **and** his property still **exists?**

A contrasting case: someone who intentionally delayed performing necessary work until *Chol HaMoed*, at which point he could claim it was a *davar ha-aved* — an unavoidable loss — and thereby permit himself to do labor that he should have completed before the holiday. Having thus circumvented the rabbinic prohibition on labor during *Chol HaMoed*, the person is not permitted to benefit from that labor. If he then died, are his sons also prohibited from benefiting? The *Gemara* suggests that here, precisely because it is a rabbinic violation and moreover because the person did not commit a direct transgression so much as engineer circumstances to exploit a leniency, we would not impose the fine on the children.

But even this does not map perfectly onto the slave case. The *Gemara* notes that the *Chol HaMoed* case might be weaker precisely because the violation was indirect — *de-lo avid issura* — the person did not really commit the prohibited act so much as maneuver around it. The slave case involves a clear and direct rabbinic violation, with the added weight of the slave's ongoing suffering.

### Shmita Precedent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, תְּנֵיתוּהָ: שָׂדֶה שֶׁנִּתְקַוְּוצָה בַּשְּׁבִיעִית, תִּזָּרַע לְמוֹצָאֵי שְׁבִיעִית. נִטַּיְּיבָה אוֹ נִדַּיְּירָה – לֹא תִּזָּרַע לְמוֹצָאֵי שְׁבִיעִית. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, נָקְטִינַן: הֱטִיבָהּ וָמֵת – בְּנוֹ זוֹרְעָהּ. אַלְמָא לְדִידֵיהּ קְנַסוּ רַבָּנַן, לִבְרֵיהּ לָא קְנַסוּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Asi **said to him: You** already **learned** in a mishna (*Shevi'it* 4:2): **A field whose thorns were removed during the Sabbatical** Year **may be sown after the conclusion of the Sabbatical** Year, since removing thorns is not full-fledged labor that renders the produce of the field prohibited. And it is taught in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Shevi'it* 3:6): If **it had been improved** with fertilizer, **or** if **it had been populated** by the owner's herd in order to fertilize the field with their manure, **it may not be sown after the conclusion of the Sabbatical** Year, for the Sages imposed a penalty to prevent one from benefiting from prohibited labor. **And Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: We have a tradition** that if one **improved** his field in a forbidden manner, **and** then **died, his son may sow it. Apparently,** we should infer that the general principle with regard to penalties is that **the Sages applied the penalty to** the one who committed the transgression **himself,** but **the Sages did not penalize his son.**

The *Gemara* ultimately resolves the question by citing the following teaching from *Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Chanina*: if someone fertilized a field during *Shmita* — which is itself a rabbinic prohibition — the field may not be planted after *Shmita* as a penalty. But if the person died, *hetiva u-met b-no zora* — if he fertilized it and died, his son may plant it. *Amar le-didei knasu Rabbanan le-brei lo knasu Rabbanan* — the rabbis imposed the fine on the transgressor himself; they did not impose it on his heirs.

*Tosafot* notes that this parallel is not perfect: one might argue that *Shmita* violation of this kind is only rabbinic, while the slave case involves both a rabbinic violation and the additional dimension of the slave's daily ongoing suffering. Nevertheless, the *Gemara* treats the *Shmita* precedent as sufficiently analogous to establish the principle: rabbinic fines are personal to the transgressor and do not pass to heirs.

### Invisible Damage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, נָקְטִינַן: טִימֵּא טְהָרוֹת שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, וָמֵת – לָא קְנַסוּ רַבָּנַן בְּנוֹ אַחֲרָיו. מַאי טַעְמָא? הֶיזֵּק שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִיכָּר לָא שְׁמֵיהּ הֶיזֵּק, וּקְנָסָא דְּרַבָּנַן הִיא – לְדִידֵיהּ קַנְסוּהוּ רַבָּנַן, לִבְרֵיהּ לָא קָא קְנַסוּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: We have a tradition** that if someone **defiled his friend's ritually pure items,** thereby incurring liability to pay for the damage that he caused, **and died** before paying, the Sages **did not penalize his son after him** to pay for the damage. **What is the reason** for this? **Damage that is not evident,** i.e., that does not involve any physical change to the goods that is visible to the eye, **is not considered damage** by Torah law; **but** since the other party did suffer a loss, there **is a penalty of the Sages. The Sages applied the penalty** only **to** the one who caused the damage **himself,** but **the Sages did not apply the penalty to his son.**

*Abaye* adds a final case that reinforces the principle. If someone took a *sheretz* — a ritually impure rodent — and deliberately rendered his neighbor's *teruma* *tamei*, this constitutes *hezek she-eino nikkar*, damage that is not visible. *Tuma* cannot be seen; the *teruma* looks exactly the same before and after. Under the strict laws of damages, you would be exempt from liability for non-visible damage, because *hezek she-eino nikkar lo shmei hezek* — non-visible damage does not halachically constitute *nezek* in the full legal sense. Nevertheless, the rabbis imposed a fine to deter such behavior. If the person then died, do the children pay the fine? *Lo knasu Rabbanan b-no acharav* — no, the rabbis did not impose this rabbinic fine on the children. *Knasa de-rabbanan hi* — it is a rabbinic penalty, and *le-didei knasu Rabbanan, le-brei lo knasu Rabbanan* — they fined the transgressor; they did not fine his heirs.

The principle is thereby established through multiple cases and articulated cleanly: rabbinic fines are inherently personal. They are a form of monetary punishment imposed on the individual who violated the rabbinic ordinance, and they do not survive him. The obligation to redeem and free the slave — however ongoing and real the slave's situation remains — does not transfer as a penalty to the master's children.
