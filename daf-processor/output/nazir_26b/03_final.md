# Nazir 26b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Maot Setumot

### Unspecified Money Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא מָעוֹת, אֲבָל בְּהֵמָה — הֲרֵי הִיא כִּמְפוֹרֶשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said** that **Rav said: They taught only** that there is a difference between unallocated and allocated money of a nazirite with regard to **money** designated for the purchase of offerings. **However,** if one designated **an animal it is** treated **as allocated.**

The Gemara on 26b opens with a statement of Rav Huna in the name of Rav: *lo shanu ela ma'ot* — the rule we have been discussing, whereby unspecified funds set aside for the *korbanot* of a *nazir* (the *chatat*, *olah*, and *shelamim*) are directed toward an *olah* when the *nazirut* is annulled or the *nazir* dies, applies only when those funds are literally money. *Aval behemah harei hi k'mefureshet* — but if instead of money, animals were set aside, those animals are treated as already specified, as though each animal's identity has been designated.

### Animals vs. Money

What exactly is the case under discussion? The Gemara is terse here, and Tosafot works through the possibilities at some length in order to establish the basic *pshat*. One might have thought the case involved the precise animals a *nazir* is obligated to bring — a female lamb for the *chatat*, a male lamb for the *olah*, and a ram for the *shelamim*. But if those are the animals in question, it is self-evident that each is already identified: they are three distinct species, and there is no ambiguity whatsoever. The *olah* animal, the *chatat* animal, and the *shelamim* animal identify themselves. In that case it would be trivially obvious that the animals are *mefurashim* — specified — and there would be nothing to teach.

Tosafot therefore concludes that what the Gemara has in mind is a different scenario: the person sanctified three cows — animals that are within the world of *korbanot* in some sense but are not themselves the animals required for the *korban nazir*, which demands a sheep for the *olah*. The intent would be to sell the cows, take the proceeds, and use the money to purchase the required animals. Now the question becomes: when three cows are sanctified with the *kedusha* of *korbanot*, and they will eventually be sold and their proceeds divided among the various sacrifices, why should they not be treated like an unspecified pile of money? Which cow's proceeds are going to which sacrifice? Surely this should be *stumah* — unspecified — in exactly the same way as an indistinct pile of coins.

### Three Cows Case

To understand why animals are not treated like unspecified money, it is necessary to work through an important distinction that Tosafot develops and that will prove central to the Gemara's entire discussion. The question is what happens when one sanctifies an object with the *kedusha* of a *korban* but the object itself is not of the type that could be brought as that *korban*. For example, if one were to sanctify a digital recorder with the *kedusha* of an *olah*, the recorder cannot itself be placed on the *mizbei'ach*; what happens is that the *kedusha* transfers to the proceeds of its sale, and those proceeds are then used to purchase the required animal. This is a normal and unproblematic transfer.

Now consider a different case: one sanctifies a cow with the *kedusha* of an *olah* for a *nazir*, which must be a sheep. The cow is within the world of *korbanot* — it is the kind of animal that could, in the right circumstances, be brought as a *korban*. Does that make a difference? According to Tosafot, it does, and it makes a critical one.

### Kedusha Transfer

The principle at work is this: if an object is sanctified for *bedek ha-bayis* — for the maintenance fund of the *Beis HaMikdash* — it has no identity as a sacrificial animal and can simply be sold at any time. If an animal is sanctified to be brought itself as a *korban olah*, its *kedusha* cannot be removed and the animal cannot be sold until it develops a *mum* — a disqualifying blemish — because only a blemish releases it from the realm of *korbanot* and permits its sale. The intermediate case is when an animal is sanctified with the *kedusha* of an *olah* but is not itself going to be brought as one — as with our cow, whose proceeds will fund a sheep *olah*. Does this middle case follow the rule of *bedek ha-bayis*, permitting immediate sale, or does it follow the rule of a *korban* animal, requiring a *mum* before sale?

Tosafot concludes from the present *sugya* that because the cow is intrinsically relevant to the world of *korbanot* — it is, after all, the kind of animal that could in principle serve as a *korban* — its *kedusha* cannot simply be transferred through a sale. The *kedusha* remains stuck to the cow until the cow develops a *mum* and thereby exits the world of *korbanot*. By contrast, if one were to sanctify a chicken with the *kedusha* of an *olah*, since a chicken is wholly outside the world of animal *korbanot*, the *kedusha* presents no obstacle to an immediate sale. It is this distinction — whether the object is *relevant* to the world of *korbanot* or not — that gives meaning to the distinction between animals with a *mum* and animals without one.

## Temimah vs. Mum

### Rav Nachman Tmimah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הָא דְּאָמְרִי בְּהֵמָה הֲרֵי הִיא כִּמְפוֹרֶשֶׁת, לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא תְּמִימָה, אֲבָל בַּעֲלַת מוּם — הֲרֵי הִיא כִּסְתוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said: When they say** that if one designates **an animal it is** considered **as allocated, they taught** this **only** if it is **unblemished** and is fit to be sacrificed itself. **However,** if one separated **a blemished** animal, even if he set aside the three required types, a female sheep, a male sheep, and a ram in its second year, each one **is** considered **as unallocated.**

Against this background, Rav Nachman adds a crucial qualification to Rav's statement: *lo shanu ela temimah* — the rule that animals are treated like *mefureshet* (specified) applies only to an unblemished animal. The reason is now clear: an unblemished cow that has been sanctified with the *kedusha* of a *korban* cannot be sold until it develops a *mum*. It will sit in the owner's field for an extended and indeterminate period. During that time, as will be developed shortly, there is reason to think its identity may become fixed in the owner's mind in a way that distinguishes it from an undifferentiated pile of money.

### Ba'alat Mum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל סְוָאר שֶׁל קוֹרוֹת — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **However,** this is **not** the case with regard to **a bar of silver [*naskha*].** If one separated three silver bars they are considered allocated, as each is a distinct item, designated for a particular offering.

*Aval ba'alat mum harei k'stumah* — but if the animal already has a blemish, it is treated like unspecified funds. An animal with a *mum* is not subject to the *kedusha* restriction that prevents sale; it can be readily sold and converted to cash. That ready convertibility places it, for halachic purposes, in the same category as cash — and therefore it follows the *halacha* of *ma'ot setumot*.

## Maot Setumot (II)

### Cows Unspecified?

This still leaves a question unanswered. Even granting that an unblemished cow presents complications around *kedusha* and sale, why should those cows ultimately be treated as *mefureshet* — specified? When the question was posed at the outset — three cows, no specification of which one's proceeds go to which sacrifice — surely this should be treated as *stumah*, unspecified, just like a pile of money. Why does the Gemara say otherwise?

## Limits of Maot

### Two Approaches

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ נְסָכָא, אֲבָל סְוָאר שֶׁל קוֹרוֹת — לָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי לְרַב פָּפָּא: מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּרַבָּנַן — דְּאָמְרִי: מָעוֹת, וְלֹא בְּהֵמָה וְלָא נְסָכָא, מָעוֹת וְלָא סְווֹרָא? אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה: מָעוֹת וְלָא עוֹפוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Even a silver bar** is considered unallocated; **however, a pile [*sevar*] of beams** is **not.** If he set aside three piles of construction beams for his offerings, they are treated as allocated money. **Rav Shimi bar Ashi said to Rav Pappa: What is the reasoning of the Rabbis,** i.e., Rav, Rav Naḥman, and Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak, **who say: Money and not an animal, and not a silver bar;** and similarly, **money and not a pile?** Do they maintain that the *halakha* of unallocated funds applies only to money and not to other items? **However, if that is so,** one should likewise say that it applies to **money and not birds.**

There are two ways to understand the limits of *maot setumot*. The first approach is that the entire *halacha* is a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* — a received tradition from Sinai — and that tradition was stated specifically with respect to *ma'ot*, cash, and not with respect to other forms of property. Under this view, even if one's three cows are genuinely unspecified — there is no clarity about which animal's proceeds will fund which sacrifice — it simply does not matter, because the *halacha* was never said for anything other than money. The cows remain non-*stumah* not because they are in fact specified, but because the special *halacha* of *setumim* never applied to them in the first place. The practical consequence is that the money cannot be directed toward an *olah*; since *chatat* funds are mixed in with everything else, the entire amount would have to be cast into the *Yam HaMelach*.

But if this is purely a formal rule limited to money, one might wonder: what about animals with a *mum*, which Rav Nachman just said are treated like *setumah*? Animals with a *mum* are also not cash. So the question becomes: where exactly is the dividing line?

### Mezuman Definition

The word that illuminates this is *mezuman* — the modern Hebrew word for cash. The root meaning is something like "ready" or "at hand," and the concept it captures is immediate transferability. The *halacha* of *setumim* was stated for things that are readily liquidable — readily converted into money for the purpose of purchasing the required *korbanot*. Something that can be sold quickly and easily in the normal course of commerce is functionally equivalent to cash and falls within the scope of the *halacha*. Something that requires time, negotiation, or waiting for the right buyer does not. On this reading, animals with a *mum* — which, since they bear no *kedusha* restriction, can be taken to market and sold without delay — are considered *mezuman*, cash-equivalent, and therefore fall within the *halacha* of *setumim*. Animals without a *mum*, which cannot be sold until a blemish appears, are not *mezuman*, not cash-equivalent, and therefore fall outside the *halacha*.

### Pre-Designation Logic

The second approach attempts to supply an internal logic for why unblemished animals are treated as *mefureshet* — specified — rather than simply excluded by a formal rule. The reasoning is as follows: when an unblemished animal has been sanctified and cannot be sold until it develops a *mum*, it may sit in the owner's possession for a long time. Over the course of that extended waiting period, the owner is likely to begin mentally allocating the animals — thinking to himself that the proceeds from this cow will fund the *olah*, and the proceeds from that cow will fund the *chatat*. If such mental pre-designation occurs, the animals are no longer genuinely unspecified; they have been informally assigned in the owner's mind, even if no formal declaration has been made.

This reasoning has an obvious difficulty, which Tosafot acknowledges: a mental assignment that has not been verbalized ordinarily has no halachic standing. Thoughts in the mind, without an act or declaration, do not usually alter the status of *kodshim*. For this reason, the cleaner and more defensible approach is simply the formal one: the *halacha* of *setumim* is limited to things that are readily liquidable — to *mezuman*, cash or cash-equivalents. Anything that requires extended time to convert to money falls outside the *halacha*, and the question of whether its identity has been mentally pre-designated is beside the point.

## Rav Shimi Challenge

### Metal vs. Wood

The foregoing discussion raises a further question about exactly where to draw the line between what counts as *mezuman* and what does not. One position implies that a *nascha* — a bar of precious metal, silver or gold — does not qualify as cash-equivalent, because selling a bar of precious metal requires waiting for the right price and is not as immediate as selling a blemished cow in an agricultural market. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak disagrees: *afilu nascha* — even a bar of metal is *mezuman* for this purpose, and the *halacha* of *setumim* applies to it. What is excluded, on his view, is *sovi d'karei* — beams of lumber. Wood requires finding a buyer who is in the market to build a house and who must inspect the material; it is simply not liquid. The precise line between metal and lumber as measures of liquidity may be somewhat surprising — one might have thought precious metal, which sells by weight, would be at least as liquid as livestock — but such is the Gemara's assumption about the relative ease of these markets.

### Money Not Birds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא הָכִי נָמֵי, אֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אֵין הַקִּינִּים מִתְפָּרְשׁוֹת, אֶלָּא אִי בִּלְקִיחַת בְּעָלִים, אִי בַּעֲשִׂיַּית כֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say: So too,** this is in fact the case, and birds cannot be considered allocated, **but** what about **this** statement **that Rav Ḥisda said: Nests,** i.e., a pair of turtle doves or pigeons, one for a burnt-offering and the other for a sin-offering, **are** considered **allocated only by either the acquisition of the owner,** if the owner designates each bird for a particular offering upon their purchase, **or by the actions of the priest** who decides which bird is for which offering when he sacrifices them. This clearly indicates that the birds are considered unallocated beforehand.

Rav Shimi bar Ashi poses a sharp question to Rav Pappa: the *rabbanan* say the *halacha* was stated for *damim* — money — and not for *behemah*, not for *nascha*, not for *sovi*. Very well. Then it should equally be: *damim v'lo ofot* — money and not birds. What does he mean by birds?

## Two Birds Case

### Unspecified Birds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּאי? הָא מָעוֹת גְּמִירִין לָהּ!
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, the question arises: **Why** is this so? Didn't **we learn this** *halakha* only with regard to **money,** whereas Rav Ḥisda's statement indicates that birds are also considered unallocated? If Rav Ḥisda's opinion is accepted, the same *halakhot* should also apply to animals, bars, and piles of beams.

The case Rav Shimi has in mind is not simply birds that someone happened to sanctify for a vague purpose. The classic *halacha* is that a *yoledes* — a woman who has given birth — is obligated to bring two birds, one as an *olah* and one as a *chatat*. She need not specify at the time she acquires the birds which is which; specification can happen either when the owner herself declares it at the time of purchase, or when the *kohen* designates the birds at the time he performs the service. This is a well-established *halacha* governing *kinnim* — pairs of birds brought for such obligations.

Now consider what happens if the woman acquires the two birds without specifying their identities and then dies before bringing them. She has two birds, one of which is a *chatat* and one an *olah*, but no one has yet declared which is which. One natural approach would be to bring the matter to the *kohen* and let the *kohen* make the designation — declaring one an *olah* and bringing it, and disposing of the other, since the *chatat* cannot be brought after the owner's death. But there is another possibility: could we treat those two unspecified birds like a pile of *ma'ot setumim*? In an unspecified pile of money containing both *chatat* funds and *olah* funds, the *halacha* is that the entire pile goes toward an *olah*. Why not apply the same *halacha* here and bring both birds as an *olah*?

Rav Shimi's challenge is essentially this: the question of whether the *halacha* of *setumim* extends to birds is not academic. Either the *halacha* is limited to money — in which case it should not apply to birds, and bringing both birds as an *olah* would be incorrect — or the *halacha* is not so limited, in which case why was it ever restricted to money in the first place?

### Counter Position

The counter-position is that the *halacha* of *setumim* should not be artificially restricted. Whether one has an unspecified pile of money, unspecified animals, unspecified bars of metal, or unspecified birds — if the *kedusha* is genuinely undifferentiated and the owner has died, the property follows the *halacha* of *setumim* and is directed toward a *nedava*, an *olah*.

### Rav Chisda Birds

Rav Chisda taught: *ein ha-kinnim mitparshot ella im be-likichat ba'alim o be-asiyat ha-kohen* — pairs of birds do not become specified except either at the time the owner purchases and designates them or at the time the *kohen* performs the service. The significance of Rav Chisda's formulation, as Tosafot explains, lies in his choice of the word *mitparshot*. He did not simply say that the birds' identity is not *nikva'im* — not established. He said they are not *mitparshot* — not explicated. *Mitparshot* is the precise opposite of *satum*. By framing it this way, Rav Chisda implicitly communicates that until one of those two events occurs — owner's designation at purchase or *kohen*'s designation at service — the birds' halachic status is *satum*, unspecified.

### Mitparshot Inference

If the birds have the status of *setumim* prior to designation, then when the owner dies before designation occurs, the normal *halacha* of *setumim* should apply: both birds go toward an *olah*, offered as a *nedava*. The inference from Rav Chisda's use of *mitparshot* is thus that birds do fall within the *halacha* of *setumim* — which challenges the position that the *halacha* is limited strictly to money.

## Birds Distinction

### Specified Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, הָא דִּתְנַן: רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: הֵבִיא שָׁלֹשׁ בְּהֵמוֹת וְלֹא פֵּירֵשׁ, הָרְאוּיָה לְחַטָּאת — תִּקְרַב חַטָּאת, לְעוֹלָה — תִּקְרַב עוֹלָה, לִשְׁלָמִים — תִּקְרַב שְׁלָמִים. אַמַּאי? הָא אָמְרַתְּ: בְּהֵמָה לָאו כִּמְפוֹרֶשֶׁת דָּמְיָא!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa **said to him: And according to your reasoning,** which leads to the conclusion that all items can have the status of unallocated funds, consider **that which we learned** in a mishna (45a): **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** If one **brought three** different **animals** for his naziriteship **and did not specify** which was for which offering, **that which is fit for a sin-offering,** a ewe, **is sacrificed as a sin-offering;** the animal suitable **for a burnt-offering,** a male sheep or bull, **is sacrificed as a burnt-offering;** and the animal fit **for a peace-offering,** any other male or female, **is sacrificed as a peace-offering.** But according to your opinion, **why** is this so? **Didn't you say** that **an animal is not considered as allocated,** but is instead like unallocated funds; how can one decide which animal to use for each offering?

The Gemara responds to Rav Shimi's challenge with a counter-question. If one wishes to say the *halacha* of *setumim* applies equally to everything — money, animals, metal, birds, without distinction — then one must contend with what Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel teaches in the Mishnah: *heivi shalosh behemot v'lo peresh* — if someone brought three animals for his *korban nazir* without specifying which is which, and it happens that he brought precisely the three required animals (a female lamb, a male lamb, and a ram), then *harau'ya le-chatat tikarev chatat, le-olah tikarev olah, ve-li-shelamim tikarev shelamim* — each animal is assigned to its appropriate identity. The female goes as the *chatat*, the male lamb as the *olah*, the ram as the *shelamim*.

The counter-position maintains that animals too should follow the *halacha* of *setumim* and are not treated as specified. If so, how does the *kedusha* of this unspecified pile of three animals divide itself so neatly? The answer is that this is not at all a difficult case: these three animals, being the exact required animals, are self-identifying. They are not genuinely unspecified in any meaningful sense. Asking why they are not treated as *stumim* is like asking why a pile of money with a large sign reading "*chatat*" on one portion and "*olah*" on another should be treated as unspecified — the question answers itself.

### Torah Process

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָתָם: ״וְלָקַח ... וְעָשָׂה״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Shimi bar Ashi **said to him:** As for the case of birds, this is an exception, as there is an explicit verse to this effect **there:** It states with regard to the owner of a bird offering: **"And he shall take** one he-lamb for a guilt-offering to be waved, to make atonement for him, and one tenth part of an ephah of fine flour mingled with oil for a meal-offering, and a *log* of oil; and two turtledoves or two young pigeons, such as his means suffice for, and the one shall be a sin-offering and the other a burnt-offering" (Leviticus 14:21–22), and it later states with regard to the priest: **"And he shall sacrifice** one of the turtledoves, or of the young pigeons, such as his means suffice for; even such as his means suffice for, the one for a sin-offering, and the other for a burnt-offering" (Leviticus 14:30–31).

What actually distinguishes the bird case and explains why birds can be *setumim* is that the Torah itself specifies the process by which birds receive their identity: *ve-lakach ve-asah* — the owner takes and the *kohen* does. The birds' identity is assigned at one of precisely two moments — acquisition or service — and until one of those moments arrives, the Torah itself leaves them undesignated. There is no analogous Torah-mandated process in the case of animals; an animal's identity as *chatat*, *olah*, or *shelamim* is determined at the moment of designation, and when the owner brings three distinct and self-identifying animals, the designation is obvious.

### Three Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: אִי בִּלְקִיחַת בְּעָלִים, אִי בַּעֲשִׂיַּית כֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Merciful One** thereby **states** that the birds are allocated and designated for their offering **either by the acquisition of the owner,** by designating each bird for a particular offering upon their purchase, **or by the action of the priest.**

The point is further underscored by the observation that the *chatat* of a *nazir* is a female animal and the *olah* is a male animal — *hacha nekeva hacha zachar*. When you have a female sheep and a male sheep in front of you, it is simply not possible to speak of genuine non-specification. The *chatat* is the female; the *olah* is the male. There is nothing *satum* about it. By contrast, two birds of the same species brought for a *yoledes*, neither yet designated, are genuinely indistinguishable. That is why the Torah's process — designation at purchase or by the *kohen* — is both necessary and meaningful in the bird case, and not applicable in the case of clearly self-differentiating animals.

## Mum Equivalence

### Hamnuna Challenge

Rav Hamnuna raises a challenge against the position that an animal with a *mum* is treated like *setumah* — like unspecified funds. The argument in favor of that position was that a blemished animal, which bears no *kedusha* restriction and can be sold immediately, is *mezuman*, cash-equivalent, and therefore falls within the *halacha* of *setumim*. But one might question whether this is correct. After all, an animal with a *mum* is still an animal; it is not money. Even on the view that certain things are treated as money-equivalents, does a blemished animal really belong in that category? Rav Hamnuna brings a *braita* to probe this question.

## Father's Funds

### Father Dies

The *braita* — which is quoted here in abbreviated form, with the full version appearing at 30b — addresses the *halacha* of a son who shaves (i.e., brings the concluding *korbanot*) for his father's *nezirut*. The case is: the father was a *nazir*, set aside money for his *korbanot* (*hefrish maot le-neziruto*), and then died. Normally, when a *nazir* dies, unspecified money goes toward an *olah*. But if the son then declares: *hareini nazir al menat she-agalei'ach al maot Abba* — I will take upon myself *nezirut* on condition that I may use my father's money for my *korbanot* — and he makes this declaration after his father's death, then the money was set aside for the father's *nezirut*, the father has died, the money is available and not yet redirected, and the son becomes a *nazir* specifically to use those funds. In this case the son does get to use the father's money.

### Both Nezirim

The second case in the *braita* is where the son does not wait for his father to die. Both the father and the son are *nezirim* simultaneously. The father sets aside money for his own *nezirut* and then dies. The son then says he will use his father's money. Here he cannot, because when the father died with money set aside for his own *nezirut*, and the son was already a *nazir* at that time, the son is not entitled to redirect those funds. The money falls to *nedava* — it follows the normal *halacha* of unspecified funds and goes toward an *olah*.

### Specified Unspecified

The *braita* continues: *haitah lo behemah meforeshet* — if the father had animals that were already specified, *chatat tamut* — the *chatat* animal is left to die — *olah tikarev olah* — the *olah* animal is brought as an *olah* — *shelamim yiplu li-shelamim* — the *shelamim* animals are brought as *shelamim*. Unspecified money follows one rule; specified animals follow their own designations individually.

## Mum Equivalence (II)

### Animals Not Money

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו אֲפִילּוּ בַּעֲלַת מוּם? בְּהֵמָה מְפוֹרֶשֶׁת כְּתִיב הָתָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now draws an inference from this *braita* relevant to Rav Hamnuna's challenge. The *braita* contrasts unspecified money, which goes to *nedava*, with specified animals, which go to their respective purposes. The Gemara asks: *mai lav afilu ba'alat mum* — is the contrast not saying that even animals with a *mum* do not have the *halacha* of *setumah*? That is, even blemished animals are treated as specified (and thus go to their own designated purposes), not like unspecified money? This would undermine the position that a blemished animal is equivalent to *ma'ot setumot*.

This would undermine the position that a blemished animal is equivalent to *ma'ot setumot*.

But the Gemara immediately notes that this inference is strained: the *braita* is explicitly speaking of *behemah meforeshet* — clearly specified animals. It is not making any statement at all about unspecified animals with blemishes. The contrast is between unspecified money and clearly specified animals, not between unspecified money and every possible type of animal. One might respond that the *braita* should have made its point more directly — if blemished animals are truly cash-equivalent, why not contrast unspecified money against unspecified blemished animals, which would be a more illuminating comparison?

### Dmei Hayinu Maot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּעֲלַת מוּם לְמַאי כְּדִישָׁהּ? לִדְמֵי. דְּמֵי הַיִּינוּ מָעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara's resolution is clean and decisive: *ba'alat mum le-mai kedishah? Li-dmei* — a blemished animal's *kedusha* is oriented toward what? Toward its monetary value. It is going to be sold; its *kedusha* lives in its sale price. *Demei hayinu ma'ot* — and proceeds are themselves money. When the *braita* speaks of *ma'ot setumot*, unspecified money, this already encompasses blemished animals, because a blemished animal's *kedusha* is money-in-waiting. There was never a category distinction to begin with; the *braita*'s reference to money naturally includes anything whose *kedusha* is expressed through monetary proceeds.

This resolution clarifies the overall shape of the *sugya*. The *halacha* of *setumim* — the rule that unspecified funds go toward an *olah* — applies to money and to anything functionally equivalent to money: things that are readily liquidable and whose *kedusha* is carried by their monetary value. A blemished animal fits squarely within that category. An unblemished animal that cannot be sold pending the appearance of a *mum* does not. And the specific *halacha* of birds is governed not by the money-equivalence analysis but by the Torah's own process of designation through *likichat ba'alim* or *asiyat ha-kohen*, which leaves the birds genuinely *setumim* until one of those moments arrives.
