# Makkot 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shimon's Five Lashes

### Shimon's Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵלּוּ הֵן הַלּוֹקִין: הַכּוֹהֵן שֶׁאָכַל תְּרוּמָה, וְהַנָּכְרִי שֶׁאָכַל תְּרוּמָה, וְהַטָּמֵא שֶׁאָכַל קֹדֶשׁ קָדָשִׁים, וְהַמִּזְבֵּחַ שֶׁנָּזַל דָּמוֹ, וְהַמִּזְבֵּחַ שֶׁנִּשְׁמַט, אוֹ שֶׁנִּשְׁרַט, וְהַכּוֹהֵן שֶׁאָכַל בִּכּוּרִים קוֹדֶם לִקְרִיָּה, הַזָּר שֶׁאָכַל מִן הַקֹּדֶשׁ, וּמִן הַמּוּמּוּ, וּמִן הַנּוֹשָׂר, וּמִן הַפִּיגּוּל, הַמִּזְבֵּחַ שֶׁנִּזְרַק דָּמוֹ בְּחוּץ לִמְחִיצָתוֹ, וְהַמִּזְבֵּחַ שֶׁנִּשְׁמַט וּנִזְרַק דָּמוֹ בִּמְחִיצָתוֹ – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מְחַיֵּב בַּחֲמִישִׁים וּשְׁתַּיִם מַלְכִּיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA. **These are** the people **who are flogged:** the priest who ate teruma; the non-priest who ate teruma; the ritually impure person who ate offerings of the most sacred order; the altar whose blood flowed away; the altar whose limbs became dislocated or were cracked; the priest who ate first fruits before the recitation of the accompanying Torah verses; the non-priest who ate from the sacred offerings, from a blemished offering, from leftover flesh or from piggul flesh; the offering whose blood was sprinkled outside its partition; and the offering whose limbs became dislocated and whose blood was sprinkled within its partition — **Rabbi Shimon holds** that these transgressions incur **fifty-two sets of lashes.**

The daf opens at the top of 18a with a recap of Rabbi Shimon's derivation of multiple distinct prohibitions from the verse *lo tuchal leechol bish'arecha* — "you shall not be able to eat within your gates." Rabbi Shimon holds that the Torah could have stated a single general prohibition covering all these cases, yet it spells out each category individually: *maaser*, *shelamim*, *bechor*, *kodshei kodashim*, *olah*, and *bikkurim*. Each repetition signals an independent *lav* — a negative prohibition carrying *malkos* — for eating a sacred item either in the wrong place or before the proper preparatory act has been performed.

The specific cases are as follows. *Bikkurim* eaten before the declaration (*kriya*) was made. *Shelamim* eaten before *zrikas hadam*, the sprinkling of the blood. *Bechor* eaten by a non-*kohen* (*zar*) even after the blood was properly sprinkled. *Kodshei kodashim* consumed outside the *beis hamikdash*, even by a *kohen* and even after *zrikas hadam*. And *olah*, which of course cannot be eaten by anyone under any circumstances. Each of these is a distinct prohibition derived from the elaboration of this verse.

### Why Repeat?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא קְרָא יַתִּירָא הוּא, מִכְּדֵי כְּתִיב ״וְהֵבֵאתָ שָׁם וְאָכַלְתָּ לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ בַּמָּקוֹם וְגוֹ׳״, לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״לֹא תוּכַל לְאוֹכְלָם״, מִיהְדָּר מְפָרֵשׁ בְּהוּ רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי? אֶלָּא לְיַחוֹדֵי לְהוּ לָאוֵי לְכׇל חַד וְחַד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the derivation is that the entire verse beginning: "You may not eat within your gates the tithe of your grain" (Deuteronomy 12:17) **is a superfluous verse. After all, it is** already **written: "And there you shall bring** your burnt-offerings, and your sacrifices, and your tithes, and the donation of your hand, and your vows, and your gift offerings, and the firstborn of your herd and of your flock, **and there you shall eat before the Lord your God"** (Deuteronomy 12:6–7); all the items that must be eaten within the walls of Jerusalem are enumerated. **Let the Merciful One write** simply: **You may not eat them,** in general terms, which would constitute a prohibition for which one would be liable to receive lashes for each of the cases enumerated. **Why do I** need **the Merciful One** to **again** specifically **enumerate** and **detail** each of **them? Rather,** this repetition serves **to designate** additional **prohibitions for each and every one** of the cases enumerated in the later verse (Deuteronomy 12:17).

The Gemara presses: if a single phrase like *lo sochlun* — "you shall not eat them" — would have conveyed the prohibition for all, why does the Torah enumerate each category explicitly? The answer the Gemara arrives at is that each individual word is there *leyachadei lehu lavei lechol chad v'chad* — to designate a separate *lav* with its own prohibition and its own potential for *malkos* in each distinct case.

### Kal V'Chomer Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מַזְהִירִין מִן הַדִּין? הָא אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר עוֹנְשִׁין מִן הַדִּין, אֵין מַזְהִירִין מִן הַדִּין! אִיסּוּרָא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to Rabbi Shimon, who derived prohibitions not written in the Torah by means of *a fortiori* inferences: **And does one** derive **a prohibition from** an *a fortiori* **inference? But even according to the one who says** that if there is a prohibition written explicitly in the Torah, **one administers punishment** based **on** an *a fortiori* **inference, one does not** derive **a prohibition from** an *a fortiori* **inference.** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Shimon does not derive prohibitions for which one is flogged from those inferences; rather, he derives **a mere prohibition.**

Yet this resolution does not fully resolve the underlying difficulty. Even if we accept that each category carries an independent *lav*, there remains the principle of *ein mazhirin min hadin* — one cannot establish a prohibition through logical inference alone. The logical inference at work here is the *kal v'chomer*: if something is prohibited in a lighter case, it is certainly prohibited in the more stringent case. For example, if *shelamim* cannot be eaten before *zrikas hadam*, then certainly *bechor* cannot. If a *zar* cannot eat *bechor*, then certainly a *zar* cannot eat *kodshei kodashim*. If *kodshei kodashim* cannot be eaten outside the *beis hamikdash*, then certainly *olah* cannot be eaten outside the *beis hamikdash*. The *kal v'chomer* runs from the weaker case to the stronger — and the rule of *ein mazhirin min hadin* says this inferential extension cannot, under normal circumstances, generate lashes.

The Gemara and Rishonim struggle with this. Rashi and Tosafos each in their own way attempt to retreat from the straightforward reading, suggesting perhaps that the weaker cases do not actually generate *malkos* in the extended applications. But the plain sense of the Gemara appears to embrace precisely that conclusion — that here, somehow, the extension via *kal v'chomer* does carry full legal weight, generating lashes even in the stronger cases inferred from the weaker. Tosafos in a lengthy discussion ultimately acknowledges the difficulty without arriving at a fully satisfying resolution: the Gemara seems committed to the position that if lashes apply in the lighter case, they apply in the heavier case by force of the *kal v'chomer*, even though this appears to violate *ein mazhirin min hadin*.

### Rava's Five Lashes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רָבָא: זָר שֶׁאָכַל מִן הָעוֹלָה לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה חוּץ לַחוֹמָה – לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לוֹקֶה חָמֵשׁ. וְלִילְקֵי נָמֵי מִשּׁוּם: ״וְזָר לֹא יֹאכַל כִּי קֹדֶשׁ הֵם״! הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דִּלְכֹהֲנִים חֲזֵי, הָכָא דִּלְכֹהֲנִים נָמֵי לָא חֲזֵי. וְלִילְקֵי נָמֵי מִשּׁוּם ״וּבָשָׂר בַּשָּׂדֶה טְרֵפָה לֹא תֹאכְלוּ״, כֵּיוָן שֶׁיָּצָא בָּשָׂר חוּץ לִמְחִיצָתוֹ – נֶאֱסָר! הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּבִפְנִים חֲזֵי, הָכָא דְּבִפְנִים נָמֵי לָא חֲזֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to **the** matter **itself, Rava says:** With regard to **a non-priest who ate** the flesh **of a burnt-offering before sprinkling** its blood, **outside the walls, according to Rabbi Shimon** he **is flogged** with **five** sets of lashes. The Gemara suggests: **And let him also be flogged** for violating the prohibition: **"And a non-priest may not eat, as they are sacred"** (Exodus 29:33). The Gemara explains: **This matter** prohibiting a non-priest from eating consecrated food applies only in a case **where** the food **is fit** for consumption **by priests. Here, where** the food **is not fit** for consumption **by priests either,** as it is not permitted for anyone to partake of a burnt-offering, there is no specific prohibition that applies to a non-priest. The Gemara suggests: **And let him also be flogged for** violating the prohibition: **"And any flesh torn of animals in the field you shall not eat"** (Exodus 22:30). From the term "in the field," a general *halakha* is derived: **Once the flesh emerged outside its partition** and is in the field, e.g., sacrificial meat that was taken outside the Tabernacle curtains that demarcate the courtyard, there is a prohibition, and the flesh is **forbidden.** The Gemara explains: **This matter,** the prohibition against eating sacrificial flesh outside the partition of the Temple courtyard, applies only in a case **where** the flesh **is fit** for consumption **inside** the courtyard. **Here,** in the case of a burnt-offering, **where** the flesh **is not fit** for consumption **inside** the courtyard **either,** as it is not permitted for anyone to partake of a burnt-offering, there is no specific prohibition that applies to a non-priest partaking of the flesh outside the courtyard.

The Gemara then cites Rava with a striking concrete illustration: *zar she'achal min ha'olah lifnei zrika chutz lachoma* — a non-*kohen* who ate from an *olah* before *zrikas hadam*, outside the walls of Yerushalayim (and outside the *beis hamikdash*) — according to Rabbi Shimon receives five sets of *malkos* from a single act of eating. The count: one, for eating an *olah* which can never be eaten by anyone; two, for eating *kodshei kodashim* outside the *beis hamikdash* — the *kal v'chomer* from *kodshei kodashim* reaches *olah*; three, for eating as a *zar*, a non-*kohen* — the *kal v'chomer* from *bechor* reaches *olah*; four, for eating before *zrikas hadam* — the *kal v'chomer* from *shelamim* reaches *olah*; and five, for eating outside Yerushalayim altogether, in addition to being outside the *beis hamikdash*. Five distinct violations, five distinct sets of *malkos*, from a single act. This is what makes clear that each prohibition is real, that each applies through the *kal v'chomer* to more severe cases, and that we are genuinely speaking of *malkos* and not merely of a theoretical prohibition.

## Additional Lashes?

### Kodshei Kodashim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִילְקֵי נָמֵי כִּדְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: ״לֹא יֵאָכֵל כִּי קֹדֶשׁ הוּא״, כֹּל שֶׁבַּקֹּדֶשׁ פָּסוּל – בָּא הַכָּתוּב לִיתֵּן לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה עַל אֲכִילָתוֹ! הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּקוֹדֶם פְּסוּלוֹ חֲזֵי, הָכָא דְּקוֹדֶם פְּסוּלוֹ נָמֵי לָא חֲזֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And let him also be flogged in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Eliezer. As Rabbi Eliezer says** that when it is stated with regard to leftover flesh and loaves from the inauguration offerings: **"It shall not be eaten because it is sacred"** (Exodus 29:34), it is derived that with regard to **any** item **that is sacrificial** and **disqualified** for whatever reason, **the verse comes to impose a prohibition upon its consumption.** The Gemara explains: **This matter** applies only in a case **where before its disqualification it was fit; here,** it is a case **where before its disqualification it was not fit either,** and therefore the prohibition does not apply.

Having established the five, the Gemara asks: why not a sixth? There is an additional verse — *ve-zar lo yochel ki kodesh heim* — "a non-*kohen* shall not eat, for they are holy," which prohibits a *zar* from eating *kodshei kodashim*. Should that not add another set of *malkos*?

The Gemara answers: *hani mili heicha delekohanei chazi* — that verse applies only where the item is fit for *kohanim* to eat. The verse begins *ve'achlu otam asher kuppar bahem* — "they shall eat from those with which atonement was made" — making explicit that this prohibition against *zar* consumption is predicated on the food being something that *kohanim* are in principle permitted to eat. Here, an *olah* cannot be eaten even by *kohanim*, so this particular prohibition does not apply. Interestingly, one could raise the same question about the *bechor*-derived *zar* prohibition earlier in the chain — for that prohibition too, one might have thought it only applies when the item is fit for *kohanim*. But the verse is more explicit in its formulation here, and the Gemara treats this exclusion as operative.

### Boundary Violation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִילְקֵי נָמֵי כְּאִידַּךְ דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל שֶׁהוּא בְּ״כָלִיל תִּהְיֶה״ – לִיתֵּן לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה עַל אֲכִילָתוֹ! אִין הָכִי נָמֵי, וְרָבָא – מֵהַאי קְרָא קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And let him also be flogged in accordance with the other** statement **of Rabbi Eliezer, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says:** With regard to **any** item **that is** included **in** the mitzva: **"It shall be burned in its entirety"** (Leviticus 6:16), the verse serves **to impose a prohibition upon its consumption,** as it is written: "It shall be burned in its entirety; it shall not be eaten" (Leviticus 6:16). The Gemara explains: **Yes, it is indeed so** that according to Rabbi Shimon one is flogged for violating this prohibition as well, **and Rava is saying** that **from this verse** he is interpreting that it is derived that one is flogged with five sets of lashes according to Rabbi Shimon.

The Gemara raises another candidate: the verse *u-vasar basadeh tereifah lo socheilu, lakelev tashlichun oto* — "flesh in the field that is torn you shall not eat, you shall cast it to the dogs." The *derasha* is: *basadeh* — "in the field" — suggests that meat which has left its proper boundary becomes prohibited. This prohibition, *chutz limechitzoso*, has wide application: to *korban* meat taken outside Yerushalayim, to *kodshei kodashim* taken outside the *beis hamikdash*, and even to a fetal limb that protruded from its mother's womb and was retracted before *shechita*. Surely this should add another prohibition to our case?

Here Tosafos offers a classic distinction — the *gavra-cheftza* analysis, the difference between the person and the object. The prohibition derived from *chutz limechitzoso* focuses on the *status of the meat itself*: once the meat left its boundary, the meat is forbidden, regardless of who is eating it or where the person is standing. By contrast, the prohibitions already counted focus on the *person's act*: is this person eating in the wrong place? These are conceptually distinct prohibitions, and in principle a scenario could exist where one applies without the other — for instance, if one leans over the boundary and consumes meat that itself remains inside. So why does this verse not add a sixth *lav*?

The Gemara answers: *hani mili heicha de-vifnim chazi* — this prohibition only applies to meat that could have been eaten had it remained within its boundaries. Here, the *olah* cannot be eaten even within the *beis hamikdash*, so the prohibition of leaving its boundary does not attach. However, this means that in a case involving a *shelamim* improperly removed, this verse could theoretically generate a second set of *malkos* — subject to the question, raised by Rashi and Tosafos, of whether the verse is too broad to function as a *lav she-biklalos*, an overly generic prohibition.

### Pasul Meat

The Gemara tries again: another verse — *lo ye'achel ki kodesh hu* — "it shall not be eaten, for it is holy" — is expounded by Rabbi Eliezer as follows: the phrase *ki kodesh hu* teaches that any sanctified meat which has been invalidated (*pasul*) carries a prohibition against eating. The derivation is from *nosar* — meat left over past its allotted time, which is holy meat that became *pasul* — but the principle is generalized: *kol she-be-kodesh passul, ba hakasuv ve-itein lo sa'aseh al achilaso*, any sanctified item that became invalid has a Torah prohibition against its consumption. Should not *olah* meat eaten outside its boundary fall under this category as well?

The Gemara's answer follows the same logic as before: *hani mili heicha di-kodem pesulo chazi* — this prohibition applies only to meat that was initially valid and became invalidated. It applies when something was once fit for consumption and then became *pasul*. Since an *olah* was never fit for consumption in the first place, this prohibition of eating *pasul* sanctified meat does not apply. The same logic would apply to *piggul* — an offering rendered invalid through improper sacrificial intent — which carries its own explicit Torah prohibition though the verse states the *onesh* without an explicit *lav*, raising a question about where the *lav* itself is learned; in principle, the same framework would apply.

### Eliezer's Olah

The Gemara raises yet another possible source for an additional prohibition: a *braita* in which Rabbi Eliezer states that the verse *kalil tihye be-lo ye'achel* — "it shall be wholly burnt, it shall not be eaten" — establishes a *lo sa'aseh* against eating an *olah*. This is a far more explicit and direct prohibition against eating an *olah* than the generic verse of *lo tuchal leechol bish'arecha*, from which Rabbi Shimon learned it. Should not this provide an additional *lav*, yielding a sixth count of *malkos*?

The Gemara concedes the point: *ai hachi nami* — indeed, Rava's count does include this verse. When Rava said one of the five lashes comes from eating an *olah*, that prohibition is in fact grounded in this explicit verse of Rabbi Eliezer — *kalil tihye be-lo ye'achel* — rather than in the general verse of *lo tuchal leechol bish'arecha*. The Gemara, notably, does not address the inverse question of why Rabbi Shimon would then need to learn the prohibition of eating an *olah* from his verse at all, given this more explicit formulation. The Gemara simply establishes that the most explicit source governs which *lav* produces *malkos* in that particular case, and the number remains five rather than growing larger.

### Kohen & Chattat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: (סִימָן כּוּזָא) כֹּהֵן שֶׁאָכַל מֵחַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה – לוֹקֶה. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאָכְלוּ אֹתָם אֲשֶׁר כֻּפַּר בָּהֶם״, לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה – אִין, לִפְנֵי כַּפָּרָה – לָא. לָאו הַבָּא מִכְּלַל עֲשֵׂה, – לָאו הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Giddel says** that **Rav says** a statement about a priest [*kohen*] and another statement about a non-priest [*zar*]. Before proceeding, the Gemara provides **a mnemonic** for these statements: ***Kaf*, *vav*, *zayin*, *alef*.** Rav says: **A priest who ate of a sin-offering or a guilt-offering before sprinkling** their blood on the altar **is flogged. What is the reason** that he is flogged? It is **as the verse states** with regard to the inauguration offerings, whose halakhic status was like that of sin-offerings and guilt-offerings in that it was permitted only for priests to partake of their flesh: **"And they shall eat them, that with which they were atoned"** (Exodus 29:33), from which it is inferred: **After atonement, yes,** they may eat the flesh of the offerings; **before atonement, no,** they may not eat it. And in Rav's opinion, the status of **a prohibition that stems from a positive mitzva is** that of **a prohibition,** for which one is flogged.

The Gemara now introduces a new statement: *Rav Giddal said in the name of Rav* — a *kohen* who ate from a *chattat* or *asham* before *zrikas hadam* receives *malkos*. This parallels the earlier discussion about *shelamim* eaten before *zrikas hadam*, but now applied to *kodshei kodashim* like *chattat* and *asham*. The question is: what is the source?

### Lav from Asei

Rav's statement is grounded in the verse *ve'achlu otam asher kuppar bahem* — "they shall eat from those through which atonement has been effected." The derivation is: *le'achar kappara in, lifnei kappara lo* — after the blood has been thrown, yes; before, no. The verse, framed positively, implicitly prohibits eating before atonement is completed. And Rav apparently held that *lav haba miklal asei lav* — a prohibition derived implicitly from a positive commandment carries the full legal weight of a *lav*, yielding *malkos*.

### Rava Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָבָא: ״וְכׇל בְּהֵמָה מַפְרֶסֶת פַּרְסָה וְשֹׁסַעַת שֶׁסַע שְׁתֵּי פְרָסוֹת מַעֲלַת גֵּרָה בַּבְּהֵמָה אֹתָהּ תֹּאכֵלוּ״, ״אֹתָהּ תֹּאכֵלוּ״ – וְאֵין בְּהֵמָה אַחֶרֶת תֹּאכֵלוּ. וְאִי כִּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ, ״אֶת זֶה לֹא תֹאכְלוּ״ לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises an objection** based on the verse: **"And every beast that splits the hoof and has the hoof cloven in two, and chews the cud among the animals, that, you may eat"** (Deuteronomy 14:6), from which a prohibition may be inferred: **"That, you may eat," but** there is **not** any **other animal** that **you may eat.** The Torah prohibits eating the flesh of any animal that lacks the indicators that it is a kosher animal. **And if** it is **as you say,** that an inference from a positive mitzva entails a prohibition, **why do I** need the following verse: **"This you may not eat"** (Deuteronomy 14:7), as it was already inferred from the mitzva? Rather, apparently, the status of a prohibition that stems from a positive mitzva is that of a positive mitzva, and one is not liable to receive lashes for its violation.

Rava challenges this immediately. The principle that an implicit negative derived from a positive commandment has the force of a *lav* is not accepted. The Gemara cites the verse about kosher animals — *ve-zos ha-beheima asher tochelu* — which implicitly prohibits eating non-kosher animals. If *lav haba miklal asei* had the weight of a *lav*, the explicit verse *et zeh lo tochelu* — "this you shall not eat" — would be superfluous. The Gemara presses the point: we never apply *lav haba miklal asei* to generate *malkos*. The Torah must state the prohibition explicitly as a negative for it to function as a *lav* with *malkos*. Violating an implied prohibition transgresses a positive commandment, not a negative one. Rava therefore rejects the original formulation of Rav's ruling.

### Zar Exempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אִיתְּמַר הָכִי אִיתְּמַר, אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: זָר שֶׁאָכַל מֵחַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה – פָּטוּר, מַאי טַעְמָא – דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאָכְלוּ אֹתָם אֲשֶׁר כֻּפַּר בָּהֶם״, כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּקָרֵינַן בֵּיהּ ״וְאָכְלוּ אוֹתָם אֲשֶׁר כֻּפַּר בָּהֶם״ – קָרֵינַן בֵּיהּ ״וְזָר לֹא יֹאכַל קֹדֶשׁ״, וְכֹל הֵיכָא דְּלָא קָרֵינַן בֵּיהּ ״וְאָכְלוּ אֹתָם אֲשֶׁר כֻּפַּר בָּהֶם״ – לָא קָרֵינַן בֵּיהּ ״וְזָר לֹא יֹאכַל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, if it was stated, this was stated: Rav Giddel says** that **Rav says: A non-priest who ate of a sin-offering or a guilt-offering before** the **sprinkling** of their blood is **exempt** from receiving lashes. **What is the reason** for this? It is **as the verse states: "And they shall eat them, that with which they were atoned…**and a non-priest may not eat, as they are sacred" (Exodus 29:33), and it is expounded as follows: **Anywhere that we read concerning it: "And they shall eat them, that with which they were atoned," we read concerning it: "And a non-priest may not eat sacrificial food." But anywhere that we do not read concerning it: "And they shall eat them, that with which they were atoned,"** e.g., in this case, where it is prohibited for priests to partake of the flesh of the offerings before the sprinkling of their blood, **we do not read concerning it: "And a non-priest may not eat."**

Accordingly, the Gemara revises the position and introduces the corrected version: *ela i itmar hachi itmar* — here is how it was actually stated. Rav Giddal said in the name of Rav: *zar she'achal michattat ve'asham lifnei zerika patur* — a *zar* who ate from a *chattat* or *asham* before *zrikas hadam* is exempt from the particular violation of *zarus*.

### Zarus Relevance

The reasoning is derived from the same verse: *ve'achlu otam asher kuppar bahem* — the *kohanim* shall eat from those through which atonement has been effected. The verse establishes when *kohanim* are permitted to eat. And the principle is: *kol heicha dekaryanan beih ve'achlu otam asher kuppar bahem, karinan bei v'zar lo yocheil* — only when the item is in the state where *kohanim* may eat from it does the prohibition of *zarus* — the violation of a non-*kohen* eating what belongs to *kohanim* — become operative. Before *zrikas hadam*, when no one may eat from the *korban*, the item is not yet in a state that is "relevant for *kohanim*," and therefore the distinct category of *zarus* does not apply. A *zar* eating before *zarikas hadam* may transgress the prohibition of eating before the blood was thrown — which applies to everyone — but does not add the distinct violation of *zarus* on top of it.

This principle stands in apparent tension with Rava's earlier case of five *malkos*, where eating as a *zar* was counted as one of the five even though the item was an *olah* eaten before *zrikas hadam*. That tension runs through the *sugya*: Rava evidently holds that all these prohibitions layer together regardless of whether the *korban* is fit for *kohanim*, while this ruling insists that *zarus* only applies when the item is in a state fit for *kohen* consumption. The two positions represent a genuine disagreement about when the concept of *zarus* is activated.

## Bikkurim Dispute

### Hanacha Makev

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי הוֹשַׁעְיָה: בִּכּוּרִים, הַנָּחָה מְעַכֶּבֶת בָּהֶן, קְרִיָּיה אֵין מְעַכֶּבֶת בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara resumes the discussion of the *halakha* that was mentioned in the mishna with regard to the Torah verses that one recites when he brings his first fruits to the Temple. **Rabbi Elazar says** that **Rabbi Hoshaya says:** With regard to **first fruits,** the lack of **placement** alongside the altar **invalidates them,** and they may not be eaten by the priest; the lack of **recitation** of the accompanying Torah verses **does not invalidate them.**

The Gemara now pivots to a fresh topic introduced at the outset: what makes *bikkurim* permissible for the *kohen* to eat — the *kriya* (the declaration recited by the owner) or the *hanacha* (the placing down of the basket before the altar)?

Rabbi Elazar said in the name of Rabbi Oshaya: *bikkurim hanacha me'akves bahem kriya ein me'akves bahem* — it is the placing down that is the critical act, not the declaration. This position stands in contrast to our *mishna*, which implies that the *kriya* is what matters, as it lists eating *bikkurim* before the declaration among acts that carry *malkos*. The verse in *parashas bikkurim* says *ve-hinachto lifnei Hashem Elokecha* — you shall place it before the Lord your God — and it is this act of placing, according to this view, that transitions the *bikkurim* into a state permissible for *kohen* consumption.

### Rot Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הָכִי? וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי הוֹשַׁעְיָא: הִפְרִישׁ בִּכּוּרִים קוֹדֶם לֶחָג, וְעָבַר עֲלֵיהֶן הֶחָג – יֵרָקְבוּ. מַאי לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא מָצֵי לְמִיקְרֵי עֲלֵיהֶן? וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ קְרִיָּיה אֵין מְעַכֶּבֶת בָּהֶן, אַמַּאי יֵרָקְבוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did Rabbi Elazar say that? But doesn't Rabbi Elazar say** that **Rabbi Hoshaya says:** If one **set aside first fruits before the festival** of *Sukkot* **and the Festival elapsed over them** while they remain in his possession, **they shall** be left to **decay,** as they cannot be rendered fit for consumption. **What, is it not** that they cannot be rendered fit **due to** the fact **that he can no** longer **recite** the Torah verses **over them,** as one may recite the Torah verses only until *Sukkot*? **And if it enters your mind** to say that the lack of **recitation does not invalidate them, why must they** be left to **decay?**

But the same Rabbi Elazar citing Rabbi Oshaya taught elsewhere: *hifrish bikkurim kodem le-chag ve-avar alav hachag, yirkavu* — if one separated *bikkurim* before *Sukkos* and the festival passed without bringing them, they must be left to rot. The rationale the Gemara proposes: since these *bikkurim* were separated at a time when the *kriya* was obligatory and now the *kriya* can no longer be performed (the declaration is only said during the period of joy associated with the harvest, from *Shavuos* through *Sukkos*; after *Sukkos* one may still bring *bikkurim* until *Chanukah*, but may no longer make the declaration), they can never achieve their proper status and must be left to rot.

But this creates a contradiction. If the *kriya* is not *me'akev* — if the placing down alone suffices — why must they rot? Why not simply bring them and perform the *hanacha*? *Mai lav mishum d'lo matzi le-mikrei alaihu* — is it not because they can no longer perform the *kriya* over them, proving that *kriya* is indeed essential?

### Zeira's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְרַבִּי זֵירָא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: כׇּל הָרָאוּי לְבִילָּה – אֵין בִּילָּה מְעַכֶּבֶת בּוֹ, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵינוֹ רָאוּי לְבִילָּה – בִּילָּה מְעַכֶּבֶת בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Elazar holds **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Zeira, as Rabbi Zeira says** a principle with regard to a meal-offering that applies in other areas as well: For **any** measure of flour **that** is **suitable for mixing** with oil in a meal-offering, the lack of **mixing does not invalidate** the meal-offering. Even though there is a mitzva to mix the oil and the flour *ab initio*, the meal-offering is fit for sacrifice even if the oil and the flour are not mixed. **And** for **any** measure of flour **that** is **not suitable for mixing** with oil in a meal-offering, the lack of **mixing invalidates** the meal-offering. Here too, although failure to recite the Torah verses does not invalidate the first fruits for consumption by the priest, that is referring only to when reciting the portion is possible. After *Sukkot*, when it is no longer possible to recite the portion, failure to recite the Torah verses invalidates the first fruits.

The resolution invokes the famous principle of Rabbi Zeira: *kol haraui lebila ein bila me'akeves bo* — anything fit for mixing is not prevented by the absence of actual mixing; *ve-kol she'ein raui lebila bila me'akeves bo* — but where mixing is not even possible, the absence of mixing does disqualify it. The context of this rule is the *minchah* offering: the flour and oil ideally should be mixed (*belila*), yet if they were not mixed, the *minchah* is still valid — provided the mixture was physically possible. A *minchah* of more than sixty units of flour (*esronos*) cannot be effectively mixed by human hands, and in that case, the lack of mixing renders it invalid.

The logical structure is subtle: it is not that the act of mixing is required for validity; rather, the *capacity* for mixing is required. If the *minchah* is the kind of thing that could theoretically be mixed, then even without actual mixing it is valid. But if it is constitutionally incapable of being mixed, the absence of mixing disqualifies it — because the disqualification flows from an inherent deficiency in the object itself.

Applied to *bikkurim*: one who holds that *kriya* is not *me'akev* does not mean that *kriya* is irrelevant. The position is more nuanced: *kriya* need not actually be performed, but the *bikkurim* must have the *capacity* for *kriya* to be performed over them. *Bikkurim* separated before *Sukkos* were obligated in *kriya*; after *Sukkos* passes, they are no longer capable of *kriya*; and therefore — even though actual *kriya* is not required — the absence of the capacity for *kriya* disqualifies them. That is why they must rot. The view that *hanacha* is *me'akev* and *kriya* is not a halfway concession: *kriya* must be possible, even if it need not be performed.

### Yochanan Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב מַתְנֵי לַהּ כִּדְרַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְקַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אַדְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בִּכּוּרִים הַנָּחָה מְעַכֶּבֶת בָּהֶן קְרִיָּיה אֵין מְעַכֶּבֶת בָּהֶן? וְהָא בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי אַסִּי מֵרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בִּכּוּרִים מֵאֵימָתַי מוּתָּרִין לְכֹהֲנִים? וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָרְאוּיִן לִקְרִיָּיה – מִשֶּׁקָּרָא עֲלֵיהֶן, וְשֶׁאֵין רְאוּיִן לִקְרִיָּיה – מִשֶּׁרָאוּ פְּנֵי הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Aḥa bar Ya'akov teaches** this *halakha* that was cited in the name of Rabbi Elazar **as** the statement **that Rabbi Asi** says that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says, and** as a result, an apparent contradiction between one statement **of Rabbi Yoḥanan and** another statement **of Rabbi Yoḥanan** is **difficult for him.** Rabbi Aḥa bar Ya'akov asks: **And did Rabbi Yoḥanan say** that with regard to **first fruits,** the lack of **placement** alongside the altar **invalidates them** and they may not be eaten; while the lack of **recitation** of the accompanying Torah verses **does not invalidate them? But didn't Rabbi Asi raise a dilemma before Rabbi Yoḥanan:** With regard to **first fruits, from when is it permitted for priests** to partake of them? **And** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** Rabbi Asi: It is permitted for the priest to partake of those first fruits **that are fit for recitation** of the accompanying Torah verses **from when he recites** those verses **over them, and** those first fruits **that are not fit for recitation** of the Torah verses are permitted **once they entered inside the Temple.**

Rav Acha bar Yaakov reported that Rav Ashi teaches this principle — that *hanacha* is *me'akev* and *kriya* is not — in the name of Rabbi Assi citing Rabbi Yochanan. But this generates a contradiction within Rabbi Yochanan's own teachings: *remi d'Rabbi Yochanan a'Rabbi Yochanan*.

For Rabbi Assi himself had once asked Rabbi Yochanan: from what point do *bikkurim* take on the sanctity that makes a *zar* who eats them liable? Rabbi Yochanan replied: for *bikkurim* that are *re'uyim le-keria* — fit for the *kriya*, meaning separated before *Sukkos* — the operative moment is when someone performs the *kriya* over them. For *bikkurim* that are not fit for *kriya*, meaning separated or brought after *Sukkos*, the operative moment is *mi-she-yiru pnei ha-bayit* — once they have been brought before the *beis hamikdash*, seen the face of the Temple.

The contradiction is double: *kashya keria a-keria, kashya hanacha a-hanacha*. On the one hand, one tradition has Rabbi Yochanan saying *kriya* is not *me'akev*, while the other has him saying *kriya* is indeed the operative moment. On the other hand, one tradition says *hanacha* is *me'akev*, while the other says that when *kriya* is not applicable the key is *re'iyat pnei ha-bayit*, not *hanacha*.

### Shimon vs. Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְרִיָּיה אַקְּרִיָּה לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, הָא רַבָּנַן. הַנָּחָה אַהַנָּחָה נָמֵי לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְהָא רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The apparent contradiction between one statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan with regard to **recitation and** another statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan with regard to **recitation** is **not difficult,** as **this** statement, that when recitation is impossible, lack of recitation invalidates the first fruits, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon** cited in the mishna, **and that** statement, which states that when recitation is impossible, lack of recitation does not invalidate the first fruits, is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis.** The apparent contradiction between one statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan with regard to **placement and** another statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan with regard to **placement** is **not difficult either,** as **this** statement, that lack of placement does not invalidate the first fruits, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, and that** statement, which states that lack of placement invalidates the first fruits, is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis.**

The Gemara resolves the first contradiction — *keria a-keria lo kashya, ha Rabbi Shimon ha Rabbanan* — by noting that Rabbi Yochanan was articulating different positions. In one statement he reports Rabbi Shimon's view, according to which *kriya* is indeed what matters — *terumat yadecha* is the phrase from which Rabbi Shimon derived that *bikkurim* before *kriya* carry *malkos*, consistent with our *mishna*. In the other statement Rabbi Yochanan speaks for the Rabbanan, who hold that it is the *hanacha* that is *me'akev*. The apparent contradiction is resolved: in one case Rabbi Yochanan presents Rabbi Shimon's position, in the other his own (or the Rabbanan's) position.

The second contradiction — *hanacha a-hanacha* — remains. In one tradition Rabbi Yochanan says *hanacha* is *me'akev*; in the other, he says that when *kriya* is not applicable the key is *re'iyat pnei ha-bayit*, not *hanacha*. The Gemara resolves this as well: *ha Rabbi Yehuda ve-ha Rabbanan* — one tradition reflects the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, the other reflects the Rabbanan.

### Hinachto Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה? דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״וְהִנַּחְתּוֹ״ – זוֹ תְּנוּפָה. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר זוֹ תְּנוּפָה, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא הַנָּחָה מַמָּשׁ? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְהִנִּיחוֹ״ – הֲרֵי הַנָּחָה אָמוּר, הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״וְהִנַּחְתּוֹ״ – זוֹ תְּנוּפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the statement of **Rabbi Yehuda?** It is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says** with regard to the verse written in the portion of first fruits: **"And you shall place it** before the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 26:10), that the reference is not to the placement of the fruits alongside the altar; rather, **this** is a reference to **waving** the first fruits. **Do you say** that **this** is a reference to **waving, or** perhaps **it is** a reference **only** to **actual placement** of the first fruits? He explains: **When it states** earlier: "And the priest shall take the basket from your hand **and place it** before the altar of the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 26:4), **placement** alongside the altar **is** already **stated; how do I realize** the meaning of the term **"And you shall place it"? This** is a reference to **waving.**

The *braita* explains Rabbi Yehuda's position. The verse says *ve-hinachto lifnei Hashem Elokecha* — "you shall place it down before the Lord your God." Rabbi Yehuda reads this as referring to the act of *tenufa*, waving, rather than literal placing down. His argument: there is a separate verse, *ve-hinicho lifnei mizbei'ach*, which already teaches literal placing down. Since the word *ve-hinachto* cannot mean the same thing as *ve-hinicho*, Rabbi Yehuda derives that *ve-hinachto* refers to *tenufa*. The implication, as Rashi explains, is that since the *Torah* only mentions the act of placing down once explicitly, it is *not* designating that act as the critical *me'akev* moment — and therefore according to Rabbi Yehuda, *hanacha* is not the determinative act. What is the determinative act? *Re'iyat pnei ha-bayis* — entry into the *beis hamikdash*.

The Rabbanan, by contrast, read both *ve-hinicho* and *ve-hinachto* as referring to placing down — two mentions of the same act, emphasizing its significance as the *me'akev* moment. Where then does the Rabbanan learn *tenufa*? This brings in Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, who represents the opposing view on the source of *tenufa*: the verse says *ve-lakach ha-kohen ha-tene mi-yadecha* — "the *kohen* shall take the basket from your hand." This teaches that the *kohen* is the active party in waving. The Gemara then develops this through a *gezeira shava* of *yad-yad* connecting *bikkurim* and *shelamim*: by *bikkurim* the verse says the *kohen* takes it from your hand, emphasizing the *kohen's* active role; by *shelamim* the verse says *yadav tavi'ena* — his hands shall bring it — emphasizing the owner's active role. The two are combined: *meni'ach kohen yadav tachat yad be'alim u-menif* — the *kohen* places his hands beneath the owner's hands and they wave together, since ultimately it is the owner's *korban* and he remains the primary actor.

### Zar Chiyuv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן תַּנָּא דִּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְלָקַח הַכֹּהֵן הַטֶּנֶא מִיָּדֶךָ״ – לִימֵּד עַל הַבִּכּוּרִים שֶׁטְּעוּנִין תְּנוּפָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב? אָתְיָא ״יָד״ ״יָד״ מִשְּׁלָמִים: כְּתִיב הָכָא ״וְלָקַח הַכֹּהֵן הַטֶּנֶא מִיָּדֶךָ״, וּכְתִיב ״יָדָיו תְּבִיאֶינָה אֵת אִשֵּׁי ה׳״, מָה כָּאן כֹּהֵן – אַף לְהַלָּן כֹּהֵן, מָה לְהַלָּן בְּעָלִים – אַף כָּאן בְּעָלִים. הָא כֵּיצַד? מַנִּיחַ כֹּהֵן יָדָיו תַּחַת יְדֵי בְּעָלִים וּמֵנִיף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **And who is the *tanna* who disagrees with Rabbi Yehuda? It is Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, as it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse written in the portion of first fruits: **"And the priest shall take the basket from your hand"** (Deuteronomy 26:4); this verse **taught about first fruits that they require waving,** this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov?** The Gemara explains: The matter **is derived** by means of a verbal analogy from one instance of the word **"hand"** written with regard to first fruits and **from** another instance of the word **"hand"** written with regard to **a peace-offering. It is written here,** with regard to first fruits: **"And the priest shall take the basket from your hand"** (Deuteronomy 26:4), **and it is written** with regard to a peace-offering: "He who offers his peace-offering to God…**his hands shall bring it, the fire of God…**to raise it as a waving before God" (Leviticus 7:29–30). **Just as here,** in the case of first fruits, it is the **priest** who takes the basket in his hand and waves it, **so too there,** in the case of the peace-offering, **a priest** performs the waving. **Just as there,** with regard to a peace-offering, it is the **owner** who performs the waving, as it is written: "He who offers…his hands shall bring it," **so too here,** the **owner** waves the first fruits. **How so;** how can the waving be performed by both the priest and the owner? The **priest places his hands beneath the hands of the owner and waves** the first fruits together with the owner.

A further dimension is introduced by Rav Adda citing Rabbi Yitzchak: *bikkurim eimasai mechayvin aleihem?* — from what point do *bikkurim* render a *zar* liable? This question parallels the earlier discussion regarding *korbanot*: the prohibition of *zarus* — a non-*kohen* eating from something designated for *kohanim* — only becomes operative when the item is in a state fit for *kohen* consumption. Until then, a non-*kohen* who eats may transgress a general prohibition of eating *bikkurim* before their time, but does not incur the distinct *zarus* liability, which is characterized by *chiyuv misa bidei shamayim*. Once the operative moment is passed — whether that moment is *kriya*, *hanacha*, or *re'iyat pnei ha-bayis* depending on one's position — the *bikkurim* are now in a state fit for *kohanim*, and a *zar* who eats from them compounds the transgression with *zarus*. The answer given here for those who follow the position of *re'iyat pnei ha-bayis*: liability commences from the moment the *bikkurim* enter the *beis hamikdash*.

### Re'iya Pnei Bayis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא בַּר אַדָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: בִּכּוּרִים מֵאֵימָתַי (מ)חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶם – מִשֶּׁיִּרְאוּ פְּנֵי הַבַּיִת. כְּמַאן – כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בִּכּוּרִים מִקְצָתָן בַּחוּץ וּמִקְצָתָן בִּפְנִים, שֶׁבַּחוּץ – הֲרֵי הֵן כְּחוּלִּין לְכׇל דִּבְרֵיהֶם, שֶׁבִּפְנִים – הֲרֵי הֵן כְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ לְכׇל דִּבְרֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava bar Adda says** that **Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** With regard to **first fruits, from when** is a non-priest who eats first fruits **liable** to receive death at the hand of Heaven **for their** consumption? One is liable **from when** the fruits **will enter inside the Temple.** The Gemara notes: **In accordance with whose** opinion is this *halakha* stated? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **this *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says:** With regard to **first fruits, some of which** are **outside and some of which** are **inside** the Temple, the halakhic status of those **that are outside** the Temple is **like** that of **non-sacred** produce **for all matters concerning them,** and the halakhic status of those **that are inside** the Temple is **like** that of **consecrated** produce **for all matters concerning them.**

Rabbi Eliezer's position is cited in support of this framework: *bikkurim miktzasam bachutz u'miktzasam bifnim* — if part of the basket is outside the *beis hamikdash* and part is inside, those portions that are outside retain the status of *chulin* for all purposes, while those inside have the status of *hekdesh* in all respects. The critical dividing line is the threshold of the *beis hamikdash*: what has entered is sanctified and now generates the full *zarus* prohibition; what remains outside has not yet reached that status. This illustrates with precision that according to this view, *re'iyat pnei ha-bayis* — the act of being brought before the *beis hamikdash* — is indeed what transforms the status of the *bikkurim* and activates the *kohen*'s right to eat them.

### Sheshes's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: בִּכּוּרִים, הַנָּחָה – מְעַכֶּבֶת בָּהֶן, קְרִיָּיה – אֵין מְעַכֶּבֶת בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Sheshet says:** With regard to **first fruits,** the lack of **placement** alongside the altar **invalidates them;** while the lack of **recitation** of the accompanying Torah verses **does not invalidate them.**

The *sugya* closes with Rav Sheshes restating the position clearly: *bikkurim hanacha me'akeves bahem kriya einah me'akeves bahem* — it is the placing down that is the critical act, and the declaration is not *me'akev*. This is presented as a view that runs against our *mishna*, and which the Gemara notes is supported by a *tanna* whose identity and source will be the subject of the following day's learning.
