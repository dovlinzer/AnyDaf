# Sotah 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Meshuch Milchama Speech

### Priest & Ark

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַתֶּם אִי אַתֶּם כֵּן כּוּ׳״, וְכׇל כָּךְ לָמָּה? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַשֵּׁם וְכׇל כִּינּוּיָו מוּנָּחִים בָּאָרוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **But you are not like them,** because, as the verse states: "For the Lord your God is He that goes with you, to fight for you against your enemies, to save you" (Deuteronomy 20:4). **And why** does the verse elaborate **so much** in spelling out the nature of God's attendance in battle? It is **because the** ineffable **name** of God **and all of His appellations** that are written on the tablets **were resting in the Ark.**

The daf opens at the very last line of 42b, continuing the discussion of the *meshuch milchama* — the anointed priest who addresses the troops before battle. The Torah declares: *Hashem elokeichem hu haholeich imachem* — "Hashem your God is the one who walks with you." The *gemara* asks: *vechol kach lama* — what is the purpose of all of this? The obvious answer is that it gives the soldiers courage. But the question, as Rashi frames it, is more precise.

### Rashi on Halicha

Rashi explains that the question is not why this statement is made at all, but what is the emphasis of the word *haholeich* — "walking" or "going" — rather than simply "is with you." The answer is: *mipnei sheHashem vechol kinuyav munachim ba'aron* — because God's name and all of the various divine appellations are actually present in the *aron*. It is therefore not merely an abstract declaration that God accompanies the army; it is a statement that the physical object representing God's presence — the *aron* — is literally going with them into battle. This raises the interesting question of whether the *aron* itself was taken out of the *Beit Hamikdash* or the *Mishkan* and brought to the battlefield, and there is a view that a separate *aron* was used for battle, distinct from the one that resided in the sanctuary.

### Midian War Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וַיִּשְׁלַח אֹתָם מֹשֶׁה אֶלֶף לַמַּטֶּה לַצָּבָא אֹתָם וְאֶת פִּינְחָס בֶּן אֱלִיעָזֶר הַכֹּהֵן לַצָּבָא וּכְלֵי הַקֹּדֶשׁ וַחֲצֹצְרוֹת הַתְּרוּעָה״. ״אֹתָם״ — אֵלּוּ סַנְהֶדְרִין, ״פִּינְחָס״ — זֶה מְשׁוּחַ מִלְחָמָה, ״וּכְלֵי הַקֹּדֶשׁ״ — זֶה אָרוֹן וְלוּחוֹת שֶׁבּוֹ, ״וַחֲצֹצְרוֹת הַתְּרוּעָה״ — אֵלּוּ הַשּׁוֹפָרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly, it says** in the verse with regard to the war against Midian: **"And Moses sent them, a thousand of every tribe, to the war, them and Pinehas** the son of Elazar the priest, to the war, with the holy vessels and the trumpets for the alarm in his hand" (Numbers 31:6). The verse is interpreted as follows: **"Them"; these** are **the Sanhedrin. "Pinehas"; he** was the priest **anointed for war. "And the holy vessels"; this** is **the Ark and the tablets that** were **within it. "And the trumpets for the alarm"; these** are **the *shofarot*.**

The *gemara* then brings a proof from the war against Midian: *vayishlach otam Moshe elef lamateh latzava, otam ve'et Pinechas ben Elazar hakohein latzava uchelei hakodesh vechatzotzrot hateruah beyado*. The *gemara* identifies the components: *otam* refers to the Sanhedrin; *Pinechas ben Elazar* is the *meshuch milchama*; *uchelei hakodesh* — the holy vessels — refers to the *aron* and the *luchot*; and *vechatzotzrot hateruah* — the trumpets of the blast — is rendered as *shofrot*. This last identification is noteworthy: *chatzotzrot* are the metal trumpets, while *shofrot* are made from an animal's horn. The *gemara* elsewhere indicates that in the Amoraic period these two terms exchanged their meanings in vernacular usage, which may explain why the *gemara* here renders the *chatzotzrot* of the *pasuk* as *shofrot*.

### Pinchas Genealogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: לֹא לְחִנָּם הָלַךְ פִּינְחָס לַמִּלְחָמָה, אֶלָּא לִיפָּרַע דִּין אֲבִי אִמּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהַמְּדָנִים מָכְרוּ אֹתוֹ אֶל מִצְרַיִם וְגוֹ׳״. לְמֵימְרָא דְּפִינְחָס מִיּוֹסֵף אָתֵי? וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״וְאֶלְעָזָר בֶּן אַהֲרֹן לָקַח לוֹ מִבְּנוֹת פּוּטִיאֵל לוֹ לְאִשָּׁה״. מַאי לָאו, דְּאָתֵי מִיִּתְרוֹ שֶׁפִּיטֵּם עֲגָלִים לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה? לָא, מִיּוֹסֵף שֶׁפִּיטְפֵּט בְּיִצְרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** A *tanna* **taught:** It was **not for nothing** that specifically **Pinehas went to war** with Midian; **rather,** it was **to exact** the rightful **judgment of his mother's father,** Joseph, **as it is stated: "And the Midianites sold him into Egypt** to Potiphar, an officer of Pharaoh's" (Genesis 37:36). The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that Pinehas came,** on his mother's side, **from** the family of **Joseph? But it is written: "And Elazar, Aaron's son, took himself a wife from the daughters of Putiel;** and she bore him Pinehas" (Exodus 6:25). **What, is it not that** Pinehas **came from** the family of **Yitro, who** was also called Putiel because he **fattened [*pittem*] calves for idol worship?** They answer: **No;** he was descended **from Joseph, who mocked [*pitpet*] his desire** by resisting the advances of Potiphar's wife.

The *gemara* then adds: *lo chinam holeich Pinechas lamilchama* — it was not for nothing that Pinchas went to war against Midian. He went specifically to avenge the honor of his mother's father, as Scripture records: *vehamidanim machru oto el Mitzrayim* — the Midianites sold Yosef into Egypt. This implies that Pinchas descended from Yosef on his maternal side.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא שְׁבָטִים מְבַזִּין אוֹתוֹ: רְאִיתֶם בֶּן פּוּטִי זֶה, בֵּן שֶׁפִּיטֵּם אֲבִי אִמּוֹ עֲגָלִים לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, יַהֲרוֹג נָשִׂיא מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל! אֶלָּא: אִי אֲבוּהּ דְּאִימֵּיהּ מִיּוֹסֵף — אִימֵּיהּ דְּאִימֵּיהּ מִיִּתְרוֹ, וְאִי אִימֵּיהּ דְּאִימֵּיהּ מִיּוֹסֵף — אֲבוּהּ דְּאִימֵּיהּ מִיִּתְרוֹ. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״מִבְּנוֹת פּוּטִיאֵל״, תְּרֵי מַשְׁמַע. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But is it not** the case that, according to an oral tradition, **the tribes** were **denigrating** Pinehas after he killed Zimri, saying: Did **you see this son of Puti, the son whose mother's father fattened cows for idol worship?** Should this man **kill a prince of Israel?** Evidently, his grandfather Puti was Yitro, not Joseph. The Gemara answers: **Rather,** Pinehas was descended from both Joseph and Yitro. **If his mother's father** descended **from Joseph, his mother's mother** descended **from Yitro. And if his mother's mother** descended **from Joseph, his mother's father** descended **from Yitro.** The Gemara confirms this resolution: The language **is also precise, as it is written:** Elazar took a wife **"from the daughters of Putiel,"** which **implies** that she came from **two** daughters of men named Putiel. The Gemara concludes: You may **learn from** the verse that this is so.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: is Pinchas really descended from Yosef? The *pasuk* states that Elazar ben Aharon took a wife from the daughters of *Putiel*, and she bore him Pinchas. So who is this *Putiel*? The *gemara* presents the classic explanation that *Putiel* alludes to Yitro — *shepitem agalim l'avoda zara* — who fattened calves for idol worship, giving rise to the name *Puti El* in the sense of fattening for the gods. This would make Pinchas a descendant of Yitro on his mother's side, not of Yosef.

The *gemara* then invokes the well-known *aggadic* tradition that after Pinchas slew Zimri, the tribes mocked him: *re'item ben Puti zeh* — "have you seen this descendant of Puti?" — *shepitem avi imo agalim l'avoda zara* — "whose grandfather fattened calves for idol worship?" — *veharag nesi miYisrael* — "and yet he had the audacity to slay a leader of Israel?" This *aggadic* understanding runs deep enough that one cannot simply discard the derivation from Yitro. The *gemara* therefore resolves the tension: Pinchas was descended from both lineages on his mother's side. Either his maternal grandfather descended from Yosef and his maternal grandmother descended from Yitro, or the reverse. The *gemara* notes that the word *Putiel* is pointed in a way that implies two roots — *dayka nami dektiv mibnot Putiel*, *trei mashma*, "the word implies two." He therefore avenged the honor of his ancestor Yosef by leading the war against the very people who sold him.

## Military Exemptions

### Three Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״וְדִבְּרוּ הַשֹּׁטְרִים אֶל הָעָם לֵאמֹר מִי הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר בָּנָה בַיִת חָדָשׁ וְלֹא חֲנָכוֹ יֵלֵךְ וְיָשֹׁב לְבֵיתוֹ וְגוֹ׳״. אֶחָד הַבּוֹנֶה בֵּית הַתֶּבֶן, בֵּית הַבָּקָר, בֵּית הָעֵצִים, בֵּית הָאוֹצָרוֹת. אֶחָד הַבּוֹנֶה, וְאֶחָד הַלּוֹקֵחַ, וְאֶחָד הַיּוֹרֵשׁ, וְאֶחָד שֶׁנִּתַּן לוֹ מַתָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna continues its discussion of the speech given before battle. **"And the officers shall speak to the people, saying: What man is there that has built a new house, and has not dedicated it? Let him go and return to his house,** lest he die in the battle, and another man dedicate it" (Deuteronomy 20:5). He is sent home if he is **one who builds a storehouse for straw, a barn for cattle, a shed for wood,** or **a warehouse.** Similarly, it applies if he is **one who builds, or** if he is **one who purchases, or** if he is **one who inherits, or** if he is **one to whom** it **is given** as **a gift.** In all these instances, the man returns from the war encampment.

The *gemara* turns to the next *mishnah*, which addresses the speech of the *shotrim* — the officers — to the assembled troops. The Torah instructs them to announce three categories of exemption: one who built a new house and did not yet inaugurate it; one who planted a vineyard and did not yet perform *chilul*; and one who betrothed a woman and did not yet marry her. Those who qualify return from the front. Alongside this, there is a category of soldiers who do not go back to the front line but remain in service providing support — supplying food, water, and maintaining the roads.

### House Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּמִי הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר נָטַע כֶּרֶם וְלֹא חִלְּלוֹ וְגוֹ׳״ — אֶחָד הַנּוֹטֵעַ כֶּרֶם, וְאֶחָד הַנּוֹטֵעַ חֲמִשָּׁה אִילָנֵי מַאֲכָל, וַאֲפִילּוּ מֵחֲמֵשֶׁת הַמִּינִין. אֶחָד הַנּוֹטֵעַ, וְאֶחָד הַמַּבְרִיךְ וְאֶחָד הַמַּרְכִּיב, וְאֶחָד הַלּוֹקֵחַ, וְאֶחָד הַיּוֹרֵשׁ, וְאֶחָד שֶׁנִּתַּן לוֹ מַתָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The next verse states: **"And what man is there that has planted a vineyard, and has not used the fruit** thereof? Let him go and return unto his house, lest he die in the battle and another man use the fruit thereof" (Deuteronomy 20:6). He is sent home if he is **one who plants** a whole **vineyard** of many vines, **or** if he is **one who plants** as few as **five fruit trees** of another variety, **and even** if these five are **from the five species.** The produce need not be all of one species. The same applies if he is **one who plants, or** if he is **one who layers** the vine, bending a branch into the ground so that it may take root and grow as a new vine, **or** if he is **one who grafts** different trees onto one another. **And** it applies if he is **one who purchases** a vineyard, **or** if he is **one who inherits** a vineyard, **or** if he is **one to whom** the vineyard **is given** as **a gift.**

The *mishnah* broadens the house category significantly. It is not limited to a standard dwelling: *echad haboneh beit hateven beit habakar beit ha'etzim* — whether it is a barn for straw, a stable for cattle, a shed for wood, or any storage facility with a roof. What matters is that it is a structure of some substance. Nor does one need to have built it himself: whether one built it, purchased it, inherited it, or received it as a gift — all are included.

### Vineyard Exemption

The vineyard exemption is likewise broad. The word *chilulo* in the *pasuk* — "and did not perform *chilul*" — is understood by the *gemara* to refer to the fourth year of *neta revai*, when the owner performs *chilul* by redeeming the fruit's sanctity onto money and spending that money in Jerusalem. This provides a window of four full years from the time of planting during which one is eligible for the exemption. (Some medieval commentators suggest that *chilul* may allude to a dedication ceremony, similar to *chanacho* by the house, but the *gemara* reads it as referring specifically to the fourth-year *kerem revai* process.)

The *mishnah* then expands the category of *kerem*: it includes not only a full vineyard but also five fruit-bearing trees — since the minimum *kerem* for purposes of other *halakhot* consists of five vines — even from five different species. And, parallel to the house, it includes not only planting but also *mavrich* (layering a branch underground to take root elsewhere), *markiv* (grafting), purchasing, inheriting, or receiving as a gift.

### Betrothal Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּמִי הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר אֵרַשׂ אִשָּׁה וְגוֹ׳״, אֶחָד הַמְאָרֵס אֶת הַבְּתוּלָה, וְאֶחָד הַמְאָרֵס אֶת הָאַלְמָנָה. אֲפִילּוּ שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם, וַאֲפִילּוּ שָׁמַע שֶׁמֵּת אָחִיו בַּמִּלְחָמָה — חוֹזֵר וּבָא לוֹ. כׇּל אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ שׁוֹמְעִין דִּבְרֵי כֹּהֵן מַעַרְכֵי מִלְחָמָה, וְחוֹזְרִין. וּמְסַפְּקִין מַיִם וּמָזוֹן, וּמְתַקְּנִין אֶת הַדְּרָכִים.
>
> **Translation:** The next verse states: **"And what man is there that has betrothed a wife,** and has not taken her? Let him go and return to his house, lest he die in the battle, and another man take her" (Deuteronomy 20:7). He is sent home if he is **one who betroths a virgin, or** if he is **one who betroths a widow.** This applies **even** if his *yevama*, his late brother's wife, is a **widow waiting for** him as **her *yavam*** to perform levirate marriage; **and even** if **he heard that his brother died in the war** and the widow begins to wait for him only then, **he returns and goes** home. **Each of these** men, although they are exempt, still **hear the address of the priest** and **the regulations of war** at the local camp, **and** thereafter **they return** to their respective homes. However, they still support the war effort, **and** they **provide water and food** for the soldiers **and repair the roads.**

The third category — betrothal — is similarly expanded. Whether the woman is a *betulah* (never-married virgin), a widow (*almanah*), or even a *shomeret yavam* — a woman awaiting *yibum* — the exemption applies. Remarkably, the *mishnah* extends this even to a case where a soldier heard during battle that his brother died in the war, leaving behind a widow: he returns as well, because he now has the obligation of *yibum*.

Those in these categories do not simply go home. They return from the front line but remain in national service, as the *mishnah* specifies: *umsapkim mayim umazon umtaknim es hadrachim* — they provide water and food and repair the roads. They serve the army in a support capacity without engaging in combat.

### No Exemption Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁאֵינָן חוֹזְרִין: הַבּוֹנֶה בֵּית שַׁעַר, אַכְסַדְרָה וּמִרְפֶּסֶת, הַנּוֹטֵעַ אַרְבָּעָ[ה] אִילָנֵי מַאֲכָל וַחֲמִשָּׁה אִילָנֵי סְרָק, הַמַּחֲזִיר אֶת גְּרוּשָׁתוֹ, אַלְמָנָה לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, גְּרוּשָׁה וַחֲלוּצָה לְכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט, מַמְזֶרֶת וּנְתִינָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְמַמְזֵר וּלְנָתִין — לֹא הָיָה חוֹזֵר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף הַבּוֹנֶה בַּיִת עַל מְכוֹנוֹ — לֹא הָיָה חוֹזֵר. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אַף הַבּוֹנֶה בֵּית לְבֵינִים בַּשָּׁרוֹן — לֹא הָיָה חוֹזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **And these** are the men **who do not return** to their homes: **One who builds a gateway,** or **an enclosed veranda [*akhsadra*], or a balcony;** or **one who plants** no more than **four fruit trees or** even **five** or more **non-fruit bearing trees;** or **one who remarries his divorced** wife. Nor is there an exemption for one who has betrothed a woman whom he is not permitted to marry: With regard to **a widow** betrothed **to a High Priest** (see Leviticus 21:7); **a divorcée or a *yevama* who performed *ḥalitza* [*ḥalutza*],** in lieu of entering into a levirate marriage, betrothed **to a common priest** (see Leviticus 21:13–15); **a *mamzeret* or a Gibeonite woman** betrothed **to an Israelite;** or **an Israelite woman** betrothed **to a *mamzer* or a Gibeonite** (see Deuteronomy 23:3); such a man **does not return** to his home. **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even one who rebuilds a house** as it stood originally **would not return. Rabbi Eliezer says: Even one who builds a** new **brick house in the Sharon would not return** because these houses are not stable and are expected to collapse periodically.

The *mishnah* then identifies cases where one does not return even to support duty. If one built a *sha'ar*, *achsadra*, or *mirpeset* — a gatehouse, colonnade, or porch — that does not qualify, because these are not habitable structures. If one planted only four fruit trees, or planted five trees that are of a species that does not bear fruit, that does not qualify. If a man remarries his own divorced wife — *hamachazir gerushato* — that does not qualify, since she is not a new wife to him. And if the betrothal or marriage involves a forbidden union — an *almanah* to a *kohen gadol*, a *gerusha* or *chalutzah* to a regular *kohen*, a *mamzeret* to a *Yisrael*, or a *bat Yisrael* to a *mamzer* or *nasin* — none of these qualify. (The *gemara* will raise the question of why forbidden unions need to be excluded, and that discussion will be addressed below.)

Rabbi Yehuda adds that one who rebuilt his house on its original foundation — *al mechono* — does not return, because it is analogous to restoring a prior state rather than inaugurating something genuinely new, similar to the logic of *hamachazir gerushato*. Rabbi Eliezer adds that even one who builds a standard brick house in the Sharon Valley does not return, because the ground there is so unstable — as recalled in the Yom Kippur prayer for the inhabitants of the Sharon — that such houses require frequent rebuilding and are not reliably permanent.

### Complete Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵלּוּ שֶׁאֵין זָזִין מִמְּקוֹמָן: בָּנָה בַּיִת וַחֲנָכוֹ, נָטַע כֶּרֶם וְחִלְּלוֹ, הַנּוֹשֵׂא אֶת אֲרוּסָתוֹ, הַכּוֹנֵס אֶת יְבִמְתּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נָקִי יִהְיֶה לְבֵיתוֹ שָׁנָה אֶחָת״. ״לְבֵיתוֹ״ — זֶה בֵּיתוֹ, ״יִהְיֶה״ — זֶה כַּרְמוֹ, ״וְשִׂמַח אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ״ — זוֹ אִשְׁתּוֹ, ״אֲשֶׁר לָקָח״ — לְהָבִיא אֶת יְבִמְתּוֹ. אֵין מַסְפִּיקִין לָהֶם מַיִם וּמָזוֹן, וְאֵין מְתַקְּנִין אֶת הַדְּרָכִים.
>
> **Translation:** **These** are the men **who do not** even **move from their places** because they do not even report to the camp: One who **built a house and dedicated it** within the year; one who **planted a vineyard and used its fruit** for less than a year; one **who marries his betrothed** and **one who marries his *yevama*,** his brother's widow who must enter into a levirate marriage or perform *ḥalitza*, **as it is stated:** "When a man takes a new wife, he shall not go out with the army…**he shall be free for his house one year,** and shall cheer his wife whom he has taken" (Deuteronomy 24:5). The mishna interprets the verse as follows: **"For his house"; this** means **his house** that he built. **"He shall be"; this** term includes **his vineyard. "And shall cheer his wife"; this is his wife. "Whom he has taken";** this phrase comes **to include his *yevama*,** who is considered his wife with respect to this *halakha* although he has not yet married her. Those who are exempt for these reasons **do not** even **provide water and food** to the soldiers, **and** they **do not repair the roads.**

The third group is those who do not even go to the front at all and are entirely exempt from any form of military service. These are: one who built a house and *chanacho* — actually inaugurated it; one who planted a vineyard and performed *chilul*; one who betrothed a woman and completed the marriage (*nisu'in*); and one who performed *yibum* with his *yevamah*. The *pasuk* specifying this final level of exemption — *naki yiheyeh l'veiso shanah achas vesimach es ishto asher lakach* — grants a full year at home to a man who has actually married, not merely betrothed. The *mishnah* applies this principle to all three categories: once the house, vineyard, or marriage has been fully actualized, one receives a complete year off from all military obligation, including support services.

This is, as a careful reading makes clear, somewhat counterintuitive. One might have expected that unfinished business — the un-inaugurated house, the unmarried bride — would produce the greater exemption, while completion would return one to duty. The *mishnah* teaches the opposite: partial actualization leads to support service; full actualization leads to complete exemption for a year. This is a *milchemet reshut*, an optional war, and the Torah's provisions apply accordingly. In a *milchemet mitzvah*, an obligatory war, all go to battle.

## Officers' Communication

### Speaker Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְדִבְּרוּ הַשֹּׁטְרִים״, יָכוֹל דְּבָרִים שֶׁל עַצְמָן? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְיָסְפוּ הַשֹּׁטְרִים״ — הֲרֵי דְּבָרִים שֶׁל עַצְמָן אָמוּר, הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״וְדִבְּרוּ הַשֹּׁטְרִים״ — בְּדִבְרֵי מְשׁוּחַ מִלְחָמָה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. הָא כֵּיצַד? — כֹּהֵן מְדַבֵּר וְשׁוֹטֵר מַשְׁמִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught: "And the officers shall speak…**What man is there that has built a new house, and has not dedicated it? Let him go and return to his house, lest he die in the battle, and another man dedicate it" (Deuteronomy 20:5). One **might** have thought that they say **their own words** and that the priest does not issue these proclamations. However, **when it says: "And the officers shall** speak **further"** (Deuteronomy 20:8), **their own words are mentioned** only in this latter verse. Evidently, then, the priest is the speaker in the earlier verse. **How** then **do I realize** the meaning of: **"And the officers shall speak"** in the first verse? **The verse is speaking of the words of the** priest **anointed for war. How so? The priest speaks** in an undertone, **and an officer calls out** the priest's words so that they are audible to the assembled.

The *gemara* now addresses the question of who speaks what. The *tanna* asks: when the Torah says *vedibru hashotrim* — and the officers shall speak — could this mean they speak entirely in their own words? Perhaps. But the Torah elsewhere says *veyasfu hashotrim* — and the officers shall add — which refers to the additional address about *hayarei verach halevav*, the fearful-hearted. That passage is clearly the officers' own speech. So what is the verse *vedibru hashotrim* referring to? The *gemara* concludes: *bedivrei meshuch milchama haksav medaber* — the Torah is speaking about the words of the *meshuch milchama*, which the officers communicate: *kohen medaber veshoter mashmia* — the *kohen* speaks and the officers amplify, projecting his words throughout the assembled ranks, precisely as a *tanna* (reciter) amplifies the words of an *amora* in the study hall.

### Three-Part Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי חֲדָא: כֹּהֵן מְדַבֵּר וְשׁוֹטֵר מַשְׁמִיעַ, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: כֹּהֵן מְדַבֵּר וְכֹהֵן מַשְׁמִיעַ, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: שׁוֹטֵר מְדַבֵּר וְשׁוֹטֵר מַשְׁמִיעַ. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָא כֵּיצַד? מִ״וְּנִגַּשׁ״ וְעַד ״וְדִבְּרוּ״ — כֹּהֵן מְדַבֵּר וְכֹהֵן מַשְׁמִיעַ, מִ״וְּדִבְּרוּ״ עַד ״וְיָסְפוּ״ — כֹּהֵן מְדַבֵּר וְשׁוֹטֵר מַשְׁמִיעַ, מִ״וְּיָסְפוּ״ וְאֵילָךְ — שׁוֹטֵר מְדַבֵּר וְשׁוֹטֵר מַשְׁמִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in **one** *baraita*: **A priest speaks, and an officer calls out** his words; **and it is taught** in **another** *baraita*: **A priest speaks and a priest calls out. And it is taught** in yet **another** *baraita*: **An officer speaks and an officer calls out. Abaye said: How** can **these** texts be reconciled? **From** the words: **"That** the priest **shall approach"** (Deuteronomy 20:2), **until** the words: **"And** the officers **shall speak"** (Deuteronomy 20:5), which consist of words of encouragement and inspiration, **a priest speaks and** another **priest calls out. From** the words **"and** the officers **shall speak" until** the words: **"And** the officers **shall** speak **further"** (Deuteronomy 20:8), which list various people who are to return home from the battle front because of happy occasions, **a priest speaks and an officer calls out. From** the words **"and** the officers **shall** speak **further" and on,** which address those who are fearful and fainthearted, **an officer speaks and** another **officer calls out.**

However, there is a difficulty: three *beraitot* seem to contradict one another. One says *kohen medaber veshoter mashmia*; a second says *kohen medaber vekohen mashmia*; a third says *shoter medaber veshoter mashmia*. Abaye resolves this by dividing the speech into three sections. From the moment the *kohen* draws near until the verse *vedibru hashotrim* — the section about courage and God's presence — the *kohen* both speaks and amplifies: *kohen medaber vekohen mashmia*. The section from *vedibru hashotrim* through the categories of house, vineyard, and betrothal is *kohen medaber veshoter mashmia* — the *kohen* speaks and the officers amplify. From *veyasfu* onward — the address about the fearful-hearted — it is *shoter medaber veshoter mashmia*, entirely the officers' domain.

The logic is coherent. The call for courage and divine presence appropriately comes entirely from the *kohen*, who embodies the sacred. The final section about the fearful soldier is entirely the officers', because it is the officers who will enforce order in the ranks — tradition records that deserters could be summarily punished — and it is they who must manage the question of who leaves and who stays. The middle section, dealing with the practical exemption categories, is a joint operation.

## House Analysis

### Hermeneutics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִי הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר בָּנָה בַיִת חָדָשׁ כּוּ׳״. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אֲשֶׁר בָּנָה״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אֲשֶׁר בָּנָה. לָקַח וְיָרַשׁ וְנִיתַּן לוֹ בְּמַתָּנָה מִנַּיִן — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מִי הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר בָּנָה״. ״בַּיִת״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בַּיִת. מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת בֵּית הַתֶּבֶן, וּבֵית הַבָּקָר, וּבֵית הָעֵצִים, וּבֵית הָאוֹצָרוֹת — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר בָּנָה״, מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. יָכוֹל שֶׁאֲנִי מְרַבֶּה אַף הַבּוֹנֶה בֵּית שַׁעַר אַכְסַדְרָה וּמִרְפֶּסֶת, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בַּיִת״, מָה בַּיִת הָרָאוּי לְדִירָה — אַף כֹּל הָרָאוּי לְדִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that the officers announce: **"What man is there that has built a new house,** and has not dedicated it? Let him go and return to his house, lest he die in the battle, and another man dedicate it" (Deuteronomy 20:5). **The Sages taught:** From the phrase **"That has built," I have** derived **only** that this applies to one who actually **built** a house. **From where** do I derive that it includes one who **purchased, or inherited, or has been given** a house **as a gift? The verse states** broadly: **"What man is there that has built."** By not merely stating: One that has built, but using the expanded "what man is there that has built," the verse includes any of these circumstances. From the term **"a house" I have** derived **only a house** in which people live. **From where** is it derived that the exemption is understood **to** also **include** one who builds **a storehouse for straw, a barn for cattle, a shed for wood, or a warehouse? The verse states: "That has built,"** which includes whatever one built, **in any case.** One **might** have thought that **I** should **include even one who builds a gateway,** or **an enclosed veranda, or a balcony;** however, **the verse states: "A house,"** which teaches that **just as a house** is, by definition, a structure that is **fit for living, so too,** this *halakha* applies to **every** structure that is potentially **fit for living,** although in practice it may be used for another purpose.

The *gemara* now probes the textual basis for each exemption category. A *baraita* addresses the house: *asher bana* — "who built" — I would know only about building. How do I know that purchasing, inheriting, or receiving as a gift are also included? *Talmud lomar: mi ha'ish asher bana* — the formulation *asher bana* is read as an expansion. Similarly: *bayit* — "house" — I would know only a house. How do I know that a barn (*beit hateven*), stable (*beit habakar*), woodshed (*beit ha'etzim*), or storehouse (*beit ha'otzarot*) are included? *Talmud lomar: asher bana mikol makom* — because the word *bana* precedes the word *bayit* in the verse, the act of building is mentioned before its object, allowing *bana* to encompass anything built that resembles a *bayit*.

### Habitable Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: ״בַּיִת״ כְּמַשְׁמָעוֹ. ״לֹא חָנַךְ״, ״וְלֹא חֲנָכוֹ״ — פְּרָט לְגַזְלָן. לֵימָא דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, דְּאִי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי הָא אָמַר: ״וְרַךְ הַלֵּבָב״, זֶה הַמִּתְיָירֵא
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says:** The word **"house"** is to be understood to mean, **as it indicates,** a building meant for human residence. With regard to the house for which a man may be sent back from the camp, since the verse does not state merely: **"And has not dedicated,"** but rather: **"And has not dedicated it,"** specifying the particular house to which the man is connected, the verse therefore **excludes a robber** who does not own his house. The Gemara asks: **Shall we say** that this opinion is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili? As, if** it were in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, didn't he say:** When the verse singles out the "fearful **and fainthearted"** (Deuteronomy 20:8), **this** is referring to **one who is afraid**

However, one might then go too far and include even a gatehouse, colonnade, or porch. The *gemara* limits this: *talmud lomar bayit* — the word *bayit* sets the threshold. *Mah bayit haraui ledirah* — just as a house is something fit for habitation, *af kol haraui ledirah* — so too, only structures fit for habitation are included. A gatehouse or porch fails this test, even though a barn technically could serve as sleeping quarters if necessary. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov dissents from the broader reading and insists that *bayit* means a *bayit* literally: only a proper dwelling qualifies.

### Chanuch & Theft

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵעֲבֵירוֹת שֶׁבְּיָדוֹ! אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, כְּגוֹן דַּעֲבַד תְּשׁוּבָה וִיהַב דְּמֵי. אִי הָכִי, הָוֵה לֵיהּ לוֹקֵחַ וְלֶיהְדַּר! כֵּיוָן דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא בְּתוֹרַת גְּזֵילָה אֲתָא לִידֵיהּ — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **because of sins that he has.** According to this interpretation, one who stole another's real estate should return home from war because of his guilt, which would seem to contradict the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov. The Gemara answers: **Even** if **you say** that this ruling is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili,** Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov still needs to exclude one in possession of a stolen house, as in a case **where one repented and gave money** to the owners of the house that he stole. Although in such a case he is not considered a criminal, the house was originally stolen, and therefore he must remain among the military ranks. The Gemara challenges this: **If so,** i.e., he repaid the owners, **he is** now a legal **purchaser** of the house **and he should return** from the military ranks. The Gemara answers: **Since initially** it **entered his possession with the status of stolen** property, he does **not** return.

The *gemara* then turns to the requirement of *lo chanacho* — that he did not yet inaugurate it. Why does the verse use the personal pronoun *chanacho* — "inaugurate it" — rather than the simpler *chanach*? The answer: *prat legazlan* — to exclude a thief. The inauguration must be something the person was legitimately entitled to perform; if the property was stolen, he has no right to inaugurate it, and it does not qualify for the exemption.

The *gemara* then asks whether this exclusion of the *gazlan* conflicts with the position of Rabbi Yosi haGelili, who interprets *verach halevav* — the fearful-hearted — as referring to one who fears his own sins: *zu hamityare me'aveirot shebeyado*. If so, a thief would already be excluded on those grounds — he is fearful of punishment for theft. Why state it separately? The *gemara* answers: the case under discussion is one where the thief has already done *teshuvah* and paid restitution. Since he has resolved his sin, he no longer qualifies as *verach halevav* due to his *aveirot*. Yet, the *gemara* counters, if he paid for it, he is now a legitimate purchaser — *havei lei lokei'ach* — and should be entitled to the exemption as one who purchased a house. The *gemara* responds: since the property originally came to him through theft — *keivan demeyikara betorat gezeilah ata lidei* — it remains outside the scope of this passage, regardless of subsequent payment.

One might ask: should we not apply *takanat hashavim* — the rabbinic principle of encouraging penitents — and allow him back from the *milchamah*? The *gemara* acknowledges the principle but maintains the exclusion nonetheless.

## Vineyard Analysis

### Five Trees Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּמִי הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר נָטַע כֶּרֶם כּוּ׳״. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אֲשֶׁר נָטַע״ — אֵין לִי אֶלָּא נָטַע. לָקַח וְיָרַשׁ וְנִיתַּן לוֹ בְּמַתָּנָה, מִנַּיִן — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּמִי הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר נָטַע״. ״כֶּרֶם״ — אֵין לִי אֶלָּא כֶּרֶם, מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת חֲמִשָּׁה אִילָנֵי מַאֲכָל וַאֲפִילּוּ מִשְּׁאָר מִינִין — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר נָטַע״. יָכוֹל שֶׁאֲנִי מְרַבֶּה הַנּוֹטֵעַ אַרְבָּעָה אִילָנֵי מַאֲכָל וַחֲמִשָּׁה אִילָנֵי סְרָק, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כֶּרֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues its discussion of those who return from the ranks. **"And what man is there that has planted a vineyard,** and has not used the fruit thereof**"** (Deuteronomy 20:6). **The Sages taught:** From the phrase **"that has planted," I have** derived **only** that it applies to one who actually **planted** a vineyard. **From where** is it derived that the military exemption likewise includes one who **purchased** it, one who **inherited** it, **and** one **to whom** it **was given as a gift? The verse states: "And what man is there that has planted."** By not merely stating: One that has planted, but using the expanded "what man is there that has planted," the verse includes any of these circumstances. From the specific term **"vineyard," I have** derived **only a vineyard; from where** do I derive that it comes **to include five fruit trees even from other species? The verse states: "That has planted,"** to include different types of trees. **Might I** include even **one who plants** only **four fruit trees and** one who plants **five** or more **non-fruit bearing trees? The verse states: "Vineyard."** The term excludes a quantity of trees that is too few to qualify as a vineyard, as well as trees that do not yield fruit at all.

The *gemara* applies the same hermeneutical method to the vineyard. *Asher nata* — "who planted" — I would know only planting. How do I know purchasing, inheriting, and receiving as a gift? *Talmud lomar: umi ha'ish asher nata kerem* — the word *asher* is expansive. And *kerem* — I would know only a vineyard. How do I know that five fruit-bearing trees of any species are included? *Talmud lomar: asher nata* — again, *nata* precedes *kerem* in the verse, allowing *nata* to encompass anything planted that resembles a *kerem*. One might then include even four fruit trees or five non-fruit-bearing trees. The *gemara* answers: *talmud lomar kerem* — it must resemble a *kerem*, and neither four trees nor barren trees constitute a *kerem*.

### Chilul & Fruit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: ״כֶּרֶם״ כְּמַשְׁמָעוֹ. ״לֹא חִלֵּל״, ״וְלֹא חִלְּלוֹ״ — פְּרָט לְמַבְרִיךְ וּלְמַרְכִּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says:** The word **"vineyard"** is to be understood **as it indicates,** and it is referring only to a grape vineyard. And since the verse does not state merely: **"And has not used** the fruit," but rather: **"And has not used** the fruit **thereof,"** it **excludes one who layers** the vine or branch **and one who grafts** different trees onto one another.

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov again defends the plain meaning: *kerem kemashma'o* — a *kerem* means what it sounds like, literally a vineyard, not five fruit trees of another species. The *gemara* also treats the phrase *lo chilalo* as containing a textual exclusion: *prat lemavrich umarkiv* — to exclude one who performed *mavrich* (layering) or *markiv* (grafting). This is puzzling, since the *mishnah* itself listed *mavrich* and *markiv* as modes that qualify for the exemption. The resolution will come in the following section.

### Grafting Exclusions

The tension is significant: the *mishnah* includes *mavrich* and *markiv* in the exemption, yet the *baraita* derived from *lo chilalo* seems to exclude them. Moreover, why would grafting be worse than simply purchasing a vineyard? The *gemara* addresses this directly in the next section.

## Grafting & Orlah

### Prohibited Grafting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: אֶחָד הַנּוֹטֵעַ וְאֶחָד הַמַּבְרִיךְ וְאֶחָד הַמַּרְכִּיב! אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּהַרְכָּבַת אִיסּוּר, כָּאן בְּהַרְכָּבַת הֶיתֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna: He is released if he is **one who plants, or** if he is **one who layers, or** if he is **one who grafts** trees? **Rabbi Zeira said** that **Rav Ḥisda said:** This is **not difficult. Here,** in the case where one does not return from the ranks, the man has performed **forbidden grafting; there,** in the case where one does return from the ranks, he has performed **permitted grafting.**

Rav Chisda, as transmitted by Rabbi Zeira, resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya, kan beharkavas issur, kan beharkavas heter* — there is no difficulty. The exclusion of *mavrich* and *markiv* from the exemption refers specifically to a case where the grafting was performed through a forbidden act — namely, *kilayim* (prohibited crossbreeding of species). When the *baraita* excludes it, it is referring to *harkavat issur*. When the *mishnah* includes *mavrich* and *markiv*, it refers to *harkavat heter*, a permitted grafting.

### Status Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי הַרְכָּבַת הֶיתֵּר הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא יַלְדָּה בְּיַלְדָּהּ — תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּבָעֵי מֶיהְדָּר מִשּׁוּם יַלְדָּה רִאשׁוֹנָה! אֶלָּא יַלְדָּה בִּזְקֵינָה. וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: יַלְדָּה שֶׁסִּיבְּכָה בִּזְקֵינָה, בָּטְלָה יַלְדָּה בַּזְּקֵינָה, וְאֵין בָּהּ דִּין עׇרְלָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to **this permitted grafting, what are the circumstances? If we say** that it is **a young** tree grafted together **with** another **young** tree, then **let him derive** the *halakha* **that one must return** from the ranks **due to the first young** tree. One should be exempt because of his first tree, which is young, and the grafted tree is irrelevant. **Rather,** it is a case in which **a young** tree is grafted with **an old** tree. The Gemara objects: **But didn't Rabbi Abbahu say:** With regard to **a young** tree **that was entangled,** i.e., grafted, **with an old** tree, **the young** one **is negated by the old** one, **and the law of *orla*** does **not** apply **to it.** Therefore, the grafted tree should likewise lose its status as a young tree, and one in this situation should not return from the ranks.

The *gemara* then asks: what exactly is a case of *harkavat heter* that would entitle one to the exemption? The *gemara* first considers *yeladah be-yeladah* — grafting a young vine (within three years, i.e., still in *orlah* status) onto another young vine. But this is rejected: *teipuk lei d'bamehadar mishum yeladah rishonah* — the exemption would apply anyway on account of the base vine itself, which is already in *orlah* status. That case requires no separate derivation from the graft.

The alternative is *yeladah be-zkeinah* — grafting a young shoot onto an older vine. Here Rabbi Abahu's ruling applies: *yeladah she-sibchah be-zkeinah batlah yeladah be-zkeinah* — a young vine that becomes incorporated into an old one takes on the status of the old vine and is no longer subject to *orlah*. If the exemption from war depends on having a vine in *orlah* status (as the *gemara* derives from *chililo*), then grafting a young vine onto an old one would not give the owner a new *orlah*-status vine; the graft loses its youth status by incorporation.

### Young on Young

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: לְעוֹלָם יַלְדָּה בְּיַלְדָּה, וּכְגוֹן דִּנְטַע לְהָךְ קַמַּיְיתָא לִסְיָיג וּלְקוֹרוֹת. דִּתְנַן: הַנּוֹטֵעַ לִסְיָיג וּלְקוֹרוֹת — פָּטוּר מִן הָעׇרְלָה. וּמַאי שְׁנָא יַלְדָּה בִּזְקֵינָה דְּבָטְלָה, וּמַאי שְׁנָא יַלְדָּה בְּיַלְדָּה דְּלָא בָּטְלָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya said: Actually,** one returns home if he grafts **a young** tree **with** another **young** tree, **and** it is a case **in which he planted this first** one **for a fence or for beams,** so that he is exempt from battle only because of the second tree, which he grafts for fruit. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Orla* 1:1): In a case of **one who plants** a tree **for a fence or** to yield wood **for beams,** the tree is **exempt from the** *halakhot* of ***orla*. The Gemara asks: **And what is different** about the case of **a young** tree paired **with an old** tree such **that** the young tree **is negated, and what is different** about the case of a **young** tree being planted for fruit paired **with a young** tree planted for wood such **that** the fruit tree **is not negated?**

Rabbi Yirmiya therefore proposes: *le-olam yeladah be-yeladah* — the case remains young-on-young, but with a critical distinction. The base vine, though young, was planted not for fruit but *li-syag u-le-kora* — as a fence or for its timber. A vine planted for non-fruit purposes is exempt from *orlah*, as the *mishnah* in another tractate teaches: *hanotei'a li-syag u-le-kora patur min ha-orlah*. So the base has no *orlah* status, and the owner of the base would have no military exemption on its account. Now someone grafts onto it a young shoot intended for fruit. That shoot is *chayav* in *orlah*, and through it the grafter earns his exemption from the war.

The case now presents a conceptual question: why does the grafted shoot retain its own *orlah* status rather than being absorbed into the base, which is *patur* from *orlah*? If — as Rabbi Abahu teaches for the opposite configuration — a young vine grafted onto an old vine loses its own status and takes on the status of the host, why doesn't the same logic apply here, so that the young shoot grafted onto the non-*orlah* base would become *patur* along with it?

### Bas Mahdar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם אִי מִימְּלִיךְ עֲלַהּ — לָאו בַּת מֶיהְדָּר הִיא, הָכָא אִי מִימְּלִיךְ עֲלַהּ — בַּת מֶיהְדָּר הִיא [דְּהָא מֵעִיקָּרָא לְפֵירֵי קָיְימָא]. מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַעָלוּ מֵאֵילֵיהֶן, דִּתְנַן: עָלוּ מֵאֵילֵיהֶן — חַיָּיבִין בְּעׇרְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** in the case of an older tree, **if one changes his mind about it** and wants it to be a young tree obligated in *orla*, **it cannot return** to its previous state, as he has grafted it. Therefore, it is exempt from the *halakhot* of *orla*. **Here,** when a tree is planted for wood, **if he changes his mind** and decides to grow it for fruit, **it can return** to its default state of being planted for fruit, **because initially it stands** to be used **for fruit.** Therefore, it is not exempt from the *halakhot* of *orla*. This ruling is **just as it is concerning** trees that **grew by themselves,** which are subject to *orla* although they were not consciously planted for their fruit. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Orla* 1:2): Fruit trees that **grew by themselves are obligated in *orla*** although the landowner did not plant them himself.

The *gemara* answers with the principle of *bas mahdar* — the potential to cause a return from war. In the case of *yeladah be-zkeinah* (young onto old), if the owner were to reconsider and want the old vine to produce fruit, it would still not subject the old vine to *orlah* (it is too old), and so there is no scenario in which the old vine becomes *bas mahdar* — no scenario in which it could ever generate a reason to return from the *milchamah*. Therefore the young shoot is fully incorporated into it.

Here, by contrast — *yeladah be-yeladah*, with the base being *patur* because it was planted for timber — if the owner were to change his mind and decide to use the base vine for fruit, it *would* then become *chayav* in *orlah*, because it is still young. *Im mimlech aleih bas mahdar hi* — if he changed his mind about the base, it would be a basis for returning from battle. Since the base at least has the *potential* to become *chayav* in *orlah*, the grafted young shoot retains its own *orlah* status and is not absorbed. The *gemara* supports this with the ruling that fruit that grows spontaneously without any planting intention is still *chayav* in *orlah* — *olu me'eileihen chayavin b'orlah* — since the potential for *orlah* obligation inheres in youth regardless of the planter's purpose. The *gemara* acknowledges that this is something of a *chiddush*, since the *bas mahdar* reasoning would suggest that merely changing one's mind about the purpose of a fence-vine would be sufficient to both impose *orlah* and entitle one to return from war.

### Joint Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלוֹקְמַהּ בְּכֶרֶם שֶׁל שְׁנֵי שׁוּתָּפִין, דְּהַאי הָדַר אַדִּידֵיהּ וְהַאי הָדַר אַדִּידֵיהּ? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת: כֶּרֶם שֶׁל שְׁנֵי שׁוּתָּפִין — אֵין חוֹזְרִין עָלָיו מֵעֶרְכֵי הַמִּלְחָמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Why is there a need to establish the mishna as discussing a case where one planted the first tree for a fence or for beams? **But let** the mishna **be established** as referring **to a vineyard belonging to two partners,** where the two trees involved in the grafting were co-owned by partners, and both trees were young. **As** in this case **this** one man **returns due to his** tree **and that** other man **returns due to his** tree. **Rav Pappa says:** Since the Gemara avoids the scenario of the vineyard belonging to partners in favor of discussing a case where one planted the first tree for a fence or for beams, **that is to say** if there is **a vineyard belonging to two partners,** evidently **they do not return for** the vineyard **from the ranks of** soldiers waging **war.**

The *gemara* then raises an alternative reading of the case of *harkavat heter*: perhaps the exemption applies to a *kerem shel shenei shutfin* — a vineyard with two partners — where one partner owns the base vine and another owns the grafted shoot. In such a case, the owner of the shoot would have a young vine in *orlah* status but could not claim exemption on account of the base. Rav Pappa derives from this discussion: *zo omeret, kerem shel shenei shutfin ein hozrin aleiv mei-orkhei hamilchama* — a jointly-owned vineyard does not entitle anyone to return from battle. One must have sole ownership to qualify for the exemption.

### Yibum Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא מֵחֲמִשָּׁה אַחִין וּמֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶן בַּמִּלְחָמָה, דְּכוּלָּן חוֹזְרִין? הָתָם — כׇּל חַד וְחַד קָרֵינָא בֵּיהּ ״אִשְׁתּוֹ״, הָכָא — כׇּל חַד וְחַד לָא קָרֵינָא בַּיהּ ״כַּרְמוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** in **what** way **is** the case of partners **different from** the following case: If there are **five brothers and one of them dies in the war,** the *halakha* is **that they all return.** Just as each brother returns because of his shared responsibility to perform levirate marriage or *ḥalitza* for the widow, each partner should return for his share in the young tree that was grafted. The Gemara answers: **There, each and every one is considered** one who must return for **his wife,** as any of them could readily marry her. However, **here, each and every one is not considered** one who must return for **his vineyard** because it does not belong exclusively to either of them.

The *gemara* challenges this from a case discussed elsewhere: if five brothers go to war and one is killed, leaving a widow who requires *yibum*, all the remaining brothers return from the front, since any one of them might fulfill the obligation of *yibum*. Is this not analogous to the partnership case — multiple people sharing a kind of joint claim, none of whom has exclusive title? The *gemara* distinguishes: *hasam kochad vechad karinan bei ishto* — there, whichever brother ultimately performs *yibum*, that woman will be exclusively his wife. The claim is individual and exclusive in its realization, even if it is currently shared. *Hacha kochad vechad lo karinan bei karmo* — here, neither partner will ever be able to say "my vineyard" in an exclusive sense. The verse requires a sense of individual ownership, and joint partnership cannot supply that.

## Mavrich & Stunted

### Branch on Veg

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: בְּמַבְרִיךְ אִילָן בְּיָרָק, וְהַאי תַּנָּא הוּא דְּתַנְיָא: הַמַּבְרִיךְ אִילָן בְּיָרָק, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל מַתִּיר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן גַּמְדָּא אִישׁ כְּפַר עַכּוֹ, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹסְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** In accordance with the *baraita* mentioned above, one does not normally return for a grafted or layered tree. However, the mishna states that one does return for a grafted or layered tree **in** a case where **one layers a tree** and grafts it **with a vegetable** plant. **And** that ruling **is** in accordance with **this *tanna*,** Rabbi Yehuda ben Gamda, as cited in a *baraita*. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Kilayim* 1:12): With regard to **one who layers a tree** and grafts it **with a vegetable** plant, the *tanna'im* engaged in a dispute concerning whether this kind of breeding is permitted. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel,** speaking **in the name of Rabbi Yehuda ben Gamda of the village of Akko, permits** one to do so, **and the Rabbis prohibit** it. Therefore, one would return from the ranks for a tree grafted to a vegetable plant, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda ben Gamda, although he would not return for the vegetable itself.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers yet another case of *harkavat heter* that would qualify for the exemption: *bemavrich ilan beyerek* — grafting a tree branch onto a vegetable plant. In such a case, the base is a vegetable, not a tree, so the grafted branch is not absorbed into a tree-host and retains its own status as a young tree, remaining *chayav* in *orlah*. This would therefore constitute a permitted grafting that carries *orlah* status and thus entitles the owner to return from battle.

### Vegetable Dispute

However, this case may itself be *harkavat issur* according to one opinion. A *baraita* records the dispute: Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel, in the name of Rabbi Yehuda ben Gamash of Kfar Akko, permits grafting a tree onto a vegetable; the Sages forbid it. According to the Sages, then, this would be a forbidden act, and the exemption derived from it would fall under *harkavat issur* rather than *harkavat heter*. According to Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel it is permitted, and in that case the branch retains its *orlah* status — since the vegetable host would never be subject to *orlah* — and the grafter would have a basis for returning from war.

### Stunted Trees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב: יַלְדָּה פְּחוּתָה מִטֶּפַח — חַיֶּיבֶת בְּעׇרְלָה כׇּל שְׁנוֹתֶיהָ, דְּמִתְחַזְיָא כְּבַת שַׁתָּא. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי שְׁתַּיִם כְּנֶגֶד שְׁתַּיִם וְאַחַת יוֹצְאָה זָנָב. אֲבָל כּוּלֵּיהּ כֶּרֶם — קָלָא אִית לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § After citing Rabbi Yoḥanan's interpretation of the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, the Gemara cites a string of other rulings that Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov. **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael, **he said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan** said **in the name of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov:** The Sages decreed that if there is **a young** grapevine **less than** one **handbreadth** tall, it **is obligated in *orla* all its years,** even after the three years mandated by the Torah, **because it appears like** a vine of **one year.** The Sages were concerned that if they permitted one to eat from such a vine, people would also eat true *orla*. **And this** prohibition **applies** to a very small section of **two** grapevines **opposite two** grapevines **and one** more vine **emerging** and growing between them, in the formation of **a tail. But** if the **entire vineyard** grows so short, **it** generates **publicity,** and people know that the vines are old enough to no longer be subject to *orla*.

The *gemara* then shifts to a related discussion brought by Rav Dimi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov. If a vine or tree is stunted, never growing more than a *tefach* in height, it is *chayav* in *orlah* for its entire life — *chayava b'orla kol shnoseha*. The reason is *d'michzi k'vas shata* — it always appears to be a one-year-old plant. Even if it is already five years old, observers will assume it is still within its first three years and therefore *orlah*, so it must always be treated as such to avoid causing others to sin. This ruling applies specifically when one has only a small stand of such stunted vines or trees — even a minimal *kerem* of five — because the configuration looks like a normal vineyard of young plants.

### Orchard Known

However, if one has an entire orchard of such stunted trees — *kula kerem* — the ruling changes. *Kala is lei* — the word gets out. Everyone in the area knows that this particular farmer has an unusual orchard of miniature trees. Since the reality is publicly known, there is no concern that observers will err, and the trees are not treated as perpetually in *orlah* status.

## Corpse Proximity

### Four Amos Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב: מֵת תּוֹפֵס אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת לִקְרִיאַת שְׁמַע, דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹעֵג לָרָשׁ חֵרֵף עֹשֵׂהוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael, **he said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan** said **in the name of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov: A corpse occupies four cubits** with regard **to** the exemption from **the recitation of *Shema*,** so that it is prohibited to recite *Shema* within this space, **as it is written** in the verse: **"Whoever mocks the poor blasphemes his Maker"** (Proverbs 17:5). Because the deceased cannot perform mitzvot, one who performs a mitzva in front of them is considered to be mocking them.

The *gemara* closes with one final tradition transmitted by Rav Dimi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov — collected here solely because it shares the same chain of transmission as the preceding rulings. *Meis tofes arba amos l'krias shema* — a corpse occupies four *amot* for the purpose of reciting *Shema*. One who is within four *amot* of a dead body may not recite *Shema*. The reason is the principle of *lo'eg l'rash chereif oseihu* — one who mocks the poor blasphemes his Maker — applied here to mean that reciting *Shema* in the presence of a *meis* who cannot himself fulfill the commandment is a form of mockery of the dead. This principle extends to other acts of religious observance as well.
