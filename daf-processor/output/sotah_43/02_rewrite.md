# Sotah 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Meshuch Milchama Speech

### Priest & Ark

The daf opens at the very last line of 42b, continuing the discussion of the *meshuch milchama* — the anointed priest who addresses the troops before battle. The Torah declares: *Hashem elokeichem hu haholeich imachem* — "Hashem your God is the one who walks with you." The *gemara* asks: *vechol kach lama* — what is the purpose of all of this? The obvious answer is that it gives the soldiers courage. But the question, as Rashi frames it, is more precise.

### Rashi on Halicha

Rashi explains that the question is not why this statement is made at all, but what is the emphasis of the word *haholeich* — "walking" or "going" — rather than simply "is with you." The answer is: *mipnei sheHashem vechol kinuyav munachim ba'aron* — because God's name and all of the various divine appellations are actually present in the *aron*. It is therefore not merely an abstract declaration that God accompanies the army; it is a statement that the physical object representing God's presence — the *aron* — is literally going with them into battle. This raises the interesting question of whether the *aron* itself was taken out of the *Beit Hamikdash* or the *Mishkan* and brought to the battlefield, and there is a view that a separate *aron* was used for battle, distinct from the one that resided in the sanctuary.

### Midian War Example

The *gemara* then brings a proof from the war against Midian: *vayishlach otam Moshe elef lamateh latzava, otam ve'et Pinechas ben Elazar hakohein latzava uchelei hakodesh vechatzotzrot hateruah beyado*. The *gemara* identifies the components: *otam* refers to the Sanhedrin; *Pinechas ben Elazar* is the *meshuch milchama*; *uchelei hakodesh* — the holy vessels — refers to the *aron* and the *luchot*; and *vechatzotzrot hateruah* — the trumpets of the blast — is rendered as *shofrot*. This last identification is noteworthy: *chatzotzrot* are the metal trumpets, while *shofrot* are made from an animal's horn. The *gemara* elsewhere indicates that in the Amoraic period these two terms exchanged their meanings in vernacular usage, which may explain why the *gemara* here renders the *chatzotzrot* of the *pasuk* as *shofrot*.

### Pinchas Genealogy

The *gemara* then adds: *lo chinam holeich Pinechas lamilchama* — it was not for nothing that Pinchas went to war against Midian. He went specifically to avenge the honor of his mother's father, as Scripture records: *vehamidanim machru oto el Mitzrayim* — the Midianites sold Yosef into Egypt. This implies that Pinchas descended from Yosef on his maternal side.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: is Pinchas really descended from Yosef? The *pasuk* states that Elazar ben Aharon took a wife from the daughters of *Putiel*, and she bore him Pinchas. So who is this *Putiel*? The *gemara* presents the classic explanation that *Putiel* alludes to Yitro — *shepitem agalim l'avoda zara* — who fattened calves for idol worship, giving rise to the name *Puti El* in the sense of fattening for the gods. This would make Pinchas a descendant of Yitro on his mother's side, not of Yosef.

The *gemara* then invokes the well-known *aggadic* tradition that after Pinchas slew Zimri, the tribes mocked him: *re'item ben Puti zeh* — "have you seen this descendant of Puti?" — *shepitem avi imo agalim l'avoda zara* — "whose grandfather fattened calves for idol worship?" — *veharag nesi miYisrael* — "and yet he had the audacity to slay a leader of Israel?" This *aggadic* understanding runs deep enough that one cannot simply discard the derivation from Yitro. The *gemara* therefore resolves the tension: Pinchas was descended from both lineages on his mother's side. Either his maternal grandfather descended from Yosef and his maternal grandmother descended from Yitro, or the reverse. The *gemara* notes that the word *Putiel* is pointed in a way that implies two roots — *dayka nami dektiv mibnot Putiel*, *trei mashma*, "the word implies two." He therefore avenged the honor of his ancestor Yosef by leading the war against the very people who sold him.

## Military Exemptions

### Three Categories

The *gemara* turns to the next *mishnah*, which addresses the speech of the *shotrim* — the officers — to the assembled troops. The Torah instructs them to announce three categories of exemption: one who built a new house and did not yet inaugurate it; one who planted a vineyard and did not yet perform *chilul*; and one who betrothed a woman and did not yet marry her. Those who qualify return from the front. Alongside this, there is a category of soldiers who do not go back to the front line but remain in service providing support — supplying food, water, and maintaining the roads.

### House Exemption

The *mishnah* broadens the house category significantly. It is not limited to a standard dwelling: *echad haboneh beit hateven beit habakar beit ha'etzim* — whether it is a barn for straw, a stable for cattle, a shed for wood, or any storage facility with a roof. What matters is that it is a structure of some substance. Nor does one need to have built it himself: whether one built it, purchased it, inherited it, or received it as a gift — all are included.

### Vineyard Exemption

The vineyard exemption is likewise broad. The word *chilulo* in the *pasuk* — "and did not perform *chilul*" — is understood by the *gemara* to refer to the fourth year of *neta revai*, when the owner performs *chilul* by redeeming the fruit's sanctity onto money and spending that money in Jerusalem. This provides a window of four full years from the time of planting during which one is eligible for the exemption. (Some medieval commentators suggest that *chilul* may allude to a dedication ceremony, similar to *chanacho* by the house, but the *gemara* reads it as referring specifically to the fourth-year *kerem revai* process.)

The *mishnah* then expands the category of *kerem*: it includes not only a full vineyard but also five fruit-bearing trees — since the minimum *kerem* for purposes of other *halakhot* consists of five vines — even from five different species. And, parallel to the house, it includes not only planting but also *mavrich* (layering a branch underground to take root elsewhere), *markiv* (grafting), purchasing, inheriting, or receiving as a gift.

### Betrothal Exemption

The third category — betrothal — is similarly expanded. Whether the woman is a *betulah* (never-married virgin), a widow (*almanah*), or even a *shomeret yavam* — a woman awaiting *yibum* — the exemption applies. Remarkably, the *mishnah* extends this even to a case where a soldier heard during battle that his brother died in the war, leaving behind a widow: he returns as well, because he now has the obligation of *yibum*.

### Support vs. Leave

Those in these categories do not simply go home. They return from the front line but remain in national service, as the *mishnah* specifies: *umsapkim mayim umazon umtaknim es hadrachim* — they provide water and food and repair the roads. They serve the army in a support capacity without engaging in combat.

### No Exemption Cases

The *mishnah* then identifies cases where one does not return even to support duty. If one built a *sha'ar*, *achsadra*, or *mirpeset* — a gatehouse, colonnade, or porch — that does not qualify, because these are not habitable structures. If one planted only four fruit trees, or planted five trees that are of a species that does not bear fruit, that does not qualify. If a man remarries his own divorced wife — *hamachazir gerushato* — that does not qualify, since she is not a new wife to him. And if the betrothal or marriage involves a forbidden union — an *almanah* to a *kohen gadol*, a *gerusha* or *chalutzah* to a regular *kohen*, a *mamzeret* to a *Yisrael*, or a *bat Yisrael* to a *mamzer* or *nasin* — none of these qualify. (The *gemara* will raise the question of why forbidden unions need to be excluded, and that discussion will be addressed below.)

Rabbi Yehuda adds that one who rebuilt his house on its original foundation — *al mechono* — does not return, because it is analogous to restoring a prior state rather than inaugurating something genuinely new, similar to the logic of *hamachazir gerushato*. Rabbi Eliezer adds that even one who builds a standard brick house in the Sharon Valley does not return, because the ground there is so unstable — as recalled in the Yom Kippur prayer for the inhabitants of the Sharon — that such houses require frequent rebuilding and are not reliably permanent.

### Complete Exemption

The third group is those who do not even go to the front at all and are entirely exempt from any form of military service. These are: one who built a house and *chanacho* — actually inaugurated it; one who planted a vineyard and performed *chilul*; one who betrothed a woman and completed the marriage (*nisu'in*); and one who performed *yibum* with his *yevamah*. The *pasuk* specifying this final level of exemption — *naki yiheyeh l'veiso shanah achas vesimach es ishto asher lakach* — grants a full year at home to a man who has actually married, not merely betrothed. The *mishnah* applies this principle to all three categories: once the house, vineyard, or marriage has been fully actualized, one receives a complete year off from all military obligation, including support services.

This is, as a careful reading makes clear, somewhat counterintuitive. One might have expected that unfinished business — the un-inaugurated house, the unmarried bride — would produce the greater exemption, while completion would return one to duty. The *mishnah* teaches the opposite: partial actualization leads to support service; full actualization leads to complete exemption for a year. This is a *milchemet reshut*, an optional war, and the Torah's provisions apply accordingly. In a *milchemet mitzvah*, an obligatory war, all go to battle.

## Officers' Communication

### Speaker Authority

The *gemara* now addresses the question of who speaks what. The *tanna* asks: when the Torah says *vedibru hashotrim* — and the officers shall speak — could this mean they speak entirely in their own words? Perhaps. But the Torah elsewhere says *veyasfu hashotrim* — and the officers shall add — which refers to the additional address about *hayarei verach halevav*, the fearful-hearted. That passage is clearly the officers' own speech. So what is the verse *vedibru hashotrim* referring to? The *gemara* concludes: *bedivrei meshuch milchama haksav medaber* — the Torah is speaking about the words of the *meshuch milchama*, which the officers communicate: *kohen medaber veshoter mashmia* — the *kohen* speaks and the officers amplify, projecting his words throughout the assembled ranks, precisely as a *tanna* (reciter) amplifies the words of an *amora* in the study hall.

### Three-Part Division

However, there is a difficulty: three *beraitot* seem to contradict one another. One says *kohen medaber veshoter mashmia*; a second says *kohen medaber vekohen mashmia*; a third says *shoter medaber veshoter mashmia*. Abaye resolves this by dividing the speech into three sections. From the moment the *kohen* draws near until the verse *vedibru hashotrim* — the section about courage and God's presence — the *kohen* both speaks and amplifies: *kohen medaber vekohen mashmia*. The section from *vedibru hashotrim* through the categories of house, vineyard, and betrothal is *kohen medaber veshoter mashmia* — the *kohen* speaks and the officers amplify. From *veyasfu* onward — the address about the fearful-hearted — it is *shoter medaber veshoter mashmia*, entirely the officers' domain.

The logic is coherent. The call for courage and divine presence appropriately comes entirely from the *kohen*, who embodies the sacred. The final section about the fearful soldier is entirely the officers', because it is the officers who will enforce order in the ranks — tradition records that deserters could be summarily punished — and it is they who must manage the question of who leaves and who stays. The middle section, dealing with the practical exemption categories, is a joint operation.

## House Analysis

### Hermeneutics

The *gemara* now probes the textual basis for each exemption category. A *baraita* addresses the house: *asher bana* — "who built" — I would know only about building. How do I know that purchasing, inheriting, or receiving as a gift are also included? *Talmud lomar: mi ha'ish asher bana* — the formulation *asher bana* is read as an expansion. Similarly: *bayit* — "house" — I would know only a house. How do I know that a barn (*beit hateven*), stable (*beit habakar*), woodshed (*beit ha'etzim*), or storehouse (*beit ha'otzarot*) are included? *Talmud lomar: asher bana mikol makom* — because the word *bana* precedes the word *bayit* in the verse, the act of building is mentioned before its object, allowing *bana* to encompass anything built that resembles a *bayit*.

### Habitable Test

However, one might then go too far and include even a gatehouse, colonnade, or porch. The *gemara* limits this: *talmud lomar bayit* — the word *bayit* sets the threshold. *Mah bayit haraui ledirah* — just as a house is something fit for habitation, *af kol haraui ledirah* — so too, only structures fit for habitation are included. A gatehouse or porch fails this test, even though a barn technically could serve as sleeping quarters if necessary. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov dissents from the broader reading and insists that *bayit* means a *bayit* literally: only a proper dwelling qualifies.

### Chanuch & Theft

The *gemara* then turns to the requirement of *lo chanacho* — that he did not yet inaugurate it. Why does the verse use the personal pronoun *chanacho* — "inaugurate it" — rather than the simpler *chanach*? The answer: *prat legazlan* — to exclude a thief. The inauguration must be something the person was legitimately entitled to perform; if the property was stolen, he has no right to inaugurate it, and it does not qualify for the exemption.

The *gemara* then asks whether this exclusion of the *gazlan* conflicts with the position of Rabbi Yosi haGelili, who interprets *verach halevav* — the fearful-hearted — as referring to one who fears his own sins: *zu hamityare me'aveirot shebeyado*. If so, a thief would already be excluded on those grounds — he is fearful of punishment for theft. Why state it separately? The *gemara* answers: the case under discussion is one where the thief has already done *teshuvah* and paid restitution. Since he has resolved his sin, he no longer qualifies as *verach halevav* due to his *aveirot*. Yet, the *gemara* counters, if he paid for it, he is now a legitimate purchaser — *havei lei lokei'ach* — and should be entitled to the exemption as one who purchased a house. The *gemara* responds: since the property originally came to him through theft — *keivan demeyikara betorat gezeilah ata lidei* — it remains outside the scope of this passage, regardless of subsequent payment.

One might ask: should we not apply *takanat hashavim* — the rabbinic principle of encouraging penitents — and allow him back from the *milchamah*? The *gemara* acknowledges the principle but maintains the exclusion nonetheless.

## Vineyard Analysis

### Five Trees Rule

The *gemara* applies the same hermeneutical method to the vineyard. *Asher nata* — "who planted" — I would know only planting. How do I know purchasing, inheriting, and receiving as a gift? *Talmud lomar: umi ha'ish asher nata kerem* — the word *asher* is expansive. And *kerem* — I would know only a vineyard. How do I know that five fruit-bearing trees of any species are included? *Talmud lomar: asher nata* — again, *nata* precedes *kerem* in the verse, allowing *nata* to encompass anything planted that resembles a *kerem*. One might then include even four fruit trees or five non-fruit-bearing trees. The *gemara* answers: *talmud lomar kerem* — it must resemble a *kerem*, and neither four trees nor barren trees constitute a *kerem*.

### Chilul & Fruit

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov again defends the plain meaning: *kerem kemashma'o* — a *kerem* means what it sounds like, literally a vineyard, not five fruit trees of another species. The *gemara* also treats the phrase *lo chilalo* as containing a textual exclusion: *prat lemavrich umarkiv* — to exclude one who performed *mavrich* (layering) or *markiv* (grafting). This is puzzling, since the *mishnah* itself listed *mavrich* and *markiv* as modes that qualify for the exemption. The resolution will come in the following section.

### Grafting Exclusions

The tension is significant: the *mishnah* includes *mavrich* and *markiv* in the exemption, yet the *baraita* derived from *lo chilalo* seems to exclude them. Moreover, why would grafting be worse than simply purchasing a vineyard? The *gemara* addresses this directly in the next section.

## Grafting & Orlah

### Prohibited Grafting

Rav Chisda, as transmitted by Rabbi Zeira, resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya, kan beharkavas issur, kan beharkavas heter* — there is no difficulty. The exclusion of *mavrich* and *markiv* from the exemption refers specifically to a case where the grafting was performed through a forbidden act — namely, *kilayim* (prohibited crossbreeding of species). When the *baraita* excludes it, it is referring to *harkavat issur*. When the *mishnah* includes *mavrich* and *markiv*, it refers to *harkavat heter*, a permitted grafting.

### Status Transfer

The *gemara* then asks: what exactly is a case of *harkavat heter* that would entitle one to the exemption? The *gemara* first considers *yeladah be-yeladah* — grafting a young vine (within three years, i.e., still in *orlah* status) onto another young vine. But this is rejected: *teipuk lei d'bamehadar mishum yeladah rishonah* — the exemption would apply anyway on account of the base vine itself, which is already in *orlah* status. That case requires no separate derivation from the graft.

The alternative is *yeladah be-zkeinah* — grafting a young shoot onto an older vine. Here Rabbi Abahu's ruling applies: *yeladah she-sibchah be-zkeinah batlah yeladah be-zkeinah* — a young vine that becomes incorporated into an old one takes on the status of the old vine and is no longer subject to *orlah*. If the exemption from war depends on having a vine in *orlah* status (as the *gemara* derives from *chililo*), then grafting a young vine onto an old one would not give the owner a new *orlah*-status vine; the graft loses its youth status by incorporation.

### Young on Young

Rabbi Yirmiya therefore proposes: *le-olam yeladah be-yeladah* — the case remains young-on-young, but with a critical distinction. The base vine, though young, was planted not for fruit but *li-syag u-le-kora* — as a fence or for its timber. A vine planted for non-fruit purposes is exempt from *orlah*, as the *mishnah* in another tractate teaches: *hanotei'a li-syag u-le-kora patur min ha-orlah*. So the base has no *orlah* status, and the owner of the base would have no military exemption on its account. Now someone grafts onto it a young shoot intended for fruit. That shoot is *chayav* in *orlah*, and through it the grafter earns his exemption from the war.

### Beams Exception

The case now presents a conceptual question: why does the grafted shoot retain its own *orlah* status rather than being absorbed into the base, which is *patur* from *orlah*? If — as Rabbi Abahu teaches for the opposite configuration — a young vine grafted onto an old vine loses its own status and takes on the status of the host, why doesn't the same logic apply here, so that the young shoot grafted onto the non-*orlah* base would become *patur* along with it?

### Bas Mahdar

The *gemara* answers with the principle of *bas mahdar* — the potential to cause a return from war. In the case of *yeladah be-zkeinah* (young onto old), if the owner were to reconsider and want the old vine to produce fruit, it would still not subject the old vine to *orlah* (it is too old), and so there is no scenario in which the old vine becomes *bas mahdar* — no scenario in which it could ever generate a reason to return from the *milchamah*. Therefore the young shoot is fully incorporated into it.

Here, by contrast — *yeladah be-yeladah*, with the base being *patur* because it was planted for timber — if the owner were to change his mind and decide to use the base vine for fruit, it *would* then become *chayav* in *orlah*, because it is still young. *Im mimlech aleih bas mahdar hi* — if he changed his mind about the base, it would be a basis for returning from battle. Since the base at least has the *potential* to become *chayav* in *orlah*, the grafted young shoot retains its own *orlah* status and is not absorbed. The *gemara* supports this with the ruling that fruit that grows spontaneously without any planting intention is still *chayav* in *orlah* — *olu me'eileihen chayavin b'orlah* — since the potential for *orlah* obligation inheres in youth regardless of the planter's purpose. The *gemara* acknowledges that this is something of a *chiddush*, since the *bas mahdar* reasoning would suggest that merely changing one's mind about the purpose of a fence-vine would be sufficient to both impose *orlah* and entitle one to return from war.

### Joint Ownership

The *gemara* then raises an alternative reading of the case of *harkavat heter*: perhaps the exemption applies to a *kerem shel shenei shutfin* — a vineyard with two partners — where one partner owns the base vine and another owns the grafted shoot. In such a case, the owner of the shoot would have a young vine in *orlah* status but could not claim exemption on account of the base. Rav Pappa derives from this discussion: *zo omeret, kerem shel shenei shutfin ein hozrin aleiv mei-orkhei hamilchama* — a jointly-owned vineyard does not entitle anyone to return from battle. One must have sole ownership to qualify for the exemption.

### Yibum Analogy

The *gemara* challenges this from a case discussed elsewhere: if five brothers go to war and one is killed, leaving a widow who requires *yibum*, all the remaining brothers return from the front, since any one of them might fulfill the obligation of *yibum*. Is this not analogous to the partnership case — multiple people sharing a kind of joint claim, none of whom has exclusive title? The *gemara* distinguishes: *hasam kochad vechad karinan bei ishto* — there, whichever brother ultimately performs *yibum*, that woman will be exclusively his wife. The claim is individual and exclusive in its realization, even if it is currently shared. *Hacha kochad vechad lo karinan bei karmo* — here, neither partner will ever be able to say "my vineyard" in an exclusive sense. The verse requires a sense of individual ownership, and joint partnership cannot supply that.

## Mavrich & Stunted

### Branch on Veg

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers yet another case of *harkavat heter* that would qualify for the exemption: *bemavrich ilan beyerek* — grafting a tree branch onto a vegetable plant. In such a case, the base is a vegetable, not a tree, so the grafted branch is not absorbed into a tree-host and retains its own status as a young tree, remaining *chayav* in *orlah*. This would therefore constitute a permitted grafting that carries *orlah* status and thus entitles the owner to return from battle.

### Vegetable Dispute

However, this case may itself be *harkavat issur* according to one opinion. A *baraita* records the dispute: Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel, in the name of Rabbi Yehuda ben Gamash of Kfar Akko, permits grafting a tree onto a vegetable; the Sages forbid it. According to the Sages, then, this would be a forbidden act, and the exemption derived from it would fall under *harkavat issur* rather than *harkavat heter*. According to Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel it is permitted, and in that case the branch retains its *orlah* status — since the vegetable host would never be subject to *orlah* — and the grafter would have a basis for returning from war.

### Stunted Trees

The *gemara* then shifts to a related discussion brought by Rav Dimi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov. If a vine or tree is stunted, never growing more than a *tefach* in height, it is *chayav* in *orlah* for its entire life — *chayava b'orla kol shenoseha*. The reason is *d'michzi k'vas shata* — it always appears to be a one-year-old plant. Even if it is already five years old, observers will assume it is still within its first three years and therefore *orlah*, so it must always be treated as such to avoid causing others to sin. This ruling applies specifically when one has only a small stand of such stunted vines or trees — even a minimal *kerem* of five — because the configuration looks like a normal vineyard of young plants.

### Orchard Known

However, if one has an entire orchard of such stunted trees — *kula kerem* — the ruling changes. *Kala is lei* — the word gets out. Everyone in the area knows that this particular farmer has an unusual orchard of miniature trees. Since the reality is publicly known, there is no concern that observers will err, and the trees are not treated as perpetually in *orlah* status.

## Corpse Proximity

### Four Amos Rule

The *gemara* closes with one final tradition transmitted by Rav Dimi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov — collected here solely because it shares the same chain of transmission as the preceding rulings. *Meis tofes arba amos l'krias shema* — a corpse occupies four *amot* for the purpose of reciting *Shema*. One who is within four *amot* of a dead body may not recite *Shema*. The reason is the principle of *lo'eg l'rash chereif oseihu* — one who mocks the poor blasphemes his Maker — applied here to mean that reciting *Shema* in the presence of a *meis* who cannot himself fulfill the commandment is a form of mockery of the dead. This principle extends to other acts of religious observance as well.
