# Shevuot 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Eliezer vs. Akiva

### Tumat Mikdash Overview

We are transitioning to the final portion of the first perek's *mishnah*, which circles back to the laws of *tumat mikdash* and the *korban oleh v'yored* — the variable-value sin-offering brought according to one's means. The basic structure of the *mishnah* is the *shetayim shehen arba* derived from the verse *v'ne'elam mimenu v'hu tamei* — "it was hidden from him and he was *tamei*." The simple reading of the *pasuk* is that a person was unaware of his *tumah*, became *tamei*, and subsequently did something prohibited: either entering the *Beit HaMikdash* or eating a *korban*. Those are the two primary cases.

### Shetayim Shehen Arba

The additional two cases — making the total four — arise when a person was aware of his *tumah* but the other element was hidden from him: either he did not realize that what he was eating was a *korban*, or he did not realize he was entering the *Beit HaMikdash*. This is the expanded reading that yields the *arba*.

### Rabbi Akiva's Ruling

*Rabbi Akiva*, however, rejects that expansion. He reads the *pasuk* according to its plain sense — *pshat* — and holds that one is *chayav* a *korban* only in cases of *helam tumah*: when the person forgot that he was *tamei*. If the person was aware of his *tumah* but forgot that he was entering the *Mikdash*, he does not bring a *korban*. This is a striking result: such a person is neither a *meizid* and therefore not subject to court punishment, nor a *shogeg* of the type the Torah addresses, and therefore not obligated in a *korban*. He simply falls between the categories. Interestingly, *Tosafot* records the view of *Rabbeinu Tam* that such a person might nonetheless bring a standard *korban chatat* since he performed an act for which, had it been intentional, he would be *chayav kares* — but this view has no clear support in the *Gemara*, and the straightforward reading is that he brings nothing and relies on *teshuvah* and *Yom Kippur*.

### Rabbi Yishmael Disagrees

*Rabbi Yishmael*, who is the author of the *mishnah* before us, disputes *Rabbi Akiva*. *Rabbi Yishmael* holds that one is *chayav* whether what was forgotten was the *tumah* — *helam tumah* — or whether what was forgotten was the *Mikdash* — *helam mikdash*. Both cases of forgetting obligate a *korban*.

### Mishnah: Rabbi Eliezer

The *mishnah* actually introduces a third voice before quoting *Rabbi Akiva* — that of *Rabbi Eliezer*, who was *Rabbi Akiva*'s own teacher. *Rabbi Eliezer omer ha-sheretz v'ne'elam mimenu, al helam sheretz chayav* — one is *chayav* if what was forgotten was specifically that one had touched a *sheretz*. *Rabbi Akiva*, meanwhile, frames his position as *al helam tumah* — on the forgetting of *tumah* in general. Both seem to be making the same fundamental point: the *korban* is owed only when one forgot one was *tamei*, not when one forgot one was in the *Mikdash*.

### Mai Beinayhu

This immediately raises the question: *mai beinaihu* — what is the practical difference between saying *helam sheretz* and *helam tumah*? They appear to be making identical claims.

## Sheretz vs. Neveilah

### Chizkiyya's Resolution

*Chizkiyya* explains that the debate between *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Akiva* concerns a case where a person knows he is *tamei* but does not know *how* he became *tamei* — specifically, whether it was through contact with a *sheretz* or through contact with a *neveilah* of an improperly slaughtered animal. The practical question seems minor, but it becomes significant once we attend to the *pesukim* themselves. The Torah, in *Vayikra*, after stating the general principle *o nefesh ki tiga b'chol davar tamei*, goes on to enumerate concrete examples: *o b'nivlat chayah tmeiah, o b'nivlat behemah tmeiah, o b'nivlat sheretz tamei v'ne'elam mimenu*. Three specific sources of *tumah* are listed. Why specify at all, given that we already know the general rule?

### Rabbi Eliezer's Position

*Rabbi Eliezer* holds that the specificity of the *pesukim* is meaningful. Since the Torah details the concrete source of *tumah*, what is required is that one initially knew precisely how one became *tamei*. Recall that for a *korban* to be owed, the structure is *yedi'ah batchilah* — initial knowledge — followed by *he'elamah* — forgetting. *Rabbi Eliezer* reads *nivlat sheretz tamei v'ne'elam* as requiring that the *ne'elam*, the forgetting, apply to the specific concrete knowledge of how one became *tamei*. If a person knows he is *tamei* but cannot recall whether it was through a *sheretz* or a *neveilah*, that gap in initial specificity means no *korban* is owed. As the *Gemara* formulates it: *Rabbi Eliezer savar be'inan ad yeida ee b'sheretz itmei ee b'neveilah itmei* — Rabbi Eliezer requires that one know whether one became *tamei* through a *sheretz* or a *neveilah*.

### Rabbi Akiva's Position

*Rabbi Akiva* disagrees. *Lo be'inan ad yeida* — one need not know that level of specificity. *V'keivan d'yada d'itmei b'olam* — since one knows that one became *tamei* in some way, that general awareness of *tumah* suffices. There is no requirement to know the precise source.

### Ulla's Source Analysis

*Ulla* offers the same explanation — *sheretz v'neveilah ika beinaihu* — and his reading of *Rabbi Eliezer* is demonstrated through an apparent internal contradiction in *Rabbi Eliezer*'s own positions, which *Ulla* then resolves. The *Gemara* presents this as: *d'Ulla rami d'Rabbi Eliezer a'd'Rabbi Eliezer u'mishni*.

---

## Mah Nafshach Principle

### Cheilev and Nosar

The problem *Ulla* raises is this: does *Rabbi Eliezer* truly require such specific concrete knowledge as a general matter when it comes to *korban chatat* obligations? The *Gemara* cites a *mishnah* in which *Rabbi Eliezer* addresses a different case: a person had before him *cheilev* — forbidden fat — and *nosar* — a *korban* left over beyond its permitted time — and ate one of the two pieces without knowing which one. Both prohibitions carry *kares* if violated intentionally; if violated unknowingly, both obligate a *chatat*. The question is whether such a person brings a *korban chatat* when he does not know which specific category of sin he committed.

### Mah Nafshach

*Rabbi Eliezer* answers: *mah nafshach* — whichever way it falls out, the result is the same. *Cheilev achal chayav, nosar achal chayav* — whichever one he ate, he is *chayav*. The same logic applies to analogous cases.

### Further Examples

The *mishnah* extends this principle to additional scenarios. If a person performed *melachah* and does not know whether he violated *Shabbos* or *Yom Kippur*, again *Rabbi Eliezer* says *mah nafshach*: *Shabbos chillail chayav, Yom Kippur chillail chayav*. The argument is that the difference in punishment between them — since one involves *misas beis din* — applies only *b'meizid*, not in a *shogeg* case. Similarly, if a man knows he slept with a woman he is forbidden to, but cannot recall whether it was his wife during *niddah* or his sister: *ishto niddah ba'al chayav, achoso ba'al chayav* — either way he is *chayav*. In all these cases, *Rabbi Eliezer* holds that the person brings a *chatat* without needing to identify the specific sin.

This presents a clear tension. If *Rabbi Eliezer* does not require concrete knowledge of the specific type of sin for a general *korban chatat*, why does he require it in the *tumat mikdash* context?

### Rabbi Yehoshua Disagrees

*Rabbi Yehoshua* objects to *Rabbi Eliezer*'s *mah nafshach* principle by citing the verse *o hoda eilav chattaso asher chata bah* — "or his sin becomes known to him through which he sinned." The word *bah* — "therein" or "through it" — indicates that one must know the specific sin through which one transgressed: *ad she'yivada lach bameh chata*, until it is concretely known to you how you sinned. The normative assumption, in line with *Rabbi Yehoshua*, is that a *korban chatat* requires specification of the sin.

---

## Eliezer and Yehoshua

### Ulla's Resolution

Having established the apparent contradiction in *Rabbi Eliezer* — he does not require specificity of sin in the *chatat* context, yet he does require specificity of *tumah* source in the *tumat mikdash* context — *Ulla* resolves it. *Hasam asher chata v'heivi amar Rachmana* — in the general *chatat* context, the Torah's emphasis is simply that you sinned and you bring a *korban*. *Kol shehu chata* — however you sinned, it doesn't matter: the focus is on the fact of the sin, and therefore specifics are not required.

### Two Different Contexts

This is a different framework from *tumat mikdash*. In the general *chatat* context, *asher chata v'heivi* — the Torah's focus is on the sin and the bringing of the offering, without specifying the mode of sin. There the word *bah* is used by *Rabbi Eliezer* not to require specificity of the category of sin, but rather to exclude the case of *mit'asek* — purely incidental action. *Asher chata bah*, "through which he sinned," teaches that the act must be associated with you in a meaningful sense. If the prohibited act occurred entirely incidentally, as a slip disconnected from any intentional act you were performing, you are not considered to have "sinned therein" and no *korban* is owed. That is the *prat l'mit'asek* — the exclusion of the *mit'asek* case — which *Rabbi Eliezer* derives from *bah*.

To clarify the distinction: a *shogeg* is a person who fully controls and intends the physical act he performs but lacks awareness of the relevant facts that would make it a sin. He intended to eat that piece of meat; he simply did not know it was *cheilev*. His act is fully his. A *mit'asek*, by contrast, performs an entirely different physical act than the one he intended — the prohibited result occurs as a purely incidental slip, so disconnected from his intention that the act cannot be attributed to him in the relevant sense. The *Gemara* offers the example of reaching to pick up one object and accidentally plucking another, still-attached one. That is *mit'asek*.

---

## Sheretz vs. Neveilah (II)

### Pesukim Analysis

The contrast with *tumat mikdash* is then explained. The *Gemara* notes: *hacha michdi kesiv b'chol davar tamei* — the Torah could have simply said "a *tamei* object" and stopped there. Yet it proceeds to specify concrete examples: *o b'nivlat chayah tmeiah, o b'nivlat behemah tmeiah, o b'nivlat sheretz tamei*. This specificity is entirely superfluous if the sole point is general *tumah*. The inference — *shema mina* — is that the Torah is deliberate: the specific mode of becoming *tamei* must be known. That is why in the *tumat mikdash* context, the framework shifts and *Rabbi Eliezer* requires that initial knowledge be concrete and specific.

*Rabbi Akiva*, for his part, answers that the concrete enumeration of sources in the *pesukim* was necessary for other reasons — specifically, to teach the ruling known as the teaching of *Rebbi*, which the *Gemara* addressed approximately twelve *dapim* earlier, and in keeping with the principle of *d'tani d'vei Rabbi Yishmael*: if a *parasha* is stated and then repeated, it was repeated only in order to convey a new *halacha*. So the specifics served another purpose and cannot be pressed into service to require specific initial knowledge of the mode of *tumah*.

---

## Eliezer and Yehoshua (II)

### Tumat Mikdash Specificity

What we observe throughout this discussion is the strikingly high degree of specificity and discernment that the *Gemara* attributes to the laws of *tumat mikdash*. The framework is not generic. Each category of *korban* on *Yom Kippur* addresses a specific dimension; separate offerings address *Rosh HaShanah*, *Rosh Chodesh*, and so forth, each calibrated to a distinct aspect of *tumat mikdash*. Against that backdrop, it is fitting that mere general awareness of *tumah* does not suffice for *Rabbi Eliezer* — the whole framework demands precision. One might connect this to the verse quoted at the end of the previous session's learning: *lehavdil bein hakodesh uvein hachol uvein hatamei uvein hatahor* — the very enterprise of *tumat mikdash* calls for a high level of vigilance and discernment. The *Gemara* does not pursue *ta'amei hamitzvot* here; it is content to ground the ruling in the *pesukim*. But the texture of those *pesukim* is coherent with the broader character of the laws.

---

## Yochanan: Mishmosh D'ors…

### Mishmosh D'orshin

*Rabbi Yochanan* now offers a fundamentally different reading of the dispute. He says: *mishmosh dorshin ika beinaihu* — the difference between *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Akiva* is not a practical halakhic disagreement at all, but only a question of which part of the *pesukim* each derives the ruling from. Both agree that the *korban* is owed only when *helam tumah* — one forgets one is *tamei*; both agree there is no *korban* for *helam mikdash* alone. *Rabbi Akiva* learns this from the word *v'hu tamei* and its attendant *v'ne'elam*, focusing on the word *tamei*. *Rabbi Eliezer* focuses on the word *sheretz*. But as a matter of *halacha*, they say the same thing.

### Rav Sheshet's Version

The *Gemara* adds: *v'iseima Rav Sheshet mishmosh dorshin ika beinaihu* — this reading is alternatively attributed to *Rav Sheshet*, with the note that *d'Rav Sheshet machlif* — *Rav Sheshet* would sometimes invert the attribution, assigning *Rabbi Eliezer*'s formulation to *Rabbi Akiva* and vice versa. Whether this was deliberate — to signal that they are not really disagreeing — or simply because the distinction was so minor that the attributions were interchangeable, the effect is the same: both *Tannaim* are making the identical halakhic point, differing only in which word of the *pasuk* they derive it from.

## Helam Zeh V'Zeh

### Rava's Inquiry

*Rava* poses a question to *Rav Nachman*: according to *Rabbi Akiva* and *Rabbi Eliezer*, the *korban* is owed only for *helam tumah*, not for *helam mikdash*. What happens when both were forgotten — when a person forgot both that he was *tamei* and that the place he entered was *Mikdash*? Is he *chayav* or *patur*?

The question is subtle. On one reading, *Rabbi Akiva* and *Rabbi Eliezer* require only that the person not have known he was *tamei* — they do not require that he be aware of the *Mikdash*. If so, the mere forgetting of *tumah* should suffice to create the *chiyuv*, regardless of whether the *Mikdash* was also forgotten. On another reading, perhaps their ruling is that the specific configuration of not-knowing-*tumah* while knowing-*Mikdash* is what triggers the *korban*, and in the absence of awareness of either element, neither side of the equation is met.

### First Response

The first answer given is the intuitive one — *harei helam tumah b'yado v'chayav*: since the element of forgetting *tumah* is present, and that is all that is required, he is *chayav*.

### Alternative Response

The alternative answer reverses this: *aderaba harei helam Mikdash b'yado v'patur* — since the element of forgetting the *Mikdash* is also present, and forgetting the *Mikdash* is what exempts, he is *patur*.

### Rav Ashi's Analysis

*Rav Ashi* proposes a more sophisticated approach: *chazinan* — let us assess the facts. *Ei mi'tumah ka parish* — if awareness of the *tumah* alone would have caused the person to separate, because it would have triggered his memory that this was a *korban* or a *Mikdash*, then *harei he'elam tumah b'yado v'chayav*: the fundamental forgotten element was the *tumah*, and he is *chayav*. *Im mi'mikdash ka parish* — if becoming aware that this was the *Beit HaMikdash* would have been sufficient to cause him to separate, because that would have triggered his memory that he was *tamei*, then *harei he'elam mikdash b'yado v'patur*: the fundamental forgotten element was the *Mikdash*, and he is *patur*.

The logic is that whichever forgotten element, if restored, would have triggered recall of the other, is the more primary forgotten element and determines the outcome.

### Ravina's Question

*Ravina* objects to *Rav Ashi*'s framework. He points out that this assessment is ultimately circular: *kelum parish mi'tumah ella mishum mikdash* — the only reason a person would separate upon being reminded of his *tumah* is because he would then realize he is about to enter the *Mikdash*. Conversely, *kelum parish mi'mikdash ella mishum tumah* — the only reason being reminded of the *Mikdash* matters is because he would then recall he is *tamei*. The two are so interdependent that asking which would trigger the other does not resolve the question; it simply rephrases it. Both were forgotten, and the analysis goes in circles.

### Rashi's Conclusion

After the exchange of *Rav Ashi* and *Ravina*, *Rashi* draws a conclusion from the *Gemara*'s direction. Looking at the words *ela lo shna* — "it makes no difference" — *Rashi* resolves the question as *patur*. This is a somewhat surprising result; one might have expected that since forgetting *tumah* was present, the *chiyuv* should attach. Nevertheless, *Rashi* concludes that when both were forgotten simultaneously and neither can be identified as the primary, the person is exempt.

## Shnei Shvilin

### Both Paths Case

The *Gemara* now introduces a *baraita*: *shnei shvilin echad tamei v'echad tahor* — there are two paths, and it is known that a corpse is buried beneath one of them, though not which. Anyone who walks on the *tamei* path will certainly become *tamei*. A person walks on both paths and then, having forgotten, enters the *Beit HaMikdash*. He is certainly *tamei* — one of the two paths made him so — and he forgot about it before entering. The ruling of the *Tanna Kamma* is: *chayav*. He is *chayav* a *korban*. There was definite *tumah*, and when he enters the *Mikdash* he is unaware of it — this is the classic *helam tumah* scenario.

### Separate Days Case

A more complex case follows: a person walks on the first path and then, while still in a state of doubtful *tumah*, enters the *Beit HaMikdash*. He then undergoes the sprinkling of *parah adumah* ashes and immersion in a *mikvah* — the process for clearing a *safeik tumah* acquired from a *meis* — and is now *tahor* in relation to path A. He then walks on path B, forgets, and enters the *Beit HaMikdash* again. Now, on either the first entry or the second entry, he sinned: on one of those days, he entered the *Mikdash* while *tamei*, albeit while in a state of *safeik tumah* at the time he was aware. In hindsight, the combination of the two episodes establishes that he definitely sinned on one of the two occasions. The *Tanna Kamma* rules: *chayav*.

### Rabbi Shimon Patur

*Rabbi Shimon* exempts in this case. Moreover, *Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda* in the name of *Rabbi Shimon* goes further — exempting even in the first case, where the person walked both paths before entering the *Mikdash*.

### Gemara Questions

The *Gemara* is puzzled by *Rabbi Shimon*'s position in the first case. *V'amai*? *Mimma nafshach, tamei hu* — make up your mind; the person is definitively *tamei*. He walked path A and path B. One of those was certainly the *tamei* path. He then entered the *Mikdash*. Why is he *patur*?

### Rava's Explanation

*Rava* explains the case: *kegon shehalach berishona uveshaah shehalach bashniya shachach shehalach berishona* — the scenario is that the person walked path A and then, at the moment he walked path B, he had already forgotten that he walked path A. So at no point was he ever aware that he was *vaday tamei*. When he was on path A, he knew he was *safeik tamei*. When he moved to path B, he forgot path A and again knew only that he was *safeik tamei* — aware of one doubtful source but not the other. He was therefore, in reality, *vaday tamei*, but he never possessed awareness of being *vaday tamei*. The question is whether this partial and overlapping awareness of *safeik* constitutes sufficient *yedi'ah* to form the basis of a *korban*.

### Miktzas Yedia Debate

This is precisely the disagreement between the *Tanna Kamma* and *Rabbi Shimon*. *Tanna Kamma savar amrinan miktzas yediyah kechol yediyah* — the *Tanna Kamma* holds that partial awareness of one's definite *tumah* is treated as full awareness: since the person was in reality certainly *tamei*, even a partial awareness of that *tumah* suffices to create the *yedi'ah batchilah* required for *chiyuv*. *V'Rabbi Shimon savar lo amrinan miktzas yediyah kechol yediyah* — *Rabbi Shimon* disagrees. Partial awareness of definite *tumah* does not count as full awareness, and without full awareness of his *vaday tumah*, there can be no *chiyuv*.

## Safek Yedia Analysis

### Yochanan: Exception Rule

The *Gemara* now addresses the earlier case — *shnei shvilin* with entries on separate days — in which the *Tanna Kamma* says *chayav* even though at no point was the person aware of *vaday tumah*, only of *safeik tumah* on each separate occasion. *Rabbi Yochanan* says: *kan asu safeik yediyah kiyediyah* — here, the law treats doubtful awareness as equivalent to full awareness. He acknowledges that this is an exception, indicated by the phrase *kan asu* — "here they made it so." The underlying rationale he will explain.

### Reish Lakish Reading

*Reish Lakish* reads the same case differently. He says: *ha mani Rabbi Yishmael hi d'amar lo beinan yedia batchila* — this case follows *Rabbi Yishmael*, who holds that one need not have been aware at the outset at all. Since *Rabbi Yishmael* does not require *yedi'ah batchilah*, even a person who was oblivious throughout can be *chayav* once he ultimately becomes aware. For *Rabbi Yishmael*, the absence of initial certain awareness is not a disqualifier, and therefore this case presents no difficulty.

---

## Cheilev and Safek Awaren…

### Safek Cheilev Cases

The *Gemara* now introduces a parallel case involving *cheilev* in order to challenge both *Rabbi Yochanan* and *Reish Lakish* from their readings of another *mishnah*. The case: *achat safek cheilev v'noda* — a person ate a piece of meat that was possibly *cheilev* and became aware of the doubt. *Safek cheilev v'noda* — then he ate another piece of possibly *cheilev* and became aware again. The combination of these two doubtful events raises the question of how many *korbanot* are owed.

### Rabbi's Ruling

*Rabbi* — *Rebbi Yehudah HaNasi* — rules: just as, if both pieces had been definite *cheilev*, he would have brought a separate *chatat* for each piece, so too here, since there are two separate moments of becoming aware of a *safeik issur*, he brings a separate *asham talui* for each episode. Two doubtful sins, two separate *ashmot taluyim*.

### Rabbi Shimon's View

*Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda* and *Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon* in the name of *Rabbi Shimon* rule otherwise: *eino mevi ela asham talui echad* — he brings only a single *asham talui*. They cite the verse *al shigagato asher shagag*: *HaTorah ribeta shegagot harbei v'asham talui echad* — the Torah speaks of many errors, yet only one *asham talui*. The implication is that for all doubtful sins of the same category, however many separate episodes of doubtful eating occurred, only one *asham talui* is brought.

### Reish Lakish Analogy

*Reish Lakish* reads *Rebbi*'s ruling as establishing a principle: *yedi'ot sfekot mitchalakot l'chata'ot* — doubtful awarenesses divide for separate *chata'ot*. His reasoning by analogy is as follows: in the case of definite *cheilev*, if a person ate two pieces in a single state of unawareness and then became aware, he brings one *korban*; if awareness intervened between the two pieces, he brings two. So the awareness — even a doubtful one, *yedi'at safeik* — divides the acts into separate obligations. If this is true for definite *cheilev* where the intermediate awareness is of a *safeik*, then by analogy, where the pieces themselves are *safek cheilev*, a *safeik* awareness divides for separate *ashmot taluyim*. Moreover, *Reish Lakish* suggests a more radical reading of *Rebbi*: even in a case where the *cheilev* is definite but the intervening awareness was only a doubtful one, that doubtful awareness is sufficient to divide it into two separate *chata'ot*. Certainly then, for *safek cheilev* with *safeik* awareness, there are two *ashmot taluyim*.

### Yochanan Disagreement

*Rabbi Yochanan* reads *Rebbi* differently. He says: *keshem she'yidi'at vaday b'alma mitchaleket l'chata'ot* — just as definite awareness divides for separate sin-offerings, *kach yedi'at safeik mitchaleket l'ashamos* — so doubtful awareness divides for separate *ashamos*. This is an analogy, not an equation: definite awareness divides for *chata'ot*, while doubtful awareness divides — not for *chata'ot* — but for separate *ashmot taluyim*. For *Rabbi Yochanan*, a *safeik* awareness is never sufficient to divide for a *chatat*; it can only divide for an *asham talui*.

## Safek Yedia Analysis (II)

### Why Exception Applies

This creates a direct contradiction: *Rabbi Yochanan* just stated, in the context of the two-paths case, that *safeik yedi'ah* counts as full *yedi'ah* in *tumat mikdash* — and now he appears to hold that *safeik yedi'ah* does not suffice as a general matter to create the full obligations of a *chatat*. The *Gemara* resolves this by pointing to the exceptional nature of *tumat mikdash*: *hacha hu d'lo kesiv yediyah b'hedya* — here, in *tumat mikdash*, the Torah never explicitly stated that awareness is required. We know of the awareness requirement only from the word *v'ne'elam* — "it was hidden from him" — which implies, by inference, that at some point it was known. Since the requirement of awareness is itself only an inference from *v'ne'elam*, and not an explicit statement like *v'hu yada* or *o hoda eilav*, the standard for what counts as awareness is lower. A doubtful awareness — being aware that one might be *tamei* — suffices.

---

## Reconciling Contradiction

### Exception in Tumat

*Rav Ashi* makes explicit what the *Gemara* has been building toward: *tumat mikdash* is an exception. Elsewhere, a *safeik yedi'ah* does not function as full *yedi'ah*. Here it does.

### Lo Kesiv Yedia B'Hedya

The reason is anchored in the text: *lo kesiv yediyah b'hedya* — awareness is not explicitly written. *Mi'v'ne'elam hu d'ka'asi* — we derive the awareness requirement only from the word *v'ne'elam*, "it was hidden," which implies prior knowledge only by implication. Since the *yedi'ah* requirement is inferred rather than stated, and the inference is correspondingly weaker, the type of awareness required is accordingly more lenient. Doubtful awareness — being aware that one might be *tamei* — suffices to satisfy this inferred requirement.

### Other Korbanot Different

By contrast, in the standard *korban chatat* framework, the Torah writes *o hoda eilav* — "or it became known to him." This is an explicit statement of awareness, not a mere inference. Accordingly: *yediyah ma'alyasa be'inan* — a full, genuine awareness is required. A *safeik* awareness does not qualify.

### Inference Logic

The weaker textual basis for the awareness requirement in *tumat mikdash* yields a weaker standard for what that awareness must be. Since we are only inferring the requirement from *v'ne'elam*, we do not read into it the full rigor of explicit awareness language.

### Reish Lakish Alternative

*Reish Lakish* maintains his alternative reading: rather than explaining the case of the two paths as an exception where *safeik yedi'ah* functions as full *yedi'ah*, he attributes that ruling to *Rabbi Yishmael*, who requires no initial awareness at all.

### Emphasizing Yishmael

The *Gemara* asks: why not simply read the case according to *Rebbi*'s opinion, since *Rebbi* also holds that *safeik yedi'ah* can be significant? *Reish Lakish* answers: *ka mashma lan d'Rabbi Yishmael lo ba'ei yediyah batchilah* — he wanted to make the affirmative point that *Rabbi Yishmael* requires no initial awareness whatsoever. This is never stated explicitly by *Rabbi Yishmael*; it is inferred from the fact that *Rabbi Yishmael* uses both instances of the word *v'ne'elam* in the *pasuk* to establish *helam tumah* and *helam mikdash* as the two operative categories, and therefore has no extra *v'ne'elam* from which to derive a requirement of *yedi'ah batchilah*. The *Gemara* notes that one might have said: even without a *pasuk*, *Rabbi Yishmael* could hold the requirement as a matter of tradition — *mi'gemara it lei*. *Reish Lakish*'s attribution makes the point that *Rabbi Yishmael* truly has no such requirement, neither from a *pasuk* nor from tradition.

With that, the *Gemara* concludes the first *perek* — *hadran alach yediyot ha'tumah* — and we leave the world of *tumat mikdash* after an extensive twenty *dapim* that occupied nearly half the *masechta* before the main subject properly begins.

## Shebuot Introduction

### Main Topic Begins

We now turn at last to the third *perek*, which brings us to the central topic of the *masechta*: *shevu'ot* — oaths. It is worth pausing to note how striking it is that approximately forty percent of *Shevuot* concerned *tumat mikdash v'kodshav* rather than oaths themselves. One possible connection is that a *shevuah*, unlike a *neder*, involves directly invoking God's name — the classic *shevuah* is *beshem Hashem*. Even when the name is not explicitly stated, it may be inferred. In that sense, a *shevuah* brings a divine presence down as witness and guarantor of one's claim, much as the *Mikdash* was the one place where the divine presence was, so to speak, geographically present on earth. But that connection remains loose. Why *Rebbi*, who authored the *Mishnah*, chose to place the laws of *tumat mikdash* here rather than in *Seder Taharot* or *Kodshim* remains genuinely unclear.

### Shebuot Structure

The *mishnah* opens with the familiar structure: *shavuot shtayim shehen arba* — there are two categories of oaths that expand to four.

### Torah's Emphasis

The Torah addresses the case of *ki tishava levatei bisfasayim lehara o lehaitiv* — swearing an utterance with one's lips to do bad or to do good. This indicates a future-oriented oath, a commitment about what one will or will not do. The *lehara o lehaitiv* formulation, as will be elaborated, corresponds broadly to active versus passive commitments.

### Four Oath Types

The two cases mentioned in the Torah become four: *shevuah she-ochal v'she-lo ochal* — swearing that one will eat or that one will not eat (future-oriented oaths), and *she-achalti v'she-lo achalti* — claiming that one did eat or did not eat (past-oriented claims). When a person swears falsely or forgetfully about past claims, that too generates a *korban* obligation under the extended framework. These four are the foundational structure of the *perek*.

## Rabbi Akiva: Kol Shehu

### Oath Violation Standard

The *mishnah* presents the following case: *shevuah shelo ochal v'achal kol shehu chayav* — if one swears not to eat something and then eats even the smallest amount, one is *chayav* a *korban*. This is not the standard *kezayit* minimum that governs most food-related prohibitions. The position cited is that of *Rabbi Akiva*.

### Rabbi Akiva's Logic

*Rabbi Akiva*'s colleagues challenge him: *heichan matzinu b'ochel kol shehu shechayav* — we have never found that eating a minimal amount generates a *chiyuv*. What is the basis for this? *Rabbi Akiva* answers: *v'chi heichan matzinu b'medaber u'meivi korban* — when have we ever found that a speech act obligates a *korban*? An oath is unique in that one brings a *korban* for violating something one said. Since this is a fundamentally different category of obligation — one generated by speech rather than by a physical act — the measure of violation is determined differently.

### Rashi's Analysis

*Rashi* explains the underlying logic: *bishvil bitul diburo* — the *korban* is brought because of *bitul diburo*, the negation of one's word. The obligation is rooted not in the physical act of eating but in the invalidation of the commitment one made. Since the whole framework is about fidelity to speech, the relevant question is what one's speech meant.

### Intent vs. Fixed Amount

This distinction is fundamental. For a prohibition like *neveilah*, the Torah defines the relevant act in terms of a *kezayit* — a fixed physical quantity, because the prohibition is about the physical act of consumption. An oath, however, is created by the person through his own words, and what constitutes a violation of that oath depends on what he intended when he spoke. If a person swore not to eat something, and then ate even a tiny amount, he has violated the spirit and the letter of his commitment — no one would say his oath permitted eating a small quantity. The amount is therefore determined not by a fixed *Torah* standard but by the scope of the oath as he formulated it. *V'keivan d'mishum bitul diburo hu, af ze bitul diburo she-amar lo ochal v'daato le'esor atzmo b'chol shehu* — since it is about negating one's word, and the intent of "I will not eat" encompasses even the smallest amount, a *kol shehu* suffices to constitute the violation.

This principle — that the measure of an oath violation is tied to the intent and scope of the oath rather than to fixed physical quantities — will be explored further as the *Gemara* on this *mishnah* unfolds.
