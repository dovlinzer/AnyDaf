# Bava Batra 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Migo & Court Integrity

### Opening & Migo Review

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: מָה לוֹ לְשַׁקֵּר? אִי בָּעֵי, אָמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁטָרָא מְעַלְּיָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: Why would he lie** and state this claim? **If** he **wants** to lie, he can **say to him** that **it is a proper bill** of sale, and he would have been deemed credible and awarded the field.

Daf 33 continues a series of cases — quite plausibly real disputes rather than mere illustrations — all centered on *chazakah* and its attendant principles, though they frequently illuminate other areas of law, most notably *migo*. Before moving to the new material, it is worth revisiting a case from the previous daf, because it raises a point of enduring importance.

The *gemara* had presented a case involving a forged *shtar*. Everyone in the community believed the document to be genuine, and then the holder himself admitted it was forged. He nonetheless argued: believe me on some underlying claim, because *migo* — had I wished, I could have continued insisting the document was legitimate, and everyone would have accepted it. The *gemara* rejected this *migo* out of hand.

### Forged Doc & Migo

The question is why. If he had the better claim available, why does *migo* not apply? Tosafot articulates what most would intuit: you do not accept a *migo* whose alternative claim would only have existed because the claimant committed wrongdoing in the first place — here, the forgery itself. Even if the lie was universally believed, that is precisely the point. The legal system cannot allow a person to construct a *migo* on the scaffolding of his own fraud.

### Rashba & False Evidence

A responsum of the Rashba engages a scenario strikingly parallel to this case. A litigant approached the Rashba and essentially asked: my underlying claim is true, I lack sufficient legitimate evidence, but I have access to means — perhaps questionable evidence — by which I could prevail in *beis din*. May I use them, on the basis of *ovid inish dina l'nafshei* — the principle that a person may take the law into his own hands to recover what is genuinely his, analogous to breaking into someone's home to retrieve one's own property?

The questioner's very act of asking, of course, suggests he is telling the truth about his underlying claim. The Rashba nevertheless reacts with alarm, and with reference to precisely this *gemara*. The core principle, even if he does not state it in so many words, is that introducing false or fabricated evidence into the proceedings of a *beis din* fatally undermines the integrity of the court system. *Ovid inish dina l'nafshei* operates in the open: you are taking a visible, transparent action to recover what is yours. That is a very different matter from corrupting the internal machinery of a court whose purpose is to arrive at the truth.

### Migo in Modern Courts

This raises a related and genuinely open question about the contemporary *beis din*. In theory, *migo* remains operative in *halacha*, and the call to bring disputes before a *beis din shel Yisrael* rather than secular courts is grounded in the famous *derashah* on *eileh hamishpatim asher tasim lifneihem* — *lifneihem v'lo lifnei hedyotos*, *lifneihem v'lo lifnei batei din shel goyim*. In practice, however, how much of the adjudication in contemporary *batei din* is actually decided through *Choshen Mishpat*? The honest answer is: very little. The overwhelming majority of contemporary *beis din* proceedings function as a combination of *pshara* (equitable compromise), and *dina d'malchusa* — effectively an arbitration panel with occasional recourse to *halachic* sources. What it means, then, for a *beis din shel Yisrael* to fulfill its mandate when it does not adjudicate strictly according to *halacha* is a question worth sitting with.

## Rabba bar Sharshom

### Mortgage Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא בַּר שַׁרְשׁוּם נָפֵק עֲלֵיהּ קָלָא דְּקָא אָכֵיל אַרְעָא דְיַתְמֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֵימָא לִי אִיזִי, גּוּפָא דְעוֹבָדָא הֵיכִי הֲוָה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַרְעָא בְּמַשְׁכַּנְתָּא הֲוָה נָקֵיטְנָא מֵאֲבוּהוֹן דְּיַתְמֵי; וַהֲוָה לִי זוּזִי אַחֲרִינֵי גַּבֵּיהּ, וַאֲכַלְתַּהּ שְׁנֵי מַשְׁכַּנְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **A rumor emerged concerning Rava bar Sharshom that** he **was profiting** from **land belonging to orphans. Abaye said to him: Tell me, my friend,** concerning **the incident itself, how is it** that this rumor was generated? Rava bar Sharshom **said to him: I was holding** on to **the land as collateral from the father of the orphans, and I had** **other money with him,** i.e., he owed me money for a different reason, for which I had no collateral, **and I profited from** the land **for** the duration of the **years of** the **collateral.**

The *gemara* at the bottom of 32b opens the next case. There was a rumor — a *kol* — that Rabba bar Sharshom was unlawfully consuming the property of orphans, *navik alei kala d'ka ochil ar'a d'yasmei*. Abaye, who could not believe the rumor was simply true, pressed him: tell me honestly, *eimli izi gufa d'uvda heichi hava* — what was the real story?

Rabba bar Sharshom explained: I had this land as *mashkanta* — as collateral — from the orphans' father. I had lent him money and held his land as security.

### Mashkanta d'Sura

The arrangement in question is *mashkanta d'Sura*, familiar from our earlier encounter in the chapter of *Perek HaZahav* in Bava Metzia. The structure works as follows: I lend Reuven a sum of money and take possession of his fields. Over, say, ten years, I harvest the produce, and at the end of that term I return the land and the debt is considered discharged. Technically this arrangement carries *ribis* concerns, since ten years of harvest from that land may well exceed the principal of the loan, but it is structured and framed as a kind of sale — I am effectively purchasing the fruit of the land for the term — and on that basis it is permitted. So Rabba bar Sharshom had been legitimately on the land, harvesting its produce, for the duration of the *mashkanta* period.

### Strategy via Migo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמֵינָא: אִי מַהְדַּרְנָא לַהּ אַרְעָא לְיַתְמֵי, וְאָמֵינָא דְּאִית לִי זוּזִי אַחֲרִינֵי גַּבֵּי דַּאֲבוּכוֹן – אֲמוּר רַבָּנַן: הַבָּא לִיפָּרַע מִנִּכְסֵי יְתוֹמִים – לָא יִפָּרַע אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה. אֶלָּא אֶכְבְּשֵׁיהּ לִשְׁטַר מַשְׁכַּנְתָּא, וְאוֹכְלַהּ שִׁיעוּר זוּזֵי, דְּמִיגּוֹ דְּאִי בָּעֵינָא אָמֵינָא לְקוּחָה הִיא בְּיָדִי – מְהֵימַנָּא, כִּי אָמֵינָא דְּאִית לִי זוּזִי גַּבַּיְיכוּ – מְהֵימַנְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **I** then **said** to myself: **If I return the land to the orphans** now that the years of collateral have finished, **and I say that I have other money with your** late **father,** I will not be able to collect it, as **the Sages say** that **one who comes to collect** a debt **from** the **property of orphans can collect only by** means of **an oath,** and I do not wish to take an oath. **Rather** than do that, **I will suppress** the **document** detailing the terms **of the collateral, and profit from** the land up to the **measure** of the **money** that their father owed me. This is legitimate, **since if I** so **desire I can say: It is purchased,** and that is why it is **in my possession,** and **I** would have been **deemed credible,** as I profited from the land for the years necessary to establish the presumption of ownership, so **when I say that I have money with you, I** am also **deemed credible.**

By the time we reach the top of 33a, the *mashkanta* term had expired and the land should have reverted to the orphans. The reason Rabba bar Sharshom had remained was that the father had also owed him money on a separate loan, *v'hava lei zuzi achrinei gabei*, and for that second loan he held a *shtar*. The complication: the *halacha* establishes that one who comes to collect a debt from orphans, even with a valid *shtar*, must take a *shevua* — an oath — that the debt has not been repaid. This is a protection for orphans who may be unaware of their father's private arrangements; a *shtar* alone does not suffice.

People — and the *gemara* itself reflects this — are deeply reluctant to take a *shevua*, even one that is truthful, for fear of any inadvertent error that might render it a *shevuat sheker*. So Rabba bar Sharshom reasoned as follows: rather than go through the formal process of producing the *shtar* and taking a *shevua*, I will simply conceal the documentation proving this was a *mashkanta* and remain on the land a further period. Since I have now been on the land for sufficient years, I could claim — even if falsely — that I purchased the land outright and have established *chazakah*. I have a *migo*: since I could have claimed *lekucha hi beyadi* and been believed, I should therefore be believed when I say that the orphans' father owed me additional money, and I should be able to collect that debt through the fruit I am taking — without a *shevua*.

The underlying mechanics deserve attention. A *mashkanta* holder, a sharecropper, and a purchaser all physically occupy someone's land. What prevents a *mashkanta* holder from later claiming he purchased it? Documentation. The same logic that requires a purchaser to retain his *shtar* for the first three years — after which *chazakah* suffices — also means that the landowner who grants a *mashkanta* or employs a sharecropper must retain documentation of that arrangement, so that if the other party later claims *chazakah* and purchase, the arrangement can be demonstrated. Remarkably, in this case the person holding the land — Rabba bar Sharshom — was also the one holding the documentation of its *mashkanta* status. He could simply suppress it, claim *chazakah*, and be believed. That, he reasoned, gave him a *migo* to collect the other debt without a *shevua*.

### Abaye's Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״לְקוּחָה בְּיָדִי״ לָא מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ – דְּהָא אִיכָּא עֲלַהּ קָלָא דְּאַרְעָא דְיַתְמֵי הִיא. אֶלָּא זִיל אַהְדְּרַהּ נִיהֲלַיְיהוּ, וְכִי גָּדְלִי יַתְמֵי – אִשְׁתַּעִי דִּינָא בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to** Rava bar Sharshom: Your reasoning is incorrect. You would **not have been able to say: It is purchased,** and that is why it is **in my possession, as** there **is publicity concerning it that** it **is land of orphans.** Therefore, you are unable to collect your debt based on the fact that you could have made a more advantageous claim [*miggo*]. **Rather, return** the land **to** the orphans now, **and when the orphans become adults,** then **litigate with them,** as you have no other option.

Abaye rejected the strategy. *Lekucha hi beyadi lo masis amrat* — you could not have made that claim. Why? *Deika kala d'ara deyasmi hi* — there was a *kol*, a widespread public perception, that this land belonged to the orphans.

This generates an obvious difficulty. He was on the land for *shnei chazakah*, the requisite years. Why does a public perception that the land belongs to the orphans undermine a *chazakah* claim? If one has truly been on a piece of land for three years, the prior ownership is precisely what *chazakah* is designed to overcome. People may have assumed a field belonged to its previous owner before a sale — no one announces every private transaction. Yet *chazakah* after three years is believed even against the prior owner's claim. Why should the orphans' situation be any different?

The answer lies in understanding precisely what the *kol* communicated. Abaye's point is that the *migo* fails — not necessarily that the *chazakah* itself is legally invalid — and for that we need to understand what the *kol* actually established. Abaye's practical conclusion is clear: return the land to the orphans, *zil ahadar nihalyahu*, and when the orphans come of age, pursue the debt through proper channels — which means producing the *shtar* and taking the *shevua*. No shortcuts.

### Rashbam vs Tosafot

Two distinct explanations are offered for why the *kol* defeats the *migo*, and they reflect a genuine disagreement about the content of the *kol* itself.

The Rashbam reads *kala d'ara deyasmi hi* as meaning that everyone perceived and understood that Rabba bar Sharshom's presence on the land was as a *mashkon* holder — that he was there as collateral-taker, not as owner. The rumor that spread was not merely that the orphans once owned it, but specifically that this man was on it as a *mashkanta* holder whose term had expired and who was now wrongfully remaining. In that context, the claim of *lekucha hi beyadi* — that he purchased it — simply would not have been believed. The *chazakah* mechanism operates on the presumption that three years of open occupation, in the absence of protest, suggests purchase. But that presumption is defeated when there is a concurrent understanding of *why* the person is on the land — namely, as a *mashkanta* holder. A sharecropper cannot establish *chazakah* through his years of work on the land, and similarly a *mashkanta* holder whose status is publicly understood cannot leverage those years into a *chazakah* claim. So the *migo* fails because the alternative claim would not actually have prevailed.

Tosafot reads the case differently — and arguably in a way that makes Rabba bar Sharshom's original reasoning more understandable. According to Tosafot, the *kol* was not that it was known to be a *mashkanta* arrangement. The *kol* was simply that the land had originally belonged to the orphans' father and that they inherited it. That is a perfectly standard scenario for *chazakah* to operate: the prior owner is known, and one claims to have purchased it. Tosafot therefore concedes that had Rabba bar Sharshom actually made the claim *lekucha hi beyadi*, he might well have been believed in court — because witnesses to prior ownership do not contradict a claim of subsequent purchase. The court does not act on public misperception; it adjudicates on evidence.

But that is precisely where Tosafot locates the flaw in the *migo*. A *migo* requires not merely that the alternative claim was *technically* available, but that it was one the claimant would realistically have been prepared to make. *Migo d'ei ba'ei amar lekucha hi beyadi hava ne'eman* — true, he would have been believed — *mikal makom lo havei migo*, yet there is no *migo*, because *delo hayisa yachol leha'iz panecha velomar kein* — he would not have had the brazenness to say it. With the *kol* already established that the orphans inherited the land from their father, for Rabba bar Sharshom to stand up in court and say "actually, the father sold it to me" — when everyone in the community perceived the orphans as the inheritors — would have meant being looked at as someone brazenly trying to rob orphans. People would not have believed him, not because the legal claim was unavailable, but because the social reality of the *kol* would have made the claim appear unconscionably brazen. This is the principle of *migo leha'iz lo amrinan*: we do not apply *migo* when the alternative claim the person did not make would have been more brazen than the one being made.

It is worth noting the distinction between what a *beis din* evaluates and what the public perceives. A court is not and should not be swayed by popular opinion; if a person had genuine evidence, the court would rule on it regardless of communal perception. The *migo* analysis, however, is precisely about what claim the person would *realistically* have made — and there, the social context of the *kol* is directly relevant. Tosafot's point is not that the court would have dismissed the claim, but that Rabba bar Sharshom himself would not have had the nerve to advance it.

## Rav Idi Bar Avin

### Inheritance Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְרִיבֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין שְׁכֵיב, וּשְׁבַק דִּיקְלָא. רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין אָמַר: אֲנָא קָרִיבְנָא טְפֵי; וְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא אֲמַר: אֲנָא קָרִיבְנָא טְפֵי. לְסוֹף אוֹדִי לֵיהּ דְּאִיהוּ קָרִיב טְפֵי, אוֹקְמַהּ רַב חִסְדָּא בִּידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A relative of Rav Idi bar Avin died and left a date tree** as an inheritance. Another relative took possession of the tree, claiming to be a closer relative than Rav Idi bar Avin. **Rav Idi bar Avin said: I am closer** in relation to the deceased than he, **and that man said: I am closer** in relation to the deceased than Rav Idi bar Avin. **Ultimately,** the other man **admitted to** Rav Idi bar Avin **that,** in fact, Rav Idi was **closer** in relation to the deceased. **Rav Ḥisda established** the date tree **in the possession of** Rav Idi bar Avin.

The next case: a relative of Rav Idi bar Avin died and left behind what the *gemara* calls a palm tree — presumably an orchard of date palms. Rav Idi bar Avin claimed to be the closer relative and therefore the heir. Another man made the same claim. The factual situation is underspecified, but the Rashbam's reading suggests that neither claimant had documentary evidence establishing their precise position in the line of inheritance. Each was asserting kinship, and there may have been a general social recognition — attending family gatherings, appearing at lifecycle events — that each was *somehow* related to the deceased, but no one could establish who was closer.

What is also unstated but apparently occurred is that the unnamed disputant — more brazen or simply better positioned — squatted on the property and began collecting the fruit. This anticipates the principle of *kol de'alim gevar*, which the *gemara* will address more directly later, but the practical result was that for a period this other party occupied the land and consumed its produce, since no one could adjudicate the competing claims.

### Admission Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לַיהְדַּר לִי פֵּירֵי דַּאֲכַל מֵהָהוּא יוֹמָא עַד הַשְׁתָּא! אָמַר: זֶה הוּא שֶׁאוֹמְרִים עָלָיו אָדָם גָּדוֹל הוּא? אַמַּאן קָא סְמִיךְ מָר – אַהַאי; הָא קָאָמַר דַּאֲנָא מְקָרַבְנָא טְפֵי! אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא לָא סְבִירָא לְהוּ הָא דְּרַב חִסְדָּא, כֵּיוָן דְּאוֹדִי – אוֹדִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Idi bar Avin **said to** Rav Ḥisda: The value of the **produce that he consumed** unlawfully **from that day** when he took possession of the tree **until now should be returned to me.** Rav Ḥisda **said:** Is **this he about** whom people **say: He is a great man? On whom is the Master basing** his claim to receive the value of the produce? **On this** other relative. **But he was saying** until this point: **I am closer** in relation to the deceased than he. Therefore, you have ownership of the tree only from the time of his admission, and not from when he took possession of the tree. The Gemara comments: **Abaye and Rava do not hold** in accordance with **this** opinion **of Rav Ḥisda,** as they hold that **once** it is so **that** the other relative **admitted** that he is not a closer relative, **he admitted** that he never had any right to the produce of the tree. Therefore, by his own admission, he is liable to reimburse Rav Idi bar Avin.

Eventually, *lesof odei leh d'ihu kariv tefei* — in the end, the disputant admitted or conceded in favor of Rav Idi bar Avin. Rav Idi bar Avin responded: fine, now return the fruit — *leihadar li peirei d'achal mehahu yoma v'ad hashta* — everything you consumed from the moment you squatted until now. This prompted a sharp reaction: is this Rav Idi bar Avin, the one people regard as such a great *talmid chacham*?

Rav Chisda pressed him: *amai ka-samchat mar a'hai d'ka-amar* — on what basis are you relying to claim the fruit? The other party has admitted or conceded, yes — but *ha ka-amar d'ana mikaravna tefei*, he is still asserting that he himself is the closer relative. So the concession, Rav Chisda argues, does not amount to a full legal retraction of his underlying claim of closer kinship; he is merely stepping aside from the contest over the land, not affirming that Rav Idi bar Avin was always the rightful owner. And if he hasn't truly retracted his earlier claim, then his concession with respect to the land is more in the nature of a *matana* — a gift, a non-contested ceding — than an admission of prior wrongdoing. On that reading, Rav Idi bar Avin would have no claim to the fruit already consumed.

Abaye and Rava disagree with Rav Chisda: *keivan de'odi, odi* — once he admitted, he admitted. By conceding the land to Rav Idi bar Avin, he effectively retracted his prior claim to have been the closer relative, and that retraction entitles Rav Idi bar Avin to recover the fruit as well.

### Gift vs Retraction

Much of the complexity here turns on how to read the *gemara*'s phrasing. The Rashbam reads the text with the words *d'ihu karev tefei* — that the disputant explicitly said Rav Idi bar Avin was the closer relative — yet even so, Rav Chisda holds that we do not interpret this as a true retraction, only as a willingness to cede the property without contesting further. Even an explicit statement conceding the other's closer kinship, on this reading, may be interpreted as nothing more than a non-contesting gift. Abaye and Rava, naturally, read the explicit admission as a genuine retraction. The Rashbam's reading makes Abaye and Rava's position more comprehensible; Rav Chisda's position, under the Rashbam, requires some work to understand.

Tosafot resolves this by reading the *gemara* without those words — *d'ihu karev tefei* — in the decisive passage. On Tosafot's *girsa*, the disputant merely conceded the land without saying anything about who was the closer relative. That makes Rav Chisda's position far more understandable: of course a party who simply says "take it" isn't admitting he was wrong about his kinship — he's just done fighting. On the other hand, Tosafot's reading makes Abaye and Rava's position — that *keivan de'odi, odi* — harder to understand, since the concession seems less clearly a retraction. Each reading illuminates one position while making the other require more explanation, and the truth lies somewhere between the two.

### Witnesses & Migo

Tosafot also offers a third explanation that substantially reframes the entire case, though it departs so far from the *girsa* we have as to essentially constitute a rewriting. On this reading, what actually occurred was that Rav Idi bar Avin brought witnesses establishing that he was a relative of the deceased. Not that he was the *closer* relative — the witnesses could not establish the comparative degree of kinship — only that he was indeed in the line of inheritance. The disputant, by contrast, had no witnesses at all; he was simply making an unsubstantiated claim.

Given this, the court ruled in Rav Idi bar Avin's favor on the land — it was a case resembling *bari v'shema*: one party's claim is established by witnesses, the other's is entirely unverified. But when Rav Idi bar Avin then demanded the fruit, the analysis shifts. The witnesses did not testify about the fruit, and they did not establish that Rav Idi bar Avin was the *closer* relative — only that he was a relative. The disputant is still able to claim that he is actually closer in kinship. And here is where *migo* enters: the disputant is admitting that he consumed the fruit, but he is doing so in the context of asserting that he was the rightful heir. Since the witnesses did not contradict this particular claim — they established only that Rav Idi bar Avin is *a* relative, not that he is the *closer* one — there is no *migo b'makom eidim* problem. The *migo* is: he could have denied taking the fruit at all, yet he admits it and offers an explanation. That admission combined with his explanation is believed regarding the fruit, since the witnesses' testimony does not reach this question.

This is on Tosafot's reading what Rav Chisda means when he asks *amai ka-samchat mar* — on what basis do you demand the fruit? You're relying on his concession of the fruit, but his concession is made in the context of claiming closer kinship, which the witnesses don't contradict. And the suggestion of Abaye and Rava — *keivan de'odi, odi* — would then need to be understood as addressing a different dimension of the case, which the *gemara* leaves to be resolved elsewhere.

## Ancestral Plot Claim

### Two Claimants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה אוֹמֵר: ״שֶׁל אֲבוֹתַי״, וְזֶה אוֹמֵר: ״שֶׁל אֲבוֹתַי״; הַאי אַיְיתִי סָהֲדִי דַּאֲבָהָתֵיהּ הוּא, וְהַאי אַיְיתִי סָהֲדִי דַּאֲכַל שְׁנֵי חֲזָקָה
>
> **Translation:** § There was an incident where two people dispute the ownership of land. **This** one **says:** The land **belonged to my ancestors** and I inherited it from them, **and that** one **says:** The land **belonged to my ancestors** and I inherited it from them. **This** one **brings witnesses that** the land **belonged to his ancestors, and that** one **brings witnesses that** he worked and **profited** from the land for **the years** necessary **for** establishing the **presumption** of ownership.

The next case: *zeh moshel avotai v'zeh moshel avotai* — one party says it was my ancestral plot, the other makes the same claim. The language of *avotai* carries significance: not merely that a father once owned it, but that it is ancestral land, inherited and retained within the family, and therefore not subject to having been sold off. One claimant, Reuven, brings witnesses establishing that the land belonged to his father. The other, Shimon, brings witnesses to *shnei chazakah* — that he has been on the land for the years required to establish *chazakah*.

### Rav Chisda Migo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מָה לוֹ לְשַׁקֵּר? אִי בָּעֵי, אָמַר לֵיהּ: מִינָּךְ זְבֵינְתַּהּ וַאֲכַלְתִּיהָ שְׁנֵי חֲזָקָה. אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא לָא סְבִירָא לְהוּ הָא דְּרַב חִסְדָּא – ״מָה לִי לְשַׁקֵּר״ בִּמְקוֹם עֵדִים – לָא אָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said:** The one who is in possession of the land is deemed credible due to the legal principle that if one would have been deemed credible had he stated one claim but instead stated another claim that accomplishes the same result, he has credibility, because **why would he lie** and state this claim? **If** he **wants** to lie, he could have **said to him: I purchased it from you and I** worked and **profited from it** for **the years** necessary for establishing the **presumption** of ownership. **Abaye and Rava do not hold** in accordance with **this** opinion **of Rav Ḥisda,** because they hold that **we do not say** that the principle of: **Why would I lie,** applies in a case **where** there are **witnesses** contradicting the claim he is stating, and in this case, witnesses testify that it belonged to the ancestors of the other claimant.

Rav Chisda rules in favor of Shimon, invoking *migo*: *ma li leshaker* — why would he lie? Since Shimon has the *chazakah* years, he could have claimed *i ba'ei amar lei minach zivintei v'achalti shnei chazakah* — "I bought it from you, and I have been here the requisite years" — and been believed. If he could have made that claim and won, then the fact that he instead says it was his ancestral plot should also be believed, even against Reuven's witnesses. Reuven's witnesses establish that at one point the land was in his father's family; they do not preclude the possibility that Shimon subsequently purchased it from Reuven's father. Since the *chazakah* claim would have been believed and would not have been contradicted by those witnesses, the *migo* allows Shimon's alternative claim — ancestral ownership — to be believed as well.

### Abaye & Rava

Abaye and Rava reject Rav Chisda's ruling. The fundamental principle they invoke is *migo b'makom eidim lo amrinan* — we do not apply *migo* in the face of witnesses who contradict the claim. Reuven's witnesses testify to his father's ownership in a manner that directly contradicts Shimon's claim that the land was his ancestral plot. One might ask whether Rav Chisda denies this principle entirely or simply reads the witnesses' testimony more narrowly — as establishing only that the land was once Reuven's family's, not that it remained so and was never sold. On that narrower reading, the witnesses might not directly contradict Shimon's claim; they would simply establish a fact about prior ownership, which is fully compatible with a subsequent purchase by Shimon's family. But the broader and more standard reading — which Abaye and Rava adopt — is that testimony establishing Reuven's family's ancestral ownership is indeed direct testimony against Shimon's claim of ancestral ownership, and *migo* cannot overcome witnesses.

## One Witness Case

### Insufficient Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לְחַבְרֵיהּ: מַאי בָּעֵית בְּהַאי אַרְעָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִינָּךְ זְבַנִי, וַאֲכַלְתִּיהָ שְׁנֵי חֲזָקָה. אֲזַל אַיְיתִי סָהֲדִי דְּאַכְלַהּ תַּרְתֵּי שְׁנֵי. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הָדְרָא אַרְעָא, וְהָדְרִי פֵּירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain** person **who said to another: What do you want with this land** of mine? The possessor **said to him** in response: **I purchased it from you and I** worked and **profited from it** for **the years** necessary for establishing the **presumption** of ownership. He then **went and brought witnesses that** he had **profited** from the land for **two years,** but he was unable to bring witnesses to testify about a third year. **Rav Naḥman said: The land reverts** back to the prior owner, **and** payment for **the produce** consumed during those two years **reverts** to the prior owner. Since the possessor was unable to substantiate his claim to the land, the assumption is that he consumed the produce unlawfully.

The *gemara* now presents a case that begins simply. A man is on someone's land and is asked, *mai ba'it b'hai ar'a* — what are you doing here? He answers: *minach zavni v'achalti shnei chazakah* — I bought it from you and I have been here for the years of *chazakah*. He goes to find witnesses to support his claim, but can only produce witnesses to two of the three required years. Rav Nachman rules: *hadar ar'a v'hadar peirei* — the land reverts to the original owner, and so does all the fruit the claimant consumed. He has not established his case, and both the land and its produce must be returned.

### Sharecropper Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זְבִיד: אִם טָעַן וְאָמַר ״לְפֵירוֹת יָרַדְתִּי״ – נֶאֱמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid said: If** initially, when questioned by the other, the one occupying the land **claimed and said: I entered** the land **to** consume its **produce** that I had purchased, he is **deemed credible.**

Rav Zvid then introduces an important qualification. The Rashbam supplies a critical word that the *gemara* leaves implicit: *if* the claimant had originally framed his claim differently — if he had said not that he purchased the land but that he came down onto it as a sharecropper or had purchased rights to the fruit — *ne'eman*, he would be believed regarding the fruit. Even though the land reverts, he would not have to return the fruit he consumed.

### Brazenness Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאו מִי אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הַאי מַאן דְּנָקֵיט מַגָּלָא וְתוּבַלְיָא, וְאָמַר: אֵיזִיל אֶיגְדְּרֵיהּ לְדִיקְלָא דִפְלָנְיָא, דְּזַבְּנֵיהּ נִיהֲלִי – מְהֵימַן? אַלְמָא לָא חֲצִיף אִינִישׁ דְּגָזַר דִּיקְלָא דְּלָאו דִּילֵיהּ; הָכָא נָמֵי, לָא חֲצִיף אִינִישׁ לְמֵיכַל פֵּירֵי דְּלָאו דִּילֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** After all, **didn't Rav Yehuda say: This one who is holding a sickle and rope [*vetovelaya*] and says: I will go cull [*igderei*]** the dates from **the date tree of so-and-so who sold it to me, is deemed credible** that he has the right to do so? **Apparently, a person is not** so **brazen that** he would **cull** the dates of **a date tree that is not his. Here too,** in the case discussed by Rav Zevid, **a person** is **not** so **brazen** as **to consume produce that is not his.**

Why would he be believed about the fruit in that scenario? The *gemara* cites Rav Yehuda's teaching: a person who is walking around with a sickle and a basket and announces publicly that he is going to harvest the palm tree of so-and-so because so-and-so sold him the fruit — *meheiman* — he is believed. The *beis din* does not intervene and treat him as a thief. The underlying principle is stated explicitly: *lo chatzif inish lemeichal piri delav dilei* — a person does not have the brazenness to go ahead and consume, openly, produce that is not his.

This needs careful delimitation. The Rashbam explains that the *gemara* is not saying the *beis din* will preemptively act on this man's behalf against an absent owner. Rather, the *beis din* simply does not step in on the owner's behalf in the absence of the owner's own protest. If the owner were to come forward and say "that man is a *gazlan*," the *beis din* would of course take up the matter. The point is only that without the owner's active claim, we accept at face value that the person openly consuming the fruit has a legitimate basis to do so — because people generally don't act that brazenly with someone else's property in public.

Tosafot questions whether the principle is even limited to public conduct, arguing it would apply even in private. But Tosafot raises a more pointed question: what is the *chidush* here at all? Fruit is *metaltelin* — movable property. If a person has physical possession of movable property, the basic principle of *chazakah mah shetachat yad adam harei hu shelo* — whatever is under a person's control is presumptively his — should already suffice. Why do we need any elaborate reasoning about brazenness and public conduct?

### Land vs Fruit Shtar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אַרְעָא נָמֵי! אַרְעָא, אָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: אַחְוִי שְׁטָרָךְ. אִי הָכִי, פֵּירֵי נָמֵי! שְׁטָרָא לְפֵירֵי לָא עָבְדִי אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** that the assumption is that he would not lie, let one be deemed credible with regard to **the land as well.** The Gemara answers: In terms of **the land, we say to him: Show your bill** of sale if you indeed purchased it. The Gemara challenges: **If that is so,** then in terms of the **produce as well,** let him be deemed credible only if he can produce documentation of his claim. The Gemara explains: It is **not common** for **people** to write **documents to** establish the right to consume **produce** alone, and one can therefore claim to have consumed the produce based on an oral agreement.

The *gemara* addresses the distinction between land and fruit directly. *Arra amina lei achvei shtarcha* — for land, we tell the claimant: show us your *shtar*. The absence of a purchase document for land is telling, because transactions in land are invariably documented. *Ei hachi piri nami* — so why not require a *shtar* for fruit as well? The answer: *shtra l'piri lo avdi inshi* — people do not customarily write documents for fruit. A sharecropping arrangement, an agreement to purchase fruit rights for a season, a simple handshake deal about a harvest — these are not the kinds of transactions that generate formal *shtarot*. Therefore, the absence of a *shtar* does not undermine the claim about fruit the way it would undermine a claim about land.

Tosafot remains unsatisfied, returning to the question of *chazakah* on *metaltelin*. He suggests the case must be read as one where the fruit is not in the claimant's direct physical possession — perhaps it has been harvested and is being held by a third party, or has not yet been formally transferred. Only in that configuration is there a genuine *chidush*: the claimant is not in direct possession and therefore cannot rely on *chazakah mah shetachat yado*, so the believability of his claim must rest on the brazenness principle and the absence of a *shtar* requirement for fruit. Once the fruit has been fully consumed or is under his direct control, the *metaltelin chazakah* would independently suffice.

### Witness Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לְחַבְרֵיהּ: מַאי בָּעֵית בְּהַאי אַרְעָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִינָּךְ זְבַנִית, וַאֲכַלְתִּיהָ שְׁנֵי חֲזָקָה. אַיְיתִי חַד סָהֲדָא דְּאַכְלַהּ תְּלָת שְׁנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain** person **who said to another: What do you want with this land** of mine? The possessor **said to him** in response: **I purchased it from you and I** worked and **profited from it** for **the years** necessary for establishing the **presumption** of ownership. He then **brought one witness** who testified **that** he **profited** from the land for the necessary **three years.**

The *gemara* then extends the case in a more interesting direction. The claimant goes looking for witnesses and finds one witness who testifies to *three* years of *chazakah* — the full requisite period, not merely two. Yet he still cannot win the case, because one witness is not two. Now the question becomes pointed: this witness was brought to support the claimant. But given that the claimant has admitted to being on the land and consuming its fruit, and given that he cannot win the *chazakah* claim with only one witness, does this one witness now *turn against* the claimant — establishing that he consumed three years of fruit without right, such that he must return it all? And if he cannot contradict the witness's testimony, must he pay?

### Adverse vs Supportive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְבוּר רַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי לְמֵימַר: הַיְינוּ נְסָכָא דְּרַבִּי אַבָּא – דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דַּחֲטַף נְסָכָא מֵחַבְרֵיהּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי, הֲוָה יָתֵיב רַבִּי אַבָּא קַמֵּיהּ. אַיְיתִי חַד סָהֲדָא דְּמִיחְטָף חֲטַפָא מִינֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, חֲטַפִי – וְדִידִי חֲטַפִי. אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי:
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis who were studying **before Abaye maintained** that it made sense **to say** that the principle in this case **is** the same as that in the case of the **piece of cast metal [*naskha*]** adjudicated **by Rabbi Abba.** The Gemara now presents that case: **As** there was **a certain man who snatched a piece of cast metal from another.** The one from whom it was taken **came before Rabbi Ami** while **Rabbi Abba was sitting before him,** and he **brought one witness** who testified **that** it was, in fact, **snatched from him.** The one who snatched it **said to him: Yes,** it is true that **I snatched** it, **but I** merely **snatched** that which was **mine. Rabbi Ami said:**

The scholars sitting before Abaye initially suggest that this case resembles the famous *naska* case of Rabbi Abba, which the *gemara* proceeds to narrate. However, Abaye ultimately distinguishes the two, and the distinction is illuminating. The *naska* case must first be understood on its own terms.

## Silver Ingot Case

### Item Taking Scenario

The case has been presented above in the blockquote for "Adverse vs Supportive."

### Rabbi Ami's Dilemma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי נִידַיְּינוּהּ דַּיָּינֵי לְהַאי דִּינָא? לִישַׁלֵּם? לֵיכָּא תְּרֵי סָהֲדִי! לִיפְטְרֵיהּ? אִיכָּא חַד סָהֲדָא! לִישְׁתְּבַע, הָא אָמַר מִיחְטָף חַטְפַהּ, וְכֵיוָן דְּאָמַר דְּחַטְפַהּ – הָוֵה לֵיהּ כְּגַזְלָן!
>
> **Translation:** **How should judges judge for this judgment?** There are reasons not to implement all potential rulings. If they were to order the one who snatched the metal **to pay** for it, that would not be the correct ruling, because **there are not two witnesses** who saw him snatch it, and the court does not force payment based on the testimony of one witness. If they were **to** accept his claim and **exempt him** entirely, that would not be the correct ruling, because **there is one witness** who testified against him. If they were to order him **to take an oath,** which is the usual response to counter the testimony of one witness, **didn't he say** that he did in fact **snatch it, and since he said that he snatched it** and there is no proof that it is his, **he is like a robber,** and the court does not allow a robber to take an oath.

Rabbi Ami articulates the dilemma with precision: *heichi nadaynu dayanei lehai dina* — how should the judges rule on this case? *Lishalem* — should Reuven simply pay? There are not two witnesses to the taking; you cannot impose payment on a defendant based on one witness alone. *Liftreih* — should Reuven simply be exempted? You cannot ignore the testimony of one witness. Normally, when one witness makes a claim against a defendant, the defendant takes a *shevua* contradicting that claim. But here, the defendant *concedes* the factual claim of the witness — that he took the item from Shimon's possession. The only *shevua* available to him would be one that directly contradicts the witness's testimony, and he cannot take that oath because he agrees with what the witness saw.

### Gazlan Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רַבִּי אַבָּא: הָוֵי מְחוּיָּב שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִישָּׁבַע, וְכׇל הַמְחוּיָּב שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִישָּׁבַע – מְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba said to them: He** is one who **is liable** to take **an oath who is unable to take an oath, and anyone who is liable** to take **an oath who is unable to take an oath** is liable to **pay.**

*Vekeivan de'amar chatafi havei leih kegazlan* — since he says he grabbed it, he is in the position of a *gazlan*, and this is not a technical designation so much as a functional one: he is not positioned to take the required *shevua* because his own admission forecloses it. The rule that applies here is *mechuyav shevua she'eino yachol lishava meshalem* — one who is obligated to take a *shevua* and cannot do so must pay. Reuven is obligated to take a *shevua* against the single witness, and since he cannot take it — his own admission acknowledging the taking is what prevents him — he must pay.

### Adverse vs Supportive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ אַבָּיֵי: מִי דָּמֵי?! הָתָם סָהֲדָא לְאוֹרוֹעֵי קָאָתֵי – כִּי אָתֵי אַחֲרִינָא בַּהֲדֵיהּ, מַפְּקִינַן לַהּ מִינֵּיהּ; הָכָא לְסַיּוֹעֵי קָא אָתֵי – כִּי אֲתָא אַחֲרִינָא, מוֹקְמִינַן לַהּ בִּידֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** these Rabbis: **Are** these two cases **comparable? There,** in Rabbi Abba's case, **the witness is coming to undermine** the position of the one who snatched the metal. This can be seen from the fact that **when** it would be the case that **another** witness **comes** to court and testifies **with** the first witness, **we** would **take away** the piece of metal **from** the one who snatched it. By contrast, **here,** in the case of the individual who brought one witness to attest to his profiting from the land, the witness **is coming to support** the possessor. This can be seen from the fact that **when another** witness would **come** to court and testify with the first witness, **we** would **establish** the land **in his possession.** Therefore, the testimony of the one witness does not render the one who profited from the land liable to take an oath.

Now Abaye explains why the *naska* case does *not* apply to the case of the single witness to three years of *chazakah*. The key distinction is the direction of the witness's testimony — not who brought the witness to court, but what the consequence of that testimony would be if there were *two* such witnesses.

*Hatam sahada le'aru'ei ka'ati* — in the *naska* case, the witness comes to damage the defendant's position. Were there two witnesses to Reuven taking the ingot from Shimon's backpack, Reuven would be a *gazlan* and would have to pay, regardless of his explanation. His claim that it was his own property would not be heard against two witnesses to the taking. Two witnesses would be fully adverse to him.

*Hacha lesiyuyei ka'asi* — here, in the *chazakah* case, the witness comes to support the claimant. Were there two witnesses to three years of occupation, the claimant would *win* the case. *Ki asiei achrini mokminan lei behadei* — if a second witness were to come, the claimant's position would be vindicated. A witness whose doubled testimony would result in the claimant's *victory* cannot be treated as an adverse witness against whom the claimant must take a *shevua*. It does not matter that this particular witness was brought by the claimant expecting support and ended up being insufficient; what matters is the nature of the testimony itself. The testimony is supportive in character — there simply is not enough of it. Insufficient supportive testimony does not transform into adverse testimony and does not trigger the *mechuyav shevua she'eino yachol lishava* mechanism.

This is a compelling and clean answer. The *naska* and *chazakah* cases are superficially similar — one witness, defendant cannot contradict — but are structurally opposite: in one, two witnesses would destroy the defendant; in the other, two witnesses would vindicate him. That structural difference determines whether the single witness is adversarial or merely insufficient, and that in turn determines whether the *shevua* mechanism is triggered.
