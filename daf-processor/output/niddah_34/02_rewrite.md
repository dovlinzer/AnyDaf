# Niddah 34 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Polelet Shichvat Zera

### Shiva Nekiyim Standard

The daf begins with a brief reprise of the discussion that closed the previous session — specifically, the question of *polelet shichvat zera*: can the days during which semen is expelled from a woman's body after intercourse be counted toward the Torah-mandated days relevant to the status of a *zavah*? While the Gemara will revisit this in fuller detail, the practical upshot is immediately noted: this discussion forms the halachic basis for the contemporary practice — at least among Ashkenazim — of waiting a minimum of five days before beginning the count of *shiva nekiyim*, even in the absence of intercourse. This five-day standard, which developed in the post-Geonic Ashkenazic tradition, flows directly from the Gemara's analysis of how long one must wait before assuming that *polelet shichvat zera* is no longer a concern. It is a profoundly practical matter that will be revisited as the sugya develops.

### Kutim Status

The Gemara then continues from ten lines into amud 2, addressing the status of Kutim with respect to *hilchot niddah*. The Kutim observe *hilchot niddah*, but their practice diverges from rabbinic law in a way that produces a leniency: because they begin counting their days of impurity differently — treating certain non-*tamei* colored blood as the start of the count — they may immerse in the *mikvah* prematurely. Consequently, their women are treated as *safek niddot* and their husbands as *safek ba'alei niddot*. Yet because this status rests on doubt, one who enters the *Beit Hamikdash* after contact with them is not *chayav* for entering in a state of *tumah*, and *terumah* that comes into contact with them is not burned — both because the *tumah* is only a *safek*.

## Rav Pappa & Rav Shmuel

### Rav Pappa Arrives

The Gemara introduces a narrative: Rav Pappa came to a place called Tava. Upon arriving, he inquired whether there was a *talmid chacham* in town whom he might greet. An elderly woman informed him that there was indeed a *tzurba me-rabbanan* there — one Rav Shmuel — who was a master of *Mishnayot* and *Baraitot*. This designation is significant: it indicates a scholar who has thoroughly mastered the Tannaitic material but is not necessarily associated with the higher-order work of *amora'im* — the give-and-take of *kashya* and *teretz*, dialectical analysis, and independent reasoning.

There is a subtle irony embedded in what follows. When the woman blesses Rav Pappa with the wish that he should become as great as Rav Shmuel, Rav Pappa's immediate inference is that since she is comparing him to a man who is, by the description given, clearly not his equal in Torah scholarship, the praise must be referring to Rav Shmuel's piety — *shema mina yere shamayim hu*, he concludes: he must be God-fearing. This tells us something about Rav Pappa's assumptions. Implicitly, he is reasoning: the compliment cannot be about his learning, since that obviously cannot exceed mine; therefore it must be about his character. There is, perhaps, a trace of *ga'avah* in this reading — or at least a certain confidence about the hierarchy of scholarly achievement.

### Rav Shmuel's Question

When Rav Pappa arrives, Rav Shmuel welcomes him warmly, serves him a substantial meal, and then proceeds to present him with a contradiction between Mishnayot. The hosting and the challenge are intertwined in the narrative — he served him an ox (*rama lei tora*) and then threw him *mishnayot* (*rama lei matnita ahadadei*), a kind of parallel generosity.

The contradiction Rav Shmuel raises is the following. Our Mishnah states that if someone touches the seat of a Kuti who is a *safek boel niddah* and then enters the *Beit Hamikdash*, he is not *chayav*, and *terumah* that touches that seat is not burned — because the *tumah* is only a *safek*. The operative principle appears to be: in cases of doubt, we do not burn *terumah*. Yet another Mishnah teaches that for six specific doubts, we do burn *terumah* — and among those six is the doubtful status of the garments of an *am ha'aretz* (*safek bigdei am ha'aretz*). The question is not merely a formal tension between two rules regarding doubt. Rashi and Tosafot both observe that the real issue is this: if we are treating a Kuti as insufficiently careful in observance, then the Kuti is, by definition, also in the category of *am ha'aretz*. So why are the garments of a Kuti not subject to the same standard as those of an *am ha'aretz*, for which we burn *terumah*?

Rav Pappa's response is that the Mishnah is speaking of a *Kuti chaver* — a Kuti who is scrupulous in matters of *tumah vetaharah* and thus does not fall into the *am ha'aretz* category. Rav Shmuel immediately pushes back with a sharp counter: *Kuti chaver boel niddah mashkachat lei?* — if the Kuti is considered a *chaver*, meticulous in his observance, how can one simultaneously suspect him of having intercourse with his wife while she is a *niddah*? The categories seem mutually exclusive.

There is, in fact, an obvious answer — one that Tosafot articulates explicitly: it is entirely possible that the Kuti is scrupulous about every law he knows, and even regarding *niddah* he believes himself to be acting correctly, but his interpretation of the law leads him to count from a different starting point. He is *savar la'asot kedat* — he thinks he is fulfilling the law. His violation, if it is one, stems not from negligence but from a sincere but mistaken legal position. This is what Tosafot writes: he is a *chaver* regarding everything that defines the distinction between a *chaver* and an *am ha'aretz*, and even in this particular matter he believes he is acting in accordance with *halacha*. Nonetheless, Rav Shmuel did not accept this answer.

### Departure & Rav Shimi

Rav Pappa left — *shabkieh*. Rashi explains: Rav Pappa left his host's house (*be'ushpizeih*) because he felt embarrassed (*mishum dechasfiah*). Not that Rav Shmuel intentionally embarrassed him — they were engaged in Torah learning — but the effect was that this man, known only as a master of *mishnayot* and *baraitot*, not as someone capable of analytical challenge, had in fact bested Rav Pappa in the exchange. This brings the story's subtext into focus. Rav Pappa had assumed that the woman's blessing could only be referring to Rav Shmuel's piety, not his learning. But it turned out that Rav Shmuel's question was genuinely sharp — sharp enough to send Rav Pappa away without a satisfactory answer. The assumption about the hierarchy, it seems, needed revision.

Rav Pappa then went to Rav Shimi bar Ashi.

### Kuti After Tevilah

Rav Shimi bar Ashi explains the Mishnah by constructing a specific and narrow scenario. He says: the Mishnah is discussing a Kuti who immersed in a *mikvah* (*bekuti shetaval*) and then stepped on the garments of a *chaver*. Here is the logic.

The ordinary concern about an *am ha'aretz* — which leads to his garments being treated as *vadai tamei* with *terumah* burned because of them — stems from his general carelessness with *tumah vetaharah*, not specifically from *niddah* laws. That kind of *tumah* is typically a one-day impurity; immersion in a *mikvah* removes it. So if the Kuti has gone to the *mikvah*, that ordinary *am ha'aretz* concern is resolved. He is no longer in the *am ha'aretz* category in the sense that would render his garments definitively *tamei*.

What remains is only the concern particular to *Kutim*: that his wife may have been a *niddah* and he may have had relations with her before her *mikvah* immersion was halakhically valid, rendering him a *safek boel niddah*. The Kuti, now freshly immersed and naked, then steps on the garments of a *chaver*. Those garments have not been rendered *tamei* as garments of an *am ha'aretz* — he immersed and was not wearing his own clothes. The only possible *tumah* affecting those garments is from his *Kuti* status. And that, Rav Shimi explains, is a *safek sefeika* — a double doubt: perhaps he did not have intercourse with his wife recently; and even if he did, perhaps she had counted correctly after all and the immersion was valid. Because it is a *safek sefeika*, we do not burn *terumah*. That is the case of the Mishnah.

The Gemara then challenges this: surely he stepped on the *chaver*'s garments wearing his sandals, and those sandals are his own clothing — *bigdei am ha'aretz* — which are treated as *vadai tamei*! The answer given is that he stepped on them while naked (*bekusi arum*) — he had just emerged from the *mikvah* and had not yet dressed. The case therefore involves no garments of his own, and the only *tumah* in play is the tenuous Kuti-derived doubt.

The Gemara thus provides two interlocking resolutions to Rav Shmuel's question. First, why isn't the Kuti's garment treated like *bigdei am ha'aretz*? Because he just immersed, removing that *tumah*. Second, even granting the concern of *boel niddah*, why is it treated as a *safek* insufficient to burn *terumah*? Because it is a *safek sefeika* — the doubt is genuinely weak and layered.

It is also worth noting, as the Gemara itself suggests at a later point, that the cleaner answer — attributed to Tosafot and implicit in what is called *Rava's first answer* — is that a Kuti who is a *chaver* in all other respects may simply have a different legal position regarding *niddah*, and the two designations are not contradictory. He is scrupulous; he merely holds differently on this particular question.

## Benot Tzaddukim

### Mishna Rules

The Gemara proceeds to the laws governing the daughters of Tzaddukim. The Mishnah states: *benot Tzaddukim bizman she-nahagu lalechet bedarchei avoteihem* — the daughters of Tzaddukim, when they follow the practices of their fathers, are treated like *Kutiyot*. Noteworthy here is that the Mishnah says *avoteihem* — their fathers — even though the discussion concerns *hilchot niddah*, which one might expect to be governed by the practices of the mothers. This reflects the assumption that the religious affiliation of the household is determined by the male line.

If they follow their fathers' Tzadduki practices, they are treated like *Kutiyot* — they may be operating under a different legal standard for when to begin counting *tamei* days, and their *tevilah* may therefore occur too early. If, however, they have separated and follow the practice of Israel (*pirshu lalechet bedarchei Yisrael*), they are treated as Israelite women and are presumed to be observing *halacha* correctly.

Rabbi Yosei holds a more lenient default: *le'olam hen k'Yisraelit* — they are always presumed to be like Israelite women, *ad she'yafrishu lalechet bedarchei avotam* — until they explicitly and actively separate themselves to follow their fathers' ways. His presumption runs in the other direction from the *Tanna Kamma*.

### Default Status

The Gemara asks what the *Tanna Kamma* holds as the default assumption when neither practice can be established. It attempts to infer this from the Mishnah's opening clause (*risha*): if following the fathers' way = *Kutiyot*, then perhaps the unstated default is like Israelite women? But then the closing clause (*seifa*) implies the reverse: if explicitly following Israel = Israelite, then the unstated default might be *Kutiyot*. The Gemara concludes that from the Mishnah itself one cannot resolve the question — *ela me'ha lo tishma*.

The inference is ultimately drawn from Rabbi Yosei's position: since Rabbi Yosei explicitly says the default is Israelite women, *mikhlal de-Tanna Kamma savar stama k'kutyot* — by contrast, the *Tanna Kamma* must hold that the unstated default is to treat them like *Kutiyot*, i.e., as *safek tamei*. This is the position *shema mina*.

### Kohen Gadol Story

To understand why Rabbi Yosei would adopt the opposite presumption, the Gemara introduces a *ma'aseh*: a certain Tzadduki was speaking with the Kohen Gadol in the marketplace — notably, a Kohen Gadol who was a *Perushi*, not a Tzadduki, which was not always the case. In the course of conversation, a stream of spittle (*tzinora*) flew from the Tzadduki's mouth and landed on the Kohen Gadol's garments. The Kohen Gadol's face turned pale (the *Rashash* notes a wordplay: the word *horiku*, from *yarok*, echoes the earlier concern about green-colored blood among the Kutim). He was devastated — having treated the Tzadduki as a *safek boel niddah* like a Kuti, the Tzadduki's spittle was *tamei* and had now *tamei'd* his garments.

He went and reported this incident to his wife. Rashi suggests it was the wife of the Tzadduki, but the contextual reading — and the Maharsha — indicates it was the Kohen Gadol's own wife, since the story would not make sense if he were referring to himself in the third person. She said to him: even though they are the wives of Tzaddukim, *mityarot hen min ha-Perushim* — they are reverent toward the Perushim, and they show their blood to the sages for evaluation.

### Women's Reverence

This is the basis for Rabbi Yosei's position: *baki'in anu bahen yoter min hakol* — we know them better than anyone else, and they are scrupulous about showing their blood to the *chachamim*. He adds one exception: there was one woman in his neighborhood who did not show her blood to the sages — *umeta*, and she died. The implicit point being that the exception proved itself.

The story carries a remarkable subtext. It illustrates that in *hilchot niddah*, it is ultimately the women who determine the communal status — not the men. Whatever the Tzadduki husbands may hold regarding *pesak halacha*, if the women themselves are showing their blood to the *Perushim* sages and following their rulings, then the women's practice governs. The male religious authority of the Tzadduki household, for all its social prominence, does not reach into this domain. This is, as noted, a striking inversion of the usual assumption that the male line defines practice. Furthermore, the word *mityarot* — they are *fearful of* or *reverent toward* — carries an irony. The Tzaddukim were in many respects stricter than the Perushim. But as we saw with the Kutim, strictness can produce leniency: beginning the count earlier leads to an earlier and potentially invalid *tevilah*. The women, in their reverence for the Perushim, actually end up following the standard that produces the correct result.

### Rava's Answer

The Gemara then raises a question parallel to the one asked about the Kuti: *veteipuk lei mishum tzinora d'am ha'aretz* — forget the Tzadduki issue entirely; the spittle of an *am ha'aretz* is treated like that of a *zav*, so the spittle should be *tamei* regardless of the *niddah* consideration!

Abaye answers: he was a *tzadduki chaver* — a Tzadduki who was nonetheless scrupulous in *hilchot tumah vetaharah*, and thus not in the *am ha'aretz* category. Rava rejects this answer with the same challenge that Rav Shmuel posed to Rav Pappa: *tzadduki chaver boel niddah mashkachat lei?* — if he is that scrupulous, how can he be simultaneously suspected of being a *boel niddah*? Rava refuses to accept the *chaver* answer, at least in the form Abaye framed it. (The obvious Tosafot-style rejoinder — that he may be scrupulous yet hold a different legal position — seems to be available here as well, but Rava sets it aside.)

Rava therefore offers a different resolution: *regel havei* — it was one of the three pilgrimage festivals, the *regalim*. On the *regalim*, the rabbis treated the *tumah* of an *am ha'aretz* as if it were *taharah*. The reason: the Torah says *vayei'asef kol ish Yisrael el ha'ir ke'ish echad chaverim* — when all of Israel gathers together as one, the verse itself calls them all *chaverim*. On the *regalim*, when *klal Yisrael* is assembled, all social and halachic distinctions that normally separate *chaverim* from *amei ha'aretz* are suspended in favor of communal unity. Therefore on a *regel*, a Tzadduki's spittle would not carry the *am ha'aretz* *tumah* — only the Tzadduki-specific *safek* would remain, and that *safek* is insufficient to burn *terumah*.

There is no small irony in calling a Tzadduki an *am ha'aretz*. The Tzaddukim were the upper class; they were anything but the hoi polloi. Yet from the perspective of *Perushi* halacha, if a person is not observing the precise laws of *tumah vetaharah* as the *chachamim* define them, he receives the legal category of *am ha'aretz*, whatever his social station.

## Dam Ovedet Kochavim

### Blood Status Intro

The Gemara now turns to a new and considerably more intricate Mishnah, treating the question of blood status. By way of introduction: the blood of a *niddah* is an *av ha-tumah* — a primary source of *tumah* — because it is blood that creates her status as a *niddah*. But there is a separate principle: a *niddah*, a *zav*, and a *zavah* also render certain of their *mayanot* — certain bodily fluids such as spit and urine — *tamei* as an *av ha-tumah*. The question that the Mishnah addresses is: what is the status of blood that comes from such a person but is not itself *niddah* blood — either because the person is not Jewish, or because the blood falls within a period in which it would not create *niddah* status? Does such blood share the *tumah* of spit and urine, or does it have no *tumah* status at all?

### Mishna Rules

The Mishnah addresses three cases. First: *dam ovedet kochavim* — the blood of a non-Jewish woman. A non-Jewish woman does not have the halachic status of a *niddah* from the Torah; her blood does not make her a *niddah* in the biblical sense. What then is the status of that blood?

Second: *dam tohar shel metzora'at* — the blood of a woman who is both a *metzora'at* and a *yoledes* (having recently given birth). She is in the *taharah* period following childbirth, during which any uterine blood is not *niddah* blood. However, she remains a *metzora'at*, and the spittle of a *metzora* is *tamei*. The question is whether this blood during the *taharah* period — not *niddah* blood, but still emanating from a *tamei* person — carries the *tumah* of spit.

Third: the blood of a *yoledes* who has not yet immersed after her *taharah* period, i.e., she is past the first or second week after childbirth but has not yet gone to the *mikvah*.

Beit Shammai rules *tahor* for the first two cases — blood that is not *niddah* blood carries no *tumah*, even as a *mayane*-status fluid. Beit Hillel disagrees and rules that it is *tamei ke-roko ukhe-mei raglav* — like spit and urine — and thus *tamei* when wet.

For the third case (the *yoledes* who has not immersed), Beit Shammai says we treat the blood as if it were *spit* — rabbinically *tamei* when wet — because one might confuse day 15 with day 14, and day 14 blood would be *niddah* blood. Beit Hillel goes further: it is *metamei lach ve-yavesh* — it is *tamei* even when dry — treating it as actual *niddah* blood. Beit Hillel's reasoning, as will be explained, is that until she immerses in the *mikvah*, her blood retains its *niddah* status; the *taharah* period changes the halachic categorization of bleeding only once she has completed the process with *tevilah*. Both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel agree that if she was also a *zavah* at the time of childbirth, her blood is *metamei lach ve-yavesh* — genuinely *zavah* blood, *tamei* even dry — since she has not yet undergone *tevilah* as a *zavah*.

### Zavah Status Debate

The Gemara asks: does Beit Shammai not accept the principle that non-Jews, while not *mitamei* from the Torah as *zavim*, were nonetheless decreed upon by the rabbis to have the status of *zavim* in all respects — including for women to have the status of *zavot*? If so, why does Beit Shammai say the blood of a non-Jewish woman is entirely *tahor*? If she is treated as a *zavah*, her blood should be *tamei*!

Beit Shammai responds: we face a dilemma. On one hand, if we give her blood the full status of *zav* blood — *tamei lach ve-yavesh* — people will mistake this rabbinic *tumah* for a biblical one, and they will begin burning *terumah* on its basis, which is inappropriate for a rabbinic enactment. On the other hand, if we treat the blood as *tamei* only when wet (like Beit Hillel's position) — essentially treating it like spit — that distinction is too subtle. People will not recognize it as rabbinic; they will think that even for Jewish women, blood is only *tamei* when wet, undermining the law of *niddah* blood being *tamei* even dry.

Beit Shammai therefore concludes: the distinction must be unmistakable. We must say the blood is entirely *tahor*, which will be an obvious signal that the non-Jewish woman's *tumah* is rabbinic and distinct from the *tumah* of a Jewish *niddah*. The subtlety of "wet but not dry" is insufficient as a marker. Beit Hillel accepts the risk of the subtle distinction, treating the blood as *tamei* when wet and *tahor* when dry — following the model of spit — and relying on that distinction to communicate the rabbinic nature of the *tumah*.

### Blood vs. Mayanot

The Gemara asks a further question. If Beit Shammai insists on making an obvious distinction to mark the rabbinic nature of the non-Jewish woman's *tumah*, why not make the obvious distinction with the *spit and urine* rather than with the blood? Say that her spit and urine are *tahor* (making the obvious distinction there) while her blood is *tamei*. This would serve the same purpose.

The Gemara answers: *roka umimei ragleha deshchichi, gazru behu rabbanan* — spit and urine are common occurrences; the rabbis enacted the decree precisely on the common cases. If we are treating a non-Jewish woman like a *zavah*, we must make that practically operative in the ways most commonly encountered. Blood from a non-Jewish woman, by contrast, is not commonly encountered — *dama dela shechichah* — and therefore the rabbis were able to leave the blood outside the decree, using that as the signal of the rabbinic (rather than biblical) nature of the *tumah*. This is the logic: be *machmir* in what is prevalent, and carve out the exception in what is rare. Beit Shammai agrees the common fluids must be *tamei*; it is the blood, as the uncommon and also legally complex case, that serves as the marker of rabbinic status.

It is worth pausing here on an additional point: the entire rabbinic decree treating non-Jews as *zavim* is not driven by a genuine concern about *tumah* in the same way that the Kuti laws were. The Kutim, being treated as Jews, could generate real *tumah*. Non-Jews cannot have biblical *tumah*. The rabbinic decree here serves a social and communal function — maintaining distinctions and barriers between Jews and non-Jews — rather than responding to a direct concern about genuine *tumah* transmission.

### Non-Jewish Man

Rava then addresses the case of a non-Jewish man. He states: *zovo tamei afilu leBeis Shammai* — his *zivah* flow, the discharge that would create *zav* status, is *tamei* even according to Beit Shammai. *Kiryo tahor afilu leBeis Hillel* — his seminal emission, by contrast, is *tahor* even according to Beit Hillel.

The reasoning follows the same structure applied to the woman. Beit Shammai requires making an obvious distinction to signal that the non-Jewish man's *tumah* is rabbinic. For the woman, the obvious distinction was made via the blood (which is *tahor*) while leaving spit and urine as *tamei*. For the man, the distinction is made via the *keri* (seminal emission), which is *tahor*, while the *zivah* discharge remains *tamei*. Why this allocation? *D'ha ika l'mevad heikera b'kiryo* — because the *keri* is available as the place to make the distinction. The *zivah* itself is the basis for treating him as a *zav* in the first place; it would be incoherent to say we treat him like a *zav* yet his *zivah* is *tahor*. So the *keri* is where the distinction is made.

The Gemara raises the reverse question: why not say the *zivah* is *tahor* and the *keri* is *tamei*? The answer: *zovo d'lo tali b'maaseh gazru bei rabbanan* — the *zivah* is not a volitional act and is therefore treated by the rabbis as the primary locus of the decree; *kiryo d'tali b'maaseh lo gazru bei rabbanan* — seminal emission, being more volitional and in some sense more controllable, is the appropriate place to leave *tahor* and thus signal the rabbinic character of the whole regime.

Both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel agree that *keri* of a non-Jewish man must be *tahor*: for Beit Shammai, because it is used as the obvious distinction (analogous to the blood of the non-Jewish woman); for Beit Hillel, because when it comes to a man there is no analogue to the "wet but not dry" distinction that works for blood. Spit and *zivah* do not admit of that subtle distinction in the same way. So Beit Hillel also must identify an entire category to declare *tahor*, and that is the *keri*.

### Semen Status

The Gemara then cites a Mishnah from tractate *Mikvaos* in support. The Mishnah states: *shichvat zera shel Yisrael tmeia b'chol makom* — the semen of a Jewish man is *tamei* in all places, *afilu b'mei ovedet kochavim* — even while inside or emanating from the body of a non-Jewish woman. *Ve-shel ovdei kochavim tehorah b'chol makom* — the semen of a non-Jewish man is *tahor* in all places, *afilu b'mei Yisraelit* — even while inside a Jewish woman. The determining factor is not the woman, but the source of the semen: Jewish semen retains its *tumah* status wherever it is found; non-Jewish semen is *tahor* wherever it is found.

The exception noted by the Mishnah is *chutz mimei raglayim she'bah* — except for the urine. The precise reading here involves some complexity regarding whether it refers to her urine or his. The sense appears to be that the urine of the non-Jewish man is *tamei* — consistent with our ruling that *zivah* and spit and urine of a non-Jewish man carry *tumah* — while his semen alone is *tahor*.

The Gemara initially considers whether the Mishnah's ruling that non-Jewish semen is *tahor* refers only to biblical law, with rabbinic *tumah* still applying. It resolves this by noting that the urine in the same passage is explicitly *tamei*, and that *tumah* is itself only rabbinic (since the non-Jewish man has no biblical *tumah*). If the urine is called *tamei* and the semen is called *tahor* in the same Mishnah, and both statuses can only be rabbinic, then the contrast makes clear that the semen is *tahor* even rabbinically — *shema mina tehorah afilu miderabbanan*.

### Rav Pappa's Question

The Gemara then connects this Mishnah to an unresolved question posed by Rav Pappa. The Mishnah has just stated that the semen of a Jewish man is *tamei* even inside the body of a non-Jewish woman. Could this resolve Rav Pappa's *ba'ya*? Rav Pappa had asked: *shichvat zera shel Yisrael b'mei ovedet kochavim mahu* — what is the status of the semen of a Jewish man that is inside the body of a non-Jewish woman?

The Gemara clarifies: Rav Pappa was not asking about within three days, when obviously the semen retains its status as *tamei*. His question was specifically about *after three days* — after 72 hours — since at that point Jewish semen ordinarily loses its potency and is presumed *tahor*. Does the same apply when the semen is inside a non-Jewish woman?

The Gemara raises two competing theories about what happens inside a non-Jewish woman's body. One theory: *Yisrael de-daige be-mitzvot, chavilei gufaihu u-matriach* — Jews are anxious about their obligations, which causes their bodies to overheat, and this heat causes the semen to spoil and lose potency within 72 hours. Non-Jews, who carry no such burden of anxiety, do not overheat in the same way, and their bodies therefore preserve semen longer. Under this theory, Jewish semen inside a non-Jewish woman might *not* spoil after 72 hours, since her body is cooler. The reverse theory: *de-achli shekatzim uremasim, chavilei gufaihu u-matriach* — non-Jews eat non-kosher food, which heats the body; therefore their bodies *also* cause semen to spoil rapidly. Under this theory, the semen would indeed spoil within 72 hours inside a non-Jewish woman as well. *Teku* — the question remains unresolved.

This is admittedly a striking passage: what appears at first glance to be a formal halachic category question — does the status of semen change depending on whose body it enters — is resolved by the Gemara through a chain of reasoning about the physiology of anxiety, dietary practice, and body temperature. It is notable that Tosafot comments on this, pointing out that the Gemara's framing of Rav Pappa's question as specifically about "after three days" — with the accompanying medical reasoning — is the Gemara's own gloss and elaboration. In other contexts (specifically in tractate Shabbat, in the chapter of Rabbi Akiva), the Gemara treats Rav Pappa's original question as a simpler one: perhaps when semen enters the body of a non-Jewish woman, its halachic status changes categorically, without reference to the three-day threshold at all. Tosafot notes that it is not uncommon for the Gemara to reframe an original statement in a way that adds a layer of interpretation not originally intended by its author.
