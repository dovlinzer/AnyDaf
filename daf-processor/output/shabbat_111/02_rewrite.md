# Shabbat 111 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Medicinal Remedies

### Remedies Overview

The present daf begins at the bottom of 110, picking up five lines from the end of the previous section. A substantial portion of the preceding days' learning was devoted to a wide range of ancient remedies, much of which may have felt remote to a modern audience. Yet the relevance is real: understanding these remedies illuminates the Gemara's underlying concern about taking medicine on Shabbat. In a world where treatment for illness meant personally preparing and administering various substances — grinding, boiling, mixing — the risk of incidentally performing prohibited *melachot* was immediate and concrete. The discussion of remedies is thus not a medical digression but a halachic framework.

### Jaundice & Sterilization

The Gemara resumes with a remedy for jaundice (*yarkona*): one takes two of the ingredients previously mentioned and boils them with mead. The Gemara immediately flags a serious problem — this remedy has a sterilizing side effect. This raises the question: how can it be permissible? We have a *braita* explicitly prohibiting a person from sterilizing himself. The *braita* derives this prohibition from the verse *u'v'artzechem lo sa'asu* — a verse in the context of blemished animals brought as *korbanot* — which is read as *bachem lo sa'asu*: do not do this to yourselves either. This derivation, attributed to the school of Rabbi Chanina, is treated as non-controversial.

## Serus Prohibition

### Biblical Source

The prohibition of *serus* — castration — applies both to animals and to humans, with both prohibitions derived from the same verse, *u'v'artzechem lo sa'asu*. The verse, though literally addressed to the bringing of blemished animals as offerings, is read expansively: do not castrate any creature in your land, including yourselves. This dual application sets the stage for the Gemara's analysis of permissible and impermissible means of sterilization.

### Direct vs. Indirect

The Gemara initially suggests that the remedy is permissible because the sterilization occurs *me'eilav* — on its own, incidentally. The language of *mechaven* (intending) versus *me'eilav* (happening on its own) might suggest that this is a question of *davar she'eino miskavein* — an unintentional act. However, as Tosafot points out, this framing is not quite accurate, since in the case of the rooster, which the Gemara adduces as a parallel, the person is in fact intending for the sterility to occur. Rabbi Yochanan states: if one wishes to sterilize a rooster, one should remove its crown (*karbalto*), and it will automatically become sterile. The logic of this proof is that both the prohibition of *serus* in humans and in animals derives from the same verse, so if removing the crown is treated as a permissible act, it must be because indirect causation of sterility is categorically different from direct castration. The distinction, then, is not between intentional and unintentional, but between direct and indirect action.

Rav Ashi challenges this proof: removing the rooster's crown does not actually castrate it. Rather, as Rav Ashi explains, *ramost rucha hu* — it is a kind of diminishment of spirit or drive; the rooster loses interest but is not physically rendered infertile. This is more analogous to a hormonal treatment that suppresses testosterone than to actual castration. The rooster proof therefore cannot serve as evidence that indirect *serus* is permitted.

### Already Sterile Cases

The Gemara pivots. Perhaps the remedy is permissible not because of indirect causation, but because the person drinking it is already sterile — *ela besaris* — and one cannot sterilize the already sterile. The Gemara raises an objection from Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: in another context, *hakol modim bemachmetz achar machmetz shechayav* — everyone agrees that a second act of leavening is punishable even after the *mincha* was already leavened. The analogy is to *serus achar serus*: the Gemara cites a verse listing various degrees of injury to the reproductive organs — *mau'ach, chatut, natuk, charut* — and derives from the redundancy that even a second act of castration upon one who is already castrated is *chayav*. The verse's inclusion of *natuk* (fully severed) after *charut* (partially severed) teaches that even when the prior injury has already rendered the person sterile, a subsequent act that further injures the same area is still a violation.

One might ask: if only something additional is being done to the body, is that the condition? The discussion suggests that the issue is any new act of castration upon the relevant organ, even if the person is no longer functionally reproductive. Whether it is the act itself or the incremental harm that matters is left somewhat open, but the Gemara treats *serus achar serus* as *chayav* as a general rule.

The Gemara therefore tries a third answer: *ela bezaken* — perhaps the person taking the remedy is elderly, past reproductive age. This too is rejected, and in a striking way. Rabbi Yochanan himself testifies, referring to certain medicinal treatments mentioned in Gittin: *hain hain hechziruni linurossai* — those very treatments restored my youth to me. A man, it turns out, is never fully beyond the possibility of fathering children, even in old age. The Gemara therefore concludes that no male is ever truly *out of the parsha* of *serus*. If he has been rendered sterile through some prior accident or surgery, *serus achar serus* still applies. If he has not, the prohibition applies directly.

### Women's Exemption

The Gemara concludes: the case where this remedy is permissible is *b'isha* — a woman. And not merely an older woman, but any woman. The prohibition of *serus* as derived from the relevant verses is framed entirely around male anatomy. There is a *machlokes tanna'im* on whether women are included in the prohibition at all. Some *tanna'im* hold that *serus* applies equally to women and carries *malkut*. Others hold it does not apply to women at *malkut* level. Among those who exclude women from *malkut*, there is a further dispute: some hold there is an *issur aseh*, others that it is only *derabbanan*. This Gemara appears to treat the prohibition as sufficiently minimal for women — at most *derabbanan* — that it does not override her medical need when drinking the remedy for jaundice.

### Age & Barrenness

Even if there is no *serus* prohibition for women, there remains the concern of *pru u'revu*. The Gemara raises Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka, who holds that women are included in the obligation of *pru u'revu* based on the verse *vayevarech osam Elokim vayomer lahem pru u'revu* — "and God blessed them both." The Gemara answers: *ikka meimra b'zakena* — one can invoke the case of an older woman, who biologically cannot bear children; and alternatively, *ee nami b'akara* — a woman who was always barren. For such a woman, *pru u'revu* is not relevant, and she may therefore take the remedy.

This passage carries significant broader implications. Tosafot raises the question: why did the Gemara not simply say the woman had already fulfilled *pru u'revu* — had a son and a daughter? Tosafot suggests this omission may be telling: it indicates that a person who has had a son and a daughter is not simply exempt from further reproduction. The obligation to have as many children as possible may persist even after the minimal requirement is met. Tosafot acknowledges, however, that this remains an open question, connected to the various positions in Yevamot about the strength of the obligation post-fulfillment.

In the context of women and *serus*, the Gemara in Yevamot contains a well-known story: a woman, disguising herself, asked her husband whether women are obligated in *pru u'revu*. He said no. She subsequently drank a sterilizing potion. While the husband was upset, the Gemara notes they had already fulfilled *pru u'revu*. This story illustrates that for women, bracketing *pru u'revu*, the prohibition of sterilization appears to be a much more limited concern. The framework suggested by the Gemara here is that the direct/indirect distinction — which for men creates at minimum a rabbinic concern even when indirect — for women, where there is no biblical prohibition, may mean that drinking such a potion is entirely unproblematic. The concern in the jaundice case and in the Yevamot case converges on this point.

### Pet Sterilization

These principles extend to the treatment of animals, which raises acute practical questions. The prohibition of *serus* applies to animals as well — it is learned from the same verse. This makes the question of neutering pets genuinely complex.

For female animals, the prohibition is somewhat less severe, paralleling the distinction between men and women discussed above. For male animals, the prohibition is biblically grounded. One might suggest having a non-Jew perform the procedure. This raises two issues. First, there is a view in the Gemara that non-Jews are also prohibited in castration as one of the *sheva mitzvos bnei Noach*, making a non-Jewish veterinarian potentially a participant in his own prohibition. Second, even if one does not rule that way, there is the general concern of instructing a non-Jew to perform an act that is prohibited to a Jew — a *machlokes rishonim* about the scope and applicability of that principle.

The question is further complicated by considerations of *tzaar baalei chayim* and animal health. While some have attempted to frame neutering within the parameters of *tzaar baalei chayim* to justify the procedure, this is not the standard understanding. The prohibition of *serus* is treated as an independent *issur*, not simply subsumed under animal welfare considerations.

For female cats in Israel, the approach has shifted to focus on female sterilization, given the somewhat more lenient position. Research is also underway on hormonal contraceptives for female cats, which would avoid the surgical question entirely. For individuals with pets who need to be neutered, the minimum requirement would be to use a non-Jewish veterinarian, and ideally to find a way to ensure that the most critical act is performed indirectly. The broader question of what acts actually constitute *serus* — particularly when the procedure is partially or fully reversible — is itself a significant area of halachic inquiry without settled consensus.

## Toothache Remedy

### Mishna Toothache

The Gemara proceeds to a new *mishna*. If someone has a toothache (*hachochesh beshinav*), they may not swallow vinegar (*lo yigma bahen et hachometz*), as this is a recognizable act of *refuah*. However, one may dip bread in vinegar in the normal course of the meal, and if it incidentally passes over the aching gum, that is permissible — the action has the appearance of ordinary eating rather than medicinal treatment.

---

## Loin Pain Remedy (III)

### Rose Oil Exception

The *mishna* continues: one who has pain in the loins (*hachochesh bematnav*) may not rub wine or vinegar into the affected area, as this is clearly remedial. One may, however, rub in plain oil, since oil is a common skin treatment that does not necessarily signal medical intent. Rose oil (*shemen vered*) is different: it is used specifically for medicinal or wound-related purposes, and the Rabbis therefore prohibited it. However, *bnei melachim* — members of the upper class — who routinely use rose oil even on weekdays for purposes of comfort and luxury, may apply it to their wounds on Shabbat, since for them it does not carry the appearance of *refuah*. Rabbi Shimon disagrees with the distinction between classes, and rules: *kol Yisrael bnei melachim hein* — all of Israel are children of kings. Since some people use rose oil in a way that does not look medicinal, we cannot uniformly classify it as obviously remedial for anyone. Rashi, noted later in the Gemara, adds that Rabbi Shimon's reasoning also reflects the principle of *lo plug* — we do not make distinctions based on class — and applies it *lekula*: since for some it is not apparent *refuah*, for all it is not apparent *refuah*.

## Toothache Remedy (II)

### Swallow Definition

The Gemara challenges the *mishna*'s prohibition on swallowing vinegar for a toothache. The *mishna* says one may not swallow vinegar. But the Gemara also cites a *braita* that states: one may not take vinegar in and spit it out (*lo yigma upolet*), but one may take it in and swallow it (*megamaye uvoleya*) — implying that actually swallowing it is permissible. So which is it: is swallowing permitted or not?

Abaye resolves the apparent contradiction: when the *mishna* says one may not "swallow" vinegar, it actually refers to the act of taking it in and spitting it out — since who would actually swallow vinegar unless they were using it as a gargle? If a person swallows it entirely, others will assume it is simply an unusual taste preference, not a medical treatment, and it would not look like *refuah*. The *mishna*'s prohibition is therefore specifically the swish-and-spit method.

### Dipping Period

Rava offers a different resolution. He maintains that the *mishna* prohibits even actual swallowing. The key distinction, he says, is not what one does with the vinegar but *when* one does it: *kan lifnei tibul kan leachar tibul* — before the dipping period of the meal or after. During the appetizer course, when it was customary to dip foods into vinegar and oil, someone taking vinegar at that moment would appear to be eating normally. After the dipping period has concluded, the only person taking vinegar would be doing so for medical reasons, and it would be obviously *refuah*. So Rava distinguishes based on timing rather than on the manner of consumption.

### Consistency

One might then ask: if vinegar before the dipping period is permitted, why not permit it after the dipping period as well, applying a *lo plug lekula* — not distinguishing between cases where the same substance is being consumed? The Gemara explains that Rava himself applies the principle of *hoil* — "since" — to reason consistently across cases. The specific application: Rava holds that there is nothing permissible on Shabbat that is prohibited on Yom Kippur (in terms of *melachot* and rabbinic prohibitions of a comparable type). The illustrative case is going to the *mikvah*.

On Shabbat, immersing in a *mikvah* is permissible for a person who needs to do so, since it might appear one is simply taking a cooling dip. On Yom Kippur, bathing for pleasure is prohibited, so immersion would obviously be for *mikvah* purposes — suggesting it should be forbidden. Yet Rava says: if it is permitted on Shabbat, it must be permitted on Yom Kippur as well, for the sake of consistency.

The Gemara subsequently notes that Rava reversed himself — *hadar bei Rava*. The question is: from which position did he reverse? The Gemara considers whether he retracted his ruling about vinegar after the dipping period, or his ruling about the *mikvah* on Yom Kippur. The Gemara concludes he could not have retracted the *mikvah* ruling, since a *braita* states explicitly that anyone obligated in immersion may immerse normally, whether on *Tisha B'Av* or on Yom Kippur. Since Rava would not argue with that *braita*, his consistency argument about the *mikvah* stands — and therefore he must have reversed himself on the vinegar, conceding that even after the dipping period, vinegar may be swallowed, since before the dipping period it appears normal. In the end, then, the position is essentially aligned with Abaye: the *mishna*'s prohibition refers only to taking vinegar in and spitting it out.

As a practical note, while the *mikvah* on Yom Kippur is technically permissible according to this analysis, we do not permit it on *Tisha B'Av*. Tosafot cites the Yerushalmi: *k'dai hu beis Eloheinu l'aved alav tevilla achat b'shanah* — the destruction of the Temple is worthy of forgoing one immersion per year.

## Loin Pain Remedy (III)

### Mishna Loin Pain

The Gemara returns to the *mishna* on loin pain. One may rub plain oil on the affected area but not wine or vinegar. Rabbi Abba bar Zavda says in the name of Rav: *halacha k'Rabbi Shimon* — we rule like Rabbi Shimon, permitting rose oil.

### Rav Contradiction

This ruling prompts a challenge. Does Rav generally follow Rabbi Shimon when Rabbi Shimon is lenient? Yet Rav Shimi bar Chiyya reports in Rav's name a ruling about tightening a rag stopper (*mesuchraya*) in a wine barrel: one may not tighten it on *Yom Tov*, because as one forces the rag deeper into the hole, wine is squeezed out of the rag. This would seem to be a case of *davar she'eino miskavein* — one is not intending to squeeze out the wine — and Rabbi Shimon holds *davar she'eino miskavein* is permitted. Yet Rav prohibits it. This appears to show Rav ruling against Rabbi Shimon on *davar she'eino miskavein*, while ruling with Rabbi Shimon on the rose oil. The comparison is admittedly between very different halachic concerns, as Tosafot notes, and the attempt to hold Rav to a uniform position across unrelated rulings of Rabbi Shimon is somewhat forced. Nevertheless, the Gemara takes the question seriously.

## Wine Barrel Case

### Barrel Case

The barrel case deserves close attention. When one tightens a rag stopper in a wine barrel, the wine absorbed in the rag is squeezed out as a consequence of that act. The concern is *sechita* — squeezing. The actor's intent is not to squeeze out the wine but to seal the hole. This seems like a paradigmatic case of *davar she'eino miskavein*, which Rabbi Shimon permits. The question is why Rav prohibits it if he rules like Rabbi Shimon.

---

## Loin Pain Remedy (III)

### Resolution

The Gemara resolves the contradiction: *b'hahu afilu Rabbi Shimon modeh* — in that barrel case, even Rabbi Shimon would agree that it is prohibited. This is because Abaye and Rava both state — *modei Rabbi Shimon b'psik reisheih v'lo yamut* — Rabbi Shimon concedes in a case of *pesik reisha*: if one cuts off a chicken's head, one cannot claim not to have intended its death. Similarly, if one tightens the rag in the barrel, it is absolutely certain that wine will be squeezed out. That is not *davar she'eino miskavein* in the sense Rabbi Shimon permits — it is *pesik reisha*, an inevitable consequence. Even Rabbi Shimon agrees such an act is prohibited. Therefore, Rav's ruling against tightening the stopper is entirely consistent with ruling like Rabbi Shimon on rose oil. In both cases Rav follows Rabbi Shimon; the barrel case falls under *pesik reisha*, which is a separate category where even Rabbi Shimon agrees.

## Davar Eino Miskavein

### Rav vs. Shmuel

With this resolution established, the Gemara takes the opportunity to address the broader halachic question: how do we actually rule on the debate between Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yehuda regarding *davar she'eino miskavein*? Rabbi Chiyya bar Ashi reports in Rav's name: *halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda* — an unintentional act is prohibited. Rav Chanan bar Ami reports in Shmuel's name: *halacha k'Rabbi Shimon* — an unintentional act is permitted. Rabbi Chiyya bar Avin then reports this as a straightforward tradition without attribution to intermediaries: Rav said we rule like Rabbi Yehuda; Shmuel said we rule like Rabbi Shimon.

So Rav indeed rules like Rabbi Yehuda that *davar she'eino miskavein* is *assur*. This returns us to the earlier question: how could Rav rule like Rabbi Shimon on the rose oil?

### Rava Explanation

Rava, together with Rabbi Chiyya bar Avin whom he calls *vireibi b'chaburah* — the lion of their study group — offers the following explanation: we rule like Rabbi Shimon that rose oil is permitted, *v'lav mita'ameih* — but not for his reasoning. One might initially think this means: Rabbi Shimon permits rose oil even though it is uncommon among the general population, because of his *bnei melachim* argument that all Jews are of royal standing. Rav would permit it without accepting that reasoning, perhaps by simply denying that rose oil is an effective remedy. But the Gemara rejects this: the *mishna* itself records that *bnei melachim* rub rose oil on their wounds, which implies it genuinely does heal. Rav cannot simply say *lo masi* — it does not work — when the text assumes it does.

The Gemara therefore explains differently: Rabbi Shimon's reasoning is that rose oil is permitted even where it is uncommon, because *kol Yisrael bnei melachim hein*. Rav's reasoning is different: *ee shechiach — in; v'ee lo shechiach — lo* — if rose oil is prevalent in that locale, it is permitted; if it is rare and expensive, it is not. In Rav's own region, rose oil was very common and inexpensive, and people used it for non-medicinal purposes. Therefore, in his region, it did not look like a *refuah*, and he permitted it. He was not actually agreeing with Rabbi Shimon's principle. In principle, Rav maintains the prohibition; his local ruling was a function of local circumstances.

This is a striking Gemara: what looks like a ruling *like* Rabbi Shimon is actually a ruling *against* Rabbi Shimon in principle, permitted only because the empirical conditions in Rav's place happened to remove the rabbinic concern.

### Practical Ruling

The practical upshot is significant. Rav rules like Rabbi Yehuda on *davar she'eino miskavein*, and Shmuel rules like Rabbi Shimon. Normally in non-monetary disputes we follow Rav over Shmuel. This would make *davar she'eino miskavein* prohibited, rendering Shabbat observance considerably more difficult across a wide range of situations, particularly in cases of *tzaar* or other pressing needs. Yet in practice, virtually no one rules like Rav here. Abaye and Rava appear to take Rabbi Shimon's position as a settled premise, as do many other Gemaras that assume the permissibility of unintentional acts. The ruling is universally in practice that *davar she'eino miskavein* is *mutar* — unless it constitutes *pesik reisha*, in which case even Rabbi Shimon agrees it is prohibited.

Where there is some debate among *rishonim* is with respect to *melacha she'eina tzricha legufa* — a *melacha* performed not for its own sake. Here too Rabbi Shimon is lenient and the overwhelming halachic practice follows him. However, the Rambam rules that *melacha she'eina tzricha legufa* is *chayav*, placing him in the stricter position. This is generally treated as a minority view and is bracketed in practice, though it remains an important position.

## Wine Barrel Case (II)

### Squeezing Forms

Returning to the barrel case, Tosafot engages with a prior question: why is tightening the stopper a problem at all? The wine squeezed out falls to the ground — it is *mekalkel*, going to waste. Tosafot explores this by developing the idea that *sechita* — squeezing — is prohibited in two distinct forms. The first is squeezing in the context of laundering (*kibusin*): when a garment soaked in water is wrung out, the act of expelling the liquid is part of the process of cleaning the garment. The second is squeezing to extract liquid for use, as when squeezing fruit. Tosafot notes that when a cloth is soaked in water, the concern is the laundering prohibition; when soaked in other liquids, the concern may be extraction. In the barrel case, the rag is soaked in wine, and some of the wine may be forced back into the barrel as the rag is tightened — meaning not all of it is *mekalkel*, and there is a genuine extraction concern. Tosafot further suggests that even if the liquid being expelled is a staining substance and the act removes some of that staining, this too might constitute a form of laundering, even if the garment is not yet clean — any removal of the staining liquid could be part of the laundering process.

### Cleaning Spills

These distinctions have immediate practical consequences. The prohibition on applying liquid to a stained garment on Shabbat — since *shriya zu hi kibuso*, soaking is itself the beginning of laundering — means that pouring water, seltzer, or salt onto a tablecloth stain on Shabbat is a real concern, possibly a *melacha de'oraita*. This is a commonly misunderstood point: it is not merely that one squeezes out the cloth while cleaning; the act of applying a substance to the stain is itself problematic.

Tosafot addresses this directly in the context of an infant who has urinated on its mother's clothes. One may not pour water on the stain, because *shriya zu hi kibuso*. The question is then what to do.

### Rabbeinu Tam Ruling

Tosafot records the following oral ruling transmitted through women: Rabbi Yitzchak (the *Ri*) found that Rabbeinu Tam — in a tradition preserved not in the *Sefer HaYashar* but conveyed orally through women who testified to it — permitted the following. The mother may wash her hands and then dry them on the soiled garment. The purpose was so that she could daven or recite blessings without the impediment of a malodorous garment. The act of drying wet hands on clothing would dirty the garment further, and this *hashchaka* — soiling — would make it categorically an act of making the garment dirty rather than cleaning it, thus removing the laundering concern.

The *Ri* himself, however, is not satisfied with this ruling. He notes that the woman's *intent* is to purify the stain — *hi mitchavenet letaher lichluch* — to remove the urine. And the manner of the act — *derech rechitza velivun* — is consistent with laundering. Therefore, the *Ri* concludes it is properly considered an act of laundering regardless of the ostensible purpose.

This brief exchange is remarkable on several levels: the mechanism by which Rabbeinu Tam's ruling was transmitted through the women who had received it orally, and the *Ri*'s willingness to engage with and ultimately reject that ruling on principled grounds. The practical lesson remains: applying any substance to a stain on Shabbat is a genuine prohibition, likely at the *de'oraita* level. The question of wiping wet hands on fabric is a separate discussion with its own considerations.

## Knots on Shabbat

### Camel & Sailor Knots

The Gemara now begins the chapter *Ve'eilu Kesharim*, which deals with the *melacha* of *kosher umatir* — tying and untying knots on Shabbat. The *mishna* identifies the *chiyuv* category: the knot of the camel driver (*kesher hagemalim*) and the knot of the sailor (*kesher hasapanim*). Just as one is *chayav* for tying these, so one is *chayav* for untying them — *matir* is a full *melacha*.

### Knot Interpretation

The Gemara asks: what exactly are the camel knot and sailor knot? Two possibilities are considered. The first is the rope tied through the nose ring of a camel, or the rope tied through the ring of a boat — ropes that are made and eventually untied when the animal or vessel moves on. The second is the ring itself — in Rashi's reading, both the nose ring of the camel and the ring on the boat were themselves fashioned from rope and tied in place. The Gemara favors the second reading: the rope through the ring is not *shel kayama* — it is not permanently fixed. The ring itself is left there for the lifetime of the camel or the boat. The *mishna*'s exemplary knots are the rings themselves.

### Kayama Definition

Rashi's understanding of *kayama* — permanence — in the context of knots is notably stringent. A knot that is left in place for days or even weeks, even one that is professionally complex, is not *shel kayama* unless it is intended to remain there permanently — for the entire life of the animal or vessel. This is a more extreme definition of permanence than what applies to, for example, other *melachot* where *kayama* may mean "lasting through Shabbat." It appears that for knots, *kayama* means genuinely permanent in an absolute sense. The Rambam reads the *mishna* differently, and the question of what constitutes a professional knot plays a role in his analysis as well, but Rashi's focus is squarely on *mamash* permanent.

The derivation of the *melacha* of tying knots from the construction of the *Mishkan* reinforces this understanding, as Rashi notes: the nets and curtain hangings of the *Mishkan* were made to remain exactly as assembled — the archetype of permanence.

### Rabbi Meier Position

Rabbi Meir offers a more lenient threshold: any knot that can be undone with one hand (*achat miyadav*) does not carry a *chiyuv*. This appears to set a lower bar than the camel and sailor knots, since the latter are highly complex and presumably very difficult to undo, requiring considerable effort. The question is how Rabbi Meir's criterion relates to the majority standard.

### One Hand Debate

The Gemara asks about a bow knot (*aniva*) according to Rabbi Meir. The question cuts to the heart of what Rabbi Meir means. Is his criterion literally physical: if one can undo the knot using a single hand, it is not *chayav*, and since a bow knot can indeed be undone by pulling one string, it is permitted? Or does *one hand* serve as a proxy for looseness — indicating that the knot is not tightly and firmly set, in which case the bow knot, while easily undone, may still be *mehadek* — tight and well-fastened — and Rabbi Meir would still count it? The Gemara leaves this unresolved: *teiku*. What is clear is that Rabbi Meir's threshold is meaningfully more lenient than that of the other *tanna'im*, whose concern focuses on complex, professional, and genuinely permanent knots.

## Permitted Knots

### Mishna Permitted

The following *mishna* introduces a different category. There are knots that carry no *chiyuv*, like the camel and sailor knots but of a lesser degree. And then there are knots that are entirely permitted *lechatchila*. The *mishna* lists: a woman may tie the straps of her garment, the strings of her hairnet, the straps of her *paskia* (a thick belt), shoe strings and sandal strings, wine flask straps, oil flask straps, and a leather covering over a pot.

### Fence Tying

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov extends this further: one may tie a rope across the opening of an animal pen to prevent the animal from leaving. This is an act that is purposefully functional and involves securing an opening — yet it too is fully permitted.

### Gemara Analysis

The Gemara notes an internal tension in the *mishna*. First the *mishna* says there are knots for which one is not *chayav* like those of the camel driver and sailor, implying they are not *chayav* but are still *assur*. Then the *mishna* says a woman may tie garment straps — apparently *lechatchila*. The Gemara resolves this: the *mishna* is describing two distinct categories within the non-*chayav* zone. The first consists of knots like the rope tied through the nose ring or the boat ring — relatively durable, quasi-professional, placed for extended periods — which are *patur aval assur*: not *chayav* but rabbinically prohibited. The second category consists of the garment straps, hairnet, shoe laces, and the like — these are *mutar* entirely.

### Classification

The classification therefore has three tiers. The top tier, *chayav*: the nose ring itself and the boat ring, permanent and professional. The middle tier, *patur aval assur*: the rope inserted through those rings, kept in place for days or weeks but intended to be undone. The bottom tier, fully *mutar*: the everyday knots of daily wear and function, clearly temporary and non-professional. The basis for the full permissibility of the bottom tier is twofold: the practical necessity of allowing normal daily activity on Shabbat, and the intrinsic halachic character of these knots — they are sufficiently temporary and casual that they do not approach the definition of *kesher* that the *melacha* was designed to prohibit.
