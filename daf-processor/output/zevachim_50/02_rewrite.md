# Zevachim 50 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Derivation Rules

### Me'akev Principle

The daf opens in the middle of a technically demanding section of the *gemara* concerning the parameters of derivation in the laws of *kodshim*. The organizing question is: what are the rules governing how we apply the thirteen hermeneutical principles when learning *halachos* of sacrificial offerings?

The first unusual feature of *kodshim* is the principle of *me'akev*. Normally, when the Torah specifies details for the performance of a *mitzvah*, we assume all those details are essential — failure to observe them invalidates the act. By *kodshim*, however, the presumption runs in the opposite direction: a detail is considered only *lemitzvah*, a preferred manner of performance, unless the Torah explicitly signals that it is *me'akev*.

### Shina Lo Hakatuv

The mechanism by which a detail is elevated to *me'akev* status is called *shina lo hakatuv le'akeiv* — the text must repeat or otherwise underscore the requirement in a way that marks it as essential. This is a surprising departure from the usual rules of *psak*, and the *mesora* of Chazal understood it to be necessary precisely because the laws of *korbanot* contain so many details that it would be untenable to treat all of them as absolutely indispensable.

### Learning Across Cases

A further complexity is that the Torah does not spell out every detail for every *korban*. The many offerings described in the Torah each have some details elaborated and others left implicit. This creates pressure to learn details from one *korban* and apply them to another through the standard hermeneutical tools — *hekkesh*, *gezeirah shavah*, *binyan av*, and *kal vachomer* — so that the requirements can be established across the full range of sacrifices.

### Lemadim Lamad Milamad

The foundational principle governing this enterprise was stated by Rabbi Yochanan: *bechol hatorah kulo lemadim lamad milamad* — throughout the entire Torah, one may derive from a derivation. If we learn a law for case A from case B, we may then take what we know about B and apply it to case C. This transitive principle is the baseline from which the *gemara* then carves out specific exceptions for *kodshim*.

### Hekkesh Min Hahekkesh

The first limitation: *davar halamad behekkesh ein chozer umelamed behekkesh* — a law derived by *hekkesh* cannot itself serve as the source for a further *hekkesh*. A *hekkesh* is a linkage the Torah creates by placing two cases side by side or explicitly equating them, such that we treat them identically in some respect. While the general principle of *lemadim lamad milamad* permits derivation from derivation, the *hekkesh* is considered too weak a foundation to support a second *hekkesh*.

### Tzafon Example

The concrete illustration: we know that the *chatas* must be slaughtered in the north (*tzafon*) of the altar because the Torah links it to the *olah* — *bimkom asher tishachat ha'olah tishachat hachatat*. That linkage is a *hekkesh*. Now the *chatas* and the *asham* are also related. Could we derive that the *asham* requires *tzafon* by building a second *hekkesh* on the first? The answer is no — *hekkesh min hahekkesh* is not permitted. The Torah had to state *tzafon* for the *asham* explicitly.

### Gezeirah Shavah Issue

A second limitation was established at the close of the previous daf: one may not build a *gezeirah shavah* on a *hekkesh*. The *gezeirah shavah* of *kodesh kodashim* — the shared phrase appearing in the verses describing both the *chatas* and the *asham* — could theoretically have been used to derive *tzafon* for the *asham* from the *chatas* (which itself learned *tzafon* via *hekkesh* from the *olah*). But we do not do this. The Torah needed to state *tzafon* explicitly by the *asham*, establishing that a *gezeirah shavah* cannot be built on a *hekkesh*.

### 4x4 Matrix

With these two prohibitions established, the *gemara* now systematically works through all the remaining combinations. The four derivation methods — *hekkesh*, *gezeirah shavah*, *binyan av*, and *kal vachomer* — can each serve as either the first step (A to B) or the second step (B to C). This yields a four-by-four matrix of sixteen possible combinations, and the *gemara* will determine, one by one, which are permitted and which are not.

## Hekkesh Column

### Hekkesh → Kal Vachomer

The first combination examined is *hekkesh* followed by *kal vachomer*: if a law is derived by *hekkesh*, may that derived law then serve as the basis for a *kal vachomer*? The answer is yes.

### Rabbi Yishmael Evidence

The proof comes from the *Tanna D'vei Rabbi Yishmael*, cited in an earlier chapter. The *gemara* there applied certain *halachos* known from an inner *chatas* — involving blood-sprinkling and immersion — to the *par* of *Yom Kippur*. The mechanism used was, first, a *hekkesh* linking the *par he'elam davar* to the *par* of the *kohen mashiach*, and then a *kal vachomer* extending the law to the *par* of *Yom Kippur*. This Tannaitic usage demonstrates that *hekkesh* followed by *kal vachomer* is an accepted mode of derivation in *kodshim*.

It is important to note the nature of this type of evidence: we are not deriving these rules logically from *pesukim*. Rather, we are drawing on Tannaitic tradition, finding actual usages that demonstrate which derivation chains were employed by the *rishonim* of the oral tradition.

### Hekkesh → Binyan Av

The fourth combination in the *hekkesh* column — *hekkesh* followed by *binyan av* — raises a harder question. A *binyan av* is a paradigm: if a principle is true of a representative case, it is extended by analogy to all sufficiently similar cases. The question is whether a law first derived via *hekkesh* can then serve as the founding case of such a paradigm.

### Asham Tzafon Rule

Rabbi Yirmiah addresses this. We have established that *tzafon* applies to the *chatas* via its *hekkesh* to the *olah*. Why could we not then extend *tzafon* to the *asham* by *binyan av* — reasoning that both are *kodshei kodashim* and the *chatas* establishes the paradigm? The very fact that the Torah had to state *tzafon* explicitly by the *asham* appears to demonstrate that one may not build a *binyan av* on a *hekkesh*. That is the intended proof.

### Binyan Av Critique

The *gemara* is unsatisfied. If the logic were simply "*binyan av* can extend *tzafon* from *chatas* to *asham*," why not also extend it directly from the *olah*? The *olah* has *tzafon* stated explicitly in the Torah without any derivation at all. Why did we need to say it by *chatas* and then by *asham*, rather than building the *binyan av* directly from *olah*?

### Category Differences

The answer is that the *binyan av* fails not because it was built on a *hekkesh*, but because the categories are intrinsically too different from one another to support the analogy. An *olah* is *kalil* — entirely consumed on the altar — unlike the *chatas* and *asham*. A *chatas* achieves atonement for sins punishable by *kares*, making it categorically weightier than an *asham*, which generally applies to lighter obligations. Each *korban* is distinctive in a way that blocks the *binyan av*. The fact that the Torah had to state *tzafon* explicitly for all three therefore proves nothing specifically about the interaction of *hekkesh* and *binyan av*.

### Binyan Av Intrinsic Issue

This conclusion is crucial: the failure of the *binyan av* here is intrinsic — the dissimilarities between *olah*, *chatas*, and *asham* independently undermine the analogy, regardless of how *tzafon* was derived for the *chatas* in the first place. We therefore cannot use this case as proof that *binyan av* cannot be built on *hekkesh*.

### Three-Way Paradigm

The *gemara* then asks a subtler question: even if a single-source *binyan av* fails because of the dissimilarities, perhaps a two-source *binyan av* — using two of the three as the founding paradigm — could overcome those objections and apply to the third. If two cases share a common feature, the combination might neutralize what would otherwise be a *pircha* (refutation) of the analogy.

### Pairwise Distinctions

The *gemara* works through each possible pairing. Could the *olah* and *chatas* together establish a paradigm for the *asham*? No — both an *olah* and a *chatas* require the Torah to state *tzafon* explicitly, and the *olah* is set apart because it is entirely burned (*kalil*), while the *chatas* is set apart because it atones for sins of *kares*. Could the *chatas* and *asham* establish a paradigm for the *olah*? No — both achieve atonement, but the *olah* does not. Could the *olah* and *asham* establish a paradigm for the *chatas*? No — both are male animals (*zecharim*), while a *chatas* is typically a female. Could the *olah* and *chatas* be used because one appears as a communal offering and one as an individual offering? Every attempted pairing yields a distinguishing feature that would undermine the *binyan av*.

The result is that there was no way to establish *tzafon* for all three by derivation — each required explicit scriptural statement. Consequently, this case provides no proof one way or the other regarding whether a *binyan av* can be built on a *hekkesh*. The question remains unresolved. It is worth noting, as an observation about the internal logic of the system, that *kal vachomer* — which succeeded where *binyan av* is uncertain — is conceptually stronger: it does not merely assert similarity but argues from the logic of greater and lesser stringency. The uncertainty about *binyan av*, by contrast, tracks the fact that it is fundamentally a pattern-matching exercise, and pattern-matching is more easily disrupted by categorical distinctions.

## Gezeirah Shavah Column

### Todah from Ma'aser

The *gemara* now turns to the second column: derivations that begin with a *gezeirah shavah*. The first question is whether a law derived by *gezeirah shavah* can then serve as the basis for a *hekkesh*. Rav Pappa advances a proof from the case of a *todah* brought from *ma'aser sheni* funds.

The principle at stake is that when one redeems *ma'aser sheni* produce onto money and then spends that money in Jerusalem, one may use it to purchase an offering whose meat one eats. A *todah* — a thanksgiving offering — may be brought with such money, provided the person specified at the time of the vow that the offering would come from *ma'aser sheni* funds.

### Sham Sham Connection

The derivation works in two steps. We know that *shlamim* may be purchased with *ma'aser sheni* money. How do we know that? From a *gezeirah shavah* of *sham sham*: the verse describing *shlamim* uses the word *sham* ("there"), as does the verse about *ma'aser sheni* consumption — *ve'achalta sham* by *shlamim* and *ve'achalta lifnei Hashem Elokecha bamakom asher yivchar leshaken shemo sham* by *ma'aser sheni*. This shared word links the two contexts and establishes that *shlamim* may come from *ma'aser sheni* funds (A to B via *gezeirah shavah*).

From there, the Torah's statement *vezot torat zevach hashlamim im al todah yakrivennu* — "this is the law of the *shlamim* if he brings it as a *todah*" — links the *todah* to the *shlamim*. This linkage (B to C via *hekkesh*) allows us to apply what is true of *shlamim* to the *todah* as well.

It is worth noting that calling the second step a *hekkesh* might seem overstated. When the Torah says "this is the law of the *shlamim* if he brings it as a *todah*," it appears to state directly that a *todah* is a species of *shlamim*, rather than merely linking the two by implication. Similarly, the statement *bimkom asher tishachat ha'olah tishachat hachatat* looks more like an explicit instruction than a *hekkesh*. Nevertheless, the *gemara* treats any move from B to C — even when fairly direct — as a *hekkesh* for purposes of this analysis.

### Rav Pappa Debate

Rav Pappa's proof establishes that *gezeirah shavah* followed by *hekkesh* is a valid derivation chain. However, the *gemara* does not let the matter rest.

### Chulin Source Issue

Mar Zutra bar Rav Mari challenges Rav Pappa: *ma'aser dagan chullin b'alma hu* — grain that is *ma'aser sheni* is, fundamentally, *chullin*. Even according to those who hold that *ma'aser sheni* has a degree of sanctity, it is certainly not in the category of *kodshim*. If the starting point of the derivation — the A in the A-to-B *gezeirah shavah* — is *chullin*, then the restrictions on chained derivations within *kodshim* simply do not apply. The case of *sham sham* tells us nothing about whether *gezeirah shavah* followed by *hekkesh* is permitted when the entire chain operates within the domain of *kodshim*.

### Restrictions Apply

Rav Pappa responds: the restrictions are not determined by where the derivation begins — the *melamed*, the source — but by what is being derived — the *lamed*, the application. Since the *shlamim* and *todah* (B and C) are *kodshim*, the relevant restrictions do apply, and nevertheless Rav Pappa holds the derivation is valid.

### Source vs Application

Rashi makes explicit the dispute implicit in this exchange. Rav Pappa holds *d'batar lamed azlinan* — we follow the derived case; so long as what is being derived is *kodshim*, the restrictions apply and must be reckoned with. Mar Zutra's implicit counter-position is *d'batar melamed azlinan* — we follow the source; if the original teaching is *chullin*, the restrictions of *kodshim* do not engage at all. This debate about whether the restrictions track the *melamed* or the *lamed* is embedded in, and clarified by, the passage before us.

### Gezeirah Shavah Chain

The *gemara* moves on to the next combination: *gezeirah shavah* followed by *gezeirah shavah*. Can a law derived by *gezeirah shavah* itself become the source for a further *gezeirah shavah*? Rami bar Chama cites a *braita* that appears to demonstrate exactly this.

### Bread Types Case

The *braita* concerns the breads that accompany a *todah*. A *todah* is brought with forty loaves: ten *chalos* (unleavened loaves), ten *rekikin* (thin wafers), ten *revuchah* (loaves prepared by first cooking in water and then frying), and ten *chamotz* (leavened loaves). The question is how we know that the *chalos* and the *rekikin* — in addition to the *revuchah* — must be made from *soles* (fine flour).

The *braita* teaches: for the *revuchah*, the requirement of *soles* is stated explicitly in the Torah. For the *chalos*, we derive it by a *gezeirah shavah* of *chalos chalos* — the word *chalos* appears both in the context of the *revuchah* and in the context of the standard *chalos*, establishing that the latter must also use *soles*. For the *rekikin*, we derive it by a *gezeirah shavah* of *matzos matzos* — the word *matzos* appears in the context of the *chalos* and in the context of the *rekikin*. Thus: *revuchah* to *chalos* via *gezeirah shavah* (*chalos chalos*), and *chalos* to *rekikin* via *gezeirah shavah* (*matzos matzos*) — a clear chain of *gezeirah shavah* upon *gezeirah shavah*.

### Direct Source Challenge

Ravina challenges this proof. He asks: *mimai d'matzos matzos me-chalos gmar* — how do we know that the *matzos matzos* derivation for the *rekikin* is drawing from the *chalos* (which was itself learned via *gezeirah shavah*)? Perhaps the *matzos* of *rekikin* is derived directly from the *mincha* baked in an oven (*maafeh tanur*) — a *mincha* brought as a *nedavah* — where the Torah explicitly uses the word *matzos* and also specifies *soles*. If so, the *matzos matzos* of *rekikin* is a direct derivation, not a *gezeirah shavah* built on a *gezeirah shavah*, and the *braita* proves nothing about chained *gezeirot shavot*.

### Cutting Up Case

Rava therefore proposes an alternative proof. He cites a different *braita* that describes the treatment of the inner *chatas* of the *kohen mashiach* — the *par* that is burned entirely outside the camp. The Torah states *v'kirbo u'firsho v'hotzi* — "its innards and its refuse he shall take out" — and the word *v'hotzi* implies a single act of removal, which in turn implies it is taken out whole rather than in pieces. Yet the Torah also says, by an *olah*, *rosho u'chrao* — "its head and its legs" — using a *gezeirah shavah* of *rosho u'chrao* (stated here by the inner *chatas* and stated there by the *olah*) to teach that the *chatas*, like the *olah*, must be cut into sections (*nitoach*) before burning. So from the *olah* to the *par kohen mashiach* is one *gezeirah shavah*.

Furthermore, the *gemara* notes, the verse also establishes that the *par* is not flayed (*hefshet*) before burning, since *v'kirbo u'firsho* indicates the innards remain enclosed — and just as the innards are not separated from the body cavity, the skin is not separated from the flesh. This is Rav Pappa's explanation of the verse *v'es or ha'par v'es kol besaro al rosho v'al kerav v'kirbo u'firsho v'hotzi* — everything is taken out as a unit, and just as the *pirsho* remains within the *kirbo*, so the skin remains on the flesh.

Then Rebbi, in a second *braita*, applies these same rules to the *par v'seir* of *Yom Kippur*. The verse there also says *or u'vasar u'feresh*, and by *gezeirah shavah* of *or u'vasar u'feresh* (stated here by *Yom Kippur* and there by the *par kohen mashiach*), we learn that the *Yom Kippur par* too is cut into sections but not flayed before burning. This is precisely *gezeirah shavah* built on *gezeirah shavah*: from *olah* to *par kohen mashiach* via one *gezeirah shavah*, and from *par kohen mashiach* to the *par v'seir* of *Yom Kippur* via a second *gezeirah shavah*. Rava's proof is thus established without any of the problems Ravina raised.

### Gezeirah Shavah → Kal Va…

With *gezeirah shavah* to *hekkesh* (debated by Rav Pappa) and *gezeirah shavah* to *gezeirah shavah* (established by Rava) now on the table, the *gemara* turns to the third combination in this column: *gezeirah shavah* followed by *kal vachomer*. May a law derived by *gezeirah shavah* serve as the basis for a *kal vachomer*?

### Strength Argument

The *gemara* employs a logical *kal vachomer* to resolve the question. We know two things: first, a *hekkesh* cannot support a further *hekkesh* (*hekkesh min hahekkesh* is forbidden), yet a *hekkesh* can support a *kal vachomer* (from *Tanna D'vei Rabbi Yishmael*). This shows that *kal vachomer* is strong enough to be built even on a weak foundation. Second, a *gezeirah shavah* is a stronger foundation than a *hekkesh*, because while you cannot build a *hekkesh* on a *hekkesh*, you can build a *gezeirah shavah* on a *gezeirah shavah* and — according to Rav Pappa — even a *hekkesh* on a *gezeirah shavah*. If the weaker foundation (*hekkesh*) permits *kal vachomer*, then certainly the stronger foundation (*gezeirah shavah*) permits *kal vachomer*.

The *gemara* notes a qualification: the first formulation of this argument relied on Rav Pappa's position that *gezeirah shavah* permits *hekkesh*, making *gezeirah shavah* demonstrably stronger than *hekkesh*. If one rejects Rav Pappa's position, that version of the argument fails. The *gemara* therefore offers an independent version: even without invoking Rav Pappa, we know that *hekkesh* cannot build on *hekkesh*, yet *gezeirah shavah* can build on *gezeirah shavah*. The capacity of a method to build on itself demonstrates its relative strength. Since *gezeirah shavah* is stronger than *hekkesh* by this measure, and *hekkesh* can still support a *kal vachomer*, *gezeirah shavah* certainly can.

### Conclusion

The *gemara* accepts this reasoning. Starting from a *gezeirah shavah* and then applying a *kal vachomer* is permitted without reservation.

### Gezeirah Shavah → Binyan…

The fourth and final combination in the *gezeirah shavah* column is *gezeirah shavah* followed by *binyan av*. The *gemara* offers no resolution: *teiku*. The question remains open.

To summarize the *gezeirah shavah* column: *gezeirah shavah* to *hekkesh* — Rav Pappa says yes, others dispute; *gezeirah shavah* to *gezeirah shavah* — yes, as proved by Rava; *gezeirah shavah* to *kal vachomer* — yes; *gezeirah shavah* to *binyan av* — *teiku*.

## Kal Vachomer Column

### Kal Vachomer → Hekkesh

The *gemara* now takes up the third column: derivations that begin with a *kal vachomer*. The first question is whether a law derived via *kal vachomer* may then become the basis for a *hekkesh*.

### Kal Vachomer → Gezeirah…

Simultaneously, the *gemara* addresses the second combination: *kal vachomer* followed by *gezeirah shavah*. Both are argued together through the same logical framework.

### Strength Comparison

The argument for *kal vachomer* to *hekkesh* proceeds as follows. We know that a *gezeirah shavah* cannot build on a *hekkesh* — that was established by Rabbi Yochanan from the case of *chatas* and *asham*. Yet even *gezeirah shavah*, despite this weakness, can when it is the *first* step support a *hekkesh* as the second step — that is Rav Pappa's position. A *kal vachomer* is demonstrably stronger than a *gezeirah shavah* as a second step, because a *kal vachomer* can build on a *hekkesh* whereas a *gezeirah shavah* cannot. If the weaker *gezeirah shavah* can, as a first step, support a *hekkesh* second step, then certainly the stronger *kal vachomer* — as a first step — can support a *hekkesh* second step.

### Buildability Logic

Similarly, for *kal vachomer* to *gezeirah shavah*: we know a *gezeirah shavah* cannot build on a *hekkesh*, but it can build on itself (*gezeirah shavah* to *gezeirah shavah*, from Rami bar Chama). Meanwhile, a *kal vachomer* is stronger than a *gezeirah shavah* because it can build on a *hekkesh* (from *Tanna D'vei Rabbi Yishmael*) while a *gezeirah shavah* cannot. If a *gezeirah shavah* — even the weaker method — can generate a further *gezeirah shavah*, then certainly a *kal vachomer* — the stronger method — can generate a *gezeirah shavah*.

### Rav Pappa Dependency

The *gemara* qualifies: the argument for *kal vachomer* to *hekkesh* depends on Rav Pappa's position. If one accepts that *gezeirah shavah* followed by *hekkesh* works, then *kal vachomer* (being stronger) certainly supports *hekkesh* as well. But if one rejects Rav Pappa — for instance, on the grounds that his proof case involved *ma'aser sheni*, which is *chullin*, and thus the restrictions of *kodshim* were never engaged — then we have no established instance of *gezeirah shavah* followed by *hekkesh* within *kodshim*, and the argument for *kal vachomer* followed by *hekkesh* also collapses. In that case, *teiku*.

### Kal Vachomer → Kal Vacho…

The third combination in the *kal vachomer* column is *kal vachomer* followed by *kal vachomer*. The *gemara* attempts to prove this is permitted.

### First Proof Attempt

The initial argument: we have shown that *gezeirah shavah* is a weaker method than *kal vachomer*, because a *gezeirah shavah* cannot build on a *hekkesh* while a *kal vachomer* can. Yet even *gezeirah shavah*, the weaker method, can support a *kal vachomer* as a second step — we established this earlier using a *kal vachomer* argument. If the weaker *gezeirah shavah* can support a *kal vachomer*, then certainly the stronger *kal vachomer* can support a *kal vachomer*.

### Circularity Problem

The *gemara* objects sharply: *v'zehu kal vachomer ben kal vachomer* — this argument is itself a *kal vachomer*, and moreover it is *ben beno shel kal vachomer* — the grandson of a *kal vachomer*. The problem is recursive. We are trying to establish whether *kal vachomer* can build on *kal vachomer*. The proof we offer is itself a *kal vachomer* — and the premise of that *kal vachomer* (that *gezeirah shavah* can support *kal vachomer*) was itself established by a *kal vachomer* in the earlier discussion. So we are using a *kal vachomer*, itself derived from a prior *kal vachomer*, to prove that *kal vachomer* can be derived from *kal vachomer*. The argument begs the question it is meant to answer.

It is worth noting that in the common *yeshiva* parlance, calling something a *kal vachomer ben beno shel kal vachomer* expresses that it is the most obvious possible inference — the "granddaddy" of logical arguments. But in its technical *talmudic* usage here, the phrase carries the opposite valence: it signals that the argument is derivative to the point of circularity.

### Non-Circular Proof

The *gemara* therefore constructs a non-circular proof. The established, uncontroversial facts — derived without any *kal vachomer* reasoning — are: *hekkesh* cannot build on *hekkesh* (*hekkesh min hahekkesh*), and yet *hekkesh* can support *kal vachomer* (from *Tanna D'vei Rabbi Yishmael*). These two data points are established from Tannaitic tradition directly, without any *kal vachomer* argument. From these two facts, we observe that *kal vachomer* is strong enough to be generated even from a weak foundation (a *hekkesh* that cannot even support another *hekkesh*). Now *kal vachomer* is a stronger foundation than *hekkesh* — we have already demonstrated this. Therefore, if the weaker *hekkesh* can generate a *kal vachomer*, certainly the stronger *kal vachomer* can generate a *kal vachomer*. This *kal vachomer* argument is genuinely first-order — it does not rely on any previously derived *kal vachomer* — and the *gemara* accepts it.

### Kal Vachomer → Binyan Av

The final combination in the *kal vachomer* column is *kal vachomer* followed by *binyan av*. Rav Yirmiah introduces a proof from the laws of *tum'ah* of birds.

### Melikat Tereifah Case

The case: a bird upon which *melikat* (*malikat*) was performed — the method of slaughter for bird-offerings, whereby the kohen cuts the back of the neck — and it was subsequently found to be a *tereifah* (mortally wounded prior to the act). Does such a bird convey *tum'ah* when swallowed (*beit ha-beli'ah*), like a *nevela*, or is it *tahor* like a properly slaughtered *tereifah* of a non-bird animal?

Rebbi Meir rules it is *tahor* and does not convey *tum'ah*. Rebbi Yehuda rules it does convey *tum'ah* — and indeed, Rebbi Yehuda holds that even an ordinary *shechitah* of a *tereifah* bird leaves it with *tum'ah*.

### Behemah Comparison

Rebbi Meir builds his case with a *kal vachomer*: a *nevela* of a *behemah* (domestic animal) is stringent — it conveys *tum'ah* both through touching and through carrying. Yet *shechitah* of that animal, even if the animal is a *tereifah*, removes it from *tum'ah*. A *nevela* of a bird (*of*) is comparatively lenient — it conveys *tum'ah* only through swallowing, not through touching or carrying. Is it not a *kal vachomer* that if *shechitah* removes *tum'ah* from the stringent *behemah*, it certainly removes *tum'ah* from the more lenient bird? This establishes that *shechitah* of a *tereifah* bird renders it *tahor*.

### Melikat Application

Building on that *kal vachomer*, Rebbi Meir then applies a *binyan av*: just as *shechitah*, which renders a bird fit for eating (*makhsheret be'akhilatah*), also purifies it from *tum'ah* (*metaheret tereifatah*) — so too *melikat*, which renders a bird-offering fit for consumption (*makhsheret me'akhilah*), should likewise purify it from *tum'ah*. This is a *mah matzinu* — a paradigm built directly on the *kal vachomer* that established *shechitah*'s purifying effect.

This appears to be a clean example of *kal vachomer* followed by *binyan av* — exactly what we want to establish.

### Chulin Caveat

The *gemara* declines to accept this as a proof: *v'lo hi* — this is not a demonstration of the rule for *kodshim*. There it is clear that the derivation starts from *chullin* — the *behemah* and the bird are both *chullin* cases. The *kal vachomer* begins entirely outside the domain of *kodshim*. When the derivation originates in *chullin*, the restrictions that govern chained derivations within *kodshim* never come into play. We therefore still do not know whether, within *kodshim* proper, a *kal vachomer* can serve as the foundation for a *binyan av*. The fourth combination in the *kal vachomer* column — like the fourth combination in the *gezeirah shavah* column — is left without resolution.

The *gemara* will address the final column — *binyan av* as the first step — in the following daf.
