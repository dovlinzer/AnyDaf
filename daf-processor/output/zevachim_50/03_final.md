# Zevachim 50 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Derivation Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ, אֵין חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד מֵהֶיקֵּשׁ – אִי מִדְּרָבָא, אִי מִדְּרָבִינָא. דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ, מַהוּ שֶׁיְּלַמֵּד בִּגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה?
>
> **Translation:** **GEMARA:** The principle that **a matter derived via a juxtaposition cannot then teach** its *halakha* **via a juxtaposition** is indicated **either from** the proof **of Rava or from** the proof **of Ravina,** both cited earlier. The Gemara asks: **What is** the *halakha* with regard to whether **a matter derived via a juxtaposition can** then **teach** its *halakha* to another matter **via a verbal analogy?**

### Me'akev Principle

The daf opens in the middle of a technically demanding section of the *gemara* concerning the parameters of derivation in the laws of *kodshim*. The organizing question is: what are the rules governing how we apply the thirteen hermeneutical principles when learning *halachos* of sacrificial offerings?

The first unusual feature of *kodshim* is the principle of *me'akev*. Normally, when the Torah specifies details for the performance of a *mitzvah*, we assume all those details are essential — failure to observe them invalidates the act. By *kodshim*, however, the presumption runs in the opposite direction: a detail is considered only *lemitzvah*, a preferred manner of performance, unless the Torah explicitly signals that it is *me'akev*.

### Shina Lo Hakatuv

The mechanism by which a detail is elevated to *me'akev* status is called *shina lo hakatuv le'akeiv* — the text must repeat or otherwise underscore the requirement in a way that marks it as essential. This is a surprising departure from the usual rules of *psak*, and the *mesora* of Chazal understood it to be necessary precisely because the laws of *korbanot* contain so many details that it would be untenable to treat all of them as absolutely indispensable.

### Learning Across Cases

A further complexity is that the Torah does not spell out every detail for every *korban*. The many offerings described in the Torah each have some details elaborated and others left implicit. This creates pressure to learn details from one *korban* and apply them to another through the standard hermeneutical tools — *hekkesh*, *gezeirah shavah*, *binyan av*, and *kal vachomer* — so that the requirements can be established across the full range of sacrifices.

### Lemadim Lamad Milamad

The foundational principle governing this enterprise was stated by Rabbi Yochanan: *bechol hatorah kulo lemadim lamad milamad* — throughout the entire Torah, one may derive from a derivation. If we learn a law for case A from case B, we may then take what we know about B and apply it to case C. This transitive principle is the baseline from which the *gemara* then carves out specific exceptions for *kodshim*.

### Hekkesh Min Hahekkesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ, אֵין חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד מֵהֶיקֵּשׁ – אִי מִדְּרָבָא, אִי מִדְּרָבִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **a matter derived via a juxtaposition cannot then teach** its *halakha* **via a juxtaposition** is indicated **either from** the proof **of Rava or from** the proof **of Ravina**

The first limitation: *davar halamad behekkesh ein chozer umelamed behekkesh* — a law derived by *hekkesh* cannot itself serve as the source for a further *hekkesh*. A *hekkesh* is a linkage the Torah creates by placing two cases side by side or explicitly equating them, such that we treat them identically in some respect. While the general principle of *lemadim lamad milamad* permits derivation from derivation, the *hekkesh* is considered too weak a foundation to support a second *hekkesh*.

### Tzafon Example

The concrete illustration: we know that the *chatas* must be slaughtered in the north (*tzafon*) of the altar because the Torah links it to the *olah* — *bimkom asher tishachat ha'olah tishachat hachatat*. That linkage is a *hekkesh*. Now the *chatas* and the *asham* are also related. Could we derive that the *asham* requires *tzafon* by building a second *hekkesh* on the first? The answer is no — *hekkesh min hahekkesh* is not permitted. The Torah had to state *tzafon* for the *asham* explicitly.

### Gezeirah Shavah Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, רַבִּי נָתָן בֶּן אַבְטוּלְמוֹס אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לִפְרִיחָה בִּבְגָדִים שֶׁהִיא טְהוֹרָה? נֶאֱמַר קָרַחַת וְגַבַּחַת בַּבְּגָדִים, וְנֶאֱמַר קָרַחַת וְגַבַּחַת בָּאָדָם; מָה לְהַלָּן – פָּרַח בְּכוּלּוֹ טָהוֹר, אַף כָּאן – פָּרַח בְּכוּלּוֹ טָהוֹר. וְהָתָם מְנָא לַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״מֵרֹאשׁוֹ וְעַד רַגְלָיו״ – וְאִיתַּקַּשׁ רֹאשׁוֹ לְרַגְלָיו; מָה לְהַלָּן, כּוּלּוֹ הָפַךְ לָבָן פֶּרַח בְּכוּלּוֹ – טָהוֹר; אַף כָּאן, כּוּלּוֹ הָפַךְ לָבָן פָּרַח בְּכוּלּוֹ – טָהוֹר. לָאו לְמֵימְרָא דְּדָבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ אֵין חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד בִּגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: **Rabbi Natan ben Avtolemos says: From where** is it derived **that** if leprosy **of garments spreads** throughout an entire garment **that it is pure?** It is derived via a verbal analogy: **A bareness within [*karaḥat*] and a bareness without [*gabbaḥat*]** are **stated with regard to** leprosy of **garments:** "And the priest shall look, after that the mark is washed; and behold, if the mark has not changed its color, and the mark has not spread, it is impure; you shall burn it in the fire; it is a fret, whether the bareness be within or without" (Leviticus 13:55); **and a bald head [*karaḥat*] and a bald forehead [*gabbaḥat*]** are **stated with regard to** leprosy of **a person:** "But if there be in the bald head, or the bald forehead, a reddish-white plague, it is leprosy breaking out in his bald head, or his bald forehead" (Leviticus 13:42). **Just as there,** with regard to a person, if the leprosy **spread throughout him** entirely he is **pure,** as the verse states: "Then the priest shall look; and behold, if the leprosy has covered all of his flesh, he shall pronounce the one who has the mark pure; it is all turned white: He is pure" (Leviticus 13:13), **so too here** with regard to garments, if the leprosy **spread throughout the entire** garment it is **pure.** The Gemara continues its proof: **But there,** with regard to the head, which serves as the source for this verbal analogy, **from where do we** derive that if the leprosy spreads throughout the head he is pure? **As it is written:** "And if the leprosy breaks out on the skin, and the leprosy covers all the skin of the one who has the mark, **from his head to his feet,** as far as it appears to the priest" (Leviticus 13:12). **And** the verse thereby **juxtaposes** leprosy on **his head to** leprosy on his **foot,** teaching the following *halakha*: **Just as there,** with regard to leprosy of the body and foot, if **its entirety turned white,** and it **spread all over him,** he is **pure, so too here,** in the case of leprosy of the head, if **its entirety turned white and it spread over all his** head, he is **pure.** Evidently, a matter derived via a juxtaposition can then teach its *halakha* to another matter via a verbal analogy. **Is this not to say that a matter derived via a juxtaposition cannot then teach** its *halakha* to another matter **via a verbal analogy?**

A second limitation was established at the close of the previous daf: one may not build a *gezeirah shavah* on a *hekkesh*. The *gezeirah shavah* of *kodesh kodashim* — the shared phrase appearing in the verses describing both the *chatas* and the *asham* — could theoretically have been used to derive *tzafon* for the *asham* from the *chatas* (which itself learned *tzafon* via *hekkesh* from the *olah*). But we do not do this. The Torah needed to state *tzafon* explicitly by the *asham*, establishing that a *gezeirah shavah* cannot be built on a *hekkesh*.

### 4x4 Matrix

With these two prohibitions established, the *gemara* now systematically works through all the remaining combinations. The four derivation methods — *hekkesh*, *gezeirah shavah*, *binyan av*, and *kal vachomer* — can each serve as either the first step (A to B) or the second step (B to C). This yields a four-by-four matrix of sixteen possible combinations, and the *gemara* will determine, one by one, which are permitted and which are not.

## Hekkesh Column

### Hekkesh → Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד מֵהֶיקֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** is apparent **from that which the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught,** discussed in the previous chapter (41a).

The first combination examined is *hekkesh* followed by *kal vachomer*: if a law is derived by *hekkesh*, may that derived law then serve as the basis for a *kal vachomer*? The answer is yes.

### Rabbi Yishmael Evidence

The proof comes from the *Tanna D'vei Rabbi Yishmael*, cited in an earlier chapter. The *gemara* there applied certain *halachos* known from an inner *chatas* — involving blood-sprinkling and immersion — to the *par* of *Yom Kippur*. The mechanism used was, first, a *hekkesh* linking the *par he'elam davar* to the *par* of the *kohen mashiach*, and then a *kal vachomer* extending the law to the *par* of *Yom Kippur*. This Tannaitic usage demonstrates that *hekkesh* followed by *kal vachomer* is an accepted mode of derivation in *kodshim*.

It is important to note the nature of this type of evidence: we are not deriving these rules logically from *pesukim*. Rather, we are drawing on Tannaitic tradition, finding actual usages that demonstrate which derivation chains were employed by the *rishonim* of the oral tradition.

### Hekkesh → Binyan Av

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ, מַהוּ שֶׁיְּלַמֵּד בְּבִנְיַן אָב? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: לָא לִכְתּוֹב צָפוֹנָה בְּאָשָׁם, וְתֵיתֵי מִבִּנְיַן אָב מֵחַטָּאת; לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא כַּתְבֵיהּ? לָאו לְמֵימְרָא דְּדָבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ אֵין חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד בְּבִנְיַן אָב?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *halakha* as to whether **a matter derived via a juxtaposition** can **teach** its *halakha* in another context **via a paradigm? Rabbi Yirmeya says:** It must be that it cannot, as, if it could, **let** the Torah **not write** the requirement to slaughter an offering in the **north** of the Temple courtyard **with regard to a guilt offering** (see Leviticus 14:13), **and** one can **derive** it **via a paradigm from** the *halakha* of **a sin offering,** since they are both offerings of the most sacred order. **For what *halakha* did** the verse **write it** with regard to a guilt offering as well? Is this **not to say that a matter derived via a juxtaposition cannot then teach** its *halakha* in another context **via a paradigm?**

The fourth combination in the *hekkesh* column — *hekkesh* followed by *binyan av* — raises a harder question. A *binyan av* is a paradigm: if a principle is true of a representative case, it is extended by analogy to all sufficiently similar cases. The question is whether a law first derived via *hekkesh* can then serve as the founding case of such a paradigm.

### Asham Tzafon Rule

Rabbi Yirmiah addresses this. We have established that *tzafon* applies to the *chatas* via its *hekkesh* to the *olah*. Why could we not then extend *tzafon* to the *asham* by *binyan av* — reasoning that both are *kodshei kodashim* and the *chatas* establishes the paradigm? The very fact that the Torah had to state *tzafon* explicitly by the *asham* appears to demonstrate that one may not build a *binyan av* on a *hekkesh*. That is the intended proof.

### Binyan Av Critique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, תֵּיתֵי מִבִּנְיַן אָב מֵעוֹלָה! מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָתֵי – מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְעוֹלָה שֶׁכֵּן כָּלִיל; חַטָּאת נָמֵי אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְחַטָּאת שֶׁכֵּן מְכַפֶּרֶת עַל חַיָּיבֵי כָרֵיתוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this proof: **But according to your reasoning,** why not **derive** the *halakha* that a guilt offering must be slaughtered in the north **from** the *halakha* of **a burnt offering, via a paradigm? What is the reason** that this *halakha* **is not derived** from there? It must be **because it can be refuted** with the following claim: **What** is notable **about a burnt offering?** It is notable **in that** the Torah teaches that **it is entirely** burned on the altar, which is not so with regard to a guilt offering. The Gemara now states its objection: In that case, the derivation from **a sin offering** via a paradigm **can also be refuted: What** is notable **about a sin offering?** It is notable **in that it atones for** those sins **liable for** punishment by ***karet*,** which is not so with regard to a guilt offering. If so, there is no proof from here.

The *gemara* is unsatisfied. If the logic were simply "*binyan av* can extend *tzafon* from *chatas* to *asham*," why not also extend it directly from the *olah*? The *olah* has *tzafon* stated explicitly in the Torah without any derivation at all. Why did we need to say it by *chatas* and then by *asham*, rather than building the *binyan av* directly from *olah*?

### Category Differences

The answer is that the *binyan av* fails not because it was built on a *hekkesh*, but because the categories are intrinsically too different from one another to support the analogy. An *olah* is *kalil* — entirely consumed on the altar — unlike the *chatas* and *asham*. A *chatas* achieves atonement for sins punishable by *kares*, making it categorically weightier than an *asham*, which generally applies to lighter obligations. Each *korban* is distinctive in a way that blocks the *binyan av*. The fact that the Torah had to state *tzafon* explicitly for all three therefore proves nothing specifically about the interaction of *hekkesh* and *binyan av*.

### Binyan Av Intrinsic Issue

This conclusion is crucial: the failure of the *binyan av* here is intrinsic — the dissimilarities between *olah*, *chatas*, and *asham* independently undermine the analogy, regardless of how *tzafon* was derived for the *chatas* in the first place. We therefore cannot use this case as proof that *binyan av* cannot be built on *hekkesh*.

### Three-Way Paradigm

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲדָא מֵחֲדָא – לָא אָתְיָא; תֵּיתֵי חֲדָא מִתַּרְתֵּי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests an alternative paradigm: Although the requirement to slaughter **one** of these three offerings, i.e., a burnt offering, sin offering, or guilt offering, in the north **cannot be derived from** any **one** of the others, one can **derive** the *halakha* concerning **one** of them **from** the other **two.**

The *gemara* then asks a subtler question: even if a single-source *binyan av* fails because of the dissimilarities, perhaps a two-source *binyan av* — using two of the three as the founding paradigm — could overcome those objections and apply to the third. If two cases share a common feature, the combination might neutralize what would otherwise be a *pircha* (refutation) of the analogy.

### Pairwise Distinctions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵהֵי תֵּיתֵי? לָא נִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא עוֹלָה, וְתֵיתֵי מֵחַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם? מָה לְהָנָךְ שֶׁכֵּן מְכַפְּרִין. לָא נִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא בְּחַטָּאת, וְתֵיתֵי מֵהָנָךְ? מָה לְהָנָךְ שֶׁכֵּן זְכָרִים. לָא נִכְתּוֹב בְּאָשָׁם, וְתֵיתֵי מֵהָנָךְ? מָה לְהָנָךְ שֶׁכֵּן יֶשְׁנָן בְּצִיבּוּר כִּבְיָחִיד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **From which** two could one **derive** the third? **Let the Merciful One not write** the requirement to slaughter the offering in the north with regard to **a burnt offering, and derive** this *halakha* **from** the *halakhot* of **a sin offering and a guilt offering.** The Gemara refutes this derivation: **What** is notable **about these** two offerings? They are notable **in that they atone** for transgressions, whereas a burnt offering does not atone for any sin. The Gemara suggests: **Let the Merciful One not write** the requirement to slaughter an offering in the north **with regard to a sin offering, and derive** this *halakha* **from** the *halakhot* of **these** others, the burnt offering and the guilt offering. The Gemara refutes this derivation: **What** is notable **about these** two offerings? They are notable **in that** they are brought only from **male** animals, whereas an individual's sin offering is a female animal. The Gemara suggests: **Let the Merciful One not write** the requirement to slaughter an offering in the north **with regard to a guilt offering, and derive** this *halakha* **from** the *halakhot* of **these** other two. The Gemara refutes this derivation: **What** is notable **about these** two offerings? They are notable **in that they are** brought **by a community just as** they are brought **by an individual,** whereas a guilt offering can be brought only by an individual.

The *gemara* works through each possible pairing. Could the *olah* and *chatas* together establish a paradigm for the *asham*? No — both an *olah* and a *chatas* require the Torah to state *tzafon* explicitly, and the *olah* is set apart because it is entirely burned (*kalil*), while the *chatas* is set apart because it atones for sins of *kares*. Could the *chatas* and *asham* establish a paradigm for the *olah*? No — both achieve atonement, but the *olah* does not. Could the *olah* and *asham* establish a paradigm for the *chatas*? No — both are male animals (*zecharim*), while a *chatas* is typically a female. Could the *olah* and *chatas* be used because one appears as a communal offering and one as an individual offering? Every attempted pairing yields a distinguishing feature that would undermine the *binyan av*.

The result is that there was no way to establish *tzafon* for all three by derivation — each required explicit scriptural statement. Consequently, this case provides no proof one way or the other regarding whether a *binyan av* can be built on a *hekkesh*. The question remains unresolved. It is worth noting, as an observation about the internal logic of the system, that *kal vachomer* — which succeeded where *binyan av* is uncertain — is conceptually stronger: it does not merely assert similarity but argues from the logic of greater and lesser stringency. The uncertainty about *binyan av*, by contrast, tracks the fact that it is fundamentally a pattern-matching exercise, and pattern-matching is more easily disrupted by categorical distinctions.

## Gezeirah Shavah Column

### Todah from Ma'aser

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בִּגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה, מַהוּ שֶׁיְּלַמֵּד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: ״וְזֹאת תּוֹרַת זֶבַח הַשְּׁלָמִים [וְגוֹ׳] אִם עַל תּוֹדָה״ – לָמַדְנוּ לְתוֹדָה שֶׁבָּא מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר, מִדְּאַשְׁכְּחַן שְׁלָמִים דְּאָתוּ מִמַּעֲשֵׂר. שְׁלָמִים גּוּפַיְיהוּ מְנָא לַן? דִּכְתִיב ״שָׁם״–״שָׁם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to its discussion of hermeneutical principles. **What is** the *halakha* as to whether **a matter derived via a verbal analogy** can **teach** its *halakha* to another matter **via a juxtaposition? Rav Pappa says:** The verse states: **"And this is the law of the sacrifice of peace offerings,** which one may offer to the Lord. **If** he sacrifices it **for a thanks offering,** then he shall offer with the thanks offering unleavened cakes mingled with oil, and unleavened wafers spread with oil, and cakes mingled with oil, of fine flour soaked" (Leviticus 7:11–12). From this juxtaposition **we have learned that** the money to purchase **a thanks offering** can **come from** money used to redeem **the** second **tithe, since we find that** the money to purchase **a peace offering** can **come from** money used to redeem the second **tithe.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that the money to purchase **peace offerings themselves** can come from money used to redeem the second tithe? **As it is written** with regard to peace offerings: "And you shall sacrifice peace offerings and shall eat **there"** (Deuteronomy 27:7). And it is written with regard to second tithe: "And you shall eat before the Lord your God, in the place which He shall choose to cause His name to dwell **there"** (Deuteronomy 14:23). The usage of the term "there" in both passages serves as a verbal analogy, and demonstrates that a *halakha* derived via a verbal analogy, that of a peace offering, can then teach a *halakha* via its juxtaposition to a thanks offering.

The *gemara* now turns to the second column: derivations that begin with a *gezeirah shavah*. The first question is whether a law derived by *gezeirah shavah* can then serve as the basis for a *hekkesh*. Rav Pappa advances a proof from the case of a *todah* brought from *ma'aser sheni* funds.

The principle at stake is that when one redeems *ma'aser sheni* produce onto money and then spends that money in Jerusalem, one may use it to purchase an offering whose meat one eats. A *todah* — a thanksgiving offering — may be brought with such money, provided the person specified at the time of the vow that the offering would come from *ma'aser sheni* funds.

### Sham Sham Connection

The derivation works in two steps. We know that *shlamim* may be purchased with *ma'aser sheni* money. How do we know that? From a *gezeirah shavah* of *sham sham*: the verse describing *shlamim* uses the word *sham* ("there"), as does the verse about *ma'aser sheni* consumption — *ve'achalta sham* by *shlamim* and *ve'achalta lifnei Hashem Elokecha bamakom asher yivchar leshaken shemo sham* by *ma'aser sheni*. This shared word links the two contexts and establishes that *shlamim* may come from *ma'aser sheni* funds (A to B via *gezeirah shavah*).

From there, the Torah's statement *vezot torat zevach hashlamim im al todah yakrivennu* — "this is the law of the *shlamim* if he brings it as a *todah*" — links the *todah* to the *shlamim*. This linkage (B to C via *hekkesh*) allows us to apply what is true of *shlamim* to the *todah* as well.

It is worth noting that calling the second step a *hekkesh* might seem overstated. When the Torah says "this is the law of the *shlamim* if he brings it as a *todah*," it appears to state directly that a *todah* is a species of *shlamim*, rather than merely linking the two by implication. Similarly, the statement *bimkom asher tishachat ha'olah tishachat hachatat* looks more like an explicit instruction than a *hekkesh*. Nevertheless, the *gemara* treats any move from B to C — even when fairly direct — as a *hekkesh* for purposes of this analysis.

### Rav Pappa Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב מָרִי לְרָבִינָא: מַעְשַׂר דָּגָן חוּלִּין בְּעָלְמָא הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra, son of Rav Mari, said to Ravina:** This is not a proof, because the **tithe of grain,** including the second tithe, **is merely non-sacred.** Therefore, it cannot be used to prove that in the realm of consecrated matters a *halakha* derived via a verbal analogy can teach a *halakha* via a juxtaposition.

Rav Pappa's proof establishes that *gezeirah shavah* followed by *hekkesh* is a valid derivation chain. However, the *gemara* does not let the matter rest.

### Chulin Source Issue

Mar Zutra bar Rav Mari challenges Rav Pappa: *ma'aser dagan chullin b'alma hu* — grain that is *ma'aser sheni* is, fundamentally, *chullin*. Even according to those who hold that *ma'aser sheni* has a degree of sanctity, it is certainly not in the category of *kodshim*. If the starting point of the derivation — the A in the A-to-B *gezeirah shavah* — is *chullin*, then the restrictions on chained derivations within *kodshim* simply do not apply. The case of *sham sham* tells us nothing about whether *gezeirah shavah* followed by *hekkesh* is permitted when the entire chain operates within the domain of *kodshim*.

### Restrictions Apply

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, אָמַר אָמְרַהּ: לָמֵד קֹדֶשׁ וּמְלַמֵּד קֹדֶשׁ?!
>
> **Translation:** Ravina **said to him:** Does the one who **said** that in the realm of consecrated matters a *halakha* derived via one of the hermeneutical principles cannot teach via another of those principles **say this** only when the *halakha* that is **derived** is in the realm of **consecrated** matters **and** the *halakha* that **teaches** is in the realm of **consecrated** matters as well? He holds that even if that which is derived is in the realm of consecrated matters it cannot teach its *halakha* via one of the hermeneutical principles. Therefore, since peace offerings are in the realm of consecrated matters, the *halakha* derived from the juxtaposition of thanks offerings to peace offerings serves as a source that a matter derived via a verbal analogy can teach its *halakha* to another matter via a juxtaposition.

Rav Pappa responds: the restrictions are not determined by where the derivation begins — the *melamed*, the source — but by what is being derived — the *lamed*, the application. Since the *shlamim* and *todah* (B and C) are *kodshim*, the relevant restrictions do apply, and nevertheless Rav Pappa holds the derivation is valid.

### Source vs Application

Rashi makes explicit the dispute implicit in this exchange. Rav Pappa holds *d'batar lamed azlinan* — we follow the derived case; so long as what is being derived is *kodshim*, the restrictions apply and must be reckoned with. Mar Zutra's implicit counter-position is *d'batar melamed azlinan* — we follow the source; if the original teaching is *chullin*, the restrictions of *kodshim* do not engage at all. This debate about whether the restrictions track the *melamed* or the *lamed* is embedded in, and clarified by, the passage before us.

### Gezeirah Shavah Chain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בִּגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה, מַהוּ שֶׁיְּלַמֵּד בִּגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה? אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא, תַּנְיָא: ״סֹלֶת מֻרְבֶּכֶת״ – לָמַדְנוּ לִרְבוּכָה שֶׁבָּאָה סוֹלֶת. חַלּוֹת מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״חַלּוֹת״–״חַלּוֹת״. רְקִיקִין מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מַצּוֹת״–״מַצּוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *halakha* as to whether **a matter derived via a verbal analogy** can **teach** its *halakha* to another matter **via** another **verbal analogy? Rami bar Ḥama says: It is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the types of bread that accompany a thanks offering: "If he sacrifices it for a thanks offering, then he shall sacrifice with the thanks offering unleavened loaves mingled with oil, and unleavened wafers spread with oil, and loaves mingled with oil, of **fine flour cooked** with oil" (Leviticus 7:12). **We have derived** from here **that** the **poached** breads accompanying the thanks offering **come from fine flour.** The *baraita* continues: **From where** is it derived that the **loaves** accompanying a thanks offering are also prepared with fine flour? **The verse states: "Loaves** mingled with oil, of fine flour cooked with oil" (Leviticus 7:12), and it also states in the same verse: "Unleavened **loaves** mingled with oil." **From where** is it derived that the unleavened **wafers** accompanying a thanks offering are also prepared with fine flour? **The verse states: "Unleavened** cakes mingled with oil" (Leviticus 7:12), and the verse also states: **"Unleavened** wafers spread with oil." The *baraita* derives via a verbal analogy that the unleavened loaves mingled with oil are prepared with fine flour, and then teaches the *halakha* concerning unleavened wafers using a verbal analogy from the *halakha* of unleavened loaves.

The *gemara* now turns to the second column: derivations that begin with a *gezeirah shavah*. The first question is whether a law derived by *gezeirah shavah* can then serve as the basis for a *hekkesh*. The proof comes from the case of a *todah* brought from *ma'aser sheni* funds, as discussed above. However, the *gemara* challenges this proof on the grounds that *ma'aser sheni* is *chullin*, and therefore the restrictions of *kodshim* never come into play.

Instead, the *gemara* continues with the question of whether a law derived by *gezeirah shavah* can then serve as the basis for another *gezeirah shavah*. Rami bar Chama cites a *braita* that appears to demonstrate exactly this.

### Bread Types Case

The *braita* concerns the breads that accompany a *todah*. A *todah* is brought with forty loaves: ten *chalos* (unleavened loaves), ten *rekikin* (thin wafers), ten *revuchah* (loaves prepared by first cooking in water and then frying), and ten *chamotz* (leavened loaves). The question is how we know that the *chalos* and the *rekikin* — in addition to the *revuchah* — must be made from *soles* (fine flour).

The *braita* teaches: for the *revuchah*, the requirement of *soles* is stated explicitly in the Torah. For the *chalos*, we derive it by a *gezeirah shavah* of *chalos chalos* — the word *chalos* appears both in the context of the *revuchah* and in the context of the standard *chalos*, establishing that the latter must also use *soles*. For the *rekikin*, we derive it by a *gezeirah shavah* of *matzos matzos* — the word *matzos* appears in the context of the *chalos* and in the context of the *rekikin*. Thus: *revuchah* to *chalos* via *gezeirah shavah* (*chalos chalos*), and *chalos* to *rekikin* via *gezeirah shavah* (*matzos matzos*) — a clear chain of *gezeirah shavah* upon *gezeirah shavah*.

### Direct Source Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא: מִמַּאי דְּ״מַצּוֹת״–״מַצּוֹת״ מֵחַלּוֹת גָּמַר? דִּלְמָא מִמַּאֲפֵה תַנּוּר גָּמַר!
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to** Rami bar Ḥama: **From where** do you know **that** the *tanna* of the *baraita* **learns** the verbal analogy of **"unleavened"** and **"unleavened" from loaves** of the thanks offering? **Perhaps** the *tanna* **learns** the verbal analogy **from** the word "unleavened" stated with regard to meal offerings **baked in an oven,** about which the verse states explicitly that they are prepared with fine flour: "And when you bring a meal offering baked in the oven, it shall be unleavened cakes of fine flour mingled with oil, or unleavened wafers spread with oil" (Leviticus 2:4). If so, there is no source that a *halakha* derived via a verbal analogy can teach a *halakha* via a verbal analogy.

Ravina challenges this proof. He asks: *mimai d'matzos matzos me-chalos gmar* — how do we know that the *matzos matzos* derivation for the *rekikin* is drawing from the *chalos* (which was itself learned via *gezeirah shavah*)? Perhaps the *matzos* of *rekikin* is derived directly from the *mincha* baked in an oven (*maafeh tanur*) — a *mincha* brought as a *nedavah* — where the Torah explicitly uses the word *matzos* and also specifies *soles*. If so, the *matzos matzos* of *rekikin* is a direct derivation, not a *gezeirah shavah* built on a *gezeirah shavah*, and the *braita* proves nothing about chained *gezeirot shavot*.

### Cutting Up Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא, תַּנְיָא: ״וְקִרְבּוֹ וּפִרְשׁוֹ וְהוֹצִיא״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמּוֹצִיאוֹ שָׁלֵם. יָכוֹל יִשְׂרְפֶנּוּ שָׁלֵם? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן: ״רֹאשׁוֹ וּכְרָעָיו״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן: ״רֹאשׁוֹ וּכְרָעָיו״; מָה לְהַלָּן עַל יְדֵי נִיתּוּחַ, אַף כָּאן עַל יְדֵי נִיתּוּחַ. אִי – מָה לְהַלָּן בְּהֶפְשֵׁט, אַף כָּאן נָמֵי בְּהֶפְשֵׁט?! תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְקִרְבּוֹ וּפִרְשׁוֹ״. מַאי תַּלְמוּדָא? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁפִּרְשׁוֹ בְּקִרְבּוֹ, כָּךְ בְּשָׂרוֹ בְּעוֹרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava says** that the source is from a different *baraita*, as **it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the bull offering of a High Priest: "And the skin of the bull and all its flesh, with its head and with its legs, **and its innards, and its dung,** the whole bull **shall he carry out** of the camp to a pure place, where the ashes are poured out, and burn it on wood with fire" (Leviticus 4:11–12). This **teaches that he brings** the offering **out whole.** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought **that he should burn it whole** as well. **It is stated here: "Its head and its legs," and it is stated there,** with regard to a burnt offering: "And he shall cut it into its pieces; and the priest shall lay them, with **its head** and its fats, in order on the wood that is on the fire which is upon the altar. But the innards **and the legs** shall he wash with water" (Leviticus 1:12–13). **Just as there,** the burnt offering is burned **by means of cutting** the offering into pieces, as explicitly stated in the verse, **so too here,** it is burned **by means of cutting** the offering into pieces. The Gemara clarifies: **If** these are compared, one could say that **just as there,** the burnt offering is prepared for being burned **by** first **flaying** it, **so too here,** the bull offering of a High Priest should **also** be prepared for being burned **by** first **flaying** it. To counter this reasoning, **the verse states: "And its innards and its dung"** (Leviticus 4:11). The Gemara asks: **What is the** biblical **derivation?** How do these words teach that there is no requirement to flay the animal? **Rav Pappa says: Just as the dung** remains **inside** the animal when the animal is burned, **so** the animal's **flesh** remains **inside its skin.** In any event, the *halakha* that the bull offering of a High Priest is cut into pieces before it is burned is derived via a verbal analogy.

Rava therefore proposes an alternative proof. He cites a different *braita* that describes the treatment of the inner *chatas* of the *kohen mashiach* — the *par* that is burned entirely outside the camp. The Torah states *v'kirbo u'firsho v'hotzi* — "its innards and its refuse he shall take out" — and the word *v'hotzi* implies a single act of removal, which in turn implies it is taken out whole rather than in pieces. Yet the Torah also says, by an *olah*, *rosho u'chrao* — "its head and its legs" — using a *gezeirah shavah* of *rosho u'chrao* (stated here by the inner *chatas* and stated there by the *olah*) to teach that the *chatas*, like the *olah*, must be cut into sections (*nitoach*) before burning. So from the *olah* to the *par kohen mashiach* is one *gezeirah shavah*.

Furthermore, the *gemara* notes, the verse also establishes that the *par* is not flayed (*hefshet*) before burning, since *v'kirbo u'firsho* indicates the innards remain enclosed — and just as the innards are not separated from the body cavity, the skin is not separated from the flesh. This is Rav Pappa's explanation of the verse *v'es or ha'par v'es kol besaro al rosho v'al kerav v'kirbo u'firsho v'hotzi* — everything is taken out as a unit, and just as the *pirsho* remains within the *kirbo*, so the skin remains on the flesh.

Then Rebbi, in a second *braita*, applies these same rules to the *par v'seir* of *Yom Kippur*. The verse there also says *or u'vasar u'feresh*, and by *gezeirah shavah* of *or u'vasar u'feresh* (stated here by *Yom Kippur* and there by the *par kohen mashiach*), we learn that the *Yom Kippur par* too is cut into sections but not flayed before burning. This is precisely *gezeirah shavah* built on *gezeirah shavah*: from *olah* to *par kohen mashiach* via one *gezeirah shavah*, and from *par kohen mashiach* to the *par v'seir* of *Yom Kippur* via a second *gezeirah shavah*. Rava's proof is thus established without any of the problems Ravina raised.

### Gezeirah Shavah → Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בִּגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה, מַהוּ שֶׁיְּלַמֵּד בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר? קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה הֶיקֵּשׁ, שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְלַמֵּד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ – אִי מִדְּרָבָא אִי מִדְּרָבִינָא, מְלַמֵּד בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר מִדְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל; גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה, הַמְלַמֶּדֶת בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ מִדְּרַב פָּפָּא, אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁתְּלַמֵּד בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *halakha* as to whether **a matter derived via a verbal analogy** can **teach** its *halakha* to another matter **via an *a fortiori*** inference? The Gemara answers: That question itself can be answered with **an *a fortiori*** inference: **And just as** a matter derived via **a juxtaposition, which cannot** then **teach** its *halakha* **via a juxtaposition,** as proven **either from** the statement **of Rava or from** the statement **of Ravina** (49b), can nevertheless **teach** its *halakha* **via an *a fortiori*** inference, as proven **from** the statement **that the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** (50a), with regard to a matter derived via **a verbal analogy, which** can **teach** its *halakha* **via a juxtaposition,** as proven **from** the statement **of Rav Pappa** (50a), **is it not logical that it should teach** its *halakha* **via an *a fortiori*** inference?

With *gezeirah shavah* to *hekkesh* (debated by Rav Pappa) and *gezeirah shavah* to *gezeirah shavah* (established by Rava) now on the table, the *gemara* turns to the third combination in this column: *gezeirah shavah* followed by *kal vachomer*. May a law derived by *gezeirah shavah* serve as the basis for a *kal vachomer*?

### Strength Argument

The *gemara* employs a logical *kal vachomer* to resolve the question. We know two things: first, a *hekkesh* cannot support a further *hekkesh* (*hekkesh min hahekkesh* is forbidden), yet a *hekkesh* can support a *kal vachomer* (from *Tanna D'vei Rabbi Yishmael*). This shows that *kal vachomer* is strong enough to be built even on a weak foundation. Second, a *gezeirah shavah* is a stronger foundation than a *hekkesh*, because while you cannot build a *hekkesh* on a *hekkesh*, you can build a *gezeirah shavah* on a *gezeirah shavah* and — according to Rav Pappa — even a *hekkesh* on a *gezeirah shavah*. If the weaker foundation (*hekkesh*) permits *kal vachomer*, then certainly the stronger foundation (*gezeirah shavah*) permits *kal vachomer*.

The *gemara* notes a qualification: the first formulation of this argument relied on Rav Pappa's position that *gezeirah shavah* permits *hekkesh*, making *gezeirah shavah* demonstrably stronger than *hekkesh*. If one rejects Rav Pappa's position, that version of the argument fails. The *gemara* therefore offers an independent version: even without invoking Rav Pappa, we know that *hekkesh* cannot build on *hekkesh*, yet *gezeirah shavah* can build on *gezeirah shavah*. The capacity of a method to build on itself demonstrates its relative strength. Since *gezeirah shavah* is stronger than *hekkesh* by this measure, and *hekkesh* can still support a *kal vachomer*, *gezeirah shavah* certainly can.

### Conclusion

The *gemara* accepts this reasoning. Starting from a *gezeirah shavah* and then applying a *kal vachomer* is permitted without reservation.

### Gezeirah Shavah → Binyan Av

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בִּגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה, מַהוּ שֶׁיְּלַמֵּד בְּבִנְיַן אָב? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *halakha* as to whether **a matter derived via a verbal analogy** can **teach** its *halakha* to another matter **via a paradigm?** The question **shall stand** unresolved.

The fourth and final combination in the *gezeirah shavah* column is *gezeirah shavah* followed by *binyan av*. The *gemara* offers no resolution: *teiku*. The question remains open.

To summarize the *gezeirah shavah* column: *gezeirah shavah* to *hekkesh* — Rav Pappa says yes, others dispute; *gezeirah shavah* to *gezeirah shavah* — yes, as proved by Rava; *gezeirah shavah* to *kal vachomer* — yes; *gezeirah shavah* to *binyan av* — *teiku*.

## Kal Vachomer Column

### Kal Vachomer → Hekkesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר, מַהוּ שֶׁיְּלַמֵּד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ? קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה, שֶׁאֵינָהּ לְמֵדָה בְּהֶיקֵּשָׁא מִדְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן – מְלַמֵּד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ מִדְּרַב פָּפָּא; קַל וָחוֹמֶר, הַלָּמֵד מֵהֶיקֵּשׁ מִדְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיְּלַמֵּד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *halakha* as to whether **a matter derived via an *a fortiori*** inference can **teach** its *halakha* to another matter **via a juxtaposition?** The Gemara answers that this itself can be derived via **an *a fortiori*** inference: **And just as** a *halakha* derived via **a verbal analogy, which cannot be derived** from another *halakha* derived **via a juxtaposition,** as proven **from** the statement **of Rabbi Yoḥanan** (49b), can nevertheless **teach** its *halakha* to another matter **via a juxtaposition,** as proven **from** the statement **of Rav Pappa** (50a), with regard to a *halakha* derived via **an *a fortiori*** inference, which **can be derived** from another *halakha* derived **via a juxtaposition,** as proven **from** the statement **that the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** (50a), **is it not logical that it should teach** its *halakha* **via a juxtaposition?**

The *gemara* now takes up the third column: derivations that begin with a *kal vachomer*. The first question is whether a law derived via *kal vachomer* may then become the basis for a *hekkesh*.

### Kal Vachomer → Gezeirah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר, מַהוּ שֶׁיְּלַמֵּד בִּגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה? קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה, שֶׁאֵינָהּ לְמֵידָה בְּהֶיקֵּשָׁא מִדְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן – מְלַמֵּד בִּגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה מִדְּרָמֵי בַּר חָמָא; קַל וָחוֹמֶר, הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ מִדְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁתְּלַמֵּד בִּגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *halakha* as to whether **a matter derived via an *a fortiori*** inference can **teach** its *halakha* to another matter **via a verbal analogy?** The Gemara answers that this itself can be derived via **an *a fortiori*** inference. **And just as** a *halakha* derived via **a verbal analogy, which cannot be derived** from another *halakha* derived **via a juxtaposition,** as proven **from** the statement **of Rabbi Yoḥanan** (49b), can nevertheless **teach** its *halakha* to another matter **via a verbal analogy,** as proven **from** the statement **of Rami bar Ḥama** (50a), with regard to a *halakha* derived via **an *a fortiori*** inference, which **can be derived** from another *halakha* derived **via a juxtaposition,** as proven **from** the statement **that the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** (50a), **is it not logical that it should teach** its *halakha* **via a verbal analogy?**

Simultaneously, the *gemara* addresses the second combination: *kal vachomer* followed by *gezeirah shavah*. Both are argued together through the same logical framework.

### Strength Comparison

The argument for *kal vachomer* to *hekkesh* proceeds as follows. We know that a *gezeirah shavah* cannot build on a *hekkesh* — that was established by Rabbi Yochanan from the case of *chatas* and *asham*. Yet even *gezeirah shavah*, despite this weakness, can when it is the *first* step support a *hekkesh* as the second step — that is Rav Pappa's position. A *kal vachomer* is demonstrably stronger than a *gezeirah shavah* as a second step, because a *kal vachomer* can build on a *hekkesh* whereas a *gezeirah shavah* cannot. If the weaker *gezeirah shavah* can, as a first step, support a *hekkesh* second step, then certainly the stronger *kal vachomer* — as a first step — can support a *hekkesh* second step.

### Buildability Logic

Similarly, for *kal vachomer* to *gezeirah shavah*: we know a *gezeirah shavah* cannot build on a *hekkesh*, but it can build on itself (*gezeirah shavah* to *gezeirah shavah*, from Rami bar Chama). Meanwhile, a *kal vachomer* is stronger than a *gezeirah shavah* because it can build on a *hekkesh* (from *Tanna D'vei Rabbi Yishmael*) while a *gezeirah shavah* cannot. If a *gezeirah shavah* — even the weaker method — can generate a further *gezeirah shavah*, then certainly a *kal vachomer* — the stronger method — can generate a *gezeirah shavah*.

### Rav Pappa Dependency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא – לְמַאן דְּאִית לֵיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא; אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּלֵית לֵיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **This works out well according to the one who accepts** the statement **of Rav Pappa. But according to the one who does not accept** the statement **of Rav Pappa,** i.e., Mar Zutra, son of Rav Mari, **what is there to say?** The Gemara comments: The question **shall stand** unresolved.

The *gemara* qualifies: the argument for *kal vachomer* to *hekkesh* depends on Rav Pappa's position. If one accepts that *gezeirah shavah* followed by *hekkesh* works, then *kal vachomer* (being stronger) certainly supports *hekkesh* as well. But if one rejects Rav Pappa — for instance, on the grounds that his proof case involved *ma'aser sheni*, which is *chullin*, and thus the restrictions of *kodshim* were never engaged — then we have no established instance of *gezeirah shavah* followed by *hekkesh* within *kodshim*, and the argument for *kal vachomer* followed by *hekkesh* also collapses. In that case, *teiku*.

### Kal Vachomer → Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר, מַהוּ שֶׁיְּלַמֵּד בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר? קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה, שֶׁאֵינָהּ לְמֵידָה בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ מִדְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן – מְלַמֵּד בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר כְּדַאֲמַרַן; קַל וָחוֹמֶר, הַלָּמֵד מֵהֶיקֵּשׁ מִדְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיְּלַמֵּד בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר?! וְזֶהוּ קַל וָחוֹמֶר בֶּן קַל וָחוֹמֶר. בֶּן בְּנוֹ שֶׁל קַל וָחוֹמֶר הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *halakha* as to whether **a matter derived via an *a fortiori*** inference can **teach** its *halakha* to another matter **via an *a fortiori*** inference? The Gemara answers that this itself can be derived via **an *a fortiori*** inference. **And just as** a *halakha* derived via **a verbal analogy, which cannot be derived** from another *halakha* derived **via a juxtaposition,** as proven **from** the statement **of Rabbi Yoḥanan** (49b), can nevertheless **teach** its *halakha* to another matter **via an *a fortiori*** inference, **as we have said,** with regard to a *halakha* derived via **an *a fortiori*** inference, which **can be derived** from another *halakha* derived **via a juxtaposition,** as proven **from** the statement **that the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** (50a), **is it not logical that it should teach** its *halakha* **via an *a fortiori*** inference? The Gemara comments: **And this is an *a fortiori*** inference that is the **son of,** i.e., is derived from, another ***a fortiori*** inference. The Gemara challenges this last statement: **It is** not the son of an *a fortiori* inference, but the **grandson of an *a fortiori*** inference, which cannot be used. The very fact that a matter derived via a verbal analogy can then teach its *halakha* via an *a fortiori* inference is itself derived from an *a fortiori* inference, as the Gemara stated.

The third combination in the *kal vachomer* column is *kal vachomer* followed by *kal vachomer*. The *gemara* attempts to prove this is permitted.

### First Proof Attempt

The initial argument: we have shown that *gezeirah shavah* is a weaker method than *kal vachomer*, because a *gezeirah shavah* cannot build on a *hekkesh* while a *kal vachomer* can. Yet even *gezeirah shavah*, the weaker method, can support a *kal vachomer* as a second step — we established this earlier using a *kal vachomer* argument. If the weaker *gezeirah shavah* can support a *kal vachomer*, then certainly the stronger *kal vachomer* can support a *kal vachomer*.

### Circularity Problem

The *gemara* objects sharply: *v'zehu kal vachomer ben kal vachomer* — this argument is itself a *kal vachomer*, and moreover it is *ben beno shel kal vachomer* — the grandson of a *kal vachomer*. The problem is recursive. We are trying to establish whether *kal vachomer* can build on *kal vachomer*. The proof we offer is itself a *kal vachomer* — and the premise of that *kal vachomer* (that *gezeirah shavah* can support *kal vachomer*) was itself established by a *kal vachomer* in the earlier discussion. So we are using a *kal vachomer*, itself derived from a prior *kal vachomer*, to prove that *kal vachomer* can be derived from *kal vachomer*. The argument begs the question it is meant to answer.

It is worth noting that in the common *yeshiva* parlance, calling something a *kal vachomer ben beno shel kal vachomer* expresses that it is the most obvious possible inference — the "granddaddy" of logical arguments. But in its technical *talmudic* usage here, the phrase carries the opposite valence: it signals that the argument is derivative to the point of circularity.

### Non-Circular Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה הֶיקֵּשׁ, שֶׁאֵינוֹ לָמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ – אִי מִדְּרָבָא אִי מִדְּרָבִינָא, מְלַמֵּד בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר מִדְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל; קַל וָחוֹמֶר, הַלָּמֵד מֵהֶיקֵּשׁ מִדְּתָנֵי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיְּלַמֵּד בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר?! וְזֶהוּ קַל וָחוֹמֶר בֶּן קַל וָחוֹמֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the *halakha* as to whether a matter derived via an *a fortiori* inference can teach its *halakha* to another matter via an *a fortiori* inference can be derived via another ***a fortiori*** inference. **And just as** a matter derived via **a juxtaposition, which cannot be derived via a juxtaposition,** as proven **either from** the statement **of Rava or from** the statement **of Ravina** (49b), can nevertheless **teach** its *halakha* **via an *a fortiori*** inference, as proven **from** the statement **that the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** (50a), with regard to a *halakha* derived via **an *a fortiori*** inference, which **can be derived** from another *halakha* derived **via a juxtaposition,** as proven **from** the statement **that the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** (50a), **is it not logical that it should teach** its *halakha* **via an *a fortiori*** inference? The Gemara comments: **And this is an *a fortiori*** inference that is the **son of** another ***a fortiori*** inference.

The *gemara* therefore constructs a non-circular proof. The established, uncontroversial facts — derived without any *kal vachomer* reasoning — are: *hekkesh* cannot build on *hekkesh* (*hekkesh min hahekkesh*), and yet *hekkesh* can support *kal vachomer* (from *Tanna D'vei Rabbi Yishmael*). These two data points are established from Tannaitic tradition directly, without any *kal vachomer* argument. From these two facts, we observe that *kal vachomer* is strong enough to be generated even from a weak foundation (a *hekkesh* that cannot even support another *hekkesh*). Now *kal vachomer* is a stronger foundation than *hekkesh* — we have already demonstrated this. Therefore, if the weaker *hekkesh* can generate a *kal vachomer*, certainly the stronger *kal vachomer* can generate a *kal vachomer*. This *kal vachomer* argument is genuinely first-order — it does not rely on any previously derived *kal vachomer* — and the *gemara* accepts it.

### Kal Vachomer → Binyan Av

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר, מַהוּ שֶׁיְּלַמֵּד בְּבִנְיַן אָב? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה, תָּא שְׁמַע: מָלַק וְנִמְצֵאת טְרֵיפָה, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אֵינָהּ מְטַמְּאָה בְּבֵית הַבְּלִיעָה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מְטַמְּאָה בְּבֵית הַבְּלִיעָה. אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר, קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה נִבְלַת בְּהֵמָה, שֶׁמְּטַמְּאָה בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא – שְׁחִיטָתָהּ מְטַהֶרֶת טְרֵיפָתָהּ מִטּוּמְאָתָהּ; נְבֵילַת עוֹף, שֶׁאֵין מְטַמֵּא בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁתְּהֵא שְׁחִיטָתָהּ מְטַהֶרֶת טְרֵיפָתָהּ מִטּוּמְאָתָהּ?! מָה מָצִינוּ בִּשְׁחִיטָה, שֶׁמַּכְשַׁרְתָּהּ בַּאֲכִילָה –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *halakha* as to whether **a matter derived via an *a fortiori*** inference can **teach** its *halakha* to another matter **via a paradigm? Rabbi Yirmeya says: Come** and **hear** a proof from a mishna (69a–b): If a priest **pinched** the nape of a bird offering properly **and it was** then **found** to be a bird **with a wound that would have caused it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*],** which disqualifies it from being used as an offering and renders it prohibited for consumption by the priests, **Rabbi Meir says:** An olive-bulk of its meat **does not render** one who swallows it **ritually impure** when it is **in the throat,** as is the *halakha* concerning a bird carcass, as the fact that it underwent pinching means that it is not in the category of a carcass. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** Its status is like that of any other carcass of a kosher bird, and its meat **renders** one who swallows it **ritually impure** when it is **in the throat.** The mishna continues: **Rabbi Meir said:** My opinion can be proved via **an *a fortiori*** inference. **And just as** with regard to **the carcass of an animal, which transmits impurity** to a person **through touching** it **and carrying** it, **its slaughter** nevertheless **purifies its *tereifa* from its impurity,** with regard to **the carcass of a bird,** which possesses less severe impurity, **as it does not transmit impurity through touching** it **and carrying** it, but only when it is in the throat, **is it not logical that its slaughter purifies its *tereifa* from its impurity?** And once it is established that slaughter purifies a bird that is a *tereifa*, it can be derived: **Just as we found with regard to** a bird's **slaughter that it permits it for consumption**

The final combination in the *kal vachomer* column is *kal vachomer* followed by *binyan av*. Rav Yirmiah introduces a proof from the laws of *tum'ah* of birds.

### Melikat Tereifah Case

The case: a bird upon which *melikat* (*malikat*) was performed — the method of slaughter for bird-offerings, whereby the kohen cuts the back of the neck — and it was subsequently found to be a *tereifah* (mortally wounded prior to the act). Does such a bird convey *tum'ah* when swallowed (*beit ha-beli'ah*), like a *nevela*, or is it *tahor* like a properly slaughtered *tereifah* of a non-bird animal?

Rebbi Meir rules it is *tahor* and does not convey *tum'ah*. Rebbi Yehuda rules it does convey *tum'ah* — and indeed, Rebbi Yehuda holds that even an ordinary *shechitah* of a *tereifah* bird leaves it with *tum'ah*.

### Behemah Comparison

Rebbi Meir builds his case with a *kal vachomer*: a *nevela* of a *behemah* (domestic animal) is stringent — it conveys *tum'ah* both through touching and through carrying. Yet *shechitah* of that animal, even if the animal is a *tereifah*, removes it from *tum'ah*. A *nevela* of a bird (*of*) is comparatively lenient — it conveys *tum'ah* only through swallowing, not through touching or carrying. Is it not a *kal vachomer* that if *shechitah* removes *tum'ah* from the stringent *behemah*, it certainly removes *tum'ah* from the more lenient bird? This establishes that *shechitah* of a *tereifah* bird renders it *tahor*.

### Melikat Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה מָצִינוּ בִּשְׁחִיטָה, שֶׁמַּכְשַׁרְתָּהּ בַּאֲכִילָה – מְטַהֶרֶת טְרֵיפָתָהּ מִטּוּמְאָתָהּ; אַף מְלִיקָה, שֶׁמַּכְשַׁרְתָּהּ בַּאֲכִילָה – תְּטַהֵר טְרֵיפָתָהּ מִטּוּמְאָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **and** it **purifies its *tereifa* from its impurity, so too** its **pinching, which permits** bird offerings **with regard to consumption, should purify its *tereifa* from its impurity.**

Building on that *kal vachomer*, Rebbi Meir then applies a *binyan av*: just as *shechitah*, which renders a bird fit for eating (*makhsheret be'akhilatah*), also purifies it from *tum'ah* (*metaheret tereifatah*) — so too *melikat*, which renders a bird-offering fit for consumption (*makhsheret me'akhilah*), should likewise purify it from *tum'ah*. This is a *mah matzinu* — a paradigm built directly on the *kal vachomer* that established *shechitah*'s purifying effect.

This appears to be a clean example of *kal vachomer* followed by *binyan av* — exactly what we want to establish.

### Chulin Caveat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הִיא; הָתָם תֶּיהְוֵי הִיא, מִשְּׁחִיטָה דְּחוּלִּין קָאָתְיָין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **And** that **is not so. Let it remain there,** i.e., one cannot learn from it, as that is a case that **comes from the slaughter of non-sacred** animals. The *halakha* of the pinching of a consecrated bird is derived through a paradigm from the *halakha* of the slaughter of a non-sacred bird, and the *halakha* of the slaughter of a non-sacred bird is derived through an *a fortiori* inference from the *halakha* of the slaughter of a non-sacred animal. Outside of the realm of consecrated matters there is no question that a matter derived via one of the hermeneutical principles can then teach its *halakha* via another principle. The entire question under discussion is only with regard to the realm of consecrated matters.

The *gemara* declines to accept this as a proof: *v'lo hi* — this is not a demonstration of the rule for *kodshim*. There it is clear that the derivation starts from *chullin* — the *behemah* and the bird are both *chullin* cases. The *kal vachomer* begins entirely outside the domain of *kodshim*. When the derivation originates in *chullin*, the restrictions that govern chained derivations within *kodshim* never come into play. We therefore still do not know whether, within *kodshim* proper, a *kal vachomer* can serve as the foundation for a *binyan av*. The fourth combination in the *kal vachomer* column — like the fourth combination in the *gezeirah shavah* column — is left without resolution.

The *gemara* will address the final column — *binyan av* as the first step — in the following daf.
