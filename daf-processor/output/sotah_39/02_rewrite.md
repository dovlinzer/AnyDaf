# Sotah 39 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tov Ayin & Egla Arufa

### Intro: Tov Ayin

The daf opens in the middle of the *sugya* on *birkas kohanim*, picking up from the bottom of 38b. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi had offered a string of statements around *birkas kohanim*, including the principle that a *bracha* communicates not merely through words but through the inner disposition of the one who gives it. The ideal is *tov ayin* — a generous, well-wishing spirit. The *tzar ayin*, by contrast, communicates its stinginess even nonverbally, even to the birds, regardless of what is said aloud. This non-verbal dimension of blessing is the backdrop for what follows.

### Tzar Ayin & Egla Arufa

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi continues: *ein egla arufa ba ela bishvil tzaray ha'ayin* — the ritual of *egla arufa*, which addresses cases of unsolved murder, only becomes necessary on account of those who are stingy of eye. The scriptural basis is the declaration made by the elders: *yadeinu lo shafchu es hadam hazeh v'eineinu lo ra'u* — our hands did not spill this blood and our eyes did not see. The Gemara notes the obvious question: would anyone suspect the elders of the court of murder? The answer is that the declaration addresses a different kind of culpability. *Ela lo ba l'yadeinu ufataruhu belo mezonos* — he came to our hands and we dismissed him without food; *velo re'inuhu vehinachnuhu belo levayah* — and we did not see to it that he had accompaniment on his journey.

### Abandoned Traveler

The scenario implied is that a traveler passed through town without receiving food or escort, and as a consequence — Rashi suggests he may have attempted to rob someone for food and was killed in the encounter — something went wrong. The stinginess of the community, its failure to take responsibility for the vulnerable person passing through, lies at the root of the tragedy. It is therefore fitting that *egla arufa* is connected to *tzar ayin*: the communal failure flows from an absence of the generous, caring spirit that *birkas kohanim* is meant to embody. It is also worth noting that the very next chapter of the tractate deals with *egla arufa*, making this a natural thematic bridge.

## All Kohanim Congregation

### Kulo Kohanim Question

The Gemara now introduces a ruling of Rav Simlai: *beis hakeneses shekula kohanim, kulam olim laduchan* — in a synagogue where all the members are *kohanim*, they all ascend to the *duchan*. The Gemara immediately raises the obvious question: *lemi mevarchin* — to whom do they give the blessing? If everyone is on the *duchan*, who remains to receive it?

### Am Besadot & Absent

Rav Zeira answers: *l'acheihem shebasados* — to their brothers in the fields. The blessing goes out to those who are part of the community even if not physically present in the synagogue. The Gemara supports this with the verse *v'es asher einenu poh imanu hayom* — and those who are not here with us today — suggesting that people who belong to the community, even if absent, can receive the blessing.

### Ones vs. Reshus

But the Gemara raises a challenge from a *baraisa* of Abba the son of Rav Minyamin bar Chiyya: *am she'achorei kohanim einam bikhlal bracha* — those who stand behind the *kohanim* are not included in the blessing. The requirement is *panim k'neged panim*, face to face. If those who are merely behind the *kohanim* are excluded, certainly those outside the synagogue entirely should be excluded. The Gemara resolves this: *lo kashya, ha d'anisi, ha d'lo anisi* — it is not difficult. The distinction is between those whose absence is coerced (*ones*) and those whose absence is voluntary (*reshus*). Those who could have been present but chose not to be have effectively removed themselves from the community and do not receive the blessing. Those whose absence is beyond their control remain connected to the *tzibur* and receive the blessing. The people standing behind the *kohanim* are in a position they could have changed — they could have been in front — and so their being behind reflects a voluntary self-exclusion.

### Split Arrangement

A *baraisa* of Rabbi Simi from Bei Bersa addresses this practically: in a congregation of all *kohanim*, some go up to the *duchan* while others remain below to answer *amen*. The ideal arrangement, then, is to split the *kohanim* so that a receiving congregation remains below.

### Minyan Requirement

The Gemara clarifies the conditions: *ha d'ishtayer bei asara, ha d'lo ishtayer bei asara* — the question is whether ten remain below or not. If the congregation is exactly ten, there is no way to send some up while keeping a *kahal* below. But if there are more than ten, some may ascend while a *minyan* remains as the receiving *tzibur*.

### Kahal Requirement

This leads to a broader principle: *vecho sevarchu es benei Yisrael* — the *bracha* is addressed to *benei Yisrael* as a collective. One does not perform *birkas kohanim* without a *tzibur* present to receive it. The *bracha* is inherently communal in character.

This raises a series of practical questions that flow naturally from the discussion. If *birkas kohanim* requires a *minyan*, what happens if the *minyan* dissipates after *shemoneh esrei* has begun? For *Sefardim* who perform *birkas kohanim* daily, this could arise in principle; for *Ashkenazim* it is largely a *Yom Tov* question. The relationship between *birkas kohanim* as a component of *chazaras hashatz* and as an independent act requiring its own *minyan* is genuinely complex.

A further nuance emerges: one needs a *minyan* present in the synagogue in order to perform *birkas kohanim* at all, but one does not require a *minyan* physically below to receive the blessing — the *am shebesados* fills that role. Ideally, however, if it can be arranged so that a *minyan* remains below, it should be. Crucially, the *kohanim* who ascend to the *duchan* do not count toward the *minyan* of those receiving the blessing, but they do count toward the overall *minyan* required to hold the service. The scenario of nine *Yisraelim* and an indefinite number of *am shebesados* filling out the quorum by themselves is plainly untenable.

The question of what constitutes the relevant community for *am shebesados* also bears consideration. In the classical case of a town where many people are out in the fields during *davening*, the connection to the local community is clear. But what of a tiny congregation where there are simply no others nearby — would the blessing extend to *Klal Yisrael* broadly, or must there be some communal association? The *sugya* seems to assume the latter.

## Exclusion & Position

### Behind Kohanim Excluded

Having established who is included in *birkas kohanim* through the lens of *ones* and *reshus*, the Gemara now takes up the formal *baraisa* of Abba the son of Rav Minyamin bar Chiyya directly: *am she'achorei kohanim einam bikhlal bracha* — those standing behind the *kohanim* are excluded from the blessing.

### No Hefsek: Height/Teivah

The Gemara immediately asks: is it not obvious? It proceeds to clarify what does *not* constitute an interruption. *Aricha be'appei gutzi* — a tall person standing in front of a shorter person — *lo mafsik*, that does not interrupt. As Rashi notes, there would be no end to this if height were an issue; we cannot require stadium seating in the synagogue. Similarly, *teiva lo mafsik* — the *teiva*, the reading platform or *bimah*, does not constitute a *hefsek* even if it sits between the congregation and the *kohanim*. The Gemara then raises the harder case: *mechitzah mai* — what about someone standing behind a partition?

### Tosafos: Self-Exclusion

Tosafos reframes the question importantly. He argues that the case of the *mechitzah* must be referring to someone who is not *anus* — because if they were *anus*, it is obvious that a *mechitzah* is no worse than being out in the fields entirely, and such a person would clearly receive the blessing along with the *am shebesados*. But if they are not *anus*, Tosafos raises a pointed question: why should being behind a *mechitzah* but in front of the *kohanim* be better than being behind the *kohanim* without a *mechitzah*? The person without a *mechitzah* who stands behind the *kohanim* seems less physically blocked, and yet they are excluded. Tosafos resolves this by explaining that standing behind the *kohanim* — *achorei oref* — is a statement. *Mar'im d'atzman ke'ilu ein habracha chashuvah b'eineihem* — they demonstrate that the blessing is not important to them. The Torah uses the language of *panim k'neged panim*; to place yourself behind them is to opt out of the face-to-face relationship. By contrast, someone standing behind a *mechitzah* but facing the *kohanim* has not made that statement. They are oriented toward the blessing, even if physically obstructed.

### Iron Wall & Connection

The Gemara then cites the well-known teaching of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *afilu mechitzah shel barzel einah mafsekes bein Yisrael l'avihem shebashamayim* — even an iron wall does not divide between Israel and their Father in heaven. This provides the conceptual foundation: physical barriers do not sever the spiritual relationship.

### Mechitzah & Core Minyan

Tosafos addresses the apparent tension with other *sugyos* that seem to require people to be in the same physical space in order to be *mitztaref* for a *minyan*. The resolution that Tosafos offers, which has become broadly accepted, distinguishes between two questions. To constitute a *tzibur* in the first place — to be able to perform *chazaras hashatz*, *devarim shebikdusha*, and *birkas kohanim* — one needs a core *minyan* in the same location. Once that core *minyan* exists and is performing these acts, then others who are behind *mechitzos* or in adjoining rooms can connect to and be included in what that *tzibur* is doing. The mechanic has something of the quality of *hashakah* in a *mikvah*: the connected body must maintain its core integrity, and from there extension is possible.

### Tosafos Drush: God's View

Tosafos closes with a passage of striking beauty. Citing the *gemara* in *Perek Ein Omdin* (32b) where Rabbi Elazar states that since the destruction of the *Beis Hamikdash*, an iron wall has separated Israel from their Father in heaven, Tosafos notes that these two teachings are not in contradiction. The *gemara* in *Berachos* is referring to the diminished reception of prayer in the absence of the *Beis Hamikdash* — *ein tefilasam miskabeles kemo shebizman Beis Hamikdash kayam*. But with respect to whether the *tzibur* is blessed through *birkas kohanim* or discharged through the *shliach tzibur* — *d'klapi avinu shebashamayim lo tali b'hefsek mechitzah* — from God's perspective, the walls between us simply do not register. There may be walls that affect how our prayers ascend, but there is no wall that prevents God's blessing from reaching Israel. This, Tosafos notes, lends itself to a broader *drush*: from God's vantage point, the barriers we perceive between ourselves and Him are not acknowledged on His side.

### Standing to the Side

The Gemara raises a further question: *tzadadim mahu* — what about those who are not behind the *kohanim* and not directly in front, but standing alongside them?

### Parah Adumah Analogy

The answer comes from a *mishnah* about the sprinkling of *parah adumah* waters. The rule there is: if one intended to sprinkle in front and the water landed behind, or intended to sprinkle behind and it landed in front, the *hazayah* is invalid. But *lefanav vehizzah al tzadadim shebifanav* — if one intended to sprinkle in front and it landed on the sides, the *hazayah* is *kesherah*. Rashi clarifies that *tzadadim shebifanav* means things that are alongside, not directly in front and not behind — and yet they are treated as "in front." The principle follows: being alongside the *kohanim* is included within the category of being in front of them. It would not make sense otherwise — in a large hall, no one could literally stand directly facing the *kohanim* at all times, and those alongside them are naturally part of the facing congregation.

## Washing Hands

### Washing & Holiness

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi teaches: *kol kohen she'lo natal yadav lo yisa kapav* — a *kohen* who has not washed his hands may not lift his hands to bless. The source is the verse *se'u yedeikhem kodesh u'varchu es Hashem* — lift up your hands in holiness and bless God. The word *kodesh* in the verse indicates that the hands must be sanctified — which is achieved through washing — before the blessing can be given.

### Rashi: Immediate Before

Rashi explains that this washing must take place *lifnei aloto laduchan* — immediately before ascending to the *duchan*. The requirement is not merely that the *kohen* washed at some point that day, but that the washing is proximate to the act.

### Tosafos: When Washing

Tosafos engages critically with a version of Rashi's text that appears to say that morning washing suffices and that a *kohen* who washed his hands in the morning and was careful (*natar*) need not wash again before ascending. Tosafos questions whether this line is authentic to Rashi or was inserted by a student — *ke'imdumeh she'hu hageh mi'hagahos talmid* — because Rashi's own language in explaining the Gemara implies that the washing must be truly proximate, *mamash samuch*, to the ascent. The Gemara itself reinforces this by quoting the verse *se'u yedeikhem kodesh u'varchu* — suggesting that *netilat yadayim* and *birkas kohanim* are to follow immediately one upon the other. The practical implication is familiar: when the *shliach tzibur* is himself a *kohen*, he must wash before the service begins so that he can proceed without interruption.

An underlying question, discussed before the *daf* began, concerns how one conceptualizes this washing — as an element of *avodah*, aligned with the *kohanim*'s washing before Temple service, or as something unique to *birkas kohanim* reflected in this particular verse. The choice of verse is telling: the Gemara does not cite the general *pasuk* about washing before *avodah*, but this specific verse connecting holiness, hands, and blessing. One might argue this makes the *netilah* particular to *birkas kohanim*, though the word *kodesh* in Biblical Hebrew does not technically mean washing the way *kiddesh* does in Mishnaic usage — itself perhaps a deliberate play on the resonance between sanctification and cleansing.

### Bracha Formula

The Gemara proceeds to ask: what *bracha* does a *kohen* recite? Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua, in the context of explaining his longevity, mentions that he never performed *nesias kapayim* without first making a *bracha* — implying that this was not universal practice. The formula articulated by Rav Zeira citing Rav Chisda is: *asher kidshanu b'mitzvosav u'vikdushaso shel Aharon v'tzivanu levarech es amo Yisrael b'ahava*.

### Aharon's Sanctity

This *bracha* is structurally distinctive. The standard form of a *birkas hamitzvah* is *asher kidshanu b'mitzvosav v'tzivanu*, but here an additional phrase is inserted: *u'vikdushaso shel Aharon* — and through the sanctity of Aharon. This insertion serves a necessary clarifying function. The word *kidshanu* with its plural suffix raises a question: who is the "we"? *Birkas kohanim* is not a *mitzvah* incumbent upon all of Israel. So the *bracha* specifies: we — the *kohanim* — possess a *kedushah* rooted in Aharon, and it is by virtue of *kedushas Aharon* that we are commanded in this *mitzvah*. This is not a claim of superiority but a necessary statement of which *kedushah* grants the obligation. It would be worth examining whether a parallel *nusach* applies to other distinctly *kohen* obligations — for instance, the *bracha* before eating a *chatas* — whether there too the *sheiltas Aharon* language appears in the formula.

### B'ahava (With Love)

The final word of the *bracha* — *b'ahava* — brings the discussion full circle. The verse *vecho sevarchu* does not itself contain the word *ahava*. Its inclusion in the *bracha* formula directly echoes the opening theme of the *sugya*: that *birkas kohanim* is not merely a ritual act but must be animated by genuine love and generosity of spirit — the *tov ayin* that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi placed at the center of authentic blessing. The *nusach* of the *bracha* is thus a theological statement: this blessing works only when it flows from *ahava*.

## Birkas Kohanim Choreogra…

### Yehi Ratzon Prayer

The Gemara now details the choreography of *birkas kohanim* step by step. *Ki akar krei'ei mai amar* — as the *kohen* begins moving his legs toward the *duchan*, what does he say? He recites a *yehi ratzon*: that the blessing should contain no *michshol v'avon* — no stumbling and no sin.

The specific content of the *yehi ratzon* invites reflection on what *michshol v'avon* might mean in this context. One reading — and a compelling one — is that the *kohen* is praying that the very blessing he is about to convey should not itself become a source of stumbling for those who receive it. Wealth, success, and divine favor can become *michsholim* when they lead people to arrogance or sin. The *kohen* standing as a conduit of blessing is thus asking that what flows through him be genuinely good for those who receive it — a prayer for the blessing's positive effect, not merely its transmission. This reading resonates with a parallel seen in the *tefillah* recited upon leaving the *beis medrash*, where one prays that Torah study not become a source of harm.

### Ribbono Shel Olam

After the blessing is complete and the *kohanim* turn their faces back toward the *aron*, the Gemara asks what they say at that moment. Rav Chisda expounded before Rav Ukva: *Ribbono Shel Olam asinu mah she'gazarta aleinu* — Master of the Universe, we have done what You decreed upon us; *aseh immanu mah she'hivtachtanu* — now do for us what You have promised us. The prayer then quotes *Hashkifah mi'me'on kodshecha min hashamayim u'varech es amcha Yisrael* — look down from Your holy dwelling in the heavens and bless Your people Israel.

What is striking about this formulation is the shift in pronouns. The *kohanim* say "do for *us*" — not "do for *them*." Having performed their distinctive role as the conduit of blessing, they now re-enter the general community of Israel as recipients. The *hivtachtanu* alludes to *Va'ani Avarchem* — and I will bless them — God's own assurance in the *pasuk* that He will confer the blessing upon Israel when the *kohanim* pronounce His name. The *Ribbono Shel Olam* does not quote this verse explicitly, and perhaps wisely so — there is a certain *chutzpah* in saying "You promised, now deliver." Instead, the *kohanim* invoke the closing verse of *Hashkifah mi'me'on kodshecha* as a request, while the implicit allusion to *Va'ani Avarchem* provides the theological grounding.

### Fingers Straight Until

Rav Chisda rules that the *kohanim* may not fold their fingers back until they have turned their faces away from the congregation. Even after the blessing is complete and *amen* has been said, the hands remain extended as long as the *kohanim* are still facing the *tzibur*. The reason is not stated explicitly, but one attractive reading is that folding the fingers might feel like a withdrawal — like taking back what has been given — and as long as one faces the congregation the gesture of blessing remains open.

### Sequencing Rules

Rav Zeira citing Rav Chisda now lays out a precise series of sequencing rules, each one requiring a stage to be fully completed before the next begins. The *korei* — the one who calls out "kohanim" — may not do so until the congregation has fully finished its *amen* to *Retzei*. The *kohanim* may not begin their *bracha* until the *korei* has finished calling. The congregation may not answer *amen* until the *kohanim* have completed their *bracha* — all the way to the final word. And the *kohanim* may not begin the next *bracha* until the congregation's *amen* to the previous one is fully concluded. Each act must be given its full space.

### Remain Until Sim Shalom

The choreography extends further. The *kohanim* may not turn their faces away from the congregation — *l'hachzir p'neihem min hatzibur* — until the *shliach tzibur* has begun *Sim Shalom*. And they may not step down from the *duchan* — *la'akor ragleihem* — until the *shliach tzibur* has completed *Sim Shalom*. The sequencing is layered: turn away only when *Sim Shalom* begins; descend only when it concludes. The sense is one of sustained presence and dignified transition rather than a quick departure once the ritual words are done.

### Tosafos: Hearing Concern

Tosafos analyzes the reason behind these sequencing requirements by asking whether they are based on the principle of *trei kali lo mishtamei* — that two voices speaking simultaneously cannot both be heard. Tosafos notes a difficulty: the Gemara elsewhere implies that when something is *chaviv*, beloved and important, *trei kali* are indeed *mishtamei*. Moreover, the *kohanim* themselves constitute multiple voices, yet they speak simultaneously without concern. Tosafos resolves that the sequencing rules are not fundamentally about the general public failing to hear. They are about the individual worshiper who is still saying *amen*: if that person is in the middle of their own *amen* when the *kohanim* begin the next *bracha*, that specific person will not be able to hear the new *bracha* they are in the midst of responding to. The concern is thus for the experience of each individual — the *kohanim* must wait not because sound is technically inaudible but because, as a matter of consideration, one should not begin a new stage while any member of the *tzibur* is still meaningfully engaged in the previous one. The Gemara's framing, however, does not seem quite so focused on the hearing of specific individuals — it has more the quality of giving each act its proper, non-overlapping space.

## Torah & Haftarah Order

### Aliyah Before Haftarah

Rabbi Tanchum citing Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *hamaftir banavi tzarich sheyikra baTorah techila* — one who reads the *haftarah* must first receive an *aliyah* in the Torah. One cannot leap directly to the *haftarah* without first being called to the Torah reading. The proper sequence of sacred acts requires traversing them in order.

### Roll Torah First

Continuing in the same sequence of teachings, Rabbi Tanchum citing Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi rules: *ein hamaftir rashai lehaftir banavi ad sheyigolel Sefer Torah* — the *maftir* may not begin the *haftarah* from the prophets until he has rolled up the *Sefer Torah* and completed that prior act. One finishes with the Torah before turning to the *haftarah*.

### Kvod Hatzibur: Coverings

Rabbi Tanchum citing Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi adds: *ein shaliach tzibur rashai lehafshit es hateiva betzibur* — the *shliach tzibur* may not remove the decorative coverings from the *teiva* while the congregation is still present, *mipnei kvod hatzibur*. After *kriat haTorah*, the elaborate coverings and cloths adorning the reading platform would be put away. To do so while the congregation is still assembled is disrespectful — stripping the *teiva* bare in their presence demeans the setting in which they have gathered.

### Torah Removal Timing

Rabbi Tanchum citing Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi rules further: *ein hatzibur rasha'in latzeit ad sheyinnatel Sefer Torah veyaniach bimkomo* — the congregation may not leave until the *Sefer Torah* has been taken and restored to its place. In the time of the Gemara, the *Sefer Torah* would sometimes be carried to a more protected private home for safekeeping. Shmuel, however, says: *ad sheyeitzei* — until it goes out, the congregation must wait, even before it has been fully restored to its destination. The Gemara resolves the apparent disagreement: *lo pligi* — they do not actually argue. *Ha d'ika pitcha achrina* — if there is another door in the synagogue, the congregation may leave by the other door while the *Sefer Torah* exits by the first, since they are not walking ahead of it. *Ha d'leika pitcha achrina* — if there is only one door, the *Sefer Torah* must go first.

### Torah Goes First

The concluding verse that grounds this entire sequence of rulings: *Acharei Hashem Elokeichem teleichu* — after God shall you walk. The *Sefer Torah*, as the tangible representation of *dvar Hashem*, goes first. The people follow. This principle of sequencing — giving precedence to what is sacred, waiting until the sacred act is complete before moving on — runs through the entire *sugya* and finds its most elemental expression here: you follow after God, not before.
