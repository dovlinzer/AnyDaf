# Gittin 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbah vs. Rava

### Two Explanations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲתִימָה לִשְׁמָהּ? אִי רַבִּי מֵאִיר – חֲתִימָה בָּעֵי, כְּתִיבָה לָא בָּעֵי! דִּתְנַן: אֵין כּוֹתְבִין בִּמְחוּבָּר לַקַּרְקַע. כְּתָבוֹ עַל הַמְחוּבָּר לַקַּרְקַע, תְּלָשׁוֹ, חֲתָמוֹ וּנְתָנוֹ לָהּ – כָּשֵׁר!
>
> **Translation:** that the **signing** must be performed **for her sake? If** it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** he **requires signing** for her sake; however, **he does not require** that the **writing** must be for her sake. **As we learned** in a mishna (21b): **One may not write** a bill of divorce **on** anything that is **attached to the ground,** e.g., a leaf attached to a tree. However, if **he wrote it on** something that is **attached to the ground,** and then he **detached it, signed it, and gave it to her,** it is **valid.**

The discussion continues from the bottom of 3b, where the central debate between Rabbah and Rava is still unfolding — a debate that will occupy us for several more days. The question is: why does the *shaliach* who delivers a *get* need to declare *bifnei nichtav ubifnei nichtam*, that it was written and signed in his presence? Is this declaration meant to testify that the *get* was executed in accordance with the laws of *lishma* — that is, composed with the proper intention directed toward this specific woman — as Rabbah holds? Or is it meant to validate the signatures on the document, so that if the *get* is ever challenged in the future, one need not face the difficulty of locating people who can authenticate those signatures? That is the position of Rava, expressed through the phrase *edim metzuyim lekaymo* — that witnesses are readily available to confirm the document's validity.

### Rabbah: Lishma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר – כְּתִיבָה בָּעֵי, חֲתִימָה לָא בָּעֵי! וְכִי תֵּימָא לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הִיא, וְכִי לָא בָּעֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר חֲתִימָה לִשְׁמָהּ – מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, מִדְּרַבָּנַן – בָּעֵי; וְהָא שְׁלֹשָׁה גִּיטִּין פְּסוּלִין דְּרַבָּנַן, וְלָא בָּעֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר חֲתִימָה לִשְׁמָהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **If** it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar,** who disagrees with Rabbi Meir and **requires** that the **writing** must be for her sake, **he does not require** that the **signing** must be for her sake. **And if you would say** that **actually** the mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, and when Rabbi Elazar does not require signing for her sake,** he means **by Torah law,** whereas **by rabbinic law he requires** that bills of divorce must be signed for her sake; **but** that is untenable, as the Sages listed **three bills of divorce** that are valid by Torah law but are **invalid by rabbinic law, and** when he disputes that ruling **Rabbi Elazar does not require** that the **signing** must be **for her sake.**

As the Gemara has already pointed out, the declaration contains two distinct components: the *shaliach* testifies both that the *get* was *written* and that it was *signed* in his presence. The term "signed" seems naturally to support Rava's concern with authenticating the signatories, while the term "written" seems to emphasize that the proper procedures governing the composition of a *get* were followed. The question then becomes: how does each position account for the component that seems to support the other?

### Rava: Signatures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה גִּיטִּין פְּסוּלִין, וְאִם נִיסַּת הַוָּלָד כָּשֵׁר: כָּתַב בִּכְתַב יָדוֹ וְאֵין עָלָיו עֵדִים, יֵשׁ עָלָיו עֵדִים וְאֵין בּוֹ זְמַן, יֵשׁ בּוֹ זְמַן וְאֵין בּוֹ אֶלָּא עֵד אֶחָד – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה גִּיטִּין פְּסוּלִין, וְאִם נִיסַּת – הַוָּלָד כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna (86a): **Three bills of divorce** are **invalid** *ab initio*, **but if** the woman **married** another man after having received one of these bills of divorce **the offspring** is of **unflawed** lineage. In other words, she is not considered to be a married woman who engaged in sexual intercourse with another man, which would render their child as one born from an adulterous relationship [*mamzer*]. And these three are: A bill of divorce that the husband **wrote in his handwriting but there are no** signatures of **witnesses on** the document; and if **there are** the signatures of **witnesses on** the document **but there is no date** written **on it;** and if **there is a date** written **on it but it has only one witness** signed on it. **These are** the **three invalid bills of divorce** concerning which the Sages said: **And if she married, the offspring** is of **unflawed** lineage.

Rava, who holds that the concern is with validating the signatures, explains that *bifnei nichtav* — the testimony about the writing — serves a different purpose altogether. Since this declaration is made by a single individual, which is itself unusual (normally signature validation requires two witnesses), people might become confused about the ordinary rules of authentication. By having the *shaliach* add that it was also written in his presence, the declaration signals that this is something distinctive — a special institution unique to *gittin*, not a standard case of *kiyum shtarot*.

### Both Terms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין עָלָיו עֵדִים – אֶלָּא שֶׁנְּתָנוֹ לָהּ בִּפְנֵי עֵדִים; כָּשֵׁר, וְגוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים. שֶׁאֵין הָעֵדִים חוֹתְמִים עַל הַגֵּט אֶלָּא מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **Rabbi Elazar says: Even though there are no** signatures of **witnesses on** the document, **but he gave it to her in the presence of** two **witnesses,** it is **a valid** bill of divorce. **And** on the basis of this bill of divorce the woman **can collect** the amount written to her in her marriage contract even **from liened property, as** Rabbi Elazar maintains **that witnesses sign the bill of divorce only for the betterment of the world.**

For Rabbah, however, who holds that the entire institution is about testifying to the *lishma* requirements, the question runs in the opposite direction: if the whole point is the writing, why must the *shaliach* also testify about the signing? The Gemara therefore assumes that Rabbah understands the *Mishnah* as reflecting a view that demands compliance with the laws of *gittin* not only in the writing but equally in the signing. This leads the Gemara into a fundamental debate that will reverberate throughout the entire *masechet*: what is it, precisely, that transforms a piece of paper into a *get*?

## Get Validity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲתָמוֹ וּנְתָנוֹ לָהּ – כָּשֵׁר! וְכִי תֵּימָא אֲנַן מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא קָא מַתְנִינַן לַהּ, אִי הָכִי, ״אוֹמֵר הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר דְּבַר תּוֹרָה״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **and he signed it and gave it to** his wife, it is **a valid** bill of divorce? **And if you would say** that **we learned** that **this** statement of Rav Naḥman applies **by Torah law,** whereas by rabbinic law Rabbi Meir concedes that a bill of divorce must be written for her sake, **if so,** Rav Naḥman **should have** said: **Rabbi Meir would say** that **by Torah law** if a husband found a document in the garbage he may use it.

The question the Gemara now frames is foundational: is it the writing of the document — executed according to the proper *halachot* — that creates the *get*? Or does the paper remain legally inert until it is signed, with the signatures themselves being the act that constitutes the *get* as a valid instrument? The Gemara presents this as a debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Eliezer, and it uses the question of *mechubar* — whether a *get* is disqualified if it was written while attached to the ground — as a testing case to expose the underlying logic of each position.

---

## R. Meir: Signing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הִיא, וְכִי לָא בָּעֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר חֲתִימָה – הֵיכָא דְּלֵיכָּא עֵדִים כְּלָל; הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא עֵדִים – בָּעֵי. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בִּמְזוּיָּיף מִתּוֹכוֹ, שֶׁהוּא פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara retracts the previous answer and states: **Actually,** the mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, and when does Rabbi Elazar not require** that the **signing** must be for her sake? **Where there are no witnesses at all** on the bill of divorce. However, **where there are witnesses, he does require** that all their signatures must be for her sake. The proof for this is **that Rabbi Abba says: Rabbi Elazar concedes with regard to** a document whose **falsification is inherent in it,** i.e., a document that is signed by disqualified witnesses, **that it is invalid.**

Rabbi Meir holds that it is the signatures alone that create the *get*. In his terminology, the signing is *karti* — from the root *karet*, meaning it "cuts," it effects the severance. Just as *koret habrit* refers to the act that seals a covenant, here the *chatima* is the act that seals the divorce. The legal consequence of this view is striking: if the *get* is nothing until it is signed, then there is no logical basis for demanding anything particular about how it was written. All the *halachic* requirements — including *lishma* — attach only to the signing. Before the signatures are affixed, the document is just paper; one could, in principle, take a pre-printed form off the shelf that happens to bear the correct names and have it signed. It is only the interval between signing and delivery where requirements like *mechubar* apply.

---

## R. Eliezer: Writing

Rabbi Eliezer takes the opposite position: the writing is what matters, and the signatures are not required at all. A *get* delivered without signatures is valid — apparently even *lechatchila*, at the Torah level, and certainly *bediavad*. What is required, however, is that the *get* be delivered in the presence of witnesses. Here an important conceptual question arises: is this witness requirement structurally parallel to the witnesses required for *kiddushin* — that is, witnesses who validate the *act* of divorce rather than the *instrument* — or do those witnesses themselves form a constitutive part of the *get*? In Rabbi Eliezer's language, this principle is *eidei mesira karti*: it is the witnesses to the delivery who "cut," who effect the divorce.

The Gemara then asks: if Rabbi Eliezer does not require signatures at all, why would *bifnei nichtam* — the testimony about the signing — ever be relevant to him? The Gemara answers by citing Rabbi Abba: *modeh Rabbi Eliezer b'mezuyaf mitocho shehu pasul* — Rabbi Eliezer concedes that a document that is "forged from within" is invalid. Rashi explains *mezuyaf mitocho* as meaning that the document was signed by relatives or otherwise disqualified witnesses. Even though one need not sign the *get* at all, if one chooses to sign it, one may not do so with invalid witnesses. The principle is that one should not create something that purports to be a valid legal instrument while building an intrinsic defect into it: if you do not want to have signatures, do not have them; but if you are going to sign, you cannot sign with disqualified witnesses.

## R. Meir: Lenient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמֵעִיקָּרָא מַאי טַעְמָא לָא מוֹקְמִינַן לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And initially, what is the reason we did not establish** the mishna **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda?**

Returning to Rabbi Meir, the Gemara now asks directly: does Rabbi Meir truly impose no requirements whatsoever on the writing? The Gemara cites Rav Nachman quoting Rabbi Meir: if someone found a pre-written *get* bearing the husband's and wife's names, had it signed by witnesses, and delivered it to the wife, it is *kasher*. The Gemara then asks whether this ruling is stated only at the Biblical level, while rabbinically the writing would still need to meet certain standards. The Gemara rejects this qualification: if Rabbi Meir's ruling were only Biblical, he would have said "from a Torah perspective it is *kasher*." Since he simply says it is *kasher*, with no qualification, it is clear that Rabbi Meir imposes no requirements on the writing even rabbinically. The practical implication follows directly: there would be no basis for a *shaliach* to testify about how the *get* was written. We are therefore still searching for the opinion that demands both the writing and the signing follow all the proper requirements.

---

## R. Eliezer: Conditional

The Gemara now revisits Rabbi Eliezer from this angle. It was shown above that Rabbi Eliezer does not require signatures. But if he does not require them, why would there be any demand that they be done *lishma* when they are affixed? The Gemara answers: it is precisely the logic of *mezuyaf mitocho* that carries this through. If one does sign, the signature must be valid — not only in the narrow sense that the witnesses must be halachically qualified, but also with regard to *lishma*. Rashi adds this point explicitly: *o shelo lishma*, or if it was done without the proper intent, that too would constitute a problem for Rabbi Eliezer.

Tosafot raises a challenge: how can one compare the case of invalid witnesses — where the invalidity is intrinsic and obvious — to the case of signing *shelo lishma*, where there is no apparent deficiency? There are valid witnesses; they simply lacked the proper intent. Tosafot's own answer is somewhat less structurally elegant: the concern is practical — that people will come to rely on those signatures to permit her to remarry or to collect her *ketubah*. His final resolution is that once signing occurs at all, we apply a rabbinic rule: *gazrinan chatima atu ketiva* — we extend the requirements of the writing to the signing, so that slippage in the one domain will not infect the other.

One might, however, suggest a more far-reaching approach. There are *Rishonim* who hold that according to Rabbi Eliezer, when signatures are affixed to the *get*, they do not merely serve as circumstantial evidence that witnesses were present — they become, at the Torah level, an integral part of the *get* itself, carrying the full testimonial weight of a classic *shtar*. If this is correct, then once one has chosen to sign the *get*, those signatures are recognized by the Torah as constitutive elements of the document, and they therefore require the same criteria — including *lishma* — as the writing itself. Under this reading, the demand for *lishma* in the signatures is not merely rabbinic caution against confusion, but potentially a Torah-level requirement that flows from the act of having incorporated testimony into the document. Tosafot's answer frames this as a rabbinic fence; but the conceptual logic could extend to the Biblical level.

A further point bears emphasis: the requirement of signatures is, in the final analysis, a *tikkun olam* — a rabbinic institution for the welfare of society. *Mideoraita*, Rabbi Eliezer would hold that an unsigned *get* takes full legal effect, and the woman is divorced. Practically, however, *beit din* will not permit her to remarry without some form of documentation. She cannot simply present herself and claim she was divorced; she bears the status of an *eshet ish*, and the court will demand proof. External witnesses could theoretically be summoned, but that is not always feasible or convenient. The practical, prudent course is always to have the document signed. The absence of a signature leaves her divorced in the eyes of Heaven but effectively unable to act on that status in this world. The institution of signed *gittin* is therefore the Rabbis' way of ensuring that *mide'oraita* validity translates into workable legal recognition.

The conclusion of this section: Rabbi Eliezer is the opinion whose framework supports requirements on both the writing and the signing. He does not require the signatures, but once they are affixed, they must follow the same rules as the writing — whether one understands this rabbinically or, as suggested above, potentially even Biblically.

## R. Yehuda: Both

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְהַדְּרִינַן אַרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דִּסְתַם מַתְנִיתִין רַבִּי מֵאִיר; מְהַדְּרִינַן אַרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּקַיְימָא לַן הִילְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ בְּגִיטִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **We seek** to explain the mishna **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** as there is a general principle **that** a ruling in **an unattributed mishna** is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir.** Likewise, **we seek** to interpret the mishna in accordance with the ruling of **Rabbi Elazar, as we maintain** in general that **the *halakha* is in accordance with his** opinion **with regard to bills of divorce.**

The Gemara then offers a further possibility through Rav Ashi: *ha mani Rabbi Yehuda hi* — the *Mishnah* could reflect the position of Rabbi Yehuda. As taught in a *baraita*, Rabbi Yehuda invalidates the *get* unless both its writing and its signing take place when the document is *betalush* — detached from the ground. While it is somewhat difficult to construct a realistic scenario where a document is written while detached but signed while still attached, the theoretical point is clear: Rabbi Yehuda regards both the writing and the signing as independently essential to the validity of the *get*, and the requirement of *mechubar* applies to both stages equally. Whether he conceptualizes this as two independent acts that together constitute the *get*, or as a unique synthesis in which both the writing and the signing are jointly necessary, is left open, but the practical demand is symmetrical.

---

## Why Not R. Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דִּתְנַן: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה פּוֹסֵל, עַד שֶׁתְּהֵא כְּתִיבָתוֹ וַחֲתִימָתוֹ בְּתָלוּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** a different explanation: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna? **It is** in accordance with a third opinion, that of **Rabbi Yehuda. As we learned** in a mishna (21b): **Rabbi Yehuda invalidates** a bill of divorce **unless its writing and signing** are performed **on** an item that is **detached** from the ground.

The Gemara immediately asks: if Rabbi Yehuda provides the cleanest fit — requiring both writing and signing — why did the Gemara not simply begin there? The answer is twofold. First, *setam Mishnah Rabbi Meir*: an anonymous *Mishnah* is presumptively attributable to Rabbi Meir, and the opening requirement of *bifnei nichtav ubifnei nichtam* is stated anonymously. It was therefore natural to first attempt to reconcile it with Rabbi Meir's position. Second, and more significantly: *dekaiyma lan hilchata keva'tei b'gittin* — the *halacha* follows Rabbi Eliezer in *gittin*. This is a remarkable moment in the *sugya*: right at the outset, in what is only the second *daf* of the *masechet*, the Gemara announces the operative ruling on one of the most fundamental questions in the entire tractate. The *halacha* is that signatures on a *get* are not required. A *get* delivered before witnesses who did not sign the document is Biblically valid. Rabbi Yehuda, whose position is neither the *stam Mishnah* nor the accepted ruling, was therefore the option of last resort.

This explains why the *sugya* works through the positions in the order it does. The initial anonymous statement is matched first to Rabbi Meir, then to Rabbi Eliezer, and only when both prove problematic does the Gemara reach Rabbi Yehuda. It is a well-constructed opening *sugya* that introduces a major conceptual debate, maps out the positions of the *Tannaim*, and announces the *halacha* — all within the first pages of the tractate.

## Medina HaYam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן, רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אַף הַמֵּבִיא מִן הָרְקָם וּמִן הַחֶגֶר. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ מִכְּפַר לוּדִּים לְלוֹד. וְאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בַּעֲיָירוֹת הַסְּמוּכוֹת לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל וּמוּבְלָעוֹת בִּתְחוּם אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל עָסְקִינַן,
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues to explain the mishna in light of the dispute between Rabba and Rava. **We learned** in the mishna that **Rabban Gamliel says: Even one who brings** a bill of divorce **from Rekem or from Ḥeger** must say: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence. **Rabbi Eliezer says: Even** one who brings a bill of divorce **from the village of Ludim to Lod. And Abaye said** in explanation of this matter: **We are dealing with towns** that are not part of the land itself, but **are near Eretz Yisrael and within the boundary of Eretz Yisrael.**

The Gemara now transitions to examining the rest of the *Mishnah* through the lens of the Rabbah-Rava debate. The *Mishnah* contains an internal dispute about what qualifies as *medinat hayam* — the overseas region or distant diaspora — that triggers the *shaliach*'s obligation to declare *bifnei nichtav ubifnei nichtam*. The dispute involves three positions: Rabban Gamliel holds that the requirement applies even to cities *semuchot le-Eretz Yisrael* — adjacent to the Land of Israel — and Rabbi Eliezer extends this further to cities that are *muvlaot b'tehum Eretz Yisrael* — geographically enclosed or surrounded by the territory of Israel. The Sages, representing the view that the requirement applies only to genuinely distant locations, take the opposing position. Abaye explains that the debate concerns towns near the border of Israel, some of which are so closely surrounded by Israelite territory that they are described as "swallowed up" within it. Rabbah bar bar Hana adds that such a place was as short a distance as from Bei Kubi to Pumbedita.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: לְדִידִי חֲזֵי לִי הָהוּא אַתְרָא, וְהָוֵי כְּמִבֵּי כוּבֵּי לְפוּמְבְּדִיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabba bar bar Ḥana said: I myself saw that place,** i.e., the distance between the village of Ludim and Lod, **and it was similar** to the distance **from Bei Kuvei to Pumbedita,** which is only a short distance.

Rashi depicts the geometry as follows: *semuchot* refers to a city right at the border; *muvlaot* refers to a city that is not merely adjacent but effectively enclosed — surrounded on multiple sides by Israelite territory. These are different degrees of closeness to Israel, and they are different degrees of removal from the category of *medinat hayam*. The Tanna Kamma says that only truly distant locations require the declaration; Rabban Gamliel says that even a city just at the border falls under the requirement; Rabbi Eliezer holds that even a *muvlaot* city — effectively surrounded by Israel — still requires it.

The Gemara asks: is this dispute best understood as reflecting the Rabbah-Rava debate? If the concern is *lishma* — cultural and legal awareness of the proper procedures — then a city right over the border is probably still part of the same cultural orbit and would know the relevant *halachot*. But if the concern is the availability of witnesses for *kiyum*, then even a nearby city over a political border represents a distinct travel barrier, and we cannot assume that witnesses will be readily accessible across it. On this reading, the debate in the *Mishnah* about near versus far is really a debate about which underlying concern governs the institution.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר הָנֵי לָא צְרִיךְ; מַאי, לָאו בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי – דְּמָר סָבַר לְפִי שֶׁאֵין בְּקִיאִין לִשְׁמָהּ, וְהָנֵי גְּמִירִי; וּמָר סָבַר לְפִי שֶׁאֵין עֵדִים מְצוּיִין לְקַיְּימוֹ, וְהָנֵי נָמֵי לָא שְׁכִיחִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the mishna: One can derive **by inference that the first *tanna*** of the mishna **holds** that in **these** places the agent **is not required** to testify that the bill of divorce was written and signed for the woman's sake. **What, is it not** the case that **they disagree with regard to this** principle, **as** one **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that the reason for saying: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence, is **because** people living overseas **are not experts** in writing a bill of divorce **for her sake, and** the residents of **these** places **are learned** in this matter, as they are near Eretz Yisrael. **And** one **Sage,** Rabban Gamliel, **holds:** The reason is **because there are no witnesses available to ratify it, and** the residents of **these** places **are also not frequently** available, as they live on the other side of the border, and it is difficult to bring witnesses from one place to another.

The Gemara, however, rejects the premise that this dispute maps directly onto the Rabbah-Rava divide. Instead, it holds that each of Rabbah and Rava can explain the debate internally within their own framework.

---

## Rabbah on Lishma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; רַבָּה מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ, וְרָבָא מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ. רַבָּה מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לְפִי שֶׁאֵין בְּקִיאִין לִשְׁמָהּ, וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי – תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר, הָנֵי כֵּיוָן דִּסְמוּכוֹת מִיגְמָר גְּמִירִי;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No,** there is no proof that this issue is a dispute among *tanna'im*, as **Rabba resolves** the different opinions in the mishna according **to his** line of **reasoning, and Rava resolves** them according **to his** line of **reasoning.** The Gemara elaborates: **Rabba resolves** them according **to his** line of **reasoning, as everyone** agrees that the reason is **because they are not experts** in writing a bill of divorce **for her sake. And here they disagree with regard to this** issue: **The first *tanna* holds** that **since** the residents of **these** cities **are** located **near** Eretz Yisrael, they **are** assumed to be **learned** in this *halakha*.

According to Rabbah — who holds the concern is *lishma* — the underlying principle is agreed upon by all: the requirement exists because communities outside Israel may not be adequately familiar with the *halachot* of *lishma*. The debate is then about where to draw the line.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲתָא רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל לְמֵימַר, מוּבְלָעוֹת גְּמִירִי, סְמוּכוֹת לָא גְּמִירִי; וַאֲתָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לְמֵימַר, מוּבְלָעוֹת נָמֵי לָא, שֶׁלֹּא תַּחְלוֹק בִּמְדִינַת הַיָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabban Gamliel came to say:** Granted, the residents of those cities that **are within** the boundary of Eretz Yisrael **are learned** in this *halakha*, however, the residents of these cities that are only **near** Eretz Yisrael **are not learned. And Rabbi Eliezer came to say,** when he mentioned the village of Ludim and the town of Lod, that the residents of those cities that **are within** the boundary of Eretz Yisrael are **also not** treated as learned, so **that you should not make a distinction within** separate areas of **the country overseas.**

The Tanna Kamma holds that cities so close to Israel are still part of the same cultural and legal sphere; proximity means they have absorbed the relevant knowledge, and no declaration is required. Rabban Gamliel distinguishes: a *muvlaot* city, surrounded on multiple sides by Israel, may indeed be culturally integrated enough to know the law — he will concede that much — but a *semuchot* city, merely adjacent without being enclosed, might not have absorbed the same standards, and he requires the declaration there. Rabbi Eliezer pushes back against any such case-by-case evaluation: we cannot go city by city assessing knowledge levels. A formal, categorical rule is necessary. The rule is simple — anything over the border requires the declaration — and Rabbi Eliezer invokes the principle of *lo plug*: we do not make distinctions within the category of *medinat hayam* based on individual circumstances.

---

## Rava on Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לְפִי שֶׁאֵין עֵדִים מְצוּיִין לְקַיְּימוֹ – וְתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר, הָנֵי כֵּיוָן דִּסְמוּכוֹת מִישְׁכָּח שְׁכִיחִי;
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, **Rava resolves** the various opinions in the mishna according **to his** line of **reasoning.** He maintains **that everyone** agrees that the reason for the declaration: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence, is **because there are no witnesses available to ratify it. And the first *tanna* holds: Since** the residents of **these** cities **are nearby** they **are frequently** available.

Rava's framework — that the concern is the availability of witnesses to authenticate the document — can explain the same dispute with equal coherence. Here too, all *Tannaim* agree on the underlying principle: the requirement exists because witnesses may not be readily available to validate signatures across geographic distances. The Tanna Kamma holds that nearby cities have enough cross-border travel that witnesses are sufficiently accessible. Rabban Gamliel distinguishes *muvlaot* from *semuchot*: a city truly surrounded by Israel will have robust travel and available witnesses, but one merely adjacent will not. Rabbi Eliezer again invokes *lo plug* and *shelo tachlok b'medinat hayam* — we do not parse the category of *medinat hayam* into subcategories; the formalist rule applies uniformly to everything across the border.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲתָא רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל לְמֵימַר, מוּבְלָעוֹת שְׁכִיחִי, סְמוּכוֹת לָא שְׁכִיחִי; וַאֲתָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לְמֵימַר, מוּבְלָעוֹת נָמֵי לָא, שֶׁלֹּא תַּחְלוֹק בִּמְדִינַת הַיָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabban Gamliel came to say:** The residents of cities **within** the boundary of Eretz Yisrael **are frequently** available. However, the residents of places that are merely **near** Eretz Yisrael **are not frequently** available. **And Rabbi Eliezer came to say** that the residents of those cities that **are within** the boundary of Eretz Yisrael are **also not** considered to be like Eretz Yisrael in this matter, despite their proximity to the Jewish settlement, so **that you should not make a distinction within** separate areas of **a country overseas.**

The Gemara's broader methodological point is significant: one can hold a single principled position and still debate its application. Whether the issue is *lishma* or *edim metzuyim*, the underlying debate about near and far is really a debate about formalism versus context. A legal system cannot operate through purely contextual, case-by-case determinations. At some point, rules must be categorical. The question is merely where to set the threshold and how rigorously to apply it.

## Molich Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ שֶׁיֹּאמַר ״בְּפָנַי נִכְתַּב וּבְפָנַי נֶחְתַּם״, אֶלָּא הַמֵּבִיא מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם וְהַמּוֹלִיךְ. מִכְּלָל דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר מוֹלִיךְ לָא צְרִיךְ; מַאי, לָאו בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי – דְּמָר סָבַר לְפִי שֶׁאֵין בְּקִיאִין לִשְׁמָהּ,
>
> **Translation:** § **We learned** in the mishna: **And the Rabbis say** that **one is required to say: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence, only** if he **brings** a bill of divorce **from a country overseas** to Eretz Yisrael, **and** the same applies to **one who delivers** a bill of divorce from Eretz Yisrael to a country overseas. The Gemara analyzes this statement: One can learn **by inference that the first *tanna*** of the mishna **holds** that **one who delivers** a bill of divorce from Eretz Yisrael to a country overseas **is not required** to say this declaration. **What, is it not** the case that **they disagree with regard to this** principle, **as** one **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that the reason for the declaration is **because they are not experts** in writing a bill of divorce **for her sake,**

The Gemara turns to the next clause of the *Mishnah*: *vehamolich* — the obligation of a *shaliach* who carries a *get* outward from Israel to the diaspora, not merely inward. The *Chachamim* say that the declaration is required both of one who brings a *get* from *medinat hayam* to Israel and of one who carries it *out* — *hamolich*. The inference is that the Tanna Kamma required the declaration only for *hamevi*, bringing it in, and not for *hamolich*. The Gemara asks: is this dispute about *molich* a direct reflection of the Rabbah-Rava debate?

On the *lishma* theory, the concern is about people in the diaspora who lack the proper knowledge. A *shaliach* carrying a *get* from Israel outward is departing from a place of expertise; there is no *lishma* problem on his end. There would therefore be no intrinsic reason to require the declaration. On the *edim* theory, however, travel barriers exist equally in both directions: witnesses in the diaspora are no more accessible to those in Israel than vice versa. The requirement should therefore apply symmetrically, and *hamolich* should require the declaration just as *hamevi* does.

This appears to be a clean test case for the Rabbah-Rava debate, and the Gemara initially entertains this reading. It then pulls back: each position can explain both the Tanna Kamma and the *Chachamim* from within its own logic.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי גְּמִירִי; וּמָר סָבַר לְפִי שֶׁאֵין עֵדִים מְצוּיִין לְקַיְּימוֹ, וְהָנֵי נָמֵי לָא שְׁכִיחִי?
>
> **Translation:** **and these** people **are learned** with regard to this *halakha*, as a bill of divorce sent from Eretz Yisrael was certainly written in the correct manner. **And** one **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds:** The reason for the testimony is **because there are no witnesses available to ratify it, and these** witnesses who travel from Eretz Yisrael overseas **are also not frequently** available.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ, וְרָבָא מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ. רַבָּה מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לְפִי שֶׁאֵין בְּקִיאִין לִשְׁמָהּ, וְהָכָא בִּגְזֵירַת מוֹלִיךְ אַטּוּ מֵבִיא קָמִיפַּלְגִי –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara again rejects the suggestion: One cannot prove that this issue is a dispute of the *tanna'im*, as **Rabba resolves** the different opinions in the mishna according **to his** line of **reasoning, and Rava resolves** them according **to his** line of **reasoning. Rabba resolves** them according **to his** line of **reasoning,** as follows: It may be **that everyone** agrees that the reason is **because they are not experts** in writing a bill of divorce **for her sake, and here they disagree with regard to a decree** applied in the case of **one who delivers** a bill of divorce to Eretz Yisrael **due to** the concern that it will be confused with the case of **one who brings** a bill of divorce from Eretz Yisrael.

Rabbah explains: *dekulei alma lefi she-ein bekiyim b'lishma*, and the debate about *molich* is whether we extend the requirement through a *gezerah* of *molich atu mevi*. The Tanna Kamma says no such extension is necessary. The *Chachamim* say that to avoid confusion — someone seeing that *hamevi* always declares might assume the requirement is universal, and become confused when *hamolich* does not — we institute a symmetric requirement. If people in *medinat hayam* observe that every *shaliach*, regardless of direction, makes this declaration, this itself serves an educational function consistent with the *lishma* concern. The Tanna Kamma is not willing to make that extension; the *Rabbanan* are.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: לָא גָּזְרִינַן מוֹלִיךְ אַטּוּ מֵבִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains **that the first *tanna* holds:** The Sages **do not decree** with regard to **one who delivers due to one who brings.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן בָּתְרָאֵי סָבְרִי: גָּזְרִינַן מוֹלִיךְ אַטּוּ מֵבִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **And the later Rabbis hold:** The Sages **do decree** with regard to **one who delivers due to one who brings** the bill of divorce from overseas.

Rava explains more simply: *dekulei alma lefi she-ein edim metzuyin lekaymo*, and the *Chachamim* who add *hamolich* are not introducing a new dispute but merely clarifying what was already implied. When the Tanna Kamma said *hamevi*, he meant it to encompass both directions; when the *Chachamim* add *hamolich* explicitly, *rabbanan batrai lefirushei taama d'Tanna Kamma hu de-atu* — they are clarifying and unpacking what the earlier statement already included. This parallels the earlier discussion of *medinat hayam* itself: just as the debate about *semuchot* and *muvlaot* was a dispute about the scope of a general term, the addition of *hamolich* is similarly an elaboration on the scope of *hamevi*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא מְתָרֵץ לְטַעְמֵיהּ: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לְפִי שֶׁאֵין עֵדִים מְצוּיִין לְקַיְּימוֹ, וְרַבָּנַן בָּתְרָאֵי – לְפָרוֹשֵׁי טַעְמֵיהּ דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא הוּא דְּאָתוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava** also **resolves** the different opinions in the mishna according **to his** line of **reasoning.** How so? He maintains **that everyone** agrees that the reason for the declaration: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence, is **because there are no witnesses available to ratify it, and the later Rabbis** do not disagree with the previous opinion. Rather, **they come to explain the reasoning of the first *tanna**.** In other words, the first *tanna* agrees that one of the reasons for this *halakha* is because witnesses are not available to ratify the bill of divorce. He simply taught the *halakha* in a concise manner.

## Medina to Medina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַמֵּבִיא גֵּט מִמְּדִינָה לִמְדִינָה בִּמְדִינַת הַיָּם – צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר ״בְּפָנַי נִכְתַּב וּבְפָנַי נֶחְתַּם״. הָא בְּאוֹתָהּ מְדִינָה בִּמְדִינַת הַיָּם – לֹא צָרִיךְ. לְרָבָא נִיחָא, לְרַבָּה קַשְׁיָא!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara attempts to cite another proof: **We learned** in the mishna: **One who brings** a bill of divorce **from one region to** another **region within a country overseas must** also **say: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence.** The Gemara infers: **But** if he brought a bill of divorce **within** one **region in a country overseas he is not required** to state this declaration. This **works out well according to** the opinion of **Rava,** as witnesses are available in the same region. However, **according to** the opinion of **Rabba,** the matter is **difficult,** as overseas residents are not experts in the *halakha* and therefore it does not matter how closely they are located to each other.

The Gemara now examines the clause: *hamevi get mimedina limedina b'medinat hayam* — one who carries a *get* from one *medina* to another within the diaspora is required to declare *bifnei nichtav ubifnei nichtam*. The Gemara asks what can be inferred from this. Both Rabbah's and Rava's concerns are clearly present when crossing from one diaspora district to another: *lishma* problems exist throughout the diaspora, and witness availability diminishes with each additional border crossed. The question is what the *Mishnah*'s language implies about cases it does not explicitly state.

---

## Rava's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא תֵּימָא: הָא בְּאוֹתָהּ מְדִינָה בִּמְדִינַת הַיָּם – לֹא צָרִיךְ, אֶלָּא אֵימָא: מִמְּדִינָה לִמְדִינָה בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל – לֹא צָרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: According to the opinion of Rabba, **do not say** that the correct inference is: **Within** one **region in a country overseas, he is not required** to state the declaration. **Rather, say** that one should infer from the mishna: **From region to region within Eretz Yisrael, he is not required** to do so.

The Gemara notes that a straightforward reading of *hamolich mimedina limedina b'medinat hayam* implies that within a *single* diaspora *medina*, the declaration would not be required. For Rava, who grounds the requirement in witness availability, this is perfectly intelligible: witnesses are more accessible within a single administrative district, where travel is less restricted. But for Rabbah, who grounds the requirement in *lishma*, the implication is more troubling. The *lishma* problem does not disappear merely because one stays within a single diaspora *medina*; the lack of proper halakhic awareness pervades the entire diaspora regardless of internal administrative divisions.

The Gemara also raises a harder challenge for Rabbah. The *Mishnah* explicitly states that one who brings a *get* within *Eretz Yisrael* does not need to make the declaration at all — including, apparently, when traveling from one region (*medina*) to another within Israel. For Rava, who focuses on witness availability, this is easily explained: Israel has special characteristics that ensure witnesses are accessible. But for Rabbah, who focuses on *lishma*, why should Israel's internal geography matter? The *lishma* requirements apply everywhere; crossing from the Galil to Yehuda involves a change of district no less than crossing within the diaspora. The distinction between Israel and the diaspora is built into Rabbah's framework at the macro level, but the internal distinction — *medina* to *medina* within Israel — is harder to derive from *lishma* concerns alone.

---

## Rabbah's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא בְהֶדְיָא קָתָנֵי לַהּ: הַמֵּבִיא גֵּט בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר ״בְּפָנַי נִכְתַּב וּבְפָנַי נֶחְתַּם״! אִי מֵהַהִיא, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי דִּיעֲבַד, אֲבָל לְכַתְּחִילָּה לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** that *halakha* need not be derived by inference, as the mishna **is teaching it explicitly: One who brings a bill of divorce** from one place to another **within Eretz Yisrael is not required to say: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence.** The Gemara answers: **If** this *halakha* were to be derived **from that** statement alone, **I would say: This applies** only **after the fact,** i.e., if someone acted in this manner then the bill of divorce is not invalidated. **However,** it might have been thought that one should **not** act in this manner ***ab initio*;** rather, the agent should state the declaration. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** by means of this inference from its first section that he need not state the declaration even *ab initio*.

The Gemara resolves Rabbah's difficulty. One might have thought, based on the *diyuk*, that staying within a single *medina* of *medinat hayam* exempts one from the declaration — which would be inexplicable if the concern is *lishma*, since *lishma* problems exist uniformly across the diaspora. The Gemara rejects this inference: even within a single diaspora *medina* the declaration is required; the *Mishnah*'s statement about traveling between *medinot* in *medinat hayam* is meant to contrast not with same-*medina* travel in the diaspora, but with *medina*-to-*medina* travel within *Eretz Yisrael*, which does not require the declaration. The *lishma* framework then handles this contrast naturally: Israel as a whole is the domain of proper halakhic knowledge, and the internal structure of Israel is irrelevant to that determination.

For Rava, the analogous challenge is met by invoking the institution of *olei regalim* — the pilgrimage festivals that brought people to Jerusalem from throughout the land. This constant, large-scale movement across all regions of Israel ensured that witnesses were readily and regularly available throughout the country, making the *edim metzuyim* concern inapplicable to any intra-Israel transit. When the Temple no longer stands, the Gemara substitutes *batei dinin d'kevi'ei* — the presence of fixed, established courts — which similarly attracted regular travel and ensured that witnesses from any corner of Israel could be found without undue difficulty. The Gemara acknowledges that this explanation works better in one direction of travel than the other, but the basic answer is that Israel's unique institutional life — whether centered on the *Beit HaMikdash* or on its network of permanent courts — provided the structural conditions under which witnesses were always effectively available, and thus the concern animating the requirement of *bifnei nichtav ubifnei nichtam* simply did not arise within its borders.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּמוֹתֵיב לַהּ הָכִי: הָא מִמְּדִינָה לִמְדִינָה בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל – לֹא צָרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers an alternate version of this discussion: **And some raise** this **objection like this,** by inferring differently from that ruling of the mishna. The mishna teaches: One who brings a bill of divorce from one region to another region within a country overseas must also say: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence. The Gemara infers from here that if he brings the document **from region to region within Eretz Yisrael, he is not required** to state the declaration.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבָּה נִיחָא, לְרָבָא קַשְׁיָא! לָא תֵּימָא: מִמְּדִינָה לִמְדִינָה בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל – לֹא צָרִיךְ, אֶלָּא אֵימָא: הָא בְּאוֹתָהּ מְדִינָה בִּמְדִינַת הַיָּם – לֹא צָרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** This **works out well according to** the opinion of **Rabba,** as the residents of Eretz Yisrael are aware of the *halakha* that a bill of divorce must be written for the woman's sake. However, it is **difficult according to** the opinion of **Rava.** The Gemara rejects this inference: **Do not say** that the mishna is teaching that **from** one **region to** another **region within Eretz Yisrael, he is not required. Rather, say: Within** one **region in a country overseas he is not required** to state the declaration.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל מִמְּדִינָה לִמְדִינָה בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, מַאי, צָרִיךְ?! לִיתְנֵי: ״הַמֵּבִיא מִמְּדִינָה לִמְדִינָה״ – סְתָם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this interpretation: **However,** if that is so, with regard to an agent who brings a bill of divorce **from region to region within Eretz Yisrael, what** is the *halakha*? Is **he required** to state the declaration? If so, **let** the mishna **teach** simply: **One who brings** a bill of divorce **from** one **region to** another **region** must state the declaration, **without specification,** and this would apply both overseas and in Eretz Yisrael.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם מִמְּדִינָה לִמְדִינָה בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל נָמֵי לֹא צָרִיךְ, דְּכֵיוָן דְּאִיכָּא עוֹלֵי רְגָלִים, מִישְׁכָּח שְׁכִיחִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** one who brings **from region to region within Eretz Yisrael is also not required** to state the declaration even according to the opinion of Rava, for the following reason: **Since there are** those **who ascend** to Jerusalem **for the pilgrimage Festival,** witnesses **are frequently** available and they can come even from one region to another.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינַח בִּזְמַן שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים; בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא בָּתֵּי דִינִין דִּקְבִיעִי – מִישְׁכָּח שְׁכִיחִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: This **works out well when the Temple is standing,** as there are those who ascend to Jerusalem for the pilgrimage Festival at that time. However, **when the Temple is not standing what can be said?** The Gemara answers: **Since there are** central **courts that are fixed** in a permanent location where everyone goes, witnesses **are frequently** available to ratify the bill of divorce.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ מֵהֶגְמוֹנְיָא לְהֶגְמוֹנְיָא. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: עִיר אַחַת הָיְתָה בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל וַעֲסָסְיוֹת שְׁמָהּ, וְהָיוּ בָּהּ שְׁנֵי הֶגְמוֹנְיוֹת שֶׁהָיוּ מַקְפִּידִין זֶה עַל זֶה, לְפִיכָךְ הוּצְרְכוּ לוֹמַר מֵהֶגְמוֹנְיָא לְהֶגְמוֹנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests a different proof: **We learned** in the mishna that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says** that **even** an agent who brings a bill of divorce **from** one **district to** another **district** must say that it was written and signed in his presence. **And Rabbi Yitzḥak said: There was one city in Eretz Yisrael, and Asasiyyot was its name, and it contained two districts that** divided the city. And its two governors **were** so **particular with each other** that they enacted travel restrictions that made it impossible to cross through the city, and **consequently they were required to say** the declaration when bringing a bill of divorce **from district to district.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרָבָא נִיחָא, לְרַבָּה קַשְׁיָא! רַבָּה אִית לֵיהּ דְּרָבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: This **works out well according to** the opinion of **Rava,** who holds that the reason is due to witnesses, as the witnesses could not pass from one district to another. However, it is **difficult according to** the opinion of **Rabba,** as he maintains that there is no need to state the declaration in Eretz Yisrael because its residents are aware of the *halakha* that a bill of divorce must be written for the woman's sake. The Gemara answers: **Rabba is of** the opinion that the reason is also in accordance with the opinion **of Rava,** i.e., Rabba agrees that one of the reasons for this *halakha* is because witnesses are not available to ratify the bill of divorce. He adds another reason, that they are not experts in the *halakha* of writing the document for her sake.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דְּאַתְיוּהּ בֵּי תְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Rather, what** then **is** the difference **between them,** i.e., between Rava's explanation and that of Rabba? The Gemara answers: **There is** a difference **between them** with regard to a case **where two** people **bring** the bill of divorce from a country overseas.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי, בְּאוֹתָהּ מְדִינָה בִּמְדִינַת הַיָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** the difference between the two explanations involves a case where one agent brought the bill of divorce from one place to another **within** a single **region in a country overseas,** where witnesses are available to ratify it.

The *sugya* of this *daf* has thus worked through the core of the *Mishnah*: the foundational debate between Rabbah and Rava, the major tannaitic dispute about what constitutes a *get*, the announced *halacha* following Rabbi Eliezer, and an analysis of how the geographic debates within the *Mishnah* — near versus far, coming in versus going out, one *medina* versus another — each reflect and are illuminated by the two competing rationales.
