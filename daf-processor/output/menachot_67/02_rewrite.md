# Menachot 67 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Akiva & Ma'aser

### Tannaitic Debate Setup

The *mishnah* taught that when the extra flour from the *omer* process is redeemed and sold, the buyer is exempt from *ma'aser* but obligated in *challah*. The underlying logic is straightforward: the obligation of *ma'aser* is defined at the moment of *miruach* — when the grain is smoothed over in the pile — and at that moment the grain was owned by the *Beis Hamikdash*, i.e., *hekdesh*, and therefore exempt. The obligation of *challah*, by contrast, is defined at the moment the flour is kneaded into dough, and by that point the grain has already been redeemed and is owned by the private individual, who is therefore obligated.

Rabbi Akiva, however, disagrees. He holds that the redeemed purchaser is obligated not only in *challah* but in *ma'aser* as well.

### Rav Kahana's Reading

Rav Kahana explains Rabbi Akiva's position by invoking a general principle attributed to him: *miruach hekdesh eino poter* — the smoothing of the grain pile performed while the grain is under *hekdesh* ownership does not exempt it from *ma'aser*. On this straightforward reading, whatever *hekdesh* did during its ownership of the grain does not create a lasting exemption once a private individual takes possession.

### Tosafot's Problem

Tosafot immediately raises a difficulty with this reading. If *hekdesh*'s *miruach* does not exempt, then the grain was *tevel* — untithed and forbidden — at the time *hekdesh* offered it as a *korban*. One may not bring as a *korban* something that is forbidden to be eaten. Clearly, then, the grain must have been exempt while it remained in *hekdesh*'s possession.

A more nuanced formulation therefore emerges: while the grain is still owned by *hekdesh*, it is indeed exempt. But the fact that *hekdesh* happened to own it at a particular stage does not cause it to retain its exempt status once the grain passes into private hands. That is what Rabbi Akiva means — not that *hekdesh* ownership never exempts, but that the exemption does not carry over to the subsequent private owner.

### Rav Sheshet's Challenge

Rav Sheshet tests this reading with a challenge from the *baraita* itself. The *baraita* records the position of the *tanna kamma*: the remainder of the three *se'im* of barley from the *omer* process is redeemed and may be eaten by anyone; one is obligated in *challah* but exempt from *ma'asrot*. Rabbi Akiva then says one is obligated in both *challah* and *ma'aser*. The *baraita* continues that the *chachamim* challenged Rabbi Akiva: if someone redeems flour from the temple treasurer in a normal case — where *hekdesh* owned flour and a private individual redeems it — the *halakhah* is that the person is obligated in *challah* (because they make the dough after redemption) but exempt from *ma'asrot* (because it became flour while owned by *hekdesh*). The same should apply to the *omer* remainder, yielding the *tanna kamma*'s ruling.

### Circular Argument Problem

The *gemara* points out that this challenge is entirely circular. The *chachamim* are proving their position against Rabbi Akiva by citing a case whose ruling is itself precisely what is under dispute. If Rabbi Akiva's position were truly a general principle — that *miruach hekdesh* never exempts from *ma'aser* — then citing the normal case of flour redeemed from *hekdesh* proves nothing, because he would simply apply his principle there as well.

The *gemara* therefore concludes that since the *chachamim* present the normal case as a valid proof against Rabbi Akiva, Rabbi Akiva must actually concede the normal case. He must hold, as a general matter, that *miruach hekdesh* does exempt — and that the *omer* is a special case for some other reason. The question becomes: what is that reason?

### Intent of Sanctification

Rav Gana bar Tachlifa posed a question to Rav Kahana that leads toward this resolution. A *baraita* records Rabbi Akiva's own rationale: *she'lo nitnu ma'os ella l'tzorech lahen* — the money used to purchase the grain was given only for the purpose of what was ultimately needed. As is known, *hekdesh* did not own grain fields; it purchased barley from private owners specifically for the *omer*. When *hekdesh* used its funds to buy the grain, the intent was to sanctify only the amount that would ultimately be needed for the *isaron* of flour used in the *omer* offering. The excess — the remainder that was sold off — was never intended to be sanctified in the first place.

On this reading, Rabbi Akiva is not making a general statement that *hekdesh* ownership fails to exempt. He agrees that it normally does. The specific feature of the *omer* purchase is that the sanctification was intentionally limited from the outset to the portion that would actually be used. The leftover was owned by *hekdesh* as a legal entity, but it was never intended to be *kadosh* as an object.

### Ownership vs. Sanctity

This distinction raises a conceptual point worth dwelling on. *Hekdesh* operates on two distinct levels: as a legal entity that can own property, and as a force that confers sanctity upon objects. Normally these two operate together — when *hekdesh* owns something, that object becomes sanctified. But they are not identical. The question of which dimension drives a given *hekdesh*-related exemption depends on the context.

By contrast, in cases involving *nezikin* — damages — the *gemara* in Bava Kamma rules that if an ox of *hekdesh* gores another's ox, or vice versa, no damages are paid. There the relevant factor is presumably not the sanctity of the object but the identity of the owner, since *hekdesh* is not a lay person subject to the normal laws of tort. By *ma'asrot*, however, what exempts the grain is not merely that *hekdesh* is the owner in a legal sense, but that the grain itself has acquired sanctified status — *kedushah*.

What Rabbi Akiva says about the *omer* purchase is therefore this: *hekdesh* did indeed purchase and legally own the entire batch of barley. But their intent was to confer *kedushah* only on the portion that would ultimately be used for the offering. The portion that in the end turned out to be superfluous may have been legally owned by *hekdesh*, but it was never the object of their sanctifying intent and therefore never actually *kadosh*. Since it was never *kadosh*, it was never exempt from *ma'asrot*, and whoever ultimately acquires it is fully obligated.

Tosafot notes a further implication: if the remainder was never truly *kadosh*, strictly speaking there was no need to redeem it. The requirement of redemption in practice is explained as a matter of appearances — since *hekdesh* was handling the entire batch, it would look to observers as though it were all consecrated, and allowing it to circulate without redemption would give the false impression that *hekdesh* property requires no redemption. The formal redemption is thus maintained for perception even though, according to Rabbi Akiva, the underlying reality is that the grain was never sanctified.

There is also an element here that resembles *yesh bereirah* — the principle of retroactive designation. Normally *yesh bereirah* refers to situations where something happens at the end and we say retroactively that it was always that way. Here the intent was stated at the outset, but the question of which portion of the grain that intent applied to was not resolved until after the fact. This is a somewhat different mechanism, but the conceptual kinship is notable.

### Rabbi Yochanan's Solution

Rabbi Yochanan confirms this resolution: *talmud aruch b'fi shel Rabbi Akiva* — it is an established teaching in the tradition of Rabbi Akiva that *she'lo nitnu ma'os ella l'tzorech lahen*, the money was given only for what was ultimately needed. Rashi elsewhere notes on the phrase *talmud aruch* that this does not mean Rabbi Akiva had received an explicit tradition to this effect; rather, Rabbi Yochanan is articulating what he understands Rabbi Akiva's reasoning to be, presenting it as if it were a formal teaching of Rabbi Akiva. The conclusion is clear: Rabbi Akiva's position on the *omer* remainder is not based on a general principle that *miruach hekdesh* fails to exempt. In general, he agrees that it does. The *omer* case is special because the sanctification was intentionally limited from the outset to the portion that was actually used.

## Miruach Framework

### Hekdesh vs. Non-Jew

Having established Rabbi Akiva's position, the *gemara* uses this as an opportunity to examine more broadly the framework governing when ownership by *hekdesh* and when ownership by a non-Jew exempts grain from *ma'asrot* at different stages of processing.

The *gemara* notes that by *hekdesh*, everyone agrees: *miruach hekdesh* — when the smoothing of the grain pile occurs while the grain is owned by *hekdesh* — exempts even if a private individual later purchases the grain. This was the common ground in the debate with Rabbi Akiva; the *chachamim* appealed to it as an established principle. But when it comes to a non-Jew: *miruach ovdei kochavim*, whether a non-Jew's ownership at the moment of *miruach* exempts, that is a dispute among *tannaim*.

### Why Hekdesh Exempts

The conceptual distinction between the two cases can be stated as follows. In both cases, the grain is owned at the moment of *miruach* by an entity that is not itself obligated in *terumos u-ma'asrot* — neither *hekdesh* nor a non-Jew is personally subject to these obligations. But *hekdesh* does more than merely own the grain; it transforms the status of the object itself. The grain becomes sanctified — *kadosh* — something that must be redeemed and that exists in a fundamentally different legal category. This change of status is what creates the exemption, and it is a more complete and robust transformation than mere transfer of ownership.

Non-Jewish ownership, by contrast, does not confer any such intrinsic status change on the grain. Whether it exempts depends on a different kind of analysis. One dimension of that question — which the *gemara* explicitly sets aside for the purposes of this discussion — is whether non-Jewish ownership of land in Israel removes or diminishes the *kedushah* of grain grown in that land, such that it would not be subject to *terumos u-ma'asrot* at all. That is the conceptual basis of the *hetter mechira*, but the *gemara* brackets that issue and proceeds under the assumption that the grain remains obligated on account of the land. The only question considered here is whether the act of *miruach* performed by or under the ownership of a non-Jew at the critical moment of processing is sufficient to exempt.

### Land Ownership Question

To be precise about the distinction: if one holds that non-Jewish ownership of the land itself exempts the grain from *terumos u-ma'asrot*, then it would not matter who performs the *miruach* — even if a Jew buys standing grain from a non-Jew and then threshes and processes it himself, the grain would remain exempt because it grew from land owned by a non-Jew. That is a separate and larger question.

The narrower question the *gemara* addresses is about the moment of processing. Here the scenario is: the grain is in principle obligated — either it belongs to a Jew who grew it on Jewish land, or for argument's sake we assume the non-Jewish land ownership does not exempt — and the only question is whether the non-Jew's ownership at the specific moment of *miruach* is sufficient to exempt the grain from *ma'asrot* even after a Jew subsequently acquires it.

### Non-Jew Loophole

This framing reveals a significant practical concern. If non-Jewish ownership at the moment of *miruach* does exempt, then a straightforward loophole is available to anyone who wants to avoid *terumos u-ma'asrot*: sell the grain to a non-Jew, have him perform the *miruach*, buy it back. The non-Jew profits from a small fee; the Jew avoids a tithe of ten or twenty percent. The *gemara* will shortly address how this loophole is handled.

### Terumot Separation Rules

The debate among *tannaim* is visible in a *baraita* about separating *terumot*. The *baraita* states: one may separate *terumah* from Jewish grain on behalf of other Jewish grain, from non-Jewish grain on behalf of other non-Jewish grain, and from Samaritan (*Kutim*) grain on behalf of other Samaritan grain — and from any of these on behalf of any other. This last provision — that one may separate from grain of any of these categories on behalf of grain of any other — is the position of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda.

Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Shimon disagree. They permit cross-designation within certain categories: Jewish grain may exempt other Jewish grain; non-Jewish grain may exempt Samaritan grain and vice versa. But one may not separate *terumah* from Jewish grain to exempt non-Jewish or Samaritan grain, nor vice versa — because the Jewish grain is biblically obligated while the non-Jewish grain is only rabbinically obligated at most, and one may not separate from fully obligated grain to exempt grain of a lesser degree of obligation, nor the reverse. The Samaritans appear here in the same category as non-Jews, a detail that Tosafot notes is in some tension with other passages in the *gemara* where the general assumption treats them more like Jews.

What this *baraita* demonstrates is the underlying debate: according to Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda, non-Jewish grain grown on non-Jewish land and processed by non-Jews is fully obligated in *terumos u-ma'asrot*, which implies both that non-Jewish land ownership does not exempt and that non-Jewish *miruach* does not exempt. According to Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Shimon, the non-Jewish grain is either exempt or of a lesser degree of obligation, consistent with the position that non-Jewish ownership or *miruach* does exempt. This is the *tannaitic* debate about *miruach ovdei kochavim*.

## Gilgul & Challah

### Parallel Key Moments

The *mishnah* identified two separate agricultural obligations — *ma'aser* and *challah* — with two different trigger moments. For *ma'aser*, the critical moment is *miruach*, the smoothing of the threshed grain into a pile. For *challah*, the critical moment is what the *gemara* calls *gilgul* — the kneading and working of the dough, or more precisely, as the *Rambam* clarifies in *Hilkhot Bikkurim*, the moment at which water is added and mixed with the flour, creating dough. The *baraita* on the *omer* remainder reflects the *tanna kamma*'s position: exempt from *ma'aser* because *hekdesh* owned the grain at *miruach*, but obligated in *challah* because the private individual owned the dough at *gilgul*.

The *gemara* now examines the parallel structure: just as *hekdesh* ownership at *miruach* exempts from *ma'aser* and non-Jewish ownership at *miruach* is disputed, what is the law when *hekdesh* or a non-Jew owns the dough at the moment of *gilgul* with respect to *challah*?

### Hekdesh at Gilgul

The *gemara* cites a *mishnah* from tractate *Challah* that addresses *hekdesh* ownership explicitly. The *mishnah* presents three cases. First: if a person sanctified his dough before *gilgul* and then redeemed it before *gilgul*, he is obligated in *challah* — the dough was in his possession as an ordinary private owner at the critical moment. Second: if he sanctified it and redeemed it after *gilgul*, he is also obligated — again, *hekdesh* was never the owner at the critical moment. Third, and most important: if he sanctified the dough before *gilgul*, then the *gizbar* — the temple treasurer — performed the *gilgul* while it was owned by *hekdesh*, and only afterward the original owner redeemed it, the dough is exempt. The reason is *she-bishaas chovasah hayesa peturah* — at the moment of obligation, it was exempt, because *hekdesh* owned it at *gilgul*.

The principle is thus symmetric: whether for *ma'aser* or *challah*, if *hekdesh* owns the grain or dough at the critical moment of processing, the subsequent private owner is exempt even after redemption.

### Rava's Question

The remaining cell in this framework is the non-Jew's ownership at *gilgul*. Rava posed the question: *gilgul oveid kochavim mai* — what is the law when a non-Jew owns the dough at the moment of *gilgul*? Is the result the same as *miruach*, where the *tannaim* disagree, or does *challah* operate differently?

### Ger Converting Case

Rava brings evidence from a *mishnah* regarding a convert. The *mishnah* states: a person who converted (*ger she-nitgayer*) and had dough in his house — if the dough was made (*na'aseit issa*) before he converted, it is exempt from *challah*; if it was made after he converted, it is obligated; if the timing is uncertain, it is treated stringently as obligated. The implicit assumption is that when a non-Jew owned the dough at the moment it became dough, it is exempt — which is why the *mishnah* requires determining the precise moment of conversion. The *gemara* understands *na'aseit issa* as equivalent to *gilgul*, though it notes that the terminology differs. As the *Rambam* and *Ra'avad* make clear, the moment of *chiyuv* for *challah* is when the water is added and mixed with the flour, even before full kneading — and *na'aseit issa* fits this description well.

On the basis of this *mishnah*, it appears that non-Jewish ownership at *gilgul* exempts from *challah* — but the question is whether this is a consensus or reflects only one side of the *tannaitic* dispute over *miruach ovdei kochavim*. The *gemara* raises both possibilities.

On one reading, this *mishnah* reflects the position of everyone, including Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda who obligate non-Jewish grain in *ma'aser*. The reason would be technical: the word *degancha* — "your grain" — appears multiple times in the verses discussing *ma'aser*, creating a mechanism by which a limiting term (*mi'ut*) followed by another limiting term (*mi'ut achar mi'ut*) becomes inclusive, thus obligating non-Jewish grain despite their ownership. But the parallel term *arisoteichem* — "your dough" — appears only twice in the verses relating to *challah*, and one of those appearances is needed to convey the required *shiur* of dough rather than to include or exclude anyone. That leaves only one term to do the excluding, and that single term excludes both non-Jewish dough and *hekdesh* dough equally, with no additional term available to come back and include non-Jewish dough. Hence, even Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda might concede that non-Jewish dough is exempt from *challah* even while they obligate non-Jewish grain in *ma'aser*.

On the alternative reading, the *mishnah* of the *ger* reflects the view of Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Shimon, who exempt non-Jewish grain from *ma'aser* as well. The debate about *miruach ovdei kochavim* would then carry over consistently to *gilgul ovdei kochavim*.

Rava confessed he did not know which of these readings was correct — he even expressed a wish that the answer would come to him in a dream. He later returned with an answer: the debate is consistent. He who says *miruach ovdei kochavim poter* will also say *gilgul ovdei kochavim poter*, and he who says *miruach ovdei kochavim eino poter* — namely Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda — will also say *gilgul ovdei kochavim eino poter*.

### Rav Pappa's First Proof

Rav Pappa challenges Rava's conclusion, arguing that the exemption for non-Jewish dough in *challah* is in fact a consensus. He cites a *Tosefta*: a non-Jew who separated a *peter chamor* — the firstborn donkey (or the sheep designated to redeem it) — and also separated *challah*, is informed that these separations have no legal force. He was not obligated, so his acts of separation confer no status: the *challah* may be eaten by non-*kohanim*, and the firstborn animal has no sanctity.

Rav Pappa draws an inference from what the *Tosefta* does not say. The *Tosefta* mentions that the non-Jew's *challah* has no status, but conspicuously omits any mention that the non-Jew's *terumah* would similarly lack status. This argument from silence suggests that the *tanna* of this *Tosefta* holds that the non-Jew's *terumah* does have force — meaning that his grain is obligated in *terumah* — while his *challah* is exempt. This would mean that the same *tanna* who holds *miruach ovdei kochavim eino poter* (obligating *terumah*) simultaneously holds *gilgul ovdei kochavim poter* (exempting *challah*). The debate would not be consistent across *ma'aser* and *challah*, contra Rava.

### Rav Pappa's Second Proof

Ravina reinforces Rav Pappa's challenge with an explicit *baraita* rather than an argument from silence. The *baraita* states: *challas ovdei kochavim ba-aretz u-trumaso be-chutz la-aretz* — the *challah* of non-Jews grown in the land of Israel, and the *terumah* of non-Jews grown outside the land, *modinu shehu patur* — we inform him that he is exempt and these separations have no legal force. The *challah* may be eaten by non-*kohanim*, and the *terumah* does not cause *dimu'a* (the prohibition that arises when *terumah* is mixed with *chullin* grain).

The clear implication is that the converse cases are legally meaningful: *terumah* of grain grown in the land of Israel by a non-Jew does have the status of *terumah*, and it does cause *dimu'a*. Yet *challah* of non-Jewish dough in the land is exempt. Again, this *tanna* distinguishes between the obligations: the non-Jew's ownership at *miruach* does not exempt from *terumah*, but his ownership at *gilgul* does exempt from *challah*. The debate is not simply parallel, as Rava assumed.

### Rabbinic Gezeirah

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty by distinguishing between the Torah's law and a rabbinic enactment. By Torah law, Rava is correct: Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Shimon are the authors of the *mishnah* of the *ger*, and their view is that non-Jewish ownership exempts consistently from *ma'aser*, *terumah*, and *challah*. According to their position, as a matter of *de-oraita* law, a non-Jew's *miruach* or *gilgul* should equally exempt.

The *baraisos* cited by Rav Pappa and Ravina, however, do not reflect the Torah's underlying law. They reflect a rabbinic *gezeirah* — specifically, the decree known as *gezeirah mishum ba'alei kisin*, a decree on account of wealthy people (*ba'alei kisin*, literally "people of pockets"). The concern is that large-scale grain merchants, seeking to avoid the substantial financial burden of *terumos u-ma'asrot* — which could amount to ten or twenty percent of their total crop — would exploit the exemption created by non-Jewish *miruach*. They would sell their grain to a non-Jewish partner, have the non-Jew perform the *miruach*, then buy it back, having effectively laundered the grain through non-Jewish ownership at the critical moment.

To close this loophole, the rabbis enacted that even according to Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Shimon — who on Torah grounds would exempt — the non-Jew's *miruach* is not treated as exempting in practice. The *baraisos* thus represent this rabbinic position: non-Jewish *miruach* does not exempt from *terumah* (because the rabbis closed the loophole), yet non-Jewish *gilgul* continues to exempt from *challah* (because no parallel *gezeirah* was enacted there). The underlying Torah law remains symmetric, as Rava said; only the rabbinic overlay creates the asymmetry.

Tosafot also addresses the scope of this *gezeirah*. There are two scenarios: a Jew who grew grain and sold it to a non-Jew to circumvent *ma'asrot*, and grain that was grown by a non-Jew from the outset. In the second case, the additional argument of non-Jewish land ownership could theoretically exempt the grain independently, making the *gezeirah* seem less necessary. Nevertheless, from other *gemarot* it appears that the rabbinic enactment applies in both cases, on the principle of *lo pelug* — the rabbis did not differentiate between scenarios when enacting a general decree.

### Public vs. Private

The *gemara* then asks: if Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Shimon hold by Torah law that non-Jewish *miruach* exempts, why not simply exploit that loophole for *challah* as well? Why is the *gezeirah* limited to *ma'aser* and *terumah*?

The answer is that by *challah* there are always other ways to avoid the obligation, and therefore the rabbinic enactment was unnecessary. The most straightforward alternative: *efshar de-afyei pachos me-chameshes reva'im kemach* — one can simply bake using less than the requisite *shiur* that creates an obligation in *challah*, which is five *reva'im* of flour plus a small additional amount. If a person wants to avoid the *challah* obligation, he can do so by adjusting the quantity of dough he makes. Since a private and easy alternative always exists, there is no need to close the non-Jew loophole — the loophole does not confer the same advantage it does for *ma'aser*.

### Alternative Loopholes

By contrast, the *gemara* notes that by *ma'aser* and *terumah*, the alternative loopholes available in principle are ones that are inherently public and therefore embarrassing to use. Rav Oshaya taught that one may avoid the obligation of *ma'aser* by bringing the grain into the house while the *motz* — the chaff — is still attached, since the obligation of *ma'aser* attaches only after the grain has been fully processed and brought in through normal channels. Similarly, one could bring the grain in through the roof or through a *karpif* (a back enclosure), bypassing the normal entryway, which also prevents the triggering of the obligation.

But these maneuvers are inherently visible: neighbors and passersby will see the grain being lowered through the roof or brought in unprocesed, and the person will be publicly identified as someone evading *ma'aser*. *Hasam be-farhesia zila bah milta* — there, it is done openly, and the disgrace attaches. Therefore people do not in practice use those loopholes.

The non-Jewish sale-and-repurchase arrangement, by contrast, is private. No one sees the transaction; it is a quiet, inconspicuous way to avoid a significant financial obligation. If the rabbis did not close that loophole explicitly, wealthy merchants would exploit it routinely. *De-hacha be-tzina lo zila bah milta* — here, the private nature of the act means there is no social deterrent. Hence the *gezeirah mishum ba'alei kisin* was necessary for *ma'aser* and *terumah*, while for *challah*, where the alternative is simply to make a smaller quantity of dough — equally private but always available — no parallel *gezeirah* was needed.

## Omer Service Mishnah

### Omer Preparation

The *mishnah* now returns to the *omer* service itself, describing the completion of the offering once the required *isaron* of flour has been set aside. The *mishnah* lists the steps: the oil (*shemen*) and frankincense (*levonah*) are applied; the *tenufah* — the waving — is performed; then the *hagashah* — the bringing near to the altar; and then the *kemitzah* — the taking of the handful. This sequence of *tenufah* before *hagashah* is consistent with the ruling in the preceding *mishnah* that for *menachot* generally, *tenufah* precedes *hagashah*.

It should be noted, however, that Rashi and Tosafot both observe that the overall order listed in the *mishnah* is not in fact the correct procedural sequence for preparing the *minchah*. The oil is not the first thing applied; rather, oil is placed in the vessel first, then the flour is added, then more oil is poured over it, then the mixture is *balal* — blended — and then a final *yetzikah* (pouring) is performed, with the *levonah* placed last so that it can be removed intact. The *mishnah* lists these elements in an abbreviated and out-of-order way, a point that both *rishonim* acknowledge without offering a full explanation.

### Kohanim Consumption

Once the *kometz* is separated and burned on the altar, the remainder of the *minchah* — consistent with the rule for all *menachot* — is eaten by the *kohanim*. This is the standard disposition of the *shirayim*, the leftover portion of any *minchah* offering.

### Public Distribution

The *mishnah* records that once the *omer* was offered, the people leaving the *Beis Hamikdash* would find that the marketplaces of Jerusalem were already full of roasted grain (*kemach kali*) for sale. Because the prohibition on *chadash* — eating grain from the new crop — applies until the *omer* is brought, the moment of the *omer*'s offering was the signal for the grain markets to open with the new harvest's produce.

### Early Cutting Debate

But this practice raised concern. For the markets to be immediately full of roasted grain at the moment the *omer* was brought, the sellers must have cut the grain before the *omer* was offered. Rabbi Meir says this was done *shelo birtzon chachamim* — without the approval of the sages. His concern is twofold. First, cutting grain before the *omer* impinges on the status of *reishit ktzirchem* — the *omer* is meant to be the first of the harvest, and premature cutting compromises that designation.

### Reishit Concerns

Second, by handling and preparing *chadash* grain while it is still forbidden — while it is still *asur* to eat before the *omer* is brought — there is concern that people might inadvertently come to eat it before the *omer*. The *gemara* will note that one way around the prohibition on cutting was to uproot the stalks by hand rather than with a sickle, since the specific verse says *reishit ktzirchem*, referring to the cutting; uprooting by hand would not technically violate this prohibition. Whether this was in fact what the sellers did, and whether the cutting was permitted even between the first cutting of the *omer* and its actual offering, are questions the *gemara* takes up on the following *daf*.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees with Rabbi Meir's assessment and holds that the *chachamim* approved of this practice — *birtzon chachamim hayu osim*. The reasoning for Rabbi Yehuda's position, and the precise contours of the relevant prohibitions, will be examined in the continuation of the *gemara*.
