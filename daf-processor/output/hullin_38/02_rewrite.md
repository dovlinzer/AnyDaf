# Hullin 38 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mesukenet & Pirchus

### Mesukenet Intro

The daf opens four lines down with the phrase *mai ninhu*, as the Gemara turns to the technical requirements governing a *mesukenet* — a moribund animal, one that collapses even when one attempts to stand it upright. Because such an animal may well have died before the *shechita* was completed, it must exhibit signs of life during the act of *shechita* itself to establish that the slaughter was performed on a living animal. These signs of life are collectively called *pirchus* — various forms of jerking and movement.

### Shmuel's Definition

Shmuel has articulated an expansively broad definition of what constitutes valid *pirchus*: any type of movement that is not *hamisa oseh* — that is, any movement that would not normally and directly result from death itself. Even mooing, Rav had noted, could constitute a valid sign. The Gemara now undertakes to sharpen this framework by asking: *mai ninhu dvarim shehamisa osa*? What precisely are the movements that death produces, and which must therefore be excluded from the category of valid *pirchus*?

### Exclusions from Valid Movement

The answer is formulated as a tradition transmitted in Shmuel's name: *haysa yadah kefufa u-feshtaytah davar shehamisa osa* — if the limb was bent and then extended outward, that extension is a natural consequence of death and does not count. By contrast, *peshutha ve-khafaftha* — if the limb was already extended and the animal pulled it inward — *dvarim she-ein hamisa osa* — that is not something death produces, and it therefore constitutes legitimate movement. The intuition is straightforward: a dying animal would be expected to go limp and spread out, not to draw a limb back toward itself.

### Mishnah: Hand Extension

The Gemara immediately asks: *mai kamashma lan* — what is this telling us that we do not already know? The Mishnah itself teaches that a *behema daka* — a small animal, a sheep or goat — *she-pashtah yadah ve-lo hechzira*, that extended its foreleg without pulling it back, is *pesulah*, invalid, *she-ein ela hotza'at ha-nefesh* — because that movement is nothing more than the departure of the soul.

### Inference: Retraction Va…

From this Mishnah one might infer that if the animal pulled its leg back, the *shechita* is valid. The Gemara, however, notes that this inference is too narrow. From the Mishnah alone one would have concluded that only the full combination — extension followed by retraction — would suffice. The novel contribution of Shmuel's tradition is that even retraction alone, without any prior extension, is valid. Extension without retraction does not suffice; retraction alone does.

### Tosefta Challenge

The Gemara then raises a challenge from a *tosefta*. Rabbi Yose cites a tradition in the name of Rabbi Meir: *go'eh be-sha'at shechita ein ze pirchus* — if the animal merely lowed during the *shechita*, that does not constitute valid *pirchus*. And Rabbi Elazar the son of Rabbi Yose adds in his father's name: even if the animal defecated (*hitila re'i*), or slapped its tail (*kishkesha be-zanava*), these too do not count. This is striking, because it amounts to a direct repudiation of Rav's positive list of valid signs. Everything Rav identified as sufficient, the Tosefta declares insufficient.

### Reconciling Lists

The Gemara works to resolve the contradiction rather than leave Rav and the Tosefta in flat opposition. The apparent conflict over mooing — *kashya go'ah ago'ah* — is resolved by distinguishing between *avi kala* and *ami kala*: a deep, resonant sound that clearly emanates from a living creature versus a weak, high sound that is merely the expulsion of residual air from the lungs as the animal expires. The former is a genuine sign of life; the latter is not. The conflict over defecation — *kashya re'i are'i* — is similarly resolved: *kan be-shotetes*, *kan be-mitarezet* — in one case the matter drips out passively, a mere evacuation of the bowel at the moment of death, while in the other it is forcefully expelled at a distance, an unmistakable sign of active muscular life.

It is worth noting a subtle structural asymmetry in the reconciliation as explained by Rashi. In the first pair — the vocal distinction — the stronger sign (*avi kala*, the deep sound) is listed first, followed by the weaker. In the second pair — the defecation distinction — the weaker case (*shotetes*, the dripping) is listed first, and the sign of life (*mitarezet*, the forceful expulsion) second. The reversal of order in an otherwise parallel structure is at least worth remarking upon, even if Rashi does not explicitly address it.

## When Sign Needed

### Rav Chisda: Middle

Having established what constitutes *pirchus*, the Gemara now turns to the question of *when* that sign of life must be exhibited. The issue is conceptually significant. What one is trying to establish is that at the moment the *shechita* was concluded, the animal was still alive — that it was the act of *shechita* itself, and not a prior natural death, that caused the animal's demise. This generates a genuine tension. If one demands the sign of life before the *shechita* is complete, one faces the concern that the animal might have died in the interval between the sign and the end of the cut. If one demands it after the *shechita* is complete, one may be asking for the impossible — a fully slaughtered animal exhibiting signs of life. Three opinions emerge in the Gemara.

Rav Chisda states that the *pirchus* must occur *be-sof shechita* — at the end of the *shechita*. The Gemara — or, according to Rashi, Rav Chisda himself — immediately reframes this: *mai besof shechita? Be-emtza shechita* — what does "the end" mean here? It means the middle, not the literal conclusion. The purpose of the formulation is *le-afokei techilat shechita d'lo* — to exclude the very beginning of the process, which proves nothing. The sign must appear during the act — close enough to the end to be meaningful — but to demand it at the absolute conclusion of the *shechita* would be to demand the unreasonable.

The textual basis offered by Rav Chisda is the Mishnah's ruling that a *behema daka* which extended its foreleg without retracting it is invalid. The Gemara reasons: if this standard applied to movements after the *shechita* was fully complete, how could any movement fail to suffice? *kol heikha de-teikhei ve-teizeil* — would you expect the animal to go on living? Even minimal movement after a completed *shechita* should be sufficient. Therefore, the Mishnah must be speaking about movements during the process itself, establishing that "end" means "middle."

### Rava: End Required

Rava takes a stricter position: *le-olam besof shechita* — the sign of life must appear at the literal end of the *shechita*. He responds to the apparent difficulty: *keshe-ani omer, kol she-eino oseh kein besof shechita, be-yadu'a she-nishmato nitla hemenu kodem lakhen* — if an animal does not exhibit this sign after the *shechita* is complete, one must conclude that it had already died before the *shechita* was finished. The logical structure is subtle. Rava is not claiming that the animal must literally be alive after the *shechita*; he is claiming that in the case of a *mesukenet*, where life during the *shechita* must be actively demonstrated, the only way to establish that it was alive at the critical moment is to see a sign of life even afterward. Absent that sign, one cannot retroactively assume the animal was alive when the *shechita* concluded.

### Rav Nachman: Beginning

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak takes the most lenient position: *pirchus she-amru bi-techilat shechita* — a sign of life exhibited even at the very beginning of the process suffices.

### Blood Squirt Proof

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak grounds his position in a Mishnah. Rabbi Shimon rules: if one performed *shechita* at night and the next morning found the walls — specifically, as Shmuel clarifies, the walls of the site of the cut itself, the *beis ha-shechita* — filled with blood, the *shechita* is valid, because one may presume the blood squirted (*zinka*), in accordance with Rabbi Elazar's position that squirting blood is a valid sign of life. The proof runs as follows: *ei amrat bishlama bi-techilat shechita shapir* — if you hold that a sign of life at the beginning of the *shechita* suffices, then of course this ruling makes sense, since one cannot know when the blood squirted. But if you hold that only a sign at the middle or the end is valid, then the ruling is puzzling — perhaps the blood squirted only at the very beginning, which on those views would not suffice. The fact that the ruling makes no inquiry into when the squirting occurred proves that even a beginning-stage sign is acceptable.

### Squirt as Stronger Sign

The Gemara offers a rejoinder: *ve-dilma shani zinuk de-adif* — perhaps squirting blood is a categorically stronger sign of life than ordinary limb movement. If so, the leniency of the ruling may reflect the superior quality of the sign rather than a general permissibility of beginning-stage *pirchus*. Squirting blood, which requires active cardiac output, might be accepted regardless of when it occurs, while other signs must still appear at the middle or end.

### Squirt Weaker Sign

The Gemara then tests whether squirting is indeed a stronger sign. One might ask whether Rabbi Eliezer's standard is more or less demanding than that of the other Sages. The Mishnah's structure is instructive: Rabban Gamliel required movement in both a hand and a foot; Rabbi Eliezer said *daya im zinka* — it suffices if blood squirted; the Sages then said *ad she-tifarches o be-yad o be-regel* — until it moves with a hand or a foot. The word *ad* — "until" — indicates that the Sages are setting a higher bar than what preceded their statement. Since the Sages must be responding to Rabbi Eliezer (if they were responding to Rabban Gamliel, they would have said "either the hand or the foot," not "until"), their use of *ad* shows that squirting is a weaker threshold than hand or foot movement. This seemingly demolishes the rejoinder.

The Gemara attempts a counterpoint, attributed via Ravina to Sama bar Chilkiya: perhaps Rabbi Eliezer is indeed more lenient than Rabban Gamliel but more demanding than the Sages — and the question of *adif* concerned only the comparison to the Sages. But the Gemara presses further: *u-mi-Rabbanan mi adif*? Is squirting really a stronger sign than what the Sages require? The Sages said *ad she-tifarches* — until it moves — indicating that hand or foot movement is the higher standard. Since that standard is stronger, and yet we accept its presence at any point during the *shechita* (according to the proof from Rabbi Shimon's ruling about nighttime *shechita*), certainly squirting — a weaker sign — should also be accepted whenever it occurs. The Gemara offers no rebuttal to this argument, leaving Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak's position in a structurally strong position at this juncture.

### Orphan Calf Proof

Rava supports his position that the sign of life must appear at the literal end of the *shechita* from a *braita* interpreting the verse in Vayikra: *shor o kesev o ez ki yivaled ve-hayah shivat yamim tachat imo u-mi-yom ha-shemini va-hala yeiratzeh* — an ox, sheep, or goat that is born must remain with its mother for seven days, and only from the eighth day onward may it be brought as an offering.

The *braita* derives several exclusions from the individual terms of the verse. *shor o kesev*: excludes hybrid crossbreeds — the animal must be of a single pure species. *o ez*: excludes an animal that phenotypically resembles a different species — it must not merely appear to be a crossbreed. *ki yivaled*: excludes a *yotzei dofen*, a Caesarean birth; only vaginal birth counts. *shivat yamim*: excludes an animal that has not yet reached the required age — it must be at least eight days old before it is acceptable. *tachat imo*: it must have had a mother.

This last point raises the case of the *yasom* — the orphan. What precisely does the verse exclude? *hai yasom, heikhi dami*? If the mother gave birth and then died, the animal had a mother momentarily and the exclusion would not apply. So the *braita* must be speaking of a case where the mother died at the very moment of birth — *ela d'mesa bahadi yelidta*, *zeh peiresh le-mita ve-zeh peiresh le-chayim* — the mother departed into death at the same instant the calf departed into life. In such a case, the calf is considered to have never had a living mother, and it may not be brought as an offering.

Tosafot raises the difficulty of *i efshar le-tzamtzem* — can one even posit a perfectly simultaneous moment? He responds that the verse may be speaking of a hypothetical the Torah nonetheless needed to address, or that in cases of doubt we rule strictly.

Rava reads this passage as proof for his position in the *mesukenet* context. The argument, as formulated by Rashi, is as follows: *ei amarta ba'inan chiyuta be-sof leida* — if one requires the mother to have been alive at the conclusion of the birth process, then the passage makes sense, because it addresses the case where the mother died at precisely the last moment. But if *chiyuta be-sof* were not required — if it sufficed for the mother to have been alive at some earlier point in the birthing process — then the verse's exclusion would have been unnecessary, since a mother who died earlier during the birth would have made the birth a *yotzei dofen* anyway. Therefore the Torah must be addressing a case where life is required at the end of the process.

Rashi derives from this that wherever the Rabbis require proof of life in a process (*kol milta de-ba'u bah rabbanan chiyuta*), that proof must manifest at the conclusion of the process (*ba'inan she-tehei chayut be-gmar diley*). By analogy, the *pirchus* in a *mesukenet* must appear at the end of the *shechita*. This is, as Rashi himself seems to acknowledge, a somewhat strained analogy — the two contexts are quite different in kind — and Tosafot offers comparably limited support. Nevertheless, it is the reasoning Rava invokes.

### Halacha Final

The Gemara concludes: *amar Rava, hilcheta* — the halacha follows Rava. The sign of life must appear at the end of the *shechita*. Examining the commentators and authorities, this appears to be the authoritative ruling.

## Movement Details

### Small Animal Front

With the halachic conclusion established, the Gemara turns to a *braita* — identified as a *tosefta* — that Rava cites as the basis for the final ruling, which spells out in detail the kinds of movement that count as valid *pirchus* and for which animals. By a *behema daka* — a small animal such as a sheep or goat — if it extended its foreleg without bending it back, that is invalid.

### Small Animal Hind

However, with respect to the hind leg of a *behema daka*, the standard is different: *bein pashtah ve-lo kefafah bein kefafah ve-lo pashtah kesheirah* — whether it extended without bending or bent without extending, either movement suffices. For the hind leg, a single unidirectional movement in either direction is valid.

### Large Animal Any

When it comes to a *behema gasah* — a large animal such as a cow — the standard is yet more lenient: *bein be-yad bein be-regel bein pashtah ve-lo kefafah bein kefafah ve-lo pashtah kesheirah* — whether it is the foreleg or the hind leg, and regardless of whether the movement is extension or retraction, any movement in any limb suffices. The only category where a single unidirectional movement is categorically invalid is the foreleg of a small animal moving outward.

### Bird Movement

When it comes to birds — *u-ve-of* — the standard is the most lenient of all: *afilu lo rifref ela gapo*, even if it did nothing more than flutter a single wing, *ve-lo kishkesh ela zenavo*, or merely flicked its tail, *harei zu pirchus* — that suffices as valid movement.

### Implicit in Mishnah

The Gemara asks: *mai kamashma lan*? What has this *tosefta* added that could not be inferred from the Mishnah? *kulam tanina hi* — it is all already implicit. The Mishnah invalidated only one specific configuration: a *behema daka* that extended its foreleg without retracting. From that precise exclusion, one can infer by *miklal lav atah shome'a hein* — by negating each element of the excluded case — that any deviation from it is valid: *yad ein, regel lo* — it is only a foreleg that is problematic, not a hind leg; *daka ein, gasa lo* — it is only a small animal, not a large one; and so on. The Gemara acknowledges, however, that one case could not have been inferred from the Mishnah alone: the bird. *of itzterikha lei d'lo tnan* — the *tosefta* was necessary to teach the case of the bird, which the Mishnah had not addressed, and which might have been assessed differently without explicit guidance.

## Non-Jew Shechita

### Basic Rule & Dispute

The Gemara now transitions to a conceptually richer domain: *shechita* performed by a Jew on behalf of a non-Jew. The next Mishnah states: *ha-shochet le-oved kochavim, shechitato kesheirah* — if a Jew performs *shechita* for a non-Jew, the *shechita* is valid. *rabbi eliezer posel* — Rabbi Eliezer declares it invalid.

### Rabbi Eliezer Logic

The Mishnah elaborates Rabbi Eliezer's position in unusually detailed fashion. *amar rabbi eliezer: afilu shechatah le-echol le-oved kochavim mi-chatzer kaved shelah, pesulah* — even if the *shechita* was performed for the explicit purpose of feeding the non-Jew from the lobe of the liver, it is still invalid. Why? *she-stam machashevet oved kochavim le-avodat kochavim* — because the default intention of a non-Jew is toward idolatrous worship.

Rabbi Eliezer's reasoning rests on two interlocking premises. First, the intent of the owner of the animal — even though it is not the owner who performs the *shechita* — is halachically relevant and can define the character of the act. Second, absent evidence to the contrary, one must presume that a non-Jew's purpose in the context of animal slaughter is to dedicate the animal or some portion of it as an offering to an idol. The fact that this applies even when the non-Jew specifically intends to eat from the liver — an organ one might assume would be consumed personally rather than sacrificed — reflects the view that even partial personal consumption does not preclude an idolatrous dedication of the rest of the animal.

### Rabbi Yosi Kal Chomer

Rabbi Yosi responds with a *kal ve-chomer*: *u-ma bi-mkom she-machshava poselet, ein ha-machshava holechet ela achar ha-oved* — in a context where intention is genuinely dispositive, namely in the realm of *kodashim*, we still hold that only the intention of the one actually performing the *avodah* matters; the intention of the owner is irrelevant, as established in *zevachim*. If that is true in the domain where thought carries the greatest legal weight, *bi-mkom she-ein machshava poselet* — in a domain like *chullin* where thought carries far less weight — *eino din she-lo tehei machshava holechet ela achar ha-shochet* — how much more so should only the *shochet*'s own intention matter, rendering the non-Jew's thoughts entirely irrelevant.

### Two Dispute Points

The Gemara proceeds to analyze the structure of the dispute. It observes that both the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer agree with Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Yosi, who had transmitted a tradition that even the owners of a *korban* can generate *pigul* — that the owners' intention is legally operative in *kodashim*. This shared premise establishes that the dispute between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer must lie elsewhere. Two distinct readings of the disagreement are proposed.

### Dispute: Intent

On the first reading, both the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer accept that the owner's thought can matter in principle — both in *kodashim* and by extension in *chullin*. Their disagreement is empirical: what does a non-Jew typically intend? *tanna kamma savar: ei shamina de-chashiv, in; ei lav, lo — stam machshavat oved kochavim le-avodat kochavim lo amrinan* — the *tanna kamma* holds that we do not presume idolatrous intent; unless we have explicit evidence that the non-Jew intended the animal as an offering, we do not penalize. Rabbi Eliezer holds the contrary: *stam machshavat oved kochavim le-avodat kochavim amrinan* — the default presumption, especially in the context of animal slaughter, is that the non-Jew intends an idolatrous offering. Then Rabbi Yosi comes along to reject the entire framework, arguing that even if we knew the non-Jew intended idolatry, *ze mechashev ve-ze oved lo amrinan* — we do not hold one person's thought operative when another person performs the act.

### Dispute: Owner Outside

The second reading — *ika de-amri* — shifts the axis of disagreement. On this view, both the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer agree that we know the non-Jew intends *avodah zarah*. Their dispute concerns whether the owner's thought can be operative in *chullin* at all. The *tanna kamma* holds: *harei milei bifnim* — perhaps the owner's thought can matter within the Temple, by *kodashim*, where thought is a central legal category, *aval bachutz, lo — chutz mi-pnim lo yafinan* — but outside the Temple we do not apply the same rule. Rabbi Eliezer holds that we do extend the rule: *yafinan chutz mi-pnim*. And Rabbi Yosi adds a final layer, arguing that even within the Temple the owner's intention should not be operative, because the principle *ze mechashev ve-ze oved lo amrinan* is absolute.

### Thought Framework

Underlying this entire discussion is a conceptual distinction worth making explicit. There are two ways to understand the role of *kavanah* — intention — in halachic contexts. One can hold that intention is a *per se* requirement: a distinct legal criterion that independently invalidates or validates an act, standing alongside the physical act itself. Alternatively, intention can be understood as what defines the act — not an independent criterion, but the lens through which the act is categorized.

This distinction is consequential for the question of whose intention matters. If an idolatrous thought per se invalidates — if thought itself is the operative mechanism — then there is at least an argument that anyone sufficiently connected to the act, including the owner, might generate the relevant legal consequence through their thought. But when we are speaking about *shechita* and the prohibition of *shechitat avodah zarah*, the mechanism is not thought per se; it is the definition of the act. A *shechita* performed as an idolatrous offering is defined as an act of worship and is forbidden. If that is the framework, then the person actually performing the *shechita* — and that person alone — defines through their intention what the act is. The owner, who is not performing any act, has no thought operative enough to redefine the act being performed by someone else.

This framework helps explain why the distinction between *kodashim* and *chullin* carries weight in the second reading of the dispute. In *kodashim*, where a *pigul* thought per se invalidates — where the thought itself is the legally operative event — it is at least plausible that anyone significantly connected to the offering can generate that invalidating thought. In *chullin*, where there is no category of thought invalidating *shechita* per se, and where what matters is solely how the act is defined, only the *shochet* defines the act. The distinction, then, is not merely about the importance of thought in one domain versus another, but about whether the halachic mechanism is thought-as-criterion or thought-as-act-definition.
