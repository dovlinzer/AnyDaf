# Hullin 38 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mesukenet & Pirchus

### Mesukenet Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי מְסוּכֶּנֶת? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כֹּל שֶׁמַּעֲמִידִין אוֹתָהּ וְאֵינָהּ עוֹמֶדֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of an animal **in danger** of imminent death? **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** It is **any** animal with regard to **which one stands it** on its feet **but it does not stand** unaided.

The daf opens four lines down with the phrase *mai ninhu*, as the Gemara turns to the technical requirements governing a *mesukenet* — a moribund animal, one that collapses even when one attempts to stand it upright. Because such an animal may well have died before the *shechita* was completed, it must exhibit signs of life during the act of *shechita* itself to establish that the slaughter was performed on a living animal. These signs of life are collectively called *pirchus* — various forms of jerking and movement.

### Shmuel's Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוֹעָה וְהֵטִילָה רֶיעִי וְכִשְׁכְּשָׁה בְּאׇזְנָהּ – הֲרֵי זֶה פִּירְכּוּס. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ לְאַבָּא לְאֹזוֹזֵי אוּנֵּי, שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר כֹּל שֶׁאֵינָהּ [עוֹשָׂה] דְּבָרִים שֶׁהַמֵּתָה עוֹשָׂה.
>
> **Translation:** If the animal **lows, or excreted excrement, or wiggled its ear** during the slaughter, **that is a convulsion,** and the slaughter renders eating the flesh of the animal permitted. Shmuel **said to them: Is it necessary** according **to Abba,** i.e., Rav, for the animal **to move its ears** during the slaughter, which requires a considerable life force? **As I say: Any** movements of the animal **that are not matters that the death** of the animal **engenders** are convulsions sufficient to render the slaughter valid.

Shmuel has articulated an expansively broad definition of what constitutes valid *pirchus*: any type of movement that is not *hamisa oseh* — that is, any movement that would not normally and directly result from death itself. Even mooing, Rav had noted, could constitute a valid sign. The Gemara now undertakes to sharpen this framework by asking: *mai ninhu dvarim shehamisa osa*? What precisely are the movements that death produces, and which must therefore be excluded from the category of valid *pirchus*?

### Exclusions from Valid Movement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי נִינְהוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁהַמֵּתָה עוֹשָׂה? אָמַר רַב עָנָן: לְדִידִי מִפָּרְשָׁא לִי מִינֵּיהּ דְּמָר שְׁמוּאֵל – הָיְתָה יָדָהּ כְּפוּפָה וּפְשָׁטַתָּה – דָּבָר שֶׁהַמֵּתָה עוֹשֶׂה, פְּשׁוּטָה וּכְפָפַתָּה – דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין הַמֵּתָה עוֹשָׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are matters that the death** of the animal **engenders? Rav Anan said:** This **was explained to me from Master Shmuel** himself: If the animal's **foreleg was bent, and** the animal **straightened it,** that is **a matter that the death** of the animal **engenders.** But if its foreleg was **straight and** the animal **bent** it, that is among the **matters that the death** of the animal **does not engender** and is a convulsion sufficient to render the slaughter valid.

The answer is formulated as a tradition transmitted in Shmuel's name: *haysa yadah kefufa u-feshtaytah davar shehamisa osa* — if the limb was bent and then extended outward, that extension is a natural consequence of death and does not count. By contrast, *peshutha ve-khafaftha* — if the limb was already extended and the animal pulled it inward — *dvarim she-ein hamisa osa* — that is not something death produces, and it therefore constitutes legitimate movement. The intuition is straightforward: a dying animal would be expected to go limp and spread out, not to draw a limb back toward itself.

### Mishnah: Hand Extension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? תְּנֵינָא: בְּהֵמָה דַּקָּה שֶׁפָּשְׁטָה יָדָהּ וְלֹא הֶחְזִירָה – פְּסוּלָה, שֶׁאֵינָהּ אֶלָּא הוֹצָאַת נֶפֶשׁ; הָא הֶחְזִירָה – כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** Shmuel **teaching us** with that statement? **We** already **learn** in the mishna: The slaughter of **a small animal that** during its slaughter **extended its foreleg** that was bent **and did not restore** it to the bent position **is not valid, as** extending the foreleg **is nothing other than** part of the natural course of **removal** of the animal's **soul** from its body and not a convulsion indicating life. **But** one may infer that if the animal does **restore** its foreleg to the bent position, that indicates life and the slaughter is **valid.**

The Gemara immediately asks: *mai kamashma lan* — what is this telling us that we do not already know? The Mishnah itself teaches that a *behema daka* — a small animal, a sheep or goat — *she-pashtah yadah ve-lo hechzira*, that extended its foreleg without pulling it back, is *pesulah*, invalid, *she-ein ela hotza'at ha-nefesh* — because that movement is nothing more than the departure of the soul.

### Inference: Retraction Valid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מִמַּתְנִיתִין, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: דַּוְקָא דְּכַיְיפָה וּפָשְׁטָה וַהֲדַר כָּיְיפָה לַהּ, אֲבָל פְּשׁוּטָה וּכְפָפַתָּה – לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If** the *halakha* is learned **from the mishna** alone, **I would say** that it is **specifically** in a case **where** the animal's foreleg had been **bent, and** the animal now **straightens** it **and then bends it,** that the slaughter is valid. **But** if the foreleg had been **straight and** the animal **bent it,** the slaughter is **not** valid. Therefore, Shmuel **teaches us** that if its foreleg was straight and the animal bent it; that is a convulsion sufficient to render the slaughter valid.

From this Mishnah one might infer that if the animal pulled its leg back, the *shechita* is valid. The Gemara, however, notes that this inference is too narrow. From the Mishnah alone one would have concluded that only the full combination — extension followed by retraction — would suffice. The novel contribution of Shmuel's tradition is that even retraction alone, without any prior extension, is valid. Extension without retraction does not suffice; retraction alone does.

### Tosefta Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר, הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: גּוֹעָה בִּשְׁעַת שְׁחִיטָה – אֵין זֶה פִּירְכּוּס. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר מִשְּׁמוֹ: אֲפִילּוּ הֵטִילָה רֶיעִי וְכִשְׁכְּשָׁה בִּזְנָבָהּ – אֵין זֶה פִּירְכּוּס. קַשְׁיָא גּוֹעָה אַגּוֹעָה, קַשְׁיָא רֶיעִי אַרֶיעִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita* to Rav's statement cited earlier. **Rabbi Yosei says** that **Rabbi Meir would say:** If an animal **lows during its slaughter, that is not** a **convulsion** sufficient to render the slaughter valid. **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Yosei, says in** the **name** of Rabbi Yosei: **Even** if the animal **excreted excrement or wagged its tail, that is not** a **convulsion** sufficient to render the slaughter valid. The Gemara now clarifies: Rabbi Yosei's statement in the *baraita* that if an animal **lows** it is not a convulsion sufficient to render the slaughter valid is **difficult,** as it contradicts the statement of Rav that if an animal **lows** it is a convulsion. And Rabbi Elazar's statement in the *baraita* that if an animal excreted **excrement** it is not a convulsion sufficient to render the slaughter valid **is difficult,** as it contradicts the statement of Rav that if an animal excreted **excrement** it is a convulsion.

The Gemara then raises a challenge from a *tosefta*. Rabbi Yose cites a tradition in the name of Rabbi Meir: *go'eh be-sha'at shechita ein ze pirchus* — if the animal merely lowed during the *shechita*, that does not constitute valid *pirchus*. And Rabbi Elazar the son of Rabbi Yose adds in his father's name: even if the animal defecated (*hitila re'i*), or slapped its tail (*kishkesha be-zanava*), these too do not count. This is striking, because it amounts to a direct repudiation of Rav's positive list of valid signs. Everything Rav identified as sufficient, the Tosefta declares insufficient.

### Reconciling Lists

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוֹעָה אַגּוֹעָה לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דְּעָבֵי קָלַהּ, הָא דְּעָמֵי קָלַהּ. רֶיעִי אַרֶיעִי נָמֵי לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּשׁוֹתֶתֶת, כָּאן בְּמַתְרֶזֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The apparent contradiction between the opinion that when an animal **lows** it is a convulsion and the opinion that when an animal **lows** it is not a convulsion is **not difficult. This** opinion, that it is a convulsion, is referring to a case **where** the animal's **voice** is **rich** and powerful, a clear indication of life; **that** opinion, that it is not a convulsion, is referring to a case **where** the animal's **voice** is **muted,** which is not an indication of life. The apparent contradiction between the opinion that when an animal excreted **excrement** it is a convulsion and the opinion that when an animal excreted **excrement** it is not a convulsion is also **not difficult. Here,** the opinion that it is not a convulsion is referring to a case where the animal **expels** the excrement **in a trickle.** That is not an indication of life. **There,** the opinion that it is a convulsion is referring to a case where the animal **expels** the excrement **with force.** That is an indication of life.

The Gemara works to resolve the contradiction rather than leave Rav and the Tosefta in flat opposition. The apparent conflict over mooing — *kashya go'ah ago'ah* — is resolved by distinguishing between *avi kala* and *ami kala*: a deep, resonant sound that clearly emanates from a living creature versus a weak, high sound that is merely the expulsion of residual air from the lungs as the animal expires. The former is a genuine sign of life; the latter is not. The conflict over defecation — *kashya re'i are'i* — is similarly resolved: *kan be-shotetes*, *kan be-mitarezet* — in one case the matter drips out passively, a mere evacuation of the bowel at the moment of death, while in the other it is forcefully expelled at a distance, an unmistakable sign of active muscular life.

It is worth noting a subtle structural asymmetry in the reconciliation as explained by Rashi. In the first pair — the vocal distinction — the stronger sign (*avi kala*, the deep sound) is listed first, followed by the weaker. In the second pair — the defecation distinction — the weaker case (*shotetes*, the dripping) is listed first, and the sign of life (*mitarezet*, the forceful expulsion) second. The reversal of order in an otherwise parallel structure is at least worth remarking upon, even if Rashi does not explicitly address it.

## When Sign Needed

### Rav Chisda: Middle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: פִּירְכּוּס שֶׁאָמְרוּ – בְּסוֹף שְׁחִיטָה. מַאי בְּסוֹף שְׁחִיטָה? בְּאֶמְצַע שְׁחִיטָה, לְאַפּוֹקֵי תְּחִלַּת שְׁחִיטָה דְּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues its definition of convulsion that indicates life. **Rav Ḥisda said:** I was taught that **the convulsion that** the Sages **said** is an indication of life is a convulsion **at the conclusion of** the act of **slaughter.** Rav Ḥisda elaborates: **What** is the meaning of: **At the conclusion of** the act of **slaughter?** It means even **in the midst of the slaughter.** The Sages said that it must be at the conclusion of the slaughter only **to exclude** convulsions at **the beginning of** the act of **slaughter, which** are **not** an indication of life.

Having established what constitutes *pirchus*, the Gemara now turns to the question of *when* that sign of life must be exhibited. The issue is conceptually significant. What one is trying to establish is that at the moment the *shechita* was concluded, the animal was still alive — that it was the act of *shechita* itself, and not a prior natural death, that caused the animal's demise. This generates a genuine tension. If one demands the sign of life before the *shechita* is complete, one faces the concern that the animal might have died in the interval between the sign and the end of the cut. If one demands it after the *shechita* is complete, one may be asking for the impossible — a fully slaughtered animal exhibiting signs of life. Three opinions emerge in the Gemara.

Rav Chisda states that the *pirchus* must occur *be-sof shechita* — at the end of the *shechita*. The Gemara — or, according to Rashi, Rav Chisda himself — immediately reframes this: *mai besof shechita? Be-emtza shechita* — what does "the end" mean here? It means the middle, not the literal conclusion. The purpose of the formulation is *le-afokei techilat shechita d'lo* — to exclude the very beginning of the process, which proves nothing. The sign must appear during the act — close enough to the end to be meaningful — but to demand it at the absolute conclusion of the *shechita* would be to demand the unreasonable.

The textual basis offered by Rav Chisda is the Mishnah's ruling that a *behema daka* which extended its foreleg without retracting it is invalid. The Gemara reasons: if this standard applied to movements after the *shechita* was fully complete, how could any movement fail to suffice? *kol heikha de-teikhei ve-teizeel* — would you expect the animal to go on living? Even minimal movement after a completed *shechita* should be sufficient. Therefore, the Mishnah must be speaking about movements during the process itself, establishing that "end" means "middle."

### Rava: End Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: לְעוֹלָם בְּסוֹף שְׁחִיטָה, שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁאֵינָהּ עוֹשֶׂה כֵּן בְּסוֹף שְׁחִיטָה – בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁנִּשְׁמָתָהּ נְטוּלָה הֵימֶנָּה קוֹדֶם לָכֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Rav Ḥisda: That is no proof. **Actually,** one could explain that the mishna is referring to a case where the animal extends its foreleg but did not restore it to the bent position at **the conclusion of** the **slaughter, as I say** with regard to **any** animal **that does not do so at the conclusion of** the **slaughter, it is known that its soul was taken from it before that** moment, and it was not alive.

Rava takes a stricter position: *le-olam besof shechita* — the sign of life must appear at the literal end of the *shechita*. He responds to the apparent difficulty: *keshe-ani omer, kol she-eino oseh kein besof shechita, be-yadu'a she-nishmato nitla hemenu kodem lakhen* — if an animal does not exhibit this sign after the *shechita* is complete, one must conclude that it had already died before the *shechita* was finished. The logical structure is subtle. Rava is not claiming that the animal must literally be alive after the *shechita*; he is claiming that in the case of a *mesukenet*, where life during the *shechita* must be actively demonstrated, the only way to establish that it was alive at the critical moment is to see a sign of life even afterward. Absent that sign, one cannot retroactively assume the animal was alive when the *shechita* concluded.

### Rav Nachman: Beginning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: פִּירְכּוּס שֶׁאָמְרוּ – בִּתְחִלַּת שְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: The convulsion that** the Sages **said** is an indication of life is a convulsion even **at the beginning of** the act of **slaughter.**

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak takes the most lenient position: *pirchus she-amru bi-techilat shechita* — a sign of life exhibited even at the very beginning of the process suffices.

### Blood Squirt Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דִּתְנַן, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: הַשּׁוֹחֵט בַּלַּיְלָה, וּלְמָחָר מָצָא כְּתָלִים מְלֵאִים דָּם – כְּשֵׁרָה, שֶׁזִּינְּקָה, וּכְמִדַּת רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כּוֹתְלֵי בֵּית שְׁחִיטָה שָׁנִינוּ. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא בִּתְחִלַּת שְׁחִיטָה – שַׁפִּיר, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ בְּסוֹף שְׁחִיטָה, לֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא בִּתְחִלַּת שְׁחִיטָה זִינְּקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak** explained further and **said: From where do I say** that this is so? It is from the mishna, **as we learned** that **Rabbi Shimon says:** In the case of **one who slaughters at night and the next day** he awoke **and found walls full of blood,** the slaughter is **valid,** as it is clear **that** the blood **spurted, and** this is **in accordance with the rule of Rabbi Eliezer. And Shmuel said** with regard to the walls mentioned in the statement of Rabbi Shimon: It is **the walls of the place of the slaughter,** i.e., the walls of the neck, not the walls of the house, that **we learned. Granted, if you say** that a convulsion even **at the beginning of** the act of **slaughter** is an indication of life, this works out **well. But if you say** that a convulsion is an indication of life only **at the conclusion of** the act of **slaughter, let us be concerned** that **perhaps** the blood **spurted** onto the walls of the neck **at the beginning of** the act of **slaughter,** and there was no indication of life.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak grounds his position in a Mishnah. Rabbi Shimon rules: if one performed *shechita* at night and the next morning found the walls — specifically, as Shmuel clarifies, the walls of the site of the cut itself, the *beis ha-shechita* — filled with blood, the *shechita* is valid, because one may presume the blood squirted (*zinka*), in accordance with Rabbi Elazar's position that squirting blood is a valid sign of life. The proof runs as follows: *ei amrat bishlama bi-techilat shechita shapir* — if you hold that a sign of life at the beginning of the *shechita* suffices, then of course this ruling makes sense, since one cannot know when the blood squirted. But if you hold that only a sign at the middle or the end is valid, then the ruling is puzzling — perhaps the blood squirted only at the very beginning, which on those views would not suffice. The fact that the ruling makes no inquiry into when the squirting occurred proves that even a beginning-stage sign is acceptable.

### Squirt as Stronger Sign

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא שָׁאנֵי זִינּוּק, דַּעֲדִיף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that proof. **But perhaps spurting is different, as** it is **superior** as an indication of life to extending or bending a foreleg, and therefore, although extending or bending a foreleg is a sufficient indicator of life only at the conclusion of the act of slaughter, spurting is a sufficient indicator even at the beginning of the act of slaughter.

The Gemara offers a rejoinder: *ve-dilma shani zinuk de-adif* — perhaps squirting blood is a categorically stronger sign of life than ordinary limb movement. If so, the leniency of the ruling may reflect the superior quality of the sign rather than a general permissibility of beginning-stage *pirchus*. Squirting blood, which requires active cardiac output, might be accepted regardless of when it occurs, while other signs must still appear at the middle or end.

### Squirt Weaker Sign

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי עֲדִיף? וְהָתְנַן, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: דַּיָּיהּ אִם זִינְּקָה, קַל מִדְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, וַעֲדִיף מִדְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is** spurting **superior? But didn't we learn** in the mishna: **Rabbi Eliezer says it is sufficient if** blood **spurted** from the neck, indicating that spurting of blood is a less substantive indication of life than those indications mentioned in the statement of Rabban Gamliel, who requires that the animal convulse with its foreleg and with its hind leg? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Eliezer's requirement of spurting is **less** substantive **than** the requirements **of Rabban Gamliel** as an indication of life, **but superior to** the requirement of **the Rabbis** as an indication of life, where the Rabbis require that the animal convulse either with its foreleg or with its hind leg.

The Gemara then tests whether squirting is indeed a stronger sign. One might ask whether Rabbi Eliezer's standard is more or less demanding than that of the other Sages. The Mishnah's structure is instructive: Rabban Gamliel required movement in both a hand and a foot; Rabbi Eliezer said *daya im zinka* — it suffices if blood squirted; the Sages then said *ad she-tifarches o be-yad o be-regel* — until it moves with a hand or a foot. The word *ad* — "until" — indicates that the Sages are setting a higher bar than what preceded their statement. Since the Sages must be responding to Rabbi Eliezer (if they were responding to Rabban Gamliel, they would have said "either the hand or the foot," not "until"), their use of *ad* shows that squirting is a weaker threshold than hand or foot movement. This seemingly demolishes the rejoinder.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: אָמַר לִי סַמָּא בַּר חִילְקַאי, אַקְשִׁי בַּהּ אֲבוּהּ דְּבַר אַבּוּבְרָם, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אֲחוּהּ דְּבַר אַבּוּבְרָם: וּמִדְּרַבָּנַן מִי עֲדִיף? וְהָא תְּנַן: וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: עַד שֶׁתְּפַרְכֵּס אוֹ בַיָּד אוֹ בָרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Samma bar Ḥilkai said to me** that **the father of bar Abuveram, and some say** that it was **the brother of bar Abuveram, raises a difficulty: And is** blood spurting **superior to** the requirement of **the Rabbis** as an indication of life? **But didn't we learn** in the mishna: **And the Rabbis say:** It is permitted **only** in a case **where it convulses with its foreleg or with its hind leg,** or in a case where it wags its tail?

The Gemara attempts a counterpoint, attributed via Ravina to Sama bar Chilkiya: perhaps Rabbi Eliezer is indeed more lenient than Rabban Gamliel but more demanding than the Sages — and the question of *adif* concerned only the comparison to the Sages. But the Gemara presses further:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּנַן אַהֵי קָיְימִי? אִילֵּימָא אַדְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל – ״כֵּיוָן שֶׁפִּירְכְּסָה״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** Ravina elaborates: **To which** statement of the *tanna'im* in the mishna **do the Rabbis stand** and respond? **If we say** that they are responding to the statement **of Rabban Gamliel,** who requires that the animal convulse with its foreleg and with its hind leg, the Rabbis **should** have said: **Once** the animal **convulsed** with its foreleg or with its hind leg or it wagged its tail, indicating that this is sufficient.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא אַדְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, וְאִי עֲדִיף, מַאי ״עַד״?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is **obvious** that the Rabbis are responding to the statement **of Rabbi Eliezer. And if** Rabbi Eliezer's opinion is **superior** to that of the Rabbis as an indication of life, **what** is the meaning of: It is permitted **only** in a case where it convulses with its foreleg or with its hind leg or it wags its tail? In that case too, the Rabbis should have said: Once the animal convulses with its foreleg or with its hind leg or it wags its tail. Apparently, blood spurting is a less substantive indication of life, and the proof of Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak remains valid: If spurting at the beginning of the act of slaughter is a sufficient indication of life to render the slaughter valid, then all the more so, extending and bending a limb are also sufficient indications of life.

*U-mi-Rabbanan mi adif*? Is squirting really a stronger sign than what the Sages require? The Sages said *ad she-tifarches* — until it moves — indicating that hand or foot movement is the higher standard. Since that standard is stronger, and yet we accept its presence at any point during the *shechita* (according to the proof from Rabbi Shimon's ruling about nighttime *shechita*), certainly squirting — a weaker sign — should also be accepted whenever it occurs. The Gemara offers no rebuttal to this argument, leaving Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak's position in a structurally strong position at this juncture.

### Orphan Calf Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: פִּירְכּוּס שֶׁאָמְרוּ – בְּסוֹף שְׁחִיטָה. אָמַר רָבָא: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דְּתַנְיָא: ״שׁוֹר אוֹ כֶשֶׂב״ – פְּרָט לְכִלְאַיִם, ״אוֹ עֵז״ – פְּרָט לְנִדְמֶה, ״כִּי יִוָּלֵד״ – פְּרָט לְיוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן, ״שִׁבְעַת יָמִים״ – פְּרָט לִמְחוּסַּר זְמַן, ״תַּחַת אִמּוֹ״ – פְּרָט לַיָּתוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: The convulsion that** the Rabbis **said** is an indication of life is a convulsion **at the conclusion of** the act of **slaughter. Rava said: From where do I say** that this is the case? It is **as it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: "When a bull or a sheep or a goat is born, it shall be seven days under its mother; and from the eighth day onward it may be accepted for an offering to the Lord" (Leviticus 22:27). The phrase **"a bull or a sheep"** is **to the exclusion of** an animal born to parents of **diverse kinds,** which may not be brought as an offering. The phrase **"or a goat"** is **to the exclusion of** an animal **that resembles** another species of animal. **"When it is born";** this is **to the exclusion of** an animal **born by caesarean section.** "It shall be **seven days";** this is **to the exclusion of** an animal **whose time has not yet** arrived. **"Under its mother";** this is **to the exclusion of** an animal that is **an orphan.**

Rava supports his position that the sign of life must appear at the literal end of the *shechita* from a *braita* interpreting the verse in Vayikra: *shor o kesev o ez ki yivaled ve-hayah shivat yamim tachat imo u-mi-yom ha-shemini va-hala yeiratzeh* — an ox, sheep, or goat that is born must remain with its mother for seven days, and only from the eighth day onward may it be brought as an offering.

The *braita* derives several exclusions from the individual terms of the verse. *shor o kesev*: excludes hybrid crossbreeds — the animal must be of a single pure species. *o ez*: excludes an animal that phenotypically resembles a different species — it must not merely appear to be a crossbreed. *ki yivaled*: excludes a *yotzei dofen*, a Caesarean birth; only vaginal birth counts. *shivat yamim*: excludes an animal that has not yet reached the required age — it must be at least eight days old before it is acceptable. *tachat imo*: it must have had a mother.

This last point raises the case of the *yasom* — the orphan. What precisely does the verse exclude?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי יָתוֹם הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דִּילִידְתֵּיהּ אִמֵּיהּ וַהֲדַר מִתָה, לְעוֹלָם תֵּיחֵי וְתֵיזִיל? אֶלָּא דְּמִתָה וַהֲדַר יְלִידְתֵּיהּ, מִ״כִּי יִוָּלֵד״ נָפְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava elaborates: **What are the circumstances** of **this orphan? If we say that its mother gave birth to it and then died,** this is unreasonable. Will the mother **continue living forever? Rather,** perhaps the reference is to a case **where** the mother **died and then gave birth to it.** The Gemara rejects that possibility, as the fact that this animal is disqualified from sacrifice **is derived from** the phrase: **"When it is born,"** since after the mother's death the newborn animal can emerge from the womb only by means of caesarean section.

*Hai yasom, heikhi dami*? If the mother gave birth and then died, the animal had a mother momentarily and the exclusion would not apply. So the *braita* must be speaking of a case where the mother died at the very moment of birth — *ela d'mesa bahadi yelidta*, *zeh peiresh le-mita ve-zeh peiresh le-chayim* — the mother departed into death at the same instant the calf departed into life.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא, זֶה פֵּירַשׁ לְמִיתָה וְזֶה פֵּירַשׁ לְחַיִּים. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא בָּעֵינַן חִיּוּתָא בְּסוֹף לֵידָה – הַיְינוּ דְּאִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא לְמַעוֹטֵי, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לָא בָּעֵינַן חִיּוּתָא בְּסוֹף לֵידָה, לְמָה לֵיהּ? מִ״כִּי יִוָּלֵד״ נָפְקָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is **obvious** that the reference is to a case where the mother died at the conclusion of the birth, at which point **this** mother **withdrew for death and that** newborn **withdrew for life. Granted, if you say** that **we require life at the conclusion of** the **birth, that is why a verse was necessary to exclude** the orphan. **But if you say** that **we do not require life at the conclusion of the birth,** and the verse is excluding only an animal that was born after its mother's death, **why is** this verse necessary to exclude it? It can **be derived from** the phrase: **"When it is born."** It may be derived from here that in any situation where the animal must be alive, it must remain alive until the end of the process. That is the case with regard to the slaughter of an animal in danger of imminent death as well. The slaughter is valid only if there is an indication of life at the end of the act of slaughter.

In such a case, the calf is considered to have never had a living mother, and it may not be brought as an offering.

Tosafot raises the difficulty of *i efshar le-tzamtzem* — can one even posit a perfectly simultaneous moment? He responds that the verse may be speaking of a hypothetical the Torah nonetheless needed to address, or that in cases of doubt we rule strictly.

Rava reads this passage as proof for his position in the *mesukenet* context. The argument, as formulated by Rashi, is as follows: *ei amarta ba'inan chiyuta be-sof leida* — if one requires the mother to have been alive at the conclusion of the birth process, then the passage makes sense, because it addresses the case where the mother died at precisely the last moment. But if *chiyuta be-sof* were not required — if it sufficed for the mother to have been alive at some earlier point in the birthing process — then the verse's exclusion would have been unnecessary, since a mother who died earlier during the birth would have made the birth a *yotzei dofen* anyway. Therefore the Torah must be addressing a case where life is required at the end of the process.

Rashi derives from this that wherever the Rabbis require proof of life in a process (*kol milta de-ba'u bah rabbanan chiyuta*), that proof must manifest at the conclusion of the process (*ba'inan she-tehei chayut be-gmar diley*). By analogy, the *pirchus* in a *mesukenet* must appear at the end of the *shechita*. This is, as Rashi himself seems to acknowledge, a somewhat strained analogy — the two contexts are quite different in kind — and Tosafot offers comparably limited support. Nevertheless, it is the reasoning Rava invokes.

### Halacha Final

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הִלְכְתָא כִּי הָא מַתְנִיתָא – בְּהֵמָה דַּקָּה שֶׁפָּשְׁטָה יָדָהּ וְלֹא הֶחְזִירָה, פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: The slaughter of a small animal in danger of imminent death that during the slaughter extended its foreleg that was bent and did not restore it to the bent position is not valid, as extending the foreleg is only part of the natural course of removal of the animal's soul from its body and not a convulsion indicating life. **Rava says** that **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion expressed in **this *baraita*:** The slaughter of **a small animal that extended its foreleg** that was bent **and did not restore it** to the bent position **is not valid.**

The Gemara concludes: *amar Rava, hilcheta* — the halacha follows Rava. The sign of life must appear at the end of the *shechita*. Examining the commentators and authorities, this appears to be the authoritative ruling.

## Movement Details

### Small Animal Front

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? בַּיָּד, אֲבָל בָּרֶגֶל, בֵּין פָּשְׁטָה וְלֹא כָּפְפָה, בֵּין כָּפְפָה וְלֹא פָּשְׁטָה – כְּשֵׁרָה. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? בְּדַקָּה, אֲבָל בְּגַסָּה, בֵּין בַּיָּד בֵּין בָּרֶגֶל, בֵּין פָּשְׁטָה וְלֹא כָּפְפָה, בֵּין כָּפְפָה וְלֹא פָּשְׁטָה – כְּשֵׁרָה. וְעוֹף, אֲפִילּוּ לֹא רִפְרֵף אֶלָּא גַּפּוֹ, וְלֹא כִּשְׁכֵּשׁ אֶלָּא זְנָבוֹ – הֲרֵי זֶה פִּירְכּוּס.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **In what** case **is this statement said?** It is **with regard to the foreleg. But with regard to the hind leg, whether** the animal **extended it and did not restore** it to the bent position **or** the animal **bent it but did not extend it,** the slaughter **is valid. In what** case **is this statement,** about extending the foreleg, **said?** It is **with regard to a small** animal. **But with regard to a large** animal, **whether** the convulsion involves its **foreleg or its hind leg,** and **whether** the animal **extended it and did not restore** it to the bent position **or** the animal **bent it but did not extend it,** the slaughter **is valid. And** with regard to **a bird, even** if it **fluttered [*rifref*] only its wing or wagged only its tail, that is a convulsion** and an indication of life.

With the halachic conclusion established, the Gemara turns to a *braita* — identified as a *tosefta* — that Rava cites as the basis for the final ruling, which spells out in detail the kinds of movement that count as valid *pirchus* and for which animals. By a *behema daka* — a small animal such as a sheep or goat — if it extended its foreleg without bending it back, that is invalid.

### Small Animal Hind


However, with respect to the hind leg of a *behema daka*, the standard is different: *bein pashtah ve-lo kefafah bein kefafah ve-lo pashtah kesheirah* — whether it extended without bending or bent without extending, either movement suffices. For the hind leg, a single unidirectional movement in either direction is valid.

### Large Animal Any


When it comes to a *behema gasah* — a large animal such as a cow — the standard is yet more lenient: *bein be-yad bein be-regel bein pashtah ve-lo kefafah bein kefafah ve-lo pashtah kesheirah* — whether it is the foreleg or the hind leg, and regardless of whether the movement is extension or retraction, any movement in any limb suffices. The only category where a single unidirectional movement is categorically invalid is the foreleg of a small animal moving outward.

### Bird Movement


When it comes to birds — *u-ve-of* — the standard is the most lenient of all: *afilu lo rifref ela gapo*, even if it did nothing more than flutter a single wing, *ve-lo kishkesh ela zenavo*, or merely flicked its tail, *harei zu pirchus* — that suffices as valid movement.

### Implicit in Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? כּוּלְּהוּ תְּנַנְהִי! בְּהֵמָה דַּקָּה שֶׁפָּשְׁטָה יָדָהּ וְלֹא הֶחְזִירָה – פְּסוּלָה, שֶׁאֵינָהּ אֶלָּא הוֹצָאַת נֶפֶשׁ. יָד – אִין, רֶגֶל – לָא; דַּקָּה – אִין, גַּסָּה – לָא. עוֹף אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ, דְּלָא תְּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** Rava **teaching us** in ruling in accordance with the *baraita*? He is teaching us **all of** those *halakhot* **we learned** in the mishna: The slaughter of **a small animal that** when being slaughtered **extended its foreleg** that was bent **and did not restore** it to the bent position **is not valid, as** extending the foreleg **is only** part of the natural course of **removal** of the animal's **soul** from its body and not a convulsion indicating life. It may be inferred from the mishna that with regard to movement of **a foreleg** alone, **yes,** it is not an indication of life, but movement of **a hind leg, no,** it is an indication of life. With regard to **a small** animal, **yes,** this is the *halakha*; with regard to **a large** animal, **no,** this is not the *halakha*, and extending a hind leg does indicate life. The Gemara answers: It **was necessary for** Rava to teach the *halakha* in the *baraita* with regard to **a bird, as we did not learn** it in the mishna.

The Gemara asks: *mai kamashma lan*? What has this *tosefta* added that could not be inferred from the Mishnah? *kulam tanina hi* — it is all already implicit. The Mishnah invalidated only one specific configuration: a *behema daka* that extended its foreleg without retracting. From that precise exclusion, one can infer by *miklal lav atah shome'a hein* — by negating each element of the excluded case — that any deviation from it is valid: *yad ein, regel lo* — it is only a foreleg that is problematic, not a hind leg; *daka ein, gasa lo* — it is only a small animal, not a large one; and so on. The Gemara acknowledges, however, that one case could not have been inferred from the Mishnah alone: the bird. *of itzterikha lei d'lo tnan* — the *tosefta* was necessary to teach the case of the bird, which the Mishnah had not addressed, and which might have been assessed differently without explicit guidance.

## Non-Jew Shechita

### Basic Rule & Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹחֵט לְנׇכְרִי – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה, וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר פּוֹסֵל. אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: אֲפִילּוּ שְׁחָטָהּ לֶאֱכוֹל לְנׇכְרִי מֵחֲצַר כָּבֵד שֶׁלָּהּ – פְּסוּלָה, שֶׁסְּתָם מַחְשֶׁבֶת נׇכְרִי לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of a Jew **who slaughters** the animal of a gentile **for a gentile, his slaughter is valid, and Rabbi Eliezer deems it not valid. Rabbi Eliezer says: Even if** the Jew **slaughtered** the animal with the intent **to feed the gentile from its diaphragm [*meḥatzar kaved*],** its slaughter is **not valid, as the unspecified intent of a gentile is** to slaughter the animal **for idol worship,** and it is prohibited to derive benefit from it.

The Gemara now transitions to a conceptually richer domain: *shechita* performed by a Jew on behalf of a non-Jew. The next Mishnah states: *ha-shochet le-oved kochavim, shechitato kesheirah* — if a Jew performs *shechita* for a non-Jew, the *shechita* is valid. *rabbi eliezer posel* — Rabbi Eliezer declares it invalid.

### Rabbi Eliezer Logic


The Mishnah elaborates Rabbi Eliezer's position in unusually detailed fashion. *amar rabbi eliezer: afilu shechatah le-echol le-oved kochavim mi-chatzer kaved shelah, pesulah* — even if the *shechita* was performed for the explicit purpose of feeding the non-Jew from the lobe of the liver, it is still invalid. Why? *she-stam machashevet oved kochavim le-avodat kochavim* — because the default intention of a non-Jew is toward idolatrous worship.

Rabbi Eliezer's reasoning rests on two interlocking premises. First, the intent of the owner of the animal — even though it is not the owner who performs the *shechita* — is halachically relevant and can define the character of the act. Second, absent evidence to the contrary, one must presume that a non-Jew's purpose in the context of animal slaughter is to dedicate the animal or some portion of it as an offering to an idol. The fact that this applies even when the non-Jew specifically intends to eat from the liver — an organ one might assume would be consumed personally rather than sacrificed — reflects the view that even partial personal consumption does not preclude an idolatrous dedication of the rest of the animal.

### Rabbi Yosi Kal Chomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: קַל וָחוֹמֶר הַדְּבָרִים, וּמָה בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁהַמַּחְשָׁבָה פּוֹסֶלֶת בְּמוּקְדָּשִׁין – אֵין הַכֹּל הוֹלֵךְ אֶלָּא אַחַר הָעוֹבֵד, מְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין מַחְשָׁבָה פּוֹסֶלֶת בְּחוּלִּין – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא הַכֹּל הוֹלֵךְ אֶלָּא אַחַר הַשּׁוֹחֵט?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says:** The **matter** of the intent of the gentile is irrelevant in this case, as can be derived by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference. **If in a place where intent** while slaughtering the animal **invalidates** the slaughter, i.e., **in sacrificial** animals, such as when slaughtering an offering with the intent to sacrifice it beyond its designated time, **everything follows only** the intent of the priest **performing the service** and not the intent of the owner, then **in a place where intent does not invalidate** the slaughter, i.e., **in non-sacred** animals, **is it not right that everything should follow only** the intent of **the one who slaughters** the animal?

Rabbi Yosi responds with a *kal ve-chomer*: *u-ma bi-mkom she-machshava poselet, ein ha-machshava holechet ela achar ha-oved* — in a context where intention is genuinely dispositive, namely in the realm of *kodashim*, we still hold that only the intention of the one actually performing the *avodah* matters; the intention of the owner is irrelevant, as established in *zevachim*. If that is true in the domain where thought carries the greatest legal weight, *bi-mkom she-ein machshava poselet* — in a domain like *chullin* where thought carries far less weight — *eino din she-lo tehei machshava holechet ela achar ha-shochet* — how much more so should only the *shochet*'s own intention matter, rendering the non-Jew's thoughts entirely irrelevant.

### Two Dispute Points

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי אִית לְהוּ דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּתַנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: שָׁמַעְתִּי שֶׁהַבְּעָלִים מְפַגְּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: In explanation of the dispute between the first *tanna* and Rabbi Eliezer, the Gemara explains: **These *tanna'im* hold** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, son of Rabbi Yosei, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer, son of Rabbi Yosei, says: I heard that the owners,** and not only the priest, **render** an offering ***piggul*** by means of improper intent. The same is true with regard to non-sacred slaughter, where the owners' intent for idol worship invalidates the slaughter, even if the slaughterer has no intent for idol worship.

The Gemara proceeds to analyze the structure of the dispute. It observes that both the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer agree with Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Yosi, who had transmitted a tradition that even the owners of a *korban* can generate *pigul* — that the owners' intention is legally operative in *kodashim*. This shared premise establishes that the dispute between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer must lie elsewhere. Two distinct readings of the disagreement are proposed.

### Dispute: Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיהוּ תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: אִי שַׁמְעִינֵיהּ דְּחַשֵּׁיב – אִין, אִי לָא – לָא, סְתָם מַחְשֶׁבֶת נׇכְרִי לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה לָא אָמְרִינַן. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר: אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא שַׁמְעִינֵיהּ דְּחַשֵּׁיב, סְתָם מַחְשֶׁבֶת נׇכְרִי לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה אָמְרִינַן. וַאֲתָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי לְמֵימַר: אַף עַל גַּב דְּשַׁמְעִינֵיהּ דְּחַשֵּׁיב, זֶה מְחַשֵּׁב וְזֶה עוֹבֵד לָא אָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **But the first *tanna* holds** that **if we heard** the gentile say **that he intends** the animal for idol worship, **yes,** his intent invalidates the slaughter, and **if** the gentile did **not** voice his intent before us, his intent does **not** invalidate the slaughter, as **we do not say** that the **unspecified intent of a gentile is for idol worship. Rabbi Eliezer holds: Although we did not hear** the gentile say **that he intends** the animal for idol worship, the slaughter is not valid, as **we say** the **unspecified intent of a gentile is for idol worship. And Rabbi Yosei comes to say** that **even though we heard** the gentile say **that he intends** the animal for idol worship, in a case where **this** owner **has intent** for idol worship **and that** other person is **performing** the slaughter, **we do not say** that the intent of the owner invalidates the slaughter.

On the first reading, both the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer accept that the owner's thought can matter in principle — both in *kodashim* and by extension in *chullin*. Their disagreement is empirical: what does a non-Jew typically intend? *tanna kamma savar: ei shamina de-chashiv, in; ei lav, lo — stam machshavat oved kochavim le-avodat kochavim lo amrinan* — the *tanna kamma* holds that we do not presume idolatrous intent; unless we have explicit evidence that the non-Jew intended the animal as an offering, we do not penalize. Rabbi Eliezer holds the contrary: *stam machshavat oved kochavim le-avodat kochavim amrinan* — the default presumption, especially in the context of animal slaughter, is that the non-Jew intends an idolatrous offering. Then Rabbi Yosi comes along to reject the entire framework, arguing that even if we knew the non-Jew intended idolatry, *ze mechashev ve-ze oved lo amrinan* — we do not hold one person's thought operative when another person performs the act.

### Dispute: Owner Outside

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: בִּדְשַׁמְעִינֵיהּ דְּחַשֵּׁיב פְּלִיגִי, תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: כִּי אָמְרִינַן ״זֶה מְחַשֵּׁב וְזֶה עוֹבֵד״ – הָנֵי מִילֵּי בִּפְנִים, אֲבָל בְּחוּץ לֹא, חוּץ מִפְּנִים
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** an alternative explanation of the mishna. It is **with regard to** a case **where we heard** the gentile say **that he intends** the animal for idol worship that the *tanna'im* **disagree. The first *tanna* holds** that **when we say** in a case where **this** owner **has** improper **intent and that** other person is **performing** the slaughter that the intent of the owner invalidates the slaughter, **this statement** applies only **inside** the Temple, in the slaughter of offerings. **But** with regard to non-sacred slaughter **outside** the Temple, the intent of the owner does **not** invalidate the slaughter, as with regard to deriving the *halakhot* of non-sacred slaughter **outside** the Temple **from** the *halakhot* of slaughter of sacrificial animals **inside** the Temple,

The second reading — *ika de-amri* — shifts the axis of disagreement. On this view, both the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer agree that we know the non-Jew intends *avodah zarah*. Their dispute concerns whether the owner's thought can be operative in *chullin* at all. The *tanna kamma* holds: *harei milei bifnim* — perhaps the owner's thought can matter within the Temple, by *kodashim*, where thought is a central legal category, *aval bachutz, lo — chutz mi-pnim lo yafinan* — but outside the Temple we do not apply the same rule. Rabbi Eliezer holds that we do extend the rule: *yafinan chutz mi-pnim*. And Rabbi Yosi adds a final layer, arguing that even within the Temple the owner's intention should not be operative, because the principle *ze mechashev ve-ze oved lo amrinan* is absolute.

### Thought Framework

Underlying this entire discussion is a conceptual distinction worth making explicit. There are two ways to understand the role of *kavanah* — intention — in halachic contexts. One can hold that intention is a *per se* requirement: a distinct legal criterion that independently invalidates or validates an act, standing alongside the physical act itself. Alternatively, intention can be understood as what defines the act — not an independent criterion, but the lens through which the act is categorized.

This distinction is consequential for the question of whose intention matters. If an idolatrous thought per se invalidates — if thought itself is the operative mechanism — then there is at least an argument that anyone sufficiently connected to the act, including the owner, might generate the relevant legal consequence through their thought. But when we are speaking about *shechita* and the prohibition of *shechitat avodah zarah*, the mechanism is not thought per se; it is the definition of the act. A *shechita* performed as an idolatrous offering is defined as an act of worship and is forbidden. If that is the framework, then the person actually performing the *shechita* — and that person alone — defines through their intention what the act is. The owner, who is not performing any act, has no thought operative enough to redefine the act being performed by someone else.

This framework helps explain why the distinction between *kodashim* and *chullin* carries weight in the second reading of the dispute. In *kodashim*, where a *pigul* thought per se invalidates — where the thought itself is the legally operative event — it is at least plausible that anyone significantly connected to the offering can generate that invalidating thought. In *chullin*, where there is no category of thought invalidating *shechita* per se, and where what matters is solely how the act is defined, only the *shochet* defines the act. The distinction, then, is not merely about the importance of thought in one domain versus another, but about whether the halachic mechanism is thought-as-criterion or thought-as-act-definition.
