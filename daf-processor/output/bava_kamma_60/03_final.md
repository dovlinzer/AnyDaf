# Bava Kamma 60 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Fire & Blowing

### Mishna: Adding Wood/Fire

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא אַחֵר וְלִיבָּה – הַמְלַבֶּה חַיָּיב. לִבְּתָה הָרוּחַ – כּוּלָּן פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** If **another came and fanned** the flame, and as a result the fire spread and caused damage, **the one who fanned it** is **liable,** since he is the proximate cause of the damage. If **the wind fanned** the flames, **all** the people involved are **exempt,** since none of them actually caused the damage.

The *mishna* establishes that when a fire exists but is too small to sustain itself — presumably one that would have gone out on its own — and someone intervenes to make it substantial, that intervening party bears liability. Three cases are given: one who adds wood to an existing fire, one who lights wood that is already present, and one who blows on a small fire and thereby turns it into a substantial and potentially dangerous flame. In each case, it is the person who transformed the fire into something dangerous who is considered its maker and who is held responsible.

This framing resonates with discussions we have seen around the *bor*: the essential question is not merely negligence in the abstract, but whose fire it is — who made it into a potential danger. That question connects directly to the broader debate of *eisho mishum chitzav* versus *eisho mishum mamono*: whether liability for fire flows from the person as an extension of himself, analogous to an arrow he shot, or from his fire as his property that caused damage.

### Liba vs. Niba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מַאן דְּתָנֵי ״לִיבָּה״ לָא מִשְׁתַּבַּשׁ, וּמַאן דְּתָנֵי ״נִיבָּה״ לָא מִשְׁתַּבַּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says** with regard to the correct text of the mishna: The **one who teaches** it using the word **fanned [*libba*] is not mistaken, and** the **one who teaches** it using the word **blew [*nibba*] is not mistaken.**

The *Gemara* opens with a textual observation from Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak. The *mishna* contains a verb meaning "to blow on" the fire, and there are two legitimate manuscript traditions for this word. *Man d'tani liba lo mishtabesh u-man d'tani niba lo mishtabesh* — whoever has the version *liba* is not in error, and whoever has the version *niba* is not in error. The version *liba* derives from *labbat esh*, the flame of fire, meaning to turn the fire into a flame. The version *niba* derives from *borei niv sefatayim*, God who creates the utterance of the lips, meaning the blowing produced by the lips. Both readings are linguistically grounded and textually valid.

### Blowing & Wind

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לִיבָּה וְלִבְּתָה הָרוּחַ, אִם יֵשׁ בְּלִבּוּיוֹ כְּדֵי לְלַבּוֹתָהּ – חַיָּיב, וְאִם לָאו – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a *baraita* in which **the Sages taught** the same idea explicitly: In a case where **one fanned** the flame **and** at the same time **the wind** fanned it, **if his fanning has** sufficient strength by itself **to fan** the flames, he is **liable** for damage caused by the fire, since even without the wind the fire would have spread. **But if** his fanning alone was **not** sufficient, he is **exempt.**

The *mishna* rules that if a person blew on a fire but the wind, rather than the blowing, was what actually turned it into a substantial flame, all parties are exempt — *libta ruach kulem patur*. This raises an immediate difficulty. It is worth noting that had the fire been left in one's possession and became dangerous, a question of responsibility to extinguish it might arise, akin to one who leaves a broken vessel in *reshut harabbim* and fails to clean it up, rendering it equivalent to his *bor*. But the *Gemara* proceeds on the assumption that the fire was left in a public space and the subsequent wind was one that could not have been anticipated.

A *braisa* then addresses the specific case where a person blew on a fire at the same time the wind was also blowing — a collaborative causation. The *braisa* rules: *im yesh belibta k'dei le-labbta chayav v'im lav patur* — if the person's blowing, considered on its own, was sufficient to create a substantial flame, he is liable; if not, he is exempt. The *Gemara* finds this troubling. If a person knowingly blows on a fire while the wind is simultaneously present, he is in effect combining his action with the forces at hand. Why should this not be treated like *zoreh* — winnowing — where a person throws grain into the air, the wind carries away the chaff, and he is fully liable for that act of *melacha* on *Shabbos*? He is using the wind as a partner in his act, and it achieves his desired result. Why, then, is he exempt in the *nezikin* case?

### Abaye: Separate Actions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁלִּיבָּה מִצַּד אֶחָד, וְלִבַּתּוּ הָרוּחַ מִצַּד אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where he fanned** the fire **from one side and the wind fanned** it **from the other side,** and the fire was blown in the direction the wind was blowing. Therefore, it is clear that his fanning did not help the fire spread, so he is exempt.

Abaye answers that the *braisa* is speaking of a case where the person's blowing and the wind's blowing act from opposite sides: *k'gon she-liba mitzad echad v'libta ruach mitzad acher*. If one imagines the fire divided in half, what the person did on his side had no meaningful effect, and the wind, operating entirely independently on the other side, is what created the flame. The two forces did not combine; they acted in parallel on separate vectors. The person's contribution was entirely inconsequential to what the wind achieved.

### Rava: Wind Predictability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁלִּיבָּה בְּרוּחַ מְצוּיָה, וְלִבַּתּוּ הָרוּחַ בְּרוּחַ שֶׁאֵינָהּ מְצוּיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** We are dealing with a case **where he fanned** it along **with a typical wind,** and this was not sufficient to cause the fire to spread, **and** suddenly **an atypical wind** came and **fanned it.** Therefore, he is exempt since he could not have anticipated this.

Rava offers a different answer. The case involves a person who blew on a fire under conditions of *ruach metzuya* — ordinary, predictable winds — and even accounting for those normal winds, his blowing was insufficient to create a flame. Then *ruach she'eina metzuya*, an extraordinary, unpredictable gust, arrived and turned it into a flame. Since the person could not have anticipated this unusual wind, he cannot be said to have harnessed the forces at his disposal. He did something insubstantial, and something entirely unrelated to and disconnected from his actions then created the danger. The question of whether he bears any responsibility to extinguish it if he is still present when this happens — given that the fire seems to develop simultaneously — is not directly addressed, though it remains an ever-present concern lurking beneath all of these *sugyot*.

### R. Zeira: Minimal Blow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר: כְּגוֹן דְּצַמְּרַהּ צַמּוֹרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said:** We are dealing with a case **where** he only **heated [*detzamera tzamurei*]** the fire by breathing on it, rather than fanning it properly.

Rabbi Zeira provides a third answer: the case involves blowing so faint as to be essentially negligible — *d'tzamra tzimurei*, the sort of breath one exhales on one's hands to warm them. Rashi understands it similarly. This was not a real blowing at all, certainly not in partnership with the wind. The wind is simply doing its own thing, and the person's act was entirely insubstantial by any measure. None of these three answers, however, embrace the straightforward reading of the *braisa*: that in every case where one's blowing was insufficient on its own, one is exempt, regardless of the wind's role.

### Rav Ashi: Winnowing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: כִּי אָמְרִינַן זוֹרֶה וְרוּחַ מְסַיַּיעְתּוֹ – הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְעִנְיַן שַׁבָּת, דִּמְלֶאכֶת מַחְשֶׁבֶת אָסְרָה תּוֹרָה; אֲבָל הָכָא – גְּרָמָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא, וּגְרָמָא בִּנְזָקִין פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: When we say** that one is liable in a case where he **winnows and the wind assists him, this statement** applies **with regard to** the *halakhot* of **Shabbat.** With regard to Shabbat, the principle is **that the Torah prohibited planned,** constructive **labor.** The primary consideration is that his objective is accomplished, even if he did not perform the entire act of labor. **But here,** in the context of damages, **he is** considered to have caused damage **merely** through **indirect** action, **and** one who causes **damage** through **indirect** action is **exempt.**

Rav Ashi takes a fundamentally different approach and offers the answer that does engage the plain meaning. *Ki amrinan zoreh v'ruach mesayato hainu l'inyan Shabbos* — when we say that winnowing with the wind's assistance makes one liable, that rule belongs specifically to *Shabbos*. The reason is *d'melechet machshevet asra Torah* — the Torah's prohibition on *Shabbos* was derived from the *mishkan* and requires intentional action directed toward a defined result. 

This application of *melechet machshevet* is striking, because ordinarily *melechet machshevet* functions as a limiting principle — if one intended one act and performed a slightly different one, there is no *chilul Shabbos*. Here, however, Rav Ashi invokes it in an expansive direction: since the person's entire intention is to separate chaff from wheat, we look past the specific mechanics of the throw and the wind's assistance and evaluate the intentional end result, which was achieved. The act of *zoreh* just is this: one throws the grain and the wind does the separating. Similarly, the act of cooking on *Shabbos* consists of placing food on a flame; the food then slowly cooks over an hour, and the person is liable even though he did not transform the food with his own hands — because that is what cooking means. One could say Rav Ashi means that *zoreh* is defined precisely as an act that uses the wind, whereas creating fire by blowing must be accomplished entirely by the person alone.

### Grama in Nezekim

For *nezikin*, however, Rav Ashi concludes: *aval hacha grama b'alma hu v'grama b'nizikin patur* — here it is merely *grama*, indirect causation, and indirect causation in the area of damages exempts the tortfeasor. This conclusion is genuinely surprising. One would expect the reverse: that *issurin* — violations of prohibition — focus narrowly on whether the precise prohibited act was performed, while *nezikin* — tort liability — is driven by negligence, and negligence clearly exists here. The person knew the wind was present and knew his action would combine with it to produce a dangerous fire. Yet he is exempt from *nezikin* liability while he would be liable for *Shabbos* violation. 

If one adopts the position of *eisho mishum chitzav*, the rationale can perhaps be understood: the fire must be traceable entirely to the person's act, and since it is not, we cannot call it his fire. If one adopts *eisho mishum mamono*, the irony deepens, though perhaps one could argue that since he did not fully create the fire — unlike the person who digs a *bor* entirely on his own — we cannot deem him the owner of this fire. Still, the broader asymmetry between *Shabbos* and *nezikin* liability remains a striking feature of this *sugya*.

### Tosafot: Two People

Tosafot press the logic further in their comment *v'hacha grama b'Nezikin*. Tosafot ask: if we replace the wind with a second person, do we reach the same result? Imagine two people who each blow on a fire, where neither blowing alone would be sufficient to create a flame, but together they are. Are we to say they are both exempt because no single one of them did it alone? Tosafot themselves find this conclusion difficult — it would be extraordinary to say that two people can together create a genuinely dangerous fire and bear no liability for it whatsoever. The resolution seems to lie in the difference between a *shutafus* — a joint enterprise — and the role of the wind. When two people each contribute half, they are equal partners and joint owners of the resulting fire. But when a person and the wind together create a fire, the wind cannot be a co-owner. One would have thought this means the person must claim full ownership of the fire he helped create using the available wind. Instead, the *Gemara* concludes the opposite: since the wind cannot be his partner, the fire is simply not his at all. It is, as Tosafot acknowledge, a bizarre result.

## Sending Out Fire

### Mishna: Sending Fire

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹלֵחַ אֶת הַבְּעֵרָה, וְאָכְלָה עֵצִים אוֹ אֲבָנִים אוֹ עָפָר – חַיָּיב; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי תֵצֵא אֵשׁ וּמָצְאָה קֹצִים, וְנֶאֱכַל גָּדִישׁ אוֹ הַקָּמָה אוֹ הַשָּׂדֶה, שַׁלֵּם יְשַׁלֵּם הַמַּבְעִר אֶת הַבְּעֵרָה״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one **sends** forth **a fire,** i.e., allows it to escape, **and it consumes wood, or stones, or earth,** he is **liable, as it is stated: "If a fire breaks out, and catches in thorns, so that a stack of grain, or standing grain, or the field, is consumed, the one who kindled the fire shall pay compensation"** (Exodus 22:5), which teaches that he is liable also for destroying the field itself.

The following *mishna* declares: *hashole'ach es hab'eira v'achla etzim o avanim o afar chayav* — one who sends out a flame and it consumed wood, stones, or dirt is obligated. Even if the fire did not fully destroy its target but merely scorched or blackened it, that constitutes fire damage sufficient to impose liability. The *mishna* then cites the verse: *ki seitzei eish u-matza kotzim v'ne'echal gadish o hakama o hasadeh shalem yeshalem hamav'ir es hab'eira* — when fire goes out and finds thorns and consumes a haystack, or standing grain, or a field, the one who kindled the fire shall pay.

The *mishna*'s choice of the verb *hashole'ach* — one who sends out — is worth noting. In Parshat Mishpatim there is a *pasuk* that reads *v'shillach es b'eiro*, which the *Gemara* interprets as referring to *shen v'regel*, but whose plain meaning suggests intentionally sending forth one's flame. This contrasts with the next verse, *ki seitzei eish* — the fire escaped — which describes an unintentional release. This distinction may track the difference between *eisho mishum chitzav* and *eisho mishum mamono*, and notably the *pasuk* of *v'shillach es b'eiro* is followed by *meitav sadehu u-meitav karmo yeshalem*, the obligation to pay from the best of one's property — a consequence that does not appear in the case of the fire that merely escaped. A related distinction between igniting fire on one's own property versus on another's will emerge shortly in the *Gemara*.

### Gemara: Multiple Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לְמָה לִי דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״קוֹצִים״, ״גָּדִישׁ״, ״קָמָה״ וְ״שָׂדֶה״?
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse cited in the mishna, **Rava says: Why do I** need **the Merciful One** to write in the Torah all of these terms: **"Thorns," "a stack of grain," "standing grain," and "field,"** which seem to be redundant?

Rava asks: *lama li d'chsav Rachmana kotzim gadish v'kama v'sadeh* — why did the Torah need to enumerate all of these examples? Could a single case not have sufficed? The *Gemara* answers: *tzrichi* — each example is necessary, because each one teaches something the others cannot.

### Common vs. Uncommon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכִי; דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״קוֹצִים״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: קוֹצִים הוּא דְּחַיַּיב רַחֲמָנָא – מִשּׁוּם דִּשְׁכִיחַ אֵשׁ גַּבַּיְיהוּ, וּשְׁכִיחַ דְּפָשַׁע; אֲבָל גָּדִישׁ – דְּלָא שְׁכִיחַ אֵשׁ גַּבַּיְיהוּ, וְלָא שְׁכִיחַ דְּפָשַׁע, אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״גָּדִישׁ״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: גָּדִישׁ חַיַּיב רַחֲמָנָא – מִשּׁוּם דְּהֶפְסֵד מְרוּבֶּה הוּא; אֲבָל קוֹצִים – דְּהֶפְסֵד מוּעָט, אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava explains: All the terms **are necessary, because if the Merciful One had written** only **"thorns"** in the Torah, **I would say** that it is specifically **thorns for which the Merciful One renders one liable, because** it is **common** for **fire** to be **near them, and** it **is common that** one is **negligent. But** with regard to **a stack of grain,** with regard to **which it is not common** for **fire** to be **near it,** as grain is valuable, so one keeps it out of harm's way, **and** it **is not common that** one is **negligent** in allowing it to catch fire, I would **say** that he should **not** be liable. **And if the Merciful One had written** only: **"A stack of grain," I would say** that it is specifically for such **a stack** that **the Merciful One renders him liable, because** it involves **a substantial** financial **loss. But** with regard to **thorns,** which involve only **a minimal loss,** I would **say** that he should **not** be liable. Therefore, the verse teaches that he is liable for damage to thorns as well.

Had the Torah mentioned only thorns and thistles — *kotzim* — one might have thought liability attaches specifically there *mishum deshachiach esh bahu*, because kindling is the sort of material that fire readily catches, making negligence on the part of the one who sent the fire highly likely. But one might have thought that if the fire traveled an unusual distance and consumed a *gadish* — a mound of grain — *d'lo shachiach esh beh*, since fire does not commonly spread that far, the person could argue that no one could have foreseen such damage, and therefore be exempt. The Torah tells us he is nonetheless liable even in that case.

Conversely, had the Torah mentioned only the *gadish* — the mound of grain — one might have thought liability is imposed there because *dehefsed merubeh*, it represents serious financial loss, and therefore the Torah holds the tortfeasor responsible. But perhaps for the minor loss of thorns and thistles one might be exempt. The Torah must therefore also explicitly include the thorns to establish that even minimal damage gives rise to liability.

### Standing Grain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״קָמָה״ לְמָה לִי? מָה קָמָה בְּגָלוּי – אַף כֹּל בְּגָלוּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Why do I** need the Torah to state the term **"standing grain"?** It is in order to teach that **just as standing grain is exposed, so too,** one is liable only for damage caused by fire **for all** items that are **exposed.** One is exempt from liability for damage to items that are concealed.

The Torah then mentions *kama* — standing grain. Why is this necessary? *Ma kama begalui af kol begalui* — just as standing grain is exposed and open, anything that is similarly exposed is included in the liability. This simultaneously establishes the exemption of *tamun ba'esh*: property that is hidden within a haystack, not visible and exposed, is exempt from fire liability. The question immediately follows: according to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that one is liable even for *tamun ba'esh* — property hidden within the haystack — what does the mention of *kama* come to teach? The answer is *lerabos kol baalei komah* — to include all property that stands upright, not limited to crops growing from the ground. Rashi understands this to encompass even animals and other types of property.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דִּמְחַיֵּיב אַנִּזְקֵי טָמוּן בָּאֵשׁ, ״קָמָה״ לְמָה לִי? לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל בַּעֲלֵי קוֹמָה. וְרַבָּנַן, לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל בַּעֲלֵי קוֹמָה מְנָא לְהוּ? נָפְקָא לְהוּ מֵ״אוֹ הַקָּמָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who deems** one **liable for a concealed** article damaged **by a fire, why do I** need the Torah to state the term: **"Standing grain"?** The Gemara answers: The term serves **to include all** items **that have stature,** i.e., trees and animals, and not only produce. The Gemara asks: **And** according to the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who derive the *halakha* of concealed articles from the term "standing grain," **from where do they** derive that **all** items **that have stature** are **included?** The Gemara answers: **They derive it from** the term: **"Or standing grain,"** since the additional word "or" is an inclusive term.

Where do the *Rabbanan*, who disagree with Rabbi Yehuda on *tamun ba'esh*, find the inclusion of *kol baalei komah*? They derive it from the phrase *o hakama* — "or the field" — where the word *o* signals an expansion beyond what was previously stated. Rabbi Yehuda, for his part, reads the *o* of *o hakama* and the *o* of *o hasadeh* as each carrying an expansive function, even though one *o* would suffice — which sets up the need to explain what each teaches.

### Field: Charring

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שָׂדֶה״ לְמָה לִי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי לִחֲכָה נִירוֹ וְסִכְסְכָה אֲבָנָיו. וְלִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״שָׂדֶה״, וְלָא בָּעֵי הָנָךְ! צְרִיכָא; דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״שָׂדֶה״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מַה שֶּׁבַּשָּׂדֶה – אִין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא – לָא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues to elaborate on the different terms in the verse: And **why do I** need the word **"field"** in the verse? It serves **to include** liability for damage in a case when the flames **licked a plowed field and charred its stones.** The Gemara asks: **But let the Merciful One write** only the term **"field," and** then it would **not require all these** other terms. If one is liable for damage to a field, which is not totally destroyed by the fire, he is certainly liable for damage to other items that are completely destroyed. The Gemara answers: It is **necessary** to write the other terms as well, **because if the Merciful One** had **written** only **"field," I would say that** for **what is in the field, yes,** one is liable, but for **anything else, no,** one is not liable. Therefore, it **teaches us** that one is liable for any damage caused by fire.

This brings us to the final term: *sadeh* — the field. *Latuyei lechniro vesichsicho avanav* — to include even one whose field was plowed but left charred, or whose stones were scorched by the fire. This appears to be the *chiddush* of the *mishna* itself: that fire damage short of actual destruction — the mere charring of plowed ground or blackening of stones — is nonetheless compensable. One might ask: if the Torah had mentioned only the field, could we not have inferred that all the other cases of actual destruction are certainly included? The *Gemara* responds that without the earlier examples, one would have interpreted *sadeh* to refer to its contents — the grain, the haystack, everything within the field — not the ground itself. Only after the earlier items are listed explicitly can *sadeh* be understood to mean the very ground of the field, establishing liability even for charring alone.

## Theodicy & Fire

### Suffering & Wicked

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: אֵין פּוּרְעָנוּת בָּאָה לָעוֹלָם אֶלָּא בִּזְמַן שֶׁהָרְשָׁעִים בָּעוֹלָם, וְאֵינָהּ מַתְחֶלֶת אֶלָּא מִן הַצַּדִּיקִים תְּחִלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי תֵצֵא אֵשׁ וּמָצְאָה קֹצִים״ – אֵימָתַי אֵשׁ יוֹצְאָה? בִּזְמַן שֶׁקּוֹצִים מְצוּיִין לָהּ. וְאֵינָהּ מַתְחֶלֶת אֶלָּא מִן הַצַּדִּיקִים תְּחִלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנֶאֱכַל גָּדִישׁ״; ״וְאָכַל גָּדִישׁ״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא ״וְנֶאֱכַל גָּדִישׁ״ – שֶׁנֶּאֱכַל גָּדִישׁ כְּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites an aggadic midrash based on this verse: **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says: Calamity befalls the world only when wicked** people are **in the world, but** the calamity **begins only with the righteous first, as it is stated** in the verse: **"If a fire breaks out, and catches in thorns,** so that a stack of grain, or standing grain, or the field, is consumed" (Exodus 22:5). **When does the fire,** i.e., calamity, **emerge?** At a time **when the thorns,** i.e., the wicked, **are found with it. But** calamity **begins only from the righteous first, as it is stated** in the continuation of the verse: **"And a stack of grain is consumed [*vene'ekhal*]." It is not stated:** If a fire breaks out, and catches in thorns, **and consumes [*ve'akhal*] the stack of grain; rather,** it states: **"A stack of grain is consumed,"** meaning **that** the **stack,** i.e., the righteous, **has already been consumed** before the thorns.

The *Gemara* transitions into an extended *aggadic* section, prompted by the verse *ki seitzei eish u-matza kotzim*. Shmuel bar Nachmani said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *ein puraniyos ba l'olam ela bizman she-resha'im ba'olam* — suffering does not come to the world except at a time when there are wicked people in the world. But the statement does not end there. *V'eina matchala ela min ha-tzadikim techila* — yet when suffering arrives, it begins with the righteous first.

The verse is read as the vehicle for both claims. *Eimasai esh yotzei? Bizman she-ha-kotzim metzuyin la* — when does the fire go out? When thorns are present to catch it. The wicked are the thorns; their presence in the world is what unleashes destructive forces. And yet *vene'echal gadish* — the haystack, representing the righteous — is consumed. Notably the Torah says *vene'echal*, not *ve'achal*: the haystack was already consumed, as though it is the first thing taken. The Rabbis here are grappling honestly with the reality of *tzadik vera lo* — the righteous who suffer — not precisely explaining why it is just, but acknowledging it as part of how the world operates.

### Destruction Indiscriminate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּאנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאַתֶּם לֹא תֵצְאוּ אִישׁ מִפֶּתַח בֵּיתוֹ עַד בֹּקֶר״? כֵּיוָן שֶׁנִּיתַּן רְשׁוּת לַמַּשְׁחִית, אֵינוֹ מַבְחִין בֵּין צַדִּיקִים לִרְשָׁעִים. וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁמַּתְחִיל מִן הַצַּדִּיקִים תְּחִלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהִכְרַתִּי מִמֵּךְ צַדִּיק וְרָשָׁע״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef taught** a *baraita*: **What** is the meaning of that **which is written** with regard to the plague of the firstborn: **"And none of you shall go out of the opening of his house until the morning"** (Exodus 12:22)? If the plague was not decreed upon the Jewish people, why were they not permitted to leave their homes? **Once permission** is granted **to the destroyer** to kill, **it does not distinguish between the righteous and the wicked. And not only** that, **but it begins with the righteous first, as it is stated** in the verse: **"And will cut off from you the righteous and the wicked"** (Ezekiel 21:8), where mention of the righteous precedes the wicked.

Rav Yosef taught: *mai dikhtiv ve'atem lo tetze'u ish mipetach beito ad boker?* — what does it mean when Moshe tells the people on the night of *makat bekhorot* not to go out of their homes until morning? It might seem as though God's protection makes it safe and the warning is merely precautionary. No — *keivan she-nitan reshut la-mashchit eino mavchin bein tzadikim le-resha'im* — once destructive forces have been granted permission to operate, they do not distinguish between the righteous and the wicked. Innocent people get caught up in the destruction. *Velo od ela she-matchil min ha-tzadikim techila* — and beyond that, it actually begins from the righteous first. The proof text is *vehikhrati mimekha tzadik varasha* — I will destroy from you both the righteous and the wicked — with the *tzadik* mentioned before the *rasha*.

### Rav Yosef Weeps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּכֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: כּוּלֵּי הַאי נָמֵי לְאַיִן דּוֹמִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: טֵיבוּתָא הוּא לְגַבַּיְיהוּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי מִפְּנֵי הָרָעָה נֶאֱסַף הַצַּדִּיק״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef cried** and said: **Are all these** righteous people **also compared to nothing** when calamity strikes? **Abaye said to him: It is goodness for** the righteous that they die first, **as it is written: "The righteous is taken away because of the evil to come"** (Isaiah 57:1), so that he will not have to endure the suffering that will befall the people.

Upon hearing this, Rav Yosef wept. *Kulei hai nami la'ayin dumin?* — are all of these righteous people worth nothing? They receive no protection at all, and it even starts with them? Abaye responded: *tivuta hu legabbeihu* — it is actually a kindness for them. For it is written *ki mipnei hara'a ne'esaf ha-tzadik* — because of the coming evil, the righteous person is gathered in. They are taken first so that they do not have to witness the suffering that is to come upon the world. The *Gemara* does not offer a full theodicy — there is no explanation of why the righteous deserve this fate — but it recognizes these realities and attempts to locate some measure of meaning within them.

## Pestilence Laws

### Day Travel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לְעוֹלָם יִכָּנֵס אָדָם בְּ״כִי טוֹב״ וְיֵצֵא בְּ״כִי טוֹב״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַתֶּם לֹא תֵצְאוּ אִישׁ מִפֶּתַח בֵּיתוֹ עַד בֹּקֶר״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: A person** should **always enter** an unfamiliar city **at** a time of **good,** i.e., while it is light, as the Torah uses the expression "It is good" with regard to the creation of light (see Genesis 1:4). This goodness is manifest in the sense of security one feels when it is light. **And** likewise, when one leaves a city **he** should **leave at** a time of **good,** meaning after sunrise the next morning, **as it is stated** in the verse: **"And none of you shall go out of the opening of his house until the morning"** (Exodus 12:22).

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *le'olam yikanes adam bechi tov v'yetze bechi tov* — a person traveling should always enter a city while it is still light and leave while it is still light. The verse cited is *vayar Elohim et ha'or ki tov* — God saw the light, that it was good. When you arrive at a destination or depart on a journey, do so in the light of day; traveling after dark exposes one to bandits, wild animals, and other dangers.

### Pestilence vs. Famine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: דֶּבֶר בָּעִיר – כַּנֵּס רַגְלֶיךָ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַתֶּם לֹא תֵצְאוּ אִישׁ מִפֶּתַח בֵּיתוֹ עַד בֹּקֶר״. וְאוֹמֵר: ״לֵךְ עַמִּי בֹּא בַחֲדָרֶיךָ, וּסְגוֹר דְּלָתְךָ בַּעֲדֶךָ״. וְאוֹמֵר: ״מִחוּץ תְּשַׁכֶּל חֶרֶב, וּמֵחֲדָרִים אֵימָה״.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** If there is **plague in the city, gather your feet,** i.e., limit the time you spend out of the house, **as it is stated** in the verse: **"And none of you shall go out of the opening of his house until the morning." And it says** in another verse: **"Come, my people, enter into your chambers, and shut your doors behind you;** hide yourself for a little moment, until the anger has passed by" (Isaiah 26:20). **And it says: "Outside the sword will bereave, and in the chambers terror"** (Deuteronomy 32:25).

A *braisa* teaches: *dever ba'ir knas raglecha* — if there is pestilence in the city, pull in your feet and stay home. The source is the verse from the night of *makat bekhorot*: do not go out of your house until morning. Additional support comes from *lekh ammi bo vakhadarekha usgor daltekha ba'adekha* — go, my people, enter your chambers and close your doors — and from *michutz teshakel cherev u-mikhadarim eima* — from outside is the sword, and from inside is fear.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְאוֹמֵר״? וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּלֵילְיָא, אֲבָל בִּימָמָא לָא; תָּא שְׁמַע: ״לֵךְ עַמִּי בֹּא בַחֲדָרֶיךָ, וּסְגוֹר דְּלָתְךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the reason for citing the additional verses introduced with the term: And it says?** The first verse seems sufficient to teach the principle that one should not emerge from one's house when there is a plague. The Gemara answers: **And if you would say** that **this matter,** the first verse that states that none of you shall go out until morning, applies only **at night, but in the day** one may think that the principle does **not** apply, for this reason the Gemara teaches: **Come** and **hear: "Come, my people, enter into your chambers, and shut your doors behind you."**

The *Gemara* explores why multiple verses are needed. Perhaps the initial verse applies only at night but not by day — the subsequent verse, which speaks generally of going in and closing one's doors, establishes the requirement even by day. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי [הֵיכָא] דְּלֵיכָּא אֵימָה מִגַּוַּאי, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא אֵימָה מִגַּוַּאי – כִּי נָפֵיק יָתֵיב בֵּינֵי אִינָשֵׁי בְּצַוְותָּא בְּעָלְמָא, טְפֵי מְעַלֵּי; תָּא שְׁמַע: ״מִחוּץ תְּשַׁכֶּל חֶרֶב וּמֵחֲדָרִים אֵימָה״ – אַף עַל גַּב דְּמֵחֲדָרִים אֵימָה, מִחוּץ תְּשַׁכֶּל חֶרֶב.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that **this matter** applies only **where there is no fear inside,** which explains why it is preferable to remain indoors, **but where there is fear inside,** one might think that **when he goes out** and **sits among people in general company** it is **better,** therefore, the Gemara introduces the third verse and says: **Come** and **hear: "Outside the sword will bereave, and in the chambers terror."** This means that **although there is terror in the chambers, outside the sword will bereave,** so it is safer to remain indoors.

And perhaps one could argue that if a person feels fear inside the house as well — *eima migavai* — he should go out and be among people, since social company might be more beneficial than isolation. The third verse addresses this: *michutz teshakel cherev u-mikhadarim eima* — even if there is fear inside, the sword makes people bereaved outside, and one is better off remaining indoors. This exchange resonates with the experiences of more recent times, including the deliberations many faced during periods of pandemic.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא, בְּעִידָּן רִתְחָא הֲוָה סָכַר כַּוֵּי, דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי עָלָה מָוֶת בְּחַלּוֹנֵינוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **At a time** when there was a **plague, Rava would close the windows** of his house, **as it is written: "For death is come up into our windows"** (Jeremiah 9:20).

Rava's practice is cited: *be'iddan ritcha hava sakhar kavei* — in a time of divine wrath, which Rashi understands as plague or pestilence, Rava would seal the windows. The supporting verse is *ki ala mavet be-khaloneinu* — death has arisen through our windows.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: רָעָב בָּעִיר – פַּזֵּר רַגְלֶיךָ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי רָעָב בָּאָרֶץ, וַיֵּרֶד אַבְרָם מִצְרַיְמָה [לָגוּר] (וַיָּגׇר) שָׁם״. וְאוֹמֵר: ״אִם אָמַרְנוּ נָבוֹא הָעִיר וְהָרָעָב בָּעִיר, וָמַתְנוּ שָׁם״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** If there is **famine in the city, spread your feet,** i.e., leave the city, **as it is stated** in the verse: **"And there was a famine in the land; and Abram went down into Egypt to sojourn there"** (Genesis 12:10). **And it says: "If we say: We will enter into the city, then the famine is in the city, and we shall die there;** and if we sit here, we die also, now come, and let us fall unto the host of the Arameans; if they save us alive, we shall live; and if they kill us, we shall but die" (II Kings 7:4).

The *braisa* then turns to famine: *ra'av ba'ir pazer raglecha* — if there is hunger in the city, spread out your feet and leave. The proof text is *vayehi ra'av ba'aretz vayered Avram Mitzrayma* — there was a famine in the land and Avram went down to Egypt. He did not stay where he was; one should seek sustenance elsewhere.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְאוֹמֵר״? וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּלֵיכָּא סְפֵק נְפָשׁוֹת, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא סְפֵק נְפָשׁוֹת – לָא; תָּא שְׁמַע: ״לְכוּ וְנִפְּלָה אֶל מַחֲנֵה אֲרָם, אִם יְחַיֻּנוּ נִחְיֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the reason for citing the second verse, introduced with the term: And it says?** The first verse seems sufficient to teach the principle. The Gemara answers: **And if you would say** that **this matter,** the principle of leaving the city, applies only **where there is no uncertainty** concerning **a life-threatening** situation, **but where there is uncertainty** concerning **a life-threatening** situation this principle does **not** apply, **come** and **hear: "Come, and let us fall unto the host of the Arameans; if they save us alive, we shall live;** and if they kill us, we shall but die."**

Additional support for leaving the city during famine comes from the episode of the *metzora'im* who were outside the city during the Aramean siege. They reasoned: *im amarnu navo ha'ir vehara'av ba'ir vamasnu* — if we go into the city, the famine will kill us; let us rather throw ourselves on the camp of Aram, *lechu venippela el machaneh Aram im yechayunu nichyeh* — if they let us live, we live; if they kill us, well, we are going to die here of starvation anyway. This second verse is needed because one might have argued that the instruction to leave during famine applies only when no other danger awaits outside — *heicha de-leka sofek nefashot* — but if leaving the city also risks one's life, perhaps it is better to stay. The verse about the lepers establishes that even when one faces mortal risk on the road, one should still leave and try one's fortune, rather than remain and starve with certainty.

### Angel of Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: דֶּבֶר בָּעִיר – אַל יְהַלֵּךְ אָדָם בְּאֶמְצַע הַדֶּרֶךְ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּלְאַךְ הַמָּוֶת מְהַלֵּךְ בְּאֶמְצַע הַדְּרָכִים. דְּכֵיוָן דִּיהִיבָא לֵיהּ רְשׁוּתָא, מְסַגֵּי לְהֶדְיָא. שָׁלוֹם בָּעִיר – אַל יְהַלֵּךְ בְּצִדֵּי דְרָכִים. דְּכֵיוָן דְּלֵית לֵיהּ רְשׁוּתָא, מִחַבֵּי חַבּוֹיֵי וּמְסַגֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** If there is **a plague in the city, a person should not walk in the middle of the road, due to** the fact **that the Angel of Death walks in the middle of the road, as, since** in Heaven **they have given him permission** to kill within the city, **he goes openly** in the middle of the road. By contrast, if there is **peace** and quiet **in the city, do not walk on the sides of the road, as, since** the Angel of Death **does not have permission** to kill within the city, **he hides** himself **and walks** on the side of the road.

Another *braisa* instructs: *dever ba'ir al yehalech adam be'emtza haderech* — if there is pestilence in the city, do not walk in the middle of the road. The reason: *mipnei she-malach hamaves mehalech be'emtza haderachim* — the *malach hamaves* walks straight down the middle of the road. Since *keivan she-nitan lo reshut maskei lehadiya* — once he has been given permission to bring death to the world, he moves openly and directly, and the center of the road is his domain. One might also observe that this is practically where other people are congregating, so the advice carries a secondary practical dimension even if its stated rationale is metaphysical.

### Peace: Center Road

When *shalom ba'ir* — there is peace in the city and no destructive force is operating — *ayeilech be-tzidei drachim* — one should not walk at the edges of the road. *Keivan de-lis lei reshut* — since the *malach hamaves* has not been given permission to act in public — *maskei u-mechabi chibuwei* — he hides himself and moves along the margins. Walking at the edges thus exposes one to those marginal, hidden forces. The implicit guidance: when things are good, walk the straight path and do not veer into marginal activities.

### Synagogue Safety

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: דֶּבֶר בָּעִיר – אַל יִכָּנֵס אָדָם יָחִיד לְבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת, שֶׁמַּלְאַךְ הַמָּוֶת מַפְקִיד שָׁם כֵּלָיו. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּלָא קָרוּ בֵּיהּ דַּרְדְּקֵי, וְלָא מְצַלּוּ בֵּיהּ עֲשָׂרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** If there is **a plague in the city, a person should not enter the synagogue alone, as the Angel of Death leaves his utensils there,** and for this reason it is a dangerous place. **And this matter,** the danger in the synagogue, applies only **when there are no children learning in** the synagogue, **and** there are **not ten** men **praying in it.** But if there are children learning or ten men praying there, it is not a dangerous place.

A further *braisa* teaches: *dever ba'ir al yikanes adam yachidi leveit ha-knesses* — during pestilence, do not enter the synagogue alone. The reason given is that *malach hamaves maskei sham kelav* — the *malach hamaves* keeps his instruments there. This may reflect the reality that the *beit ha-knesses* functioned not merely as a house of prayer but as the central communal gathering place — the site from which decrees affecting the entire community would emanate. Entering alone, without the protective presence of a congregation, leaves one exposed.

This rule is qualified: *vehani mili heicha delo karu bei dardekei velo matzlu bei asara* — this applies only when there is no *cheder* with children learning there and no *minyan* present. When those conditions are met and people are present, one is protected even within the *beit ha-knesses*.

### Dogs as Omens

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כְּלָבִים בּוֹכִים – מַלְאַךְ הַמָּוֶת בָּא לָעִיר. כְּלָבִים מְשַׂחֲקִים – אֵלִיָּהוּ הַנָּבִיא בָּא לָעִיר. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי דְּלֵית בְּהוּ נְקֵבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** If the **dogs** in a certain place **are crying** for no reason, it is a sign that they feel the **Angel of Death has come to the city.** If the **dogs are playing,** it is a sign that they feel that **Elijah the prophet has come to the city. These matters** apply only **if there is no female** dog among them. If there is a female dog nearby, their crying or playing is likely due to her presence.

Finally, a *braisa* offers signs carried by dogs: *klavim bochim malach hamaves ba l'ir* — if dogs are howling and crying, the *malach hamaves* is coming to the city, animals sensing that something terrible is about to occur. *Klavim mesachakim* — if the dogs are playing and excited, *Eliyahu hanavi ba l'ir* — Elijah the prophet is coming to the city. The *Gemara* adds a qualification: *vehani mili delo is behu nekeiva* — this applies only if there is no female dog among them, since in that case the excitement may have an entirely mundane explanation.

## Parable & Zion

### Two Wives Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב אַמֵּי וְרַב אַסִּי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא. מָר אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לֵימָא מָר שְׁמַעְתְּתָא, וּמָר אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לֵימָא מָר אַגָּדְתָּא. פָּתַח לְמֵימַר אַגָּדְתָּא – וְלָא שָׁבֵיק מָר, פָּתַח לְמֵימַר שְׁמַעְתָּתָא – וְלָא שָׁבֵיק מָר.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ami and Rav Asi sat before Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa.** One **Sage said to** Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa: **Let the Master say** words of ***halakha*, and** the other **Sage said to** Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa: **Let the Master say** words of ***aggada*.** Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa **began to say** words of ***aggada* but** one **Sage did not let him,** so he **began to say** words of ***halakha* but** the other **Sage did not let him.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם: אֶמְשׁוֹל לָכֶם מָשָׁל, לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לְאָדָם שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים, אַחַת יַלְדָּה וְאַחַת זְקֵינָה. יַלְדָּה מְלַקֶּטֶת לוֹ לְבָנוֹת, זְקֵינָה מְלַקֶּטֶת לוֹ שְׁחוֹרוֹת. נִמְצָא קֵרֵחַ מִכָּאן וּמִכָּאן.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa **said to them: I will relate a parable. To what can this be compared?** It can be compared **to a man who has two wives, one young and one old. The young** wife **pulls out his white** hairs, so that her husband will appear younger. **The old** wife **pulls out his black** hairs so that he will appear older. And it **turns out** that he is **bald from here and from there,** i.e., completely bald, due to the actions of both of his wives.

Rav Ami and Rav Assi were sitting before Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha. One asked for *halacha*, the other for *aggadta*. Unable to satisfy both simultaneously, Rabbi Yitzchak said: let me give you a parable. *Le-adam she-yesh lo shtei nashim, achat yelada ve-achat zekena* — a man had two wives, one young and one old. The young wife would pluck out his white hairs, not wanting him to look old. The old wife would pluck out his dark hairs, not wanting him to look young. *Nimtzeu kereach mikan u-mikan* — he ends up bald on both sides, bald of his white hairs and bald of his dark hairs, entirely bald either way. The phrase *kereach mikan u-mikan* captures a situation of being pulled in opposite directions with no winning outcome.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן: אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא לְכוּ מִלְּתָא דְּשָׁוְיָא לְתַרְוַיְיכוּ: ״כִּי תֵצֵא אֵשׁ וּמָצְאָה קֹצִים״ – ״תֵּצֵא״ מֵעַצְמָהּ, ״שַׁלֵּם יְשַׁלֵּם הַמַּבְעִר אֶת הַבְּעֵרָה״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa continued and **said to them: If so, I will say to you a matter that is appropriate to both of you,** which contains both *halakha* and *aggada*. In the verse that states: **"If a fire breaks out, and catches in thorns"** (Exodus 22:5), the term **"breaks out"** indicates that it breaks out **by itself.** Yet, the continuation of the verse states: **"The one who kindled the fire shall pay compensation,"** which indicates that he must pay only if the fire spread due to his negligence.

So, Rabbi Yitzchak concluded, let me tell you something that is good for both of you — a blending of *aggadta* and *halacha*, which was in fact a classic form from the time of the *Gemara* and the early *Geonim*. The *Sheiltos* of Rav Achai Gaon exemplifies this structure: a *drasha* that opens with a *halacha* question and, in the course of homiletic exposition, arrives at its resolution. What follows here does precisely that.

### Fire as Divine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: עָלַי לְשַׁלֵּם אֶת הַבְּעֵרָה שֶׁהִבְעַרְתִּי – אֲנִי הִצַּיתִּי אֵשׁ בְּצִיּוֹן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּצֶּת אֵשׁ בְּצִיּוֹן, וַתֹּאכַל יְסוֹדוֹתֶיהָ״; וַאֲנִי עָתִיד לִבְנוֹתָהּ בָּאֵשׁ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַאֲנִי אֶהְיֶה לָהּ חוֹמַת אֵשׁ סָבִיב, וּלְכָבוֹד אֶהְיֶה בְתוֹכָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse can be explained allegorically: **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said** that although the fire broke out in the Temple due to the sins of the Jewish people, **it is incumbent upon Me to pay** restitution **for the fire that I kindled. I,** God, **kindled a fire in Zion, as it is stated:** "The Lord has accomplished His fury, He has poured out His fierce anger; **and He has kindled a fire in Zion, which has devoured its foundations"** (Lamentations 4:11). **And I will build it with fire** in the **future, as it is stated: "For I,** says the Lord, **will be for her a wall of fire round about; and I will be the glory in her midst"** (Zechariah 2:9).

The verse is *ki tetze esh u-matza kotzim*. The word *tetze* — it went out — implies the fire escaped of its own accord. Yet the verse then concludes *shalem yeshalem ha-mavir es ha-beirah* — the one who kindled it shall pay — using the word *mavir*, which refers to one who deliberately lit a fire. Rabbi Yitzchak reads this as a statement about divine punishment. *Ki tetze esh* — when destruction goes forth into the world — *tetze me-atzmah*, it goes out as though on its own, as when the *Beit HaMikdash* was destroyed: it happened because of our sins, it was not a direct divine intervention in the simple sense, and in that way it escaped, so to speak. *Shalem yeshalem ha-mavir* — and yet God treats himself as the *mavir*, the one who deliberately kindled it, and accepts responsibility.

### God's Rebuilding

*Amar HaKadosh Baruch Hu: alai leshalem et ha-beirah she-hivarti* — it is upon Me to pay for the fire that I lit. *Ani hitzati esh be-Tzion* — I lit the fire against Zion — as the verse says *va-yatzet esh be-Tzion va-tochal yesodoteha* — it consumed the foundations. And as a consequence and compensation, *ve-ani atid livnotah be-esh* — I will build it again with fire, a new *Beit HaMikdash*. The proof text is *ve-ani ehyeh lah chomat esh saviv u-chavod ehyeh be-tochah* — I will be for it as a wall of fire round about, and for glory in its midst. This is the *aggadta* portion of Rabbi Yitzchak's combined teaching.

### Esho Mishum Chitzav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַעְתְּתָא – פָּתַח הַכָּתוּב בְּנִזְקֵי מָמוֹנוֹ, וְסִיֵּים בְּנִזְקֵי גּוּפוֹ; לוֹמַר לָךְ: אִשּׁוֹ מִשּׁוּם חִצָּיו.
>
> **Translation:** There is **a *halakha*** that can be learned from the verse in Exodus, as **the verse begins with damage** caused through one's **property:** "If a fire breaks out," **and concludes with damage** caused by **one's body:** "The one who kindled the fire." This indicates that when damage is caused by fire, it is considered as though the person who kindled the fire caused the damage directly with his body. That serves **to say to you** that the liability for **his fire** damage is **due to** its similarity to **his arrows.** Just as one who shoots an arrow and causes damage is liable because the damage was caused directly through his action, so too, one who kindles a fire that causes damage is liable because it is considered as though the damage were caused directly by his actions.

Now the *halacha*. The verse begins by describing fire damage as though it were a property tort — the fire escaped, analogous to an ox that went out and caused damage — but concludes by calling the responsible party the *mavir*, the one who lit it, implying personal culpability. *Patach ve-chatav bi-nizkei mamon ve-siyem bi-nizkei gufo* — it opens with language of property damage and closes with language of bodily act. *Lomar lach esho mishum chitzav* — this teaches that the liability for fire is understood as *eisho mishum chitzav*, an obligation rooted in the person's act as an extension of himself, rather than *eisho mishum mamono*, the liability of one whose property caused damage. This serves as a proof text in the larger *sugya* we encountered earlier on that foundational question.

## Tamun Ba'esh

### David's Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּתְאַוֶּה דָוִד וַיֹּאמַר: מִי יַשְׁקֵנִי מַיִם מִבֹּאר בֵּית לֶחֶם אֲשֶׁר בַּשָּׁעַר. וַיִּבְקְעוּ שְׁלֹשֶׁת הַגִּבּוֹרִים בְּמַחֲנֵה פְלִשְׁתִּים, וַיִּשְׁאֲבוּ מַיִם מִבֹּאר בֵּית לֶחֶם אֲשֶׁר בַּשַּׁעַר [וְגוֹ׳]״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues with another statement of *aggada* on a related topic: The verse states: **"And David longed, and said: Oh, that one would give me water to drink of the well of Bethlehem, which is by the gate! And the three mighty men broke through the host of the Philistines, and drew water out of the well of Bethlehem, that was by the gate,** and took it, and brought it to David; but he would not drink it, but poured it out to the Lord" (II Samuel 23:15–16). The Sages understood that David was not simply asking for water, but was using the term as a metaphor referring to Torah, and he was raising a halakhic dilemma.

The *Gemara* brings an episode from the wars of David against the Philistines. *Vayyisaveh David vayomer mi yashkeni mayim mi-bor Beit Lechem asher ba-sha'ar* — David expressed a longing for water from the well of Beit Lechem that is in the gate. *Va-yifke'u sheloshet ha-gibborim be-machaneh Plishtim va-yish'avu mayim* — three of his mighty men broke through the Philistine camp and drew the water. At the surface this reads as a literal thirst, but the *Gemara* will interpret it through the classic rabbinic metaphor in which water signifies Torah and *sha'ar* — the gate — signifies the place where the elders sit and teach, so that the desire was for a *halacha* ruling from the sages of Beit Lechem.

### Hidden Property

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ? אָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: טָמוּן בָּאֵשׁ קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ, אִי כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אִי כְּרַבָּנַן, וּפְשַׁטוּ לֵיהּ מַאי דִּפְשַׁטוּ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the dilemma** that David **is raising? Rava says** that **Rav Naḥman says: He was asking** about the *halakha* with regard to **a concealed** article damaged by **a fire.** He wanted to know whether the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that one is liable to pay for such damage, or **whether** the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who hold that one is exempt from liability for damage by fire to concealed articles. **And** the Sages in Bethlehem **answered him what they answered him.**

*Mai ka miba'ei lei?* — what precisely was David asking? Rava said in the name of Rav Nachman: *tamun ba-esh ka miba'ei lei* — he was asking about *tamun ba'esh*, property hidden inside a haystack that is consumed by fire: does the one who caused the fire bear liability for the hidden contents? The debate was whether the ruling follows Rabbi Yehuda, who holds one liable for *tamun ba'esh*, or the *Rabbanan*, who exempt. *U-pashtulei mai de-pashtulei* — they sent him an answer, but since the debate between Rabbi Yehuda and the *Rabbanan* was never resolved, we cannot know what the answer was. The reason this episode was associated with *tamun ba'esh* at all will become clearer as the surrounding verses mention *gadishim* — mounds of grain — provoking a connection to this *halacha*. Rashi suggests it is possible the question arose practically, from an actual burning of a grain pile, prompting inquiry into liability for hidden objects.

### Barley Mounds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: גְּדִישִׁים דִּשְׂעוֹרִים דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל הֲווֹ, דַּהֲווֹ מִטַּמְּרִי פְּלִשְׁתִּים בְּהוּ, וְקָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: מַהוּ לְהַצִּיל עַצְמוֹ בְּמָמוֹן חֲבֵירוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna stated** a different explanation of the verse: **There were stacks of barley belonging to Jews in which the Philistines were hiding, and** David wanted to burn down the stacks to kill the Philistines and save his own life. **He raised the dilemma: What is** the *halakha*? Is it permitted **to save oneself** by destroying **the property of another?**

Rav Huna offers an entirely different reading of what David's question was. *Gedishim di-se'orim di-Yisrael havu de-havu mitmurei Plishtim behu* — there were mounds of barley owned by Israelites in which Philistine soldiers were hiding. *Vekamiba'ya lei mahu lehatzil atzmo bemamon chaveiro* — and David's question was: is it permissible to save oneself by destroying another person's property? Could he order the burning of this barley — owned by a fellow Israelite — in order to flush out or destroy the Philistines hiding within?

### King's License

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ: אָסוּר לְהַצִּיל עַצְמוֹ בְּמָמוֹן חֲבֵירוֹ; אֲבָל אַתָּה מֶלֶךְ אַתָּה, [וּמֶלֶךְ] פּוֹרֵץ לַעֲשׂוֹת לוֹ דֶּרֶךְ וְאֵין מוֹחִין בְּיָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **They sent** the following answer **to him: It is prohibited to save oneself** by destroying **the property of another. But you are king, and a king may breach the fence** of an individual **in order to form a path for himself, and none may protest his** action, i.e., the normal *halakhot* of damage do not apply to you since you are king.

*Shalchu lei assur lehatzil atzmo bemamon chaveiro* — the reply was that it is forbidden to save oneself by means of another's property. This is a striking ruling. After all, the only acts one may not perform even to save one's life are the three cardinal sins: *avoda zara, gilui arayot, ve-shpichut damim*. Property damage does not fall in that category, and one might expect that destroying someone's barley to save lives would be straightforwardly permitted, especially given the expectation of compensation. Tosafot note that Rashi seems to read the *Gemara*'s question as literally asking whether it is permitted at all. But in light of the difficulty — since it is hard to argue that property destruction for *pikuach nefesh* is actually prohibited — the overwhelming view of the *Rishonim* is that the underlying question was about liability: if you destroy another's property in the course of *pikuach nefesh*, must you compensate him? And the answer is yes, you are liable.

As to David specifically: *aval atah melech atah melech poretz la'asot lo derech* — you are a king, and a king has special license; a king may break through another's field to make a path and *ein mochin beyado* — no one can restrain him. The king's license here means that even if such an act would normally entail liability, the king operates under different legal conditions. There is also a broader question lurking here: do the ordinary civil laws governing property and compensation apply the same way in wartime as they do in peacetime? That question is raised but not resolved.

### Taking Barley

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבָּה בַּר מָרִי אָמְרוּ: גְּדִישִׁים דִּשְׂעוֹרִין דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל הֲווֹ, וּגְדִישִׁין דַּעֲדָשִׁים דִּפְלִשְׁתִּים; וְקָא מִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מַהוּ לִיטּוֹל גְּדִישִׁין שֶׁל שְׂעוֹרִין דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל לִיתֵּן לִפְנֵי בְּהֶמְתּוֹ, עַל מְנָת לְשַׁלֵּם גְּדִישִׁין שֶׁל עֲדָשִׁים דִּפְלִשְׁתִּים?
>
> **Translation:** **The Rabbis, and some say** that it was **Rabba bar Mari,** give an alternative explanation of the dilemma and **said: The stacks of barley belonged to Jews, and** there were **stacks of lentils belonging to the Philistines.** David needed barley to feed his animals. **And** David **raised the** following **dilemma: What is** the *halakha*? I know that I may take the lentils belonging to a gentile to feed my animals, but is it permitted **to take a stack of barley** belonging to **a Jew, to place before one's animal** for it to consume, **with the intent to pay** the owner of the barley with the **stacks of lentils belonging to the Philistines?**

The *Rabbanan* — and some attribute this version to Rabba bar Mari — offer a different account altogether. There were *gedishim di-se'orim di-Yisrael* — mounds of barley owned by Israelites — and *gedishim di-adashim di-Plishtim* — mounds of lentils owned by Philistines. David's question was: *mahu litol gedishim shel se'orim di-Yisrael liten lifnei behemto* — may we take the Israelite barley without permission to feed our animals during the military campaign, *al menat leshallem gedishim shel adashim di-Plishtim* — with the intention of compensating the Israelite owner afterward with Philistine lentils captured in battle, which would actually be worth more? So in the end the Israelite owner would profit — but at the moment of taking, the act is one of unauthorized seizure.

### Barley & Lentils

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ: ״חֲבֹל יָשִׁיב רָשָׁע, גְּזֵלָה יְשַׁלֵּם״ – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁגְּזֵילָה מְשַׁלֵּם, רָשָׁע הוּא. אֲבָל אַתָּה מֶלֶךְ אַתָּה, וּמֶלֶךְ פּוֹרֵץ לַעֲשׂוֹת לוֹ דֶּרֶךְ וְאֵין מוֹחִין בְּיָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages of Bethlehem **sent** the following reply **to him: "If the wicked restore the pledge, give back that which he had taken by robbery,** walk in the statutes of life, committing no iniquity; he shall surely live, he shall not die" (Ezekiel 33:15). This verse teaches that **even though** the robber **repays** the value of the **stolen item, he** is nevertheless considered to be **wicked,** and is described as such in the verse, and a commoner would not be allowed to act as you asked. **But you are king, and a king may breach the fence** of an individual **in order to form a path for himself, and none may protest his** action.

The answer sent back was: *chaval yashiv rasha gezeila yeshallem* — even if one repays the stolen property, *rasha hu* — one is still wicked for having stolen it. *Af al pi she-gezeila meshallem* — paying back does not retroactively transform the act of theft into something permissible. And again the response adds: *aval atah melech umelech poretz la'asot lo derech ve'ein mochin beyado* — but you, as king, have the special license that permits what would otherwise be restricted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְאַחְלוֹפֵי – הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב חַד קְרָא: ״וַתְּהִי שָׁם חֶלְקַת הַשָּׂדֶה מְלֵאָה עֲדָשִׁים״, וּכְתִיב חַד קְרָא: ״וַתְּהִי חֶלְקַת הַשָּׂדֶה מְלֵאָה שְׂעוֹרִים״;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the different explanations: **Granted, according to the one who says** that David was asking whether he could take the stacks of barley and **exchange** them, i.e., repay the owners of the barley, with stacks of lentils, **this is as it is written** in **one verse:** "And the Philistines were gathered together into a troop, **where was a plot of ground full of lentils;** and the people fled from the Philistines" (II Samuel 23:11), **and it is written** in **one** other **verse:** "He was with David at Pas Dammim, and there the Philistines were gathered together to battle, **where was a plot of ground full of barley;** and the people fled from before the Philistines" (I Chronicles 11:13). This apparent contradiction can be reconciled by saying that there were two fields, one of barley and one of lentils.

A textual question then arises: one verse in *Divrei HaYamim* describes the field as full of lentils, while a parallel verse in *Shmuel* describes it as full of barley. According to the version of Rav Huna — that the question involved Israelite barley in which Philistines were hiding — both verses can be accounted for: there were piles of barley and David was asking about them, which explains one verse, and presumably there were also lentil piles, which explains the other. According to the *Rabbanan*'s version — that the question involved taking Israelite barley to feed the animals and repaying with Philistine lentils — the two crops are both part of the narrative: the barley was Israelite property that was taken, and the lentils were Philistine property with which compensation was to be made. The two verses record each commodity in turn, and both are necessary to the full account.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְמִקְלֵי, מַאי אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ לְהָנֵי תְּרֵי קְרָאֵי? אָמַר לָךְ: דַּהֲווֹ נָמֵי גְּדִישִׁים דַּעֲדָשִׁים דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, דַּהֲווֹ מִיטַּמְּרוּ בְּהוּ פְּלִשְׁתִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **But according to** Rav Huna, **the one who says** that David's question was asked because he wanted **to burn** the stacks of barley, for **what** purpose **does he require these two verses?** How does he explain this contradiction? Rav Huna could have **said to you that there were also stacks of lentils belonging to Jews, inside which the Philistines were hiding.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְמִקְלֵי – הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּתְיַצֵּב בְּתוֹךְ הַחֶלְקָה וַיַּצִּילֶהָ״; אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְאַחְלוֹפֵי, מַאי ״וַיַּצִּילֶהָ״?
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, according to the one who says** that David asked his question because he wanted **to burn** the stacks, **this is as it is written** in the following verse with regard to David: **"But he stood in the midst of the plot, and saved it,** and slew the Philistines; and the Lord performed a great victory" (II Samuel 23:12). **But according to the one who says** that David's question was asked **with regard to exchanging** the lentils for the barley, **what** is the meaning of the phrase: **"And saved it"?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּלָא שְׁבַק לְהוּ לְאַחְלוֹפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis answer that David saved it in **that he did not permit them to exchange** the value of the barley with the lentils.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב תְּרֵי קְרָאֵי;
>
> **Translation:** **Granted,** according to both of **these two** opinions, **this is as it is written** in **two** distinct **verses,** one describing the field of lentils and one describing the field of barley.
