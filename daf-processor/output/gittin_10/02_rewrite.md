# Gittin 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Get & Shtar Similarities

### Intro & Baraita

We begin on amud 10b, picking up from the *mishna*'s observation that both a *get* for a woman and a *shtar shichrur* — a writ of manumission for a slave — share the requirement of *befanai nichtav uvefanai nechtam*. The *gemara* had quoted a *baraita* listing several points of comparison between these two documents, and that *baraita* functions almost as a foreshadowing of the mishnayot that follow, since each case it enumerates will be addressed in turn. It is worth briefly reviewing the list.

### Befanai Requirement

*Tanu rabbanan*: in three ways are *gittei nashim* and *shichrurei avadim* alike. The first is *shetzarich lomar befanai nichtav uvefanai nechtam* — the delivering agent must declare that the document was written and signed in his presence.

### Samaritan Witnesses

The second point: any *get* — meaning any *shtar* — that bears the signature of a Samaritan witness, an *ed Kuti*, is invalid, *chutz migittei nashim u'shichrurei avadim* — except for a *get* for a woman and a writ of manumission for a slave. That is a striking leniency, and we will return to it in the mishnayot that follow.

### Non-Jewish Courts

The third point: all documents that emerge from non-Jewish courts, *arkaot shel akum*, are valid even though non-Jews signed them — with the exception of a *get* for a woman and a *shtar shichrur* for a slave. The general validity of secular documents for monetary matters flows from the principles of *dinei mamonot* and *dina d'malchuta dina*: such documents function as evidence that a transaction occurred, much as we rely today on bank-processed deeds without requiring a formally executed *halachic* instrument. But a *get* and a *shtar shichrur* are not merely evidentiary; they are instruments that effect a change of personal status. They must therefore be executed according to *halacha* in the classical sense.

### Rabbi Meir Retraction

According to Rabbi Meir, there is a fourth point of comparison: *omer ten get ze l'ishti v'shtar shichrur ze l'avdi, ratza lachazor b'shneihem yachazor* — if a person hands a messenger a *get* for his wife or a *shtar shichrur* for his slave for delivery, he may retract either before it reaches its intended recipient.

### Zachin Dispute

Why is this even a question? One might assume that of course the master can retract — the document has not yet been delivered. The answer is that the *chachamim* disagree with Rabbi Meir specifically regarding the *shtar shichrur*. Their view is grounded in the principle of *zachin l'adam shelo befanav*: one may act in another person's interest without their knowledge or consent. Since freedom is unambiguously a *zechut* — a benefit — for the slave, the moment the master hands the document to the messenger, that messenger automatically becomes an agent on the slave's behalf. The slave is thereby freed at that moment, even before the document reaches his hands. By a *get* for a woman, we cannot assume she wishes to be divorced, so she is not divorced until the *get* reaches her; but for the slave's *shtar*, receipt by the messenger suffices. Rabbi Meir disagrees: it is not self-evidently in the slave's best interest to be freed — freedom carries its own obligations and uncertainties — and therefore the slave is not freed until the document actually reaches him. That is Rabbi Meir's fourth point of comparison between *get* and *shtar shichrur*.

The *gemara* then asks: the *chachamim* say there are three ways — *bishlama l'rabbanan minyana l'matuhei had'Rabbi Meir* — the number three is meant to exclude Rabbi Meir's position, to say we do not count that fourth case as a shared characteristic, since for the *chachamim* the slave is freed the moment the messenger receives the *shtar*, making it different from the *get*. But what does Rabbi Meir's number four exclude? *L'matuhei ha d'tanya* — it excludes what we learn in the following *baraita*.

## Illiterate Witnesses

### Stencil Method

The *baraita* addresses witnesses who do not know how to sign their own names. *Mekayemim lahem niyar chalak u'memalim et hakera'im d'yo*: one tears a blank piece of paper and fills the cutouts with ink — in other words, one fashions a stencil and has the witnesses fill in the letters of their name. That counts as a valid signature, even though it bears none of the distinctive character of the witness's own handwriting.

### Get Nashim Leniency

*Amar Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: bameh devarim amurim — be'gittei nashim*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds that this stencil allowance applies only to a *get* for a woman. *Aval shichrurei avadim u'she'ar kol shetarot — im yode'im likrot v'lachtom chotmim v'im lav ein chotmim*: for a writ of manumission or any other document, if the witnesses know how to read and sign they may do so, and if not they may not sign at all. Remarkably, the leniency is greater precisely for the weightiest personal-status document of all. The reason, as Rashi explains, is *mishum takkanat agunos*: the concern that a husband may be about to embark on a sea voyage, or is gravely ill and may soon die, creating a *yibbum* situation. The wife needs that *get*, and we therefore extend special allowances to make it possible. No such leniency is extended to *shichrurei avadim* or any other document.

So what is Rabbi Meir excluding with his number four? He is saying that the leniency of the stencil and reading assistance is not something shared uniquely by *get* and *shtar shichrur* — either it applies to all documents (*tanna kamma*'s position) or it applies only to a *get* and not to a *shtar shichrur* (Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's position). In neither case is it a point of symmetry between the two documents, and that is what his count of four is meant to signal.

### Cannot Read/Sign

The *gemara* pauses on Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's formulation — *im yode'im likrot v'lachtom* — and raises a difficulty: *kriya man dachar shemei*? Who mentioned reading? The entire prior discussion concerned only signing, not reading. The *gemara* answers: *chisura mechsara v'hachi katani* — the *baraita* is abbreviated and must be read as follows. If witnesses do not know how to read, *korin lifneihem v'chotmin* — the document is read aloud before them and they then sign with their own signatures. If they do not know how to sign, one makes the stencil for them. Both accommodations can be applied simultaneously: the document can be read to witnesses who cannot read it themselves, and a stencil provided to witnesses who cannot write their own names.

### Ed Mipi Ed Issue

*Tosafot* raises a pointed question on this arrangement. If someone reads the *shtar* to me and I sign my name to it, am I not in the position of *ed mi'pi ed* — a witness testifying based on another witness's account rather than direct knowledge? I only know what is in the document because you told me what is in it. How can I affix my signature to something I have not myself read?

*Tosafot* resolves this by noting that the witnesses are not being asked to testify that certain events occurred; they are attesting that what is written in the *shtar* is what is written there — *she'ein tzarich l'ha'id al ikkar hama'aseh ella she'omer she'kach katuv bash'tar*. My signature does not say "I witnessed a transaction"; it says "I affirm that these are the words of this document." The distinction matters. Still, *Tosafot* acknowledges a degree of awkwardness: you are telling me the contents of the document and I am then signing to them, which carries at least a resemblance to *ed mi'pi ed*.

One can press the question further depending on the type of document. A *shtar ra'aya* — an evidentiary document — essentially has the witnesses saying: these words in the document represent what occurred. A *shtar kinyan*, a document that effects a legal act such as a *get*, is somewhat different: the witnesses are not so much reporting past events as they are giving voice to the husband's expressed will, validating and instantiating it. My signature on a *get* is my attestation that this is the intent of the husband — I am, in a sense, lending my testimony to the act itself rather than to an underlying factual event. Either way, there are two remarkable allowances visible in this *baraita*: that a stencil-filled name counts as a signature, and that a witness who was read the document rather than reading it himself can validly attest to its contents. According to the *tanna kamma* both allowances are available for all *shtarot*, which is quite far-reaching; according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel they are restricted to *gittei nashim*.

## Death Before Delivery

### Post-Mortem Invalidity

The *gemara* now asks: *v'tu lika* — are there no additional cases in which a *get* and a *shtar shichrur* are alike? *Ve-ha'ika* — consider the case: *ha'omer tenu get zeh l'ishti v'shtar shichrur zeh l'avdi umet lo yitnu achar mitah*. If a man says — using the plural form, *tenu*, addressed to witnesses — "write and deliver a *get* to my wife" or "write and deliver a *shtar shichrur* to my slave," and he then dies, those documents may not be given after his death. One cannot divorce one's wife posthumously, nor free one's slave posthumously. Rashi notes the significance of the plural: the man is speaking to multiple people, meaning the document has not yet been written. (If a completed *shtar shichrur* had already been handed to a single messenger, the slave would have been freed immediately per the *chachamim*'s view — but here nothing has been written yet, no one can receive it on the slave's behalf, and the master dies.)

### Shechiv Merah Property

The contrast is with a dying man who says *tenu maneh l'ploni* — "give such-and-such sum to so-and-so" — and dies. In that case, the bequest *is* honored after death. This flows from either the principle of *mitzvah lekayem divrei hamet* — the obligation to fulfill the expressed wishes of the deceased — or, as Rashi frames it, from the principle of *divrei shechiv merah kichtuvim uchimsorim dami*: the words of a deathbed declaration are treated as if they were already written and delivered, so that upon his death the property transfer takes effect automatically. The question of which principle governs — whether the heirs bear an obligation to carry out his wishes, or whether the transfer takes legal effect at the moment of death — is a broader discussion in *halacha*; but either way, money bequeathed in this fashion is distributed.

Why does the same reasoning not apply to freeing a slave or divorcing a wife? The answer: freeing a slave is not simply a transfer of property. Even if we say *give my slave to Reuven* might resemble a property transfer, *free my slave* implicates personal status and requires a specific *shtar shichrur* executed by the current master. The wife's case is even more obvious — she is already a *widow*, an *almanah*, and the *get* is moot. Neither situation is analogous to straightforward property distribution.

### Living Principal

The *gemara* presses further. What if the dying man said: write a *document* giving a hundred dinars to so-and-so, and then he dies — may the document be written and delivered posthumously? *Shema lo gamar lehaknoto ela beshtar ve'ein shtar le'achar mitah*: perhaps he intended the transfer to be effected only through a *shtar*, and a *shtar* has no validity once the principal has died. A *shtar* is, at its core, an expression of the living will of the party who executes it. It cannot be written on behalf of someone already dead, and even if written, it cannot take effect without a living principal behind it.

### Shtar vs Property

The distinction therefore is this: if a person on his deathbed distributes property without requiring a *shtar* — simply declaring who gets what — the transfer can take effect at his death, functioning much like an inheritance assignment. But if he stipulates that the transfer is to be accomplished through a *shtar*, the *shtar* must be executed while he lives. This principle applies equally to all *shtarot* — whether a *get*, a *shtar shichrur*, or a deed of sale. Since this is not a point of distinction unique to *get* and *shtar shichrur* but rather a general rule governing all *shtarot*, the *baraita* does not include it in its list.

## Lishmah/Mechubar

### Rabbinic Only

The *gemara* now asks: *v'tu lika* — why not include other requirements that apply equally to *get* and *shtar shichrur*? Specifically, *va-ha ika lishmah* — both require that the document be written *lishmah*, for the sake of this particular woman or this particular slave — and *va-ha ika mechubar* — both must be written on something that is detached from the ground, not still attached (*mechubar*), following the principle of *ve-katav ve-natan*. These are genuine shared requirements. Why are they absent from the list?

The *gemara* answers: *ki tani pesula de-rabbanan* — the *baraita* lists only rabbinic disqualifications. *De-oraita lo tani* — biblical requirements were not included in the enumeration.

### Court Challenge

This answer is immediately challenged: *ve-ha arkaot* — what about the case of non-Jewish courts, which is listed? That would appear to be a biblical disqualification: a *get* executed through a non-Jewish court with non-Jewish signatories is biblically invalid. How can the list be limited to rabbinic matters if this case is included?

### Eidei Mesira

The *gemara* responds: the case of non-Jewish court documents is only rabbinically problematic, because we are operating within the framework of Rabbi Eliezer's view — *d'amar eidim mesira kirti* — that what effects the *get* is the witnesses present at the moment of delivery, not the witnesses who signed the document. If so, a *get* delivered before valid Jewish witnesses is biblically valid even if non-Jews signed it. The non-Jewish signatures are only a rabbinic concern. One must imagine the specific scenario in which the document was written in accordance with *halacha* — perhaps by a Jewish scribe — but subsequently registered and signed in a non-Jewish court. In that case, it was delivered before kosher witnesses, is biblically valid, and the defect of non-Jewish signatures is rabbinic only.

### Rabbi Shimon Agrees

The *gemara* supports this by cross-referencing the later *mishna*: *Rabbi Shimon omer af elu kesherim* — Rabbi Shimon validates a *get* bearing non-Jewish signatures entirely. As Rav Zeira explains: *yarad Rabbi Shimon l'shitato shel Rabbi Eliezer d'amar eidim mesira kirti* — Rabbi Shimon aligns himself with Rabbi Eliezer's position that the witnesses of delivery are what matter. This implies that the *tanna kamma* in that *mishna*, who invalidates such a *get*, must hold that biblical validity depends on the signed witnesses. One might therefore conclude that the *tanna kamma* considers the non-Jewish signatures a biblical problem — which would undercut the *gemara*'s claim that the list contains only rabbinic disqualifications.

The *gemara* deflects: *ika beinaihu shemot muvhakin* — the debate between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Shimon is not about biblical validity at all. Both may hold like Rabbi Eliezer that it is biblically valid by virtue of the delivery witnesses. The debate is whether it is *rabbinically* valid. Even rabbinically, asks the *gemara*, why should it be invalid? Because we worry that people will rely on the non-Jewish signatures rather than investigating whether valid delivery witnesses exist. Now, if the signatures are obviously non-Jewish names — say, recognizably non-Jewish — then Rabbi Shimon is not concerned that anyone will rely on them, and he validates the *get*. The *tanna kamma*, however, does not permit any *get* bearing non-Jewish names, regardless of how obvious, out of concern for the general principle. Both positions, then, treat the non-Jewish signatures as a rabbinic concern only, and the list is consistent.

### Unique to Get

But then the *gemara* raises a fresh challenge: *va-hachazara de-oraita ve-katani* — the case of retraction (*chazara*), which Rabbi Meir lists as the fourth point, is a biblical matter. The husband's ability to retract before delivery is not a rabbinic construct. So the claim that the list is limited to rabbinic invalidations cannot stand.

The *gemara* therefore abandons that organizing principle entirely and substitutes another: *ki ktani milta d'leisa b'kiddushin, milta d'isa b'kiddushin lo ktani* — the list enumerates only those features that are unique to *get* and *shtar shichrur* and do not apply to *kiddushin*. Cases that are equally true of a *shtar kiddushin* were not included.

Under this rubric, *lishmah* and *mechubar* are excluded because they apply to *shtar kiddushin* as well — we derive *shtar kiddushin*'s requirements from *get*, so the same standards apply. By contrast, *befanai nichtav* is not required for a *shtar kiddushin* because there is no *agunah* concern in the context of marriage. The *ed Kuti* leniency does not extend to *kiddushin*. And the matter of non-Jewish court signatures: Rashi explains that while the same underlying concern about reliance on non-kosher signatures technically exists in *kiddushin*, the cost of rabbinically invalidating a *shtar kiddushin* bearing non-Jewish signatures would be far too severe — if we said she is not betrothed, she would go marry another man, which would constitute a grave violation. Therefore the rabbinic disqualification was never applied to *kiddushin* in the first place, and the point remains one that distinguishes *get* and *shtar shichrur* from *kiddushin*.

## Chazara Analysis

### Chazara Question

Having established that the organizing principle is uniqueness relative to *kiddushin*, the *gemara* immediately challenges whether *chazara* — retraction — truly qualifies. *Ve'hachazara gufa ita be'kiddushin*: retraction exists in *kiddushin* too. If I send a *shaliach* with a ring to betroth a woman and I retract before she receives it, she is obviously not betrothed. So why is *chazara* listed as something unique to *get* and *shtar shichrur*?

### Two-Sided vs One

*Tosafot* answers that the *chiddush* of retraction is not that retraction *is possible*, but rather that it is still possible even given the unilateral nature of the act. A *get* and a *shtar shichrur* are both acts the husband or master may perform against the other party's will — *bishlichut ba'al karchah*. In *kiddushin*, by contrast, the woman must consent; the entire act is bilateral and depends entirely on her acceptance. It is therefore self-evident that the husband can retract before she receives anything, because nothing is even close to finalized until she agrees. There is no *hava amina* that delivery to the *shaliach* alone could have effected the marriage.

With a *get* or a *shtar shichrur*, however, since the act is unilateral and requires no agreement from the other side, one might have thought that handing the document to the *shaliach* already finalizes the matter — particularly in the case of *shtar shichrur*, where the *chachamim* argue the slave is freed at that moment through *zachin l'adam shelo befanav*. Rabbi Meir's teaching is precisely that even in these unilateral transactions, retraction remains possible before the document reaches its recipient. That is a genuine *chiddush* — novel and non-obvious — which is why it merits inclusion in the list. By *kiddushin* no such novel teaching is needed.

## Samaritan Leniency

### Mishna Rule

The *gemara* now turns to examine the individual cases from the *baraita*, beginning with the *mishna*: *kol get sheyesh alav ed Kuti pasul* — any *get* (meaning any *shtar*) bearing a Samaritan witness is invalid — *chutz migittei nashim u'shichrurei avadim* — except for a *get* for a woman and a *shtar shichrur* for a slave.

The *mishna* then recounts a *maaseh*: a *get* was brought before Rabban Gamliel in a village, and its witnesses were both Samaritan witnesses — *edei Kutim* — and he validated it. This is striking, because until now the formulation *ed Kuti* suggested that we are speaking of one Samaritan alongside a Jewish witness. This precedent suggests that even two Samaritan witnesses might suffice, at least for Rabban Gamliel.

### Tanaic Debate

Before the *gemara* can identify whose opinion underlies the *mishna*, it presents a *baraita* setting out three distinct positions on the reliability of Samaritans (*Kutim*), using the question of *matzat Kuti* — Samaritan-baked *matzah* — as the test case.

The *tanna kamma* holds that Samaritan *matzah* is not only permissible but one may even fulfill the *mitzvah* of eating *matzah* on *Pesach* with it. This means the *tanna kamma* trusts Samaritans to have baked the *matzah* with the intention of *shimur*, watching for the sake of the *mitzvah* — *leshem chovat matzah* — a requirement drawn from *uvaerev tochelu matzot*. Even knowing it was not *chametz* would not by itself be enough to fulfill the *mitzvah* of *matzah*; the *matzah* must have been guarded with the proper intent. The fact that the *tanna kamma* assumes Samaritans achieve this standard represents a remarkable degree of trust, since this is precisely the kind of detail — a *talmud Torah* she-be'al peh requirement about intentionality — that one would not obviously expect Samaritans to follow.

Rabbi Eliezer disagrees sharply: *oser lefi she'ein beki'in bedikdukei mitzvot* — they are not expert in the fine points of the commandments, and one must therefore be concerned not only that the *matzah* may fail the *mitzvah* requirement but that it may even be *chametz*.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel takes an intermediate position: *kol mitzvah shehechziku bah Kutim harbeh medakdekim bah yoter mi-Yisrael* — any *mitzvah* that the Samaritans have taken hold of, they observe with even greater punctiliousness than natural-born Jews. The practical implication is that one must evaluate each *mitzvah* individually: has this particular practice been embraced by the Samaritan community or not? If yes, one may rely on them completely in that area; if not, one cannot rely on them at all.

The relevant background here is that the Samaritans' status as Jews is itself contested. Their ancestors were resettled in the Land of Israel toward the end of the First Temple period, initially continuing pagan practices, eventually adopting the laws of the land's God after the famous episode of the lions — and the question of whether that constituted a valid conversion has been debated ever since. The Samaritans maintained their own *Sefer Torah* with significant textual variants and developed their own forms of observance. The debate in the *baraita* is not about whether they are considered Jewish — all three opinions appear to assume they are — but about how much one can rely on the quality of their observance in any given area.

### Opinion ID

*Manni matnitin* — whose view does our *mishna* reflect, given that it is lenient with Samaritan witnesses specifically for *get* and *shtar shichrur* but not for other documents?

The *tanna kamma* cannot be the source: if one trusts Samaritans without qualification, their witnesses should be valid for all documents, not just these two. Rabbi Eliezer cannot be the source: if one never trusts Samaritans, even *get* and *shtar shichrur* should be excluded. Only Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, with his context-sensitive approach, can logically support the *mishna*'s distinction. And so the *gemara* initially proposes: the *mishna* follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who holds that Samaritans have embraced the standards required for *get* and *shtar shichrur* but not for other documents.

### One Witness Puzzle

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: *im hachi mai irya chad* — if Samaritans are trusted in this area, why does the *mishna* speak of one Samaritan witness? If they are reliable for *get* and *shtar shichrur*, two should be equally valid. *Afilu terei nami* — even two of them ought to be acceptable. And the *mishna* implies that only one is acceptable alongside a Jewish witness.

Conversely, if we say the *mishna* follows Rabbi Eliezer, who does not trust Samaritans at all — then why is even one *ed Kuti* allowed for a *get* and a *shtar shichrur*?

This difficulty renders the easy identification of the *mishna*'s author impossible. Moreover, the question of what it even means for Samaritans to have "embraced" the practice of signing *gittin* as opposed to other documents is conceptually puzzling. Observance of *Shabbat* or *matzah* is a ritual one can adopt. But signing documents honestly? The question of whether they are *ne'emanim* in signing a *get* versus signing a bill of sale seems to reduce to a question about truthfulness in different contexts: do they take personal-status documents more seriously than commercial ones? One could construct such an argument — similar to observing that even in contemporary society people may treat matters of personal legal standing with greater gravity than commercial paperwork — but it is admittedly an unusual axis on which to divide trustworthiness.

*Tosafot* also notes a further dimension: a *gemara* elsewhere raises the problem of *lifnei iver* — even if Samaritans are personally careful about a given practice, it does not necessarily follow that they will be careful on your behalf. So even if a Samaritan's own *matzah* is beyond reproach, how do you know they guarded *your* matzah with sufficient care?

### Validation Theory

Rav Elazar resolves the puzzle about one witness: *lo hechshiru bo ela ed echad Kuti bilvad* — we validated only a single Samaritan alongside a single Jewish witness. And the mechanism is not that Samaritans are trusted in this area, but rather that the presence of the Jewish signature implicitly validates the Samaritan's. *D'i lav d'Kuti chaver hava, lo havei machtim lei mikamei* — if this Samaritan were not a trustworthy individual, the Jewish witness would not have allowed his name to appear above his own, or equivalently, the Jew would not have signed his name below the Samaritan's. By affixing his signature after the Samaritan's, the Jewish witness implicitly testifies to the reliability of the Samaritan. The document does not depend on trusting Samaritans as a class; it depends on the individual Jewish signatory vouching, through his subsequent signature, for this particular Samaritan. *Tosafot* notes the corollary: biblically, the Samaritan's signature may in principle be acceptable; the concern about reliability is rabbinic, and the Jewish co-signature resolves it rabbinically.

### Validation Problem

The *gemara* immediately challenges: *ei hachi, afilu she'ar shtarot nami* — if this validation mechanism works, why not extend it to all documents? If a Jewish witness signs after a Samaritan, he implicitly endorses the Samaritan's signature regardless of what kind of document it is. Why is the allowance limited to *get* and *shtar shichrur*?

The answer: *ravcha shavik l'man d'kashish minei* — a witness may leave a blank line above his signature as a courtesy, allowing for a more senior or distinguished person to sign in the space above him. If I sign on the second line, it does not necessarily mean I knew who signed above me or implicitly endorsed them. Perhaps I simply left that line open for an honoree, and you later procured a Samaritan to fill it in — in a different room, at a different time — without my knowledge. My signing second therefore does not establish that I witnessed the Samaritan's signature or validated it. This concern applies equally to *get* as to any other document: I might have left a blank line on the *get* too for a more senior witness, and the Samaritan filled it afterward.

### Mutual Presence

*Amar Rav Pappa zos omeres* — this tells us, says Rav Pappa, that *eidei ha-get ein chasmin zeh belo zeh* — witnesses signing a *get* must all sign in each other's presence. This heightened requirement — which does not apply to other documents — is what makes the validation mechanism work for *get* alone. Because all witnesses must be present simultaneously when the *get* is signed, if the Jewish witness signed after the Samaritan, he was necessarily in the room when the Samaritan signed. He cannot claim he left a blank line and someone else filled it in later; the rule is that all signatures must occur in mutual presence. His subsequent signature therefore does constitute an implicit endorsement of the Samaritan's signature, in a way that is simply not possible with other documents where no such requirement exists.

### Kulchem Fraud

*Amar Rav Ashi gezeira mishum kulchem* — Rav Ashi explains the reason for this heightened requirement. A husband may stipulate at the time of the *get*'s writing that all present in the room must sign — *kulchem*, all of you. This is consistent with the notion that a *get* is, in a sense, the embodiment of the husband's will, and he may impose more demanding conditions on its validity. If he says *kulchem*, then the *get* is valid only if every person present signs; if fewer than all of them sign, the *get* is *pasul*. This creates a real danger: a husband could invoke *kulchem*, witnesses could then scatter, and a deliverer could collect signatures from two or three of the original witnesses while leaving others out. The resulting document would appear facially valid — it bears two *kosher* signatures — but would be *pasul* because not all whom the husband designated signed it. The woman would believe she is divorced when she is not.

To prevent this fraud, the *gemara* requires that all witnesses sign in each other's presence. That way, if *kulchem* was invoked, no witness can sign without all others being present to refuse to participate if the full quorum is not in the room. The requirement of *ain chasmin zeh belo zeh* is thus a protective enactment to ensure that the *kulchem* stipulation cannot be circumvented. And as a byproduct of this rule, a Jewish witness who signs after a Samaritan necessarily witnessed the Samaritan's signature, lending the validation effect that Rav Elazar described.

### Conclusion

With this structure in place, the *gemara* returns to Rav Elazar's teaching: *lo hechshiru bo ela ed echad Kuti bilvad*. The *mishna*'s rule of *ed Kuti* refers to one Samaritan, with a later-signing Jewish witness providing the implicit validation. *Amar Abaye tani eido* — Abaye reads the *maaseh* of Rabban Gamliel's case as involving a single Samaritan witness (removing the letter *yud* from *eidav* to read *eido*, "his witness"). *Rava amar le-olam terei* — Rava preserves the plural: Rabban Gamliel indeed validated a *get* with two Samaritan witnesses. *Rabban Gamliel miflag palig* — Rabban Gamliel takes the more lenient position; the text requires a *chisura mechsara* supplement to read: Rabban Gamliel validates even with two Samaritan witnesses. He may hold, consistent with the more trusting of the *tana'ic* views, that Samaritans are generally reliable in the area of *gittin* — whether because they have embraced that domain of practice or because he broadly trusts their testimony.

The overall picture, then, is this: the general rule is that Samaritan witnesses are invalid for signing documents. For *get* and *shtar shichrur*, a single Samaritan witness is permitted under the standard view, because the Jewish witness who signs afterward necessarily validates the Samaritan's signature — a mechanism enabled by the unique requirement of *ain chasmin zeh belo zeh*, itself enacted to prevent the *kulchem* fraud. Rabban Gamliel goes further, permitting two Samaritan witnesses on a *get*, apparently operating from a more expansive trust in Samaritan reliability in matters of personal status.
