# Rosh Hashanah 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Critical Moment Overview

### Development Stage Questi…

The daf opens in the midst of a fundamental question: at what stage of development does one examine a crop in order to determine which year it belongs to? The year itself is established by *rosh hashanah* — the first of *tishrei* — which determines one's position in the *shemita* cycle, whether produce from one year may be taken on another, and related matters. But the operative question is which developmental milestone triggers the assignment to a given year.

By way of review, a *braita* cited at the close of the previous daf (12b, approximately ten lines from the bottom) establishes:

> תנן התם: התלתן משתצמח, והתבואה והזיתים משיביאו שליש.

That is, fenugreek goes by the time it sprouts, while grain (*tvuah*) and olives go by *havi'u shalish* — once they have developed a third. What exactly does "a third" mean? The agricultural scholar Yehoshua Felix, in his work on agriculture in the land of Israel during the period of the *mishna*, explains that *hevi'u shalish* means that the grain's weight has reached one third of its ultimate weight — a definition that renders the criterion more quantifiable, even if still difficult to assess by the naked eye.

The *gemara* further clarifies that by fenugreek, *titzmach* means *titzmach lizraim* — it has sprouted to the point where the seeds are fit for replanting. *Tosafot* draws a conceptual link here: *havi'u shalish* in grain similarly means that the seeds within have reached a stage at which they are viable for replanting.

### Vegetable Picking

The case of vegetables was addressed through a *braita* in the earlier discussion, which taught that the critical moment for vegetables is *lekita* — the act of picking. Vegetables picked before *rosh hashanah* belong to the preceding year; those picked after belong to the new year.

### Obligation vs. Dating

Before proceeding to the main discussion, an important distinction must be drawn — one that generates much of the confusion in this sugya. There are two entirely separate questions that both invoke the concept of *hava'at shalish*, and it is critical not to conflate them.

The first question is: when does produce become obligated in *terumot u'ma'asrot*? This is a question about the status of the produce itself — is it sufficiently developed to be considered food or fruit such that the obligation of tithing attaches? *Tosafot* notes that the opening *mishnayot* of tractate *Ma'asrot* enumerate the various signs of development for different types of fruit and produce, and explains that all of these are simply applications of the single principle of *hava'at shalish* — the moment at which tithing obligations begin. Before this point, even if one were to designate *terumah*, it would have no legal effect. After *hava'at shalish* and until *gmar melacha* — the completion of processing — one may snack from the produce but is not obligated to tithe until processing is complete. After *gmar melacha* (such as heaping grain into a pile and bringing it into the house, or bundling vegetables), even snacking is prohibited without tithing. For this entire framework, *hava'at shalish* serves as the universal threshold across all produce types.

### Chaviv Framework

This first framework — the question of when the *chiyuv* in *terumot u'ma'asrot* begins — applies uniformly. Everything, whether fruit, vegetables, or grain, goes by *hava'at shalish* as the entry point into the tithing system. The gradient runs from before *hava'at shalish* (no obligation, tithes ineffective) through the window between *hava'at shalish* and *gmar melacha* (snacking permitted, tithing effective if done) to after *gmar melacha* (snacking prohibited, full obligation).

### Year Determination

The second question — the one actually under discussion — is categorically different: which *year* does the produce belong to? This matters for *shemita* status, for *maaser sheni* versus *maaser ani*, for the prohibition of taking from one year's produce on another year's, and so on. The year is determined by *rosh hashanah*, but the question is: which developmental milestone locks in the year?

Here, unlike in the obligation framework, the answer varies by produce type. The milestone differs depending on whether one is dealing with grain, fruit, or vegetables.

### Produce-Type Moments

The different milestones for different produce categories are as follows. For *yerek* (vegetables), the determinative moment is *lekita* — picking. For *tvuah* (grain) and olives, it is *hava'at shalish* — a third developed. For *peiros* (fruit trees), it is *chanata* — blossoming. And for *kitniyot* (legumes and seeds such as rice, millet, poppy, and sesame), the answer will prove to be *hashrashah* — taking root — for reasons the *gemara* will explain.

To illustrate with grain: if wheat is planted in one year and growing, but does not reach *shalish* until the following year, it belongs to the year in which it reached *shalish*. The *chiyuv* question and the year-dating question are answered by the same criterion for grain, but for vegetables and fruit they diverge entirely. The confusion arises because the terminology of *shalish* appears in both frameworks, but for distinct purposes.

## Sukkot Proof & Chall (II)

### Source of Third

With the framework clarified, the *gemara* returns to the primary inquiry: what is the source for the rule that a third of development determines the year to which grain belongs?

> מנא הני מילי?

### Sukkot Verse Logic

Abaye transmits in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, who transmits it in the name of Rabbi Yosi HaGlili, a derivation from the following verse:

> מקץ שבע שנים במועד שנת השמיטה בחג הסוכות.

The verse speaks of "the end of seven years, at the appointed time of the *shemita* year, on *Chag HaSukkot*." The *gemara* presses: if we are at the end of seven years, it is already the eighth year — the *sukkot* referred to falls in year eight, not in the *shemita* year. Why then does the verse call it "*shnat hashmita*" — the *shemita* year?

The answer: the verse is teaching that any grain (*tvuah*) which had developed a third before *rosh hashanah* of the *shemita* year — even if it continues growing into the eighth year — retains the sanctity of *shemita* produce. The verse calls it the *shemita* year because some produce, by virtue of having reached *shalish* before *rosh hashanah*, still carries *shemita* status even as it is harvested in the eighth year.

But this teaches only that the timing of *hava'at shalish* relative to *rosh hashanah* is consequential for *shemita* status. It does not yet establish why the specific threshold of a *third* is the operative criterion. A further step is needed.

### Precision Challenge

Rabbi Zeira raises a prior challenge entirely. Perhaps, he suggests, the verse is not speaking about the sanctity of produce at all. Perhaps it simply means that the laws of *shemita* extend into the eighth year as a matter of calendar — one continues abstaining from labor even past *rosh hashanah*, similar to the principle of *mosifin mechol al hakodesh*, adding from the weekday onto the holy. In that reading, no inference about produce development can be drawn.

The *gemara* rejects this: *lo salka daitach*. The verse elsewhere says:

> וחג האסיף בצאת השנה באספך את מעשיך מן השדה.

The festival of *asif* at the end of the year, when you gather in your produce from the field. The word *asif* appears twice — once as the name of the festival (*Chag HaAsif*) and once in the phrase *be'aspecha* (when you gather). Since the gathering is already stated explicitly in *be'aspecha*, the *asif* in the festival's name must be conveying something additional.

The *gemara* reads *asif* here not as gathering (*asifah*) but as harvesting (*katzir*). This is somewhat striking, since one does not typically harvest grain at *sukkot* time — Felix notes it is legumes that are harvested then, not grain. Nevertheless, the *gemara* interprets the verse to be saying: if you are harvesting grain at *sukkot* time, that grain is still called "at the end of the year" (*betzeit hashanah*) — meaning it remains connected to the preceding year. And the logic is: if grain is harvested at *sukkot*, it must have reached *shalish* before *rosh hashanah* of the previous year. Thus whatever reached *shalish* before *rosh hashanah* is considered part of the previous year. The criterion of a third is derived by retroactive inference: since grain harvested at *sukkot* is connected to the prior year, and since the *gemara* establishes that this requires having been a third developed before *rosh hashanah*, it follows that reaching a third is the operative moment.

Rabbi Yirmiyah then poses a challenge to Rabbi Zeira: can the rabbis really distinguish between grain that is a third developed and grain that is less than a third? Is such precision achievable — and is it really possible to retroactively conclude from the fact of a *sukkot* harvest that the grain must have been precisely a third developed by *rosh hashanah*?

### Measurement Standards

Rabbi Zeira responds sharply:

> לאו אמינא לך? לא תיפק נפשך לבר מהלכתא. כל מדת חכמים כן הוא.

"Have I not told you — do not take yourself outside the halakhic system. This is how all rabbinic measurements work."

He illustrates with a series of parallel cases: a *mikveh* requires forty *sa'ah* of water — forty *sa'ah* minus a thumbnail (or, as *Tosafot* says, even minus a single drop) is invalid. Food contracts impurity if it is the size of an egg; an egg minus a sesame seed does not. A piece of cloth is susceptible to *midras* impurity if it measures three by three *tefachim*; remove a single thread and it is not. In each case, the threshold is a formalized estimate grounded in a real-world correlation — the forty *sa'ah* corresponds to the volume of water needed to cover a person's body; the egg corresponds to the amount of food that fills the gullet — but the rabbis do not claim scientific precision. They establish an average, formalize it, and it becomes the *halacha*, even if perfect precision in every individual case is unattainable.

The same applies to *shalish*. Based on what can be harvested at *sukkot*, the rabbis established the working criterion that grain harvested then must have been a third developed before *rosh hashanah*. That estimate is then formalized as the rule, and one does the best one can to assess it.

## Omer Story & Ownership

### Omer Source Question

Following the discussion of whether the criterion of *shalish* can be practically identified, Rabbi Yirmiyah withdraws his challenge — not because Rabbi Zeira's defense satisfied him, but because separate evidence demonstrates that the rabbis can indeed distinguish between more and less than a third.

That evidence emerges from a question posed by Rav Kahana's colleagues:

> עומר שהקריבו ישראל בכניסתן לארץ מהיכן הקריבו?

When *Bnei Yisrael* entered the land of Israel under Yehoshua, they brought the *omer* offering. Where did the grain for this *omer* come from? They entered the land on the tenth of *Nissan* and brought the *omer* six days later, on the sixteenth. The grain they found growing had been cultivated and was developing before they arrived.

### Non-Jewish Grain Problem

The problem: the Torah specifies regarding the *omer*:

> קצירכם — ולא קציר נכרי.

"Your harvest" — not the harvest of a non-Jew. Grain that was grown under the auspices of non-Jews would not be valid for the *omer*. Yet this is precisely the grain they found upon entry. How could they have used it?

### Omer Verification

Before answering, the *gemara* questions the premise: did they actually bring the *omer* that first year?

> ממאי דאקריבו? דילמא לא אקריבו?

### Eating New Grain

The *gemara* responds: *lo salka daitach*. The verse in Yehoshua states:

> ויאכלו מעבור הארץ ממחרת הפסח מצות וקלוי.

They ate from the produce of the land on the day after *pesach*. The implication of *mimocharat haPesach* — on the morrow of *pesach* — is that prior to that point they had not eaten from the land's grain. What changed on the day after *pesach*? They had brought the *omer*, which is offered on *mimocharat haPesach*, and it was the *omer* that permitted the new grain. The verse thus implies that they did indeed bring the *omer*, and only after doing so did they eat from the new grain.

### Pesach Terminology

This verse raises an interesting side observation. *Tosafot* cites a challenge from Rav Avraham Ibn Ezra — notably one of the very few instances where *Tosafot* quotes this Sephardic authority. The challenge concerns the meaning of *pesach* in the phrase *mimocharat haPesach*. In the Torah, the term *pesach* generally refers to the day on which the *korban pesach* is slaughtered — the fourteenth of *Nissan* — while the holiday itself is called *Chag HaMatzot*. If so, *mimocharat haPesach* would mean the fifteenth of *Nissan* — which is the first day of *yom tov*, not the first day of *chol hamoed*. This would actually support the rabbinic position that the *omer* is brought on the sixteenth — the day after the first day of *yom tov* — rather than the Sadducean interpretation that it is brought on the day after the first *Shabbat* following the holiday. Ibn Ezra notes, however, that the word *pesach* can also refer to the festival itself, and various resolutions are offered.

The *gemara* clearly reads *pesach* as referring to the fifteenth of *Nissan*, making *mimocharat haPesach* the sixteenth, which is the day the *omer* was brought and which then permitted the new grain. Some *rishonim*, including the Rambam, hold that this verse in Yehoshua itself constitutes the strongest proof that the *omer* is brought on the sixteenth — the day after the first day of *yom tov* — since the verse says *mimocharat haPesach*, not *mimocharat hashabbat*. Yet the persuasiveness of this proof depends entirely on how one reads the word *pesach*, which is itself precisely what is in dispute.

### Ownership Criterion

Returning to the main question, the *gemara* asks how, given the prohibition of *ktzirchem velo ktzir nochri*, *Bnei Yisrael* could have used the grain growing in the land for the *omer*. Rav Kahana answers:

> כל שלא הביא שליש ביד נכרי.

The determinative criterion for whether grain is considered Jewish or non-Jewish is *hava'at shalish*. If the grain reached its critical threshold of a third of development while in the possession of a non-Jew, it is identified as non-Jewish grain. If it reached a third under Jewish possession, it is Jewish grain. When *Bnei Yisrael* entered the land, some of the grain had not yet reached a third; they took possession; it reached *shalish* under their auspices; and thus it was valid as Jewish grain, usable for the *omer*.

This demonstrates that *hava'at shalish* functions as the criterion not only for dating produce to a particular year, but also for determining the "ownership identity" of grain — whether it is considered to have grown under Jewish or non-Jewish jurisdiction.

### Discernment Challenge

This answer is cited by Rabbi Yirmiyah as evidence that the rabbis can indeed distinguish between grain that has and has not reached a third. But Rabbi Yirmiyah himself then raises a challenge: how do we know that when *Bnei Yisrael* entered the land they actually had to assess whether the grain had reached *shalish* or not? Perhaps the grain had not sprouted at all by the time of entry, such that it developed entirely from zero to full under Jewish possession, with no assessment of partial development required. In that scenario, the story would not prove that partial development can be gauged.

> ודילמא אתו ולא קים להו — אבל היכא דאתו ריבעא בין שליש לפחות משליש לא קים להו.

Perhaps where grain was a quarter developed — somewhere between a third and nothing — one still could not determine which side of the threshold it had crossed.

### Grain Development Timeli…

The *gemara* immediately rules out the scenario in which no development had occurred at all upon entry:

> דכתיב: ויעלו מן הירדן בעשור לחודש.

They ascended from the Jordan on the tenth of the month. The *omer* was brought on the sixteenth. If the grain had not sprouted at all on the tenth, it could not have grown from zero to fully harvestable in five days.

But the *gemara* presses: suppose it had developed a quarter by entry. The same problem remains — how could it have grown from a quarter to fully harvestable in five days?

The *gemara* offers a resolution:

> ארץ צבי כתיב בה.

The land of Israel is described as *eretz tzvi* — the land of the deer. As Rashi explains:

> מה צבי קל ברגליו מכל החיות אף ארץ ישראל קלה לבשל פירותיה מכל הארצות.

Just as the deer is the swiftest of all creatures, so too the land of Israel ripens its fruit faster than all other lands. This miraculous quality explains how grain could develop from a partial stage to full harvest-readiness in five days.

But the *gemara* notes that this same reasoning cuts against the proof. If one invokes *eretz tzvi* to explain growth from a quarter to full in five days, the very same logic could explain growth from zero to full in five days — in which case the grain may not have been partially developed at entry, and no assessment of *shalish* would have been necessary. The question of whether *Bnei Yisrael* could actually discern the threshold is thus left unresolved.

## Sukkot Proof & Chall (II)

### Asif Language Challenge

The *gemara* returns to the core derivation for the criterion of *shalish* — the reading of *Chag HaAsif betzeit hashanah* as referring to the harvest of grain at *sukkot* time. Rabbi Chanina challenges this interpretation:

> מי מצית אמרת דהאי אסיף קציר הוא?

How can you claim that *asif* in this verse means harvesting (*katzir*) rather than gathering? The verse elsewhere states:

> באספך מגרנך ומיקבך.

When you gather in from your granaries and your wine vats. Clearly *asifah* means bringing stored produce inside, not harvesting from the field. Moreover, the master teaches that this very language — *psolet goren v'yekev* — is what the Torah uses to indicate the materials from which *sechach* may be made. One makes the *sukkah*'s covering from the remnants of the granary and wine vat, meaning plant matter that grows from the ground and is not susceptible to *tumah*.

Rashi explains the force of the challenge: if the word *asif* is already being used in the *drasha* of *Sukkot* to teach what *sechach* is made of — the remnants of the *goren* and *yekev* — then the phrase *Chag HaAsif* is simply another name for *Chag HaSukkot*, and it is entirely the festival's designation. There is no surplus in the word *asif* that would allow for the additional derivation that grain harvested at *sukkot* must have been a third developed before *rosh hashanah*. The entire *drasha* that yielded the criterion of *shalish* collapses.

### Proof Destroyed

Rabbi Zeira responds with a measure of dismay:

> האי מילתא הוה בידן, ואתא רבי חנינא שדא בה נרגא.

We had this *drasha* in our hands, and Rabbi Chanina came and threw a hammer into it.

It is worth noting that this exact expression — "we had a good thing and someone came and threw a hammer in it" — occurs very rarely in *Shas*, and with a striking parallel. In tractate *Sukkot*, the *gemara* derives from the same verse of *Chag HaAsif* the rule that *sechach* must be made from the remnants of the granary and wine vat — material that grows from the ground and is not susceptible to *tumah*. There too, Rabbi Chanina raises an objection (perhaps frozen wine could serve as *sechach*), and there too the *gemara* uses this same image of a hammer being thrown. The convergence suggests a deliberate verbal and conceptual connection between the two sugyot, both of which depend on the same verse and encounter the same challenger.

With the *Sukkot* proof demolished, the question reverts: where does the rule of *shalish* for grain actually come from?

## Deuteronomy Source

### Three/Third Wordplay

A new source is now proposed from a *braita* in the name of Rabbi Yonatan ben Yosef:

> ועשת את התבואה לשלוש השנים.

This is the verse from *Vayikra* in which God promises that the produce of the sixth year will suffice for three years — the sixth year itself, the *shemita* year, and the year following *shemita* while waiting for the new harvest. Rabbi Yonatan derives the rule of *shalish* through a *drasha*:

> אל תקרי לשלוש אלא לשליש.

Do not read *lishlosh* — "for three years" — but rather *lishlish* — "for a third." The verse then teaches: the *tvuah* is considered *asui* — complete, established — once it has reached a third of its development. This is a classic al-tikrei *drasha*, trading on the similar sounds of *lishlosh* and *lishlish*, and it provides the foundation for the rule that grain goes by *hava'at shalish*.

### Redundancy Question

The *gemara* objects: we need this verse for its straightforward meaning — *p'shat* — which is that God promises an abundance of produce to sustain the Jewish people through the *shemita* and *yovel* years. If the verse is consumed by this *drasha*, how does its literal promise get conveyed?

### Completion Concept

The *gemara* answers: *ksiv kra achrina* — the literal content is already taught by a different verse:

> וזרעתם את השנה השמינית ואכלתם מן התבואה ישן עד השנה התשיעית.

You will plant in the eighth year and eat from the old produce until the ninth year. The blessing of abundance — that the sixth year's produce sustains through the *shemita* and into the year of the new harvest — is thus already taught by this verse. The verse *ve'asas et hatevuah lishlosh hashanim* is therefore available for the *drasha* of *lishlish*, teaching that once grain has reached a third of its development it is considered complete and is determined by the year in which that threshold was crossed.

## Kitniyot Takeover

### Legume Categories

The *mishna* teaches:

> האורז והדוחן והפרגין והשומשמין שהשרישו לפני ראש השנה — מותרין בשביעית, ומתעשרין לשנה שעברה; ואם לאו — אסורין בשביעית, ומתעשרין לשנה הבאה.

Rice (*orez*), millet (*dochan*), poppy (*pargin*), and sesame (*shumshamin*) — if they took root (*hishrishu*) before *rosh hashanah*, they are permitted in the *shemita* year and are tithed according to the previous year. If not, they are subject to the restrictions of *shemita* produce and tithed according to the following year.

The operative criterion for these *kitniyot* is thus *hashrashah* — taking root — rather than *lekita* (picking), *hava'at shalish* (a third developed), or *chanata* (blossoming). This is immediately puzzling. All other criteria correspond to meaningful stages of maturation. *Hashrashah* is the very beginning of growth — the plant has barely begun to exist. Why should this earliest of stages be determinative?

### Moment Summary

Rava provides a useful summary of the landscape:

> אמרו רבנן: אילן — בתר חנטה; תבואה וזיתים — בתר שליש; ירק — בתר לקיטה.

Trees go by *chanata* (blossoming); grain and olives go by *shalish* (a third developed); vegetables go by *lekita* (picking). Into which of these categories do *kitniyot* fall?

### Vegetable Logic

Rava answers his own question. Logically, legumes most resemble vegetables — certainly rice and sesame do not seem like fruit trees, and while their kernel-like seeds might suggest grain, the intuitive categorization, as Rashi frames it, is with *yerek*. If *kitniyot* are like vegetables, the determinative moment should be *lekita* — picking.

### Convenience Logic

But there is a problem with applying *lekita* to *kitniyot*. As Rava explains:

> מתוך שעשויין פרחין פרחין — עשו רבנן בתר השרשה.

*Kitniyot* are picked on an ongoing, continuous basis — the pods are harvested repeatedly over an extended season. Rashi explains that *prachin prachin* refers to cracking open the pods continuously over time. Because of this pattern of ongoing, spread-out picking, any given batch of legumes will contain a mixture of produce picked before and after *rosh hashanah*. If the criterion were *lekita*, determining which year's produce one is dealing with at any given moment would be perpetually confused.

The solution the rabbis adopted was *hashrashah* — taking root. Even though the legumes are harvested over a long period, the planting — and therefore the rooting — happens all at once, at the beginning of the season. By pegging the determining moment to *hashrashah*, one has a single unambiguous point in time that applies to all the legumes in a given planting, regardless of when they are individually harvested.

There is a further question that Rashi's analysis raises: why should convenience drive the legal rule? Shouldn't the halacha follow an intrinsic stage of development rather than what is administratively tidy? The answer is that *kitniyot* in this context is entirely rabbinic in nature. Since the underlying obligation is rabbinic, the rabbis have latitude to structure the rule in whatever way is most workable — and they chose *hashrashah* precisely because it provides a clean, unified moment.

Regarding vegetables (*yerek*), the logic of *lekita* is that vegetables grow continuously, always getting larger and ripening further. At any given moment, the vegetable is connected to the current period of growth. It is not, so to speak, "from" an earlier year in the way that a tree's fruit, once it has blossomed, is locked into its year. The vegetable's identity at any moment reflects its most recent period of growth, and thus the moment of picking determines its year.

### Mixing Solution

Abaye challenges Rava's rationale. The concern about a mixture of pre- and post-*rosh hashanah* produce is real, but why is *hashrashah* the only solution? Why not simply follow the mixing procedure described elsewhere?

> ויצבור גורנו לתוכו.

Pile the threshing floor together — take all the harvested legumes, pile them together, and separate *terumot u'ma'asrot* from the entire mass. If the old and new produce are thoroughly mixed in the pile, any ten units one designates as *maaser* will contain a proportional representation of old and new. If 40% of the pile is old and 60% new, then the designated *maaser* will consist of four parts old and six parts new, which then serves as *maaser* for the respective portions — old for old, new for new — without any confusion.

This solution does not address every complication — if the two years carry different *halachot* (one being *maaser sheni* and another *maaser ani*, or one being *shemita*), the mixing trick does not resolve the substantive discrepancy. But as a method of avoiding confusion over old-and-new mixing, it seems functional.

### Equal Distribution

A *braita* in the name of Rabbi Yosi ben Kipar citing Rabbi Shimon Shezuri provides a case in point: Egyptian fava beans (*pol hamitzri*) planted for their seeds (in which case they go by *hashrashah*) — some of which took root before *rosh hashanah* and some after. The *braita* states that one may not take *terumot u'ma'asrot* from one batch on behalf of the other, since one may not tithe from new produce on behalf of old or vice versa. Yet the solution offered is precisely what Abaye proposed:

> צוברו גורנו לתוכו — ונמצא תורם ומעשר מן החדש שבו על החדש שבו ומן הישן שבו על הישן שבו.

Pile the threshing floor together and the *maaser* one takes will automatically represent an equal distribution — the old-produce portion of the *maaser* covers the old produce and the new portion covers the new.

### Distribution Dispute

Rava responds to Abaye's challenge:

> רבי שמעון שזורי גמרת? רבי שמעון שזורי סבר יש בילה; רבנן סברי אין בילה.

The *braita* follows Rabbi Shimon Shezuri, who holds *yesh bilah* — that solid matter genuinely mixes such that any sample drawn from the pile will reflect an equal distribution. The rabbis, however, hold *ein bilah* — that solids do not truly mix in this way, and there is no guarantee of equal distribution. Since the general rabbinic position is *ein bilah*, the mixing trick cannot be relied upon, and *lekita* as the criterion for *kitniyot* would produce an intractable mixture problem. It is for this reason that the rabbis established *hashrashah* as the determinative moment.

### Halachic Ruling

Rav Yitzchak bar Nachmani reports in the name of Shmuel:

> הלכה כרבי יוסי בן כיפר שאמר משום רבי שמעון שזורי.

The *halacha* follows Rabbi Shimon Shezuri. The mixing procedure is valid, and one may rely on equal distribution.

### Shmuel Contradiction

This ruling immediately creates a difficulty. If Shmuel holds that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Shimon Shezuri's position of *yesh bilah*, then we have an apparent contradiction with a different statement of Shmuel's:

> לכל אין בילה חוץ מיין ושמן.

For all substances, there is no *bilah* — no equal mixing — with the exception of wine with wine and oil with oil. Only liquids mix equally; solids do not. This directly contradicts the attribution of *yesh bilah* to Shmuel.

### Finish Time Standard

The resolution, which the *gemara* introduces here and will elaborate in the following amud, is that Shmuel does indeed hold *ein bilah* for solids. The statement that he rules like Rabbi Shimon Shezuri must be understood differently. There is a third principle of Shmuel's that mediates:

> הכל הולך אחר גמר פרי.

Everything goes by when the fruit finishes developing — *gmar pri*.

Shmuel does not actually hold that *kitniyot* go by *hashrashah*. Rather, he holds that they go by *gmar pri* — when growth is complete. Under this view, if one has beans that rooted before *rosh hashanah* and beans that rooted after *rosh hashanah*, the mixing trick works not because of *yesh bilah* but because there is no mixture in the first place. All the beans — regardless of when they took root — finish developing in the following year. They therefore all belong to the same year, and there is nothing to mix. Shmuel's endorsement of the *braita*'s solution is not an endorsement of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri's *yesh bilah* position; it is an endorsement of the outcome, arrived at by an entirely different route.

This makes for a subtle and somewhat counterintuitive result: Shmuel is cited as ruling like Rabbi Shimon Shezuri, but he does not actually agree with Rabbi Shimon Shezuri's reasoning. He agrees with the practical conclusion in the *braita*'s case while rejecting the theoretical foundation. The *gemara* will return to clarify this further on the following amud.
