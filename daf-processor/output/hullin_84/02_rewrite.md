# Hullin 84 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kisuy Hadam Intro

### Mitzvah Scope

The daf begins a new *perek*: *kisuy hadam*, the mitzvah of covering the blood of a slaughtered animal. Because the preceding daf was not covered in the previous session, the discussion opens on 83b, immediately following the *mishnah* that opens this *perek*.

That *mishnah* follows the pattern established by the opening *mishnayos* of *Oto Ve'et Beno* and other *perakim*: it delineates the scope of the *mitzvah*. *Kisuy hadam* applies only to *chayah* (a wild animal) and *of* (a bird), as the verse states — if a person captures a *chayah* or an *of*. The *mishnah* further clarifies that even domesticated animals of these species, which are raised rather than captured, nonetheless require *kisuy hadam*. Notably excluded are sacrifices: although one cannot bring a *chayah* as a sacrifice, one can bring a bird sacrifice, yet the *mitzvah* of *kisuy hadam* does not apply in that case — which would be strange indeed, since it would seemingly require throwing dirt onto the *mizbeach*, where the blood of those birds is dashed.

One might initially suppose that the exemption for sacrifices stems from the fact that birds brought as sacrifices are not slaughtered in the conventional sense but rather undergo *melikah*. However, *Tosafot* already anticipates the *Gemara*'s observation: the verse governing *kisuy hadam* does not say "if you slaughter," but rather *ve-shafach et damo* — "if you spill the blood." The exemption for sacrifices therefore cannot rest simply on the absence of conventional *shechita*.

### Dirt Above & Below

Before asking why *kisuy hadam* does not apply to sacrifices, the *Gemara* addresses the mechanics of performing the *mitzvah* itself. The basic process is that after slaughtering, blood pools on the ground, and one takes loose, sandy dirt — not clumped earth — and spreads it on top so that the blood is covered. What is less expected is the requirement that there also be a layer of loose dirt beneath the blood.

### Rabbi Zeira Drasha

This requirement derives from the *drashah* of Rabbi Zeira: *ha-shocheit tzarich she-yitein afar le-matah ve-afar le-malah* — the one who slaughters must place loose dirt below and loose dirt above. The source is the verse *ve-shafach et damo ve-chisahu be-afar*, "and he shall spill the blood and cover it in dirt." The letter *bet* in *be-afar* normally means "with," but Rabbi Zeira reads it as "in" — meaning the blood must be *within* dirt, implying a layer both below and above.

*Tosafot* makes an important clarification: Rabbi Zeira does not require the *shocheit* to perform a separate act of laying dirt beneath. The *mitzvah* act itself remains the placement of dirt above. What Rabbi Zeira requires is simply that loose dirt be *present* below at the moment of slaughter. If the animal happened to be slaughtered on loose ground, that satisfies the requirement without any additional act. On a stone floor, however, one would need to spread loose dirt before slaughtering, since no loose layer would otherwise be present. The validity of the *shechita* itself is entirely unaffected by the presence or absence of *kisuy hadam* — as with *Oto Ve'et Beno*, failure to perform the *mitzvah* does not render the meat non-kosher.

The *Gemara* elsewhere records a striking application of this principle: a case where a hunter shot birds with a specially sharpened arrow that performed *shechita* while the bird was in mid-flight. The question arose of how *kisuy hadam* could be performed, given the requirement of dirt below. The answer given is that the hunter had spread loose dirt throughout the valley beforehand.

## Sacrifices Exempt

### Mizbeach Obstacle

With Rabbi Zeira's requirement in hand, the *Gemara* now asks: *mukdashin mai ta'ama lo?* — why does *kisuy hadam* not apply to sacrifices? Specifically, the question concerns bird sacrifices, since *chayah* cannot be brought as a sacrifice at all. The difficulty is that the blood of a bird sacrifice goes onto the *mizbeach*. To satisfy Rabbi Zeira's requirement, one would need a layer of loose dirt on the altar itself before the blood lands — which presents obvious problems.

The *Gemara* explores whether one could place a layer of dirt on the *mizbeach* and simply leave it there (*le-vatlei*). The answer is that doing so would constitute *ke-mosif binyan* — adding to the structure of the altar. The verse states *ha-kol bi-chtav mi-yad Hashem alai hiskil*, that the design of the Temple was conveyed through divine instruction; one cannot unilaterally alter the structure of the *mizbeach* by expanding it with an additional layer of dirt. *Tosafot* notes some difficulty with this, since there are indications that the dimensions of the *mizbeach* were modified in the Second Temple. Rabbeinu Tam therefore suggests the prohibition may be against altering the *mizbeach* from its perfect square proportions; adding dirt to one side would change that. Yet *Tosafot* responds that one could then add dirt to all sides proportionally. The various opinions remain in tension, but in all of them the conclusion stands: one cannot add a permanent layer of dirt to the *mizbeach*.

### Chatzitza Problem

The *Gemara* then asks: what if one places dirt on the *mizbeach* without any intention of leaving it there permanently, merely as a temporary layer for the purpose of *kisuy hadam*? The answer is that the dirt would then constitute a *chatzitza* — a separation between the blood and the altar surface. If the dirt is incorporated into the altar, the blood strikes the altar wall and the altar has been structurally altered. If the dirt remains a distinct layer, it interposes between the blood and the altar. Either way, the problem cannot be resolved.

One might argue that a *chatzitza* is problematic only when it serves no necessary function, and here the dirt is necessary for the *mitzvah* of *kisuy hadam*. The *Gemara* does not explicitly raise this objection, though it is acknowledged to be at least theoretically debatable. The *Gemara*'s assumption, however, is that the dirt serves a different *mitzvah* from the one requiring blood to contact the *mizbeach*, and so the *chatzitza* problem stands.

---

## Theoretical Possibility

### Kol Hara'uy L'Bila

Even granting the *chatzitza* problem, the *Gemara* is not yet satisfied. It notes: *nehi de-le-matah lo efshar* — granted that you cannot have the layer of dirt below — *le-malah efshar* — at least you can still place dirt above. Why not perform *kisuy hadam* with only the upper layer? Does Rabbi Zeira's requirement truly invalidate the *mitzvah* when no dirt is below?

To address this, the *Gemara* invokes another famous teaching of Rabbi Zeira: *kol ha-ra'uy le-vila ein bila me'akevet bo, ve-chol she-eino ra'uy le-vila bila me'akevet bo* — if something is theoretically capable of being done, its non-performance does not invalidate; but if something is theoretically impossible, then its impossibility does render the act deficient.

### Mincha Mixing

The principle was formulated in the context of *bila* — the mixing of oil into a *mincha* (flour offering). If one brought sixty *esronim* of flour as a single *mincha*, one could theoretically mix it with the requisite amount of oil, even if in practice such a large quantity cannot be thoroughly mixed by hand. If one failed to mix it, the *mincha* remains valid, since mixing was at least theoretically possible. But if one brought sixty-one *esronim* — a quantity that cannot even in theory be adequately mixed — then the absence of mixing is fundamentally disqualifying. The principle is that theoretical possibility, not actual performance, determines whether an unfulfilled requirement becomes an absolute disqualification.

### Mikveh Application

A more accessible application: when one immerses in a *mikveh*, one is not required to open one's mouth and allow water to enter and perform a *tevilah* inside. Nevertheless, if there is a *chatzitza* inside the mouth, the immersion is invalid — because the water must at least *theoretically* be able to reach that interior space. Theoretical accessibility is required; actual immersion inside the mouth is not.

### Theoretical Impossibility

The *Gemara* now applies this principle to the case of sacrificial blood. When one slaughters a *chayah* on ground where there happens to be bovine blood pooled beneath — so that the blood of the *chayah* rests above that bovine blood rather than above loose dirt — one is nonetheless obligated to perform *kisuy hadam*. This demonstrates that the literal requirement of dirt below is not indispensable in practice. But why? Because theoretically, before slaughtering the *chayah*, one could have walked the animal a few steps to a spot where loose dirt was present. The theoretical possibility of having dirt below was there, even if it was not actualized.

By contrast, when blood falls onto the *mizbeach* from a bird sacrifice, there is no theoretical possibility of having loose dirt below — the obstacles examined above make it structurally and halakhically impossible. Since the requirement is theoretically impossible to fulfill, the entire framework of *kisuy hadam* cannot apply.

## Sacrifices Exempt (II)

### Blood Relocation

The *Gemara* continues pressing: *ve-ligrerei u-le-chasyei* — why not simply scrape the blood off the altar surface, relocate it to a spot where there is loose dirt below, and then perform *kisuy hadam* there? The *mitzvah* does not explicitly require that *kisuy* be performed *in situ*.

### Why Not Move?

In support of this proposal, the *Gemara* cites a *beraisa*: if blood splattered away from the collection area during slaughter, or if blood remained on the knife, one is still obligated to cover it. This poses a renewed question in light of Rabbi Zeira's requirement: how does one provide loose dirt below blood that is on a knife? The answer must be that one scrapes the blood off the knife, relocates it to a surface where loose dirt is present, and performs *kisuy hadam* there. Since this is precisely the procedure required for blood on a knife, the *Gemara* argues that the same should apply to blood on the *mizbeach* — scrape it off and cover it elsewhere.

### Alternate Coverage

This line of reasoning leads to a striking moment in the *sugyah*: the *Gemara* temporarily entertains the possibility that *kisuy hadam* actually *did* apply to bird sacrifices, such that each time a bird was sacrificed in the *Beis Ha-Mikdash*, part of the procedure would have been to scrape the blood off the *mizbeach* and perform *kisuy hadam* elsewhere. This is acknowledged to be surprising. One might have expected the verse — *ve-shafach et damo ve-chisahu be-afar* — to imply that covering must follow immediately upon spilling, without any intervening step. But the *Gemara* notes that the earlier sources about relocating blood before covering already involve a re-reading of that immediacy, based precisely on Rabbi Zeira's requirement of dirt below. And it is the same Rabbi Zeira's teaching that generates the difficulty about sacrifices in the first place.

## Metzora Exemption

### Bedek Habayis

The *Gemara* ultimately retreats from the idea that bird sacrifices required *kisuy hadam* and offers a different re-reading of the *mishnah*'s exemption. *Hachi b'mai askinan? B'kodshei vedek ha-bayis* — perhaps the *mishnah*'s exemption for *mukdashin* refers not to bird sacrifices offered on the *mizbeach*, but to animals that have been sanctified as property of the Temple treasury (*kodshei vedek ha-bayis*), not as sacrifices at all. This is admittedly a stretch, since *mukdashin* in the previous *mishnah* of *Oto Ve'et Beno* clearly referred to sacrifices, but the *Gemara* pursues it for the moment.

### Shechita She'eina Re'uya

The rationale for exempting *kodshei vedek ha-bayis* is as follows. An animal dedicated to the Temple treasury cannot be eaten — it is holy and must be sold, with the proceeds going to the Temple. Since the *shechita* of such an animal does not render the meat edible, it constitutes *shechita she-einah re'uyah* — a slaughter that does not produce fit meat. And the verse governing *kisuy hadam* states *chayah o of asher ye'achel* — "a *chayah* or bird that will be eaten" — which conditions the obligation upon the slaughter producing edible results. Therefore, if the slaughter does not make the meat fit for consumption, there is no obligation of *kisuy hadam*.

One might ask: why not simply redeem the animal, thereby removing its sacred status and rendering the meat edible? The *Gemara* raises this possibility — *ve-liphrekinhu ve-lichsinhu*, redeem it and then cover the blood — but *Tosafot* observes a deeper problem: even after redemption, the animal is no longer *kodshim*, so the case would no longer be an instance of *mukdashin* being exempt at all. The *Gemara*'s actual objection, however, is different: redemption requires *ha'amadah ve-ha'arachah* — the animal must be presented alive before a *kohen* for valuation. Once slaughtered, the animal cannot be stood up, and therefore cannot be redeemed.

### Tannaitic Views

The difficulty is that this explanation combines two positions that belong to different *Tannaim* and are not normally attributed to the same authority. The *Gemara* identifies the tension: if the *mishnah* follows Rabbi Meir, who holds that *kol ha-yu bi-chlal ha'amadah ve-ha'arachah* — that even animals dedicated to the Temple treasury must be alive for redemption — then the explanation of why redemption is unavailable works. But Rabbi Meir also holds *shechita she-einah re'uyah shemah shechita* — that even a slaughter that does not produce fit meat counts as a valid *shechita*. He would therefore not use the non-fitness of the meat as the reason to exempt from *kisuy hadam*.

Conversely, if the *mishnah* follows Rabbi Shimon, who holds *shechita she-einah re'uyah lav shemah shechita* — providing the rationale for exempting *kodshei vedek ha-bayis* from *kisuy hadam* — Rabbi Shimon also holds *lo hayu bi-chlal ha'amadah ve-ha'arachah*, that *kodshei vedek ha-bayis* do not require the animal to be alive for redemption. Under Rabbi Shimon, the animal could be redeemed even after slaughter, undermining the claim that redemption is impossible.

### Rav Yosef Resolution

Rav Yosef resolves the difficulty: *Rabbi hi* — the author of the *mishnah* is Rabbi, the editor of the *Mishnah*, who *ve-nasiv lah aliba de-tannai* — sometimes adopts the position of one *Tanna* and sometimes another. On the question of *shechita she-einah re'uyah*, Rabbi holds like Rabbi Shimon: because the verse says *asher ye'achel*, a *shechita* that does not produce edible meat does not count, and therefore there is no obligation of *kisuy hadam*. This is worth pausing over, because throughout the previous *perek* of *Oto Ve'et Beno*, the assumption was that a non-fit *shechita* still counts as *shechita* and still constitutes a transgression. Here, because the verse says *asher ye'achel*, Rabbi holds the opposite. On the question of *ha'amadah ve-ha'arachah*, Rabbi holds like Rabbi Meir: even *kodshei vedek ha-bayis* require the animal to be alive for redemption, so once slaughtered, redemption is impossible.

### Immediate Fitness

The *Gemara* then offers an alternative resolution that encompasses even *kodshei mizbeach* — actual sacrifices — and does not require the *Rav Yosef* analysis at all. *Va-iba'it eima kula Rabbi Shimon hi* — one can explain everything according to Rabbi Shimon alone. The key is a different reading of the verse: *ve-shafach ve-chisa*, "he shall spill and cover." The verse implies *mi she-eino mechusar ela sheficha ve-chisuy* — something that requires no intervening step between the spilling and the covering. For *kodshei vedek ha-bayis*, even if one could theoretically redeem the animal (as Rabbi Shimon holds), the process of redemption must intervene between the *shechita* and the *kisuy*. Since something is required to occur between the spilling of blood and the covering — namely, *pidyon* (redemption) — the verse excludes this case.

*Shemechusar sheficha pidyah ve-chisuy* — the sequence would be: spilling, then redemption, then covering. The verse demands that only spilling and covering be required, with nothing in between. The principle that fitness must be *immediate*, not contingent upon subsequent steps, is what excludes these cases. As the *Gemara* notes, this connects to discussions raised earlier about what happens when the *shechita* is not fit at the moment it is performed but only becomes fit afterwards.

Returning now to *kodshei mizbeach* proper: *ve-hashta de-asisit le-hachi* — now that we have arrived at this interpretation — *afilu teima kodshei mizbeach*, we can extend the exclusion to actual sacrifices as well. The reason: *shemechusar sheficha grara ve-chisuy* — after the blood is spilled on the altar, one would need to scrape it off before covering it. That scraping (*geira*) is an intervening step between the spilling and the covering. Since something must occur between the two acts, the verse excludes the case. *Tosafot* distinguishes this from the case of blood on a knife: there, the blood's presence on the knife was incidental, not structurally necessary. With sacrifice, the blood *must* strike the altar — that contact is inherent to the procedure — so an intervening step is necessarily built into any attempt to perform *kisuy hadam*.

The overall conclusion is striking: the exclusion of sacrifices from *kisuy hadam* is not based on a direct verse excluding them, but rather on a combination of this reading of *ve-shafach ve-chisa* and Rabbi Zeira's *chidush* that loose dirt must be present below. Without Rabbi Zeira's requirement, the *Gemara* would have no basis for saying *kisuy* cannot be performed on the altar, and the entire logical chain that excludes sacrifices collapses.

## Verse-Based Exclusions

### Chayah O Of

Having worked through the rabbinic and logical basis for excluding sacrifices, the *Gemara* now turns to a more direct textual derivation. Mar bar Rav Ashi cites the verse itself: *chayah o of* — "a *chayah* or a bird." The comparison is instructive: *mah chayah einah kedoshah* — just as the *mitzvah* applies to a *chayah*, which can never be brought as a sacrifice, *af of eino kadosh* — so too it applies only to birds that, like a *chayah*, are not sacrifices. The verse thus creates a direct analogy between *chayah* and non-sacrificial birds, excluding from *kisuy hadam* those birds that happen to be offered on the *mizbeach*.

This approach is cleaner than the one based on Rabbi Zeira, since it does not require the intermediate steps about dirt below, the *chatzitza* problem, or the impossibility of scraping blood off the altar. It goes directly to the heart of the matter: *kisuy hadam* is a feature of things that stand outside the world of sacrifices. There is something structurally fitting about this. *Chayah* and *of* share two distinctive characteristics relative to *behemah*: their blood must be covered, and their *chelev* (fat) may be eaten rather than offered on the altar. *Behemah*, by contrast, has its blood dashed on the altar and its *chelev* burned there. *Chayah* and *of*, standing outside that sacrificial framework, have their blood and fat treated differently — the fat may be consumed, and the blood must be buried. When a bird enters the sacrificial framework, it no longer resembles a *chayah* in the relevant sense, and the obligation of *kisuy hadam* falls away.

### Species Distinction

The *Gemara* raises a possible alternative reading of Mar bar Rav Ashi's derivation. Perhaps the *chayah*–*of* analogy operates at the level of *species* rather than individual status: just as a *chayah* is a species that *never* produces sacrifices, so too *kisuy hadam* applies only to species of birds that never produce sacrifices. Under this reading, turtledoves and pigeons — which *do* serve as sacrificial species — would be excluded from *kisuy hadam* even when the specific bird in question is not a sacrifice.

### Sacrifice Status

The *Gemara* rejects the species-based reading. Just as within the category of *chayot* no distinction is drawn — all *chayot* require *kisuy hadam* without any sub-categorization — so too among birds no species distinction should be drawn. The operative distinction is not species but individual status: if the specific bird happens to be a sacrifice, no *kisuy* is required; if it is not a sacrifice, *kisuy* is required regardless of species. Turtledoves and pigeons that are not being brought as sacrifices thus require *kisuy hadam*, and only individual bird sacrifices are exempt.

### Yaakov Mina

At this point the *Gemara* shifts focus from birds to the exclusion of *behemah* (domesticated animals such as cattle, sheep, and goats), and the discussion takes an interesting turn. Yaakov Mina'ah — a *min*, a heretic, one of those figures in the *Gemara* who challenges rabbinic interpretation of verses, likely in the mode of early Jewish Christians who engaged in Pharisaic-style textual reasoning — poses a question to Rava.

He observes: *kaima lan chayah bichlal behemah le-simanin* — it is established that a *chayah* is included within *behemah* for the purpose of the kosher characteristics. The Torah's enumeration of split hoof and cud-chewing as markers of kashrut is stated in the context of *behemah*, yet it applies equally to *chayot*. This demonstrates a degree of interchangeability between the categories. Therefore, *eima nami behemah bichlal chayah le-chisuy* — by the same logic, perhaps *behemah* is included within *chayah* for the purpose of *kisuy hadam*. Why not read the verse's reference to *chayah* as encompassing all animals, including cattle and sheep?

The question is not entirely without textual foundation, since ordinarily the context of the verse — speaking of *trapping* — implies wild animals rather than domesticated ones. But Yaakov Mina'ah presses the point: if *chayah* can mean "generic animal" in one context, why not here?

### Blood Like Water

Rava responds by citing a verse: *al ha-aretz tishpechenu ka-mayim* — "you shall spill it on the ground like water." This verse, from the section in Devarim permitting meat consumption outside the Temple once Eretz Yisrael is settled, addresses the blood of *behemah* slaughtered as ordinary food rather than as sacrifices. Its instruction is that the blood should simply be spilled on the ground like water — that is, without any special treatment. This is the source for the exemption of *behemah* from *kisuy hadam*: *ma mayim lo ba'u chisuy af hai nami lo ba'u chisuy* — just as water requires no covering, so too the blood of *behemah* requires no covering.

### Blood Not Water

Yaakov Mina'ah, however, presses further: if blood is called "water" by the verse, *yitbelu vo* — why not immerse in a *mikveh* filled with blood? This may seem like a provocation, but it carries a possible subtext. If this interlocutor is indeed a Jewish Christian, there is an evident theological resonance to the question of whether purification is achieved through water or through blood — a reference, perhaps, to baptism and to the Christian theology of purification through the blood of Jesus. Whether or not that subtext is intended, the *Gemara* takes the question seriously on its halachic merits.

The response is that the verse explicitly says *ach mayan u-vor mikve mayim yihyeh tahor* — a wellspring or a pit, a gathering of water, shall be pure — limiting valid *mikveh* to actual water and excluding other liquids. Yaakov Mina'ah pushes back: perhaps those exclusions refer only to other liquids that are never called "water" by the Torah, while blood, having been called water by the verse, should still qualify. The *Gemara* responds that there are two limitation-words in that verse — *mayan*, *mikve*, and *vor* with the word *mayim* associated with each — yielding three exclusionary expressions. Even if two of them exclude other liquids from the two types of *mikveh* (standing water and flowing water), the third is available to exclude blood specifically. The conclusion holds: blood is not water for purposes of *mikveh*, and *behemah* blood does not require *kisuy hadam*.

The *Gemara* has now established the scope of *kisuy hadam* through a combination of logical deduction, rabbinic principles, and direct textual derivation: it applies to *chayah* and non-sacrificial *of*, and not to *behemah* or to sacrificial birds.

## Derech Eretz

### Capturing Lesson

Having completed the *halachic* analysis of *kisuy hadam*, the *Gemara* turns to a series of *aggadic* teachings that emerge from the same verses — teachings of considerable relevance to anyone concerned with conscientious consumption.

The *Gemara* cites a *beraisa* on the phrase *asher yatzud* — "who captures" a *chayah* or *of*. One might have thought the obligation of *kisuy hadam* is limited to animals actually caught in the wild. The *beraisa* asks: *nitzodim ve-omdim me'aleihem*? What about animals like geese and chickens (*avazim ve-tarnegolim*) that are raised domestically and never needed to be hunted? The answer is *tzayid mi-kol makom* — "capturing" applies regardless; even domesticated animals require *kisuy hadam*. If so, why does the verse say "who captures" at all? *Limdah Torah derech eretz* — the Torah is teaching a proper way of conduct: *shelo yochal adam basar ela be-hazmanah hazot* — a person should not eat meat without the kind of deliberateness and preparation implied by going out to capture an animal. Meat should not be eaten casually or without forethought.

### Meat Moderation

A parallel *beraisa* develops this theme in the context of *behemah*. The verse in Devarim states that when God expands your borders and you desire to eat meat, you may do so. *Limdah Torah derech eretz* — the Torah teaches proper conduct: *shelo yochal adam basar ela le-te'avon* — one should eat meat only out of genuine desire, not as a matter of routine. Meat is not to be a staple but an occasional indulgence prompted by real appetite.

### Distant Location

The *beraisa* continues: one might think that if you have this desire, you can simply go to the market and purchase pre-slaughtered meat. The verse responds: *ve-zavachta mi-bekarcha u-mi-tzoncha* — "you shall slaughter from your cattle and your flocks." One must own the slaughtering process, not simply consume what another has already prepared. The dominant concern of the *Gemara* here appears to be moderation of appetite and responsible consumption — making meat require enough effort that one does not consume it indiscriminately. A secondary dimension, more salient perhaps to a modern sensibility, is the encounter with the reality of what it means to take an animal's life, rather than receiving it in sanitized, pre-packaged form.

### All Livestock

The *beraisa* presses further: one might suppose that having resolved to slaughter one's own animals, one may simply turn all of one's cattle and sheep into food. The verse says *mi-bekarcha* — "from your cattle" — not *kol bekarcha* — "all your cattle." Similarly *mi-tzoncha*, not *kol tzoncha*. Moderation governs not only frequency but also scale. Even when slaughtering one's own animals for legitimate consumption, one must not consume them without restraint.

## Financial Diet Scale

### One Maneh

From these verses, Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah draws a practical scale. One who has a *maneh* — a modest sum — should purchase for his pot a measure of vegetables. Meat has no place in the diet of someone at this financial level.

### Ten Maneh

One who has ten *manehs* may introduce fish into his diet. The ratio of roughly ten to one between the cost of fish and vegetables reflects something like the actual price differential of the time.

### Fifty Maneh

One who has fifty *manehs* — five times the fish threshold — may then add meat to his diet. Rashi notes that while in the Babylonian context meat was more expensive than fish, the reverse was true in other locations, and *Tosafot* adds that the scale applies accordingly depending on local conditions.

### Hundred Maneh

One who has a hundred *manehs* may have a pot of food every day. This last step raises an interesting question about the *Gemara*'s intent. Rashi does not read this as necessarily meaning a pot of meat every day; it may mean a fine meal — *gourmet* by the standards of the day — without necessarily increasing the meat component. The concern is less about limiting protein than about ensuring that one does not spend on food what ought to be spent on other necessities. Once one has ample means, a daily fine meal is appropriate — but it does not override the earlier principle of moderation in meat consumption.

## Rav's Response

### Rav's Concern

Rav declared: *tzrichinnan lachush le-divrei zaken* — we must take seriously the guidance of "the elder," that is, Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah, when making our dietary decisions.

### Healthy Families

Rabbi Yochanan responded: Rav — whom he calls *Abba*, which may have been his given name, *Rav* being an honorific — *mi-mishpachat bri'im havei*, comes from an exceptionally healthy family. Such a person can sustain a more austere diet. His constitution allows him to observe Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah's scale more strictly.

### Market Meat

But for others — *aval ke-gon anu*, but for people like us — *mi she-yesh lo perutah be-toch kiso yeritzena le-chanvani* — whoever has even a coin should run to the shopkeeper and buy what he needs. Rabbi Yochanan implicitly sets aside the requirement that one must own the slaughter oneself and the broader constraints on purchasing convenience food, acknowledging that not everyone can sustain the ideal of complete dietary austerity.

### Borrow Money

Rav Nachman goes further: *ke-gon anu, lovin ve-ochlin* — for people like us, who perhaps have greater physical needs or more limited means, one may even borrow money in order to eat adequately. The earlier emphasis on spending within one's means gives way, for those with genuine dietary needs, to the priority of proper nourishment. The *sugyah* thus ends with a revealing tension: the ideal is moderation and restraint, but the concession is that genuine physical need overrides the ascetic scale.

## Mishlei Wealth

### Sheep Garments

The *Gemara* now cites a series of verses from Mishlei, each interpreted to extend the theme of responsible, moderate use of one's resources. *Kevasim le-lvushecha* — "sheep for your garments"; *mi-gez kevasim yihyeh levushecha* — "from the shearing of your sheep shall come your clothing." The point is that one should make use of one's sheep for wool and clothing rather than slaughtering them for meat. This reinforces the prior teaching: do not deplete your flock through consumption when it can provide ongoing benefit.

### Field vs Flocks

*U-mechir sadeh atudim* — the price of a field, for flocks. The *Gemara* derives: *le-olam yimkor adam sadeh ve-yikach atudim, ve-al yimkor atudim ve-yikach sadeh* — a person should be prepared to sell his field in order to acquire flocks of sheep, but should not sell his flocks to acquire a field. This is striking, because other *Gemarot* emphasize the importance of holding real estate. Here, in the context of discussing how many things sheep provide — milk, wool, and ongoing yield — the teaching is that the productive capacity of a flock can outweigh the value of land.

### Goat Milk

*Ve-dei chalav izim* — "and sufficient milk of goats." *Dayo le-adam she-yitparnes me-chalav gedayim u-tela'im she-betoch beito* — it is sufficient for a person to sustain himself on the milk and dairy products of the goats and sheep in his yard. The *Gemara* notes that in antiquity people did not commonly drink fresh milk but rather consumed fermented and cultured dairy products — sour cream, cottage cheese, hard cheese, and similar preparations. The verse counsels contentment with these products, without proceeding to slaughter the animals for meat.

### Self Before Household

*Le-lachmecha le-lechem beitekha* — "for your bread, and the bread of your household." The *drashah*: *lachmecha kodem le-lechem beitekha* — your own bread takes priority over the bread of your household. One must ensure one's own sustenance before attending to others. This principle, however, will be immediately qualified.

## Child Rearing

### No Meat Wine

Mar Zutra the son of Rav Nachman offers a teaching on the continuation of the verse: *ve-chayim le-na'arotecha* — "and life to your maidens," or perhaps more broadly, "life to your young ones." The *drashah* is: *ten chayim le-na'arotecha* — give life to your youth. *Mikan limdah Torah derech eretz shelo yilamed adam et beno basar ve-yayin* — from here the Torah teaches proper conduct: do not raise your son on meat and wine. Do not habituate children to a diet of indulgence and luxury.

### Youth Life

The positive formulation of the same teaching is that a modest diet — dairy, vegetables, the produce of one's flock — *gives life* to children. By raising them on moderate, sustainable food, one grants them health, good habits, and the capacity for self-restraint.

### Ben Sorer

The teaching echoes the *halacha* of the *ben sorer u-moreh*, the "rebellious son" whose defining characteristic, as the Torah describes, is excessive consumption of meat and wine. The child raised from the outset on indulgent eating has, in a sense, been set on that path. The *Gemara* implies that the environment of meat and wine — even when the family can afford it — plants the seeds of the intemperate behavior that the Torah condemns.

## Wealth Sheep

### Small Animals

Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *ha-rotzeh she-yitasher ya'asok bi-vehemah daka* — one who wishes to become wealthy should engage in raising small animals, meaning sheep and goats. They provide wool and milk and yield an ongoing income without requiring the constant capital outlay that larger livestock demand.

### Ashterot

Rav Chisda adds a philological observation: *mai de-chtiv ve-ashterot tzonecha* — what is meant by the term *ashterot* for flocks? *She-ma'ashirot et ba'aleihen* — they enrich their owners. The term derives from the root of wealth (*osher*), because of the steady yield of wool and milk that flocks provide to those who tend them wisely.

## Balanced Care

### Weigh Words

Rav Avira taught — sometimes in the name of Rav Ami, sometimes in the name of Rav Assi — on the verse: *tov ish chonein u-malveh, yechalkel devarav be-mishpat* — "good is the man who is gracious and lends; he orders his affairs with justice." *Le-olam yochal adam ve-yishteh pachot mi-ma she-yesh lo* — a person should always eat and drink *less* than he can actually afford. This principle of calibrated restraint — weighing one's consumption against one's means and deliberately consuming below capacity — is the practice of one who "orders his affairs with justice."

### Clothing Self

*Ve-yilbash ve-yitkaseh be-ma she-yesh lo* — clothe yourself in accordance with what you have. Unlike eating, which is purely a matter of physical appetite, clothing is about self-respect and how one is perceived by the community. One should dress appropriately and well, within one's means — not with false austerity that invites disrespect, but also not beyond one's station. The wool garments mentioned earlier represent this ideal: dignified, appropriate, neither ostentatious nor neglectful.

### Honor Family

*Ve-yichabed ishto u-vanav yoter mi-ma she-yesh lo* — and one should honor one's wife and children *more* than one's means would suggest. Here the principle of taking care of oneself first, enunciated a moment ago with respect to food, is immediately counterbalanced. For one's own consumption, restraint and self-limitation are the rule. But for the needs of wife and children — their honor, their dignity, the provision of what they require — one should extend beyond one's comfortable capacity.

### God Dependency

The reason for this asymmetry is given: *she-hein tluyin bo* — they depend on him, so he bears a heightened responsibility toward them. *Ve-hu taluy be-mi she-amar ve-hayah ha-olam* — and he himself depends on the One who spoke and brought the world into being. One's own needs are ultimately in God's hands; the obligation to provide for others is in one's own hands. Therefore: eat less than you can afford, clothe yourself as you can afford, and give your family more than you can afford — because God will provide for you, but they can only depend on you. This teaching is encapsulated in the principle famously attributed to Rav Yisroel Salanter: *yenems gashmiyus iz daynes ruchniyus* — another person's physical needs are your spiritual obligation.
