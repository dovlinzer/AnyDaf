# Bava Kamma 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro to Bava Kamma

### Babas & Seder Nezikin

*Bava kamma* means "first gate." *Bava metzia* is the middle gate, and *Bava batra* is the last gate — the names are simply a way of saying section one, section two, section three. Interestingly, this same nomenclature appears elsewhere in *Shas*: Masechet Keilim in *taharos* also has thirty *perakim* divided into three sections of ten, each called *bava kamma*, *bava metzia*, and *bava batra*. The parallel is not coincidental. The *gemara* itself references the fact that our three *babas* were originally one *masechta* called Masechet Nezikin, subsequently divided into three sections of ten *perakim* each.

This organizational history resolves a longstanding curiosity about the ordering of *masechtos* in *Shas*. The standard principle is that *masechtos* are ordered by size, measured in number of *perakim*. Yet Sanhedrin, which has eleven *perakim*, appears after the *babas*, each of which has ten. And Makkos, with only three *perakim*, follows Sanhedrin and precedes the larger Avodah Zarah. The resolution: Makkos was originally part of Sanhedrin, giving Sanhedrin a combined fourteen *perakim*. And since the three *babas* together comprised thirty *perakim* as a single *masechta*, they rightly precede Sanhedrin.

The thematic connection between the *babas* and Sanhedrin is also worth noting. Both *Bava kamma* and *Bava metzia* are heavily rooted in *parshas mishpatim* — the civil law sections of the Torah. Sanhedrin and Makkos address the other dimension of that same material: not what the civil law is, but how it is adjudicated, how cases are brought before courts and judged. The Rambam's *Mishneh Torah* reflects precisely this division, with one section called *Mishpatim* and another called *Shoftim*. The progression through *Seder nezikin* thus follows an internal logic: first the substance of civil law, then the institutions and procedures that enforce it.

### Learning Methodology

*Bava kamma* is, of the three *babas*, the most coherent and self-contained. It engages directly with a relatively small number of *pesukim* in *parshas mishpatim* and develops an elaborate body of law from them. What makes it particularly distinctive — and what makes it so rewarding to learn — is the balance it strikes between *midrashei halacha* and classic Talmudic *sugyos*. Unlike some *masechtos* that are almost entirely extrapolated from a few *pesukim* without ever explicitly engaging them (Gittin being a notable example), and unlike purely exegetical contexts where one works through *pesukim* without the developed give-and-take of Talmudic argument, *Bava kamma* does both simultaneously. It constantly engages with the *pesukim* and *midrashei halacha* while extending and extrapolating outward into the full dialectical development of *sugyos*. This makes it a particularly rich example of *Torah she-ba'al peh* at work.

### Nizkei Mammon Content

The first six *perakim* of *Bava kamma* are devoted to what is called *nizkei mammon*. The term refers not to the thing being damaged but to the damaging entity — specifically, cases in which your property causes damage to another person or their property. The paradigmatic cases are your animal: your ox goes out and gores, eats someone's grain, or tramples someone's vessels. But *nizkei mammon* extends beyond animals to include more indirect forms of causation through concrete objects you are responsible for — a pit you dug, a fire you lit.

One might ask whether a pit or a fire truly constitutes "your property" causing damage in the same sense as your ox. The conceptual question is real: if I dig a pit in the middle of a public road, in what sense is it my property? If I light a fire and it spreads, do I "own" the fire? The answer is that these cases are not simply about ownership of the damaging object. What unites them is that there is some concrete thing — your animal, your pit, your fire — that brought about damage to another's person or property. This distinguishes them from *grama*, indirect causation with no intervening concrete agent, for which one is generally exempt. The defining feature of *nizkei mammon* is that something concrete and identifiable — traceable to you — caused the harm.

Within those first six *perakim*, the *gemara* distinguishes between *mamono hamazik*, cases where your property causes damage, and *adam hamazik*, cases where you yourself directly cause damage to another's property.

The remaining *perakim* deal with deliberate human acts. *Perakim* 7, 9, and 10 address *gneiva u'gezeila* — theft and burglary, the willful taking of another's property either openly or covertly. *Perek* 8 is *hachovel*, covering *chavala*, personal injury: not damage to property but physical harm inflicted on another person, which carries a substantially greater degree of liability.

It is worth pausing to notice what this taxonomy implies. If we construct a matrix: a human causing damage to another's property is *adam hamazik*; a human injuring another person is *chavala*; one's property causing damage — whether to another's property or another's person — falls uniformly under *mamono hamazik*. Strikingly, the law does not distinguish between whether your ox injures someone's property or injures their person. One might expect that personal injury inflicted by an animal should be treated more severely, just as *chavala* is treated more severely when inflicted by a human. Yet as we will see, that distinction does not carry over into the law of *mamono hamazik* — and that itself is a significant observation.

## Four Damage Categories

### Mishnah Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אַרְבָּעָה אֲבוֹת נְזִיקִין: הַשּׁוֹר, וְהַבּוֹר, וְהַמַּבְעֶה, וְהַהֶבְעֵר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: There are **four primary categories of damage: The** category of **Ox; and the** category of **Pit; and the** category of ***Maveh*,** which, based on a discussion in the Gemara refers either to the tooth of an animal that causes damage or to a person who causes damage; **and the** category of **Fire.**

The first *mishna* opens: *arba'ah avot nezikin* — there are four primary categories of damage. These are: *hashor*, the ox; *habor*, the pit; *hamaveh*, a disputed term the *gemara* will examine; and *hahev'er*, fire.

The term *maveh* is genuinely obscure. The *gemara* will debate its meaning: some hold it refers to *shen*, the eating or tooth category of animal damage; others hold it refers to *adam hamazik*, a person who directly causes damage. It is a strange word to use for either concept, and that strangeness is itself part of the discussion.

### Ordering & Mnemonics

The ordering of the four categories — *shor*, *bor*, *maveh*, *hev'er* — raises an immediate question. Rashi explains that the *mishna* arranged them in the order they appear in *parshas mishpatim*. Tosafot disputes this, arguing that depending on how one identifies what these categories represent, this is not in fact the order of their appearance in *parshas mishpatim*. The fact that this debate is even possible is striking.

The answer that is clearly correct — and is given by Rabbeinu Peretz — is that the order has nothing to do with the sequence of *pesukim* and nothing to do with any logical hierarchy. The order is mnemonic. *Shor, bor, maveh, hev'er* — the words sound similar, they almost rhyme, and this made them easy to remember when teaching orally. This also explains the choice of the unusual word *maveh* itself: whatever *maveh* means in terms of legal content, the reason the *mishna* selected that particular term is that it sounds like *hev'er*. The mnemonic value drove the vocabulary.

### Categorical Distinctions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הֲרֵי הַשּׁוֹר כַּהֲרֵי הַמַּבְעֶה, וְלֹא הֲרֵי הַמַּבְעֶה כַּהֲרֵי הַשּׁוֹר; וְלֹא זֶה וָזֶה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן רוּחַ חַיִּים, כַּהֲרֵי הָאֵשׁ שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ רוּחַ חַיִּים; וְלֹא זֶה וָזֶה שֶׁדַּרְכָּן לֵילֵךְ וּלְהַזִּיק, כַּהֲרֵי הַבּוֹר שֶׁאֵין דַּרְכּוֹ לֵילֵךְ וּלְהַזִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** Each of these categories is unique; therefore, the *halakhot* of one cannot be derived from another. The defining characteristic of **the** primary category of **Ox is not similar to** the defining characteristic of **the** primary category of ***Maveh*, and** the defining characteristic of **the** primary category of ***Maveh* is not similar to** the defining characteristic of **the** primary category of **Ox. And** the defining characteristics of **this** category of Ox **and that** category of *Maveh*, **in which there is a living spirit** that causes damage, **are not similar to** the defining characteristic of **the** category of **Fire, in which there is no living spirit. And** the defining characteristics of **this** primary category of Ox and *Maveh* **and that** primary category of Fire, **in which** the **typical manner** of their components is **to proceed** from one place to another **and** cause **damage, are not similar to** the defining characteristic of **the** primary category of **Pit, in which** the **typical manner** of its components is **not to proceed** from one place to another **and** cause **damage;** rather, it remains in place and the damage is caused by the injured party proceeding and encountering the obstacle.

The *mishna* then proceeds to explain why all four categories are necessary — why no subset of them could have been sufficient to infer the others. The argument is structured as a classic *lo ha'rei zeh ke'ha'rei zeh*: the characteristics of *shor* are not like those of *maveh*, and vice versa, because each possesses a feature that the other lacks which could independently justify liability. Even combining the two together does not allow us to infer the liability of *ha'esh*, fire. Why not? *Velo zeh va'zeh she'yesh bahen ruach chayim* — both *shor* and *maveh* (whether we take that to mean an animal or a person) are living beings with consciousness, and one might argue that the special obligation that attaches to living beings is what grounds liability in those cases. Fire is inanimate, and perhaps the rules should be different for something that lacks any animate quality.

But even knowing the liability of *shor*, *maveh*, and *ha'esh* together, one could not have inferred the liability for *bor*, the pit. Why not? *She'darkan leilech u'lehazik* — all three of the former have a way of going out and actively producing damage. They move, spread, or act. The pit does not move. It sits in place and people come to it and fall in. This is a fundamentally different type of danger. *She'ein darko leilech u'lehazik* — the pit has no propensity to go out and damage; rather, it poses a passive hazard.

## Common Denominator

### Hatzad Hashaveh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן – שֶׁדַּרְכָּן לְהַזִּיק, וּשְׁמִירָתָן עָלֶיךָ; וּכְשֶׁהִזִּיק – חָב הַמַּזִּיק לְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי נֶזֶק, בְּמֵיטַב הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **The common denominator of** the components in all of **these** primary categories is **that** it is **their typical manner to cause damage, and** the responsibility for **their safeguarding** to prevent them from causing damage is incumbent **upon you,** the owner of the animal or generator of the fire or the pit. **And when** a component of any of these categories **causes damage, the** owner or generator of the component that **caused** the **damage is obligated to pay** restitution **for damage with best**-quality **land.**

The *hatzad hashaveh* is doing significant halakhic work. By identifying the unifying principle across all four categories, the *mishna* creates a general rule that can be extended to cases that do not fall neatly into any of the four named categories. If something has a propensity to cause damage and you bear responsibility for watching it, then when it causes damage you are liable — even if it is not an ox, a pit, a fire, or fire in the classical sense.

### Meitav Ha'aretz

The *mishna* adds that payment, when made in land rather than currency, must be made with *meitav ha'aretz* — the finest quality land. This detail will receive its own extended treatment in the *gemara*, and we will return to it there.

### Liability Frameworks

Beyond the specific rule about *meitav ha'aretz*, the *hatzad hashaveh* raises a deeper conceptual question that will guide the entire first six *perakim*: how do we conceptualize the nature of the liability itself?

One approach is that liability is fault-based. I am liable because I failed in my duty — I should have watched my animal, I should not have dug an unguarded pit, I should not have lit an uncontrolled fire. The technical term for this is *p'shia*, negligence, and on this view my liability is rooted in my culpability. The *mishna*'s language seems to favor this reading: it says *u'shmiratan alecha*, you have an obligation to watch it, and when damage occurs, it calls you the *mazik* — you are the one who caused the damage. The emphasis is on your responsibility, your duty, and your culpability.

The other approach is absolute liability — *mamon hamazik*. On this view, it does not matter whether you were negligent. Your property caused damage, and therefore you pay. The question of whether you tried to watch it is secondary at best.

The practical stakes are high. The two approaches diverge when determining what degree of *shmirah*, watchfulness, is sufficient to exempt you from liability. If we start from a presumption of liability based on ownership, then perhaps only the most rigorous watchfulness would relieve you of that liability. If we start from a presumption that you are only liable upon fault, then demonstrating that you took reasonable precautions might be enough to establish that no fault attaches. Both frameworks are present in the *rishonim* and *acharonim*, and the tension between them runs throughout the first half of the *masechta*.

How does the *vav* of *u'shmiratan alecha* function in the *mishna*? Is it conjunctive — meaning that *shmiratan alecha* is a consequence of *darkan lehazik*, so that having a propensity to damage automatically generates an obligation of watchfulness, followed in turn by liability upon failure? Or is it characterizing — meaning that what defines the category of cases to which liability attaches is precisely those things you were obligated to watch? The first reading makes watchfulness a product of the basic liability relationship. The second makes the obligation to watch the precondition that triggers liability. The question is subtle but consequential, and it is embedded right in the *mishna*'s formulation.

## Avot & Toldot

### Parallels: Shabbos & Tuma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מִדְּקָתָנֵי ״אָבוֹת״ – מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא תּוֹלְדוֹת; תּוֹלְדוֹתֵיהֶן כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן, אוֹ לָאו כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **From** the fact **that** the mishna **teaches** its ruling employing the term: **Primary categories, by inference, there are subcategories** of those primary categories. The Gemara asks: Are **their subcategories similar to them,** i.e., to their respective primary categories, so that the same *halakhot* apply to them, **or** are they **dissimilar to them?**

The *gemara* immediately notices that by using the term *arba'ah avot nezikin* — four *fathers* of damage — the *mishna* implies the existence of *toldot*, derivative subcategories. The *gemara* then asks: are these *toldot* like their *avot* in terms of the *halachos* that apply to them, or not?

To set up the question, the *gemara* draws on two other areas of *halacha* in which the *av*/*toldah* framework appears. In the laws of Shabbos, the *mishna* lists thirty-nine *avot melachos* — primary categories of prohibited labor. The term *avot* implies the existence of *toldot*. And in Shabbos the rule is: *lo shna av chatas velo shna toldah chatas* — whether one violated a primary category or a derivative, if done *beshogeg*, one brings a *chatas*; if done willfully, the punishment is the same as well. The *toldot* are fully *keyotzei bahen* — they have the identical *halachic* weight as their *avot*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּבֵּי שַׁבָּת תְּנַן: אֲבוֹת מְלָאכוֹת – אַרְבָּעִים חָסֵר אַחַת. ״אָבוֹת״ – מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא תּוֹלָדוֹת, תּוֹלְדוֹתֵיהֶן כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן; לָא שְׁנָא אָב – חַטָּאת, וְלָא שְׁנָא תּוֹלָדָה – חַטָּאת; לָא שְׁנָא אָב – סְקִילָה, וְלָא שְׁנָא תּוֹלָדָה – סְקִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites additional areas of *halakha* where there are primary categories and subcategories and considers the relationship between them: **With regard to Shabbat we learned** in a mishna (*Shabbat* 73a): **The primary categories of labor** prohibited on Shabbat number **forty-less-one.** There too, from the fact that the mishna teaches its ruling employing the term: **Primary categories, by inference, there are** also **subcategories. With regard to the primary categories of labor prohibited on Shabbat, their subcategories are similar to them,** as **it is no different** if one unwittingly performed labor that is **a primary category,** for which he is liable to bring **a sin-offering, and it is no different** if one unwittingly performed labor that is **a subcategory,** for which he is liable to bring **a sin-offering. Likewise, it is no different** if one intentionally performed labor that is **a primary category,** for which he is liable to be executed by **stoning, and it is no different** if one intentionally performed labor that is **a subcategory,** for which he is liable to be executed by **stoning.**

In the laws of *tuma*, a different picture emerges. The *avot hatuma* — primary sources of impurity, such as a *sheretz*, *shichvas zera*, a *metzora*, a *nida*, a *zav*, and *tmei meis* — each have the capacity to convey *tuma* to persons and vessels alike. The *toldot*, by contrast — derivatives that have come into contact with an *av* and become a *rishon* — can only convey *tuma* to food and liquids. They cannot render a person or vessel *tamei*. Thus *toldosehen lav keyotzei bahen* — in the laws of *tuma*, the *toldot* do not carry the same *halachic* weight as their *avot*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּבַּי טוּמְאוֹת תְּנַן: אָבוֹת הַטּוּמְאוֹת – הַשֶּׁרֶץ, וְהַשִׁכְבַת זֶרַע, וּטְמֵא מֵת. תּוֹלְדוֹתֵיהֶן לָאו כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן; דְּאִילּוּ אָב – מְטַמֵּא אָדָם וְכֵלִים, וְאִילּוּ תּוֹלָדוֹת – אוֹכָלִין וּמַשְׁקִין מְטַמֵּא, אָדָם וְכֵלִים לֹא מְטַמֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to ritual impurities, we learned** in the mishna (*Kelim* 1:1): **The primary categories of ritual impurity** are as follows: Any of the eight species of **the creeping animal** enumerated in the Torah (Leviticus 11:29–30), **and semen, and one who is impure** with impurity imparted by a human **corpse. A person, a vessel, or food that is rendered impure through contact with an item classified as a primary category of ritual impurity is characterized as a subcategory. In that domain, their subcategories are dissimilar to them, as** any person or item classified as **a primary category** of ritual impurity **impurifies a person and** impurifies any **vessels** with which it comes into contact, **while** a person or item classified as **a subcategory of ritual impurity impurifies food or drink, but does not impurify a person or vessels.**

It is worth emphasizing that the word *av* and *toldah* are being used in meaningfully different senses across these three domains. In *nezikin* and Shabbos, *av* and *toldah* describe a conceptual categorization: certain acts or damage types are identified as primary cases (often those explicitly described in the Torah or Mishkan), and derivative cases are those that share the relevant characteristics but are not the identical concrete case. The question is whether they are categorized together for *halachic* purposes. In *tuma*, by contrast, the relationship is causal and sequential — the *toldah* is itself a derived *tuma* that was transmitted through contact. The conceptual framework is different even though the same vocabulary is used.

### Halakhic Implications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין אָב לְתוֹלָדָה? נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ, דְּאִילּוּ עָבֵיד שְׁתֵּי אָבוֹת בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי, אִי נָמֵי שְׁתֵּי תוֹלָדוֹת בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי – מִחַיַּיב אַכׇּל חֲדָא וַחֲדָא; וְאִילּוּ עָבֵיד אָב וְתוֹלָדָה דִּידֵיהּ – לָא מִחַיַּיב אֶלָּא חֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And what** difference **is there between a primary category and a subcategory?** The Gemara explains: The practical **difference** is **that if one** unwittingly **performs two** labors classified as different **primary categories together,** during a single lapse of awareness, or, **alternatively,** if one unwittingly performs **two** labors classified as **subcategories** of two different primary categories **together,** during a single lapse of awareness, **one is liable** to bring a sin-offering **for each and every** labor that he performed. Each primary category of labor is an independent transgression. **While if one** unwittingly **performs** a labor that is **a primary category and** another labor classified as **its subcategory** during a single lapse of awareness, **he is liable** to bring **only one** sin-offering.

Given that in Shabbos the *toldot* are fully equivalent to the *avot*, one might ask: why bother with the categorization at all? If every *melachah* and every *toldah* carry the same liability, what *nafka minah* is there to the distinction?

The *gemara* answers: the *nefka minah* emerges when multiple acts of the same category are performed in a single lapse of awareness (*beshogeg*). If one performs two acts that fall within the same category — whether two *avot* of the same *melachah* or two *toldot* of the same *melachah* — one brings only a single *chatas*. If one performs acts that fall into two different categories, one brings two *korbanos*. The categorization determines how acts are bundled for the purpose of *korban* obligation.

The *gemara* introduces a wrinkle regarding *av* and its own *toldah*. On the straightforward reading, the rule is simply that everything within a single category is one *korban*, and everything across categories generates separate *korbanos*. But one could read the *gemara* differently: perhaps the specific relationship between an *av* and its particular *toldah* creates a kind of overshadowing — the *av* absorbs its own *toldah*, such that even a combination of *av* and that *toldah* yields only one *korban*. On this alternative reading, two *avot* of the same *melachah*, or two *toldot* of the same *melachah*, would generate two *korbanos* — the combination that yields only one is specifically the pairing of an *av* with its own derivative. The accepted *halacha*, however, does not follow this reading; the operative rule is simply by category, not by the specific *av*-*toldah* pairing.

### Rabbi Eliezer Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר – דִּמְחַיֵּיב אַתּוֹלָדָה בִּמְקוֹם אָב; אַמַּאי קָרֵי לֵיהּ אָב, וְאַמַּאי קָרֵי לַהּ תּוֹלָדָה? הָךְ דַּהֲוָה בְּמִשְׁכָּן חֲשִׁיבָא – קָרֵי לֵיהּ אָב, הָךְ דְּלָא הֲוֵי בְּמִשְׁכָּן חֲשִׁיבָא – קָרֵי לַהּ תּוֹלָדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to Rabbi Eliezer, who deems one liable** to bring two sin-offerings even if one performs a labor classified as **a subcategory together with** a labor that is **its primary category, why is** one labor **characterized** as **a primary category and why is** the other labor **characterized** as **a subcategory?** The Gemara explains: Of the labors prohibited on Shabbat, **that which was a significant** labor **in the Tabernacle,** the Sages **characterized** it as **a primary category,** and **that which was not a significant labor in the Tabernacle,** the Sages **characterized** it as **a subcategory.** The labors prohibited on Shabbat are derived from the labors employed in the construction of the Tabernacle; therefore, their classification is also based on their significance in its construction.

Rabbi Eliezer disagrees. He holds that if one performs an *av* and its own *toldah* together, one is obligated to bring two *korbanos* — one for each. Given this position, the *gemara* asks: according to Rabbi Eliezer, what *nafka minah* is there to calling something an *av* versus a *toldah* at all? If both carry the same liability, and both generate their own *korban* even when performed together, the labels carry no *halachic* content.

The *gemara* answers: *hach d'havah b'mishkan chashiva kari lei av* — what was present in the *Mishkan* and was of significance is called an *av*; what was not of such significance in the *Mishkan* is called a *toldah*. According to Rabbi Eliezer, then, the *av*/*toldah* distinction is purely taxonomic — a way of labeling and organizing, indicating which acts are derived from practices of the *Mishkan* and which are derivative of those. There is no *halachic* consequence whatsoever to the categorization; one could replace the entire structure with an undifferentiated list of prohibited acts and nothing in the law would change.

A subsidiary question about Rabbi Eliezer's formulation: does *b'mishkan chashiva* mean that any act performed in the *Mishkan* qualifies as an *av*, or does the *av* require both that it occurred in the *Mishkan* and that it was of sufficient creative or constructive significance? Not every act performed in the construction of the *Mishkan* rises to the level of a *melacha*; polishing a piece of metal, for instance, would not. The *avot melachos* must be those acts from the *Mishkan* that were of genuine creative significance — but the precise criterion for identifying which acts meet that threshold is itself a subject of discussion.

Returning to the main question: what is the situation in *nezikin*? The *gemara* states that some *toldot* are like their *avot* and some are not — and then proceeds to survey each of the *avot nezikin* in turn, identifying their definitions, their *toldot*, and whether the relevant *halachos* apply equally. This survey constitutes the opening *sugya* of the *masechta*: a question posed by Rav Pappa (*yesh keyotzei vahen v'yesh lav keyotzei vahen* — some are like them and some are not), around which the *gemara* constructs an introductory tour of all the major categories and their characteristics. The *shabbos* and *tuma* parallels were scaffolding to raise the question; from here forward the discussion concerns *nezikin* exclusively.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא מַאי? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: יֵשׁ מֵהֶן כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן, וְיֵשׁ מֵהֶן לָאו כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** After determining that there are instances where the legal status of subcategories is like that of primary categories, e.g., Shabbat, and there are instances where the legal status of subcategories is dissimilar to that of primary categories, e.g., ritual impurity, the Gemara asks: **Here,** with regard to the laws of damages, **what** is the relationship between the primary categories and their subcategories? **Rav Pappa said: There are, among** the primary categories of damage, some whose subcategories **are similar to them, and there are, among them,** some whose subcategories **are dissimilar to them.**

## Three Ox Categories

### Keren/Goring

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה אָבוֹת נֶאֶמְרוּ בְּשׁוֹר – הַקֶּרֶן, וְהַשֵּׁן, וְהָרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** § Seeking to clarify Rav Pappa's statement, the Gemara cites a *baraita* that delineates the primary categories of damage. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Three primary categories** of damage **were stated** in the Torah **with regard to an ox.** An ox causes damage in three ways, and each is classified as a distinct primary category of damage represented by a part of the body of the ox: There is **the** category of **Goring [*keren*],** literally, horn. This is referring to an ox goring a person or an animal and causing damage. **And** there is **the** category of **Eating [*shen*],** literally, tooth. This is referring to one's ox causing damage by consuming another person's produce. **And** there is **the** category of **Trampling [*regel*],** literally, foot. This is referring to an ox trampling another person's belongings and causing damage. These are classified as primary categories because they are mentioned explicitly in the Torah.

A *beraisa* teaches: *gimmel avot ne'emru b'shor* — three primary categories are stated with regard to the ox. These are *keren* (the horn, goring), *shen* (the tooth, eating), and *regel* (the leg, trampling). Each of these is a distinct *av* within the broader category of *shor*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קֶרֶן מְנָלַן? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כִּי יִגַּח״ – אֵין נְגִיחָה אֶלָּא קֶרֶן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּעַשׂ לוֹ צִדְקִיָּה בֶן כְּנַעֲנָה קַרְנֵי בַרְזֶל, וַיֹּאמֶר: כֹּה אָמַר ה׳, בְּאֵלֶּה תְּנַגַּח אֶת אֲרָם וְגוֹ׳״. וְאוֹמֵר: ״בְּכוֹר שׁוֹרוֹ הָדָר לוֹ, וְקַרְנֵי רְאֵם קַרְנָיו, בָּהֶם עַמִּים יְנַגַּח״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **From where do we** derive the primary category of **Goring?** The source is **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "And **if** an ox **gores** a man or a woman" (Exodus 21:28); and **goring is** performed **only** with **a horn, as it is stated: "And Zedekiah, son of Chenaanah, made himself horns of iron, and said: So says the Lord: With these shall you gore the Arameans,** until they are consumed" (I Kings 22:11). **And** the verse also **states: "His firstborn bull, majesty is his, and his horns are the horns of the wild ox; with them he shall gore the nations"** (Deuteronomy 33:17).

Where do we derive the category of *keren*? The *gemara* cites the verse *ki yigach* — if an ox gores. And *ein negichah ela b'keren* — goring refers specifically to the horn. The *gemara* brings two *pesukim* to establish this: first, from the *navi*, where Tzidkiyah made iron horns and the prophecy was spoken: *b'eileh tinagach et aram* — with these you shall gore Aram, linking the horn (*karnei barzel*) explicitly with the act of *negichah*. Second, from the Torah itself: *bechor shoro hadar lo u'karnei re'em karnav bahem amim yenagach* — the horns of this creature are his glory, and with them he will gore nations.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְאוֹמֵר״? וְכִי תֵּימָא דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה מִדִּבְרֵי קַבָּלָה לָא יָלְפִינַן, תָּא שְׁמַע: ״בְּכוֹר שׁוֹרוֹ הָדָר לוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interrupts its citation of the *baraita* and asks: **What** is the purpose of citing the additional verse introduced with the term: **And** the verse also **states?** The Gemara answers: **And if you would say** that the first verse cited is not a legitimate source as it is a verse from the Prophets, and **we do not derive Torah matters from the texts of the tradition,** i.e., Prophets and Writings, **come** and **hear** proof from the Torah: **"His firstborn bull, majesty is his."**

The *gemara* asks why a second *pasuk* was necessary. The answer: we cannot derive a *halacha* in the Torah from *divrei kabbalah* — the words of the prophets. The verse in *Melachim* is not establishing a legal rule; it is merely illustrating the meaning of a word. This is not deriving *halacha* from *nevi'im* — it is lexicographic, the equivalent of consulting a dictionary to understand what *negichah* means when the Torah uses the term *ki yigach*. The second *pasuk* from the Torah itself therefore remains necessary.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי מֵילָף הוּא?! גִּילּוּי מִילְּתָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא, דִּנְגִיחָה בְּקֶרֶן הוּא! אֶלָּא מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא, כִּי פַּלֵּיג רַחֲמָנָא בֵּין תָּם לְמוּעָד – הָנֵי מִילֵּי בִּתְלוּשָׁה, אֲבָל בִּמְחוּבֶּרֶת – אֵימָא כּוּלָּהּ מוּעֶדֶת הִיא; תָּא שְׁמַע: ״בְּכוֹר שׁוֹרוֹ הָדָר לוֹ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the possibility that the reason a second verse was cited is that the primary category of Goring cannot be derived from a verse in the Prophets: **But is this** a halakhic **derivation? It is a mere disclosure of the matter, that goring is** performed **with a horn.** A verse in the Prophets can certainly serve as a source for that disclosure. **Rather,** the reason the *baraita* cites a second verse is **lest you say,** based on the first verse, that **when the Merciful One distinguishes between** liability for damage caused by **an innocuous** ox, for which the owner is liable to pay half of the damages for the first three times that it gores, **and** liability for damage caused by **a forewarned** ox, which already gored three times and whose owner was cautioned to prevent the ox from goring, for which he is liable to pay the full damages, **that statement** applies only to damage caused **with a detached** horn, like the horn of Zedekiah described in the verse, e.g., if an animal held a detached horn in its mouth and caused damage with it; **but** for damage that an ox caused **with** a horn **attached** to its head, **say** that in **all** cases the legal status of the ox **is** that of **a forewarned** ox and its owner is liable to pay for all of the damage. Therefore, the *baraita* says: **Come** and **hear** a proof from another verse: **"His firstborn bull, majesty is his,** and his horns are the horns of the wild ox; with them he shall gore the nations," where the reference is to a horn attached to the ox's head. Evidently, when an ox gores with its own horns there is a distinction between an innocuous ox and a forewarned ox.

But if we already know the meaning of *negichah* from the first *pasuk*, what does the Torah's *pasuk* add? The *gemara* answers: without the Torah's *pasuk*, one might have argued that the Torah's distinction between *tam* (the innocent, non-habitual ox, liable for only half-damages for the first three gorings) and *muad* (the habitual offender, liable for full damages thereafter) applies specifically to detached horns — the kind Tzidkiyah made, a horn not affixed to the animal. An animal goring with such a detached horn would pay only half. But if it gores with its natural, attached horns, one might have said the animal is always considered *muad* and always pays full. The Torah *pasuk*, which speaks of the horn as the animal's own — *karnei re'em karnav* — clarifies that the same *tam*/*muad* distinction applies even to natural, attached horns.

### Negifah vs Negichah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוֹלָדָה דְקֶרֶן מַאי הִיא? נְגִיפָה, נְשִׁיכָה, רְבִיצָה, וּבְעִיטָה. מַאי שְׁנָא נְגִיחָה דְּקָרֵי לַהּ אָב – דִּכְתִיב ״כִּי יִגַּח״, נְגִיפָה נָמֵי כְּתִיב ״כִּי יִגֹּף״! הַאי נְגִיפָה – נְגִיחָה הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: פָּתַח בִּ״נְגִיפָה״ וְסִיֵּים בִּ״נְגִיחָה״, לוֹמַר לָךְ: זוֹ הִיא נְגִיפָה זוֹ הִיא נְגִיחָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resumes its citation of the *baraita*: **What is a subcategory of Goring?** It includes any action that an ox performs with its body with the objective of inflicting damage: **Pushing [*negifa*], biting, crouching** upon items with the objective of inflicting damage, **and kicking. What is different** about **goring that it is characterized** as **a primary category** of damage, **as it is written** explicitly in the verse: "And **if** an ox **gores** a man or a woman" (Exodus 21:28); accordingly, ***negifa*** should **also** be characterized as a primary category, **as it is written: "If** one man's ox **hurts [*yiggof* ]** the ox of another" (Exodus 21:35)? The Gemara answers: **This *negifa*** mentioned in the verse, **is** actually a reference to **goring, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that the verses states: "And if one man's ox hurts [*yiggof* ] the ox of another…or if it is known that the ox was a goring ox in time past" (Exodus 21:35–36). The verse **began** its description of the case **with** the term ***negifa* and** it **concluded with** the term **goring to say to you** that in this context the two terms describe the same action: **This** action **is *negifa*** and **this** same action **is goring.**

The *beraisa* listed *negifah* (pushing) as the first *toldah* of *keren*. But the *gemara* notes that the term *negifah* also appears in the Torah itself — *ki yigof shor ish es shor re'eihu* — which would seem to make *negifah* an *av*, not a *toldah*. The *gemara* resolves this: the *negifah* in the Torah's *pasuk* means the same thing as *negichah* — goring. How do we know? *Pasach b'negifah v'siyem b'negichah* — the *pasuk* opens by describing the incident with the word *yigof* and then continues by saying *shor nagach hu mitmol shilshom*, confirming the ox is a habitual *nagach*, a gorer. The same act described as *negifah* is immediately thereafter called *negichah*. Thus *negifah* in the *Torah* means goring; *negifah* in the *beraisa* means pushing.

### Muad Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא גַּבֵּי אָדָם דִּכְתִיב ״כִּי יִגַּח״, וּמַאי שְׁנָא גַּבֵּי בְּהֵמָה דִּכְתִיב ״כִּי יִגֹּף״? אָדָם – דְּאִית לֵיהּ מַזָּלָא, כְּתִיב ״כִּי יִגַּח״; בְּהֵמָה – דְּלֵית לַהּ מַזָּלָא, כְּתִיב ״כִּי יִגֹּף״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If the two terms are interchangeable, **what is different with regard to** an ox goring **a person that it is written:** "And **if** an ox **gores** a man or a woman" (Exodus 21:28), **and what is different with regard to** an ox goring **an animal that it is written: "If** one man's ox **hurts [*yiggof* ]** the ox of another" (Exodus 21:35)? The Gemara explains: With regard to **a person, who has** the **ingenuity** to defend himself and is not easily injured, **it is written: "If** an ox **gores,"** a term indicating an attack of greater force. With regard to **an animal, which does not have** the **ingenuity** to defend itself and is more easily injured, **it is written: "If** an ox **hurts [*yiggof* ],"** a term indicating an attack of lesser force. The term *yiggof* is related to the term *magefa*, meaning plague. The Torah employs that term with regard to the goring of an animal to indicate that when an animal is gored, regardless of the force of the blow, it will likely result in its death.

The distinction between *negifah* and *negichah* yields a *halachic* ruling in passing. The Torah uses *negichah* exclusively when an ox attacks a human (*ki yigach*), while it uses both *negifah* and *negichah* when an ox attacks another animal. The *gemara* explains: *adam d'is lei mazla* — a person has a form of cosmic protection, or perhaps more practically, a person's own wits and alertness afford natural protection from danger. An ox intent on harming a person would need to be truly focused and determined — hence only the term *negichah*, which implies a more concentrated, deliberate act of attack. An animal, *d'lais lah mazla*, lacks this form of protection and is more susceptible to opportunistic harm — hence the Torah also uses *negifah*, which implies a less intentional, more incidental impact.

Rashi offers two interpretations of the nuance between the terms. Either *yigof* connotes a more opportunistic encounter (the ox happened upon the other animal rather than seeking it out), or *yigof* connotes a less forceful impact — a slight push that might injure an animal but would not necessarily injure a person, who requires the greater force implied by *yigach*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִלְּתָא אַגַּב אוֹרְחֵיהּ קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – דְּמוּעָד לְאָדָם הָוֵי מוּעָד לִבְהֵמָה וּמוּעָד לִבְהֵמָה לָא הָוֵי מוּעָד לְאָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Torah's use of these terms **teaches us a matter in passing:** Because the effort required for the ox to gore a person to death is greater than the effort required for the ox to gore an animal to death, the *halakha* is **that** an ox that is **forewarned with regard to** goring **a person is** also **forewarned with regard to an animal. But** an ox that is **forewarned with regard to an animal is not forewarned with regard to a person.**

The *halachic* consequence: *muad l'adam havei muad l'behemah u'muad l'behemah lo havei muad l'adam*. If an ox has been established as a habitual gorer of humans, it is presumed habitual for animals as well — the greater viciousness required to injure a person certainly encompasses the lesser threshold needed to harm an animal. But if an ox is established as habitual for goring animals, that habituation does not extend to humans. Animals are easier to injure; habituation for them does not establish that the ox has crossed the threshold required to be classified as *muad* for attacking people. Accordingly, if an ox that has gored animals three times then gores a human, the owner still pays only half-damages, as the ox is not yet *muad* with respect to humans.

### Keren Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילֵּימָא אַהָנֵי; מַאי שְׁנָא קֶרֶן – דְּכַוּוֹנָתוֹ לְהַזִּיק, וּמָמוֹנְךָ וּשְׁמִירָתוֹ עָלֶיךָ; הָנֵי נָמֵי – כַּוּוֹנָתָן לְהַזִּיק, וּמָמוֹנְךָ וּשְׁמִירָתָן עָלֶיךָ!
>
> **Translation:** **If we say** that his reference was **to these** subcategories of Goring, **what is different** about **Goring** that defines it as a unique primary category? What is different is **that** the **objective** of the ox's action **is to cause damage, and** the ox is **your property, and** responsibility for **its safeguarding,** to prevent it from causing damage, is incumbent **upon you,** its owner. In **these** subcategories of Goring, i.e., pushing [*negifa*], biting, crouching, and kicking, **as well,** the **objective** of the oxen's actions **is to cause damage, and** the oxen are **your property, and** responsibility for **their safeguarding,** to prevent your oxen from causing damage, is incumbent **upon you.**

The *gemara* now turns to the conceptual definition of *keren*. One might have thought that what defines *keren* is the body part used — goring with the horn, and therefore biting with the mouth should be a *toldah* of *shen* (since it uses the mouth), not of *keren*. The *gemara* rejects this entirely. The categories are not defined by anatomy. They are defined by the nature and character of the act.

The defining characteristic of *keren* is *kavanato lehazik* — the animal's intention is to cause damage. It is not seeking pleasure or simply moving in its natural way; it is actively directing itself to harm. This is what constitutes an act of *keren*.

### Shen & Regel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא תּוֹלָדָה דְקֶרֶן כְּקֶרֶן, וְכִי קָאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא – אַשֵּׁן וָרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is apparent that the status of **a subcategory of Goring is like** that of the primary category of **Goring, and when Rav Pappa says:** There are among them some whose subcategories are dissimilar to them, he was referring **to Eating and Trampling.**

Accordingly, *shen* is not defined by the use of teeth but by a specific character of the act: *yesh hana'ah l'hezeika* — the animal derives pleasure from the act that causes the damage. An ox eating someone's grain is the paradigm: the animal satisfies its appetite, and damage to the owner's property is incidental to that satisfaction. A *shen*-type act is one driven by the animal's desire for physical gratification. On this basis, if an animal rubs its body against a wall to scratch itself and the wall collapses, that is a *shen* act — it is pursuing its own pleasure, and the damage is collateral.

Biting someone, by contrast, is not *shen*. The animal is not biting to eat or to obtain gratification; it is biting as an act of aggression. That is *keren*. The body part is irrelevant.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֵׁן וָרֶגֶל הֵיכָא כְּתִיבִי? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְשִׁלַּח״ – זֶה הָרֶגֶל, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״מְשַׁלְּחֵי רֶגֶל הַשּׁוֹר וְהַחֲמוֹר״. ״וּבִעֵר״ – זוֹ הַשֵּׁן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Where are Eating and Trampling written** in the Torah that led them to be classified as primary categories? The Gemara answers: The source is **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "If a man causes a field or vineyard to be eaten, and he sends forth his animal, and it consumed in the field of another" (Exodus 22:4). The two parts of the verse are referring to different categories: **"And he sends forth," this is** a reference to **the** primary category of **Trampling,** as sending forth results in the animal trampling another's produce and damaging it, **and likewise it states:** "Happy are you that sow beside all waters **that send forth the feet of the ox and the donkey"** (Isaiah 32:20). Clearly the term "send forth" is a reference to trampling by the feet of the animal. **"And it consumed," this is** a reference to **the** primary category of **Eating.**

*Regel* is similarly defined not by the use of legs but by a distinct character: *hezeko matzui* — damage that occurs in the normal course of the animal's natural movement. When an animal walks and inadvertently knocks something over, that is *regel*. It is neither seeking pleasure nor intending harm; damage simply occurs as a consequence of its ordinary locomotion. *Shen* and *regel* are therefore frequently grouped together: both are categories in which the animal is not *mekavanin lehazik*, not intending damage. The difference is that *shen* involves pursuit of pleasure while *regel* involves normal movement. An act of pushing, if deliberate, is *keren* regardless of which limb performs it; accidental bumping in the course of walking is *regel* regardless of which part of the body makes contact.

### Toldot of Keren

The *toldot* of *keren* enumerated by the *beraisa* are: *negifah* (pushing), *neshichah* (biting), *revitzah* (crouching), and *be'itah* (kicking). What unites them and places them in the category of *keren* is that in each case the animal is acting with *kavanato lehazik* — its purpose is to cause harm. The fact that it uses a different body part or a different mode of attack does not remove the act from the *keren* category, because the category is defined by intention, not by anatomy.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְשִׁיכָה – תּוֹלָדָה דְשֵׁן הִיא! לָא; שֵׁן – יֵשׁ הֲנָאָה לְהֶזֵּיקָהּ, הָא – אֵין הֲנָאָה לְהֶזֵּיקָהּ. רְבִיצָה וּבְעִיטָה – תּוֹלָדָה דְרֶגֶל הִיא! לָא; רֶגֶל – הֶזֵּיקָהּ מָצוּי, הָנֵי – אֵין הֶזֵּיקָן מָצוּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the classification in the *baraita* of biting, crouching, and kicking as subcategories of Goring: Isn't **biting a subcategory of Eating,** as the animal both eats and bites with its teeth? The Gemara answers: **No,** in cases included in the primary category of **Eating, there is pleasure** for the animal **in** the course of **its** causing **damage.** In **this** case of damage caused by biting, **there is no** intrinsic **pleasure** for the animal **in** the course of the **damage** that **it** causes, as when the ox bites forcefully, the exclusive objective of the action is to cause damage. The Gemara asks: Aren't **crouching** upon items **and kicking** items in order to damage them each **a subcategory of Trampling,** as the animal crouches by bending its legs and kicks with its feet? The Gemara answers: **No,** in cases included in the primary category of Trampling, the **damage is commonplace,** as it is caused in the course of the animal's walking; in **these** cases of crouching and kicking, the **damage is not commonplace,** as animals do not typically kick or crouch upon utensils.

*Revitzah* and *be'itah* are similarly not classified as *toldot* of *regel*, even though they involve the animal's body positioning or leg movement. The *gemara* explains: *regel* is *hezeko matzui* — damage that arises incidentally in the course of normal movement. Crouching or kicking to cause harm are not acts of normal movement; they are acts directed toward causing damage. Hence they belong to *keren*.

### Rav Pappa Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא ״תּוֹלְדוֹתֵיהֶן לָאו כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן״ דְּאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, אַהֵיָיא?
>
> **Translation:** After citing the subcategories listed in the *baraita*, the Gemara resumes its analysis of the statement of Rav Pappa: **But** with regard to the statement **that Rav Pappa said: There are among them** some whose subcategories **are dissimilar to them, to which** primary category was Rav Pappa referring?

The *gemara* now returns to Rav Pappa's original question. Having established the definitions of *keren*, *shen*, and *regel*, and having identified the *toldot* of *keren*, the *gemara* asks: when Rav Pappa said that some *toldot* are *keyotzei vahen* and some are *lav keyotzei vahen*, to which *toldot* was he referring?

The answer is that the *toldot* of *keren* are indeed *keyotzei vahen* — they follow the same rules as *keren* itself. The reasoning is straightforward: just as *keren* is characterized by *kavanato lehazik* and the owner bears *shmiratan alecha*, the same is true of every *toldah* of *keren*. They share the same defining characteristic — intentional harm — and the same relationship of owner responsibility. There is no basis to treat them differently from the *av*.

When Rav Pappa says that some *toldot* are *lav keyotzei vahen*, he is referring to the *toldot* of *shen* and *regel*. Those will be examined in the continuation of the *sugya* — but the groundwork has now been laid. We have the three conceptual definitions: *kavanato lehazik* for *keren*, *hana'ah leheziko* for *shen*, and *hezeko matzui* for *regel*. With these definitions in hand, the *gemara* will now proceed to ask, for each category, what its *toldot* are and whether they follow the same *halachos* as the *av* — the very question with which this opening *sugya* of *Bava kamma* began.
