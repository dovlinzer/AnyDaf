# Meilah 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Gizbar & Meila Intro

### Opening Context

We begin at the top of the *mishnah* on daf 21a — still engaged with the central theme of *meilah*. Before turning to the new material, two points from the previous session warrant clarification, as both bear directly on what follows.

### Ravad on Taking

The *Ravad* makes clear that the principle requiring one to give an object to another person before the act is considered stealing is not limited to the *gizbar* alone. It applies to anyone who has a legitimate right to handle the item. So long as such a person merely moves an object from one place to another — without transferring it out of their control to a third party — that act does not constitute theft. Of course, this is distinct from the case where one walks into a temple storehouse and removes an item to one's home; there, no transfer to a third party is required for the taking itself to constitute *meilah*. The *Ravad*'s expansion beyond the *gizbar* is significant, because several of the cases we are about to read do not appear to involve a *gizbar* at all, yet operate on the same underlying principle: mere taking is insufficient; it is the transfer to another that constitutes the act of misappropriation.

### Pikadon & Shlichus Yad

A related principle: when one holds an object belonging to another as a *pikadon* — keeping it in one's home for safekeeping — the concept of *shlichus yad* applies. *Shlichus yad* refers to moving an item with intent to appropriate it for oneself. In the ordinary law of guardians, the very act of moving it with such intent constitutes a tortious taking. Here, however, in the domain of *meilah*, this is insufficient. Where one has a legitimate right to have the item in one's possession, the act of *meilah* requires actually giving the item out to someone else. The *gizbar* who deposits *hekdesh* funds in one's home for safekeeping — once the guardian engages in *shlichus yad* — has still not committed *meilah* by mere movement; the item must leave that person's control entirely.

### Rambam on Kedusha Loss

The second preliminary point concerns a *Tosefta* that the *Rambam* rules in accordance with. We discussed several days ago the conceptually striking idea that after an act of *meilah*, *hekdesh* property loses its *kedushah* — though this applies only to *kedushas damim* and *kedushas bedek habayis*, never to *kedushas haguf*. The *Rambam*, basing himself on the *Tosefta*, draws a further distinction: even for *kedushas bedek habayis* and *kedushas damim*, the loss of *kedushah* following *meilah* applies only to the *meilah* of stealing — that is, taking the object and giving it to another — not to the *meilah* of mere benefit. If one merely warmed oneself with wool belonging to *hekdesh*, that constitutes *meilah* in the sense of incurring liability, but it does not cause the item to lose its *kedushah*. The *kedushah* departs only when the item is stolen outright — taken away and given to someone else.

This makes enormous conceptual sense. Since the *kedushah* of *bedek habayis* is fundamentally about ownership, the most profound act that could remove an item from *hekdesh*'s domain is the act of transferring it to another. Mere enjoyment, though forbidden, lacks the power to sever that ownership. The *Ravad* disagrees and holds that this reading, though derived from the *Tosefta*, does not represent the operative *halachah*. But conceptually, the *Rambam*'s position is compelling and worth holding in mind.

### Shinui & Pagam

One might ask: what about *shinui* — a physical transformation of the *hekdesh* object? Building a beam into one's home, for example, may itself constitute an act of stealing by virtue of the transformation it effects. Whether such a *shinui* requires a full *perutah* of *pagam* alongside a full *perutah* of *hanaah*, or whether some other accounting applies, remains somewhat unsettled — the relevant *mishnah* seems to point in one direction, while other sources do not fully corroborate it — and the question may be considered still open.

## Agent & Instruction

### Different Window Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אָמַר לוֹ: ״הָבֵא לִי מִן הַחַלּוֹן אוֹ מִן הַדְּלוֹסְקָמָא״, וְהֵבִיא לוֹ. אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמַר בַּעַל הַבַּיִת: ״לֹא הָיָה בְּלִבִּי אֶלָּא מִזֶּה״, וְהֵבִיא מִזֶּה – בַּעַל הַבַּיִת מָעַל. אֲבָל אִם אָמַר לוֹ: ״הָבֵא לִי מִן הַחַלּוֹן״, וְהֵבִיא לוֹ מִן הַדְּלוֹסְקָמָא, אוֹ ״מִן הַדְּלוֹסְקָמָא״, וְהֵבִיא לוֹ מִן הַחַלּוֹן – הַשָּׁלִיחַ מָעַל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If the homeowner **said to** the agent: **Bring me** this item or this money **from the window** in the wall **or from the chest [*hadeluskema*], and** the agent obeyed and **brought** it **to him** from the place that he instructed him, **even though the homeowner said: In my heart,** my desire was **only** that he should bring me the item **from that** other place, **and** as **he brought** it **from** this place he did not fulfill my instructions, nevertheless **the homeowner is liable for misuse** if the item or money is consecrated, as the agent did in fact fulfill his instructions. **But** if the homeowner **said to** the agent: **Bring me** this item or this money **from the window** in the wall, **and** the agent **brought** it **to him from the chest; or** if the homeowner said to the agent: Bring me this item or this money **from the chest, and** the agent **brought** it **to him from the window, the agent is liable for misuse.**

The *mishnah* with which we begin belongs to the *perek* entitled *Hashaliach She'asah*, the chapter dealing with the agent who performs his *shlichus*. The conceptual backbone of this chapter is the particular type of *meilah* that involves sending an agent to take *hekdesh* property and give it to another. This is not the *meilah* of benefit — where I enjoy something belonging to *hekdesh* — but the *meilah* of misappropriation, of causing *hekdesh* property to be removed from its sanctified domain. Since the agent acts on my behalf, his act is attributed to me; it is as though I am the one who has taken the property away from *hekdesh*.

The *mishnah* opens: *amar lo, havei li min hachalon o min hadluskema* — he said to his agent, bring me from the window or from the box (*dluskema*). The agent does exactly as instructed. Now suppose the sender later claims, "I didn't mean that window, I meant the other one; I didn't mean that box, I meant a different one." Even so — *ba'al habayis maal* — it is the owner's *meilah*, because the operative principle is *devarim shebelev einam devarim*: what one was privately thinking is legally irrelevant. What matters is what was said. Since the agent did what was said, he was acting on the owner's behalf, and the *meilah* is attributed to the owner.

### Where is Meila?

One might ask: the agent simply brought the item to the owner — he moved it from one place to another. Where precisely is the act of *meilah*? The most straightforward answer is that the owner here is not the *gizbar*; he has no legitimate right to handle *hekdesh* property. For someone without such a right, the very act of taking — not merely moving within one's own domain — can itself constitute *meilah*. The principle that "mere moving is not *meilah*" applies specifically where one has a right to handle the item. Where no such right exists, the taking is itself the act of *meilah*.

That said, there is a general reluctance in the *Gemara* to treat simple physical movement as the constitutive act of *meilah*, even where no such right exists. The scenario of the *mishnah* does seem to imply that the person has some right to be handling the property — which would then push the *meilah* to the moment it is given out to a third party. *Tosafos* concludes that ultimately the *meilah* occurs when, after the agent takes the item and brings it to the owner, the owner passes it on to someone else.

### Deviation = Agent Moel

The *mishnah* then presents the contrasting case: *aval im amar lo, havei li min hachalon veheivi lo min hadluskema* — if the owner said "bring from the window" and the agent brought from the box, or vice versa — *hashaliach maal*, it is the *shaliach* who has committed *meilah*. Even if the switch is minor and the items are functionally equivalent, any deviation from the precise instruction breaks the chain of *shlichus*. The agent was acting on his own, not on the owner's behalf. And here again the question of where the *meilah* occurs — the taking, or the giving — remains somewhat unresolved, with the clearest answer being that where the owner has no right to handle the property, the act of taking is itself *meilah*.

## Incompetent Agents

### Incompetent as Shaliach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁלַּח בְּיַד חֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן, אִם עָשׂוּ שְׁלִיחוּתוֹ – בַּעַל הַבַּיִת מָעַל, לֹא עָשׂוּ שְׁלִיחוּתוֹ – חֶנְווֹנִי מֵעַל.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where the homeowner **sent** consecrated money **in the hand of a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor,** who lack halakhic competence and cannot be commissioned as agents, in order to purchase an item from a storekeeper, **if they performed his agency, the homeowner is liable for misuse,** as his instructions were fulfilled. If **they did not perform his agency** but purchased a different item from the storekeeper, **the storekeeper is liable for misuse.**

*Shelach beyad cheresh shoteh vekatan* — the *mishnah* now addresses the case where the owner sends a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor to carry out the mission. These three categories of persons — *cheresh shoteh vekatan* — are ordinarily not considered *bar da'as*, not possessed of legal competence. They cannot serve as agents for *terumah*, for a *get*, for *kiddushin* — there is no *shlichus* through them in those domains. Yet the *mishnah* rules: *im asu shlichuso* — if they did what they were sent to do — *ba'al habayis maal*. It is the owner's *meilah*. Remarkably, even these legally incompetent persons can function as effective conduits of the owner's act of *meilah*.

### Non-Compliance = Storekeeper…

If, however, they did *not* follow instructions — *lo asu shlichuso* — then *chanvani maal*: the storekeeper is the one who has committed *meilah*. To understand this: the owner sent them with *hekdesh* money, say, to buy provisions at a shop. If they carried out the errand as instructed, the act of handing the money to the shopkeeper removes it from the owner's control — and since that was done on the owner's behalf, it is his *meilah*. If they did *not* follow instructions, then the owner's *shlichus* was not executed. Ordinarily, a competent adult agent who deviates would himself be *mo'el*. But because a *cheresh shoteh vekatan* cannot bear legal responsibility for a purposive act, neither the owner nor the incompetent agent can be identified as the *mo'el*. The *meilah* therefore falls to the *chanvani* — the storekeeper — at the moment he actually spends the money.

This raises an obvious concern: the storekeeper is entirely innocent. He has no way of knowing that the coin handed to him by an incompetent agent was *hekdesh* money being misused. One might argue this is a case of *ones* — duress or blamelessness. Yet *meilah* is an offense committed *beshogeig* — inadvertently — and the question is whether total blamelessness through *ones* differs from mere inadvertence. As we will see shortly, it may not. The *Mefaresh* will address this directly.

## Owner Remembers Mid-Mission

### Storekeeper's Spending

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁלַּח בְּיַד פִּקֵּחַ, וְנִזְכַּר עַד שֶׁלֹּא הִגִּיעַ אֵצֶל חֶנְווֹנִי – חֶנְווֹנִי מָעַל לִכְשֶׁיּוֹצִיא.
>
> **Translation:** If the homeowner **sent** the money **in the hand of** a halakhically **competent** person **and** the homeowner **remembered** that the money was consecrated **before** the agent **reached the storekeeper, the storekeeper is liable for misuse when he spends** the money for his personal use. The homeowner is exempt from liability for misuse, because once he remembers that the money is consecrated his misuse is no longer unwitting, and one is liable to bring an offering for misuse only for unwitting misuse of consecrated property.

Having established the *chanvani*'s liability in the case of incompetent agents, the *mishnah* moves to a parallel scenario involving a competent agent. *Shalach beyad pikeach* — the owner sent a fully competent adult. *Venizkar ad shelo higia eitzel chanvani* — and before the agent reached the shopkeeper, the owner remembered: this was *hekdesh* money.

Why does the owner's remembering make any difference? Because *meilah* is by definition an inadvertent act. Once the owner is aware that *hekdesh* funds are being misused, the *meilah* can no longer be attributed to him — he is now acting with knowledge, and the relevant category of culpable *meilah* (incurring the obligation to bring a *korban* and pay a *chomesh*, as well as the property leaving *hekdesh*'s domain) requires *shogeig*. So the owner is removed from the picture.

Can the liability fall to the agent? The agent sent by a competent owner who follows instructions is ordinarily treated as an extension of the owner: if the owner is not *mo'el*, neither is the agent. The *mishnah* therefore rules: *chanvani maal lechsheeyotzi* — the *chanvani* becomes *mo'el* when he actually spends the money. The moment of *meilah* is not when the *chanvani* receives the money — even though he is a third party receiving *hekdesh* funds — but only when he subsequently expends it. This further underscores the principle, consistent throughout this *perek*, that receipt alone is not *meilah*; the act of removing from one's control — here, the *chanvani* spending the money — is what triggers it.

### Redemption Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד יַעֲשֶׂה? נוֹטֵל פְּרוּטָה אוֹ כְלִי, וְאוֹמֵר: ״פְּרוּטָה שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ בְּכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁהוּא מְחוּלָּל עַל זֶה״, שֶׁהַהֶקְדֵּשׁ נִפְדֶּה בְּכֶסֶף וּבְשָׁוֶה כֶּסֶף.
>
> **Translation:** **What shall** the homeowner **do** in a case where he remembers that the money is consecrated, in order to prevent the storekeeper from liability for misuse? **He takes one *peruta* or a vessel and says:** The **consecrated *peruta*, wherever it may be, is desacralized with this** *peruta* or vessel. The *peruta* is thereby desacralized, **as a consecrated item is desacralized with money and with** an item that has **the equivalent** value of **money.** The result is that the storekeeper spends non-sacred money.

If the owner wishes to prevent the *chanvani* from becoming *mo'el*, the *mishnah* instructs: *notel prutah o kli* — he takes a coin or vessel of his own — *ve'omer: prutah shel hekdesh bechol makom shehu mechulal al zeh* — and declares that wherever the *hekdesh* coin presently is, whether with the agent or already with the *chanvani*, its *kedushah* is hereby transferred onto this object in his hand. He then redeems the *kedushah* by submitting that object to *hekdesh*, since *hekdesh nifdeh bekesef u'veshaveh kesef* — *hekdesh* may be redeemed with money or with any item of monetary value. Crucially, even though the money is in the *chanvani*'s physical possession, it still belongs to *hekdesh* — and *hekdesh* property can be redeemed regardless of where it is located, since it is the owner who originally sanctified it and whose authority over the redemption is therefore not dependent on physical control.

## Olives & Monkey Analogy

### Olives Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: עֲשָׂאוּם כְּמַעֲטָן שֶׁל זֵיתִים, דִּתְנַן: הַזֵּיתִים, מֵאֵימָתַי מְקַבְּלִין טוּמְאָה? מִשֶּׁיַּזִּיעוּ זֵיעַת הַמַּעֲטָן, וְלֹא זֵיעַת הַקּוּפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says** in explanation: The Sages **rendered** these individuals halakhically **like a vat [*kema'atan*] of olives** with regard to ritual impurity. Since it is beneficial for the homeowner when the moisture of the olives, i.e., liquid that seeps from the olives, leaks into a vat, as explained below, it is considered as though the homeowner had intent for the moisture to be there. Similarly, with regard to these categories of people, since it suits the homeowner when they do what he requested it is considered as though they acted with his intent. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Teharot* 9:1): **When do the olives** become **susceptible to** ritual **impurity? From when they exude** moisture. This is referring specifically to the **moisture** that emerges into **the vat, but not the moisture** that emerges into **the basket.** The moisture that comes into the vat softens the olives and is beneficial to the olive oil production, as it renders them easier to press. For this reason it is considered as though the homeowner has intent for the moisture to be there, and that moisture therefore renders the olives it touches susceptible to ritual impurity. By contrast, moisture in the basket serves no purpose, as it trickles out through holes in the basket. Consequently, it is not there with the intent of the homeowner and does not render the olives susceptible to ritual impurity.

The *Gemara* now turns to the striking *chiddush* of the *mishnah* — that sending a *cheresh shoteh vekatan* who follows instructions constitutes the owner's *meilah*, despite the absence of any recognized legal agency. *Rabbi Elazar* addresses this: *asam kematan shel zeisim* — he made them like a vat of olives.

The reference is to the *halachah* of when olives become susceptible to *tumah*. Olives receive *tumah* only after liquid touches them and after the owner desires that liquid contact. The relevant stage is *mi'sheyaziyu* — when they sweat in the vat. In a basket during picking, the owner does not want the olive-liquid to escape; in the vat, however, the owner does want it, or at least welcomes it. The liquid that exudes in the vat renders the olives *mekabbel tumah* — and this happens according to the owner's will, without any direct act on his part. *Tosafos* notes that this is a strange analogy: in the olive case, no concept of *action* was ever in play; the *halachah* was never premised on a direct act of the owner, so the fact that it operates through will rather than deed proves very little. The analogy is therefore weak, and *Tosafos* does not find it persuasive.

### Eruv Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: כְּאוֹתָהּ שֶׁשָּׁנִינוּ, נְתָנוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי הַקּוֹף וְהוֹלִיכוֹ, אוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי הַפִּיל וְהוֹלִיכוֹ, וְאָמַר לְאַחֵר לְקַבְּלוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ – הֲרֵי זֶה עֵירוּב. אַלְמָא קָא עָבְדָא שְׁלִיחוּתֵיהּ. הָכִי נָמֵי, אִיתְעֲבִיד שְׁלִיחוּתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** This case of a deaf-mute, imbecile or minor, is **like that which we learned** in a *baraita* with regard to placing food for a joining of Shabbat boundaries, i.e., an *eiruv*: If **one placed** the food of the *eiruv* **on a monkey, and** the monkey **brought it** to the place where he wanted the *eiruv* deposited, **or** if he placed it **on** a trained **elephant, and** the elephant **brought it** to the proper location, **and he told another** person **to receive it from** the animal, **it is** a valid ***eiruv*. Evidently, the agency is performed** by the monkey or elephant, despite the fact that they are unfit to serve as agents. **So too,** in the case of the deaf-mute, imbecile, or minor, **the agency is performed** despite the fact that they cannot be involved in agency.

*Rabbi Yochanan* offers a better proof: *kahahu detanina* — as we learned in *Eruvin*. If one places an *eiruv* on a monkey and the monkey carries it to the designated location, or places it on an elephant and the elephant transports it — *harei zeh eiruv*, it is a valid *eiruv*. Here is a case where the person did not perform the act himself, nor through any technically recognized agent, yet the act counts as his. *Alma*, the *Gemara* infers — *ka'avid shelichuteih* — sometimes it suffices that something is accomplished through one's initiative, even without formal *shlichus*. *Hacha nami avid shelichuteih* — so too here, the owner's mission was accomplished through the *cheresh shoteh vekatan*.

### Tosafos Challenge

*Tosafos* raises a difficulty: there is another *Gemara* in *Eruvin* that appears to say the monkey-*eiruv* does not work. *Tosafos* first suggests the resolution may lie in the parenthetical clause of our *Gemara*: *ve'amar le'acher lekabbelo mimmenu* — someone else took it off the monkey and placed it down. If a second person received it and deposited it properly, perhaps that is why the *eiruv* is valid. But *Tosafos* rejects this resolution as well, because if the validity rested entirely on that second person's act, then the proof would collapse — the significance would be the second person's action, not the monkey's transport.

*Tosafos* concludes that the distinction between the valid and invalid monkey-*eiruv* cases must lie in the circumstances of the deposit at the endpoint: in one case it simply fell off haphazardly; in the other, the owner was observing and the item was placed down in a manner connected to the owner's initiative. What emerges is that not every causal connection suffices — the owner must be involved at some meaningful level — but full, technically recognized *shlichus* is not required either.

### Meila as Removal

This leads to the conceptual clarification that illuminates the entire *perek*. Normally, *ein shaliach lidvar aveirah* — the principle that agency does not operate for a sinful act, since it is the agent's sin and not the principal's. Why then does *meilah* work differently? Because the way *meilah* is conceptualized, the focus is not on the sinful act itself but on the *effect*: the removal of *hekdesh* property from its sanctified domain. If that removal was accomplished through the owner's initiative — even through incompetent persons acting on his behalf — then it is the owner who caused it to happen. The same logic applies to the earlier example: if I take oil belonging to *hekdesh* and anoint another person with it, I am the one who caused the *hanaah* to occur, even though someone else received the benefit. The causal role of the owner in directing the property out of *hekdesh* is the operative criterion, and it need not pass through formal legal *shlichus* to be his *meilah*.

## Owner Remembers Mid-Mission

### Owner's Remembrance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: נִזְכַּר בַּעַל הַבַּיִת וְלֹא נִזְכַּר שָׁלִיחַ – הַשָּׁלִיחַ מָעַל, נִזְכְּרוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם – חֶנְוָנִי מָעַל!
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita*: If **the homeowner remembered** that the money was consecrated, **but the agent did not remember, the agent is liable for misuse.** If **they both remembered** that the money is consecrated, the **storekeeper is liable for misuse.**

The *Gemara* now revisits the case of the owner who remembers mid-mission. We established that if the owner remembers — *nizkar ba'al habayis* — before the agent reaches the *chanvani*, the owner is no longer *mo'el* and the *chanvani* becomes *mo'el* upon spending. The *Gemara* now asks: is it sufficient that *only* the owner remembers, without the agent remembering, to remove the owner from liability?

### Only Owner Remembers

A *braisa* is introduced: *nizkar ba'al habayis velo nizkar shaliach* — if the owner remembered but the agent did not — *ma'al* — the *shaliach* becomes *mo'el*. The logic: once the owner is removed from the picture, the agent is no longer operating as a mere extension of the owner. Since he did not remember, he is still acting inadvertently. He becomes the *mo'el*, even though he had no idea the funds were *hekdesh* and was simply carrying out instructions he was given before the owner's moment of recollection.

### Both Remembered

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מַתְנִיתִין נָמֵי, כְּשֶׁנִּזְכְּרוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said** in resolution of this contradiction: **The mishna** is **also** referring to a case **where both** the agent and the agent **remembered** that the money was consecrated, and for this reason the storekeeper alone is liable for misuse.

This creates a tension with the *mishnah*, which states simply that the *chanvani* is *mo'el* — implying that neither the owner nor the agent is *mo'el*. *Rav Sheshet* resolves this: *matnitin keshenizkeru sheneihem* — the *mishnah* is dealing with a case where both the owner and the agent remembered. One might wonder: if the agent also remembered, why is he still going to the *chanvani*? Perhaps he is a servant who feels compelled to carry out his master's instruction regardless, or perhaps he reasons that the owner sanctified the money and it is not his place to countermand the errand. In any case, the *mishnah* assumes both parties are aware, and therefore neither the owner nor the agent bears the liability — it passes to the *chanvani* when he spends the money.

### Hair-Suspended Halacha

The *Mefaresh* here confronts the discomfort that underlies all of these scenarios directly. He quotes from *Masechet Chagigah* the famous formulation that some *halachot* are *kehararim hateluyin besa'arah* — like mountains suspended by a thread. This is one of them. *Deshaliach anya da mai ka'avid* — what did the poor agent do? He was simply following the owner's instructions, and now we are told he is *mo'el* because the owner happened to remember. Nobody here is truly at fault, yet someone must be *mo'el*. It is a real problem, notes the *Mefaresh* — everybody in the relevant scenario is effectively *ones* — blameless — except for the owner at the outset. Yet *ones* does not negate *meilah*. That is the *halachah*, striking as it is.

## Fractional & Partial

### Half-Coin Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נָתַן לוֹ פְּרוּטָה, אָמַר לוֹ: ״הָבֵא לִי בְּחֶצְיָהּ נֵרוֹת וּבְחֶצְיָהּ פְּתִילוֹת״ וְהָלַךְ וְהֵבִיא לוֹ בְּכוּלָּהּ פְּתִילוֹת אוֹ בְּכוּלָּהּ נֵרוֹת, אוֹ שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ: ״הָבֵא לִי בְּכוּלָּהּ נֵרוֹת״, אוֹ ״בְּכוּלָּהּ פְּתִילוֹת״, וְהָלַךְ וְהֵבִיא בְּחֶצְיָהּ נֵרוֹת וּבְחֶצְיָהּ פְּתִילוֹת – שְׁנֵיהֶם לֹא מָעֲלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If the homeowner **gave** the agent **one** consecrated ***peruta*** and **said to him: Bring me lamps [*nerot*] with one-half of it and wicks with one-half of it, and** the agent **went and brought him wicks with the entire** *peruta*, **or lamps with the entire** *peruta*; **or** in a case **where** the homeowner **said to** the agent: **Bring me lamps with the entire** *peruta* **or wicks with the entire** *peruta*, **and** the agent **went and brought** him **lamps with one-half of it and wicks with one-half of it, both of them are not liable** for misuse of the *peruta*. In both cases, the homeowner is exempt because his instructions were fulfilled only with regard to half of a *peruta*, and the agent is exempt as he spent only half of a *peruta* on his own initiative.

The *mishnah* now introduces a series of cases involving fractional amounts and partial compliance. *Natan lo prutah ve'amar lo: havei li bechetzya neirot uvechetzya psilot* — the owner gave the agent a *prutah* and instructed him to spend half on oil lamps and half on wicks. If the agent spent it all on wicks, or all on lamps; or alternatively, if the owner said spend it all on one thing and the agent split it half and half — *sheneihem lo ma'alu*: neither of them committed *meilah*. The reasoning: in each case, one half-*prutah* was spent in compliance with the owner's instruction, making it the owner's act — but a half-*prutah* is below the threshold for *meilah*. The other half-*prutah* was spent contrary to instructions, making it the agent's act — but again, only a half-*prutah*, insufficient for *meilah*. Neither amount reaches the minimum required, and so neither party is *mo'el*.

### Wrong Store = Deviation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אִם אָמַר לוֹ: ״הָבֵא לִי בְּחֶצְיָהּ נֵרוֹת מִמָּקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי וּבְחֶצְיָהּ פְּתִילוֹת מִמָּקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״, וְהָלַךְ וְהֵבִיא לוֹ נֵרוֹת מִמְּקוֹם פְּתִילוֹת, וּפְתִילוֹת מִמְּקוֹם נֵרוֹת – הַשָּׁלִיחַ מָעַל.
>
> **Translation:** **But if** the homeowner **said to** the agent: **Bring me lamps from such and such place with one-half of** the *peruta* **and wicks from such and such place with one-half of** the *peruta*, **and** the agent **went and brought him lamps from the place** that he designated for **wicks, and wicks from the place** that he designated for **lamps, the agent is liable for misuse,** as he deviated from the homeowner's instructions by the sum of an entire *peruta*.

However, if the owner specified not only the items but the precise stores — bring lamps from this place and wicks from that place — and the agent reversed the sources, then *hashaliach maal* for both amounts. Any deviation from instructions, however slight, dissolves the *shlichus*. Since the agent acted entirely on his own for both purchases, both half-*prutos* are his, and together they constitute a full *prutah* — sufficient for *meilah*.

### Two Coins, Two Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתַן לוֹ שְׁתֵּי פְרוּטוֹת, וְאָמַר לוֹ: ״לֵךְ וְהָבֵא לִי אֶתְרוֹג״, וְהָלַךְ וְהֵבִיא לוֹ בְּאַחַת אֶתְרוֹג וּבְאַחַת רִימּוֹן – שְׁנֵיהֶם מָעֲלוּ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בַּעַל הַבַּיִת לֹא מָעַל, שֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר לוֹ: אֶתְרוֹג גָּדוֹל הָיִיתִי מְבַקֵּשׁ, וְהֵבֵאתָ לִי קָטָן וְרַע.
>
> **Translation:** If the homeowner **gave** the agent **two** consecrated ***perutot*, and said to him: Go and bring me an *etrog*, and he went and brought him an *etrog* with one** *peruta* **and a pomegranate with one** *peruta*, **both of them are liable for misuse.** The homeowner is liable because his agency was performed with the sum of one *peruta*, and the agent is liable because he deviated from the homeowner's instructions with one *peruta*. **Rabbi Yehuda says: The homeowner is not liable for misuse, as he** can **say to** the agent: **I was seeking a large *etrog*** worth two *perutot*, **and you brought me a small, inferior** *etrog* worth one *peruta*.

*Nasan lo shtei prutos ve'amar lo: leich veheivi li esrog* — the owner gave the agent two *prutos* and sent him to purchase an *esrog*. The agent returned with an *esrog* purchased for one *prutah* and a pomegranate for the other. *Sheneihem ma'alu* — both are *mo'el*. The owner is *mo'el* for the *esrog* purchase, since buying an *esrog* with one *prutah* is treated as at least partial compliance with the instruction — thus it is the owner's act for that *prutah*. The agent is *mo'el* for the pomegranate purchase, which was entirely his own deviation — thus his act for that *prutah*. Each has committed *meilah* with a full *prutah*.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Dissent

*Rabbi Yehuda* disagrees: *ba'al habayis lo ma'al* — the owner did not commit *meilah* even for the *esrog*. His reasoning: *esrog gadol hayisi mevakesh veheiveisa li katan vera* — "I asked for a large, fine *esrog*, and you brought me a small, inferior one." An *esrog* costing one *prutah* is not a cheaper version of what was requested — it is an entirely different object. It is not partial compliance; it is complete non-compliance. Since the agent did not perform the owner's *shlichus* at all, even for that *prutah*, the owner bears no liability.

### Dinar & Shirt Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתַן לוֹ דִּינַר זָהָב, וְאָמַר לוֹ: לֵךְ וְהָבֵא לִי חָלוּק, וְהָלַךְ וְהֵבִיא לוֹ בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה חָלוּק וּבִשְׁלֹשָׁה טַלִּית – שְׁנֵיהֶם מָעֲלוּ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בַּעַל הַבַּיִת לֹא מָעַל, שֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר לוֹ: חָלוּק גָּדוֹל הָיִיתִי מְבַקֵּשׁ, וְהֵבֵאתָ לִי קָטָן וְרַע.
>
> **Translation:** If the homeowner **gave** the agent a consecrated **gold dinar,** which is worth twenty-five silver dinars, as four silver dinars constitute a *sela*; **and said to** the agent: **Go and bring me a robe, and** the agent **went and brought him a robe with three** *sela* **and a cloak with three** *sela*, **both of them are liable for misuse.** The homeowner is liable because his agency was performed with the purchase of the robe for three *sela*, and the agent is liable because he deviated from the homeowner's instructions by purchasing the cloak. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The **homeowner is not liable for misuse, as he** can **say to** the agent: **I was seeking a large robe** worth a gold dinar **and you brought me a small, inferior** robe worth three *sela*, i.e., twelve silver dinars.

A parallel case: *nasan lo dinar zahav ve'amar lo: leich vehavei li chaluk* — the owner gave the agent a gold *dinar* and instructed him to buy a garment. The agent returned with a shirt purchased for three *sela'im* and spent the other three *sela'im* on an outer cloak (*tallis*, meaning an outer garment, not a fringed prayer shawl). *Sheneihem ma'alu* — according to the *Tanna Kamma*, both are *mo'el*: the owner for the shirt (partial compliance), the agent for the cloak (his own deviation). *Rabbi Yehuda* again disagrees: *chaluk gadol hayisi mevakesh veheiveisa li katan vera* — "I wanted a fine garment; you brought me an inferior, cheap one." What was purchased is not half of what was asked for; it is something categorically different, and therefore the owner's *shlichus* was never executed.

## Half-Compliance Analysis

### Half-Acre Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ, מַאן דְּאָמַר לִשְׁלוּחוֹ: ״זִיל זְבֵן לִי כּוֹרָא דְאַרְעָא״, וַאֲזַל וּזְבַן לֵיהּ לִיתְכָּא – קָנֵי לוֹקֵחַ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara notes: One can **learn from** the mishna the resolution to an unresolved dilemma in tractate *Ketubot* (98b), that in a case of **one who said to his agent: Go** and **sell** on **my** behalf **a *kor* of land, and he went and sold for him a half**-***kor*, the purchaser acquires** the half-*kor* of land that he purchased. Although the agent did not fulfill his agency entirely, the part that he did perform is valid.

The *Gemara* draws an inference from the *Tanna Kamma*'s position. If an owner instructs his agent to purchase a full acre of land and the agent buys only a half-acre, does the agent retain his status as *shaliach* for the portion he did acquire? The *Tanna Kamma*'s ruling in the *mishnah* — that buying an *esrog* for one *prutah* when sent to spend two still counts as the owner's *shlichus* for that *prutah* — implies *kanah lokeiach*: the purchaser acquires on the owner's behalf even when only half the mandate is fulfilled.

### Sale Price Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הָכָא הֵיכִי דָּמֵי – כְּגוֹן דְּאַיְיתִי לֵיהּ שָׁוֶה שֵׁשׁ בְּשָׁלֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Some Sages **say** that one cannot infer this resolution from the mishna. **What are the circumstances** of the mishna **here?** It is referring a case **where** the agent **brought him** a robe **worth six** *sela*, i.e., the value of the gold *dinar* that the homeowner gave him, which he acquired **for three** *sela*. If so, the homeowner received exactly what he wanted and the agent did not deviate from the agency, except that he also purchased a cloak without being instructed to do so. This is dissimilar to the case of an agent who sells a half-*kor* of land.

The *Gemara* immediately qualifies this inference. Perhaps the *mishnah*'s case is not one of doing half the job, but of doing the entire job at half the price: *de'aisei lei shaveh sheish bishalosh* — the agent acquired an item worth six *sela'im* for three, because it happened to be on sale. In that reading, the agent did accomplish exactly what was asked — he obtained the full quality of item requested — but used only half the allocated funds to do so. This would explain why the *Tanna Kamma* considers the owner's *shlichus* fulfilled: the mandate was fully executed, just at a bargain. No inference can be drawn from this to the case where the agent simply did half the work.

### Yehuda Counterargument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא סֵיפָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בַּעַל הַבַּיִת לֹא מָעַל, שֶׁהוּא יָכוֹל לוֹמַר לוֹ: ״חָלוּק גָּדוֹל הָיִיתִי מְבַקֵּשׁ, וְהֵבֵאתָ לִי חָלוּק קָטָן וְרַע״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: If that is the case of the mishna, **say the latter clause: Rabbi Yehuda says:** The **homeowner is not liable for misuse, as he** can **say to** the agent: **I was seeking a large robe** worth a gold dinar, **and you brought me a small, inferior robe** worth three *sela*. It is clear from the mishna that the homeowner did not receive exactly what he wanted, but rather an inferior robe.

If that is the correct reading of the *mishnah*, why does *Rabbi Yehuda* disagree even in the *esrog* case? The *Gemara* answers: *Rabbi Yehuda* reasons that the *shlichus* was not only to obtain the item but to spend the full amount given. If the item was on sale, the owner can rightly protest: *ei havas dinar kula, hayisi shavei shenei dinar* — had you spent the full *dinar*, you could have brought me something worth twice as much. Since the agent withheld part of the funds, he failed a component of the *shlichus* — namely, the obligation to deploy the full allocation in the owner's interest. Therefore, even in the sale-price scenario, *Rabbi Yehuda* does not consider the *shlichus* fully performed, and the owner is not *mo'el*.

### Kitnis Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּקִטְנִית שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם מָעֲלוּ, שֶׁהַקִּטְנִית בִּפְרוּטָה וְקִטְנִית בְּדִינָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **This too stands to reason,** that this is the correct interpretation of the mishna, **as it teaches** in **the latter clause,** i.e., it is taught in the *Tosefta* (2:4): **Rabbi Yehuda concedes in** a case where the agent purchased only part of the **legumes** which the one who appointed him requested, **that both are liable for misuse of consecrated property.** The reason for this ruling is **that** a small amount of **legumes** is always sold **for one *peruta* and** a larger amount of **legumes** is invariably sold **for a dinar.** One can infer from Rabbi Yehuda's statement that in the case of another item, e.g., a robe, purchasing a superior-quality robe for a higher price would bring additional benefit to the homeowner.

A *braisa* is cited: *modeh Rabbi Yehuda bekitnis she-sheneihem ma'alu* — *Rabbi Yehuda* concedes that when it comes to *kitnis* — legumes and similar divisible commodities — if the agent bought only half the quantity, both the owner and agent are *mo'el*. *She-hakitnis beprutah vekitnis bedin* — *kitnis* sells for a *prutah* and for a *dinar*; the pricing is simply proportional to quantity.

### Divisible vs. Indivisible

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי בְּאַתְרָא דְּזָבְנִי בְּשׁוּמָא – גַּבֵּי קִטְנִית נָמֵי, דְּיָהֵב סֶלַע מוֹזְלִי לֵיהּ טְפֵי! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בְּדוּכְתָּא דִּמְזַבְּנִי בְּכַנֵּי כַּנֵּי, כַּנָּא כַּנָּא בִּפְרוּטָה, דְּהָתָם פְּסִיק מִילְּתַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*: **What are the circumstances? If** it is referring **to a town where they sell** legumes **by appraisal** of an item's value, then **with regard to** these **legumes as well,** purchasing a larger quantity should reduce the price, which would mean **that** if one **gave a *sela*** the price of the legumes would **become cheaper. Rav Pappa said:** The *Tosefta* is referring **to a town where they sell** and set the price **by containers,** and each **container** is the same price: **Each and every container** is sold **for one *peruta*.** The significance of this fact is **that there the matter is fixed,** i.e., each container of legumes is sold for the same price, regardless of the quantity of legumes purchased; there is no reduction for buying in bulk.

Why is *kitnis* different from shirts and *esrogim*? The distinction is conceptually clear. A shirt purchased for half the allocated amount is not a cheaper version of the shirt requested — it is an objectively inferior garment, a categorically different object. An *esrog* bought for one *prutah* when two were given is not half an *esrog* — it is a small, inferior fruit that cannot serve the same purpose. A half-acre of land is not a diminished version of a full acre — it cannot serve the same agricultural or legal functions. But half a bushel of *kitnis* is simply half of what was requested, no more and no less. You brought one pound when asked for two — that is genuinely half the mission accomplished, and the owner has no legitimate grievance that you "brought the wrong thing."

The same distinction separates *kitnis* from land in the reverse direction: by land, half an acre is not half a whole acre in any functional sense; by *kitnis*, half the amount is precisely half the amount.

### Bargain Argument Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* *אִי בְּאַתְרָא דְּזָבְנִי בְּשׁוּמָא – גַּבֵּי קִטְנִית נָמֵי, דְּיָהֵב סֶלַע מוֹזְלִי לֵיהּ טְפֵי! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בְּדוּכְתָּא דִּמְזַבְּנִי בְּכַנֵּי כַּנֵּי, כַּנָּא כַּנָּא בִּפְרוּטָה, דְּהָתָם פְּסִיק מִילְּתַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*: **What are the circumstances? If** it is referring **to a town where they sell** legumes **by appraisal** of an item's value, then **with regard to** these **legumes as well,** purchasing a larger quantity should reduce the price, which would mean **that** if one **gave a *sela*** the price of the legumes would **become cheaper. Rav Pappa said:** The *Tosefta* is referring **to a town where they sell** and set the price **by containers,** and each **container** is the same price: **Each and every container** is sold **for one *peruta*.** The significance of this fact is **that there the matter is fixed,** i.e., each container of legumes is sold for the same price, regardless of the quantity of legumes purchased; there is no reduction for buying in bulk.

*Rav Papa* refines the analysis further. If *Rabbi Yehuda*'s entire argument rests on the claim that the agent could have obtained a better bargain by spending the full amount — *ei yahavta sela mozli tefei* — then that argument should apply to *kitnis* as well: had you spent the full amount, you might have received a better price per unit. Why then does *Rabbi Yehuda* concede by *kitnis*?

*Rav Papa* answers: *beduchta de-mizvni bekanei* — in places where *kitnis* is sold by exact measure, at a fixed price per unit. *Kanei kanei beprutah* — each fixed measure has a fixed price. *Psika milsaiya* — the amount is precise and invariable; there are no bargains to be had. Since the entire premise of *Rabbi Yehuda*'s argument — that the agent cost the owner a better deal by not spending the full allocation — cannot arise in a market where prices are exact and uniform, *Rabbi Yehuda* concedes. The *Gemara* thus concludes that the debate between *Rabbi Yehuda* and the *Tanna Kamma* is exclusively about the sale-price scenario, and both would agree that straightforward partial compliance — spending half and getting half the quantity — fulfills the *shlichus* for the portion actually performed. The difference with *kitnis* is simply that bargain pricing structurally cannot occur there, so *Rabbi Yehuda*'s objection never applies.

## Money Changer Case

### Unbound Permission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּפְקִיד מָעוֹת אֵצֶל שׁוּלְחָנִי, אִם צְרוּרִין – לֹא יִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן. לְפִיכָךְ, אִם הוֹצִיא – מָעַל. אִם מוּתָּרִין – יִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן. לְפִיכָךְ, אִם הוֹצִיא – לֹא מָעַל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who deposits** consecrated **money with a money changer, if** the money **is bound,** the money changer **may not use it. Therefore, if** the money changer **spent** the money, **he is liable for** its **misuse.** If the money **was unbound he may use it,** and **therefore if** the money changer **spent** the money, **he is not liable for** its **misuse.**

*Hamaphkid ma'os etzel shulchani* — if one deposits coins with a *shulchani*, a money changer. All of the preceding cases have reinforced a consistent principle: even without the formal status of *gizbar*, there is no *meilah* through the mere act of taking; the money must actually be transferred out of someone's control to a third party. The *shulchani* case extends this principle further.

When money is deposited with a money changer in an unbound state — coins loose, not knotted in a bundle — there is an implicit social understanding that the money changer may use those coins in the course of his business, reimbursing them as needed. *Yishtamesh bahen* — he fundamentally has an implicit license to use them. Therefore, *im hotzi lo ma'al* — if he spends the *hekdesh* money, the *shulchani* himself has not committed *meilah*. The spending is effectively done with permission; it is as though the owner directed it. It is therefore the owner — or whoever ultimately receives the benefit — who bears the liability.

### Bound Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵצֶל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת – בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ לֹא יִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן. לְפִיכָךְ, אִם הוֹצִיא – מָעַל. הַחֶנְווֹנִי – כְּבַעַל הַבַּיִת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כְּשׁוּלְחָנִי.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, if one deposited money **with a homeowner, whether** it **is bound or whether** it **is unbound,** the one with whom it was deposited **may not use it,** and **therefore if he spent** the money, **he is liable for misuse.** In this regard, the halakhic status of **a storekeeper is like** that of **a homeowner;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says:** The halakhic status of a storekeeper is **like** that of **a money changer.**

If, however, the money was deposited *betzrur* — bound in a knotted pouch — *lo yishtamesh bahen*: the accepted convention is that a money changer given bound money for safekeeping may not use it. There is no implicit license. Accordingly, if he does spend it, *im hotzi ma'al* — he is *mo'el* the moment he expends it, acting entirely without permission, on his own authority.

The *mishnah* continues: if the money is deposited not with a money changer but with an ordinary homeowner, *bein kach uvein kach lo yishtamesh bahen* — in either case, bound or unbound, there is no presumption of any license to use the funds. A private individual given money to watch does not operate under any commercial convention allowing use. Therefore, if he spends it, *im hotzi ma'al* — he is *mo'el* the moment he does so.

The *Rambam*'s position is more nuanced. He holds that even in the unbound case with the *shulchani*, the owner is not *mo'el*, because the owner never instructed the *shulchani* to spend the money — the *shulchani* was acting on his own, even if with implicit permission. The implicit license makes the *shulchani*'s act different from a straightforward violation, but it does not transform it into the owner's act. Since the *shulchani* is acting on his own initiative and not as the owner's *shaliach*, the owner bears no liability. The person who ultimately receives the money from the *shulchani* will become *mo'el* when he spends it.

This creates a subtle but striking distinction: implicit permission apparently negates the *shulchani*'s *meilah* without replacing it with the owner's. The *shulchani* is not violating anything by his own lights; the owner did not direct him to do it; and so the *meilah* shifts downstream to whoever eventually uses the funds. Whether this fully satisfies the logic of *meilah* — given that the *shulchani* did in fact transfer *hekdesh* money to a third party — is a point of genuine tension. The contrast with the case where the *shaliach* is *mo'el* even when the owner remembers and even when the agent is wholly unwitting makes the *shulchani*'s exemption all the more striking.

### Storekeeper Status

The discussion concerning the *chanvani* — the storekeeper — is presented within the mishna itself above: the *Tanna Kamma* (Rabbi Meir) holds: *hachanvani keba'al habayis* — the storekeeper is like an ordinary homeowner; there is no implicit permission, and if he spends the money he is *mo'el* immediately upon doing so. *Rabbi Yehuda omer: keshulchani* — he is like a money changer; there is an implicit license in the commercial context of a storekeeper, and the analysis proceeds accordingly.

## Mixed Coins Debate

### First vs. Last Coin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נָפְלָה פְּרוּטָה שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ בְּתוֹךְ כִּיסוֹ, אוֹ שֶׁאָמַר ״פְּרוּטָה בְּכִיס זֶה הֶקְדֵּשׁ״, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהוֹצִיא אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה – מָעַל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: עַד שֶׁיּוֹצִיא אֶת כָּל הַכִּיס.
>
> **Translation:** If **a consecrated *peruta* fell into one's purse,** in which there were non-sacred *perutot*, **or** in a case **where one said: One *peruta* in this purse is consecrated, once he spent the first** *peruta* from the purse for non-sacred purposes, **he is liable for** its **misuse.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. And the Rabbis say:** He is not liable for misuse **until he spends** all the *perutot* **in the entire purse,** as only then is it certain that he spent the consecrated *peruta*.

*Nafal prutah shel hekdesh betoch kiso* — a coin of *hekdesh* accidentally fell into one's purse. Or: *amar prutah bekisi hekdesh* — one declared, "a coin in my purse is *hekdesh*," without specifying which one. *Rabbi Akiva* says: *kivan shehotzi et harishonah ma'al* — as soon as one withdraws the first coin, one has committed *meilah*. The *Chachamim* say: *ad sheyotzi et kol hakis* — only after spending every coin in the purse.

The *Chachamim*'s position is intuitive: until all the coins are spent, one cannot know which one was the *hekdesh* coin, so no single act of spending can be identified as *meilah*. *Rabbi Akiva*'s position, at face value, is puzzling: how can one be *mo'el* with the first coin when it is a genuine *safek* as to which coin is which?

### Asham Taluy Explanation

*Tosafos* quotes the *Tosefta* to clarify. The *Tosefta* explains that *Rabbi Akiva* does not mean straightforward *meilah* when one withdraws the first coin. Rather, one incurs the obligation to bring an *asham taluy* — the conditional guilt offering brought when there is genuine uncertainty as to whether a sin was committed. This recalls the debate in *Kerisot* about whether an *asham taluy* can be brought for *meilah*: *Rabbi Akiva* maintained it can; the *Chachamim* disagreed. Consistent with that position, *Rabbi Akiva* here holds that spending the first coin creates a *safek meilah* requiring an *asham taluy*, and that after spending all the coins, one would bring the definitive *asham vadai* for confirmed *meilah*.

### Bitel Berov Question

*Tosafos* raises a further question: why is the *hekdesh* coin not *batel berov* — nullified by the majority of ordinary coins in the purse? And *Tosafos* insists: do not invoke rabbinic principles such as *davar sheyesh lo matirin*, because this is a *de'oraisa* question of *meilah*. *Tosafos* offers two answers: perhaps the case involves only two coins, so there is no *rov* in the first place; alternatively, perhaps a coin qualifies as a *davar chashuv* — a significant object — and significant objects are not nullified even at the *de'oraisa* level.

### Status vs. Ownership

There is, however, a more fundamental reason why *bitul* should not apply here, and it follows from the very nature of *hekdesh*. *Bitul berov* operates with respect to *status* or *issur* — when an identity is unknown, the majority determines the legal character of the mixture. But *bitul* does not operate with respect to *ownership*. If someone accidentally drops a hundred-dollar bill into a box of my money, I cannot invoke *batel berov* and claim ownership of all the bills; the rightful owner still owns his money, even if neither of us can identify which precise bill it is. *Hekdesh* retains ownership of its coin even when it falls into a mixed purse. One would certainly set aside a coin and return it to *hekdesh* — the *kedushah* does not dissolve merely because the coin's identity is uncertain. The principle of *bitul* addresses *issur*, not ownership — and that distinction resolves the question at its root.

### Explicit 'From' Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לַחֲכָמִים בְּאוֹמֵר ״פְּרוּטָה מִן כִּיס זֶה הֶקְדֵּשׁ״, שֶׁהוּא מוֹצִיא וְהוֹלֵךְ עַד שֶׁיּוֹצִיא אֶת כָּל הַכִּיס.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Akiva concedes to the Rabbis in** a case **where one says: One *peruta* from** the coins in **this purse is consecrated, that he may continue spending** the *perutot* in the purse for non-sacred purposes and becomes liable for misuse only **once he spends** all the *perutot* **in the entire purse.** His formulation indicates that his desire was that the final remaining *peruta* in the purse would be consecrated, and therefore one is liable for misuse only when he spends that *peruta*.

*U-modeh Rabbi Akiva le-Chachamim be-omer: prutah min hakis zeh hekdesh* — *Rabbi Akiva* concedes to the *Chachamim* in a variant formulation: if one says not "a coin *in* this purse is *hekdesh*" (*bekis*) but "a coin *from* this purse is *hekdesh*" (*min kis*), then *shemotzi veholech ad sheyotzi et kol hakis* — one is spending until the very last coin is spent, since the language implies the residual, not the immediate. In the *bekhis* formulation, *Rabbi Akiva* reads the language as designating the first coin taken; in the *min kis* formulation — or more precisely, in the explicit stipulation of the *seifa* — he agrees that the last coin is meant.

### Breira Parallel

The *Gemara* brings a parallel from *terumot uma'asrot* concerning the principle of *breira* — retroactive designation. When purchasing wine from Samaritans where *teruma* and *ma'aser* have not been separated, one may declare in advance which portions will be set aside for various obligations and then drink immediately, relying on separation to be performed later. This principle of *breira* allows the final portions remaining to be retroactively designated. However, the Sages differ on whether *breira* operates: *Rabbi Meir* permits it, while *Rabbi Yehuda*, *Rabbi Yosi*, and *Rabbi Shimon* prohibit it. 

The question *Rav Papa* presses is: even according to *Rabbi Meir*, who permits this, it appears that the retroactive designation of the final quantity requires explicit stipulation to function. If so, why does the *seifa* of our *mishnah* — where *Rabbi Akiva* agrees that the last coin is the *hekdesh* one — not require equally explicit language?

### Explicit Stipulation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: סֵיפָא דְּמַתְנִיתִין בְּאוֹמֵר ״לֹא יִפָּטֵר כִּיס זֶה מִן הַהֶקְדֵּשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** Reish Lakish: **The latter clause of the mishna** is referring to a situation **where one said: This purse will not be exempt from consecration.** In other words, his desire was that he would not spend all of the *perutot* in the purse without consecrating one to the Temple. Consequently, he is liable for misuse only when he uses the last coin in the purse for non-sacred purposes and leaves none to be consecrated.

The *Gemara* answers consistently: *seifa be'omer lo yipater kis zeh min hahekdesh* — the *seifa* of the *mishnah* is a case where one explicitly declared, "this purse will not be free of *hekdesh*," meaning some *hekdesh* will remain until the very last coin is withdrawn. It is that explicit stipulation — not merely the word *min* as opposed to *be* — that generates the "last coin" interpretation. Without such explicit language, *Rabbi Akiva* would not say the last coin is designated; he might say the best coin, or the first, but the plain language of the *mishnah* without stipulation does not compel the "last" reading. The *Chachamim* throughout take the conservative position that the uncertainty persists until all coins are exhausted; *Rabbi Akiva* differs only at the margins, and even he requires explicit formulation to invoke the last-coin rule.
