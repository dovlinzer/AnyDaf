# Meilah 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Opening & Ravad

### Intro to Daf 21

We begin at the final *daf* of Masechet Meilah, daf 21a, completing our study of the laws of *me'ilah* — the misappropriation of consecrated property. Before turning to the new *mishnah*, two observations from the previous session deserve elaboration, as both bear directly on what follows.

### Ravad's Expansion

The first concerns the Ravad's understanding of the principle governing when an act of removal from *hekdesh* constitutes *me'ilah*. The point discussed earlier — that merely moving consecrated property from one location to another does not constitute the act of stealing from *hekdesh*, and that *me'ilah* requires transferring the object to a third party — is not limited to the *gizbar* (the Temple treasurer). The Ravad states explicitly that this principle applies to anyone who has an established right to handle the property. So long as such a person is moving it within the scope of his authority, that act alone is not *me'ilah*. The *me'ilah* occurs only when the item is transferred out of his control entirely. The Ravad makes the obvious qualification that if someone enters a Temple storehouse without any right to be there and removes an item, the act of taking itself is *me'ilah* — no transfer to a third party is needed. But within the domain of one who has the right to handle the property, the principle stands.

### Shlichus Yad vs Rights

This is significant for the cases we are about to read, which do not appear to involve a *gizbar* specifically yet operate under the same principle: that mere handling is not *me'ilah* for those with a right to the property. It is worth noting the contrast with the concept of *shlichus yad* — where a *shomer* (guardian) who takes an object under his care for personal use commits an act of misappropriation through that very act of intention and movement. In the laws of *me'ilah*, however, for one who has a genuine right to access the property, that threshold is insufficient. An overt act of transferring the object out of one's control — giving it to someone else — is required.

### Rambam on Kedusha Loss

The second preliminary point concerns a ruling of the Rambam based on the *Tosefta*, which was mentioned earlier and now can be stated more precisely. The principle that an object loses its *kedushah* after *me'ilah* has occurred — and thereby passes into *chullin* — applies, the Rambam rules, only to the *me'ilah* of stealing, not to the *me'ilah* of mere benefit. If one merely warms himself with consecrated wool, for example, that act of benefit constitutes *me'ilah* and requires atonement, but it does not strip the object of its *kedushah*. The *kedushah* is removed only when the object is taken and given to another — an act of dispossession. This makes conceptual sense: since the *kedushah* of *bedek habayis* is fundamentally a matter of ownership, the act most capable of severing that ownership relation is giving the object away. Passive benefit, though prohibited, lacks the power to effect that severance.

The Ravad disagrees with this ruling, maintaining that the Rambam has derived it from the *Tosefta* against the weight of normative *halachah*. Nonetheless, the conceptual framework the Rambam articulates is striking and illuminating. An act of *shinui* — transformation, such as building a beam into a wall — could also constitute a form of "stealing" in this sense, which connects to the earlier *gemara*'s discussion of the beam. Whether a full *perutah* of *pagam* and a full *perutah* of *hana'ah* are each independently required remains, for now, an open question.

## Shaliach & Window/Box

### Intro to Shaliach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אָמַר לוֹ: "הָבֵא לִי מִן הַחַלּוֹן אוֹ מִן הַדְּלוֹסְקָמָא", וְהֵבִיא לוֹ. אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמַר בַּעַל הַבַּיִת: "לֹא הָיָה בְּלִבִּי אֶלָּא מִזֶּה", וְהֵבִיא מִזֶּה – בַּעַל הַבַּיִת מָעַל. אֲבָל אִם אָמַר לוֹ: "הָבֵא לִי מִן הַחַלּוֹן", וְהֵבִיא לוֹ מִן הַדְּלוֹסְקָמָא, אוֹ "מִן הַדְּלוֹסְקָמָא", וְהֵבִיא לוֹ מִן הַחַלּוֹן – הַשָּׁלִיחַ מָעַל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If the homeowner **said to** the agent: **Bring me** this item or this money **from the window** in the wall **or from the chest [*hadeluskema*], and** the agent obeyed and **brought** it **to him** from the place that he instructed him, **even though the homeowner said: In my heart,** my desire was **only** that he should bring me the item **from that** other place, **and** as **he brought** it **from** this place he did not fulfill my instructions, nevertheless **the homeowner is liable for misuse** if the item or money is consecrated, as the agent did in fact fulfill his instructions. **But** if the homeowner **said to** the agent: **Bring me** this item or this money **from the window** in the wall, **and** the agent **brought** it **to him from the chest; or** if the homeowner said to the agent: Bring me this item or this money **from the chest, and** the agent **brought** it **to him from the window, the agent is liable for misuse.**

We now turn to the *mishnah* at the top of daf 21a, which belongs to the chapter known as *HaShaliach She'asah* — the *shaliach* who fulfilled his agency. The entire chapter is built around the question of when agency in *me'ilah* is operative. This is not the ordinary case of agency for benefit — if one simply instructs another to eat some consecrated meat and he does so, that is the agent's benefit and the agent's *me'ilah*. The *me'ilah* framework of this chapter is specifically about acts of dispossession — taking *hekdesh* property and transferring it elsewhere. Since the agent performs that act on behalf of the one who sent him, it is attributed to the sender. This is the conceptual foundation for why *shlichus* functions here at all, and the chapter probes its limits case by case.

### Window/Box Case

*Amar lo* — he said to him: bring me from the window or from the *dluskema* (a box or cabinet), and the agent did as instructed. Even if the *ba'al habayis* later says, "I only meant the other window" or "the other box," the rule is *devarim shebelev einam devarim* — one's unexpressed intentions are legally irrelevant. Since the agent did exactly what was said, the act is attributed to the sender and the *ba'al habayis* is *mo'el*.

A question arises: if the agent merely moved the object from one location to another and delivered it to the sender, where precisely is the act of *me'ilah*? The most natural answer is that unlike a *gizbar*, an ordinary person has no right to be handling consecrated property at all, and therefore the very act of taking it is *me'ilah*. However, the context of the case — which seems to imply the sender does have some relationship to the property — creates hesitation about reducing *me'ilah* to a bare act of moving. Tosafos ultimately suggests that the *me'ilah* is located in the subsequent act of transferring the item to a third party. That resolution, while technically coherent, sidesteps the question of how the intermediate act is characterized.

### Act of Meilah Location

The general principle that emerges is that there is a strong reluctance throughout this chapter to identify the act of *me'ilah* with mere movement or taking. Even in cases where one who does not have a right to the property is involved, the *gemara* gravitates toward requiring some more definitive act of dispossession. This tension runs through all the subsequent cases as well.

### Shaliach Deviation

The *mishnah* then presents the contrasting case: *aval im amar lo* — but if he said to him, bring from the window, and he brought from the box; or bring from the box, and he brought from the window — then *hashaliach ma'al*, the agent is the one who committed *me'ilah*. Even if the two locations are functionally equivalent and the items are identical in kind, any deviation from the precise instructions given breaks the chain of agency. The agent is no longer acting on the sender's behalf; he acted on his own initiative, and the act of *me'ilah* is his. The precise location at which the dispossession from *hekdesh* occurs in these cases remains somewhat ambiguous — the same underlying tension applies — but the attribution is clear: the agent, not the sender, bears responsibility.

## Cheresh/Shoteh/Katan

### Incompetent Agents

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁלַּח בְּיַד חֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן, אִם עָשׂוּ שְׁלִיחוּתוֹ – בַּעַל הַבַּיִת מָעַל, לֹא עָשׂוּ שְׁלִיחוּתוֹ – חֶנְווֹנִי מָעַל.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where the homeowner **sent** consecrated money **in the hand of a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor,** who lack halakhic competence and cannot be commissioned as agents, in order to purchase an item from a storekeeper, **if they performed his agency, the homeowner is liable for misuse,** as his instructions were fulfilled. **If they did not perform his agency** but purchased a different item from the storekeeper, **the storekeeper is liable for misuse.**

The *mishnah* proceeds to a remarkable case: *shalach beyad cheresh shoteh vekatan* — one sent the *hekdesh* money through a deaf-mute, a mentally incompetent person, or a minor. Ordinarily, these three categories — collectively called *cheresh shoteh vekatan* — are not *bnei da'as*, not legally competent persons, and cannot serve as agents for any purpose. If one appoints such a person as a *shaliach* to separate *terumah*, to deliver a *get*, or to effect *kiddushin*, the act is void. There is no *shlichus*.

Yet here the *mishnah* rules: *im asu shlichuto* — if they carried out what he sent them to do, *ba'al habayis ma'al* — the sender is *mo'el*. Conversely, *lo asu shlichuto* — if they did not follow his instructions, *chanvani ma'al* — the storekeeper is *mo'el* when he subsequently spends the money.

The scenario the *mishnah* envisions is that the sender gave the incompetent person money and instructed him to go to the market and purchase something. If the person did exactly that, the act is attributed to the sender as if he had done it himself. If the person deviated — say, purchasing something other than what was requested — then the sending cannot be attributed to the sender at all. Since the intermediary cannot bear personal responsibility by virtue of his incompetence, and since the act is not attributed to the sender, the *mo'el* becomes the storekeeper at the moment he accepts and spends the money.

### Gemara Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? דְּבָרִים שֶׁבַּלֵּב אֵינָם דְּבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that if the agent did as instructed by the homeowner, even if the homeowner claimed that he had another desire, it is the homeowner who is liable for misuse. The Gemara asks: **What is** the mishna **teaching us?** The Gemara answers: It is teaching that unspoken **matters that** remain **in the heart are not** significant **matters.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁילַּח בְּיַד חֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן, אִם עָשׂוּ [וְכוּ׳]. וְהָא לָאו בְּנֵי שְׁלִיחוּתָא נִינְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: In a case where the homeowner **sent** consecrated money **in the hand of a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor,** who lack halakhic competence and cannot be commissioned as agents, in order to purchase an item from a storekeeper, **if they performed** his agency, the homeowner is liable for misuse, as his instructions were fulfilled. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But they cannot be** involved in **agency.** Since these groups of people lack halakhic competence, they cannot be agents; if so, how can the homeowner be liable for misuse?

This question has broad ramifications. The particular character of *me'ilah* must be examined to understand how agency here operates at all. The underlying conceptual point is that *me'ilah* in this chapter is not about benefit but about removal — having *hekdesh* property redirected away from its sacred purpose. The question is therefore not whether these individuals can serve as agents in the technical legal sense, but whether their actions, when done according to the sender's instructions, can be causally attributed to him as the one who initiated and directed the removal. The *gemara* will now explore this through analogy.

One important ramification of the framework presented by the *mishnah*: the storekeeper who receives the money from the incompetent intermediary may be completely innocent. He has no way of knowing that the money is *hekdesh*, that the intermediary is incompetent, or that the sender has no idea the transaction is taking place. Yet the *mishnah* holds him *mo'el* when he spends the money. This is a case of virtually pure *ones* — compulsion or blamelessness — and yet *me'ilah* attaches. The *mefaresh* notes this explicitly, as we shall see.

Before reaching the *gemara*'s two proposed analogies, it is worth noting the broader structure of the case when a fully competent *shaliach* is involved. If I send a competent adult and he does what I said, I am *mo'el*. If he does not follow my instructions, he is *mo'el*. This is the standard framework established earlier in the chapter.

### Olives Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: עֲשָׂאוּם כְּמַעֲטָן שֶׁל זֵיתִים, דִּתְנַן: הַזֵּיתִים, מֵאֵימָתַי מְקַבְּלִין טוּמְאָה? מִשֶּׁיַּזִּיעוּ זֵיעַת הַמַּעֲטָן, וְלֹא זֵיעַת הַקּוּפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says** in explanation: The Sages **rendered** these individuals halakhically **like a vat [*kema'atan*] of olives** with regard to ritual impurity. Since it is beneficial for the homeowner when the moisture of the olives, i.e., liquid that seeps from the olives, leaks into a vat, as explained below, it is considered as though the homeowner had intent for the moisture to be there. Similarly, with regard to these categories of people, since it suits the homeowner when they do what he requested it is considered as though they acted with his intent. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Teharot* 9:1): **When do the olives** become **susceptible to** ritual **impurity? From when they exude** moisture. This is referring specifically to the **moisture** that emerges into **the vat, but not the moisture** that emerges into **the basket.** The moisture that comes into the vat softens the olives and is beneficial to the olive oil production, as it renders them easier to press. For this reason it is considered as though the homeowner has intent for the moisture to be there, and that moisture therefore renders the olives it touches susceptible to ritual impurity. By contrast, moisture in the basket serves no purpose, as it trickles out through holes in the basket. Consequently, it is not there with the intent of the homeowner and does not render the olives susceptible to ritual impurity.

Rabbi Elazar offers the first attempt at explaining why the incompetent messenger's act can be attributed to the sender: *asam k'ma'atan shel zeisim* — he made them like a vat of olives. The reference is to the *halachah* regarding when olives become susceptible to *tumah*. Olives become susceptible once liquid has touched them under conditions where the owner desires that liquid contact. The *mishnah* teaches: *misheyaz'u* — from when they sweat in the vat. When olives are collected into a carrying basket, the liquid that seeps out is unwanted; but once placed in the pressing vat, the owner wants the oil to begin flowing. The sweating that occurs in the vat — without any direct human action, simply by virtue of being placed there according to the owner's will — renders the olives susceptible to *tumah*.

Rabbi Elazar's point: sometimes an act is attributed to you not because you did it directly, but because it occurred according to your will, within a situation you deliberately created. Here too, the sender deliberately directed these individuals and they acted according to his will — that is sufficient to attribute the act to him.

Tosafos, however, finds this analogy strained. In the case of the olives, no action was ever conceptually required in the first place. The question from the beginning was simply whether the seeping liquid is desired, and the answer depends entirely on the owner's will. There was never a framework of "action" to be replaced by "causation." The analogy therefore fails to address the core difficulty: how does one's causal initiative substitute for a legally constituted act of agency?

### Monkey/Elephant Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: כְּאוֹתָהּ שֶׁשָּׁנִינוּ, נְתָנוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי הַקּוֹף וְהוֹלִיכוֹ, אוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי הַפִּיל וְהוֹלִיכוֹ, וְאָמַר לְאַחֵר לְקַבְּלוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ – הֲרֵי זֶה עֵירוּב. אַלְמָא קָא עָבְדָא שְׁלִיחוּתֵיהּ. הָכִי נָמֵי, אִיתְעֲבִיד שְׁלִיחוּתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** This case of a deaf-mute, imbecile or minor, is **like that which we learned** in a *baraita* with regard to placing food for a joining of Shabbat boundaries, i.e., an *eiruv*: If **one placed** the food of the *eiruv* **on a monkey, and** the monkey **brought it** to the place where he wanted the *eiruv* deposited, **or** if he placed it **on** a trained **elephant, and** the elephant **brought it** to the proper location, **and he told another** person **to receive it from** the animal, **it is** a valid ***eiruv*. Evidently, the agency is performed** by the monkey or elephant, despite the fact that they are unfit to serve as agents. **So too,** in the case of the deaf-mute, imbecile, or minor, **the agency is performed** despite the fact that they cannot be involved in agency.

Rabbi Yochanan offers a superior proof: *k'hahu de-tanina* — as we learned in *Eruvin*. If one placed his *eiruv techumin* on the back of a monkey and the monkey carried it to the designated spot, or placed it on an elephant and the elephant transported it — *harei zeh eiruv*, it counts as a valid *eiruv*.

This is a compelling parallel. The monkey and elephant are not agents in any legal sense. They cannot be appointed, they have no legal standing, and yet the *eiruv* is valid because the owner's item was transported to the correct location through the owner's initiative. *Alma ka-avid shlichuteih* — we see from here that "acting through your agency" does not require a legally competent agent; it requires only that the act was accomplished through your causal initiative, in accordance with your intent. *Hacha nami avid shlichuteih* — here too, when the incompetent messenger does what he was sent to do, it is as if the sender acted.

### Tosafos Challenge

Tosafos raises a complication: there is another *gemara* in *Eruvin* that appears to rule that placing an *eiruv* on a monkey or elephant does *not* work. How are these two passages reconciled?

Tosafos first suggests that the case in which it works is when another person was stationed at the destination to receive the *eiruv* and take it off the animal — and this is the parenthetical clause in the *gemara*: *ve-amar le-acher lekabbelo mimenu* ("and he told another to receive it from it"). But Tosafos then notes that this resolution undermines Rabbi Yochanan's proof entirely: if the reason it works is that a second person performed a meaningful act at the end, then it is that second person's act — not the monkey's conveyance — that makes the *eiruv* valid. The proof would not demonstrate anything about acting through a non-agent.

Tosafos therefore revises the answer: the distinction between the two *gemaros* in *Eruvin* is that in one case the animal simply wandered off on its own or the *eiruv* fell off without the owner observing, whereas in the other case the owner was watching as the *eiruv* was placed down — so his involvement and awareness at the moment of placement constitute sufficient participation to attribute the act to him. The upshot is that causal involvement must meet some minimum threshold of active engagement; not every attenuated chain of causation suffices. But where that threshold is met — where one sets the process in motion and it unfolds as intended — the act is attributed to him even without the formal apparatus of *shlichus*.

### Causal Involvement

This framework — attribution through causal initiative rather than through formal agency — is what allows *me'ilah* to function here even where *ein shaliach li-dvar aveirah* ("there is no agency for a sinful act") would ordinarily block it. The reason that rule does not apply here is that *me'ilah* in this chapter is not conceptualized primarily as a sin committed by the sender; it is conceptualized as the *effect* of having *hekdesh* property removed or misdirected through the sender's initiative. If that effect came about through his direction and because of his actions — whether through a competent adult, an incompetent messenger, or even a trained animal — he is the one who caused the removal, and the *me'ilah* is his.

A parallel noted in the *gemara*: if one takes consecrated oil and pours it on another person, it is the pourer — not the person who absorbed the benefit — who is *mo'el*, even though the other person received the benefit. The causal act of diverting the *hekdesh* property belongs to the one who set it in motion.

## Remembering Cases

### Shav Ve'afikeih

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁלַּח בְּיַד פִּקֵּחַ, וְנִזְכַּר עַד שֶׁלֹּא הִגִּיעַ אֵצֶל חֶנְווֹנִי – חֶנְווֹנִי מָעַל לִכְשֶׁיּוֹצִיא. כֵּיצַד יַעֲשֶׂה? נוֹטֵל פְּרוּטָה אוֹ כְלִי, וְאוֹמֵר: "פְּרוּטָה שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ בְּכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁהוּא מְחוּלָּל עַל זֶה", שֶׁהַהֶקְדֵּשׁ נִפְדֶּה בְּכֶסֶף וּבְשָׁוֶה כֶּסֶף.
>
> **Translation:** If the homeowner **sent** the money **in the hand of** a halakhically **competent** person **and** the homeowner **remembered** that the money was consecrated **before** the agent **reached the storekeeper, the storekeeper is liable for misuse when he spends** the money for his personal use. The homeowner is exempt from liability for misuse, because once he remembers that the money is consecrated his misuse is no longer unwitting, and one is liable to bring an offering for misuse only for unwitting misuse of consecrated property. **What shall** the homeowner **do** in a case where he remembers that the money is consecrated, in order to prevent the storekeeper from liability for misuse? **He takes one *peruta* or a vessel and says:** The **consecrated *peruta*, wherever it may be, is desacralized with this** *peruta* or vessel. The *peruta* is thereby desacralized, **as a consecrated item is desacralized with money and with** an item that has **the equivalent** value of **money.** The result is that the storekeeper spends non-sacred money.

The *mishnah* turns to the case where the *ba'al habayis* remembers mid-process that the money he sent is *hekdesh*. The *gemara* introduces the concept of *shav ve'afikeih* — the master can reclaim the *kedushah* by performing *pidyon* (redemption), transferring the *kedushah* from the coin held by the storekeeper onto another object of value, thereby neutralizing the risk of *me'ilah* for the storekeeper when he eventually spends the money. This is possible because the money belongs to *hekdesh*, not to the storekeeper, and *hekdesh* property can be redeemed regardless of whose physical possession it is in.

### Braisa: One Remembers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: נִזְכַּר בַּעַל הַבַּיִת וְלֹא נִזְכַּר שָׁלִיחַ – הַשָּׁלִיחַ מָעַל, נִזְכְּרוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם – חֶנְוָנִי מָעַל!
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita*: If **the homeowner remembered** that the money was consecrated, **but the agent did not remember, the agent is liable for misuse.** If **they both remembered** that the money is consecrated, the **storekeeper is liable for misuse.**

The *mishnah* states that when the *ba'al habayis* remembers before the *shaliach* reaches the storekeeper, the *chanvani* becomes *mo'el* when he spends the money. But this formulation prompts a challenge from a *braisa*: *nizkar ba'al habayis velo nizkar shaliach* — if the *ba'al habayis* remembers but the *shaliach* does not, *hashaliach mahu* — the *shaliach* is the *mo'el*. That is: once the *ba'al habayis* is removed from the equation by his awareness, the *shaliach* — who is still acting unknowingly — becomes the relevant party. The *shaliach* is not simply an extension of the *ba'al habayis* such that the *ba'al habayis*'s awareness automatically transfers to him. Once the *ba'al habayis* is aware and thus cannot be *mo'el*, and the *shaliach* is still unaware and acting, the *me'ilah* falls on the *shaliach*. Only then, when the *shaliach* gives the money to the *chanvani*, does the question of the *chanvani*'s *me'ilah* arise.

### Both Remember

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מַתְנִיתִין נָמֵי, כְּשֶׁנִּזְכְּרוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said** in resolution of this contradiction: **The mishna** is **also** referring to a case **where both** the agent and the agent **remembered** that the money was consecrated, and for this reason the storekeeper alone is liable for misuse.

Rav Sheshet resolves the apparent tension between the *mishnah* and the *braisa*: *matnitin keshenizkeru sheneihem* — the *mishnah* speaks of a case where both the *ba'al habayis* and the *shaliach* remember. When both are aware that the money is *hekdesh*, neither can be *mo'el* — the *ba'al habayis* because he is aware, the *shaliach* because... and here the case becomes curious. Perhaps the *shaliach*, knowing it is *hekdesh*, continues anyway because he feels bound by the instructions of his master. He is aware but proceeds. Even so, since the *ba'al habayis* has been severed from the chain of agency by his awareness, it is not his *me'ilah*. And since the *shaliach* is now acting with knowledge, the *me'ilah* is also not his in the conventional *shogeg* sense. It therefore falls to the *chanvani* when he spends the money.

### Problem of Ones

The discussion surfaces a profound difficulty. In multiple scenarios across this *mishnah*, parties who are entirely blameless — acting in complete ignorance, under what amounts to *ones* — are held liable for *me'ilah*. The *chanvani* who receives money from an incompetent messenger has no way of knowing the money is *hekdesh*. The *shaliach* who forgot that the money was *hekdesh* and is carrying out his master's instructions is acting without fault. Yet *me'ilah* attaches to them.

The question of whether *ones* exempts from *me'ilah* was raised earlier, but the *mishnah*'s cases seem to indicate it does not — at least not in the full sense. *Me'ilah* is an act of *beshogeg*, inadvertence, and the *gemara* elsewhere discusses what the punishment for *meizid* (deliberate) *me'ilah* would be. But the framework of *me'ilah* as a *korban*-and-*chomesh* obligation does not appear to require the level of negligence one might expect.

### Mountains/Hair

The *mefaresh* acknowledges this directly by citing the famous *mishnah* in Masechet Chagigah: certain *halachot* are like mountains suspended by a hair — vast in their practical consequences but resting on exceedingly slender scriptural support. The law here — that a *shaliach* who acted entirely as instructed is *mo'el* simply because the *ba'al habayis* happened to remember — falls into this category. As the *mefaresh* puts it: *deshaliach anya da mai ka'avid* — what did this poor *shaliach* do? He was simply following his master's instructions. Yet he is *mo'el*. This is the reality of the *halachah*, even though it is genuinely difficult to justify intuitively. The *gemara* acknowledges the difficulty and does not resolve it away; it is a *halachah* like mountains suspended by a hair, binding but not easily rationalized.

## Partial Fulfillment

### Half/Half Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נָתַן לוֹ פְּרוּטָה, אָמַר לוֹ: "הָבֵא לִי בְּחֶצְיָהּ נֵרוֹת וּבְחֶצְיָהּ פְּתִילוֹת" וְהָלַךְ וְהֵבִיא לוֹ בְּכוּלָּהּ פְּתִילוֹת אוֹ בְּכוּלָּהּ נֵרוֹת, אוֹ שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ: "הָבֵא לִי בְּכוּלָּהּ נֵרוֹת", אוֹ "בְּכוּלָּהּ פְּתִילוֹת", וְהָלַךְ וְהֵבִיא בְּחֶצְיָהּ נֵרוֹת וּבְחֶצְיָהּ פְּתִילוֹת – שְׁנֵיהֶם לֹא מָעֲלוּ. אֲבָל אִם אָמַר לוֹ: "הָבֵא לִי בְּחֶצְיָהּ נֵרוֹת מִמָּקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי וּבְחֶצְיָהּ פְּתִילוֹת מִמָּקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי", וְהָלַךְ וְהֵבִיא לוֹ נֵרוֹת מִמְּקוֹם פְּתִילוֹת, וּפְתִילוֹת מִמְּקוֹם נֵרוֹת – הַשָּׁלִיחַ מָעַל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If the homeowner **gave** the agent **one** consecrated ***peruta*** and **said to him: Bring me lamps [*nerot*] with one-half of it and wicks with one-half of it, and** the agent **went and brought him wicks with the entire** *peruta*, **or lamps with the entire** *peruta*; **or** in a case **where** the homeowner **said to** the agent: **Bring me lamps with the entire** *peruta* **or wicks with the entire** *peruta*, **and** the agent **went and brought** him **lamps with one-half of it and wicks with one-half of it, both of them are not liable** for misuse of the *peruta*. In both cases, the homeowner is exempt because his instructions were fulfilled only with regard to half of a *peruta*, and the agent is exempt as he spent only half of a *peruta* on his own initiative. **But if** the homeowner **said to** the agent: **Bring me lamps from such and such place with one-half of** the *peruta* **and wicks from such and such place with one-half of** the *peruta*, **and** the agent **went and brought him lamps from the place** that he designated for **wicks, and wicks from the place** that he designated for **lamps, the agent is liable for misuse,** as he deviated from the homeowner's instructions by the sum of an entire *peruta*.

The *mishnah* now presents a series of cases involving partial fulfillment of instructions, exploring when the *shaliach*'s deviation from his mandate makes him the *mo'el* and when the *ba'al habayis* retains that status for the portion of the instructions that was followed.

The first case: *natan lo perutah* — he gave him one *perutah*. He said: spend half on oil lamps and half on wicks. The *shaliach* went and spent the entire *perutah* on wicks alone, or the entire *perutah* on lamps alone. Alternatively: he instructed him to spend it all on wicks or all on lamps, and the *shaliach* split it half and half. In both scenarios — *sheneihem lo ma'alu* — neither committed *me'ilah*.

The reasoning: in any of these configurations, for each half-*perutah* unit, either the *ba'al habayis* directed it (so the *me'ilah* would be his) or the *shaliach* acted on his own (so the *me'ilah* would be his). But there is no *me'ilah* on a half-*perutah*. The two halves cannot be combined because they belong to two different parties — one half is the *ba'al habayis*'s action and one half is the *shaliach*'s action. Since neither has a full *perutah* of *me'ilah*, neither is *mo'el*.

### Both Not Moel

This case illustrates the principle that the threshold of a *perutah* applies individually to each party. Even where there is a technical deviation and a technical fulfillment, if neither party's involvement reaches the *perutah* threshold independently, no *me'ilah* has occurred.

### Store Deviation

However: *aval im amar lei* — if he specified the exact stores — bring half for lamps from this store and half for wicks from that store — and the *shaliach* reversed which store he went to for which item, even though everything else was done correctly, *hashaliach ma'al*. Any deviation from the specific instructions severs the agency entirely. Since the *shaliach* deviated for both halves, both half-*perutot* belong to his unauthorized acts, and together they constitute a full *perutah* of *me'ilah* — his *me'ilah*.

### Etrog/Pomegranate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתַן לוֹ שְׁתֵּי פְרוּטוֹת, וְאָמַר לוֹ: "לֵךְ וְהָבֵא לִי אֶתְרוֹג", וְהָלַךְ וְהֵבִיא לוֹ בְּאַחַת אֶתְרוֹג וּבְאַחַת רִימּוֹן – שְׁנֵיהֶם מָעֲלוּ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בַּעַל הַבַּיִת לֹא מָעַל, שֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר לוֹ: אֶתְרוֹג גָּדוֹל הָיִיתִי מְבַקֵּשׁ, וְהֵבֵאתָ לִי קָטָן וְרַע.
>
> **Translation:** If the homeowner **gave** the agent **two** consecrated ***perutot*, and said to him: Go and bring me an *etrog*, and he went and brought him an *etrog* with one** *peruta* **and a pomegranate with one** *peruta*, **both of them are liable for misuse.** The homeowner is liable because his agency was performed with the sum of one *peruta*, and the agent is liable because he deviated from the homeowner's instructions with one *peruta*. **Rabbi Yehuda says: The homeowner is not liable for misuse, as he** can **say to** the agent: **I was seeking a large *etrog*** worth two *perutot*, **and you brought me a small, inferior** *etrog* worth one *peruta*.

The second case: *nasan lo shtei prutos* — two *perutot* were given, with instructions to buy an *esrog*. The *shaliach* spent one *perutah* on an *esrog* and the other on a pomegranate. *Sheneihem ma'alu* — both are *mo'el*. The *ba'al habayis* is *mo'el* for the *esrog* purchase, because the *shaliach* did at least partially fulfill his instructions — he spent one *perutah* on the item requested. That half is the *ba'al habayis*'s *me'ilah*. The *shaliach* then spent the remaining *perutah* on his own initiative, making that his *me'ilah*.

### Rabbi Yehuda Dispute

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *ba'al habayis lo ma'al* — the sender is not *mo'el* even for the *esrog*. His reasoning: *esrog gadol hayisi mevakesh v'heiveisa li katan vera* — "I wanted a large, fine *esrog*, and you brought me a small, inferior one." Spending only half the allocated money on the requested item does not constitute fulfillment of the agency, because the sender's intent was to acquire a quality specimen with the full sum. A cheap, small *esrog* is not half of what was requested — it is not what was requested at all. Therefore, the *shaliach* did not fulfill the *ba'al habayis*'s *shlichus* even for the *esrog*, and no portion of the *me'ilah* belongs to the sender.

### Shirt/Tallis Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתַן לוֹ דִּינַר זָהָב, וְאָמַר לוֹ: לֵךְ וְהָבֵא לִי חָלוּק, וְהָלַךְ וְהֵבִיא לוֹ בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה חָלוּק וּבִשְׁלֹשָׁה טַלִּית – שְׁנֵיהֶם מָעֲלוּ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בַּעַל הַבַּיִת לֹא מָעַל, שֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר לוֹ: חָלוּק גָּדוֹל הָיִיתִי מְבַקֵּשׁ, וְהֵבֵאתָ לִי קָטָן וְרַע.
>
> **Translation:** If the homeowner **gave** the agent a consecrated **gold dinar,** which is worth twenty-five silver dinars, as four silver dinars constitute a *sela*; **and said to** the agent: **Go and bring me a robe,** **and** the agent **went and brought him a robe with three** *sela* **and a cloak with three** *sela*, **both of them are liable for misuse.** The homeowner is liable because his agency was performed with the purchase of the robe for three *sela*, and the agent is liable because he deviated from the homeowner's instructions by purchasing the cloak. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The **homeowner is not liable for misuse, as he** can **say to** the agent: **I was seeking a large robe** worth a gold dinar **and you brought me a small, inferior** robe worth three *sela*, i.e., twelve silver dinars.

The third case: *nasan lo dinar zahav* — a golden *dinar* was given with instructions to purchase a fine garment. The *shaliach* spent three *slaim* on a shirt and the other three *slaim* — the remaining half of the *dinar* — on a *tallis* (here meaning a large outer wrap, not a *tallis* with *tzitzis*). *Sheneihem ma'alu* — both are *mo'el*, by the same reasoning as the *esrog* case: the *ba'al habayis* is *mo'el* for the shirt (since the *shaliach* did at least purchase the requested type of garment), and the *shaliach* is *mo'el* for the *tallis*.

### Cheap Shirt

Rabbi Yehuda again: *ba'al habayis lo ma'al* — the sender is not *mo'el*. *Chaluk gadol hayisi mevakesh v'heiveisa li chaluk katan vera* — "I wanted a fine, substantial garment, and you brought me a small, inferior one." Spending only half the sum does not constitute fulfillment of the instruction; it produces a fundamentally different object than what was requested, and therefore the *shaliach* did not act as the *ba'al habayis*'s agent at all.

## Half vs Half Price

### Half Purchase Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ, מַאן דְּאָמַר לִשְׁלוּחוֹ: "זִיל זְבֵן לִי כּוֹרָא דְאַרְעָא", וַאֲזַל וּזְבַן לֵיהּ לִיתְכָּא – קָנֵי לוֹקֵחַ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara notes: One can **learn from** the mishna the resolution to an unresolved dilemma in tractate *Ketubot* (98b), that in a case of **one who said to his agent: Go** and **sell** on **my** behalf **a *kor* of land, and he went and sold for him a half**-***kor*, the purchaser acquires** the half-*kor* of land that he purchased. Although the agent did not fulfill his agency entirely, the part that he did perform is valid.

The *gemara* draws an inference from the position of the *Tanna Kamma* (the first, anonymous opinion that disputes Rabbi Yehuda). *Man de-amar lishliacho zil zavin li kura de-ara* — if one sends his agent to purchase an acre of land, and the agent returns having purchased only a half-acre — *kani lokeiach* — the buyer acquires it. This inference follows from the *Tanna Kamma*'s position that purchasing the *esrog* for one *perutah* instead of two still counts as partial fulfillment of the agency. If doing half of the task is still acting as an agent, then purchasing a half-acre when instructed to buy an acre should similarly constitute a binding acquisition for the sender.

### Sale Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הָכָא הֵיכִי דָּמֵי – כְּגוֹן דְּאַיְיתִי לֵיהּ שָׁוֶה שֵׁשׁ בְּשָׁלֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Some Sages **say** that one cannot infer this resolution from the mishna. **What are the circumstances** of the mishna **here?** It is referring a case **where** the agent **brought him** a robe **worth six** *sela*, i.e., the value of the gold *dinar* that the homeowner gave him, which he acquired **for three** *sela*. If so, the homeowner received exactly what he wanted and the agent did not deviate from the agency, except that he also purchased a cloak without being instructed to do so. This is dissimilar to the case of an agent who sells a half-*kor* of land.

The *gemara* rejects this inference: *hacha heichi dami* — what is the actual case in the *mishnah*? *De-aisei lei shaveh sheish bishlosh* — the *shaliach* brought him something worth six for the price of three. The item's actual value matched what was requested; it simply happened to be available at half price. In that case, the *shaliach* fully performed his task — he acquired the item the *ba'al habayis* wanted — and the *shlichus* is intact. One cannot extrapolate from this to a case where the *shaliach* actually did only half of the task.

### Sale vs Half Task

This distinction — between doing the full task at a bargain price versus doing only half the task — is conceptually important. In the bargain case, the *shaliach* obtained exactly what was requested; the question is only about money. In the half-task case, the *shaliach* obtained something qualitatively or quantitatively different from what was requested.

### Full Expenditure Point

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא סֵיפָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בַּעַל הַבַּיִת לֹא מָעַל, שֶׁהוּא יָכוֹל לוֹמַר לוֹ: "חָלוּק גָּדוֹל הָיִיתִי מְבַקֵּשׁ, וְהֵבֵאתָ לִי חָלוּק קָטָן וְרַע"! דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: אִי יָהֲבַתְּ דִּינָר כּוּלֵּיהּ, אַיְיתֵית לִי שָׁוֶה שְׁנֵי דִינָרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: If that is the case of the mishna, **say the latter clause: Rabbi Yehuda says:** The **homeowner is not liable for misuse, as he** can **say to** the agent: **I was seeking a large robe** worth a gold dinar, **and you brought me a small, inferior robe** worth three *sela*. It is clear from the mishna that the homeowner did not receive exactly what he wanted, but rather an inferior robe. The Gemara explains that Rabbi Yehuda means **that** the homeowner could have **said to** the agent: Since you chanced upon a merchant who reduced his prices to such a degree, **if you had given the entire dinar** as I requested, **you** could have **brought me** a much finer robe, **worth** at least **two dinars.**

Rabbi Yehuda, however, challenges even the bargain case: even if the item obtained is what was requested, part of the *shlichus* was to spend the entire amount. If the full sum had been spent, an even finer item could have been obtained — *ai havas dinar kula, hayisi shaveh shnei dinar* — "if you had spent the full *dinar*, I could have gotten a shirt worth two *dinar*." Therefore, the *shaliach* did not fully execute the agency even in the bargain case, and the *ba'al habayis* is not *mo'el*.

The *gemara* thus reads the dispute between the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Yehuda as specifically about this bargain scenario. The *Tanna Kamma* holds that obtaining the requested item — regardless of price paid — constitutes fulfillment of the *shlichus*. Rabbi Yehuda holds that full expenditure of the allocated sum is itself part of the mandate, and failing to spend the full amount means failing to fully execute the agency. On the question of whether doing literally half a task constitutes partial agency, both may actually agree that it does not — that case is simply not what the *mishnah* is debating.

## Kitnis Special Case

### Kitnis Concession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּקִטְנִית שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם מָעֲלוּ, שֶׁהַקִּטְנִית בִּפְרוּטָה וְקִטְנִית בְּדִינָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **This too stands to reason,** that this is the correct interpretation of the mishna, **as it teaches** in **the latter clause,** i.e., it is taught in the *Tosefta* (2:4): **Rabbi Yehuda concedes in** a case where the agent purchased only part of the **legumes** which the one who appointed him requested, **that both are liable for misuse of consecrated property.** The reason for this ruling is **that** a small amount of **legumes** is always sold **for one *peruta* and** a larger amount of **legumes** is invariably sold **for a dinar.** One can infer from Rabbi Yehuda's statement that in the case of another item, e.g., a robe, purchasing a superior-quality robe for a higher price would bring additional benefit to the homeowner.

A *braisa* is cited: *modeh Rabbi Yehuda be-kitnis she-sheneihem ma'alu* — Rabbi Yehuda concedes that in the case of *kitnis* (legumes or similar uniformly measured commodities), if the *shaliach* spent only half the money on the requested *kitnis*, both the *ba'al habayis* and the *shaliach* are *mo'el*. This is the opposite of Rabbi Yehuda's ruling in the shirt and *esrog* cases. Why does Rabbi Yehuda concede here?

### Why Kitnis Differs

The answer is intuitive upon reflection. When one asks for a shirt or an *esrog* and the *shaliach* returns with a cheaper, smaller version, the sender can legitimately say: this is a fundamentally different object than what I requested. A Kmart shirt is not half an Armani shirt; a small, inferior *esrog* is not half a large, fine *esrog*. They are categorically different things. The *shlichus* was not partially fulfilled — it was entirely missed.

By contrast, *kitnis* is a homogeneous commodity sold by measure. One pound of lentils is simply half of two pounds of lentils. It is not a different object; it is a smaller quantity of the same thing. If the *ba'al habayis* sent for two pounds and the *shaliach* returned with one pound, the *shaliach* did at least half of what was requested. One cannot say that one pound of lentils is entirely different from two pounds — it is precisely half of what was asked. Similarly, land by the half-acre does present a genuine complication (a full acre enables uses a half-acre cannot), but *kitnis* by the half-measure does not. Rabbi Yehuda therefore agrees that the *ba'al habayis* is *mo'el* for the half he received and the *shaliach* is *mo'el* for the half he spent on his own initiative.

### Bargain Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי בְּאַתְרָא דְּזָבְנִי בְּשׁוּמָא – גַּבֵּי קִטְנִית נָמֵי, דְּיָהֵב סֶלַע מוֹזְלִי לֵיהּ טְפֵי! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בְּדוּכְתָּא דִּמְזַבְּנִי בְּכַנֵּי כַּנֵּי, כַּנָּא כַּנָּא בִּפְרוּטָה, דְּהָתָם פְּסִיק מִילְּתַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*: **What are the circumstances? If** it is referring **to a town where they sell** legumes **by appraisal** of an item's value, then **with regard to** these **legumes as well,** purchasing a larger quantity should reduce the price, which would mean **that** if one **gave a *sela*** the price of the legumes would **become cheaper. Rav Pappa said:** The *Tosefta* is referring **to a town where they sell** and set the price **by containers,** and each **container** is the same price: **Each and every container** is sold **for one *peruta*.** The significance of this fact is **that there the matter is fixed,** i.e., each container of legumes is sold for the same price, regardless of the quantity of legumes purchased; there is no reduction for buying in bulk.

The *gemara* challenges this concession, pressing on the internal consistency of Rabbi Yehuda's position. If Rabbi Yehuda's entire argument against the *Tanna Kamma* in the shirt case is that the *shaliach* could have obtained a better bargain had he spent the full amount — *ai yahavta sela, mozli tefei* — then why doesn't the same logic apply to *kitnis*? If you had given the full *sela* for lentils, the merchant would have given you a better price per unit. So even by *kitnis*, Rabbi Yehuda should argue that the *ba'al habayis* could have gotten more for his money.

### Rav Papa's Answer

Rav Papa resolves the difficulty: *beduchta de-mizvni bekanei* — the concession of Rabbi Yehuda in *kitnis* applies specifically to places where the commodity is sold by exact fixed measure. *Kanei kanei, beperuta* — each measure has a set, fixed price; one *perutah* per measure, and so on. In such a market, there are no volume discounts, no bargains, no economy of scale. Every measure costs the same regardless of how much one buys. Therefore, Rabbi Yehuda's argument — "you cheated me of a better bargain" — simply does not apply. There was no better bargain to be had. In that context, Rabbi Yehuda concedes that spending half is a genuine partial fulfillment of the *shlichus*, and both parties are *mo'el*.

The implication the *gemara* draws is clarifying: the entire dispute between the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Yehuda is not about whether doing literally half a task counts as agency (on which both would agree it does not), but specifically about the bargain scenario, where the full item was obtained at less than full price. And Rabbi Yehuda's argument — that the *shlichus* includes spending the full amount to maximize value — is neutralized in precisely those markets where fixed pricing makes maximization impossible.

## Shulchani Case

### Bound/Unbound

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּפְקִיד מָעוֹת אֵצֶל שׁוּלְחָנִי, אִם צְרוּרִין – לֹא יִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן. לְפִיכָךְ, אִם הוֹצִיא – מָעַל. אִם מוּתָּרִין – יִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן. לְפִיכָךְ, אִם הוֹצִיא – לֹא מָעַל. אֵצֶל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת – בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ לֹא יִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן. לְפִיכָךְ, אִם הוֹצִיא – מָעַל. הַחֶנְווֹנִי – כְּבַעַל הַבַּיִת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כְּשׁוּלְחָנִי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who deposits** consecrated **money with a money changer, if** the money **is bound,** the money changer **may not use it. Therefore, if** the money changer **spent** the money, **he is liable for** its **misuse.** If the money **was unbound he may use it,** and **therefore if** the money changer **spent** the money, **he is not liable for** its **misuse.** By contrast, if one deposited money **with a homeowner, whether** it **is bound or whether** it **is unbound,** the one with whom it was deposited **may not use it,** and **therefore if he spent** the money, **he is liable for misuse.** In this regard, the halakhic status of **a storekeeper is like** that of **a homeowner;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says:** The halakhic status of a storekeeper is **like** that of **a money changer.**

The final *mishnah* of the tractate opens a new category: *hamaphkid ma'os etzel shulchani* — one who deposits *hekdesh* money with a *shulchani* (money changer). The underlying premise of all these cases, as before, is that *me'ilah* does not occur at the moment of taking or holding; the money must actually pass out of control — be spent, transferred, or given to a third party — before *me'ilah* is incurred. This confirms that the principle requiring actual dispossession, not merely handling, extends well beyond the *gizbar* and applies to any case where a party has some relationship to the property.

If the money was placed with the *shulchani* in a bound-up pouch — *bitzrur* — the norm is that the money changer is not permitted to use it. A bound purse signals that the depositor does not consent to the money changer accessing the funds. Therefore, if the *shulchani* uses the money — *im hotzi ma'al* — as soon as he spends it, that is his *me'ilah*. He had no right to use the money, acted entirely on his own, and the dispossession is attributable solely to him.

### Implicit Permission

If the money was not bound up — *im mutarim* — the understanding is different. An unbound deposit with a money changer carries an implicit license: it is well understood that money changers regularly use the funds in their possession and replace them as needed. The *ba'al habayis*, by depositing loose coins with a *shulchani*, is implicitly permitting him to use them. Therefore — *ulephichach im hotzi lo ma'al* — if the *shulchani* spends the money, he is not *mo'el*. He acted with implicit permission, which transforms the character of the act. This is something more than mere inadvertence or *ones*: it is actual, if implicit, permission conferred by the owner.

### Chanvani Liability

If neither the *ba'al habayis* nor the *shulchani* is *mo'el*, who is? The *mo'el* is whoever ultimately receives and spends the money — the *chanvani* (storekeeper) or whoever else accepts the coin in a transaction. At that point, the *hekdesh* money leaves *hekdesh*'s realm through the act of a third party who had no right or permission from *hekdesh* itself.

### Rambam View

The Rambam's position extends this logic: since the *ba'al habayis* never explicitly instructed the *shulchani* to spend the money, and the *shulchani* acted — albeit with implicit permission — on his own initiative, neither is *mo'el*. The Rambam draws a firm line: *me'ilah* of the sender type requires that the sender actually directed the spending. Implicit permission without directive instruction does not create the kind of agency-in-effect that makes the sender *mo'el*. The *me'ilah* thus falls on the end recipient when he uses the money.

This ruling is conceptually interesting and somewhat counterintuitive. The *shulchani*, by spending the money, is taking *hekdesh* property and directing it to secular use. One might have thought that even with implicit permission, the act of spending is an act of *me'ilah* — after all, in other contexts we saw that even a complete *ones* does not exempt from *me'ilah*. Yet the Rambam holds that genuine implicit permission — not merely a mistaken belief that one has permission, but actual permission conferred by the owner — alters the characterization of the act sufficiently to remove *me'ilah* liability from the one who was given that permission.

### Regular Homeowner

The *mishnah* contrasts the *shulchani* with the ordinary homeowner: *bein kach u'vein kach lo yishtamesh bahen* — whether the money is bound or unbound, a regular private individual entrusted with money for safekeeping has no implied permission to use it. Private individuals are not in the business of using deposited funds; no such norm exists. Therefore, if the ordinary *baal habayis* entrusted with money uses it — *lefichach im hotzi ma'al* — he is *mo'el* as soon as he spends it. He acted without any permission, and the act of dispossession is entirely his.

### Chanvani Status

The *mishnah* concludes with a dispute about the *chanvani* (storekeeper). Is a storekeeper more like the *shulchani* or more like the ordinary homeowner? *HaChanvani k'va'al habayis divrei Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi Meir says the storekeeper is like the ordinary homeowner; there is no implicit license for him to use deposited money, even if it is unbound. *Rabbi Yehuda omer k'shulchani* — Rabbi Yehuda says he is like a money changer and does have implicit permission to use unbound funds.

What emerges from this final *mishnah* as a whole: the tractate consistently resists locating *me'ilah* in the act of mere taking or handling. *Me'ilah* requires actual dispossession — money leaving *hekdesh*'s realm through spending or transfer. The concept of permission, whether explicit or implicit, plays a significant role in determining to whom that act of dispossession is attributed. And the Rambam's position introduces an additional nuance: even implicit permission, where genuine, breaks the chain in a way that pure inadvertence does not.

## Mixed Coins

### Coin Drop

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפְלָה פְּרוּטָה שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ בְּתוֹךְ כִּיסוֹ, אוֹ שֶׁאָמַר "פְּרוּטָה בְּכִיס זֶה הֶקְדֵּשׁ", כֵּיוָן שֶׁהוֹצִיא אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה – מָעַל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: עַד שֶׁיּוֹצִיא אֶת כָּל הַכִּיס.
>
> **Translation:** If **a consecrated *peruta* fell into one's purse,** in which there were non-sacred *perutot*, **or** in a case **where one said: One *peruta* in this purse is consecrated, once he spent the first** *peruta* from the purse for non-sacred purposes, **he is liable for** its **misuse.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. And the Rabbis say:** He is not liable for misuse **until he spends** all the *perutot* **in the entire purse,** as only then is it certain that he spent the consecrated *peruta*.

The *mishnah* now introduces a different scenario: *nafal pruta shel hekdesh betoch kiso* — a *hekdesh* coin accidentally fell into one's purse. Or: one declared *pruta bekisi hekdesh* — one coin in my purse is *hekdesh* — without specifying which one.

### Rabbi Akiva

Rabbi Akiva says: *kivan she-hotzi et harishona ma'al* — as soon as he takes out the first coin, he has committed *me'ilah*.

### Chachamim View

The *Chachamim* say: *ad she-yotzi et kol hakis* — only after he has spent all the coins in the purse has *me'ilah* certainly occurred. The *Chachamim*'s position is intuitive: until all the coins are spent, there is always a *safek* (doubt) as to whether the particular coin spent was the *hekdesh* one. *Me'ilah* with certainty can only be established once all coins have been disbursed.

### Batel BeRov Q

Tosafos raises a searching question: why doesn't the principle of *batel berov* apply? If a *hekdesh* coin falls into a purse containing many other coins, the *hekdesh* coin should be nullified by the majority, rendering the entire purse *chullin*. Tosafos anticipates the standard answers — *davar sheish lo matirin*, or other rabbinic reasons for blocking *bitul* — but notes that those principles are rabbinic in origin, whereas the question of *me'ilah* is a Torah-level concern. Those answers therefore do not resolve the question at the *de-oraita* level.

### Asham Taluy

Tosafos first notes that the *Tosefta* may resolve the difficulty with Rabbi Akiva's position. According to the *Tosefta*, Rabbi Akiva does not mean that spending the first coin constitutes definite *me'ilah*; rather, it creates a *safek me'ilah* for which one brings an *asham taluy*. This connects to a debate in Masechet Kerisos: Rabbi Akiva held that one can bring an *asham taluy* for doubtful *me'ilah*, while the *Chachamim* disagreed. Without the *Tosefta*'s clarification, Rabbi Akiva's ruling in the *mishnah* would be difficult to understand — how can the first coin taken be *vadai me'ilah* when there is manifestly a *safek* as to whether it is the *hekdesh* coin? The *Tosefta* resolves this: Rabbi Akiva means it creates *safek* liability, not *vadai* liability. After all coins are spent, one brings an *asham vadai* for the certain *me'ilah* that has by then occurred.

### Status/Ownership

Returning to Tosafos's question about *batul berov*: the most fundamental answer lies in a distinction between *bitul* as applied to *status* versus *bitul* as applied to *ownership*. The principle of *bitul berov* operates in the domain of *status* — when a forbidden substance is mixed with a permitted majority, the *issur* status is annulled and the mixture is treated as entirely permitted. But ownership is different. If Michael drops a hundred dollars into a box of my bills, I cannot declare his money *batel* in the majority and claim it all as mine. Ownership persists through the mixing; it is not subject to *bitul*. Since the *hekdesh* coin retains *hekdesh* ownership — it belongs to *hekdesh* regardless of where it is found — the concept of *bitul*, which addresses status rather than ownership rights, simply does not apply. One cannot tell *hekdesh*, "I'm sorry, your coin was nullified in the majority." The coin must be found or replaced. This is the conceptual resolution, and it applies at the *de-oraita* level.

### Explicit Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לַחֲכָמִים בְּאוֹמֵר "פְּרוּטָה מִן כִּיס זֶה הֶקְדֵּשׁ", שֶׁהוּא מוֹצִיא וְהוֹלֵךְ עַד שֶׁיּוֹצִיא אֶת כָּל הַכִּיס.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Akiva concedes to the Rabbis in** a case where **one says: One *peruta* from** the coins in **this purse is consecrated, that he may continue spending** the *perutot* in the purse for non-sacred purposes and becomes liable for misuse only **once he spends** all the *perutot* **in the entire purse.** His formulation indicates that his desire was that the final remaining *peruta* in the purse would be consecrated, and therefore one is liable for misuse only when he spends that *peruta*.

The *mishnah* then notes: *u-modeh Rabbi Akiva le-Chachamim* — Rabbi Akiva agrees with the *Chachamim* in the case where one says *pruta min hakis zeh Hekdesh* ("a coin from this purse is *hekdesh*") rather than *pruta bekis zeh* ("a coin in this purse is *hekdesh*"). In that case — *she-motzi ve-holekh ad she-yotzi et kol hakis* — one must spend through all the coins before *me'ilah* is certain; the last coin taken is the *hekdesh* one. The difference between "in" and "from" seems to indicate whether the first or the last coin is designated as *hekdesh*, but the *gemara* will interrogate this.

## Which Coin/Ox

### In vs From

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אֲמַר: רָמֵי לֵיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאי שְׁנָא רֵישָׁא וּמַאי שְׁנָא סֵיפָא?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that Rabbi Akiva concedes to the Rabbis in a case where one says: One *peruta* from the coins in this purse is consecrated, that he may continue spending the *perutot* in the purse for non-sacred purposes and that he becomes liable for misuse only once he spends all the *perutot* in the entire purse. With regard to this case, the Gemara relates that **when Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said** that **Reish Lakish raised a contradiction to Rabbi Yoḥanan: What is different** in **the first clause,** which addresses a situation where one says: One *peruta* in this purse is consecrated, in which case Rabbi Akiva disagrees with the Rabbis, **and what is different** in **the latter clause,** where he agrees with them?

The *gemara* opens by questioning the distinction the *mishnah* draws between saying a coin is *hekdesh* "in" the purse versus "from" the purse.

### Reish Lakish Q

Reish Lakish challenges Rabbi Yochanan: *mai shna reisha u-mai shna seifa* — what meaningful difference is there between these two formulations? Both describe a single unidentified coin in the same purse. The linguistic variation seems too thin to carry the weight of such a fundamental distinction in *me'ilah* liability.

### Yochanan Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: סֵיפָא בְּאוֹמֵר "לֹא יִפָּטֵור כִּיס זֶה מִן הַהֶקְדֵּשׁ".
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** Reish Lakish: **The latter clause** of the mishna is referring to a case **where one said: This purse will not be exempt from consecration.** In other words, his desire was that he would not spend all of the *perutot* in the purse without consecrating one to the Temple. Consequently, he is liable for misuse only when he uses the last coin in the purse for non-sacred purposes and leaves none to be consecrated.

Rabbi Yochanan responds: the *seifa* (*u-modeh Rabbi Akiva*) does not actually describe a case where someone said *min hakis zeh* without further specification. Rather, it refers to a case where one explicitly said *lo yipater kis zeh min hahekdesh* — "this purse will not be free of *hekdesh*," meaning there will always remain a *hekdesh* coin in the purse until the very last one is taken. That explicit, unusual formulation designates the last coin. Without such explicit language, one would not naturally read any standard declaration as designating the last coin.

### Oxen Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין אֲמַר: כִּיסִין אַשְּׁווֹרִים רְמָא לֵיהּ, דִּתְנַן: הָאוֹמֵר "אֶחָד מִשְּׁווֹרַיי הֶקְדֵּשׁ" – הָיוּ לוֹ שְׁנַיִם, הַגָּדוֹל שֶׁבָּהֶן הֶקְדֵּשׁ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: סֵיפָא בְּאוֹמֵר "לֹא יִפָּטֵור כִּיס זֶה מִן הַהֶקְדֵּשׁ".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further relates that **when Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia **he said** that Reish Lakish **raised a contradiction to** Rabbi Yoḥanan **between** the mishna here, discussing **purses, and** another mishna, discussing **bulls. As we learned** in a mishna (*Menaḥot* 108b): With regard to **one who says: One of my bulls is consecrated,** if **he had two** bulls, then **the larger of them is consecrated.** The reason is that there is an assumption that one consecrates generously, and therefore he would have had the larger of the two bulls in mind. If so, in the case of the purses, why does the mishna not rule that his intent was for the best, i.e., the least worn-out, of the *perutot*? Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: The latter clause** of the mishna is referring to a case **where one said: This purse will not be exempt from consecration.** In other words, he did not have in mind the best of the *perutot*, but the last of the coins.

A parallel case is raised from another *mishnah*: if one says *echad mishorai hekdesh* — one of my oxen is *hekdesh* — and he has two oxen, *hagadol shebahen hekdesh* — the larger one is *hekdesh*, because we assume one sanctifies generously, giving the best of what one has. If that principle applies to oxen, why don't we apply it to coins and say the most valuable coin in the purse is the *hekdesh* one, rather than defaulting to the first or the last? The *gemara* takes note of this challenge.

The answer Rabbi Yochanan gives is the same: in the *seifa* of the *mishnah*, the designation of "last coin" is not derived from any inference or default rule — it is derived from the explicit language *lo yipater kis zeh min hahekdesh*, which directly indicates that the purse will retain a *hekdesh* coin until its very end.

### Wine/Breira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: כִּיסִין אַלּוּגִּין רְמָא לֵיהּ, דִּתְנַן: הַלּוֹקֵחַ יַיִן מִבֵּין הַכּוּתִים, אוֹמֵר: "שְׁנֵי לוּגִּין שֶׁאֲנִי עָתִיד לְהַפְרִישׁ – הֲרֵי הֵן תְּרוּמָה, עֲשָׂרָה – מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן, תִּשְׁעָה – מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי", וּמֵיחֵל וְשׁוֹתֶה מִיָּד, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹסְרִים! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: סֵיפָא דְּמַתְנִיתִין בְּאוֹמֵר "לֹא יִפָּטֵר כִּיס זֶה מִן הַהֶקְדֵּשׁ".
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said** yet another version of Reish Lakish's question. **He raised a contradiction between** the mishna here involving **purses** and another mishna referring **to a *log*** measure. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Demai* 7:4): In the case of **one who purchases wine from among the Samaritans [*Kutim*]** and there is reason to suspect that *teruma* and tithes were not separated, and he cannot separate them before the start of Shabbat, what should he do? The mishna explains that he proceeds as follows. If, for example, there are one hundred *log* of wine in the barrels, he **says: Two *log* that I will separate in the future are *teruma*,** as the established average measure of *teruma* is one-fiftieth; **ten** *log* are **first tithe;** and one-tenth of the remainder, which is **nine** *log*, are **second tithe. And he desacralizes** the second tithe that he will separate in the future, transferring its sanctity to money, **and he may drink** the wine in the barrel **immediately,** relying on the separation that he will perform later. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.** **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon prohibit** one from doing so. These three Sages reject the principle of retroactive designation, upon which this method relies, as at the time of the declaration the identity of the particular portions of wine that will be *teruma* and tithes is unknown. Even according to Rabbi Meir, one can rely on retroactive designation only in a case where he says: That I will separate in the future. In the case of the mishna here, each *peruta* might be the consecrated one, and one cannot rely on the last one. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: The latter clause of the mishna** is referring to a situation **where one said: This purse will not be exempt from consecration,** in which case he is understood as referring to the last *peruta* left in the purse.

A *braisa* is cited involving a related case: *halokeach yayin miben hakutim* — one who purchased wine from Samaritans, where even *terumah* (not merely *ma'aser*) must be assumed not to have been separated. If it is *erev Shabbat* and one cannot perform the full *hafrasha* now, one may declare: the two *lugin* that I will eventually separate shall be *terumah*, the ten that I will separate shall be *ma'aser rishon*, the nine I will separate shall be *ma'aser sheni*, and the *kedushah* of the *ma'aser sheni* is transferred onto a coin — and then one may drink immediately. This is the principle of *breira*: the portions not yet physically designated are identified retroactively as *terumah* and *ma'aser* by virtue of the declaration made now and the subsequent act of separation.

Rabbi Meir permits this — *yesh breira*, the retroactive designation is valid. Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Yossi, and Rabbi Shimon forbid it — *ein breira*, there is no retroactive designation; the portions must be identified in real time.

### Breira Challenge

Tosafos raises the connection to our case: according to Rabbi Meir, does the *breira* framework — which requires explicit declaration for the designated portion to be identified — support or complicate Rabbi Akiva's position regarding the last coin? Tosafos notes that if Rabbi Meir requires explicit stipulation to make the last portion effective as *terumah*, then Rabbi Akiva's position — that in the *seifa* the last coin is *hekdesh* without explicit language — appears inconsistent. How does designating the last coin work without such specification?

### Final Resolution

The *gemara* gives the same answer throughout this section: the *seifa* of the *mishnah* is not a case without explicit language. It is specifically the case of *lo yipater kis zeh min hahekdesh* — an explicit declaration that the purse will not be free of *hekdesh*, which directly and unambiguously designates the last remaining coin. Rabbi Akiva would not derive "last coin" from any default rule or *breira* principle; the explicit language of the declaration creates the designation. Without that language, no such designation would operate retroactively, and the case would not differ from the *reisha*.

## Conclusion

### End of Meilah

The *mishnah* closes with the case of mixed coins, marking the end of our study of Masechet Meilah. *Hadran alach Shaliach She'asa Shlichuto u-slika lah Masechet Me'ilah* — We return to discuss the *shaliach* who fulfilled his agency, and the Tractate of Meilah is complete.
