# Niddah 70 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shivat Nekiyim Counting

### Uncertain Bleeding Case

The daf we are picking up — at the top of 69b — is in the midst of a discussion about whether the position of Rabbi Eliezer, as interpreted by Rav, is that the *shivat nekiyim* can be satisfied not only by counting the first and seventh day, not only by counting just the first day, but even by counting only the seventh day at the very end. Based on this understanding, the *gemara* attempts either to challenge or to prove this position from various cases involving a woman who immerses in the *mikvah* multiple times on account of *tevilah bizmanah*.

The specific case before us is that of a woman who comes before the *beis din* having bled, but who does not know how many days she bled, nor when she bled. The case is built around uncertainty: perhaps she is concluding her seven nights of *niddah*, perhaps she is a *zavah* who has completed her seven clean days, or perhaps she saw blood a moment before arriving, in which case she would require an additional seven days beyond today — meaning eight days total, plus all the nights in between.

### Evening Arrival Rules

When this uncertain woman arrives in the evening, the *baraita* states: *yahavinan lah tami l'niddah v'sheva l'ziva* — she requires eight immersions for *niddah* and seven for *zavah*. The *gemara* immediately presses on why the count for *zavah* would differ from that for *niddah*.

### Zavah vs. Niddah

The *gemara* explains why the count for *niddah* comes to eight nights. If she arrives on a Sunday night, at one extreme her seven days of *niddah* may have run from Monday through Sunday, with Sunday night now being her immersion night. At the other extreme, she may have just seen blood moments before arriving — meaning Sunday night is effectively Monday, and she would count forward eight nights until the following Sunday night. Interpolating across all possible scenarios, the maximum span is eight nights.

The *gemara* then corrects the count for *zavah* as well: *ziva tami ba'i* — she also requires eight days for *zavah*, not seven. The *gemara* revises accordingly: *sheva l'niddah v'tami l'ziva*. But then it immediately asks again: if she arrives at night, the *niddah* count should also be eight, not seven. The *gemara* resolves this by noting that *zavah* is fixed — it does not matter whether she arrives by day or by night, the count is always eight. *Niddah*, by contrast, depends on when she arrives: if by night, it is eight; if by day, it is seven. Since the *baraita* stated a fixed number of fifteen, it simply presented the minimum case without elaborating on the night variant, which would yield sixteen.

### Counting Not Required

With this in hand, the *gemara* draws an inference. If one holds that the counting of *shivat nekiyim* must occur sequentially, day by day, before the *beis din* — *sefurim lefaneinu ba'inan* — then there is no coherent reason for all these multiple immersions. The woman has not counted the days as they unfolded, so she certainly cannot be *tehorah* now. And yet the *baraita* contemplates her immersing on any of these days. This proves that such sequential counting is not required, which is consistent with Rav's reading of Rabbi Eliezer: it is sufficient to establish the status only at the end of the seven days.

### Rav Acha Challenges

At this point, Rav Acha son of Rav Yosef raises an objection to Rav Ashi: *lav kematartzinan lah* — you have already taken liberties with this *baraita*. Not only have you acknowledged that it sometimes yields sixteen rather than the stated fifteen, but more significantly, the original version of the *baraita* stated that the woman said she had not bled at all. If she did not bleed, why is she going to the *mikvah*? The *baraita* was already reinterpreted to mean: she bled but has no idea how much. Once such reinterpretation is already in play, one further step in the same direction is permissible.

### Alternative Interpretati…

The *gemara* therefore proposes: *tirtza hachi* — interpret the *baraita* this way. She says: *safarti ve'eini yoda'at kama safarti* — I counted, but I do not know how many days I counted. Perhaps I counted as a *niddah*, perhaps as a *zavah*, perhaps I counted at all, perhaps I did not. On this reading, *matbilin otah tesha tvilot* — she is immersed nine times.

The *gemara* then notes: if she says she at least counted something, then we know she has already initiated the process for at least one day — *chad yoma meiha ee efshar d'la safra* — and she should be missing one immersion accordingly. The *gemara* resolves this by pushing further: she does not even know whether she counted at all. Read the *baraita* as saying: *eini yoda'at im safarti im lo safarti* — I do not know whether I counted or not. The *baraita* is thus completely reread, but the essential point stands.

### Rav's Leniency

The upshot of the entire discussion is that there are clear indications in the *baraita* that a *zavah* does not need to count from the very beginning of the seven clean days. It is sufficient that she finds herself *tehorah* on the last day, performs a *bedikah*, and immerses — as opposed to the positions of Rabbi Akiva, who required daily counting, and Rabbi Yehoshua, who required that only counted days be used, and even a version of Rabbi Eliezer's view that required at minimum the framing of a beginning and an end. Rav's position is the most lenient: one need not structure the process from beginning to end; marking it at the conclusion is sufficient.

## Practical Halachot

### Positions Overview

Having established the range of positions within the *gemara* itself — from Rabbi Akiva's requirement of daily counting, through Rabbi Yehoshua's intermediate view, to Rabbi Eliezer's beginning-and-end requirement, and finally to Rav's most lenient reading — the discussion turns to how these positions were received by the *rishonim* and *poskim*.

### Shulchan Aruch Ruling

The *Shulchan Aruch* rules in accordance with the majority of *rishonim* who read the *mishnah* as establishing that counting only the first and seventh days is a *bedi'avad* dispensation, which implies that *lechatchila* a woman is obligated to count all seven days. This is the normative ruling.

### Raavad's Lenience

The Ra'avad, however, takes a notably lenient approach, essentially following Rav's position that any day — even a single day in the middle of the seven — can suffice, without requiring either endpoint to frame the count. This is not the *lechatchila* ruling but is relevant in cases of need.

### Sefer HaTruma

The *Sefer HaTruma*, following the view attributed to Rabbi Chanina, holds that one specifically requires *davka* the first and seventh days, and adds *ve'ein lehakel* — there is no room for leniency. This raises significant practical questions about how absolute the *ve'ein lehakel* is in a genuine *sha'at hadchak*, and whether even in this strict opinion the requirement for first and seventh need not mean literally the endpoints but could be satisfied by any two occasions during the week.

### Extreme Cases

The practical question that most urgently arises from all of this is: what happens when a woman forgets to perform her *bedikos* during the *shivat nekiyim*? What is the minimum that renders her count acceptable? This question has occupied many *poskim* and connects directly to the underlying logic of the entire requirement.

### Consciousness Requirement

A further question raised among the *acharonim* is whether the *bedikah* requires conscious awareness. That is, is *v'safrah lah* an injunction that demands a deliberate, conscious act of marking each day, or is it simply a verification that seven clean days have in fact elapsed? This question turns out to have implications in both directions — toward leniency and toward stringency.

### Constant Bleeding Cases

The Chasam Sofer applies this framework *l'kula* in the extreme case of a woman with a wound — a *maka* — who is in a state of constant bleeding and can never produce a clean *bedikah*. One approach is to attribute the blood to the wound rather than to uterine bleeding. But during the *shivat nekiyim*, that attribution alone is not sufficient; the requirement is not merely to rule out a problem but to actively verify the state of *taharah*. How can the requirement of an active *bedikah* be met when physical examination always yields blood?

The Chasam Sofer suggests that perhaps the entire point of the *bedikah* during the *shivat nekiyim* is to consciously mark the days. On this reading, in extreme cases there might be room for a substitute: if a woman is sufficiently attentive and inwardly senses that the bleeding is not uterine — even without performing the physical examination — that conscious awareness might serve in place of the *bedikah*, given that the underlying assumption of *taharah* is already in place. This is a fascinating leniency invoked only in the most difficult circumstances.

### Stringent Views

Some *acharonim* invoke the same underlying concept in the opposite direction, toward stringency. Consider a woman who performs the initial *bedikah* properly but then, in the middle of the week, forgets she is in the midst of counting *shivat nekiyim* — or who performs a *bedikah*, assumes the result is problematic, concludes she is *tamei* again and must start over, and then days later shows the cloth to a rabbi who rules it was in fact acceptable. The question is: does the lack of conscious awareness that one is within the *shivat nekiyim* during those intervening days constitute a disruption of the count?

If the Chasam Sofer's leniency is correct — that conscious awareness is all that is ultimately required — then that awareness functions as an independent criterion, and its absence becomes potentially disqualifying. Some *acharonim* hold that if a woman, even while technically clean, believed herself to be outside the *shivat nekiyim*, her count may be invalid. This stands in direct tension with the *gemara*'s own logic, which suggests that a woman can arrive at the seventh day without any prior counting and still be *tehorah*. The tension between these positions tracks precisely the dispute between Rav and the more demanding readings of the *mishnah*.

### Torah Interpretation

The *pasuk* reads: *v'safrah lah shivat yamim achar taharah* — she shall count for herself seven days after her *taharah*. The question is what *v'safrah lah* requires. The more demanding readings hold that counting is itself a *mitzvah*, an active daily marking, analogous perhaps to *sefiras ha'omer*. The more lenient readings hold that *v'safrah lah* means nothing more than: ascertain that seven clean days have elapsed since she last bled. On this reading, there is no independent act of counting — merely the retrospective knowledge that seven clean, unbloodied days have passed. Knowing that one did not see blood is not the same as performing a deliberate act of counting each day as it arrives.

This interpretive question underlies the entire range of positions discussed above. It also explains why the question of a *berachah* before the *shivat nekiyim* — analogous to the *berachah* before *sefiras ha'omer* — was raised and rejected in the *poskim*: if the counting is not an independent *mitzvah* but only a procedural verification, there is nothing over which to recite a *berachah*. With this, the discussion of *hefsek taharah* and *shivat nekiyim* concludes, and the tractate shifts toward its final chapters, which return to the broader themes of *tumah* and *taharah*.

## Tumah Categories

### Five Categories

The *mishnah* opens this section by listing five categories of individuals whose *tumah* requires special attention: *hazav v'hazavah v'haniddah v'hayoledet v'hametzora* — a *zav*, a *zavah*, a *niddah*, a *yoledet*, and a *metzora*. One might ask why the *mishnah* specifies *zav* and *zavah* rather than *metzora* and *metzorat*. The answer is likely that because the preceding discussion focused so heavily on the *zavah*, the *mishnah* wishes to explicitly include the male counterpart.

### Mishkav U'Moshav Tumah

What these five share is the unique *tumah* category of *mishkav u'moshav* — the *tumah* that attaches to surfaces upon which they sit or lie. The *mishnah* states that they convey *tumah be'masa* — through being carried — even after death, *ad she'yimakeh habasar*, until their flesh decomposes. The *gemara* immediately asks: what is novel here? Any *meis* conveys *tumah be'masa*. What is specific to these five?

The *gemara* answers: *ela mai be'masa be'even mesama* — the *mishnah* is speaking about a particular form of *tumah* conveyed through the mechanism of *even mesama*. An *even mesama* is a large stone that, when interposed between the *tamei* individual and an object beneath it, does not exert significant weight downward. Ordinarily, the *tumah* of *mishkav u'moshav* extends through weight — if a *zav* or *zavah* is sitting on something, even many layers removed, the thing beneath is considered their *mishkav* or *moshav* and becomes an *av hatumah*. In the case of an *even mesama*, however, where the weight is negligible or wholly blocked, the object beneath does not become an *av hatumah* but rather a *rishon l'tumah*. The *mishnah* teaches that even this attenuated form of *tumah* — the *even mesama* status — continues to apply to these five individuals even after death, until the flesh decomposes.

### Non-Jews Status

The *mishnah* then raises the case of a non-Jew who dies: *akum she'meis tahor min hatamei*. When a non-Jew was alive, the rabbis decreed that he carries a *tumah* analogous to that of a *zav*. Yet when he dies, this extended *tumah* — even the limited *even mesama* form — does not continue. Why?

The *gemara* explains: *lefi she'ein tumaso michayim mideivrei Torah mideivrei sofrim* — because his *tumah* during his lifetime was purely rabbinic, not biblical. The special continuation of *tumah* after death is predicated on the fact that when alive these individuals carried biblical *tumah* of *mishkav u'moshav*. For a non-Jew, whose *zav*-like *tumah* was only rabbinic in origin, there is no basis to extend even the minimal post-mortem *tumah*.

This touches on a broader question that is very much alive in contemporary *halacha*: the question of a *kohen* entering a hospital or a medical school. The general ruling is that non-Jews do not convey *tumas ohel* — *tumah* transmitted by being under the same roof — but they do convey *tumah* through direct contact. There are those who are stringent and hold that even *tumas ohel* applies by non-Jews, leading some *kohanim* to avoid institutions that house non-Jewish remains, including, for example, museums with mummies.

### Even Mesama

The *gemara* cites a verse to illustrate the *even mesama*: *va'haitit even chada v'sumat al pum guva* — a single stone was placed upon the mouth of a pit. The basic case is: a corpse of one of these five individuals is lying or sitting upon a surface, with a large stone interposed. The object beneath the stone becomes a *rishon l'tumah* by virtue of the continuing *mishkav u'moshav* status of the deceased, even though the weight does not fully transmit through. This is the special *tumah* that persists until decomposition.

### Fainting Concern

The *gemara* then asks why this *tumah* extends only until decomposition — why not immediately upon death? Rava answers: *gezeirah shema yitalfa* — it is a rabbinic decree out of concern that the person may have only fainted and not actually died. As long as there is a possibility that the person is still alive, we apply the *tumah* of their living status — *zav*, *zavah*, *niddah*, and so on — so that we do not inadvertently rule objects clean that were in contact with a still-living *tamei* individual. A tradition cited in the name of Rabbi Eliezer goes further, extending this concern until *ad she'yibaka keriso* — until the stomach actually bursts open — though the relationship between these two standards is not fully elaborated.

This concern about fainting is more than an antiquarian curiosity. It connects directly to the broader literature on determining the moment of death — a question that became acute in the 19th century when the fear of premature burial was widespread. The *gemara* here is invoked in that halachic literature precisely because it demonstrates that the rabbis themselves were attentive to the possibility that a person who appears dead may in fact only have fainted. The *gemara*'s practical resolution — continuing to assign a limited form of *tumah* — can be read as a legal mechanism that keeps that awareness alive and may help prevent burial of someone who has merely lost consciousness.

## Alexandria Questions

### Q1: Death Tumah

The *gemara* introduces a *baraita*: *shneim asar devarim sha'alu anshei Aleksandria et Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya* — twelve things the people of Alexandria asked Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya. The people of Alexandria were presumably Jewish, as the nature of the questions makes clear, but Alexandria was a heavily Hellenized Jewish city, and the character of the questions reflects a philosophically inclined audience. The twelve questions are organized into four groups of three: three questions of wisdom (meaning *halacha*), three of *aggadah*, three foolish questions, and three questions of *derech eretz*.

The first *halachic* question ties directly into the preceding *mishnah*: do a *zav*, *zavah*, *niddah*, *yoledet*, and *metzora* continue to convey *tumah be'masa* after death, and until when? Rabbi Yehoshua answers: *ad she'imok habasar* — until the flesh decomposes, precisely as the *mishnah* established. This is the direct connection between the Alexandrian questions and the *mishnah* that precedes them.

### Q2: Bas Meshulachas

The second *halachic* question is: *bas meshulachas ma hi l'kohen* — what is the status of the daughter born of a forbidden remarriage with respect to *kehunah*?

The background: the Torah prohibits a man from remarrying his divorced wife after she has been married to another man. If he violates this prohibition and does remarry her, what status does the child of that union carry? The question is whether such a child is a *mamzer* — which would require a prohibition under penalty of *kares* or worse — or a *challalah* — which would apply when the offspring results from a relationship specifically prohibited to *kohanim*, even where the prohibition is only a negative commandment (*lav*), such as a *kohen gadol* marrying an *almana* or a *kohen* marrying a *gerushah*. Here, however, the prohibition against remarriage is neither *kares* nor specifically a *kohen* prohibition. It is a universal *lav*. So neither category seems to fit straightforwardly.

The *gemara* considers a *kal va'chomer*: if in the case of an *almana* and a *kohen gadol* — where the prohibition is not universal, applying only within the framework of *kehunah* — the child is nonetheless *pogum* and disqualified from marrying *kohanim*, then surely in the case of this remarriage — which is a universal prohibition applying to all Jews — the child should likewise be disqualified. 

The counter-argument is that the logic of *challalah* runs through the *mitchaleles* mechanism: when a *kohen* has forbidden relations specifically tied to *kehunah*, not only does he transgress, but the woman herself undergoes a personal status change — she becomes a *challalah*, disqualified from marrying other *kohanim*. It is *because* she herself becomes a *challalah* that the child born of this forbidden union inherits the disqualified status. In the case of the forbidden remarriage, however, the woman does not herself become *mitchaleles* — her status undergoes no personal change of this kind. And since she herself remains unaffected in this respect, perhaps the child is likewise unaffected.

Rabbi Yehoshua resolves the question by citing the verse: *hi toeva* — she is an abomination. The word *hi* — "she" — is exclusive: *hi toeva, ve'ein baneha toevim* — she is the abomination, but her children are not. The child bears no disqualified status. The *Tosafot* note a broader question lurking here: why do we assume with certainty that the woman herself does not become *mitchaleles* and forbidden to *kohanim*? The answer is precisely that this prohibition is not a *kehunah*-specific prohibition. The entire mechanism of *challalah* — whereby both the woman and her offspring are affected — operates only within the domain of prohibitions unique to *kohanim*. Since this remarriage prohibition applies universally to all Jewish men, neither the woman nor her child falls into the *challalah* framework. The *pasuk*'s emphasis on *hi* confirms this: the Torah marks the act as personally blameworthy for her, but the consequences do not extend to her offspring.

### Q3: Mixed Korbanot

The third *halachic* question: *shnei metzoraim shenitarvu korbanoteihem zeh bazeh* — two *metzoraim* whose *korbanot* became mixed up with one another. A *metzora* cannot complete his purification process without bringing his designated *korbanot*. If their animals became intermingled so that neither can identify which animal belongs to whom, and in the meantime one of them dies, the surviving *metzora* faces an apparently insoluble problem. He cannot bring both animals, because one of them might be a *chatas shemeisu ba'aleha* — a *chatas* whose owner has died — and such a *chatas* cannot be offered. Yet he cannot simply forgo the *chatas*, because without it he remains a *metzora*. And he cannot bring a *chatas* voluntarily as a *nedavah*, since voluntary *chataot* are not permitted.

### Impoverishment Solution

Rabbi Yehoshua's answer is striking: *kosvin nichseichem le'acheirim* — write over all your property to someone else, make yourself legally poor, *v'havei ani*, and then *meivi chatas ha'of haba'ah al hasafek* — bring a bird *chatas* that is brought even in a case of *safek*. A poor *metzora* brings a bird *chatas* rather than an animal *chatas*, and uniquely — as discussed elsewhere in this tractate in the context of a *yoledet* — a bird *chatas* may be brought even when one is uncertain whether the obligation applies. By impoverishing himself, the *metzora* converts the nature of his *chatas* obligation, enabling him to bring it despite the uncertainty about whose animal was already offered.

### Status Determination

The *gemara* presses: but what about the *asham*? The purification of a *metzora* also requires an *asham*, and the blood of the *asham* is applied to the *metzora*'s ear, thumb, and toe as part of the purification rite. An *asham* cannot be brought on a *safek*, and it is an animal offering — it cannot be converted into a bird offering the way the *chatas* can.

Shmuel's answer: *keshekarav ashamo* — the *asham* was already brought beforehand; all that remains is the *chatas*.

Rav Sheshes challenges this sharply: *gavra raba kishmuel leima hachi* — could a person as great as Shmuel give such an answer? To appreciate the challenge, one must understand the debate about *when* a *metzora*'s status as rich or poor is determined for purposes of *korbanot*. There is a *mishnah* that records a three-way dispute. Rabbi Shimon holds that it is determined at the time of the *chatas*. Rabbi Yehudah holds that it is determined at the time of the *asham*. Rabbi Eliezer holds that it is determined at the time of the birds — the ceremony performed a week before the *korbanot* are brought, when two birds are dipped in blood and one is set free.

If the status is fixed at the time of the *asham* — as Rabbi Yehudah holds — then it is too late to impoverish oneself once the *asham* has been brought. The fact that the *asham* was brought early provides no advantage; the rich status was locked in at that moment, and the *chatas* must therefore be brought as a *chatas* of a wealthy person — which the uncertainty prevents. Shmuel's answer only works if the status has already been fixed before the person impoverishes himself, which means Rabbi Yehudah's position presents a serious obstacle.

### Stipulation Option

Rabbi Shimon's view, however, offers a simpler path. If the status is determined at the time of the *chatas*, and the *chatas* has not yet been brought, then one can still impoverish oneself beforehand and bring the bird *chatas* on *safek*. Moreover — and this is the point the *gemara* now develops — according to Rabbi Shimon there is no need to have already brought the *asham* at all, because Rabbi Shimon holds that one may bring an *asham* even under conditions of doubt, through a stipulation.

The procedure Rabbi Shimon describes: the *metzora* brings his *asham* along with its *log* of oil and stands at *sha'ar nikanor* — the gate of Nicanor, at the threshold of the *azarah*, which is as far as a *metzora* may enter — and stipulates: if I am truly a *metzora* obligated to bring this offering, then this is my *asham* with its *log*; if I am not obligated, then let this animal be a *shelamim nedavah*. This stipulation is possible with an *asham* but not with a *chatas*, because a *chatas* and a *shelamim* have incompatible blood rites — a *chatas* requires application of blood to the upper corners of the *mizbeach*, while a *shelamim* requires the lower corners — whereas an *asham* and a *shelamim* share compatible rites, can both be male animals, and both have their blood applied in the same location. The animal is thus brought conditionally as either an *asham* or a *shelamim*.

Since the animal might be either, it must be treated with maximum stringency in both directions simultaneously. It is slaughtered in the north of the *azarah* — as required for *kodshei kodashim*, which the *asham* is — and the blood is applied to the *metzora*'s ear, thumb, and toe, as part of the purification rite. At the same time, as a possible *shelamim*, it requires *semichah* (the laying of hands by the owner), *nesachim* (libations), and *tenufat chazeh va'shok* (waving of the breast and thigh). Regarding the requirement of *semichah* on this offering: Rashi holds that the *semichah* here is required only because of the possible *shelamim* status, since an *asham* does not require *semichah*, while Tosafot disagrees and holds that *semichah* is performed on an *asham* as well — though the *asham metzora* may have its own particular question in this regard. As for *nesachim*, Tosafot notes that the *asham metzora* itself requires *nesachim* regardless, so in this case the requirement applies whether the animal is the *asham* or the *shelamim*.

The animal is eaten only by male *kohanim* and only for one day and night — applying the more stringent rules of the *asham* rather than the two-day eating period of a *shelamim*. The *chachamim* did not accept Rabbi Shimon's ruling, however, for a specific reason: *mipnei shemeivi kodashim l'beit hapesul* — because this approach risks bringing *kodashim* to a state of disqualification. If the animal is in fact a *shelamim*, it could rightfully be eaten by a wider group of people and for two days. By restricting it to male *kohanim* for one day, one may be causing the remainder to be destroyed prematurely, which is prohibited.

### Shmuel's Position

The *gemara* thus concludes: *Shmuel savar lah k'Rabbi Shimon b'chada u'plig alei b'chada* — Shmuel agreed with Rabbi Shimon in one respect but disagreed in another. Shmuel agreed with Rabbi Shimon that the status of rich or poor is determined at the time of the *chatas*, meaning one can still impoverish oneself and bring the bird *chatas* on *safek*. But Shmuel did not accept Rabbi Shimon's view that an *asham* can be brought conditionally through stipulation — he did not rule in accordance with Rabbi Shimon's *takanah* for the *asham*. Therefore, Shmuel could not rely on the stipulation mechanism for the *asham*, and was compelled to say that the *asham* had already been brought beforehand, leaving only the *chatas* unresolved.

## Agada Questions

### God's Will Sinners

The *gemara* now turns to the three questions of *aggadah*. The first: one *pasuk* says *ki lo achpotz b'mot hamet* — I do not desire the death of the sinner — while another *pasuk* says *ki chafetz Hashem lahamitam* — God desired to kill them. Does God wish for sinners to die or not?

The answer given is: *kan b'osim teshuvah kan b'she'einam osim teshuvah* — the first verse refers to those who do *teshuvah*, and the second to those who do not. One might prefer to read *osim teshuvah* not merely as having already repented, but as encompassing those for whom repentance remains an open possibility — since, as we declare on *Rosh Hashanah* and *Yom Kippur*, God's desire is that people should live and that the door to *teshuvah* should remain open. Only when it is definitively foreclosed does the second verse apply.

### Favor vs. Justice

The second question: one *pasuk* says *ki lo yisa fanim v'lo yikach shochad* — God does not show favor or accept bribes — while another *pasuk* says *yisa Hashem panav elecha* — may God lift His face toward you. Does God show favor or not?

Tosafot note that this is actually not as sharp a contradiction as it might appear. The second verse does not say that God lifts *your* face — the idiom for showing favoritism — but rather that God lifts *His own* face toward you, suggesting warmth and attentiveness rather than partiality. One could cite other verses that pose the tension more sharply. Nevertheless, the *gemara* answers.

### Decree Timing

The resolution is: *kan kodem gzar din kan l'achar gzar din* — the first verse applies after the judicial decree has been sealed, at which point strict justice prevails, while the second verse applies before the decree is sealed, when God may show leniency and overlook certain things. Tosafot explore the interplay between this answer and the previous one about *teshuvah*: does *teshuvah* remain effective after *gzar din*? If it does, then God is still showing a form of favor even after the decree — which might seem to contradict the first verse. If *teshuvah* no longer avails after *gzar din*, that raises its own profound difficulties. Tosafot draw on other *gemarot* to navigate these questions, though a full treatment of that discussion would require further time.

### Jerusalem's Fate

The third *aggadic* question is particularly resonant in the context of *Tisha B'Av*: one verse says *ki bachar Hashem b'Tzion* — God has chosen Zion — while another verse in *Yirmeyahu* states that from the very day the city was built, it was a source of divine anger: *ki al api v'al chamati hayta li ha'ir hazot min hayom asher banu otah*. If God was planning the city's destruction from the day it was built, in what sense did He choose it?

### Chosenness Timing

The *gemara* answers: *kan kodem shensa Shlomo et bat Paroh kan l'achar shensa Shlomo et bat Paroh* — one verse refers to the period before Solomon married Pharaoh's daughter, and the other to the period after. The city was genuinely chosen during the era of David and the early reign of Solomon. But when Solomon married Pharaoh's daughter, this was a sign — or perhaps the turning point — marking the decline of the religious and moral standard that was supposed to characterize the Jewish commonwealth. From that moment, the trajectory toward destruction was set.

This is a weighty answer. The verse in *Yirmeyahu* speaks of anger *from the day it was built*, which is not precisely the same as the day Solomon married Pharaoh's daughter. The verse is read as referring broadly to the period surrounding the Temple's construction and dedication. But the underlying theological point is stark: the chosenness was real but conditional, and was compromised from nearly the moment of its establishment.

The questions themselves, taken together, are not merely exercises in resolving textual contradictions. They reflect genuine philosophical concerns — how does one integrate the experience of destruction and exile with a belief in chosenness? How does divine justice relate to divine compassion? These are not merely *pilpul*; they are the questions of a Hellenized Jewish community trying to make sense of Jewish theology, and they remain as live today as they were in Alexandria.

## Foolish Questions

### Lot's Wife

The three *divrei borot* — questions Rabbi Yehoshua considered foolish — follow next. The first: *ishto shel Lot mahu she-titameh* — does the pillar of salt that was once Lot's wife convey *tumat met*? Rabbi Yehoshua answers: *met metameh ve'ein netsiv melach metameh* — a corpse conveys *tumah*, but a pillar of salt does not. Underlying this, of course, is a profound question about transformation: at what point has something undergone sufficient change that it is no longer considered the same entity as the original? The *gemara*'s dismissal of the question as foolish suggests that the transformation into salt is understood to be total and irreversible.

### Resurrected Son

The second question: *ben haShunamit mahu she-yitameh* — does the son of the Shunamite, who was brought back to life by Elisha, cause *tumah*? Is he a kind of living corpse — a zombie, as it were? Rabbi Yehoshua answers: *met metameh ve'ein chai metameh* — a corpse causes *tumah*, but a living person does not. He is alive; the question dissolves. One of the *mefarshim* adds a nuance: although the son himself is not a *tamei* corpse, those who were present in the room when he was dead would have contracted *tumat ohel*. And indeed the question of whether the son himself, having been dead, might carry some residual *tumah* from his own prior state of death is one some *mefarshim* consider.

The question touches on a genuinely important issue in medical ethics as well. The concept of *techiyat hametim* — resurrection of the dead — intersects with the halachic question of clinical death. If a person is halachically dead but resuscitation is possible, is there an obligation to resuscitate? Is that obligation subsumed under *pikuach nefesh*, even when one might technically define the person as already dead? Does resuscitation constitute, in a sense, performing *techiyat hametim*? These are questions the responsa literature has engaged seriously.

### Future Resurrection

The third question: *metim le'atid lavo tsrichim haza'ah shelishi ve-shvi'i o ein tsrichim* — when the dead are resurrected in the future, will they require sprinkling of the *mei chattat* — the purification waters made from the ashes of the *parah adumah* — on the third and seventh days, as would ordinarily be required for one who contracted *tumat met*? They have, after all, been dead.

### Moshe's Answer

Rabbi Yehoshua's response: *likshe-yichyu ne-chakem lahem* — when they come back to life, we will figure it out then. In an alternate version, the response is more pointed: *likshe-yavo Moshe Rabbeinu imahem* — when that happens, Moshe himself will have come back to life along with them; we can ask him. The sarcasm is unmistakable, and it is worth noting that Rabbi Yehoshua was among the more rationalist of the *tannaim*. One can appreciate his impatience with questions about hypothetical future scenarios involving *techiyat hametim*. At the same time, it is fair to ask why these questions are classified as *divrei borot* — the question about the resurrection at least has some internal logic, even if it anticipates a reality not yet arrived.

## Practical Questions

### Wisdom

The final three questions, the *divrei derech eretz*, concern practical paths to wisdom, wealth, and male children. The first: *mah ya'aseh adam veyichkam* — what should a person do if they wish to become wise?

### Wisdom Balance

Rabbi Yehoshua answers: *yirbeh b'yeshivah v'yam'it b'sechorah* — spend much time sitting and learning, and minimize time spent running after business. The people of Alexandria respond: *harbeh asu chen v'lo ho'il lahem* — many have tried that and it did not succeed for them. The response — whether from Rabbi Yehoshua or from them, the attribution is not entirely clear — is: *yivakshu rachamim mimi shehachochmah shelo* — they should seek divine mercy from the One to Whom all wisdom belongs, as the verse says: *ki Hashem yiten chochmah mipiv da'at u'tvunah* — God grants wisdom; from His mouth come knowledge and understanding.

### King Mashal

Rabbi Chiyya adds a *mashal*: a mortal king who makes a feast for his servants sends as a gift to those he loves from the very food before him. So too, if one is beloved of God, the gift of wisdom flows from what is before God Himself. The *gemara* then asks: if wisdom ultimately comes from prayer, why emphasize sitting and learning? *D'ha b'lo ha lo sagi* — because one cannot have one without the other. Sitting and learning without praying will not produce wisdom; praying without sitting and learning is equally insufficient. Perhaps the *mashal* of the king's gift adds a further dimension: the very act of dedicating oneself to Torah study demonstrates the love and relationship that makes one worthy of receiving the divine gift. It is not merely a practical step — do your part, God does His — but rather the disposition that constitutes one as the *ohev* worthy of this blessing.

### Wealth

The second question: *mah ya'aseh adam veyit'asher* — what should a person do to become wealthy? Rabbi Yehoshua answers: *yirbeh b'sechorah v'yisa v'yiten be'emunah* — engage extensively in commerce, and conduct business with integrity and honesty.

### Wealth Balance

Again the rejoinder: *harbeh asu chen v'lo ho'il lahem* — many tried this and did not succeed. Again the answer: *yivakesh rachamim mimi she'ha'osher shelo* — one must seek mercy from the One to Whom all wealth belongs, as the verse says: *li hakesef v'li hazahav* — to Me is the silver and the gold. And again: *d'ha b'lo ha lo sagi* — neither alone is sufficient.

### Hagra Emendation

Interestingly, the *Hagra* removes *yirbeh b'sechorah* from the text. His reasoning is that extensive involvement in commerce necessarily comes at the expense of *talmud Torah*. In the *Hagra*'s view — one that reflects his own almost total dedication to Torah study — it is possible to earn a livelihood and conduct business with integrity without needing to maximize commercial activity. The *yirbeh b'sechorah* is an accommodation to a worldly path; the *Hagra* preferred to read the text without it, leaving the emphasis entirely on honesty in one's dealings and prayer. Whether this reflects a different manuscript tradition or the *Hagra*'s own intuition about the correct text is not clear.

### Male Children

The third question: *mah ya'aseh adam veyihyu lo banim zacharim* — what should a person do to merit male children? This was, of course, the universal aspiration of the ancient world.

### Children Balance

Rabbi Yehoshua answers: *yisa ishah hahogenet lo* — he should marry a woman who is fitting and suited to him — *v'yikadesh atzmo b'sha'at tashmish* — and he should sanctify himself at the moment of marital relations. Rashi explains *yikadesh atzmo* as *leshammesh b'tzniyut* — to conduct oneself with modesty during the act. The exact meaning of this instruction in light of the *gemara*'s broader discussions about marital relations is a subject of larger discussion.

### Wife's Pleasure

Once again: many tried this without success; one must also seek mercy from God, as the verse says: *hinei nachalat Hashem banim, s'char pri habaten* — sons are an inheritance of God, the fruit of the womb is a reward. And once again: *d'ha b'lo ha lo sagi*.

The *gemara* then closes with a *derasha* that takes the final verse in a different direction. What is the *s'char pri habaten* — the reward of the fruit of the womb? Rabbi Chama son of Rabbi Chanina expounds: *bis'char shemash'hin atzman baben kedei shetazria ishto techilah* — the reward is given to those husbands who hold themselves back during marital relations in order that their wives should experience pleasure and reach her release first. The verse's language of *s'char* — reward — is read as pointing to the husband's act of restraint and consideration. One who ensures his wife's pleasure first is rewarded by God with the blessing of children. The tractate ends on this note.
