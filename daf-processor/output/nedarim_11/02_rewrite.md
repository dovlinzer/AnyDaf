# Nedarim 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kinnuyim Framework

### Kinnuyim Definition

The eleventh daf of Nedarim is brief in terms of raw text — as the lecturer observes, the entire daf amounts to only a handful of sentences — yet it demands close attention to the logic of vow-language. To orient ourselves, we return briefly to the *mishna* at the bottom of 10b, which introduces the category of *kinnuyim*. Unlike the concept of *yad*, which involves filling in a blank where part of a sentence has been left incomplete, *kinnuyim* operates differently: the sentence is formally complete, but we must determine what certain words mean — that is, what particular terms stand in for. The first category of *kinnuyim* explored earlier in the tractate involved phonetic variants of the word *konam* (*konas*, *konach*, and similar forms). Here we encounter a different type: words that are not variants of *konam* at all, but rather serve as stand-ins for the word *korban* itself.

### Mishna Categories

The *mishna* introduces the formula *ha'omer lachullin she'ochal lach* — "one who says: that which I eat of yours is *lachullin*." The word *lachullin* is a Palestinian Aramaic construction meaning "non-*chulin*," i.e., non-secular, and thus the statement is interpreted to mean that what he eats is *hekdesh*. The *mishna* continues: *lo kasher velo dachi* — "not *kasher*" or "not pure (*tahor*)" — noting that some have the variant reading *lo tahor*. These formulations are surprising: *kasher* and *tahor* are not inherently associated with *hekdesh*, and *tamei* does not exclusively describe *korban* status. Nevertheless, the principle of *stam nedarim l'hachmir* — that ambiguous vow-language is interpreted stringently — leads us to read these terms as invoking *korban* status. More directly, *tamei*, *notar*, and *pigul* are explicitly listed as forbidden formulas, since *notar* and *pigul* occur only in the context of *korbanot*, and even *tamei*, though applicable elsewhere, is understood here as invoking the *issur* status of a *korban*.

### Altar Associations

The *mishna* then lists a further set of expressions: *ke'imra* (like a sheep), *kedirim* (like the pens), *ke'etzim* (like the wood), *ke'ishim* (like the fires on the altar), *ke'mizbeach* (like the altar), *ke'heichal* (like the sanctuary), *ke'yerushalayim* (like Jerusalem). None of these objects is itself a *davar hanadur* in the primary sense — one does not vow the pens or Jerusalem itself — but all are associated with the *korbanot* and the *mizbeiach*, and thus the *mishna* rules that *neder be'achad mikol hameshamshim mizbeach*, a vow invoking any object that serves the altar, even without explicit mention of *korban*, constitutes a binding vow as if *korban* had been stated.

### Yerushalayim Debate

The *mishna* concludes with a named position: *Rabbi Yehudah omer ha'omer Yerushalayim lo amar klum* — Rabbi Yehudah holds that one who simply says "Yerushalayim" has said nothing of legal consequence. The question raised immediately is whether Rabbi Yehudah is rejecting the formula *ke'Yerushalayim* as well, or whether his objection is specifically to the bare form "Yerushalayim" without the comparative prefix *ke*. The *Gemara* will clarify this distinction in the discussion that follows.

## Miklal Lav Analysis

### Miklal Lav Assumption

The *Gemara* opens its analysis with the word *savruha* — "they assumed" — signaling a preliminary interpretation that may subsequently be revised. The working assumption is that *lachullin* operates via the principle of *miklal lav ata shomea hen*: from a negative statement one infers its positive counterpart. On this reading, saying "what I eat of yours is non-*chulin*" invokes, by logical inference, the positive status of *korban*, thereby rendering the food forbidden.

### Rabbi Meir Principle

The *Gemara* immediately raises a problem: if this is the mechanism, then whose position does the *mishna* reflect? If it is Rabbi Meir's, there is a difficulty — for Rabbi Meir explicitly rejects the principle of *miklal lav ata shomea hen*. The *Gemara* cites the relevant *mishna*: *Rabbi Meir omer kol tnai she'eino k'tnai B'nei Gad u'B'nei Reuven eino tnai* — any conditional stipulation not formulated like the condition of the tribes of Gad and Reuven is not a valid *tnai*.

### Bnei Gad Example

What was unique about the formulation in that episode? The Torah stated both sides of the condition explicitly: if they cross over with you, they will receive their inheritance on the eastern bank; and if they do not cross over, they will not receive it there. Rabbi Meir's position is that you cannot rely on one side of a conditional to imply the other. One might ask: is the Torah not simply being explicit about something logically obvious? Rabbi Meir's answer is precisely the opposite — the Torah states both sides *because* one side does not logically entail the other. The explicit statement of both halves demonstrates that without such explicit formulation, we may not infer the reverse.

### Rabbi Meir Logic

There are two distinct ways to understand Rabbi Meir's position. The first is that this is fundamentally a requirement of *speech acts*: regardless of what is logically deducible, the rules governing *tnai* or *neder* require that both sides be explicitly articulated. Perhaps in ordinary matters of intent (*da'as*) an implicit understanding would suffice, but a *tnai* is not merely a question of subjective intent. A *tnai* is an artificial legal mechanism — distinct from a case where someone simply never would have entered an agreement under certain circumstances — and such a mechanism demands precise verbal formulation. Similarly with *nedarim*: it is not what is in one's mind that counts, but what one actually said. The second way to understand Rabbi Meir goes further: he may be making a claim about logic itself.

### Propositional Logic

Anyone familiar with basic propositional logic will recognize that "if P then Q" does not entail "if not P then not Q." If it rains I will give you ten dollars — but even if it doesn't rain, perhaps I will still give you ten dollars. The only valid inference from "if P then Q" is "if not Q then not P." Thus, saying that if the tribes cross over they will receive their inheritance in *Ever HaYarden* does not logically imply that if they do not cross over they will forfeit it. There may be other grounds on which they would receive or not receive. On this reading, Rabbi Meir's objection is not merely procedural but substantive: the reverse proposition is simply not derivable from the original statement, and therefore one who says only "if P then Q" has not legally committed to "if not P then not Q." Applied to *nedarim*: saying "the food I would eat of yours is *chulin*" does not logically entail that the food is *korban*, since "not *chulin*" does not necessarily mean *korban* by logical necessity alone.

## Yad vs. Kinnuy

### Mishnah Attribution

Given that the *Gemara* has established that if *lachullin* works through *miklal lav*, the *mishna* cannot reflect Rabbi Meir's position, the *Gemara* initially suggests the *mishna* must follow Rabbi Yehudah — who does accept *miklal lav ata shomea hen*. But this creates a new problem: the *mishna* concludes with Rabbi Yehudah's own distinct ruling (*Rabbi Yehudah omer ha'omer Yerushalayim lo amar klum*), which implies that the rest of the *mishna* is not Rabbi Yehudah. Citing the end of the *mishna* as Rabbi Yehudah necessarily implies the beginning is not — a standard Talmudic inference (*mideseifa Rabbi Yehudah, reisha lav Rabbi Yehudah*).

### Tosafot Rid Position

This is precisely where the *Tosafot Rid* enters with a far-reaching observation. The initial assumption of the *Gemara* — the *savruha* — that *lachullin* works via *miklal lav* is, according to the *Tosafot Rid*, fundamentally mistaken, and this is the very assumption the *Gemara* ultimately rejects (though the exact point of rejection is debated). The *Tosafot Rid* argues that saying "this object is *lachullin*" is not a conditional inference at all. It is a direct description: the word *lachullin* is simply a synonym, or *kinnuy*, for *hekdesh*. If I say "you are impure," I am not inferring anything — I am saying you are *tamei*. The word "impure" means *tamei*; it does not mean "not pure" in the sense of a logical negation from which the positive must be inferred. Similarly, *lachullin* — non-*chulin* — simply means *korban*, and this has nothing to do with Rabbi Meir's principle.

### Yad Kinnuy Distinction

This analysis illuminates a deeper distinction: the difference between *yad* and *kinnuy*. A *yad* arises when a sentence is grammatically incomplete and something must be supplied to fill a blank; it is a kind of literary ellipsis. A *kinnuy* arises when the sentence is formally complete but a particular word requires interpretation — we must determine what that word stands for. When the *mishna* lists *tamei*, *notar*, *pigul*, *ke'imra*, *ke'mizbeach*, and *ke'Yerushalayim*, it is plainly operating in the register of *kinnuy*: each of these is a stand-in word for *korban*. Similarly, *lachullin* is a stand-in for *korban*. The person uttered a full sentence; the task is to decode what the word means.

This is why it is, in truth, strange that the *Gemara* introduced *miklal lav ata shomea hen* at all in this context — it is a principle about conditional propositions, not about vocabulary. If I call something "like the pen of sheep" (*kedirim*), I am not making a conditional statement; I am naming the object with a certain term. We do not say this involves *miklal lav*. The same logic applies to *lachullin*. The *savruha* imported a logical framework that does not naturally fit the category of *kinnuyim*, and the *Tosafot Rid* argues this is precisely what must be abandoned as the *sugya* proceeds.

### Ran's Categories

The *Ran* offers a complementary analysis that explains another structural feature of the *mishna*: why some *kinnuyim* require the prefix *ke* (like) and others do not. His answer concerns what each expression is actually doing linguistically. Terms like *tamei*, *notar*, and *pigul* foreground the *issur* status of the object itself — they describe it as having the identity of a forbidden *korban* substance. These terms highlight the halachic character of the thing being vowed, making the sentence meaningful without any comparative prefix.

### Ki Necessity

By contrast, when one says "this is *mizbeach*" or "this is Yerushalayim," those expressions do not themselves identify the object as a *davar assur*. If I say "this loaf of bread is the *mizbeiach*," the sentence is meaningless — bread is obviously not the altar. But if I say "this bread is *ke'mizbeiach*" — like the altar — I am drawing an associative comparison: I am relating this bread to the concept of things brought on the *mizbeiach*, and within that conceptual framework the bread acquires the status of a *davar hanadur*. The *ke* prefix creates the necessary semantic bridge. Without it, when the reference object is something like Yerushalayim or a sheep-pen, the statement is simply incoherent as a vow. This is exactly what Rabbi Yehudah is saying when he rules that *Yerushalayim* alone — without the *ke* — is ineffective: the bare word creates no meaningful associative link.

## Ki Yerushalayim

### Mishnah Attribution

With this framework in place, the *Gemara* resolves the authorship question by asserting *kulah Rabbi Yehudah hi* — the entire *mishna* is Rabbi Yehudah's position. Rabbi Yehudah is not arguing with the body of the *mishna*; he is adding a specification to it.

### Yerushalayim Distinction

Specifically, Rabbi Yehudah draws a line between *ke'Yerushalayim* and bare *Yerushalayim*. The earlier portion of the *mishna*, which lists *ke'Yerushalayim* among the valid *kinnuyim*, is Rabbi Yehudah's own ruling — *ke'Yerushalayim* works. What Rabbi Yehudah then adds, in the clause attributed to him, is that *Yerushalayim* without the *ke* does not work.

### Yerushalayim Scope

The reason is not arbitrary. *Ke'Yerushalayim* works because it directs the listener toward Yerushalayim's identity as the site of *korbanot* — the comparative form channels the association toward the *Beis HaMikdash* and the sacrificial service. But the bare word *Yerushalayim* encompasses an enormous range of things: the Knesset, museums, the Biblical Zoo, countless institutions and objects that have no connection to *korbanot* whatsoever. Without the *ke* to focus the comparison, saying "Yerushalayim" does not reliably invoke *korban* identity, and the vow is therefore without legal effect.

## Ki Yerushalayim…

### Braita Strictness

The *Gemara* then introduces a *braita* which presents an even stricter position: *Rabbi Yehudah omer ha'omer ki'Yerushalayim lo amar klum* — even *ke'Yerushalayim* is ineffective — *ad sheyidor bidvar hakorev biYerushalayim*, unless one explicitly vows by something actually offered in Yerushalayim. Even the comparative prefix does not suffice when the reference point is Yerushalayim itself, which remains too diffuse. The *Gemara* resolves the tension between this *braita* and the *mishna* by invoking *trei tanna'ei aliba d'Rabbi Yehudah* — there are two distinct traditions within Rabbi Yehudah's school, one of which holds that *ke'Yerushalayim* suffices and one that does not.

## Conditional Phrasing

### Permissible Formulas

The *Gemara* now turns to a *braita* that analyzes several formulations involving the word *chulin* itself. The *braita* rules that *chulin*, *hachulin*, and *kachulin* — whether one says "secular," "the secular," or "like the secular" — are all non-binding formulations. And this applies whether the person says *she'ochal lach* (that which I eat of yours is *chulin*) or *shelo ochal lach* (that which I do not eat of yours is *chulin*). These formulas are *mutar*.

### Forbidden Formula

By contrast, the *braita* rules that *lachullin she'ochal lach* — the food I eat of yours is *lachullin* — is *assur*. Here the prefix *la* transforms the word: *lachullin* means non-*chulin*, i.e., *korban*, and declaring one's food to be *lachullin* is therefore equivalent to declaring it *hekdesh*.

### Tosafot Rid Reversal

It is here that the *Tosafot Rid* locates the reversal of the *Gemara*'s opening assumption. The *braita* clearly demonstrates that *lachullin* is simply a word meaning *hekdesh* — a definitional synonym, not a logical inference. Even Rabbi Meir, who rejects *miklal lav ata shomea hen*, can accept that *lachullin* renders the food forbidden, because no conditional reasoning is involved. The word *lachullin* directly names the object as *korban*, just as the word "impure" directly names something as *tamei* without requiring any inferential step. The *Tosafot Rid* concludes that the *mishna* can therefore reflect Rabbi Meir's position after all, and the *savruha* at the top of 11a should be entirely abandoned. For completeness: other *rishonim* who do not accept the *Tosafot Rid*'s approach resort to more strained readings — one excises the problematic phrase from the text entirely, and the *Ra'avad* eliminates the *vav* and reads the formula as *le-challin*, i.e., *challot todah* (loaves of the thanksgiving offering), an interpretation the lecturer candidly characterizes as extremely forced.

### Conditional vs. Direct

The *Gemara*'s structure at this point becomes clearer when we attend to the precise mechanism at work in each formula. The cases of *chulin lo ochal lach* and *shelo ochal lach* that are ruled *mutar* both involve a kind of conditional or inferential structure: "your non-food is *chulin*" might seem to imply, via *miklal lav*, that your food is *korban* — but Rabbi Meir's principle blocks that inference, and therefore those formulas are *mutar*. By contrast, *lachullin she'ochal lach* is not a conditional statement at all. It directly describes the food as non-*chulin*, and non-*chulin* simply is *korban*. No inference is required or invoked.

### Permitted Formula

The third formula in the *braita* — *lachullin lo ochal lach*, "that which I will not eat of yours is *lachullin*" — is ruled *mutar*. Why? Because even granting that *lachullin* means *korban*, the person has not declared any specific food to be forbidden. They have said that the things they do not eat are *lachullin* — but this does not render the food in front of them *assur*. The statement does not focus on the *davar hanadur* in any meaningful sense; at most it refers abstractly to non-food objects or to the act of non-eating, neither of which constitutes a binding *neder*. The *Gemara* eventually clarifies, via the analysis of *she-ani* versus *she-eini*, that a valid *lashon neder* must attach to the object itself, not to the speaker's action.

## Le-Korban Strategy

### Le-Korban Mishna

The *Gemara* now introduces a later *mishna* presenting a parallel case: *le-korban lo ochal lach* — "that which I will not eat of yours is *le-korban*" — and records that Rabbi Meir rules this *assur*. This is initially puzzling. If Rabbi Meir rejects *miklal lav ata shomea hen*, why should declaring one's non-food to be *le-korban* render anything forbidden? The apparent reasoning would be: "my non-food is *korban*," therefore "my food is not *korban*" — but that is precisely the inference Rabbi Meir claims cannot be drawn.

### Rav Abba Reinterpretation

The *Gemara* resolves this through Rav Abba's reading: *na'aseh k'omer le-korban yehei, lefikhach lo ochal lach* — we interpret the person as having said "let it be a *korban*; therefore I will not eat it." On this reading, *le-korban* is parsed as *lekorban* — it should become a *korban* — and *lo ochal lach* is a natural consequence: since it is now a *korban*, I will not eat it. This is standard *konam* language: the speaker declares the object a *korban* and states the practical upshot. There is no *miklal lav* involved; the person simply said *konam* by another route, and even Rabbi Meir accepts this.

### Parallel Question

The *Gemara* then asks the obvious parallel question: if *lachullin* means *korban*, then *lachullin lo ochal lach* should be identical to *le-korban lo ochal lach*. Why, then, does the *braita* rule *lachullin lo ochal lach* as *mutar*? We should be able to apply the same parsing strategy — "it is *lachullin* (i.e., a *korban*), therefore I will not eat it" — and rule it *assur*.

### Braita Author Position

The *Gemara* answers with a principle of partial agreement: *hai tanna savar lah ke-Rabbi Meir b'chada u'palig leh b'chada* — the author of this *braita* agrees with Rabbi Meir in one respect but disagrees in another. The *braita* follows Rabbi Meir in rejecting *miklal lav ata shomea hen* — that is the basis for ruling the *chulin lo ochal lach* formulas *mutar*. But the *braita* does not follow the reading of the later *mishna* in which we parse the sentence as "it is a *korban*, therefore I will not eat it." Without that interpretive move, *le-korban lo ochal lach* would simply mean "my non-food is a *korban*," which says nothing about the food in front of the person and therefore carries no binding force.

### Abstract Concept

With this established, the *Gemara* can explain why *lachullin lo ochal lach* is *mutar* for this *tanna*. The sentence, read straightforwardly and without the "therefore" parsing, declares that the things one does not eat are *lachullin* — i.e., *korban*. But this refers to no concrete food object. The statement is, in effect, abstract — it either refers to non-food items or to the concept of non-eating, neither of which constitutes a *davar hanadur* with any practical force. Since a valid *neder* must attach to a real object that one would otherwise eat, this formula leaves all food *mutar*.

## She-Ani Debate

### Scope Examples

A practical illustration helps clarify the underlying principle. One cannot make one's computer or one's car forbidden through the language of *nedarim*, because these are not foods — there is no relationship of eating to be governed. The language of a *neder* must address an object that is in the domain of eating, not arbitrary items in the world.

### She-Ani Formula

This is why the classic formulation is *konam she-ani ochal lach* — "that which I would eat of yours is *konam*." The focus is on the food object: those things I would ordinarily eat are hereby declared *korban*. The second half of the statement ("therefore I will not eat") is not the *neder* itself but a natural description of its consequence. The *neder* is in the first clause, where the object is identified and its status declared.

### Object Focus

The principle that emerges is that *lashon neder* must focus on the object, not on the speaker's action. A *neder* is not a *shevuah*. A *shevuah* binds the person — "I swear I will not eat." A *neder* binds the object — "this food is *hekdesh* to me." If the language points to the speaker's action rather than to the object's status, it may constitute *lashon shevuah* at most, but it does not qualify as a *neder* in the technical sense.

### She-Eini Question

This principle generates a significant dispute among the *rishonim* concerning two variants of the same formula. *Konam she-ani ochal lach* — "that which I eat of yours is *konam*" — clearly refers to the food object, and is unambiguously a valid *neder*. But what of *konam she-eini ochal lach* — "that which I do not eat of yours is *konam*"? There is a *machloket rishonim* on this question. On one reading, this can still be parsed as "it is *konam*, therefore I will not eat it" — the *she-eini* being the consequence rather than the subject. On another reading, *she-eini ochal lach* is fundamentally a statement about what the speaker will do, not about what the object is, and therefore it does not constitute proper *lashon neder*. This maps directly onto the tension in the *Gemara* between the formula ruled *assur* in the later *mishna* (which requires the sentence-splitting interpretation) and the formula ruled *mutar* in the *braita* (which refuses to split the sentence and therefore reads everything as referring abstractly to the act of non-eating rather than to the food itself).

## Korban Statuses

### Status List

The *Gemara* now opens a new section of the *mishna*: *tahor v'tamai notar u'pigul assur* — one who declares food to be like that which is *tahor*, *tamei*, *notar*, or *pigul* has made a binding vow. The question is what, precisely, is being invoked by each of these terms.

### Shelamim Analogy

Rami bar Chama poses a pointed question: what is the status of one who says *harei alai ke-vasar zivchei shelamim le-achar zerikat damim* — "this [bread] should be upon me like the meat of *shelamim* offerings after the blood has been thrown"? The complication is that at the moment of the statement, the meat in question is in its permitted state — it is the very meat one is currently eating. Does this formulation function as a valid *kinnuy* for *korban*?

### Permitted State

The *Gemara* first clarifies the question. If the person explicitly stated the full formula — "like *shelamim* meat after blood-throwing" — they have clearly referenced the meat in its *mutar* state. Grabbing onto something in its permitted condition (*be-heteira kamatpis*) seems problematic as a basis for a *neder*: you have named a *davar hanadur* that is currently *mutar*, and a *davar hanadur* should be an object in a state of *issur*.

### Live Example

The *Gemara* therefore refines the scenario: imagine that someone is actively eating *shelamim* meat — the blood has been thrown, the meat is permitted, and the person is mid-meal — and another individual comes and places a piece of bread nearby, saying *ze ke-ze*: "this bread should be like this meat." What is the halachic status of the bread?

### Status Identity

This generates the *Gemara*'s central question: *be-ikaro kamatpis o be-heiter kamatpis* — is the person "grabbing onto" the original identity of the meat (as a *korban shelamim* prior to *zerikat damim*, when it was *assur*), or are they grabbing onto its current permitted status? The concept of *matpis* — latching the *neder* onto a pre-existing identified object to define the status of a new one — is crucial here. If the speaker intends to invoke the original *korban* identity of the meat, then this is a valid *kinnuy*: the bread is being declared like a *korban*, and the *neder* is binding. If the speaker is relating to the meat as it currently stands — a *mutar* food — then there is no *davar hanadur* with *issur* status onto which the *neder* can be anchored, and the *neder* would be ineffective. This question — whether *be-ikaro kamatpis* or *be-heiter kamatpis* — is left unresolved as the daf concludes, a fitting cliffhanger for a section whose every sentence proves inexhaustible.
