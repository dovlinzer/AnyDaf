# Sotah 46 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Equidistant Towns

### Body Equidistant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נִמְצָא מְכֻוּוֹן בֵּין שְׁתֵּי עֲיָירוֹת — שְׁתֵּיהֶן מְבִיאוֹת שְׁתֵּי עֲגָלוֹת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. וְאֵין יְרוּשָׁלָיִם מְבִיאָה עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה. נִמְצָא רֹאשׁוֹ בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד וְגוּפוֹ בְּמָקוֹם אַחֵר — מוֹלִיכִין הָרֹאשׁ אֵצֶל הַגּוּף, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: הַגּוּף אֵצֶל הָרֹאשׁ. מֵאַיִן הָיוּ מוֹדְדִין? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: מִטִּיבּוּרוֹ, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מֵחוֹטְמוֹ, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: מִמָּקוֹם שֶׁנַּעֲשֶׂה חָלָל, מִצַּוָּארוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If the slain person **is found precisely between two cities,** the inhabitants of **the two of them bring two heifers** total; this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer. And** the inhabitants of **Jerusalem do not bring a heifer whose neck is broken,** even if Jerusalem is the city closest to the slain victim. If the **head of** the corpse **was found in one place and his body** was found **in a different place, they bring the head next to the body;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer. Rabbi Akiva says:** They bring **the body next to the head. From where** on the body **would they measure** the distance? **Rabbi Eliezer says: From his navel. Rabbi Akiva says: From his nose. Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: From the place where he became a slain** person, which is **from the neck.**

The *mishnah* before us concerns the ritual of *egla arufa*, the broken-necked calf performed when an unidentified murder victim is found in an open field. The procedure requires the elders of the *beit din hagadol* in Yerushalayim to come out and measure which nearby town — specifically, which town possessing its own *beit din* — is closest to the corpse, and it is that town's elders who must carry out the ritual.

The *mishnah* addresses a striking edge case: *nimtza mechuvon bein shtei ayarot* — what if the body is found precisely equidistant between two towns?

### R. Eliezer: Two Calves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר — קָסָבַר אֶפְשָׁר לְצַמְצֵם, וּ״קְרוֹבָה״, וַאֲפִילּוּ קְרוֹבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara explains: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi Eliezer,** that when a body is found precisely between two cities, the inhabitants of each city bring a heifer? His ruling is based on two factors. First, **he holds** that **it is possible to measure precisely** and that it is a real possibility to determine that both cities are exactly the same distance from the corpse. **And** second, he interprets the term **"That is nearest"** (Deuteronomy 21:3), to be referring not only to one city. It can **even** be understood as: **That are nearest,** so that the *halakhot* apply to more than one city.

*Shteihen mevi'ot shtei agalot divrei Rabbi Eliezer* — Rabbi Eliezer rules that each town brings its own calf. The phrasing makes clear that it is not both towns jointly bringing two calves, but each town independently bringing one. It is worth noting that the *mishnah* here cites this as *divrei Rabbi Eliezer* without recording a dissenting view — a formulation that clearly implies the ruling was contested, even if the opposing opinion is not spelled out.

This ruling might seem strange. After all, there is only one body and, one might think, only one responsible town. One could have suggested that the two *batei din* cooperate in bringing a single calf with a *tenai* — a stipulation that if the body is closer to one town, the calf counts as that town's *egla*, and if closer to the other, it counts as the other's. Alternatively, one might have argued for complete exemption: since the verse specifies *ha'ir hakerova*, the nearby city (in the singular), perhaps when no single city qualifies as definitively nearest, the entire obligation lapses. Rabbi Eliezer rejects both options and requires two calves.

### Precise Measurement

Rabbi Eliezer holds that *efshar l'tzamtzem* — precise equidistance is a real and legally cognizable possibility. The position of *i'efshar l'tzamtzem* would hold that in principle one town must always be fractionally closer, and our inability to measure with sufficient precision merely creates a *safek*. On that view, you would indeed bring one calf with a *tenai*. But Rabbi Eliezer maintains that *efshar l'tzamtzem* — genuine equidistance can exist, and when it does, each town bears its own independent obligation.

As for the concern that the singular language of *ha'ir hakerova* might exempt the case of two towns entirely, the *gemara* dismisses this: *v'afillu krovot* — the word *kerova* is not understood as excluding the scenario of multiple equidistant towns. There is no textual basis for such an exclusion.

### Jerusalem Exempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין יְרוּשָׁלָיִם מְבִיאָה עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה — דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״לְרִשְׁתָּהּ״, וְקָסָבַר: יְרוּשָׁלַיִם לֹא נִתְחַלְּקָה לִשְׁבָטִים.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught: **And** the inhabitants of **Jerusalem do not bring a heifer whose neck is broken.** The Gemara explains: This is **because the verse states:** "If one be found slain in the land which the Lord your God has given **you to possess it"** (Deuteronomy 21:1), **and** this *tanna* **holds** that **Jerusalem was not divided among the tribes** in the division of Eretz Yisrael. It was not given as a possession to any particular person but belongs to all; therefore the *halakha* of the heifer whose neck is broken does not apply to it.

The *mishnah* also states that Yerushalayim does not bring an *egla arufa*, even if it happens to be the nearest city. The *gemara* explains: *de'amar kra lerishtah* — the verse ties the obligation to a city given as an inheritance to one of the tribes, and Yerushalayim was never apportioned to any individual tribe. It remained, in a sense, an inter-tribal city, and therefore does not fall within the category of *lerishtah*.

## Separated Head

### Head vs. Body

The *mishnah* continues with another unusual case: *nimtza rosho b'makom echad v'gufo b'makom acher* — the head of the corpse is found in one location and the body in another. Rabbi Eliezer rules *molichin et harosh etzel haguf* — you bring the head to the body. Rabbi Akiva rules the opposite: *haguf etzel harosh* — you bring the body to the head.

At first glance this dispute seems to replicate the later dispute at the end of the *mishnah* about where one measures from. Rabbi Eliezer, who focuses on the body, says one measures from the belly button; Rabbi Akiva, who focuses on the head, says one measures from the nostrils. The positions appear perfectly parallel. However, the fact that the *mishnah* presents them as two separate disputes suggests that the earlier dispute — about which part of the body you bring to which — is dealing with a distinct question.

### Met Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? אִילֵּימָא לְעִנְיַן מְדִידָה קָמִיפַּלְגִי, הָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא ״מֵאַיִן הָיוּ מוֹדְדִין״, מִכְּלָל דְּרֵישָׁא לָא בִּמְדִידָה עָסְקִינַן! אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: בְּמֵת מִצְוָה קָנָה מְקוֹמוֹ קָמִיפַּלְגִי, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: לְקוֹבְרוֹ קָנָה מְקוֹמוֹ, וְהֵיכָא דְּנִמְצָא רֹאשׁוֹ בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד וְגוּפוֹ בְּמָקוֹם אַחֵר — מוֹלִיכִין הָרֹאשׁ אֵצֶל הַגּוּף, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: הַגּוּף אֵצֶל הָרֹאשׁ. בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי — מָר סָבַר: גּוּפֵיהּ בְּדוּכְתֵּיהּ נָפֵיל, רֵישָׁא דְּנָאדֵי וְנָפֵיל, וּמָר סָבַר: רֵישָׁא הֵיכָא דְּנָפֵיל נָפֵיל, גּוּפָא הוּא דְּרָהֵיט אָזֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the *halakha* of a corpse whose **head was found in** one **place** and its body elsewhere, the Gemara asks: **With regard to what** *halakha* **do they disagree? If we say they disagree with regard to** whether the **measurement** is taken from the head or the body, **from** the fact **that the latter clause teaches: From where would they measure** the distance, **it may be inferred that** in **the first clause we are not dealing with measurement. Rabbi Yitzḥak said: They disagree with regard to** a different matter, the question of whether **a corpse with no one to bury it [*met mitzva*] acquires its place,** meaning if an unattended corpse must be buried where it is found. **And this is what** the mishna **is saying:** With regard **to burying him,** the victim **acquires his place,** and he is buried there. The mishna continues: **And** in a case **where his head is found in one place and his body** is found **in a different place, they bring the head next to the body** and bury him there; this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer. Rabbi Akiva says:** They bring **the body next to the head.** The Gemara explains: **With regard to what do they disagree?** They both agree that he should be buried in the place where he was killed, but one **Sage,** Rabbi Eliezer, **holds** that **his body fell in its place,** and it was **the head that rolled away and fell. And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Akiva, **holds** that **his head fell where it fell,** and **it was the body that went and continued** onward. Therefore, the body is brought to the head.

The *gemara* resolves the apparent duplication. Rabbi Yitzchak explains that the first dispute is actually about burial: *be'met mitzvah kanah mekomo* — a *met mitzvah*, a corpse with no one to bury it, acquires its place of rest where it lies. The question when the head and body are separated is therefore: to which location does the body "belong" for purposes of burial? Rabbi Eliezer says you bring the head to the body, since the body is buried where it is found. Rabbi Akiva says you bring the body to the head.

### Head or Body Moved

The *gemara* then asks: what is the underlying disagreement that drives these two rulings? *Mar savar gufa b'duchtei nafal reisha denada venafal* — one opinion holds that the body fell in place at the spot of the killing, and it was the head that flew off. Therefore the location of the body is the true location of the death, and the head must be brought there. *V'mar savar reisha heicha denafal nafal gufa hu derahit v'azal* — the other opinion holds that the head fell right where it was severed, while the body kept moving forward — as a body sometimes does when suddenly decapitated — before finally falling at a different spot. On this view, the head's location is the true locus of death.

### Measurement Points

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵאַיִן הָיוּ מוֹדְדִין. בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? מָר סָבַר: עִיקַּר חִיּוּתָא בְּאַפֵּיהּ, וּמָר סָבַר: עִיקַּר חִיּוּתָא בְּטִיבּוּרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that there is a dispute concerning the question: **From where** on the body **would they measure** the distance? The Gemara asks: **With regard to what do they disagree?** One **Sage,** Rabbi Akiva, **holds:** A person's **life is** sustained **mainly in his nose,** in his respiratory system. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Eliezer, **holds: His life is mainly in** the area of **his navel,** in his digestive system.

Turning to the second dispute, the *mishnah* presents three views on which part of the body to measure from when determining which town is nearest. Rabbi Eliezer says *mitiburo* — from the belly button. Rabbi Akiva says *mechotamo* — from the nostrils. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov says *mimakom shena'asah chalal* — from the place that made the person a *chalal*, a slain corpse — which he identifies as *mitzavaro*, the neck.

### Life Force

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כִּי הָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי: מֵהֵיכָן הַוָּלָד נוֹצָר — מֵרֹאשׁוֹ, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״מִמְּעֵי אִמִּי אַתָּה גוֹזִי״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״גׇּזִּי נִזְרֵךְ וְהַשְׁלִיכִי וְגוֹ׳״. אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: מִטִּיבּוּרוֹ, וּמְשַׁלֵּחַ שׇׁרְשׁוֹ אֵילָךְ. אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא אַבָּא שָׁאוּל, עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן יְצִירָה, דְּכִי מִיתְּצַר וְלָד מִמְּצִיעֲתֵיהּ מִיתְּצַר, אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן חִיּוּתָא — דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא בְּאַפֵּיהּ הוּא, דִּכְתִיב: ״כׇּל אֲשֶׁר נִשְׁמַת רוּחַ חַיִּים בְּאַפָּיו וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Shall we say that these *tanna'im* are like those *tanna'im*,** who had a dispute as it is taught in a *baraita*: **From where is an embryo formed? From its head, and so** the verse **states: "Out of my mother's womb You pulled me [*gozi*]"** (Psalms 71:6). **And** the proof that "*gozi*" is referring to the head is from the verse that **states: "Cut off [*gozi*] your hair, and cast it away"** (Jeremiah 7:29). In this verse, the term *gozi* relates to the hair of the head. **Abba Shaul says:** An embryo is formed **from its navel, and it sends its roots forth.** This dispute concerning the initial formation of an embryo also appears to depend on where the main source of life in a person is. **You** can **even say** that both *tanna'im* of the mishna agree with **Abba Shaul,** as **Abba Shaul says** his opinion **only with regard to the forming** of an embryo, **that when an embryo is formed,** it is **formed from its middle. But with regard to life, everyone,** i.e., both *tanna'im* in the *baraita*, agree that **it is in his nose, as it is written: "All in whose nostrils was the breath of the spirit of life"** (Genesis 7:22).

The *gemara* identifies the underlying question between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva: *mar savar ikar chayuta b'apei* — one holds that the essential life force of a person resides in the nostrils; *v'mar savar ikar chayuta b'tibura* — the other holds it resides in the belly button. This debate also arises in the context of *pikuach nefesh* in Yoma, specifically regarding how to determine whether a person trapped under rubble is still alive. Some wish to read this as a dispute about whether life is defined by respiration or by circulation — though the belly button's connection to circulation is unclear. It seems more likely that both positions are describing breath, with the belly button understood as relating to the diaphragm and the rhythmic rise and fall of breathing.

### Development & Life

The *gemara* entertains the possibility that this dispute parallels a separate *tannaitic* debate: *me'heichan ha'valad notzar* — from where does a fetus develop in the womb? One *tanna* says from the head; *Abba Shaul* says from the belly button, since the belly button is at the center, and development radiates outward in all directions. Each cites the verse *mi'mei immi atah gozi* in support.

The *gemara* concludes, however, that even *Abba Shaul* would agree that the locus of *chiyuta* — life force — is the nostrils. *Ad kan lo ka'amar Abba Shaul ela le'inyan yetzirah* — his position concerns *yetzirah*, fetal development, not the seat of life itself. *Aval le'inyan chiyuta dechulei alma b'apeih hu* — when it comes to the life force, everyone agrees it is in the nostrils, as the verse states: *kol asher nishmat ruach chayyim b'appav* — all in whose nostrils is the breath of life. The *gemara* thus consistently comes down on the side of breath in the nostrils as the defining measure of life — a point of considerable significance for contemporary halachic discussions surrounding the definition of death.

### R. Elazar: Neck

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: מִמָּקוֹם שֶׁנַּעֲשֶׂה חָלָל — מִצַּוָּארוֹ. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב? כְּדִכְתִיב: ״לָתֵת אוֹתָךְ אֶל צַוְּארֵי חַלְלֵי רְשָׁעִים״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught another opinion. **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says:** The distance should be measured **from the place where** the victim **became a slain** person, **from his neck.** The Gemara poses a question: **What is the reason of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov?** The Gemara answers: **As it is written: "To lay you upon the necks of the wicked who are to be slain"** (Ezekiel 21:34), which shows that being slain occurs at the neck.

Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov's position — that one measures from the neck — derives from the verse *latet otcha al tzavverei challalei resha'im*, which locates the image of a *chalal*, a sword-slain corpse, at the neck. Since the typical *chalal* involves decapitation, the neck is the defining anatomical locus of that category of death.

## Ritual Procedure

### Elders Depart

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נִפְטְרוּ זִקְנֵי יְרוּשָׁלָיִם וְהָלְכוּ לָהֶן. זִקְנֵי אוֹתָהּ הָעִיר מְבִיאִין עֶגְלַת בָּקָר אֲשֶׁר לֹא מָשְׁכָה בְּעוֹל, וְאֵין הַמּוּם פּוֹסֵל בָּהּ, וּמוֹרִידִין אוֹתָהּ לְנַחַל אֵיתָן. ״אֵיתָן״ כְּמַשְׁמָעוֹ: קָשֶׁה. אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ אֵיתָן — כָּשֵׁר. וְעוֹרְפִין אוֹתָהּ בְּקוֹפִיץ מֵאֲחוֹרֶיהָ. וּמְקוֹמָהּ אָסוּר מִלִּזְרוֹעַ וּמִלַּעֲבוֹד, וּמוּתָּר לִסְרוֹק שָׁם פִּשְׁתָּן וּלְנַקֵּר שָׁם אֲבָנִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna continues to describe the ritual. After they would take the measurement, **the Elders of Jerusalem took their leave and went** away. **The Elders of the city** that is closest to the corpse **bring a heifer from cattle, which has not pulled a yoke. But a blemish does not disqualify it,** because, unlike the description of the red heifer, the Torah does not state that it must be without blemish. **And they bring it down to a stream** that is ***eitan*. *Eitan*** in this context means **as** the word generally **indicates,** powerful. The stream must have **a forceful** flow. The mishna comments: **Even if it is not forceful, it is a valid** site for the ritual. **And they break the neck of** the heifer **from behind with a cleaver. And** with regard to **its place,** where the heifer was standing when its neck was broken, **it is prohibited** for that ground **to be sown or to be worked, but** it is **permitted to comb flax there or to cut stones there.**

Once the nearest city has been identified through measurement, *niftaru ziknei Yerushalayim v'halchu lahem* — the elders of Yerushalayim depart. The ritual itself is now the responsibility of the local elders of the identified town.

### Calf Requirements

*V'ziknei otah ha'ir mevi'in eglat bakar* — the elders of that town bring a calf. The calf must be *asher lo mashcha b'ol* — one that has not drawn a yoke, meaning it must never have been used for labor. Notably, however, *v'ein ha'mum posel bah* — a physical blemish does not invalidate it. The presence or absence of a *mum* is irrelevant; only prior *avoda*, labor, disqualifies.

### Wadi Eitan

*U'moridin otah l'nachal eitan* — they bring it down to a *nachal eitan*, a hard wadi. *Eitan k'mashma'o kasheh* — the word *eitan* in its plain sense means hard, referring to bedrock. Nevertheless, *af al pi she'eino eitan kasher* — even if the wadi is not actually of hard bedrock, it remains valid after the fact; the requirement of hard rock is only *lechatchilah*.

### Breaking the Neck

*V'orfin otah b'kofitz me'achoreha* — they break its neck from behind using a hatchet. This is done from behind the neck, not the front.

### Ground Forbidden

*U'mekomo assur milizro'a umila'avod le'olam* — the location where the ritual was performed is permanently forbidden for planting and agricultural work.

### Permitted Uses

*U'mutar lisrok sham pishtan ul'naker sham avanim* — it is, however, permitted to comb flax there or to clear stones from the area, since these activities do not involve working the soil itself. The prohibition falls specifically on uses that engage the ground; standing or sitting on the location, or conducting activities that merely take place upon it without making use of the earth, remain permitted.

## Declaration & Blessing

### Hand Washing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זִקְנֵי אוֹתָהּ הָעִיר רוֹחֲצִין אֶת יְדֵיהֶם בַּמַּיִם בִּמְקוֹם עֲרִיפָה שֶׁל עֶגְלָה, וְאוֹמְרִים ״יָדֵינוּ לֹא שָׁפְכוּ אֶת הַדָּם הַזֶּה וְעֵינֵינוּ לֹא רָאוּ״. וְכִי עַל דַּעְתֵּינוּ עָלְתָה שֶׁזִּקְנֵי בֵּית דִּין שׁוֹפְכֵי דָּמִים הֵן? אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא בָּא עַל יָדֵינוּ וּפְטַרְנוּהוּ (בְּלֹא מָזוֹן), וְלֹא רְאִינוּהוּ וְהִנַּחְנוּהוּ (בְּלֹא לְוָיָיה).
>
> **Translation:** **The Elders of that city** would then **wash their hands in water in** the **place of the breaking of the neck of the heifer, and they would recite: "Our hands did not spill this blood, nor did our eyes see"** (Deuteronomy 21:7). The mishna explains: **But did it enter our minds that the Elders of the court are spillers of blood,** that they must make such a declaration? **Rather,** they mean to declare **that** the victim **did not come to us and** then **we let him take his leave without food, and we did not see him and** then **leave him** alone to depart **without accompaniment.** They therefore attest that they took care of all his needs and are not responsible for his death even indirectly.

*Ziknei oto ir rochtzim et yedeihem b'mayim b'makom arifah* — the elders of the town wash their hands with water at the place where the neck was broken, over the calf. There is something visually striking about this: on the one hand, the washing of hands in a ritual context conveys assumption of responsibility, a cleansing of moral contamination; on the other hand, it calls to mind the idiom of "washing one's hands of" a matter, of disavowal. Perhaps both ideas are present — that there was a kind of defilement, a residual moral burden, that must be actively addressed and cleansed through the ritual act.

### Three Declarations

Following the hand washing, the elders declare: *yadeinu lo shafchu et hadam hazeh v'eineinu lo ra'u* — our hands did not shed this blood and our eyes did not see.

The *gemara* asks: surely no one suspected the *beit din* of murder — why must they declare that their hands did not spill the blood? The answer: *ela shelo ba leyadenu upatarnuhu belo mezonot velo ra'inuhu v'hinachnuhu belo levayah* — the declaration is not a denial of having committed murder, but an assertion of having fulfilled their communal obligations. They are affirming that this traveler did not come to them seeking help and get turned away without food; that they did not see him passing through and fail to accompany him.

This is a remarkable and demanding standard. The declaration does not merely address the question of direct culpability; it addresses the question of whether the community exercised sufficient care for the stranger in their midst. If a man had passed through town asking for shelter or assistance, and the *beit din* was aware of him and did nothing, they would be unable to make this declaration. The ritual thus encodes a positive communal responsibility for the welfare of travelers.

### Priestly Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַכֹּהֲנִים אוֹמְרִים: ״כַּפֵּר לְעַמְּךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל אֲשֶׁר פָּדִיתָ ה׳ וְאַל תִּתֵּן דָּם נָקִי בְּקֶרֶב עַמְּךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל״. לֹא הָיוּ צְרִיכִין לוֹמַר ״וְנִכַּפֵּר לָהֶם הַדָּם״, אֶלָּא רוּחַ הַקּוֹדֶשׁ מְבַשַּׂרְתָּן: אֵימָתַי שֶׁתַּעֲשׂוּ כָּכָה — הַדָּם מִתְכַּפֵּר לָהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **And the priests recite: "Forgive, Lord, Your people Israel, whom You have redeemed, and suffer not innocent blood to remain in the midst of Your people Israel"** (Deuteronomy 21:8). **They did not have to recite** the conclusion of the verse: **"And the blood shall be forgiven for them,"** as this is not part of the priests' statement, **but** rather **the Divine Spirit informs them: When you shall do so, the blood is forgiven for you.**

*V'hakohanim omrim kaper l'amcha Yisrael asher padita Hashem v'al titein dam naki b'kerev amcha Yisrael* — the *kohanim* then recite the prayer: atone for Your people Israel whom You have redeemed, God, and do not place the guilt of innocent blood in the midst of Your people.

### Divine Atonement

The *mishnah* notes that the final clause of the *pasuk* — *v'nichpar lahem hadam*, and the blood shall be atoned for them — is not part of the *kohanim*'s declaration. Rather, *ela ruach hakodesh mevasertam* — the divine spirit announces it to them as a promise: *eimatai sheta'asu kachah hadam yitkaper lahem* — when you perform this ritual, atonement for the blood will follow.

This raises a striking question about the theological structure of the *egla arufa* ritual. The *mishnah* earlier noted that the calf is killed by breaking its neck from behind — not by *shechitah* from the front, as in a *korban*. Why? The deliberate departure from the form of sacrifice signals that this is not a *korban* in the conventional sense. Yet the ritual does involve the shedding of blood in a context of atonement.

One way to understand this is that the *egla arufa* functions not as a sacrifice but as a substitute execution. The verse states *ki la'aretz lo yechupar ladam asher shupach bah ki im bedam shofcho* — the only true atonement for innocent blood is the execution of the murderer. Since the murderer is unknown and cannot be executed, the calf is slain in his stead. The blood being atoned for is the victim's innocent blood; the calf's blood is not what atones — rather, it is the act of execution, performed upon an innocent creature in place of the guilty one, that achieves atonement. This may be precisely why *avoda*, prior labor, disqualifies the calf: not only must it be young and never have borne offspring, it must also be wholly untarnished by human use — innocent, in the fullest sense.

## Mum & Avoda

### Blemish Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וִיהֵא מוּם פּוֹסֵל בְּעֶגְלָה מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה פָּרָה שֶׁאֵין הַשָּׁנִים פּוֹסְלוֹת בָּהּ, מוּם פּוֹסֵל בָּהּ, עֶגְלָה שֶׁשָּׁנִים פּוֹסְלוֹת בָּהּ — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיְּהֵא מוּם פּוֹסֵל בָּהּ! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״אֲשֶׁר אֵין בָּהּ מוּם״ — בָּהּ מוּם פּוֹסֵל, וְאֵין מוּם פּוֹסֵל בְּעֶגְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the mishna's statement that the heifer is not disqualified by a blemish, the Gemara suggests: **And a blemish should disqualify in** the case of the **heifer, by** means of **an *a fortiori*** inference: **And if** in the case of the red heifer, **which is not disqualified** by **years,** as it may be of any age, and yet **a blemish disqualifies it,** then **a heifer** for this ritual, **which is disqualified by years,** as it is valid only until two years of age, **is it not logical that a blemish should disqualify it?** The Gemara answers: It **is different there,** in the case of the red heifer, **as the verse states: "Wherein [*bah*] has no blemish"** (Numbers 19:2). This serves as an exclusion and teaches that it is only **with regard to it [*bah*]** that **a blemish is disqualifying, but a blemish is not disqualifying with regard to** the **heifer** of the ritual of the breaking of the neck.

The *mishnah*'s ruling that a *mum* does not disqualify the *egla* invites scrutiny. One might have expected the opposite conclusion based on a *kal v'chomer* from *parah adumah*.

### Kal VeChomer

The *gemara* constructs the argument: *u'mah parah she'ein ha'shanim posel bah mum posel bah* — by a *parah adumah* the age of the animal is irrelevant, and yet a *mum* disqualifies it. *Eglah she'shanim posel bah* — an *eglah* must be young (the word *eglah* itself implies a young animal), so age is a disqualifying factor. If even *parah adumah*, where age is not an issue, requires the absence of a *mum*, surely an *eglah*, which is already stricter with respect to age, should require the absence of a *mum* as well. The *kal v'chomer* seems compelling.

### Mum vs. Avoda

The *gemara* rejects it: *shani hatam de'amar kra asher ein bah mum* — by *parah adumah*, the Torah explicitly specifies *asher ein bah mum*, that there be no blemish in it. The word *bah* — "in it" — limits the requirement to *parah adumah* specifically. The absence of a *mum* is a requirement *bah*, in the *parah*, and need not be extended elsewhere.

But this immediately generates a counter-argument: if the word *bah* by *parah adumah* restricts the *mum* requirement to *parah* alone and prevents us from extending it to *eglah*, then by the same logic the word *bah* by *eglah* — which says *asher lo uvad bah*, that it was not worked — should restrict the *avoda* disqualification to *eglah* alone and prevent us from extending it to *parah adumah*. Yet we clearly do apply the *avoda* requirement to *parah adumah* (as seen from the ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav discussed below). How can we apply *avoda* from *eglah* to *parah* while simultaneously refusing to apply *mum* from *parah* to *eglah*?

### Avoda & Kodshim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה לֹא יְהוּ שְׁאָר עֲבוֹדוֹת פּוֹסְלוֹת בָּהּ. הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְמַעוֹטֵי קֳדָשִׁים, דְּלָא פָּסְלָה בְּהוּ עֲבוֹדָה. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךָ אָמֵינָא לֵיתֵי בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר מֵעֶגְלָה: וּמָה עֶגְלָה שֶׁאֵין מוּם פּוֹסֵל בָּהּ — עֲבוֹדָה פּוֹסֶלֶת בָּהּ, קֳדָשִׁים שֶׁמּוּם פּוֹסֵל(ת) בָּהֶן — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁעֲבוֹדָה פּוֹסֶלֶת בָּהֶן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **However, if that is so,** if the word "*bah*" precludes a derivation by an *a fortiori* inference, then any **other labor** performed with the red heifer, apart from pulling a yoke, **should not disqualify it.** While the verse disqualifies a red heifer only if it pulled a yoke, as it states: "And upon which never came a yoke" (Numbers 19:2), a similar *a fortiori* inference could be learned from the heifer whose neck is to be broken to disqualify a red heifer that has performed any labor. However, since the verse states with regard to the heifer whose neck is to be broken: "That has not been worked with [*bah*]" (Deuteronomy 21:3), this indicates that labor is disqualifying only for "*bah*," a heifer whose neck is to be broken, but not for a red heifer. **That** word "*bah*" **is required by** Rav Yehuda in order **to exclude sacred** offerings, i.e., **which are not disqualified by labor,** and one may bring an animal that has been used for labor as an offering. **It might enter your mind to say** that this **should be derived by an *a fortiori*** inference **from a heifer** whose neck is broken, as follows: **And if** with regard to **a heifer** whose neck is broken, **which is not disqualified by a blemish, labor** nevertheless **disqualifies it,** then with regard to **sacred** offerings, **which are disqualified by a blemish, is it not right that labor should disqualify them?** In order to counter this argument, the word "*bah*" teaches us that a sacred offering is not disqualified by labor.

The resolution is elegant and surprising. The *gemara* states: *hahi miba'ei lei l'mi'utei kodshim d'lo pasla behu avoda* — the word *bah* by *eglah* is not being used to exclude *parah adumah* from the *avoda* requirement. Rather, it is being used to exclude *kodshim* — sacrificial animals — from the *avoda* requirement. Remarkably, prior labor does not disqualify a *korban*.

This is counterintuitive. One would naturally assume that *kodshim* are subject to at least as stringent requirements as *eglah* and *parah adumah*. But the *gemara* holds the opposite: the disqualification of *avoda* is specific to these two non-*korban* contexts and does not apply to consecrated offerings. An animal that has been used for agricultural labor can still be brought as a *korban*, as long as it otherwise meets the required criteria.

The *bah* by *parah adumah* therefore functions as follows: it restricts the *mum* requirement to *parah adumah*, excluding *eglah* (which indeed has no *mum* requirement). The *bah* by *eglah* restricts the *avoda* requirement to *eglah*, excluding not *parah* but *kodshim*. The two *bah*s are doing different limiting work and are not in tension.

### Three Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָדָשִׁים דְּלָא פָּסְלָה בְּהוּ עֲבוֹדָה מֵהָכָא נָפְקָא? מֵהָתָם נָפְקָא: ״עַוֶּרֶת אוֹ שָׁבוּר אוֹ חָרוּץ אוֹ יַבֶּלֶת אוֹ גָרָב אוֹ יַלֶּפֶת לֹא תַקְרִיבוּ אֵלֶּה לַה׳״, אֵלֶּה אִי אַתָּה מַקְרִיב, אֲבָל אַתָּה מַקְרִיב קֳדָשִׁים שֶׁנֶּעֶבְדָה בָּהֶן עֲבוֹדָה! אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךָ אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּעָבַד בָּהֶן עֲבוֹדַת הֶיתֵּר, אֲבָל עֲבוֹדַת אִיסּוּר אֵימָא לִיתַּסְרוּ, צְרִיכָא. וְהָא נָמֵי מֵהָכָא נָפְקָא — ״וּמִיַּד בֶּן נֵכָר לֹא תַקְרִיבוּ אֶת לֶחֶם אֱלֹהֵיכֶם מִכׇּל אֵלֶּה״, אֵלֶּה אִי אַתָּה מַקְרִיב, אֲבָל אַתָּה מַקְרִיב קֳדָשִׁים שֶׁנֶּעֶבְדָה בָּהֶן עֲבוֹדָה! אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךָ אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּעָבַד בָּהֶן כְּשֶׁהֵן חוּלִּין, אֲבָל עָבַד בָּהֶן כְּשֶׁהֵן קֳדָשִׁים — אֵימָא לִיתַּסְרוּ, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this suggested *a fortiori* inference, the Gemara observes that **it can be refuted** in the following manner: **What** about the fact that **a heifer** whose neck is broken **is disqualified by years,** as once it reaches two years of age it is no longer classified as a heifer? As it is clear that the heifer whose neck is to be broken carries some restrictions that do not apply to sacred offerings, perhaps being disqualified by labor is another such restriction. The Gemara refutes this argument: **Is that to say that there are no sacred** offerings **that are disqualified by years?** There are several offerings that may be brought only in their first or second year, and **where the verse is necessary** to teach that sacred offerings are not disqualified by labor, it is with regard to **those sacred** offerings **that are disqualified by years.** With regard to this suggested *a fortiori* inference, the Gemara observes that **it can be refuted** in the following manner: **What** about the fact that **a heifer** whose neck is broken **is disqualified by years,** as once it reaches two years of age it is no longer classified as a heifer? As it is clear that the heifer whose neck is to be broken carries some restrictions that do not apply to sacred offerings, perhaps being disqualified by labor is another such restriction. The Gemara refutes this argument: **Is that to say that there are no sacred** offerings **that are disqualified by years?** There are several offerings that may be brought only in their first or second year, and **where the verse is necessary** to teach that sacred offerings are not disqualified by labor, it is with regard to **those sacred** offerings **that are disqualified by years. But is** the *halakha* **that sacred** offerings **are not disqualified by labor derived from this** verse? **It is derived from elsewhere.** The verse states with regard to sacred offerings: **"Blind, or broken, or maimed, or having a wart, or scabbed, or scurvy, you shall not offer these to the Lord"** (Leviticus 22:22). This verse serves to create an exclusion, teaching that it is **these** that **you may not sacrifice, but you may sacrifice sacred** animals **that have been used for labor.** The Gemara answers: It **was necessary** to state the *halakha* twice. **It might enter your mind to say** that **this** *halakha*, that one may sacrifice animals that have been used for labor, **applies** only in a case where **they were used for permitted labor, but** if they were used for **prohibited labor,** e.g., on Shabbat, you might **say that it is prohibited** to bring **them** as offerings. In order to refute this argument, it is **necessary** to state the *halakha* again. The Gemara poses another question: **But this** *halakha* that prohibited labor does not disqualify offerings **is also derived from here,** a verse with regard to the sacrifice of blemished animals: **"And from the hand of a stranger you shall not offer the bread of your God from any of these, because…**there is a blemish in them" (Leviticus 22:25). This verse emphasizes that it is only **"these,"** i.e., blemished animals, that **you may not sacrifice, but you may sacrifice sacred** animals **that have been used for labor.** Since this verse is discussing the possibility of accepting offerings from a gentile, who presumably also performed prohibited labor with the animal, this demonstrates that prohibited labor does not disqualify animals from being sacrificed as offerings. The Gemara answers: It **was necessary** to teach this *halakha* a third time. **It might enter your mind to say: This** *halakha*, that labor does not disqualify offerings, **applies** only where **one performed labor with them when they were non-sacred** and afterward dedicated them as offerings, **but** if **one performed labor with them when they were** already **sacred** animals, you might **say that it is prohibited** to bring **them** as offerings. In order to refute this argument, it is **necessary** to teach this *halakha* in three separate places.

The *gemara* then asks: is this really where we learn that *avoda* does not disqualify *kodshim*? Isn't that derived from the verse *iver o shavur o charutz o yavelet o garav o yalefet lo takrivu eleh la-Hashem* — a list of blemishes that disqualify offerings? *Eleh atah makriv* — only these disqualify, but an animal that was used for labor you may bring. So we already know *avoda* is not a problem for *kodshim* from this verse.

The *gemara* responds that this verse alone would not have been sufficient. Without the *bah* by *eglah*, one might have thought the *eleh* verse only exempts *avodat heter* — labor performed when it was still ordinary, unconsecrated property — but *avodat issur*, labor performed in a prohibited manner (such as being yoked in a forbidden mixture), might still create a metaphysical defect. The *bah* teaches that even *avodat issur* does not disqualify.

A second verse — *u'mi'yad ben nechar lo takrivu et lechem Eloheikhem mi'kol eleh* — also teaches the exemption. But even two verses would not have been enough: one might have thought the exemption applies only when the labor was performed while the animal was still *chullin*, unconsecrated property, but not when the labor was performed after the animal was already consecrated as *kadosh*. The third source — the *bah* by *eglah* — teaches that even labor performed on an animal after it was already consecrated as a *korban* does not disqualify it.

This is a remarkable ruling. Even if one violated the sanctity of the *korban* by using it for labor after it had been designated, the offering remains valid. It may well be that the person incurred *me'ilah* — misappropriation of consecrated property — but the *korban* itself retains its halachic status.

The overall picture is therefore illuminating: *eglah* has no *mum* requirement but does have an *avoda* requirement. *Korbanot* have a *mum* requirement but no *avoda* requirement. *Parah adumah* has both — and in this sense stands in the middle, sharing characteristics with both.

### Bundle of Sacks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: הִנִּיחַ עָלֶיהָ עוּדָּה שֶׁל שַׂקִּין פְּסוּלָה, וּבְעֶגְלָה עַד שֶׁתִּמְשׁוֹךְ. מֵיתִיבִי: ״עֹל״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא עוֹל, שְׁאָר עֲבוֹדוֹת מִנַּיִן? אָמַרְתָּ, קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה עֶגְלָה שֶׁאֵין מוּם פּוֹסֵל בָּהּ, שְׁאָר עֲבוֹדוֹת פּוֹסְלוֹת בָּהּ, פָּרָה שֶׁמּוּם פּוֹסֵל בָּהּ — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁשְּׁאָר עֲבוֹדוֹת פּוֹסְלִין בָּהּ? וְאִם נַפְשְׁךָ לוֹמַר: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״עֹל״ וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״עֹל״. מָה לְהַלָּן שְׁאָר עֲבוֹדוֹת פּוֹסְלוֹת בָּהּ — אַף כָּאן שְׁאָר עֲבוֹדוֹת פּוֹסְלוֹת. וּמִמָּקוֹם שֶׁבָּאתָה: מָה לְהַלָּן עַד שֶׁתִּמְשׁוֹךְ, אַף כָּאן עַד שֶׁתִּמְשׁוֹךְ!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to discuss **the** matter **itself: Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** If **one placed a bundle of sacks on** a red heifer, **it is disqualified. And** as **for a heifer** whose neck is broken, it is not disqualified **until it pulls** a burden. The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: It states with regard to the red heifer: "That upon which never came **a yoke"** (Numbers 19:2). **I have** derived **only a yoke; from where** do I derive that other types of labor also disqualify the animal? **You** can **say** the following ***a fortiori*** inference: **And if** with regard to **a heifer** whose neck is broken, **which is not disqualified by a blemish, other** types of **labor disqualify it,** then with regard to a red **heifer, which is disqualified by a blemish, is it not right that other** types of **labor should disqualify it?** **And if it is your wish to say** that this *a fortiori* inference is unsound, you can learn this *halakha* by a verbal analogy: **It is stated here,** with regard to the red heifer, **"yoke"** (Numbers 19:2), **and it is stated there,** with regard to the heifer whose neck is broken, **"yoke"** (Deuteronomy 21:3). **Just as there, other** types of **labor disqualify it, so too here,** in the case of the red heifer, **other** types of **labor disqualify** it. The Gemara raises an objection to this verbal analogy: **And from the place that you came** you can offer an alternative exposition: **Just as below,** in the case of a heifer whose neck is broken, it is not disqualified by carrying a burden **until it pulls** the yoke, **so too here,** a red heifer should not be disqualified **until it pulls** the yoke, contrary to the statement of Rav.

The *gemara* now returns to examine the statement of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: *ha'maniach chavilah shel sakin al ha'parah* — if one placed a bundle of sacks upon the *parah adumah*, it is invalid. This is striking because a bundle of sacks is not an *ol*, a yoke — yet it disqualifies. Meanwhile, the rule by *eglah* is *u've'eglah ad she'timshoch* — by an *eglah* the animal must actually draw the load, actually walk with it, for the *avoda* to be operative.

Why the asymmetry? Why is *parah adumah* disqualified merely by having a burden placed upon it, while *eglah* requires the animal to actually move?

### Gezeirah Shava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּאִיכָּא דְּמַיְיתֵי לַהּ מֵעֶגְלָה, אִיכָּא דְּמַיְיתֵי לַהּ מִגּוּפַהּ דְּפָרָה. דְּתַנְיָא: ״עֹל״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא עוֹל. שְׁאָר עֲבוֹדוֹת מִנַּיִן — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא עָלָה עָלֶיהָ עֹל״ מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. אִם כֵּן מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עֹל״ — עוֹל פּוֹסֵל בֵּין בִּשְׁעַת עֲבוֹדָה בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת עֲבוֹדָה, שְׁאָר עֲבוֹדוֹת אֵין פּוֹסְלוֹת אֶלָּא בִּשְׁעַת עֲבוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers the objection to the statement of Rav from the *baraita*: It **is** a dispute among ***tanna'im*, as there are those who cite** the source of **this** *halakha*, that labor disqualifies a red heifer, by verbal analogy **from a heifer** whose neck is broken, and therefore the red heifer is disqualified only if it pulls the burden. **There are** also **those who cite** the source of **this** *halakha* **from** a red **heifer itself,** and consequently they disqualify the red heifer even if it did not pull the yoke. This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to a red heifer: From the term **"yoke" I have** derived **only** that **a yoke** disqualifies a red heifer; **from where** do I derive the **other** types of **labor? The verse states: "That upon which never came a yoke"** (Numbers 19:2). The verse could be read with a pause after the word "came," which would teach that it is disqualified **in any case,** no matter what labor was performed with it. **If so, what** is the meaning when **the verse states "yoke,"** if all forms of labor disqualify it? It teaches us that **a yoke** placed on the animal **disqualifies** it **whether** the yoke was on the animal **at the time of** performing **labor** or **whether** it was on the animal **not at the time of** performing **labor,** i.e., it was merely placed on the animal. However, **other** types of **labor** actions **disqualify** animals **only at the time of** actually performing **labor.** Rav ruled in accordance with this opinion.

The answer lies in the different legal sources for the two rulings. By *eglah*, the verse reads *asher lo mashcha b'ol* — which did not draw with a yoke. The term *mashcha*, drawing, connotes actual movement. And by *parah adumah*, the verse reads *asher lo alah aleha ol* — upon which no yoke has gone up. If we were to learn *parah* from *eglah* via a *gezeirah shavah* on the shared word *ol* — *ne'emar kan ol v'ne'emar lehalan ol* — then we would expect *parah* to also require actual movement, *meshichah*, just as *eglah* does.

The *gemara* confirms this logic: *mah lehalan ad she'timshoch af kan ad she'timshoch* — if the source is *eglah*, the threshold should be the same. Rav Yehuda's ruling that even placing a bundle of sacks without movement disqualifies the *parah* must therefore reflect an alternative source that does not derive from *eglah*.

This is indeed a *tannaitic* dispute: some derive the *avoda* disqualification for *parah* from *eglah* via the *gezeirah shavah*, in which case the definition is parallel — movement is required. Others derive it from the internal language of the *parah adumah* verse itself, in which case the conceptual framework is different.

### Ol vs. Avoda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא עָלָה עָלֶיהָ״ — כָּלַל, ״עֹל״ — פָּרַט, כְּלָל וּפְרָט, אֵין בַּכְּלָל אֶלָּא מָה שֶׁבַּפְּרָט: עוֹל אִין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא — לָא! ״אֲשֶׁר״ — רִבּוּיָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **And** perhaps one can **say** a different exposition of the verse: **"That upon which never came"** is **a generalization** that disqualifies the animal after any type of labor, while **"yoke"** is **a detail.** There is **a generalization and a detail,** and the principle of halakhic exposition in that case is that **there is nothing in the generalization other** than **what is in the detail.** Therefore, with regard to **a yoke, yes,** it will disqualify an animal from being used as a red heifer; but with regard to **anything else, no,** it will not disqualify the animal. The Gemara answers: **"That** upon which never came" **is an amplification,** and the addition of this term results in this verse not belonging to the category of generalizations and details. The Gemara comments: **And a case like this is also taught in** a *baraita* **with regard to a heifer** whose neck is broken: From the word **"yoke" I have** derived **only** that **a yoke** disqualifies; **from where** do I derive the **other** types of **labor? The** same **verse states: "That has not been worked with"** (Deuteronomy 21:3), to teach that it is disqualified **in any case,** no matter what labor was performed with it. **If so, what** is the meaning when **the verse states "yoke"?** It serves to teach us that **a yoke** placed on the animal **disqualifies** it **whether** the yoke was on the animal **at the time of** performing **labor or whether** it was on the animal **not at the time of performing labor,** i.e., it was merely placed on the animal, whereas **other** types of **labor** actions **disqualify** animals **only at the time of** actually performing **labor.** The Gemara raises an objection: **And** perhaps one can **say** a different exposition of the verse: **"That has not been worked with"** is **a generalization** that disqualifies the animal after any type of labor, while **"yoke"** is **a detail.** There is **a generalization and a detail,** and the principle of halakhic exposition in that case is that **there is nothing in the generalization other** than **what is in the detail,** which means: With regard to **a yoke, yes,** it will disqualify an animal, but with regard to **anything else, no,** it will not disqualify it. The Gemara answers: The phrase **"that** has not been worked with" **is an amplification,** and the addition of this term results in this verse not belonging to the category of generalizations and details.

According to the view that derives the rule from the *parah* verse itself, the operative phrase is not *avoda* at all. The disqualification comes from the words *asher lo alah aleha* — upon which nothing has gone up. The word *ol*, yoke, at the end of the phrase then has a specific additional function: *ol posel bein be'sha'at avoda bein shelo be'sha'at avoda* — a yoke disqualifies whether placed for the purpose of work or not. The mere presence of a yoke upon the *parah* is disqualifying, regardless of intent or use. For all other burdens, however, they disqualify only *be'sha'at avoda* — in the context of actual labor use. Rashi explains this to mean not that the animal must have walked, but that the burden must have been placed upon it for the purpose of work, not merely set down in passing.

On this reading, the conceptual framework for *parah adumah* is not fundamentally about *avoda* at all — it is about *alah aleha*, having anything placed upon it. The significance of the word *ol* in the verse is specifically to inform us that a yoke, even placed without any intent to use it for work, is immediately disqualifying.

### Tefach Movement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, בְּעַי מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מְשִׁיכַת עוֹל בְּכַמָּה? אָמַר לִי: כִּמְלֹא עוֹל. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: לְאׇרְכּוֹ אוֹ לְרׇחְבּוֹ? אֲמַר לְהוּ הַהוּא מֵרַבָּנַן וְרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב שְׁמֵיהּ: לְדִידִי מִפָּרְשָׁא לִי מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מְשִׁיכַת עוֹל לְרׇחְבּוֹ טֶפַח. וְלֵימָא טֶפַח? הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן שִׁיעוּרָא דְּעוֹל — טֶפַח הָוֵי. לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ — לְמִקָּח וּמִמְכָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu said: I asked of Rabbi Yoḥanan:** This **pulling of a yoke** that disqualifies a heifer whose neck is broken, **with how much,** i.e., how far, must the animal pull the yoke for it to be disqualified? **He said to me: Like** the measure of the size of **a full yoke. A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Does this mean according **to its length or** according **to its width? One of the Sages, and Rabbi Ya'akov was his name, said to them: It was explained to me personally by Rabbi Yoḥanan himself: The pulling of a yoke is** according **to its width,** which is **a handbreadth. And** since he stated a fixed measurement, **let him** merely **state: A handbreadth.** Why was it necessary to add that this is the width of a yoke? The Gemara answers: **This teaches us** that **the measure** of **a yoke** along its width **is a handbreadth. What difference is there** in knowing this fact? This teaches that **in** the case of **commercial transactions,** a buyer may retract his purchase if the yoke he was given is less than a handbreadth wide.

By contrast, for *eglah*, where the standard is *meshichah* — actual drawing — the *gemara* records a tradition from Rabbi Yochanan as transmitted by Rabbi Yaakov: *meshichat ol l'rochbo tefach* — the drawing of the yoke requires the animal to have moved the width of a yoke, which is a *tefach*. Rabbi Abbahu had posed the question of whether the relevant dimension was the length or the width of the yoke; Rabbi Yaakov transmitted that Rabbi Yochanan had specified the width, which is a *tefach*. This formulation also conveys a practical corollary for commercial purposes: a yoke that is less than a *tefach* in width does not qualify as a yoke at all and could be rejected in a sale transaction.

## Theology

### Never Produced

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן שָׁאוּל, מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: הָבֵיא עֶגְלָה בְּנַחַל? אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: יָבֹא דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא עָשָׂה פֵּירוֹת, וְיֵעָרֵף בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין עוֹשֶׂה פֵּירוֹת, וִיכַפֵּר עַל מִי שֶׁלֹּא הִנִּיחוּ[הוּ] לַעֲשׂוֹת פֵּירוֹת. מַאי פֵּירוֹת? אִילֵימָא פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה — אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה אַזָּקֵן וְאַסָּרִיס הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלָא עָרְפִינַן?! אֶלָּא מִצְוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Shaul says: For what** reason **did the Torah say** to **bring a heifer** whose neck is broken **to a stream? The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: Let something that did not produce fruit,** i.e., a heifer that has not given birth, **come and** have its neck **be broken at** a stream that flows forcefully, which is **a place that does not produce fruit, and atone for** the murder of **one who was not given an opportunity to produce fruit.** The Gemara asks: **What** is this **fruit** that he was not given an opportunity to produce? **If we say** it refers to **being fruitful and multiplying,** i.e., that the killer prevented him from having more children, **but if that is so,** in the case **of an elderly person or a eunuch, so too** will you say **that we do not break** the heifer's neck because they could not have had any more children even had they lived? **Rather,** the fruit are **mitzvot,** as the killer deprived the victim of the opportunity to perform additional mitzvot.

Rabbi Yochanan ben Shaul offers a theological reading of why the Torah requires both a calf and a wadi: *mipnei mah amra Torah hevi eglah b'nachal* — why did the Torah say to bring a calf down to the wadi? *Amar ha'Kadosh Baruch Hu yavo davar shelo asah peirot* — the Holy One said: let come something that has never produced fruit. The calf, young and never used for labor, is an animal that has produced nothing; the wadi, hard and barren, is a place that produces nothing.

### Wasted Potential

*V'ye'aref bimkom she'ein oseh peirot vichaper al mi shelo hinicho la'asot peirot* — and let it be killed in a place that does not produce anything, to atone for one who was not permitted to produce fruit. The victim — murdered, perhaps in youth — was a person whose potential was cut off before it could be realized.

### Fruit = Mitzvot

The *gemara* asks: *mai peiros* — what do we mean by "fruit" in this context? If it means *priyah v'rivyah*, children, then one would not perform *egla arufa* for an elderly man or a sterile person who could not have had children regardless — which is surely not the case. Rather, *ela mitzvot* — "fruit" means deeds, the good acts and *mitzvot* one might have performed in a lifetime. Every murder is an assault not merely on a life but on all the potential *mitzvot* that life would have generated. The calf and the wadi together form a symbolic tableau of unrealized potential, offering atonement for a world diminished by the act of violence.

### Ancient Ground

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִנַּיִן לְאֵיתָן שֶׁהוּא קָשֶׁה — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֵיתָן מוֹשָׁבֶךָ וְשִׂים בַּסֶּלַע קִנֶּךָ״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״שִׁמְעוּ הָרִים אֶת רִיב ה׳ וְהָאֵיתָנִים מֹסְדֵי אָרֶץ״. אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: מִנַּיִן לְ״אֵיתָן״ שֶׁהוּא יָשָׁן — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״גּוֹי אֵיתָן הוּא, גּוֹי מֵעוֹלָם הוּא״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: From where** is it derived **that *eitan* is forceful?** It is **as** it **is stated:** **"Firm [*eitan*] is your dwelling-place, and your nest is set in the rock"** (Numbers 24:21), **and it states: "Hear, O you mountains, the Lord's controversy, and the enduring rocks [*eitanim*], the foundations of the earth"** (Micah 6:2). The use of the word in these verses indicates that "*eitan*" means something hard, like a rock or a mountain. **Others say** a different explanation of the word *eitan*: **From where** is it derived **that *eitan* means old? As** it **is stated: "It is an ancient [*eitan*] nation, a nation from of old"** (Jeremiah 5:15).

The *gemara* also explores the meaning of *nachal eitan*. One opinion derives from verses that *eitan* denotes hardness — a wadi of hard, rocky ground. Another opinion, *acherim omrim*, holds that *eitan* means ancient: *nachal eitan* is a wadi whose soil has never been disturbed, ground that goes back to primordial times, ground that carries with it no history of human cultivation. The two readings are not necessarily in conflict; Rashi reads *eitan* as ground that has never been dug up, which combines both the sense of hardness and antiquity.

## Ground Restrictions

### From Behind

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְעוֹרְפִין אוֹתָהּ בְּקוֹפִיץ מֵאֲחוֹרֶיהָ״. מַאי טַעְמָא — גָּמַר עֲרִיפָה עֲרִיפָה מֵחַטַּאת הָעוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: **And they break the neck [*orfin*] of** the heifer **from behind with a cleaver.** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** that the Sages understood that the heifer is killed in this manner? They **derive** that the term ***arifa*,** which describes what is done to the heifer, refers to breaking the back of the neck, **from** the term ***arifa*** stated with regard to the **bird** brought as **a sin-offering** (see Leviticus 5:8).

*V'orfin otah b'kofitz me'achoreha* — the neck is broken from behind with a hatchet. The *gemara* derives this through *gezeirah shavah*: *gamar arifa arifa me'chatat ha'of* — the word *arifa* here is linked to the word *arifa* used in the context of *chatat ha'of*, the bird sin-offering, where the priest pinches the neck from behind. Just as there the action is performed from the nape of the neck, so here too the breaking of the neck is from behind.

### Past vs. Future

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּמְקוֹמָהּ, אָסוּר מִלִּזְרוֹעַ וּמִלֵּיעָבֵד״. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא יֵעָבֵד בּוֹ וְלֹא יִזָּרֵעַ״ — לְשֶׁעָבַר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה. רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן אוֹמֵר: לְהַבָּא. רָבָא אָמַר: לְהַבָּא — דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְלֹא יִזָּרֵעַ״, כִּי פְּלִיגִי לְשֶׁעָבַר. רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה סָבַר: מִי כְּתִיב ״וְלֹא יְעוּבַּד״? וְרַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מִי כְּתִיב ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא נֶעֱבַד״? וְרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה: ״אֲשֶׁר״ — לְשֶׁעָבַר מַשְׁמַע, וְרַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: ״אֲשֶׁר״ — רִבּוּיָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught further: **And** with regard to **its place,** it **is prohibited** for that ground **to be sown or to be worked. The Sages taught:** The verse: **"Which may be neither worked nor sown"** (Deuteronomy 21:4) is referring **to the past,** that is, a place which has not previously been worked or sown. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yoshiya. Rabbi Yonatan says:** It speaks **of the future,** meaning it is prohibited to sow or work the land from that point onward. **Rava said:** As **for the future, everyone agrees** that it is prohibited to sow or work the land, **as it is written** "neither worked **nor sown"** in the future tense. **When they disagree** is with regard to **the past. Rabbi Yoshiya,** who disqualifies a place that was sown beforehand, **holds: Does it state: And shall not be worked,** in the form of a future command? **And Rabbi Yonatan** responds: **Does it state: And was not worked,** in the past tense? **And Rabbi Yoshiya** answers: The term **"which" indicates the past. And** as for **Rabbi Yonatan,** in his opinion the term **"which" is** a term of **amplification,** as will be explained later in the Gemara, and it is not referring to the past.

The verse states *asher lo ye'aved bah v'lo yizare'a* regarding the site of the *egla arufa*. A *tannaitic* dispute addresses whether this language refers to the past or the future. Rabbi Yoshiya reads it as describing the past: the wadi must be one that has not previously been worked or planted. Rabbi Yonatan reads it as prescribing the future: henceforth, this ground may not be worked or planted.

Rava clarifies: *lehaba d'chulei alma lo peligi* — on the future prohibition, there is no dispute. *V'lo yizare'a* means "shall not be seeded" — this is universally accepted as a prospective prohibition. The debate is only about *leshe'avar*, whether the verse additionally specifies that the ground must have been previously unworked. Rabbi Yoshiya argues that *asher* introduces a description of a past state; Rabbi Yonatan argues that had the Torah meant the past it would have said *ne'evad*, and the form used implies future obligation.

### All Soil Usage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוּתָּר לִסְרוֹק שָׁם פִּשְׁתָּן וּלְנַקֵּר שָׁם אֲבָנִים. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא יֵעָבֵד בּוֹ וְלֹא יִזָּרֵעַ״ — אֵין לִי אֶלָּא זְרִיעָה, שְׁאָר עֲבוֹדוֹת מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא יֵעָבֵד בּוֹ״ — מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. אִם כֵּן מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְלֹא יִזָּרֵעַ״? לוֹמַר לְךָ: מָה זְרִיעָה מְיוּחֶדֶת שֶׁהִיא בְּגוּפָהּ שֶׁל קַרְקַע, אַף כֹּל שֶׁהוּא בְּגוּפָהּ שֶׁל קַרְקַע. יָצָא סְרִיקַת פִּשְׁתָּן וְנִיקּוּר אֲבָנִים, שֶׁאֵינָן בְּגוּפָהּ שֶׁל קַרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: **But it is permitted to comb flax there or to cut stones there. The Sages taught:** From the phrase **"which may be neither worked nor sown," I have** derived **only sowing; from where** do I derive that **other** types of **labor** are also prohibited? **The verse states: "Which may be neither worked,"** indicating that it may not be worked **in any manner.** The *baraita* continues: **If so, why does the verse** also need to **state "nor sown"?** It is in order **to say to you: Just as sowing is unique** in **that it is** labor performed **on the land itself, so too, all** labor **that is** performed **on the land itself** is prohibited. This **excludes combing flax and cutting stones, which are not** done **on the land itself.**

A *braita* extends the scope of the future prohibition. The verse says *asher lo ye'aved bo* — it shall not be worked — and *lo yizare'a* — and it shall not be planted. From the combination, the *braita* derives: *ein li ela zri'ah, she'ar avodot minayin* — I know only that planting is forbidden; how do I know all other forms of soil work are also forbidden? *Talmud lomar asher lo ye'aved bo mikol makom* — the phrase "shall not be worked" encompasses all soil use. The specific mention of planting then teaches the principle: *mah zri'ah meyuchedet she'hi b'gufah shel karka, af kol she'hi b'gufah shel karka assur* — just as planting is distinctive in that it engages the body of the earth itself, so anything that engages the body of the earth is forbidden. *Yatza srikat pishtan v'nikur avanim* — excluded are combing flax and clearing stones, which take place upon the ground without working the earth itself.

## Accompaniment

### Declaration Details

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כּוֹפִין לִלְוָיָה, שֶׁשְּׂכַר הַלְוָיָה אֵין לָהּ שִׁיעוּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּרְאוּ הַשּׁוֹמְרִים אִישׁ יוֹצֵא מִן הָעִיר וַיֹּאמְרוּ לוֹ הַרְאֵנוּ נָא אֶת מְבוֹא הָעִיר וְעָשִׂינוּ עִמְּךָ חָסֶד״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּרְאֵם אֶת מְבוֹא הָעִיר״, וּמָה חֶסֶד עָשׂוּ עִמּוֹ — שֶׁכׇּל אוֹתָהּ הָעִיר הָרְגוּ לְפִי חֶרֶב, וְאוֹתוֹ הָאִישׁ וּמִשְׁפַּחְתּוֹ שָׁלְחוּ. ״וַיֵּלֶךְ הָאִישׁ אֶרֶץ הַחִתִּים וַיִּבֶן עִיר וַיִּקְרָא שְׁמָהּ לוּז הוּא שְׁמָהּ עַד הַיּוֹם הַזֶּה״, תַּנְיָא: הִיא לוּז שֶׁצּוֹבְעִין בָּהּ תְּכֵלֶת, הִיא לוּז שֶׁבָּא סַנְחֵרִיב וְלֹא בִּלְבְּלָהּ, נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר וְלֹא הֶחְרִיבָהּ, וְאַף מַלְאַךְ הַמָּוֶת אֵין לוֹ רְשׁוּת לַעֲבוֹר בָּהּ. אֶלָּא זְקֵנִים שֶׁבָּהּ, בִּזְמַן שֶׁדַּעְתָּן קָצָה עֲלֵיהֶן יוֹצְאִין חוּץ לַחוֹמָה, וְהֵן מֵתִים.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Meir would say: There is coercion with regard to accompaniment,** i.e., one who does not want to accompany another is nevertheless required to do so, **as the reward for accompaniment is without measure.** The proof of the importance of accompaniment is from a verse, **as it is stated** with regard to when the Jewish people laid siege to the city of Bethel: **"And the watchers saw a man come out of the city, and they said to him: Show us, please, the entrance into the city, and we will deal kindly with you"** (Judges 1:24), **and it is written: "And he showed them the entrance to the city"** (Judges 1:25). **And what kindness did they perform with him?** It is **that they killed the entire city by the sword, but that man and his family they sent** free. The Gemara elaborates on the reward received in that story. The next verse states: **"And the man went to the land of the Hittites, and he built a city, and he called its name Luz; that is its name to this day"** (Judges 1:26). **It is taught** in a *baraita*: This **is** the city **Luz where sky blue** wool **is dyed.** It **is** the same city **Luz where,** although **Sennacherib came** and exiled many nations from place to place, he **did not disarrange** and exile **its** inhabitants; **Nebuchadnezzar,** who conquered many lands, **did not destroy it; and even the angel of death has no permission to pass through it. Rather, its Elders, when they have decided that they have reached the end** of life, **go outside the** city **wall and die.**

Returning to the elders' declaration: *lo ba leyadenu upatarnuhu belo mezonot v'lo ra'inuhu v'hinachnuhu belo levayah* — the stranger did not come before us such that we dismissed him without food, and we did not see him pass through and leave him without accompaniment. The declaration contains within it two distinct communal obligations: the provision of sustenance (*mezonot*) and *levayah*, accompaniment.

### R. Meir: Enforcement

*Hachi darish Rabbi Meir omer kofin l'levayah* — Rabbi Meir taught: we coerce accompaniment. The obligation of *levayah* is not merely a matter of good character; it is of such fundamental importance that it may be enforced. *She'sechar ha'levayah ein lo shiur* — the reward for accompanying one's fellow has no measure.

### City of Luz

Rabbi Meir derives this from the story in *Shoftim* involving the city of Luz. When the Israelites were conquering the land, watchmen saw a man leaving a city. *Vayomru lo hareinu na et mevo ha'ir* — they asked him to show them the entrance to the city. *Ve'asinu imach chesed* — and we will deal kindly with you. He showed them, and while the entire city was put to the sword, *v'oto ha'ish umishpachto shilechu* — that man and his family were sent free. He went to the land of the Hittites, built a new city, and named it Luz — and the *gemara* identifies this as the Luz of later tradition, a city that dyed *techelet*, survived the campaigns of Sancheriv and Nevuchadnetzar undisturbed, and into which the Angel of Death has no entry. When its elders finally tire of life, they must leave the city walls in order to die.

This is an astonishing reward. The man had arguably betrayed his own people — he was a collaborator with the conquering force. Yet the *gemara* reads his act as one of *levayah*: he accompanied them toward their destination, showing them the way. The theological message is that the act of accompanying someone on their way carries merit of an almost unbounded kind, even when the context is fraught.

### Canaanite Pointer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא דְּבָרִים קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה כְּנַעֲנִי זֶה שֶׁלֹּא דִּיבֵּר בְּפִיו, וְלֹא הָלַךְ בְּרַגְלָיו — גָּרַם הַצָּלָה לוֹ וּלְזַרְעוֹ עַד סוֹף כׇּל הַדּוֹרוֹת, מִי שֶׁעוֹשֶׂה לְוָיָה בְּרַגְלָיו — עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Are** these **matters not** inferred ***a fortiori*: And if this Canaanite, who did not speak with his mouth** and explicitly tell them where the city entrance was, **and did not walk** with them **by foot,** but merely indicated the correct path to them, nevertheless **caused himself** to be **rescued and** also had the merit to provide rescue **for his descendants until the end of all generations,** then with regard to **one who accompanies** another **by foot, all the more so** will his reward be great.

The *gemara* then intensifies the lesson through a *kal v'chomer*: *uma kena'ani ze shelo diber befiv v'lo halach beraglav* — this Canaanite neither spoke aloud nor walked alongside them; he merely indicated the way — whether with his lips or with his finger, the *amora'im* dispute — *garam hatzalah lo ul'zar'o ad sof kol hadoros*. He brought salvation to himself and his descendants until the end of all generations. *Mi she'oseh levayah beraglav* — one who actually accompanies another on foot — *al achat kama v'chama* — how much more so will such a person merit.

### Torah Accompaniment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: הַמְהַלֵּךְ בַּדֶּרֶךְ וְאֵין לוֹ לְוָיָה — יַעֲסוֹק בַּתּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי לִוְיַת חֵן הֵם לְרֹאשֶׁךָ וַעֲנָקִים לְגַרְגְּרֹתֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: One who walks along the way without** having someone to **accompany** him **should occupy himself with** words of **Torah, as it is stated** with regard to words of Torah: **"For they shall be a chaplet of grace to your head, and chains around your neck"** (Proverbs 1:9).

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi addresses the case of one who travels alone with no one to accompany him: *yiasok b'Torah* — he should occupy himself with Torah. The verse says *ki livyat chen hem l'roshecha* — for they are an ornament of grace for your head — a play on the word *levayah*, accompaniment. Torah accompanies the solitary traveler and affords the protection that human accompaniment would otherwise provide.

### Pharaoh & Abraham

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: בִּשְׁבִיל אַרְבָּעָה פְּסִיעוֹת שֶׁלִּוָּה פַּרְעֹה לְאַבְרָהָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְצַו עָלָיו פַּרְעֹה אֲנָשִׁים וְגוֹ׳״, נִשְׁתַּעְבֵּד בְּבָנָיו אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת שָׁנָה — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַעֲבָדוּם וְעִנּוּ אֹתָם אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת שָׁנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi** further **says: Due to four steps that Pharaoh accompanied Abraham, as it is stated: "And Pharaoh gave men charge concerning him,** and they brought him on the way, and his wife, and all that he had" (Genesis 12:20), Pharaoh **enslaved** Abraham's **descendants** for **four hundred years, as it is stated: "And shall serve them, and they shall afflict them four hundred years"** (Genesis 15:13).

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi further teaches: *bishvil arba pesi'ot sheleivah Paroh l'Avraham* — because Pharaoh walked four steps to accompany Avraham, as the verse states *vayetzav alav Paroh anashim vayishalchu oto v'et ishto v'et kol asher lo* — he commanded men to escort Avraham and his wife and all his possessions — *nishtabedu b'vanav arba meot shanah* — Avraham's descendants were enslaved for four hundred years, as the verse states *va'avaddum v'inu otam arba meot shanah*. Four steps of accompaniment yielded four hundred years of dominion. This is a striking and paradoxical formulation: the reward for Pharaoh's *levayah* was the power to subjugate the very family he had honored. The arithmetic — four steps, four hundred years — makes the principle vivid and arresting.

### Four Amot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כׇּל הַמְלַוֶּה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בָּעִיר — אֵינוֹ נִיזּוֹק. רָבִינָא אַלְוְיֵהּ לְרָבָא בַּר יִצְחָק אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בָּעִיר, מְטָא לִידֵיהּ הֶיזֵּיקָא וְאִיתַּצִּיל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: Anyone who accompanies his friend four cubits in a city will come to no harm** by accompanying him. The Gemara relates: **Ravina accompanied Rava bar Yitzḥak four cubits in a city. He came close to harm, but he was saved.**

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav teaches: *ha'malveh et chavero arba amot b'ir eino nizok* — one who accompanies a neighbor even four *amot* within a city will not be harmed. The protective benefit of *levayah* accrues to the one who accompanies. Beyond the formal spiritual merit, there is a practical logic: a traveler accompanied even briefly signals to bystanders that he has a protector and is not unguarded. Walking someone even a short distance out of one's home communicates care and connection, discouraging those who might otherwise take advantage of a stranger traveling alone.

### Ravina & Rescue

The *gemara* concludes this section with an illustrative incident: *Ravina alveiya l'Rava bar Yitzchak arba amot b'ir* — Ravina accompanied Rava bar Yitzchak four *amot* in the city. *Mata lideih hezika v'ishtaziv* — he subsequently encountered something that would have caused him injury, and he was saved from it. The protective promise of *levayah* materialized in a concrete and immediate way.
