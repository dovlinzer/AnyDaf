# Sotah 46 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Equidistant Towns

### Body Equidistant

The *mishnah* before us concerns the ritual of *egla arufa*, the broken-necked calf performed when an unidentified murder victim is found in an open field. The procedure requires the elders of the *beit din hagadol* in Yerushalayim to come out and measure which nearby town — specifically, which town possessing its own *beit din* — is closest to the corpse, and it is that town's elders who must carry out the ritual.

The *mishnah* addresses a striking edge case: *nimtza mechuvon bein shtei ayarot* — what if the body is found precisely equidistant between two towns?

### R. Eliezer: Two Calves

*Shteihen mevi'ot shtei agalot divrei Rabbi Eliezer* — Rabbi Eliezer rules that each town brings its own calf. The phrasing makes clear that it is not both towns jointly bringing two calves, but each town independently bringing one. It is worth noting that the *mishnah* here cites this as *divrei Rabbi Eliezer* without recording a dissenting view — a formulation that clearly implies the ruling was contested, even if the opposing opinion is not spelled out.

This ruling might seem strange. After all, there is only one body and, one might think, only one responsible town. One could have suggested that the two *batei din* cooperate in bringing a single calf with a *tenai* — a stipulation that if the body is closer to one town, the calf counts as that town's *egla*, and if closer to the other, it counts as the other's. Alternatively, one might have argued for complete exemption: since the verse specifies *ha'ir hakerova*, the nearby city (in the singular), perhaps when no single city qualifies as definitively nearest, the entire obligation lapses. Rabbi Eliezer rejects both options and requires two calves.

### Precise Measurement

The *gemara* explains the reasoning: *kasavar efshar l'tzamtzem* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that precise equidistance is a real and legally cognizable possibility. The position of *i'efshar l'tzamtzem* would hold that in principle one town must always be fractionally closer, and our inability to measure with sufficient precision merely creates a *safek*. On that view, you would indeed bring one calf with a *tenai*. But Rabbi Eliezer maintains that *efshar l'tzamtzem* — genuine equidistance can exist, and when it does, each town bears its own independent obligation.

As for the concern that the singular language of *ha'ir hakerova* might exempt the case of two towns entirely, the *gemara* dismisses this: *v'afillu krovot* — the word *kerova* is not understood as excluding the scenario of multiple equidistant towns. There is no textual basis for such an exclusion.

### Jerusalem Exempt

The *mishnah* also states that Yerushalayim does not bring an *egla arufa*, even if it happens to be the nearest city. The *gemara* explains: *de'amar kra lerishtah* — the verse ties the obligation to a city given as an inheritance to one of the tribes, and Yerushalayim was never apportioned to any individual tribe. It remained, in a sense, an inter-tribal city, and therefore does not fall within the category of *lerishtah*.

## Separated Head

### Head vs. Body

The *mishnah* continues with another unusual case: *nimtza rosho b'makom echad v'gufo b'makom acher* — the head of the corpse is found in one location and the body in another. Rabbi Eliezer rules *molichin et harosh etzel haguf* — you bring the head to the body. Rabbi Akiva rules the opposite: *haguf etzel harosh* — you bring the body to the head.

At first glance this dispute seems to replicate the later dispute at the end of the *mishnah* about where one measures from. Rabbi Eliezer, who focuses on the body, says one measures from the belly button; Rabbi Akiva, who focuses on the head, says one measures from the nostrils. The positions appear perfectly parallel. However, the fact that the *mishnah* presents them as two separate disputes suggests that the earlier dispute — about which part of the body you bring to which — is dealing with a distinct question.

### Met Mitzvah

The *gemara* resolves the apparent duplication. Rabbi Yitzchak explains that the first dispute is actually about burial: *be'met mitzvah kanah mekomo* — a *met mitzvah*, a corpse with no one to bury it, acquires its place of rest where it lies. The question when the head and body are separated is therefore: to which location does the body "belong" for purposes of burial? Rabbi Eliezer says you bring the head to the body, since the body is buried where it is found. Rabbi Akiva says you bring the body to the head.

### Head or Body Moved

The *gemara* then asks: what is the underlying disagreement that drives these two rulings? *Mar savar gufa b'duchtei nafal reisha denada venafal* — one opinion holds that the body fell in place at the spot of the killing, and it was the head that flew off. Therefore the location of the body is the true location of the death, and the head must be brought there. *V'mar savar reisha heicha denafal nafal gufa hu derahit v'azal* — the other opinion holds that the head fell right where it was severed, while the body kept moving forward — as a body sometimes does when suddenly decapitated — before finally falling at a different spot. On this view, the head's location is the true locus of death.

### Measurement Points

Turning to the second dispute, the *mishnah* presents three views on which part of the body to measure from when determining which town is nearest. Rabbi Eliezer says *mitiburo* — from the belly button. Rabbi Akiva says *mechotamo* — from the nostrils. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov says *mimakom shena'asah chalal* — from the place that made the person a *chalal*, a slain corpse — which he identifies as *mitzavaro*, the neck.

### Life Force

The *gemara* identifies the underlying question between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva: *mar savar ikar chayuta b'apei* — one holds that the essential life force of a person resides in the nostrils; *v'mar savar ikar chayuta b'tibura* — the other holds it resides in the belly button. This debate also arises in the context of *pikuach nefesh* in Yoma, specifically regarding how to determine whether a person trapped under rubble is still alive. Some wish to read this as a dispute about whether life is defined by respiration or by circulation — though the belly button's connection to circulation is unclear. It seems more likely that both positions are describing breath, with the belly button understood as relating to the diaphragm and the rhythmic rise and fall of breathing.

### Development & Life

The *gemara* entertains the possibility that this dispute parallels a separate *tannaitic* debate: *me'heichan ha'valad notzar* — from where does a fetus develop in the womb? One *tanna* says from the head; *Abba Shaul* says from the belly button, since the belly button is at the center, and development radiates outward in all directions. Each cites the verse *mi'mei immi atah gozi* in support.

The *gemara* concludes, however, that even *Abba Shaul* would agree that the locus of *chiyuta* — life force — is the nostrils. *Ad kan lo ka'amar Abba Shaul ela le'inyan yetzirah* — his position concerns *yetzirah*, fetal development, not the seat of life itself. *Aval le'inyan chiyuta dechulei alma b'apeih hu* — when it comes to the life force, everyone agrees it is in the nostrils, as the verse states: *kol asher nishmat ruach chayyim b'appav* — all in whose nostrils is the breath of life. The *gemara* thus consistently comes down on the side of breath in the nostrils as the defining measure of life — a point of considerable significance for contemporary halachic discussions surrounding the definition of death.

### R. Elazar: Neck

Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov's position — that one measures from the neck — derives from the verse *latet otcha al tzavverei challalei resha'im*, which locates the image of a *chalal*, a sword-slain corpse, at the neck. Since the typical *chalal* involves decapitation, the neck is the defining anatomical locus of that category of death.

## Ritual Procedure

### Elders Depart

Once the nearest city has been identified through measurement, *niftaru ziknei Yerushalayim v'halchu lahem* — the elders of Yerushalayim depart. The ritual itself is now the responsibility of the local elders of the identified town.

### Calf Requirements

*V'ziknei otah ha'ir mevi'in eglat bakar* — the elders of that town bring a calf. The calf must be *asher lo mashcha b'ol* — one that has not drawn a yoke, meaning it must never have been used for labor. Notably, however, *v'ein ha'mum posel bah* — a physical blemish does not invalidate it. The presence or absence of a *mum* is irrelevant; only prior *avoda*, labor, disqualifies.

### Wadi Eitan

*U'moridin otah l'nachal eitan* — they bring it down to a *nachal eitan*, a hard wadi. *Eitan k'mashma'o kasheh* — the word *eitan* in its plain sense means hard, referring to bedrock. Nevertheless, *af al pi she'eino eitan kasher* — even if the wadi is not actually of hard bedrock, it remains valid after the fact; the requirement of hard rock is only *lechatchilah*.

### Breaking the Neck

*V'orfin otah b'kofitz me'achoreha* — they break its neck from behind using a hatchet. This is done from behind the neck, not the front.

### Ground Forbidden

*U'mekomo assur milizro'a umila'avod le'olam* — the location where the ritual was performed is permanently forbidden for planting and agricultural work.

### Permitted Uses

*U'mutar lisrok sham pishtan ul'naker sham avanim* — it is, however, permitted to comb flax there or to clear stones from the area, since these activities do not involve working the soil itself. The prohibition falls specifically on uses that engage the ground; standing or sitting on the location, or conducting activities that merely take place upon it without making use of the earth, remain permitted.

## Declaration & Blessing

### Hand Washing

*Ziknei oto ir rochtzim et yedeihem b'mayim b'makom arifah* — the elders of the town wash their hands with water at the place where the neck was broken, over the calf. There is something visually striking about this: on the one hand, the washing of hands in a ritual context conveys assumption of responsibility, a cleansing of moral contamination; on the other hand, it calls to mind the idiom of "washing one's hands of" a matter, of disavowal. Perhaps both ideas are present — that there was a kind of defilement, a residual moral burden, that must be actively addressed and cleansed through the ritual act.

### Three Declarations

Following the hand washing, the elders declare: *yadeinu lo shafchu et hadam hazeh v'eineinu lo ra'u* — our hands did not shed this blood and our eyes did not see.

The *gemara* asks: surely no one suspected the *beit din* of murder — why must they declare that their hands did not spill the blood? The answer: *ela shelo ba leyadenu upatarnuhu belo mezonot velo ra'inuhu v'hinachnuhu belo levayah* — the declaration is not a denial of having committed murder, but an assertion of having fulfilled their communal obligations. They are affirming that this traveler did not come to them seeking help and get turned away without food; that they did not see him passing through and fail to accompany him.

This is a remarkable and demanding standard. The declaration does not merely address the question of direct culpability; it addresses the question of whether the community exercised sufficient care for the stranger in their midst. If a man had passed through town asking for shelter or assistance, and the *beit din* was aware of him and did nothing, they would be unable to make this declaration. The ritual thus encodes a positive communal responsibility for the welfare of travelers.

### Priestly Prayer

*V'hakohanim omrim kaper l'amcha Yisrael asher padita Hashem v'al titein dam naki b'kerev amcha Yisrael* — the *kohanim* then recite the prayer: atone for Your people Israel whom You have redeemed, God, and do not place the guilt of innocent blood in the midst of Your people.

### Divine Atonement

The *mishnah* notes that the final clause of the *pasuk* — *v'nichpar lahem hadam*, and the blood shall be atoned for them — is not part of the *kohanim*'s declaration. Rather, *ela ruach hakodesh mevasertam* — the divine spirit announces it to them as a promise: *eimatai sheta'asu kachah hadam yitkaper lahem* — when you perform this ritual, atonement for the blood will follow.

This raises a striking question about the theological structure of the *egla arufa* ritual. The *mishnah* earlier noted that the calf is killed by breaking its neck from behind — not by *shechitah* from the front, as in a *korban*. Why? The deliberate departure from the form of sacrifice signals that this is not a *korban* in the conventional sense. Yet the ritual does involve the shedding of blood in a context of atonement.

One way to understand this is that the *egla arufa* functions not as a sacrifice but as a substitute execution. The verse states *ki la'aretz lo yechupar ladam asher shupach bah ki im bedam shofcho* — the only true atonement for innocent blood is the execution of the murderer. Since the murderer is unknown and cannot be executed, the calf is slain in his stead. The blood being atoned for is the victim's innocent blood; the calf's blood is not what atones — rather, it is the act of execution, performed upon an innocent creature in place of the guilty one, that achieves atonement. This may be precisely why *avoda*, prior labor, disqualifies the calf: not only must it be young and never have borne offspring, it must also be wholly untarnished by human use — innocent, in the fullest sense.

## Mum & Avoda

### Blemish Permitted

The *mishnah*'s ruling that a *mum* does not disqualify the *egla* invites scrutiny. One might have expected the opposite conclusion based on a *kal v'chomer* from *parah adumah*.

### Kal VeChomer

The *gemara* constructs the argument: *u'mah parah she'ein ha'shanim posel bah mum posel bah* — by a *parah adumah* the age of the animal is irrelevant, and yet a *mum* disqualifies it. *Eglah she'shanim posel bah* — an *eglah* must be young (the word *eglah* itself implies a young animal), so age is a disqualifying factor. If even *parah adumah*, where age is not an issue, requires the absence of a *mum*, surely an *eglah*, which is already stricter with respect to age, should require the absence of a *mum* as well. The *kal v'chomer* seems compelling.

### Mum vs. Avoda

The *gemara* rejects it: *shani hatam de'amar kra asher ein bah mum* — by *parah adumah*, the Torah explicitly specifies *asher ein bah mum*, that there be no blemish in it. The word *bah* — "in it" — limits the requirement to *parah adumah* specifically. The absence of a *mum* is a requirement *bah*, in the *parah*, and need not be extended elsewhere.

But this immediately generates a counter-argument: if the word *bah* by *parah adumah* restricts the *mum* requirement to *parah* alone and prevents us from extending it to *eglah*, then by the same logic the word *bah* by *eglah* — which says *asher lo uvad bah*, that it was not worked — should restrict the *avoda* disqualification to *eglah* alone and prevent us from extending it to *parah adumah*. Yet we clearly do apply the *avoda* requirement to *parah adumah* (as seen from the ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav discussed below). How can we apply *avoda* from *eglah* to *parah* while simultaneously refusing to apply *mum* from *parah* to *eglah*?

### Avoda & Kodshim

The resolution is elegant and surprising. The *gemara* states: *hahi miba'ei lei l'mi'utei kodshim d'lo pasla behu avoda* — the word *bah* by *eglah* is not being used to exclude *parah adumah* from the *avoda* requirement. Rather, it is being used to exclude *kodshim* — sacrificial animals — from the *avoda* requirement. Remarkably, prior labor does not disqualify a *korban*.

This is counterintuitive. One would naturally assume that *kodshim* are subject to at least as stringent requirements as *eglah* and *parah adumah*. But the *gemara* holds the opposite: the disqualification of *avoda* is specific to these two non-*korban* contexts and does not apply to consecrated offerings. An animal that has been used for agricultural labor can still be brought as a *korban*, as long as it otherwise meets the required criteria.

The *bah* by *parah adumah* therefore functions as follows: it restricts the *mum* requirement to *parah adumah*, excluding *eglah* (which indeed has no *mum* requirement). The *bah* by *eglah* restricts the *avoda* requirement to *eglah*, excluding not *parah* but *kodshim*. The two *bah*s are doing different limiting work and are not in tension.

### Three Sources

The *gemara* then asks: is this really where we learn that *avoda* does not disqualify *kodshim*? Isn't that derived from the verse *iver o shavur o charutz o yavelet o garav o yalefet lo takrivu eleh la-Hashem* — a list of blemishes that disqualify offerings? *Eleh atah makriv* — only these disqualify, but an animal that was used for labor you may bring. So we already know *avoda* is not a problem for *kodshim* from this verse.

The *gemara* responds that this verse alone would not have been sufficient. Without the *bah* by *eglah*, one might have thought the *eleh* verse only exempts *avodat heter* — labor performed when it was still ordinary, unconsecrated property — but *avodat issur*, labor performed in a prohibited manner (such as being yoked in a forbidden mixture), might still create a metaphysical defect. The *bah* teaches that even *avodat issur* does not disqualify.

A second verse — *u'mi'yad ben nechar lo takrivu et lechem Eloheikhem mi'kol eleh* — also teaches the exemption. But even two verses would not have been enough: one might have thought the exemption applies only when the labor was performed while the animal was still *chullin*, unconsecrated property, but not when the labor was performed after the animal was already consecrated as *kadosh*. The third source — the *bah* by *eglah* — teaches that even labor performed on an animal after it was already consecrated as a *korban* does not disqualify it.

This is a remarkable ruling. Even if one violated the sanctity of the *korban* by using it for labor after it had been designated, the offering remains valid. It may well be that the person incurred *me'ilah* — misappropriation of consecrated property — but the *korban* itself retains its halachic status.

The overall picture is therefore illuminating: *eglah* has no *mum* requirement but does have an *avoda* requirement. *Korbanot* have a *mum* requirement but no *avoda* requirement. *Parah adumah* has both — and in this sense stands in the middle, sharing characteristics with both.

### Bundle of Sacks

The *gemara* now returns to examine the statement of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: *ha'maniach chavilah shel sakin al ha'parah* — if one placed a bundle of sacks upon the *parah adumah*, it is invalid. This is striking because a bundle of sacks is not an *ol*, a yoke — yet it disqualifies. Meanwhile, the rule by *eglah* is *u've'eglah ad she'timshoch* — by an *eglah* the animal must actually draw the load, actually walk with it, for the *avoda* to be operative.

Why the asymmetry? Why is *parah adumah* disqualified merely by having a burden placed upon it, while *eglah* requires the animal to actually move?

### Gezeirah Shava

The answer lies in the different legal sources for the two rulings. By *eglah*, the verse reads *asher lo mashcha b'ol* — which did not draw with a yoke. The term *mashcha*, drawing, connotes actual movement. And by *parah adumah*, the verse reads *asher lo alah aleha ol* — upon which no yoke has gone up. If we were to learn *parah* from *eglah* via a *gezeirah shavah* on the shared word *ol* — *ne'emar kan ol v'ne'emar lehalan ol* — then we would expect *parah* to also require actual movement, *meshichah*, just as *eglah* does.

The *gemara* confirms this logic: *mah lehalan ad she'timshoch af kan ad she'timshoch* — if the source is *eglah*, the threshold should be the same. Rav Yehuda's ruling that even placing a bundle of sacks without movement disqualifies the *parah* must therefore reflect an alternative source that does not derive from *eglah*.

This is indeed a *tannaitic* dispute: some derive the *avoda* disqualification for *parah* from *eglah* via the *gezeirah shavah*, in which case the definition is parallel — movement is required. Others derive it from the internal language of the *parah adumah* verse itself, in which case the conceptual framework is different.

### Ol vs. Avoda

According to the view that derives the rule from the *parah* verse itself, the operative phrase is not *avoda* at all. The disqualification comes from the words *asher lo alah aleha* — upon which nothing has gone up. The word *ol*, yoke, at the end of the phrase then has a specific additional function: *ol posel bein be'sha'at avoda bein shelo be'sha'at avoda* — a yoke disqualifies whether placed for the purpose of work or not. The mere presence of a yoke upon the *parah* is disqualifying, regardless of intent or use. For all other burdens, however, they disqualify only *be'sha'at avoda* — in the context of actual labor use. Rashi explains this to mean not that the animal must have walked, but that the burden must have been placed upon it for the purpose of work, not merely set down in passing.

On this reading, the conceptual framework for *parah adumah* is not fundamentally about *avoda* at all — it is about *alah aleha*, having anything placed upon it. The significance of the word *ol* in the verse is specifically to inform us that a yoke, even placed without any intent to use it for work, is immediately disqualifying.

### Tefach Movement

By contrast, for *eglah*, where the standard is *meshichah* — actual drawing — the *gemara* records a tradition from Rabbi Yochanan as transmitted by Rabbi Yaakov: *meshichat ol l'rochbo tefach* — the drawing of the yoke requires the animal to have moved the width of a yoke, which is a *tefach*. Rabbi Abbahu had posed the question of whether the relevant dimension was the length or the width of the yoke; Rabbi Yaakov transmitted that Rabbi Yochanan had specified the width, which is a *tefach*. This formulation also conveys a practical corollary for commercial purposes: a yoke that is less than a *tefach* in width does not qualify as a yoke at all and could be rejected in a sale transaction.

## Theology

### Never Produced

Rabbi Yochanan ben Shaul offers a theological reading of why the Torah requires both a calf and a wadi: *mipnei mah amra Torah hevi eglah b'nachal* — why did the Torah say to bring a calf down to the wadi? *Amar ha'Kadosh Baruch Hu yavo davar shelo asah peirot* — the Holy One said: let come something that has never produced fruit. The calf, young and never used for labor, is an animal that has produced nothing; the wadi, hard and barren, is a place that produces nothing.

### Wasted Potential

*V'ye'aref bimkom she'ein oseh peirot vichaper al mi shelo hinicho la'asot peirot* — and let it be killed in a place that does not produce anything, to atone for one who was not permitted to produce fruit. The victim — murdered, perhaps in youth — was a person whose potential was cut off before it could be realized.

### Fruit = Mitzvot

The *gemara* asks: *mai peiros* — what do we mean by "fruit" in this context? If it means *priyah v'rivyah*, children, then one would not perform *egla arufa* for an elderly man or a sterile person who could not have had children regardless — which is surely not the case. Rather, *ela mitzvot* — "fruit" means deeds, the good acts and *mitzvot* one might have performed in a lifetime. Every murder is an assault not merely on a life but on all the potential *mitzvot* that life would have generated. The calf and the wadi together form a symbolic tableau of unrealized potential, offering atonement for a world diminished by the act of violence.

### Ancient Ground

The *gemara* also explores the meaning of *nachal eitan*. One opinion derives from verses that *eitan* denotes hardness — a wadi of hard, rocky ground. Another opinion, *acherim omrim*, holds that *eitan* means ancient: *nachal eitan* is a wadi whose soil has never been disturbed, ground that goes back to primordial times, ground that carries with it no history of human cultivation. The two readings are not necessarily in conflict; Rashi reads *eitan* as ground that has never been dug up, which combines both the sense of hardness and antiquity.

## Ground Restrictions

### From Behind

*V'orfin otah b'kofitz me'achoreha* — the neck is broken from behind with a hatchet. The *gemara* derives this through *gezeirah shavah*: *gamar arifa arifa me'chatat ha'of* — the word *arifa* here is linked to the word *arifa* used in the context of *chatat ha'of*, the bird sin-offering, where the priest pinches the neck from behind. Just as there the action is performed from the nape of the neck, so here too the breaking of the neck is from behind.

### Past vs. Future

The verse states *asher lo ye'aved bah v'lo yizare'a* regarding the site of the *egla arufa*. A *tannaitic* dispute addresses whether this language refers to the past or the future. Rabbi Yoshiya reads it as describing the past: the wadi must be one that has not previously been worked or planted. Rabbi Yonatan reads it as prescribing the future: henceforth, this ground may not be worked or planted.

Rava clarifies: *lehaba d'chulei alma lo peligi* — on the future prohibition, there is no dispute. *V'lo yizare'a* means "shall not be seeded" — this is universally accepted as a prospective prohibition. The debate is only about *leshe'avar*, whether the verse additionally specifies that the ground must have been previously unworked. Rabbi Yoshiya argues that *asher* introduces a description of a past state; Rabbi Yonatan argues that had the Torah meant the past it would have said *ne'evad*, and the form used implies future obligation.

### All Soil Usage

A *braita* extends the scope of the future prohibition. The verse says *asher lo ye'aved bo* — it shall not be worked — and *lo yizare'a* — and it shall not be planted. From the combination, the *braita* derives: *ein li ela zri'ah, she'ar avodot minayin* — I know only that planting is forbidden; how do I know all other forms of soil work are also forbidden? *Talmud lomar asher lo ye'aved bo mikol makom* — the phrase "shall not be worked" encompasses all soil use. The specific mention of planting then teaches the principle: *mah zri'ah meyuchedet she'hi b'gufah shel karka, af kol she'hi b'gufah shel karka assur* — just as planting is distinctive in that it engages the body of the earth itself, so anything that engages the body of the earth is forbidden. *Yatza srikat pishtan v'nikur avanim* — excluded are combing flax and clearing stones, which take place upon the ground without working the earth itself.

## Accompaniment

### Declaration Details

Returning to the elders' declaration: *lo ba leyadenu upatarnuhu belo mezonot v'lo ra'inuhu v'hinachnuhu belo levayah* — the stranger did not come before us such that we dismissed him without food, and we did not see him pass through and leave him without accompaniment. The declaration contains within it two distinct communal obligations: the provision of sustenance (*mezonot*) and *levayah*, accompaniment.

### R. Meir: Enforcement

*Hachi darish Rabbi Meir omer kofin l'levayah* — Rabbi Meir taught: we coerce accompaniment. The obligation of *levayah* is not merely a matter of good character; it is of such fundamental importance that it may be enforced. *She'sechar ha'levayah ein lo shiur* — the reward for accompanying one's fellow has no measure.

### City of Luz

Rabbi Meir derives this from the story in *Shoftim* involving the city of Luz. When the Israelites were conquering the land, watchmen saw a man leaving a city. *Vayomru lo hareinu na et mevo ha'ir* — they asked him to show them the entrance to the city. *Ve'asinu imach chesed* — and we will deal kindly with you. He showed them, and while the entire city was put to the sword, *v'oto ha'ish umishpachto shilechu* — that man and his family were sent free. He went to the land of the Hittites, built a new city, and named it Luz — and the *gemara* identifies this as the Luz of later tradition, a city that dyed *techelet*, survived the campaigns of Sancheriv and Nevuchadnetzar undisturbed, and into which the Angel of Death has no entry. When its elders finally tire of life, they must leave the city walls in order to die.

This is an astonishing reward. The man had arguably betrayed his own people — he was a collaborator with the conquering force. Yet the *gemara* reads his act as one of *levayah*: he accompanied them toward their destination, showing them the way. The theological message is that the act of accompanying someone on their way carries merit of an almost unbounded kind, even when the context is fraught.

### Canaanite Pointer

The *gemara* then intensifies the lesson through a *kal v'chomer*: *uma kena'ani ze shelo diber befiv v'lo halach beraglav* — this Canaanite neither spoke aloud nor walked alongside them; he merely indicated the way — whether with his lips or with his finger, the *amora'im* dispute — *garam hatzalah lo ul'zar'o ad sof kol hadoros*. He brought salvation to himself and his descendants until the end of all generations. *Mi she'oseh levayah beraglav* — one who actually accompanies another on foot — *al achat kama v'chama* — how much more so will such a person merit.

### Torah Accompaniment

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi addresses the case of one who travels alone with no one to accompany him: *yiasok b'Torah* — he should occupy himself with Torah. The verse says *ki livyat chen hem l'roshecha* — for they are an ornament of grace for your head — a play on the word *levayah*, accompaniment. Torah accompanies the solitary traveler and affords the protection that human accompaniment would otherwise provide.

### Pharaoh & Abraham

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi further teaches: *bishvil arba pesi'ot sheleivah Paroh l'Avraham* — because Pharaoh walked four steps to accompany Avraham, as the verse states *vayetzav alav Paroh anashim vayishalchu oto v'et ishto v'et kol asher lo* — he commanded men to escort Avraham and his wife and all his possessions — *nishtabedu b'vanav arba meot shanah* — Avraham's descendants were enslaved for four hundred years, as the verse states *va'avaddum v'inu otam arba meot shanah*. Four steps of accompaniment yielded four hundred years of dominion. This is a striking and paradoxical formulation: the reward for Pharaoh's *levayah* was the power to subjugate the very family he had honored. The arithmetic — four steps, four hundred years — makes the principle vivid and arresting.

### Four Amot

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav teaches: *ha'malveh et chavero arba amot b'ir eino nizok* — one who accompanies a neighbor even four *amot* within a city will not be harmed. The protective benefit of *levayah* accrues to the one who accompanies. Beyond the formal spiritual merit, there is a practical logic: a traveler accompanied even briefly signals to bystanders that he has a protector and is not unguarded. Walking someone even a short distance out of one's home communicates care and connection, discouraging those who might otherwise take advantage of a stranger traveling alone.

### Ravina & Rescue

The *gemara* concludes this section with an illustrative incident: *Ravina alveiya l'Rava bar Yitzchak arba amot b'ir* — Ravina accompanied Rava bar Yitzchak four *amot* in the city. *Mata lideih hezika v'ishtaziv* — he subsequently encountered something that would have caused him injury, and he was saved from it. The protective promise of *levayah* materialized in a concrete and immediate way.
