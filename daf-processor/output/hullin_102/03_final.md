# Hullin 102 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ever Min HaChai Intro

### Definition & Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי נוֹהֵג בִּבְהֵמָה, חַיָּה וָעוֹף, בֵּין טְמֵאִין וּבֵין טְהוֹרִין, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ נוֹהֵג אֶלָּא בִּטְהוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught in a *baraita*: The prohibition of eating **a limb from a living animal applies** whether the limb comes from **a domesticated animal, an undomesticated animal, or a bird,** and **whether** it is from **a non-kosher** species **or** from **a kosher** species; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Elazar. But the Rabbis say:** The prohibition of eating a limb from a living animal **applies only to** a limb from **a kosher** species.

The daf under discussion is 102, though the material begins at the bottom of 101b with the words *tana rabanan*. Having previously examined the debate concerning whether *gid hanasheh* applies to non-kosher animals, the Gemara now turns to a parallel — though distinct — discussion regarding *ever min hachai*.

The *braita* opens: *ever min hachai noheg bivehemah chayah ve'of bein tme'in bein tehorin divrei Rabbi Yehudah* — the prohibition of *ever min hachai* applies to domesticated animals (*behemah*), wild animals (*chayah*), and even birds, whether kosher or non-kosher. This is the position of Rabbi Yehudah. The same Rabbi Yehudah who holds that *gid hanasheh* applies to non-kosher animals maintains the same position regarding *ever min hachai*. Without being stated otherwise, the discussion presupposes a Jewish audience, though the application to *bnei Noach* will emerge shortly.

One implication of Rabbi Yehudah's view is that when a Jew eats an *ever min hachai* from a non-kosher animal, two prohibitions are simultaneously violated — the prohibition of non-kosher food and the prohibition of *ever min hachai*. This raises the question of *issur chal al issur*, which the Gemara will address in turn.

### Verse Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וּשְׁנֵיהֶן מִקְרָא אֶחָד דָּרְשׁוּ, ״רַק חֲזַק לְבִלְתִּי אֲכֹל הַדָּם כִּי הַדָּם הוּא הַנָּפֶשׁ וְלֹא תֹאכַל הַנֶּפֶשׁ עִם הַבָּשָׂר״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: And both of them,** i.e., the Rabbis as well as Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Elazar, **derived** their opinions **from one verse: "Only be steadfast in not eating the blood, for the blood is the life; and you shall not eat the life with the flesh"** (Deuteronomy 12:23).

Rabbi Yochanan teaches that both Rabbi Yehudah and his disputants are interpreting a single verse: *rak chazak levilti achol hadam ki hadam hu hanefesh velo tochal hanefesh im habasar* — "Only be strong not to eat the blood, for the blood is the life force, and you shall not eat the life force with the meat" (Devarim 12:23). This verse is understood as the source for *ever min hachai*, and indeed for *basar min hachai* as well.

Notably, the prohibition is not stated in terms of a limb explicitly; rather, *nefesh* — life force — is read as referring to the limb. Rashi explains this by noting that just as life, once lost, does not return, so too a limb severed from an animal does not regenerate. The verse thus prohibits eating the *nefesh* — understood as the limb — *im habasar*, while the life force remains in the meat, i.e., while the animal is still alive.

A subtler point emerges from the structure of the verse. The word *vav* connecting *velo tochal hanefesh im habasar* to what precedes it is significant: without the *vav*, one might read the clause as an independent prohibition concerning blood. With the *vav*, the phrase is connected to the blood prohibition, and the shift in the referent of *nefesh* — from blood in the preceding clause to the limb or life force in the following clause — becomes the crux of the interpretive debate. The reading *lo tochal hanefesh im habasar* is taken to mean: do not eat the limb when the life force is still in the animal, which is the prohibition of *ever min hachai*.

### Yehudah & Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר סָבְרִי: כֹּל שֶׁאַתָּה מְצוֶּּוה עַל דָּמוֹ אַתָּה מְצוֶּּוה עַל אֵבָרָיו, וְהָנֵי טְמֵאִין נָמֵי, הוֹאִיל וְאַתָּה מְצוֶּּוה עַל דָּמָן אַתָּה מְצוֶּּוה עַל אֵבָרָין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Elazar hold** that with regard to **any** animal **whose blood you are commanded** not to eat, **you are commanded with regard to its limbs,** i.e., you are prohibited from eating its limbs that were severed while it was still alive. Consequently, with regard to **these non-kosher** species **also, since you are commanded** not to eat **their blood, you are commanded with regard to their limbs.**

The Gemara records: *vedivrei Rabbi Yehudah veRabbi Eliezer* — Rabbi Eliezer joins Rabbi Yehudah in holding that *ever min hachai* applies to non-kosher animals. The reasoning: *kol she'atah metzuveh al damo atah metzuveh al evarav* — any animal whose blood one is prohibited from eating, one is likewise prohibited from eating its limbs when torn off alive. Since the blood of non-kosher animals is prohibited — the prohibition of blood applies across the board, including to non-kosher species — it follows that *ever min hachai* from non-kosher animals is equally prohibited. There is no categorical exclusion, and no insuperable problem of *issur chal al issur* stands in the way, for reasons that will be spelled out.

### Rabbis' Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: ״לֹא תֹאכַל הַנֶּפֶשׁ עִם הַבָּשָׂר״, אֶלָּא בָּשָׂר לְחוֹדֵיהּ, כֹּל שֶׁבְּשָׂרוֹ מוּתָּר – אַתָּה מְצוֶּּוה עַל אֵבָרָיו, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵין בְּשָׂרוֹ מוּתָּר – אִי אַתָּה מְצוֶּּוה עַל אֵבָרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis hold** that the verse indicates: **"And you shall not eat the life with the flesh"** (Deuteronomy 12:23), but **rather** you shall eat **the flesh alone,** i.e., when the animal is no longer alive. Consequently, with regard to **any** animal **whose flesh is permitted** when it is slaughtered, **you are commanded with regard to its limbs,** i.e., you are prohibited from eating its limbs that were severed while it was still alive. **But** with regard to **any** animal **whose flesh is not permitted** when it is slaughtered, **you are not commanded with regard to its limbs,** i.e., the prohibition of eating limbs that were severed while it was still alive does not apply.

This is a categorical exclusion closely analogous to the Rabbis' position by *gid hanasheh*: just as the prohibition of the *gid* applies only to animals whose meat may be eaten, the prohibition of *ever min hachai* applies only to animals whose flesh is otherwise permissible. The underlying logic is not merely technical. The Torah's concern is with imposing additional restrictions upon what one is already permitted — do not eat the *gid*, do not eat a limb torn from a living animal — within the domain of permissible food. For animals that are already categorically forbidden, that logic does not obtain. One is not going to be eating non-kosher animals in any capacity, so there is no need for a special prohibition on their limbs. The restriction is meaningful precisely within the space of what is permitted, not outside it.

## Why Need Verse

### Yehudah Needs No Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, לְמָה לֵיהּ קְרָא? לֵיתֵי אִיסּוּר אֵבֶר לֵיחוּל עַל אִיסּוּר טוּמְאָה, שֶׁכֵּן אִיסּוּרוֹ נוֹהֵג בִּבְנֵי נֹחַ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** according to **Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that a prohibition takes effect upon an already existing, less stringent prohibition, **why does he** need to derive this from **a verse? Let the prohibition of** eating **a limb** from a living animal **come and take effect on the prohibition** of eating meat from **a non-kosher** animal, as it is more stringent **since its prohibition applies** also **to descendants of Noah,** i.e., gentiles.

Having established the positions of Rabbi Yehudah and Rabbi Eliezer on one side and the Rabbis on the other, the Gemara raises a question specifically about Rabbi Yehudah: *lama li kra*? Why does Rabbi Yehudah need a verse to establish that *ever min hachai* applies to non-kosher animals? The Gemara suggests that he should not need one at all. *Leiti issur evier liychol al issur tumah* — the prohibition of *ever min hachai* should simply take effect on top of the pre-existing prohibition against non-kosher food without any special scriptural mandate, because the second prohibition is broader in scope than the first. The argument: *sheken issur noheig bivnei Noach* — *ever min hachai* is a prohibition that applies to *bnei Noach*, to all non-Jews, and therefore encompasses a wider group of people than the prohibition of non-kosher food, which applies only to Jews. Since the second prohibition (*ever min hachai*) has a larger scope than the first (the prohibition of non-kosher animals, which burdens only Jews), it qualifies as an *issur kolel* and should be able to take effect even where the first prohibition already exists, without requiring a special verse.

### Verse for Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין הָכִי נָמֵי, וְכִי אִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Yes,** that **is indeed so.** Rabbi Yehuda does not need to derive this *halakha* from a verse, **and the verse was necessary** only **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar,** who holds that a prohibition does not take effect where there is an already existing prohibition, even if the second prohibition is more stringent.

The Gemara resolves the question: *ein hachi nami* — indeed, you are correct. According to Rabbi Yehudah, no verse was needed; the larger scope of *ever min hachai* is sufficient to allow it to take effect even on non-kosher animals. *V'chi itzterich kra l'Rabbi Eliezer* — the verse is needed for Rabbi Eliezer. Rabbi Eliezer's general view regarding *issur chal al issur* is not independently established; we cannot assume he would ordinarily permit one prohibition to take effect on top of another even on the basis of larger scope. Hence a special scriptural derivation is required in his case, linking the prohibition of *ever min hachai* to the prohibition of blood and thereby extending it to non-kosher animals just as the blood prohibition extends to them.

### Issur Kolel/Mosif

This analysis invites a fuller consideration of the categories that permit *issur chal al issur*. The basic principle is that one prohibition generally does not take effect upon an item already subject to another. However, there are recognized exceptions.

The first is *issur kolel* — a more inclusive prohibition. This can mean that the second prohibition encompasses more objects than the first: for instance, on *Yom Kippur*, not only is non-kosher meat forbidden, but all food is forbidden. When I become ritually impure (*tamei*), not only is the specific meat before me prohibited, but all *korban* meat is now prohibited to me. In both cases, the second prohibition sweeps in a wider category of objects and therefore takes effect even where the first prohibition already applies.

The case at hand — *ever min hachai* applying to non-kosher animals — illustrates a different dimension of *issur kolel*: not more objects but more persons become subject to the prohibition. *Ever min hachai* applies to all *bnei Noach*, and therefore when a limb is torn from a non-kosher animal, the prohibition applies to all people. This wider scope of persons subject to the second prohibition is what enables it to take effect on top of the first. A comparable example would be if a man's sister subsequently married; she was already forbidden to him, but now, as a married woman, she becomes forbidden to all men — a broader prohibition applies, and that breadth is what creates the additional prohibition for him as well.

The second category, *issur mosif*, is related but distinct. Rashi understands it as a weightier prohibition, while the more broadly accepted understanding is that it refers precisely to the category of more persons becoming prohibited — the very category illustrated above. These represent the two primary categories — *issur kolel* and *issur mosif* — under which one prohibition can ordinarily take effect on top of another. A third consideration, *issur chamur*, involves a prohibition that is weightier in some other sense: it may be forbidden in benefit (*hana'ah*), it may carry *kares*, or it may have applied to the children of Yaakov prior to Sinai. These are secondary considerations; the primary operative categories are those of larger scope.

The Gemara's conclusion is that even for one who does not generally accept Rabbi Yehudah's position regarding *gid hanasheh* and the weight of *bnei Noach*-era prohibitions, and even for one uncertain about whether larger scope suffices on its own, here by *ever min hachai* in a non-kosher animal there are two grounds for the prohibition to take effect: the larger scope (*issur kolel* of *bnei Noach*) and a special verse linking the prohibition to blood, which extends it to non-kosher animals just as the blood prohibition extends to them.

## Braita & Bnei Noach

### Braita Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי נוֹהֵג בִּבְהֵמָה חַיָּה וָעוֹף, בֵּין טְמֵאָה בֵּין טְהוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״רַק חֲזַק לְבִלְתִּי אֲכֹל הַדָּם״. כֹּל שֶׁאַתָּה מְצוֶּּוה עַל דָּמוֹ – אַתָּה מְצוֶּּוה עַל אֵבָרָיו, וְכֹל שֶׁאִי אַתָּה מְצוֶּּוה עַל דָּמוֹ – אִי אַתָּה מְצוֶּּוה עַל אֵבָרָיו, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ נוֹהֵג אֶלָּא בִּטְהוֹרִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״לֹא תֹאכַל הַנֶּפֶשׁ עִם הַבָּשָׂר״, אֶלָּא בָּשָׂר לְחוֹדֵיהּ, כֹּל שֶׁבְּשָׂרוֹ מוּתָּר – אַתָּה מְצוֶּּוה עַל אֵבָרָיו, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵין בְּשָׂרוֹ מוּתָּר – אֵין אַתָּה מְצוֶּּוה עַל אֵבָרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **This** fact that it is Rabbi Elazar who derives this *halakha* from the verse, as opposed to Rabbi Yehuda, **is also taught** in a *baraita*: The prohibition of eating **a limb from a living** animal **applies with regard to** a limb from **a domesticated animal, an undomesticated animal, or a bird,** and **whether** it is from **a non-kosher** species **or a kosher** species, **as it is stated: "Only be steadfast in not eating the blood,** for the blood is the life; and you shall not eat the life with the flesh"** (Deuteronomy 12:23). This verse indicates that with regard to **any** species **whose blood you are commanded** not to eat, **you are commanded with regard to its limbs,** i.e., you are prohibited from eating its limbs that were severed while it was still alive. **And any** species **about whose blood you are not commanded,** i.e., with regard to which the prohibition of eating blood does not apply, **you are not commanded with regard to their limbs.** Since the prohibition of eating blood applies even with regard to non-kosher species, the prohibition of eating limbs severed from a living animal also applies; this is **the statement of Rabbi Elazar. And the Rabbis say:** The prohibition of eating a limb from a living animal **applies only to kosher** species, **as it is stated** in the verse: **"And you shall not eat the life with the flesh"** (Deuteronomy 12:23), but **rather** you shall eat **the flesh alone,** i.e., when the animal is no longer alive. Consequently, with regard to **any** animal **whose flesh is permitted** when it is slaughtered, **you are commanded with regard to its limbs,** i.e., you are prohibited from eating its limbs that were severed while it was still alive. **But** with regard to **any** animal **whose flesh is not permitted** when it is slaughtered, **you are not commanded with regard to its limbs,** i.e., the prohibition of eating limbs that were severed while it was still alive does not apply.

The Gemara brings a *braita* in support: *ever min hachai noheg b'vehema chaya v'of bein tmeia bein tehora* — *ever min hachai* applies to domesticated animals, wild animals, and birds, whether kosher or non-kosher. The verse adduced: *rak chazak l'vilti achol hadam* — and from there the principle: *kol she'atah mtzuvah al damo atah metzuveh al evarav* — any creature whose blood is prohibited, its limbs are likewise prohibited when torn off alive. This includes non-kosher animals and even birds, since the blood prohibition extends to birds. Conversely, *ve-chol she-ei atah metzuveh al damo ei atah metzuveh al evarav* — for creatures whose blood is not prohibited, such as fish and grasshoppers, there is no prohibition of *ever min hachai*. This teaching is attributed to Rabbi Eliezer — and the Gemara notes this pointedly, since it is precisely for Rabbi Eliezer that the verse is needed, as established above. Rabbi Yehudah requires no verse; the *braita* clarifies the *drasha* and identifies its primary recipient.

The Rabbis' counter-position is stated in the same *braita*: *velo tochal hanefesh im habasar* — *ela basar le-chud*, and since the prohibition is framed around eating the meat when the life force remains in the animal, it is limited to *kol she-besaro mutar* — animals whose meat is otherwise permissible. *Kol she-ein besaro mutar ein atah metzuveh al evarav* — by non-kosher animals, no such prohibition applies. This is, again, less a matter of *issur chal al issur* and more a categorical exclusion grounded in the plain sense of the verse: the Torah's restriction on *ever min hachai* is addressed to what one eats, and non-kosher animals were never within the domain of permissible eating.

### Rabbi Meir Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ נוֹהֵג אֶלָּא בִּבְהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir says** that the prohibition of eating a limb from a living animal **applies only with regard to a kosher** species **of domesticated animal,** but not with regard to undomesticated animals or fowl, even if they are kosher.

The Gemara now introduces a third position. Rabbi Meir holds that *ever min hachai* applies not only to kosher animals but specifically to kosher *behemah* — domesticated animals — to the exclusion of *chayah* (wild animals) and birds. His reasoning is textual: the verse introducing the entire passage reads *ve-zavachta mi-be-karecha u-mi-tzoncha* — "and you shall slaughter from your cattle and from your flock." The prohibition of blood and the prohibition of *ever min hachai* derived from this context are therefore limited to the species explicitly mentioned in the introduction: cattle and sheep. Birds and wild animals are excluded, not because of any philosophical argument, but because the verse's own framing does not encompass them.

The Rabbis, by contrast, read *dibra hatorah bahoveh* — the Torah speaks in the customary manner; the mention of cattle and sheep does not restrict the verse's reach. The blood prohibition is not limited to cattle and sheep, and neither is *ever min hachai*.

### Bnei Noach Apply

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל, אָמַר רַב: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, אֲבָל בְּבֶן נֹחַ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מוּזְהָר עַל הַטְּמֵאִים כַּטְּהוֹרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Giddel says** that **Rav says:** The **dispute** between the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Eliezer about whether the prohibition of eating a limb severed from a living animal applies with regard to non-kosher species is only **with regard to Jews. But with regard to descendants of Noah,** i.e., gentiles, **everyone agrees** that they are **prohibited from** eating a limb from a living **non-kosher** species of animal just **like** they are prohibited from eating a limb from a living **kosher** species.

Rav Gidel citing Rav states: *machloket beYisrael* — the entire debate about whether *ever min hachai* applies to non-kosher animals pertains to Jews. *Aval bivnei Noach divrei hakol muzar le-tme'im ke-tehorim* — but for non-Jews, all opinions agree that *ever min hachai* applies to non-kosher animals just as to kosher ones. For *bnei Noach*, there is no issue of *issur chal al issur* with respect to non-kosher animals, since non-kosher animals are not prohibited to them in the first place. And the verse's implied logic — that the restriction applies only to animals one is otherwise permitted to eat — poses no obstacle either, since for *bnei Noach*, non-kosher animals are entirely permissible. There is therefore no reason to exclude non-kosher animals from the scope of *ever min hachai* for *bnei Noach*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי – בֶּן נֹחַ מוּזְהָר עָלָיו עַל הַטְּמֵאִים כַּטְּהוֹרִים, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל אֵינוֹ מוּזְהָר אֶלָּא עַל הַטְּהוֹרִין בִּלְבַד. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי ״טְהוֹרָה״, וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי ״טְהוֹרִים״, וְרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments that **this is also taught** in a *baraita*: **A descendant of Noah is prohibited** from eating **a limb from a living** animal from **non-kosher** species just **as** from **kosher** species. **But a Jew is prohibited** from eating a limb from a living animal of **kosher** species **only. There are** those **who say** that the correct wording of the *baraita* is that a Jew is prohibited from eating a limb from a living **kosher** species, in singular, **and** it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** who holds that the prohibition is limited to kosher domestic animals. And **there are** those **who say** that the correct wording of the *baraita* is that a Jew is prohibited from eating a limb from living **kosher** species, in plural, **and** it is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who hold that the prohibition also applies to a limb from a living bird or a living undomesticated animal of kosher species.

This does not mean that *bnei Noach* are in a stricter position than Jews in any absolute sense. A Jew who eats *ever min hachai* from a camel violates the prohibition against eating camel. A non-Jew who does the same violates the prohibition of *ever min hachai*. Each receives one prohibition; neither receives two. The *braita* confirms this: *ben Noach muzar lo al ever min hachai al hatme'im ketehorim* — a *ben Noach* is prohibited in *ever min hachai* from non-kosher animals just as from kosher ones. *VeYisrael eino muzar ela al hatehorin bilvad* — and a Jew is subject to *ever min hachai* only regarding kosher animals, in accordance with the position of the *chakhamim*.

A textual variant in the *braita* is noted: some versions read *tehorah* (singular), which would align the ruling for Jews with Rabbi Meir's position — limiting *ever min hachai* to domesticated kosher animals only. Other versions read *tehorim* (plural), aligning with the *chakhamim*, who include all kosher animals: birds, cattle, sheep, and deer alike.

## Non-Kosher Bird Mishna

### Mishna Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁיזְבִי: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא, אָכַל אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי מִמֶּנָּה אֵינוֹ סוֹפֵג אַרְבָּעִים, וְאֵין שְׁחִיטָה מְטַהַרְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheizvi said: We learn** in the mishna **as well** that a gentile is prohibited from eating a limb from a living being even with regard to non-kosher species (*Teharot* 1:3): If one **ate a limb** severed **from a living** non-kosher bird, **he does not incur forty** lashes, **and slaughter does not purify it,** i.e., cause it to become permitted for consumption.

Rav Shizvi notes that the following *mishna* supports the foregoing discussion. The *mishna* states: *achal ever min hachai mimena eino sofeg arba'im* — one who ate an *ever min hachai* from a non-kosher bird does not receive forty lashes. Furthermore, *ve'ein shechitata metahareta* — slaughtering it does not render it *tahor*.

Two difficulties arise immediately. First, why does one not receive lashes for eating the non-kosher bird itself? Second, the phrase *ein shechitata metahareta* requires clarification — does it refer to *tumah* (ritual impurity) or to *kashrus*?

### Jews vs Bnei Noach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי? אִילֵימָא בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל – פְּשִׁיטָא דְּאֵין שְׁחִיטָה מְטַהַרְתָּהּ! אֶלָּא לָאו בִּבְנֵי נֹחַ, מִכְּלָל דְּאָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheizvi clarifies: **With regard to what** case does the mishna issue this ruling? **If we say** that it is referring **to a Jew** who ate the limb, it is **obvious that slaughter does not purify it,** because it is a non-kosher species and cannot be made kosher. **Rather** is it **not** referring **to descendants of Noah** and teaching that even after the bird is slaughtered the limb severed while it was alive remains forbidden? **By inference,** it is clear from the mishna **that it is prohibited** for a gentile to eat a limb severed from a living being even from a non-kosher species.

The Gemara works through these questions. If the *mishna* is addressing a Jew (*ileima beYisrael*), then the statement that *shechita* does not render it *tahor* is trivially obvious — no one imagines that slaughtering a non-kosher bird makes it kosher. And the *tumah* reading is equally unavailable: a non-kosher bird does not carry the *tumah* of *neveilah* in the first place. The *tumah* of birds — a distinctive and unusual category — applies only to kosher birds that died without proper *shechita*, and only upon being swallowed. A non-kosher bird carries no *tumas neveilah* regardless of how it died. So *ein shechitata metahareta* cannot be read as a statement about *tumah* if we are discussing a non-kosher bird, and it cannot be a meaningful statement about *kashrus* if we are talking about Jews. The *mishna* must therefore be addressing *bnei Noach*.

For *bnei Noach*, the question is concrete: they are permitted to eat non-kosher animals, but they are prohibited in *ever min hachai*. What is the status of *shechita* in resolving that prohibition? This leads directly to the next subsection.

### Shechita Effects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מָנִי בַּר פַּטִּישׁ רָמֵי רֵישָׁא אַסֵּיפָא, וּמְשַׁנֵּי: רֵישָׁא בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, וְסֵיפָא בְּבֶן נֹחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Mani bar Pattish raises a contradiction** between **the first clause** of that mishna, which states that one does not receive lashes for eating a limb severed from a living non-kosher bird, **and the latter clause** of the mishna, which states that a limb severed from a living non-kosher bird remains forbidden after the bird is slaughtered. The first clause indicates that the prohibition of eating a limb severed from a living creature applies only to kosher species, while the latter clause indicates that it applies also to non-kosher species. **And he answers** this contradiction by explaining that **the first clause** is referring **to a Jew and the latter clause** is referring **to a descendant of Noah.**

The question of *shechita*'s effect on *ever min hachai* differs between Jews and *bnei Noach*. For a Jew, the moment an animal is slaughtered, it is considered halachically dead and permissible to be eaten; even if the animal is still flopping about, a limb taken after *shechita* is not *ever min hachai*. For a *ben Noach*, by contrast, the animal is not considered dead until it has actually ceased moving; *shechita* alone does not suffice to remove the prohibition of *ever min hachai*. The animal must be biologically and physically still.

This raises a further question: if a Jew slaughters a kosher animal and it is still flopping, may a *ben Noach* eat a limb from it? Some hold that since for the *ben Noach* the animal may not yet be considered dead, such a limb would remain prohibited to him as *ever min hachai*. This creates a potential paradox — something permissible for the Jew but forbidden to the *ben Noach*. As a result, some authorities hold that since the animal is treated as dead for purposes of Jewish law (the *shechita* having taken effect), it is treated as dead for the *ben Noach* as well, and he may eat the limb. Others go the opposite direction and impose the stricter standard even on the Jew. The resolution is not uniform.

For a non-kosher animal — say, an ostrich — the situation is different. *Shechita* does not render the ostrich permissible to the Jew in any case. And therefore, *shechita* does not remove the *ever min hachai* prohibition from the ostrich for the *ben Noach* either. *Ein shechitata metahareta* — the *shechita* does not render it permissible to the *ben Noach* so long as the animal is still alive. Only when it is fully dead does the *ever min hachai* concern cease.

### Proof from Mishna

This confirms Rav's ruling: *mach'loket beYisrael* — but for *bnei Noach*, the prohibition of *ever min hachai* extends to non-kosher animals universally.

The contradiction between the beginning and the end of the *mishna* — the opening, which speaks of not receiving lashes, seemingly implying no prohibition of *ever min hachai* by non-kosher birds, and the conclusion, which implies the opposite — is resolved by Rav Mani bar Patish: *reisha b'Yisrael v'seifa b'Bnei Noach*. The opening of the *mishna* addresses Jews, for whom *ever min hachai* does not apply to non-kosher animals and who therefore do not receive lashes for that prohibition (though the question of why they also do not receive lashes for the non-kosher prohibition is addressed separately). The conclusion of the *mishna* addresses *bnei Noach*, for whom *ever min hachai* does apply to non-kosher animals, and for whom *shechita* does not resolve the prohibition as long as the animal continues to move.

## Shiur for Transgression

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי צָרִיךְ כְּזַיִת, מַאי טַעְמָא? אֲכִילָה כְּתִיבָה בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says: A limb** severed **from a living** animal **requires an olive-bulk** in order to render one who eats it liable to receive lashes. **What is the reason** for this? It is because the term: **Eating, is written with regard to** the prohibition of a limb severed from a living animal, and the definition of eating is the consumption of at least one olive-bulk.

Having concluded the discussion of which animals are subject to *ever min hachai*, the Gemara turns to the question of the *shiur* — how much must one eat to transgress. Rav states: *ever min hachai tzarich kezayis* — one must eat a *kezayis* to transgress the prohibition of *ever min hachai*, just as is the rule for all other food prohibitions. The reason: *achila kesiva bah* — the verse employs the language of eating (*lo tochal*), and eating in halachic contexts requires a *kezayis*.

### Rav Amram's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב עַמְרָם: אָכַל אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי מִמֶּנָּה – אֵינוֹ סוֹפֵג אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים, וְאֵין שְׁחִיטָה מְטַהַרְתָּהּ. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ בָּעֵינַן כְּזַיִת – תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּקָאָכֵל כְּזַיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Amram raises an objection** from the mishna cited above (*Teharot* 1:3): If one **ate a limb** severed **from a living** non-kosher bird, **he does not incur the forty** lashes, **and slaughter does not purify it,** i.e., cause it to become permitted. **But if it enters your mind** that **we require an olive-bulk** in order for one to receive lashes for eating a limb severed from a living creature, that mishna must be referring to such a case; therefore, it should **emerge** that he is liable to receive lashes **because he ate an olive-bulk** of a non-kosher bird.

Rav Amram challenges this from the *mishna* cited earlier. The *mishna* states that one who eats *ever min hachai* from a non-kosher bird does not receive lashes. The question: if Rav is correct that a *kezayis* is required, then in the scenario the *mishna* describes, the person has eaten a *kezayis* worth of the limb — so why does he not receive lashes for eating a *kezayis* of a non-kosher bird? *V'isalka daatach be'inan kezayis teipok lei d'achal kezayis* — if the scenario involves eating a *kezayis* of the *ever*, one ought to be liable for eating a *kezayis* of a non-kosher bird regardless. The fact that the *mishna* says no lashes are incurred suggests that the *ever min hachai* scenario does not necessarily involve eating a *kezayis* of the animal's meat, which in turn implies that a *kezayis* of meat is not what is required.

### Meat vs Limb

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ בָּשָׂר גִּידִין וַעֲצָמוֹת, הָכָא נָמֵי בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ בָּשָׂר גִּידִין וַעֲצָמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This can be explained **as Rav Naḥman said** with regard to another issue, that it is referring to a case **of** eating **a small amount of meat** together with **sinews and bones** and the total volume is an olive-bulk. **Here also,** the mishna is referring to a case **of** eating **a small amount of meat** together with **sinews and bones.**

The Gemara resolves this tension by invoking an explanation attributed to Rav Nachman. *Bemashu basar gidim ve-ha'atzamot* — the limb in question contains only a small amount of actual meat, while the rest of its *kezayis* is composed of sinews and bones. The *kezayis* required for *ever min hachai* is a *kezayis* of the *ever* as a whole — meat, sinews, and bones combined — not a *kezayis* of meat alone. The prohibition of non-kosher food, by contrast, requires a *kezayis* of meat specifically. Therefore, it is possible for a limb to constitute a *kezayis* for purposes of *ever min hachai* (meat plus bone together reaching a *kezayis*) while not providing a *kezayis* of meat (since the meat component alone is less than a *kezayis*). In such a case, one would transgress *ever min hachai* without incurring lashes for the non-kosher prohibition — exactly what the *mishna* says.

## Whole Unit Principle

### Living Bird Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַב: אָכַל צִפּוֹר טְהוֹרָה בְּחַיֶּיהָ – בְּכׇל שֶׁהוּא, בְּמִיתָתָהּ – בִּכְזַיִת, וּטְמֵאָה בֵּין בְּחַיֶּיהָ בֵּין בְּמִיתָתָהּ – בְּכׇל שֶׁהוּא, הָכָא נָמֵי בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ בָּשָׂר גִּידִים וַעֲצָמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another challenge to Rav's statement that one receives lashes for eating a limb severed from a living animal only if he eats an olive-bulk: **Come** and **hear** a proof in this regard, **as Rav said:** If one **ate a kosher bird alive,** one is punished with lashes **for any amount,** i.e., even if he ate less than an olive-bulk. If the bird **was dead** and was not ritually slaughtered, one is punished with lashes only if he ate **an olive-bulk. And** if one ate a whole bird of **a non-kosher** species, **whether** he ate it **when it was alive or dead,** he is punished with lashes **for any amount,** i.e., even if the bird contained less than an olive-bulk of meat. The Gemara answers: **Here also** explain that Rav's statement is referring to a case **of** eating **a small amount of meat** along with **sinews and bones,** and the total volume is an olive-bulk.

The Gemara brings a further teaching from Rav: *achal tzipor tehora be-chayeha be-chol shehu* — if one ate a whole living kosher bird, one transgresses even with the smallest amount. The entire bird, eaten alive, constitutes an *ever min hachai* violation without any *kezayis* requirement, because eating the whole unit is sufficient.

### Dead vs Non-Kosher

If the bird is dead, the analysis changes. *U-ve-mitata be-kezayit* — a dead bird creates a *neveilah* concern, and for *neveilah* a *kezayis* is universally required. The Torah does not identify the dead animal as a specific unit that is forbidden; rather, the prohibition of *neveilah* concerns the meat generically, and therefore the standard *kezayis* measure governs.

If the bird is non-kosher, the rule is different in the other direction: *bein be-chayeha bein be-mitata be-mashehu* — whether alive or dead, the smallest amount suffices. A non-kosher bird is forbidden as a unit — *al tochal* applies to the specific entity — and the principle of *biryah* (an entire creature) applies. If one ate the entire non-kosher ant, or the entire non-kosher bird, regardless of whether the quantity is less than a *kezayis*, one transgresses, because the Torah prohibits the entity itself.

The distinction can be stated as follows. The principle of *biryah* — that a whole entity suffices even without a *kezayis* — depends on whether the Torah identifies the forbidden item as a specific unit. When the Torah says do not eat non-kosher birds, it identifies the bird as the unit, and eating the whole bird transgresses even if it is tiny. When the Torah says do not eat from a *neveilah*, it refers to the flesh generically, not to any specific creature, and therefore the standard *kezayis* applies. *Ever min hachai*, when the whole living bird is eaten, is treated like the former: the *ever* or the unit is identified as what is prohibited, and the whole suffices.

### Small Bird Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: נָטַל צִפּוֹר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ כְּזַיִת וַאֲכָלוֹ – רַבִּי פּוֹטֵר, וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בַּר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מְחַיֵּיב. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: קַל וָחוֹמֶר, עַל אֵבֶר מִמֶּנָּה חַיָּיב, עַל כּוּלָּהּ לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן! חֲנָקָהּ וַאֲכָלָהּ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל בִּכְזַיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** another challenge based upon a *baraita*: With regard to one who **took** a live **bird** of a kosher species **that did not have** a volume of **an olive-bulk and ate it, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **exempts** him from lashes **and Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon deems** him **liable** for lashes. **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, said:** He should be liable based upon **an *a fortiori*** inference: If one **is liable** to receive lashes **for** eating **a limb of** a living animal, **all the more so** is it **not** clear that one is liable to receive lashes **for** eating **all of it?** The *baraita* concludes: With regard to one who **strangled** the bird **and ate it, everyone agrees** that he is liable to receive lashes only if it had the volume **of an olive-bulk.**

A *braita* introduces a further case: *natal tzipor she-ein bo kezayit ve-achalo* — one took a bird that does not contain a *kezayis* in the entire bird and ate it. Rabbi (Rebbe) holds one exempt; Rabbi Elazar son of Rabbi Shimon holds one liable. Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon argues by way of *kal vachomer*: *al ever mimena chay al kula lo kol shekein* — if one is liable for eating even a limb torn from a living animal, one is certainly liable for eating the whole animal alive. Rebbe's position, by implication, is that the prohibition of *ever min hachai* is specifically addressed to a limb removed from a living animal; eating the entire living animal whole is a different act, not captured by the prohibition of *ever min hachai*. Rashi suggests that eating the whole animal would still constitute *basar min hachai*, for which a *kezayis* of meat would be required.

If one strangled the bird and then ate it — *chanka ve'achla* — all agree that a *kezayis* is required, since at that point the prohibition is *neveilah* rather than *ever min hachai*.

### Limb Division Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי, אֶלָּא דְּמָר סָבַר: בְּחַיֶּיהָ לְאֵבָרִים עוֹמֶדֶת, וּמָר סָבַר: בְּחַיֶּיהָ לָאו לְאֵבָרִים עוֹמֶדֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*: Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, **disagree only with regard to** one who ate an entire live bird, **as** one **Sage,** Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, **holds** that even **during its life** all creatures **stand** to be divided **into limbs,** i.e., they will ultimately be cut into pieces. Consequently, each of its limbs is considered a separate entity, and even if one eats a limb without first separating it from the rest of the body, one violates the prohibition of eating a limb from a living being. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **holds** that **during its life** it **does not stand** to be divided **into limbs.** Therefore, one does not violate the prohibition of eating a limb from a living being unless he severs a limb from the body.

The Gemara frames the debate between Rebbe and Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon as turning on a conceptual question: *bechayei evarim omedet, u-mar savar bechayei lav evarim omedet* — does one regard a living animal as already subdivided into its constituent limbs, or does one regard a living animal as a unified living creature not yet conceived of as limbs? If the animal is conceptually already divided into limbs while alive, then eating the whole thing is eating all the limbs together, and one transgresses *ever min hachai*. If the animal is regarded as a single living entity rather than as a collection of limbs, then eating it whole does not constitute eating an *ever*, and one does not transgress *ever min hachai* on those grounds.

Crucially, the Gemara notes that on one matter all agree:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִיהָא לָא בָּעֵינַן כְּזַיִת, אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ בָּשָׂר, גִּידִים וַעֲצָמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** But **in any event, everyone** agrees that **we do not require** one to eat **an olive-bulk** of meat in order to be liable for lashes for eating a limb from a living being, which is the basis of the *a fortiori* inference of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon. **Rav Naḥman says** in response: Explain that the *baraita* is referring to a case **where** one ate **a small amount of meat** together with **sinews and bones,** making a total of an olive-bulk.

No *kezayis* is required. The case in the *braita* involves a bird with no *kezayis* in the whole bird, yet Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon holds one liable and Rebbe holds one exempt — not because of the *kezayis*, but because of the question of whether the whole constitutes an *ever*. Neither party is troubled by the absence of a *kezayis*. This itself is a significant implication: eating an *ever*, or an entire unit equivalent to an *ever*, does not require a *kezayis*.

## Kezayit Resolution

### Limb Kezayit Reinterpret

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי, דִּבְכוּלֵּיהּ לֵית בֵּיהּ כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר, וּבְחַד אֵבֶר אִית כְּזַיִת בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ בָּשָׂר גִּידִין וַעֲצָמוֹת? אָמַר רַב שֵׁרֵבְיָא: אִין, בִּקְלָנִיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this answer: **But is there any** bird **that does not have an olive-bulk of meat in its whole** body, **but in one limb it has an olive-bulk** of the combination of **a small amount of meat** together with **sinews and bones? Rav Sherevya said: Yes,** this is the case **with regard to** the ***kelanita*,** a bird that is very small and scrawny.

At this point the Gemara attempts to reconcile all of this with Rav's original statement that *ever min hachai* requires a *kezayis*. The resolution offered is a reinterpretation: when the *braita* says the bird does not contain a *kezayis*, it does not mean the bird has no *kezayis* at all. It means there is not a *kezayis* of meat alone — *bemashu basar, gidim va'atzamot* — only a small amount of meat, with the remainder being sinews and bones. The *kezayis* of the *ever* (meat plus bone and sinew together) does exist; what is absent is a *kezayis* of meat in isolation. This allows Rav's position — that a *kezayis* of the *ever* is required — to remain intact.

The Gemara is candid that this reinterpretation is a stretch. The simple sense of the *braita* and the proofs adduced is that an *ever*, as a whole unit, suffices even without a *kezayis*. Rav's position requires a more contrived reading of the sources, and the Gemara's resolution consistently employs the same device: interpreting "no *kezayis*" to mean no *kezayis* of meat, while maintaining that there is a *kezayis* of the whole limb.

### Kalanisa Bird

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא סֵיפָא: חֲנָקָהּ וַאֲכָלָהּ, דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל בִּכְזַיִת, וְהָא קְלָנִיתָא עוֹף טָמֵא הוּא, וְאָמַר רַב: טְמֵאָה, בֵּין בְּחַיֶּיהָ בֵּין בְּמִיתָתָהּ, בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ! אֶלָּא כְּעֵין קְלָנִיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this answer that the *baraita* is referring to a *kelanita*. **Say the latter clause:** With regard to one who **strangled** the bird **and ate it, everyone agrees** that he is liable to receive lashes only if it had the volume of **an olive-bulk. But a *kelanita* is a non-kosher bird, and Rav says:** If one eats a whole **non-kosher** bird, **whether** he eats it **when it is alive or dead,** he is punished with lashes **for** eating **any amount.** The Gemara answers: The *baraita* is not referring to an actual *kelanita*, but **rather** to a kosher species of bird **similar** to **a *kelanita*** in that it is small and scrawny.

The Gemara presses the scenario further. If one holds that a *kezayis* of the limb is required — meaning even bone and sinew count toward the *kezayis* — then the case of eating a whole bird involves a bird where the entire bird has a *kezayis* (otherwise no transgression would occur), yet has no *kezayis* of meat. Furthermore, the *braita* indicates that if one eats a limb, all agree one transgresses, which on Rav's view means the limb has a *kezayis*. We are therefore describing a bird where a single limb has a *kezayis* and yet the entire bird has no *kezayis* of meat. This would be a remarkably bony and spare bird. Rav Shiviya confirms that such a bird exists: the *kalanisa*, a notoriously bony bird with very little meat, such that one limb might constitute a *kezayis* (of bone, sinew, and flesh together) while the whole bird yields less than a *kezayis* of meat.

The Gemara immediately notes a difficulty: the *kalanisa* is a non-kosher bird. And Rav himself stated that for a non-kosher bird — whether alive or dead — one transgresses with the smallest amount (*be-mashehu*), because the *biryah* principle applies. The entire framing of the *braita*, where the debate between Rebbe and Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon hinges on whether one needs a *kezayis*, presupposes that the bird in question is kosher. For a non-kosher bird, there is no debate — one transgresses as a *biryah* regardless of size. The Gemara therefore concludes: *ela k'ein kalanisa* — not the *kalanisa* itself, which is non-kosher, but a kosher bird of similar characteristics: extremely bony, with minimal meat, where one limb reaches a *kezayis* but the whole bird provides less than a *kezayis* of meat. Whether such a kosher bird actually exists is uncertain, but the Gemara treats the description as not entirely impossible.

## Intent & Limbs

### Rava's Intent Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר סָבַר רַבִּי מַחְשְׁבֶת אֳכָלִין שְׁמָהּ מַחְשָׁבָה, חִישֵּׁב לְאוֹכְלָהּ אֵבֶר אֵבֶר, וַאֲכָלָהּ כּוּלָּהּ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: If you say** that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds** that **thought** with regard to **food is considered thought,** i.e., one's thoughts have halakhic significance in determining a food's status, then in a case where one **thought to eat** the bird **limb** by **limb, and** instead **ate it all** at once, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi would hold that he is **liable** for transgressing the prohibition of eating a limb from a living animal.

The Gemara turns to a striking application of subjective intent. Rava proposes: *im timtza lomar savar Rebbi machsheves ochalim shemah machshavah* — were one to say that Rebbe holds that the way one thinks about one's eating (*machsheves ochalim*) has legal significance, then the following would follow. *Chisheiv le-ochlah eiver eiver ve'achla kulla k'chayyei* — if, while the bird is still alive, one thought to oneself specifically about eating it limb by limb (for instance, thinking about the leg while the bird is still walking), and then swallowed the whole bird alive, Rebbe would concede that one has transgressed *ever min hachai*. The reason: one's subjective framing has already divided the animal into limbs in one's mind, and therefore when one ate it, the act is treated as having eaten an *ever* rather than a whole living unit.

This connects to a broader principle of *machsheves ochalim* — the significance of how one defines what one is eating. The related concept of *achshavei* (found elsewhere in the *poskim*, particularly in contexts of *Pesach* and matters of medicine) concerns elevating something ordinarily unfit for eating to the status of food by virtue of consuming it. The present case is different: it is not about whether something counts as food at all, but about whether a living animal is treated as subdivided into limbs or as a unified creature. Nevertheless, both share the feature of allowing subjective intent to shape how one defines the object of one's eating.

### Abaye's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וּמִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דְּאִילּוּ אָכֵיל לֵיהּ אַחֵר – לָא מִיחַיַּיב, וְאָכֵיל לֵיהּ הַאי – מִיחַיַּיב?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rava: **But is there anything** with regard to which **if another** person **ate it he would not be liable, but** if **this** person **ate it he is liable?**

Abaye objects: *umi ika midi d'ilu achla acher lo mechayev v'achla hai mechayev* — is there anything where if another person ate it they would not be liable, yet if this person eats it they would be liable? Abaye finds it deeply problematic to allow individual subjective intent to determine whether a halachic transgression occurs. The bird is what it is; its status should not vary based on what different people happen to be thinking. If one person's *machshavah* makes the bird into limbs while another person's neutral state of mind does not, then the same bird is simultaneously divided and undivided depending on who is looking at it — a result Abaye finds untenable.

### Rava's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זֶה לְפִי מַחְשַׁבְתּוֹ, וְזֶה לְפִי מַחְשַׁבְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to** Abaye: There can be different outcomes for different people who perform the same act, as **this** individual's action is judged **according to his thought and that** individual's action is judged **according to his thought.**

Rava's response is direct: *zeh lefi machshavto v'zeh lefi machshavto* — each person's transgression is determined by his own perspective. For the person who had the *machshavah* dividing the animal into limbs, the act of eating is treated as eating *ever min hachai*. For another person with no such intent, the act is treated as eating a whole living creature. Rava is not troubled by the divergence; each individual's subjective framing governs his own halachic reality.

### Reverse Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: אִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר סָבַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בַּר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַחְשְׁבֶת אֳכָלִין שְׁמָהּ מַחְשָׁבָה, חִישֵּׁב לְאוֹכְלָהּ מֵתָה, וַאֲכָלָהּ חַיָּה – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava** also **said: If you say** that **Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon holds** that **thought** with regard to **food is considered thought,** then in a case where one at first **intended to eat** the entire bird after it had **died, but** ultimately he **ate it alive,** Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon would hold that he is **exempt** from lashes, as according to his thought it does not stand to be divided into limbs while alive.

Rava then applies the same logic in the opposite direction. *Im timtza lomar savar Rebbe Elazar bar Rebbe Shimon machsheves ochalim shemah machshavah* — if Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon also holds that *machshavah* matters, and if the person eating the bird was thinking about it in the mode of its eventual death — planning how he would slaughter and prepare it, not thinking of its living limbs at all — *chishev l'ochlah misa*, then *v'achla chaya patur* — if he then ate the whole bird alive, he would be exempt. His subjective intent has un-divided the animal in his mind; he was not treating it as a collection of limbs, and therefore eating the whole does not constitute eating an *ever*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וּמִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי, דְּאִילּוּ אָכֵיל לֵיהּ אַחֵר מִחַיַּיב, וְאָכֵיל לֵיהּ הַאי פָּטוּר?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rava: **But is there anything that if another** person **ate it he would be liable, but if** this person **ate it he is exempt?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זֶה לְפִי מַחְשַׁבְתּוֹ, וְזֶה לְפִי מַחְשַׁבְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to** Abaye: **This** individual's action is judged **according to his thought and that** individual's action is judged **according to his thought.**

Abaye objects identically: *umi ika midi d'ilu achla acher mechayev v'achla hai patur* — how can it be that for anyone else who eats this bird (they being in the default state of treating it as limbs, per Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon's view) the act is a transgression, yet for this specific person, who was thinking about it differently, the act is exempt? Rava responds again: *zeh lefi machshavto v'zeh lefi machshavto* — each goes according to his own perspective.

It bears noting that the weight of halachic tradition sides with Abaye on this point. The general principle in *halacha* is that objective standards govern, and it is uncommon for individual subjective intent to redefine the halachic nature of an act for that person alone. Rava's position represents a genuinely striking departure from the norm.
