# Ketubot 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## First/Second Wedding

### Mishnah Overview

The second *perek* opens with a *mishnah* describing a dispute that arises not over the woman's physical state on the wedding night, but over the nature of the wedding itself — decades later, when she is being divorced and there is no *ketubah* to produce. The debate is not "why wasn't she a virgin?" but rather "was this a first or second wedding?" The *gemara* teaches that in such a case, the woman requires witnesses who can testify to the indicators of a first wedding: that she left her father's home with a full wedding procession, that roasted grain was distributed, that three hundred guests attended, or similar markers of a *nisu'in besulah*. Without those indicators, she would not be believed, because the operative principle is *ha-motzi mei-chaveiro alav ha-re'ayah* — the burden of proof rests on the one seeking to extract payment.

The *gemara* opens on 16a by asking: since witnesses are required, does this mean the *mishnah* does not follow Rabban Gamliel? After all, Rabban Gamliel consistently rules that the woman is believed. The *gemara* responds: *afillu teima Rabban Gamliel* — one can say even this *mishnah* follows Rabban Gamliel.

### Bari v'Bari Analysis

The *gemara* distinguishes the case of the first *perek* from this one. In the earlier cases, the dispute was *bari v'shema* — she makes a definite claim and he responds only with uncertainty. Here, both parties make definite claims: she says with certainty that it was a first wedding, and he says with equal certainty that it was a second. This is *bari u'bari* — two equally firm competing claims — and that is a qualitatively different situation. The principle of *ha-motzi mei-chaveiro alav ha-re'ayah* applies with full force.

### Rabban Gamliel Concedes

The *gemara* proceeds to establish that Rabban Gamliel concedes this case. The logical structure of the *mishnah* supports this: the *mishnah* closes with the statement *u'modeh Rabbi Yehoshua* — Rabbi Yehoshua concedes a certain case. The *gemara* argues that this concession only makes sense if the earlier portion of the *mishnah* represents Rabban Gamliel's concession in the *bari u'bari* scenario. If the entire *mishnah* were authored from Rabbi Yehoshua's perspective, then to whom is Rabbi Yehoshua conceding? The structure implies a mutual yielding: Rabban Gamliel concedes that in a *bari u'bari* dispute she does not win, and Rabbi Yehoshua in turn concedes a different point — the case of *ha-peh she'asar hu ha-peh she'hitir*.

### Rov Nashim Betulot

One might ask: even in a *bari u'bari* scenario, why not simply apply *rov nashim betulot nisos* — the statistical majority that most women marry for the first time? Since most weddings are first weddings, her claim is probabilistically more likely to be true. The *gemara* acknowledges this consideration and notes that this is precisely what might lead one to think Rabban Gamliel would still rule in her favor even in a *bari u'bari* case, treating her *bari* as somewhat stronger by virtue of the *rov*. The *gemara* will return to the question of *holchin b'mammon achar ha-rov* — whether a statistical majority is sufficient to extract money from someone — but signals here that this is not sufficient. A definite competing claim cannot simply be overridden by probability alone.

## Migo vs. Peh She'Asar

### R. Yehoshua Concedes

The *gemara* now pivots. It explains that Rabbi Yehoshua's concession — *u'modeh Rabbi Yehoshua* — need not be read as a response to a concession by Rabban Gamliel in the current *mishnah* at all. Rather, *amigo ka'i v'pirka kamma ka'i*: Rabbi Yehoshua is going back to a *migo* that was raised in the first *perek*. In that earlier *perek*, Rabban Gamliel argued that the woman should be believed, at least in part because she had a *migo* — a more advantageous claim she could have made but did not. Rabbi Yehoshua rejected that *migo*. Now, in this *mishnah*, Rabbi Yehoshua declares that while he rejected that *migo*, he will concede a case that is *stronger* than a *migo* — namely, *ha-peh she'asar hu ha-peh she'hitir*.

The case in question is the closing clause of the *mishnah*, where a man says: "This field that I have been occupying was originally your father's — and I bought it from him." He is the one who introduces the entire scenario. The other party had no idea a claim was even forthcoming. Rabbi Yehoshua concedes that in such a case the man is believed. Rabbi Yehoshua is thus not conceding to anything Rabban Gamliel said in this *mishnah*; he is drawing a contrast with his own earlier position rejecting *migo*.

### Four Migo Cases

To understand exactly what *migo* Rabbi Yehoshua rejected, the *gemara* works backward through all the *mishnayot* of the first *perek*, asking in each case whether there was indeed a *migo* that Rabban Gamliel relied upon.

The first case reviewed is *hi she'muberet* — she is visibly pregnant, and she identifies the father as a *kohen* of unimpeachable lineage. The *gemara* asks: what *migo* does she have? *Harei kreisa bein shineha* — her pregnancy is manifest; everyone already knows she had relations with some man. She is already making the best claim she could. There is no *migo* here at all.

The second case is *hi m'daberet im echad* — she was seen in conversation (or in seclusion) with a man, and she identifies him as a *kohen*. The *gemara* notes that this case depends on how one reads the language. According to Zeiri, who understands *m'daberet* as referring to *nistera* — seclusion — there is indeed a *migo*: she could have denied that any relations occurred (*migo d'i ba'i amra lo nistarti*), yet she concedes they did. That concession, under circumstances where she did not need to make it, gives her claim credibility. This is a *migo* that Rabbi Yehoshua refuses to accept. According to Rav Ashi, however, *m'daberet* means she actually had relations and that is already established — in which case there is no *migo*, and Rabbi Yehoshua's rejection of her claim is unremarkable.

The third case is *hi omeret mukat etz ani* — she claims her lack of *betulim* is due to a physical injury, and he claims she had relations. Here too the *gemara* examines whether there is a *migo*. According to Rabbi Eliezer, who rules that if she was *be'ulah* she receives nothing, a *migo* exists: she could have claimed *mukat etz tachteich* — the injury occurred after the *kiddushin* — and thereby claimed the full 200. Instead she claims the injury preceded the *kiddushin*, reducing her claim to 100. That self-reduction is a powerful *migo*. But according to Rabbi Yochanan, who rules that even a *mukat etz* from before the wedding receives 200, she is already making the maximum possible claim and there is no *migo*.

### Rape Case Migo

The fourth and earliest *mishnah* — *ha-nosei et ha'isha v'lo matza lah betulim* — is the one where the *migo* is beyond dispute. She claims she was raped after the *kiddushin*; he claims she had relations before. Here she clearly possesses a stronger claim she did not make: she could have said *mukat etz ani tachteich*, an injury after the *kiddushin*, which would have entitled her to 200 and would not have disqualified her from marrying a *kohen*. Instead she claims rape, which entitles her to 200 but *does* disqualify her from a *kohen* — *d'lo pasla nafsha mi-kehuna*. By making the more damaging claim, she shows that she is not trying to maximize her position, which is the essence of a *migo*. Rabban Gamliel believes her, and the *gemara* explains that this is because she has a *bari* claim reinforced by a *migo*. Rashi adds that even in cases where there is no *migo*, Rabban Gamliel has another basis for belief: *chezkat ha-guf* — the presumption that she has not transgressed, that she did not sleep with a man who would have rendered her unfit. So Rabban Gamliel always has her *bari* claim, sometimes plus a *migo*, and always plus a *chazaka*.

Rabbi Yehoshua's position, by contrast, is stated in its full force: even a *bari* against a *shema*, even a *bari* reinforced by a *migo*, is not sufficient for him to believe her. He responds: *behai migo d'hacha modina lach, behahu migo d'hatam peligina alach* — the *migo* in this second *perek*'s field case I concede; the *migo* back there in the first *perek* I rejected. This elegantly reviews the entire earlier debate while advancing the analysis.

### Field Example

The *gemara* now asks: *mai shena hai migo me-hai migo* — what distinguishes the *migo* that Rabbi Yehoshua accepts from the one he rejects? The *gemara's* answer is: *hacha ein shor shachut lefanecha, hatam harei shor shachut lefanecha* — here there is no slaughtered ox before you; there, there is a slaughtered ox before you.

The image requires explanation. When a woman's lack of *betulim* is established — when the sheets have been examined, or the fact is simply not in dispute — that is a concrete, undeniable datum that demands an explanation. Like a slaughtered ox lying in the road, the fact is before you and you must account for it. Under those circumstances, the choice between alternative explanations is not the same as introducing a claim out of nowhere. Even if she chooses the more self-damaging explanation, she is still operating under the constraint of having to explain observable evidence. That is what Rabbi Yehoshua does not find convincing as a *migo*.

### Rashi/Tosfos Debate

Rashi and Tosafot understand this point differently, and the difference is significant.

Rashi explains that the superiority of *ha-peh she'asar* lies in the fact that the claimant was not even being challenged. Nobody approached Reuven; nobody sued him or asked him about the field. He simply volunteers: "This field used to belong to your father — and I bought it from him." He could have remained completely silent. *Migo d'i ba'i shatik* — since he could have said nothing at all, the fact that he makes this admission is the strongest possible form of trustworthiness. When, by contrast, a woman must explain an established fact about her body, she cannot say "I'm making nothing up," because the datum is there and demands explanation.

Tosafot does not accept this framing. According to Tosafot, the field case in the *mishnah* does involve a *ta'ana* against him — the other party is indeed claiming the field. The difference, Tosafot argues, is not about whether the person is being challenged, but about whether there is concrete evidence that must be answered. In the field case, the opposing party claims the field was his father's, but there is no evidence that forces an explanation. The defendant could simply say *lo hayu devarim me'olam* — I don't know what you are talking about, I never saw your father in my life. Since he could deny everything, his *migo* is powerful: he has the option of total denial. But when the evidence is already before the court — the *shor shachut* — no such total denial is available, and the range of alternative claims is constrained to explanations, not denials. That is what Tosafot means by *shor shachut lefanecha*: there is a dead animal in front of you; you cannot claim it is alive; you can only offer competing accounts of how it died.

This debate between Rashi and Tosafot has concrete implications: according to Rashi, Rabbi Yehoshua accepts *ha-peh she'asar* only when the claimant was not being challenged at all. According to Tosafot, Rabbi Yehoshua would accept it even when challenged, provided the alternative available to the claimant was a complete denial rather than merely a different explanation of established facts.

## Rov Presumption

### Qol Counteracts Rov

With the structure of the debate clarified — Rabban Gamliel concedes the *bari u'bari* case, Rabbi Yehoshua concedes the *ha-peh she'asar* case — the *gemara* returns to the question left open earlier. If *rov nashim betulot nisos*, if the statistical majority of women are married for the first time, why does the absence of witnesses leave her without recourse? Why not let the *rov* itself carry her claim, at least according to Rabban Gamliel?

Ravina answers: *mishum de-ika le-meimar rov nashim betulot nisos u'mi'ut almanot, v'chol ha-nisos betula yesh lah kol* — it is true that most women's weddings are first weddings, but there is a countervailing consideration. Every first wedding generates *kol* — public knowledge, communal awareness, memory. A first wedding is announced; three hundred guests are invited; people remember. Therefore, when twenty years later no one can be found who remembers this as a first wedding, that absence of *kol* undermines the force of the *rov*. The statistical likelihood is balanced — and neutralized — by the inference that had it truly been a first wedding, people would have remembered.

### Balance of Rov/Qol

The *gemara* presses this reasoning. If every first wedding produces *kol*, then even the testimony of witnesses should be unnecessary — we would simply ask whether there is communal memory. And conversely, if the absence of *kol* is so telling, should we not distrust even witnesses who come forward, on the grounds that the absence of broad awareness is suspicious?

The *gemara* refines Ravina's statement: *rov ha-nisos betulot yesh lahen kol* — most, but not all, first weddings generate persistent *kol*. The *rov* and the expected *kol* together create a presumption, but since neither is absolute, they balance one another. The result is that without witnesses, we have neither a strong presumption for her nor conclusive evidence against her — a genuine equilibrium that leaves the burden of proof on her. When witnesses do come, they provide the concrete evidence that tips the balance. And since the concern is not about *kol* in isolation but about the interplay between statistical likelihood and communal memory, witnesses testifying to concrete acts at the wedding override the abstract inference from the absence of general awareness.

## Rov Presumption…

### Witness Evidence

Having established that witnesses can overcome the absence of *kol*, the *gemara* specifies what those witnesses must testify to. The case at hand is witnesses who testify that she went out in *behinuma* — the bedecked wedding procession indicative of a first marriage. This is the kind of direct, concrete testimony about a specific act that constitutes genuine evidence rather than an inference from probability or the presence or absence of communal memory.

## Receipts/Shover

### Double Collection Risk

With witnesses established as sufficient to allow collection, a new problem emerges. If she collects her *ketubah* on the basis of witnesses in one *beit din*, what prevents her from going to another *beit din* in another city — where nothing is known of the first proceeding — producing a different set of witnesses from the same wedding, and collecting a second time? A first wedding would have had many guests; there is no shortage of potential witnesses. The *gemara* articulates this concern clearly: *dilma mafka edim be-hai bei dina ve-gavya, ve-hadar mafka le-kesuvatah be-hai bei dina ve-gavya* — perhaps she will produce witnesses in one court and collect, then produce her *ketubah* (or more witnesses) in another court and collect again. If the husband has no protection against this, the system is exposed to abuse.

### Shovar Solution

Rabbi Abba's response is direct: *zos omeres kotvin shovar* — this case teaches that we write a receipt. The husband, having paid out the *ketubah*, holds a *shovar* — a written receipt — that protects him against a subsequent claim. The conceptual challenge underlying this is significant. The normal principle in financial law is that a creditor holds the document, and once a debt is paid, it is the creditor's responsibility to return or destroy the document. Demanding that the debtor hold a receipt forever is a burden. One view in the Talmud elsewhere resolves this by simply refusing to allow collection without producing the original document: if you have lost your *shtar*, you cannot collect, regardless of whether you can prove the debt. Under that view, there is no need for receipts — but the cost falls entirely on the creditor who has lost documentation.

### Rav Pappa Position

Rav Pappa takes a more nuanced position. He does not simply accept the conclusion that receipts are written. Rather, he argues: *bi-makom she'ein kotvin ketubah askinan* — the entire discussion concerns a place where *ketubot* are not normally written at all. The *ketubah* obligation derives from rabbinic enactment and does not require a written document; some communities relied entirely on witnesses without producing a written *shtar*. In such a place, there is no document to return or destroy, so a system of receipts is necessary. But in a place where *ketubot* are normally written, Rav Pappa maintains that no receipts are written: the responsibility lies with the document, and if the document cannot be produced, collection cannot proceed.

This position has a stark implication. If a woman loses her *ketubah*, or if it is destroyed, in a community where *ketubot* are written — according to Rav Pappa she cannot collect at all, unless she can prove the document was definitively destroyed. The inability to produce the document is her problem, not the court's, and the court will not fashion an alternative mechanism.

### Baraita Reinterpretation

The *gemara* notes that this same debate between Rabbi Abba and Rav Pappa appears not only in connection with the *mishnah* but also in connection with a *baraita*, and the *baraita* version is more explicit and therefore more revealing.

The *baraita* lists three scenarios: *avda kesuvatah* — she lost her *ketubah*; *hitmina kesuvatah* — she secreted it away; *nisrefa kesuvatah* — it was burned. In all three cases, the *baraita* says, she may produce witnesses to the wedding ceremonies — dancing before her, *kos shel besora*, the *mappa shel betulim* — and collect. The *baraita* then concludes: *kesuvatah masayim*, she collects 200.

Since the *baraita* clearly began with a case where a *ketubah* existed — it was lost, hidden, or burned — and yet allows collection on the basis of witnesses, this appears to be direct evidence that even in a place where *ketubot* are written, there is a mechanism for collecting without the document, which supports Rabbi Abba's position that receipts are written.

The *gemara* presses Rav Pappa: how can you maintain your position given that the *baraita* explicitly says the *ketubah* was lost and she can still collect? Rav Pappa responds with a forced but internally consistent reading. *Avda*, he says, means she claims it was lost — but claiming a loss is treated like hiding it: *ki hitmina bifneinu dami*. If she says "I lost it," the court suspects she is hiding it somewhere to use later. Therefore, Rav Pappa reads the *baraita* as a dialogue: when she says *avda*, we say *hitmina* — we suspect concealment — and we do not let her collect. The only case where she may collect is *nisrefa* — the *ketubah* was burned and we have evidence of the destruction.

Under this reading, the three terms in the *baraita* are not three parallel cases each permitting collection. Rather, *avda* is the opening claim, *hitmina* is the court's response expressing suspicion, and only *nisrefa* is the scenario where collection is actually permitted. This is a significant *chiddush* to extract from a *baraita* whose plain meaning seems to permit collection in all three cases.

### Final Shovar Rule

The *gemara* then turns to the situation we have been circling: even where witnesses to the wedding can be produced, the double-collection risk remains. The *gemara* closes this discussion with a clear principle: *bi-makom d'lo efshar vadai kasvinan shovar* — where there is no alternative, a *shovar* is certainly written. This is the position that I had previewed earlier in the discussion, and the *gemara* arrives at it here as a *svara* that even Rav Pappa must acknowledge.

The practical upshot is as follows. According to Rav Pappa: in a community where *ketubot* are written, no receipts — the document governs, and if it cannot be produced (other than in a case of proven destruction), collection is impossible. In a community where *ketubot* are not written, or where the document was proven destroyed, there is no alternative, and a *shovar* must be written to prevent double collection. The more permissive view — that of Rabbi Abba — would write a *shovar* even in communities where *ketubot* are normally produced, thereby allowing collection whenever witnesses can testify to the nature of the wedding, with the husband protected by his receipt.

## Wedding Practices

### Kos Shel Besora

Having established that witnesses can testify to wedding ceremonies as proof of a first marriage, the *gemara* — and the *baraita* cited earlier — enumerates those ceremonies. The first is *kos shel besora*, the cup of good tidings. Rav Adda bar Ahava explains: *kos yayin shel terumah mevi'in lefaneha* — they would bring before her a cup filled with *terumah* wine. The symbolism, he says, is *re'uya hayta zu le'echol bi-terumah* — she is fit to eat *terumah*, meaning that if she is marrying a *kohen*, her status as a woman who has never had relations with a man forbidden to a *kohen* is being publicly affirmed.

Rav Pappa objects: *atu almana mi lo achla bi-terumah?* — Does a widow not also eat *terumah* if she marries a *kohen*? Her being a non-virgin does not disqualify her from *terumah*, so this symbolism does not track the *betulah/be'ulah* distinction at all. Rav Pappa therefore offers his own explanation: *zu reishit ke-terumah* — this cup is like *terumah* in its quality of being *reishit*, a first. Just as *terumah* is the first portion separated, this night is her first, and the cup symbolizes that *rishonah*, that primacy.

### Wine Barrel Symbolism

A *baraita* cites Rabbi Yehuda's view that they would bring before her a barrel of wine. Rav Adda bar Ahava again supplies the interpretive key: *besulah mevi'in lefaneha setumah* — for a *besulah* they would bring a sealed cask; *be'ulah mevi'in lefaneha petucha* — for a *be'ulah* they would bring an open one. The symbolism is entirely transparent, and the wine barrel — sealed or open — would serve as direct concrete evidence of the woman's status at the time of her wedding.

### Concrete vs. Absence

The *gemara* asks: why not simply use the sealed barrel as an indicator, and the *absence* of any barrel as the indicator for a *be'ulah*? Why go through the effort of displaying an open barrel?

The answer returns to the principle established earlier in the discussion of *shor shachut*: we need concrete positive evidence, not merely the absence of evidence. *Zimnin di-tafsa masayim* — sometimes she will have already seized her husband's property and will claim 200, forcing him to bear the burden under *ha-motzi mei-chaveiro alav ha-re'ayah*. If she claims *besulah havina* and the only counter-evidence is that no sealed barrel was displayed, she can readily explain: *itnusi hu di-itnusu* — they were too caught up in the celebration, or too drunk, and simply never got around to the barrel ceremony. The absence of the practice proves nothing in the face of that ready excuse. But if an open barrel was explicitly brought before her, that is a concrete act — a *shor shachut* — that cannot be explained away, and that will serve as definitive evidence of her *be'ulah* status at the time of the wedding.
