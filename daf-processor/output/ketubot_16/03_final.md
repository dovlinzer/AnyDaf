# Ketubot 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## First/Second Wedding

### Mishnah Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁנִּתְאַרְמְלָה אוֹ שֶׁנִּתְגָּרְשָׁה. הִיא אוֹמֶרֶת: בְּתוּלָה נְשָׂאתַנִי, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: לֹא כִּי, אֶלָּא אַלְמָנָה נְשָׂאתִיךְ. אִם יֵשׁ עֵדִים שֶׁיָּצָאת בְּהִינוּמָא וְרֹאשָׁהּ פָּרוּעַ — כְּתוּבָּתָהּ מָאתַיִם. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: אַף חִילּוּק קְלָיוֹת רְאָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a woman who was widowed or divorced,** and is now claiming payment of her marriage contract that is not before the court, and **she says: You married me** as **a virgin,** who is entitled to two hundred dinars, **and he says: No; rather, I married you** as **a widow,** who is entitled to one hundred dinars, then, **if there are witnesses that she went out** of her father's house to her wedding **with a *hinnuma* or** with **her hair uncovered,** in a manner typical of virgins, payment of **her marriage contract is two hundred** dinars. **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka says: Even** testimony that there was **distribution of roasted grain,** which was customary at weddings of virgins, constitutes **proof** that she is a virgin.

The second *perek* opens with a *mishnah* describing a dispute that arises not over the woman's physical state on the wedding night, but over the nature of the wedding itself — decades later, when she is being divorced and there is no *ketubah* to produce. The debate is not "why wasn't she a virgin?" but rather "was this a first or second wedding?" The *gemara* teaches that in such a case, the woman requires witnesses who can testify to the indicators of a first wedding: that she left her father's home with a full wedding procession, that roasted grain was distributed, that three hundred guests attended, or similar markers of a *nisu'in besulah*. Without those indicators, she would not be believed, because the operative principle is *ha-motzi mei-chaveiro alav ha-re'ayah* — the burden of proof rests on the one seeking to extract payment.

The *gemara* opens on 16a by asking: since witnesses are required, does this mean the *mishnah* does not follow Rabban Gamliel? After all, Rabban Gamliel consistently rules that the woman is believed. The *gemara* responds: *afillu teima Rabban Gamliel* — one can say even this *mishnah* follows Rabban Gamliel.

### Bari v'Bari Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הָתָם אֶלָּא בְּבָרִי וְשֶׁמָּא. אֲבָל הָכָא בְּבָרִי וּבָרִי — לָא אָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Even** if **you will say** that the ruling in the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabban Gamliel, Rabban Gamliel stated** his opinion **only there, in** a case where the claim of the bride is **certain and** the claim of the groom is **uncertain,** as the groom does not know what actually happened. **However, here, in** a case where the claim of the bride is **certain and** the claim of the groom is also **certain,** as he is certain that he married her as a widow, Rabban Gamliel **did not say** that her claim is deemed credible.

The *gemara* distinguishes the case of the first *perek* from this one. In the earlier cases, the dispute was *bari v'shema* — she makes a definite claim and he responds only with uncertainty. Here, both parties make definite claims: she says with certainty that it was a first wedding, and he says with equal certainty that it was a second. This is *bari u'bari* — two equally firm competing claims — and that is a qualitatively different situation. The principle of *ha-motzi mei-chaveiro alav ha-re'ayah* applies with full force.

### Rabban Gamliel Concedes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי, ״וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ״: אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא אַיְירִי רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בְּמוֹדֶה — שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לָא אַיְירִי רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בְּמוֹדֶה — רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְמַאן מוֹדֵה?
>
> **Translation:** **And it also stands to reason** that the first clause of the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Rabban Gamliel, who concedes that without witnesses the woman's claim is not deemed credible, despite the fact that the case is comparable to one of a certain claim and an uncertain claim, **as** the mishna **teaches: And Rabbi Yehoshua concedes. Granted, if you say that Rabban Gamliel is speaking in** the first clause of the mishna and **he concedes** that even though it is similar to a case of certain and uncertain, her claim is not accepted, it works out **well.** Rabban Gamliel concedes to Rabbi Yehoshua in the first clause of the mishna and the mishna cites a case where Rabbi Yehoshua concedes to Rabban Gamliel. **However, if you say that Rabban Gamliel is not speaking in** the first clause of the mishna and **he** does not **concede, to whom does Rabbi Yehoshua concede** in the latter clause?

The *gemara* proceeds to establish that Rabban Gamliel concedes this case. The logical structure of the *mishnah* supports this: the *mishnah* closes with the statement *u'modeh Rabbi Yehoshua* — Rabbi Yehoshua concedes a certain case. The *gemara* argues that this concession only makes sense if the earlier portion of the *mishnah* represents Rabban Gamliel's concession in the *bari u'bari* scenario. If the entire *mishnah* were authored from Rabbi Yehoshua's perspective, then to whom is Rabbi Yehoshua conceding? The structure implies a mutual yielding: Rabban Gamliel concedes that in a *bari u'bari* dispute she does not win, and Rabbi Yehoshua in turn concedes a different point — the case of *ha-peh she'asar hu ha-peh she'hitir*.

### Rov Nashim Betulot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּדְקָאָרֵי לַהּ, מַאי קָאָרֵי לַהּ? הָא בָּרִי וּבָרִי הוּא! כֵּיוָן דְּרוֹב נָשִׁים בְּתוּלוֹת נִישָּׂאוֹת, כִּי בָרִי וְשֶׁמָּא דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And he who asked** the question, **why did he ask it?** The cases are clearly different, as **this is** a case of **a certain** claim **and a certain** claim. The Gemara answers: **Since most women are married** as **virgins,** one might have thought that the legal status in this case **is like** that of a **case of a certain** claim **and an uncertain** claim, as her claim is supported by a majority of cases.

One might ask: even in a *bari u'bari* scenario, why not simply apply *rov nashim betulot nisos* — the statistical majority that most women marry for the first time? Since most weddings are first weddings, her claim is probabilistically more likely to be true. The *gemara* acknowledges this consideration and notes that this is precisely what might lead one to think Rabban Gamliel would still rule in her favor even in a *bari u'bari* case, treating her *bari* as somewhat stronger by virtue of the *rov*. The *gemara* will return to the question of *holchin b'mammon achar ha-rov* — whether a statistical majority is sufficient to extract money from someone — but signals here that this is not sufficient. A definite competing claim cannot simply be overridden by probability alone.

## Migo vs. Peh She'Asar

### R. Yehoshua Concedes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי סָבְרַתְּ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אַהַאי פִּירְקִין קָאֵי? אַמִּגּוֹ קָאֵי, וְאַפִּירְקִין קַמָּא קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that proof: **Do you think that** the statement of **Rabbi Yehoshua is** in reference **to** a mishna in **this chapter?** Actually, **it is** in reference **to** the principle of ***miggo*, and it is** in reference **to the first chapter.** Rabbi Yehoshua is saying that although he does not accept the claim supported by a *miggo* in the first chapter, here he accepts the claim supported by the principle: The mouth that prohibited is the mouth that permitted, which is based on the same reasoning as *miggo*, i.e., the fact that he could have made a more advantageous claim lends credibility to the less advantageous claim. In this case, he could have remained silent and the field would have remained in his possession. If challenged, he could have claimed that the field was his. Therefore, his less advantageous claim, that the field was not originally his but he purchased it from the father of the claimant, is accepted.

The *gemara* now pivots. It explains that Rabbi Yehoshua's concession — *u'modeh Rabbi Yehoshua* — need not be read as a response to a concession by Rabban Gamliel in the current *mishnah* at all. Rather, *amigo ka'i v'pirka kamma ka'i*: Rabbi Yehoshua is going back to a *migo* that was raised in the first *perek*. In that earlier *perek*, Rabban Gamliel argued that the woman should be believed, at least in part because she had a *migo* — a more advantageous claim she could have made but did not. Rabbi Yehoshua rejected that *migo*. Now, in this *mishnah*, Rabbi Yehoshua declares that while he rejected that *migo*, he will concede a case that is *stronger* than a *migo* — namely, *ha-peh she'asar hu ha-peh she'hitir*.

The case in question is the closing clause of the *mishnah*, where a man says: "This field that I have been occupying was originally your father's — and I bought it from him." He is the one who introduces the entire scenario. The other party had no idea a claim was even forthcoming. Rabbi Yehoshua concedes that in such a case the man is believed. Rabbi Yehoshua is thus not conceding to anything Rabban Gamliel said in this *mishnah*; he is drawing a contrast with his own earlier position rejecting *migo*.

### Four Migo Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַהֵיָיא? אִילֵּימָא אַהָא: הָיְתָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת, וְאָמְרוּ לָהּ ״מָה טִיבוֹ שֶׁל עוּבָּר זֶה״. ״מֵאִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי וְכֹהֵן הוּא״, רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמְרִים: נֶאֱמֶנֶת, רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: לֹא מִפִּיהָ אָנוּ חַיִּים — הָתָם מַאי מִגּוֹ אִיכָּא? הֲרֵי כְּרֵיסָהּ בֵּין שִׁינֶּיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: In reference **to which** case in the first chapter did Rabbi Yehoshua make his statement? **If you say** that it is in reference **to this** case (13a): **If** a single woman **was pregnant, and** people **said to her: What is the nature of that fetus, and she says to them: It is from a man** called **so-and-so and he is a priest, Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Eliezer say: She is deemed credible, and Rabbi Yehoshua says:** It is **not** based on the statement emerging **from her mouth** that **we conduct our lives. There, what *miggo* is there** lending credibility to her claim? In that case, **her belly is between her teeth,** i.e., her pregnancy is conspicuous, and consequently she does not have the option of making the more advantageous claim that she did not engage in intercourse.

To understand exactly what *migo* Rabbi Yehoshua rejected, the *gemara* works backward through all the *mishnayot* of the first *perek*, asking in each case whether there was indeed a *migo* that Rabban Gamliel relied upon.

The first case reviewed is *hi she'muberet* — she is visibly pregnant, and she identifies the father as a *kohen* of unimpeachable lineage. The *gemara* asks: what *migo* does she have? *Harei kreisa bein shineha* — her pregnancy is manifest; everyone already knows she had relations with some man. She is already making the best claim she could. There is no *migo* here at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אַהָא: רָאוּהָ מְדַבֶּרֶת עִם אֶחָד, וְאָמְרוּ לָהּ: ״מָה טִיבוֹ שֶׁל אִישׁ זֶה״. ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי וְכֹהֵן הוּא״, רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמְרִים: נֶאֱמֶנֶת, רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: לֹא מִפִּיהָ אָנוּ חַיִּים. הָתָם מַאי מִגּוֹ אִיכָּא? הָנִיחָא לִזְעֵירִי דְּאָמַר: מַאי ״מְדַבֶּרֶת״ — נִסְתְּרָה, מִגּוֹ דְּאִי בָּעֲיָא אָמְרָה לֹא נִבְעַלְתִּי, וְקָאָמְרָה נִבְעַלְתִּי — מְהֵימְנָא. אֶלָּא לְרַב אַסִּי, דְּאָמַר מַאי ״מְדַבֶּרֶת״ — נִבְעֶלֶת, מַאי מִגּוֹ אִיכָּא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is in reference **to this** case (13a): **If** people **saw** a woman **speaking to one** man, **and they said to her: What is the nature of this man?** And she said to them: **He is a man** called **so-and-so and he is a priest, Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Eliezer say: She is deemed credible, and Rabbi Yehoshua says:** It is **not** based on the statement emerging **from her mouth** that **we conduct our lives.** Again this is challenged: **There, what *miggo* is there? This** works out **well according to Ze'eiri, who said: What** is the meaning of **speaking** mentioned in the mishna? It means that **she secluded herself** with a man. In this case there is **a *miggo*. Since, if she wished** to lie, **she could have said: I did not engage in intercourse** at all, **and** instead **she said: I engaged in intercourse** with a man of unflawed lineage. Therefore, **she is deemed credible** according to Rabban Gamliel. **However, according to Rav Asi, who said: What is** the meaning of **speaking?** It means that **she engaged in intercourse, what *miggo* is there?** There was no better claim available to her.

The second case is *hi m'daberet im echad* — she was seen in conversation (or in seclusion) with a man, and she identifies him as a *kohen*. The *gemara* notes that this case depends on how one reads the language. According to Zeiri, who understands *m'daberet* as referring to *nistera* — seclusion — there is indeed a *migo*: she could have denied that any relations occurred (*migo d'i ba'i amra lo nistarti*), yet she concedes they did. That concession, under circumstances where she did not need to make it, gives her claim credibility. This is a *migo* that Rabbi Yehoshua refuses to accept. According to Rav Ashi, however, *m'daberet* means she actually had relations and that is already established — in which case there is no *migo*, and Rabbi Yehoshua's rejection of her claim is unremarkable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא אַהָא: הִיא אוֹמֶרֶת מוּכַּת עֵץ אֲנִי, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: לֹא כִי, אֶלָּא דְּרוּסַת אִישׁ אַתְּ. רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמְרִים: נֶאֱמֶנֶת, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: לֹא מִפִּיהָ אָנוּ חַיִּין — הָתָם מַאי מִגּוֹ אִיכָּא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is in reference **to this** case (13a), where **she says: I** am one whose hymen **was ruptured by wood, and** the groom **says: No; rather, you are** one who **was trampled by a man. Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Eliezer say: She is deemed credible,** and **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** It is **not** on the basis of the statement emerging **from her mouth** that **we conduct our lives. There, what *miggo* is there?**

The third case is *hi omeret mukat etz ani* — she claims her lack of *betulim* is due to a physical injury, and he claims she had relations. Here too the *gemara* examines whether there is a *migo*. According to Rabbi Eliezer, who rules that if she was *be'ulah* she receives nothing, a *migo* exists: she could have claimed *mukat etz tachteich* — the injury occurred after the *kiddushin* — and thereby claimed the full 200. Instead she claims the injury preceded the *kiddushin*, reducing her claim to 100. That self-reduction is a powerful *migo*. But according to Rabbi Yochanan, who rules that even a *mukat etz* from before the wedding receives 200, she is already making the maximum possible claim and there is no *migo*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר: בְּמָנֶה וְלֹא כְּלוּם — מִגּוֹ דְּאִי בָּעֲיָא אָמְרָה: ״מוּכַּת עֵץ אֲנִי תַּחְתֶּיךָ״, וְאִית לַהּ מָאתַיִם, וְקָאָמְרָה: מֵעִיקָּרָא, דְּלֵית לַהּ אֶלָּא מָנֶה, מְהֵימְנָא. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דְּאָמַר: בְּמָאתַיִם וּמָנֶה. מַאי מִגּוֹ אִיכָּא?
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, according to Rabbi Elazar, who said** that the bride claims that she is entitled to a marriage contract of **one hundred dinars, and** the groom claims that she is entitled to **nothing at all,** as in that case, there is a ***miggo*. Since, if she wished** to lie, **she could have said: I** am one whose hymen **was ruptured by wood under your** authority after betrothal, **and she would have** been entitled to **two hundred** dinars, as she was a virgin at betrothal. **And** therefore, when **she says** that her hymen was already ruptured **initially,** prior to betrothal, **when she is** entitled to **only one hundred dinars, she is deemed credible. However, according to Rabbi Yoḥanan, who said:** The bride claims that she is entitled to a marriage contract of **two hundred** dinars; **and** the groom claims that that she is entitled to a marriage contract of **one hundred dinars, what *miggo* is there?** Her claim is the most advantageous claim available to her.

### Rape Case Migo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אַהָא: הַנּוֹשֵׂא אֶת הָאִשָּׁה וְלֹא מָצָא לָהּ בְּתוּלִים, הִיא אוֹמֶרֶת: ״מִשֶּׁאֵרַסְתַּנִי נֶאֱנַסְתִּי, וְנִסְתַּחֲפָה שָׂדֵהוּ״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא כִי, אֶלָּא עַד שֶׁלֹּא אֵירַסְתִּיךְ״. רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמְרִים: נֶאֱמֶנֶת, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: לֹא מִפִּיהָ אָנוּ חַיִּים. דְּמִגּוֹ דְּאִי בָּעֲיָא אָמְרָה ״מוּכַּת עֵץ אֲנִי תַּחְתֶּיךָ״, דְּלָא קָא פָסְלָה נַפְשָׁהּ מִכְּהוּנָּה. וְקָאָמְרָה: ״נֶאֱנַסְתִּי״, דְּקָא פָּסְלָה נַפְשָׁהּ מִכְּהוּנָּה, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי קָאָמַר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל דִּמְהֵימְנָא. וְקָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: בְּהַאי מִגּוֹ דְּהָכָא — מוֹדֵינָא לָךְ, בְּהָהוּא מִגּוֹ דְּהָתָם — פְּלִיגְנָא עִילָּווֹךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is in reference **to this** case (12b): One **who marries a woman and did not find her hymen** intact, and **she says: After you betrothed me, I was raped and his field was inundated,** i.e., it is attributable to your own misfortune. **And he says: No; rather,** you were raped **before I betrothed you,** and my transaction was a mistaken transaction. **Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Eliezer say: She is deemed credible,** and **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** It is **not** based on the statement emerging **from her mouth** that **we conduct our lives, as,** in that case there is **a *miggo*. Since, if she wished** to lie, **she could have said: I** am one whose hymen **was ruptured by wood under your** jurisdiction after betrothal, which is a more advantageous claim, **because she does not** thereby **disqualify herself from** marrying into **the priesthood. But she said: I was raped** after betrothal, which is a less advantageous claim, **because she disqualified herself from the priesthood. Therefore, Rabban Gamliel says that she is deemed credible. And Rabbi Yehoshua says to Rabban Gamliel: With regard to this *miggo*** in the mishna **here, I concede to you** that the *miggo* is effective. **With regard to that *miggo* there** in the first chapter, **I disagree with you.**

The fourth and earliest *mishnah* — *ha-nosei et ha'isha v'lo matza lah betulim* — is the one where the *migo* is beyond dispute. She claims she was raped after the *kiddushin*; he claims she had relations before. Here she clearly possesses a stronger claim she did not make: she could have said *mukat etz ani tachteich*, an injury after the *kiddushin*, which would have entitled her to 200 and would not have disqualified her from marrying a *kohen*. Instead she claims rape, which entitles her to 200 but *does* disqualify her from a *kohen* — *d'lo pasla nafsha mi-kehuna*. By making the more damaging claim, she shows that she is not trying to maximize her position, which is the essence of a *migo*. Rabban Gamliel believes her, and the *gemara* explains that this is because she has a *bari* claim reinforced by a *migo*. Rashi adds that even in cases where there is no *migo*, Rabban Gamliel has another basis for belief: *chezkat ha-guf* — the presumption that she has not transgressed, that she did not sleep with a man who would have rendered her unfit. So Rabban Gamliel always has her *bari* claim, sometimes plus a *migo*, and always plus a *chazaka*.

Rabbi Yehoshua's position, by contrast, is stated in its full force: even a *bari* against a *shema*, even a *bari* reinforced by a *migo*, is not sufficient for him to believe her. He responds: *behai migo d'hacha modina lach, behahu migo d'hatam peligina alach* — the *migo* in this second *perek*'s field case I concede; the *migo* back there in the first *perek* I rejected. This elegantly reviews the entire earlier debate while advancing the analysis.

### Field Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדִי הַאי מִגּוֹ וְהַאי מִגּוֹ, מַאי שְׁנָא הַאי מִגּוֹ מֵהַאי מִגּוֹ? הָכָא — אֵין שׁוֹר שָׁחוּט לְפָנֶיךָ. הָתָם — הֲרֵי שׁוֹר שָׁחוּט לְפָנֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But **after all, this is** a case of ***miggo* and that is** a case of ***miggo*.** In **what** way, in the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua, **is this *miggo* different from that *miggo*?** The Gemara answers: **Here,** in the case of contested ownership of the field, **there is no slaughtered ox before you,** i.e., there is no reason to question his claim of ownership, as the field is in his possession. However, **there,** in the case of the woman who was found not to be a virgin, **there is a slaughtered ox before you,** i.e., there is reason to question her virginity, and it is only in response to that question that she makes her claim. Therefore, although it is supported by a *miggo*, Rabbi Yehoshua does not accept her claim.

The *gemara* now asks: *mai shena hai migo me-hai migo* — what distinguishes the *migo* that Rabbi Yehoshua accepts from the one he rejects? The *gemara's* answer is: *hacha ein shor shachut lefanecha, hatam harei shor shachut lefanecha* — here there is no slaughtered ox before you; there, there is a slaughtered ox before you.

The image requires explanation. When a woman's lack of *betulim* is established — when the sheets have been examined, or the fact is simply not in dispute — that is a concrete, undeniable datum that demands an explanation. Like a slaughtered ox lying in the road, the fact is before you and you must account for it. Under those circumstances, the choice between alternative explanations is not the same as introducing a claim out of nowhere. Even if she chooses the more self-damaging explanation, she is still operating under the constraint of having to explain observable evidence. That is what Rabbi Yehoshua does not find convincing as a *migo*.

### Rashi/Tosfos Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהַפֶּה שֶׁאָסַר — הוּא הַפֶּה שֶׁהִתִּיר. וְאִם יֵשׁ עֵדִים שֶׁהִיא שֶׁל אָבִיו, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לְקַחְתִּיהָ הֵימֶנּוּ״ — אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן.
>
> **Translation:** This is so, **as the mouth that prohibited,** i.e., claimed that the field had belonged to the other's father, **is the mouth that permitted,** i.e., claimed that he purchased the field. Even if he had not admitted that it had belonged to the other's father, the field would have remained in his possession. Therefore, his claim is accepted. **However, if there are witnesses that the field belonged to his father, and** the one who has the field in his possession **says: I purchased it from him, he is not deemed credible** and his claim is rejected.

Rashi and Tosafot understand this point differently, and the difference is significant.

Rashi explains that the superiority of *ha-peh she'asar* lies in the fact that the claimant was not even being challenged. Nobody approached Reuven; nobody sued him or asked him about the field. He simply volunteers: "This field used to belong to your father — and I bought it from him." He could have remained completely silent. *Migo d'i ba'i shatik* — since he could have said nothing at all, the fact that he makes this admission is the strongest possible form of trustworthiness. When, by contrast, a woman must explain an established fact about her body, she cannot say "I'm making nothing up," because the datum is there and demands explanation.

Tosafot does not accept this framing. According to Tosafot, the field case in the *mishnah* does involve a *ta'ana* against him — the other party is indeed claiming the field. The difference, Tosafot argues, is not about whether the person is being challenged, but about whether there is concrete evidence that must be answered. In the field case, the opposing party claims the field was his father's, but there is no evidence that forces an explanation. The defendant could simply say *lo hayu devarim me'olam* — I don't know what you are talking about, I never saw your father in my life. Since he could deny everything, his *migo* is powerful: he has the option of total denial. But when the evidence is already before the court — the *shor shachut* — no such total denial is available, and the range of alternative claims is constrained to explanations, not denials. That is what Tosafot means by *shor shachut lefanecha*: there is a dead animal in front of you; you cannot claim it is alive; you can only offer competing accounts of how it died.

This debate between Rashi and Tosafot has concrete implications: according to Rashi, Rabbi Yehoshua accepts *ha-peh she'asar* only when the claimant was not being challenged at all. According to Tosafot, Rabbi Yehoshua would accept it even when challenged, provided the alternative available to the claimant was a complete denial rather than merely a different explanation of established facts.

## Rov Presumption

### Qol Counteracts Rov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵיוָן דְּרוֹב נָשִׁים בְּתוּלוֹת נִישָּׂאוֹת, כִּי לָא אָתוּ עֵדִים מַאי הָוֵי! אָמַר רָבִינָא: מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר רוֹב נָשִׁים בְּתוּלוֹת נִישָּׂאוֹת, וּמִיעוּט אַלְמָנוֹת, וְכׇל הַנִּשֵּׂאת בְּתוּלָה — יֵשׁ לָהּ קוֹל,
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara resumes discussion of the inference that it drew at the outset with regard to witnesses that the bride was a virgin. **And since** the Gemara established earlier that the woman's claim is supported by the fact **that most women are married** as **virgins, if witnesses did not come, what of it?** That majority should be sufficient to establish that she married as a virgin. **Ravina said:** It is **because there is** room **to say** that although **most women are married** as **virgins and a minority** of women marry as **widows** or non-virgins, there is an additional presumption: The marriage of **anyone who is married** as **a virgin generates publicity,**

With the structure of the debate clarified — Rabban Gamliel concedes the *bari u'bari* case, Rabbi Yehoshua concedes the *ha-peh she'asar* case — the *gemara* returns to the question left open earlier. If *rov nashim betulot nisos*, if the statistical majority of women are married for the first time, why does the absence of witnesses leave her without recourse? Why not let the *rov* itself carry her claim, at least according to Rabban Gamliel?

Ravina answers: *mishum de-ika le-meimar rov nashim betulot nisos u'mi'ut almanot, v'chol ha-nisos betula yesh lah kol* — it is true that most women's weddings are first weddings, but there is a countervailing consideration. Every first wedding generates *kol* — public knowledge, communal awareness, memory. A first wedding is announced; three hundred guests are invited; people remember. Therefore, when twenty years later no one can be found who remembers this as a first wedding, that absence of *kol* undermines the force of the *rov*. The statistical likelihood is balanced — and neutralized — by the inference that had it truly been a first wedding, people would have remembered.

### Balance of Rov/Qol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזוֹ, הוֹאִיל וְאֵין לָהּ קוֹל — אִיתְּרַע לֵהּ רוּבָּא. אִי כׇּל הַנִּשֵּׂאת בְּתוּלָה יֵשׁ לָהּ קוֹל — כִּי אֲתוֹ עֵדִים, מַאי הָוֵי? הָנָךְ סָהֲדֵי שַׁקָּרֵי נִינְהוּ! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבִינָא: רוֹב הַנִּשֵּׂאת בְּתוּלָה יֵשׁ לָהּ קוֹל, וְזוֹ הוֹאִיל וְאֵין לָהּ קוֹל — אִיתְּרַע לֵהּ רוּבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **and** with regard to **this** woman, **because her** marriage **did not generate publicity, the** effect of the **majority is undermined.** The Gemara asks: **If** in fact, the marriage of **anyone who is married** as **a virgin generates publicity,** and the marriage of this woman did not generate publicity, **when witnesses come, what of it? These are false witnesses,** as their testimony runs counter to the presumption governing all marriages. **Rather, Ravina said** that it is not a universal presumption, but a majority. The marriage of **most** women **who are married** as **virgins generates publicity, but** for **this** woman, **since her** marriage **did not generate publicity, the** effect of the **majority is undermined.** Therefore, the testimony that she went out of her father's house to her wedding with a *hinnuma* overrides the lack of publicity.

The *gemara* presses this reasoning. If every first wedding produces *kol*, then even the testimony of witnesses should be unnecessary — we would simply ask whether there is communal memory. And conversely, if the absence of *kol* is so telling, should we not distrust even witnesses who come forward, on the grounds that the absence of broad awareness is suspicious?

The *gemara* refines Ravina's statement: *rov ha-nisos betulot yesh lahen kol* — most, but not all, first weddings generate persistent *kol*. The *rov* and the expected *kol* together create a presumption, but since neither is absolute, they balance one another. The result is that without witnesses, we have neither a strong presumption for her nor conclusive evidence against her — a genuine equilibrium that leaves the burden of proof on her. When witnesses do come, they provide the concrete evidence that tips the balance. And since the concern is not about *kol* in isolation but about the interplay between statistical likelihood and communal memory, witnesses testifying to concrete acts at the wedding override the abstract inference from the absence of general awareness.

## Receipts/Shovar

### Double Collection Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם יֵשׁ עֵדִים שֶׁיָּצְתָה בְּהִינוּמָא וְכוּ׳. וְלֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא מַפְּקָא עֵדִים בְּהַאי בֵּי דִינָא, וְגָבְיָא, וַהֲדַר מַפְּקָא לַהּ לִכְתוּבָה בְּהַאי בֵּית דִּין, וְגָבְיָא בַּהּ? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת כּוֹתְבִין שׁוֹבָר. רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין כּוֹתְבִין כְּתוּבָּה עָסְקִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** § It was stated in the mishna: **If there are witnesses that she went out** of her father's house to her wedding **with a *hinnuma*,** or with her hair uncovered, in a manner typical of virgins, payment of her marriage contract is two hundred dinars. The Gemara asks: **And** since she collects payment without producing her marriage contract, **let us be concerned** that **she might produce witnesses in this court and collect** payment, **and then produce** her **marriage contract in that** other **court and collect with it** payment a second time. **Rabbi Abbahu said: This indicates** that **one writes a receipt** indicating that the woman received payment. Were the woman to attempt to collect payment of her marriage contract a second time, her husband would produce the receipt. **Rav Pappa said: We are dealing** in the mishna **with a place where one does not write a marriage contract.** It is only in a case where there is no concern lest she produce her marriage contract that she collects payment based on the testimony of witnesses.

With witnesses established as sufficient to allow collection, a new problem emerges. If she collects her *ketubah* on the basis of witnesses in one *beit din*, what prevents her from going to another *beit din* in another city — where nothing is known of the first proceeding — producing a different set of witnesses from the same wedding, and collecting a second time? A first wedding would have had many guests; there is no shortage of potential witnesses. The *gemara* articulates this concern clearly: *dilma mafka edim be-hai bei dina ve-gavya, ve-hadar mafka le-kesuvatah be-hai bei dina ve-gavya* — perhaps she will produce witnesses in one court and collect, then produce her *ketubah* (or more witnesses) in another court and collect again. If the husband has no protection against this, the system is exposed to abuse.

### Shovar Solution

Rabbi Abba's response is direct: *zos omeres kotvin shovar* — this case teaches that we write a receipt. The husband, having paid out the *ketubah*, holds a *shovar* — a written receipt — that protects him against a subsequent claim. The conceptual challenge underlying this is significant. The normal principle in financial law is that a creditor holds the document, and once a debt is paid, it is the creditor's responsibility to return or destroy the document. Demanding that the debtor hold a receipt forever is a burden. One view in the Talmud elsewhere resolves this by simply refusing to allow collection without producing the original document: if you have lost your *shtar*, you cannot collect, regardless of whether you can prove the debt. Under that view, there is no need for receipts — but the cost falls entirely on the creditor who has lost documentation.

### Rav Pappa Position

Rav Pappa takes a more nuanced position. He does not simply accept the conclusion that receipts are written. Rather, he argues: *bi-makom she'ein kotvin ketubah askinan* — the entire discussion concerns a place where *ketubot* are not normally written at all. The *ketubah* obligation derives from rabbinic enactment and does not require a written document; some communities relied entirely on witnesses without producing a written *shtar*. In such a place, there is no document to return or destroy, so a system of receipts is necessary. But in a place where *ketubot* are normally written, Rav Pappa maintains that no receipts are written: the responsibility lies with the document, and if the document cannot be produced, collection cannot proceed.

This position has a stark implication. If a woman loses her *ketubah*, or if it is destroyed, in a community where *ketubot* are written — according to Rav Pappa she cannot collect at all, unless she can prove the document was definitively destroyed. The inability to produce the document is her problem, not the court's, and the court will not fashion an alternative mechanism.

### Baraita Reinterpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַבָּרַיְיתָא: אִיבְּדָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ, הִטְמִינָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ, נִשְׂרְפָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ, רָקְדוּ לְפָנֶיהָ, שָׂחֲקוּ לְפָנֶיהָ, הֶעֱבִירוּ לְפָנֶיהָ כּוֹס שֶׁל בְּשׂוֹרָה, אוֹ מַפָּה שֶׁל בְּתוּלִים. אִם יֵשׁ לָהּ עֵדִים בְּאֶחָד מִכׇּל אֵלּוּ — כְּתוּבָּתָהּ מָאתַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** some **who teach** the dispute between Rabbi Abbahu and Rav Pappa with regard **to the *baraita*** that says: In a case where a woman **lost her marriage contract** or **concealed her marriage contract** and she claims that she is unable to find it; or **her marriage contract was burned,** and there is no proof with regard to the sum to which she is entitled; or practices performed exclusively at the weddings of virgins were performed at her wedding, e.g., people **danced before her,** or **played before her,** or **passed before her a cup of** good **tidings or a cloth of virginity; if she has witnesses with regard to any one of these** practices, **her marriage contract is two hundred** dinars.

The *gemara* notes that this same debate between Rabbi Abba and Rav Pappa appears not only in connection with the *mishnah* but also in connection with a *baraita*, and the *baraita* version is more explicit and therefore more revealing.

The *baraita* lists three scenarios: *avda kesuvatah* — she lost her *ketubah*; *hitmina kesuvatah* — she secreted it away; *nisrefa kesuvatah* — it was burned. In all three cases, the *baraita* says, she may produce witnesses to the wedding ceremonies — dancing before her, *kos shel besora*, the *mappa shel betulim* — and collect. The *baraita* then concludes: *kesuvatah masayim*, she collects 200.

Since the *baraita* clearly began with a case where a *ketubah* existed — it was lost, hidden, or burned — and yet allows collection on the basis of witnesses, this appears to be direct evidence that even in a place where *ketubot* are written, there is a mechanism for collecting without the document, which supports Rabbi Abba's position that receipts are written.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא מַפְּקָא עֵדִים בְּהַאי בֵּית דִּין וְגָבְיָא, וַהֲדַר מַפְּקָא לִכְתוּבָּתָהּ בְּהַאי בֵּית דִּין וְגָבְיָא בַּהּ! אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת כּוֹתְבִין שׁוֹבָר. רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין כּוֹתְבִין כְּתוּבָּה עָסְקִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** since she collects payment without producing her marriage contract, **let us be concerned** that **she might produce witnesses in this court and collect** payment, **and then produce** her **marriage contract in that** other **court and collect** payment **with it** a second time. **Rabbi Abbahu said: This indicates** that **one writes a receipt** indicating that the woman received payment. Were the woman to attempt to collect payment of her marriage contract a second time, her husband would produce the receipt. **Rav Pappa said: We are dealing** in the mishna **with a place where one does not write a marriage contract.**

The *gemara* presses Rav Pappa: how can you maintain your position given that the *baraita* explicitly says the *ketubah* was lost and she can still collect? Rav Pappa responds with a forced but internally consistent reading. *Avda*, he says, means she claims it was lost — but claiming a loss is treated like hiding it: *ki hitmina bifneinu dami*. If she says "I lost it," the court suspects she is hiding it somewhere to use later. Therefore, Rav Pappa reads the *baraita* as a dialogue: when she says *avda*, we say *hitmina* — we suspect concealment — and we do not let her collect. The only case where she may collect is *nisrefa* — the *ketubah* was burned and we have evidence of the destruction.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״אִיבְּדָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ״ קָתָנֵי! דִּכְתַב לַהּ אִיהוּ. סוֹף סוֹף מַפְּקָא לַהּ וְגָבְיָא בַּהּ! מַאי ״אִיבְּדָה״ — אִיבְּדָה בָּאוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** how could Rav Pappa say that the *baraita* is dealing with a place where one does not write a marriage contract? **Isn't it taught** in that *baraita*: If a woman **lost her marriage contract?** The Gemara answers: The *baraita* is referring to a case **where** her husband **wrote her** a marriage contract contrary to the local custom. The Gemara asks: If he wrote her a marriage contract, the concern remains that **ultimately she** will **produce** the marriage contract **and collect** payment **with it** a second time. The Gemara answers: **What** is the meaning of **lost?** The woman **lost** her marriage contract **in the fire.** In that case, there is no longer any concern.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי הַיְינוּ ״נִשְׂרְפָה״? וְעוֹד: הִטְמִינָה, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? וְתוּ: אִיבְּדָה לְמָה לִי? אֶלָּא: כֹּל אִיבְּדָה — כִּי הִטְמִינָה בְּפָנֵינוּ דָּמֵי, וְלָא יָהֲבִינַן לַהּ עַד דְּאָמְרִי עֵדִים נִשְׂרְפָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, that is** the case of: Her marriage contract **was burned,** listed separately in the *baraita*. **And furthermore, what is there to say** with regard to the case of **concealed,** where the concern that she will collect payment twice remains? **And furthermore,** if burned comes to explain the meaning of lost, **why do I** need the *baraita* to list the case of **lost** at all? It would have been sufficient for the *baraita* to mention the case of a burned marriage contract. **Rather,** the meaning of the *baraita* is: The legal status of **any** case where the woman claims that **she lost** her marriage contract **is like** that of a case **where she concealed** it **before us, and we give her** payment of her marriage contract **only when the witnesses say that her marriage contract was burned.** Otherwise, even if witnesses testify that the practices characteristic of the wedding of a virgin were performed at her wedding, she does not collect payment of her marriage contract.

Under this reading, the three terms in the *baraita* are not three parallel cases each permitting collection. Rather, *avda* is the opening claim, *hitmina* is the court's response expressing suspicion, and only *nisrefa* is the scenario where collection is actually permitted. This is a significant *chiddush* to extract from a *baraita* whose plain meaning seems to permit collection in all three cases.

### Final Shovar Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם יֵשׁ עֵדִים כּוּ׳. וְלֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא מַפְּקָא עֵדֵי הִינּוּמָא בְּהַאי בֵּית דִּין וְגָבְיָא, וַהֲדַר מַפְּקָא עֵדֵי הִינּוּמָא בְּבֵי דִינָא אַחֲרִינָא וְגָבְיָא! בְּמָקוֹם דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר וַדַּאי כָּתְבִינַן שׁוֹבָר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to that same passage in the mishna: **If there are witnesses** that she went out of her father's house to her wedding with a *hinnuma*, or with her hair uncovered, in a manner typical of virgins, payment of her marriage contract is two hundred dinars, the Gemara asks: **But let us be concerned lest she** first **produce witnesses** that she went out with **a *hinnuma*, in this court, and collect** payment, **and then produce witnesses** that she went out with **a *hinnuma*, in that court, and collect** payment a second time. The Gemara answers: **In a place where it is not possible** to guarantee that she will not collect her marriage contract more than once in any other way, **certainly we write a receipt,** even according to the opinion that as a rule, one does not write a receipt.

The *gemara* then turns to the situation we have been circling: even where witnesses to the wedding can be produced, the double-collection risk remains. The *gemara* closes this discussion with a clear principle: *bi-makom d'lo efshar vadai kasvinan shovar* — where there is no alternative, a *shovar* is certainly written. This is the position that I had previewed earlier in the discussion, and the *gemara* arrives at it here as a *svara* that even Rav Pappa must acknowledge.

The practical upshot is as follows. According to Rav Pappa: in a community where *ketubot* are written, no receipts — the document governs, and if it cannot be produced (other than in a case of proven destruction), collection is impossible. In a community where *ketubot* are not written, or where the document was proven destroyed, there is no alternative, and a *shovar* must be written to prevent double collection. The more permissive view — that of Rabbi Abba — would write a *shovar* even in communities where *ketubot* are normally produced, thereby allowing collection whenever witnesses can testify to the nature of the wedding, with the husband protected by his receipt.

## Wedding Practices

### Kos Shel Besora

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֶעֱבִירוּ לְפָנֶיהָ כּוֹס שֶׁל בְּשׂוֹרָה. מַאי ״כּוֹס שֶׁל בְּשׂוֹרָה״? אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: כּוֹס יַיִן שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה מַעֲבִירִין לְפָנֶיהָ. כְּלוֹמַר: רְאוּיָה הָיְתָה זוֹ לֶאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָה. מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: אַטּוּ אַלְמָנָה מִי לָא אָכְלָה בִּתְרוּמָה? אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: זוֹ רֵאשִׁית, כִּתְרוּמָה רֵאשִׁית.
>
> **Translation:** § It is taught in that *baraita*: Or **passed before her a cup of** good **tidings.** The Gemara asks: **What is a cup of good tidings? Rav Adda bar Ahava says: A cup of *teruma* wine is passed before** the virgin bride, **meaning** that **this** woman **would have been eligible to eat *teruma*** had she married a priest. **Rav Pappa** strongly **objects to** this: **Is that to say** that **a widow does not eat *teruma*** if she marries a priest? Clearly she does. Therefore, what is the proof from *teruma* that she is a virgin? **Rather, Rav Pappa says:** The cup of *teruma* is passed before her to indicate that **this bride is first,** as she has not yet engaged in intercourse, **like *teruma*** that **is** the **first** gift separated from the produce.

Having established that witnesses can testify to wedding ceremonies as proof of a first marriage, the *gemara* — and the *baraita* cited earlier — enumerates those ceremonies. The first is *kos shel besora*, the cup of good tidings. Rav Adda bar Ahava explains: *kos yayin shel terumah mevi'in lefaneha* — they would bring before her a cup filled with *terumah* wine. The symbolism, he says, is *re'uya hayta zu le'echol bi-terumah* — she is fit to eat *terumah*, meaning that if she is marrying a *kohen*, her status as a woman who has never had relations with a man forbidden to a *kohen* is being publicly affirmed.

Rav Pappa objects: *atu almana mi lo achla bi-terumah?* — Does a widow not also eat *terumah* if she marries a *kohen*? Her being a non-virgin does not disqualify her from *terumah*, so this symbolism does not track the *betulah/be'ulah* distinction at all. Rav Pappa therefore offers his own explanation: *zu reishit ke-terumah* — this cup is like *terumah* in its quality of being *reishit*, a first. Just as *terumah* is the first portion separated, this night is her first, and the cup symbolizes that *rishonah*, that primacy.

### Wine Barrel Symbolism

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: חָבִית שֶׁל יַיִן מַעֲבִירִין לְפָנֶיהָ. אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: בְּתוּלָה — מַעֲבִירִין לְפָנֶיהָ סְתוּמָה, בְּעוּלָה — מַעֲבִירִין לְפָנֶיהָ פְּתוּחָה. אַמַּאי? נִיעַבַּר קַמֵּי בְּתוּלָה וְקַמֵּי בְּעוּלָה לָא נִיעַבַּר כְּלָל! זִימְנִין דְּתָפְסָה מָאתַיִם, וְאָמְרָה: אֲנָא בְּתוּלָה הֲוַאי, וְהַאי דְּלָא עַבַּרוּ קַמַּאי — אִתְּנוֹסֵי הוּא דְּאִתְּנִיסוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The custom is that **one passes a barrel of wine before her. Rav Adda bar Ahava said: One passes a sealed** barrel of wine **before a virgin, and one passes an open** barrel of wine **before a non-virgin.** The Gemara asks: **Why** is that necessary? **Let us pass** the sealed barrel **before the virgin, and before the non-virgin let us not pass** a barrel **at all.** Why is it necessary to publicize the fact that she is a non-virgin? The Gemara explains: It is necessary, as, **at times** there could be a case where a non-virgin unilaterally **seized two hundred** dinars as payment for her marriage contract **and said: I was a virgin, and** the fact **that they did not pass** a sealed barrel **before me was due to circumstances beyond their control.** In order to prevent deceit of that kind, an open barrel is passed before the non-virgin, so that people will remember that she is not a virgin.

A *baraita* cites Rabbi Yehuda's view that they would bring before her a barrel of wine. Rav Adda bar Ahava again supplies the interpretive key: *besulah mevi'in lefaneha setumah* — for a *besulah* they would bring a sealed cask; *be'ulah mevi'in lefaneha petucha* — for a *be'ulah* they would bring an open one. The symbolism is entirely transparent, and the wine barrel — sealed or open — would serve as direct concrete evidence of the woman's status at the time of her wedding.

### Concrete vs. Absence

The *gemara* asks: why not simply use the sealed barrel as an indicator, and the *absence* of any barrel as the indicator for a *be'ulah*? Why go through the effort of displaying an open barrel?

The answer returns to the principle established earlier in the discussion of *shor shachut*: we need concrete positive evidence, not merely the absence of evidence. *Zimnin di-tafsa masayim* — sometimes she will have already seized her husband's property and will claim 200, forcing him to bear the burden under *ha-motzi mei-chaveiro alav ha-re'ayah*. If she claims *besulah havina* and the only counter-evidence is that no sealed barrel was displayed, she can readily explain: *itnusi hu di-itnusu* — they were too caught up in the celebration, or too drunk, and simply never got around to the barrel ceremony. The absence of the practice proves nothing in the face of that ready excuse. But if an open barrel was explicitly brought before her, that is a concrete act — a *shor shachut* — that cannot be explained away, and that will serve as definitive evidence of her *be'ulah* status at the time of the wedding.
