# Yevamot 75 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mechussar Kipurim

### Intro: Mechussar Kipurim

The daf opens at approximately ten lines down on 75a, at the words *asher yithar*. We are completing the discussion of how we know that a *mechussar kipurim* — one who has undergone immersion and waited until nightfall but has not yet brought the required *korban* — may eat *terumah* but may not eat *kodshim*. The preceding analysis established three progressive stages of purification, derived primarily from the case of the *yoledes*: after immersion in the *mikvah* one may eat *maaser*; after nightfall one may eat *terumah*; and only after bringing one's *korban* does one attain full *tahara* permitting the eating of *kodshim*. A key point in learning from the *yoledes* was demonstrating that even a person still in the process of completing the *korban* obligation is already permitted to eat *terumah* once nightfall has passed.

### Dissenting Tana View

The *gemara* now addresses an alternative reading. The *tana de-bei Rabbi Yishmael* had read the verse *ad asher yithar* as referring to a *zav* who had seen a flow only twice and a *metzora* who was at the final stage of purification — individuals who do not require a *korban* — so that the phrase means only "until nightfall." But the dissenting *tana* reads the verse as referring to a classic *zav* with three episodes of flux (*zav ba'al gimel re'iyot*) and a fully quarantined *metzora muchlat*, who do require a *korban*. According to this view, *ad asher yithar* means "until he brings his *kapparah*," and the prohibition against eating *kodshim* (*u-vakodshim lo yokhal*) refers to actual *korbanot* — meaning one may not eat one's own sacrificial portions until one has completed the atonement process.

### Two Pesukim Analysis

This raises the question: according to the dissenting *tana*, why do we need two verses — the verse in Leviticus governing the *zav* and *metzora*, and the verse by the *yoledes* (*ve-chiper alav ha-kohen ve-taherah*) — both teaching that one may eat *korbanot* only after bringing the *korban*? The *gemara* answers that both are necessary. Had the Torah written only the verse regarding the *yoledes*, one might have thought the requirement is specific to her case because her *tum'a* is so extensive — spanning forty days for a boy and eighty for a girl — and therefore one might reason more leniently about the *zav* whose process is shorter. Conversely, had the Torah written only the verse regarding the *zav*, one might have said that his *tum'a* is strict precisely because it admits of no exceptions (*lo hutchal mikelalo*), whereas the *yoledes*, whose flow during the thirty-three or sixty-six days of *dam tohar* does not increase her *tum'a*, might be treated more leniently even before bringing her *korban*. Both verses are therefore needed, and in either case the conclusion is the same: a *mechussar kipurim* may eat *terumah* but not *kodshim*.

## Tvul Yom & Contact

### Vessels & Tvul Yom

The *gemara* now cites the verse *bamayim yuva ve-tamei ad ha-erev ve-taher*, which appears in the context of non-kosher animals and their carcasses, and which addresses the case of a vessel that has become *tamei* through contact with such a carcass. The verse prescribes that the vessel be immersed in water and remains *tamei* until nightfall — the same principle of *tvul yom* applies to vessels. One might ask: what is the *chidush* of this verse if the principle of *tvul yom* is already known? The answer points to something deeper than the application to vessels.

### Rav Zeira's Insight

Rav Zeira explains: the verse comes to teach us the law of *negi'ah* — contact. Until now the entire discussion has been framed in terms of eating: after immersion, when may one eat *maaser*? After nightfall, when may one eat *terumah*? But the question of what a *tvul yom* may touch is a separate matter entirely. A *tvul yom* holds the status of a *sheini le-tum'a*. A *sheini* does not transmit *tum'a* to *chullin* or *maaser*, but does render *terumah tamei* by contact. The verse, by speaking of the vessel — which cannot eat anything — teaches that the category of *tvul yom* is a problem not only for eating *terumah* but for coming into contact with *terumah*. These are not the same prohibition, and one cannot automatically derive the latter from the former.

A *Baraita* elaborates: the phrase *ve-tamei ad ha-erev ve-taher* creates a tension between *ve-tamei* ("it is still *tamei*") and *ve-taher* ("it becomes *tahor*"). The *drasha* notes that *ve-taher* is written in a form that implies an already-present degree of *tahara*, not purely a future state. The *gemara* resolves the tension: *kan le-ma'aser, kan le-terumah* — relative to *maaser* it is already *tahor*, but relative to *terumah* it remains *tamei*. This establishes a two-tiered status for the *tvul yom*.

### Kal Vachomer

Could one reverse the hierarchy — treating *maaser* as the more stringent category? The *gemara* rejects this: it is straightforward that eating *terumah* is more stringent than eating *maaser* — this we have already established — and therefore the parallel holds that touching *terumah* is more stringent than touching *maaser*. The argument proceeds with its own internal logic: since a *tvul yom* is prohibited from eating *terumah*, it follows naturally that this same status makes contact with *terumah* problematic.

### Yoledes & Tamei Status

The *gemara* then offers an alternative source: *bechol kodesh lo tiga ve-el ha-mikdash lo tavo*, the verse describing the *yoledes* during her thirty-three or sixty-six days of *dam tohar*. Her status during this period is that of a *tvul yom shel yom aroch* — a long-day *tvul yom* — since she has immersed but is still awaiting the night before her *korban*. The verse says she may not "touch" anything holy (*kodesh*). One might suppose this directly prohibits contact. However, the *gemara* demonstrates that *bechol kodesh lo tiga* means "do not eat *terumah*," not "do not physically touch." The analogy is drawn between *kodesh* and *mikdash*: just as entering the *mikdash* while *tamei* carries the penalty of *karet* — a punishment involving *netilat neshamah* — so too the prohibition of *kodesh* must refer to something that carries such a penalty. Since one is never liable to *karet* for merely touching *terumah*, only for eating it while *tamei*, the verse must be speaking of eating, not touching.

Yet the verse uses the language of *tiga* — touching — rather than *achila* — eating. The *gemara* explains: *negi'ah ke-achilah* — touching is being compared to eating, teaching that the same prohibition applies to contact as applies to eating. Just as the *tvul yom* cannot eat *terumah*, she cannot touch it either. The same parallel hierarchy applies: a *tvul yom* may eat *maaser* but not *terumah*, and similarly may touch *maaser* but may not touch *terumah*; a *mechussar kipurim* may eat *terumah* but not *kodshim*, and the same follows for contact.

## Ptzua Daka Marriage

### Intro: Ptzua Daka

The *gemara* now shifts to a new section of the *mishnah*, turning to the laws of *ptzua daka* and *krus shofcha* — those whose reproductive injury prohibits them from marrying a naturally born Jewish woman, as derived from the verse *lo yavo bi-khal Hashem*. The *mishnah* notes that a *ptzua daka* who is a *kohen* retains his ability to eat *terumah* — his own status is not compromised — but his wife loses the ability to eat *terumah* because by having relations with a man forbidden to her she becomes a *chalalah*, and a *chalalah* is excluded from eating *terumah*. This asymmetry is striking: the verse speaks primarily to him, yet his *terumah* status is unaffected while hers is transformed.

### Wife's Status Change

An interesting case arises at the end of the *mishnah*: what if the man's condition developed after they were already married but before they had consummated the marriage? The woman has not yet had relations with him, has not yet become a *chalalah*, and therefore has not yet been invalidated. The question is whether she should nonetheless be prohibited from eating *terumah* on the grounds that consummation — and thus her invalidation — is inevitable. The *mishnah* rules that she may continue eating *terumah* until she has actually had relations with him under these circumstances.

### Rabbi Elazar Debate

The *gemara* asks: whose position does this *mishnah* reflect? *Mishtameret le-vi'ah pesulah de-Oraita achla?* Who holds that a woman "watching" for a *bi'ah* that is forbidden and that will surely come — yet has not happened — may still eat *terumah*? Rabbi Elazar identifies this as the position of Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon in an earlier dispute, which addressed the analogous case of a widow (*almanah*) betrothed to a *Kohen Gadol*, or a divorcée betrothed to a regular *kohen*, where *erusin* has taken place but the marriage has not yet been consummated. In those cases as well, the question is whether the anticipated — but not yet actualized — *bi'ah psulab* disqualifies her from *terumah*. Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon said she may still eat, and our *mishnah* follows that ruling.

### Rabbi Yochanan Reply

Rabbi Yochanan offers a broader reading: even according to Rabbi Meir — who in the *erusin* case prohibits eating *terumah* because the anticipated invalidation is so clear — here the *mishnah* would be different. Why? *She-kvar achlah* — she has already been eating *terumah* throughout the marriage. Under these circumstances, one does not force her to stop eating based on something that has not yet happened. The ruling is not to initiate eating when invalidation is on the horizon, but where she has already been eating validly, she is not told to stop until her status actually changes.

Rabbi Elazar pushes back: *she-kvar achlah lo amrinan* — the fact that one has been eating is not in itself a halakhically relevant consideration. He offers a proof: a *bat Yisrael* married to a *kohen* who was eating *terumah* during the marriage certainly cannot continue eating after her husband dies, even though *she-kvar achlah*. Rabbi Yochanan responds: *hasam pasak kinyana* — there the marriage bond has ceased entirely. But here the relationship has not ended; she is still married. The only reason to stop her would be anticipation of a future event that has not yet occurred, and so long as her actual status has not changed, and she has been eating legitimately, she may continue.

## Ptzua Daka Definition

### Testicle Injuries

The *gemara* now turns to a detailed and practically significant discussion of what physically constitutes *ptzua daka*. The *mishnah* states: *eizu p'suah dakah — kol she-niftzu ha-beitzim shelo*, any case in which the testicles have been *niftzu*. Rashi understands *niftzu* as deriving from the word *petza* — a wound, specifically a cutting-type injury, such as one made with a knife. Rashi's reading should be kept in mind when other translations are considered.

### Three Injury Types

The *Baraita* elaborates further: the injuries include *niftzu* (wounded), *nikbu* (punctured, i.e., a hole has been made), and *nimoku* (withered or shrunken). Rashi explains *nimoku* as *machamas makkah nimoku ufachtu lahem mei'aleihem* — they shrank as a result of an injury. This is significant: Rashi specifies that it was *machamas makkah*, due to a blow, because the entire discussion — as becomes explicit shortly — pertains only to injuries caused through human agency (*bidei adam*). Rabbi Yishmael son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka adds: *ve-afillu chasru* — even if a small portion is simply cut away. He reports this ruling in the name of the sages of *Kerem Beyavneh*.

The *gemara* also derives from the word *dakah* a third injury type — crushing — alongside cutting and severing.

### Three Body Parts

Rava then teaches that all three injury types are interchangeable across all three relevant body parts: the *gid* (penis), the *beitzim* (testicles), and the *chutei beitzim* (the ducts, analogous to the vas deferens, that connect the testicles to the penis). The full formulation is: *ptzua* (wounded/punctured) in any of them, *dach* (crushed) in any of them, *karus* (severed) in any of them — whether the injury occurred to the *gid*, the *beitzim*, or the *chutei beitzim*.

## One Testicle Status

### Rabbi Yishmael Teaching

Rabbi Yishmael son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka reported from the sages of *Kerem Beyavneh*: *kol she-ein lo ella beitzah achas eino ella saris chamah* — whoever has only one testicle is like a *saris chamah*, and is *kasher*. This teaching is immediately surprising. We have just established that even a wound to a single testicle is disqualifying, so surely the complete absence of one testicle should be even more severe. And the comparison to a *saris chamah* — a man rendered sterile naturally, typically through illness or fever — raises its own complications, since we are not necessarily speaking of sterility.

### Saris Chama Clarified

The *gemara* presses: is he literally a *saris chamah*? That cannot be, since a *saris chamah* is someone rendered sterile through a natural process such as illness, whereas missing one testicle may be a birth defect with no such connection. The *gemara* clarifies: *ela harei hu ke-saris chamah* — he is not a *saris chamah*, but he is *like* a *saris chamah*, and just as a *saris chamah* is *kasher*, so too is he.

### Bidei Shamayim

Rashi's explanation is that the case involves a birth defect — the person was born with only one testicle — and the ruling follows from the principle that *bidei shamayim* conditions do not disqualify. The comparison to *saris chamah* fits perfectly on Rashi's reading: just as a *saris chamah* is naturally sterile and yet *kasher* because it is *bidei shamayim*, so too someone born with one testicle is *kasher* by the same principle. Tosafot, however, reads the case differently and says we are dealing with *bidei adam* — a case where one testicle was removed through human action. On Tosafot's reading, the ruling would represent a disagreement with the earlier position, or alternatively, an ironic inversion: a complete removal of a testicle may be *less* problematic than an injury to it, because an injury to the testicle may cause infertility whereas removal of a complete testicle — as Tosafot observes from practical experience — need not.

### Tosafot Surgical Evidence

Tosafot's evidence is remarkable. At the bottom of 75a, and continuing onto 75b, Tosafot writes: *ve-od de-bidei adam nami hachazinan kama bnei adam she-gomemim mehen beitza achat u-molidin* — and furthermore, even when done through human agency, we see a number of people from whom one testicle has been removed who nonetheless father children. Tosafot adds that we cannot simply respond by saying *d'lo mistavra lomar tzei ve-chakur bneihem me'eimat* — that all these men's children are suspected of being *mamzerim* and their wives have been unfaithful. That would be absurd given the scale of the phenomenon. So Tosafot concludes that removal of a single testicle does not render a person infertile and therefore does not create the status of *ptzua daka*.

This raises a further question about what precisely Tosafot is observing historically. He appears to be referring to surgical removal of a testicle as a common practice in his time. He even notes, toward the end of that *tosafot*, that since there is a *Yerushalmi* opinion that the right testicle may be treated differently from the left, *ve-tov le-hazhir la-refuim la-chtoch shel smol im efshar* — it is advisable to instruct physicians, if they are removing one, to remove the left one if possible. The medical and halachic engagement here is striking.

### Rav Moshe Reassessment

This leads to a significant responsum by Rav Moshe Feinstein. A man had undergone a testicular biopsy — a small portion of tissue was removed from a testicle — in order to assist with fertility treatments. The earlier *Baraita* had explicitly stated *ve-afillu chasru* — even if a small portion is cut away, it is disqualifying. On a straightforward reading, a biopsy would seem to fall under this category, since it is *bidei adam* and involves the removal of even a small portion. Rav Moshe argues, however, that the *gemara's* entire framework ties the disqualification to infertility. Since modern medicine demonstrates that such a biopsy does not cause infertility — indeed, the very purpose of the procedure was to enable fertility — the *gemara's* criterion of *chasru* must be understood as referring only to cases where infertility results. When it does not, the *din* of *ptzua daka* does not apply. This is a striking example of the interplay between halachic categories and empirical medical knowledge.

## Fertility & Causation

### Case: Man in Palm Tree

The *gemara* now asks directly: *ve-nekev lo molid?* — is it truly the case that a person with a puncture cannot father children? A case is cited: a man climbed a palm tree (*salikal'dikla*) and a thorn punctured his testicle (*charzeia silu be-beitzim*). A viscous stream then emerged from his penis (*ve-nafak minei ke-chut de-mugla*), and he subsequently fathered children. This would seem to prove that *nekev* — puncture — does not necessarily cause infertility.

### Rav's Skeptical Reply

Shmuel transmitted this story to Rav. Rav's response was dismissive: *tzei u-vtzor bivnav mei'ayin heim* — go and investigate the origins of his children. Rav was not prepared to accept the empirical evidence at face value and instead suggested that the children might not actually be his. This response represents one pole in the ongoing tension between empirical observation and established *halachic* categories: rather than revising the *halakha*, Rav preferred to question the apparent facts.

### Natural Causes Kosher

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *ptzua daka bidei shamayim kasher* — if the condition arose through natural causes, the person is *kasher*. The category of *bidei shamayim* includes birth defects, illness, and conditions arising from the natural functioning of the body. The question of whether a physical trauma caused by an external but non-human event — such as a falling tree branch struck by lightning — would be considered *bidei shamayim* is left open, but standard *bidei shamayim* cases such as illness and congenital conditions are clearly excluded from the prohibition.

### Ptzua vs. Haptzua

Rava derives this distinction from the language of the verse itself: the Torah writes *ptzua* and not *ha-ptzua*. *Ha-ptzua* would suggest a permanent, pre-existing state — how one was born. *Ptzua* indicates that this occurred to the person as an event, implying that the disqualification requires an act of injury, not a natural condition.

### Mamzer Comparison

A *Baraita* reinforces this: *ne'emar lo yavo ptzua daka ve-ne'emar lo yavo mamzer* — the Torah employs the same language of *lo yavo* for both *ptzua daka* and *mamzer*. Just as *mamzeirus* arises only through a human act — a forbidden sexual union — *ma le-halan bidei adam, af kan bidei adam* — so too *ptzua daka* applies only when the injury resulted from human agency. Where the condition is entirely natural, there is no disqualification.

### Rav Herzog Principle

A further and quite radical extension of the *bidei shamayim* category is proposed in a responsum by Rav Herzog. He addresses a case where a person had a natural condition — such as cancer — which required surgery, and the surgery itself constituted one of the physical injuries that would ordinarily create the status of *ptzua daka*. One would initially classify this as *bidei adam*, since a surgeon performed the procedure. Rav Herzog argues otherwise by analogy to the *mamzer* case. With *mamzeirus*, the human act (*bidei adam*) occurs at the beginning of the process — the forbidden union — while the rest of the process, the gestation and birth of the child, is *bidei shamayim*. What makes something *bidei adam* is that the initiating cause was a human act. By parallel reasoning, where the initiating cause — the underlying condition — was *bidei shamayim*, the fact that a surgeon then treated it does not transform the entire sequence into *bidei adam*. The initial cause governs the classification. Therefore, surgery performed to treat a naturally occurring condition remains in the *bidei shamayim* category, and the resulting state does not disqualify the person.

## Injuries Interchangeable

### Rava's Rule

Rava's teaching, noted above, is that all three injury types (*ptzua*, *dach*, *karus*) apply to all three body parts (*gid*, *beitzim*, *chutei beitzim*). The *gemara* spells this out fully: whether the *gid* is wounded, whether the *beitzim* are wounded, whether the *chutei beitzim* are wounded; whether the *gid* is crushed, whether the *beitzim* are crushed, whether the *chutei beitzim* are crushed; whether the *gid* is severed, whether the *beitzim* are severed, whether the *chutei beitzim* are severed — in all cases the *din* of *ptzua daka* or *krus shofcha* applies.

### Bar Hamduri Challenge

Bar Hamduri challenged Rava: *mimai de-hai ptzua daka be-oto makom?* — how do we know that *ptzua daka* refers specifically to the genitals? Perhaps *ptzua* (wounded) and *dakah* (crushed) describe any kind of bodily wound, such as a head injury?

### Future Generations Test

Rava responds: from the fact that the verse does not enumerate *dorot* — future generations — as it does by *Ammoni u-Mitzri* (*dor shlishi yavo lahem*) or *mamzer* (*lo yavo ad dor asiri*). The absence of any generational qualifier implies that we are discussing an injury the very nature of which precludes future generations — i.e., an injury affecting fertility, which necessarily points to the genitals.

### Alternative Explanation

Bar Hamduri counters: perhaps the verse mentions no future generations simply because only the man himself is forbidden — *ihu hu de-assur, brei u-bar brei kashrei* — only he is disqualified, not his descendants. On this reading the absence of generational language proves nothing about the nature or location of the injury.

### Krus Shofcha Parallel

The *gemara* therefore turns to the companion term: *ptzua daka* is juxtaposed with *krus shofcha*. The word *shofcha* — from *lishpoch*, to pour out — evokes a pouring or spilling of fluid. *Krus shofcha* thus means a severing that causes a flow, rather than a stream. This makes plain that we are speaking of the genitals, since the *shofcha* terminology describes what ordinarily emerges as a stream now emerging as a flood because of the injury. Thus by analogy, *ptzua daka* must also be speaking of the same bodily location.

### Stream vs. Flow

But the *gemara* then asks: how do we know that *krus shofcha* itself refers to the genitals? Perhaps *shofcha* refers to a wound on the lip, causing profuse bleeding? The *gemara* responds: *shafcha ketiv bi-mkom she-shofech* — the word *shafcha* indicates a place where fluid ordinarily flows, but now, as a result of the cutting (*krus*), it pours out as a flood rather than a stream. *Mi she-al yedei krita shofech* — it must be a part of the body that, because of the injury, now pours where it formerly streamed. *She-lo al yedei krita eino shofech ela mekale'ach* — without the injury, the fluid from that location would emerge only as a stream, not a flow. This excludes the nose and mouth, where fluids flow freely regardless of injury. The combination of "a place where fluid ordinarily exits as a stream" and "only as a result of the cutting does it now pour" identifies the genitals unambiguously.

## Corona Cases

### Through-Piercing Case

The *mishnah* had stated: *ve-im nishtayer me-ha'atara afillu ke-chut ha-sa'arah kasher* — if even a hair-thin strip of the *atara* (corona) remains toward the body, the man is *kasher*. The implication is that the disqualifying injury to the penis (*krus*) must extend from the corona toward the body; an injury affecting only the portion from the corona toward the tip is not disqualifying.

A specific case is now posed: *nikav le-mata me-atara she-kenegdo le-mala me-atara* — what if a puncture begins below the *atara* (i.e., on the side of the tip) but passes entirely through the thickness of the penis and emerges on the other side above the *atara* (i.e., on the side toward the body)? Does the point of entry — which would not be disqualifying — determine the status, or does the point of exit — which is toward the body — disqualify?

### Corona Debate

Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba was inclined to rule *le-achshurei* — that such a case is *kasher*, since the puncture originates from below the *atara*. Rabbi Assi corrected him, citing Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *atara kol she-hi me'akevet* — even a minimal amount of the *atara* is decisive. As long as any portion of the puncture emerges from the side of the corona toward the body, it is disqualifying. The fact that part of the puncture originates from the other side does not save the case.

### Hair-Thin Strip Rule

Returning to the ruling about the severed penis: if even a hair-thin strip of the corona remains toward the body, the person is *kasher*. This rule requires clarification.

### Circumference Question

Ravina raised the following question: when the Sages say that a hair-thin strip (*ke-chut ha-sa'arah*) of the *atara* suffices — must this strip extend across the full circumference of the *atara*, or does a *rov* (majority) of the circumference suffice?

### Majority Answer

Rabba Tospa'a answered Ravina: a hair-thin strip across the majority (*rov*) of the circumference, toward the body (*ve-chelapi reisha*), is sufficient. The *Gra* notes that there is an *alef* indicating a variant reading of *kula* (the entire circumference) rather than *ruba* (majority), and so the precise textual tradition here is a matter of some question. But on the version of the text as cited, Rabba Tospa'a's answer is that majority suffices.

## Thickness Removal

### Quill Case

Rav Huna rules on two cases involving partial removal of the penis's thickness rather than complete severance. The first is *ke-kulmus* — like a quill. When scribes prepared quills, they would shave down the thickness of the shaft to create a tapered tip. Similarly, if the thickness of the penis was cut away on one side, creating a tapered appearance without puncturing all the way through, Rav Huna rules this is *kesherah* — valid.

### Drain Pipe Case

The second case is *ke-marzav* — like a drain pipe. Here the exterior remains intact but the interior flesh has been hollowed out, leaving the internal piping (*gid*) in place but removing the surrounding tissue. Rav Huna rules this is *pesulah* — disqualifying.

### Air Exposure Test

What is Rav Huna's reasoning? *Hai sholet ba-avira, ve-hai lo sholet ba-avira* — in the case of the pipe, the interior workings of the penis are exposed to air, which somehow leads to infertility. In the case of the quill, only the external thickness is affected; the interior remains protected and unexposed to air. This is Rav Huna's determinative criterion.

### Rav Chisda Opposite

Rav Chisda rules precisely the opposite: *ke-marzav kesherah, ke-kulmus pesulah* — the pipe is valid, the quill is disqualifying.

### Friction Theory

Rav Chisda's reasoning is different: *hai garid ve-hai lo garid* — one case draws out semen effectively during intercourse and the other does not. Rashi explains: if the outer thickness of the penis is cut away, friction during intercourse is reduced, and this impairs the ejaculation of semen, thus affecting fertility. In the case where the interior is hollowed out but the thickness remains, friction is preserved and ejaculation is normal. The criterion for Rav Chisda is not air exposure but the mechanics of fertilization.

### Rava Agrees

Rava sides with Rav Huna: *ke-vatei de-Rav Huna mistavra* — Rav Huna's reasoning is persuasive. The air-exposure criterion is the more compelling one.

### Faucet Analogy

Rava addresses Rav Chisda's friction argument directly: even if the outer thickness is reduced, this need not impair fertility, because *midi de-havah a-barza de-chavita* — it is like the faucet inserted into a barrel. Rashi explains that such a spout, though it may be wide at one end, narrows as it enters the barrel; similarly, even if the exterior of the penis is thinned, during deeper penetration the surrounding tissue narrows and provides friction at that point. Therefore, reduction in outer thickness does not necessarily impair the drawing out of semen, and Rav Chisda's concern about *gridusa* — the friction mechanism — is answered.

### Final Ruling

Ravina reported to Mareimar: *hachi amar Mar Zutra mishemei de-Rav Pappa, hilchasa bein ke-kulmus bein ke-marzav kesherah* — the *halakha* is that in both the quill case and the drain pipe case, the ruling is *kasher*. The *gemara* then notes a further question — *mihu miba'ei lei le-mata me-heter le-mala* — regarding the precise location of the injury relative to the permissible and prohibited zones, which carries over into the continuation of the *gemara*.
