# Yevamot 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Ervah Review

### Opening & Drashos

The daf begins at the bottom of 8b, at the *gufa* — the main body of the *sugya*. At this point we have already worked through the question of why a *pasuk* is necessary to establish that there is no *yibum* in the case of *ervah*. The difficulty was that one might have tried to derive the exclusion from the case of *achos isha* — wife's sister — on the grounds that *yibum* already overrides that aspect of the *ervah* prohibition, and precisely for that reason a special *pasuk* is needed to place the case of wife's sister back into a *yibum* context. That was the first *drasha*, based on the word *aleha*.

The second *drasha* was Rabbi's — more was being read into the *pasuk* of *velakach veyibmah* — and it was less immediately transparent. Rabbi reads the phrase as indicating that only she, and not another, is subject to the obligation of *yibum*, implying that there are cases where the *mitzvah* cannot be performed: specifically, the case of *ervah* and the case of *tzarat ervah*.

A further critical point emerged in this context: once the Gemara introduces the concept of *tzarat ervah*, one might reasonably have assumed that the *tzara* prohibition applies even outside the context of *yibum*. If a man's brother dies and one of the brother's wives is an *ervah* to the surviving brother, perhaps the co-wife is also forbidden to him as a *tzarat ervah* even in ordinary circumstances — not merely at the moment of *yibum*. The *pasuk* therefore had to make emphatically clear that *tzarat ervah* is only a category in the context of *yibum*. The logic is that since there is no *mitzvas yibum* with the co-wife of an *ervah*, she falls back under the prohibition of *eshet ach* — at least, that is the most straightforward explanation.

## Rabbi's Drasha

### Velakach Veyibmah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: ״וְלָקַח״ ״וּלְקָחָהּ״, ״וְיִבֵּם״ ״וְיִבְּמָהּ״, לֶאֱסוֹר צָרוֹת וַעֲרָיוֹת. מִידֵי ״צָרוֹת״ כְּתִיבָא הָכָא? וְעוֹד: צָרוֹת מִ״לִּצְרוֹר״ נָפְקָא!
>
> **Translation:** § While clarifying the exposition of the phrase "with her," an alternative interpretation attributed to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi was mentioned. The Gemara discusses **the** matter **itself. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The verse does not say **have intercourse,** but **"have intercourse with her."** And it does not say **and consummate the levirate marriage,** but **"and consummate the levirate marriage with her."** These phrases serve **to prohibit rival wives and** women **with whom relations are forbidden.** The Gemara asks: **Are rival wives written here?** In other words, what is the connection between the verse and the topic of rival wives, an issue that is not even mentioned in the verse? **And furthermore:** The prohibition with regard to **rival wives is derived** from the phrase **"To be a rival wife [*litzror*],"** not from this source.

With that background in place, the *gufa* turns to examine Rabbi's *drasha* from *velakach veyibmah* directly. Rabbi argues: the *pasuk* could have written *velakach* alone, and it could have written *veyibem* alone, yet it says *velakcha veyibmah* — with the extra *hei* in each word — *le-asur tzaros ervayos*: to exclude the co-wives of *arayos* from the obligation of *yibum*. As Rava noted in the preceding discussion, the exclusion of *ervah* herself could be taken for granted; it is specifically *tzarat ervah* that requires a textual source.

### Tzara Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לִצְרוֹר״ מַפֵּיק לֵיהּ רַבִּי לְכִדְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. צָרָה הָכָא כְּתִיב? הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִם כֵּן, לֵימָא קְרָא ״וְלָקַח״, מַאי ״וּלְקָחָהּ״ — כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא תְּרֵי לִקּוּחִין, דְּאִי בָּעֵי נָסֵיב הַאי וְאִי בָּעֵי נָסֵיב הַאי — שַׁרְיָא. וְאִי לָא — תַּרְוַיְיהוּ אֲסִירִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **derives** a different *halakha* **from** the phrase **"to be a rival wife," in accordance with that** statement **of Rabbi Shimon.** In Rabbi Shimon's opinion, the exemption of two sisters from levirate marriage applies not only when the *yavam* is married to one of the sisters, but also if two sisters who come before him for levirate marriage were previously married to two of his brothers. In that case, the levirate bond itself is sufficient for them to be considered rival wives of one another. And with regard to the previous question: **Are rival wives written here?** The answer is that **this is what he is saying: If so,** that the verse does not come to exempt rival wives, **let the verse state** only: **Have intercourse. What is** the meaning of **"have intercourse with her"?** It serves to limit the case to one woman and not two, in the following manner: **Anywhere that there are two** possibilities of **having intercourse,** i.e., there is a choice between marrying one of two women in levirate marriage, in such a way **that if he wants he may marry this one, and if he wants he may marry that one,** then each one is **permitted. And if** he does **not** have a choice and is obligated to marry one of them, e.g., if the other is a forbidden relation to him, **they are both prohibited,** including the rival wife.

The Gemara immediately presses: where exactly is the concept of *tzara* to be found in this *pasuk*? And further, is not *tzara* already derived from the word *litzror* in the *pasuk* concerning *achos isha* — where the very root connoting *tzara* appears in the context of a *ervah*? Why abandon so elegant a source? The Gemara answers: Rabbi needs *litzror* for an entirely different *drasha* — one that will be explained presently — and therefore *litzror* is not available to Rabbi for the ordinary case of *tzarat ervah*.

## Zika & Achos Zekuka

### Zika & Achos Zekuka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְיִבְּמָהּ״ — בִּמְקוֹם יִיבּוּם הוּא דַּאֲסִירָא צָרָה, שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹם יִיבּוּם — שַׁרְיָא צָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** When the verse states: **"And consummate the levirate marriage with her,"** it comes to teach the following. **It is in a place** where **levirate marriage** is applicable **that a rival wife is prohibited,** whereas **in a place** where **levirate marriage** is **not** applicable, **a rival wife is permitted.**

The *drasha* Rabbi applies to *litzror* concerns the concept of *zika* — the quasi-connection that exists between a surviving brother and his deceased brother's widows prior to *yibum*. The understanding of *zika* is that some remnant of the deceased brother's marriage continues and attaches itself to the living brother; when *yibum* is actually performed, that potential is actualized into a full marriage.

The *case* Rabbi derives from *litzror* is the following: two brothers — call them Reuven and Shimon — are each married to one of two sisters. Both brothers die. A third brother, Levi, now has a *mitzvas yibum* to both women. One might have thought: since Levi is not personally forbidden to either Rachel or Leah, let him choose one of them and perform *yibum* with her. But Rabbi Shimon rules that he cannot perform *yibum* at all. Here is why: because Levi has a *zika* — a quasi-*kiddushin* — to both women simultaneously. If he performs *yibum* with Rachel, he thereby becomes fully married to the sister of Leah, to whom he still has an active *zika*. Since *zika* functions as a form of quasi-*kiddushin*, completing the marriage with one renders him the husband of the *achot zekukato* — the sister of the woman to whom he remains *zakuk*. That is a genuine *ervah* problem.

Rashi's language is precise on this point: *v'zika havei k'ktzat kiddushin* — *zika* is like a partial *kiddushin* — and therefore *v'zekukaso havei ke-ishto* — the woman to whom he has a *zika* is treated as his wife. He cannot do *yibum* with either one, though he must perform *chalitza*. The source for this ruling is *litzror*: when two *yevamos* become competitors — *tzaros* — to one another through the *zika*, as when they are sisters, the *pasuk* teaches *lo yihyeh lecha likuchin afilu be-achas mehen* — you cannot even execute a *likuchin* with either of them.

This is the case of *achos zekuka* — the sister of the woman to whom there is *zika* — and it is a categorically different situation from the ordinary *tzarat ervah*. This explains why Rabbi cannot use *litzror* for the standard case of *tzarat ervah*: he needs it for this more specialized *drasha*.

## Two Wives & Choice

### Two Wives & Choice

The Gemara returns to the original question: how does Rabbi actually read the concept of *tzara* into the *pasuk* of *velakcha veyibmah*? The answer proceeds as follows. The *pasuk* uses the form *velakcha* — with the extra *hei* — rather than the simpler *velakach*. The significance of *velakcha* is that it implies *hi v'lo acheres* — this one and not another — which only makes sense if there are two women to choose between. The *pasuk* is therefore speaking about a situation with two potential *likuchin*, where the brother could take this one or that one.

The derivation is: *kol heicha d'ika terei likuchin d'ee ba'i nasiv hai v'ee ba'i nasiv hai, sharei* — wherever there are two marriages potentially available, and the brother is free to choose either, then there is a *mitzvas yibum*. But *v'ee lo, trevaihu asirin* — if he cannot exercise a free choice with either of them, both are forbidden. The implicit premise — already established — is that there is no *mitzvah* of *yibum* with an *ervah*. So if one of the two wives is an *ervah*, the brother cannot freely choose her; and since he cannot choose either freely, neither one is subject to *yibum*. That is how *tzara* is read into *velakcha*.

### Viyibmah & Context

The extra *hei* of *veyibmah* contributes the following: *B'makom yibum hu d'asira tzara, shelo b'makom yibum sharya tzara* — the prohibition of *tzara* applies only in the context of *yibum*, not outside it. As reviewed earlier, the concern might have arisen that a *tzarat ervah* should be forbidden as a general matter. *Veyibmah* makes clear that this prohibition is confined strictly to the moment of *yibum* obligation. Outside that context, the co-wife of an *ervah* carries no such disability. This completes Rabbi's reading of *velakcha veyibmah*.

## Cross-Exam Drashos

### Velakcha & Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הַאי ״וּלְקָחָהּ״ — מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לְהוּ לְכִדְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: ״וּלְקָחָהּ״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמְּגָרְשָׁה בְּגֵט וּמַחְזִירָהּ. ״וְיִבְּמָהּ״ — עַל כׇּרְחָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **the Rabbis,** who do not interpret the verse in this manner, **what do they do with this** emphasis on **"have intercourse with her"?** How do they interpret it? The Gemara responds: **They need this for that which Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina** said, **as Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina said: "And have intercourse with her"** means marriage, i.e., she is fully his wife from that moment onward. This **teaches that he divorces her with a bill of divorce** after levirate marriage, and she can no longer be released by *ḥalitza*, **and he** may subsequently **remarry her** if he so wishes. And the verse **"and consummate the levirate marriage with her,"** this means **against her will.** Although betrothal in general does not take effect without the woman's consent, levirate marriage can be effected against her will.

With both *drashos* now mapped out, the Gemara proceeds to its characteristic cross-examination: what does each opinion do with the textual sources relied upon by the other? The Rabbanan use *velakcha* and *veyibmah* differently. The extra *hei* in *velakcha*, according to the statement of Rabbi Yose berabbi Chanina, teaches that *yibum* creates a normal marriage status: *she-megarshah be-get u-machzirah* — the *yavam* may divorce the *yevama* with a *get*, and if he does, he may subsequently remarry her.

Two important *chiddushim* are embedded in this ruling. First, after *yibum* is performed, there can be a divorce — one might have thought the *mitzvah* created a permanent bond. Second, and more striking, even after he divorces her, she does not revert to being *achos ishto*. One might have supposed that the dispensation that enabled the *yibum* was exhausted by the first act, or at minimum by the marriage as a whole, such that upon divorce she would reassume her former status as a forbidden relative. The *pasuk* teaches otherwise: *velakcha* tells us that *yibum* confers a completely normal *kiddushin* status, with all the attendant consequences — divorce and remarriage alike.

### Viyibmah & Consent

The extra *hei* of *veyibmah*, according to the Rabbanan, teaches that *yibum* may be effectuated even *al korchah* — even against the woman's will. This is a conceptually significant distinction from ordinary *kiddushin*, which requires the woman's consent. The explanation is that since the *zika* already transfers to the living brother — constituting a quasi-*kiddushin* already in place — the subsequent act of intercourse merely actualizes a potential that preexists. Therefore neither explicit consent nor even conscious intention is required. The Gemara extends this principle: if the *yavam* did not realize the woman was his *yevama* — or even, in the Gemara's more extreme formulation, if he fell from a roof and accidentally had intercourse with her — the *yibum* is nonetheless valid. The requirement of *kavanah* that applies in other halachic contexts does not apply here.

### Rabbi's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי? דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא — מִ״לְּאִשָּׁה״ נָפְקָא, עַל כׇּרְחָהּ — מִ״יְּבָמָה יָבֹא עָלֶיהָ״ נָפְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** from where does **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi derive these widely accepted *halakhot*? The Gemara responds: **That** *halakha* cited by **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina,** that a woman taken in levirate marriage has the status of a regular wife who may be divorced and remarried, **is derived from** the verse "and take her to him **to be his wife"** (Deuteronomy 25:5), which means that she will be his wife for all purposes, like any other wife. And the *halakha* that levirate marriage can be effected **against her will is derived from "her brother-in-law will have intercourse with her,"** which indicates that it can be achieved without her consent.

The Gemara then asks: since Rabbi uses *velakcha veyibmah* to derive the laws of *tzarat ervah*, where does he obtain the rulings that the Rabbanan derive from those words — the normal marriage status enabling divorce and remarriage, and the *al korchah* principle? Rabbi answers: both are derived from other places in the same passage. The rule that *yibum* creates a marriage with full wife status — enabling divorce and remarriage — he derives from the words *velakachah lo le-ishah*, "and he shall take her to be his wife," which establishes the normal *ishah* status. The *al korchah* principle — that the act is valid even without consent — he derives from *yevamah yavo aleha*, "the brother-in-law shall come upon her," which by its phrasing implies that the act takes effect regardless of circumstance.

### Aleha & Kares

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי, הַאי ״עָלֶיהָ״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: אֵין חַיָּיבִין בֵּית דִּין אֶלָּא עַל דָּבָר שֶׁזְּדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְשִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת. וְכֵן הַמָּשִׁיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what does Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **do with this** superfluous phrase, **"with her"?** The Gemara responds: **He needs it for that which is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to a different matter. The Sages said: **The court is obligated** to bring a bull as a sin-offering for an unwittingly committed communal sin on account of an erroneous *halakha* they taught **only for a matter whose intentional** transgression incurs ***karet* and whose unwitting** violation necessitates the sacrifice of **a sin-offering. And similarly, the anointed** High Priest, who also brings a bull for an unwittingly committed sin, is obligated to do so only if his mistake involved a matter whose intentional transgression incurs *karet* and whose unwitting transgression necessitates the sacrifice of a sin-offering.

This brings the Gemara to a pivotal question: what does Rabbi do with the word *aleha* — the word the Rabbanan use to situate the prohibition of *tzarat ervah* within the context of *yibum*? The answer is that Rabbi uses *aleha* for an entirely different, and far-reaching, legal principle derived from a *braisa*: the category of sin for which *beis din* and private individuals bring a *chatat* offering is limited exclusively to sins whose deliberate violation is punishable by *kares* and whose inadvertent commission by a private individual incurs a *chatat*.

The *pasuk* in *Vayikra* concerning the *chatat* of the congregation contains the phrase *asher chatu aleha*; the *pasuk* in the *ervah* section concerning *achos isha* likewise contains the word *aleha*. By means of a *gezeirah shavah*, Rabbi links these two instances: just as *aleha* by *achos isha* pertains to something punishable by *kares*, so too *aleha* in the context of the congregational *chatat* indicates that the relevant category of sin is specifically one punishable by *kares*. According to Rabbi, then, *aleha* by *achos isha* teaches not about *tzarat ervah* — which he derives elsewhere — but about the threshold of *kares* that defines the entire domain of *chatat* liability.

## Chatat Categories

### Baraita Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, אֶלָּא עַל דָּבָר שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְשִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת. וּתְנַן נָמֵי: כׇּל מִצְוָה שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְעַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת, יָחִיד — מֵבִיא כִּשְׂבָּה אוֹ שְׂעִירָה, נָשִׂיא — מֵבִיא שָׂעִיר, מָשִׁיחַ וּבֵית דִּין — מְבִיאִין פַּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** a community brings a bull for an unwitting communal sin **for idolatrous worship only** if the act of idolatry involved **a matter that for its intentional** violation **one is liable to** receive ***karet* and for its unwitting** violation one is liable to bring **a sin-offering. And we also learned** in a mishna: With regard to **any mitzva in the Torah for whose intentional** violation **one is liable to** receive ***karet* and for its unwitting** violation one is liable to bring **a sin-offering,** if a private **individual** sinned unwittingly, he **brings a ewe or a she-goat.** If it was **a *Nasi*, he brings a goat** for a sin of this kind, and if the sinner was **an anointed** High Priest **or the court, they bring a bull.**

The *braisa* cited here articulates the principle with precision: *ein chayavin beis din ela al davar shezdonoh kares v'shigagato chatat* — *beis din* incurs liability for a ruling error only when the sin in question is one that, if committed intentionally, would render the individual liable to *kares*, and if committed inadvertently, would require a private individual to bring a *chatat*. The same limitation applies to the *korban* of the *Kohen Gadol* (*mashiach*) when he sins, and similarly to the congregational *korban* for *avodah zarah* — only the categories of *avodah zarah* that carry *kares* for intentional violation fall within this framework.

This distinction within *avodah zarah* may seem surprising. Not all forms of *avodah zarah* carry the same weight. Rashi specifies the classic *avodah zarah* acts that do incur *kares*: slaughtering to an idol (*zivuach*), burning incense (*ketores*), libation (*nissukh*), and prostration (*hishtachavayah*). But more expressive acts of devotion — embracing an idol or kissing it — fall under a *lav* rather than *kares* and do not trigger the congregational *korban* if performed inadvertently en masse. The halachic world of *chatat* is thus consistently bounded by the *kares* threshold.

### Congregation General

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, יָחִיד נָשִׂיא וּמָשִׁיחַ — מְבִיאִין שְׂעִירָה, צִבּוּר — מְבִיאִים פַּר וְשָׂעִיר, פַּר לְעוֹלָה וְשָׂעִיר לְחַטָּאת. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְנוֹדְעָה הַחַטָּאת אֲשֶׁר חָטְאוּ עָלֶיהָ״, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״עָלֶיהָ״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״עָלֶיהָ״. מָה לְהַלָּן דָּבָר שֶׁחַיָּיבִים עַל זְדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְעַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת, אַף כָּאן דָּבָר שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְעַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** if they unwittingly sinned in a matter **involving idolatrous worship,** a private **individual, a *Nasi*, and an anointed** High Priest **bring a she-goat,** whereas **a community brings a bull and a goat,** the **bull as a burnt-offering and** the **goat as a sin-offering.** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters,** that one must bring a sin-offering for this transgression, derived? The Gemara answers: **As the Sages taught** with regard to an unwitting communal sin offering: **"And the sin by which they have sinned is known"** (Leviticus 4:14). **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: It is stated here,** with regard to a sin-offering: **"By which [*aleha*]," and it is stated there,** with regard to the prohibition with regard to a wife's sister: **"Unto [*aleha*]."** This teaches by verbal analogy: **Just as there,** in the case of a wife's sister, it is **a matter that for its intentional** violation **one is liable to** receive ***karet* and for its unwitting** violation one is liable to bring **a sin-offering, so too here,** the offering is brought for **a matter that for its intentional** violation **one is liable to** receive ***karet* and for its unwitting** violation one is liable to bring **a sin-offering.**

The *pasuk* that anchors this principle for the congregation is *v'noda hachatat asher chatu aleha* (Vayikra 4), in the section discussing the inadvertent communal sin. The word *aleha* links, by *gezeirah shavah*, to the word *aleha* in the *ervah* passage, where *achos isha* carries *kares*. The result is that the congregational *chatat* obligation is established as pertaining only to sins of the *kares* category.

### Kohen Gadol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן צִבּוּר, מָשִׁיחַ מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב בְּמָשִׁיחַ: ״לְאַשְׁמַת הָעָם״, הֲרֵי מָשִׁיחַ כְּצִבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **We found** a source for the *halakha* that the offering brought by the community is a sin-offering; **from where do we** derive that **an anointed** High Priest also brings a sin-offering for this transgression? **As it is written** with regard **to an anointed** High Priest: "If the anointed priest shall sin **so as to bring guilt on the people"** (Leviticus 4:3), which indicates that **an anointed** High Priest brings an offering **like the community.**

For the *Kohen Gadol* — referred to in the *pasuk* as the *mashiach* — the derivation takes a different route. The *pasuk* states *im hakohen hamashiach yecheta le-ashmat ha-am* — if the anointed Kohen sins to the guilt of the people. The Gemara reads *le-ashmat ha-am* not merely in its plain sense (that the nation bears the consequences of the Kohen Gadol's error) but halachically: *harei mashiach k'tzibbur* — the Kohen Gadol's sin is treated as equivalent to the sin of the people. Since the congregational *chatat* is limited to *kares*-category sins (established above), the *Kohen Gadol*'s *chatat* is likewise so limited.

### Nasi & Yachid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָחִיד וְנָשִׂיא — אָתְיָא ״מִצְוֹת״ ״מִצְוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** From where is it derived that a private **individual and a *Nasi*** bring sin-offerings only for a severe transgression of this kind? **This is derived** by verbal analogy between the words **"mitzvot"** and **"mitzvot"** that are stated three times, with regard to the community's sin-offering, a private individual's sin-offering, and the sin-offering of a *Nasi*: "Any of the mitzvot of the Lord that He has commanded not to be done" (Leviticus 4:2, 13, 22).

For the *nasi* and the private individual, the derivation proceeds via *gezeirah shavah* using the word *mitzvos*. The *pasuk* concerning the *nasi* reads *asher nasi yecheta v'asa achas mikol mitzvos Hashem*, and the *pasuk* for the individual reads *v'nefesh achas techeta v'asata achas mikol mitzvos*. Both contain *mikol mitzvos*. The same phrase *v'asu achas mikol mitzvos* appears in the congregational *chatat* section in *Vayikra*. Through the verbal parallel of *mitzvos* with *mitzvos*, the *kares* requirement established for the congregation (via *aleha*) is extended to the *nasi* and the *yachid*.

A whiteboard layout helps clarify the chain: (1) the congregational *chatat* in *Vayikra* contains *aleha*, linking through the *ervah* passage to *kares*; (2) the phrase *le-ashmat ha-am* equates the *Kohen Gadol* with the congregation, extending the *kares* requirement upward to him; (3) the *gezeirah shavah* of *mitzvos*/*mitzvos* brings the *nasi* and the *yachid* into the same orbit. Then, for the congregational *korban* of *avodah zarah* in *Bamidbar* 15, the *gezeirah shavah* of *me'einei*/*me'einei* — the phrase *v'hayah im me'einei ha-eidah* in *Bamidbar* connects with *v'ne'elam davar me'einei ha-kahal* in *Vayikra* — links the *avodah zarah* case to the same *kares* standard. For the case of a private individual committing *avodah zarah* inadvertently, the *pasuk* of *v'im nefesh achat* with its linking *vav* is read as tying that individual to the congregational *avodah zarah* standard, allowing the derivation to flow downward from the congregation to individuals, or upward from the individual to the congregation, depending on the direction of reasoning required.

The overarching rule is thus established: whether the sinner is a private individual, a king, a *Kohen Gadol*, or the collective — and whether the sin involves the general corpus of *mitzvos* or the specific category of *avodah zarah* — the *chatat* obligation is uniformly limited to transgressions for which intentional violation incurs *kares*.

### Rabbanan's Torah Achas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הַאי סְבָרָא מְנָא לְהוּ? נָפְקָא לְהוּ, מִדְּמַקְרֵי לֵיהּ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לִבְרֵיהּ: ״תּוֹרָה אַחַת יִהְיֶה לָכֶם לָעוֹשֶׂה בִּשְׁגָגָה״, ״וְהַנֶּפֶשׁ אֲשֶׁר תַּעֲשֶׂה בְּיָד רָמָה״ — הוּקְּשָׁה כָּל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה: מָה עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — דָּבָר שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְעַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת, אַף כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְעַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **the Rabbis,** who interpret "with her" with regard to rival wives, **from where do they derive this conclusion** that one is liable to bring the offering for idolatrous worship only for a transgression whose intentional violation incurs *karet*? The Gemara answers: **They derive it from that which Rabbi Yehoshua** ben Levi **read out to his son** to teach him the verses that summarize the case of unwitting idol worship: **"You shall have one law [*Torah*] for him that does unwittingly. But the soul that does with a high hand,** whether he be home born or a stranger, he blasphemes the Lord; and that soul shall be cut off from among his people" (Numbers 15:29–30). In this manner, **the entire Torah is juxtaposed with idolatrous worship. Just as** in the case of **idolatrous worship** there is no obligation to bring an offering unless it is **a matter that for its intentional** violation **one is liable to** receive ***karet* and for its unwitting** violation one is liable to bring **a sin-offering,** as indicated by the verse: "That soul shall be cut off from among his people," **so too,** the same applies to **any matter that for its intentional** violation **one is liable to** receive ***karet* and for its unwitting** violation one is liable to bring **a sin-offering.**

Now the Gemara asks: the Rabbanan use *aleha* for *tzarat ervah* in the *yibum* context — so where do they derive the principle that the *chatat* is limited to *kares*-category sins? Their source is a *pasuk* that Rav Yehoshua read to his son, from *Bamidbar* 15: *torah achas yihyeh lachem l'oseh bishgagah* — one law shall apply to all who sin inadvertently. What immediately follows in the *pasuk* is *v'hanefesh asher ta'aseh b'yad ramah... venikratah* — the soul who acts with a raised hand is cut off. Some of *Chazal* read *megadef* in that verse as a reference to *avodah zarah*, making the sequence: one law for inadvertent sin, followed by *kares* for intentional *avodah zarah*. The Gemara draws the inference: *hukshah kol hatorah kulah l'avodat kochavim* — the entire Torah is compared to *avodah zarah*. Just as *avodah zarah* is a sin for which intentional violation incurs *kares* and inadvertent violation requires a *chatat*, so too all the sins for which a *chatat* is required must share that characteristic.

The limitation of *torah achas*, however, is that it is written *l'oseh bishgagah* — for the individual sinner. It therefore establishes the rule for all individual categories: *yachid*, *nasi*, and *mashiach*. It does not, however, address the congregational *chatat* cases. For the congregation's *chatat* in general *mitzvos*, the Rabbanan still require the *me'einei*/*me'einei* link; and the direction of derivation between the *avodah zarah* congregation and the general congregation can run in both directions using the *vav* connecting the cases.

## Rabbi's Torah Achas

### Same Korban Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיפוֹךְ אֲנָא: בִּמְקוֹם מִצְוָה — הִיא אֲסוּרָה וְצָרָתָהּ מוּתֶּרֶת, וְשֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹם מִצְוָה — שְׁתֵּיהֶן אֲסוּרוֹת. וְרַבִּי, הַאי ״תּוֹרָה אַחַת״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: לְפִי שֶׁמָּצִינוּ שֶׁחִלֵּק הַכָּתוּב בֵּין יְחִידִים לִמְרוּבִּים, יְחִידִים בִּסְקִילָה — לְפִיכָךְ מָמוֹנָן פָּלֵט, מְרוּבִּין בְּסַיִיף — לְפִיכָךְ מָמוֹנָן אָבֵד. יָכוֹל נַחְלוֹק בְּקׇרְבְּנוֹתֵיהֶן — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תּוֹרָה אַחַת יִהְיֶה לָכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **I** can **reverse this** and suggest the opposite. **In a place** where there is **a mitzva,** the forbidden relation **is prohibited and her rival wife is permitted, and in a place** where there is **no mitzva, both of them are prohibited.** And with regard to **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: **And what does** he **do with this** verse: **"One law"?** The Gemara answers: **He requires it for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **Since we find that the Torah differentiates between** private **individuals** who sinned **and the many** who sinned in idol worship. Private **individuals** who worshipped idols are sentenced **to stoning,** and **therefore their money is spared** from destruction. Although the sinner is put to death, his possessions are not destroyed. By contrast, the **many** are condemned to the lighter death penalty of decapitation **by sword,** the punishment for those who worshipped idols in an idolatrous city, and **therefore their money is forfeited,** as by Torah law all possessions belonging to the residents of an idolatrous city must be burned. Due to this difference, one **might** have thought that **we** should likewise **differentiate between their offerings,** i.e., if an individual and a community unwittingly practiced idolatrous worship, different offerings are brought in the two cases. Therefore, **the verse states: "One law for you."** The word: "you" is in the plural, which indicates that an individual and a community that worshipped idols are subject to the same *halakha*.

If the Rabbanan use *torah achas* to establish the *kares* threshold for individuals, what does Rabbi do with *torah achas* — since he uses *aleha* for the *kares* principle and has no need for *torah achas* for that purpose? Rabbi applies *torah achas* to a different *halacha* taught in a *braisa*: since we find in the Torah itself that individuals and a community are treated differently for *avodah zarah* — individuals receive *skilah* and their property is not destroyed, while a community that collectively worships *avodah zarah* is treated like an *ir hanidachat* and sentenced to death by sword with their property destroyed — one might have thought that this distinction should carry over into their respective *korbanot* as well, such that a city that performed *avodah zarah bishogeg* would bring a different *korban* than an individual who did so. *Torah achas yihyeh lachem* comes to teach that in this respect there is no distinction — one law governs all, the same *korban* applies regardless of whether the transgressor was a single person or an entire city acting collectively without a *beis din* ruling.

### Chilkiya Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב חִלְקִיָּה מֵהַגְרוֹנְיָא: טַעְמָא דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״תּוֹרָה אַחַת יִהְיֶה לָכֶם״, הָא לָאו הָכִי, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא נַחְלוֹק. מַאי לַיְיתוֹ? לַיְיתוֹ פַּר — צִבּוּר בִּשְׁאָר מִצְוֹת הוּא דְּמַיְיתוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥilkiya from** the city of **Hagronya strongly objects to this: The reason** for this ruling is **that the Merciful One** specifically **writes: "One law for you,"** from which it may be inferred that **if that were not so, I would say** that **we** should **differentiate** between the offerings of an individual and a community. If so, it is appropriate to ask: **What** offering **would they bring** if we were to differentiate? You cannot say **they would bring a bull,** as **it is a community** that sinned unwittingly **in** transgressing **other mitzvot who bring** a bull, and as this case is written separately, clearly its *halakha* is different.

Rebbi Chilkiya of Hagronya pressed this reading: if not for *torah achas*, what *korban* did you think such a collective would bring that differs from what an individual brings? The question is pointed — every available animal was already assigned to a specific *korban* category.

### Impossible Combinations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּשְׂבָּה — יָחִיד בִּשְׁאָר מִצְוֹת הוּא דְּמַיְיתֵי, שְׂעִיר — נָשִׂיא בִּשְׁאָר מִצְוֹת הוּא דְּמַיְיתֵי. פַּר לְעוֹלָה וְשָׂעִיר לְחַטָּאת — צִבּוּר בָּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הוּא דְּמַיְיתוּ, וְאֶלָּא מַאי, שְׂעִירָה — יָחִיד נָמֵי הַיְינוּ קׇרְבָּנוֹ! אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וְצִבּוּר בְּהוֹרָאָה מַיְיתוּ פַּר לְעוֹלָה וְשָׂעִיר לְחַטָּאת, אִינְהוּ נָמֵי נַיְתוֹ אִיפְּכָא. אִי נָמֵי, צָרִיךְ וְאֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, you cannot say that they should bring **a ewe,** as **it is an individual** who sinned **in** transgressing **the other mitzvot who brings** a ewe. Nor can you say that they bring a **goat,** as **it is a *Nasi*** who sinned **in** transgressing **the other mitzvot who brings** a goat. And you cannot say that they would bring **a bull as a burnt-offering and a goat as a sin-offering,** as **it is the community,** i.e., the majority of Jews, who sinned **in** performing **idolatrous worship who bring** these. **Rather, what** might they bring? If one were to suggest **a she-goat,** that would not be appropriate, because **this is the offering** of a private **individual as well,** since an individual brings a ewe or a she-goat as a sin-offering. Apparently there is no need for a special inference in this case, as there are no other possibilities. It **is necessary** for a verse to teach that there is no difference between the offerings of an individual and a community, as it might **enter your mind to say: Since a community** that sinned **by** the erroneous **instruction** of the court that it is permitted to practice idolatry **brings a bull as a burnt-offering and a goat as a sin-offering,** then, when a community sins of its own accord **they too** should **bring** these animals, but **the opposite,** i.e., a bull as a sin-offering and a goat as a burnt-offering. **Alternatively,** it could be argued: Theoretically a community **must** bring a different form of atonement from that of an individual, yet **they have no remedy,** as the Torah did not specify which offering is brought for a sin of the many. Therefore, the verse **teaches us** by means of the phrase "one law for you" that there is indeed an offering for the many who practiced idol worship.

The Gemara's response is instructive. Going through the possibilities: a bull (*par*) is already what the congregation brings for a general *chatat*; a female sheep (*kisbah*) is what a private individual brings; a male goat (*sa'ir*) is the *nasi*'s *korban*; a bull for *olah* and male goat for *chatat* together is the congregational *korban* for *avodah zarah*; a female goat (*se'irah*) coincides with what an individual brings for *avodah zarah*. Every available *korban* combination is already occupied. *Tosafos* note that the Gemara did not even propose a male sheep or ram — and the question of why not is itself left unresolved. The upshot is: without *torah achas*, one would have been forced to conclude either that the city brings an unprecedented *korban* combination — such as the reverse of the congregational *avodah zarah* offering, a *se'ir* for *olah* and *par* for *chatat* — or that the obligation exists in principle but has no possible fulfillment. *Torah achas* resolves this by teaching that the *korban* of a city sinning collectively mirrors that of an individual exactly, with no distinction.

## Missing Mother

### Levi's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ לֵוִי לְרַבִּי: מַאי אִירְיָא דְּתָנֵי חֲמֵשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה? לִיתְנֵי שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כִּמְדוּמֶּה לִי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ מוֹחַ בְּקׇדְקֳדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § After discussing different interpretations of the relevant verses, the Gemara addresses the details of the *halakhot* of the mishna. It first considers the numbers that comprise the structure of the mishna as a whole. The Sage **Levi said to Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: **Why does** the *tanna* **specifically teach** the cases of **fifteen women? Let him teach** the cases of **sixteen** women, including the case of a rival wife of a forbidden relation. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him:** Judging by his question, **it seems to me that** this Sage **has no brain in his head.**

With the derivations of *tzarat ervah* and the *chatat* threshold fully worked out — a discussion that has spanned considerable ground — the Gemara returns to the *mishnah* and raises a fresh question. Levi said to Rabbi: *mai irya d'tani chamesh esreh* — why does the *mishnah* list specifically fifteen women? It should list sixteen — *lisni shesh esreh*. Someone is conspicuously absent from the list: *imo*, his mother. How could a brother be married to the other brother's mother? Through a half-brother on the father's side, whose mother was not the deceased brother's wife but merely a woman with whom the father had relations. That scenario does not fall under *eshet av* — the father's wife — and might therefore seem to be a case worth listing.

### Anusat Aviv Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי דַּעְתָּךְ, אִמּוֹ אֲנוּסַת אָבִיו? אִמּוֹ אֲנוּסַת אָבִיו — פְּלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבָּנַן הִיא, וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא לָא קָא מַיְירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi proceeded to explain the question and the answer himself: **What is your thinking?** Is it that the *tanna* should have included the case of **his mother** who had been **raped by his father?** In the opinion of the Rabbis, a woman raped by one's father is permitted in marriage to another of his sons. Consequently, it is possible that she might give birth to a son and later marry one of the other sons of the rapist. If her husband subsequently died childless, this woman would come before the brother of the deceased for levirate marriage, who is in this case her son. If so, there are in fact sixteen women with whom relations are forbidden. However, continued Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, this is incorrect, as the case of **his mother raped by his father is** a matter of **dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis.** According to Rabbi Yehuda, a woman raped by one's father could never come before him for levirate marriage, as it is prohibited for her to marry his brothers. **And** the *tanna* of this mishna **does not deal with disputed** cases. The mishna enumerates only those cases that are accepted by all opinions, not those that are a matter of dispute.

Rabbi's response was pointed: *kimdumeh li she-ein lo moach b'kadkodo* — it seems to me you have no brain in your head. The bluntness of the rebuke is notable, particularly since the question is not unreasonable. The substantive answer is this: the case in question — a mother who is *anusat aviv*, a woman the father had sex with outside of marriage — is a matter of *machlokes* between Rabbi Yehuda and the *Rabbanan*. Rabbi Yehuda holds that a man is forbidden not only to marry his father's wife but also to marry any woman his father had sexual relations with, even without marriage. According to Rabbi Yehuda, the scenario where a brother could be married to the other brother's mother through an *anusat av* relationship simply never arises — she would always be forbidden to that son under Rabbi Yehuda's ruling. Since the question is contested, it does not belong in the *mishnah*'s list of unambiguous cases.

An aside on the formulation *anusat aviv*: it is worth noting the discomfort in using the language of rape to describe a scenario that could equally be described as *mefutah* — a seduced or consenting woman. The apparent reason the *mishnah* prefers *anusat aviv* may be precisely to absolve the woman of any transgression, specifically the possible *lav* of *lo tihyeh kedeshah*. If the father raped her, she bears no legal responsibility. Whether that *lav* would even apply to a consenting relationship is itself a major *machlokes rishonim* — the *Rambam* considers such a prohibition to apply — but by framing the case as *anusat*, only the father's sin is in play.

### Unanimous Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא? וַהֲרֵי אִיסּוּר מִצְוָה וְאִיסּוּר קְדוּשָּׁה, דִּפְלִיגִי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְרַבָּנַן — וְקָתָנֵי! בְּפִרְקִין קָא אָמְרִינַן. וְהָא בֵּית שַׁמַּאי מַתִּירִין אֶת הַצָּרוֹת לָאַחִין, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹסְרִין! בֵּית שַׁמַּאי בִּמְקוֹם בֵּית הִלֵּל אֵינָהּ מִשְׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** does the *tanna* really **not** mention disputed cases? **But** there are the cases of **a prohibition** resulting from **a mitzva** of the Sages, **and a prohibition** stemming from **sanctity.** In other words, the *tanna* does refer to the cases of a woman who one is prohibited to marry by decree of the Sages, e.g., a distant secondary forbidden relation, and one who is prohibited due to a regular prohibition that preserves the sanctity of the Jewish people or the priesthood. These are cases concerning **which Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbis disagree** whether these women are entirely exempt from both levirate marriage and *ḥalitza* like a forbidden relation, or whether the mitzva of levirate marriage does in fact apply but since the *yavam* may not marry her in practice, she must be released by *ḥalitza*. **And** yet these disputed cases **are taught** in the mishna. The Gemara responds: When **we say** that only accepted *halakhot* are included in the mishna, we are referring only to **our** present **chapter,** whereas the dispute between Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbis is stated in the second chapter. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** in this chapter it is stated that **Beit Shammai permit rival wives to the brothers,** as they hold that there is no prohibition against rival wives of those with whom relations are forbidden, **and Beit Hillel prohibit** this. Evidently, the first chapter does deal with disputed cases. The Gemara answers: When **Beit Shammai** express an opinion **where Beit Hillel** disagree, their opinion is considered as if it were **not** in the **mishna,** i.e., it is completely disregarded, as it is commonly accepted that the opinion of Beit Shammai is rejected and not ruled as *halakha*. Therefore, this cannot be considered a true dispute.

The Gemara formalizes the principle underlying Rabbi's response: the *mishnah* in this first *perek* deals exclusively with positions that are unanimous — *lo ka-mairi ela be-pirkin*, we are speaking within our chapter. The later chapters of *Yevamos* do contain *mishnayos* that record debates, such as the dispute between Rabbi Akiva and the *Rabbanan* concerning whether women forbidden by a *lav* are treated the same as those forbidden by *kares* for purposes of *yibum*. Rabbi Akiva takes the stringent view that an *issur lav* has the same exempting force as an *issur kares*, and further that offspring of an *issur lav* union are *mamzerim* — a position that is not the accepted ruling. The cases of *issur kedushah* — such as where the deceased brother was married to a *mamzeres*, where *kiddushin* is technically binding but the marriage was transgressive — similarly generate their own category: one must perform *chalitza* but not *yibum*, since the *yibum* cannot be halachically executed despite the underlying *zika* still creating an obligation. These cases involve genuine dispute and therefore fall outside the scope of the *mishnah*'s opening list.

### Beis Shammai Noted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בְּעוֹלָמוֹ, דִּפְלִיגִי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וְרַבָּנַן — וְקָתָנֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a further difficulty: **But** there is the case of **a wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist,** This is a case concerning **which Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis disagree, and** yet it **is taught.**

The Gemara raises one final issue: the *mishnah* framed its entire list as women who exempt their *tzaros* from *yibum* and *chalitza*. But this presupposes that the category of *tzarat ervah* is halachically operative — precisely what *Beis Shammai* denies. *Beis Shammai* holds that *tzaros* remain fully subject to *yibum* or *chalitza*; they recognize no category that exempts a *tzara* by virtue of her co-wife being an *ervah*. If so, the entire framework of the *mishnah* — which presupposes the *halacha* of *tzarat ervah* — is not a universally held position. How then can the *mishnah* claim to be dealing only with unanimous rulings?

The Gemara answers: *Beis Shammai bi-makom Beis Hillel einah mishnah* — where *Beis Hillel* rules, *Beis Shammai* does not count as an independent position for purposes of establishing the *mishnah*'s baseline. The list is a *Beis Hillel* position, and *Beis Hillel*'s rulings are treated as the operative consensus. With that, the *mishnah* is vindicated as a statement of unanimous *halacha* — within the bounds of *Beis Hillel* — and the *machlokes* of *anusat aviv* explains why mother is absent from the list.
