# Yevamot 106 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Private Chalitza Story

### Mishna Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁחָלְצוּ בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִין, וּבָא מַעֲשֶׂה לִפְנֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְהִכְשִׁירָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A story is told** about **an incident** in which a couple **once performed *ḥalitza*** between themselves in private while alone in prison, and the case later came before Rabbi Akiva and he validated it.

The daf opens at the bottom of 105b, with the conclusion of the *mishna*'s treatment of *chalitza* performed outside the formal framework of a *beit din*. The *mishna* had cited a range of views regarding the minimum requirements for a valid *chalitza*, culminating in the opinion that perhaps even two witnesses — or fewer — suffice. Rabbi Shimon ben Yochanan HaSandlar is among those who held that a *beit din* is not strictly necessary. The practical ruling, however, is that *bedi'avad* one does need a *beit din*, and *lechatchila* three judges is sufficient — indeed, even *lechatchila* one need not convene five, though the *Gemara* from the previous daf established that doing it with five is preferable for the sake of greater publicization.

### Proof of Event

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ מִי יָדַעְנָא?
>
> **Translation:** How can we know what happened between him and her?

The *mishna* then records a story: *ma'aseh b'echad she'chalatz beino l'veinah b'veit ha'asurim* — a man performed *chalitza* with his *yevama* privately, in prison, and the matter came before Rabbi Akiva, who declared it *kasher*. On this the *Gemara* asks: *beino l'veinah mi yadeinan* — if the act was entirely private, how do we know it occurred? Even if the parties themselves report it, on what basis can a *beit din* rule accordingly?

### Eidim Mibachutz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: וְעֵדִים רוֹאִין אוֹתוֹ מִבַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: And** the *ḥalitza* was validated because **there were witnesses who saw them from outside** the prison, who testified that the *ḥalitza* was performed properly.

Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel answers: *b'eidim ra'uhu she-tzofin mibachutz* — there were witnesses who observed the proceedings from the outside. They were not themselves a *beit din* and were not physically present as part of the ceremony, but they provided sufficient evidence that the event had in fact taken place.

### Types of Witnesses

This raises a deeper distinction. Witnesses in halachic contexts serve two distinct functions. *Eidei kiyum* are witnesses whose presence actually effectuates and validates the legal act — as in *kiddushin*, where even certainty that the act occurred is insufficient without witnesses who legally constitute the act. *Eidei birur*, by contrast, serve only the evidentiary function of establishing that something happened. Here, the witnesses observing from outside operated only as *eidei birur*. They were certainly not a *beit din*, and likely did not even fulfill the role of *eidei kiyum* for the *chalitza* itself. In principle the act was done entirely privately; there is simply sufficient evidence that it occurred.

### Location Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁחָלְצוּ בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ אַבָּרַאי, וּבָא מַעֲשֶׂה לִפְנֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִין, אוֹ דִלְמָא: מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁחָלְצוּ בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִין? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִין הָיָה מַעֲשֶׂה, וּלְבֵית הָאֲסוּרִין בָּא מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the students in the house of study with regard to the incident recorded in the mishna in which a private *ḥalitza* performed in a prison was validated: **Did the incident in which they performed *ḥalitza* between him and her** privately actually **take place outside** in a different locale, **and** the reference to prison is that **the case came before Rabbi Akiva** when he was confined **in prison? Or, perhaps the incident when they performed *ḥalitza* between him and her took place in prison,** and then this case came before Rabbi Akiva? **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: The** *ḥalitza* **incident took place in prison, and** also **the case came** to Rabbi Akiva when he was **in prison.**

A further question is then raised: when the *mishna* says the story occurred in *beit ha'asurim* and came before Rabbi Akiva — who exactly was in prison? Did the *chalitza* take place outside, with the case subsequently brought to Rabbi Akiva who was imprisoned by the Romans? Or did the *chalitza* itself occur inside the prison, and was then reported to Rabbi Akiva who was elsewhere? Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav resolves it: *b'veit ha'asurim haya ma'aseh ul'veit ha'asurim ba ma'aseh* — the event happened in prison, and it was brought to Rabbi Akiva in prison as well.

### Tosafot Analysis

Tosafot notes that this question is somewhat puzzling, since the *mishna*'s own language appears unambiguous. The text reads: *ma'aseh b'echad she'chalatz beino l'veinah b'veit ha'asurim u'va ma'aseh lifnei Rabbi Akiva v'hichshir* — clearly placing the event in prison and the reporting to Rabbi Akiva thereafter. Tosafot explains that the ambiguity only arises if one removes the *vav* of *u'va ma'aseh*, yielding a reading where *b'veit ha'asurim* modifies the arrival before Rabbi Akiva rather than the event itself — though even that reading is a stretch. The reason Tosafot pursues this is that the *Yerushalmi* preserves an explicit tradition that this question was brought to Rabbi Akiva while he was incarcerated, and the *Gemara* Bavli is attempting to read that tradition into the *mishna* as well.

### Secret Message

Tosafot cites the *Yerushalmi*'s colorful account of how the question reached Rabbi Akiva in prison. Yochanan HaSandlar — himself mentioned in the *mishna* as one who ruled that a *beit din* is not required — passed by the prison of Rabbi Akiva. Posing as a peddler, he called out: *man ba'ei nach'tin, man ba'ei tzinon* — who needs needles, who needs pins — as cover for delivering a covert halachic message. Slipped into his peddler's cry was the question: *chalitza beno uveinah, mahu* — what is the law if *chalitza* was performed privately? Rabbi Akiva, understanding, thrust his head through the prison bars and replied in kind, using the word *kushin* — some type of pin or implement — whose sound evokes the word *kasher*: it is *kasher*.

## Mutat & Muafit

### Chalitza Mutat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חֲלִיצָה מוּטְעֵת — כְּשֵׁירָה. אֵי זוֹ הִיא חֲלִיצָה מוּטְעֵת?
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: A mistaken *ḥalitza* is valid.** The Gemara asks: **What constitutes a mistaken *ḥalitza*?**

The *Gemara* moves to the top of 106a with a *baraita*: *chalitza mutat keshera* — a *chalitza* performed under deception or trickery is valid. The *baraita* then asks: what constitutes *chalitza mutat*?

### Reish Lakish's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כֹּל שֶׁאוֹמְרִים לוֹ: חֲלוֹץ, וּבְכָךְ אַתָּה כּוֹנְסָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish said: Any** case **in which they say** to a *yavam* who is not well versed in *halakha*: **Let her remove** your shoe, **and in doing so you will take her in** marriage, i.e., the *yavam* understands that by allowing *ḥalitza* he will actually be marrying her. Although he actually intended to marry her, having allowed her to remove his shoe validates the *ḥalitza*.

Reish Lakish defines it as a case where the *yavam* wished to perform *yibum* but was told: *chalot uv'chach konesah* — go through this *chalitza* ceremony and through it you will acquire her as your wife. In other words, he was misled into believing that the *chalitza* ceremony was itself a form of *yibum*. Since he may have been unaware of what the ceremony entailed, and since *bedi'avad* the recitation of the verses is not indispensable, the *chalitza* might be considered valid despite his misunderstanding.

### Rabbi Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אֲנִי שׁוֹנֶה: בֵּין שֶׁנִּתְכַּוֵּון הוּא וְלֹא נִתְכַּוְּונָה הִיא, בֵּין שֶׁנִּתְכַּוְּונָה הִיא וְלֹא נִתְכַּוֵּון הוּא — חֲלִיצָתָהּ פְּסוּלָה, עַד שֶׁיִּתְכַּוְּונוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם כְּאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to him: I teach that whether** in a case where **he had intended** to perform valid *ḥalitza* **and she did not intend, or whether she had intended and he did not intend, the *ḥalitza* is invalid, unless they both intend together as one** to perform a proper *ḥalitza* that would permit her to marry others.

Rabbi Yochanan objects sharply. He cites a teaching: whether he had intent and she did not, or she had intent and he did not, or neither of them had intent — *chalitzatah pesulah*. Both parties must intend for the act to take effect as a *chalitza*. How then can Reish Lakish say this is valid? If the man believed he was performing an act of *yibum*, his *kavanah* was for its very opposite — he cannot be said to have intended an effective *chalitza*.

### Money Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כֹּל שֶׁאוֹמְרִים לוֹ: ״חֲלוֹץ לָהּ עַל מְנָת שֶׁתִּתֵּן לְךָ מָאתַיִם זוּז״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** a mistaken *ḥalitza* that is valid refers to **any** case **in which they say to him: Let her perform *ḥalitza* on you,** with the intention of releasing her bond, **on the condition that she will give you two hundred dinars** afterward, and even if she does not give him the money the *ḥalitza* is valid, as the stipulated condition is not binding.

Rabbi Yochanan therefore reframes the definition of valid *chalitza mutat*: *kol she'omrim lo chalot lamah she'titein lach matayim zuz* — they tell him to perform *chalitza* and in exchange she will give him two hundred *zuz*, but she never actually intends to pay, and in the end does not. In this case, the man fully understood he was performing *chalitza*; he simply expected compensation that never materialized. Since he intended the act as *chalitza*, it is valid — the unkept promise is the *mutat*, the deception, but it does not undermine the *chalitza* itself.

### Woman's Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּאִשָּׁה אַחַת שֶׁנָּפְלָה לִפְנֵי יָבָם שֶׁאֵין הָגוּן לָהּ, וְאָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״חֲלוֹץ לָהּ עַל מְנָת שֶׁתִּתֵּן לְךָ מָאתַיִם זוּז״, וּבָא מַעֲשֶׂה לִפְנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא וְהִכְשִׁירָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And an incident** occurred **involving a certain woman, who happened before her *yavam*** for levirate marriage, **yet he was not suitable for her, and they,** the judges, **said to him: Let her perform *ḥalitza* on the condition that she will give you two hundred dinars.** Afterward, when she did not pay, **the incident came before Rabbi Ḥiyya and he validated that** *ḥalitza*.

A corroborating *baraita* is then cited. *Ma'aseh b'isha achas she'nafelah lifnei yavam she'einah haguna lo* — a woman fell before a *yavam* who was not appropriate for her. They told him: perform *chalitza* and she will give you two hundred *zuz*. The case came before Rabbi Akiva, who validated it even though the money was never paid.

### Conditions Logic

This raises an obvious question: if the *chalitza* was made *al menat* — on the condition that she pay him two hundred *zuz* — why is the *chalitza* valid when the condition was not fulfilled? A condition that is not met should render the act void. The answer lies in a principle cited by Rashi from the *Gemara* in Ketubot: any act that cannot be delegated to a *shaliach* cannot be made conditional. Agency is possible only where a person holds the legal power to effect a transaction and can therefore transfer that power. Where agency is impossible, conditions too cannot bind.

### Rav Chaim

This leads to a fundamental conceptual point developed by Rav Chaim. He asks why it is that *chalitza* performed by a minor woman is, according to a serious opinion, nonetheless valid, when ordinarily a minor cannot perform legally binding acts. His answer addresses the very structure of *chalitza* and *yibum*. The *Torah* does not invest the *yavam* or the *yevama* with power to effect a change of status. Rather, the *Torah* itself severs or creates the bond when the prescribed ritual is performed. The parties are not empowered actors — they are participants in a ritual whose legal consequence the *Torah* draws automatically. This is why *yibum* can take effect even through an inadvertent act of intercourse: the *Torah* creates the bond upon the act occurring, regardless of *da'at*. Similarly, *chalitza* does not require the parties to "do" something in the sense of exercising legal capacity — they go through the motions and the *Torah* effects the change.

This understanding explains why agency is impossible: you can appoint a *shaliach* to exercise a power you hold, but if you hold no power — if it is the *Torah* that does the effecting — there is nothing to transfer. And since conditions are structurally linked to agency, the same reasoning applies: you cannot condition whether the *Torah* will or will not sever a bond upon the ritual's completion. Therefore, even if the money is not paid, the *chalitza* is fully valid.

## Rabbi Chiya Case

### Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא דַּאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא. אֲמַר לַהּ: בִּתִּי, עֲמוֹדִי! אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: אֵימָא יְשִׁיבָתָהּ זוֹ הִיא עֲמִידָתָהּ. אֲמַר לַהּ: יָדְעַתְּ לֵיהּ? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: אִין, מָמוֹנָא הוּא דַּחֲזָא לַהּ וְקָבְעֵי לְמֵיכְלֵיהּ מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **One man came before Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba** with his *yevama* in order to have the court convince her to perform a levirate marriage. Rabbi Ḥiyya **said to her: My daughter, stand up,** for we are beginning to discuss your case now, and the participants must stand. **She said to him: Say that her sitting,** referring to her desire to remain seated as an act of refusal of even contemplating the possibility of performing levirate marriage, **is** therefore **tantamount to her standing,** as levirate marriage is not an option for her. In other words, the option that will enable her to remain standing proud in the future is not to enter into levirate marriage with this man. Rabbi Ḥiyya **said to her:** Are you acquainted with this *yavam* and **do you know him** well enough to know why he wants to perform levirate marriage with you although you are not interested? **She said to him: Yes, it is money that he saw in her,** a euphemism for herself, **and he wants to consume** it by taking **it from her,** and therefore he wishes to enter levirate marriage.

The *Gemara* now introduces a case involving Rabbi Chiya bar Abba. A woman had fallen before a *yavam* who was inappropriate for her. Her mother indicated to Rabbi Chiya bar Abba that the man was interested not in the woman but in her money — he intended to perform *yibum*, take her assets, and then divorce her.

### First Trick

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לוֹ: חֲלוֹץ לָהּ, וּבְכָךְ אַתָּה כּוֹנְסָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **He said** to the *yavam*: **Let her remove** your shoe, **and in doing so you will take her in** marriage, for he wanted to mislead him into allowing *ḥalitza*, which would disqualify a subsequent levirate marriage between them.

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba attempted to induce the *yavam* to perform *chalitza* by telling him: *chalot lah betachat hakonsa* — perform *chalitza* and through it you will be married to her. This is precisely the maneuver that Reish Lakish had proposed and that Rabbi Yochanan had rejected.

### Invalid Chalitza

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְבָתַר דַּחֲלַץ לַהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַשְׁתָּא, מִינָּךְ אִפַּסְלָא לַהּ, חֲלוֹץ לַהּ חֲלִיצָה מְעַלַּיְיתָא, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּתִישְׁתְּרֵי לְעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **After he allowed her to perform *ḥalitza*,** Rabbi Ḥiyya **said** to the *yavam*: **Now, she is disqualified for you** forever, since you allowed her to perform *ḥalitza*. Although you thought it was an act of marriage, she is no longer permitted to marry you, so you have nothing to lose if you permit her to marry others. Therefore, allow her **to perform valid proper *ḥalitza*, so she will be permitted to others.** By performing a second *ḥalitza*, even Rabbi Yoḥanan, who disqualified this form of a mistaken *ḥalitza*, would have no problem permitting her to remarry based on the second *ḥalitza*.

Indeed, the first *chalitza* was not a valid one, since the man's intent was for *yibum*, not *chalitza*. However, Rabbi Chiya bar Abba then confronted him after the fact: while the *chalitza* did not achieve what you thought it would — it does not make her your wife — it nonetheless constitutes a *chalitza pesulah*, a defective *chalitza*. And the *halacha* is that after a *chalitza pesulah*, *yibum* is no longer permitted. Since *yibum* is now foreclosed in any case, Rabbi Chiya bar Abba urged him: *chalotz lah chalitza ma'alyasa* — you might as well now perform a proper *chalitza* to free her entirely. Of course, this placed him in a precarious situation, since the man, feeling deceived, might well refuse the second *chalitza* out of spite.

## Rav Papa Case

### Money-Seeker

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּת חֲמוּהּ, דְּרַב פָּפָּא נָפְלָה לִפְנֵי יָבָם שֶׁאֵין הָגוּן לָהּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** It is told: **The daughter of Rav Pappa's father-in-law,** i.e., his sister-in-law, **happened before her *yavam*** for levirate marriage, **yet he was not suitable for her,** although he wished to perform levirate marriage. The case **came before Abaye.**

A parallel case is then introduced. The daughter of the father-in-law of Rav Papa — apparently a sister-in-law or close female relative — fell before a *yavam* who was not suitable for her. His motivation, too, was purely financial: he was interested only in consuming her money. Rav Papa, concerned for her welfare, came before Abaye.

## Rabbi Chiya Case…

### Money Trick

This section continues the narrative of Rabbi Chiya bar Abba with an explanation of the preferable resolution — one that will become directly relevant to the Rav Papa case. Rather than relying on the trickery of making the *yavam* believe the *chalitza* is a form of *yibum*, the better approach is to promise the *yavam* money as inducement for the *chalitza*, without intending to pay. Since, as established above, conditions cannot attach to *chalitza*, the *chalitza* is valid regardless of whether the money is delivered.

## Rav Papa Case…

### Abaye's Trick

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: חֲלוֹץ לָהּ, וּבְכָךְ אַתָּה כּוֹנְסָהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא: לָא סָבַר לַהּ מָר לְהָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן?
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to** the *yavam*: **Let her remove** your shoe, **and in doing so you will take her in** marriage. **Rav Pappa said to him: Does the Master,** i.e., do you, **not accept what Rabbi Yoḥanan said,** that this type of *ḥalitza* does not work at all?

Abaye, addressing the *yavam* in the Rav Papa case, initially proposed the same approach that Rabbi Chiya bar Abba had tried: *chalot lah betachat hakonsa* — perform *chalitza* and you will thereby be married to her. Rav Papa immediately challenged Abaye: does the master not hold the ruling of Rabbi Yochanan, that this form of deception invalidates the *chalitza*?

### Rav Papa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְאֶלָּא הֵיכִי אֵימָא לֵיהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חֲלוֹץ לָהּ עַל מְנָת שֶׁתִּתֵּן לְךָ מָאתַיִם זוּז. לְבָתַר דַּחֲלַץ לַהּ, אֲמַר לַהּ: זִיל הַב לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to** Abaye that he should say to him as Rabbi Yoḥanan himself suggested: **Let her perform *ḥalitza* on the condition that she will give you two hundred dinars.** Convince him to allow *ḥalitza* on the basis that he will profit financially from it. Abaye told the *yavam* to do so and he did. **After he let her perform *ḥalitza*,** Abaye **said to** Rav Pappa's sister-in-law: **Go give him** the money, for you have agreed to give him two hundred dinars.

Abaye conceded and asked: so what should I tell him? Rav Papa replied: tell him *chalot lah al menat she'titein lach matayim zuz* — perform *chalitza* and she will give you two hundred *zuz*. This is the valid form of *chalitza mutat*. The *yavam* proceeded to perform *chalitza*.

### Payment Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מְשַׁטָּה אֲנִי בָּךְ עֲבַדָה לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa **said** to Abaye on her behalf that a case of: **I was fooling you,** was what **she did to him.** She never seriously intended to give him the money when accepting his stipulated condition, and even though the *ḥalitza* is valid one cannot force her to pay.

After the *chalitza* was completed, Abaye told the woman she should pay the man the two hundred *zuz* — not because the *chalitza* required it to be valid, but because she had made a promise, and one ought to honor one's word. Rav Papa objected: she can say *meshata ni bach* — I was only joking with you, I never really intended to pay.

Here the *Gemara* enters territory of *Choshen Mishpat* rather than *Even HaEzer*. Even granting that the *chalitza* is valid regardless, why should she be free of the monetary obligation? Rashi notes the distinction between a gratuitous promise — which is not legally binding — and *sechirus*, a fee for services rendered. If she agreed to pay him two hundred *zuz* for performing *chalitza*, that looks like a contracted fee, and ordinarily one is bound by a contracted fee.

### River Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי לָא תַּנְיָא: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיָה בּוֹרֵחַ מִבֵּית הָאֲסוּרִין, וְהָיְתָה מַעְבּוֹרֶת לְפָנָיו, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: טוֹל דִּינָר וְהַעֲבִירֵנִי — אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא שְׂכָרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **One who was running away from prison** and came **upon a ferry. He said to** the ferry man: **Take a dinar,** i.e., he offered to pay an amount much larger than the standard fee, **and take me across** the river. Despite the escapee's commitment, it is ruled in the *baraita* that the ferryman **receives nothing other than his usual rate,** as the escapee is legally exempt from paying the higher amount he had agreed to pay.

Rav Papa's answer draws on a *baraita*: a person fleeing from prison came to a river crossing and said to the boatman, *tol dinar v'ha'avireini* — take a *dinar* and ferry me across. He is obligated only to pay the normal going rate, not the inflated sum — because the boatman took advantage of the man's desperate circumstances, and in such situations the claim of *meshata ni bach* is available: the agreed price is not binding beyond what the service actually warrants. *Chalitza* is a *mitzvah*, not a commercial transaction. No one is normally paid for performing it. Any agreement to pay is therefore not a genuine commercial arrangement, and the woman may invoke *meshata ni bach* to be released from the obligation.

### Death Curse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲבוּךְ הֵיכָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּמָתָא. אִימָּךְ הֵיכָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּמָתָא. יְהַב בְּהוּ עֵינֵיהּ (וּשְׁכִיבָן).
>
> **Translation:** Abaye was amazed at Rav Pappa's sharpness, as he was a young man at the time of this incident. Therefore, **he said** to Rav Pappa: **Where is your father? He said to him:** He is **in the city. Where is your mother? He said: In the city.** Abaye, who was orphaned in his youth, felt that a large part of Rav Pappa's success came because his parents lived in close proximity to him and provided for all his needs, freeing him from any need to get involved in business affairs and enabling him to immerse himself in Torah without distractions. Abaye felt a twinge of jealousy and **set his gaze upon them,** Rav Pappa's parents, in the pain that he did not have similarly supportive parents, **and both** Rav Pappa's father and mother **died.**

Abaye, apparently frustrated, asked Rav Papa: where is your father? In the city. Where is your mother? In the city. Abaye then cast his gaze upon them, and they died. The reason for Abaye's anger is not immediately apparent, since both he and Rav Papa were working toward the same goal. One explanation, cited in the name of the Ravyatz, is that Abaye did not intend to harm Rav Papa's parents; rather, by contemplating Rav Papa's good fortune in having his parents nearby, Abaye inadvertently caused divine scrutiny to fall upon them, with tragic results.

### Fee Conditions

Tosafot adds an important qualification to the principle of *meshata ni bach*. The claim is available specifically in a case where an exorbitant sum was demanded far in excess of the normal rate — as with the boatman exploiting a fugitive's desperation. Where the agreed fee actually reflects the going rate, or where the person agreeing to perform the service bears genuine risk or cost, the claim of *meshata ni bach* is unavailable and the obligation stands.

## Get vs Chalitza

### Tricked Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חֲלִיצָה מוּטְעֵת — כְּשֵׁירָה. גֵּט מוּטְעֶה — פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: A mistaken *ḥalitza* is valid,** while **a mistaken bill of divorce is invalid.**

A *baraita* now draws the contrast systematically. *Chalitza mutat keshera* — a *chalitza* performed under financial inducement that is never paid is valid. *Get mutat pasul* — a *get* obtained under a broken promise of payment is invalid. The reason follows directly from the earlier analysis: a *get* is a legal act that can be given through a *shaliach*, and therefore conditions can attach to it. If the *get* was given *al menat* that she pay him a sum of money, and the money is not paid, the condition is not fulfilled and the *get* is void. *Chalitza*, by contrast, cannot be made conditional, and therefore the unkept promise does not affect its validity.

### Conditional Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲלִיצָה מְעוּשֵּׂית — פְּסוּלָה. גֵּט מְעוּשֶּׂה — כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **A coerced *ḥalitza* is invalid,** while **a coerced bill of divorce is valid.**

This distinction — that *chalitza* cannot be conditioned because it cannot be delegated to an agent — has already been fully developed above, and the *baraita* confirms it. The *baraita* continues: *chalitza muafit pesulah* — a *chalitza* that was outright compelled, with no pretense of the *yavam*'s agreement, is invalid. *Get meuaseh kasher* — a *get* that was forced by a *beit din* is valid, as will be learned in *Gittin*.

### Difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי דְּאָמַר ״רוֹצֶה אֲנִי״ — אֲפִילּוּ חֲלִיצָה נָמֵי, וְאִי לָא אָמַר ״רוֹצֶה אֲנִי״ — גֵּט נָמֵי לָא. הָכִי קָאָמַר: חֲלִיצָה מוּטְעֵת לְעוֹלָם כָּשֵׁר, וְגֵט מוּטְעֶה לְעוֹלָם פָּסוּל. חֲלִיצָה מְעוּשֵּׂית וְגֵט מְעוּשֶּׂה — זִימְנִין כָּשֵׁר וְזִימְנִין פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances** of a coerced bill of divorce? **If** they force him until **he says: I want to** give the bill of divorce, **then even** this type of ***ḥalitza* also** should be valid, as although he was initially coerced, he acquiesced. **And if he did not say** by the end of the giving of the bill of divorce: **I want to** divorce her, then **even this** type of coerced **bill of divorce** should **also not** be acceptable. The Gemara answers that **this is what** the Sage **said: A mistaken *ḥalitza* is always valid,** while **a mistaken bill of divorce is always invalid. A coerced *ḥalitza* and a coerced bill of divorce are sometimes valid and sometimes invalid.**

The *Gemara* presses: what is the principled distinction? If the *yavam* says *rotzeh ani* — I am now willing — after being compelled, then surely *chalitza* too should be valid; and if he does not say *rotzeh ani*, then the *get* too should be invalid. On simple analysis there seems to be no asymmetry between them. The *Gemara* resolves this: *hachi ka'amar* — the *baraita* must be read as distinguishing cases. *Chalitza mutat* is always valid; *get mutat* is always invalid. *Chalitza muafit* and *get meuaseh* are each sometimes valid and sometimes not — and the determinative factor is whether the party ultimately says *rotzeh ani*.

### Korban Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״יַקְרִיב אוֹתוֹ״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁכּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ. יָכוֹל בְּעַל כׇּרְחוֹ — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לִרְצוֹנוֹ״. הָא כֵּיצַד? כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר ״רוֹצֶה אֲנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: It is said with regard to some offerings: **"He shall offer it"** (Leviticus 1:3). This **teaches that they may coerce him** to bring the offering he owes. **I might have thought this means** that he brings the offering totally **against his will. Therefore, the** continuation of that **verse states: "In accordance with his will"** (Leviticus 1:3). **How** can **these** two contradictory expositions **be** reconciled? **They coerce him** by imposing fines or penalties **until he says: I want to.**

A *baraita* about *korbanot* anchors the principle: *yakriv oto* — he shall offer it — teaches that *beit din* compels a person who refuses to bring an obligatory *korban*. *Yachol al korcho* — might this mean against his will entirely? *Talmud lomar lirtzono* — the verse says it must be with his will. The resolution: *kofin oto ad she'yomar rotzeh ani* — one compels him until he says he wishes to do it.

### Willingness Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן אַתָּה מוֹצֵא בְּגִיטֵּי נָשִׁים, כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר ״רוֹצֶה אֲנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly, you find the same** principle **with respect to bills of divorce for women,** as it is prohibited for anyone other than the husband to write the bill of divorce, but **they coerce him until he says: I want to** divorce her, and then write the bill of divorce on his behalf.

The same principle applies to *gittin*: *kofin oto ad she'yomar rotzeh ani*. And the *baraita* draws the parallel explicitly. The forced act is validated not by the compulsion but by the eventual expression of willingness.

### Motivation vs Desire

This reflects a striking philosophical distinction the *Gemara* draws between motivation and desire. The *Gemara* even permits, in a number of contexts, transactions entered into under extreme duress — on the theory that whenever someone sells property, it is because economic necessity forced them to, and external compulsion operates no differently. What matters is not the source of motivation but the present state of will. If the *yavam* now says *rotzeh ani*, his motivation for reaching that point is irrelevant — he presently desires it, and the act is valid. The distinction between external compulsion and internal desire, the *Gemara* teaches, is less sharp than one might assume.

## Beit Din & Docs

### Rav's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב סְחוֹרָה אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: חוֹלְצִין אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין מַכִּירִין, מְמָאֲנִין אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין מַכִּירִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** that **Rav Seḥora said** that **Rav Huna said:** Judges can allow a man and woman to **conduct *ḥalitza* even if** the judges **do not recognize** the participants. In other words, even if they do not have complete testimony before them that proves that these two people are a *yavam* and a *yevama*, if two people wish to perform *ḥalitza*, the judges are not required to check their identities. Likewise, with regard to women **making declarations of refusal:** If a young woman after reaching majority comes to make a declaration of refusal against her husband, she may do so, **even if** witnesses **do not recognize** her and they do not know for sure that she is the wife of the supposed husband.

The *Gemara* turns to a ruling transmitted through a chain: Rav said in the name of Rava who said in the name of Rav Sechora who said in the name of Rav Huna — *choltzin af al pi she'ein makirim*, a *beit din* may conduct a *chalitza* ceremony even if they do not personally know who the parties are. Similarly, they may conduct a *mi'un* ceremony without knowing the parties.

### Document Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִיכָךְ אֵין כּוֹתְבִין גֵּט חֲלִיצָה אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן מַכִּירִין, וְאֵין כּוֹתְבִין גֵּט מֵיאוּן אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן מַכִּירִין, דְּחָיְישִׁינַן לְבֵית דִּין טוֹעִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Therefore,** in cases where the woman is not identified, although the court may perform *ḥalitza* and refusals, it **may not write a document of *ḥalitza*,** i.e., a document attesting that *ḥalitza* took place, **unless they,** the judges, **recognize her. And** witnesses to the act **may not write a document of a declaration of refusal,** i.e., a document attesting that a refusal took place, **unless they,** the judges, **recognize** the woman, **as we are concerned** about the possibility **of a mistaken court.** Perhaps a court will not know that such a document is not complete proof that the action was conducted properly, and will consider it as proof that it was the *yevama* in this document who removed the shoe, or the wife in this document who made a declaration of refusal. Since the first court can conduct *ḥalitza* and refusals without accepting witnesses attesting to the identities of the involved parties, a second court cannot rely on these attesting documents alone, but must verify the identities before declaring the women eligible for marriage.

The consequence follows directly: *lefikach ein kotvim get chalitza ela im ken makirim* — witnesses cannot write the documentary record of the *chalitza* unless they personally know who the parties are. The reasoning is that since the *beit din* performs the ceremony without verifying identity, there is a real possibility that the man was not in fact the *yavam* — a woman might arrange for a stranger to go through the ceremony. If the witnesses write the document without knowing the man, they would be attesting to something they cannot verify.

### Future Courts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא דִּידֵיהּ אוֹמֵר: אֵין חוֹלְצִין אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן מַכִּירִין, וְאֵין מְמָאֲנִין אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן מַכִּירִין. לְפִיכָךְ כּוֹתְבִין גֵּט חֲלִיצָה אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין מַכִּירִין, וְכוֹתְבִין גֵּט מֵיאוּן אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין מַכִּירִין, וְלָא חָיְישִׁינַן לְבֵית דִּין טוֹעִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava himself said** the opposite of what he quoted in the name of others: A court **may not conduct *ḥalitza* unless they,** the judges, **recognize** the participants, **and** a court **may not convene a declaration of refusal unless they,** the judges, **recognize** the young woman. **Therefore,** witnesses **may write a document of *ḥalitza* even if they do not recognize** the woman themselves, as one who witnessed a court conduct *ḥalitza* can be sure that the court already checked the party's identities thoroughly. **And** witnesses **may write a document of refusal even if they do not recognize** the young woman who has refused, relying on the fact that witnesses must have already attested to their identities. **And we need not be concerned about** the possibility of **a mistaken court,** as there is no reason to fear that the first court conducted the case without properly identifying the participants.

The concern is specifically for *beit din to'eh* — a later *beit din* that receives the document and assumes it was properly conducted, not realizing that the original *beit din* may have been misled about who the *yavam* was. Therefore the witnesses, who do have the capacity to know the parties, bear the responsibility of verification before committing anything to writing. Rava, on his own account, takes a stricter position on the front end: *ein choltzin ela im ken makirim* — a *beit din* may not conduct the ceremony at all unless they know the parties. This is the straightforward rule, and it is difficult to argue otherwise. The consequence of Rava's view for the documentary question is the inverse: *v'lefikach kotvim get chalitza af al pi she'ein makirim* — the witnesses may write the document even if they themselves do not know the man. Since the *beit din* has already established the identity of the parties before proceeding, the witnesses need not independently verify. The first *beit din* has done its due diligence, and no later *beit din* will be misled.

## Chalitza Ceremony

### Initial Advice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִצְוַת חֲלִיצָה: בָּא הוּא וִיבִמְתּוֹ לְבֵית דִּין, וְהֵן מַשִּׂיאִין לוֹ עֵצָה הַהוֹגֶנֶת לוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְקָרְאוּ לוֹ זִקְנֵי עִירוֹ וְדִבְּרוּ אֵלָיו״,
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The mitzva of *ḥalitza*** is performed as follows: **He and his *yevama* come to the court, and** the scholars of the court **give him advice appropriate for him,** whether to enter levirate marriage or to perform *ḥalitza*, **as it is stated: "And the Elders of his city shall call him and speak to him"** (Deuteronomy 25:8).

The final *mishna* of the *perek* describes the complete *chalitza* ceremony step by step. He and his *yevama* come before the *beit din*, and the *beit din* gives them appropriate guidance — *eitzah ha'hogenet lo* — advice suitable to his situation, counseling whether *yibum* or *chalitza* is the proper course.

### Woman Declares

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִיא אוֹמֶרֶת: ״מֵאֵן יְבָמִי לְהָקִים לְאָחִיו שֵׁם בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל לֹא אָבָה יַבְּמִי״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא חָפַצְתִּי לְקַחְתָּהּ״. וּבִלְשׁוֹן הַקֹּדֶשׁ הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** If they decide to perform *ḥalitza*, **she says: "My brother-in-law refused to establish a name for his brother in Israel, he did not wish to consummate the levirate marriage"** (Deuteronomy 25:7), **and** afterward **he says: "I do not wish to take her"** (Deuteronomy 25:8). **And they would say** these statements **in the sacred** Hebrew **language** and not in any other language.

Once the decision for *chalitza* is made, the woman recites: *me'ein yivmi l'hakim l'achiv shem b'Yisrael lo avah yivmi* — my *yavam* does not wish to establish his brother's name in Israel; he does not desire to do *yibum*.

### Man Declares

The man then recites: *lo chafatzti lekachtah* — I do not desire to take her. The *mishna* notes that this recitation is not strictly *me'akev* but constitutes the proper procedure. Both declarations are made *b'lashon hakodesh*, in Hebrew — as the *mishna* in *Sotah* enumerates among the acts that must be performed in the original Hebrew.

### Shoe Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְנִגְּשָׁה יְבִמְתּוֹ אֵלָיו לְעֵינֵי הַזְּקֵנִים וְחָלְצָה נַעֲלוֹ מֵעַל רַגְלוֹ וְיָרְקָה בְּפָנָיו״, רוֹק הַנִּרְאֶה לַדַּיָּינִים.
>
> **Translation:** Afterward, the shoe is removed and she spits before him, as is written: **"His *yevama* shall approach him, before the Elders, and remove his shoe from on his foot and spit before him"** (Deuteronomy 25:9), which indicates that **this spittle** must be **visible to the judges.**

*V'nigshah yevimto eilav l'einei hazekenim v'chaltzah na'alo me'al raglo* — the *yevama* approaches him before the elders and removes his shoe from his foot, following the verse.

### Spitting

*V'yarkah b'fanav* — and she spits before him. The *mishna* inserts: *rok ha'nireh la'dayanim* — the spit must be visible to the judges. Whether spitting is strictly *me'akev* has been debated, but the requirement that it be visible to the judges is essential.

### Final Verses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְעָנְתָה וְאָמְרָה כָּכָה יֵעָשֶׂה לָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר לֹא יִבְנֶה אֶת בֵּית אָחִיו״. עַד כָּאן הָיוּ מַקְרִין. וּכְשֶׁהִקְרָא רַבִּי הוּרְקָנוֹס תַּחַת הָאֵלָה בִּכְפַר עֵיטָם, וְגָמַר אֶת כׇּל הַפָּרָשָׁה, הוּחְזְקוּ לִהְיוֹת גּוֹמְרִין כׇּל הַפָּרָשָׁה. ״וְנִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל בֵּית חֲלוּץ הַנָּעַל״.
>
> **Translation:** **"And** she shall **respond and say: So shall it be done to the man who does not build his brother's house"** (Deuteronomy 25:9). **Up until this point** the judges **would prompt** the parties **to recite** the text that they are required to say. **And when Rabbi Hyrkanus once prompted** the participants in *ḥalitza* **under the *ela* tree in the village** of **Eitam, he** prompted them to **finish reciting the whole** Torah **passage,** after which **they established the custom of completing the whole** Torah **passage.** Therefore, they continue and say the following verse: **"And his name shall be called in Israel: The house of he who had his shoe removed"** (Deuteronomy 25:10).

She then recites: *kachah ye'aseh la'ish asher lo yivneh et beit achiv* — thus shall be done to the man who does not build his brother's house. Until this point, the *beit din* would prompt her to recite each phrase after them, and the ceremony concluded with that verse. But *u'cheshe'hikri Rabbi Horkanus tachas ha'elah b'Kfar Eitam v'gamar kol haparasha* — when Rabbi Horkanus officiated under the *elah* tree in Kfar Eitam, he had her continue to the end of the verse: *v'nikra shemo b'Yisrael beit chalutz hana'al* — and his name shall be called in Israel: the house of the removed shoe. After Rabbi Horkanus instituted this, the practice spread and it became standard to have her recite the full verse through to that concluding phrase.

### Who Declares

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִצְוָה בַּדַּיָּינִין, וְלֹא מִצְוָה בַּתַּלְמִידִים. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מִצְוָה עַל כׇּל הָעוֹמְדִים שָׁם לוֹמַר ״חֲלוּץ הַנָּעַל״.
>
> **Translation:** **This mitzva** of saying: The house of he who had his shoe removed, applies **to the judges, but not to the students,** i.e., the students of the judges and other onlookers who are present. **Rabbi Yehuda says: It is a mitzva upon all those present to say: He who had his shoe removed.**

The *mishna* then states: *mitzvah b'dayanim v'lo mitzvah b'talmidim* — the obligation to make the declaration *beit chalutz hana'al* rests with the judges, not the students observing the proceedings. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: the verse says *b'Yisrael* — before Israel — implying that all those present at the ceremony should call out *chalutz hana'al*.

## Verse Recitation

### Woman's Recitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַאי מַאן דְּמַקְרֵי גֵּט חֲלִיצָה, לָא לַיקְרֵי לְדִידַהּ ״לֹא״ לְחוֹדֵיהּ וְ״אָבָה יַבְּמִי״ לְחוֹדֵיהּ, דְּמַשְׁמַע: ״אָבָה יַבְּמִי״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Abaye said** concerning the details of these *halakhot*: **The one who prompts** the *yavam* and the *yevama* **to read** the text for the **bill of *ḥalitza* should not prompt her** by reciting **"He did not"** (Deuteronomy 25:7) **by itself, and "wish to consummate the levirate marriage"** (Deuteronomy 25:7) **by itself, because** such a way of reading **sounds** to one who hears only the second phrase **like** he is saying: **My *yavam* does wish to consummate the marriage.**

Abaye rules that the person conducting the *chalitza* ceremony — the member of the *beit din* who is having the parties recite their lines — must be careful not to have her recite the words with pauses that break the verse into misleading segments.

### Lo Aba Yibmi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: ״לֹא אָבָה יַבְּמִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** he should prompt her all at once consecutively: **"He did not wish to consummate the levirate marriage"** (Deuteronomy 25:7), which will ensure that the intended meaning is clear.

Specifically: do not have her say *lo* alone and then pause before *aba yibmi*. The verse is *lo aba yibmi* — my *yavam* does not wish — but if she says *lo* and pauses, the phrase *aba yibmi* standing alone means "my *yavam* does wish." She must say the full phrase *lo aba yibmi* in one breath.

### Lo Chafatzti

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא לַיקְרֵי לְדִידֵיהּ ״לֹא״ לְחוֹדֵיהּ ״חָפַצְתִּי״ לְחוֹדֵיהּ, דְּמַשְׁמַע ״חָפַצְתִּי לְקַחְתָּהּ״, אֶלָּא ״לֹא חָפַצְתִּי לְקַחְתָּהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And he should not read to the** *yavam*: "I **do not" by itself,** followed by: **"Wish to** take her" **by itself, as it sounds** to one who heard only the second phrase **like: I do wish to take her. Rather,** he should read together at once: **"I do not wish to take her."**

Similarly, do not have him say *lo* and then pause before *chafatzti lekachtah*. That creates the impression *chafatzti lekachtah* — I do desire to take her. He must say *lo chafatzti lekachtah* as one continuous phrase.

### Rava Lenient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אַפְסוֹקֵי מִילְּתָא הִיא, וְאַפְסוֹקֵי מִילְּתָא לֵית לַן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: It is a** mere **interruption in the matter. And we have no** problem with regard to **an interruption in the matter** as it is basically just a pause for taking a breath**.**

Rava takes a more lenient position: *pesukei milta* — making a pause is simply a matter of completing one's sentence. The context makes clear what is meant, and a brief pause between *lo* and the rest of the phrase does not create a genuine misunderstanding.

### Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מוֹדֵה רָבָא בְּ״לֹא אָבָה יַבְּמִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to him:** Although Rava disagreed with Abaye about interruptions in the recitation of: "I do not wish to take her," **Rava concedes concerning** the recitation of: **"He did not wish to consummate the levirate marriage,"** as this recitation is essential and must be recited properly.

Nevertheless, Rava agrees that for the specific phrase *lo aba yibmi* there is no room for leniency, and it must be said as a single phrase. Rashi explains: unlike *lo chafatzti lekachtah*, which begins the verse and has nothing preceding it to create ambiguity, the phrase *lo aba yibmi* appears mid-verse, following *me'ein yibmi l'hakim shem l'achiv b'Yisrael*. In that context, a pause after *lo* is far more likely to be heard as a denial of what preceded: "that's not right — *aba yibmi*, actually he does want to." Therefore this particular phrase must be delivered without interruption.

### Care Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְרַב כָּהֲנָא דְּקָמִצְטַעַר וּמַקְרֵי לַהּ ״לֹא אָבָה יַבְּמִי״, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא סָבַר לֵיהּ מָר לְהָא דְּרָבָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מוֹדֵה רָבָא בְּ״לֹא אָבָה יַבְּמִי״.
>
> **Translation:** It is told: **Rav Ashi found Rav Kahana painstakingly trying to prompt** a certain *yevama*, so that she would recite: **"He did not wish to consummate the levirate marriage"** all at once, but the *yevama* did not understand and was distorting the meaning by not reciting the words together. Rav Ashi **said to him: Does the Master not hold that which Rava said,** that the proper pausing during the recitation is not indispensable? **He said to him:** Although Rava disagreed with Abaye about interruptions in the recitation of: "I do not wish to take her," **Rava concedes concerning** the recitation of: **"He did not wish to consummate the levirate marriage,"** as this recitation is essential and must be recited properly.

The *Gemara* illustrates the importance of this ruling. Rav Papa and Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua once observed Rav Kahana going to great lengths to have the *yevama* recite the words *lo aba yibmi* all as one phrase. They asked: does the master not hold like Rava's lenient view? Rav Kahana replied: Rava concedes regarding *lo aba yibmi*. Even Rava agrees that for that specific phrase, pauses are not acceptable.

## Get Writing

### Fragment Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַאי מַאן דְּכָתֵב גִּיטָּא דַּחֲלִיצְתָּא — לִיכְתּוֹב הָכִי: אַקְרֵינוּהָ לְדִידַהּ מִן ״מֵאֵן יְבָמִי״ עַד ״אָבָה יַבְּמִי״, וְאַקְרֵינוֹהִי לְדִידֵיהּ מִן ״לֹא״ עַד ״לְקַחְתָּהּ״, וְאַקְרֵינוּהָ לְדִידַהּ מִן ״כָּכָה״ וְעַד ״חֲלוּץ הַנָּעַל״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Kahana added that **Abaye** also **said: One who writes a bill of *ḥalitza* must write as follows: We prompted her to recite from "My brother-in-law refused" until "he did not wish to consummate the levirate marriage"** (Deuteronomy 25:7); **and we prompted him to recite from** "I do **not" until "take her"** (Deuteronomy 25:8). **And we prompted her to recite from "So shall"** (Deuteronomy 25:9) **until "he who had his shoe removed"** (Deuteronomy 25:10).

Abaye rules regarding the writing of the *get chalitza* — the documentary record of the ceremony. One should not write out the complete biblical verses. Rather: *akrinuhu l'didah min me'ein yibmi ad lo aba yibmi* — we had her recite from *me'ein yibmi* until *lo aba yibmi*, citing the opening and closing words but not writing out the full text. Similarly: *akrinuhu l'dideih min lo ad lekachtah* — we had him recite from *lo* until *lekachtah*, deliberately omitting the middle word *chafatzti*. And for her subsequent verse: from *kacha* until *vuchalutz hana'al*.

### Sirtuv Issue

The reason for these deliberate gaps is the prohibition against writing biblical passages without *sirtut* — the scored lines that must accompany Torah writing. If one were to write out the full verses, three or more consecutive words of scripture would appear in the document without the requisite scoring, raising a concern about treating biblical text improperly outside the context of a sacred scroll. By dropping a word in the middle, the document avoids transcribing a complete biblical phrase.

### Man's Verse

For the man's verse, the document records: from *lo* until *lekachtah* — with *chafatzti* omitted so that no three consecutive biblical words appear in sequence.

### Woman's Verses

For the woman's verse at the conclusion of the ceremony, the document records: from *kacha* until *vuchalutz hana'al*, similarly structured to avoid a complete biblical phrase.

### Mar Zutra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר זוּטְרָא מְשַׂרְטֵט וְכָתֵיב לְכוּלַּהּ פָּרָשָׁה. מַתְקֵיף לַהּ מָר בַּר אִידִי: וְהָא לֹא נִיתַּן לִיכָּתֵב! וְהִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּמָר זוּטְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra would score** lines of a parchment **and write the whole** *ḥalitza* **passage** on it as a bill of *ḥalitza*, so it would be displayed before the *ḥalitza* participants. **Mar bar Idi strongly objected to this: But it is unable to be written** like this on its own, as the Torah may be written only in a complete state, and it is prohibited to write parts of the Torah when there is no mitzva to write them separately. The Gemara comments: Even so, **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Mar Zutra,** because there is an aspect of a mitzva here, as it is being written as part of a bill of *ḥalitza*, not for its own purpose.

Mar Zutra followed a different practice: *mesartet v'chatav lechulah parasha* — he scored the document with *sirtut* and wrote out the entire *parasha*. He evidently held that with proper scoring the concern is resolved.

### Objection

Rav Papa bar Idi challenged this: *veha lo nitenu lichtov* — one is not permitted to write out individual sections of the Torah as standalone passages. The *Gemara* in *Gittin*, Perek Hanizukin, teaches that one does not write a *megillah* — a miniature scroll of select passages — for a child to learn from, precisely because Torah must be written as a complete unit.

### Resolution

The *Gemara* rules: *v'hilchata k'Mar Zutra* — the law follows Mar Zutra. The prohibition against writing isolated Torah passages applies where one is writing it *ada'ata d'parasha* — as a small self-contained excerpt intended to function as a standalone Torah text. The *get chalitza* is different: *v'lo lekdusha hu* — it is not written as a sacred text but as a legal document, to record what was recited during the ceremony. Since the intent is documentary rather than scriptural, writing the full passage with *sirtut* is permitted.

## Spitting Details

### Wind Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: רָקְקָה וּקְלָטַתּוּ הָרוּחַ — לֹא עָשְׂתָה וְלֹא כְּלוּם. מַאי טַעְמָא — ״וְיָרְקָה בְּפָנָיו״ בָּעֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** If the *yevama* **spat, but the wind seized** her saliva and it never landed in front of the face of the *yavam*, **she did nothing,** and her actions have no halakhic significance. **What is the reason** for this? **We require** that **"she spit before him,"** as is mentioned in the verse.

Abaye rules: if the woman spat and the wind carried the spit away — *lo asa velo klum* — it counts for nothing. The *Torah* requires *v'yarkah b'fanav* — she shall spit *before him*, meaning the spit must arrive in his presence.

### Presence

The requirement is not that the spit physically strike him, but that it land in front of him in a way that constitutes spitting *before* him.

### Height Factor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִלְכָּךְ, הוּא אָרוֹךְ וְהִיא גּוּצָה, קְלָטַתְהוּ הָרוּחַ — אִיכָּא ״בְּפָנָיו״. הִיא אֲרוּכָּה וְהוּא גּוּץ — בָּעֵינַן עַד דְּמָטֵי לַהֲדֵי אַפֵּיהּ וַהֲדַר אָזֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** **Therefore, if he is tall and she is short and the wind seized it,** the requirement of **"before him" is satisfied,** because the moment the saliva left her mouth it was in front of the face of the *yavam*. **But if she is tall and he is short, we require that the saliva reach the space across from his face, and afterward it may go** with the wind; i.e., if she was taller than him and the saliva was taken by the wind before it reached the height of his face, she did not fulfill her obligation.

Height differences between the parties affect whether this requirement is met. If he is tall and she is short, then even if the wind blows the spit away after it leaves her mouth, it was already *b'fanav* — before his face — the moment it emerged, since he was towering above her as she spit in his direction. In that case the *chalitza* is valid even if the spit is carried off. If she is tall and he is short, the spit leaves her mouth at a height above his face. The requirement — *ba'inan ad demati lahadi apeih* — is that the spit must actually descend to the level of his face before any dispersion occurs. If the wind catches it before it reaches his face level, the requirement has not been met and the *chalitza* is compromised.

### Eating Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אֲכַלָה תּוּמָא וְרָקַת, אֲכַלָה גַּרְגִּישְׁתָּא וְרָקַתה — לֹא עָשְׂתָה וְלֹא כְּלוּם. מַאי טַעְמָא — ״וְיָרְקָה״ מֵעַצְמָהּ בָּעֵינַן, וְלֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** If **she ate garlic and spat,** or if **she ate *gargishta* clay,** i.e., a type of clay once chewed for medicinal purposes, **and spat, she did nothing** and her actions have no halakhic significance, for this is not called spitting. **What is the reason** for this? **We require that "she shall spit" on her own, and this is not** satisfied here, for in this case she spits only on account of another thing that causes a pooling of saliva in her mouth.

Rava adds: if the woman ate garlic, or ate clay, and then spat — *lo asa velo klum* — it does not count.

### Own Accord

The reason: *v'yarkah me'atzma ba'inan* — the spitting must come from her own initiative and from her natural saliva. If she ate something that induced her to spit, or that altered the character of what she produced, the act is not genuinely *her* spitting but something caused by the food. This fails the requirement.

### Judge Witness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: צְרִיכִי דַּיָּינֵי לְמִיחְזֵי רוּקָּא כִּי נָפֵיק מִפּוּמַּהּ דִּיבָמָה. דִּכְתִיב: ״לְעֵינֵי הַזְּקֵנִים ... וְיָרְקָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava said: The judges must see the spittle when it leaves the mouth of the *yevama*,** and the mere fact that she spat on the ground is insufficient, for this is not called spitting. **What is the reason** for this? **We require that "she shall spit" on her own, and this is not** satisfied here, for in this case she spits only on account of another thing that causes a pooling of saliva in her mouth. **As it is written: "Before the Elders…and spit,"** indicating that the spitting must take place before the eyes of the judges.

Rava further rules: *tzrichi dayanei lemichzi roka ki nafik mipuma d'yevama* — the judges must see the spit at the moment it leaves the *yevama*'s mouth. The verse states *l'einei hazekenim v'yarkah* — to the eyes of the elders and she shall spit — meaning the act of spitting itself must be directly witnessed by the judges, not merely inferred from its result.

## Final Proclamation

### Name Change

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְנִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל בֵּית חֲלוּץ הַנָּעַל״ — מִצְוָה בַּדַּיָּינִין וְלֹא בַּתַּלְמִידִים.
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the mishna: With regard to the verse **"And his name shall be called in Israel: The house of he who had his shoe removed," there is a mitzva upon the judges to recite this but** it is **not** a requirement **for the students** or onlookers.

*V'nikra shemo b'Yisrael beit chalutz hana'al* — and his name shall be called in Israel: the house of the one whose shoe was removed. This declaration concludes the ceremony and formally fixes his new status.

### Declaration Role

The *mishna* rules: *mitzvah b'dayanim velo b'talmidim* — the obligation of this declaration falls upon the judges, not upon students or observers. Rabbi Yehuda holds, as noted above, that *b'Yisrael* implies all those present.

### Tarfon Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: פַּעַם אַחַת הָיִינוּ יוֹשְׁבִים לִפְנֵי רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן, וּבָאתָה יְבָמָה לַחְלוֹץ, וְאָמַר לָנוּ, עָנוּ כּוּלְּכֶם: ״חֲלוּץ הַנָּעַל״, ״חֲלוּץ הַנָּעַל״, ״חֲלוּץ הַנָּעַל״.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says: Once we were sitting before Rabbi Tarfon and a *yevama* came to perform *ḥalitza*. He said to us: You must all answer: "He who had his shoe removed," "He who had his shoe removed," "He who had his shoe removed,"** stating that portion of the verse three times.

A *baraita* records: Rabbi Yehuda said — once we were sitting before Rabbi Tarfon and a *yevama* came to perform *chalitza*. Rabbi Tarfon said to all of us: say *chalutza na, chalutza na, chalutza na* — three times in succession, in a manner similar to other rabbinic proclamations of permitted status (*mutar lach, mutar lach, mutar lach*). This triple declaration publicly and ceremonially marks the finality of the *chalitza* and the new status of the *chalutzah*.

### Perek End

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַרַן עֲלָךְ מִצְוַת חֲלִיצָה
>
> **Translation:**

*Hadran alach mitzvas chalitza* — with the conclusion of this *baraita*, the *perek* of *mitzvas chalitza* formally ends.
