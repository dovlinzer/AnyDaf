# Rosh Hashanah 17 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Arba'ah Devarim

### Intro to Four Things

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: אַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים מְקָרְעִין גְּזַר דִּינוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם, אֵלּוּ הֵן: צְדָקָה, צְעָקָה, שִׁינּוּי הַשֵּׁם, וְשִׁינּוּי מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak said: A person's sentence is torn up on** account of **four** types of **actions. These are:** Giving **charity, crying out** in prayer, **a change of** one's **name, and a change of** one's **deeds** for the better.

The daf begins with a series of statements by Rabbi Yitzchak relating to *din*, and we pick up approximately ten lines before the text widens on 16b. The Gemara introduces: *arba'ah devarim mekari'in gezar dino shel adam* — four things that tear up a person's final judgment. This evokes the broader theme running through the sugya: even after a *din* has been rendered, it is not necessarily immutable. There remains the possibility of reversal, and these four things are precisely that mechanism.

### Tzedakah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְדָקָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּצְדָקָה תַּצִּיל מִמָּוֶת״.
>
> **Translation:** Giving **charity, as it is written: "And charity delivers from death"** (Proverbs 10:2);

The first is *tzedakah*, derived from the verse *v'tzedakah tatzil mimavet* — charity will save from death.

### Tze'akah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְעָקָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּצְעֲקוּ אֶל ה׳ בַּצַּר לָהֶם וּמִמְּצוּקוֹתֵיהֶם יוֹצִיאֵם״.
>
> **Translation:** **crying out** in prayer, **as it is written: "Then they cry to the Lord in their trouble, and He brings them out of their distresses"** (Psalms 107:28);

The second is *tze'akah* — crying out to God — derived from the verse *vayitz'aku el Hashem batzar lahem mimtzukoteihem yotzi'em*: they cried out to God in their travail, and from their anguish He took them out.

### Shinui Hashem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁינּוּי הַשֵּׁם, דִּכְתִיב: ״שָׂרַי אִשְׁתְּךָ לֹא תִקְרָא אֶת שְׁמָהּ שָׂרָי כִּי שָׂרָה שְׁמָהּ״, וּכְתִיב: ״וּבֵרַכְתִּי אוֹתָהּ וְגַם נָתַתִּי מִמֶּנָּה לְךָ בֵּן״.
>
> **Translation:** **a change of** one's **name, as it is written:** "As for **Sarai your wife, you shall not call her name Sarai, but Sarah shall her name be"** (Genesis 17:15), **and it is written** there: **"And I will bless her, and I will also give you a son from her"** (Genesis 17:16);

The third is *shinui hashem* — changing one's name — derived from the verse in which God tells Avraham: *Sarai ishtecha lo tikra et shemah Sarai ki Sarah shemah* — you shall not call her Sarai, for Sarah is her name — followed immediately by the blessing that she would bear a son.

### Shinui Ma'aseh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁינּוּי מַעֲשֶׂה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּרְא הָאֱלֹהִים אֶת מַעֲשֵׂיהֶם״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּנָּחֶם הָאֱלֹהִים עַל הָרָעָה אֲשֶׁר דִּבֶּר לַעֲשׂוֹת לָהֶם וְלֹא עָשָׂה״.
>
> **Translation:** **a change of** one's **deeds** for the better, **as it is written: "And God saw their deeds"** (Jonah 3:10), **and it is written** there: **"And God repented of the evil, which He had said He would do to them, and He did not do** it" (Jonah 3:10).

The fourth is *shinui ma'aseh* — changing one's deeds — derived from *vayar Elokim et ma'aseihem*, God saw the deeds of the people of Nineveh, and *vayinachem ha'Elokim al hara'ah asher diber la'asot lahem v'lo asah* — God relented from the evil He had spoken of doing to them.

### Teshuvah Connection

Three of these four map directly onto the famous liturgical formula: *teshuvah, tzedakah, tefillah ma'avirin et ro'a hagezeirah* — repentance, charity, and prayer avert the evil decree. Notably, *shinui hashem* is absent from that formulation. One explanation is that *shinui hashem* may itself be a means of achieving *teshuvah*: the Rambam discusses how changing one's name is an expression of the desire to become a new person, to identify as a different kind of individual. Alternatively, the Maharsha argues that *shinui hashem* is categorically different from the other three, in that it is not about reversing an evil decree so much as changing a status quo for the better — Sarai did not have children, and now she can. In that sense it is not quite of the same weight as the others, which are truly mechanisms of *mekari'in gezar dino*.

### Shinui Makom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף שִׁינּוּי מָקוֹם, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֶל אַבְרָם לֶךְ לְךָ מֵאַרְצְךָ״, וַהֲדַר: ״וְאֶעֶשְׂךָ לְגוֹי גָּדוֹל״.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say: Also, a change of** one's **place** of residence cancels an evil judgment, **as it is written: "And the Lord said to Abram: Go you out of your county"** (Genesis 12:1), **and afterward** it is written: **"And I will make of you a great nation"** (Genesis 12: 2).

The Gemara adds: *v'yesh omrim af shinui makom* — and some say that changing one's place also belongs on this list — derived from *vayomer Hashem el Avram lech lecha me'artzecha*, and only afterward *v'e'escha l'goy gadol*, you will become a great nation. Here again, as with *shinui hashem*, the verse speaks more about achieving a greater fortune than about being saved from an evil decree.

### Teshuvah Integration

The Rambam also addresses *shinui makom* in the context of *teshuvah*: moving to a new place can represent a fresh start with a clean slate, and beyond that, the Rambam consistently emphasizes how one's surroundings and environment shape one's conduct and standards of behavior. In this way, both *shinui hashem* and *shinui makom* can be understood as dimensions of *teshuvah* more broadly conceived.

## Hakbilat P'nei Rav

### Greeting Master

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: חַיָּיב אָדָם לְהַקְבִּיל פְּנֵי רַבּוֹ בָּרֶגֶל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מַדּוּעַ אַתְּ הוֹלֶכֶת אֵלָיו הַיּוֹם לֹא חֹדֶשׁ וְלֹא שַׁבָּת״, מִכְּלָל דִּבְחֹדֶשׁ וְשַׁבָּת אִיבְּעִי לַהּ לְמֵיזַל.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak said: A person is obligated to** go out and **greet his teacher on a Festival, as it is stated** that the husband of the Shunamite woman asked, when she was readying herself to go to the prophet: **"Why will you go to him today; it is neither the New Moon nor Shabbat"** (II Kings 4:23). **By inference,** we learn **that on the New Moon and on Shabbat,** which in this context means a Festival that is a day of rest, **she was required to go.**

The Gemara transitions to another statement of Rabbi Yitzchak, though the theme now shifts away from *din*: *chayav adam lehakbil p'nei rabo baregel* — a person is obligated to greet his master on the festivals. The scriptural source is the question posed to the Shunamite woman when she set out to visit Elisha after her son died: *madua at holechet elav hayom lo chodesh v'lo shabbat* — why are you going to him today? It is not *Rosh Chodesh* and it is not *Shabbat*. The Gemara reads this as a *michlal*: by implication, on *Rosh Chodesh* and *Shabbat* one is indeed obligated to go. Of course, the *tanna* spoke of *regalim*, not *Rosh Chodesh* and *Shabbat*. The connection is admittedly not entirely clear, and the difficulty of demanding a visit on every *Rosh Chodesh* and every *Shabbat* would make the requirement unduly burdensome. There is another Gemara that addresses this at greater length, and we will not pursue it further here.

### Taharah Baregel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: חַיָּיב אָדָם לְטַהֵר אֶת עַצְמוֹ בָּרֶגֶל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבְנִבְלָתָם לֹא תִגָּעוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak said: A person is obligated to purify himself on a Festival, as it is stated: "And their carcasses you shall not touch;** they are impure to you" (Leviticus 11:8).

Rabbi Yitzchak continues: *chayav adam l'taher atzmo baregel* — a person is obligated to purify himself on the festivals. The scriptural source is *uv'nivlatam lo tiga'u* — do not touch the carcasses of unclean animals. A *braisa* elaborates: one might have thought this verse prohibits ordinary Israelites from touching a *nevelah*. The *talmud lomar* is the verse *emor el hakohanim bnei Aharon* — it is the *kohanim* who bear the prohibition against *tum'ah*, not ordinary Israelites. In fact, a straightforward *kal v'chomer* confirms this: if even the more severe *tum'ah* of a human corpse applies only to *kohanim* and not to ordinary Jews, how much more certainly does the lighter *tum'ah* of an animal carcass not apply to them. If so, what is the meaning of *uv'nivlatam lo tiga'u*? It must be speaking in the context of the *regel*. The *rishonim* debate whether this constitutes a genuine prohibition or rather practical advice — that in order to perform *aliyah laregel* and bring a *korban* at the *Beis Hamikdash*, one would need to be in a state of *taharah*, and the verse is thus providing guidance rather than issuing a formal prohibition. Moreover, if it is a formal prohibition, one might reasonably assume it applies only when *aliyah laregel* is actually practiced — what logical basis would it have in the absence of a *Beis Hamikdash*?

### Post-Churban Aliyah

When these two obligations — greeting one's master and immersing in a *mikveh* — are considered together, it becomes apparent that Rabbi Yitzchak, who lived after the *Churban Habayis*, seems to be attempting to recreate something of the *aliyah laregel* experience in a world without a Temple. Where once one would ascend to the *Beis Hamikdash*, stand in the presence of the *Shechina*, and fulfill *lo yera'u fanai reikam* — appearing before God's presence not empty-handed — now the practice involves purifying oneself in a *mikveh* and proceeding to greet one's *rav*. The effect is unmistakably to substitute the *talmid chacham* for the *Beis Hamikdash* as the locus of the *regel* experience — a striking post-*Churban* reimagining of the festival pilgrimage.

## Three Books

### Books Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי כְּרוּסְפָּדַאי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁלֹשָׁה סְפָרִים נִפְתָּחִין בְּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה, אֶחָד שֶׁל רְשָׁעִים גְּמוּרִין, וְאֶחָד שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים גְּמוּרִין, וְאֶחָד שֶׁל בֵּינוֹנִיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Kruspedai said that Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Three books are opened on Rosh HaShana** before the Holy One, Blessed be He: **One of wholly wicked** people, **and one of wholly righteous** people, **and one of middling** people whose good and bad deeds are equally balanced.

The Gemara now turns to a statement of Rav Kruspedai — whose Aramaic name, incidentally, is the word for *tzitzis* — citing Rabbi Yochanan: *shlosha sefarim niftachim b'Rosh Hashanah* — three books are opened on Rosh Hashanah. It is worth pausing to appreciate that none of this is self-evident. The idea of a *yom hadin*, of *ketivah* and *chatimah*, of books being opened — these are particular metaphors and conceptual frameworks that Chazal are introducing and developing. The basic notion that Rosh Hashanah is a time when we beseech God for a good year, and that there are moments when particular things — rain, fruit, and so forth — are determined, does not by itself imply the full-blown theology of a *yom hadin*. These are being built step by step throughout the sugya.

### Tzaddikim and Resha'im

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צַדִּיקִים גְּמוּרִים — נִכְתָּבִין וְנֶחְתָּמִין לְאַלְתַּר לְחַיִּים, רְשָׁעִים גְּמוּרִים — נִכְתָּבִין וְנֶחְתָּמִין לְאַלְתַּר לְמִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Wholly righteous** people **are immediately written and sealed for life; wholly wicked** people **are immediately written and sealed for death;**

The three books are as follows. *Tzadikim gemurim* — the completely righteous — are immediately written and sealed for life: *nechtavim v'nechtamim l'altar l'chayim*. *Resha'im gemurim* — the completely wicked — are immediately written and sealed for death: *nechtavim v'nechtamim l'altar l'mitah*.

### Beinonim Suspended

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּינוֹנִיִּים — תְּלוּיִין וְעוֹמְדִין מֵרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה וְעַד יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים, זָכוּ — נִכְתָּבִין לְחַיִּים, לֹא זָכוּ — נִכְתָּבִין לְמִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** and **middling** people **are left** with their judgment **suspended from Rosh HaShana until Yom Kippur,** their fate remaining undecided. If **they merit,** through the good deeds and mitzvot that they perform during this period, **they are written for life;** if **they do not** so **merit, they are written for death.**

*Beinonim* — the intermediate category — are suspended: *tluyim v'omdim me'Rosh Hashanah v'ad Yom Hakippurim*. If they merit, they are written for life; if they do not merit, they are written for death. One notices here that the familiar two-stage process of *ketivah* and *chatimah* does not quite work out as straightforwardly as one might assume. The *tzadikim* and *resha'im* receive both stages immediately; the *beinonim* do not actually receive the *ketivah* until Yom Kippur. So the picture is not that everyone is written on Rosh Hashanah and sealed by Yom Kippur. Rather, the *beinonim* have a period of suspended judgment in which they are not yet written at all.

### Sefer Chayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין, מַאי קְרָא: ״יִמָּחוּ מִסֵּפֶר חַיִּים וְעִם צַדִּיקִים אַל יִכָּתֵבוּ״. ״יִמָּחוּ מִסֵּפֶר״ — זֶה סִפְרָן שֶׁל רְשָׁעִים גְּמוּרִים, ״חַיִּים״ — זֶה סִפְרָן שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים, ״וְעִם צַדִּיקִים אַל יִכָּתֵבוּ״ — זֶה סִפְרָן שֶׁל בֵּינוֹנִיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Avin said: What is the verse** that alludes to this? **"Let them be blotted out of the book of the living, but not be written with the righteous"** (Psalms 69:29). **"Let them be blotted out of the book"; this is the book of wholly wicked** people, who are blotted out from the world. **"Of the living"; this is the book of** wholly **righteous** people. **"But not be written with the righteous"; this is the book of middling** people, who are written in a separate book, not with the righteous.

Rabbi Abin derives these categories from the verse *yimachu misefer chayim v'im tzaddikim al yikatevu* — let them be erased from the book of life and not be written with the righteous. The Gemara reads: *yimachu misefer* — to be erased from the book — this is the book of the *resha'im gemurim*, who are removed from the book of life; *chayim* — the book of life — this is the book of the *tzadikim*; and *v'im tzaddikim al yikatevu* — they will not yet be written with the righteous — this refers to the *beinonim*, whose writing is deferred. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר, מֵהָכָא: ״וְאִם אַיִן מְחֵנִי נָא מִסִּפְרְךָ אֲשֶׁר כָּתָבְתָּ״, ״מְחֵנִי נָא״ — זֶה סִפְרָן שֶׁל רְשָׁעִים, ״מִסִּפְרְךָ״ — זֶה סִפְרָן שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים, ״אֲשֶׁר כָּתָבְתָּ״ — זֶה סִפְרָן שֶׁל בֵּינוֹנִיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** This matter is derived **from here: "And if not, blot me, I pray You, out of Your book which you have written"** (Exodus 32:32). **"Blot me, I pray You"; this is the book of** wholly **wicked** people, who are blotted out from the world. **"Out of Your book"; this is the book of** wholly **righteous** people, which is special and attributed to God Himself. **"Which You have written"; this is the book of middling** people.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak derives similarly from Moshe's plea: *ve'im ayin mechani na misefercha asher katavta* — if not, erase me from the book that You have written. *Mechani na* — the erasure — refers to the book of the *resha'im*; *misefercha* — Your book — is the book of the *tzadikim*; and *asher katavta* — that You have written — refers to the *beinonim*, whose writing is still pending.

It is also worth noting that the Gemara's language for the *resha'im* is not that they are written in a book of death, but rather that they are erased from the book of life. This connects to a broader question in Chazal's conception of *gehinnom*: is it a place of active punishment, or is it simply the absence of eternal life? The Rambam, among others, understands *gehinnom* as the soul's extinction rather than as an underworld of torment — and the language of *mechiyah*, erasure, rather than inscription in a book of death, is consistent with that reading.

### Rishonic Approaches

The obvious difficulty with this Gemara is that its apparent premise — that the righteous live and the wicked die — does not correspond to observable reality. The *rishonim* offer three main responses.

The Ramban effectively turns the Gemara into a tautology: the words *tzaddik* and *rasha* here do not denote moral categories but legal ones — *tzaddik bedino* and *rasha bedino*, one found innocent and one found guilty. The righteous in this Gemara are those whom God has judged worthy of life; the wicked are those judged worthy of death. This makes the Gemara's only real *chiddush* the timing — that the decision is made on Rosh Hashanah — but it says nothing about the moral criteria God employs. This reading is not very compelling as *peshat* and not particularly illuminating as theology.

The Rambam attempts to read the Gemara more literally, maintaining that it really does concern whether one's *mitzvot* outweigh one's *avonot* or vice versa, while acknowledging that God's calculus is opaque to human perception. Sins we consider minor may weigh heavily; acts we consider great may not. In the end the accounting is precise, even if we cannot follow it — which is not always satisfying when one observes an apparent *tzaddik* dying young or a *rasha* flourishing.

Tosafot offers what has become the standard response of Chazal to the problem of *schar va'onesh* in general: defer the reckoning to the world to come. According to Tosafot, the Gemara is not speaking about whether a person will live or die in the coming year but about whether they will merit *chayei olam haba*. A *tzaddik* is determined to merit the world to come; a *rasha* is determined not to. This dissolves the empirical problem but raises its own difficulty: why would a yearly judgment be needed for a determination that logically belongs at the moment of death? And it is entirely at odds with the liturgical context of Rosh Hashanah, which is concerned with the year ahead rather than with final eschatological destiny.

## Final Day Judgment

### Beis Shammai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: שָׁלֹשׁ כִּתּוֹת הֵן לְיוֹם הַדִּין: אַחַת שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים גְּמוּרִים, וְאַחַת שֶׁל רְשָׁעִים גְּמוּרִים, וְאַחַת שֶׁל בֵּינוֹנִיִּים. צַדִּיקִים גְּמוּרִים — נִכְתָּבִין וְנֶחְתָּמִין לְאַלְתַּר לְחַיֵּי עוֹלָם, רְשָׁעִים גְּמוּרִים — נִכְתָּבִין וְנֶחְתָּמִין לְאַלְתַּר לְגֵיהִנָּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְרַבִּים מִיְּשֵׁנֵי אַדְמַת עָפָר יָקִיצוּ אֵלֶּה לְחַיֵּי עוֹלָם וְאֵלֶּה לַחֲרָפוֹת לְדִרְאוֹן עוֹלָם״, בֵּינוֹנִיִּים — יוֹרְדִין לְגֵיהִנָּם,
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Beit Shammai say: There will be three groups** of people **on the** great **Day of Judgment** at the end of days: **One of wholly righteous** people, **one of wholly wicked** people, **and one of middling** people. Wholly **righteous** people **will immediately be written and sealed for eternal life. Wholly wicked** people **will immediately be written and sealed for Gehenna, as it is stated: "And many of those who sleep in the dust of the earth shall wake, some to eternal life and some to shame and everlasting contempt"** (Daniel 12:2). **Middling** people **will descend to Gehenna** to be cleansed and to achieve atonement for their sins,

The Gemara now introduces a *braisa* from Beis Shammai on an altogether different plane of judgment — not the annual determination on Rosh Hashanah but the *yom hadin* at the end of days, the final judgment of the messianic era and the time of *techiyat hameitim*. *Gimel kitos hein l'yom hadin* — three groups stand for the final day of judgment: one of *tzadikim gemurim*, one of *resha'im gemurim*, and one of *beinonim*.

### Tzaddikim and Resha'im F…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְצַפְצְפִין וְעוֹלִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהֵבֵאתִי אֶת הַשְּׁלִישִׁית בָּאֵשׁ וּצְרַפְתִּים כִּצְרוֹף אֶת הַכֶּסֶף וּבְחַנְתִּים כִּבְחוֹן אֶת הַזָּהָב הוּא יִקְרָא בִשְׁמִי וַאֲנִי אֶעֱנֶה אוֹתוֹ״, וַעֲלֵיהֶם אָמְרָה חַנָּה: ״ה׳ מֵמִית וּמְחַיֶּה מוֹרִיד שְׁאוֹל וַיָּעַל״.
>
> **Translation:** **and they will cry out** in their pain **and** eventually **ascend** from there, **as it is stated: "And I will bring the third part through the fire, and will refine them as silver is refined, and will try them as gold is tried; they shall call on My name, and I will answer them"** (Zechariah 13:9). This is referring to the members of the third group, who require refinement and cleansing. **And about them, Hannah said: "The Lord kills, and gives life; he brings down to the grave, and brings up"** (I Samuel 2:6).

*Tzadikim gemurim* are immediately written and sealed for *chayei olam* — eternal life. Notice the shift in language from the earlier Gemara: where before it said *l'chayim*, here it says *l'chayei olam*, eternal life — appropriate to this eschatological setting in which physical, bodily existence in the world to come is presumably what is at stake. *Resha'im gemurim* are sealed immediately for *gehinnom*. Again, the language changes: in the earlier Gemara it said *l'mitah*, death; here it says *l'gehinnom*. The verse cited is from Daniel: *rabim miyeshenei admat afar yakitzu* — many who sleep in the dust will awaken — *eileh l'chayei olam*, these to eternal life, *v'eileh lachaparot l'dira'on olam*, those to shame and eternal damnation.

### Beinonim Purification

The *beinonim*, according to Beis Shammai, *yordim l'gehinnom umitzaftzefin v'olim* — go down to *gehinnom*, cry out, and rise up. The verse from Zechariah reads: *veheiveisi et hashlishit ba'esh* — I will bring the third group into the fire — *vetzaraftim ketzruf es hakesef uvechantim kivechon es hazahav* — I will smelt them as one smelts silver and test them as gold is tested — *hu yikra bishmi va'ani e'eneh oto* — he will call out in my name and I will answer him. The fire of *gehinnom*, for the *beinonim*, is thus a process of purification rather than final destruction — an idea that has obvious structural parallels to the Catholic doctrine of Purgatory. Through this process, the *beinonim* earn their place in eternal life. *Va'alehem amrah Chanah: Hashem meimis umechayeh, morid she'ol vaya'al* — God brings death and life, brings down to the underworld and brings up — this refers to those who go through *gehinnom* and emerge.

Beis Shammai's framework is thus essentially one of strict *din*: the *tzadikim* earn eternal life, the *resha'im* are condemned, and the *beinonim* must undergo a purgative process before they can merit *chayei olam*. Even Beis Shammai acknowledges some mechanism for the *beinonim* to ultimately attain reward, but it comes only after punishment.

### Beis Hillel Compassion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: ״וְרַב חֶסֶד״ — מַטֶּה כְּלַפֵּי חֶסֶד. וַעֲלֵיהֶם אָמַר דָּוִד: ״אָהַבְתִּי כִּי יִשְׁמַע ה׳ אֶת קוֹלִי״, וַעֲלֵיהֶם אָמַר דָּוִד כׇּל הַפָּרָשָׁה כּוּלָּהּ — ״דַּלּוֹתִי וְלִי יְהוֹשִׁיעַ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Beit Hillel say:** He Who is **"and abundant in kindness"** (Exodus 34:6) **tilts** the scales **in favor of kindness,** so that middling people should not have to pass through Gehenna. **And about them, David said: "I love the Lord, Who hears my voice** and my supplications" (Psalms 116:1). **And about them, David said the entire passage: "I was brought low [*daloti*] and He saved me"** (Psalms 116:6). Although they are poor [*dalim*] in mitzvot, God saves them.

Beis Hillel takes a more compassionate position: *v'rav chesed mateh klapei chesed* — the One who is great in *chesed* will tip the scales toward *chesed*. For Beis Hillel, at least some of the ordinary *beinonim* are spared the need for purification altogether — God leans in their favor from the outset. Regarding them David says *ahavti ki yishma Hashem et koli* — I rejoice that God has heard my supplication. Tosafot notes that the fuller context of that psalm includes *afafuni chevlei mavet*, the bonds of death surrounded me, and *daloti v'li yehoshia*, I was poor and God saved me — meaning even one who was undeserving, even one who was deserving of death, God ultimately had compassion and saved. Another reading of *daloti*: the root *dal* means to draw up, as in drawing water — God lifted this person up even from the depths of their deserved fate. That is the *rav chesed mateh klapei chesed*.

### Minim and Apikorsim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הַמִּינִין וְהַמָּסוֹרוֹת וְהָאֶפִּיקוֹרְסִים שֶׁכָּפְרוּ בַּתּוֹרָה, וְשֶׁכָּפְרוּ בִּתְחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים, וְשֶׁפֵּירְשׁוּ מִדַּרְכֵי צִבּוּר, וְשֶׁנָּתְנוּ חִיתִּיתָם בְּאֶרֶץ חַיִּים, וְשֶׁחָטְאוּ וְהֶחְטִיאוּ אֶת הָרַבִּים, כְּגוֹן יָרׇבְעָם בֶּן נְבָט וַחֲבֵירָיו — יוֹרְדִין לְגֵיהִנָּם וְנִידּוֹנִין בָּהּ לְדוֹרֵי דּוֹרוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְיָצְאוּ וְרָאוּ בְּפִגְרֵי הָאֲנָשִׁים הַפּוֹשְׁעִים בִּי וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **But the heretics; and the informers; and the apostates [*apikorsim*];** and those **who denied the Torah; and** those who **denied the resurrection of the dead; and** those who **separated from the ways of** the Jewish **community** and refused to share the suffering; **and** those **who cast their fear over the land of the living; and** those who **sinned and caused the masses to sin, for example, Jeroboam, son of Nebat, and his company;** all of these people **descend to Gehenna and are judged there for generations and generations, as it is stated: "And they shall go forth, and look upon the carcasses of the men that have rebelled against Me;** for their worm shall not die; neither shall their fire be quenched; and they shall be an abhorrence to all flesh" (Isaiah 66:24).

Having established the three basic groups, the Gemara turns to those who deserve special — and especially severe — treatment: *haminim v'hamasorot v'ha'apikorsim* — the heretics, the informers, and the *apikorsim* — along with *she'kafru baTorah* (those who denied the Torah), *she'kafru bit'chiyat hameitim* (those who denied resurrection), *she'pirshu midarchei tzibur* (those who separated themselves from the ways of the community), *she'natnu chititam b'eretz chayim* (those who put their fear upon the living — meaning those who exercised oppressive authority over others), and *she'chat'u v'hichti'u et harabim* — those who sinned and caused the masses to sin — with Yerovam ben Nevat and his colleagues cited as the paradigmatic examples.

Rashi illuminates these categories: *minim* are those who perverted the words of the living God to produce sectarian movements — the *Tzadukim* and *Baitosim* — while *apikoros*, as the Gemara in Sanhedrin defines it, means one who denigrates *talmidei chachamim*, thereby undermining rabbinic authority. The *masorot* — informers — correspond to those who separate from the ways of the community. What is striking in Rashi's readings is that the emphasis falls not on private heterodoxy but on the public dimension: *chat'u v'hichti'u et harabim*, leading the masses astray. It is not merely that a person holds incorrect views privately, but that they corrode the community's relationship with Torah and its leadership.

### Eternal Gehennom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גֵּיהִנָּם כָּלֶה וְהֵן אֵינָן כָּלִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְצוּרָם לְבַלּוֹת שְׁאוֹל מִזְּבוּל לוֹ״. וְכׇל כָּךְ לָמָּה — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁפָּשְׁטוּ יְדֵיהֶם בִּזְבוּל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִזְּבוּל לוֹ״, וְאֵין ״זְבוּל״ אֶלָּא בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בָּנֹה בָנִיתִי בֵּית זְבוּל לָךְ״, וַעֲלֵיהֶם אָמְרָה חַנָּה: ״ה׳ יֵחַתּוּ מְרִיבָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** **Gehenna will terminate, but they** still **will not terminate, as it is stated: "And their form shall wear away the netherworld, so that there be no dwelling for Him"** (Psalms 49:15); that is to say, Gehenna itself will be worn away before their punishment has come to an end. **And why** are they punished **so severely? Because they stretched out their hands against** God's **dwelling,** the Temple, and everything else that is sanctified, **as it is stated: "So that there be no dwelling [*zevul*] for Him." Dwelling [*zevul*] is referring** here **only to the Temple, as it is stated: "I have built You a house for dwelling [*zevul*] in"** (I Kings 8:13). **And about them Hannah said: "The adversaries of the Lord shall be broken in pieces"** (I Samuel 2:10).

These figures *yardu l'gehinnom v'nidonim bah l'dorot dorot* — go down to *gehinnom* and are judged there for generations upon generations. After twelve months, ordinary severe sinners have their bodies destroyed and their souls burned; but for this category there is no terminus. *Gehinnom kala v'hein einam kalin* — even *gehinnom* itself will come to an end, but their punishment will not. The verse: *v'tzuram l'valot she'ol* — their form will wear out the underworld, meaning they will outlast *gehinnom* itself, which is categorically not a good thing. The verse continues: *mizvul lo*, which refers to the Temple — *v'ein z'vul ela Beis Hamikdash*, as in *bano baniti beit z'vul lach*. Their punishment is thus tied to having, through their sins, stretched their hand against the *Beis Hamikdash* — which Rashi interprets as meaning that their conduct caused the destruction of the Temple, with Yerovam ben Nevat serving as the archetypal figure. *Va'alehem amrah Chanah: Hashem yeichatu m'rivav* — those who war against God shall tremble.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר אָבִין: וּפְנֵיהֶם דּוֹמִין לְשׁוּלֵי קְדֵירָה. וְאָמַר רָבָא: וְאִינְהוּ מִשַּׁפִּירֵי שַׁפִּירֵי בְּנֵי מָחוֹזָא, וּמִקַּרְיִין ״בְּנֵי גֵיהִנָּם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Avin said: And their faces** on the Day of Judgment **will be** black and sooty **like the bottom of a pot. And Rava said: And they shall include the most handsome,** i.e., upstanding, **of the people of Meḥoza,** as Rava thought that even the most upstanding people of the city of Meḥoza were wicked, **and they shall be called the people of Gehenna.**

Rava adds a sharp and pointed observation: *v'inhu mishapirei shapirei b'nei Mechuza* — and these people are none other than the finest of the fine of Mechuza, the most cosmopolitan and wealthy of his own city. Rava had a well-documented critical relationship with the affluent populace of Mechuza, and here he is unambiguous: *v'kari lehu b'nei gehinnom*.

## Poshei Yisrael

### Poshei Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פּוֹשְׁעֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּגוּפָן, וּפוֹשְׁעֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם בְּגוּפָן — יוֹרְדִין לְגֵיהִנָּם וְנִידּוֹנִין בָּהּ שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **The rebellious Jews** who have sinned **with their bodies and** also **the rebellious** people **of the nations of the world** who have sinned **with their bodies descend to Gehenna and are judged there for twelve months.**

The Gemara returns to clarify the structure of Beis Hillel's position. *U'Beis Hillel omrim v'rav chesed mateh klapei chesed* — but what about the verse that says the third group is brought into fire? *Hatam b'poshei Yisrael begufan* — that verse refers specifically to *poshei Yisrael begufan*, Jewish sinners with their very bodies, not to ordinary *beinonim*. The normal *beinonim* God tips in favor of compassion; those who carry *poshei Yisrael begufan* sins must be purified. The distinction is thus one of quality, not quantity. A *beinoni* who is fifty-fifty but whose sins include sins of this severe category must undergo the purgative process; a *beinoni* whose sins are merely ordinary receives God's compassion directly.

### Tefillin Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פּוֹשְׁעֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּגוּפָן, מַאי נִיהוּ? אָמַר רַב: קַרְקַפְתָּא דְּלָא מַנַּח תְּפִלִּין. פּוֹשְׁעֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם בְּגוּפָן — אָמַר רַב: בַּעֲבֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **The rebellious Jews** who have sinned **with their bodies, who are they? Rav said:** This is referring to **the skull that did not** ever **don phylacteries.** The Gemara asks further: **The rebellious** ones **of the nations of the world** who sin **with their bodies,** who are they? **Rav said:** They are those who engage **in the sin,** i.e., forbidden sexual relations.

*Poshei Yisrael begufan mai ninhu* — what exactly is this category? *Amar Rav: karkafta d'lo manach tefillin* — a head that never wore *tefillin*. This is a striking and perhaps surprising definition. One might have expected something far more grave. The significance, however, lies not merely in the neglect of a *mitzvah* but in what *tefillin* represents. Chazal teach *ki shem Hashem nikra alecha* — God's name is called upon you — *elu tefillin sheberosh*, this refers to the *tefillin* on one's head. Wearing *tefillin* is not simply an action performed with one's body; it is the very marking of one's body and personhood with a Jewish identity, with identification with Torah and with God. Unlike other *aveirot* — desecrating *Shabbat*, eating *treifos* — which are performed *b'gufa* but are nonetheless specific acts, not wearing *tefillin* is a statement about the identity of the person's very self. It is less like an action and more like rejecting the identity that should define one's being. In this sense it parallels, to some degree, the category of one who reversed his *bris milah* or never received one.

### Tosafot Qualifications

Tosafot, aware that in the period of the *Ba'alei HaTosafot* there were Jews who did not consistently wear *tefillin* or *mezuzah*, works carefully to limit the scope of this ruling. First, he cites the *Mechilta*: *haosek baTorah patur min hatefillin* — one who is engaged in Torah study is considered as if wearing *tefillin*, since *tefillin* embody the principle *l'ma'an tihye Torat Hashem b'ficha*. Learning Torah substitutes for the *mitzvah*. Second, Rabbeinu Tam specifies that the category of *poshei Yisrael begufan* applies only when the *mitzvah* is *bezuyah alav* — despised in one's eyes — as in *d'megunah alav retzuah shel tefillin sheberosh*, one who finds the very strap of *tefillin* repugnant. By contrast, one who refrains because he fears he cannot maintain the standard of *guf naki* required — as discussed in connection with Elisha Ba'al Knafayim in *Bameh Tomnin* — is not in this category. Finally, and most broadly, Tosafot notes: *v'chol hanei d'shmatin b'shelo asah teshuvah* — all of this applies only where the person did not do *teshuvah*. *Teshuvah* fixes it entirely.

### Abusive Leaders

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁנָּתְנוּ חִיתִּיתָם בְּאֶרֶץ חַיִּים — אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: זֶה פַּרְנָס הַמַּטִּיל אֵימָה יְתֵירָה עַל הַצִּבּוּר שֶׁלֹּא לְשֵׁם שָׁמַיִם. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כׇּל פַּרְנָס הַמַּטִּיל אֵימָה יְתֵירָה עַל הַצִּבּוּר שֶׁלֹּא לְשֵׁם שָׁמַיִם אֵינוֹ רוֹאֶה בֵּן תַּלְמִיד חָכָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לָכֵן יְרֵאוּהוּ אֲנָשִׁים לֹא יִרְאֶה כׇּל חַכְמֵי לֵב״.
>
> **Translation:** **And those who cast their fear over the land of the living,** who are they? **Rav Ḥisda said: This is referring to** a communal **leader [*parnas*] who casts excessive fear on the community not for the sake of Heaven. Rav Yehuda said that Rav said: Any** community **leader who casts excessive fear on the community not for the sake of Heaven will** be punished and **not see any Torah scholar among his sons, as it is stated: "Men do therefore fear him; he sees not any who are wise of heart"** (Job 37:24). One who brings others to fear him will not merit having wise-hearted people in his family.

*She'natnu chititam b'eretz chayim* — those who instilled fear in the land of the living. Rav Chisda identifies this as *parnas hamatil eimah yeteirah al hatzibur shelo l'shem shamayim* — a communal leader who imposes excessive fear upon the community not for the sake of Heaven. Exerting authority and inspiring appropriate reverence may at times be legitimate, but doing so for personal aggrandizement rather than for Heaven's sake places one in the company of the gravest sinners. Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav extends this: any such leader will not see a child who is a *talmid chacham* — derived from *lo yireh kol chachmei lev*. This consequence, while far less severe than the fiery punishments described earlier, is significant in its own right.

## Chesed and Measure

### Kovsho v'Nosei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: ״וְרַב חֶסֶד״ — מַטֶּה כְּלַפֵּי חֶסֶד. הֵיכִי עָבֵיד? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: כּוֹבְשׁוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יָשׁוּב יְרַחֲמֵנוּ יִכְבּוֹשׁ עֲוֹנוֹתֵינוּ״. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא אָמַר: נוֹשֵׂא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נוֹשֵׂא עָוֹן וְעוֹבֵר עַל פֶּשַׁע״.
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the *baraita*: **Beit Hillel say:** He who is **"and abundant in kindness"** (Exodus 34:6) **tilts** the scales **in favor of kindness,** so that middling people will not have to pass through Gehenna. The Gemara asks: **How does He do** this? **Rabbi Eliezer says: He pushes down** on the side of the merits, tipping the scale in their favor, **as it is stated: "He will again have compassion upon us; He will push down our iniquities"** (Micah 7:19). **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina said: He bears,** i.e., raises, the side of the sins, **as it is stated: "He bears [*noseh*] sin and forgives transgression"** (Micah 7:18).

Returning to Beis Hillel's position, the Gemara asks: *heichi avid* — how exactly does God tip the scales toward *chesed*? Two approaches are given. Rabbi Eliezer says *kovsho* — God submerges or suppresses the sins — from *yashuv yerachamenu yikhbosh avonoteinu*, God will suppress our sins. Rabbi Yose bar Hanina says *nosei* — God lifts them off — from *nosei avon v'over al pesha*, bearing sin and forgoing transgression. The precise distinction between these two modes of divine compassion is subtle. One reading is that *kovsho* acknowledges the sin but brackets it — the sin is not erased but the person is spared punishment. *Nosei* is more thoroughgoing — the sin is actually lifted off, carried away, as though relieved from the person's burden. Rashi frames it through the image of scales: *kovsho* is to press down on the side of the *mitzvot* so that they outweigh the sins; *nosei* is to lift the sins themselves so they do not weigh down the scale. Rashi's metaphor is apt but, as the Gemara notes, it is his own elaboration rather than the language of the text itself.

### Ma'avir Rishon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: מַעֲבִיר רִאשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן, וְכֵן הִיא הַמִּדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** A Sage **from the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** in a *baraita*: **He overlooks** each and every **first** transgression, **and that is the attribute** of mercy, that God forgives one's first sin, and therefore He tips the scale in favor of the merits.

A *Tanna devei Rabbi Yishmael* introduces the concept of *ma'avir rishon rishon* — God passes over the first sin, overlooking it, and this is the *middah*. The word *middah* here carries a double resonance: on the one hand it means the measure or attribute by which God acts; on the other, *middah* implies precision, exact calculation — and there is something quietly ironic in declaring that the precise measure is to forego precision. One reading connects *v'chen hi hamiddah* to the *Yud Gimmel Middot* of *rachamim*, making *ma'avir rishon rishon* itself one of the attributes of divine mercy. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וְעָוֹן עַצְמוֹ אֵינוֹ נִמְחָק. דְּאִי אִיכָּא רוּבָּא עֲוֹנוֹת — מִחֲשִׁיב בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: But** that **sin itself,** which God overlooks, **is not** completely **erased; if** the individual's actions are still **mostly sins,** God **counts** the overlooked sin **with them** and metes out punishment accordingly.

Rava adds an important qualification: *veavon atzmo eino nimchak* — even when God passes over the first sin, it is not erased. If in the end the person accumulates a majority of sins, that forgiven sin gets added back to the ledger and counted against them.

### Forgiveness Reciprocal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: כׇּל הַמַּעֲבִיר עַל מִדּוֹתָיו — מַעֲבִירִין לוֹ עַל כׇּל פְּשָׁעָיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נוֹשֵׂא עָוֹן וְעוֹבֵר עַל פֶּשַׁע״. לְמִי נוֹשֵׂא עָוֹן — לְמִי שֶׁעוֹבֵר עַל פֶּשַׁע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava** understood this verse differently and **said:** With regard to **whoever forgoes his reckonings** with others for injustices done to him, the heavenly court in turn **forgoes** punishment **for all his sins, as it is stated: "He bears sin and forgives transgression"** (Micah 7:18). **Whose sins does He bear?** The sins **of one who forgoes his reckonings** with others for injustices committed against him.

Rava also derives a principle of *middah keneged middah* in the realm of compassion: *kol hama'avir al middotav* — whoever is willing to forgo their own precise accounting, who does not demand exact judgment against those who wrong them, and instead says *mochel*, I forgive — God will respond in kind. *Nosei avon v'over al pesha: lemi nosei avon? L'mi she'over al pesha* — for whom does God bear sin? For the one who himself passes over sins done against him. The reciprocity is direct and explicit: our willingness to forgive other human beings becomes the very basis for God's willingness to forgive us.

### Rav Huna Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ חֲלַשׁ, עָל רַב פָּפָּא לְשַׁיּוֹלֵי בֵּיהּ. חַזְיֵיהּ דַּחֲלִישׁ לֵיהּ עָלְמָא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: צְבִיתוּ לֵיהּ זְוַודְתָּא. לְסוֹף אִיתְּפַח, הֲוָה מִיכְּסִיף רַב פָּפָּא לְמִיחְזְיֵיהּ. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מַאי חֲזֵית? אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִין, הָכִי הֲוָה, וַאֲמַר לְהוּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: הוֹאִיל וְלָא מוֹקֵים בְּמִילֵּיהּ — לָא תְּקוּמוּ בַּהֲדֵיהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נוֹשֵׂא עָוֹן וְעוֹבֵר עַל פֶּשַׁע״, לְמִי נוֹשֵׂא עָוֹן — לְעוֹבֵר פֶּשַׁע.
>
> **Translation:** It is related that **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, became sick,** and **Rav Pappa went into** his home to **inquire about his** well-being. **He saw that the world was growing weak for** Rav Huna, i.e., he was dying. Rav Pappa **said to** his attendants: **Prepare his provisions [*zavdata*],** i.e., his shrouds. **In the end,** Rav Huna **recovered. Rav Pappa was embarrassed to** go and **see him,** as it seemed as if he had decreed Rav Huna's death. Rav Huna's friends **said to him: What did you see** when you were lying there suspended between life and death? **He said to them: Yes, it was so,** I was truly close to dying, **but the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to** the heavenly court: **Since he does not stand on his rights,** i.e., he is ready to waive what is due him, **you** too **should not be exacting** with him in his judgment, **as it is stated: "He bears [*noseh*] sin and forgives transgression." Whose sins does He bear?** The sins **of one who forgoes his reckonings** with others for injustices committed against him.

This principle finds dramatic confirmation in the story of Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua, who became gravely ill. Rav Pappa came to visit and, seeing that Rav Huna's condition was dire, instructed those present to prepare *zavda* — an accompanying care package, meaning *tachrichim*, burial shrouds — for the journey ahead. In the end, Rav Huna recovered. Rav Pappa, understandably embarrassed, eventually encountered Rav Huna and asked: *mai chazit* — what did you see, what transpired at the threshold of death? Rav Huna answered: yes, the decree against me was as you saw. But God said to the heavenly court: *hoil v'lo mokim bemileih* — since he does not stand upon his own accounting, since he is willing to forgo things done against him — *lo takumu bahadei* — we too should not be exacting against him. The heavenly judgment was reversed on the grounds that Rav Huna did not demand that others be held to precise account for wrongs done to him. This is not mere *drasha* but a report of what was actually witnessed in the heavenly tribunal.

### She'erit Nachalato

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לִשְׁאֵרִית נַחֲלָתוֹ״, אָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר חֲנִינָא: אַלְיָה — וְקוֹץ בָּהּ: ״לִשְׁאֵרִית נַחֲלָתוֹ״ — וְלֹא לְכׇל נַחֲלָתוֹ, לְמִי שֶׁמֵּשִׂים עַצְמוֹ כְּשִׁירַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** That same verse continues: "He bears sins and forgives transgression **for the remnant of His inheritance"** (Micah 7:18). **Rav Aḥa bar Ḥanina said:** This is like the **fat tail that has a thorn in it,** i.e., something good that contains something bad. God forgives and pardons only **"for the remnant of His inheritance," but not for all His inheritance.** That is to say, God forgives sins only **for one who regards himself as a remainder,** i.e., of only secondary importance.

The verse cited — *nosei avon v'over al pesha lish'erit nachalato* — to the remnant of His heritage — contains a qualification. Rabbi Acha bar Chanina notes: *alyah vekotz bah* — this is like a fatty tail, delicious but with a thorn. The promise that God forgives is real, but it is not universal. *Lish'erit nachalato v'lo lechol nachalato* — God forgives the remnant of His inheritance, not the entirety. Who is *she'erit nachalato*? *Lemi shemesim atzmo keshirayim* — those who make themselves like leftovers, like the residue, meaning those who are humble and do not assert themselves with haughtiness. Only such people will receive this divine forgiveness.

## Justice vs. Compassion

### Tzaddik and Chasid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא רָמֵי, כְּתִיב: ״צַדִּיק ה׳ בְּכׇל דְּרָכָיו״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְחָסִיד בְּכׇל מַעֲשָׂיו״! בַּתְּחִלָּה — צַדִּיק, וּלְבַסּוֹף — חָסִיד.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Huna raised a contradiction** between the two halves of a verse. **It is written: "The Lord is righteous [*tzaddik*] in all His ways"** (Psalms 145:17), indicating that God acts in accordance with the attribute of strict justice [*tzedek*], **and** then **it is written** in the same verse: **"And kind [*ḥasid*] in all His works,"** implying that He acts with grace and loving-kindness [*ḥesed*], going beyond the letter of the law. Rav Huna explained: **Initially,** at the time of judgment, **He is righteous, but in the end,** at the time of punishment, **He is gracious.**

Rav Huna identifies a scriptural tension: *tzaddik Hashem bechol derachav* — God is a *tzaddik* — and *chasid bechol ma'asav* — God is a *chasid*. Is He exacting or compassionate? The resolution: *betchilah tzaddik* — initially, God seeks to judge with precise justice — *ulevasof chasid* — but when justice alone would condemn too many, He turns to compassion.

### Chesed and Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר רָמֵי, כְּתִיב: ״וּלְךָ ה׳ חָסֶד״, וּכְתִיב: ״כִּי אַתָּה תְשַׁלֵּם לְאִישׁ כְּמַעֲשֵׂהוּ״. בַּתְּחִלָּה — ״כִּי אַתָּה תְּשַׁלֵּם כְּמַעֲשֵׂהוּ״, וּלְבַסּוֹף — ״וּלְךָ ה׳ חָסֶד״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar raised a** similar **contradiction: It is written: "But to you, O Lord, belongs kindness"** (Psalms 62:13), implying that God acts beyond the letter of the law, **and** then **it is written** in the same verse: **"For You render to a man according to his deeds,"** implying that He rewards and punishes measure for measure. Rabbi Elazar answered: **Initially,** at the time of judgment: **"For You render to a man according to his deeds"; but in the end,** at the time of punishment: **"But to You, O Lord, belongs kindness."**

Rabbi Eliezer identifies a parallel tension: *velecha Hashem chesed* — You are compassionate — versus *ki atah tishalem le'ish kema'aseihu* — You compensate each person precisely according to their deeds. Again, the resolution runs in the same direction: *betchilah ki atah tishalem kema'aseihu*, God first applies the precise measure of justice; *ulevasof velecha Hashem chesed*, when justice threatens to overwhelm, compassion takes over.

### Rav Chesed v'Emet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילְפַי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אִילְפָא רָמֵי, כְּתִיב: ״וְרַב חֶסֶד״, וּכְתִיב: ״וֶאֱמֶת״. בַּתְּחִלָּה — ״וֶאֱמֶת״, וּלְבַסּוֹף — ״וְרַב חֶסֶד״.
>
> **Translation:** **Ilfai, and some say** it was the Sage **Ilfa,** also **raised a contradiction: It is written** in the list of God's attributes: **"And abundant in kindness"** (Exodus 34:6), **and it is written** in the same verse: **"And truth,"** which implies the attribute of justice. He answered: **Initially,** at the time of judgment: **"And truth,"** i.e., God employs strict justice, **but in the end,** when He sees that the world cannot survive on judgment based only on truth and justice: **"And abundant in kindness,"** i.e., He is merciful.

Ilfi — or some say Ilfa — raises a further contradiction: *v'rav chesed* — God is great in compassion — and *v'emet* — God is truth. Compassion or truth? *Betchilah b'emet*, God begins with truth; *ulevasof rav chesed*, He ends with great compassion. One might object that in the verse itself, *chesed* precedes *emet*. Perhaps that ordering is itself the message: God recognizes from the outset that truth alone will be insufficient, that the eventual recourse to *chesed* is already anticipated at the moment of judgment. The commitment to truth is genuine but is framed from the beginning within the larger horizon of compassion.

## Yud Gimmel Middot

### Moshe's Lesson

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיַּעֲבוֹר ה׳ עַל פָּנָיו וַיִּקְרָא״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אִלְמָלֵא מִקְרָא כָּתוּב, אִי אֶפְשָׁר לְאוֹמְרוֹ. מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנִּתְעַטֵּף הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא כִּשְׁלִיחַ צִבּוּר, וְהֶרְאָה לוֹ לְמֹשֶׁה סֵדֶר תְּפִלָּה. אָמַר לוֹ: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל חוֹטְאִין — יַעֲשׂוּ לְפָנַי כַּסֵּדֶר הַזֶּה וַאֲנִי מוֹחֵל לָהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** § The verse states: **"And the Lord passed by before him, and proclaimed"** (Exodus 34:6). **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Were it not** explicitly **written in the verse, it would be impossible to say this,** as it would be insulting to God's honor. The verse **teaches that the Holy One, Blessed be He, wrapped Himself** in a prayer shawl **like a prayer leader and showed Moses** the structure of the **order of** the **prayer. He said to him: Whenever the Jewish people sin, let them act before Me in accordance with this order.** Let the prayer leader wrap himself in a prayer shawl and publicly recite the thirteen attributes of mercy, **and I will forgive them.**

The Gemara now arrives at the *Yud Gimmel Middot* themselves. The verse: *vayavor Hashem al panav vayikra* — and the Lord passed by before him and proclaimed. Rabbi Yochanan teaches — and prefaces with *ilmalei mikra katuv iy efshar l'omro*, were it not written in the verse one could not say such a thing — that God wrapped Himself like a *shliach tzibbur* and showed Moshe the *seder tefillah*, the order of prayer. The language of *vayavor* evokes the rabbinic phrase *over lifnei hatevah* — passing before the ark to lead the congregation — and the Gemara reads God's act of passing before Moshe as precisely this: demonstrating how to lead in prayer, how to invoke divine compassion. God said to Moshe: whenever the Jewish people sin, let them perform this rite before Me — the recitation of *Hashem, Hashem* — and I will forgive them. This is the foundation for the centrality of the *Yud Gimmel Middot* throughout *selichot* and the *Aseret Yemei Teshuvah*.

### Compassion Beforehand

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״ה׳ ה׳״ — אֲנִי הוּא קוֹדֶם שֶׁיֶּחְטָא הָאָדָם, וַאֲנִי הוּא לְאַחַר שֶׁיֶּחְטָא הָאָדָם וְיַעֲשֶׂה תְּשׁוּבָה — ״אֵל רַחוּם וְחַנּוּן״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse continues: **"The Lord, the Lord,"** and it should be understood as follows: **I am He before a person sins, and I am He after a person sins and performs repentance,** as God does not recall for him his first sins, since He is always **"God, merciful and gracious"** (Exodus 34:6).

The Gemara elaborates on the opening words: *Hashem, Hashem* — *ani hu kodem sheyecheta adam v'ani hu l'achar sheyecheta adam v'ya'aseh teshuvah* — I am God before a person sins, and I am God after a person sins and does *teshuvah*. The repetition of the divine name thus encodes two temporal dimensions of God's compassion: a compassion that precedes and sustains even before sin occurs, and a compassion that awaits and receives the sinner after the fact, sustaining them until they do *teshuvah*.

### Brit Middot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: בְּרִית כְּרוּתָה לִשְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה מִדּוֹת שֶׁאֵינָן חוֹזְרוֹת רֵיקָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הִנֵּה אָנֹכִי כּוֹרֵת בְּרִית״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said: A covenant was made with the thirteen attributes that they will not return empty-handed,** meaning that if one mentions them, he will certainly be answered, **as it is stated** in this regard: **"Behold, I make a covenant"** (Exodus 34:10).

Rav Yehuda teaches: *berit kerutah l'Yud Gimmel Middot she'einam chozrot reikam* — there is a covenant forged with the thirteen *middot* such that whoever recites them will not return empty-handed. The verse: *hineh anochi korit berit* — behold I am making a covenant — which follows the revelation of the *Yud Gimmel Middot*, confirms that the covenant is bound to those very attributes. Their recitation is therefore not merely liturgical expression but the activation of a *berit*, a covenantal commitment by God Himself to receive Israel's prayer with compassion. Tosafot undertakes an extended discussion of exactly how to count thirteen from the words of the verse, a question also debated among the *Ge'onim* and *rishonim* — how each word or phrase maps onto one of the thirteen attributes — but the Gemara itself here does not enumerate them individually, passing directly to the teaching of the *berit* that guarantees their efficacy.
