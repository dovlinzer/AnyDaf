# Avodah Zarah 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three Days & Sacrifices

### Intro & Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לֹא יִשְׁתַּתְּפוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל עִם הַגּוֹיִם בִּשְׁלוֹשַׁת יָמִים לִפְנֵי אֵידֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Jews may not engage in business transactions with gentiles** for **three days before their festivals.**

The present daf picks up at the bottom of 5b, returning to the *mishna* that prohibits conducting business with pagans for three days before their holidays. Having concluded the opening *aggadata*, the *gemara* now seeks to understand the source and rationale for the specific number of three days. Notably, rather than appealing to empirical evidence about how pagans actually prepared for their festivals, the *gemara* situates the analysis within familiar rabbinic frameworks — drawing a comparison either to Jewish practice or to the laws of *bnei Noach*.

The *gemara* observes an apparent inconsistency: for Jews, the preparatory period before a holiday varies widely — sometimes one day, sometimes thirty. Why specifically three days? The answer offered is that for *bnei Noach*, whose sacrificial animals need only be inspected for a missing limb (rather than subjected to the full range of blemish-checks required for Jewish *korbanos*), three days is a sufficient lead-up period. *Rashi* acknowledges the implicit tension here: the *gemara* seems to assume that the pagan practices of the *gemara*'s era are in some sense a continuation of the *bnei Noach* framework — that whatever vestiges of those ancient norms survived informed the contemporary three-day period.

### Two Frameworks

Before proceeding to the *gemara*'s technical derivation, it is important to hold in mind a foundational question about what the prohibition is actually about. The entire discussion of sacrificial preparation might suggest that the core problem is that commercial dealings assist pagans in preparing for their sacrifices — whether by supplying animals or other accoutrements of worship. This is one coherent framework: the prohibition is sacrifice-focused.

However, *Rashi* had already introduced a very different framework at the outset, on the *mishna* itself: any business interaction generates goodwill and satisfaction, and that heightened sense of joy and gratitude will lead the pagan to give thanks to his god on his holiday. On this account, the connection to sacrifices is not constitutive of the prohibition — rather, it is simply that the three-day preparatory window for sacrifices defines the relevant period, such that any business conducted during that window becomes colored by the approaching holiday and risks generating *azil u'modeh* — thanksgiving to the pagan deity.

*Tosafot* attempts to reconcile these two frameworks by explaining that although the *gemara* foregrounds the sacrifice-preparation period, the underlying concern need not be limited to assisting with sacrifices. Rather, the three-day sacrifice-preparation window establishes this as the heightened period before the holiday, during which any commercial interaction is likely to translate into religious gratitude. On this reading, the sacrifice focus explains the *duration* of the prohibition, not its *content*. Both frameworks will prove important as the *gemara* develops.

### Defects in Sacrifices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁמְּחֻסַּר אֵבָר אָסוּר לִבְנֵי נֹחַ? דַּרְשִׁינַן: ״וּמִכׇּל הַחַי מִכׇּל בָּשָׂר שְׁנַיִם מִכׇּל״, וְקָתָנֵי לִשְׁנַיִם מִכׇּל הַחַי — מִלּוּ שֶׁחַיִּים רָאשִׁים שֶׁל אֵבָרֶיהָ שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם עַיִן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses **the** source for the law **that an animal lacking a limb is prohibited for descendants of Noah.** How do we know this? **We expound:** "And from all living things, from all flesh, two of each" (Genesis 7:15). The verse **teaches with regard to the two of each** that they must be **from all living things,** meaning **the animal must have all its** [the animal's] **limbs alive and present, such that** there is no missing **limb.**

The *gemara* grounds the *bnei Noach* sacrificial standard in a *derasha* attributed to Rabbi Elazar. The verse in the context of Noach reads: *u'mikol hachai mikol basar shnayim mikol* — "and from all living things, from all flesh, two of each." Rabbi Elazar derives from the phrase *mikol hachai* — "from all the living" — that the animal brought must be one in which all the limbs are alive and present. *Amar Torah: havei behema she'chayin rashei eivareha shelo* — the Torah is saying the animal must have all its limbs intact. Hence, *mechusar eivar*, an animal missing a limb, is invalid for a *bnei Noach* sacrifice.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמָא מִשּׁוּם דִּתְרֵיפָה קָתָנֵי? תְּרֵיפָה מִנַּיִן? דַּכְתִּיב: ״לְהַחֲיוֹת זֶרַע״, תְּרֵיפָה דִּי לֹא מִתְחַיַּת מַמָּשׁ, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי לָא לַחֲיוֹת זֶרַע. הִלְכָּךְ מִלִּשְׁנַיִם מִכׇּל הַחַי, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges **this derivation: Perhaps the phrase** "from all living things" **is needed** not to exclude a *mechusar eivar*, **but rather to exclude a *tereifa*** — an animal with a fatal internal injury. **From where** is **the exclusion of *tereifa* derived?** The Gemara responds: **As it is written: "To keep seed alive,"** and since **a *tereifa*, which cannot** possibly **survive,** cannot produce offspring, it **is excluded from** the purpose of **"to keep seed alive. Therefore,** from the phrase "two of each from all living things," **conclude from** this that the animal must have all its limbs.

The *gemara* then challenges this derivation: perhaps *mikol hachai* is needed not to exclude a *mechusar eivar* but rather to exclude a *tereifa* — an animal with a fatal internal injury? The *gemara* responds: the exclusion of *tereifa* is derived separately, from the end of the verse, *l'hachayot zera* — "to keep seed alive" — since a *tereifa*, which cannot survive the year, presumably cannot bear offspring. Having thus derived the *tereifa* exclusion from *l'hachayot zera*, the phrase *mikol hachai* is freed up to teach that all limbs must be present.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְדִיעוֹת הַנּוֹטְלוֹת תְּרֵיפָה יוֹלֶדֶת, מַאי עוֹבַדִּין? אִם כֵּן, מִשּׁוּם דִּתְרֵיפָה קָתָנֵי! אָמַר: "אִתָּךְ" בִּדְמוּיָא לָךְ. אָמַר קְרָא: בִּדְמוּיָא לְךָ, אֵלּוּ הַבְּהֵמוֹת שֶׁתִּהְיוּ דּוֹמוֹת לְךָ, וּתְרֵיפָה לָא דּוֹמָא לְךָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara immediately complicates this **by noting that it works well only according to the view that a *tereifa* cannot give birth. For on that view,** "to keep seed alive" **excludes *tereifa*, and** "from all living things" **is available to exclude** an animal missing a limb. **But according to the view that a *tereifa* can in fact give birth, what then?** The Gemara answers **by invoking a different phrase from the passage: With you** — "**with you**" — meaning **in similarity to you.** The Torah **says: The animals brought must be similar to you, i.e.,** Noach himself. **This phrase excludes *tereifa*,** since **a *tereifa* is not similar to you,** Noach.

The *gemara* immediately complicates this by noting that it works well only according to the view that a *tereifa* cannot give birth (*tereifa eina yoledet*). For on that view, *l'hachayot zera* excludes *tereifa*, and *mikol hachai* is available to exclude *mechusar eivar*. But according to the view that a *tereifa* can in fact give birth (*tereifa yoledet*), what then? If *l'hachayot zera* cannot exclude *tereifa*, we would need *mikol hachai* for that exclusion — and would have no verse remaining to exclude *mechusar eivar*.

The *gemara* answers by invoking a different phrase from the passage: *itcha* — "with you." *Amar kra itcha bi-domim lach* — the Torah says the animals brought must be similar to you, i.e., Noach himself. This phrase excludes *tereifa* regardless of whether a *tereifa* can bear offspring. But then, one might ask: if *itcha* excludes *tereifa*, and *l'hachayot zera* establishes the purpose of propagation — what remains for *mikol hachai*? The answer is: *mikol hachai* is now available to exclude *mechusar eivar*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמָא נוֹחַ גּוּפֵיהּ תְּרֵיפָה הָוָה! אָמַר: תָּמִים כְּתִיב. וּשְׁמָא תָּמִים בִּדְרָכָיו? צַדִּיק כְּתִיב! הִלְכָּךְ תָּמִים מִגּוּפוּ.
>
> **Translation:** A further challenge arises: **Perhaps Noach himself was a *tereifa*, in which case the comparison would not exclude a *tereifa* animal at all.** The Gemara dismisses this: ***Tamim* is written** — the text describes Noach as *tamim*, complete, unblemished. **But perhaps *tamim* refers to completeness in his ways,** referring to moral perfection rather than physical wholeness? ***Tzadik* is written** — the text also calls him *tzadik*, righteous, **which already covers moral uprightness.** The distinction is illuminating: *tzadik* denotes upright conduct, while *tamim* denotes an integrated, unblemished character — not always the same thing. **Therefore *tamim* must refer to physical wholeness** of his **body.**

A further challenge arises: perhaps Noach himself was a *tereifa*, in which case the comparison *itcha* would not exclude a *tereifa* animal at all. The *gemara* dismisses this: *tamim ksiv* — the text describes Noach as *tamim*, complete, unblemished. But perhaps *tamim b'drachav* — complete in his ways, referring to moral perfection rather than physical wholeness? *Tzadik ksiv* — the text also calls him *tzadik*, righteous, which already covers moral uprightness. The distinction is illuminating: *tzadik* denotes upright conduct, while *tamim* denotes an integrated, unblemished character — not always the same thing. Therefore *tamim* must refer to physical wholeness, not moral rectitude.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמָא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא רָצָה בִּתְרֵיפוֹת! לוֹ סַלְקָא דַּעְתְּךָ דְּנוֹחַ גּוּפֵיהּ תְּרֵיפָה הָוָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds a final dismissal: **Perhaps the Almighty wanted** Noach to bring *tereifa* animals? This reasoning cannot be correct, for **it cannot enter your mind that Noach himself was a *tereifa*.** If Noach were a *tereifa*, why would the Almighty instruct him to bring animals similar to himself? That would imply God specifically wanted *tereifa* animals brought. The only coherent reading is that the Torah's comparison to Noach was intended to ensure the animals were *not* defective — and therefore Noach himself must have been unblemished.

The *gemara* adds a final dismissal: the very suggestion that Noach was a *tereifa* is untenable. *Lo salka datech d'Noach gufei tereifa havae* — for if Noach were a *tereifa*, why would the Almighty instruct him to bring animals similar to himself? That would imply God specifically wanted *tereifa* animals brought. The only coherent reading is that the Torah's comparison to Noach was intended to ensure the animals were *not* defective — and therefore Noach himself must have been unblemished.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי "אִתָּךְ" אוּקְמִי לִתְרֵיפָה, "לְהַחֲיוֹת זֶרַע" לְמַאי? אֲמַר: כְּדִי שֶׁלֹּא תֹאמַר: מִשּׁוּם שֶׁרִצָּה בִּשְׁכִיחוּתָן, הָיוּ בָּאִים, אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: "לְהַחֲיוֹת זֶרַע" — בִּשְׁבִיל זֶרַע בָּאִים.
>
> **Translation:** Finally, the Gemara asks: **If "with you" excludes *tereifa*, why does the Torah need "to keep seed alive" at all?** The Gemara answers: **Without "to keep seed alive," one might have thought** that the animals were brought merely for Noach's companionship during the long months on the ark — **in which case even elderly or sterile animals would have sufficed.** The verse **"to keep seed alive" clarifies** that **the purpose was propagation** — the animals needed to be capable of bearing offspring after the flood. The result of the full *derasha* is thus: *itcha* — not a *tereifa*; *l'hachayot zera* — capable of reproduction; *mikol hachai* — no missing limb. And this standard explains why three days suffices for *bnei Noach* sacrifice-preparation.

Finally, the *gemara* asks: if *itcha* excludes *tereifa*, why does the Torah need *l'hachayot zera* at all? The *gemara* answers: without *l'hachayot zera*, one might have thought the animals were brought merely for Noach's companionship during the long months on the ark — in which case even elderly or sterile animals (*zaken* and *saris*) would have sufficed. The verse *l'hachayot zera* clarifies that the purpose was propagation — the animals needed to be capable of bearing offspring after the flood. The result of the full *derasha* is thus: *itcha* — not a *tereifa*; *l'hachayot zera* — capable of reproduction; *mikol hachai* — no missing limb. And this standard explains why three days suffices for *bnei Noach* sacrifice-preparation.

## Three Days Count

### Inclusive or Exclusive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה לִפְנֵיהֶם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה לְאַחֲרֵיהֶם אָסוּר. וְלֵית הָכָא טִינוּף, דִּלְמָא הֵן וְאֵידֵיהֶן?
>
> **Translation:** **Three days before and three days after the holiday are forbidden.** But the issue arising is as follows: **And there is no** apparent **problem here, or** perhaps **the three days include the holidays themselves?**

The *gemara* now turns to a different question: when the *mishna* says three days before their holidays are forbidden, does the count of three include the holiday day itself (*heim v'eideihen*), or does it mean three days in addition to the holiday day (*heim b'lo eideihen*)? While the word *lifnei* — "before" — might seem to settle this at once, the *gemara* pursues the question through several steps.

### Yishmael's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב תַּחְלִיפָא בַּר אַבְדִּימִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: נוֹצְרִי, לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל לְעוֹלָם אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Taḥlifa bar Avdimi says** that **Shmuel says:** With regard to **a Christian, according to the statement of Rabbi Yishmael it is always prohibited** for a Jew to engage in business with him. Since his festival takes place every Sunday and the three days before and after Sunday constitute the entire week, one cannot engage in business with a Christian on any day of the week.

The first source adduced is a *braisa* in which Rabbi Yishmael states: *shlosha lifneihem u'shlosha achareihem assur* — three days before and three days after the holiday are forbidden. The *gemara* observes: if the count of three included the holiday day itself, then Rabbi Yishmael would be counting the holiday day twice — once as the third day before the holiday and again as the first day after. This would be a strange way to legislate. The *gemara* acknowledges this awkwardness but deflects it: since Rabbi Yishmael already stated "three before," the formulation demanded stylistic symmetry by also saying "three after," even if this technically results in double-counting. Despite this deflection, the underlying logic clearly favors the reading that *three* means three days not including the holiday itself.

The *gemara* then cites the ruling of Rav Tachlifa bar Avdimi in the name of Shmuel: *yom echad l'divrei Rabbi Yishmael l'olam assur* — one particular group, according to Rabbi Yishmael, is always forbidden for business dealings. The original *girsa* of the text names *Notzrim* — Christians. Since their weekly holy day is Sunday, applying Rabbi Yishmael's formula of three days before and three days after yields a prohibition covering every day of the week: Thursday, Friday, and Saturday before Sunday; Sunday itself; and Monday, Tuesday, and Wednesday after. There is accordingly never a permissible day for commercial dealings with Christians.

### Christian Holidays

This passage is of considerable historical and legal significance. It represents one of the clearest statements in the *gemara* that Christianity is categorized as *avodah zarah* — idolatrous worship — and that its practitioners are subject to the full prohibitions of the tractate. Nearly all *Rishonim* take this classification for granted, though it is striking that few of them explicitly cite this *gemara* as their proof-text; the assumption was evidently so self-evident as to require no demonstration. The *Rambam* likewise rules accordingly, though in censored editions the reference to Christians (*Notzrim*) was replaced with more neutral language.

The sole major dissent among the *Rishonim* is that of the *Meiri*, who maintains that Christianity does not fall within the category of *avodah zarah*. Confronted with this *gemara*, the *Meiri* proposes a striking alternative reading: the word *Notzri* in the text does not refer to Christians at all, but rather to the people of *Nevuchadnezzar* — Babylonians — who worshiped the sun. Since sun-worshipers would naturally celebrate on Sunday, the prohibition would similarly apply every week, but the referent would be entirely different. It is true that in the *gemara* elsewhere, Jewish Christians are typically designated as *minim* rather than *Notzrim*, which lends some surface plausibility to the *Meiri*'s reading — though it remains a minority position.

*Tosafot* notes an additional *chiddush* in Shmuel's ruling. One might have thought that a holiday occurring every week — like Sunday — would not rise to the level of a significant enough *eid* to trigger the three-day prohibition, just as *Shabbat* is not treated as a pagan festival. Shmuel's statement teaches that even a weekly observance constitutes a genuine holiday for purposes of this prohibition.

The question naturally arises: what *tikrovet* — what sacrificial preparation — is there for Christians on a Sunday? If the entire rationale for the three-day period is anchored in sacrifice-preparation, what sacrificial activity precedes Sunday worship? *Tosafot* addresses this by noting that Christians' offerings are monetary — a point that then opens a further question about whether Rabbi Yochanan's position elsewhere about monetary offerings might complicate matters. The suggestion of *Rabbeinu Tam* — that commercial dealings with Christians could be permitted — involves framing Christianity as a religion without genuine sacrifices. How *Rabbeinu Tam* would reconcile that with Rabbi Yishmael's explicit statement here remains a question worth examining, though a full resolution is beyond the scope of the present discussion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חָנָן בַּר רָבָא: קָלֶנְדָּא — שְׁמוֹנָה יָמִים אַחַר תְּקוּפָה, סְטַרְנוּרָא — שְׁמוֹנָה יָמִים לִפְנֵי תְּקוּפָה, וְסִימָנָךְ — ״אָחוֹר וָקֶדֶם צַרְתָּנִי וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥanan bar Rava says:** When are these festivals celebrated? **Kalenda** is celebrated during the **eight days after** the winter **solstice,** and **Saturnalia** is celebrated during the **eight days before** the winter **solstice. And your mnemonic** to remember which festival is that the one that occurs after the solstice is mentioned first in the mishna, and the festival that takes place before the solstice is mentioned after, as in the verse: **"You have hemmed me in behind and before,** and laid Your Hand upon me" (Psalms 139:5), where the word "before" appears after the term "behind."**

The *gemara* also addresses the broader structure of the *eidei ha'kochavim* — pagan holidays — by noting the following: Rabbi Chanan bar Rava states that *Kalanda* falls eight days after the winter solstice (*tekufah*), and *Saturnayas* (Saturnalia) falls eight days before the winter solstice. These are multi-day festivals spanning, respectively, the period leading into and immediately following the winter solstice, with a small gap between them. The *gemara* notes that the *mishna* mentions them in reverse chronological order — *Kalanda* first, then Saturnalia — and offers a mnemonic: *achor vakedem tzartani*, "after and before You have created me," from Psalms — a phrase that also evokes the primordial human experience of watching the days shorten and then lengthen, a theme the *gemara* elsewhere associates with the origin of these festivals.

This context also serves the ongoing question of whether *three* includes the holiday day itself. If the holiday of *Kalanda* spans eight days, and the word *three* in the *mishna* counted the holiday itself as one of the three, then the *mishna* should have said *ten* days are forbidden — eight festival days plus two additional preparatory days. Since the *mishna* says three, the *gemara* entertains the possibility that it collapses the entire festival into a single unit and counts that unit as one day. This response is acknowledged as somewhat strained, and the discussion continues.

### Lifnei Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: "לִפְנֵי אֵידֵיהֶן שֶׁל עוֹבְדֵי כׇּכְבִּים שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים" — שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים לִפְנֵי אֵידוֹ, וְאִם כֵּן, הֵם וְלֹא אֵידוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi resolves the question** definitively **by reading the *mishna* itself closely. The *mishna* states: "Before the holidays of idol worshipers, three days."** The word *lifnei* — "before" — **places the holiday to one side and then counts three days. The three days are thus explicitly prior to, and not inclusive of, the holiday. Had the count included the holiday, the *mishna* would more naturally have stated:** "their holidays, three days," **a formulation that could plausibly mean a three-day period centered on or including the holiday itself. The use of *lifnei* as a predicate makes the exclusion of the holiday day unambiguous. *Shema mina: heim v'lo eideihen* — the three days do not include the holiday day.**

Rav Ashi resolves the question definitively by reading the *mishna* itself closely. The *mishna* states: *lifnei eydeihen shel ovdei kochavim shlosha yamim* — "before the holidays of idol worshipers, three days." The word *lifnei* — "before" — places the holiday to one side and then counts three days. The three days are thus explicitly prior to, and not inclusive of, the holiday. Had the count included the holiday, the *mishna* would more naturally have stated: *eydeihen shel ovdei kochavim shlosha yamim* — "their holidays, three days" — a formulation that could plausibly mean a three-day period centered on or including the holiday itself. The use of *lifnei* as a predicate makes the exclusion of the holiday day unambiguous. *Shema mina: heim v'lo eideihen* — the three days do not include the holiday day. This is the *gemara*'s final conclusion.

## Core Dispute

### Harvacha vs. Lifnei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיסְתַּבְּרָא לִי דִּמְנַחְמְנָא בְּהָנְיָא הוּא, אֲבָל לִיתָן לוֹ בְּשָׁעַת הִשְׁתַּדְּלוּתוֹ — מִיתָּסֵר מִשּׁוּם לִפְנֵי עִוֵּר. דְּאִי אִית לֵיהּ בְּהֵמָה לְדִידֵיהּ, אִם מִשּׁוּם הַרְוָוּחַ — אִיכָּא, אִם מִשּׁוּם לִפְנֵי עִוֵּר — לֵית לֵיהּ צֹרֶךְ בִּידָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes **what** fundamental concern **motivates the prohibition.** One possibility is suggested: It seems to me that the concern about **providing the pagan with abundance** is the issue — the Jew provides the pagan with an abundance of resources that fuels his holiday enthusiasm. **But when it comes to giving him something at the moment of his** effort and work — this would be **prohibited due to** the biblical prohibition against placing **a stumbling block before the blind.** **The** difference would be evident in a case **where** the pagan **already has an animal for himself. If the concern is** about providing him with **abundance, there is a problem** — by providing another animal, the Jew adds to that abundance. **If the concern is** the biblical prohibition against **placing a stumbling block before the blind, there is no prohibition**, as **he has no** need **of your** involvement.

The *gemara* now arrives at what is arguably the central analytical question underlying the entire chapter: what, precisely, is the nature of the prohibition against conducting business in the days before a pagan holiday? Two possibilities are offered. The first is *harvacha* — the concern that commercial dealings provide the pagan with an abundance of resources or an expanded sense of wealth that fuels his holiday enthusiasm. The second is *lifnei iver lo titein michshol* — the prohibition against placing a stumbling block before the blind, i.e., that by providing the pagan with goods or money, one enables or facilitates the idolatrous act of sacrifice.

The *nafka mina* — practical difference — between these two rationales centers on a case where the pagan already possesses an animal: *d'eis lei behema ledidei*. Note again the explicit framing in terms of animals, consistent with the sacrifice-centered perspective running through the preceding discussion. If the concern is *harvacha*, there is a problem — by selling him yet another animal, one expands his resources further. But if the concern is *lifnei iver*, there may be no violation — since the pagan could have sacrificed his own animal without any assistance, he does not require the Jew's involvement. *Ha'is lei ledidei* — he already has it himself.

At this stage, the two frameworks can be understood as two degrees of enabling. *Lifnei iver* in its strictest sense requires that the pagan could not have committed the transgression without the Jew's help — the Jew was indispensable. *Harvacha*, on this reading, captures a broader form of facilitation: even if the pagan could have sinned without the Jew's involvement, the Jew has made it easier, more likely, or more expansive. The Jew has, in effect, opened up opportunities, removed hesitation, and enabled a more robust act of worship. Both accounts, on this reading, are still firmly anchored in the sacrifice context.

*Rashi*, however, reads the passage differently. On *Rashi*'s reading, *harvacha* does not mean making sacrificial opportunities more expansive. Rather, *de'ravach ve'azil u'modeh le'avodat kochavim* — through the profit or increase generated by the transaction, the pagan goes and gives thanks to his idol. And the *issur* triggered is *lo yishama al picha* — "the name of other gods shall not be heard through your mouth" — meaning that by causing the pagan to invoke his deity's name in a religious context of thanksgiving, the Jew violates the biblical prohibition against causing the name of an alien deity to be heard through one's agency. On *Rashi*'s reading, then, the concern is no longer primarily about sacrifice at all, but about generating the gratitude that produces religious invocation. This is a significantly broader reading, and it anticipates the *azil u'modeh* framework that will come to dominate the *gemara*'s conclusion.

### Lifnei Iver Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁלֹּא יוֹשִׁיט אָדָם כּוֹס שֶׁל יַיִן לְנָזִיר, וְאֵבֶר מִן הַחַי לִבְנֵי נֹחַ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְלִפְנֵי עִוֵּר לֹא תִתֵּן מִכְשֹׁל״; וְהָא הָכָא, דְּכִי לָא יָהֲבִינַן לֵיהּ, שָׁקְלִי אִיהוּ, וְקָעָבַר מִשּׁוּם ״לִפְנֵי עִוֵּר לֹא תִתֵּן מִכְשֹׁל״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara then asks **a pointed question: is it really true that if the pagan already possesses an animal, the Jew does not transgress *lifnei iver*?** The *gemara* **invokes a *braisa* in the name of Rabbi Natan: How do we know that one may not hand a *nazir* a cup of wine, or serve a limb torn from a living animal to a *ben Noach*? The verse states: "You shall not put a stumbling block before the blind" (Leviticus 19:14).** **Now, in the case of the *nazir*, surely he could simply take the wine himself** — **and yet Rabbi Natan teaches that handing it to him transgresses *lifnei iver*.** This appears to undercut the *gemara*'s assumption that *lifnei iver* requires the pagan to be unable to access the forbidden item without the Jew's help.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? דְּקָאֵי בִּתְרֵי עֶבְרֵי נַהֲרָא. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי ״לֹא יוֹשִׁיט״ וְלָא קָתָנֵי ״לֹא יִתֵּן״, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where they are standing on** the **two sides** of **a river,** and therefore the recipient could not have taken it himself. Since his help was instrumental, the one who conveyed the item has violated the prohibition of putting a stumbling block before the blind. The Gemara adds: The language of the *baraita* **is also precise, as it teaches:** A person **may not extend, and it does not teach: One may not give. Learn from** the usage of the term extend that the *baraita* is referring to one located on one side of a river, who extends the item to the one on the other side.

The *gemara* resolves this through the foundational principle of *trei avrei d'nahara* — two banks of a river. *Lifnei iver* is violated only when the one who is being assisted is on the opposite bank of the river and truly cannot reach the item without the other's help. The proof that Rabbi Natan's *braisa* refers to such a case lies in the precise language used: the verse says *lo yoshit* — do not extend, do not reach out — rather than simply *lo yitein*, do not give. The verb *yoshit* implies a stretching across a divide, indicating that the prohibition applies only where the recipient genuinely cannot access the item independently.

This principle carries far-reaching implications. The mere fact that a particular transaction assists someone in sinning does not automatically constitute *lifnei iver* — only where the Jew's involvement is truly necessary does the biblical prohibition apply. If the pagan could acquire the item elsewhere, or could accomplish the forbidden act through his own means, the Jew's involvement does not create *lifnei iver* liability. This immediately generates a famous question in the *Rishonim*: can a Jew sell objects of worship to a pagan if another Jew competitor would sell them anyway? Each seller might argue that *trei avrei d'nahara* is inapplicable — the pagan does not need this particular Jew, since the other Jew stands ready to supply him. The *Rishonim* engage at length with such scenarios.

*Tosafot* extends the *trei avrei d'nahara* principle to a further case: what if a *nazir* asks a Jew to hand him his *own* cup of wine, a cup that already belongs to the *nazir*? If the *nazir* cannot reach it himself, handing it over would still violate *lifnei iver*, even though the *nazir* owns the item. *Tosafot* then applies this to the case of Jewish apostates who have lapsed into idolatry: if such a person asks a Jew to hand over something the apostate already owns, and the apostate cannot access it independently, the Jew may be constrained by *lifnei iver*. The critical variables in each case are whether the potential transgressor could have acted without the Jew's assistance, and whether the context creates a reasonable presumption that the item will be used for a prohibited purpose.

The *gemara* does not resolve the original question — whether the prohibition is *harvacha* or *lifnei iver* — at this stage. What has been established is that *lifnei iver* applies only in *trei avrei d'nahara* scenarios, while *harvacha* covers a broader range of facilitation. The broader debate is deferred while the *gemara* shifts to a related discussion.

### Durable Goods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לֹא אָסְרוּ אֶלָּא בְּדָבָר הַמִּתְקַיֵּים, אֲבָל בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ מִתְקַיֵּים — לֹא, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּדָבָר הַמִּתְקַיֵּים נָשָׂא וְנָתַן — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** A **_braisa_** is introduced establishing that the three-day prohibition applies only to ***davar ha-mitkayem*** — **durable goods. Non-durable goods** (*davar she-eino mitkayem*) **may be sold to pagans even in the prohibited period.** Furthermore, ***Rashi*** explains the distinction: if the good is non-durable, **it will not still be present when the holiday arrives.** Since the concern is that the item will remind the pagan of the transaction on his holiday and thereby generate thanksgiving, a perishable item that will not survive until the holiday poses no such risk.

Non-durable goods (*davar she-eino mitkayem*) may be sold to pagans even in the prohibited period. *Rashi* explains the distinction: if the good is non-durable, it will not still be present when the holiday arrives. An alternative reading presents itself naturally: a non-durable transaction is simply not significant enough to generate religious gratitude. One does not give thanks to one's god for purchasing a good tomato. On *Rashi*'s reading, the critical variable is the item's presence on the holiday itself; on the alternative reading, it is the transaction's subjective weight and capacity to generate a sense of divine favor.

The distinction has a striking implication: the *braisa* also specifies that even for durable goods, if the transaction was already completed (*nasa v'natan mutar*), the Jew may retain the proceeds after the fact. This becomes relevant to the debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish: Rabbi Yochanan holds that a transaction completed in the prohibited period remains retroactively forbidden, while Resh Lakish holds it is permissible after the fact. The *braisa* limiting the prohibition to durable goods — and explicitly permitting retention of proceeds even for durable goods after the fact — serves as a source for Resh Lakish's position.

### Caesarian Coin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא מִינָאָה דְּשַׁדַּר לֵיהּ דִּינָרָא קֵיסָרְנָאָה לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה נְשִׂיאָה בְּיוֹם אֵידוֹ, הֲוָה יָתֵיב רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ קַמֵּיהּ. אֲמַר: הֵיכִי אֶעֱבֵיד? אֶשְׁקְלֵיהּ — אָזֵיל וּמוֹדֶה, לָא אֶשְׁקְלֵיהּ — הָוְיָא לֵיהּ אֵיבָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: טוֹל וּזְרוֹק אוֹתוֹ לְבוֹר בְּפָנָיו. אָמַר: כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן דְּהָוְיָא לֵיהּ אֵיבָה! כִּלְאַחַר יָד הוּא דְּקָאָמֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **There was an incident involving a certain heretic who sent a Caesarean dinar to Rabbi Yehuda Nesia on the day of** the heretic's **festival.** Rabbi Yehuda Nesia **said** to **Reish Lakish,** who **was sitting before him: What shall I do?** If **I take** the dinar, **he will go and thank** his idol for the success of his endeavor, but if **I do not take** the dinar, **he will harbor enmity** toward me. **Reish Lakish said to him: Take** it **and throw it into a pit in the presence of** the heretic. Rabbi Yehuda Nesia **said: All the more so,** this will cause **him to harbor enmity** toward me. Reish Lakish explained: **I said,** i.e., I meant, **that** you should throw it **in an unusual manner,** so that it looks as though the dinar inadvertently fell from your hand into the pit.

A practical episode is recounted in this context. A certain *mina* — the exact identification is uncertain; the term sometimes denotes a Jewish heretic and sometimes a pagan — sent a *dinar kaisrona*, a Caesarian coin, to Rabbi Yehudah Nesia on the occasion of the *mina*'s holiday. Gift-giving was part of the pagan holiday ritual, a kind of *mishloach manot* of the festival. Resh Lakish happened to be sitting before Rabbi Yehudah Nesia when the gift arrived. Rabbi Yehudah Nesia was uncertain what to do: should he accept it? If he did, the sender would *azil u'modeh* — go and give thanks to his god for the privilege of having his gift received by the distinguished Jewish *nasi*. Should he refuse it? That would generate *eivah* — animosity toward the Jewish community, a serious communal concern.

Resh Lakish offered a practical solution: accept the gift, and then arrange for it to fall down a drain or pit — but in a manner that appears accidental, *ke'man de'nafal* — as though it simply slipped. Rabbi Yehudah Nesia objected that this would be even worse — deliberately destroying a gift is more insulting than refusing it. Resh Lakish clarified: the drop must look genuinely unintentional. That way, the *mina* would have the satisfaction of seeing his gift accepted — and thus would not harbor *eivah* — but since nothing would remain of the gift, there would be no lasting occasion for *azil u'modeh*.

This episode carries several significant points. First, it explicitly introduces *eivah* — animosity — as a factor capable of modulating behavior in this domain, a consideration that features prominently in *Rishonim* discussions of leniency. *Tosafot* is careful to note, however, that the episode does not prove *eivah* would have been sufficient by itself to permit accepting the gift. Resh Lakish did not simply say "accept it because of *eivah*"; he devised a mechanism to neutralize the *azil u'modeh* concern while also addressing the *eivah* concern. The two were handled simultaneously.

Second, and more fundamentally, this episode marks a decisive shift in the *gemara*'s conceptual framework. The earlier discussion had been conducted in the vocabulary of *korbanos*, *lifnei iver*, and *kamarvach* — all of which could be read within a sacrifice-focused framework. Here, the concern is stated with unmistakable clarity as *azil u'modeh* — the pagan's going and giving thanks to his deity — with no reference to sacrifice whatsoever. The gift is a durable good — a coin — that, if retained, would stand as an ongoing source of gratitude and religious thanksgiving. This is why Resh Lakish's solution requires its elimination, not merely its acceptance.

## Mishna Cases

### Lending & Borrowing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְהַשְׁאִילָן וְלִשְׁאוֹל מֵהֶן כּוּ׳. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְהַשְׁאִילָן — דְּקָא מַרְוַוח לְהוּ, אֲבָל לִשְׁאוֹל מֵהֶן — מַעוֹטֵי קָא מְמַעֵט לְהוּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: גְּזֵרָה לִשְׁאוֹל מֵהֶן אַטּוּ לְהַשְׁאִילָן. רָבָא אָמַר: כּוּלָּהּ מִשּׁוּם דְּאָזֵיל וּמוֹדֶה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that **it is prohibited to lend them** items **and to borrow** items **from them** during the three days preceding their festivals. The Gemara asks: **Granted, it is prohibited to lend** the items **to them, as** this **causes them** to have **a profit. But** why is it prohibited **to borrow** the items **from them** during this period? Doesn't this serve to **reduce for them** the property they possess during the festival? **Abaye said:** The Sages issued **a decree** that it is prohibited **to borrow** the items **from them due to** the concern that he might come **to lend** the items **to them. Rava said: All of it,** lending and borrowing, **is** prohibited for the same reason, **as** in either situation the gentile might **go and give thanks** to his idol, as he will be pleased that the Jew was forced to borrow the items from him.

The *gemara* now works systematically through the specific cases enumerated in the *mishna*: lending objects, borrowing objects, lending money, borrowing money, paying a debt, and collecting a debt. The organizing question is whether each case is prohibited in its own right or only as an extension of the core prohibition.

The analysis begins with lending and borrowing objects. *Bishlama lehashilan* — it is straightforward why lending objects to a pagan is forbidden: *dekamravach lehu* — you are expanding their resources, providing them with increase. But *aval lish'ol mehen* — why is borrowing objects from them forbidden? Borrowing does the opposite of *harvacha*: it removes an item from their possession, causing *miuta* — a decrease rather than an increase.

Abaye answers: *gezeria lish'ol mehen atu lehashilan* — borrowing is forbidden only as a *gezeira*, a rabbinic fence, lest one come to lend. On Abaye's account, the prohibition against borrowing is entirely derivative of the prohibition against lending; borrowing has no independent foundation in the underlying rationale.

### Rava's Generic View

Rava rejects Abaye's *gezeira* framework and offers a unified account: *kulam mishum d'azil u'modei hu* — all the cases are forbidden because in every one of them, the pagan *azil u'modeh* — goes and gives thanks to his deity. On Rava's reading, it is not simply that the prohibited side of each pair generates increase and the other side is forbidden as a precaution. Rather, every form of commercial interaction — regardless of which party benefits materially — generates a sense of satisfaction and goodwill that leads the pagan to religious thanksgiving.

This is a fundamentally different and significantly more expansive conception of the prohibition. Rather than grounding the *issur* in the mechanics of increase and decrease, Rava roots it in the psychology of gratitude: any transaction that makes the pagan feel good about his relationship with a Jew, or about his material situation on the eve of his holiday, creates a risk of *azil u'modeh*. Why would borrowing objects from a pagan cause him to give thanks? Because *chashiva lei milta* — it is significant to him that this Jew needed to come to him. The pagan's sense of pride and status at being depended upon is itself a source of satisfaction that translates into holiday thanksgiving.

### Debt Transactions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְהַלְווֹתָם וְלִלְווֹת מֵהֶן. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְהַלְווֹתָם — מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא מַרְוַוח לְהוּ, אֶלָּא לִלְווֹת מֵהֶן — אַמַּאי? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: גְּזֵרָה לִלְווֹת מֵהֶן אַטּוּ לְהַלְווֹתָם. רָבָא אָמַר: כּוּלָּהּ מִשּׁוּם דְּאָזֵיל וּמוֹדֶה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna further teaches that it is prohibited **to lend** money **to them or to borrow** money **from them.** The Gemara asks: **Granted, it is prohibited to lend** money **to them, as** this **causes them** to have **a profit. But** if one wants **to borrow** money **from them, why** is it prohibited? **Abaye said:** The Sages issued **a decree** that it is prohibited **to borrow** money **from them, due to** the concern that he might come **to lend** money **to them. Rava said: All** of it, lending and borrowing money, is prohibited for the same reason, **as** in either situation the gentile **will go and give thanks** to his object of idol worship.

The same analytical framework is applied to the cases of paying and collecting debts. *Bishlama lifroa lahen* — it is understandable why paying a debt owed to a pagan is forbidden: *mishum d'kamravach lehu* — you are providing them increase, restoring their funds. But *ella lifroa mehen* — why is collecting a debt from them forbidden? When the pagan pays what he owes, he loses money: *mi'uta kamami'at lehu* — you are causing him a decrease.

Abaye again answers with a *gezeira*: collecting is forbidden lest one come to pay. Rava again invokes the universal principle: *kulam mishum d'azil u'modei hu*. Even when paying a debt, the pagan ultimately feels relief at having discharged his obligation, and that relief may generate its own form of gratitude. The *gemara* notes the force of this claim: one might think that the material loss of repaying a debt precludes any sense of thanksgiving — *tzara is lei v'lo azil u'modei*. This is precisely why the *gemara* states *u'tzricha* — all the cases need to be specified, because each one might individually have been thought to fall outside the prohibition on some independent ground.

### Unified Azil u'Modeh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכִי, דְּאִי תְּנָא לָשֵׂאת וְלָתֵת עִמָּהֶן, מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא מַרְוַוח לְהוּ וְאָזֵיל וּמוֹדֶה, אֲבָל לִשְׁאוֹל מֵהֶן, דְּמַעוֹטֵי קָא מְמַעֵט לְהוּ — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי. וְאִי תְּנָא לִשְׁאוֹל מֵהֶן, מִשּׁוּם דַּחֲשִׁיבָא לֵיהּ מִילְּתָא, וְאָזֵיל וּמוֹדֶה. אֲבָל לִלְווֹת מֵהֶן, צַעֲרָא בְּעָלְמָא אִית לֵיהּ, אָמַר: ״תּוּב לָא הָדְרִי זוּזֵי״. וְאִי תְּנָא לִלְווֹת מֵהֶן, מִשּׁוּם דְּקָאָמַר: ״בְּעַל כָּרְחֵיהּ מִיפְּרַעְנָא״, וְהַשְׁתָּא מִיהָא אָזֵיל וּמוֹדֶה, אֲבָל לִיפָּרַע מֵהֶן, דְּתוּ לָא הָדְרִי זוּזֵי, אֵימָא צַעֲרָא אִית לֵיהּ, וְלָא אָזֵיל וּמוֹדֶה — צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** all of the prohibitions listed in the mishna **are necessary. As, if** the mishna had **taught** only that it is prohibited **to engage with them in business,** one could have said that the reason for the prohibition is **because it causes** the gentile to have **a profit, and he will go and give thanks** to his idol. **But** with regard to **borrowing** items **from them, which** serves to **reduce for them** the property they possess during the festival, one may **well** do so. **And if** the mishna had further **taught** only that it is prohibited **to borrow** items **from them,** one might have thought that this is **because the matter is significant to** the gentile, as he is pleased that the Jew is forced to borrow items from him, **and** therefore he might **go and give thanks. But** it might have been supposed that **to borrow** money **from them** is permitted, as **there is only distress for** the gentile when he lends money, as he would **say:** My **money will not return to me again,** since the borrower may never repay the loan. **And if** the mishna had **taught** in addition only that it is prohibited **to borrow** money **from them,** one might have thought that this is **because** the gentile **says: I will** forcibly **collect payment** from the Jew **against his will,** by means of the promissory note, **and now in any event he** will **go and give thanks** that the Jew is forced to borrow money from him. **But** with regard to **collecting payment from them, as** this **money will never return to him again,** one might **say** that **he has distress** about paying back the debt, **and** he will **not go and give thanks.** Since one might have reached these conclusions, it is **necessary** for the mishna to state each ruling explicitly.

Having worked through all the cases, the *gemara* arrives at a synthesis. The separate frameworks that opened the discussion — *lifnei iver*, *harvacha*, the sacrifice-centered analysis — give way in the end to a single, encompassing rationale articulated by Rava: *azil u'modeh*. Every commercial interaction, whether it involves increase or decrease, whether the Jew is the giver or the taker, whether it involves objects or money or debts, carries the risk that the pagan will experience the transaction as an occasion for religious gratitude and will mark his holiday by giving thanks to his deity.

*Tosafot* attempts to preserve a role for Abaye's position by suggesting that even Abaye does not categorically deny any element of *azil u'modeh* in the derivative cases — the *gezeira* was legislated precisely because those cases carry some minimal risk of thanksgiving as well, even if it is not their primary characteristic. On this reading, the difference between Abaye and Rava is one of degree and framing: Abaye holds that the derivative cases are technically permissible in their own right and forbidden only as a precautionary extension, while Rava holds that every case is independently prohibited.

The conceptual journey of the *sugya* is striking. It begins with a tightly focused discussion about sacrifice preparation and the laws of *bnei Noach*, in which the prohibition appears rooted in concerns about enabling idolatrous *korbanos* — either through strict *lifnei iver* or through *harvacha* understood as expanding the pagan's sacrificial capacity. By the *sugya*'s conclusion, all of that has been subsumed into Rava's broader principle. The prohibition is not primarily about sacrifices, nor about the specific mechanics of enabling worship. It is about the social and religious dynamic of commercial interaction itself — the way in which any dealings that leave the pagan feeling good can become an occasion for invoking his deity's name in thanksgiving. That is *azil u'modeh*, and under Rava's reading, it is the *gemara*'s final and encompassing word on why business with pagans in the days before their holidays is forbidden.
