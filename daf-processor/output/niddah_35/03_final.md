# Niddah 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dam Tehorah Shemetzora'at

### Ma'ayanot and Av Hatumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּם טׇהֳרָה שֶׁל מְצוֹרַעַת, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: טְהוֹרָה, בִּלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא תִּהְיֶה רָטָּה. בֵּית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מְטַמְּאָה לַח וְיָבֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **blood** discharged **by** a female Jewish **leper** during the days **of purity** of a woman who gives birth, **Beit Shammai say: It is pure, provided that it is not moist.** In other words, the blood imparts impurity only while it is moist, but once it dries it is pure. **Beit Hillel say: It imparts impurity** whether it is **moist or dry.**

The daf opens, technically beginning partway through 34b, with a case whose very elegance lies in how precisely it isolates a difficult theoretical question about *tumah*. The governing principle is that the spit, urine, and certain other bodily fluids of a *zav*, *zavah*, and *niddah* carry the status of *av hatumah* — a primary source of impurity. The organizing concept underlying this category is *mit'agel veyotzei*: these are substances that collect within the body and then are expelled, a description that fits urine and saliva quite naturally.

The question arises: what of uterine blood? Ordinarily the question is moot, because uterine blood is itself the very basis of a woman's status as a *niddah* or *zavah*, and is therefore *tamei* by virtue of being the source of *tumah* rather than by virtue of being a bodily fluid. It does not obviously fall into the *mit'agel veyotzei* category — blood does not collect and pool in the way urine does — so under what circumstances could the question become live?

The *Mishnah* constructs precisely that scenario. Consider a woman who has just given birth and is now in her postpartum period — the thirty-three or sixty-six days following the initial week or two during which the blood is designated *dam tehorah*, pure blood. During this window the blood is halachically *tahor* and does not render her a *niddah* or *zavah*. If she has not yet immersed in a *mikvah* following childbirth, the *taharah* of the blood is also technically moot — she is already *tamei* as a *yoledes*. But add a further complication: she is a *metzora'at*, a woman afflicted with *tzara'at*. The halacha governing a *metzora* is that certain bodily fluids of one afflicted with *tzara'at* carry the status of *av hatumah*. The question is therefore this: does her uterine blood during this postpartum period of *dam tehorah* partake of the status of the *ma'ayanot* — the wellspring fluids — of a *metzora'at*?

### Beit Shammai vs. Beit Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: טְהוֹרָה, בִּלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא תִּהְיֶה רָטָּה. בֵּית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מְטַמְּאָה לַח וְיָבֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Beit Shammai say: It is pure, provided that it is not moist.** In other words, the blood imparts impurity only while it is moist, but once it dries it is pure. **Beit Hillel say: It imparts impurity** whether it is **moist or dry.**

*Beit Shammai* rules that this blood is *tahor* and does not have the status of a *metzora'at*'s bodily fluids. *Beit Hillel* rules that it is *tamei* — that the uterine blood does in fact carry the halachic status of such a fluid. The debate between them turns on how to read a verse in *Parashat Zav*.

### Drasha: Lazachar Velanekevah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּבֵית הִלֵּל? אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: ״לַזָּכָר״ — לְרַבּוֹת מְצוֹרָע לְמַעְיְנוֹתָיו, ״וְלַנְּקֵבָה״ — לְרַבּוֹת מְצוֹרַעַת לְמַעְיְנוֹתֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reasoning of Beit Hillel?** Rabbi Yitzḥak says: The verse states at the conclusion of the passage discussing impure individuals: "Whether it be a male or a female" (Leviticus 15:33). **"Whether it be a male"** serves **to include the sources** of bodily emissions **of a male leper,** teaching that they also impart impurity. Likewise, the phrase **"or a female"** serves **to include the sources** of bodily emissions **of a female leper,** teaching that they too impart impurity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַעְיְנוֹתֶיהָ? אִילֵּימָא שְׁאָר מַעְיְנוֹתֶיהָ — מִזָּכָר נָפְקָא! אֶלָּא לְדָמָהּ, לְטַמֵּא דַּם טׇהֳרָה שֶׁלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is meant by the **sources** of bodily emissions of a female leper? **If we say** that this is referring to **the rest of her sources,** e.g., her saliva and urine, this can be **derived from** the *halakha* of **a male** leper. **Rather,** it is referring **to her blood,** and the verse serves **to render impure** the **blood** of a leper discharged during the days of **her purity.**

The *Gemara* introduces the position of *Beit Hillel* via a *drasha* transmitted by *Rabbi Yitzchak*. At the conclusion of the *parashah* dealing with *zav* and *zavah*, the verse states: *"Hazav vezovo lazachar velanekevah ule'ish asher tetzei mimenu shichvat zara"* — this is the *tumah* of the flow, for the male and the female, and for a man from whom semen issues. The redundancy is striking: the entire preceding passage has been discussing men and women with flows, so why does the verse now add "for the male and for the female"? *Rabbi Yitzchak* teaches: *lazachar lerabot metzora lema'ayanotav* — the word "male" comes to include the *metzora*, that his bodily fluids likewise carry this elevated status of impurity. Similarly, *velanekevah lerabot metzora'at lema'ayanoteha* — "and female" comes to include the *metzora'at*, that her wellspring fluids are *tamei* as well.

But this still requires a further step to reach the blood. The *Gemara* presses: if *velanekevah* is coming to include the *metzora'at*'s fluids, is it teaching us about her ordinary fluids — spit and urine? That would be unnecessary, because once the *Torah* taught us this about the male *metzora*, we could infer it applies to the female as well. There is no obvious reason to distinguish between them with respect to saliva and urine. Therefore, *ela ledamah* — it must be that the word "female" comes specifically to address something uniquely female, namely her blood: *letammei dam tehorah shelah*, to render *tamei* even the blood that is halachically pure as to its *niddah*/*zavah* status, but which is now treated as the bodily fluid of a *metzora'at*.

### Beit Hillel Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל? לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא בִּנְקֵבָה וְלָא בָּעֵי זָכָר, וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא: וּמָה נְקֵבָה, שֶׁאֵינָהּ טְעוּנָה פְּרִיעָה וּפְרִימָה, וְאֵינָהּ אֲסוּרָה בְּתַשְׁמִישׁ הַמִּטָּה — רַבִּי רַחֲמָנָא מַעְיְנוֹתֶיהָ, זָכָר — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** **And** how do **Beit Hillel** respond? They respond as follows: If the verse serves to include only the saliva and urine of a female leper, then **let the Merciful One write** this *halakha* only **with regard to a female** leper. **And** there will be **no need** to write it with regard to **a male** leper, as **I will say** that the saliva and urine of a male leper are impure by an *a fortiori* inference: **And what, if with regard to a female** leper, **who is not required to let her hair grow and rend her garments, and** it **is not prohibited** for her **to** engage in **sexual intercourse, the Merciful One** nevertheless **includes her sources** of bodily emissions as impure, then in the case of **a male** leper, to whom all the aforementioned requirements apply, is it **not all the more so** that his emissions are impure?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן ״לְזָכָר״ — תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לִנְקֵבָה, וְאִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְמַעְיְנוֹתֶיהָ — תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לְדָמָהּ, לְטַמֵּא דַּם טׇהֳרָה שֶׁלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **if** the phrase "whether it be a male" **is not** needed for the **matter of a male** leper, as this *halakha* may be derived by an *a fortiori* inference, **apply it** to the **matter of a female** leper. **And if it is not** needed for the **matter of her** other **sources** of bodily emissions, as they are derived from the phrase "or a female," **apply it** to the **matter of her blood,** i.e., **to render impure** the **blood of her** days of **purity.**

*Beit Hillel*'s full argument unfolds as follows. The *Torah* could have stated the law of the *metzora'at*'s fluids with reference to the female alone. Had it done so, we could have inferred the male a fortiori: if even a female *metzora'at*, whose obligations of *tzara'at* practice are less severe than a male's — he must let his hair grow wild, rend his garments, and abstain from marital relations, none of which apply to her — even she has her fluids rendered *tamei*, is it not all the more so true of the male *metzora*? Therefore the verse did not need to state it by the male. And since the male formulation is unnecessary, we apply the hermeneutical principle *im eino inyan*: if it has nothing new to teach regarding the male *metzora*'s ordinary fluids, redirect it to teach something novel about the female — specifically, her uterine blood. *Uve'im eino inyan lema'ayanoteha*, and if it is also not needed to teach about her ordinary fluids since those could be derived from the male, redirect it further: *tnehu inyan ledam* — let it teach about her blood.

The underlying reason that blood requires special derivation is precisely because it is not naturally in the *mit'agel veyotzei* category — it is not a fluid that pools and is expelled in the manner of urine or saliva. *Beit Hillel* acknowledges this, but maintains that the extra word in the verse establishes it as though it were in that category.

### Beit Shammai Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי? נְקֵבָה מִזָּכָר לָא אָתְיָא, דְּאִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְזָכָר שֶׁכֵּן טָעוּן פְּרִיעָה וּפְרִימָה, וְאָסוּר בְּתַשְׁמִישׁ הַמִּטָּה, תֹּאמַר בַּנְּקֵבָה דְּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how do **Beit Shammai,** who maintain the blood is ritually pure, interpret the verse? They contend that the *halakha* with regard to the emissions of **a female** leper **cannot be derived from** that of **a male** leper, **as** such a comparison **can be refuted** as follows: **What** is unique **about a male** leper? He is unique **in that he is required to let his hair grow and rend his garments, and he is prohibited to** engage in **sexual intercourse.** Can **you say,** then, that the same *halakhot* should apply to the emissions **of a female** leper, **to** whom all the aforementioned requirements do **not** apply? Consequently, the verse is necessary to teach that the saliva and urine of a female leper are impure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל? לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא בִּנְקֵבָה וְלָא בָּעֵי זָכָר, וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא: וּמָה נְקֵבָה, שֶׁאֵינָהּ טְעוּנָה פְּרִיעָה וּפְרִימָה, וְאֵינָהּ אֲסוּרָה בְּתַשְׁמִישׁ הַמִּטָּה — רַבִּי רַחֲמָנָא מַעְיְנוֹתֶיהָ, זָכָר — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how do **Beit Hillel** respond? They respond as follows: If the verse serves to include only the saliva and urine of a female leper, then **let the Merciful One write** this *halakha* only **with regard to a female** leper. **And** there will be **no need** to write it with regard to **a male** leper, as **I will say** that the saliva and urine of a male leper are impure by an *a fortiori* inference: **And what, if with regard to a female** leper, **who is not required to let her hair grow and rend her garments, and** it **is not prohibited** for her **to** engage in **sexual intercourse, the Merciful One** nevertheless **includes her sources** of bodily emissions as impure, then in the case of **a male** leper, to whom all the aforementioned requirements apply, is it **not all the more so** that his emissions are impure?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן ״לְזָכָר״ — תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לִנְקֵבָה, וְאִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְמַעְיְנוֹתֶיהָ — תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לְדָמָהּ, לְטַמֵּא דַּם טׇהֳרָה שֶׁלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **if** the phrase "whether it be a male" **is not** needed for the **matter of a male** leper, as this *halakha* may be derived by an *a fortiori* inference, **apply it** to the **matter of a female** leper. **And if it is not** needed for the **matter of her** other **sources** of bodily emissions, as they are derived from the phrase "or a female," **apply it** to the **matter of her blood,** i.e., **to render impure** the **blood of her** days of **purity.**

*Beit Shammai* contests this entire edifice at multiple points. First, they argue that one cannot in fact dispense with *lazachar* by inferring the male from the female, because *de'ika lemifrach* — there is a valid counter-argument: the male *metzora* is in certain respects more stringent than the female, with his requirements of *pri'ah* and *frimah* (disheveled hair and torn garments) and his prohibition from marital relations. Since the female's *tzara'at* obligations are lighter, one might have thought that only she, with her more lenient status, needs a special verse — and therefore the verse about the male was indeed needed and cannot be redirected.

*Beit Hillel* responds: then reverse the argument. State it by the female and let me infer the male — since even the female, with her lighter *tzara'at* obligations, has her fluids rendered *tamei*, the male is surely included. So the verse about the male is indeed superfluous and can be redirected. *Beit Shammai* counters: one cannot so easily dismiss the male formulation, because the female has a stringency of her own in the laws of *zov* and *zava* — namely that a woman can become *tamei* as a *niddah* or *zavah* even when the flow arises from external stimulation (*ones*), whereas for a man, a flow caused by external stimulation does not make him a *zav*. Since the female category is in this respect weightier, you cannot automatically infer the male from the female. *Beit Hillel* rejoins: we are discussing the laws of a *metzora*, not the laws of *zav* and *zavah* — why should stringencies that belong to an entirely different domain of *tumah* affect the inference here? *Beit Shammai* maintains that once a category of *tumah* is in any way weightier, we cannot use it as the source for inference to another category.

Finally, *Beit Shammai* offers an alternative account of what *lazachar* comes to teach. Rather than redirecting the verse to include a new type of fluid, it teaches *kol she-hu zachar bein gadol bein katan* — any male, whether adult or minor. This is the source for the rule that even a one-day-old male can become a *zav*. *Beit Hillel*, by contrast, derives the inclusion of minors from the phrase *zot torat hazav* — "this is the law of the *zav*" — a broad encompassing statement. Since *Beit Hillel* learns the minor's status from *zot torat hazav*, the word *lazachar* remains available to be redirected to teach about the uterine blood of the *metzora'at*. The debate thus comes down, at its core, to competing uses of available textual surplus.

## Rav Yosef on Zav Katan

### Reiyah Rishonah Shel Zav Katan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: כִּי פָשֵׁיט רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ בְּזָב, בָּעֵי הָכִי: רְאִיָּיה רִאשׁוֹנָה שֶׁל זָב קָטָן מַהוּ שֶׁתְּטַמֵּא בְּמַגָּע?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef says: When Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish taught** the *halakhot* **of a *zav*, he raised this dilemma:** With regard to the **first sighting** of *ziva* **of a *zav*** who is a **minor, what is** the *halakha* **as to** whether **it imparts impurity through contact?**

The *Gemara* now shifts to a series of related conceptual questions — questions that are, as one might say, more structurally interesting than the *drasha* debate just concluded. *Rav Yosef* reports that whenever *Reish Lakish* would teach the laws of *zav*, he would invariably pose the following question: *re'iyah rishonah shel zav katan mahu shet'tamei bemaga* — does the first flow of a minor *zav* cause impurity through contact?

To appreciate the question, one must recall the basic framework. A man's penile discharge is classified as either *shichvat zera* (semen) or *zov* (the *zav* flow, a non-seminal discharge, pale in color and thinner than semen). Semen makes him a *ba'al keri*, a one-day *tumah*. The *zov* flow: once makes him like a *ba'al keri* in terms of *tumah* status; twice gives him a seven-day *tumah*; three times requires a *korban*. The critical halachic point is that after one *zov* flow, the *tumah* is halachically equivalent to that of *ba'al keri*.

Now the question: can a minor become a *ba'al keri*? No — the laws of *shichvat zera* are tied to an act of marital relations, which halachically is defined as applicable only from age nine and above. Below that age, semen does not generate *tumah*. So if the first *zov* flow carries the same *tumah* as *shichvat zera*, perhaps a minor's first flow also does not generate *tumah* — since he cannot become a *ba'al keri*, perhaps the same logic exempts him. The verse states *zot torat hazav asher tetzei mimenu shichvat zera* — "this is the law of the *zav* from whom semen issues" — from which it is derived: *kol sheshichvat zera shelo metamei re'iyah rishonah shelo metamei* — any person whose semen generates *tumah*, his first *zov* flow likewise generates *tumah*. But a minor's semen does not generate *tumah* — so perhaps his first *zov* flow likewise does not. On the other hand, perhaps we should not press the analogy so far: after all, if the minor has two flows, those will combine to make him a full *zav*. He is already in the process of becoming a *zav* — perhaps that is sufficient to establish that even the first flow generates *tumah*.

### Rava's Proof from Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: תָּא שְׁמַע, ״זֹאת תּוֹרַת הַזָּב״ — בֵּין גָּדוֹל בֵּין קָטָן, מָה גָּדוֹל — רְאִיָּיה רִאשׁוֹנָה שֶׁלּוֹ מְטַמְּאָה, אַף קָטָן — רְאִיָּיה רִאשׁוֹנָה נָמֵי מְטַמְּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: **"This is the law of the *zav*," whether** he is **an adult** or **whether** he is **a minor.** Since the verse equates an adult and a minor, **just as** with regard to **an adult his first sighting imparts impurity, so too,** with regard to **a minor,** his **first sighting imparts impurity as well.**

*Rava* resolves the question. The verse *zot torat hazav* is interpreted as a broad statement: *bein gadol bein katan* — whether adult or minor. The *drasha* from the *braita* is: just as an adult's first flow generates *tumah*, so too a minor's first flow generates *tumah*. The answer is thus clear: the analogy to *shichvat zera* does not override the independent rule that a minor can be a *zav*, and even his first flow makes him *tamei*.

### Rav Yosef on Metzora

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: רְאִיָּיה רִאשׁוֹנָה שֶׁל מְצוֹרָע מַהוּ שֶׁתְּטַמֵּא בְּמַשָּׂא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara earlier stated that the phrase "whether it be a male" serves to include the sources of bodily emissions of a male leper, teaching that they impart impurity. In this regard **Rav Yosef raises a dilemma:** With regard to the **first sighting** of *ziva* **of a leper, what is** the *halakha* **as to** whether **it imparts impurity** even **through carrying?**

Having resolved the question about the minor *zav*, *Rav Yosef* poses an analogous question — now about a *metzora*. This question will carry the discussion into the next section.

## Makom Ziva Ma'ayan

### Metzora's First Flow Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: רְאִיָּיה רִאשׁוֹנָה שֶׁל מְצוֹרָע מַהוּ שֶׁתְּטַמֵּא בְּמַשָּׂא? מְקוֹם זִיבָה מַעְיָן הוּא וּמְטַמֵּא, אוֹ דִילְמָא לָאו מַעְיָן הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara earlier stated that the phrase "whether it be a male" serves to include the sources of bodily emissions of a male leper, teaching that they impart impurity. In this regard **Rav Yosef raises a dilemma:** Granted, the first sighting of *ziva* of an otherwise ritually pure individual imparts impurity only through contact, as stated above, and only the second sighting imparts impurity through carrying. But with regard to the **first sighting** of *ziva* **of a leper, what is** the *halakha* **as to** whether **it imparts impurity** even **through carrying?** Does one say that the **place of *ziva*** in one's body **is** considered **a source, and** therefore his *ziva* **imparts impurity** through carrying, like all the sources of bodily emissions of a leper? **Or perhaps** the place of *ziva* **is not** considered **a source,** in which case the first sighting of *ziva* does not impart impurity through carrying.

The question posed is: *re'iyah rishonah shel metzora mahu shet'titma bemasa* — does the first *zov* flow of a *metzora* render *tamei* through carrying (*masa*)? The conceptual structure here is directly analogous to the question we explored regarding a woman's uterine blood. Normally, a man's *zov* flow poses no question about its fluid status: it is the source of *tumah*, making him a *zav* or at minimum a *ba'al keri*, so of course it is *tamei*. But if the man is already a *metzora*, the question becomes tractable: the *zov* flow does not meaningfully change his *tumah* status, since he is already *tamei* from *tzara'at*. It is at best making him a *ba'al keri* in addition to being a *metzora*, but it is not causing him to become a *zav*. So — is this first flow, which is not generating a new category of *tumah* for him, nevertheless considered one of the *ma'ayanot* of the *metzora*, such that it carries *tumah* through *masa*?

The distinction between *maga* (contact) and *masa* (carrying) matters critically here. The *tumah* of *ba'al keri* — and of the first *zov* flow, which is treated like *ba'al keri* — is transmitted by *maga*, not by *masa*. Only the second *zov* flow, which makes him a full *zav*, generates *tumah* through *masa*. The bodily fluids of a *metzora*, however, are *av hatumah* and carry *tumah* through *masa* as well. The question therefore is: does this first *zov* flow, sitting in the *ba'al keri* category for ordinary purposes, gain the more stringent status of a *metzora*'s *ma'ayan* and thereby generate *tumah* through *masa*?

### Rava: Zov Tamei Hu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: תָּא שְׁמַע, ״זוֹבוֹ טָמֵא הוּא״ — לִימֵּד עַל הַזּוֹב שֶׁהוּא טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: Come** and **hear** proof from a *baraita*: The verse states: "When any man has an issue out of his flesh, **his issue [*zovo*] is unclean"** (Leviticus 15:2). The verse **teaches with regard to the *zov* that it is impure,** and that it imparts impurity through carrying.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי? אִילֵימָא בְּזָב גְּרֵידָא — לַאֲחֵרִים גּוֹרֵם טוּמְאָה, לְעַצְמוֹ לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא בְּזָב מְצוֹרָע,
>
> **Translation:** **To what** case is the verse referring? **If we say** it is referring **to** one who is **just a *zav*** and not a leper as well, and the verse is teaching that any drop of *ziva* emitted by him imparts impurity through carrying, there is no need for the verse to teach this. After all, if this drop of *ziva* **causes impurity for others,** i.e., if the person emitting the drop imparts impurity through carrying, is it **not all the more so** that the drop **itself** imparts impurity through carrying? **Rather,** it is **obvious** that the verse is referring **to** a drop of *ziva* from **a *zav*** who is also **a leper.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִדְּאִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא לְרַבּוֹיֵי בִּרְאִיָּיה שְׁנִיָּה, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: מְקוֹם זִיבָה לָאו מַעְיָן הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava concludes: **And** as the verse mentions the word "issue" twice, it is evident that it is referring to a second sighting of *ziva*. **From** the fact that **a verse was necessary to include a second sighting** of *ziva* of a leper, teaching that his *ziva* imparts impurity through carrying, **conclude from it** that the **place of *ziva* is not** considered **a source.** If it were a source, then even the first sighting of *ziva* would impart impurity through carrying.

*Rava* attempts to resolve the question. The verse states *zovo tamei hu* — "his flow, it is *tamei*." The *Gemara* asks: about what case must the verse speak to tell us something non-obvious? If it is speaking about a simple *zav* whose second flow makes him a full *zav*, there is nothing to prove — of course a flow that makes someone a *zav* is itself *tamei*. *Ela peshita* — so the verse must be addressing the case of a *zav metzora*, where the flow's effect on his *tumah* status is relatively moot. And specifically, the *Gemara* argues, the verse must be speaking about the second *zov* flow of a *metzora* — because by the second flow he genuinely is becoming a *zav*, and yet one might have thought that since he is already *tamei* as a *metzora*, this second flow is also somehow not a true source of *tumah*; the verse therefore tells us it is.

The inference: since the verse was needed to establish that the *second* flow of a *metzora* is *tamei*, we can deduce that the *first* flow, about which no such verse was needed, is *not* considered a *ma'ayan* — *shema mina makom ziva lav ma'ayan hu*. The mere fact of being the site from which *zova* flows is insufficient. It is only when the flow genuinely constitutes a *zov* — i.e., the second flow making him a *zav* — that it has the status of a *ma'ayan*.

### Rav Yehudah's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב יְהוּדָה מִדִּסְקַרְתָּא לְרָבָא: מִמַּאי? דִּילְמָא לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ בְּזָב גְּרֵידָא, וּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ לַאֲחֵרִים גּוֹרֵם טוּמְאָה, לְעַצְמוֹ לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן — שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁגּוֹרֵם טוּמְאָה לַאֲחֵרִים, וְהוּא עַצְמוֹ טָהוֹר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda of Diskarta said to Rava: From where** do you know that the verse is referring to a *zav* who is also a leper? **Actually, perhaps I will say to you** that the verse is referring **to** the *ziva* of one who is **just a *zav*. And** as for **that** *a fortiori* inference **that you said:** If this drop of *ziva* **causes impurity for others,** is it **not all the more so** that the drop **itself** imparts impurity through carrying, one can counter that inference. The case of the **scapegoat** brought on Yom Kippur **will prove** that this *a fortiori* inference is not valid, **as it causes impurity to others,** since the dispatcher of the scapegoat is rendered ritually impure, **and** yet the goat **itself is pure,** as a living animal cannot be rendered impure.

*Rav Yehudah of Diskarta* challenges *Rava*'s reasoning. The entire proof rests on the claim that the verse *zovo tamei hu* could not be speaking about a simple *zav* whose second flow makes him a *zav*, because that would be self-evident — a flow that generates *zav* status is of course *tamei*. But is that self-evident? *Rav Yehudah* invokes the *seir hamishtaleach* — the goat sent to Azazel on Yom Kippur, which generates *tumah* in the one who sends it, and yet the goat itself remains *tahor*. This demonstrates that something can cause *tumah* in others without itself being a source of *tumah*. Therefore, it is not obvious that even a flow making a man a *zav* is itself *tamei* — and the verse may indeed have been needed for that ordinary case, leaving nothing to infer about the *metzora* case.

### Abaye's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מַאי תִּבְּעֵי לֵיהּ, וְהָא הוּא דְּאָמַר ״זֹאת תּוֹרַת הַזָּב״ — בֵּין גָּדוֹל בֵּין קָטָן, וְכֵיוָן דְּנָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵהָתָם, אִיַּיתַּר לֵיהּ ״לַזָּכָר״ לְרַבּוֹת מְצוֹרָע לְמַעְיְנוֹתָיו, (נקבה) [״וְלַנְּקֵבָה״] לְרַבּוֹת מְצוֹרַעַת לְמַעְיְנוֹתֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the dilemma raised by Rav Yosef about the first sighting of *ziva* of a leper, **Abaye said: What** is the reason **he raises** such **a dilemma? But it was he who said** that when the verse states: **"This is the law of the *zav*"** (Leviticus 15:32), it thereby teaches that the *halakhot* of a *zav* apply **whether** he is **an adult** or **whether** he is **a minor. And since he derives** this *halakha* **from there,** the verse: "And of them that have an issue [*vehazav*] of *ziva*, whether it be a male or a female" (Leviticus 15:33), **remains** available **for him** to derive as follows: **"Whether it be a male"** serves **to include a male leper** with regard **to his sources** of bodily emissions, and "or **a female"** serves **to include a female leper** with regard **to her sources** of bodily emissions.

*Abaye* then clarifies the logical structure of the proof. The key is that *Rav Yosef* himself — the one who posed the original question — holds that *lazachar* in the verse is the source for including the *metzora*'s fluids, and *velanekevah* for including the *metzora'at*'s fluids. This was established in the earlier debate between *Beit Hillel* and *Beit Shammai*. Having used *lazachar* for that purpose, it is further available to link the *metzora*'s fluids *lerabot she'af chumrei metzora lezav gamur* — to subject them to the same laws as the full *zav*'s flows.

### Metzora Linked to Zav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַקְּשֵׁיהּ רַחֲמָנָא מְצוֹרָע לְזָב גָּמוּר — מָה זָב גָּמוּר מְטַמֵּא בְּמַשָּׂא, אַף רְאִיָּיה רִאשׁוֹנָה שֶׁל מְצוֹרָע מְטַמֵּא בְּמַשָּׂא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** as this verse discusses a full-fledged *zav*, and the word "issue" is mentioned twice, **the Merciful One compares a leper to a full-fledged *zav*: Just as a full-fledged *zav* imparts impurity through carrying, so too,** the **first sighting** of *ziva* **of a leper imparts impurity through carrying.**

The verse's formulation *lazov et zovo lazachar velanekevah* links the fluids of the *metzora* to *lazov et zovo* — to the flows of a genuine *zav*. Just as the second flow of a full *zav* generates *tumah* through *masa*, so too the flows of a *metzora* generate *tumah* through *masa*. The question then is whether this linkage extends to the first flow, which in the ordinary context of a *zav* would not generate *tumah* by *masa* but only by *maga*.

### First Flow Not Ma'ayan

The conclusion that emerges is that the first flow of a *metzora* is not a *ma'ayan* — it does not generate *tumah* through *masa* — while the second flow does. The broader conceptual picture: the analysis of what constitutes a *ma'ayan* depends not merely on the anatomical source of the fluid but on whether in that instance the flow genuinely creates a new *tumah* category. Where it does, as with the second *zov* flow, it is indeed a *ma'ayan*; where it does not, as with the first, it is not.

## First Flow and Ones

### Reiyah Rishonah Be'ones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: רְאִיָּיה רִאשׁוֹנָה שֶׁל זָב מְטַמְּאָה בְּאוֹנֶס, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״זֹאת תּוֹרַת הַזָּב וַאֲשֶׁר תֵּצֵא מִמֶּנּוּ שִׁכְבַת זָרַע״, מָה שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע מְטַמֵּא בְּאוֹנֶס, אַף רְאִיָּיה רִאשׁוֹנָה שֶׁל זָב מְטַמְּאָה בְּאוֹנֶס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna says:** The **first sighting** of *ziva* **of a *zav* imparts ritual impurity** to one who comes into contact with it, even if the emission occurred **due to circumstances beyond** his **control, as it is stated: "This is the law of the *zav*, and of him from whom the flow of seed goes out"** (Leviticus 15:32). The verse compares the first sighting of *ziva* to a seminal emission: **Just as semen imparts impurity** even if it occurs **due to circumstances beyond** his **control, so too,** the **first sighting of a *zav* imparts impurity** even if it occurs **due to circumstances beyond** his **control.**

*Rav Huna* raises yet another dimension of the first *zov* flow question: *re'iyah rishonah shel zav metamei be'ones* — does the first flow of a *zav* generate *tumah* even when caused by external stimulation? This directly parallels the question of how far the analogy to *shichvat zera* extends.

The halacha of *zav* ordinarily requires that one first investigate whether the flow was caused by external factors — eating something unusual, heavy lifting, exertion, and so forth — and if it was, the flow is not counted toward *zav* status. *Shichvat zera*, semen, generates *tumah* even when due to *ones*. Since the first *zov* flow carries the same *tumah* as *shichvat zera*, perhaps it too generates *tumah* even when externally caused. Or perhaps, since it occurs in the context of *zav* physiology, it retains the *zav* rule and is exempt if externally caused.

### Mishnah on Checking Flows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: רָאָה רְאִיָּיה רִאשׁוֹנָה — בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the statement of Rav Huna: **Come** and **hear** a mishna (*Zavim* 2:2): With regard to a man who **saw a first sighting** of *ziva*, **one examines him** to determine whether the discharge was caused by circumstances beyond his control.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לַָאו לְטוּמְאָה? לָא, לְקׇרְבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **What, is it not** that the purpose of this examination is **to** clarify that he does not have **ritual impurity,** i.e., if the discharge was due to circumstances beyond his control he remains pure, which contradicts the statement of Rav Huna? The Gemara responds: **No,** the purpose of this examination is **to** determine whether he will be obligated to bring **an offering** if he experiences another two discharges of *ziva*. If the first sighting was caused by circumstances beyond his control, it is not counted toward the three sightings that render one liable to bring an offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בַּשְּׁנִיָּה בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ. לְמַאי? אִילֵימָא לְקׇרְבָּן, אֲבָל לְטוּמְאָה לָא? אִקְּרִי כָּאן ״מִבְּשָׂרוֹ״ וְלֹא מֵחֲמַת אוֹנְסוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** the latter clause of the same mishna: **When** he experiences **the second** sighting of *ziva*, **one examines him** to determine whether the discharge was caused by circumstances beyond his control. **For what** purpose does one examine him? **If we say** that it is **to** exempt him from bringing **an offering** in the event that he experiences a third discharge **but not to** clarify that he does not have **ritual impurity,** this is untenable, as one may **read here** the verse: "An issue **out of his flesh"** (Leviticus 15:2), from which it is derived that one is **not** rendered a *zav* if the discharge occurred **due to circumstances beyond his control.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו לְטוּמְאָה, וּמִדְּסֵיפָא לְטוּמְאָה — רֵישָׁא נָמֵי לְטוּמְאָה?
>
> **Translation:** Rather, is it not** that the examination serves **to** clarify that he does not have **ritual impurity? And from** the fact **that** the examination in **the latter clause** is **for** purposes of **impurity,** one may conclude that the examination of **the first clause** is **also for** purposes of **impurity.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִידֵּי אִירְיָא? הָא כִּדְאִיתַאּ, וְהָא כִּדְאִיתַאּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **Are the cases comparable? This** case is **as it is, and that** case is **as it is.** In other words, it is possible that each examination is intended for a different purpose. In particular, the first examination is meant to exempt him from bringing an offering, and the second examination pertains to both the offering and ritual impurity.

The *Gemara* attempts to resolve this from the *Mishnah*: *ra'ah re'iyah rishonah bodkin oto* — if he has his first flow, they check him for external stimulation. One might think: this checking is to determine whether the flow is *tamei* — implying that if caused externally, it is *not tamei*. The *Gemara* deflects: perhaps the checking is only for purposes of the *korban* — to determine whether this flow can combine with subsequent flows toward a *korban* obligation — but the flow is *tamei* regardless. Then the *Gemara* extends to the second flow: *bishniyah bodkin oto*, you also check the second flow. Why check the second flow? If the intent were only about the *korban*, why check the second — since the *korban* requires three? The answer is that by the second flow, the verse *mibesaro velo machmat onso* establishes that only a flow from within, not from external stimulation, renders him *tamei*. So the checking of the second flow is clearly about *tumah*. And since the *Mishnah* uses the same language for the first and second flows, it seems to follow that checking the first flow is also about *tumah*.

However, the *Gemara* pushes back: *midi irya* — these may simply be independent cases, each governed by its own logic. The second flow's checking is about *tumah*, the first flow's checking may be about whether it can combine toward a *korban*, while the first flow itself is *tamei* regardless.

### Tanna Kamma vs. Rabbi Elazar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר, אַף בַּשְּׁלִישִׁי בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ מִפְּנֵי הַקׇּרְבָּן, מִכְּלָל דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא מִפְּנֵי הַטּוּמְאָה קָאָמַר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** the same mishna, which states that **Rabbi Eliezer says: Even** after **the third** discharge **one examines him, because of the offering.** In other words, if the third discharge occurred due to circumstances beyond his control, he is not liable to bring an offering. From the fact that according to Rabbi Eliezer the examination is due to the offering, one may conclude **by inference that the first** *tanna* **is saying** the examinations are **for ritual impurity.** If so, then according to the mishna one who has an initial discharge of *ziva* due to circumstances beyond his control remains pure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לְקׇרְבָּן, וְהָכָא בְּ״אֶתִּים״ קָא מִיפַּלְגִי: רַבָּנַן לָא דָּרְשִׁי ״אֶתִּים״, וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר דָּרֵישׁ ״אֶתִּים״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No,** this is not the proper explanation of the mishna. Rather, **everyone agrees** that the examination serves **to** exempt him from bringing **an offering. And here they disagree with regard to** whether one interprets instances of the word **"*et*"** in a verse. With regard to a *zav*, the verse states: "And of them that have an issue of *ziva* [*vehazav et zovo*], whether it be a male or a female" (Leviticus 15:33). **The Rabbis do not interpret** instances of the word **"*et*," and Rabbi Eliezer interprets** instances of the word **"*et*."**

The discussion then turns to the dispute between the *Tanna Kamma* and *Rabbi Elazar* about checking the third flow. *Rabbi Elazar* holds that one checks even the third flow for *ones*, lest it lead to an improper *korban*. The *Tanna Kamma* does not require checking the third flow for *korban* purposes. The *Gemara* suggests this debate may imply that the *Tanna Kamma* requires checking for *tumah* purposes even through the earlier flows, but the *Gemara* rejects this as well: both agree that checking for *korban* purposes is sometimes necessary; they disagree only about whether *ones* matters at flow three for the *korban*, with the *Tanna Kamma* holding it does not (the verse *lazachar velanekevah* equates flow three with the *zavah*, whose *ones* is disregarded), while *Rabbi Elazar* holds that the verse extends the *ones* exemption through flow three. Neither view yields a clear answer about the first flow and *tumah*.

### Rabbi Yitzchak's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: רַבִּי יִצְחָק אוֹמֵר, וַהֲלֹא זָב בִּכְלַל בַּעַל קֶרִי הָיָה, וְלָמָּה יָצָא — לְהָקֵל עָלָיו וּלְהַחְמִיר עָלָיו; לְהָקֵל עָלָיו — שֶׁאֵין מְטַמֵּא בְּאוֹנֶס, וּלְהַחְמִיר עָלָיו — שֶׁהוּא עוֹשֶׂה מִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to refute the statement of Rav Huna: **Come** and **hear** that which **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: But wasn't a *zav* included in** the category of **one who experienced a seminal emission? Why,** then, **was he taken out** and discussed in a separate passage? In order **to be lenient with him and to be stringent with him** relative to the *halakhot* of one who experienced a seminal emission. Rabbi Yitzḥak elaborates: The separate passage serves **to be lenient with him, as he is not rendered impure through** an emission that occurs **due to circumstances beyond** his **control,** unlike one who experienced a seminal emission. **And** the separate passage serves **to be stringent with him,**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהוּא עוֹשֶׂה מִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב.
>
> **Translation:** **as he renders** impure the **bedding** upon which he lies **and** the **seat** upon which he sits, like a primary source of ritual impurity, which is not the case for one who experienced a seminal emission.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימַת? אִילֵּימָא בִּרְאִיָּיה שְׁנִיָּה — הֵיכָא הֲוָה בִּכְלַל בַּעַל קֶרִי? אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא בִּרְאִיָּיה רִאשׁוֹנָה, וְקָתָנֵי לְהָקֵל עָלָיו שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא בְּאוֹנֶס!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes Rabbi Yitzḥak's statement: **When** does this statement apply, i.e., to which sighting of *ziva* is Rabbi Yitzḥak referring? **If we say** he is referring **to** the **second sighting** this is untenable, for **where** in the verse **was** such a person **included in** the category of **one who experienced a seminal emission?** After the second sighting one is considered a full-fledged *zav*. **Rather,** it is **obvious** that he is referring **to** the **first sighting. And** yet Rabbi Yitzḥak **teaches:** The separate passage discussing a *zav* serves **to be lenient with him, as** a *zav* **is not rendered impure through** an emission that occurs **due to circumstances beyond** his **control.** This contradicts the statement of Rav Huna.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרָא לְהַחְמִיר עָלָיו שֶׁהוּא עוֹשֶׂה מִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב, בִּרְאִיָּיה רִאשׁוֹנָה בַּר מִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **And how can you understand** that Rabbi Yitzḥak is referring **to** the first sighting of *ziva*? But Rabbi Yitzḥak also says: The separate passage serves **to be stringent with him, as he renders** impure the **bedding** upon which he lies **and** the **seat** upon which he sits. **With the first sighting** of *ziva* **is** one **fit** to render impure his **bedding or** his **seat?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר, רַבִּי יִצְחָק אוֹמֵר: וַהֲלֹא זָב בִּכְלַל בַּעַל קֶרִי הָיָה בִּרְאִיָּיה רִאשׁוֹנָה, וְלָמָּה יָצָא בִּרְאִיָּיה שְׁנִיָּיה — לְהָקֵל עָלָיו וּלְהַחְמִיר עָלָיו, לְהָקֵל עָלָיו — שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא בְּאוֹנֶס, וּלְהַחְמִיר עָלָיו — שֶׁהוּא עוֹשֶׂה מִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, this** is what **he is saying: Rabbi Yitzḥak says: But wasn't a *zav* with** his **first sighting included** in the category of **one who experienced a seminal emission? Why,** then, **was he taken out** and discussed in a separate passage **with regard to** his **second sighting?** In order **to be lenient with him and to be stringent with him.** In other words, the passage serves **to be lenient with him, as he is not rendered impure** through an emission that occurs **due to circumstances beyond** his **control. And** it serves **to be stringent with him, as he renders** impure the **bedding** upon which he lies **and** the **seat** upon which he sits.

*Rabbi Yitzchak* resolves the question with a sharp structural observation. He states: *velo zav bichlal ba'al keri haya* — a *zav* was already included in the category of *ba'al keri*. Why then was the *zav* singled out as a distinct category? *Lehakel alav ulehachmir alav* — both to be lenient with him and to be strict with him. Lenient: *she'ein metamei be'ones* — unlike *ba'al keri*, the *zav* does not generate *tumah* when the flow is externally caused. Strict: *she'hu oseh mishkav umoshav av hatumah* — the *zav* renders the objects he sits or lies upon an *av hatumah*, which *ba'al keri* does not.

The *Gemara* then clarifies what stage is being described. One cannot read this as referring to after the second flow — because then why say the *zav* "was included in the category of *ba'al keri*"? After two flows he is a full *zav*, never having been treated as a *ba'al keri* at that stage. So it must be referring to the first flow — that is when a *zav* carries the status of *ba'al keri*. And the *kal* aspect, the leniency, is that at the stage of the second flow, unlike the first, *ones* does not generate *tumah*. The clear implication — left implicit by the *Gemara* but embedded in the structure — is that the first flow, which is in the *ba'al keri* category, *does* generate *tumah* even through *ones*. The *Gemara* notes that the text does not spell this out entirely, but the reading of the *braita* strongly suggests it. The conclusion that emerges: the first flow is analogous to *shichvat zera* even in this respect, while the second flow departs from the *ba'al keri* category in the lenient direction (no *ones*) as well as the strict direction (seven-day *tumah*, *mishkav umoshav*).

### Zov vs. Zera Description

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: זוֹב דּוֹמֶה לְמֵי בָצֵק שֶׁל שְׂעוֹרִים. זוֹב בָּא מִבְּשַׂר הַמֵּת, שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע בָּא מִבְּשַׂר הַחַי. זוֹב — דֵּיהֶה, וְדוֹמֶה לְלוֹבֶן בֵּיצָה הַמּוּזֶרֶת, שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע קְשׁוּרָה, וְדוֹמָה לְלוֹבֶן בֵּיצָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ מוּזֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to *ziva*, **Rav Huna says:** The discharge of ***ziva* is similar to water of barley dough.** Whereas the discharge of ***ziva* comes from dead flesh,** i.e., when one's penis is flaccid, **semen comes from living flesh,** when one's penis is erect. Moreover, the discharge of ***ziva* is runny, and is similar** in appearance **to the white of a unfertilized egg.** By contrast, **semen is viscous, and** it **is similar** in appearance **to the white of an egg that is not unfertilized,** i.e., a fertilized egg.

The *Gemara* closes this section with *Rav Huna*'s physical description of the difference between *zov* and *zera* — since for a woman the distinction between *niddah* and *zavah* is temporal, but for a man it is physiological. *Zov* resembles the water of barley dough — pale and thin. It issues from the non-erect penis. *Shichvat zera* is more viscous, more cohesive, and resembles the white of a fertilized egg, issuing from the erect penis. (Whether *muzeres* here means fertilized or beaten is noted as a matter of some uncertainty — *Rashi* takes it to mean fertilized.)

## Dam Hayoledes Shelo Tavla

### Beit Hillel's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמְרוּ לָהֶן בֵּית הִלֵּל לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי: אִי אַתֶּם מוֹדִים בְּנִדָּה שֶׁלֹּא טָבְלָה וְרָאֲתָה דָּם, שֶׁהִיא טְמֵאָה? אָמְרוּ לָהֶם בֵּית שַׁמַּאי: לֹא, אִם אֲמַרְתֶּם בְּנִדָּה שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ טָבְלָה וְרָאֲתָה טְמֵאָה, תֹּאמְרוּ בְּיוֹלֶדֶת שֶׁאִם טָבְלָה וְרָאֲתָה שֶׁהִיא טְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this dispute, it **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Beit Hillel said to Beit Shammai: Do you not concede with regard to a menstruating woman who did not immerse** after seven days **and** thereafter **saw blood, that she is impure** as a menstruating woman in every sense? If so, a woman who failed to immerse after childbirth should likewise be impure as a menstruating woman. **Beit Shammai said to them: No,** this is not a legitimate comparison. Even **if you say** this is true **with regard to a menstruating woman,** there the *halakha* is **that even** in a case where **she immersed and** immediately **saw** blood thereafter, she is **impure. Will you say** that this *halakha* applies **with regard to a woman who gave birth,** where the *halakha* is **that if she immersed and** then **saw** blood **she is pure?** Therefore, even if a woman who gave birth neglected to immerse and experienced bleeding, she is not considered a full-fledged menstruating woman, and the blood does not impart impurity whether it is moist or dry.

The *Gemara* now turns to a case structurally similar to the *metzora'at* question but with a different twist. A woman gave birth, is now in her postpartum period — the thirty-three or sixty-six days of *dam tehorah* — but has not yet immersed in a *mikvah*. She is still *tamei* as a *yoledes*. What is the status of blood she now sees?

This case inverts the earlier one. In the *metzora'at* discussion, the question was whether blood that is not generating *niddah* or *zavah tumah* can nonetheless be classified as a bodily fluid of the *metzora'at*. Here, the question is about the character of the blood itself: is it *dam tehorah* — pure blood — or does the woman's continued *tum'ah* status, by virtue of not having immersed, somehow affect the blood's status?

*Beit Hillel* opens the exchange by challenging *Beit Shammai*: do you not agree that a *niddah* who has completed her seven days but has not yet gone to the *mikvah*, and who then sees blood, is *tamei* — fully *tamei* as *dam niddah* or *dam zavah*, even if the blood dries? If so, why should this *yoledes*, who also has not gone to the *mikvah*, be different?

### Beit Shammai's Response

*Beit Shammai* rejects the analogy. By a *niddah*, even if she immerses and later bleeds, the blood makes her *tamei* again — the blood is always a source of *tumah* when it emerges. By the *yoledes*, however, had she gone to the *mikvah* and then bled during the thirty-three or sixty-six days, that blood would do nothing to her — it is *dam tehorah*. This shows that the blood itself during this period is *tahor*. *Beit Shammai* therefore holds that even without *mikvah* immersion, the blood during the thirty-three or sixty-six days is intrinsically *tahor* — perhaps it has some secondary level of *tumah* by rabbinic enactment, but it is not fundamentally *dam niddah* or *dam zavah*.

### Proof from Zavah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לָהֶם: יוֹלֶדֶת בְּזוֹב תּוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁאִם טָבְלָה וְרָאֲתָה לְאַחַר יְמֵי סְפִירָה — טְהוֹרָה, לֹא טָבְלָה וְרָאֲתָה — טְמֵאָה!
>
> **Translation:** Beit Hillel **said to** Beit Shammai: The *halakha* of **a woman who gives birth as a *zava* will prove** this is in fact a legitimate comparison. A woman who gives birth as a *zava* may immerse only after experiencing seven clean days. The *halakha* is **that if she immersed** in her days of purity **and** then **saw** blood **after** the seven **days of counting** for *ziva*, she is **pure,** as she is in her days of purity. But if **she did not immerse and she saw** blood, she is **impure.** If so, the same should apply to a woman who gave birth and did not immerse at the conclusion of her days of impurity: She should be considered a full-fledged menstruating woman as long as she has not immersed.

*Beit Hillel* responds with a case that both sides agree upon: a *yoledes beza'vah* — a woman who gave birth while she was a *zavah*. Both agree that if she immerses after her seven clean days and then bleeds during the thirty-three or sixty-six days, the blood is *tehorah*. And both agree that if she has not yet completed her seven clean days and bleeds during the thirty-three or sixty-six days, that blood is *tamei* — it prevents her from counting a clean day. So, *Beit Hillel* argues, look at the case where she has completed her seven clean days but has not yet immersed — if she bleeds, should that blood be *tamei* or *tahor*? It is analogous to the *yoledes* who has not immersed: everything is done except the *mikvah*. If *Beit Shammai* agrees that the *zavah* in this position is still *tamei* until she immerses, then why not say the same for the *yoledes*?

### Conditional Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לָהֶם: הוּא הַדִּין, וְהִיא הַתְּשׁוּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** Beit Shammai **said to them: The same is true and** this **is the refutation,** i.e., we maintain that even in the case of a woman who gave birth as a *zava* and failed to immerse after seven clean days, her blood imparts impurity only while moist.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דִּפְלִיגִי? וְהָתְנַן: וּמוֹדִים בְּיוֹלֶדֶת בְּזוֹב שֶׁהִיא מְטַמְּאָה לַח וְיָבֵשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that** Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel **disagree** with regard to a woman who gave birth as a *zava* and counted seven clean days but did not immerse? **But didn't we learn** in the mishna: **And** Beit Shammai **concede** to Beit Hillel **in** the case of **a woman who gives birth as a *zava*, that** any blood **she** sees **imparts impurity** whether it is **moist or dry?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא — כָּאן שֶׁסָּפְרָה, כָּאן שֶׁלֹּא סָפְרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: This is **not difficult. Here,** in the *baraita*, Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai disagree with regard to a woman **who counted** seven clean days for her *ziva*. In such a case Beit Shammai maintain that any blood she sees imparts impurity only when moist. **There,** in the mishna, they agree with regard to a woman **who did not** yet **count** seven clean days for her *ziva*. In such an instance, even Beit Shammai concede that her blood imparts impurity whether it is moist or dry.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: יוֹלֶדֶת בְּזוֹב שֶׁסָּפְרָה וְלֹא טָבְלָה וְרָאֲתָה, הָלְכוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי לְשִׁיטָתָן, וּבֵית הִלֵּל לְשִׁיטָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And it is taught** likewise in a *baraita*: With regard to **a woman who gives birth as a *zava*, who counted** seven clean days after the conclusion of her days of impurity **but did not** yet **immerse, and** she subsequently **saw** blood, **Beit Shammai follow their opinion** with regard to any woman who gave birth and concluded her days of impurity but did not yet immerse, **and Beit Hillel** likewise **follow their opinion.** In other words, according to Beit Shammai her blood imparts impurity only while moist, whereas according to Beit Hillel it imparts impurity whether moist or dry.

*Beit Shammai*'s response is *hu hadin vehu hateshuvah* — the same answer applies. Whatever we say about the *yoledes*, we say about the *zavah* in that position. A *braita* then confirms that indeed, in the case of a *yoledes beza'vah* who has counted her seven clean days but not yet immersed, the two schools follow their respective positions: *Beit Shammai* to their principle and *Beit Hillel* to theirs. The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction between this and a *Mishnah* that implies they agree: the *Mishnah*'s agreement applies only to a *zavah* who is still in the middle of counting her seven clean days — in that case, both agree that bleeding during the thirty-three or sixty-six days is *tamei*, because she is still counting and the blood interrupts her *sefirah*. Only once the seven clean days are complete does the disagreement arise.

### When Blood Becomes Tahor

The conceptual core of the debate may now be stated clearly. *Beit Shammai* holds that the *Torah* purifies the blood of a *yoledes* automatically upon her reaching day 8 or day 15 — the transition is calendrical. *Beit Hillel* holds that the *Torah* does not purify the blood until after she has immersed in a *mikvah* — the transition requires both the calendar and the act of immersion. Until that point, even though the blood would in ordinary circumstances be *dam tehorah*, it retains its *tamei* character by virtue of her not yet having completed the process of *tahara*.

## Ma'ayan Echad or Shnei

### Rav: One Wellspring

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: רַב אָמַר: מַעְיָן אֶחָד הוּא — הַתּוֹרָה טִמְּאַתּוּ וְהַתּוֹרָה טִהֲרַתּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to blood emitted by a woman during her days of purity after childbirth, **it was stated** that there is a dispute between the Sages. **Rav says: It is** from **one source** in a woman's body that pure and impure blood are emitted, but **the Torah rendered impure** the blood emitted during her days of impurity **and the Torah rendered pure** the blood emitted during her days of purity.

The *Gemara* now introduces a dispute between *Rav* and *Levi* that places the entire preceding debate about the *yoledes* into a broader theoretical framework. *Rav* says: *ma'ayan echad hu* — there is one wellspring. All the uterine blood, throughout the postpartum period, flows from the same physical source. The *Torah* simply assigns different halachic statuses to it: *tamei* during the first seven or fourteen days, *tahor* from day 8 or 15, *tamei* again after day 40 or 80. This is a pure halachic designation, not a physical distinction. *Hatorah timaso vehatorah tiharto* — the *Torah* declared it *tamei* and the *Torah* declared it *tahor*.

### Levi: Two Wellsprings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵוִי אָמַר: שְׁנֵי מַעְיָנוֹת הֵם — נִסְתָּם הַטָּמֵא נִפְתַּח הַטָּהוֹר, נִסְתָּם הַטָּהוֹר נִפְתַּח הַטָּמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Levi says: There are two sources** in a woman's body. Blood emitted during her days of impurity emerges from one source, while blood emitted during her days of purity emerges from the other, and these two sources are not active simultaneously. Rather, when **the** source of the **impure** blood **is closed,** i.e., following her days of impurity, **the** source of the **pure** blood **opens,** and when **the** source of the **pure** blood **is closed,** at the conclusion of her days of purity, either thirty-three days for a male child or sixty-six days for a female child, **the** source of the **impure** blood **is opened.**

*Levi* disagrees: *shnei ma'ayanot hein* — they are two distinct wellsprings. The *tumah* source physically closes at the end of day 7 or 14: *nistam hatamei*. A different, *tahor* source then opens: *niftach hatahor*. At day 40 or 80, the *tahor* source closes and the *tamei* source reopens. For *Levi*, the change in halachic status reflects an underlying physiological change in the source of the blood.

### Continuous Flow Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵינַיְיהוּ: שׁוֹפַעַת מִתּוֹךְ שִׁבְעָה לְאַחַר שִׁבְעָה, וּמִתּוֹךְ אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר לְאַחַר אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר, וּמִתּוֹךְ אַרְבָּעִים לְאַחַר אַרְבָּעִים, וּמִתּוֹךְ שְׁמֹנִים לְאַחַר שְׁמֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between** the opinions of Rav and Levi? The Gemara responds: **There is** a practical difference **between them** with regard to the following cases: A woman who **continuously discharges** menstrual blood **from within seven** days of giving birth to a male until sometime **after** those **seven** days, during her days of purity; **and** likewise, a woman who continuously discharges menstrual blood **from within fourteen** days of giving birth to a female until sometime **after** those **fourteen** days, during her days of purity; **and** a woman who continuously discharges menstrual blood **from within forty** days of giving birth to a male until sometime **after** those **forty** days, i.e., after the conclusion of her days of purity; **and** a woman who continuously discharges menstrual blood **from within eighty** days of giving birth to a female until sometime **after** those **eighty** days, i.e., after the conclusion of her days of purity.

The practical difference emerges in cases of continuous flow across the status boundaries. If a woman is bleeding steadily from within day 7 into day 8 — a single uninterrupted flow — does the blood that spans the boundary carry the status of the earlier or the later period? The same question arises at the end: a flow that begins within day 40 and continues into day 41.

### Rav's and Levi's Stringencies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַב — רֵישָׁא לְקוּלָּא, וְסֵיפָא לְחוּמְרָא;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **According to Rav,** who maintains that both pure and impure blood emerge from the same source, in the cases described in **the first clause,** i.e., if she continuously discharged menstrual blood from within her days of impurity until sometime during her days of purity, one is **to** be **lenient,** In other words, any blood emitted during her days of purity is pure, since the Torah rendered it pure. **And** in the cases described in **the latter clause,** when the discharge begins during her days of purity and continues until after the conclusion of her days of purity, one is **to** be **stringent,** as the Torah deemed impure any blood emitted after her days of purity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְלֵוִי — רֵישָׁא לְחוּמְרָא, וְסֵיפָא לְקוּלָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **According to Levi,** who says that there are two different sources in the body, in the cases described in **the first clause** one is **to** be **stringent,** as the continuous flow of blood indicates this blood is emanating from the source of the impure blood, and the Torah deemed pure only the blood that emerges from the source of the pure blood. **And** in the cases described in **the latter clause** one is **to** be **lenient,** as the continuous flow of blood indicates this blood is from the source of the pure blood.

For *Rav*, since there is a single wellspring and the change is purely halachic, the transition is sharp: the moment day 8 begins, the blood is *tahor*, regardless of continuity with the earlier flow. This makes *Rav* lenient at the beginning of the process (*reisha lekula*) — as soon as the calendar turns, the blood changes status. But at the end of the process, the moment day 41 begins, the blood again becomes *tamei*, even if it is a continuation of the same flow that was *tahor* the moment before — making *Rav* strict there (*seifa lechumra*).

*Levi* is the reverse. Since the change in status reflects a physical change of source, a continuous flow from within day 7 into day 8 remains problematic: the source has not yet switched, and so the flow cannot be assumed to have become *tahor* (*reisha lechumra*). But at the end of the process, a continuous flow from within day 40 into day 41 remains *tahor*: the physical *tahor* source has not yet closed, and the blood is still issuing from the same *tahor* wellspring (*seifa lekula*).

### Connection to Beit Hillel

The *Gemara* connects this debate back to the dispute between *Beit Hillel* and *Beit Shammai*. *Beit Hillel*'s position — that blood does not become *dam tehorah* until after the woman has immersed in a *mikvah*, not merely upon reaching day 8 or 15 — fits naturally with the view that there is *one* wellspring: *ma'ayan echad*. If the change were physical, tied to the opening and closing of distinct anatomical sources, the *mikvah* would be irrelevant — the blood would automatically shift character once the new source opened. But if the change is halachic, one can readily add the requirement that the *mikvah* be part of the halachic process by which the status shifts. *Beit Hillel*'s position thus implies that the *Torah*'s *tahara* of the *yoledes*'s blood is not automatic upon the calendar date but requires the act of immersion — a position coherent only if one understands the blood as having a single physical source whose halachic status the *Torah* assigns and reassigns. The *Gemara* closes with this connection, to be continued in the following daf.
