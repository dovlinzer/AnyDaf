# Taanit 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Private Fasting Rules

### Components of Ta'anit Yachid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לֹוֶה אָדָם תַּעֲנִיתוֹ וּפוֹרֵעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: A person may borrow his fast and repay.**

The *Gemara* on this *daf* continues a sustained discussion of the private fast, *ta'anit yachid*, returning to it briefly before the next *mishna*. Three distinct components have already emerged in the preceding sugya: the requirement of *kabbalah mibe'od yom* — accepting the fast while it is still day — the obligation to maintain the fast through the end of the day, and now a third element introduced here by Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: *loveh adam ta'anito upore'a* — a person may borrow against his fast and pay it back. The language is striking. It frames the private fast not as a fixed obligation tied to a particular calendar day, but as a kind of personal debt that can be discharged on a different occasion. If circumstances arise that make fasting on the designated day difficult or impossible, one may eat, and fulfill the fast on another day.

### Shmuel's Non-Binding View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אַמְרִיתַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אָמַר לִי: וְכִי נֶדֶר קַבֵּל עֲלֵיהּ, דְּלָא סַגִּי דְּלָא מְשַׁלֵּם? לְצַעוֹרֵי נַפְשֵׁיהּ קַבֵּיל עֲלֵיהּ. אִי מָצֵי — מְצַעַר נַפְשֵׁיהּ, אִי לָא מָצֵי — לָא מְצַעַר נַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **When I said this** *halakha* **before Shmuel, he said to me: And did he take a vow upon himself,** which would mean **that it is not possible for him not to repay** it? **He took upon himself to cause himself discomfort** on that day. **If he is able,** he must **cause himself discomfort; if he is unable, he does not** need to **cause himself discomfort.**

When Rav Yehuda reported this teaching to *Shmuel*, the response was astonishing: *vechi neder kibal alei d'lo sagi d'lo m'tzarei nafshia?* — Is this some kind of vow, that it would not be sufficient unless he fulfilled it? *Letzarei nafshia kibal alei* — he merely accepted upon himself to undergo some self-denial. If he is able to do it, well and good. If not, he simply does not. On this reading, Shmuel holds that the private fast carries no binding legal force whatsoever. It is nothing more than a good intention, a voluntary expression of self-denial, without the weight of obligation. If it is not fulfilled, nothing has been violated.

This position is, however, quite difficult to reconcile with what we learned previously, where it was Shmuel himself who insisted that a *ta'anit* requires *kabbalah mibe'od yom* — accepting it while still daytime on the preceding day. That requirement strongly implies a transformation of the act into something with legal weight, something akin to a vow and an obligation. Why else would the timing of the *kabbalah* matter so much if the entire undertaking were nothing more than a pious wish? The two versions of Shmuel appear to be in tension with each other.

### Shmuel's Vow Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לֹוֶה אָדָם תַּעֲנִיתוֹ וּפוֹרֵעַ. כִּי אַמְרִיתַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אָמַר לִי: פְּשִׁיטָא! לֹא יְהֵא אֶלָּא נֶדֶר. נֶדֶר — מִי לָא מָצֵי בָּעֵי לְשַׁלּוֹמֵי וּמֵיזַל לִמְחַר וּלְיוֹמָא אַחֲרִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** a different version of this discussion. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: One may borrow his fast and repay. When I said this** *halakha* **before Shmuel, he said to me:** This is **obvious. Let it be** considered **only a vow;** even so, **is he not required to repay a vow on the next day or on another day?**

The *Gemara* therefore offers an alternative version of the same exchange: *ika d'amri*, some say. In this version, when Rav Yehuda reported the teaching of Rav — that one may borrow and repay a fast — Shmuel responded not with a dismissal but with astonishment: *peshita*, of course! *Lo yehei ela neder* — it should be no different from a *neder*. If someone takes a vow, can he not pay it the following day or another day altogether? This version is far more consistent with what we know of Shmuel's position, since it takes for granted both that the private fast is legally binding in the manner of a vow, and that the specific day of fulfillment is not absolutely fixed, such that it may be discharged on a different occasion. The question, however, remains: how can a vow simply be deferred to another day?

### Types of Vows

To understand this, one must distinguish between two fundamentally different conceptions of a *neder*. The biblical *neder* is essentially a personal commitment — an acceptance of obligation — as illustrated by the vow of *Yaakov Avinu*: *vayidor Yaakov neder lemor im yiheye Elokim imadi ushmarani baderech hazeh*, and if God watches over me, I will give a tenth of all that I receive, and this stone I have set as a *matzeva* will be the House of God. This is a promise to perform a religious act. The specific date of performance is not necessarily what defines whether the vow has been fulfilled; what matters is the act itself. Indeed, God later appeared to Yaakov at *Beit El* and reminded him that it was time to discharge his *neder*, implying that some latitude exists regarding when the act is finally carried out.

The rabbis also speak of *nidrei mitzvah* — vows relating to the performance of a religious act — as a recognized category. For example, one who vows to learn a hundred *daf* for someone's *shloshim*, or to complete certain *mishnayos* by a particular date: if the deadline passes without fulfillment, is there still an obligation to follow through? The principle of *bal te'acher*, the prohibition against unreasonably delaying vow fulfillment, applies, yet missing a single appointed day does not necessarily sever the obligation entirely. The vow remains, even if imperfectly timed.

This stands in sharp contrast to the rabbinic conception of a *neder*, which is not about personal obligation at all but about transforming the halakhic status of an object. When one says "all fruit is forbidden to me," one has not sworn to refrain from eating; one has rendered the fruit itself into a *davar assur* through the power of speech — in the same way one may sanctify an object by designating it as consecrated, or render something forbidden through declaration. A *shvuah*, by contrast, is an oath that invokes God as witness to one's own conduct; its violation is a desecration of God's name, as in *lo yenakeh Hashem es asher yisa es shemo lashav*.

Some authorities have attempted to apply the rabbinic model of *neder* to a private fast, arguing that through the *kabbalah* one transforms the day itself — the way one might sanctify an object, one makes that specific day a *yom ta'anit*, a forbidden day from which one may not derive benefit. But this model does not accommodate the idea of deferral and repayment, since one has changed the status of a specific day, not incurred a personal debt. The framework of borrowing and paying back is far more coherent within the biblical model of *neder*, understood as *nidrei mitzvah* — a personal religious obligation, the act itself being the primary thing, with the date being secondary. On this reading, *Shmuel* takes it for granted that the fast operates in precisely this mode: it is a personal commitment to give something — one's fasting — to God, and like other such commitments, it can be discharged on a different day without invalidating the essential act.

### Steak Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידֵּי אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי רַב אַסִּי. עֲבַדוּ לֵיהּ עִגְלָא תִּילְתָּא, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: לִיטְעוֹם מָר מִידֵּי. אֲמַר לְהוּ: בְּתַעֲנִיתָא יָתֵיבְנָא. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: וְלוֹזֵיף מָר וְלִיפְרַע, לָא סָבַר מָר לְהָא דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לֹוֶה אָדָם תַּעֲנִיתוֹ וּפוֹרֵעַ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehoshua, son of Rav Idi, happened** to visit **the house of Rav Asi. They prepared a third-born calf,** whose meat is high quality, **for him. They said to him: Let the Master taste something. He said to them: I am sitting in** the observance of **a fast** and may not eat. **They said to him: And let the Master borrow and repay** the fast. **Doesn't the Master hold** in accordance with **this** *halakha* that **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: A person may borrow his fast and repay?**

This principle is illustrated through a vivid episode. Rav Yehoshua the son of Rav Idi came to the house of Rav Ashi. The household prepared for him an *egla tilta* — a choice veal, considered the finest dish in the *Gemara* — and placed it before him. He did not eat. They said to him: *lita'om mar midi*, taste a little of it. He replied: *beta'anisa yasivna*, I am fasting. At this point the people at the table said to him: *lozif mar uleifrei* — borrow and pay it back! Based on the ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav, you can eat today and make up your fast on another day.

What is particularly striking here is that this offer of deferral was extended apparently without any special circumstance. It was not said because the man was ill, or because refusing would cause great social awkwardness, or because some urgent matter required him to eat. It seems simply that a superb steak appeared before him and the view of those present was: eat, you can fast tomorrow. This implies that the right to borrow and repay operates with considerable ease, not requiring any exceptional justification.

Another noteworthy point is that Rav Yehoshua was already in the midst of his fast. The *Gemara* does not suggest that only a fast not yet begun can be deferred; the implication is that even a fast already underway may be transferred to another day. This assumption, though embedded casually in the story, raises significant questions.

### Dream Fasting Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: תַּעֲנִית חֲלוֹם הוּא. וְאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר מַחְסֵיָא אָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אָמַר רַב: יָפָה תַּעֲנִית לַחֲלוֹם כְּאֵשׁ לִנְעוֹרֶת. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: וּבוֹ בַּיּוֹם. וְאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת. מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ? לִיתֵּיב תַּעֲנִיתָא לְתַעֲנִיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Said to them: It is a fast for a dream.** He had dreamt a bad dream, and fasting is the prescribed remedy for such a dream. **And Rabba bar Meḥasseya said** that **Rav Ḥama bar Gurya said** that **Rav said: A fast is effective to** neutralize a bad **dream like fire** is effective for burning **chaff. Rav Ḥisda said:** The fast is effective specifically **on that day** that one dreamed. **And Rav Yosef said: And** one suffering from a bad dream is permitted to fast **even on Shabbat.** The Gemara asks: **What is the remedy** for one who has denigrated Shabbat by fasting? **Let him sit in** observance of another **fast,** on another day, to atone **for** his **fast** on Shabbat.

Rav Yehoshua's reply identifies the single exception to the borrowing principle: *tanis chalom hu* — this is a fast for a dream. He had dreamt a bad dream, and fasting is the prescribed remedy for such a dream. As the *Gemara* cites in the name of Rabba bar Machasya citing Rav Chama bar Gurya citing Rav: *yaffa tanis l'chalom k'esh l'neoros* — a fast is as effective for a dream as fire is for flax. The image is one of instantaneous consumption: just as fire burns through flax in a moment, the fast burns away the negative portent of the dream.

Critically, Rav Chisda adds: *uvo bayom* — the fast must take place on the same day, meaning the day following the dream. And Rav Yosef goes so far as to say that a *tanis chalom* may even be observed on *Shabbat*, despite the general obligation to derive pleasure from the Shabbat day, *oneg Shabbat* — so urgent is the need to act immediately. It follows that a *tanis chalom* cannot be borrowed and deferred, since its entire efficacy depends on the immediacy of the response. Delay defeats the purpose.

The *Gemara* adds a further complication: if one did fast on *Shabbat* for a bad dream, one has simultaneously violated the positive obligation of *oneg Shabbat*. The remedy — *leisiv tanita l'tanita* — is to observe an additional fast to account for the one conducted improperly on *Shabbat*. This curious solution echoes the broader logic of fasting as a transferable act: one fast to make up for another. One might also observe, drawing on the earlier discussion, that just as eating on *Shabbat* confers a special sanctity on the day, transforming an ordinary day into a fast day endows it with a kind of religious significance — a parallel way of elevating a day through a designated act.

It is worth noting here the broader theological context: even as earlier in the *Gemara* there were serious questions raised about the religious value of *ta'anit yachid* when practiced outside the framework of communal crisis, the entire discussion here takes for granted that the private fast is a religiously meaningful and legitimate act. The fact that it is placed in the category of *nidrei mitzvah*, that it requires genuine fulfillment, that there is a recognized remedy when a fast fails — all of this presupposes that the individual act of fasting carries authentic religious weight.

### Tosafos Limitations

*Tosafos* is not entirely comfortable with the apparent ease with which borrowing and deferral is permitted, and seeks to qualify the ruling. Reading the earlier formulation of *Shmuel* — that if one is able to fast, do so, if not, one need not — *Tosafos* construes that passage as providing the limiting principle for the borrowing framework. The rule of *loveh adam ta'anito upore'a* applies, according to *Tosafos*, only where the person genuinely cannot fast: *davka heicha d'lo mati mitzarei nafshia* — specifically where one is unable to impose the self-denial. Where one is capable of fasting on the designated day, one may not simply elect to defer.

This reading is difficult to reconcile with the steak story, which involved no indication of incapacity. Unless one argues that encountering a truly exceptional dish constitutes a form of inability — a stretch, even if one has sympathies in that direction — the story appears to contemplate deferral even in circumstances of full ability.

*Tosafos* further raises a question about whether an already-begun fast may be deferred. If one has already passed the morning, or has reached the midday meal without eating, perhaps the day itself has already been established as a *ta'anit*, such that borrowing against it is no longer available. The fast may have crystallized into an irrevocable observance once a significant portion of it has elapsed. This too sits uneasily with the steak story, which appears to assume that borrowing remains available even mid-fast. *Tosafos* leaves this as an open question — a responsible and reasonable reservation, even if the *maskana* of the *Gemara* seems to push the other way.

One additional subtlety deserves mention: the *ta'anit chalom* also lacks the *kabbalah mibe'od yom* that is ordinarily required to establish the legal status of a private fast. This raises the question of how precisely a *tanis chalom* works halakhically — whether its efficacy in counteracting the dream requires that it bear full legal status as a *ta'anit*, or whether some independent power inheres in the act of self-denial regardless of formal legal status. The *Gemara* does not resolve this, but it is a genuine and penetrating question.

## Communal Fasting

### Three-Plus-Three Fast Cycle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָבְרוּ אֵלּוּ וְלֹא נַעֲנוּ — בֵּית דִּין גּוֹזְרִין שָׁלֹשׁ תַּעֲנִיּוֹת אֲחֵרוֹת עַל הַצִּבּוּר. אוֹכְלִין וְשׁוֹתִין מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם, וַאֲסוּרִין בִּמְלָאכָה וּבִרְחִיצָה וּבְסִיכָה וּבִנְעִילַת הַסַּנְדָּל וּבְתַשְׁמִישׁ הַמִּטָּה, וְנוֹעֲלִין אֶת הַמֶּרְחֲצָאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **If these** three regular fasts have **passed and they have not been answered** with rain, the **court decrees three other fasts upon the community.** These are severe fasts, in which one **may eat and drink** only **while it is still day,** before the beginning of the night of the fast, **and** on the day of the fast itself **they are prohibited** to engage **in** the performance of **work, in bathing, in smearing with oil, in wearing shoes, and in marital relations; and they lock the bathhouses** so that no one should come to bathe on that day.

The *Gemara* now turns to the next *mishna*, which continues the account of the communal response to drought. The sequence established previously had *yechidim* — private, especially pious individuals — beginning to fast when the seventeenth of *Marcheshvan* arrived without rain. When *Rosh Chodesh Kislev* arrived and still no rain had fallen, the community began its three fasts. Those first three communal fasts, it is important to note, were daytime fasts only, without the additional restrictions characteristic of a full *ta'anit tzibur*. They were fasts of the community without the full panoply of self-denial.

### Full-Blown Restrictions

The *mishna* now proceeds to describe what occurs if those three communal fasts pass without result: *avru elu v'lo na'anu* — these passed and they were not answered. At this point, *Beit Din* decrees three additional fasts upon the community. The language shifts decisively. *Ochlin v'shosim mibe'od yom* — one may eat and drink only while it is still daylight before the fast begins. This is a full-blown fast beginning from the night before, in the classic mode of *ta'anit tzibur*. To the prohibition of eating is added the prohibition of *melacha* — labor — as well as the four additional forms of self-denial familiar from *Yom Kippur*: bathing (*rechitza*), anointing (*sicha*), wearing shoes (*neilat hasandal*), and marital relations (*tashmish hamitah*). The bathhouses are closed: *ve'no'alin et hamerchatzaot*.

### Seven More Fasts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָבְרוּ אֵלּוּ וְלֹא נַעֲנוּ, בֵּית דִּין גּוֹזְרִין עֲלֵיהֶן עוֹד שֶׁבַע שֶׁהֵן שְׁלוֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה תַּעֲנִיּוֹת עַל הַצִּבּוּר. הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ יְתֵירוֹת עַל הָרִאשׁוֹנוֹת, שֶׁבְּאֵלּוּ מַתְרִיעִין, וְנוֹעֲלִין אֶת הַחֲנוּיוֹת. בַּשֵּׁנִי מַטִּין עִם חֲשֵׁיכָה, וּבַחֲמִישִׁי מוּתָּרִין מִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹד הַשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **If these** three fasts have **passed and they** still **have not been answered,** the **court decrees on them another seven fasts, which are** a total **of thirteen fasts, upon the community,** not including the first three fasts observed by individuals. **These** seven fast days **are more** severe **than the first ones, as on these** days, in addition to all the earlier stringencies, they **sound the alarm,** as will be explained in the Gemara, **and they lock the stores.** Although shops must remained closed most of the time on these days, **on Monday** they open them **a little at nightfall** to allow people to purchase food for breaking their fast, **and on Thursday they are permitted** to open the stores all day **in deference to Shabbat,** so that people may purchase food for the sacred day.

If these three full fasts also pass without result, *Beit Din* decrees seven additional fasts, bringing the total to thirteen: the initial three individual fasts, then three communal, then seven. The seven final fasts are described as exceeding the earlier ones in severity: *she'be'elu matri'in* — in these, the *shofar* is sounded. The situation has become grave enough to warrant the most dramatic public expression of communal distress. Additionally, the stores are locked: *ve'no'alin et hachanuyot*. Certain leniencies are built in around commercial necessity: on Mondays, as darkness approaches, stores may open briefly so that people can purchase food for the following morning. On Thursdays, stores may open in honor of *Shabbat* preparation — *mutarin mipnei kvod haShabbat*.

### Crisis Escalation

At this point it is important to pause and observe a significant shift in the language of the *mishna*. The first set of fasts was triggered by the formula: *higia v'lo yardu geshamim* — the time arrived and rain did not fall. That is a response to a physical absence, a natural failure. The second set of three, and subsequently the seven, are triggered by: *avru elu v'lo na'anu* — these passed and they were not answered. The difference is profound. The first phase responds to the absence of rain as a material crisis: we need water, we are fasting to bring rain. The second phase responds to the silence of God: we are fasting, and God is not responding. This is no longer a material or economic crisis; it is a spiritual and relational one.

The *na'anu* — being answered — points to the hoped-for divine response to the communal fast and prayer. To not be answered is to discover that the fast has not moved God, that the relationship between the community and the divine is in some deeper state of rupture. To press the point with an analogy: if one needs something from a parent and the parent does not pick up the phone for two weeks, one first worries about not getting what one needs. After a certain point, the worry shifts to the relationship itself. The community has crossed that threshold. The stakes are no longer merely agricultural; they are theological.

This distinction carries halakhic implications as well. The full-blown *ta'anit tzibur*, with its nighttime commencement, its prohibition of labor, its closure of bathhouses and stores, and eventually the sounding of the *shofar* — all of these are responses not merely to the absence of rain but to the silence of God. The community must come together not just to petition but to grapple with its own standing before the Almighty.

The public closure of the stores and bathhouses deserves particular attention. Even in the earlier three-fast period, where *melacha* was already prohibited, the *mishna* still specifies that the stores are locked. This suggests something beyond the logical consequence of individual prohibition. It is a communal, regulatory expression of the severity of the moment: the entire street is transformed, the public character of the day is visible to anyone who walks through it. In the same way one feels the day in a city that observes *Shabbat*, the closed storefronts and shuttered bathhouses make the reality of the fast palpable in the public sphere, not just in the interior behavior of individuals.

### Shift to Mourning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָבְרוּ אֵלּוּ וְלֹא נַעֲנוּ, מְמַעֲטִין בְּמַשָּׂא וּמַתָּן, בְּבִנְיָן וּבִנְטִיעָה, בְּאֵירוּסִין וּבְנִישּׂוּאִין, וּבִשְׁאֵילַת שָׁלוֹם בֵּין אָדָם לַחֲבֵירוֹ — כִּבְנֵי אָדָם הַנְּזוּפִין לַמָּקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **If these** fasts have **passed and they have not been answered** the court does not decree additional fasts, but the entire community observes the customs of mourning. **They decrease** their engagement **in business** transactions, **in building and planting, in betrothals and marriages, and in greetings between** each **person and his fellow, like people who have been rebuked by God.**

If all thirteen fasts pass without result, the community enters a new mode entirely: *mema'atin bemasa umatan* — decreasing commercial activity; *bebinyan uvineti'ah* — in building and planting; *be'erusin uvenisu'in* — in betrothals and marriages; and *uvishe'ilat shalom bein adam lachaveiro* — in greeting one another and inquiring after each other's welfare. The *mishna* names this posture: *kivnei adam hanizufim lamakom* — like people who are being rejected by God.

The shift from *ta'anit* to *avelut* is arresting. Fasting has ceased to be the primary response. After thirteen communal fasts have failed to elicit rain or divine acknowledgment, the community is no longer trying to change God's mind. That effort has run its course. The appropriate response now is to accept the reality — God's displeasure or distance — and to demonstrate, through practices of mourning, that this reality is understood and felt as the tragedy it is. Just as when God told the people *lo e'eleh bekirbcha*, I will not go up among you, the people mourned — demonstrating that they grasped the weight of what it meant to be distanced from God — so too here. Not fasting, but mourning, becomes the expression of the community's recognition that it is, in some sense, *nozuf* — rejected, or at least distanced.

### Yechidim Persistence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיְּחִידִים חוֹזְרִין וּמִתְעַנִּין עַד שֶׁיֵּצֵא נִיסָן. יָצָא נִיסָן וְיָרְדוּ גְּשָׁמִים — סִימַן קְלָלָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הֲלוֹא קְצִיר חִטִּים הַיּוֹם וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **The individuals,** i.e., Torah scholars, **resume fasting** every Monday and Thursday **until** the month of **Nisan ends.** After this date they no longer pray for rain, since if **Nisan has ended and rains** subsequently **fall, they are a sign of a curse, as it is stated: "Is not the wheat harvest today?** I will call to the Lord that He may send thunder and rain, and you will know and see that your wickedness is great" (I Samuel 12:17). The wheat harvest is around the time of *Shavuot*, well after Nisan.

The *mishna* adds one final and striking element: *vehayechidim chozrim umit'anim* — the private, especially pious individuals resume fasting, and they continue: *ad sheyeitzei Nissan* — until Nissan has concluded. The *yechidim* are distinguished here as those who neither began fasting when the community did, having started earlier at the seventeenth of *Marcheshvan*, nor stop when the community stops. They persist even through the period when communal fasting has given way to mourning practices, maintaining their vigil of petition and hope. They are the ones who feel the earliest tremors of crisis and the last to abandon the effort of changing the divine decree.

If rain finally falls after Nissan has ended: *siman kelalah* — it is a sign of curse. This should not be misread as a purely agricultural statement, though there may be an element of that — late rain damages rather than helps crops. More fundamentally, it is a *siman*, a signal. God is communicating something about the relationship. The verse cited, *Halo katzir chitim hayom* — is it not the wheat harvest today? — will be explored further in the *Gemara*, but the thrust is clear: rain at this point does not signal divine bounty but divine displeasure. The timing itself carries a message.

This *siman kelalah* echoes the first *mishna* of the tractate, which taught that rain during *Sukkot* is also a *siman kelalah* — rain too early, a sign of rejection. Here, rain too late carries the same negative valence. The *siman* in both cases is not primarily about the rain itself but about what the timing reveals of the divine posture. One might also notice the particular role of the *yechidim* here: they start earlier and end later, and their persistence may reflect not only heightened sensitivity to communal crisis but something akin to what we will later see in the story of *Choni Hame'agel* — the idea that certain individuals possess a special relationship with God that gives their prayers singular power, and that perhaps where the community's prayers have failed, theirs may yet succeed.

## Fast Structure & Meaning

### Labor Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא כּוּלְּהוּ, אִית בְּהוּ תַּעֲנוּג: רְחִיצָה וְסִיכָה וְתַשְׁמִישׁ הַמִּטָּה, אֲבָל מְלָאכָה — צַעַר הוּא! אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״קַדְּשׁוּ צוֹם קִרְאוּ עֲצָרָה אִסְפוּ זְקֵנִים״, כַּעֲצֶרֶת, מָה עֲצֶרֶת אָסוּר בַּעֲשִׂיַּית מְלָאכָה — אַף תַּעֲנִית אָסוּר בַּעֲשִׂיַּית מְלָאכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, all of** the other actions are prohibited, **as they** provide **pleasure,** namely, **bathing, smearing, and marital relations. However, working is** a cause of **suffering.** Why was it decreed that one must refrain from working? **Rav Ḥisda said** that **Rav Yirmeya bar Abba said** as that **the verse states: "Sanctify a fast, call a solemn assembly, gather the elders"** (Joel 1:14), this indicates that a fast day is **like** a day of **assembly. Just as** on a day of **assembly,** i.e., a Festival, it **is prohibited** to engage **in working, so too,** on **a fast** it **is prohibited** to engage **in working.**

Returning now to the *Gemara's* analysis of the *mishna*, the first question raised is one that might strike a reader as counterintuitive. The *mishna* lists the prohibition of labor on a communal fast alongside restrictions like bathing and anointing. But while bathing and anointing are straightforwardly forms of *ta'anug* — indulgence — not working is generally experienced as a *tzaar*, a burden or imposition, at least from the perspective of someone who must earn a living. Why should the cessation of labor be categorized alongside pleasures that are being withheld?

Rav Chisda citing Rav Yirmeyah bar Abba answers by deriving the prohibition from the verse in *Yeshayahu*: *Kadshu tzom kiru atzarah, ispu zekenim* — sanctify a fast, call an assembly of gathering, gather the elders. The verse uses the word *atzarah*, which is the same term applied to *Shemini Atzeret* and *Shavuot* — days on which *melacha* is prohibited. The *Gemara* draws the analogy: just as an *atzeret* carries a prohibition of labor, so too the communal fast carries a prohibition of labor — *mah Atzeret assur be'asiyat melacha af ta'anit assur be'asiyat melacha*.

### Scriptural Source

The deeper logic goes beyond the formal analogy. The word *kadshu* — sanctify — is notable. The fast day, at this level of communal severity, is being invested with a kind of sanctity, a special character that sets it apart from an ordinary day. Prohibiting labor, like the *Yom Tov* prohibition, gives expression to that special character. But more than sanctity, the prohibition achieves something practical: it compels everyone to stop their ordinary activities, to cease being distracted by their work and commerce, and to come together in shared attention to what is happening. The community cannot be scattered across its workplaces while it is supposed to be gathered in response to divine silence.

A subsequent question in the *Gemara* probes whether, by analogy to *Yom Tov*, the prohibition of *melacha* should also begin the night before the fast. After all, these fasts begin from the night before — the community does not eat from the previous evening onward. Rashi explains that the *Gemara* will later establish that the *issur melacha* begins only with the day itself, not the night. The reason is drawn from the same verse: *ispu zekenim* — gather the elders. As Rabbi Yirmeyah bar Abba explained, the public gathering of elders takes place in the daytime; gathering at night is not the normative mode of communal assembly. Therefore, the aspect of the fast that is modeled on the communal gathering — the prohibition of labor — also applies only during the day, even though the fast itself and other restrictions begin the night before.

The *Gemara* further asks whether the prohibition should begin only from noon, since noon is also a reasonable hour for communal gathering. The response — cited in the name of Rav Huna — is that *mitzaphra kinufya*: the gathering takes place in the morning. Morning is the right time to bring people together, and that is when the public dimension of the day, including the labor prohibition, takes effect.

### Community Inspection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי עָבְדִי? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִצַּפְרָא עַד פַּלְגָא דְיוֹמָא מְעַיְּינִינַן בְּמִילֵּי דְמָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **How do they act** on a fast day? **Abaye said: From the morning until the middle of the day they examine the affairs of the town** by checking if there are any deficiencies or corruptions in the city, moral or otherwise, and attempt to fix them, as these problems may have been the cause of the Divine punishment.

The *Gemara* then asks: *heichi avdi* — how is the fast day actually structured? Abaye describes the shape of the day in three stages. From morning until midday: *me'aynin bemilei demata* — one examines the matters of the city. This is a crucial element that might otherwise seem puzzling. What do civic or municipal matters have to do with a fast for rain?

Rashi's gloss is illuminating: *drisha vachakira livdok bemaaseihen shel benei ha'ir im gezel vechamas bideyhen umefaysin otan* — inquiry and investigation to examine the conduct of the townspeople, to determine whether theft or robbery is in their hands, and to appease the wronged parties. The morning of the fast day is devoted to a communal audit: have wrongs been done? Are there debts unpaid, injuries uncompensated, people who have been cheated or robbed and never made whole? If so, these must be identified and corrected.

### Justice vs. Repentance

This emphasis on *milei demata* — civic rectification — rather than *teshuva* deserves careful attention, because it represents a particular and perhaps surprising framing of how a community responds to divine silence. The instinctive modern religious response might be to say: God is not answering because we sinned; therefore we must do *teshuva* — feel regret, commit to change, undergo interior transformation. The *Gemara* here points in a different direction. The first order of business is not interior repentance but the repair of concrete wrongs.

*Teshuva* as a concept focuses on the inner process: recognition of sin, remorse, confession, and genuine commitment not to repeat the failing. What is described here is something more pragmatic and more immediately correctable: someone was robbed and has not been compensated; a debt was never paid; a wrong was done and never righted. These are not problems that await interior transformation — they await action. Fix the wrong. Return the money. Make the person whole.

This distinction applies even within the domain of *bein adam lachaveiro*. If one damaged another's property and now pays for the damage, the practical wrong has been corrected, but that alone does not constitute *teshuva* in the full sense. Yet the *Gemara's* emphasis here is on the correction, not on the spiritual process. The world has been put out of balance; the task is to restore the balance. This is not a rejection of *teshuva* as a value, but the *Gemara* is pointing to a more immediate and perhaps more tractable first step.

### Liturgical Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ, רִבְעָא דְיוֹמָא קָרֵינַן בְּסִפְרָא וְאַפְטַרְתָּא, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ בָּעֵינַן רַחֲמֵי, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיָּקוּמוּ עַל עׇמְדָם וַיִּקְרְאוּ בְּסֵפֶר תּוֹרַת ה׳ אֱלֹהֵיהֶם רְבִעִית הַיּוֹם וּרְבִעִית מִתְוַדִּים וּמִשְׁתַּחֲוִים לַה׳ אֱלֹהֵיהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **From this** point **forward,** for **a quarter of the day they read** a portion **from the Torah and** a portion from **the Prophets** [*haftara*]. **From this** point **forward, they pray** and petition **for mercy, as it is stated: "And they stood up in their place and they read in the book of the Torah of the Lord their God a fourth part of the day; and another fourth part they confessed, and prostrated themselves before the Lord their God"** (Nehemiah 9:3).

After the morning devoted to examining and rectifying civic wrongs, the second quarter of the day — from noon onward — is given over to reading from the *Sefer Torah* and the *Haftarah*: *riv'ah deyoma karinan besifra va'aftarta*. The readings chosen for fast days are those that prompt deep religious reflection and confrontation with the gravity of the community's situation. And then, in the final portion of the day: prayer. *Mikan ve'eilach* — from that point onward — the community prays.

### Action Before Prayer

This sequencing is remarkable and worth dwelling on. In a discussion that has been centrally concerned with *ta'anit* and *tefillah* — fasting and prayer — the *Gemara* here makes clear that prayer comes last. First the community gathers. First it examines what is wrong. First it makes things right. Then it reads. Then it prays. The sequence is not: pray, and if God does not answer, fast more. The sequence is: fix what is broken, and then approach God.

### Ezra Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַפְרָם בַּר פָּפָּא אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: כׇּל שֶׁהוּא מִשּׁוּם אֵבֶל, כְּגוֹן תִּשְׁעָה בְּאָב וְאָבֵל — אָסוּר בֵּין בְּחַמִּין בֵּין בְּצוֹנֵן, כׇּל שֶׁהוּא מִשּׁוּם תַּעֲנוּג, כְּגוֹן תַּעֲנִית צִבּוּר — בְּחַמִּין אָסוּר, בְּצוֹנֵן מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rafram bar Pappa said** that **Rav Ḥisda said: Anything that is** prohibited **due to mourning, for example,** bathing on the **Ninth of Av, or** prohibited for a private **mourner,** is **prohibited both in hot water and in cold water. Anything that is** prohibited **due to pleasure, for example,** bathing on **a communal fast,** is **prohibited in hot water, but** is **permitted in cold water,** provided one washes for the sake of cleanliness.

The Gemara provides scriptural support for this ordering from the book of *Ezra*: *ve'elai ye'asefu kol chareid bedivrei Elohei Yisrael al ma'al hagolah* — they gathered to Ezra regarding the trespass of the exile, the sin of intermarriage and breach of covenant. The communal problem — a failing that had compromised the relationship between the people and God — was addressed first, examined and confronted. Only then does the verse record: *uvminchat ha'erev kamti mita'anita uvfarsi chapai el Hashem* — and at the time of the afternoon offering I arose from my fasting and spread my hands to God. Rashi clarifies that this does not mean Ezra ended his fast and ate; rather, in the midst of his suffering and with his garments rent, he turned to prayer. The prayer emerges from within the state of distress and self-confrontation, not as a replacement for it.

The verse from *Yeshayahu* read on *Yom Kippur* is recalled here in spirit: *Halo zeh tsom evcharehu* — is this the fast I desire? — followed by a catalogue of justice and compassion that God actually demands. A fast disconnected from the repair of social wrongs is not the fast God seeks. The *Gemara* enshrines this principle in the structure of the fast day itself: first you fix things, then you pray.

The example of the people of *Nineveh* (*Anshei Ninveh*) is also invoked in this context. God saw not their sackcloth and their fasting — *vayar et sakkam veta'anitam* — but rather *vayar ki shavu midarkam hara'ah* — He saw that they turned from their evil way. The outward act of fasting matters far less than the inward and practical transformation it is meant to accompany. Again, whether this reaches the full interior process of *teshuva* or represents something more structural — a community taking responsibility for fixing what has gone wrong — remains an open question, but the direction the *Gemara* points is consistent throughout.

## Mourning vs. Fasting

### Two Categories

Having established through the *mishna* and its discussion the broad framework of communal fast practice, the *Gemara* now turns to a more precise analytical question: what is the halakhic structure of the various self-denials associated with fast days, and how do they differ depending on whether the context is *avelut* or *ta'anit*? Rav Chisda articulates the governing principle by establishing two distinct categories.

### Avelut Severity

The first category is *kol shehu mishum avel* — any observance whose purpose is mourning. The paradigmatic cases are *Tisha B'Av* and the state of an *avel* during *shiva*. In this category, the person is forbidden from using both hot and cold water. Even cold water, which provides minimal comfort and could hardly be called an indulgence, is prohibited.

### Ta'anit Leniency

The second category is *kol shehu mishum ta'anug* — any observance whose purpose is the denial of pleasure or the embrace of suffering. The paradigmatic case here is *ta'anit tzibur*, the communal fast, where along with the prohibition of eating one also refrains from bathing, anointing, and so forth. In this category, only hot water is prohibited; cold water is permitted. *B'chamin assur b'tzonen mutar*.

### Reasoning

The distinction reflects a genuine difference in what each category of observance is meant to achieve. The *ta'anit* framework focuses on *letzarei nafshei* — imposing suffering or denying indulgences. The restrictions are about withholding pleasure, withdrawing from comfort. Hot water for bathing is a pleasure; cold water is not — or at least not obviously so. Cold water meets a basic need without rising to the level of indulgence. Therefore, within the *ta'anit* framework, cold water is not the target of the prohibition.

*Avelut*, by contrast, carries a heightened severity. As Rashi phrases it: *d'it lei tzara tuva* — there is an especially great personal anguish. The mourner is overwhelmed by grief; the community on *Tisha B'Av* is overwhelmed by the weight of historical catastrophe. In this state, even cold water is forbidden — not because cold water is a luxury, but because the totality of self-withdrawal from ordinary life is the expression of the mourning state. The person in acute mourning does not even have the presence of mind for the basic acts of self-care; they are entirely consumed by their grief.

One might have expected the logic to run in the opposite direction — that *ta'anit*, with its explicit focus on suffering, would be more stringent, while *avelut*, which permits eating, would be more lenient on other fronts. The *Gemara* inverts this expectation. The answer may lie in precisely the fact that *ta'anit* is specifically about withholding pleasure, *ta'anug*, and therefore its restrictions track the category of pleasure precisely. *Avelut* is about something different — total immersion in the reality of loss — and that totalizing immersion extends even to things that are not pleasures in any meaningful sense.

### Bathhouse Closure

The closure of bathhouses during *ta'anit tzibur*, mentioned in the *mishna*, is now understood through this lens. Bathhouses in the ancient world were primarily steam facilities, establishments of hot water and heated rooms. The closure of the bathhouse underscores and gives public expression to the prohibition of hot water specifically — it is the *chamim*, the hot water, that is the focus of the restriction on a fast day. Cold water, by contrast, falls outside the prohibition.

### Mikvah Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל חַיָּיבֵי טְבִילוֹת טוֹבְלִין כְּדַרְכָּן, בֵּין בְּתִשְׁעָה בְּאָב בֵּין בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **All** who are **obligated in immersions immerse** themselves **in their** usual **manner, both on** the **Ninth of Av and on Yom Kippur.**

A *braita* is cited to probe the cold-water question on the *avelut* side: *kol chayavei tvilot tovlin kedarkan bein b'Tisha B'Av bein b'Yom HaKippurim* — all who are obligated to immerse in the *mikvah* may do so in the normal fashion on both *Tisha B'Av* and *Yom Kippur*. Though this is not the ruling we follow in practice, it is the position of this *braita*, and it illuminates the halakhic landscape. The fact that the *braita* feels compelled to state that these individuals *may* immerse on *Tisha B'Av* implies that bathing on *Tisha B'Av* is otherwise categorically forbidden — and since *mikvah* water is cold, it follows that even cold water is prohibited on *Tisha B'Av*. There would be no need for a special dispensation if cold water were in any case permitted.

Rabbi Chanina *sgan hakohanim* adds the evocative statement: *kedai hu beit elokeinu l'abeid alav tvilah b'paam achat b'shanah* — the destruction of the Temple is sufficient reason to forego immersion in the *mikvah* one time a year. Even for those who are obligated, it is appropriate to miss immersion on *Tisha B'Av* in recognition of what the day represents. If cold water were permitted on *Tisha B'Av*, his statement would be puzzling — why make the sacrifice of missing *mikvah* when one could simply immerse in cold water without violating anything? The implication is clear: cold water is forbidden, and therefore even those with an obligation to immerse are foregoing something real by abstaining.

### Hot/Cold Status

The *Gemara* anticipates a possible rebuttal: perhaps the *braita* is discussing a case where only hot water is available — specifically the hot springs of Tiberias, *chamei Tverya*, which are naturally heated and therefore constitute genuine hot water without the drawbacks of drawn water (*she'uvim*). If so, the question of whether cold water is forbidden on *Tisha B'Av* would remain unanswered by the *braita*. But the *Gemara* pushes back: if that were the scenario, then Rabbi Chanina *sgan hakohanim's* statement — that it is appropriate to forgo *mikvah* one day a year for the sake of the Temple's memory — loses its force. If cold water were available and permitted, one could simply go to a cold *mikvah*; there would be nothing to forego. The force of his statement depends precisely on the assumption that cold water itself is also forbidden on *Tisha B'Av*. This strongly supports the position that *avelut*, including *Tisha B'Av*, prohibits even cold water.

One further complication is noted but left for the continuing discussion: *Yom Kippur* presents its own case. Unlike *ta'anit tzibur*, which prohibits only hot water, and unlike *Tisha B'Av*, which is classified under *avelut* — *Yom Kippur* also prohibits cold water, yet it does not fit neatly into either of the two categories established by Rav Chisda. *Yom Kippur* is not simply *avelut*, nor is it simply *ta'anit*; it may constitute a third category of its own. This question, and the ongoing question of whether the hot-water-only principle for *ta'anit tzibur* has been established with full certainty or only partially proven, will carry forward into the continuation of the *sugya* on the following *daf*.
