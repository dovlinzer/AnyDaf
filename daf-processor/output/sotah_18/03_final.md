# Sotah 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ketivah & Mechika Lishmah

### Two Scrolls, One Vessel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: כָּתַב שְׁתֵּי מְגִילּוֹת לִשְׁתֵּי סוֹטוֹת, וּמְחָקָן לְתוֹךְ כּוֹס אֶחָד, מַהוּ? כְּתִיבָה לִשְׁמָהּ בָּעֵינַן, וְהָאִיכָּא, אוֹ דִילְמָא בָּעֵינַן נָמֵי מְחִיקָה לִשְׁמָהּ?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava raised a dilemma: If one wrote two scrolls for two** separate ***sota* women but** then **erased** both of the scrolls **in one cup, what is** the *halakha*? Do **we require** that only the **writing** be performed **for the sake of** a specific woman, in which case **that is** accomplished here? **Or perhaps we require** that **also** the **erasure** be performed **for the sake of** a specific woman, which is not accomplished here, since both scrolls are erased together?

The daf opens with a question posed by Rava (*ba'ei Rava*) concerning the practical mechanics of the *sotah* ritual. Suppose a *kohen* is processing two *sotot* simultaneously and writes two *megillot* — one for each woman — and then scrapes them both into a single vessel. May both women drink from that one vessel?

### Individual Process Requirements

The underlying issue is the requirement of *ketivah lishmah* — that the scroll must be written specifically for her, as the verse states *vechatav lah hakohen*, "and the priest shall write for her." This requirement has already been established: the scroll cannot be pre-written, and it must be composed after she takes the oath. The *ketivah lishmah* requirement is clearly satisfied here, since each scroll was written individually for its respective woman. But the Gemara raises a further possibility: perhaps *mechika lishmah* is also required — that the act of scraping must itself be individualized for each woman.

The concern with simultaneous scraping is that even if one's knife moved across one woman's parchment for her sake and across the other woman's parchment for hers, the overall *process* of producing the bitter waters was not focused on one individual woman. Rather, it was a single process being conducted for two women at once. Whether *lishmah* requires that the action be understood as distinctly oriented toward a single individual — not merely labeled in one's mind — is the substance of the question.

### Mixed Waters Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר בָּעֵינַן נָמֵי מְחִיקָה לִשְׁמָהּ, מְחָקָן בִּשְׁתֵּי כוֹסוֹת וְחָזַר וְעֵירְבָן, מַהוּ? מְחִיקָה לִשְׁמָהּ בָּעֵינַן, וְהָאִיכָּא, אוֹ דִילְמָא הָא לָאו דִּידַהּ קָא שָׁתְיָא וְהָא לָאו דִּידַהּ קָא שָׁתְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that **we require** that **also** the **erasure** be **for the sake of** each specific woman, then if the priest **erased them in two** different **cups and** afterward **mixed** the water from both together **again, what is** the *halakha*? **Do we require** that only the **erasure** be **for the sake of** a specific woman, in which case **that is** accomplished here? **Or perhaps** since **this** *sota* **does not drink** from only **her own** water **and that** *sota* **does not drink** from only **her own** water, the water is disqualified?

The Gemara then extends the question through a series of progressive scenarios. Suppose one concedes that *mechika lishmah* is required, and proceeds correctly: each woman's scroll is scraped into its own separate vessel. Now, however, the contents of those two vessels are poured together into one. The *ketivah* and *mechika* were each performed properly and individually, but the resulting waters are now commingled. Is that a problem?

The Gemara formulates this concern sharply: *hai lav didah ka shatya v'hai lav didah ka shatya* — each woman is drinking water that is not exclusively hers. The question is whether drinking requires that the water be precisely the water prepared for that individual.

### Breira & Re-division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר הָא לָאו דִּידַהּ קָא שָׁתְיָא וְהָא לָאו דִּידַהּ קָא שָׁתְיָא, חָזַר וְחִלְּקָן, מַהוּ? יֵשׁ בְּרֵירָה, אוֹ אֵין בְּרֵירָה? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** furthermore, **if you say** that the water is disqualified because **this one does not drink** from only **her own** water **and that one does not drink** from only **her own** water, what if after mixing the two cups of water together the priest **divided them again** into two cups and gave one to each? **What is** the *halakha* then? **Is there** retroactive **clarification,** in which case one may claim that each woman drank her own water, **or is there no** retroactive **clarification?** The Gemara responds: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The Gemara carries the inquiry one step further: suppose after mixing the waters, one then re-divides them into two separate vessels. Is this valid? The Gemara invokes the concept of *breira* — the legal principle of retroactive determination. Do we say that upon re-division, each vessel is deemed to contain the water that was originally hers? Or do we say *ein breira*, and the re-division accomplishes nothing, since the waters cannot magically self-separate along their original lines?

The Gemara concludes all three sub-questions with *teiku* — the matter remains unresolved.

One might note how peculiar it would be to invoke *breira* in the conventional sense here. Normally, *breira* concerns situations where something was never distinctly identified at the outset and we ask retroactively which portion was always meant to belong to a particular party — such as brothers dividing an inherited estate, or grain designated as *terumah* only after the fact. In those cases, nothing was mixed; rather, something existed in an undifferentiated state and was eventually assigned. But here, two separately prepared, distinguishable waters were fully merged. Saying that upon re-division each woman receives precisely her original water seems to invoke not *breira* in the usual halakhic sense but something approaching outright magic.

### Tosafot on Breira

Tosafot addresses this difficulty directly and at length. Tosafot begins by noting that ordinary *breira* cannot mean that a fully diffused mixture retroactively resolves back into its original components. If that were possible, one could solve any case of *taarovet* — say, *terumah* wine mixed into *chullin* wine — simply by pouring into two vessels and declaring that the *terumah* went into one of them. Clearly that is untenable.

Tosafot therefore reframes the Gemara's question. The real concern with commingling the waters, Tosafot argues, is not that each woman happens to drink water that belongs to another woman. Drinking someone else's water is not itself the problem. Rather, when the two separately prepared waters are merged into a single vessel from which both women drink, the two distinct ritual processes are effectively collapsed into one. The individuality of each woman's *sotah* ordeal is dissolved when everything ends in a single common cup.

If that is the correct framing, then what the Gemara means when it asks about *yesh breira* after re-division is not whether the waters physically self-separate along their original lines — that is impossible and Tosafot explicitly rejects it — but rather whether re-dividing into separate vessels restores the appearance and legal reality of two distinct processes. Once each woman is drinking from her own cup — a cup dedicated exclusively to her, in which only she is drinking — the *mechika* performed for her and the *ketivah* written for her can again be understood as a complete, individualized ritual, even if some of the other woman's water happens to be admixed within. The re-division may be sufficient to reconstitute the distinctiveness of the process in each woman's case.

Tosafot acknowledges that this reading is difficult and the full formulation remains complex, but the thrust is: *yesh breira* in this context means not physical self-selection but rather that re-division re-establishes the ritual distinctiveness that the commingling had disrupted. The Gemara, notably, frames all of this in purely halakhic terms — whether the process was conducted *for her* — rather than in terms of any metaphysical power inhering in the waters themselves.

## Drinking Methods

### Straw/Sieve Drinking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: הִשְׁקָהּ בְּסִיב, מַהוּ? בִּשְׁפוֹפֶרֶת, מַהוּ? דֶּרֶךְ שְׁתִיָּה בְּכָךְ, אוֹ אֵין דֶּרֶךְ שְׁתִיָּה בְּכָךְ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised a dilemma: If** the priest **administered** the bitter water to the *sota* **to drink through** a palm **fiber, what is** the *halakha*? Or if he administered it **through a tube, what is** the *halakha*? **Is this** considered **a normal manner of drinking, or is it not** considered **a normal manner of drinking,** in which case the act is invalid? The Gemara responds: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Rava poses an additional question: *hashka besiv* — what is the law if the woman drank through a fiber, or through a straw (*bishfoferet*)? Is that a valid mode of drinking? The operative question is *miderech shtiah bekach o ein derech shtiah bekach* — is this a normal manner of drinking or not? The Gemara concludes *teiku*.

Tosafot notes a parallel case discussed elsewhere in the Talmud regarding eating *matzah* when it is wrapped in fiber (*karchah besiv*) — whether that constitutes valid *achilah*. However, Tosafot points out that the cases are distinguishable: when food is encased in something, the casing interposes between the food and the throat, raising a question of *chatzitzah*. By contrast, a straw or fiber tube does not actually surround the liquid as it passes down the throat; it merely conveys the liquid from cup to mouth. The question here is therefore more genuinely whether drinking via a conveyance is *derech shtiah* — the normal manner of drinking as envisioned by the Torah — rather than whether there is an interposition.

The *Aruch* is cited with an interpretation that sheds further light. According to the *Aruch*, the fiber (*siv*) was placed into the water, absorbed it like a sponge, and then the saturated fiber was brought to her mouth, from which she sucked the water out. This makes the question considerably more compelling, since it is far less obvious that sucking water from an absorbent material constitutes *derech shtiah* in the manner of the verse *vehishka et hamayim* — "and he shall make her drink the water."

### Spilled Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב אָשֵׁי: נִשְׁפְּכוּ מֵהֶן וְנִשְׁתַּיְּירוּ מֵהֶן, מַהוּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi raised a dilemma:** If some **of the** water of the *sota* **spilled** out **and some of it remained** in the cup, **what is** the *halakha*? Is it sufficient for the woman to drink some of the water in which the scroll has been erased or must she drink all of it? The Gemara responds: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Rav Ashi raises a final question: *nishpechu mehen venishtayru mehen* — what if some of the water spilled and she drank only what remained? The Gemara again concludes *teiku*.

## Two Oaths & Curse

### Repeated Oath in Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב: שְׁתֵּי שְׁבוּעוֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בַּסּוֹטָה. לָמָּה? אַחַת קוֹדֶם שֶׁנִּמְחֲקָה מְגִילָּה, וְאַחַת לְאַחַר שֶׁנִּמְחֲקָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Zeira says** that **Rav says:** With regard to **the two oaths that are stated with regard to the *sota*:** "And the priest shall cause her to swear" (Numbers 5:19), and: "Then the priest shall cause the woman to swear with the oath of cursing" (Numbers 5:21), **why** are they both necessary? **One** must be administered **before the scroll is erased and one** must be administered **after it is erased.**

Rabbi Zeira, citing Rav, raises the question: *shtei shevuot ha'amurot beSotah lamah* — why does the Torah mention the making of an oath twice in the *sotah* passage? The structure of the relevant verses is as follows: the first instance of *vehishbia otah hakohen* introduces the conditional formulation — if you have not gone astray you will be cleared, and if you have gone astray... — without completing the consequence. The second instance of *vehishbia hakohen et ha'ishah bishvuat ha'alah* then introduces the explicit curse: God shall make you an *alah* and an oath among your people, the water shall enter your belly.

One might have thought that one oath precedes the scraping of the scroll and the other follows it. But Rava objects: both oaths as they appear in the text clearly precede the scraping. That interpretation is therefore untenable.

### Oath with/without Alah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבָא: תַּרְוַיְיהוּ קוֹדֶם שֶׁנִּמְחֲקָה מְגִילָּה כְּתִיבָן? אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: אַחַת שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ עִמָּהּ אָלָה, וְאַחַת שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין עִמָּהּ אָלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava objects to this: Both of the** oaths **are written** in the Torah **before** any mention of **the scroll** being **erased.** What is the basis to claim that one oath was administered afterward? **Rather, Rava said:** While both oaths are administered before the *sota* drinks, the two oaths are different: **One is an oath that has a curse with it, and one is an oath that does not have a curse with it.**

Rava therefore offers an alternative: one instance refers to a *shvuah she'yesh imah alah* — an oath accompanied by a curse — and the other to a *shvuah she'ein imah alah* — an oath without a curse. The first, more conditional presentation is an oath without an explicit curse, while the second invokes the full force of the *alah*.

### Defining Shvuah Sheyesh…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ עִמָּהּ אָלָה? אָמַר רַב עַמְרָם אָמַר רַב: ״מַשְׁבִּיעַנִי עָלַיִךְ שֶׁלֹּא נִטְמֵאת, שֶׁאִם נִטְמֵאת יָבוֹאוּ בִּיךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of **an oath that has a curse with it?** What is the language of this oath? **Rav Amram says** that **Rav says:** The priest says: **I administer an oath to you** that you are honest in your claim **that you were not defiled, as, if you were defiled,** all these curses **will come upon you.**

The Gemara then struggles to define precisely what constitutes a *shvuah she'yesh imah alah*. Rav Abba citing Rav offers: *mashpiani alayich she-lo nitmeis she-im nitmeis yavo'u bach* — I am making you swear that you did not sin, because if you sinned the water will come upon you. The curse functions as the motivating consequence of the oath — swear truthfully and understand that if you are lying, this is the result.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אָלָה לְחוֹדַהּ קָיְימָא וּשְׁבוּעָה לְחוֹדַהּ קָיְימָא! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: ״מַשְׁבִּיעַנִי עָלַיִךְ שֶׁאִם נִטְמֵאת יָבוֹאוּ בִּיךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** This is insufficient, as **the curse stands by itself and the oath stands by itself.** They are said in separate statements, and it cannot be considered to be an oath with a curse. **Rather, Rava said:** The priest says: **I administer an oath to you that if you were defiled,** all these curses **will come upon you.**

Rava objects that this construction leaves the curse as a separate, parallel element: the *alah* stands on its own and the *shvuah* stands on its own, but the oath does not actually incorporate the curse. Rava therefore proposes: *mashpiani alayich she-im nitmeis yavo'u bach* — I am making you swear that if you have sinned the waters will come upon you. But Rav Ashi counters: that formulation contains a curse but where is the oath? She is merely being told what will happen; she is not attesting to anything.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אָלָה — אִיכָּא, שְׁבוּעָה — לֵיכָּא! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: ״מַשְׁבִּיעַנִי עָלַיִךְ שֶׁלֹּא נִטְמֵאת, וְאִם נִטְמֵאת יָבוֹאוּ בִּיךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** Even this is insufficient, as **there is a curse** but **there is no oath** that she was not defiled. **Rather, Rav Ashi said:** The priest must say: **I administer an oath to you that you were not defiled and** that **if you were defiled** all these curses **will come upon you.** Here the oath itself includes the curse.

Rav Ashi ultimately proposes: *mashpiani alayich she-lo nitmeis v'im nitmeis yavo'u bach* — I am making you swear that you did not sin, and if you sinned let the waters come upon you. The precise distinction between this and Rav Abba's original formulation is not entirely transparent, and the Gemara acknowledges the difficulty.

### Rashi on Two Oaths

Rashi offers a clarifying framework. In Rashi's reading, the *shvuah* of the woman is exclusively her attestation to the truth of her declaration: *mashpiani alayich she-yehai emes mah she-amart lefaneinu she-lo nitmeis* — I am making you swear that what you have said before us — that you did not sin — is true. That is the *shvuah* in the strict sense: the woman swears to the veracity of her own statement.

The second element — *v'im nitmeis yavo'u bach mayim* — is the *alah*, the curse, which the *kohen* calls upon to take effect upon the woman if she is lying. Rashi understands this as the *kohen* forswearing the curse to do its duty — invoking the *alah* as one might invoke witnesses or heavenly forces — in the manner of *ha'azinu hashamayim va'adabera*, calling upon heaven and earth as witnesses. The *kohen* is not administering the curse as an addendum; he is actively summoning it into its role. Thus there are genuinely two distinct acts: the woman's oath attesting to her truthfulness, and the *kohen*'s invocation of the curse to operate upon her if her oath is false. The *shvuah* and the *alah* are separate in nature but bound together in the ceremony.

## Amen Amen & Scope

### Double Amen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עַל מָה הִיא אוֹמֶרֶת ״אָמֵן״ ״אָמֵן״ — אָמֵן עַל הָאָלָה, אָמֵן עַל הַשְּׁבוּעָה. אָמֵן מֵאִישׁ זֶה, אָמֵן מֵאִישׁ אַחֵר. אָמֵן שֶׁלֹּא שָׂטִיתִי אֲרוּסָה וּנְשׂוּאָה
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **With regard to what does she say: "Amen, amen"** (Number 5:22), twice, as recorded in the verse? The mishna explains that it includes of the following: **Amen on the curse,** as she accepts the curse upon herself if she is guilty, and **amen on the oath,** as she declares that she is not defiled. She states: **Amen** if I committed adultery **with this man** about whom I was warned, **amen** if I committed adultery **with another man. Amen that I did not stray** when I was **betrothed nor** after I was **married,**

The Mishnah states that the woman responds *amen amen* — the double affirmation. The Gemara explains: *amen al ha-alah, amen al ha-shvuah* — one *amen* accepts the *alah* and one *amen* affirms the *shvuah*. That is, with one *amen* she declares: yes, I accept the consequence — let the water come upon me if I have lied. With the other she declares: yes, I am swearing that I am telling the truth. Rashi accordingly reads *amen shelo nitmesi* as *kabbalas shvuah* — acceptance of the oath — and *amen v'im nitmesi yavo'u vi* as *kabbalas alah* — acceptance of the curse.

The principle of *gilgul shvuah* is also operative here. Once a party has been validly brought before the court and compelled to take an oath on some matter, additional oaths may be attached. Consequently, since the husband is entitled to compel her oath regarding the specific man of suspicion, the court can also extend the oath to cover any man — she is swearing to her fidelity broadly, not only with respect to the particular individual named in the *kinui*.

### Different Men & Periods

The Mishnah further specifies: *amen me'ish zeh, amen me'ish acher* — one *amen* affirming she did not sin with this man, and one *amen* affirming she did not sin with any other man. The *sotah* ritual thus functions as an occasion to extend the scope of the oath well beyond the specific suspected incident.

### Arusah & Nesuah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם, וּכְנוּסָה. אָמֵן שֶׁלֹּא נִטְמֵאתִי, וְאִם נִטְמֵאתִי — יָבוֹאוּ בִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **nor as a widow waiting for my *yavam*** to perform levirate marriage, since a woman at that stage is prohibited from engaging in sexual intercourse with any men, **nor** when **married** through levirate marriage to the *yavam*; **amen that I did not become defiled, and if I did become defiled, may** all these curses **come upon me.**

The Mishnah states: *amen shelo satisi arusah unesua'ah* — she says *amen* that she did not commit adultery when she was an *arusah* and when she was a *nesuah*. The husband can ordinarily initiate the *sotah* process only during the period of *nisuin*, but since the oath is being administered, its scope may extend retroactively to her period of *eirusin* as well. Were she to have committed adultery during *eirusin*, she would have been forbidden to him; therefore the oath may encompass that period.

### Shomeret Yevam Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל שֶׁתִּבָּעֵל וְלֹא הָיְתָה אֲסוּרָה לוֹ — לֹא הָיָה מַתְנֶה עִמָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **This is the principle:** In **every** case **where** if **she would engage in sexual intercourse** with someone else **she would not become forbidden to** her husband due to this act, **he may not stipulate with her** that her oath include that act. The oath can include only cases in which she would be rendered forbidden to him.

The Mishnah adds: *veshomeret yevama mekunesah* — the oath may also extend to conduct during her period as a *shomeret yavam*, after her first husband died and before the *yavam* performed *yibbum*. This raises an immediate difficulty: it is not at all obvious that a *shomeret yavam* who sleeps with another man is thereby forbidden to the *yavam*. The prohibition of *lo tihiyeh eshes hameit hachutzah le'ish zar* is an *issur lav*, a negative prohibition, but that does not automatically mean that such conduct renders her *asurah* to the brother-in-law in the way that adultery renders a woman forbidden to her husband. The Mishnah's inclusion of this case implies that it does — and the Gemara will interrogate that implication.

The Mishnah concludes with the principle: *zeh klal, kol she-bi-ybeah lo haytah asurah lo, lo hayah matneh imah* — any circumstance in which intercourse with another man would not render her forbidden to him is outside the scope of the oath. The converse likewise holds: wherever such intercourse would render her forbidden, that period falls within the scope of the oath.

## Shomeret Yevam Forbidden?

### Rav Hamnuna Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם שֶׁזִּינְּתָה — אֲסוּרָה לִיבָמָהּ. מִמַּאי — מִדְּקָתָנֵי שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם וּכְנוּסָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Hamnuna says:** In the case of **a widow awaiting her *yavam* who committed adultery, she** becomes **forbidden to her *yavam*. From where** is this derived? It is **from** the fact **that** the mishna **teaches: Nor as a widow waiting for my *yavam*** to perform levirate marriage, **nor** when **married** through levirate marriage.

Rav Hamnuna draws a significant halakhic conclusion from the structure of the Mishnah: *shomeret yavam she-zintah asurah li-yvamah* — a woman awaiting *yibbum* who commits adultery is forbidden to the *yavam*. His argument is straightforward: the Mishnah allows the *yavam* to extend the oath to cover her conduct as a *shomeret yavam*. But the Mishnah also states explicitly that oath extension is only possible where intercourse would have rendered her forbidden. The only way both clauses can coexist is if sleeping with another man as a *shomeret yavam* does in fact render her forbidden to the *yavam*.

### EY Disagreement & Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא אֲסִירָה — מִשּׁוּם הָכִי מַתְנֵה בַּהֲדַהּ, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לָא אֲסִירָה — הֵיכִי מַתְנֵה בַּהֲדַהּ? וְהָתְנַן, זֶה הַכְּלָל: כֹּל שֶׁאִילּוּ תִּיבָּעֵל וְלֹא תְּהֵא אֲסוּרָה לוֹ — לֹא הָיָה מַתְנֶה עִמָּהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Granted, if you say** that a woman awaiting levirate marriage who committed adultery **is forbidden** to the *yavam*, **due to that** reason a husband **may stipulate with** a *sota* that she take an oath that she did not commit adultery while she was awaiting levirate marriage with him. **But if you say that** such a woman **does not become forbidden, how can** a husband **stipulate** this **with her? But didn't we learn** in the mishna that **this is the principle:** In **every** case **where** if **she would engage in sexual intercourse** with someone else **she would not become forbidden to** her husband due to this act, **he may not stipulate with her** that her oath include that act? Clearly she must become forbidden to the *yavam* if she commits adultery.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי בְּמַעְרְבָא: לֵית הִילְכְתָא כְּרַב הַמְנוּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **They say in the West,** Eretz Yisrael: **The *halakha* is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Hamnuna.** A woman awaiting levirate marriage who commits adultery does not become forbidden to the *yavam*.

The Sages of Eretz Yisrael, however, rule otherwise: *leis hilkheta ke-Rav Hamnuna*. Despite acknowledging that Rav Hamnuna's reading of the Mishnah is compelling as *peshat*, they reject his conclusion. Their reasoning: the Mishnah's inclusion of the *shomeret yavam* case reflects a minority opinion. *Ha manni Rabbi Akiva hi* — this is the position of Rabbi Akiva.

### Why Not Rav Hamnuna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא דְּקָתָנֵי ״שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם וּכְנוּסָה״ — הָא מַנִּי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא, דְּאָמַר: אֵין קִידּוּשִׁין תּוֹפְסִין בְּחַיָּיבֵי לָאוִין, וּמְשַׁוֵּי לַהּ כִּי עֶרְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** with regard to **that which is taught** in the mishna: **Nor as a widow waiting for my *yavam*** to perform levirate marriage, **nor** when **married** through levirate marriage, in accordance with **whose** opinion **is this? It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who says: Betrothal does not take effect** even **for** those who would be merely **liable for** violating ordinary **prohibitions** were they to be married. All agree that betrothal does not take effect for forbidden unions that carry the punishment of *karet*, e.g., unions between siblings. However, according to Rabbi Akiva, in matters of personal status an ordinary Torah prohibition is equivalent to prohibitions that carry the punishment of *karet*. **And** he therefore holds that just as a wife who commits adultery, rendering her liable to receive *karet*, is forbidden to her husband and must be divorced, so too, a widow awaiting her *yavam* who commits adultery, violating an ordinary Torah prohibition, **is considered to be a woman forbidden to** her *yavam*.

Rabbi Akiva holds a sweeping general principle: *ein kiddushin topsin be-chayavei lavin* — *kiddushin* is not binding when contracted with one who is prohibited by a negative commandment. Moreover, the offspring of such a union is a *mamzer*. Under Rabbi Akiva's framework, any prohibition at the level of *issur lav* is treated with extraordinary legal weight: not merely does one violate the prohibition, but the relationship itself is legally unrecognized, the *kiddushin* is void, and the child bears the status of *mamzer*.

Applied to *shomeret yavam*: the prohibition *lo tihiyeh eshes hameit hachutzah le'ish zar* is an *issur lav*, and under Rabbi Akiva's general approach any relationship entered into in violation of a *lav* is treated as if it were a prohibited union in the full sense — *kiddushin* does not bind, and the offspring is a *mamzer*. It is this general position of Rabbi Akiva that explains why, in his view, a *shomeret yavam* who sleeps with another man would be forbidden to the *yavam*: not because *yibbum* constitutes a quasi-marriage in some special sense, but because Rabbi Akiva weights every *issur lav* so heavily.

Rav Hamnuna's approach, by contrast, was not premised on Rabbi Akiva's general principle. He argued from the distinctive nature of *yibbum* itself: the *zikah* — the halakhic bond connecting the widow to the brother — is a form of quasi-marriage, and betrayal of that bond is akin to adultery. That is why, in his view, she is forbidden to him.

The Sages of Eretz Yisrael make a crucial distinction: the Mishnah's extension of the oath to the *shomeret yavam* case is not a universally held position. It reflects Rabbi Akiva's particular view. We do not rule like Rabbi Akiva here, and therefore a *shomeret yavam* who sleeps with another man is not, in the view of the majority, rendered forbidden to the *yavam*. The *kiddushin* of another man with her would be binding, she does not produce a *mamzer*, and she remains eligible for *yibbum*.

It is worth noting that there is no other case of an *issur lav* that creates the kind of relational context in which "sleeping with another man" could constitute something analogous to adultery. The only two legal frameworks in which a woman's sexual conduct with another man impinges upon an existing relational obligation are *nisuin* and *shomeret yavam*. Rabbi Akiva's position about *chayavei lavin* is thus distinctively operative precisely in the *shomeret yavam* case — not because *yibbum* is uniquely quasi-marital, but because Rabbi Akiva's principle is general, and *shomeret yavam* happens to be the only *issur lav* context in which the question of such betrayal can even arise.

## Oath Scope Extension

### Previous & Later Marriages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: מַהוּ שֶׁיַּתְנֶה אָדָם עַל נִישּׂוּאִין הָרִאשׁוֹנִים, עַל נִישּׂוּאֵי אָחִיו, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raised a dilemma:** If a man divorced his wife and then remarried her, and she then secluded herself and came to drink the water of a *sota*, **what is** the *halakha* as to **whether a man** in that situation **may stipulate** with her **concerning** their **first marriage?** May he compel her to include in her oath that she did not commit adultery then either? Similarly, may a *yavam* stipulate with his *yevama* **concerning the marriage of his brother,** requiring her to attest that she did not commit adultery prior to the brother's death? **What is** the *halakha*?

Rabbi Yirmiah raises a further pair of questions regarding the extension of the *sotah* oath. First: *mahu she-yatne adam al nisuin harishonim* — can a man stipulate that the oath cover conduct during a previous marriage between them? That is, if a man divorced his wife and then remarried her, and now in the second marriage she is undergoing the *sotah* process, can he extend the oath to encompass any adultery she may have committed during the first marriage? Second: *al nisuei achiv* — can a *yavam* who performed *yibbum* extend the oath to cover conduct during the *arusah* period of the deceased brother's marriage, when that deceased brother was the husband?

These questions should be understood in light of the Mishnah's concluding principle: oath extension is permissible only where intercourse would have rendered her forbidden. Both scenarios above would satisfy that condition. Were she to have committed adultery during their first marriage, she would currently be forbidden to him in the second marriage. Were she to have committed adultery during the period when she was married to the deceased brother, she would have been ineligible for *yibbum*; the *yavam* could not have married her at all.

### Forbidden = Oath Allowed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, זֶה הַכְּלָל: כֹּל שֶׁתִּיבָּעֵל וְלֹא תְּהֵא אֲסוּרָה לוֹ — לֹא הָיָה מַתְנֶה עִמָּהּ. הָא אֲסִירָה — הָכִי נָמֵי דְּמַתְנֶה. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proposes: **Come** and **hear** evidence from the conclusion of the mishna: **This is the principle:** In **every** case **where** if **she would engage in sexual intercourse** with someone else **she would not become forbidden to** her husband due to this act, **he may not stipulate with her** that her oath include that act. One may **therefore** infer **that he may indeed stipulate** with her concerning any case in which **she** would become **forbidden.** In both of the cases in question the woman would have become forbidden to her husband if she had been guilty. Therefore, the Gemara concludes: Indeed, **conclude from** the mishna that he is entitled to stipulate in these cases.

The Gemara resolves Rabbi Yirmiah's questions by citing the Mishnah's general rule: *kol shetiba'el velo te'aser lo, lo hayah matneh imah* — wherever intercourse would not forbid her to him, he cannot stipulate; the logical contrapositive confirms that wherever it would forbid her, he may stipulate. Since in both of Rabbi Yirmiah's cases the conduct in question would render her forbidden in the relevant relational context, the oath extension is permitted. *Shema mina* — this may be derived from the rule.

One may also note a subtle point regarding the word *matneh*. Throughout this discussion, when the Gemara refers to the extension of the oath beyond its immediate default scope, it consistently uses the language of *matneh* — stipulation, active extension. This implies that such extensions are not automatic. The *yavam* or husband must explicitly extend the oath if he wishes it to apply to these additional circumstances; they do not take effect without such stipulation.

### Future Marriage Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב אָשֵׁי: מַהוּ שֶׁיַּתְנֶה אָדָם עַל נִישּׂוּאִין הָאַחֲרוֹנִים? הַשְׁתָּא מִיהָא לָא אֲסִירָה לֵיהּ, אוֹ דִילְמָא זִימְנִין דִּמְגָרֵשׁ לַהּ וַהֲדַר מַהְדַּר לַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi raised a dilemma: What is** the *halakha* as to **whether a man may stipulate** with his wife **concerning a later marriage,** in the event that he would divorce her and then remarry her? Do we say: **Now, at least,** if she remains faithful during this marriage, **she is not forbidden to him? Or perhaps** this includes a later marriage, as **sometimes** a man **divorces** his wife **and remarries her,** and if she then commits adultery she will become forbidden to him.

Rav Ashi poses the converse case: *mahu she-yatne adam al nisuin ha'achronim* — can a man, in the context of Rabbi Meir's position that the oath can be future-oriented, stipulate that the oath cover conduct during a potential second marriage between them, if they were to divorce and remarry? The argument in favor: if she were to commit adultery after they remarried, she would be forbidden to him in that second marriage. The argument against: in the context of the current marriage, that future adultery does not render her presently forbidden. *Hashta meiha lo asira lei* — right now she is not forbidden to him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַכֹּל שָׁוִין שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה מַתְנֶה עִמָּהּ לֹא עַל קוֹדֶם שֶׁתִּתְאָרֵס, וְלֹא עַל אַחַר שֶׁתִּתְגָּרֵשׁ. נִסְתְּרָה לְאֶחָד וְנִטְמֵאת, וְאַחַר כָּךְ יַחְזִירֶנָּה — לֹא הָיָה מַתְנֶה. הָא יַחְזִירֶנָּה וְתִיטָּמֵא, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּמַתְנֵי. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear** evidence from the mishna: **All agree that he may stipulate with her** through this oath **neither with regard to** what she did **before becoming betrothed** to him, **nor with regard to** what she will do **after she becomes divorced** from him. And if while divorced she **secluded herself** with another man **and became defiled, and afterward** her husband **took her back** and remarried her, **he may not stipulate** with her with regard to the period in which she was divorced, since that act does not render her forbidden to him. The Gemara infers: **But** if **he remarries her and she** then **becomes defiled,** she would be forbidden to him. Therefore, **he may indeed stipulate** with her in advance with regard to this. The Gemara states: Indeed, **conclude from** the mishna that this is so.

The Gemara resolves this from the Mishnah's language as well. The Mishnah specifies that he cannot stipulate about the period of divorce, but does not say he cannot stipulate about a future remarriage. By implication, stipulating about conduct during a potential remarriage is permissible — just as he may stipulate about conduct during a prior marriage — since in both cases, were she to sin at those times, she would be forbidden to him in that marital context. The symmetry is exact: past first marriage and future second marriage are treated equivalently. *Shema mina*.

## Double Jeopardy

### Three Jeopardy Views

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״זֹאת תּוֹרַת הַקְּנָאֹת״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָאִשָּׁה שׁוֹתָה וְשׁוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** The verse states: **"This is the law of jealousy"** (Numbers 5:29), indicating that the same law is to be carried out in all cases of jealousy. This **teaches that the woman drinks and repeats,** i.e., she must drink a second time if she becomes a *sota* again.

The *baraita* is introduced: *zot torat hakanot* — "this is the law of jealousy." The *tana kama* reads *zot torat* as an expansive formulation: *melamed she-isha shota ve-shona* — a woman may be made to drink the waters twice.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״זֹאת״, שֶׁאֵין הָאִשָּׁה שׁוֹתָה וְשׁוֹנָה. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מַעֲשֶׂה וְהֵעִיד לְפָנֵינוּ נְחוּנְיָא חוֹפֵר שִׁיחִין שֶׁהָאִשָּׁה שׁוֹתָה וְשׁוֹנָה, וְקִיבַּלְנוּ עֵדוּתוֹ בִּשְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים, אֲבָל לֹא בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The word **"this"** in the verse is a restricting term, indicating **that the woman does not drink and repeat. Rabbi Yehuda said:** There was **an incident** in which **Neḥunya the ditch digger testified before us** in the name of his teachers **that the woman drinks and repeats, and we accepted his testimony with regard to two men, but not with regard to one man.** Even if she drinks the water of a *sota* while married to her first husband, she must drink again after violating a warning by her second husband. However, one husband cannot have his wife drink twice.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *zot* is a limiting word, implying *she'einah shota ve-shona* — she may not be made to drink a second time.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין הָאִשָּׁה שׁוֹתָה וְשׁוֹנָה, בֵּין בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד בֵּין בִּשְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* concludes: **And the Rabbis say: The woman does not drink and repeat, whether with regard to one man or with regard to two men.**

The *Chachamim* introduce a third position: *ein isha shota ve-shona bein be-ish echad bein bishnei anashim* — a woman never undergoes the ordeal a second time, whether the second instance involves the same husband or a different one.

Thus the full spectrum: the *tana kama* permits a second ordeal even with the same husband; Rabbi Yehuda permits it with a different husband but not the same; the *Chachamim* prohibit a second ordeal altogether, regardless of the husband.

### Nechunya Testimony

Rabbi Yehuda reports: *maaseh ve-heid lefaneinu Nechunya Chofer Shichin* — Nechunya the Digger of Wells testified before us that a woman may drink twice — but this was accepted only *bishnei anashim*, with two different husbands, not with the same husband. Tosafot cite a *Yerushalmi* in which a woman named Korkemit actually underwent the ordeal three times before Shemaya and Avtalyon — evidence that the ritual did occur historically, and that the debate about double jeopardy reflects a genuine and consequential disagreement. Whether Nechunya's testimony was to a received halakhic tradition or to something he personally witnessed is left open.

The Gemara's presentation of three positions — unlimited repetition, repetition with a different husband only, and no repetition at all — remains to be analyzed further in the discussion that follows.
