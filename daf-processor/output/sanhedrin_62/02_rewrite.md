# Sanhedrin 62 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ahavah Umiyirah

### Acts Without Belief

The daf opens with a discussion that is formally situated not in *Maseches Avodah Zarah* but here in *Sanhedrin* — and this itself is worth noting, since it is in this tractate that the Torah defines precisely which acts of *avodah zarah* carry the death penalty. The *gemara* introduces the case with the word *itmar*, signaling an *amoraic* dispute: what is the law governing one who performs an act of *avodah zarah* — slaughtering an animal (*zivuach*), burning incense (*kitur*), pouring a libation (*nisuch*), bowing down (*hishtachavah*), or any act recognized as the particular mode of worship of a given idol — but does so *me'ahavah umiyirah*, out of love and fear, rather than out of genuine theological belief?

### Rashi Clarifies

*Rashi* explains that *meahava umiyirah* means out of love and fear of a *person*, not of the idol itself — *meahavas adam umeyiras adam velo chashva beliba lelohut*. He did not believe in his heart that this object is divine. The historical resonance is clear: in the medieval period, Jews converted to Christianity not out of conviction but out of social ambition or out of fear of forced conversion and persecution — yet they went through the full motions of worship. The question is whether performing the recognized acts of *avodah zarah* with full awareness that these are the prescribed acts of that worship, but without the attendant belief, constitutes *avodah zarah* for which one is *chayav*.

This question is sharpened by the structure of the *mishnah* itself, which lists specific acts of worship and then separately mentions *vehamikabel alav beloha* — one who accepts the idol as his god. Normally, the performance of the act implicitly carries that acceptance, but what if we know that is not the case?

### Abaye vs. Rava

The *amoraic* dispute is stated directly. *Abaye amar chayav*: Abaye holds one is liable. *Rava amar patur*: Rava holds one is exempt.

### Rava's Counterargument

Rava's reasoning is straightforward: *ei kiblah alei beloha in, ei lo lo* — if one accepted it as a god, yes, one is liable; if not, no. The act without the belief does not constitute *avodah zarah*.

### Abaye's Proof

Abaye attempts to derive his position from the *mishnah*'s formulation. The *mishnah* says "one who worships" and then lists the specific acts. Abaye argues that the opening phrase — "one who worships" — extends liability even to one who does the act regardless of motivation or accompanying belief. Rava, however, deflects this reading: as Rabbi Yirmiya has explained elsewhere in the *sugya*, *oved* refers to a case of worship performed in the normative manner of that idol (*kidarka*), while the specific acts of *zivuach*, *kitur*, and *nisuch* are liable even when performed in a non-standard manner (*shelo kidarka*) because they are classic modes of divine service. The word *oved* is not adding a case of *meahava umiyirah*.

### Haman & Bowing

Abaye then brings a *braisa* as proof. The verse states *lo tishtachaveh lahem velo ta'avdeim* — do not bow down to them and do not worship them. *Lahem atah mishtachaveh aval atah mishtachaveh la'adam kemoscha*: one may not bow to an idol, but one may bow to a fellow human being, as indeed occurs throughout the Torah — Yaakov bows to Esav, and so on — as a gesture of greeting or submission, not of worship. One might then ask: perhaps one could even bow to someone who presents himself as a god and demands worship, like Haman? The assumption among the *rishonim* is that Haman did present himself in this way, which is why Mordechai's refusal was so provocative — had it been merely a show of political deference, the provocation would be inexplicable. The *braisa* therefore adds *velo ta'avdeim*: do not worship them — that extra phrase comes to include even a human being who treats himself as a god. One is forbidden from bowing even to such a person.

This appears to support Abaye, since people would have been bowing to Haman *out of fear* — and yet the *braisa* says this is forbidden as *avodah zarah*. If *miyirah* exempts, why would the *braisa* need to prohibit it?

### Rava's Distinction

Rava responds: *keHaman velo keHaman* — like Haman, but not exactly Haman. The case is like Haman in the sense that the prohibition concerns a human being who presents himself as a god. But it is not exactly the Haman case in the critical respect: *de'ilu Haman miyirah v'hacha lav miyirah* — in the actual Haman case, had people bowed to him, they would not have been *chayav* for *avodah zarah*, because they would have done so out of fear, not out of genuine acceptance of Haman as a deity. The *braisa* uses Haman as the paradigmatic example of a human being claiming divinity, but that very case — where the bowing is done *miyirah* — is precisely the case Rava considers exempt from *chiyuv misa*.

## Martyrdom

### Question Posed

This leads directly to what *Tosafot* identifies as the pressing question. If, according to Rava, an act of *avodah zarah* performed out of *miyirah* is not technically *avodah zarah* at all, why does the law of *yehareg v'al ya'avor* apply? If someone holds a sword to a Jew's throat and demands that he bow down to an idol, he should simply bow — since according to Rava it would not constitute a genuine act of *avodah zarah*. This is a very strong question.

### Tosafot Resolves

*Tosafot*, at the bottom of the amud, addresses this directly: *af al gav deve-perek ben sorer u-moreh amar ba-avodat kochavim yehareg v'al ya'avor, yesh omrim de-chayav limsor atzmo le-chatchilah, mi-kol makom heicha de-lo masar atzmo lo michayav misa*. Yes, one is obligated to give up one's life — but the obligation of *yehareg v'al ya'avor* is not identical to the question of whether the act constitutes a technical violation of *avodah zarah*.

### V'Ahavta Principle

The source of *yehareg v'al ya'avor* is not the laws of *avodah zarah* per se but the obligation derived from *v'ahavta et Hashem Elokecha* — the commandment to love God, to not betray one's faith. Consider: if someone holds a gun to a Jew's head and demands he deny God, accept atheism, or convert to Islam — none of those involve *avodah zarah* technically — yet *yehareg v'al ya'avor* still applies. What demands martyrdom is the act of betraying one's relationship with God, not merely whether the specific transgression meets the formal definition of *avodah zarah*. This is why, *Tosafot* explains, even according to Rava, *yehareg v'al ya'avor* would apply to the Haman case.

### Rambam View

The *Rambam* draws a related but distinct distinction in his *Iggeres Teiman*, dealing with Jews who had converted under duress and wished to return. Even though *yehareg v'al ya'avor* applied and they should have given their lives, when they ultimately submitted under compulsion, the coercion (*ones*) means that the technical act of *avodah zarah* may be treated differently. They may have been *meizid* in violating the *mitzvah* of *mesiras nefesh* — the obligation of martyrdom derived from *v'ahavta es Hashem Elokecha* — but with respect to the formal act of *avodah zarah*, they were *ones*. This reinforces *Tosafot*'s framework: the act under duress, even if required of one to resist unto death, is categorically distinct from whether the act itself constitutes the *chiyuv* of *avodah zarah*. This same logic, incidentally, could explain why *Tosafot* offers *kiddush Hashem* as the reason Mordechai would not bow to Haman even according to Rava — it was *yehareg v'al ya'avor* precisely because it would have appeared to the world as an act of betraying God, even if, technically, it was not *avodah zarah* under compulsion.

## Kohen Gadol Rules

### Braita Introduced

Abaye brings yet another proof, this time from a *braita* concerning a *kohen mashiach* — that is, a *kohen gadol* — who sins in the domain of *avodah zarah*. The general rule for a *kohen gadol* is that his *korban chatas* differs from that of an ordinary Israelite: he brings a *par* (ox) rather than a *seirah* (female goat or sheep), and critically, he is only liable if he both ruled incorrectly for himself and then acted on that ruling — a dual requirement learned from the case of the *par he'elem davar shel tzibur*, the bull brought when the *sanhedrin* itself rules incorrectly. When it comes to *avodah zarah*, however, the Torah specifies a male goat — a *seir* — even for an individual Israelite who transgresses *beshogeg*. Since the *kohen gadol* brings the same offering as the Israelite for this transgression, the question arises: does the *kohen gadol* still require the double condition (erroneous ruling plus act), or does he follow the Israelite's rule and become liable through mistaken action alone?

### Rabbi vs. Sages

The *braita* records a dispute. *Rebbi* says: for the *kohen mashiach* in a case of *avodah zarah*, it is sufficient that he commits *shiggas ma'aseh* — a mistaken act — and he need not have issued a mistaken *psak* for himself first. The *chachamim* say: even here, *he'elam davar* is required — he must have ruled incorrectly for himself — since his general liability still follows the framework of the *kohen gadol*, notwithstanding the identical *korban*. Both agree, however, that the *korban* itself is *k'seirah k'yachid* — like the offering of an ordinary individual. And both agree further that the *kohen gadol* does not bring an *asham taluy* even in a case of doubt about *avodah zarah*, since the *asham taluy* applies only under conditions that the *kohen gadol*'s circumstances do not meet.

### Shiggas Ma'aseh

The *gemara* now asks: what exactly is the case of *shiggas ma'aseh* by *avodah zarah*? This is the crux. For *Shabbos*, a person can transgress *beshogeg* straightforwardly — he forgot it was *Shabbos*, or he forgot that a particular act was forbidden on *Shabbos* — and he nonetheless committed the act. But for *avodah zarah*, what does it mean to err unintentionally? The *gemara* examines three scenarios.

### Three Scenarios

First: he passed by what he thought was a *beis haknesses* and bowed down, intending to worship God. *Harei libo lashamayim* — his heart was directed toward Heaven. This is not *shogeg*; it is not *avodah zarah* at all, since there was no act of worshipping anything alien. Second: he saw a statue and bowed before it. If he accepted it as a deity — *meizid hu* — he is fully liable and not in the category of *shogeg*. If he did not accept it as a deity but bowed as a gesture of respect — *lo klum hu* — it is nothing, not *avodah zarah* at all. The *gemara* arrives at an apparent impasse: either the facts are wrong (and then it is not *avodah zarah* at all), or he accepted it as a god (and then he is *meizid*, not *shogeg*).

### Ahavah Umiyirah Case

The *gemara* concludes: *ela lav me'ahavah umiyirah* — the only coherent case of *shiggas ma'aseh* by *avodah zarah* is precisely the *meahava umiyirah* scenario. He knew this was an idol, he knew these acts were defined as acts of *avodah zarah*, but he believed — incorrectly — that performing them under circumstances of love and fear rendered him exempt. That belief was his *shogeg*. This is the mistake that was not about the act itself but about the *halacha* governing his circumstances. The *gemara* notes that this is the very point Abaye needs: the only way a *kohen gadol* can transgress *avodah zarah* through *shiggas ma'aseh* alone, without a full *he'elam davar* in the manner of a typical erroneous *psak*, is precisely the *meahava umiyirah* case. His mistake was thinking that *meahava umiyirah* was permitted — not that *avodah zarah* itself was permitted, which would be gross ignorance — and so while it was a mistaken *halachic* position, it was sufficiently distinct from the normal *he'elam davar* of a *kohen gadol* to qualify as *shiggas ma'aseh* alone. This is somewhat fine-grained reasoning, and the *gemara* itself acknowledges it as such.

### Rava's Case

For Rava, who holds that *meahava umiyirah* is genuinely exempt, this scenario cannot serve as the *shiggas ma'aseh* case — because there is no *chiyuv* there at all. Rava therefore must say the case is *be'omer mutar legamrei*: the *kohen gadol* thought *avodah zarah* was entirely permitted. This is such a gross misunderstanding that it does not constitute the normal *he'elam davar* of a *kohen gadol*, who typically knows the basic contours of the prohibition but errs in a detail. Rather, *he'elam davar* is *kiyum miktzas uvitul miktzas* — knowing the general framework but getting a specific application wrong. To think the entire prohibition of *avodah zarah* is nonexistent is so far from normal *psak* error that it qualifies as *shiggas ma'aseh* without *he'elam davar*. The *gemara* acknowledges: *omer mutar haynu he'elam davar* — isn't thinking something is permitted the very definition of *he'elam davar*? And replies: *omer mutar legamrei* — when one thinks the entire prohibition is nonexistent, that is categorically different. It is not a case of the normal *he'elam davar* that a *kohen gadol*'s framework contemplates.

Before moving on, a brief word on the *Rambam*'s position regarding *meahava umiyirah* more broadly. Unlike *Rashi* and most *rishonim*, *Rambam* explains *meahava umiyirah* as doing the acts of *avodah zarah* out of love or fear of the *deity itself* — wanting to curry favor with it or avoid its displeasure — but without truly accepting it as a supreme being. According to *Rambam*, that kind of transactional relationship with an idol — hoping it will provide benefit, fearing its wrath — does not constitute the full acceptance of a god. Real acceptance of something as a god means believing it to be the supreme being and acting accordingly. Anything short of that, for *Rambam*, is not the *emunah* — or its antithesis — that the prohibition is aimed at. This yields a remarkably rigorous standard for what it means to truly commit *avodah zarah* in the Rambam's view.

## Multiple Acts

### Zakkai Teaching

The *gemara* now introduces a new teaching: *Tani Rabbi Zakkai kamei de-Rabbi Yochanan* — Rabbi Zakkai taught in front of Rabbi Yochanan. If someone performed *zivach, kitar, niseach, v'hishtachavah* — slaughtered, burned incense, poured a libation, and bowed down — all in one *he'elam*, one continuous state of forgetfulness, *eino chayav ela achas* — he brings only one *korban chatas*. The reasoning is that since all these are simply forms of *avodah*, performing them in one unbroken state of ignorance is analogous to eating multiple *kezaysim* of *chelev* without interruption — where one brings only a single *korban*, since it is all one act in one *he'elam*.

### Yochanan Rejects

Rabbi Yochanan's response was terse and unequivocal: *puk tani li-vara* — go teach this outside. It is not accepted. This is a strong rejection, implying the ruling is simply wrong and outside the bounds of accepted teaching.

### Fire Debate

Rabbi Abba explains the background to this rejection by invoking the dispute between Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Natan regarding the verse *lo teva'aru esh b'chol moshvoseichem b'yom ha-Shabbas* — do not kindle fire on Shabbos. The Torah singles out fire from among all the *melachos*. Why? Rabbi Yosi says: *hav'arah lelav yatza* — it is singled out to teach that fire is merely a *lav*, a negative prohibition, without the death penalty. Rabbi Natan says: *lechalaik yatza* — it is singled out to teach that fire is treated as a distinct category, and from this we derive the principle that all *melachos* are treated separately (*chiluk melachos*), so that doing multiple *melachos* in one *he'elam* generates multiple *korbanot*. Rabbi Abba suggests that the same logic applies by *avodah zarah*: just as fire is singled out from other *melachos*, *hishtachavah* is singled out from *lo ta'avdeim*. According to Rabbi Yosi, that would mean *hishtachavah* is merely a *lav* but not a basis for *chiluk*; according to Rabbi Natan, it teaches *chiluk avodos*. Rabbi Zakkai's teaching would then align with Rabbi Yosi. So why did Rabbi Yochanan reject it outright?

### Hishtachavah Distinction

The *gemara* points to a fundamental asymmetry that makes this parallel untenable. By *Shabbos*, there is a *machloket* between Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Natan precisely because Rabbi Yosi has another source entirely for *chiluk melachos* — the *pasuk* of *me'achas mehena*, as we will see — and therefore he can say fire's singling out teaches a *lav*, not *chiluk*. But by *avodah zarah*, if one does not learn *chiluk avodos* from *hishtachavah*, there is nowhere else to learn it. And since the principle of *chiluk avodos* by *avodah zarah* parallels the principle of *chiluk melachos* by *Shabbos* — which is universally accepted — it follows that *everyone* must agree that *hishtachavah* teaches *chiluk avodos*. There is no room for a view like Rabbi Yosi's here, because by *Shabbos* Rabbi Yosi only disagrees about *fire* because he has *me'achas mehena* as his source for *chiluk*. By *avodah zarah*, absent any alternative source, *hishtachavah* must be doing that work universally.

### Yosef Clarifies

Rav Yosef puts the argument precisely: *dilma ad kan lo ka'amar Rabbi Yossi hatam hav'arah lelav yatza denafka lei sidur melachos me'achas meihena* — perhaps Rabbi Yosi only says fire teaches a *lav* because he already derives the principle of treating each *melacha* separately from *me'achas mehena*. Since he has that source, he can deploy fire to teach something else. But for *avodah zarah*, where no such alternative source exists, *d'kulei alma hishtachava l'chalek yatza* — everyone agrees that *hishtachavah* is singled out to teach *chiluk avodos*. That is why Rabbi Yochanan rejected Rabbi Zakkai's teaching outright: it goes against a principle that is unanimously held.

## Me'Achas Mehena

### Pasuk Explained

The *gemara* now turns to the *pasuk* itself: *ve'asah me'achas mehena* — and he does from one of these. From this phrase, Rabbi Yonasan derives multiple distinct teachings: *achat, me'achat, heina, meheinah* — one, from one, many, from many — and then two further compound cases: *achas she'hi heina* and *heina she'hi achas* — one that is many, and many that is one.

### Shimon Case

*Achat* — one: if one intended to write the name *Shimon* on *Shabbos* and wrote it in full, one is *chayav*. *Me'achas* — from one: if one only wrote *shin mem* — the word *sheim* — having intended to write *Shimon*, since those two letters already constitute a complete word, one is *chayav* even though one did not complete the intended act. The partial act, insofar as it constitutes a completed *melacha*, is sufficient.

### Categories

*Heina* — many: this teaches that there are *avos melacha*, multiple primary categories of *chilul Shabbos*. *Meheinah* — from many: this teaches *toldos*, derivative forms of those primary categories.

### One That Is Many

*Achas she'hi heina* — one that is many: *shegagas Shabbas v'shigagas melachos* — one knew it was *Shabbos* but forgot each individual *melacha* separately — forgot that kindling fire was forbidden, forgot that kneading was forbidden, and so on. Since each forgetfulness is distinct, each *melacha* generates a separate *he'elam* and therefore a separate *chatas*. One act of identity — knowing it is *Shabbos* — combines with multiple distinct acts of forgetfulness about the individual *melachos*, yielding multiple *korbanot*.

### Many That Is One

*Heina she'hi achas* — many that is one: *shegagas Shabbas v'zadon melachos* — one knew full well that each individual *melacha* was forbidden, but forgot that it was *Shabbos* at all. Since there is only one thing forgotten — that it is *Shabbos* — everything falls within a single *he'elam* and one brings only one *korban*. The principle that emerges is clear: it is the structure of one's forgetfulness, not the number of acts, that determines the number of *korbanot*. And this principle of *chiluk melachos* is learned from *me'achas mehena*, which means that even Rabbi Yosi, who uses fire for a different *drasha*, already possesses this principle and need not derive it from fire.

### Avodah Framework

The *gemara* then applies this framework to *avodah zarah*. Could one apply the same *pasuk* of *me'achas mehena* to derive *chiluk avodos* there as well? The attempt is made:

*Achas* — one: *zvicha*, slaughtering an animal — that is a complete act. *Me'achas*: one *siman* — one of the two passages of the trachea and esophagus, analogous to *sheim miShimon*. *Heina*: the primary acts — *zivuach, kitur, nisuch, v'hishtachavah* — the four recognized forms of worship. *Meheinah*, the derivative: the *gemara* gives the example of *shiber makel l'faneha* — breaking a stick before the idol. This is considered analogous to *shechita*, though the *gemara* qualifies that this applies only where the stick is itself a meaningful object of worship for that particular idol — for instance, if worshippers normally dance with this stick before it — making its destruction a meaningful act of devotion comparable to *shechita*. This is treated as a *tolda* of *zivuach*.

Then the compound cases. *Achas she'hi heina* — one knew this was *avodah zarah* but forgot separately that *nisuch* was forbidden and forgot separately that *hishtachavah* was forbidden: each forgetfulness is distinct, generating multiple *korbanot*. *Heina she'hi achas* — *shiggas avodas kochavim b'zadon avodos*: one knew these individual *avodos* were forbidden but forgot *avodah zarah* altogether, yielding one *korban*. But this last scenario circles back to the same difficulty already encountered: what does it mean to forget *avodah zarah* while knowing that specific acts are forbidden? If one did not realize this object was an idol, one has not committed *avodah zarah* at all; if one knew it was an idol and accepted it as a god, one is *meizid*. The *gemara* returns to the same answer as before: *ela mei'ahavah umiyirah* — the overarching thing one forgot was that *meahava umiyirah* constitutes *avodah zarah*. For Abaye, this works as a genuine *shogeg*. For Rava, who holds one is exempt *meahava umiyirah*, the only available scenario is *be'omer mutar* — thinking *avodah zarah* entirely permitted — which the *gemara* equates with the single overarching forgetfulness, analogous to forgetting it is *Shabbos*. In that case, one brings a single *korban* regardless.

## Me'Achas Inapplicable

### Different Korbanos

Having worked through how *me'achas mehena* might apply to *avodah zarah*, the *gemara* now withdraws the entire suggestion. The *pasuk* of *me'achas mehena* appears in the section of the Torah dealing with standard *korban chatas* obligations, where the Torah explicitly distinguishes between different classes of people: a *kohen mashiach* brings a *par*, a *nasi* brings a *seir*, and an individual brings a *kisbah* or *seirah*. The diversity of *korbanot* — and the distinctions that *me'achas mehena* is meant to navigate — presupposes this very structure.

### Same Korban

By *avodah zarah*, however, the *mishna* establishes — and the *amoraim* agree — *v'shavin she'bis'eirah k'yachid*: everyone, from the *kohen gadol* to the ordinary Israelite, brings the same offering. The entire framework of differentiated *korbanot* simply does not apply.

### Hishtachavah Source

Since *me'achas mehena* is embedded in a context that presupposes distinctions of *korbanot* that do not exist in the *avodah zarah* section, it cannot be applied there. The *gemara* concludes: *v'su lo midi* — and nothing more need be said. The *chiluk avodos* of *avodah zarah* is derived, as established, from *hishtachavah* alone.

## Shmuel Teaching

### Shabbos Stricter

When Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda came, he clarified that what Rabbi Zakkai actually taught before Rabbi Yochanan was not the teaching about bringing one *korban* for multiple *avodos*. Rather, the *braisa* was a comparative one: *chomer b'Shabbos mish'ar mitzvos v'chomer b'sh'ar mitzvos mi'be'Shabbos* — there are ways in which *Shabbos* is stricter than other *mitzvos*, and there are ways in which other *mitzvos* are stricter than *Shabbos*. The strictness of *Shabbos*: if one performs two separate *melachos* in one *he'elam*, one is liable separately for each — *chayav al kol achas v'achas*.

### Other Mitzvos

*Ma she'ein kein b'sh'ar mitzvos*: for other *mitzvos*, where there is no principle of *chiluk* among sub-forms of the same prohibition, doing the same violation multiple times in one *he'elam* yields only one *korban*. If one ate *chelev* and *chelev* without interruption of awareness, it is all one *he'elam* and one *korban*.

### Reverse Stringency

The reverse strictness: *b'sh'ar mitzvos, shogeg belo miskavein chayav* — for other *mitzvos*, one who acts *beshogeg* without specific intent (*lo miskavein*) is nonetheless liable for a *korban*. For *Shabbos*, by contrast, *mis'asek* — performing one action while intending another — is exempt: *meleches machsheves asra Torah*, the Torah prohibited only deliberate creative work. So if one meant to pick up an object and accidentally performed a *melacha*, one is *patur* by *Shabbos*, whereas certain other prohibitions would hold one liable even in that scenario.

## Analogy Test

### Consistency Question

Having introduced this *braisa*, the *gemara* must now determine whether it can be made to work consistently.

### Proposed Parallel

The *gemara* proposes that the comparative structure can be made coherent. The strictness of *Shabbos* — that two distinct *melachos* in one *he'elam* generate two *korbanot* — can be illustrated by *ktzira v'techina*, harvesting and grinding, two genuinely distinct categories. The corresponding leniency of other *mitzvos* — where sub-forms of one prohibition yield only one *korban* — could be illustrated by *avodah zarah*, as per Rabbi Ami's teaching: *ziveach v'kitar v'niseach b'he'elam echad eino chayav ela achas* — if one slaughtered, burned incense, and poured a libation in a single *he'elam*, one brings only one *korban*, since these three are all derived from *lo ta'avdeim* and arguably constitute one category. This would make the *braisa* coherent as a genuine contrast: *Shabbos* distinguishes among its categories and imposes multiple *korbanot*; *avodah zarah* (in the sub-category of *zivuach, kitur, nisuch*) does not.

### Rejection Reason

The *gemara* rejects this, however, because the second half of the *braisa* — the reverse strictness — cannot be accommodated. The *braisa* says that other *mitzvos* are liable even where *Shabbos* is exempt, specifically in the case of *shogeg belo miskavein* — unintentional action without specific intent, i.e., *mis'asek*. For *Shabbos*, *mis'asek* is *patur*; for other *mitzvos*, *chayav*. But as the *gemara* has now established, there is no genuine case of *mis'asek* for *avodah zarah* that yields liability.

### Mistaken Action

The *gemara* runs through the scenarios once more. If one bowed before what one thought was a synagogue — *harei libo lashamayim*, one's heart was directed heavenward — it is not *avodah zarah* at all. If one saw a statue and bowed: if one accepted it as a deity, one is *meizid*; if not, it is nothing. There is no middle case — no case structurally parallel to *mis'asek* on *Shabbos* — where one performs an act thinking it will not be *avodah zarah* but it turns out to be, and yet one is *chayav*.

### Avodah Gap

This is the fundamental structural gap. By *Shabbos*, *mis'asek* is possible because the act of creative work can occur independently of whether one's conscious intention was directed toward it. By *avodah zarah*, the act is constituted in part by what is in one's mind — specifically, one's orientation toward the object of worship — and so the gap that *mis'asek* exploits on *Shabbos* simply does not exist here. If the mental element is absent, the act is not *avodah zarah*; if it is present, one is fully *meizid*.

### Unresolved

The *gemara* returns, at the conclusion of the *amud*, to the options already explored: *meahava umiyirah* and *omer mutar*. For Abaye, *meahava umiyirah* could serve as the analogue — one thought it was permitted and acted on that mistaken belief; for Rava, only *omer mutar* is available. But *omer mutar* is also a case where one would be *chayav* a *korban* for *Shabbos*, so it cannot serve as the case where *avodah zarah* is *chayav* and *Shabbos* is *patur*. The *braisa* cannot be made to work by *avodah zarah*, and the *gemara* leaves the question open — the *daf* will continue from here.
