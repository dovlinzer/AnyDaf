# Bekhorot 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Money vs. Meshicha

### Non-Jew Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה ״עֲמִיתֶךָ״ בְּאַחַת, אַף גּוֹי נָמֵי בְּאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Just as** property is acquired from **your counterpart through** only **one** act of acquisition, i.e., pulling, **so too,** property is acquired from **a gentile through** only **one** act of acquisition, i.e., payment of money.

The daf opens in the middle of a discussion that began on 13b. The topic is the power of non-Jewish ownership to exempt an animal from the obligation of *bechor*. Having established this principle in the first *perek* with respect to donkeys, the Gemara now applies it to *bechor* of sheep, goats, and cattle. The prior discussion had established that a non-Jew acquires from a Jew through *kesef* — the exchange of money — though the opinion of Amemar had introduced the competing view that acquisition is effected through *meshicha*, the physical taking of possession. The practical consequences of this dispute are significant, and the Gemara now brings a challenge to resolve the question.

### Scrap Metal Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַלּוֹקֵחַ גְּרוּטָאוֹת מִן הַגּוֹיִם, וּמָצָא בָּהֶן עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — אִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא נָתַן מָעוֹת מָשַׁךְ — יַחֲזִיר, וְאִם מִשֶּׁנָּתַן מָעוֹת מָשַׁךְ — יוֹלִיךְ הֲנָאָה לְיָם הַמֶּלַח.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **one who buys broken vessels [*gerutaot*]** made of gold or silver **from the gentiles and finds among them** an object of **idol worship, if he pulled** the object of idol worship, thereby performing an act of acquisition, **before he gave** the **money** to the gentile, **he may return** the object of idol worship to the gentile. But **if he pulled** it **after he gave** the **money** to the gentile, he may not return it. Since the idol's status was not revoked, he must **take** it and cast it **into the Dead Sea.**

The Gemara raises an objection (*meitivei*) from a *mishna* in Avodah Zarah — a text the same audience will have encountered when studying that tractate. The case involves someone who purchases scrap metal from a non-Jew and discovers an idol among the refuse. The *mishna* presents two scenarios: if the buyer performed *meshicha* — drew the metal toward himself — before paying, he may return the merchandise, presumably because the transaction had not yet taken effect. If, however, he performed *meshicha* after having paid the money, he may no longer reverse the sale; since he already owns the idol and may not benefit from it, he must take its monetary value and cast it into the Dead Sea. The structural logic of the *mishna* appears to require *both* payment and *meshicha* for the transaction to take effect — a position that fits neither the "money alone" view nor the "*meshicha* alone" view the Gemara had been developing.

### Meshicha Puzzle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מָעוֹת קוֹנוֹת, מְשִׁיכָה לְמָה לִּי? הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — שֶׁקִּיבֵּל עָלָיו לָדוּן בְּדִינֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל. אִי הָכִי, מָעוֹת לְמָה לִּי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the difficulty: **And if you say** that payment of **money acquires** items from gentiles, **why do I** need **pulling** in order to effect the Jew's acquisition of the idol? The Gemara answers: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where** the gentile **accepted upon himself to** have this transaction **adjudicated by Jewish law.** The Gemara raises an objection: **If so, why do I** need the payment of **money** in order to effect the Jew's acquisition of the idol?

The Gemara presses the difficulty directly. If one says that money (*maos*) effects the transaction, why does *meshicha* matter at all? The Gemara responds: the *braita* is discussing a case where the parties stipulated (*kibel alav l'dun dinei Yisrael*) that the transaction would follow Jewish law — that is, would be governed by *meshicha*. Hence the end of the *mishna* specifies that *meshicha* was performed. But then the beginning is troublesome: if *meshicha* works, why could the buyer back out when he had already performed *meshicha* without paying? The Gemara reformulates: even the beginning involves *meshicha*, and the *mishna* is saying that even where *meshicha* was performed, if money had not yet been paid, the transaction takes effect — but the buyer may still withdraw because the entire *mishna* is structured around *meshicha* as the operative act. Yet this reading immediately raises the inverse problem: *eima hachi kashya reisha* — if *meshicha* alone is what matters in both clauses, why does the end of the *mishna* mention money at all?

### Abaye's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: רֵישָׁא מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא מִקָּח טָעוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** in explanation: The *halakha* in **the first clause** is ruled as it is **because there is a mistaken transaction,** as the Jew did not wish to purchase the idol.

Abaye resolves the contradiction as follows: in both the beginning and the end of the *mishna*, the buyer has performed *meshicha* and the transaction has taken effect. The reason the buyer in the *reisha* may nonetheless reverse the sale is that it is a case of *mekach taos* — a purchase made in error. He never anticipated finding an idol among the scrap metal, and therefore he may undo the transaction. Rava immediately challenges this: if there is a *mekach taos* in the *reisha*, surely there is an equally valid *mekach taos* in the *seifa* — in both cases the buyer was surprised by the presence of the idol. The error is identical.

### Rava's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: רֵישָׁא וְסֵיפָא מִקָּח טָעוּת, רֵישָׁא דְּלָא יָהֵיב זוּזֵי — לָא מִיחְזֵי כַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בְּיַד יִשְׂרָאֵל, סֵיפָא דְּיָהֵיב זוּזֵי — מִיחְזֵי כַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בְּיַד יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** The purchase in **the first clause and** the purchase in **the latter clause** are both **a mistaken transaction, but** in the case presented in **the first clause, where** the Jew **did not pay** him the **dinars, it does not appear as** though there is an object of **idol worship in the possession of a Jew,** and so he may return it. By contrast, in **the latter clause, where** the Jew **did pay him the dinars, it appears as** though there is an object of **idol worship in the possession of a Jew,** and therefore he must cast away the object rather than return it.

Rava therefore offers his own resolution. He accepts that both clauses involve *meshicha* as the act that effects the transaction, and he accepts that both clauses technically involve *mekach taos*. The sole operative difference between the *reisha* and the *seifa* is whether money was paid. But the reason this makes a difference is not that money effects the transaction — it does not — but rather that it creates an *appearance* problem. In the *reisha*, where the buyer has not yet paid, reversing the sale does not look suspicious: since no money changed hands, onlookers will understand that the buyer simply backed out before completing the deal, and returning the merchandise does not look like selling an idol for profit. In the *seifa*, however, where money has already been paid, the object appears to be firmly in the buyer's possession. When he returns the idol and receives his money back, it looks to observers as though he is selling the idol — which is prohibited. The critical point is that what *effects* the transaction in both clauses is *meshicha*; money serves only to determine whether returning the object creates the problematic appearance of trading in idols.

One might ask: even in the *reisha*, a bystander could observe the idol being handed back. Rava's underlying assumption is that if no payment was made, observers will understand from the context that the buyer never truly finalized the acquisition and simply withdrew upon discovering the idol. Once money has exchanged hands, however, the perception of full ownership is stronger, and any subsequent return looks like a deliberate sale. The Rabbis, as with any functioning legal system, establish general rules rather than requiring case-by-case justification from each individual. The rule is calibrated toward the scenario most likely to produce misinterpretation.

Abaye's own alternative explanation of the monetary difference is also recorded. In the *reisha*, Abaye says it is a genuine *mekach taos* because the buyer had not yet paid and therefore bore no special responsibility to investigate. In the *seifa*, it is not treated as *mekach taos* — not because the error did not occur, but because once payment has been made, before performing *meshicha* the buyer ought to have examined the goods (*ki kamashich ibaei lei leyunei v'hadar limshoch*). Having already laid out his money, a buyer has greater incentive and responsibility to inspect what he is about to take possession of. His failure to do so shifts the risk to him — the principle of *caveat emptor*.

### Tosafot & Caveat

Tosafot raises a qualification: in ordinary cases of *mekach taos*, we do not apply the rule that prior payment obligates pre-*meshicha* inspection. That is a departure from standard halacha. Tosafot explains that purchasing from a non-Jew is genuinely different here: *shani lokeach me-oved kochavim de-ibaei lei le-asukei daateh tefei shema yesh bo avodas kochavim* — when buying from a non-Jew, a buyer should have anticipated the elevated possibility that idol-related items might be present. The scenario is therefore already close to the threshold where the likelihood of encountering the problematic item would, on its own, preclude any claim of *mekach taos* altogether. The payment tips the scales: it raises the buyer's duty of care to the level where he can no longer plead ignorance. Tosafot is careful to note that this logic applies only where the frequency of the risk is already high enough to make the claim of surprise relatively weak; if the risk were routine and well-known, no *mekach taos* claim would be available regardless of payment.

### Rav Ashi View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: מִדְּרֵישָׁא מְשִׁיכָה אֵינָהּ קוֹנָה, סֵיפָא נָמֵי מְשִׁיכָה אֵינָהּ קוֹנָה, וְאַיְּידֵי דִּתְנָא רֵישָׁא ״מָשַׁךְ״, תְּנָא סֵיפָא נָמֵי ״מָשַׁךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites two more interpretations of the *baraita*. **Rav Ashi says:** The *baraita* is in fact discussing a case where the transaction was subject to gentile law, according to which only monetary payment effects acquisition, and as for the aforementioned difficulty raised to the opinion of Rabbi Oshaya from the latter clause: If so, why do I need pulling in order to effect the Jew's acquisition of the idol; pulling is not in fact required. Rather, **from** the fact that in **the first clause, pulling does not effect acquisition,** in **the latter clause too, pulling does not effect acquisition. But since the first clause taught** the word: **Pulled,** in order to teach that this method is ineffective, **the latter clause also taught** the word: **Pulled,** despite the fact that its mention is unnecessary.

Rav Ashi offers the simplest reading of the entire *braita*. He argues that *meshicha* is irrelevant throughout: *midi de-reisha meshicha eina kona seifa nami meshicha eina kona*. In both the *reisha* and the *seifa*, *meshicha* does not effect the transaction. The only operative factor is the giving of money. The *reisha* permits reversal precisely because no money was paid; the *seifa* forbids reversal because money was paid. Why, then, does the *mishna* mention *meshicha* at all in both clauses? Simply because the *mishna* began by discussing *meshicha* in the *reisha* and carried the language through to the *seifa* — *v'aidi de-tana reisha mashach, tana nami seifa mashach* — but *meshicha* is substantively irrelevant. This reading aligns directly with the dominant position argued throughout the preceding discussion: what effects a transaction between a Jew and a non-Jew is the giving of money.

### Ravina View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: מִדְּסֵיפָא מְשִׁיכָה קוֹנָה, רֵישָׁא נָמֵי מְשִׁיכָה קוֹנָה, וְרֵישָׁא הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִם לֹא נָתַן וְלֹא מָשַׁךְ — יַחְזוֹר. מַאי יַחְזוֹר? יַחְזוֹר בִּדְבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said:** The *baraita* is discussing a case where it was agreed that the transaction would be subject to Jewish law. **From** the fact that in **the latter clause, pulling effects acquisition,** in **the first clause too, pulling effects acquisition. And** as for the difficulty with regard to **the first clause,** which states that the Jew may return the item even though he already pulled it, **this** is what the *baraita* **is saying: If** the buyer **did not give** the money to the gentile **and** also **did not pull** the item, **he returns** the item to the seller. The Gemara adds: **What** does the term: **Returns,** mean in this context? It means that the Jew **returns,** i.e., reneges, **on** his **verbal** commitment.

Ravina takes the opposite position. Since in the *seifa* the outcome turns on *meshicha* — the *mishna* says that after paying and drawing the object close the buyer cannot reverse the sale — the *reisha* must be read the same way: *mi-de-seifa meshicha kana, reisha nami meshicha kana*. Under this reading, the *reisha* must be reinterpreted: the phrase *yachzir* means that the buyer may withdraw from his oral *commitment*, not from a completed transaction, since neither *meshicha* nor money had yet occurred. This reading introduces the concept of *mechusrei amana*, discussed next.

### Jew-NonJew Trust

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָסָבַר: דְּבָרִים יֵשׁ בָּהֶם מִשּׁוּם מְחוּסְּרֵי אֲמָנָה, וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי — יִשְׂרָאֵל מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, דְּקָיְימִי בְּדִבּוּרַיְיהוּ, אֲבָל יִשְׂרָאֵל מִגּוֹיִם, דְּאִינְהוּ לָא קָיְימִי בְּדִבּוּרַיְיהוּ — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: It was necessary for the *tanna* to teach this explicitly, as **he holds:** Reneging on **a verbal** commitment that was unaccompanied by an act of acquisition **constitutes an act of bad faith** between two parties. **And** he holds that **this statement** applies only to a case where **a Jew** purchases an item **from** another **Jew, as they** are assumed to **uphold their verbal** commitments. **But** in a case of **a Jew** who purchases an item **from gentiles, as they** are **not** assumed **to uphold their verbal** commitments, reneging on one's word does **not** constitute an act of bad faith between the parties, since neither party relies upon the commitment of the other in the first place.

The concept of *mechusrei amana* — breach of faith — holds that even though an oral undertaking to buy or sell has no binding legal force, withdrawing from such a commitment is nonetheless considered a form of faithlessness. Words of commitment carry moral weight; reneging on them, even when legally permissible, is regarded as a failing. The Gemara adds a notable qualification: *v'hani milei Yisrael mi-Yisrael d'kaimi b'diburaihu* — this concern for keeping one's word applies between Jew and Jew, where both parties are understood to stand behind their verbal commitments. Between a Jew and a non-Jew, however, the Gemara expresses a different view: *aval Yisrael mi-akum d'inu lo kaimi b'diburaihu* — the perception, at least as articulated in this Talmudic context, is that the standard of mutual verbal reliability does not hold in the same way, and therefore one is not equally bound by an oral commitment in a Jew–non-Jew transaction. This statement reflects the historical circumstances of the rabbinic period and the fraught relationship between the Jewish community and the surrounding non-Jewish authorities during that era; it should not be read as a timeless normative claim about interpersonal ethics.

### Halachic Summary

The dominant thrust of the entire discussion is clear: what effects a transaction between a Jew and a non-Jew is the giving of *kesef*, money — not *meshicha*. The Amora who argued for *meshicha* represents a dissenting view, and even within the *braita* that mentioned both *meshicha* and money, the various *amoraim* who sought to uphold the *meshicha* position did so by positing a special stipulation (*kibel alav l'dun b'dinei Yisrael*), not as the default rule. Rashi rules in accordance with the dominant *sugya*: the transaction is effected by money. The majority of *Rishonim*, however, reason differently. Since the general rule for Jews is that *kesef* acquires only by Torah law, while the Rabbis substituted *meshicha* in its place, the reverse principle applies for non-Jews: what the Rabbis substituted for Jews (*meshicha*) does not apply, and therefore *kesef* — the Torah-level mode — functions. Tosafot and most *Rishonim* therefore rule that *meshicha* is what effects the transaction with a non-Jew, contrary to the apparently dominant position of our *sugya*.

The practical consequence is enormous. The *halacha* governing the sale of *chametz* before Pesach, the transfer of land in *Eretz Yisrael* for *Shmita* purposes, and the transfer of a *bechor* to a non-Jew to avoid the obligation — all depend on what mode of transaction is operative. Since the *Rishonim* could not reach full consensus, the accepted practice is to perform both: to have the non-Jew give money *and* take physical possession. An additional mechanism widely employed is the rental of the land or premises on which the *chametz* or animal stands. Since all authorities agree that a non-Jew can acquire real property through money, and since an object resting on one's land is considered to be in one's possession, the combination of a monetary land-rental with constructive possession through location satisfies all opinions. Whether *chalipin* — a symbolic barter transaction using a handkerchief — also works is a further question debated among the authorities. This entire literature flows directly from the unresolved tension in our *sugya*.

## Blemish Before Sanctity

### Mishna Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַקֳּדָשִׁים שֶׁקָּדַם מוּם קָבוּעַ לְהֶקְדֵּשָׁן וְנִפְדּוּ — חַיָּיבִין בַּבְּכוֹרָה וּבַמַּתָּנוֹת, וְיוֹצְאִין לְחוּלִּין לִיגָּזֵז וְלֵיעָבֵד, וְלָדָן וַחֲלָבָן מוּתָּר לְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **All sacrificial** animals in **which a permanent blemish preceded their consecration** do not assume inherent sanctity and only their value is consecrated, **and** once **they were redeemed,** they **are obligated in** the mitzva of **a firstborn,** i.e., their offspring are subject to being counted a firstborn, **and in the** priestly **gifts** of the foreleg, the jaw, and the maw, **and they can emerge** from their sacred status and assume complete **non-sacred** status in order **to be shorn and to be utilized for labor.** And **their offspring and their milk are permitted after their redemption.**

At the top of 14a, the Gemara opens a new *mishna* — one that appears in *Chullin* as well, though without full analysis there, and which will receive extended treatment in *Temurah*. The *mishna* presents two contrasting cases involving sanctified animals and the timing of their blemishes.

### Blemish First

The first case: *kol hakodashim shekadmu mum kavua l'hekdeshan* — any sanctified animal that had a permanent, non-transitory blemish before it was sanctified. Suppose one takes a sheep with a broken leg — a fixed blemish — and declares it an *olah*. Since the animal was blemished at the moment of sanctification, it was never capable of attaining the sanctity appropriate to the altar. The attempt to confer *kedushat mizbeach* — altar sanctity — therefore produced only *kedushat damim*, value sanctity: the animal must be sold and the proceeds used to purchase a *korban*. This is categorically different from full *kedushat korban*, and it is also distinct from simple *kedushat bedek habayit* — sanctity for the temple treasury — since even here the proceeds are directed toward the altar rather than mere maintenance.

### No Korban Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשּׁוֹחֲטָן בַּחוּץ פָּטוּר, וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה, וְאִם מֵתוּ — יִפָּדוּ, חוּץ מִן הַבְּכוֹר וּמִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **And one who slaughters them outside** the Temple courtyard is **exempt** from *karet*, **and** those animals **do not render** an animal that was **a substitute** for them consecrated. **And if** these animals **died** before they were redeemed, **they may be redeemed** and fed to dogs, and they do not require burial, **except for the firstborn and the** animal **tithe.** With regard to these two types of offerings, even if they were blemished before they became consecrated they assume inherent sanctity, like other offerings that were consecrated and subsequently became blemished.

Because the animal never attained genuine *kedushat korban*, the *mishna* rules that after it is redeemed, its offspring are subject to the obligation of *bechor*, and when it is slaughtered, one must give the *matanot* — *zero'a, lechayayim v'keiva* — to the *kohen*. These obligations are waived only when an animal carries residual *kedusha*; this animal, having never possessed *kedushat korban*, retains no such residual status once redeemed. Furthermore, slaughtering it outside the *Beit HaMikdash* does not incur liability for the prohibition of *shechutei chutz*, since that prohibition applies only to animals with genuine *kedushat korban*.

### Post-Redemption Use

The animal also cannot be shorn or put to work even after redemption. The Torah's phrase *tizbach v'lo giza, v'achalta v'lo avoda* — you may slaughter it but not shear it, eat from it but not work it — is understood by the Sages to apply to *psulei mukdashin*, animals once destined for the altar that were redeemed on account of a blemish. Since this animal started as a proper *korban*, these restrictions continue even after redemption. Similarly, the offspring and the milk of such an animal remain forbidden even after redemption.

Once redeemed, the animal exits fully to the status of *chullin*: it may be shorn and worked — *ligazez v'la'avod* — without restriction. This stands in deliberate contrast to an animal that was sanctified while unblemished and subsequently became blemished: such an animal, even after redemption, retains residual *kedusha* and cannot be shorn or put to work. Here, by contrast, the sanctity is wholly extinguished upon redemption. The offspring and milk are likewise permitted — *ladan v'chalavan mutar* — after redemption. If the animal dies before redemption, it may still be redeemed, since the two grounds that ordinarily bar redemption of a deceased *korban* — the requirement to stand the animal before the priest (*he'emid oso hakohen*), and the rule that one may not redeem *kodashim* merely to feed the dogs — do not apply to an animal that never had *kedushat korban*.

Additionally, attempting to perform *temurah* — transferring the *kedusha* to another animal — produces no effect, since *temurah* is operative only for genuine *korbanos*.

### Bechor/Maaser

The *mishna* notes two exceptions to this first case: *bechor* and *maaser behema*. For both of these, even if the blemish preceded the *kedusha*, the animal still acquires a sanctity equivalent to *kedushat korban*. The reason is that in both cases the *kedusha* arises not from a human declaration but from a physical reality — birth and counting, respectively. A firstborn animal acquires its sanctity by virtue of being born; the tenth animal counted in the annual tally acquires its sanctity by virtue of being the tenth. In neither case does the timing of the blemish relative to the moment of sanctification matter, because the sanctity is imposed automatically by the Torah regardless of condition. The Torah states explicitly that the tenth animal is holy whether it is good or bad — that is, whether or not it is blemished. Similarly, a blemished *bechor* is not redeemed in the normal way; rather, the *kohen* slaughters and eats it outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, and it retains a quasi-*korban* sanctity throughout.

### Sanctity First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל שֶׁקָּדַם הֶקְדֵּשָׁן אֶת מוּמָן, אוֹ מוּם עוֹבֵר לְהֶקְדֵּשָׁן, וּלְאַחַר מִכָּאן נוֹלַד לָהֶן מוּם קָבוּעַ לְהֶקְדֵּשָׁן וְנִפְדּוּ — פְּטוּרִין מִן הַבְּכוֹרָה וּמִן הַמַּתָּנוֹת, וְאֵינָן יוֹצְאִין לְחוּלִּין לִיגָּזֵז וְלֵיעָבֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **And all** sacrificial animals **whose consecration preceded their blemish, or** who had **a temporary blemish** prior **to their consecration and afterward developed a permanent blemish and they were redeemed, they are exempt from,** i.e., their offspring are not counted, **a firstborn, and from the gifts** of the foreleg, the jaw, and the maw, **and they do not** completely **emerge** from their sacred status and assume **non-sacred** status in order **to be shorn and to be utilized for labor.**

The second case: *v'chol shekodam hekdeshan mumam* — animals that were sanctified before they acquired their permanent blemish. Even if the animal had a transitory blemish at the time of sanctification, that is irrelevant; what matters is that a permanent blemish arose only after it had already been sanctified in an unblemished state. In such a case, the original sanctity was full *kedushat korban*. That *kedusha* does not dissipate when the blemish appears; it persists, and even after the animal is redeemed, a residual sanctity remains.

### Residual Status

Because of this residual *kedusha*, the *mishna* rules that even after such an animal has been redeemed, its offspring are exempt from *bechor* and one need not give *matanot* to the *kohen* when it is slaughtered. The residual *kedusha* — though it no longer renders the animal fit for the altar — is sufficient to suspend these obligations.

### Post-Redemption Limits

The animal also cannot be shorn or put to work even after redemption. The Torah's phrase *tizbach v'lo giza, v'achalta v'lo avoda* — you may slaughter it but not shear it, eat from it but not work it — is understood by the Sages to apply to *psulei mukdashin*, animals once destined for the altar that were redeemed on account of a blemish. Since this animal started as a proper *korban*, these restrictions continue even after redemption. Similarly, the offspring and the milk of such an animal remain forbidden even after redemption.

### Shechita & Temurah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָדָן וַחֲלָבָן אָסוּר לְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָן, וְהַשּׁוֹחֲטָן בַּחוּץ חַיָּיב, וְעוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה, וְאִם מֵתוּ — יִקָּבֵרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** And **their offspring,** which were conceived prior to redemption, **and their milk, are prohibited after their redemption. And one who slaughters them outside** the Temple courtyard is **liable** to receive *karet*, **and** those animals **render** an animal that was **a substitute** for them consecrated. **And if** these animals **died** before they were redeemed, they may not be redeemed and fed to dogs; rather, **they must be buried.**

Because these animals retain *kedushat korban*, two further consequences follow. First, if such an animal is slaughtered outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, one is liable for the prohibition of *shechutei chutz* — but only in the qualified case where the blemish is subtle enough that had the animal been brought to the altar, it would have been permitted to remain there *bedi'eved*, such as a cataract. Second, *temurah* is operative: if one attempts to exchange such an animal with an unblemished one, both the original and the substitute acquire *kedusha*.

### Redemption Test

The Gemara opens its analysis with a straightforward observation from the language of the first case of the *mishna*.

## Damim Sanctity

### Sanctity Exempts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּנִפְדּוּ, הָא לֹא נִפְדּוּ — פְּטוּרִין מִן הַבְּכוֹרָה וּמִן הַמַּתָּנוֹת. קָסָבַר: קְדוּשַּׁת דָּמִים מְדַחָה מִן הַבְּכוֹרָה וּמִן הַמַּתָּנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that all animals that were blemished prior to their consecration are subject to their offspring being counted a firstborn and to the obligation of the gifts once they have been redeemed. The Gemara infers: **The reason** that they are subject to the obligations of a firstborn and the gifts is **that they were redeemed. But if they were not redeemed, they are exempt from** their offspring being counted **a firstborn and from the** obligation of the priestly **gifts.** The Gemara comments: This indicates that the *tanna* of the mishna **holds: Sanctity** that inheres in an animal's **value defers,** i.e., renders it exempt, **from** the mitzva **of the firstborn and from the gifts.**

The Gemara notes that the *mishna* specifies: *ta'ima d'nifdu* — the reason the animal is obligated in *bechor* and *matanot* is that it has been redeemed. The implication is that had it not yet been redeemed, it would still be exempt from *bechor* even though its sanctity was never *kedushat korban*. From this the Gemara infers: *kasvar kedushas damim madcha min habechora u'min hamatanos* — even a mere sanctity of value (*kedushas damim*), not altar sanctity, is sufficient to exempt an animal from the obligation of *bechor* and from the obligation of priestly gifts, as long as that sanctity remains in place.

This is a significant and precise point. The exemption from *bechor* is not contingent on an animal having full *kedushat korban*; it flows from the presence of *any* form of *kedusha*. So long as the animal is *kadosh* — even if only with *kedushas damim* — it is exempt.

### Donkey Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיוֹצְאִין לְחוּלִּין וְכוּ׳. טַעְמָא דְּנִפְדּוּ, הָא לֹא נִפְדּוּ — אֲסִירִי בְּגִיזָּה וַעֲבוֹדָה. מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, דְּאָמַר: קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת אֲסוּרִין בְּגִיזָּה וַעֲבוֹדָה?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna further teaches: **And they can emerge** from their sacred status and assume complete **non-sacred** status in order to be shorn and to be utilized for labor. The Gemara infers: **The reason** they become non-sacred is **that they were redeemed. But if they were not redeemed,** they remain sacred and **are prohibited with regard to shearing and labor.** The Gemara notes: **This supports** the opinion **of Rabbi Eliezer, who says:** Animals **consecrated for the Temple maintenance are prohibited with regard to shearing and labor.**

This principle is illustrated with a case of an animal that could never enter the *korban* world at all. If one sanctifies a donkey for the temple treasury — *hekdesh*, but purely as a monetary contribution, not as a *korban* — and that donkey subsequently gives birth, the newborn animal is exempt from the *mitzvah* of *peter chamor*. Although the donkey's sanctity is purely *kedushat bedek habayit* and has nothing to do with the altar, the mere presence of *kedusha* is sufficient to prevent the offspring from being subject to the obligation of *peter chamor*. This reinforces the broader principle: while the animal carries any form of *kedusha*, the exemption holds.

### Shearing Prohibited

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: לָא, קְדוּשַּׁת דָּמִים לַמִּזְבֵּחַ הוּא דְּמִיחַלְּפָא בִּקְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף לַמִּזְבֵּחַ, גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן, אֲבָל קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: The Sages **said: No;** the mishna does not necessarily support the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer. It is possible that **it is** only with regard to an animal that has **sanctity** that inheres in its **value** and that was consecrated **for the altar, which** can be mistakenly **exchanged** with an animal that possesses **inherent sanctity** and was likewise consecrated **for the altar,** that **the Sages decreed** that it is prohibited to shear it and perform labor **with it,** due to a concern that one might confuse it with an animal that possesses inherent sanctity and ultimately violate a Torah prohibition. **But** with regard to animals **consecrated for the Temple maintenance, no** such decree was issued, as this concern does not apply.

The Gemara then turns to another implication of the *mishna*: *ha lo nifdu assur b'giza v'avoda* — before redemption, even this animal that started with a blemish and never possessed *kedushat korban* may not be shorn or worked. This is puzzling. The prohibition on shearing and working applies to *korbanos*; it is derived from the verse *tizbach v'lo giza, v'achalta v'lo avoda*. But this animal is not a *korban*. Even the temple treasury could theoretically shear it and sell the wool for profit — that would seem to be entirely within the spirit of the animal's consecration for monetary purposes. Why, then, is shearing forbidden?

The Gemara notes that this ruling supports the position of Rabbi Elazar, who holds that *kodshei bedek habayit assur b'giza v'avoda* — even animals sanctified purely for the treasury may not be shorn or worked. The reason would presumably be an appearance-based prohibition: it looks too similar to what one does with a genuine *korban*, and permitting it would create confusion.

### Two Sanctity Types

However, the Gemara qualifies this immediately: the case of our *mishna* does not constitute strong support for Rabbi Elazar's position. Our case is fundamentally different from a straightforward *bedek habayit* animal, because here the owner attempted to sanctify the animal *for the altar* — he tried to declare it an *olah* or some other *korban*. What he achieved was a *kedushas damim l'mizbeach* — a value-sanctity directed toward eventual procurement of a *korban* — rather than a simple treasury sanctity. This animal is therefore much more likely to be confused with an actual *korban*, and the rabbinic prohibition on shearing and working it before redemption is readily understandable even without Rabbi Elazar's broader principle. A straightforward *bedek habayit* dedication — where the owner's intention was always purely monetary — might not generate the same degree of confusion, and therefore may fall outside the prohibition. The *mishna* thus does not prove Rabbi Elazar's view with respect to *kodshei bedek habayit* proper.

## Offspring & Milk

### Simple Case

The *mishna* also states that after redemption the offspring and milk of the first case — the blemished animal that was sanctified — are permitted. Before redemption, they are part of the *hekdesh* estate, since offspring and milk of a consecrated animal take on that animal's sanctity and belong to the temple.

### Pregnancy Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָדָן וַחֲלָבָן מוּתָּר כּוּ׳. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּאִיעַבַּר וְאִתְיְלִיד לְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָן — פְּשִׁיטָא, חוּלִּין נִינְהוּ! אֶלָּא דְּאִיעַבַּר לִפְנֵי פִּדְיוֹנָן, וְאִתְיְלִיד לְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **their offspring and their milk are permitted** after their redemption. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of the case of the mishna, in which the offspring of blemished redeemed animals are permitted? **If we say** that the mishna is referring to a case **where** the animals **became pregnant and gave birth after their redemption,** then it is **obvious** that the offspring are permitted, as **they are non-sacred** animals from the moment of conception. **Rather,** it must be referring to a case **where they became pregnant before their redemption, and gave birth after their redemption,** and the mishna is teaching that the offspring are permitted despite the fact they were conceived while their mothers were consecrated.

The Gemara asks: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? If the animal became pregnant and gave birth *after* redemption, the permissibility of its offspring and milk is self-evident — the *mishna* has already said it becomes *chullin* entirely upon redemption. The Gemara therefore specifies that the *mishna* is discussing the case where the animal became pregnant *before* redemption and gave birth *after* redemption. That is the meaningful case: the fetus was gestating while the mother was still *kadosh*, and one might have thought the offspring retains some residual *kedusha*. The *mishna* teaches that once the mother is fully redeemed — her *kedusha* extinguished entirely — the offspring born thereafter is also fully *chullin*, because the act of redemption removed the *kedusha* from mother and fetus alike.

### Redeemable?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לִפְנֵי פִּדְיוֹנָן — אֲסִירִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers from this ruling: **But** if the animals gave birth **before their redemption,** the offspring **are prohibited** until they are redeemed.

This raises a further question. Before redemption, the offspring of such an animal also carry a *kedusha* — but what kind, and under what rules? Since the mother's *kedusha* was never *kedushat korban* and could never have led to this animal standing on the altar, the offspring likewise cannot be brought as a *korban*. Can such offspring — which are themselves unblemished — be redeemed without requiring a blemish? Under ordinary rules, a *korban* cannot be redeemed unless it is blemished. But these offspring are in a peculiar intermediate position: they have *kedusha*, but it is a *kedusha* that was always disqualified from the altar. Is the requirement for a blemish before redemption applicable to them?

### Source Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִפְדִּין תְּמִימִים אוֹ אֵין נִפְדִּין תְּמִימִין? תָּא שְׁמַע: הַמַּתְפִּיס בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין קְבוּעִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ, וְיָלְדוּ — יִמָּכְרוּ, וְאֵין צְרִיכִין מוּם, שֶׁאֵין קְדוּשָּׁה חָלָה עֲלֵיהֶן, שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא טָפֵל חָמוּר מִן הָעִיקָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The *halakha* is that the offspring of an animal that possesses inherent sanctity is born sanctified, and therefore it cannot be redeemed until it develops a blemish. The Gemara asks: With regard to the offspring of an animal that possesses sanctity that inheres in its value, **are they redeemed unblemished, or are they not redeemed unblemished?** In other words, can they be redeemed immediately, or must one wait until they develop a blemish? The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: With regard to **one who dedicates permanently blemished** animals **for the altar, and** those animals **gave birth,** their offspring **may be sold** in order to be redeemed **and they do not require a blemish** to render their sale permitted. The reason is **that they are not imbued with sanctity,** and **that the subsidiary** sanctity of the offspring should **not be** more **stringent than the primary** sanctity of the mother.

An explicit teaching resolves the question: *hamakdish ba'alei mumim kev'u'im legabei mizbe'ach v'yaldu — yimachru v'ein tzerichim mum* — one who sanctifies animals with permanent blemishes for the altar and they give birth, those offspring may be sold without requiring a blemish. *She'ein kedusha chalah aleihem* — the altar sanctity does not attach to the offspring, since it never attached to the mother.

### Tafel Chamur

The principle underlying this ruling is stated explicitly: *v'shelo yehei tafel chamur min ha'ikar* — the derivative should not be more stringent than the source. Since the mother herself does not bear *kedushat mizbeach*, her offspring likewise do not, and imposing a blemish requirement on the offspring that does not apply to the mother would make the derivative stricter than the foundation — an inversion the *halacha* does not countenance.

## Sanctity Upgrade

### Upgrade Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא, שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא טָפֵל חָמוּר מִן הָעִיקָּר, אֲבָל הִקְדִּישׁ זָכָר לְדָמָיו — קָדוֹשׁ קְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף. מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרָבָא, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: הִקְדִּישׁ זָכָר לְדָמָיו — קָדוֹשׁ קְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **The reason** that the *baraita* rules that the sanctity for the altar does not take effect is **that the subsidiary** sanctity should **not be** more **stringent than the primary** sanctity, i.e., the only reason it is not sanctified for the altar is because its sanctity is derived from its mother, which cannot be sanctified for the altar as it is blemished. **But** if **one consecrated** an unblemished **male** animal **for its value,** where there is no secondary consecration, **it is sanctified with inherent sanctity.** The Gemara adds that **this supports** the opinion **of Rava, as Rava says:** If **one consecrated a male** animal **for its value, it is sanctified with inherent sanctity.**

Having established that offspring of an animal with *kedushas damim l'mizbeach* can be redeemed without a blemish because the mother's *kedusha* limits theirs, the Gemara draws a contrast. What about the reverse situation? Suppose one takes a perfect, unblemished male animal — one that is fully eligible to be brought as an *olah* — and explicitly declares it sanctified only for its *monetary value* (*ledamav*), intending it to be sold with the proceeds directed to the temple treasury rather than brought directly as a *korban*. Does reality override intention? Since the physical animal is perfectly eligible to stand on the altar, does the verbal sanctification for treasury purposes automatically upgrade to full *kedushat haguf* — intrinsic altar sanctity?

### Rava's Rule

The Gemara concludes that this supports the position of Rava: *hikdish zachar ledamim kadosh kedushat haguf* — if one sanctifies a perfect male animal for its value, it nonetheless acquires intrinsic *kedusha* appropriate to a *korban*. The reasoning emerges from the contrast with the offspring case. When an unblemished offspring is born to a mother with limited *kedusha*, the limitation comes from the mother: her *kedusha* is confined, and the child's is equally confined. But when no mother is involved — when one simply performs a verbal sanctification — there is no external limiting factor. The animal is eligible for the altar; nothing prevents the full upgrade; and therefore the *halacha* shortcircuits the indirect route and recognizes the animal as a *korban* directly.

### Rashi Limit

Rashi qualifies the scope of this rule. The principle applies specifically to the middle category — *kedushas damim l'mizbeach* — where the *kedusha*, though expressed as a monetary sanctification, is inherently oriented toward the eventual procurement of a *korban*. In that case, if one says "I am sanctifying this animal to sell it and use the proceeds for a *korban*," the logic of upgrading is sound: the animal itself *is* the *korban*, so why take the indirect route? But if one intended straightforward *bedek habayit* sanctity — purely for temple maintenance with no connection to the altar — the same automatic upgrade would not apply. Rashi's reading confines Rava's principle to cases where the monetary sanctification is inherently directed toward a *korban* as its ultimate purpose.

## Blemish Korban

### Exempt Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשּׁוֹחֲטָן בַּחוּץ פָּטוּר. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מַתְנֵי ״חַיָּיב״, וּמוֹקֵי לַהּ בְּבָמַת יָחִיד.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches with regard to animals whose blemish preceded their consecration: **And one who slaughters them outside** the Temple courtyard is **exempt.** The Gemara notes that **Rabbi Elazar teaches** the mishna as follows: And one who slaughters them outside the Temple courtyard is **liable, and he interprets** the mishna as **referring to** a case of a private **altar of an individual.** In other words, the mishna is not teaching that one is liable to receive *karet* for slaughtering an animal outside the Temple courtyard, but that he is liable to receive lashes for sacrificing a blemished animal upon a private altar.

The discussion now turns to a broader series of halachic derivations. The general rule — as reflected in the first case of the *mishna* — is that if an animal has *kedushas damim* rather than *kedushat korban*, slaughtering it outside the *Beit HaMikdash* does not incur the liability of *shechutei chutz*. This is the baseline from which the Gemara proceeds.

### R. Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִנַּיִן לַזּוֹבֵחַ בְּהֵמָה בַּעֲלַת מוּם בְּבָמַת יָחִיד בִּשְׁעַת הֶיתֵּר הַבָּמוֹת, שֶׁהוּא בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא תִזְבַּח לַה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ שׁוֹר וָשֶׂה״, אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְבָמָה גְּדוֹלָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״עַוֶּרֶת אוֹ שָׁבוּר וְגוֹ׳״, תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לְבָמַת יָחִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Elazar says: From where** is it derived **that one who slaughters a blemished animal upon** a private **altar of an individual at a time when** private **altars are permitted is in** violation of **a prohibition?** It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated: "You shall not sacrifice to the Lord an ox, or a sheep,** in which is a blemish" (Deuteronomy 17:1). **If** this verse **is not** referring **to** the **matter of a great** public **altar,** since that *halakha* is derived from a different verse, **as it is written: "Blind or broken**…you shall not sacrifice these unto the Lord" (Leviticus 22:22), **apply** the verse in Deuteronomy to the **matter of** a private **altar of an individual.**

Rabbi Eliezer dissents, invoking a private *bamah*. He argues that the verse in Devarim — *lo tizbach l'Hashem Elokecha shor va'seh... kol davar ra* — "do not slaughter to God an ox or a sheep with any blemish" — addresses a case that goes beyond the central sanctuary. The verse in Vayikra already enumerates all the blemishes that disqualify an animal from the *Beit HaMikdash*; the repetition in Devarim must therefore be teaching something additional. Rabbi Eliezer reads it as applying to a *bamat yachid* — a private altar operative during the period when such altars were permitted. Even on a private altar, bringing a blemished animal constitutes a transgression of a negative commandment. The majority of the *mishna*, reading the case as exempt, do not agree with Rabbi Eliezer's structural use of the verse — the *mishna* applies it to a period with a functioning *Beit HaMikdash* and the *mishna*'s own ruling is *patur*.

### Hermeneutics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא: אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְקָדָשִׁים — תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לִבְכוֹר, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וְקָדוֹשׁ כְּשֶׁהוּא בַּעַל מוּם — לִיקְרַב נָמֵי כְּשֶׁהוּא בַּעַל מוּם, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: But one can **say** with regard to the verse in Deuteronomy: **If** this verse **is not** referring **to** the **matter of** regular **sacrificial** animals, which is derived from the verse in Leviticus, **apply it** to the **matter of a firstborn,** as it might **enter your mind to say: Since** a firstborn animal **is sanctified** even **when it is** a permanently **blemished** animal, **let it also be sacrificed** upon the altar **when it is a blemished animal.** The verse in Deuteronomy would therefore **teach us that** this is **not** the *halakha*, i.e., a blemished firstborn animal may not be sacrificed upon the altar. What, then, is the reason that the *halakha* of a private altar of an individual, rather than the *halakha* of firstborn animals, is derived from the verse in Deuteronomy?

The Gemara then conducts a careful hermeneutical exercise. If the extra verse in Devarim is not needed for the categories Rabbi Eliezer assigns it to, there must be a reason for its inclusion. The Gemara works through each category of animal that possesses *kedushat korban* independent of its blemish, asking in each case whether one might have thought the animal could be offered despite its blemish — and showing that each such case is excluded through its own verse or *gezera shava*.

For *bechor*: although a *bechor* acquires its sanctity regardless of whether the blemish preceded or followed birth, the Torah states explicitly — *piseiach o iver lo tizbachenu* — that a lame or blind *bechor* may not be offered. There is thus no need for an extra verse to establish that a blemished *bechor* cannot be brought. Similarly for *maaser behema*: the Gemara proposes that the extra verse might be needed to teach that a blemished *maaser* animal cannot be offered, since the Torah says the tenth is *kadosh* regardless of whether it is *tov* or *ra* — good or bad, that is, unblemished or blemished. But the Gemara deflects: *maaser* is linked to *bechor* through a *gezera shava* — both employ the word *haavara* ("passing under the rod" or "passing to") — and from the explicit rule about *bechor*, one derives the same for *maaser*. No additional verse is required.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: בְּכוֹר בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ ״פִּסֵּחַ אוֹ עִוֵּר לֹא תִזְבָּחֶנּוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said** in explanation: Concerning **a firstborn,** the *halakha* is **written explicitly with regard to it:** "Every firstborn…if there be any blemish therein, **lameness, or blindness…you shall not sacrifice it"** (Deuteronomy 15:19–21). Consequently, the verse in Deuteronomy, chapter 17, cannot be referring to the *halakha* that a firstborn animal may not be sacrificed upon the altar, as that is already taught in chapter 15.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְקָדָשִׁים — תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לְמַעֲשֵׂר, דְּסָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וְקָדוֹשׁ בְּמוּמוֹ, דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא יְבַקֵּר בֵּין טוֹב לָרַע״, נִקְרַב נָמֵי בְּמוּמֵיהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: **But** still, one can **say: If** the verse in chapter 17 **is not** referring **to** the **matter of sacrificial** animals, which is derived from the verse in Leviticus, **apply it to** the **matter of** the animal **tithe, as** it might **enter your mind to say: Since** a tithed animal is **sacred** even **with its blemish, as it is written: "He shall not inquire whether it be good or bad"** (Leviticus 27:33), **let it also be sacrificed with its blemish.** The verse in Deuteronomy (17:1) would therefore **teach us that** this is **not** the *halakha*, i.e., a blemished tithed animal may not be sacrificed upon the altar.

The same logic applies to *temurah*: the Torah states that even if the substituted animal is *ra* — blemished — it still acquires *kedusha* (*v'lo yachlifenu v'lo yamir oto tov b'ra*). One might have thought that since the blemish does not prevent *kedusha* from attaching to the exchanged animal, the animal could also be brought with its blemish. But the Torah says *v'haya hu u'tmurato yihye kodesh* — the *temurah* has the same status as the original: just as the original *korban* cannot be brought with a blemish, neither can the *temurah*. The extra verse is therefore unnecessary for *temurah* as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: מַעֲשֵׂר נָמֵי גָּמַר ״הַעֲבָרָה״ ״הַעֲבָרָה״ מִבְּכוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said** in response: With regard to the animal **tithe as well,** it is unnecessary for the verse to teach this *halakha*, as it is **derived from** a verbal analogy between the word: **Passing,** mentioned with regard to tithed animals (see Leviticus 27:32), and **passing** mentioned with regard to **the firstborn** (see Exodus 13:12). Just as a blemished firstborn animal is not sacrificed upon the altar, so too, a blemished tithed animal is not sacrificed upon the altar.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא: תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לִתְמוּרַת קָדָשִׁים, דְּסָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּקְדוֹשָׁה כְּשֶׁהִיא בַּעֲלַת מוּם, דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא יַחֲלִיפֶנּוּ וְלֹא יָמִיר אוֹתוֹ וְגוֹ׳״, קָרֵיבָה נָמֵי כְּשֶׁהִיא בַּעֲלַת מוּם, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara persists: But **say** that one should **apply** Deuteronomy 17:1 **to** the **matter of** animals **substituted for sacrificial** animals, **as** it might **enter your mind to say: Since** a substitute becomes **sacred** even **when it is blemished, as it is written: "He shall not exchange it, nor substitute it,** a good for a bad, or a bad for a good; and if he shall at all substitute animal for animal, then both it and that for which it is substituted shall be holy" (Leviticus 27:10), **it may likewise be sacrificed when it is blemished.** The verse in Deuteronomy would therefore **teach us that** this is **not** the *halakha*, as a blemished substitute cannot be sacrificed upon the altar.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהָיָה הוּא וּתְמוּרָתוֹ״, מַקִּישׁ תְּמוּרָתוֹ לוֹ — מָה הוּא בַּעַל מוּם לֹא, אַף תְּמוּרָתוֹ בַּעַל מוּם לֹא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The *halakha* with regard to a substitute is already established, as **the verse states: "It and that for which it is substituted** shall be holy." The verse **compares a substitute to** the consecrated animal for which it was substituted: **Just as** the consecrated animal is **not** sacrificed if it is **blemished, so too, its substitute** is **not** sacrificed if it is **blemished.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא: אֵימָא, תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לְוַלְדוֹת קָדָשִׁים, דְּסָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּקְדוֹשִׁין כְּשֶׁהֵן בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין אַגַּב אִמָּן, כְּשֶׁהֵן בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין נָמֵי קָרְבִי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents a final objection: **Rabbi Zeira objects to** the claim that Deuteronomy 17:1 should be understood as referring to a private altar of an individual. **Say** instead that one should **apply it to** the **matter of the offspring of sacrificial** animals, **as** it could **enter your mind to say: Since they are sacred** even **when they are blemished, on account of their mother, they may also be sacrificed when they are blemished.** Deuteronomy 17:1 would therefore **teach us** that this is **not** the *halakha*.

Rav Zevid raises the case of *vladot kodashim* — offspring born to a *korban* animal that are themselves blemished. Since the mother's *kedushat korban* passes to the offspring even when the offspring is born blemished — the *kedusha* attaches *agav iman*, by virtue of the mother — one might have thought that such offspring could be brought despite their blemish. The Gemara responds with a *baraita* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *rak kodshecha asher yihyu lecha u'nedarecha* — "only your sacrifices and your vows" — is read to include *temurot* and *vladot*, placing them on equal footing with a regular vowed *korban*. Just as a vowed *korban* with a blemish cannot be brought, neither can a *temura* or a *vlad* that has a blemish.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: כְּבָר פַּסְקַהּ תַּנָּא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״רַק קָדָשֶׁיךָ אֲשֶׁר יִהְיוּ לְךָ וּנְדָרֶיךָ״ — ״רַק קָדָשֶׁיךָ״ אֵלּוּ הַתְּמוּרוֹת, ״אֲשֶׁר יִהְיוּ לְךָ״ אֵלּוּ הַוְּולָדוֹת, ״וּנְדָרֶיךָ״ — הִקִּישָׁן הַכָּתוּב לְנֶדֶר, מָה נֶדֶר בַּעַל מוּם לֹא, אַף הָנֵי נָמֵי בַּעַל מוּם לֹא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says** in response: **The *tanna* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael has already ruled** that the blemished offspring of sacrificial animals cannot be sacrificed upon the altar, from a different verse. **As the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught:** The verse states: **"Only your sacred items which you have, and your vows,** you shall take, and go to the place which the Lord shall choose" (Deuteronomy 12:26). **"Only your sacred items"; these are the substitutes. "Which you have"; these are the offspring.** When it states: **"And your vows,"** the verse thereby **juxtaposes** animal substitutes and offspring **with** an animal consecrated by **a vow,** teaching that **just as** an animal consecrated by **a vow** is **not** sacrificed if it is **blemished, so too, these are also not** sacrificed if they are **blemished.** Accordingly, Deuteronomy 17:1 must be understood as referring to a private altar of an individual, as maintained by Rabbi Elazar.

### Unified Principle

Having worked through all these cases, the Gemara arrives at a unified principle: every animal that possesses *kedushat korban* — regardless of whether that *kedusha* attached in spite of a pre-existing blemish — is nonetheless entirely barred from being brought on the altar while blemished. The *kedusha* and the disqualification coexist: the animal is *kadosh* even with its blemish, but that *kedusha* cannot be realized through sacrifice until and unless the blemish is removed. This demonstration has the further consequence of freeing the extra verse in Devarim from having to teach any of these cases, leaving it available to support Rabbi Eliezer's position that bringing a blemished animal on a private *bamah* during the era when such altars were permitted likewise transgresses a prohibition.

The overall arc of this extended *sugya* has thus retraced and deepened the core distinctions of the *mishna* — between *kedusha* that attaches regardless of blemish and *kedusha* that requires an unblemished animal at the moment of sanctification — while establishing that across all categories, the presence of *kedusha* and the capacity to be offered are two distinct questions, and a blemish answers the second even when it cannot negate the first.
