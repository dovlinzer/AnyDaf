# Zevachim 115 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav Chilkiya Asham

### Chilkiya Thesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִלְקִיָּה (דְּבֵי) רַב טוֹבִי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לִשְׁמוֹ; אֲבָל שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ – חַיָּיב, הוֹאִיל וְרָאוּי לְשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ בִּפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥilkiya,** a Sage **from the school of Rav Tovi, says: They taught** this **only** with regard to one who slaughters a guilt offering outside the Temple courtyard **for its own sake. But** if he slaughtered it outside the Temple courtyard **not for its own sake** but for the sake of a different offering, he is **liable** for having sacrificed outside the courtyard. This is **because** it was **fit to** be sacrificed **not for its own sake inside** the Temple courtyard, as a guilt offering that was slaughtered not for its own sake is fit for sacrifice (see 2a).

The daf opens three lines down at *lameid mem mesayayei*, where the Gemara attempts to marshal support for what is, by any measure, a striking and radical position advanced by Rav Chilkiya. The scenario Rav Chilkiya addresses is this: a person *shachts* an *asham* on a *bama* outside the *Beis HaMikdash* before its time — meaning the individual who is obligated in the *asham*, say a *nazir* or a *metzora*, has not yet reached the point at which this *korban* is due. Were such a *korban* to be brought *lishmo* inside the *Beis HaMikdash*, it would not discharge the owner's obligation, and indeed it would be invalid. The question Rav Chilkiya raises is whether, when one *shachts* this *asham* outside and *shelo lishmo* — not for its designated name — one is liable for *shechutei chutz*.

### Identity Change

Rav Chilkiya's answer is yes, one is *chayav*. And this is where the genuine *chiddush* lies. Normally we assume that *shelo lishmo* invalidates a *korban* but does not do so completely — the owner is not *yotzei*, but the *korban* itself remains *kasher*. What Rav Chilkiya appears to be saying is something far more novel: *shelo lishmo* can, to some degree, change the very identity of the *korban*. An *asham* before its time is invalid. One would assume that an *asham shelo lishmo* before its time would be worse, or at the very least equally invalid. But Rav Chilkiya argues the opposite: slaughtering an *asham shelo lishmo* before its time — whether one is recasting it as an *olah*, a *shelamim*, or something else — renders it something other than a full *asham*, and therefore not subject to the invalidity that attaches to an *asham* brought prematurely. The result is that it is somehow more *kasher*, though Rav Chilkiya does not fully spell out what it then becomes or all the practical consequences that follow.

### Huna Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב הוּנָא: וְכִי יֵשׁ לְךָ דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ כָּשֵׁר לִשְׁמוֹ, וְכָשֵׁר שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna objects to** Rabbi Ḥilkiya's statement that a guilt offering whose time has not yet arrived is fit to be sacrificed inside if it is slaughtered not for its own sake: **And is there anything that is not fit** if its action is performed **for its own sake, but is fit** if its action is performed **not for its sake?**

Rav Huna challenged this position with an intuitive objection: how can performing a *korban shelo lishmo* make it *better*? That is the question the Gemara now takes up, as a series of *beraitot* is brought to bear on Rav Chilkiya's position.

## First Braita

### Olah vs Asham

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: יָכוֹל שֶׁאֲנִי מוֹצִיא אַף עוֹלַת מְחוּסַּר זְמַן בִּבְעָלִים, וַאֲשַׁם נָזִיר וַאֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שׁוֹר״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם, ״כֶּשֶׂב״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם, ״עֵז״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. וְאִילּוּ חַטָּאת – שַׁיְּירַהּ;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that the following *baraita* **supports** the opinion of Rabbi Ḥilkiya, who holds that one who slaughters a guilt offering whose time has not yet arrived, outside the courtyard, not for its own sake, is liable: One **might** have thought **that I exclude** from the category of those who are liable for slaughtering outside the courtyard **even** one who slaughters **a burnt offering whose time has not yet** arrived **due to** its being premature **for its owner,** e.g., a woman after childbirth whose term of impurity is not yet finished, **and** one who slaughters **the guilt offering of a nazirite and the guilt offering of a leper** before they are fit to sacrifice their offerings.
>
> **To counter this, the verse states** with regard to slaughter outside the courtyard: "Whatever person there be of the house of Israel that slaughters an ox, or lamb, or goat, in the camp, or that slaughters it outside the camp" (Leviticus 17:3). **"Ox"** indicates **in any case** of an ox, **"lamb"** indicates **in any case** of a lamb, and **"goat"** indicates **in any case** of a goat, that one is liable for slaughtering them outside the courtyard; **while a sin offering** was **omitted** from the cases in the *baraita* where one who slaughters the offering outside the courtyard is liable.

The first *braita* begins by asking whether one might exempt from *shechutei chutz* an *olah* brought before the owner's time had arrived — for instance, a *yoledet* who brings her *olah* before her forty or eighty days have elapsed. The *braita* answers: no, one is still *chayav*. Even though the owner's obligation has not yet been triggered, an *olah* is intrinsically valid at any time, so there is no exemption. The *braita* continues: perhaps one should also exempt *asham nazir* and *asham metzora* whose time has not yet arrived. Again, the *braita* rules *chayav*, deriving this from the repetition of *shor* and *kesev* in the verse *asher yishchat shor o kesev bamachaneh* — the doubling of the terms is inclusive, coming to extend liability to these cases.

The *braita* then implies a distinction: while one would be *chayav* for an *asham*, one would be *patur* for a *chatat*. This immediately demands clarification. *Bemai askinan* — what exactly is the case? If the *korbanot* are being brought *bizmano*, when it is already the proper time for both, then of course one is *chayav* in either case, and there is nothing novel to say about an *asham*. The very fact that the *braita* needed a *pasuk* to teach the liability implies we are not in a simple *bizmano* case. *Ela shelo bizmano* — it must be that we are speaking of a case before the proper time. And since the *braita* implies *chayav* for the *asham* but *patur* for the *chatat*, this supports Rav Chilkiya's position precisely: *shelo lishmo* changes the identity of the *asham* in a way that makes it valid, while for a *chatat* no such transformation is available.

### Gemara Weak Read

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא בִּזְמַנּוֹ – מַאי אִירְיָא אָשָׁם? אֲפִילּוּ חַטָּאת נָמֵי! אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא לִזְמַנּוֹ. וּבְמַאי? אִילֵימָא לִשְׁמוֹ – אָשָׁם אַמַּאי חַיָּיב? אֶלָּא לָאו שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ? לְעוֹלָם בִּזְמַנּוֹ וְשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What are we dealing with** in this *baraita* when it discusses the guilt offering of a nazirite and the guilt offering of a leper? **If we say** that it is dealing with a guilt offering that was slaughtered outside the courtyard **at its** proper **time, why** state that one is liable **specifically** for **a guilt offering?** One would be liable for slaughtering **a sin offering** outside the courtyard at its time **as well. Rather,** it is discussing a guilt offering that was slaughtered **not at its time,** i.e., when the nazirite or leper's time had not yet arrived.
>
> The Gemara explains: **And what** is the *baraita* dealing **with? If we say** that it is dealing with one who slaughtered it **for its sake, why** would he be **liable** for **a guilt offering** that was slaughtered outside the courtyard if it is not fit for sacrifice? **Rather, is** the *baraita* **not** dealing with one who slaughtered it **not for its sake,** and the *baraita* states that one would be liable, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Ḥilkiya? The Gemara rejects this: **Actually,** the *baraita* is dealing with one who slaughtered a guilt offering outside the courtyard **at its** proper **time and not for its sake.**

The Gemara, however, resists this reading. It proposes instead: *le'olam bizmano v'shelo lishmo* — in fact, we are speaking of a case where it is the proper time, but the *korban* was offered *shelo lishmo*. On this reading, the *chiddush* is not that an *asham* before its time becomes valid through *shelo lishmo*, but rather that even *shelo lishmo* at the proper time does not exempt from *shechutei chutz* liability. One might have thought that since *shelo lishmo* would have invalidated the *korban* inside the *Beis HaMikdash*, it should also remove the liability outside. The *braita* teaches otherwise: at the moment of *shechita* the *korban* was *ra'uy l'pesach ohel moed*, and the fact that the intent at that moment would have invalidated it had it been inside does not remove the liability for *shechutei chutz*.

### Braita Chiddush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְנָא טָפֵל, וְהוּא הַדִּין עִיקָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the *tanna* of the *baraita* **taught:** Guilt offering, which, in this context, is **secondary** to the sin offering, as it is derived from the latter. **But the same is true** of the **primary,** i.e., the sin offering: One who slaughters it outside the courtyard not for its sake is liable.

On this second reading, then, the *braita* is not distinguishing between *asham* and *chatat* at all. Rather, it is teaching about *asham* — and by extension *chatat*, since they are comparable — that *bizmano shelo lishmo* still generates *chiyuv*. According to this view, the reference to *asham* in the first *braita* is meant to include *chatat* as well, relying on the position of Rabbi Eliezer who equates *asham* to *chatat*. The *tanna* selected *asham* as its main example, but the same rule applies to *chatat*, and the *braita* is following Rabbi Eliezer who says *makshinan asham l'chatat* — the two are treated as a single category.

### Shelo Lishmo Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: מַקְּשִׁינַן אָשָׁם לְחַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** although there is ostensibly no need for an additional verse from which to derive the *halakha* in this case, as a guilt offering that was slaughtered not for its sake inside the courtyard is fit for sacrifice, the *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, who says: We juxtapose a guilt offering with a sin offering** in the verse: "As is the sin offering, so is the guilt offering; there is one law for them" (Leviticus 7:7), from which it may be derived that they have equivalent halakhic status. Therefore, a guilt offering that was slaughtered not for its sake is disqualified, and consequently one might say that one who slaughters a guilt offering not for its sake outside the Temple courtyard is exempt, as it is not fit for sacrifice. Therefore, the derivation from the verse is necessary.

The Gemara then articulates the upshot of this reading: the first *braita* (which discusses *asham* and liability) and the subsequent *braita* (which discusses *chatat* and exemption) are actually talking about different scenarios — the first, *bizmano shelo lishmo*, where one is *chayav*; the second, *shelo bizmano* and *shelo lishmo* together, where one is *patur* because the *korban* is not *ra'uy l'pesach ohel moed*. Under this reading, there is no meaningful difference between *asham* and *chatat*, and no support for Rav Chilkiya.

This is, as the Gemara itself seems to acknowledge, a weak answer. The plain implication of juxtaposing the two *beraitot* — one singling out *asham* as *chayav* and the other singling out *chatat* as *patur* — is that there is a genuine distinction between the two *korbanot*. The Gemara's response essentially erases that distinction by saying one *braita* means *asham* and also *chatat*, and the other means *chatat* and also *asham*, just in different situations. That is a strained read, and the Gemara seems to strain against itself to avoid endorsing Rav Chilkiya through these first two sources.

## Second Braita

### Chatat Lacking Time

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: יָכוֹל שֶׁאֲנִי מְרַבֶּה עוֹלַת מְחוּסַּר זְמַן – בְּגוּפָהּ, וְחַטָּאת – בֵּין בְּגוּפָהּ בֵּין בִּבְעָלִים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאֶל פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״ – כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ רָאוּי לָבֹא בְּפֶתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד, אֵין חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן. וְאִילּוּ אָשָׁם – שַׁיְּירֵהּ;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a *baraita* in support of Rabbi Ḥilkiya: One **might** have thought **that I include** in liability even one who slaughters **a burnt offering whose time has not yet** arrived because it is **intrinsically** lacking, i.e., eight days have not yet passed; **and** one who slaughters **a sin offering** whose time has not yet arrived, **whether** because it is **intrinsically** lacking **or** because it is premature for **the owner.**
>
> To counter this, **the verse states** with regard to one who slaughters outside the courtyard: **"And** has not brought it **to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting"** (Leviticus 17:4), which teaches that for **any** sacrifice **that is unfit to be brought to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting, one is not liable for** slaughtering it outside the courtyard. **But a guilt offering** whose time has not yet arrived was **omitted** by the *tanna*, from which it can be inferred that one who slaughters a guilt offering outside the courtyard is liable.

A second *braita* is introduced as a still more explicit source. It addresses an *olah* and a *chatat* that are *mechusar zman* — lacking the required time. By an *olah*, being *mechusar zman* in the *ba'alim* (the owner not yet obligated) does not matter, as an *olah* is always valid and one is *chayav* for *shechutei chutz*. But for a *chatat* where the owner's time of obligation has not yet arrived, the *braita* rules *patur*, deriving this from the verse *v'el pesach ohel moed lo hevi'o* — anything not fit to be brought to *petach ohel moed* does not incur liability. The implication is that for a *chatat* in this state, *shelo bizmano* renders it unfit, and consequently there is no *chiyuv* outside.

### Chayav vs Patur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא לִשְׁמוֹ – אָשָׁם אַמַּאי חַיָּיב? אֶלָּא לָאו שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ? לְעוֹלָם שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ, וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא – דְּמַקֵּישׁ אָשָׁם לְחַטָּאת. תְּנָא עִיקָּר, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן לְטָפֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are we dealing with? If we say** that the *baraita* is dealing with a case where one slaughtered a sin offering whose time has not yet arrived, outside the courtyard **for its sake,** then one who slaughters **a guilt offering should also be exempt,** as it is an offering whose time has not yet arrived. **Rather, is it not** dealing with a case of one who slaughtered a sin offering whose time has not yet arrived, outside the courtyard **not for its sake?** In this case, one would be exempt for slaughtering it outside the courtyard, as if it is slaughtered inside the courtyard not for its sake it is disqualified. But in the case of a guilt offering whose time has not yet arrived one would be liable, since it is fit for sacrifice, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Ḥilkiya.
>
> **Actually,** the *baraita* is dealing with one who slaughtered a sin offering whose time has not yet arrived, outside the courtyard **not for its sake. And** nevertheless, it may not be inferred that in the case of a guilt offering one would be liable, as this *baraita* may **be** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, who juxtaposes a guilt offering with a sin offering;** and one who slaughters a guilt offering is also exempt. The *tanna* of the *baraita* omitted mention of a guilt offering because he **taught:** Sin offering, which is the **primary** case of the disqualification of an offering slaughtered not for its sake, **and all the more so** it applies **to** the **secondary** case, i.e., the guilt offering.

The Gemara analyzes: *v'ilu asham shayrei* — the *braita* omitted *asham* from its list of exempt cases, implying that an *asham* in the same scenario — before the owner's time — would generate *chiyuv*. And again the question is pressed: *bemai askinan*? If the case is *lishmo*, then an *asham* before its time is clearly invalid, and one would also be *patur* — so why single out *chatat* as exempt if *asham* would equally be exempt? *Ela lav shelo lishmo* — it must be *shelo lishmo*. And if that is the case, then for a *chatat shelo bizmano shelo lishmo* one is *patur*, while for an *asham shelo bizmano shelo lishmo* one is *chayav* — which is precisely the proof to Rav Chilkiya's position.

### Gemara Pushback

Once again the Gemara deflects: *l'olam shelo lishmo* — yes, it is *shelo lishmo*, but the *braita* following Rabbi Eliezer treats *asham* and *chatat* identically, and one is *patur* for both. The *braita* mentions *chatat* as the primary example, but the same exemption applies to *asham* as well. As before, this is a demonstrably weak answer, eliminating the very distinction the *beraitot* appear to draw. The simple reading of these two *beraitot* side by side strongly supports Rav Chilkiya — that *asham shelo bizmano shelo lishmo* is *chayav* while *chatat* in the same situation is *patur* — and the Gemara's attempts to read around this are, by its own implicit admission, strained.

## Rav Dimi

### Rav Levi Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: דְּכִי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר, תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי לֵיוַאי: יָכוֹל שֶׁאֲנִי מוֹצִיא אַף עוֹלַת מְחוּסַּר זְמַן בִּבְעָלִים, אֲשַׁם נָזִיר וַאֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע; מִנַּיִן? וְנָסֵיב לְהוּ תַּלְמוּדָא לְחִיּוּבָא, וְלָא יָדַעְנָא מַאי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another proof for the opinion of Rabbi Ḥilkiya, **as when Rav Dimi came** to Babylonia from Eretz Yisrael **he said** that **the school of Rabbi Livai taught** a *baraita*: One **might** have thought **that I exclude** from the category of those who are liable for slaughtering offerings outside the Temple courtyard **even** one who slaughters **a burnt offering whose time has not yet** arrived **due to** its being premature **for its owner,** or who slaughters **a guilt offering of a nazirite or the guilt offering of a leper** whose time has not yet arrived due to its being premature for its owner. **From where** is it derived that one is liable for doing so? Rav Dimi added: **And** the Sages **brought a derivation** from a verse to prove that one is **liable, but I do not know what** the derivation **is.**

The Gemara now presents a more explicit and harder-to-deflect source. When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he transmitted the following from the house of Rav Levi: perhaps one might exempt from *shechutei chutz* an *olat mechusar zman b'va'alim* — an *olah* where the owner's time has not yet come. As established, an *olah* is always valid, so one is *chayav*. The *braita* then also includes *asham nazir* and *asham metzora* in the cases one might think to exempt, and concludes one is *chayav* for these as well — with the *pasuk* of *shor mikol makom kesev mikol makom* providing the derivation, as Ravina notes.

### Beit Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי תַּלְמוּדָא? אָמַר רָבִינָא: ״שׁוֹר״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם, ״כֶּשֶׂב״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם, ״עֵז״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. הָא מַאי רוּמְיָא? כִּדְאָמְרַתְּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What is the** biblical **derivation? Ravina said** that it is derived from the verse cited above with regard to offerings that are slaughtered outside the courtyard, where **"ox"** indicates **in any case** of an ox, **"lamb"** indicates **in any case** of a lamb, and **"goat"** indicates **in any case** of a goat. This supports the opinion of Rabbi Ḥilkiya, as the *baraita* is discussing a guilt offering whose time has not yet arrived that is slaughtered not for its sake, and contradicts the opinion of Rav Huna. The Gemara asks: **What contradiction** is **this?** It can be resolved **as you stated** earlier: That the *baraita* is dealing with a guilt offering that was slaughtered not for its sake at the proper time, and is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer.

Now the *braita* from Rav Levi's house turns to what happens inside the *Beis HaMikdash*. An *asham nazir* or *asham metzora* slaughtered *shelo lishmon* inside — *ksherim v'lo alu l'va'alim l'shum chova* — are valid, though they do not discharge the owner's obligation. However, if these *ashamot* were slaughtered *mechusar zman beva'alim* — before the owner's time had arrived — or if the animals were two years old rather than within the proper age range, they are *pasul*.

### Dimi Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מִשּׁוּם דְּרָמֵי דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי לֵיוַאי אַדְּתָנֵי לֵוִי: אֲשַׁם נָזִיר וַאֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן – כְּשֵׁירִין, וְלֹא עָלוּ לַבְּעָלִים לְשׁוּם חוֹבָה. שְׁחָטָן מְחוּסַּר זְמַן בִּבְעָלִים, אוֹ שֶׁהָיוּ בְנֵי שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים וּשְׁחָטָן – פְּסוּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said** that according to the opinion of Rav Dimi, the *baraita* cannot be interpreted as dealing with a guilt offering that was slaughtered at its proper time, **because he raises a contradiction between** the *baraita* **that the school of Rabbi Livai taught** and a *baraita* **that Levi taught: The guilt offering of a nazirite and the guilt offering of a leper that one slaughtered not for their sakes are fit, but they do not satisfy the obligation of the owner.**
>
> If one **slaughtered them** in a case where the offering was **premature due to its owner, or** if the lambs **were in their second year** when one **slaughtered them,** and not in their first year as required, they **are disqualified,** and one who slaughters them outside the Temple courtyard is not liable.

Rav Dimi noted an apparent contradiction. The first *braita* implies that an *asham shelo bizmano* is *kasher* — otherwise why would one be *chayav* for *shechutei chutz* outside? Yet the second *braita* from Rav Levi's house states explicitly that an *asham shelo bizmano* inside the *Beis HaMikdash* is *pasul*. How can the same scenario be *kasher* enough to generate *chiyuv* outside yet *pasul* when performed inside?

It is important to appreciate what Rav Dimi's question reveals. By framing it this way — asking why the *braita* implies *kasher* outside when the other *braita* says *pasul* inside — Rav Dimi implicitly demonstrates that his reading of the original *braita* was the straightforward one: that when it says one is *chayav* for an *asham* outside, it means *shelo bizmano shelo lishmo*. This shows that the Gemara's earlier deflections, however clever, do not represent the authentic understanding of those sources.

### Dimi Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְשַׁנֵּי רַב דִּימִי: לָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן לִשְׁמוֹ, כָּאן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Dimi answers:** This is **not difficult. Here,** in the *baraita* that states that one who slaughters outside the courtyard is exempt, it is referring to a case where the animal whose time has not yet arrived was slaughtered **for its sake; there,** in the *baraita* that teaches that one who slaughters outside the courtyard is liable, it is referring to a case where the offering was slaughtered **not for its sake.**

Rav Dimi resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya — kan lishmo, kan shelo lishmo*. The *braita* that says *shelo bizmano* is *pasul* inside is speaking of *lishmo* — when the *asham* is slaughtered in its own identity, before its time, it is invalid. The *braita* that implies *shelo bizmano* is *kasher* enough to create *chiyuv* outside is speaking of *shelo lishmo* — when one slaughters the *asham shelo lishmo*, the *shelo lishmo* changes its identity in such a way that it is no longer truly *shelo bizmano*, because it is no longer fully an *asham*. This is precisely Rav Chilkiya's position: *shelo lishmo* transforms the *korban's* identity, making it more *kasher*, not less. Rav Dimi's question and answer together serve as the clearest demonstration thus far that the strong reading of all these *beraitot* — contra the Gemara's earlier weak deflections — is indeed that there is a real distinction between *asham* and *chatat*, and the reason is the identity-transforming power of *shelo lishmo* by an *asham*.

## Rav Ashi

### Ashi Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי רָמֵי מַתְנִיתִין אַבָּרַיְיתָא, וּמְשַׁנֵּי: כָּאן לִשְׁמוֹ, וְכָאן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, **Rav Ashi raises a contradiction between the mishna,** which teaches that one who slaughters a guilt offering whose time has not yet arrived outside the courtyard is exempt, **and the *baraita*,** which teaches that one who does so is liable. **And** Rav Ashi **answers: Here,** the mishna that teaches that one is exempt is referring to a case where the offering was slaughtered **for its sake. And there,** the *baraita* that teaches that one is liable is referring to a case where the offering was slaughtered **not for its sake,** in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Ḥilkiya.

Rav Ashi then raised a parallel challenge of his own, *rami masnisin a-braita u'meshani kan lishmo v'kan shelo lishmo*. The *Mishnah* rules that one who slaughters an *asham* outside the *Beis HaMikdash* is *patur*, while the *braita* rules *chayav*. These appear to contradict one another directly.

### Ashi Answer

Rav Ashi resolves the tension with the same distinction: the *Mishnah* is speaking of *lishmo* — when the *asham* is slaughtered in its own name before its time, it is *pasul*, and one is *patur* outside. The *braita* is speaking of *shelo lishmo* — the *shelo lishmo* recasts the *korban's* identity, making it *kasher* enough that one is *chayav* for *shechutei chutz* outside. Rav Ashi's independent derivation of the same conclusion from a different contradiction confirms the force and necessity of Rav Chilkiya's position.

## Huna Concession

### Two Ashamot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתֵּיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא? אָמַר לְךָ רַב הוּנָא: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁהִפְרִישׁ שְׁתֵּי אֲשָׁמוֹת לְאַחְרָיוּת, דְּחַד מִינַּיְיהוּ מֵעִיקָּרָא עוֹלָה הִיא;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara says: If so, **shall we say** that this **is a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rav Huna,** who holds that one who slaughters a guilt offering whose time has not yet arrived not for its sake outside the courtyard is exempt?
>
> The Gemara replies that **Rav Huna** could have **said to you: What are we dealing with here,** in the *baraita* that deems one liable for a guilt offering that was slaughtered not for its sake? We are dealing with a case **where** one **separated two guilt offerings as a guarantee,** so that atonement would be achieved through the sacrifice of the second if the first was lost, but before the proper time arrived he slaughtered one of them as a burnt offering rather than as a guilt offering. In this case there would be liability for slaughtering outside the courtyard, as is the case with one who slaughters a burnt offering outside the courtyard, **since one of them,** i.e., the one that will not effect atonement, **is a burnt offering from the outset.**

All of this evidence, the Gemara observes, stands against Rav Huna, who had challenged Rav Chilkiya by asking how *shelo lishmo* could make something better. *Leima tehe tuvta d'Rav Huna* — should this all not constitute a definitive refutation of Rav Huna's position? The Gemara answers that Rav Huna can respond: *hacha bemai askinan* — here we are dealing with a specific scenario. Namely, one who separated *shtei ashamot le'achrayut* — from the very outset, two animals were set aside, one as the actual *asham* and the other as a backup in case the first is lost.

### Olah Identity

In this scenario, one of the two animals will inevitably become an *olah* — for if one of the *ashamot* is used to discharge the obligation and the other is no longer needed, the second, unlike a *chatat* (which would face *misah*), becomes an *olah*. Since from the moment of separation it is already determined that one of these animals will be an *olah*, when one slaughters it *shelo lishmo* — designating it as an *olah* — one is not mysteriously improving a *pasul asham* through *shelo lishmo*. Rather, one has simply identified which animal was already fated to be an *olah* and acted accordingly. That animal's identity as an *olah* was in place from the beginning, and therefore the *shelo lishmo* is consistent with a genuine *olah* designation, making it valid and generating *chiyuv* outside the *Beis HaMikdash*.

### Grazing Asham

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְרַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב. דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: אָשָׁם שֶׁנִּיתַּק לִרְעִיָּה, וּשְׁחָטוֹ סְתָם – כָּשֵׁר לָעוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** This is **in accordance with** the statement that **Rav Huna** says that **Rav says. As Rav Huna says** that **Rav says:** With regard to **a guilt offering** whose owner died or achieved atonement through a different guilt offering and which **was consigned to grazing** in the field until it develops a blemish, **and** prior to its being consigned one **slaughtered it without specification** of its purpose, it is **fit as a burnt offering.**

Rav Huna supports this with a precedent he himself stated in the name of Rav: an *asham shenitak lire'iya* — an *asham* set aside for grazing because the other animal was used to discharge the obligation — if slaughtered *stam*, without explicit intent, is *kasher l'olah*. In that case, once it is clear the animal will not be used as the *asham*, slaughtering it without specific intent is sufficient for it to be valid as an *olah*. By analogy, in the case of two *ashamot* separated from the outset, the animal that will become the *olah* is already on that trajectory, and intentionally designating it as an *olah* — the *shelo lishmo* — makes it valid. Rav Huna thus concedes that *shelo lishmo* can indeed make something more valid, but only because the animal was never fully a simple *asham* to begin with — one of the pair was inevitably an *olah* from the moment of separation.

## New Topic

### Burning Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּעֲלֶה מִבְּשַׂר חַטָּאת [וְכוּ׳]. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִנַּיִן לַמַּעֲלֶה מִבְּשַׂר חַטָּאת, וּמִבְּשַׂר אָשָׁם, וּמִבְּשַׂר קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, וּמִבְּשַׂר קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, וּמִמּוֹתַר הָעוֹמֶר, וּשְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם, וְלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים, וּשְׁיָרֵי מְנָחוֹת – שֶׁפָּטוּר?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **One who offers up** outside the Temple courtyard a portion **of the meat of a sin offering** that is eaten, or who offers up a portion of several other items, is exempt. With regard to the reasoning behind this *halakha*, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **that one who offers up** outside the Temple courtyard a portion **of the meat of a sin offering, or** a portion **of the meat of a guilt offering, or** a portion **of the meat of offerings of the most sacred order, or** a portion **of the meat of offerings of lesser sanctity, or** a portion **of the surplus of the *omer* offering, or the two loaves, or the shewbread, or the remainder of meal offerings** is **exempt,** as all these are eaten by the priests and not sacrificed on the altar?

The Gemara now transitions to a new topic from the *Mishnah*: burning the meat of a *korban* or a *mincha* that is ordinarily meant to be eaten. If one burns such items on an external altar, one is *patur* from the prohibition of *shechutei chutz*, since burning is not the prescribed treatment for these items inside the *Beis HaMikdash*.

### Enumeration

A *braita* enumerates the items that fall under this exemption: the meat of a *chatat*, the meat of an *asham*, the meat of *kodshei kodashim* — which would include something like *zivchei shalmei tzibur* — the meat of *kodashim kalim*, the leftover of the *omer*, *shtei halechem*, *lechem hapanim*, and *shiyarei mincha*. All of these are items meant to be eaten, and burning them on an outside altar does not generate liability.

## Burning Derivation

### Pasuk Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עוֹלָה״ – מָה עוֹלָה שֶׁהִיא רְאוּיָה לְהַעֲלָאָה, אַף כֹּל שֶׁרְאוּיָה לְהַעֲלָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The verse states** with regard to the prohibition against sacrificing outside the Temple courtyard: "Whatever man…that sacrifices **a burnt offering** or sacrifice, and brings it not to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting, to sacrifice it to the Lord, that man shall be cut off from his people" (Leviticus 17:8–9). The term "burnt offering" teaches: **Just as a burnt offering is fit for offering up** upon the altar, **so too, anything that is fit for offering up** is included in the prohibition. All of the offerings listed in the *baraita* are not sacrificed upon the altar but given to the priests.

The derivation is sourced from the verse *asher ya'aleh olah o zavach v'el pesach ohel moed lo hevi'o*. The Gemara reasons: just as an *olah* is a *korban* whose prescribed completion inside the *Beis HaMikdash* is burning on the *mizbeach*, so too, only things whose designated completion is burning are included in the prohibition. Things that have an earlier prescribed finishing stage — namely, being eaten — are not included. The principle is that the prohibition attaches to activities or substances that mirror the *olah*, whose entire process culminates in fire.

### Prep Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנַּיִן שֶׁאַף הַיּוֹצֵק, וְהַבּוֹלֵל, וְהַפּוֹתֵת, וְהַמּוֹלֵחַ, וְהַמֵּנִיף, וְהַמַּגִּישׁ, וְהַמְסַדֵּר הַשֻּׁלְחָן, וְהַמֵּטִיב אֶת הַנֵּרוֹת, וְהַקּוֹמֵץ, וְהַמְקַבֵּל בַּחוּץ – שֶׁפָּטוּר?
>
> **Translation:** **From where** is it derived **that even** with regard to **one who pours** oil onto the meal offering, **and one who mixes** the oil into the flour of the meal offering, **and one who breaks** the loaves of the meal offering into pieces, **and one who salts** the meal offering or other offerings, **and one who waves** the meal offering, **and one who brings** the meal offering to the corner of an altar that he constructs outside the courtyard, **and one who arranges** the shewbread on **the table** outside the Sanctuary, **and one who removes the ashes** from **the lamps** of the Candelabrum, **and one who removes a handful** from a meal offering, **and one who collects the blood** of an offering in a vessel, if he did so **outside** the Temple courtyard he is **exempt.**

The Gemara then extends this analysis to *avodos* — preparatory ritual acts. One might have thought that performing various *avodot* outside the *Beis HaMikdash* would also generate liability. The *braita* lists the following as *patur*: *hayotzek* (pouring oil on the *mincha*), *habolel* (mixing), *hapotes* (breaking up after baking), *hamole'ach* (salting), *hamenif* (waving), *hamagish* (touching the *mincha* to the altar), *hamesader hashulchan* (arranging the bread on the *shulchan*), *hametiv es hamenoros* (cleaning the *menorah*), *hakometz* (taking the *kometz*), and *hamekabel bachutz* (catching the blood). All of these, if performed outside, are *patur*.

### Process Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר יַעֲלֶה עוֹלָה אוֹ זָבַח״ – מָה הַעֲלָאָה שֶׁהִיא גְּמַר עֲבוֹדָה, אַף כֹּל שֶׁהוּא גְּמַר עֲבוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The verse states: "That sacrifices a burnt offering or sacrifice"** (Leviticus 17:8). **Just as sacrificing is the conclusion of** the sacrificial **service, so too, any** rite **that is the conclusion of** a sacrificial **service** is included. All of these are excluded from the prohibition, as there are rites that follow them.

The underlying principle is the same: *mah ha'olah sheg'mar avodah* — the *olah*'s burning is the culmination of the entire process. Only acts that represent the *gemar avodah* — the definitive completion of the process — are included in the prohibition. Acts that are middle stages are exempt. This is why *zrikas hadam* does generate liability — it is not a middle stage but the end of the process. Similarly, *nisuch hayayin* — the wine libation poured on the altar — is the culmination of its own process and therefore also *chayav*. By contrast, everything listed in the *braita* constitutes a preparatory or intermediate stage, and therefore falls outside the scope of the prohibition. *Shechita*, while technically also a middle stage, is explicitly included in the verse. *Kmitza*, which is analogous to *shechita*, is not explicit in the *pesukim*, and therefore, since it is a middle stage, it is *patur* outside.

## Bechor Avoda

### Mishnah Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁלֹּא הוּקַם הַמִּשְׁכָּן [וְכוּ׳]. יָתֵיב רַב הוּנָא בַּר רַב קַטִּינָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא, וְקָא קָרֵי: ״וַיִּשְׁלַח אֶת נַעֲרֵי בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי: קָרְבוּ וּפָסְקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Until the Tabernacle was established,** private altars were permitted and the sacrificial service was performed by the firstborn. The Gemara relates that **Rav Huna bar Rav Ketina was sitting before Rav Ḥisda and was reading** this verse with regard to the revelation at Sinai: **"And he sent the young men of the children of Israel,** who offered burnt offerings and sacrificed peace offerings of oxen to the Lord" (Exodus 24:5). The young men referred to in the verse were the firstborn of the Jewish people. Rav Ḥisda **said to him: This** is what **Rabbi Asi said: They sacrificed** the offerings **and** then **ceased** to serve; after that day, the firstborn no longer performed the sacrificial service.

The Gemara now introduces a challenge to this picture. Rav Huna bar Rav Ketina was sitting before Rav Chisda and was reading the verse *vayishlach et na'arei bnei Yisrael vayizbechu zevachim shelamim l'Hashem parim* — the young men of Bnei Yisrael were sent and they slaughtered *shelamim* for God at Har Sinai. Rav Chisda said to him: thus did Rav Assi say — *u'pasku*, and they stopped after that. That is, the *bechorot* brought *korbanot* only at Har Sinai, and from that point forward, even before the *Mishkan* was erected, the *bechor* no longer performed the *avoda*. This contradicts the *Mishnah*'s claim that the *bechor* continued until the *Mishkan* was put up, since Har Sinai was approximately ten months before the *Mishkan* was completed.

### Kohanim Immediately

Rashi, however, reads the text differently. Rather than *u'pasku* — "they stopped" — Rashi's version has the sense that Rav Chisda interrupted the reading at *vayishlach et na'arei bnei Yisrael*, putting a full stop there. On this reading, the *na'arei bnei Yisrael* were sent merely to fetch the animals or assist in some logistical capacity. The actual *shechita* and *avoda* — *vayizbechu zevachim shelamim l'Hashem parim* — was performed by the *Kohanim* even then. Under Rashi's reading, the *Kohanim* had already assumed their role even at the moment of Har Sinai, and the *bechorot* were never officiating in the *avoda* at all.

## Braita Support

### Braita Main

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: עַד שֶׁלֹּא הוּקַם הַמִּשְׁכָּן – הַבָּמוֹת מוּתָּרוֹת, וַעֲבוֹדָה בִּבְכוֹרוֹת, וְהַכֹּל כְּשֵׁירִין לְהִקָּרֵיב; בְּהֵמָה, חַיָּה וָעוֹף, זְכָרִים וּנְקֵבוֹת, תְּמִימִין וּבַעֲלֵי מוּמִין, טְהוֹרִין אֲבָל לֹא טְמֵאִין.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Until the Tabernacle was established,** private **altars** were **permitted, the** sacrificial **service** was performed **by the firstborn, and all** animals were **fit to be sacrificed: A domesticated animal, an undomesticated animal, or a bird; males and females; unblemished and blemished** animals. All animal sacrifices were brought from animals and birds that were **kosher, but not** from **non-kosher** species.

A *braita* is introduced that supports the *Mishnah's* position that the *bechorot* performed the *avoda* until the *Mishkan* was erected. The *braita* states: *ad shelo hukam haMishkan*, before the *Mishkan* was erected, *bamot* were permitted and the *avoda* was performed by the *bechorot*. Furthermore, all kosher animals were acceptable for offering: *behema, chaya, v'of* — domesticated animals, wild animals, and birds alike.

### Animal Details

The *braita* elaborates: animals could be male or female, *temimim u-va'alei mumim* — unblemished or blemished. The sole requirement was that the animal be *tahor* — a kosher species — as non-kosher animals were excluded. And all of these offerings were brought as *olot*. This last point will be questioned in the Gemara, since the verse at Har Sinai mentions *shelamim* as well, but the *braita's* general assertion is that before the *Mishkan* the *olah* was the dominant form of offering.

### Flaying Detail

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַכֹּל קָרְבוּ עוֹלוֹת. וְעוֹלָה שֶׁהִקְרִיבוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל בַּמִּדְבָּר – טְעוּנָה הֶפְשֵׁט וְנִיתּוּחַ. וְגוֹיִם בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה רַשָּׁאִין לַעֲשׂוֹת כֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** **And all** offerings brought before the construction of the Tabernacle were **sacrificed** as **burnt offerings. And** the **burnt offering that the Jewish people sacrificed in the wilderness** before the Tabernacle was established **required flaying** of the skin **and cutting** into pieces. **And today, gentiles are permitted to** sacrifice offerings on private altars. The *baraita* states explicitly that until the Tabernacle was constructed, the sacrificial service was performed by the firstborn, and the burnt offering required flaying and cutting.

Remarkably, however, the *braita* states that one detail of the *olah* already applied even in this earlier period: *ve'ola she'hikrivuYisrael ba'midbar te'una hafshet v'nituach* — the *olah* that Yisrael brought in the *midbar*, before the *Mishkan*, required *hafshet v'nituach*, the flaying and chopping. This is striking because all the other details of sacrificial law — restriction to certain species, exclusion of blemished animals, designation of *Kohanim* — had not yet come into force. Yet this one technical detail of the *olah* already applied. The Gemara will grapple with this anomaly, noting also that non-Jews today, who are not subject to the prohibition of *bamot*, may still bring offerings on private altars.

## Kohanim Timing

### Bechor Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא; דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְגַם הַכֹּהֲנִים הַנִּגָּשִׁים אֶל ה׳ יִתְקַדָּשׁוּ״ – רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה אוֹמֵר: זוֹ פְּרִישׁוּת בְּכוֹרוֹת. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: זוֹ פְּרִישׁוּת נָדָב וַאֲבִיהוּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda replied that with regard to the firstborn, it **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: God said to Moses on Mount Sinai: **"And let the priests also that come near to the Lord sanctify themselves,** lest the Lord break forth upon them" (Exodus 19:22). In other words, they should separate themselves and not approach the mountain. This command was given one day after the burnt offerings and peace offerings were sacrificed in anticipation of the revelation at Sinai. With regard to this command, **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says: This** command is a reference to **the separation of** the **firstborn,** as they functioned as priests. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: This** command is a reference to **the separation of Nadav and Avihu,** who were priests.

The question of when the *Kohanim* assumed their role — at Har Sinai or only at the *Mishkan* — is resolved by identifying it as a debate among *Tannaim*. The *braita* asks: who are the *kohanim hanigashim el Hashem* of whom the Torah speaks at Har Sinai when God commands that the *kohanim* who draw near to God must sanctify themselves? Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha says: *zu prishus bechorot* — this directive was addressed to the *bechorot*, who had the priestly status at Har Sinai. Under his view, the transition did not occur until the *Mishkan*, consistent with the *Mishnah's* ruling.

### Kohanim Version

Rabbi Judah HaNasi (Rabbi) disagrees: *zu prishus Nadav v'Avihu* — the directive at Har Sinai was already addressed to Nadav and Avihu, who were already functioning as *Kohanim*. Under this view, the transition to *Kehuna* had already occurred at or before Har Sinai, and the *bechorot* never continued in that role past that point.

### Nadav Avihu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר זוֹ פְּרִישׁוּת נָדָב וַאֲבִיהוּא – הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״הוּא אֲשֶׁר דִּבֶּר ה׳ לֵאמֹר בִּקְרֹבַי אֶקָּדֵשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to the one who says** that the command for the priests to sanctify themselves is referring to **the separation of Nadav and Avihu, this is** the meaning of that **which is written** after their death on the eighth day of the inauguration of the Tabernacle: "Then Moses said to Aaron: **This is it that the Lord spoke, saying: Through them that are near to Me I will be sanctified…**and Aaron held his peace" (Leviticus 10:3). Nadav and Avihu had already been warned not to draw too close: "Lest the Lord break forth upon them."

The Gemara then asks: according to Rabbi, who holds that it was Nadav and Avihu who were warned at Har Sinai, this supports the verse *hu asher diber Hashem* — that Moshe's statement to Aharon upon the death of his sons, namely *bikrovai ekadesh*, is understood as a fulfillment of what God had said even at Har Sinai. Those who draw close to God risk the very sanctification through death that fell upon Nadav and Avihu. The verse in the context of building the *Mishkan* — *v'noadeti shama livnei Yisrael v'nikdash bichvodi* — is read not as *bichvodi* but *bimchubadai*: I will be sanctified through those who honor Me, meaning that those who draw too close, even if they are the most honored, will face divine judgment, and that judgment itself sanctifies God before all of Israel.

## Nadav Avihu

### Sinai Warning

At Har Sinai, God had warned that those who draw near must maintain their distance — *pen yifrotz bam Hashem* — lest God burst out against them. This warning was directed, according to Rabbi, at Nadav and Avihu specifically, signaling their priestly status even at that moment.

### Moshe Consolation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר זוֹ פְּרִישׁוּת בְּכוֹרוֹת – הֵיכָא רְמִיזָא? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְנֹעַדְתִּי שָׁמָּה לִבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְנִקְדַּשׁ בִּכְבֹדִי״ – אַל תִּקְרֵי ״בִּכְבוֹדִי״, אֶלָּא ״(בִּמְכוּבָּדַיי) [בִּכְבוּדַיי]״.
>
> **Translation:** **But according to the one who says** that the command for the priests to sanctify themselves is referring to **the separation of** the **firstborn, where is the allusion** to the fact that God would be sanctified through Nadav and Avihu? The Gemara replies: **As it is written: "And there I will meet with the children of Israel; and it shall be sanctified by My glory"** (Exodus 29:43). **Do not read** it as **"by My glory [*bikhvodi*]"; rather,** read it as: **By My honored ones [*bimekhubadai*].** God will be sanctified by those considered honored by God when He reveals Himself in the Tabernacle.

When Nadav and Avihu died after the *Mishkan* was erected — killed for bringing a *eish zarah*, a strange fire — Moshe spoke to Aharon and said: this is precisely what God had told you, *bikrovai ekadesh* — I will be sanctified through those who are closest to Me. The death of Nadav and Avihu, as terrible as it was, communicated to all of Israel the awesome holiness that surrounds God's presence, and in that sense sanctified His name before the entire nation.

### Silence Reward

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר זֶה אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְמֹשֶׁה וְלֹא יָדְעוּ, עַד שֶׁמֵּתוּ בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן. כֵּיוָן שֶׁמֵּתוּ בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן, אָמַר לוֹ: אַהֲרֹן אָחִי, לֹא מֵתוּ בָּנֶיךָ אֶלָּא לְהַקְדִּישׁ שְׁמוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא. כֵּיוָן שֶׁיָּדַע אַהֲרֹן שֶׁבָּנָיו יְדוּעֵי מָקוֹם הֵן, שָׁתַק וְקִבֵּל שָׂכָר; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּדֹּם אַהֲרֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said this statement to Moses, but** Moses **did not know** its meaning **until the sons of Aaron died. Once the sons of Aaron died,** Moses **said to him: Aaron, my brother, your sons died only to sanctify the name of the Holy One, Blessed be He. When Aaron knew that his sons were beloved by the Omnipresent, he was silent and received a reward, as it is stated: "And Aaron held his peace [*vayidom*]."**

Aharon's response — *vaydome Aharon*, Aharon fell silent — is analyzed with care. The Gemara does not describe a man who was fully consoled or who intellectually accepted the explanation. It describes a man who, despite grief, despite the inadequacy of any explanation, permitted himself to receive God's judgment in silence. He did not protest. He did not demand an accounting. And for that silence — *v'kibel sachar* — he received reward.

### Silent Acceptance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן בְּדָוִד הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״דּוֹם לַה׳ וְהִתְחוֹלֵל לוֹ״ – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמַּפִּיל לְךָ חֲלָלִים חֲלָלִים, אַתְּ שְׁתוֹק. וְכֵן בִּשְׁלֹמֹה הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״עֵת לַחֲשׁוֹת וְעֵת לְדַבֵּר״ – פְּעָמִים שֶׁשּׁוֹתֵק וּמְקַבֵּל שָׂכָר עַל הַשְּׁתִיקָה, פְּעָמִים מְדַבֵּר וּמְקַבֵּל שָׂכָר עַל הַדִּבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And likewise in** a verse written by **David** it **states: "Resign yourself [*dom*] to the Lord, and wait patiently [*vehitḥolel*] for Him"** (Psalms 37:7). **Although He strikes down many corpses [*ḥalalim*]** around **you, you be silent** and do not complain. **And likewise in** a verse written by **Solomon** it **states: "A time to keep silence, and a time to speak"** (Ecclesiastes 3:7). There are **times that** one **is silent and receives reward for the silence,** and at **times** one **speaks and receives reward for the speech.**

The Gemara finds this principle of rewarded silence expressed elsewhere in *Tanach*. The verse in Psalms — *dome la'Hashem v'hitcholeil lo* — is rendered: be silent before God and long for Him, even when it seems as though corpses are falling around you, *af al pi shemapil lach chalalim chalalim*, still withhold protest. And Kohelet teaches: *eis lachashos v'eis l'dabber* — there is a time to be silent and a time to speak. Sometimes silence is the demanded response, and the reward is for that silence itself. Sometimes speech is demanded, and the reward is for that. The two are not opposed; the wisdom lies in recognizing which moment calls for which.

### Judgment Glory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״נוֹרָא אֱלֹהִים מִמִּקְדָּשֶׁךָ״? אַל תִּיקְרֵי ״מִמִּקְדָּשֶׁךָ״ אֶלָּא ״מִמְּקוּדָּשֶׁיךָ״ – בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעוֹשֶׂה הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא דִּין בִּקְדוֹשָׁיו, מִתְיָירֵא וּמִתְעַלֶּה וּמִתְהַלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is what Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "Awesome is God out of your holy places"** (Psalms 68:36)? **Do not read** it as: **"From your holy places [*mimikdashekha*]"; rather,** read it as: **From your holy ones [*mimekudashekha*]. When the Holy One, Blessed be He, carries out judgment upon His holy ones, He is feared, and exalted, and praised** by all.

The Gemara concludes with a teaching of Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: what is meant by *nora elohim mimikdashecha* — God, You are awesome from Your holy place? Read not *mimikdashecha* but *mimikudashecha* — from those who sanctify You. When God executes judgment even against those who are close to Him, against the holy ones themselves, He is feared, glorified, and elevated. The very willingness to hold even the most beloved and dedicated to the strictest standard is what manifests God's true awesomeness before the world.

## Tannaitic Timing

### Ishmael Gradual

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: הַכֹּל כְּשֵׁירִין לְהַקְרִיב. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וַיִּבֶן נֹחַ מִזְבֵּחַ לַה׳ וַיִּקַּח מִכֹּל הַבְּהֵמָה הַטְּהוֹרָה וּמִכׇּל עוֹף הַטָּהוֹר״; בְּהֵמָה – כְּמַשְׁמָעוֹ. חַיָּה – בִּכְלַל בְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Master said** in the *baraita*: Before the Tabernacle was established, **all** animals were **fit to be sacrificed:** A domesticated animal, an undomesticated animal, or a bird. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rav Huna said: As the verse states** with regard to the offering that was sacrificed after the flood: **"And Noah built an altar to the Lord, and took of every pure animal, and of every pure fowl,** and offered burnt offerings on the altar" (Genesis 8:20). The Gemara explains: **"Animal [*behema*],"** is understood **in accordance with its plain meaning,** a domesticated animal, and the same is true of fowl; **an undomesticated animal [*ḥayya*]** is **included in** the term **"*behema*"** that is stated in the verse.

The Gemara now addresses the apparent anomaly regarding *hafshet v'nituach*. How is it that this one detail of the *olah* applied already before the *Mishkan*, while all other specifics — species restrictions, exclusion of blemishes, *Kohanim* performing the service — did not? The Gemara identifies this too as a *tannaitic* debate. Rabbi Yishmael holds: *klalos ne'emru b'Sinai, pratos b'Ohel Moed* — the general principles were communicated at Har Sinai, but the specific details were given only at *Ohel Moed*, meaning at the beginning of *Sefer Vayikra* once the *Mishkan* was standing. On this view, the detail of *hafshet v'nituach* did not apply before the *Mishkan*, because the detailed laws of *olah* were not yet in force.

### Akiva Complete

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּרֵי תַּנָּאֵי הִיא; דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: כְּלָלוֹת נֶאֶמְרוּ בְּסִינַי, וּפְרָטוֹת בְּאֹהֶל מוֹעֵד. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: כְּלָלוֹת וּפְרָטוֹת נֶאֶמְרוּ בְּסִינַי, וְנִשְׁנוּ בְּאֹהֶל מוֹעֵד, וְנִשְׁתַּלְּשׁוּ בְּעַרְבוֹת מוֹאָב.
>
> **Translation:** This **is** a dispute between the opinions of **two *tanna'im*. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yishmael says:** The **general statements,** i.e., the principles of the Torah, **were said at Sinai, and** the **details** of the mitzvot that are explicated in Leviticus were said to Moses **in the Tent of Meeting.** This includes the *halakha* that the burnt offering must be flayed and cut into pieces. Consequently, it could not have been in effect before the construction of the Tabernacle.
>
> **Rabbi Akiva says:** Both **general statements and** the **details** of mitzvot **were said at Sinai and** later **taught again in the Tent of Meeting, and** taught **a third time** by Moses to the Jewish people **in the plains of Moab,** when he taught the Torah to the people (see Deuteronomy 1:1). According to Rabbi Akiva's opinion, the *halakha* of flaying and cutting into pieces was in effect when the Torah was given, even before the construction of the Tabernacle.

Rabbi Akiva holds the opposite: *klalos u'pratos ne'emru b'Sinai* — both the general principles and the specific details were already stated at Har Sinai. They were then repeated at *Ohel Moed*, and repeated again a third time at *Arvot Moav* before the entry into the Land. On this view, *hafshet v'nituach* did apply even before the *Mishkan*, because all details of the *olah* had already been communicated.

### Flaying Anomaly

This generates a genuine difficulty. If Rabbi Akiva holds that all details were already given at Har Sinai, then *hafshet v'nituach* correctly applies before the *Mishkan*. But what about all the other details — the restriction to certain species, the requirement of unblemished animals, the designation of *Kohanim*? Those evidently did not apply before the *Mishkan*. So even on Rabbi Akiva's view, it seems strange to single out *hafshet v'nituach* as already operative while treating all other *olah* specifics as not yet in force. The *braita's* picture — all kosher animals, male and female, blemished and unblemished, were brought before the *Mishkan* — implies that those laws were not yet applicable, yet *hafshet v'nituach* somehow was. The asymmetry is striking, and the Gemara does not fully resolve it here.

### Animal Acceptance

A related question is how we know that before the *Mishkan*, all types of animals — male and female, blemished and unblemished — were in fact acceptable. The source for this leniency, and how it interacts with the broader question of which rules applied and which did not in the period between Har Sinai and the erection of the *Mishkan*, remains to be worked out. The Gemara leaves this for continued discussion, and the shiur will resume tomorrow with these questions.
