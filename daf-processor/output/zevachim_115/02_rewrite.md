# Zevachim 115 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav Chilkiya Asham

### Chilkiya Thesis

The daf opens three lines down at *lameid mem mesayayei*, where the Gemara attempts to marshal support for what is, by any measure, a striking and radical position advanced by Rav Chilkiya. The scenario Rav Chilkiya addresses is this: a person *shachts* an *asham* on a *bama* outside the *Beis HaMikdash* before its time — meaning the individual who is obligated in the *asham*, say a *nazir* or a *metzora*, has not yet reached the point at which this *korban* is due. Were such a *korban* to be brought *lishmo* inside the *Beis HaMikdash*, it would not discharge the owner's obligation, and indeed it would be invalid. The question Rav Chilkiya raises is whether, when one *shachts* this *asham* outside and *shelo lishmo* — not for its designated name — one is liable for *shechutei chutz*.

### Identity Change

Rav Chilkiya's answer is yes, one is *chayav*. And this is where the genuine *chiddush* lies. Normally we assume that *shelo lishmo* invalidates a *korban* but does not do so completely — the owner is not *yotzei*, but the *korban* itself remains *kasher*. What Rav Chilkiya appears to be saying is something far more novel: *shelo lishmo* can, to some degree, change the very identity of the *korban*. An *asham* before its time is invalid. One would assume that an *asham shelo lishmo* before its time would be worse, or at the very least equally invalid. But Rav Chilkiya argues the opposite: slaughtering an *asham shelo lishmo* before its time — whether one is recasting it as an *olah*, a *shelamim*, or something else — renders it something other than a full *asham*, and therefore not subject to the invalidity that attaches to an *asham* brought prematurely. The result is that it is somehow more *kasher*, though Rav Chilkiya does not fully spell out what it then becomes or all the practical consequences that follow.

### Huna Challenge

Rav Huna challenged this position with an intuitive objection: how can performing a *korban shelo lishmo* make it *better*? That is the question the Gemara now takes up, as a series of *beraitot* is brought to bear on Rav Chilkiya's position.

---

## First Braita

### Olah vs Asham

The first *braita* begins by asking whether one might exempt from *shechutei chutz* an *olah* brought before the owner's time had arrived — for instance, a *yoledet* who brings her *olah* before her forty or eighty days have elapsed. The *braita* answers: no, one is still *chayav*. Even though the owner's obligation has not yet been triggered, an *olah* is intrinsically valid at any time, so there is no exemption. The *braita* continues: perhaps one should also exempt *asham nazir* and *asham metzora* whose time has not yet arrived. Again, the *braita* rules *chayav*, deriving this from the repetition of *shor* and *kesev* in the verse *asher yishchat shor o kesev bamachaneh* — the doubling of the terms is inclusive, coming to extend liability to these cases.

The *braita* then implies a distinction: while one would be *chayav* for an *asham*, one would be *patur* for a *chatat*. This immediately demands clarification. *Bemai askinan* — what exactly is the case? If the *korbanot* are being brought *bizmano*, when it is already the proper time for both, then of course one is *chayav* in either case, and there is nothing novel to say about an *asham*. The very fact that the *braita* needed a *pasuk* to teach the liability implies we are not in a simple *bizmano* case. *Ela shelo bizmano* — it must be that we are speaking of a case before the proper time. And since the *braita* implies *chayav* for the *asham* but *patur* for the *chatat*, this supports Rav Chilkiya's position precisely: *shelo lishmo* changes the identity of the *asham* in a way that makes it valid, while for a *chatat* no such transformation is available.

### Gemara Weak Read

The Gemara, however, resists this reading. It proposes instead: *le'olam bizmano v'shelo lishmo* — in fact, we are speaking of a case where it is the proper time, but the *korban* was offered *shelo lishmo*. On this reading, the *chiddush* is not that an *asham* before its time becomes valid through *shelo lishmo*, but rather that even *shelo lishmo* at the proper time does not exempt from *shechutei chutz* liability. One might have thought that since *shelo lishmo* would have invalidated the *korban* inside the *Beis HaMikdash*, it should also remove the liability outside. The *braita* teaches otherwise: at the moment of *shechita* the *korban* was *ra'uy l'pesach ohel moed*, and the fact that the intent at that moment would have invalidated it had it been inside does not remove the liability for *shechutei chutz*.

### Braita Chiddush

On this second reading, then, the *braita* is not distinguishing between *asham* and *chatat* at all. Rather, it is teaching about *asham* — and by extension *chatat*, since they are comparable — that *bizmano shelo lishmo* still generates *chiyuv*. According to this view, the reference to *asham* in the first *braita* is meant to include *chatat* as well, relying on the position of Rabbi Eliezer who equates *asham* to *chatat*. The *tanna* selected *asham* as its main example, but the same rule applies to *chatat*, and the *braita* is following Rabbi Eliezer who says *makshinan asham l'chatat* — the two are treated as a single category.

### Shelo Lishmo Limits

The Gemara then articulates the upshot of this reading: the first *braita* (which discusses *asham* and liability) and the subsequent *braita* (which discusses *chatat* and exemption) are actually talking about different scenarios — the first, *bizmano shelo lishmo*, where one is *chayav*; the second, *shelo bizmano* and *shelo lishmo* together, where one is *patur* because the *korban* is not *ra'uy l'pesach ohel moed*. Under this reading, there is no meaningful difference between *asham* and *chatat*, and no support for Rav Chilkiya.

This is, as the Gemara itself seems to acknowledge, a weak answer. The plain implication of juxtaposing the two *beraitot* — one singling out *asham* as *chayav* and the other singling out *chatat* as *patur* — is that there is a genuine distinction between the two *korbanot*. The Gemara's response essentially erases that distinction by saying one *braita* means *asham* and also *chatat*, and the other means *chatat* and also *asham*, just in different situations. That is a strained read, and the Gemara seems to strain against itself to avoid endorsing Rav Chilkiya through these first two sources.

---

## Second Braita

### Chatat Lacking Time

A second *braita* is introduced as a still more explicit source. It addresses an *olah* and a *chatat* that are *mechusar zman* — lacking the required time. By an *olah*, being *mechusar zman* in the *ba'alim* (the owner not yet obligated) does not matter, as an *olah* is always valid and one is *chayav* for *shechutei chutz*. But for a *chatat* where the owner's time of obligation has not yet arrived, the *braita* rules *patur*, deriving this from the verse *v'el pesach ohel moed lo hevi'o* — anything not fit to be brought to *petach ohel moed* does not incur liability. The implication is that for a *chatat* in this state, *shelo bizmano* renders it unfit, and consequently there is no *chiyuv* outside.

### Chayav vs Patur

The Gemara analyzes: *v'ilu asham shayrei* — the *braita* omitted *asham* from its list of exempt cases, implying that an *asham* in the same scenario — before the owner's time — would generate *chiyuv*. And again the question is pressed: *bemai askinan*? If the case is *lishmo*, then an *asham* before its time is clearly invalid, and one would also be *patur* — so why single out *chatat* as exempt if *asham* would equally be exempt? *Ela lav shelo lishmo* — it must be *shelo lishmo*. And if that is the case, then for a *chatat shelo bizmano shelo lishmo* one is *patur*, while for an *asham shelo bizmano shelo lishmo* one is *chayav* — which is precisely the proof to Rav Chilkiya's position.

### Gemara Pushback

Once again the Gemara deflects: *l'olam shelo lishmo* — yes, it is *shelo lishmo*, but the *braita* following Rabbi Eliezer treats *asham* and *chatat* identically, and one is *patur* for both. The *braita* mentions *chatat* as the primary example, but the same exemption applies to *asham* as well. As before, this is a demonstrably weak answer, eliminating the very distinction the *beraitot* appear to draw. The simple reading of these two *beraitot* side by side strongly supports Rav Chilkiya — that *asham shelo bizmano shelo lishmo* is *chayav* while *chatat* in the same situation is *patur* — and the Gemara's attempts to read around this are, by its own implicit admission, strained.

---

## Rav Dimi

### Rav Levi Braita

The Gemara now presents a more explicit and harder-to-deflect source. When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he transmitted the following from the house of Rav Levi: perhaps one might exempt from *shechutei chutz* an *olat mechusar zman b'va'alim* — an *olah* where the owner's time has not yet come. As established, an *olah* is always valid, so one is *chayav*. The *braita* then also includes *asham nazir* and *asham metzora* in the cases one might think to exempt, and concludes one is *chayav* for these as well — with the *pasuk* of *shor mikol makom kesev mikol makom* providing the derivation, as Ravina notes.

### Beit Status

Now the *braita* from Rav Levi's house turns to what happens inside the *Beis HaMikdash*. An *asham nazir* or *asham metzora* slaughtered *shelo lishmon* inside — *ksherim v'lo alu l'va'alim l'shum chova* — are valid, though they do not discharge the owner's obligation. However, if these *ashamot* were slaughtered *mechusar zman beva'alim* — before the owner's time had arrived — or if the animals were two years old rather than within the proper age range, they are *pasul*.

### Dimi Question

Rav Dimi noted an apparent contradiction. The first *braita* implies that an *asham shelo bizmano* is *kasher* — otherwise why would one be *chayav* for *shechutei chutz* outside? Yet the second *braita* from Rav Levi's house states explicitly that an *asham shelo bizmano* inside the *Beis HaMikdash* is *pasul*. How can the same scenario be *kasher* enough to generate *chiyuv* outside yet *pasul* when performed inside?

It is important to appreciate what Rav Dimi's question reveals. By framing it this way — asking why the *braita* implies *kasher* outside when the other *braita* says *pasul* inside — Rav Dimi implicitly demonstrates that his reading of the original *braita* was the straightforward one: that when it says one is *chayav* for an *asham* outside, it means *shelo bizmano shelo lishmo*. This shows that the Gemara's earlier deflections, however clever, do not represent the authentic understanding of those sources.

### Dimi Answer

Rav Dimi resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya — kan lishmo, kan shelo lishmo*. The *braita* that says *shelo bizmano* is *pasul* inside is speaking of *lishmo* — when the *asham* is slaughtered in its own identity, before its time, it is invalid. The *braita* that implies *shelo bizmano* is *kasher* enough to create *chiyuv* outside is speaking of *shelo lishmo* — when one slaughters the *asham shelo lishmo*, the *shelo lishmo* changes its identity in such a way that it is no longer truly *shelo bizmano*, because it is no longer fully an *asham*. This is precisely Rav Chilkiya's position: *shelo lishmo* transforms the *korban's* identity, making it more *kasher*, not less. Rav Dimi's question and answer together serve as the clearest demonstration thus far that the strong reading of all these *beraitot* — contra the Gemara's earlier weak deflections — is indeed that there is a real distinction between *asham* and *chatat*, and the reason is the identity-transforming power of *shelo lishmo* by an *asham*.

---

## Rav Ashi

### Ashi Challenge

Rav Ashi then raised a parallel challenge of his own, *rami masnisin a-braita u'meshani kan lishmo v'kan shelo lishmo*. The *Mishnah* rules that one who slaughters an *asham* outside the *Beis HaMikdash* is *patur*, while the *braita* rules *chayav*. These appear to contradict one another directly.

### Ashi Answer

Rav Ashi resolves the tension with the same distinction: the *Mishnah* is speaking of *lishmo* — when the *asham* is slaughtered in its own name before its time, it is *pasul*, and one is *patur* outside. The *braita* is speaking of *shelo lishmo* — the *shelo lishmo* recasts the *korban's* identity, making it *kasher* enough that one is *chayav* for *shechutei chutz* outside. Rav Ashi's independent derivation of the same conclusion from a different contradiction confirms the force and necessity of Rav Chilkiya's position.

---

## Huna Concession

### Two Ashamot

All of this evidence, the Gemara observes, stands against Rav Huna, who had challenged Rav Chilkiya by asking how *shelo lishmo* could make something better. *Leima tehe tuvta d'Rav Huna* — should this all not constitute a definitive refutation of Rav Huna's position? The Gemara answers that Rav Huna can respond: *hacha bemai askinan* — here we are dealing with a specific scenario. Namely, one who separated *shtei ashamot le'achrayut* — from the very outset, two animals were set aside, one as the actual *asham* and the other as a backup in case the first is lost.

### Olah Identity

In this scenario, one of the two animals will inevitably become an *olah* — for if one of the *ashamot* is used to discharge the obligation and the other is no longer needed, the second, unlike a *chatat* (which would face *misah*), becomes an *olah*. Since from the moment of separation it is already determined that one of these animals will be an *olah*, when one slaughters it *shelo lishmo* — designating it as an *olah* — one is not mysteriously improving a *pasul asham* through *shelo lishmo*. Rather, one has simply identified which animal was already fated to be an *olah* and acted accordingly. That animal's identity as an *olah* was in place from the beginning, and therefore the *shelo lishmo* is consistent with a genuine *olah* designation, making it valid and generating *chiyuv* outside the *Beis HaMikdash*.

### Grazing Asham

Rav Huna supports this with a precedent he himself stated in the name of Rav: an *asham shenitak lire'iya* — an *asham* set aside for grazing because the other animal was used to discharge the obligation — if slaughtered *stam*, without explicit intent, is *kasher l'olah*. In that case, once it is clear the animal will not be used as the *asham*, slaughtering it without specific intent is sufficient for it to be valid as an *olah*. By analogy, in the case of two *ashamot* separated from the outset, the animal that will become the *olah* is already on that trajectory, and intentionally designating it as an *olah* — the *shelo lishmo* — makes it valid. Rav Huna thus concedes that *shelo lishmo* can indeed make something more valid, but only because the animal was never fully a simple *asham* to begin with — one of the pair was inevitably an *olah* from the moment of separation.

---

## New Topic

### Burning Exemption

The Gemara now transitions to a new topic from the *Mishnah*: burning the meat of a *korban* or a *mincha* that is ordinarily meant to be eaten. If one burns such items on an external altar, one is *patur* from the prohibition of *shechutei chutz*, since burning is not the prescribed treatment for these items inside the *Beis HaMikdash*.

### Enumeration

A *braita* enumerates the items that fall under this exemption: the meat of a *chatat*, the meat of an *asham*, the meat of *kodshei kodashim* — which would include something like *zivchei shalmei tzibur* — the meat of *kodashim kalim*, the leftover of the *omer*, *shtei halechem*, *lechem hapanim*, and *shiyarei mincha*. All of these are items meant to be eaten, and burning them on an outside altar does not generate liability.

---

## Burning Derivation

### Pasuk Logic

The derivation is sourced from the verse *asher ya'aleh olah o zavach v'el pesach ohel moed lo hevi'o*. The Gemara reasons: just as an *olah* is a *korban* whose prescribed completion inside the *Beis HaMikdash* is burning on the *mizbeach*, so too, only things whose designated completion is burning are included in the prohibition. Things that have an earlier prescribed finishing stage — namely, being eaten — are not included. The principle is that the prohibition attaches to activities or substances that mirror the *olah*, whose entire process culminates in fire.

### Prep Acts

The Gemara then extends this analysis to *avodos* — preparatory ritual acts. One might have thought that performing various *avodot* outside the *Beis HaMikdash* would also generate liability. The *braita* lists the following as *patur*: *hayotzek* (pouring oil on the *mincha*), *habolel* (mixing), *hapotes* (breaking up after baking), *hamole'ach* (salting), *hamenif* (waving), *hamagish* (touching the *mincha* to the altar), *hamesader hashulchan* (arranging the bread on the *shulchan*), *hametiv es hamenoros* (cleaning the *menorah*), *hakometz* (taking the *kometz*), and *hamekabel bachutz* (catching the blood). All of these, if performed outside, are *patur*.

### Process Principle

The underlying principle is the same: *mah ha'olah sheg'mar avodah* — the *olah*'s burning is the culmination of the entire process. Only acts that represent the *gemar avodah* — the definitive completion of the process — are included in the prohibition. Acts that are middle stages are exempt. This is why *zrikas hadam* does generate liability — it is not a middle stage but the end of the process. Similarly, *nisuch hayayin* — the wine libation poured on the altar — is the culmination of its own process and therefore also *chayav*. By contrast, everything listed in the *braita* constitutes a preparatory or intermediate stage, and therefore falls outside the scope of the prohibition. *Shechita*, while technically also a middle stage, is explicitly included in the verse. *Kmitza*, which is analogous to *shechita*, is not explicit in the *pesukim*, and therefore, since it is a middle stage, it is *patur* outside.

## Bechor Avoda

### Mishnah Claim

The Gemara now turns to what is one of the more historically fascinating topics in the *masechet*: who performed the *avoda* before the *Mishkan* was erected. The *Mishnah* states clearly: *ad shelo hukam haMishkan*, before the *Mishkan* was put up, it was the *bechor* — the firstborn — who performed the sacred service. The assumption is that the transition to *Kohanim* under Aharon and his sons occurred only on the first of Nissan of the second year in the desert, when the *Mishkan* was completed and dedicated. Until that point, the firstborn held the priestly role.

### Sinai Only

The Gemara now introduces a challenge to this picture. Rav Huna bar Rav Ketina was sitting before Rav Chisda and was reading the verse *vayishlach et na'arei bnei Yisrael vayizbechu zevachim shelamim l'Hashem parim* — the young men of Bnei Yisrael were sent and they slaughtered *shelamim* for God at Har Sinai. Rav Chisda said to him: thus did Rav Assi say — *u'pasku*, and they stopped after that. That is, the *bechorot* brought *korbanot* only at Har Sinai, and from that point forward, even before the *Mishkan* was erected, the *bechor* no longer performed the *avoda*. This contradicts the *Mishnah*'s claim that the *bechor* continued until the *Mishkan* was put up, since Har Sinai was approximately ten months before the *Mishkan* was completed.

### Kohanim Immediately

Rashi, however, reads the text differently. Rather than *u'pasku* — "they stopped" — Rashi's version has the sense that Rav Chisda interrupted the reading at *vayishlach et na'arei bnei Yisrael*, putting a full stop there. On this reading, the *na'arei bnei Yisrael* were sent merely to fetch the animals or assist in some logistical capacity. The actual *shechita* and *avoda* — *vayizbechu zevachim shelamim l'Hashem parim* — was performed by the *Kohanim* even then. Under Rashi's reading, the *Kohanim* had already assumed their role even at the moment of Har Sinai, and the *bechorot* were never officiating in the *avoda* at all.

---

## Braita Support

### Braita Main

A *braita* is introduced that supports the *Mishnah's* position that the *bechorot* performed the *avoda* until the *Mishkan* was erected. The *braita* states: *ad shelo hukam haMishkan*, before the *Mishkan* was erected, *bamot* were permitted and the *avoda* was performed by the *bechorot*. Furthermore, all kosher animals were acceptable for offering: *behema, chaya, v'of* — domesticated animals, wild animals, and birds alike.

### Animal Details

The *braita* elaborates: animals could be male or female, *temimim u-va'alei mumim* — unblemished or blemished. The sole requirement was that the animal be *tahor* — a kosher species — as non-kosher animals were excluded. And all of these offerings were brought as *olot*. This last point will be questioned in the Gemara, since the verse at Har Sinai mentions *shelamim* as well, but the *braita's* general assertion is that before the *Mishkan* the *olah* was the dominant form of offering.

### Flaying Detail

Remarkably, however, the *braita* states that one detail of the *olah* already applied even in this earlier period: *ve'ola she'hikrivuYisrael ba'midbar te'una hafshet v'nituach* — the *olah* that Yisrael brought in the *midbar*, before the *Mishkan*, required *hafshet v'nituach*, the flaying and chopping. This is striking because all the other details of sacrificial law — restriction to certain species, exclusion of blemished animals, designation of *Kohanim* — had not yet come into force. Yet this one technical detail of the *olah* already applied. The Gemara will grapple with this anomaly, noting also that non-Jews today, who are not subject to the prohibition of *bamot*, may still bring offerings on private altars.

---

## Kohanim Timing

### Bechor Version

The question of when the *Kohanim* assumed their role — at Har Sinai or only at the *Mishkan* — is resolved by identifying it as a debate among *Tannaim*. The *braita* asks: who are the *kohanim hanigashim el Hashem* of whom the Torah speaks at Har Sinai when God commands that the *kohanim* who draw near to God must sanctify themselves? Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha says: *zu prishus bechorot* — this directive was addressed to the *bechorot*, who had the priestly status at Har Sinai. Under his view, the transition did not occur until the *Mishkan*, consistent with the *Mishnah's* ruling.

### Kohanim Version

Rabbi Judah HaNasi (Rabbi) disagrees: *zu prishus Nadav v'Avihu* — the directive at Har Sinai was already addressed to Nadav and Avihu, who were already functioning as *Kohanim*. Under this view, the transition to *Kehuna* had already occurred at or before Har Sinai, and the *bechorot* never continued in that role past that point.

### Nadav Avihu

The Gemara then asks: according to Rabbi, who holds that it was Nadav and Avihu who were warned at Har Sinai, this supports the verse *hu asher diber Hashem* — that Moshe's statement to Aharon upon the death of his sons, namely *bikrovai ekadesh*, is understood as a fulfillment of what God had said even at Har Sinai. Those who draw close to God risk the very sanctification through death that fell upon Nadav and Avihu. The verse in the context of building the *Mishkan* — *v'noadeti shama livnei Yisrael v'nikdash bichvodi* — is read not as *bichvodi* but *bimchubadai*: I will be sanctified through those who honor Me, meaning that those who draw too close, even if they are the most honored, will face divine judgment, and that judgment itself sanctifies God before all of Israel.

---

## Nadav Avihu

### Sinai Warning

At Har Sinai, God had warned that those who draw near must maintain their distance — *pen yifrotz bam Hashem* — lest God burst out against them. This warning was directed, according to Rabbi, at Nadav and Avihu specifically, signaling their priestly status even at that moment.

### Moshe Consolation

When Nadav and Avihu died after the *Mishkan* was erected — killed for bringing a *eish zarah*, a strange fire — Moshe spoke to Aharon and said: this is precisely what God had told you, *bikrovai ekadesh* — I will be sanctified through those who are closest to Me. The death of Nadav and Avihu, as terrible as it was, communicated to all of Israel the awesome holiness that surrounds God's presence, and in that sense sanctified His name before the entire nation.

### Silence Reward

Aharon's response — *vaydome Aharon*, Aharon fell silent — is analyzed with care. The Gemara does not describe a man who was fully consoled or who intellectually accepted the explanation. It describes a man who, despite grief, despite the inadequacy of any explanation, permitted himself to receive God's judgment in silence. He did not protest. He did not demand an accounting. And for that silence — *v'kibel sachar* — he received reward.

### Silent Acceptance

The Gemara finds this principle of rewarded silence expressed elsewhere in *Tanach*. The verse in Psalms — *dome la'Hashem v'hitcholeil lo* — is rendered: be silent before God and long for Him, even when it seems as though corpses are falling around you, *af al pi shemapil lach chalalim chalalim*, still withhold protest. And Kohelet teaches: *eis lachashos v'eis l'dabber* — there is a time to be silent and a time to speak. Sometimes silence is the demanded response, and the reward is for that silence itself. Sometimes speech is demanded, and the reward is for that. The two are not opposed; the wisdom lies in recognizing which moment calls for which.

### Judgment Glory

The Gemara concludes with a teaching of Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: what is meant by *nora elohim mimikdashecha* — God, You are awesome from Your holy place? Read not *mimikdashecha* but *mimikudashecha* — from those who sanctify You. When God executes judgment even against those who are close to Him, against the holy ones themselves, He is feared, glorified, and elevated. The very willingness to hold even the most beloved and dedicated to the strictest standard is what manifests God's true awesomeness before the world.

---

## Tannaitic Timing

### Ishmael Gradual

The Gemara now addresses the apparent anomaly regarding *hafshet v'nituach*. How is it that this one detail of the *olah* applied already before the *Mishkan*, while all other specifics — species restrictions, exclusion of blemishes, *Kohanim* performing the service — did not? The Gemara identifies this too as a *tannaitic* debate. Rabbi Yishmael holds: *klalos ne'emru b'Sinai, pratos b'Ohel Moed* — the general principles were communicated at Har Sinai, but the specific details were given only at *Ohel Moed*, meaning at the beginning of *Sefer Vayikra* once the *Mishkan* was standing. On this view, the detail of *hafshet v'nituach* did not apply before the *Mishkan*, because the detailed laws of *olah* were not yet in force.

### Akiva Complete

Rabbi Akiva holds the opposite: *klalos u'pratos ne'emru b'Sinai* — both the general principles and the specific details were already stated at Har Sinai. They were then repeated at *Ohel Moed*, and repeated again a third time at *Arvot Moav* before the entry into the Land. On this view, *hafshet v'nituach* did apply even before the *Mishkan*, because all details of the *olah* had already been communicated.

### Flaying Anomaly

This generates a genuine difficulty. If Rabbi Akiva holds that all details were already given at Har Sinai, then *hafshet v'nituach* correctly applies before the *Mishkan*. But what about all the other details — the restriction to certain species, the requirement of unblemished animals, the designation of *Kohanim*? Those evidently did not apply before the *Mishkan*. So even on Rabbi Akiva's view, it seems strange to single out *hafshet v'nituach* as already operative while treating all other *olah* specifics as not yet in force. The *braita's* picture — all kosher animals, male and female, blemished and unblemished, were brought before the *Mishkan* — implies that those laws were not yet applicable, yet *hafshet v'nituach* somehow was. The asymmetry is striking, and the Gemara does not fully resolve it here.

### Animal Acceptance

A related question is how we know that before the *Mishkan*, all types of animals — male and female, blemished and unblemished — were in fact acceptable. The source for this leniency, and how it interacts with the broader question of which rules applied and which did not in the period between Har Sinai and the erection of the *Mishkan*, remains to be worked out. The Gemara leaves this for continued discussion, and the shiur will resume tomorrow with these questions.
