# Bava Metzia 108 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Forest & River

### Hitkoshshu Vakoshu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא – לָא מִיסְתַּגֵּי לְהוּ. רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא הֲוָה לֵיהּ הָהוּא אִבָּא אַגּוּדָּא דְנַהֲרָא, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: נִיקּוֹץ מָר! אֲמַר לְהוּ: קוּצוּ עִילָּאֵי וְתַתָּאֵי, וַהֲדַר נִיקּוֹץ אֲנָא. הֵיכִי עָבֵיד הָכִי? וְהָכְתִיב: ״הִתְקוֹשְׁשׁוּ וָקוֹשּׁוּ״, וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: קְשׁוֹט עַצְמְךָ וְאַחַר כָּךְ קְשׁוֹט אֲחֵרִים!
>
> **Translation:** **and if not, they will be unable to walk.** The Gemara continues: **Rabba bar Rav Huna had a certain forest on the bank of a river. They said to him: Let the Master cut down** the trees on the riverbank in accordance with the above statement. Rabba bar Rav Huna **said to them: Let those above and below me along the river cut down their trees first, and then I will cut down my trees.** The Gemara asks: **How can he do so, i.e., wait for others to act? But isn't it written: "Gather yourselves together, and gather [*hitkosheshu vakoshu*]"** (Zephaniah 2:1), **and Reish Lakish says** concerning this: **Adorn [*keshot*] yourself and afterward adorn others.** Therefore, one must first perform the required action himself before offering advice to others.

The *daf* opens with a case that actually begins five lines from the bottom of the previous page. Rabba bar Rav Huna owned a forest situated on the bank of a river. The public came to him and said that he ought to cut down the vegetation along the riverbank, since people needed that cleared space to pull their barges along the shore. Rabba bar Rav Huna refused, saying: let the people above me and below me cut first, and then I will follow suit.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this stance: how could he do that? Does not the verse teach *hitkoshshu vakoshu* — gather yourselves and then gather others — and did not Reish Lakish interpret this to mean *kesshot atzmecha ve'achar kach kesshot acheirim*, adorn yourself first and only then concern yourself with others? On this reading, Rabba bar Rav Huna had no business demanding that others act before him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם אִבָּא דְּבֵי פַרְזַק רוּפִילָא הֲוָה, וְאָמַר: אִי קָיְיצוּ – קָיֵיצְנָא, וְאִי לָא קָיְיצוּ – אַמַּאי אִיקּוֹץ. דְּאִי מִמַּתְחִי לְהוּ – אַשְׁלַיְיהוּ מִסְתַּגִּי לְהוּ, וְאִי לָא – לָא מִיסְתַּגֵּי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **There** it **was the forest of the house of Parzak, the general, and** it was obvious that they would pay no attention to a Jewish scholar. Rabba bar Rav Huna therefore **said: If** the workers of the Persian officer **cut down, I will cut down** as well, **and if** they do **not cut down, why should I cut down** for no purpose? **Since if** the pullers can **stretch their ropes they can go** along this side of the river, **and if not, they will be unable to walk.**

The *gemara* answers that the situation was exceptional. The other portion of the forest along that stretch of river was owned by a man named Parzak, a Persian military official. The principle of *hitkoshshu vakoshu* presupposes that one's own action will influence or prompt others to do the same. Rabba bar Rav Huna had no leverage over Parzak; he could not expect a Persian general to follow his lead. Furthermore, the opposite bank of the river was already fully cleared, and bargemen were using that side without difficulty. If he cut down his portion on this side, the cleared strip would remain isolated — the bargemen would have no reason to cross to his side for a mere forty feet of cleared bank. His portion, in practical terms, served no public purpose unless the entire stretch was cleared, and since he had no ability to compel Parzak, he was justified in waiting.

### Curse & Consequences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר רַב נַחְמָן הֲוָה קָא אָזֵיל בְּאַרְבָּא, חֲזָא הָהוּא אִבָּא דְּקָאֵי אַגּוּדָּא דְנַהְרָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: דְּמַאן? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: דְּרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא. אָמַר: ״וְיַד הַשָּׂרִים וְהַסְּגָנִים הָיְתָה בַּמַּעַל הַזֶּה רִאשׁוֹנָה״. אֲמַר לְהוּ: קוּצוּ. קַצּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a related incident: **Rabba bar Rav Naḥman was going on a boat** and **saw a certain forest that** was **located** right **on the riverbank,** as its trees had not been cut down to make room for the pullers. **He said to** those who were with him: **To whom** does this forest belong? **They said to him:** It belongs **to Rabba bar Rav Huna.** Rabba bar Rav Naḥman **said:** This is reminiscent of the verse: **"And the hand of the princes and the rulers has been first in this faithlessness"** (Ezra 9:2), because a renowned scholar is acting improperly. Rabba bar Rav Naḥman **said to them: Cut down, cut down** to clear a path.

Rabba bar Rav Nachman was traveling by boat when he spotted a forest whose riverbank had not been cleared. He asked whose it was and was told it belonged to Rabba bar Rav Huna. Displeased, he quoted the verse: *yad hasarim vehasganim hayta bamaal haze rishona* — the hand of the officers and the ministers was foremost in this act of unfaithfulness. He then instructed those present: cut it down, cut it down.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ דְּקַיִיץ, אֲמַר: מַאן קַצְיֵיהּ – תִּקּוֹץ עַנְפֵיהּ. אָמְרִי כּוּלְּהוּ שְׁנֵי דְּרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא, לָא אִקַּיַּים לֵיהּ זַרְעָא לְרַבָּה בַּר רַב נַחְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Huna arrived** and **found that** his forest had been **cut down.** Since he was within his rights not to cut down his trees, as explained above, he grew angry and **pronounced** a curse: He **who cut down** this forest **should** have **his branches cut down.** The Sages **said:** Although he was unaware of the identity of the perpetrator, the Sage's curse was nevertheless fulfilled, and consequently **all the** remaining **years that Rabba bar Rav Huna** was alive, **the seed of Rabba bar Rav Naḥman did not last,** as his children, his branches, died in his lifetime.

When Rabba bar Rav Huna returned and found that the forest had been cut, he was furious. He declared: whoever cut this down — may his branches be cut off. Rashi explains that "branches" here refers to his children. The *gemara* records that throughout the lifetime of Rabba bar Rav Huna, none of the children of Rabba bar Rav Nachman survived.

### Equity & Justice

This is a striking passage. The underlying legal position of Rabba bar Rav Huna was sound — he had genuinely demonstrated why his particular circumstances exempted him from the otherwise applicable obligation to clear the riverbank. Yet the response to having that clearing done against his will was a curse with devastating consequences. The *gemara* does not explain the severity of the reaction. It is worth noting that such extreme outcomes appear in other Talmudic narratives as well, and no clean resolution of the equitable tension is offered. The narrative simply stands.

One question that arises is about the involvement of Rabba bar Rav Nachman himself. He was also a rabbinic leader — a *sar* or *sagan* in the sense invoked by the verse he cited. The verse *yad hasarim vehasganim* was presumably being applied to Rabba bar Rav Huna, yet it was directed at him by another leader. The *gemara* is silent on this tension as well.

There is also a broader point about what is at stake in this passage. The space along the riverbank was not quite a *reshut harabbim* in the classic legal sense — a true *reshut harabbim* is a thoroughfare to which the public has free passage in all directions. The riverbank was something different, more like a zone that functions publicly without technically being *hefker*. Nonetheless, the underlying issue is a communal one: what obligations does private ownership create in spaces that the public depends upon?

## Public Works

### Wall & Gate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הַכֹּל לְאִיגְלֵי גַפָּא, וַאֲפִילּוּ מִיַּתְמֵי – אֲבָל רַבָּנַן לָא. מַאי טַעְמָא? רַבָּנַן לָא צְרִיכִי נְטִירוּתָא. לְכַרְיָא דְפַתְיָא – וַאֲפִילּוּ מֵרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says: All** participate in the payment **for the construction of the** city **wall, and** this sum is collected **even from orphans, but not** from **the Torah scholars. What is the reason** for this? **The Torah scholars do not require protection,** as the merit of their Torah study protects them from harm. By contrast, money is collected **for the digging of** a river or **a well** for drinking water, **even from the Torah scholars.**

This communal theme continues with a statement of Rav Yehuda regarding a city wall and its gate. Rav Yehuda rules that everyone is obligated to participate in repairing the city wall and gate — *ha-kol le-iglei gappa* — including orphans, who are typically exempt from many communal obligations. Rabbis, however, are exempt. The reason given is that *rabbanan lo tzerikhei netiruta*: the rabbis do not require protection. The implication is that divine protection attends Torah scholars, rendering the physical security of the wall irrelevant to them.

This may appear inequitable — the scholars surely benefit from the wall's protection along with everyone else. Within the metaphysical assumptions of the *gemara*, however, the exemption is internally coherent: the obligation to share in the construction of a communal security infrastructure is tied to benefiting from it, and if the rabbis genuinely do not depend on it for their safety, it would be inequitable to require them to contribute. The analogy to other contexts where *talmidei chachamim* enjoy exemptions from marketplace obligations or public levies is instructive: sometimes the reason is their benefit to the community through Torah, sometimes their dignity, and sometimes, as here, the argument that they simply do not require what the obligation is designed to provide.

### Water Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא נָפְקִי בְּכָלוֹזָא. אֲבָל לְכָלוֹזָא – לָא, דְּרַבָּנַן לָאו בְּנֵי מִיפַּק בְּכָלוֹזָא נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And we said** this *halakha* **only if** the town inhabitants do **not go out in a crowd** to perform the work themselves but pay workers to act on their behalf. **But** if they go out **in a crowd,** Torah scholars do **not** have to join them, **as Torah scholars are not** among those **who go out in a crowd** to perform work in public view.

Water sources, however, are treated differently. Rav Yehuda rules that for repairing a *ma'ayan* — a spring or water source — even the rabbis must contribute: *va-afilu me-rabbanan*. The qualification is that this applies only when the work is being done by hiring others to perform it. The rabbis are not people who go out themselves as part of a labor force — *rabbanan lav bnei meipak be-akhloza ninhu* — it is beneath their dignity to join a work crew for physical labor. But they do pay their share of the cost of hiring others. This is consistent with a recurring pattern throughout *Nezikin* where rabbinic exemptions pertain to personal participation in labor but not to financial contribution.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: לְכַרְיָא דְנַהְרָא – תַּתָּאֵי מְסַיְּיעִי עִילָּאֵי, עִילָּאֵי לָא מְסַיְּיעִי תַּתָּאֵי. וְחִילּוּפָא בְּמַיָּא דְמִיטְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says: With regard to the digging of a river,** i.e., the periodic deepening of a riverbed to prevent it from blocking up, **the lower ones,** i.e., those who live by the bottom of the river, must **assist the upper ones** in digging it and fixing it, as those located at the bottom of the river stand to gain from any work performed down to their houses. But **the upper ones do not** need to **assist the lower ones,** as the reverse is not the case. **And the opposite** is true **with regard to** the digging of a ditch to remove **rainwater.** In that case, those who live higher up are interested in the operation and therefore must help the lower ones, but the latter need not aid the higher ones in doing so in the upper area.

The Rav Yehuda further articulates the asymmetry *within* the water infrastructure itself. When the obligation involves dredging a river or maintaining an irrigation channel — *kharaya denahara* — the *tattai*, those lower on the irrigation chain, must help the *ilai*, those at the upper end. The reverse does not hold: the *ilai* need not help the *tattai* with the portions of the channel that serve only those below. This seems unequal, but the *gemara* balances it with the opposite rule for rainwater drainage: when the concern is getting runoff out of the city, the *ilai* — those higher on the hill — must help the *tattai* at the drainage outlet, because if the exit point at the bottom of the city is blocked, everyone's drainage backs up. The people at the very top of the hill, however, are solely responsible for clearing their own portion at the summit, since a blockage there affects only them. The two systems together produce a rough equity: each party bears obligations in one direction that they do not bear in the other.

### Braita on Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: חָמֵשׁ גַּנּוֹת הַמִּסְתַּפְּקוֹת מַיִם מִמַּעְיָן אֶחָד, וְנִתְקַלְקֵל הַמַּעְיָין – כּוּלָּם מְתַקְּנוֹת עִם הָעֶלְיוֹנָה. נִמְצֵאת הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה מְתַקֶּנֶת עִם כּוּלָּן וּמְתַקֶּנֶת לְעַצְמָהּ. וְכֵן חָמֵשׁ חֲצֵרוֹת שֶׁהָיוּ מְקַלְּחוֹת מַיִם לְבִיב אֶחָד, וְנִתְקַלְקֵל הַבִּיב – כּוּלָּן מְתַקְּנוֹת עִם הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה, נִמְצֵאת הָעֶלְיוֹנָה מְתַקֶּנֶת עִם כּוּלָּן וּמְתַקֶּנֶת לְעַצְמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: If there were **five gardens that draw** their **water** requirements **from one spring and the spring became damaged, all** must help **fix** it **with** the owner of **the upper** garden, near whose garden the damage occurred. As a **result** of this ruling, the owner of **the lower** garden **fixes** it **with all of them** in the above case, **and fixes it for himself** if the damage occurred in the lower area. **And similarly,** if there were **five courtyards that would run off water into a single sewer and the sewer became damaged, all** must help **fix** it **with** the owner of **the lower** courtyard, near whose courtyard the damage occurred. The **result** is that the owner of **the upper** courtyard **fixes** the sewer **with all of them and fixes it for himself** if the damage affected his courtyard alone. This is in accordance with Rav Yehuda's ruling.

A *braita* confirms this teaching, and it is worth noting that this particular *braita* is unusual for a *tanya nami hachi* passage. Such passages typically introduce a *tannaitic* source whose wording is virtually identical to the Amoraic statement just given — a feature that has made academic scholars suspicious of many such *braitot*, since they often appear only in the Bavli and not in the Tosefta or Yerushalmi. Here, the teaching is the same, but the language of the *braita* differs from Rav Yehuda's formulation.

The *braita* states: five gardens that draw water from a single *ma'ayan* — if the spring is damaged, all five must help repair the portion used by the uppermost field, and the lowermost field must help repair every portion of the channel, but bears sole responsibility for maintaining the final stretch that serves only itself. The analogous rule for drainage runs in reverse: five courtyards that drain into a single channel — if the channel fails, all must help repair the section leading out from the lowermost courtyard, while the uppermost courtyard must help with every section but is alone responsible for its own portion at the top.

## Riverbank Rights

### Shmuel's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַאי מַאן דְּאַחְזֵיק בְּרַקְתָּא דְנַהְרָא – חֲצִיפָא הָוֵי, סַלּוֹקֵי לָא מְסַלְּקִינַן לֵיהּ. וְהָאִידָּנָא דְּקָא כָּתְבִי פָּרְסָאֵי: קְנֵי לָךְ עַד מְלֵי צַוְּארֵי סוּסְיָא מַיָּא – סַלּוֹקֵי נָמֵי מְסַלְּקִינַן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel says: One who takes possession of** an open space left along **a riverbank** for the purpose of loading and unloading in order to plow and plant there during the time that it is temporarily unused **is impudent.** As for **removing** him, **we do not remove him,** as this piece of land is considered ownerless. **And nowadays, when the Persians write** to one who acquires land alongside a river: **Acquire for yourself** the field **up to** the portion of the river itself where **the water reaches a horse's neck, we even** go as far as to **remove him** from the plot of land, as it belongs to the owner of the field.

Returning to the question of riverbank ownership, Shmuel establishes the following rule: one who takes control — *achzik* — of a riverbank has acted brazenly, *chatsifei havei*, but we do not remove him, *siluki lo mesalkinan lei*. According to the commentators, "taking control" here refers specifically to paying the head tax to the Persian authorities on a piece of land, which confers a degree of legal standing under Persian law even without proper title under Jewish law. The riverbank is a zone the public depends upon, so occupying it is *chutzpadik* — but the rabbis do not forcibly evict the person who has done so.

### Persian Contract

A later development, however, changes this. Once the Persians introduced — or once the Jewish community adopted — a standard contract formulation granting the landowner rights to the riverbank up to the point where a horse is neck-deep in water, the legal landscape shifted. The riverbank is now definitively part of the adjacent landowner's domain. If someone else comes and attempts to occupy or take control of that strip, we do not merely call it brazen — we also remove him. *Siluki nami mesalkinan lei.*

### Brothers & Partners

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: הַאי מַאן דְּאַחְזֵיק בֵּינֵי אַחֵי וּבֵינֵי שׁוּתָּפֵי, חֲצִיפָא הָוֵי. סַלּוֹקֵי לָא מְסַלְּקִינַן לֵיהּ. וְרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: נָמֵי מְסַלְּקִינַן. וְאִי מִשּׁוּם דִּינָא דְּבַר מִצְרָא – לָא מְסַלְּקִינַן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: One who takes possession** of land that is located **between** the land of **brothers or between** the land of **partners** and causes them trouble **is impudent.** As for **removing** him, **we do not remove him,** as they have no real claim against him. **And Rav Naḥman said: We even** go as far as to **remove** him, as one should not do anything that harms another. **And if** the complaint against him is **due to the *halakha* of one** whose field **borders** the field of his neighbor, as they owned fields bordering on this one, **we do not remove him.**

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav applies a similar structure to land taken from among brothers or among partners. Such a taking is also *chatsifei havei* — it is improper — but Rav holds that we do not remove the person who has done it. Rav Nachman disagrees: we do remove such a person.

### Nahardai & Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְהַרְדָּעֵי אָמְרִי: אֲפִילּוּ מִשּׁוּם דִּינָא דְּבַר מִצְרָא – מְסַלְּקִינַן לֵיהּ, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְעָשִׂיתָ הַיָּשָׁר וְהַטּוֹב בְּעֵינֵי ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **of Neharde'a say: Even** if his claim was **due to the *halakha* of one** whose field **borders** the field of his neighbor, **we** still **remove him, as it is stated: "And you shall do that which is right and good in the eyes of the Lord"** (Deuteronomy 6:18). One should not perform an action that is not right and good, even if he is legally entitled to do so.

The *gemara* then introduces the position of the *Nahardai*: even in cases of *dina de-bar metsara* — the neighbor's right of first refusal, which is the subject of the remainder of the *daf* — we remove the interloper. Their basis is the verse *ve'asita hayashar vehatov be'einei Hashem*, "you shall do what is good and upright in the eyes of God." It is this verse that provides the scriptural underpinning for the entire institution of *dina de-bar metsara*.

## Bar Metzra Basics

### Definition & Basis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא אִימְּלִיךְ בֵּיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵיזִיל אֶיזְבּוֹן? וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל זְבוֹן. צְרִיךְ לְמִיקְנֵא מִינֵּיהּ, אוֹ לָא? רָבִינָא אָמַר: לָא צְרִיךְ לְמִיקְנֵא מִינֵּיהּ, נְהַרְדָּעֵי אָמְרִי: צְרִיךְ לְמִיקְנֵא מִינֵּיהּ. וְהִלְכְתָא: צְרִיךְ לְמִיקְנֵא מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** With the above *halakhot* in mind, the Gemara asks: If the stranger **came to consult with** one of the owners of the fields, and **said to him:** Shall **I go** and **acquire** the field, **and** the latter **said to him, go** and **acquire** it, as I will raise no objection, is it **necessary to** perform an act of **acquisition with him** to solidify the agreement? **Or** perhaps his mere promise is sufficient and it is **not** necessary? **Ravina said:** It is **not necessary to** perform an act of **acquisition with him,** while the Sages **of Neharde'a say:** It is **necessary to** perform an act of **acquisition with him.** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is that it is **necessary to** perform an act of **acquisition with him.**

The *metsara* is literally the border mound that separates two fields. The *bar metsara* — the one who owns the adjacent field — has a privileged claim when the owner of the central field wishes to sell. The basis, as the *Nahardai* cited, is the verse *ve'asita hayashar vehatov*: the *chachamim* interpreted the obligation to do what is good and upright to include giving the neighboring landowner the right of first refusal when one's land comes up for sale. The rule operates with real teeth: if a landowner sells to a third party — a *lokeach* from elsewhere — without offering the land to his neighbor, the neighbor has the right to approach the *lokeach*, pay him what he paid, and take the field. The *lokeach*, in the framework by which this is explained, was acting as the agent of the neighbor when he purchased, which is how the neighbor gains the ability to simply hand over the money and assume ownership.

### Kicking Off

The mechanism by which the neighbor asserts his right is straightforward: he comes to the *lokeach*, pays the price that was paid, and the *lokeach* must leave. The neighbor does not need to go back to the original seller; he takes the land directly from the *lokeach* by reimbursing him.

### Kinyan Required

A question arises when the *lokeach* — before purchasing — approaches the neighboring *bar metsara* and asks whether it is acceptable to proceed. The neighbor says: go ahead and buy. Does this permission eliminate the neighbor's right, or must the neighbor still perform a formal *kinyan* to extinguish his claim?

Ravina holds that no *kinyan* is required — the permission is sufficient. The *Nahardai* hold that a *kinyan* is required. The *gemara* concludes: *ve-hilkheta tzarich lemiknei minei* — the *halacha* follows the *Nahardai*, a formal *kinyan* is needed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ צְרִיךְ לְמִיקְנֵא מִינֵּיהּ, אִי לָא קְנוֹ מִינֵּיהּ – אִיַּיקּוּר וְזוּל בִּרְשׁוּתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **Now that you have said** that it is **necessary to** perform an act of **acquisition with** the neighbor for the right to purchase the field, **if he did not** perform an act of **acquisition with him** and purchased the field, and the field **increased or decreased** in value, the price fluctuation occurs **in the domain of** the owner of the bordering field. The buyer's purchase is considered a purchase on behalf of the neighbor, who then reimburses the buyer.

Rashi explains the reasoning: without a *kinyan*, the neighbor can claim *mashti hayiti bach* — I was not being serious. The neighbor retains the right to say his permission was merely casual or even joking, not a legally operative waiver. By contrast, if the *lokeach* has already purchased and the neighbor subsequently signals acceptance without having been asked in advance, no such ambiguity applies — the purchase is done, and the neighbor's acquiescence is unambiguous. As the *Rambam* notes, if one purchased already and the neighbor then concedes, the neighbor's right is extinguished without need for a *kinyan*.

## Bar Metzra Pricing

### Below Market

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זְבַן בִּמְאָה וְשָׁוֵי מָאתַן, (חָזֵינָא) [חָזֵינַן]: אִי לְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא קָא מוֹזֵילא וּמְזַבְּנִין – יָהֵיב לֵיהּ מְאָה וְשָׁקֵיל לֵיהּ. וְאִי לָא – יָהֵיב לֵיהּ מָאתַן וְשָׁקֵיל לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Accordingly, if this buyer **bought** it **for one hundred** dinars **and** the field **was worth two hundred** dinars, in order to determine how much money the neighbor must give him, **we determine** why the owner sold the field to the buyer at this price: **If he sells to everyone** at that **cheap** price, the neighbor **gives** the buyer **one hundred** dinars **and takes it,** as the neighbor could have bought it for this sum himself. **But if** the owner does **not** sell to everyone at this price and this buyer was given a discount, the neighbor **gives** the buyer **two hundred** dinars, the market value of the field, **and takes it.**

Once we have established that a *kinyan* is required and that — if it has not been performed — the neighbor can assert his right, we turn to the question of price. If the *lokeach* purchased the field for a hundred *zuz* when it was worth two hundred, we first evaluate whether the below-market price was available to anyone. If the seller was offering the land at half price to the general public — perhaps he needed to leave quickly and was liquidating everything at a discount — then when the neighbor comes to assert his right and displace the *lokeach*, he pays only a hundred: *yehav lei me'ah ve-shakil lei*. He takes the land at the same discounted price the *lokeach* received.

### Above Market

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זְבַן בְּמָאתַן וְשָׁוְיָא מְאָה, סְבוּר מִינָּה, מָצֵי אָמַר לֵיהּ: לְתַקּוֹנֵי שַׁדַּרְתָּיךָ וְלָא לְעַוּוֹתֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר קַשִּׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: הָכִי אָמְרִי נְהַרְדָּעֵי מִשּׁוּם דְּרַב נַחְמָן: אֵין אוֹנָאָה לְקַרְקָעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** In the converse case, if **he bought** it **for two hundred** dinars **and** the field was **worth one hundred** dinars, the Sages **understood** that the neighbor **can say to** the buyer: **I sent you** to act **for my benefit, but not** to act **to my detriment.** Since the field will not remain in your possession, you are effectively my agent, and I am not prepared to pay more than its market value due to your mistake. **Mar the Elder, son of Rav Ḥisda, said to Rav Ashi: This** is what the Sages **of Neharde'a say in the name of Rav Naḥman: There is no exploitation with regard to real estate,** as land has no fixed value, and therefore it cannot be said that the buyer overpaid, and he is given whatever sum he spent.

The opposite case: the *lokeach* paid two hundred for a field worth only one hundred. Presumably the seller and the *lokeach* had a special relationship, and part of what the *lokeach* paid was effectively a gift in the guise of an inflated purchase price. When the neighbor now seeks to assert his right and displace the *lokeach*, he must pay two hundred — the full amount the *lokeach* actually paid — since the *lokeach* genuinely parted with that sum and cannot be left worse off.

### No Ona'ah

What is the principled basis for these rules? Mar Kashisha, son of Rav Chisda, cites the position of the *Nahardai* in the name of Rav Nachman: *ein ona'ah le-karka'ot* — the law of *ona'ah*, price fraud or overcharge, does not apply to real estate. Because real estate is not subject to *ona'ah*, the price paid — whatever it is — becomes the operative value of the transaction. The neighbor therefore pays whatever the *lokeach* actually paid, whether above or below the apparent market rate, since that is the legally recognized price of the land.

## Bar Metzra Exceptions

### Strip in Middle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זַבֵּין לֵיהּ גְּרִיוָא דְּאַרְעָא בְּמִיצְעָא נִכְסֵיהּ, חָזֵינַן אִי עִידִּית הִיא, אִי זִיבּוּרִית הִיא – זְבִינֵיהּ זְבִינֵי. וְאִי לָא, אִיעָרוֹמֵי קָא מַעֲרֵים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses a related case: If one **sold to** another **a *beit se'a* of land in the middle of his property** so that the buyer is surrounded on all sides by the seller's fields, **we see** what type of land it is: **Whether** the land **is superior-quality** land **or whether** it **is inferior-quality** land, **his sale** is a valid **sale,** as it is a distinctive piece of land. In that case, the seller's neighbors cannot object, as their fields do not actually border on this plot. **But if** this field is **not** of any distinct quality, he is certainly trying to **employ an artifice.** His plan is to then purchase another plot of land from this owner, one that does border on the field of a neighbor. By first buying the plot in the middle, he is trying to establish himself as a neighbor so that the other neighbors will not have the first right of purchase relative to him. Therefore, the neighbors may prevent him from buying the second plot of land.

Having established the general contours of *dina de-bar metsara*, the *gemara* turns to the many situations in which it does not apply. The first involves a strip of land sold from the interior of a field. If I sell a narrow strip located in the middle of my holdings — not along the outer edge — we examine whether that strip is distinct in quality from the surrounding land. If it is of particularly high quality (*idit*) or particularly poor quality (*ziburit*), then *zavina zavina* — the sale stands without issue, as the strip is genuinely separate in character from the rest of the field. But if the strip is identical in quality to the surrounding land, the sale is characterized as *ha'aramah* — a circumvention — and is not permitted. The reason: by purchasing an undifferentiated strip in the middle of someone's field, the buyer becomes a *bar metsara* with respect to the land on both sides, and could then use that newly acquired right of first refusal to systematically buy up the entire field in stages, bypassing the original neighbors who should have had priority. When the strip is qualitatively distinct, no such mechanism is available, and the sale is entirely legitimate.

### Gifts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתָּנָה לֵית בָּהּ מִשּׁוּם דִּינָא דְּבַר מִצְרָא. אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: אִי כְּתַב לֵיהּ אַחְרָיוּת – אִית בָּהּ מִשּׁוּם דִּינָא דְּבַר מִצְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss the *halakha* of one whose field borders that of his neighbor. With regard to **a gift, it is not subject to the *halakha* of one** whose field **borders** the field of his neighbor, as one can give a gift to whomever he chooses. **Ameimar said: If he wrote** a property **guarantee to** the recipient of the gift that if the field is seized for payment of a debt of the giver the giver of the gift will compensate the recipient for his loss, **it is subject to the *halakha* of one** whose field **borders** of the field of his neighbor. In that case the supposed gift has the appearance of a sale, so the neighbor can force the recipient to sell the plot to him.

A gift (*matanah*) to a third party is not subject to *dina de-bar metsara*. I may give my land to whomever I choose; the neighbor has no right of first refusal over a gift, because *dina de-bar metsara* is predicated on the framework of sale. However, if the "gift" comes with conditions or is accompanied by a contract that includes *achrayut* — provisions establishing certain ongoing obligations or guarantees about the property — Rashi notes that such a transaction is not truly a *matanah* and may not enjoy this exemption.

### All Properties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָכַר כׇּל נְכָסָיו לְאֶחָד – לֵית בַּהּ מִשּׁוּם דִּינָא דְּבַר מִצְרָא. לִבְעָלִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים – לֵית בַּהּ מִשּׁוּם דִּינָא דְּבַר מִצְרָא. זְבַן מִגּוֹי וְזַבֵּין לְגוֹי – לֵית בַּהּ מִשּׁוּם דִּינָא דְּבַר מִצְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** If a seller **sold all his property to a single** person, **this** sale **is not subject to the *halakha* of one** whose field **borders** the field of his neighbor, as the seller is not required to leave out one particular field if the buyer is acquiring all his property. Similarly, if the seller sold it back **to the previous owners, this** sale **is not subject to the *halakha* of one** whose field **borders** the field of his neighbor. If a buyer **bought** a field **from a gentile or** a seller **sold** a field **to a gentile, this** purchase or sale **is not subject to the *halakha* of one** whose field **borders** the field of his neighbor.

If I sell all of my properties at once to a single buyer — *machar kol nekhasav le-echad* — *dina de-bar metsara* does not apply, even if one of those properties happens to be adjacent to a neighbor who would otherwise have the right of first refusal.

Rashi explains: the properties may be located in different places — one in the east, one in the west. If a neighbor of one of them comes and objects, the *chachamim* did not institute *dina de-bar metsara* in a way that would require the *lokeach* to step aside and allow the neighbor to purchase just that one parcel, because doing so would harm the seller. The seller may be unable to sell the bundle without that piece, or he may be leaving and needs to liquidate everything together. The principle of *ve'asita hayashar vehatov* cuts both ways: we must do right by the seller as well as by the neighbor. If honoring the neighbor's right of first refusal causes a genuine loss to the seller — preventing him from completing a package sale — then the *takkanah* of *bar metsara* was never meant to reach that far. Even so, where it is genuinely possible to sell one parcel separately without harming the seller, it may still be preferable to offer the neighbor that opportunity. There is simply no *chiyuv* compelling it.

### Non-Jewish Party

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זְבַן מִגּוֹי – דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲרִי אַבְרַחִי לָךְ מִמִּצְרָךְ. זַבֵּין לְגוֹי – גּוֹי וַדַּאי לָאו בַּר ״וְעָשִׂיתָ הַיָּשָׁר וְהַטּוֹב״ הוּא. שַׁמּוֹתֵי וַדַּאי מְשַׁמְּתִינַן לֵיהּ, עַד דִּמְקַבֵּל עֲלֵיהּ כֹּל אוּנְסֵי דְּאָתֵי לֵיהּ מֵחֲמָתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies this ruling: If a buyer **bought** the field **from a gentile** it does not apply, **as he** can **say to** the neighbor: It is better for you that I bought the field, as **I have chased away a lion for you from the border;** since the neighbor certainly prefers having a Jewish neighbor to having a gentile neighbor. If a seller **sold** a field **to a gentile,** the **gentile is certainly not bound** by the command of: **"And you shall do that which is right and good in the eyes of the Lord"** (Deuteronomy 6:18). The gentile is therefore under no obligation to refrain from purchasing this land. Nevertheless, **we certainly excommunicate** the one who sold it to the gentile **until he accepts upon himself** responsibility for **all** damage resulting from **accidents that** might **befall** the neighbor **on** the gentile's **account.**

When either party to the transaction is a non-Jew, *dina de-bar metsara* does not apply — but for different reasons depending on the direction of the sale. If a Jew buys land from a non-Jew (*zavan me-akum*), the Jewish buyer can say to the original neighbor: *arya avrakhi lach mimitzerach* — I have driven a lion away from your border. The non-Jewish neighbor was no asset; the incoming Jewish owner is presumably preferable. If a Jew sells to a non-Jew (*zavin le-akum*), the basis for *dina de-bar metsara* is a positive obligation of the Torah — *ve'asita hayashar vehatov* — which does not apply to non-Jews. The non-Jewish buyer is simply not subject to the framework in which *ve'asita hayashar vehatov* operates.

Nevertheless, one who sells to a non-Jew is placed in a form of *cherem* — a communal sanction — until he accepts full responsibility for any harm that comes to his Jewish neighbor as a result of having a non-Jew installed in that adjacent property.

### Mortgaged Property

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַשְׁכַּנְתָּא לֵית בַּהּ מִשּׁוּם דִּינָא דְּבַר מִצְרָא. דְּאָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אֲמַרוּ לִי סָבֵי דְּמָתָא מַחְסֵיָא: מַאי ״מַשְׁכַּנְתָּא״ – דִּשְׁכוּנָה גַּבֵּיהּ. מַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ – לְדִינָא דְּבַר מִצְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: If he sold a field previously given as **a mortgage** to the one to whom it was mortgaged, **this** sale **is not subject to the *halakha* of one** whose field **borders** the field of his neighbor, **as Rav Ashi said: The elders of** the town of **Mata Meḥasya said to me: What** is the meaning of the word **mortgage [*mashkanta*]?** It means **that** it **resides [*shekhuna*] with** the one to whom it was mortgaged. The Gemara asks: **What difference does it make** what the word means? The Gemara answers: It is relevant **with regard to the *halakha* of one** whose field **borders** the field of his neighbor in that the person to whom the field is mortgaged has more rights than bordering neighbors, as he lays claim to a measure of ownership over the land.

Mortgaged property (*mashkanta*) is similarly exempt from *dina de-bar metsara*. Rav Ashi cites the elders of Mata Mechasya for a reason embedded in the very word: *mashkanta* derives from the same root as *shachen* — neighbor — because the mortgagee is treated as though he were already the neighbor. The practical implication for *dina de-bar metsara* is that the mortgagee has the status of a *bar metsara* himself.

### Distant or Bad Land

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִמְכּוֹר בְּרָחוֹק וְלִגְאוֹל בְּקָרוֹב, בְּרַע וְלִגְאוֹל בְּיָפֶה – לֵית בַּהּ מִשּׁוּם דִּינָא דְּבַר מִצְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** If one sought **to sell a distant** field **and to redeem,** i.e., purchase for himself, **a close** one, or if he sold **a bad** one **to redeem a good** one, **this** sale **is not subject to the *halakha* of one** whose field **borders** the field of his neighbor. Rather, he may sell his field whenever he has the opportunity.

Selling distant land in order to purchase closer land, or selling poor-quality land (*ziburit*) in order to purchase superior land (*idit*), is also exempt from *dina de-bar metsara*. Rashi explains: if the seller is compelled to sell because the land is far away and wishes to consolidate holdings closer to home, the *chachamim* did not institute *bar metsara* in a way that would cause the seller a loss. The entire basis of *dina de-bar metsara* is *ve'asita hayashar vehatov* — doing what is equitable. Equity requires that we not compel the seller to forgo a transaction that genuinely serves his interests. As Rashi formulates it: just as we must do right by the neighbor, so too we must do right by the seller. When the two conflict, neither consideration automatically overrides the other, and in this case the seller's need to improve or consolidate his holdings takes precedence.

### Necessary Costs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִכְרָגָא וְלִמְזוֹנֵי וְלִקְבוּרָה – לֵית בַּהּ מִשּׁוּם דִּינָא דְּבַר מִצְרָא. דְּאָמְרִי נְהַרְדְּעָאֵי: לִכְרָגָא, לִמְזוֹנֵי וְלִקְבוּרָה מְזַבְּנִינַן בְּלָא אַכְרַזְתָּא. לְאִשָּׁה וּלְיַתְמֵי וּלְשׁוּתָּפֵי – לֵית בַּהּ מִשּׁוּם דִּינָא דְּבַר מִצְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, if he sells his field to pay for necessities, such as **for taxes, for** his wife and daughters' **sustenance, or for** the **burial** of one of his family members, **this** sale **is not subject to the *halakha* of one** whose field **borders** the field of his neighbor. This is **because** the Sages of **Neharde'a said: For taxes, for sustenance, and for burial we sell** a field **without a proclamation,** as such matters are pressing and urgent and should not be delayed out of consideration for the rights of a bordering neighbor. Similarly, if he sold the field **to a woman,** who does not usually chase after vendors, **or to orphans, or to** his **partners, this** sale **is not subject to the *halakha* of one** whose field **borders** the field of his neighbor.

Three categories of financial necessity also eliminate the right of first refusal: *l'karga*, *le-mezonei*, *u-likvura* — to pay the head tax, to provide for those one is obligated to support, and to cover burial costs.

These three categories each invoke a different dimension of obligation. *Karga* — the poll tax owed to the ruling authorities — is a personal financial obligation imposed by the external political order. *Mezonei* — food and support for dependents — derives from contractual and rabbinic obligations, falling within the domain of family law. *Kvura* — the burial of the dead — invokes a religious and ritual obligation that operates on a more fundamental level, one that all are bound by to some degree.

The source for all three is the *Nahardai*, who state: for *karga*, *mezonei*, and *kvura* we sell without making a public announcement (*achrazta*). The normal requirement to publicize an impending sale — which gives the neighbor the opportunity to exercise the right of first refusal — is suspended when one of these three pressing needs is the occasion for the sale.

### Woman & Orphans

Woman and orphans are also exempt from *dina de-bar metsara*, though again the reasons differ. Rashi and the *Rambam* both explain the exemption for a woman by noting that in the social context in which these *takkanot* were formulated, it was not customary (*orach ar'a*) for a woman to venture out and negotiate for the best available land. The presumption is that a woman would simply purchase the first suitable land she found; requiring her to yield to a neighbor's right of first refusal would be unreasonably burdensome in her circumstances. The exemption for orphans rests on a simple principle of *chesed* — it would simply not be kind to deprive orphans of the opportunity to acquire property by requiring them to step aside for a neighbor.

## Priority Rules

### Hierarchy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁכֵינֵי הָעִיר וּשְׁכֵינֵי שָׂדֶה – שְׁכֵינֵי הָעִיר קוֹדְמִין. שָׁכֵן וְתַלְמִיד חָכָם – תַּלְמִיד חָכָם קוֹדֵם. קָרוֹב וְתַלְמִיד חָכָם – תַּלְמִיד חָכָם קוֹדֵם.
>
> **Translation:** If various individuals have equal rights to the field, such as both are bordering neighbors, but some of them are **neighbors** whose fields are adjacent to his on the side **of the city,** i.e., their fields are between the city and the field being sold; **and** others are **neighbors** whose fields are adjacent to his on the side **of the field,** i.e., their fields are between the field being sold and the area further from the city, the **neighbors** whose fields are adjacent to his on the side **of the city** receive **precedence.** If one is a regular **neighbor and** the other is **a Torah scholar, the Torah scholar** receives **precedence.** If one is **a relative and** the other is **a Torah scholar,** here too, **the Torah scholar** receives **precedence.**

Stepping back from the specific rules of *bar metsara*, the *gemara* addresses the broader question of priority among competing claimants. A resident of the same city as the seller has priority over someone who lives in a more distant field: *shechina de-mata kodem*. Between a neighbor and a *talmid chacham*, the *talmid chacham* takes priority. Between a relative and a *talmid chacham*, the *talmid chacham* again takes priority.

### Neighbor vs. Relative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: שָׁכֵן וְקָרוֹב מַאי? תָּא שְׁמַע: ״טוֹב שָׁכֵן קָרוֹב מֵאָח רָחוֹק״.
>
> **Translation:** A dilemma was raised before the Sages: With regard to **a neighbor and a relative, what** is the *halakha*? Which of them takes precedence? The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** an answer from the following verse: **"Better a neighbor who is near than a brother who is far"** (Proverbs 27:10).

The question then arises: what about a neighbor versus a relative, when no *talmid chacham* is involved? The *gemara* brings a verse from Mishlei: *tov shachen karov me-ach rachok* — a near neighbor is better than a distant brother. The neighbor takes priority over the relative.

### Payment Terms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי זוּזֵי טָבֵי וְהָנֵי זוּזֵי תְּקוּלֵי – לֵית בֵּיהּ מִשּׁוּם דִּינָא דְּבַר מִצְרָא. הָנֵי צַיְירִי וְהָנֵי שְׁרוּ – לֵית בֵּיהּ מִשּׁוּם דִּינָא דְּבַר מִצְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** If two people sought to acquire a field, and **these** coins that the first produces for payment are **good dinars, and those** coins that the second uses are **weighed dinars,** which are preferable to the good dinars, **this** sale **is not subject to the *halakha* of one** whose field **borders** the field of his neighbor, as the owner can say he prefers the superior quality coins. If **these** coins were **wrapped up and those** were **loose, this** sale **is not subject to the *halakha* of one** whose field **borders** on the field his neighbor, as he may sell his field to the one whose money is ready to be counted.

The quality and type of currency offered can also create differentiation among competing claimants. If one person offers coins of superior quality (*tavei*) and another offers heavier or more cumbersome coins (*tekulei*), there is no issue of *dina de-bar metsara* — the seller may accept the better payment and is not compelled to defer to the neighbor. Similarly, if one person offers minted coins and another offers unminted metal, the distinction in payment form can override the neighbor's priority. These distinctions are likely of limited practical relevance today, where currency is standardized, though one might imagine analogous issues arising with payment in different currencies or non-standard instruments.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: אֵיזִיל וְאֶטְרַח וְאַיְיתֵי זוּזֵי – לָא נָטְרִינַן לֵיהּ. אָמַר: אֵיזִיל אַיְיתֵי זוּזֵי, חָזֵינַן: אִי גַּבְרָא דַּאֲמִיד הוּא דְּאָזֵיל וּמַיְיתֵי זוּזֵי – נָטְרִינַן לֵיהּ, וְאִי לָא – לָא נָטְרִינַן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** If the neighbor **said: I will go and expend effort and bring money, we do not wait for him,** despite his status as a bordering neighbor, if someone else is available who is prepared to pay immediately. If **he said: I will go bring money, we see** what his financial status is: **If he is a person who is assessed** as one **who can go and bring money** without delay, **we wait for him, but if not, we do not wait for him.**

The *bar metsara* also has no right to request a waiting period in which to gather funds. If the neighbor says *izil ve-aitei zuzei* — let me go and bring the money — we do not wait for him: *lo natrinan lei*. The right of first refusal means the right to purchase *now*, not the right to hold the sale open while one arranges financing. This is not merely a practical constraint; it reflects the structure of the *takkanah* itself. The obligation of *ve'asita hayashar vehatov* was never intended to prevent the seller from completing a timely transaction. Requiring the seller to wait imposes a loss on him, and *bar metsara* was instituted precisely in cases where doing right by the neighbor causes no loss to the seller.

### Capability Test

The *gemara* does, however, recognize a middle case. If the neighbor says not merely that he wants to find money, but specifically *izil, ve-tarach, ve-aitei zuzei* — I am going to go, make an effort, and bring the money — then we look at the circumstances: *chazinan*. If this is a person who is genuinely likely to be able to produce the funds — *gavra de-amid hu* — we wait for him. If he is not such a person, we do not wait. The determination is practical: is this a realistic claim or a stalling tactic? The answer guides whether a brief waiting period is granted.

## Buildings & Trees

### Building on Land

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַרְעָא דְּחַד וּבָתֵּי דְּחַד – מָרֵי אַרְעָא מְעַכֵּב אַמָּרֵי בָּתֵּי, מָרֵי בָּתֵּי לָא מְעַכֵּב אַמָּרֵי דְּאַרְעָא. אַרְעָא דְּחַד וְדִיקְלֵי דְּחַד – מָרֵי דְּאַרְעָא מָצֵי מְעַכֵּב אַמָּרֵי דִּקְלֵי, מָרֵי דִּיקְלֵי לָא מָצֵי מְעַכֵּב אַמָּרֵי דְּאַרְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** If the **land** belonged **to one** person **and the houses** on the land belonged **to** another **one, the owner of the land prevents the owner of the houses** from selling his houses to someone else, as he has the first right of purchase. By contrast, **the owner of the houses does not prevent the owner of the land** from selling his land, as one can change his place of residence with relative ease, so he is not considered tied to the land. Similarly, if the **land** belonged **to one and** its **palm trees to** another **one, the owner of the land can prevent the owner of the palm trees** from selling the trees to another, but **the owner of the palm trees cannot prevent the owner of the land** from selling his land to another.

A distinct set of issues concerns the relationship between ownership of land and ownership of structures or plants sitting on that land. It was evidently a common situation in the Talmudic world for buildings to stand on land not owned by the building's occupants. When such a property comes up for sale, the owner of the land can prevent the owner of the building from selling the building to a third party — the landowner has a form of *bar metsara* interest in the building, since ideally his building should sit on his own land. But the owner of the building cannot prevent the landowner from selling the land; the building owner is not the landowner's *bar metsara* and has no recognized interest that overrides the landowner's freedom to convey his property.

### Trees on Land

The same principle applies to trees. The landowner can prevent the tree owner from selling the trees on his land to another party, but the tree owner cannot prevent the landowner from selling the land itself.

### Residential Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַרְעָא לְבָתֵּי וְאַרְעָא לְזַרְעָא – יִשּׁוּב עֲדִיף, וְלֵית בַּהּ מִשּׁוּם דִּינָא דְּבַר מִצְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** If two people wanted to purchase the land, but one desired the **land for** building **houses and** the other wished to purchase **the land for planting, the settling** of the land through construction of houses is **preferable, and this** sale **is not subject to the *halakha* of one** whose field **borders** the field of his neighbor. Therefore, he may sell to the one who wants to build a house there, even if he is not a bordering neighbor and the other potential buyer is.

Finally, if a seller has a choice between conveying land to a buyer who will use it for residential development and one who will use it for agriculture, the residential use takes priority: *yishuv adif*. Furthermore, there is no issue of *dina de-bar metsara* in such a case. Rashi explains: *yishuv de-vattei adif le-didin* — residential settlement is of greater benefit. It is not simply a matter of one good being preferred over another comparable good; it is rather that providing homes for people is a greater contribution to the settlement of the land (*yishuv ha'aretz*) than additional cultivation. When the two goods are genuinely unequal in this way, the framework of *ve'asita hayashar vehatov* does not compel giving the neighbor right of first refusal over the superior outcome.

## Physical Separation

### Natural Fence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַפְסֵיק מְשׁוּנִּיתָא אוֹ רִיכְבָּא דְּדִיקְלָא, (חָזֵינָא) [חָזֵינַן]: אִם יָכוֹל לְהַכְנִיס בָּהּ אֲפִילּוּ תֶּלֶם אֶחָד – אִית בַּהּ מִשּׁוּם דִּינָא דְּבַר מִצְרָא, וְאִי לָא – לֵית בַּהּ מִשּׁוּם דִּינָא דְּבַר מִצְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** If **a jagged edge of rock or a row of palm trees served as a barrier** between two bordering fields, **we see** whether any open space exists. **If** the owner of the adjacent field **can insert even a single furrow there** that comes into contact with the other field, **this** sale **is subject to the *halakha* of one** whose field **borders** the field of his neighbor. **But if** sufficient space for a furrow does **not** exist, **this** sale **is not subject to the *halakha* of one** whose field **borders** the field of his neighbor.

With the foundational rules of *dina de-bar metsara* established, the *gemara* closes by addressing when a person qualifies as a *bar metsara* at all. The question arises when there is a physical barrier — a *piska mishnita* (a row of stones) or a *richba de-dikla* (a row of palm trees) — separating the neighbor's field from the field being sold. Does the neighbor still qualify as a *bar metsara*?

The answer depends on whether the barrier can be penetrated for even a single furrow of planting: *chazinan i yachil le-hachnis bah afilu telem echad*. If the neighbor can plant even one row through or past the barrier — establishing that his field is in practical agricultural continuity with the adjacent field — then *dina de-bar metsara* applies. The justification for *bar metsara* is precisely that the neighbor wishes to extend his rows of planting into the adjacent field; if that extension is even minimally possible despite the barrier, his priority right survives. If it is not possible, the barrier creates a genuine agricultural discontinuity, and the neighbor has no right of first refusal.

### Multiple Neighbors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי אַרְבָּעָה בְּנֵי מִצְרָנֵי, דְּקָדֵים חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ וְזָבֵין – זְבִינֵיהּ זְבִינֵי. וְאִי כּוּלְּהוּ אָתוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי – פָּלְגוּ לַהּ בְּקַרְנְזִיל.
>
> **Translation:** In a case of **those four bordering neighbors** who surround a field that is for sale from all four sides, if **one of them preceded** the others **and purchased** it, **his purchase** is a valid **purchase** and the others cannot object. **And if they all came simultaneously** to purchase it, then **they divide** the plot of land, **with** two bisecting **diagonal** lines so that each receives a portion near his field.

A final case: four neighbors, one on each side of a field being sold. If one of the four arrives first and purchases — *kadim chad minaihu ve-zaven* — the sale stands and he has prevailed. Priority goes to the first to act. If all four arrive simultaneously — *i kulhu atu ba-hadi hadadi* — the field is divided among them diagonally, *palgu lah be-karneizil*, yielding four triangular portions, one for each neighbor. Rashi provides a small diagram in the margin illustrating the diagonal division, which appears in most standard editions of the *gemara*.

With that, the *gemara*'s treatment of *dina de-bar metsara* concludes.
