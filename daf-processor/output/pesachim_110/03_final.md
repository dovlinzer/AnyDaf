# Pesachim 110 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Arba Kosot & Pairs

### Four Cups Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא יִפְחֲתוּ לוֹ מֵאַרְבָּעָה. הֵיכִי מְתַקְּנִי רַבָּנַן מִידֵּי דְּאָתֵי בֵּהּ לִידֵי סַכָּנָה, וְהָתַנְיָא: לֹא יֹאכַל אָדָם תְּרֵי, וְלֹא יִשְׁתֶּה תְּרֵי, וְלֹא יְקַנַּח תְּרֵי, וְלֹא יַעֲשֶׂה צְרָכָיו תְּרֵי?
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that even with regard to the poorest of Jews, the charity distributors should **not give him less than four cups** of wine. The Gemara asks: **How could the Sages establish a matter through which one will come to expose himself to danger? But wasn't it taught in a *baraita*: A person should not eat pairs, i.e., an even number of food items; and he should not drink pairs of cups; and he should not wipe himself with pairs; and he should not attend to his sexual needs in pairs.** The concern was that one who uses pairs exposes himself to sorcery or demons. Why would the Sages require one to drink an even number of cups and thereby place himself in a position of danger?

Today's *daf* is 110, and we pick up at the very top of 110a. The Gemara is discussing the *arba kosot* and is troubled by the fact that four is an even number — doing things in pairs presumably exposes one to *mazikin*, as we will see, and potentially to the dangers of witchcraft as well.

### Three Solutions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, אָמַר קְרָא: ״לֵיל שִׁמּוּרִים״ — לַיִל הַמְשׁוּמָּר וּבָא מִן הַמַּזִּיקִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said that the verse said: "It was a night of watching to the Lord" (Exodus 12:42), which indicates that Passover night is a night that remains guarded from** demons and **harmful spirits** of all kinds. Therefore, there is no cause for concern about this form of danger on this particular night.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: כּוֹס שֶׁל בְּרָכָה מִצְטָרֵף לְטוֹבָה, וְאֵינוֹ מִצְטָרֵף לְרָעָה. רָבִינָא אָמַר: אַרְבָּעָה כָּסֵי תַּקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן דֶּרֶךְ חֵירוּת, כׇּל חַד וְחַד מִצְוָה בְּאַפֵּי נַפְשַׁהּ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said a different answer: The cup of blessing** for Grace after Meals on Passover night is used in the performance of an additional mitzva and is not simply an expression of freedom. Therefore, **it combines** with the other cups **for the good, i.e., to fulfill the mitzva to drink four cups, and it does not combine for the bad.** With regard to the danger of drinking pairs of cups, it is as though one drinks only three cups. **Ravina said: The Sages instituted four separate cups, each of which is consumed in a manner that demonstrates freedom. Each and every one is a distinct mitzva** in its own right. In other words, each cup is treated separately and one is not considered to be drinking in pairs.

The Gemara offers several answers. One is that Passover night is *leil shimurim* — a night of divine protection — so the concern simply does not apply. A second answer is that each of the four cups is an independent *mitzvah* and need not be viewed as comprising pairs. A third answer, which will echo throughout today's *daf*, is that one of the four cups is the *kos shel birkat hamazon*, and a *kos shel bracha* is not *mitztaref l'ra'ah* — it does not combine to create harm. It therefore does not count toward an even number, effectively reducing four to three. It is worth noting that the other cups are also associated with *mitzvot*, yet only the *kos shel birkat hamazon* receives this treatment. Today's *daf* is devoted entirely to the concern of doing things in pairs and the demonic dangers such behavior may invite.

## Baraisa: No Pairs

### Eating/Drinking/Sex

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לֹא יַעֲשֶׂה צְרָכָיו תְּרֵי״, אַמַּאי? נִמְלָךְ הוּא! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: לֹא יֹאכַל תְּרֵי וְיִשְׁתֶּה תְּרֵי, וְלֹא יַעֲשֶׂה צְרָכָיו אֲפִילּוּ פַּעַם אַחַת — דִּילְמָא חָלֵישׁ וּמִיתְּרַע.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* taught that **one should not attend to his sexual needs in pairs. The Gemara asks: Why should one be concerned for this; he has changed his mind?** One does not plan in advance to engage in marital relations twice, and therefore the two acts should not combine to form a dangerous pair. **Abaye said: This is what the *tanna* is saying:** i.e., the *baraita* should be understood in the following manner: **One should not eat in pairs nor drink in pairs, and if he does so he should not attend to his sexual needs** right afterward **even once, lest he is weakened** by the act **and will be harmed** for having eaten or drunk in pairs.

Finishing the *Baraisa* cited on the previous *daf*: one should not eat in pairs, drink in pairs, wipe oneself in the bathroom twice, or engage in sexual relations twice in a single night. The Gemara picks up with the last prohibition — *lo ya'aseh tzorcho trei*, one should not attend to one's needs twice — and asks why this should be prohibited. It answers: *nimlach hu* — the second act is a fresh decision. Having once concluded the act, the person later resolves anew, making the second instance an independent act rather than part of a pair, analogous to the argument made earlier regarding each cup of wine being a distinct act.

### Abaye: Health Issue

*Amar Abaye*: *hachi ka'amar* — the *Baraisa* is actually saying something entirely different. Do not eat twice and drink twice, and furthermore do not engage in sexual relations even once after eating and drinking, lest one become weakened and expose oneself to harm. According to Abaye, the *Baraisa* is not about pairs at all but is rather a piece of health advice, cautioning against certain combinations of physical activity — much like the common warning against vigorous exertion immediately after eating.

## Wine & Danger

### Two Cups Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שׁוֹתֶה כִּפְלַיִם — דָּמוֹ בְּרֹאשׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught in another *baraita*: If one drinks in pairs his blood is upon his head,** i.e., he bears responsibility for his own demise.

*Tanu rabanan*: one who drinks double — *shoteh keflayim* — has his blood upon his own head. He is responsible for whatever befalls him, having voluntarily exposed himself to danger.

### Rav Yehuda: Outdoors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: אֵימָתַי — בִּזְמַן שֶׁלֹּא רָאָה פְּנֵי הַשּׁוּק, אֲבָל רָאָה פְּנֵי הַשּׁוּק — הָרְשׁוּת בְּיָדוֹ. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: חֲזֵינָא לֵיהּ לְרַב חֲנַנְיָא בַּר בִּיבִי, דְּאַכֹּל כָּסָא הֲוָה נָפֵיק וְחָזֵי אַפֵּי שׁוּקָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said: When is that the case? When one did not leave the house and view the marketplace** between cups. **However, if he saw the marketplace** after the first cup, **he has permission** to drink another cup without concern. Likewise, **Rav Ashi said: I saw Rav Ḥananya bar Beivai** follow this policy: **Upon drinking each cup, he would leave the house and view the marketplace.**

*Amar Rav Yehuda*: this rule applies only when the person did not go outdoors between the two cups — *bizman shelo ra pnei shuk*. But if he went out and saw the marketplace — *ra pnei hashuk* — then it is permitted, since going outside makes each glass a sufficiently independent act that the pair is dissolved. *Amar Rav Ashi*: I observed Rav Hananya bar Abibi who, on every glass of wine, *havei nafik v'chazei apei shuka* — would step outside and see the marketplace. One might wonder whether any outdoor exposure suffices or whether a genuine excursion is required, but in any case Rav Hananya bar Abibi's practice was to interrupt each cup with a walk outside, after which he could drink any number of glasses he wished.

### Travel/Sleep Danger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא לָצֵאת לַדֶּרֶךְ, אֲבָל בְּבֵיתוֹ — לָא. אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: וּלְיָשֵׁן — כְּלָצֵאת לַדֶּרֶךְ דָּמֵי. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: וְלָצֵאת לְבֵית הַכִּסֵּא — כְּלָצֵאת לַדֶּרֶךְ דָּמֵי. וּבְבֵיתוֹ לָא? וְהָא רָבָא מָנֵי כְּשׁוּרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And we said** that there is concern for the safety of one who drinks in pairs **only when he intends to set out on the road** after drinking, **but if he intends to remain in his home** there is **no** need for concern. **Rabbi Zeira said: And one who plans to sleep is comparable to** one who is **setting out on the road. He should be concerned that he might be harmed. Rav Pappa said: And going to the bathroom is comparable to setting out on the road. The Gemara asks: And if one intends to remain in his home, is there no cause for concern? But Rava would count the beams** of the house to keep track of the number of cups he had drunk so as to ensure that he would not consume an even number.

The Gemara now qualifies further: *v'lo amrin ela l'atzis laderech* — this entire concern applies only when, after drinking in pairs, one is about to travel. *Aval beveisa lo* — if one is simply staying home, it is not a problem. The Gemara's reasoning reflects the broader concern that travel exposes one to *mazikin* and other harmful forces, making one more vulnerable when one has already drunk in pairs — a kind of spiritual equivalent of drinking and driving.

*Amar Rav Zeira*: going to sleep is as dangerous as setting out on a journey — *v'lemishan k'latzis laderech dami*. This is striking because one might naturally say that sleeping it off is the safe option, but the Gemara reasons that during sleep the soul departs and one is therefore more exposed to malevolent forces.

*Amar Rav Papa*: similarly, having to leave the house to go to the outhouse — a predictable consequence of having drunk a good deal — is also treated as dangerous as traveling. In effect, one must remain entirely indoors after drinking in pairs.

### Rava Counts Beams

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַבָּיֵי, כִּי שָׁתֵי חַד כָּסָא, מְנַקֵּיט לֵיהּ אִימֵּיהּ תְּרֵי כָסֵי בִּתְרֵי יְדֵיהּ. וְרַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, כִּי הֲוָה שָׁתֵי תְּרֵי כָסֵי, מְנַקֵּיט לֵיהּ שַׁמָּעֵיהּ חַד כָּסָא, חַד כָּסָא — מְנַקֵּיט לֵיהּ תְּרֵי כָסֵי בִּתְרֵי יְדֵיהּ! אָדָם חָשׁוּב שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And likewise Abaye, when he would drink one cup, his mother would** immediately **place two cups in his two hands** so that he would not inadvertently drink only one more cup and thereby expose himself to the danger of drinking in pairs. **And similarly, when Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak would drink two cups, his attendant would** immediately **place one more cup in his hand,** and if he would drink **one cup,** the attendant would **place two cups in his two hands. These reports indicate that one should be concerned for his safety after drinking an even number of cups, even when he remains at home. The Gemara answers: An important person is different.** The demons focus their attention on him, and he must therefore be more careful than the average person.

This prompts the Gemara to ask: surely even at home there is some concern? And indeed, we see that there was. When Rava would drink at home, *manei kisurei* — he would count the ceiling beams in order to track whether he was drinking an even or odd number of cups. He developed a system by which he would note which beam he was looking at when he was on an odd number versus an even number, so that he always knew whether he needed to drink another cup.

### Abaye's Mother

When Abaye would be drinking, his nursemaid — the woman who raised him, since both his parents died at or before his birth, and whom the Gemara refers to as his mother — would proactively bring him two cups at a time in each hand, *terei kasei bitrei yadei*. If he had one cup and asked for another, she would have two ready to ensure he would not end on an even number. This practice of Abaye's nursemaid raises an interesting observation from a student: Shabbos candles are often arrayed in five, never four. This may reflect the same sensibility — although the Gemara itself runs in the opposite direction, treating lower numbers as more of a problem and higher multiples as progressively less concerning.

The Gemara then introduces the concept of *adam chashuv shani* — an important person is different. The *Rashbam* explains: *mazikin* are more invested in harming a person of stature than an ordinary individual, and therefore even at home an *adam chashuv* must take extra care. This is a distinctive use of the concept, which ordinarily concerns the heightened public scrutiny to which prominent people are subject. Here, however, the elevated standard applies in private, because the *mazikin* themselves treat such individuals as more valuable targets.

## Thresholds Two-Ten

### Ulla: Ten Safe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: עֲשָׂרָה כּוֹסוֹת אֵין בָּהֶם מִשּׁוּם זוּגוֹת. עוּלָּא לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר עוּלָּא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: עֲשָׂרָה כּוֹסוֹת תִּיקְּנוּ חֲכָמִים בְּבֵית הָאֵבֶל, וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ עֲשָׂרָה כּוֹסוֹת יֵשׁ בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם זוּגוֹת, הֵיכִי קָיְימִי רַבָּנַן וְתַקִּינוּ מִילְּתָא דְּאָתֵי לִידֵי סַכָּנָה?! אֲבָל תְּמָנְיָא יֵשׁ בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם זוּגוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said: Ten cups contain no element of the danger associated with pairs. Ulla** rules here **in accordance with his reasoning** stated elsewhere, **as Ulla said, and some say it was taught in a *baraita*: The Sages instituted** that one must drink **ten cups in the house of a mourner** during the meal of comfort. **And if it could enter your mind that ten cups do contain the element of danger associated with pairs, how could the Sages arise and institute something that might bring a person to a state of danger? However, eight cups do contain the element of danger associated with pairs.**

*Amar Ulla*: *asara kosos ein bahem mishum zugos* — ten cups do not fall under the concern of pairs. Once numbers grow large enough, we no longer perceive them as doubles. If one is going to drink a great deal, simply drinking all the way to ten puts one in the clear.

### Beis Avel Takana

Ulla's reasoning flows from his own ruling elsewhere: *asara kosos tiknu chachamim b'veis ha'avel* — the Rabbis instituted a practice of drinking ten cups of wine in a house of mourning, linked to the meal and the special *brachot* associated with comforting mourners. Since the Sages would not institute a practice that exposes people to danger, ten must clearly be a safe number. Tosafos notes that an alternative argument was available — the *kos shel birkat hamazon* could reduce the effective count by one — but this is Ulla's own approach.

One might ask how it is that we no longer observe this practice of ten cups in the *beis avel*, with its associated *brachot*. This is a significant question. Liturgy does evolve — practices are added and others fall away — and one suspects that as the ritual of ten cups became socially impractical in certain communities it was gradually abandoned. Yet it remains striking that even the accompanying *brachot* disappeared. When we later study *Ketubot* or *Berachot*, where these issues arise directly, the question deserves further attention.

The Gemara reasons: *v'isalka da'atach asara kosi yesh bahem mishum zugos, heichi kaimei rabanan v'tiknu milta d'ika sakana* — if ten were a problem of pairs, how could the Rabbis have instituted a practice that would expose mourners to danger?

### Ulla: Eight-Four

However, according to Ulla, eight is still problematic — *aval tamanya yesh bahem mishum zugos*.

### Shalom: Seven Safe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא וְרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: ״שָׁלוֹם״, לְטוֹבָה — מִצְטָרֵף, לְרָעָה — לֹא מִצְטָרֵף. אֲבָל שִׁיתָּא יֵשׁ בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם זוּגוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna both say that eight is also safe from the dangers of pairs, as the number seven, represented by the word ***shalom*, combines** with the previous cups **for the good** but **does not combine for the bad.** The final verse of the priestly benediction reads: "The Lord lift His countenance upon you and give you peace [*shalom*]" (Numbers 6:26). The word *shalom*, the seventh Hebrew word in this verse, has a purely positive connotation. Rav Ḥisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna therefore maintain that the seventh cup combines with the previous six only for good purposes. After the seventh cup, i.e., from the eighth cup and on, the cups constitute pairs for the good but not for the bad. **However, six cups do contain the element of danger associated with pairs.**

*Rav Chisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna* both say: *shalom letovah mitztaref, lera lo mitztaref* — *shalom* combines for good but not for bad. The reference is to *Birchas Kohanim*: *yevarechecha Hashem veyishmerecha, ya'er Hashem panav eleicha veyechuneka, yisa Hashem panav eleicha veyasem lecha shalom*. The word *shalom* is the seventh word, meaning that the seventh cup is the cup of *shalom*. Since *shalom* combines for good but not for bad, cup number seven will always land one at an odd number — it counts toward beneficial combinations but never creates a harmful even number. Therefore, from seven onward one is safe.

### Veyechuneka: Five

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: ״וִיחֻנֶּךָּ״, לְטוֹבָה — מִצְטָרֵף. לְרָעָה — לֹא מִצְטָרֵף. אֲבָל אַרְבָּעָה יֵשׁ בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם זוּגוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba and Rav Yosef both say that even drinking six cups is not dangerous. The reason is that the fifth cup, represented by the word ***viḥuneka*** in the second verse of the priestly benediction: "The Lord make His face to shine upon you, and be gracious to you [*viḥuneka*]" (Numbers 6:25), combines** with the previous cups **for the good** but **does not combine for the bad. However, four cups do contain the element of danger associated with pairs.**

*Rabba and Rav Yosef* both say: *veyechuneka letovah mitztaref, lera lo mitztaref*. The middle blessing of *Birchas Kohanim* — *ya'er Hashem panav eleicha veyechuneka* — ends with the word *veyechuneka*, which is the fifth word of that blessing. Cup number five therefore has the same protective property: it counts for good but never for bad, making five and anything beyond it safe.

### Veyishmerecha: Three

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: ״וְיִשְׁמְרֶךָ״, לְטוֹבָה — מִצְטָרֵף, לְרָעָה — לֹא מִצְטָרֵף.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye and Rava both say that even the number four is not dangerous, as ***veyishmerekha*,** the third word in the first verse of the priestly benediction, reads: "The Lord bless you and keep you [*veyishmerekha*]" (Numbers 6:24). **It combines for the good** but **does not combine for the bad.**

*Abaye and Rava* both say: *veyishmerecha letovah mitztaref, lera lo mitztaref*. The first blessing — *yevarechecha Hashem veyishmerecha* — ends with *veyishmerecha*, which falls at position three. Cup number three is thus protected. By the same logic, once one has had three cups, any subsequent cup can always be reckoned against the anchor of three, meaning one will always be considered at an odd number. The practical upshot is that only two cups remain genuinely problematic.

### Historical Lessening

The *Rashbam* makes an important observation here, noting explicitly that *kemoshehayu hadorot zo achar zo hayu mekilin bizugot v'holchin u'pochasin* — as the generations succeeded one another, the concern about *zugot* was progressively relaxed. The entire exercise of invoking *Birchas Kohanim* — arguing that eight is fine because of *shalom*, that six is fine because of *veyechuneka*, that four is fine because of *veyishmerecha* — is itself a historical process of downplaying the concern, successively narrowing the danger zone until almost nothing remains. The Rashbam reads this as a deliberate trajectory rather than simply a series of independent rulings.

It should also be noted that this heightened concern with *zugot* is characteristically a *Bavli* phenomenon and is not found in the *Yerushalmi*. Scholars have pointed to the influence of the surrounding Zoroastrian cultural environment in Babylonia on precisely this sort of demonological concern — the preoccupation with even numbers, dangerous forces, and protective incantations. It is a *Bavli* concern, and one that diminishes over time even within the Babylonian tradition itself.

### Only Two Problem

According to Abaye and Rava, only two cups remain a genuine problem. This point connects back to Abaye and Rava themselves: they were the very Amoraim who took such elaborate precautions as counting beams and receiving pre-poured extra cups from a nursemaid. Even while dramatically reducing the theoretical scope of the *zugot* concern, they still considered two cups a real danger — though perhaps the additional considerations of *adam chashuv* applied to them specifically.

## Rava's Practice

### Rava Four Cups

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַזְדָּא רָבָא לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּרָבָא אַפְּקִינְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן בְּאַרְבָּעָה כּוֹסוֹת, אַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיתַּזַּק רָבָא בַּר לֵיוַאי, לָא חַשׁ לַהּ לְמִילְּתָא, דְּאָמַר: הָהוּא מִשּׁוּם דְּאוֹתְבַן בְּפִירְקָא הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava follows his standard line of reasoning** in this regard, **as Rava** allowed **the Sages to leave** after having drunk **four cups** and was not concerned for their safety. **Although Rava bar Livai was injured on one such occasion, Rava was not concerned that the matter** had been caused by his consumption of an even number of cups, **as he said: That** injury occurred **because** Rava bar Livai **challenged me during the public lecture.** It is improper for a student to raise difficulties against his rabbi during a public lecture, lest the rabbi be embarrassed by his inability to answer.

*V'azda Rava l'taameih* — and Rava acted in keeping with his own reasoning. Before his rabbinic students would leave his house to return home for *Shabbos* or other occasions, Rava would see them off with a drink, and sometimes they would consume four cups of wine together before departing. This is consistent with Rava's view that more than two is not a problem. *Af al gav d'itzik Rava bar Livai* — notwithstanding the fact that Rava bar Livai was once harmed after leaving Rava's house following four cups of wine.

### Confirmation Bias

How does Rava deal with this apparent counterevidence? *Lo chash lah lemilsa* — he was not concerned about it. *De-amar, ahu mishum de-osfin be-pirka havei* — that, says Rava, happened because Rava bar Livai was always challenging and pestering him during his lectures. The harm came from that behavior, not from the four cups of wine. The *Rashbam* specifies that he had publicly embarrassed Rava.

This passage is genuinely fascinating from an epistemological standpoint. If one already believes in *mazikin* and in the danger of paired cups, then the one occasion when someone drinks in pairs and is harmed seems to confirm the belief powerfully, while the many occasions when nothing happens are quietly set aside. Conversely, if one believes that more than two is not a problem, then the one case of harm is readily attributed to some other cause — in this case, the student's impudence in the lecture hall. The evidence that supports one's thesis commands attention; the rest is discarded. The Gemara here illustrates, with unusual clarity, how prior assumptions shape the interpretation of observable outcomes.

## Demons & Witches (II)

### Ashmedai King

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, אָמַר לִי יוֹסֵף שֵׁידָא: אַשְׁמְדַאי מַלְכָּא דְשֵׁידֵי — מְמוּנֶּה הוּא אַכּוּלְּהוּ זוּגֵי, וּמַלְכָּא לָא אִיקְּרִי מַזִּיק. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי לַהּ לְהַאי גִּיסָא: אַדְּרַבָּה, מַלְכָּא [רַתְחָנָא הוּא], מַאי דְּבָעֵי עָבֵיד, שֶׁהַמֶּלֶךְ פּוֹרֵץ גָּדֵר לַעֲשׂוֹת לוֹ דֶּרֶךְ וְאֵין מוֹחִין בְּיָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: Yosef the Demon said to me: Ashmedai, the king of the demons, is appointed over all who perform actions in pairs, and a king is not called a harmful spirit.** A king would not cause harm. Consequently, there is no reason to fear the harm of demons for having performed an action in pairs. **Some say this statement in this manner: On the contrary, he is an angry king** who **does what he wants,** as the *halakha* is **that a king may breach the fence** of an individual **in order to form a path for himself, and none may protest his** action. Similarly, the king of demons has full license to harm people who perform actions in pairs.

*Amar Rav Yosef*: Yosef the *shed* told me — *amli Yosef Shida* — that *Ashmedai malka de-sheidei* — Ashmedai, king of all the *sheidim* — is the one appointed over all the matters of pairs, *memunei hu akulu zugei*. It is he who controls the ability to harm people when they act in pairs. *U'malka lo ikri mazik* — a king does not want to be called a destroyer or a damager. The implication is that the concern of pairs is ultimately under royal control, and a king who wishes to protect his reputation is unlikely to be overly aggressive in exercising it.

### Two Versions

*Ika de-amri* — there are those who have a different reading. *Adderaba* — on the contrary: a king, or on some *girsa* *ratchana hu* — an angry king — does whatever he wishes, *mai de-ba'i avid*. Just as the *halacha* states that a king may breach a fence to make himself a path — *she-hamelech poretz geder la'asos lo derech* — and *ein mochin be-yado*, no one can stop him, so too Ashmedai, being a king, feels entirely free to harm those who expose themselves through pairs. According to this version, the concern is heightened rather than mitigated.

### Rav Papa Shedim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, אָמַר לִי יוֹסֵף שֵׁידָא: בִּתְרֵי קָטְלִינַן, בְּאַרְבְּעָה לָא קָטְלִינַן, בְּאַרְבְּעָה מַזְּקִינַן. בִּתְרֵי, בֵּין בְּשׁוֹגֵג בֵּין בְּמֵזִיד. בְּאַרְבְּעָה, בְּמֵזִיד — אִין, בְּשׁוֹגֵג — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Yosef the Demon said to me:** If one drinks **two cups, we** demons **kill** him; if he drinks **four, we do not kill** him. But this person who drank **four, we harm** him. There is another difference between two and four: **With regard to one who drinks two, whether he did so unwittingly or intentionally, we harm him. With regard to one who drinks four,** if he does so **intentionally, yes,** he is harmed; if he does so **unwittingly, no,** he will not be harmed.

*Amar Rav Papa*: Yosef the *shed* told me — *be-trei katlinan* — with two cups, if someone drinks just two, the *sheidim* will be able to kill. *V'arba*, four is dangerous but less so: *lo katlinan* — we cannot kill, *aval be-arba mazakinan* — but we can cause damage. Furthermore, with two cups, *bein be-shogeg bein be-mezid* — whether the person knowingly drinks in pairs or does so unknowingly — the danger is equal. With four cups, however, *be-mezid in* — the danger applies only to one who drinks knowingly; *be-shogeg lo* — if one does so without awareness, there is no danger.

### Thumb Incant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אִישְׁתְּלִי וְאִיקְּרִי וּנְפַק, מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ? לִינְקוֹט זַקְפָּא דִידֵיהּ דְּיַמִּינֵיהּ בִּידָא דִשְׂמָאלֵיהּ וְזַקְפָּא דִשְׂמָאלֵיהּ בִּידָא דְיַמִּינֵיהּ, וְנֵימָא הָכִי: ״אַתּוּן וַאֲנָא — הָא תְּלָתָא״. וְאִי שְׁמִיעַ לֵיהּ דְּאָמַר: ״אַתּוּן וַאֲנָא — הָא אַרְבְּעָה״, נֵימָא לֵיהּ: ״אַתּוּן וַאֲנָא — הָא חַמְשָׁה״. וְאִי שְׁמִיעַ לֵיהּ דְּאָמַר: ״אַתּוּן וַאֲנָא — הָא שִׁיתָּא״, נֵימָא לֵיהּ: ״אַתּוּן וַאֲנָא — הָא שִׁבְעָה״. הֲוָה עוֹבָדָא עַד מְאָה וְחַד, וּפְקַע שֵׁידָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And if one forgets and it happens that he goes outside** after having drunk an even number of cups, **what is his solution?** The Gemara answers: He should **take his right thumb in his left hand, and his left thumb in his right hand, and say as follows: You, my thumbs, and I are three,** which is not a pair. **And if he hears** a voice **that says: You and I are four,** which makes a pair, he should **say to it: You and I are five. And if he hears it say: You and I are six,** he should **say to it: You and I are seven. The Gemara relates that there was an incident** in which someone kept counting after the demon **until** he reached **a hundred and one, and the demon burst** in anger.

If someone forgot to count and has now left the bar having drunk an even number of cups, what is his remedy? *Linchis zakfa de-yada de-yamina be-yada de-smala* — hold the thumb of your right hand in your left hand, and hold the thumb of your left hand in your right hand, and say: *atun va-ana hatlasa* — you and I make three. Counting the cups drunk plus oneself yields an odd number, restoring safety.

### Shed Bursts

If a voice responds — *ishmeia lei de-amar* — claiming *atun va-ana ha-arba'a*, you and I make four — restoring the even count — one should respond: *atun va-ana ha-chamisha*, you and I make five. The back-and-forth continues: if the voice counters with six, respond with seven; and so on. *Va-hava uvda*, there was an actual incident in which this exchange continued *ad mea ve-chad* — all the way to 101 — at which point *u-paka sheida*, the *shed* burst. Sufficient perseverance in this incantation can thus not only protect oneself but apparently destroy the *shed* entirely.

### Rishtina Witches

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: אֲמַרָה לִי רֵישָׁתִינְהִי דְּנָשִׁים כַּשְׁפָנִיּוֹת: הַאי מַאן דְּפָגַע בְּהוּ בְּנָשִׁים כַּשְׁפָנִיּוֹת, נֵימָא הָכִי: ״חָרֵי חַמִּימֵי בְּדִיקּוּלָא בַּזְיָיא לְפוּמַּיְיכוּ נְשֵׁי דְּחָרָשַׁיָּיא, קָרַח קָרְחַיְיכִי, פָּרַח פָּרְחַיְיכִי, אִיבַּדּוּר תַּבְלוּנַיְיכִי, פָּרְחָא זִיקָא לְמוֹרִיקָא חַדְתָּא דְּנָקְטִיתוּ נָשִׁים כַּשְׁפָנִיּוֹת, אַדְּחַנַּנִּי וְחַנַּנְכִי לָא אֲתֵיתִי לְגוֹ, הַשְׁתָּא דַּאֲתֵיתִי לְגוֹ — קַרְחַנְנִי וַחֲנַנְכִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar said: The chief of witches said to me: One who encounters witches should say this incantation: Hot feces in torn date baskets in your mouth, witches; may your hairs fall out** because you use them for witchcraft; **your crumbs, which you use for witchcraft, should scatter in the wind; your spices,** which you use for your witchcraft, **should scatter; the wind should blow away the fresh saffron that you witches hold** to perform your witchcraft. **As long as I was shown favor** from Heaven **and you showed me favor, I did not come here. Now that I have come here, your favor toward me has cooled and you should find favor.**

*Amar Meimar*: Rishtina — the head matron of the women witches, the *nashei kashfanyasa* — said to me the following. She is, in effect, the presiding power of the practitioners of *keshafim*, the parallel figure on the demonic side to Yosef the *shed* on the demonic side. If one stumbles into a gathering of women witches, one should say: *chari chamimi be-dikula de-zaya le-pumeichu nashei de-charshaya* — hot excrement in a torn basket to your mouths, women who perform sorcery. *Kerach karchaichi* — your hair should be bald; *perach perchaichi* — your tools should scatter. The *Rashbam* explains that witches used their hair in their brews, and that *perchaichi* refers either to an instrument of their craft or to pieces of bread used in their potions, all of which should be dispersed and rendered ineffective. *Ibdur tavlineichu* — your potions and fragrances should be scattered; *parcha zika le-morika chadata de-nakitu* — the wind should blow away the fresh saffron you are holding, another ingredient in the witches' preparations. The incantation concludes: *nashei kashfanyasa, ad de-chanani ve-chananechi, lo asisi le-go* — as long as I was protected and had my good fortune, I never came into your midst; *hashta de-asisi le-go, kar chanani ve-chananechi* — now that I have come among you, my protective favor has cooled and I am exposed.

It is worth pausing to note that the identification of women specifically with witchcraft reflects a deeply embedded cultural demonization of women that runs through ancient and medieval societies — a theme that resurfaces repeatedly and extends well beyond the pages of the Talmud into later European history.

## Geography Limits (II)

### Eretz Yisrael No Care

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַעְרְבָא לָא קָפְדִי אַזּוּגֵי. רַב דִּימִי מִנְּהַרְדְּעָא קָפֵיד אֲפִילּוּ אַרוּשְׁמָא דְחָבִיתָא. הֲוָה עוֹבָדָא וּפְקַע חָבִיתָא. כְּלָלָא דְמִילְּתָא, כׇּל דְּקָפֵיד — קָפְדִי בַּהֲדֵיהּ, וּדְלָא קָפֵיד — לָא קָפְדִי בַּהֲדֵיהּ, וּמִיהוּ לְמֵיחַשׁ מִיבְּעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **in the West, Eretz Yisrael, they were not particular with regard to pairs. Rav Dimi from Neharde'a was particular** about pairs **even with regard to the signs on a barrel;** he would not write pairs of symbols on a barrel. **There was an incident in which there were pairs of symbols on a barrel and the barrel burst. The rule of the matter** is that **all who are particular** about pairs, the demons **are particular with him; and if one is not particular, they are not particular with him. However, one is required to be concerned** about the harm that might result from purposely performing actions in pairs.

*Be-ma'arava lo kapdi a-zugei* — in *Eretz Yisrael* they are not careful about *zugot* at all. This is a strikingly important acknowledgment, occupying only four words in the text, that the entire elaborate system of concern about pairs is a specifically Babylonian preoccupation and is unknown in the land of Israel. The society one inhabits shapes one's perception of and vulnerability to these forces.

### Rav Dimi Four Foods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר: שְׁתֵּי בֵיצִים, וּשְׁתֵּי אֱגוֹזִין, שְׁתֵּי קִישּׁוּאִין, וְדָבָר אַחֵר — הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי. וּמִסְתַּפְּקָא לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן מַאי נִיהוּ דָּבָר אַחֵר, וּגְזוּר רַבָּנַן בְּכוּלְּהוּ זוּגֵי מִשּׁוּם דָּבָר אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said:** With regard to **two eggs, two nuts, two cucumbers, and another matter, there is a *halakha*** transmitted **to Moses from Sinai** that they are dangerous in pairs. **But the Sages are uncertain about what the other matter is, and** therefore **the Sages decreed that all pairs** are prohibited **due to that other matter.**

*Ki ata Rav Dimi amar* — when Rav Dimi came from *Eretz Yisrael* he reported the following. *Shtei beitzim u'shtei egozim, shtei kishu'im u'davar acher* — two eggs, two nuts, two gourds, and one other item. These four specific foods are subject to the pairs concern — *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai*. That term is used here, as elsewhere by *Amoraim*, in a broader sense than its literal meaning, simply indicating a very ancient and authoritative tradition. The Rabbis, however, forgot which food was the fourth — *u'mistapka lehu l'rabbanan mai nihu davar acher*. Since they did not know what the fourth was, they extended the concern of pairs to everything — *v'gazru rabbanan be'kulehu zugei mishum davar acher*. All of the elaborate Babylonian preoccupation with pairs across all foods and drinks is therefore, from the perspective of this Eretz Yisrael tradition as mediated through the *Bavli*, simply a stringency adopted because one of four specific foods was forgotten. One might read this as the *Bavli* politely but significantly deflating the entire edifice of Babylonian *zugot* practice.

## Exemptions

### Witchcraft vs Mazikin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דַּאֲמַרַן עֲשָׂרָה, תְּמָנְיָא, שִׁיתָּא, אַרְבְּעָה אֵין בָּהֶם מִשּׁוּם זוּגֵי, לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן מַזִּיקִין. אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן כְּשָׁפִים — אֲפִילּוּ טוּבָא נָמֵי חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara notes that **that which we said** above, that the numbers **ten, eight, six, and four do not cause the danger associated with pairs, we said only with regard to harmful spirits. However, with regard to witchcraft, we are concerned even with regard to** one who performed an activity **a greater** number of times.

Having seemingly narrowed the concern almost to nothing, the Gemara steps back: *lo amran ela l'inyan mazikin* — when we said that numbers such as ten, eight, six, and four are not a problem of pairs, that applies only to the concern of *mazikin*. *Aval l'inyan keshafim, afilu tuvei nami chaishinan* — but with regard to witchcraft, even a large even number is dangerous. Unlike the general demonic danger of *mazikin*, which diminishes with higher multiples, the vulnerability to deliberate *keshafim* directed at a person is not reduced by drinking large quantities — any even number remains a concern. This qualification applies specifically in places where there are actually practitioners of witchcraft.

### Curse Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּגָרְשַׁהּ לִדְבֵיתְהוּ. אִזִיל אִינַּסְבָה לְחַנְוָאָה. כׇּל יוֹמָא הֲוָה אָזֵיל וְשָׁתֵי חַמְרָא. הֲוָה קָא עָבְדָא לֵיהּ כְּשָׁפִים וְלָא קָא מַהְנְיָא לַהּ בֵּיהּ, מִשּׁוּם דַּהֲוָה מִזְדְּהַר בְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ בְּזוּגָא.
>
> **Translation:** This is **like that** incident involving **a certain man who divorced his wife. She went and married a shopkeeper** who sold wine in his store. **Every day, the first husband would go and drink wine** in that shop. His ex-wife **would perform witchcraft upon him, and it would not be effective for her in her attempts to harm him because he was careful with regard to pairs.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹמָא חַד אִשְׁתִּי טוּבָא, וְלָא הֲוָה יָדַע כַּמָּה שָׁתֵי. עַד שִׁיתְּסַר הֲוָה צְיִיל וְאִיזְדְּהַר בְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ לָא הֲוָה צְיִיל וְלָא אִיזְדְּהַר בְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ, אַפֵּיקְתֵּיהּ בְּזוּגָא. כִּי הֲוָה אָזֵיל, גָּס בֵּיהּ הָהוּא טַיָּיעָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: גַּבְרָא קְטִילָא הוּא דְּאָזֵיל הָכָא. אָזֵיל חַבְּקֵיהּ לְדִיקְלָא, צְוַוח דִּיקְלָא וּפְקַע הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **One day he drank a lot, and he did not know how much he drank. Until** he drank **sixteen cups, he was lucid and was careful with regard to himself,** to keep track of how many cups he had drunk. **From here onward he was not lucid and was not careful to watch himself, and she caused him to leave** after having consumed **a pair,** i.e., an even number of cups. **As he walked, a certain Arab met him and, noticing that he was bewitched, said to him: It is a dead man who walks here. He went and hugged a palm tree** for support; **the palm tree dried out** due to the witchcraft, **and he burst.**

*D'hahu gavra d'garsha l'dveitehu* — a man divorced his wife. *Azla insava l'chanu'ah* — she went and married an innkeeper who served wine. Every day the ex-husband would come to the inn and drink wine there — *kol yoma hava ati v'shati chamra*. Meanwhile, his ex-wife in the back would be performing *keshafim* against him. *V'lo ka mahanya beh mishum d'hava mizaher b'nafsheh b'zuga* — but her witchcraft had no effect, because he was always careful to drink an odd number of cups.

One day he drank a great deal — *yoma chad ishti tuvei* — and lost count of how many he had consumed. Up through sixteen cups he remained clear-minded and careful — *ad shitsar hava tzil u'mizaher b'nafsheh*. Beyond sixteen — *mikan v'eilach lo hava tzil* — he was no longer clear-headed and stopped being careful. *V'afik leh b'zuga* — and he left with an even number, no longer paying attention to the count. As he walked home, *paga beh hahu taya* — a wandering merchant encountered him and said: *gavra ktila hu d'azil hacha* — a dead man is walking here. The man went and embraced a palm tree — *azil chavkeh l'dikla* — and the palm tree cried out and he burst. Some *girsaot* have *u'paka dikla* — the palm tree burst, as though the hex somehow transferred to the tree; our text has *u'paka hu*, he himself burst. Either way, the story demonstrates that drinking pairs, even in large quantities, renders one vulnerable to directed *keshafim*.

### Plates Bread

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב עַוִּירָא: קְעָרוֹת וְכִכָּרוֹת אֵין בָּהֶם מִשּׁוּם זוּגוֹת. כְּלָלָא דְּמִילְּתָא: כׇּל שֶׁגְּמָרוֹ בִּידֵי אָדָם — אֵין בָּהֶם מִשּׁוּם זוּגוֹת. גְּמָרוֹ בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם — בְּמִילֵּי מִינֵי דְמֵיכַל חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Avira said: Plates and loaves do not contain the element of danger associated with pairs. The rule of the matter** is that **anything** whose production **was completed by people,** whether a vessel or food, **they do not contain the element of danger associated with pairs. Conversely, if the object was completed by Heaven,** e.g., **with regard to types of food, we are concerned.**

*Amar Rav Avira*: *karot v'kikarot ein bahem mishum zugot* — plates and loaves of bread do not fall under the concern of pairs. The *Rashbam* explains that *k'arot* here means eating two platefuls of food.

### Made by Whom

The *Rashbam* explains the difference: *kol she'gemaru bidei adam, ein bahem mishum zugot* — anything whose completion is in human hands does not have the *zugot* concern. *Kol she'gemaru bidei shamayim, b'mili minei d'michal, chaishinan* — anything whose final form is the work of heaven and which is a foodstuff, there we are concerned. Wine, for instance, is in some sense closer to its natural state — the product of grapes left to ferment — while bread and cooked dishes are more thoroughly human constructions. This distinction between *bidei adam* and *bidei shamayim* is the operative principle.

### Bar Drinking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲנוּת — אֵין בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם זוּגוֹת. נִמְלָךְ — אֵין בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם זוּגוֹת. אוֹרֵחַ — אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם זוּגוֹת. אִשָּׁה — אֵין בָּהּ מִשּׁוּם זוּגוֹת. וְאִי אִשָּׁה חֲשׁוּבָה, חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, **a store does not contain the element of danger associated with pairs. If one eats there, there is no danger, as this is not his regular place. If one changed one's mind** after drinking an odd number of cups and added one more, his drinking **does not contain the element of danger associated with pairs,** as he did not initially intend to drink an even number. The behavior of **a guest** who eats or drinks in someone else's house **does not contain the element of danger associated with pairs,** as his host determines how much he will eat and drink. The behavior of **a woman does not contain the element of danger associated with pairs,** as demons are not particular with regard to how much a woman eats or drinks. **But if she is an important woman, we are concerned.**

*Chanut ein bahem mishum zugot* — drinking in a bar does not have the *zugot* concern. The *Rashbam* explains that each new glass ordered in a bar constitutes a fresh decision — *nimlach*, a new act of will — and therefore the cups do not combine into a pair. Alternatively, even two glasses at two different bars do not combine. One might ask: what about the story of the ex-husband and the witchcraft in the inn? The *Rashbam* distinguishes: that was a case of specifically directed witchcraft, whereas the generic *zugot* concern addressed here involves the general vulnerability to *mazikin*, not a targeted hex.

### Guests Ad Hoc

*Ore'ach ein bo mishum zugot* — a guest does not have the *zugot* concern. Since the guest does not know whether the host will offer another glass, each cup is a new and separate event. The Gemara's earlier discussion of Rava pouring four cups for his students before they departed may be slightly different — there may have been an established practice of drinking multiple cups in that setting — but the general principle is that unplanned, ad hoc drinking does not combine.

### Women Drinking

*Isha ein ba mishum zugot* — a woman does not have the *zugot* concern. This connects to the earlier discussion of *isha chashuva* and *haseiva*. In Roman society there was a significant period during which it was considered inappropriate for women to drink wine at all — a norm that softened over time — and this social reality is reflected here. Because a woman drinking wine is not considered normative drinking — not a *kevi'us* in the way that a man's drinking at home is — each occurrence is treated as an exception, and the cups do not combine. However, if she is an *isha chashuva*, a woman of standing for whom drinking wine is more socially normal, the concern does apply.

### Health Drinks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִינָּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: אִיסְפָּרָגוֹס — מִצְטָרֵף לְטוֹבָה, וְאֵין מִצְטָרֵף לְרָעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥinnana, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: *Isparegus*,** a wine-based drink with added spices that people would regularly consume in the morning, **combines** with the number of cups of wine one has already consumed **for the good,** to raise the total to an odd number, **and does not combine for the bad,** to bring the sum to an even number.

*Amar Rav Chinena brei de-Rav Yehoshua*: *ispargus mitztaref letovah ve'ein mitztaref lera'ah* — the *ispargus* drink — an asparagus-based health beverage, prepared according to the *Rashbam* by soaking in wine or another alcoholic beverage and drunk each morning before eating for medicinal purposes — combines for good but does not combine for bad. Like the *kos shel bracha* and the words of *Birchas Kohanim*, it can land one at an odd, beneficial number but will never contribute to a dangerous even number.

This passage touches on a broader methodological question about whether one is bound to accept *Chazal*'s authority in medical matters. The *Geonim* generally held that one need not, on the grounds that *nishtanu hateva* — natural circumstances change — and this very item, asparagus, is one of the early points at which that principle is invoked. Interestingly, Rav Natan Ba'al Ha'Aruch, in his dictionary *Ha'Aruch* (likely tenth century, written in Rome), discusses this medical drink under the relevant entry and quotes the *Geonim* who hold that the Gemara's medical guidance need not be followed. It is striking that the same *daf* that discusses *sheidim* and protective incantations also generates one of the earliest recorded statements that *Chazal*'s empirical claims are not binding — the one concern seemingly more remote from our experience than the other.

## Doubt & Drinks

### Even Odd Doubt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: זוּגֵי — לְחוּמְרָא. וְאָמְרִי לֵיהּ: זוּגֵי — לְקוּלָּא. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: תְּרֵי דְחַמְרָא וְחַד דְּשִׁיכְרָא — לָא מִצְטָרֵף, תְּרֵי דְשִׁיכְרָא וְחַד דְּחַמְרָא — מִצְטָרֵף.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said in the name of Rava:** If one is in doubt as to whether he has drunk in **pairs, he should be stringent** and drink another cup. **And some say** that one who is uncertain with regard to **pairs** should be **lenient** and not drink an additional cup, lest the additional cup be the even number. **Rav Yosef said: Two cups of wine and one cup of beer do not combine; two cups of beer and one cup of wine do combine.**

*Amar Ravina mishmei de-Rava*: if one has lost track of whether one's count is even or odd — a *safek* — what does one do? *Zugei lechumra*: be stringent, meaning drink another glass of wine. The *Rashbam* explains: if one was already at an odd number and now drinks another, that cup combines for good — *mitztaref letovah* — and will land one safely at an even number; but if one was already at an even number, this additional cup, drunk out of doubt, does not combine for bad — *lera lo mitztaref* — and therefore resolves the *safek* in one's favor. *V'amrei lah zugei lekula*: others say one should be lenient, not drink another glass. This approach invokes the principle discussed earlier — that not being *makpid* is itself protective.

### Wine Beer Separate

*Amar Rav Yosef*: *trei chamra ve-chad de-shichra lo mitztarfe* — two cups of wine and one of beer do not combine. Adding one cup of beer to two cups of wine does not render the total odd in any meaningful sense. *Trei de-shichra ve-chad de-chamra mitztarfe* — two cups of beer and one of wine, however, do combine. The more important drink — wine — combines upward; the less important one — beer — does not.

### Tumah Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסִימָנָיךְ, זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל הַמְחוּבָּר לוֹ מִן הֶחָמוּר מִמֶּנּוּ — טָמֵא, מִן הַקַּל מִמֶּנּוּ — טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **And your mnemonic** by which to remember this rule is a mishna concerning the *halakhot* of ritual purity. **This is the rule: With regard to anything attached to an object, if the smaller piece is more stringent than the larger one, the combined object is ritually impure;** if the attached substance **is more lenient than it, the combined object is ritually pure. In other words, if a small piece of a type of fabric that contracts ritual impurity when it is relatively small, which is a stringency, is attached to a larger object comprised of a less valuable fabric that contracts ritual impurity only when it is bigger, the two materials combine to form a unified fabric that contracts ritual impurity if together they amount to the larger requisite size. However, if there is more of the stringent material, the two substances do not combine halakhically to form the amount of the smaller requisite size.** Similarly, in the case of pairs, the wine is more significant than the beer. Therefore, the wine combines with the beer but not vice versa.

The mnemonic offered: *zeh ha-klal, kol ha-muchbar lo min ha-chamur mimenu tamei, min ha-kal mimenu tahor* — the rule in the laws of *tumah* regarding hybrid fabrics is that a lesser fabric sewn onto a more important one does not combine to constitute a significant garment, but a more important fabric sewn onto a lesser one does combine, lending significance to the whole. The same principle applies here: the more important item draws the lesser one along with it, but the lesser item does not pull the more important one into its category.

## Before During After

### Rav Nachman Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַב: תְּרֵי קַמֵּי תַּכָּא וְחַד אַתַּכָּא — מִצְטָרְפִי. חַד מִקַּמֵּי תַּכָּא וּתְרֵי אַתַּכָּא — לֹא מִצְטָרְפִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said that Rav said:** If one drinks **two cups before the table** is brought and the meal begins **and one cup over the table they combine,** the person is not considered to have drunk a pair of cups. However, if one drinks **one cup before the table is brought and two cups over the table they do not combine;** the two cups he drank during the meal are considered a pair.

*Amar Rav Nachman amar Rav*: *trei kammei takka ve-chad attei takka mitztarfe* — two cups before the meal and one during the meal combine. *Chad mikmei takka ve-trei attei takka lo mitztarfe* — one cup before the meal and two during the meal do not combine. The before-meal cups are somehow treated as more significant, and they draw the during-meal cup into their category.

### Rav Mesharshiya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: אַטּוּ אֲנַן לְתַקּוֹנֵי תַּכָּא קָא בָּעֵינַן? לְתַקּוֹנֵי גַּבְרָא בָּעֵינַן, וְגַבְרָא קָא מִיתְּקַן וְקָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mesharshiya strongly objects to this** ruling: **Is that to say that we need to resolve the problem of pairs with regard to the table?** Is the presence of the table the decisive factor here? **We need to resolve the problem with regard to the person, and with regard to the person it is considered resolved.** He began drinking before the table was brought, and he has consumed an odd number of cups.

*Matkif lah Rav Mesharshiya*: Rav Mesharshiya challenges this. *Atu anan le-tikunei takka ka-beinan?* — are we trying to fix the plate, the dish? *Le-tikunei gavra ka-beinan* — we are trying to fix the person. *Ve-gavra mitkan ve-kayei* — and the person drank three cups; he should simply be considered to have drunk three cups, regardless of when they fell relative to the meal.

### Two After One

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא תְּרֵי אַתַּכָּא וְחַד לְבָתַר תַּכָּא — לָא מִצְטָרְפִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Mesharshiya said: Everyone agrees that if one drank two cups over the table** during the meal **and one after the table** has been removed, **they do not combine.**

Despite this challenge, the Gemara concludes: *de-chulei alma trei attei takka ve-chad le-vatar takka lo mitztarfe* — everyone agrees that two cups during the meal and one cup after do not combine, and the person in that situation remains susceptible to the *zugot* concern. We will take up this thread in the next *daf*.
