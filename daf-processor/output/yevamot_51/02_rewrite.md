# Yevamot 51 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Framework: Acts After Ac…

### Mishna Overview

The Mishna on daf 51 works through an intricate series of permutations involving four distinct acts in the context of *yibum*: *maimar*, *get*, *biah*, and *chalitzah*. The governing question is what happens when one act follows another — whether performed by the same *yavam* with the same *yevama*, or across different brothers-in-law and different co-wives. Before entering the *Gemara*'s analysis, it is worth fixing the basic framework in mind.

### Rabbinic vs. Torah Acts

The four acts divide into two categories based on their legal standing. *Maimar* and *get* are rabbinic in this context. *Maimar* is an act of *kidushin* — literally *harei at mekudeshet li* — performed in the proper form, and *get* is a standard bill of divorce, executed properly in every technical respect. The critical point is that because these acts take place within the framework of *yibum*, their operative force is only *derabbanan*, not *d'oraisa*. *Biah* and *chalitzah*, by contrast, are *d'oraisa*: they constitute the Torah-mandated mechanisms for either completing or releasing the *yibum* bond. This distinction between rabbinic and Torah-level acts is the fulcrum on which the entire Mishnaic debate turns.

### Debate Structure

With that framework in place, the dispute between Rabban Gamliel and the *chachamim* can be stated precisely. Rabban Gamliel holds: *ein get achar get v'lo maimar achar maimar* — once a *get* or *maimar* has been given, no subsequent act of the same kind carries any consequence, even though those acts operate only rabbinically. And of course, *biah* after *biah* and *chalitzah* after *chalitzah* are similarly without effect, since the Torah-level act has already been completed. The *chachamim* agree regarding *biah* and *chalitzah*, but disagree regarding *get* and *maimar*: *yesh get achar get v'yesh maimar achar maimar* — because these acts are only rabbinic, they do not fully terminate the situation, and a second act of the same or a different type may still carry halakhic import. The debate thus focuses on a single axis: how absolute is a first rabbinic act against anything that comes afterwards?

## Foundational Principles

### Mehani Concept

Before entering the dispute itself, the *Gemara* at the bottom of 51b steps back to establish the conceptual foundations. The opening question is: *mahani mai ta'ama* — on what basis did the Rabbis institute the concept that a *get* or *maimar* has any effect at all in the *yibum* context? The term *mehani* here does not mean that these acts accomplish everything. A *get* given to a *yevama* does not function as a *chalitzah*, which is required *d'oraisa*; a *maimar* does not constitute *yibum*, which requires actual *biah*. What *mehani* means is that these acts carry halakhic implications. A *get* given to a *yevama*, for instance, closes off the possibility of *yibum* — *pasla kol ha'achim*; the other brothers can no longer perform *yibum* with her, and she takes on a status analogous to a divorced wife, prohibiting marriage to the *yavam*'s relatives. A *maimar* similarly creates a quasi-marital relationship, implicating the prohibitions involving relatives of the *yevama* and affecting the ability to perform *yibum* with another co-wife.

### Public Confusion Risk

The *Gemara* then explains why the Rabbis could not simply declare these acts meaningless. The argument is one of public confusion: *get l'hotzi v'chalitzah l'hotzi* — a *get* normally functions to release a full wife, and *chalitzah* functions to release a *yevama*. Were the Rabbis to say that a *get* given to a *yevama* is entirely meaningless, people would observe such a *get* being given and then watch as the *yavam* proceeds to perform *yibum* nonetheless. They would conclude that *chalitzah* is similarly meaningless — *chalitzah nami lo mahani* — and would come to believe that only *yibum* itself counts. The result would be that after a *chalitzah* they might still permit *biah* — *v'atei l'miba'al achar chalitzah* — which would be a severe violation. The same logic applies to *maimar*: were it declared null, people would conclude from parallel reasoning that *biah* in this context is also ineffective, leading to the possibility of *biah* after *biah* — violating the principle of *bayis echad hu boneh*.

### Rabbinic Necessity

The practical upshot is that the Rabbis needed these institutions to carry some weight in order to prevent the entire halakhic structure of *yibum* and *chalitzah* from becoming unintelligible to the public. Whether this represents a purely reactive rabbinic response to existing popular practice or a more proactive rabbinic design is a nuanced question — the *Gemara*'s framing here sounds reactive, as though people were already performing these acts and the Rabbis could not simply negate them. Other sources may present a more deliberately constructive picture of the institution of *maimar* in particular. In any case, the conclusion is firm: *get* and *maimar* in the *yibum* context must carry *some* halakhic meaning — *mehani* — even if not the full force of the Torah-level acts they parallel.

## Biah Pesulah Doctrin (II)

### Biah After Get

The *Gemara* continues: *u'mai ta'ama amru rabanan bi'ah pesulah yesh achareiha klum* — why did the Rabbis say that after an invalid *biah* (*biah pesulah*), subsequent acts remain meaningful? To answer this, one must understand what *biah pesulah* actually is. The Mishna at the close of the preceding discussion states: *bizman she'hi b'techilah ein achareiha klum* — when *biah* comes at the outset of the *yibum* process, the act of *yibum* is complete and nothing afterward counts. But *ba'emtza uv'sof* — when *biah* comes after something else has already transpired — things that follow it can still be meaningful.

The paradigmatic case of *biah pesulah* involves a scenario where the *yavam* gave a *get* to one *yevama* and then performed *biah* with another. The *Gemara* reasons: *gezeirah bi'ah achar get mishum bi'ah achar chalitzah* — the Rabbis treat *biah* following a *get* as problematic by analogy to *biah* following *chalitzah*. The logic is as follows. Suppose, instead of giving a *get* to the first *yevama*, the *yavam* had performed *chalitzah* with her — then *biah* with the second would be entirely forbidden, as *chalitzah* definitively closes the process. Since *get* and *chalitzah* are easily confused, and since permitting *biah* after *get* risks being confused with the forbidden *biah* after *chalitzah*, the Rabbis characterize *biah* after *get* as *biah pesulah* as well. The consequence is that although *d'oraisa* the *biah* constitutes valid *yibum* with the second *yevama*, the Rabbis do not allow the *yavam* to remain in that relationship; he must give her a *get* and she requires *chalitzah* to be fully released.

### Gezeirah Logic

A parallel case of *biah pesulah* arises when *biah* follows a *maimar*. If the *yavam* performed *maimar* with one *yevama* and then *biah* with another, the principle *bayis echad hu boneh v'eino boneh shnei batim* — that a man builds one household, not two — means that had the first act been full *yibum* rather than *maimar*, the *biah* with the second would be impermissible. Since *maimar* is a quasi-*yibum*, the *Gemara* treats *biah* after *maimar* as *biah pesulah* by a *gezeirah*: *gezeirah bi'ah achar ma'amar mishum bi'ah achar biah*.

The crucial consequence of *biah pesulah* is that the relationship is not considered fully consummated. Something remains unresolved, and therefore subsequent acts — giving a *get*, performing *maimar*, or doing *chalitzah* — can still carry halakhic meaning. The *Gemara* designates this with the phrase *yesh achareiha klum* — there is still something after it.

### Chalitzah vs. Biah

At this point one might ask: if *biah* after *get* is rendered *pesulah* because of its similarity to the forbidden *biah* after *chalitzah*, why does *chalitzah* after *get* not similarly become *chalitzah pesulah*? The *Gemara* addresses this directly: *mai nigzar* — what decree should we make? If we were to treat *chalitzah* after *get* as *pesulah* because it resembles *chalitzah* after *chalitzah*, the consequence would only be that we demand further *chalitzahs* — *kol ki hani tachlots v'teitzei* — and since *chalitzah* is not a forbidden act, requiring additional *chalitzahs* carries no grave consequence. It does affect the woman's status as a *chalutzah* and her prohibition to a *kohen*, but it does not generate the kind of severe *arayot* violation that motivates the *gezeirah* regarding *biah*. *Biah* with a woman one is forbidden to is a fundamentally different order of problem from performing an extra *chalitzah*. Therefore the *gezeirah* applies to *biah pesulah* but not to *chalitzah*: a *chalitzah* that follows a *get* or a *maimar* remains fully valid and ends the process — *ein achar chalitzah klum*.

## Rabban Gamliel's Model

### All-or-Nothing Logic

The *Gemara* now turns to explain Rabban Gamliel's position in depth. *Mai ta'ama d'Rabban Gamliel* — what is Rabban Gamliel's reasoning that even *get* after *get* and *maimar* after *maimar* are without effect? The *Gemara* answers: *demispeka lei get ee dachi ee lo dachi, maimar ee kani ee lo kani* — Rabban Gamliel is uncertain whether a *get* in the *yibum* context fully "pushes off" (*dachi*) all the *yevamot*, or does nothing at all; and similarly whether *maimar* fully "acquires" (*kani*) the *yevama* in a quasi-marital bond, or accomplishes nothing.

The binary nature of this uncertainty is what drives his ruling. If the *get* fully pushes off — *ee ka madchi* — then the first *get* has already resolved the situation with respect to all the *yevamot*, and the second *get* finds nothing left to accomplish: *basar mai ka avid*. If the *get* accomplishes nothing — *v'ee kama lo dachi* — then even the first *get* did nothing, and the second certainly does not: *basar nami lo dachi*. Either the first fully worked or neither works. The same analysis applies to *maimar*: if the first *maimar* fully acquired the *yevama*, the second is superfluous; if the first acquired nothing, the second is no better. The only meaningful act is therefore always the first one — which *might* be fully effective — while every subsequent act of the same kind is certainly without consequence.

### Consistency Argument

This binary model has a striking implication that Tosafot and Rashi both highlight. Rabban Gamliel says *ein get achar get v'ein maimar achar maimar* — but he appears to concede that a *get* after a *maimar*, or a *maimar* after a *get*, *can* be meaningful. If he truly held that these rabbinic acts function at full force, why would mixing them matter? The binary model explains this elegantly. The uncertainty about *get* and the uncertainty about *maimar* are independent of one another: *get* might be effective or might not; *maimar* might acquire or might not. They need not share the same outcome. Therefore, if *maimar* was effective and *get* was not, the *get* after *maimar* does something previously unaddressed, and vice versa. Two acts of the same type, however, necessarily target the same outcome — either the first worked and the second is redundant, or the first failed and the second will likewise fail.

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye raises a sharp difficulty against Rabban Gamliel's binary model. The Mishna acknowledges that Rabban Gamliel concedes the existence of *biah pesulah* — that *biah* following *get* or *maimar* remains problematic and subsequent acts carry meaning. Abaye asks: *v'ee salka da'atach d'Rabban Gamliel havyah k'viah d'lechatchilah v'tikni* — if the *maimar* is either fully effective or fully zero, as Rabban Gamliel maintains, how does *biah pesulah* arise at all? If the *maimar* with the first *yevama* was fully effective, then the subsequent *biah* with the second is like *biah* that comes *b'techilah* — at the outset — and nothing afterward counts: *ein achareiha klum*. If the *maimar* was zero, then the subsequent *biah* is a clean *biah* not preceded by anything, and again nothing afterward would count. On either prong of the binary, there is no room for *biah pesulah*. Yet Rabban Gamliel concedes the category. The binary model, taken strictly, cannot account for it.

## Fifty-Percent Model

### Two Halves Concept

Abaye's challenge dissolves Rabban Gamliel's pure binary framework and compels a different account of how *get* and *maimar* function. *Ella amar Abaye* — rather, Abaye says, the correct understanding is that the Rabbis established *get* and *maimar* in the *yibum* context not as all-or-nothing propositions, but as acts that each address exactly one half of the *zikah* bond. The *zikah* — the quasi-spousal connection that ties the *yevama* to the *yavam* — is not a single undifferentiated legal relationship but is conceptually composed of two distinct components.

### Slot Division

One half of the *zikah* is the *kinyan* component — the aspect of acquisition and quasi-marital attachment. This is the half that *maimar* addresses. The other half is the *dechiyah* component — the aspect of release and severance. This is the half that *get* addresses. *Maimar* fills the *kinyan* slot; *get* fills the *dechiyah* slot. Each act operates on its own distinct portion of the *zikah*: *bechad tzad mahani bah get uvechad tzad mahani bah maimar*.

### Same vs. Different Acts

This fifty-percent model explains the full pattern of what does and does not carry effect. *Get achar get lo dachi* — a second *get* cannot do anything because the first *get* already addressed the *dechiyah* half; there is nothing remaining in that slot for the second to work on. *Maimar achar maimar lo kani* — a second *maimar* similarly finds the *kinyan* half already addressed. But *get achar maimar* and *maimar achar get* do carry meaning, because each act addresses the half that the other left untouched. The cross-combination works precisely because the two acts target distinct halves of the *zikah*.

### Partial Connection Effect

The fifty-percent model also explains *biah pesulah* in a way the binary model could not. When a *maimar* was performed with one *yevama*, the *yavam* has forged a half-connection to her — the *kinyan* half of the *zikah* has been engaged. When he then performs *biah* with a second *yevama*, this is not a *biah* that comes against the backdrop of either zero or a full prior relationship. There is a genuine, if partial, prior bond to the first *yevama* — the released half of the *zikah* remains unreleased. This unresolved partial connection makes the *biah* with the second *yevama* a *biah pesulah*: it is not the clean, first-in-time *biah* of a complete *yibum* situation. The relationship is therefore not treated as concluded, and subsequent acts retain halakhic significance. The intermediate state is not a conceptual fiction born of doubt — it is a real halakhic condition arising from the structure of the *zikah* itself.

The *Gemara* elaborates further: *biah pesulah* occupies an intermediate position — stronger than *maimar* in some respects, weaker in others. *Adifa mi'maimar* — stronger than *maimar* — in that if a second *maimar* follows the first, it is meaningless because it tries to fill an already-occupied slot; but *biah* after *maimar* does carry force because the *biah*, being *d'oraisa*, effectively dominates and causes us to view the situation as *biah* followed by *maimar* — a different pairing that can still be operative. *Gri'ah mi'maimar* — weaker than *maimar* — in that if *maimar* had filled its half of the *zikah*, a second *maimar* that follows would find nothing left; but because *biah* fills that slot in a weaker mode, the *maimar* that follows it remains operative rather than being treated as a redundant overlay.

---

## Biah Pesulah Doctrin (II)

### Chalitzah Efficacy

The fifty-percent model also clarifies why *chalitzah* that follows *maimar* or *get* remains fully valid and terminates the process. *Chalitzah* is not an act that targets one half of the *zikah* to the exclusion of the other; it is a Torah-level act of release that cuts through the entire bond. Whether the *kinyan* half or the *dechiyah* half has or has not been previously addressed at the rabbinic level, *chalitzah d'oraisa* dissolves whatever remains. There is therefore no basis for treating it as *chalitzah pesulah* — it accomplishes its full work regardless of what preceded it. The consequence is that once *chalitzah* has been performed, the process is genuinely complete: *ein achar chalitzah klum*. The *yavam* may need to address whatever rabbinic acts were done beforehand — for example, giving a *get* to the *yevama* with whom *maimar* was performed — but the *chalitzah* itself is unimpaired and conclusive.

---

## Chachamim's Response

### Individual Slots

The *chachamim* accept the basic architecture of the fifty-percent model — that every relationship between a *yavam* and a *yevama* contains a *kinyan* slot addressable by *maimar* and a *dechiyah* slot addressable by *get* — but they contest Rabban Gamliel's premise that these slots are shared across the entire class of *yevamot* and *yevamim*. The *chachamim* hold: *kol chad v'chad tikinu lei Rabbanan get u'maimar b'yevama* — the Rabbis established a *get* and a *maimar* for each individual brother-in-law with each individual *yevama*. Every *yavam*–*yevama* pairing has its own independent set of slots.

### Problematic Efficacy

This individualization means that even when one *yavam* has performed *maimar* with a particular *yevama* — filling the *kinyan* slot in their relationship — a second *yavam* can come and perform *maimar* with that same *yevama*, addressing *his* *kinyan* slot in *their* pairing, which is distinct. Similarly, a second *maimar* with a different *yevama* addresses that pairing's independent slot. The result is that what Rabban Gamliel treats as exhausted — because the class-wide slot has been filled — the *chachamim* treat as potentially still operative, because each pairing maintains its own capacity. This does not mean that subsequent acts confer rights or privileges upon the second *yavam*; rather, it means they generate halakhic complications, obligations, and prohibitions — the need for additional *gitin*, *chalitzot*, and so forth. Once these entanglements accumulate, the practical result is a complex web of relationships that must each be individually resolved. The *chachamim*'s disagreement with Rabban Gamliel is therefore not about whether the fifty-percent model is correct, but about whether the slots are pooled or individuated.

## Two Sisters, One Brother

### RG: Two Gets Rule

The *Gemara* now presents the practical cases from the *baraita* that illustrate the dispute. The case of two *yevamot* and one *yavam*: he gave a *get* to one and a *get* to the other — *natan get l'zo v'get l'zo*. Rabban Gamliel rules: *choletz l'rishonah v'asur b'krovoteha u'mutar b'krovot shniyah* — he does *chalitzah* with the first *yevama*, since the second *get* was entirely without effect, and he is forbidden to her relatives. With the second *yevama*, the *get* was meaningless and she carries no *grusha* status; he is therefore permitted to her relatives.

### Chachamim: Both Divorced

The *chachamim* rule differently: *natan get l'zo v'get l'zo asur b'krovot shteihen* — the second *get* also carries meaning, rendering her a quasi-*gerushah*, and he is forbidden to the relatives of both. Moreover, since both carry *grusha* status, *chalitzah l'achat mehen* — a *chalitzah* to either one suffices, as neither has a halakhic advantage over the other.

### Parallel: Two Brothers

The *baraita* notes the symmetric case: *v'chen ata omer b'shnei yevamim v'yevamah achat* — the same principles apply when two brothers-in-law each give a *get* to the same *yevama*. According to Rabban Gamliel, only the first *yavam*'s *get* carries effect; he is forbidden to her relatives. The second *yavam*'s *get* is without consequence; he remains permitted to her relatives. The *chachamim* hold that the second *yavam*'s *get* also carries effect, and he too is forbidden to her relatives.

### RG: Two Maimars Rule

Turning to the *maimar* cases: two *yevamot* and one *yavam*, and he performed *maimar* with both — *asa ma'amar b'zo u'ma'amar b'zo*. Rabban Gamliel rules: *noten get l'rishonah v'choletz lah v'asur b'krovoteha u'mutar b'krovot shniyah* — only the first *maimar* was operative, so he must give a *get* for the *maimar* and perform *chalitzah* with the first *yevama*, and *chalitzah* should not be done with the second so as not to impose *chalutzah* status upon her unnecessarily, since the second *maimar* was meaningless. He is forbidden to the first's relatives and permitted to the second's.

### Chachamim: Both Need Get

The *chachamim* rule: *noten get l'shteihen* — the second *maimar* was also operative, so both *yevamot* require a *get*. Because both carry quasi-*gerushah* status, he is forbidden to the relatives of both — *asur b'krovot shteihen* — and *chalitzah l'achat mehen* is sufficient. Again, the symmetric case of two brothers-in-law performing *maimar* with a single *yevama* follows the same logic in both directions.

## Chalitzah After Get

### Contradiction Setup

The *Gemara* raises a challenge from Shmuel's earlier ruling. Rabban Gamliel has said that in the case of two *gets* and two *yevamot*, one need only do *chalitzah* with the first *yevama* — *choletz l'rishonah* — and that is sufficient. But Shmuel had stated: *chalatz l'ba'alas haget lo nifterah tzarah* — if the *yavam* did *chalitzah* with the *yevama* who had received a *get*, the co-wife is *not* thereby released. Shmuel's position is that *chalitzah* following a *get* is a weakened *chalitzah* — something like *chalitzah pesulah* — because the *get* already diminished the *zikah* bond, and therefore this *chalitzah* does not exempt the co-wife; all co-wives must receive individual *chalitzahs*. This appears to flatly contradict Rabban Gamliel's ruling that one *chalitzah* to the first *yevama* suffices.

### Shmuel's Ruling

The *Gemara* resolves this by placing Shmuel's ruling in its proper doctrinal context. Shmuel himself explains: *ki amina ana aliba d'man d'amar yeish zikah* — when I said that *chalitzah* after *get* is weakened and requires multiple *chalitzahs*, I was speaking according to the view that there is a *zikah*, a genuine quasi-spousal bond connecting the *yevama* to the *yavam*. On that view, the *get* has already eroded the *zikah*, and the *chalitzah* that follows it is correspondingly diminished in force.

### RG: No Zikah

Rabban Gamliel, however, holds *ein zikah* — there is no *zikah*. While there is a *mitzvah* of *chalitzah* binding the *yevama* to the process, Rabban Gamliel does not recognize the category of a quasi-marital bond called *zikah* as such.

### Consequence: One Chalitz…

If there is no *zikah* to be weakened, then a *get* given to a *yevama* cannot diminish what does not exist. The *chalitzah* that follows the *get* is therefore not a weakened *chalitzah* — it is as full and effective as any other *chalitzah*. Consequently, Rabban Gamliel can consistently hold that one *chalitzah* with the first *yevama* suffices, with no need to repeat the process for the second. Shmuel's ruling of multiple *chalitzahs* and Rabban Gamliel's ruling of a single *chalitzah* reflect not a contradiction but the application of each position to its own foundational premise — *yeish zikah* versus *ein zikah* — and the two rulings are entirely coherent with one another.
