# Bava Metzia 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Arusah & Ketubah

### Problem Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ טַעְמָא דְּנִמְלַךְ שֶׁלֹּא לִתְּנָן, הָא אָמַר: ״תְּנוּ״ – נוֹתְנִין, וַאֲפִילּוּ לִזְמַן מְרוּבֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: It can be inferred from the mishna that the only **reason that** these documents are not returned is that there is a concern that the person obligated by the document **reconsidered with regard to them** and decided **not to deliver them. But** if the writer **says: Give** this found document to the intended recipient, the finder **must give** it to him. **And** since the mishna places no limitation on this, presumably this is the *halakha* **even** if **a long time** passed since it was lost, and there is no concern that perhaps the document belongs to someone else with the same name.

The daf opens in the middle of a broader discussion: can a husband claim *parati* — that he has already paid — against a standing obligation imposed by rabbinic legislation, a *ma'aseh beis din*? Specifically, the question at hand is whether the principle articulated by Rebbi Yochanan — that *ha-to'en achar ma'aseh beis din lo amar klum*, one who claims payment against a court-imposed obligation is not believed — can be proven from the case of a woman who collects her *kesuvah* even without holding a written *kesuvah* document, relying instead on her *get*.

Before returning to that larger argument, the *gemara* works through a subsidiary question: does a woman who was an *arusah* — that is, she had received *kiddushin* but had not yet proceeded to *nisuin*, she had received the ring but had not yet established a shared home or undergone the *chuppah* — have an entitlement to a *kesuvah* if her husband dies?

### Chiya bar Ami

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא, מִדְּתָנֵי רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אַמֵּי: אִשְׁתּוֹ אֲרוּסָה, לֹא אוֹנֵן וְלֹא מִטַּמֵּא לָהּ, וְכֵן הִיא לֹא אוֹנֶנֶת וְלֹא מִטַּמְּאָה לוֹ. מֵתָה – אֵינוֹ יוֹרְשָׁהּ, מֵת הוּא – גּוֹבָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** if one would say that the marriage contract of a betrothed woman is **instead** derived **from that which Rav Ḥiyya bar Ami teaches,** that is also difficult. He teaches: **One does not enter acute mourning** on the day of the death of **his betrothed wife, nor may he become ritually impure at her** funeral if she dies, if he is a priest; **and similarly, she does not enter acute mourning for him** if he dies, **and** she **may not become ritually impure at his** funeral. If **she dies, he does not inherit her** property. If **he dies, she collects** payment of **her marriage contract.**

The *gemara* cites a teaching of Rebbi Chiya bar Ami bearing on the status of the *arusah*. The teaching states: *ishto arusah lo onen velo mitameh lah* — if a man's wife is only an *arusah* and she dies, he does not become an *onen* and, if he is a *kohen*, he does not contract *tumah* on her behalf. This is because the verse *ki im lish'ero hakarov eilav* — his flesh that is close to him — implies that the full relationship of *basar echad* does not apply until they actually begin living together in *nisuin*. Similarly, *ve-chein hi* — the same applies in reverse: if he dies, she incurs none of the corresponding obligations. Furthermore, *meisah eino yorshah* — if she dies, he does not inherit her, since all the reciprocal rights of inheritance, support, and housing take effect only from *nisuin*.

Yet the teaching concludes: *meis hu gova kesuvasah* — if he dies, she collects her *kesuvah*. Here, then, is an explicit source that an *arusah* has a right to the *kesuvah*.

### Abyei Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְמָא דִּכְתַב לַהּ, וְכִי תֵּימָא: דִּכְתַב לַהּ, מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? מֵתָה אֵינוֹ יוֹרְשָׁהּ אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** If it is derived from here that a betrothed woman receives payment of a marriage contract, this is not proof, as **perhaps** this too is referring to a case **where he wrote** a marriage contract **for her. And if you would say that** if it is referring to a case where **he wrote her** a marriage contract, **what** is the purpose **of stating** this? One could answer that while this clause is obvious, **it was necessary for** Rav Ḥiyya bar Ami to state that conversely, if **she dies, he does not inherit her** property.

The *gemara* immediately raises a challenge: *dilma de-kasav lah* — perhaps the teaching speaks only of a case in which the husband explicitly wrote the *kesuvah* at the time of *erusin*, and without such explicit writing she would have no entitlement until *nisuin*. One might ask why the teaching would bother stating that she collects the *kesuvah* in such an obvious case. The answer is that the primary novelty (*chiddush*) of the passage is the ruling *meisah eino yorshah* — that he does not inherit her — and the *kesuvah* clause came along incidentally. So there is no proof that an *arusah* collects a *kesuvah* by force of rabbinic enactment alone, absent an explicit written document.

This matters greatly for the earlier discussion. Abaye had sought to push aside the proof for Rebbi Yochanan's principle by arguing that the case of the woman collecting based on her *get* — in a locale where a *kesuvah* is not normally written — is not evidence of *ma'aseh beis din* at all. Rather, in such a locale the *get* itself functions as a *kesuvah*. The woman holds a *shtar*, and one is never believed to say *parati* against a *shtar* in the creditor's possession. The *ma'aseh beis din* principle need not be invoked.

### Get as Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אַבָּיֵי מִגּוּפַהּ דְּמַתְנִיתִין קָא הָדַר בֵּיהּ, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין כּוֹתְבִין כְּתוּבָה עָסְקִינַן, דְּגֵט הַיְינוּ כְּתוּבְּתַהּ, אַטּוּ גֵּט מָנֶה מָאתַיִם כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye retracted** his objection to Rabbi Yoḥanan's proof from the mishna, not because of the case of a widow from betrothal, but due to an indication **from** within **the mishna itself. Because if it enters your mind** that **we are dealing with a place where they do not write a marriage contract, where** a woman's **bill of divorce is** effectively **her marriage contract,** and therefore she can use her bill of divorce to collect payment of her marriage contract, that does not make sense; **is it written in a bill of divorce** that the husband is liable to pay the wife the **one hundred** or **two hundred dinars** she is owed? In fact, this is not written in a bill of divorce.

The *gemara* now sets out to demonstrate why Abaye ultimately abandoned this line of reasoning — not on account of anything external, but *mi-gufei de-masnisin*, from the internal logic of the *mishnah* itself. The *gemara* poses the question: if the *get* is to function as a *kesuvah* in a locale where no *kesuvah* is written, what does it actually prove?

*Atu get maneh masayim kesiv bei?* — Does the *get* state the amount? Does it say whether the husband owed 100 or 200 *zuz*, or whether he had added a *tosefes*? Obviously not. The *get* alone cannot establish the specifics of the debt. The only way the amount can be known is by invoking the standing rabbinic obligation — the *ma'aseh beis din* — which sets the presumptive amounts.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: כֵּיוָן דְּתַקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן לְמִגְבֵּא (לַהּ) [בֵּיהּ] כְּמַאן דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ דָּמֵי, לִטְעוֹן וְלֵימָא פָּרַעְתִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** even **if you would say** that **since the Sages instituted** that **she** use the bill of divorce **to collect** her marriage contract, **it is considered as though** the liability of the husband to pay one hundred or two hundred dinars **is written in it,** and it would still be problematic to say that the bill of divorce is sufficient for her to collect payment. The husband **should** still be able to **claim** that he is exempt, **and say: I** already **paid** it.

One might respond: *keivan de-takinu rabanan le-migba lei, ke-man de-kasav lah dami* — since the Rabbis enacted that she collects, it is as if the amount were written, and thus the *get* can be treated as a *shtar* attesting to that known amount. If so, the husband should not be believed to say *parati* because he is contradicted by a *shtar* she holds.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא דְּאָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: אִי פְּרַעְתַּהּ אִיבְּעִי לָךְ לְמִיקְרְעֵיהּ, אָמַר לַן: לָא שְׁבַקְתַּן, אָמְרָה: בָּעֵינָא לְאִנְּסוֹבֵי בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say that** if the husband would state such a claim, **we would say to him: If,** in fact, **you paid her, you should have torn up** the bill of divorce, and **he could** respond and **say to us: She did not allow** me to tear it up, because **she said: I need** the bill of divorce **to remarry, by** using **it** as proof that I am divorced.

But the *gemara* presses further: *ve-leisima lei parati* — why shouldn't he be believed? Ordinarily, when a debtor says he has paid and the creditor still holds the *shtar*, the debtor is not believed because if he had truly paid, he should have insisted on tearing up the *shtar*. But here, the *get* cannot simply be torn, because the woman needs it intact to prove she is divorced and to remarry. As Ravina notes, *linsuvei beih* — she needs the *get* to be whole. So the husband has a ready answer: I paid, but I could not take back or destroy the *get* because she would not permit it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא אָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: אִיבְּעִי לָךְ לְמִיקְרְעֵיהּ וּמִכְתַּב אַגַּבֵּיהּ, גִּיטָּא דְּנַן דִּקְרַעְנוּהּו לָא מִשּׁוּם דְּגִיטָּא פְּסוּלָה הוּא, אֶלָּא כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא תִּגְבֵּי בֵּיהּ זִמְנָא אַחֲרִיתִי. אַטּוּ כֹּל דְּמַגְבֵּי, בְּבֵי דִינָא מַגְבֵּי?
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that **we would** then **say to him: You should have torn up** the bill of divorce **and written on the back of it:** The reason **that we tore up this bill of divorce is not because it is an invalid bill of divorce,** but **rather** it is **in order that** the woman **not collect** payment of her marriage contract **again with it,** this suggestion is not always applicable. **Does everyone who collects** payment of a marriage contract **collect** payment **in court,** where it is possible to write such a legal statement? Therefore, the suggestion that a bill of divorce serves as a marriage contract remains untenable. This leads to the conclusion that the basis for collecting payment of a marriage contract where such a document does not exist must be a court enactment, in accordance with the interpretation of Rabbi Yoḥanan.

The *gemara* counters: could he not have torn it and had the *beis din* write on the back that *ketivat dena de-karnuhu* — this *get* was torn not because it is invalid but so that she cannot use it to collect her *kesuvah* a second time? The *gemara* then disposes of this: *atu kol de-migbi be-vei dina migbi* — does every payment of a debt happen in front of a *beis din*? She approaches him, he pays her the 200 *zuz*, there is no *beis din* present, she will not agree to tear it, and there is nothing he can do. He therefore has a perfectly plausible explanation for why she still holds the *get* even after payment.

The upshot is decisive. If the reason the husband is not believed were merely that she holds a *shtar*, there would be a legitimate counter-argument — he could plausibly claim payment despite her holding the *get*. The fact that he is nonetheless not believed forces the conclusion that it is the *ma'aseh beis din* itself — the standing court-imposed obligation of *kesuvah* — that strips him of his credibility. This is the proof for Rebbi Yochanan's principle.

As a practical postscript: while the specific cases discussed above did not yield a proof for the *arusah's* entitlement, the *halacha* is nonetheless that an *arusah* does collect a base *kesuvah*, though she does not receive any *tosefes* unless it was explicitly written.

## Parati & Ma'aseh

### Get vs. Shtar

The *gemara* has now established, through the process of elimination described above, that the husband's inability to claim *parati* against the *kesuvah* obligation is rooted in the nature of *ma'aseh beis din* — a court-imposed obligation that cannot be waived by a unilateral claim of payment. The *get*, it turns out, cannot serve as a functional *shtar* for these purposes, because it does not contain the amount and because the husband has a ready excuse for why she still holds it even after payment.

### Tearing Get

The *gemara* had suggested a possible mechanism: have the *get* torn and have the *beis din* write on its back a notation clarifying that the tearing does not render the *get* invalid, but is done solely to prevent its reuse for collection of the *kesuvah*. This would eliminate the husband's excuse and allow the *get* to function as a *shtar* whose continued possession by the wife would preclude his claim of *parati*.

### Practical Issue

This theoretical solution founders on a practical point: *atu kol de-migbi be-vei dina migbi* — not every debt is paid in the presence of a *beis din*. The payment of a *kesuvah* commonly takes place directly between husband and wife. In such a setting, the wife has no obligation to surrender the *get*, the *beis din* is not present to supervise any notation, and the husband has no enforceable recourse. His continued inability to demand the tearing of the *get* therefore constitutes a genuine and plausible excuse. This excuse does not exist in the case of ordinary *shtarot*, where the creditor has no independent reason to retain the document.

### Conclusion

The conclusion that emerges from this entire analysis is that the principle of Rebbi Yochanan stands confirmed: one who owes an obligation imposed by *ma'aseh beis din* and claims to have paid it is not believed, and the inability to claim *parati* in the *get* case is attributable to that principle rather than to the *get's* functioning as a *shtar*. The *get* does not contain the amount owed, and the husband retains a plausible excuse for its non-return — two features that prevent it from operating as a conventional *shtar*. Thus the woman's ability to collect her *kesuvah* even without a written document, and the husband's inability to counter with *parati*, derives from the *ma'aseh beis din* itself.

## Found Documents

### Don't Return

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מָצָא גִּיטֵּי נָשִׁים וְשִׁחְרוּרֵי עֲבָדִים, דְּיָיתֵיקֵי, מַתָּנָה, וְשׁוֹבָרִין – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יַחֲזִיר. שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: כְּתוּבִין הָיוּ, וְנִמְלַךְ עֲלֵיהֶן שֶׁלֹּא לִתְּנָן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one found bills of divorce, or** bills of **manumission of slaves,** or **wills,** or deeds of **a gift, or receipts, he may not return** these items to the one who is presumed to have lost them, **as I say** it is possible that **they were written and** then the writer **reconsidered about them** and decided **not to deliver them.**

The *mishnah* now turns to a different but related set of cases involving found documents. *Motzei gittei nashim ve-shichrurei avadim* — one who finds a *get* for a woman, a writ of manumission freeing a slave, a *diatiki* (a deathbed declaration distributing one's property, akin to a will), a *matanah* (a gift document transferring a parcel of land), or a *shover* (a receipt acknowledging that a debt was paid, issued when the creditor did not have the original *shtar* to return). In all these cases: *harei zeh lo yachzir* — do not return the document to the intended beneficiary. Do not give the *get* to the wife, do not give the manumission to the slave, do not give the gift document to the recipient, do not give the receipt to the debtor.

### Changed Mind

The reason for this rule is: *she-ani omer ketuvin hayu* — perhaps these documents were written but then *venimlach aleihen she-lo litnam* — the writer changed his mind and decided not to deliver them. Because we do not know whether the document fell from the writer before delivery or from the intended recipient after receipt, and because the concern that the writer changed his mind is real, we cannot assume the document was actually given. We therefore hold it rather than completing a delivery that may never have been intended.

### Explicit Consent

The *gemara* draws an inference from the *mishnah*: *ta'ama de-nimlach she-lo litnam* — the stated concern is that the writer changed his mind. This implies *ha amar tenu, nosnin* — that if the writer explicitly says "give it," we do give it, because the only reason we were holding back was the concern about a change of mind, and that concern is now resolved. Moreover, this would apply *va-afilu l'zman merubeh* — even if the document was found long after it was lost, because once the writer affirms his intention, the concern dissipates regardless of the elapsed time.

## Lost Get Problem

### Braita Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: הַמֵּבִיא גֵּט, וְאָבַד הֵימֶנּוּ – מְצָאוֹ לְאַלְתַּר כָּשֵׁר, אִם לָאו – פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a mishna (*Gittin* 27a): With regard to an agent **who was bringing a bill of divorce** to a woman, **and it was lost by him,** if he **found it immediately,** the bill of divorce is still **valid. If not,** then it is **not valid,** as it is possible that the bill of divorce that he found is not the same one that he lost, and this second bill of divorce belongs to someone else whose name and wife's name are identical to the names of the husband and wife in the lost bill of divorce.

This inference, however, generates a difficulty when compared with another source. A *braita* in *Gittin* states: *ha-meivi get ve-avad heimenu* — a *shaliach* who is conveying a *get* and loses it — *matza'o l'altar kasher ve-im lav pasul* — if he finds it immediately, it is valid; if not, it is invalid. The reason is that if time has passed, we fear that the *get* he found is not the same one he lost but rather belongs to a different couple who happen to share the same names.

### Contradiction

This *braita* stands in apparent contradiction to our *mishnah*. The *mishnah* implies that if the husband says "give it," you give it even after a long time. But the *braita* says that after a significant delay, the *get* is invalid — we are concerned it is a different *get* with the same names. If so, even if the husband affirms he wants to divorce his wife, how can we give this *get* when we cannot be sure it is his?

### Rava's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן – בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁהַשְּׁיָירוֹת מְצוּיוֹת, כָּאן – בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין הַשַּׁיָּירוֹת מְצוּיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba says:** This is **not difficult,** because **there,** in tractate *Gittin*, the mishna is stated **with regard to a place where caravans** passing through **are common,** and there is a concern that the found bill of divorce belongs to someone else with the identical name. By contrast, the mishna **here** is stated **with regard to a place where caravans** passing through **are uncommon,** so there is no such concern.

Rava resolves the contradiction: *kan be-makom she-shayarot metzuyot, kan be-makom she-ein shayarot metzuyot* — the two sources address different situations. When caravans are common in the area — when it is a busy thoroughfare through which travelers from many locales constantly pass — there is a meaningful concern that the found *get* might belong to a different couple from elsewhere who share the same names. The *braita* that invalidates a *get* found after a delay speaks of such a location. The *mishnah*, which implies that you give the *get* upon the husband's say-so even if time has passed, speaks of a location where caravans are not common. In such a place, there is no realistic concern that another couple with identical names happened to leave a *get* in the same area.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁהַשְּׁיָירוֹת מְצוּיוֹת – וְהוּא שֶׁהוּחְזְקוּ שְׁנֵי יוֹסֵף בֶּן שִׁמְעוֹן בְּעִיר אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And even in a place where caravans** passing through **are common,** there is not always a concern that the bill of divorce may belong to another man with an identical name, **and** this concern **is** only **where** it **has been established** that there are **two** men named, for example, **Yosef ben Shimon in** that **one city.**

The *gemara* then introduces an additional restriction. Even in a place where caravans are common, Rava adds a second criterion: *ve-hu she-huchzeku shnei Yosef ben Shimon be-ir achat* — we are only genuinely concerned that the found *get* is a different one if it is also known that two couples with the same names reside in that same city. A *get* always specifies the city, so if it says Yosef ben Shimon of this city divorces his wife Sarah bas Rachel, the concern that it belongs to a different couple only arises concretely if we know that such a second couple exists in that city. Without that knowledge, the mere fact of a busy thoroughfare does not suffice to disqualify the *get*.

## Rava's Criteria

### Rav Huna Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, קַשְׁיָא דְּרַבָּה אַדְּרַבָּה. דְּהָהוּא גִּיטָּא דְּאִשְׁתְּכַח בֵּי דִינָא דְּרַב הוּנָא, דַּהֲוָה כְּתִוב בֵּיהּ בִּשְׁוִירֵי מָתָא דְּעַל רָכִיס נַהֲרָא. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: חָיְישִׁינַן לִשְׁנֵי שְׁוִירֵי, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא לְרַבָּה: פּוֹק עַיֵּין בָּהּ, דִּלְאוּרְתָּא בָּעֵי מִינָּךְ רַב הוּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **As, if you do not say so,** that this concern is taken into account only in a place where it is known that there are two people with this same name, then there is a **difficulty** presented in the form of a contradiction between this statement **of Rabba and** another statement **of Rabba. As** there was **a certain bill of divorce that was found** in **the court of Rav Huna, in which it was written** that the bill of divorce was written **in Sheviri City, which** is located **on the Rakhis River. Rav Huna said** about this: **We are concerned about** the possibility that there are **two** cities named **Sheviri** and that this bill of divorce may belong to someone else who lives in the other Sheviri, and therefore it should not be returned. **And Rav Ḥisda said to Rabba** about this issue: **Go out** and **examine** this *halakha*,

The two-criterion requirement attributed to Rava emerges from a specific episode. A *get* was found in the *beis din* of Rav Huna. The *get* bore the notation that it was written in the city of Shviri situated on the river Rechis. A *shaliach* came forward claiming it was the *get* he had lost. Rav Huna ruled *chashinan le-shnei Shviri* — we are concerned that there may be another city also called Shviri situated on the same river, with a couple bearing the same names. He therefore declined to return the *get* to the *shaliach*.

### Chisda's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְפַק, דַּק וְאַשְׁכַּח. דִּתְנַן: כׇּל מַעֲשֵׂה בֵּית דִּין – הֲרֵי זֶה יַחְזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **as in the evening Rav Huna will ask you** about it. **He went out, examined** it, **and discovered** a relevant source, **as we learned** in a mishna (20a): **One must return any court enactment,** i.e., a promissory note that has been authenticated by the court, to its owner. Since the bill of divorce was found in the court, it is in this category and must be returned.

Rav Chisda instructed Rava to go and research the matter, as Rav Huna would need an answer by evening. In Rav Huna's case, it was known that there were not two couples with the same names within the same city — yet Rav Huna was still concerned, on account of the possibility of an entirely different city by the same name. This suggests that *shayarot metzuyot* — the presence of a busy thoroughfare — alone is sufficient reason not to return, without needing to establish the presence of duplicate names in the same locale.

### Ma'aseh Beis Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא בֵּי דִינָא דְּרַב הוּנָא, דְּכִי מְקוֹם שֶׁהַשְּׁיָירוֹת מְצוּיוֹת דָּמֵי, וְקָא פָּשֵׁיט רַבָּה דְּיַחְזִיר, אַלְמָא אִי הוּחְזְקוּ שְׁנֵי יוֹסֵף בֶּן שִׁמְעוֹן – אִין, אִי לָא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes its proof that even in a place where passing caravans are common, the concern that the bill of divorce belongs to another couple applies only if it is known that there is another couple in the same locale with the same names as those written in the bill of divorce: **And the court of Rav Huna is comparable to a place where** passing **caravans are common,** as many people from different places pass through for judgment. **And** yet, **Rabba resolved that** if one finds a bill of divorce there, **he should return** it. **Evidently** he holds that **if** it is **established** that there are **two** people named **Yosef ben Shimon** in the city, then there is **indeed** a concern and the document should not be returned, but **if not,** there is **no** concern.

Rava went and researched, and found the following passage in the *mishnah* of this very *perek*: *kol ma'aseh beis din harei zeh yachzir* — anything that has undergone court processing, such as a *shtar chov* whose signatures were validated by the *beis din*, is returned to the *malveh*. Rava understood this *mishnah* as addressing a document found in the courthouse itself. And a courthouse, he reasoned, is precisely the kind of place that qualifies as *makom she-shayarot metzuyot* — a place where people are constantly coming and going from many different locations. Yet the *mishnah* says to return it. This implies that *shayarot metzuyot* alone is not sufficient to withhold the document.

### Two Criteria

From this, Rava concluded that only when both conditions are met — *shayarot metzuyot* and known duplicate names — do we decline to return the document. If only one condition is present, we return it. This explains his ruling in the Rav Huna case as well: Rava told Rav Huna that the *get* should be returned, since it was established that there were not two couples with the same names in that city, and the *ma'aseh beis din* precedent demonstrated that *shayarot metzuyot* alone does not require withholding.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲבַד רַבָּה עוֹבָדָא בְּהָהוּא גִּיטָּא דְּאִשְׁתְּכַח בֵּי כִיתָּנָא דְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא כִּשְׁמַעְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rabba performed an action,** i.e., issued a practical ruling, **with regard to a certain bill of divorce that was found** in **a flax house in** the city of **Pumbedita, in accordance with his *halakha*,** and he instructed that the bill of divorce should be returned.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: הֵיכָא דִּמְזַבְּנִי כִּיתָּנָא וְהוּא שֶׁלֹּא הוּחְזְקוּ, אַף עַל גַּב דִּשְׁכִיחָן שְׁיָירָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** There is disagreement as to the exact details of the case. **There are** those **who say** that this occurred in the place **where** people **sell flax, and** it is **specifically** because it **was not established** that two couples with the same names lived in the city where the bill of divorce was written that Rabba ruled that the bill of divorce should be returned **despite** the fact **that** passing **caravans are common** there.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: הֵיכָא דְּתָרוּ כִּיתָּנָא וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּהוּחְזְקוּ, דְּלָא (שְׁכִיחָא שְׁיָירוֹת).
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** those **who say** that it occurred in the place **where** people **soak flax, and** he ruled that the bill of divorce should be returned **even though it was established** that there were two couples with the same names living in the city where the bill of divorce was written, **as** passing **caravans are uncommon** there.

The *gemara* records that Rava acted on this *halacha* in practice in the flax district of Pumbedita. One version of the story holds that *kitna* refers to the marketplace where flax is sold — a busy, high-traffic location — and Rava returned the *get* because there was no known duplication of names. A second version holds that *kitna* refers to the industrial area where flax is soaked, a quiet location where caravans are not common, and Rava returned the *get* because despite a known duplication of names, *shayarot metzuyot* were absent. Both versions confirm the two-criterion framework: the absence of either criterion is sufficient to permit return.

## R. Zeira's Q

### Zeira's Framing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי זֵירָא רָמֵי מַתְנִיתִין אַבָּרַיְיתָא וּמְשַׁנֵּי. תְּנַן: הַמֵּבִיא גֵּט וְאָבַד הֵימֶנּוּ, מְצָאוֹ לְאַלְתַּר – כָּשֵׁר, וְאִם לָאו – פָּסוּל. וּרְמִינְהִי: מָצָא גֵּט אִשָּׁה בַּשּׁוּק, בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַבַּעַל מוֹדֶה – יַחְזִיר לְאִשָּׁה. אֵין הַבַּעַל מוֹדֶה – לֹא יַחְזִיר לֹא לָזֶה וְלֹא לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rabbi Zeira raises a contradiction between the mishna and a *baraita*, and he resolves** the contradiction employing the same distinction. **We learned** in the mishna: With regard to an agent **who was bringing a bill of divorce** to a woman **and he lost it,** if **he found it immediately,** the bill of divorce is still **valid, but if not, it is not valid. And** Rabbi Zeira **raises a contradiction** between this mishna and a *baraita* that states: If one **found a woman's bill of divorce in the marketplace,** in a case **when the husband admits** that he wrote and gave it to the wife, the finder **must return** it **to the wife;** but if **the husband does not admit** to this, **he may return** it **neither to this one,** the husband, **nor to that one,** the wife.

Rabbi Zeira raises a parallel question, though he frames it differently from Rava. Rather than contrasting two *mishnayot*, he contrasts a *mishnah* with a *braita*. The *mishnah* in *Gittin* — the same one Rava cited — states that if a *shaliach* loses a *get* and finds it only after a significant delay, the *get* is invalid, because we fear it may be someone else's. Rabbi Zeira then cites a *braita* in *Gittin*: *matza get isha ba-shuk* — if one finds a woman's *get* in the marketplace — *be-zman she-ha-baal modeh yachzir le-isha* — if the husband acknowledges that he had given it, you return it to the wife; *ein ha-baal modeh lo yachzir lo le-zeh ve-lo le-zeh* — if he does not acknowledge it, you give it to neither party.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַבַּעַל מוֹדֶה – יַחְזִיר לָאִשָּׁה, וַאֲפִילּוּ לִזְמַן מְרוּבֶּה!
>
> **Translation:** **In any event,** the *baraita* **teaches** that in a case **when the husband admits** that he wrote it, the finder **must return** it **to the wife, and** this is the *halakha* **even** if it was found **after a long time.**

The *braita* thus states categorically that if the husband acknowledges it, the *get* is returned to the wife — and this is *va-afilu l'zman merubeh*, presumably even after a substantial delay. But this seems to contradict the *mishnah* that says a *get* found after a delay is invalid because we fear it is a different one. Why, then, does the *braita* permit return simply because the husband acknowledges it?

### Zeira's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְשַׁנֵּי: כָּאן בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁהַשְּׁיָירוֹת מְצוּיוֹת, וְכָאן בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין הַשְּׁיָירוֹת מְצוּיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rabbi Zeira **answers** that **here,** in the case of the mishna, the bill of divorce is valid only if it is found immediately, as it is a case where it is found **in a place where** passing **caravans are common. And there,** in the *baraita*, the bill of divorce can be returned even if it was found after a long time, as it is a case where it is found **in a place where** passing **caravans are uncommon.**

Rabbi Zeira resolves the tension exactly as Rava did: *kan be-makom she-shayarot metzuyot, kan be-makom she-ein shayarot metzuyot* — the *mishnah* that invalidates the *get* found after a delay addresses a location with heavy caravan traffic, where the concern for duplicate names is realistic. The *braita* that permits return upon the husband's acknowledgment addresses a location without such traffic, where no such concern applies.

### One vs Two

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: וְהוּא שֶׁהוּחְזְקוּ דְּלָא נַהְדַּר, וְהַיְינוּ דְּרַבָּה. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא הוּחְזְקוּ לָא נַהְדַּר, וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara compares the rulings of Rabba and Rabbi Zeira. **There are** those **who say,** with regard to Rabbi Zeira's statement that the finder should not return the bill of divorce in a place where passing caravans are common: **And** this applies **specifically** in a case **where** it is **established** that there are two couples in the town with the same names. In that case, Rabbi Zeira holds **that** the bill of divorce **should not be returned, and this is** the same ruling as that **of Rabba. And there are** those **who say:** In a place where passing caravans are common, **even if** it is **not established** that there are two couples with the same names, the bill of divorce **should not be returned, and** Rabbi Zeira **disagrees with** the ruling **of Rabba.**

At this point the *gemara* presents an internal dispute regarding Rabbi Zeira's position. One opinion holds that Rabbi Zeira, like Rava, requires both conditions — *shayarot metzuyot* and known duplication of names — before declining to return the *get*: *ve-hu she-huchzaku de-lo nehadar* — it is only when both criteria are present that we do not return it, and this is identical to Rava's view. A second opinion holds that Rabbi Zeira requires only one criterion: the presence of *shayarot metzuyot* alone suffices to withhold the *get*, even without established knowledge of a second couple bearing the same names. On this reading, Rabbi Zeira and Rava disagree: Rava demands both conditions; Rabbi Zeira demands only one.

## Rava vs Zeira

### Rava's Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא רַבָּה לָא אָמַר כְּרַבִּי זֵירָא, מַתְנִיתִין אַלִּימָא לֵיהּ לְאַקְשׁוֹיֵי. אֶלָּא רַבִּי זֵירָא, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּרַבָּה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, Rabba does not state** his explanation **in accordance with** that of **Rabbi Zeira** and raise a contradiction from the *baraita*, as **he** holds that **a mishna** serves as **a stronger** basis **for raising a difficulty** than a *baraita*, as the Mishna, redacted by Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, employs more precise language; **but what is the reason** that **Rabbi Zeira does not state** his explanation **in accordance with** that of **Rabba** and raise a contradiction from the Mishna?

The *gemara* asks: given that Rava and Rabbi Zeira present essentially the same logical difficulty and arrive at the same structural resolution, why did each scholar frame his question the way he did? Rava contrasted two *mishnayot*; Rabbi Zeira contrasted a *mishnah* with a *braita*. The first part of the answer is straightforward: *matnitin alima leh le-akshuyei* — Rava preferred to draw his contradiction from two *mishnayot*, since they carry equal authority and are presumed to reflect a unified *halachic* stance. A contradiction between two *stam mishna* formulations is stronger and more compelling than one between a *mishnah* and a *braita*.

### Zeira's Rebuttal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ, מִי קָא תָנֵי: הָא אָמַר ״תְּנוּ״ – נוֹתְנִין וַאֲפִילּוּ לִזְמַן מְרוּבֶּה? דִּלְמָא הָא אָמַר ״תְּנוּ״ – נוֹתְנִין, וּלְעוֹלָם כִּדְקַיְימָא לַן לְאַלְתַּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Zeira could have **said to you: Does** the mishna actually **teach that** if the one who wrote the document **says: Give** it to the intended recipient, the finder **must give** it to him, **and** that this is the *halakha* **even** if **a long time** passed since it was lost? This was only an inference from the mishna. **Perhaps** the mishna merely means to indicate **that** if the writer **says: Give** it to the intended recipient, the finder **must give** it to him, **but actually,** this is to be understood **as we maintain** in the mishna in *Gittin*, that this *halakha* applies only if the document was found **immediately.** Therefore, Rabbi Zeira posed his question from the *baraita*.

Now the *gemara* asks from the other direction: why did Rabbi Zeira not use the same source Rava did — namely, our *mishnah* here in Bava Metzia, which implies that if the husband says to give the *get*, one would give it? Why did he instead bring the *braita*? The answer is that our *mishnah's* implication is less than ironclad. The *mishnah* says that the reason we do not deliver a found *get* to the intended party is the concern that the writer changed his mind — implying that if he had not changed his mind, we would give it. But this implication does not necessarily extend to a *get* found after a long delay.

One might think our *mishnah* implies: if the husband says give it, we give it *va-afilu l'zman merubeh*, even after a long time. But Rabbi Zeira would say: *dilma ha-amar tenunu u-le'olam ke-dikayma lan le-altar* — perhaps the *mishnah* only implies that if the husband says give it and the *get* was found immediately, then yes, you give it. But if found after a delay, perhaps even the husband's word is insufficient, and the *mishnah* simply says nothing about the case of late discovery. The *mishnah's* implication is limited to the case before it; it does not affirmatively rule on the delayed-discovery scenario.

### Explicit vs Implied

The *braita*, by contrast, makes an explicit and categorical statement: *be-zman she-ha-baal modeh yachzir le-isha* — if the husband acknowledges it, you give it to the wife, with no qualification regarding timing. If this were only true when the *get* is found immediately, the *braita* would have said so. An unqualified categorical statement implies that the rule holds in all cases, including after a long delay. It is therefore this explicit, unqualified ruling that creates the genuine tension with the *mishnah* in *Gittin*, and that is why Rabbi Zeira preferred to frame his question around the *braita*.

## No Return Rule

### R. Yirmeyah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אָמַר, כְּגוֹן דְּקָא אָמְרִי עֵדִים: מֵעוֹלָם לֹא חָתַמְנוּ אֶלָּא עַל גֵּט אֶחָד שֶׁל יוֹסֵף בֶּן שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya states** an alternative resolution to the contradiction between the mishna here and the *baraita*, on the one hand, and the mishna in *Gittin* on the other: A found bill of divorce should be returned only in a case **where** the **witnesses** who signed the bill of divorce **say: We have never signed a bill of divorce of** a person named **Yosef ben Shimon other than** this **one,** in which case there is no concern that the bill of divorce belongs to someone else.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא לֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא אִתְרְמִי שְׁמָא כִּשְׁמָא, וְעֵדִים כְּעֵדִים, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so, what** is the purpose **of stating** that one returns the bill of divorce? Since it clearly belongs to him, there is no question that it must be returned to him. The Gemara answers that it is necessary **lest you say** that **one should be concerned** that **perhaps it happened** that another bill of divorce was written in which **the names** of the husband and the wife **are identical to the names** of the husband and wife of the second bill of divorce, **and** the names of the **witnesses** on that bill of divorce **are identical to** the names of the **witnesses** on this bill of divorce, when in fact they are different witnesses. To counter this, the mishna **teaches us** that this is not a concern.

The *gemara* now presents positions that move in the opposite direction entirely. Rather than asking what special circumstances are required before we *withhold* the *get*, these authorities ask what special circumstances are required before we *return* it. Rabbi Yirmeya states that the case in which the *braita* says to return the *get* to the wife — when the husband acknowledges it — is not the rule but the exception. The rule is that we never return it; we are always concerned, even in a location without caravans and even without known duplicate names. The special case that permits return is when the witnesses signed on the *get* testify: *me'olam lo chasamnu ela al get echad she-yatza bein Shimon* — we have never signed a *get* between these parties other than this one. Since the witnesses affirm that only a single such *get* exists in the world, the concern that the found document belongs to someone else is entirely eliminated.

One might ask: if the witnesses say this, it is obvious the *get* should be returned — what *chiddush* does this add? The *gemara* explains: one might have thought to remain concerned that there exists a second couple bearing exactly the same names as the couple named in the *get* and also signed by witnesses bearing exactly the same names. The *gemara* teaches that we do not extend our concern to such a remote possibility. Rabbi Yirmeya's position is thus at the far end of the spectrum: return requires positive affirmative evidence, and absent such evidence the default is not to return.

### R. Ashi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר, כְּגוֹן דְּקָא אָמַר: נֶקֶב יֵשׁ בּוֹ בְּצַד אוֹת פְּלוֹנִית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi stated** another resolution to the contradiction: The bill of divorce should be returned only in a case **where** the person claiming to have lost it provides a clear-cut distinguishing mark, e.g., **he says: There is a hole in** the bill of divorce **next to such and such a letter.**

Rav Ashi takes yet another approach to the special case that permits return. He says the case is one where the *shaliach* who lost the *get* provides a *siman* — an identifying mark: *nekev yesh bo be-tzad os pelonis* — there is a hole in the *get* adjacent to a specific letter. He is able to describe precisely where the hole is located, and upon examination, the found *get* matches that description.

### Siman Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדַוְקָא בְּצַד אוֹת פְּלוֹנִית, אֲבָל נֶקֶב בְּעָלְמָא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** Rav Ashi permits one to return such a bill of divorce **specifically** when the one claiming to have lost it says that the hole is **next to such and such a letter,** as that is a clear-cut distinguishing mark. **But** if he said **only** that it had **a hole** without mentioning its precise location, one should **not** return the bill of divorce, as that is not considered a clear-cut distinguishing mark.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ סִימָנִים אִי דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא אִי דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rav Ashi is uncertain whether** a lost item is returned to its owner on the basis of **distinguishing marks by Torah law** or **whether** it is **by rabbinic law.** Therefore, in the case of a bill of divorce, he holds that one may rely only on a clear-cut distinguishing mark, as everyone agrees that a lost item is returned to its owner on the basis of a clear-cut distinguishing mark by Torah law.

The *gemara* specifies: *davka be-tzad os pelonis* — only if the *shaliach* is specific about which letter the hole is next to. *Aval nekev be-alma lo* — if he merely says there is a hole somewhere in the document, that does not suffice. A generic hole is not meaningful evidence; many *gittin* may have holes, and this does not prove that the found *get* is his.

This reflects the distinction between a *siman muvhak* — an identifying mark so specific and unusual that it effectively constitutes definitive identification — and a *siman she-eino muvhak* — a mark that narrows the field but does not constitute conclusive proof. Why does Rav Ashi require specifically a *siman muvhak*? Because *Rav Ashi mesafeka lei simanin de-Oraisa o de-Rabanan* — Rav Ashi was uncertain whether a non-definitive *siman* carries *de-Oraisa* validity or only *de-Rabanan* authority. If it is only *de-Rabanan*, then while it may suffice for monetary questions such as identifying a lost object (*metziah*) — where rabbinic enactments carry broad latitude — it would not suffice for a *get*, which determines the personal status (*issur ve-hetter*) of the woman and her eligibility to remarry. Since Rav Ashi could not resolve this question, he would only permit return of the *get* on the basis of a *siman muvhak*, which is strong enough to satisfy even a *de-Oraisa* standard.

The positions thus divide into two camps. Rava and Rabbi Zeira hold that the default is to return a found *get* when the husband affirms his intent, and we withhold it only when special circumstances — one or two criteria, depending on the version of Rabbi Zeira's view — create a genuine concern that it may be someone else's *get*. Rabbi Yirmeya and Rav Ashi hold the opposite: the default is not to return the found *get*, and return requires positive special evidence — either the testimony of the *eidim* that only one such *get* exists, or a *siman muvhak* from the *shaliach*.
