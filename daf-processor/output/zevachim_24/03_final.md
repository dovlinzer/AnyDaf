# Zevachim 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Standing During Service

### Standing Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹשֵׁב מְנָלַן? אָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, אָמַר קְרָא: ״לַעֲמֹד לְשָׁרֵת״ – לַעֲמִידָה בְּחַרְתִּיו, וְלֹא לִישִׁיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? **Rava says** that **Rav Naḥman says: The verse states** with regard to the priests: "For the Lord your God has chosen him out of all your tribes, **to stand to minister"** (Deuteronomy 18:5). The verse indicates that **I have chosen him for standing and not for sitting.**

The Gemara on 23b opens with a discussion of *yoshev* — sitting — as one of the conditions that invalidates the *avodah*. The question posed is *minalan*: what is the scriptural basis for this rule?

### Scriptural Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לַעֲמֹד לְשָׁרֵת״ – מִצְוָה; כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״הָעֹמְדִים״ – שָׁנָה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב לְעַכֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: "To stand to minister,"** indicates that there is **a mitzva** to perform the service while standing. **When it says:** "Then he shall minister in the name of the Lord his God, as all his brethren the Levites do, **who stand** there before the Lord" (Deuteronomy 18:7), **the verse repeats** the matter **to invalidate** rites that are performed while not standing.

Rav Nachman, cited by Rava, points to the verse *la'amod lishareis* — "to stand and to serve" — which describes how God chose the *kohanim* from among all the tribes to stand before Him and perform the service. The Gemara derives from the framing of the verse — *la'amida bechartiv velo leyoshiva*, "I have chosen him when he is standing, not when he is sitting" — that a *kohen* performing the *avodah* while seated has not fulfilled the service. The verse could simply have stated *ein sheirus ela be'amida*, that the proper manner of service is standing. The specific language of *bechirah*, divine election, however, opens the door to a broader consequence, as we shall see.

### Repetition Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: מִכְּדִי יוֹשֵׁב כְּזָר דָּמֵי וּמַחֵיל עֲבוֹדָה, אֵימָא: מָה זָר בְּמִיתָה – אַף יוֹשֵׁב בְּמִיתָה! אַלְּמָה תַּנְיָא: אֲבָל עָרֵל, אוֹנֵן, יוֹשֵׁב – אֵינָן בְּמִיתָה אֶלָּא בְּאַזְהָרָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Rav Naḥman: Now, one who is sitting is considered like a non-priest and desecrates** the **service.** Therefore, **I will say: Just as a non-priest** who performs a rite is liable **to** receive **death** at the hand of Heaven, **so too one who is sitting** should be liable **to** receive **death** at the hand of Heaven. If so, **why is it taught** in a *baraita*: **But one who is uncircumcised, an acute mourner,** and **one who is sitting are not** liable **to** receive **death** at the hand of Heaven if they performed rites; **rather,** they simply transgress **a prohibition?**

A *baraisa* observes that had the Torah stated *la'amod lishareis* only once, one might have concluded it is a mere *mitzvah* — a directive to perform the service while standing, but not a *me'akev* condition. However, the Torah repeats the concept in a second verse, *ha'omdim sham lifnei Hashem* — "those who stand there before God." The principle *shana alav hakesuv le'akev* holds that when the Torah doubles a requirement in this fashion, the repetition signals that the condition is indispensable even *b'dieved*: service performed while seated is retroactively invalid.

### Sitting = Non-Priest

Rava then poses a sharper formulation. If a *kohen* who is seated is, by virtue of the language of *bechirah*, no longer in the category of "the chosen" — then *yoshev kezari dami*, a sitting *kohen* is like a non-*kohen*. This has practical consequences: it means the *avodah* performed while sitting is invalid. But one might push the analogy further and argue that since a *zar* who performs the *avodah* incurs the divine death penalty, perhaps a sitting *kohen* should as well.

### Kezari Limitations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵי מְחוּסַּר בְּגָדִים וְשֶׁלֹּא רָחוּץ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם – שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִין הַבָּאִין כְּאֶחָד, וְכׇל שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִין כְּאֶחָד – אֵין מְלַמְּדִין.
>
> **Translation:** That is taught **because** the case of a priest **lacking** the requisite priestly **vestments and** that of **one whose hands and feet are not washed are two verses that come as one,** as the verse states explicitly for each case that if they perform rites they are liable to receive death at the hand of Heaven. **And any two verses that come as one do not teach** their common aspect to similar cases. Therefore, one cannot assume that analogous prohibitions carry penalty of death at the hand of Heaven as well.

A *baraisa* forecloses this conclusion. It lists several categories — an *arel* (uncircumcised *kohen*), an *onen* (one in immediate mourning), and a *yoshev* (one sitting) — and states explicitly that these individuals do not incur the divine *misa* death penalty. They do invalidate the *avodah* and they do transgress a negative prohibition, but they are not liable to divine death.

Why not? If they are *kezari dami*, why not extend the full consequences of the *zar* comparison? The Gemara answers with the rule of *shenei kesuvim ba'im k'echad* — two explicit scriptural passages teach that specific comparable categories do incur *misa* (the *kohen* who serves without the complete *bigdei kehuna*, and the one who has not washed hands and feet). These two explicit cases establish themselves as exceptions, not as the basis for a general rule.

### Two Verses Rule

The principle *shenei kesuvim ba'im k'echad ein melamdin* holds that when two explicit verses establish a rule for particular cases, one cannot generalize from those cases to other situations. Rashi and Tosafos both note the difficulty here: the argument against applying the death penalty to a sitting *kohen* need not depend on generalizing from those two verses at all, since we are independently deriving his *zar*-like status from the language of *bechirah*. Tosafos resolves this by pointing to a parallel principle: just as by the *bigdei kehuna* — where we already say *ein bigdeihem aleihem ein kehunatam aleihem*, they are not true *kohanim* when not wearing the vestments — the Torah still needed to state the death penalty explicitly, so too here. The lesson is that even when someone is deemed *kezari dami*, that comparison does not automatically carry the death penalty unless the Torah says so. The practical conclusion stands: a *kohen* who performs the *avodah* while sitting invalidates it and transgresses a prohibition, but is not subject to divine death.

## Standing on Objects

### Invalid Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹמֵד עַל גַּבֵּי כֵּלִים, עַל גַּבֵּי בְּהֵמָה, עַל גַּבֵּי רַגְלֵי חֲבֵירוֹ – פָּסוּל. מְנָלַן? דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: הוֹאִיל וְרִצְפָּה מְקַדֶּשֶׁת וּכְלֵי שָׁרֵת מְקַדְּשִׁים; מָה כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת – לֹא יְהֵא דָּבָר חוֹצֵץ בֵּינוֹ לְבֵין כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת, אַף רִצְפָּה – לֹא יְהֵא דָּבָר חוֹצֵץ בֵּינוֹ לְבֵין הָרִצְפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that one who is **standing upon vessels,** or **upon an animal,** or **upon the feet of another,** is **unfit** to perform rites. The Gemara elaborates: **From where do we** derive this? This is derived **as** the Sage **from the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: Since the floor** of the Temple courtyard **sanctifies** the priest to perform the service, **and service vessels,** i.e., priestly vestments, also **sanctify** him, one may draw a comparison between the two: **Just as** with regard to **service vessels, nothing may interpose between** the priest **and** the **service vessel, so too** with regard to the **floor, nothing may interpose between** the priest **and the floor.**

The Gemara now turns to cases enumerated in the Mishna. A *kohen* who performs the *avodah* while standing on top of a vessel (*al gabei keilim*), on top of an animal (*al gabei behema*), or on his fellow's foot (*al gabei raglei chaveiro*) — in all these cases the *avodah* is *pasul*. The implicit point of the Mishna is that there must be no *chatzitza* — no interposition — between the *kohen*'s feet and the floor of the *azarah*.

### Floor Sanctification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא; דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן כְּלִי – מִשּׁוּם דְּלָאו מִינָא דְּבָשָׂר נִינְהוּ; אֲבָל בְּהֵמָה, דְּמִינָא דְּבָשָׂר הוּא – אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בְּהֵמָה – דְּלָא מִינָא דְּאָדָם הוּא; אֲבָל חֲבֵירוֹ, דְּאָדָם הוּא – אֵימָא לָא; צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And** each of the three cases mentioned in the mishna is **necessary. As, had** the mishna **taught us** only that **a vessel** interposes between the priest's feet and the floor, one might have thought that this is only **because it is not a type of flesh, but** with regard to **an animal, which is a type of flesh, I will say** that it **does not** constitute an interposition. **And had it taught us** only that **an animal** interposes, one might have thought that this is only **because it is not of the human species, but another** person, **who is human, I will say** that he does **not** interpose. Therefore, each is **necessary.**

The Gemara cites a *baraisa* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *haritzpah mekadeshet ve-klei shareis mekadeshim* — the floor sanctifies and the *klei shareis* sanctify. There is a significant question regarding the correct *girsa* of this passage. If one reads *mekudeshet* and *mekudashim* — the floor has been sanctified, and the *klei shareis* have been sanctified — the passage simply describes the sanctity of these objects. If one reads *mekadeshet* and *mekadeshim* — the floor sanctifies, and the *klei shareis* sanctify — the passage describes an active role these objects play in elevating other things or persons that come into contact with them.

### Holy Vessel Analogy

Reading the passage in the manner of *mekadeshet*, which appears to be the approach of Tosafos, yields a richer teaching. The principle *ma klei shareis lo yehei davar chotzetz beino uvein klei shareis* — just as when the *kohen* holds the holy vessel there must be nothing interposing between his hand and the vessel — so too, *af ritzpa lo yehei davar chotzetz beino uvein haritzpa*, there must be nothing interposing between him and the floor.

Rashi reads the *klei shareis* of the analogy as the bowl used to catch the blood, and draws the following lesson: just as the *kohen* must hold the holy vessel directly — the principle *lekicha be'itzmo shel kohen* requires him to grip it without any intervening material — so too his feet must be in direct contact with the holy floor. The *kohen* must be directly connected to all the sanctified objects involved in the *avodah*, whether they are above him in his hands or below him underfoot.

Tosafos offers what is described as a more internally consistent reading of the *mekadeshet* version. If the *klei shareis* actively sanctify, one would expect this to mean that blood or other sacrificial material placed into the bowl is elevated to a higher degree of sanctity — and indeed the Gemara elsewhere teaches that the *klei shareis* do precisely this, raising what is placed in them from *kedushat peh* to a more intrinsic sanctity. But there is then no natural analogy between the *klei shareis* sanctifying the blood and the floor sanctifying the *kohen*. The two are not parallel.

Tosafos therefore proposes that *klei shareis* in this passage refers not to the vessels but to the *bigdei kehuna*, the priestly vestments. There is precedent for using the term *klei shareis* to encompass the sacred garments. On this reading, the *baraisa* draws a parallel between two sanctifying processes: just as the *bigdei kehuna*, worn directly against the skin, sanctify the *kohen* and render him fit for service — *ein bigdeihem aleihem ein kehunatam aleihem*, when they are not wearing the garments their priestly status does not apply — so too the floor of the *Beis Hamikdash*, when touched directly by the *kohen*'s bare feet, participates in this same transformative process. The *kohen* entering the *Beis Hamikdash* and standing barefoot on its floor becomes, in a sense, part of the *mikdash* itself. This reading resonates with the broader theme of the *miluim*, the inauguration of the *kohanim*, in which the anointing oil was applied both to the vessels of the *Mishkan* and to the *kohanim* themselves — transforming them, as it were, into *klei kodesh*.

### Garments Interpretation

The Tosafos reading — identifying the *klei shareis* in the *baraisa* with the *bigdei kehuna* — also illuminates why a *chatzitza* between the garments and the skin is prohibited. The garments do not merely cover the *kohen*; they sanctify him. Any interposition prevents this sanctifying contact from taking effect, just as a *chatzitza* in the bowl prevents the vessel from sanctifying the blood. The parallel holds elegantly if one accepts this identification of terms.

### Necessity of Cases

The Gemara asks why the Mishna needed to enumerate all three cases — the vessel, the animal, and the fellow's foot. The answer is that each case might have been considered permissible on its own grounds. One might have thought that a vessel, being inanimate and clearly distinct from human flesh, is obviously a *chatzitza*, but an animal — *de'mina d'basar hu*, being flesh like the *kohen* — might not be considered an interposition. And one might have conceded that an animal is not truly *min b'mino* since it is not of the same species, but a fellow human being — *d'adam hu*, of the same species — might be considered close enough to not constitute a *chatzitza*. The Mishna therefore had to specify all three cases to teach that in none of them is the *chatzitza* overlooked. By implication, standing on one's own foot is not mentioned — and is understood not to be a problem, both because of the *min b'mino* consideration and for reasons that emerge in the next section.

## Standing Without Support

### Eliezer's Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: רַגְלוֹ אַחַת עַל הַכְּלִי וְרַגְלוֹ אַחַת עַל הָרִצְפָּה, רַגְלוֹ אַחַת עַל הָאֶבֶן וְרַגְלוֹ אַחַת עַל הָרִצְפָּה – רוֹאִין; כֹּל שֶׁאִילּוּ יִנָּטֵל הַכְּלִי וְתִנָּטֵל הָאֶבֶן, יָכוֹל לַעֲמוֹד עַל רַגְלוֹ אַחַת וְיַעֲבוֹד – עֲבוֹדָתוֹ כְּשֵׁירָה; וְאִם לָאו – עֲבוֹדָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says:** If **one of** the priest's **feet** was **on a vessel and one of his feet** was **on the floor,** or if **one of his feet** was **on a stone and one of his feet** was **on the floor,** one **sees: Any** case **where, were the vessel to be removed or the stone removed, he** would still be **able to stand on one foot and perform rites, his service** is **valid. But if** he would **not** be able to stand on one foot, **his service** is **disqualified.**

The Gemara cites a *baraisa* in the name of Rabbi Eliezer addressing mixed cases: a *kohen* with one foot on a vessel and one foot on the floor, or one foot on an unattached stone and one on the floor. The operative test is: *roin* — one assesses the situation. If, were the vessel or stone removed, the *kohen* could still stand on the one foot that remains on the floor and perform the *avodah*, then *avodato keshera* — the service is valid. This implies that standing on one foot is in principle permissible for performing the *avodah*. This rule, it is noted, carries over into other halachic contexts where standing is required.

Conversely, if the *kohen* could not stand and perform without the support of the object, then *avodah pesulah* — the service is invalid, because he is in effect standing on that object.

### Loosened Stone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי אַמֵּי: נִדַּלְדְּלָה הָאֶבֶן וְעָמַד עָלֶיהָ, מַהוּ? הֵיכָא דְּאֵין דַּעְתּוֹ לְחַבְּרָהּ – לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, דְּוַדַּאי חָיְיצָא. כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ – דְּדַעְתּוֹ לְחַבְּרָהּ. מַאי? כֵּיוָן דְּדַעְתּוֹ לְחַבְּרָהּ – כְּמָה דִּמְחַבְּרָא דָּמְיָא; אוֹ דִילְמָא, הַשְׁתָּא מִיהָא הָא תְּלִישָׁא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami raises a dilemma:** If one of **the stone** tiles of the Temple floor **came loose** and began to wobble, **and** the priest **stood on it, what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara elaborates: **Do not raise the dilemma** with regard to a case **where he does not intend to attach** the stone to the floor again, **as it certainly interposes** in such a case, since it is considered an item separate from the Temple. Rather, **when you raise this dilemma,** do so with regard to a case **where he intends to attach it** again. In such a case, **what** is the *halakha*? Does one say that **since he intends to attach it, it is considered like that which is** already **attached? Or perhaps now, at least, it is disconnected,** and it is considered a separate item.

Rabbi Ami poses a more subtle question: what if one of the stones of the *azarah* floor has become loose (*nidaldelah even*) — no longer cemented in place, though it is still sitting in position? If a *kohen* performs the *avodah* while standing on it, what is the rule? The Gemara notes that if there is no intention to re-cement the stone, there is no question — *devadai chotzetz*, it is clearly considered something extraneous and the *avodah* is invalid. The question arises only when the intention is to re-cement it (*deda'ato lechabra*). In that case, one might argue *keivan deda'ato lechabra keman demachabra damya* — since it is intended to be reattached, it is treated as if it already is, and the *avodah* would be valid. Or one might say that right now it is in fact detached, and that is the operative reality. The Gemara leaves this unresolved.

### Missing Stone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה זוּטֵי בָּעֵי לַהּ הָכִי, בָּעֵי רַבִּי אַמֵּי: נֶעֶקְרָה הָאֶבֶן וְעָמַד בִּמְקוֹמָהּ, מַהוּ? מַאי קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ? כִּי קַדֵּישׁ דָּוִד – רִצְפָּה עֶלְיוֹנָה קַדֵּישׁ; אוֹ דִילְמָא עַד לְאַרְעִית תְּהוֹמָא קַדֵּישׁ, וְתִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ כׇּל הָעֲזָרָה כּוּלָּהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba Zuti raises this dilemma like this: Rabbi Ami raises a dilemma:** If one of **the stone** tiles **was uprooted and** the priest **stood in its** empty **place, what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara interjects: **What is the dilemma he is raising?** Perhaps this is the dilemma: **When** King **David sanctified** the site of the Temple, **did he sanctify** the **upper** level of the **floor** but nothing underneath it, **or perhaps he sanctified** it **to the depths of the earth? But** if this is the dilemma, **let him raise the dilemma** with regard to **the entire Temple courtyard,** i.e., what is the *halakha* if the entire floor is removed?

A question attributed to Rabba Zuta addresses the adjacent case: *nikrah even* — the stone has been fully removed, leaving an indentation or hole in the floor. If a *kohen* stands in that hole to perform the *avodah*, what is the rule?

The Gemara first asks what is even at issue. If one assumed that David sanctified only the upper paving (*ritzpah elyona kadish*), then standing in a hole would mean standing on unsanctified ground, and the *avodah* would certainly be invalid. But if one assumes *ad ar'ah detehoma kadish* — that the sanctity extends all the way down through the earth beneath — then even the ground below the paving is sanctified floor of the *Beis Hamikdash*. The Gemara challenges this framing: if that is the question, one should simply ask whether a *kohen* standing on bare earth, with all the stones stripped away from the entire *azarah*, would be valid. That question is more straightforward and more general. The Gemara answers that in fact Rabbi Ami assumed the deeper earth *is* sanctified. His question is something else entirely.

### Hole Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ דְּעַד אַרְעִית תְּהוֹמָא קַדֵּישׁ; וְהָכִי קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: דֶּרֶךְ שֵׁירוּת בְּכָךְ, אוֹ אֵין דֶּרֶךְ שֵׁירוּת בְּכָךְ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Actually,** it is **obvious to him that** King David **sanctified** the site of the Temple **to the depths of the earth, and this is the dilemma that he raised:** When a priest stands on an unfinished part of the Temple floor, **is this** a normal **manner of ministration, or is this not** a normal **manner of ministration?** Concerning the dilemma itself, the Gemara responds: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The actual question Rabbi Ami is raising is one of *derech sheirut*: if a *kohen* is standing in a hole — below the level of the surrounding floor — is that the *derech sheirut*, the proper manner of service? Even if the ground beneath him is sanctified, perhaps the dignified and proper mode of performing the *avodah* requires that the *kohen* be standing at floor level rather than sunken into a pit. The Gemara does not resolve this question, and it stands as *teiku*.

The broader upshot of this section is clear: the *kohen* must be directly in contact with the sanctified floor, and the precise parameters of what constitutes valid standing — one foot versus two, a loosened stone, a hole in the floor — generate a cascade of increasingly fine-grained questions.

## Left Hand Usage

### Rabbi Shimon View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קִיבֵּל בִּשְׂמֹאל – פָּסוּל, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַכְשִׁיר. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״בְּאֶצְבָּעוֹ״ ״וְלָקַח״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא קַבָּלָה אֶלָּא בְּיָמִין. ״בְּאֶצְבָּעוֹ וְנָתַן״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא נְתִינָה אֶלָּא בְּיָמִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If the priest **collected** the blood **with** his **left** hand, the service is **disqualified, and Rabbi Shimon deems it fit. The Sages taught:** The verse states: "And the priest shall take of the blood of the sin offering with his finger and put it upon the corners of the altar" (Leviticus 4:25). The conjunction of the term **"with his finger"** and the term **"and** the priest **shall take" teaches that** the **collection** of the blood **may be** performed **only with** the **right** hand, since the word "finger" in the context of priesthood is always referring to the right hand. Likewise, the phrase **"with his finger and put it" teaches that** the **placing** of the blood **may be** performed **only with** the **right** hand.

The Mishna teaches *kibul b'smol pasul* — catching the blood with the left hand invalidates the *avodah* — and then states that Rabbi Shimon permits it. This is a focused position: Rabbi Shimon is not saying that any *avodah* may be performed with the left hand, but specifically the *kabbalah*, the catching of the blood.

### Etzba Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וְכִי נֶאֶמְרָה יָד בְּקַבָּלָה?! אֶלָּא ״בְּאֶצְבָּעוֹ וְנָתַן״ – שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא נְתִינָה אֶלָּא בְּיָמִין; הוֹאִיל וְלֹא נֶאֶמְרָה יָד בְּקַבָּלָה – קִיבֵּל בִּשְׂמֹאל כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said: But is** the word: **Hand, stated with regard to** the **collection** of the blood? **Rather,** the verse states only: **"With his finger and put it,"** teaching **that** the **placing** of the blood **may be** performed **only with** the **right** hand. **Since** the word: **Hand, is not stated with regard to** the **collection** of the blood, even if **one collected** the blood **with** his **left** hand, the offering is **fit.**

The Gemara cites a *baraisa* that analyzes the verse *velakach hakohen midam hachatat b'etzbao venatan* — "and the *kohen* shall take from the blood of the *chatat* with his finger and place." The word *b'etzbao*, "with his finger," is the crux of the matter. Based on a *gezeirah shavah* with the *metzora*, where the Torah specifies *etzbao hayemanit* — his right finger — the Tanna Kamma reads *etzba* throughout the *avodah* context as indicating the right hand or finger. Accordingly, the Tanna Kamma reads *b'etzbao* as applying to both the *lekichah* (the catching of the blood) and the *netinah* (the placing of the blood on the altar horns): both must be performed with the right hand.

### Tanna Kamma View

The *baraisa* states it explicitly: *b'etzbao velakach* — the word *b'etzbao* teaches that the *kabbalah* must be with the right; *b'etzbao venatan* — the *netinah* must be with the right. The Tanna Kamma reads the word *b'etzbao* as modifying both the preceding act and the following act, *nidrash lefanav ule'acharav*.

### Shimon's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן – מִמָּה נַפְשָׁךְ; אִי אִית לֵיהּ גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה – כִּי לֹא נֶאֶמְרָה יָד בְּקַבָּלָה מַאי הָוֵי? וְאִי לֵית לֵיהּ גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה – כִּי נֶאֶמְרָה יָד בְּקַבָּלָה מַאי הָוֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** as for **Rabbi Shimon, whichever** way **you** look at it, his opinion is difficult. **If he holds** that one derives **a verbal analogy** from the passage discussing a leper (Leviticus, chapter 14), which indicates that all references to hands and fingers mean specifically the right hand, then even **if** the word: **Hand, is not stated with regard to** the **collection** of the blood, **what of it?** In any event, the word "finger" is stated. **And if he does not hold** that one derives this **verbal analogy,** then even **if** the word: **Hand, were stated with regard to** the **collection** of the blood, **what of it?** The word would have no significance.

Rabbi Shimon challenges this. He notes that the verse does not use the word *yad*, hand, in connection with the *kabbalah* — it only uses the word *etzba*, finger. Since you cannot catch the blood in a vessel using only your finger, the word *etzba* cannot be referring to the catching. It can only be referring to the placing of the blood on the altar horns, which is indeed performed with the finger dipped in the blood. Therefore *b'etzbao venatan* — the *etzba* term modifies only the *netinah*, not the *lekichah*.

The Gemara raises a difficulty with this explanation. Rav Sama the son of Rav Ashi points out to Ravina that one could in principle hold the vessel with a finger by fashioning an *ozen*, a handle, on the rim of the bowl — enabling the *kohen* to catch the blood while gripping the vessel through the handle with a single finger. This technical possibility undermines the argument that *etzba* cannot refer to the catching.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב סַמָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אָשֵׁי לְרָבִינָא: אִיפְשָׁר דְּעָבֵיד לֵיהּ אֹזֶן לִשְׂפַת מִזְרָק, וּמְקַבֵּל בַּהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Samma, son of Rav Ashi, said to Ravina:** But **it is possible to fashion** a small **handle for the rim of** the **bowl,** and one could then hold the bowl with his finger **and collect** the blood **in it.** If so, it is possible for one to collect the blood with only his finger.

Abaye therefore reframes the entire dispute. He sets aside Rabbi Shimon's stated reason about *etzba* versus *yad*, and argues that the fundamental disagreement is about *bemikra nidrash lefanav ule'acharav* — whether a word in the middle of a verse is read both forward and backward. The Tanna Kamma holds that *b'etzbao* refers to both the preceding *lekichah* and the following *netinah*. Rabbi Shimon holds that it refers only to the following word, *venatan* — it is read forward (*lefanav*) but not backward. This is a clean and sufficient explanation of the disagreement, without requiring any argument about what the finger can or cannot do.

### Abaye Reinterprets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּמִקְרָא נִדְרָשׁ לְפָנָיו וּלְאַחֲרָיו קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye says:** **They disagree with regard to** whether **a verse is interpreted** based on juxtaposition to the language **preceding it and** to the language **following it.** According to Rabbi Shimon, a verse is interpreted only based on the language following it. Therefore, when the verse states: "And the priest shall take of the blood of the sin offering with his finger and put it upon the corners of the altar," the word "finger" is referring only to the placing of the blood and not its collection. The Rabbis hold that the verse is also interpreted based on the language preceding it. Accordingly, they require that both rites be performed with the right hand.

## Elazar's Position

### Elazar's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן – מַפְּקָא מִדַּאֲבוּהּ, וּמַפְּקָא מִדְּרַבָּנַן. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר אֶצְבַּע בְּקַבָּלָה – שִׁינָּה בַּקַּבָּלָה, פָּסוּל; בַּנְּתִינָה, כָּשֵׁר. וְכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר אֶצְבַּע בִּנְתִינָה – שִׁינָּה בַּנְּתִינָה, פָּסוּל; בַּקַּבָּלָה, כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: This** statement **of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, diverges from** the opinion **of his father, and it diverges from** the opinion **of the Rabbis: As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says:** In **every instance** in the Torah **that** the word **"finger" is stated with regard to collection** of the blood and not with regard to placing of the blood on the altar, if the priest **deviated** from the proper method **of collection** and performed it with the left hand, the offering is **disqualified,** but if he deviated from the proper method **of placing** the blood, the offering remains **fit. And** in **every instance that** the word **"finger" is stated** only **with regard to placing** the blood, if the priest **deviated** from the proper method **of placing,** the offering is **disqualified,** but if he deviated from the proper method **of collection,** the offering remains **fit.**

Abaye notes that Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon staked out a position that differs from both his father's and from the Tanna Kamma's. The *baraisa* states his view: *kol makom shene'emar etzba bekabbalah* — wherever the word *etzba* is associated with the *kabbalah* (the catching of the blood), then if one altered the *kabbalah* and used the left hand, it is *pasul*; but the *netinah* performed with the left hand would be *kasher*. Conversely, wherever *etzba* is stated in connection with the *netinah*, altering the *netinah* would be *pasul* while the *kabbalah* could be done with the left hand.

### Par Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵיכָן נֶאֱמַר אֶצְבַּע בִּנְתִינָה? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְלָקַחְתָּ מִדַּם הַפָּר וְנָתַתָּ עַל קַרְנֹת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ בְּאֶצְבָּעֶךָ״, וְקָסָבַר: מִקְרָא נִדְרָשׁ לְפָנָיו, וְלֹא לִפְנֵי פָנָיו וּלְאַחֲרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And where is** the word **"finger" stated** only **with regard to placing? As it is written: "And you shall take of the blood of the bullock and put it upon the corners of the altar with your finger"** (Exodus 29:12). **And he holds** that **a verse is interpreted** based on juxtaposition to the language immediately **preceding it and not** to the language **before that** which immediately **precedes it, nor** to the language **following it.**

Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon does not take a single uniform position on which act requires the right hand. Instead, he determines it case by case based on where in the verse the word *etzba* appears. He holds *mikra nidrash lefanav* — the word refers only to what immediately precedes it, to the immediately prior act. This is why in our verse, where *b'etzbao* comes after *velakach* and before *venatan*, he reads it as modifying only the *lekichah* — the *kabbalah* must be with the right hand, not the *netinah*. This is precisely the mirror image of his father Rabbi Shimon's position.

Where does Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon derive the rule that there are cases where the *netinah* requires the right hand and the *kabbalah* does not? From the verse concerning the *par hamiluim*, the bull of the inauguration offering: *velakachta midam hapar venatatah al karnot hamizbeach b'etzbe'cha* — "and you shall take from the blood of the bull and place it on the horns of the altar with your finger." Here, *b'etzbe'cha* appears unmistakably after *venatatah* and not after *velakachta*. There is no grammatical ambiguity: the word *etzba* modifies only the placing, not the catching. For the *par hamiluim*, therefore, Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon rules that the *netinah* requires the right hand and the *kabbalah* does not.

### Derash Principle

Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon's hermeneutical principle is *mikra nidrash lefanav velo lifnei fanav* — a word is read as modifying only the immediately preceding element, not what comes two positions back — *velo l'acharav*, and not what follows it. This stands in contrast to the Tanna Kamma (*nidrash lefanav ule'acharav*, both directions) and to Rabbi Shimon (*nidrash lefanav* only, meaning toward what follows). The result is a third, distinct position: by the standard *chatat* verse, the *etzba* term modifies the *kabbalah* and not the *netinah*; by the *par hamiluim* verse, it modifies the *netinah* and not the *kabbalah*. Each verse is read on its own terms.

This position is described as genuinely striking: Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon assigns the requirement of the right hand to the *kabbalah* rather than the *netinah*, even though the *netinah* — the placement of blood on the horns of the altar — would seem to be the more significant act. One possible explanation is that the *kabbalah* in the *klei shareis* is itself part of the sanctifying of the blood, and so carries its own weight.

## Right Hand Tests

### Yochanan Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר אֶצְבַּע וּכְהוּנָּה – אֵינָהּ אֶלָּא יָמִין.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In **every instance** in the Torah **that it is stated** that an action is performed with **a finger and** by members of the **priesthood,** it may be performed **only** with the **right** hand.

The Gemara now introduces a broader principle in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, cited by Rabbah bar bar Chanah: *kol makom shene'emar etzba u'kehunah einah ela yamin* — wherever the Torah uses the word *etzba* (finger) and/or *kehunah* (the language of priesthood, i.e., the word *kohen*), it indicates the right hand is required.

### Metzora Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתִּין תַּרְתֵּי בָּעֵינַן – כְּדִכְתִיב: ״וְלָקַח הַכֹּהֵן מִדַּם הַחַטָּאת בְּאֶצְבָּעוֹ״, וְיָלֵיף מִמְּצוֹרָע – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְטָבַל הַכֹּהֵן אֶת אֶצְבָּעוֹ הַיְמָנִית״. וַהֲרֵי קְמִיצָה – דְּלָא כְּתִib בְּהוּ אֶלָּא כְּהוּנָּה; וּתְנַן: קָמַץ בִּשְׂמֹאל – פָּסוּל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: It might **enter our mind** to say that this means that **we require both** a finger and the priesthood to be stated together in the verse in order to mandate use of the right hand, e.g., **as it is written: "And the priest shall take of the blood of the sin offering with his finger"** (Leviticus 4:25). **And** the fact that this verse is referring to a finger from his right hand **is derived from a leper, as it is written: "And the priest shall dip his right finger"** (Leviticus 14:16). This cannot be correct, **as** there is the verse that addresses **the removal of a handful** from a meal offering, **in which only** the **priesthood is written, and** yet **we learned** in a mishna (*Menaḥot* 6a): If one **removed the handful with** his **left** hand, the meal offering is **disqualified.**

The Gemara initially assumes that *tarti ba'inan* — both terms are needed together. The basis is our verse from the *chatat*: *velakach hakohen midam hachatat b'etzbao*, which contains both *kohen* and *etzba*. This is then connected by *gezeirah shavah* to the *metzora* verse: *vetaval hakohen et etzbao hayemanit* — where the *kohen* dips his right finger — which makes explicit that the combination of *kohen* and *etzba* indicates *yamin*, the right side.

### Mincha Exception

The Gemara challenges this with the *kemitza* of the *mincha*. When the *kohen* takes the handful of flour, the verse states *vekamatz hakohen* — the *kohen* shall take — using the language of *kehunah* without the word *etzba*, since the act involves multiple fingers, not a single digit. Yet the *Mishna* rules *kamatz b'smol pasul* — if he used his left hand it is invalid. Thus the word *kohen* alone, without *etzba*, is sufficient to require the right hand. This forces a broadening of Rabbi Yochanan's principle.

### Rule Broadened

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: אוֹ אֶצְבַּע, אוֹ כְּהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava says:** This statement means that if the verse mentions **either a finger or** the **priesthood,** only the right hand may be used.

Rava accordingly reformulates: *o etzba o kehunah* — either the word *etzba* alone or the language of *kehunah* alone is sufficient to indicate that the right hand is required. One does not need both terms together.

## Kehuna Limitation

### Limbs Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הֲרֵי הוֹלָכַת אֵבָרִים לַכֶּבֶשׁ – דִּכְתִיב בְּהוּ כְּהוּנָּה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהִקְרִיב הַכֹּהֵן אֶת הַכֹּל [וְהִקְטִיר] הַמִּזְבֵּחָה״; וְאָמַר מָר: זוֹ הוֹלָכַת אֵבָרִים לַכֶּבֶשׁ; וּתְנַן: הָרֶגֶל שֶׁל יָמִין בִּשְׂמֹאלוֹ, וּבֵית עוֹרָהּ לַחוּץ!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rava: But this is contradicted by the verse discussing **the conveyance of** the **limbs** of the daily burnt offering **to the ramp** of the altar, **as priesthood is written with regard to it, as it is written: "And the priest shall sacrifice the whole** and make it smoke **upon the altar"** (Leviticus 1:13), **and the Master said** that **this** verse is referring to the **conveyance of** the **limbs to the ramp. And** yet **we learned** in a mishna (*Tamid* 31b): When the priest conveys the limbs to the ramp, **the foot of** the **right** side of the offering is carried **in the left** hand **of** the priest, **and the place of its skin,** i.e., the side of the limb covered in skin, is held facing **outward.** Clearly, use of the left hand does not disqualify the conveyance of the limbs.

Abaye immediately raises a difficulty. If the mere presence of the word *kohen* in a verse is enough to require the right hand, then the act of *holachat evarim lakevesh* — carrying the limbs of the sacrifice to the ramp — should also require the right hand, since the verse states *vehikriv hakohen et hakol*, using the language of *kehunah*. Yet the *Mishna* elsewhere describes how a *kohen* would carry the right foreleg of the animal in his left hand while holding the head in his right, and this is unproblematic. Clearly the left hand is permitted for bringing the limbs.

### Central Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** [כִּי אָמְרִינַן] אוֹ אֶצְבַּע אוֹ כְּהוּנָּה – בְּדָבָר הַמְעַכֵּב כַּפָּרָה, דּוּמְיָא דִּמְצוֹרָע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **When we say** that if the verse states **either finger or priesthood** then the left hand is disqualified, this is only **with regard to a matter that precludes atonement,** i.e., a rite whose performance is indispensable to the atonement, **similar to** the sprinkling of the oil on the **leper** (see Leviticus 14:16). The conveyance of the limbs, by contrast, is not indispensable to atonement.

The Gemara resolves this by introducing a limiting principle: *ki amrinan o etzba o kehunah bidvar hame'akev kapparah* — we say that either *etzba* or *kehunah* indicates the right hand only when the act is central to the atonement, *dumya demetzora*, comparable to the paradigmatic acts associated with the *metzora* ritual. The bringing of limbs to the ramp, while part of the service, is not itself an act without which the atonement is incomplete. When it is not a *davar hame'akev kapparah*, the presence of the word *kohen* in the verse does not impose the requirement of the right hand.

### Catching Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי קַבָּלָה – דִּכְתַב בְּהוּ כְּהוּנָּה, וְדָבָר הַמְעַכֵּב בְּכַפָּרָה הוּא; וּתְנַן: קִיבֵּל בִּשְׂמֹאל – פָּסוּל, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַכְשִׁיר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** isn't there the **collection** of the blood in a service vessel, **about which priesthood is written and** which **is a matter that precludes atonement? And** yet **we learned** in the mishna: If **one collected** the blood **with** his **left** hand, the blood is **disqualified** for offering, **and Rabbi Shimon deems** it **fit.**

The Gemara then applies this principle to the *kabbalah*, the catching of the blood. The *kabbalah* is clearly a *davar hame'akev kapparah*, and the verse uses the language of *kehunah* (*velakach hakohen*). By the newly broadened principle — *o etzba o kehunah* — the word *kohen* alone should suffice to require the right hand. Yet Rabbi Shimon rules that the *kabbalah* with the left hand is *kasher*. How can this be?

### Shimon Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן תַּרְתֵּי בָּעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Rabbi Shimon requires** that **both** matters appear in the verse, i.e., both finger and priesthood.

The Gemara answers: *Rebbi Shimon tarti ba'ei* — Rabbi Shimon requires both terms together. For Rabbi Shimon, the word *kehunah* alone is not sufficient to mandate the right hand; he needs the word *etzba* as well. And since *etzba* appears in the verse but (according to Rabbi Shimon's reading) refers only to the *netinah* and not the *kabbalah*, the *kabbalah* lacks the *etzba* requirement and may be performed with the left hand. The broadened rule — *o etzba o kehunah* — reflects the position of the *rabbanan*, not of Rabbi Shimon.

### Zerika Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי זְרִיקָה – דְּלָא כְּתִב בְּהוּ אֶלָּא כְּהוּנָּה; וּתְנַן: זָרַק בִּשְׂמֹאל – פָּסוּל, וְלָא פְּלִיג רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** isn't there the **sprinkling** of the blood, **concerning which only** the **priesthood is written** in the verse, **and we learned:** If **one sprinkled** the blood **with** his **left** hand it is **disqualified; and Rabbi Shimon does not disagree** with this ruling, indicating that Rabbi Shimon holds that a mention of the priesthood does not require a mention of "finger"?

The Gemara now notices a further apparent problem. If Rabbi Shimon's entire position rests on the word *etzba* — and the word *etzba* appears in the verse only in connection with the *netinah* — then by the same logic, the *zerika*, the throwing of the blood onto the altar, should also be permitted with the left hand. The verse describing *zerika* uses *kehunah* language but not *etzba*. Rabbi Shimon, who requires *etzba*, should therefore permit left-hand *zerika* as well. Yet the *Mishna* rules *zarak b'smol pasul* without attributing any dissent to Rabbi Shimon.

### Zerika Disagreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: פְּלִיג בְּבָרַיְיתָא – דְּתַנְיָא: קִיבֵּל בִּשְׂמֹאל – פָּסוּל, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַכְשִׁיר. זָרַק בִּשְׂמֹאל – פָּסוּל, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַכְשִׁיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: He disagrees** with this ruling **in a *baraita*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If **one collected** the blood **with** his **left** hand, it is **disqualified, and Rabbi Shimon deems** it **fit.** Additionally, if **one sprinkled** the blood **with** his **left** hand, it is **disqualified, and Rabbi Shimon deems** it **fit.**

Abaye resolves the difficulty: Rabbi Shimon does in fact disagree about *zerika* as well. A *baraisa* makes this explicit — *zarak b'smol pasul, Rabbi Shimon machshir* — if one threw the blood with the left hand it is invalid, and Rabbi Shimon says it is valid. The absence of Rabbi Shimon's dissent from the *Mishna* on *zerika* does not mean he agrees; the *baraisa* supplies the missing dissent.

### Etzba Locations

The complete picture of where the word *etzba* appears in the Torah in the context of blood service now clarifies the entire structure of the debate. The word *etzba* appears explicitly only in connection with the *netinah* of the *chatat* — the placement of blood on the altar horns. It does not appear by the *kabbalah* and it does not appear by the *zerika*.

### Final Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רָבָא: ״יָד״–״יָד״ לִקְמִיצָה, ״רֶגֶל״–״רֶגֶל״ לַחֲלִיצָה, ״אֹזֶן״–״אֹזֶן״ לִרְצִיעָה; לְמָה לִי? מִדְּרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה נָפְקָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But that which Rava says** with regard to the superfluous phrases in the passage discussing a leper: One derives a verbal analogy between **"hand"** and **"hand"** mentioned with regard **to the removal of a handful** from a meal offering, to indicate that the latter must also be performed with the right hand. Additionally, one derives a verbal analogy between **"foot"** and **"foot"** mentioned with regard **to** the ***ḥalitza*** ritual. And one derives a verbal analogy between **"ear"** and **"ear"** mentioned with regard **to** the **piercing** of a Hebrew slave's ear with an awl. One may ask: **Why do I** need the first analogy? The requirement that the handful be removed with the right hand can be **derived from** the statement **of Rabba bar bar Ḥana** above, since priesthood is mentioned in the verse describing it.

The final framework emerges as follows. The *rabbanan* hold that either the word *etzba* or the language of *kehunah* is sufficient to require the right hand, for any act that is a *davar hame'akev kapparah*. Since *kehunah* language appears by the *kabbalah* and by the *zerika*, both of these require the right hand according to the *rabbanan*, in addition to the *netinah* of the *chatat* where *etzba* appears. Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, holds that only the word *etzba* is operative, and not *kehunah* alone. Since *etzba* appears only in connection with the *netinah* of the *chatat*, that is the only act for which Rabbi Shimon requires the right hand. The *kabbalah* and the *zerika* — where only *kehunah* language appears — may, according to Rabbi Shimon, be performed with the left hand without invalidating the *avodah*.
