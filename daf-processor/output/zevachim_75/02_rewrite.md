# Zevachim 75 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Pasul Animals

### Opening

The daf under discussion is Zevachim 75, though we begin on 74b, roughly halfway down the page at the words *haroveiah v'hanirba*. The *mishna* has been discussing the case of animals becoming mixed up with one another, and it presents a long list of animals that are *pasul* to be brought as *korbanot* for a variety of reasons.

### Mishna Overview

The *mishna* enumerates multiple categories of disqualified animals. *Roveiah* and *nirba* refer to an animal that played either the male or female role in an act of bestiality. *Muktza* is an animal that was set aside for *avodah zarah* but was not actually worshipped. *Ne'evad* is an animal that was itself worshipped. *Etnan* refers to the prohibition derived from the verse concerning payment given to a prostitute. *Mechir kelev* describes an animal whose value was used as purchasing power to acquire a dog — a prohibition explicit in the *Torah*. *Kilayim* refers to a crossbreed — for instance, sheep with wild sheep, or goats with wild goats. And *treifa* refers to an animal that has undergone a disqualifying injury, a topic that will be treated at great length in *Masechet Chullin*.

### Disqualifications

The *gemara*'s *sugia* here focuses primarily on the case of the *treifa*. *Bishlama kulhu* — for most of the animals on this list, one can readily understand how the problematic case arises: the animal has some non-physical disqualification, arising from a sin committed with it or from the manner in which it was used, so that *lo yadia* — it is not visually apparent — and if it becomes mixed into a larger flock, the problem of identification emerges naturally. But the *gemara* asks: *ela hai treifa heichi dami?* — what exactly is the case of a *treifa*? If an animal has sustained a serious enough injury to render it a *treifa*, that injury ought to be visible. How then could it become confused with other animals?

## Treifa Problem

### Treifa Mystery

This question is the pivot around which the *sugia* turns. Three *Amoraim* each offer their own answer, and the *gemara* provides no resolution among them — rather, each opinion stands on its own, connected to other *machlokot* elsewhere.

### Rabbi Yannai

*Amri de-vei Rabbi Yannai* — the school of Rabbi Yannai teaches: *hacha b'mai askinan, kegon de-irav nikuvat hakotz b'drusat haze'ev* — the case is where two different types of injured animals have become mixed with one another. One is *nikuvat hakotz*, an animal punctured by a thorn, which in all likelihood has not pierced any inner organ and therefore does not render the animal a *treifa*. The other is *drusat haze'ev*, an animal attacked by a wolf, where there is a far higher likelihood that internal organs were damaged and the animal has indeed become a *treifa*. According to Rabbi Yannai, both animals bear outward injuries that look similar enough to cause confusion, even though one is a *treifa* and one is not.

### Reish Lakish

Reish Lakish offers a different scenario: *kegon de-it'arev bin'fulah* — the case involves an animal that fell, a *nefulah*. One might then ask: simply examine the animal to determine whether it is truly a *treifa*. The answer is that Reish Lakish holds the position that even if the animal stood up again after its fall (*amda*), it still requires *me'et le'et* — a twenty-four hour observation period — before one may conclude it was unharmed. And even if it was subsequently able to walk (*halcha*), it still requires *bedika* — a post-*shechita* examination. This is because, in Reish Lakish's view, once an animal has sustained an injury, its *chazakat kashrut* is disrupted. The animal is no longer presumed healthy; rather, the burden of proof has shifted, and one must now affirmatively demonstrate that it has recovered. Under this framework, an animal of uncertain status after a fall could easily become mixed with others before a definitive determination has been made.

### Rabbi Yirmiya

Rabbi Yirmiya offers a third approach: *kegon de-it'arev bivlad treifa* — the case involves not an animal that itself bears a visible injury, but rather a *vlad treifa*, an animal born from a mother who was herself a *treifa*. Such an animal carries a disqualification but has no physical mark by which it can be identified. This is precisely why the mixture is intractable. Rabbi Yirmiya's position is grounded in the view of Rabbi Eliezer: *de-amar vlad treifa lo yikarev legavei hamizbe'ach* — that the offspring of a *treifa* mother is disqualified from being brought as a *korban*.

To sharpen the point: all three *Amoraim* are answering the same underlying question — how can a *treifa* animal get confused among others? Rabbi Yannai's answer is that there *is* a visible injury on the *treifa*, but another animal in the flock also has a similar-looking injury that is not disqualifying, creating genuine confusion between the two. Reish Lakish's answer is that the animal's status is genuinely uncertain, having not yet been definitively resolved through the requisite observation and examination. And Rabbi Yirmiya's answer is that the *treifa* animal in question bears no injury at all — its disqualification is genealogical, and therefore invisible.

## Rejections

### Against Yannai

The *gemara* now explains why each *Amora* rejects the other positions. Why do Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yirmiya not accept Rabbi Yannai's answer? Because *bein nikuvat hakotz ledrusat haze'ev meda yadi'a* — the wound from a thorn and the wound from a wolf are actually distinguishable upon examination. *Hai mashich ve-hai agil* — the wolf's bite produces an elongated wound (the wolf tears at the flesh with multiple teeth, leaving a drawn-out mark), while the thorn produces a circular puncture. This distinction — *mashuch ve-agul* — appears elsewhere as well, for instance in discussions of *ketamim* in *Hilchot Niddah*, though in that context we are generally not strict about distinguishing between them. In the context of identifying a *treifa*, however, Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yirmiya maintain that the distinction is visually discernible and therefore Rabbi Yannai's case does not present a genuine problem of confusion.

### Against Lakish

Why do Rabbi Yannai and Rabbi Yirmiya not accept Reish Lakish's answer? *Kasvari amda einah tzerichah me'et le'et, halcha einah tzerichah bedikah* — they hold the more lenient position: if an animal stood up after its fall, no twenty-four hour waiting period is required, and if it was able to walk, no post-*shechita* examination is necessary. In their view, the animal's ability to stand and walk is itself sufficient demonstration that it did not sustain a serious injury. The *chazakah* is not disrupted by the fall once the animal has recovered its mobility, and therefore the scenario Reish Lakish describes — where an animal of uncertain status mingles with others before its condition is resolved — simply does not arise under their ruling.

### Against Yirmiya

Why do Rabbi Yannai and Reish Lakish not accept Rabbi Yirmiya's answer? Because *ke-Rabbi Eliezer lo mukmi* — they do not hold like Rabbi Eliezer that the *vlad treifa* is disqualified at all. If the offspring of a *treifa* mother is not itself considered a *treifa* in their view, then Rabbi Yirmiya's case is not a case of a *treifa* mixed with other animals. The elegance of the *sugia* lies precisely here: each *Amora* has a coherent position that generates both its own answer to the original question and principled grounds for rejecting every alternative.

## Korbanot Mix

### Kodashim Principle

The *mishna* continues with a new principle: *kodashim be-kodashim min be-mino, zeh yikarev leshem mi she-hu ve-zeh yikarev leshem mi she-hu* — when *korbanot* of the same type are mixed together and the owners cannot identify their individual animals, each animal is brought with the general intention that it be offered on behalf of whomever owns it. Rather than designating it for a specific person, the *kohen* employs a catch-all *machshavah* that covers whichever owner's animal this turns out to be.

### Semichah Problem

The *gemara* raises a challenge: *ve-ha ba'ei semichah* — does not a *korban* require *semichah*, the laying of hands by the owner? If the owners cannot identify which animal is theirs, how can *semichah* be performed *lechatchilah*? The *gemara* acknowledges that *semichah* is not *me'akev be-di'avad* — it does not invalidate the *korban* after the fact — but there remains a *chiyuv* to perform it, and deliberately bringing a *korban* without it seems less than ideal. The question, then, is how the *mishna* can endorse this procedure as a legitimate resolution.

### Rav Yosef

*Amar Rav Yosef*: the *mishna* must be speaking specifically about *korban nashim* — offerings brought by women — because women are not required to perform *semichah*. There is accordingly no problem of failing to fulfill the obligation of *semichah* when the owners cannot identify their animals.

## Abaye Challenge

### Baraita Cases

Abaye challenges Rav Yosef. He cites a *baraita*: *korban yachid she-nitarev be-korban yachid, u-ve-korban tzibbur she-nitarev be-korban tzibbur* — if one individual's *korban* was mixed with another individual's *korban*, or if one communal *korban* was mixed with another communal *korban*, or even if an individual's *korban* was mixed with a communal *korban* (*korban yachid ve-korban tzibbur she-nitarvu zeh ba-zeh*) — in all these cases, *noten arba matanot mikkol echad ve-echad*: the blood of each *korban* must be applied in four *matanot* on the four *karnot hamizbe'ach*, because one of the animals in the mixture might be a *korban chatat* for which this is required.

*Ve-im natan matanah mikkol echad yatza* — if, *be-di'avad*, only one *matanah* was given from each animal individually, that suffices, since even for a *chatat* a single *matanah* is valid after the fact. *Ve-im natan arba mikkullan yatza* — and even if the blood of all the animals was mixed together and only four *matanot* were given from the combined blood, that too is *yotzei*.

The *baraita* then distinguishes between two scenarios: *bemei devarim amurim keshe-nitarvu chayyim* — this applies when the animals were mixed while still alive; *aval nitarvu shechutim* — but if they were mixed after *shechita*, *noten arba matanot mikkullan* — one gives four *matanot* from each, and *ve-im natan matanah achat mikkullan yatza* — even one *matanah* from all the blood together suffices.

Abaye's challenge to Rav Yosef emerges from the *baraita*'s language: *katani yachid — dumya de-tzibbur* — the *baraita* discusses an individual's *korban* in terms that parallel the communal *korban*. Communal *korbanot* are funded by the *shekalim* donated by men (*gavrei*), and therefore the *tzibbur*'s *korbanot* are as if owned by men. By analogy, the *yachid* cases in the *baraita* should likewise refer to men's offerings — thereby undermining Rav Yosef's claim that the *mishna* speaks only of women's *korbanot*.

### Rava Interprets

*Amar Rava*: in fact this *baraita* supports Rav Yosef rather than contradicting him. Rava reads the *baraita*'s distinction more carefully. The contrast between animals mixed while alive and blood mixed after *shechita* is not really about the timing per se, but about the physical configuration. *She-nitarvu shechutim ke-ein chayyim* — "mixed after *shechita* in a manner similar to when alive" means that even after *shechita*, the blood was kept in separate containers (*be-kossot*), treating each animal's blood as distinct — analogous to the animals being separate while alive. *Aval be-vulul* — if, however, the blood was blended together into one vessel, then one gives four *matanot* from the combined blood, and a single *matanah* from the mixture suffices.

### Rabbi's Volume Test

*Rebbi omer*: *ro'in et ha-matanah im yesh bah kedei la-zeh ve-kedei la-zeh, keshera; ve-im lav, pesulah* — we examine whether the *matanah* placed on the *mizbe'ach* contains enough blood to account for a valid portion from each *korban*. If there is sufficient volume, it is *keshera*; if not, it is *pesulah*.

This raises a question: where does Rebbi derive the principle that the blood must be of sufficient quantity? For a *korban chatat*, only a minimal amount of blood would seem necessary. The *gemara* notes that a *Tosefta* in *Parah* records Rebbi as saying, in the name of Rabbi Eliezer: *hazaya kol she-hu metaheret* — even the tiniest drop of *mei chatat* suffices for purification, with no minimum measure required (*hazaya eina tzerichah shi'ur*). This appears to cut against Rebbi's position regarding the *matanah* of blood.

## Parah Rules

### Eliezer Minimum

The *Tosefta* in *Parah* records Rebbi stating, within the framework of Rabbi Eliezer's view: *hazaya kol she-hu metaheret* — that even the most minuscule quantity of *mei chatat* landing on the *tamei* person effects purification. There is no defined minimum measure of water required.

### Parah vs. Korban

The *gemara* raises the apparent difficulty: if Rebbi himself holds, following Rabbi Eliezer, that there is no minimum quantity for *hazaya*, how can he require a minimum volume for a *matanah* of blood? The resolution offered is *hazaya le-chud ve-netinah le-chud* — the act of sprinkling (*hazaya*) for *parah adumah* is categorically distinct from the act of placing blood (*netinah*) on the *mizbe'ach* for a *korban*. The very nature of sprinkling already implies atomized, minimal quantities. By contrast, the *matanah* of blood for a *korban chatat* — applied with the *kohen*'s finger to the *keren* — and certainly the *shtayim she-hen arba* applied with a vessel (*kos*) — involves a substantial, definite quantity of blood. The two acts are sufficiently different in kind that one may distinguish between them, and Rebbi's position on *parah* does not undermine his position on *korbanot*.

## Bechor Ma'aser

### Grazing Till Mum

The *mishna* addresses the case of *nitarvu bechor u-ma'aser* — when disqualified animals have been mixed with *bechor* animals or with *ma'aser* animals. *Yireu ad she-yistavu* — the entire group must graze until they develop a *mum*. Once a *mum* appears, *veyachlu ke-vechor u-ke-ma'aser* — they are eaten in the manner appropriate to a *bechor* or *ma'aser* that developed a blemish. The *mishna* then concludes: *ha-kol yecholim le-hitarev chutz min ha-chatat ve-ha-asham* — all categories of *korbanot* may potentially become mixed together and admit of resolution, except for *chatat* and *asham*, which have a unique and personal connection to the owner on account of the specific sin being atoned for.

### Bechor Restrictions

*Amar Rabbi bar Chama* — Rabbi bar Chama raises a series of questions about the practical implications of the *bechor*'s status. According to Beit Shammai, even a *bechor* that has developed a *mum* — and has therefore become, in a sense, ordinary — may not be fed to a woman in *niddah*. There is some residual *kedushat bechor* that persists. Rabbi bar Chama then asks a series of follow-on questions: *temuraso mahu* — what is the status of an animal exchanged for a *bechor* through *temurah*? Since *bechor eino nifdah* — a *bechor* cannot be redeemed — can one make a *temurah* with it at all? And separately: *bechor eino nishkal be-litra* — the *kohen* may not sell the *bechor*'s meat by weight in the marketplace; what then of its *temuraso* — does the same restriction apply to the animal received in exchange?

### Dual Kedusha

*Rami bar Chama* poses a related question to Rava: *hitpis bechor le-vedek ha-bayit mahu* — what if one attempted to donate a *bechor* to *bedek ha-bayit*, the Temple maintenance fund, thereby seeking to infuse it with *kedushat bedek ha-bayit*? *Mahu she-yishkol be-litra* — would this then permit the meat to be weighed out and sold retail after *shechita*? The reasoning: *ravcha de-hekdesh adif* — increasing the financial return for *hekdesh* by selling at retail prices might override the usual *bechor* restriction. On the other hand, *zilusa de-vechor adif* — perhaps one should instead give precedence to the dignity of the *bechor* and its associated laws, even at the cost of a lower yield for *hekdesh*.

*Rabbi Yosi bar Zavda* attempts to bring a proof from our *mishna*: the fact that animals mixed with *bechor* and *ma'aser* must graze until they develop a *mum* and are then eaten *ke-vechor u-ke-ma'aser* suggests that the *bechor* retains its distinctive restrictions even after developing a *mum* — including the prohibition against weighing the meat. This would seem to argue against any leniency flowing from the donation to *bedek ha-bayit*.

Rav Huna and Rabbi Chizkiya, students of Rabbi Yirmiya, respond: *mi dami? Hatam shtei kedushot u-shnei gufin* — in the *mishna*'s case, we have two distinct sanctities (*kedushat bechor* and the disqualified animal's status) in two separate bodies. *Hacha shtei kedushot ve-guf echad* — here, by contrast, we are discussing an attempt to impose two sanctities on one and the same animal: *kedushat bechor* and *kedushat bedek ha-bayit* simultaneously. That is a fundamentally different configuration and the *mishna* may not constitute a proof.

*Matkif lah Rabbi Yosi bar Avin*: if someone declared, "Redeem for me my *bechor* that I donated to *bedek ha-bayit*" — one would not heed him, since *bechor* cannot be redeemed (*eino nifdeh*). If the attempted donation to *bedek ha-bayit* cannot even be undone through *pidyon*, it suggests the donation itself may not have taken meaningful effect. *Amar Rabbi Ami*: *klum hikne zeh ela ma she-kanu lo* — the *bedek ha-bayit* does not genuinely acquire the *bechor*, because the *bechor* already bears its own *kedusha* as a *korban* and an animal destined for the altar cannot simply be redirected to *bedek ha-bayit* in this fashion. The attempted donation of a *tam* animal earmarked as a *korban* to *bedek ha-bayit* does not succeed in the ordinary way.

## Chatat Asham

### Gender Distinctions

The *gemara* returns to the *mishna*'s rule that *chatat* and *asham* cannot be mixed with other *korbanot*. The question is: why are these two specifically exceptional? One might suggest *de-hai zachar ve-hai nekeva* — that the *chatat* is typically a female animal and the *asham* is always male, and so distinguishing between them by gender seems straightforward. But the *gemara* immediately notes *chatat ve-ola nami* — the same logic would apply to *chatat* and *olah*, since *olah* must also be male. So why can a *chatat* not become mixed with an *olah*?

### Age & Type

The *gemara* responds: *ika se'ir nasi* — the *chatat* brought by the *nasi* must be a male *se'ir* (goat), which could in principle become confused with a male *olah*. Then: *hai se'ar ve-hai tzemar* — but a goat has hair (*se'ar*) and a sheep has wool (*tzemer*), so one can still distinguish between them visually.

The *gemara* then asks: *pesach ve-asham lama lo mitarev* — why then cannot a *korban Pesach* and a *korban asham* become mixed? Both can be sheep. The response is *hai ben shana ve-hai ben shtei shanim* — the *korban Pesach* must be in its first year and the *korban asham* must be in its second year, so their age serves as a distinguishing feature. The *gemara* counters: *ika asham nazir ve-asham metzora* — the *asham* of a *nazir* and the *asham* of a *metzora* need not be older animals and could fall within the same age range as a *korban Pesach*.

### Age Counting

*Ve-iba'it eima* — alternatively, one may say: *ika ben shana de-michzei ke-ven shtei shanim, ve-ika ben shtei shanim de-michzei ke-ven shana* — a one-year-old animal may look like a two-year-old, and vice versa. Moreover, the ages of animals are not counted from their individual birthdays. Since animals have a birthing season and are born around the same time of year, they are classified by seasonal cohort — from *Rosh Chodesh Elul* or *Rosh Chodesh Tishrei* — rather than by the precise date of birth. As a result, there is genuine ambiguity about whether a given animal falls into the one-year or two-year category, and this can generate real cases of confusion even when examining animals of nominally different age classes.

## Mixed Pieces

### Strictest Rule

The final *mishna* of the daf addresses a distinct case: *nitarvu chatichah be-chatichah* — when pieces of meat from different *korbanot* have already been cut and become mixed together, or *kodshei kodashim be-kodashim kalim* — when meat from those *korbanot* eaten only by *kohanim* (*kodshei kodashim*) is mixed with meat from those that may be eaten even by *Yisraelim* (*kodashim kalim*), or when meat from *korbanot* permitted for only one day becomes mixed with meat permitted for two days — *yochlum ke-chamur she-bahen* — in all such cases, they are eaten in accordance with the strictest applicable rule.

### Leftovers Concern

Rabbi Shimon's position, which is that one eats the mixture according to the most stringent category, is challenged: *ein mevivin kodashim le-beit ha-psul* — one should not bring *kodashim* into a situation of disqualification. If meat permissible to many people is now restricted to *kohanim* alone, the time window is shorter and the pool of permitted consumers is smaller, creating a significant risk that the meat will become *nosar* — leftover beyond the permitted time — and thereby be disqualified retroactively.

### Practical Override

The resolution is that for *chatichot* — pieces of meat that have already been slaughtered, over which the *zrikot* have already been performed, and which have already been cut up — the objection does not stand. One is no longer in a position to choose whether or not to bring the *korban*; the situation is already fixed. In such circumstances, the concern about *nosar* is outweighed by practical necessity. Rabbi Shimon's lenient approach — applying the strictest rule to the mixture and allowing the meat to be eaten under those constraints — is vindicated: of all the concerns one might weigh, the speculative future risk of *nosar* does not warrant preventing the valid consumption of what is already before us.
