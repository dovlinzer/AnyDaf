# Zevachim 75 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Pasul Animals

### Opening

The daf under discussion is Zevachim 75, though we begin on 74b, roughly halfway down the page at the words *haroveiah v'hanirba*. The *mishna* has been discussing the case of animals becoming mixed up with one another, and it presents a long list of animals that are *pasul* to be brought as *korbanot* for a variety of reasons.

### Mishna Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָרוֹבֵעַ וְהַנִּרְבָּע, וְהַמֻּקְצֶה לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וְהַנֶּעֱבַד, וְהַאֶתְנַן וּמְחִיר כֶּלֶב, וְהַכִּלְאַיִם, וְהַטְּרֵיפָה – פְּסוּלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **An animal that copulated with a person, or an animal that was the object of bestiality, an animal that was set aside for idol worship or one that was worshipped as a deity, an animal that was given as payment to a prostitute or as the price of a dog, or an animal born of a mixture of diverse kinds, or an animal that is a *tereifa*** — **are** all **disqualified** from being sacrificed on the altar.

The *mishna* enumerates multiple categories of disqualified animals. *Roveiah* and *nirba* refer to an animal that played either the male or female role in an act of bestiality. *Muktza* is an animal that was set aside for *avodah zarah* but was not actually worshipped. *Ne'evad* is an animal that was itself worshipped. *Etnan* refers to the prohibition derived from the verse concerning payment given to a prostitute. *Mechir kelev* describes an animal whose value was used as purchasing power to acquire a dog — a prohibition explicit in the *Torah*. *Kilayim* refers to a crossbreed — for instance, sheep with wild sheep, or goats with wild goats. And *treifa* refers to an animal that has undergone a disqualifying injury, a topic that will be treated at great length in *Masechet Chullin*.

### Disqualifications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא כּוּלְּהוּ – לָא יְדִיעַ; אֶלָּא הַאי טְרֵיפָה הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי יָדַיע לֵיהּ – לֵיתֵי וְלִישְׁקְלֵיהּ! אִי לָא יָדַע לֵיהּ – מְנָא יָדַע דְּאִיעָרַב?
>
> **Translation:** **Granted,** with regard to **all of** the other examples in this list, the prohibited animal is **not known.** In other words, it is physically indistinguishable from the other animals. **But** with regard to **this *tereifa*** animal, **what are the circumstances? If,** due to its physical impairment, it is **known to him** which animal it is, **let him come and take it** from there, and all the other animals will be permitted. **If it is not known to him, how does he know that** a *tereifa* animal **was intermingled** with others in the first place?

The *gemara*'s *sugia* here focuses primarily on the case of the *treifa*. *Bishlama kulhu* — for most of the animals on this list, one can readily understand how the problematic case arises: the animal has some non-physical disqualification, arising from a sin committed with it or from the manner in which it was used, so that *lo yadia* — it is not visually apparent — and if it becomes mixed into a larger flock, the problem of identification emerges naturally. But the *gemara* asks: *ela hai treifa heichi dami?* — what exactly is the case of a *treifa*? If an animal has sustained a serious enough injury to render it a *treifa*, that injury ought to be visible. How then could it become confused with other animals?

## Treifa Problem

### Treifa Mystery

This question is the pivot around which the *sugia* turns. Three *Amoraim* each offer their own answer, and the *gemara* provides no resolution among them — rather, each opinion stands on its own, connected to other *machlokot* elsewhere.

### Rabbi Yannai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן דְּאִיעָרַב נְקוּבַת הַקּוֹץ בִּדְרוּסַת הַזְּאֵב.
>
> **Translation:** **They say** in **the school of Rabbi Yannai: What are we dealing with here?** We are dealing with a case **where** an animal that was **pierced by a thorn,** which does not render it a *tereifa*, **was intermingled with** an animal that was **clawed by a wolf,** which renders it a *tereifa*. Since the skin of both animals has been pierced, one cannot identify the *tereifa*.

*Amri de-vei Rabbi Yannai* — the school of Rabbi Yannai teaches: *hacha b'mai askinan, kegon de-irav nikuvat hakotz b'drusat haze'ev* — the case is where two different types of injured animals have become mixed with one another. One is *nikuvat hakotz*, an animal punctured by a thorn, which in all likelihood has not pierced any inner organ and therefore does not render the animal a *treifa*. The other is *drusat haze'ev*, an animal attacked by a wolf, where there is a far higher likelihood that internal organs were damaged and the animal has indeed become a *treifa*. According to Rabbi Yannai, both animals bear outward injuries that look similar enough to cause confusion, even though one is a *treifa* and one is not.

### Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: [כְּגוֹן] דְּאִיעָרַב בִּנְפוּלָה. נְפוּלָה נָמֵי לִיבְדְּקַהּ! קָסָבַר: עָמְדָה – צְרִיכָה מֵעֵת לְעֵת, הָלְכָה – צְרִיכָה בְּדִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says** there is a different answer: The mishna is discussing a case **where** a healthy animal **was intermingled with a fallen** animal, i.e., one that has fallen from a great height. A fallen animal is prohibited in case it is a *tereifa*, despite the fact that it bears no external sign of injury. The Gemara raises a difficulty: With regard to **a fallen** animal **as well, let us examine it** and see if it can walk by itself, in which case it is not a *tereifa*. The Gemara answers: According to the opinion of Reish Lakish, even after an examination of this kind it is still a possible *tereifa*, which is prohibited to be sacrificed upon the altar, as **he holds** that if an animal fell and **stood** up again, **it requires** a twenty-four-hour waiting **period** to determine if it is in fact injured. Furthermore, even if it both stood up and **walked** after the fall, **it requires inspection** after slaughter to determine whether it was injured by the fall and rendered a *tereifa*.

Reish Lakish offers a different scenario: *kegon de-it'arev bin'fulah* — the case involves an animal that fell, a *nefulah*. One might then ask: simply examine the animal to determine whether it is truly a *treifa*. The answer is that Reish Lakish holds the position that even if the animal stood up again after its fall (*amda*), it still requires *me'et le'et* — a twenty-four hour observation period — before one may conclude it was unharmed. And even if it was subsequently able to walk (*halcha*), it still requires *bedika* — a post-*shechita* examination. This is because, in Reish Lakish's view, once an animal has sustained an injury, its *chazakat kashrut* is disrupted. The animal is no longer presumed healthy; rather, the burden of proof has shifted, and one must now affirmatively demonstrate that it has recovered. Under this framework, an animal of uncertain status after a fall could easily become mixed with others before a definitive determination has been made.

### Rabbi Yirmiya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אָמַר: כְּגוֹן דְּאִיעָרַיב בִּוְלַד טְרֵיפָה – וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: וְלַד טְרֵיפָה לֹא יִקְרַב לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya says** there is a third answer: The mishna is referring to a case **where** a healthy animal **was intermingled with the offspring of a *tereifa*,** which of course bears no sign of a *tereifa*. **And** this **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, who says: The offspring of a *tereifa* may not be sacrificed upon the altar.**

Rabbi Yirmiya offers a third approach: *kegon de-it'arev bivlad treifa* — the case involves not an animal that itself bears a visible injury, but rather a *vlad treifa*, an animal born from a mother who was herself a *treifa*. Such an animal carries a disqualification but has no physical mark by which it can be identified. This is precisely why the mixture is intractable. Rabbi Yirmiya's position is grounded in the view of Rabbi Eliezer: *de-amar vlad treifa lo yikarev legavei hamizbe'ach* — that the offspring of a *treifa* mother is disqualified from being brought as a *korban*.

To sharpen the point: all three *Amoraim* are answering the same underlying question — how can a *treifa* animal get confused among others? Rabbi Yannai's answer is that there *is* a visible injury on the *treifa*, but another animal in the flock also has a similar-looking injury that is not disqualifying, creating genuine confusion between the two. Reish Lakish's answer is that the animal's status is genuinely uncertain, having not yet been definitively resolved through the requisite observation and examination. And Rabbi Yirmiya's answer is that the *treifa* animal in question bears no injury at all — its disqualification is genealogical, and therefore invisible.

## Rejections

### Against Yannai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כּוּלְּהוּ כְּרַבִּי יַנַּאי לָא אָמְרִי – בֵּין נְקוּבַת הַקּוֹץ לִדְרוּסַת הַזְּאֵב מִידָּע יְדִיעַ; הַאי מְשִׁיךְ וְהַאי עֲגִיל.
>
> **Translation:** **All of them,** i.e., Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yirmeya, **do not say** that the explanation of the mishna is **in accordance with** the explanation of **Rabbi Yannai** that an animal pierced by a thorn became mixed up with one clawed by a wolf, as they maintain that the difference **between** an animal **pierced by a thorn and** one that was **clawed by a wolf is known,** as **this** perforation caused by a wolf is **elongated, and that** perforation caused by a thorn is **round.**

The *gemara* now explains why each *Amora* rejects the other positions. Why do Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yirmiya not accept Rabbi Yannai's answer? Because *bein nikuvat hakotz ledrusat haze'ev meda yadi'a* — the wound from a thorn and the wound from a wolf are actually distinguishable upon examination. *Hai mashich ve-hai agil* — the wolf's bite produces an elongated wound (the wolf tears at the flesh with multiple teeth, leaving a drawn-out mark), while the thorn produces a circular puncture. This distinction — *mashuch ve-agul* — appears elsewhere as well, for instance in discussions of *ketamim* in *Hilchot Niddah*, though in that context we are generally not strict about distinguishing between them. In the context of identifying a *treifa*, however, Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yirmiya maintain that the distinction is visually discernible and therefore Rabbi Yannai's case does not present a genuine problem of confusion.

### Against Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ לָא אָמְרִי – קָסָבְרִי: עָמְדָה – אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה מֵעֵת לְעֵת, הָלְכָה – אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה בְּדִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: Rabbi Yannai and Rabbi Yirmeya **do not say** that the explanation of the mishna is **in accordance with** the explanation of **Reish Lakish,** that the mishna is discussing a case where a healthy animal became intermingled with one that has fallen, as **they hold** that if a fallen animal **stood,** it **does not require** a twenty-four-hour waiting **period,** and if it **walked it does not require** any further **inspection** after slaughter at all. Consequently, one can simply examine the animals to see if they can walk, and if they can, they are fit.

Why do Rabbi Yannai and Rabbi Yirmiya not accept Reish Lakish's answer? *Kasvari amda einah tzerichah me'et le'et, halcha einah tzerichah bedikah* — they hold the more lenient position: if an animal stood up after its fall, no twenty-four hour waiting period is required, and if it was able to walk, no post-*shechita* examination is necessary. In their view, the animal's ability to stand and walk is itself sufficient demonstration that it did not sustain a serious injury. The *chazakah* is not disrupted by the fall once the animal has recovered its mobility, and therefore the scenario Reish Lakish describes — where an animal of uncertain status mingles with others before its condition is resolved — simply does not arise under their ruling.

### Against Yirmiya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה לָא אָמְרִי – כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לָא מוֹקְמִי.
>
> **Translation:** Finally, Rabbi Yannai and Reish Lakish **do not say** that the explanation of the mishna is **in accordance with** the explanation of **Rabbi Yirmeya,** that the mishna is discussing a case where a healthy animal became intermingled with the offspring of a *tereifa*, as they **do not** want to **interpret** the mishna **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer** because the *halakha* does not follow his ruling.

Why do Rabbi Yannai and Reish Lakish not accept Rabbi Yirmiya's answer? Because *ke-Rabbi Eliezer lo mukmi* — they do not hold like Rabbi Eliezer that the *vlad treifa* is disqualified at all. If the offspring of a *treifa* mother is not itself considered a *treifa* in their view, then Rabbi Yirmiya's case is not a case of a *treifa* mixed with other animals. The elegance of the *sugia* lies precisely here: each *Amora* has a coherent position that generates both its own answer to the original question and principled grounds for rejecting every alternative.

## Korbanot Mix

### Kodashim Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָדָשִׁים בְּקָדָשִׁים מִין בְּמִינוֹ, זֶה יִקְרַב לְשֵׁם מִי שֶׁהוּא, וְזֶה יִקְרַב לְשֵׁם מִי שֶׁהוּא.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where **sacrificial** animals were intermingled **with** other **sacrificial** animals, if it was an animal of one **type** of offering **with** animals of the same **type** of offering, one should sacrifice this animal for the sake of whoever is its owner and one should sacrifice that animal for the sake of whoever is its owner, and both owners fulfill their obligations.

The *mishna* continues with a new principle: *kodashim be-kodashim min be-mino, zeh yikarev leshem mi she-hu ve-zeh yikarev leshem mi she-hu* — when *korbanot* of the same type are mixed together and the owners cannot identify their individual animals, each animal is brought with the general intention that it be offered on behalf of whomever owns it. Rather than designating it for a specific person, the *kohen* employs a catch-all *machshavah* that covers whichever owner's animal this turns out to be.

### Semichah Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא בָּעֵי סְמִיכָה!
>
> **Translation:** **But** each animal **requires placing hands** on its head, a rite that must be performed by its owner, and in this case the owner is unknown.

The *gemara* raises a challenge: *ve-ha ba'ei semichah* — does not a *korban* require *semichah*, the laying of hands by the owner? If the owners cannot identify which animal is theirs, how can *semichah* be performed *lechatchilah*? The *gemara* acknowledges that *semichah* is not *me'akev be-di'avad* — it does not invalidate the *korban* after the fact — but there remains a *chiyuv* to perform it, and deliberately bringing a *korban* without it seems less than ideal. The question, then, is how the *mishna* can endorse this procedure as a legitimate resolution.

### Rav Yosef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: בְּקׇרְבַּן נָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef says:** The *halakha* of the mishna is stated **with regard to an offering of women,** who do not perform the placing of hands.

*Amar Rav Yosef*: the *mishna* must be speaking specifically about *korban nashim* — offerings brought by women — because women are not required to perform *semichah*. There is accordingly no problem of failing to fulfill the obligation of *semichah* when the owners cannot identify their animals.

## Abaye Challenge

### Baraita Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: קׇרְבַּן יָחִיד שֶׁנִּתְעָרֵב בְּקׇרְבַּן יָחִיד, וְקׇרְבַּן צִיבּוּר שֶׁנִּתְעָרֵב בְּקׇרְבַּן צִיבּוּר, וְקׇרְבַּן יָחִיד וְקׇרְבַּן צִיבּוּר שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבוּ זֶה בָּזֶה – נוֹתֵן אַרְבַּע מַתָּנוֹת מִכׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד. וְאִם נָתַן מַתָּנָה מִכׇּל אֶחָד – יָצָא, וְאִם נָתַן אַרְבַּע מִכּוּלָּן – יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to** this from a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 8:22): With regard to **the offering of an individual that was intermingled with** another **offering of an individual, and** likewise **a communal offering that was intermingled with** another **communal offering, or the offering of an individual and a communal offering that were intermingled with each other,** the priest **places four placements** of blood **from each and every one** of them on the altar, and in this manner fulfills the obligation of the blood rites of all the offerings. **But if he placed** one **placement from each one,** he has **fulfilled** his obligation. **And** likewise, **if he placed four** placements **from all of them** together, he has **fulfilled** his obligation.

Abaye challenges Rav Yosef. He cites a *baraita*: *korban yachid she-nitarev be-korban yachid, u-ve-korban tzibbur she-nitarev be-korban tzibbur* — if one individual's *korban* was mixed with another individual's *korban*, or if one communal *korban* was mixed with another communal *korban*, or even if an individual's *korban* was mixed with a communal *korban* (*korban yachid ve-korban tzibbur she-nitarvu zeh ba-zeh*) — in all these cases, *noten arba matanot mikkol echad ve-echad*: the blood of each *korban* must be applied in four *matanot* on the four *karnot hamizbe'ach*, because one of the animals in the mixture might be a *korban chatat* for which this is required.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבוּ חַיִּין, אֲבָל נִתְעָרְבוּ שְׁחוּטִין – נוֹתֵן אַרְבַּע מַתָּנוֹת מִכּוּלָּן. וְאִם נָתַן מַתָּנָה אַחַת מִכּוּלָּן – יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **In what** case **is this statement,** that he places from the blood of each of the offerings *ab initio*, **said?** In a case **where** the offerings **were intermingled** when they were still **alive,** before they were sacrificed, and therefore the priest could perform four placements from the blood of each animal separately. **But** if **slaughtered** animals **were** then **intermingled,** i.e., their blood became mixed together in one container, the priest **places four placements from all of them** together, only one set of four. **And** in any case, **if he placed one placement from all of them,** he has **fulfilled** his obligation, as the blood of all these offerings is placed on the outer altar, and with regard to all of them one fulfills the obligation after the fact with a single placement.

*Ve-im natan matanah mikkol echad yatza* — if, *be-di'avad*, only one *matanah* was given from each animal individually, that suffices, since even for a *chatat* a single *matanah* is valid after the fact. *Ve-im natan arba mikkullan yatza* — and even if the blood of all the animals was mixed together and only four *matanot* were given from the combined blood, that too is *yotzei*.

The *baraita* then distinguishes between two scenarios: *bemei devarim amurim keshe-nitarvu chayyim* — this applies when the animals were mixed while still alive; *aval nitarvu shechutim* — but if they were mixed after *shechita*, *noten arba matanot mikkullan* — one gives four *matanot* from each, and *ve-im natan matanah achat mikkullan yatza* — even one *matanah* from all the blood together suffices.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי יָחִיד דּוּמְיָא דְּצִיבּוּר – מָה צִיבּוּר גַּבְרֵי, אַף יָחִיד גַּבְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the difficulty: This *baraita* indicates that if live offerings became intermingled, each is sacrificed for its owner, despite the fact that these offerings are owned by men and therefore require placing of the hands. It is evident that the *baraita* is referring to offerings of men, as it **teaches** the case of the offering of **an individual** adjacent to, and therefore **similar to,** the case of **a communal** offering: **Just as a communal** offering is sacrificed by **men, so too,** the offering of **an individual** that is being discussed here also belongs to **men.** This presents a difficulty to the explanation of Rav Yosef.

Abaye's challenge to Rav Yosef emerges from the *baraita*'s language: *katani yachid — dumya de-tzibbur* — the *baraita* discusses an individual's *korban* in terms that parallel the communal *korban*. Communal *korbanot* are funded by the *shekalim* donated by men (*gavrei*), and therefore the *tzibbur*'s *korbanot* are as if owned by men. By analogy, the *yachid* cases in the *baraita* should likewise refer to men's offerings — thereby undermining Rav Yosef's claim that the *mishna* speaks only of women's *korbanot*.

### Rava Interprets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וְתִסְבְּרָא הָא מְתָרַצְתָּא הִיא?! דְּקָתָנֵי: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – כְּשֶׁנִּתְעָרְבוּ חַיִּין, אֲבָל נִתְעָרְבוּ שְׁחוּטִין – לָא. מָה לִי חַיִּים מָה לִי שְׁחוּטִין?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: And can you understand** the *baraita* in this manner? Is **this** *baraita* **accurate** as it stands? There is another difficulty with the *baraita*, **as it teaches: In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said in a case **where** these offerings **were intermingled** when they were still **alive. But** if **slaughtered** animals **were intermingled** it is **not** said. This is problematic, as **what** difference is it **to me** whether the animals are **alive** or **whether** they are **slaughtered?** This is not the decisive factor, as even if the animals were slaughtered, if the blood of each is in a separate vessel the priest should perform separate placements from the blood of each.

*Amar Rava*: in fact this *baraita* supports Rav Yosef rather than contradicting him. Rava reads the *baraita*'s distinction more carefully. The contrast between animals mixed while alive and blood mixed after *shechita* is not really about the timing per se, but about the physical configuration.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבוּ שְׁחוּטִין כְּעֵין חַיִּים, בְּכוֹסוֹת; אֲבָל בְּבוֹלֵל – נוֹתֵן אַרְבַּע מַתָּנוֹת לְכוּלָּן. וְאִם נָתַן מַתָּנָה אַחַת לְכוּלָּן – יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues: **Rather, this** is what the *baraita* **is saying: In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said in a case **where** these offerings **were intermingled** after they were **slaughtered,** but they were **similar to living** animals, i.e., the blood of each animal was **in** separate **cups. But** in a situation **where** the blood of these animals was **mixed** together in a single cup, the priest **places four placements** of blood **from all of them. And if he placed one placement from all of them,** he has **fulfilled** his obligation. If so, this *baraita* has no bearing on the issue of offerings that became intermingled while they were still alive, as it deals exclusively with the blood of animals that have been slaughtered.

*She-nitarvu shechutim ke-ein chayyim* — "mixed after *shechita* in a manner similar to when alive" means that even after *shechita*, the blood was kept in separate containers (*be-kossot*), treating each animal's blood as distinct — analogous to the animals being separate while alive. *Aval be-vulul* — if, however, the blood was blended together into one vessel, then one gives four *matanot* from the combined blood, and a single *matanah* from the mixture suffices.

### Rabbi's Volume Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: רוֹאִין אֶת הַמַּתָּנָה; אִם יֵשׁ בָּהּ כְּדֵי לָזֶה וּכְדֵי לָזֶה – כְּשֵׁרָה, וְאִם לָאו – פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: One assesses the** blood of the **placement** given from each animal; **if there is enough in** that blood for a placement of blood **for this** offering **and enough for that** one, it is **fit, but if not,** the offering is **disqualified.**

*Rebbi omer*: *ro'in et ha-matanah im yesh bah kedei la-zeh ve-kedei la-zeh, keshera; ve-im lav, pesulah* — we examine whether the *matanah* placed on the *mizbe'ach* contains enough blood to account for a valid portion from each *korban*. If there is sufficient volume, it is *keshera*; if not, it is *pesulah*.

This raises a question: where does Rebbi derive the principle that the blood must be of sufficient quantity? For a *korban chatat*, only a minimal amount of blood would seem necessary.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִית לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי הַאי סְבָרָא?! וְהָא תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי: לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, הַזָּאָה כֹּל שֶׁהִיא – מְטַהֶרֶת; הַזָּאָה אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה שִׁיעוּר; הַזָּאָה מֶחֱצָה כָּשֵׁר וּמֶחֱצָה פָּסוּל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **asks: And does Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **hold** in accordance with **this** line of **reasoning,** that there must be a minimum amount of blood placed from each offering? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Para* 9:5) with regard to the water of purification into which water that is invalid for sprinkling fell that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: According to the statement of Rabbi Eliezer, a sprinkling of any** amount of the water of purification on someone rendered impure by impurity imparted by a corpse **purifies** him from his ritual impurity, as **sprinkling does not require** a minimum **measure** to purify him. Likewise, **a sprinkling** of water that is **half fit and half unfit** for sprinkling serves to purify the individual. Evidently, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi maintains that no minimum amount is required for the sprinkling of the water of purification, and the same should apply to the sprinkling of blood on the altar.

The *gemara* notes that a *Tosefta* in *Parah* records Rebbi as saying, in the name of Rabbi Eliezer: *hazaya kol she-hu metaheret* — even the tiniest drop of *mei chatat* suffices for purification, with no minimum measure required (*hazaya eina tzerichah shi'ur*). This appears to cut against Rebbi's position regarding the *matanah* of blood.

## Parah Rules

### Eliezer Minimum

The *Tosefta* in *Parah* records Rebbi stating, within the framework of Rabbi Eliezer's view: *hazaya kol she-hu metaheret* — that even the most minuscule quantity of *mei chatat* landing on the *tamei* person effects purification. There is no defined minimum measure of water required.

### Parah vs. Korban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִדְבָרָיו דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר קָאָמַר. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הַזָּאָה לְחוּד וּנְתִינָה לְחוּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **stated** his opinion **in accordance with the statement of Rabbi Eliezer,** but he himself does not agree with this opinion. **And if you wish, say** instead that **sprinkling** of water of purification **is discrete and placement** of blood on the altar **is discrete,** i.e., these are two separate *halakhot* that have no bearing on each other.

The *gemara* raises the apparent difficulty: if Rebbi himself holds, following Rabbi Eliezer, that there is no minimum quantity for *hazaya*, how can he require a minimum volume for a *matanah* of blood? The resolution offered is *hazaya le-chud ve-netinah le-chud* — the act of sprinkling (*hazaya*) for *parah adumah* is categorically distinct from the act of placing blood (*netinah*) on the *mizbe'ach* for a *korban*. The very nature of sprinkling already implies atomized, minimal quantities. By contrast, the *matanah* of blood for a *korban chatat* — applied with the *kohen*'s finger to the *keren* — and certainly the *shtayim she-hen arba* applied with a vessel (*kos*) — involves a substantial, definite quantity of blood. The two acts are sufficiently different in kind that one may distinguish between them, and Rebbi's position on *parah* does not undermine his position on *korbanot*.

## Bechor Ma'aser

### Grazing Till Mum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְעָרְבוּ בִּבְכוֹר וּמַעֲשֵׂר – יִרְעוּ עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֲבוּ, וְיֵאָכְלוּ כִּבְכוֹר וּכְמַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where sacrificial animals **were intermingled with a firstborn** offering **or with** an animal **tithe** offering, they shall graze until they develop a *mum*. Once a *mum* appears, **they are eaten in the manner appropriate to a firstborn or as an animal tithe offering** that developed a blemish.

The *mishna* addresses the case of *nitarvu bechor u-ma'aser* — when disqualified animals have been mixed with *bechor* animals or with *ma'aser* animals. *Yireu ad she-yistavu* — the entire group must graze until they develop a *mum*. Once a *mum* appears, *veyachlu ke-vechor u-ke-ma'aser* — they are eaten in the manner appropriate to a *bechor* or *ma'aser* that developed a blemish.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל יְכוֹלִין לְהִתְעָרֵב, חוּץ מִן הַחַטָּאת וְהַאָשָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** The *mishna* then concludes: **All categories of *korbanot* may potentially become mixed together and admit of resolution, except for a sin offering and a guilt offering**, which have a unique and personal connection to the owner on account of the specific sin being atoned for.

The *mishna* then concludes: *ha-kol yecholim le-hitarev chutz min ha-chatat ve-ha-asham* — all categories of *korbanot* may potentially become mixed together and admit of resolution, except for *chatat* and *asham*, which have a unique and personal connection to the owner on account of the specific sin being atoned for.

### Bechor Restrictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: בְּכוֹר לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי – אֵין מַאֲכִילִין לְנִדּוֹת; תְּמוּרָתוֹ מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama says:** With regard to **a firstborn** offering, **according to** the opinion of **Beit Shammai,** who hold (*Bekhorot* 33a) that even after a firstborn offering develops a blemish the priest may not eat it in a state of ritual impurity, and therefore **one may not feed** it **to menstruating women, what is** the *halakha* with regard to **its substitute?** That is, if the owner of a firstborn animal stated with regard to a non-sacred animal in his possession: This animal is a substitute for the firstborn offering, is a menstruating woman permitted to eat that substitute after it develops a blemish?

*Amar Rabbi bar Chama* — Rabbi bar Chama raises a series of questions about the practical implications of the *bechor*'s status. According to Beit Shammai, even a *bechor* that has developed a *mum* — and has therefore become, in a sense, ordinary — may not be fed to a woman in *niddah*. There is some residual *kedushat bechor* that persists.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּכוֹר אֵינוֹ נִפְדֶּה; תְּמוּרָתוֹ מַהוּ? בְּכוֹר אֵינוֹ נִשְׁקָל בְּלִיטְרָא; תְּמוּרָתוֹ מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** Rami bar Ḥama further asks: **A firstborn** offering itself **may not be redeemed,** even after it develops a blemish, but **what is** the *halakha* with regard to redeeming **its substitute?** He likewise inquired: **A firstborn** offering itself, even after it develops a blemish, **may not be weighed** and sold **by the *litra*,** in the manner that non-sacred meat is sold. **What is** the *halakha* of **its substitute** in this regard?

Rabbi bar Chama then asks a series of follow-on questions: *temuraso mahu* — what is the status of an animal exchanged for a *bechor* through *temurah*? Since *bechor eino nifdah* — a *bechor* cannot be redeemed — can one make a *temurah* with it at all? And separately: *bechor eino nishkal be-litra* — the *kohen* may not sell the *bechor*'s meat by weight in the marketplace; what then of its *temuraso* — does the same restriction apply to the animal received in exchange?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, תַּנְיָא: בְּכוֹר וּמַעֲשֵׂר מִשֶּׁהוּמְמוּ – עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה, וּתְמוּרָתָן כְּיוֹצֵא בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says** that an answer to these questions **is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a firstborn** offering **or** an animal **tithe** offering, **from when they develop a blemish they** can be used to **render** another animal **a substitute, and their substitute** has the same status **as them.** Their substitutes have the same *halakhot* as they do.

### Dual Kedusha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: הִתְפִּיס בְּכוֹר לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת, מַהוּ שֶׁיִּשְׁקוֹל בְּלִיטְרָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama raises a dilemma:** If the priest who received a firstborn offering **dedicated** the **firstborn** offering **to the Temple maintenance,** i.e., he vowed to give the value of the firstborn offering to the Temple, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to the matter **that he may weigh** its meat **by the *litra*?**

*Rami bar Chama* poses a related question to Rava: *hitpis bechor le-vedek ha-bayit mahu* — what if one attempted to donate a *bechor* to *bedek ha-bayit*, the Temple maintenance fund, thereby seeking to infuse it with *kedushat bedek ha-bayit*? *Mahu she-yishkol be-litra* — would this then permit the meat to be weighed out and sold retail after *shechita*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַוְוחָא דְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ עֲדִיף, אוֹ דִלְמָא זִילוּתָא דִּבְכוֹר עָדִיף?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the dilemma: In this case, is consideration of **the profit of the Temple** treasury **preferable, or perhaps** avoidance of the **demeaning of the firstborn** offering is **preferable.** If the profit of the Temple treasury is preferable, the firstborn offering should be weighed in the normal manner so as to increase its price; a buyer will pay more for meat he is able to resell afterward by exact weight. If avoidance of demeaning the firstborn offering is preferable, it should not be weighed by the *litra*, even though a loss to the Temple would result.

The reasoning: *ravcha de-hekdesh adif* — increasing the financial return for *hekdesh* by selling at retail prices might override the usual *bechor* restriction. On the other hand, *zilusa de-vechor adif* — perhaps one should instead give precedence to the dignity of the *bechor* and its associated laws, even at the cost of a lower yield for *hekdesh*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר זְבִידָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: נִתְעָרְבוּ בִּבְכוֹר וּבְמַעֲשֵׂר – יִרְעוּ עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֲבוּ, וְיֵאָכְלוּ כִּבְכוֹר וּכְמַעֲשֵׂר. לָאו לְמֵימְרָא דְּאֵינוֹ נִשְׁקָל בְּלִיטְרָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei bar Zevida says: Come** and **hear** a proof from the ruling of the mishna: In a case where sacrificial animals **were intermingled with a firstborn** offering **or with** an animal **tithe** offering, **they shall graze until they become unfit** for sacrifice **and they shall** both **be eaten as a firstborn** offering **or as** an animal **tithe** offering. Is this **not** meaning **to say that** the sacrificial animal that became intermingled with a firstborn offering **is** also **not weighed by the *litra*,** despite the fact that this causes a slight loss to the Temple?

*Rabbi's Volume Test* attempts to bring a proof from our *mishna*: the fact that animals mixed with *bechor* and *ma'aser* must graze until they develop a *mum* and are then eaten *ke-vechor u-ke-ma'aser* suggests that the *bechor* retains its distinctive restrictions even after developing a *mum* — including the prohibition against weighing the meat. This would seem to argue against any leniency flowing from the donation to *bedek ha-bayit*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא וְרַבִּי חִזְקִיָּה תַּלְמִידֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אָמְרִי: מִי דָּמֵי?! הָתָם שְׁתֵּי קְדוּשּׁוֹת וּשְׁנֵי גּוּפִין, הָכָא שְׁתֵּי קְדוּשּׁוֹת וְגוּף אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna and Rabbi Ḥizkiyya,** the **students of Rabbi Yirmeya, say: Are** these cases **comparable? There,** the mishna is referring to **two** separate **sanctities,** the sanctity of a firstborn offering and that of another offering, **and two** separate **bodies,** i.e., two different animals, and in such a case one may not degrade the sanctity of the firstborn due to the other animal with which it is intermingled. Conversely, **here,** with regard to one who vows to give the value of a firstborn to the Temple, there are **two sanctities,** a firstborn offering and the Temple maintenance, **but** they are both found in **one body.** In this case, as the sanctity of the Temple maintenance itself is in the firstborn offering, it may be weighed by the *litra*, because of the profit the Temple will gain.

Rav Huna and Rabbi Chizkiya, students of Rabbi Yirmiya, respond: *mi dami? Hatam shtei kedushot u-shnei gufin* — in the *mishna*'s case, we have two distinct sanctities (*kedushat bechor* and the disqualified animal's status) in two separate bodies. *Hacha shtei kedushot ve-guf echad* — here, by contrast, we are discussing an attempt to impose two sanctities on one and the same animal: *kedushat bechor* and *kedushat bedek ha-bayit* simultaneously. That is a fundamentally different configuration and the *mishna* may not constitute a proof.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר אָבִין, מָה אִילּוּ אָמַר: ״הַפְדּוּ לִי בְּכוֹר שֶׁהִתְפִּיסוֹ לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת״ – כְּלוּם שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ?! ״הַפְדּוּ״?! רַחֲמָנָא אָמַר: ״לֹא תִפְדֶּה״!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosei bar Avin objects to this: What** would be the *halakha* **if one says: Redeem for me,** i.e., I wish to redeem, **a firstborn** offering **that he** had previously **dedicated** as the object of his vow **to the Temple maintenance? Does** the court **listen to him?** His request is certainly not granted, as by Torah law a firstborn offering may not be redeemed. Similarly, the fact that he dedicated the value of the firstborn offering to the Temple should not negate the prohibition of weighing it by the *litra*. The Gemara questions this comparison: One cannot raise a difficulty from a case where someone says: **Redeem** the firstborn offering for me, as **the Merciful One states** with regard to a firstborn offering: "But the firstborn of a bull, or the firstborn of a sheep, or the firstborn of a goat, **you shall not redeem;** they are sacred" (Numbers 18:17). But the weighing of the offering by the *litra* is not prohibited by Torah law.

*Matkif lah Rabbi Yosi bar Avin*: if someone declared, "Redeem for me my *bechor* that I donated to *bedek ha-bayit*" — one would not heed him, since *bechor* cannot be redeemed (*eino nifdeh*). If the attempted donation to *bedek ha-bayit* cannot even be undone through *pidyon*, it suggests the donation itself may not have taken meaningful effect.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: כְּלוּם הִקְנָה זֶה – אֶלָּא מַה שֶּׁקָּנוּי לוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara suggests a different resolution of Rami bar Ḥama's dilemma: **Rabbi Ami says: Can this** priest **transfer** to the Temple maintenance anything **other than that which was transferred to him?** In other words, just as the priest who took the vow may not weigh the firstborn by the *litra* and gain a profit, the same applies to the Temple treasury, the recipient of his vow.

*Amar Rabbi Ami*: *klum hikne zeh ela ma she-kanu lo* — the *bedek ha-bayit* does not genuinely acquire the *bechor*, because the *bechor* already bears its own *kedusha* as a *korban* and an animal destined for the altar cannot simply be redirected to *bedek ha-bayit* in this fashion. The attempted donation of a *tam* animal earmarked as a *korban* to *bedek ha-bayit* does not succeed in the ordinary way.

## Chatat Asham

### Gender Distinctions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל יְכוֹלִין לְהִתְעָרֵב כּוּ׳. מַאי שְׁנָא חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם – דְּהַאי זָכָר וְהַאי נְקֵבָה;
>
> **Translation:** The *mishna* teaches that **all offerings can become intermingled,** except for a sin offering and a guilt offering. The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **a sin offering and a guilt offering,** that they cannot become intermingled? Is the reason **that this,** a guilt offering, is always **a male, and that,** a sin offering, is always **a female?**

The *gemara* returns to the *mishna*'s rule that *chatat* and *asham* cannot be mixed with other *korbanot*. The question is: why are these two specifically exceptional? One might suggest *de-hai zachar ve-hai nekeva* — that the *chatat* is typically a female animal and the *asham* is always male, and so distinguishing between them by gender seems straightforward.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַטָּאת וְעוֹלָה נָמֵי! אִיכָּא שְׂעִיר נָשִׂיא; הַאי שֵׂיעָר וְהַאי צֶמֶר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this explanation: **A sin offering and a burnt offering are also** like this, as a sin offering is always female while a burnt offering is always male. The Gemara answers that although the standard sin offering is always female, **there is** the **goat** of the ***Nasi*,** which is a male sin offering, and therefore it can become intermingled with a male goat burnt offering. And as to the statement of the mishna that a sin offering cannot become intermingled with a guilt offering despite the male sin offering of the *Nasi*, that is because **this** goat has straight **hair and that** guilt offering comes only from sheep or rams, which have **wool,** and the wool is curly.

But the *gemara* immediately notes *chatat ve-ola nami* — the same logic would apply to *chatat* and *olah*, since *olah* must also be male. So why can a *chatat* not become mixed with an *olah*?

### Age & Type

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פֶּסַח וְאָשָׁם נָמֵי לָא מִיעָרַב – הַאי בֶּן שָׁנָה וְהַאי בֶּן שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **A Paschal offering and a guilt offering also cannot become intermingled,** as **this** Paschal offering is **in its first year, and that** guilt offering is **in its second year.**

The *gemara* then asks: *pesach ve-asham lama lo mitarev* — why then cannot a *korban Pesach* and a *korban asham* become mixed? Both can be sheep. The response is *hai ben shana ve-hai ben shtei shanim* — the *korban Pesach* must be in its first year and the *korban asham* must be in its second year, so their age serves as a distinguishing feature.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא אֲשַׁם נָזִיר וַאֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: אִיכָּא בֶּן שָׁנָה דְּמִיחֲזֵי כְּבֶן שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים, וְאִיכָּא בֶּן שְׁתַּיִם דְּמִיחֲזֵי כְּבֶן שָׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **answers that there is the guilt offering of a nazirite and the guilt offering of a leper,** which must be in their first year. **And if you wish, say** instead that an animal in its first year can become intermingled with an animal in its second year, as **there is** an animal **in its first year that looks like** an animal **in its second year, and** likewise **there is** an animal **in its second year that looks like** an animal **in its first year.**

The *gemara* counters: *ika asham nazir ve-asham metzora* — the *asham* of a *nazir* and the *asham* of a *metzora* need not be older animals and could fall within the same age range as a *korban Pesach*.

### Age Counting

*Ve-iba'it eima* — alternatively, one may say: *ika ben shana de-michzei ke-ven shtei shanim, ve-ika ben shtei shanim de-michzei ke-ven shana* — a one-year-old animal may look like a two-year-old, and vice versa. Moreover, the ages of animals are not counted from their individual birthdays. Since animals have a birthing season and are born around the same time of year, they are classified by seasonal cohort — from *Rosh Chodesh Elul* or *Rosh Chodesh Tishrei* — rather than by the precise date of birth. As a result, there is genuine ambiguity about whether a given animal falls into the one-year or two-year category, and this can generate real cases of confusion even when examining animals of nominally different age classes.

## Mixed Pieces

### Strictest Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אָשָׁם שֶׁנִּתְעָרֵב בִּשְׁלָמִים – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵיהֶם יִשָּׁחֲטוּ בַּצָּפוֹן, וְיֵאָכְלוּ כֶּחָמוּר שֶׁבָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a guilt offering that was intermingled with a peace offering, Rabbi Shimon says: Both of them should be slaughtered in the north** of the Temple courtyard, as a guilt offering must be slaughtered in the north while a peace offering may be slaughtered anywhere in the courtyard. **And** they both **must be eaten in accordance with** the *halakha* of **the** more **stringent of them,** i.e., the guilt offering, with the following *halakhot*: They may be eaten only in the courtyard rather than throughout Jerusalem; by male priests and not by any ritually pure Jew; and on the day they were sacrificed and the following night, and not on the day they were sacrificed, the following day, and the intervening night.

The final *mishna* of the daf addresses a distinct case: *nitarvu chatichah be-chatichah* — when pieces of meat from different *korbanot* have already been cut and become mixed together, or *kodshei kodashim be-kodashim kalim* — when meat from those *korbanot* eaten only by *kohanim* (*kodshei kodashim*) is mixed with meat from those that may be eaten even by *Yisraelim* (*kodashim kalim*), or when meat from *korbanot* permitted for only one day becomes mixed with meat permitted for two days — *yochlum ke-chamur she-bahen* — in all such cases, they are eaten in accordance with the strictest applicable rule.

### Leftovers Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵין מְבִיאִין קָדָשִׁים לְבֵית הַפְּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to** Rabbi Shimon: One may not limit the time of the consumption of an offering, as **one may not bring sacrificial** animals **to the status of unfitness.** According to Rabbi Shimon's opinion, the peace offering becomes leftover, *notar*, the morning after it is sacrificed, and not at the end of that day, as is the *halakha* concerning peace offerings. Rather, the Rabbis hold, the owner shall wait until these animals become blemished, redeem them, and bring an offering of each type that is worth the monetary value of the higher-quality animal among them.

Rabbi Shimon's position, which is that one eats the mixture according to the most stringent category, is challenged: *ein mevivin kodashim le-beit ha-psul* — one should not bring *kodashim* into a situation of disqualification. If meat permissible to many people is now restricted to *kohanim* alone, the time window is shorter and the pool of permitted consumers is smaller, creating a significant risk that the meat will become *nosar* — leftover beyond the permitted time — and thereby be disqualified retroactively.

### Practical Override

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְעָרְבוּ חֲתִיכוֹת בַּחֲתִיכוֹת – קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, הַנֶּאֱכָלִין לְיוֹם אֶחָד בְּנֶאֱכָלִין לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וְלַיְלָה – יֵאָכְלוּ כֶּחָמוּר שֶׁבָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna adds: Even according to the opinion of the Rabbis, if **pieces** of the meat of one offering **were intermingled with pieces** of the meat of another offering, e.g., meat from **offerings of the most sacred order with** meat from **offerings of lesser sanctity;** or if pieces of meat from offerings **eaten for one day** and the following night were intermingled **with** pieces of meat from offerings **eaten for two days and** one **night,** since in that case the remedy with regard to offerings that were intermingled cannot be implemented, they both **must be eaten in accordance with** the *halakha* of **the** more **stringent of them.**

The resolution is that for *chatichot* — pieces of meat that have already been slaughtered, over which the *zrikot* have already been performed, and which have already been cut up — the objection does not stand. One is no longer in a position to choose whether or not to bring the *korban*; the situation is already fixed. In such circumstances, the concern about *nosar* is outweighed by practical necessity. Rabbi Shimon's lenient approach — applying the strictest rule to the mixture and allowing the meat to be eaten under those constraints — is vindicated: of all the concerns one might weigh, the speculative future risk of *nosar* does not warrant preventing the valid consumption of what is already before us.
