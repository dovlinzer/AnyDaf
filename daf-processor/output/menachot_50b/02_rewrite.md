# Menachot 50b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chinuch & Kiddush

### Three Inaugurations

The mishnah catalogues several distinct inaugurations of the Tabernacle vessels. The golden inner altar — the *mizbe'ach hazahav*, synonymous with the *mizbe'ach haketoret* — is inaugurated through the *ketoret hasamim*. The outer altar, the *mizbe'ach ha'olah*, is inaugurated through the *tamid shel shachar*. And now the mishnah turns to the third item: *velo et hashulchan ela belechem hapanim beshabbat* — the *shulchan* is inaugurated specifically through the *lechem hapanim*, and specifically on *Shabbat*.

### Chinuch vs Kiddush

The Gemara asks whether the mishnah's insistence on *Shabbat* carries an implicit permissive inference: since it says you cannot *be-mechanech* the *shulchan* except through the *lechem hapanim* on *Shabbat*, perhaps the *chinuch* (inauguration) requires *Shabbat*, but mere *kiddush* (sanctification) could be accomplished on a weekday?

The Gemara rejects this reading entirely. The mishnah is not trying to separate *chinuch* from *kiddush* — it is coming to tell us precisely the opposite: *chinuch ve'kiddush shulchan beshabbat hu* — the inauguration and the sanctification occur together, at one and the same moment. Do not infer from the word *chinuch* that the two can be disentangled.

### Menora Lighting

The point becomes clearer from the fourth item in the mishnah's list: *velo et hamenora ela beshiva neroteiha bein ha'arbayim* — the *menora* is inaugurated only through the lighting of its seven lamps in the evening. This is simply stating what the Torah already prescribes: the lamps are lit at night, not during the day. There is no daytime lighting. The *kiddush* comes precisely when the proper time arrives, and the *chinuch* follows as a matter of course. Since both arrive simultaneously, you must wait for the designated moment. For the *shulchan*, that moment is *Shabbat*, as the Torah specifies for the *lechem hapanim*.

## Ketoret of Nesi'im

### Individual Ketoret

The Gemara now introduces a *baraita*: *zehu ketoret she'alta leyachid hamizbe'ach hachitzon hora'at sha'ah hayta* — this refers to an *ketoret* offering brought by an individual on the outer altar, and it was a one-time dispensation. The reference is to the *nesi'im*, the tribal princes, each of whom brought among his offerings a *kaf* filled with *ketoret*. This offering was placed on the outer *mizbe'ach* — which is the striking anomaly, since the *ketoret* is ordinarily brought on the inner *mizbe'ach hazahav* — and it was a *hora'at sha'ah*, an exceptional temporary instruction, not a permanent norm.

### Voluntary Ketoret

The Gemara proceeds to parse the implications from multiple angles. The *baraita* noted that an individual brought *ketoret* on the outer altar. Does this mean that, as a rule, an individual may not bring *ketoret* on the outer altar — but could bring it on the inner? And conversely, does it mean only an individual is excluded from the outer altar, while the community (*tzibbur*) could bring *ketoret* there?

Taking this further: might an individual bring *ketoret* as a voluntary offering (*nedavah*)? After all, the Torah says *kol aniyah motze picha tishmor ve'asita* — one may fulfill a vow of *nedavah* — and we know that voluntary offerings are routinely brought. Could *ketoret* function similarly?

The answer is drawn from the *pasuk* in *parashat Tetzaveh* which commands: *lo ta'alu alav ketoret zarah* — you shall not offer *ketoret zarah*, alien incense, upon the inner altar. This prohibition encompasses any *ketoret* that is not the prescribed communal *ketoret*. A *yachid* bringing a voluntary *ketoret* would constitute *ketoret zarah* — and that is categorically forbidden on the inner altar.

### Ketoret Zarah

The Gemara then asks whether, while an individual is barred from the inner altar, maybe the *tzibbur* could bring a voluntary *ketoret* there. After all, the community does bring *ketoret* regularly. But *lo ta'alu* is a plural formulation — Rashi notes this explicitly — and it excludes the community as well from bringing anything other than the prescribed communal obligation on the inner altar.

Having disposed of the inner altar, the question shifts: perhaps *ketoret* — whether brought by an individual or by the community — could be offered on the outer *mizbe'ach hachitzon*? After all, the inner altar's prohibition has been established, but nothing yet seems to address the outer one.

### Klal Ufrat Analysis

The answer to this final question comes from the end of *parashat Ki Tisa*, where God speaks to Moshe regarding Betzalel's charge. The passage lists the vessels: the *Ohel Moed*, the *shulchan*, the *mizbe'ach haketoret*, the *shemen hamishchah*, and the *ketoret hasamim* — and it is bookended by the general phrase *ve'asu et kol asher tzivitcha ya'asu*, "they shall do all that I have commanded you," appearing at both the opening and close.

This structure is a *klal uferat uklal* — a general statement, specific details, and another general statement — from which the rule is *ein lecha ela mah she'amur be'inyan*: you cannot derive any permission beyond what the specific content of the passage itself specifies. And what does it specify? The *mizbe'ach haketoret* — the inner golden altar — as the designated site. The outer altar is implicitly excluded. *Mah she'amur bebinyan* refers exclusively to the inner *mizbe'ach*: that is where the *ketoret* belongs, brought as an obligation by the community.

The logic, when a question is raised about it, runs as follows. The passage describing the construction of the *mishkan* and its vessels was not crafted to permit an individual to bring a personal *nedavah*. Its frame of reference is the *chovah* — the communal obligation — and the inner altar. Once the *klal uferat uklal* deduction is applied, neither community nor individual may bring *ketoret* on the outer altar, and neither may do so as a voluntary offering on the inner one.

### Final Ketoret Rule

The conclusion is stated with the logic of *lo mibaya*. It goes without saying that the *tzibbur* cannot bring *ketoret* on the outer *mizbe'ach*, since there is no precedent (*lo ashkachan*) for that ever occurring. It likewise goes without saying that an individual cannot bring it on the inner *mizbe'ach*, since again there is no precedent. But one might have thought that even an individual could bring a voluntary *ketoret* on the outer *mizbe'ach* — after all, we do find a precedent in the *nesi'im*. The answer is: that was a *hora'at sha'ah*, a unique one-time dispensation, which by definition implies that nothing like it may be replicated going forward.

The upshot: the *ketoret* may be brought only by the *tzibbur*, only as an obligation, and only on the inner *mizbe'ach hazahav*. Every other configuration is excluded.

## Chavitin Structure

### Chavitin Intro

The Gemara now introduces a new mishnah concerning the *chavitin* of the *Kohen Gadol*. The relevant *pesukim* are in *parashat Tzav* (Vayikra 6). The passage begins: *zeh korban Aharon uvanav asher yakrivu l'Hashem b'yom himashach oto* — this is the offering of Aharon and his sons which they shall bring to God on the day he is anointed. It is an *asirit ha'ephah* of *solet*, brought as a *tamid*, with half in the morning and half in the evening: *machatzitah baboker umachatzitah ba'erev*.

The key point is that while the offering is ultimately divided and offered in two halves, it does not begin as two halves. The *chavitin* — so named because, as the continuation of the passage specifies, it is made *al machavat*, on a frying pan — is prepared as a unified whole. The mishnah states: *chavitei Kohen Gadol lo hayu ba'in chatzayan* — they do not come in halves. Rather, *mevi isaron shalem ve-chotzehu* — one brings a full *isaron* and then divides it — *makriv mechtzah baboker umechtzah bein ha'arbayim*.

### Whole Unit

The conceptual significance of this rule is established by the *baraita*: had the Torah simply written *minchah machatzit* (a half-portion offering), one might have concluded that a *Kohen* brings half an *isaron* in the morning from his own funds and another half in the evening, each half independent of the other. But the Torah writes *machatzitah* — half *of it*, with the possessive suffix indicating half of a previously constituted whole. The Gemara derives: *me-chatzi meshalem hu makriv* — he offers half that comes from a whole. The procedure is therefore: bring a complete *isaron*, halve it, offer one half in the morning, the other half in the evening.

### Priest Death

What happens if the half designated for the evening becomes *tamei* or is lost? Here again the principle applies: the *Kohen* may not simply bring a new half-*isaron* from his own funds. He must bring another complete *isaron*, divide it, offer one half, and let the other half be disposed of. Consequently, on such a day: *nimtzu shenei chatza'in krevim ve-shenei chatza'in ovdin* — two halves are offered and two halves are lost, drawn from two separate *isronot*.

The same analysis applies when the *Kohen Gadol* dies between the morning and evening offerings. A new *Kohen Gadol* is appointed to serve at *minchah*, and the question is posed: may the new *Kohen Gadol* bring just his half from his own funds, or use the remaining half from the deceased's *isaron*? The *baraita* answers from the same word: *machatzitah ba'erev* — *machetza mishalem hu mevi umakriv*, he must bring half that derives from a full unit. He therefore brings a new complete *isaron*, divides it, offers one half in the evening, and the remaining half is lost. Combined with the morning half that the first *Kohen Gadol* already offered (the second half of which can no longer be brought since the *Kohen* died), the day ends with two halves offered and two halves disposed of — each pair coming from a different *isaron*.

### Two Halves Lost

A *baraita* formalizes this: *mechetza rishon umechetza sheni te'ubar tzuratan veyeitz'u l'beit ha-sreifa* — both the first half and the second half that are not offered must have their form changed (*te'ubar tzuratan*), meaning they must be left overnight, and only then taken to the *beit ha-sreifa* for burning.

## Spoilage & Burning

### Overnight Spoilage

The term *te'ubar tzuratan* — literally "their form is altered" — is borrowed language, more naturally applied to meat (which would visibly change overnight without refrigeration), but applied here by analogy to a flour offering. Practically, it means leaving the remaining halves overnight, after which they are taken to the *beit ha-sreifa* and burned.

### Second Half Question

This requirement prompts a pointed question from Rav Nachman. He understands why the first half — the half belonging to the deceased *Kohen Gadol* that cannot be offered — must undergo *ibur tzurah* before burning. It was originally intended for the *mizbe'ach*, so it requires a process of invalidation before it can be burned. But why does the second half of the new *Kohen Gadol*'s *isaron* need to stay overnight? That half was never going to be offered from the outset. The new *Kohen* brings a full *isaron*, divides it, offers one half in the evening, and the other half was — from the moment of division — destined not to go on the *mizbe'ach*. Why does something that was never valid require overnight invalidation?

### Pigul Comparison

The response invokes *pigul*: even an offering rendered invalid through improper intent (*pigul*) — which by definition can no longer be placed on the *mizbe'ach* — still requires *ibur tzurah* before burning. The source is the school of Rabbah bar Avuha: *afilu pigul ta'un ibur tzurah*. By the same principle, anything designated for the *mizbe'ach* that cannot ultimately be offered must go through this overnight process before burning. That is simply the rule — anything headed for the *beit ha-sreifa* must first be left overnight.

### Rashi's View

Rashi offers an additional rationale, one that applies even according to the *rabbanan* who hold that *pigul* does not require overnight spoilage before burning. Rashi's reasoning focuses on the nature of the division itself. When the new *Kohen Gadol* brings his full *isaron* and divides it in two, it is not as if one specific half was always designated for the *mizbe'ach* and the other was always destined to be discarded. Either half could have been the one offered; either half could have been the one set aside. Since both halves were potentially candidates for the *mizbe'ach* — *i ba'ei hai makriv ve'i ba'ei hai makriv*, he could have offered this one or that one — both halves are, in a sense, attached to the *mizbe'ach*. The altar lays claim to both, as it were. Therefore, even the half that is ultimately not offered requires overnight treatment before it may be burned.

## Three Methods

### Order Challenge

The *pasuk* in Vayikra 6 describes the preparation of the *chavitin* with a sequence that is, on its face, puzzling: *al machavat bashemen te'aseh murbechet tevi'ena tufinei minchat pitim takriv* — on a pan, with oil it shall be made, scalded (*murbechet*), you shall bring it baked (*tufinei*), a broken offering you shall bring. Three distinct processes appear: frying (on the *machavat*), scalding (*murbechet*, where water is poured on it), and baking (*tufinei*). The Torah lists them in that order: fried, scalded, baked. Yet the question of which process actually precedes which — specifically whether baking comes before frying or after — is where the debate will focus.

### Visual Demo

To appreciate the practical stakes, it helps to see the three states. The first is the fried state: the dough placed on the *machavat*, the frying pan, cooked in oil. The second is the scalded state (*murbechet*): water is poured over or applied to the dough. The third is the baked state (*tufinei*): placed in an oven and baked.

The question of sequence is not merely academic. If one fries the dough and then pours water over it, the result may turn to mush — a concern that is at least intuitive even if not technically disqualifying, since the *chavitin* is not eaten (*vekhol minchat Kohen Gadol tihiyeh chalil lo tei'achel*). Yet the offering must still be aesthetically presentable. One might argue that the water step logically comes first — before anything is done to the dough — or perhaps last. What the *baraita* will address, however, is not the position of the scalding step per se, but the relative order of baking and frying.

Note also that a misprint in Rashi seems to suggest he is addressing the scalding step in the debate, but this does not appear to be correct. The debate is specifically about whether baking precedes frying or frying precedes baking; the scalding step's placement, while curious given the Torah's stated order, is not the subject of the *machloketh*.

### Frying vs Baking

The *amora'im* disagree directly on this question. Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba says in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *ofeyhan ve-achar kach metagnah* — one bakes first and then fries. Rabbi Assi says in the name of Rabbi Chanina: *metagnah ve-achar kach ofeyah* — one fries first and then bakes.

Each position is supported by a reading of the word *tufinei*. Rabbi Chiyya, following Rabbi Yochanan, reads *tufinei na'eh* — a beautiful, attractive baking. Since frying tends to char or otherwise mark the surface, baking should come first while the dough is still pristine, so that the baked result is *na'eh*, aesthetically fine. Rabbi Assi reads *tufinei na* — baked in a state of *na*, meaning partially done, referring to something not yet fully cooked through its own process. This implies that the baking comes after the frying has already partially prepared it.

This dispute maps directly onto a *tannaitic* debate. The *Tanna Kamma* reads *tufinei na* (frying first, supporting Rabbi Assi). Rabbi, however, reads *tufinei na'eh* (baking first, supporting Rabbi Yochanan). A third opinion, that of Rabbi Yose, reads *tufinei rabbah* — a great or abundant baking — and Rashi offers three explanations for this position. One understanding is that both *na* and *na'eh* apply, which would yield the sequence: bake, fry, bake — the dough is baked once, then fried, then baked again. A second explanation of *tufinei rabbah* is that it means making the dough large: by spreading it on the *machavat* one can move the pan to extend the dough, which points to frying first so as to expand it, then baking. A third explanation reads not *rabbah* but *rakah* — soft — meaning a great deal of oil is used in frying so that the dough becomes tender, and then it is baked. Thus under the first reading of Rabbi Yose the order is bake-fry-bake; under the second and third readings it is fry-then-bake.

## Location & Sabbath

### Inside Requirement

A mishnah cited from elsewhere states: *chavitei Kohen Gadol lishatan va'afiyatan bifnim dochot et hashabbat* — the kneading and baking of the *Kohen Gadol*'s *chavitin* must be performed inside the Temple precincts, and this work overrides the Sabbath. These preparations may not be done at home and brought in; they must be carried out within the *azarah* itself.

### Freshness Rule

From where do we derive this? Rav Huna says it follows from *tufinei na'eh* — it must be baked beautifully — and *afilu me'etmol ipshah*, even the day before it would become stale. Since the preparation must be done fresh each day to ensure the offering looks its best, and since the *chavitin* is brought daily, this necessarily requires work on the Sabbath as well. Abaye derives it differently, from the word *tamid* in the *pasuk* — just as the *temidin* are offered on every day including *Shabbat*, so too must this offering be prepared on every day. Rava derives it from the technical rule that any item that must be prepared in a *keli* (vessel) is invalidated by *lina* (staying overnight), so preparation must occur that very day — and this applies on *Shabbat* as well.

### Preservation Method

Rav Yosef raises a challenge: why is Sabbath violation necessary? Perhaps the *chavitin* could be prepared the day before and then wrapped in vegetable leaves to preserve its freshness, avoiding the need to cook on the Sabbath. The implicit question is pointed: this offering is neither eaten nor tasted — it is ultimately burned in its entirety — so why must one violate the Sabbath simply so it should appear fresh? The Gemara responds: *de-vairabbi Yishmael tanna te'aseh afilu be-shabbat, te'aseh afilu be-tumah* — the school of Rabbi Yishmael derives from the word *te'aseh* in the *pasuk* that the preparation is required even on *Shabbat* and even in a state of communal *tumah*. Rava's derivation via *al machavat* and *lina* similarly compels same-day preparation with no room for a workaround.

## Oil Quantity

### Comparison Setup

Having established that the *chavitin* must be prepared on a *machavat* with oil (*bashemen te'aseh*), the question turns to quantity: how much oil is required? The *baraita* acknowledges it does not know and proceeds to examine two possible analogs. The first is the *tamid* offering — which the *chavitin* resembles in several respects, given that both are brought constantly, both override *Shabbat*, and both override *tumah*. The second analog is a voluntary *minchah* offering (*minchat nedavah*), brought by an individual. These two comparisons yield starkly different answers: the *tamid* requires three *lugin* of oil per *isaron*, while the voluntary *minchah* requires only one *log*.

### Tamid Approach

Rabbi Shimon beno shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka resolves the question by appeal to the *pasuk* itself. The verse describing the *chavitin* uses the word *tamid* — *solet minchah tamid*. Since it says *tamid*, just as the *minchat temidin* uses three *lugin* of oil per *isaron*, so too should the *chavitin*: *ma minchat temidin sheloshah lugin isaron, af zu sheloshah lugin isaron*.

### Proportionality

Rabbi Shimon independently approaches the question through the word *bashemen* — "with oil" — which implies adding a substantial quantity of oil. But how much? He considers two possible comparisons. On one hand, the *minchat kevasim* (the flour offering accompanying a lamb) uses three *lugin* per *isaron*. On the other hand, the *minchat parim ve-eilim* (accompanying rams and bulls) uses two *lugin* per *isaron*. Which is the correct analog?

The resolution comes from a principle of proportionality: *denin minchah ha-ba'ah be-isaron mi-minchah ha-ba'ah be-isaron* — we compare a *minchah* that comes in a single *isaron* to another *minchah* that comes in a single *isaron*. We do not compare it to offerings that come in two or three *isronot*, since those proportions are structurally different. The lamb offering comes with one *isaron*, just like the *chavitin*, and its oil ratio is three *lugin* per *isaron*. That ratio fits proportionately for the *chavitin*; the ram-and-bull ratio does not match when scaled to a single *isaron*. Therefore: three *lugin*.

### Contradiction

A difficulty now emerges from within the *baraita* itself. It opened by saying *bashemen lehosif lo shemen* — *bashemen* teaches that one must add a generous quantity of oil, implying a large amount. Yet it then proposed comparing the *chavitin* to the *minchat nedavah* — which requires only one *log*. These two positions appear contradictory: if *bashemen* means to add a lot, one *log* hardly qualifies; and if the comparison to the *nedavah* is valid, there is nothing especially large about one *log*.

### Bahsemen Logic

The resolution is attributed to the school of Rabbah bar Avuha, and its logic is clarified by Abaye: the one who said *bashemen lehosif shemen* is Rabbi Shimon. The reasoning is as follows. Since the *pasuk* already says *al machavat* — on the frying pan — it is obvious that oil must be used, because you do not fry on a *machavat* without oil. If *bashemen* merely meant "use oil," it would be superfluous given *al machavat*. Therefore *bashemen* must mean something additional: add more oil than the *machavat* itself implies. It signals a generous, additional quantity of oil.

But then, why propose comparing it to the *nedavah* at all? Because Rabbi Shimon entertained the possibility that *bashemen* might not be teaching a quantity but merely emphasizing the type — confirming that oil must be used, lest one think the *chavitin* is like a *charvot* (a dry flour offering, not fried), since some *menachot* are indeed brought without oil. *Bashemen* would then rule out the dry alternative, without specifying a large quantity. It is only after this possibility is considered and the comparison to the *nedavah* (one *log*) explored that Rabbi Shimon concludes the logical comparisons are inconclusive, making the explicit *bashemen* indispensable as a directive to use more oil — and then the deductive work of proportionality brings him to three *lugin*.

### Rava Reconciles

Abaye and Rava both hold that the entire passage reflects Rabbi Shimon's reasoning, but they organize the internal steps somewhat differently. Rava's version: *bashemen* signals additional oil; if there were no explicit *bashemen*, Rabbi Shimon would have tried to derive the amount by analogy — comparing it to the *tamid* (three *lugin*) or to the *nedavah* (one *log*) — but these logical comparisons prove inconclusive because valid arguments point in both directions. Therefore the explicit word *bashemen* is indispensable, and Rabbi Shimon then establishes three *lugin* through the proportionality argument comparing *isaron*-to-*isaron* offerings. In both Abaye's and Rava's accounts, all the positions in the passage belong to Rabbi Shimon beno shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, with the internal sequence reflecting his own working-through of the question before arriving at the final ruling of three *lugin* per *isaron*.
