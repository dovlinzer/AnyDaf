# Menachot 50b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chinuch & Kiddush

### Three Inaugurations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא אֶת הַשֻּׁלְחָן אֶלָּא בְּלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים בְּשַׁבָּת
>
> **Translation:** **And** use of a new **Table** was initiated **only with** the arrangement of **the shewbread on Shabbat.**

The mishnah catalogues several distinct inaugurations of the Tabernacle vessels. The golden inner altar — the *mizbe'ach hazahav*, synonymous with the *mizbe'ach haketoret* — is inaugurated through the *ketoret hasamim*. The outer altar, the *mizbe'ach ha'olah*, is inaugurated through the *tamid shel shachar*. And now the mishnah turns to the third item: *velo et hashulchan ela belechem hapanim beshabbat* — the *shulchan* is inaugurated specifically through the *lechem hapanim*, and specifically on *Shabbat*.

### Chinuch vs Kiddush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִיא גּוּפַהּ קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּחִינּוּךְ וְקִידּוּשׁ דְּשֻׁלְחָן בְּשַׁבָּת הוּא, כִּדְקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: וְלֹא אֶת הַמְּנוֹרָה אֶלָּא בְּשִׁבְעָה נֵרוֹתֶיהָ בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The mishna **teaches us this** *halakha* **itself, that the initiation** of the use of a new Table **and the sanctification of** the shewbread when it is placed on **the Table** occur only **on Shabbat.** This is **as** the mishna **teaches** in **the latter clause** with regard to the initiation of the use of a new Candelabrum: **And** use of a new **Candelabrum** was initiated **only with** the kindling **of its seven lamps in the afternoon.**

The Gemara asks whether the mishnah's insistence on *Shabbat* carries an implicit permissive inference: since it says you cannot *be-mechanech* the *shulchan* except through the *lechem hapanim* on *Shabbat*, perhaps the *chinuch* (inauguration) requires *Shabbat*, but mere *kiddush* (sanctification) could be accomplished on a weekday?

The Gemara rejects this reading entirely. The mishnah is not trying to separate *chinuch* from *kiddush* — it is coming to tell us precisely the opposite: *chinuch ve'kiddush shulchan beshabbat hu* — the inauguration and the sanctification occur together, at one and the same moment. Do not infer from the word *chinuch* that the two can be disentangled.

### Menora Lighting

The point becomes clearer from the fourth item in the mishnah's list: *velo et hamenora ela beshiva neroteiha bein ha'arbayim* — the *menora* is inaugurated only through the lighting of its seven lamps in the evening. This is simply stating what the Torah already prescribes: the lamps are lit at night, not during the day. There is no daytime lighting. The *kiddush* comes precisely when the proper time arrives, and the *chinuch* follows as a matter of course. Since both arrive simultaneously, you must wait for the designated moment. For the *shulchan*, that moment is *Shabbat*, as the Torah specifies for the *lechem hapanim*.

## Ketoret of Nesi'im

### Individual Ketoret

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: (זֶהוּ) [זוֹהִי] קְטֹרֶת שֶׁעָלְתָה לְיָחִיד עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן, וְהוֹרָאַת שָׁעָה הָיְתָה. הֵיכָא? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בַּנְּשִׂיאִים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **This is incense that was offered for an individual** rather than the community **on the external altar,** and not on the golden altar as usual; **and this was a provisional edict,** permitted temporarily for that time only. The Gemara clarifies: To **what** case is the *baraita* referring? **Rav Pappa said:** It is referring to the incense brought **by the** tribal **princes** at the inauguration of the Tabernacle (see Numbers, chapter 7).

The Gemara now introduces a *baraita*: *zehu ketoret she'alta leyachid hamizbe'ach hachitzon hora'at sha'ah hayta* — this refers to an *ketoret* offering brought by an individual on the outer altar, and it was a one-time dispensation. The reference is to the *nesi'im*, the tribal princes, each of whom brought among his offerings a *kaf* filled with *ketoret*. This offering was placed on the outer *mizbe'ach* — which is the striking anomaly, since the *ketoret* is ordinarily brought on the inner *mizbe'ach hazahav* — and it was a *hora'at sha'ah*, an exceptional temporary instruction, not a permanent norm.

### Voluntary Ketoret

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא יָחִיד עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן הוּא דְּלָא, הָא עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי מַקְרֵיב? וְתוּ: עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן יָחִיד הוּא דְּלָא, הָא צִיבּוּר מַקְרְבִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** with regard to the incense of **an individual, is it** only **on the external altar that** it is **not** generally permitted to be burned, **but** an individual **may sacrifice** incense **on the inner altar,** as indicated by the *baraita*? **And furthermore, on the external altar, is it** only an **individual who may not** sacrifice incense, **but the community may sacrifice** incense on the external altar?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: יָכוֹל יְהֵא יָחִיד מִתְנַדֵּב וּמֵבִיא כְּיוֹצֵא בָּהּ נְדָבָה, וְקוֹרֵא אֲנִי בָּהּ ״מוֹצָא שְׂפָתֶיךָ תִּשְׁמֹר וְעָשִׂיתָ״? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא תַעֲלוּ עָלָיו קְטֹרֶת זָרָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* contrary to those two inferences: One **might** have thought that **an individual may voluntarily donate and bring** incense **similar to** the incense brought by the tribal princes to the Temple as **a gift** offering, **and I** will **read with regard to** this incense, as in the case of other gift offerings: **"That which has gone out of your lips you shall observe and do"** (Deuteronomy 23:24). Therefore, **the verse states** concerning the inner altar: **"You shall bring no strange incense thereon"** (Exodus 30:9). This indicates that an individual may not sacrifice incense even on the inner altar.

The Gemara proceeds to parse the implications from multiple angles. The *baraita* noted that an individual brought *ketoret* on the outer altar. Does this mean that, as a rule, an individual may not bring *ketoret* on the outer altar — but could bring it on the inner? And conversely, does it mean only an individual is excluded from the outer altar, while the community (*tzibbur*) could bring *ketoret* there?

Taking this further: might an individual bring *ketoret* as a voluntary offering (*nedavah*)? After all, the Torah says *kol aniyah motze picha tishmor ve'asita* — one may fulfill a vow of *nedavah* — and we know that voluntary offerings are routinely brought. Could *ketoret* function similarly?

The answer is drawn from the *pasuk* in *parashat Tetzaveh* which commands: *lo ta'alu alav ketoret zarah* — you shall not offer *ketoret zarah*, alien incense, upon the inner altar. This prohibition encompasses any *ketoret* that is not the prescribed communal *ketoret*. A *yachid* bringing a voluntary *ketoret* would constitute *ketoret zarah* — and that is categorically forbidden on the inner altar.

### Ketoret Zarah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לֹא יְהֵא יָחִיד מֵבִיא, שֶׁאֵין מֵבִיא חוֹבָתוֹ כְּיוֹצֵא בָּהּ,
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that only **an individual may not bring** a gift of incense on the inner altar, **as** an individual **does not bring his obligatory** offering **similar to this** gift of incense, i.e., since an individual is never obligated to sacrifice incense, he may not voluntarily sacrifice incense either;

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל צִבּוּר יְהֵא מֵבִיא, שֶׁמֵּבִיא חוֹבָה כְּיוֹצֵא בָּהּ, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לֹא תַעֲלוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **but a community may bring** incense as a gift offering, **as** the community **does bring its obligatory** offering **similar to this,** i.e., since the community is obligated to sacrifice incense it can also voluntarily sacrifice incense. Therefore, **the verse states: You shall not bring [*lo ta'alu*]** strange incense thereon" (Exodus 30:9). The fact that the verse formulates the prohibition with the plural word *ta'alu* indicates that even the community may not sacrifice incense as a voluntary gift offering.

The Gemara then asks whether, while an individual is barred from the inner altar, maybe the *tzibbur* could bring a voluntary *ketoret* there. After all, the community does bring *ketoret* regularly. But *lo ta'alu* is a plural formulation — Rashi notes this explicitly — and it excludes the community as well from bringing anything other than the prescribed communal obligation on the inner altar.

### Klal Ufrat Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לֹא יַעֲלוּ עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי, אֲבָל יַעֲלוּ עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֶת שֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה וְאֶת קְטֹרֶת הַסַּמִּים לַקֹּדֶשׁ כְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר צִוִּיתִךָ יַעֲשׂוּ״ – אֵין לְךָ אֶלָּא מַה שֶּׁאָמוּר בָּעִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought that the community **may not bring** a gift offering of incense **on the inner altar, but** it **may bring** incense **on the external altar.** Therefore, **the verse states: "And the anointing oil, and the incense of sweet spices for the sacred place; according to all that I have commanded you shall they do"** (Exodus 31:11). This teaches that one **has** the right to do **only that which is stated with regard to the matter,** without deviation. Consequently, incense may be sacrificed only by the community, only when there is an obligation to sacrifice it, and it must be burned only on the inner altar. This contradicts the implication of the previously cited *baraita* that it is permitted to burn the incense of an individual on the golden altar or the incense of a community on the outer altar.

Having disposed of the inner altar, the question shifts: perhaps *ketoret* — whether brought by an individual or by the community — could be offered on the outer *mizbe'ach hachitzon*? After all, the inner altar's prohibition has been established, but nothing yet seems to address the outer one.

The answer to this final question comes from the end of *parashat Ki Tisa*, where God speaks to Moshe regarding Betzalel's charge. The passage lists the vessels: the *Ohel Moed*, the *shulchan*, the *mizbe'ach haketoret*, the *shemen hamishchah*, and the *ketoret hasamim* — and it is bookended by the general phrase *ve'asu et kol asher tzivitcha ya'asu*, "they shall do all that I have commanded you," appearing at both the opening and close.

This structure is a *klal uferat uklal* — a general statement, specific details, and another general statement — from which the rule is *ein lecha ela mah she'amur be'inyan*: you cannot derive any permission beyond what the specific content of the passage itself specifies. And what does it specify? The *mizbe'ach haketoret* — the inner golden altar — as the designated site. The outer altar is implicitly excluded. *Mah she'amur bebinyan* refers exclusively to the inner *mizbe'ach*: that is where the *ketoret* belongs, brought as an obligation by the community.

### Final Ketoret Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר, לָא מִיבַּעְיָא צִיבּוּר עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן, דְּלָא אַשְׁכְּחַן, וְלָא מִיבַּעְיָא יָחִיד עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי, דְּלָא אַשְׁכְּחַן, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ יָחִיד עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן, דְּאַשְׁכְּחַן בִּנְשִׂיאִים – הוֹרָאַת שָׁעָה הָיְתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** This is not problematic. The *baraita* **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary,** as follows. **It is not necessary** to state that **the community** may not sacrifice incense **on the external altar, as we have not found** a precedent for it. **And** similarly, **it is not necessary** to state that **an individual** may not sacrifice incense **on the inner altar, as we have not found** a precedent for it. **But** it is necessary to state that it is **even** prohibited for **an individual** to sacrifice incense **on the external altar,** despite the fact **that we have** ostensibly **found** a precedent for it **in** the case of **the** tribal **princes;** as that **was a provisional edict** and therefore cannot serve as a precedent.

The logic, when a question is raised about it, runs as follows. The passage describing the construction of the *mishkan* and its vessels was not crafted to permit an individual to bring a personal *nedavah*. Its frame of reference is the *chovah* — the communal obligation — and the inner altar. Once the *klal uferat uklal* deduction is applied, neither community nor individual may bring *ketoret* on the outer altar, and neither may do so as a voluntary offering on the inner one.

The conclusion is stated with the logic of *lo mibaya*. It goes without saying that the *tzibbur* cannot bring *ketoret* on the outer *mizbe'ach*, since there is no precedent (*lo ashkachan*) for that ever occurring. It likewise goes without saying that an individual cannot bring it on the inner *mizbe'ach*, since again there is no precedent. But one might have thought that even an individual could bring a voluntary *ketoret* on the outer *mizbe'ach* — after all, we do find a precedent in the *nesi'im*. The answer is: that was a *hora'at sha'ah*, a unique one-time dispensation, which by definition implies that nothing like it may be replicated going forward.

The upshot: the *ketoret* may be brought only by the *tzibbur*, only as an obligation, and only on the inner *mizbe'ach hazahav*. Every other configuration is excluded.

## Chavitin Structure

### Chavitin Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חֲבִיתֵּי כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל לֹא הָיוּ בָּאִין חֲצָאִין, אֶלָּא מֵבִיא עִשָּׂרוֹן שָׁלֵם וְחוֹצֵהוּ, מַקְרִיב מֶחֱצָה בַּבֹּקֶר וּמֶחֱצָה בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The twelve loaves of *matza* prepared from a tenth of an ephah of flour of the **griddle-cake** offering **of the High Priest did not come** from the house of the High Priest **in halves. Rather,** the High Priest **brings** from his house **a complete tenth** of an ephah of flour (see Leviticus 6:13) **and divides it** in half, and he **sacrifices half in the morning and half in the afternoon.**

The Gemara now introduces a new mishnah concerning the *chavitin* of the *Kohen Gadol*. The relevant *pesukim* are in *parashat Tzav* (Vayikra 6). The passage begins: *zeh korban Aharon uvanav asher yakrivu l'Hashem b'yom himashach oto* — this is the offering of Aharon and his sons which they shall bring to God on the day he is anointed. It is an *asirit ha'ephah* of *solet*, brought as a *tamid*, with half in the morning and half in the evening: *machatzitah baboker umachatzitah ba'erev*.

The key point is that while the offering is ultimately divided and offered in two halves, it does not begin as two halves. The *chavitin* — so named because, as the continuation of the passage specifies, it is made *al machavat*, on a frying pan — is prepared as a unified whole. The mishnah states: *chavitei Kohen Gadol lo hayu ba'in chatzayan* — they do not come in halves. Rather, *mevi isaron shalem ve-chotzehu* — one brings a full *isaron* and then divides it — *makriv mechtzah baboker umechtzah bein ha'arbayim*.

### Whole Unit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אִילּוּ נֶאֱמַר ״מִנְחָה מַחֲצִית״, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר מֵבִיא חֲצִי עִשָּׂרוֹן מִבֵּיתוֹ שַׁחֲרִית וּמַקְרִיב, חֲצִי עִשָּׂרוֹן מִבֵּיתוֹ עַרְבִית וּמַקְרִיב. תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״מַחֲצִיתָהּ בַּבֹּקֶר וּמַחֲצִיתָהּ בָּעָרֶב״ – מֶחֱצָה מִשָּׁלֵם הוּא מַקְרִיב, הָא כֵּיצַד? מֵבִיא עִשָּׂרוֹן שָׁלֵם וְחוֹצֵהוּ, וּמַקְרִיב מֶחֱצָה בַּבֹּקֶר וּמֶחֱצָה בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites that which **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*, commenting on the verse: "This is the offering of Aaron and of his sons, which they shall offer to the Lord on the day when he is anointed: The tenth part of an ephah of fine flour for a meal offering perpetually, half of it in the morning, and half of it in the evening" (Leviticus 6:13). **If** the verse had **stated: A meal offering** perpetually, **half** in the morning and half in the evening; **I would have said** that the High Priest **brings half of a tenth** of an ephah **in the morning and sacrifices** it, and then he brings **half of a tenth** of an ephah **in the afternoon and sacrifices** it. Since **the verse states: "Half of it in the morning, and half of it in the evening"** (Leviticus 6:13), it teaches that **he sacrifices half of a complete** tenth of an ephah. **How so?** The High Priest **brings** from his house **a complete tenth** of an ephah of fine flour, **and divides it** in half, **and sacrifices half in the morning and half in the afternoon.**

The conceptual significance of this rule is established by the *baraita*: had the Torah simply written *minchah machatzit* (a half-portion offering), one might have concluded that a *Kohen* brings half an *isaron* in the morning from his own funds and another half in the evening, each half independent of the other. But the Torah writes *machatzitah* — half *of it*, with the possessive suffix indicating half of a previously constituted whole. The Gemara derives: *me-chatzi meshalem hu makriv* — he offers half that comes from a whole. The procedure is therefore: bring a complete *isaron*, halve it, offer one half in the morning, the other half in the evening.

### Priest Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִטְמָא מֶחֱצָה שֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם, אוֹ שֶׁאָבַד, יָכוֹל יָבִיא חֲצִי עִשָּׂרוֹן מִבֵּיתוֹ עַרְבִית וְיַקְרִיב? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״מַחֲצִיתָהּ בַּבֹּקֶר וּמַחֲצִיתָהּ בָּעָרֶב״ – מֶחֱצָה מִשָּׁלֵם הוּא מֵבִיא. הָא כֵּיצַד? מֵבִיא עִשָּׂרוֹן שָׁלֵם [מִבֵּיתוֹ], וְחוֹצֵהוּ, וּמַקְרִיב מֶחֱצָה, וּמֶחֱצָה אָבֵד. נִמְצְאוּ שְׁנֵי חֲצָאִין קְרֵיבִין, וּשְׁנֵי חֲצָאִין אוֹבְדִין.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where the **half of** a tenth of an ephah that was supposed to be offered in **the afternoon became impure or was lost** after the High Priest sacrificed the first half in the morning, one **might** have thought that **he should bring half of a tenth** of an ephah **from his house and sacrifice** it. Therefore, **the verse states: "Half of it in the morning, and half of it in the evening,"** which teaches that **he brings half of a complete** tenth of an ephah. **How so?** The High Priest **brings a complete tenth** of an ephah of fine flour **from his house and divides it** in half, **and he sacrifices half, and** the other **half** is not sacrificed and **is lost. Consequently, two halves** of a tenth of an ephah **are sacrificed,** the half that was sacrificed in the morning from the original tenth of an ephah, and half of the replacement tenth of an ephah, **and** the other **two halves are lost.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל שֶׁהִקְרִיב מֶחֱצָה שַׁחֲרִית וָמֵת, וּמִינּוּ אַחֵר תַּחְתָּיו, יָכוֹל יָבִיא חֲצִי עִשָּׂרוֹן מִבֵּיתוֹ, אוֹ חֲצִי עֶשְׂרוֹנוֹ שֶׁל רִאשׁוֹן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וּמַחֲצִיתָהּ בָּעָרֶב״ – מֶחֱצָה מִשָּׁלֵם הוּא מֵבִיא וּמַקְרִיב. הָא כֵּיצַד? מֵבִיא עִשָּׂרוֹן שָׁלֵם, וְחוֹצֵהוּ, וּמַקְרִיב, וּמֶחֱצָה אָבֵד. נִמְצְאוּ שְׁנֵי חֲצָאִין אוֹבְדִין, וּשְׁנֵי חֲצָאִין קְרֵיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of a High **Priest who sacrificed half in the morning and died, and they appointed another** High **Priest in his stead,** one **might** have thought that the second High Priest **should bring half of a tenth** of an ephah **from his house** and sacrifice it, **or** that he should sacrifice the remaining **half of a tenth** of an ephah **of the first** High Priest. Therefore, **the verse states: "And half of it in the evening,"** which teaches that he **brings and sacrifices half of a complete** tenth of an ephah. **How so?** The replacement High Priest **brings a complete tenth** of an ephah of fine flour **from his house and divides it** in half, **and he sacrifices** half, **and half** is not sacrificed and **is lost. Consequently, two halves** of a tenth of an ephah **are lost,** half of the tenth of an ephah brought by each priest, **and** the other **two halves are sacrificed.**

What happens if the half designated for the evening becomes *tamei* or is lost? Here again the principle applies: the *Kohen* may not simply bring a new half-*isaron* from his own funds. He must bring another complete *isaron*, divide it, offer one half, and let the other half be disposed of. Consequently, on such a day: *nimtzu shenei chatza'in krevim ve-shenei chatza'in ovdin* — two halves are offered and two halves are lost, drawn from two separate *isronot*.

The same analysis applies when the *Kohen Gadol* dies between the morning and evening offerings. A new *Kohen Gadol* is appointed to serve at *minchah*, and the question is posed: may the new *Kohen Gadol* bring just his half from his own funds, or use the remaining half from the deceased's *isaron*? The *baraita* answers from the same word: *machatzitah ba'erev* — *machetza mishalem hu mevi umakriv*, he must bring half that derives from a full unit. He therefore brings a new complete *isaron*, divides it, offers one half in the evening, and the remaining half is lost. Combined with the morning half that the first *Kohen Gadol* already offered (the second half of which can no longer be brought since the *Kohen* died), the day ends with two halves offered and two halves disposed of — each pair coming from a different *isaron*.

### Two Halves Lost

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: מֶחֱצָה רִאשׁוֹן וּמֶחֱצָה שֵׁנִי – תְּעוּבַּר צוּרָתָן, וְיֵצְאוּ לְבֵית הַשְּׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the two halves of a tenth of an ephah that are lost, **a *tanna*,** i.e., a Sage who recited *baraitot*, **taught** a *baraita* **before Rav Naḥman:** With regard to **the half** that was not sacrificed by the **first** High Priest, who died, **and the half** brought but not sacrificed by the **second** High Priest who replaced him, **their form should decay,** i.e., they should be left overnight so they become disqualified, **and** then they should be **brought out to the place** designated **for burning.**

A *baraita* formalizes this: *mechetza rishon umechetza sheni te'ubar tzuratan veyeitz'u l'beit ha-sreifa* — both the first half and the second half that are not offered must have their form changed (*te'ubar tzuratan*), meaning they must be left overnight, and only then taken to the *beit ha-sreifa* for burning.

## Spoilage & Burning

### Overnight Spoilage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן: בִּשְׁלָמָא רִאשׁוֹן אִיחֲזִי לְהַקְרָבָה, אֶלָּא שֵׁנִי לְמָה לֵיהּ עִיבּוּר צוּרָה? מֵעִיקָּרָא לְאִיבּוּד קָא אָתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said to** the *tanna*: **Granted,** the half that was not sacrificed by the **first** High Priest should be left overnight before it is burned, because it was initially **fit for sacrifice** before the first High Priest died. **But** with regard to the half that was not sacrificed by the **second** High Priest, **why** must **it** be left overnight so that its **form decays?** It was **brought to be lost from the outset,** i.e., when the full tenth of an ephah was brought it was known that only half would be sacrificed and half would be lost. Consequently, it is unnecessary to leave it overnight before burning it.

The term *te'ubar tzuratan* — literally "their form is altered" — is borrowed language, more naturally applied to meat (which would visibly change overnight without refrigeration), but applied here by analogy to a flour offering. Practically, it means leaving the remaining halves overnight, after which they are taken to the *beit ha-sreifa* and burned.

### Second Half Question

This requirement prompts a pointed question from Rav Nachman. He understands why the first half — the half belonging to the deceased *Kohen Gadol* that cannot be offered — must undergo *ibur tzurah* before burning. It was originally intended for the *mizbe'ach*, so it requires a process of invalidation before it can be burned. But why does the second half of the new *Kohen Gadol*'s *isaron* need to stay overnight? That half was never going to be offered from the outset. The new *Kohen* brings a full *isaron*, divides it, offers one half in the evening, and the other half was — from the moment of division — destined not to go on the *mizbe'ach*. Why does something that was never valid require overnight invalidation?

### Pigul Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּאֲמַר לָךְ, מַנִּי? תַּנָּא דְּבֵי רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ הוּא, דְּאָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ פִּיגּוּל טָעוּן עִיבּוּר צוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman continued: **Who is** the one **who said** this *baraita* **to you? It was the *tanna* of the school of Rabba bar Avuh, who says:** All disqualified offerings, **even *piggul*,** which is disqualified by Torah law, **require decay of form** before they are burned.

The response invokes *pigul*: even an offering rendered invalid through improper intent (*pigul*) — which by definition can no longer be placed on the *mizbe'ach* — still requires *ibur tzurah* before burning. The source is the school of Rabbah bar Avuha: *afilu pigul ta'un ibur tzurah*. By the same principle, anything designated for the *mizbe'ach* that cannot ultimately be offered must go through this overnight process before burning. That is simply the rule — anything headed for the *beit ha-sreifa* must first be left overnight.

### Rashi's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, כֵּיוָן דִּבְעִידָּנָא (דְּפָלְגִי בְּהוּ) [דְּפַלְגִינְהוּ], אִי בָּעֵי הַאי מַקְרֵיב, וְאִי בָּעֵי הַאי מַקְרֵיב – מִיחְזָא חֲזוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** The *baraita* can be understood **even** if **you say** it is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis** who disagree with Rabba bar Avuh and hold that *piggul* does not require decay of form. Nevertheless, **since at the time when** the second High Priest **divides the** two halves, **if he wants** he can **sacrifice this** half, **and if he wants** he can **sacrifice that** other half, both halves are considered **fit** to be sacrificed and may not be burned until they are left overnight.

Rashi offers an additional rationale, one that applies even according to the *rabbanan* who hold that *pigul* does not require overnight spoilage before burning. Rashi's reasoning focuses on the nature of the division itself. When the new *Kohen Gadol* brings his full *isaron* and divides it in two, it is not as if one specific half was always designated for the *mizbe'ach* and the other was always destined to be discarded. Either half could have been the one offered; either half could have been the one set aside. Since both halves were potentially candidates for the *mizbe'ach* — *i ba'ei hai makriv ve'i ba'ei hai makriv*, he could have offered this one or that one — both halves are, in a sense, attached to the *mizbe'ach*. The altar lays claim to both, as it were. Therefore, even the half that is ultimately not offered requires overnight treatment before it may be burned.

## Three Methods

### Order Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: חֲבִיתֵּי כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, כֵּיצַד עוֹשִׂין אוֹתָן? רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אוֹפָהּ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מְטַגְּנָהּ. רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מְטַגְּנָהּ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ אוֹפָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites that which **was stated** further about the griddle-cake offering: **How are the griddle-cake** offerings of **the High Priest prepared?** The verse seems to prescribe a variety of methods of preparation: "On a griddle it shall be made with oil; when it is soaked, you shall bring it in; and baked pieces [*tufinei*] of the meal offering shall you sacrifice for a pleasing aroma to the Lord" (Leviticus 6:14). **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba** says that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that it is prepared in the following manner: The individual preparing it **bakes it** in an oven **and afterward he fries it** in a pan. **Rabbi Asi** says that **Rabbi Ḥanina says** that it is prepared in the following manner: The individual preparing it **fries it** in a pan **and afterward he bakes** it in an oven.

The *pasuk* in Vayikra 6 describes the preparation of the *chavitin* with a sequence that is, on its face, puzzling: *al machavat bashemen te'aseh murbechet tevi'ena tufinei minchat pitim takriv* — on a pan, with oil it shall be made, scalded (*murbechet*), you shall bring it baked (*tufinei*), a broken offering you shall bring. Three distinct processes appear: frying (on the *machavat*), scalding (*murbechet*, where water is poured on it), and baking (*tufinei*). The Torah lists them in that order: fried, scalded, baked. Yet the question of which process actually precedes which — specifically whether baking comes before frying or after — is where the debate will focus.

### Visual Demo

To appreciate the practical stakes, it helps to see the three states. The first is the fried state: the dough placed on the *machavat*, the frying pan, cooked in oil. The second is the scalded state (*murbechet*): water is poured over or applied to the dough. The third is the baked state (*tufinei*): placed in an oven and baked.

The question of sequence is not merely academic. If one fries the dough and then pours water over it, the result may turn to mush — a concern that is at least intuitive even if not technically disqualifying, since the *chavitin* is not eaten (*vekhol minchat Kohen Gadol tihiyeh chalil lo tei'achel*). Yet the offering must still be aesthetically presentable. One might argue that the water step logically comes first — before anything is done to the dough — or perhaps last. What the *baraita* will address, however, is not the position of the scalding step per se, but the relative order of baking and frying.

Note also that a misprint in Rashi seems to suggest he is addressing the scalding step in the debate, but this does not appear to be correct. The debate is specifically about whether baking precedes frying or frying precedes baking; the scalding step's placement, while curious given the Torah's stated order, is not the subject of the *machloketh*.

### Frying vs Baking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: כְּוָותֵיהּ דִּידִי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, ״תֻּפִינֵי״ – תֹּאפֶינָּה נָאָה. רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר: כְּוָותֵיהּ דִּידִי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, ״תֻּפִינֵי״ – תֹּאפֶינָּה נָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said: It stands to reason in accordance with my** opinion because the verse states: "And baked pieces [*tufinei*] of the meal offering." The word for **baked pieces [*tufinei*]** should be understood as meaning that **they shall be baked** when they are still **beautiful [*te'afena na'a*],** i.e., before being fried. **Rabbi Asi said: It stands to reason in accordance with my** opinion because the word *tufinei* should be understood as meaning that **they shall be baked** when they are already **partially cooked [*te'afena na*],** i.e., after being fried.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי: ״תֻּפִינֵי״ – תֹּאפֶינָּה נָא. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: תֹּאפֶינָּה נָאָה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: תֹּאפֶינָּה רַבָּה. אִית לֵיהּ נָא, וְאִית לֵיהּ נָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** This dispute between the *amora'im* is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*** in a *baraita*: The word for baked pieces [*tufinei*] should be understood as meaning that **they shall be baked** when they are already **partially cooked [*te'afena na*]. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** It should be understood as meaning that **they shall be baked** when they are still **beautiful [*te'afena na'a*]. Rabbi Yosei says:** The word *tufinei* is plural, indicating that the pieces **should be baked extensively,** i.e., more than once. Consequently, Rabbi Yosei **accepts** the opinion that the pieces should be baked when they are already **partially cooked, and he** also **accepts** the opinion that they should be baked when they are **beautiful.** Therefore the offering should first be baked, then fried, then baked again.

Each position is supported by a reading of the word *tufinei*. Rabbi Chiyya, following Rabbi Yochanan, reads *tufinei na'eh* — a beautiful, attractive baking. Since frying tends to char or otherwise mark the surface, baking should come first while the dough is still pristine, so that the baked result is *na'eh*, aesthetically fine. Rabbi Assi reads *tufinei na* — baked in a state of *na*, meaning partially done, referring to something not yet fully cooked through its own process. This implies that the baking comes after the frying has already partially prepared it.

This dispute maps directly onto a *tannaitic* debate. The *Tanna Kamma* reads *tufinei na* (frying first, supporting Rabbi Assi). Rabbi, however, reads *tufinei na'eh* (baking first, supporting Rabbi Yochanan). A third opinion, that of Rabbi Yose, reads *tufinei rabbah* — a great or abundant baking — and Rashi offers three explanations for this position. One understanding is that both *na* and *na'eh* apply, which would yield the sequence: bake, fry, bake — the dough is baked once, then fried, then baked again. A second explanation of *tufinei rabbah* is that it means making the dough large: by spreading it on the *machavat* one can move the pan to extend the dough, which points to frying first so as to expand it, then baking. A third explanation reads not *rabbah* but *rakah* — soft — meaning a great deal of oil is used in frying so that the dough becomes tender, and then it is baked. Thus under the first reading of Rabbi Yose the order is bake-fry-bake; under the second and third readings it is fry-then-bake.

## Location & Sabbath

### Inside Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: חֲבִיתֵּי כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל – לִישָׁתָן וַעֲרִיכָתָן וַאֲפִיָּיתָן בִּפְנִים, וְדוֹחוֹת אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** § **We learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (96a): Concerning the twelve loaves of **the High Priest's griddle-cake** offering, of which six are sacrificed in the morning and six in the evening, **their kneading, and forming of their** loaves, **and their baking are** performed **inside** the Temple courtyard, **and** all labors involved in those actions **override Shabbat.**

A mishnah cited from elsewhere states: *chavitei Kohen Gadol lishatan va'afiyatan bifnim dochot et hashabbat* — the kneading and baking of the *Kohen Gadol*'s *chavitin* must be performed inside the Temple precincts, and this work overrides the Sabbath. These preparations may not be done at home and brought in; they must be carried out within the *azarah* itself.

### Freshness Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: ״תֻּפִינֵי״ – תֹּאפֶינָּה נָאָה, וְאִי אָפֵי לַהּ מֵאֶתְמוֹל – אִינַּשְׁפָה לַהּ. מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: אֵימָא דְּכָבֵישׁ לֵיהּ בְּיַרְקָא! דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנָא: ״תֵּעָשֶׂה״ – וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת, ״תֵּעָשֶׂה״ – וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּטוּמְאָה. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אָמַר קְרָא ״סֹלֶת מִנְחָה תָּמִיד״, הֲרֵי הִיא כְּמִנְחַת תְּמִידִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter,** i.e., that these actions override Shabbat, derived? **Rav Huna said:** The verse says ***tufinei*,** meaning that when it is already **baked it must** still **be beautiful [*te'afena na'a*]. And if** one would **bake it yesterday,** on the eve of Shabbat, **it** would become **swollen [*inshefa*]** and no longer beautiful. **Rav Yosef objects to this:** If the purpose of baking them on Shabbat is to ensure they remain fresh, **say that** the loaves should be baked before Shabbat and **covered with greens** to preserve them. A Sage from **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught:** The verse states: "On a griddle it **shall be made** with oil" (Leviticus 6:14), which teaches that it shall be made under all circumstances, **even on Shabbat.** Similarly, this phrase **"shall be made"** teaches that it should be made **even in** a state of **ritual impurity.** **Abaye said** that there is a different explanation: The **verse states: "Fine flour for a meal offering perpetually"** (Leviticus 6:13); this teaches that the *halakha* of the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest **is like** that of **the meal offering** that is a component **of the daily offerings.** The daily offerings override Shabbat, as the verse says: "This is the burnt offering of every Shabbat, beside the continual burnt offering, and the drink offering thereof" (Numbers 28:10). Therefore, preparing the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest likewise overrides Shabbat.

From where do we derive this? Rav Huna says it follows from *tufinei na'eh* — it must be baked beautifully — and *afilu me'etmol ipshah*, even the day before it would become stale. Since the preparation must be done fresh each day to ensure the offering looks its best, and since the *chavitin* is brought daily, this necessarily requires work on the Sabbath as well. Abaye derives it differently, from the word *tamid* in the *pasuk* — just as the *temidin* are offered on every day including *Shabbat*, so too must this offering be prepared on every day. Rava derives it from the technical rule that any item that must be prepared in a *keli* (vessel) is invalidated by *lina* (staying overnight), so preparation must occur that very day — and this applies on *Shabbat* as well.

### Preservation Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: ״עַל מַחֲבַת״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁטְּעוּנָה כְּלִי, וְאִי אָפֵי לַהּ מֵאֶתְמוֹל – אִיפְּסִילָא לַיהּ בְּלִינָה. תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: ״עַל מַחֲבַת״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁטְּעוּנָה כְּלִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** that the basis for it overriding Shabbat is the fact that the verse states: **"On a griddle"** (Leviticus 6:14), which **teaches that** the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest **requires a vessel.** Therefore, **if he had baked it the previous day** rather than on Shabbat, it would be **disqualified by** being **left overnight,** since the loaves had already been consecrated in a service vessel. It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava: "On a griddle" teaches that** the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest **requires a vessel.**

Rav Yosef raises a challenge: why is Sabbath violation necessary? Perhaps the *chavitin* could be prepared the day before and then wrapped in vegetable leaves to preserve its freshness, avoiding the need to cook on the Sabbath. The implicit question is pointed: this offering is neither eaten nor tasted — it is ultimately burned in its entirety — so why must one violate the Sabbath simply so it should appear fresh? The Gemara responds: *de-vairabbi Yishmael tanna te'aseh afilu be-shabbat, te'aseh afilu be-tumah* — the school of Rabbi Yishmael derives from the word *te'aseh* in the *pasuk* that the preparation is required even on *Shabbat* and even in a state of communal *tumah*. Rava's derivation via *al machavat* and *lina* similarly compels same-day preparation with no room for a workaround.

## Oil Quantity

### Comparison Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בַּשֶּׁמֶן״ – לְהוֹסִיף לָהּ שֶׁמֶן, וְאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ כַּמָּה. הֲרֵינִי דָּן: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״שֶׁמֶן״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן בְּמִנְחַת נְסָכִים ״שֶׁמֶן״ – מָה לְהַלָּן שְׁלֹשֶׁת לוּגִּין לְעִשָּׂרוֹן, אַף כָּאן שְׁלֹשֶׁת לוּגִּין לְעִשָּׂרוֹן. אוֹ כְּלָךְ לְדֶרֶךְ זוֹ: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״שֶׁמֶן״, וְנֶאֱמַר בְּמִנְחַת נְדָבָה ״שֶׁמֶן״, מָה לְהַלָּן לוֹג אֶחָד, אַף כָּאן לוֹג אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: The continuation of the verse states: "It shall be made **with the oil."** The fact that the verse makes reference to "the oil" rather than just oil indicates that one is supposed **to add** extra **oil to it,** but **I do not know how much** oil to add. Therefore **I** must **deduce** as follows: **It is stated here,** concerning the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest: **"Oil," and it is stated there, with regard to the meal offering** brought with the **libations** that accompany animal offerings: "A tenth part of an ephah of fine flour mingled with the fourth part of a *hin* of beaten **oil"** (Exodus 29:40). **Just as there,** with regard to the meal offering brought with the libations, the amount of oil required is **three *log* per tenth** of an ephah of flour; **so too here,** in the case of the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest, one brings **three *log*** of oil **per tenth** of an ephah of flour. **Or** perhaps, **go this way: It is stated here,** concerning the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest: **"Oil," and it is stated with regard to** the **voluntary meal offering:** "And when anyone brings a meal offering to the Lord, his offering shall be of fine flour; and he shall pour **oil** upon it" (Leviticus 2:1). **Just as there,** with regard to the voluntary meal offering, one brings **one *log*** of oil for each tenth of an ephah of flour (see 88a); **so too here,** one brings **one *log*** of oil for each tenth of an ephah of flour.

Having established that the *chavitin* must be prepared on a *machavat* with oil (*bashemen te'aseh*), the question turns to quantity: how much oil is required? The *baraita* acknowledges it does not know and proceeds to examine two possible analogs. The first is the *tamid* offering — which the *chavitin* resembles in several respects, given that both are brought constantly, both override *Shabbat*, and both override *tumah*. The second analog is a voluntary *minchah* offering (*minchat nedavah*), brought by an individual. These two comparisons yield starkly different answers: the *tamid* requires three *lugin* of oil per *isaron*, while the voluntary *minchah* requires only one *log*.

### Tamid Approach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִרְאֶה לְמִי דּוֹמֶה? דָּנִין תבש״ט מתבש״ט: תָּדִיר, בָּאָה חוֹבָה, דּוֹחָה שַׁבָּת, דּוֹחָה טוּמְאָה. וְאֵין דָּנִין תבש״ט מִשֶּׁאֵינוֹ תבש״ט. אוֹ כְּלָךְ לְדֶרֶךְ זוֹ: דָּנִין יגי״ל מיגי״ל – יָחִיד, בִּגְלַל עַצְמָהּ, יַיִן, לְבוֹנָה. וְאֵין דָּנִין יגי״ל, מִשֶּׁאֵינוֹ יגי״ל. רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: ״סֹלֶת מִנְחָה תָּמִיד״ – הֲרֵי הִיא לְךָ כְּמִנְחַת תְּמִידִין, מָה מִנְחַת תְּמִידִין שְׁלֹשָׁה לוּגִּין לְעִשָּׂרוֹן, אַף זוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה לוּגִּין לְעִשָּׂרוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to these two possibilities, **let us see to which** case **it is** more **similar,** i.e., which is a better comparison to the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest. Perhaps **we** should **derive** the *halakha* with regard to the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest, which has characteristics represented by the letters ***tav*, *beit*, *shin*, *tet*, from** the *halakha* with regard to a meal offering brought with libations, which also has the characteristics represented by the letters ***tav*, *beit*, *shin*, *tet*.** These characteristics are that they are **frequent [*tadir*],** as these offerings are sacrificed twice daily; they are **brought [*ba'ah*] as an obligation;** they **override Shabbat;** and they **override impurity [*tuma*].** **And we should not derive** the *halakha* with regard to the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest, which has the characteristics represented by the letters ***tav*, *beit*, *shin*, *tet*, from** the voluntary meal offering, which **does not** have the characteristics represented by the letters ***tav*, *beit*, *shin*, *tet*.**** **Or** perhaps, **go this way: We** should **derive** the *halakha* with regard to the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest, which has the characteristics represented by the letters ***yod*, *gimmel*, *yod*, *lamed*, from** the voluntary meal offering, which also has the characteristics represented by the letters ***yod*, *gimmel*, *yod*, *lamed*,** which stand for the following *halakhot*: Each of these offerings may be brought by **an individual [*yaḥid*];** each is **brought for [*biglal*] its own sake,** rather than accompanying another offering; they are not accompanied by **wine [*yayin*]** for a libation; and they require **frankincense [*levona*].** **And we should not derive** the *halakha* with regard to the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest, which has the characteristics represented by the letters ***yod*, *gimmel*, *yod*, *lamed*, from** the *halakha* with regard to the meal offering brought with libations, **which does not** have the characteristics represented by the letters ***yod*, *gimmel*, *yod*, *lamed*.** Consequently, the comparisons in both directions are equally compelling. The *baraita* continues its determination of how much oil is brought with the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest. **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, says:** "This is the offering of Aaron…the tenth part of an ephah of **fine flour for a meal offering perpetually [*tamid*],** half of it in the morning, and half of it in the evening" (Leviticus 6:13). The fact that the verse makes reference to the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest as *tamid* teaches that **it is in** the same category as **the meal offering** component **of the daily offerings [*temidin*]. Just as the meal offering** component **of the daily offerings** requires **three *log*** of oil **for** each **tenth** of an ephah of flour, **so too this** griddle-cake offering of the High Priest requires **three *log*** of oil **for** each **tenth** of an ephah of flour.

Rabbi Shimon beno shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka resolves the question by appeal to the *pasuk* itself. The verse describing the *chavitin* uses the word *tamid* — *solet minchah tamid*. Since it says *tamid*, just as the *minchat temidin* uses three *lugin* of oil per *isaron*, so too should the *chavitin*: *ma minchat temidin sheloshah lugin isaron, af zu sheloshah lugin isaron*.

### Proportionality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: רִיבָּה כָּאן שֶׁמֶן, וְרִיבָּה בְּמִנְחַת כְּבָשִׂים שֶׁמֶן, מָה לְהַלָּן שְׁלֹשֶׁת לוּגִּין לְעִשָּׂרוֹן, אַף כָּאן שְׁלֹשֶׁת לוּגִּין לְעִשָּׂרוֹן. אוֹ כְּלָךְ לְדֶרֶךְ זוֹ: רִיבָּה כָּאן שֶׁמֶן, וְרִיבָּה בְּמִנְחַת פָּרִים וְאֵילִים שֶׁמֶן, מָה לְהַלָּן שְׁנֵי לוּגִּין לְעִשָּׂרוֹן, אַף כָּאן שְׁנֵי לוּגִּין לְעִשָּׂרוֹן? נִרְאֶה לְמִי דּוֹמֶה: דָּנִין מִנְחָה הַבָּאָה עִשָּׂרוֹן, מִמִּנְחָה הַבָּאָה בְּעִשָּׂרוֹן, וְאֵין דָּנִין מִנְחָה הַבָּאָה עִשָּׂרוֹן, מִמִּנְחָה הַבָּאָה שְׁנַיִם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה עֶשְׂרוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** The verse **adds** to the amount of **oil** that is required **here,** with regard to the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest, **and** it similarly **adds** to the amount of **oil** that is required **there,** in the case of the **meal offering** that accompanies the sacrifice of **sheep. Just as there,** in the case of the meal offering that accompanies the sacrifice of sheep, **three *log*** of oil are required **for** each **tenth** of an ephah of flour, **so too here,** in the case of the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest, **three *log*** of oil are required **for** each **tenth** of an ephah of flour. **Or** perhaps, **go this way:** The verse **adds** to the amount of **oil** that is required **here,** and it similarly **adds** to the amount of **oil** that is required in the case of **the meal offering** that accompanies the sacrifice of **bulls and rams. Just as there,** in the case of the meal offering that accompanies the sacrifice of bulls and rams, **two *log*** of oil are required **for** each **tenth** of an ephah of flour, **so too here,** in the case of the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest, **two *log*** of oil are required **for** each **tenth** of an ephah of flour. With regard to these two possibilities, **let us see to which** case **it is** more **similar,** i.e., which is a better comparison to the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest. Perhaps **we** should **derive** the *halakha* with regard to the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest, which is **a meal offering that comes** in the amount of **a tenth** of an ephah of flour, **from** the meal offering that accompanies the sacrifice of sheep, which is also **a meal offering that comes** in the amount of **a tenth** of an ephah of flour. **And we should not derive** the *halakha* with regard to **a meal offering that comes** in the amount of **a tenth** of an ephah of flour **from** the *halakha* in the case of **a meal offering that comes** in the amount of **two or three tenths** of an ephah of flour, such as the meal offerings that accompany the sacrifice of bulls and rams.

Rabbi Shimon independently approaches the question through the word *bashemen* — "with oil" — which implies adding a substantial quantity of oil. But how much? He considers two possible comparisons. On one hand, the *minchat kevasim* (the flour offering accompanying a lamb) uses three *lugin* per *isaron*. On the other hand, the *minchat parim ve-eilim* (accompanying rams and bulls) uses two *lugin* per *isaron*. Which is the correct analog?

The resolution comes from a principle of proportionality: *denin minchah ha-ba'ah be-isaron mi-minchah ha-ba'ah be-isaron* — we compare a *minchah* that comes in a single *isaron* to another *minchah* that comes in a single *isaron*. We do not compare it to offerings that come in two or three *isronot*, since those proportions are structurally different. The lamb offering comes with one *isaron*, just like the *chavitin*, and its oil ratio is three *lugin* per *isaron*. That ratio fits proportionately for the *chavitin*; the ram-and-bull ratio does not match when scaled to a single *isaron*. Therefore: three *lugin*.

### Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא גּוּפַאּ קַשְׁיָא, אָמְרַתְּ ״בְּשֶׁמֶן״ לְהוֹסִיף לָהּ שֶׁמֶן, וַהֲדַר תָּנֵי: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״שֶׁמֶן״, וְנֶאֱמַר בְּמִנְחַת נְדָבָה ״שֶׁמֶן״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question with regard to the beginning of the *baraita*: **This** *baraita* **itself** is **difficult,** as it contains an internal contradiction. First **you said** that the expression **"with the oil"** stated in the verse concerning the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest serves **to add** extra **oil,** which indicates that more than the basic amount of one *log* of oil is required. **And then it teaches: It is stated here,** concerning the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest: **"Oil," and it is stated with regard to** the **voluntary meal offering** that it must be brought with **"oil."** The *baraita* suggests that just as one *log* of oil is brought with the voluntary meal offering, so too one *log* is brought with the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest.

A difficulty now emerges from within the *baraita* itself. It opened by saying *bashemen lehosif lo shemen* — *bashemen* teaches that one must add a generous quantity of oil, implying a large amount. Yet it then proposed comparing the *chavitin* to the *minchat nedavah* — which requires only one *log*. These two positions appear contradictory: if *bashemen* means to add a lot, one *log* hardly qualifies; and if the comparison to the *nedavah* is valid, there is nothing especially large about one *log*.

### Bahsemen Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מַאן תְּנָא בַּשֶּׁמֶן לְהוֹסִיף? רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, וְאִילּוּ לֹא נֶאֱמַר קָאָמַר. וּבְדִינָא מַאן קָא מַהְדַּר? רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: ״בְּשֶׁמֶן״ – לְהוֹסִיף לָהּ שֶׁמֶן, דְּאִי לִקְבּוֹעַ שֶׁמֶן – לָא צְרִיךְ, כֵּיוָן דִּכְתִיב בַּהּ ״עַל מַחֲבַת״ – כְּמִנְחַת מַחֲבַת דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** In answer to this question, **Abaye said: Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** that the term **"with the oil"** with regard to the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest serves **to add** extra oil? **It is Rabbi Shimon,** who holds that three *log* of oil are required, as derived from the meal offering that accompanies the sacrifice of sheep. **And** Rabbi Shimon **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **If it were not stated.** If the verse had not indicated by the term "with the oil" that extra oil is required, it would have been possible to derive from the voluntary meal offering that only one *log* is required. **And who** is the *tanna* who **responds** to Rabbi Shimon's **derivation** and suggests that in fact the amount of oil can be derived from the case of the voluntary meal offering? It is **Rabbi Yishmael.** **And this** is what Rabbi Shimon **is saying:** The term **"with the oil"** serves **to add** extra **oil to it. As, if** the purpose of that term were merely **to establish** the basic fact that the offering must include **oil,** a verse is **not needed** to teach that. **Since it is written with** respect to the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest: **"On a griddle [*maḥavat*]"** (Leviticus 6:14), it is **comparable to a pan [*maḥavat*]** meal offering, which requires oil.

The resolution is attributed to the school of Rabbah bar Avuha, and its logic is clarified by Abaye: the one who said *bashemen lehosif shemen* is Rabbi Shimon. The reasoning is as follows. Since the *pasuk* already says *al machavat* — on the frying pan — it is obvious that oil must be used, because you do not fry on a *machavat* without oil. If *bashemen* merely meant "use oil," it would be superfluous given *al machavat*. Therefore *bashemen* must mean something additional: add more oil than the *machavat* itself implies. It signals a generous, additional quantity of oil.

But then, why propose comparing it to the *nedavah* at all? Because Rabbi Shimon entertained the possibility that *bashemen* might not be teaching a quantity but merely emphasizing the type — confirming that oil must be used, lest one think the *chavitin* is like a *charvot* (a dry flour offering, not fried), since some *menachot* are indeed brought without oil. *Bashemen* would then rule out the dry alternative, without specifying a large quantity. It is only after this possibility is considered and the comparison to the *nedavah* (one *log*) explored that Rabbi Shimon concludes the logical comparisons are inconclusive, making the explicit *bashemen* indispensable as a directive to use more oil — and then the deductive work of proportionality brings him to three *lugin*.

### Rava Reconciles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לִקְבּוֹעַ לָהּ שֶׁמֶן, דְּאִי לָא כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״בַּשֶּׁמֶן״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא תֶּיהְוֵי כְּמִנְחַת חוֹטֵא. הֲדַר אָמַר: תִּיהְוֵי נָמֵי לִקְבּוֹעַ לָהּ שֶׁמֶן, תֵּיתֵי מִדִּינָא, וְדָן דִּינָא וְלָא אָתְיָא לֵיהּ, וְאַצְרְכַהּ קְרָא ״סֹלֶת מִנְחָה תָּמִיד״, כְּדִמְסַיֵּים רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל מִילְּתֵיהּ. רַבָּה אָמַר: כּוּלַּהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן [וְאִילּוּ לֹא נֶאֱמַר קָאָמַר]. וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: ״בְּשֶׁמֶן״ לְהוֹסִיף לָהּ שֶׁמֶן, דְּאִי לִקְבּוֹעַ לָהּ שֶׁמֶן לָא צְרִיךְ, כֵּיוָן דִּכְתִיב בַּהּ ״עַל מַחֲבַת״ כְּמַחֲבַת דָּמְיָא, וְעַד שֶׁלֹּא יֵאָמֵר ״בְּשֶׁמֶן״ יֵשׁ לִי בַּדִּין. וְדָן דִּינָא, לָא אָתְיָא לֵיהּ, וְאַצְרְכַהּ ״בְּשֶׁמֶן״, הֲדַר אָמַר: תֶּיהְוֵי כְּמִנְחַת פָּרִים וְאֵילִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Or** perhaps the purpose of the verse is **to establish only** that the offering must include **oil,** and it is necessary to teach that **because if the Merciful One had not stated: "With the oil," I would say: Let it be like** the **meal offering of a sinner,** which does not include oil. Rabbi Shimon **then said: Let it even be** that the purpose of the verse is **to establish only** that **it** requires **oil,** and even so one can **arrive** at the conclusion that three *log* are required due to the **derivation** based upon the verbal analogy from the meal offering brought with the libations that accompany animal offerings. **But** although Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, attempts to **employ** this **derivation he is unsuccessful,** as there is a counter-indication from another verbal analogy to the voluntary meal offering. Therefore, Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, **needed** to derive the *halakha* from the **verse: "Fine flour for a meal offering perpetually [*tamid*]"** (Leviticus 6:13), **as Rabbi Yishmael,** son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, **concluded his statement** in the *baraita*. **Rabba said** that the *baraita* should be understood differently: The **entire** *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, and** Rabbi Shimon **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **If it were not stated.** **And this** is what Rabbi Shimon **is saying:** The term **"with the oil"** serves **to add** extra **oil to it. As, if** the purpose of that term were merely **to establish** that the offering must include **oil,** a verse is **not needed** to teach that. **Since it is written with** respect to the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest: **"On a griddle [*maḥavat*]"** (Leviticus 6:14), it is **comparable to a pan [*maḥavat*]** meal offering, which requires oil. **And** even **if** the verse **had not stated: "With the oil," I have** a manner of **derivation** for the fact that more than one *log* of oil is required, based upon a verbal analogy. **But** although Rabbi Shimon attempts to **employ** this **derivation he is unsuccessful,** as there is a counter-indication from another verbal analogy, **and** therefore the term **"with the oil" is needed** to teach that more than one *log* of oil is required. Nevertheless, this teaches only that more oil than usual is required, but the specific amount still must be clarified. Rabbi Shimon **then said: Let** the griddle-cake offering of the High Priest **be like the meal offering** that accompanies the sacrifice of **bulls and rams,** which require two *log* of oil per ephah of flour.

Abaye and Rava both hold that the entire passage reflects Rabbi Shimon's reasoning, but they organize the internal steps somewhat differently. Rava's version: *bashemen* signals additional oil; if there were no explicit *bashemen*, Rabbi Shimon would have tried to derive the amount by analogy — comparing it to the *tamid* (three *lugin*) or to the *nedavah* (one *log*) — but these logical comparisons prove inconclusive because valid arguments point in both directions. Therefore the explicit word *bashemen* is indispensable, and Rabbi Shimon then establishes three *lugin* through the proportionality argument comparing *isaron*-to-*isaron* offerings. In both Abaye's and Rava's accounts, all the positions in the passage belong to Rabbi Shimon beno shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, with the internal sequence reflecting his own working-through of the question before arriving at the final ruling of three *lugin* per *isaron*.
