# Ketubot 44b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Motzi Shem Ra Intro

### Overview & Ketubah

The *sugya* we now enter is that of *motzi shem ra* — the case in which a husband rises the morning after his wedding and claims his wife was not a virgin. This topic is one of two places in the Torah where the framework of *na'arah betulah* and virginity converges, and it connects directly to the very first *mishnah* of the tractate. The institution of the *ketubah* itself is rooted in this passage: if the husband is proven a liar, he pays one hundred *kessef*, which is double the fifty *kessef* of the *mohar* — the bride-price that serves as the basis for the Rabbinic institution of the *ketubah*. (To be precise, fifty *kessef* equals two hundred *zuz*, so one hundred *kessef* equals four hundred *zuz* — the relationship is one of *keifel*, doubling.)

### Mishnah Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַגִּיּוֹרֶת שֶׁנִּתְגַּיְּרָה בִּתָּהּ עִמָּהּ וְזִינְּתָה — הֲרֵי זוֹ בְּחֶנֶק, אֵין לָהּ לֹא פֶּתַח בֵּית הָאָב, וְלֹא מֵאָה סֶלַע. הָיְתָה הוֹרָתָהּ שֶׁלֹּא בִּקְדוּשָּׁה וְלֵידָתָהּ בִּקְדוּשָּׁה — הֲרֵי זוֹ בִּסְקִילָה, וְאֵין לָהּ לֹא פֶּתַח בֵּית הָאָב, וְלֹא מֵאָה סֶלַע. הָיְתָה הוֹרָתָהּ וְלֵידָתָהּ בִּקְדוּשָּׁה, הֲרֵי הִיא כְּבַת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכׇל דְּבָרֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of a female **convert whose daughter converted with her and** later, as a young woman, the daughter **engaged in licentious sexual relations** when she was betrothed, **she is** executed **by strangulation,** not stoning, the method of execution that would be employed had she been born Jewish. **She has neither** the *halakha* of being executed at the **entrance to** her **father's house,** as in the case of a woman who was born Jewish who committed this crime, **nor** does she receive **one hundred *sela*** if her husband defamed her by falsely claiming that she had committed adultery. The reason is that the verses state "Israel" (Deuteronomy 22:19, 21) with regard to these *halakhot*, indicating that these *halakhot* apply only to those born as Jews. However, if the daughter's **conception occurred** when her mother **was not** yet **in** a state of **sanctity,** i.e., when she was still a gentile, **but her birth** took place when her mother was **in** a state of **sanctity,** as her mother converted during her pregnancy, **this** daughter is punishable **by stoning** if she committed adultery as a betrothed young woman. However, **she has neither** the *halakha* of being executed at the **entrance to** her **father's house, nor** the right to **one hundred *sela*** if it turns out that her husband defamed her. If **her conception and birth occurred** when her mother **was in** a state of **sanctity,** i.e., after she converted, **she is like a** regular **Jewish woman in all matters.**

The *mishnah* addressed several cases that bear on the application of these laws. One case involves a woman who converted — touching on the phrase *be-Yisrael* in the verse, since such a woman was not originally *Yisrael*. Another involves a woman whose mother converted while pregnant, meaning she was conceived before her mother's conversion and born after. This woman has no legal father in the relevant sense, implicating the phrase *beis aviha*. These cases raise the question of how much the special *halachos* of *na'arah betulah* depend on the full framing of the Torah's language.

### Special Halachos

The special *halachos* of *na'arah betulah me'orasah* are these: if she committed adultery, she receives *sekilah* rather than the ordinary *chenek* that applies to adultery in general; and if the husband is lying and spreading a false report, he pays one hundred *kessef* and receives *malkus*. The operative question throughout this *sugya* is which of these consequences depends on the full configuration of *na'arah betulah me'orasah*, and how much the surrounding phrases — *navala be-Yisrael*, *beis aviha* — affect each of these *dinim* individually.

## Sekilah Derivation

### Reish Lakish's Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״וָמֵתָה״, לְרַבּוֹת הוֹרָתָהּ שֶׁלֹּא בִּקְדוּשָּׁה וְלֵידָתָהּ בִּקְדוּשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: Since the rulings of the mishna are based on the principle that the special *halakhot* of a betrothed young woman who committed adultery apply only to a woman who was born Jewish, the Gemara questions the *halakha* that a woman who was conceived when her mother was a gentile but born when her mother was Jewish is executed via stoning: **From where are these matters** derived? **Reish Lakish said: As the verse states:** "And the men of her city shall stone her with stones **that she die"** (Deuteronomy 22:21). The phrase "that she die" is superfluous and comes **to include** one **whose conception** occurred when her mother **was not** yet **in** a state of **sanctity but her birth** took place when her mother was **in** a state of **sanctity.**

The *gemara* opens with the question *mina hani mili* — from where are all these laws derived? Reish Lakish answers: from the word *u-mesah* in the verse. The verse states *ve-sakluha kol anshei ira ba-avanim va-mesah* — they shall stone her and she shall die. Since stoning obviously results in death, the word *u-mesah* is redundant and therefore available for a *derasha*.

### Conception Non-Jewish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי מִילְקָא נָמֵי נִילְקֵי, וּמֵאָה סֶלַע נָמֵי לְשַׁלֵּם! אָמַר קְרָא ״וָמֵתָה״ — לְמִיתָה נִתְרַבְּתָה וְלֹא לִקְנָס. וְאֵימָא לְרַבּוֹת הוֹרָתָהּ וְלֵידָתָהּ בִּקְדוּשָּׁה? הָהִיא יִשְׂרְאֵלִית מְעַלַּיְיתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** if the verse equates her to a regular Jewish woman, **let** her husband **also be flogged** if he defames her, **and let him also pay** the **one hundred *sela*.** The Gemara answers that **the verse states: "That she die"** (Deuteronomy 22:21), which indicates that **she was included with regard to** the **death** penalty **but not with regard to** the **fine.** The Gemara asks another question: **Say** that this verse comes **to include** only a girl **whose conception and birth** both occurred when her mother was **in** a state of **sanctity.** The Gemara responds: **That** girl **is a full-fledged Jewish woman,** and there is no difference between her and any other Jewish woman.

Reish Lakish uses this extra word *u-mesah* to include the case of *horasah she-lo bi-kdusha ve-liddasah bi-kdusha* — a woman conceived when her mother was not yet Jewish but born after her mother converted. To review the *mishnah*'s ruling: if a woman converted as an infant, she is entirely outside this *parshah* and receives *chenek* rather than *sekilah*. But if her mother converted while pregnant with her, the *mishnah* says she does receive *sekilah* despite having no legal father. The source for retaining *sekilah* in this intermediate case is the extra *u-mesah*.

### Limitation to Meisa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא לְרַבּוֹת הוֹרָתָהּ וְלֵידָתָהּ שֶׁלֹּא בִּקְדוּשָּׁה! אִם כֵּן, ״בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל״ מַאי אַהֲנִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question from the opposite perspective: **And say** that the verse comes **to include** even one **whose conception and birth** both occurred when her mother was **not in** a state of **sanctity.** The Gemara answers: **If so,** the phrase **"in Israel"** (Deuteronomy 22:21), **what purpose does it** serve? This expression indicates that this *halakha* applies only to a woman who was born Jewish.

The *gemara* immediately challenges: *i hachi*, once we include this woman via *u-mesah*, why not include her for all the *halachos* — meaning, why shouldn't the husband also pay the hundred *kessef* and receive *malkus* if he is lying? The *gemara* answers: *le-misa israbsa ve-lo le-knas* — she was included only for the *din* of *misah*, not for the fine. The word *u-mesah* is the mechanism of inclusion, and it extends only to the punishment of death.

The *gemara* then asks: perhaps *u-mesah* is not including this intermediate case at all, but rather is simply including the ordinary case of a woman born after her mother's conversion — *horasah ve-liddasah bi-kdusha*? The *gemara* dismisses this: *hi Yisrael me-ikara hi* — such a woman is fully Jewish from the outset and requires no special inclusion.

### Exclusion Logic

A further possibility is raised: perhaps *u-mesah* includes even a woman who converted as an infant — *horasah ve-liddasah she-lo bi-kdusha*? The *gemara* rejects this as well: *be-Yisrael mai ahni lei* — if the extra *u-mesah* is doing that work, what purpose does the word *be-Yisrael* serve? Obviously *be-Yisrael* is not simply excluding non-Jews — that is self-evident. So *be-Yisrael* must be doing something, namely excluding someone who is in some sense not fully rooted in Jewish status from birth. That exclusion reaches the *halachos* of the hundred *kessef* and *malkus*, removing the husband from those obligations. Meanwhile, the extra *u-mesah* overrides that exclusion specifically with regard to *sekilah*, retaining her in the *parshah* for that punishment alone.

## Yesomah & Knas

### Rav Yose on Yesomah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: הַמּוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע עַל הַיְּתוֹמָה פָּטוּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנָתְנוּ לַאֲבִי הַנַּעֲרָה״ — פְּרָט לְזוֹ שֶׁאֵין לָהּ אָב.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina said: The defamer of an orphan** girl **is exempt, as it is stated:** "And they shall fine him a hundred shekels of silver, **and give them to the father of the young woman"** (Deuteronomy 22:19), which **excludes this one who does not have a father.**

Having established the case of the woman conceived as a non-Jew, the *gemara* turns to a second case — the *yesomah*, a woman whose father is dead. Rav Yose bar Chanina teaches: *hamotzi shem ra al ha-yesomah patur* — one who spreads an evil report against a fatherless woman is exempt from the special punishments. The *mishnah* had already indicated that such a woman still receives *sekilah* — that was not considered a great *chiddush*, since even the woman conceived as a non-Jew retained *sekilah*. What the *mishnah* did not address explicitly is whether the husband's obligations — the hundred *kessef* and *malkus* — still apply when there is no living father.

### Source Exclusion

Rav Yose bar Chanina's source is the verse *venatenu le-avi ha-na'arah* — the payment is given to the father of the *na'arah*. *Prat lezo she-ein lah av* — this excludes the case where she has no father. The payment of the hundred *kessef* is directed to the father, and without a father, the obligation does not attach.

### Challenge from Mefateh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר אָבִין, וְאִיתֵּימָ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר זְבִידָא: ״וְאִם מָאֵן יְמָאֵן אָבִיהָ״ — לְרַבּוֹת יְתוֹמָה לִקְנָס, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei bar Avin, and some say** it was **Rabbi Yosei bar Zevida, raised an objection to this** from the following *baraita*: The verse states with regard to a seduced young woman: **"If her father utterly refuse [*ma'en yima'en*]** to give her to him, he shall pay money according to the dowry of virgins" (Exodus 22:16). The double phrase "utterly refuse [*ma'en yima'en*]" comes **to include an orphan for** the **fine,** i.e., if she does not have a father and she herself refuses to marry her seducer, he must pay her the fine. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili.** This proves that the fact that the girl does not have a father does not exempt her seducer from paying the fine.

Rav Yosef bar Avin — some say Rav Yosef bar Zvida — raises a challenge from a *braisa* discussing the case of *mefateh*, seduction. The verse there uses the double formulation *ma'en yema'en aviha*, which Rabbi Yose HaGlili interprets as including the *yesomah* — even if there is no father, the *knas* is still paid in a case of seduction. If the *knas* applies by *mefateh* without a father, why should it not apply equally in *motzi shem ra*?

### Obligation Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא מוֹתֵיב לַהּ וְהוּא מְפָרֵק לַהּ: בָּבָא עָלֶיהָ וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִתְיַתְּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states that Rabbi Yosei bar Avin **raised the objection and he resolved it:** Rabbi Yosei HaGelili refers to **one who had intercourse with her and afterward was orphaned.** Since she had a father when the incident occurred, he is obligated to pay her the fine.

The *gemara* notes that the challenge and its resolution come from the same voice: *hu mosev lah ve-hu miparek lah*. The distinction is *be-vallah ve-achar kach nis'asama* — the *braisa* about *mefateh* is discussing a case where the father was alive at the time of the act and died afterward. In that case the obligation had already crystallized, and it remains payable even after the father's death — to her, or to the brothers, as discussed elsewhere. But if there was no father at the time of the original act, the obligation never came into existence in the first place, since the *knas* is by its nature payable to the father. This reasoning applies equally to *ones*, *mefateh*, and *motzi shem ra*: if there was a father at the time of the act, the obligation exists and survives his subsequent death; if there was never a father, there is no obligation.

### Rava's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: חַיָּיב. מִמַּאי — מִדְּתָנֵי אַמֵּי: בְּתוּלַת יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְלֹא בְּתוּלַת גֵּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said,** in contrast to Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina, that one who defames an orphan is **obligated** to pay the fine. **From where** does he learn this? He learns this **from** the fact that **Ami taught** that the fine applies to one who defamed **"a virgin of Israel"** (Deuteronomy 22:19) **and** does **not** apply to one who defamed **a virgin** who is **a convert.**

Rava, however, disagrees and rules *chayav* — the fine does apply even when there is no living father. Rava's position extends not only to *motzi shem ra* but presumably to *mefateh* and *ones* as well. His reasoning is drawn from the teaching of Ami.

### Betulat Yisrael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל מִיחַיַּיב, הַיְינוּ דְּאִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא לְמַעוֹטֵי גֵּרִים. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל כְּהַאי גַוְונָא פָּטוּר, הַשְׁתָּא בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל פָּטוּר, בְּגֵרִים מִיבַּעְיָא?!
>
> **Translation:** Rava elaborates: **Granted, if you say** that **in a case like this,** where a woman has no father, **with regard to** a woman who was born as **a Jew, he is obligated** to pay, **that is** why **it was necessary** for **the verse to exclude converts.** Every convert is considered like an orphan, as the familial connection with her parents is severed upon her conversion, and therefore it is as though she did not have a father. **However, if you say that in a case like this involving** a woman born as **a Jew he is exempt, now** if in a case **involving** a woman born as **a Jew** he is **exempt, is** it **necessary** to derive from a verse **that converts** are not entitled to the fine? The fact that there is such a derivation in the case of converts indicates that in the case of a Jewish-born orphan, the defamer must pay a fine.

Ami teaches: *betulat Yisrael ve-lo betulat gerim* — the verse *ki motzi shem ra al betulat Yisrael* specifies a *betulat Yisrael*, excluding the convert. This exclusion of converts is meaningful only against a baseline in which the ordinary case — a naturally born Jewish woman whose father happens to have died — still obligates the *knas*.

### Reconciliation Logic

The *gemara* makes this explicit: *i amarta bishlama ki hai gavna be-Yisrael mechayev* — if in the standard case of a Jewish woman with no living father the *knas* still applies, then the exclusion of *betulat gerim* is meaningful, because it tells us that the woman who came through conversion is a special exclusion beyond the mere absence of a father. But *ela i amarta be-Yisrael ki hai gavna patur* — if a natural-born Jewish *yesomah* were also exempt, then the exclusion of the convert would be entirely superfluous, since the convert is obviously in a more attenuated position than a Jewish *yesomah*. The force of the exclusion *betulat gerim* therefore proves that the ordinary *yesomah* who is naturally born Jewish remains fully obligated in the *knas*. The payment in that case would be made to her directly.

## Motzi Shem Ra & Ketanah

### Reish Lakish on Ketanah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הַמּוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע עַל הַקְּטַנָּה — פָּטוּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנָתְנוּ לַאֲבִי הַנַּעֲרָה״, ״נַעֲרָה״ מָלֵא דִּבֵּר הַכָּתוּב.
>
> **Translation:** § **Reish Lakish said: The defamer** of **a minor** girl **is exempt, as it is stated: "And give them to the father of the young woman [*na'ara*]"** (Deuteronomy 22:19). The word *na'ara* is written in full, with the letter *heh* at the end, whereas elsewhere in the Torah it is written without the *heh*. This indicates that **the verse was speaking** of a female who has **fully** attained the status of **a young woman,** rather than a minor who has not yet reached the state of being a young woman.

The *gemara* now shifts from the question of *yesomah* to the question of age. Reish Lakish teaches: *hamotzi shem ra al ha-ketanah patur* — one who spreads an evil report against a minor is exempt. While there was a debate between Rabbi Meir and the Sages regarding *ones u-mefateh* and whether a *ketanah* is included, Reish Lakish holds that in *motzi shem ra* everyone would agree the minor is excluded.

### Father-Based Exemption

Reish Lakish's initial source is the verse *venatenu le-avi ha-na'arah*, where *na'arah* is written in full — *na'arah malei diber ha-katuv* — with the letter *hey*, indicating a full *na'arah*, which the *derasha* reads as specifying an adult. Beyond the textual point, there is also a compelling logical basis: the act of *motzi shem ra* against a minor is not nearly as consequential as against an adult, because even if the husband's claim were believed, the minor could never be executed as a result — since *pituyas ketanah ones* is the law, sexual activity with a minor is always treated as statutory rape. The minor would never be found guilty of voluntary adultery and thus could never receive the capital punishment this *parshah* contemplates.

### Rav Acha's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר אַבָּא: טַעְמָא דִּכְתִיב בַּהּ ״הַנַּעֲרָה״, הָא לָאו הָכִי הֲוָה אָמֵינָא אֲפִילּוּ קְטַנָּה?! הָא כְּתִיב: ״וְאִם אֱמֶת הָיָה הַדָּבָר הַזֶּה לֹא נִמְצְאוּ בְתוּלִים לַנַּעֲרָה. וְהוֹצִיאוּ אֶת הַנַּעֲרָה אֶל פֶּתַח בֵּית אָבִיהָ וּסְקָלוּהָ״, וּקְטַנָּה לָאו בַּת עוֹנָשִׁין הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Abba** strongly **objects to this:** Is it correct that **the reason is that it is written** with regard to **her "*na'ara*"** in full, **but if that were not so, I would say** that **even a minor** is included in this *halakha*? **Isn't it written: "But if this matter is true, that the tokens of virginity were not found in this young woman, then they shall bring out the young woman to the entrance to her father's house and** the men of her city shall **stone her"** (Deuteronomy 22:20–21)? **And** since **a minor is not eligible for punishment,** this verse evidently is referring to a young woman, not a minor, and therefore there is no need for the aforementioned exposition.

Rav Acha bar Abba challenges Reish Lakish's derivation. He argues: the mere presence of the letter *hey* in *na'arah* is not sufficient to derive that we are discussing an adult. Without the *hey*, *ta'ima de-ketav beh* — without that *hey*, I would have thought even a minor is included. But, he continues, the Torah itself makes clear that an adult is meant from context: *ha-ketiv ve-im emet hayah ha-davar ha-zeh lo nimtzu betulim la-na'arah ve-hotzi'u et ha-na'arah petach beit aviha u-skaluha* — the text explicitly involves execution. Since a minor cannot be executed, the *parshah* is obviously discussing an adult. The extra *hey* therefore seems unnecessary — the context already implies adulthood.

### Context & Execution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: כָּאן ״נַעֲרָה״, הָא כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״נַעֲרָ״ — אֲפִילּוּ קְטַנָּה בַּמַּשְׁמָע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the verse should be understood as follows: **Here,** where it is evident that the Torah is referring to a young woman, it writes ***na'ara*** with a *heh*, from which it may be inferred that **wherever it is stated *na'ara*** without a *heh* at the end, **it indicates** that the verse is referring **even** to **a minor** girl. The term *na'ara* without a *heh* is referring to both a minor and a young woman and excludes only an adult woman.

The *gemara* accepts the force of this observation. *Ve-ketanah lav bat onshin hi* — a minor is not subject to capital punishment. Therefore: *ela kan na'arah* — the starting point is the explicit context of the *parshah*, which clearly addresses an adult. It is not the letter *hey* that teaches us we require an adult; rather, the entire scenario of execution makes it self-evident.

### Linguistic Rule

Once we establish from context that this *parshah* discusses an adult, a general linguistic principle follows. *Ha-kol makom she-ne'emar na'arah* without the *hey* — written as *na'ar* — *afilu ketanah be-mashma* — even a minor is included. The derivation thus proceeds in stages: the context makes clear we are dealing with an adult, and once we know that, the presence or absence of the *hey* in other places becomes a systematic indicator of whether the reference is to an adult or potentially to a minor as well. By *motzi shem ra*, then, the woman must unambiguously be an adult *na'arah* — neither a *ketanah* nor, as will emerge, a *bogeret*.

## Three Cases Adultery

### Case 1: After Nisuin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי שֵׁילָא: שָׁלֹשׁ מִדּוֹת בַּנַּעֲרָה: בָּאוּ לָהּ עֵדִים בְּבֵית חָמִיהָ שֶׁזִּינְּתָה בְּבֵית אָבִיהָ — סוֹקְלִין אוֹתָהּ עַל פֶּתַח בֵּית אָבִיהָ, כְּלוֹמַר: רְאוּ גִּידּוּלִים שֶׁגִּידַּלְתֶּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Sheila taught** in a *baraita*: There are **three** different **circumstances with regard to a young woman** who has been defamed. If **witnesses came** to testify about **her** when she was **in her father-in-law's house,** i.e., after she was married, and stated **that she committed adultery in her father's house,** when she was betrothed, **one stones her at the entrance to her father's house, as though to say: See what you have brought up.**

The *gemara* presents a *braisa* from Shila: *shalosh middot be-na'arah* — there are three scenarios involving the *na'arah me'orasah* who committed adultery. The first is the classical case of the Torah: *ba'u lah eidim be-veit chamiha she-zineta be-veit aviha* — witnesses come when she is already in her father-in-law's house, meaning after *nisuin*, and testify that she committed adultery while she was still in her father's house, meaning during *erusin*. This is the paradigmatic case of *na'arah me'orasah*, and one notes that *chazal* understand the Torah's apparently evidentiary procedure — producing the bedsheet — as in fact a shorthand for witnesses whose testimony is subsequently tested and either confirmed or refuted by *eidim zomemim*. In this case: *soklin osah al pesach beis aviha* — she is stoned at the doorway of her father's house.

### Sekilah Location

The symbolism here is explicit in the *braisa*: *re'u gidulim she-gidalten* — look at what you have raised. The stoning at the father's doorway is an act of casting shame back upon the household that produced her. The case differs from the second scenario, where the adultery is discovered before *nisuin*, in which case execution takes place at the city gate — *al pesach sha'ar ha-ir* — in keeping with the verse in the *parshah* of *na'arah me'orasah* that says *ve-hotzeisem et sheneihem el sha'ar ha-ir ha-hi*. Why is the location different? When the matter is discovered after *nisuin*, the husband and his family are the injured party in a specific and intimate way; the symbolism of shame cast back on the father's house fits that context. When it is discovered before *nisuin*, the act is equally severe but the dynamic of injury is not yet framed in the same way, so the punishment is public but without that directed element of shame. The two locations thus reflect not just the legal stage of the relationship but the distinct moral and symbolic messaging each scenario conveys.

### Case 2: Becomes Bogerit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּאוּ לָהּ עֵדִים בְּבֵית אָבִיהָ שֶׁזִּינְּתָה בְּבֵית אָבִיהָ — סוֹקְלִין אוֹתָהּ עַל פֶּתַח שַׁעַר הָעִיר. סָרְחָה וּלְבַסּוֹף בָּגְרָה — תִּידּוֹן בְּחֶנֶק.
>
> **Translation:** If **witnesses came** to testify about **her** when she was **in her father's house,** i.e., when she was betrothed, and testified **that she committed adultery in her father's house, one stones her at the entrance to the gate of the city.** If **she went astray** and sinned when she was a young woman **and subsequently reached majority,** i.e., she became a grown woman, **she is sentenced to strangulation,** which is the punishment for a grown woman who committed adultery.

The third scenario introduced by Shila is the most striking: *sarcha u-vasof bagra* — she committed adultery as a *na'arah me'orasah*, but by the time the matter is discovered and adjudicated she has become a *bogeret*, an adult woman no longer in her father's domain. In this case, Shila rules: *tidon be-chenek* — she is judged by strangulation, not by *sekilah*.

### Shila's Position

This is a remarkable ruling. The sin was committed when she was a *na'arah me'orasah* and was therefore deserving of *sekilah*. Yet Shila holds that the applicable punishment is determined not by the time of the sin but by the woman's current status at the time of judgment. Were she to commit the same act now as a *bogeret*, the punishment would be *chenek*; therefore, even though she deserved *sekilah* when she sinned, she now receives only *chenek*. The principle Shila articulates is: *kol heika de-ishtani gufa ishtani ktala* — when the body changes, the punishment changes.

## Sin vs. Status Debate

### Braita Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּכֹל הֵיכָא דְּאִישְׁתַּנִּי גּוּפָא אִישְׁתַּנִּי קְטָלָא? וּרְמִינְהִי: נַעֲרָה הַמְאוֹרָסָה שֶׁזִּינְּתָה, וּמִשֶּׁבָּגְרָה הוֹצִיא עָלֶיהָ שֵׁם רַע הוּא אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה, וְאֵינוֹ נוֹתֵן מֵאָה סֶלַע, הִיא וְזוֹמְמֶיהָ מַקְדִּימִין לְבֵית הַסְּקִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to this *halakha*: **Is that to say that** in **any** case **where her body has changed** after her sin, the manner in which she is put to **death changes** as well? The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from the following *baraita*: If there is **a betrothed young woman who** allegedly **committed adultery, and after she reached majority** she married and her husband **defamed her,** accusing her of having committed adultery during the period of betrothal, **he is not flogged and does not give** the **one hundred *sela*** if she is proven innocent, as these punishments are limited to one who defames a young woman (Deuteronomy 22:19). However, if she is guilty, **she and her conspiring witnesses are brought early** in the morning **to the place of stoning.** This proves that although her body changed between the time of the sin and the time of her punishment, she is stoned nevertheless.

The *gemara* immediately raises a contradiction from another *braisa*. That *braisa* discusses a case where a *na'arah me'orasah* committed adultery, and after she became a *bogeret* the husband spread the evil report — *u-mishe-bagra hotzi aleiha shem ra*. The *braisa* states: *hu eino lokeh ve-eino noten me'ah sela* — if he is lying, he does not receive *malkus* and does not pay the hundred *sela*, because the act of *motzi shem ra* occurred when she was already a *bogeret* and the category therefore does not apply to him. Up to this point, the ruling follows a logic consistent with Shila.

But the *braisa* continues: *hi ve-zomemeiha makdimim le-veit ha-sekilah* — she and the false witnesses go to be stoned. The *gemara* objects: *hi ve-zomemeiha salka da'atach* — it cannot be both! Either she did it and is stoned, or the witnesses lied and they are stoned; they cannot both be stoned. The *gemara* therefore emends: *ela o hi o zomemeiha makdimim le-veit ha-sekilah* — either she or the false witnesses are put to death by *sekilah*. The critical point follows: if it was true and she actually committed adultery as a *na'arah betulah*, even though it is now five years later and she is a *bogeret*, she receives *sekilah* — the punishment she deserved at the time of the sin. This is a direct contradiction of Shila, who said she should receive *chenek* based on her current status.

The *gemara* acknowledges this is apparently a *machloket tanna'im* and attempts to reconcile the sources before resorting to that conclusion.

### Rava's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִיא וְזוֹמְמֶיהָ סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! אֶלָּא: אוֹ הִיא אוֹ זוֹמְמֶיהָ מַקְדִּימִין לְבֵית הַסְּקִילָה. אָמַר רָבָא: מוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע קָאָמְרַתְּ? שָׁאנֵי מוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע דְּחִידּוּשׁ הוּא, דְּהָא נִכְנְסָה לְחוּפָּה וְלֹא נִבְעֲלָה, בְּעָלְמָא, וְזִינְּתָה — בְּחֶנֶק, וְאִילּוּ מוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע — בִּסְקִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara digresses to analyze the last clause of this *baraita*: Can it **enter your mind** to say that both **she and her conspiring witnesses,** i.e., witnesses who falsely testified that she committed adultery, are executed? If the witnesses who testified against her were telling the truth and she sinned, only she is liable to be stoned, and if the court discovers that they were false, conspiring witnesses, then they are stoned and she is exempt. **Rather,** the text of the *baraita* should be emended so that it reads: **Either she or her conspiring witnesses are brought early** in the morning **to the place of stoning.** In answer to the contradiction, **Rava said: A defamer, you said? A defamer is different as it is a novel** *halakha*. Certain aspects of this case do not apply to other *halakhot* **as, generally,** if a woman who **entered the** wedding canopy **and did not** yet **have intercourse** with her husband subsequently **committed adultery,** she is executed **by strangulation,** which is the punishment for a married woman who committed adultery. **However,** in the case of **a defamer,** if the woman is guilty she is executed **by stoning,** despite the fact that if she would commit the sin in her current state, as a married woman, she would be executed via strangulation. This proves that in the case of defamation, the method of execution is determined by the time when the sin was committed, although her status has since changed.

Rava proposes a distinction: *motzi shem ra ka-amart* — are you speaking of the *motzi shem ra* scenario? *Shani motzi shem ra de-chidusho* — *motzi shem ra* is different because it is itself a *chiddush*. Normally, witnesses come and testify that a woman committed adultery; we judge her based on her current status. But the entire category of *motzi shem ra* is already exceptional.

The exceptional nature of *motzi shem ra* is illustrated by the following case. Consider a woman who entered the *chuppah* but the marriage was not yet consummated — *nichnesah le-chuppah ve-lo niv'alah*. She is technically a *nesu'ah*. If she were now to commit adultery, the punishment would be *chenek*, not *sekilah*, because the category of *na'arah me'orasah* no longer applies once she has entered the *chuppah*. Yet if this is the *motzi shem ra* scenario — if the husband makes his claim the morning after — and witnesses prove he is telling the truth, she receives *sekilah*. The fact that she is already a *nesu'ah* does not change the outcome; the *din* of *motzi shem ra* goes by the original act, not by her status at the moment of discovery. Rava thus argues: since *motzi shem ra* is already an exception in the case of the *nesu'ah*, one who holds Shila's position can say the same extends to the *bogeret* — *motzi shem ra* simply always follows the time of the original act, while for all other cases the principle of judging by current status remains intact.

### Why Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְרָבָא: דִּלְמָא כִּי חַדֵּית רַחֲמָנָא הֵיכָא דְּלָא אִישְׁתַּנִּי גּוּפָא, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּאִישְׁתַּנִּי גּוּפָא, לָא חַדֵּית רַחֲמָנָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said to Rava: Perhaps when the Merciful One introduced the novelty** of the *halakha* of a defamer, it was applied only to a case **where her body has not changed** and she is still a young woman. **However,** in a case **where her body has changed** and she has become a grown woman, **the Merciful One did not introduce the novelty** of this *halakha*, and she is liable to strangulation just as she would be liable to strangulation if she had committed the sin as a grown woman.

The *gemara* challenges this: why should *motzi shem ra* be treated as an exception to the current-status rule? The argument seems to use clear evidence against Shila's position as proof of an exception, rather than as a refutation. One who starts with Shila's assumption and then encounters the *nesu'ah* case could simply conclude that *motzi shem ra* is categorically different — it always reverts to the time of the original act — while other adultery cases continue to follow current status. But is this a principled distinction or merely a defensive maneuver?

### Body Change Criterion

The *gemara* pushes back through Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua. If Shila's rule is that when the *body changes* the punishment changes, then the *nesu'ah* case cannot establish *motzi shem ra* as an exception to that rule. Why? Because in the *nesu'ah* case the body has *not* changed — she is essentially the same person. Her status has shifted but her physical person has not. *Ki chidesh rachmana heicha de-lav ishtani gufa* — the Torah's novelty in that case operates where there is no significant change in the person. Therefore, one cannot infer from the *nesu'ah* case that *motzi shem ra* would similarly override the rule even when the body *has* changed, i.e., when she has become a *bogeret*. If Shila's premise is that a genuine change in the person demands current-status judgment, then the *nesu'ah* case does not dislodge that premise, and we are left with a genuine contradiction between Shila and the *braisa* that gives *sekilah* even when she is a *bogeret*.

## Tannaitic Status Debate

### Priest Korban Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: אִישְׁתַּנִּי וְלָא אִישְׁתַּנִּי, תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דִּתְנַן: חָטְאוּ עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִתְמַנּוּ, וְנִתְמַנּוּ, הֲרֵי הֵן כְּהֶדְיוֹטוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** This question of whether the method of her execution **changes or does not change** if her body has changed **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as we learned** in a mishna (*Horayot* 10a), with regard to the special offering of a High Priest or a king who sinned unwittingly (see Leviticus 4:3–12, 22–26): If **they sinned before they were appointed, and they were** subsequently **appointed,** and they became aware of their transgression only after their appointment, **they are like commoners.** They must bring a female sheep or goat, like any ordinary individual who sinned, rather than the bull brought by a High Priest who has sinned or the male goat brought by a king who has sinned.

The *gemara* now invokes the solution of *machloket tanna'im*, supported by a *mishnah* that connects directly to this week's *parshah*. The *mishnah* in *Kodshim* discusses cases involving the *chatas* offering: a *nasi* brings a male goat and a *kohen gadol* brings a bull, whereas an ordinary *Yisrael* brings a female sheep or goat. The question is: if a person sinned before receiving a new appointment — as *nasi* or *kohen gadol* — and then was appointed before the obligation was discharged, by which standard is the obligation measured?

The *mishnah* states: *chatu ad she-lo nitmanu ve-nitmanu harei hen ke-hedyotot* — if they sinned before their appointment and then were appointed, they bring the *korban* of a lay person, since the obligation was incurred at the time of the sin.

### Rabbi Shimon vs. Tanna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אִם נוֹדַע לָהֶם עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִתְמַנּוּ — חַיָּיבִים, מִשֶּׁנִּתְמַנּוּ — פְּטוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says: If** their sin **became known to them before they were appointed,** even if they did not bring their offering before they were appointed, **they are liable** to bring the offering of a commoner. However, if their sin became known to them **after they were appointed, they are** entirely **exempt** from bringing an offering as their change in status necessitates a corresponding change in their offering, and therefore their first obligation is entirely nullified. This shows that according to Rabbi Shimon, a change in status retroactively affects one's liability for a transgression he committed in his previous status. A similar *halakha* should apply in the case of a betrothed young woman who committed adultery and reached majority before her sin became known.

Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *noda lahem ad she-lo nitmanu chayavim* — if they became aware of their sin before the appointment, they are obligated as lay persons. *Aval mishe-nitmanu pturim* — but if the awareness came only after they were appointed, they are exempt. Rabbi Shimon holds that there are two critical moments: the time of the sin and the time the obligation crystallizes (i.e., when the person becomes aware of the transgression). If those two moments straddle a change in status, the obligations do not align and the result is exemption. This parallels the debate about whether punishment follows the time of the sin or the time of execution: the *tanna kamma* holds that the time of sin is determinative throughout; Rabbi Shimon introduces the moment of crystallization as a second pivot.

### Gemara's Difficulty

The *gemara* raises a difficulty. If we accept Rabbi Shimon's logic — that both the time of the sin and the time of the obligation are relevant, and a discrepancy between them produces exemption — then one would expect the same logic to apply to capital cases. If a woman deserves *sekilah* at the time of the sin but *chenek* at the time of execution, why should the result be *chenek* rather than nothing? Apples and oranges cannot be combined; by *korban* the result is exemption, so perhaps by capital punishment it should also be exemption. The *gemara* presses: *im kein leitei korban ki de-hashtah* — by the same logic applied to *korban*, the result should be bring the *korban* appropriate to the current status, just as you propose giving *chenek* based on current status. But Rabbi Shimon says bring nothing at all. Why, then, in the capital case would you give the lesser punishment rather than no punishment?

It is worth noting that *Tosafot* points out an available answer that the *gemara* does not explicitly take up: unlike different types of *korbanot*, *sekilah* and *chenek* exist on a linear hierarchy, with *chenek* being the lesser. Indeed, elsewhere the *gemara* discusses a case where death-row inmates deserving different modes of execution are confused, and the ruling is to give them all *chenek* — demonstrating that *chenek* can legitimately discharge even a *sekilah* obligation. The common denominator of *sekilah* and *chenek* is *chenek*, and giving *chenek* to one who deserves *sekilah* is a legitimate mode of execution. *Tosafot* observes that this reasoning would resolve the *gemara*'s objection, but the *gemara* itself does not pursue it.

### Execution Parallel

Rather than resolving the *machloket tanna'im* approach, the *gemara* concludes that it cannot satisfactorily be sustained. The attempt to equate the capital-punishment question to the *korban* question — saying that if you follow current status you should impose the current punishment, and by parallel to *korban* where a discrepancy yields nothing, you should yield nothing here — leaves the *gemara* without a clean resolution.

### Rabbi Yochanan's Rewrite

The *gemara* reports that Rabbi Yochanan, rather than continue developing these lines of reasoning, simply instructs the *tanna sefaina* — the reciter of *baraisos* — to amend the text: *tani tidon be-sekilah*. The *braisa* that Shila attributed to himself, which ruled *chenek*, should be rewritten to say *sekilah*. The ruling that punishment follows current status, giving the woman *chenek* because she is now a *bogeret*, is simply wrong and the text is to be corrected. Punishment follows the time of the sin, and she receives *sekilah*.

## Final Resolution

### Reconsideration

Even after the *braisa* is rewritten, the *gemara* raises one further question. The Torah specifies *na'arah ha-me'orasah* — perhaps this means the laws apply only when the woman is discovered *as* a *na'arah me'orasah*, i.e., she must still be in that status at the time of the court proceedings? On this reading, *sheila*'s original position might not have been wrong on its merits; rather, the Torah's language itself might confine the category to women who are currently *na'arah me'orasah* when brought before the court.

### Rabbi Ila's Interpretation

Rabbi Ila addresses this from the verse itself: *amar kra ha-na'arah* — the definite article in *ha-na'arah* signals *zu ha-na'arah she-asata kvar* — this refers to the one who *was* the *na'arah* at the time of the sin. She is called *ha-na'arah* not because she is a *na'arah* now but because she was the *na'arah* in question. This resolves any reading of the verse as demanding current status and confirms that the punishment tracks the time of the sin.

### Rav Yitzchak Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: זוּ מַעֲשֶׂיהָ גָּרְמוּ לָהּ וְזֶה כִּמְצַחְצֵחַ שְׂפָתָיו גָּרְמוּ לוֹ. לָהּ — מַעֲשֶׂיהָ, כְּשֶׁהִיא זָנְסָה נַעֲרָה זָנְסָה. לוֹ — כִּמְצַחְצֵחַ שְׂפָתָיו. וֵאימַת חִיַּיב — בְּהַהִיא שָׁעָתָא. וְהַהִיא שָׁעָתָא בָּגְרָה הֲוַאי!
>
> **Translation:** However, the final resolution turns on a different axis entirely, introduced by Rav Yitzchak bar Avin — some say Rav Yitzchak bar Abba. When Rabbi Chanina had pressed Rabbi Ila with the implication that if punishment follows the time of the sin, then the *motzi shem ra* case should likewise follow the time of the alleged sin — meaning even if the husband libels her when she is a *bogeret*, he should incur the full obligations — Rabbi Ila responded with incredulity (*rachmana litzlan mei-hai da'ata*), and Rabbi Chanina returned the sentiment in kind (*rachmana litzlan mi-da'ata didach*). The exchange is pointed; the *gemara* then resolves it cleanly. **Ve-ta'ama mai** — why is the *motzi shem ra* case genuinely different? Rav Yitzchak bar Avin teaches: **zu ma'aseha garmu lah ve-zeh kimatz sefasav garmu lo** — for her, it was her own actions that brought the punishment upon her, **keshe-hi zanesa na'arah zanesa** — when she committed adultery she committed it as a *na'arah*, and it is that act which is the basis of judgment. For him, however, it is the movement of his lips — his libel — that brings the punishment upon him. **Ve-eimas kamichayev** — when does he become obligated? **Ha-hi sha'ta** — at the moment he commits the *motzi shem ra*. **Ve-ha-hi sha'ta bogeret haviah** — and at that moment she is a *bogeret*. Therefore his act of slander simply does not fall within the category of *motzi shem ra*, which requires that the slander be directed against a *na'arah me'orasah*. The two cases are thus entirely distinct in principle: her act and his act are different acts occurring at different times, and each is judged by the time at which the relevant act was committed.

However, the final resolution turns on a different axis entirely, introduced by Rav Yitzchak bar Avin — some say Rav Yitzchak bar Abba. When Rabbi Chanina had pressed Rabbi Ila with the implication that if punishment follows the time of the sin, then the *motzi shem ra* case should likewise follow the time of the alleged sin — meaning even if the husband libels her when she is a *bogeret*, he should incur the full obligations — Rabbi Ila responded with incredulity (*rachmana litzlan mei-hai da'ata*), and Rabbi Chanina returned the sentiment in kind (*rachmana litzlan mi-da'ata didach*). The exchange is pointed; the *gemara* then resolves it cleanly.

*Ve-ta'ama mai* — why is the *motzi shem ra* case genuinely different? Rav Yitzchak bar Avin teaches: *zu ma'aseha garmu lah ve-zeh kimatz sefasav garmu lo* — for her, it was her own actions that brought the punishment upon her, *keshe-hi zanesa na'arah zanesa* — when she committed adultery she committed it as a *na'arah*, and it is that act which is the basis of judgment. For him, however, it is the movement of his lips — his libel — that brings the punishment upon him. *Ve-eimas kamichayev* — when does he become obligated? *Ha-hi sha'ta* — at the moment he commits the *motzi shem ra*. *Ve-ha-hi sha'ta bogeret haviah* — and at that moment she is a *bogeret*. Therefore his act of slander simply does not fall within the category of *motzi shem ra*, which requires that the slander be directed against a *na'arah me'orasah*. The two cases are thus entirely distinct in principle: her act and his act are different acts occurring at different times, and each is judged by the time at which the relevant act was committed.

## Stoning Locations

### Father's Doorway

The *gemara* turns to the practical rules governing where *sekilah* takes place in these cases. The standard ruling: *soklin osah al pesach beis aviha* — she is stoned at the doorway of her father's house. This applies in the paradigmatic case where the matter is discovered after *nisuin* and the adultery occurred during *erusin*.

### City Gate Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יֵשׁ לָהּ אָב וְאֵין לָהּ פֶּתַח בֵּית הָאָב, יֵשׁ לָהּ פֶּתַח בֵּית הָאָב וְאֵין לָהּ אָב — הֲרֵי זוֹ בִּסְקִילָה. לֹא נֶאֱמַר פֶּתַח בֵּית אָב אֶלָּא לְמִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** If a young woman who is betrothed commits adultery and **she has a father but does not have an entrance to** her **father's house,** i.e., if her father does not possess a house of his own, or if **she has an entrance to** her **father's house but does not have a father,** as he has passed away, **she is** nevertheless executed **via stoning, as** the requirement that she is to be executed at the **entrance to her father's house is stated only for a mitzva** but it is not an indispensable requirement.

If the father has no home — *ein lah pesach beis av* — then: *soklin osah al pesach sha'ar ha-ir ha-hi* — she is stoned at the gate of the city. This mirrors what the Torah prescribes in the general *na'arah me'orasah* case, and it also applies, as established earlier, when the discovery occurs before *nisuin*.

### Beis Din Alternative

If the city is majority non-Jewish — *ube-ir she-rubah ovdei kochavim* — the city gate carries no appropriate Jewish communal symbolism, and so: *soklin osah al pesach beis din* — she is stoned at the entrance of the *beis din*. The court itself then serves as the institutional expression of communal judgment. The same rule applies to the one who worships *avodah zarah*: *soklin oto be-sha'ar she-avad bo* — he is stoned at the gate of the city where the worship occurred, so that the consequence is visible to the community most affected by the idolatry. And if that city is predominantly non-Jewish: *soklin oto al pesach beis din*.

### Avodah Zarah Parallel

The *gemara* notes this parallel explicitly: *kayotze ba-davar atah omer* — and similarly you may say regarding *avodah zarah*. The motivation for stoning at the specific location of the idolatry is the concern about its pernicious communal effects; the statement must be made to the people in the place where the act occurred. This provides the structural parallel to the *na'arah me'orasah* case, and the two sets of laws illuminate each other.

### Textual Derivation

The *gemara* asks *mina hani mili* — from where are these rules derived? The *baraisa* cites *she'arecha* — "your gates" — appearing twice in the passage about *avodah zarah*: once describing where the sin was committed and once prescribing where the stoning takes place. *Ne'emar she'arecha le-malah ve-ne'emar she'arecha le-matah* — the word *she'arecha* appears above and below. *Mah she'arecha ha-amor le-malah sha'ar she-avad bo* — just as the upper occurrence refers to the gate of the city where the worship took place, *af she'arecha ha-amor le-matah sha'ar she-avad bo* — so too the lower occurrence, prescribing execution, refers to the same gate. Furthermore, *she'arecha* — "your gates" — excludes *sha'arei ovdei kochavim*, the gates of non-Jewish cities, which establishes the *beis din* alternative. The *gemara* notes that if *she'arecha* were already used solely to identify which city's gate is meant, one could simply have written *shaar* without the possessive; the full form *she'arecha* therefore teaches both things simultaneously. *Shema mina tartei* — derive two laws from it.

For the *na'arah me'orasah* case, the Torah does not use *she'arecha* but rather *petach beis aviha*. The derivation connecting the two cases is attributed to Rabbi Abbahu: *gamar petach mi-petach u-fetach mi-sha'ar ve-sha'ar mi-she'arecha* — a *gezerah shavah* or *hekesh* linking *petach* as it appears in the *na'arah* passage to *petach* as used in the context of the *mishkan*, and from there to *sha'ar*, and from *sha'ar* to *she'arecha* in the *avodah zarah* passage. Rashi characterizes this derivation as *asmachta* — a textual support rather than a strict *derasha*. The practical upshot is clear: the standard case is stoning at *petach beis aviha*; if there is no *petach beis av* or if the discovery preceded *nisuin*, it is at the *sha'ar* of the city; if the city is non-Jewish, it is at the entrance of *beis din*.
