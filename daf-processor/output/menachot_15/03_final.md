# Menachot 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Piggul & Rabbinic

### Chatzi Matir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בִּשְׁעַת שְׁחִיטָה חִישֵּׁב לֶאֱכוֹל חֲצִי זַיִת, וּבִשְׁעַת זְרִיקָה חִישֵּׁב לֶאֱכוֹל חֲצִי זַיִת – פִּיגּוּל, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁשְּׁחִיטָה וּזְרִיקָה מִצְטָרְפִין.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If **at the time of** the **slaughter** of an offering one **had intent to consume half an olive-bulk** of its meat the next day, **and at the time of** the **sprinkling** of the blood **he had intent to consume half** of another **olive-bulk** of meat the next day, the offering is ***piggul*, as** intentions that occur during the **slaughter and sprinkling combine** to render an offering *piggul*.

The daf opens four lines before the *mishnah* on 14b, completing the previous *sugya*. The discussion had been focused on the principle of *chatzi matir* — the case where one plans, during the *shechitah* of one of the two lambs of *Shavuos*, to have a *piggul* thought involving only a *kezayis* of that one lamb in the wrong location, but not extending the thought to the other lamb. The *gemara* now turns not to *chatzi matir* as such, but to what it calls *chatzi achila* or *chatzi zayis*: a case in which, during one complete *avodah* — the *shechitah* — one thinks about half a *kezayis*, and during another complete *avodah* — the *zrikah* — one thinks about the other half *kezayis*. A *braisa* had established that this mechanism does work; the question was whether it extends to intermediate *avodos*. A second *braisa* suggested it does not, and the *gemara* attempted to identify whether this constitutes a *tannaim* dispute.

The *gemara* had cited *Rebbe* to the effect that if one performs a *chatzi kezayis* thought by one lamb and a *chatzi kezayis* thought by another lamb, they do not combine — but that case is dismissed because each lamb represents only half of the work, making it also a *chatzi matir*. The *gemara* then notes that if *Rebbe* truly held that a *chatzi zayis* from two separate *avodos* combine to create *piggul*, he should have issued a *gzeira* in that case as well. This observation lays the groundwork for an important principle: in many *mishnayos* where the ruling is not *piggul* but *passul*, that *passul* is in fact *passul d'rabbanan*, a rabbinic prohibition arising from proximity to genuine *piggul*.

### Passul D'Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמִי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּטַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן מִשּׁוּם הָכִי הוּא, דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: מוֹדִים חֲכָמִים לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּמִנְחַת חוֹטֵא וּמִנְחַת קְנָאוֹת שֶׁאִם פִּיגֵּל בַּקּוֹמֶץ שֶׁפִּיגּוּל, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ כָּרֵת, שֶׁהַקּוֹמֶץ הוּא הַמַּתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **So too, it is reasonable that the reasoning of the Rabbis is due to that** explanation, i.e., they disqualified the offerings in the aforementioned cases due to the fact that in similar instances the offering is *piggul*. This is evident from the fact **that** it **is taught** in **the latter clause** of the mishna (16a): **The Rabbis concede to Rabbi Meir in** the case of **the meal offering of a sinner and the meal offering of jealousy that if one had intent of *piggul* during** the burning of **the handful,** the meal offering is ***piggul* and one is liable** to receive ***karet* for** its consumption, **as the handful is the** sole **permitting factor.**

The *gemara* states, five lines above the word *matnisin* on 14b: *hachi nami mistavra* — it makes sense — *d'taama d'rabbanan mishum hachi hu*, that the reason the Rabbis in certain cases declare something *passul* rather than fully *mutar* is precisely this concern about where leniency might lead — a form of *gzeira*.

### Minchat Chotei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בִּשְׁעַת שְׁחִיטָה חִישֵּׁב לֶאֱכוֹל חֲצִי זַיִת, וּבִשְׁעַת זְרִיקָה חִישֵּׁב לֶאֱכוֹל חֲצִי זַיִת – פִּיגּוּל, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁשְּׁחִיטָה וּזְרִיקָה מִצְטָרְפִין. וּמוֹדִים חֲכָמִים לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּמִנְחַת חוֹטֵא וּמִנְחַת קְנָאוֹת שֶׁאִם פִּיגֵּל בַּקּוֹמֶץ שֶׁפִּיגּוּל, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ כָּרֵת, שֶׁהַקּוֹמֶץ הוּא הַמַּתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If **at the time of** the **slaughter** of an offering one **had intent to consume half an olive-bulk** of its meat the next day, **and at the time of** the **sprinkling** of the blood **he had intent to consume half** of another **olive-bulk** of meat the next day, the offering is ***piggul*, as** intentions that occur during the **slaughter and sprinkling combine** to render an offering *piggul*. And the Rabbis concede to *Rebbe Meir* regarding the *minchas chotei* and the *minchas kenaos*, the *mincha* of a sinner and the *mincha* of the *sotah*, neither of which contains *levonah*. In these cases, **the Rabbis concede to Rabbi Meir in** the case of **the meal offering of a sinner and the meal offering of jealousy that if one had intent of *piggul* during** the burning of **the handful,** the meal offering is ***piggul* and one is liable** to receive ***karet* for** its consumption, **as the handful is the** sole **permitting factor.**

The end of the *braisa* addressing *chatzi matir* reads: *modim chachamim l'Rebbe Meir b'minchas chotei u'minchas kenaos* — the Rabbis concede to *Rebbe Meir* regarding the *minchas chotei* and the *minchas kenaos*, the *mincha* of a sinner and the *mincha* of the *sotah*, neither of which contains *levonah*. In these cases, the *gemara* says, if one had a *piggul* thought during the *kometz*, *piggul* is achieved and one is *chayav kares*, because there the *kometz* alone is the *matir* — *she'hakometz hu hamatir*. One might ask why this needs to be stated at all, since it is obvious that where there is only one *avodah*, a *piggul* thought during that *avodah* completes the violation. The *gemara* answers: *lav hacha ka'mashma lan*. Rather, it is teaching us why the Rabbis say *passul* even when there is a *levonah* and the thought is expressed only at the *kmitzoh* — the *chatzi matir* scenario. Why not leave such a case entirely *mutar*? Because there exists an analogous scenario in which the *kometz* alone genuinely creates *piggul* — namely, the *minchas chotei*. Since that parallel case exists, the Rabbis impose a *passul* even in the incomplete case, lest the slippery slope lead to treating the genuine *piggul* case with insufficient stringency. The *gemara* accepts this proof.

The takeaway of this closing section of the prior *sugya* is thus the consistent refrain throughout many *mishnayos* — "not *piggul* but *passul*" — reflects a rabbinic prohibition grounded in proximity to genuine *piggul*, because there exists an analogous case where the act truly is *piggul*.

## Tumah & Krban

### Nitoshah Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נִטְמֵאת אַחַת מִן הַחַלּוֹת, אוֹ אֶחָד מִן הַסְּדָרִים – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵיהֶם יֵצְאוּ לְבֵית הַשְּׂרֵיפָה, שֶׁאֵין קׇרְבַּן צִיבּוּר חָלוּק. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: הַטָּמֵא בְּטוּמְאָתוֹ וְהַטָּהוֹר יֵאָכֵל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one of the** two **loaves** of *Shavuot* **or one of the** two **arrangements** of the shewbread **became ritually impure, Rabbi Yehuda says: Both must be taken to the place of burning** like any other disqualified offering, **as no communal offering is divided.** That is, it is either fit in its entirety or unfit in its entirety. **And the Rabbis say: The impure one** remains **in its** state of **impurity and the pure one shall be eaten.**

The *mishnah* concerns *tumah*, not *piggul*, and its setting is the *menachos* associated with a *korban* — offerings that are of particular interest in this tractate because their *matir* lies in another element, such as the *bazichin* or the lambs. The *mishnah* thus provides a natural complement to the prior *piggul* discussions: in those discussions, the *gemara* explored how a thought affecting one part of a *korban* can spread to the whole; here it explores how *tumah* affecting one part operates.

### Yehuda Debate

*Rabbi Yehuda* rules: *shneihem yeitziu l'veis hasreifah* — both go out to be burned. His reasoning: *she'ein korban tzibbur chaluk* — one does not divide a *korban tzibbur*. It is conceived as a unified whole, and if one part becomes *tamei*, the entire *korban* is treated as if it were *tamei*. *Chachamim* disagree: *hatamei b'tumaso v'hatahor yeiacheil* — the *tamei* portion remains *tamei* and the *tahor* portion is eaten.

This is a notable contrast to the prior *mishnah*, where *Rabbi Yosi* held that a *piggul* thought affecting one loaf does not affect the remaining loaves — an unusual position, since the dominant principle is that *piggul* applied to part of a *korban* spreads to the whole. In that *piggul* context, the *chachamim* hold the more expansive view. By *tumah*, the positions are different: *tumah* ordinarily affects only what it touches, and the *tahor* portions remain *tahor* — except according to *Rabbi Yehuda*, who applies the *ein korban tzibbur chaluk* principle here as well.

### Lifnei Zrikah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מַחְלוֹקֶת לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה, אֲבָל לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל הַטָּמֵא בְּטוּמְאָתוֹ וְהַטָּהוֹר יֵאָכֵל.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Elazar says:** The **dispute** between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis applies only to a case where one loaf became ritually impure **before** the **sprinkling** of the blood of the lambs, as this is the act that renders the loaves permitted for consumption. Accordingly, they disagree whether the sprinkling is effective in permitting the remaining pure loaf for consumption. **But** in a case where one loaf was rendered impure **after the sprinkling,** meaning that both loaves were initially permitted for consumption, **everyone agrees** that **the impure one** remains **in its** state of **impurity and the pure one shall be eaten.**

*Rabbi Elazar* establishes: *machlokes lifnei zrikah* — the dispute between *Rabbi Yehuda* and *chachamim* applies only when the *tumah* occurred before *zrikas hadam* in the case of the lambs and bread, or before the burning of the *levonah* in the case of the *lechem hapanim*. But *l'achar zrikah* — after the *zrikah* — everyone agrees: *hatamei b'tumaso v'hatahor yeiacheil*. The question then becomes: what specifically is at issue before the *zrikah* that changes matters?

### Tzitz & Eating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה, בְּמַאי פְּלִיגִי? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בְּצִיץ מְרַצֶּה עַל אֲכִילוֹת קָא מִיפַּלְגִי,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** in the case where the loaves become impure **before** the **sprinkling** of the blood, **with regard to what** principle **do they disagree? Rav Pappa said: They disagree with regard to** the **frontplate** of the High Priest, i.e., whether it **effects acceptance** only for the impurity of items sacrificed on the altar, or even **for** the impurity of items that would normally be **consumed** by the priests.

*Rav Pappa* answers: *b'tzitz meratzeh al ha'achilos kamiflagi* — the debate turns on whether the *tzitz* is effective with respect to the eating dimension of *tumah*. This requires some background. The *tzitz*, the golden headplate worn by the *Kohen Gadol*, operates on the principle of *meratzeh al avon hakodashim asher yakdishu Bnei Yisrael* — it papers over problems of *tumah* regarding the *korbanos* themselves. The verse specifies *avon hakodashim*, not *avon hamakdishim* — it concerns the *korbanos* being *tamei*, not the people being *tamei*. A person who is *tamei* and eats a *korban* remains *chayav kares* regardless; the *tzitz* does not extend to permit eating.

### Tzitz Achilos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: הַצִּיץ מְרַצֶּה עַל אֲכִילוֹת, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: אֵין הַצִּיץ מְרַצֶּה עַל אֲכִילוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Rabbis hold** that **the frontplate effects acceptance for** items that are normally **consumed** by the priests but have become ritually impure. Consequently, the sprinkling of the blood in this case is an entirely valid act that is capable of rendering the remaining pure loaf permitted for consumption. **And Rabbi Yehuda holds** that **the frontplate does not effect acceptance for** items that are **consumed** by the priests and have become impure. Accordingly, the sprinkling of the blood is ineffective in rendering the remaining pure loaf permitted for consumption.

The *gemara* now applies this to the case before us. If part of this *mincha* is *tamei*, can the *shechitah* of the lambs or the offering of the *bazichin* proceed? In an animal *korban*, missing meat does not prevent the *zrikah*; but if a *mincha* is *chaser* — deficient — one cannot bring the *ketores* or burn the *bazichin*. The concern here is that the *tamei* portion renders the *mincha* deficient, blocking the next stage. The *tzitz* does not permit eating from the *tamei* portion, but the question is whether it sufficiently papers over the problem to allow the *hakravah* to proceed — making the *korban* effective even if eating is still prohibited.

*Rabbanan* hold that the *tzitz meratzeh al ha'achilos*: even though what is being placed on the *mizbeach* is not the *tamei* object itself — it is the *kvashim* or the *bazichin* — the *tzitz* brackets the *tumah* problem enough that the *hakravah* is valid. *Rabbi Yehuda* holds *ein ha'tzitz meratzeh al ha'achilos*: the *tzitz* does not help here, and therefore it is as if the *mincha* is *chaser*, disabling the *hakravah*. On *Rabbi Yehuda*'s view, the reason everything must be burned is not that the *tumah* spreads conceptually to the whole *korban*, but rather that the *tumah* creates a deficiency that prevents the *zrikah* from being performed at all — and since the *zrikah* never occurred, the entire *korban* is lost.

### Tzitz Philosophy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נָתָן לְרַב פָּפָּא: וְהָא עוֹלִין, דְּהַצִּיץ מְרַצֶּה עַל הָעוֹלִין, וּפְלִיגִי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Natan, said to Rav Pappa:** Can this be the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis? **But** what about items that normally **ascend** upon the altar? Even Rabbi Yehuda concedes **that the frontplate effects acceptance for** impure items **that** normally **ascend** the altar, **and** Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis nevertheless **disagree** with regard to the remaining item in a case of this kind.

A student raised the question: the *tzitz* is worn only by the *Kohen Gadol*, and there is a question whether it is effective only when he is wearing it or whether the very availability of the *tzitz* suffices. More fundamentally, what is the religious meaning of the *tzitz*'s atoning power?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: נִטְמָא אֶחָד מִן הַבָּזִיכִין, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵיהֶם יֵעָשׂוּ בְּטוּמְאָה, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין קׇרְבַּן צִיבּוּר חָלוּק, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: הַטָּמֵא בְּטוּמְאָתוֹ וְהַטָּהוֹר בְּטׇהֳרָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara provides the source for this claim. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If **one of the bowls** of frankincense accompanying the shewbread, which are meant to be burned upon the altar, **became impure, Rabbi Yehuda says** that the rites of **both of them may be performed in impurity,** i.e., the priest may even render the second bowl impure and burn both of them together, **as no communal offering is divided,** and the mitzva to sacrifice communal offerings overrides the prohibition against rendering them impure. **And the Rabbis say: The impure one** remains **in its** state of **impurity and the pure one** remains **in its** state of **purity.** Evidently, their dispute does not depend on whether the frontplate effects acceptance.

The *gemara* connects this to the phrase *Kodesh LaHashem* engraved on the *tzitz*, and to the function of the *Kohen Gadol* on *Yom Kippur* — whose entire *avodah* is to be *metaher* the *Beis HaMikdash* from *tumos Bnei Yisrael*. The *tzitz* serves as a focus on the Divine, allowing God's presence to remain in the *Mikdash* by somehow absorbing the *tumah*. The deeper point, as articulated in the discussion, is the verse *hashochein itam besoch tumosam* — God declares His intention to dwell among Israel even within their *tumah*. Other conditions of disqualification, such as *arla*, are not *hutrah b'tzibbur*; only *tumah* is overridden in the public sphere. This is because the entire existence of the *Mikdash* is a divine acknowledgment that God's desire to dwell *besoch Bnei Yisrael* will override *tumah* problems — an acknowledgment of human frailty and a divine declaration of presence nonetheless.

As one student aptly noted, the *tzitz* does not cancel *tumah*, it does not achieve *tahara*, but it is *meratzeh* — it lets things proceed. This distinction parallels what Rav Soloveitchik describes as the difference between *kapara* and *tahara*: *kapara* forestalls consequences without fully resolving the underlying condition. The *tzitz* is precisely *meratzeh* in this sense — not atonement in the sense of full repair, but a going-forward in the face of acknowledged imperfection.

### Tumah Hutra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: תָּא שְׁמַע, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ שֵׁבֶט אֶחָד טָמֵא וְכׇל הַשְּׁבָטִים טְהוֹרִין – יַעֲשׂוּ בְּטוּמְאָה, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין קׇרְבַּן צִיבּוּר חָלוּק, וְהָכָא מַאי הַצִּיץ מְרַצֶּה אִיכָּא?
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, Rav Ashi said: Come** and **hear** an additional proof that the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis does not concern the frontplate, as we learn in a mishna (*Pesaḥim* 80a) with regard to the consumption of the Paschal offering in a state of impurity, that **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even** if **one tribe is ritually impure and all** the rest of **the tribes are pure,** all the tribes **may perform** the rite of the Paschal offering **in** a state of **impurity, as no communal offerings are divided.** Rav Ashi explains: **But here, what** relevance **is there** to the question of whether **the frontplate effects acceptance?** The frontplate effects acceptance for offerings that have become impure; but it does not render it permitted for one who is ritually impure to sacrifice an offering.

*Rav Huna the son of Rav Nachman* challenged *Rav Pappa*: if the debate is really about the *tzitz*, then even in the case of *olin* — objects actually placed on the *mizbeach* — where everyone concedes the *tzitz* is effective, we should find agreement. Yet a *braisa* shows the same dispute applies even there: *nitma echad min habazichin* — one of the *bazichin* becomes *tamei*. *Rabbi Yehuda* says: *shneihem ya'asu b'tumah*, you might as well perform both in a state of *tumah*. Since it is a *korban tzibbur* and *tumah hutrah b'tzibbur*, you may proceed even with the *tamei bazich*. But *Rabbi Yehuda* goes further: since we already conceive of the entire *korban tzibbur* as if it is *tamei* once any part of it is *tamei*, there is no fault in making the other *bazich tamei* as well. *Chachamim* say: *hatamei b'tumato v'hatahor b'taharato* — the *tamei* is *tamei* and the *tahor* remains *tahor*; you do not spread *tumah* gratuitously.

This makes clear that *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position has nothing to do with the effectiveness of the *tzitz* — since he is actually permitting the intentional extension of *tumah*, the *tzitz* cannot be the operative mechanism. His position is entirely about how a *korban tzibbur* is conceptually conceived as an indivisible unit: when any part becomes *tamei*, the whole is treated as *tamei*, and consequences flow from that across the board.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, הָאָמַר רָבִינָא: תָּא שְׁמַע, נִטְמֵאת אַחַת מִן הַחַלּוֹת אוֹ אֶחָד מִן הַסְּדָרִין, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵיהֶם יֵצְאוּ לְבֵית הַשְּׂרֵיפָה, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין קׇרְבַּן צִיבּוּר חָלוּק, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: הַטָּמֵא בְּטוּמְאָתוֹ וְהַטָּהוֹר יֵאָכֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, doesn't Ravina say: Come** and **hear** a proof that the matter of the frontplate cannot be the subject of the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis, as the mishna teaches: If **one of the** two **loaves** brought on *Shavuot* **or one of the** two **arrangements** of shewbread **became ritually impure, Rabbi Yehuda says: Both must be taken to the place of burning, as no communal offering is divided. And the Rabbis say: The impure one** remains **in its impurity and the pure one may be eaten.**

*Rav Ashi* adduces a further proof. A *braisa* states: if an entire *shevet* becomes *tamei* while the rest of Israel is *tahor*, *Rabbi Yehuda* says they all bring the *korban Pesach b'tumah*. The reasoning: a *shevet* constitutes its own *kahal*, its own mini-*tzibbur*. Once a defined *tzibbur* is *tamei*, *tumah hutrah b'tzibbur* applies — and since *Rabbi Yehuda* holds that *tumah* affecting any part of the communal body is treated as affecting the whole, everyone may bring their *korban Pesach b'tumah*. Here the *tzitz* is entirely irrelevant — it operates only on the *korban* itself, not on the *tumah* of people — and yet the same principle of *ein korban tzibbur chaluk* produces the same outcome.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אִיתָא, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין הַצִּיץ מְרַצֶּה עַל אֲכִילוֹת מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: תַּלְמוּד עָרוּךְ הוּא בְּפִיו שֶׁל רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, שֶׁאֵין קׇרְבַּן צִיבּוּר חָלוּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the difficulty: **And if it is so,** i.e., that the dispute between them concerns the frontplate, then Rabbi Yehuda **should have** said: They are both burned, **because the frontplate does not effect acceptance for** impure items that are **consumed** by the priests. **Rather, Rabbi Yochanan says: It is a settled tradition in the mouth of Rabbi Yehuda that no communal offering is divided,** and if one part of an offering becomes impure, the entire offering is disqualified.

*Ravina* then cites the *mishnah* itself: *nitma achas min hachalos o achas min hasedarim* — if one of the loaves or one of the columns becomes *tamei* — *Rabbi Yehuda omer shneihem yetzu l'veis hasreifa*, and *chachamim* say the *tahor* portion is eaten. The point *Ravina* raises: if *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position truly rests on the *tzitz* not being *meratzeh* for *achilos*, one would expect the *mishnah* to state the reason that way. Instead, *Rabbi Yehuda* gives his standard reason: *she'ein korban tzibbur chaluk*. From this *Ravina* concludes that the *tzitz* explanation is not *Rabbi Yehuda*'s reasoning at all.

### Ein Krban Chaluk

The *gemara* accepts this: *ela amar Rabbi Yochanan* — *Rabbi Yochanan* says — *limud aruch hu b'fiv shel Rabbi Yehuda* — it is a set, arranged teaching in the mouth of *Rabbi Yehuda* that *ein korban tzibbur chaluk*, that a *korban tzibbur* is not divided. *Rashi* notes that when *Rabbi Yochanan* uses the phrase *limud aruch*, he means that *Rabbi Yehuda* received this as a tradition from his teachers, since there is no compelling scriptural derivation or logical necessity that would independently generate this rule. It was transmitted to him as a *halakha*.

*Rabbi Yehuda*'s position thus rests entirely on this principle: wherever you are dealing with a *korban tzibbur*, if part becomes *tamei*, it is as if all of it is *tamei*. The practical consequence of this depends on context. Where *tumah* means you cannot eat — as with the remnant of *shtei halechem* or *lechem hapanim* — then if one part is *tamei*, do not eat any of it. Where *tumah* means you are permitted to bring the *korban* because *tumah hutrah b'tzibbur* — as when a *shevet* is *tamei* or when one of the *bazichin* becomes *tamei* — then the logic cuts the other way: once *tumah* is acknowledged in any part of the *korban tzibbur*, it applies to everything, and therefore one might as well bring all of it in *tumah*. The impact of the principle *ein korban tzibbur chaluk* thus depends entirely on what the *tumah* permits or forbids in the specific case.

## Toda & Bread

### Toda Mefagelet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַתּוֹדָה מְפַגֶּלֶת אֶת הַלֶּחֶם, וְהַלֶּחֶם אֵינוֹ מְפַגֵּל אֶת הַתּוֹדָה. כֵּיצַד? שָׁחַט אֶת הַתּוֹדָה לֶאֱכוֹל מִמֶּנָּה לְמָחָר – הִיא וְהַלֶּחֶם מְפוּגָּלִין, לֶאֱכוֹל מִן הַלֶּחֶם לְמָחָר – הַלֶּחֶם מְפוּגָּל וְהַתּוֹדָה אֵינָהּ מְפוּגֶּלֶת. הַכְּבָשִׂים מְפַגְּלִין אֶת הַלֶּחֶם, וְהַלֶּחֶם אֵינוֹ מְפַגֵּל אֶת הַכְּבָשִׂים. כֵּיצַד? הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַכְּבָשִׂים לֶאֱכוֹל מֵהֶן לְמָחָר – הֵם וְהַלֶּחֶם מְפוּגָּלִין, לֶאֱכוֹל אֶת הַלֶּחֶם לְמָחָר – הַלֶּחֶם מְפוּגָּל וְהַכְּבָשִׂים אֵינָן מְפוּגָּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The thanks offering renders the** accompanying **loaves *piggul* but the loaves do not render the thanks offering *piggul*. How so?** If **one slaughtered the thanks offering,** which may be consumed only during the day it is slaughtered and the night thereafter, with the intent **to partake of it the next day,** the offering **and the** accompanying **loaves are rendered *piggul*.** If he slaughtered it with the intent **to partake of the loaves the next day, the loaves are rendered *piggul* and the thanks offering is not *piggul*.** Likewise, **the lambs** sacrificed with the two loaves meal offering on *Shavuot* **render the** accompanying **loaves *piggul*, but the loaves do not render the lambs *piggul*. How so?** If **one slaughtered the lambs,** which may be consumed only during the day they are slaughtered and the night thereafter, with the intent **to partake of them the next day,** the lambs **and the** accompanying **loaves are rendered *piggul*.** If he did so with the intent **to partake of the loaves the next day, the loaves are rendered *piggul* and the lambs are not *piggul*.**

The *mishnah* states: *HaToda mefageles es halechem v'halechem eino mefagel es haToda* — the *toda* sacrifice renders its accompanying bread *piggul*, but the bread does not render the *toda piggul*. Similarly: *hakvashim mefaglin es halechem v'halechem eino mefagel es hakvashim* — the two lambs of *Shavuos* render the bread *piggul*, but the bread does not render the lambs *piggul*.

The *mishnah* then works through the cases. If one slaughters the *toda* intending to eat its meat the next day, *hi vehalechem mefugalim* — both the animal and the bread are *piggul*, because once the animal is *piggul* the principle applies to everything dependent on it. But if one intends to eat only the bread the next day, *halechem mefugal v'hatoda einah mefugeles* — the bread is *piggul* but the *toda* itself is not affected, because the thought was directed at the secondary, dependent element and cannot retroactively affect the primary *korban*.

The same structure governs the two lambs of *Shavuos*. If the lambs are made *piggul*, the bread of *shtei halechem* is also *piggul*; but if only the bread is targeted by the *piggul* thought, the lambs remain valid. The *mishnah* and *gemara* here explore the structure of dependency as expressed through *piggul*, making *Shavuos* — with its two lambs, each a *chatzi machshir*, and the *shtei halechem* dependent upon them — a particularly rich model for these discussions.

### Lachmei Toda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא, דְּאָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: מִנַּיִן לְלַחְמֵי תוֹדָה שֶׁנִּקְרְאוּ תּוֹדָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהִקְרִיב עַל זֶבַח הַתּוֹדָה חַלּוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that a thanks offering renders its accompanying loaves *piggul*? **If we say** that it is **due to that** which **Rav Kahana** says, this is problematic. **As Rav Kahana says: From where** is it derived that **the loaves of a thanks offering are** themselves **called a thanks offering?** It is derived from **that** which **is stated** in the verse: **"Then he shall offer with the sacrifice of thanks offering loaves"** (Leviticus 7:12). The juxtaposition of the words "thanks offering" and "loaves" indicates that the loaves are themselves called a thanks offering.

The *gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama* — what is the reason for these laws? Perhaps it follows from what *Rav Kahana* said: *minayin le'lachmei todah she'nikru todah* — how do we know that the breads accompanying the *toda* are themselves called by the name *todah*? He derives it from the verse *v'hikriv al zevach hatodah challos* — the word *todah* is juxtaposed with *challos*, implying that the bread shares the identity of the *toda*. If so, one might expect the relationship to run symmetrically — if the bread is called *toda*, then a thought directed at the bread should also affect the *toda*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי אִיפְּכָא נָמֵי, הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא: לֶחֶם אִיקְּרִי תּוֹדָה, תּוֹדָה לָא אִיקְּרִי לֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains why the *halakha* of the mishna cannot be derived from Rav Kahana's exposition. **If so,** then **the opposite** should be the *halakha* **as well,** that intent of *piggul* with regard to the loaves should likewise render the thanks offering *piggul*. The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **This** is **not difficult,** as the **loaves are called a thanks offering,** but **a thanks offering is not called loaves.**

The *gemara* answers: *lechem ikrei todah, todah lo ikrei lechem* — the bread is called *todah*, but *todah* is not called bread. If I am thinking about the *toda*, that encompasses everything named *toda*, including the bread. But if I am thinking about bread, bread means bread — it does not carry with it the animal *korban* upon which it depends.

### Glal Todah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא דְּקָתָנֵי: הַכְּבָשִׂים מְפַגְּלִין אֶת הַלֶּחֶם וְהַלֶּחֶם אֵינוֹ מְפַגֵּל אֶת הַכְּבָשִׂים, לֶחֶם הֵיכָא אַשְׁכְּחַן דְּאִיקְּרִי כְּבָשִׂים? אֶלָּא לָאו הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא: לֶחֶם גְּלַל תּוֹדָה, וְאֵין תּוֹדָה גְּלַל לֶחֶם, לֶחֶם גְּלַל כְּבָשִׂים, וְאֵין כְּבָשִׂים גְּלַל לֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** with regard to **that which** the mishna **teaches: The lambs** sacrificed with the two loaves meal offering on *Shavuot* **render the** accompanying **loaves *piggul* but the loaves do not render the lambs *piggul*, where do we find that** the two **loaves are called lambs? Rather, is it not** correct that **this is the reason** why the thanks offering renders the loaves *piggul* but not vice versa: The **bread** is brought **on account of [*gelal*]** the **thanks offering, but** the **thanks offering is not** brought **on account of** the **bread,** i.e., the thanks offering is the primary element of the sacrifice. Similarly, the two loaves of **bread are** brought **on account of** the **lambs, and** the **lambs are not** brought **on account of** the **bread.**

The *gemara* then raises the case of the lambs of *Shavuos*: *heicha ashchan de'ikrei kvasim lechem* — we have no verse calling the *shtei halechem* by the name *kvashim*, and yet the *mishnah* states that the lambs render the bread *piggul*. This shows that the ruling cannot be based on the biblical juxtaposition alone. The *gemara* therefore rejects *Rav Kahana*'s derivation as the operative reason and instead identifies the underlying logic: *lechem glal todah* — the bread comes by reason of, or in the wake of, the *toda*; *v'ein hatodah glal de'lechem* — the *toda* does not come because of the bread. Similarly, *lechem glal de'kvasim* — the *shtei halechem* is drawn along in the wake of the lambs — *v'ein kvasim glal de'lechem* — and the lambs are not drawn along by the bread.

The word *glal*, related to *galgal* — to roll — means something like "rolled along with" or "drawn in the wake of." It is not a question of what motivates one to bring the *korban* in the first place; one might well say that the focus of *Shavuos* is the *shtei halechem*, and the lambs are brought for the purpose of enabling that unique bread *korban*. But *glal* here describes operational dependency, not motivational priority: the bread's validity flows from and depends upon the lambs, while the lambs stand independently. The *toda* does not depend on its bread; it is the bread that depends on the *toda*.

### Tenufah Union

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכִי, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן תּוֹדָה – הָתָם הוּא דְּכִי מְפַגֵּל בְּלֶחֶם לָא מִפַּגְּלָא תּוֹדָה, מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא הוּזְקְקוּ זֶה לָזֶה בִּתְנוּפָה, אֲבָל כְּבָשִׂים דְּהוּזְקְקוּ זֶה לָזֶה בִּתְנוּפָה – אֵימָא כִּי מְפַגֵּל בְּלֶחֶם לִיפַּגְּלִי נָמֵי כְּבָשִׂים, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** both of these *halakhot* **are necessary, as, had** the mishna **taught us** the *halakha* only in the case of **a thanks offering,** then one might say: **It is** only **there,** with regard to a thanks offering, **that when one renders the loaves *piggul* the thanks offering is not rendered *piggul*, because they were not bound to one another by waving,** i.e., the mitzva of waving the thanks offering may be fulfilled without the bread. **But** with regard to the **lambs,** in **which** the two items **were bound to one another by waving,** as the two loaves are waved together with the lambs, one might **say** that **when he renders the bread *piggul*,** the **lambs should be rendered *piggul* as well.** Therefore, it was **necessary** for the mishna to teach this *halakha* also with regard to the case of the lambs.

The *gemara* continues: *u'tzrichi* — both cases, the *toda* and the *Shavuos* lambs, needed to be stated. Had the *mishnah* only taught the case of *toda*, one might have reasoned that there the bread does not affect the *toda* because they are clearly distinct objects — *d'lo huskeku ze la'ze b'tnufah*, because the *toda* and its bread are not waved together in the *tenufah* ritual. Since they undergo no shared ritual, they are more conceptually distinct, and it makes sense that the bread cannot affect the *toda*. One might then suppose that by the lambs of *Shavuos*, where *d'huskeku ze la'ze b'tnufah* — the two lambs and the two loaves are waved together in a single act — perhaps that act of unification is so binding that the bread does affect the lambs. The *mishnah* therefore needed to teach both cases, establishing that in both instances the same principle applies: the bread is dependent on the animal *korban*, and that relationship is asymmetric regardless of whether there is a shared waving ritual.

It is worth noting that although the *gemara* earlier cited a verse connecting the breads of *toda* to the *toda* itself, by this point the *pesuk* ceases to function as the operative reason. The ruling is entirely derived from the logical structure of dependency. What defines something as a unified halakhic unit is whether the *avodah* is performed together — not what a verse calls the object by name.

## Chatzi Kezayis Mix

### Rav's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מֵרַב: הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַתּוֹדָה לֶאֱכוֹל כְּזַיִת מִמֶּנָּה וּמִלַּחְמָהּ לְמָחָר, מַהוּ לְאִיפַּגּוֹלֵי? תּוֹדָה לָא מִיבַּעְיָא לִי, הַשְׁתָּא כּוּלּוֹ מִלַּחְמָהּ לָא מִיפַּגְּלָא, מִמֶּנָּה וּמִלַּחְמָהּ מִיבַּעְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Elazar raised a dilemma before Rav:** If **one slaughters the thanks offering** with the intent **to consume an olive-bulk from it and from its loaves the next day, what is** the *halakha*? Rabbi Elazar elaborates: **I do not raise the dilemma with regard to rendering** the **thanks offering *piggul*,** for the following reason: **Now** that in a case where his intent was to consume **the entire** olive-bulk **from its loaves** alone, the thanks offering **is not rendered *piggul*,** in accordance with the mishna's ruling that *piggul* intent with regard to the loaves does not render the thanks offering *piggul*, then in a case where his intent is to consume half an olive-bulk **from** the thanks offering **and** half an olive-bulk **from its loaves,** in which case the offering is rendered *piggul* only if the two intentions of less than a full measure are combined, is it **necessary** to teach that the thanks offering is not *piggul*?

*Rav Eliezer* posed a question to *Rav*: *hashocheit es hatodah v'leechol kezayis mimenah u'milachmah l'machar* — if one slaughters the *toda* intending to eat tomorrow a *kezayis* composed of half from the *toda* meat and half from the bread, what is the law?

The question regarding the *toda* itself becoming *piggul* does not arise, as *Rav Eliezer* notes: *hashta kula milachma lo mifagla* — since even a full *kezayis* directed entirely at the bread does not cause the animal *korban* to become *piggul*, certainly a hybrid thought incorporating half a *kezayis* of bread will not do so. The question is specifically whether the bread becomes *piggul*: since the bread is dependent on the *toda*, and a *piggul* thought about a full *kezayis* of the *toda* meat renders the bread *piggul*, can half a *kezayis* of meat combine with half a *kezayis* of bread to render the bread *piggul*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לִי לְאִיפַּגּוֹלֵי לֶחֶם, מִי מִצְטָרְפָה תּוֹדָה לְאִיפַּגּוֹלֵי לְלֶחֶם אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** Rather, **when I raise the dilemma,** it is with regard **to rendering the loaves *piggul*,** which can be rendered *piggul* via intent concerning the loaves alone. In this case, **does** the intention of the priest with regard to the **thanks offering combine** with his intention concerning the loaves **to render the loaves *piggul*, or not?**

### Kal VaChomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַף בְּזוֹ, הַלֶּחֶם מְפוּגָּל וְהַתּוֹדָה אֵינָהּ מְפוּגֶּלֶת. וְאַמַּאי? לֵימָא קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה תּוֹדָה הַמְפַגֵּל אֵין מִתְפַּגֵּל, הַבָּא לְפַגֵּל וְלֹא פִּיגֵּל – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא יִתְפַּגֵּל?
>
> **Translation:** Rav **said to** Rabbi Elazar: **Even in this** case, **the loaves are rendered *piggul* and the thanks offering is not rendered *piggul*.** The Gemara asks: **But why** should the loaves be rendered *piggul*? **Let us say** the following ***a fortiori*** inference: **And if** the **thanks offering, which** in this case serves to **render** the loaves ***piggul*, is** itself **not rendered *piggul*,** then the loaves, **which come to render** the thanks offering ***piggul*, but do not render** it ***piggul*,** as the intent to consume half an olive-bulk from the loaves does not combine with the intent to consume half an olive-bulk from the thanks offering to render the thanks offering *piggul*, **is it not logical that the** loaves themselves **should not be rendered *piggul*?**

*Rav* answered: *af zo* — yes, even in this case, the bread is *piggul* while the *toda* is not. From the bread's perspective, it is all one: the thought encompasses a full *kezayis* of what is connected to the bread's status, and the bread is therefore *piggul*.

The *gemara* challenges this ruling with a *kal vachomer*. The argument runs as follows: the meat — in this hybrid scenario — is the *mefagel*, the element that is participating in making the bread *piggul*. Yet the meat itself does not become *piggul*; it is not *mitpageil*. The bread, on the other hand, has been *haba lefagel velo figeil* — it has been attempting to render the meat *piggul* but cannot do so. If the very thing that makes the bread *piggul* is itself exempt from becoming *piggul*, is it not all the more so that the bread — which could not even make the meat *piggul* — should certainly be protected from becoming *piggul* itself?

### Kilayim Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמְרִינַן קַל וָחוֹמֶר כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאֶחָד שֶׁזָּרַע כַּרְמוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ סְמָדַר, וּבָא מַעֲשֶׂה לִפְנֵי חֲכָמִים, וְאָסְרוּ אֶת הַזְּרָעִים וְהִתִּירוּ אֶת הַגְּפָנִים. וְאַמַּאי? לֵימָא קַל וָחוֹמֶר הוּא: וּמָה הָאוֹסֵר אֵינוֹ נֶאֱסָר, הַבָּא לֶאֱסוֹר וְלֹא אָסַר – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא יִתְאַסֵּר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And do we say an *a fortiori*** inference **in this way? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: There was **an incident involving one who planted** seeds in **the vineyard of an another** when the grapes on the vines were **budding, and** the **incident came before the Sages and they deemed the seeds prohibited** due to the prohibition against planting diverse kinds in a vineyard, **but they deemed the vines permitted.** The Gemara continues: **But why** did they deem the seeds prohibited? **Let** the Sages **say** through an analogous ***a fortiori*** inference that the seeds should be permitted: **And if that which renders** an item **prohibited,** i.e., the vine, which causes the seeds to be prohibited, **is** itself **not prohibited,** then with regard to the seeds, **which come to render** the vines **prohibited but did not render** the vines **prohibited, is it not logical that they should not be rendered prohibited?**

The *gemara* then asks: do we in fact apply a *kal vachomer* of this kind? A *braisa* records a case: someone planted his neighbor's vineyard when the grapes were still at the *semadar* stage — not yet fully formed, meaning the vines could still acquire the prohibition of *kilayim*. The principle of *ein adam oser davar she'eino shelo* — one cannot render forbidden something that does not belong to oneself — generally protects the owner's property from prohibitions incurred through another's act. However, since this person physically planted seeds in the vineyard, a physical *kilayim* reality was created. The case was brought before the Sages, who ruled: the seeds are forbidden, but the vine is permitted, based on *ein adam oser davar she'eino shelo*.

Now, could one not apply a *kal vachomer* here as well? The vine — the *oser*, the thing that renders the seeds forbidden — is itself not rendered forbidden. The seeds — *haba leesor velo asar*, coming to make the vine forbidden but failing to do so — should certainly not themselves become forbidden. The *gemara* notes that this *kal vachomer* was not applied, which would seem to undermine the *kal vachomer* in the *piggul* case as well.

### Kanbus Luf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא? הָתָם קַנְבּוֹס וְלוּף אָסְרָה תּוֹרָה, דִּתְנַן: הָיְתָה שָׂדֵהוּ זְרוּעָה קַנְבּוֹס וְלוֹף לֹא יְהֵא זוֹרֵעַ עַל גַּבֵּיהֶם, שֶׁהֵן עוֹשׂוֹת לְשָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים – שְׁאָר זְרָעִים מִדְּרַבָּנַן הוּא דַּאֲסִירִי. הַאי דְּעָבֵיד אִיסּוּרָא – קַנְסוּהּ רַבָּנַן, הַאי דְּלָא עָבֵד אִיסּוּרָא – לָא קַנְסוּהּ רַבָּנַן. אֲבָל הָכָא, לֵימָא קַל וָחוֹמֶר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the comparison: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** in the *baraita*, only **hemp and arum** are **prohibited** by **Torah** law to be sown in a vineyard, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Kilayim* 2:5): If **one's field was sown** with **hemp and arum, he should not sow above them, as they produce** a yield only once every **three years. Other seeds are prohibited by rabbinic** law. Therefore, with regard to **this** person **who committed a transgression** by planting the seeds in the vineyard of another, **the Sages penalized him** and deemed his seeds prohibited, but as for **that** person **who did not commit a transgression,** i.e., the owner of the vineyard, **the Sages did not penalize him. But here,** in the case of *piggul*, which is a biblical prohibition, **let us say** such **an *a fortiori*** inference.

The *gemara* rejects the comparison: *hatam kanvus v'luf asrah Torah* — there, the Torah only specifically prohibited *kanvus* (hemp) and *luf* (identified as *arum palaestinum*) as non-grain plants that create *kilayim* in a vineyard; *shar zerai midrabbanan hu d'asiri* — all other seeds and plants are only rabbinically forbidden in this context. Since the prohibition on these seeds in the vineyard is rabbinic, the mechanism is not a natural spreading prohibition but a *kenas* — a fine imposed by the Rabbis. *Hai d'avid issura kansua rabbanan* — the one who committed the wrong act is penalized; *d'lo avid issura lo kansua rabbanan* — the one who did no wrong is not penalized. The structure of the *kilayim* ruling is therefore not about how the prohibition mechanically operates but about whom the Rabbis chose to punish. You cannot derive a *kal vachomer* from a fine, because fines are imposed selectively on wrongdoers, not based on abstract logical principles about the mechanism of *issur* and *ne'esar*.

By contrast, by *piggul* — *aval hacha leima kal vachomer* — the prohibition is not a fine but the natural operation of halakhic status. Therefore, a *kal vachomer* about the mechanism of *mefagel* and *mitpageil* is in principle applicable here, and the *gemara* does not definitively dismiss it.

### Kvashim Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַכְּבָשִׂים, בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מֵרַב: הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַכְּבָשִׂים לֶאֱכוֹל כְּזַיִת מֵהֶן וּמִלַּחְמָן, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **And there are those who teach** the dilemma of Rabbi Elazar **with regard to the lambs** brought with the two loaves, and not with regard to a thanks offering. **Rabbi Elazar raised a dilemma before Rav:** In a case where **one slaughters the lambs** with the intent **to consume an olive-bulk from them and from their loaves** the next day, **what is** the *halakha*?

The *gemara* then states: *v'ika d'matni lah a'kvasim* — there is a parallel version of this entire discussion formulated with respect to the lambs of *Shavuos* rather than the *toda*. *Rav Eliezer* asks *Rav*: if one slaughters the lambs intending to eat a hybrid *kezayis* — half from the lambs and half from the *shtei halechem* — what is the law? The question about whether the lambs become *piggul* is again dismissed immediately: even a full *kezayis* directed at the bread does not make the lambs *piggul*, so certainly a hybrid does not. The question is whether the half *kezayis* of lamb meat combines with the half *kezayis* of bread to make the bread *piggul*. *Rav* gives the same answer — *af bezo halechem mifgal v'hakvasim einam mefugalim* — and the *gemara* raises the same *kal vachomer* and the same *kilayim* reductio. The two versions of the question are substantively identical; the reason both versions were transmitted is discussed below.

### Chaveiro Term

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אַבָּא זוּטֵי בָּעֵי לַהּ הָכִי, בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מֵרַב: הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַכֶּבֶשׂ לֶאֱכוֹל כְּזַיִת מֵחֲבֵירוֹ לְמָחָר, מַהוּ? ״חֲבֵירוֹ״ כֶּבֶשׂ מַשְׁמַע, וְלָא מְפַגֵּל, אוֹ דִלְמָא ״לֶחֶם״ מַשְׁמַע, וּמְפַגֵּל לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba the small** taught that Rabbi Elazar **raises the dilemma** in **this** manner: **Rabbi Elazar raised a dilemma before Rav:** If **one slaughters** one of **the lambs** brought on *Shavuot* with the two loaves intending **to consume an olive-bulk from the other the next day, what is** the *halakha*? When this individual thinks of **the other,** does he **mean** the other **lamb, and** if so, the lamb **is not rendered *piggul*,** as one permitting factor does not render another permitting factor *piggul*? **Or perhaps** when this individual thinks of the other he **means** the other part of the offering, i.e., the two **loaves, and** if so his intention **renders it *piggul*,** as taught in the mishna.

The *gemara* cites a further question attributed to *Rabbi Abba Zuti*, reporting on behalf of *Rabbi Elazar* who asked *Rav*: *hashochet es hakeves leechol kezayis mechaveiro l'machar* — if one slaughters one of the two lambs of *Shavuos* intending to eat tomorrow a *kezayis* from its *chaveiro*, what is the law? The word *chaveiro* is ambiguous. Does it mean the fellow lamb — the other one of the two *kvashim* — or does it mean the bread, which is also associated with and in some sense the "companion" of the lamb?

If *chaveiro* means the other lamb, the answer is straightforward: there is no *piggul* at all, because of the principle *ein matir mefagel matir* — one *matir* does not render another *matir piggul*. The two lambs each function as *matir* for the *shtei halechem*, but they do not function as *matir* for each other; a thought during the *shechitah* of one lamb about eating the other lamb is simply irrelevant to the validity of either.

### Ayin Matir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תְּנֵיתוּהָ, שָׁחַט אֶחָד מִן הַכְּבָשִׂים לֶאֱכוֹל מִמֶּנּוּ לְמָחָר – הוּא פִּיגּוּל וַחֲבֵירוֹ כָּשֵׁר, לֶאֱכוֹל מֵחֲבֵירוֹ לְמָחָר – שְׁנֵיהֶם כְּשֵׁרִים, אַלְמָא ״חֲבֵירוֹ״ כֶּבֶשׂ מַשְׁמַע! דִּלְמָא דְּפָרֵישׁ וְאָמַר ״חֲבֵירוֹ כֶּבֶשׂ״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav **said to** Rabbi Elazar: **You learned** in a mishna (16a): If **one slaughtered one of the lambs** with the intent **to partake of it the next day, that** lamb is ***piggul* and the other** is **fit.** If he slaughtered one lamb with the intent **to partake of the other the next day, both** lambs **are fit,** as one permitting factor does not render another permitting factor *piggul*. Rav concludes: **Evidently,** in this context the term: **The other, means** the other **lamb.** The Gemara rejects this proof: **Perhaps** the mishna is discussing a case **where he clarifies and says: The other lamb,** but the mishna is not referring to a case where he merely thought: The other.

The *gemara* answers by pointing to a later *mishnah*: *shachat echad min hakvasim leechol mimenu l'machar hu piggul v'chaveiro kasher* — one slaughters one of the lambs intending to eat from it the next day; it is *piggul* and its *chaveiro* is valid. There, the *mishnah* uses *chaveiro* to mean the fellow lamb. Thus *leechol mechaveiro l'machar* — slaughtering with intent to eat the fellow lamb tomorrow — produces a ruling of *shneihem kesherim*, both are valid, because *ein matir mefagel matir*.

This established that *chaveiro* in the *mishnah* refers to the other lamb. The *gemara* deflects: perhaps the *mishnah* only means the other lamb because the context makes it explicit — *d'parish v'amar chaveiro keves* — but absent that context, the word *chaveiro* is not a fixed technical term and could refer to the bread. The *gemara* does not fully resolve which interpretation *Rav Eliezer* intended.

What the discussion illuminates, however, is the full structure of the *Shavuos* model of dependencies: two lambs, each a *chatzi matir* for the *shtei halechem*, dependent upon both; a thought during one lamb's *shechitah* affects the lamb itself and the bread dependent on it, but it does not affect the other lamb, because the two lambs are not dependent on each other.

As for why both versions of *Rav Eliezer*'s question — the *toda* version and the *kvashim* version — needed to be transmitted: *maan d'matki lah l'toda kol she'ken kvashim* — the one who formulated the question about the *toda* would certainly apply the same reasoning to the lambs of *Shavuos*. But *maan d'matki l'kvasim* — the one who specifically formulated it about the lambs might not have applied it to the *toda*, because the lambs and the *shtei halechem* are *huskeku ze la'ze b'tnufah* — they are bound together in the shared waving ritual — making them more of a unified entity, whereas the *toda* and its bread are not, making them more distinct. It was therefore necessary to teach both cases explicitly.

## Nesachim

### Zevach Mefagel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַזֶּבַח מְפַגֵּל אֶת הַנְּסָכִים, מִשֶּׁקָּדְשׁוּ בִּכְלִי, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. הַנְּסָכִים אֵינָן מְפַגְּלִים אֶת הַזֶּבַח. כֵּיצַד? הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַזֶּבַח לֶאֱכוֹל מִמֶּנּוּ לְמָחָר – הוּא וּנְסָכָיו מְפוּגָּלִין, לְהַקְרִיב נְסָכָיו לְמָחָר – הַנְּסָכִים מְפוּגָּלִין, הַזֶּבַח אֵינוֹ מְפוּגָּל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The** animal **offering renders the** accompanying **libations** and meal offerings ***piggul* from** the moment **that they were consecrated in the vessel,** but not before; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. The libations do not render the** animal **offering *piggul*. How so?** In the case of **one who slaughters the offering** with the intent **to partake of it the next day,** the offering **and its libations are rendered *piggul*.** But if one slaughters the offering with the intent **to sacrifice its libations the next day, the libations are rendered *piggul*,** while **the offering is not *piggul*.**

A new *mishnah* extends these principles to *nesachim* — the wine libation and the flour-and-oil mixture accompanying a *korban*. *Hazevach mefagel es hanesachim* — the *zevach* renders the *nesachim piggul*; *hanesachim einam mefaglim es hazevach* — but the *nesachim* do not render the *zevach piggul*. This is a direct application of the dependency logic established for the *toda* and the *Shavuos* lambs: the *nesachim* depend on the *zevach*, and not the reverse.

The *mishnah* works through the case: *hashocheit es hazevach le'echol mimenu l'machar* — if one slaughters the sacrifice intending to eat from it tomorrow, *hu u'nesachav mefugalim* — it and the *nesachim* are *piggul*. *L'hakriv nesachav l'machar* — if one intends only to bring the *nesachim* tomorrow, *hanesachim mefugalim hazevach eino mefugal* — the *nesachim* are *piggul* but the *zevach* is unaffected.

### Rabbi Meir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: נִסְכֵי בְּהֵמָה חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁדַּם הַזֶּבַח מַתִּירָן לִקְרַב, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to the **libations of an animal** offering, **one is liable for** eating **them due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*, as the blood of the offering permits them to be offered** on the altar, and any item that becomes permitted for consumption or for sacrifice through a permitting factor can be rendered *piggul*, and one who partakes of such an item after its permitting factors were sacrificed is liable to receive *karet*. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

The *chiddush* of this *mishnah* lies not in the general principle — which follows directly from the *toda* and *kvashim* cases — but in the question of when the *nesachim* become identified with the *korban* such that the *korban*'s status can affect them. *Misheyikdeshu bichli divrei Rabbi Meir* — once the *nesachim* have been placed into a vessel, according to *Rabbi Meir*, they are identified with this *korban* and the *zevach*'s *piggul* status applies to them.

### Unified Unit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר: וַהֲלֹא אָדָם מֵבִיא זִבְחוֹ הַיּוֹם וּנְסָכָיו עַד עֲשָׂרָה יָמִים! אָמַר לָהֶן: אַף אֲנִי לֹא אָמַרְתִּי אֶלָּא בְּבָאִין עִם הַזֶּבַח. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֶפְשָׁר לְשַׁנּוֹתוֹ לְזֶבַח אַחֵר!
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to Rabbi Meir: But a person may bring his offering today and** the accompanying **libations from now until** even **ten days** later. Evidently, then, the blood of the offering does render the libations permitted. Rabbi Meir **said to them: I, too, spoke only about** libations that **come** to be sacrificed together **with the offering** and were already sanctified in a service vessel for that purpose. The Rabbis **said to him:** But **it is possible to switch** the libations **for** use with **another offering.** Clearly, then, they are not considered an indispensable part of that offering.

The *gemara* cites the Rabbis' objection: *halo adam meivi zivcho hayom u'nesachav ad asura yamim* — a person may bring his animal sacrifice today and his *nesachim* as late as ten days later. This demonstrates that the *nesachim* are not inherently one unit with the *zevach*. Moreover — and this point bears on the very nature of *piggul*, which is defined by time — the *korban* must be eaten within its designated time, while the *nesachim* retain their own separate time parameters. If they are not tied together in time, why should a *piggul* thought about the *zevach* define the temporal status of the *nesachim*?

### Separate Days

This is what distinguishes the *nesachim* from the *lachmei toda* and the *shtei halechem*. The breads of the *toda* are brought with the *toda* and have no other purpose; the *shtei halechem* are brought with the lambs of *Shavuos* and wave together with them. Those things are identified. But the *nesachim* may be brought on an entirely different day — and that very permissibility indicates that the Torah has declared them a distinct entity, not bound to the *zevach* in the same way.

### Rava's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: קָסָבַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר, הוּקְבְּעוּ בִּשְׁחִיטָה, כְּלַחְמֵי תוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Rabbi Meir holds** that the libations **are fixed** to this particular offering **at** the time of its **slaughter,** and one may not use them with another offering. Accordingly, these libations are rendered *piggul* on account of intent during the slaughter of the offering, just **like the loaves of a thanks offering,** which are fixed to a particular thanks offering upon its slaughter and become *piggul* on account of intent during the slaughter of that particular thanks offering.

*Rava* articulates *Rabbi Meir*'s position: *kasavar Rabbi Meir hukvu bishchitah ke'lachmei toda* — *Rabbi Meir* holds that the *nesachim* become fixed as one unit with the *zevach* at the moment of *shechitah*, just as the breads of the *toda* become identified at *shechitah*. The rationale: until *shechitah*, yes, the *nesachim* happen to be present with the owner in the *Mikdash*, but they have not yet been formally linked to this *korban*. At the moment of *shechitah*, when the owner stands before the *Mikdash* with both the animal and the *nesachim*, that act binds them together as one. The *Chachamim* disagree: since the Torah permitted the *nesachim* to be brought separately, they always retain their independent status, and no act of the owner can permanently bind them to a specific *korban*.

### Log Shemen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לוֹג שֶׁמֶן שֶׁל מְצוֹרָע חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁדַּם אָשָׁם מַתִּירוֹ לַבְּהוֹנוֹת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר: וַהֲלֹא אָדָם מֵבִיא אֲשָׁמוֹ הַיּוֹם, וְלוּגּוֹ מִיכָּן וְעַד עֲשָׂרָה יָמִים. אָמַר לָהֶן: אַף אֲנִי לֹא אָמַרְתִּי אֶלָּא בְּבָא עִם הָאָשָׁם, אֶפְשָׁר לְשַׁנּוֹתוֹ לְאָשָׁם אַחֵר. אָמַר רָבָא: קָסָבַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר הוּקְבְּעוּ בִּשְׁחִיטָה, כְּלַחְמֵי תוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** In a similar vein, **the Sages taught:** With regard to the ***log* of oil of the leper** (see Leviticus 14:10–20), **one is liable for** eating **it due to** violation of the prohibition of ***piggul*** if the guilt offering that this oil accompanied became *piggul*, **as** the **blood** of the offering **permits it to** be placed on **the** right **thumb and big toe** of the leper. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.** The Rabbis **said to Rabbi Meir: But a person may bring his guilt offering today and** the accompanying ***log*** of oil **from now until** even **ten days** later. Evidently, the *log* of oil is not considered part of the guilt offering, and therefore it should not be rendered *piggul* on account of it. Rabbi Meir **said to them: I, too, spoke only about** a *log* of oil that **comes with the guilt** offering. The Rabbis said to him: But even in this case, the oil should not be considered part of the offering, as **it is possible to switch** the oil **for** use with **another guilt offering. Rava said: Rabbi Meir holds** that the *log* of oil **is fixed** to this particular guilt offering **at** the time of its **slaughter** and is therefore rendered *piggul* on account of it, just **like the loaves of a thanks offering,** as the slaughter of the thanks offering fixes the accompanying loaves to that particular offering.

The *gemara* then brings a parallel case involving the *matzora*: *log shemen shel matzora chayavin alav mishum piggul* — the *log* of oil brought by the *metzora* can become *piggul*, because *dam asham matiro le'ihana* — the sprinkling of the blood of the *asham* permits the use of the oil. *Rabbi Meir* says so; the Rabbis object with the same argument — *adam meivi ashamo hayom ve'logo mikan ad asura yamim* — one may bring the *asham* today and the oil up to ten days later, demonstrating they are not one unit. *Rabbi Meir* answers that he only said this when they are brought together. The Rabbis counter that even when brought together, the oil can always be redirected to another *asham* — *efshar leshnoso le'asham acher* — and therefore it never becomes irrevocably identified with this *korban*. *Rava* again states *Rabbi Meir*'s response: *kasavar Rabbi Meir hukvu bishchitah ke'lachmei toda* — the oil is fixed as one unit with the *asham* at the moment of *shechitah*, just as the bread of *toda* is.

The fundamental dispute between *Rabbi Meir* and the *Chachamim* thus comes into focus: *Rabbi Meir* holds that once the owner brings the *nesachim* or the *log* to the *Mikdash* and performs the *shechitah*, that moment of conjunction creates a halakhic unity equivalent to the unity of the *toda* and its bread. The *Chachamim* hold that because the Torah explicitly permitted bringing them on separate days, they can never be fully identified as a single unit — their independent status is not overcome by the accident of being present together, since the owner always retains the option of separating them.
