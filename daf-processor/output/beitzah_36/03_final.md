# Beitzah 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mashilin & Hefsed

### Intro to Mashilin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַשִּׁילִין פֵּירוֹת דֶּרֶךְ אֲרוּבָּה בְּיוֹם טוֹב, אֲבָל לֹא בְּשַׁבָּת. וּמְכַסִּים פֵּירוֹת בְּכֵלִים מִפְּנֵי הַדֶּלֶף, וְכֵן כַּדֵּי יַיִן וְכַדֵּי שֶׁמֶן. וְנוֹתְנִין כְּלִי תַּחַת הַדֶּלֶף בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **One may lower produce,** which had been laid out on a roof to dry, into the house **through a skylight on a Festival,** in order to prevent it from becoming ruined in the rain. Although it is a strenuous activity, it is permitted do to so on a Festival in order to prevent a financial loss; **however,** one may **not** do so **on Shabbat. And one may cover produce** inside a building **with cloths** to prevent damage **due to a leak** in the ceiling over it, **and similarly** one may cover **jugs of wine and jugs of oil** for the same reason. **And one may place a vessel beneath a leak** in order to catch the water **on Shabbat,** to prevent it from dirtying the house.

We begin the fifth and final *perek* of *Beitzah*, with one week remaining in the *masechta*. Daf 36 picks up from the bottom of 35b, seven lines from the end, at the word *tnan*. The context is the *mishnah* that discusses the permissibility of various protective actions on *Yom Tov*: specifically, the allowance to lower fruit from a rooftop through a skylight, and to cover produce that is about to be rained upon — even though this is not done for the sake of *ochel nefesh*, since one will not be eating the fruit that day. Nevertheless, the action is permitted in order to prevent *hefsed*. The counterbalancing concern, as Rashi notes, is one of *tircha* — excessive effort — and, always lurking in the background, the concern of *uvda d'chol*, conducting oneself in a manner indistinguishable from ordinary weekday activity.

### Mishnah: Fruit & Rain

The *mishnah* teaches: *mashilin peiros derech arubba b'Yom Tov* — one may lower fruit through the skylight on *Yom Tov* from the rooftop. Similarly, fruit may be covered to protect it from rain, even though the motivation is not immediate consumption but rather the prevention of spoilage.

### Ad Kama: Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: מַשִּׁילִין פֵּירוֹת דֶּרֶךְ אֲרוּבָּה בְּיוֹם טוֹב. עַד כַּמָּה? אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּאוֹתָהּ שֶׁשָּׁנִינוּ: מְפַנִּין אַרְבַּע וְחָמֵשׁ קוּפּוֹת שֶׁל תֶּבֶן וְשֶׁל תְּבוּאָה מִפְּנֵי הָאוֹרְחִים, וּמִפְּנֵי בִּטּוּל בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the *halakha* in the mishna: **We learned** that **one may lower produce through a skylight on a Festival.** The Gemara asks: **Up to how much** produce may be lowered in this manner? At what point is it considered to be too strenuous an activity to be performed on the Festival? **Rabbi Zeira said** that **Rav Asi said, and some say** that **Rav Asi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** It is **like that which we learned** in a mishna with regard to a different case: **One may clear out four or five sacks of hay or grain** from a room on Shabbat **due to visitors,** to clear a place for them to sit, **or due to suspension of** study in **the study hall,** i.e., to make room there for more people, who would not be able to study Torah otherwise. Here too, only four or five sacks' worth of produce may be lowered from the roof.

The Gemara immediately asks: *ad kama* — how much is one permitted to do before the activity crosses the threshold into problematic *tircha* or *uvda d'chol*? The answer given is attributed in two versions: *amar Rav Zeira Rav Assi*, and in another formulation, *amar Rav Assi amar Rabbi Yochanan*: *k'mot sheshaninu* — like what we taught in *Shabbat*. A parallel discussion about moving fruit and grain appears in tractate *Shabbat*, and the parameters established there should guide us here as well.

## Shabbat Parallel

### 4-5 Baskets

The *mishnah* in *Shabbat* teaches: *mafnin arba v'chamesha kuppot shel teven v'shel tvuah* — one may clear away the equivalent of four or five baskets of straw or grain. Two justifications are offered: *mipnei orchim*, in order to make space for guests, and *u'mipnei bittul beit HaMidrash*, to prevent the cancellation of Torah study — that is, to open up space where people can gather to learn. The Gemara understands this not as an open-ended allowance but as establishing a quantitative limit.

### Chamira vs. Kala

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאִיכָּא בִּטּוּל בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּלֵיכָּא בִּטּוּל בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ — לָא. אִי נָמֵי: הָתָם הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא, דְּאַרְבַּע וְחָמֵשׁ קוּפּוֹת שְׁרֵי מִשּׁוּם שַׁבָּת דַּחֲמִירָא וְלָא אָתֵי לְזַלְזוֹלֵי בַּיהּ, אֲבָל יוֹם טוֹב דְּקִיל וְאָתֵי לְזַלְזוֹלֵי בֵּיהּ — כְּלָל כְּלָל לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection to the comparison of the two cases. **But perhaps there it is different, since there is** the matter of preventing **suspension of** study in **the study hall** or of providing hospitality to guests, i.e., moving those items is permitted in order to facilitate a mitzva. **But here, where there is no suspension of** study in **the study hall,** i.e., no facilitation of any mitzva, they did **not** permit one to move such a large amount. **Alternatively: There, this is the reasoning that four or five sacks are permitted: Because Shabbat is severe** in people's eyes **and they will not come to belittle it; but** on **a Festival, which is** regarded more **lightly and which** people **might come to belittle,** one may **not** move the items **at all.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי לְאִידַּךְ גִּיסָא: הָתָם הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא, דְּלֵיכָּא הֶפְסֵד מָמוֹן, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּאִיכָּא הֶפְסֵד מָמוֹן, אֲפִילּוּ טוּבָא נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** a claim can be made **from the other perspective: There, this is the reasoning** that it is permitted to carry only four or five sacks: **Because there is no monetary loss** involved. **But here, where there is monetary loss** if the produce is not moved, one may carry **even a larger amount** than four or five sacks.

The Gemara immediately notes that the two cases differ in important ways and raises arguments in both directions. On one hand: *shani hatam d'ika bittul beit HaMidrash* — perhaps the *Shabbat* case is more lenient because the justification there (guests and Torah study) is weightier, whereas here on *Yom Tov* — *d'leika bittul beit HaMidrash* — where no such consideration applies, we would not permit as much. On the other hand, one can argue the exact opposite: *hatam hai ta'ama arba v'chamesha kuppot shari mishum Shabbat d'chamira v'lo ati lezilzulei bei* — perhaps it is precisely because *Shabbat* is weightier that even a generous allowance of four or five baskets does not lead to disregard for its laws. *Aval Yom Tov d'kila ati lezilzulei bei* — but *Yom Tov*, which is experientially lighter, and during which one already engages in considerably more activity by virtue of permitted cooking and food preparation, may lead people to treat it too casually. Therefore, on *Yom Tov* one might say *clal clal lo* — we would not permit it at all. This, of course, cannot mean a total prohibition since the *mishnah* explicitly permits the action; it means we would not permit it to the same degree.

Both arguments are coherent. One can argue that *Shabbat* should be more lenient because of the pull of *bittul beit HaMidrash* and because its own gravity ensures it is not taken lightly. Or one can argue that *Yom Tov* should be more lenient because the concern of *hefsed mamon* — financial loss — is a more pressing justification than guests or Torah study. Beyond the simple concern about waste, *hefsed mamon* also carries a practical dimension: if no leniency is granted, people may simply act on their own and incur even greater violations.

### Uvda D'chol Worry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם תְּנַן ״אֲבָל לֹא אֶת הָאוֹצָר״, וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מַאי ״אֲבָל לֹא אֶת הָאוֹצָר״ — אֲבָל לֹא יִגְמוֹר אֶת הָאוֹצָר כּוּלּוֹ, דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְאַשְׁוֹיֵי גּוּמּוֹת. הָכָא מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses another question with regard to the same issue. **We learned elsewhere,** at the end of the mishna cited above concerning clearing out sacks for guests and study: **But** one **may not** clear out **a storeroom. And Shmuel said: What is** the meaning of: **But not a storeroom?** It means: **But one may not finish** clearing out **the entire storeroom** while clearing out the sacks, exposing the floor of the storeroom. The reason this is prohibited is **lest he come to level out depressions** in the dirt floor of the storeroom, which would constitute a biblically prohibited labor. **What would be** the *halakha* **here,** with regard to lowering the produce from the roof on a Festival to prevent its ruin in the rain? Is it prohibited also in this case to remove all of it and thereby expose the floor of the roof?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם הוּא בְּשַׁבָּת דְּאָסוּר — מִשּׁוּם דַּחֲמִיר, אֲבָל יוֹם טוֹב דְּקִיל — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי. אוֹ דִלְמָא: הָתָם דְּאִיכָּא בִּטּוּל בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ — אָמְרַתְּ לָא, הָכָא דְּלֵיכָּא בִּטּוּל בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara specifies the possible arguments: Perhaps **it is there, on Shabbat, that it is prohibited, because** punishment for Shabbat desecration is **severe, but** on **a Festival, which** has a **lighter** punishment for desecration, **it is acceptable. Or perhaps** it can be argued to the contrary: **There,** in the case of the sacks on Shabbat, even though **there is suspension of** study in **the study hall,** i.e., the clearing out facilitates a mitzva, **you say** that they did **not** permit exposing the floor. **Here,** in the case of lowering produce on a Festival to prevent its ruin, **where there is no suspension of** study in **the study hall,** i.e., clearing out the produce does not facilitate any mitzva, is it **not all the more so** prohibited?

The Gemara continues to press the comparison across several parameters. The first concerns whether one may clear out an entire storage space (*otzar*). The *mishnah* in *Shabbat* teaches *aval lo es ha'otzar* — one may not clear out the entire shed. Shmuel explains: *aval lo yigmor es ha'otzar kulo* — do not empty the entire space. Even if the shed contains only four or five baskets, one may not clear the whole floor. The reason: *dilma asi l'ashvuyei gumos* — if the entire floor is exposed, one may come to fill in furrows and indentations, which would constitute a prohibited act akin to *boneh* on a dirt floor.

The Gemara then asks whether the same restriction applies to clearing a roof on *Yom Tov*. One argument runs: *hatam hu b'Shabbos d'assir mishum d'chamir* — on *Shabbat*, which is weightier, the concern that one will come to violate justifies even distant protective enactments. *Aval Yom Tov d'kal* — but since *Yom Tov*'s violation is less severe, we would not extend the *gezeira* to prohibit clearing the whole space; *shappir dami*, it would be permitted. Conversely: *hatam d'ikka bittul beit HaMidrash amarta lo* — if even when there exists the strong justification of preventing the loss of Torah study we still do not permit clearing the entire space, then *hacha d'leika bittul beit HaMidrash lo kol she-ken* — here on *Yom Tov*, where that justification is absent, surely we should be equally or more restrictive.

It is worth noting that the concern about sweeping the floor seems more naturally connected to the *Shabbat* case, where people intend to use the cleared space and would want to tidy it up. The roof scenario, by contrast, involves lowering fruit — one does not plan to sit on the roof, and dirt floors are not an obvious feature of rooftops. So even the conceptual underpinning of the restriction maps imperfectly onto the *Yom Tov* case. No resolution is reached.

## Full Space Clearing

### Lo et HaOtzar

As noted above, Shmuel clarifies that the *mishnah*'s prohibition against clearing the *otzar* refers to the prohibition against clearing it entirely: *aval lo yigmor es ha'otzar kulo*. Even when the total contents fall within the permitted range of four or five baskets, one may not leave the whole floor exposed.

### Furrows Concern

The rationale is that a completely cleared floor invites sweeping and leveling: *dilma asi l'ashvuyei gumos* — one will come to smooth over the furrows and depressions in the dirt floor, which constitutes a form of forbidden labor on *Shabbat* analogous to *boneh*.

### Roof vs. Shed

When applying this restriction to the roof on *Yom Tov*, the Gemara proceeds as outlined above — arguing in both directions without resolution. What is clear is that the Gemara takes seriously both the possibility of stringency (since *Yom Tov*'s lighter character may require more protective fencing) and the possibility of leniency (since the specific concern about sweeping a dirt floor does not transfer cleanly to a rooftop context, and the violation of *Yom Tov* is less weighty than that of *Shabbat*).

## Migag Legag Limit

### Skylight Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכָא תְּנַן: מַשִּׁילִין פֵּירוֹת דֶּרֶךְ אֲרוּבָּה בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּאוֹתוֹ הַגָּג, אֲבָל מִגַּג לְגַג — לָא. וְתַנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין מִגַּג לְגַג אֲפִילּוּ כְּשֶׁגַּגּוֹתֵיהֶן שָׁוִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a further question. **And here we learned** in the mishna: **One may lower produce through a skylight on a Festival, and Rav Naḥman said: They taught** this *halakha* **only with regard to the same roof,** i.e., only if the skylight is in the same roof where the produce is located, **but** to carry the produce **from** one **roof to** another **roof** in order to lower it through a skylight in the second roof is **not** permitted. This would involve too much exertion to be permitted on the Festival. **And** this ruling **is also taught** in a *baraita*: **One may not carry from** one **roof to** another **roof, even when** the two **roofs are on the same level** and there is no extra effort of lifting or lowering the produce while transporting it between the roofs.

The Gemara introduces a ruling by Rav Nachman that introduces a new dimension of the *Yom Tov* case, and then asks whether the same applies to *Shabbat*. Rav Nachman rules: *lo shanu ela b'oso gag* — the permission to lower fruit was only taught with respect to the same roof; *aval migag legag lo* — but one may not transport fruit from one rooftop to another rooftop and then lower it through the other skylight. This constitutes excessive *tircha* or *uvda d'chol*, or both.

### Tania: Migag Legag

A *braita* confirms this: *ein metaltelin migag legag* — one may not move produce from one rooftop to another, *afilu kshe-gagoseihen shavin* — even when the two roofs are level with one another and no climbing or descending is required. The mere act of crossing from one rooftop to another, even with minimal effort, already exceeds the permitted scope.

### Shabbat Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם מַאי? (כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן שַׁבָּת דַּחֲמִירָא, אוֹ דִלְמָא:) הָכָא הוּא דְּאָסוּר — מִשּׁוּם יוֹם טוֹב דְּקִיל, וְאָתֵי לְזַלְזוֹלֵי בֵּיהּ, אֲבָל שַׁבָּת דַּחֲמִירָא, וְלָא אָתֵי לְזַלְזוֹלֵי בַּהּ — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The question arises: **There,** in the case of moving sacks on Shabbat for guests or for study, **what is** the *halakha*? May the sacks be moved from one roof or house to another for this purpose? Perhaps **all the more so** they may not be moved on **Shabbat,** because Shabbat is more **severe** than a Festival? **Or perhaps** it can be argued to the contrary: **It is here,** with regard to a Festival, **that** it is **prohibited** to transfer from one roof to another, **because a Festival** is regarded **lightly** by people **and they might** consequently **come to belittle it; but** on **Shabbat, which is severe** in people's eyes **and** so **they will not come to belittle it, it is acceptable** to transfer even from one house to another.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִלְמָא: מָה הָכָא דְּאִיכָּא הֶפְסֵד פֵּירוֹת — אָמְרַתְּ לָא, הָתָם דְּלֵיכָּא הֶפְסֵד פֵּירוֹת — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps** it may be argued: **If here,** in the case of clearing produce from the roof, **when there is** the issue of the **loss of** the **produce, you say** he may **not** transfer from one roof to another, then **there,** in the case of moving sacks on Shabbat for guests or study, **when there is no** issue of **loss of produce,** is it **not all the more so** prohibited?

The Gemara asks: *hatam mai* — what would be the ruling in the analogous *Shabbat* scenario, where instead of lowering fruit from a roof, one would need to transport grain some distance from one shed to another? The Gemara then presents competing arguments in the now-familiar structure. One argument: *hacha hu d'asar mishum Yom Tov d'kal v'asi l'zalzulei beih* — it is specifically here on *Yom Tov* that we impose extra restrictions, since *Yom Tov*'s lighter character means people may come to treat it disrespectfully; *aval Shabbos d'chamira leih* — but *Shabbat*, which is weightier, does not require as many protective fences, and such extra effort might well be permitted. The counter-argument: *mah hacha d'ika hefsed peiros amarta lo* — since there is a concern for loss of produce here and even so the restriction applies, then *hatam d'leika hefsed peiros lo kol she-ken* — where there is no such loss involved, the restriction should apply all the more. *Teiku* — the question remains unresolved.

The broader pattern becomes clear: in every case, there are arguments to say *Shabbat* should be more lenient (it's weightier, so people are more careful) and arguments to say *Yom Tov* should be more lenient (the loss of produce justifies latitude). Neither consistently prevails.

## No Ropes or Ladders

### Braita: Rope Ban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא (תְּנַן): לֹא יְשַׁלְשְׁלֵם בְּחֶבֶל בְּחַלּוֹנוֹת, וְלֹא יוֹרִידֵם דֶּרֶךְ סוּלָּמוֹת, הָתָם מַאי? הָכָא בְּיוֹם טוֹב הוּא דְּאָסוּר — דְּלֵיכָּא בִּטּוּל בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ, אֲבָל שַׁבָּת, דְּאִיכָּא בִּטּוּל בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents yet another dilemma: **Here,** with regard to bringing produce into one's house from the roof, **we learned** in a *baraita*: If there is no skylight from the roof to the house, necessitating another method of moving the produce out of the rain, **he may not lower them by** means of **a rope through the windows, nor may he take them down by way of ladders. There,** with regard to moving sacks on Shabbat, **what is** the *halakha*? May they be moved by ropes or using a ladder? Perhaps it is only **here,** in the case of moving produce out of the rain **on a Festival, that** it is **prohibited,** because produce left on a roof **does not** entail **suspension of** a mitzva such as study in **the study hall; but** on **Shabbat, when there is** the possibility that leaving the sacks in their current location will lead to **suspension of** study in **the study hall, it is acceptable** to remove them even via windows and ladders.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִלְמָא: הָכָא דְּאִיכָּא הֶפְסֵד פֵּירוֹת — אָמְרַתְּ לָא, הָתָם דְּלֵיכָּא הֶפְסֵד פֵּירוֹת — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן. תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps** it can be argued to the contrary: **Here,** in the case of the produce on the roof, **when there is** the issue of the **loss of** the **produce, you say** it is **not** permitted. **There,** in the case of clearing out sacks on Shabbat, **where there is no** issue of the **loss of produce,** should it **not all the more so** be prohibited to lower them via windows and ladders? No resolution was found, so the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

A *braita* further specifies the limitations on how fruit may be lowered: *lo yeshalshelem b'chevel b'chalonos v'lo yoridem derech sulamos* — one may not lower fruit by means of a rope through a window, nor may one bring it down by way of a ladder.

### Direct Drop Only

The only permitted method is, in effect, dropping it directly through the skylight. If the roof is enclosed by a wall with a window, and one must lift the produce and lower it through the window — that is prohibited. Similarly, bringing it down via a ladder is forbidden. The reason, as with the roof-to-roof restriction, is either excessive *tircha* in Rashi's reading, or the broader concern of *uvda d'chol* — the entire operation begins to resemble ordinary weekday labor. The *mishnah*'s permission is thus narrowly confined.

The Gemara again poses the question *hatam mai* — what would the *Shabbat* equivalent be? — and again works through the competing arguments. On *Yom Tov*, one might restrict the method because *d'leika bittul beit HaMidrash* — there is no pressing reason such as Torah study — while on *Shabbat*, where *bittul beit HaMidrash* does apply, the more cumbersome method might be permitted. Or one might argue the reverse: *hacha ika hefsed peiros* — here, the concern of produce loss is present, and even so the restriction holds; *hatam d'leika hefsed peiros lo kol she-ken* — where there is no such concern, the restriction should apply with even greater force. Again: *teiku*.

## Covering Fruit

### Fruit Protection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְכַסִּים אֶת הַפֵּירוֹת. אָמַר עוּלָּא: וַאֲפִילּוּ אַוֵּירָא דְלִבְנֵי. רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר: פֵּירוֹת הָרְאוּיִן. וְאַזְדָּא רַבִּי יִצְחָק לְטַעְמֵיהּ. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: אֵין כְּלִי נִיטָּל אֶלָּא לְדָבָר הַנִּיטָּל בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the mishna: **And one may cover produce** with cloths to prevent damage due to a leak. **Ulla said: And even a row of bricks** that might be ruined by the rain may be covered to prevent damage. Although the *halakha* in the mishna mentions produce, it is not limited to that case, but extends to any item liable to be spoiled. **Rabbi Yitzḥak said:** It applies only to an item like **produce, which is fit** for use on the Festival, but not to items such as bricks, which are designated for building and are not fit for use on the Festival. The Gemara comments: **And Rabbi Yitzḥak follows** his line of **reasoning** in this regard, **as Rabbi Yitzḥak said: A vessel,** even if it is of the type that may be handled on Shabbat, **may be handled** on Shabbat **only** if it is going to be used **for something that may** itself **be handled on Shabbat,** but not for the sake of set-aside [*muktze*] objects. Since the bricks are *muktze*, one may not handle cloths to cover the bricks.

The *mishnah* also permits covering fruit when rain threatens. The justification is *hefsed* — the fruit, typically grain or other produce, will begin to rot if left exposed. This is permitted even though it is not an act of *ochel nefesh* and even though it involves a degree of effort.

### Ulla: Bricks Too

Ulla extends this ruling: *v'afilu avira d'livnei* — one may even cover bricks stacked for construction purposes. Rashi explains this refers to bricks that are set aside for building and are therefore *muktzah*. The *chiddush* is that despite their status as *muktzah*, one may cover them to protect them from rain, since rain will damage them and create delay and wasted effort when they need to dry.

### Reb Yitzchak: No Muktzah

Rabbi Yitzchak disagrees: *peiros haru'uyin* — one may only cover items that are themselves fit and permitted to be handled. One may not expend effort — by moving a vessel or a tarp — for the sake of something that is itself *muktzah* and cannot be handled. The Gemara notes that this ruling is consistent with Rabbi Yitzchak's general principle: *amar Rabbi Yitzchak ein kli nital ela l'davar hanital b'Shabbos* — a vessel may only be moved for the sake of something that is itself permitted to be moved on *Shabbat*. The *muktzah* status of the object in question thus extends, in Rabbi Yitzchak's view, to prohibit even indirect acts taken for its benefit.

## Muktzah Movement

### Food Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: מְכַסִּים אֶת הַפֵּירוֹת בְּכֵלִים. פֵּירוֹת — אִין, אַוֵּירָא דְלִבְנֵי — לָא! הוּא הַדִּין דַּאֲפִילּוּ אַוֵּירָא דְלִבְנֵי, וְאַיְּידֵי דִּתְנָא רֵישָׁא מַשִּׁילִין פֵּירוֹת — תְּנָא סֵיפָא נָמֵי מְכַסִּים אֶת הַפֵּירוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to find a proof for this view: **We learned** in the mishna: **One may cover produce with cloths,** which seems to imply: **Produce, yes,** because it may be handled on the Festival, but *muktze* items such as **a row of bricks, no.** The Gemara rejects this argument: This is no proof, as it is possible that **the same is true even** for **a row of bricks,** i.e., that they may be covered. But **since** the *tanna* **taught in the first clause** of the mishna: **One may lower produce,** and there it is referring specifically to produce, as bricks may not be handled at all and surely not lowered from the roof, **he taught also in the latter clause: One may cover produce.** The example of produce was chosen to parallel the first clause in the mishna, not in order to imply exclusion of bricks.

The Gemara attempts to resolve this dispute by scrutinizing the *mishnah*'s language. The *mishnah* says *mechassin es ha'peiros b'keilim* — one covers the fruit with vessels. The inference is attempted: since the *mishnah* specifically mentions *peiros* — fruit — perhaps this is intentional, indicating that only non-*muktzah* items may be protected, but *muktzah* objects like bricks (*arraiya d'livnei*) may not. The Gemara deflects this: *hu hadin afilu arraiya d'livnei* — the same rule applies even for covering bricks. The *mishnah* mentioned fruit only because the earlier clause dealt with fruit — *mashilin peiros* — and so the later clause followed suit. The example of fruit is not restrictive; it is simply contextually parallel.

### Tevel Wine & Oil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: וְכֵן כַּדֵּי יַיִן וְכֵן כַּדֵּי שֶׁמֶן! הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ כַּדֵּי יַיִן וְכַדֵּי שֶׁמֶן דְּהֶתֵּירָא, הָא תְּנָא לֵיהּ רֵישָׁא פֵּירוֹת? כַּדֵּי יַיִן וְכַדֵּי שֶׁמֶן אִצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: לְהֶפְסֵד מְרוּבֶּה — חָשְׁשׁוּ, לְהֶפְסֵד מוּעָט — לֹא חָשְׁשׁוּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers a different proof. **We learned** in the mishna: **And similarly** one may cover **jugs of wine and jugs of oil** due to a leak in the ceiling. This choice of examples seems to indicate that one may cover only things that are fit for use on the Festival, as opposed to objects such as bricks, which are *muktze*. The Gemara rejects this proof: **With what are we dealing here? With** jugs that contain wine and oil **that are untithed,** which are not fit for Festival use and are therefore *muktze*. And the same would be true for bricks. The Gemara goes further: **So, too, it is** in fact more **reasonable** that this is the case, **as if it enters your mind** that the mishna is referring to **jugs of wine and jugs of oil** containing **permitted** liquids, **didn't** the *tanna* already **teach in the first clause** of this part of the mishna that it is permitted to cover **produce?** What new information would be added by specifying jugs as well? It is possible that the mishna is referring specifically to jugs containing permitted liquids. Nevertheless, **it was necessary for** the *tanna* to add the example of **jugs of wine and jugs of oil,** as **it could enter your mind to say** that the Sages **were concerned over a substantial loss,** such as of produce, which can be ruined by drops of rain that leak on it. But **with regard to a minor loss,** such as drops of rain falling into a wine jug or an oil jug, **they were not concerned,** and they did not permit covering them. The mishna therefore **teaches us** that those may be covered as well.

The *mishnah* continues: *v'chein kadi yayin v'kadi shemen* — and similarly one may cover jugs of wine and oil. The Gemara asks: why give a second example of food when we already have fruit? The Gemara answers: *hacha b'mai askinan b'tevel* — the wine and oil here are *tevel*, produce from which *terumos u'ma'asros* have not yet been separated, and which is therefore *muktzah*. As Tosafot notes, while separating *terumos u'ma'asros* retroactively removes the *muktzah* status, the wine and oil remain *muktzah* for the time being.

### Small Loss OK

The Gemara then entertains a different reading: perhaps the second example of wine and oil is not about *tevel* and *muktzah* at all, but rather comes to teach that even a minor loss — *hefsed mu'at* — justifies covering. One might have thought: *l'hefsed merubeh chasshinan* — we are concerned about significant spoilage (like grain rotting in the rain), but *l'hefsed mu'at lo chasshinan* — for a minor loss, such as some water seeping into a barrel of wine, we are not concerned. The *mishnah* therefore teaches that even a modest loss suffices to permit the protective action. The Gemara leaves both readings on the table — the *tevel* reading and the *hefsed mu'at* reading — with neither fully dispositive.

The underlying question of whether one may move objects on behalf of *muktzah* items — whether the *mishnah*'s food-based examples reflect a principled limitation or are merely contextually determined — thus remains open as the Gemara turns to examine a series of Tannaitic parallels.

## Shabbat Parallels

### Pot Under Drip

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: נוֹתְנִין כְּלִי תַּחַת הַדֶּלֶף בְּשַׁבָּת. בְּדֶלֶף הָרָאוּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises objections against Rabbi Yitzḥak's view: **We learned** in the mishna: **One may place a vessel beneath a leak on Shabbat.** It is permitted, then, to bring a bucket for the purpose of containing the water leaking into the house, although that water is ostensibly not fit for drinking and is therefore *muktze*. The Gemara rejects this objection: The case in the mishna is of **leakage** of water **that** is in fact **fit** to be drunk, at least by animals, and is consequently fit for Festival use.

The Gemara brings a *mishnah* in *Shabbat*: *nosnim kli tachat ha'delef b'Shabbat* — one may place a vessel under a roof drip on *Shabbat*. A roof drip is presumably dirty water that would be *muktzah*, yet we are placing a pot there for its sake. One might counter that the motive is protecting the floor, not collecting the water — but the Gemara treats this as equivalent to moving something for the sake of *muktzah*. The Gemara therefore limits this case: *b'delef ha'rauy* — we are speaking of a drip that is fit for use, such as clean water coming off the roof, which is not *muktzah*.

### Mat Over Bricks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: פּוֹרְסִין מַחְצֶלֶת עַל גַּבֵּי לְבֵנִים בְּשַׁבָּת! דְּאִיַּיתּוּר מִבִּנְיָנָא, דַּחֲזֵי לְמִזְגֵּא עֲלַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** another objection from a *baraita*: **One may spread a mat over bricks on Shabbat** to protect against rain. The *baraita* explicitly permits covering bricks, which Rabbi Yitzḥak prohibited. The Gemara rejects this argument: This *baraita* is referring to bricks **that were left over from building** and are no longer designated for use in building, and **which are** consequently **fit for** use on the Festival by **sitting on** them.

A *braita* states: *porsin machatzel al gabei l'veinim b'Shabbat* — one may spread a mat over bricks on *Shabbat*. Prima facie this is a clear case of protecting *muktzah* bricks. The Gemara limits this as well: *d'yitmi mibinyana d'chazi l'mizgalaya* — these are leftover bricks from a construction project that is complete, and they are fit to be sat upon. Bricks that are no longer designated for active construction use, and which by their context are understood as objects people would casually use for sitting, are not *muktzah*. A further *braita* mentions spreading a mat over stones (*avanim*) on *Shabbat*, and again the Gemara limits this: *b'avanim mkurzalot d'chazyun l'beit ha'kisei* — these are smoothed stones suitable for use in place of toilet paper, and therefore not *muktzah*.

### Hive Protection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: פּוֹרְסִין מַחְצֶלֶת עַל גַּבֵּי כַּוֶּרֶת דְּבוֹרִים בְּשַׁבָּת, בַּחַמָּה — מִפְּנֵי הַחַמָּה, וּבַגְּשָׁמִים — מִפְּנֵי הַגְּשָׁמִים, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִתְכַּוֵּין לָצוּד! הָתָם נָמֵי, דְּאִיכָּא דְּבַשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** an objection from a different source. **One may spread a mat over a beehive on Shabbat** to protect it from the elements, **in the sun due to the sun, and in the rain due to the rain, provided he does not have the intent to trap** the bees inside by covering the hive, as trapping is prohibited on Shabbat. A beehive and its bees are not fit for Shabbat use, yet it is permitted to handle a mat in order to cover the hive. The Gemara rejects this: **There, too,** the reference is to an item that is fit for Shabbat use, as it is discussing a hive **when there is honey** in it, which can be eaten on Shabbat. It is therefore permitted to handle the mat for the sake of the honey.

A *braita* teaches: *porsin machatzel al gabei kiveret devorim b'Shabbat* — one may spread a mat over a beehive on *Shabbat*, both to shield it from sun and from rain. The Gemara observes that at this point the cumulative weight of the evidence is suggestive: the *braita* offers a beehive, bricks, and stones — all of which, on their face, are *muktzah*. In each case the Gemara finds a way to reclassify them as not truly *muktzah*, but the sheer breadth of these cases seems to indicate that one may indeed move things to protect *muktzah* objects. The Gemara nevertheless continues to press the "special case" approach.

## Beehive Intent

### Honey Designation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב עוּקְבָא מִמֵּישָׁן לְרַב אָשֵׁי: הָתִינַח בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה, דְּאִיכָּא דְּבַשׁ. בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְאוֹתָן שְׁתֵּי חַלּוֹת. אוֹתָן שְׁתֵּי חַלּוֹת מוּקְצוֹת הֵן! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — שֶׁחִשֵּׁב עֲלֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ukva from Meishan said to Rav Ashi:** This explanation works out **well with** regard to **the summer, when there is honey,** but **in the rainy season,** when there is no honey in beehives, **what can be said?** The *baraita* explicitly mentioned the two phrases in the sun and in the rain. The Gemara answers: This *halakha* **is necessary only for those two honeycombs** left in the beehive in the winter to sustain the bees. The Gemara questions this: **Are those two honeycombs** not ***muktze*,** as they have clearly been left for the sake of the bees, and not to be used by humans? The Gemara replies: **With what** case **are we dealing here?** This is a case **when** the beekeeper **had in mind** before the Festival that he was going to take them from the bees and eat **them** himself.

The *braita* adds the qualification *u'vilvad shelo yitkaven latzud* — provided one does not intend to trap the bees. The Gemara addresses the beehive's *muktzah* status: *hatam nami d'ika devash* — there is honey inside, and one is effectively moving the mat to protect the honey, which is not *muktzah*. This prompts the challenge from Mar Ukva of Meishan to Rav Ashi: *hateneich biyemot ha'chama d'ika devash* — this works in summer when there is honey; *biyemot ha'geshamim mai ikka l'meimar* — but the *braita* also speaks of protection from rain, which is a wintertime concern, and in winter there is no honey. The Gemara responds: *lo nitzrecha ela l'otan shetei chalot* — even in winter, two combs of honey are left in the hive to feed the bees through the cold months, and it is those combs one is protecting.

Mar Ukva presses further: *otan shetei chalot muktzot hen* — those two combs are themselves *muktzah*, since the beekeeper deliberately leaves them so the bees will survive and the business will continue. The Gemara answers: *hacha b'mai askinan she'chishev aleihem* — this is speaking of a case where the beekeeper designated the combs before *Shabbat*, indicating he would make use of them if necessary, thereby removing their *muktzah* status.

### Trapping Worry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לֹא חִשֵּׁב עֲלֵיהֶם מַאי — אָסוּר? אַדְּתָנֵי וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִתְכַּוֵּין לָצוּד, לִפְלוֹג וְלִתְנֵי בְּדִידַהּ: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — שֶׁחִשֵּׁב עֲלֵיהֶן, אֲבָל לֹא חִשֵּׁב עֲלֵיהֶם — אָסוּר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection to this interpretation. **But if he did not have in mind** to take **them** for himself, **what** would be the *halakha*? Wouldn't it be **prohibited** to spread a mat over the hive? If so, when the *baraita* goes on to specify that sometimes it is prohibited to cover the hive, **rather than teaching: As long as he does not have the intent to trap** the bees, introducing a totally new factor into the discussion, **let it make a distinction within the case itself** by saying: **In what** case **is this statement said,** that the beehive may be covered? **When he had in mind** beforehand to take the honeycombs; **but** if **he did not have in mind** to take **them, it is prohibited.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁחִשֵּׁב עֲלֵיהֶן — וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִתְכַּוֵּין לָצוּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **This is what** the *tanna* **is saying: Even if he had in mind** to take the honeycombs, so that there is no problem of the hive's being *muktze*, it is still permitted to cover it **provided he does not have intent to trap** the bees.

The Gemara then asks: *v'i lo chishev aleihem mai* — and what if he had not designated them? *Assur* — it would be forbidden, since they would be *muktzah*. This being so, the *braita* should have made its limiting case the *lack of prior designation* rather than the issue of intent to trap. *Lifrosh v'litni bedidi* — it should have simply said: when applies, and when does it not?

The Gemara responds: *hachi ka'amar* — what the *braita* means to say is that even when designation has been made and the honey is not *muktzah*, there remains a further condition that can invalidate the act: *af al pi she'chishev aleihem*, even when he designated them, *u'vilvad she'lo yitkaven latzud* — he must not cover the hive in a way that traps the bees.

### Not Intent-Based

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתָּא — כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאִית לֵיהּ מוּקְצֶה? אֵימָא סֵיפָא: וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִתְכַּוֵּין לָצוּד — אֲתָאן לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר: דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּוֵּין מוּתָּר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a further objection against this interpretation of the *baraita*. **In what** manner **did you establish** and explain this *baraita*? **In accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who,** in disagreement with Rabbi Shimon, **holds** that the *halakhot* of ***muktze*** apply. But now **say the latter clause** of the *baraita*: **Provided he does not have the intent to trap.** This indicates that even though the bees may be trapped in the process of covering, it is permitted if this was not his intention. If so, **we have come to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** who, in disagreement with Rabbi Yehuda, **said: An unintentional act is permitted** even though it leads inadvertently to a prohibited result. This interpretation of the *baraita* is internally conflicted, half in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda and half in accordance with Rabbi Shimon.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? וְהָא אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בִּפְסִיק רֵישֵׁיהּ וְלָא יְמוּת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument. **And** how can **you understand** that the *baraita* follows the view **of Rabbi Shimon** at all? **But didn't Abaye and Rava both say: Rabbi Shimon concedes** that even an unintentional act is prohibited **in** a case of: **Cut off its head and will it not die?** In this case the person covering the hive with a mat inevitably traps the bees, even if he does not have intent to do so, and this act should be prohibited even by Rabbi Shimon.

The Gemara now scrutinizes this last clause carefully. The phrase *shelo yitkaven* — that he not *intend* to trap — suggests that intent is the operative factor. But this presents a difficulty: Abaye and Rava both rule *modeh Rabbi Shimon b'psik reisha v'lo yamut* — even Rabbi Shimon, who permits unintended acts (*davar she'eino miskavein*), agrees that when a prohibited consequence is inevitable, intent is irrelevant. If spreading the mat will definitely trap the bees, why should lack of intent make any difference?

The Gemara therefore reframes the clause. *L'olam kullah Rabbi Yehuda hi* — the entire *braita* follows Rabbi Yehuda, who holds of *muktzah*, and the "intent" language is not about mental state but about physical action. *D'hacha b'mai askinan d'is bei kavei* — the case involves a hive that has small openings through which bees can escape. Therefore it is not a true case of *tzeida* — trapping — at all. *Ella eima u'vilvad shelo ya'asenah metzudah* — the clause should be understood as: do not cover it in a way that actually seals them in. The prohibition is not on intending to trap, but on covering in a manner that results in genuine entrapment. Leave them an escape route.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: מִי קָתָנֵי בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה וּבִימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים? בַּחַמָּה מִפְּנֵי הַחַמָּה וּבַגְּשָׁמִים מִפְּנֵי הַגְּשָׁמִים קָתָנֵי — בְּיוֹמֵי נִיסָן וּבְיוֹמֵי תִשְׁרֵי, דְּאִיכָּא חַמָּה, וְאִיכָּא גְּשָׁמִים, וְאִיכָּא דְּבַשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** a different elucidation: **Is it taught** in the *baraita*: **In the summer, and: In the rainy season?** No, **it is taught: In the sun due to the sun and in the rain due to the rain.** The *baraita* speaks not of the summer and the rainy season, but **of the** spring **days of Nisan and the** autumn **days of Tishrei, when there is** sometimes **sun and there is** sometimes **rain, and** when **there is** also **honey** in the hive. It is possible, then, that the *baraita* permits covering the hive during these seasons because of the honey that is in it, as initially proposed.

The Gemara anticipates a further objection: *peshita* — this is obvious. If you actually trap them, you have clearly violated the prohibition regardless of intent. The answer is that the *braita* comes to rule on a borderline case: bees are not ordinarily the subject of trapping (*shelo b'mino nitzod*) — the prohibition of *tzeida* applies most clearly to animals like deer. Since the prohibition as applied to bees is only *d'rabbanan*, one might have thought that since one does not intend to trap and the bees are not a primary target of trapping, there is room for leniency. The *braita* teaches that even in this case, if the mat is spread in a way that definitively traps them, it is forbidden.

Tosafot adds a nuance: even if the *braita* is read as being about Rabbi Yehuda, there may still be a residual difference between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon in how to define entrapment in borderline cases. Rabbi Yehuda, who does not consider intent relevant, might count as "trapped" a situation where escape is theoretically possible but highly unlikely. Rabbi Shimon, who is more permissive regarding unintended consequences, might hold that unless escape is truly impossible (*psik reisha*), the bees are not considered trapped. The Gemara's resolution, however, focuses on the physical configuration: the instruction is to ensure the hive has openings, making this a question of what one *does*, not what one *intends*.

Rav Ashi revisits the earlier problem about summer versus winter. He notes that the *braita* does not say "in summer" and "in winter" — it says *bachama mipnei ha'chama u'vageshamim mipnei ha'geshamim*: cover it because of the sun's heat, cover it because of rain. Rain can fall in summer; sun can be a problem in winter. *Tani bimei Nisan u'vimei Tishrei* — we may be speaking of spring or fall, when both sunshine and rain are present and when honey is available in the hive. The dilemma about the honey-free winter is therefore resolved by recognizing that the two weather conditions need not correspond to the two seasons.

The Gemara's bottom line regarding the core *muktzah* question is this: our *mishnah*'s exclusive use of food examples seems to restrict the allowance to non-*muktzah* objects. Yet every Tannaitic source in *Shabbat* — the roof drip, the bricks, the stones, the beehive — appears on its face to permit covering or protecting *muktzah* objects. In each case the Gemara successfully reclassifies the object as non-*muktzah* through a special interpretive move. One could have deflected all of these sources at once by attributing them all to Rabbi Shimon, who does not accept *muktzah* in the same scope as Rabbi Yehuda. The Gemara's refusal to do so — its insistence on reading them all as consistent with Rabbi Yehuda — implicitly strengthens the principle that moving something for the sake of *muktzah* is indeed forbidden, even as the *peshat* of those same sources continues to pull in the opposite direction.

## Geraf Shel Rei

### Bed Strategy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּי רִחְיָא דְּאַבָּיֵי דְּלוּף. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּה, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל עַיְּילֵיהּ לְפוּרְיָךְ לְהָתָם, דְּלֶהֱוֵי כִּגְרָף שֶׁל רְעִי, וְאַפְּקֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Abaye's millhouse** once **developed a leak** on Shabbat. Abaye was concerned about the potential damage to the millstones, which were made partly of clay and which would become ruined from the leaking water, and he did not have enough buckets to catch all the water without emptying and refilling them. But the water was unfit for drinking and was therefore *muktze* and could not be removed. Abaye **came before Rabba** to ask him how to proceed. Rabba **said to him: Go** and **bring your bed into** the millhouse, **so that** the dirty water **will be** considered **like a container of excrement,** which, despite being *muktze*, may be removed from one's presence due to its repulsive nature, **and** then **remove** the water.

The Gemara now takes a brief turn to a narrative involving Abaye's mill. During a rain, the millstones were getting wet, and Abaye came before Rava to ask what could be done. Rava's advice: *zil ayeilei lepurayich lehasam* — go bring your bed into the mill room. *D'leihavei k'geraf shel re'i* — this will render the millstone's situation equivalent to a *geraf shel re'i*, a chamber pot: the rain dripping onto the millstone, in a room where you are now sleeping, becomes intolerable and disgusting to be around, giving you grounds to remove the millstone even though it is effectively *muktzah*.

### Abaye Hesitant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב אַבָּיֵי וְקָא קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ: וְכִי עוֹשִׂין גְּרָף שֶׁל רְעִי לְכַתְּחִלָּה? אַדְּהָכִי נְפַל בֵּי רִחְיָא דְאַבָּיֵי. אָמַר: תֵּיתֵי לִי דַּעֲבַרִי אַדְּמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye sat** and examined the matter **and posed a difficulty: And may one initiate** a situation of **a container of excrement,** i.e., may one intentionally place any repulsive matter into a situation which will bother him and will then have to be removed, ***ab initio*? In the meantime,** as he was deliberating the issue, **Abaye's millhouse collapsed. He said: I had** this **coming to me for having gone against the words of my master,** Rabba, by not following his ruling unquestioningly.

Abaye did not act on this *heter*. The reason: *v'chi odin geraf shel re'i lechatchila* — may one deliberately construct a *geraf shel re'i* situation in order to create the pretext for removing *muktzah*? To Abaye, this felt like too clever a maneuver — engineering the circumstances specifically so as to manufacture an excuse for a leniency. While he sat deliberating, the mill collapsed. He then lamented: *teifach lei d'avri admar* — I deserve this, for I did not follow my teacher's ruling.

Tosafot notes that while Abaye's scruple is understandable, Rava's position may have been that the case was already borderline legitimate: the situation was already unpleasant and damaging, and Abaye was not creating a *geraf shel re'i* from nothing — he was merely placing himself within proximity of an already-developing problem. This contextual proximity, rather than artificial construction, is what lends the leniency its basis.

### Disgusting Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: גְּרָף שֶׁל רְעִי וְעָבִיט שֶׁל מֵימֵי רַגְלַיִם — מוּתָּר לְהוֹצִיאָן לָאַשְׁפָּה, וּכְשֶׁהוּא מַחְזִירוֹ — נוֹתֵן בּוֹ מַיִם וּמַחְזִירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said:** With regard to **a container of excrement and a container of urine, it is permitted to remove them** on Shabbat **to a garbage heap. And when he returns** the container to the house **he must place water in it** first **and** then **return it,** for it is prohibited to carry these containers alone, as their foul odor makes them *muktze* due to their repulsive nature.

The principle of *geraf shel rei* — that one may move a disgusting item even though it is *muktzah* — is a recognized exception within the laws of *muktzah*. Its application is not limited to literal excrement; as the cases below illustrate, it extends to anything that is sufficiently revolting and whose presence in one's living space is intolerable.

### Dead Rat Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְבוּר מִינַּהּ: גְּרָף שֶׁל רְעִי, אַגַּב מָנָא — אִין, בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ — לָא. תָּא שְׁמַע: דְּהָהוּא עַכְבַּרְתָּא דְּאִשְׁתְּכַח בֵּי אִסְפַּרְמָקֵי דְּרַב אָשֵׁי. אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב אָשֵׁי: נִקְטֻהּ בְּצוּצִיתֵהּ וְאַפְּקוּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Some Sages at first **understood from** the wording of Shmuel's statement that with regard to removing **a container of excrement on account of the vessel,** i.e., along with its vessel: **Yes,** this is permitted; but to remove the excrement **by itself,** without a vessel containing it: **No,** this is prohibited. The Gemara counters this conclusion with the following story: **Come** and **hear that a certain** dead **mouse was discovered in Rav Ashi's storeroom** for **spices** [***isparmekei***]. **Rav Ashi said to them: Take hold of it by its tail and remove it.** This shows that repulsive matter may be removed even directly.

Shmuel formalizes the principle: *geraf shel re'i v'avit shel mei raglayim mutar l'hotzi'an l'ashpah* — a chamber pot for excrement and a vessel for urine may be taken out to the refuse heap. The Gemara then asks about replacing the chamber pot after it has been emptied: *nosein bo mayim u'machziro* — one puts water into it, and may then return it with the water inside, making the act of carrying it back more defensible.

The Gemara initially considers whether the principle of *geraf shel re'i* only permits moving the disgusting item in conjunction with a vessel — *agav mana*, by way of the containing vessel — but not by directly handling the repulsive contents themselves. The doubt is then resolved. A story is cited: there was a dead rat or weasel found in the perfume room of Rav Ashi on *Shabbat*. Rav Ashi instructed those present: *naktuha b'danveteih v'afkuha* — take it by the tail and remove it. Here one is directly handling the carcass, not by way of a container. This establishes that the *geraf shel rei* allowance extends even to direct handling, provided the object is sufficiently revolting and its presence intolerable in one's space. The juxtaposition of a putrefying carcass in a perfume room captures the principle well: the intolerable degradation of one's environment is what triggers the exception.

## Shvut Mishna

### Equal on Both

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם שְׁבוּת, מִשּׁוּם רְשׁוּת, מִשּׁוּם מִצְוָה, בְּשַׁבָּת — חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו בְּיוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Any act for which one is liable due to a rabbinic decree** made to enhance the character of Shabbat as a day of **rest [*shevut*];** or if it is notable **because it is optional,** i.e., it involves an aspect of a mitzva but is not a complete mitzva; or if it is notable **because it is** a full-fledged **mitzva,** if it is prohibited **on Shabbat, one is liable for it on a Festival** as well.

The Gemara now arrives at a *mishnah* that introduces several important conceptual categories. *Kol she'chayavin alav mishum shevus, mishum reshus, o mishum mitzvah — b'Shabbos chayavin alav b'Yom Tov*. Anything that is rabbinically prohibited under the heading of *shevus*, and equally anything that falls under *reshus* or *mitzvah* (as explained below), is equally forbidden on both *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*. Two principles are embedded here: first, this entire category of rabbinic enactments applies identically across both holy days; second, the presence of some justification — whether *reshus* (discretionary benefit) or *mitzvah* (commandment) — does not override the prohibition. The *mishnah* thus stands in interesting tension with the preceding discussion, which found considerable ambiguity about whether *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* track each other, and which recognized that *hefsed* and *bittul beit HaMidrash* could generate latitude for specific acts.

### Climbing & Riding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ הֵן מִשּׁוּם שְׁבוּת: לֹא עוֹלִין בָּאִילָן, וְלֹא רוֹכְבִין עַל גַּבֵּי בְּהֵמָה, וְלֹא שָׁטִין עַל פְּנֵי הַמַּיִם, וְלֹא מְטַפְּחִין וְלֹא מְסַפְּקִין וְלֹא מְרַקְּדִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And these are** the acts prohibited by the Sages **as *shevut*: One may not climb a tree** on Shabbat, **nor ride on an animal, nor swim in the water, nor clap** his hands together, **nor clap** his hand on the **thigh, nor dance.**

The *mishnah* enumerates the *shevus* category — pure rabbinic prohibitions with no countervailing justification: *lo olin b'ilan* — one does not climb a tree; *v'lo rochvin al gabei beheimah* — one does not ride an animal; *v'lo shatin al pnei hamayim* — one does not float or travel on water.

### Clapping & Dancing

The list continues: *v'lo metapchin, v'lo mesapkin, v'lo merakdin* — one does not clap hands, does not slap one's thigh, and does not dance. Rashi distinguishes the first two as two forms of clapping. All of these are included in the *shevus* category.

### Din & Kiddushin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ הֵן מִשּׁוּם רְשׁוּת: לֹא דָּנִין, וְלֹא מְקַדְּשִׁין, וְלֹא חוֹלְצִין, וְלֹא מְיַבְּמִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And the following** are acts that are prohibited on Shabbat and are notable **because they are optional,** i.e., which involve an aspect of a mitzva but are not complete mitzvot: **One may not judge, nor betroth** a woman, **nor perform *ḥalitza*,** which is done in lieu of levirate marriage, **nor perform levirate marriage.**

The *mishnah* then lists the *reshus* category — activities one might have thought had sufficient justification, yet which are nonetheless forbidden: *lo danin* — one does not convene a court; *v'lo mekadeshin* — one does not perform *kiddushin*; *v'lo choltzin* — one does not perform *chalitza*; *v'lo meyabmin* — one does not perform *yibum*. The Gemara will note a certain strangeness here: *kiddushin*, *chalitza*, and *yibum* would normally be categorized as *mitzvah* rather than *reshus*, and the Gemara will address this classification.

### Mitzvot Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ הֵן מִשּׁוּם מִצְוָה: לֹא מַקְדִּישִׁין, וְלֹא מַעֲרִיכִין, וְלֹא מַחֲרִימִין, וְלֹא מַגְבִּיהִין תְּרוּמָה וּמַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **And the following** are prohibited on Shabbat despite the fact that they are notable **because of the** full-fledged **mitzva** involved in them: **One may not consecrate, nor take a valuation vow** (see Leviticus 27), **nor consecrate** objects for use by the priests or the Temple, **nor separate *teruma* and tithes** from produce.

Finally, the *mitzvah* category: *lo makdishin* — one does not sanctify objects to the Temple; *lo maarichin* — one does not undertake an *erech* obligation; *lo machrimin* — one does not declare objects *cherem*; these represent three distinct forms of Temple-related dedications. *V'lo magbihin terumos u'ma'asros* — and one does not separate *terumos* and *ma'asros*.

The *mishnah* then concludes with a striking formulation: *kol eilu b'Yom Tov amru, kal v'chomer b'Shabbos* — all of these were taught with reference to *Yom Tov*, and how much more so do they apply on *Shabbat*. This is surprising, since the opening clause stated the converse: what is forbidden on *Shabbat* is also forbidden on *Yom Tov*. Here the original context appears to have been *Yom Tov*, and the application to *Shabbat* follows by *kal v'chomer*. The *masechta* is *Beitzah*, not *Shabbat*. The *mishnah* then closes with *ein bein Yom Tov l'Shabbos ella ochel nefesh bilvad* — the only difference between *Yom Tov* and *Shabbat* is acts directly related to eating. This final line is characteristic of the *ein bein* formulations found in the *mishnayot* of *Megillah* and is generally regarded as early. Whether this casts the entire *mishnah* as early, or whether these are two distinct components joined together, is a fascinating question in its own right.

It is also worth noting that the *reshus* and *mitzvah* categories function differently from the *shevus* category in a structural sense. The *shevus* items were identified as activities to avoid, and the prohibition was framed directly around those acts. The *reshut* and *mitzvah* items represent cases where a pre-existing rabbinic prohibition encounters a potentially overriding consideration — yet the *mishnah* rules that the override does not apply. This is distinct from the *geraf shel re'i* framework we encountered earlier, where an exceptional circumstance does succeed in overriding a rabbinic restriction.

## Bavli Reframe

### Tree & Animal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לֹא עוֹלִין בָּאִילָן — גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא יִתְלוֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara clarifies the reasons for each of these *halakhot*: **One may not climb a tree.** This is **a decree** that was made **lest one detach** branches or leaves as he climbs, thereby transgressing the prohibited labor of reaping.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא רוֹכְבִין עַל גַּבֵּי בְּהֵמָה — גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא יֵצֵא חוּץ לַתְּחוּם. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תְּחוּמִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא! אֶלָּא: גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא יַחְתּוֹךְ זְמוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Nor ride on an animal:** This is **a decree** that was made **lest one go beyond the** Shabbat **limit** on the animal. The Gemara asks: Can one then **learn from here** that the prohibition against venturing beyond **the** Shabbat **limits,** which applies also to Festivals, **is by Torah law?** If the prohibition with regard to the Shabbat limit were rabbinic, the Sages would not have reinforced it with the additional decree against riding an animal. It is known that this is a matter of dispute; in light of this explanation of the mishna it would be a proof that Shabbat boundaries are of Torah origin. **Rather,** give a different reason for the prohibition: It is **a decree** that was made **lest one cut off a branch** to use as a riding switch, and thereby perform the labor of reaping, which is prohibited by Torah law.

The Gemara provides reasons for each *shevus* prohibition in the form of a *gezeira*: *lo olin b'ilan, gezeira shema yitlosh* — the prohibition on climbing a tree is a safeguard lest one pull off a branch. *V'lo rochvin al gabei beheimah, gezeira shema yachtotz zmora* — riding an animal is prohibited lest one break off a branch to use as a switch to prod the animal.

A brief digression arises: one might infer from the formulation *gezeira shema yeitzei chutz l'tchum* — lest one ride beyond the *techum* — that *tchumin* are biblical, since one constructs a *gezeira* to protect a biblical violation. The Gemara deflects this by substituting the alternative *gezeira* about cutting a branch, thereby avoiding any inference about the biblical status of *tchumin*.

### Swimming & Music

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא שָׁטִין עַל פְּנֵי הַמַּיִם — גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא יַעֲשֶׂה חָבִית שֶׁל שַׁיָּיטִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Nor swim in the water:** This is **a decree** that was made **lest one make a swimmer's barrel,** i.e., an improvised flotation device used to teach beginners how to swim.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא מְטַפְּחִין וְלֹא מְסַפְּקִין וְלֹא מְרַקְּדִין — גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא יְתַקֵּן כְּלֵי שִׁיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Nor clap** one's hands together, **nor clap his hand on the thigh, nor dance:** All of these are prohibited due to **a decree** that was made **lest one fashion a musical instrument** to accompany his clapping or dancing.

### Gezeira Framework

This is a striking move by the *Bavli*, and it deserves careful attention. The Gemara has taken the category of *shevus* and reframed each of its constituent prohibitions as a *gezeira* — a protective fence around some other violation. In doing so, the *Bavli* departs from what is likely the original meaning of *shevus*. Riding an animal on *Shabbat*, floating on a river, clapping and jumping in a field — these do not feel like acts that require the explanation that one might eventually come to cut a branch or repair an instrument. The sense is rather that these activities are intrinsically problematic on *Shabbat*: they are, in the language developed throughout this tractate, *uvda d'chol* — they belong to the weekday world of commerce, transport, and work. Climbing trees was the activity of agricultural laborers. Traveling by animal or by raft was a primary means of transporting goods. Clapping and jumping in a field, as the *Tosefta* makes clear, was a method for scaring birds away from crops. These are paradigmatic work-related activities.

The concept of *shevus* is not, etymologically or conceptually, about *gezeirot*. *Shevus* points toward *Shabbat* — *shavat*, rest. It is about preserving the quality and experience of the day, about demarcating the sacred from the ordinary. Many scholars believe that in the *mishnah*, the *Tosefta*, and likely in the *Yerushalmi* as well, these prohibitions remain grounded in the intrinsic concern of *uvda d'chol*: they are things that are fundamentally out of place on *Shabbat*, and specific acts were identified and prohibited accordingly. Looking at the *reshus* and *mitzvah* categories in this *mishnah*, the same logic applies: convening a court, performing *kiddushin*, sanctifying objects to the Temple — these are not prohibited because one might thereby come to violate some other act. They are themselves activities of a certain character that belong to the weekday sphere.

### Gezeira Implications

The *Bavli*'s reframing of *shevus* as *gezeira* carries significant halakhic implications. On one hand, framing these prohibitions as *gezeirot* arguably devalues the independent weight of *uvda d'chol* as a concern. If climbing a tree is only prohibited because you might pull off a branch, it becomes harder to articulate why *uvda d'chol* in general should constrain activity on *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* — the concern seems to dissolve into a collection of specific technical safeguards rather than a general principle about the nature of the day.

On the other hand, the *gezeira* framework opens the door to contextual exceptions. Tosafot famously uses this logic to permit clapping and dancing in synagogue on *Shabbat*: since nowadays people are not capable of repairing musical instruments quickly or easily, the concern that animated the *gezeira* no longer applies, and therefore the *gezeira* itself does not operate. One can only reason this way if the prohibition is grounded in a *gezeira* — a safeguard against some external violation — rather than being an intrinsic expression of *shevus*. If the prohibition on clapping were understood as a direct statement that clapping is *uvda d'chol* — inherently incompatible with the sanctity of *Shabbat* — no contextual exception would be possible.

The same logic applies across the most familiar rabbinic restrictions governing *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*. The *Bavli* frames the prohibition on blowing *shofar* on *Shabbat*, on reading the *megillah* on *Shabbat*, on taking the *lulav* on *Shabbat*, all as *gezeirot shema yavi* — lest one come to carry in the public domain. This is not necessarily what those prohibitions were originally about. Blowing the *shofar*, for instance, could more naturally be understood as involving the creation of sound through a musical instrument — the sort of activity that disturbs the stillness and quiet that is central to the *Shabbat* experience. The *gezeira* framing, while providing important halakhic flexibility, displaces the more fundamental concern about what kind of day *Shabbat* is meant to be. Recognizing this tension — between the *Bavli*'s systematic *gezeira* framework and the earlier, more experientially-grounded understanding of *shevus* — is essential to understanding how the halakhic tradition developed, and why some of the categories we have spent this entire *daf* examining feel both precise and, in certain respects, elusive.
