# Sukkah 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Animal Wall Debate

### Recap & Framework

Today's daf is 24, and we pick up already halfway into 24a. To recall the discussion from the previous session: there was a debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda about whether one may use an animal as a wall of a *sukkah*. The *gemara* framed that debate in two ways. According to Abaye, the concern underlying Rabbi Meir's disqualification is that the animal might die. According to Rav Zeira, the concern is that the animal might run away — a form of *gezeirah*. In either case, the worry is that the *sukkah* wall would then shrink to less than *asarah tefachim*.

The *gemara* then challenged Abaye's framing: how could Abaye characterize the debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda as turning on the concern for an animal's death, when a separate *mishnah* appears to assign them reversed positions on exactly that question? The case in question involved the wife of a *kohen*: if the *kohen* traveled abroad, his wife may continue eating *terumah* on the assumption that he is still alive — suggesting no concern for his death. But if the *kohen* gave her a *get* with the stipulation "this shall take effect one minute before I die," she may not eat *terumah*, because every moment could be the moment before his death. Abaye sought to link this case to a *machlokes* between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda — but the positions turned out to be the reverse of what he needed for his reading of the animal wall debate.

### Grain & Breira

The *mishnah* Abaye cited to establish the debate involved taking *terumos u-ma'asros* from grain purchased from *Kutim* (Samaritans). Two points deserve clarification. First, the grain in question is treated as *vadai tevel* — not *safek*, not *demai*, but definitively untithed grain. Second, the scenario involves *bein hashmashos*, the twilight period which is a *safek Shabbos*. One is ordinarily forbidden to designate *terumos u-ma'asros* from *vadai tevel* during *bein hashmashos* — not because of a separate *gezeirah*, but because doing so constitutes *tikkun*, fixing the grain, which is impermissible on *Shabbos* or even during the *safek* period. However, the *mishnah* permits a roundabout approach: rather than directly designating which portion is *terumah* now, one takes the grain and declares that the *terumos u-ma'asros* will be designated after *Shabbos* — and that later designation retroactively applies to what was done now. This is the concept of *breira*: what one designates *Motzaei Shabbos* retroactively determines the identity of what was separated *bein hashmashos*.

Rabbi Meir permits this approach; Rabbi Yehuda does not. In Abaye's reading, this dispute reflects a concern about whether the wine flasks containing the wine will break before *Motzaei Shabbos* — if they break, there will be nothing to designate after *Shabbos*, and the earlier act will retroactively have been invalid. Thus Rabbi Yehuda, who forbids the practice, would seem to be the one concerned that the flask will break, while Rabbi Meir, who permits it, is unconcerned.

### Reconciling Views

This creates the apparent contradiction. In the animal wall case (as Abaye framed it), Rabbi Meir is concerned the animal will die and Rabbi Yehuda is not. But in the wine flask case, Rabbi Meir seems unconcerned about breakage while Rabbi Yehuda is concerned. The positions appear reversed.

The *gemara*'s resolution is to say *eippoch* — reverse the attribution. The concern about the *kohen*'s wife eating *terumah* is not mapped onto the wine flask debate, but rather onto the animal wall debate itself. The general principle animating the animal wall case — *Rabbi Meir chish l'miyuta*, Rabbi Meir is concerned about minority possibilities, and *Rabbi Yehuda lo chish l'miyuta*, Rabbi Yehuda is not — is the same principle at stake in the *kohen*'s wife scenario: are we concerned the *kohen* might die? As the *gemara* cites explicitly: *asa'o livehema dofen lesukkah, Rabbi Meir posel, Rabbi Yehuda machshir* — one who made an animal a wall of a *sukkah*: Rabbi Meir disqualifies, Rabbi Yehuda validates.

### Wine Flask Problem

Having established that mapping, the *gemara* must now account for the wine flask case. If Rabbi Meir is concerned about death — the animal dying, the *kohen* dying — why is he not concerned about the flask breaking? And if Rabbi Yehuda is unconcerned about death, why does he appear concerned about the flask?

On the first question, the *gemara* answers: *misa sh'chicha, bkiyat hanod lo sh'chicha* — death is common, a flask breaking is not. Moreover, *v'efshar d'matzei lei lishomer* — one can arrange for a guard to watch over the flask; no one can protect against death. So Rabbi Meir's concern about death is consistent with his lack of concern about flask breakage: they are simply not equivalent risks.

On the second question, the *gemara* resolves it more fundamentally. Rabbi Yehuda's objection to the wine flask scheme has nothing to do with flask breakage at all. *Ta'ama d'Rabbi Yehuda lav mishum d'chaish l'vkiyat hanod* — Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning is not that he fears the flask will break. *Ella mishum d'leis lei breira* — he simply does not accept the concept of *breira*. He rejects the entire mechanism by which a later designation can retroactively define a prior act. Death, broken flasks — none of that is his concern. His concern is purely conceptual: one cannot use *breira* to retroactively identify which wine was separated as *terumah*.

But then the *gemara* notes a difficulty. The *mishnah* itself records that Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Yosi, and Rabbi Shimon challenged Rabbi Meir: *"Iy atta modeh shema yibaka hanod v'nimtza zeh shoteh tevalim l'mafrea?"* — do you not concede that if the flask breaks, this person will retroactively have drunk *tevel*? And Rabbi Meir responded: *l'chsheyibaka* — I'll worry about it when it happens. This exchange seems to show that Rabbi Yehuda himself raises the concern of flask breakage, contradicting the *gemara*'s claim that he is indifferent to it.

The *gemara* resolves this: *Rabbi Yehuda hu d'ka'amar l'Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi Yehuda is arguing to Rabbi Meir on Rabbi Meir's own terms. *L'didi* — as far as I myself am concerned, *leis lei breira*, I reject *breira* entirely and this scheme is impermissible on that basis alone. But since *you*, Rabbi Meir, accept *breira* — at least concede that even on your own view you should be concerned the flask might break. Rabbi Meir replies that he is not worried about it. This is not Rabbi Yehuda expressing his own concern about flask breakage; it is a rhetorical argument directed at Rabbi Meir from within Rabbi Meir's own framework.

### Status Quo Analysis

With the wine flask issue resolved, the *gemara* turns to a further question about consistency: if Rabbi Yehuda is not concerned about death generally, how does one explain the well-known *mishnah* at the opening of *Yoma*, where Rabbi Yehuda rules that a replacement wife must be prepared for the *Kohen Gadol* — *shema tamus ishto*, lest his wife die? That would seem to show Rabbi Yehuda very much concerned about death.

The *gemara* answers: *mah lo asu b'chapara* — extraordinary precautions are taken for the *Yom Kippur* service. The *Kohen Gadol*'s situation is uniquely sensitive and the measures taken there are not representative of general halachic reasoning.

It is worth noting, however, that a different *gemara* elsewhere presents a more conceptually refined distinction between the two cases involving the *kohen*'s wife. The two scenarios differ not in how much we worry about death, but in the nature of the uncertainty involved. When the *kohen* travels abroad and the wife continues eating *terumah*, the question is: *shema met*, is he currently dead? For this we invoke the principle of status quo — he was alive when he left, we assume he remains alive. But when the *kohen* gives a *get* and stipulates that it takes effect one minute before his death, the question is not whether he is dead *now* — he is clearly alive now. The question is: *shema yamut*, might he die a minute from now? The fact that he is currently alive does not tell us what will happen in the next minute. Status quo establishes present facts; it cannot predict the future. This is a fundamentally different logical structure, and that *gemara* explains the two cases through this distinction rather than through a dispute about how worried we are about the probability of death. This distinction is not developed here in our *gemara*, which instead frames the question as a debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda.

### Tnai vs. Breira

The discussion of *breira* naturally raises the question of how it differs from *tnai* — a conditional act. The distinction is conceptually important, though the boundary between them is a subject of extensive treatment by the *Tosafot*.

A *tnai* is a condition on whether an act takes effect. All the constituent elements of the act are defined: this is the *get*, this is the woman to whom it is given, this is the *kesef kiddushin*. What is held in abeyance is only whether the act will become operative, depending on whether a specified condition is met. If the condition is met, the act takes effect — potentially retroactively, from the moment it was performed.

*Breira*, by contrast, is a situation in which the very objects constituting the act are not defined at the time of performance. The question of which wine is the *terumah* and which is *chulin* — the constituent subject-matter of the act of *hafrashat terumah* itself — is not yet determined. That is what *breira* resolves retroactively: not whether the act takes effect, but what the act was actually performed *on*. The later designation fills in the undefined object of the earlier act.

There are, it should be noted, one or two *gemaras* that appear to conflate the two categories, and significant discussion among the *Rishonim* about whether these categories are always cleanly separable.

## Ruach Analysis

### Shift in Framework

All of the foregoing — the extended analysis of who is concerned about death, who about running away, how the wine flask case fits in, how it maps onto the *kohen*'s wife — operated under Abaye's explanatory framework: Rabbi Meir disqualifies the animal wall because he is concerned it might die or run away. The *gemara* framed this explicitly as a rabbinic concern: *mide'oraisa mechitza ma'alya hi, verabbanan hu d'gazru bah* — biblically it is a perfectly valid *mechitza*, and the rabbinic disqualification is a protective decree.

The problem with this entire framework is that it can only ever produce *stringency*. If the concern is merely that the animal might die or run away — leaving you unwittingly without a valid wall — that concern justifies prohibiting the use of animals. It cannot justify treating the animal as having a *lesser* status than an ordinary inanimate object. Yet the *gemara* cites a *mishnah* that does exactly that, and in a direction that is more lenient than the norm: *Rabbi Yehuda metamei mishum golel* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that an animal used as such has the *tumah* of a *golel*, a gravestone slab; *veRabbi Meir metaher* — while Rabbi Meir holds it is *tahor*.

A *gezeirah* — a protective decree — cannot explain a leniency. If Rabbi Meir's concern were purely precautionary, he would simply prohibit using the animal; he would not go further and declare that in the *tumah* context it lacks the status an object in its position would ordinarily carry. There must be something more fundamental at stake in Rabbi Meir's position. The entire death-and-running-away framework is therefore set aside.

### Mechitza by Spirit

*Ella amar Rav Acha bar Yaakov* — Rav Acha bar Yaakov offers an entirely different explanation of Rabbi Meir's reasoning. *Kasavar Rabbi Meir: kol mechitza she'omedes beruach einah mechitza* — Rabbi Meir holds that any *mechitza* that stands only by virtue of *ruach*, the animating life-spirit, is not a valid *mechitza*.

This formulation is striking because the standard criterion for a *mechitza* has been the opposite: *kol mechitza she'einah yechola la'amod beruach metzuya einah mechitza* — any *mechitza* that cannot withstand an ordinary wind is not a valid *mechitza*. The same word *ruach* now appears in a very different role. Previously, *ruach* was the external force a wall must be able to resist. Here, *ruach* is the internal animating force that sustains the wall — and according to Rabbi Meir, a wall sustained in this way lacks halachic substance.

### Two Interpretations

The *gemara* records a second version of Rav Acha bar Yaakov's statement. *Ika d'amri*: some say his formulation was: *kol mechitza she'eina asuya biydei adam einah mechitza* — any *mechitza* not made by human hands is not a valid *mechitza*.

*Mai beinaihu* — what is the practical difference between these two formulations? *Ika beinaihu d'ukma b'nod tapuach* — the case of an inflated vessel, a large balloon, used as a wall. According to the first version — a *mechitza* standing by *ruach* is invalid — the balloon fails: it stands only because of the air inside it, which is *ruach*. According to the second version — the problem is that it is not made by human hands — the balloon is perfectly valid: it was made and inflated by human beings.

### Physical Substance

The concept underlying the first version is that the halachic status of a *mechitza* requires material substance — *mamash*. Air, and the animating life-spirit of a living creature, are both classified as *ruach*: something real in a physical sense, as air does have weight, yet experienced as non-material, invisible, intangible. For Rabbi Meir, a structure sustained by something of this non-physical character cannot constitute a *mechitza*, because what gives it its standing is not a genuinely material substance.

An analogy might be drawn to the concept of *mamash* in other areas of *halacha* — where, for instance, a substance that is imperceptible but lends form to an object may or may not be accorded halachic significance depending on context. Here, the claim is the inverse: what sustains this wall and gives it its shape is not a tangible physical substance, and therefore the wall does not have the halachic character of a *mechitza*.

### Golel & Tumah

The second formulation — requiring that a *mechitza* be *made by human hands* — is harder to sustain, precisely because Rabbi Meir does not appear to disqualify trees used as walls of a *sukkah*. Trees are not made by human hands, and yet Rabbi Meir does not rule against them. Moreover, extending the principle to all naturally occurring formations would eliminate the use of rock faces, earthen banks, and other geological features as walls for any halachic purpose — an implication the sources do not seem to support. The first formulation, focusing on the animating *ruach* of a living creature as the specific deficiency, fits more precisely with the scope of Rabbi Meir's position.

This analysis also resolves the *tumah* question that prompted the shift in frameworks. When Rabbi Meir rules that the animal used as a wall does not have the *tumah* of a *golel*, it is not because of a precautionary decree that happens to result in leniency. Rather, since for Rabbi Meir the halachic significance of this object in its role — as something standing in the position of a grave marker — depends entirely on the animating life force sustaining it, and since that life force is *ruach* and therefore halachically non-substantial, the animal cannot be granted the status of a *golel*. The ruling is not a *gezeirah*; it follows directly from the same underlying principle. A halachic status cannot be anchored in something that is, from Rabbi Meir's perspective, not genuinely physical.

## Get on Animal

### Not Alive, Not Food

Having established Rabbi Meir's position through Rav Acha bar Yaakov's framework, the *gemara* turns to Rabbi Yose HaGlili, who adds a further restriction: one may not write a *get* on an animal. The *gemara* cites a *braisa* that derives this from the verse: *ve-kasav lah sefer krisus* — and he shall write her a *sefer krisus*, a bill of divorce.

The *braisa* reasons as follows. The word *sefer* — book — would seem to limit the permissible writing surface to something parchment-like. Yet the verse also says *ve-kasav* — he shall write — without qualification, implying that any surface is acceptable. If *ve-kasav* broadens the permissible surfaces beyond *sefer*, what is the function of the word *sefer*? It comes to teach: just as a book (*sefer*) is something that has no life-spirit (*ruach chayim*) and is not food, so too anything on which a *get* may be written must be something that has no *ruach chayim* and is not food. An animal, being alive, fails the first criterion. This is Rabbi Yose HaGlili's source.

### Tosafot Analysis

A natural question arises: why specifically these two characteristics? A piece of *klaf* has many properties — it is thin, it is smooth, it comes from an animal, it is classically used for writing. Why select "not alive" and "not food" as the operative criteria to the exclusion of others?

*Tosafot* addresses this directly. The closing line of a key *Tosafot* states: *be-chol echad kefi mah she-domeh la-chachamim inyan ha-ra'uy lo* — in each instance, [the relevant characteristics are identified] according to what the *chachamim* deem the appropriate matter for that context. There is no algorithmic method for determining, in a *klal u-ferat u-klal* or similar derivation, which attributes of the *prat* (the specific item used as the interpretive paradigm) are the ones that define the scope of the rule. That determination is itself an act of halachic reasoning, a matter of discretion exercised by the *chachamim* based on their understanding of what is most contextually appropriate.

This is a significant methodological statement about the nature of *Torah she-be'al peh*. It does not mean that the *chachamim* are free to choose arbitrarily; it means that the exercise of halachic interpretation is not mechanical, and that identifying the salient characteristics of a paradigm case requires judgment that cannot be fully reduced to formal rules.

One might add — though *Tosafot* does not say this explicitly — that there is a broader question among the *Rishonim* about how many of the *gemara*'s *drashos* represent a genuine derivation of halachic content from the text, versus cases where the content was known through tradition and the *drashah* serves as an *asmachta* or as a hermeneutically-articulated confirmation. This distinction matters: an *asmachta* in the strict sense implies only rabbinic authority, while a genuine derivation carries biblical weight. The *gemara* typically presents its derivations as substantive, but some *Rishonim* understand at least certain cases as the retroactive articulation of an antecedently known rule.

### Sefer Krisus Debate

The Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Yose HaGlili's reading. According to the Rabbis, *sefer* in the verse does not mean "book" in the sense that would limit permissible materials. Rather, *le-sfiras devarim be'alma hu de-ata* — the word *sefer krisus* comes to say: write a narrative of severance, a document that narrates the fact of the divorce. It does not restrict the material on which the *get* is written. Since the Rabbis never used *sefer* to limit permissible materials, they have no need to invoke *ve-kasav* to broaden them.

The *gemara* then asks: if that is so, what do the Rabbis learn from *ve-kasav*? The answer is: *bi-chsiva misgareshes ve-eina misgareshes be-chesef* — a woman is divorced only through an act of writing, not through the giving of money. One might have thought, from the *gezeirah shavah* linking *yetzia* (departure from marriage) and *havayah* (entry into marriage), that just as marriage can be effected with money, so too divorce might be effected with money. *Ve-kasav* therefore establishes: only through writing.

### Severance Rules

Rabbi Yose HaGlili, having used *ve-kasav* together with *sefer* to establish what materials are permissible, must find a different source to exclude monetary divorce. He derives it from the juxtaposition of *sefer krisus* itself: *sefer karas ve-ein davar acher karas* — only through a *sefer* does the severance take effect, and not through anything else.

The Rabbis, in turn, derive something else from *sefer krisus*: the requirement that the *get* effect complete and unconditional severance. This emerges from a *braisa*: if a husband gives his wife a *get* on the condition that she never drink wine for the rest of her life, or never visit her father's house again — *ein zeh krisus*, this is not a valid *get*. The condition binds her to her husband indefinitely, since one can never be certain the condition will not be violated. A *get* given under such a condition never fully severs the relationship. If, however, the condition is limited in time — *al menas she-loshim yom*, for thirty days — *harei zeh krisus*, it is valid, because after thirty days she will be fully and completely free.

Rabbi Yose HaGlili learns the same requirement — complete severance — from the doubling of *kares krisus*: the two forms of the same root appearing together indicate two teachings, one about permissible materials and one about complete severance. The Rabbis respond: *kares krisus lo darshi* — they do not read significance into this doubling.

## Tree Walls

### Tree Walls Mishnah

The *gemara* moves to a new *mishnah*: *ha-oseh sukato bein ha-ilanot veha-ilanot defanot lah, kesheirah* — one who makes his *sukkah* between trees, with the trees serving as its walls, the *sukkah* is valid. This does not initially appear to be a dramatic ruling.

### Swaying Problem

Rav Acha bar Yaakov's principle, cited earlier in the context of the animal wall, is now invoked: *kol mechitza she'einah yechola la'amod beruach metzuya einah mechitza* — any *mechitza* that cannot withstand an ordinary wind is not a valid *mechitza*. This applies universally — to *sukkah*, to *Shabbos* domains, to any halachic context requiring a wall. The problem the *gemara* raises is then obvious: *veha ka'azil ve'atei* — the trees sway in the wind. How can they serve as valid walls?

### Binding Solution

The *gemara* resolves this by specifying the conditions under which the *mishnah*'s ruling applies. *Hacha be-mai askinan* — what are we dealing with? *B'kishim* — old, solid trees whose trunks do not sway. But what about the foliage? Even on an old tree, the canopy sways. The *gemara* answers: *ve'avzayei behotza vedafna* — the foliage is tied down with ropes and cords. With the foliage secured, the walls as a whole can withstand a normal wind.

### Non-Usage Concern

This leads the *gemara* to ask: *ai hachi mai lemimra* — if everything is tied down and the *sukkah* is entirely valid for structural reasons, what is the *chiddush* of the *mishnah*? Why does the *mishnah* need to tell us that trees may serve as walls?

The answer is: *mahu d'teima nigzor dilma atei l'ishtamushi b'ilan* — one might have thought we should issue a *gezeirah* prohibiting the use of trees as walls, lest one come to make use of the tree itself on *Yom Tov*. Using a tree on *Yom Tov* — placing objects on it, breaking off branches — is forbidden. If one's *sukkah* is built around trees, one might inadvertently interact with the tree in a prohibited way. *Ka mashma lan* — the *mishnah* teaches that we do not issue such a decree. The *chiddush* is not structural but halachic policy: we do not extend the prohibition to the use of trees as *sukkah* walls out of concern that one might come to use the tree improperly.

## Extended Cases

### Well Corners

The *gemara* continues to probe the principle of a *mechitza* that sways in the wind through a series of cases. The first involves the well-known construct of corners (*diyomadin*) placed around a public well to create a *reshut hayachid*, permitting drawing water from it on *Shabbos*. The question is what materials may serve as those corners. The *gemara* cites: *hayah sham ilan o geder o mechitzat hakanim, nidon mishum diyomed* — if there was a tree, or a stone fence, or a wall of reeds at one of the required corners, it may serve as a corner for this purpose. The *gemara* addresses the swaying objection as before: *hacha nami mishum de'avdye behotza vedafna* — here too, it is tied down with ropes.

### Willow Branches

The *gemara* adduces another case: *ilan hamaysech al ha'aretz* — a tree whose branches hang downward and form a canopy over the ground, like a willow. *Im ein nofo gavoha min ha'aretz sheloshah tefachim, metaltelin tachtav* — if the lowest point of the hanging branches is within three *tefachim* of the ground, one may carry beneath it on *Shabbos*. The picture is of branches descending from the tree and approaching the ground on all sides, such that if the gap between the branch-tips and the ground is less than three *tefachim*, the principle of *lavud* renders them as if touching the ground, forming continuous walls. The enclosed space beneath the canopy then constitutes a *reshut hayachid*.

### Tying vs. Natural

The same challenge is raised: *ha ka'azil ve'atei* — the branches sway in the wind and will move above three *tefachim* from the ground. The answer is again: *hacha nami de'avdye behotza vedafna* — tied down.

But this creates a new problem. If the branches are tied down by human effort, then this is a human-made *mechitza*. A human-made *mechitza* that is created for the purpose of habitation should, in principle, create a full *reshut hayachid* of unlimited size. *Metaltel bekulah* — one should be able to carry in the entire enclosed space regardless of its dimensions. Yet Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua rules: *ein metaltelin bo ela beit satayim* — one may only carry within a *beit satayim*, an area of 5000 square *amot* (fifty by one hundred *amot*). Why should this limitation apply if human hands have secured the *mechitza*?

### Beit Satayim Limit

The *beit satayim* limitation ordinarily applies to enclosures that were not *hukaf ledira* — not surrounded for the purpose of habitation. The paradigmatic case is an area enclosed for some purpose other than dwelling — for instance, a fence erected around a forest to keep out intruders. Such an enclosure, even if structurally sound, lacks the character of a dwelling-space and therefore does not qualify as a full *reshut hayachid* beyond the *beit satayim* limit.

If the willow's branches were entirely natural and no human intervention occurred, this would explain the limitation straightforwardly: no human surrounded the area for habitation; it is simply a natural formation. But once a human tied down the branches, the *mechitza* was made by human hands. The question is whether it was made for the purpose of habitation — *hukaf ledira* — which would remove the limitation.

### For the Air

The *gemara* answers: *mishum dehavi dira detashmisheih la'avir* — because this is a *dira* whose use is oriented toward the air, toward the outside. *Vechol dira detashmisheih la'avir ein metaltelin bo ela beit satayim* — and any such space, even if enclosed by human hands, is subject to the *beit satayim* limit.

Rashi explains: the person who takes shelter beneath the overhanging tree does so not for the sake of being enclosed in a private space, but in order to look out and attend to what is happening outside — watching crops or flocks from a shaded position. The enclosed space is thus instrumentally oriented toward its exterior, not toward creating an interior dwelling. That is the sense of *dira detashmisheih la'avir*: the enclosure exists for the sake of the open air beyond it. Such an enclosure, even when formed by deliberate human action, does not achieve the status of *hukaf ledira* that would grant it the full dimensions of a *reshut hayachid*.

### Mound & Crevice

The *gemara* adduces further cases. One who begins *Shabbos* atop a mound — *gavo'ah asarah*, ten *tefachim* high, and between four *amot* and *beit satayim* in area — may treat the edges of the mound as virtual walls and measure his *techum* of two thousand *amot* from the outer edges rather than from his own position. The mound is a *reshut hayachid*, but only up to *beit satayim*, because it is entirely natural. Similarly, *benika* — a crevice ten *tefachim* deep and between four *amot* and *beit satayim* in area — functions the same way.

A third case: if one began *Shabbos* in the midst of cut wheat, *shibbolim makkifot oto* — with sheaves of grain surrounding him — *mehallich et kullah vechutz lah alpaim amah* — the entire enclosed area is treated as his base and he measures two thousand *amot* from its outer boundary. The *gemara* again raises the swaying objection — *afgav de'azal ve'atei* — and resolves it: *dekatarnehu bemichta vehadiv bemichta* — the sheaves were tied down.

The practical concern underlying much of this discussion bears on the contemporary question of canvas *sukkah* walls that billow in the wind. The question is not merely whether the wall sways, but whether, after the wind acts on it, the wall retains its halachic status as a wall. If the canvas, even when billowing, remains within three *tefachim* of its original position and remains anchored at the bottom, it may still satisfy the requirements of *lavud* and not lose its wall-status. What *einah yechola la'amod beruach metzuya* must mean, on this analysis, is that the wind moves the wall so dramatically that it can no longer function as a wall at all — not merely that it shifts somewhat. This is why contemporary *sukkot* with canvas walls commonly use straps to ensure the walls remain secured even when the canvas itself billows outward.
