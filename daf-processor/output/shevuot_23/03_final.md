# Shevuot 23 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Previous Material Review

### Opening & Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״, וְאָכַל וְשָׁתָה – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל וְשֶׁלֹּא אֶשְׁתֶּה״, וְאָכַל וְשָׁתָה – חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם. ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״, וְאָכַל פַּת חִטִּין וּפַת שְׂעוֹרִין וּפַת כּוּסְּמִין – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל פַּת חִטִּין וּפַת שְׂעוֹרִין וּפַת כּוּסְּמִין״, וְאָכַל – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת. ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אֶשְׁתֶּה״, וְשָׁתָה מַשְׁקִין הַרְבֵּה – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אֶשְׁתֶּה יַיִן וְשֶׁמֶן וּדְבַשׁ״, וְשָׁתָה – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one said: On my **oath I will not eat, and** then he **ate and drank, he is liable** to bring **only one** offering, because an oath to refrain from eating includes refraining from drinking. If he said: On my **oath I will not eat and I will not drink, and** then he **ate and drank,** he is **liable** to bring **two** offerings. If he said: On my **oath I will not eat, and** then he **ate wheat bread and barley bread and spelt bread, he is liable** to bring **only one** offering. If he said: On my **oath I will not eat wheat bread or barley bread or spelt bread, and** then he **ate** all of them, he is **liable** to bring an offering **for each and every one.** If he said: On my **oath I will not drink, and** then he **drank several** kinds of **liquids, he is liable** to bring **only one** offering. If he said: On my **oath I will not drink wine or oil or honey, and** then **he drank** all of them, he is **liable** to bring an offering **for each and every one.**

The present daf opens by picking up with the *mishna* at the bottom of 22b. The preceding material covered a wide range of foundational topics in *hilchot shevuot*, and the discussion now turns to questions of how the framing of a *shvua* affects the number of *korbanot* incurred upon its violation.

### Topics Summary

Among the principles already examined: the distinction between *shvuat shav* and *shvuat sheker*, the mechanism of *hatfasat shvua*, the status of *konam*, the meaning of *achilah* in various formulations, and the question of whether the minimum measure triggering liability is a *kolshehu* or a *kezayit*. The principle of *mushba v'omed* — that an oath cannot take effect on something one is already forbidden to do — has also figured prominently. These concepts now provide the backdrop for the *mishna*'s treatment of single versus multiple oaths and their corresponding *korbanot*.

## One Oath vs. Multiple

### Eating Includes Drinking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִירוֹשׁ חַמְרָא הוּא, וּכְתִיב: ״וְאָכַלְתָּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** since the Hebrew word ***tirosh*** mentioned in the verse **is wine, and it is written** with regard to it: **"And you shall eat,"** this indicates that drinking is an activity included in eating.

The *mishna* opens with the case of *shvua shelo ochal* — a person who takes an oath not to eat — and then proceeds to eat and drink. Although eating and drinking are two distinct acts, the *mishna* teaches that both fall under the single rubric of *achilah*: consuming, broadly understood. Implicit in this ruling, and soon to be the focus of the *gemara*, is the principle that *shtiyah* — drinking — is included within the word *ochal*. Since both transgressions flow from one *shvua*, the person is *eino chayav ela achas*: liable for only one *korban*, even if he was not made aware between the two acts. This parallels the rule for other repeated violations of the same *aveira* — eating two *kezaysim* of *chametz* on *Pesach* or of forbidden fat without intervening awareness yields one *korban*, not two.

### Two Oaths, Two Korbanot

The *mishna* then introduces the case of *shvua shelo ochal v'shelo eshteh* — a person who takes an oath that he will not eat and will not drink. One might have supposed that since he uses only a single utterance of the word *shvua*, the whole statement constitutes one oath. The *chiddush* of the *mishna* is that we regard this as two distinct *shvuot*, because he identified two separate acts. Accordingly, if he then eats and drinks — even without intervening awareness — he is *chayav shtayim*: obligated in two *korbanot*. The two acts are treated as two separate sins, as though he had taken the oath not to eat one day and the oath not to drink the next.

### Shabbat Melachot Analogy

There is a further *chiddush* embedded here. One might object: even granting that these are two separate *shvuot*, is he not still transgressing the same underlying *aveira*? After all, the prohibition is the same — violating the sanctity of one's word through a false oath — and so perhaps a single *korban* should suffice regardless of how many *shvuot* were involved.

The best analogy is *Shabbos*. If a person forgot both that it was forbidden to cook on *Shabbos* and that it was forbidden to kindle a fire, and he then kindled a fire and cooked without becoming aware between the two acts, he brings two *chata'ot* — two separate sin offerings — because those are two distinct *melachos*. Yes, he transgressed one underlying sin of violating *Shabbos*, but the Torah's principle of *chiluk melachos* treats each *melacha* as a discrete transgression warranting its own *korban*. The same logic applies here: *shvuah* likewise recognizes that two separately articulated *shvuot*, even if they address overlapping subject matter, are treated as two distinct transgressions requiring two *korbanot*.

## Bread & Liquid Types

### Bread Types Cases

The *mishna* continues with cases involving different categories of food and drink. If a person takes an oath *shelo ochal* — not to eat — and then eats three types of bread: wheat bread, barley bread, and spelt bread, he is *eino chayav ela achas*. All three are merely three ways of transgressing the same *shvua*; one *korban* suffices.

By contrast, if he explicitly formulates his oath as *shvua shelo ochal pas chitin u'pas seorin u'pas kusmin* — that he will not eat bread of wheat, bread of barley, and bread of spelt — this is treated as three distinct *shvuot*. Eating all three makes him *chayav al kol achas v'achas*: liable for each separately, for three *korbanot*.

### Liquid Types Cases

The same structure governs liquids. A person who takes a *shvua shelo eshteh* and then drinks many different liquids is *eino chayav ela achas* — one *korban* for multiple violations of the same oath. But one who explicitly swears *shelo eshteh yayin v'shemen u'dvash* — that he will not drink wine, oil, and honey — and then drinks all three, is *chayav al kol achas v'achas*.

It is worth noting that oil and honey raise an intuitive question: these are not beverages in the ordinary sense. Drinking oil is hardly a normal act; honey one might more naturally describe as eating. The *chiddush* may be that since these are liquids, they fall under the prohibition of *shtiyah* even when they are not commonly consumed in that form.

### Inedible Foods & Drinks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״, וְאָכַל אֳוכָלִין שֶׁאֵינָן רְאוּיִן לַאֲכִילָה, וְשָׁתָה מַשְׁקִין שֶׁאֵינָן רְאוּיִן לִשְׁתִיָּה – פָּטוּר. ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״, וְאָכַל נְבֵילוֹת וּטְרֵיפוֹת, שְׁקָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים – חַיָּיב. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר. אָמַר: ״קוּנָּם אִשְׁתִּי נֶהֱנֵית לִי אִם אָכַלְתִּי הַיּוֹם״, וְהוּא אָכַל נְבֵילוֹת וּטְרֵיפוֹת, שְׁקָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים – הֲרֵי אִשְׁתּוֹ אֲסוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** If he said: On my **oath I will not eat, and he ate foods that are inedible or drank liquids that are not potable,** he is **exempt.** If he said: On my **oath I will not eat, and** then he **ate** the meat of unslaughtered **carcasses or *tereifot*, repugnant creatures or creeping animals,** he is **liable. And Rabbi Shimon deems** him **exempt,** since he is already under oath from Mount Sinai not to eat them and an oath cannot take effect where another oath is in force. But if he **said:** It is ***konam*** for **my wife to derive benefit from me if I ate today, and he had eaten carcasses or *tereifot*, repugnant creatures or creeping animals, his wife is prohibited** from deriving benefit from him.

The *mishna* further rules that if a person takes an oath not to eat and then consumes foods or liquids that are *einam reuyim l'achilah* — unfit for consumption — he is *patur*. The same standards that govern other dietary prohibitions apply: you must consume something that is halachically considered food. Just as a person eating dirt on *Yom Kippur* or *Pesach* would not be *over* the prohibition, dirt and similarly unfit substances are outside the scope of the oath.

### Konam Conditional Oath

The *mishna* then addresses the case of a person who makes a conditional *konam*: *konam ishti nehenis li im achalti hayom* — if I have eaten today, my wife is forbidden in benefit to me. This mechanism functions as an alternative to a standard *shvua*: rather than swearing directly, a person backs his denial with a conditional *neder*, making something forbidden to him contingent on the truth of his assertion.

If such a person then ate *neveilot u'treifot shekatzim u'remasim* — non-kosher food — the *mishna* rules *harei ishto asurah*: his wife is indeed forbidden, because eating non-kosher food counts as eating for this purpose. The question of whether a *shvua* can take effect on forbidden food is the subject of debate between the *tanna kamma* and *Rabbi Shimon*, who exempts. As will be developed in the *gemara*, Rabbi Shimon's exemption may rest on the principle of *mushba v'omed*: one is already forbidden from *Sinai* to eat that food, so a *shvua* cannot add a new layer of prohibition.

## Achilah Includes Shtiyah

### Svara Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״, וְשָׁתָה – חַיָּיב. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא סְבָרָא, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin says** that **Shmuel says:** If one said: On my **oath I will not eat, and** then **he drank,** he is **liable. If you wish,** you may **propose a logical argument** for this ruling, **and if you wish,** you may **cite a verse** to explain it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא סְבָרָא – דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ אִינָשׁ לְחַבְרֵיהּ: ״נִטְעוֹם מִידֵּי״, וְעָיְילִי וְאָכְלִי וְשָׁתוּ. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא קְרָא – שְׁתִיָּה בִּכְלַל אֲכִילָה; דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מִנַּיִן לִשְׁתִיָּה שֶׁהִיא בִּכְלַל אֲכִילָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאָכַלְתָּ לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ בַּמָּקוֹם אֲשֶׁר יִבְחַר לְשַׁכֵּן שְׁמוֹ שָׁם מַעְשַׂר דְּגָנְךָ וְתִירֹשְׁךָ״;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **If you wish,** you may **propose a logical argument** for this ruling: It is clear that drinking is included in eating from the fact **that a person will say to another: Let's** have **a taste** of **something, and they go in and eat and drink. And if you wish, cite a verse** as the source for this ruling, **as Reish Lakish says: From where** is it derived **that drinking is included in eating? It is** derived **from that** which **is stated: "And you shall eat before the Lord your God, in the place that He shall choose to cause His name to dwell there, the tithe of your grain, of your *tirosh*,** and of your oil" (Deuteronomy 14:23).

Rabbi Chiya bar Avin said in the name of Shmuel: if a person takes a *shvua shelo ochal* — not to eat — and then drinks, he is *chayav*. This makes explicit what the *mishna* implies: *shtiyah bichlal achilah*, drinking is subsumed within eating.

The *gemara* offers two routes to this conclusion: *iba'it eima svara* — logical reasoning — or *iba'it eima kra* — a scriptural proof. The *svara* is based on ordinary usage: when one person says to another *neito midi* — let's go have something — and they then go and eat and drink, it is natural to say that the invitation to "taste something" or "have something" encompasses drinking as well. The *svara* is admittedly not airtight: that one might drink while being invited to eat could simply mean the drinking is incidental to the eating, not that the word *ochel* refers to the drink. Some textual variants have the example as going to get something to drink upon such an invitation, which would be a stronger illustration — but then the operative word is "taste," not "eat," and the proof still does not establish that the word *achilah* itself encompasses drinking. So the *svara* is suggestive but not conclusive.

### Resh Lakish Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב, מֵהָכָא: ״וְנָתַתָּה הַכֶּסֶף בְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר תְּאַוֶּה נַפְשְׁךָ, בַּבָּקָר וּבַצֹּאן וּבַיַּיִן וּבַשֵּׁכָר״; יַיִן חַמְרָא הוּא, וּכְתִיב: ״וְאָכַלְתָּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said:** Cite **from here,** a verse written with regard to money with which one has redeemed second tithe: **"And you shall bestow the money for whatsoever your soul desires, for oxen, or for sheep, or for wine [*uvayyayin*], or for strong drink,** or for whatsoever your soul asks of you; and you shall eat there before the Lord your God, and you shall rejoice, you and your household" (Deuteronomy 14:26). The Hebrew word ***yayin*** mentioned in the verse **is wine, and it is written: "And you shall eat."**

Resh Lakish offers a more authoritative grounding in a *pasuk*. The *gemara* cites *ve'achalta lifnei Hashem Elokecha bamakom asher yivchar*, referring to the laws of *ma'aser sheni*, where the Torah says *ma'asar deganecha tiroshcha v'yitzharecha* — the tithe of your grain, your wine, and your oil — and uses the word *ve'achalta*, "and you shall eat it." Since *tirosh* is wine — a liquid — and the Torah describes consuming it with the word *achilah*, this demonstrates that *achilah* can include drinking.

### Rav Acha Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא הָכָא נָמֵי עַל יְדֵי אֲנִיגְרוֹן? ״שֵׁכָר״ כְּתִיב, מִידֵּי דִּמְשַׁכַּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But perhaps here** this verse is **also** referring to consuming wine **by means of an *anigeron*.** The Gemara rejects this: **"Strong drink" is written** in the verse, referring to **something intoxicating,** and wine mixed into a stew is not intoxicating.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא דְּבֵילָה קְעִילִית? דְּתַנְיָא: אָכַל דְּבֵילָה קְעִילִית וְשָׁתָה דְּבַשׁ וְחָלָב, וְנִכְנַס לַמִּקְדָּשׁ וְעָבַד – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But perhaps** the intoxicating substance referred to in the verse is pressed **figs from Ke'ila, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: A priest who **ate** pressed **figs from Ke'ila or drank honey or milk and** then **entered the Temple and performed** the sacrificial rites is **liable** for violating the prohibition against conducting the Temple service while intoxicated.

The *gemara* pushes back: perhaps *ve'achalta* in the context of *ma'aser sheni* refers not to drinking wine straight but to consuming it in a dish — the *anigron*, described by Rabbah bar Shmuel as a dish made from beet water, or the *achsigron*, made from cooked vegetables. If wine was consumed as part of a cooked preparation, one might be said to "eat" wine without any implication that *achilah* encompasses drinking.

Rav Acha bar Yaakov therefore adduces a different verse, also from the laws of *ma'aser sheni*: *ve'natata hakesef be'chol asher t'aveh nafshecha, ba'vakar u'va'tzon u'ba'yayin u'ba'shechar* — you shall give the money for whatever your heart desires, for cattle, for sheep, for wine, for *shechar* — and the verse concludes *ve'achalta lifnei Hashem Elokecha*, "and you shall eat it before the Lord your God." The word *shechar* denotes something intoxicating, *midi de'meshaker*, and if it can intoxicate, one must be drinking it straight rather than consuming it in a small admixture through food.

The *gemara* tests this as well: perhaps *dveilah k'ilit* — pressed figs from K'ilit — are also capable of intoxication, as the *baraisa* indeed teaches that eating those figs, or drinking honey or milk, constitutes a state of intoxication sufficient to disqualify one from performing the Temple service. So perhaps *shechar* here refers to such solid intoxicants.

### Gezeirah Shavah Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא גָּמַר ״שֵׁכָר״–״שֵׁכָר״ מִנָּזִיר; מָה לְהַלָּן יַיִן, אַף כָּאן יַיִן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** one **derives** the meaning of **"strong drink"** in this verse by means of a verbal analogy **from** the verse about **a nazirite:** "He shall abstain from wine and **strong drink:** He shall drink no vinegar of wine, or vinegar of strong drink, neither shall he drink any liquor of grapes, nor eat fresh grapes or dried" (Numbers 6:3). **Just as there,** the strong drink that is forbidden to the nazirite is referring to a type of **wine, so too here,** the verse is referring to **wine.**

The *gemara* resolves this by means of a *gezeirah shavah*: *shechar shechar mi'nazir*. The same word *shechar* appears in the laws of the *nazir* — *mi'yayin v'shechar yazir* — and there we know it refers to wine-derived liquids and not to solid intoxicants like pressed figs. Therefore here too, *shechar* refers to something one drinks, and yet the *pasuk* describes it with *ve'achalta* — establishing that *achilah* encompasses *shtiyah*.

### Svara vs. Pasuk Debate

This passage — *iba'it eima svara, iba'it eima kra* — has long been cited as a window into a foundational principle of *Torah she'ba'al peh*. The *gemara*'s willingness to say "if you want, I can prove it with logic, if you want, I can prove it with a verse" implies that human reason and scriptural derivation are of equivalent weight as sources of halachic knowledge. This is, in fact, the precise opposite of fundamentalism, which would insist on deriving everything from the simple meaning of a verse to the exclusion of independent reasoning. Here the *gemara* treats them as interchangeable.

Tosafot, on 22b, notes that this phrasing appears in a number of places throughout the *gemara* and raises the question sharply: *v'teima, keivan d'ika svara, lama li kra?* — If we already have the logical proof, why do we need the verse? Why did the Torah bother to indicate this if we could have derived it ourselves? Tosafot's answer is that this depends on the cogency of the *svara*: when the logic is not entirely compelling on its own, the *pasuk* serves to confirm and validate the reasoning. The present case is actually a good illustration of that principle — the *svara* was not persuasive, and so the *kra* does the heavier lifting. One may also note that elsewhere the *gemara* goes further still, suggesting that a sufficiently strong *svara* can override a *pasuk*, as when the *gemara* says *shavik likra d'ihu dachik u'mokei anafsheh* — set aside the verse, it is strained, and the logic stands on its own.

## Shtiyah vs. Achilah

### Rava's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״, וְאָכַל וְשָׁתָה – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא שְׁתִיָּה בִּכְלַל אֲכִילָה, אִיצְטְרִיךְ לֵיהּ לְתַנָּא לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן דְּאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ שְׁתִיָּה לָאו בִּכְלַל אֲכִילָה, ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״, וְאָכַל וְעָשָׂה מְלָאכָה – מִי אִיצְטְרִיךְ לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן דְּאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת?!
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava said: We learn** in the mishna **as well** that drinking is included in eating. The mishna teaches: If one said: On my **oath I will not eat, and** then he **ate and drank, he is liable** to bring **only one** offering. **Granted, if you say** that **drinking is included in eating,** that is why **it was necessary for the *tanna* to teach us that he is liable** to bring **only one** offering. **But if you say** that **drinking is not included in eating,** there is no reason for the *tanna* to teach that it is only one violation. If one says: On my **oath I will not eat, and he ate and performed** some kind of **labor, would it be necessary to teach us that** the person is **liable** to bring **only one** offering? He never took an oath prohibiting labor.

Rava observes that the *mishna* itself confirms the principle *shtiyah bichlal achilah*. The *mishna* states: *shvua shelo ochal v'achal v'shata, eino chayav ela achas* — if he took an oath not to eat and then both ate and drank, he is liable for only one *korban*. If drinking were simply not included in *achilah*, then the *mishna*'s ruling would be trivially obvious: of course you bring only one *korban* when you eat after having sworn not to eat, regardless of whether you also happen to scratch your back or drive somewhere. The fact that the *mishna* found it necessary to tell us that eating and drinking together yields only one *korban* proves that drinking is indeed a violation of the oath — but since both violations stem from the same *shvua*, it is one *korban*. Were drinking simply irrelevant to the oath, no ruling would have been needed.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מַאי, שְׁתִיָּה בִּכְלַל אֲכִילָה?! אֵימָא סֵיפָא: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל וְשֶׁלֹּא אֶשְׁתֶּה״, וְאָכַל וְשָׁתָה – חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם. כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר ״שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״ – אִיתְּסַר לֵיהּ בִּשְׁתִיָּה; כִּי אָמַר ״שֶׁלֹּא אֶשְׁתֶּה״ – אַמַּאי חַיָּיב? אִילּוּ אָמַר ״שֶׁלֹּא אֶשְׁתֶּה״ תְּרֵי זִימְנֵי, מִי מִיחַיַּיב תַּרְתֵּי?!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: What, rather,** is your conclusion; that **drinking is included in eating? Say the latter clause** of the mishna: If he said: On my **oath I will not eat and I will not drink, and** then he **ate and drank,** he is **liable** to bring **two** offerings. According to you, Rava, **once he said: I will not eat, drinking became forbidden to him,** so **when he said: I will not drink, why** is he rendered **liable** to bring a separate offering? **If he had said: I will not drink, two times, would he be liable** to bring **two** offerings?

Abaye poses a sharp challenge. Grant, he says, that *shtiyah bichlal achilah* — drinking is included in eating. Then consider the case of *shvua shelo ochal v'shelo eshteh*. The moment the person finishes saying the words *shvua shelo ochal* — before he has even uttered the rest of the sentence — he is already *issur lei bi-shtiyah*: forbidden in drinking as well, since drinking is subsumed in his oath not to eat. When he then adds *v'shelo eshteh* — and not to drink — why should those words create any new prohibition? The principle of *ein shvua chalah al shvua* should apply: one oath cannot take effect on something already forbidden by an earlier oath. The result should be that the second clause never takes effect, and if he then eats and drinks, he is liable for only one *korban* — for violating the first oath. Yet the *mishna* says he is *chayav shtayim*.

### Rava's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם דְּאָמַר ״שֶׁלֹּא אֶשְׁתֶּה״, וַהֲדַר אָמַר ״שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״; דִּשְׁתִיָּה בִּכְלַל אֲכִילָה אִיתַהּ, אֲכִילָה בִּכְלָל שְׁתִיָּה לֵיתַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: There,** in the mishna, it is actually a case **where he said: I will not drink, and then said: I will not eat.** Although **it is** so **that drinking is included in eating, it is not** so that **eating is included in drinking,** and the oath not to drink went into effect independently of the oath not to eat. Therefore, he is liable to bring two offerings.

Rava replies that the *mishna* should be understood as stating the clauses in reverse order: the person first said *shelo eshteh* — not to drink — and then said *shelo ochal* — not to eat. In this sequence, drinking was separately prohibited by the first clause, and eating — together with its implicit inclusion of drinking — was prohibited by the second. Since *shtiyah bichlal achilah* but *achilah* is not included in *shtiyah*, the oath not to drink prohibited only drinking, while the subsequent oath not to eat now prohibited eating and thereby also redeclared the prohibition on drinking under a new *shvua*. Thus both oaths are in force, and the *mishna*'s ruling of *chayav shtayim* follows.

### Context Defines Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אָמַר שְׁבוּעָה ״שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל וְשֶׁלֹּא אֶשְׁתֶּה״, וְאָכַל וְשָׁתָה, מַאי – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת? אִי הָכִי, אַדְּתָנֵי רֵישָׁא: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״ וְאָכַל וְשָׁתָה אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת; לִיתְנֵי: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל וְשֶׁלֹּא אֶשְׁתֶּה״ אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת – וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן ״שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״ לְחוֹדֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** according to this, if **he said:** On my **oath I will not eat and I will not drink, and he** then **ate and drank, what** is the *halakha*? Is it that **he is liable** to bring **only one** offering? **If that is so, rather than teaching** in **the first clause** of the mishna that one who said: On my **oath I will not eat, and** then **ate and drank, is liable** to bring **only one** offering, **let** the *tanna* **teach** the following: If one says: On my **oath I will not eat and I will not drink,** and he ate and drank, **he is liable** to bring **only one** offering. From that ruling it could be concluded that when one takes an oath: **I will not eat, alone, all the more so** is he liable to bring only one offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְעוֹלָם כִּדְקָתָנֵי; וְשָׁאנֵי הָכָא, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר ״שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״ וַהֲדַר אָמַר ״שֶׁלֹּא אֶשְׁתֶּה״ – גַּלִּי אַדַּעְתֵּיהּ דְּהָךְ אֲכִילָה דְּאָמַר, אֲכִילָה גְּרֵידְתָּא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the mishna **actually** is **as it is taught,** that one who took an oath not to eat and drink, and then ate and drank, is liable to bring two offerings, even though drinking is included in eating. **And here** it **is different. Since he said: I will not eat, and then said: I will not drink, he revealed his intention that the eating that he stated** first **is eating alone** and does not include drinking.

Abaye presses further: if Rava reverses the order of the *mishna* to make it work, then Rava is conceding that if the clauses were stated in the natural order — *shelo ochal v'shelo eshteh* — drinking would indeed not take effect as a second prohibition, yielding only one *korban*. But then the *mishna* ought to have taught the more novel case: that even when formulated as *shelo ochal v'shelo eshteh*, you are *eino chayav ela achas*. Teaching that case would have been a greater *chiddush*.

The *gemara* resolves this with an important conceptual point: *keivan d'amar shelo ochal v'hadar amar shelo eshteh, gali adatei de'hach achilah d'amar, achilah geridasa hi* — when a person says "I will not eat and I will not drink," the very fact that he adds the clause about drinking reveals that in his formulation, *achilah* means eating alone, in the narrow sense, without the extension to drinking. Context governs the scope of the word. When *ochel* appears by itself, it encompasses all forms of consumption including liquids; but when a person pairs it explicitly with *eshteh*, he signals that in this sentence *ochel* refers only to solid food. Accordingly, the two clauses create two genuinely independent prohibitions — one on eating and one on drinking — and the *mishna* correctly rules *chayav shtayim*.

## Alternative Phrasing Rule

### Bread Repetition Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל, וְאָכַל פַּת חִטִּין כּוּ׳. וְדִלְמָא לְמִיפְטַר נַפְשֵׁיהּ מֵאַחְרָנְיָיתָא קָאָתֵי?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if one said: On my **oath I will not eat, and** then he **ate wheat bread** and barley bread and spelt bread, he is liable to bring only one offering. If he said: On my oath I will not eat wheat bread or barley bread or spelt bread, and then he ate all of them, he is liable to bring an offering for each one. The Gemara challenges: **But perhaps** when he details each type of bread it **comes to** ensure that he **exempts himself from** having **other** foods included in the oath, not to indicate that each type of bread is a separate oath.

The *gemara* turns to the *mishna*'s ruling that naming bread of wheat, bread of barley, and bread of spelt separately creates three distinct *shvuot*. The underlying principle, which the *gemara* now develops, is that separate naming produces separate *shvuot* only if there was an alternative, grouped phrasing available that would have expressed the same content as a unified oath. Because the person chose to name them separately rather than bundling them, we treat each as its own *shvua*.

The *gemara* immediately raises a problem: perhaps the person was not creating three separate *shvuot* at all, but was simply specifying three particular types of bread to exclude others. Maybe he just wanted to say he would not eat *these* three types of bread and not, say, rye.

### Separation Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵימַר ״חִטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִין וְכוּסְּמִין״. וְדִלְמָא לָכוֹס?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: If that was his intention, then **he could have** just **said: Wheat, barley, and spelt,** without mentioning bread each time. From the fact that he mentioned bread each time, it can be understood that his intention was that each be considered a separate oath. The Gemara rejects this: **But** if he did not mention bread, **perhaps** his oath could be interpreted **to** prohibit **chewing [*lakhos*]** whole kernels of these grains, and he consequently mentioned bread to limit the oath to bread.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵימַר ״פַּת חִטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִין וְכוּסְּמִין״. וְדִלְמָא ״פַּת חִטִּין״ – לֶאֱכוֹל, ״שְׂעוֹרִין וְכוּסְּמִין״ – לָכוֹס?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: If his intention was to ensure that his oath does not extend to other foods, **he could have** just **said: Bread** made from **wheat, or barley, or spelt,** and in that way his oath would be limited to just bread. The Gemara rejects this: If he had said it that way, **perhaps** his oath could be interpreted to prohibit him from **eating wheat bread** and from **chewing** whole kernels of **barley or spelt.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵימַר ״פַּת חִטִּין וְשֶׁל שְׂעוֹרִין וְשֶׁל כּוּסְּמִין״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: If his intention was to ensure that his oath does not extend to other foods, **he could have** just **said: Bread** made from **wheat, and from barley, and from spelt,** thereby limiting his oath to types of bread.

The *gemara* works through this methodically. If the person simply wanted to prohibit these three and no others, he should have said *chitin u'seorin v'chusmin* — wheat, barley, and spelt — without the word *pas*. But that phrasing would have meant he could not chew on the raw grains themselves; the word *pas* — bread — was necessary to make clear that bread is the subject. Very well, then he should have said *pas chitin u'seorin v'chusmin* — bread of wheat, barley, and spelt — in a single phrase. The *gemara* notes that this formulation might have been ambiguous: it could be read as bread of wheat on the one hand and raw barley and spelt on the other.

So the required phrasing would have been *pas chitin v'shel seorin v'shel kusmin* — bread of wheat and of barley and of spelt — using the connective *shel* to make clear that all three follow under *pas*. It is worth noting that this alternative phrasing is no shorter — in fact, it is slightly longer — than what was actually said. The *gemara*'s point is not that the alternative is easier to say, but that it would have more clearly expressed a unified prohibition. Since such a phrasing was available and the person did not use it, we conclude that the repetition of *pas* before each grain was deliberate and signals three separate *shvuot*.

### Alternative Phrasing Tests Continued

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא עַל יְדֵי תַּעֲרוֹבֶת?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **But** if he had said it that way, **perhaps** his oath could be interpreted to prohibit only bread made **from a mixture** of all these grains.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא ״וְכֵן שֶׁל שְׂעוֹרִים וְכֵן שֶׁל כּוּסְּמִין״; ״פַּת״ ״פַּת״ לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לְחַלֵּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: If the specification in the mishna serves merely to ensure that his oath does not extend to other foods, have him **say:** Bread made from wheat **and so from barley and so from spelt. Why do I** need to repeat: **Bread, bread,** each time? **Conclude from it** that the point of repeating the word bread each time is **to individuate** each statement as a separate oath.

The *gemara* presses further: even *pas chitin v'shel seorin v'shel kusmin* might have been misunderstood as referring to a bread made of all three grains mixed together — a *taaroves*. To dispel that reading entirely, one would have had to say *pas chitin v'chein shel seorin v'chein shel kusmin* — bread of wheat and similarly of barley and similarly of spelt. This formulation, though considerably more cumbersome, would have been entirely unambiguous. The key principle that emerges is that we do not require the alternative phrasing to have been the natural or easy way to speak; we only require that some alternative phrasing *could* have conveyed the unified sense. Since such alternatives exist, the fact that the person said *pas pas pas* — bread of wheat, bread of barley, bread of spelt — is treated as signaling separation, and each is a distinct *shvua*.

### Rav Papa's Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אֶשְׁתֶּה״ וְשָׁתָה מַשְׁקִין הַרְבֵּה, אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת כּוּ׳. בִּשְׁלָמָא הָתָם, דְּאָמְרַתְּ מְיַיתְּרָא לֵיהּ ״פַּת״ ״פַּת״ לְחִיּוּבָא; אֶלָּא הָכָא, מַאי הֲוָה לְמֵימַר? דִּלְמָא לְמִיפְטַר נַפְשֵׁיהּ מִמַּשְׁקִין אַחֲרִינֵי קָאָתֵי!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If he said: On my **oath I will not drink, and** then **he drank several** kinds of **liquids, he is liable** to bring **only one** offering. If he says: On my oath I will not drink wine, or oil, or honey, and he drank all of them, he is liable to bring an offering for each and every one. The Gemara asks: **Granted, there,** in the case of the bread, the ruling of the mishna is understood, **as you said** that the word **bread** before barley, and the word **bread** before spelt **are superfluous** and serve **to** extend his **liability** so that each is considered an independent oath. **But here,** where there is no superfluous language, **what was there to say? Perhaps** the oath **comes to** ensure that he **exempts himself** so that the oath does not extend **to other liquids.** Since that is a possibility, he should not be liable for breaking three separate oaths when he drank all three liquids.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הָכָא בְּמוּנָּחִין לְפָנָיו עָסְקִינַן, שֶׁהָיָה לוֹ לוֹמַר ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אֶשְׁתֶּה אֵלּוּ״. וְדִלְמָא אֵלּוּ הוּא דְּלָא שָׁתֵינָא, אַחֲרִינֵי שָׁתֵינָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: We are dealing here with** a situation where all three liquids are **placed before him.** Under the circumstances, if he merely wished to indicate that his oath is limited to these liquids, **he could have said:** On my **oath I will not drink these.** Since he instead specified the liquids in the oath, it was in order to indicate that each should be considered as a separate oath. The Gemara challenges: **But** had he said: I will not drink these, **perhaps** his oath would be interpreted as meaning: **It is these** liquids, which are before me right now, **that I will not drink,** but **I will drink other** wine, oil, and honey.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּאָמַר ״שְׁבוּעָה דְּלָא שָׁתֵינָא כְּגוֹן אֵלּוּ״. דִּלְמָא כְּגוֹן אֵלּוּ דְּלָא שָׁתֵינָא; בְּצִיר מֵהָכִי וּטְפֵי מֵהָכִי שָׁתֵינָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather,** if he merely wished to indicate that his oath is limited to these types of liquids, it would be a case **where he said:** On my **oath I will not drink** liquids **such as these.** The Gemara challenges: Had he said it that way, **perhaps** it could be interpreted as referring to the volume of the liquids: **I will not drink** liquids **such as these,** but **I will drink less than this or more than this.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כְּגוֹן דְּאָמַר: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אֶשְׁתֶּה מִמִּין אֵלּוּ״. וְדִלְמָא מִין אֵלּוּ הוּא דְּלָא שָׁתֵינָא, הָא אִינְהוּ גּוּפַיְיהוּ שָׁתֵינָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather,** if he merely wished to indicate that his oath is limited to these types of liquids, it is a case **where he said:** On my **oath I will not drink from these types** of liquids. The Gemara challenges: **But** had he said it that way, **perhaps** his oath would be interpreted to mean: **I will not drink these types** of liquids in general, **but I will drink these particular** liquids in front of me.

When the *gemara* turns to the case of wine, oil, and honey, it faces a different challenge. There, the person simply names three distinct liquids; there is no obvious single word that would have encompassed precisely those three and no others. So why does naming them separately create three *shvuot*?

Rav Papa explains: we are dealing with a case where these three items are physically present before the person — *munachim lefanav*. In that context, the person could easily have said *shvua shelo eshteh eilu* — I swear I will not drink any of these — and that would have expressed a single unified prohibition on all three. Or he could have said *shvua delo shatina kegon elu* — I will not drink anything like these — to encompass these types generally. The *gemara* tests and refines these alternatives: *kegon elu* might have been taken to refer to that specific quantity rather than the type; so a still more precise formulation would be *shvua shelo eshteh mi'min elu* — I will not drink from these types of liquids. And if even that left some ambiguity — whether "these types" excludes the very items before him — one could have said *shelo eshteh elu u'mi'mineihem* — these and anything of their type. Since such a phrasing was possible and would have clearly unified the oath, we read the person's choice to name wine, oil, and honey individually as creating three separate *shvuot*.

### Rav Acha Bar Acha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כְּגוֹן דְּאָמַר: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אֶשְׁתֶּה אֵלּוּ וּמִינַּיְיהוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: If the point of the specification of the liquids in the mishna was merely to ensure that his oath does not extend to other liquids, have him **say:** On my oath **I will not drink these and** liquids of **their types.** Since he instead specified: Wine, oil, and honey, one may conclude that his intention was to individuate each liquid as a separate oath.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא אָמַר: בִּמְסַרְהֵב בּוֹ חֲבֵירוֹ עָסְקִינַן, דְּאָמַר לוֹ: ״בּוֹא וּשְׁתֵה עִמִּי יַיִן וְשֶׁמֶן וּדְבַשׁ״. דְּהָיָה לוֹ לוֹמַר: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אֶשְׁתֶּה עִמְּךָ״, ״יַיִן וְשֶׁמֶן וּדְבַשׁ״ לְמָה לִי? לְחַיֵּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, said: We are dealing with** a case in **which another is importuning him** to drink, **as** he **said to him: Come, drink wine, and oil, and honey with me;** if he wanted him to desist, **he should have said:** On my **oath I will not drink with you.** Under these circumstances, **why do I** need him to specify: **Wine, and oil, and honey?** The specification of the liquids serves to indicate his intention **to render** himself **liable for each and every one.**

Rav Acha brei de'Rav Ika provides yet another context in which the separation principle applies to the liquids case. He describes a *pessirah* setting: a friend is pressing the person to come drink with him, urging him to join for wine and oil and honey. In that context, the person could simply have said *shvua shelo eshteh imcha* — I swear I will not drink with you — and that single phrase would have covered all the liquids that were being offered. The fact that the person instead named wine, oil, and honey individually shows that he was identifying each one separately, and that identification — which in this context required more deliberate effort — creates three distinct *shvuot*. The *gemara* does not explicitly require that naming individually always be more effortful than a unified alternative; but Rav Acha's formulation suggests that when the individual naming required greater specification than a simpler bundled phrasing would have, that distinguishes the items as separate *shvuot*.

## Litigation Oaths

### Return Goods Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: ״תֵּן לִי חִטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִין וְכוּסְּמִין שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי כְּלוּם״ – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי חִטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִין וְכוּסְּמִין״ – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** § **We learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (36b) with regard to an oath concerning a deposit, that if one says to his bailee: **Give me my wheat, barley, and spelt** that are **in your possession,** and the bailee lies and says: On my **oath nothing of yours is in my possession, he is liable** to bring **only one** guilt-offering. But if the bailee says: On my **oath I do not have in my possession** any **wheat, barley, or spelt that belong to you,** he is **liable** to bring a separate offering **for each and every one.**

The *gemara* now cites a *mishna* from later in the *masechta* dealing with oaths in the context of litigation. One who claims: "Give me wheat, barley, and spelt that I deposited with you," and the respondent takes a single oath — *shvua she'ein lecha beyadi klum*, I swear I owe you nothing — and is lying, he is *eino chayav ela achas*: one *korban* for one *shvua*. However, if he takes an oath specifically denying each item — *shvua she'ein lecha beyadi chitin u'seorin v'chusmin* — and he is lying about all of them, he is *chayav al kol achas v'achas*: liable separately for each, for three *korbanot*.

An important distinction applies here between this *shvuat shav* or *shvuat sheker* in a litigation context and the future-oriented *shvuat bituy* discussed earlier. By a future oath — *shelo ochal* — if a person repeats the same oath twice, the principle of *ein shvua chalah al shvua* means the second oath never takes effect and only one *korban* is incurred. By an oath in litigation about the past, by contrast, each separate false oath generates its own independent liability, even if the content is redundant.

### Half-Pruta Combination

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֲפִילּוּ פְּרוּטָה מִכּוּלָּם מִצְטָרֶפֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says** with regard to that mishna that he is liable **even** if the value **of all** three species **combines** to amount to as little as one ***peruta*.**

Rabbi Yochanan observes: *afilu pruta mikam mitztarefes* — even a half-*pruta*'s worth of each type can combine to create liability. The practical difficulty is this: a person is only liable for a litigation oath if he is denying something worth at least a *pruta*. If he swore a single global oath — I owe you nothing — and in fact owed half a *pruta* of wheat and half a *pruta* of barley, the two amounts combine to meet the *pruta* threshold and he is liable on the global oath. But if he made three separate specific oaths — no wheat, no barley, no spelt — and owed only half a *pruta* of each, no individual oath covered a full *pruta*'s worth. The combination across separate oath-categories is what Rabbi Yochanan addresses.

### Rav Acha vs. Ravina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַב אַחָא וְרָבִינָא; חַד אָמַר: אַפְּרָטֵי מִיחַיַּיב, אַכְּלָלֵי לָא מִיחַיַּיב; וְחַד אָמַר: אַכְּלָלֵי נָמֵי מִיחַיַּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa and Ravina disagree with regard to this. One said:** When the bailee says: On my oath I do not have in my possession any wheat, barley, or spelt that belong to you, **he is liable** to bring a guilt-offering only **for** violating the **specific** oaths for individual types of grain, and **he is not liable for** violating a general oath that he does not have anything in his possession. Consequently, Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement that the different types of grains can be combined to amount to one *peruta* is referring only to the oath in the first clause of the mishna where he did not specify the grains. **And one said: He is also liable for** violating a general oath, and in the latter clause of the mishna he is liable for violating four oaths: One general oath that he does not have anything in his possession and three specific oaths, one for each type of grain. Consequently, Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement applies also to where he specifies the grains, and he is liable to bring one offering even when all three species combine to amount to only one *peruta*.

Rav Acha and Ravina debate how to read the formulation *shvua she'ein lecha beyadi chitin u'seorin v'chusmin*. One opinion holds that this contains four *shvuot*: a global oath — I owe you nothing — plus three specific oaths, one for each type of grain. Under this reading, one is *chayav* both on the specifics and on the general. The other opinion holds that no global oath is embedded in the statement; the statement consists only of three specific oaths, and the general framing is merely introductory, not itself a separate *shvua*. The practical difference concerns both the number of *korbanot* and the application of Rabbi Yochanan's half-*pruta* rule. Under the view that a global oath exists, half-*pruta* amounts of different types can combine to create liability on the global oath, explaining Rabbi Yochanan's statement. Under the view that only the specifics exist, it is harder to account for cross-category combination, and Rabbi Yochanan's statement must refer back to the earlier single-oath case.

## Rava's Application to Oath Language

### Rava's Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Here,** in the case of one who takes an oath that he will not eat wheat bread, barley bread, or spelt bread, **what** is the *halakha*? Is there a dispute as to whether he is considered to have taken a general oath alongside the specific oaths?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם מִיחַיַּיב אַכְּלָלָא וּמִיחַיַּיב אַפְּרָטָא, דְּהָא אִי מִשְׁתְּבַע וַהֲדַר מִשְׁתְּבַע – מִיחַיַּיב תַּרְתֵּי. הָכָא, אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אִיתָא בִּכְלָלָא, אַפְּרָטֵי אַמַּאי מִיחַיַּיב? מוּשְׁבָּע וְעוֹמֵד הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: How can** these cases **be compared? There,** in the case of an oath of a deposit, he is **liable for** the **general** oath **and for** the **specific** oath, **since if** a bailee **takes an oath** denying that he is in possession of a deposit **and then takes another oath** to the same effect, he is **liable** for taking **two** false oaths on a deposit. **Here,** with regard to an oath to prohibit oneself from eating, **if it enters your mind** that **he is bound by a general** oath, **why would he be liable for the specific** oaths? **He is already under an oath** due to the general oath, and an oath cannot take effect when the matter it prohibits is already forbidden by another oath.

Rava applies these principles back to the case of the oath not to drink wine, oil, and honey. One might ask whether the formulation *shvua shelo eshteh yayin v'shemen u'dvash* should similarly be read as containing a global oath — a general prohibition on drinking — embedded within the specific names. Rava rejects this: *hachi hashta?* How can you compare? In the litigation context, it is possible to be liable separately on a general oath and on specific oaths because each repetition of a false oath creates independent liability regardless of overlap. But in the future-oriented context of *shvuat bituy*, if you read the opening words as a general prohibition on all drinking, the specific prohibitions on wine, oil, and honey could never take effect — they would be cases of *mushba v'omed*, already subsumed under the general. Since the *mishna* clearly holds the person liable separately for each of the three, we must conclude that no general oath is embedded in the statement; the person's liability rests only on the three specific *shvuot*. Reading a global prohibition into the sentence would, paradoxically, eliminate the specific *shvuot* that are the very basis of the multiple-*korban* ruling.

## Treif Food Paradox

### Treif vs. Inedible

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל, וְאָכַל פַּת חִטִּין כּוּ׳. הָא גוּפַאּ קַשְׁיָא – אָמְרַתְּ: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״, וְאָכַל אֳוכָלִין שֶׁאֵין רְאוּיִן לַאֲכִילָה וְשָׁתָה מַשְׁקִין שֶׁאֵין רְאוּיִן לִשְׁתִיָּה – פָּטוּר; וַהֲדַר תָּנֵי: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״, וְאָכַל נְבֵילוֹת וּטְרֵיפוֹת, שְׁקָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים – חַיָּיב; מַאי שְׁנָא רֵישָׁא דְּפָטוּר, וּמַאי שְׁנָא סֵיפָא דְּחַיָּיב?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if one said: On my **oath I will not eat,** and he ate foods that are inedible, he is exempt. If he said: On my oath I will not eat, and then he ate the meat of unslaughtered carcasses, he is liable. The Gemara asks: **This** mishna **itself** is **difficult. You said** that if one said: On my **oath I will not eat, and he ate foods that are inedible, or drank liquids that are not potable,** he is **exempt. And then** the mishna **teaches** that if one says: On my **oath I will not eat, and** then he **ate** the meat of **unslaughtered carcasses or *tereifot*, repugnant creatures or creeping animals,** he is **liable. What is different** about **the first clause that** he is **exempt and what is different** about **the latter clause that** he is **liable?** Non-kosher animals are also unfit to be eaten.

The *mishna* appears to present a contradiction. It first rules that one who takes an oath not to eat and then consumes foods that are *einam reuyim l'achilah* — not fit for eating — is *patur*. It then rules that one who takes an oath not to eat and consumes *neveilot u'treifot shekatzim u'remasim* — non-kosher meat and forbidden creatures — is *chayav*. The question is immediate: isn't non-kosher food also "not fit for eating"?

The obvious resolution, which the *gemara* has in fact noted elsewhere, is that there is a difference between something that is physically unfit for consumption — such as dirt or rotten matter — and something that is physically edible but halachically forbidden. Non-kosher food falls in the second category: it is fit to be eaten in the physical sense and is therefore within the scope of an oath not to eat. Inedible substances are outside that scope entirely, just as eating dirt on *Yom Kippur* would not constitute a violation of the *Yom Kippur* fast.

### Stam vs. Meforash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא; רֵישָׁא בִּסְתָם, וְסֵיפָא בִּמְפָרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This** is **not difficult. The first clause** is **where** he took an oath not to eat **without specifying** what is included in it. Presumably, his oath did not include items that are not ordinarily eaten. **And the latter clause** is a case **where he specifies** what it is he will not eat, e.g., unslaughtered carcasses, and nevertheless eats them.

The *gemara* initially offers a different resolution: *reisha bi'stam v'seifa bi'meforash* — the first clause deals with a general oath while the second deals with an explicit oath. Under this reading, a simple, unspecified *shvua shelo ochal* might not be understood to include non-kosher food, perhaps treating it as effectively inedible for halachic purposes. But if a person explicitly specifies in his oath that he will not eat, say, *chazir*, then it does take effect, in the same way one could explicitly swear not to eat dirt — such a *shvua* would be binding even on something ordinarily outside the scope of eating.

### Mushba V'Omed Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְפָרֵשׁ נָמֵי גּוּפֵיהּ תִּיקְשֵׁי – אַמַּאי מוּשְׁבָּע מֵהַר סִינַי הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **You may also raise a difficulty** with regard to the case where he **specifies** that he will not eat non-kosher items: **Why? He is under oath from Mount Sinai,** and an oath does not take effect to prohibit that which is already forbidden.

The *gemara* immediately recognizes that this answer raises a deeper problem: *amai mushba v'omed me'Har Sinai hu?* Even if the person is explicit, why should the *shvua* take effect at all? He is already forbidden from eating non-kosher food by the oath of Sinai. The principle of *mushba v'omed* holds that a personal oath cannot create a new prohibition on something already prohibited; the new *shvua* simply has nothing to grip.

### Bundling Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דְּאָמְרִי: בְּכוֹלֵל דְּבָרִים הַמּוּתָּרִין עִם דְּבָרִים הָאֲסוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav and Shmuel and Rabbi Yoḥanan** all **say** that this is a case **where he incorporates** into the oath that he will not eat some **permitted items, along with** the statement concerning the **forbidden items.** Since the oath takes effect with regard to the permitted items, it extends also to the forbidden ones.

Rav, Shmuel, and Rabbi Yochanan all offer the same answer: *be'kollel dvar hamutar im dvar ha-asur* — the *shvua* takes effect when the person bundles a permitted item together with a forbidden one. If the person says, "I swear I will not eat apples and *chazir*," the oath takes hold on the apples — which were previously permitted — and by virtue of that grip, it extends to encompass the *chazir* as well. The *shvua* is not floating unsupported; it is anchored in the permitted item and radiates outward to cover the forbidden one as part of a unified prohibition.

### Reish Lakish Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: אִי אַתָּה מוֹצֵא אֶלָּא אִי בִּמְפָרֵשׁ חֲצִי שִׁיעוּר – וְאַלִּיבָּא דְרַבָּנַן; אִי בִּסְתָם – אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: אָדָם אוֹסֵר עַצְמוֹ בְּכׇל שֶׁהוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Reish Lakish says: You find** that one is liable for eating non-kosher food as the result of an oath **only if** it is an oath **where** he **specifies** that it includes **a half-measure, and in accordance with** the opinion **of the Rabbis** that one is not liable for eating a half-measure unless it is specified in the oath. Since eating a half-measure is not prohibited by Torah law, the oath takes effect. Alternatively, you find that one is liable **if** he took the oath **without specifying** that the oath prohibits less than the usual measure, and **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva, who says** that **a person renders himself prohibited from** eating **any amount** by taking an oath not to eat.

Reish Lakish disagrees. Merely bundling a permitted item with a forbidden one, in his view, is insufficient to make the *shvua* take effect on the forbidden item. The forbidden item — say, *chazir* — is already the subject of a standing prohibition, and a *shvua* cannot add to that regardless of what else is included in the same statement.

For Reish Lakish, the only way a *shvua* takes effect with respect to forbidden food is if the oath forbids something that was, until that moment, permitted to do. That condition is satisfied in one of two ways. First, if the person explicitly specifies less than a *kezayit* — *meforesh chatzi shiur* — because eating less than a *kezayit* of non-kosher food is not biblically prohibited, a *shvua* can forbid that sub-threshold amount and thereby genuinely create a new prohibition. Second, even without explicit specification, according to the view of Rabbi Akiva — who holds that a person's *shvua* can make even the smallest amount forbidden to him, *d'amar oser et atzmo be'chol she'hu* — the general statement of the oath automatically extends to quantities that are otherwise permitted, and the *shvua* takes effect on that basis. For Reish Lakish, then: *ee ata motzei ella im mefaresh chatzi shiur va'aliba de'rabbanan* — you cannot find a case where the *shvua* takes effect on non-kosher food unless the person explicitly specified less than a *kezayit*, according to the rabbis — *ee b'stam aliba de'Rabbi Akiva* — or unless one follows Rabbi Akiva, for whom even a general oath automatically applies to sub-*kezayit* amounts. This debate between Rav, Shmuel, and Rabbi Yochanan on the one hand and Reish Lakish on the other will be developed further in the discussion to follow.
