# Hullin 51 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Masess & Beit Hakosot

### Four Stomachs Intro

The sugya opens with a brief orientation to the anatomy under discussion. Ruminants possess, in effect, four stomach compartments. The first and largest is the *keres* (rumen), where food initially passes and begins to be ground down. From there it enters the *masess* and the *beit hakosot*, two intermediate chambers where further grinding occurs. Food deemed sufficiently processed moves on to the *keivah* — the true stomach, analogous to the human stomach — where digestion takes place; food that requires more processing is pumped back up through the esophagus to be chewed again.

### Holes: Between vs. Outside

The halachic framework for these two intermediate chambers differs from other organs precisely because of their functional relationship to one another: since food naturally passes between the *masess* and the *beit hakosot* in both directions depending on the stage of processing, the membrane dividing them is effectively permeable. A hole in the wall *between* the two chambers is therefore not a concern — it does not render the animal a *treifa* — because food traverses that wall in the normal course of digestion. It is only a hole that penetrates from the inner cavity of the *beit hakosot* all the way through to the outside that constitutes a *treifa*, and as we shall see, that wall is itself double-layered, introducing additional nuance.

## Holes in Beit Hakoso (II)

### Needle Perforation Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַחַט שֶׁנִּמְצֵאת בְּעוֹבִי בֵּית הַכּוֹסוֹת מִצַּד אֶחָד – כְּשֵׁרָה, מִשְּׁנֵי צְדָדִים – טְרֵפָה. נִמְצָא עָלֶיהָ קוֹרֶט דָּם –
>
> **Translation:** **a needle that is found in the thickness of the reticulum,** if it pierced the wall **from** only **one side,** the animal is **kosher.** If it pierced the wall **from both sides,** i.e., the needle completely pierced the wall of the stomach, it is **a *tereifa*.** But even if it protrudes from both sides, it is not always a *tereifa*, and it may be inspected further: If **a drop of** congealed **blood is found on top of** the needle,

The *Gemara* at the top of 51a continues with a *baraita*: if a needle is found embedded in the wall of the *beit hakosot*, the ruling depends on how far it has penetrated. The *beit hakosot* has two concentric layers of wall. If the needle has gone through only one of those layers — *mitzad echad*, from one side — the animal is *kesherah*. Only if it has passed through *both* walls, fully bridging the cavity to the outside, is it a *treifa*. This mirrors the general principle applied to many organs: a hole must traverse the entire thickness of the wall to disqualify the animal.

### Blood Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁלִּפְנֵי שְׁחִיטָה, לֹא נִמְצָא עָלֶיהָ קוֹרֶט דָּם – בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁלְּאַחַר שְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **it is certain that** the perforation was created **before** the **slaughter** of the animal, and it is therefore a *tereifa*. If **a drop of blood is not found on it, it is certain that it** occurred **after** the **slaughter,** when the blood of the animal had stopped flowing. The animal is therefore kosher.

Even when a needle has penetrated both walls and would otherwise constitute a *treifa*, there remains the question of *when* the hole was made. Throughout the past several days of learning, we have encountered a recurring principle: if the perforation occurred after *shechita*, it cannot retroactively render the animal a *treifa*. The *Gemara* now applies this to the needle case. If a drop of blood (*koret dam*) is found on the needle, this is considered proof that the piercing occurred before *shechita* — when blood was still coursing freely through the animal — and the *treifa* ruling stands.

### No Blood = After Shechita

Conversely, if no blood is found on the needle — *lo nimtsa lah koret dam* — this is taken as evidence that the hole was made *after shechita*, and the animal is permitted. This is a striking conclusion. Even with direct physical evidence of a foreign object having created the hole, the absence of blood adhering to it suffices to attribute the piercing to the post-*shechita* period, when far less blood is in active circulation through the tissue. The logic will be elaborated shortly, but the practical upshot is already clear: the absence of blood on a foreign object is a powerful exculpatory indicator.

## Scab & Mekach Taot

### Scab Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִגְלִיד פִּי הַמַּכָּה – בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁשְּׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים קוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה. לֹא הִגְלִיד פִּי הַמַּכָּה – הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** If **a scab covered the opening of the wound,** i.e., the perforation, **it is certain that** the perforation occurred **three days before** the **slaughter.** Consequently, if the animal was sold less than three days before the slaughter, the buyer can claim that the transaction was performed in error, as he did not intend to purchase a *tereifa* animal, and the seller must refund the buyer. If **a scab did not cover the opening of the wound,** and it is uncertain whether the perforation occurred before or after the animal was purchased, **the burden of proof rests upon the claimant.** Accordingly, the buyer must furnish proof that the perforation occurred before the purchase in order to demand a refund.

The *baraita* continues: *higlit pi hamaka* — if the opening of the wound has formed a scab. A scab's formation is taken as evidence that the hole is at least three days old, since it takes approximately three days for that kind of natural crust to develop. This establishes not only that the hole predated *shechita* but also that it was already present for at least three days prior.

One might ask: once we know the hole predates *shechita*, the animal is a *treifa* regardless — why does the three-day timeframe matter? The answer becomes apparent when we consider the commercial context. If someone purchased an animal within the last three days and then slaughtered it only to discover a *treifa*, the presence of a scab is direct evidence that the defect existed at the time of purchase. This opens the door to a claim of *mekach ta'os* — a defective sale — and a demand for reimbursement.

### Burden of Proof

This brings the *Gemara* to a remarkable halachic discussion. If a seller sells a cow to a buyer who proceeds to slaughter it and discovers it is a *treifa*, can the buyer demand the money back? The answer seems counterintuitive at first glance: yes, in principle the buyer can — even if the seller had no knowledge of the defect and there was no misrepresentation. The principle of *mekach ta'os* can operate even without deceit; if the item was clearly not fit for the purpose for which it was purchased, the sale may be voided.

This seems deeply problematic from a commercial standpoint. A seller of livestock would be assuming enormous risk: a meaningful percentage of animals are *treifos*, and if every such case entitled the buyer to a refund of a slaughtered animal (now worth only what can be sold to non-Jews, not at kosher prices, and certainly not returnable alive), the business would be untenable.

The resolution, for the vast majority of cases, is the principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav haraya* — the burden of proof falls on the one seeking to extract from another. If the buyer has already paid and now wants the money back, it is the buyer who must prove that the defect existed at the time of purchase. In most cases this is impossible, and so the claim fails. The seller can simply say: how do you know the animal did not become a *treifa* in your possession, after I sold it to you?

### Kan Nimtza Kan Haya

*Tosafot* introduces an additional dimension. Beyond the question of who holds the money, there may be an independent presumptive principle: *kan nimtza kan haya* — where the defect was discovered, there it is presumed to have originated. Under this logic, if the *treifa* was found in the buyer's possession, we presume it arose during that time, regardless of who holds the money. The scab case is the exception: the scab is direct evidence that the hole is at least three days old and therefore predates the purchase, shifting the presumption.

This produces a somewhat unusual system. The default assumption, even when a defect is discovered, is that it arose under the current owner's watch — and only concrete evidence, like a scab, overrides that. Without such evidence, *hamotzi mechavero alav haraya* protects the seller (if payment has been made), or *kan nimtza kan haya* supplies the presumption (as *Tosafot* would have it), leaving the buyer without recourse in most cases.

### Seller's Risk

There is an additional variable: the timing of payment. Since halachic acquisition (*kinyan*) takes effect when the buyer takes possession of the animal — independent of whether payment has been made — it is possible that the buyer takes possession and has not yet paid. In that case, the seller is now the one trying to extract money from the buyer. If the buyer then claims the animal was a *treifa* at the time of sale, it is the seller who bears the burden of proof. Similarly, the buyer's intended use matters: if it is clear that the buyer was a butcher purchasing the animal for *shechita*, the seller cannot later claim he assumed the animal was being purchased for plowing. But if the buyer's intended use is ambiguous, that too may factor into the analysis.

The underlying commercial reality is stark: in a world where a certain percentage of animals will inevitably be *treifos*, the seller is, in effect, assuming that risk in most cases where the buyer can make a showing. The principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav haraya* rescues the seller in the majority of situations, but it does not eliminate the exposure entirely.

## Foreign Objects & Blood Evidence

### Needle vs. Natural Holes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא מִכֹּל נְקוּבֵי דְּעָלְמָא, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵיכָּא דָּם טָרֵיף מָר? הָתָם לֵיכָּא מִידֵּי לְמִיסְרַךְ, הָכָא כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא מַחַט, אִי אִיתָא דְּקוֹדֶם שְׁחִיטָה הוּא – מִיסְרָךְ הֲוָה סָרֵיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** in **what** way **is** this case **different from all other perforations, where even though there is no blood** on the wound the **Master deems** the animal **a *tereifa*?** The Gemara responds: **There,** in all other cases, **there is nothing to** which the blood can **attach.** Even if the wound had bled, the blood would be reabsorbed into the flesh. **Here, since there is a needle,** it follows that **if it is** the case that the perforation occurred **before slaughter,** blood from the wound **would have attached** to the needle. Accordingly, if there is no blood on the needle, it is certain that the perforation occurred after slaughter.

The *Gemara* now returns to address a question that had been left implicit: why, in the needle case, can we use the absence of blood as a basis for ruling the animal permitted? In other cases of holes — naturally occurring perforations — even without blood we would not feel comfortable attributing the hole to the post-*shechita* period. What makes the needle different?

The *Gemara* explains: *hasam leka midi lemisrach* — in cases of naturally occurring holes, there is no foreign object for the blood to adhere to. *Hacha*, here, since there is a needle, *ei isa dekodem shechita hu* — if it truly was pierced before *shechita*, when blood was freely flowing through the living animal, blood would necessarily have adhered to the metal. The needle provides a surface for the blood to stick to. Therefore, if the needle is clean, one can conclude with confidence that the piercing occurred after *shechita*, when circulation had largely ceased.

This crystallizes an important structural principle running through this entire section: holes in internal organs are not solved primarily by membranes or fat occluding them, though those mechanisms exist. They are solved, far more commonly, by attributing them to the post-*shechita* period through various evidentiary means — yesterday through comparison wounds made by the *shochet*, and now through the physical properties of whatever foreign object caused the hole. A hole made by a foreign object that leaves no blood behind is, in this framework, presumptively post-*shechita*.

## Rav Avira's Story

### Needle Half-Through Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב סָפְרָא לְאַבָּיֵי: חֲזִי מָר הַאי צוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן דַּאֲתָא מִמַּעְרְבָא, וְאָמַר: רַב עַוִּירָא שְׁמֵנִי, וְאָמַר: מַעֲשֶׂה וּבָא לִפְנֵי רַבִּי מַחַט שֶׁנִּמְצֵאת בְּעוֹבִי בֵּית הַכּוֹסוֹת מִצַּד אֶחָד, וּטְרָפָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Safra said to Abaye:** Did **the Master see a Torah scholar who came from the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **and said: My name is Rav Avira? He said** that there was **an incident that came before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi involving **a needle that was found in the thickness of the reticulum** protruding **from** only **one side, and he deemed** the animal **a *tereifa*.**

This is deeply troubling. The *baraita* we have just been learning makes unambiguous that a needle through a single wall is *kesherah*. For a needle that penetrated both walls — fully through — it is a *treifa*. Abaye, hearing this, is understandably unsettled: this appears to be a direct contradiction of established *halacha* in the name of the foremost authority of the Tannaitic period.

### Abaye's Investigation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לֵיהּ לָא אֲתָא לְגַבֵּיהּ, אֲזַל הוּא לְגַבֵּיהּ. הֲוָה קָאֵי אַאִיגָּרָא, אָמַר: נֵיחוֹת מָר וְנֵיתֵי. לָא נָחֵית, סָלֵיק הוּא לְגַבֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵימָא לִי גּוּפָא דְעוֹבָדָא הֵיכִי הֲוָה?
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **sent** a message **to** Rav Avira calling for him to come and explain the matter, since this contradicts the *baraita* that states that the animal is a *tereifa* only if the needle protrudes from both sides. Rav Avira **did not come before him,** so Abaye **went before** Rav Avira. Rav Avira **was standing on the roof.** Abaye **said to him: Let Master descend and come,** but Rav Avira **did not descend.** Abaye **ascended to him** and **said to him: Say to me, what were the circumstances of the incident itself?**

Abaye sends word to Rav Avira requesting that he come and discuss the matter. Rav Avira declines to come. Abaye, rather than letting the matter drop, goes to him. He finds Rav Avira up on the roof engaged in some task. Abaye asks him to come down so they can sit and speak. Rav Avira refuses to descend. So Abaye climbs up to him.

The scene is worth pausing on. Here is Abaye — a leading *amora* of his generation — climbing onto someone's roof because this person has information he needs, despite the fact that the man has refused every invitation to simply come and talk. The episode reflects something important about Abaye's character: the imperative to get the facts straight overrides considerations of personal dignity.

Once on the roof, Abaye says: *eima li gufa de'uvda heichi hava* — tell me the details of the incident, exactly how it unfolded.

### Blood Drop Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַפְטִיר כְּנֵסִיּוֹת אֲנָא, לְעֵילָּא מֵרַבִּי רַבָּה. וַהֲוָה רַב הוּנָא צִיפּוֹרָאָה וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי מָדָאָה יוֹשְׁבִין לְפָנָיו, וּבָאת לִפְנֵי רַבִּי מַחַט שֶׁנִּמְצֵאת בְּעוֹבִי בֵּית הַכּוֹסוֹת מִצַּד אֶחָד, וַהֲפָכָהּ רַבִּי וּמָצָא עָלֶיהָ קוֹרֶט דָּם וּטְרָפָהּ, וְאָמַר: אִם אֵין שָׁם מַכָּה, קוֹרֶט דָּם מִנַּיִן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: טְרָא טַרְיֵיהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא! מַתְנִיתִין הִיא: הֶמְסֵס וּבֵית הַכּוֹסוֹת שֶׁנִּיקְּבוּ לַחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Avira **said to him: I am a director of assemblies** in the study hall. I was standing **above the Great Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and Rav Huna of Tzippori and Rabbi Yosei of Media were sitting before him, and a needle came before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **that was found in the thickness of the reticulum** protruding **from one side,** i.e., the inside, **and Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **turned** the reticulum **over and found a drop of blood on** the outside, parallel to the wound on the inside, **and he deemed** the animal **a *tereifa*. And he said: If there is no wound** on the outside **there** as well, **from where** is this **drop of blood?** Abaye **said to** Rav Avira: **He caused that man trouble** needlessly, i.e., you troubled me for no reason. **This is** nothing more than **the mishna,** which states that an animal is a *tereifa* if the **omasum or the reticulum was perforated to the outside.**

Rav Avira explains that he was the *maftir* — the one who would assemble and dismiss Rabbi's students, the gatherer and releaser of the congregation before Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi the Great. Sitting before Rabbi at the time were two otherwise unknown figures: Rav Huna of Tzippori and Rabbi Yosi of Medea (or a similar locale). It is worth noting that the chronology here is somewhat strained: Abaye lived roughly from 278 to 338 CE, while Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi flourished around 200 CE. For Rav Avira to have personally served in Rabbi's academy and still be alive and accessible to Abaye would have required him to have been quite young in Rabbi's time and quite elderly when Abaye sought him out — the timeline just barely works, but it is pushing it.

Rav Avira continues: the case that came before Rabbi was indeed a needle in the wall of the *beit hakosot* from one side only — *mitzad echad*. And Rabbi declared it a *treifa*.

At this point, Rav Avira reveals the crucial detail that had been lost in transmission. Rabbi examined the wall and turned it over — *hafcha* — and on the other side found a drop of blood, *koret dam*. Even though no visible hole could be seen on the far side, the drop of blood established that the needle had in fact penetrated all the way through. The presence of blood on the far wall was the evidence that the perforation was complete — the needle had traversed both layers. On *that* basis, Rabbi declared it a *treifa*. The ruling was never about a needle through only one wall. It was always about a needle that had in fact gone all the way through, as established by the blood evidence on the other side.

As Rav Avira himself explains: *im ein sham maka koret dam minalan* — if there were no perforation going all the way through, how would a drop of blood have gotten to the far side at all?

### Broken Telephone

Abaye's reaction is exasperation. The ruling is simply our *mishnah*: a needle that goes through to the outside is a *treifa*. Rabbi merely inferred that it had gone all the way through from the presence of the blood drop. There was no novel ruling, no departure from established *halacha*, no precedent that a one-sided perforation could disqualify an animal.

The episode is a vivid illustration of the problem of *broken telephone* in the transmission of traditions. A story circulates that Rabbi declared a one-sided needle a *treifa* — and that, taken at face value, appears to overturn well-established law. The reality, once Abaye goes through the considerable effort of extracting the full details, is that the ruling was entirely conventional; the dramatic detail — Rabbi turned the wall over and found a blood drop on the far side — had simply dropped out of the transmission. The lesson drawn is not about the specific law of needles and walls, but about the imperative to pursue the full factual record of any tradition rather than accepting a summary that may have lost something crucial in the retelling. There is also, as noted, something quietly powerful in the image of Abaye climbing onto a stranger's roof in pursuit of that record, regardless of what it cost him in effort or dignity — even if he permitted himself a moment of frustration when it turned out to have been unnecessary.

## Nefulah & Risuk Eivarim

### Nefulah Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפְלָה מִן הַגָּג
>
> **Translation:** An animal that **fell from the roof**

The *sugya* now turns to a new category of *treifa* that does not depend on finding any specific lesion or perforation in any organ: *nefulah*, the fallen animal. The *mishnah* states simply *nafla min hagag* — it fell from the roof — and declares it a *treifa*. This is a fundamentally different type of disqualification. Unlike holes in the lung or perforations of the stomach, the *treifa* here is not identified by pointing to a damaged organ. Rather, the very event of the fall is itself the disqualifying factor, under the category known as *risuk eivarim* — the shattering or loosening of the limbs.

The *Gemara* will make two significant additions to the bare *mishnah*, one stringent and one lenient. The stringent addition: even if you were to check all of the internal organs and find nothing abnormal, that bedika would be insufficient to permit the animal. The *risuk eivarim* is itself a form of *treifa*, not merely a presumption that some other *treifa* lurks inside. The lenient addition — which we will reach later — is that the problem can be resolved if the animal subsequently gets up and walks around, or survives for twenty-four hours. This leniency is hinted at in a parallel *mishnah* regarding birds, which speaks of an animal that survives *me'eis le'eis*, twenty-four hours, after a traumatic impact.

On the question of how severe a fall is necessary to trigger this concern: the *Gemara* in *Bava Kamma* establishes that the threshold is ten *tefachim*, roughly three feet. That may not sound dramatic, but the angle and manner of landing obviously matter enormously.

### Found Below Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הִנִּיחַ בְּהֵמָה לְמַעְלָה, וּבָא וּמְצָאָהּ לְמַטָּה – אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין מִשּׁוּם רִיסּוּקֵי אֵבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna says:** If one **left an animal above,** on the roof, **and he came** back **and found it below,** but did not see it fall, **one** need **not be concerned with regard to the shattering of limbs.** One may presume that it jumped intentionally and was not injured.

Rav Huna introduces a significant qualification: *hiniach behemah l'maalo u-va u-matsah l'mato* — if you left an animal on a roof and returned to find it at the bottom, we are not concerned about *risuk eivarim*. The reason is epistemic: we do not know *how* it got down. Perhaps it climbed down. Perhaps it jumped rather than fell. Since we cannot establish that it actually fell in the problematic manner, we do not impose the concern.

### Goat Skylight Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּדְיָא דַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ לְרָבִינָא, דַּחֲזָא חוּשְׁלָא בְּאִיפּוּמָא דְּגַר, נְפַל מֵאִיגָּרָא לְאַרְעָא, אַתְיוּהּ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב אָשֵׁי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָא דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא ״הִנִּיחַ בְּהֵמָה לְמַעְלָה וּבָא וּמְצָאָהּ לְמַטָּה אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין מִשּׁוּם רִיסּוּקֵי אֵבָרִים״, מִשּׁוּם דְּאִית לַהּ מִידֵּי לְמִסְרַךְ, וְהַאי לֵית לֵיהּ מִידֵּי לְמִסְרַךְ? אוֹ דִלְמָא מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְדָה נַפְשָׁהּ, וְהַאי נָמֵי אָמֵדה נַפְשֵׁהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain kid belonging to Ravina that saw barley groats through** an open **skylight. It jumped** down through the skylight and **fell from the roof to the ground.** The case **came before Rav Ashi,** and Ravina **said to him:** What is the rationale for **this** statement **that Rav Huna says,** that if one **left an animal above and he came** back **and found it below, one** need **not be concerned about the** possible **shattering of limbs?** Is it **because** the animal usually **has something to grab hold of?** If so, since **this** kid jumped through a skylight, it **did not have something to grab hold of,** and one must be concerned. **Or perhaps** it is **because** the animal **evaluates itself** and determines that it can jump without injury. If so, **this** kid **also evaluated itself** before jumping, and one need not be concerned.

This ruling is immediately tested by a case from Ravina's household. Ravina had a goat that apparently caught sight of barley visible through a skylight in the roof. The goat jumped from the roof to the ground below in order to get at the barley. The case came before Rav Ashi. Ravina's question was whether Rav Huna's ruling — that finding an animal below without witnessing the fall is not a concern — applies here, where the mechanism of descent is known. The goat did not climb down; it leaped through a skylight. Does Rav Huna's principle help here, and if so, why?

### Amda Nafsha Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְדָה נַפְשָׁהּ, וְהַאי נָמֵי אָמֵדה נַפְשֵׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said to him:** It is **because** the animal **evaluates itself** before jumping, **and this** kid **also evaluated itself** before jumping. Therefore, one need not be concerned about the possible shattering of limbs.

Rav Ashi identifies two possible rationales for Rav Huna's ruling, and the choice between them determines the outcome in the goat case.

The first rationale: perhaps the animal is not a concern because when descending from a wall, it may have gripped the edge of the wall — *midi lemisrach*, something to adhere to — and thereby broken its fall. If this is the reason, it would not apply to the goat, which leaped through a skylight in the middle of a roof with nothing to grip on the way down.

The second rationale: *amda nafsha* — the animal assessed its own strength. An animal that willfully jumps knows its own capabilities and is able to orient itself for landing; it knows whether it can make the leap safely, and it will land on its feet and absorb the impact. If this is the reason, then the goat case is not different at all — the goat also made a voluntary leap and assessed what it was doing.

Rav Ashi's answer is that the latter rationale is correct: *mishum d'amda nafsha*, because the animal gauged itself, *v'hai nami amda nafsha*, and the same is true of this goat. A voluntary jump is categorically different from an accidental fall, and it does not trigger the *risuk eivarim* concern regardless of whether there was anything for the animal to grip on the way down.

## Ramming & Impact

### Mutual Ramming Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: זְכָרִים הַמְנַגְּחִין זֶה אֶת זֶה – אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין מִשּׁוּם רִיסּוּקֵי אֵבָרִים, אַף עַל גַּב דְּמִידְּווּ וְקָיְימִי – צִימְרָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דְּנָקֵט לְהוּ; אִי נְפוּל לְאַרְעָא – וַדַּאי חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna** also **says:** With regard to **rams that butt one another, one** need **not be concerned with regard to shattering of limbs. Even though they are in pain and stand** still, it is **only a fever that afflicts them;** one need not be concerned about the possibility of severe injuries. But **if they fall to the ground** due to the impact, **we certainly must be concerned** that their limbs may have been shattered.

Rav Huna rules that rams engaging in their characteristic mutual ramming behavior — *dichrei hamenagchin zeh et zeh* — are not a concern for *risuk eivarim*, even when the impact is strong enough to make them groan in pain. The groaning, the *Gemara* explains, reflects the exertion involved, not a genuine traumatic injury of the kind that would loosen limbs. As long as neither animal is thrown to the ground, the violence of the ramming does not rise to the level of concern.

However, *i nefal l'ara* — if as a result of the impact one animal is thrown down to the ground — then we are indeed concerned. Even without a fall from a height, the impact of hitting the ground after being knocked down by a ram is sufficient to raise the question of *risuk eivarim*.

### Thieves Throwing Rams

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מְנַשֵּׁי: הָנֵי דִּכְרֵי דְּגָנְבִי גַּנָּבֵי – אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין מִשּׁוּם רִיסּוּק אֵבָרִים, מַאי טַעְמָא? כִּי שָׁדוּ לְהוּ אַמׇּתְנַיְיהוּ שָׁדוּ לְהוּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִירְהֲטוּ קַמַּיְיהוּ. אַהְדְּרִינְהוּ – וַדַּאי חָיְישִׁינַן; וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי דְּאַהְדְּרִינְהוּ מֵחֲמַת יִרְאָה, אֲבָל מֵחֲמַת תְּשׁוּבָה – תְּשׁוּבָה מְעַלַּיְיתָא הוּא דְּעָבְדִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Menashei says:** With regard to **these rams that thieves steal** and throw over the fence, **one** need **not be concerned with regard to the shattering of limbs. What is the reason** for this? **When** the thieves **throw them** over the fence, **they throw them** so that they land **on their hips,** where they will not be injured, **so that they will** be able to **run before them.** But if the thieves **returned them** to the owner, **we certainly must be concerned** that their limbs may have been shattered, since thieves do not throw them carefully when returning them. **And this statement** applies only **when they return them due to fear** of being caught, or are otherwise forced to return them. **But** if they return them **due to repentance, they have performed full-fledged repentance** and will take care to return them without injury.

Rav Menasheh addresses a case that captures something of the texture of everyday life in the rabbinic period: thieves who steal rams by throwing them over walls. This would presumably render the rams subject to *risuk eivarim*, since they are being thrown and falling. Rav Menasheh rules that we are not concerned. The reason: *amatzvayei shadi lehu* — the thieves throw them so that the rams land on their feet (literally "on their sides," but meaning feet-first). The thieves' practical interest dictates the manner of the throw: they want the animal to land and start running so they can make their escape. A ram thrown feet-first can absorb the landing and will not suffer *risuk eivarim*.

### Theft Return Cases

The situation changes when the thieves throw the animal back. If they hear the authorities approaching and hastily toss the ram back over the wall to avoid being caught — *mechamat yira*, out of fear — we are indeed concerned, because in their panic they are not careful about how the animal lands.

But if the thieves act out of genuine *teshuvah* — they made their escape, and subsequently, moved by remorse, they return the animal — then we assume they acted with care, *teshuvah maliyuta hi de-avdi*, they are doing a sincere *teshuvah* and would not have thrown the animal carelessly. In that case, we assume they returned it carefully and we are not concerned. How one determines after the fact whether the return was driven by fear or by repentance is not specified explicitly; presumably the timing and circumstances would provide some indication — a return well after the fact, with no imminent danger, more readily suggests genuine *teshuvah*.

### Stick Striking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: הִכָּהּ עַל רֹאשָׁהּ וְהָלְכָה לָהּ כְּלַפֵּי זְנָבָהּ, עַל זְנָבָהּ וְהָלְכָה לָהּ כְּלַפֵּי רֹאשָׁהּ, כְּנֶגֶד כׇּל הַשִּׁדְרָה כּוּלָּהּ – אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין מִשּׁוּם רִיסּוּקֵי אֵבָרִים, וְאִי שְׁלֵים חוּטְרָא אַפַּלְגֵי דְּגַבָּא – חָיְישִׁינַן; וְאִי אִית בַּהּ קִיטְרֵי – חָיְישִׁינַן; וְאִי מַחְיַיהּ אַפַּסְקִית – חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** If **one hit** an animal **on its head** with a stick, **and** the length of the stick **continued toward its back;** or if one hit the animal **on its tail and** the length of the stick **continued toward its head,** such that in either case the blow extended **along the entire spine, one** need **not be concerned with regard to the shattering of limbs. But if the** length of the **stick concluded at the middle of the back, we** must be **concerned** that the strike caused an injury to the spine. **And if there are knots,** i.e., protrusions, **on** the stick, **we** must be **concerned** that it injured the spine, even if the stick fell across the entire back. **And if he struck it like a slash** across the width of the back, **we** must **be concerned** that it injured the spine.

Rav Yehuda says in the name of Rav: if one strikes an animal on its head with a long stick — long enough that the length of the stick spans the full length of the animal from head to tail — and the impact travels toward the tail, or conversely, one strikes at the tail and the motion carries toward the head along the full length of the spine, we are *not* concerned about *risuk eivarim*. The reason is that when a stick of such length comes down along the length of the animal, the force is distributed and attenuated; the tip of the stick is actually doing relatively little work at any one point. The impact does not create the kind of concentrated trauma to the spine that would cause *risuk eivarim*.

However, if the stick is shorter and its impact terminates at the midpoint of the animal's back — *palga de-gaba* — then we *are* concerned, because the force of impact is concentrated at a single point along the spine and can cause real damage.

Additionally, even a long stick causes concern if it has knots — *kitrei* — the protruding nodes where branches once joined the trunk. These knots create localized points of impact, so that when the stick comes down, even a distributed blow delivers concentrated force at each knot. Similarly, if one strikes the animal *a-pitya* — width-wise, perpendicular to the length of the animal — that is a direct impact on the spine at a single concentrated point, and we are again concerned.

In all these cases of concern, the underlying problem — *risuk eivarim* — remains to be addressed. As will become clear shortly, subsequent movement by the animal is the key to resolving it. Notably, the *Gemara* has been building an extensive catalogue of concerning scenarios without yet stating how they are resolved, and the resolution — walking, standing — will only be articulated at the end.

## Birth & Recovery

### Birth Not Traumatic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: בֵּית הָרֶחֶם אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם רִיסּוּקֵי אֵבָרִים. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: תַּנְיָא דִּמְסַיַּיע לָךְ, תִּינוֹק בֶּן יוֹם אֶחָד
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says: The womb is not subject to** concern about possible **shattered limbs.** In other words, one need not be concerned that the limbs of a newborn calf may have been shattered by the narrow birth canal. **Rava said to Rav Naḥman:** That which **is taught** in a *baraita* **supports your** opinion: **A one-day-old infant**

Rav Nachman rules: *beis harechem ein bo mishum risuk eivarim* — the womb is not a source of *risuk eivarim*. The experience of being born does not constitute the kind of traumatic impact that would classify the newborn animal as a *treifa* under this category. Rava supports this with a *mishnah*: a one-day-old infant boy can become a *zav* if he has a penile emission. The laws of *zav* require that the emission be a genuine pathological flow, not one caused by external trauma — *mibsaro v'lo mechamat onso*. If birth itself were the kind of physical trauma that could cause *risuk eivarim* in an animal, we would have reason to say that a newborn's emission is a product of that trauma, and he would not qualify as a *zav*. The fact that a one-day-old boy *can* become a *zav* implies that we do not regard birth as that kind of externally imposed physical trauma.

### Cesarean Section

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְטַמֵּא בְּזִיבָה, וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם רִיסּוּקֵי אֵבָרִים, אִיקְרִי כָּאן ״מִבְּשָׂרוֹ״ וְלֹא מֵחֲמַת אוֹנְסוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **becomes** ritually **impure** if he experiences a gonorrhea-like **discharge [*ziva*]. And if it should enter your mind** that a newborn **is subject to** concern about possible **shattered limbs, read here** the ruling stated with regard to *ziva*, that one's discharge renders one impure only when it issues **"out of his flesh"** (Leviticus 15:2), **but not** when it issues **due to circumstances beyond his control,** e.g., due to illness. Therefore, if one must be concerned that a newborn's limbs may have shattered during birth, his discharge would not render him impure.

The *Gemara* pushes back: perhaps this proof is limited to cases of *derech dofen* — cesarean section — where the infant was simply removed from the womb without passing through the birth canal. Perhaps natural birth *is* traumatic, and it is only the cesarean case where the *zav* status applies cleanly. The argument from the newborn *zav* would then be insufficient to prove that natural birth is not a cause of *risuk eivarim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, כְּגוֹן שֶׁיָּצָא דֶּרֶךְ דּוֹפֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: One cannot bring a proof from this *baraita*, since **here we are dealing with** a case **where** the infant **left** the womb **by caesarean section** and did not exit through the narrow birth canal. By contrast, after normal births, a newborn's discharge does not render it impure, since one is concerned about the possibility of shattered limbs.

The *Gemara* then considers a second proof: *egel shenolad b'yom tov shochtin oso b'yom tov* — a calf born on *Yom Tov* may be slaughtered on that same *Yom Tov*, since it is not *muktzeh*. The reasoning is that even while still in the womb before *Yom Tov* began, the fetus was in principle fit to be eaten (had the mother been slaughtered), and thus the calf is not newly fit on *Yom Tov* in a way that would make it *muktzeh*. But if birth caused *risuk eivarim*, how could one slaughter the calf on the day of its birth? It might be a *treifa*! The *Gemara* again deflects: perhaps this refers to a calf born by cesarean section — *derech dofen* — which is not subject to the physical trauma of natural birth.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: עֵגֶל שֶׁנּוֹלַד בְּיוֹם טוֹב – שׁוֹחֲטִין אוֹתוֹ בְּיוֹם טוֹב. הָכָא נָמֵי, כְּגוֹן שֶׁיָּצָא דֶּרֶךְ דּוֹפֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** proof from a *baraita*: If **a calf was born on a Festival, one may slaughter it** and eat it **on** the **Festival.** One need not wait twenty-four hours before deeming the animal kosher, as one does for an animal that fell from a roof. Apparently, one need not be concerned that its limbs were shattered during the birth. The Gemara responds: **Here, too,** the *baraita* is referring to a case **where** the calf **left** the womb **by caesarean section.** But after normal births, one must wait twenty-four hours before slaughtering the calf.

The second variant of the proof involves a *bechor* — a firstborn animal — born on *Yom Tov* already bearing a blemish (*mum*). Both sides of the *muktzeh* debate agree that such an animal, born with its *mum*, can be slaughtered on *Yom Tov* because it was fit to be eaten from the moment of birth. Here the *Gemara* attempts the same deflection — perhaps it was born by cesarean section — but this is immediately foreclosed. An animal born *derech dofen* cannot have *bechor* status at all. As *Tosafot* emphasizes, the entire concept of *bechor* depends on *peter rechem* — emerging naturally from the womb — and Rabbi Yochanan states explicitly that even Rabbi Shimon, who holds that cesarean birth creates *tumah* obligations for the mother in the context of human childbirth, would agree that for purposes of sacrifices the animal is not sanctified. Since the case must involve a *bechor*, it must involve natural birth, and yet slaughter on the day of birth is unproblematic. This would seem to prove definitively that natural birth does not cause *risuk eivarim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: וְשָׁוִין שֶׁאִם נוֹלַד הוּא וּמוּמוֹ עִמּוֹ, שֶׁזֶּה מִן הַמּוּכָן. וְכִי תֵּימָא הָכָא נָמֵי שֶׁיָּצָא דֶּרֶךְ דּוֹפֶן – דֶּרֶךְ דּוֹפֶן מִי קָדוֹשׁ? וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מוֹדֶה הָיָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְעִנְיַן קָדָשִׁים שֶׁאֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** proof from another *baraita*: **And** all **agree that if** a firstborn animal **was born** on a Festival **and its blemish** was born **with it,** i.e., if it was born with a blemish that removes its sanctity and renders it permitted for consumption, **it is** considered to be **prepared** for the Festival and may be eaten. Evidently, one need not wait twenty-four hours before deeming it kosher. **And if you would say** that **here too,** the *baraita* deals with a case **where** the animal **left** the womb **by caesarean section,** one might respond: **Is** a firstborn animal born **by caesarean section sanctified? But doesn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say: Rabbi Shimon would concede with regard to sacrificial** animals, e.g., a firstborn, **that** an animal born by caesarean section **is not sanctified?** Therefore, the *baraita* must be referring to an animal born naturally.

### Standing Up Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, שֶׁהִפְרִיס עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Here we are dealing with** a firstborn **that spread out** its legs **on the ground** and stood up immediately after birth. In such a case the animal is certainly not a *tereifa* on account of shattered limbs.

The *Gemara* offers a final escape: *shehifris al gabi karka* — perhaps the case is one where the animal had already gotten to its feet. This finally introduces, even if only implicitly, the principle that subsequent movement resolves the *risuk eivarim* concern. *Rashi* suggests that even the beginning of the process — the animal moving into position to stand — is sufficient. The significance of this deflection is that it points toward the resolution that will be stated explicitly in the next section: what the animal does after the traumatic event determines whether the *risuk eivarim* concern remains.

### Slaughterhouse Casting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: בֵּית הַמִּטְבָּחַיִם אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם רִיסּוּקֵי אֵבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Naḥman says:** If an animal was thrown to the ground in **the slaughterhouse** in preparation for slaughter, it **is not subject to** concern for **shattered limbs.**

Rav Nachman adds a parallel ruling: *beis hamatbechayim ein bo mishum risuk eivarim* — the slaughterhouse casting procedure does not cause *risuk eivarim*. When an animal is brought down for slaughter — through tumbling machines or other means of flipping it to expose the neck — even though this might seem like the kind of impact that triggers the concern, it does not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא תּוֹרָא דִּנְפַל, וְאִישְׁתְּמַע קָל גְּנִיחוֹתֵיהּ, עָל רַב יִצְחָק בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר מָרְתָא שְׁקַל מִשּׁוּפְרֵי שׁוּפְרֵי. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן: מְנָא לָךְ הָא? אֲמַר לְהוּ: הָכִי אָמַר רַב: צִפׇּרְנָיו נוֹעֵץ עַד שֶׁמַּגִּיעַ לָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **A certain bull fell** in the slaughterhouse, **and its bellowing was audible** due to the blow. Still, **Rav Yitzḥak bar Shmuel bar Marta entered** and **took from the very best** portions of the bull and was not concerned that it may have been a *tereifa*. **The Sages said to him: From where** did you learn **this?** Rav Yitzḥak bar Shmuel bar Marta **said to them: This is what Rav said:** When falling, the bull **digs in its hooves until it reaches the earth.** Therefore, one need not be concerned that it fell roughly.

The *Gemara* illustrates with a graphic incident: an ox was cast down, and its groaning could be heard from a distance. Rav Yitzchak bar Shmuel bar Marta nevertheless slaughtered it and took the choicest cut of meat — a demonstration that it was permitted. When questioned by the other rabbis about his basis, he cited Rav: *tzippornav no'etz ad shemagia l'aretz* — the animal digs in its hooves as it is being brought down, resisting the fall. That resistance, that instinctive bracing against the ground as the animal is pushed down from a standing position, breaks the impact. It is categorically different from falling from a height without preparation or control. The animal, in effect, partially controls its own descent, and that is sufficient to eliminate the *risuk eivarim* concern.

The description is deliberately unflinching: the animal is in evident pain, its distress audible from a distance. Yet the legal analysis is entirely technical — not whether this constitutes *tza'ar ba'alei chayim*, but whether the specific biomechanics of the impact meet the threshold for *risuk eivarim*. The *Gemara* throughout this discussion is concerned with *kashrut*, not with suffering, and the two analyses are kept entirely separate.

## Movement Resolution

### Standing Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: עָמְדָה – אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה מֵעֵת לְעֵת, בְּדִיקָה – וַדַּאי בָּעֲיָא, הָלְכָה – אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה בְּדִיקָה. רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אַחַת זוֹ וְאַחַת זוֹ צְרִיכָה בְּדִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** If an animal fell and **stood** up again, **it does not require** a twenty-four-hour **period** mentioned in the mishna (56b) to determine if it may be slaughtered. Nevertheless, **it certainly requires inspection** after slaughter to determine whether it was injured by the fall and rendered a *tereifa*. But if it both stood up and **walked** after the fall, **it does not** even **require inspection** after slaughter. **Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi says: Both** in **this** case **and** in **that** case, i.e., even if it walked after the fall, **it requires inspection.**

Rav Yehuda says in the name of Rav: *amda eina tzricha me'eis le'eis* — if the animal stood up after the fall, it does not require waiting twenty-four hours. This is the first explicit statement that standing up resolves the problem, and it implicitly confirms that waiting twenty-four hours also works — it simply turns out not to be necessary if the animal stands. The parallel with the *mishnah* by birds, which speaks of surviving *me'eis le'eis* after a traumatic impact, has been in the background throughout this discussion; here it is quietly confirmed and then superseded by the standing criterion.

However, *bedika badei ba'ya* — standing up, while eliminating the twenty-four-hour requirement, does not eliminate the need for a *bedika* of the internal organs after *shechita*. The animal gets out of the specific *risuk eivarim* status, but *shechita* must still be followed by an internal examination.

### Walking vs. Standing

The optimal outcome is walking. *Halacha* dictates that if the animal walked, *eina tzricha bedika* — no internal examination is required. Walking is the clearest and most complete evidence that no serious internal damage occurred, and it removes all residual concerns. Standing alone resolves the *risuk eivarim* concern but leaves the *bedika* requirement in place.

### Partial Movement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַחָא אָמַר רַב: פָּשְׁטָה יָדָהּ לַעֲמוֹד, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא עָמְדָה. עָקְרָה רַגְלָהּ לְהַלֵּךְ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא הָלְכָה. וְרַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: נִנְעֲרָה לַעֲמוֹד, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא עָמְדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yirmeya bar Aḥa says** that **Rav says:** If the animal **stretched out its foreleg to stand, even if it did not** actually **stand,** it is considered to have stood and does not require a twenty-four-hour waiting period. If **it raised its leg to walk, even if it did not** actually **walk,** it is considered to have walked and does not require inspection according to Rav. **And Rav Ḥisda says:** Even if it did not stretch out its foreleg, but simply **struggled to stand, even if it did not stand,** it is considered to have stood and may be slaughtered that day.

Rav Yirmeya bar Abba says in the name of Rav: *pashta yada l'amod* — even if the animal merely stretched out a foreleg in an attempt to stand, *af al pi shelo amda*, even without actually achieving a standing position, that suffices as equivalent to standing. Similarly, *akra ragla l'halech* — if it moved a hind leg in preparation for walking, even without actually walking, *af al pi shelo halcha*, that also counts. Rav Chisda goes further: *niera l'amod* — even if the animal only shifted its body weight in a motion suggesting it was trying to stand, without extending any limb, that suffices as standing.

### Final Halacha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא: הֵיכָא דְּנָפְלָה מִן הַגָּג בִּדְלָא יָדְעָה, וְעָמְדָה וְלֹא הָלְכָה – צְרִיכָה בְּדִיקָה, וְאֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה מֵעֵת לְעֵת. וְאִם הָלְכָה – אֲפִילּוּ בְּדִיקָה נָמֵי לָא צְרִיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is: In any case **where** an animal **fell from the roof unawares,** i.e., unintentionally, **and stood but did not walk,** it **requires inspection** after slaughter **but does not require** a twenty-four-hour **period** before slaughter. **And if it walked, it does not even require an inspection.**

The concluding *halacha* — likely a Geonic addition to the text — synthesizes the discussion. If an animal fell from a roof unknowingly, meaning it neither jumped willfully nor broke its fall: if it subsequently stood up without walking, *tzricha bedika v'eina tzricha me'eis le'eis* — a *bedika* is required after *shechita*, but there is no need to wait twenty-four hours. If it walked, *afilu bedika nami lo tzricha* — even the *bedika* is waived entirely.

Notably, the final *halacha* does not adopt the more lenient positions of Rav Yirmeya bar Abba and Rav Chisda: mere stretching of a limb or shifting of the body does not qualify as "standing" for these purposes. The animal must actually stand or actually walk. Partial movement, however indicative of life and recovery, is not sufficient under the final ruling.
