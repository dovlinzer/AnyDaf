# Niddah 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## 24-Hour Concern Basis

### Rava's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּרַבָּנַן? אִשָּׁה מַרְגֶּשֶׁת בְּעַצְמָהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אִם כֵּן, תְּהֵא דַּיָּה שְׁעָתָהּ! וְרַבָּה לְחַדּוֹדֵי לְאַבָּיֵי הוּא דְּבָעֵי. אֶלָּא מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּרַבָּנַן?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba says: What** is the **reason** for the opinion **of the Rabbis? A woman** can **sense within herself** if she is experiencing a flow of blood. **Abaye said to** Rabba: **If so, her time should be sufficient,** as there should be no concern that her flow began earlier. The Gemara explains: **And Rabba** did not in fact mean this explanation seriously; rather, he **wanted to hone** the mind of **Abaye.** The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **what** is the real **reason** for the opinion **of the Rabbis?**

The Gemara on 4b opens with a question posed by Rava: *mai ta'amaihu d'rabbanan* — what is the reason underlying the position of the Rabbis? To appreciate why this question arises, one must recall the broader debate that has occupied the preceding discussion. The Houses of Shammai and Hillel disagree sharply about how far back a woman must extend her retroactive concern upon discovering that she has become a *niddah*. Against this backdrop, the Rabbis adopt a middle position: one does have to be concerned retroactively, but the concern extends no further than twenty-four hours. The question is what exactly grounds this twenty-four-hour limit, and where it comes from.

Rava's own initial formulation — *isha margeshes b'atzma*, a woman perceives when she is menstruating — would seem to support Shammai's position, eliminating retroactive concern altogether. Abaye immediately seizes on this: *im ken tehei daya sha'ata*, if so, it should be sufficient from the moment she finds the blood. The Gemara concedes that Rava's formulation was essentially a rhetorical probe — *uchedei lechadudei le'Abaye hu deba'ei*, he was posing it to sharpen Abaye's thinking, not to defend it as a genuine position. Rava, after all, was Abaye's teacher, and the Gemara understands the exchange as a form of intellectual provocation rather than a serious legal argument.

### Twice-Daily Checks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חֲכָמִים תִּקְּנוּ לָהֶן לִבְנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁיְּהוּ בּוֹדְקוֹת עַצְמָן שַׁחֲרִית וְעַרְבִית, שַׁחֲרִית — לְהַכְשִׁיר טְהָרוֹת שֶׁל לַיְלָה, וְעַרְבִית — לְהַכְשִׁיר טְהָרוֹת שֶׁל יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is **in accordance with that which Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel says: The Sages instituted that the Jewish women should examine themselves** twice each day, **morning and evening.** The **morning** examination is in order **to render fit** the **ritually pure** items **of the night,** i.e., any items that she touched the night before. **And** the **evening** examination is in order **to render fit** the **pure** items that she touched **during the day.**

The Gemara then turns to the actual foundation of the Rabbis' position. It cites the teaching of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *chachamim tiknu lahen livnot Yisrael sheyihyu bodkot atzman shacharis v'arvit* — the Sages instituted for Jewish women that they should examine themselves twice daily, in the morning and in the evening. This was the standard practice during the era when matters of *tumah v'taharah* were actively observed. Women would check throughout the month, not only around the time they expected their period.

The morning check, performed at first light, was intended *lehachshir shel lailah* — to ratify the purity of what had been done the night before. If a woman checked at sunrise and found she was *tahor*, then even if she later discovered she had become *tamei*, anything she had handled the prior night — for instance, *terumah* — would be protected. The evening check, performed at the onset of night, served the analogous function of protecting what had occurred during the day.

### Check-Based Protection

The practical logic of this arrangement is straightforward. If a woman checks at sunrise and her check is clean, she has established a point of *taharah* that limits any subsequent retroactive concern. Whatever she did the previous night is safe. The check does not prove she was *tahor* throughout the night; it simply establishes a boundary before which one need not extend concern. The same applies in the other direction: a clean check at nightfall protects the day's activities.

This framework explains the Rabbis' position naturally. Since women are supposed to be checking twice daily, a clean check at any given point establishes that she was *tahor* up to that moment. The question of retroactive impurity only arises when she has *not* been doing her checks as required.

### Negligence Penalty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזוֹ הוֹאִיל וְלֹא בָּדְקָה — הִפְסִידָה עוֹנָה. מַאי ״עוֹנָה״? עוֹנָה יְתֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its explanation: **And this** woman, **since she did not examine** herself in accordance with the rabbinic enactment, **she loses** the status of the ritually pure items she touched over the **period [*ona*]** of a day or a night. The Gemara asks: **What** is meant by: **Period** of a day or a night? Doesn't her retroactive impurity status extend back for a twenty-four-hour period? The Gemara answers: It means **an additional period** of a day or a night, i.e., twenty-four hours in total.

Here the logic of the twenty-four-hour limit comes into focus. The Gemara explains: *vezo, ho'il v'lo badka, hifsida onah* — this woman, since she did not perform her checks, she has lost an *onah*. An *onah* in tractate Niddah refers not to a twelve-hour period in the clock-based sense, but to a unit of time defined by natural boundaries — either daytime, from sunrise to sunset, or nighttime, from sunset to sunrise.

Consider a concrete scenario. A woman finds at six in the evening that she has become *tamei*. Had she been doing what she was supposed to do, she would have checked at sunrise and that check would have been her last established point of *taharah*. The most she would have had to go back would have been to the prior sunrise — approximately twelve hours. But because she has been negligent and not checking as required, the Rabbis impose a consequence: they add one additional *onah* as a kind of *knas*, a penalty. Instead of going back only to the prior sunrise, she must go back an additional *onah*, to the prior sunset. That gives a total of twenty-four hours.

The underlying principle is clear: *me'ikar ha-din*, by strict law, she is not *tamei* retroactively at all. Even Hillel would agree with this. All of these retroactive concerns are purely *derabbanan*. The Rabbis are not claiming she was biologically a *niddah* twenty-four hours earlier; they are imposing a structure of consequences — lenient enough not to be draconian, but stringent enough to reflect some penalty for irresponsibility.

### Three Units Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְרָבָא: וְהָא זִימְנִין מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ שָׁלֹשׁ עוֹנוֹת בְּמֵעֵת לְעֵת! הִשְׁווּ חֲכָמִים מִדּוֹתֵיהֶן, שֶׁלֹּא תַּחְלוֹק בְּמֵעֵת לְעֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says to Rava: But** occasionally **you find three periods** of day or night within a twenty-four-hour period. For example, if she examined herself on Monday afternoon and finds blood, then the twenty-four-hour period extending back to Sunday afternoon includes three periods of day or night: Monday day, Sunday night and Sunday day from the time of her most recent examination, as these twenty-four hours do not fit precisely into two such periods. The Gemara answers: **The Sages rendered their measures equal, so** that **one should not differentiate** between cases. In other words, they wanted to issue a uniform ruling that applies universally **and** therefore they established a set twenty-four-hour **period** of retroactive impurity, regardless of the circumstances.

Rav Papa poses a sharp question to Rava: *zimnin d'mashkachat lah gimmel onot b'ma'et l'ma'et* — there are cases where twenty-four hours actually spans three *onot*. If a woman checks at three in the afternoon and goes back exactly twenty-four hours, she crosses into three distinct units: from three in the afternoon back to the prior six in the morning is one *onah*; from six in the morning back to the prior six in the evening is a second; and from six in the evening back to three in the afternoon is a third. Twenty-four hours, but three *onot* crossed.

This seems to undercut the framing that the penalty is one additional *onah*. In the normative case — when the most recent check occurs precisely at a unit boundary — two *onot* equals twenty-four hours cleanly. But when the check occurs mid-*onah*, as in the three-in-the-afternoon example, twenty-four hours will inevitably cross into a third unit.

### Lo Plug Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא חוֹטֵא נִשְׂכָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that they established the set twenty-four-hour period **so that the sinner should not profit** from his transgression. If the extent of retroactive impurity would be fixed at one additional period of day or night, a woman who remembers and examines herself in the early morning would be impure retroactively for a full twenty-four hours, back to early morning the previous day, whereas one who waits until noon would be impure only for the period of the morning and the previous night.

The Rabbis' response is elegant: *heicha d'amur rabbanan milta, lo liplug b'ma'et l'ma'et* — where the Rabbis established a rule, they did not make distinctions. They decreed twenty-four hours uniformly. The rationale is one of consistency: since in the standard case where the most recent check falls at a unit boundary, two *onot* equals exactly twenty-four hours, the Rabbis simply fixed twenty-four hours as the invariant rule. It is worth noting — as one sharp listener observed — that the phrase *lo plug* is formulated in Hebrew rather than Aramaic, suggesting it may derive from an earlier *tannaitic* source.

### Sinner Not Rewarded

A second rationale is offered as well: *iba'it eima, shelo yehei chotei niskar* — if you prefer, the concern is that the sinner should not be rewarded. If the rule were stated simply as "one additional *onah*" rather than "twenty-four hours," then a woman who checked at three in the afternoon — mid-*onah* — would actually end up going back *less* than twenty-four hours, potentially fewer than two full *onot*. This would perversely benefit her relative to the woman who checked at the proper unit boundary.

One might object that the woman who checks at three in the afternoon is actually checking earlier than the prescribed nightfall check and is arguably being somewhat responsible. But the Gemara's framing treats the three-in-the-afternoon check as effectively a late morning check rather than an early evening check, meaning she is still viewed as deficient in her practice. The principle is that in the aggregate she has been irresponsible, and the rule should not inadvertently reward that.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דְּאִתְּנִיסָה, וְלָא בְּדַקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the difference **between** these two answers? The Gemara answers: The difference **between** them is in a case **where she was prevented** by outside circumstances **and did not perform an examination.** According to the second answer she would not be considered a sinner and therefore she would be impure only for an additional period of a day or night. By contrast, according to the first answer her impure status would span twenty-four hours regardless of the circumstances.

The Gemara then asks: *mai beinaihu* — what practical difference exists between the two rationales? The answer: a case of *ones*, an unforeseen circumstance. If a woman genuinely tried to check at sunrise but was prevented by an emergency beyond her control, she is not a *chotei* and the *shelo yehei chotei niskar* rationale would not apply to her. Under the *lo plug* rationale, it makes no difference — twenty-four hours is always twenty-four hours. The Gemara does not pursue this bifurcation further, implying that the *lo plug* principle is the operative one. The Gemara has effectively acknowledged that this entire framework is a policy-driven *gezerah*, not a biological claim about when *niddah* status begins.

## Mesuleket Damim

### Regular Period Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל אִשָּׁה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ וֶסֶת [וְכוּ׳]. לֵימָא מַתְנִיתִין רַבִּי דּוֹסָא הִיא, וְלָא רַבָּנַן? דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אַרְבַּע נָשִׁים דַּיָּין שְׁעָתָן — בְּתוּלָה, מְעוּבֶּרֶת, מְנִיקָה, וּזְקֵנָה. רַבִּי דּוֹסָא אוֹמֵר: כׇּל אִשָּׁה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ וֶסֶת דַּיָּה שְׁעָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: For **any woman who has a fixed** menstrual **cycle,** and she examined herself at that time and discovered blood, her time is sufficient, and she transmits impurity only from that time onward. The Gemara asks: **Shall we say** that the mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Dosa, and not** in accordance with **the** opinion of **the Rabbis? As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says: There are four** categories of **women** for whom the *halakha* is that **their time is sufficient: A virgin,** i.e., a girl who has never experienced menstruation, **a pregnant woman, a nursing** mother, **and an elderly woman. Rabbi Dosa says:** For **every woman who has a fixed** menstrual **cycle,** and she examined herself at that time and discovered blood, **her time is sufficient,** and it is only from that stage that she transmits ritual impurity.

The Mishnah introduces a rule regarding a woman who has a regular cycle — *kol isha sheyesh lah veset* — stating that she is *dayah sha'atah*, it is sufficient from the moment she finds the blood. The Gemara immediately raises a concern: perhaps this Mishnah represents only the view of Rabbi Dosa and not the Sages generally.

### Four Categories

The Gemara cites a *baraita* in which Rabbi Eliezer enumerates four categories of women who are always *dayah sha'atan* — for whom discovering blood is treated as occurring at the moment of discovery, with no retroactive concern: *betulat damim*, a girl who has never menstruated; *me'uberet*, a pregnant woman; *menika*, a nursing woman; and *zekena*, an elderly, post-menopausal woman. What unites these four is that they are women for whom menstruation is physiologically unexpected. They constitute the category the Gemara calls *mesuleket damim* — women for whom blood flow is absent or anomalous — and it is precisely because blood is not anticipated that when blood does appear, we assume it occurred only at the moment of discovery.

### Dosa's Extension

Rabbi Dosa adds a fifth category: *kol isha sheyesh lah veset daya sha'atah* — any woman who has a regular cycle is also *dayah sha'atah* when she finds blood. The reasoning behind Rabbi Dosa's position is that a woman with a regular *veset* can be considered *mesuleket damim* during the rest of the month, i.e., outside her expected period. Just as a pregnant or nursing woman has no reason to expect blood at any time and is therefore *mesuleket damim* comprehensively, so too the woman with a *veset* has every reason *not* to expect blood outside her predictable window. Her regularity makes her a de facto *mesuleket damim* for the remainder of the month. If the Mishnah then states that a woman with a *veset* is *dayah sha'atah*, the Gemara wonders whether this reflects Rabbi Dosa's minority view alone.

### Position Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי דּוֹסָא אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת וִסְתָּהּ, אֲבָל בִּשְׁעַת וִסְתָּהּ מוֹדוּ לֵיהּ, וּמַתְנִיתִין בִּשְׁעַת וִסְתָּהּ, וְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **You** may **even say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis. The Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Dosa only in** the case of a woman who discovers blood at an irregular time, **not at the fixed time of her** menstrual **cycle. But** if she discovers blood **at the fixed time of her** menstrual **cycle, they agree with him** that her time is sufficient and there is no retroactive impurity. **And the mishna** is referring to a woman who discovers blood **at the fixed time of her** menstrual **cycle, and** therefore **everyone agrees** that her time is sufficient.

The Gemara attempts to show that the Mishnah may in fact represent the unanimous view of the Sages. The key move is to distinguish between two scenarios: a woman who bleeds *b'sha'at vestah*, during her expected period, and a woman who bleeds *shelo b'sha'at vestah*, outside her expected period.

The Gemara's first approach is relatively lenient: perhaps when she bleeds during her expected period, everyone agrees she is *dayah sha'atah* — there is no reason to suspect the bleeding began earlier than the moment of discovery, since the blood arrived exactly on schedule. The debate between Rabbi Dosa and the Sages would then be confined to the rest of the month: is a woman with a *veset* considered *mesuleket damim* outside her expected time, such that she is also *dayah sha'atah* then?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דְּרַבִּי דּוֹסָא אֲפִילּוּ שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת וִסְתָּהּ אֲמַר? מַאן תְּנָא לְהָא דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: אִשָּׁה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ וֶסֶת — כִּתְמָהּ טָמֵא לְמַפְרֵעַ, שֶׁאִם תִּרְאֶה שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת וִסְתָּהּ — מְטַמְּאָה מֵעֵת לְעֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **By inference,** one can conclude **that Rabbi Dosa says** that her time is sufficient **even** if she discovers blood **not at the fixed time of her** menstrual **cycle.** If so, **who** is the *tanna* who **taught** the following *baraita*? **As the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a woman who has a fixed** menstrual **cycle** who finds a blood stain, **her** blood **stain** is **impure retroactively** from when the garment in question was laundered. The reason is **that if she sees** a flow of menstrual blood **not at the fixed time of her** menstrual **cycle,** it renders her impure retroactively **for** a twenty-four-hour **period.** Therefore, the blood stain likewise renders her retroactively impure.

To test this reading, the Gemara cites a *baraita* stating that a woman with a regular *veset* whose stain (*kesem*) is found outside her expected time is *tamei* retroactively for twenty-four hours — *metamma me'et l'et*. The *baraita* draws an analogy: since even an actual *re'iyah* (definite sighting of blood) outside her expected time makes her *tamei* retroactively, the same can be said for a stain. The implication is that outside her expected period, there is retroactive concern — which seems to contradict the lenient first approach.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא רַבָּנַן הִיא, וְלָא רַבִּי דּוֹסָא? אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי דּוֹסָא, עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיג רַבִּי דּוֹסָא עֲלַיְיהוּ דְּרַבָּנַן אֶלָּא בִּשְׁעַת וִסְתָּהּ, אֲבָל שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת וִסְתָּהּ מוֹדֵי לְהוּ, וּמַתְנִיתִין בִּשְׁעַת וִסְתָּהּ וְרַבִּי דּוֹסָא הִיא,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes its question: **Shall we say** that this *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis and not** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Dosa?** The Gemara answers: **You** may **even say** that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Dosa,** as one can claim that **Rabbi Dosa disagrees with the Rabbis only** in a case where a woman sees menstrual blood **at the fixed time of her** menstrual **cycle. But** if she sees blood **not at the fixed time of her** menstrual **cycle, he agrees** with them that she is impure retroactively. **And** according to this answer **the mishna,** which deals with a woman who discovers blood **at the fixed time of her** menstrual **cycle, is** only in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Dosa,** in contrast to the earlier claim.

This leads the Gemara to a stricter second reading. Perhaps *shelo b'sha'at vestah*, everyone agrees that she is concerned twenty-four hours back, and the debate between Rabbi Dosa and the Sages is limited to *b'sha'at vestah* — whether, during her expected period, she is *dayah sha'atah*. On this reading, our Mishnah, which says a woman with a *veset* is *dayah sha'atah*, would represent Rabbi Dosa's view alone, while everyone agrees about the retroactive concern outside the expected period.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא לְאוֹקוּמֵי לְקוּלָּא וּלְחוּמְרָא — לְחוּמְרָא מוֹקְמִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Since there is** a way **to interpret** it **as a leniency,** i.e., that all agree that blood discovered at the fixed time of menstruation renders items impure only from that point onward, **and** it can also be interpreted in a manner that leads **to a stringency,** i.e., everyone agrees that blood discovered not at her fixed time for menstruation causes retroactive impurity, and furthermore the Rabbis hold that this is the *halakha* even when blood is discovered at her fixed time of menstruation, **we interpret** it the way that leads **to a stringency.**

The Gemara then raises a methodological question: given that one can read the debate as covering either the more lenient or the more stringent scenario, which reading should one prefer? The Gemara answers: *le-chumra mokminan* — we establish the debate in its more stringent form. Rather than saying the debate is about the lenient ruling (the rest of the month) and everyone agrees on the strict ruling (during the period), we say the debate is about the stringent ruling (during the period) and everyone agrees on the stricter implication (during the rest of the month). This is a notable methodological principle and connects to a broader concept in halachic reasoning, related to the rule that when one can *makkish* a *pasuk* either stringently or leniently, one applies it stringently. The Gemara acknowledges the complexity here, since ordinarily *safek d'rabbanan le-kula* — doubts in rabbinic matters are resolved leniently — yet in the domain of *taharos*, where the general practice was to be scrupulous, the stricter reading gains traction.

### Final Framework

The final framework that emerges is as follows. The debate between Rabbi Dosa and the Sages applies specifically to *b'sha'at vestah*, the time of her expected period. Rabbi Dosa holds she is *dayah sha'atah* even there, while the Sages dispute this. The Mishnah follows Rabbi Dosa. Outside the expected period — *shelo b'sha'at vestah* — everyone agrees that a woman with a *veset* is concerned twenty-four hours retroactively. She is not treated as *mesuleket damim* during that time.

## Bloodstains Rules

### Stain Retroactivity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי: שֶׁאִם תִּרְאֶה שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת וִסְתָּהּ מְטַמְּאָה מֵעֵת לְעֵת. טַעְמָא דְּאִשָּׁה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ וֶסֶת הוּא, דִּפְלִיגִי רַבָּנַן בֵּין כִּתְמָהּ לִרְאִיָּיתָהּ,
>
> **Translation:** § It **is taught** in the *baraita*: If a woman who has a fixed menstrual cycle finds a blood stain, her blood stain is retroactively impure. The Gemara analyzes this statement: The **reason** for this ruling is **that** she is **a woman who has a fixed** menstrual **cycle;** therefore, **the Rabbis distinguish between her** blood **stain,** which causes retroactive impurity like a sighting not at her fixed time, **and her sighting** of her menstrual flow at its fixed time, which does not cause retroactive impurity.

The Gemara turns to the question of *ketamim* — bloodstains found on a garment. The scenario is inherently ambiguous: unlike an actual *re'iyah*, where a woman directly observes bleeding and the timing is tied to the moment of discovery, a stain on a garment may have been deposited at any point since the garment was last laundered. This asymmetry makes the rules governing stains both more complex and in some ways more severe.

The *baraita* states that a woman with a *veset* who finds a stain outside her expected period is *tamei* retroactively — not just twenty-four hours, but going back to the last time the garment was laundered. The Gemara explains the logic: since even an actual *re'iyah* outside her expected period subjects this woman to twenty-four hours of retroactive concern, it would not be paradoxical to extend retroactive concern to stains as well, even over a longer span.

### Analogy Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא שְׁאָר נָשִׁים שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים דַּיָּין שְׁעָתָן — כִּתְמָן כִּרְאִיָּיתָן.
>
> **Translation:** It can be inferred from here that in the case of those **other women,** e.g., a pregnant or elderly woman, **with regard to** whom **the Sages stated: Their time is sufficient** and their menstrual flow never causes retroactive impurity, the same *halakha* applies to **their** blood **stains as** to **their sighting** of their menstrual flow, i.e., the stains do not cause retroactive impurity.

The principle underlying this ruling is one of proportionality and internal consistency. The Gemara reasons that it is not absurd to treat stains more stringently retroactively than actual sightings, because even for actual sightings there are circumstances — namely, *shelo b'sha'at vestah* — where retroactive concern applies. The stain rule therefore finds its justification in the fact that the woman is not categorically exempt from retroactive concern. Since there exist moments when she is subject to it with certainty, the Rabbis are entitled to extend the same logic, in a modified form, to the ambiguous case of stains.

### Chanina's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַנִּי? רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס הִיא, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס: כׇּל הַנָּשִׁים כִּתְמָן טָמֵא לְמַפְרֵעַ, וְנָשִׁים שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים דַּיָּין שְׁעָתָן — כִּתְמָן כִּרְאִיָּיתָן, חוּץ מִתִּינוֹקֶת שֶׁלֹּא הִגִּיעַ זְמַנָּהּ לִרְאוֹת, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ סְדִינִין שֶׁלָּהּ מְלוּכְלָכִין בְּדָם — אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: In accordance with **whose** opinion is this *baraita* taught? The Gemara answers: **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus, as Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel says in the name of Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus:** The *halakha* **with regard to all women** is that **their** blood **stains are impure retroactively. But** any **women with regard to** whom **the Sages stated: Their time is sufficient, their** blood **stains** share the same ruling **as their sighting** of their menstrual flow, i.e., they do not cause retroactive impurity. This is the *halakha* **except** for the case of **a young girl whose time to see** the flow of menstrual blood **has not arrived.** With regard to her, **even if her sheets are soiled with blood one** need **not be concerned for it,** i.e., her blood stains do not cause any impurity at all, even from that point onward.

The Gemara identifies this framework with the position of Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos, transmitted by Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *kol hanashim kitman tamei l'mafrei'a* — for all women, their stains are *tamei* retroactively. However, for the women the Rabbis designated as *dayah sha'atan* — those who are *mesuleket damim* — *kitman k're'iyatan*, their stains are treated analogously to their actual sightings. Since when such a woman sees blood there is no retroactive concern, so too when she finds a stain there is no retroactive concern.

One notable exception applies in the more lenient direction: a girl who has not yet reached the age at which menstruation is expected — a prepubescent child — is not only free from retroactive concern but is not rendered *tamei* at all even if her bedsheets are heavily stained with blood. In a society with limited hygiene, the source of the blood is genuinely indeterminate, and since she has no physiological reason to be menstruating, the stain is attributed elsewhere entirely.

### Meir vs. Chanina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִית לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא כֶּתֶם כְּלָל? וְהָתַנְיָא: כׇּל הַנָּשִׁים כִּתְמָן טָמֵא, וְנָשִׁים שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים דַּיָּין שְׁעָתָן — כִּתְמָן טָמֵא. רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס אוֹמֵר: נָשִׁים שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים דַּיָּין שְׁעָתָן — אֵין לָהֶן כֶּתֶם. מַאי לָאו אֵין לָהֶן כֶּתֶם כְּלָל? לָא, אֵין לָהֶן כֶּתֶם לְמַפְרֵעַ, אֲבָל יֵשׁ לָהֶן כֶּתֶם מִכָּאן וּלְהַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** does **Rabbi Ḥanina** ben Antigonus **hold** that a blood **stain** causes ritual impurity **at all? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The *halakha* with regard to **all women** is that **their** blood **stains are impure. And** similarly, **women with regard to** whom **the Sages stated: Their time is sufficient, their** blood **stains are** also **impure. Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus says:** The *halakha* of **women with regard to** whom **the Sages stated: Their time is sufficient,** is that **they do not have** the ritual impurity of a blood **stain. What, is it not** correct to say that this means **they have no** impurity caused by a blood **stain at all?** The Gemara answers: **No,** it means that **they do not have** impurity caused by a blood **stain retroactively, but they do have** the impurity caused by a blood **stain from here onward.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר אֲפִילּוּ לְמַפְרֵעַ? אִין, רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּמַחְמֵיר גַּבֵּי כְתָמִים. דְּתַנְיָא: כׇּל הַנָּשִׁים — כִּתְמָן טָמֵא לְמַפְרֵעַ, וְנָשִׁים שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים דַּיָּין שְׁעָתָן — כִּתְמָן טָמֵא לְמַפְרֵעַ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From the fact that** Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus holds that a blood stain imparts ritual impurity from that point onward, does this not indicate that **the first *tanna* holds** that it causes ritual impurity **even retroactively?** The Gemara answers: **Yes,** the ruling of the first *tanna* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who is stringent with regard to** blood **stains. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The *halakha* with regard to **all women** is that **their** blood **stains are impure retroactively. And** similarly, **women with regard to** whom **the Sages stated: Their time is sufficient, their** blood **stains are** also **impure retroactively.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

Against Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos stands the position of Rabbi Meir. A *baraita* records the disagreement explicitly: *kol hanashim kitman tamei l'mafrei'a* — all women, their stains are *tamei* retroactively to the last time the garment was laundered. Crucially, Rabbi Meir includes the *mesuleket damim* women in this rule. Even a pregnant woman, a nursing woman, or an elderly woman who finds a stain on her garment is *tamei* retroactively. Rabbi Meir is known throughout the tractate for his stringency in matters of *ketamim*, and the *Tosafot* note this pattern elsewhere as well.

## Bloodstains Rules…

### Chanina Clarified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִית לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא כֶּתֶם כְּלָל? וְהָתַנְיָא: כׇּל הַנָּשִׁים כִּתְמָן טָמֵא, וְנָשִׁים שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים דַּיָּין שְׁעָתָן — כִּתְמָן טָמֵא. רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס אוֹמֵר: נָשִׁים שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים דַּיָּין שְׁעָתָן — אֵין לָהֶן כֶּתֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** does **Rabbi Ḥanina** ben Antigonus **hold** that a blood **stain** causes ritual impurity **at all? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The *halakha* with regard to **all women** is that **their** blood **stains are impure. And** similarly, **women with regard to** whom **the Sages stated: Their time is sufficient, their** blood **stains are** also **impure. Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus says:** The *halakha* of **women with regard to** whom **the Sages stated: Their time is sufficient,** is that **they do not have** the ritual impurity of a blood **stain.**

The Gemara pauses to clarify Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos's position more precisely. When Rabbi Chanina says *ein lahem kesem* — these women have no *kesem* — does he mean the stain is completely *tahor* and carries no *tumah* status whatsoever? The Gemara answers: *lo, ein lahem kesem l'mafrei'a* — he means only that their stain carries no retroactive *tamei* status. He does not mean the stain is entirely without halachic significance. *Aval yesh lahem kesem mikan ul'haba* — from this point forward, the stain does render her *tamei*. The *kesem* is treated in the same way as an actual *re'iyah* for these women: it takes effect from the moment of discovery, not from any prior point.

### Age Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָתַי הִגִּיעַ זְמַנָּהּ לִרְאוֹת? מִשֶּׁהִגִּיעוּ יְמֵי הַנְּעוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus says:** The *halakha* of **women with regard to** whom **the Sages stated: Their time is sufficient,** is that **their** blood **stains** share the same ruling **as their sighting** of their menstrual flow, i.e., they cause ritual impurity from that point onward. **And** in the case of **a young girl, if her time to see** the flow of menstrual blood **has arrived,** then **she has** impurity status when she finds a blood **stain. But if her time to see** the flow of menstrual blood **has not** yet **arrived, she does not have** impurity status when she finds a blood **stain.** The Gemara asks: **And when** does a young girl **arrive** at **her time to see** the flow of menstrual blood? The Gemara answers: **When the days of** her **young womanhood have arrived.**

The Gemara clarifies the age threshold at which a girl enters the framework of *kesem* impurity at all. *Vetinokes shehigi'a zmanah lir'ot yesh lah kesem* — a girl who has reached the time when she might be expected to menstruate, even if she has not yet done so, is subject to the laws of *ketamim*. *V'shelo higi'a zmanah lir'ot, ein lah kesem* — a girl who has not yet reached that age is not subject to *kesem* impurity at all, even if blood is found on her bedsheets. As for when this threshold arrives: *mishehigi'u yemei hani'urim* — from when the days of youth arrive, which Rashi explains as whichever comes first: physical signs of puberty, reaching the age of twelve, or some combination of age and development that the Sages associate with the onset of potential menstruation.

## Sex & Exams

### Pre-Exam Fails

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַמְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת בְּעֵדִים כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: עֵד שֶׁלִּפְנֵי תַשְׁמִישׁ אֵינוֹ מְמַעֵט כִּפְקִידָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַב קַטִּינָא: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁמְּהוּמָּה לְבֵיתָהּ. וְכִי מְהוּמָּה לְבֵיתָהּ מַאי הָוֵי? מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁמְּהוּמָּה לְבֵיתָהּ — אֵינָהּ מַכְנֶסֶת לְחוֹרִין וְלִסְדָקִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And** with regard to a woman **who engages in intercourse while** using **examination cloths,** with which she ascertains whether the menstrual flow began, since the halakhic status of that act is like that of an examination, it reduces the time from a twenty-four-hour period and reduces the time from examination to examination. **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The **examination cloth** with **which** she examined herself **before intercourse does not reduce** the time from a twenty-four-hour period **like** a regular **examination.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion of Shmuel? **Rav Ketina says: Since she is excited [*shemehuma*]** and hurried **for** intercourse with **her husband,** it is not considered a proper examination. The Gemara asks: **And** even **if she is excited for** intercourse with **her husband, what of it?** Why does that invalidate her examination? The Gemara answers: **Since she is excited** and hurried **for** intercourse with **her husband, she does not insert** the examination cloth **into** her **recesses and folds.**

The Gemara now turns to the laws governing a woman who uses *edim* — examination cloths — in connection with sexual relations. The *eid* is so named because it bears testimony to her status. Rav Yehuda teaches in the name of Shmuel: *eid shel lifnei tashmish eino k'fitichah* — the examination cloth used before *tashmish* does not serve to limit the retroactive period. It cannot be used to establish a prior point of *taharah* for the purposes of *taharos*.

Rav Kattina explains the reason: *mitoch shemehuma l'beisa* — because she is rushed toward the act, and in her haste, *eina machneset l'chorin* — she does not insert the cloth into the nooks and crannies. The Gemara's language here is deliberately evocative of *bedikat chametz*: the physical examination of the vaginal canal, which must sweep all edges carefully, is compared to checking a house for *chametz* in its nooks and recesses. The *chazal* employ the metaphor of a house (*bayit*) consistently — not only is the act itself referred to as *tashmish ha-mitah*, but the entire internal anatomy is described in the Gemara elsewhere with architectural metaphors (*bayit*, *aliyah*, *cheder*, *prozdor*). The rushed pre-coital examination, however thorough the woman intends it to be, cannot be relied upon for the demanding standard of *taharos*.

### Cloth Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַמְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת בְּעֵדִים, הֲרֵי זוֹ כִּפְקִידָה. מַאי לָאו חַד לִפְנֵי תַשְׁמִישׁ וְחַד לְאַחַר תַּשְׁמִישׁ? לָא, אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי לְאַחַר תַּשְׁמִישׁ, וְאֶחָד לוֹ וְאֶחָד לָהּ. כְּדִתְנַן: דֶּרֶךְ בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל מְשַׁמְּשׁוֹת בִּשְׁנֵי עֵדִים, אֶחָד לוֹ וְאֶחָד לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **We learned** in the mishna: And a woman **who engages in intercourse while** using **examination cloths,** the halakhic status of that act **is like that of an examination.** The mishna refers to examination cloths in the plural. **What, is it not** speaking of **one** cloth used **before intercourse and one** cloth used **after intercourse?** The Gemara answers: **No, this** cloth **and that** cloth are both used **after intercourse; and** the reason why the mishna uses the plural is that **one** cloth is **for his** use **and one** is **for her** use. **As we learned** in a mishna (14a): It is the **custom of Jewish women to engage in intercourse** with their husbands **with two examination cloths, one for his** use **and one for her** use.

The Mishnah states *ha-meshameshes b'eidim harei zu k'pkida* — a woman who uses examination cloths, this counts as an established check. The Gemara asks: does the dual form *eidim* refer to one cloth before and one cloth after *tashmish*, or both cloths after? If one before and one after, the Mishnah would be teaching that the pre-coital cloth is valid after all — directly contradicting Rav Yehuda's ruling.

The Gemara resolves the question: both cloths are after the act. The practice of Jewish women (*derech bnot Yisrael*), as stated in a Mishnah, is to use two cloths after *tashmish* — *echad lo v'echad lah*, one for him and one for her. The husband checks himself and she checks internally. If either finds blood, it is attributed to her. This explains why the Mishnah speaks of two cloths: both are post-coital, and the post-coital check is the one that counts.

### Mandate & Validation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מַאי? אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא חַד לִפְנֵי תַשְׁמִישׁ וְחַד לְאַחַר תַּשְׁמִישׁ — אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתְּךָ אָמֵינָא: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁמְּהוּמָּה לְבֵיתָהּ לָא בָּדְקָה שַׁפִּיר, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן הֲרֵי זוֹ כִּפְקִידָה. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי לְאַחַר תַּשְׁמִישׁ — פְּשִׁיטָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is this? Granted, if you say** that the mishna is referring to **one** cloth used **before intercourse and one** used **after intercourse,** it **was necessary** for the mishna to mention both examinations, as it might **enter your mind to say** that **since she is excited** and hurried **for** intercourse with **her husband, she did not examine** herself **properly.** Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that her examination with a cloth before intercourse **is** considered **as** a halakhically valid **examination. But if you say** that **this** cloth **and that** cloth are his and her cloths used **after intercourse,** isn't it **obvious** that they are treated as halakhic examinations? Why is this ruling necessary?

The Gemara then considers an alternative reading of the Mishnah. Rather than treating it as a description of practice — "in the case of a woman who uses cloths" — it reads *u'meshameshes b'eidim* as a mandate: the Rabbis required both a pre-coital and a post-coital check. The Mishnah then declares that at least one of those checks — the post-coital one — counts as a valid *pkida* to limit retroactive concern.

This interpretation clarifies an important point: if the pre-coital check is rabbinically mandated but does not serve the *taharos* function, it must serve *some* function. The Gemara implies that it serves the function of giving the couple reasonable confidence that she is not currently a *niddah*, which is a lower halachic threshold than the stringent standard required for *taharos*. This connects to a broader discussion in the *rishonim* about whether the standard of a valid *bedikah* for the husband's *issur* is the same as for *tumah v'taharah* — and the present Gemara suggests these may be distinct standards.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא, שֶׁמָּא תִּרְאֶה טִפַּת דָּם כְּחַרְדָּל וּתְחַפֶּנָּהּ שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The *halakha* that the examinations performed after intercourse count as valid examinations is necessary, **lest you say** that **perhaps** she might **see a drop of blood corresponding** to the size of **a mustard seed and** a drop of **semen covered it.** In other words, it is possible that she discharged a tiny amount of menstrual blood that went undetected, as it was covered by a drop of semen. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that one does not need to be concerned for this possibility.

### Semen Coverage

Why might one have thought the post-coital check is insufficient? The Gemara explains: *shema tzira k'chardal* — perhaps she has a drop of blood as small as a mustard seed, which is then obscured by the semen. The post-coital check might produce a false negative, missing a minute amount of blood that is covered over. The Gemara teaches that we do not entertain this concern — the post-coital check is treated as valid and does limit the retroactive period. This *chidush* is necessary precisely because without it one might have suspected that semen coverage renders the post-coital check unreliable.

### Scope of Rule

Both Rashi and *Tosafot* emphasize that the laws of examination cloths in connection with *tashmish* apply to a woman with a regular *veset* even *shelo b'sha'at vestah* — outside her expected period. This underscores just how comprehensive the checking regimen was in the era of active *tumah v'taharah* observance: twice daily throughout the month, regardless of expected period, plus before and after every act of *tashmish*. The woman with a regular cycle, outside her expected window, was no less subject to this framework. The Gemara's attention to this point reinforces what was established earlier — that *shelo b'sha'at vestah*, even such a woman is not treated as *mesuleket damim* and remains subject to retroactive concern.

## Purity by Checking

### Preparation Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד דַּיָּה שְׁעָתָהּ וְכוּ׳. לְמָה לִי לְמִיתְנֵי: ״הָיְתָה יוֹשֶׁבֶת בַּמִּטָּה וַעֲסֻקָה בִּטְהָרוֹת״? לִיתְנֵי: ״הָיְתָה עֲסוּקָה בִּטְהָרוֹת וּפָרְשָׁה וְרָאֲתָה״!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches with regard to a woman who has a fixed menstrual cycle: **Her time is sufficient, how so?** If the woman was sitting in a bed and engaged in handling ritually pure items, and she left the bed and saw blood, she is impure and those items are pure. The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need the mishna **to teach:** If the woman **was sitting in a bed and engaged in** handling **pure items? Let** the mishna **teach** the same ruling without mentioning the bed: If she **was engaged in** handling **pure items and she left and saw** blood. The detail that she was sitting in a bed is apparently superfluous.

The Mishnah discusses a woman who, while seated on a bed and engaged in preparing ritually pure foods (*ta'aros*), gets up and discovers blood. Since she is *dayah sha'atah* — she had a regular *veset* and this occurred during her expected period — the *ta'aros* she was handling remain *tahor*. The Gemara asks: why does the Mishnah specifically mention the bed? It could simply have said she was engaged in *ta'aros* and subsequently discovered blood. Why the detail about the bed?

### Bed as Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: טַעְמָא דְּדַיָּהּ שְׁעָתָהּ, הָא מֵעֵת לְעֵת — מִטָּה נָמֵי מְטַמְּיָא. מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לִזְעֵירִי, דְּאָמַר זְעֵירִי: מֵעֵת לְעֵת שֶׁבְּנִדָּה עוֹשָׂה מִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב, לְטַמֵּא אָדָם לְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This** detail **teaches us** that the **reason** why the bed is not rendered impure is **that her time is sufficient** and there is no retroactive impurity. It can be inferred that in a case where she is impure retroactively for a twenty-four-hour **period, her bed is also rendered impure.** This **supports** the opinion **of Ze'eiri, as Ze'eiri said:** The level of impurity of the retroactive twenty-four-hour **period of a menstruating woman renders** impure **a bed** upon which she lies **and a chair** upon which she sits, **to** the extent that they **transmit impurity to a person** who comes in contact with them, **to** the extent that he **transmits impurity to the garments** he is wearing.

The Gemara answers: the Mishnah mentions the bed to communicate an additional principle beyond the *ta'aros* themselves. *Ha me'et l'et, mitah nami mitamya* — had she been subject to twenty-four hours of retroactive concern, the bed too would have become *tamei*. The bed is introduced as the example precisely because it carries its own distinctive halachic status, beyond the straightforward case of the food and vessels she was handling. The Mishnah's inclusion of the bed thus illustrates the full scope of what is at stake when one establishes retroactive *niddah* status.

### Zeiri's Teaching

This reading is supported by a teaching of Zeiri: *me'et l'et she'b'niddah osah mishkav u'moshav l'tamei adam u'l'tamei begadim* — the twenty-four-hour retroactive period of *niddah* creates a full *mishkav u'moshav* status, with the capacity to render persons *tamei* and to render garments *tamei*.

The significance of this statement lies in the distinction between ordinary *tumah* transmission and the special status of *mishkav u'moshav*. Ordinarily, an *av ha-tumah* makes whatever it contacts a *rishon*, which in turn can only convey *tumah* to food. But when a *niddah* sits or lies on something, the Torah grants that object the status of an *av ha-tumah* in its own right — *mishkav u'moshav*. It can then render persons *tamei* and render even garments a *rishon*. The *l'tamei begadim* formulation is precise: if a person sits on the *mishkav* of a *niddah*, not only does he become a *rishon*, but the clothes he is wearing become a *rishon* as well — not because they are touching the *mishkav* directly, but because the halachah of *mishkav* encompasses the person and his garments as a unit.

Zeiri's ruling establishes that this full *mishkav u'moshav* status is applied retroactively across the twenty-four-hour period. When we declare her a *niddah* retroactively, we are not merely saying that the foods she touched are *tamei*; we are saying that whatever she sat or lay on during those twenty-four hours carries the status of *mishkav*, with all its consequences. This is what the Mishnah is teaching by mentioning the bed — it signals that the retroactive declaration is comprehensive.

### Inanimate Status Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדִי הַאי מִטָּה דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ דַּעַת לִישָּׁאֵל הוּא, וְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ דַּעַת לִישָּׁאֵל — סְפֵקוֹ טָהוֹר! תַּרְגְּמַהּ זְעֵירִי כְּשֶׁחַבְרוֹתֶיהָ נוֹשְׂאוֹת אוֹתָהּ בַּמִּטָּה, דְּהָוְיָא לַיהּ יַד חַבְרוֹתֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** This Gemara raises a difficulty: **Now, this bed** upon which she sat **is an entity that lacks consciousness** in order for it **to be asked,** and the principle with regard to **any entity that lacks consciousness** in order for it **to be asked** is that the item with **uncertain** status is deemed **pure.** The Gemara explains: **Ze'eiri interpreted** his ruling as applying specifically to a case **where her friends are carrying her in the bed, where** the bed **is** considered as the extended **hand of her friends.** In other words, it is part of an entity that has consciousness in order for it to be asked, and therefore the item with uncertain status is deemed impure.

The Gemara raises a difficulty. A bed is an inanimate object — *d'leit bei da'at lishoel*, it lacks the capacity to be asked. A foundational principle of *tumah v'taharah* is that when there is a *safek tumah* regarding an object that has no *da'at* — no ability to be questioned about the circumstances — the doubt is resolved leniently and the object is *tahor*. If so, how can we declare the bed *tamei* on the basis of a retroactive *safek*?

### Human Agency

Zeiri himself provides the answer: *targuma Zeiri d'shekhavorteha nosot osah b'mitah* — the case is where her companions are carrying her while she lies on the bed, lifting and transporting her. In that scenario, the companions are persons with *da'at* and can be asked about the circumstances. Because they are carrying the bed, the bed is considered an extension of them, acquiring *da'at* through their agency — *yahavei lei yad chavroteiha*, the bed is treated as though it has *da'at* by virtue of the hands of the carriers. The principle that an inanimate object cannot confer *tumah* through a doubtful circumstance therefore does not apply when a person with *da'at* is involved in transmitting that *tumah*.

### Yochanan's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: סְפֵק טוּמְאָה הַבָּאָה בִּידֵי אָדָם — נִשְׁאָלִין עָלֶיהָ, אֲפִילּוּ בִּכְלִי מוּנָּח עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע כְּמִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ דַּעַת לִישָּׁאֵל, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין חַבְרוֹתֶיהָ נוֹשְׂאוֹת אוֹתָהּ בַּמִּטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara provides another answer: **And now that Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** In a case of **uncertain ritual impurity that comes** about **by the hand of a person,** i.e., through his involvement, the owner of the vessel must **consult** a Sage **about it,** i.e., it is considered an entity that has consciousness in order for it to be asked, as in such a case **even with regard to a vessel that is placed upon the ground,** which is certainly incapable of providing an answer if asked, its halakhic status is **like** that of **an item that has consciousness** in order for it **to be asked.** With this statement in mind, one can explain that according to Ze'eiri a menstruating woman transmits impurity to a bed **even though her friends are not carrying her in the bed.** Rather, as the bed's ritual impurity was caused by the hand of a person, it has the halakhic status of an item that has consciousness in order for it to be asked.

The Gemara then introduces a broader principle from Rabbi Yochanan that extends this logic significantly: *safek tumah haba bidei adam nishalin aleha afilu b'icheli munach al gabei karka* — a doubtful *tumah* that comes about through human agency is adjudicated as *tamei*, even when the vessel receiving the *tumah* is resting on the ground with no human involvement on its end.

The significance of this teaching is that it redefines the operative category of *da'at*. Previously, the question was: does the *recipient* of the *tumah* have *da'at*? Rabbi Yochanan establishes that the relevant *da'at* can reside in the *transmitter* — the person conveying the *tumah* — rather than in the object becoming *tamei*. As long as a human being with *da'at* is involved in the chain of transmission, the safek is treated as *tamei*.

This rules out the need for the friends-carrying-the-bed scenario entirely. Even when the bed is simply lying on the ground and no one is carrying it, as long as the *tumah* is being transmitted through a human being — the *niddah* herself, whose status is the basis of the bed's *tumah* — the bed's lack of *da'at* does not resolve the doubt in the direction of *taharah*. The residual category of *ein bo da'at lishoel* that would yield *tahor* is now confined to cases where *no* human agency is involved at any stage of the *tumah* transmission — for instance, a loaf of bread left on a shelf and a *tamei* garment on the ground, with no person present to have caused contact between them. The session concludes with this principle, to be continued in the following *daf*.
