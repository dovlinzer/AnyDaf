# Nedarim 72b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Perek Structure & Setup

### Intro: Perek Organization

The daf opens at 72b with a new *mishnah*, still within the broader theme of the father and the groom who together annul a daughter's vows. Yet as we will see, this *mishnah* raises — almost in passing — a central and far-reaching question that the *Gemara* proceeds to explore at length.

It is worth pausing at the outset to note something characteristic about the organization of this *perek*, as indeed of many *masechtos* and *perakim* throughout the Talmud. Rather than beginning with the most straightforward *halachos* — explaining what the twenty-four-hour window for *hafarah* consists of, what *hakama* means, how the process works in its basic form — the *perek* opens instead with the more obscure and in many ways more fascinating cases: the precise nature of the partnership between father and groom, who is the *ikar*, how the combination functions. All of the more foundational material gets addressed along the way, as it becomes relevant. This is simply another demonstration that the *Gemara* and the *Mishnayos* were organized for those already in the conversation, not as a beginner's introduction to the laws of *nedarim*.

### Mishnah: Blanket Annulment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ דֶּרֶךְ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, עַד שֶׁלֹּא הָיְתָה בִּתּוֹ יוֹצְאָה מֵאֶצְלוֹ, אוֹמֵר לָהּ: כׇּל נְדָרִים שֶׁנָּדַרְתְּ בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתִי — הֲרֵי הֵן מוּפָרִין. וְכֵן הַבַּעַל, עַד שֶׁלֹּא תִּכָּנֵס לִרְשׁוּתוֹ, אוֹמֵר לָהּ: כׇּל נְדָרִים שֶׁנָּדַרְתְּ עַד שֶׁלֹּא תִּכָּנְסִי לִרְשׁוּתִי — הֲרֵי הֵן מוּפָרִין. שֶׁמִּשֶּׁתִּכָּנֵס לִרְשׁוּתוֹ — אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְהָפֵר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The practice of Torah scholars** is to ensure that a woman about to be married should not be encumbered by any vows. A father, **before his daughter would leave him** through marriage, **would say to her: All vows that you vowed in my house are hereby nullified. And similarly, the husband, before she would enter his jurisdiction,** i.e., while they were still betrothed, **would say to her: All vows that you vowed before you entered my jurisdiction are hereby nullified.** This was necessary **because once she enters his jurisdiction he cannot nullify** the vows she made before that.

The *mishnah* at the top of 72b opens with a striking phrase: *derech talmidei chachamim* — it is the way of Torah scholars. It is notable that the *mishnah* directly identifies this practice with the category of *talmidei chachamim*; the term is common in the *Gemara* but relatively rare as a *mishnah*'s own formulation. Perhaps it means that one must actually be a *talmid chacham* to know that this practice is both possible and unproblematic — that he may annul vows he has not yet heard about.

The *mishnah* describes the following: before a man's daughter goes to *nisu'in* — and *yotzet me'etzlo*, leaving the father's house, conceptually means *nisu'in*, since during *eirusin* she remains in the father's domain — the father would say to her: *kol nedarim shenadart betoch beiti harei hem mufarim*, any vows you have taken in my house are hereby annulled. He gives her a blanket annulment, similar in spirit to the *hatarat nedarim* of *erev Rosh Hashanah*. The framing is deliberately open-ended: if she had already told him the specific vows, there would be no need for the general formula, and the clock would already be running. The phrasing makes clear that the father has not heard the specific vows; he is annulling whatever may exist.

### Father & Groom Roles

To understand why both father and groom must participate, it is necessary to recall the underlying structure. During *kiddushin*, the daughter remains conceptually in the father's house; *nisu'in* is when she leaves. The father alone can annul vows made before *kiddushin*, and in partnership with the *arus* he can annul vows made after *kiddushin* but before *nisu'in*. Once *nisu'in* occurs, the father is entirely out of the picture. If vows predate *nisu'in*, the husband cannot annul them unilaterally after *nisu'in* because they are *kodmin* — they precede his period of sole authority. The only window for addressing such vows is before *nisu'in*, which is precisely why the *mishnah* describes the father and groom each making their blanket declarations beforehand.

The *mishnah* continues: *vechen haba'al* — so too the groom, before she enters his domain, would say: *kol nedarim shenadart ad shelo tikanesi lirshuti harei hem mufarim*, any vows you may have taken before the *chuppah* are hereby annulled. Once *nisu'in* occurs, *eino yachol lehafer*, he can no longer address those earlier vows, for they are *kodmin*.

The clear implication of the *mishnah* is that neither the father nor the groom has actually heard the specific vows they are annulling. The blanket language and the absence of any running clock make this evident. And it is precisely this implication that the *Gemara* will take as its opening question.

## Shmiea Requirements

### Q1: Annul Without Hearing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בָּעֵי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: בַּעַל, מַהוּ שֶׁיָּפֵר בְּלֹא שְׁמִיעָה? ״וְשָׁמַע אִישָׁהּ״ דַּוְקָא הוּא, אוֹ לָאו דַּוְקָא הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rami bar Ḥama asks:** Concerning **a husband, what is** the *halakha* **with regard** to his **nullifying** a vow **without hearing** it? In other words, can a husband state a general nullification of his wife's vows without being aware of any particular vow? When the verse states: **"And her husband hears** it, on the day that he hears it, and holds his peace at her, then her vows shall be ratified" (Numbers 30:8), **is** that referring **specifically** to a situation where he actually heard of a vow, and only then he can nullify it? **Or is** it **not specifically** referring to such a situation, and the mention of hearing is merely because the ordinary situation is that the husband nullifies a vow once he hears it?

It is almost comical, in a certain sense, to take a *pasuk* from the *Torah* and simply declare it *lav davka*. The question, however, is more nuanced than simple dismissal of the phrase. What *lav davka* likely means in this context is not that *beyom shmo'o* carries no significance whatsoever, but rather that it is not a prerequisite for the act of annulment. Its operative role would be different: from the moment the husband hears, the clock begins running and he has twenty-four hours to act. Failing to annul within twenty-four hours of hearing is treated as *hakama* — acquiescence by silence. On this reading, hearing is not what enables the annulment; rather, it is what starts the countdown. The husband could, in principle, annul even before he has heard, and *beyom shmo'o* would simply mean that having heard, he must now act within the day.

The *Gemara* does not spell this out explicitly, but it seems the most coherent *pshat* if one is going to say *lav davka* at all — it is not a wholesale dismissal of the phrase, but a reframing of its function.

### Opening Conversation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: דֶּרֶךְ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, עַד שֶׁלֹּא יָצָאת בִּתּוֹ מֵאֶצְלוֹ, אוֹמֵר לָהּ: כׇּל נְדָרִים שֶׁנָּדַרְתְּ בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתִי — הֲרֵי הֵן מוּפָרִין, וְהָא לָא שְׁמַע!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Come** and **hear** the mishna: **The practice of Torah scholars** is that a father, **before his daughter would leave him** through marriage, **would say to her: All vows that you vowed in my house are hereby nullified.** Rava points out: **But** the father **did not hear** her vows, so it must be that one can nullify vows without knowledge that they were actually made.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְכִי שָׁמַע הוּא דְּמֵיפַר. אִם כֵּן, כִּי לָא שְׁמַע, לְמָה לֵיהּ לְמֵימַר? הָא קָמַשְׁמַע לַן: אוֹרְחֵיהּ דְּצוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן לְהַדּוֹרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this conclusion: The mishna means that the father states a preemptive nullification that **when he** will **hear** a particular vow **is when he nullifies** it. The vow is not actually nullified until he hears it. The Gemara asks: **If so, when he has not** actually **heard** those vows yet, **why** is it necessary **for him to state** preemptively that the vows will be nullified; why not wait until he actually hears the vow? The Gemara answers: **This teaches us** that it is **the practice of a Torah scholar to pursue** such matters, in order to prompt his daughter or his betrothed to inform him of vows she took, which will then be nullified when he hears of them.

The *Gemara* seeks a proof from our *mishnah*: *ta shema*, come and hear — *derech talmidei chachamim ad shelo yatza bito me'etzlo amar lah kol nedarim shenadart betoch beiti harei hen mufarim — vehu lo shama*, and he did not hear. How does this work?

The *Gemara* offers an initial interpretation: *nehi d'chi shama hu d'mepar* — really, he only annuls when he hears. The declaration he makes now, before he has heard anything, is not itself an act of annulment. Rather, it is an opening to conversation — *orcha d'tzurba merabanan*, the way of a Torah scholar is *lehadurei*, to go back over it, to initiate a dialogue so that his daughter will articulate any vows she may have taken, and then he can properly annul them having heard. The blanket statement draws her into sharing, whereupon the real act of annulment can occur.

This reading is, admittedly, very difficult to square with the plain meaning of the *mishnah*. It imposes a great deal onto a text that reads simply as a completed act.

### Act vs. Effect Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע מִסֵּיפָא: וְכֵן הַבַּעַל, עַד שֶׁלֹּא תִּכָּנֵס לִרְשׁוּתוֹ, אוֹמֵר לָהּ. הָכָא נָמֵי, דְּאָמַר לַהּ: ״לְכִי שָׁמַעְנָא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear from the latter clause** of the mishna: **And similarly, the husband, before she would enter his jurisdiction,** i.e., while they were still betrothed, **would say to her:** All vows that you vowed before you entered my jurisdiction are hereby nullified. This implies that he can nullify vows without hearing them. The Gemara responds: **Here too,** it means **that he says to her: When I hear** the particular vow, then it will be nullified.

The *Gemara* then turns to the *seifa*: *vechen haba'al ad shelo tikanes l'rishuto amar lah* — the groom too says it before the *chuppah*. Here too, did he not act without hearing? The *Gemara* responds: *hacha nami d'amar lah l'chi shamana* — here too, he says to her: I will annul it when I hear. Some manuscript readings elaborate this in the same language as before; others give only the compressed formulation, *l'chi shamana*, meaning the annulment takes effect upon hearing.

This reading opens up a conceptually interesting middle position: perhaps hearing is required not for the *act* of annulment, but for the *effect* of annulment. The husband performs the act of *hafarah* now, but it takes hold only once he has actually heard. This is structurally somewhat reminiscent of debates around *davar shelo ba l'olam*: the puzzle of whether and when legal acts can be initiated regarding things that are not yet fully present. One might compare it to the case of a man who says *harei at mekudeshet li* before he has converted — not only is there an external impediment, but the very act seems to have no legal substrate. Yet the *Gemara* here is searching for a middle path: the act can be initiated now, and the effect will crystallize upon hearing.

### Ran: Father vs. Husband

The Ran raises a pointed question on this middle position: if this explanation works for the husband — he does the act now and it takes effect when he hears — why doesn't the *Gemara* give the same answer for the father? Why is the father's case handled differently, with the device of opening a conversation to get her to articulate the vow?

The Ran's answer turns on the difference in timing. For the father, the problem is that by the time he hears, it may already be after the *chuppah* — at which point his authority over her vows is entirely gone. At the moment of hearing, she is completely outside his domain. For that combination to work — the act done while she was still in his house, the effect crystallizing after *nisu'in* when he has no authority — neither moment is one in which he is actually in a position to annul. The two halves cannot meaningfully combine.

For the husband, the situation is different. He performs the act now as an *arus*, at which point he has at least partial authority. He will hear after *nisu'in*, when he is fully in the role of *ba'al* and unambiguously empowered to annul new vows. The act of annulment and the moment of hearing therefore both occur within legitimate windows of some authority, and it is coherent for them to combine. The effect taking hold upon hearing, at a time when he is fully in control, is a workable legal structure.

## Future Vows Debate

### Future Vows Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הָאוֹמֵר לְאִשְׁתּוֹ ״כׇּל נְדָרִים שֶׁתִּדּוֹרִי עַד שֶׁאָבֹא מִמָּקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי הֲרֵי הֵן קַיָּימִין״ — לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם. ״הֲרֵי הֵן מוּפָרִין״, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: מוּפָר. וְהָא לָא שְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** another mishna to answer the question (*Nedarim* 75a): **One who says to his wife: All vows that you vow until I arrive from such and such a place are hereby ratified, has not said anything,** i.e., the vows are not ratified. If he says: All vows that you vow until then **are hereby nullified, Rabbi Eliezer says:** They are **nullified.** The Gemara comments: **But he did not** actually **hear** the particular vows, so one can infer from this that he need not hear her vows in order to nullify them.

The *Gemara* brings another *mishnah* — located two pages later at 75a — as a further proof. The case: a man says to his wife, *kol hanedarim shetidderi mikan ad she'avo mimakom ploni* — any vows you make from now until I return from such-and-such place. He is about to leave on a trip and wishes to address whatever vows his wife may take in his absence.

### No Kiyum Before Existence

If he declares *harei hen kayamim* — I am establishing them — *lo amar klum*, he has accomplished nothing. There is a universal consensus on this: one cannot perform *kiyum*, the act of establishing or affirming a vow, on something that does not yet exist. *Kiyum* is about anchoring and strengthening an existing vow; there is nothing to anchor if the vow has not yet been taken.

### R. Eliezer: Hafarah Before Existence

However, if he says *harei hen mufarim* — I am annulling them — *Rabbi Eliezer omer mufar*: Rabbi Eliezer holds this works. His reasoning: *im hefer nedarim sheba'u l'chlal issur, ra'uy yafer nedarim shelo ba'u l'chlal issur* — if one can annul a vow once it has already taken effect as a prohibition, how much more so should one be able to prevent it from ever taking effect in the first place. *Hafarah* is fundamentally about eliminating a prohibition; a preventive measure, he argues, is at least as valid as a curative one. The vow that does not yet exist is in an even more accessible state for annulment — there is less there to dissolve. This also explains why Rabbi Eliezer distinguishes *hafarah* from *kiyum*: *kiyum* requires something present to strengthen, whereas *hafarah* is about removal or prevention, and prevention is easier than cure.

### Chachamim: Parallel Requirement

The *Chachamim* respond from the verse: *ishah yekimenu ve'ishah yeferenu* — the husband shall establish it and the husband shall annul it. *Et sheba l'chlal hakeim ba l'chlal hafer, lo ba l'chlal hakeim lo ba l'chlal hafer* — only something that falls within the category of being establishable can be annulled; something that cannot be established cannot be annulled. This may be a *gezeirat hakatuv*, a formal Scriptural decree — the mechanism of annulment simply operates on existing vows — or it may reflect the logical symmetry that *ishah yekimenu ve'ishah yeferenu* presents *hakama* and *hafarah* as parallel operations on the same object. Either way, the *Chachamim* insist that the vow must exist before either operation can apply. Rabbi Eliezer concedes that *kiyum* requires existence, and the *Chachamim* argue that *hafarah* must be constrained in the same way.

### Act & Effect Upon Hearing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי, דְּאָמַר: ״לְכִי שָׁמַעְנָא״. וּלְמָה לִי מִן הַשְׁתָּא? לְכִי שָׁמַע לֵיפַר לַהּ! קָסָבַר: דִּלְמָא מִטְּרִידְנָא הָהִיא שַׁעְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **Here too,** one can understand the situation to be **that he says: When I hear** the particular vow, it will be nullified. The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **why do I** need, i.e., why must the husband state his nullification, **from now; let him nullify** them **for her when he** actually **hears** them. The Gemara answers: **He reasons: Perhaps I will be preoccupied at that moment** and will forget to nullify them. He therefore nullifies the vows beforehand, so that the nullification will take effect automatically when he hears them.

Now the *Gemara* returns to the *shmiea* question. In this *mishnah*, nobody — neither Rabbi Eliezer nor the *Chachamim* — raises any objection about the fact that the husband has not heard the vows. The entire debate is whether *hafarah* can apply to vows not yet in existence; the absence of hearing goes unremarked. The *Gemara* notes: *v'ha lo shama* — he has not heard — and yet this concerns no one?

The *Gemara* answers: *hacha nami d'amar lich'she'eshamana* — here too, he says, I am annulling it, and it will take effect when I hear. Once again: the act is performed now; the effect waits upon hearing. The *Chachamim*'s objection to Rabbi Eliezer is that the vow does not yet exist, not that hearing is missing. Both sides operate within the assumption that hearing is what the effect waits upon, but neither treats its absence as independently disqualifying the act.

### Why State It Now

This raises an obvious question: if the annulment only takes effect when he hears, why must he make the declaration before departing? Why not simply annul each vow as he hears about it upon his return?

The *Gemara* answers: *kasavar dilma matridna hi sha'ata* — he worries that he will be distracted at that later moment. Coming home from a trip, there are many demands on his attention. His wife might tell him about a vow she took, and by the time he has settled and remembered to formally address it, twenty-four hours will have passed and his silence will have been treated as *hakama*. He therefore initiates the process now, before he is distracted, setting things in motion so that the effect can crystallize upon hearing without risk of the window closing.

## Epitropos & Shaliach

### Epitropos Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הָאוֹמֵר לְאַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס ״כׇּל נְדָרִים שֶׁנּוֹדֶרֶת אִשְׁתִּי מִכָּאן וְעַד שֶׁאָבֹא מִמָּקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי הָפֵר״, וְהֵפֵר לֵהּ, יָכוֹל יְהוּ מוּפָרִין — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִישָׁהּ יְקִימֶנּוּ וְאִישָׁהּ יְפֵרֶנּוּ״. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: In the case of **one who says to a steward [*apotropos*]** appointed to manage his affairs in his absence: **All vows that my wife vows from now until I arrive from such and such a place** you should **nullify, and** the steward **nullified** the vows **for her,** one **might** have thought that **they would be nullified.** Therefore, **the verse states: "Her husband may ratify it, or her husband may nullify it"** (Numbers 30:14). The repetition of "her husband" teaches that it is the husband alone who may nullify his wife's vows; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yoshiya.**

A further *mishnah* — also upcoming in the *masechta* — introduces the case of the *epitropos*: a man is about to leave on a trip and says to the steward running his household, *kol nedarim shenoderet ishti mikan ve'ad she'avo mimakom ploni* — any vows my wife takes from now until I return, *hafer*, annul them. The steward hears her take a vow and annuls it. Has the annulment taken effect?

### R. Yoshiya vs. R. Yonatan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מָצִינוּ בְּכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ שְׁלוּחוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם כְּמוֹתוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yonatan said to him: We have found everywhere in the Torah** that the legal status of **a person's agent is like** that of **himself.** Therefore, a steward can nullify the vows on the husband's behalf.

Rabbi Yoshiya says no: *ishah yekimenu ve'ishah yeferenu* — only the husband can annul; this is not a power that can be transferred. Rabbi Yonatan responds: *matzinu bechol hatorah kullaha shelucho shel adam kemoso* — throughout the entire Torah, an agent stands in the place of the one who appointed him. Why should this power be different?

### Transferability of Powers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה לָא קָאָמַר אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דִּגְזֵירַת הַכָּתוּב הוּא ״אִישָׁהּ יְקִימֶנּוּ וְאִישָׁהּ יְפֵרֶנּוּ״, אֲבָל דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא שְׁלוּחוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם כְּמוֹתוֹ. וְהָא לָא שְׁמִיעַ לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara points out: **And even Rabbi Yoshiya says** that a steward cannot nullify the wife's vows **only because it is a Torah edict,** based upon the words **"her husband may ratify it, or her husband may nullify it"** (Numbers 30:14). **But** according **to everyone,** the principle that the legal status of **a person's agent is like** that of **himself** is generally valid. The only objection to the steward nullifying the vows is the Torah edict. The Gemara asks: **But** these vows **were not heard by** the steward? This indicates that not having heard the vows is not an obstacle to nullification.

The *Gemara* notes that the debate between Rabbi Yoshiya and Rabbi Yonatan concerns whether this particular power can be delegated. Not all legal capacities are transferable. There is a category of acts that are not merely about being invested with a power but about personally and physically performing something. *Chalitza* is the classic example — one cannot appoint a *shaliach* to perform *chalitza* on one's behalf, because the act is constituted by the personal participation of the principals. Similarly, one cannot appoint a *shaliach* to perform a *mitzvah* in the purely personal sense. The conceptual framework, developed in the Brisker tradition, distinguishes between a power invested in the person that can be transferred, and a personal act that cannot. The debate here is precisely which category *hafarah* falls into.

The *Gemara* adds a clarifying point: even Rabbi Yoshiya, who says the *epitropos* cannot annul, concedes that this is only *mishum gezeirat hakatuv* — because of the Scriptural decree of *ishah yekimenu ve'ishah yeferenu*. In principle, the act of annulment is of a type that *could* be transferred; it is the verse that restricts it to the husband. *Avald'chulei alma shelucho shel adam kemoso* — everyone agrees that absent that verse, transfer would be conceptually valid.

### Shaliach Hearing Problem

This concession generates a striking problem. If the *shaliach* could in principle annul on the husband's behalf, how would that work given that the *shaliach* heard the vow but the husband did not? If hearing is what empowers the annulment, and the husband has not heard, then the husband is not in a position to annul — and an agent cannot possess authority that the principal himself lacks.

### Ran: Hearing as Circumstance

The Ran resolves this by drawing a precise conceptual distinction. Hearing, he explains, is not itself a legal power — it is the *circumstances* under which the power to annul arises. One cannot transfer circumstances to a *shaliach*. What one can transfer is a legal power, once it has been activated by the relevant circumstances. The hearing itself — a passive, experiential state — is not the kind of act that can be delegated. The *shaliach* hearing is not equivalent to the husband hearing in any legally operative sense.

Once this distinction is drawn, the *Gemara*'s assumption becomes intelligible: if we could transfer the *power* of annulment (absent Rabbi Yoshiya's verse), the *shaliach*'s hearing would serve as the circumstances activating that power, and the husband's prior delegation would constitute the transfer of the now-activated power. But there is no way to delegate the *hearing itself* so as to produce the same result as if the husband personally heard. Hearing is circumstance, not power.

### Tosafot: No Shaliach for Hearing

Tosafot's answer, found in manuscript traditions added to this *perek*, states the point more directly: *de'inyan shemi'ah eino na'aseh shaliach* — in the matter of hearing, one cannot make a *shaliach*. The reasoning implicit in this formulation is the same: hearing is not a legal act, and therefore not the kind of thing for which agency can be constituted. You can transfer a legal power; you cannot make another person hear for you in any legally operative sense.

The *Gemara*'s resolution in this case — *hacha nami de'amar lah lechish'shameina mefer lach* — is structurally the same as the previous cases. The husband tells the *shaliach*: annul when you hear, and it will take effect when I hear. The *shaliach* performs the act; the effect waits upon the husband's own hearing. Why use a *shaliach* at all if he can do it himself upon hearing? *Savar dilma mitradna* — he worries about distraction. The *shaliach* heard and acted; as soon as the husband hears, the effect crystallizes. The pattern is now entirely consistent across all the cases the *Gemara* has examined.

## Cheresh Question

### Can Cheresh Annul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: חֵרֵשׁ, מַהוּ שֶׁיָּפֵר לְאִשְׁתּוֹ? אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר בַּעַל מֵיפֵר בְּלֹא שְׁמִיעָה — מִשּׁוּם דְּבַר מִישְׁמַע הוּא, אֲבָל חֵרֵשׁ דְּלָאו בַּר מִישְׁמַע הוּא, הַיְינוּ דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rami bar Ḥama asks:** With regard to **a deaf man, what is** the *halakha* **with regard to his nullifying** vows **for his wife? If you say** that **a husband** who is not deaf **can nullify** a vow **without hearing** it, then perhaps this is **because he is capable of hearing. But** with regard to **a deaf man, who is not capable of hearing,** perhaps **this is** an application of the principle derived from the statement **of Rabbi Zeira.**

Rami Bar Chama now poses his second question: *cheresh mahu she-yafer l'ishto?* Can a deaf man — a *cheresh* in the Talmudic sense of one who speaks but cannot hear — annul vows for his wife? This question is built on the assumption that the husband does not need to actually hear in order to annul. If hearing were literally required, the question would be moot. The question therefore presupposes at least some degree of *lav davka* in the *shmiea* requirement, and asks whether even the residual requirement — membership in the *category* of those capable of hearing — matters.

### Bar Shmiya Requirement

The formulation: *im timtzei lomar ba'al mefer belo shemia* — if you say the husband can annul without hearing — *mishum de-bar nishma hu*, it is because he belongs to the category of hearing. *Aval cheresh de-lav bar nishma hu* — but a deaf person who is not in that category — can he annul at all? This would apply the principle of Rabbi Zeira.

### R. Zeira's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: כׇּל הָרָאוּי לְבִילָּה — אֵין בִּילָּה מְעַכֶּבֶת בּוֹ, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵין רָאוּי לְבִילָּה — בִּילָּה מְעַכֶּבֶת בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Zeira said:** For **any** amount of flour **suitable for mingling** with oil in a meal-offering, **mingling is not indispensable for it.** Even though it is a mitzva to mingle the flour and oil *ab initio*, if they were not mingled, the meal-offering is still valid. **But** for **any** amount of flour **not suitable for mingling, mingling is indispensable for it,** and such a meal-offering is invalid. The principle is: *Ab initio* requirements prevent the fulfillment of a mitzva in situations where they are not merely absent but impossible. In this case, the deaf man does not merely not hear the vow, it is impossible for him to do so.

Rabbi Zeira's principle: *kol ha-rauy l'villah ein billah me-akevet bo, vechol she-ein rauy l'villah billah me-akevet bo* — anything fit to be mixed, the mixing does not actually prevent validity even if not performed; but if it is not fit to be mixed, then mixing — had it been possible — would have been required, and its impossibility renders it invalid. The context is the *mincha* offering: the Torah requires the flour and oil to be mixed, but if the *mincha* is of such a large quantity that mixing is physically impossible, it is invalid — whereas if mixing is possible but simply not done, it remains valid. Potential, not actual performance, is what matters; but the potential must be real.

Applied here: a normal husband is *bar shemia* — capable of hearing — and therefore the fact that he did not actually hear does not prevent annulment. But a *cheresh* lacks even the potential. The question is whether this disqualifies him.

Tosafot and others note a broader theoretical question lurking here: how does one determine, across different *mitzvos* and legal acts, when the principle of *rauy* — fitness or potential — is sufficient in place of actual performance? The *Gemara* does not resolve this general question but applies the specific query to the *cheresh*.

### Beraita: Prat L'Eishet Cheresh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִלְמָא: ״וְשָׁמַע אִישָׁהּ״ לָא מְעַכֵּב. אָמַר רָבָא: תָּא שְׁמַע ״וְשָׁמַע אִישָׁהּ״ — פְּרָט לְאֵשֶׁת חֵרֵשׁ. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps** the phrase **"and her husband hears it"** (Numbers 30:8) **does not** mean that hearing **is indispensable** to the nullification of a vow, so that even a deaf man can nullify his wife's vows. **Rava said: Come** and **hear** a *baraita* interpreting that verse: **"And her husband hears it";** this **excludes the wife of a deaf man.** The Gemara concludes: **Learn from** this *baraita* that a deaf man cannot nullify his wife's vows.

Rava brings a *beraita* in response: *veshamah ishah — prat l'eishet cheresh* — "and her husband heard" — this comes to exclude the wife of a deaf man. The exclusion is explicit and unambiguous.

### Category Membership Matters

From this *beraita*, many *Rishonim* draw an important inference. The *beraita* specifically singles out the *cheresh* as excluded. If the mere failure to hear were itself a disqualification, there would be no need to identify the *cheresh* as a special case — anyone who did not hear would already be excluded. The fact that the *beraita* reserves its exclusion for the *cheresh* specifically implies that in ordinary cases, the husband's not having heard is not itself an obstacle. What disqualifies the *cheresh* is not that he failed to hear this particular vow, but that he is constitutively outside the category of hearing — he is *lav bar shemia*. A hearing husband who simply did not hear a particular vow remains *bar shemia*, and can therefore annul.

This is a striking conclusion. The *Torah* says *veshamah ishah*, and the *halacha* — as understood by most *Rishonim* — is that the husband need not actually hear. The *pshat* understanding of *beyom shmo'o* is perhaps best read as defining the window of *hakama*: from the moment of hearing, the husband has twenty-four hours before his silence becomes acquiescence. But the act of annulment itself is not contingent on prior hearing.

## Two Wives Question

### Two Wives Simultaneously

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בַּעַל, מַהוּ שֶׁיָּפֵר לִשְׁתֵּי נָשָׁיו בְּבַת אַחַת? ״אוֹתָהּ״ דַּוְקָא, אוֹ לָאו דַּוְקָא?
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Concerning **a husband, what is** the *halakha* **with regard to nullifying** vows **for his two wives simultaneously?** Do the words "but if her husband disallows **her** on the day that he hears it, and he nullifies her vow which is upon her" (Numbers 30:9), stated in the singular, refer **specifically** to one wife? **Or,** perhaps it does **not** refer **specifically** to one wife, and a husband can nullify the vows of more than one wife simultaneously.

Rami Bar Chama poses a third question: *ba'al mahu she-yafer l'shtei nashav b'vat achat?* Can a husband with two wives annul vows for both of them simultaneously? He has two wives who have both made vows, and he says *mufar lachem* — it is annulled for you — addressing both at once. Is that effective?

### Singular 'Otah' in Verse

The question turns on the language of the *pesukim*. The relevant phrase is *yafer otah* — he annuls *her*, singular. The text consistently uses the singular form: *ve'im henia osah*, and her husband disallowed *her*; *ve'im bayom shmo'o isha yania osah*; *ve'hefer es nidrah*. The *hafarah* is grammatically directed at one woman at a time. Is this *davka* — meaning annulment must be performed individually for each wife — or *lav davka*, meaning the plural could be encompassed?

It is worth noting that in these *pesukim* the verb of *hafarah* applies to the *neder*, while the verb *yania* — disallowing — applies to *her*, the woman. The singular forms run through both verbs, suggesting the entire process is conceived as an individual act directed at one wife and her vow.

### Sota Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: אֵין מַשְׁקִין שְׁתֵּי סוֹטוֹת כְּאַחַת, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁלִּבָּהּ גַּס בַּחֲבֶירְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: In the *sota* ritual, performed by women suspected by their husbands of having committed adultery, **two *sota* women are not given to drink** the bitter waters **as one.** This is **because the heart** of each becomes **emboldened [*gas*] in the** presence of **the other** woman, and if one is guilty she will lack the humility to confess.

Ravina brings a proof from *sota*: *ein mashkin shtei sotos ke'achas* — one may not administer the *sota* waters to two *sotos* simultaneously. The ritual involving a wife suspected of adultery must be performed individually, not for two women at once.

### Reason for Separation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לֹא מִן הַשֵּׁם הוּא זֶה, אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וְהִשְׁקָהּ״ — אוֹתָהּ, לְבַדָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: This is not for** that **reason [*lo min hashem hu zeh*], but because it is stated: "And he shall make her drink"** (Numbers 5:27), which indicates **her by herself.** Similarly, the words "disallows her" (Numbers 30:9) should be read as referring specifically to a single woman, indicating that a man cannot nullify the vows of two wives simultaneously.

The *mishnah* in *sota* records a dispute about the reason. The *Tanna Kamma* says: *mipnei she-libbah gas ba-chavertah* — because one woman's heart becomes emboldened by her companion. Seeing the other woman undergo the ritual without visible consequence, she may lose her fear and not confess even if guilty. Rabbi Yehuda says the reason is simply from the verse: *ve'hishkah osah levaddah* — and he shall make *her* drink, *levaddah* — alone, individually.

### Reason for Separation (Continued)

The *Rishonim* debate whether the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Yehuda are in substantive disagreement or whether the *Tanna Kamma* is simply explaining the rationale behind the verse, in the manner of Rabbi Shimon who is *doresh ta'ama dikra*. If the *Tanna Kamma* also relies on the verse fundamentally, then all opinions would require the procedure to be individual. This debate affects the parallel case of *hafarah*.

### Individual Annulment Required

The *Gemara*'s apparent conclusion is that annulment must be performed for each wife individually. The consistent use of the singular throughout the *pesukim*, reinforced by the *sota* parallel, points in that direction. Precisely how the *Tanna Kamma*'s position in the *sota* case affects the *hafarah* parallel remains somewhat open, but the overall thrust of the *Gemara* is toward requiring individual annulment.

## Bogeret & Almanah

### Mishnah: Bogeret & Almanah

The daf concludes with a *mishnah* concerning several cases in which a woman is technically still in the stage of *eirusin* but the time for *nisu'in* has effectively arrived. A *bogeret* — a fully adult woman — who has waited twelve months after *kiddushin*; or an *almanah* — a widow — for whom *chazal* set only a thirty-day waiting period between *kiddushin* and *nisu'in* when that period has elapsed. These are scenarios in which *nisu'in* has not yet occurred but is expected imminently.

### R. Eliezer: Payment = Authority

Rabbi Eliezer rules: *ho'il uba'alah chayav bimezonoseha* — since the husband is now obligated to provide for her food, this is sufficient to confer upon him the ability to annul her vows even without actual *nisu'in*. His financial obligation establishes his effective relationship to her as that of a husband, and therefore he can annul unilaterally — without the father's participation — even though *de'oraisa* she is technically still in the *eirusin* stage. This is puzzling: if she is not *halachically* a full wife, how can he have the annulment authority of a husband? The question presses especially given that this is a *leniency* — we are granting him a power he would not normally have.

### Chachamim: Actual Nisu'in

The *Chachamim* disagree: *ein haba'al mefer ad shetikanes lirshuto* — the husband cannot annul until she genuinely enters his domain through actual *nisu'in*. The formal legal status must be achieved; the financial obligation does not substitute for the real thing.

## Terumah & Status

### Rava: Reconciliation

Rava observes that Rabbi Eliezer's position here aligns with the *Mishnah Rishonah* — an earlier ruling — on a parallel question. *Rabbi Eliezer u-Mishnah Rishonah amru davar echad* — they said the same thing.

### Terumah After 12 Months

The *Mishnah Rishonah* in *Kesubot* ruled: *nosnin l'vsulah yod bet chodesh l'parnes atzmah* — a virgin is given twelve months to prepare her trousseau. If twelve months pass and the husband has not yet brought her to *nisu'in*, his financial responsibilities kick in — *ochelet mishelo*, she eats at his expense — and more strikingly: *ve'ochelet b'terumah* — if he is a *kohen*, she may even eat *terumah*, despite not yet being married. Normally a woman must be genuinely *nesuah* to a *kohen* to eat *terumah*. The *Mishnah Rishonah* extended this right to her based on the elapsed time, treating her effective status as already spousal even before *nisu'in*.

### Later Disallowance

A later *beit din* subsequently retracted this position: *eina isha ochelet b'terumah ad shetikanes l'chuppah* — a woman may not eat *terumah* until she has actually entered the *chuppah*. The later ruling tightened the requirement, refusing to extend spousal status for *issur* purposes before *nisu'in* had actually occurred. Thus the *Mishnah Rishonah* was willing to treat the twelve-month threshold as conferring near-marital status for *terumah* consumption; the later ruling was not. Rabbi Eliezer's position in our *mishnah* — that the husband's food obligation translates into annulment authority — maps onto the *Mishnah Rishonah*'s analogous expansion of spousal status.

## Abaye's Objection

### Terumah vs. Nedarim

Abaye objects to Rava's alignment: *dilma lo hi* — perhaps the two positions are not actually the same. The *Mishnah Rishonah* may have permitted *terumah* consumption only because *terumah bizman hazeh* is a *d'rabbanan* concern; the rabbis had the flexibility to relax it when circumstances warranted. But *nedarim* are *d'oraita* — the Torah itself controls who may annul. Even if the *Mishnah Rishonah* was willing to stretch the concept of spousal status for a rabbinic prohibition like *terumah*, it would never have granted the husband power to annul vows, which is a Torah-level matter. *Aval nedarim d'oraita eima lo* — for vows, we would not have extended that power.

### Rabbinic vs. Torah Issue

What does it mean to say *terumah* is *d'rabbanan* in this context? The Talmud elsewhere discusses which *pasuk* establishes the wife's right to eat *terumah*: is it *anshei beito hem yochlu b'lachmo* — people of his household — which would require *nisu'in*; or is it *kinyan kaspo hu yochal b'lachmo* — that which he acquired with his money — which would apply from *kiddushin*? The *Yerushalmi* reads *beito* and holds that even *d'oraita* she requires *nisu'in*. The *Bavli* reads *kinyan kaspo* and holds that *d'oraita* even an *arusa* may eat *terumah*. On the Bavli's reading, the restriction of *terumah* to *nesuot* is entirely rabbinic, making the *Mishnah Rishonah*'s leniency a relaxation of a rabbinic stringency — which is a very different matter from relaxing a Torah-level restriction on who may perform *hafarah*.

### Why Terumah Restricted

Why did the rabbis restrict *terumah* to *nesuot* in the first place if *d'oraita* an *arusa* is permitted? The most straightforward reason is practical: while still in her father's house during *eirusin*, she is likely to share the *terumah* with members of her family who are not *kohanim*, which would constitute a Torah violation. There is also a secondary concern — the possibility that the *kiddushin* will be undone by a *mekach ta'os*, retroactively rendering her never married, in which case her eating *terumah* would have been improper. Once the twelve months have passed and she is effectively standing at the threshold of *nisu'in*, the rabbis were willing — in the *Mishnah Rishonah* — to permit her to eat, since the sharing concern is less acute and the marriage is imminent. But this entire flexibility, Abaye argues, operates within the rabbinic sphere, and tells us nothing about the Torah-level rules of *hafarah*.

## Al Da'as Ba'al

### Implicit Husband Consent

The *Gemara* introduces the teaching of Rav Pinchas in the name of Rava: *kol hanoderet al da'as ba'alah hi noderet* — any woman who takes a vow, she vows with her husband's assent in mind. When a woman who is already in a marital relationship — or a relationship effectively equivalent to marriage — takes a vow, it is presumed that she intends the vow to be conditional upon her husband's acceptance. This is not an explicit *tnai* that she articulates, but a psychological reality about how she understands her own act.

### Psychological Intent

This reasoning applies specifically to a woman who has been betrothed for twelve months, the stage at which Rabbi Eliezer permits the husband to annul. At that point, her sense of herself as bound to this man — as living within the framework of their upcoming marriage — is sufficiently developed that when she takes a vow, her implicit orientation is toward a life with him. She vows, as it were, on the understanding that he would agree. This psychological fact, Rav Pinchas argues, is what grounds the husband's ability to annul at that stage.

### Vows vs. Terumah

This reasoning, however, applies to *nedarim* specifically. When a woman takes a vow, her subjective intent shapes the vow's structure. The concept of *al da'as ba'alah* captures something internal to the vow itself — it was taken with an implicit understanding. *Terumah* consumption, by contrast, is entirely an external legal status: she either has the right to eat *terumah* or she does not, and that depends on her formal legal standing, not on any psychological orientation she holds. The *Mishnah Rishonah* granted her *terumah* rights based on an analogous assessment of her practical status; the later *beit din* withdrew it. But for *nedarim*, the psychological dimension is different — the vow's own structure already incorporates the husband's hypothetical consent, and therefore *hafarah* is possible even before technical *nisu'in*.

## Ran: Redefining Husband

### Al Da'as Non-Husband

The Ran raises a sharp question against the *al da'as ba'alah* framework. If the mechanism is that she vowed with somebody else's assent in mind, can that person — whoever it is — now annul the vow? For instance, if someone says *al da'as* of a third party, not a husband at all, does that person acquire annulment power? And conversely, if *al da'as ba'alah hi noderet* is the operative principle, does the husband lose the twenty-four-hour window — can he annul at any time, since the vow was always implicitly conditional on his assent? The formal mechanics of *hafarah* — the specific language required, the twenty-four-hour limit — seem to sit in tension with a model that reduces *hafarah* to an implicit *tnai*.

### Formal Hafarah Mechanism

The Ran resolves this by insisting on the formal character of *hafarah*. *Hafarah* is not a *tnai* — it is not simply that the vow dissolves because a condition was not met. *Hafarah* is a formal Torah mechanism: it requires specific language, it operates within a twenty-four-hour window, it is a *gezeirat hakatuv* that the Torah invested in the husband. *U-gzeirat hakatuv hu, u-mishum hachi ein kol leshonot shavim bo* — proper language is required; not every formulation will do. Any view that reduces *hafarah* to the operation of an implicit *tnai* cannot account for why the husband must use the formal *lashon shel hafarah* or why a twenty-four-hour window applies at all.

### Torah's Rationale

So what role does *al da'as ba'alah* play? The Ran articulates it as follows: *hafarah* is indeed a formal Torah mechanism, complete with its own rules. But the Torah had a reason for investing this mechanism in the husband specifically. That reason is the presumption that the wife, when she takes a vow, does so with her husband in mind — *mishum d'al da'as ba'al hi noderet*. The Torah's decision to empower the husband is grounded in this psychological reality about the wife's mental state when she vows. This is a reading of the *ta'ama d'kra* — the reason behind the verse — and it is a strikingly woman-centric framing. The power is not given to the husband because he is the dominant party in whose house she lives and whose authority overrides hers. Rather, the Torah empowers him because the vow itself was implicitly made with him in mind, and it is therefore appropriate that he should be the one who can address it.

### Expanding Husband Category

From this understanding of the Torah's rationale, the Ran draws a conclusion about the scope of the category "husband" as the Torah uses it. If the reason the Torah empowers the *ba'al* is that the wife takes vows with him in mind, then the category of *ba'al* for purposes of *hafarah* should include any person who genuinely occupies that role in her psychological universe. After twelve months of *kiddushin*, when the relationship has matured to the point that she is practically living for the moment of *nisu'in*, when he is providing for her food, when she thinks of herself as functionally his wife — at that stage, an *arus* fits within the Torah's conception of *ba'al* for purposes of *hafarah*. The Torah says *ishah*, husband, and Rabbi Eliezer argues that the Torah would recognize the *arus* in this stage as falling within that category, precisely because the reason for the category is fully operative.

### Role-Based Limitation

The Ran is careful to confine this reasoning. It is not an invitation to say that any person whom a woman holds in high regard, or with whose approval she takes vows in mind, can thereby annul those vows. The power of *hafarah* flows through the formal Torah mechanism. What the *al da'as* reasoning does is define who fits within the Torah's category of *ba'al* for purposes of that mechanism. That category can be extended to an *arus* who stands in the husband's role — who is, in every relevant sense, the person she is married to, albeit not yet under *chuppah*. It cannot be extended to an arbitrary third party who is not in that role at all. The formal structure of *hafarah* remains intact; what expands, carefully and on principled grounds, is the definition of who qualifies as the *ba'al* whom the Torah empowered to perform it. This, the Ran concludes, is the most coherent account of Rabbi Eliezer's position — and it explains how *hafarah* before *nisu'in* is not a departure from the Torah's framework but a faithful application of its underlying logic.
