# Bava Batra 73 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Boat Sale & Inclusions

### New Perek Intro

With daf 73 we begin a new *perek*, *Hamocher Es Hasfina* — "one who sells a boat." The basic framework of inquiry is the same as the previous *perek*: when one sells something, what is included in the sale? The shift, however, is significant. We are moving away from landed property and into the realm of *metaltelin* — movable property — beginning with a boat, which, though enormous, is itself a *metaltelin*. As will become apparent, this distinction carries important conceptual consequences for what is and is not included in a sale. It is also worth noting at the outset that while this *perek* opens with a legal *mishnah*, the bulk of the next several *dappim* will be dominated by *aggadata* — non-legal narrative material of a markedly fantastical character, the interpretation of which occupied the *rishonim* considerably.

### Mishnah: Boat Sale

The *mishnah* states: *hamocher es hasfina machar es hatoran* — one who sells a boat has also sold the mast; *es hanes*, the sails; *ve'es ha'ugin*, the anchors; and *ve'es kol hamanhiginosah*, all those things that cause it to move — the oars. These items, though not physically fused to the hull, are included in the sale by default.

### Included vs. Excluded

The *mishnah* continues: *aval lo machar lo es ha'avadim* — but one has not sold the slaves who serve as crew. Even though the crew is indispensable to the operation of the vessel, they are excluded. Likewise excluded are *hamartzufim* — the sacks or containers — and *ha'antiki* — the cargo itself. The *chiddush* of the *mishnah* is therefore notable: things that are movable and not physically attached to the boat are nevertheless included in the sale, whereas when land is sold, movables not physically attached are generally excluded. Here, precisely because everything is *metaltelin*, related *metaltelin* may be bundled together in the transaction. What remains excluded are the crew (who move under their own volition and possess a distinct legal identity), the cargo (which is variable and incidental to the vessel itself), and the sacks (which are understood as appurtenant to the cargo rather than to the boat).

### Kol Mah Shebetochah

The *mishnah* concludes with an exception clause: *ubizman she'amar lo hi vechol mah shebetochah* — if the seller said "it and everything in it," then *harei kulan mechorin* — all of it is sold, including the slaves and cargo. A comparison with the earlier *mishnah* on the sale of a city (*hamocher es ha'ir*, 68a) is instructive. There too, the formula *hi vechol mah shebetochah* was required to include *behemah va'avadim*. The conceptual parallel is clear, though the precise texture of what is "in" a boat differs from what is "in" a city, given that the crew are a functional necessity of the vessel in a way that slaves need not be of a town.

## Boat Component Terms

### Toran: The Mast

The *gemara* proceeds to define the terms used in the *mishnah*. *Toran* is *iskarya* — the mast. The *Rashbam* explains it as the tall pole over which a sail is spread at the prow of the ship. The *gemara* adduces a biblical source: *erez milevanon lakachu la'asos toran alayich* — "they took cedar from Lebanon to make a mast upon you" (Yechezkel 27:5). The use of cedar — a famously tall and strong tree — makes clear by context that we are speaking of a mast.

### Nes: The Sails

*Nes* is identified as *adra* — the sail. The biblical source is: *sheish berikmah mimitzrayim hayah mafriseich lihyos lach lenes* — "embroidered linen from Egypt was spread over you to be for you as a sail" (Yechezkel 27:7). The image of something woven and spread confirms the identification. One might note the broader semantic range of the word *nes*: a flag, a miracle, something raised high to be seen. The *Ramban* elsewhere discusses this connection — the purpose of a *nisayon* is to raise up and demonstrate, as one raises a banner — and there is a possible conceptual link between the *nes* as ensign or flag and the *nes* as sail, both being things that are unfurled and visible. The *Rashbam*'s reading makes clear that in context this refers to the mainsail.

### Ugin: Anchors

*Tani Rabbi Chiya*: *eilu oginin* — these are the anchors. The *gemara* sources this from the book of Rut: *halehen te'ageina* — "will you be anchored to them?" (Rut 1:13), referring to Naomi's daughters-in-law waiting, bound in place, for husbands who may never come. The word *te'ageina* is cognate with *ugin*, both drawing on the root of *aguna* — a woman who is "anchored," unable to move forward. The *Rashbam* explains that the anchors (*oginin*) are iron implements thrown into the water to hold the ship in place: *umashlichin bamayim l'ha'amid hasafina b'makom echad*. It is striking that the same root yields both the nautical anchor and the *halachic* concept of the *aguna*.

### Manhigin: Oars

*Amar Rabbi Abba*: *eilu hameshotin shelah* — these are the oars. Two biblical sources are offered. The first: *alonim mibashan asu moshotaich* — "oaks of Bashan they made into your oars" (Yechezkel 27:6), again using a large hardwood tree, establishing by context that *mashot* are the instruments of rowing. The second source: *vayerdu me'oniyoteihem kol tofshei mashot* — "all those who hold the oar came down from their ships" (ibid. 27:29), where the *mashot* are clearly the instruments grasped by those who row.

### Additional Items

A *baraita* adds two items not mentioned in the *mishnah* itself. *Hamocher et hasafina machar et ha'iskala* — the sale of a boat includes the gangplank, the ramp used to board and disembark. The *Rashbam* identifies *iskala* as the gangplank. The sale also includes *bor hamayim shebetochah* — the cistern of fresh water kept aboard the vessel. A subtle question arises as to whether this means the cistern together with the water in it, or merely the cistern itself; one might argue that fresh water, being a consumable, is more like cargo and should perhaps be excluded.

## Chattel vs. Land Logic

### Movables with Chattel

Before proceeding to the disputes of *Rabbi Natan* and *Sumchus*, it is worth pausing to articulate the conceptual distinction between the rules governing the sale of land and those governing the sale of chattel. When land is sold, movables — *metaltelin* — are generally not included in the sale unless explicitly transferred, even if those movables are necessary for the use of the land. Here, by contrast, we sell the sail, the mast, the oars, and the gangplank together with the boat, even though all of these are independently movable and none are permanently fixed to the hull. The reason is that the boat is itself a *metaltelin*, and as such it is more natural to view the ensemble of related *metaltelin* as a unified package.

### Rashbam's Framework

The *Rashbam* introduces a broader conceptual framework to explain why this *perek* follows immediately after the *mishnah* and *sugya* concerning *hekdesh*. The previous *perek* dealt with what is included when one consecrates or gives as a gift a piece of landed property — and the rule there was expansive: *hekdesh* and *matanah* of land include even items that a regular sale would not convey. The *Rashbam* explains that this *perek* comes to teach the reverse principle for *chattel*: when one is *makdish* a boat, one does not thereby consecrate the cargo, the slaves, or the other excluded items — *lo hikdish et elu* — as is taught further on in this *perek*.

### Rashbam on Gifts

The *Rashbam* elaborates: *vechol hani dechashivna befirkin deleil* — all the items discussed in the previous *perek* (a bathhouse, an olive press, a city, a field) — *culhu mekarkei ninhu* — they are all landed property. Because land is the most fundamental and legally significant form of property, there is a natural tendency to view appurtenant items as secondary extensions of the land itself. Therefore, when land is the subject of a *hekdesh* or *matanah*, additional items become packaged together with it. *Niknin ktzat agav ar'a legabei hekdesh umatanah* — they are acquired, in a sense, as appendages of the land in the contexts of consecration and gift.

By contrast, this *perek* concerns *metaltelin* exclusively — *vechol hani dehai pirka metaltelin ninhu*. And the *Rashbam* states the principle: *lo shena mecher velo shena matanah vehekdesh, culhon shavin behani delo machar* — whether sale, gift, or *hekdesh*, they are equal: what is excluded from a sale is equally excluded from a gift or consecration. There is thus an apparent irony. On one hand, precisely because it is all *metaltelin*, a sale of a boat does include other movable items in a way a sale of land does not. On the other hand, the elevated legal status of land means that gifts and *hekdesh* of land sweep in more than gifts and *hekdesh* of chattel do. The two principles point in opposite directions, and fully reconciling them is not straightforward. Still, the *Rashbam*'s observation is important for understanding why the *mishnah* is placed here, immediately following the *hekdesh* discussion.

## Boat Component Terms (II)

### Betzi vs. Dugit

*Rabbi Natan omer*: one who sells a boat also sells the *betzi* — the small auxiliary vessel, essentially a dinghy, towed alongside the main ship and used to transport passengers and goods between the ship and the shore, since large vessels cannot dock directly on land. *Sumchus omer*: one who sells a boat has sold the *dugit*. *Amar Rava*: *betzi haynu dugit* — they are the same thing. If they are identical, why do the two *tanna'im* use different names?

### Regional Terminology

The *gemara* offers a geographically sensitive explanation. *Rabbi Natan bava'ah hava* — Rabbi Natan was from Babylonia, and so he used the Babylonian Aramaic term *butzi*, as in the expression *butzi'atza demayshan* — the little boats of Mayshan, a body of water in the Babylonian region. The word *butzi* is related to *bitza* — mud or marsh — evoking the marshy waterways of that region. *Sumchus devar Eretz Yisrael* — Sumchus, who was from the Land of Israel, used the term *dugit*, as in the verse *acharitchen besirot dugah* (Amos 4:2). The *Rashbam* explains that *dugit* connotes a small fishing vessel. Both terms describe the same object; the difference is entirely regional and linguistic.

## Aggadic Waves & Salvation

### Seafarers' Stories

With the legal discussion of boat terminology concluded, the *gemara* transitions into a major section of *aggadata*. *Amar Rabbah*: *ishtau li nechutei yama* — those who go down to the sea have told me these stories. Rabbah is thus reporting what seafarers related to him, not necessarily vouching for their literal truth. These tales belong to the long tradition of maritime travel accounts — a genre always prone to dramatic amplification — and the *gemara* presents them in that spirit.

### Wave & Divine Names

The first account: *hai gala d'mitba l'sefinah* — when a wave comes to submerge a boat, *mitchazi ki tzitzita denura* — one sees at its crest what appears like a fringe of white fire. These are the white caps visible at the top of a cresting wave. *Umachinan lei b'alveta d'chakik aleih* — and one strikes the wave with a rod upon which are carved the words: *Ehyeh Asher Ehyeh, Yah Hashem Tzevaot, Amen Amen Selah* — several divine names. Striking the wave with such a rod causes it to subside — *v'nayach* — and the ship is saved.

### Aggada Interpretation

This account — and the several that follow — raised for the *rishonim* the fundamental question of how to read *aggadata*. The *Rambam*, in the introduction to *Perek Chelek* in his *Perush Hamishnayot*, identifies three categories of readers. The first are those who take all *aggadot* of *Chazal* literally and believe them as stated; the *Rambam* calls these the fools. The second are those who also read them literally but conclude on that basis that *Chazal* were foolish — these are the *reshaim*. The third category — the wise — understands that *Chazal* never intended these as literal accounts but as metaphors. The *Rashba* and others take a somewhat more moderate position, inclining toward accepting *aggadot* as true in principle while acknowledging that certain accounts simply cannot be taken literally. It is largely the *aggadot* of this section of *Bava Batra* that became the primary exhibit in this debate.

### Maharsha: Nations

The *Maharsha* interprets the imagery of the wave according to a political-historical framework. The waves represent the *goyim* — the non-Jewish nations — that have brought devastation upon the Jewish people throughout history. The white caps at the crest, the fringe of fire, evoke the terrifying power of imperial conquest. The divine names carved into the rod represent God's protection of Israel; it is that protection alone which prevents the wave from overwhelming the ship entirely.

### HaGra: Yetzer Hara

The *Vilna Gaon* (the *HaGra*) reads the same passage as a map of internal spiritual struggle. The wave, on this reading, is the *yetzer hara* — the evil inclination. The white fringe at its top is significant: the *yetzer hara* presents itself as pure and appealing, with a bright and innocent appearance, making the danger all the more insidious. The divine names on the rod represent the spiritual resources available to combat the *yetzer hara* from within. This interpretation shifts the entire drama from the external world to the inner life of the human being navigating the turbulent sea of existence.

## Massive Wave Sizes

### Wave Measurements

The next account returns to Rabbah reporting from the seafarers: *bein gala l'gala tlas mei'a parsei* — between one wave and the next, a distance of 300 *parsa*. A *parsa* is four *mil*, and a *mil* is roughly a kilometer, making 300 *parsa* approximately 1,200 kilometers. *Verum'yei d'gala tlas mei'a parsa* — and the height of an individual wave, likewise 300 *parsa*. For context, the highest wave ever recorded in modern times was roughly 400 to 500 meters — a small fraction of a *parsa*. The figure of 300 is, throughout *rabbinic* literature, a number of hyperbole; as one might say today "a million people," Chazal use 300 to convey overwhelming magnitude.

### Lifted to Stars

*Zimna chada hava azinan b'orchas* — once we were on a sea journey. *V'dalinan gala ad d'chazinan beei marbeisa d'kochava zuta* — a wave lifted us up until we could see the resting place of a small star, elevated to the very heights of the sky. The *Rashbam* explains that the star was situated in an expanse equivalent in measure to forty *grivyei* of mustard seeds — an extraordinarily large space. *V'ee dalinan tfei havina miklyinan mei'hevlei* — had the wave lifted them any higher, they would have been scorched by the heat of the star, since the seafarers understood the stars to radiate not only light but also intense heat.

### Wave Dialogue

*V'rami lei gala kala l'chaverei* — and this wave, having reached such a height, called out to another wave in a kind of dialogue. It said: *chavartai shavakt midi b'alma d'lo shatfetei* — "my friend, have you left anything in the world that you have not drowned?" Having risen to such an extreme height, the wave presumed that it must have submerged the entire earth. *Emei lian v'navdei* — "tell me, and I will come and destroy it."

### Sand Boundary

The other wave replied: *pook chazi gvurtei d'marach* — "go and see the power of your Master." *Mlo chusa chala v'lo avri* — there is a line of sand, no wider than a hair's breadth, that I cannot cross. Despite all my power and height, I cannot cast myself beyond the shore. The *gemara* cites the verse: *Ha'osi lo tiru neum Hashem im panai lo tachilu asher samti chol gvul layam* — "Will you not fear Me, says Hashem? I have placed sand as the boundary of the sea" (Yirmiyahu 5:22). *Chok olam v'lo ya'avrenhu* — an eternal decree that the sea shall not surpass it. The *Maharsha*'s reading situates this exchange as an allegory of the nations of the world: mighty imperial powers, swollen with self-confidence, imagining they have consumed the entire world, only to be told that God's decree sets limits even on the most fearsome of earthly forces.

## Hormin Demon Tale

### Hormin Running

*Amar Rabbah lididi chazi li Hormin bar Lilit* — Rabbah said: I myself saw the demon Hormin, son of Lilit, *ki ka rahit akupa deshura demachuza* — running along the top of the walls surrounding Machuza. *Verahit parsha ki rachiv chayuta mitutei velo yachil lei* — alongside him a horseman riding at full gallop could not keep pace with him. The demon's speed was superhuman.

### Mule Acrobatics

On one occasion, the demon was provided with two mules — *tarti kudenayta* — which were positioned on two separate bridges over the river Rugnag. *Veshavar mehai lehai umehai lehai* — the demon leaped back and forth between the mules spanning the river. *Venakit trei mizgei dechamra bidei* — all the while he held two cups of wine, *umorik mehai lehai umehai lehai* — pouring wine from one cup, leaping, and catching it in the other without spilling a drop. *Velo natfa midi le'ara* — not a drop hit the ground. *Ve'oto hayom ya'alu shamayim yerdu tehomot hava* — this was on a day of violent storms, when even those at sea were being tossed between sky and depths, and he performed this feat without difficulty.

### Half-Demon Status

*Ad desham'u be malkuta vekatlohu* — until the news reached the king's palace, and they killed him. The *Rashbam* offers two explanations of which king this was. His first explanation is the king of the *shedim* — the king of demons — who was displeased that Hormin was displaying his powers before humans. His second explanation invokes Caesar, who feared that so powerful an entity might threaten his own rule. The *Rashbam* explains the mechanism that made Hormin vulnerable: *shehaya oto shed adam haba al hashidah vayador bein hanashim* — this demon was not a pure *shed* but the offspring of a human father and a female demon, a *shidah*, which is why he could ultimately be killed.

### Yetzer Hara Meaning

One reading of this passage frames it as an allegory for the *yetzer hara* and the dangers of self-indulgence. Hormin bar Lilit — the son of Lilit, who in tradition is associated with seduction and temptation — represents the heights of undisciplined self-gratification. His spectacular acrobatic performance on the walls, with wine cups flying between leaps, is a portrait of self-display and indulgence. The mules — sterile animals incapable of reproduction — underscore the ultimate barrenness of a life devoted to self-indulgence. The wall he runs upon represents the *Torah*, which is meant to serve as protection and boundary; instead, Hormin uses it as a stage for his performance. His activities, for all their dazzle, produce nothing.

## Antelope Beast Tale

### Antelope Size

*Amar Rabbah lididi chazi li orzila bar yoma* — Rabbah said: I myself saw a *re'em* — a great beast, understood to be some form of antelope or similar large creature — that was only one day old, and yet *dehava kehar Tavor* — it was the size of Mount Tabor. *Har Tavor kama havei? Arba parsei* — Mount Tabor is four *parsa* in extent.

### Antelope Measurements

*Umshacha d'tzavara tlata parsei* — the length of its neck alone was three *parsa*. *Uvei marba'ata d'reisha* — the resting place of its head — *parsa u'palga* — a *parsa* and a half.

### Dung Blocks Jordan

*Rma kufta vsachra l'Yardena* — it relieved itself, and the volume of its excrement was sufficient to block the Jordan River. The *Rashbam* notes that the rushing current of the Jordan eventually forced its way through. This, then, is a creature of almost incomprehensible scale — day-old, and already vast enough to block a major river.

## Chain of Creatures

### Creature Chain

*V'amar Rabbah bar bar Chana lididi chazi li hahi akrukashta* — Rabbah bar bar Chana said: I myself saw this *akrukashta*, which the *Rashbam* identifies as a *tzfardei'a* — a frog — *d'havya ki akra d'Hagronya* — that was the size of the town of Hagronya. *V'akra d'Hagronya kama havya? Shitin batei* — that town comprised sixty houses. *Ata tanina uvalah* — a *tanin* came and swallowed it. The word *tanin* has a range of meanings — sea monster, crocodile, serpent — and the *Rashbam* renders it here as a sea monster. Then *ata pushkantza* — a raven came — *uvalah l'tanina* — and swallowed the sea monster. *Vsillik yatib b'ilana* — the raven then flew up and settled on a tree branch.

### Tree's Strength

*Ta chazi kama nafish cheila d'ilana* — consider how powerful that tree must have been to support, on a single branch, the raven that had eaten the sea monster that had eaten the frog the size of sixty houses.

### Maharsha: Scholars

The *Maharsha* develops an allegorical reading of this chain. The *talmidei chachamim* are compared to the frogs — given to speech and to the waters of *Torah*. The successive predators represent the nations of the world, each consuming what came before. What allows everything to be sustained in the end — the tree — is the *Torah* itself, of which it is written that it is a *tree of life to those who hold fast to it* (*Mishlei* 3:18). The *Torah*, on this reading, is the force that sustains the entire chain; the strength of the tree is the strength of *Torah* learning, which supports the world even when all other forces are at work to devour one another.

## Massive Fish Narrative

### Fish & Cities

*V'amar Rabbah* — and Rabbah said: *zimna chada hava ka azlinan b'sefinta v'chazinan ha-hu kavra* — once I was traveling on a boat and I saw a certain fish. *V'yatba leh achlatina b'usya* — a parasite of some kind settled itself in the fish's nostrils, *v'adchuhu maya* — and the water cast it ashore, *u'shadyuhu l'guda* — and it was thrown onto the bank. *V'charuv mineh shitin machozei* — and this fish, falling ashore, destroyed sixty cities.

### Fish Utilization

*V'achalu mineh shitin machozei* — sixty other cities ate from its fresh flesh. *U'malchu mineh shitin machozei* — sixty more cities salted its preserved flesh for future consumption. *U'malu mi-chad galgala d'eineh tlat me'ah garbei mishcha* — from a single eye of this fish, three hundred barrels of oil were extracted. *V'chi hadran l'vatar treisar yarchei shata* — when Rabbah bar bar Chana returned to that place a year later, *chazinan d'havu mnasrei migarmei mtalalta* — they were sawing its bones into beams, *v'yatvi l'mivneinhu hanach machozei* — to use in rebuilding the sixty cities the fish had destroyed when it fell.

### Fish as Tzaddik

One interpretive tradition, offered in the *beit midrash*, identifies the fish with a *tzaddik* — a righteous person. God is exacting with the righteous even in small matters; the tiny parasite represents the minor transgression that can prove fatal to one who is otherwise great. The three sets of sixty cities — those destroyed, those that ate fresh, those that preserved — represent three different classes of people and three different responses to the death of a righteous individual: some are devastated, some draw immediate sustenance and meaning from his legacy, and some preserve and transmit that legacy to future generations.

## Fish Island Deception

### Island Mistake

*Va'amar Rabbah bar bar Chana* — Rabbah bar bar Chana said: *zimna chada hava ka azlinan b'sefinata v'chazinan ha-hu kavra d'yatba leh chala agabeih* — once I was on a boat, and I saw a fish on whose back sand had accumulated. *V'kadach agma ilaveih* — and reeds and grass were growing from the sand. *Savrinan yaveshta hi* — we concluded that this was a small island in the middle of the sea.

### Fish Overturns

*V'salkinan* — we boarded it. *V'afinan u'vashlinan agabeih* — we baked and cooked upon it, making a camp and a fire. *U'chad cham gabeih* — when the back of the fish began to heat up from our fire, *ithapich* — it overturned. *V'i lav d'hava mkarva sefinata hava tav'inan* — had our boat not been close by, we would have drowned.

### Wicked Nations

On one allegorical reading, the fish that appears as a hospitable island represents the wicked nations, who initially present themselves as friendly and welcoming. One settles in, makes oneself comfortable, builds a fire — and then the surface overturns, betrayal replaces welcome, and one is cast into the sea. The boat that saves the travelers represents divine assistance, the *Shechinah* that remains close even when the surrounding world proves treacherous.

## Fish Journey Duration

### Three Days Travel

*Va'amar Rabbah bar bar Chana*: *zimna chada havai azilna b'safinta*, once I was traveling on a boat, *vesagai safinta bein shitsa leshitsa dekivra derabbah* — and the boat was traveling between one fin and the other of a great fish — *tlata yomi vetlata leilvata* — for three days and three nights.

### Wind Dynamics

*Ihu bezikeifa veanan beshipula* — the fish was traveling against the wind, and we were traveling with the wind at our backs, meaning that the fish and the boat were moving in opposite directions, and our effective speed relative to the fish should have been the sum of both velocities. Even so, it took three days and three nights to traverse the length of the fish from fin to fin.

### Boat Speed

*Vechi teima lo masgya safinta tuva* — one might suggest that the boat simply was not very fast, and that is why the journey took so long. The *gemara* rejects this: *dechi ata Rav Dimi amar, kemeicham kumkuma demaya masgya shitin parsei* — when Rav Dimi came up from Bavel he reported that this boat traveled sixty *parsa* in the time it takes to boil a kettle — a matter of minutes. The boat was extraordinarily fast.

### Fish Speed

*Veshadi parasha geira dekadma lei* — and if a horseman were to shoot an arrow, the fish would outpace it. The fish was faster than an arrow shot at full gallop.

### Nations vs. Jews

An allegorical reading situates the fish and the boat as symbols of the nations of the world and the Jewish people respectively. The other nations dwell in their homelands like fish in water, at ease and moving with the current. The Jewish people, in exile, are always traveling against the wind — laboring against the grain of the surrounding world's power and momentum. And yet, supported by *Torah*, they travel with the wind behind them in a spiritual sense. The three days of the journey evoke the three days of fasting and repentance that mark transformative moments in Jewish experience.

### Fish Identity

*Va'amar Rav Ashi*: *hahi gildana de'yama havai* — that fish was identified as a *gildana*, a particular species. *De'it lei tartei shaitsei* — it is characterized by its two distinctive fins, which is why the boat could travel between them for three days.

## Giant Bird Vision

### Bird Proportions

*Va'amar Rabbah bar bar Chana*: *zimna chada havai azilna b'safinta vechazina hahi tsipra* — once I was on a boat and I saw a bird — *dekayim ad karsulei bemaya* — standing in the water up to its ankles — *verishei berakia* — with its head reaching up into the heavens.

### Shallow Assumption

*Ve'amina leika maya* — we concluded that the water must be shallow. This was a natural inference: if the bird was only ankle-deep, surely the water could not be very deep. *Uva'inan lemichat le'akorei nafshin* — and we prepared to go down into the water to cool ourselves off.

### Heavenly Warning

*Venafak bat kala ve'amar lan* — a heavenly voice went out and said to us: *lo teichatu hacha* — do not go down here. *Denafla lei chatsitsa levar nagra ha sheva shanim vlo kamatya ara* — a carpenter's axe fell into this water seven years ago and has not yet reached the bottom.

### Strong Current

*Vlo mishum dnefishi maya ela mishum dradifi maya* — it is not that the water itself is extraordinarily deep, but rather that the current is so powerful and fast-moving that it prevented the axe from sinking to the bottom. The danger is not depth alone but the ferocity of the current.

### Jewish People Symbol

One reading identifies the bird as a symbol of the Jewish people: *raglaim*, feet, planted in the world of physical reality, while the head — the aspiration, the *neshamah*, the intellectual and spiritual dimension — reaches toward the heavens. The warning against descending into the water cautions against going too deep into esoteric disciplines — *kabbalah* and *nistar* — that may appear accessible, like shallow water, but whose currents are in fact dangerous to those not prepared for them. What looks manageable to the masters of those disciplines appears easy only because of their mastery; to others, the current can overwhelm.

### Physical Pleasures Danger

An alternative reading of the same imagery understands the warning differently. The water that looks shallow and inviting represents the pleasures and indulgences of the surrounding nations. The Jewish people, seeing the nations at ease in their environment, may be tempted to think: it looks comfortable, it looks manageable, let us join them. The heavenly voice warns that what appears to be a gentle, shallow pool of tranquility is in fact turbulent and dangerous. The *shalva* — the apparent serenity — of the nations' way of life conceals currents that can sweep away everything.

## Fat Geese Story

### Geese Fattiness

*Va'amar Rabbah bar bar Chana*: *zimna chada hava ka azilna b'midbara* — once I was going through the wilderness — *v'chazina hanei avazi dishamti gadpaihu mishamnaihu* — and I saw geese whose feathers were falling off because of their extraordinary fatness. *V'kangadi nachalei d'mishcha mitutaiahu* — and rivers of fat were flowing beneath them, pressed out from their bodies against the ground.

### Future Benefit Query

### Geese Response

*Amina lehu* — I said to these geese: *it lan l'gavaichu chelka l'alma d'ati* — in the world to come, will we have a portion from you? Will we be able to eat you? *Chada dali gadpa* — one lifted its wing. *V'chada dali atma* — another lifted its leg, each apparently indicating that yes, one would be able to eat from it in the future.

### Accountability Statement

*Ki ata l'kamei d'Rabbi Elazar amar li: atidin Yisrael liten aleihem et hadin* — when Rabbah bar bar Chana came before Rabbi Elazar and told him this, Rabbi Elazar said: in the future, Israel will have to give an accounting for these geese.

### Messiah Delay

The *Rashbam* explains: *shebechatatam mitakev mashiach* — because of Israel's sins, the Messiah is being delayed. These geese were created for the Messianic feast, fattening themselves in anticipation of that day. But because the delay is prolonged, *yesh lahem tzaar baalei chayim l'otam avazim machmat shumnan* — the geese suffer greatly from their own corpulence, a form of *tzaar baalei chayim* — animal suffering — caused by their extraordinary fatness. Since Israel's failure to merit the redemption is what prolongs the geese's suffering, Israel will be held accountable for that suffering. The *Rashbam*'s comment brings to mind the contemporary practice of force-feeding geese for foie gras — a practice that causes comparable suffering — and underscores the moral seriousness with which the suffering of animals is treated in *halachic* thought.

## Arab Tracker Tale

### Smell Navigation

*Va'amar Rabbah bar bar Chana*: *zimna chada hava ka azilna b'midbara v'itlavai behadan hahu taya* — once I was traveling through the wilderness and a Bedouin traveler joined our company. *D'hava shakil afra v'morach leh* — he would take some sand, smell it, and then say: *ha orcha l'duchta palan v'ha orcha l'duchta palan* — this is the way to such-and-such place, and this is the way to such-and-such other place. He could navigate by smelling the desert sand alone.

### Testing Method

We asked him: *kama marchikan mimaya* — how far are we from water? *Hav li afra* — give me some sand. *Yavina lei* — we gave him sand, and he smelled it.

### Distance Accuracy

*Amar lan: tamni parsei* — he said: eight *parsa* away. We gave him sand again, and this time he said *tlasa parsei* — three *parsa*. *Afichta lei* — we reversed what we gave him, presenting him with sand from a different location to test him. *V'lo yachilna lei* — and we could not disprove him or catch him in error. Whatever test was applied, he met it accurately.

### Tracking Verified

The account concludes with the acknowledgment that trackers of this kind genuinely exist — people with extraordinary sensory attunement to their environment, capable of navigation by means that seem almost impossible to observers. Whatever the metaphorical resonances one might find in this tale, it is also simply a report of remarkable human skill, presented by Rabbah bar bar Chana as something he himself witnessed and could not refute.
