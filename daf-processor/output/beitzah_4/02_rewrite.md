# Beitzah 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Egg in Mixtures

### Egg Bitel Challenge

The daf begins by picking up at the bottom of 3b, continuing the discussion of a *brayta* concerning the status of an egg laid on *Yom Tov*. One of the rulings in that *brayta* was that in a case of doubt, the egg is forbidden. The *Gemara* challenges this on several grounds. If the underlying prohibition is rabbinic in nature — such as the concern about fruit falling from a tree or flowing liquids, which constitutes *muktzah* — then a doubt in a rabbinic matter should ordinarily be treated leniently. The *Gemara* therefore suggests that perhaps the *brayta* is addressing a case involving *trefah*, a biblical concern, rather than a purely rabbinic *Yom Tov* issue.

But this creates a further difficulty. The *brayta* also states that if such an egg becomes mixed up with a thousand others, all of them are forbidden. If the issue is *Yom Tov*-related, this is well understood: the principle of *dabar she'yesh lo matirin* — that something which will become permitted on its own need not and should not be permitted through other means — explains why the egg cannot be nullified through a mixture. You simply wait until tomorrow. But if the underlying issue is *trefah*, why would the egg not become *batel* when mixed with so many others?

The *Gemara* suggests that the egg might be considered a *dabar she'be'minyan* — a counted item — and as such retains its individual identity and cannot be subsumed into a mixture. However, this only holds if one applies the opinion that anything with a tendency to be sold by count (*kol she'darko limnot*) is never *batel*. If the narrower position is correct — that only items *exclusively* sold by count (*et she'darko limnot*) are exempt from *bitul* — then an egg, which is sometimes sold by volume or weight rather than individually, ought to become *batel*. The question therefore remains: why, on the *trefah* reading, is the egg not *batel*?

### Rav Pappa: Counted Items

*Rav Pappa* resolves this by identifying the author of the *brayta* in question. He says: *hai tanna tanna de'litra ketzi'ot hu* — the author of this *brayta* is the same *tanna* who authored the *brayta* of the *litra ketzi'ot*, the barrel of pressed figs. That source establishes the principle: *kol dabar she'be'minyan afilu de'rabbanan lo battel* — anything that is sometimes sold by count is never *batel*, even if the prohibition involved is only rabbinic. And *kal va'homer*, how much more so in a case of a biblical concern like *trefah*.

This resolves the difficulty. The reason the egg is not *batel* even in the *trefah* reading is not that eggs are exclusively sold by count — they are not — but because this particular *tanna* holds that even things *sometimes* sold by count are never *batel*, and this applies even to rabbinic-level prohibitions.

### Pressed Figs Brayta

The *brayta* of the *litra ketzi'ot* describes the following scenario. In ancient times, figs were pressed into large rounds or cakes and stacked within barrels (*chaviot*). Merchants would sell sections of these pressed-fig rounds by weight or volume. Now suppose one *litra* — a measured quantity — of such figs that are *terumah* was pressed into the top of one of many barrels, but you no longer know which barrel. The normal rule for *terumah* falling into *chulin* is that one requires a hundred times the amount of *terumah* to achieve *bitul*. But the question here is: what counts as "a hundred times"? Should you tally the total volume of figs across all barrels, or must you count specifically the tops of the barrels — since you know the *terumah* was pressed at the top, and the only genuine uncertainty is which barrel's top contains it?

A further nuance: *terumah* of fruit, as opposed to *terumah* of grain, wine, or oil (*dagan tirosh veyitzhar*), is only a rabbinic obligation. This bears on how stringently the requirement is applied. One might argue that, particularly for rabbinic *terumah*, leniency in calculating the required ratio is appropriate. The Rabbis in some contexts did go easy in precisely this way — not by waiving the requirement, but by permitting a creative interpretation of what satisfies it.

The *brayta* sets out this scenario in three parallel formulations: pressing figs into a barrel (*chavit*) without knowing which barrel; pressing them into a round container (*igul*) without knowing which one; and pressing them into a cylindrical vessel (*kiveret*) without knowing which. In all three cases, the essential question is the same: what quantity of permissible figs is required to render the *terumah batel*?

### Barrels Debate

*Rabbi Meir* in the *brayta* presents a debate between *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Yehoshua* on this question, though *Rabbi Yehuda* presents the same debate in a different formulation — and the two versions assign the positions in reverse.

According to *Rabbi Meir*'s version: *Rabbi Eliezer* takes a lenient approach, ruling *ro'in et ha'elyonot ke'ilu hen perudot* — we treat the tops of the barrels as if they are distinct, while *hatachtonot ma'alot et ha'elyonot* — the bottoms of the barrels can be counted together with the tops to create *bitul*. In other words, you need not find a hundred other barrel-tops; you may include the total volume of all the figs across the barrels, tops and bottoms alike, to achieve the hundredfold ratio. *Rabbi Yehoshua*, in this version, is more stringent: *im yesh me'ah pumim ya'alu* — only if there are a hundred other barrel-tops does *bitul* take effect. If not, the tops of all the barrels are forbidden but the bottoms are permitted.

According to *Rabbi Yehuda*'s version, the positions are shifted one degree of stringency. *Rabbi Eliezer* already holds the stricter view: you must have a hundred other tops. *Rabbi Yehoshua* goes even further, ruling: *afilu yesh sham shelosh me'ot pumim lo ya'alu* — even if there are three hundred other barrel-tops, *bitul* still does not apply.

The *Gemara* then notes a third case: if the *terumah* figs were pressed into a barrel but you do not know *where within* the barrel they are — north, south, top, bottom — then *divrei hakol ya'alu*, all agree that *bitul* works. This makes sense because in that scenario the uncertainty is not just which barrel-top, but where among all the figs in all the barrels the *terumah* may be, and it is genuinely distributed throughout. *Rav Pappa* clarifies that this is the correct reading of the final line of the *brayta*: "pressed into an *igul* without knowing where" means not knowing the position *within* the barrel, and in that case everyone agrees.

The significance of this entire discussion for our original question is now clear. *Rabbi Yehoshua* in *Rabbi Yehuda*'s version holds that even three hundred tops do not create *bitul*. Why? Because these pressed-fig rounds are sometimes sold by count — you might say "sell me one round of pressed figs." Since they are sometimes sold as individual units, this *tanna* holds that they never become *batel*, and this applies even to rabbinic-level *terumah*. This is precisely the opinion *Rav Pappa* identifies as the one underpinning our *brayta* about the egg. An egg, too, is sometimes sold by count. Therefore, according to this *tanna*, even a rabbinic concern — and certainly a biblical one like *trefah* — is never rendered *batel* when the item in question is sometimes counted.

The concept of *dabar she'be'minyan* can be understood intuitively: *bitul* operates by dissolving an item's individual identity into a larger whole. But an item that is sold and treated as a discrete, countable unit resists this dissolution. Each egg, like each person, has its own identity and refuses to become merely part of an aggregate. Because of this irreducible individuality, *bitul* simply cannot take hold.

This resolution, though logically coherent, is admittedly a somewhat strained reading of the *brayta*. It requires introducing the category of *trefah* into a *brayta* that appears on its face to be discussing a *Yom Tov* concern, and then invoking an expansive opinion about *dabar she'be'minyan* to explain why even that *trefah*-based concern would not be subject to *bitul*. Both steps carry some difficulty.

### Rav Ashi: Yom Tov Issue

*Rav Ashi* offers an alternative that is textually cleaner. He says: *le'olam safek Yom Tov safek chol* — the *brayta* is indeed discussing only the straightforward *Yom Tov* concern, exactly as it appears. There is no need to introduce *trefah* at all. The egg is *muktzah* due to the rabbinic concern about items that came into being on *Yom Tov* — fruit that drops from a tree, liquids that flow — and the underlying prohibition is rabbinic throughout.

The question then returns: why should a *safek* — a mere doubt as to whether the egg was laid on *Yom Tov* or on a weekday — be treated stringently? The ordinary rule is *safek de'rabbanan l'kula* — we are lenient in cases of doubt when the underlying prohibition is only rabbinic.

*Rav Ashi*'s answer: *havei davar she'yesh lo matirin afilu be'rabbanan lo batil*. The same principle that explains why the egg mixed into a thousand others is not *batel* — namely, that something which will become permitted on its own (*davar she'yesh lo matirin*) cannot be dissolved through *bitul* — applies equally to explain why a doubt about such an item does not invoke the leniency of *safek de'rabbanan*. Why invoke that principle of leniency when you can simply wait until tomorrow? The egg will be permitted then; use it then.

This is a striking extension of *davar she'yesh lo matirin*. Until now the principle was understood as applying specifically within the laws of *ta'aroves* — mixtures. *Rav Ashi* applies it to the entirely separate domain of how we adjudicate doubt in rabbinic matters. The practical implications are considerable. One prominent example: the question of *chadash* — consuming grain of uncertain vintage, possibly grown before the *omer* offering of the previous *Pesach*. Many medieval authorities permitted this by constructing a *safek sefeka*, a double doubt: perhaps the grain was from the prior year's harvest; and even if it was this year's, perhaps it took root before the *omer*. But *chadash* is precisely a *davar she'yesh lo matirin* — it becomes permitted after the second day of *Pesach* the following year. If *Rav Ashi*'s principle is applied beyond *ta'aroves* to the general logic of permitting something in the face of doubt, then the entire *safek sefeka* argument for *chadash* would be undermined: why rely on a constructed double doubt when you can simply wait until next year?

One might distinguish between a *safek sefeka* and a simple *safek de'rabbanan*, but the principle as articulated here has far-reaching potential. *Rav Ashi*'s read of the *brayta* is the cleaner one — everything remains within the framework of the *Yom Tov* egg, no detour through *trefah* is required — but the implications of extending *davar she'yesh lo matirin* into the logic of halakhic doubt are profound.

## Egg & Mother

### Rabbi Eliezer Brayta

The *Gemara* now introduces a *Tosefta*: *Acherim omrim mishum Rabbi Eliezer* — others say in the name of *Rabbi Eliezer*: *beitzah techal hi ve-imma* — the egg may be eaten, and so may its mother. The *Gemara* asks what the case must be. If the hen was designated for eating (*tarnegolet ha-omedet la-achilah*), then it should obviously be permitted — as *Rashi* notes, nobody debates the permissibility of the mother in such a case. Why would it need to be stated? The *Gemara* therefore considers the alternative: perhaps the *brayta* is discussing a hen designated for laying eggs. But in that case the hen would be *muktzah*, and stating that both may be eaten becomes puzzling in the opposite direction.

### Hen Sans Purpose

*Rav Zeira* explains: *tochal agav imah* — you may eat the egg on the basis of its mother. *Abaye* then specifies the case: *kegon she-lekachah stam* — a hen that was purchased without specifying its purpose. The buyer has not yet decided whether this hen will be slaughtered for food or kept for laying eggs.

This is precisely the case that resolves the difficulty. If the hen was set aside for eating, there is no *muktzah* issue — neither for the hen nor for the egg. If it was set aside for laying, both would be *muktzah*. But when no determination has been made, the *halacha* depends on what ultimately happens. If you slaughter it on *Yom Tov* — *huverar d'la-achilah omedet* — it is thereby retroactively clarified that this was a hen intended for food, and both the hen and the egg are permitted. If you do not slaughter it through *Yom Tov* — *lo nishcheta huverar d'l'beitzim omedet* — that itself indicates that the hen was being kept for eggs, rendering both hen and egg *muktzah*.

This seemingly incidental phrase — "and its mother" — thus leads the *Gemara* to address the case of *stam*, the undesignated hen, and the use of *breira*, retroactive clarification, to determine the egg's status. It is worth noting that one need not even invoke *breira* as a formal principle here; there is a reasonable argument that an item of uncertain *muktzah* status — *safek muktzah* — may itself be permitted, since *muktzah* requires a definitive designation. The *Gemara*, however, resolves it through the mechanism of retroactive determination via the fate of the mother.

### Guzma Interpretation

*Rav Mari* offers a simpler and more elegant explanation of the *brayta*: *guzma katani* — the *brayta* employs *guzma*, embellishment for emphasis. The *chiddush* of the teaching is exclusively about the egg. But to convey just how permitted the egg is, the *brayta* states in expansive terms: eat the egg, eat the mother, eat the chick, eat the shell. The mention of the mother, the chick, and the shell adds nothing legally; it is rhetorical amplification.

A second *brayta* is cited in support: *Acherim omrim mishum Rabbi Eliezer* — the same tradition of *Rabbi Eliezer* — states: *beitzatah te'achel, hi, ve-imah, ve-efrochah, ve-klipatah* — eat the egg, eat the mother, eat the chick, and eat the shell. *Gezuntaheit* — eat everything. Taken literally, this cannot be the intent, since no one eats an eggshell. The *Gemara* therefore considers whether *klipatah* might mean the chick *within* its shell — that is, an egg containing a live chick. But this too is untenable: there is a debate between *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* and the *rabbanan* about a chick that has emerged from the egg but not yet opened its eyes. That debate, the *Gemara* says, only applies once the chick has entered the world (*yatza l'avir ha-olam*). While the chick is still within the shell, all agree it is forbidden — it has the status of *eiver min ha-chai*, a limb from a living animal, since it remains part of the mother. Eating the chick-in-the-shell is therefore *assur* regardless of *Yom Tov*. The phrase cannot be taken as a *halakhic* ruling; it must be *guzma*. And the same applies to the earlier version: eat the egg, eat the mother — *guzma*, pure embellishment to underscore the permissibility of the egg.

What this *brayta* also reveals is that *Rabbi Eliezer* — who was, notably, of *Beis Shammai* — held the *Beis Shammai* position that the egg laid on *Yom Tov* is permitted, at least when the hen is designated for eating. The tradition is not an independent one; it is the *Beis Shammai* position transmitted in the name of one of its prominent adherents.

## Shabbat/Yom Tov Egg

### Shabbat-Yom Tov Egg

The *Gemara* now turns to a new *itmar*: the case of *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* falling back to back. *Rav* says: *nolda be-zeh asurah be-zeh* — if an egg is born on one of the two days, it remains forbidden on the other. *Rabbi Yochanan* says the opposite: *nolda be-zeh muteret be-zeh* — if born on one day, it is permitted on the following day.

### Kedusha Unity

One might suppose the debate turns on whether *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* are regarded as a single continuous *kedusha* or as two distinct *kedushos*. If they are one *kedusha*, then an egg born on either day is still within that single prohibited period when the other day arrives, and remains forbidden. If they are two *kedushos*, then on the second day the egg is treated as something that already existed beforehand, and there is no reason to prohibit it.

However, the *Gemara* immediately rejects this framing. *Rav* himself explicitly rules, in a different context, *halakha k'arba'a zekenim ve-aliba de-Rabbi Eliezer d'amar shtei kedushot hen* — the *halacha* follows the four elders according to *Rabbi Eliezer* who says *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* constitute two *kedushos*. *Rav* cannot, therefore, be forbidding the egg because he views them as one *kedusha*.

### Hachana Debate

The *Gemara* resolves: *hacha be-hachana de-Rabbah kamiflegi* — the dispute concerns *hachana de-Rabbah*, the principle introduced by *Rabbah* requiring that an item be "prepared" during a weekday to be usable on *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov*. Both sides agree it is *shtei kedushot*. The question is whether the fact that the egg's formation was completed on one sacred day — rather than on a weekday — renders it unavailable for the next sacred day. *Rav* holds that *hachana de-Rabbah* applies, and therefore an egg finished on *Yom Tov* or *Shabbat* is not considered "prepared" and cannot be used on the subsequent sacred day. *Rabbi Yochanan* does not accept *hachana de-Rabbah* as a binding concern, and therefore since the egg was in existence on the prior day, it may be used on the day that follows.

The *Gemara* anchors this debate in the *Tannaim*: one view holds that if the egg was born on *Shabbat*, it may be eaten on the following *Yom Tov*, and vice versa. *Rabbi Yehuda* in the name of *Rabbi Eliezer* says the debate of *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* remains active even in this case — *Beis Shammai* permitting and *Beis Hillel* forbidding. This *Tannaitic* debate is the precursor to the *Amoraic* dispute of *Rav* and *Rabbi Yochanan*, and the root of the disagreement, the *Gemara* confirms, is precisely *hachana de-Rabbah*.

### Rav Adda Story

The *Gemara* illustrates the practical stakes with a story. The proprietor of *Rav Adda bar Ahava*'s lodgings had eggs that were born on *Yom Tov* and wanted to know whether he could use them on the following *Shabbat*. More specifically, could he cook them on *Yom Tov* for consumption on *Shabbat*? The question of cooking on *Yom Tov* for the sake of the following *Shabbat* is itself a significant issue involving *Eruv Tavshilin*. But *Rav Adda bar Ahava* pointed out a prior difficulty: even according to *Rabbi Yochanan*, who permits using the egg on the following day, that permission is only to swallow it raw — *le-gomao le-machar*. Today on *Yom Tov*, when it was laid, it is *muktzah*, and you cannot handle it. The question of cooking for tomorrow therefore does not even arise: you cannot touch the egg today. Tomorrow — on *Shabbat* — it may be eaten, but only raw, since you cannot cook on *Shabbat*. The proprietor's assumption that he could cook it today in preparation for tomorrow rested on a misapplication of *Rabbi Yochanan*'s leniency.

The *Gemara* then cites an explicit *brayta* that supports this: *ein metaltelin osa* — neither an egg born on *Shabbat* nor one born on *Yom Tov* may be moved, neither to cover a vessel nor to prop up a bed-leg. This confirms that on the day it is laid, the egg is *muktzah* per *Beis Hillel*, even according to those who permit it the following day.

### Rav Pappa Story

A parallel story is told about *Rav Pappa*'s establishment. A man came to him with the inverse case: eggs born on *Shabbat*, and a question whether they could be eaten on the following *Yom Tov*. *Rav Pappa* declined to answer immediately, telling the man to return the next day. The reason, the *Gemara* explains, is that *Rav* would not appoint an *amora* to relay his rulings on the night of *Yom Tov* — *mishum shichrus*, because he had been drinking. This is not incidental: just as the *Torah*'s prohibition on priestly service while intoxicated connects the state of mind required for teaching *halacha* to the prohibition of *shikrus*, *Rav Pappa* knew that in the course of giving a *shiur* while drunk he might blurt out an unreliable *psak*. He therefore deferred.

When the man returned the next day, *Rav Pappa* said: it is fortunate I did not answer yesterday. Had I done so, he said, *isru hashta istalai* — I would have erred. He would have reflexively said *Rav u-Rabbi Yochanan halacha ke-Rabbi Yochanan* — we rule like *Rabbi Yochanan*, and therefore the egg born on *Shabbat* is permitted on the following *Yom Tov*. But upon sober reflection, he withdrew that answer.

### Rava's Ruling

*Rava* then establishes the binding rule: *hilchata kevusei de-Rav behani telasa* — the *halacha* follows *Rav* in these three matters, *bein lekula bein lechumra*, whether the ruling of *Rav* is the lenient or the stringent one. One of these three is the case of the egg and the juxtaposed days of *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*. The final *halacha* is therefore like *Rav*: an egg born on one of the two days remains forbidden on the other, due to *hachana de-Rabbah*.

## Fallen Wood

### Fallen Wood Rule

*Rabbi Yochanan* now introduces a case that is related but distinct. If branches fell from a tree on *Shabbat*, they may not be used as firewood on the following *Yom Tov* — *assur le-hatzikam be-Yom Tov*. He anticipates the obvious objection — "but by an egg you said it *is* permitted on the following day" — and preempts it: *al tachshiveini beitza*, do not compare this case to the egg.

### Egg vs. Wood

The distinction is fundamental and deserves careful attention. The egg was forbidden on *Shabbat* for one reason only: *muktzah*. On *Shabbat* itself the egg could theoretically have been swallowed raw; the only impediment was the *muktzah* prohibition. When we then say the egg is permitted on *Yom Tov*, we are conveying a clear and coherent message: *muktzah* is a day-specific prohibition, and *shtei kedushot* means the second day stands on its own. Anyone who hears that the egg — which could have been eaten yesterday — is permitted today will understand that *muktzah* ended with *Shabbat* and does not carry over. The two-day structure is internally transparent.

The wood, however, is different. A branch that has fallen from a tree on *Shabbat* has no permissible use on *Shabbat* whatsoever — you cannot burn wood on *Shabbat*. If we were then to say that this same branch may be used on the following *Yom Tov*, an observer would draw the wrong conclusion. He would reason: the branch was not used yesterday — but that was because there was nothing to do with a branch on *Shabbat*, not because of *muktzah*. If we allow it on *Yom Tov*, he will infer that fallen branches are not *muktzah* at all, and from there he will conclude that branches fallen on *Yom Tov* itself were already permissible. The experiential logic collapses the two days into one, precisely the inference we want to avoid. Permitting the wood on *Yom Tov* would thus create the false impression that *muktzah* never applied, undermining the entire framework.

This is a subtle but important distinction. It is not that the two cases involve different principles. Both involve *shtei kedushot* and *muktzah*. The difference is phenomenological: by the egg, the reason for non-use on *Shabbat* is transparent and understood; by the wood, it is not, and the inference drawn from permitting it on the following day would be structurally misleading. Notably, the same logic suggests — as one might observe — that if a branch falls on the first day of *Yom Tov*, it *could* be permissible on the second day of *Yom Tov*, since burning wood is something one can do on *Yom Tov*, and the parallel to the egg would then hold.

### Extra Wood Solution

*Rav Masna* addresses a practical scenario: what if wood falls from an overhanging tree directly into a lit oven on *Yom Tov*? In this case, *marbe lahem etzim muchanim umasiken* — one should add additional, pre-prepared permitted wood into the oven, creating a majority of permitted wood, and then use the fire. The *muktzah* wood is rendered *batel* within the larger quantity through *rov*, majority.

### Bitel Muktzah Query

The *Gemara* challenges this on two grounds. First: when you stir the fire, are you not inevitably stirring the *muktzah* wood as well, which constitutes moving *muktzah*? Second, and more fundamentally: is this not a case of *mevattel issur l'chatchila* — proactively causing the nullification of a forbidden item? We have a *mishnah* that states explicitly: *ein mevatlin issur l'chatchila* — one does not create *bitul* of a forbidden substance at the outset. *Bitul* is a *post facto* relief, not a deliberate strategy.

On the first question, the *Gemara* resolves that once a majority of the wood is permitted — *keivan de-ruba heteira ninhu* — when one stirs the fire one is considered to be stirring the permitted majority. The *muktzah* minority loses its legal significance within the whole.

### Bitel Resolution

On the second question — the prohibition against proactive *bitul* — the *Gemara* answers: *hani mili de'Oraisa*, that rule applies to biblical prohibitions. *Aval be'rabbanan mevatlinan* — but for a rabbinic prohibition, one may actively create *bitul*. Since *muktzah* is rabbinic, throwing in extra wood to achieve *bitul* is permissible.

A critical caveat must be noted here. This statement, taken at face value, would have sweeping implications: a large proportion of *kashrut* problems are rabbinic in nature, and if proactive *bitul* were universally permitted in rabbinic matters, *kashrut* practice would be transformed. Other *Gemaras* make clear that this is not the correct universal rule, and *poskim* throughout the generations have understood this passage as referring specifically to the weak form of rabbinic prohibition represented by *muktzah*, especially in this context where the wood is in the process of being actively consumed by fire. The *Gemara* itself shortly supplies a further reason: *mikla kali issura* — the prohibited item is in the process of being burned up and is losing its identity as it burns, such that treating it as something possessing ongoing status requiring standard *bitul* rules is itself inappropriate. The statement *aval be'rabbanan mevatlinan* should therefore not be generalized beyond the context that supports it.

The *Gemara* then raises a final complication. *Rav Ashi* had argued earlier in the daf that *davar she'yesh lo matirin* prevents *bitul* even in rabbinic matters. The *muktzah* wood is precisely a *davar she'yesh lo matirin* — it will become permitted tonight when *Yom Tov* ends. If that principle blocks *bitul* even for rabbinic concerns, how can we permit *bitul* of the wood? The *Gemara* answers: *hani mili heicha de-ise l'issura be'einah* — that rule applies when the prohibited object still exists in its full form. *Hacha mikla kali issura* — here, the wood is being actively burned and is in the process of losing its identity entirely. A branch that is being consumed by fire cannot meaningfully be described as something that "will become permitted tomorrow," because by tomorrow it will no longer exist. The principle of *davar she'yesh lo matirin* therefore does not apply to an object in the act of destruction, and *bitul* can take effect.
