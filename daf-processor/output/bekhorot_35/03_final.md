# Bekhorot 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bridegroom Tzara'as

### Yitaher Tenan Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: ״יִטְהַר״ תְּנַן, אוֹ ״וְיִטְהָר״ תְּנַן?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara analyzes Rabbi Eliezer's statement: When he will develop another leprous mark, he will become ritually pure [*yit'har*] from the first. **Rav Pappa asks:** What is the correct version of Rabbi Eliezer's statement? **Did we learn: *Yit'har*, or did we learn: *Veyit'har*,** and he will become ritually pure? *Yit'har* indicates that he will immediately be rendered ritually pure from the first leprous mark upon the appearance of the second mark. *Veyit'har*, by contrast, indicates that the punitive ritual impurity by rabbinic law imparted by the first mark remains until the second mark is deemed ritually pure.

The *daf* opens by completing the wrap-up discussion of *tzara'as* that began on 34b. *Ba'ei Rav Papa* — Rav Papa poses the question: *yitaher tenan o ve-yitaher tenan*? The *mishnah* rules that if a person cuts away a *nega* in order to prevent a *kohen* from declaring him *tamei*, he must wait until a second *nega* appears and runs its course, at which point he becomes *tahor* from the first as well. The question is how to read the text: does the *advent* of the second *nega* itself purify him from the first, or must he wait until he becomes *tahor* from the second before the first is resolved?

### Chosson Tzara'as

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? לְחָתָן שֶׁנִּרְאָה בּוֹ נֶגַע. דִּתְנַן: חָתָן שֶׁנִּרְאָה בּוֹ נֶגַע — נוֹתְנִין לוֹ שִׁבְעָה יָמִים, לוֹ וּלְאִצְטְלִיתוֹ וְלִכְסוּתוֹ, וְכֵן בָּרֶגֶל — נוֹתְנִין לוֹ כׇּל יְמֵי הָרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the** practical **difference** between the two versions? After all, he is in any case ritually impure due to the second mark until that mark is deemed pure. The Gemara explains that there is a difference in the case **of a bridegroom** who had removed a leprous mark **upon whom** a new **leprous mark appeared** during the seven days of his wedding celebrations. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Nega'im* 3:2): In the case of **a bridegroom upon whom a leprous mark appeared,** the court **grants him seven days** of his wedding celebrations before the mark is examined by a priest. This grace period is granted to him whether the mark appeared **on him,** i.e., his body, **or on his cloak, or on** any other **garment of his. And similarly,** with regard to any individual upon whom a leprous mark appeared **during a pilgrimage Festival,** the court **grants him all the days of the pilgrimage Festival** as a grace period, during which the mark is not examined by a priest.

At first glance the question seems academic — either way, as long as the second *nega* is present he remains *tamei*. The *Gemara* therefore asks *le-mai nafka mina*, and the answer lies in a specific case.

The practical difference involves a *chosson* who develops a *nega*. As the *mishnah* in *Nega'im* teaches: *chosson she-nira bo nega*, a bridegroom who shows signs of *tzara'as* is given seven days before any ruling is issued — *notnim lo shiva yamim* — out of respect for his *simcha*. This deferral applies not only to him personally but even to his garments and his house. Similarly, *ve-chen ba-regel notnim lo kol yemei ha-regel*: for any person, if the *nega* appears during *yom tov*, the ruling is postponed until after the full seven days of the festival.

### First vs. Second Spot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ ״יִטְהַר״ תְּנַן — מִקַּמַּיְיתָא טְהַר לֵיהּ, לְבָתְרָיְיתָא נָטְרִין לֵיהּ שִׁבְעָה יְמֵי מִשְׁתֶּה. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ: ״וְיִטְהַר״ תְּנַן — סוֹף סוֹף, כִּי לָא מְטַמֵּית לְבָתְרָיְיתָא, הָא מִיטַּמֵּא וְקָאֵי מִקַּמַּיְיתָא. מַאי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **If you say** that **we learned** that the correct reading of Rabbi Eliezer's statement is ***yit'har*,** i.e., the individual is rendered ritually pure upon the development of the new mark, this means that he is ritually pure during the seven-day period, as **he has** already **become ritually pure** of the punitive impurity **from the first** mark. As **for the latter** mark, **we wait the seven days of feasting for him,** before the mark is examined by a priest. He is therefore ritually pure in all regards. **But if you say** that **we learned** that the correct reading of Rabbi Eliezer's statement is ***veyit'har*,** and the punitive impurity of the first mark remains intact until the ritual purity of the second mark has been confirmed, the grace period has no effect. The reason is that **ultimately,** even if the individual **does not** yet **contract ritual impurity from the latter** mark, **he remains ritually impure on** account of **the first** mark until the purification of the latter mark. The Gemara asks: **What** is the resolution to Rav Pappa's query? Since there is no clear resolution, the Gemara concludes that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The question is posed cleanly: *ei amart yitaher tenan*, the second spot's arrival itself grants *tahor* status from the first — *mi-kamaisa tihar lei*, and we then defer the second under *le-basraisa natrinan lei sheva yemei mishta*, giving the *chosson* a genuine period of *taharah*. *Ella ei amart ve-yitaher tenan*, then even without the *kohen* ruling on the second, he remains *tamei* from the first until the second is fully purified — *sof sof ke-ilu metamei lei le-basraisa, ha metamei ve-kaimi mi-kamaisa* — and the wedding week provides no relief. The *Gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teiku*.

## Kansu Beno Acharav

### Son Fined Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה מֵרַבִּי זֵירָא: צוֹרֵם אוֹזֶן בַּבְּכוֹר וָמֵת, מַהוּ לִקְנוֹס בְּנוֹ אַחֲרָיו?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that one who slits the ear of a firstborn offering is prohibited indefinitely from slaughtering the animal on account of that blemish. **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma before Rabbi Zeira:** In the case of **one who slits the ear of a firstborn** offering **and** subsequently **dies, what is** the *halakha* **with regard to penalizing his son after** the father's death? Does the penalty prohibiting consumption of the firstborn offering extend to the son?

With the *tzara'as* discussion concluded, the *Gemara* returns to the central topic of *bechor*: the rabbinic fine imposed on one who intentionally inflicts a *mum* on a *bechor*. *Ba'ei minei Rabbi Yirmiyah me-Rabbi Zeira* — Rabbi Yirmiyah poses the question to Rabbi Zeira: *tzorem ozen be-bechor u-meis* — if a person punctures the ear of a *bechor* and then dies, *mahu liknos beno acharav*? Will the son be fined as well?

### Servant & Field Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר: מוֹכֵר עַבְדּוֹ לַגּוֹיִם וָמֵת — קָנְסוּ בְּנוֹ אַחֲרָיו, דְּכֹל יוֹמָא וְיוֹמָא מַפְקַע לֵיהּ מִמִּצְווֹת. אִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר: כִּוֵּן מְלַאכְתּוֹ בַּמּוֹעֵד וָמֵת — לֹא קָנְסוּ בְּנוֹ אַחֲרָיו, מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא עַבְדֵהּ לְאִיסּוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara compares this dilemma to two similar cases. **If you say** the *halakha* should be derived from the case of **one who sells his** Canaanite **slave to gentiles and** the seller subsequently **dies,** where the Sages **penalized his son after** the father's death and required him to redeem the slave, this is not a direct parallel. The Gemara explains: It is possible that the penalty is extended to the son only there, **as each day** that the slave is in the servitude of the gentile master, that master **prevents** the slave **from** performing **mitzvot.** A Canaanite slave is obligated to perform the same mitzvot as a Jewish woman, and he is unable to fulfill the mitzvot when in the possession of a gentile. Since this reason does not apply in a case of a firstborn offering, the case of the Canaanite slave cannot be cited as a source to resolve this dilemma. And **if you say** the *halakha* should be derived from the case of **one who planned** from the outset to perform **his labor on the** intermediate days of **a Festival,** by placing himself in a situation in which the labor should in theory be permitted, i.e., that a significant monetary loss would otherwise be incurred, that comparison is also inaccurate. The Gemara elaborates: In such a case the Sages penalized him by deeming it prohibited for him to perform the labor and by removing his rights to any of the finished products, but if **he died** before the start of the Festival, **they did not penalize his son after** the father's death. This case is not a direct parallel either, as it is possible that it is only there that the penalty is not extended to the son, **since** the father **did not** yet **perform a prohibited act,** as he did not carry out any action on the Festival before he died.

The ear is the paradigmatic example of an intentionally inflicted *mum* — it does not heal, and we will see shortly why it features so prominently in the *mishnah*. The underlying tension in the question is genuine: if the fine is understood primarily as a *knas*, a punishment directed at the transgressor, then once that person is dead, the rationale for the fine evaporates. On the other hand, one might argue that the purpose is societal — to create a disincentive for anyone who might contemplate deathbed manipulation, ensuring that heirs cannot benefit either. The *Gemara* approaches the question by comparing it to several parallel cases.

### Abaye's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא מַאי? לְדִידֵיהּ קְנַסוּ רַבָּנַן, וְהָא לֵיתֵיהּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא לְמָמוֹנֵיהּ קְנַסוּ רַבָּנַן, וְהָא אִיתֵיהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תְּנֵיתוּהָ, שָׂדֶה שֶׁנִּתְקַוְּוצָה בִּשְׁבִיעִית — תִּזָּרַע לְמוֹצָאֵי שְׁבִיעִית, נִיטַּיְּיבָה נִידַּיְּירָה — לֹא תִּזָּרַע לְמוֹצָאֵי שְׁבִיעִית. וְאָמַר רַב אַסִּי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: נָקְטִינַן, הֱטִיבָהּ וָמֵת — בְּנוֹ זוֹרְעָהּ. אַלְמָא לְדִידֵיהּ קְנַסוּ רַבָּנַן, לִבְרֵיהּ לָא קְנַסוּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** If so, **here,** in the case of mutilating the ear of a firstborn offering, **what** is the *halakha*? Should one say that **the Sages penalized** only **him,** i.e., the father, **and he is no longer** alive? **Or perhaps the Sages imposed** the **penalty upon his property,** i.e., that no benefit may be derived from the animal, **and that** animal still **exists** in the possession of his heirs? Rabbi Zeira **said to** Rabbi Yirmeya: **You learned** the resolution to your dilemma in a mishna (*Shevi'it* 4:2). **A field whose thorns were removed during the Sabbatical** Year **may be sown at the conclusion of the Sabbatical** Year, as removing thorns is not full-fledged labor that renders the field prohibited. But if **it had been improved** with fertilizer, or if **it had been enclosed** so that the animals inside would fertilize it with their manure, **it may not be sown at the conclusion of the Sabbatical** Year, as both these acts cause significant improvements to the field. The Sages imposed a penalty that one may not derive benefit from prohibited labor. **And Rav Asi, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: We have a tradition** that if one **improved** his field in a prohibited manner **and** then **died, his son may sow it. Apparently, the Sages penalized** only **him,** i.e., the father, but **the Sages did not penalize his son.** **Abaye says: We have a tradition**

The *Gemara* resolves the question by citing a *mishnah* in the laws of *shemita*. *Sadeh she-nitnaksha ba-shevi'it* — a field from which one cleared thorns and thistles on *shemita* without actually working the soil may still be planted after *shemita*. But one who manured or penned animals in the field on *shemita* — thereby genuinely improving it — is fined and may not plant it after *shemita* either. *Ve-amar Rabbi Yossi bar Chanina*: *hetiva u-meis, beno zor'ah* — if the father fertilized the field and died, the son *may* plant it. *Alma le-didei kansu rabanan, livrei lo kansu rabanan* — we fine the person, not the son.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טִימֵּא טְהָרוֹת וָמֵת — לֹא קָנְסוּ בְּנוֹ אַחֲרָיו. מַאי טַעְמָא? הֶיזֵּק שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִיכָּר לָא שְׁמֵיהּ הֶיזֵּק, קְנָסָא דְּרַבָּנַן — לְדִידֵיהּ קְנַסוּ רַבָּנַן, לִבְרֵיהּ לָא קְנַסוּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** that if **one rendered impure** the **ritually pure items** of another **and died** before paying, the Sages **did not penalize his son after his** death and require him to pay for the damage. **What is the reason** for this? The reason is that **damage that is not evident,** i.e., which does not involve any visible change, **is not considered damage** by Torah law. There is **a penalty** imposed **by rabbinic law,** as the injured party suffered a loss, but **the Sages penalized** only **him; the Sages did not penalize his son.**

*Amar Abaye* anticipates the objection: the field case may not be fully analogous to the *bechor*, because by the field the fine is action-directed — we are telling the transgressor not to do this. By the *bechor*, one could argue more formalistically that the rabbis made a declaration about the object itself — the *mum* is treated as non-existent — which would naturally extend to the heir. Abaye does not press the distinction but rather embraces the comparison, and the *Gemara* treats the principle of *le-didei kansu rabanan, livrei lo kansu rabanan* as the operative rule across cases.

### Terumah Case

The *Gemara* extends the principle. *Tima taharotav u-meis* — here is the case discussed in *Gittin*: a person intentionally causes someone else's *terumah* to become *tamei* by casting a *sharetz* upon it, rendering it worthless. Ordinarily no payment is collected for such damage because *hezek she-eino nikar lo shmei hezek* — damage that is not visually apparent does not constitute actionable injury under biblical law. Yet if done intentionally, *Chazal* imposed liability. If the perpetrator dies, must his son pay? The *Gemara* cites this principle of *le-didei kansu rabanan, livrei lo kansu rabanan* — the Sages fined the person, not the son. One might ask whether policy considerations should override this conclusion — after all, a real person has suffered a real loss, and allowing the debt to die with the transgressor seems like an obvious loophole. The *Gemara* here is consistent in its approach: it categorizes these cases as *knas* and applies the general rule, leaving the harder policy questions to the side. The real analytical challenge — noted but not fully resolved here — is determining which situations qualify as *knas* in the first place, as opposed to *takanos* or corrections of substantive law, since that threshold determination governs everything that follows. The *sugya* in *Tosafot* and other *masechtos* develops this question further; for our purposes, the *Gemara* has established the principle firmly.

## Intentional Blemish Cases

### Quaestor Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מַעֲשֶׂה בְּזָכָר שֶׁל רְחֵלִים זָקֵן, וּשְׂעָרוֹ מְדוּלְדָּל, וְרָאָהוּ קַסְטוֹר אֶחָד וְאָמַר: מָה טִיבוֹ שֶׁל זֶה? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: בְּכוֹר הוּא, וְאֵינוֹ נִשְׁחָט אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הָיָה בּוֹ מוּם. נָטַל (פיגום) [פִּיגְיוֹם] וְצָרַם אׇזְנוֹ, וּבָא מַעֲשֶׂה לִפְנֵי חֲכָמִים וְהִתִּירוּ, וְאַחַר שֶׁהִתִּירוּ הָלַךְ וְצֵירֵם בְּאׇזְנֵי בְכוֹרוֹת אֲחֵרִים וְאָסְרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: There was **an incident involving an old ram whose hair was** long and **dangling,** because it was a firstborn offering. **And one** Roman **quaestor [*kastor*] saw it and said** to its owner: **What is the status [*tivo*] of this** animal that you allowed it to grow old and you did not slaughter it? **They said to him:** It **is a firstborn** offering, **and** therefore **it may be slaughtered only if it has a blemish.** The quaestor **took a dagger [*pigom*] and slit its ear. And the incident came before** the **Sages** for a ruling, **and they deemed** its slaughter **permitted. And after** the Sages **deemed** its slaughter **permitted,** the quaestor **went and slit the ears of other firstborn** offerings, **but** in these cases the Sages **deemed** their slaughter **prohibited,** despite the fact that they were now blemished.

The *Gemara* now returns to the question of precisely what *le-da'ato* — intentionality — means when it comes to inflicting a *mum* on a *bechor*, and introduces a remarkable *mishnah*. *Maaseh be-zachar she-peruchav zekenim*: there was an incident involving a very old ram whose wool had never been sheared — as a *bechor* without a *mum* it could not be shorn — and was accordingly trailing and scraggly. *Ve-ra'ahu kastor echad*: a certain Roman official, a *quaestor* (a revenue and legal officer), encountered it and asked what was going on with this animal. The people told him, *bechor hu ve-eino nishchat ela im ken hayah bo mum* — it is a firstborn, and it cannot be *shechted* unless it has a blemish. The official then took a *pigum* — a pugio, a double-bladed Roman dagger — and *tzaram be-ozno*, punctured its ear. *U-va ma'aseh lifnei chachamim va-hitiru*: the case came before the Sages and they declared it permitted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פַּעַם אַחַת הָיוּ תִּינוֹקוֹת מְשַׂחֲקִין בַּשָּׂדֶה, וְקָשְׁרוּ זַנְבֵי טְלָאִים זֶה בָּזֶה, וְנִפְסְקָה זְנָבוֹ שֶׁל אֶחָד מֵהֶם, וַהֲרֵי הוּא בְּכוֹר, וּבָא מַעֲשֶׂה לִפְנֵי חֲכָמִים וְהִתִּירוּ. רָאוּ שֶׁהִתִּירוּ, הָלְכוּ וְקָשְׁרוּ זַנְבוֹת בְּכוֹרוֹת אֲחֵרִים, וְאָסְרוּ. זֶה הַכְּלָל: כֹּל שֶׁהוּא לְדַעְתּוֹ — אָסוּר, שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעְתּוֹ — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **One time children were playing in the field and they tied the tails of lambs to each other, and the tail of one of them was severed, and it was a firstborn** offering. **And the incident came before the Sages** for a ruling **and they deemed** its slaughter **permitted.** The people who **saw that they deemed** its slaughter **permitted went and tied the tails of other firstborn** offerings, **and** the Sages **deemed** their slaughter **prohibited. This** is **the principle:** With regard to **any** blemish **that is** caused **intentionally,** the animal's slaughter is **prohibited;** if the blemish is caused **unintentionally,** the animal's slaughter is **permitted.**

It is worth noting that the *pigum* — the instrument of piercing — lends its name to the paradigmatic act of inflicting a *mum* on a *bechor*: *pogem ozen shel bechor*. Whether the term derives from this story or simply reflects the ease with which an ear wound is inflicted and yet never heals is an interesting question in itself.

The first ruling — that the animal is *mutar* — seems straightforward if one frames it as: the non-Jew acted on his own initiative, nobody commissioned him, and therefore there is no *le-da'ato* on the part of the Israelite owner. The difficulty is that the non-Jew plainly understood what he was doing and why; he was acting deliberately to benefit the Jewish owner. *Rashi* attempts to address this by arguing that at the time the non-Jew acted he did not yet know that the *chachamim* would actually permit the *bechor* — he was, in Rashi's reading, uncertain about the legal effect of his action. This is admittedly difficult: why would he do it if he didn't believe it would help?

*Ve-achar she-hitiru, halach ve-tzaram be-oznei bechorot acherim* — after hearing the ruling, the quaestor went and started puncturing the ears of other people's *bechorot*. *Va-asru* — and then the Sages forbade it. Taken at face value, one might read the reversal as a policy measure: once a single permissive ruling leads to systematic exploitation, the policy itself must be retracted. But *Rashi* and the *Gemara* read it differently: the first act, whatever degree of awareness it involved, did not constitute an act performed with the specific knowledge that it would render the *bechor* permitted. Once the non-Jew heard the ruling and knew with certainty, each subsequent act was performed *le-da'ato* — with full knowledge of the legal effect — and therefore forbidden. The *Gemara* does not read this as a blanket prospective prohibition on all future cases of the same type; it is specifically the post-ruling acts that are forbidden because of the actor's now-certain intent.

The tension is real. If even the first act was being performed to benefit the Jewish owner, it is somewhat difficult to describe it as *shelo le-da'ato*. One way to resolve this — though the *Gemara* does not state it explicitly — is to distinguish between doing something that *benefits* another and doing something *at another's behest*. The first time the non-Jew acted, he was doing the Jew a favor; the Jew had not prompted him, had not hinted at it, had not expressed desire that it be done. After hearing the ruling, the non-Jew was effectively acting as the Jew's *shaliach*, and at that point the Jew's implicit desire becomes the operative factor.

### Children Tail Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ פַּעַם אַחֶרֶת הָיָה כּוּ׳. וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן גּוֹי — דְּלָא אָתֵי לְמִיסְרַךְ, אֲבָל קָטָן דְּאָתֵי לְמִיסְרַךְ — אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna mentions that **one time** children **were** involved in a case in which they unintentionally blemished a firstborn offering whose subsequent slaughter the Sages deemed permitted. The Gemara notes: **And** although the first clause of the mishna mentions a similar occurrence, the incident involving the children is **necessary. As, had** the *tanna* **taught us** only the incident involving the **gentile,** it might have been thought that the Sages permitted the slaughter of the firstborn offering only there, **as** it is **not** a problem if the gentile **will acquire the habit** of causing blemishes, because that prohibition does not apply to gentiles. **But** with regard to a Jewish **minor,** it is a concern **that he** is likely **to acquire the habit** of causing blemishes in firstborn offerings, and therefore one might **say** that even the first, unintentional time should **not** be permitted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמוֹעִינַן קָטָן, מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אָתֵי לְאִיחַלּוֹפֵי בְּגָדוֹל, אֲבָל גּוֹי דְּאָתֵי לְאִיחַלּוֹפֵי בְּגָדוֹל — אֵימָא לָא. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And had** the *tanna* **taught us** only the incident involving **a minor,** it might have been thought that the Sages deemed the firstborn offering permitted only there, **since** there is **no** concern that people **will come to confuse** a minor **with an adult.** People would not erroneously conclude that it is permitted to cause a blemish on a firstborn offering intentionally merely due to an incident involving a minor. **But** in the case of **a gentile** adult, where this is a concern **that** people would **come to confuse** him **with** a Jewish **adult,** one might **say** that even the first, unintentional time should **not** be permitted. It is therefore **necessary** for the mishna to teach both cases.

A parallel incident is then cited: *pa'am achat hayu tinokot mesachakim ba-sadeh ve-kashru zinvei telaim zeh ba-zeh* — some children were playing in a field and tied the tails of sheep together, *va-nifsaka zanavo shel echad me-hem* — and one animal's tail was pulled off. The animal turned out to be a *bechor*. *Ba ma'aseh lifnei chachamim va-hitiru* — the case came before the Sages and they permitted it, since the act was entirely unintentional. But *ra'u she-hitiru ve-holchu ve-kashru zinvos bechorot acheirim* — once they saw the first case had been permitted, they went and tied other *bechorot'* tails together, *va-asru*.

The same interpretive question arises: is this a prospective policy reversal, or is it only the second set of acts that are forbidden because the children now knew what effect their action would have? The *Gemara* adopts the latter reading, which sets up the concluding principle of the *mishnah*.

Both cases are needed — *tzericha*. Had the *Gemara* cited only the non-Jew's case, one might have thought that the first act was innocuous precisely because there was no concern it would be repeated: a freak occurrence by a Roman official would not lead to Israelites following suit. The novelty of the children's case is that allowing children to do something will very plausibly lead to repetition — *de-ati le-misrach* — because children, once told something is permitted, will do it again. On that logic, one might have argued the first case involving children should never have been permitted. Conversely, had the *Gemara* cited only the children's case, one might have thought Israelites would not follow the precedent of a non-Jew — *de-lo ati le-achlufi be-gadol* — so that non-Jewish acts would always remain in a category apart. *Tzericha*: you need both.

The *Gemara* also notes that the concern about children acting again carries a *chinuch* dimension — it is an *aveira* for a Jewish child to do this, and we do not want to habituate children to such behavior. No corresponding *aveira* concern applies to the non-Jew, which is why the initial permissive ruling presented no *chinuch* problem in that case.

### Intentionality Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל: כֹּל שֶׁהוּא לְדַעְתּוֹ אָסוּר, שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעְתּוֹ מוּתָּר. לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵיתוֹיֵי גְּרָמָא. שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת — לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מֵסִיחַ לְפִי תוּמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that **this** is **the principle:** In the case of **any** blemish **that is** caused **with** his **intent,** the animal's slaughter is **prohibited.** The Gemara explains: **What** does this principle serve **to add?** It serves **to add an indirect** action, i.e., a blemish caused in this manner is also considered an intentional act and the animal may not be slaughtered on its account. The Gemara further inquires: What is added by the phrase in the second part of the principle: If the blemish was caused **without** his **intent,** the animal's slaughter is permitted? This serves **to add** an instance where a gentile did not inquire about the nature of the firstborn offering, but rather discovered it from one who **speaks offhandedly.**

*Zeh ha-klal: kol she-hu le-da'ato asur, shelo le-da'ato mutar* — this is the governing principle: done with awareness of the legal effect, forbidden; done without such awareness, permitted.

The *Gemara* asks what the principle comes to include beyond the stated cases. *Le-atuyei grama*: it includes even indirect causation. As the *Gemara* discussed on the previous *daf*, even if one creates the conditions for a *mum* to occur without directly inflicting it — the small *kre'ach-lach* made for dogs, for instance — if this was done intentionally, not only is the act itself prohibited but the resulting *mum* does not permit *shechita*. Conversely, *shelo le-da'ato mutar* includes even *masiach le-pi tumo* — speaking casually and offhandedly. If a Jew mentions to a friend, without directing his words at any non-Jew, that the animal has no *mum* yet, and a non-Jew overhearing the remark spontaneously acts — that too would be permitted, even though the earlier case of a direct response to a non-Jew was already permitted.

What is striking about this entire *sugya*, taken together, is the degree to which it points toward *policy* considerations rather than a pure *knas* framework. The concern with children repeating the act, the concern with non-Jews acting *as an agent* for the Jew, the focus on whether the Jew's intent to benefit is operative — all of this fits more naturally within a framework of protecting the institution of *bechor* from being systematically undermined. Yet the *Gemara* continues to frame the analysis in terms of *knas*, and that framing creates genuine tension with the conclusions it reaches.

### Kicking Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָיָה בְּכוֹר רֹדְפוֹ, בְּעָטוֹ, וְעָשָׂה בּוֹ מוּם — הֲרֵי זֶה שׁוֹחֲטִין עָלָיו. גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁבְּעָטוֹ בִּשְׁעַת רְדִיפָה, אֲבָל לְאַחַר רְדִיפָה — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one's **firstborn** offering **was pursuing him,** and **he kicked** the animal **and caused a blemish in it, he may slaughter** the animal **on** account of **that** blemish. GEMARA: **Rav Pappa says: They taught** that the firstborn offering may be slaughtered **only** in an instance **where he kicked it at the time of its pursuit. But** if the individual kicked the animal **after** its **pursuit,** it may **not** be slaughtered, as he intended to cause a blemish in order to render it permitted to be slaughtered.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: מוּתָּר לְהַטִּיל מוּם בִּבְכוֹר קוֹדֶם שֶׁיָּצָא לַאֲוִיר הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda says: It is permitted to cause a blemish in a firstborn** fetus **before it emerges into the air of the world,** as it obtains its sacred status only when it is born.

Rav Chisda, citing Rav Katina, adds an important clarification about the case of a direct verbal response to a non-Jew: *lo shanu ela de-amar lei* — the first case is permitted only when the Israelite responded by saying the animal cannot be *shechted* unless it *has* a *mum*, using language that describes the existing condition. *Aval im amar lei ye'aseh bo mum* — but if the Israelite said "unless a *mum* is *made* in it," using the passive but clearly implying human agency, *ke-man de-amar lei zil avid bei mum dami* — it is as though he said to the non-Jew: go make a *mum*. In that case it would be forbidden even the first time.

*Amar Rava*: this distinction is rejected. *Mimela hu* — the passive voice is not an instruction; *na'aseh* can simply mean something that happens on its own, just as *hayah bo mum* describes a *mum* that exists. If the Israelite said "unless a *mum* comes to be in it," that is not a directive. Rava insists that only an unambiguous explicit instruction crosses the line.

The analogy to *amira le-akum* on *Shabbat* is apt here. On *Shabbat*, a Jew who describes his situation — "how dark it is in this room" — has not issued an *amira*; a Jew who says "it would be wonderful if someone turned on the light" has crossed into *remez* territory. The same distinction operates here. A passive construction describing a condition is not an *amira*; only language that functionally directs the non-Jew's action would constitute one.

The *mishnah* then addresses a case on the opposite end of the spectrum. *Haya bechor rodfo* — if a *bechor* ram was chasing a person, *be-ato ve-asah bo mum* — and the person kicked it defensively and in so doing created a *mum*, *harei zeh yishachet alav*: the animal may be *shechted*. *Amar Rav Papa: lo shanu ela she-be'at be-sha'at redifa* — this applies only if the kick occurred during the chase itself. *Aval achar redifa* — if after the ram stopped pursuing him the person kicked it out of residual anger, *assur*. The *Gemara* presents two formulations of this ruling. One version holds the post-chase kick is clearly problematic — one might argue the person was simply venting *tzaraosav*, remembering his suffering, rather than acting to create a *mum*, but *kah mashma lan* that we are still concerned his intent was to inflict a *mum*. The other version is that even a post-chase kick is permitted — *afilu achar redifa nami* — because *tzayreh hu de-madkar*: he is acting out of remembered pain and anguish, not to create a *mum*, and we are not concerned.

## Pre-Birth & Visibility

### Pre-Birth Blemish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: גַּדְיָא בְּאוּדְנֵיהּ, אִימְּרָא בְּשִׂפְוָותֵיהּ. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אִימְּרָא נָמֵי בְּאוּדְנֵיהּ, אֵימוֹר דֶּרֶךְ צְדָעָיו נְפַק.
>
> **Translation:** Applying this ruling, **Rava said:** In the case of **a kid,** it is practical to cause a blemish **on its ear.** Since a kid's ears are long they emerge from the birth canal prior to the head. With regard to **a lamb,** whose ears are short and do not appear before the head, it is practical to cause a blemish only **on its lip. There are** those **who say** that there is a different version of this statement: In the case of **a lamb as well,** it is practical to cause a blemish **on its ear,** as one can **say** that **it emerges** from the birth canal **through its temples,** in which case its ears are visible first.

*Amar Rav Yehuda: muttar le-hatil mum be-bechor kodem she-yetze le-avir ha-olam* — it is permitted to inflict a *mum* on a *bechor* before it has fully emerged into the world. This is another practical solution to the difficulty of managing a *bechor*, parallel to the early-period strategy noted by the *Rishonim* and later codified in the *Shulchan Aruch*. The idea here is that while the animal is still partially within the womb — before the majority of its head has emerged and it is considered halachically born — any blemish inflicted is not a prohibited act.

### Goat Ears, Sheep Lips

The practical application depends on the species. For a *gadya* — a goat — one works with the *oznei*, the ears, since goats have long ears that protrude well before the majority of the head emerges. For an *imra* — a sheep — the ears are shorter, so one uses *bi-sfatei*, the lips, which similarly emerge before the head is fully born. A cut to the lip will not heal and constitutes a permanent *mum*. An alternate tradition — *ika de-amri* — holds that even a sheep's ear may be used if the animal happens to emerge sideways, *derech tzad nafak*, with the ears preceding the head. *Tosafot* reads *eima* here not as a permissive "one could say" but as a definitive description: if the birth happens to be sideways, the ears indeed come out first and may be used.

### Visible When Bleating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אָכֵל וְלָא מִיחֲזֵי, פָּעֵי וּמִיחֲזֵי — הָוֵי מוּמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** In a case where a firstborn has a blemish in its mouth, if, when it **eats,** the blemish **is not visible,** but when it opens its mouth wide and **cries out, it is visible,** this **is** considered **a blemish,** rendering the animal fit for slaughter.

*Amar Rava*: if an animal has a blemish on the inside of its mouth that is not visible while it eats but becomes visible *pa'i* — when it bleats — *u-machzi*, and can be seen from the outside, *havei muma* — this constitutes a valid *mum*. The visibility standard for a *mum* is that it must be externally observable; the question is what counts as external when the blemish is technically internal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: רָבָא נָמֵי טַעְמָא דְמַתְנִיתִין מְפָרֵשׁ, מַאי טַעְמָא נֶעְקְרוּ הָוֵי מוּמָא — מִשּׁוּם דְּכִי פָּעֵי מִיחְזֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says** in response: **Rava** is not in fact teaching a novel *halakha*. Rather, he **is explaining the reason** for the ruling **of the mishna: What is the reason** that if the internal gums **were extracted it is** considered **a blemish?** It is **that when** the animal opens its mouth wide and **cries out,** the blemish **is visible.**

*Rav Papa* responds that Rava is not introducing a new principle but explaining the *Gemara* — that is, providing the *peshat* of the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* lists among valid *mumim* the case of *ha-chitei ha-chitzonot she-nifgemu* — outer incisors that have been chipped — and *ha-penimiyot she-ne'ekru* — inner molars that have been completely extracted. The question is why extracted inner molars, which cannot be seen normally, count as externally visible. *Mai ta'ama de-ne'ekru havei muma? Mishum de-chi pa'i machzi* — because when the animal bleats, the absence of those molars becomes visible. That defines the outer visibility threshold: a blemish seen when the animal opens its mouth to bleat is considered sufficiently external to qualify.

## Witness Credibility

### Mishnah Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַמּוּמִין הָרְאוּיִין לָבֹא בִּידֵי אָדָם, רוֹעֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל נֶאֱמָנִים, רוֹעִים כֹּהֲנִים אֵינָן נֶאֱמָנִים. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמָן הוּא עַל שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, וְאֵין נֶאֱמָן עַל שֶׁל עַצְמוֹ. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: הֶחָשׁוּד עַל הַדֶּבֶר, לֹא דָּנוֹ וְלֹא מְעִידוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **all the blemishes that are capable of being brought about by a person, Israelite shepherds are deemed credible** to testify that the blemishes were not caused intentionally. But **priest-shepherds are not deemed credible,** as they are the beneficiaries if the firstborn is blemished. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** A priest is **deemed credible** to testify **about** the firstborn **of another, but is not deemed credible** to testify **about** the firstborn belonging **to him. Rabbi Meir says:** A priest **who is suspect about the matter** of causing a blemish may **neither adjudicate nor testify** in cases involving that matter, even on behalf of another.

The *Gemara* now pivots to the equally consequential question of credibility: who may testify that a *mum* on a *bechor* occurred naturally and was not intentionally inflicted? The *mishnah* states: *kol ha-mumim ha-re'uyim lavo bi-dei adam* — for any blemish that could have been humanly inflicted, credibility is required. Blemishes that obviously could not have been produced by human agency — a supernumerary limb, congenital blindness, or the like — require no such testimony. For the rest: *ro'ei Yisrael ne'emanim, ro'ei Kohanim einam ne'emanim* — Israelite *ro'im* are believed, *kohanim ro'im* are not.

The *mishnah*'s formulation is itself ambiguous. *Ro'ei Yisrael* in construct form (*smichut*) means "shepherds of Israelites" — describing who the owner is. *Ro'im Yisra'elim*, by contrast, would describe the shepherd's identity. The *Gemara* notes that the *mishnah* actually uses both constructions — *ro'ei Yisrael* and *ro'im Kohanim* — and the textual variation will be at the heart of the *Gemara*'s debate.

### Yochanan Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, חַד אָמַר: ״רוֹעֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ בֵּי כֹהֲנִים — נֶאֱמָנִים, לִלְגִימָא לָא חָיְישִׁינַן. רוֹעֵי כֹּהֲנִים בֵּי יִשְׂרָאֵל — אֵין נֶאֱמָנִים, מֵימָר אָמַר: כֵּיוָן דְּקָא טָרַחְנָא בֵּיהּ, לָא שָׁבֵיק לְדִידִי וְיָהֵיב לְאַחֲרִינֵי. וְהוּא הַדִּין כֹּהֵן לְכֹהֵן, דְּחָיְשִׁינַן לְגוֹמְלִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Elazar** disagree with regard to the meaning of the mishna. **One** of them **says** that the case of **Israelite shepherds** is where the shepherds are in **the house of priests,** i.e., in the employ of a priest. These shepherds are **deemed credible** to testify that the blemishes were not caused intentionally, as **we are not concerned** they are lying **for a swallow [*lilegima*]** of the firstborn that their priest employer might give them in exchange. And the case of **priest-shepherds** is where the shepherds are in **the house of an Israelite,** i.e., in the latter's employ. In such a case, the priest-shepherds **are not deemed credible** to testify that the blemishes were not caused intentionally, as the priest-shepherd **can say: Since I have toiled with** this firstborn offering, my Israelite employer **will not forsake me and give** it **to another** priest. The priest is assumed to be lying, as he has a motive to cause a blemish in the animal. **And the same is true** in the case of **a priest** who testifies **for** the sake of another **priest.** Although the first priest would not derive benefit from his testimony immediately, he is nevertheless not deemed credible to testify, **as we are concerned about reciprocal** behavior, i.e., the other priest might later repay the favor and lie on his behalf.

Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar dispute which reading is primary. One opinion reads the *mishnah* as going by the identity of the shepherd: *ro'ei Yisrael bei Kohanim ne'emanim* — even if the shepherd is employed by a *kohen*, if the shepherd himself is an Israelite he is believed. The rationale is that we are not concerned he will compromise himself for small incidental benefit — *legima lo chaishinan*, we do not worry that the *kohen* will reward him with a meal of *shechted bechor*. He has no sufficient stake to lie.

Conversely, *ro'ei Kohanim be-Yisrael einam ne'emanim* — a *kohen* shepherd employed by an Israelite is not believed. The *kohen* shepherd reasons: *keivan de-katarchana bei*, I have worked with this animal faithfully — surely when the time comes my master will give the *bechor* to me rather than to some other *kohen*. Since he anticipates receiving the animal, he cannot be trusted. Furthermore, *ve-hu ha-din Kohen le-Kohen*: a *kohen* testifying about a neighboring *kohen*'s *bechor* is equally untrustworthy, because *chashiyan le-gomlin* — we are concerned for reciprocity. Today I testify for your *bechor*; tomorrow you testify for mine.

### Shimon Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲתָא רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל לְמֵימַר: נֶאֱמָן הוּא עַל שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, וְאֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן עַל שֶׁל עַצְמוֹ. וַאֲתָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר לְמֵימַר: הֶחָשׁוּד עַל הַדֶּבֶר לֹא דָּנוֹ וְלֹא מְעִידוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel comes to say** that the testimony of a priest in such a case, when he testifies **about** the firstborn **of another** priest, **is deemed credible,** as there is no concern about reciprocal behavior. But **he is not deemed** credible **with regard to his own** firstborn. **And Rabbi Meir comes to say** that a priest, **who is suspect about the matter** of causing a blemish, may **neither adjudicate nor testify** in cases involving that matter, even on behalf of another. The difference between the opinion of Rabbi Meir and that of the first *tanna* will be discussed further on.

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* disagrees with both the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Meir. He says: *ne'eman hu al shel chaveiro ve-eino ne'eman al shel atzmo* — one is believed to testify about another person's animal, and is not believed only about one's own. Whether you are an Israelite or a *kohen*, if you are testifying about someone else's *bechor* — even a family member's according to some readings — we do not worry about the small benefits that might flow to you. The concern of reciprocity and the concern of incidental reward (*legima*) are simply not weighty enough to override the basic presumption of credibility. You are disqualified only when it is your own sheep, because then you are a direct and immediate beneficiary.

### Owner Determines

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחַד אָמַר: ״רוֹעֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ וְהֵן כֹּהֲנִים — נֶאֱמָנִים, מֵימָר אָמַר: לָא שָׁבֵיק צוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן וְיָהֵיב לְדִידִי. ״רוֹעֵי כֹּהֲנִים״ וְהֵן יִשְׂרָאֵל — אֵינָן נֶאֱמָנִים, דְּחָיְשִׁינַן לִלְגִימָא. וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן כֹּהֵן לְכֹהֵן, דְּחָיְשִׁינַן לְגוֹמְלִין, וְחָיְשִׁינַן לִלְגִימָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites the other explanation of the mishna: **And** the other **one** of the two *amora'im* **says:** The case of **Israelite shepherds** is where the shepherds **are priests** in the employ of an Israelite. The mishna is teaching that these shepherds **are deemed credible** to testify that the blemishes on their employer's firstborn offering were not caused intentionally, as they are not suspected of lying in order to obtain its meat. The reason is that it is assumed that the priest-shepherd would **say:** My Israelite employer **will not forsake** a priest who is **a Torah scholar and give** the blemished animal **to me,** an unlearned shepherd. The case of **priest-shepherds** is where the shepherds **are Israelites** in the employ of a priest. These shepherds are **not deemed credible** to testify, **as we are concerned** that they might be lying **for a swallow** of the firstborn that their priest employer would give them in exchange. **And all the more so** the testimony of **a priest for** the sake of another **priest** is not deemed credible, **as we are concerned that** the two might engage in **reciprocal** behavior, **and we are concerned** that the priest-shepherd might be lying **for a swallow** of the firstborn offering.

The second major reading of the *mishnah* holds that the operative question is not who the shepherd is but who the *owner* is — that is, who benefits from the testimony. Under this reading: *ro'ei Yisrael she-hen Kohanim ne'emanim* — shepherds employed by Israelites, even if those shepherds are themselves *kohanim*, are believed. The *kohen* shepherd reasons: *lo shevik tzurba mei-rabbanan ve-yahiv li didi* — why would this Israelite owner give the *bechor* to me, a lowly shepherd, rather than to a distinguished *kohen*? He does not expect to benefit and therefore has no motive to lie.

*Ro'ei Kohanim ve-hen Yisrael einam ne'emanim* — shepherds of *kohanim*, even if those shepherds are Israelites, are not believed. Here, the *kohen* owner directly benefits from the testimony, and we fear the shepherd will be rewarded — *chashinan le-legima*. And *kol she-kein Kohen le-Kohen d'lo* — certainly one *kohen* cannot testify for another *kohen*'s *bechor*, because in that case we face *both* concerns simultaneously: *chashinan le-gomlin ve-chashinan le-legima* — both the reciprocity concern and the reward concern apply together.

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* then comes and says: both concerns are overstated. You may testify for another's animal entirely regardless of who you are and regardless of who benefits. Only self-testimony is excluded.

### Meir Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: רוֹעֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְהֵן כֹּהֲנִים נֶאֱמָנִים — הַיְינוּ דַּאֲתָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר לְמֵימַר: הֶחָשׁוּד עַל הַדָּבָר לֹא דָּנוֹ וְלֹא מְעִידוֹ. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: רוֹעֵי כֹּהֲנִים בֵּי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֵין נֶאֱמָנִים — מַאי אֲתָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן? הַיְינוּ תַּנָּא קַמָּא! אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קַפּוֹסַאי, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קַפּוֹסַאי אוֹמֵר: בְּכוֹר בֵּי כֹהֵן צָרִיךְ שְׁנַיִם מִן הַשּׁוּק לְהָעִיד עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Granted, according to** the second opinion, i.e., **the one who says** the first *tanna* maintains that the case of **Israelite shepherds** is where the shepherds **are priests** in the employ of an Israelite, and they **are deemed credible** to testify, **this is** the reason **that Rabbi Meir comes to** disagree and **say** that a priest **who is suspect about the matter** of causing a blemish may **neither adjudicate nor testify** even for the sake of an Israelite employer. **But according to** the first opinion, i.e., **the one who says** that the first *tanna* maintains that the case of **priest-shepherds** is where the shepherds are in **an Israelite's house,** and they **are not deemed credible** to testify, **what is Rabbi Meir coming to teach us? This is** the same opinion as that of the **first *tanna*** himself. The Gemara answers: **There is** a difference **between** the opinions of the first *tanna* and Rabbi Meir **with regard to** a statement **of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Kefusai. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Kefusai says:** If **a firstborn** animal **in the house of a priest** developed a blemish, **two** people **from the marketplace,** i.e., not from the priest's household, are **required to testify about it** that the blemish was not caused deliberately. This unqualified statement indicates that the two people can even be priests, provided that they are not household members.

Rabbi Meir then articulates a categorically different principle: *ha-chashud al ha-davar lo dano ve-lo me'ido* — anyone who is suspect regarding a matter may neither judge it nor testify about it. *Kohanim* as a class are suspect when it comes to *bechorot* — they have a systemic interest in the institution and have been known to take liberties. They cannot be trusted regardless of context, regardless of whether they are shepherds or bystanders, regardless of whether it is their own animal or a total stranger's. *Yisraelim* are not suspect as a class.

How does Rabbi Meir's position differ from the first reading of the *tanna kamma*, which also held that a *kohen* is not believed? The difference surfaces in the case of *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Kapusai*, who rules: *bechor be-veit Kohen tzarich shnayim min ha-shuk le-haid alav* — a *bechor* in the possession of a *kohen* requires two disinterested witnesses from the marketplace to establish that a *mum* occurred naturally. The *tanna kamma*'s concern with *kohanim* was circumstantial — based on possible reward or reciprocity. Two strangers with no connection to each other and no relationship with the *kohen* would, under the *tanna kamma*'s framework, adequately address both concerns. But Rabbi Meir, who distrusts *kohanim* categorically, would still refuse to accept two *kohanim* from the marketplace regardless of their independence, because the disqualification is not circumstantial but class-based. This is the precise *nafka mina* between the two positions.

## Safek Bechor Trust

### Owners Testify

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן אָזְלָא הָא דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַב קַטִּינָא: סְפֵק בְּכוֹר שֶׁנּוֹלַד בֵּי יִשְׂרָאֵל, צָרִיךְ שְׁנַיִם מִן הַשּׁוּק לְהָעִיד עָלָיו? כְּמַאן? כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קַפּוֹסַאי. רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: בְּעָלִים מְעִידִין עָלָיו, דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, מַעֲשֵׂר לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר מִי מֵעִיד עָלָיו?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses a related statement: **In accordance with whose** opinion **is that which Rav Ḥisda says** that **Rav Ketina says:** In the case of an animal whose status as a **firstborn** is **uncertain that was born** in the **house,** i.e., in the possession, **of an Israelite,** e.g., it was uncertain whether the mother had previously given birth, in which case the animal remains in the possession of the Israelite and may be eaten upon developing a blemish, **two** people **from the marketplace** are **required to testify about it?** **In accordance with whose** opinion is this statement? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Kefusai.** Just as he requires two independent people to testify about a firstborn offering in the possession of a priest, as he is suspected of intentionally causing the blemish, so too, he requires two independent people to testify in the case of an uncertain status of a firstborn offering born in the possession of an Israelite. **Rav Naḥman** disagrees and **says:** The Israelite **owners** themselves **may testify about it. As, if you do not say so,** but instead maintain that any involved person is suspected of deliberately causing a blemish in his firstborn animal, this poses a difficulty with regard to the *halakha* of an animal **tithe** offering, which may also be eaten if it developed a blemish. **According to Rabbi Meir, who can testify about it?** Rabbi Meir maintains that anyone who is suspect about the matter of causing a blemish on his own behalf may neither adjudicate nor testify in cases involving that matter even on behalf of another. If so, how can anyone, even an Israelite, testify about any blemished animal-tithe offering? It must be that an Israelite is not suspected of deliberately causing blemishes.

*Keman azla ha de-amar Rav Chisda amar Rav Katina: safek bechor she-nolad be-Yisrael tzarich shnayim min ha-shuk le-haid alav* — a doubtful firstborn in the possession of an Israelite requires two witnesses from the marketplace. This ruling seems aligned with Rabbi Yehoshua ben Kapusai, who consistently demands two disinterested strangers. *Rav Nachman* objects: he holds that even the *owners themselves* can testify — *ba'alim me'idin alav*.

Rav Nachman argues by analogy: *de-ai lo teima hachi, ma'aser le-Rabbi Meir mi me'id alav?* If we distrusted every possible beneficiary, then for *ma'aser behemah* — every tenth animal, which must be treated as *maaser* and whose *mum* permits ordinary consumption — nobody could ever testify, since any Israelite could one day have a *ma'aser* with a *mum* and would therefore be a potential future beneficiary of any testimony he gives today. The *Gemara* responds: *ma'aser vadai ne'eman*. By *ma'aser*, there was always an easy way to cheat had one wanted to — *de-ee ba'ei sharei bei muma be-chulei edrei* — one could have inflicted a *mum* before setting the animal apart for *ma'aser* in the first place. Since the person chose honesty when dishonesty was readily available, we trust him. The *ma'aser* case does not generalize.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֵׂר וַדַּאי מְהֵימַן, דְּאִי בָּעֵי שָׁדֵי בֵּיהּ מוּמָא בְּכוּלֵּיהּ עֶדְרֵיהּ. אֶלָּא סְפֵק בְּכוֹר, לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר מִי מֵעִיד עָלָיו? וְכִי תֵּימָא, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלֵית לֵיהּ תַּקַּנְתָּא, וְהָתְנַן, שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁחֲלִיפָיו בְּיַד כֹּהֵן פָּטוּר מִן הַמַּתָּנוֹת, וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר מְחַיֵּיב! אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: בְּעָלִים מְעִידִין עָלָיו, כֹּהֲנִים הוּא דַּחֲשִׁידִי אַמּוּמֵי, יִשְׂרָאֵל לָא חֲשִׁידִי אַמּוּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the animal **tithe** offering, the owner is **certainly deemed credible** to testify that its blemish occurred naturally, **because if he wants,** he could legitimately **cause a blemish in his entire flock** before the obligation to separate tithes came into effect. **Rather,** this is what Rav Naḥman stated: If an Israelite is not deemed credible to testify about a blemish found on an animal whose status as a **firstborn** is **uncertain,** then **according to Rabbi Meir, who can testify about it?** Both Israelites and priests stand to gain if their animal whose status as a firstborn is uncertain develops a blemish, and therefore no one should be deemed credible to testify about it. **Rather, learn from this** that the Israelite **owners may testify about** their animals whose status as a firstborn are uncertain, despite the fact that priests may not. The reason is that **it is** only **priests who are suspected of** causing **blemishes; Israelites are not suspected of** causing **blemishes.**

The *Gemara* therefore presses the *safek bechor* question more sharply: *safek bechor le-Rabbi Meir mi me'id alav?* If Rabbi Meir distrusts potential beneficiaries categorically, and any Israelite is a potential beneficiary of *safek bechor* through the mechanism of reciprocity, then nobody can ever testify, and an Israelite with a *safek bechor* could never *shecht* it. That would leave *ein lo takanta* — no remedy whatsoever. Yet the *Gemara* shows from an unambiguous *mishnah* that Rabbi Meir does permit *shechita* of a *safek bechor* in the case of *chalipha*.

### Class Suspicion

The operative principle is not "are you a potential beneficiary" but rather "are you a member of a class known to be compromised in this area." *Kohanim*, who are systematically immersed in the world of *bechor*, have demonstrated a pattern of taking liberties; they are distrusted as a class. *Yisraelim*, even when they have a direct stake in the outcome — even when it is their own *safek bechor* — are not suspect as a class. The ordinary person's commitment to *kashrut* and honest practice is presumed sufficient, even where self-interest is present.

This is a striking conclusion. Rabbi Meir's categorical rejection of *kohanim* does not derive from a theory of pure self-interest — if it did, it would equally disqualify any potential beneficiary. Rather, it derives from a sociological assessment: *kohanim* as a group have compromised themselves with respect to *bechorot*, and that class-level suspicion cannot be cured by strangers or numbers or circumstances. *Yisraelim* have not, and no circumstantial self-interest is sufficient to apply the same categorical exclusion to them.

### Chalipha Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אִיתְּמַר: רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, רָבָא אָמַר: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § It **was stated: Rav Naḥman says** that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel,** that a priest is deemed credible to testify about the blemished firstborn animal of another priest, even if he is a household member. **Rava says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** that one's household members are not deemed credible to testify.

The *Gemara* concludes with a citation from the *mishnah* relevant to Rabbi Meir's view. *Chalifa be-yad Kohen patur min ha-matanot* — Rabbi Yosei rules that if one had two animals, one of which was a *bechor* and one of which was not, and traded one to a *kohen* without knowing which was which, the animal retained by the Israelite is exempt from the priestly gifts (*zero'a lechayayim ve-keiva*). *Ve-Rabbi Meir mechayev* — Rabbi Meir obligates. The fact that Rabbi Meir obligates in *matanot* in this scenario implies that the Israelite has been able to *shecht* his *safek bechor* and eat it, confirming that Rabbi Meir does indeed allow an Israelite owner to testify about his own *safek bechor* — consistent with the conclusion that his class-based suspicion applies only to *kohanim*.

The full implications of Rabbi Meir's position — in particular, whether a *kohen* afflicted with *tzara'as* can ever be declared *tahor* if we categorically distrust the testimony of *kohanim* — are an open and pointed question, which the *Gemara* will take up when the *daf* continues.
