# Bekhorot 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bridegroom Tzara'as

### Yitaher Tenan Debate

The *daf* opens by completing the wrap-up discussion of *tzara'as* that began on 34b. *Ba'ei Rav Papa* — Rav Papa poses the question: *yitaher tenan o ve-yitaher tenan*? The *mishnah* rules that if a person cuts away a *nega* in order to prevent a *kohen* from declaring him *tamei*, he must wait until a second *nega* appears and runs its course, at which point he becomes *tahor* from the first as well. The question is how to read the text: does the *advent* of the second *nega* itself purify him from the first, or must he wait until he becomes *tahor* from the second before the first is resolved?

At first glance the question seems academic — either way, as long as the second *nega* is present he remains *tamei*. The *Gemara* therefore asks *le-mai nafka mina*, and the answer lies in a specific case.

### Chosson Tzara'as

The practical difference involves a *chosson* who develops a *nega*. As the *mishnah* in *Nega'im* teaches: *chosson she-nira bo nega*, a bridegroom who shows signs of *tzara'as* is given seven days before any ruling is issued — *notnim lo shiva yamim* — out of respect for his *simcha*. This deferral applies not only to him personally but even to his garments and his house. Similarly, *ve-chen ba-regel notnim lo kol yemei ha-regel*: for any person, if the *nega* appears during *yom tov*, the ruling is postponed until after the full seven days of the festival.

Now imagine that during this deferral period a second *nega* appears. If we read *yitaher tenan* — that the arrival of the second *nega* itself extinguishes the first — then from the moment the second spot appears, the person becomes *tahor* from the first, and we then defer examination of the second for the seven days of *sheva brachos*. During that window, he is actually *tahor*. But if we read *ve-yitaher tenan* — that he must first become *tahor* from the second before the first is resolved — then he never achieves *tahor* status during the wedding week, because the unresolved first *nega* keeps him *tamei* until the second has been purified. The two readings yield meaningfully different practical outcomes for the *chosson* during *sheva brachos*.

### First vs. Second Spot

The question is posed cleanly: *ei amart yitaher tenan*, the second spot's arrival itself grants *tahor* status from the first — *mi-kamaisa tihar lei*, and we then defer the second under *le-basraisa natrinan lei sheva yemei mishta*, giving the *chosson* a genuine period of *taharah*. *Ella ei amart ve-yitaher tenan*, then even without the *kohen* ruling on the second, he remains *tamei* from the first until the second is fully purified — *sof sof ke-ilu metamei lei le-basraisa, ha metamei ve-kaimi mi-kamaisa* — and the wedding week provides no relief. The *Gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teiku*.

## Kansu Beno Acharav

### Son Fined Question

With the *tzara'as* discussion concluded, the *Gemara* returns to the central topic of *bechor*: the rabbinic fine imposed on one who intentionally inflicts a *mum* on a *bechor*. *Ba'ei minei Rabbi Yirmiyah me-Rabbi Zeira* — Rabbi Yirmiyah poses the question to Rabbi Zeira: *tzorem ozen be-bechor u-meis* — if a person punctures the ear of a *bechor* and then dies, *mahu liknos beno acharav*? Will the son be fined as well?

The ear is the paradigmatic example of an intentionally inflicted *mum* — it does not heal, and we will see shortly why it features so prominently in the *mishnah*. The underlying tension in the question is genuine: if the fine is understood primarily as a *knas*, a punishment directed at the transgressor, then once that person is dead, the rationale for the fine evaporates. On the other hand, one might argue that the purpose is societal — to create a disincentive for anyone who might contemplate deathbed manipulation, ensuring that heirs cannot benefit either. The *Gemara* approaches the question by comparing it to several parallel cases.

### Servant & Field Cases

The *Gemara* introduces two other situations in which the question of *kansu beno acharav* arises. The first: *mocher avdo le-kochavim u-meis* — if someone sells his Jewish slave to a non-Jew. Since such a slave is obligated in *mitzvot*, selling him to a non-Jew effectively strips him of the ability to fulfill them. *Chazal* therefore required the seller to redeem the slave at up to ten times the original sale price. If the seller dies, must the son redeem him? The *Gemara* reasons that in this case the answer may well be yes — not because of a general rule that fines pass to heirs, but because *be-chol yomo ve-yomo mafkei lei mi-mitzvot*: every single day the slave remains with the non-Jew he is prevented from performing *mitzvot*. The concern is for the welfare of the slave himself, not merely for punishing the seller. Because the rationale is remedial rather than punitive, it may apply even after the transgressor's death.

The second case involves one who deliberately plans his agricultural labor for *chol ha-mo'ed*. *Chazal* permitted working a field on *chol ha-mo'ed* only in cases of *davar ha-aved* — unavoidable financial loss — not when the person intentionally scheduled his work for that time. If such a person dies having done so, must his son refrain from planting? Here the *Gemara* suggests the son may be exempt — *keivan moachda be-mo'ada u-meis lo kansu beno acharav* — because the father had not yet even completed the transgression when he died. There was no act that concretely needed undoing. The *Gemara* itself acknowledges that framing these cases as *knas* is somewhat strained: in the field case, one could simply say the father never qualified for the *davar ha-aved* exception to begin with.

With these two cases before it — one where the son likely does bear consequences, one where he likely does not — the *Gemara* asks *hacha mai*: what about our case of the *bechor*? The father did perform a concrete act; but unlike the slave case, there is no ongoing victim requiring active remedy. The *Gemara* presents two framings. *Le-didei kansu rabanan* — perhaps the rabbis directed their fine at the person himself, and since he is gone, it cannot reach the son. *O dilma le-mamonei kansu rabanan* — alternatively, they made a statement not about the person but about the property: this animal can no longer yield benefit, and since the animal *ve-hai itei*, the property still exists, the restriction ought to travel with it to the heir.

### Abaye's Resolution

The *Gemara* resolves the question by citing a *mishnah* in the laws of *shemita*. *Sadeh she-nitnaksha ba-shevi'it* — a field from which one cleared thorns and thistles on *shemita* without actually working the soil may still be planted after *shemita*. But one who manured or penned animals in the field on *shemita* — thereby genuinely improving it — is fined and may not plant it after *shemita* either. *Ve-amar Rabbi Yossi bar Chanina*: *hetiva u-meis, beno zor'ah* — if the father fertilized the field and died, the son *may* plant it. *Alma le-didei kansu rabanan, livrei lo kansu rabanan* — we fine the person, not the son.

*Amar Abaye* anticipates the objection: the field case may not be fully analogous to the *bechor*, because by the field the fine is action-directed — we are telling the transgressor not to do this. By the *bechor*, one could argue more formalistically that the rabbis made a declaration about the object itself — the *mum* is treated as non-existent — which would naturally extend to the heir. Abaye does not press the distinction but rather embraces the comparison, and the *Gemara* treats the principle of *le-didei kansu rabanan, livrei lo kansu rabanan* as the operative rule across cases.

### Terumah Case

Abaye proceeds to extend the principle. *Tima taharotav u-meis* — here is the case discussed in *Gittin*: a person intentionally causes someone else's *terumah* to become *tamei* by casting a *sharetz* upon it, rendering it worthless. Ordinarily no payment is collected for such damage because *hezek she-eino nikar lo shmei hezek* — damage that is not visually apparent does not constitute actionable injury under biblical law. Yet if done intentionally, *Chazal* imposed liability. If the perpetrator dies, must his son pay? Abaye says: *lo kansu beno acharav* — the son does not pay. *Mai ta'ama*? Because *hezek she-eino nikar* is only a *rabbinic* fine — *knas de-rabanan hu* — and *didei kansu rabanan, le-vrei lo kansu rabanan*: they fined him, not his son.

One might ask whether policy considerations should override this conclusion — after all, a real person has suffered a real loss, and allowing the debt to die with the transgressor seems like an obvious loophole. The *Gemara* here is consistent in its approach: it categorizes these cases as *knas* and applies the general rule, leaving the harder policy questions to the side. The real analytical challenge — noted but not fully resolved here — is determining which situations qualify as *knas* in the first place, as opposed to *takanos* or corrections of substantive law, since that threshold determination governs everything that follows. The *sugya* in *Tosafot* and other *masechtos* develops this question further; for our purposes, the *Gemara* has established the principle firmly.

## Intentional Blemish Cases

### Quaestor Story

The *Gemara* now returns to the question of precisely what *le-da'ato* — intentionality — means when it comes to inflicting a *mum* on a *bechor*, and introduces a remarkable *mishnah*. *Maaseh be-zachar she-peruchav zekenim*: there was an incident involving a very old ram whose wool had never been sheared — as a *bechor* without a *mum* it could not be shorn — and was accordingly trailing and scraggly. *Ve-ra'ahu kastor echad*: a certain Roman official, a *quaestor* (a revenue and legal officer), encountered it and asked what was going on with this animal. The people told him, *bechor hu ve-eino nishchat ela im ken hayah bo mum* — it is a firstborn, and it cannot be *shechted* unless it has a blemish. The official then took a *pigum* — a pugio, a double-bladed Roman dagger — and *tzaram be-ozno*, punctured its ear. *U-va ma'aseh lifnei chachamim va-hitiru*: the case came before the Sages and they declared it permitted.

It is worth noting that the *pigum* — the instrument of piercing — lends its name to the paradigmatic act of inflicting a *mum* on a *bechor*: *pogem ozen shel bechor*. Whether the term derives from this story or simply reflects the ease with which an ear wound is inflicted and yet never heals is an interesting question in itself.

The first ruling — that the animal is *mutar* — seems straightforward if one frames it as: the non-Jew acted on his own initiative, nobody commissioned him, and therefore there is no *le-da'ato* on the part of the Israelite owner. The difficulty is that the non-Jew plainly understood what he was doing and why; he was acting deliberately to benefit the Jewish owner. *Rashi* attempts to address this by arguing that at the time the non-Jew acted he did not yet know that the *chachamim* would actually permit the *bechor* — he was, in Rashi's reading, uncertain about the legal effect of his action. This is admittedly difficult: why would he do it if he didn't believe it would help?

*Ve-achar she-hitiru, halach ve-tzaram be-oznei bechorot acherim* — after hearing the ruling, the quaestor went and started puncturing the ears of other people's *bechorot*. *Va-asru* — and then the Sages forbade it. Taken at face value, one might read the reversal as a policy measure: once a single permissive ruling leads to systematic exploitation, the policy itself must be retracted. But *Rashi* and the *Gemara* read it differently: the first act, whatever degree of awareness it involved, did not constitute an act performed with the specific knowledge that it would render the *bechor* permitted. Once the non-Jew heard the ruling and knew with certainty, each subsequent act was performed *le-da'ato* — with full knowledge of the legal effect — and therefore forbidden. The *Gemara* does not read this as a blanket prospective prohibition on all future cases of the same type; it is specifically the post-ruling acts that are forbidden because of the actor's now-certain intent.

The tension is real. If even the first act was being performed to benefit the Jewish owner, it is somewhat difficult to describe it as *shelo le-da'ato*. One way to resolve this — though the *Gemara* does not state it explicitly — is to distinguish between doing something that *benefits* another and doing something *at another's behest*. The first time the non-Jew acted, he was doing the Jew a favor; the Jew had not prompted him, had not hinted at it, had not expressed desire that it be done. After hearing the ruling, the non-Jew was effectively acting as the Jew's *shaliach*, and at that point the Jew's implicit desire becomes the operative factor.

### Children Tail Case

A parallel incident is then cited: *pa'am achat hayu tinokot mesachakim ba-sadeh ve-kashru zinvei telaim zeh ba-zeh* — some children were playing in a field and tied the tails of sheep together, *va-nifsaka zanavo shel echad me-hem* — and one animal's tail was pulled off. The animal turned out to be a *bechor*. *Ba ma'aseh lifnei chachamim va-hitiru* — the case came before the Sages and they permitted it, since the act was entirely unintentional. But *ra'u she-hitiru ve-holchu ve-kashru zinvos bechorot acheirim* — once they saw the first case had been permitted, they went and tied other *bechorot'* tails together, *va-asru*.

The same interpretive question arises: is this a prospective policy reversal, or is it only the second set of acts that are forbidden because the children now knew what effect their action would have? The *Gemara* adopts the latter reading, which sets up the concluding principle of the *mishnah*.

Both cases are needed — *tzericha*. Had the *Gemara* cited only the non-Jew's case, one might have thought that the first act was innocuous precisely because there was no concern it would be repeated: a freak occurrence by a Roman official would not lead to Israelites following suit. The novelty of the children's case is that allowing children to do something will very plausibly lead to repetition — *de-ati le-misrach* — because children, once told something is permitted, will do it again. On that logic, one might have argued the first case involving children should never have been permitted. Conversely, had the *Gemara* cited only the children's case, one might have thought Israelites would not follow the precedent of a non-Jew — *de-lo ati le-achlufi be-gadol* — so that non-Jewish acts would always remain in a category apart. *Tzericha*: you need both.

The *Gemara* also notes that the concern about children acting again carries a *chinuch* dimension — it is an *aveira* for a Jewish child to do this, and we do not want to habituate children to such behavior. No corresponding *aveira* concern applies to the non-Jew, which is why the initial permissive ruling presented no *chinuch* problem in that case.

### Intentionality Principle

*Zeh ha-klal: kol she-hu le-da'ato asur, shelo le-da'ato mutar* — this is the governing principle: done with awareness of the legal effect, forbidden; done without such awareness, permitted.

The *Gemara* asks what the principle comes to include beyond the stated cases. *Le-atuyei grama*: it includes even indirect causation. As the *Gemara* discussed on the previous *daf*, even if one creates the conditions for a *mum* to occur without directly inflicting it — the small *kre'ach-lach* made for dogs, for instance — if this was done intentionally, not only is the act itself prohibited but the resulting *mum* does not permit *shechita*. Conversely, *shelo le-da'ato mutar* includes even *masiach le-fi tumo* — speaking casually and offhandedly. If a Jew mentions to a friend, without directing his words at any non-Jew, that the animal has no *mum* yet, and a non-Jew overhearing the remark spontaneously acts — that too would be permitted, even though the earlier case of a direct response to a non-Jew was already permitted.

What is striking about this entire *sugya*, taken together, is the degree to which it points toward *policy* considerations rather than a pure *knas* framework. The concern with children repeating the act, the concern with non-Jews acting *as an agent* for the Jew, the focus on whether the Jew's intent to benefit is operative — all of this fits more naturally within a framework of protecting the institution of *bechor* from being systematically undermined. Yet the *Gemara* continues to frame the analysis in terms of *knas*, and that framing creates genuine tension with the conclusions it reaches.

### Kicking Analysis

Rav Chisda, citing Rav Katina, adds an important clarification about the case of a direct verbal response to a non-Jew: *lo shanu ela de-amar lei* — the first case is permitted only when the Israelite responded by saying the animal cannot be *shechted* unless it *has* a *mum*, using language that describes the existing condition. *Aval im amar lei ye'aseh bo mum* — but if the Israelite said "unless a *mum* is *made* in it," using the passive but clearly implying human agency, *ke-man de-amar lei zil avid bei mum dami* — it is as though he said to the non-Jew: go make a *mum*. In that case it would be forbidden even the first time.

*Amar Rava*: this distinction is rejected. *Mimela hu* — the passive voice is not an instruction; *na'aseh* can simply mean something that happens on its own, just as *hayah bo mum* describes a *mum* that exists. If the Israelite said "unless a *mum* comes to be in it," that is not a directive. Rava insists that only an unambiguous explicit instruction crosses the line.

The analogy to *amira le-akum* on *Shabbat* is apt here. On *Shabbat*, a Jew who describes his situation — "how dark it is in this room" — has not issued an *amira*; a Jew who says "it would be wonderful if someone turned on the light" has crossed into *remez* territory. The same distinction operates here. A passive construction describing a condition is not an *amira*; only language that functionally directs the non-Jew's action would constitute one.

The *mishnah* then addresses a case on the opposite end of the spectrum. *Haya bechor rodfo* — if a *bechor* ram was chasing a person, *be-ato ve-asah bo mum* — and the person kicked it defensively and in so doing created a *mum*, *harei zeh yishachet alav*: the animal may be *shechted*. *Amar Rav Papa: lo shanu ela she-be'at be-sha'at redifa* — this applies only if the kick occurred during the chase itself. *Aval achar redifa* — if after the ram stopped pursuing him the person kicked it out of residual anger, *assur*. The *Gemara* presents two formulations of this ruling. One version holds the post-chase kick is clearly problematic — one might argue the person was simply venting *tzaraosav*, remembering his suffering, rather than acting to create a *mum*, but *kah mashma lan* that we are still concerned his intent was to inflict a *mum*. The other version is that even a post-chase kick is permitted — *afilu achar redifa nami* — because *tzayreh hu de-madkar*: he is acting out of remembered pain and anguish, not to create a *mum*, and we are not concerned.

## Pre-Birth & Visibility

### Pre-Birth Blemish

*Amar Rav Yehuda: muttar le-hatil mum be-bechor kodem she-yetze le-avir ha-olam* — it is permitted to inflict a *mum* on a *bechor* before it has fully emerged into the world. This is another practical solution to the difficulty of managing a *bechor*, parallel to the early-period strategy noted by the *Rishonim* and later codified in the *Shulchan Aruch*. The idea here is that while the animal is still partially within the womb — before the majority of its head has emerged and it is considered halachically born — any blemish inflicted is not a prohibited act.

### Goat Ears, Sheep Lips

*Amar Rava*: the practical application depends on the species. For a *gadya* — a goat — one works with the *oznei*, the ears, since goats have long ears that protrude well before the majority of the head emerges. For an *imra* — a sheep — the ears are shorter, so one uses *bi-sfatei*, the lips, which similarly emerge before the head is fully born. A cut to the lip will not heal and constitutes a permanent *mum*. An alternate tradition — *ika de-amri* — holds that even a sheep's ear may be used if the animal happens to emerge sideways, *derech tzad nafak*, with the ears preceding the head. *Tosafot* reads *eima* here not as a permissive "one could say" but as a definitive description: if the birth happens to be sideways, the ears indeed come out first and may be used.

### Visible When Bleating

*Amar Rava*: if an animal has a blemish on the inside of its mouth that is not visible while it eats but becomes visible *pa'i* — when it bleats — *u-machzi*, and can be seen from the outside, *havei muma* — this constitutes a valid *mum*. The visibility standard for a *mum* is that it must be externally observable; the question is what counts as external when the blemish is technically internal.

*Rav Papa* responds that Rava is not introducing a new principle but explaining the *Gemara* — that is, providing the *peshat* of the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* lists among valid *mumim* the case of *ha-chitei ha-chitzonot she-nifgemu* — outer incisors that have been chipped — and *ha-penimiyot she-ne'ekru* — inner molars that have been completely extracted. The question is why extracted inner molars, which cannot be seen normally, count as externally visible. *Mai ta'ama de-ne'ekru havei muma? Mishum de-chi pa'i machzi* — because when the animal bleats, the absence of those molars becomes visible. That defines the outer visibility threshold: a blemish seen when the animal opens its mouth to bleat is considered sufficiently external to qualify.

## Witness Credibility

### Mishnah Framework

The *Gemara* now pivots to the equally consequential question of credibility: who may testify that a *mum* on a *bechor* occurred naturally and was not intentionally inflicted? The *mishnah* states: *kol ha-mumim ha-re'uyim lavo bi-dei adam* — for any blemish that could have been humanly inflicted, credibility is required. Blemishes that obviously could not have been produced by human agency — a supernumerary limb, congenital blindness, or the like — require no such testimony. For the rest: *ro'ei Yisrael ne'emanim, ro'ei Kohanim einam ne'emanim* — Israelite *ro'im* are believed, *kohanim ro'im* are not.

The *mishnah*'s formulation is itself ambiguous. *Ro'ei Yisrael* in construct form (*smichut*) means "shepherds of Israelites" — describing who the owner is. *Ro'im Yisra'elim*, by contrast, would describe the shepherd's identity. The *Gemara* notes that the *mishnah* actually uses both constructions — *ro'ei Yisrael* and *ro'im Kohanim* — and the textual variation will be at the heart of the *Gemara*'s debate.

### Yochanan Opinion

Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar dispute which reading is primary. One opinion reads the *mishnah* as going by the identity of the shepherd: *ro'ei Yisrael bei Kohanim ne'emanim* — even if the shepherd is employed by a *kohen*, if the shepherd himself is an Israelite he is believed. The rationale is that we are not concerned he will compromise himself for small incidental benefit — *legima lo chaishinan*, we do not worry that the *kohen* will reward him with a meal of *shechted bechor*. He has no sufficient stake to lie.

Conversely, *ro'ei Kohanim be-Yisrael einam ne'emanim* — a *kohen* shepherd employed by an Israelite is not believed. The *kohen* shepherd reasons: *keivan de-katarchana bei*, I have worked with this animal faithfully — surely when the time comes my master will give the *bechor* to me rather than to some other *kohen*. Since he anticipates receiving the animal, he cannot be trusted. Furthermore, *ve-hu ha-din Kohen le-Kohen*: a *kohen* testifying about a neighboring *kohen*'s *bechor* is equally untrustworthy, because *chashiyan le-gomlin* — we are concerned for reciprocity. Today I testify for your *bechor*; tomorrow you testify for mine.

### Shimon Opinion

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* disagrees with both the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Meir. He says: *ne'eman hu al shel chaveiro ve-eino ne'eman al shel atzmo* — one is believed to testify about another person's animal, and is not believed only about one's own. Whether you are an Israelite or a *kohen*, if you are testifying about someone else's *bechor* — even a family member's according to some readings — we do not worry about the small benefits that might flow to you. The concern of reciprocity and the concern of incidental reward (*legima*) are simply not weighty enough to override the basic presumption of credibility. You are disqualified only when it is your own sheep, because then you are a direct and immediate beneficiary.

### Owner Determines

The second major reading of the *mishnah* holds that the operative question is not who the shepherd is but who the *owner* is — that is, who benefits from the testimony. Under this reading: *ro'ei Yisrael she-hen Kohanim ne'emanim* — shepherds employed by Israelites, even if those shepherds are themselves *kohanim*, are believed. The *kohen* shepherd reasons: *lo shevik tzurba mei-rabbanan ve-yahiv li didi* — why would this Israelite owner give the *bechor* to me, a lowly shepherd, rather than to a distinguished *kohen*? He does not expect to benefit and therefore has no motive to lie.

*Ro'ei Kohanim ve-hen Yisrael einam ne'emanim* — shepherds of *kohanim*, even if those shepherds are Israelites, are not believed. Here, the *kohen* owner directly benefits from the testimony, and we fear the shepherd will be rewarded — *chashinan le-legima*. And *kol she-kein Kohen le-Kohen d'lo* — certainly one *kohen* cannot testify for another *kohen*'s *bechor*, because in that case we face *both* concerns simultaneously: *chashinan le-gomlin ve-chashinan le-legima* — both the reciprocity concern and the reward concern apply together.

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* then comes and says: both concerns are overstated. You may testify for another's animal entirely regardless of who you are and regardless of who benefits. Only self-testimony is excluded.

### Meir Opinion

Rabbi Meir then articulates a categorically different principle: *ha-chashud al ha-davar lo dano ve-lo me'ido* — anyone who is suspect regarding a matter may neither judge it nor testify about it. *Kohanim* as a class are suspect when it comes to *bechorot* — they have a systemic interest in the institution and have been known to take liberties. They cannot be trusted regardless of context, regardless of whether they are shepherds or bystanders, regardless of whether it is their own animal or a total stranger's. *Yisraelim* are not suspect as a class.

How does Rabbi Meir's position differ from the first reading of the *tanna kamma*, which also held that a *kohen* is not believed? The difference surfaces in the case of *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Kapusai*, who rules: *bechor be-veit Kohen tzarich shnayim min ha-shuk le-haid alav* — a *bechor* in the possession of a *kohen* requires two disinterested witnesses from the marketplace to establish that a *mum* occurred naturally. The *tanna kamma*'s concern with *kohanim* was circumstantial — based on possible reward or reciprocity. Two strangers with no connection to each other and no relationship with the *kohen* would, under the *tanna kamma*'s framework, adequately address both concerns. But Rabbi Meir, who distrusts *kohanim* categorically, would still refuse to accept two *kohanim* from the marketplace regardless of their independence, because the disqualification is not circumstantial but class-based. This is the precise *nafka mina* between the two positions.

## Safek Bechor Trust

### Owners Testify

*Keman azla ha de-amar Rav Chisda amar Rav Katina: safek bechor she-nolad be-Yisrael tzarich shnayim min ha-shuk le-haid alav* — a doubtful firstborn in the possession of an Israelite requires two witnesses from the marketplace. This ruling seems aligned with Rabbi Yehoshua ben Kapusai, who consistently demands two disinterested strangers. *Rav Nachman* objects: he holds that even the *owners themselves* can testify — *ba'alim me'idin alav*.

Rav Nachman argues by analogy: *de-ai lo teima hachi, ma'aser le-Rabbi Meir mi me'id alav?* If we distrusted every possible beneficiary, then for *ma'aser behemah* — every tenth animal, which must be treated as *maaser* and whose *mum* permits ordinary consumption — nobody could ever testify, since any Israelite could one day have a *ma'aser* with a *mum* and would therefore be a potential future beneficiary of any testimony he gives today. The *Gemara* responds: *ma'aser vadai ne'eman*. By *ma'aser*, there was always an easy way to cheat had one wanted to — *de-ee ba'ei sharei bei muma be-chulei edrei* — one could have inflicted a *mum* before setting the animal apart for *ma'aser* in the first place. Since the person chose honesty when dishonesty was readily available, we trust him. The *ma'aser* case does not generalize.

The *Gemara* therefore presses the *safek bechor* question more sharply: *safek bechor le-Rabbi Meir mi me'id alav?* If Rabbi Meir distrusts potential beneficiaries categorically, and any Israelite is a potential beneficiary of *safek bechor* through the mechanism of reciprocity, then nobody can ever testify, and an Israelite with a *safek bechor* could never *shecht* it. That would leave *ein lo takanta* — no remedy whatsoever. Yet the *Gemara* shows from an unambiguous *mishnah* that Rabbi Meir does permit *shechita* of a *safek bechor* in the case of *chalipha*.

### Class Suspicion

*Ella shema mina: ba'alim me'idin alav* — we must therefore conclude that even owners can testify. *Kohanim hu de-chashidei a-mumei, Yisrael lo chashidei a-mumei*. The operative principle is not "are you a potential beneficiary" but rather "are you a member of a class known to be compromised in this area." *Kohanim*, who are systematically immersed in the world of *bechor*, have demonstrated a pattern of taking liberties; they are distrusted as a class. *Yisraelim*, even when they have a direct stake in the outcome — even when it is their own *safek bechor* — are not suspect as a class. The ordinary person's commitment to *kashrut* and honest practice is presumed sufficient, even where self-interest is present.

This is a striking conclusion. Rabbi Meir's categorical rejection of *kohanim* does not derive from a theory of pure self-interest — if it did, it would equally disqualify any potential beneficiary. Rather, it derives from a sociological assessment: *kohanim* as a group have compromised themselves with respect to *bechorot*, and that class-level suspicion cannot be cured by strangers or numbers or circumstances. *Yisraelim* have not, and no circumstantial self-interest is sufficient to apply the same categorical exclusion to them.

### Chalipha Case

The *Gemara* concludes with a citation from the *mishnah* relevant to Rabbi Meir's view. *Chalifa be-yad Kohen patur min ha-matanot* — Rabbi Yosei rules that if one had two animals, one of which was a *bechor* and one of which was not, and traded one to a *kohen* without knowing which was which, the animal retained by the Israelite is exempt from the priestly gifts (*zero'a lechayayim ve-keiva*). *Ve-Rabbi Meir mechayev* — Rabbi Meir obligates. The fact that Rabbi Meir obligates in *matanot* in this scenario implies that the Israelite has been able to *shecht* his *safek bechor* and eat it, confirming that Rabbi Meir does indeed allow an Israelite owner to testify about his own *safek bechor* — consistent with the conclusion that his class-based suspicion applies only to *kohanim*.

The full implications of Rabbi Meir's position — in particular, whether a *kohen* afflicted with *tzara'as* can ever be declared *tahor* if we categorically distrust the testimony of *kohanim* — are an open and pointed question, which the *Gemara* will take up when the *daf* continues.
