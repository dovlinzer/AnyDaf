# Chagigah 24b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hand Contamination

### Kodesh vs Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַתְּרוּמָה אִם נִטְמְאוּ יָדַיִם — לֹא שְׁנִיּוֹת הֵן, אֲבָל בַּקֹּדֶשׁ — שְׁנִיּוֹת הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** And with regard to *teruma*, if one's hands became impure, they do not become impure to the second degree. But with regard to sacrificial food, the hands are impure to the second degree.

The daf opens with a further distinction between the laws governing *kodesh* and *terumah*. The case at hand concerns the purity of hands: if one hand becomes *tamei*, what is the status of the other? As a baseline, we assume that a person has washed both hands and has been careful not to allow them to touch anything problematic. If one hand then comes into contact with a source of rabbinic *tumah* — rendering it a *sheni* — what happens to the other?

The answer depends on whether we are dealing with *terumah* or *kodesh*. For *terumah*, the contamination of one hand does not affect the other. For *kodesh*, it does.

### Rashi's View

There is, however, a significant debate between Rashi and Tosfos about what precisely the *mishnah* means when it says that by *kodesh*, one hand renders the other *tamei*. According to Rashi, the case is one where hand one — the *tamei* hand — actually touches hand two. Normally, one *sheni* cannot make something else into a *sheni*; the *chiddush* of *kodesh* is that it can, but only through direct contact between the two hands.

## Chiburin Concept

### Chiburin Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁיזְבִי: בְּחִיבּוּרִין שָׁנוּ, אֲבָל שֶׁלֹּא בְּחִיבּוּרִין — לֹא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheizevi said:** When they said that with regard to sacrificial food a hand that is rendered impure renders the other hand impure as well, **they taught** this only for a situation **when** the pure hand is **in contact** with the sacrificial food when the impure hand touches it. **But** if the pure hand is **not in contact** with the sacrificial food, **no,** the pure hand is not rendered impure by touching the impure hand.

The Gemara introduces the principle of *chiburin* — connection — through the statement of Rav Shezbi: *be-chiburin shanu*, "we are only speaking of when there is connection." The contamination transfer between hands operates only when the hands are in some form of simultaneous contact or linkage; if they are fully separate, the contamination does not pass from one to the other.

## Hand Contamination (II)

### Tosfos's View

Tosfos goes further than Rashi. According to Tosfos, the *mishnah* means that as soon as one hand becomes *tamei*, both hands are automatically considered *tamei* — no touching between the two hands is required. Tosfos's reading of *chiburin* is correspondingly different. The classic example Tosfos employs is somewhat ironic: *sifrei kodesh*, sacred books, which rabbinic law treats as a source of hand-*tumah*. In Tosfos's framework, the scenario is this: hand one is touching the *sefer*, which causes it to become a *sheni*; simultaneously, hand two is touching hand one. The result is that hand two also becomes a *sheni* through this chain of simultaneous contact — not because hand one independently transmitted *tumah* to hand two, but because the entire circuit functions as a single extended act of touching, like electricity passing through a conductor. The *chiburin*, on Tosfos's reading, describes the condition under which the *tumah* is *received*: both hands must be in contact at the same moment.

For Rashi, by contrast, the *chiburin* describes the condition under which the *tumah* is *transferred*. Here, the *tahor* hand is already touching the *kodesh* object, and the *tamei* hand is simultaneously touching the *tahor* hand, thereby channeling the *tumah* through to the *kodesh*. The direction of travel is reversed from Tosfos's model, but the structural logic — one extended conducting act — is the same. Either way, the principle is that the transfer only works when the connection is live and simultaneous; once the hands are separate, the mechanism does not operate.

## Chiburin Concept (II)

### Rashi's Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: יָד נְגוּבָה מְטַמָּא חֲבֶירְתָּהּ לְטַמֵּא לַקֹּדֶשׁ, אֲבָל לֹא לַתְּרוּמָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לִפְסוֹל, אֲבָל לֹא לְטַמֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to** Rav Sheizevi from the following teaching: Even **a dry hand** that is impure **renders its counterpart,** i.e., the other hand, **impure, to** the extent that the second hand will now **render impure** any food that it touches. This is true with regard **to sacrificial** food **but not** with regard **to *teruma*.** This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says:** The second hand is not rendered impure to such a severe extent. It can merely **disqualify** sacrificial food that it touches, by rendering it impure to the fourth degree, **but not render** it **impure** with third-degree impurity.

### Gemara's Challenge

The Gemara's challenge to the *chiburin* principle is sharp. The *braisa* specifically highlights the novelty that a *yad neguvah*, a dry hand, has this power. But if we limit the entire case to one of simultaneous contact — where the conceptual logic is not that hand one independently made hand two *tamei*, but rather that they together constitute one extended touching — then why does dryness matter at all? The principle of *chiburin* treats the two hands as a single unified act; within that framework, there is no reason why the normal requirement of liquid to enable *tumah* transfer should apply. The *chiddush* of dryness only makes sense if the hands are *not* in simultaneous contact — if hand one became *tamei* on its own and then, as a *sheni*, proceeded to make hand two *tamei* independently. That would normally be impossible (a *sheni* does not make another *sheni*), and the *chiddush* would then be that by *kodesh*, even a dry *sheni* hand can accomplish this. But if we demand *chiburin* and explain the mechanism as extended touching, there is no novelty in saying the hand is dry — of course it can work dry, since dryness is irrelevant to an extended act of contact.

### Extended Touch Logic

The conceptual distinction between the two models is fundamental. If we say the mechanism requires simultaneous contact — A touching B touching C — then the logic is not that A rendered B *tamei* and B in turn rendered C *tamei*. Rather, the entire chain functions as if A is directly, albeit indirectly, touching C. The intermediate element is not an independent transmitter; it is part of the extended act. Within this model, the usual requirement of liquid for *tumah* transfer is beside the point, because we are not describing an independent transmission event at all. By contrast, if we say the mechanism operates even without simultaneous contact — hand one became *tamei*, hand two later touched hand one, and that is sufficient — then we are describing a genuine act of *tumah* transfer from one *sheni* to another, which is normally forbidden. The *chiddush* is precisely that *kodesh* allows this even when the second hand is dry.

## Lakish vs Yochanan

### Resh Lakish's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא יָדוֹ, אֲבָל יַד חֲבֵירוֹ — לֹא.
>
> **Translation:** **It was also stated** that *amora'im* disputed a similar issue: **Reish Lakish said: They taught** that one hand renders the other impure **only** if the second hand is **his** own **hand,** **but** an impure hand does **not** render impure **the hand of another.**

### Yochanan's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֶחָד יָדוֹ וְאֶחָד יַד חֲבֵירוֹ, בְּאוֹתָהּ הַיָּד. לִפְסוֹל, אֲבָל לֹא לְטַמֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** An impure hand renders another hand impure **whether it is his** own **hand or the hand of another,** provided the second hand is touched **by the same hand** that came into contact with the impurity. Moreover, the impure hand affects the food it touches only **to disqualify** it, **but not to render** it **impure.**

### Original Hand Power

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּאוֹתָהּ הַיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** provided the second hand is touched **by the same hand** that came into contact with the impurity.

Both Rabbi Yochanan and, ultimately, Resh Lakish agree on a second point, captured in the phrase *ba'otah hayad* — "in that same hand," meaning the original hand. This principle holds that only the hand which was originally *tamei* carries this special power to render another hand *tamei*. The second hand — which became *tamei* as a result of touching the first — does not inherit that power. So the original left hand, for example, can render the right hand *tamei*; but the right hand, now itself *tamei* as a derivative, cannot then go and render a third hand *tamei*. The power is vested in the original, not propagated through the chain.

## Secondary Hand Level

### Posel vs Metamei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִפְסוֹל אֲבָל לֹא לְטַמֵּא תַּנָּאֵי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this opinion, that an impure hand affects the sacrificial food only **to disqualify** it **but not to render** it **impure, is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.

Closely connected to the question of *ba'otah hayad* is the question of how *strong* the derived hand's *tumah* actually is. The *braisa* quoted by Rabbi Yose the son of Rabbi Yehuda had stated that a hand makes *kodesh pasul* but not *tamei* in the full sense. To understand this, one must recall the graduated levels of *tumah* applicable to *kodesh*: a *sheni* renders *kodesh* a *shelishi*, which can still pass on *tumah* to a *revii* — that is what it means to be *metamei kodesh*. If the derived hand is treated as a *sheni*, it would make *kodesh* a *shelishi* — and that is *metamei*. But if the derived hand is treated as a *shelishi* — a step weaker than the original — it would make *kodesh* only a *revii*, which ends the chain. That is *posel* without *metamei*. Rabbi Yose the son of Rabbi Yehuda's view, then, is that the second hand is not on a par with the first; it is weaker, a *shelishi* rather than a *sheni*.

## Lakish vs Yochanan (II)

### Resh Lakish Retracts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ הֲדַר בֵּיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָה אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אֶחָד יָדוֹ וְאֶחָד יַד חֲבֵירוֹ, בְּאוֹתָהּ הַיָּד, לִפְסוֹל אֲבָל לֹא לְטַמֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Reish Lakish, too, retracted** his own opinion in favor of Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion. For **Rabbi Yona said** that **Rabbi Ami said** that **Reish Lakish said:** The decree that one hand renders another impure applies **whether it is his** own **hand or the hand of another,** provided the second hand is touched **by the same hand** that came into contact with the impurity. Moreover, the impure hand affects the food it touches only **to disqualify** it, **but not to render** it **impure.**

The Gemara then resolves the dispute between Resh Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan: Resh Lakish ultimately retracted and agreed with Rabbi Yochanan that even another person's hand can be rendered *tamei* by the original *tamei* hand. The Gemara derives this from the fact that the *mishnah* repeats the statement that one hand can render its fellow *tamei* for *kodesh* but not for *terumah*. Since the first clause already established this principle, the repetition must be coming to add something — specifically, that the reach of this rule extends not only to one's own second hand but to another person's hand as well. When Resh Lakish came around, he stated the principle in full: whether it is one's own hand or another person's hand, the original *tamei* hand can make it *tamei* — *ba'otah hayad* — with the same caveat that only the original, not the derivative, has this power, and only to the degree of *posel* rather than *metamei* in the full sense.

The connection between these two questions — can it affect another person's hand, and is the second hand a *sheni* or *shelishi* — is not accidental. If the only valid mechanism is simultaneous contact, the whole process is best understood as a single extended act involving what functions as one agent. Under that reading, it is most natural to speak of both hands being one's own, and also most natural to treat the second hand as equivalent in strength to the first, since conceptually it is part of the same act. But once we say the mechanism can work even when the two hands are separate — first one became *tamei*, then it touched the other — then we are describing a genuine chain of transfer, and the second hand is derivative. Under that framework, it makes equal sense whether the second hand is yours or someone else's, since what matters is the transfer, not the extended unity. And under that framework, the second hand is logically weaker — a *shelishi* rather than a *sheni*. These two linked conclusions, that another person's hand can be affected and that the derived hand is a *shelishi*, are thus two sides of the same conceptual coin, and it is notable that when Resh Lakish recants on the first point, he also affirms the second.

## Secondary Hand Level (II)

### Tannaim Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: כׇּל הַפּוֹסֵל בִּתְרוּמָה — מְטַמָּא יָדַיִם לִהְיוֹת שְׁנִיּוֹת, וְיָד מְטַמָּא חֲבֶירְתָּהּ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יָדַיִם שְׁנִיּוֹת הֵן, וְאֵין שֵׁנִי עוֹשֶׂה שֵׁנִי בַּחוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna (*Yadayim* 3:2): **Anything that disqualifies *teruma*** by contact with it, i.e., anything that is impure at least to the second degree, **renders the hands impure** to the **second** degree. **And** furthermore, **a hand** that is impure to the second degree **renders its counterpart,** the other hand, **impure;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehoshua. But the Rabbis say: Hands** themselves **are** impure to the **second** degree, **and** that which is impure to the **second** degree **cannot impart** second-degree impurity to something else **with regard to non-sacred** food.

The Gemara then establishes that the question of whether the second hand is a *sheni* or *shelishi* is itself a *machloket* among *Tannaim*. The *mishnah* in another tractate teaches that anything which *posels terumah* also renders hands *tamei* to the level of *shniyot*, and that one hand can render its fellow *tamei* — in the words of the *mishnah*, *veyad metamei chavertah* — and this is the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua. The *Chachamim*, however, say that hands are *shniyot*, and a *sheni* does not make another *sheni*. The Gemara considers whether the *Chachamim* might be understood as saying not that the second hand acquires no *tumah* at all, but only that it acquires the status of a *shelishi* rather than a *sheni* — thus disagreeing with Rabbi Yehoshua only about the *degree* of the second hand's *tumah*, not about whether some *tumah* is transferred at all.

### Shelishi Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כִּי הָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי, דְּתַנְיָא: יָד נְגוּבָה מְטַמָּא אֶת חֲבֶירְתָּהּ לְטַמֵּא בַּקֹּדֶשׁ, אֲבָל לֹא לַתְּרוּמָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אוֹתָהּ יָד לִפְסוֹל, אֲבָל לֹא לְטַמֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the issue of which degree of impurity is imparted to the second hand is **like** the following dispute between ***tanna'im*, as it is taught** in the *baraita* cited earlier: Even **a dry hand** that is impure **renders its counterpart,** i.e., the other hand, **impure to** the extent that the second hand will now **render impure** food that it touches. This is true with regard **to sacrificial** food **but not** with regard **to *teruma*.** This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: The** second **hand** is not rendered impure to such a severe extent. It can merely **disqualify** sacrificial food that it touches, by making it impure to the fourth degree, **but not render** it **impure** to the third degree.

The Gemara, however, rejects this reading. Perhaps, it says, the *Chachamim* reject the entire principle — the second hand acquires nothing, not even the status of *shelishi*. The Gemara then turns to a *braisa* which states explicitly: *yad neguvah metamei chavertah letamei b'kodesh* — a dry hand renders its fellow *tamei* so that the second hand can make *kodesh tamei* — according to Rabbi Yose. And Rabbi Yose the son of Rabbi Yehuda says: it only makes the second hand *lifsol* but not *letamei* — meaning the second hand is a *shelishi*, not a *sheni*. This *braisa* maps precisely onto the Amoraic dispute: Rabbi Yose corresponds to the view that the second hand is a full *sheni* and can make *kodesh* a *shelishi*; Rabbi Yose the son of Rabbi Yehuda corresponds to the view that the second hand is only a *shelishi* and can make *kodesh* only a *revii*. The Gemara concludes that this is the *tannaitic* underpinning of the later debate.

## Hands & Food

### Dry Food Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹכְלִין אוֹכָלִים נְגוּבִין בְּיָדַיִם מְסוֹאָבוֹת — בַּתְּרוּמָה מֻתָּר, וּבַּקֹּדֶשׁ אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** One may eat dry foods, i.e., foods that have never been wetted and are thus not susceptible to impurity, **with impure hands** in the case of *teruma*, but not in the case of sacrificial food.

The *mishnah* adds another distinction between *kodesh* and *terumah*: even for dry food — produce that has never come into contact with liquid and is therefore not susceptible to *tumah* — one must wash one's hands before handling *kodesh*, but not before handling *terumah*.

### Chanana's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס: וְכִי יֵשׁ נְגוּבָה לַקֹּדֶשׁ? וַהֲלֹא חִיבַּת הַקֹּדֶשׁ מַכְשַׁרְתָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that there is a difficulty with this statement of the mishna: **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus said: Now, is there** such a possibility as **dry** food, i.e., food that is not susceptible to impurity, with regard **to sacrificial** food? **Is it not so that the reverence** accorded **to sacrificial** food itself **renders it fit** to contract impurity even if it has never been in contact with liquid at all?

### Eating Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, כְּגוֹן שֶׁתָּחַב לוֹ חֲבֵירוֹ לְתוֹךְ פִּיו, אוֹ שֶׁתָּחַב הוּא לְעַצְמוֹ בְּכוּשׁ וּבְכַרְכֵּר, וּבִיקֵּשׁ לֶאֱכוֹל צְנוֹן וּבָצָל שֶׁל חוּלִּין עִמָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **No,** it is **necessary** for the mishna to state this. And the case is not one of dry sacrificial food, as was first assumed. Rather, the mishna is dealing with a case in **which, for example, his friend inserted** sacrificial food or *teruma* **into his mouth, or,** alternatively, a case in **which he inserted** it **into his own** mouth **by** means of **a spindle or whorl,** which are wooden vessels without receptacles that cannot contract impurity. In either event the food arrives in his mouth without being rendered impure by his impure hands. **And** then, while the sacrificial food or *teruma* is still in his mouth, he **wishes to** put into his mouth and **eat a non-sacred radish or onion along with them.**

### Gezeira Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְקֹדֶשׁ גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן, לִתְרוּמָה לָא גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבַּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Regarding sacrificial food the Sages enacted a decree against** doing so, lest the person inadvertently touch the sacrificial food in his mouth with his impure hand. This decree, the mishna teaches, applies only to sacrificial food, **but in** a case in which he had ***teruma*** in his mouth **the Sages did not enact a decree against it.**

The Gemara resolves this by reframing the case. The ruling is not about directly touching dry *kodesh* with unwashed hands — that, as Rabbi Chanana correctly notes, is obvious. Rather, the case is this: a person is eating *kodesh* meat — say, *shlamim* — in a situation where the meat was placed in his mouth without his hands touching it, either by someone else placing it there or by using a wooden skewer (*kush b'karkar*) that has no *beit kibul* and is therefore *tahor*. No hands touched the *kodesh*. Now this person wants to eat some *chullin* — onions or radish, for instance — that has never been made wet and is therefore not susceptible to *tumah*. His hands are *tamei* as a *sheni*, but the dry *chullin* produces no *tumah* issue. Can he use his hands to place the dry *chullin* in his mouth while *kodesh* is already there?

The answer is that for *kodesh*, the Sages decreed that he may not do so. The concern is that in the process of feeding himself the dry *chullin*, he might inadvertently touch the *kodesh* meat in his mouth. Even though this seems remote, the Sages were more protective around *kodesh* and enacted this *gezeira*. For *terumah*, however, no such *gezeira* was enacted — one may feed oneself dry *chullin* while chewing *terumah* without concern that the hands will touch the *terumah* in the mouth. This is a further illustration of the asymmetry between the two categories: in some respects *kodesh* is treated more stringently than *terumah* even though in other respects it appears more lenient.

## Status Changes

### Onen & Mechusar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹנֵן וּמְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים כּוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמָא — כֵּיוָן דְּעַד הָאִידָּנָא הֲווֹ אֲסִירִי, אַצְרְכִינְהוּ רַבָּנַן טְבִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **An acute mourner and one who is lacking atonement** require immersion in order to eat sacrificial food, but this is not necessary for *teruma*. The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** for this decree? **Since until now it was prohibited** for **them** to partake of sacrificial food and they therefore might not have guarded themselves properly from impurity, **the Sages required them** to undergo **immersion** before eating sacrificial food.

The *mishnah* then discusses two further cases requiring immersion in a *mikvah* before eating *kodesh* but not before eating *terumah*: the *onen* — a person who has lost a close relative and whose dead has not yet been buried — and the *mechusar kipurim* — a person who has completed the process of purification but has not yet brought the final sacrifice that would fully restore his status.

### Mikvah Requirement

Both the *onen* and the *mechusar kipurim* may eat *terumah* even before immersing. But before eating *kodesh*, they must first immerse in a *mikvah*.

### Status Logic

The Gemara explains the reasoning with a striking conceptual point. The change in status for both of these individuals is triggered by something external to the person: the *onen*'s status changes because the burial occurs, and the *mechusar kipurim*'s status changes because a *korban* is brought in the *Beit HaMikdash*. In each case, an event outside the person's body alters his standing. Yet the consequence — that he is now permitted to eat *kodesh* — is a change in his own personal status. Because something external has changed, producing a new personal status for the individual, the Sages required an act that works on the person's own body — immersion in a *mikvah* — to formally mark and effectuate that new status. This logic is particularly elegant: the external event alone is insufficient; there must be a corresponding internal transformation, an act done to the body, to bring the person's physical state in line with his changed standing.

One participant in the shiur noted a striking irony inherent in this entire system: a non-kohen who is ritually impure can never in practice eat *terumah*, yet that same person, as a member of the community who has brought his *shlamim*, may eat the meat of his *korban* in Jerusalem — and that is *kodesh*. The higher category is paradoxically accessible to him in a way the lower one is not.

## Belief in Testimony

### Yehuda's Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חוֹמֶר בַּתְּרוּמָה, שֶׁבִּיהוּדָה נֶאֱמָנִין עַל טׇהֳרַת יַיִן וָשֶׁמֶן כׇּל יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה, וּבִשְׁעַת הַגִּיתּוֹת וְהַבַּדִּים — אַף עַל הַתְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The previous mishna listed stringencies that apply to sacrificial food but not to *teruma*. However, there are also **stringencies** that apply **to *teruma*** over sacrificial food: **In Judea** all people, even people who are not generally meticulous in their observance of the *halakhot* of ritual purity [*amei ha'aretz*], **are trusted with regard to the purity of** consecrated **wine and oil** throughout **all the days of the year. And during the period of the winepress and olive press,** when grapes and olives are pressed and made into wine and oil, respectively, they are trusted **even with regard to** the purity of ***teruma,** as all people, including *amei ha'aretz*, purify their vessels for this season.

The *mishnah* transitions to a new topic: the degree to which we believe *amei ha'aretz* — non-scholars — regarding the purity of their produce. The section is introduced under the heading *chomer b'terumah* — a stringency of *terumah* — because, as will become clear, *terumah* is treated more strictly than *kodesh* in this domain: the *am ha'aretz* is believed year-round regarding *kodesh*, but only seasonally regarding *terumah*. This is one of the running inversions of the daf.

Rabbi Yehuda teaches that in Judea — the region that includes Jerusalem — *amei ha'aretz* are believed regarding the purity of their wine and oil designated as *kodesh* throughout the entire year. This leniency is grounded in two considerations. First, there is a policy motivation: if we were to refuse to accept their *kodesh* produce, these individuals would be entirely excluded from participation in the *Beit HaMikdash*, potentially driving them to erect their own alternative system. Second, there is the implicit assumption that people are indeed more scrupulous with what they designate as *kodesh*, knowing it is destined for the Temple. Both motivations converge to produce a year-round policy of trust regarding *kodesh*.

### Terumah Season

For *terumah*, however, the *am ha'aretz* is generally not believed. We cannot trust him to have maintained the purity of *terumah* throughout the year. Yet the *mishnah* identifies a seasonal exception: during *bish'at hagitot v'habadim* — the time of the pressing of the grapes and the pressing of the olives — they are believed even for *terumah*. Rashi explains that this is because during the pressing season it is a communal experience: everyone brings their *kelim* to the *mikvah*, everything is immersed and rendered *tahor*, and then the pressing begins. The process of producing the oil and wine is thus naturally preceded by a collective purification effort, making it reasonable to assume that what is produced at that time is indeed *tahor*.

Rashi adds, later in his commentary, a second dimension: policy considerations. The pressing season is when the overwhelming majority of *terumah* wine and oil is produced and distributed. The *amei ha'aretz* were far more numerous than the *chaverim* and far more engaged in agriculture. To refuse to accept *terumah* during the very season when almost all *terumah* is produced would effectively eliminate the practical viability of *terumah* for *kohanim*. Both considerations — the facts on the ground and the overriding policy interest — support the seasonal leniency.

It is worth pausing on the broader portrait of the *am ha'aretz* that this discussion implies. These were not people who were generally non-observant. They were punctilious about *Shabbat*, *kashrus*, and most areas of *halacha*. The specific concerns about them related to the fine details of *tumah v'taharah* and, in some cases, the separation of *ma'asrot* — which involved giving up as much as twenty percent of one's agricultural income. It would be a mistake to generalize from those concerns into a broader characterization of their religious commitment.

### Deferral Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָבְרוּ הַגִּיתּוֹת וְהַבַּדִּים, וְהֵבִיאוּ לוֹ חָבִית שֶׁל יַיִן שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה — לֹא יְקַבְּלֶנָּה מִמֶּנּוּ, אֲבָל מַנִּיחָהּ לַגַּת הַבָּאָה. וְאִם אָמַר לוֹ הִפְרַשְׁתִּי לְתוֹכָהּ רְבִיעִית קֹדֶשׁ — נֶאֱמָן.
>
> **Translation:** But once **the** periods of the **winepress and olive press have passed,** if *amei ha'aretz* **brought to him,** i.e., to a priest who is meticulous concerning the *halakhot* of ritual purity [*ḥaver*], a **barrel of *teruma* wine, he may not accept it from them,** as *amei ha'aretz* are not trusted with regard to matters of ritual purity during the rest of the year. **But** the giver **may leave it** over **for the following winepress** season, in the following year, at which point the *ḥaver* priest may accept it from him, although it was prohibited for him to accept the same barrel beforehand. **And if** the giver **said to** the priest: **I separated** and placed **into** this barrel of *teruma* **a quarter**-*log* **of sacrificial** wine or oil, **he is trusted** with regard to the entire contents of the barrel. Since an *am ha'aretz* is trusted with regard to the purity of sacrificial food, he is also believed with regard to *teruma* that is mingled with the sacrificial food.

Once the pressing season has passed, a *kohen* who is presented with a barrel of *terumah* wine by an *am ha'aretz* cannot accept it. The question of when it was produced is irrelevant; what matters is the moment the claim is being made. If the season has passed and the *am ha'aretz* comes to the *kohen* saying "here is my *terumah*," the *kohen* must decline. The practical solution offered by the *mishnah* is *manicha l'gat habaa* — leave it aside until the next pressing season. At that point, the same barrel can be presented again, and the *kohen* can accept it. The rule is deliberately simple and predictable precisely to avoid requiring *kohanim* to adjudicate the provenance and timing of every barrel.

There is, however, a way out within the current season. If the *am ha'aretz* says that from this very barrel of wine he had already separated out a *revii* — a small measure — for *kodesh*, for *nesachim*, then the *kohen* can accept the entire barrel. Since we believe the *am ha'aretz* year-round regarding *kodesh*, and he has stated that part of this barrel was designated and treated as *kodesh*, that testimony extends credibility to the purity of the whole barrel.

## Mixed Produce

### Mixed Belief

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּדֵּי יַיִן וְכַדֵּי שֶׁמֶן הַמְדוּמָּעוֹת — נֶאֱמָנִין עֲלֵיהֶם בִּשְׁעַת הַגִּיתּוֹת וְהַבַּדִּים, וְקוֹדֶם לַגִּיתּוֹת שִׁבְעִים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Jugs of wine and jugs of oil** **that are mingled,** *amei ha'aretz* **are trusted with regard to them during the period of the winepress and the olive press, and** also up to **seventy days before the winepress,** for that is when people begin to purify their vessels in preparation for the wine-pressing season.

A further case: barrels of wine and oil that are *midumaot* — mixed, meaning the *terumah* has not been separated from the *chullin*, or *terumah* has been mixed back in with *chullin*. The *mishnah* says such mixed barrels are believed during the pressing season, parallel to the standard rule for *terumah* itself.

### Pre-Pressing Period

The *mishnah* adds that for this category of mixed produce, the period of credibility extends to seventy days prior to the actual pressing — *v'kodem l'gitot shivim yom*. This is a meaningful extension: it begins more than two months before the pressing season itself. Whether this extension also applies retroactively to the general *terumah* credibility rule discussed earlier, or whether it is specific to the case of mixed produce, is a question the Gemara engages with.

## Geographic Limits

### Samaritan Strip

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בִּיהוּדָה אִין, וּבַגָּלִיל לָא, מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁרְצוּעָה שֶׁל כּוּתִים מַפְסֶקֶת בֵּינֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that *amei ha'aretz* are trusted with regard to the purity of sacrificial wine and oil in Judea. The Gemara infers: **In Judea, yes, but in the Galilee, no. What is the reason** for this distinction between the two places? **Reish Lakish said:** It is **because a strip** of land inhabited **by Samaritans [*Kutim*] separates between** Judea and the Galilee, and it is impossible to travel from one land to the other without traversing this strip.

Rabbi Yehuda's leniency — that *amei ha'aretz* are believed about their *kodesh* year-round — applies specifically in Judea. It does not apply in the Galilee. The reason, as Resh Lakish explains, is that a strip of Samaritan-inhabited territory — *retzu'ah shel Kusim* — separates the Galilee from Judea. This strip, though geographically within the borders of Eretz Yisrael, carries the halachic status of *eretz ha'amim* — non-Jewish territory — because it is not inhabited by Jews.

### Eretz Ha'amim

The halachic implications of *eretz ha'amim* are severe. The ground of non-Jewish territory is treated as carrying *tumah* equivalent to *tumas meis* — the Sages understood that bodies of the dead were buried throughout non-Jewish territory. Anyone or anything passing through such territory becomes *tamei*. This creates an insurmountable problem for the Galilee: even if an *am ha'aretz* there has maintained his *kodesh* wine and oil in perfect *tahara*, there is no way to transport it to the *Beit HaMikdash* in Jerusalem. To get there, one must traverse the Samaritan strip. The entire rationale for believing the *am ha'aretz* about *kodesh* year-round is the need to enable delivery to the *Beit HaMikdash*. If delivery is impossible regardless, there is no basis for the year-round leniency.

### Transport Failure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵיתֵיב בְּשִׁידָּה תֵּיבָה וּמִגְדָּל? הָא מַנִּי — רַבִּי הִיא, דְּאָמַר: אֹהֶל זָרוּק — לָאו שְׁמֵיהּ אֹהֶל. דְּתַנְיָא: הַנִּכְנָס לְאֶרֶץ הָעַמִּים בְּשִׁידָּה תֵּיבָה וּמִגְדָּל, רַבִּי מְטַמֵּא, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מְטַהֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **And let** the residents of the Galilee **place** the wine and oil and transport it to Judea **in a** closed **box, a chest, or a closet,** whose contents cannot contract impurity, as they have the status of separate tents. The Gemara answers: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who said: A thrown tent,** i.e., a moving tent, **is not called a** proper **tent,** and therefore its contents are subject to impurity. In our case, then, the contents would contract the impurity decreed upon the lands of non-Jewish nations. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: Concerning **one who enters a land of non-Jewish nations** sitting **in a box, a chest, or a closet, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **declares** him to be **impure, and Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, declares** him to be **pure.**

The Gemara considers whether creative solutions might circumvent the problem of passing through *eretz ha'amim*. Could one not transport the *kodesh* in a box, chest, or cabinet (*migdal, shidah, teivah*) — containers that would keep the *kodesh* suspended above the ground, never in contact with the contaminated earth? The Gemara answers that this fails because of the principle that *ohel zaruk lav shemei ohel* — a "thrown" canopy, meaning a structure that is not rooted in the ground, does not have the status of an *ohel*. A proper *ohel* intercepts *tumah* that rises from below and prevents it from spreading upward to whatever is inside. But if the structure carrying the *kodesh* is itself suspended — not anchored in the ground — then it is not an *ohel* in the halachic sense, and the *tumah* of *eretz ha'amim* below passes through it and reaches the *kodesh* inside. This is the view of Rebbi; Rabbi Yose bar Yehudah disagrees and would rule the person *tahor*, but according to Rebbi the transportation by box fails. A *braisa* confirms: one who enters *eretz ha'amim* inside a chest or cabinet — Rebbi rules *tamei*, Rabbi Yose bar Yehudah rules *tahor*.

The relevance of this principle to the modern world was noted: an airplane flying over a cemetery presents the same question of *ohel zaruk*. Is the airplane, suspended in the air and not rooted in the ground, an *ohel* that intercepts the *tumah* rising from the graves below, or does the *tumah* pass through?

### Kodesh Sealing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלַיְיתוּהּ בִּכְלִי חֶרֶס הַמּוּקָּף צָמִיד פָּתִיל! אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, שׁוֹנִין: אֵין הַקֹּדֶשׁ נִיצּוֹל בְּצָמִיד פָּתִיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a further difficulty: **And let them bring** oil and wine to the Temple **in an earthenware vessel sealed** with **a tightly bound cover,** which cannot contract impurity even if it is in the same tent as a corpse, as it states: "And every open vessel, which has no covering tightly bound upon it, is unclean" (Numbers 19:15). **Rabbi Eliezer said:** The Sages **taught** in a *baraita*: **Sacrificial** food, unlike other items, is **not spared** from impurity **by** being in a container with **a tightly bound cover.**

The Gemara then considers another potential solution: transporting the *kodesh* in a pottery vessel sealed with a *tzamid pasil* — a tight seal over the opening. The halachic principle is that a pottery vessel (*kli cheres*) becomes *tamei* only through its internal airspace; *tumah* enters through the opening. A *tzamid pasil* — a seal over the mouth of the vessel — prevents *tumah* from entering the airspace and thus protects the contents. The Gemara cites Rabbi Elazar who teaches: *ein kodesh nitzol b'tzamid pasil* — *kodesh* cannot be protected by a tight seal. This is another stringency unique to *kodesh*.

The Gemara challenges this from a *mishnah* which states that the water of the *Parah Adumah* — *chatas* water — cannot be protected by a *tzamid pasil*. One might infer from this that only the *chatas* water, the highest level, lacks this protection, while ordinary *kodesh* retains it. The Gemara rejects the inference: the *mishnah* about *chatas* water is speaking of water that has not yet been fully sanctified by mixing in the ashes. Water that has been fully sanctified — and indeed *kodesh* generally — also cannot be protected by the *tzamid pasil*. The seal is effective only for *chullin* and *terumah*, not for *kodesh* or *kodashim*. Accordingly, the sealed pottery vessel is likewise no solution for transporting *kodesh* through *eretz ha'amim*.

### Eliyahu Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר עוּלָּא: חַבְרַיָּיא מְדַכַּן בְּגָלִילָא! מַנִּיחִין, לִכְשֶׁיָּבֹא אֵלִיָּהוּ וִיטַהֲרֶנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another difficulty: **But didn't Ulla say: *Ḥaverim* purify** their wine and oil, i.e., they produce their wine and oil by the standards of purity used for sacrificial food **in the Galilee,** to be used for sacrificial purposes? This indicates that there must have been some way of transporting them from the Galilee to the Temple, for otherwise why would they have prepared such items? The Gemara answers: Indeed, they could not transfer these items to the Temple. Rather, they would **leave** them in their place, **and** their thought was that **when Elijah comes** in messianic times **and purifies** the road from Galilee to Judea, these items will become eligible for use.

The Gemara concludes with a remark from Ulla that the *chaverim* — the scrupulous scholars — in the Galilee do in fact purify their *kodesh* produce. The obvious question is why, given that there is no *Beit HaMikdash* in Ulla's time (several generations after the destruction) and, even setting that aside, no way to transport the *kodesh* from the Galilee to Jerusalem. The Gemara's answer is both pragmatic and eschatological: *manichin osa ad she-yavo Eliyahu v'yit'harenu* — they set it aside until Eliyahu HaNavi comes and resolves the problem. When Eliyahu HaNavi arrives to herald the final redemption, he will clarify how *kodesh* from the Galilee can be brought to the *Beit HaMikdash* without becoming *tamei*.

The two *chiddushim* of this entire section thus emerge in sharp relief. First, *kodesh* is treated more leniently than *terumah* when it comes to trusting the *am ha'aretz* — the holier the object, the more inclusive the policy, precisely because the stakes of exclusion are so much higher. But second, this leniency is tightly bounded by geography: it applies only in Judea, where the *am ha'aretz* has real access to the *Beit HaMikdash* and there is an actual need to accept his *kodesh*. For *terumah*, which does not require the *Beit HaMikdash*, the geographic restriction does not apply in the same way, but the temporal restriction — the pressing season — applies wherever one is.
