# Shekalim 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shekalim in Daf Yomi

### Why Shekalim in Daf Yomi

We begin *Masechet Shekalim*, which we have in the *Yerushalmi*. A natural question presents itself: why is *Shekalim* included in *Daf Yomi* at all, given that it exists only as a *Yerushalmi* tractate? The answer is likely twofold. First, *Shekalim* is printed within the *Bavli*, and there is no other *Yerushalmi* tractate that receives this treatment. Second — and this may explain both why it appears in the *Bavli* and why it was included in *Daf Yomi* — *Shekalim* is the one tractate in *Seder Moed* that is otherwise unrepresented in the *Bavli*. Unlike *Seder Zeraim*, where only *Brachot* has *Bavli* coverage, *Shekalim* is the single omission in *Moed*, and being a short tractate, it may have been included for the sake of completion.

### Order of Masechtot

*Shekalim* appears after *Yoma* in the ordering of tractates within *Seder Moed*. The standard principle of organization is by the number of *perakim*, which generally — though not always — aligns with thematic and calendrical sequence. *Shabbat* and *Eruvin* come first naturally; *Pesachim* follows, since the verse *HaChodesh hazeh lachem* establishes *Nissan* as primary. *Yoma*, covering *Yom Kippur*, does not fit cleanly into that sequence, which is why the *Bavli* effectively reorders things so that *Rosh Hashanah*, *Yoma*, and *Sukkah* follow one another in the order of the *Tishrei* festivals. In truth, however, the structural order has *Yoma* coming before *Shekalim* simply because *Yoma* has more *perakim*. The reason *Shekalim* is nonetheless placed near *Pesach* in our study is probably because its subject matter — the collection of the half-shekel — begins on *Rosh Chodesh Adar* and is directed toward *Rosh Chodesh Nissan*, placing it squarely in the *Pesach* thematic orbit.

### Yerushalmi References

A practical note on referencing the *Yerushalmi*: citations are typically given either by *perek* and *halachah*, or by *amud* according to the original Venice printing. In that printing, each side of a page contained two columns, meaning that an *amud* designated a column — not a side. References to, say, 63c would designate column three of a given page, and columns could run up to *dalet* on each physical page. For our purposes, we will use the *Bavli* pagination format since that is how the tractate is organized before us.

It should also be noted that the *girsah* — the textual reading — of the *Yerushalmi* is considerably less stable than that of the *Bavli*. This is largely because the *Yerushalmi* was studied far less, and we effectively have only one complete manuscript: the Leiden manuscript. Other fragments exist, but the survival of this entire corpus rested on that single witness. This does not mean it is necessarily accurate — it is simply what we have. The instability of the text sometimes results from scribes who did not fully understand the *Yerushalmi*'s Aramaic and adjusted the text accordingly; at other times, it reflects genuine uncertainty about the correct reading.

### Roman Tax Parallels

Before turning to the *Gemara* itself, it is worth noting a historical parallel that illuminates why *Masechet Shekalim* was particularly relevant to *Eretz Yisrael*. Under Roman rule, there existed a form of annual taxation with some similarity to the half-shekel collection. Beyond the Roman Jewish tax, there was also a tax collected by the *Nasi* — the Patriarch — from Jewish communities not only within *Yisrael* but from the diaspora as well. This parallel between the ancient *shekalim* collection and the later institutional taxation of the Jewish community is worth bearing in mind as we work through this tractate.

## Adar Activities

### Road & Mikva Repair

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְתַקְּנִין אֶת הַדְּרָכִים וְאֶת הָרְחוֹבוֹת וְאֶת מִקְווֹת הַמַּיִם וְעוֹשִׂין כָּל צוֹרְכֵי הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** "One repairs the roads, and the streets, and water pools, and one looks after all public needs."

After the opening discussion of collecting the *shekalim* beginning on *Rosh Chodesh Adar*, the *Mishnah* continues: *metaknin es hadrachim ve'es harechovot ve'es mikva'ot hamayim ve'osim kol tzorchei harabbim* — also in *Adar*, one fixes the roads and the alleys and the *mikva'ot*, and performs all the communal needs. The rationale is clear: the winter rainy season is drawing to a close, spring is approaching, and people will soon be traveling up *laregel* for the *yom tov*. The roads need to be cleared and repaired after the rains, and the *mikva'ot* — which were essentially holes in the ground collecting rainwater, not the refined structures we have today — need to be cleared of mud and debris that would render them unusable.

### Tzorchei Rabbim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵילּוּ הֵן צוֹרְכֵי הָרַבִּים. דָּנִין דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת וְדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת דִּינֵי מַכּוֹת וּפוֹדִין עֲרָכִין וַחֲרָמִין וְהֶקְדֵּישׁוֹת וּמַשְׁקִין אֵת הַסּוֹטָה וְשׂוֹרְפִין אֶת הַפָּרָה וְעוֹרְפִין עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה וְרוֹצְעִין עֶבֶד עִבְרִי וּמְטָהֲרִין אֵת הַמְּצוֹרָע וּמְפָרְקִין אֶת הַמִּנְעָל מֵעַל גַּבֵּי הָאֵימוּם וְאֵין מַחֲזִירִין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** "The following are the public needs: One judges civil suits, and capital crimes, and cases of flogging, and one redeems valuations, and bans, and dedications, one lets the suspected adulteress drink, and burns the Cow, and one breaks the neck of the calf whose neck was to be broken, and one pierces the ear of a Hebrew slave, and one purifies the sufferer from skin disease, and removes the shoe from the block but one may not return it."

The *Gemara* now quotes a *Mishnah* from *Moed Katan*: *eilu hein tzorchei harabbim* — these are the communal needs. This is a separate use of the category of *tzorchei harabbim*, and in *Moed Katan* the context is things that may be done on *Chol HaMoed*. The *Yerushalmi* quotes it here because of the thematic parallel, but this should not lead us to conclude that the following list consists of activities specific to *Adar*.

The *Mishnah* in *Moed Katan* lists: *danim dinei mamonot* — one may hold civil courts on *Chol HaMoed*; *udinei nefashot* — courts dealing with capital matters; *udinei makot* — or cases of lashes. The court system, in other words, continues to function. *Ufodin erchin vecharamin vehekdeshot* — one may also perform various transactions related to the *Beit HaMikdash*: redeeming property that has been consecrated. A brief note on terminology: *hekdesh* sanctifies the object itself; *cherem* is a form of dedication whose beneficiary — the *Kohanim* or the *Beit HaMikdash* — is a matter of some dispute; *erchin* involves a fixed value paid based on the age and gender of a person one designates. All these transactional activities relating to the *Beit HaMikdash* may be performed on *Chol HaMoed*.

*Umashkin es hasota* — one may also administer the *sotah* waters. This is somewhat striking, since it raises the question of how the *sotah* procedure fits into the category of *tzorchei harabbim*. The *Gemara* in *Sota* grapples with how much of the *sotah* procedure is to be understood as a legal process as opposed to a ritual performed in the *Beit HaMikdash*. *Usorfin es hapara* — one burns the *parah adumah* if ashes are needed. *Veorfin egla arufa* — one performs the *egla arufa* ceremony, which is clearly a communal concern. *Verotzin eved Ivri* — one pierces the ear of a Hebrew slave who wishes to become a permanent bondsman. This last item is somewhat puzzling, since it seems to be a private concern. It does involve the slave's household — *ishto ve'es banav* — so it affects multiple people, but why it qualifies as *tzorchei harabbim* is not entirely clear. *Umetarin es hametzora* — one performs the purification process for a *metzora*, which is a communal concern since one does not want a *metzora* remaining within the community.

### Why Quote Moed Katan

A question presents itself: the *Gemara* quotes this *Mishnah* from *Moed Katan* but does not develop it further or explain what it adds to our *Mishnah* in *Shekalim*. The reason the *Yerushalmi* introduces this *Mishnah* appears to be specifically because of its concluding items: *metzaynin es hakevarot* — designating graves — and *veyotzin al hakilayim* — going out to uproot *kilayim*. These are precisely the same two items with which our *Mishnah* in *Shekalim* concludes: *umetzaynin es hakevarot veyotzin af al hakilayim*. The citation is thus meant to illuminate those two specific *mitzvot* mentioned in our *Mishnah* and their parallel appearance in the context of *Chol HaMoed*.

### Graves & Kilayim

This parallel, however, immediately raises a difficulty. Our *Mishnah* instructs that one designates graves and uproots *kilayim* in *Adar*, and the *Mishnah* in *Moed Katan* says one does these same things on *Chol HaMoed*. But *Chol HaMoed Pesach* comes a full month after *Adar*. If it was already done in *Adar*, why must it be done again on *Chol HaMoed*? The *Gemara* will address this question in detail as we turn to the two topics in sequence.

## Marking Graves

### Rain Washes Away Marks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְצַיְּנִין עַל הַקְּבָרוֹת. לֹא כְבָר צִיְינוּ מֵאֲדָר. תִּיפְתָּר שֶׁיָּרַד שֶׁטֶף שֶׁלְגְּשָׁמִים וּשְׁטָפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** "One marks the graves." Were they not already marked in Adar? Explain it if there was a flood caused by rain which rinsed it off.

The *Gemara* asks directly: *lo kevar tzeinu me'Adar* — did we not already designate the graves back in *Adar*? Why would it be necessary to do so again on *Chol HaMoed*? The answer offered is: *tipatar she'yarad shetef shel geshamim ve'shafah* — one can explain the case as one where a heavy downpour of rain came and washed away the designations. The markers were made with lime painted on stones or on the ground, and a powerful rain could obliterate them entirely. If the rains were particularly heavy and prolonged, then even though the graves were marked in *Adar*, they might need to be marked again by *Chol HaMoed Nisan*.

A note on terminology: *tipatar* is the *Yerushalmi*'s way of contextualizing a teaching — explaining it as applying to a specific circumstance. It functions similarly to the *Bavli*'s *hacha bemai askinan*, though not identically. Both are means of establishing a particular contextual reading to resolve an apparent difficulty.

### Calendar & Rain Factors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיוֹצְאִין אַף עַל הַכִּלְאָיִם. לֹא כְבָר יָצְאוּ בַּאֲדָר. תִּיפְתָּר שֶׁהָיְתָה הַשָּׁנָה אֲפֵילָה וְאֵין הַצְּמָחִין נִיכָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** "And also one goes to inspect for kilayim. Did they not already go in Adar? Explain it if the year was late and the plants not recognizable."

A similar question is then asked about *kilayim*: *lo kevar yatzu me'Adar* — did they not already go out in *Adar* to uproot the errant sprouts? The answer here is: *tipatar she'haya hashana afeila ve'ein hatzmachim nikarim* — the year was *afeila*, and the sprouts were not yet visible. *Afeila* can mean dark, suggesting prolonged overcast or rainy conditions that obscured the sprouts. But it more likely refers to the calendar being out of alignment — the solar and agricultural seasons had not yet fully synchronized with the calendar. If the year had not been properly intercalated, *Adar* might fall earlier than its proper spring position, meaning that the sprouts had not yet emerged by the time the *Adar* inspection was conducted. Only by *Chol HaMoed Nisan*, when the season had genuinely warmed, did the sprouts become visible. This is also why the *Gemara* will shortly discuss the relationship between *ibbur hashanah* — adding a month to the year — and the agricultural calendar.

Our *Mishnah* uses the phrase *kol tzorchei harabbim*, which evokes the *Moed Katan* list of *tzorchei harabbim* for *Chol HaMoed*. The *Gemara* quotes that *Mishnah* but does not elaborate on it further in the context of *Adar*, since these are activities performed throughout the year, not uniquely in *Adar*.

### Ezekiel Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנַיִין לְצִיּוּן. רִבִּי בֶּרֶכְיָה רִבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר בַּת יַעֲקֹב בְשֵׁם רִבִּי חוּנְיָיא דִבְרַת חַוְורָן רִבִּי יוֹסֵה אָמְרֵי לָהּ רִבִּי יָעֲקֹב בַּר אָחָא בְשֵׁם רִבִּי חוּנְיָיא דִבְרַת חַוְורָן רִבִּי חִזְקִיָּה רִבִּי עוּזִּיאֵל בְּרֵיהּ דְּרִבִּי חוּנְיָיה דְּבֵית חַוְורָן בְּשֵׁם רִבִּי חוּנְיָיא דְּבֵית חַוְורָן. טָמֵא טָמֵא יִקְרָא. כְּדֵי שֶׁתְּהֵא הַטּוּמְאָה קוֹרְאָה לָךְ בְּפִיהָ וְאוֹמֶרֶת לָךְ. פְּרוֹשׁ. רִבִּי אִילָא בְשֵׁם רִבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָן. וְעָבְרוּ הָעוֹבְרִים בָּאָרֶץ וְרָאָה עֶצֶם אָדָם וּבָנָה אֶצְלוֹ צִיּוּן.
>
> **Translation:** "From where about marks? Rebbi Berekhiah, Rebbi Jacob the son of the daughter of Jacob, in the name of Rebbi Onias from Hauran. Rebbi Yose said it, Rebbi Jacob bar Aḥa in the name of Rebbi Onias from Hauran. Rebbi Ḥizqiah, Rebbi Uziel the son of Rebbi Onias from Hauran in the name of Rebbi Onias from Hauran: *impure, impure, he shall call out*; the impurity itself has to call out and say to you: go away! Rebbi Hila in the name of Rebbi Samuel bar Naḥman: *The emissaries shall crisscross the land; if one sees a bone of a human he builds a sign near it*."

Having established that grave designation is required both in *Adar* and potentially again on *Chol HaMoed*, the *Gemara* asks: *minayin l'tziyun* — what is the scriptural source for designating graves at all?

The tradition is transmitted through a chain of *Amoraim* — there is some textual uncertainty about the exact names involved, with different *girsaot* giving *Rav Yaakov bar Bas Yaakov* in the name of *Rav Chunya de'Bas Chavrin*, or *Rav Yaakov bar Acha* in the name of *Chunya de'Bas Chavrin*, or *Rav Uziel brei de'Rav Chunya de'Beis Chovrin*. The place name — *Beis Chovrin*, *Bas Chavrin*, and similar variants — appears to refer to a location in the *Negev*, possibly near *Nachal Chaverim* in the area of *Sde Boker*. It is striking that the *Tanna* may well have originated from that region.

One source brought for the designation of graves is the verse relating to the *metzora*: *ve'tamei tamei yikra* — "tamei, tamei, he shall call out." This is interpreted to mean that *tumah* itself announces its own presence — *kedei she'tehei hatumah koreiah befiha*, so that the impurity cries out to warn people away: *ve'omeret lach perosh*, "it says to you: separate yourself." By extension, all forms of *tumah* should make themselves known so that people can avoid them.

A second, more direct source is cited by *Rav Ila* in the name of *Rav Shmuel bar Nachman*, from *Yechezkel*: *ve'avru ha'ovrim ba'aretz ve'ra'ah etzem adam uvanah etzlo tziyun* — "and those who pass through the land will see a human bone and build a marker by it." According to its *peshat*, this verse describes marking a location in order to then move the bone, but from it the *Gemara* derives the practice of building a marker adjacent to a grave.

### Marker Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיכָן שֶׁמְצַיְּנִין עַל הָעֲצָמוֹת. אָדָם. מִיכָן שֶׁמְצַיְּנִין עַל הַשִּׁזְרָה וְעַל הַגּולְגּוֹלֶת. וּבָנָה. מִיכָן שֶׁמְצַיְּנִין עַל גַּבֵּי אֶבֶן קְבוּעָה. אִם אוֹמֵר אַתְּ עַל גַּבֵּי אֶבֶן תְּלוּשָׁה. אַף הִיא הוֹלֶכֶת וּמְטַמֵּא בְמָקוֹם אַחֵר. אֶצְלוֹ. לִמְקוֹם טַהֲרָה. צִיּוּן.
>
> **Translation:** "{*A bone*}, from here that one makes signs for bones. *A human*, from here that one makes signs for spine and skull. *He builds*, from here that one makes signs on fixed stones. If you say on loose ones, it would move and make other places impure. *Near it*, on a place of purity. *A sign*, from here the marks."

The verse in *Yechezkel* mentions *etzem adam* — a human bone — and from this the *Gemara* derives that markers are placed specifically over bones: *mikan she'metzaynin al ha'atzamot*. This emphasis on bones is significant for two reasons. First, as we will see, a marker is not made over human flesh alone, because flesh decomposes and the marker would outlast its purpose. Second, the concern driving grave designation is that *Kohanim* not walk over the grave and contract *tumah be'ohel* — impurity transmitted by being under the same covering as human remains. Only bones generate *tumah be'ohel*, and only when present in sufficient quantity, with the exceptions of the skull and the spine, which convey *tumah* even as individual bones.

From *adam* — "of a man" — the *Gemara* derives: *mikan she'metzaynin al hashidrah ve'al hagulgoles*, that markers are placed even for the spine and the skull individually, as these generate *tumah be'ohel* even without the requisite quantity of other bones.

From *uvanah* — "and he shall build" — the *Gemara* derives that markers must be fixed structures: *mikan she'metzaynin al gabei even kevuah*, placed on a stone that is embedded in the ground rather than a loose movable stone. The reason is straightforward: *im omer at al gabei even telushah af hi holechet u-metamet be-makom acher* — if the marker is painted on a movable stone, that stone will be moved, and people will identify the wrong location as dangerous while remaining unaware of the real one.

From *etzlo* — "beside it" — the *Gemara* derives: *le-makom taharah tziyun* — the marker must be placed in *tahor* space, adjacent to but not directly over the grave. The purpose is to warn a person before they reach the danger zone, not after.

Each element of the verse thus yields a distinct *halachah* about how grave designation is to be performed.

### One vs Two Markers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָצָא אֶבֶן אַחַת מְצוּיֶינֶת. אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין מְקַיְימִין כֵּן הַמַּאֲהִיל עָלֶיהָ טָמֵא. אֲנִי אוֹמֵר. מֵת מְצוּיָין וְהָיָה נָתוּן תַּחְתֶּיהָ. הָיוּ שְׁתַיִם. הַמַּאֲהִיל עָלֶיהֶן טהוֹר וּבֵינֵיהֶן טָמֵא. אִם הָיָה חוֹרֶשׁ בֵּנְתַּיִים הֲרֵי הֵן כִּיחִידִיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** "If one found a single marked stone, even though one should not keep it so, if somebody forms a tent over it he is impure; I say a marked corpse was under it. If there were two, he who forms a tent over any one of them is pure; between them he is impure. If between them was a ploughed strip they are single stones."

The *Gemara* then addresses what to assume when one actually encounters a marker. If one stone is found marked, even though the proper practice is to place the marker away from the *met*, one cannot rely on this and assume safety: *af al pi she'ein mekayamin ken ha-ma'ahil aleha tamei*. Even though markers are not supposed to be placed directly over the *met*, the marker may have been placed incorrectly, or the *met* may have been positioned — *metzuyan*, perhaps folded over — such that it ended up directly beneath the marker. One who walks over a single marker must therefore treat oneself as *tamei*.

If, however, two marked stones are found, the analysis shifts: *ha-monach aleihem tahor u-vineihem tamei* — the stones themselves are in *tahor* space, and it is the area between them that is *tamei*. Two markers together clearly demarcate an area, and one may assume the stones stand at the edges with the grave between them. However, if the ground between the two stones was plowed — *haya charush be'ntayim* — one would not normally plow over a grave, so the two stones revert to the status of individual markers: *harey hen ki-yechidot*, with *tumah* assumed directly beneath each.

### Flesh vs Bones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנֵּי אֵין מְצַיְּנִין עַל הַבָּשָׂר שֶׁמָּא נִתְאַכֵּל הַבָּשָׂר. רִבִּי יוּסְטָא בַּר שׁוּנֵם בְּעָא קוֹמֵי רִבִּי מָנָא. וְלֹא נִמְצָא מְטַמֵּא טַהֲרוֹת לְמַפְרֵעַ. אָמַר לֵיהּ. מוטָּב שֶׁיִּתְקַלְקְלוּ בוֹ לְשָׁעָה וְאַל יִתְקַלְקְלוּ בוֹ לְעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** "It was stated: One does not mark flesh, for perhaps it will decompose. Rebbi Justus bar Shunem asked before Rebbi Mana: Will that not cause pure food to be retroactively made impure? He said to him, it is better that these should become unusable for a limited time than that {the earth} become unusable forever."

A *Baraita* is now cited: *ein metzaynin al ha-basar* — one does not place a marker over human flesh that has no bones. The reason is *shema yitakel ha-basar* — lest the flesh decompose. Once the flesh is gone, the marker remains but the *tumah* does not, and the permanent marker will mislead people into thinking a location is *tamei* when it no longer is.

*Rabbi Yose bar Abun* posed a question before *Rabbi Mana*: *velo nimtza metamei teharot le-mafrea* — by declining to mark the location of flesh, will we not end up causing people to contract *tumah* unknowingly during the period before decomposition, treating themselves as *tahor* when they are in fact *tamei*? *Rabbi Mana* responded with a striking formulation: *mutav she-yitkalkelu vo la-sha'ah ve'al yitkalkelu vo le-olam* — it is preferable that there be a temporary period of error, during which people become *tamei* unknowingly, rather than establish a permanent error that causes people to treat themselves as *tamei* indefinitely when they are in fact *tahor*. This is a remarkable ruling: *Rabbi Mana* is willing to accept a *kula* — permitting people to be in a state of unwitting *tumah* for a limited time — in order to avoid an unnecessary permanent *chumra* that would wrongly restrict people forever.

## Kilayim Removal

### Original Uprooting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** משנה: אָמַר רִבִּי יְהוּדָה בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ עוֹקְרִין וּמַשְׁלִיכִין לִפְנֵיהֶם. מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ עוֹבְרֵי עֲבֵירָה הָיוּ עוֹקְרִין וּמַשְׁלִיכִין לַדְּרָכִים. הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁיְּהוּ מַבְקִירִין אֶת כָּל הַשָּׂדֶה. הלכה: רִבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר. תַּנֵּי אָמַר רִבִּי יְהוּדְה. בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ עוֹקְרִין וּמַשְׁלִיכִין לִפְנֵיהֶם וְהָיוּ שְׂמֵיחִין שְׁתֵּי שְׂמָחוֹת. אַחַת שֶׁהָיוּ מְנַכְשִׁין שָׂדוֹתֵיהֶם וְאַחַת שֶׁהָיוּ נֶהֱנִין מִן (הַגֶּזֶל) [הַכִּלְאַיִם].
>
> **Translation:** "MISHNAH: Rebbi Jehudah says, originally they were uprooting and throwing down before them... HALAKHAH: ...originally they were uprooting and throwing down before them; they were twice happy, first that they weeded their fields and second that they could use the *kilaim*."

*Rav Yehuda* states — citing a *Baraita* — that the history of enforcing *kilayim* removal unfolded in three stages. The background is that when people failed to prevent errant seeds from growing in their fields — grape sprouts appearing among grain, for instance — the *beit din* would send inspectors to uproot the offending growth. In the first stage, *barishona hayu okrin umashlichin lifneihem* — they would uproot the *kilayim* and throw it down in front of the field owners. The owners, however, were delighted: *hayu smeichim she'yesh mocheh*. They were happy for two reasons. First, *achas she'hayu menachshin sadeoteihem* — the *beit din* was effectively weeding their fields for them, relieving them of the labor. Second, *ve'achas she'hayu nehenin min hakilayim* — they would then feed the uprooted growth to their animals and derive benefit from it, which, while perhaps technically permissible for *kilayim* that is not *kilei hakerem*, was nonetheless inappropriate behavior that the enforcement was inadvertently encouraging.

### Kilayim on Roads

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ עוֹבְרֵי עֲבֵירָה הָיוּ מַשְׁלִיכִין עַל הַדְּרָכִים. אַף עַל פִּי כֵן הָיוּ שְׂמִחִים שֶׁהָיוּ מְנַכְשִׁין שָׂדוֹתֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** "When the number of transgressors rose, they were throwing it on the roadways, and still they were happy that they weeded their fields."

Once *mishe-rabu ovrei aveira* — sinners multiplied and the perverse incentive became clear — the policy shifted: *hayu mashlichim al hadrahim* — they would throw the uprooted *kilayim* onto the public roads rather than in front of the owners, thereby preventing them from feeding it to their animals. Yet even this did not fully solve the problem: *hayu smeichim achas she'hayu menachshin sadeoteihem* — the owners were still happy, because the *beit din* was still doing their weeding for them.

### Field Becomes Hefker

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁיְּהוּ מַבְקִירִין אֶת כָּל הַשָּׂדֶה.
>
> **Translation:** "They instituted that they were declaring the entire field as ownerless."

The authorities therefore enacted a more severe measure: *hitkinu she'yehe mafkirin es kol hasadeh kullah* — they enacted that if a field was found to contain *kilayim*, the entire field would be declared *hefker*. Whether this meant the crops, the land itself, or both could be seized, the consequence was a genuine and serious financial penalty.

### Perverse Incentives

This sequence illustrates a broader phenomenon — one with a name in contemporary discourse — where a regulatory response to unwanted behavior produces the exact opposite of the intended effect. Each escalation in enforcement created a new perverse incentive: the *beit din*'s removal of *kilayim* was being exploited as a free agricultural service, and even throwing the growth into the road was insufficient. Only the threat of losing the entire field forced a change in behavior. It is the Talmudic enactment of *hefker* as a *knas* — a punitive fine — that finally produces the desired result.

## Hefker Beit Din

### Ezra's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנַיִיִן שֶׁהֶבְקֵר בֵּית דִּין הֶבְקֵר. כָּתוּב וְכֹל אֲשֶׁר לֹא יָבֹא לִשְׁלשֶׁת הַיָּמִים כַּעֲצַת הַשָּׂרִים וְהַזְּקֵנִים יָחֳרַם כָּל רְכוּשׁוֹ וְהוּא יִבָּדֵל מִקְּהַל הַגּוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** "From where that a declaration of forfeiture by a court makes it ownerless? It is written: *Anybody who will not come within three days conforming to the decree of the rulers and the elders, all his property shall be devoted to destruction and he shall be separated from the community of the Diaspora*."

The *Mishnah*'s ruling that *beit din* can declare a field *hefker* as a penalty leads naturally into the classic principle of *hefker beis din hefker* — that property declared ownerless by *beit din* is indeed legally ownerless. The *Gemara* asks: *minayin she-hefker beis din hefker* — what is the source for this principle? The proof text comes from *Sefer Ezra*: *ve'chol asher lo yavo lishloshes yamim ba'atzas hasarim vehazkeinim yachoram kol rechusho vehu yibadel mikhal hagola* — whoever does not appear within three days, by the ruling of the leaders and elders, all his property shall be forfeited and he shall be expelled from the community. *Yachoram kol rechusho* — "all his property shall be banned" — is understood to mean that *beit din* can remove a person's ownership of property and render it available for anyone to take. This is explicitly a punitive context, a *knas*, and that is precisely the context of our *Mishnah* here.

It is worth noting that the *Bavli* applies *hefker beis din hefker* far more broadly, using it to validate ordinary *takkanot* of *beit din* in civil matters generally — not only punitive ones. But neither the *pasuk* from *Ezra* nor our *Mishnah* provides a basis for that broader application; both are squarely in the context of penalty.

### Ibbur Year Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנַיִין שֶׁהִיא פְטוּרָה מִן הַמַּעְשְׂרוֹת. רִבִּי יוֹנָתָן בְּרֵיהּ דְּרִבִּי יִצְחָק בַר אָחָה שָׁמַע לָהּ מִן הָדָא. אֵין מְעַבְּרִין אֶת הַשָּׁנָה לֹא בַשְּׁבִיעִית וְלֹא בְמוֹצָאֵי שְׁבִיעִית. וְאִם עִיבְּרוּהָ הֲרֵי זוֹ מְעוּבֶּרֶת. וְחוֹדֶשׁ אֶחָד שֶׁהוּא מוֹסִיף לֹא פָטוּר מִמַּעְשְׂרוֹת הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** "From where that it is free from tithes? Rebbi Jonathan, the son of Rebbi Isaac bar Aḥa understood it from the following: 'One intercalates years neither in the Sabbatical year, nor in the year following the Sabbatical; but if they did intercalate it is intercalated.' The one month he adds, is it not free from tithes?"

The *Gemara* then asks: *minayin she-patur min hama'asrot* — how do we know that property declared *hefker* by *beit din* is exempt from *ma'asrot*? This might seem obvious — once something is *hefker* it is *hefker*, and all the normal consequences of *hefker* should follow. But the *Gemara* takes the question seriously.

*Rav Yonatan brei de'Rav Yitzchak bar Acha* attempts a proof from the following: *ein ma'avrin es hashana lo bisvi'is velo bemotzei shvi'is* — one does not add an intercalary month to a *shemittah* year or to the year following *shemittah*. The reason for not doing so in *shemittah* is that one does not want to extend the hardships of *shemittah* by an additional month. The reason in *motzei shvi'is* is that people desperately need the new grain, and adding a month delays the *Omer* offering and thereby postpones the permissibility of the new crop by an additional month. Nevertheless, *ve'im ibruha harei zu me'uberes* — if *beit din* did add such a month, the intercalation stands. And *vechodesh echad she'hu mosif lo patur min hama'asrot* — the added month, and anything that grows during it, is exempt from *ma'asrot*. Apparently, then, *beit din*'s act of adding a month is sufficient to create an exemption from *ma'asrot*.

### Refuting Ibbur Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כְדוֹן שְׁבִיעִית. מוֹצָאֵי שְׁבִיעִית [מַאי]. אָמַר רִבִּי אָבוּן. שֶׁלֹּא לְרַבּוֹת בְּאִיסּוּר חָדָשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** "That refers to the Sabbatical year. What about the year after the Sabbatical? Rebbi Abun said, not to prolong the prohibition of new grain."

*Rabbi Avun* rejects this proof decisively: *leit at shemia mineh klum* — you cannot derive anything from it. The reason *beit din* can add a month and thereby extend *shemittah* — resulting in an automatic exemption from *ma'asrot* — is not because of any general principle of *hefker beis din hefker*. It is because of the biblical mandate: *shamor es chodesh ha'aviv* — "observe the month of spring." *Beit din* has an explicit *mitzvah* to ensure that *Nissan* falls in its proper season. Their authority to intercalate derives directly from that *pasuk*, and the consequences of intercalation — including the extension of *shemittah* and the resulting *petur* from *ma'asrot* — flow from that biblical power, not from any act of *hefker*. The proof is therefore irrelevant to our question.

### Calendar Sync Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי מָנָא. הָדָא דְאַתְּ אָמַר. בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה כְּשֶׁהָיוּ הָשָּׁנִים כְּתִיקֻנָן. אֲבָל עַכְשָׁיו שֶׁאֵין הָשָּׁנִים כְּתִיקֻנָן הִיא שְׁמִתָּה הִיא שֶׁל שְׁאָר שְׁנֵי שָׁבוּעַ.
>
> **Translation:** "Rebbi Mana said, that refers to earlier times when years were in order, but now that years are not in order, the Sabbatical year is like any other year."

The *Gemara* had noted that *beit din* does not add an intercalary month in *shemittah* or *motzei shvi'is*. A clarification is offered by *Rav Mana*: *hada de'at amar barishona she'hayu hashanim ke'tiknan* — that rule applied in the early period, when the calendar years were properly synchronized. *Aval achshav she'einan hashanim ke'tiknan* — but now, when the calendar years are not in their proper alignment, *hi shemittah hi shel she'ar shenei shavua* — the same would apply to *shemittah* as to other years, meaning intercalation may proceed.

The meaning of "now the years are not properly aligned" has been understood in different ways. Some read it as referring to the period after the *Churban*, when *Yovel* was no longer observed and *shemittah* itself was perhaps only *derabanan* — though this reading is difficult since *shemittah* is observed even today. Others prefer a different *girsah*: not *hi she'shvi'is hi shel she'ar shenei shavua* but *hi shel motzei shvi'is hi shel she'ar shenei shavua* — meaning that *Rav Mana* is explaining specifically why *motzei shvi'is* can also have a month added: if an intercalation is being made, it is because the calendar genuinely requires it, the spring has not yet arrived, and therefore adding a month is warranted even in *motzei shvi'is*.

Additionally, *Rabbi Avun* had already noted a separate reason why intercalation in *shemittah* became permissible: *Rebbe* had relaxed the prohibition against importing vegetables from *chutz la'aretz*. Before that ruling, importing *chutz la'aretz* produce was forbidden — whether for reasons of *tumah* of *chutz la'aretz* soil or to support local agriculture. Once *Rebbe* permitted imports, the hardship of *shemittah* was substantially reduced, since one could continue eating imported vegetables even without domestically grown produce. The rationale for refusing to intercalate in *shemittah* — that it would extend the hardship — accordingly fell away.

### Leket Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גָּדִישׁ שֶׁלֹּא לוּקַּט תַּחְתָּיו כָּל הַנּוֹגְעוֹת בָּאָרֶץ הֲרֵי הֵן שֶׁלְעֲנִיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** "For a grain stack under which gleanings were not collected, all ears that touch the ground are for the poor."

Having rejected the *ibbur* proof, the *Gemara* asks: *ve'haida amar da* — where, then, is it actually demonstrated that *hefker beis din hefker* creates a *petur* from *ma'asrot*? The real proof comes from the following case: *gadi she'lo lukat tachtav kol ha-nogea ba'aretz harei hen shel aniyim* — a grain pile beneath which *leket* had not first been cleared. The *halachah* is that all the grain immediately touching the ground beneath the pile has the status of *leket*, which belongs to the poor. And since *leket* is *hefker*, it carries no obligation of *ma'asrot*. However, the *Gemara* recognizes that this is a rabbinic rule — biblically, not all the grain touching the ground is *leket*. This is therefore an enactment of the Rabbis, an act of *hefker beis din hefker*, and the fact that it generates a *petur* from *ma'asrot* demonstrates that *hefker beis din hefker* does indeed produce that consequence.

### Hefker Aniyim Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רִבִּי אִמִּי בְשֵׁם רִבִּי שׁמְעוֹן בֵּן לָקִישׁ. דְּבֵית שַׂמַּי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** "Rebbi Immi in the name of Rebbi Simeon ben Laqish: This is from the House of Shammai."

*Rabbi Ami* in the name of *Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish* challenges this proof: *sedei Shammai hi zu* — that *halachah* follows only *Beit Shammai*. There is a famous dispute between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* about *hefker la'aniyim* — whether one can declare property *hefker* selectively for the poor alone. *Beit Shammai* holds that *hefker* to a subset is valid *hefker*; *Beit Hillel* holds that true *hefker* must be available to everyone — one cannot remove ownership only toward the poor. According to *Beit Hillel*, therefore, grain that has been designated only for the *aniyim* never achieves the legal status of *hefker*, and the *aniyim* who take it would accordingly be required to separate *ma'aser* from it.

### Rabbinic Penalty Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר לֵיהּ רִבִּי יוֹסֵי. שָׁמַעְנוּ שֶׁהוּא פָטוּר מִמַּעְשְׂרוֹת דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מִשּׁוּם קְנָס.
>
> **Translation:** "Rebbi Yose said to him: We understand that according to everybody, this is a fine."

*Rabbi Yosei* resolves the difficulty: *shamanu she-patur min hama'aser divrei hakol mishum kensa* — everyone agrees, even *Beit Hillel*, that in this case the grain is exempt from *ma'aser*, and the reason is that it is a rabbinic *knas*. The debate between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* about *hefker la'aniyim* concerns a private individual's ability to make something *hefker* selectively. But what we have here is not a private individual's act — it is an enactment of *beit din*, an exercise of *hefker beis din hefker*, which is a fundamentally different category. Because it is a rabbinic penalty, it carries the full force of *hefker*, rendering the property completely ownerless — even if, practically, only the *aniyim* can collect it — and therefore even *Beit Hillel* would agree that no *ma'aser* is required. The *chiddush* being established here deserves emphasis: the principle of *hefker beis din hefker* does not merely mean that *beit din* has announced that anyone may take the property while the original owner retains ownership until someone does. Rather, the property is genuinely removed from the owner's possession before anyone takes it, achieving a true moment of ownerlessness — and it is that status which generates the *petur* from *ma'aser*.

## Shekel Collection

### 15th Adar: Changers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** משנה: בַּחֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׁר בּוֹ שׁוּלְחָנוֹת יוֹשְׁבִין בַּמְּדִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** "MISHNAH: On the Fifteenth, money changers were sitting in the country side."

On the fifteenth of *Adar*, *shulchanot hayu yoshvin ba-medinah* — money changers were stationed throughout the towns and cities. This was done to make it convenient for people to exchange their currency for *machtzis ha-shekel*. The precise meaning of *medinah* here — whether it refers to *Yerushalayim* specifically or to the major population centers more broadly — involves some discussion, but the intent is accessibility.

### 25th Adar: Temple

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעֶשְׁרִים וַחֲמִשָּׁה בוֹ יָשְׁבוּ בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ. מִשֶּׁיָּשְׁבוּ בַּמִּקְְדָּשׁ הִתְחִילוּ לְמַשְׁכֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** "On the Twenty Fifth they were sitting in the Temple. From the date they were sitting in the Temple one started to take pledges."

On the twenty-fifth of *Adar*, *yoshvin ba-mikdash* — the money changers relocated to the *Beit HaMikdash*. This might seem counterintuitive: as the deadline approaches and urgency grows, why move the changers to fewer, more centralized locations rather than maintaining their broad distribution? One way to understand this is that by this point, the majority of the money had already been collected and was converging on *Yerushalayim* in any case. Those who had not yet paid would need to come to *Yerushalayim* to arrange their payment directly at the source. As one might say: if you register early, you can do it remotely; if you wait until the last moment, you have to come in person.

## Shekel Collection

### Mashkon Seizure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶת מִי מְמַשְְׁכְּנִין לְוִיִים וְיִשְׂרְאֵלִים וְגֵרִים וַעֲבָדִים מְשֻׁחְרָרִין אֲבָל לֹא נָשִׁים וְלֹא עֲבָדִים וְלֹא קְטַנִּים.
>
> **Translation:** "From whom does one take pledges? From Levites, Israel, proselytes, and freed slaves, but not from women, slaves, and minors."

From the time the money changers relocated to the *Mikdash*, enforcement intensified: *hitchilu le-mashken* — they began seizing property as collateral from those who had not yet paid. *Mashkon* refers to the taking of a pledge or lien — *beit din* would seize property and hold it until the *shekel* was paid. The goal was to ensure complete participation.

The *Mishnah* specifies who was subject to seizure: *leviyim ve-yisre'elim, gerim va-avadim meshuchrarim* — *Leviyim*, *Yisre'elim*, converts, and freed slaves. Any Jewish male adult, with defined exceptions, was subject to this enforcement.

### Kohanim Exempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לֹא נָשִׁים וְלֹא עֲבָדִים וְלֹא קְטַנִּים. וְכָל קָטָן שֶׁהִתְחִיל אָבִיו לִשְׁקוֹל עַל יָדוֹ אֵינוֹ פּוֹסֵק. אֵין מְמַשְׁכְּנִין אֶת הַכֹּהֲנִים מִפְּנֵי דַּרְכֵּי שָׁלוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** "But not from women, slaves, and minors. Any minor for whom his father started to give the *sheqel* does not stop any more. However one does not take pledges from Cohanim because of communal peace."

*Kohanim* were exempt from the seizure of their property, and the *Mishnah* offers one reason: *mipnei darchei shalom* — for the sake of peace. The *Kohanim* were the workers of the *Beit HaMikdash*, and seizing their property for contributions to the *Beit HaMikdash* would breed resentment and animosity. The sense of *darchei shalom* here is closely related to considerations of honor and dignity — a point the *Gemara* will restate as *darchei kavod*, ways of respect. One did not want the institution's own servants to be visibly subject to coercive enforcement. Women, slaves, and minors were also exempt — women and slaves because the *machtzis ha-shekel* obligation in the *Torah* is formulated for men, and minors because the obligation had not yet applied to them.

### Ben Bukhri Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי יְהוּדָה הֵעִיד בֶּן כּוּבָרִי בְּיַבְנֶה שֶׁכָּל כֹּהֵן שֶׁהוּא שׁוֹקֵל אֵינוֹ חוֹטֵא.
>
> **Translation:** "Rebbi Jehudah said: 'Ben Kukhri testified at Jabneh that any Cohen who pays the *sheqel* does not sin.'"

*Rabbi Yehuda* cites a testimony given by *Ben Bukhri* in *Yavneh* — the rabbinic center established after the *Churban* where sages with living memory of *Beit HaMikdash* practice convened to preserve that knowledge. *Ben Bukhri*'s formulation was: *kol kohen she-shokel eino chotei* — any *Kohen* who gave a *shekel* was not sinning. This implies that it was optional — one could give without transgressing, but one was not obligated. The framing presupposes that the *Kohanim* had made an argument that giving was actually problematic, and *Ben Bukhri* sought to counter at least part of that argument by establishing that giving was permissible.

### Kohanim Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא שֶׁהַכֹּהֲנִים דּוֹרְשִׁין מִקְרָא זֶה לְעַצְמָן וְכָל מִנְחַת כֹּהֵן כָּלִיל תִּהְיֶה לֹא תֵאָכֵל הוֹאִיל וְהָעוֹמֶר וּשְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם וְלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים שֶׁלָּנוּ הֵיאָךְ הֵם נֶאֱכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** "Only the Cohanim explain the following verse for their benefit: *Any flour offering of a Cohen shall be totalled, it may not be eaten*. If the *`omer*, the two breads, and the shew bread are ours, how may they be eaten?"

The *Kohanim*'s argument was drawn from the verse *ve'chol minchat kohen kalil tihyeh lo te'achel* — any *minchah* of a *Kohen* must be completely burned and cannot be eaten. Their reasoning ran as follows: the communal sacrifices purchased with *shekalim* include the *Omer*, the *shtei ha-lechem*, and the *lechem ha-panim*. If the *Kohanim* contributed to the communal fund, they would hold ownership in those sacrifices. Since a *Kohen*'s *minchah* must be entirely burned, those communal *minchot* could no longer be eaten. In the interest of the *tzibbur*, they argued, the *Kohanim* must refrain from contributing their *shekel*. The argument has a certain psychological familiarity — much like a synagogue professional who reasons that his labor exempts him from financial contribution — but it served the *Kohanim*'s convenience remarkably well.

### RYBZ Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי לֹא כִּי אֶלָּא כָּל כֹּהֵן שֶׁאֵינוֹ שׁוֹקֵל חוֹטֵא.
>
> **Translation:** "Rabban Joḥanan ben Zakkai told him, on the contrary, any Cohen who does not pay the *sheqel* does sin."

*Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai*, the head of *Yavneh*, rejected *Ben Bukhri*'s formulation outright: *lo ki, ella kol kohen she'eino shokel chotei* — the correct position is the reverse: a *Kohen* who does *not* give a *shekel* is sinning. It is not that giving is optional; it is mandatory, and failure to give is a transgression. He acknowledged, however, the *Kohanim*'s interpretive move: *ella she'hakohanim dorshin mikra zeh le'atzman* — the *Kohanim* read this verse self-servingly, to their own benefit.

### Communal vs Private

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי בֶּרֶכְיָה. טַעֲמֵיהּ דְּרַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי. זֶה יִתְּנוּ. שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר שְׁבָטִים יִתְּנוּ. רִבִּי טָבִי בְשֵׁם רַב הַמְנוּנָא. כֵּן מֵשִׁיבִין חֲכָמִים לְרִבִּי יְהוּדָה. חַטָּאת יָחִיד מֵתָה. אֵין חַטָּאת הַצִּיבּוּר מֵתָה. מִנְחַת הַיָּחִיד קְרֵיבָה כָלִיל וְאֵין מִנְחַת צִיבּוּר קְרֵיבָה כָלִיל.
>
> **Translation:** "Rebbi Berekhiah said, the reason of Rabban Joḥanan ben Zakkai is *This they shall give*, twelve tribes shall give. Rebbi Tabi in the name of Rav Hamnuna: So answer the Sages to Rebbi Jehudah. A private purification sacrifice is let to die; a public purification sacrifice is not left to die. A private flour offering is brought totally, but no public flour offering is brought totally."

The *Chachamim*'s rebuttal to the *Kohanim*'s argument turns on the fundamental distinction between private and communal sacrifices. The *Chachamim* argue: *chatat yachid meta, ein chatat tzibbur meta* — a private *chatat* whose owner dies must be left to die, but a communal *chatat* does not. This demonstrates that a communal sacrifice is not simply the aggregate of individual contributions — it is a categorically distinct entity, a corporate matter of the *tzibbur*. Similarly, *minchat yachid kareva kalil* — a private *Kohen*'s *minchah* is entirely burned — but this does not apply to a communal *minchah*. Once the *Kohanim*'s contributions are absorbed into the communal fund, they are no longer *Kohen* property in any relevant sense; ownership has transferred to the *tzibbur* as a distinct entity.

### Yehuda Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקַשְׁיָא. מֵשִׁיבִין לָאָדָם דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ מוֹדֶה בוֹ. שֶׁאֵין חַטָּאת צִיבּוּר מֵתָה. רִבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר תָּמוּת. וְהוּא מוֹתִיב לוֹן תוּ. לֹא נִדְבַת יָחִיד הִיא. וְאִינּוּן מֵתִיבִין לֵיהּ. מִכֵּיוָן שֶׁנִּמְסְרָה לַצִּיבּוּר כְּמִי שֶׁהִיא נִדְבַת צִיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** "This is difficult, how can one argue with a person about something with which he does not agree? 'For no public purification sacrifice is left to die; Rebbi Jehudah says, it shall be left to die.' And he objects to them, are these not private flour offerings? They answer him, from the moment when it is delivered to the public, it is treated as a public offering."

The *Gemara* raises a sharp objection: *meshivin le'adam davar she'eino modeh bo* — you cannot refute someone with a proof he does not accept. The proof from the *chatat* — the difference between a private *chatat* that must die and a communal *chatat* that does not — depends on a *halachah* that *Rabbi Yehuda* himself disputes. *Detnan: she'ein chatat tzibbur meta, Rabbi Yehuda omer tamus* — the *Mishnah* records that *Rabbi Yehuda* holds that a communal *chatat* also must be left to die. If *Rabbi Yehuda* does not concede the premise — that communal and private *chatat* differ in this respect — how can it serve as a proof against him?

The *Gemara* responds: even granting *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position, once an individual's contribution is handed over to the *tzibbur* — *keivan she-nimsar la-tzibbur ke'mi she-hu nidvas tzibbur* — it becomes communal property. It is no longer a proportional private stake in a shared asset; it has been subsumed into the collective. Therefore, even without conceding the *chatat* distinction, one can argue that the very act of contributing to the communal fund transforms the private contribution into a communal one, eliminating any basis for the *Kohanim*'s concern.

### Gematria Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב כֹּל הָעוֹבֵר עַל הַפְּקוּדִים.
>
> **Translation:** "It is written, *everybody being counted*."

An additional proof is offered by *Rabbi Berechiah*: the word *zeh* — "this is what they shall give" — has the *gematria* value of 12, representing the twelve tribes: *yud-bet shevatim yitnu* — all twelve tribes are obligated to give. This proof works against the *Kohanim*'s exemption, though it should be noted that the number twelve can be arrived at in more than one way given the complexities of tribal counting in the *Torah*.

### Pikudim Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי יוּדָה וְרִבִּי נְחֶמְיָה. חַד אָמַר. כָּל דַּעֲבַר בָּיַּמָּה יִתֵּן. וְחֳרָנָה אָמַר. כָּל דַּעֲבַר עַל פִּקּוּדַיָיא יִתֵּן. מָאן דַאֲמַר כָּל דַּעֲבַר בָּיַּמָּא יִתֵּן. מְסַיֵיעַ לְרַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי. מָאן. כֹּל דַּעֲבַר עַל פִקוּדַיָיא יִתֵּן. מְסַיֵיעַ לְבֶן כּובְרִי.
>
> **Translation:** "Rebbi Jehudah and Rebbi Neḥemiah. One said, everyone who crossed the Sea shall give, the other one said, everyone being counted shall give. He who said, everyone who crossed the Sea shall give, supports Rabban Joḥanan ben Zakkai; he who said, everyone being counted shall give, supports Ben Kovri."

Finally, the *Gemara* presents a dispute between *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Nechemia* on the meaning of *kol ha'over al ha-pikudim* — "all who pass through the counting." One opinion holds: *kol d'avar be'yama* — this refers to all who passed through the *Yam Suf*, meaning all *Bnei Yisrael* without exception are obligated to give. The other holds: *kol d'avar al pikudaya yitein* — only those included in *eileh ha-pekudim*, the standard census count, are obligated. Since the *Kohanim* — and the *Leviyim* — were counted separately in a distinct census, they would under this reading be excluded from the obligation entirely. This last view supports *Ben Bukhri*'s position, while the former supports *Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai*'s view that all of *Bnei Yisrael* — including the *Kohanim* — are obligated to give.

## Minor Exemptions

### Minor Definition

Returning to those exempt from the *mashkon* — the seizure of property — the *Mishnah* listed *katanim*, minors. The question of what constitutes a minor for purposes of the *machtzis ha-shekel* is answered by reference to the scriptural language of *me'esrim shanah va-ma'alah* — "from twenty years and above" — which appears in connection with the census and the *shekalim*. Under one *girsah*, a minor for this purpose is anyone under twenty, even if he has already reached halachic adulthood at thirteen.

### Paternal Obligation

The *Mishnah* records: *ve'chol katan she'hitchil aviv lishkol al yado shuv eino posek* — any minor for whom the father has already begun to contribute, the father may not subsequently stop. Once the practice of giving on a son's behalf has been established, it has the force of a commitment that cannot simply be abandoned. This evokes the broader principle that once a practice has been undertaken three times it acquires a degree of binding force, though here the context is specifically the *machtzis ha-shekel*.

### Minor Seizure Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הלכה: בַּחֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר בּוֹ כול׳. הָא לִתְבּוֹעַ תּוֹבְעִין. הָדָא דְתֵימַר בְּשֶׁהֵבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת. אֲבָל אִם לֹא הֵבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת לְֹא בְדָא. וּלְמַשְׁכֵּן אֵין מְמַשְׁכְּנִין עַד שֶׁיָּבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** "HALAKHAH: But one requests? Here you are saying, one requests, but there you are saying, one does not request. There if he grew two pubic hairs, and here if he did not grow two pubic hairs."

Although the minor's exemption, the *Mishnah* indicates that contributions are still *requested* from a minor who has reached a certain threshold. Specifically, *hada deteimar bishetei se'arot* — this applies to one who has already grown two pubic hairs, signifying physical maturity and bar mitzvah status. Such a person is no longer a *katan* for other purposes and would therefore still be asked to contribute. But even so, his property is not seized.

The question of exactly when seizure becomes applicable is subject to a textual dispute. One *girsah* reads: *af al pi she'heivi shetei se'arot, ad she'yehei ben esrim* — even after developing two pubic hairs, property is not seized until he reaches twenty years of age. This is a striking *halachah*, suggesting that the *machtzis ha-shekel* obligation tracks a different threshold than ordinary halachic majority — following the *psukim* that speak explicitly of *me'esrim shanah va-ma'alah* rather than the standard bar mitzvah age of thirteen. Under an alternative *girsah* that lacks this condition, the two pubic hairs alone might be the operative threshold, though why they would be mentioned if the child has not yet reached thirteen remains difficult to explain.
