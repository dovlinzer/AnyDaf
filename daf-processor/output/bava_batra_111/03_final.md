# Bava Batra 111 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Inheritance Hierarchy

### Prior Coverage

Today's daf is 111. We pick up on the last line of 110b, continuing through the roster of people who inherit from one another. The previous daf covered several key relationships. A father inheriting from his children is not derived from the plain sense of the *psukim* but is learned through a *drasha* — specifically, the phrase *she'ero hakaro elav*, "his flesh who is close to him," which inserts the father into the order of inheritance between the children and the brother. The plain meaning of the *psukim* establishes that when someone dies, the estate first passes to sons, then to daughters. The father is then inserted by derivation, and the brothers follow. The brother is explicit in the *psukim*: *ve'im ein lo bat unetatem et nachalato le'achiv*, "and if he has no daughter, you shall give his inheritance to his brother" — though the Gemara's critical point there was that "brother" is defined as a paternal half-brother, governed by the principle that *mishpachat av kriya mishpacha, mishpachat em eina kriya mishpacha* — the patriarchal family is called a family, the maternal family is not. So the full sequence from the previous daf is: son, daughter, father, brother, and father's brother. That covers essentially the entire Mishnah, aside from two figures who are not derived from the plain sense of the *psukim*: a wife and a mother.

### New Topics Intro

We now turn to those two remaining cases — a man inheriting from his wife, and a man inheriting from his mother. The word *she'ero*, used in various contexts, is related to flesh — *ki achinu vesarenu hu*, "for he is our brother, our flesh." The *Ramban* elaborates on this in connection with the *pasuk* *vayihyu levasar echad*, which is relevant to husband and wife — and there is of course the usage in *she'er kesuta ve'onata* as well. The Gemara now asks how we know that a man inherits from his mother, and separately, how we know that a man inherits from his wife.

## Son Inherits Mother

### Tosafot Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן:
>
> **Translation:** **From where are these matters** derived? The Gemara answers: **As the Sages taught:**

The Gemara asks: *mena hanei milei* — where do we know this from? Before proceeding to the Gemara's answer, Tosafot raises a pointed question: why do we need a special derivation at all? We have already established the general order of inheritance. If that order applies when a man dies, it should apply equally when a woman dies. When a woman dies, her son should obviously inherit her, just as when a man dies, his son inherits him. What requires a *pasuk*?

### Tribal Disruption

Tosafot answers with a distinction that cuts to the heart of what this entire sugya is about. For other cases involving women who die — a man's daughter or sister, for example — it is self-evident that her father or brother would inherit her, because the property remains entirely within the patriarchal family. There is no disruption, and so no *chiddush* is needed. Men and women are treated identically in this regard: *bein lirash bein lehorish*, whether inheriting or bequeathing.

But the case of a son inheriting from his mother is genuinely different. Tosafot explains: *lefi shenisvet nachala lah mimateh aviha al yedei haben* — because the inheritance that came to her from her father's tribe now passes, through her son, potentially out of that tribe entirely. If a woman born to *shevet HaDan* marries a man from *shevet Reuven*, the property she received from her Danite family — her ancestral portion in the territory of Dan — will now pass to her son, who is a Reuvenite. Geographically and tribally, a patch of Danite land ends up held by someone from Reuven.

This is profoundly disruptive because even *yovel* does not undo it. When the Jubilee year arrives and *ish el nachalato yashuvu* — each person returns to his ancestral holding — this son retains that land. It was his *nachala* that came through his mother. It does not revert to the tribe of Dan. Every tribe could end up with scattered patches belonging to people from entirely different tribes. So this is the reason a *pasuk* is needed specifically for a son inheriting from his mother: we are permitting the *nachala* to leave the *mishpachat av* through the maternal line, and that requires an explicit textual basis.

This is also why the uncles of the *Bnot Tzelafchad* complained. When a woman inherits and then passes that inheritance on — whether to her husband or to her son — the property leaves the ancestral tribe. That is precisely what their complaint was about. And this, Tosafot explains, is why the Torah requires a *chiddush* for the case of the mother: it is not simply restating the general inheritance rules; it is authorizing a transfer of *nachala* that goes outside the patriarchal household.

### Braita Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְכׇל בַּת יֹרֶשֶׁת נַחֲלָה מִמַּטּוֹת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ – הֵיאַךְ בַּת יוֹרֶשֶׁת שְׁנֵי מַטּוֹת? אֶלָּא זוֹ שֶׁאָבִיהָ מִשֵּׁבֶט אֶחָד וְאִמָּהּ מִשֵּׁבֶט אַחֵר, וּמֵתוּ, וִירָשָׁתַן.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And every daughter who possesses an inheritance from the tribes of the children of Israel"** (Numbers 36:8). Noting the plural in the phrase "from the tribes of the children of Israel," the *baraita* asks: **How** can **a daughter inherit** land from **two tribes? Rather, this** is referring to a daughter **whose father** is **from one tribe but her mother** is **from another tribe, and they** both **died, and she inherited** from both of **them.** Therefore, it can be seen in this verse that a daughter inherits from her mother.

The Gemara answers with a *braita*. The *pasuk* states: *vechol bat yoreshet nachala mimatot bnei Yisrael* — any daughter who inherits an inheritance from the tribes of Israel. The Gemara asks: *heich bat yoreshet shnei matot?* — how can a daughter inherit from two tribes? The answer: this is a case where her father is from one tribe and her mother is from another tribe, both die, and she inherits from both. With no sons surviving, she inherits from two separate tribes.

From this the *braita* derives: *ve'ein li ela bat* — I know only that a daughter inherits from her mother. *Ben minayin?* — how do I know a son does?

### Qal Va-Chomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא בַּת, בֵּן מִנַּיִן? אָמַרְתָּ קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה בַּת, שֶׁהוֹרַע כֹּחָהּ בְּנִכְסֵי הָאָב – יִפָּה כֹּחָהּ בְּנִכְסֵי הָאֵם; בֵּן, שֶׁיִּפָּה כֹּחוֹ בְּנִכְסֵי הָאָב – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיִּפָּה כֹּחוֹ בְּנִכְסֵי הָאֵם? וּמִמָּקוֹם שֶׁבָּאתָ – מָה לְהַלָּן בֵּן קוֹדֵם לַבַּת, אַף כָּאן בֵּן קוֹדֵם לַבַּת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **I have** derived **only** that **a daughter** inherits from her mother; **from where** do I derive that **a son** inherits from his mother as well? **You** can **say an *a fortiori*** inference: **And just as a daughter, whose power is diminished with regard to** her **father's property** in that she does not inherit it when there is a son, yet her power **is enhanced with regard to** her **mother's property** in that she inherits it; with regard to **a son, whose power is enhanced with regard to** his **father's property** in that he is first in the order of inheritance, **is it not logical that his power is enhanced with regard to** his **mother's property?** The *baraita* continues: **And from the place that you came,** i.e., from the inference that was just stated, one can say the following: **Just as there,** with regard to a father's property, **a son precedes a daughter, so too here,** with regard to a mother's property, **a son precedes a daughter.** This is the opinion of the first *tanna*.

The *braita* answers with a *kal va-chomer*: a daughter, whose legal standing regarding the father's estate is weaker — she inherits only in the absence of sons — nevertheless clearly inherits from her mother. A son, whose standing by the father's estate is stronger — he is first in the order of inheritance — surely inherits from his mother as well. *Eino din she-yafe kocho b'nichsei ha-em?*

One might pause here to appreciate the *hava amina*. As Tosafot noted, the effect of a woman dying and passing her estate to her son is that the property immediately leaves the tribe. If she passes to her daughter instead, the disruption is at least not immediate: the daughter might marry within the mother's original tribe, or might not, but the transfer is one step further removed. If she passes to her son, the property crosses tribal lines without delay. So there is a genuine reason one might have thought the derivation applies only to daughters and not to sons.

The *braita* then continues: *u-mimakom she-bata* — from the same source from which you derived the son's right to inherit, apply the same logic. Just as by the father the son precedes the daughter, so too by the mother the son precedes the daughter: *ben kodem le-bat*.

Rabbi Yossi bar Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Yossi then cite a ruling in the name of Rabbi Zechariah ben HaKatzav, which takes a fundamentally different position.

### Rabbi Zechariah Equal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אָמְרוּ, מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי זְכַרְיָה בֶּן הַקַּצָּב: אֶחָד הַבֵּן וְאֶחָד הַבַּת, שָׁוִין בְּנִכְסֵי הָאֵם. מַאי טַעְמָא? דַּיּוֹ לַבָּא מִן הַדִּין לִהְיוֹת כַּנִּדּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, and Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Yosei, said in the name of Rabbi Zekharya ben Hakatzav: Both the son and the daughter are equal with regard to the mother's property. What is the reason** for his ruling? **It is sufficient for the** conclusion that **emerges from** an *a fortiori* **inference to be like** its **source.** According to this principle, a *halakha* derived by means of an *a fortiori* inference cannot go beyond the *halakhot* of the source from which it is derived. Since the *halakha* that a son inherits from his mother was derived via an *a fortiori* inference from the *halakha* that a daughter inherits from her mother, it cannot be that this inference should lead to the conclusion that a son precedes a daughter in inheriting from their mother.

Rabbi Zechariah ben HaKatzav rules: *echad ha-ben ve-echad ha-bat shavin be-nichsei ha-em* — sons and daughters inherit equally from the mother's estate.

His reasoning is the principle of *dayo*: when a law is derived through a *kal va-chomer*, the derived rule cannot exceed the source from which it was learned. The son was brought in by learning from the daughter — since the daughter inherits, certainly the son does. But this logic only establishes that the son inherits; it does not prove that he inherits more than the daughter. *Dayo la-ba min ha-din lihyot ke-nadon*: it is sufficient for the derived case to equal the source case. Therefore, son and daughter divide equally.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא קַמָּא – לָא דָּרֵישׁ ״דַּיּוֹ״?! וְהָא ״דַּיּוֹ״ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And doesn't the first *tanna*** also **interpret** verses employing the principle of: **It is sufficient** for the conclusion that emerges from an *a fortiori* inference to be like its source? **But** the principle of: **It is sufficient,** which limits the extent of *a fortiori* inference, **is** found **in the Torah,** so how could he disagree with it?

The Gemara asks: does the *Tanna Kamma* — who rules that the son comes first — not accept the principle of *dayo*? After all, *dayo* is a biblical rule embedded in the framework of *kal va-chomer* itself. The source text is the episode of Miriam, where God says: if her father had spit in her face, she would be embarrassed for seven days. By *kal va-chomer*, if God Himself is angered, she should be shut out for more than seven days — perhaps fourteen. Yet the *pasuk* says seven days. Why? *Dayo la-ba min ha-din lihyot ke-nadon*: the derived case cannot exceed its source. (Tosafot notes that the logic would in principle yield more than seven, but since periods of exclusion come in seven-day units, and we cannot know precisely how much more, the *dayo* principle limits it to the original seven.)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנְיָא: מִדִּין קַל וָחוֹמֶר כֵּיצַד? ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֶל מֹשֶׁה: וְאָבִיהָ יָרֹק יָרַק בְּפָנֶיהָ, הֲלֹא תִכָּלֵם שִׁבְעַת יָמִים?״ קַל וָחוֹמֶר לַשְּׁכִינָה – אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר! אֶלָּא דַּיּוֹ לַבָּא מִן הַדִּין לִהְיוֹת כַּנִּדּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that explains the hermeneutical principles: **How** does **an *a fortiori* inference** work? The verse states with regard to Miriam, after she spoke ill of her brother Moses: **"And the Lord said to Moses: If her father had but spit in her face, should she not hide in shame seven days?"** (Numbers 12:14). The verse is saying that if a father spits in the face of his daughter and reprimands her, she would feel shame for seven days. By **an *a fortiori*** inference it is derived that Miriam, who suffered the more severe reprimand **of the Divine Presence,** should be ostracized for **fourteen** days. Why then, was Miriam ostracized for only seven days? **Rather, it is sufficient for the** conclusion that **emerges from** an *a fortiori* **inference to be like** its **source,** so her punishment cannot be for longer than the punishment of one who is reprimanded by a father.

So why does the *Tanna Kamma* rule that the son comes before the daughter from the mother's estate, apparently exceeding the *dayo* limitation? 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָלְמָא דָּרֵישׁ ״דַּיּוֹ״; וְשָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״מִמַּטּוֹת״ – מַקִּישׁ מַטֵּה הָאֵם לְמַטֵּה הָאָב; מָה מַטֵּה הָאָב – בֵּן קוֹדֵם לַבַּת, אַף מַטֵּה הָאֵם – בֵּן קוֹדֵם לַבַּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Generally,** the first *tanna* also **interprets** verses employing the principle that begins with: **It is sufficient, but it is different here** with regard to inheritance, **as the verse states:** "And every daughter who possesses an inheritance from the tribes of the children of Israel" (Numbers 36:8). The verse **juxtaposes** the **tribe of the mother to** the **tribe of the father.** Therefore, the first *tanna* holds that **just as** with regard to the **tribe of the father,** i.e., inheriting from one's father, **a son precedes a daughter, so too,** with regard to the **tribe of the mother,** i.e., inheriting from one's mother, **a son precedes a daughter.**

The Gemara answers: *d'l'rabbanan be'alma darsha* — of course the *Tanna Kamma* accepts *dayo* in general. But this case is different, because the *pasuk* itself provides an additional basis. The phrase *mimatot bnei Yisrael* contains the word *matot*, tribes — in the plural. This creates an *hekesh*, a juxtaposition: inheriting from the mother's tribe and inheriting from the father's tribe are placed side by side in that single word. Just as by the father's tribe the son precedes the daughter, so too by the mother's tribe the son precedes the daughter. This is not purely a *kal va-chomer* subject to *dayo*; it is an *hekesh* that transfers the rule directly. Hence the *Tanna Kamma* can rule that the son comes first without violating *dayo*.

## Mother's Estate Debate

### Rav Nisa'i Rebuke

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נִיתַּאי סָבַר לְמֶעְבַּד עוֹבָדָא כְּרַבִּי זְכַרְיָה בֶּן הַקַּצָּב; אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל: כְּמַאן – כִּזְכַרְיָה? אָפֵס זְכַרְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Nittai thought to perform an action,** i.e., issue a ruling, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Zekharya ben HaKatzav,** who taught that a son and daughter are equal with regard to inheriting from their mother. **Shmuel said to him: In accordance with whose** opinion do you wish to issue this ruling, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Zekharya? Zekharya is nil [*afes*],** i.e., his statement was not accepted as *halakha*.

The debate over this question — whether sons and daughters inherit equally from the mother, or whether sons retain their priority — generated intense controversy among the Amoraim. Rav Nisa'i wished to rule in practice like Rabbi Zechariah ben HaKatzav and divide the mother's estate equally between sons and daughters. Shmuel rebuked him sharply: *ke-man? ke-Zechariah? efes Zechariah* — who are you ruling like? Zechariah is a zero; forget him entirely.

### Rabbi Tavla Rav Nachman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי טַבְלָא עֲבַד עוֹבָדָא כְּרַבִּי זְכַרְיָה בֶּן הַקַּצָּב. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן: מַאי הַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַב חִינָּנָא בַּר שֶׁלֶמְיָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי זְכַרְיָה בֶּן הַקַּצָּב. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל אַהְדַּר (בָּךְ), וְאִי לָא – מַפֵּיקְנָא לָךְ רַב חִינָּנָא בַּר שֶׁלֶמְיָא מֵאוּנָּיךְ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates further: **Rabbi Tavla performed an action,** i.e., issued a ruling, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Zekharya ben HaKatzav. Rav Naḥman said to him: What is this?** Rabbi Tavla **said to him: As Rav Ḥinnana bar Shelamya says in the name of Rav:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Zekharya ben HaKatzav.** Rav Naḥman **said to him: Go retract your** ruling, **and if** you do **not, I will take Rav Ḥinnana bar Shelamya out of your ear,** i.e., I will punish you severely for ruling against the accepted *halakha*.

Rabbi Tavla actually issued a ruling in practice following Rabbi Zechariah ben HaKatzav, dividing the mother's estate equally. Rav Nachman confronted him: *mai hai?* — what is this? Rabbi Tavla defended himself by citing a tradition in the name of Rav — through Rabbi Chinana bar Shmaya — that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Zechariah ben HaKatzav. Rav Nachman was unmoved. He told Rabbi Tavla: *zil la-hadar bakh* — go and reverse yourself. He demanded not merely a change of position, but reversal of the actual ruling in the case at hand — taking the property back from the daughters and giving it to the sons. *Ve'ei lo, mafki-na lakh Rabbi Chinana bar Shmaya me-unakh* — and if you do not, I will pull Rabbi Chinana bar Shmaya right out of your ears. The *Rashbam* explains this as a threat to place Rabbi Chinana bar Shmaya in *cherem*, which would invalidate him as a source and prevent Rabbi Tavla from relying on him. *Rabbeinu Gershom* interprets it differently: Rav Nachman is threatening to compel Rabbi Tavla to compensate the sons from his own assets for having improperly awarded property to the daughters.

### Rav Huna Bar Chiya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא בַּר חִיָּיא סָבַר לְמֶעְבַּד עוֹבָדָא כְּרַבִּי זְכַרְיָה בֶּן הַקַּצָּב; אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן: מַאי הַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי זְכַרְיָה בֶּן הַקַּצָּב. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֶשְׁלַח לֵיהּ? אִיכְּסִיף. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַשְׁתָּא כִּי נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא, אַיתְרֵיסְתְּ לְקִבְלִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates further: **Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya thought to perform an action,** i.e., issue a ruling, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Zekharya ben HaKatzav. Rav Naḥman said to him: What is this?** Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya **said to him: As Rav Huna says** that **Rav says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Zekharya ben HaKatzav.** Rav Naḥman **said to him: Shall I send** a messenger **to** Rav Huna to ask him if that is indeed what he said? Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya **was embarrassed,** as he was not certain that Rav Huna actually said this ruling. Rav Naḥman **said to him: Now, if Rav Huna were** to have **died, you would have been brazen** enough to say **before me** that this is what he said, but since he is still alive, you cannot be so brazen.

Rav Huna bar Chiya similarly wished to rule like Rabbi Zechariah ben HaKatzav. Rav Nachman again confronted him: *mai hai?* Rav Huna bar Chiya cited a tradition from Rav Huna in the name of Rav — not merely through Rabbi Chinana bar Shmaya, but through the great authority Rav Huna himself — that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Zechariah ben HaKatzav. Rav Nachman responded: *eshlach lei?* — shall I send to Rav Huna and ask him directly whether he really holds this position? At that, Rav Huna bar Chiya was embarrassed — *ichsif* — and backed down. The *Rashbam* explains that he had honestly transmitted the tradition from Rav Huna, but feared that under Rav Nachman's relentless pressure, Rav Huna himself might reverse his position if directly confronted. Rav Nachman pressed further: *hashta delav dachna apeih de-Rav Huna* — now that Rav Huna is still alive and I can go check your claim, you concede; had he not been available, you would have stood your ground against me unjustifiably.

It is worth noting which authorities line up on each side. Rav Nachman — Shmuel's foremost student in Babylonia — is fiercely opposed to the equal-division position, and he invokes Shmuel himself on this. Those who favor equal division appeal to traditions in the name of Rav. Rav himself came originally from *Eretz Yisrael*, where women had considerably stronger legal standing than in Babylonia — and Rav Nachman is a thoroughgoing Babylonian. Whether that geographic and cultural divide contributes to this dispute is worth considering, though it cannot be stated with certainty.

### Rav Nachman Traditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיהוּ כְּמַאן סַבְרַהּ? כִּי הָא דְּרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי זְכַרְיָה בֶּן הַקַּצָּב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And he,** Rav Naḥman, **in accordance with whose** opinion **does he hold?** As there were various sages who ruled in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Zekharya ben HaKatzav, why did Rav Naḥman not agree? The Gemara answers: He holds **in accordance with this** statement **of Rav and Shmuel, who both say:** The ***halakha*** is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Zekharya ben HaKatzav.**

Rav Nachman's own position rests on a different tradition from Rav — one that explicitly contradicts what the other authorities cited: *ki ha de-Rav u-Shmuel, de'amri tarveihu ein halacha ke-Rabbi Zechariah ben HaKatzav* — both Rav and Shmuel said: the *halacha* does not follow Rabbi Zechariah ben HaKatzav. So we have a genuine dispute about what Rav himself ruled, with Rav Nachman holding that Rav and Shmuel both rejected equal division.

## Patriarchal Logic

### Tribes Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיסְתְּמִיךְ וְאָזֵיל רַבִּי יַנַּאי אַכַּתְפָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׂמְלַאי שַׁמָּעֵיהּ, וְאָתֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה נְשִׂיאָה לְאַפַּיְיהוּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בַּר אִינָשׁ דַּאֲתָא לְקִיבְלַנָא, הוּא יָאֵי, וְגוּלְּתֵיהּ יָאֵי. כִּי מְטָא לְגַבֵּיהּ, גַּשְּׁשַׁהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דֵּין – שִׁיעוּרֵיהּ כְּשַׂק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rabbi Yannai,** who had already grown old and whose eyes were dim, **would walk leaning on the shoulder of Rabbi Simlai, his attendant, and Rabbi Yehuda Nesia came toward them.** Rabbi Simlai **said to** Rabbi Yannai: The **person who is coming toward us is handsome and his cloak** is **handsome. When** Rabbi Yehuda Nesia **reached him,** Rabbi Yannai **felt** his cloak and **said to** Rabbi Simlai: Concerning **this** cloak, **its measurement** with regard to the *halakhot* of ritual impurity **is like the** measurement of **sackcloth,** meaning it was a cloak of inferior quality, as it was thick and rough.

The Gemara now introduces a narrative that brings the conceptual stakes into sharp focus. Rabbi Yannai, elderly and infirm, was being carried on the shoulders of his attendant Rav Simlai. His attendant described a man approaching them — a striking individual with a beautiful cloak. When they drew close, Rabbi Yannai felt the garment and remarked: *dein shirei tasak* — this is an inferior material, the dimensions of which would qualify as sackcloth for *tumah* purposes. Rabbi Yehuda Nesia, grandson of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, then asked Rabbi Yannai: *minayin le-ven she-kodem le-vat be-nichsei ha-em* — what is the source that a son precedes a daughter in inheriting from the mother?

Rabbi Yannai answered from the *hekesh* we have already discussed: the *pasuk* says *matot* — tribes — placing the mother's tribe and the father's tribe in one word. Just as by the father's tribe the son comes before the daughter, so too by the mother's tribe.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ: מִנַּיִן לְבֵן שֶׁקּוֹדֵם לַבַּת בְּנִכְסֵי הָאֵם? אָמַר לֵיהּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״מַטּוֹת״ – מַקִּישׁ מַטֵּה הָאֵם לְמַטֵּה הָאָב; מָה מַטֵּה הָאָב – בֵּן קוֹדֵם לַבַּת, אַף מַטֵּה הָאֵם – בֵּן קוֹדֵם לַבַּת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי – מָה מַטֵּה הָאָב, בְּכוֹר נוֹטֵל פִּי שְׁנַיִם; אַף מַטֵּה הָאֵם, בְּכוֹר נוֹטֵל פִּי שְׁנַיִם?!
>
> **Translation:** When Rabbi Yehuda Nesia met Rabbi Yannai he **asked him: From where** is it derived **that a son precedes a daughter with regard to the mother's property?** Rabbi Yannai **said to him: As it is written:** "And every daughter who possesses an inheritance from **the tribes** of the children of Israel" (Numbers 36:8). The verse **juxtaposes** the **tribe of the mother to** the **tribe of the father.** This teaches that **just as** with regard to the **tribe of the father a son precedes a daughter, so too,** with regard to the **tribe of the mother, a son precedes a daughter.** Rabbi Yehuda Nesia **said to him: If** so, if these two *halakhot* are in fact juxtaposed, the following should also be derived: **Just as** with regard to the inheritance of **the father's tribe a firstborn takes a double** portion, **so too,** with regard to inheritance from **the mother's tribe,** when he inherits from his mother **a firstborn takes a double** portion.

### Bechor Double Portion

Rabbi Yehuda Nesia pressed further: *ei ma mateh ha-av bechor notel pi shnayim, af mateh ha-em bechor notel pi shnayim* — if you are drawing a complete parallel between the mother's tribe and the father's tribe, then just as the firstborn son takes a double portion from the father, the firstborn son should take a double portion from the mother as well. Once you adopt the non-egalitarian position and rule that sons take priority, why not go all the way and apply the full law of *bechora* — double portion — to the mother's estate?

Rabbi Yannai told his attendant: *gud* — let's go. This person, he said, does not really want to learn — *leit dein tzevi lemilaf* — he just wants to be contentious. The question may have felt like retaliation for the remark about the cloak. But the Gemara is unwilling to let it drop. The question is entirely legitimate: if we are applying the *hekesh* fully, what is the basis for excluding *pi shnayim*?

## Bechor Mother Estate

### Abaye Lo Pronoun

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְשַׁמָּעֵיהּ: גּוּד, לֵית דֵּין צָבֵי לְמֵילַף. וְטַעְמָא מַאי? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, אָמַר קְרָא: ״בְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר יִמָּצֵא לוֹ״; ״לוֹ״ – וְלֹא לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to his attendant: Take** me away from here, as **this** man, Rabbi Yehuda Nesia, **does not desire to learn** but is instead raising difficulties that are easily resolved. **And what is the reason** he does not receive a double portion? **Abaye said: The verse** concerning the double portion received by a firstborn son **states:** "By giving him a double portion **of all that he has"** (Deuteronomy 21:17), indicating that he receives a double portion in all that **he** has, **but not** in all that **she** has.

Abaye answers from the *pasuk* governing the *bechor*: *bechol asher yimatzei lo* — the firstborn receives double from everything found belonging to *him*. The *lo* — "to him" — refers to the father. The firstborn gets double from the father's estate, not from the mother's.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי בָּחוּר שֶׁנָּשָׂא אַלְמָנָה, אֲבָל בָּחוּר שֶׁנָּשָׂא בְּתוּלָה – הָכִי נָמֵי דְּשָׁקֵיל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** why not **say** that **this statement** applies in the case of **a bachelor who married a widow,** i.e., a man who marries a woman who already has children from another man, so that the firstborn son is his firstborn but not hers, **but** in the case of **a bachelor who married a virgin, so too** the *halakha* will be **that** their firstborn son **will take** a double portion of his mother's inheritance?

The Gemara challenges: perhaps this *pasuk* is limited to a case of a *bachur* — a man on his first marriage — who married an *almanah*, a widow. In that case the child is a *bechor* for the father but not for the mother, since she already had children. Perhaps it is only in that configuration — firstborn to the father, not firstborn to the mother — that the child does not receive double from the mother's estate. But if the child is both the father's firstborn and the mother's firstborn, perhaps he would receive double from her estate as well.

This raises an important underlying distinction. The *bechor* for *nachalah* purposes is defined by the father — *reshit ono*, his first strength — not by the mother. For *pidyon haben*, the relevant status is *peter rechem*, the first issue of the womb, and thus defined by the mother. The two can come apart: if a man previously had a son from another woman and now has a son with a new wife, that new son is a *peter rechem* for his mother and obligates *pidyon haben*, but does not receive the double portion of *bechora* for *nachalah*, since the father's firstborn is elsewhere. Conversely, a man's firstborn son by his first wife is the *bechor le-nachalah* regardless of whether his mother previously had children.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, אָמַר קְרָא: ״רֵאשִׁית אֹנוֹ״; ״אוֹנוֹ״ – וְלֹא אוֹנָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** There is a different derivation teaching that *halakha*, as the same **verse states:** "For he is **the first fruits of his strength [*ono*]"** (Deuteronomy 21:17), indicating that the *halakha* of the double portion is stated with regard to **his strength, but not her strength,** thereby excluding a son who is the firstborn of his mother alone.

The Gemara further notes that *reshit ono* also teaches that the *bechor le-nachalah* must be a viable birth — a child whose death would cause the father grief, *mi she-libo davah alav*. If a woman had a prior miscarriage or non-viable birth, that does not affect who qualifies as *peter rechem* only if it occurs before the threshold of forty days; but for *nachalah*, what matters is the first viable son born to the father.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא מִבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ לַבָּא אַחַר נְפָלִים, דְּלֶהֱוֵי בְּכוֹר לְנַחֲלָה – מִי שֶׁלִּבּוֹ דָּוֶוה עָלָיו, יָצָא זֶה שֶׁאֵין לִבּוֹ דָּוֶוה עָלָיו!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: But **that** verse **is required to** derive the *halakha* of a child who **came** into the world **after** his mother gave birth to **non-viable newborns** who did not reach the full term of nine months. This verse teaches **that** such a firstborn son **should be a firstborn with regard to inheritance,** even though he is not a firstborn with regard to the mitzva to redeem one's firstborn son. How is this *halakha* derived from the verse? The term "his strength [*ono*]" is understood as referring to acute mourning [*aninut*], in the following manner: A firstborn son for the purpose of inheritance of land is only **one whose** father's **heart is pained about him** when he dies, i.e., a son who lives beyond birth, **excluding this** non-viable newborn **whose** father's **heart is not pained about him** when he dies. Therefore, the next viable son to be born after a non-viable newborn is considered the firstborn.

The Gemara then pushes back: perhaps the *pasuk* ראשית אונו — *reshit ono* — is needed entirely for this latter purpose, to teach about the viability requirement, and cannot do double duty as a proof that the double portion comes only from the father's estate.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, לֵימָא קְרָא: ״כִּי הוּא רֵאשִׁית אוֹן״ – מַאי ״אוֹנוֹ״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If so,** that the sole purpose of the term: "His strength" is to teach that *halakha*, **let the verse say: For he is the first fruit of strength [*on*],** omitting the possessive "his," represented by the letter *vav*. **What** is derived from the more expanded term: **"His strength [*ono*],"** which indicates specifically the father's strength? **Conclude two** conclusions **from it,** both with regard to mourning and with regard to the status of the firstborn as the father's heir specifically.

The Gemara responds: *im ken leima kra ki hu reshit on* — if the only purpose were to establish viability, the *pasuk* could simply have said "his first strength" without the personal pronoun *ono*, "his." The presence of *ono* — the possessive — teaches something additional: *shema mina tartei* — derive two things from it. Both that it must be the father's firstborn viable son, and that the double portion applies only to the father's estate and not the mother's.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי, אֵימָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי אַלְמוֹן שֶׁנָּשָׂא בְּתוּלָה, אֲבָל בָּחוּר שֶׁנָּשָׂא בְּתוּלָה – הָכִי נָמֵי דְּשָׁקֵיל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** one can **still say** that **this statement** applies in a case of **a widower who married a virgin,** so that he already has a firstborn from his previous marriage who is the first fruit of his strength. And the son born to him and his second wife is not the first fruit of his strength, and it is this type of firstborn who does not inherit a double portion of his mother's estate. **But** in a case of **a bachelor who married a virgin, so too** the *halakha* will be **that** their shared firstborn son **will take** a double portion of his mother's inheritance.

A final challenge: perhaps the *pasuk* ראשית אונו applies only to a case of an *almon* — a man on his second marriage — who married a *betulah*. In that case the son is a *bechor* to his mother but not to his father. Maybe in that limited configuration — where the firstborn status derives only from the mother and not from the father — the son does not receive double from his mother's estate, since his *bechora* status lacks the father's stamp. But if the son is a full *bechor* to his father and simultaneously the mother's firstborn, perhaps he should receive a double portion from her estate.

### Rava Father Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״לוֹ מִשְׁפַּט הַבְּכֹרָה״ – מִשְׁפַּט הַבְּכוֹרָה לָאִישׁ, וְלֹא מִשְׁפַּט הַבְּכוֹרָה לָאִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** There is a different derivation teaching that *halakha*, as the same **verse states: "The right of the firstborn is his"** (Deuteronomy 21:17). The emphasis on "his" indicates that **the primogeniture** applies specifically **to a man, and the primogeniture does not** apply **to a woman,** whether the woman is the one inheriting or is the legator.

Rava resolves this definitively. The *pasuk* says *mishpat ha-bechora lo* — "the law of firstborn is to him." Rava reads *lo* not as referring to the son, but as referring to a male parent: *mishpat ha-bechora le-ish ve-lo mishpat ha-bechora le-ishah* — the law of firstborn applies to a man's estate, not to a woman's estate. The double portion of *bechora* is a rule governing what flows from a father, not from a mother. Full stop.

The irony, as the Gemara itself makes apparent, is that Rabbi Yannai's dismissal — *this man doesn't want to learn* — was wrong. It took the Gemara considerable effort, through Abaye's preliminary answer and Rava's final resolution, to establish that the son coming first by the mother's estate does not entail the firstborn receiving a double portion. This confirms that once the property passes through a woman, the full patriarchal structure — with its hierarchy of priority and its double portion for the firstborn — no longer applies intact. The son retains his priority, but the special law of *bechora*, which is the signature institution of the patriarchal inheritance system, applies only when inheriting from a father.

## Husband Inherits Wife

### Rava Sharp Knife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאִישׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ וְכוּ׳. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״שְׁאֵרוֹ״ – זוֹ אִשְׁתּוֹ, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהַבַּעַל יוֹרֵשׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ. יָכוֹל אַף הִיא תִּירָשֶׁנּוּ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְיָרַשׁ אוֹתָהּ״ – הוּא יוֹרֵשׁ אוֹתָהּ, וְאֵין הִיא יוֹרֶשֶׁת אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And a man** with regard to **his wife** inherits but does not bequeath. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? The Gemara explains: **As the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse concerning inheritance states: "And if his father has no brothers, then you shall give his inheritance to **his kinsman [*she'ero*]** who is next to him of his family, and he shall inherit it" (Numbers 27:11). **This** kinsman is **one's wife,** and the Torah **teaches that a husband inherits** from **his wife,** as the Gemara will explain later. One **might** have thought that **she would also inherit** from **him;** therefore, **the verse states: "And he shall inherit it,"** with the word "it" written in the feminine "*otah*," which can also be translated as: Her. This teaches that it is **he who inherits** from **her, but she does not inherit** from **him.**

The Gemara now turns to a husband inheriting from his wife. One Tanna derives this from the words *she'ero zo ishto* — "his flesh, that is his wife" — reading the phrase *ve-nataten et nachalato li-she'ero* as giving the estate to the wife. From there, the Gemara derives by implication — *hu yoresh otah ve-ein hi yoreshet oto* — that he inherits from her, but she does not inherit from him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא קְרָאֵי לָאו הָכִי כְּתִיבִי! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, תָּרֵיץ הָכִי: ״וּנְתַתֶּם אֶת נַחֲלָתוֹ לַקָּרוֹב אֵלָיו, שְׁאֵרוֹ וְיָרַשׁ אֹתָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this explanation: **But the verses are not written like this.** The verse states that "his kinsman" inherits from the deceased, so that if that term is referring to one's wife, a wife should inherit from her husband. **Abaye said: Answer like this,** i.e., as if the verse were split into two parts. The first part is: **Then you shall give his inheritance to he who is next to him,** which is a general statement with regard to the inheritance of relatives. The second is: **His kinsman, and he shall inherit it,** meaning that a husband inherits from his kinsman, referring to his wife.

Abaye proposes a rereading of the *pasuk* to extract this: *ve-nataten et nachalat she'ero lo*, rearranging the words to mean "you shall give the inheritance of his wife to him."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: סַכִּינָא חֲרִיפָא מַפְסְקָא קְרָאֵי?! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: ״וּנְתַתֶּם אֶת נַחֲלַת שְׁאֵרוֹ לוֹ״ – קָא סָבַר גּוֹרְעִין וּמוֹסִיפִין, וְדוֹרְשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** Does **a sharp knife cut verses?** How can you split the verse, rearrange it and omit letters from its words? Abaye's derivation rearranges the words and omits the letter *lamed* from the term "to his kinsman [*leshe'ero*]." **Rather, Rava said: This** is what the verse **is saying: And you shall give the inheritance of his kinsman [*she'ero*] to him.** The letters should be arranged differently, removing the letter *lamed* from the word *leshe'ero* and the letter *vav* from the word *naḥalato* and combining them to form the word *lo*, meaning: To him. Accordingly, the verse teaches that a husband inherits from his wife. The Gemara explains that Rava **holds that** the Sages **subtract and add and interpret homiletically,** meaning that letters can be removed from words and appended to each other, and a *halakha* can be derived from the new word formed by the combination of letters.

Rava objects: *sakina charifa mafseka kra'ei?* — is a sharp knife going to cut the *pasuk* like that? You cannot dismember the verse so drastically. Instead, Rava proposes a minimal rearrangement — transposing two letters (*vav* and *lamed*) between *nachalato* and *li-she'ero*, yielding *nachalat she'ero lo*, "the inheritance of his wife to him" — operating on the principle of *gore'in, mosifin, ve-dorshin*: you may subtract a letter, add it elsewhere, and derive your law.

### Rambam Oraita Status

Because these *drashot* are so strained — involving substantial rereading of the *pasuk* — the Rambam holds that a husband's inheritance from his wife is only *derabbanan*, not a biblical law. Nearly all other Rishonim disagree and treat it as a *de'oraita* derivation. The Rambam's position has further implications: he also holds that a *kohen* becoming *tameh* to his wife is permitted only *derabbanan*, on the grounds that the Rabbis treated her as a *meit mitzvah* — an unattended corpse — obligating her husband to bury her. The other Rishonim assume the full *de'oraita* reading throughout.

### She'ero Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי תַּנָּא מַיְיתֵי לַהּ מֵהָכָא – דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְיָרַשׁ אוֹתָהּ״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהַבַּעַל יוֹרֵשׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And this *tanna* cites** the *halakha* that a husband inherits from his wife **from here, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **"And he shall inherit it,"** with the word "it" written in the feminine "*otah*," which can also be translated as: Her. This **teaches that a husband inherits** from **his wife;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva,** similar to the derivation cited in the *baraita* above.

The *braita* states: *she'ero zo ishto* — "his flesh, that is his wife." The *pasuk* *ki im li-she'ero ha-karov elav* — in the context of the laws of *tumah* for a *kohen* — refers to blood relatives: *le-imo, le-aviv, le-vno, le-vito, le-achiv, u-le-achoto ha-betulah*. The wife is not mentioned there. So the *pshat* of *she'ero* would normally mean blood relative, not wife. Once we read *she'ero* as the wife in the inheritance context, the *pshat* would suggest that the wife inherits from the husband. The Gemara then has to rework the verse to derive the opposite — that the husband inherits from the wife. This creates a double difficulty: first, the word *she'ero* probably means blood relative; second, once we extend it to wife, the plain reading implies she inherits from him, and we have to strain further to reverse that.

One might ask: if the *halacha* were simply that a husband does not inherit from his wife, who would inherit her? Her father, her brother — the property would return to her ancestral family. It is specifically the rule that the husband inherits that ensures the *nachala* will leave her tribe, passing to a man from a different tribal affiliation. This is exactly what the uncles of *Bnot Tzelafchad* feared — that once women inherit, the property could transfer to another tribe, whether through the wife's sons or through her husband.

### Abaye Hermeneutics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְכׇל בַּת יֹרֶשֶׁת נַחֲלָה מִמַּטּוֹת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, לְאֶחָד מִמִּשְׁפַּחַת וְגוֹ׳״ – בַּהֲסָבַת הַבַּעַל הַכָּתוּב מְדַבַּר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* quotes another *tanna*. **Rabbi Yishmael says:** This derivation **is not necessary, as** the verse **states: "And every daughter who possesses an inheritance from the tribes of the children of Israel** shall be wife **to one of the family** of the tribe of her father" (Numbers 36:8). The purpose of this mitzva was so that the land she inherited from her father's tribe will not be transferred to another tribe upon her death. **The verse speaks of a transfer** of inheritance from one tribe to another **by means of the husband,** i.e., she must not marry a man from another tribe so that the land will not be transferred to that tribe when her husband inherits from her upon her death. This indicates that a husband inherits from his wife.

As noted above, Abaye proposes to read the *pasuk* as *ve-nataten et nachalato li-karov elav she'ero, ve-yarash otah* — taking *she'ero* as a separate clause that addresses the wife specifically: as for his *she'ero*, his wife, *ve-yarash otah*, he shall inherit from her. Rava rejects this as too violent a restructuring of the verse — hence the "sharp knife" objection — and instead proposes the minimal two-letter transposition yielding *nachalat she'ero lo*, supported by the hermeneutic principle of *gore'in u-mosifin ve-dorshin*.

### Akiva Yishmael Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל בַּת יֹרֶשֶׁת נַחֲלָה מִמַּטּוֹת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, לְאֶחָד מִמִּשְׁפַּחַת וְגוֹ׳״ – בַּהֲסָבַת הַבַּעַל הַכָּתוּב מְדַבַּר. וְאוֹמֵר: ״וְלֹא תִסֹּב נַחֲלָה לִבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מִמַּטֶּה אֶל מַטֶּה״. וְאוֹמֵר: ״וְלֹא תִסֹּב נַחֲלָה מִמַּטֶּה לְמַטֶּה אַחֵר״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yishmael continues: **And** another verse **states: "So shall no inheritance of the children of Israel transfer from tribe to tribe"** (Numbers 36:7), **and** another verse **states: "So shall no inheritance transfer from one tribe to another tribe"** (Numbers 36:9), also indicating that a husband inherits from his wife.

Rabbi Akiva derives the rule directly from the words *ve-yarash otah* — he shall inherit from her — reading those two words in isolation as a standalone statement: the husband inherits from his wife. Rabbi Yishmael says this is unnecessary: *eino tzarich* — the rule can be derived from elsewhere.

### Yishmael Five Proofs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹמֵר: ״וְאֶלְעָזָר בֶּן אַהֲרֹן מֵת, וַיִּקְבְּרוּ אֹתוֹ בְּגִבְעַת פִּנְחָס בְּנוֹ״ – וְכִי מִנַּיִן לְפִנְחָס שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה לוֹ לְאֶלְעָזָר? אֶלָּא מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנָּשָׂא פִּנְחָס אִשָּׁה וּמֵתָה, וִירָשָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yishmael continues: **And** the verse in the Prophets **states: "And Elazar, the son of Aaron, died; and they buried him in the Hill of Pinehas his son,** which was given him in Mount Ephraim" (Joshua 24:33). **And from where** did **Pinehas have** land **that** his father, **Elazar, did not have? Rather,** this **teaches that Pinehas married a woman** who inherited her father's land, **and she died and he inherited** from **her** so that he had his own land. This also indicates that a husband inherits from his wife.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹמֵר: ״וּשְׂגוּב הוֹלִיד אֶת יָאִיר, וַיְהִי לוֹ עֶשְׂרִים וְשָׁלֹשׁ עָרִים בְּאֶרֶץ הַגִּלְעָד״ –
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yishmael continues. **And** the verse in the Writings **states: "And Seguv begot Yair, who had twenty-three cities in the land of Gilead"** (I Chronicles 2:22).

Rabbi Yishmael marshals a series of *psukim* to demonstrate that a husband inherits from his wife, drawing from the context of *Bnot Tzelafchad*. The *pasuk* states *ve-chol bat yoreshet nachala mi-matot Bnei Yisrael le-echad mi-mishpachat mateh aviha tihyeh le-ish le-ishah* — any daughter who inherits shall marry within her tribe — *ve-al esek ha-ba'al ha-katuv medaber*, and it is the matter of the husband that the *pasuk* is addressing. If a woman with an inheritance marries, the property goes to a different tribe — which implies the husband inherits it.

The *pasuk ve-lo tisov nachala li-vnei Yisrael mi-mateh el mateh acher* — the inheritance shall not transfer from tribe to tribe — and a nearly identical *pasuk* that follows both point to the same conclusion: this prohibition is necessary only because a husband inheriting from his wife is the mechanism by which the transfer occurs.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מִנַּיִן לְיָאִיר שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה לוֹ לִשְׂגוּב? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנָּשָׂא יָאִיר אִשָּׁה וּמֵתָה, וִירָשָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where** did **Yair have** land **that** his father, **Seguv, did not have?** Rather, this **teaches that Yair married a woman** who inherited her father's land, **and she died and he inherited** from **her** so that he had his own land. This also indicates that a husband inherits from his wife.

Rabbi Yishmael also cites two narrative proofs. First: *ve-Elazar ben Aharon met va-yikberu oto be-givat Pinchas bno* — Elazar died and was buried in the plot of his son Pinchas. Where did Pinchas get a plot that had not come from his father Elazar? *Ela melamed she-nasa Pinchas isha u-meta ve-yarsha* — it must be that Pinchas married a woman, she died, and he inherited from her. Second: *u-Segev holid et Yair va-yihyu lo esrim ve-shalosh arim be-eretz ha-Gilad* — Segev fathered Yair, who had twenty-three cities in Gilead. Where did Yair get cities that his father Segev did not have? *Melamed she-nasa Yair isha u-meta ve-yarsha* — he must have married a woman and inherited from her.

The Gemara notes that each of these proofs is inconclusive on its own — Pinchas might have purchased the land, or received it through his mother; Yair's cities might similarly have come through his mother rather than a wife. The question of whether any of these *psukim* conclusively establishes that it is specifically the *husband* who inherits the wife, rather than the wife's property passing to her *sons*, remains to be resolved — and that discussion will continue.
