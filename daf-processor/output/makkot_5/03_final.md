# Makkot 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Edim Zomemim Basics

### Witnesses & Conspiracy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין הָעֵדִים נַעֲשִׂים זוֹמְמִין עַד שֶׁיָּזֵימּוּ אֶת עַצְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Witnesses are not rendered conspiring** witnesses **until** the witnesses who come to render them conspiring **impeach** the witnesses **themselves** and not merely their testimony.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? אָמְרוּ ״מְעִידִין אֲנִי בְּאִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי שֶׁהָרַג אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ״. אָמְרוּ לָהֶם: ״הֵיאַךְ אַתֶּם מְעִידִין? שֶׁהֲרֵי נֶהֱרָג זֶה אוֹ הַהוֹרֵג זֶה הָיָה עִמָּנוּ אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם בְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי!״ – אֵין אֵלּוּ זוֹמְמִין. אֲבָל אָמְרוּ לָהֶם: ״הֵיאַךְ אַתֶּם מְעִידִין? שֶׁהֲרֵי אַתֶּם הֱיִיתֶם עִמָּנוּ אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם בְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי!״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ זוֹמְמִין, וְנֶהֱרָגִין עַל פִּיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **How so?** A set of witnesses **said: We testify with regard to a man** called **so-and-so that he killed a person,** and they attested to the precise time and place that the murder took place. Then, a second set of witnesses came to court and **said to them: How can you testify** about this event? **This** person who **was killed, or this** person **who killed, was with us,** i.e., with the second set of witnesses, on **that day in such and such place,** which is not the location identified by the first set of witnesses. In that case, although the second set of witnesses contradicted the testimony of the first set, **these** first witnesses are **not** rendered **conspiring** witnesses. **But** if the second set of witnesses came to court and **said to them: How can you testify** about that event? **You were with us** on **that day in such and such place.** In this case, **these** first witnesses are rendered **conspiring** witnesses, **and are executed on the basis of their,** i.e., the second set's, testimony.

The second *mishnah* on daf 5a opens with a foundational statement about *edim zomemim*: *ein ha-edim na'asim zomemim ad she-yazumu et atzman* — witnesses cannot be declared conspiratorial and subjected to the reciprocal punishment until their testimony is proven conspiratorial through their own persons. The *mishnah* then asks *keitzad* — how does this work in practice?

The *mishnah* gives the following case: witnesses testify that so-and-so killed someone. Another set of witnesses then comes forward and says, "How can you testify to this murder? The murder victim — or the murderer — was with us in a different place at that very time." The *mishnah* rules: *ein elu zomemim*. These are not *edim zomemim*, and the conspiratorial punishment does not apply.

What the second set of witnesses has done is undermine the *content* of the testimony — they have shown that the alleged murder could not have happened as described, because the victim or perpetrator was elsewhere. But this is a fundamentally different kind of challenge than *hazamah*. It is *hakhashah* — contradiction of the facts — and while it may invalidate the testimony, it does not make the witnesses *zomemim*.

By contrast, if the second group of witnesses says, "How can you testify to this? *You* were with *us* in a different place on that day" — then the rule is *harei elu zomemim*. The decisive element is not whether the murder could have occurred, but whether *these witnesses* could have been present to observe it. The falsehood must attach to the witnesses themselves, to their very capacity to have witnessed what they claim. This is the essential mechanism of *hazamah*.

### Hakhashah vs Hazamah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּאוּ אֲחֵרִים וֶהֱזִימּוּם, בָּאוּ אֲחֵרִים וֶהֱזִימּוּם, אֲפִילּוּ מֵאָה – כּוּלָּם יֵהָרְגוּ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִיסְטָטִית הִיא זוֹ, וְאֵינוֹ נֶהֱרָג אֶלָּא כַּת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** If **other** witnesses, i.e., a third set, **came** and corroborated the testimony of the first set of witnesses, and the second set of witnesses testified that this third set of witnesses were also with them elsewhere that day **and rendered them conspiring** witnesses, and similarly, if yet **other** witnesses, i.e., a fourth set, **came** and corroborated the testimony of the first set of witnesses **and** the second set **rendered them conspiring** witnesses, **even** if **one hundred** sets of witnesses were all rendered conspiring witnesses by the same second set of witnesses, **all of them are executed** on the basis of their testimony, as the authority of two witnesses is equivalent to the authority of numerous witnesses. **Rabbi Yehuda says: This** situation where a set of witnesses renders all the others conspiring witnesses **is a conspiracy [*istatit*],** as there is room for suspicion that they simply decided to impeach all witnesses who offer that testimony, **and** it is **only the first set alone** that is **executed.**

The distinction between *hakhashah* and *hazamah* is the structural heart of this *mishnah*. *Hakhashah* attacks the truth of the alleged events; *hazamah* attacks the ability of the witnesses to have been present at those events. Only *hazamah* triggers the reciprocal punishment of *edim zomemim*.

The *mishnah* continues: if yet another set of witnesses comes and *hizimum* — proves the second group of witnesses to have been elsewhere as well — those too become *zomemim*. And if yet another group comes and does the same to the third group, and so on — *afilu me'ah*, even a hundred times — *kulam yeiharegu*, all of them are executed. Each successive group has been proven unable to have witnessed what they claimed, and so each is swept into the category of *zomemim*.

*Rabbi Yehuda* disagrees. His objection is grounded in the observation that with multiple groups contradicting each other in a chain, the process can reverse itself: the first group says the defendant committed murder, the second group invalidates them, the third group invalidates the second, which in principle restores credibility to the first — and so on indefinitely, back and forth. *Rabbi Yehuda* characterizes such a situation as an *istatit* — a cabal, an organized conspiracy — and holds that *ve-eino neherag ela kat harishona bilvad*, only the first group of witnesses, the ones who actually came to cause the defendant's death, should be executed. The subsequent groups, in his view, are too entangled in what appears to be a broader conspiratorial scheme to be held individually liable in the same way.

The *Gemara* will elaborate on this further, but already at this stage the *tanna kamma* and *Rabbi Yehuda* are in clear tension over how to handle cascading layers of *hazamah*.

### Sheker in Testimony Core

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב אַדָּא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהִנֵּה עֵד שֶׁקֶר הָעֵד שֶׁקֶר עָנָה״ – עַד שֶׁתִּשָּׁקֵר גּוּפָהּ שֶׁל עֵדוּת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the *halakha* that witnesses are rendered conspiring witnesses only if the second set testifies that the first set was with them elsewhere at that time, and not if they directly contradict the testimony of the first set, the Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rav Adda said:** It is derived from a verse, **as the verse states** with regard to conspiring witnesses: **"And the witness is a false witness; he testified falsely** against his brother" (Deuteronomy 19:18), indicating that they are not rendered conspiring witnesses **until the body of the testimony is rendered false,** i.e., the testimony with regard to the actual witnesses was proven wrong, as they were not there at the time of the event in question.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנוּ: ״לַעֲנוֹת בּוֹ סָרָה״ – עַד שֶׁתִּסְרֶה גּוּפָהּ שֶׁל עֵדוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** a different source for this *halakha*. It is stated in that passage: "If an unrighteous witness rise up against any man **to bear perverted witness against him"** (Deuteronomy 19:16), indicating that the witnesses are not rendered conspiring witnesses **until the body of the testimony is rendered perverted,** but not through contradiction of any aspect of the testimony.

The *Gemara* asks *mena hanei milei* — what is the source for the principle of *hazamah*? *Rav Adda* cites the verse *ve-hineh ed sheker ha-ed sheker ana* — the witness is a false witness, he has spoken falsely. The operative requirement is *ad she-tishaker gufa shel edut* — the falsehood must occupy the core of the testimony itself. This is why *hazamah* targets specifically the question of where the witnesses were at the time of the alleged crime and whether they could have witnessed it. Undermining the facts of what happened — who was where, whether the crime took place — does not constitute the kind of falsehood that the *pasuk* demands.

*Debei Rabbi Yishmael* derives the same principle from a different verse, using the phrase *la-anot bo sara* — to testify falsely about him — with the requirement being *ad she-tisreh gufo shel edut*, until there is a *sara*, a turning aside or flaw, at the very core of the testimony.

*Rava* notes that *lesareh* carries the connotation of *lasur* — to turn aside. The difference in emphasis between *Rav Adda* and *Rabbi Yishmael*'s school is subtle but worth noting: according to *Rav Adda*, the focus is on *sheker*, outright falsehood; according to *Rabbi Yishmael*'s school, the operative concept is *sara*, a flaw or deviation, which might represent a somewhat lower threshold — not necessarily total fabrication, but a defect at the core of the testimony that is sufficient to undermine it. Whether this represents a substantively different standard or simply a different *drasha* arriving at the same conclusion remains an open question.

## Geography & Time

### Visibility Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: בָּאוּ שְׁנַיִם וְאָמְרוּ: ״בְּמִזְרַח בִּירָה הָרַג פְּלוֹנִי אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ״, וּבָאוּ שְׁנַיִם וְאָמְרוּ: ״וַהֲלֹא בְּמַעֲרַב בִּירָה עִמָּנוּ הֱיִיתֶם!״, חָזֵינַן, אִי כִּדְקָיְימִי בְּמַעֲרַב בִּירָה מִיחְזֵא חָזוּ לְמִזְרַח בִּירָה – אֵין אֵלּוּ זוֹמְמִין, וְאִם לָאו – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ זוֹמְמִין.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava says: Two** witnesses **came and said: So-and-so killed a person to the east of a building [*bira*], and two** other witnesses **came** to court **and said** to the first set: **But were you not with us to the west of the building** at that time? How can you testify to an incident that transpired on the other side of the building? **We see: If, when** people **are standing to the west of the building they see to the east of the building, these** witnesses **are not conspiring** witnesses. **But if** it is **not** possible to see from one side of the building to the other, **these** witnesses **are conspiring** witnesses.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: לֵיחוּשׁ לִנְהוֹרָא בָּרְיָא, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious?** The Gemara answers: **Lest you say: Let us be concerned about** the possibility that these witnesses have particularly **good eyesight** and were able to see that far despite a typical person being unable to see that far. Therefore, Rava **teaches us** that one does not take that possibility into account.

*Rava* proceeds to apply these principles to concrete spatial and temporal cases. The first case: two witnesses testify that on the eastern side of a certain building, so-and-so killed someone. A second pair of witnesses then says, "How can you testify to this? You were with us on the western side of that same building at that time." What is the law?

*Rava* rules: *chazinan* — we go and investigate. If it is physically possible to see the eastern side of the building from the western side — that is, if the geometry of the structure permits a sightline from one side to the other — then *ein elu zomemim*, they are not *zomemim*, because we cannot rule out that they had visual access to what they claimed to have witnessed. But if it is impossible to see from one side to the other, *harei elu zomemim* — they are *zomemim*.

The *Gemara* asks *peshita* — isn't it obvious that one should simply investigate this question? The answer is that one might have thought we should be concerned about *nahora barya*, exceptionally good eyesight. Perhaps the witnesses possessed extraordinary vision that would have allowed them to see what normal people could not. *Ka mashma lan* — the ruling teaches us that we do not take such a possibility into account. We assess the case based on normal human capacity. The consequence is that witnesses who genuinely did observe the murder through some unusual circumstance — a perfect alignment of windows, a sliver of sightline — could in theory be declared *zomemim* and executed, while the actual murderer goes free. The system operates based on normal assumptions, not exceptional ones.

### Travel Distances

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: בָּאוּ שְׁנַיִם וְאָמְרוּ ״בְּסוּרָא בְּצַפְרָא בְּחַד בְּשַׁבְּתָא הָרַג פְּלוֹנִי אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ״, וּבָאוּ שְׁנַיִם וְאָמְרוּ: ״בְּפַנְיָא בְּחַד בְּשַׁבְּתָא עִמָּנוּ הֱיִיתֶם בִּנְהַרְדְּעָא!״, חָזֵינַן, אִי מִצַּפְרָא לְפַנְיָא מָצֵי אָזֵיל מִסּוּרָא לִנְהַרְדְּעָא – לָא הָווּ זוֹמְמִין, וְאִי לָאו – הָווּ זוֹמְמִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says:** If **two** witnesses **came and said: So-and-so killed a person in Sura in the morning on Sunday, and two** other witnesses **came** to court **and said** to the first set: **In the evening on Sunday you were with us in Neharde'a, we see: If** one **is able to travel from Sura to Neharde'a from morning until evening they are not conspiring** witnesses, as conceivably they could have witnessed the murder in Sura and traveled to Neharde'a by evening. **And if** it is **not** possible to travel that distance in that period of time, **they are conspiring** witnesses.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא לֵיחוּשׁ לְגַמְלָא פָּרָחָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious?** The Gemara answers: **Lest you say: Let us be concerned about** the possibility that these witnesses traveled on **a flying camel,** i.e., one that runs so quickly that it enabled them to traverse the distance faster than the typical person. Therefore, Rava **teaches us** that one need not take that possibility into account.

*Rava* presents a second case of the same structure, now involving geography rather than architecture. Two witnesses testify that on Sunday morning in Sura, so-and-so killed someone. A second pair of witnesses comes and says, "On Sunday you were with us in Nehardea." Again, *chazinan* — we investigate. Is it possible to travel from Sura to Nehardea in a single day, from morning to evening? If yes, *lo havu zomemim* — they are not *zomemim*, since they might have witnessed the murder in the morning and then traveled to Nehardea. If no, *havu zomemim* — they are *zomemim*.

Again the *Gemara* asks *peshita* — why is this not obvious?

### Flying Camel Principle

The answer is that one might have thought we should be concerned about *gamla parcha* — a flying camel, some extraordinary mode of transportation that would allow travel beyond normal human capacity. *Ka mashma lan* — the ruling teaches us that we do not factor in such possibilities. We assess travel based on normal means.

This "flying camel principle" appears elsewhere in the *Bavli* — toward the end of *Gittin* and *Sotah*, for instance — and reflects an awareness, at least theoretical, of faster-than-ordinary transport. The *Bavli* entertains such possibilities in ways the *Yerushalmi* does not. The halakhic point, however, is clear: miraculous or superhuman possibilities are not factored into *hazamah* determinations. We go by what is normally possible.

This principle has interesting modern implications. Since *Hazal* measured time by distance — the standard of seventy-two minutes corresponding to the time to walk four *mil* — and since modern transportation renders distances that once took a full day traversable in a short time, questions arise in various halachic contexts about whether such measurements are governed by distance or by the underlying time unit. The flying camel principle suggests that *Hazal* measured based on normal conditions of their era, which invites reflection on how those standards apply today.

## Multiple Hazamot

### Defendant Guilty, Different Date

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: בָּאוּ שְׁנַיִם וְאָמְרוּ ״בְּחַד בְּשַׁבְּתָא הָרַג פְּלוֹנִי אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ״, וּבָאוּ שְׁנַיִם וְאָמְרוּ ״עִמָּנוּ הֱיִיתֶם בְּחַד בְּשַׁבְּתָא, אֶלָּא בִּתְרֵי בְּשַׁבְּתָא הָרַג פְּלוֹנִי אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ״. וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ אָמְרוּ ״עֶרֶב שַׁבָּת הָרַג פְּלוֹנִי אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ״ – נֶהֱרָגִין, דִּבְעִידָּנָא דְּקָא מַסְהֲדִי, גַּבְרָא לָאו בַּר קְטָלָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says:** If **two** witnesses **came and said: On Sunday so-and-so killed a person, and two** other witnesses **came and said: You were with us on Sunday, but on Monday** that same **so-and-so killed** that same **person. Moreover, even** if the second set of witnesses **said:** On **Shabbat eve so-and-so killed** that same **person,** the first set of witnesses **is executed,** despite the fact that the person against whom they testified was liable to be executed without their testimony. The reason they are liable is **that at the time that they testified,** conspiring to have him executed by the court, **the man** was **not** yet **liable** for **execution** by the court.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? תְּנֵינָא: לְפִיכָךְ, נִמְצֵאת אַחַת מֵהֶן זוֹמֶמֶת – הוּא וָהֵן נֶהֱרָגִין, וְהַשְּׁנִיָּה פְּטוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** Rava **teaching us? We learn** in a mishna (6b) with regard to two sets of witnesses who testified that one person killed another: **Therefore,** if **one of** the sets of witnesses **was found** to be a set of **conspiring** witnesses, **he,** the accused, **and they,** the conspiring witnesses, **are executed** by the court, **and the second** set of witnesses **is exempt.** The accused is executed because the testimony of the witnesses who were not rendered conspiring witnesses remains unchallenged. The first set of witnesses is executed because they were rendered conspiring witnesses. Clearly, conspiring witnesses may be executed even though they testified against a guilty person who was sentenced to death, provided that their testimony was delivered before he was sentenced.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֵיפָא, ״מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בִּגְמַר דִּין״, אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ: בָּאוּ שְׁנַיִם וְאָמְרוּ ״בְּחַד בְּשַׁבְּתָא נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי״, וּבָאוּ שְׁנַיִם וְאָמְרוּ ״בְּחַד בְּשַׁבְּתָא עִמָּנוּ הֱיִיתֶם, אֶלָּא בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי״. וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ אָמְרוּ ״בִּתְרֵי בְּשַׁבְּתָא נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי״ – אֵין אֵלּוּ נֶהֱרָגִין. דִּבְעִידָּנָא דְּקָא מַסְהֲדִי, גַּבְרָא בַּר קְטָלָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It was **the latter clause** of this *halakha*, teaching **that it is not so with regard to** the **verdict,** that contained a novel element, and therefore it **was necessary for** Rava to teach it, and in the context of teaching the latter *halakha* he taught the first case as well. The latter clause is: If **two** witnesses **came** on Tuesday **and said: On Sunday so-and-so was sentenced** to death in a certain court, **and two** other witnesses **came and said: On Sunday you were with us** elsewhere and you could not have witnessed the verdict in that court, **but on Shabbat eve** the same **so-and-so was sentenced** to death, the first set of witnesses are not executed. **Moreover, even** if the second set of witnesses **said: On Monday** the same **so-and-so was sentenced** to death, the first set of witnesses **are not executed, as at the time that they testified** against him, on Tuesday, **the man was** already **liable to be executed,** and it is as though they conspired to kill a dead man.

The case of *gmar din* already having been issued is the crucial distinction. Two witnesses testify that on Sunday, the final verdict in so-and-so's murder case was issued by the court. A second pair of witnesses comes and says that on Sunday the first pair was with them elsewhere. The second pair adds: actually, the verdict was finalized on Friday. Or even, they say, it was finalized on Monday. The ruling: *ein elu neheragin* — the first pair, even though proven to be *zomemim*, is not executed.

Why not? Because *be-idna de-ka-mashadi gavra bar katla hu* — at the time being testified about, the defendant was already a convicted man. The *gmar din* had already been issued. The first pair of witnesses was not testifying about a crime; they were testifying about a court ruling. Their false testimony about *when* the verdict was issued does not alter the defendant's legal status — he was already subject to execution. The witnesses' testimony is therefore not what causes his death. It is the prior ruling of the court that does so. Since the mechanism of *edim zomemim* operates through reciprocity — the punishment they sought to bring upon the defendant is visited upon them — and since their testimony was not the operative cause of the death sentence, they cannot receive the death penalty as *edim zomemim*.

This principle reflects a profound legal insight: at *gmar din*, the locus of responsibility for the defendant's fate shifts from the witnesses to the court. Before *gmar din*, the testimony of the witnesses is what holds his fate in the balance. After *gmar din*, the court's ruling has taken over. Any subsequent testimony is merely about the facts of the court's decision, not about the crime itself. Thus even if such testimony is proven false through *hazamah*, the witnesses cannot receive the reciprocal punishment.

This principle connects to a broader theme regarding *gmar din* as a legal status point — the moment at which a person becomes, in a meaningful legal sense, a *bar katla*, a dead man walking. *Mishnayot* in *Arachin* and elsewhere discuss the implications of this status for various halachic questions, such as whether someone who takes a personal *erech* vow after *gmar din* has done so as a living person or as someone whose life is already forfeit in a legally operative sense. The *gmar din* is not merely procedural; it is a genuine legal transformation.

One might wonder: in practice, could such a scenario arise in *Babylonia*, where *dinei nefashot* were not conducted? *Rava* frames his cases using Sura and Nehardea — cities in *Bavel*. Presumably such examples would have applied before the *churban*, when capital jurisdiction existed, though the question is worth noting.

### Monetary Penalties & Gmar Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן לְעִנְיַן תַּשְׁלוּמֵי קְנָס: בָּאוּ שְׁנַיִם וְאָמְרוּ ״בְּחַד בְּשַׁבְּתָא גָּנַב וְטָבַח וּמָכַר״, וּבָאוּ שְׁנַיִם וְאָמְרוּ ״בְּחַד בְּשַׁבְּתָא עִמָּנוּ הֱיִיתֶם, אֶלָּא בִּתְרֵי בְּשַׁבְּתָא גָּנַב וְטָבַח וּמָכַר״ – מְשַׁלְּמִין. וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ אָמְרוּ ״בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת גָּנַב וְטָבַח וּמָכַר״ – מְשַׁלְּמִין, דִּבְעִידָּנָא דְּקָא מַסְהֲדִי גַּבְרָא לָאו בַּר תַּשְׁלוּמִין הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And likewise,** the same *halakha* that applies with regard to witnesses who testify about one who is sentenced to death applies **to the matter of payments of a fine. If two** witnesses **came and said: On Sunday** this person **stole and** then **slaughtered or sold** an ox or a lamb, thereby rendering him liable to pay a fine of four or five times the value of the stolen animal (see Exodus 21:37), **and** then **two** other witnesses **came and said** to the first set of witnesses: **On Sunday you were with us** elsewhere, **but on Monday** this same person **stole and** then **slaughtered or sold** an ox or a lamb, the first set of witnesses **pay** the accused four or five times the value of the stolen animal. This is because at the time that according to their testimony he stole the animals, he had not yet stolen. **Moreover, even** if the second set of witnesses **said:** It was earlier, **on Shabbat eve,** that he **stole and** then **slaughtered or sold** the animals, the first set of witnesses **pay** the accused the amount of the fine he would have had to pay, **as at the time that they testified the** accused **man** had **not** yet been found **liable for payment** by the court, and he could have admitted his guilt and exempted himself from paying the fine. They conspired to render him liable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּאוּ שְׁנַיִם וְאָמְרוּ ״בְּחַד בְּשַׁבְּתָא גָּנַב וְטָבַח וּמָכַר וְנִגְמַר דִּינוֹ״, וּבָאוּ שְׁנַיִם וְאָמְרוּ ״בְּחַד בְּשַׁבְּתָא עִמָּנוּ הֱיִיתֶם, אֶלָּא עֶרֶב שַׁבָּת גָּנַב וְטָבַח וּמָכַר וְנִגְמַר דִּינוֹ״, וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ אָמְרוּ ״בְּחַד בְּשַׁבְּתָא גָּנַב וְטָבַח וּמָכַר, וּבִתְרֵי בְּשַׁבְּתָא נִגְמַר דִּינוֹ״ – אֵין מְשַׁלְּמִין, דִּבְעִידָּנָא דְּקָא מַסְהֲדִי גַּבְרָא בַּר תַּשְׁלוּמִין הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** But if **two** witnesses **came** on Tuesday **and said: On Sunday** this person **stole and** then **slaughtered or sold** an ox or a lamb **and was sentenced** to pay a fine of four or five times the value of the stolen animal, **and two** other witnesses **came and said: On Sunday you were with us, but** that person **stole and** then **slaughtered or sold** an ox or a lamb, **and was sentenced on Shabbat eve,** the first set of witnesses are exempt from payment. **Moreover, even** if the second set of witnesses **said: On Sunday he stole and** then **slaughtered or sold** an ox or a lamb, corroborating the testimony of the first set, **and on Monday he was sentenced** to pay the fine, the first set of witnesses **do not pay** the accused. This is **because at the time that they testified** against him, on Tuesday, **the man** had already been found **liable for payment** by the court, and therefore they are not conspiring witnesses.

The same principle extends to monetary *knas* cases. *Ve-chen le-inyan tashlumei knas* — the same logic applies to fines imposed by a court. Two witnesses testify that on Sunday so-and-so stole a cow and slaughtered it or sold it — an act that incurs the fourfold or fivefold payment of *daled* or *hei*. A second pair of witnesses says, "On Sunday you were with us." The second pair also testifies that in fact the theft and slaughter occurred on Monday, or even on Friday. The ruling: *meshalmin* — the first pair pays. Because *be-idna de-ka-mashadi gavra, gavra lav bar tashlumin hu* — at the time they testified, the defendant had not yet been declared liable to pay the fine. Their testimony was the operative cause of his financial liability, so the reciprocal punishment applies to them.

But if the witnesses testify that on Sunday the court finalized its ruling that the defendant owed the *knas*, and a second pair says, "On Sunday you were with us, and in fact the court finalized the ruling on Friday" — or even on Monday — then *ein meshalmin*, the first pair does not pay. Because *be-idna de-ka-mashadi, gavra bar tashlumin hu* — at the time of the *gmar din* being testified about, the defendant's financial liability had already been fixed by the court. Their testimony is about the court's ruling, not the underlying act, and the ruling is what created the obligation. Once the *gmar din* has occurred, the witnesses' role is no longer operative, and *hazamah* of their testimony about the timing of the verdict does not trigger the reciprocal penalty.

## Rabbi Yehuda's Position

### Rabbi Yehuda: Only First

The *mishnah* teaches that **Rabbi Yehuda says** that if a single set of witnesses rendered numerous sets of witnesses, all of whom testified that one person killed another, conspiring witnesses, **this** situation **is a conspiracy,** and only the first set of conspiring witnesses is executed.

### Rava's Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אִיסְטָטִית הִיא זוֹ, אֲפִילּוּ כַּת רִאשׁוֹנָה נָמֵי לָא! אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: שֶׁקָדְמוּ וְהָרְגוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** Rabbi Yehuda states that **this** situation **is a conspiracy,** and there is suspicion that the second set is not truthful, let **even** the **first set** of witnesses who were rendered conspiring witnesses based on their testimony **also not** be executed. **Rabbi Abbahu said:** The mishna is referring to a case **where** the judges **already executed** the first set of witnesses. Rabbi Yehuda is saying that no witnesses are executed other than the first set of witnesses, who were already executed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי דַהֲוָה הֲוָה! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִם אֵינָהּ אֶלָּא כַּת אַחַת – נֶהֱרֶגֶת, אִי אִיכָּא טְפֵי – אֵין נֶהֱרָגִין. הָא ״בִּלְבַד״ קָאָמַר! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: If so, **what was,** already **was;** there is no point in stating it as a *halakha*. **Rather, Rava said this** is what Rabbi Yehuda **is saying: If it is only one set** of witnesses that is rendered conspiring witnesses by the second set, the witnesses **are executed; if there is more** than one set **they are not executed** at all. The Gemara asks: **But doesn't** Rabbi Yehuda **say:** It is only the first set **alone** that is executed? This indicates that contrary to Rava's explanation, it is a case involving more than one set of witnesses. The Gemara notes: Indeed, this matter is **difficult.**

*Rava* clarifies *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position: *im eino ella kat achat, neherget* — if there is only a single pair of *hazamah* witnesses against the first set, those first witnesses are executed. *Ei ika tefei, ein nehergin* — but if there are multiple groups in the chain, none of them are executed, according to *Rabbi Yehuda*. The suspicion of *istatit* — of coordinated conspiracy — falls over the entire structure, and once that suspicion takes hold, no single group can be singled out for execution.

### Bilvad & Kasya

Yet *Rabbi Yehuda*'s formulation uses the word *bilvad* — "only" — as in "only the first group." This word implies that there is indeed a case with *more* than one group, from which we exclude all but the first. But *Rava*'s explanation posits that in the scenario *Rabbi Yehuda* addresses, when there are multiple groups, *none* of them are executed. So why does *Rabbi Yehuda* say "only the first"? This creates a *kashya* — a difficulty — that the *Gemara* leaves unresolved.

One could suggest that *bilvad* simply means: in a case where there are two sets of witnesses total — one original set and one set of *hazamah* witnesses — we kill the first set *bilvad*, since there is no further group to cast suspicion on the entire chain. But the *bilvad* still sits awkwardly given *Rava*'s formulation. A note suggests that *Rabbi Yehuda*'s statement in the *mishnah* may need to be read as referring specifically to a scenario where the *hazamah* applies only to the first group alone — but this reading, too, requires further research. The *Gemara* leaves it as a *kashya*.

## Woman & Chazakah

### Woman Case Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא אִיתְּתָא דַּאֲתַאי סָהֲדִי וְאִישְׁתַּקּוּר, אַיְיתִי סָהֲדִי וְאִישְׁתַּקּוּר. אֲזַלָה אַיְיתִי סָהֲדִי אַחֲרִינֵי דְּלָא אִישְׁתַּקּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the dispute in the mishna, the Gemara relates: There was **a certain woman who brought witnesses** to testify on her behalf, **and they were** proven to be **liars. She brought** other **witnesses, and they** too **were** proven to be **liars. She went** and **brought** yet **other witnesses, who were not** proven to be **liars.**

### Reish Lakish & Chazakah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הוּחְזְקָה זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** There is an amoraic dispute whether the testimony of the third set of witnesses is accepted. **Reish Lakish said: This** woman **has assumed the presumptive status** of dishonesty because of her repeated reliance on false witnesses; therefore, the testimony of the third set is rejected.

### Rabbi Elazar's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אִם הִיא הוּחְזְקָה, כׇּל יִשְׂרָאֵל מִי הוּחְזְקוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said to him: If she has assumed the presumptive status** of dishonesty, **has the entire Jewish people assumed** that **presumptive status?** Why assume that these witnesses are dishonest?

### Rabbi Yochanan Intervenes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זִימְנִין הָווּ יָתְבִי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אֲתָא כִּי הַאי מַעֲשֶׂה לְקַמַּיְיהוּ. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הוּחְזְקָה זוֹ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אִם הוּחְזְקָה זוֹ – כׇּל יִשְׂרָאֵל מִי הוּחְזְקוּ? הֲדַר חַזְיֵהּ לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בִּישׁוּת, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שָׁמְעַתְּ מִילֵּי מִבַּר נַפָּחָא וְלָא אֲמַרְתְּ לִי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: On another **occasion,** Reish Lakish and Rabbi Elazar **were sitting before Rabbi Yoḥanan** and **an incident similar to this** one **came before them** for judgment. **Reish Lakish said: This** woman **has assumed the presumptive status** of dishonesty. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to him: If she has assumed the presumptive status** of dishonesty, **has the entire Jewish people assumed** that **presumptive status?** When Reish Lakish heard Rabbi Yoḥanan respond in a manner identical to the earlier response of Rabbi Elazar, **he turned** his head and **glared angrily at Rabbi Elazar,** and **he said to him: You heard** this **matter from bar Nappaḥa,** i.e., Rabbi Yoḥanan, **and you did not say** it **to me in his name?** Had I known that you were stating Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion I would have accepted it.

### Known vs Future

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דְּאָמַר כְּרַבָּנַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that **Reish Lakish stated** his opinion that this woman has assumed the presumptive status of dishonesty **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who invalidates the testimony of witnesses based on suspicion that arises due to the circumstances even though there is no proof that they lied. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan stated** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who do not invalidate testimony based on unsubstantiated suspicion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי לָךְ אֲפִילּוּ לְרַבָּנַן – עַד כָּאן לָא קָא אָמְרִי רַבָּנַן הָתָם, דְּלֵיכָּא דְּקָא מְהַדַּר. אֲבָל הָכָא, אִיכָּא הָא דְּקָא מְהַדְּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **Reish Lakish** could **say to you: I state** my opinion **even in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** as **the Rabbis say** that one relies on witnesses who render multiple sets of witnesses conspiring witnesses **only there,** in the mishna, in a case **where there is no one who is seeking** to hire witnesses to testify on his behalf, and one could assert that their testimony is true. **But here, there is this** woman **who is seeking** to hire witnesses to testify on her behalf, which arouses suspicion that she hired them to lie on her behalf.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר לָךְ: אֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי אֲפִילּוּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הָתָם, דְּאָמְרִינַן: ״אַטּוּ כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא גַּבֵּי הָנֵי הָווּ קָיְימִי?״, אֲבָל הָכָא, הָנֵי יָדְעִי בְּסָהֲדוּתָא, וְהָנֵי לָא יָדְעִי בְּסָהֲדוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan** could **say to you: I state** my opinion **even in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** as **Rabbi Yehuda says** that the testimony of the second set is invalid based on unsubstantiated suspicion **only there,** in the mishna, where circumstances exacerbate the suspicion that they are lying, **as we say: Is that to say** that **everyone,** the numerous sets of witnesses, **was standing near these** witnesses who testify in order to render them conspiring witnesses? **But here,** perhaps **these** witnesses who came last and were not proven to be liars **know** the content **of the testimony, and these** witnesses who were proven to be liars **do not know** the content **of the testimony.** The fact that the testimony of the first sets of witnesses was rendered void has no bearing on the status of other witnesses.

The *Gemara* now introduces a different case involving multiple sets of contradicting witnesses. A woman came before the court; witnesses were brought on her behalf, and they *ishtakur* — they were shown to contradict each other during the court's cross-examination, *drishat beit din*. Then she brought additional witnesses, and they too contradicted each other. Then she brought yet another group who did not contradict each other.

*Reish Lakish* rules: *huchzekah zo* — this woman has now established a *chazakah*, a presumption, that she corrupts or tampers with witnesses. Two consecutive instances of her witnesses contradicting each other under examination is sufficient to establish this presumption. According to *Reish Lakish*, the *chazakah* is established by two instances.

*Rabbi Elazar* objects: *im hi huchzekah, kol Yisrael mi michuzku?* — if she has established a *chazakah*, does that mean all of Israel is presumed to be lying? The suspicion attaches to one person, yet we would be casting doubt on the testimony of entirely different witnesses who testified on her behalf. We should not, argues *Rabbi Elazar*, allow suspicion of a single individual to undermine the presumed reliability of unrelated witnesses.

The *Gemara* relates that on another occasion, when *Reish Lakish* made this ruling in the presence of *Rabbi Yochanan*, *Rabbi Yochanan* responded with the same objection: *im huchzekah zo, kol Yisrael huchzaku* — if she has established a *chazakah*, does that mean all of Israel is presumed to be suspect? It becomes clear that *Rabbi Elazar* had in fact heard this position from *Rabbi Yochanan* but had not attributed it to him.

When *Reish Lakish* realized this — that *Rabbi Elazar* had stated *Rabbi Yochanan*'s view without attribution — he turned to *Rabbi Elazar* with displeasure, *be-fanim ze'ufot*. *Reish Lakish* had learned so much of his *Torah* from *Rabbi Yochanan* that had he known this ruling was from *Rabbi Yochanan*, he would have retracted his own position rather than dispute it. He was upset that *Rabbi Elazar* had cited the position without naming its source. *Rabbi Elazar*, in turn, was embarrassed by *Reish Lakish*'s reaction: *shamat mili mi-bar Nafha ve-lo amarta li mi-shmeh* — you heard this from *Rabbi Yochanan* and you didn't tell me it was in his name?

The *Gemara* then asks whether this dispute between *Reish Lakish* and *Rabbi Yochanan* simply maps onto the dispute between *Rabbi Yehuda* and the *rabanan* in the *mishnah*. The *Gemara* rejects this: each *amora* can defend their position even within the opposing *tanna*'s framework.

*Reish Lakish* can say: *ana de-amri lakh afilu rabanan* — my position is consistent even with the *rabanan*. The *rabanan*'s rule in the *mishnah* — that all multiple groups of *zomemim* are executed — applies *hacha*, in the *mishnah*'s case, because once the chain of events has concluded, *de-ika hadra*, there is no way to revisit the case. But in our case of the woman, *ika de-ka mahadar* — the process is still open, the *drishat beit din* is still available, we can still investigate these witnesses. In such an open-ended situation, *Reish Lakish* would say the *chazakah* should not cast suspicion.

*Rabbi Yochanan* can similarly say: *ana de-amri afilu Rabbi Yehuda* — my position is consistent even with *Rabbi Yehuda*. *Rabbi Yehuda*'s rule in the *mishnah* — that only the first group is executed — applies when all the witnesses are already before us. But in our case of the woman, *hanei yad'ei be-sahaduta ve-hanei lo yad'ei be-sahaduta* — the earlier groups knew about each other's testimony and contradicted each other during examination, whereas future witnesses would not have the same prior knowledge. That distinction changes the analysis; the *chazakah* is operating on future unknown witnesses, not on witnesses who are already known to be in conflict with each other.

## Timing & Tzedukim

### Timing of Execution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין הָעֵדִים זוֹמְמִין נֶהֱרָגִין עַד שֶׁיִּגָּמֵר הַדִּין. שֶׁהֲרֵי הַצַּדּוּקִין אוֹמְרִים: עַד שֶׁיֵּהָרֵג, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״נֶפֶשׁ תַּחַת נָפֶשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The conspiring witnesses are executed only** if they are rendered conspiring witnesses after **the verdict** of the accused **is concluded.** This is in contrast to the opinion of the Sadducees, **as the Sadducees say:** Conspiring witnesses are executed **only** if they are rendered conspiring witnesses after the accused **is killed** on the basis of their testimony, **as it is stated: "A life for a life"** (Exodus 21:23; see Deuteronomy 19:21).

The next *mishnah* states: *ein edim zomemim neheragim ad she-yigamer ha-din* — *edim zomemim* are not executed until the *gmar din* has been issued. This establishes a clear temporal boundary: between the acceptance of testimony (*kabbalat edut*) and the *gmar din*, *hazamah* can be applied and the witnesses executed. But before *gmar din*, and after execution of the defendant, the reciprocal punishment does not apply.

### Tzedukim's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לָהֶם חֲכָמִים: וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״וַעֲשִׂיתֶם לוֹ כַּאֲשֶׁר זָמַם לַעֲשׂוֹת לְאָחִיו״, וַהֲרֵי אָחִיו קַיָּים! וְאִם כֵּן לָמָּה נֶאֱמַר ״נֶפֶשׁ תַּחַת נָפֶשׁ״? יָכוֹל מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁקִּבְּלוּ עֵדוּתָן יֵהָרְגוּ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״נֶפֶשׁ תַּחַת נָפֶשׁ״, הָא אֵינָם נֶהֱרָגִין עַד שֶׁיִּגָּמֵר הַדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **The Rabbis said to** the Sadducees: **But wasn't it already stated: "And you shall do to him as he conspired to do to his brother"** (Deuteronomy 19:19), **and** this latter verse indicates that **his** accused **brother is alive? And if so, why is it stated: "A life for a life"?** One **might** have thought that if they are rendered conspiring witnesses **from the moment** the judges **accepted their testimony** in court, **they will be executed,** even though no verdict was concluded. Therefore, **the verse states: "A life for a life,"** teaching that **they are executed only** if they are rendered conspiring witnesses after **the verdict** of the accused **will be concluded,** from the moment that the court is on the verge of taking his life.

*Sheharei ha-Tzedukim omrim ad she-yehareg* — the *Tzedukim* hold that the reciprocal capital punishment for *edim zomemim* applies only after the defendant has actually been killed. They derive this from *nefesh tachat nefesh* — a life for a life — arguing that the reciprocity requires an actual death to have occurred. Only once the false witnesses have caused a real execution should they themselves be executed.

### Nefesh Tachat Nefesh

The *chachamim* respond by citing a different verse: *ve-asitem lo ka-asher zamam la-asot le-ahiv* — you shall do to him as he plotted to do to his brother. The critical point is *ve-harei ahiv kayyam* — his brother, the defendant, is still alive. The *Torah*'s formulation *ka-asher zamam*, "as he plotted," indicates that the *zomemim*'s punishment is triggered by what they *intended* to do, not by the completed act. Therefore, as long as the defendant has not yet been executed, the *hazamah* can still be applied.

If so, one might think that the *zomemim* should be executed as soon as the testimony is accepted — *mi-she-kiblu edutan yeihareigu*. This is where *nefesh tachat nefesh* comes in: the verse teaches that there must be a *nefesh* at stake — that is, the defendant's life must already be forfeit in a legally operative sense — before the reciprocal punishment takes effect. And a *nefesh* becomes forfeit at the *gmar din*, not at the moment of the *kabbalat edut*. Thus *nefesh tachat nefesh* functions not to push the threshold later, as the *Tzedukim* argue, but to confirm that the threshold is *gmar din*: only after the verdict is final has the defendant's life been legally placed at risk by the witnesses' testimony.

The three time points are thus: *kabbalat edut* — too early; *gmar din* — the threshold; actual *hariga* — too late. This interacts deeply with the broader halachic significance of *gmar din* as a legal transformation in the defendant's status, touching questions discussed in *Arachin* and elsewhere about what it means to be a *bar katla* from the moment the verdict is rendered.

### Kal Vachomer Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנָא, בְּרִיבִּי אוֹמֵר: לֹא הָרְגוּ – נֶהֱרָגִין, הָרְגוּ – אֵין נֶהֱרָגִין. אָמַר אָבִיו: בְּנִי, לָאו קַל וָחוֹמֶר הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: It is **taught** with regard to the *halakha* in the mishna that a Sage referred to as **the Distinguished** [***Beribbi***] **says:** If the conspiring witnesses have **not** yet **killed** the accused with their testimony **they are executed,** but if **they killed** the accused with their testimony **they are not executed. The father of** that Sage, who was also a prominent Sage, **said** to him: **My son, is** this matter **not** derived through **an *a fortiori*** inference? If, when they were unsuccessful in their attempt to kill the accused they are executed, all the more so if they were successful in killing him should they be executed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: לִימַּדְתָּנוּ רַבֵּינוּ שֶׁאֵין עוֹנְשִׁין מִן הַדִּין. דְּתַנְיָא: ״אִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יִקַּח [אֶת] אֲחֹתוֹ בַּת אָבִיו אוֹ בַת אִמּוֹ״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בַּת אָבִיו שֶׁלֹּא בַּת אִמּוֹ וּבַת אִמּוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בַּת אָבִיו. בַּת אִמּוֹ וּבַת אָבִיו מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עֶרְוַת אֲחֹתוֹ גִּלָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to** his father: **You have taught us, our teacher, that one does not administer punishment** based **on** an *a fortiori* **inference.** The punishment must be stated in the Torah. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that among the relatives with whom engaging in intercourse is forbidden it states: **"A man who takes his sister, the daughter of his father or the daughter of his mother"** (Leviticus 20:17): **I have** derived **only the daughter of his father who is not the daughter of his mother, or the daughter of his mother who is not the daughter of his father. From where** is it derived that one is liable for engaging in intercourse with his sister who is both **the daughter of his mother and the daughter of his father?** It is derived from a verse, as **the verse states: "He has uncovered the nakedness of his sister"** (Leviticus 20:17), indicating that one is liable for engaging in intercourse with any sister.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁלֹּא יֵאָמֵר יֵשׁ לִי בַּדִּין: אִם עָנַשׁ עַל בַּת אָבִיו שֶׁלֹּא בַּת אִמּוֹ, וּבַת אִמּוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בַּת אָבִיו – בַּת אָבִיו וּבַת אִמּוֹ לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? הָא לָמַדְתָּ שֶׁאֵין עוֹנְשִׁין מִן הַדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: Even **if** the verse **had not stated** that one is liable for engaging in intercourse with his sister, i.e., one's sister with whom he has both parents in common, **I have** proof **from** an *a fortiori* **inference: If** the Torah **punished** an individual for engaging in intercourse **with the daughter of his father who is not the daughter of his mother, or** for engaging in intercourse with **the daughter of his mother who is not the daughter of his father,** is it **not all the more so** clear that he should be punished for engaging in intercourse with his sister who is both **the daughter of his father and the daughter of his mother?** From the fact that the Torah explicitly prohibited intercourse in that case and did not rely on the inference, **you learn that one does not administer punishment** based **on** an *a fortiori* **inference.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹנֶשׁ שָׁמַעְנוּ, אַזְהָרָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עֶרְוַת אֲחוֹתְךָ בַת אָבִיךָ אוֹ בַת אִמֶּךָ״. אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בַּת אָבִיו שֶׁלֹּא בַּת אִמּוֹ, וּבַת אִמּוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בַּת אָבִיו. בַּת אָבִיו וּבַת אִמּוֹ מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עֶרְוַת בַּת אֵשֶׁת אָבִיךָ מוֹלֶדֶת אָבִיךָ אֲחוֹתְךָ הִוא״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **We heard** from that verse (Leviticus 20:17) the **punishment** for engaging in intercourse with one's sister with whom he has both parents in common. **From where** is the **prohibition** against engaging in those acts of intercourse derived? It is derived from a verse, as **the verse states: "The nakedness of your sister, the daughter of your father or the daughter of your mother…**you shall not uncover" (Leviticus 18:9). **I have** derived the prohibition against engaging in intercourse **only** with **the daughter of his father who is not the daughter of his mother, or** with **the daughter of his mother who is not the daughter of his father. From where** do I derive a prohibition against engaging in intercourse with his sister who is both **the daughter of his father and the daughter of his mother?** It is derived from a verse, as **the verse states: "The nakedness of the daughter of your father's wife, born of your father; she is your sister"** (Leviticus 18:11), indicating that engaging in intercourse with any sister is prohibited.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁלֹּא יֵאָמֵר יֵשׁ לִי מִן הַדִּין: מָה אִם הוּזְהַר עַל בַּת אִמּוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בַּת אָבִיו, וּבַת אָבִיו שֶׁלֹּא בַּת אִמּוֹ, בַּת אָבִיו וּבַת אִמּוֹ לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? הָא לָמַדְתָּ שֶׁאֵין מַזְהִירִין מִן הַדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: Even **if** the verse **had not stated** that engaging in intercourse with one's sister with whom he has both parents in common is forbidden, **I have** proof for this **from** an *a fortiori* **inference: If one was prohibited** from engaging in intercourse **with the daughter of his mother who is not the daughter of his father, and** with **the daughter of his father who is not the daughter of his mother,** is it **not all the more so** clear that he is prohibited from engaging in intercourse with his sister who is both **the daughter of his father and the daughter of his mother? You learn** from this **that one does not** derive **a prohibition** based **on** an *a fortiori* **inference.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַיָּיבֵי מַלְקִיּוֹת מִנַּיִן – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״רָשָׁע״ ״רָשָׁע״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **From where** is it derived that one does not administer punishment to **those liable** to receive **lashes** based on an *a fortiori* inference, and that the principle is not limited to capital punishment? **The verse states** a verbal analogy between the term "wicked" written with regard to those liable to be executed and the term "wicked" written with regard to those liable to receive lashes. With regard to those liable to be executed, it is written: "Who is **wicked** and deserves to die" (Numbers 35:31). With regard to those liable to receive lashes, it is written: "And it shall be if the **wicked** is deserving of lashes" (Deuteronomy 25:2).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַיָּיבֵי גָּלִיּוֹת מִנַּיִן – אָתְיָא ״רֹצֵחַ״ ״רֹצֵחַ״.
>
> **Translation:** **From where** is it derived that one does not administer punishment to **those liable to be exiled** based on an *a fortiori* inference? It is **derived** by means of a verbal analogy between the term **"murderer"** written with regard to those who kill intentionally (see Numbers 35:21) and the term **"murderer"** written with regard to those who kill unwittingly (see Numbers 35:11). The conclusion is that one does not administer any punishment based on an *a fortiori* inference.

The *Gemara* records a remarkable exchange. *Tana be-ribbi* — a certain scholar — formulates the principle: *lo haragu, neheragin; haragu, ein neheragin* — if the *zomemim* did not cause the defendant to be killed, they are themselves executed; if he was already killed, they are not. His father — apparently *Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi* himself — responds: *lav kal va-chomer hu?* Is this not derivable by *kal va-chomer*? If we execute them when they failed to kill him, how much more so should we execute them when they actually succeeded in causing his death?

The son replies: *limadetanu rabbeinu* — my teacher, did you not teach me that *ein onshin min ha-din* — we cannot impose punishments derived through *kal va-chomer*?

The proof is brought from the laws of incestuous relationships. The *Torah* mentions separately a paternal half-sister and a maternal half-sister, specifying punishment for relations with each. One might think to derive by *kal va-chomer* that a full sister — who is both a paternal and maternal sibling — should certainly be prohibited, since even each half-relationship alone is punishable. But the *Torah* writes explicitly *ervas achoso gila* — he uncovered his sister's nakedness — rather than allowing us to derive it. The very need for the explicit verse proves that we cannot derive the punishment from a *kal va-chomer*. Moreover, even the *azhara* — the prohibition, not merely the punishment — cannot be derived for capital offenses through *kal va-chomer*; the *Torah* provides explicit verses for both the *onesh* and the *azhara* of the full sister.

For offenses incurring *malkot*, the connection is learned through a *gezeirah shavah* from the word *rasha*. For cases of manslaughter requiring *galut*, the connection is learned through a *gezeirah shavah* from the word *rotze'ah*. In neither case is *kal va-chomer* the operative mode of deriving a punishment.

## Tavai & Shetach

### Tavai Executes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן טָבַאי: אֶרְאֶה בְּנֶחָמָה אִם לֹא הָרַגְתִּי עֵד זוֹמֵם. לְהוֹצִיא מִלִּבָּן שֶׁל צַדּוּקִים, שֶׁהָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: אֵין הָעֵדִים זוֹמְמִין נֶהֱרָגִין עַד שֶׁיֵּהָרֵג הַנִּדּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the dispute between the Sadducees and the Sages, **it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda ben Tabbai says** in the form of an oath: **I will** not **see the** future **consolation** of the Jewish people **if I did not** as a member of the court **kill** a single **conspiring witness,** in order **to eradicate** this reasoning **from the hearts of the Sadducees, who would say: The conspiring witnesses are executed only** if they are rendered conspiring witnesses after the accused **will be killed.** Rabbi Yehuda ben Tabbai killed the conspiring witness while the accused remained alive.

### Shetach Rebukes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן שָׁטַח: אֶרְאֶה בְּנֶחָמָה אִם לֹא שָׁפַכְתָּ דָּם נָקִי, שֶׁהֲרֵי אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: אֵין הָעֵדִים זוֹמְמִין נֶהֱרָגִין עַד שֶׁיִּזּוֹמּוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם, וְאֵין לוֹקִין עַד שֶׁיִּזּוֹמּוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Shimon ben Shataḥ said to him: I will** not **see the consolation** of the Jewish people **if you did not shed** thereby **innocent blood, as the Sages said: Conspiring witnesses are not executed unless both of them are rendered conspiring** witnesses, **and they are not flogged unless both of them are rendered conspiring** witnesses. In this case, only one was rendered a conspiring witness.

### Tavai's Repentance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיָּד קִבֵּל עָלָיו רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן טָבַאי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מוֹרֶה הוֹרָאָה אֶלָּא לִפְנֵי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן שָׁטַח. וְכׇל יָמָיו שֶׁל רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן טָבַאי הָיָה מִשְׁתַּטֵּחַ עַל קִבְרוֹ שֶׁל אוֹתוֹ הָעֵד, וְהָיָה קוֹלוֹ נִשְׁמָע, וְכִסְבוּרִין הָעָם לוֹמַר: קוֹלוֹ שֶׁל הָרוּג. אָמַר: קוֹלִי שֶׁלִּי הוּא, תֵּדְעוּ – לְמָחָר הוּא מֵת, אֵין קוֹלוֹ נִשְׁמָע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda ben Tabbai immediately accepted** a commitment **upon himself that he would issue a halakhic ruling only** when he was **before Shimon ben Shataḥ,** to avoid mistakes in the future. **And** throughout **all of Rabbi Yehuda ben Tabbai's days he would** tearfully **prostrate himself on the grave of that witness** whom he executed, to request forgiveness for having done so, **and his voice was heard** from a distance. **And the people thought to say** that it was **the voice of** the **executed** witness that was heard. Rabbi Yehuda ben Tabbai **said** to them: **It is my voice. Know** that this is so, as **tomorrow,** i.e., sometime in the future, **he,** referring to himself, **will die,** and **his voice will no** longer **be heard.**

### Rav Acha's Comment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: דִּלְמָא בְּדִינָא קָם בַּהֲדֵיהּ, אִי נָמֵי פַּיּוֹסֵי פַּיְּיסֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi:** The fact that the voice will cease after Rabbi Yehuda ben Tabbai's death is inconclusive as proof that the voice is not that of the executed witness. **Perhaps** the reason that the voice of the executed person will no longer be heard is that **he confronted** Rabbi Yehuda ben Tabbai **in trial** before the heavenly court, obviating the need for crying from his grave. **Alternatively,** perhaps Rabbi Yehuda ben Tabbai **appeased** the executed witness in the World-to-Come, and there is silence because no grievances remained.

*Rabbi Yehuda ben Tavai* declared — *ereh be-nechama im lo haragti ed zomem* — "I should merit to see the times of *mashiach* if I did not execute an *ed zomem* in order to contradict the position of the *Tzedukim*." The *Tzedukim*, as we have seen, held that *edim zomemim* should not be executed unless the defendant had already been killed. *Rabbi Yehuda ben Tavai* took it upon himself to execute an *ed zomem* specifically before the defendant was killed — that is, in accordance with rabbinic law and against the *Tzeduki* position — as a public demonstration of the correct ruling.

*Shimon ben Shetach* responded immediately: *ereh be-nechama im lo shafachta dam naki* — "You should merit to see the times of *mashiach* if you have not spilled innocent blood." His rebuke was based on a specific halachic point: *ein edim zomemim neheragin ad she-yizomu sheneihem* — *edim zomemim* cannot be executed until *both* of them have been proven to be *zomemim*. And similarly, *ein lokin ad she-yizomu sheneihem* — you cannot administer *malkot* until both have been proven. *Rabbi Yehuda ben Tavai* had executed only one of the two witnesses, without establishing *hazamah* against both. By the halachic standard, he had therefore killed someone unjustly.

*Rabbi Yehuda ben Tavai* immediately accepted *Shimon ben Shetach*'s authority and undertook not to issue any halachic ruling except in *Shimon ben Shetach*'s presence. Furthermore, for the rest of his life he would go prostrate himself on the grave of that *ed zomem* whom he had wrongfully executed. He would cry there, and his voice could be heard. The people developed an urban legend: they believed the sound they heard was the voice of the unjustly executed man crying out from the grave. *Rabbi Yehuda ben Tavai* clarified: *koli sheli hu* — it is my own voice. And as proof, he added: *ted'u, le-machar hu met, ein kolo nishma* — you will know this is so when I die, because then the voice will no longer be heard.

Many generations later, *Rav Acha berei de-Rava* said to *Rav Ashi*: *dilma be-dina kam bahadei; inami piyusei piyusei* — perhaps that *ed zomem* had a legal claim against *Rabbi Yehuda ben Tavai*; he could still appease him. Even if the executed witness had a legitimate grievance, the act of ongoing repentance and appeasement — the prostrations, the weeping, the lifelong penance — still had value and purpose. The *Gemara* closes the sugya on this note.
