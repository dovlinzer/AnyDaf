# Megillah 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Minor Reading Megillah

### Katan & Megillah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַכְשִׁיר בְּקָטָן. (דְּתַנְיָא) אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: קָטָן הָיִיתִי, וּקְרִיתִיהָ לְמַעְלָה מֵרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן וּזְקֵנִים בְּלוֹד. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵין מְבִיאִין רְאָיָה מִן הַקָּטָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says that a minor is fit** to read the Megilla. **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda said:** I can offer proof to my opinion, as when **I was a minor I** myself **read** the Megilla **before Rabbi Tarfon and the** other **Elders in Lod. They said to him** in response: **One cannot bring a proof from** the testimony of **a minor.**

The daf opens ten lines down, at the passage beginning *Yehuda machshir b'katan* — Reb Yehuda permits a *katan*, a minor, to read the *Megillah*. This is a striking position, since ordinarily minors are not obligated in *mitzvos* and therefore cannot discharge the obligations of others. Yet Reb Yehuda maintains that a *katan* may do so. The *Gemara* has already made a distinction, earlier in the sugya, between a *katan she'higgia lachinuch* — a minor who has reached the age of training — and one who has not, though the *Gemara* does not fully unpack that distinction here.

### Proof from Lod

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָטָן הָיִיתִי, וּקְרִיתִיהָ לְמַעְלָה מֵרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן וּזְקֵנִים בְּלוֹד
>
> **Translation:** When **I was a minor I** myself **read** the Megilla **before Rabbi Tarfon and the** other **Elders in Lod**

The *Gemara* cites a *braisa* in which Reb Yehuda offers autobiographical proof: *katan hayiti v'kirisiyah l'mala me-Reb Tarfon v'hazekeinim b'Lod* — "I was a minor and I read it before Reb Tarfon and the elders in Lod." The word *l'mala* is noteworthy; it may mean he was reading from a raised platform. There are discussions about where the *aron* was positioned relative to the *sheliach tzibbur*, though the architecture of ancient synagogues does not clearly support the notion of a sunken platform in the literal sense.

### Testimony Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מְבִיאִין רְאָיָה מִן הַקָּטָן.
>
> **Translation:** **One cannot bring a proof from** the testimony of **a minor.**

The response to Reb Yehuda's proof is swift: *ein mevi'im re'ayah min ha'katan* — one cannot adduce testimony from a minor. Whatever Reb Yehuda did as a child cannot serve as legal evidence, since the testimony of a minor is inadmissible. The situation has a certain structural irony: by the time anyone could challenge such evidence, the circumstances that would make it relevant have already passed.

### Rebbi Yehuda Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי: קָטָן הָיִיתִי, וּקְרִיתִיהָ לְמַעְלָה מֵרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵין מְבִיאִין רְאָיָה מִן הַמַּתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a different *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said:** When **I was a minor I read** the Megilla **before Rabbi Yehuda. They said to him: One cannot bring a proof** that an act is permitted **from the** behavior of the very **one who permits** it.

A second *braisa* compounds the problem. Rebbi Yehuda HaNasi himself says: *katan hayiti v'kirisiyah l'mala me-Rebbi Yehuda* — "I was a minor and I read it in the presence of Rebbi Yehuda." But the response here is different: *ein mevi'im re'ayah min ha'matir* — one cannot bring proof from the one who permits the act. The fact that Rebbi Yehuda, who himself holds that a *katan* may read, allowed this to happen proves nothing to those who disagree.

### Gemara Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימְרוּ לֵיהּ: אֵין מְבִיאִין רְאָיָה מִן הַקָּטָן! חֲדָא וְעוֹד קָאָמְרוּ לֵיהּ. חֲדָא: דְּקָטָן הָיִיתָ, וְעוֹד: אֲפִילּוּ גָּדוֹל הָיִיתָ — אֵין מְבִיאִין רְאָיָה מִן הַמַּתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let them say to him,** as the Sages said to Rabbi Yehuda in the previous *baraita*, that **one cannot bring a proof from** the testimony of a **minor.** The Gemara answers: **They said one** thing to him **and** then **another;** i.e., they rejected him with a twofold argument: **One** objection is that **you were a minor** at that time, and therefore your testimony is disqualified. **And furthermore, even** if **you had been an adult** at that time and you had testified that you saw some other minor read the Megilla before Rabbi Yehuda, **one cannot bring a proof** that an act is permitted **from the** behavior of the very **one who permits** it.

The *Gemara* then asks: why does the response to the first proof not suffice? Why add the second? The answer is *chada v'od kamru lo* — they were making two independent points. First, you were a *katan* at the time, so your testimony is inadmissible. Second, even if you had been an adult and were reporting what you witnessed — even then, seeing the one who permits the act act permissively does not constitute broader proof of permissibility.

### Tosafos & Obligation

The *Gemara* leaves the position of Reb Yehuda without extended analysis, but *Tosafos* spells out the conceptual framework. The reading of the *Megillah* is a rabbinic obligation, and therefore one might argue that a *katan*, who bears a rabbinic obligation of his own, could discharge another person's rabbinic obligation. Some *poskim* and *mefarshim* do draw a further distinction: the nighttime *Megillah* reading is characterized as *mitzvah d'rabbanan* in the full sense, while the daytime reading is framed as a *mitzvah midivrei soferim*, derived from the verses of the *Megillah* itself and therefore standing at a somewhat higher level. Either way, neither reading is biblical, and this opens the question of whether one who is rabbinically obligated may discharge the obligation of another.

*Tosafos* himself, however, recognizes two difficulties with this reasoning and does not find it straightforward. The first is what *Tosafos* terms the problem of a double *derabbanan*: the *katan* is rabbinically obligated in a *mitzvah* that is itself only rabbinic. This places him one level lower than an adult who is also obligated in a rabbinic *mitzvah*, and it is not self-evident that simply being within the domain of rabbinic obligation means that all distinctions of level dissolve.

The second difficulty is conceptually arresting: in what sense is the *katan* himself rabbinically obligated? Normally, when we speak of a child's obligation in *mitzvos*, we understand this as an obligation upon the *parents* — it is the father (or, on other views, the mother as well) who is obligated in *chinuch*, in training the child. To say that the *katan* himself bears a rabbinic obligation is somewhat paradoxical: since he is excluded from the system of biblical obligation, on what basis do the Rabbis obligate him directly? Yet the position of Reb Yehuda seems to presuppose something like that idea.

### Pirsumei Nisa

An entirely different conceptual framework may be applicable here. Rather than analyzing the question through the lens of personal obligation — whose level of *chiyuv* is sufficient to discharge whose — one might approach the *Megillah* reading through the lens of *pirsumei nisa*, the publicizing of the miracle. The *kiyum* of *pirsumei nisa* is an inherently communal and public act, and perhaps the relevant question is not whether this individual's personal obligation matches that of the listeners, but whether this public ritual has been performed.

A parallel may be drawn to the laws of *aliyos la'Torah*. The famous *Mishnah* teaches *hakol olin liminyan shivah* — in principle, anyone may be counted among the seven *aliyos*, and indeed the tradition records *be'emes amru* that a woman or a *katan* may receive an *aliyah*, subject to considerations of *kevod ha'tzibur*. The ordinary principles governing *mitzvas asei shehazman grama* or the requirement of being a *bar chiyuva* simply do not apply in the same way to *krias haTorah*, because the framework there is communal, not personal. Perhaps something similar is operative by the *Megillah* reading.

### Megillah vs Torah

This comparison, however, invites an important distinction. *Krias haTorah* is exclusively a communal obligation — one who stays home and does not attend the *tzibbur* reading has no personal obligation to read the Torah on his own. Because the obligation is entirely communal in structure, the requirement that the reader share the same personal *chiyuv* as the listeners is relaxed. The *Megillah*, by contrast, is understood as a personal obligation: each individual is independently obligated to hear the *Megillah*. To suggest that a *katan* can discharge this personal obligation through a *pirsumei nisa* framework is therefore a more significant *chiddush*.

### Women & Minors

One further observation: the *Mishnah* here excludes from valid *Megillah* reading the *cheiresh* (the deaf-mute), the *shoteh* (one who is mentally incapacitated), and the *katan*. Women are conspicuously absent from this list of exclusions. The first *Mishnah* in Arachin states *hakol chayavin* — the straightforward reading of which is that not only are women obligated in the *Megillah*, but that they are obligated at the same level as men and may therefore read for men as well. It is only through the *Tosefta*, the Bahag, and related material that the idea enters Ashkenazic thought that women's obligation may be at a different level. Nothing in the *Gemara* itself indicates such a distinction.

## Timing of Mitzvos

### Day Mitzvos List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין קוֹרִין אֶת הַמְּגִילָּה, וְלֹא מָלִין, וְלֹא טוֹבְלִין, וְלֹא מַזִּין, וְכֵן שׁוֹמֶרֶת יוֹם כְּנֶגֶד יוֹם — לֹא תִּטְבּוֹל עַד שֶׁתָּנֵץ הַחַמָּה. וְכוּלָּן שֶׁעָשׂוּ מִשֶּׁעָלָה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר — כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **One may not read the Megilla, nor perform a circumcision, nor immerse** himself in a ritual bath, **nor sprinkle** water of purification to purify people and objects that had contracted ritual impurity through contact with a corpse until after sunrise. **And also a woman who observes a** clean **day for** each **day** she experiences a discharge, i.e., a woman who experienced one or two days of non-menstrual bleeding, and must now wait until a day has passed without any discharge of blood before regaining ritual purity, she too **may not immerse herself until the sun has risen. And** with regard to **all these** activities that are supposed to be performed during the day, **if one did them after daybreak,** i.e., after the appearance of the first light of the sun, even before sunrise, **they are valid,** as at this point it is already considered daytime.

The *Mishnah* now turns to the *when* of the *Megillah* and related *mitzvos*. Having addressed the *how* (the manner of reading) and the *who* (who is obligated), the *Mishnah* pivots to timing, and in so doing makes a much broader statement about the category of *mitzvos* that are performed during the day. The *Mishnah* lists: *Megillah* reading, *bris milah*, *tevilah*, *hazah* (the sprinkling of the *parah adumah* ashes for one who is *tamei meis*), and *shomeres yom k'neged yom* — a woman who experienced abnormal uterine bleeding for one or two days outside her menstrual period, who must observe one clean day and then immerse.

### Day Definition

All of these are *day mitzvos*, and the *Mishnah* makes a further point: while these *mitzvos* should *lechatchila* be performed beginning at sunrise — *neitz hachamah* — if one performed them from the rising of the morning star (*amud hashachar*), it is *kasher*. The morning star is understood to precede sunrise by seventy-two minutes, or four *mil*. The *Mishnah* thus draws a distinction between the *lechatchila* starting point (sunrise) and the *bedi'avad* starting point (the morning star), and we will return shortly to the conceptual basis of that distinction.

### Megillah Day Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָלַן? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהַיָּמִים הָאֵלֶּה נִזְכָּרִים וְנַעֲשִׂים״ — בַּיּוֹם אִין, בַּלַּיְלָה לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive the *halakha* taught in the mishna that the Megilla may be read only during the day? The Gemara answers: **As the verse states: "And that these days should be remembered and kept"** (Esther 9:28). The word "days" indicates **during the day, yes,** but **at night, no.**

The *Gemara* asks: from where is all this derived? It begins with the *Megillah*. The verse states *v'hayamim ha'eleh nizkarim v'naasim* — "these days are remembered and done." The word *hayamim*, days, implies *bayom* — in the daytime — and not at night. This establishes the *Megillah* as a day *mitzvah*. The *Gemara* will go on to establish the *amud hashachar* / *neitz hachamah* framework, but the threshold question is first to confirm that each item on the list is indeed a day *mitzvah* to begin with.

### Night vs Day

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: חַיָּיב אָדָם לִקְרוֹת אֶת הַמְּגִילָּה בַּלַּיְלָה וְלִשְׁנוֹתָהּ בַּיּוֹם! כִּי קָתָנֵי — אַדְּיוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Let us say that this is a conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: A person is obligated to read the Megilla at night and** then **repeat it during the day.** The Gemara rejects this: There is no proof from here, **as when** the mishna **teaches** that the Megilla may be read only during the day, it was referring to **the daytime** reading, but the nighttime reading is not considered here at all.

Having established that the *Megillah* is a day *mitzvah*, the *Gemara* raises a difficulty: does this not contradict Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, who ruled that one is obligated to read the *Megillah* at night and repeat it during the day? The verse seems to speak only of daytime, implying there is no nighttime obligation. The *Gemara* resolves this by limiting the *Mishnah*'s discussion: *ki tanya ad hayom* — the *Mishnah* is addressing only the day reading, not purporting to exclude a nighttime reading.

That said, this resolution is not without controversy. The *Pnei Yehoshua* — or, according to some, the *Shagas Aryeh* — observes that the entire tannaitic literature, the *mishnayos* and the *braisos*, shows no awareness of a nighttime *Megillah* obligation. The nighttime reading was an innovation of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi. On this view, the *Mishnah* does not merely omit the nighttime reading — it simply does not know of it. The daytime reading is therefore the primary *mitzvah*, characterized as a *mitzvah midivrei soferim* because it is grounded in the verses of the *Megillah* itself, while the nighttime reading is described as a *mitzvah d'rabbanan*. This has practical implications: if one must choose between a nighttime reading and a daytime reading, one should attend the daytime reading, which is the primary *mitzvah*.

### Bris Milah Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא מָלִין וְכוּ׳. דִּכְתִיב: ״וּבַיּוֹם הַשְּׁמִינִי יִמּוֹל״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: **And one may not perform a circumcision** until after sunrise, **as it is written: "And on the eighth day he shall be circumcised"** (Leviticus 12:3).

For *bris milah*, the source is the verse *u'vayom hashemini yimol* — "and on the eighth day he shall circumcise." This establishes that the *mitzvah* begins with the day of the eighth and not the preceding night. An interesting question is raised regarding a *milah shelo bizmanah* — a circumcision performed not on the eighth day but later: is the nighttime exclusion limited to the night preceding the eighth day, or does it apply more broadly whenever the *milah* is ultimately performed? This question is in fact subject to discussion elsewhere.

The observation is also made, in connection with what was discussed earlier about the *Megillah*, that the distinction between the nighttime and daytime *Megillah* readings creates an opening for certain contemporary discussions. Even for those who wish to take seriously the *Tosafos*-based concern that women's obligation may be at a different level than men's, that concern might be limited to the daytime reading — since the nighttime reading is itself already at a lower level of obligation, and the conceptual framework may accordingly differ.

### Tevilah Hazah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא טוֹבְלִין וְלֹא מַזִּין וְכוּ׳. דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהִזָּה הַטָּהוֹר עַל הַטָּמֵא [וְגוֹ׳] בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי״, וְאִיתַּקַּשׁ טְבִילָה לְהַזָּיָה.
>
> **Translation:** § It is further taught in the mishna: **And one may not immerse** himself in a ritual bath, **or sprinkle** waters of purification until after sunrise. This too is derived from a verse, **as it is written: "And the pure person shall sprinkle upon the impure** on the third day **and on the seventh day;** and on the seventh day he shall purify himself and wash his clothes, and bathe himself in water, and shall be pure at evening" (Numbers 19:19), which teaches that the sprinkling must take place during the day and not at night. **And immersion is likened to sprinkling,** as it too is mentioned in the verse, "and bathe himself in water," so that whatever is invalid with respect to sprinkling is also invalid with respect to immersion.

For *tevilah* and *hazah* — the immersion and sprinkling associated with purification from corpse-impurity — the source is the verse *v'hizah ha'tahor al ha'tamei beyom hashevi'i*, "and the pure one shall sprinkle on the impure one on the seventh day." This establishes that the sprinkling must occur during the day of the seventh. The *Gemara* then links *tevilah* to *hazah* by way of *itkash* — the two are juxtaposed in the verse — and concludes that immersion must also be during the day.

The question is: which immersion? As *Tosafos* explains at length, there are actually two *tevilos* in the context of *parah adumah* purification. One immersion precedes the sprinkling, preparing the person for the act of sprinkling; this one is not clearly stated in the verses. A second immersion follows the sprinkling. *Tosafos* initially suggests that the *Mishnah* must be referring to the pre-sprinkling *tevilah*, but evidence from other *Gemaros* indicates that this immersion may in fact be performed at night. That reading is therefore difficult.

If, on the other hand, the reference is to the post-sprinkling *tevilah*, a different problem arises. The general rule regarding *tevilah* is that the earliest moment one may immerse is the daytime of the seventh day, not the preceding night. But once that threshold has been crossed — once the day of the seventh has arrived — and one has not yet immersed, the *tevilah* may subsequently be performed even at night. The same logic would apply here: once the sprinkling has been done (which must be by day), the subsequent immersion need not itself be restricted to daytime. It would already be day by the time the sprinkling was completed, and the *tevilah* could follow that evening.

### Tosafos Tevilah

*Tosafos* resolves this with a creative reading: the word *tovlin* in the *Mishnah* does not refer to going to the *mikvah* at all. Rather, it refers to the dipping (*tevilah*) of the hyssop — the bundle of herbs used to apply the *parah adumah* mixture — into the water. This must be done during the day, and if the hyssop was dipped at night and the sprinkling was performed the following day, it is invalid. *Tosafos* brings evidence to support this reading: even dipping the hyssop immediately before *amud hashachar*, with the sprinkling following moments after dawn, would be problematic if the dipping itself occurred at night.

### Shomeres Yom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן שׁוֹמֶרֶת יוֹם כְּנֶגֶד יוֹם לֹא תִּטְבּוֹל עַד שֶׁתָּנֵץ הַחַמָּה וְכוּ׳. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַאי שְׁנָא שׁוֹמֶרֶת יוֹם כְּנֶגֶד יוֹם מִכׇּל חַיָּיבֵי טְבִילוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **And also a woman who observes a day for a day may not immerse herself until the sun has risen.** The Gemara asks: This **is obvious. What is different about a woman who observes a day for a day,** who must immerse herself in a ritual bath, **from all the others who are obligated to immerse themselves,** as it was already taught that one may not immerse himself in a ritual bath until it is day?

The *Mishnah* then singles out *shomeres yom k'neged yom* — "she observes a day corresponding to the day" — specifying that she may not immerse until *neitz hachamah*, sunrise. The *Gemara* immediately asks: why is this worth stating? Is this not the rule for all who must observe a waiting period before immersion? Anyone required to count days before immersing — a *zav*, a *zavah*, a *metzora*, or one who is *tamei meis* — must wait until the beginning of the seventh day; the night preceding the seventh day does not suffice. Why single out the *shomeres yom*?

### Zavah vs Zav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיצְטְרִיךְ: סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא תֶּיהְוֵי כִּרְאִיָּה רִאשׁוֹנָה שֶׁל זָב — וּרְאִיָּה רִאשׁוֹנָה שֶׁל זָב אִיתַּקַּשׁ לְבַעַל קֶרִי, דִּכְתִיב: ״זֹאת תּוֹרַת הַזָּב וַאֲשֶׁר תֵּצֵא מִמֶּנּוּ שִׁכְבַת זָרַע״. מָה בַּעַל קֶרִי טוֹבֵל בַּיּוֹם — הַאי נָמֵי לִיטְבּוֹל בְּיוֹמֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It is** nevertheless **necessary** to mention separately the case of a woman who observes a day for a day. As, **it might enter your mind to say that** this woman's bleeding **should be treated like the first emission of a *zav*,** a man who experiences a gonorrhea-like secretion, in that just as a man attains the status of a full-fledged *zav* once he has three such emissions, so too, a woman attains the status of a full-fledged *zava* once she experiences three days of bleeding. **And the first emission of a *zav* is likened to one who experienced a seminal discharge, as it is written: "This is the *halakha* of him that has an issue and of him whose semen goes from him"** (Leviticus 15:32). From this it is learned: **Just as one who experienced a seminal discharge immerses on the** same **day** that he had the discharge, **so too, that one,** the *zav*, **may immerse himself on the** same **day** that he had the emission.

The *Gemara* answers: one might have thought the *shomeres yom* should be treated differently and actually be permitted to immerse on the night preceding her clean day. The reasoning would run as follows: the verse *v'zos toras ha'zav asher teitzei mimenu shichvas zera* links the *zav* (a man with abnormal genital discharge) who has experienced only one episode to a *ba'al keri* (one who has had a seminal emission) by way of a *hekesh*. A *ba'al keri* immerses on the very day the emission occurred — there is no waiting period of a full day. If one episode of *zav*-like discharge is treated like a *ba'al keri*, and a *ba'al keri* immerses the same day, perhaps the *shomeres yom* — whose status is less severe than a full *zavah gedolah* — should similarly be permitted to immerse without waiting for the following morning.

### Sefira Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא בִּימָמָא לָא מָצְיָא טָבְלָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״כׇּל יְמֵי זוֹבָהּ כְּמִשְׁכַּב נִדָּתָהּ יִהְיֶה לָּהּ״, בְּלֵילְיָא מִיהַת לֶיעְבֵּיד מִקְצָת שִׁימּוּר, וְתִיטְבּוֹל, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן כֵּיוָן דְּבָעֲיָא סְפִירָה —
>
> **Translation:** **And** although **this one,** i.e., a woman who observes a day for a day, **cannot immerse on the** same **day** that she experienced the bleeding, **as it is written: "All the days of her issue shall be to her as the bed of her menstruation"** (Leviticus 15:26), which teaches that she remains the entire day of her issue in her impure state and must wait until the day is over before she can immerse herself, nevertheless, one might have said that **at least during the night** following the day of her issue **she should** be able to **perform a partial observation,** i.e., she should verify that part of the night has gone by without bleeding, **and** then **immerse herself** at night, without waiting until morning. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that **since she is required to count** one day of purity after her day of impurity,

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְפִירָה בִּימָמָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** and **counting** can only be done **during the day** and not at night, as it says: "And she shall count for herself seven days" (Leviticus 15:28), she cannot immerse herself until after sunrise, although here she has to count only one day.

The answer is that *keivan d'ba'aya sfirah* — since the concept of counting is present here, since the *zavah gedolah* must count *shivah nekiyim* (seven clean days) with explicit enumeration as per the verse *v'safrah lah*, and the *shomeres yom* is structurally related to the *zavah* — *sfirah b'imama hi*: counting requires daytime. A clean day does not count as a clean day unless it has entered daytime. It is not enough that she experienced a period of no bleeding; the *yom*, the day, must actually register as a daytime period for it to count as the corresponding clean day.

### Seven-Day Principle

This leads the *Gemara* to articulate a broader principle: wherever the Torah designates a seven-day counting period — *zav*, *zavah*, *metzora*, *tamei meis* — the period ends with the arrival of the seventh day, meaning the beginning of the day, not the preceding night. This is analogous to the rule in *aveilut* that *miktzas hayom k'kulo*: a mourner does not rise from mourning the night before the seventh day, even though halachically the seventh day has begun; one must wait until the morning of the seventh day. So too here, the earliest one may immerse after a counting period is the morning of the seventh day. And even the *shomeres yom*, whose period is not seven days but rather a single day of *shmirah*, participates in this principle: since there is an element of counting — a *yom* must be designated and observed — that designation requires entering actual daytime.

Once the threshold has been reached, however — once the day of the seventh has begun, or for the *shomeres yom*, once the clean day has commenced — if the immersion is postponed, it may certainly be performed that following night.

## Day/Night Definitions

### Light Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רָבָא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וַיִּקְרָא אֱלֹהִים לָאוֹר יוֹם״, לַמֵּאִיר וּבָא קְרָאוֹ ״יוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived, that from daybreak it is already considered daytime? **Rava said: As the verse states: "And God called the light** [or] **day"** (Genesis 1:5), meaning: **To that which was becoming lighter and lighter he called day.**

The *Mishnah* states that all of these *mitzvos*, if performed from *amud hashachar* onward, are *kasher*. The *Gemara* now asks: *mana hani mili* — from where do we know this? Rava derives it from the verse *Vayikra Elokim la'or yom* — "and God called the light day."

### Beginning Light

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לַמֵּאִיר וּבָא קְרָאוֹ ״יוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** **To that which was becoming lighter and lighter he called day.**

The *drasha* reads: *l'me'ir u'va kara yom* — at the moment the light was beginning to illuminate, He called that period "day." In other words, "day" is not synonymous with "light" per se, but rather refers to the period that begins when it starts to become light — which is before sunrise, at *amud hashachar*. So *amud hashachar* already falls within the category of day.

### Symmetry Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה: ״וְלַחֹשֶׁךְ קָרָא לָיְלָה״ [לַמַּחְשִׁיךְ וּבָא קָרָא לַיְלָה]? הָא קַיְימָא לַן דְּעַד צֵאת הַכּוֹכָבִים לָאו לַיְלָה הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty with this interpretation: **However, if** it is **so** that Rava's interpretation of this phrase is correct, the following phrase: **"And the darkness** [***ḥoshekh***] **He called night"** (Genesis 1:5), should be interpreted in a similar fashion: **That which was becoming darker and darker He called night,** so that immediately after sunset it would be considered nighttime. But don't **we maintain** that **until the stars come out it is not nighttime?** We are forced to say that *ḥoshekh* literally means darkness, and similarly, *or* in the first part of the verse literally means light.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: if you read the verse as meaning "the beginning of light is called day," then symmetrically, the verse *v'lachoshech kara lailah* — "and He called the darkness night" — should be read as *l'machshich u'va kara lailah*, meaning as it begins to get dark it is called night. But that would mean dusk is already night — and we hold that *tzeis hakochavim*, the emergence of the stars, marks the beginning of night, not the earlier onset of darkness. So this verse alone cannot serve as the source.

### Nehemiah Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא, מֵהָכָא: ״וַאֲנַחְנוּ עוֹשִׂים בַּמְּלָאכָה וְחֶצְיָם מַחֲזִיקִים בָּרְמָחִים מֵעֲלוֹת הַשַּׁחַר עַד צֵאת הַכּוֹכָבִים״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״(וְהָיָה) לָנוּ הַלַּיְלָה (לְמִשְׁמָר)״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Zeira said:** We derive this *halakha* **from here,** as it is stated: **"So we labored in the work; and half of them held the spears from the rising of the morning till the stars appeared"** (Nehemiah 4:15), where "rising of the morning" means daybreak, **and** the next verse **states: "So that in the night they may be a guard to us;** and labor in the day" (Nehemiah 4:16). This demonstrates that the day begins with the dawn.

Rabbi Zeira therefore provides a different source, drawn from the book of Nehemiah, which describes the building of the *Beis Hamikdash*. The verse states: *v'anachnu osim b'malacha... me'alos hashachar ad tzeis hakochavim* — "and we were doing the work... from the morning star until the stars came out." And the text then says *v'hayu lanu halailah l'mishmor v'hayom malachah* — "the nighttime was for guarding and the daytime was for work." This defines the workday — explicitly labeling it *yom* — as the period from *alos hashachar* to *tzeis hakochavim*. Day, in other words, spans from the morning star to the emergence of stars at night.

### Alos to Neitz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי וְאוֹמֵר? וְכִי תֵּימָא: מִשֶּׁעָלָה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר לָאו יְמָמָא, וּמִכִּי עָרְבָא שִׁמְשָׁא לֵילְיָא, וְאִינְהוּ מְקַדְּמִי וּמְחַשְּׁכִי — תָּא שְׁמַע: ״(וְהָיָה) לָנוּ הַלַּיְלָה מִשְׁמָר וְהַיּוֹם מְלָאכָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies Rabbi Zeira's statement: **What** need is there for the additional verse introduced by the words **"and it states"?** Why does the first proof-text not suffice? The Gemara explains: The second verse comes to deflect the following possible objection: **You might say that** even **after "the rising of the morning" it is not** yet considered **day, and that from** the time **when the sun sets it is** already considered **night, and** in this particular incident it happened that **they began** their work **early,** before the official beginning of daytime, **and remained** working **late,** after the official end of daytime. Therefore, Rabbi Zeira continued and said: **Come** and **hear that which** is stated in the next verse: **"So that in the night they may be a guard to us; and labor in the day."** The entire time during which they worked is referred to as "day," which proves that the day begins at daybreak.

The *Gemara* anticipates a challenge: perhaps the builders were simply starting early and ending late — working before dawn and after dusk — and the verse's use of the word *yom* refers only to the middle portion of that work, not to the full span from *alos* to *tzeis*. The *Gemara* responds by invoking the second verse: *v'hayu lanu halailah l'mishmor v'hayom malachah* — the explicit labeling of the period of work as *yom* demonstrates that the full span from *alos* to *tzeis* is classified as daytime. This is why the second verse is necessary; without it, one could have argued that the workers merely extended their hours beyond the technical boundaries of day.

### Rashi Clarity

This establishes that *amud hashachar* marks the beginning of day, which explains why *bedi'avad* these *mitzvos* are valid if performed from *amud hashachar*. But it does not immediately explain why *lechatchila* one should wait until *neitz hachamah*. Rashi's explanation is practical: sunrise is an easily identifiable and universally recognized moment. If the standard were set at *amud hashachar*, people would not know precisely when to begin, conditions like cloud cover could lead to premature performance, and errors would result. Waiting for *neitz hachamah* is therefore a rabbinic safeguard ensuring clarity.

### Rav S. Framework

Rav Soloveitchik proposes a more conceptual framework. The temporal landscape consists of four boundary points: *amud hashachar*, sunrise, sunset, and *tzeis hakochavim*. The period between sunrise and sunset is clearly day; the period before *amud hashachar* and after *tzeis hakochavim* is clearly night. But the two intermediate zones — from *amud hashachar* to sunrise and from sunset to *tzeis hakochavim* — are gray zones, neither fully day nor fully night.

### Lemair U'Va

On Rav Soloveitchik's reading, the period from *amud hashachar* to *neitz hachamah* has the halachic status of *bedi'avad* day — it is genuinely part of the day, but not yet fully day. It is a partial, incipient day. Therefore, *lechatchila* one does not perform these *mitzvos* during this period, since it is not full day; but *bedi'avad*, since it is halachically within the domain of day, one has fulfilled the obligation. The distinction between *lechatchila* and *bedi'avad* reflects not a rabbinic fence but a conceptual reality: the morning twilight zone participates in the category of day but has not yet reached its full expression.

### Bein Hashmashot

The corresponding gray zone at the other end — *bein hashmashot*, dusk, from sunset until *tzeis hakochavim* — carries a different halachic status. *Bein hashmashot* is classified as *safek*, doubt: it is uncertain whether it is day or night. Anything done during *bein hashmashot* has the status of *safek yom safek lailah*.

### Safek vs Bedia'avad

This raises a natural question: if the morning gray zone (*amud hashachar* to *neitz hachamah*) is classified as *bedi'avad* day, why is the corresponding evening gray zone not classified as *bedi'avad* night? Why does one zone receive a *bedi'avad* determination and the other receive a *safek*?

Rav Soloveitchik's answer, in effect, appeals to the psychological and phenomenological character of each zone. In the morning, light is growing — however dark it still is, the trajectory is toward greater light, and one perceives the period as the nascent beginning of day. The growing light allows us to classify the period, even inchoately, as "becoming day." In the evening, however, even as darkness encroaches, we remain reluctant to relinquish the day. The remnants of daylight still present during *bein hashmashot* prevent us from tipping the period definitively into night. As a result, it is not classified as *bedi'avad* night, but rather as a genuine mixture of day and night — a *safek* in the ontological sense — that cannot be resolved in either direction.

## Mitzvos Lists

### All Day Things

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַיּוֹם כָּשֵׁר לִקְרִיאַת הַמְּגִילָּה, וְלִקְרִיאַת הַהַלֵּל, וְלִתְקִיעַת שׁוֹפָר, וְלִנְטִילַת לוּלָב, וְלִתְפִלַּת הַמּוּסָפִין, וּלְמוּסָפִין,
>
> **Translation:** **Although it is preferable to fulfill a particular day's mitzva at the earliest possible hour, the entire day is a valid** time **for reading the Megilla; for reciting *hallel*; for sounding the *shofar*** on Rosh HaShana; **for taking the *lulav*** and the other species on Sukkot; **for the additional prayer** recited on Shabbat and other occasions; **and for the additional offerings** sacrificed in the Temple on these occasions.

The subsequent *Mishnah* continues the theme of timing, but its emphasis shifts: from the question of *when* these *mitzvos* begin (addressed in the previous *Mishnah*) to the question of how *long* they extend. *Kol hayom kasher* — the entire day is valid — is the governing principle. These are *day mitzvos* that, while typically performed in the morning, may in principle be performed at any point throughout the day. A parallel principle governs *night mitzvos*: *kol halaila kasher*, the entire night is valid for those *mitzvos* designated as nighttime acts.

### Day List 1

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְוִידּוּי הַפָּרִים, וּלְוִידּוּי מַעֲשֵׂר, וּלְוִידּוּי יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the entire day is also a valid time **for the confession over the bulls** brought by the Sanhedrin or by the High Priest to atone for mistakes they had made in their instruction to the people; **for the declaration** made on the last day of Passover in the fourth and seventh year of the Sabbatical cycle, stating that one's obligations **with regard to tithes** have been properly fulfilled (see Deuteronomy 26:12–15); **and for the confession** of sins made by the High Priest **on Yom Kippur** over the special offerings brought on that day.

The *Mishnah* opens its enumeration of *day mitzvos*: reading of the *Megillah* — for although we perform *krias haMegillah* in the morning, one who did not do so may still fulfill the *mitzvah* later in the day. *Hallel* — though ordinarily recited at *Shacharis*, if somehow omitted, it may be said at any point in the day. *Tekias shofar* — though we blow the *shofar* toward the beginning of the day, it is technically valid throughout the day. *Netilas lulav* — taking the *lulav*. *Tefilas Musafin* and *Musafin* — both the prayer of *Musaf* and the actual *korban Musaf*, both of which are valid the entire day. This is itself a *chiddush*: unlike *Shacharis*, which has a defined time window in the morning, or *Mincha*, which is in the afternoon, *Musaf* may be brought at any point in the day.

The *Mishnah* continues: *viduy haparim* — the confession recited over the special communal and individual bull-offerings, such as *par he'elam davar shel tzibur* and the *par Kohen Gadol*. *Viduy ma'aser* — the declaration made after distributing the accumulated *ma'aser* from one's home. *Viduy Yom HaKippurim* — the confession recited on *Yom Kippur*, referring specifically to the *Kohen Gadol*'s confessions over the animals.

### Temple Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִסְמִיכָה, לִשְׁחִיטָה, לִתְנוּפָה, לְהַגָּשָׁה, לִקְמִיצָה, וּלְהַקְטָרָה, לִמְלִיקָה, וּלְקַבָּלָה, וּלְהַזָּיָה,
>
> **Translation:** The entire day is also a valid time **for placing hands** on the head of an offering; **for slaughtering** an offering; **for waving** those offerings that require waving in the Temple; **for bringing** meal-offerings **near** to the altar; **for scooping out a fistful** of flour from a meal-offering in order to burn it on the altar; **and for burning** the fistful of flour on the altar; **for pinching** the necks of the turtledoves and young pigeons sacrificed as offerings in the Temple; **and for receiving** the blood of an offering in a vessel; **and for sprinkling** blood on the altar and on the curtain separating between the Holy and the Holy of Holies.

The list of *Temple* service-acts follows: *semicha* — the laying of hands on the *korban*; *shechitah* — the slaughter of the *korban*; *tenufah* — the waving performed with various *korbanos*. All acts of *avodah* in the *Beis HaMikdash* require daytime and are valid throughout the day. The list continues: *hagashah* — the touching of the *minchah* to the *mizbeach* as part of the *avodah*; *kmitzah* — the taking of the handful from the *minchah*; *haktarah* — the burning of that handful; *melikah* — the severing of the neck of a bird-sacrifice; *kabalah* — the catching of the blood of an animal sacrifice; *hazayah* — the sprinkling of the blood.

### Purification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְהַשְׁקָיַית סוֹטָה, וְלַעֲרִיפַת הָעֶגְלָה, וּלְטׇהֳרַת הַמְּצוֹרָע.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the entire day is also a valid time **for giving a woman suspected by her husband of having been unfaithful [*sota*] to drink** from the bitter waters (see Numbers 5:11–31); **for breaking the neck** of the heifer as part of the procedure followed when a corpse is found outside a town and it is not known who caused his death (see Deuteronomy 21:1–9); **and for** all the steps in the **purification process of the leper** (see Leviticus 14:1–20).

Further ritual acts conclude the daytime list: *hashkayas sotah* — administering the *sotah* water to the woman suspected of adultery; *arifas ha'eglah* — the breaking of the neck of the calf in the *eglah arufah* ceremony for an unsolved murder; *taharas ha'metzora* — the purification ritual of the *metzora*, involving the birds, the sprinkling, and all its associated acts.

### Night Mitzvos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה כָּשֵׁר לִקְצִירַת הָעוֹמֶר, וּלְהֶקְטֵר חֲלָבִים, וְאֵבָרִים. זֶה הַכְּלָל: דָּבָר שֶׁמִּצְוָתוֹ בַּיּוֹם — כָּשֵׁר כׇּל הַיּוֹם. דָּבָר שֶׁמִּצְוָתוֹ בַּלַּיְלָה — כָּשֵׁר כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** Correspondingly, all the mitzvot that must be performed at night may be performed anytime during the night: **The entire night is** a valid time for **reaping the *omer*** of barley on the night following the first day of Passover, **for burning the fats** of offerings that had been brought during the preceding day, **and** for burning **the limbs** of burnt-offerings. **This is the principle: Something that it is a mitzva** to perform **during the day is valid** if performed anytime during **the entire day; something that it is a mitzva** to perform **at night is valid** if performed anytime during **the entire night.**

Two nighttime *mitzvos* are then enumerated: *ketziras ha'omer* — the cutting of the grain for the *omer* offering — and *haktaras chalavim v'eivarim* — the burning of the fats and limbs of sacrifices that were not completed during the day. The burning of fats is a *mitzvah* that begins the day before and continues through the night; it is valid the entire night.

### Ze Ha'klal

The *Mishnah* then states the *ze ha'klal*: *davar shemitzvaso bayom kasher kol hayom; davar shemitzvaso balaila kasher kol halaila* — a *day mitzvah* is valid the entire day; a *night mitzvah* is valid the entire night. This is a useful organizing principle, though it calls for scrutiny, since not every *mitzvah* clearly falls within its scope. *Shacharis* is not valid the entire day; *Mincha* is not valid the entire night. Calling *haktaras chalavim* a *night mitzvah* is also somewhat unusual since it begins the preceding day. So the *ze ha'klal* is a principle that requires careful qualification.

### Megillah Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָלַן? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהַיָּמִים הָאֵלֶּה נִזְכָּרִים וְנַעֲשִׂים״. לִקְרִיאַת הַהַלֵּל, דִּכְתִיב: ״מִמִּזְרַח שֶׁמֶשׁ עַד מְבוֹאוֹ״. (רַבִּי יוֹסֵי) אוֹמֵר: ״זֶה הַיּוֹם עָשָׂה ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that these mitzvot were commanded to be performed specifically during the day? With regard to reading the Megilla, **the verse states: "That these days should be remembered and kept"** (Esther 9:28). **For reciting the *hallel*,** the proof is from **that which is written** in *hallel*: **"From the rising of the sun to its setting,** the Lord's name is to be praised" (Psalms 113:3). **Rabbi Yosei said:** The proof is from another verse in *hallel*: **"This is the day that the Lord has made"** (Psalms 118:24), implying that it is to be recited during the day and not at night.

The *Gemara* then proceeds to provide sources for each item in the *Mishnah*'s list. For *Megillah*: the verse *hayamim ha'eileh nizkarim v'naasim* — "these days are remembered and done" — uses the word *yamim*, implying the full span of the day and providing no basis to limit the *mitzvah* to specific hours.

### Hallel Shofar

For *Hallel*: *mimizrach shemesh ad mevo'o* — "from the rising of the sun to its setting," indicating the full day. Rav Yosef offers an alternative: *zeh hayom asah Hashem* — "this is the day that God made," which equally implies the entirety of the day. For *tekias shofar*: *yom teruah yihiyeh lachem* — "a day of blasting shall be for you," with no further restriction.

### Lulav Musaf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִנְטִילַת לוּלָב, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּלְקַחְתֶּם לָכֶם בַּיּוֹם הָרִאשׁוֹן״. וְלִתְקִיעַת שׁוֹפָר, דִּכְתִיב: ״יוֹם תְּרוּעָה יִהְיֶה לָכֶם״. וּלְמוּסָפִין, דִּכְתִיב: ״דְּבַר יוֹם בְּיוֹמוֹ״. וְלִתְפִלַּת הַמּוּסָפִין — כְּמוּסָפִין שַׁוְּיוּהָ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And** daytime is the time **for taking the *lulav*, as it is written: "And you shall take for yourselves on the first day** the fruit of a beautiful tree, branches of a date palm, and boughs of a dense-leaved tree, and willows of the brook" (Leviticus 23:40). Daytime is also the time **for sounding the *shofar*, as it is written: "It is a day of sounding the *shofar* to you"** (Numbers 29:1). Likewise, the time **for** the **additional offerings** is day, **as it is written** with regard to these offerings: "To sacrifice an offering made by fire to the Lord, a burnt-offering, and a meal-offering, a sacrifice, and libations, **each on its own day"** (Leviticus 23:37). **And** this is also so **for the additional prayer,** because **the Sages made it equivalent to** those **additional offerings.**

For *lulav*: *u'lakachtem lachem bayom harishon* — "and you shall take for yourselves on the first day," with *bayom* implying the full day. For *Musafin* — both the sacrifice and the prayer — the source is *devar yom biyomo* — "a matter of a day in its day," and since the prayer parallels the sacrifice, *k'Musafin shavei rabanan*.

### Sotah Confession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְוִידּוּי פָּרִים, דְּיָלֵיף ״כַּפָּרָה״ ״כַּפָּרָה״ מִיּוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים. דְּתַנְיָא גַּבֵּי יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים: ״וְכִפֶּר בַּעֲדוֹ וּבְעַד בֵּיתוֹ״ — בְּכַפָּרַת דְּבָרִים הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. וְכַפָּרָה בִּימָמָא הוּא, דִּכְתִיב: ״בַּיּוֹם הַזֶּה יְכַפֵּר עֲלֵיכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** daytime is the time for **the confession over the bulls, as** this is **derived** by way of a verbal analogy between one instance of **atonement** in this context and another instance of **atonement** in the context of **Yom Kippur. As it is taught** in a *baraita* **with regard to Yom Kippur,** the verse states: "And Aaron shall present the bull of the sin-offering that is his, **and atone for himself and for his household"** (Leviticus 16:11). **The verse speaks of atonement** achieved through **words,** i.e., the atonement here is not referring to the sacrifice of offerings and the sprinkling of blood, but rather to atonement achieved through confession. **And the atonement** of Yom Kippur **is** only **during the day, as it is written:** "For **on that day will He atone for you"** (Leviticus 16:30).

For *viduy parim* — the confession over the bull-offerings — the *Gemara* derives it through a *gezeirah shavah* of *kaparah kaparah*, comparing the word "atonement" in those cases to *Yom Kippur*. By *Yom Kippur* the verse says *u'veyom hazeh yechaper aleichem* — "on this day shall atonement be made for you" — establishing that the confession must occur during the day, though at any point in the day. For *viduy ma'aser*: the verse *ve'amarta lifnei Hashem Elokecha* describes the declaration, followed immediately by *hayom hazeh Hashem Elokecha m'tzavecha* — "this day God commands you" — indicating that the declaration must be made by day.

In connection with *sotah*, the *Gemara* derives the day requirement through a *gezeirah shavah* of *torah torah*: the verse by *sotah* says *v'asah lah ha'Kohen es kol hatorah hazos*, and connects to the principle of *mishpat*, judgment, which operates in the daytime — *mah mishpat bayom, af sotah bayom*. The comparison of *sotah* to *mishpat* is worth pausing over. The *sotah* procedure in the Torah is in many respects not a legal proceeding in the conventional sense: the husband suspects his wife and brings her to the *Beis HaMikdash* for a ritual. Yet the Rabbis introduced an elaborate legal apparatus surrounding the *sotah* process: the husband must first formally warn her in front of witnesses; there must be witnesses to the *yichud*, the private meeting with the suspected man. In this way, while the ultimate resolution of the *sotah* matter still occurs through the ritual of the bitter waters rather than through a *beit din* verdict, the Rabbis worked to remove it from the domain of pure spousal discretion and place it within a framework of objective procedure and witness testimony. Comparing *sotah* to *mishpat* — and therefore to the daytime domain of legal process — may thus reflect something real about how the Rabbis conceptualized the *sotah* process.

### Sotah Legal

This is a motif worth keeping in mind when the tractate *Sotah* is studied: the *Torah* itself begins the process of channeling what might otherwise be a blood feud or private ritual into a controlled legal framework — analogous, in some ways, to the *arei miklat* institution, where the Torah similarly takes what had been a matter of clan vengeance and brings it under the supervision of a judicial process (*ad omdo lifnei ha'edah la'mishpat*). The Rabbis continue that trajectory, requiring increasingly formal procedures before the *sotah* ritual may be performed. Seeing *sotah* through the lens of *mishpat* here is therefore conceptually resonant, not merely a technical verbal linkage.

### Eglah Metzora

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְוִידּוּי מַעֲשֵׂר וְכוּ׳, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאָמַרְתָּ לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ בִּעַרְתִּי הַקֹּדֶשׁ מִן הַבַּיִת״, וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ: ״הַיּוֹם הַזֶּה ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ מְצַוְּךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** daytime is the time **for the declaration with regard to tithes, as it is written** in the formula of this declaration: **"And you shall say before the Lord your God, I have removed the sacred things out of my house"** (Deuteronomy 26:13–15); **and juxtaposed to** that passage it is written: **"This day the Lord your God has commanded you** to do" (Deuteronomy 26:16), implying during the day and not at night.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִסְמִיכָה וְלִשְׁחִיטָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְסָמַךְ ... וְשָׁחַט״, וּכְתִיב בַּהּ בִּשְׁחִיטָה: ״בַּיּוֹם זִבְחֲכֶם״. וְלִתְנוּפָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּיוֹם הֲנִיפְכֶם אֶת הָעוֹמֶר״.
>
> **Translation:** **For placing hands** on the head of an offering **and for slaughtering** an offering, it is derived **as it is written: "And he shall lay** his hand upon the head of his offering, **and slaughter it"** (Leviticus 3:8), comparing the laying of hands to slaughtering. **And it is written with regard to slaughtering: "On the day that you slaughter"** (Leviticus 19:6), meaning during the day and not at night. **And for waving** the offerings that require waving, it is derived **as it is written: "And on the day you wave the *omer*"** (Leviticus 23:12).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְהַגָּשָׁה — דְּאִיתַּקַּשׁ לִתְנוּפָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְלָקַח הַכֹּהֵן מִיַּד הָאִשָּׁה אֵת מִנְחַת הַקְּנָאוֹת וְהֵנִיף... וְהִקְרִיב״. וְלִמְלִיקָה וְלִקְמִיצָה וּלְהַקְטָרָה וּלְהַזָּיָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״בַּיּוֹם צַוּוֹתוֹ אֶת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard **to bringing** the meal-offerings **near** the altar, **it is likened to waving, as it is written: "And the priest shall take the meal-offering of jealousy from the woman's hand, and shall wave** the offering before the Lord, **and sacrifice** it upon the altar" (Numbers 5:25). The words "sacrifice it" are referring to bringing the offering near the altar. **And for scooping out a fistful** of flour, **and for pinching** the necks of the bird-offerings, **and for burning** the fistful of flour on the altar, **and for sprinkling** the blood, these are derived **as it is written:** "This is the law of the burnt-offering, of the meal-offering, and of the sin-offering, and of the guilt-offering, and of the consecration-offering, and of the sacrifice of the peace-offering; which the Lord commanded Moses on Mount Sinai on **the day that he commanded the children of Israel** to present their offerings" (Leviticus 7:37–38).

For *eglah arufah*: the *Gemara* derives the day requirement from the word *kaparah* — *kaper l'amcha Yisrael* — and since all sacrificial atonements are performed during the day, so too the *eglah arufah*. For *taharas ha'metzora*: the verse explicitly states *beyom taharato* — "on the day of his purification."

### Night Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְהַשְׁקָיַית סוֹטָה — אָתְיָא ״תּוֹרָה״ ״תּוֹרָה״. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וְעָשָׂה לָהּ הַכֹּהֵן אֵת כׇּל הַתּוֹרָה הַזֹּאת״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״עַל פִּי הַתּוֹרָה אֲשֶׁר יוֹרוּךָ וְעַל הַמִּשְׁפָּט״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard **to giving the *sota* to drink** from the bitter waters, this **is derived** from a verbal analogy between one instance of the word **"Torah"** and another instance of the word **"Torah." It is written here** with respect to a *sota*: **"And the priest shall execute upon her all this Torah"** (Numbers 5:30), **and it is written there** with regard to judgment: **"According to the Torah, which they shall teach you, and according to the judgment,** which they shall tell you" (Deuteronomy 17:11).

For the two nighttime *mitzvos*, the *Gemara* does not provide a full derivation here. For *ketziras ha'omer* it simply states *d'amar mar: ketzirah v'sfirah balailah v'hava'ah bayom* — the cutting and the counting of the *omer* are at night and the bringing of the *omer* is by day. This is a statement of received principle rather than a fresh *drasha*. The full derivation from *pesukim* exists elsewhere in the *Gemara* — in tractate Menachot — but is not reproduced here. For *haktaras chalavim*: the verse *kol halaila ad haboker* states explicitly that the fire-pyre burns all night until morning.

## Applications

### Klal Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל: דָּבָר שֶׁמִּצְוָתוֹ בְּיוֹם — כָּשֵׁר כׇּל הַיּוֹם. זֶה הַכְּלָל לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי סִידּוּר בָּזִיכִין וְסִלּוּק בָּזִיכִין,
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **This is the principle: Something that it is a mitzva** to perform **during the day is valid** if **performed** any time during **the entire day.** The Gemara asks: As the mishna has seemingly mentioned all daytime mitzvot explicitly, the words: **This is the principle, are to add what?** The Gemara answers: This principle comes **to include the arranging of the vessels** of frankincense alongside the shewbread in the Temple, **and the removal of** those **vessels** at the end of the week, as the verse does not specify the time when these procedures should be performed.

After stating the *ze ha'klal*, the *Gemara* asks what additional case the general principle comes to include — *l'atuyei mai*? Rashi suggests it comes to include *tefillin*, which is indeed a *day mitzvah* valid the entire day.

### Omer Forgotten

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: סִילֵּק אֶת הַיְּשָׁנָה שַׁחֲרִית, וְסִידֵּר אֶת הַחֲדָשָׁה עַרְבִית — אֵין בְּכָךְ כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this mishna would consequently be **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei said: If one removed the old** shewbread and frankincense **in the morning and arranged the new** ones **toward the evening,** i.e., at the end of the day, **there is nothing** wrong **with this,** as it suffices if the changeover is made any time over the course of the same day. The Sages, however, maintain that the new ones must be set in place immediately after the old ones have been removed.

The *Gemara*, however, says it comes to include *siddur bezichin v'silluk bezichin* — the arrangement and removal of the incense-cups placed alongside the *lechem hapanim* on the *shulchan* — which may be performed at any point in the day. *Tosafos*, in this section, raises the question of *sefirat ha'omer*: if one forgot to count the *omer* at night, may one count by day?

### Omer Night

The sources for *ketziras ha'omer* at night — derived through a *gezeirah shavah* and from the laws of *Pesach* — establish that the cutting and counting are nighttime acts. *Tosafos* engages with the question of what happens *bedi'avad* if these acts were done by day.

### Omer Only Night

The *Mishnah* here — *kol halaila kasher* — states that nighttime acts are valid the entire night, but does not explicitly say they can be performed by day *bedi'avad*. There is a *braisa* elsewhere (*niktzar bayom kasher*) suggesting that if the *omer* was cut by day it is valid. Yet *Tosafos*, invoking Rabbeinu Tam, rules that the categorical formulation of this *Mishnah* — where the *omer* cutting appears as a paradigmatic *night mitzvah* — is more stringent than that *braisa*, and the *stama d'matnitin* here takes precedence. Since the cutting can only be at night and not the following day, the same must apply to the counting of the *omer*: if one forgot to count at night, one cannot count the following day with a *berachah*.

### Lechem Hapanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים (״לִפְנֵי ה׳ תָּמִיד״) — שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא שׁוּלְחָן בְּלֹא לֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **And,** according to Rabbi Yosei, **how do I uphold** that which is written with regard to the shewbread: "He shall set it in order **before the Lord continually"** (Leviticus 24:8), implying that the bread must be on the table at all times? It means only **that the table should not be** an entire day **without the bread,** but if there is bread on the table for even a part of the day, it is considered as being there "continually."

Back to the *ze ha'klal*: in the context of *day mitzvos*, the principle comes to include the arrangement and removal of the *lechem hapanim* — the showbread — and specifically the incense-cups (*bezichin*) placed upon it, which may be done at any point in the day.

### Rabbi Yosi

A *braisa* is cited presenting Rabbi Yosi's view on the showbread itself. Rabbi Yosi holds that even if the old loaves were removed and the incense was offered in the morning, and the new loaves were not placed until the afternoon — leaving the *shulchan* without bread for many hours — this is still acceptable. The verse *lifanai tamid* — "before Me continually" — means, according to Rabbi Yosi, only that the *shulchan* should not go through the night without bread; a daytime gap, even a lengthy one, is tolerable. The principle *shelo yalin* — that the table should not pass a night without bread — sets the operative constraint, and as long as both morning and late afternoon are framed by bread on the table, the requirement is met.

### Pesach Midnight

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר שֶׁמִּצְוָתוֹ בַּלַּיְלָה — כָּשֵׁר כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה. לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna concludes: **Something that it is a mitzva** to perform **at night may be performed the entire night.** The Gemara asks: **What** does this principle come **to add** that has not already been mentioned explicitly?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאֵתוֹיֵי אֲכִילַת פְּסָחִים, וּדְלָא כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה. דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְאָכְלוּ אֶת הַבָּשָׂר בַּלַּיְלָה הַזֶּה״, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״בַּלַּיְלָה הַזֶּה״ וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״וְעָבַרְתִּי בְאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם בַּלַּיְלָה הַזֶּה״, מַה לְהַלָּן עַד חֲצוֹת, אַף כָּאן עַד חֲצוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It comes **to include the eating of the Paschal offering,** and consequently this mishna is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that it is written: **"And they shall eat the meat on that night"** (Exodus 12:8). **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya said: It is stated here: "On that night," and it is stated further on: "And I will pass through the land of Egypt on that night"** (Exodus 12:12). **Just as there,** when God passed through the land of Egypt, it was **until midnight, so too here,** the Paschal offering may be eaten only **until midnight.** The mishna, which asserts that the Paschal offering may be eaten all night, is not in accordance with Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya.

The *ze ha'klal* regarding *night mitzvos* — *davar shemitzvaso balaila kasher kol halaila* — comes to include the eating of the *Pesach* sacrifice, but not in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar ben Azaria. A *braisa* records: the verse *v'achlu es habasar balailah hazeh* — "and they shall eat the meat on this night." Rabbi Elazar ben Azaria applies a *gezeirah shavah* from the word *balailah hazeh*, linking the eating of the *Pesach* to God's passing through Egypt — which, as derived elsewhere, occurred at midnight. He therefore concludes that the *Pesach* may only be eaten until midnight.

### Tosafos Pesach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַרַן עֲלָךְ הַקּוֹרֵא לְמַפְרֵעַ
>
> **Translation:** May we return to you "One who reads out of order."

The *Mishnah*'s principle, *kol halaila kasher*, implicitly rejects Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya: the *Pesach* eating is valid the entire night, not only until midnight. However, *Tosafos* rules against the *Mishnah* here and in favor of Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya. *Tosafos* argues that a *stam mishnah* in the chapter of *Arvei Pesachim* aligns with Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya — the *Pesach* sacrifice after midnight renders the hands impure (*metamei es hayadayim*), implying it should have been consumed before that point — and that *stam mishnah* takes precedence.

*Tosafos* then draws out the practical implications directly: one must ensure that the *matzah* is eaten before midnight — *tzarich lemaher le'echol matzah beleil Pesachim kodem chatzos*. This applies not only to the initial *kezayis* of *matzah*, which fulfills the main *mitzvah*, but even to the *afikoman* — since the obligation of *matzah* in our time is *d'oraisa*. The *Hallel* recited after the *afikoman*, by contrast, need not be completed before midnight, since it is *miderabbanan* and the stringency does not apply to the same degree.

*הדרן עלך הקורא למפרע.*
