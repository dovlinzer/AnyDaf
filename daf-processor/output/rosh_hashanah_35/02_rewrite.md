# Rosh Hashanah 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blessings & Shofar

### Daf Introduction

This daf — daf 35, the final daf of Masechet Rosh Hashanah — opens by picking up in the middle of 34b. The *mishna* there records the case of *mi sheberach*: one who recited the *brachot* first and was subsequently provided with a *shofar*, proceeds to blow — *tokeia, marea, vetokeia* — after the fact. The *mishna* thus makes explicit what has been the taken-for-granted assumption throughout: the *brachot* in question are the *brachot* of *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros*, and they are sufficiently independent of the *shofar* that each can stand alone when circumstances require. If no *shofar* is available, one davens *shemoneh esrei* with *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* but without *tekios*; if a *shofar* becomes available afterward, one blows without renewed mention of any *bracha*. The separate *bracha* over *shofar* blowing — which becomes a fixed feature once the practice of blowing *tekios* outside of *shemoneh esrei* is established — develops later, in the time of the *Geonim*.

### Malchuyos Framework

Although the *mishna*'s treatment of the *seder habrachot* makes the integration of *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* with the *tekios* explicit — *malchuyos vetokeia*, *zichronos vetokeia*, *shofros vetokeia* — it is worth noting that this is a point on which Rabbi Akiva himself is emphatic: *im eino tokeia lama hu mazkir* — if one is not blowing, what purpose does mentioning them serve? For Rabbi Akiva, the two are inherently intertwined.

### Shofar Primary

The *gemara* states tersely: *tama* — the reason the *mishna* permits the separation is *delo havei lei shofar meikara*, that the *shofar* was unavailable from the outset. The inference follows: *ha havei lei shofar meikara*, once a *shofar* is available, *ki shama lehu aseder brachot shama lehu* — one hears the *tekios* in the course of the *brachot* themselves. The normal state of affairs presupposes their integration.

### Private Davening Case

The *gemara* then records the case of Rav Pappa bar Shmuel, who rose to daven privately and instructed his attendant: *ki nahirna lach, tka li* — when I signal you, blow for me. Since he was davening silently and not serving as *shliach tzibur*, there was no communal structure through which anyone could know when he had completed each *bracha*. He therefore arranged a personal signal so that the *tekios* could be coordinated with his private *shemoneh esrei*.

### Minyan Requirement

Rava, however, rejects this arrangement: *lo amru ela bechaver ir* — they only permitted the *tekios* to be blown during *shemoneh esrei* in the context of a *minyan*, a *chaver ir*, which Rashi explains as a communal gathering. A *braita* confirms: *keshehu shoman shoman al haseder ve'al seder habrachot* — when one hears the *tekios*, one hears them in order and in the order of the *brachot*. The *braita* then specifies: *bemei devarim amurim*, this applies *bechaver ir*; *aval shelo bechaver ir*, when there is no *minyan*, one still hears the *tekios* in their proper order — *tokeia, marea, vetokeia* — but not in coordination with *shemoneh esrei*.

## Braita: Obligation

### Hearing in Order

The *braita* continues with a striking series of distinctions. *Veyachid shelo taka chavero tokeia lo* — if an individual has not blown *shofar*, another may blow on his behalf. *Veyachid shelo berach ein chavero mevarech alav* — but if an individual has not davened *shemoneh esrei*, another cannot daven for him. The *braita* then adds: *umitzvah betokein yoteir min hamevarchin* — the greater *mitzvah* belongs to the blowers rather than the blessers. The practical application: if two villages face one another, one with a *baal tokeia* and one capable of conducting *shemoneh esrei*, and one must choose — *holchin limakom shetokein ve'ein holchin limakom shemevarchin* — one goes to the village that blows, not the one that prays.

### Individual via Others

The *gemara* initially challenges this as obvious — *pshita*: *hai d'oraisa hai d'rabbanan*, the *tekios* are biblical while *tefillah* and *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* are rabbinic. The *gemara* responds that the real novelty is this: even if one village offers a certain *baal tefillah* while the other offers only a doubtful *baal tokeia*, one would still choose the *safek* of a *d'oraisa* over the *vadai* of a *d'rabbanan*.

### D'oraisa vs. D'rabbanan

This distinction — *tekios* as *d'oraisa* and *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* as *d'rabbanan* — is important and deserves attention, because it runs against a position that Rashi appears to hold in several places, and which the *Ramban* and other *rishonim* cite this *gemara* to challenge. The *Torah* does say *yom terua yihyeh lachem* and *zichron terua yihyeh lachem*, but what precisely those phrases mandate — how many *tekios*, how many *teruos* — is not spelled out explicitly. The liturgical construction of *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros*, by contrast, is far more derived and inferential, and the general assumption in halachic literature is that liturgy — outside perhaps *shema*, *birkat hamazon*, and *birkat hatorah* — is rabbinic in character. This *gemara* reinforces that assumption in the clearest of terms.

### Rashi's Integration

The difficulty is that Rashi, in a number of places, seems to hold that *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* carry *d'oraisa* force. The answer Rashi would give — though he does not state it explicitly here — is tied to his interpretation of a *gemara* discussed the previous day, where the phrase *tekios m'akvot zo et zo, brachot m'akvot zo et zo* appears. Most *rishonim* read that phrase as saying that the *tekios* are each indispensable to one another and the *brachot* are each indispensable to one another — that is, one cannot take partial credit for a partial set. Rashi, however, reads it differently: one is not *yotzei* the *tekios* without the *brachot*, and one is not *yotzei* the *brachot* without the *tekios*. They must come together. Of course, the *mishna* itself makes clear that they can come apart in extenuating circumstances. What Rashi must therefore mean is that there are two levels of fulfillment: when combined, the *tekios* together with *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* constitute the full *d'oraisa* *kiyum* of the *mitzvah*; when separated, each becomes diminished. At that stage of separation, only the *shofar* blowing retains its *d'oraisa* character. *Malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* without the *tekios* are rabbinic. Hence, in the case the *braita* discusses — two villages, one offering only *tekios* and one offering only *tefillah* — the two are already separated, and at that point the *tekios* alone are *d'oraisa*. This *gemara* is therefore not a disproof of Rashi, because Rashi would argue that the very scenario under discussion is one of separation, and therefore the ranking the *braita* assigns is correct even on his view. Rashi's deeper position is that at a *d'oraisa* level, *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* belong together with the *tekios* — they are an integral part of the *d'oraisa* shofar obligation when performed in combination.

### Communal Prayer

The *braita*'s requirement that *tekios* during *shemoneh esrei* occur only with a *minyan* is itself significant. It supports those who hold that one only blows *tekios* during *chazarat hashatz* — for if the integration were permitted during silent *shemoneh esrei*, there would be no obvious reason to limit it to a *minyan*. What this seems to be saying is that the power of combining *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* with the *tekios* inheres specifically in the *tefillat hatzibur* — the community praying as a unified body.

Rav Soloveitchik articulates a useful conceptual distinction here: one's silent *shemoneh esrei* in a *minyan* is *tefillah b'tzibur* — prayer that takes place in the presence of a community — whereas *chazarat hashatz* is *tefillat hatzibur* — the entire community praying as a single unit through the mouthpiece of the *chazzan*. What this *gemara* would then be saying is that the integration of *shofar* and *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* as a unified act of prayer belongs to the *tefillat hatzibur* specifically — not to each individual's private prayer, even when that prayer occurs in the presence of others.

One way to sharpen this further: if *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* are derived from the *chatzotzros* — from the verse *al hatzar hatzorer eschem v'hareyosem bachatzotzros* — then the *chatzotzros* represent a fundamentally communal calling-out, the communal mode of *simcha* and petition, and that is precisely where the integration of *tekios* with *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* finds its home.

Those who permit the *tekios* during silent *shemoneh esrei* in a *minyan* would need to argue that even in individual silent prayer, there is a communal dimension present — we are all davening our private prayers as part of a community — and that communal dimension suffices. But the *braita*'s restriction to *bechaver ir* seems to work against that view, since that communal dimension exists in any *minyan* setting, not only in *chazarat hashatz*.

The *braita*'s final distinction — that one can be *yotzei* *tekios* through another's blowing but cannot be *yotzei* *shemoneh esrei* through another's davening — flows naturally from this framework. The *tekios* belong to the communal sphere and can therefore be conveyed through a *shomea k'oneh*-type mechanism. *Tefillah*, however, is something that must originate from within the individual. A number of *rishonim* cite a *Yerushalmi* explaining that *shomea k'oneh* does not operate for *tefillah*, *krias shema*, and similarly personal obligations, because these require initiation from the person themselves. One cannot piggyback on another's prayer with God. The *zimun* and the institution of the *shliach tzibur* provide the only recognized structures through which one person's prayer can serve another — and that is what sets the stage for the debate that follows.

## Shliach Tzibur Debate

### Gamliel Position

The *gemara* presents a *braita* that frames the central dispute of this section. The opening position states: *kesheim sheshliach tzibur chayav kach kol yachid veyachid chayav* — just as the *shliach tzibur* is obligated, so too every individual is obligated to daven his own *shemoneh esrei*. Rabban Gamliel disagrees and holds that the *shliach tzibur* is *motzi* everyone — the entire congregation fulfills its obligation through the *chazarat hashatz*.

A *braita* then records the exchange between Rabban Gamliel and the *chachamim*. The *chachamim* challenge him: *lidvarecha lamah tzibbur mispallelin?* — according to you, why does anyone bother with a private *shemoneh esrei*?

### Preparation Purpose

Rabban Gamliel's answer is striking: *kedei lehasdir shliach tzibur tefillaso* — in order to give the *shliach tzibur* an opportunity to prepare, to say it through once so that he has it straight before repeating it. Beyond that purpose, there is, according to Rabban Gamliel, no real function to private *shemoneh esrei* when one is in the presence of a *tzibur*.

This takes Rabban Gamliel considerably further than simply saying that the *shliach tzibur* can serve as a vehicle for those who do not know the *tefillah*. What he appears to be saying is that when one is present in a *tzibur*, the relevant mode of prayer is *tefillat hatzibur*, not *tefillat yachid*. One is praying to God as part of a community, in a different mode entirely. The only reason the individual's private *shemoneh esrei* exists in that context is as a preparation for the *shliach tzibur's* repetition.

This raises the question of whether Rabban Gamliel intends this as a general principle applying throughout the year, or specifically for Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur. The most natural reading of his position, applied broadly, would be that whenever one is present in *shul*, in a *tzibur*, the private *shemoneh esrei* serves no independent purpose. One is fully included in the communal prayer.

### Eino Baki Only

The *chachamim* respond with a counter-question: *lamah shliach tzibur yored lifnei hateivah?* — if the *shliach tzibur* is not *motzi* anyone, why do we have *chazarat hashatz* at all? The *chachamim* answer: *kedei lehotzi es she'eino baki* — in order to be *motzi* the person who does not know the *tefillah* by heart. On this reading, *chazarat hashatz* has a single technical function: providing a vehicle for the *eino baki*. It does not constitute a communal prayer that discharges the congregation's individual obligations. Those who know the *tefillah* remain responsible for their own.

This represents a genuinely radical divergence of underlying conceptions. Rabban Gamliel holds that in the context of a *tzibur*, private prayer effectively disappears — there is *tefillat hatzibur* and nothing else. The *chachamim*, understood on their most extreme reading, hold that there is no real *tefillat hatzibur* at all — only a technical mechanism to assist the *eino baki* — and individuals remain entirely responsible for their own obligations.

### Gamliel's Response

Rabban Gamliel pushes back: *keshem shemotzi et she'eino baki kach motzi et habaki* — the same mechanism that works for the *eino baki* works for the *baki* as well. The reason it works is not a technical workaround for those who cannot daven, but rather the inherent nature of communal prayer: the *shliach tzibur* speaks for the entire community, and that representation functions equally regardless of each individual's personal proficiency.

### Concession Claim

Rabba bar Bar Chana then reports in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *modim chachamim le-Rabban Gamliel* — the *chachamim* conceded to Rabban Gamliel. Rav, by contrast, maintains: *adayin hi machloket* — it remains an unresolved dispute.

The *gemara* then records that when Rabba bar Bar Chana transmitted Rabbi Yochanan's statement that the *chachamim* conceded, Resh Lakish pushed back — *iflag alei Resh Lakish* — and himself said *adayin hi machloket*. So we have Rabbi Yochanan maintaining that the *chachamim* conceded, while Rav and Resh Lakish both maintain the debate stands unresolved.

## Dispute Resolution

### Nachman's Reading

The *gemara* then asks: did Rabbi Yochanan really say the *chachamim* conceded? This question arises because another tradition in his name states: *hilchata ke-Rabban Gamliel* — we rule like Rabban Gamliel. If one rules on a matter, that implies the underlying dispute is still live. *Miklal d'peligi* — the very act of ruling presupposes an ongoing disagreement. So Rabbi Yochanan cannot simultaneously hold that the *chachamim* conceded and that we rule like Rabban Gamliel.

### Limited Scope

The resolution comes from Rabbi Chanah Tzippora'ah, who clarifies what Rabbi Yochanan's tradition actually stated in full: *halacha ke-Rabban Gamliel be-vrachot de-Rosh Hashanah ve-shel Yom Kippurim* — we rule like Rabban Gamliel specifically in the *brachot* of Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur. This implies that in those cases the *chachamim* still dispute Rabban Gamliel, and we are ruling against them. For ordinary *tefillah* throughout the year, the same debate may persist, and we rule like Rabban Gamliel there as well. The mention of Yom Kippur alongside Rosh Hashanah in this context is somewhat surprising, since the earlier discussion focused on Rosh Hashanah. The *Ran* resolves this by pointing out that throughout this chapter, *Yom Kippur* consistently refers to the Yom Kippur of *yovel*, which carries the same *brachot* as Rosh Hashanah. That is what the *gemara* means here when it pairs the two.

### Rabbi Meir Position

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction in Rabbi Yochanan's statements through Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak's reading. When earlier traditions said *modim chachamim le-Rabban Gamliel*, they did not mean the *chachamim* of our *mishna* reversed themselves. They meant that another Tanna — Rabbi Meir — agrees with Rabban Gamliel. A *braita* is then cited: *brachot shel Rosh Hashanah ve-shel Yom HaKippurim shliach tzibur motzi et harabim yedei chovasan, divrei Rabbi Meir* — the *shliach tzibur* fulfills the congregation's obligation in the *brachot* of Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur; this is the position of Rabbi Meir. The *chachamim* in that *braita* respond with the same language as our *mishna*: *keshem sheshliach tzibur chayav kach kol yachid veyachid chayav*. The *chachamim* of our *mishna* thus never conceded; what Rabbi Yochanan reported was simply that Rabbi Meir stands alongside Rabban Gamliel.

### Ruling & Debate

On this reading, the position of the *chachamim* in our *mishna* — that each individual remains obligated — stands unretracted. We rule like Rabban Gamliel, and there is a supporting *tanna* in the form of Rabbi Meir, but the dispute itself remains active. Whether the debate extends to ordinary *tefillah* throughout the year or is confined to Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur, and whether we rule like Rabban Gamliel in all cases or only those, are questions this passage leaves partially open. What is clear is that the *halacha* follows Rabban Gamliel for the *brachot* of Rosh Hashanah.

## Length Problem

### Distinction Question

Having established that we rule like Rabban Gamliel specifically for Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur, the *gemara* asks: *mai shna hani* — what is different about those days? Why does Rabban Gamliel's principle apply with particular force there?

### Many Verses

The initial proposal is *mishum d'nafishei krai* — because there are many verses in *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros*, making the *tefillah* far longer and more complex than what most people could manage independently.

### Verse Shortcut

The *gemara* immediately refutes this: Rav Chananel says in the name of Rav that once one has said *u'vetoratcha katuv leimor* — "and so it is written in Your Torah" — one need not actually quote the *pasuk*. If the verse problem can be circumvented so easily, why would the multiplicity of verses be the reason for Rabban Gamliel's special ruling?

### Many Blessings

The *gemara* therefore corrects itself: *ela mishum d'avshei brachot* — no, the true problem is the sheer number of *brachot*. Even granting that one can substitute the formula *u'vetoratcha katuv leimor* for the individual verses, there remain three complete *brachot* — *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* — that constitute a substantial and unusual addition to the standard *shemoneh esrei*. That length and complexity is what most people cannot manage alone, and that is why Rabban Gamliel's position carries particular force on Rosh Hashanah.

### Verses Required?

The simple reading of the *gemara* — and not the reading Rashi adopts — is that the formula *u'vetoratcha katuv leimor* provides a way of dispensing with the verses in *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* altogether. This creates an apparent difficulty: earlier in the tractate the *gemara* debated how many *pesukim* are required — ten according to some, three according to others — but nobody suggested fewer than three. How can this *gemara* imply that a summary formula suffices?

### Two Tracks

Tosafot resolves this by proposing two distinct tracks. If one chooses to cite *pesukim* in *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros*, a minimum number applies — whether three or ten according to the relevant opinion. But one is not compelled to quote *pesukim* at all. One may instead say *malchuyos* and append *kemo shekatuv b'toratcha*, *kemo shekatuv al yedei nevi'echa*, *kemo shekatuv b'divrei kodshecha* — invoking each section of Tanach by reference without quoting — and that suffices. The minimum-verse rules govern only those who elect to quote; if one does not quote at all, the summary formula is a complete alternative.

### Three-Verse Link

The *Ran*, in explaining the *Rif*'s position, offers a different reading. He argues that the *Rif* understands the formula *u'vetoratcha katuv leimor* not as an alternative track but as an interpretation of the ruling of Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri, who requires only three *pesukim* — one from Torah, one from *Ketuvim*, and one from *Nevi'im*. On this reading, the phrase *u'vetoratcha katuv leimor* itself counts as the *pasuk* from Torah, a parallel phrase for *Nevi'im* serves as the second, and the phrase for *Ketuvim* as the third. We rule like Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri that three suffice, one from each section, and these formulaic phrases are themselves the fulfillment of that requirement. This is thus not a third alternative separate from the ten-or-three debate; it is a way of satisfying the three-*pasuk* requirement with the most minimal possible formulation.

### Rashi's Reading

Rashi refuses the premise that *u'vetoratcha katuv leimor* provides a shortcut for *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* at all. Rashi holds that where the *gemara* speaks of *nafishei krai* — too many verses — it is referring to the *pesukim* of *musaf*, the verses associated with the *korban* offering, not to the verses of *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros*. The formula *u'vetoratcha katuv leimor* applies to the *musaf korban* verses, allowing one to reference them without quoting them in full. When the *gemara* then shifts and says the real problem is *avshei brachot*, Rashi understands that to mean the *pesukim* of *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* — because those *brachot* are long and their verses cannot be replaced by the formula. On Rashi's reading, the formula is unavailable for *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros*, and those *pesukim* must be recited in full.

### Musaf Verses

Tosafot takes sharp issue with Rashi's reading. For Tosafot, it is the *musaf* verses — not the *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* verses — that cannot be replaced by a formula. The reason is theologically significant: *tefillah* serves as a replacement for the *korbanot*, and if one wants the *musaf* prayer to function as a substitute for the *musaf* offering, one must verbalize the actual *pesukim* describing that offering. *Mikraot shel musaf tzarich l'hazkir l'olam sheheim b'makom hakorbanot* — the verses of *musaf* must always be stated explicitly because they stand in place of the *korbanot*. The formula works for the verses of *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros*, not for the *musaf korban* verses. Rashi and Tosafot thus arrive at precisely opposite conclusions about which set of verses is reducible.

### Rosh Chodesh Musaf

Tosafot raises a further question about the *musaf* of Rosh Chodesh, which coincides with Rosh Hashanah. Rashi holds that both the Rosh Hashanah *musaf* and the Rosh Chodesh *musaf* must be mentioned. Tosafot disagrees on whether the *pesukim* must be recited and also on whether Rosh Chodesh itself should be explicitly mentioned. Tosafot rules that one need not mention Rosh Chodesh explicitly; it is already subsumed in the phrase *milvad olat hachodesh u'minchatah* — the standard formulation — and for the *chatat* one says *b'shnei se'irim l'chaper u'shnei tmidim k'hilchatam*, which is the very language of our established *nusach*. The *Ran* offers a striking rationale for why Rosh Chodesh should not be highlighted: if one emphasizes that the day has the status of Rosh Chodesh, people will begin thinking of it primarily as Rosh Chodesh and recall that on a two-day Rosh Chodesh the second day is the true Rosh Chodesh, thereby undermining the sanctity of the first day of Rosh Hashanah. By not drawing attention to the Rosh Chodesh dimension, we protect the integrity of the day itself.

These are the debates concerning which *tefillah* we are discussing, a point worth making explicit: the entire discussion about the *shliach tzibur* being *motzi* everyone, and about the length of the *brachot*, concerns the *musaf* prayer. According to most *rishonim* and the accepted *halacha*, *malchuyos*, *zichronos*, and *shofros* are said only in *musaf*, not in every *tefillah* of Rosh Hashanah.

## Preparation Issues

### Mental Ordering

Rav Elazar states a general principle: *l'olam yasdir adam tfilato v'achar kach yitpallel* — a person should always first order his prayer mentally and only then daven. This is especially relevant in an oral culture without *siddurim*, where one must carry the entire *tefillah* in one's head before beginning.

### Special Occasions

Rav Abba qualifies this: *mistabra milta d'Rabbi Eliezer b'vrachot shel Rosh Hashanah v'shel Yom HaKippurim v'shel prakim* — the principle applies specifically to the *brachot* of Rosh Hashanah, Yom Kippur, and *prakim* — the infrequent prayers, such as the *shemoneh esrei* of Yom Tov. These are *tefillot* one does not normally say, and one must prepare as one would prepare *leining* before *leyning* — do not get up without having practiced. For the daily *tefillah*, however, which one knows by heart, no such preparation is required.

### Yehuda Exception

The *gemara* challenges this from the practice of Rav Yehuda, who would order his *tefillah* even before his regular daily prayer. If Rav Abba says the daily *tefillah* requires no preparation, how do we account for Rav Yehuda's practice?

### Monthly Davening

The *gemara* resolves this: *shani Rav Yehuda*, Rav Yehuda is exceptional — *keivan d'matzli mitlatin yomin l'tlatin yomin*, because he davened only once a month. There is a *gemara* elsewhere indicating that those immersed in constant Torah study may be exempt from prayer during their learning. Rav Yehuda would complete his review of his entire learning — Rashi suggests he would *chazar* through *Shas* once a month — and upon completing that cycle, he would pause and daven. For Rav Yehuda, therefore, *k'prakim dami* — each instance of davening is like a Yom Tov prayer, an infrequent event requiring advance preparation.

## Communal Exemption

### Field Workers

A *baraita* transmitted by Rabbi Abba bar Avira in the name of Rav Shimon Chasida states: *poter haya Rabban Gamliel afilu am she'basadot* — Rabban Gamliel would exempt even the people in the fields. This is not merely saying that those present in *shul* are *yotzei* through the *shliach tzibur*; it implies that Rabban Gamliel's principle, taken to its logical conclusion, extends even to those who cannot come to *shul* at all. The *tefillat hatzibur* speaks for the entire community, and the entire community — including those prevented from attending — is represented by it.

### City Objection

The *gemara* raises an immediate objection. If those in the fields are exempt, does it not follow — *kol sh'ken* — that those who remain in the city should certainly be exempt? But that conclusion seems wrong. Those in the fields have no choice; they are working and cannot attend. Those who are in the city, however, could come and choose not to — and by not coming, they are *poresh min hatzibur*, separating themselves from the community. This is comparable to the principle of those who stand behind the *kohanim* during *birkat kohanim* and are therefore not included in the blessing: by positioning themselves outside the gathered community, they exclude themselves.

### Necessity Criterion

The resolution, brought by Ravin in the name of Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi in the name of Rabbi Shimon Chasida, limits Rabban Gamliel's exemption precisely: *lo patar Rabban Gamliel ella am she'basadot* — he exempts only those in the fields, meaning those prevented from attending by necessity, *mishum d'anisei bim'lacha* — because they are compelled by their labor. Those in the city who could attend but do not are not exempted; *aval ba'ir lo*. They have separated themselves from the community and therefore cannot claim the benefit of its communal prayer.

### Full Attendance

An additional condition is implied by the Ran and grounded in the *Rif*: even for those who do attend, the exemption applies only to those who are present from the beginning to the end of *chazarat hashatz* — *shamu me'reshitah v'ad sofah*. Partial attendance does not suffice. One is either fully part of the communal *tefillah* or one is not.

### Communal Nature

The theological thrust of Rabban Gamliel's position comes into clearest focus here. His exemption is not predicated on a mechanism of *shomea k'oneh* — the principle by which hearing another's recitation serves as one's own. Rabban Gamliel himself made this clear earlier: in the context of a *tzibur*, he sees no need for private *shemoneh esrei* at all, not merely because one can be *yotzei* through the *shliach tzibur*, but because the mode of prayer in a communal setting is fundamentally different. It is *tefillat hatzibur* — the prayer of the entire community as a unified entity — and that prayer is offered by one *shliach tzibur* on behalf of the whole. This is not one person discharging another's individual obligation; it is an entirely different category of prayer in which all members of the community participate by virtue of being part of that community. For those unable to be physically present due to genuine necessity, they remain part of that community and therefore part of its prayer. Those who absent themselves by choice have severed that connection and cannot be included.
