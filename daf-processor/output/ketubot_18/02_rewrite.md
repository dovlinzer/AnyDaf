# Ketubot 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Field vs. Loan Example

### Introduction & Overview

The daf opens by returning to a question left hanging from the previous discussion: why did Rabbi Yehoshua, when illustrating the principle of *peh she-asar hu peh she-hitir*, choose the example of a field — "this field belonged to your father, and I purchased it from him" — rather than some other example? The remainder of this section of the *gemara*, and indeed a substantial portion of the rest of this *perek*, will feel at times like a digression into the territory of Bava Metzia, exploring *to'en v'nitan*, *modeh b'miktzat*, and related principles of monetary law. But this is not a digression. The *mishnayot* that follow in this *perek* are built around the principle of *peh she-asar* as it applies across a range of cases, many of them far removed from the original *ketubot* context. *Peh she-asar* in *dinei mamonos* is precisely the subject, and the *gemara* is using the question about Rabbi Yehoshua's choice of example as a gateway into that broader discussion.

### Loan Case Doesn't Fit

The *gemara* asks: why not frame the case as a loan? Why not say that Rabbi Yehoshua's example of *peh she-asar* is a person who tells his friend, "I borrowed a hundred dollars from you and I paid it back," where no external evidence exists? That too would be a clean *peh she-asar* — the same mouth that admitted the debt also claimed repayment. The answer the *gemara* gives is that such a case would not work for the *mishnah*'s structure. The *mishnah* is structured in two halves: when the person freely offers up all the information, *peh she-asar*, he is believed; but when witnesses independently establish the first part of his claim, he is not believed regarding the second. Applied to a loan, the *seifa* would have to say: if there are witnesses that he borrowed the money, and he now claims repayment, he is not believed. But that is simply not true.

### Repayment Without Witnesses

The *gemara* has already established the principle: *ha-malveh et chaveiro b'edim eino tzarich l'poreo b'edim* — if someone lends money to another with only witnesses (as opposed to a written *shtar*), the borrower is not required to produce witnesses at the time of repayment. He may simply pay back the money, and his claim that he did so is believed. This is the established *halacha*. And if that is the case, then even where witnesses testify to the original loan, the borrower's unilateral claim of repayment remains credible. The *seifa* the *gemara* would have constructed — that witnesses to the borrowing render the repayment claim unbelievable — would therefore be halachically false. Hence, the loan scenario cannot serve as Rabbi Yehoshua's example.

### Business & Policy Impact

This *halacha* is striking from a practical standpoint. One might reasonably worry about its societal effects. If a borrower can simply claim repayment without any corroboration even when witnesses exist to the original debt, that creates a significant disincentive for lenders. On the other hand, the ruling may function as a quiet push toward writing a *shtar* for every transaction, which has its own advantages in terms of documentation and clarity. But then again, the burdens of formalizing every loan also carry costs — they make lending more cumbersome, and history has shown that anything which makes lending more difficult tends to disadvantage those who most need credit. The tension here is real, even if the *halacha* is clear.

### Audacity Presumption

The underlying logic of this ruling is a *chazakah* that will recur throughout the *gemara*: *ein adam meiz panav bifnei ba'al chovo* — no one has the audacity to look the person who did them the favor of lending money straight in the eye and brazenly deny repayment if they never actually paid. This *chazakah* is weighty enough, in the *gemara*'s view, to counteract the evidentiary force of witnesses to the original loan. The logic is reinforced by the nature of a loan itself: unlike a disputed ownership claim, a loan is by its very nature meant to be repaid. Witnesses to a loan are witnesses to a transaction whose next natural step is repayment. Testifying to the existence of the loan does not foreclose the obvious possibility that the expected repayment followed. The *chazakah* then adds that a person would not lie about that repayment to the very creditor who helped him. The *halacha* stands, and consequently, the loan example cannot fit the *mishnah*'s structure.

## Loan to Father, Claim by…

### Case: Half Consumed

Having explained why the loan example fails, the *gemara* presses further: why not use a different case? Specifically, why not have Rabbi Yehoshua's example be a person who says to a friend, *maneh l'avicha b'yadi v'he'achalti pras* — "a hundred dollars of your father's is in my hands, and I consumed half of it"? In other words, the father lent me a hundred dollars, I still owe fifty, and I am now voluntarily disclosing this to you, the son. This too appears to be a clean *peh she-asar* scenario: the same mouth that disclosed the debt also limits it. And structurally, it seems like it could parallel the *mishnah*'s two-part structure — free disclosure leads to belief, while witnesses to the borrowing would lead to a different result.

### Modeh B'Miktzat Concept

Before analyzing why this case cannot serve as Rabbi Yehoshua's example, the *gemara* introduces a necessary background concept: *modeh b'miktzat*. If someone is confronted with a claim — "you owe me a hundred dollars" — and responds, "I only owe you fifty," that partial admission creates a legal obligation to take an oath regarding the disputed remainder. This is a Torah-level rule. And the obvious question, already anticipated by the *gemara*, is why this person does not simply have a *miggo* — a logical argument from a stronger position. If he were truly lying, he could have denied the entire claim. By admitting half, he is actually weakening his own position. So why should his partial concession not be treated as fully credible on the strength of the *miggo* that he could have denied everything?

The answer, as the *gemara* will spell out through *Rabba*'s principle, is precisely that he does not have a *miggo*, because the *chazakah* of *ein adam meiz panav bifnei ba'al chovo* forecloses the option he supposedly "could have" taken. He could not, psychologically and socially speaking, have denied everything to the very person who lent him the money. And therefore the "stronger position" from which the *miggo* would be drawn — complete denial — was never actually available to him. Since he could not have denied the whole debt, partially admitting it earns him no evidentiary credit, and he must swear about the remainder.

Now, what happens when there is no formal *ta'anah* — no active claim being pressed? What if the son has made no demand, and the borrower simply volunteers the information? Is this person still a *modeh b'miktzat* with an obligation to swear? Or is the absence of a formal claim dispositive?

### Rabbis: Meshiv Aveida

The *gemara* explains that this case cannot be used as Rabbi Yehoshua's example because it does not work according to either of the two positions among the *tana'im*. According to the *Chachamim*, the borrower in this scenario is always treated as a *meshiv aveida* — someone who returns a lost object. The logic, as *Tosafot* frames it, is essentially a *miggo*: when dealing with the son rather than the father himself, the borrower is fully prepared to deny the entire debt. The son does not know his father's business dealings; there would be no social or psychological pressure preventing a complete denial. Therefore, any admission at all is entirely voluntary, an act of pure good faith, analogous to returning a found wallet. Just as we cannot demand an oath from someone who returns a found object — doing so would destroy the incentive to ever return anything — so too we do not impose an oath here.

This logic holds even when witnesses independently establish the first part — that the father made the loan. After all, we have already established that even with witnesses to a loan, the borrower is believed to claim repayment. If even full repayment could be claimed credibly in the face of witnesses, then a partial repayment claim to the son, where the borrower could have denied everything, is all the more clearly a *meshiv aveida*. The *Chachamim* therefore hold that in all variants of this case — whether or not there are witnesses to the original loan — the borrower is exempt from an oath. This means the *mishnah*'s structure cannot be reproduced: there is no scenario where witnesses to the first half of the claim render the second half unbelievable.

### R. Eliezer: Oath Required

At the other extreme, Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov holds that even when the borrower volunteers the information entirely on his own, he is still obligated to take an oath. This emerges from a *beraita*: *pe'amim she'adam nishba al ta'anat atzmo* — sometimes a person takes an oath even on a claim that he himself raised, not in response to any demand from the other party. The example given is precisely this case: if someone says "I borrowed a hundred dollars from your father and consumed half," he must take an oath. The *Chachamim* respond that he is no different from a *meshiv aveida* and is exempt.

So at first glance, Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's position seems to be that *peh she-asar* does not even work here to produce an exemption. The *gemara* questions this: does Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov not accept the *meshiv aveida* principle at all? The *gemara* introduces *Rav*'s answer: the *beraita*'s language of *ta'anat atzmo* is not to be taken literally. There is in fact a *ta'anah* — a claim — being pressed. It is just that the one pressing it appears to be a *katan*, a minor, whose claim would ordinarily carry no legal weight, and so it is described as if no claim were being made at all.

### Son's Claim Weight Debate

The *gemara* immediately questions this: but we know that one does not take an oath in response to a claim from a *cheresh*, *shoteh*, or *katan*! And so the *gemara* clarifies: *mai katan? gadol* — "what is meant by minor? A full adult." He is called *katan* only in the metaphorical sense that he is uninformed regarding his father's affairs — a junior figure who lacks access to the information of his father's business dealings.

This clarification then exposes the real heart of the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov and the *Chachamim*, as *Tosafot* elegantly formulates it. The two sides are not really arguing about whether *meshiv aveida* is a valid principle. They are arguing about whether the son's claim carries the same psychological weight as the father's own claim. The *Chachamim* hold that a borrower is fully prepared to deny everything when facing the son — the son's claim, in the borrower's mind, carries no weight, and therefore any admission is purely voluntary, a genuine *meshiv aveida*. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov holds that the son's claim does carry weight — just as one would not be prepared to completely deny a debt to the creditor himself, one is equally unprepared to completely deny it to the creditor's heir. Therefore the admission of half is not freely voluntary; it is the same *modeh b'miktzat* dynamic as dealing with the father directly, and an oath is required.

The reason the *beraita* phrases it as *ta'anat atzmo* is not because Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov considers it literally a self-generated claim. Rather, the *Chachamim* consider the son's claim to have no weight — from their perspective it is *as if* no claim were being made. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov is correcting that premise, insisting that what the *Chachamim* call *ta'anat atzmo* is in fact a real *ta'anah*, and that he treats the son's position the same as the father's.

### No Chutzpah to Creditor

The *gemara* now invokes *Rabba*'s principle to spell out the underlying logic more fully. *Rabba* asks: why does the Torah require a *modeh b'miktzat* to take an oath at all? Why is there no *miggo* — I could have denied everything, so why not believe me when I concede half? The answer is the *chazakah*: *ein adam meiz panav bifnei ba'al chovo*. A person simply will not deny an entire debt to the very face of the creditor who helped him. And if he could not have taken the "stronger position" of complete denial — because human psychology forecloses it — then there is no *miggo*. He was never able to claim the whole thing, so admitting half earns him no credit. We therefore suspect him of lying about the other half, and the Torah imposes the oath to pressure a full admission.

The *gemara* then completes the psychological portrait: if this person is not prepared to lie completely, then one might ask — why is he lying at all? Why not just admit the whole debt? The answer is that he genuinely intends to pay eventually, but does not have the funds right now. He is buying time — *lishtamit* — rationalizing his partial denial as a delay tactic rather than outright theft. He has not psychologically committed to stealing; he is deferring. This explains both why the oath is effective (he is not a committed thief who would simply take a false oath) and why the partial denial occurs in the first place.

### Oath Compels Admission

This framework then maps directly onto the debate about the son. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov holds *lo shna bo v'lo shna b'vno* — there is no difference between the father and the son. The borrower is not prepared to baldly deny the entire debt even to the son, and therefore any admission is not a free *meshiv aveida* but a constrained *modeh b'miktzat*. The *Chachamim* hold *bo hu d'eino me'iz, aval b'vno me'iz* — he is not brazen enough to deny the whole thing to the creditor himself, but toward the son he is fully prepared to deny everything. Therefore any admission to the son is entirely voluntary, making him a genuine *meshiv aveida*, and he is exempt from an oath even where there are independent witnesses to the original loan.

The conclusion is clear: according to the *Chachamim*, this case cannot illustrate the *mishnah*'s structure because the borrower is always exempt regardless of whether witnesses exist. According to Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, this case cannot illustrate the *mishnah*'s structure because the borrower is always obligated to swear regardless of whether he volunteers the information. Neither position produces the bifurcation the *mishnah* requires — free disclosure yields exemption; witnesses to the first part yield obligation. Hence, the field example remains the uniquely appropriate illustration, and Rabbi Yehoshua's choice is vindicated.

## Document Validation & Re…

### Witness Self-Validation…

The *mishnah* now pivots to a new class of *peh she-asar* cases involving the authentication of documents. The scenario is as follows: a *shtar* is produced in court but has not yet been independently verified. The process of *kiyum shtarot* — validating a document's signatures — normally requires either bringing the signatories themselves to affirm their signatures, producing witnesses who recognize those signatures, or comparing them against a previously certified document. Until this process is complete, the *shtar* does not have the standing of formally delivered testimony.

In the *mishnah*'s case, the witnesses themselves come forward and affirm: yes, that is our signature. But in the very same statement they add a disqualifying claim — we were minors when we signed it, or we were relatives of one of the parties, or we were coerced into signing a document we knew to be false. In the first two instances, the underlying transaction may well have occurred, but the testimony is invalid because the witnesses were legally disqualified. In the third instance — coercion — the transaction itself allegedly never occurred; the witnesses were forced at gunpoint, so to speak, to sign a fabricated document.

The *mishnah* rules *harei elu ne'emanim* — they are believed. This is a *peh she-asar*: the same mouth that validated the document also neutralized it. However, if other witnesses independently verify the signatures, or if a previously authenticated document with matching signatures establishes their validity, then the signatories are no longer believed to add the disqualifying claim. At that point, the first half of their statement is no longer theirs to give — it has been established independently — and *peh she-asar* does not apply.

### Peh She-Asar in Documents

Before proceeding, it is worth pausing to examine what *peh she-asar* actually means in this context, because there are two possible understandings, and they lead to different legal consequences. One understanding is that *peh she-asar hu peh she-hitir* means we accept both halves of the statement: we believe both that it is their signature and that the disqualifying circumstance is true. The alternative understanding is that the two halves of the statement cancel each other out entirely — as if nothing was said, and the document remains in its prior unvalidated state.

The practical difference emerges in a case where accepting the first half of the statement would trigger some independent legal consequence. If, for example, the witnesses say "it is our signature, but we were minors," and we treat the first half as believed and thus the *shtar* as validated, then we have effectively admitted live testimony into evidence. At that point, can the second half of the statement undo what the first half set in motion? Or has the first half already triggered an independent effect that the second half cannot reverse?

*Tosafot* addresses this and indicates that in the *mishnah*'s framework, the *peh she-asar* logic functions more like neutralization — the witnesses are treated as having said nothing. It is not that we validated the *shtar* and then un-validated it; rather, the entire combined statement never constituted a *kiyum* of the *shtar* at all. The two halves are so intertwined — indeed, *Rashi* even characterizes it as delivered *toch kedei dibbur*, as a single uninterrupted utterance — that they function as a unit that leaves the *shtar* exactly where it was.

### Rami bar Chama's Distinc…

*Rami bar Chama* introduces an important qualification that applies to the first part of the *mishnah* — the case where the witnesses themselves are the ones affirming their signatures, before any independent verification. He distinguishes between two types of claimed coercion. When the witnesses say *anusim hayinu machmat nefashot* — we were coerced under mortal threat, a gun was held to our heads — they are believed. But when they say *anusim hayinu machmat mamon* — we were coerced by financial threat, the man threatened to destroy our property unless we signed — they are not believed as to the disqualifying claim.

The reason for this distinction is the principle of *ein adam meisim atzmo rasha*. When witnesses claim that they signed a false document under threat of financial loss, they are admitting to having committed the biblical prohibition of *edut sheker* — bearing false testimony — for a reason that halachah does not recognize as sufficient justification. A threat to one's property does not permit transgressing a Torah prohibition; only a mortal threat creates that exemption. Therefore, by saying "we signed falsely because he threatened our finances," they are declaring themselves *resha'im* — wicked, in the technical legal sense of having violated a biblical law. And the rule is that a court does not admit self-incriminating testimony of this kind. The statement is therefore inadmissible insofar as it declares them wicked, and we are left with only the first part of their combined statement: the validation of the *shtar*.

This is a revealing illustration of how *peh she-asar* operates. The *gemara* here is not treating the two halves as mutually canceling. Rather, it is saying: we are prepared in principle to believe both halves — but sometimes, after accepting the first half, a legal rule prevents us from accepting the second. *Ein adam meisim atzmo rasha* is precisely such a rule. The court accepts the validation of the *shtar* and is then legally barred from registering the self-incriminating second half of the statement. The result is that the *shtar* stands.

By contrast, when the claimed coercion was mortal — *machmat nefashot* — no transgression occurred. Signing a false document to save one's life is halachically permitted. Therefore there is no self-incrimination, no *rasha*, no bar to accepting the second half of the statement, and *peh she-asar* operates in its full form: both halves are believed, and the document is neutralized.

The case of minors and relatives stands differently again. Claiming to have been a minor or a relative when signing does not constitute a claim of having borne false testimony at all. The witnesses in those cases are not saying the underlying transaction was fabricated — only that their testimony was legally invalid. They are not *resha'im*; they are simply disqualified witnesses. There is therefore no bar from *ein adam meisim atzmo rasha* to accepting the second half of their statement, and *peh she-asar* functions straightforwardly.

The *gemara* then considers a further objection raised by *Rava*. Even granting that two witnesses speaking together constitute testimony — so that their combined declaration that they were coerced under mortal threat should be treated as live, valid testimony against the document — there is a fundamental problem: *keivan shehigid, shuv eino chozer umagid*. Once a person has testified, he cannot retract his testimony. This principle, rooted in the language of the verse *ve'im lo yagid venasa avono*, establishes that the moment testimony is delivered and is no longer within *toch kedei dibbur*, it is irrevocable. How, then, can these witnesses now contradict what they effectively testified to when they signed the document?

### Testimony Non-Retraction

The principle of *keivan shehigid shuv eino chozer umagid* is absolute once testimony has been delivered. *Rava* raises this as a challenge to *Rami bar Chama*'s ruling: even in the case of mortal coercion, once the document has been validated independently, the witnesses' subsequent oral declaration that it was all fabricated amounts to a retraction of their prior testimony, and such a retraction is legally ineffective. One might have thought that the retraction should be accepted because the original testimony was not delivered orally in court but was embodied in a signed document — perhaps a document does not carry the same weight as live courtroom testimony. But this is precisely where *Reish Lakish*'s ruling intervenes.

### Reish Lakish on Document…

*Reish Lakish* holds: *edim ha-chatumim al ha-shtar na'asu ke-mi she-nichkera edutam be-veit din* — witnesses who sign a document have the same legal standing as witnesses whose oral testimony has been formally cross-examined in court. The signature on a validated *shtar* is equivalent in evidentiary weight to direct courtroom testimony. Once the *shtar* has been independently validated, the witnesses cannot retract. The earlier part of the *mishnah* — where the witnesses themselves are the source of validation and are therefore believed to add the qualifying claim — accordingly applies only to that first scenario, before independent validation. Once the *seifa*'s condition is met and the signatures are established through external means, no subsequent claim by the witnesses can dislodge the document.

### Ein Adam Meisim Atzmo Ra…

The principle of *ein adam meisim atzmo rasha* — that a person is not believed, and indeed that the court will not give legal force to testimony, when it would require treating the speaker as a *rasha* — applies specifically to the case of financial coercion. In that case, the witnesses have admitted to having committed *edut sheker* for an insufficient reason. Their self-incriminating declaration is inadmissible. The *shtar* stands, and the second half of their statement — the part that would have voided it — cannot be entered into evidence. This is the structure *Rami bar Chama* describes, and it holds for the *reisha* of the *mishnah* where independent validation has not yet occurred. The combined effect of *ein adam meisim atzmo rasha* and the rule of *keivan shehigid* means that in the *seifa* — where the document has been externally validated — the witnesses are never believed to retract their testimony under any circumstances.

## Rabbi Meir's Strict View

### Meir: No Self-Disqualifi…

The *gemara* now introduces a *beraita* that records a further stringency. *Tanu Rabbanan*: *ein ne'emanim le-foslo* — they are not believed to disqualify the document — *divrei Rabbi Meir*. The *Chachamim* say they are believed. This applies to the *reisha* of the *mishnah* — the case where the witnesses themselves are the ones validating the *shtar* and simultaneously claiming disqualification. The *Chachamim* hold that this is a classic *peh she-asar*: the same mouth that validated, disqualified. Rabbi Meir does not accept the disqualifying claim even here.

### Presumptions Override Cl…

Rabbi Meir's position requires explanation, since on the surface the two halves of the statement appear perfectly matched: the mouth that bound also released. The *gemara*'s answer distinguishes between accepting the *peh she-asar* logic in principle and recognizing that the first half of the statement, once accepted, can trigger presumptions strong enough to override the second half. For Rabbi Meir, *peh she-asar* does not mean the two halves automatically neutralize each other. Rather, the first half is accepted on its own terms, and the second half must be weighed against whatever it stands against — including firm halachic presumptions.

### Chazaka: Valid Witnesses…

In the case where the witnesses claim to have been relatives of a party, the *gemara* explains Rabbi Meir's ruling through the following reasoning: *malveh gufeih meidak dayyek u-michtav* — a creditor who goes to the trouble of having a document written takes care to ensure that the witnesses he uses are valid witnesses. A person protecting his financial interests will not carelessly use relatives as signatories. This is a *chazakah*: an established pattern of human behavior. The witnesses' claim that they were relatives therefore runs directly against this presumption, and the presumption wins.

Similarly, *Reish Lakish* provides the parallel *chazakah* regarding minors: *ein ha-edim chotmim al ha-shtar ella im ken na'asa be-gadol* — witnesses do not sign a document unless it was executed when they were adults. Minors simply do not appear as signatories on formal legal documents. Once we have accepted the first half of the witnesses' statement — that the signatures are genuine — the *chazakah* kicks in and tells us that genuine adult signatories means the transaction was executed with adult witnesses. Their subsequent claim that they were minors is overridden by this presumption. The *peh she-asar* logic gives the second half of the statement a degree of credibility, but that credibility is not sufficient to overcome an established presumption operating in the other direction.

### Coercion Claim Status

The *gemara* then asks about the case of claimed coercion. In the cases of relatives and minors, Rabbi Meir is able to invoke *chazakot* — it simply does not happen that creditors use relatives or minors as witnesses. But coercion is different. Sadly, coercion does happen. People do find themselves forced to sign documents at threat. There is no equivalent *chazakah* that coerced signatures are vanishingly rare. So why does Rabbi Meir not believe them when they claim coercion? The answer to this will be taken up in the next discussion — the *gemara* closes here, leaving that thread open for continuation.

What the overall structure of Rabbi Meir's position reveals, however, is a particular understanding of *peh she-asar*: it does not operate as an automatic mutual cancellation. It means that we grant the second half of the statement a claim to credibility — and in ordinary circumstances, that credibility is sufficient, which is why the *Chachamim* accept it. But Rabbi Meir holds that wherever the second half runs against a strong presumption — a *chazakah* that the disqualifying circumstance simply does not occur — the presumption takes precedence, and only the first half of the statement is accepted. The document stands.
