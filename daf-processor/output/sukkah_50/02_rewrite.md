# Sukkah 50 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nisuch Hamayim

### Perek Overview

We conclude the discussion of the *nisuch hamayim* at the end of the fourth *perek* of Sukkah, which has been devoted to the collection of *mitzvot* that apply on *Sukkot*. The *perek* began with *lulav*, elaborated on *aravah* — which has both an aspect outside the *Mikdash* and a specific *Mikdash* ritual — and has been dominated by the laws of *nisuch hamayim*, the water libation. This water libation theme will carry forward into the fifth *perek*, which opens with the *Simchat Beit HaShoevah*, the celebration that took place the preceding night. The *avodah* itself — the pouring of the water — was performed during the day, accompanying the *tamid shel shachar*, together with the wine libations. The *Simchat Beit HaShoevah* was the all-night celebration that led into that morning ritual.

### Weekday vs Shabbat

The final *Mishna* of the fourth *perek* states that what they did on a weekday, they also did on *Shabbat* — with one logistical adjustment. On a weekday, they would draw water from the spring. On *erev Shabbat*, they would draw the water in advance, fill it into a barrel, and leave it there so that on *Shabbat* morning they could draw from the barrel rather than carry it from outside. The barrel was kept within the *Mikdash*, already positioned for use the next morning.

### Non-Sanctified Barrel

The *Mishna* specifies that the barrel used was a gold barrel that was *ainah mekudeshes* — non-sanctified. The *Gemara* asks the obvious question: *v'na'avid b'mekudeshes* — why not simply use a sanctified gold barrel? Why insist on a non-sanctified vessel?

Ze'iri explains that this *Tanna* holds two principles. First, *ain shiur l'mayim* — there is no exact prescribed amount of water; while we use three *log*, it need not be precisely three *log*. Second, *klei shares mekadshin shelo m'da'as* — sanctified vessels sanctify their contents even without intent.

To understand why both principles are necessary, consider the underlying concern. For inanimate objects such as a *mincha*, *kedushah* at the level of *kedushat haguf* — intrinsic sanctity, the sanctity of a *korban* proper — is conferred when the item is placed into a *kli shares*. Once something reaches *kedushat haguf*, the full consequences of *korban* status apply: if it remains overnight (*linah*), it becomes invalid; if it is removed from the *Beit HaMikdash*, it becomes invalid. Therefore, if the barrel were sanctified and one filled it with water the day before, the water would acquire *kedushat haguf* and become invalid by the following morning — precisely when it was needed.

Now, why must Ze'iri state *shelo m'da'as*? Because one might argue that *klei shares* only sanctify when the officiant intends them to do so. If the *Kohen* specifically intended that the water should not yet be sanctified — since it would not be used until morning — perhaps that intent could prevent the sanctification from taking effect. *Shelo m'da'as* answers this: the mere act of placing something in a *kli shares* imparts sanctity, regardless of intent, even contrary intent.

And why is *ain shiur l'mayim* also required? Because one might concede that intent is irrelevant, yet argue that the objective reality of the situation prevents sanctification. A *kli shares* and *kedushat haguf* represent an object that is, so to speak, *ready to be offered* — in the full state of being about to be used for the service. If the barrel contains five *log* and the libation requires three, the objective reality suggests the water is not yet ready: it is not in the precise state of an offering prepared for use. The *Gemara* therefore adds *ain shiur l'mayim*: technically, any amount suffices, so the objective reality cannot provide this escape. Once the water is in the vessel, it is automatically sanctified regardless of quantity.

It is worth noting that *Tosafot* raises an important question here. Why is the concern specifically about *linah* — invalidation by remaining overnight? The moment one walks outside the *Mikdash* to draw the water, the vessel is outside the Temple precinct, and if it were sanctified it would immediately become invalid through *yotzei* — being removed from the sacred space. The answer is that something cannot become sanctified outside the *Mikdash* in the first place. Sanctification requires the vessel to be inside the *Beit HaMikdash*. Only once the water is brought in does the sanctification take effect — and at that point, the problem of *linah* applies.

### Sanctification Without I…

Chizkiyya offers a counterargument: *kli shares ein mekadshin ela midaas* — sanctified vessels do not sanctify their contents unless one intends them to do so. According to Chizkiyya, the officiant could stipulate that the water should not be sanctified when it is placed in the barrel on *erev Shabbat*, and this would be effective. If so, there should be no problem using a sanctified barrel — simply stipulate that the sanctification not take effect until morning.

### Gezeira Concern

The *Gemara* responds that even granting Chizkiyya's position, there is a rabbinic concern: *gezeira shema yomru l'daas niskadshu* — a decree against using a sanctified barrel lest people observe that a sanctified vessel was used and the water was kept overnight, and draw the mistaken conclusion that sanctified objects may routinely be left overnight. Since no one else can see the officiant's internal stipulation, onlookers will think the water was sanctified in the normal way and will derive an incorrect *halacha* from the practice.

### Rav Zeira's Position

Rav Zeira, in the name of Rav Yannai, extends the concern even further. Even if one accepts both of the following propositions — that there is a fixed exact measurement of three *log* for the *nisuch hamayim*, and that *klei shares* do not sanctify without intent — there is still a reason not to use a sanctified barrel. In this scenario one might think: since the barrel obviously contains much more than three *log*, any observer will objectively recognize that the water cannot yet be in the state of a prepared offering, and no one will draw any mistaken conclusions. Rav Zeira answers that even so, there is a concern: *gezeira shema yomru l'kiddush yadayim v'raglayim mela'un* — people will not know why the barrel was filled. They will assume it was filled for the *Kohen Gadol*'s hand-washing the next morning, a practice that had no fixed minimum amount of water, and they will then conclude that sanctified water could be left overnight and used the following day. Thus across all the variants — whether or not there is an exact measurement, whether or not intent matters — there is always grounds for concern about using a sanctified vessel.

### Uncovered Water Problem

The *Mishna* further states that if the water drawn on *erev Shabbat* was spilled or left uncovered (*nitgalta*), it could not be used. The *Gemara* focuses on the problem of uncovered water. As is well known from other *Mishnayot*, *mayim megulim* — uncovered liquids — are forbidden out of concern that a snake may have drunk from them and introduced venom.

The *Gemara* asks: why not pass the uncovered water through a *mesannenet* — a strainer or filter — to remove the poison? Perhaps our *Mishna* does not follow the opinion of Rabbi Nechemia, since there is a *tannaitic* debate on this point. A *Baraita* teaches that if a strainer is placed over water, the water is still considered to have the status of *gilui* — exposure — and may not be used. Rabbi Nechemia qualifies this: when is the water still considered exposed? Only when the lower vessel — the one actually containing the liquid — is itself uncovered, so that a snake could access it from below. However, if the lower vessel is covered, even if the upper vessel is uncovered, there is no problem. The reasoning is that *eires nachash* — snake venom — *domeh lisvog, tzaf ve'omeid bimkomo* — is like a sponge, floating at the surface, and will be filtered out as the liquid passes through.

### Filter & Sanctity

The *Gemara* answers that even according to Rabbi Nechemia, such water cannot be used for the *nisuch hamayim*. Rabbi Nechemia only permits filtered water for a *hedyot* — an ordinary person. For *gavoah* — for God — would he ever sanction such a thing? The *Gemara* invokes the *pasuk*: *hakrivehu na lefachatecha hayirtzecha o hayissa panecha* — would you bring such a thing before your governor and expect it to find favor? Even if technically the water is safe to drink after filtration, the fact that it had to undergo such a process makes it categorically unacceptable as an offering on the *mizbei'ach*.

There is a deeper point here worth noting. What makes water that has been filtered through a *mesannenet* unacceptable is not necessarily any residual fear of poison. Rather, it is the inherent degraded character of the water itself — that it was in such a state in the first place. Even if one were fully confident the filter worked perfectly, there is something unsavory about using water that passed through such a process, something analogous to drinking water one knows was reclaimed from waste, however thoroughly treated. The concern for the *Mikdash* is not merely technical safety but the dignity of what is brought before God.

## Thematic Integration

### Malachi Theme

Before closing the fourth *perek*, it is worth pausing to note a striking structural connection. The third *perek* opened with the *lulav* framed in the language of a *korban*: the *Gemara* there derived the invalidity of a stolen *lulav* from the concept of *mitzvah haba'ah ba'aveirah*, and it did so through a *pasuk*: *ve'heveitem gazul v'et hapiseach v'et hacholeh* — is this the type of thing you want to bring before God? Something stolen, something lame, something sick? Now, the fourth *perek* closes with the same question, invoked against using filtered water for the *nisuch hamayim*: *hakrivehu na lefachatecha hayirtzecha o hayissa panecha*. Not only are these two *pesukim* making the same theological point, they are both drawn from the very same *parasha* — **Malachi, Chapter 1**.

### Opening & Closing

This is not coincidental. The entire first chapter of Malachi is a *parasha* of God's grievance against the people for the degradation of His service. The opening of the relevant passage reads:

> *V'im adonim ani aye morai amar Hashem tzevaot lachem hakohanim bozei shemi v'amartem bameh bazinu et shemecha. Magishim al mizbechi lechem m'go'al v'amartem bameh ge'al nucha. B'omrachem shulchan Hashem nivzeh hu. V'chi tagishun iver lizboach ein ra v'chi tagishu piseach v'choleh ein ra. Hakrivehu na l'pachatecha hayirtzecha o hayissa panecha amar Hashem tzevaot.*

You bring the blind, the lame, the sick, and you say there is nothing wrong with it. Would you bring that to your governor? And then the *parasha* continues:

> *Mimizrach shemesh v'ad mevo'o gadol shemi bagoyim uv'chol makom muktar mugash lishmi mincha tehorah ki gadol shemi bagoyim amar Hashem tzevaot.*

My name is great among the nations — yet look at how you treat Me. And then:

> *V'havetem gazul v'et hapiseach v'et hacholeh v'havetem et hamincha ha'ertzeh otah miyedchem amar Hashem.*

This context carries a tone of *klalah* — prophetic rebuke — which somewhat complicates the celebratory associations one might initially expect. Yet if *Sukkot* is, at its core, about drawing close to God and continuing the spiritual work initiated on *Yom Kippur*, and if the *lulav*, the *aravah*, the *nisuch hamayim*, and all the *Mikdash* rituals of these *perakim* represent the ideal of offering our very best to God — then the way the third and fourth *perek* are bookended by two *pesukim* from this same *parasha* in Malachi is remarkably powerful. The theme of *Sukkot* — agricultural success, gratitude, joy before God — is inseparable from the question of what is truly worthy to be brought before Him.

## Simchat Beit HaShoeva

### Fifth Perek Intro

We now open the fifth *perek* of Maseches Sukkah, which concerns the *Simchat Beit HaShoevah* — the all-night celebration that began the evening before the drawing of the water and continued through the night, building toward the morning *nisuch hamayim*.

### Chalil Schedule

The *Mishna* states: *hechalil chamishah v'shishah* — the *chalil* (a flute or recorder-like instrument) was played five or six days. This refers to the musical instruments played for the *Simchat Beit HaShoevah*. The *Mishna* continues: *she'einah dochah lo et haShabbat v'lo et Yom Tov* — this music did not override *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov*. Since the *nisuch hamayim* was performed each of the seven days of *Sukkot*, and the *Simchat Beit HaShoevah* celebration began the night before each drawing, one would expect seven nights of celebration. However, since *Yom Tov* itself fell on the first night and the music could not be played then, that eliminated at least one night. If *Shabbat* also fell during *Sukkot*, it too would be excluded — both the musical celebration the night before and the water-drawing itself, since carrying from outside the *Mikdash* on *Shabbat* was not done. Hence the formula: five or six days.

### Textual Variants

The *Gemara* at the top of 51b records that there were two variant readings of the name of the celebration. One *tanna* read *Shoeva* — the drawing — which is the familiar and intuitive reading, evoking *u'shavtem mayim b'sasson*, the drawing of water with joy. The other *tanna* read *Chashuva* — the important — yielding *Simchat Beit HaChashuva*. This variant is striking evidence of an oral culture: in written form such a confusion would be nearly impossible, but in oral transmission the similarity of *Shoeva* and *Chashuva* could plausibly yield two traditions.

Mar Zutra rules that neither version is corrupt. The reading of *Shoeva* is not corrupt, as it is grounded in the *pasuk u'shavtem mayim b'sasson* — the drawing of water with joy. The reading of *Chashuva* is also not corrupt, as Rav Nachman explains: *mitzvah chashuva hi u'va'ah misheshet yimei bereishit* — this is a *mitzvah* of great importance and it derives from the six days of Creation.

### Divine Spirit Symbolism

*Tosafot* cites the *Yerushalmi* in connection with the reading of *Shoeva*: *shemisham shoavim Ruach HaKodesh, she'haShechina shorah mitoch Simcha* — from the *Simchat Beit HaShoevah* they drew the divine spirit, for the *Shechina* rests only amidst joy. The *Yerushalmi* derives this from the verse *vayehi kenagen hamenagen vatehi alav ruach Hashem* — and it was when the musician played that the spirit of God rested upon him — and from the story of Yona.

This symbolic reading is particularly valuable because the literal significance of water — as the essential ingredient of agricultural success — can be difficult for those outside *Eretz Yisrael* to feel viscerally. But the deeper symbolism connects powerfully to the spiritual arc of the season. Beginning from *Rosh Hashanah*, through *Yom Kippur*, into the *Sukkah* with its *ananei kavod*, the theme throughout has been the drawing of God's presence into our lives. The *Simchat Beit HaShoevah*, understood this way, is not merely joy in anticipation of rain — it is the joy of closeness to God, and that joy itself enables the *Shechina* to rest. The drawing of water becomes the drawing of divine spirit, and the *Simcha* is not only the response to blessing received but the very vehicle through which further blessing flows.

### Creation Motif

The connection to *sheishet yimei bereishit* deserves elaboration. *Rashi* explains that Rav Nachman refers to what was discussed the previous *daf* — that the underground caverns of the *Mikdash* (*shittin*) were created from the six days of Creation in order to receive the libations. This is somewhat curious, since those caverns primarily received the wine libations offered year-round, and the water libation was performed only during the one week of *Sukkot*. One might also note that the *Simchat Beit HaShoevah* would be held at most six nights — corresponding to *sheishet yimei bereishit*.

A broader reading presents itself as well. Way back at the beginning of the *masechet*, one of the derivations connecting the *s'chach* of the *Sukkah* to *ananei kavod* drew on the verse *v'eid ya'aleh min ha'aretz v'hishka et kol pnei ha'adamah* — the original mist that rose from the earth to water all of the ground in the story of *beriah*. The *Sukkah* and its associations with God's presence evoke not only the *midbar* experience but the primordial creative moment when God first brought water to the world. Water runs through creation from its very opening — *tohu vavohu*, earth and water, the mists of the beginning. The *Simchat Beit HaShoevah*, with its water motif, its joy at the renewal of the natural cycle, and its resonance with *beri'at ha'olam*, pulls all of this together: the beginning of a new year, the drawing of God's presence, and the celebration of the world as God created it.

## Musical Instruments

### Chalil on Shabbat

The *Gemara* now cites a *tannaitic* dispute: *hachalil docheh et haShabbat* — the *chalil* overrides *Shabbat* — *divrei Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda*. The *Chachamim* go further and say: *af Yom Tov eino docheh* — not only does it not override *Shabbat*, it does not override *Yom Tov* either. This appears to be the ruling of our *Mishna*.

### Rav Yosef Distinction

Rav Yosef clarifies what this dispute is actually about. It would be a mistake to think that anyone is suggesting the musical instruments of the *Simchat Beit HaShoevah* celebration — the all-night party — could override *Shabbat*. That was never on the table; it is simply *Simcha*, joy, and not an *avodah*, and no one disputes that it cannot override *Shabbat*. The debate, Rav Yosef says, concerns the *shir shel korban* — the song of the Levites that accompanied the wine libations of the communal obligatory sacrifices, namely the *tamid shel shachar*, the *tamid shel bein ha'arba'ayim*, and the *mussafin*. With every such sacrifice, at the moment of the pouring of the *nesachim*, the Levites would sing — this is the principle of *ein shira ela al ha'yayin*, that song is performed over wine, which is also the basis for performing *kiddush* and *havdalah* over wine. That song was accompanied by musical instruments. The question is whether those instruments, played at the time of the *korban*, constitute an *avodah* that overrides *Shabbat*.

### Ikar Shira Debate

Rav Yosef formulates the underlying issue: *ikar shira b'kli* — is the essence of the *shira* the musical instruments — or *ikar shira b'peh* — is the essence the vocal song, with instruments serving merely as accompaniment?

If *ikar shira b'kli*, then the musical instrument is the central *avodah* of the Levites, the *avodah* being the instrumental performance itself, and since it is an *avodah* it would override *Shabbat*. This is the position of Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda.

If *ikar shira b'peh*, then the primary *avodah* is the vocal song, and the instruments are ancillary. They are not the essential *avodah*, and therefore they do not override *Shabbat*. This is the position of the *Rabbanan*.

An important *halachic* note attaches here. The *Rambam* rules *ikar shira b'peh*, and this has a practical consequence: since the instrumental music is not the core *avodah*, it need not be performed exclusively by Levites. Even *Yisraelim* could participate in the instrumental music. By contrast, the *chatzotzrot* — the silver trumpets — which are explicitly commanded in the *Torah* (*u'v'yom simchatchem u'v'moadichem u'v'roshei chodsheichem u'tkatem bachatzotzrot*), are a full *Torah*-ordained *avodah* and are performed exclusively by *Kohanim*. The Levites' *avodah* in the *Mikdash*, after the era of the *Mishkan* (when they carried the *Mishkan*), consisted of two things: guarding the gates of the *Mikdash* and performing the *shir*. It is the nature of that *shir* — whether instrumental or vocal — that is in dispute.

*Tosafot* raises a difficulty here. The playing of musical instruments on *Shabbat* is ordinarily only a *rabbinic* prohibition — the concern being, for example, that one might come to fix the instrument. And we have the general principle of *ein shvut b'Mikdash* — rabbinic *Shabbat* concerns are set aside in the *Mikdash* for the sake of the *avodah*. If so, why would this be a serious debate at all? Even if the instruments are not strictly *ikar*, and even if playing them is only rabbinically prohibited, surely *ein shvut b'Mikdash* would resolve the question. *Tosafot* notes that other *Gemaras* seem to treat the playing of instruments in the *Mikdash* on *Shabbat* as a non-issue for precisely this reason, yet our *Gemara* treats it as a genuine *machloket*. This tension remains noteworthy.

### Wooden Vessels Link

Rav Yosef now explains the source for his interpretation of the *machloket* — namely, that it concerns the *shir shel korban* and the question of *ikar shira b'kli* or *b'peh*. He cites a *Baraita*: if one fashioned *klei shares* — service vessels of the *Mikdash* — from wood, Rabbi invalidates them and Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda declares them valid.

The connection Rav Yosef draws is as follows. The *chalil* used in the *Mikdash* — like all flutes — was made of wood or reed. It could not be coated with metal; other *Gemaras* state explicitly that the *Mikdash* flute was wooden. If the instrumental music is *ikar shira b'kli*, then those wooden instruments are *klei shares* — holy service vessels — and they demonstrate that wooden *klei shares* are valid. This is Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda's position: *man de'machshir savar ikar shira b'kli*, the one who validates wooden vessels holds that the instrumental music is the primary *avodah*, and the wooden musical instruments are themselves *klei shares*, thereby establishing that wooden *klei shares* are acceptable. *V'yalfinan me'avuva de'Moshe* — and we derive this from the flute that dates to the days of Moshe. *Rashi* explains: there was a flute in the *Mikdash* from the days of Moshe, a reed instrument, and they even sought at one point to plate it in gold — the very antiquity and sanctity of this wooden instrument grounds the principle.

Conversely, *man de'pasel savar ikar shira b'peh* — the one who invalidates wooden vessels holds that the *ikar avodah* of the Levites is the vocal song, not the instrumental music. Therefore the wooden instruments were never *klei shares* to begin with, and they provide no precedent. *Delo yalfinan me'avuva de'Moshe* — we do not derive from Moshe's flute.

### Impossible Cases Rule

The *Gemara* now offers a counterproposal: perhaps the two positions in the *machloket* need not map onto the question of *ikar shira b'kli* versus *b'peh* at all. Perhaps *everyone* agrees that *ikar shira b'kli* — the musical instruments are primary — and therefore everyone agrees that the wooden instruments of the Levites were *klei shares*. So what is the debate about wooden *klei shares*?

*D'hacha b'danin efshar mish'ee efshar kamiflagi* — here the dispute concerns the hermeneutic principle of whether one may learn from a case of *ee efshar* (no available alternative) to a case of *efshar* (where alternatives exist). The wooden musical instruments were made of wood *out of necessity* — the sonic properties required for the service could not be achieved with metal. This was a case of *ee efshar*: there was no alternative. One might argue that this necessity justified wooden *klei shares* in that specific context, but that this does not establish a general principle permitting wooden *klei shares* where metal could have been used instead.

*Man de'machshir* holds *danin efshar mish'ee efshar* — one may draw a legal principle from a case of no alternative and apply it to cases where alternatives exist. The wooden instruments teach that wooden *klei shares* are valid as a general rule. *Man de'pasel* holds *lo danin efshar mish'ee efshar* — a case that arose from necessity cannot set a precedent for cases where necessity is absent.

The *Gemara* then offers a further alternative: *v'ee ba'it eima d'kulei alma d'ikar shira b'peh v'ein danin efshar mish'ee efshar* — everyone could hold both that *ikar shira b'peh* and that one cannot derive from *ee efshar* to *efshar*. In this reading, the wooden musical instruments are not *klei shares* and the impossibility argument is irrelevant — yet there is still a debate about whether wooden *klei shares* can be derived. The source of *that* debate, the *Gemara* now says, is an entirely different question: how to apply hermeneutic principles to derive from the *Menorah* what materials are permissible for *klei shares*.

## Hermeneutics & Menorah

### Hermeneutic Methods

The *Gemara* now introduces the underlying dispute about hermeneutics as applied to the *Menorah*. The *Menorah* functions as the paradigm case for the permissible materials of *klei shares*. The question is: given the *Torah*'s description of the *Menorah*, what can be derived about other *klei shares*? And the answer depends entirely on which hermeneutic system one employs — *klal u'prat* or *ribuy u'miyut*.

### Klal U'Prat Structure

The *klal u'prat* system works as follows. When the *Torah* presents a general statement (*klal*) followed by a specific example (*prat*), the principle of *ein baklal ella mah shebaprat* applies: you limit the general to precisely what the specific contains. An illuminating example from *Parshat Ma'aser Sheni*: the *Torah* says *v'natata hakesef b'chol asher te'aveh nafshecha* — you may spend the money on whatever you desire — and then enumerates *bakar u'tzon v'yayin v'shachar* — cattle, sheep, wine, and strong drink — followed by *u'v'chol asher tishalecha nafshecha* — and whatever else you desire. At the *klal-prat* stage (general, then specific), the rule is: I said "anything," but what I really mean is the specific examples that follow. The opening "anything" does not mean literally anything; it is delimited by the *prat*.

When a second *klal* follows, yielding the structure of *klal u'prat u'klal*, the rule shifts: you may include anything *similar* to the *prat*. The first *klal* says "anything," the *prat* gives examples, and the second *klal* says "really anything" — but the combination means: anything that resembles the specific examples. You can serve hamburgers or chicken; you cannot serve tofu salad, which is completely different in kind. The result is *ke'ein haprat* — like the *prat*, similar in character to what was specified.

### Ribuy U'Miyut Approach

*Ribuy u'miyut* operates by a different logic. Here the terms are not *klal* (general/specifying) and *prat* (specific), but *ribuy* (inclusive/expanding) and *miyut* (limiting). The crucial difference is that whereas *prat* is specific, *miyut* is merely limiting without being fully specific. This means that at the *ribuy-miyut* stage (the equivalent of the two-step *klal-prat*), one already arrives at the conclusion that the *ribuy u'miyut* system only reaches at the three-step level. That is, a single *miyut* already implies "anything like the example given," not "only exactly the example." When a second *ribuy* follows (*ribuy u'miyut u'ribuy*), the result is all-inclusive except for something completely different in kind. Where *klal u'prat u'klal* produced "anything similar to the *prat*," *ribuy u'miyut u'ribuy* produces "anything except what is entirely unlike."

### Menorah Application

How does this play out with the *Menorah*? The relevant verses present: *ve'asita menorat* (and you shall make a *menorah*) — *klal* / *ribuy* — followed by *zahav tahor* (pure gold) — *prat* / *miyut* — followed by *mikshah tei'aseh hamenorah* (it shall be hammered work, the *menorah* shall be made) — which reopens the general.

Rabbi uses *klal u'prat*. *Ve'asita menorat* is *klal*, *zahav tahor* is *prat*, *mikshah tei'aseh hamenorah* is a second *klal* — giving *klal u'prat u'klal*. The rule: anything similar to the *prat*. The *prat* is gold — *mah haprat meforash shel matechet, af kol shel matechet* — since gold is a metal, anything similar must also be a metal. The *Menorah* could be made of any metal, and by extension, *klei shares* must be metallic. Wood is excluded.

Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda uses *ribuy u'miyut*. *Ve'asita menorat* is *ribuy*, *zahav tahor* is *miyut*, *mikshah tei'aseh hamenorah* is a second *ribuy* — giving *ribuy u'miyut u'ribuy*. The rule: anything except what is completely unlike. The *miyut* of gold means gold is the example, but the *ribuy u'miyut* structure already implies "anything like gold" at stage one — meaning metal in general. The final *ribuy* then expands further to include anything at all, with the only exclusion being something entirely different in kind. *Mai mi'et? Mi'et shel cheres* — the only thing excluded is pottery. Wood, however, is not so completely unlike metal as to be excluded, and therefore wooden *klei shares* are valid.

This hermeneutic dispute, then, is entirely independent of the question of *ikar shira b'kli* or *b'peh*, and independent of the question of *danin efshar mish'ee efshar*. It provides a third possible basis on which the debate about wooden *klei shares* could rest — one grounded purely in the methodology of *Torah* interpretation.
