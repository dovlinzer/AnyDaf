# Zevachim 72 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bitul & Forbidden Animals

### Daf Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִיבְטְלוּ בְּרוּבָּא! וְכִי תֵּימָא חֲשִׁיבִי וְלָא בָּטְלִי – הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: "כׇּל שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לִימָּנוֹת" שָׁנִינוּ;
>
> **Translation:** **But let** the prohibited animals **be nullified in a majority,** as is the *halakha* concerning other matters, in which the minority items assume the status of the majority. **And if you would say** in response that animals **are significant,** as they are counted individually **and** therefore **they are not nullified** in a majority, this answer is unsatisfactory. The Gemara elaborates: **This** suggested answer **works out well according to the one who says** that **we learned** in the mishna discussing nullification in a majority (see *Orla* 3:6–7): **Any** item **whose manner is** also **to be counted,** i.e., that are sometimes sold by unit rather than weight or volume, is considered significant. This definition includes animals, as they are sometimes sold as individual animals, and therefore they would be considered significant.

The daf before us belongs to the eighth *perek* of Zevachim, a chapter that introduced the following principle: if one animal that is forbidden in *hana'ah* — such as a *shor haniskal*, an ox condemned to stoning, or a *chatat hameisos*, a sin-offering that must be left to die — becomes mixed with a group of sacrificial animals, none of those animals may be brought as offerings, and none may be used for any benefit. The forbidden animal is not *batel*, and therefore every animal in the mixture must be assumed to be the forbidden one; they are all left to die. Similarly, if an animal was disqualified as a sacrifice but not forbidden in *hana'ah* — for instance, a *treifah* or an animal born by Caesarean section — the animals need not be left to die, but none may be brought as an offering. The Mishnah describes how one waits for the animals to develop a blemish and then redeems their value.

The animating question of our daf, however, is more fundamental: the Mishnah states that the forbidden animal is not *batel* even if it falls into a mixture of ten thousand. Whatever happened to the laws of *bitul*?

### Why No Bitul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: "אֶת שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לִימָּנוֹת" שָׁנִינוּ, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? דִּתְנַן: מִי שֶׁהָיוּ לוֹ חֲבִילֵי תִּילְתָּן שֶׁל כִּלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם –
>
> **Translation:** **But according to the one who says** that **we learned** in that mishna: An item **whose manner is** exclusively **to be counted,** i.e., one that is always sold by unit, is considered significant, **what can be said?** Although animals are often sold by unit, they are occasionally sold as part of a herd, and would therefore not be considered significant. The Gemara cites the mishna in which this dispute appears. **As we learned** (*Orla* 3:6–7): With regard to **one who had bundles of fenugreek,** a type of legume, that were **diverse kinds** planted **in a vineyard,** from which it is prohibited to derive benefit,

The Gemara poses the question directly: *venivtelu berova* — let the forbidden animal simply be nullified by the majority. The opening move of the sugya is to clarify what the phrase *berova* means in this context. Some *rishonim* note that when the Gemara asks "let it be *batel berov*," it does not necessarily mean precisely a majority; rather, it means let it be nullified by whatever standard number one might choose. The Gemara's immediate response is *vechi teima chashiv velo batil* — perhaps one would say that an animal, being a substantial and significant object, simply cannot be nullified. But this answer immediately runs into a difficulty, and the Gemara draws on a debate in the Mishnah about how to conceptualize which objects are significant enough to resist nullification.

### Kol vs. Et Shedarko

The Gemara introduces the distinction between two formulations: *kol shedarko limanot* and *et shedarko limanot*. The concept of significance that prevents *bitul* is framed in terms of whether an item is "normally counted." But the precise language matters enormously. *Kol shedarko limanot* — literally, "anything whose way it is to be counted" — encompasses things that are naturally sold or traded by the unit, even if they are sometimes sold by weight or volume as well. *Et shedarko limanot* — "that which [only] its way is to be counted" — is a narrower formulation, encompassing only those items that are exclusively sold by count and never by any other measure. The Gemara observes that the answer of *chashiv* — significance — works well according to one of these readings, but runs into difficulty according to the other, and the nature of that difficulty drives the entire subsequent discussion.

## Identity & Discreteness

### Watermelons vs. Cars

The difference between *kol shedarko limanot* and *et shedarko limanot* is best understood through practical examples. Watermelons or bananas are sometimes sold individually — four for a dollar on the street — and sometimes sold by the pound in a store. They are things that it is natural to count, and you would speak of having a hundred watermelons rather than a thousand pounds of watermelons, but they are not exclusively sold by count. That is *kol shedarko limanot*: things for which counting by unit is the natural mode, though not the only mode. Cars, on the other hand, are never sold by weight or volume. No one buys three tons of cars. Each car is always treated as a discrete unit. That is *et shedarko limanot*: things that are only and exclusively counted.

It should be noted that this distinction is not simply a function of cost. Rashi observes that eggs, for example, would not have qualified in earlier times as *et shedarko limanot* because one could buy a basket of eggs without specifying an exact count, or a farmer might add an extra egg as a bonus — so eggs were not sold with the strict individuation characteristic of *et shedarko limanot*.

### Logic of Bitul

Understanding the mechanism of *bitul* is essential for appreciating why this distinction matters. The consensus among most *rishonim* is that *bitul* of *yavesh beyavesh* — dry matter mixed with dry matter — works on principles analogous to *lach belach*, wet mixture. When a drop of blood falls into a pot of chicken soup, we say it loses its identity: there is no longer any distinct, concrete presence of blood — it fully disperses, and trace amounts are as if they do not exist. That is the logic of *bitul*.

Now consider a discrete object. If one has a problematic item mixed into a large collection of similar items — say, one tomato affected by *kilayim* among a hundred tomatoes — the argument is that because we cannot identify which tomato is problematic, the problematic one is, as it were, overwhelmed by the mass of the others and ceases to have an independent existence. We look at the collection as an undifferentiated whole, and within that whole the single problematic item is submerged and stops existing. This is the conceptual logic of *bitul*: a thing loses its identity in the mass.

But this logic depends on our actually looking at the collection as an undifferentiated whole, with no item possessing a distinct, individual identity. Once an item has genuine individual significance — once we always look at it as a discrete, independently weighty unit — the logic of *bitul* collapses. We cannot say this item has been submerged and lost its identity if we always regard it as a separate and independent entity. The question is therefore: what tells us that we regard each individual item as independently significant? The answer the Gemara proposes is the commercial mode of transaction. If items are sold by count — if you would say "I have a hundred such items" rather than "I have a hundred pounds of such material" — it means that in practice, each unit is always regarded separately and independently. And if each unit retains its distinct identity, it cannot be lost in the mass.

### People & Identity

There is a striking broader principle here that runs beneath the surface of this discussion. One never counts money and then counts people in the same way. Money can be summed: five dollars plus five dollars is ten dollars, and the individual coins or bills lose their separate identity in the aggregate. But a person cannot be summed in this way. Michael is always Michael; David is always David. One cannot amass them into a collective quantity where each individual ceases to have a distinct existence. No individual is interchangeable with another, and therefore the logic that works for amorphous collections — that the problematic element is swallowed by the larger mass — cannot work for persons.

This is precisely the principle underlying *et shedarko limanot* and *kol shedarko limanot*: when items always retain their distinct identity, when we cannot look at them as an undifferentiated mass without singling out each one individually, *bitul* does not apply. The threshold for that determination is whether these items are sold by count.

## Mishnah: Kilayim

### Fenugreek Bundles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יִדָּלְקוּ. נִתְעָרְבוּ בַּאֲחֵרִים (וַאֲחֵרִים בַּאֲחֵרִים) – כּוּלָּן יִדָּלְקוּ. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יַעֲלוּ בְּאֶחָד וּמָאתַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** those bundles **must be burned.** If the bundles **were intermingled with others, and** those **others** were intermingled **with others, they all must be burned.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: They can be nullified when** the total is **201** items, i.e., one prohibited item intermingled with two hundred permitted ones.

The Gemara grounds this entire debate in a Mishnah from the laws of *kilayim*. The case: someone had bundles of fenugreek that were *kilayim* — grown in a vineyard in violation of the prohibition of *kil'ei hakerem* — and they must therefore be burned. If such a bundle becomes mixed with other, permitted bundles, the Mishnah discusses what happens. (There is also a textual variant involving the formula *b'acher b'acherim* — where one bundle gets mixed into a hundred, and then one of that hundred gets mixed into another hundred — raising the question of whether *sfek sfeika*, double doubt, applies here or whether that principle is limited to *avoda zara*. That is itself one of the major discussions among the *Tosafot* on this daf.)

### Rabbi Meir's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לִמָּנוֹת – מְקַדֵּשׁ. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ מְקַדֵּשׁ אֶלָּא שִׁשָּׁה דְּבָרִים בִּלְבַד. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: שִׁבְעָה, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: אֱגוֹזֵי פֶרֶךְ, וְרִימּוֹנֵי בָאדָן, וְחָבִיּוֹת סְתוּמוֹת, וְחִילְפֵי תְרָדִין, וְקִילְחֵי כְרוּב, וְדַלַּעַת יְוָנִית. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מוֹסִיף: אַף כִּכָּרוֹת שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: Rabbi Meir holds that they all must be burned, **as Rabbi Meir would say: Any** item **whose manner is to be counted renders** its mixture **prohibited,** as it is considered significant and cannot be nullified. **And the Rabbis say: Only six items** are sufficiently significant to **render** their mixture **prohibited. Rabbi Akiva says:** There are **seven. And they are: Nuts** with **brittle** shells, **and pomegranates from Badan, and sealed barrels** of wine, **and beet greens, and cabbage stalks, and Greek gourd. Rabbi Akiva adds: Loaves of a homeowner** are **also** in this category.

Rabbi Meir rules that even one forbidden bundle mixed into ten thousand forbids them all: *mekadesh* — it sanctifies everything, rendering it all forbidden. His reasoning: *kol shedarko limanot* (or, in the marginal variant, *et shedarko limanot*). Since fenugreek bundles are naturally sold by count, each bundle retains its individual significance and therefore cannot become *batel*, no matter how large the surrounding majority. Rabbi Meir thus holds that there is a general principle: any item that falls into the category of "normally counted" cannot be nullified.

### Sages & Rabbi Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָרָאוּי לְעׇרְלָה – עׇרְלָה, הָרָאוּי לְכִלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם – כִּלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: Different prohibitions apply to these items. **That which is fit** to be forbidden due **to the prohibition against eating the fruit of a tree during the first three years after its planting [*orla*],** i.e., nuts, pomegranates, and sealed barrels of wine, prohibit their mixture as ***orla*. That which is fit** to be forbidden due **to diverse kinds** planted **in a vineyard,** i.e., beets, cabbage, and gourd, prohibit their mixture as **diverse kinds in a vineyard.**

The Sages reject Rabbi Meir's broad principle entirely. They rule that fenugreek bundles are *batel* in 200 — the standard ratio for *kilayim*, which is double the 100 required for *terumah* (the elevated ratio reflecting the greater severity of *kilayim*, which is forbidden in *hana'ah*). According to the Sages, the principle of "normally counted" does not create a categorical exemption from *bitul*. Only six specific items are so significant that they resist *bitul* in any quantity. Rabbi Akiva adds a seventh: freshly baked home-baked loaves. The six (or seven) items enumerated are: the nuts of *Perach*; pomegranates of *Bedan* (*Rimonei Bedan*); sealed barrels of wine; bunches of beets; heads of cabbage; a Greek gourd; and, according to Rabbi Akiva, home-baked loaves. Some of these, being fruit, would fall under *orla*; others fall under *kilayim*.

The Sages and Rabbi Akiva thus have a fundamentally different structure: not a general principle governing any normally-counted item, but a closed (or nearly closed) list of items whose exceptional significance is recognized individually.

### Tosafot's Question

This is where Tosafot raises a pointed and arresting question. Tosafot observes that the entire debate between Rabbi Meir, the Sages, and Rabbi Akiva seems irrelevant to explaining why a head of cattle cannot be *batel*. Even if one accepts the narrower position of the Sages — that *bitul* fails only for the six or seven specified items — a head of cattle is surely at least as significant as a pomegranate from *Bedan*. Why should cattle's status depend on whether the operative principle is *kol shedarko limanot* or *et shedarko limanot*? If you have a box full of *Bedan* pomegranates alongside a herd of cattle, one would intuitively look at each individual cow as more independently weighty than each individual pomegranate. Tosafot does not arrive at a fully satisfying resolution, but the question itself is the natural and obvious one to ask.

## Yochanan vs. Lakish

### Rabbi Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: "אֶת שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לִמָּנוֹת" שָׁנִינוּ, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אוֹמֵר: "כׇּל שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לִמָּנוֹת" שָׁנִינוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And it was stated** that there is a dispute between *amora'im* **with regard to** the wording of Rabbi Meir's opinion in this mishna. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that **we learned:** Only an item **whose manner is** exclusively **to be counted** is significant and cannot be nullified, and it therefore renders its mixture prohibited according to the opinion of Rabbi Meir. **And Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says** that **we learned: Any** item **whose manner is** also **to be counted** is significant and cannot be nullified.

The Gemara reports an *amoraic* dispute about the correct reading of Rabbi Meir's language in the Mishnah. Rabbi Yochanan says: the correct version in the Mishnah is *et darko limnot* — things that are *only* counted, the more restrictive formulation. That is what Rabbi Meir means when he says an item is not *batel*: it must be something exclusively sold by count.

### Reish Lakish

Reish Lakish disagrees: the correct version is *kol darko limnot* — things that are *generally* counted, the more expansive formulation. Even something that is *sometimes* sold by count, though not exclusively so, falls within Rabbi Meir's principle and resists *bitul*.

### Problem for Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַאי תַּנָּא – תַּנָּא דְּלִיטְרָא קְצִיעוֹת הוּא, דְּאָמַר:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara reiterates its question: **This works out well according to** the opinion of **Reish Lakish, but according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, what can be said?** According to his opinion, since animals are not sold exclusively by unit, they are not sufficiently significant. Therefore, a prohibited animal should be nullified in a simple majority. **Rav Pappa says:** According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, **this *tanna*,** who says that a prohibited animal cannot be nullified, **is** the *tanna* **of** the *halakha* concerning **a *litra* of dried figs, who says:**

The Gemara immediately identifies the difficulty: this works out well according to Reish Lakish. Cattle are sometimes sold by the head — "how many head of cattle do you have?" — and that is enough to place them within the *kol shedarko limanot* category, explaining why they are not *batel*. But according to Rabbi Yochanan, who reads Rabbi Meir as requiring *et shedarko limanot* — exclusively counted — cattle would not qualify, since cattle are sometimes sold as part of a herd without individual counting. What can Rabbi Yochanan say?

As Tosafot notes, this is itself strange: intuitively, even according to Rabbi Yochanan, a head of cattle seems weighty enough to resist *bitul* on its own terms. But the Gemara apparently does not pursue that line of reasoning, and instead turns to a different answer.

Tosafot raises yet another question at this juncture: there is an independent principle that a *beriah* — a whole, discrete unit — is not *batel*. Should a cow not qualify as a *beriah*? Tosafot develops a precise definition of *beriah*: what makes something a *beriah* is not merely that it is large or intact, but that *it* is the specific thing the Torah has identified as forbidden. When the Torah says not to eat the *gid hanasheh*, the sciatic nerve is the *beriah* — it is the exact object the Torah named as the prohibition, and therefore even less than a *kezayit* incurs lashes and it is not *batel*. Similarly, any flying insect is a *beriah* because the Torah identified that specific creature as the forbidden thing.

A *shor haniskal*, however, is different. The Torah does not say "do not eat the ox"; it says "do not eat from the *meat* of" the condemned ox. The cow is not the unit the Torah has identified as forbidden — it is the meat that is forbidden, and the meat is simply an aggregate of material. Because the Torah never designated the cow itself as the forbidden unit, the cow does not achieve the status of *beriah*, regardless of its size, vitality, or individual importance. Therefore the *beriah* principle cannot rescue the ruling that the cow is not *batel*.

## Rav Papa & Pressed Figs

### Pressed Figs Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ מִנְיָן – אֲפִילּוּ בִּדְרַבָּנַן לָא בָּטֵיל, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן בִּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Any item that is counted, even** if it is prohibited **by rabbinic law,** e.g., *teruma* of fruit, **cannot be nullified, and all the more so** items prohibited **by Torah law,** such as animals that are disqualified for the altar, as in the mishna.

Rav Papa offers a resolution. He says: the *tana* of our Mishnah — the one who rules that a cow is not *batel* even in ten thousand — is the *tana* of the case of *litra ketziot*, pressed figs. That *tana* holds: *kol davar she'yesh bo minyan afilu be-rabbanan lo batil* — anything that is sometimes sold by count is not *batel*, even when the prohibition involved is only rabbinic. This is the position of *kol shedarko limanot* — not restricted to items that are *exclusively* counted, but encompassing anything for which counting by unit is a natural mode of transaction. And since cattle are sometimes sold by the head, they fall within this principle.

Rav Papa's move is this: whatever precisely Rabbi Meir meant, there exists a *tana* who takes the broader position that *any* item which is *sometimes* counted resists *bitul* — and that *tana* applies the rule even to rabbinic prohibitions. *Kal vachomer*, all the more so does it apply to something biblically forbidden. Our Mishnah reflects the opinion of this *tana*, and on that basis the ruling that the cow is not *batel* is coherent.

### Eliezer vs. Yehoshua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: לִיטְרָא קְצִיעוֹת שֶׁדְּרָסָהּ עַל פִּי עִיגּוּל, וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ בְּאֵיזוֹ עִיגּוּל דְּרָסָהּ; עַל פִּי חָבִית, וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ בְּאֵיזוֹ חָבִית דְּרָסָהּ; עַל פִּי כַּוֶּורֶת, וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ בְּאֵיזוֹ כַּוֶּורֶת דְּרָסָהּ –
>
> **Translation:** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Terumot* 5:11): The *baraita* discusses three cases, all of which relate to the tithing of figs, which is an obligation by rabbinic law. The first is the case of **a *litra* of** untithed **dried figs** that were pressed in different vessels and shaped into circles, **that** one placed into a barrel containing tithed figs, and during the process of producing a circle he **pressed** the figs **onto the opening of** one of the **circular** vessels in which the circles are formed, **and he does not know into which circular** vessel **he pressed it.** The second is the case in which he recalls that he pressed it **on the opening of a barrel** containing tithed figs, **but he does not know into which barrel he pressed it.** The third case is that he recalls that he pressed it **on the opening of** a straw **receptacle** containing tithed figs, **but he does not know into which receptacle he pressed it.** In all of these cases, there is a dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda as to the details of a dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua.

The Gemara now identifies who this *tana* is by citing a *beraita* about pressed figs. The case: someone had a *litra* (a measure by weight or volume) of pressed figs, and stomped them into one of a series of barrel-tops — the method by which rounds of pressed figs, much like rounds of cheese, were produced. One would pack figs into a barrel, press them down so they formed a consolidated round on top, and repeat the process with successive barrels, eventually producing cylinders of pressed rounds. The *terumah* figs were stomped into one of these barrel-tops, and the person does not know which barrel. There are a hundred barrels in total.

Everyone agrees that *terumah* is nullified in a ratio of 100 to 1. The question is: what counts as the denominator? Do we look at all of the pressed figs across all the barrels — in which case even a handful of large barrels with many figs might suffice — or do we count the barrel-tops themselves?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: רוֹאִין אֶת הָעֶלְיוֹנוֹת כְּאִילּוּ הֵן פְּרוּדוֹת, וְהַתַּחְתּוֹנוֹת מַעֲלוֹת אֶת הָעֶלְיוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir says** that **Rabbi Eliezer says: One views the upper** layers of possibly untithed dried figs **as though they are separate** pieces, rather than one unit. **And the lower** ones, which were there beforehand and have certainly been tithed, **nullify the upper** ones, as there are enough circles of figs to nullify the upper layer. One does not need to tithe the figs at the top of any of the containers.

Rabbi Meir reports the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua. Rabbi Eliezer takes the lenient position: one looks at the top layer as if it were detached and free-floating throughout the combined mass. Since the doubt is which barrel-top contains the *terumah*, and if we treat the *terumah* round as separated and mixed throughout all the figs, we need only 100 times the quantity of the *terumah* figs — which might be achieved within relatively few barrels. The key distinction is whether the forbidden item has a *fixed location* in the barrel-top or is free-floating: if free-floating, the entire combined mass of all the figs becomes the denominator.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: אִם יֵשׁ מֵאָה פּוּמִּין – יַעֲלוּ, וְאִם לָאו – הַפּוּמִּין אֲסוּרִין וְהַשּׁוּלַיִם מוּתָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Meir continues: By contrast, **Rabbi Yehoshua says: If there are one hundred openings** of containers present there, the untithed *litra* of figs on the opening of one of the containers **is nullified** in a ratio of one part of untithed figs to one hundred parts of similar, tithed figs. **But if not,** all of **the** layers of figs at the **openings** of the containers **are prohibited,** i.e., viewed as untithed, as one of them certainly contains an untithed *litra* that has not been nullified. **And the** figs on the **insides** of the vessels **are permitted,** as the untithed figs certainly did not reach there. This is Rabbi Meir's version of the dispute.

Rabbi Yehoshua, in Rabbi Meir's version, is more stringent: one focuses exclusively on the barrel-tops. Only if there are 100 other barrel-tops is the *terumah* top *batel*. If there are not 100 tops, the top of every barrel is treated as forbidden (or as a mixture that only a *kohen* may eat), while the contents below the top layer are permitted — since the doubt pertains only to the tops.

### Shedarko Limanot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אִם יֵשׁ שָׁם מֵאָה פּוּמִּין – יַעֲלוּ, וְאִם לָאו – הַפּוּמִּין אֲסוּרִין וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Yehuda says** that **Rabbi Eliezer says: If there are one hundred openings** of containers with tithed figs present **there,** in addition to the untithed figs, **it is nullified** in the one hundred. **But if not,** all of **the** layers of figs at the **openings** of the containers **are prohibited,** i.e., viewed as untithed, as one of them certainly contains an untithed *litra* that has not been nullified. And the figs on the insides of the vessels are permitted, as the untithed figs certainly did not reach there.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ יֵשׁ שָׁם שְׁלֹשָׁה מֵאוֹת פּוּמִּין – לֹא יַעֲלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda continues his statement: By contrast, **Rabbi Yehoshua says: Even** if **there are three hundred openings** present **there,** the layer at the top of the container is **not nullified.** This *litra* cannot be nullified in any manner, as Rabbi Yehoshua maintains that even an item occasionally sold by unit, such as a circle of dried figs, can never be nullified.

Rabbi Yehuda presents a different version of the same debate. In Rabbi Yehuda's account, Rabbi Eliezer rules that one needs 100 barrel-tops — a moderately stringent position. Rabbi Yehoshua goes further still: even 300 barrel-tops are not sufficient to render the *terumah* round *batel*. The reason: this is a *davar shedarko limanot* — the rounds of pressed figs are sometimes sold and counted individually, and therefore each round retains its individual significance and cannot be nullified regardless of how many other rounds surround it. This is the *tana* Rav Papa had in mind: Rabbi Yehoshua (in Rabbi Yehuda's version) applies *kol shedarko limanot* — something sometimes counted — even to a rabbinic prohibition (*terumah* of fruit being rabbinic, or *terumah* in the present day being rabbinic), and rules that it is not *batel* in any quantity.

### No Fixed Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרָסָהּ בְּעִיגּוּל, וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ בְּאֵיזֶה מְקוֹם בְּעִיגּוּל דְּרָסָהּ – אוֹ לִצְפוֹנָהּ אוֹ לִדְרוֹמָהּ, דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל יַעֲלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda continues: But if one **pressed** the *litra* of dried figs **into a circular** vessel along with other dried figs, **but does not know onto which place,** i.e., which side, **of the circular** vessel **he pressed it, whether,** e.g., **to its northern** side **or to its southern** side, in this case, as the prohibited *litra* is not located in a defined place and it cannot be distinguished from the others, it is not considered an item of significance, and **everyone agrees** that it is **nullified.** Accordingly, the ruling of the mishna that animals that are disqualified from being sacrificed are not nullified is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua as stated by Rabbi Eliezer, that an item occasionally sold by unit is not nullified. Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement, that only an item whose manner is exclusively to be counted is significant and cannot be nullified, is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir.

The *beraita* concludes with an important limiting case. If one stomped the *terumah* figs into a barrel but does not know *where* within the barrel they were placed — whether to the north or south side, whether they are mixed throughout — then *divrei hakol ya'alu*: everyone agrees that the *terumah* is *batel*. The reasoning is clear: when the forbidden material has no fixed, distinct location and is simply dispersed throughout the barrel, it cannot be treated as a discrete, countable unit. The principle of *shedarko limanot* applies when the forbidden item occupies a specific, identifiable place — the top of a particular barrel — so that it retains the character of a discrete unit that one would count. When it is simply smeared throughout the whole barrel with no distinct location, it has lost that character entirely, and *bitul* applies normally.

## Living Things

### Rava's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים חֲשִׁיבִי וְלָא בָּטְלִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says: You** may **even say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** i.e., Rabbi Meir, who maintains that an item that is not always counted is nullified in a majority. The reason is that **living creatures are significant, and** therefore **they are not nullified.**

Rav Papa's answer, while resolving the Mishnah's ruling on its own terms, implicitly leaves it as the position of only some *tannaim* — those who hold *kol shedarko limanot*. The Sages and Rabbi Akiva, who reject that general principle and restrict non-*bitul* to their enumerated list, might still hold that a cow *is* subject to *bitul*. This would be troubling: give me a break, it is a cow.

Rava therefore provides the ultimate, definitive answer — and the one that is *halacha*. Rava says: *afilu teima rabbanan* — even according to the Sages, who reject the principle of *kol shedarko limanot* and limit non-*bitul* to their six (or seven) specific items, a cow is not *batel*. Why? Because *ba'alei chayyim chashuvin velo batlin* — living creatures are intrinsically significant and do not lose their identity through *bitul*.

The principle here returns to the fundamental logic discussed earlier. Objects — even important objects — can in principle be submerged into an undifferentiated mass, even if the threshold for that is high. But a living being is categorically different. A living creature always retains its distinct, independent identity. It cannot be summed or amassed into an aggregate the way objects can. Just as one counts money but cannot truly "count" people in the same reductive sense — because each person remains irreducibly and individually themselves — so too a living animal always stands before us as an independently significant entity that cannot be absorbed into the surrounding mass and nullified. This is not a matter of commercial practice or halachic categorization; it is an expression of the intrinsic nature of living things. And on this, Rava says, all opinions agree.
