# Horayot 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Beis Din Rulings

### Classic Case

We begin with the *mishnah* at the bottom of 4b, the second wide line, which continues to address the precise conditions under which a *psak* of *beis din* obligates them to bring their special *korban*. The classic case is stated succinctly: *horu beis din shogegin vechol hakahal shogegin mevi'im par* — *beis din* ruled in error, clearly without deliberate intent to mislead, and the entire community followed them in error, meaning they were *toleh b'beis din*, trusting the court rather than acting on their own initiative. This is the paradigmatic scenario in which *beis din* brings the bull, the special communal sacrifice.

### Deliberate Beis Din

The *mishnah* then addresses a striking variation: *beis din* was *mezid* — deliberately misruling — while the community sinned innocently, following what they believed to be a legitimate *psak*. In that case, *mezidin ve'asu shogegin mevi'im kisbah use'irah* — the individuals bring a sheep or goat for their private sin. One might reasonably object: these laypeople trusted *beis din* in good faith, and simply because *beis din* chose to act as *resha'im* and intentionally *pasken* incorrectly, must these blameless individuals bring a *korban*? The answer, as the *mishnah* makes clear, is that this is not solely a matter of blameworthiness. Even though they are blameless, the sin was committed, and since it cannot be attributed to *beis din* — because a deliberate misdirection does not constitute a genuine *psak* — that connection is severed. Without a valid *psak*, the individuals' acts remain their own private sins and cannot be subsumed under the corporate *hora'ah* of *beis din*.

This is admittedly a strange *halacha*, particularly given the earlier *mishnah*'s discussion of individuals who could be exempt even when *beis din* retracts, on the grounds that they believed they were following *beis din*. Yet it is consistent with the principle that a *korban* is not exclusively tied to moral blame; where a sin occurs, a *korban* may be required regardless.

### Deliberate Community

The reverse case: *beis din* ruled honestly and in error, but the people deliberately sinned. The *mishnah* states *harei elu peturim* — the willful sinners are exempt from a *korban*. It is counterintuitive in the sense that their conduct is morally worse, yet they bring nothing, since the *korban* for deliberate sin is not a *chatas*.

### Partial Mezid

The *gemara* then notes that the *mishnah*'s formulation — *hashogeg dumya demezid* — implies that if someone's error contained an element of *mezid*, a *korban* would still be required. The *gemara* asks: what would constitute a *shogeg karov lemezid*? The answer given: *shehoru beis din shecheilev mutar venischalaf lo beshuman ve'achalo* — *beis din* ruled that a certain fat was permissible, but the individual who intended to eat the permitted fat inadvertently ate the forbidden fat instead. This is the same case introduced at the very opening of the *masechta*: on one hand, had he known it was the forbidden fat, he would have eaten it anyway, trusting *beis din*; on the other hand, in the actual case, he was not consciously following *beis din* — he was trying to eat the permitted fat. The *gemara* asks whether this should resolve Rami bar Chama's earlier question about whether such a person is *toleh b'beis din* and *patur*, or *toleh be'atzmo* and *chayav*.

The *gemara* deflects: *amar lach mishum detana reisha mezidin ve'asu shogegin tana seifa shogegin ve'asu mezidin* — stylistically, the end of the *mishnah* is simply the mirror image of its beginning. Since the first case had *beis din* as *mezid* and the community as *shogeg*, the last case has *beis din* as *shogeg* and the community as *mezid*. This symmetry does not mean that every case just short of full *mezid* would create a *chiyuv*. There may well be an intermediate case where the individual is also *patur*. Rami bar Chama's question, therefore, remains unresolved.

---

## Meir vs. Yehuda

### New Mishnah

We now move to a new *mishnah*: *horu beis din ve'asu kol hakahal o rubam al pihem* — *beis din* issued their ruling and all, or the majority, of the community followed them. *Mevi'in par* — this is the case where they bring an ox. In a case of *avoda zara*, *mevi'in par ve'sa'ir* — they bring both an ox as an *olah* and a goat as a *chatas*, corresponding to two separate *parshiyos* in the *Torah*.

### Rebbi Meir

*Divrei Rabbi Meir* — this is Rabbi Meir's position, which aligns with the intuitive assumption: it is *beis din*'s obligation, and *beis din* brings the *korban*.

### Rebbi Yehuda

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees in two related ways: *Rabbi Yehuda omer yod-beis shevatim mevi'in yod-beis parim* — each *shevet* brings its own *korban*. In a case of *avoda zara*, each brings both an ox and a goat, for a total of twenty-four offerings. This represents a double departure from Rabbi Meir: first, it shifts the obligation from *beis din* to the *tzibbur*; second, it views the *tzibbur* not as a single entity but as a confederation of twelve tribes, each constituting its own *tzibbur* and bearing its own separate obligation.

---

## Corporate Sin

### Unifying Role

This raises a conceptual question about what it means for the *tzibbur* to bring the *korban*. One might ask: if the *korban* is for the *tzibbur*'s sin, why is a *psak* of *beis din* required at all? If the majority of *Klal Yisrael* sins, why not simply say that whenever enough individuals transgress, a corporate *korban* is brought? The answer is that without the *psak* of *beis din*, even the most widespread transgression remains a collection of individual sins — in your home you eat the forbidden food, in my home I eat the forbidden food, but there is no shared cause, no unifying act. What the *psak* of *beis din* accomplishes is to provide that common cause: the leadership of *Klal Yisrael* ruled this way and the people followed, transforming what might otherwise have been a multitude of private errors into a single corporate sin. The *korban* is thus not for the wrong *psak* itself but for the sin of the community — a sin that only registers as corporate, rather than merely aggregate, because *beis din*'s ruling created the unifying condition.

An alternative conceptualization would hold that the *korban* is indeed for the wrong *psak* of *beis din*, but that ultimate responsibility for *beis din*'s error rests with the *tzibbur*, in the way a community might bear responsibility for the failures of its leadership. This approach is less compelling, both because the responsibility for a wrong ruling properly begins with the court that issued it, and because, as one might fairly observe, the *beis din* is not elected by the community in the first place.

### Tribal Units

The more attractive framework, then, is a genuine debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda: is the *korban* for the sin of the *psak*, in which case *beis din* brings it, or is it for the sin of the action — which, by virtue of the *beis din*'s ruling, now constitutes a corporate transgression — in which case the *tzibbur* brings it?

---

## Meir vs. Yehuda…

### Rebbi Shimon

Rabbi Shimon combines both positions: *Rabbi Shimon omer yod-gimmel parim* — thirteen oxen in total, and in a case of *avoda zara*, thirteen oxen and thirteen goats. He holds that both *beis din* and the *tzibbur* bring, and each individual *shevet* brings its own *korban* as well: *par ve'se'ir le'kol shevet va'shevet u'far ve'se'ir le'veis din*.

### Partial Tribes

The *mishnah* then addresses the case where not all twelve tribes sinned, but only seven or the majority of each of seven: *horu beis din ve'asu zayin shevatim o rubam al pihen mevi'in par u've'avoda zara mevi'in par ve'se'ir divrei Rabbi Meir*. Rabbi Meir is consistent: once the majority of *Klal Yisrael* follows *beis din*, one ox is brought — and in *avoda zara*, one ox and one goat.

*Rabbi Yehuda omer zayin shevatim she'chatu mevi'in zayin parim* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that the seven tribes that sinned bring seven oxen. But then he adds an important further ruling: *u'shear shevatim shelo chatu mevi'in al yedeihem par* — the remaining tribes that did not sin also bring, each one a *par*, albeit in a secondary capacity. The concept here is *kol Yisrael areivim zeh ba'zeh* — the entire nation bears a degree of mutual responsibility. The primary obligation falls on the sinning tribes; the others bring through them, as it were, because when something of such magnitude occurs, no *shevet* is entirely uninvolved. The secondary tribes are not the prime *chayavin*, and presumably their *chiyuv* would only be operative after the sinning tribes had first fulfilled their own obligation.

Rabbi Shimon does not accept this notion of secondary *grira*. He maintains his dual structure — each sinning tribe brings, and *beis din* brings — but does not extend the obligation to tribes that did not sin.

---

## Secondary Obligation

### Grira Principle

With respect to the case where seven tribes sin, Rabbi Yehuda holds that even the remaining tribes who did not sin bring *al yedei hachotim* — through the sinning tribes. The conceptual basis is that mutual responsibility among all of Israel is sufficient to generate a secondary obligation on those who did not themselves transgress.

---

## Secondary Obligation…

### Mutual Basis

The principle underlying Rabbi Yehuda's *grira* is *kol Yisrael areivim zeh ba'zeh* — all of Israel is responsible for one another. Where the majority of *shevatim* have sinned through the ruling of *beis din*, the gravity of the event is such that no tribe stands entirely apart from it. There is, the logic suggests, enough responsibility to go around: the primary obligation belongs to those who actually sinned, but the remaining tribes bear a secondary layer of accountability, grounded in their shared national identity and mutual responsibility.

One might ask what *al yedeihem* means in practice. While the *gemara* does not spell it out explicitly, one could reason that the secondary obligation of the non-sinning tribes would only be pressed after the sinning tribes had owned their primary responsibility. The non-sinning tribes would not be the first ones called upon to bring; they bring only in the wake of the primary *chiyuv* being fulfilled.

### No Secondary

Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, rejects the *grira* framework entirely. For him, the *korban* is brought by those who are primarily obligated — the sinning tribes and *beis din* — and there is no secondary obligation on tribes that did not transgress.

---

## Corporate Sin…

### Mutual Responsibility

The broader conceptual point here reinforces the *tzibbur*-centered reading of why *beis din*'s *psak* is necessary. The *psak* transforms the individual acts into a corporate sin precisely because of this principle of mutual responsibility: when *beis din* rules and the nation follows, what is created is not merely a statistical accumulation of individual errors but a shared national act, one for which all of Israel, in varying degrees, bears responsibility.

---

## Local vs. Central

### Local Beis Din

The *mishnah* proceeds to a case where it is not the *beis din hagadol* that ruled but the *beis din* of an individual tribe: *horu beis din shel echad min ha'shevatim ve'asah oso ha'shevet al pihen* — the local *beis din* of one tribe issued a ruling and that tribe followed it. *Oso shevet hu chayav ve'shear kol ha'shevatim peturim divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — that tribe is obligated and all the other tribes are exempt.

### Tribal Entity

For Rabbi Yehuda, this is entirely coherent given his general position. If a tribe sins without any *beis din* ruling at all, it is simply a collection of individuals sinning individually. But if the tribe sins through the ruling of its own *beis din*, that ruling unifies the tribe as a corporate entity — and therefore that tribal entity bears the obligation to bring a *korban*. There is no need for the *beis din hagadol*; what matters is the unifying role of a *psak*, even if that *psak* comes from the tribe's own local court.

### Central Court

The *chachamim* disagree: *ein chayavin ela al hora'at beis din hagadol bilvad* — the obligation is only generated by a ruling of the *beis din hagadol*, as it says *v'im kol adat Yisrael yishgu* — the congregation of all Israel, not the congregation of a single tribe. This position could be identified with Rabbi Meir, for whom the entire obligation flows from the *beis din hagadol*, or even with Rabbi Shimon, who, while he focuses on individual tribes, may still require the central *beis din* to generate the relevant *hora'ah*.

---

## Knowledge Requirement

### Specific Sin

The *gemara* cites a *baraisa*: *yadu shehoru veta'u mah horu, yachol yihiyu chayavin* — if *beis din* knows that they ruled wrongly but does not know which ruling was wrong, one might think they would still be obligated. Rashi explains the case: two questions came before *beis din*, one concerning a type of *cheilev* and one concerning a type of *dam*, both *issur karet*. They ruled that in case X the *cheilev* was permissible, and in case Y the *dam* was permissible. All of *Klal Yisrael* followed and ate accordingly. Then *beis din* realized that one of the two rulings had been wrong — but they could not recall which one. The conditions for a *korban* obligation are technically met: a wrong *psak* led to all of *Klal Yisrael* sinning. Yet *talmud lomar venoda'a hachatat* — the *Torah* requires that the sin itself be known, not merely that they know they are sinners. *V'lo sheyadu hachotim* — it is not sufficient to know that they have transgressed; they must know the specific nature of the sin. *Asher chata'u* — the particular sin must be identified.

### Derasha

The *baraisa* continues: *asher chata'u* — the double language of "which they sinned" teaches that if two tribes sin, they bring two oxen; if three sin, they bring three. At first glance this might suggest that two *individuals* caught in a *beis din*-related sin would each bring a special *korban*, which would be unprecedented. The *baraisa* therefore qualifies: *talmud lomar hakahal* — it says "the congregation," meaning it is not individuals but tribal units that are the relevant measure. *Vechol kahal vekahal chayav* — every individual *kahal*, meaning every individual *shevet*, is separately obligated.

The *baraisa* then elaborates in language that is explicitly Rabbi Yehuda's: if two tribes sinned they bring two oxen; if seven sinned they bring seven. And the remaining tribes that did not sin bring as well, each one its own *par*, *al yedeihen* — through the sinning tribes. It is made explicit here that even when the ruling comes from the *beis din hagadol*, an individual tribe that sinned will bring, and by extension every other tribe will bring through it.

### Kahal Interpretation

*Velach ne'emar kahal lechayev al kol kahal vekahal divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — Rabbi Yehuda derives the entire framework of individual tribal obligation from the word *kahal*. Each *kahal*, each tribal unit, is independently obligated. This single word, as the *gemara* will explain, is the textual anchor for multiple elements of Rabbi Yehuda's position.

---

## Textual Basis

### Meir's Reading

*Maitama d'Rabbi Yehuda* — the *gemara* now turns to mapping each *tanna*'s position onto the *psukim* of the *parasha*. The relevant verse in *Parshat Vayikra* reads: *v'im kol adat Yisrael yishgu, vene'elam davar me'einei hakahal, ve'asu achat mikol mitzvot Hashem asher lo te'aseina ve'ashemu, venoda'a hachatat asher chata'u aleha, vehikrivu hakahal*. The keywords are: *kol adat Yisrael yishgu* — the *tzibbur* sinned; *vene'elam davar me'einei hakahal* — *beis din* ruled incorrectly, since the *einayim*, the eyes, refer to *beis din*; *ve'asu* in the plural — the community acted; and finally *vehikrivu hakahal* — the *kahal* brings. The central dispute is whether *hakahal* in the final phrase refers back to *beis din* (as the *einei hakahal*) or to the *tzibbur* itself.

Rabbi Meir reads *me'einei hakahal* and *hikrivu hakahal* as a unified expression. Since *me'einei hakahal* contextualizes the entire phrase around *beis din*, *hikrivu hakahal* also refers to *beis din*. *Ma kahal ha'amur lemalah beis din velo tzibbur, af kahal ha'amur lematah beis din velo tzibbur* — just as the upper *kahal* refers to *beis din*, so too the lower *kahal* refers exclusively to *beis din*. Rabbi Meir does not treat the *heh* of *hakahal* as an independent derivational unit, and therefore works with only two mentions of *kahal*.

### Shimon's Link

Rabbi Shimon uses the same gezerah shavah-style linkage between the upper and lower *kahal*, but reaches a different conclusion. He reads *me'einei hakahal* as placing *beis din* in relation to the *kahal*, not as making *beis din* the *kahal* itself: *ma kahal ha'amur lemalah beis din im hakahal, af kan amar lematah beis din im hakahal* — just as the upper text places *beis din* together with the *kahal*, so too the lower text obligates *beis din* together with the *kahal*. Both bring. He counts three effective mentions of *kahal*: *me'einei hakahal* counts as one (the *heh* being grammatically required and not an independent derivation), plus the word *kahal* lower, and the *heh* lower counts separately as a third. One of these teaches that every individual tribe (*kol kahal vekahal*) is obligated; the other two link upper and lower to establish the joint obligation of both *beis din* and the *tzibbur*.

---

## Knowledge Requirement…

### Rebbi Eliezer

The *gemara* asks whose position the *baraisa* reflects when it requires that the *specific sin* be known. *Rav Yehuda amar Rav ve'i teima Rava, delo k'Rebbi Eliezer* — it is not in accordance with Rabbi Eliezer. Rabbi Eliezer's position concerns an individual who ate something unknown but definitely forbidden — for instance, the person in front of whom either *cheilev* or *nosar* was placed, and who ate it without knowing which. Rabbi Eliezer says *mah nafishach*: whatever it was, you are *chayav* a *korban*, since *im cheilev achal chayav, nosar chayav* — whichever sin was committed, it generates an obligation. By extension, Rabbi Eliezer would hold that *beis din* must bring a *korban* even without knowing which specific ruling was wrong.

Rav Ashi, however, argues that even Rabbi Eliezer could agree in this case: *shani hacha de'chsiv asher chata'u aleha* — here, the *Torah* uses the specific phrase *asher chata'u aleha*, which requires precise identification of the sin. Whereas in the case of an individual, the phrase *asher chata bah* serves a different purpose — to exclude *mit'asek*, someone who performs the forbidden act entirely inadvertently without any meaningful engagement with it — and therefore Rabbi Eliezer can hold that an individual need not identify the precise sin, here in the communal context the phrase *asher chata'u aleha* has no other role to play and therefore requires knowledge of the specific sin. Even Rabbi Eliezer would concede this point.

The *gemara* notes that by an individual's *korban* the verse also says *asher chata bah* — specifying the sin — yet Rabbi Eliezer nonetheless holds his position. The resolution is that Rabbi Eliezer uses that phrase for *perat le'mita'sek*, excluding the purely accidental act, and thus has no remainder from which to derive the requirement of knowing the specific sin. Here, that other derivation is unavailable, leaving the phrase to require specific knowledge.

---

## Textual Basis…

### Four Mentions

*Mai ta'ama d'Rabbi Yehuda* — the *gemara* now returns to mapping the positions onto the *psukim*. Rabbi Yehuda's position is grounded in his reading of four appearances of *kahal* in the *parasha*: the word *kahal* twice in the verse and the *heh* prefix each time counted separately, yielding four derivable mentions. *Kasavar arba kahali ksiv*. One mention teaches that each individual tribal unit (*kol kahal vekahal*) is separately obligated. One teaches that the *hora'ah* rests with *beis din* while the *ma'aseh* belongs to the *kahal* — the *tzibbur* does the sinning. One establishes the principle of *grira* — that even non-sinning tribes bring through the sinning ones. And the fourth teaches that even a tribe following its own local *beis din* is sufficient to create the tribal *korban* obligation. These four conceptual points are each anchored to one of the four *kahal* mentions.

### Three Mentions

Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, counts only three effective mentions: *Rabbi Shimon tlata kahali ksivi*. He does not count the *heh* in *me'einei hakahal* as an independent mention because, as the *gemara* explains, *orchei d'kra hu* — grammatical convention requires the definite article here, as one would naturally say *me'einei d'planya*, "from the eyes of so-and-so." One cannot say *me'einei kahal* without the *heh*, and therefore the *heh* there carries no independent derivational weight. With three mentions, Rabbi Shimon derives: one for the individual tribal obligation (*kol kahal vekahal*), and the other two to establish the linkage between upper and lower texts — that *beis din* is obligated together with the *kahal*, yielding his combined structure. He does not derive *grira* and he does not derive the local *beis din* provision, which is why he rejects both of those ideas.

---

## Dual Measures

### Me'einei Source

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar holds the unusual position that a *rov* can be established by either of two independent measures. *Ksiv v'hayah im me'einei ha'eidah* — the *Torah* by the *avoda zara parasha* at the end of *Parshat Shelach* uses the phrase *me'einei ha'eidah ne'estah lishgaga, ve'asu kol ha'eidah par ben bakar echad l'olah... u'se'ir izim echad l'chatas*. From the word *me'einei*, with its *mem* prefix, Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar derives an implication of *miut* — a minority or partial element, suggesting that not the totality is required. From the closing phrase *l'chol ha'am bishgaga* — to all the people — he derives that a *rov*, a majority, is required. These two implications seem to pull in opposite directions: one suggests even a minority suffices, the other demands a majority. The resolution is that you need a majority by at least one measure, and even a partial element satisfies the other. Either measure of majority, taken alone, is sufficient.

### Six or Seven

The practical application: *asu vav vehen rubo shel kahal, o zayin af al pi she'einan rubo shel kahal, chayavin* — if six tribes sinned and they constitute the majority of the population of *Klal Yisrael*, or if seven tribes sinned even though their combined population does not constitute a majority of the people, the obligation applies. One is the majority by count of tribes, the other by count of population.

Taken to its logical extreme, this creates a striking result: one could theoretically take the seven smallest tribes, have only the majority of each of those seven act on the *hora'ah*, and end up with a situation where a quite small fraction of the actual population of *Klal Yisrael* triggers the communal *korban* obligation.

The *gemara* then addresses where Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Meir derive the rule that the *hora'ah* belongs to *beis din* while the *ma'aseh* belongs to the *kahal* — since, unlike Rabbi Yehuda, they do not have a spare *kahal* mention to make that derivation. *Amar Abaye d'amar kra: v'hayah im me'einei ha'eidah ne'estah lishgaga* — here in the *avoda zara parasha*, *ne'estah* — "it was done" — is in the passive third person, implying that it was done by others, not by *beis din* itself. *Beis din* provides the ruling; the people do the sinning. *Rava amar: mi'lchol ha'am bishgaga* — from the end of the verse, which places the *shegaga* upon *ha'am*, the people. Both verses are needed: without *ne'estah* one might think that the *ma'aseh* must be done by the majority; without *l'chol ha'am bishgaga* one might think *beis din* must also perform the sin in practice. These principles are then extended from *avoda zara* to ordinary sins by a *gezerah shavah* of *me'einei me'einei* — the word *me'einei* appears in both *parshiyos*, linking them and making the criteria parallel.

---

## Secondary Scope

### One Tribe Question

The *gemara* raises the question that was implicit in the earlier sources: *shevet echad l'Rabbi Yehuda b'horu beis din hagadol, mi maitu shear shvatim atuya?* — if the *beis din hagadol* ruled and only one tribe followed and sinned, do the other tribes also bring by virtue of *grira*? The ambiguity arises because Rabbi Yehuda's statement of *grira* in the *mishnah* appeared in the context of seven tribes sinning. Was his secondary obligation tied to the majority having sinned?

### Majority Theory

One resolution: *mi amrinan shiva shvatim hu d'maitu shear shvatim bahadeihu* — it is only when seven tribes sin that the other tribes bear secondary responsibility. The logic would be that since there is a *rov* of tribes, the magnitude of the event is such that all of *Klal Yisrael* must share the burden. But if only one tribe sinned, there is no *rov*, and the non-sinning tribes have no reason to be pulled in. That tribe's failing is their own issue.

### Central Court Theory

The alternative resolution: *lo shena* — it makes no difference. Any time a full *shevet* sins, the national shared responsibility applies, and the other tribes bring as well. What creates the national character of the event is not the number of tribes that sinned but the fact that it was the *beis din hagadol* whose ruling caused the sin.

---

## Secondary Scope…

### Textual Proof

*Tashma* — the *gemara* now resolves the ambiguity from a close reading of our *mishnah*. Rabbi Yehuda says there: *shevet she'asa b'horat beis dino, oso shevet chayav v'shear kol shevatim peturin* — when a tribe follows its own local *beis din*, that tribe brings and all others are exempt. *Lama li lemisni v'shaar shevatim peturin?* — why did the *mishnah* need to state that the other tribes are exempt? Since it already said *oso shevet chayav*, the exemption of the others would follow automatically. The extra phrase must therefore be teaching something: it is specifically in the case of a local *beis din* ruling that the other tribes are *peturim* — implying that if it had been the *beis din hagadol* that ruled, even the other tribes would be *chayav*.

### Collective via Central

*Shema mina* — the proof is confirmed. For Rabbi Yehuda, what creates the collective responsibility of all tribes is not whether a majority of tribes sinned, but whether the ruling originated from the *beis din hagadol*. The *beis din hagadol* represents all of *Klal Yisrael*; its ruling transforms even one tribe's sin into a national event, pulling every other tribe into a degree of secondary responsibility. If the ruling came only from a local tribal court, the event remains parochial and the other tribes are entirely uninvolved.

---

## Local vs. Central…

### One Tribe Korban

The *gemara*'s *diyuk* in the *mishnah* further clarifies Rabbi Yehuda's position: even if only one tribe sinned, as long as it followed the ruling of the *beis din hagadol*, that single tribe brings its *korban* — and the remaining eleven tribes bring as well through the principle of *grira*. The *beis din hagadol* is the key factor that makes any tribal sin a matter of national concern.

---

## Shimon Single Tribe

### Shimon Question

The *gemara* now raises the parallel question for Rabbi Shimon: *shevet echad l'Rebbi Shimon b'horu beis din hagadol, mi mayisi o lo?* — does Rabbi Shimon hold that even a single tribe brings when the *beis din hagadol* has ruled? Rabbi Shimon has to this point only spoken in terms of majority scenarios. Does his individual tribal obligation extend to a case where only one tribe sinned?

### One or Two

To resolve this, the *gemara* revisits the *baraisa* that records a dispute between an unnamed *tanna kamma* who requires one *korban* and Rabbi Shimon who requires two. *Bemaei askinan?* — what is the case? If seven tribes sinned, Rabbi Shimon would require eight — one per tribe plus one for *beis din*. So it cannot be seven tribes. Therefore it must be one tribe sinning.

### Mixed Majority

But then: what is the *tanna kamma*'s position? If it is Rabbi Meir, he requires a majority, which is absent here. If it is Rabbi Yehuda, then based on the *grira* principle just established, all twelve tribes should bring, not just one. Neither classic position produces a clean reading where the *tanna kamma* says "one." The *gemara* therefore proposes reading the *baraisa* as the case of six tribes that constitute the majority of the population, or seven tribes that do not — the special mixed-majority case of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar. In that reading, the *tanna kamma* (Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar) would say one *korban* for the *beis din*, since this counts as sufficient majority for that obligation, while Rabbi Shimon would add one more for all the tribes combined. However, this reading produces a conceptually strange result: if a full majority of tribes sin, each tribe brings individually; but if it is this mixed majority-minority configuration, all tribes are lumped together as a single unit. This inversion is difficult to account for, unless one follows Rashi's reading that the scenario involves one tribe that itself constitutes the majority of *Klal Yisrael*'s population — in which case the logic is cleaner, since that one tribe's sin is in effect the majority by the population measure, and one for *beis din* plus one for the tribe yields two.

### Central Court Proof

*Tashma* — the *gemara* finds a cleaner resolution. *Vachachamim omrim l'olam eino chayav ela al hora'at beis din hagadol* — the *chachamim* at the end of the *mishnah* say the obligation exists only through a ruling of the *beis din hagadol*. *Man chachamim?* — who are these *chachamim*? If it is Rabbi Meir, the issue is not merely the absence of *beis din hagadol* but also the absence of a majority of *Klal Yisrael* sinning. The *chachamim*'s formulation suggests that the single correction needed is the type of *beis din*, not the proportion of sinners. *Ella lav Rebbi Shimon hi* — therefore it must be Rabbi Shimon speaking as *chachamim*: Rabbi Shimon agrees that an individual tribe can bring its *korban*, but insists that a ruling of the *beis din hagadol* is required to generate that obligation.

### Conclusion

*Shema mina* — the conclusion is established. For Rabbi Shimon, when the *beis din hagadol* rules and even one tribe follows and sins, that tribe is obligated to bring its *korban*. The *beis din hagadol*'s ruling is the necessary and sufficient jurisdictional condition for the tribal *korban* obligation to apply, even at the level of a single *shevet*.

## Kahal Textual

### Joshua Verse

The *gemara* now turns to the textual question underlying all of the above: where do Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon derive the idea that an individual tribe can itself be called a *kahal*? The first source cited: *d'chsiv v'yaamod Yehoshafat bikahal Yehuda viYerushalayim b'veis Hashem lifnei hachatzer hachadasha* — Yehoshafat stood in the *kahal* of Yehudah and Yerushalayim. *Mai chadasha, amar Rabbi Yochanan* — what was new about this *chatzer*? Rabbi Yochanan explains: they enacted a new ruling that a *tvul yom* may not enter the *machaneh Leviyah*. In any event, the verse uses the word *kahal* to refer to the tribe of Yehudah, suggesting that a single tribe constitutes a *kahal*.

### Jerusalem Challenge

*Masif lah Rav Acha bar Yaakov* — Rav Acha bar Yaakov challenges this proof: since the verse says "Yehudah and Yerushalayim," and Yerushalayim included a portion belonging to the tribe of Binyamin, perhaps you need at least two tribes present for the designation *kahal* to apply. The verse cannot prove that a single tribe alone is called *kahal*.

### Jacob Verse

Rav Acha bar Yaakov therefore offers an alternative source: *d'chsiv v'yomer elai hineni mafracha v'hirbisicha u'nesaticha likahal amim* — the angel says to Yaakov, "I will make you fruitful and multiply you and make you a *kahal* of nations." *Man d'asid d'misyalid lei b'hahu sha'ata* — who was about to be born to Yaakov at that moment? Binyamin. Therefore the *Torah* is saying that from Yaakov another *kahal* would emerge — Binyamin — and from this one sees that a single tribe is called a *kahal*.

### Twelve Tribes

*Amar lei Rav Shisha breh de'Rav Kahana* — Rav Shisha son of Rav Kahana raises a challenge: perhaps the verse means that when Binyamin is born, completing the twelve tribes, then *nesaticha likahal amim* — all twelve together will be a *kahal*. The verse would then be saying that the full complement of twelve constitutes a *kahal*, not that Binyamin alone is one.

### Benjamin Proof

The *gemara* rejects this reading: *ella yod-beis shvatim ikru kahal, yod-aleph shvatim lo ikru kahal?* — if the verse means that only all twelve together are called a *kahal*, does it follow that eleven tribes are not a *kahal*? Surely everyone would agree that a majority of seven tribes is called a *kahal*. Therefore the reading that only twelve together constitute a *kahal* is untenable. Since that reading must be rejected, the verse can only mean what it straightforwardly suggests: that Binyamin himself — a single tribe — is being called a *kahal*. *Shema mina* — this establishes the textual basis for the ruling that an individual tribe is a *kahal*, and therefore capable of bearing its own *korban* obligation as a corporate unit.
