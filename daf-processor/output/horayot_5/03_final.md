# Horayot 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Beis Din Rulings

### Classic Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הוֹרוּ בֵּית דִּין שׁוֹגְגִין, וְעָשׂוּ כָּל הַקָּהָל שׁוֹגְגִין – מְבִיאִין פַּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If the judges of **the court issued** an erroneous **ruling unwittingly, and the entire congregation performed** a transgression **unwittingly** on the basis of their ruling, the court **brings a bull,** as it is stated in the Torah with regard to an unwitting communal sin-offering.

We begin with the *mishnah* at the bottom of 4b, the second wide line, which continues to address the precise conditions under which a *psak* of *beis din* obligates them to bring their special *korban*. The classic case is stated succinctly: *horu beis din shogegin vechol hakahal shogegin mevi'im par* — *beis din* ruled in error, clearly without deliberate intent to mislead, and the entire community followed them in error, meaning they were *toleh b'beis din*, trusting the court rather than acting on their own initiative. This is the paradigmatic scenario in which *beis din* brings the bull, the special communal sacrifice.

### Deliberate Beis Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְזִידִין, וְעָשׂוּ שׁוֹגְגִין – מְבִיאִין כִּשְׂבָּה וּשְׂעִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** If the court issued the erroneous ruling **intentionally,** as they knew that their ruling was incorrect, **and** the congregation **performed** a transgression **unwittingly** on the basis of the ruling of the court, each member of the congregation **brings a female lamb or a female goat** as an individual sin-offering.

The *mishnah* then addresses a striking variation: *beis din* was *mezid* — deliberately misruling — while the community sinned innocently, following what they believed to be a legitimate *psak*. In that case, *mezidin ve'asu shogegin mevi'im kisbah use'irah* — the individuals bring a sheep or goat for their private sin. One might reasonably object: these laypeople trusted *beis din* in good faith, and simply because *beis din* chose to act as *resha'im* and intentionally *pasken* incorrectly, must these blameless individuals bring a *korban*? The answer, as the *mishnah* makes clear, is that this is not solely a matter of blameworthiness. Even though they are blameless, the sin was committed, and since it cannot be attributed to *beis din* — because a deliberate misdirection does not constitute a genuine *psak* — that connection is severed. Without a valid *psak*, the individuals' acts remain their own private sins and cannot be subsumed under the corporate *hora'ah* of *beis din*.

This is admittedly a strange *halacha*, particularly given the earlier *mishnah*'s discussion of individuals who could be exempt even when *beis din* retracts, on the grounds that they believed they were following *beis din*. Yet it is consistent with the principle that a *korban* is not exclusively tied to moral blame; where a sin occurs, a *korban* may be required regardless.

### Deliberate Community

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוֹגְגִין, וְעָשׂוּ מְזִידִין – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** If the court issued the erroneous ruling **unwittingly and** the congregation **performed** a transgression **intentionally,** i.e., with the knowledge that the ruling of the court was erroneous, **these** people **are exempt** from bringing an offering.

The reverse case: *beis din* ruled honestly and in error, but the people deliberately sinned. The *mishnah* states *harei elu peturim* — the willful sinners are exempt from a *korban*. It is counterintuitive in the sense that their conduct is morally worse, yet they bring nothing, since the *korban* for deliberate sin is not a *chatas*.

### Partial Mezid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ שׁוֹגְגִין וְעָשׂוּ מְזִידִין – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ פְּטוּרִין. הָא שׁוֹגֵג דּוּמְיָא דְּמֵזִיד – חַיָּיב, וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי? שֶׁהוֹרוּ בֵּית דִּין שֶׁחֵלֶב מוּתָּר, וְנִתְחַלֵּף לוֹ בְּשׁוּמָּן וַאֲכָלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches: If the court issued the erroneous ruling **unwittingly and** the congregation **performed** a transgression **intentionally, these** people **are exempt.** The Gemara infers: **But** if the person performed a transgression **unwittingly** in a manner **similar to** that of **an intentional** transgression, the court is **liable. And what are the circumstances** of that performance? It is a case **where the court issued a ruling that** forbidden **fat** is **permitted, and** the forbidden **fat became confused for** a person **with** permitted **fat and he ate** the forbidden fat.

The *gemara* then notes that the *mishnah*'s formulation — *hashogeg dumya demezid* — implies that if someone's error contained an element of *mezid*, a *korban* would still be required. The *gemara* asks: what would constitute a *shogeg karov lemezid*? The answer given: *shehoru beis din shecheilev mutar venischalaf lo beshuman ve'achalo* — *beis din* ruled that a certain fat was permissible, but the individual who intended to eat the permitted fat inadvertently ate the forbidden fat instead. This is the same case introduced at the very opening of the *masechta*: on one hand, had he known it was the forbidden fat, he would have eaten it anyway, trusting *beis din*; on the other hand, in the actual case, he was not consciously following *beis din* — he was trying to eat the permitted fat. The *gemara* asks whether this should resolve Rami bar Chama's earlier question about whether such a person is *toleh b'beis din* and *patur*, or *toleh be'atzmo* and *chayav*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ, מִשּׁוּם דִּתְנָא רֵישָׁא: מְזִידִין וְעָשׂוּ שׁוֹגְגִין, תְּנָא סֵיפָא: שׁוֹגְגִין וְעָשׂוּ מְזִידִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rami bar Ḥama could **say to you** that the dilemma cannot be resolved based on this inference, as **due to** the fact **that** the *tanna* **taught the first clause** of the mishna: If the court issued the erroneous ruling **intentionally, and** the congregation **performed** a transgression **unwittingly, he taught the latter clause** employing a similar style: If the court issued the erroneous ruling **unwittingly and** the congregation **performed** a transgression **intentionally.**

The *gemara* deflects: *amar lach mishum detana reisha mezidin ve'asu shogegin tana seifa shogegin ve'asu mezidin* — stylistically, the end of the *mishnah* is simply the mirror image of its beginning. Since the first case had *beis din* as *mezid* and the community as *shogeg*, the last case has *beis din* as *shogeg* and the community as *mezid*. This symmetry does not mean that every case just short of full *mezid* would create a *chiyuv*. There may well be an intermediate case where the individual is also *patur*. Rami bar Chama's question, therefore, remains unresolved.

## Meir vs. Yehuda (II)

### New Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הוֹרוּ בֵּית דִּין וְעָשׂוּ כׇּל הַקָּהָל אוֹ רוּבָּן עַל פִּיהֶן – מְבִיאִין פַּר. וּבַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – מְבִיאִין פַּר וְשָׂעִיר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If the judges of **the court issued** an erroneous **ruling and the entire congregation or a majority thereof performed** a transgression **on the basis of their** ruling, the judges **bring a bull** as an unwitting communal sin-offering. **And** if the erroneous ruling **involved idol worship,** the judges **bring a bull and a goat,** as it is written in the Torah (see Numbers 15:24); this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

We now move to a new *mishnah*: *horu beis din ve'asu kol hakahal o rubam al pihem* — *beis din* issued their ruling and all, or the majority, of the community followed them. *Mevi'in par* — this is the case where they bring an ox. In a case of *avoda zara*, *mevi'in par ve'sa'ir* — they bring both an ox as an *olah* and a goat as a *chatas*, corresponding to two separate *parshiyos* in the *Torah*.

### Rebbi Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר שְׁבָטִים – מְבִיאִין שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר פָּרִים. וּבַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – מְבִיאִין שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר פָּרִים וּשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר שְׂעִירִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** It is not the court that brings the offering, it is the people. **Twelve tribes,** each of which performed a transgression, **bring twelve bulls,** i.e., each tribe brings one, **and for idol worship they bring twelve bulls and twelve goats,** as each tribe is a congregation.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees in two related ways: *Rabbi Yehuda omer yod-beis shevatim mevi'in yod-beis parim* — each *shevet* brings its own *korban*. In a case of *avoda zara*, each brings both an ox and a goat, for a total of twenty-four offerings. This represents a double departure from Rabbi Meir: first, it shifts the obligation from *beis din* to the *tzibbur*; second, it views the *tzibbur* not as a single entity but as a confederation of twelve tribes, each constituting its own *tzibbur* and bearing its own separate obligation.

---

## Corporate Sin (II)

### Unifying Role

This raises a conceptual question about what it means for the *tzibbur* to bring the *korban*. One might ask: if the *korban* is for the *tzibbur*'s sin, why is a *psak* of *beis din* required at all? If the majority of *Klal Yisrael* sins, why not simply say that whenever enough individuals transgress, a corporate *korban* is brought? The answer is that without the *psak* of *beis din*, even the most widespread transgression remains a collection of individual sins — in your home you eat the forbidden food, in my home I eat the forbidden food, but there is no shared cause, no unifying act. What the *psak* of *beis din* accomplishes is to provide that common cause: the leadership of *Klal Yisrael* ruled this way and the people followed, transforming what might otherwise have been a multitude of private errors into a single corporate sin. The *korban* is thus not for the wrong *psak* itself but for the sin of the community — a sin that only registers as corporate, rather than merely aggregate, because *beis din*'s ruling created the unifying condition.

An alternative conceptualization would hold that the *korban* is indeed for the wrong *psak* of *beis din*, but that ultimate responsibility for *beis din*'s error rests with the *tzibbur*, in the way a community might bear responsibility for the failures of its leadership. This approach is less compelling, both because the responsibility for a wrong ruling properly begins with the court that issued it, and because, as one might fairly observe, the *beis din* is not elected by the community in the first place.

### Tribal Units

The more attractive framework, then, is a genuine debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda: is the *korban* for the sin of the *psak*, in which case *beis din* brings it, or is it for the sin of the action — which, by virtue of the *beis din*'s ruling, now constitutes a corporate transgression — in which case the *tzibbur* brings it?

## Meir vs. Yehuda (II)

### Rebbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר פָּרִים, וּבַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר פָּרִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר שְׂעִירִים, פַּר וְשָׂעִיר לְכׇל שֵׁבֶט וְשֵׁבֶט, פַּר וְשָׂעִיר לְבֵית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** They bring **thirteen bulls; and for idol worship** they bring **thirteen bulls and thirteen goats, a bull and a goat for each and every tribe and a bull and a goat for** the **court.**

Rabbi Shimon combines both positions: *Rabbi Shimon omer yod-gimmel parim* — thirteen oxen in total, and in a case of *avoda zara*, thirteen oxen and thirteen goats. He holds that both *beis din* and the *tzibbur* bring, and each individual *shevet* brings its own *korban* as well: *par ve'se'ir le'kol shevet va'shevet u'far ve'se'ir le'veis din*.

### Partial Tribes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹרוּ בֵּית דִּין וְעָשׂוּ שִׁבְעָה שְׁבָטִים אוֹ רוּבָּן עַל פִּיהֶן – מְבִיאִין פַּר, וּבַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – מְבִיאִין פַּר וְשָׂעִיר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שִׁבְעָה שְׁבָטִים שֶׁחָטְאוּ – מְבִיאִין שִׁבְעָה פָּרִים, וּשְׁאָר שְׁבָטִים שֶׁלֹּא חָטְאוּ – מְבִיאִין עַל יְדֵיהֶן פַּר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: If the judges of the **court issued a ruling, and** at least **seven tribes, or a majority of** each of those tribes, **performed** a transgression **on the basis of their** ruling, the judges **bring a bull; and for idol worship they bring a bull and a goat.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says:** The **seven tribes that sinned bring seven bulls,** i.e., each tribe brings one bull, **and** each of **the rest of the tribes,** i.e., those **that did not sin, brings** one **bull on the basis of** the sin of the other tribes.

The *mishnah* then addresses the case where not all twelve tribes sinned, but only seven or the majority of each of seven: *horu beis din ve'asu zayin shevatim o rubam al pihen mevi'in par u've'avoda zara mevi'in par ve'se'ir divrei Rabbi Meir*. Rabbi Meir is consistent: once the majority of *Klal Yisrael* follows *beis din*, one ox is brought — and in *avoda zara*, one ox and one goat.

*Rabbi Yehuda omer zayin shevatim she'chatu mevi'in zayin parim* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that the seven tribes that sinned bring seven oxen. But then he adds an important further ruling: *u'shear shevatim shelo chatu mevi'in al yedeihem par* — the remaining tribes that did not sin also bring, each one a *par*, albeit in a secondary capacity. The concept here is *kol Yisrael areivim zeh ba'zeh* — the entire nation bears a degree of mutual responsibility. The primary obligation falls on the sinning tribes; the others bring through them, as it were, because when something of such magnitude occurs, no *shevet* is entirely uninvolved. The secondary tribes are not the prime *chayavin*, and presumably their *chiyuv* would only be operative after the sinning tribes had first fulfilled their own obligation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: שְׁמוֹנָה פָּרִים, וּבַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – שְׁמוֹנָה פָּרִים וּשְׁמוֹנָה שְׂעִירִים, פַּר וְשָׂעִיר לְכׇל שֵׁבֶט וְשֵׁבֶט, פַּר וְשָׂעִיר לְבֵית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** When seven tribes sin **eight bulls** are brought as offerings, one bull for each and every tribe and one bull for the court. **And for idol worship, eight bulls and eight goats** are brought, one **bull and** one **goat for each and every tribe** and one **bull and** one **goat for** the **court.**

Rabbi Shimon does not accept this notion of secondary *grira*. He maintains his dual structure — each sinning tribe brings, and *beis din* brings — but does not extend the obligation to tribes that did not sin.

## Secondary Obligation (II)

### Grira Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף אֵלּוּ שֶׁלֹּא חָטְאוּ מְבִיאִין עַל יְדֵי חוֹטְאִין. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **as even those who did not sin bring** an offering **on the basis of** the actions of the **sinners.** **Conclude** that this is the case.

With respect to the case where seven tribes sin, Rabbi Yehuda holds that even the remaining tribes who did not sin bring *al yedei hachotim* — through the sinning tribes. The conceptual basis is that mutual responsibility among all of Israel is sufficient to generate a secondary obligation on those who did not themselves transgress.

---

## Secondary Obligation (II)

### Mutual Basis

The principle underlying Rabbi Yehuda's *grira* is *kol Yisrael areivim zeh ba'zeh* — all of Israel is responsible for one another. Where the majority of *shevatim* have sinned through the ruling of *beis din*, the gravity of the event is such that no tribe stands entirely apart from it. There is, the logic suggests, enough responsibility to go around: the primary obligation belongs to those who actually sinned, but the remaining tribes bear a secondary layer of accountability, grounded in their shared national identity and mutual responsibility.

One might ask what *al yedeihem* means in practice. While the *gemara* does not spell it out explicitly, one could reason that the secondary obligation of the non-sinning tribes would only be pressed after the sinning tribes had owned their primary responsibility. The non-sinning tribes would not be the first ones called upon to bring; they bring only in the wake of the primary *chiyuv* being fulfilled.

### No Secondary

Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, rejects the *grira* framework entirely. For him, the *korban* is brought by those who are primarily obligated — the sinning tribes and *beis din* — and there is no secondary obligation on tribes that did not transgress.

---

## Corporate Sin (II)

### Mutual Responsibility

The broader conceptual point here reinforces the *tzibbur*-centered reading of why *beis din*'s *psak* is necessary. The *psak* transforms the individual acts into a corporate sin precisely because of this principle of mutual responsibility: when *beis din* rules and the nation follows, what is created is not merely a statistical accumulation of individual errors but a shared national act, one for which all of Israel, in varying degrees, bears responsibility.

## Local vs. Central (II)

### Local Beis Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹרוּ בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל אֶחָד מִן הַשְּׁבָטִים, וְעָשָׂה אוֹתוֹ הַשֵּׁבֶט עַל פִּיהֶן – אוֹתוֹ שֵׁבֶט הוּא חַיָּיב, וּשְׁאָר כׇּל הַשְּׁבָטִים פְּטוּרִין, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין חַיָּיבִין אֶלָּא עַל הוֹרָיוֹת בֵּית דִּין הַגָּדוֹל בִּלְבַד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאִם כׇּל עֲדַת יִשְׂרָאֵל יִשְׁגּוּ״, וְלֹא עֲדַת אוֹתוֹ שֵׁבֶט.
>
> **Translation:** If the **court of one of the tribes issued a ruling and** the majority of **that tribe performed** a transgression **on the basis of its** ruling, **that tribe is liable** to bring an offering **and the rest of all the tribes** are **exempt;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. And the Rabbis say: One is liable** to bring an offering for an unwitting communal sin **only for rulings of the High Court alone, as it is stated: "And if the entire assembly of Israel shall act unwittingly"** (Leviticus 4:13), from which it is derived that there is liability only for a ruling of the assembly, i.e., the court, of the entire people, **but not** for a ruling of **the assembly of that** particular **tribe.**

The *mishnah* proceeds to a case where it is not the *beis din hagadol* that ruled but the *beis din* of an individual tribe: *horu beis din shel echad min ha'shevatim ve'asah oso ha'shevet al pihen* — the local *beis din* of one tribe issued a ruling and that tribe followed it. *Oso shevet hu chayav ve'shear kol ha'shevatim peturim divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — that tribe is obligated and all the other tribes are exempt.

### Tribal Entity

For Rabbi Yehuda, this is entirely coherent given his general position. If a tribe sins without any *beis din* ruling at all, it is simply a collection of individuals sinning individually. But if the tribe sins through the ruling of its own *beis din*, that ruling unifies the tribe as a corporate entity — and therefore that tribal entity bears the obligation to bring a *korban*. There is no need for the *beis din hagadol*; what matters is the unifying role of a *psak*, even if that *psak* comes from the tribe's own local court.

### Central Court

The *chachamim* disagree: *ein chayavin ela al hora'at beis din hagadol bilvad* — the obligation is only generated by a ruling of the *beis din hagadol*, as it says *v'im kol adat Yisrael yishgu* — the congregation of all Israel, not the congregation of a single tribe. This position could be identified with Rabbi Meir, for whom the entire obligation flows from the *beis din hagadol*, or even with Rabbi Shimon, who, while he focuses on individual tribes, may still require the central *beis din* to generate the relevant *hora'ah*.

## Knowledge Requiremen (II)

### Specific Sin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יָדְעוּ שֶׁהוֹרוּ, וְטָעוּ מָה הוֹרוּ, יָכוֹל יְהוּ חַיָּיבִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְנוֹדְעָה הַחַטָּאת״, וְלֹא שֶׁיִּוָּדְעוּ הַחוֹטְאִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara elaborates on the dispute cited in the mishna. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In a case where the judges **knew that they issued** an erroneous **ruling, and erred** in remembering which erroneous **ruling they issued,** one **might** have thought that **they would be liable** to bring an offering for this. **The verse states: "And the sin** that they sinned **became known,** the congregation shall sacrifice" (Leviticus 4:14), indicating that liability is only in a case where the sin became known to the court that issued the erroneous ruling, **and not** in a case **where it became known** only **to the sinners.** In this case, the sinners are certain that they sinned, but the court does not know the sin with regard to which it issued the ruling.

The *gemara* cites a *baraisa*: *yadu shehoru veta'u mah horu, yachol yihiyu chayavin* — if *beis din* knows that they ruled wrongly but does not know which ruling was wrong, one might think they would still be obligated. Rashi explains the case: two questions came before *beis din*, one concerning a type of *cheilev* and one concerning a type of *dam*, both *issur karet*. They ruled that in case X the *cheilev* was permissible, and in case Y the *dam* was permissible. All of *Klal Yisrael* followed and ate accordingly. Then *beis din* realized that one of the two rulings had been wrong — but they could not recall which one. The conditions for a *korban* obligation are technically met: a wrong *psak* led to all of *Klal Yisrael* sinning. Yet *talmud lomar venoda'a hachatat* — the *Torah* requires that the sin itself be known, not merely that they know they are sinners. *V'lo sheyadu hachotim* — it is not sufficient to know that they have transgressed; they must know the specific nature of the sin. *Asher chata'u* — the particular sin must be identified.

### Derasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֲשֶׁר חָטְאוּ״, חָטְאוּ שְׁנֵי שְׁבָטִים – מְבִיאִין שְׁנֵי פָרִים, חָטְאוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה – מְבִיאִין שְׁלֹשָׁה, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אוֹמֵר אֶלָּא חָטְאוּ שְׁנֵי יְחִידִים – מְבִיאִין שְׁנֵי פָרִים, חָטְאוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה – מְבִיאִין שְׁלֹשָׁה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״הַקָּהָל״, הַקָּהָל חַיָּיב, וְכׇל קָהָל וְקָהָל חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** From the phrase **"that they sinned"** it is derived: If **two tribes sinned they bring two bulls,** one from each tribe; if **three** tribes **sinned they bring three** bulls, one from each tribe. **Or** perhaps that phrase **says only** that if **two individuals sinned they bring two bulls,** one from each individual; if **three** individuals **sinned they bring three** bulls, one from each individual. Therefore, **the verse states: "The congregation,"** from which it is derived: **The congregation** is **liable, and each and every congregation** is **liable.**

The *baraisa* continues: *asher chata'u* — the double language of "which they sinned" teaches that if two tribes sin, they bring two oxen; if three sin, they bring three. At first glance this might suggest that two *individuals* caught in a *beis din*-related sin would each bring a special *korban*, which would be unprecedented. The *baraisa* therefore qualifies: *talmud lomar hakahal* — it says "the congregation," meaning it is not individuals but tribal units that are the relevant measure. *Vechol kahal vekahal chayav* — every individual *kahal*, meaning every individual *shevet*, is separately obligated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? חָטְאוּ שְׁנֵי שְׁבָטִים – מְבִיאִין שְׁנֵי פָרִים, חָטְאוּ שִׁבְעָה – מְבִיאִין שִׁבְעָה, וּשְׁאָר שְׁבָטִים שֶׁלֹּא חָטְאוּ – מְבִיאִין עַל יְדֵיהֶן פַּר פַּר, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ אֵלּוּ שֶׁלֹּא חָטְאוּ מְבִיאִין עַל יְדֵי הַחוֹטְאִים, לְכָךְ נֶאֱמַר ״קָהָל״ לְחַיֵּיב עַל כׇּל קָהָל וְקָהָל. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **How** so? If **two tribes sinned they bring two bulls.** If **seven** tribes **sinned they bring seven** bulls. **And each** of **the rest of the tribes that did not sin brings** one **bull on the basis of** the sin of the other tribes, **as even those** tribes **that did not sin bring** an offering **on the basis of** the actions of **the sinners. For that** reason **"congregation" is stated** in the verse, **to render each and every congregation liable.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

The *baraisa* then elaborates in language that is explicitly Rabbi Yehuda's: if two tribes sinned they bring two oxen; if seven sinned they bring seven. And the remaining tribes that did not sin bring as well, each one its own *par*, *al yedeihen* — through the sinning tribes. It is made explicit here that even when the ruling comes from the *beis din hagadol*, an individual tribe that sinned will bring, and by extension every other tribe will bring through it.

### Kahal Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: שִׁבְעָה שְׁבָטִים שֶׁחָטְאוּ – מְבִיאִין שִׁבְעָה פָּרִים, וּבֵית דִּין מְבִיאִין עַל יְדֵיהֶן פַּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר לְמַטָּה ״קָהָל״ וְנֶאֱמַר לְמַעְלָה ״קָהָל״. מָה ״קָהָל״ הָאָמוּר לְמַעְלָה – בֵּית דִּין עִם הַקָּהָל, אַף ״קָהָל״ הָאָמוּר לְמַטָּה – בֵּית דִּין עִם הַקָּהָל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says: Seven tribes that sinned bring seven bulls, and** the **court brings** one **bull on the basis of their** actions, **as** the term **"congregation" is stated below:** "The congregation shall sacrifice" (Leviticus 4:14), **and** the term **"congregation" is stated above:** "And the matter is hidden from the eyes of the congregation" (Leviticus 4:13). **Just as** with regard to the term **"congregation" that is stated above** the reference is to the **court,** i.e., "the eyes of the congregation," together **with "the congregation," so too,** with regard to the term **"congregation" that is stated below,** with regard to liability to bring an offering the reference is to the **court** together **with the congregation.**

*Velach ne'emar kahal lechayev al kol kahal vekahal divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — Rabbi Yehuda derives the entire framework of individual tribal obligation from the word *kahal*. Each *kahal*, each tribal unit, is independently obligated. This single word, as the *gemara* will explain, is the textual anchor for multiple elements of Rabbi Yehuda's position.

## Textual Basis (II)

### Meir's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: שִׁבְעָה שְׁבָטִים שֶׁחָטְאוּ – בֵּית דִּין מְבִיאִין עַל יְדֵיהֶם פַּר, וְהֵן פְּטוּרִין, נֶאֱמַר ״קָהָל״ לְמַטָּה וְנֶאֱמַר ״קָהָל״ לְמַעְלָה, מָה ״קָהָל״ הָאָמוּר לְמַעְלָה – בֵּית דִּין וְלֹא צִבּוּר, אַף ״קָהָל״ הָאָמוּר לְמַטָּה – בֵּית דִּין וְלֹא צִבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir says:** In the case of **seven tribes that sinned,** the **court brings a bull on the basis of their** sin **and** the members of the tribe **are exempt,** as the term **"congregation" is stated below:** "The congregation shall sacrifice" (Leviticus 4:14), **and** the term **"congregation" is stated above:** "And the matter is hidden from the eyes of the congregation" (Leviticus 4:13). **Just as** with regard to the term **"congregation" that is stated above** the reference is to the **court and not** to the **public, so too,** with regard to the term **"congregation" that is stated below** the reference is to the **court and not** to the **public.**

*Maitama d'Rabbi Yehuda* — the *gemara* now turns to mapping each *tanna*'s position onto the *psukim* of the *parasha*. The relevant verse in *Parshat Vayikra* reads: *v'im kol adat Yisrael yishgu, vene'elam davar me'einei hakahal, ve'asu achat mikol mitzvot Hashem asher lo te'aseina ve'ashemu, venoda'a hachatat asher chata'u aleha, vehikrivu hakahal*. The keywords are: *kol adat Yisrael yishgu* — the *tzibbur* sinned; *vene'elam davar me'einei hakahal* — *beis din* ruled incorrectly, since the *einayim*, the eyes, refer to *beis din*; *ve'asu* in the plural — the community acted; and finally *vehikrivu hakahal* — the *kahal* brings. The central dispute is whether *hakahal* in the final phrase refers back to *beis din* (as the *einei hakahal*) or to the *tzibbur* itself.

Rabbi Meir reads *me'einei hakahal* and *hikrivu hakahal* as a unified expression. Since *me'einei hakahal* contextualizes the entire phrase around *beis din*, *hikrivu hakahal* also refers to *beis din*. *Ma kahal ha'amur lemalah beis din velo tzibbur, af kahal ha'amur lematah beis din velo tzibbur* — just as the upper *kahal* refers to *beis din*, so too the lower *kahal* refers exclusively to *beis din*. Rabbi Meir does not treat the *heh* of *hakahal* as an independent derivational unit, and therefore works with only two mentions of *kahal*.

### Shimon's Link

The *gemara* notes that by an individual's *korban* the verse also says *asher chata bah* — specifying the sin — yet Rabbi Eliezer nonetheless holds his position. The resolution is that Rabbi Eliezer uses that phrase for *perat le'mita'sek*, excluding the purely accidental act, and thus has no remainder from which to derive the requirement of knowing the specific sin. Here, that other derivation is unavailable, leaving the phrase to require specific knowledge.

## Knowledge Requiremen (II)

### Rebbi Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, וְאִיתֵּימָא רָבָא: דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. דִּתְנַן, אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: מָה נַפְשָׁךְ? אִם חֵלֶב אָכַל – חַיָּיב, נוֹתָר אָכַל – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said, and some say** it was **Rava** who said, **that** this is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, as we learned** in a *baraita* that there is a dispute with regard to one who had two pieces before him, one of them prohibited because it is forbidden fat and one of them prohibited because it is left over from an offering after the time allotted for its consumption [*notar*], and he ate one, but does not know whether he ate the forbidden fat or the *notar*. **Rabbi Eliezer says: Whichever** way **you** look at it, he is liable to bring a sin-offering. **If** it was forbidden **fat** that **he ate** he is **liable,** and if it was ***notar*** that **he ate** he is **liable.** Therefore, he must bring an offering. Likewise, in the case in the *baraita*, since the court issued an erroneous ruling and it is known that the erroneous ruling was with regard to a matter for which it is liable to bring an offering, according to Rabbi Eliezer it should make no difference that they do not know the precise nature of their error, and they should be liable to bring an offering. Nevertheless, the ruling in the *baraita* is that they are exempt, counter to what one would expect to be the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, שָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״אֲשֶׁר חָטְאוּ עָלֶיהָ״. הָתָם נָמֵי הָכְתִיב: ״אֲשֶׁר חָטָא בָּהּ״! הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ: פְּרָט לַמִּתְעַסֵּק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Even** if **you say** that the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** one can explain the ruling of the *baraita*. **Here,** in the *baraita*, it **is different, as it is written:** "And the sin **that they sinned** became known" (Leviticus 4:14). The verse underscores that the judges must be aware of the specific sin for which they are liable to bring an offering. The Gemara asks: **There too,** with regard to an individual sin-offering, **isn't it written:** "Or if his sin **that he sinned** became known to him" (Leviticus 4:23), leading to the same conclusion? The Gemara answers: In the case of the individual sin-offering, **that** expression **is necessary for** the *tanna* **to exclude** one who **acts unawares.** One is liable to bring a sin-offering for an unwitting transgression only if he performs the action with intent.

Rav Ashi, however, argues that even Rabbi Eliezer could agree in this case: *shani hacha de'chsiv asher chata'u aleha* — here, the *Torah* uses the specific phrase *asher chata'u aleha*, which requires precise identification of the sin. Whereas in the case of an individual, the phrase *asher chata bah* serves a different purpose — to exclude *mit'asek*, someone who performs the forbidden act entirely inadvertently without any meaningful engagement with it — and therefore Rabbi Eliezer can hold that an individual need not identify the precise sin, here in the communal context the phrase *asher chata'u aleha* has no other role to play and therefore requires knowledge of the specific sin. Even Rabbi Eliezer would concede this point.

## Textual Basis (II)

### Four Mentions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? קָסָבַר: אַרְבָּעָה קְהָלֵי כְּתִיבִי – ״קָהָל״, ״הַקָּהָל״, ״קָהָל״, ״הַקָּהָל״. חַד לְחַיֵּיב עַל כׇּל קָהָל וְקָהָל, וְחַד לְהוֹרָאָה תְּלוּיָה בְּבֵית דִּין וּמַעֲשֶׂה תָּלוּי בַּקָּהָל. וְחַד לִגְרִירָה. וְחַד לְשֵׁבֶט שֶׁעָשָׂה בְּהוֹרָאַת בֵּית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reasoning of Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that even one tribe that sinned is liable to bring an offering? The Gemara answers that **he holds:** There are **four** mentions of the term **congregation written: Congregation, the congregation, congregation, the congregation.** **One** serves **to render each and every congregation liable,** as a tribe is also called a congregation; **and one** serves **to** establish that the **ruling is dependent on** the **court and** the **action is dependent on the congregation; and one** serves **to** teach the concept of **drawing,** i.e., a tribe that did not sin is drawn after a tribe that sinned and is liable to bring an offering; **and one** serves **to** teach that **a tribe that performed** a transgression **on** the basis of **the ruling of** its tribal **court** is liable to bring a bull as an unwitting communal sin-offering.

*Mai ta'ama d'Rabbi Yehuda* — the *gemara* now returns to mapping the positions onto the *psukim*. Rabbi Yehuda's position is grounded in his reading of four appearances of *kahal* in the *parasha*: the word *kahal* twice in the verse and the *heh* prefix each time counted separately, yielding four derivable mentions. *Kasavar arba kahali ksiv*. One mention teaches that each individual tribal unit (*kol kahal vekahal*) is separately obligated. One teaches that the *hora'ah* rests with *beis din* while the *ma'aseh* belongs to the *kahal* — the *tzibbur* does the sinning. One establishes the principle of *grira* — that even non-sinning tribes bring through the sinning ones. And the fourth teaches that even a tribe following its own local *beis din* is sufficient to create the tribal *korban* obligation. These four conceptual points are each anchored to one of the four *kahal* mentions.

### Three Mentions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן תְּלָתָא קְהָלֵי כְּתִיבִי: ״הַקָּהָל״, ״קָהָל״, ״הַקָּהָל״. ״מֵעֵינֵי הַקָּהָל״ אוֹרְחֵיהּ דִּקְרָא הוּא, כִּדְאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: מֵעֵינֵי דִּפְלָנְיָא. חַד לְחַיֵּיב עַל כׇּל קָהָל וְקָהָל, וּתְרֵי אַחֲרִינֵי: נֶאֱמַר לְמַטָּה ״קָהָל״ וְנֶאֱמַר לְמַעְלָה ״קָהָל״, מָה לְהַלָּן בֵּית דִּין עִם הַקָּהָל – אַף כָּאן בֵּית דִּין עִם הַקָּהָל.
>
> **Translation:** **And** what is the reasoning of **Rabbi Shimon,** who holds that seven tribes that sinned bring seven bulls and the court brings one bull? He holds that there are **three** mentions of the term **congregation written.** The first two are: **The congregation, congregation,** which he interprets in the manner that Rabbi Yehuda interpreted. He counts the third and last mention of the term: **The congregation,** as in the verse: **"From the eyes of the congregation." This is** written in **the** typical **manner of the verse, as people say: From the eyes of so-and-so,** and the definitive article "the" does not serve to teach another *halakha*. **Of those three, one** serves **to render each and every congregation liable,** as a tribe is also called congregation, **and** the **other two** come so that a verbal analogy can be derived: The term **"congregation" is stated below, and** the term **"congregation" is stated above. Just as** with regard to the term "congregation" that is stated **there,** above, the reference is to the **court,** i.e., "the eyes of the congregation," together **with the congregation, so too,** with regard to the term "congregation" that is stated **here** concerning liability to bring an offering, the reference is to the **court** together **with the congregation.**

Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, counts only three effective mentions: *Rabbi Shimon tlata kahali ksivi*. He does not count the *heh* in *me'einei hakahal* as an independent mention because, as the *gemara* explains, *orchei d'kra hu* — grammatical convention requires the definite article here, as one would naturally say *me'einei d'planya*, "from the eyes of so-and-so." One cannot say *me'einei kahal* without the *heh*, and therefore the *heh* there carries no independent derivational weight. With three mentions, Rabbi Shimon derives: one for the individual tribal obligation (*kol kahal vekahal*), and the other two to establish the linkage between upper and lower texts — that *beis din* is obligated together with the *kahal*, yielding his combined structure. He does not derive *grira* and he does not derive the local *beis din* provision, which is why he rejects both of those ideas.

## Dual Measures

### Me'einei Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: כְּתִיב: ״וְהָיָה אִם מֵעֵינֵי הָעֵדָה״ – אַלְמָא מִיעוּטָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״מֵעֵינֵי״. וּכְתִיב: ״כִּי לְכׇל הָעָם בִּשְׁגָגָה״, לְמֵימְרָא דְּרוּבָּא – אִין, מִיעוּטָא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And what is the reasoning of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar,** who holds that if six tribes sinned and in terms of population they constitute a majority of the congregation, or if seven tribes sinned even though in terms of population they do not constitute a majority of the congregation, the court brings a bull? **It is written: "Then it shall be, if** it was performed unwittingly, hidden **from the eyes of the congregation"** (Numbers 15:24). **Apparently,** the reference is even to **a minority** of the people, **as "from the eyes" is written,** indicating: Not all of them, but some of them. **And it is written: "As for all the people** it was performed **unwittingly"** (Numbers 15:26), **to say that** based on the principle: The halakhic status of a majority is like that of the entirety, if **the majority** of the congregation sinned, then **yes,** the court is liable to bring an offering. If **the minority** sinned, then **no,** the court is not liable.

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar holds the unusual position that a *rov* can be established by either of two independent measures. *Ksiv v'hayah im me'einei ha'eidah* — the *Torah* by the *avoda zara parasha* at the end of *Parshat Shelach* uses the phrase *me'einei ha'eidah ne'estah lishgaga, ve'asu kol ha'eidah par ben bakar echad l'olah... u'se'ir izim echad l'chatas*. From the word *me'einei*, with its *mem* prefix, Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar derives an implication of *miut* — a minority or partial element, suggesting that not the totality is required. From the closing phrase *l'chol ha'am bishgaga* — to all the people — he derives that a *rov*, a majority, is required. These two implications seem to pull in opposite directions: one suggests even a minority suffices, the other demands a majority. The resolution is that you need a majority by at least one measure, and even a partial element satisfies the other. Either measure of majority, taken alone, is sufficient.

### Six or Seven

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כֵּיצַד? עָשׂוּ שִׁשָּׁה וְהֵן רוּבּוֹ שֶׁל קָהָל, אוֹ שִׁבְעָה אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינָן רוּבּוֹ שֶׁל קָהָל – חַיָּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** **How so?** How can this apparent contradiction be resolved? The Gemara explains: If **six** tribes **sinned and they** constitute **a majority of** the **congregation, or** if **seven** tribes sinned, **even though** in terms of population **they do not** constitute **a majority of** the **congregation,** the court is **liable** to bring a bull.

The practical application: *asu vav vehen rubo shel kahal, o zayin af al pi she'einan rubo shel kahal, chayavin* — if six tribes sinned and they constitute the majority of the population of *Klal Yisrael*, or if seven tribes sinned even though their combined population does not constitute a majority of the people, the obligation applies. One is the majority by count of tribes, the other by count of population.

Taken to its logical extreme, this creates a striking result: one could theoretically take the seven smallest tribes, have only the majority of each of those seven act on the *hora'ah*, and end up with a situation where a quite small fraction of the actual population of *Klal Yisrael* triggers the communal *korban* obligation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּהוֹרָאָה תְּלוּיָה בְּבֵית דִּין וּמַעֲשֶׂה תָּלוּי בַּקָּהָל, מְנָא לְהוּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהָיָה אִם מֵעֵינֵי הָעֵדָה נֶעֶשְׂתָה לִשְׁגָגָה״. רָבָא אָמַר: ״מִלְּכׇל הָעָם בִּשְׁגָגָה״. וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״וְהָיָה אִם מֵעֵינֵי הָעֵדָה נֶעֶשְׂתָה לִשְׁגָגָה״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: אֲפִילּוּ מִיעוּטָא, לְהָכִי כְּתִיב ״לְכׇל הָעָם בִּשְׁגָגָה״. וְאִי כְּתַב ״לְכׇל הָעָם בִּשְׁגָגָה״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: עַד דְּעָבְדִי בֵּית דִּין בַּהֲדֵי רוּבָּא, לְהָכִי כְּתִיב ״וְהָיָה אִם מֵעֵינֵי הָעֵדָה נֶעֶשְׂתָה לִשְׁגָגָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Meir, from where do they** derive the fact **that** the **ruling is dependent on** the **court and** the **action is dependent on the congregation? Abaye said** that they derive it from a verse, **as the verse states** in the passive form: **"Then it shall be, if it was performed unwittingly, hidden from the eyes of the congregation"** (Number 15:24), indicating that the action of the congregation was generated by the ruling of the court. **Rava said:** It is derived **from** that which is stated: **"For all the people** it was performed **unwittingly"** (Numbers 15:26). The term "all the people" indicates that the unwitting action is common to all, the court through its ruling and the people through their action. The Gemara notes: **And** both of the verses are **necessary. As, had the Merciful One written** only: **"Then it shall be, if it was performed unwittingly, hidden from the eyes of the congregation"** (Number 15:24), **I would say:** There is liability to bring an offering **even** if only **a minority** of the congregation sinned. **Therefore, it is written: "For all the people** it was performed **unwittingly." And had** the Merciful One **written** only: **"For all the people** it was performed **unwittingly," I would say:** There is no liability **unless** the **court performs** the transgression **together with the majority** of the congregation, as the verse appears to refer to a single unwitting action. **Therefore, it is written: "Then it shall be, if it was performed unwittingly, hidden from the eyes of the congregation,"** indicating that the unwitting act was performed on the basis of the ruling of the court, and the action of the court is not significant in this regard.

The *gemara* then addresses where Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Meir derive the rule that the *hora'ah* belongs to *beis din* while the *ma'aseh* belongs to the *kahal* — since, unlike Rabbi Yehuda, they do not have a spare *kahal* mention to make that derivation. *Amar Abaye d'amar kra: v'hayah im me'einei ha'eidah ne'estah lishgaga* — here in the *avoda zara parasha*, *ne'estah* — "it was done" — is in the passive third person, implying that it was done by others, not by *beis din* itself. *Beis din* provides the ruling; the people do the sinning. *Rava amar: mi'lchol ha'am bishgaga* — from the end of the verse, which places the *shegaga* upon *ha'am*, the people. Both verses are needed: without *ne'estah* one might think that the *ma'aseh* must be done by the majority; without *l'chol ha'am bishgaga* one might think *beis din* must also perform the sin in practice. These principles are then extended from *avoda zara* to ordinary sins by a *gezerah shavah* of *me'einei me'einei* — the word *me'einei* appears in both *parshiyos*, linking them and making the criteria parallel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא כִּי כְּתִיבִי הָנֵי קְרָאֵי בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הוּא דִּכְתִיבִי. יָלְפִינַן ״מֵעֵינֵי״ ״מֵעֵינֵי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But when these verses are written, they are written with regard to** an erroneous ruling involving **idol worship,** not concerning erroneous rulings involving other transgressions. The Gemara explains: **We derive** it by means of a verbal analogy between **"from the eyes of** the congregation" written with regard to other transgressions, and **"from the eyes of** the congregation" written concerning idol worship.

## Secondary Scope (II)

### One Tribe Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹרוּ בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל אֶחָד וְכוּ׳. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: שֵׁבֶט אֶחָד לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּהוֹרָאַת בֵּית דִּין הַגָּדוֹל, מִי מַיְיתוּ שְׁאָר שְׁבָטִים אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If the **court of one** of the tribes **issued a ruling,** and the majority of that tribe performed a transgression on the basis of its ruling, that tribe is liable to bring an offering and the rest of all the tribes are exempt; this is the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **According to Rabbi Yehuda,** in the case of **one tribe** that performed a transgression **on** the basis of **the ruling of the High Court, are** the **other tribes** liable to **bring** an offering **or not?**

The *gemara* raises the question that was implicit in the earlier sources: *shevet echad l'Rabbi Yehuda b'horu beis din hagadol, mi maitu shear shvatim atuya?* — if the *beis din hagadol* ruled and only one tribe followed and sinned, do the other tribes also bring by virtue of *grira*? The ambiguity arises because Rabbi Yehuda's statement of *grira* in the *mishnah* appeared in the context of seven tribes sinning. Was his secondary obligation tied to the majority having sinned?

### Majority Theory

The Gemara elaborates: **Do we say** that **it is** specifically in a case where **seven tribes** sinned **that other tribes** each **bring** an offering **together with them because there is a majority** of the tribes that sinned, **but** in a case where only **one tribe** sinned, **where there is no majority** of the tribes that sinned, they do **not? Or perhaps** it **is no different,** and the other tribes are drawn after even one tribe.

One resolution: *mi amrinan shiva shvatim hu d'maitu shear shvatim bahadeihu* — it is only when seven tribes sin that the other tribes bear secondary responsibility. The logic would be that since there is a *rov* of tribes, the magnitude of the event is such that all of *Klal Yisrael* must share the burden. But if only one tribe sinned, there is no *rov*, and the non-sinning tribes have no reason to be pulled in. That tribe's failing is their own issue.

### Central Court Theory

The alternative resolution: *lo shena* — it makes no difference. Any time a full *shevet* sins, the national shared responsibility applies, and the other tribes bring as well. What creates the national character of the event is not the number of tribes that sinned but the fact that it was the *beis din hagadol* whose ruling caused the sin.

## Secondary Scope (II)

### Textual Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מָה הֵן מְבִיאִין? פַּר אֶחָד, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵי פָרִים. בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא שֶׁחָטְאוּ שִׁבְעָה, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן תְּמָנְיָא בָּעֵי! אֶלָּא דְּחָטָא שֵׁבֶט אֶחָד. בְּמַאי? אִי בְּהוֹרָאַת בֵּית דִּינוֹ, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לֵית לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא לָאו בְּהוֹרָאַת בֵּית דִּין הַגָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** resolution of this dilemma from a *baraita*, which teaches: **What do they bring?** They bring **one bull; Rabbi Shimon says: Two bulls.** The Gemara elaborates: **What are we dealing with** in this *baraita*? **If we say** the reference is to a case **where seven** tribes **sinned, Rabbi Shimon requires eight** bulls, one for each tribe that sinned and one for the court. **Rather,** the reference must be to a case **where one tribe sinned.** The Gemara clarifies: **In what** circumstances did the tribe sin? **If** the tribe sinned **on** the basis of **the ruling of its** tribal **court, Rabbi Shimon does not accept** the *halakha* that an erroneous ruling of the tribal court renders them liable to bring an offering. **Rather, is it not** so that they sinned **on** the basis of **a ruling of the High Court?**

*Tashma* — the *gemara* now resolves the ambiguity from a close reading of our *mishnah*. Rabbi Yehuda says there: *shevet she'asa b'horat beis dino, oso shevet chayav v'shear kol shevatim peturin* — when a tribe follows its own local *beis din*, that tribe brings and all others are exempt. *Lama li lemisni v'shaar shevatim peturin?* — why did the *mishnah* need to state that the other tribes are exempt? Since it already said *oso shevet chayav*, the exemption of the others would follow automatically. The extra phrase must therefore be teaching something: it is specifically in the case of a local *beis din* ruling that the other tribes are *peturim* — implying that if it had been the *beis din hagadol* that ruled, even the other tribes would be *chayav*.

### Collective via Central

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שֵׁבֶט שֶׁעָשָׂה בְּהוֹרָאַת בֵּית דִּינוֹ – אוֹתוֹ הַשֵּׁבֶט חַיָּיב, וּשְׁאָר כׇּל הַשְּׁבָטִים פְּטוּרִין. וּבְהוֹרָאַת בֵּית דִּין הַגָּדוֹל – אֲפִילּוּ שְׁאָר שְׁבָטִים חַיָּיבִים. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution to the dilemma from a *baraita*, which teaches: **Rabbi Yehuda says:** In the case of **a tribe that performed** a transgression **on** the basis of **a ruling of its** tribal **court, that tribe** is **liable and all the rest of the tribes** are **exempt. But** if it was **on** the basis of **the ruling of the High Court, even the rest of** the **tribes** are **liable.** The Gemara concludes: **Learn from it** that Rabbi Yehuda holds that the other tribes are liable in a case where one tribe performed a transgression on the basis of the ruling of the High Court.

*Shema mina* — the proof is confirmed. For Rabbi Yehuda, what creates the collective responsibility of all tribes is not whether a majority of tribes sinned, but whether the ruling originated from the *beis din hagadol*. The *beis din hagadol* represents all of *Klal Yisrael*; its ruling transforms even one tribe's sin into a national event, pulling every other tribe into a degree of secondary responsibility. If the ruling came only from a local tribal court, the event remains parochial and the other tribes are entirely uninvolved.

## Local vs. Central (II)

### One Tribe Korban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מַתְנִיתִין נָמֵי דַּיְקָא, דְּקָתָנֵי: וְעָשָׂה אוֹתוֹ הַשֵּׁבֶט עַל פִּיהֶם – אוֹתוֹ הַשֵּׁבֶט חַיָּיב וּשְׁאָר כׇּל הַשְּׁבָטִים פְּטוּרִין. לְמָה לִי לְמִיתְנָא וּשְׁאָר שְׁבָטִים פְּטוּרִים? הָא תְּנָא אוֹתוֹ הַשֵּׁבֶט חַיָּיב, וְכֵיוָן דִּתְנָא אוֹתוֹ הַשֵּׁבֶט חַיָּיב, מִמֵּילָא יָדַעְנָא שְׁאָר שְׁבָטִים פְּטוּרִין! אֶלָּא הָא קָמַשְׁמַע לָן, בְּהוֹרָאַת בֵּית דִּינוֹ הוּא דִּשְׁאָר שְׁבָטִים פְּטוּרִים, אֲבָל בְּהוֹרָאַת בֵּית דִּין הַגָּדוֹל – אֲפִילּוּ שְׁאָר שְׁבָטִים חַיָּיבִין. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** The language of **the mishna is also precise** in support of that conclusion, **as** the *tanna* **teaches:** If the court of one of the tribes issued an erroneous ruling **and** the majority of **that tribe performed** a transgression **on the basis of its** ruling, **that tribe** is **liable** to bring an offering **and all the rest of the tribes** are **exempt.** He explains: **Why do I** need the *tanna* **to teach: And the rest of the tribes** are **exempt? Didn't he** already **teach: That tribe** is **liable? And since he teaches: That tribe** is **liable, we know automatically** that the **rest of** the **tribes** are **exempt. Rather, this** *tanna* **is teaching us** that **it is** specifically in a case where the tribe performed an unwitting transgression **on** the basis of **the ruling of its** tribal **court that the rest of** the **tribes are exempt; but** in a case where the tribe performed an unwitting transgression **on** the basis of **the ruling of the High Court, even the rest of** the **tribes** are **liable.** The Gemara concludes: **Learn from** the mishna that this is Rabbi Yehuda's opinion.

The *gemara*'s *diyuk* in the *mishnah* further clarifies Rabbi Yehuda's position: even if only one tribe sinned, as long as it followed the ruling of the *beis din hagadol*, that single tribe brings its *korban* — and the remaining eleven tribes bring as well through the principle of *grira*. The *beis din hagadol* is the key factor that makes any tribal sin a matter of national concern.

## Shimon Single Tribe

### Shimon Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: שֵׁבֶט אֶחָד שֶׁעָשָׂה בְּהוֹרָאַת בֵּית דִּין הַגָּדוֹל, לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מִי מַיְיתֵי אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: In a case **where one tribe performed** a transgression **on** the basis of **the ruling of the High Court, according to Rabbi Shimon are they** liable to **bring** an offering **or not?**

The *gemara* now raises the parallel question for Rabbi Shimon: *shevet echad l'Rebbi Shimon b'horu beis din hagadol, mi mayisi o lo?* — does Rabbi Shimon hold that even a single tribe brings when the *beis din hagadol* has ruled? Rabbi Shimon has to this point only spoken in terms of majority scenarios. Does his individual tribal obligation extend to a case where only one tribe sinned?

### One or Two

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מָה הֵן מְבִיאִין? פַּר אֶחָד, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵי פָרִים. בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא שֶׁחָטְאוּ שִׁבְעָה – שְׁנֵי פָרִים? שְׁמֹנָה פָּרִים בָּעֵי! אֶלָּא דְּחָטָא שֵׁבֶט אֶחָד, וּבְמַאי? אִילֵימָא בְּהוֹרָאַת בֵּית דִּינוֹ, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לֵית לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא בְּהוֹרָאַת בֵּית דִּין הַגָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution to this dilemma from the *baraita* cited earlier: **What do they bring?** They bring **one bull; Rabbi Shimon says: Two bulls.** The Gemara elaborates: **What are we dealing with** in this *baraita*? **If we say** that the reference is to a case **where seven** tribes **sinned,** that is difficult. In that case, are **two bulls** brought? **Eight bulls are required** according to Rabbi Shimon. **Rather,** the reference is to a case **where one tribe sinned.** The Gemara clarifies: **And in what** circumstances did the tribe sin? **If we say** that the tribe sinned **on** the basis of **the ruling of its** tribal **court, Rabbi Shimon does not accept** the *halakha* that an erroneous ruling of the tribal court renders them liable to bring an offering. **Rather,** the circumstances are that they sinned **on** the basis of **a ruling of the High Court.** Apparently, even Rabbi Shimon agrees that in this case one tribe brings an offering.

To resolve this, the *gemara* revisits the *baraisa* that records a dispute between an unnamed *tanna kamma* who requires one *korban* and Rabbi Shimon who requires two. *Bemaei askinan?* — what is the case? If seven tribes sinned, Rabbi Shimon would require eight — one per tribe plus one for *beis din*. So it cannot be seven tribes. Therefore it must be one tribe sinning.

### Mixed Majority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרָא: תַּנָּא קַמָּא מַנִּי? אִי רַבִּי מֵאִיר – הָא רוּבָּא בָּעֵי, אִי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה – שְׁאָר שְׁבָטִים נָמֵי מַיְיתוּ! אֶלָּא הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר הִיא, וְכִדְתַנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof and **says** there is an alternative explanation of the *baraita*. **What are we dealing with here?** With a case **where six tribes sinned and** in terms of population **they** constitute **a majority of** the **congregation, and** the first *tanna* in the *baraita* **is Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says in the name of** Rabbi Meir: If **six tribes sinned and** in terms of population **they** constitute **a majority of** the **congregation, or** if **seven** tribes, which constitute a majority of the tribes, sinned, **even though** in terms of population **they do not** constitute **a majority of** the **congregation,** the court **brings a bull.**

But then: what is the *tanna kamma*'s position? If it is Rabbi Meir, he requires a majority, which is absent here. If it is Rabbi Yehuda, then based on the *grira* principle just established, all twelve tribes should bring, not just one. Neither classic position produces a clean reading where the *tanna kamma* says "one." The *gemara* therefore proposes reading the *baraisa* as the case of six tribes that constitute the majority of the population, or seven tribes that do not — the special mixed-majority case of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar. In that reading, the *tanna kamma* (Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar) would say one *korban* for the *beis din*, since this counts as sufficient majority for that obligation, while Rabbi Shimon would add one more for all the tribes combined. However, this reading produces a conceptually strange result: if a full majority of tribes sin, each tribe brings individually; but if it is this mixed majority-minority configuration, all tribes are lumped together as a single unit. This inversion is difficult to account for, unless one follows Rashi's reading that the scenario involves one tribe that itself constitutes the majority of *Klal Yisrael*'s population — in which case the logic is cleaner, since that one tribe's sin is in effect the majority by the population measure, and one for *beis din* plus one for the tribe yields two.

### Central Court Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לְעוֹלָם אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל הוֹרָאַת בֵּית דִּין הַגָּדוֹל. מַאן חֲכָמִים? אִילֵימָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר, רוּבָּא בָּעֵי! אֶלָּא לָאו, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא! שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution to the dilemma from the mishna, which teaches: **And the Rabbis say: One** tribe **is never liable, other than for** violating a transgression on the basis of **the ruling of the High Court.** The Gemara clarifies: **Who are the Rabbis** cited in the mishna? **If we say** that the Rabbis are **Rabbi Meir, he requires** that **a majority** perform a transgression in order to be liable. **Rather, is it not** that the Rabbis are **Rabbi Shimon?** The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from it** that Rabbi Shimon holds that one tribe that performed a transgression on the basis of the ruling of the High Court is liable to bring a bull.

*Tashma* — the *gemara* finds a cleaner resolution. *Vachachamim omrim l'olam eino chayav ela al hora'at beis din hagadol* — the *chachamim* at the end of the *mishnah* say the obligation exists only through a ruling of the *beis din hagadol*. *Man chachamim?* — who are these *chachamim*? If it is Rabbi Meir, the issue is not merely the absence of *beis din hagadol* but also the absence of a majority of *Klal Yisrael* sinning. The *chachamim*'s formulation suggests that the single correction needed is the type of *beis din*, not the proportion of sinners. *Ella lav Rebbi Shimon hi* — therefore it must be Rabbi Shimon speaking as *chachamim*: Rabbi Shimon agrees that an individual tribe can bring its *korban*, but insists that a ruling of the *beis din hagadol* is required to generate that obligation.

### Conclusion

For Rabbi Shimon, when the *beis din hagadol* rules and even one tribe follows and sins, that tribe is obligated to bring its *korban*. The *beis din hagadol*'s ruling is the necessary and sufficient jurisdictional condition for the tribal *korban* obligation to apply, even at the level of a single *shevet*.

## Kahal Textual

### Joshua Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, שֵׁבֶט אֶחָד דְּאִקְּרִי קָהָל מְנָא לְהוּ? אָמְרִי, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּעֲמֹד יְהוֹשָׁפָט בִּקְהַל יְהוּדָה וִירוּשָׁלִַם (נָכַח חֲצַר בֵּית ה׳) [בְּבֵית ה׳ לִפְנֵי הֶחָצֵר] הַחֲדָשָׁה״. מַאי ״חֲדָשָׁה״? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, שֶׁחִידְּשׁוּ דְּבָרִים וְאָמְרוּ: טְבוּל יוֹם, אַל יִכָּנֵס בְּמַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § **And Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon,** who hold one tribe that performs a transgression liable, **from where do they** derive that **one tribe is characterized as a congregation?** The Sages **say** that it is derived from a verse, **as it is written: "And Jehoshaphat stood in the congregation of Judah and Jerusalem before the new courtyard in the house of the Lord"** (II Chronicles 20:5). The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of **"new"** in this verse? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says that** on that occasion **they introduced new matters and said: One** who was ritually impure **who immersed that day** and is waiting for nightfall for the purification process to be completed **may not enter** even **the Levite camp.** From the fact that the verse employs the term "congregation" in reference to Judah and Jerusalem, it can be inferred that one tribe is characterized as a congregation.

The *gemara* now turns to the textual question underlying all of the above: where do Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon derive the idea that an individual tribe can itself be called a *kahal*? The first source cited: *d'chsiv v'yaamod Yehoshafat bikahal Yehuda viYerushalayim b'veis Hashem lifnei hachatzer hachadasha* — Yehoshafat stood in the *kahal* of Yehudah and Yerushalayim. *Mai chadasha, amar Rabbi Yochanan* — what was new about this *chatzer*? Rabbi Yochanan explains: they enacted a new ruling that a *tvul yom* may not enter the *machaneh Leviyah*. In any event, the verse uses the word *kahal* to refer to the tribe of Yehudah, suggesting that a single tribe constitutes a *kahal*.

### Jerusalem Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא שָׁאנֵי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם, דַּהֲוָה נָמֵי בִּנְיָמִין! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלַי הִנְנִי מַפְרְךָ וְהִרְבִּיתִךָ וּנְתַתִּיךָ לִקְהַל עַמִּים וְגוֹ׳״ מַאן אִתְיְלִיד לֵיהּ הָהִיא שַׁעְתָּא? בִּנְיָמִין, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ הָכִי קָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: מִתְיְלִיד לָךְ הַשְׁתָּא קָהָל אַחֲרִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov objects to this: From where** do you draw this conclusion? **Perhaps Jerusalem is different, as** Jerusalem **was also** in the tribal land of **Benjamin.** Accordingly, it was shared by two tribes. **Rather, Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said** that proof that a tribe is characterized as a congregation is derived from another verse, **as it is written** that Jacob recounted what God said to him when he returned from Paddan: **"And said to me: Behold, I will make you fruitful and multiply you, and I will make of you a congregation of peoples"** (Genesis 48:4). **Who was born to him** from **that time** on? Only **Benjamin. Conclude from it** that **this** is what **the Merciful One is saying** to Jacob: **Another congregation shall be now born to you.** Apparently, one tribe is characterized as a congregation.

*Masif lah Rav Acha bar Yaakov* — Rav Acha bar Yaakov challenges this proof: since the verse says "Yehudah and Yerushalayim," and Yerushalayim included a portion belonging to the tribe of Binyamin, perhaps you need at least two tribes present for the designation *kahal* to apply. The verse cannot prove that a single tribe alone is called *kahal*.

Rav Acha bar Yaakov therefore offers an alternative source: *d'chsiv v'yomer elai hineni mafracha v'hirbisicha u'nesaticha likahal amim* — the angel says to Yaakov, "I will make you fruitful and multiply you and make you a *kahal* of nations." *Man d'asid d'misyalid lei b'hahu sha'ata* — who was about to be born to Yaakov at that moment? Binyamin. Therefore the *Torah* is saying that from Yaakov another *kahal* would emerge — Binyamin — and from this one sees that a single tribe is called a *kahal*.

### Twelve Tribes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב שַׁבָּא לְרַב כָּהֲנָא, דִּלְמָא הָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא: לְכִי מִתְיְלִיד לָךְ בִּנְיָמִין, הוּא דְּהָווּ שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר שְׁבָטִים דְּמִתְקַרְיַית קָהָל! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֶלָּא שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר שְׁבָטִים אִיקְּרוּ קָהָל, אַחַד עָשָׂר שְׁבָטִים לֹא אִיקְּרוּ קָהָל?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shabba said to Rav Kahana: Perhaps this** is what **the Merciful One is saying** to Jacob: **It is when Benjamin is born to you that you will have twelve tribes, who are** together **characterized as a congregation.** Rav Kahana **said to him: But** is that to say that **twelve tribes are characterized as a congregation,** and **eleven tribes are not characterized as a congregation?** It has already been established that even two tribes are characterized as a congregation. Rather, it is apparent from this verse that even one tribe is characterized as a congregation.

*Amar lei Rav Shisha breh de'Rav Kahana* — Rav Shisha son of Rav Kahana raises a challenge: perhaps the verse means that when Binyamin is born, completing the twelve tribes, then *nesaticha likahal amim* — all twelve together will be a *kahal*. The verse would then be saying that the full complement of twelve constitutes a *kahal*, not that Binyamin alone is one.

The *gemara* rejects this reading: *ella yod-beis shvatim ikru kahal, yod-aleph shvatim lo ikru kahal?* — if the verse means that only all twelve together are called a *kahal*, does it follow that eleven tribes are not a *kahal*? Surely everyone would agree that a majority of seven tribes is called a *kahal*. Therefore the reading that only twelve together constitute a *kahal* is untenable. Since that reading must be rejected, the verse can only mean what it straightforwardly suggests: that Binyamin himself — a single tribe — is being called a *kahal*. *Shema mina* — this establishes the textual basis for the ruling that an individual tribe is a *kahal*, and therefore capable of bearing its own *korban* obligation as a corporate unit.
