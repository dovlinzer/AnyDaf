# Keritot 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Human Blood Status

### Blood Categories Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּפֵירַשׁ. כִּי קָאָמַר רַב – בִּדְלָא פֵּירַשׁ, כִּדְתַנְיָא: דָּם שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי כִּכָּר – גּוֹרְרוֹ וְאוֹכְלוֹ, שֶׁל בֵּין הַשִּׁינַּיִם – מוֹצֵץ וּבוֹלֵעַ וְאֵינוֹ חוֹשֵׁשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **where** the blood was **separated** from a person. By contrast, **when Rav** Sheshet **said** that one need not abstain from human blood even *ab initio*, he was referring to a case **where** it was **not separated** from a person. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If **blood was on a loaf** of bread, one **may scrape off** the blood **and** then **consume** the bread. If blood **was between** the **teeth, he may suck** it **and swallow** it **without concern.**

The current *perek* opens with the *parsha* of *dam shechita*, concerned primarily with which categories of blood fall under the *issur* of blood consumption. The *mishnah* at the bottom of 21b establishes that the blood of humans, fish, and grasshoppers is permitted. The discussion of fish blood invoked the concept of *maris ayin*: although the language of the *mishnah* was *assur*, the context indicated a rabbinic concern that fish blood gathered into a cup resembles forbidden animal blood — a concern dispelled if the fish scales are kept nearby. Human blood was similarly characterized as *assur*, though the precise degree of that prohibition requires further analysis. There are essentially two distinct cases: blood pooled into a cup, which genuinely resembles animal blood, and blood that has left the body but is not gathered, such as blood visible on a loaf of bread (*dam al ha-kikar*).

### Rav Sheshes on Human Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: דַּם מְהַלְּכֵי שְׁתַּיִם – אֲפִילּוּ מִצְוַת פְּרוֹשׁ אֵין בּוֹ. מֵיתִיבִי: דַּם הַטְּחוֹל, דַּם הַלֵּב, דַּם הַכְּלָיוֹת, דַּם אֵבָרִים – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה. דַּם מְהַלְּכֵי שְׁתַּיִם, דַּם שְׁרָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים – אָסוּר וְאֵין חַיָּיבִ[ין] עָלָיו! כִּי תַּנְיָא אָסוּר –
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet says:** With regard to the **blood** of **bipeds, there is not even a command to abstain** from consuming it *ab initio*. The Gemara **raises an objection** from the *baraita*: **Blood of the spleen, blood of the heart, blood of the kidneys,** and **blood of limbs,** all **these are** forbidden for consumption **by** a regular **prohibition.** By contrast, the **blood of bipeds,** i.e., human beings, and the **blood of creeping animals and crawling things** are **forbidden but one is not liable for** their consumption. The Gemara answers: **When** it **is taught** in that *baraita* that human blood **is forbidden,** that is referring to a case

On this question, Rav Sheshes states that for *dam mehalechei shtayim* — the blood of bipeds — not even a *mitzvas prisha* applies. This is a striking formulation: Rav Sheshes is not merely saying that human blood carries no biblical or even rabbinic prohibition in the technical sense, but that it is not even subject to the kind of religious fastidiousness or unseemly quality that would otherwise attach to it. There is no requirement to distance oneself from it whatsoever.

### Braita on Blood Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְמַתְנֵי לַהּ לְהָא דְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת עַל הָדָא, דְּתַנְיָא: יָכוֹל חֲלֵב מְהַלְּכֵי שְׁתַּיִם יְהֵא אוֹכְלוֹ עוֹבֵר בְּלָאו? וְדִין הוּא: מָה בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה שֶׁהֵקַלְתָּה בְּמַגָּעָהּ, הֶחְמַרְתָּה בַּחֲלָבָהּ, מְהַלְּכֵי שְׁתַּיִם שֶׁהֶחְמַרְתָּה בְּמַגָּעָן – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁתַּחְמִיר בַּחֲלָבָן?
>
> **Translation:** **Some teach this** statement **of Rav Sheshet with regard to this** *baraita*, **as it is taught:** One **might** have thought that **one who consumes** the **milk** of **bipeds would violate a prohibition. And** this conclusion could be derived based upon **a logical inference: Just as** with regard to **a non-kosher animal, where you were lenient with regard to its contact,** i.e., it cannot render people or items impure through contact when it is alive, **you were stringent with regard to its milk,** which is prohibited, so too in the case of **bipeds, where you were stringent with regard to their contact,** as living people can render other people and objects impure, **isn't it logical that you should be stringent with regard to their milk?**

The *gemara* challenges this with the *braita* cited the previous day: the blood of the *tichol*, the heart (*dam ha-lev*), the kidneys (*dam ha-klayos*), and the other organs (*dam ha-eivarim*) — these carry a *lo ta'aseh*, a negative biblical prohibition, even though they are not *dam ha-neshama*, the life blood. Then the *braita* continues: *dam mehalechei shtayim*, the blood of bipeds, as well as *dam shratzim ve-remasim*, the blood of rodents and creeping creatures, is *assur* — but one does not incur *chiyuv* for it. That is, it is not merely that *kares* does not apply (which was the ruling for organ blood); rather, not even a negative biblical prohibition is transgressed. Nevertheless, it is still *assur* — apparently at a rabbinic level. The blood of rodents, it should be noted, may well be biblically forbidden, but as part of the rodent itself rather than as *blood*. Yet human blood appears clearly from this *braita* to carry at minimum a rabbinic prohibition. How, then, can Rav Sheshes say there is no problem whatsoever?

### Separation Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְזֶה לָכֶם הַטָּמֵא״ – זֶה טָמֵא, וְאֵין חֲלֵב מְהַלְּכֵי שְׁתַּיִם טָמֵא אֶלָּא טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states: "And these are they which are impure to you"** (Leviticus 11:29), which indicates that **these** animals enumerated in that chapter are **non-kosher** and their milk is forbidden for consumption, **but the milk of bipeds is not impure** but **rather pure,** i.e., kosher.

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *ki tanya assur*, when the *braita* says human blood is forbidden, that refers specifically to blood that has separated from the body (*de-peirash*). When Rav Sheshes said there is not even a *mitzvas prisha*, he was speaking of blood that has not yet left the body (*de-lo peirash*). The *gemara* then cites the relevant distinction: *dam she-al gabei ha-kikar*, blood that has gotten onto a loaf of bread, must be scraped off before eating. But *she-bein ha-shinnayim*, blood still caught between the teeth and within the mouth, one may simply suck out and swallow without concern. The principle, then, is that once blood separates from the body it becomes problematic, and the statement of Rav Sheshes applies only to blood still within the body.

This leaves a certain ambiguity in the middle case: blood that has left the body but is not gathered into a vessel — the *kikar* case. Is the *kikar* case categorized as a full *issur de-rabbanan* similar to blood pooled in a cup, or is it merely the level of *mitzvas prisha*, an aesthetic-religious impropriety short of technical prohibition? The *gemara*'s resolution does not fully resolve that question. A related practical question raised by the *poskim* is the case of a bleeding finger: if one sucks or licks one's own finger, the blood is coming from outside the mouth into the mouth, yet it is arguably still connected to the body. The precise definition of *prisha* — separation — in that case is a matter of discussion.

### Milk vs. Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹצִיא אֶת הֶחָלָב, שֶׁאֵין שָׁוֶה בַּכֹּל, וְלֹא אוֹצִיא הַדָּם שֶׁשָּׁוֶה בַּכֹּל! תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְזֶה לָכֶם הַטָּמֵא״ – זֶה טָמֵא, וְאֵין דַּם מְהַלְּכֵי שְׁתַּיִם טָמֵא אֶלָּא טָהוֹר. אָמַר (לה) רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אֲפִילּוּ מִצְוַת פְּרוֹשׁ אֵין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, one might have thought that **I should exclude** only **the milk** of women from forbidden status, as the forbidden status of milk **does not** apply **equally to all** creatures, since the milk of kosher animals is permitted, but **I should not exclude** human **blood** from the prohibition against consuming blood, **which** does apply **equally to all** creatures, since even the blood of kosher animals is forbidden. Consequently, **the verse states: "And these are they which are impure to you,"** which indicates that **this is impure, but the blood of bipeds is not impure** but **rather pure,** i.e., kosher. It was in this context that **Rav Sheshet said:** With regard to the blood of bipeds, **there is not even a command to abstain** from consuming it *ab initio*.

Some authorities attach the statement of Rav Sheshes not to our *mishnah* but to a different *braita*, one that appears in the *gemara* of Ketubot and concerns the status of human milk. That *braita* raises a *kal va-chomer*: a non-*kosher* animal, even though its corpse creates less *tumah* than a human corpse, still has its milk forbidden by a negative prohibition. Human beings, whose corpse generates far greater *tumah* than an animal carcass — as *zavim*, *niddos*, and in various other ways — would seemingly have their milk forbidden *a fortiori*, and perhaps even with a *lav*. The *braita* rejects this: the verse says *ve-zeh lachem ha-tamei*, "this is impure for you," referring specifically to the animal world. *Zeh tamei* — only these categories of animals generate the prohibitions of forbidden food, and therefore milk of humans is not *tamei* but *tahor* and entirely permitted.

The same verse is then applied to human blood. One might have thought that since blood is uniformly forbidden across all animals, human blood should be no exception. The *braita* teaches otherwise: *zeh tamei* — the prohibition is specifically within the animal world, and human blood is likewise *tahor* by *din Torah*. This is a different *drasha* from the *klal u-frat u-klal* derivation cited elsewhere, but both arrive at the same conclusion: human blood carries no biblical prohibition. It is on this *braita* that Rav Sheshes states his ruling — *afilu mitzvas prisha ein bo*, there is not even a duty of fastidiousness — declaring that even the most minimal concern does not apply to human blood.

The comparison to human milk raises an interesting parallel. The *gemara* in Ketubot discusses whether a child, after being weaned, may return to nurse from his mother, using the language *k'ilu yonek sheketz*, as if one were nursing from something repugnant. This suggests that the concept of *prisha* is not simply *maris ayin* — it does not depend on whether something resembles forbidden blood or milk. There is a separate category of seemliness and religious appropriateness. This same quality applies to human blood in certain contexts, and the *gemara* in Ketubot explores whether it applies to human milk as well.

## Life Blood Definition

### Heart Blood Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: הַלֵּב, קוֹרְעוֹ וּמוֹצִיא אֶת דָּמוֹ, לֹא קְרָעוֹ – אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר עָלָיו. אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּלֵב עוֹף, הוֹאִיל וְאֵין בּוֹ כְּזַיִת, אֲבָל לֵב בְּהֵמָה דְּיֵשׁ בּוֹ כְּזַיִת – אָסוּר, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** § **We learned** in a mishna **there** (*Ḥullin* 109a): One who wants to eat **the heart** of a slaughtered animal **tears it and removes its blood,** and only then may he cook it and eat it. If **he did not tear** the heart before cooking it and he ate it, **he does not violate** the prohibition and is not liable to receive *karet* for consuming blood. The Gemara comments: **Rabbi Zeira says** that **Rav says: They taught** this **only with regard to the heart of a bird, since it does not contain an olive-bulk** of blood. **But the heart of an animal, which does contain an olive-bulk** of blood, **is forbidden and one is liable** to receive ***karet* for its** consumption.

The *gemara* now cites a *mishnah* from Chullin: *ha-lev kor'o u-motzi es damo* — the heart of a slaughtered animal must be cut open and its blood expressed. If one did not do so, one does not transgress a prohibition retroactively, but the requirement is to make an incision and remove the blood from the heart cavity before eating. This *mishnah* provides the entry point for a broader discussion of *dam ha-neshama*, the life blood.

### Heart Blood Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵעָלְמָא מֵהֵיכָא אָתֵי לֵיהּ? אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהַנְּשָׁמָה יוֹצְאָה מִישְׂרָף שָׂרֵיף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where does** blood **come to** the heart **from outside? Rabbi Zeira said: At the time when the soul departs,** i.e., when the animal is dying, the heart **draws [*sharif* ]** blood from elsewhere, and one is liable to receive *karet* for consuming this blood.

The *gemara* asks a remarkable physiological question: where did the blood in the heart come from? This question makes more sense in light of the ancient understanding of cardiac anatomy. The *gemara* and *Rashi* reflect a view, common to classical antiquity and maintained well into the early modern period, that the heart is not an organ of blood circulation but rather an organ of breathing. According to this view, breath enters through the lungs, passes to the heart to be warmed, and then is distributed throughout the body. Indeed, the *Chacham Tzvi*, who wrote an extensive *teshuvah* on the significance of the heart in the context of a famous chicken case, speaks consistently of the heart as circulating *breath*, not blood. It was only William Harvey, approximately four hundred years ago, who demonstrated that the heart circulates blood, and even then the halakhic literature was slow to incorporate this understanding.

Within this framework, the phrase *mishas ha-neshama yotze'ah* — from the moment the *neshama* departs — becomes precisely comprehensible. *Neshama* is closely related to *neshima*, breathing. When the animal dies, the breath (*neshama*) that was in the heart exits, creating a vacuum, and blood is thereby drawn into the heart. The *gemara*'s question is therefore not asking how life blood enters the heart as a matter of circulation; it is asking how any blood at all came to be in the heart, given that the heart's function was understood to be respiratory. The answer — that the exit of the breath at death creates a vacuum that draws blood in — is the *gemara*'s own explanation of what *dam ha-neshama* means in the context of the heart.

### Death Definition

This discussion connects naturally to the halakhic definition of death, which throughout the *gemara* and classical authorities is consistently defined in terms of breathing rather than cardiac activity. The relevant texts, from *Rashi* forward, define death by the cessation of spontaneous respiration. Where a *Rashi* mentions the heart in the context of checking for signs of life, even there the heart is conceived as part of the respiratory system, not as an independent circulatory organ. Any mention of the heart in pre-modern halakhic literature must be understood against this background: those authorities were discussing the heart as they understood it — a breathing apparatus — not as modern medicine understands it.

This has direct implications for contemporary discussions of brain death and organ donation. The practical question in organ donation is whether death may be declared upon cessation of breathing even if the heart continues to beat. The heart has an internal pacemaker and will continue to beat independently even after respiration ceases; if one waits for cardiac arrest, the other organs are deprived of oxygen and become unsuitable for donation. If the halakhic definition of death is cessation of spontaneous breathing — as every *gemara* and every classical *posek* states — then the question of brain death as an alternative criterion becomes in many ways beside the point. The real halakhic question is not brain death versus breathing, but whether both breathing *and* circulation are required, or only breathing. And on this point, the *gemara* and all the *rishonim* speak exclusively of breathing.

### Bloodletting Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּם שֶׁהַנְּשָׁמָה יוֹצְאָה בּוֹ – חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו. אִיתְּמַר: אֵיזֶהוּ דַּם הַקָּזָה שֶׁהַנְּשָׁמָה תְּלוּיָה בּוֹ? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמְּקַלֵּחַ. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: מִטִּיפָּה הַמַּשְׁחֶרֶת וְאֵילָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: With regard to **blood** that spurted during bloodletting with **which the soul departs, one is liable** to receive *karet* **for** consuming **it** intentionally or to bring a sin offering for consuming it unwittingly. **It was stated: What** is defined as the **blood** that spurts during **bloodletting upon which the soul depends,** whose consumption entails *karet*? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: As long as it is spurting** forcefully, it is included in this category of blood. **Reish Lakish says: From the** last **black drop and onward,** i.e., even before the blood spurts out forcefully, it is included in this category. The first drops of blood are black, and once these have ended and the first red drops have begun to emerge, it is the blood upon which the soul depends.

Having established what *dam ha-neshama* means in the context of an animal dying, the *gemara* turns to the parallel case during bloodletting (*hakazah*). During bloodletting, the animal does not die, so one cannot speak literally of blood "departing with the *neshama*." The relevant formulation therefore shifts to *dam she-ha-neshama teluyah bo* — blood upon which the *neshama* is dependent, i.e., life blood. What exactly is that during the process of bloodletting?

The process of bloodletting, as described by the *gemara* and *Rashi*, proceeds in stages: first a small drip, then a powerful squirting flow (*kilu'ach*), then a dripping tail at the end. There is also mention of an initial black drop (*tipa mashcheret*) at the outset, before the red blood begins.

Rabbi Yochanan holds that only the blood in the squirting stage constitutes *dam ha-neshama*; the initial and final dripping stages are *dam tamtzit*, non-life blood. Resh Lakish holds that from the black drop onward (*mi-tipa mashcheret va-eilach*) — meaning once the red blood begins, even if it is only dripping — it is life blood.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: אֵיזֶהוּ דַּם הַקָּזָה שֶׁהַנְּשָׁמָה יוֹצְאָה בּוֹ? כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמְּקַלֵּחַ. יָצָא דַּם הַתַּמְצִית, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא שׁוֹתֵת. מַאי לַָאו אֲפִילּוּ רִאשׁוֹן וְאַחֲרוֹן, דְּשׁוֹתֵת הוּא – כְּדַם הַתַּמְצִית דָּמֵי? תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **What is** defined as **blood** that spurts during **bloodletting with which the soul departs? As long as it is spurting** forcefully, it is considered this type of blood. This **excludes blood of exudate,** i.e., that oozes from the animal after the spurt concludes, **because** at this stage the blood merely **flows** rather than spurting forcefully. The Gemara infers: **What, is it not** correct to say that **even** with regard to the **first and last** appearances of blood to emerge, if **it flows it is considered as blood of exudate?** This is apparently **a conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Reish Lakish.**

The *gemara* challenges Resh Lakish from a *braita*: *kol zman she-mekale'ach* — only as long as the blood is squirting is it life blood; *yatza dam ha-tamtzit*, excluded is the dripping blood, *mipnei she-hu shotet*, because it only drips. This *braita* appears to support Rabbi Yochanan fully, contradicting Resh Lakish. The *gemara* attempts to reinterpret the *braita* on behalf of Resh Lakish: the *braita* is actually excluding the black blood (*dam ha-mashchir*), while conceding that the initial and final red dripping blood — even though it only drips — is still *dam ha-nefesh*. However, it is acknowledged that this reading is difficult to sustain from the plain language of the *braita*. Resh Lakish is ultimately left responding *tannai* — admitting that his position corresponds to a minority opinion among the *tannaim*, specifically that of Rabbi Shimon, while Rabbi Yochanan's position corresponds to that of Rabbi Elazar (or Rabbi, according to some variants).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְמַעוֹטֵי דָּם הַמַּשְׁחִיר, אֲבָל דָּם רִאשׁוֹן וְאַחֲרוֹן, אַף עַל גַּב דְּהוּא שׁוֹתֵת – הַיְינוּ דַּם הַנֶּפֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** the *baraita* serves **to exclude** only **black blood; but** with regard to the **first and last** appearances of red **blood,** which emerge, respectively, after the black blood but before the blood spurts and after the blood is no longer spurting forcefully, **even though it flows, this is** considered **blood** with which **the soul** departs.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: אֵיזֶהוּ דַּם רִאשׁוֹן? כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמְּקַלֵּחַ. יָצָא רִאשׁוֹן וְאַחֲרוֹן, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא שׁוֹתֵת. תְּיוּבְתֵּיהּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ! אָמַר לָךְ: תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: אֵיזֶהוּ דַּם הַנֶּפֶשׁ? כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁמְּקַלֵּחַ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מִטִּיפָּה הַמַּשְׁחֶרֶת וְאֵילָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a different *baraita*: **What is** defined as the **first** appearance of **blood? As long as it is spurting** forcefully, it is considered the first appearance of blood. This **excludes** the **first and last** appearances of blood, **because** that blood **flows.** This is apparently **a conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Reish Lakish.** The Gemara answers: Reish Lakish could **say to you** that this matter **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **What is the blood** with which **the soul** departs? **As long as it is spurting** out, it is considered this type of blood; this is **the statement of Rabbi Elazar. Rabbi Shimon says: From the** last **black drop and onward** it is considered blood with which the soul departs.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״וְדַם חֲלָלִים יִשְׁתֶּה״, פְּרָט לְדָם קִילּוּחַ שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַכְשִׁיר אֶת הַזְּרָעִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara mentions a related *halakha* involving blood. **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: "And drinks the blood of the slain"** (Numbers 23:24). This teaches that the blood that is considered a halakhically recognized liquid, and therefore renders other items susceptible to ritual impurity, is only blood that is exuded after death. This serves to **exclude blood** that **spurts** out forcefully at the time of the slaughter, before the animal is actually dead. Consequently, this type of blood **does not render seeds susceptible to** ritual impurity.

A further *braita* is cited from the school of Rabbi Yishmael, deriving from the verse *ve-dam chalalim yishte* — "and they will drink the blood of corpses" — that blood is classified as a liquid capable of rendering produce susceptible to *tumah* (*machshir*). The *gemara* specifies that this applies to post-mortem blood of humans, while the squirting blood (*dam kilu'ach*), which is life blood, is excluded from being *machshir*. Tosafot notes that the rule is the reverse for *behema*: there, the squirting blood is *machshir*, derived from a separate *pasuk* (*al ha-aretz tishpachenu ka-mayim*), while the post-mortem blood is not. One suggested explanation for why the initial drop appears black is that a small drop coagulates and darkens far more rapidly than a larger volume of blood — an interesting observation that fits the *gemara*'s description well.

### Two Vessel Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה מֵרַבִּי זֵירָא: הִקִּיז דָּם לִבְהֵמָה וְקִיבֵּל דָּמָהּ בִּשְׁנֵי כוֹסוֹת, מַהוּ? עַל הָרִאשׁוֹן – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל חַיָּיב, עַל הָאַחֲרוֹן – מִי מִיחַיַּיב אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the prohibition against consuming blood. **Rabbi Yirmeya raised a dilemma before Rabbi Zeira:** If **one performed bloodletting upon an animal and received its blood in two cups,** from which he drank, **what is** the *halakha*? **With regard to the first** cup, **everyone agrees** that he is **liable,** as it contains blood that spurted forcefully. **With regard to the last** cup, which does not contain blood that spurted forcefully from the animal, **is he liable or not?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פְּלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאִיתְּמַר: הִקִּיז דָּם לִבְהֵמָה וְקִיבֵּל דָּמָהּ בִּשְׁנֵי כוֹסוֹת – רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: חַיָּיב שְׁתֵּי חַטָּאוֹת. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira **said to him:** This is **a dispute** between **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish, as it was stated:** If **one performed bloodletting upon an animal and received its blood in two cups, Reish Lakish says** that he is **liable** to bring **two sin offerings** if he drank the contents of the cups in two lapses of awareness, as one is liable to receive *karet* even for blood that emerges after spurting; **and Rabbi Yoḥanan says he is liable** to bring **only one** sin offering, for the blood in the first cup.

Rami bar Chama posed a question to Rabbi Zeira: if one lets blood from an animal and collects it into two vessels — the first filling up and then a second being used — what is the law? Rabbi Zeira replies that this is simply a direct application of the dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish. According to Resh Lakish, both vessels contain life blood (since the red blood throughout is *dam ha-neshama*), and therefore if one ate from both, one would be liable for two *chatta'ot*. According to Rabbi Yochanan, only the first vessel — which primarily contains the squirting blood — consists of life blood, while the second vessel contains only the dripping end-blood, which is *dam tamtzit*. Consequently, Rabbi Yochanan holds one is liable only one *chattat*, for consumption of the first vessel.

## Rabbi Yehuda's View

### All Blood Kares

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מְחַיֵּיב בְּדַם הַתַּמְצִית. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְעִנְיַן כַּפָּרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי הַדָּם הוּא בַּנֶּפֶשׁ יְכַפֵּר״ – דָּם שֶׁהַנֶּפֶשׁ יוֹצְאָה בּוֹ מְכַפֵּר, וְשֶׁאֵין הַנֶּפֶשׁ יוֹצְאָה בּוֹ אֵינוֹ מְכַפֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that one is not liable for consuming blood of exudate, i.e., that oozes from the neck of the animal after the initial spurt of blood concludes. **Rabbi Yehuda deems** one **liable in** the case of **blood of exudate.** The Gemara adds that **Rabbi Elazar says: Rabbi Yehuda concedes with regard to atonement** that if one sacrifices an offering and the priest sprinkles the blood of exudate on the corners of the altar, the blood does not atone. This is **as it is stated: "For it is the blood that makes atonement by reason of the soul"** (Leviticus 17:11), which teaches that **blood with which the soul departs,** i.e., the blood that spurts immediately upon slaughter, **atones, but** blood **with which the soul does not depart,** i.e., the blood that drains out after the initial spurt, **does not atone.**

Having concluded the discussion of the bloodletting dispute, the *gemara* notes the position of Rabbi Yehuda: *Rabbi Yehuda mechayev be-dam ha-tamtzit*. Rabbi Yehuda holds that one incurs *kares* not only for consuming *dam ha-neshama* but for all blood of animals — including *dam tamtzit*, the non-life blood. The question of exactly how broadly this extends — whether it includes blood absorbed within the walls of organs — is left somewhat open, but any blood that exits through the veins is not subject to the distinction between life blood and non-life blood according to Rabbi Yehuda.

### Atonement Blood

Rabbi Elazar clarifies an important caveat: *modeh Rabbi Yehuda le-inyan kapparah*. Even though Rabbi Yehuda treats all blood equally for purposes of the *kares* prohibition on consumption, he agrees that for purposes of atonement — i.e., blood used in sacrificial service on the *mizbe'ach* — only life blood qualifies. The *pasuk* states *ki ha-dam hu ba-nefesh yechapper*: the blood atones through the *nefesh*, through its life force. Therefore *dam she-ha-nefesh yotze'ah bo mekhapper*, only blood with which the *neshama* departs can effect atonement, *ve-she-eino ha-nefesh yotze'ah bo eino mekhapper*. The two categories are conceptually maintained even in Rabbi Yehuda's system; they differ only in their practical consequence for eating.

### Sifra Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״דָּם״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״כׇּל דָּם״?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: We learn** this in a *baraita*, **as well, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that the verse states: "And any man from the house of Israel, or from the stranger who resides among them, who consumes any blood, I will set My face against that soul that consumes blood, and will cut him off from among his people" (Leviticus 17:10). The verse could simply have stated **"blood"; what** is the meaning when **the verse states "any blood"?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי הַדָּם הוּא בַּנֶּפֶשׁ יְכַפֵּר״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא דַּם קָדָשִׁים שֶׁהַנֶּפֶשׁ יוֹצְאָה בּוֹ, שֶׁהוּא מְכַפֵּר, דַּם חוּלִּין וְדַם הַתַּמְצִית, מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כׇּל דָּם״. סְתָם סִיפְרָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* explains: **Since it is stated: "For it is the blood that makes atonement by reason of the soul," I have** derived **nothing other than** the fact that consuming the **blood of sacrificial** animals **with which the soul departs** renders one liable to receive *karet*, **as** this blood **atones. From where** is it derived that the same applies to **blood of non-sacred** animals **and blood of exudate? The verse states: "Any blood." Who is** presumed to be the author of any **unattributed** *baraita* found in the ***Sifra*,** a collection of halakhic midrash on Leviticus, which is the source of this *baraita*? **It is Rabbi Yehuda.** This proves that according to Rabbi Yehuda, blood of exudate does not effect atonement.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak supports this reading from a *braita* that tracks with the *Sifra*, the halakhic *midrash* on Vayikra, which is attributed to Rabbi Yehuda. The *pasuk* states *kol dam lo tochelu* — you shall not eat any blood. The *braita* asks: why does the *pasuk* say *kol dam*, all blood? Since it has already been stated *ki ha-dam hu ba-nefesh yechapper*, one might have thought that only sacrificial life blood incurs *kares* — perhaps the prohibition is specifically calibrated to blood that serves the atonement function. *Kol dam* — the word *kol*, any — comes to teach that even *dam chullin* (non-consecrated blood) and *dam tamtzit* (non-life blood) are included in the prohibition, and one does incur *kares* for them. Yet the very structure of the *drasha* demonstrates that Rabbi Yehuda acknowledges the category of *dam ha-neshama* as the blood used for sacrificial atonement — it is only for the eating prohibition that he extends *kares* to all blood. And as the *gemara* notes: *stam Sifra mani Rabbi Yehuda hi* — the unattributed position of the *Sifra* reflects the view of Rabbi Yehuda.

## Asham Talui Scope

### Me'ilah Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מְחַיֵּיב עַל סְפֵק מְעִילָה אָשָׁם תָּלוּי, וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִים. וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא שֶׁאֵינוֹ מֵבִיא אֶת מְעִילָתוֹ עַד שֶׁיִּתְוַדַּע לוֹ שֶׁיָּבִיא עִמּוֹ אָשָׁם וַדַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: This mishna resumes discussion of the provisional guilt offering addressed in the previous chapter. **Rabbi Akiva deems** one **liable** to bring **a provisional guilt offering for** a case where he is **uncertain** whether he is guilty of **misuse** of consecrated property, a transgression that renders one liable to bring a definite guilt offering (see Leviticus 5:15). **And the Rabbis deem him exempt,** as one brings a provisional guilt offering only in a case of uncertainty as to whether he is liable to bring a sin offering, not a guilt offering. **And Rabbi Akiva concedes that one does not bring** payment for **his misuse until it becomes** definitely **known to him** that he is guilty of misuse, **as** then **he will bring a definite guilt offering with** his payment.

The *gemara* returns to the main topic of the *perek*: the scope of *asham talui*. It is established that one brings an *asham talui* for doubtful violation of a sin that would otherwise carry a *chatat*. The question here is whether an *asham talui* is also brought when one is uncertain whether one committed *me'ilah* — misappropriation of consecrated property. A definite *me'ilah* requires an *asham me'ilos*. Does a doubtful *me'ilah* require an *asham talui*? Rabbi Akiva says yes; the *chachamim* exempt.

*Me'ilah* differs from the standard *chatat* case in two ways. First, the definite violation calls for an *asham*, not a *chatat*. Second, willful *me'ilah* does not carry *kares*: according to Rabbi it carries *misah bidei shamayim*, and according to the *chachamim* it is a negative prohibition — but no one holds it carries *kares*.

Rabbi Akiva further concedes: *umodeh Rabbi Akiva she-eino meivi es me'ilaso ad she-yivada lo* — even according to Rabbi Akiva, who holds one brings an *asham talui* for doubtful *me'ilah*, the actual principal payment (*me'ilaso*, the money owed to *hekdesh*) together with the added fifth (*chomesh*) is not brought until one knows for certain that one actually misappropriated. The *asham talui* itself is not conditioned on payment; the financial obligation awaits certainty. When one does become certain, one then brings the *asham vadai* and pays.

### Tarfon's Strategy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: מָה לָזֶה מֵבִיא שְׁתֵּי אֲשָׁמוֹת? אֶלָּא מֵבִיא מְעִילָה וְחוּמְשָׁהּ, וְיָבִיא אָשָׁם בִּשְׁנֵי סְלָעִים, וְיֹאמַר: ״אִם וַדַּאי מָעַלְתִּי – זוֹ מְעִילָתִי וְזוֹ אֲשָׁמִי, וְאִם סָפֵק מָעַלְתִּי – הַמָּעוֹת נְדָבָה, וְאָשָׁם תָּלוּי״. שֶׁמִּמִּין שֶׁהוּא מֵבִיא עַל הוֹדַע, מֵבִיא עַל לֹא הוֹדַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Tarfon said: For what** purpose **does that** person **bring two guilt offerings,** one provisional and one definite? **Rather,** at the outset **one brings** the payment for **misuse** of consecrated property **and its** additional payment of **one-fifth,** as mandated by Torah law, **and he will** then **bring a guilt offering** worth **two *sela* and say: If** it is **certain** that **I misused** consecrated property, **this is** payment for **my misuse and this is my** definite **guilt offering. And if** it is **uncertain** whether **I misused** consecrated property, **the money is a contribution** to the Temple fund for the purchase of communal offerings **and the guilt offering** is **provisional, as from the** same **type** of animal **that one brings** a guilt offering **for** a case where it is **known** to him that he is guilty of misuse, he likewise **brings** a guilt offering **for** a case where it is **unknown** to him.

Rabbi Tarfon, who agrees in principle with Rabbi Akiva that an *asham talui* is appropriate for doubtful *me'ilah*, offers a pragmatic alternative: why force someone to bring two *ashamos* — an *asham talui* now and, upon becoming aware, an *asham vadai* later? Since both the *asham talui* and the *asham vadai* are the same species of animal, bring one now and stipulate (*al tnai*). The stipulation runs as follows: bring the *me'ilah* money and the additional fifth, bring an *asham* worth two *sela'im*, and declare: *im vadai ma'alti*, if it turns out I definitely sinned, then the money is my repayment and this is my *asham vadai*; *ve-im safek ma'alti*, if it turns out I only doubtfully sinned and no *asham vadai* is ever required, then the money becomes a gift (*nedavah*) and this *asham* is my *asham talui*. The advantage is clear: one animal, one payment, one act, covering both contingencies. *She-min she-hu meivi al hoda meivi al lo hoda* — it is the same animal whether brought for a certain sin or a doubtful one.

### Akiva Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: נִרְאִין דְּבָרָיו בִּמְעִילָה מְעוּטָה. הֲרֵי שֶׁיָּבוֹא לְיָדוֹ סְפֵק מְעִילָה בְּמֵאָה מָנֶה, לֹא יָפֶה לוֹ שֶׁיָּבִיא אָשָׁם בִּשְׁתֵּי סְלָעִים, וְלֹא יָבִיא סְפֵק מְעִילָה בְּמֵאָה מָנֶה? מוֹדֶה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לְרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן בִּמְעִילָה מְעוּטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: The statement of** Rabbi Tarfon **appears** correct **in** the case of **minimal misuse,** but in a case **where he is confronted with** a case of **uncertainty** with regard to **misuse** valued at **ten thousand dinars, would it not be preferable for him that he will** now **bring** a provisional **guilt offering** valued **at two *sela* and he will not bring** payment now for **uncertain misuse** valued **at ten thousand dinars?** The mishna concludes: Apparently, **Rabbi Akiva concedes to Rabbi Tarfon in** the case of **minimal misuse.** He agrees that at the outset one brings payment for misuse and its additional payment of one-fifth, and conditionally brings a guilt offering.

Rabbi Akiva accepts this strategy in principle but objects in the case of a large *me'ilah*. If the amount potentially misappropriated is small — a *prutah* or a trivial sum — fine, the strategy is reasonable. But *sheyavo le-safek me'ilah be-me'ah maneh*: what if one might have misappropriated a hundred *maneh* from *hekdesh*? Rabbi Tarfon's strategy requires paying back the full principal plus the fifth right now, up front, even though one may never have had any obligation to pay. One may never discover whether one sinned at all. To save the cost of one animal, Rabbi Tarfon is forcing the person to pay out an enormous sum of money that may never have been owed. In that case, *lo yafeh lo sheyavi asham bishnei sela'im* — it is better for him to bring the *asham talui* for two *sela'im* now and not pay the money, and perhaps it will never become clear that he sinned, and he will never owe a cent. Rabbi Akiva therefore agrees with Rabbi Tarfon only in the case of *me'ilah mu'etes*, a small amount. The entire exchange here is, at one level, pragmatic advice; yet as the *gemara* will show, substantive halakhic principles are derived from it.

### Gezeirah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְאִם נֶפֶשׁ״ – לְחַיֵּיב עַל סְפֵק מְעִילוֹת אָשָׁם תָּלוּי, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the issue of whether one brings a provisional guilt offering for an uncertain misuse of consecrated property, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: Immediately following the passage in the Torah discussing a guilt offering for misuse of consecrated property the Torah introduces the *halakhot* of a provisional guilt offering by stating: **"And if anyone** sin" (Leviticus 5:17). The term "and" serves **to render** one **liable** to bring **a provisional guilt offering for uncertain misuse** of consecrated property; this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. And the Rabbis deem** him **exempt** in such a case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּגְמִירִי ״מִצְוֹת״ ״מִצְוֹת״ מֵחַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** It is **because they derive** the details of the *halakha* with regard to the provisional guilt offering written in the verse: "And if anyone sin, and perform one of all the **commandments** of the Lord that are not to be performed" (Leviticus 5:17), by means of a verbal analogy **from** the verse stated with regard to **a sin offering:** "And if any one of the common people sin through error, in his performance of any of the **commandments** of the Lord that may not be performed" (Leviticus 4:27).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְהַלָּן דָּבָר שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְעַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת, וְעַל סְפֵיקוֹ אָשָׁם תָּלוּי, אַף כֹּל שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת וְעַל סְפֵקוֹ חַטָּאת וְעַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ אָשָׁם תָּלוּי,
>
> **Translation:** The verbal analogy indicates that **just as there,** the sin offering is brought only for **an act that renders one liable to** be punished with ***karet* for its intentional** violation **and** liable to bring **a sin offering for its unwitting** violation, **and** to bring **a provisional guilt offering for its uncertain** violation, **so too,** for **every** transgression **for which one is liable to** be punished with ***karet* for its intentional** violation **and** one is liable to bring **a sin offering for its unwitting** violation, one is also liable to bring **a provisional guilt offering for its uncertain** violation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאַפּוֹקֵי מְעִילָה, דְּאֵין חַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת, דְּתַנְיָא: הֵזִיד בִּמְעִילָה – רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: בְּמִיתָה, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בְּאַזְהָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** This verbal analogy serves **to exclude misuse** of consecrated property from the transgressions for which one would bring a provisional guilt offering in a case of uncertainty, **as one is not liable** to receive ***karet* for its intentional** violation. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who intentionally misused** a consecrated item, **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says** that he is liable to the punishment of **death** at the hand of Heaven. **And the Rabbis say:** He has violated a standard **prohibition,** and is flogged. Everyone agrees that he is not liable to receive *karet*.

The *gemara* now turns to the question of how the *chachamim* know to exempt doubtful *me'ilah* from *asham talui*. The *pasuk* concerning *asham talui* reads: *ve-im nefesh ki techeta ve-asatah achat mi-kol mitzvot Hashem asher lo tease'inah ve-lo yada ve-ashem*. The word *mitzvot* appears here and also by the *chatat*, linking the two contexts through a *gezeirah shavah* of *mitzvot-mitzvot*. The *gezeirah shavah* teaches: just as the *chatat* applies only to a sin that carries *kares* when done willfully and a *chatat* when done unwittingly, so too the *asham talui* applies only to such a sin. *Me'ilah* fails on both counts: it does not carry *kares* for willful violation, and the definite violation does not call for a *chatat* but an *asham*. The *gezeirah shavah* therefore excludes *me'ilah* from the scope of *asham talui*.

### Vav vs. Gezeirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי, דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: לְמֵדִין עֶלְיוֹן מִתַּחְתּוֹן, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: אֵין לְמֵדִין עֶלְיוֹן מִתַּחְתּוֹן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that they disagree about this** following method of halakhic derivation: **That Rabbi Akiva holds** that when the Torah connects two topics with the term "and" **one learns** the *halakhot* of the **earlier** passage **from those in the** later passage. Consequently, it can be derived that one who is uncertain about whether he misused consecrated property must bring a provisional guilt offering. **And the Rabbis hold** that **one does not learn** the *halakhot* in the **earlier** passage **from those of the** later passage.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לְמֵדִין עֶלְיוֹן מִתַּחְתּוֹן, דְּאִם כֵּן, צָפוֹן בְּבֶן בָּקָר לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לְהוּ,
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** In fact, **everyone** agrees that **one learns** the *halakhot* of the **earlier** passage **from those of the** later passage, **as if** one does not say **so, you will not find** a source for the requirement **that a young bull** brought as a burnt offering must be slaughtered in the **north** section of the Temple courtyard. This is derived from the fact that the Torah states the *halakhot* of a young bull brought as a burnt offering (Leviticus 1:3–9) and then states: "And if his offering is of the flock, from the sheep or from the goats, as a burnt offering, an unblemished male he shall present it, and he shall slaughter it on the side of the altar northward" (Leviticus 1:10–11).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכָא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן דְּפָטְרִי: דִּגְמִירִי ״מִצְוֹת״ ״מִצְוֹת״ מֵחַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, here,** in the case of one who is uncertain whether he misused consecrated property, **this is** the **reason that the Rabbis deem** him **exempt** from bringing a provisional guilt offering: It is **because they derive** the details of the *halakha* with regard to the provisional guilt offering written in the verse: "And if anyone sin, and perform one of all the **commandments** of the Lord that are not to be performed" (Leviticus 5:17), by means of a verbal analogy **from** the verse stated with regard to **a sin offering:** "And if any one of the common people sin through error, in his performance of any of the **commandments** of the Lord that may not be performed" (Leviticus 4:27).

Rabbi Akiva's position appears initially to rest on the juxtaposition of the *parsha* of *asham me'ilos* and the *parsha* of *asham talui* — one might think he simply learns the upper *parsha* from the lower. But the *gemara* establishes that all opinions agree to the principle of *lamedim elyon mi-tachton*, learning the first passage from the second, for otherwise one could never derive that cattle offerings for an *olah* must be slaughtered in the north (since that detail appears only in the second *parsha*). The real dispute must be elsewhere.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דַּאֲמַר קְרָא: ״וְאִם נֶפֶשׁ״ – וָיו מוֹסִיף עַל עִנְיָן רִאשׁוֹן, וְיִלְמַד עֶלְיוֹן מִתַּחְתּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, this is** the **reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva, as the verse states** with regard to the provisional guilt offering: **"And if anyone** sin" (Leviticus 5:17). The term "and" in the form of the letter ***vav*, adds to the previous matter.** When a sentence begins with the conjunction *vav*, it is a continuation of the previous discussion, **and** therefore the *halakhot* of the **earlier** passage, in this case misuse of consecrated property, **are learned from the later** passage. Consequently, one brings a provisional guilt offering even for uncertain misuse of consecrated property.

The *gemara* therefore identifies a specific letter that carries the argument for Rabbi Akiva: the word *ve-im nefesh* opens with a *vav* — *vav mosif al inyan rishon*, the *vav* adds to the preceding subject and teaches that one learns the *asham me'ilos* context from what follows. This is not mere juxtaposition but a formal derivation. Rabbi Akiva holds that this *vav* overrides the limitation imposed by the *gezeirah shavah*: the *vav* specifically introduces *asham talui* as applicable to *me'ilah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: תַּחְתּוֹן הוּא דְּגָמַר מֵעֶלְיוֹן לְאָשָׁם בְּכֶסֶף שְׁקָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis maintain** that it is the *halakhot* of the **later** passage that **are derived from the earlier** passage. Consequently, the ram brought **for** a provisional **guilt offering** must be **worth** at least two **silver shekels,** just like the ram brought as a guilt offering in a case where one is certain that he misused consecrated property (see Leviticus 5:15).

The *chachamim* respond: they accept the *vav* as operative, but they hold that what it teaches is not that one brings an *asham talui* for doubtful *me'ilah*, but rather the converse — *tachton hu de-gamar me-elyon* — the lower *parsha* of *asham talui* learns from the *parsha* of *asham me'ilos* the rule of *kesef shekalim*, the minimum cost of two *shekalim*. The *vav* links them in that direction: it teaches that an *asham talui* costs at minimum two *sela'im*, just as *asham me'ilos* specifies *kesef shekalim*. The *gezeirah shavah* of *mitzvot-mitzvot*, in their view, overrides the *vav* and precludes bringing an *asham talui* for *me'ilah*; the *vav*'s sole remaining function is to transmit the minimum cost in the other direction.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: אֵין הֶיקֵּשׁ לְמֶחֱצָה. מִכְּלָל דְּרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: יֵשׁ הֶיקֵּשׁ לְמֶחֱצָה? וְהָא קַיְימָא לַן דְּאֵין הֶיקֵּשׁ לְמֶחֱצָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And** how would **Rabbi Akiva** respond to this opinion? He **holds** that **there is no juxtaposition for half** of a matter. Consequently, one must also derive the *halakhot* of the earlier passage from the later passage, and one is liable to bring a provisional guilt offering for uncertain misuse of consecrated property. The Gemara raises a difficulty: Should one conclude **by inference that the Rabbis hold** that **there is a juxtaposition for half** of a matter? **But** this is difficult, because **we maintain** as a principle **that there is no juxtaposition for half** of a matter.

### Akiva Vav View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דַּאֲמַר קְרָא: ״וְאִם נֶפֶשׁ״ – וָיו מוֹסִיף עַל עִנְיָן רִאשׁוֹן, וְיִלְמַד עֶלְיוֹן מִתַּחְתּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, this is** the **reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva, as the verse states** with regard to the provisional guilt offering: **"And if anyone** sin" (Leviticus 5:17). The term "and" in the form of the letter ***vav*, adds to the previous matter.** When a sentence begins with the conjunction *vav*, it is a continuation of the previous discussion, **and** therefore the *halakhot* of the **earlier** passage, in this case misuse of consecrated property, **are learned from the later** passage. Consequently, one brings a provisional guilt offering even for uncertain misuse of consecrated property.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אָשָׁם בְּכֶסֶף שְׁקָלִים נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ״זֹּאת תּוֹרַת הָאָשָׁם״ – תּוֹרָה אַחַת לְכׇל הָאֲשָׁמוֹת, לְאָשָׁם דִּבְכֶסֶף שְׁקָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Akiva derives** the *halakha* that the provisional **guilt offering** must be worth at least two **silver shekels from** the verse: **"This is the law of the guilt offering"** (Leviticus 7:1). This teaches that there is **one law for all of the guilt offerings,** and therefore each **guilt offering** must be **worth** at least two **silver shekels.** Since it is not necessary for him to derive this *halakha* from the juxtaposition of the passages concerning misuse of consecrated property and the provisional guilt offering, and the verbal analogy cannot entirely negate the comparison indicated by this juxtaposition, Rabbi Akiva derives from the juxtaposition that one is liable to bring a provisional guilt offering for uncertain misuse of consecrated property.

Rabbi Akiva's rejoinder is that he does not need the *vav* to derive the minimum cost of an *asham talui*. He derives that from elsewhere: the *pasuk* *zos toras ha-asham*, "this is the *torah* of the *asham*" — *torah achas le-chol ha-ashamos*, one law applies to all *ashamos*, including the minimum cost of two *shekalim*. Since he has an independent source for minimum cost, the *vav* must be fulfilling a different function — namely, teaching that one brings an *asham talui* for doubtful *me'ilah*. The *vav* overrides the limiting implication of the *gezeirah shavah*, establishing an exception specific to *asham me'ilos*.

### Chachamim Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן? אַף עַל גַּב דִּכְתִיב ״זֹאת תּוֹרַת הָאָשָׁם״, אִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִכְתַּב ״וְאִם נֶפֶשׁ״ – וָיו מוֹסִיף עַל עִנְיָן רִאשׁוֹן, וְיִלְמַד תַּחְתּוֹן מֵעֶלְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis** would reply that **even though it is written: "This is the law of the guilt offering,"** nevertheless it **was necessary** for the verse **to write: "And if anyone** sin," in which the letter ***vav* adds to the previous matter and** indicates that the *halakhot* of the **later** passage **are learned from the earlier** passage. This teaches that a provisional guilt offering must be worth at least two silver shekels.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי מִ״זֹּאת תּוֹרַת הָאָשָׁם״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: כִּי אָמֵינָא תּוֹרָה אַחַת לְכׇל אֲשָׁמוֹת – הָנֵי מִילֵּי בִּשְׁאָר אֲשָׁמוֹת וַדָּאִין,
>
> **Translation:** **And if** you would say that this could be derived **from** the verse: **"This is the law of the guilt offering,"** that is impossible, as **I would say: When I say** that there is **one law for all of the guilt offerings, that statement** applies only **to other definite guilt offerings.** In other words, just as the guilt offering for definite misuse of consecrated property must be worth at least two silver shekels, the same applies to all definite guilt offerings.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אָשָׁם תָּלוּי, כֵּיוָן דְּעַל סְפֵק חֵלֶב קָאָתֵי, אֵימָא: לָא יְהֵא סְפֵיקוֹ חָמוּר מִוַּדָּאוֹ, מָה וַדָּאוֹ – חַטָּאת בַּת דַּנְקָא, אַף סְפֵיקוֹ – אָשָׁם בַּר דַּנְקָא, אַהָכִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא: ״וְאִם נֶפֶשׁ״ – וָיו מוֹסִיף עַל עִנְיָן רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **But** with regard to the **provisional guilt offering, since it comes for an uncertain** violation, e.g., in a case where one is uncertain whether he ate forbidden **fat,** one might **say** that the offering one brings for **its uncertain** violation **should not be** more **stringent than** the offering one brings for **its definite** violation: **Just as** for **its definite** violation one may bring **a sin offering worth** even **one-sixth [*danka*]** of a dinar, **so too,** for **its uncertain** violation one may bring **a guilt offering worth** even **one-sixth** of a dinar. It is **for this** reason that **the Merciful One wrote: "And if anyone** sin," in which the letter ***vav* adds to the previous matter.** By contrast, since Rabbi Akiva does not derive the requirement for a provisional guilt offering to be worth at least two silver shekels from this verse, he derives from it that one brings a provisional guilt offering in a case of uncertain misuse of consecrated property.

The *chachamim* reject this by pointing out that even according to those who accept *zos toras ha-asham* as a general equalization, one would not automatically apply the minimum cost to *asham talui*. Here is the logic: if I definitely sinned and bring a *chatat*, there is no minimum cost — a *chatat* can cost as little as a *danka*, a sixth of a *sela*. One might reason *kal va-chomer* that an *asham talui*, brought for a doubtful sin, should be no more costly than what one brings for a definite sin. Therefore even with *zos toras ha-asham*, I might have assumed *asham talui* also carries no minimum cost, like a *chatat*. The *vav* is therefore needed specifically to teach that the minimum cost of *asham me'ilos* — two *shekalim* — applies to *asham talui* as well, overriding what one might have assumed from comparing it to a *chatat*. With the *vav* doing that work, there is nothing left to derive regarding bringing an *asham talui* for *me'ilah*, and the *gezeirah shavah* stands in full force.

The underlying structure of the debate is therefore clear: Rabbi Akiva and the *chachamim* both accept that *ein gezeirah shavah le-mechetzah* and *ein hekesh le-mechetzah* — one cannot apply a *gezeirah shavah* or a *hekesh* halfway. They both accept the force of the *vav*. The dispute is which hermeneutical principle controls: does the *vav* override the *gezeirah shavah* so that *asham talui* applies to *me'ilah*, or does the *gezeirah shavah* override the *vav* so that the *vav*'s sole function is to transmit minimum cost? Rabbi Akiva says the *vav* wins because minimum cost is derivable elsewhere; the *chachamim* say the *gezeirah shavah* wins and the *vav* is needed for minimum cost regardless.

## Stipulated Asham

### Akiva Concession

The *mishnah* concludes with Rabbi Akiva conceding that Rabbi Tarfon's strategy of bringing a single stipulated *asham* is sound when the amount potentially misappropriated is small (*me'ilah mu'etes*), while objecting when the amount is large. This establishes the practical contours of the debate.

### Stipulation Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר רָבָא, תְּנִי: אִם בִּסְפֵיקוֹ עוֹמֵד לְעוֹלָם – יְהֵא אָשָׁם תָּלוּי, שֶׁמִּמִּין שֶׁהֵבִיא עַל הוֹדַע, מְבִיאוֹ עַל שֶׁלֹּא הוֹדַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: According to Rabbi Tarfon, **what is** this individual **saying?** How can he say: If it is uncertain, when he clearly is uncertain about whether he misused consecrated property? **Rava said** that one should **teach** the mishna as follows: **If its uncertainty persists forever,** this offering **should be a provisional guilt offering,** whereas if it becomes known that I misused consecrated property, this should be a regular guilt offering. This is an effective stipulation, **as** the offering **one brings** in a case where it is **known** to him that he misused consecrated property is **from** the same **species** as the offering **he brings** in a case where it is **unknown** to him whether he misused consecrated property.

The *gemara* scrutinizes the exact language of the stipulation proposed by Rabbi Tarfon. The formulation *ve-im safek ma'alti* — "if I doubtfully sinned" — is problematic: the person has already doubtfully sinned. One cannot use this as the conditional arm of a stipulation, since the condition has already been met (or not met) in the past. The stipulation has to be formulated in terms of what will become known in the future, not in terms of what has already transpired.

### Rav Huna Formula

Rav Huna therefore reformulates the stipulation: *im be-sefeko omed le-olam yehei asham talui* — if this matter remains in doubt forever, if I never in my life discover whether I sinned, let this be an *asham talui*; but if it does become clear (*hoda'ah*), let the earlier *asham* retroactively serve as the *asham vadai*. The animal is the same whether brought for a certain sin or a doubtful one (*she-min she-hu meivi al hoda meivi al lo hoda*), so this stipulation is structurally coherent.

### Awareness Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סוֹף סוֹף, כִּי מִתְיְדַע לֵיהּ בָּעֵי לְאֵיתוֹיֵי אָשָׁם וַדַּאי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **Ultimately,** even according to Rabbi Tarfon one cannot necessarily avoid bringing two guilt offerings. Since at the time when he brought his first offering he was uncertain whether he had misused consecrated property, **when it becomes known to him** that he misused consecrated property **he is required to bring a definite guilt offering.**

The *gemara* raises a deeper objection: even under Rav Huna's reformulation, once the person does eventually discover that he sinned, he is still obligated to bring an *asham vadai*. And we have established throughout this *masechta* that a *chatat* — and by extension an *asham* — cannot be brought until one is aware of the sin (*o hoda eilav chatato*). If awareness is the trigger for obligation, how can one bring an *asham vadai* now, before that awareness exists? The earlier *korban* cannot fulfill an obligation whose precondition has not yet been met.

### Rava Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מִדִּבְרֵי שְׁנֵיהֶם נִלְמַד – אָשָׁם וַדַּאי לָא בָּעֵי יְדִיעָה לְכַתְּחִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** In response to this question **Rava said: From the statement of both of** the *tanna'im*, i.e., Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva, it can **be learned** that **a definite guilt offering does not require knowledge** that the individual definitely sinned ***ab initio*.** Consequently, as long as it eventually becomes known that he sinned, he has fulfilled his obligation to bring a definite guilt offering.

Rava resolves this from the combined testimony of Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva, who agree in principle on the mechanism even as they disagree on its prudence. From both of them one may derive: *asham vadai lo ba'ei yedi'ah be-techilah* — an *asham vadai* does not require that awareness precede the bringing of the *korban*. This is precisely how an *asham vadai* differs from a *chatat*: by a *chatat*, the verse *o hoda eilav chatato* makes prior awareness a precondition for the *chiyuv*. For an *asham vadai*, awareness is still required — it is not enough that God knows one sinned if the person never discovers it — but the requirement can be satisfied retroactively. If one brings the *korban* now and becomes aware of the sin afterward, that subsequent awareness is sufficient to have the *korban* function as an *asham vadai*. The *korban* need not wait for awareness; rather, awareness, when it comes, reaches back and validates the *korban* already brought.

The practical implication of the entire discussion is therefore this: Rabbi Tarfon's strategy of a stipulated *asham* is halakhically valid precisely because of this principle. Contingent on future awareness, the same *korban* can serve either as an *asham talui* (if awareness never comes) or as an *asham vadai* (if it does). The only remaining debate is the practical one flagged by Rabbi Akiva: whether it is wise to pay the full principal now, when the amount in question may be substantial and one may never incur the obligation at all.
