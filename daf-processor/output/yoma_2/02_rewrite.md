# Yoma 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Masechet Organizatio (II)

### Welcome & Overview

Welcome to the beginning of *Masechet Yoma* and to our return to the Bavli. The placement of *Masechet Yoma* within *Shas* raises an immediate question about organization. In the *Mishnayot*, the *masechtot* of *Moed* are arranged in a particular sequence, but in the standard printed Bavli edition the order differs. The reason is that the Bavli organizes *masechtot* by size — defined by number of *perakim* — so *Shabbos* comes first as the largest, followed by *Eruvin* and then *Pesachim*.

### Order in Bavli vs Mishna

Once you have the three *masechtot* of *Yoma*, *Sukkah*, and *Rosh Hashanah*, it is easy to reorganize them to follow the calendar sequence of the *yamim tovim*. The year's *regalim* begin with *Nisan*, as the *Torah* treats *Pesach* as the opening of the festival cycle, and so the Bavli places *Pesachim* first, followed by *Rosh Hashanah*, *Yoma*, and *Sukkah* — a perfect chronological arc. In the *Mishnayot*, however, *Yoma* precedes *Rosh Hashanah*, and *Shekalim* follows *Yoma*. The *Daf Yomi* cycle we are now following adheres to the *Mishnayot* order, placing *Yoma* before *Rosh Hashanah*. Interestingly, the *Daf Yomi* continues to follow the *Mishnayot* sequence even after *Yoma*, proceeding to *Sukkah*, then *Beitzah*, then *Rosh Hashanah* — the actual order of the *Mishnayot* — rather than the reorganized Bavli sequence.

### Timeline & Length

*Masechet Yoma* runs to approximately eighty-eight *daf*, making it a roughly three-month commitment. After *Yoma*, nearly every remaining *masechet* in *Moed* is approximately one month in length — with *Sukkah* being a partial exception — leaving us about eleven months until the completion of *Seder Moed*.

## Kohen Gadol Separati (II)

### Seven-Day Separation

The *masechet* opens: *shivat yamim kodem Yom HaKippurim mafrishin kohen gadol mibeito lelishkat parhedrin* — seven days before *Yom Kippur*, they would separate the *kohen gadol* from his home and bring him to the *Lishkat Parhedrin*. Seven days before *Yom Kippur* is *Tzom Gedaliah*, meaning the separation took place right at the start of the *Aseret Yemei Teshuva*, immediately after the two days of *Rosh Hashanah*. The *Lishkat Parhedrin* was a chamber situated at the boundary between the *Ezrat Nashim* and the *Ezrat Yisrael*, essentially at the entrance to the *azara*, and this is where the *kohen gadol* would remain for the entire week leading up to *Yom Kippur*.

## Masechet Organizatio (II)

### Eight Perakim Structure

It is worth pausing to note the literary architecture of *Masechet Yoma* itself. The *masechet* contains eight *perakim*, of which the first seven deal entirely with *Avodat Yom HaKippurim* — the Temple service — while only the eighth addresses *Yom Kippur* as we observe it today: the *inuyim*, *teshuva*, and the experience of the day itself. This parallels the very structure described in the opening *Mishna*: seven days of preparation culminating in the eighth day, which is *Yom Kippur* itself. Seven *perakim* of preparation leading to the eighth, which is the day — there is an elegant structural parallel that is difficult to regard as coincidental.

A comparable structure appears in *Masechet Shabbat*, where six *perakim* deal with preparatory matters and issues of carrying before the seventh *perek*, *Klal Gadol*, finally lists the *lamed-tet melachot* — as if the six preceding *perakim* represent the six days of labor leading to the seventh, which is *Shabbat* itself. The *Rambam* mirrors this in his *Mishneh Torah*, where six introductory *perakim* precede the seventh *perek* that begins the laws of the *lamed-tet melachot*. The same numerological structure — seven leading to eight — appears to be operative here in *Yoma*.

## Kohen Gadol Separati (II)

### Substitute Kohen

Continuing the *Mishna*: *u'matkinin lo kohen acher tachtav shema ye'arei bo psul* — they would also prepare another *kohen* as a stand-in in case the *kohen gadol* were to become disqualified. Since he was carefully sequestered and protected during this period, the most plausible concern for *tuma* was that he would experience a seminal emission. The stand-in was not the regular *segan*, the designated deputy *kohen gadol*, but rather a specifically designated understudy appointed for this contingency.

### Backup Wife & Ein Ladava…

*Rabbi Yehuda omer af isha acheret matkinin lo* — Rabbi Yehuda adds that they would also prepare for him a second wife, in case his wife should die during this period. The basis is the *pasuk*: *v'chiper ba'ado u-va'ad beito* — "he shall atone for himself and for his household" — and *beito zo ishto*, "his household" means his wife. A *kohen gadol* who is unmarried is invalid to perform the *Yom Kippur* service. There is a broader principle running through *halacha* — touching on the *shliach tzibbur* and members of the *Sanhedrin* as well — that being married draws a person out of a purely self-oriented existence and renders him more capable of representing and empathizing with others. Here the requirement is grounded in a *pasuk*, but the thematic resonance with those other contexts is notable.

The *Mishna* records the objection raised against Rabbi Yehuda: *im ken ein ladavar sof* — if so, there is no end to the matter. If we worry that his wife might die, we should also worry that the backup wife might die, and then we should need a third, and a fourth, and so on. The response implicit in the objection is instructive about the nature of *halachic* risk assessment. The concern about the *kohen gadol* becoming *tamei* through seminal emission is a reasonable likelihood and therefore warrants a contingency plan. But the concern that his wife will suddenly die is far-fetched; and if we entertain far-fetched possibilities, we cannot stop — *ein ladavar sof*, reductio ad absurdum. A reasonable approach prepares for seminal emission but does not spiral into unlimited contingency planning for a wife dying and a backup wife dying after her.

## Parah Adumah Parallel

### Kohen Hasoref Separation

The *Gemara* now cites a parallel *Mishna* from *Masechet Parah*: *shivat yamim kodem srifat hapara hayu mafrishin kohen hasoref et hapara mibeito lelishka she'al p'nei habira, tzfonah mizrachah* — similarly, seven days before the burning of the *para aduma*, the *kohen* who would burn it was separated from his home to a chamber that was "on the face of the *bira*," in the northeast.

### Lishkat Beis Even

This chamber was called the *Lishkat Beis Even* — the Chamber of Stone. Why the name? *Shekol ma'aseha bichlei glalim bichlei avanim uvichlei adama* — because all vessels used there were made of *glalim* (dried animal dung), stone, and unworked earth. Critically, none of these materials are susceptible to *tuma*: stone vessels, vessels of dried dung, and unfired mud — not fired *cheres* (earthenware) but simply hardened sun-dried earth — cannot become *tamei*. The chamber was named for the stone vessels that so characterized its contents.

### Stringencies & Sadducees

The *Gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama* — what is the reason they insisted on exclusively *tuma*-proof vessels? The answer: *keivan detvul yom kasher bapara* — because, as we are taught, a *tvul yom* is valid for the *para aduma*. And not merely valid — they would go out of their way to ensure that the *kohen* performing the burning was in fact a *tvul yom*.

The *Mishna* in *Masechet Para* records: *metama'im hayu hakohen hasoref et hapara u'mativlin oto* — they would render the *kohen* who burned the *para* *tamei* and then immerse him in the *mikveh*, after which he would begin the *avoda* as a *tvul yom*, while still in the diminished state before nightfall completed his purification. The reason: *lehotzi miliban shel tzedukim* — to uproot the position of the Sadducees, *shehayu omrim bime'orvei shemesh hayta ne'eset* — who held that the *para aduma* could only be performed by someone who had achieved complete *tahara* after nightfall.

This is a striking deliberate choice. Normally the Rabbis, even when something is technically permitted, would prefer to perform a *mitzva* in the most *lechatchila* manner possible. Here they went in the opposite direction — actively engineering a state of *tuma* — to publicly demonstrate the rabbinic position against the Sadducees. It is worth remembering how significant the Sadducean presence was in the Temple. As Josephus and other sources make clear, many *kohanim* were Sadducees. The Sadducees did not, strictly speaking, reject *Torah Sheba'al Peh* as a matter of principle the way the later *Karaim* did, but they had fundamental interpretive differences with the Rabbis on certain critical questions. The Sadducee question was live and consequential in the Temple's operation, and this is precisely why — as we will see later in the *masechet* — the *kohen gadol* is made to swear on *Erev Yom Kippur* that he will follow the rabbinic approach and not the Sadducean one.

### Tosafot on Tvul Yom

Now, once they allowed themselves this leniency in order to make a point, they counterbalanced it with extreme stringency in all other aspects. *Tiknu rabanan klei galal uchelei avanim uchelei adamah* — they instituted that all vessels used would be ones incapable of receiving *tuma*, *kedei dela lezalzlu bah* — so that this single leniency would not allow the whole procedure to be treated lightly.

*Tosafot* takes the analysis a step further and significantly tempers the apparent radicalism of the *Mishna*. *Tosafot* says: *lav betumah d'Oraisa timuhu* — they never actually rendered him *tamei* with biblical-level *tuma*. They would never have allowed the *kohen* to be genuinely, biblically *tamei*. Indeed, from the *perek Ein Dorshin* we know that even the garments of those who ate *kodshim* — *kohanim* who ate *korbanos* — were considered *tamei* for the purposes of the *para aduma kohen*, so stringent was the standard. How could one imagine they would permit genuine biblical *tuma*?

Rather, *Tosafot* explains, citing the *Mishna* in *Parah* 3: *samchu yadeihem alav veshavu veomrim lo ishi Kohen Gadol red tevol achas* — they would lay their hands on him and tell him, "My lord, *kohen gadol*, go and immerse once." The act that rendered him "*tamei*" was merely the touch of another person. Because the *kohen* performing the *para* was being held to such an extraordinary standard of *tahara* — whereby even contact with *kohanim* who ate *korbanos* constituted *tumah* for him — this rabbinic-level *tuma* from that touch was what he immersed from. He was then a *tvul yom* not from biblical *tuma* but from a *tumah derabanan*.

*Tosafot*'s reading is that the so-called leniency was barely a leniency at all; if anything, the entire procedure began from a platform of extraordinary *chumra*, since the *kohen* was already being treated as if any human contact made him *tamei*. The single point of "leniency" — immersion and then proceeding as a *tvul yom* — was itself an expression of rabbinic-level *tuma* from an almost trivial contact, not genuine biblical impurity.

## Chamber Location & Signi…

### Why Stone & Northeast

The *Gemara* asks two further questions about the *Lishkat Beis Even*: why was it called *beis even* (we have explained this above), and *mai shna tzfonah mizrachah* — why specifically was this chamber located in the northeast?

*Keivan de-chatat hi ve-chatat teuna tzfonah* — because the *para aduma* is called a *chatat*, and a *chatat* is performed in the north side of the *mizbe'ach*. Additionally, *al nochach pnei ohel moed* — the *para aduma* is performed facing the *Ohel Moed*, meaning the *kohen* stood on *Har HaMishcha* — the Mount of Olives (*Har HaZeitim*), which lies to the east of *Har HaBayit* — and faced westward toward the *Beis HaMikdash*, sprinkling the blood in that direction. Since the *para aduma* is called a *chatat*, which ordinarily indicates the north, and since it is physically performed to the east with the *kohen* facing the *Beis HaMikdash*, *tekinu lah rabanan lishkat tzfonah mizrachah* — the Rabbis established her preparatory chamber in the northeast, *ke-heichi de-lehvei leh hekera*, so that it symbolizes both dimensions at once — the north of *chatat* and the east of the actual performance on *Har HaMishcha*.

### Definition of Bira

The *Gemara* then asks *mai bira* — what does the phrase *al p'nei habira* mean? Two *Amoraim* disagree. *Rabbah bar Bar Chana* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan* says: it was a specific location on *Har HaBayit* that was called *bira* by name. *Resh Lakish* disagrees and says: the entire *Mikdash* is referred to as *bira* — as in the phrase *habira asher hachinoti*, the "capital" or "fortress" that *David HaMelech* said he had prepared. On this reading, *al p'nei habira* tells us only that the chamber faced or was adjacent to the *Beis HaMikdash*, without specifying its exact location.

### Inside vs Outside Ezrah

*Tosafot* draws an important distinction between the two chambers — the one for the *para aduma* and the one for the *kohen gadol*. The *Lishkat Parhedrin*, in which the *kohen gadol* was sequestered before *Yom Kippur*, was located within the *azara* proper: *lishkas parhedrin she-bnuyah bekodesh* — because all of his *avoda* on *Yom Kippur* would take place within the *azara*, the *heichal*, and the *Kodesh Kodashim*, it was fitting that his preparatory chamber also be within the sanctified space.

By contrast, the *kohen* of the *para aduma* performed his *avoda* outside the *Beis HaMikdash* entirely — on *Har HaMishcha* — and therefore *perishaso le-lishkah she-al p'nei habirah u-she-chutz la-azrah*: his chamber was one that was outside the *azara* proper, perhaps in the *Ezrat Nashim* or elsewhere on *Har HaBayit*. The principle is elegantly consistent: the location of the preparatory chamber mirrors the location of the *avoda* for which the *kohen* is being prepared.

## Seven-Day Source

### Tzivah-Tzivah Source

Having established the practice, the *Gemara* asks *mena hanei milei* — from where do we derive the requirement for a seven-day separation? *Rabbi Minyomi bar Chilkiyah* in the name of *Rav Machasiyah bar Idi* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan* cites the *pasuk* from *Parshat Tzav* concerning the *milu'im*: *ka'asher asah bayom hazeh tziva Hashem la'asot lechaper aleichem* — "as he has done this day, so God has commanded to do, to atone for you." During the *milu'im*, *Aharon* and his sons were separated for seven days in the courtyard of the *Mishkan* in preparation for the inauguration of the *avoda* on the eighth day. The *pasuk* at the close of that episode appears somewhat redundant — God commanded to do this, and God commanded to atone for you — but the *Gemara* reads the two verbs as carrying distinct referents.

*La'asot* — "to do" in the future — means *elu ma'aseh parah*: when you similarly perform this type of act in the future, do it as you did today, with a seven-day separation before the eighth. *Lechaper* — "to atone" — means *elu ma'asei Yom HaKippurim*: this refers to the *avoda* of *Yom Kippur*. Neither the *para aduma* nor *Yom Kippur* had yet been commanded at the point of the *milu'im*, but the *pasuk* indicates that both are anticipated.

The *Gemara* immediately asks: *bishlama kulei kra befarah lo misukam* — the entire *pasuk* cannot be referring only to the *para aduma*, because it says *lechaper* and *ufarah lav bas kaparah hi* — the *para aduma* is not an act of *kapara*. While it is true that *chatat* can mean purification rather than sin-atonement, and indeed by *Yom Kippur* itself the *pasuk* speaks of *v'chipper es mikdash ha-kodesh u-mi-tumos Bnei Yisrael* — cleansing from *tumah* — the *Gemara* treats *kapara* as not applicable to the *para aduma*. So the *pasuk* must be addressing two things.

But then perhaps the entire *pasuk* refers only to *Yom Kippur*? The *Gemara* answers by invoking a *gezeira shava* of *tziva-tziva*: *ela yalif tziva tziva*. The *pasuk* at the *milu'im* says *tziva Hashem la'asot*. The *pasuk* opening the commandment of *para aduma* says *zos chukas hatorah asher tziva Hashem*. Both use the word *tziva*. This *gezeira shava* links the *milu'im* passage to the *para aduma* passage, and since the *milu'im* involved seven days of separation preceding the eighth day of inauguration, we learn that the *para aduma* similarly requires a seven-day separation. And *lechaper* remains available to indicate *Yom Kippur*.

But why not construct a *tziva-tziva gezeira shava* with *Yom Kippur* instead? After all, the *pasuk* at the conclusion of the *Yom Kippur* section says *vaya'as ka'asher tziva Hashem es Moshe* — "and he did as God had commanded Moses." The *Gemara* answers: *danin tziva d'lifnei asiya mitziva d'lifnei asiya, v'ein danin tziva d'le'achar asiya mitziva d'lifnei asiya*. We link a *tziva* that precedes the act to another *tziva* that precedes the act. The *pasuk* by the *milu'im* says *tziva... la'asot* — "commanded to do" — commanding before the performance. The *para aduma* likewise opens with *asher tziva Hashem* — commanding before the act. But the *Yom Kippur* *pasuk*, *vaya'as ka'asher tziva*, appears at the end, after the entire *avoda* has been described — it is a *tziva d'le'achar asiya*, a command-reference after the fact. We therefore do not link it to the command-reference that precedes the act.

### Timing of Command

What of the possibility that *la'asot* refers to all *korbanos*, since the *Torah* also says *beyom tzovoso es bnei Yisrael lehakriv es korbanaihem* — "on the day He commanded the children of Israel to bring their offerings" — which is also a *tziva* preceding an act of bringing? The *Gemara* answers: *danin tziva mitziva, v'ein danin tzovoso mitziva*. The *milu'im* *pasuk* says *tziva* and the *para aduma* *pasuk* says *tziva* — an exact verbal match. The *korbanot* *pasuk* uses *tzovoso*, a different conjugation.

The *Gemara* anticipates an objection: does the precise form of the word really matter? *Detana devei Rebbi Yishmael* — from the school of *Rebbi Yishmael*, who is known for his somewhat broader hermeneutical approach relative to *Rebbi Akiva*, we find that he makes a *gezeira shava* between *veshav hakohen* and *uva hakohen* in the laws of *tzara'as* — *shav* and *ba* are entirely different words, yet *zu hi shiva zu hi bia*, since "returning" and "coming" mean the same thing in that context, he links them. If *Rebbi Yishmael* is willing to link two completely different words on the basis of shared meaning, why should an inexact match between *tziva* and *tzovoso* prevent a *gezeira shava*?

The *Gemara* answers: *hanei mili heicha d'leika d'dami lei* — that principle applies only when there is no better option, when the closest match available is a semantic one. But *aval heicha d'ika d'dami lei* — where there is an exact verbal match available, *mid'dami lei yalfinan*, we derive the law from the closer match rather than the more distant one. Since *tziva* and *tziva* are an exact match, we prefer that linkage over *tziva* to *tzovoso*.

This discussion opens into a broader reflection on the nature of *gezeira shava* and the *mesorah* underlying it. The *Ramban*, in his *Hassagot* on the *Rambam*'s *Sefer Hamitzvot*, raises the following question: if a *gezeira shava* requires a *mesorah* going back to *Sinai*, why does the *Gemara* suggest alternatives — why not use this *gezeira shava*, why not use that one? If the *mesorah* fixed the precise *gezeira shava*, there would be nothing to discuss. The *Ramban* answers that the *mesorah* was not the exact formulation of the *gezeira shava* but rather the underlying *halacha* itself — the tradition that a seven-day separation was required for the *para aduma* and *Yom Kippur*, derivable via *gezeira shava* from the *milu'im*. Within that framework, it remained to determine which precise linguistic link best instantiated the derivation. The *mesorah* established the *what*; the *drasha* works out the *how*.

This bears on a broader question that has occupied both *Rishonim* and contemporary scholars: when *Chazal* derived *halacha* from *pesukim*, were they genuinely discovering laws through exegesis, or were they retroactively anchoring already-established traditions in scriptural language? The *Ramban*'s approach suggests the latter was at least sometimes operative — the tradition preceded the *drasha*, and the *drasha* expressed it in a canonical form. That does not make it an *asmachta*; the *Ramban* holds that the practice itself traced to *Har Sinai*. But the specific *gezeira shava* formulating that practice in terms of *tziva-tziva* may have been a post-facto articulation of what was always known.

## Alternatives Rejected

### All Korbanos Rejected

The *Gemara* works through a series of alternative applications for the seven-day separation derived from *milu'im*, rejecting each in turn. First: *ve'eima tziva d'korbanos* — perhaps the principle applies to all *korbanos*, requiring every *kohen* to observe a seven-day separation before performing any Temple service. This is not unreasonable on its face: *kohanim* serve only briefly, and a week of preparation prior to service would be fitting.

The *Gemara* raises the counter-suggestion: *niba'ei prisha l'chulei mishmeres beis av* — one could require each *beis av* (the daily subdivision of the weekly *mishmar*) to prepare for its day of service. Each *kohen* knows in advance which day of the week his *beis av* is scheduled to work; he could simply begin his seven-day preparation the week before. The objection is answered on principled grounds: *donin davar shekavu'a lo zman midavar shekavu'a lo zman* — we derive from *milu'im*, which was a singular fixed occasion, only to things that similarly have a single fixed time. *Korbanos* are brought every day of the year; they are not a single special occasion of sufficient gravity to demand the kind of preparation that *milu'im* models.

### Regalim Rejected

Perhaps the requirement should apply to the *regalim*, the pilgrimage festivals — a far more significant category than quotidian *korbanos*? The *Gemara* rejects this as well: *donin davar shenoheg pa'am achas b'shana midavar shenoheg pa'am achas b'shana* — we learn from something that occurs once a year to something that occurs once a year. The *regalim*, though individually significant, occur three times a year as a group, and are not analogous to a single unique annual occasion. The *milu'im* was once in a lifetime; the relevant structural parallel is to something that occurs exactly once annually, not three times.

### Specific Regalim Rejected

Could the principle apply to one specific *regel*? Two candidates present themselves. *Ei Chag HaMatzos* — *Pesach* might be chosen because *hakesuv* opens the calendar cycle with it, as the *Torah*'s festival legislation begins with *Pesach*. Or *ei Chag HaSuccos* — *Sukkot* might be chosen because *ho'il u'merubah mitzvosav*, it carries the greatest concentration of *mitzvos* among the *regalim*, receives the most detailed treatment in the *pesukim*, and carries the eschatological weight of *Zechariah*'s prophecy that all nations will gather *lachog es Chag HaSuccos*.

The *Gemara* rejects both with a single principle: *donin prishas shiva l'yom echad miprishas shiva l'yom echad, v'ein donin prishas shiva l'shiva miprishas shiva l'yom echad* — we learn seven days of preparation for a single day from the model of seven days of preparation for a single day. *Chag HaMatzos* and *Chag HaSuccos* each last seven days; preparing for them would be a seven-day preparation for seven days, not for a single climactic day. The *milu'im* model is specifically seven unsanctified days leading to one special eighth day, not seven days leading into a seven-day *yom tov*.

## Shemini Atzeret Status

### Rav Ashi's Argument

The *Gemara* raises one further candidate: *ve'eima shemini* — perhaps the principle should apply to *Shemini Atzeret*, since *Sukkot* would then serve as seven days of preparation for the single day of *Shemini Atzeret* — precisely the structure of *d'prishas shiva l'yom echad*. The *Gemara* rejects this on formal grounds: *donin davar she'ein kedusha lefanav midavar she'ein kedusha lefanav* — we derive from a scenario in which the preparatory period carries no inherent sanctity to another such scenario. Before the *milu'im*, the seven days were ordinary. Before *Yom Kippur*, the *Aseret Yemei Teshuva* are *chol*. But the seven days of *Sukkot* are *yom tov*, days of *kedusha*, and one cannot derive from seven unsanctified preparatory days to seven sanctified *yom tov* days.

One might attempt a *kal vachomer*: if something with no sanctity before it still demands such extensive preparation, then surely something with seven days of *kedusha* before it should demand even more. *Rav Ashi* offers a more fundamental objection: *mi ika midi de'ikar regel lo ba prisha tafel didei ba prisha* — is there any case in which the primary *regel* does not require preparation, yet its appendage does? *Sukkot* is the main *regel*; *Shemini Atzeret* is, in some sense, its culmination. If we do not require a seven-day preparation for *Sukkot* itself, it is absurd to require one for *Shemini Atzeret*.

### Pazarkeshav Framework

The *Gemara* preempts an objection: *va'afillu leman de'amar shemini regel bifnei atzmo hu* — even granting the view that *Shemini Atzeret* is an independent *regel*, that independence is limited to the matters summarized by the acronym *pazarkeshav*.

*Pei* — *payis bifnei atzmo*: a new lottery (*payis*) is conducted on *Shemini Atzeret* for the distribution of *korbanos* among the *mishmarot*, rather than continuing from wherever *Sukkot* left off. Even if some *mishmarot* had brought fewer bulls over the seven days of *Sukkot*, *Shemini Atzeret* starts fresh with a new lottery, treating itself as entirely independent. *Zayin* — *zman bifnei atzmo*: a *Shehecheyanu* is recited on *Shemini Atzeret*, as opposed to the last day of *Pesach*, which does not receive its own *Shehecheyanu*. *Reish* — *regel bifnei atzmo*: it has its own name (*Shemini Atzeret*) and its *mitzvot* differ from those of *Sukkot* — there is no *lulav* and no *sukka* obligation. *Kuf* — *korban bifnei atzmo*: its *musaf* offering does not follow the descending pattern of the seventy bulls over *Sukkot* but has its own distinct form; notably, the *Torah* introduces it with an independent formulation, *u'vayom hashmini*, rather than the continuation pattern used for the intermediate days. *Shin* — *shir bifnei atzmo*: the *shir shel yom* on *Shemini Atzeret* differs from those of *Sukkot*. The *shir shel yom* throughout *Sukkot* reflected the theme of gathering produce and the obligations of *matnot aniyim* — *va'ger, ve'aniyecha, ve'ha'almanah ve'hayatom, lo ta'azvenu* — appropriate to harvest time. The *Shemini Atzeret shir* is distinct, though *Rashi* notes *v'lo pirshu lanu eizeh shirah* — it was not transmitted to us which *shir* it was; *Tosafot* cites the tradition that it was *Lamnatzeach al hashminit*. *Vet* — *bracha bifnei atzmo*: it has its own *bracha*. *Rashi* cites a *Tosefta* regarding a special blessing given to the king; *Tosafot* offers the more practically relevant explanation that in *Birkat HaMazon* and *Shemoneh Esrei* the *chag* is identified not as *Chag HaSukkot* but as *Shemini Chag HaAtzeret*, reflecting an independent identity.

### Tashlumim Status

Despite all these dimensions of independence, *Shemini Atzeret* nevertheless retains a subordinate relationship to *Sukkot* in one critical respect: *le'inyan tashlumin tashlumin derishon hu* — with regard to making up missed *korbanos*, it functions as *tashlumim* of the first day. If one failed to bring the *korban chagiga* on the first day of *Sukkot*, one may bring it on any of the subsequent days of *Sukkot* — and this makeup window extends through *Shemini Atzeret* as well. A missed *chagiga* of *Sukkot* can be remedied on *Shemini Atzeret*. This means that however independent *Shemini Atzeret* may be across the six dimensions of *pazarkeshav*, it remains, in this one dimension, the tail end of *Sukkot*. And that is precisely *Rav Ashi*'s point: if *Sukkot* itself does not require a seven-day preparation, then *Shemini Atzeret* — which remains *Sukkot*'s *tashlumim* — cannot require one either. The question of how to balance *Shemini Atzeret*'s independence against its continuing relationship to *Sukkot* will receive further treatment as we continue.
