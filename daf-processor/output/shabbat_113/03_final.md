# Shabbat 113 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kesher Uman & Weaver's R…

### Bucket & Belt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ קוֹשְׁרִין דְּלִי בְּפָסִקְיָא, אֲבָל לֹא בְּחֶבֶל. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַתִּיר. כְּלָל אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: כׇּל קֶשֶׁר שֶׁאֵינוֹ שֶׁל קַיָּימָא — אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may tie a bucket with a belt** on Shabbat, as he will certainly not leave it tied to the bucket, and therefore it is not a permanent knot. **But** one may **not** tie a bucket **with a rope. Rabbi Yehuda permits** doing so. **Rabbi Yehuda stated a principle:** With regard to **any knot that is not permanent, one is not liable** for tying **it.**

The daf opens with a new *mishnah* that continues the theme of knots on *Shabbos*. By way of background: we had established that knots which are completely permanent — or, according to the Rambam, a combination of *kesher uman* (a professional knot) and permanence — are biblically forbidden. Knots lacking both of those qualities are permitted, and those occupying an intermediate position are rabbinically forbidden.

The *mishnah* before us introduces a concrete case: *koshin dli bipaskia* — one may tie a bucket to the side of a well using a *paskia*, a belt, rather than a rope. The reason is evident: no one leaves his belt tied to a well. Whatever the objective strength of the knot may be, the subjective and contextual reality is that it will be undone very shortly. This is a significant principle — the nature of the knot is assessed not solely by its physical characteristics but also by the intent and circumstances of the one tying it. However, *aval lo b'chevel* — one may not use a rope, since a rope would normally be left there and would therefore constitute at minimum a rabbinically forbidden knot, and potentially a biblical one depending on its permanence and professional character.

### Rebbe Yehuda's Principle

*Rebbe Yehuda matir* — Rabbi Yehuda rules permissively even with a rope. He then states a general principle: *kol kesher she'eino shel kayama ein chayavin alav* — any knot that is not permanent, one is not liable for. *Tosafot* raises a pointed question: haven't we already established that non-permanent knots are not a problem? And further, Rabbi Yehuda is not merely exempting after the fact — he is permitting the tying *l'chatchila* to begin with. *Tosafot* resolves this by arguing that Rabbi Yehuda's general principle is not actually connected to the preceding line about the bucket at all. It loops back to clarify the very first *mishnah* in this section regarding which knots incur liability — it is a separate and independent statement, not a continuation of the bucket discussion.

### Chevel D'Gardi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ חֶבֶל דְּמַאי? אִי לֵימָא חֶבֶל דְּעָלְמָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַתִּיר? קֶשֶׁר שֶׁל קַיָּימָא הוּא! אֶלָּא חֶבֶל דְּגַרְדִּי. לְמֵימְרָא דְּרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי גָּזְרִינַן חֶבֶל דְּגַרְדִּי אַטּוּ חֶבֶל דְּעָלְמָא, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר לָא גָּזְרִינַן?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: We learned in the mishna that according to the first *tanna*, it is prohibited to tie a rope to a bucket on Shabbat, and Rabbi Yehuda permits doing so. The Gemara asks: **A rope of what** kind? **If you say** it is referring to **a standard rope,** does **Rabbi Yehuda permit** doing so? **It is a permanent knot. Rather,** it is referring to **a weaver's rope;** since the weaver needs it for his work, he will untie it after Shabbat. The Gemara asks: **Is that to say that the Rabbis hold** that **we issue a decree** prohibiting **a weaver's rope due to a standard rope, and Rabbi Yehuda holds** that **we do not issue a decree?**

The *Gemara* asks: *chevel d'mai* — what type of rope does Rabbi Yehuda permit when tying the bucket to the well? If it is an ordinary rope — *chevel d'alma* — surely that is a *kesher shel kayama*, a permanent knot, and how could Rabbi Yehuda permit it? The *Gemara* therefore concludes: *ela chevel d'garde* — it must be referring to a weaver's rope. This is something that closely resembles, or may even be identical in appearance to, a regular rope, but which belongs to a weaver and is used in some professional capacity — perhaps worn as a sash or used in his craft. Come *motzaei Shabbos*, the weaver will need it back; he will not simply leave it attached to the well. The practical temporariness is clear in context even if the object looks indistinguishable from an ordinary rope.

### Mizchilaf/Confusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי גָּזְרִינַן חֶבֶל דְּגַרְדִּי אַטּוּ חֶבֶל דְּעָלְמָא, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר לָא גָּזְרִינַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that to say that the Rabbis hold** that **we issue a decree** prohibiting **a weaver's rope due to a standard rope, and Rabbi Yehuda holds** that **we do not issue a decree?**

With this established, the *Gemara* reframes the entire dispute between the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda. The Rabbis hold that we are concerned: *gazrinan chevel d'garde atu chevel d'alma* — we worry that permitting a weaver's rope will lead people to use an ordinary rope, since they look alike. Rabbi Yehuda holds *lo gazrinan* — he is not concerned that people will make this mistake. This is all an inference of the *Gemara*, not explicit in the *mishnah*, but the *Gemara* requires it to explain how Rabbi Yehuda could sanction what he does.

### Slipknot Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: חֶבֶל דְּלִי שֶׁנִּפְסַק לֹא יְהֵא קוֹשְׁרוֹ אֶלָּא עוֹנְבוֹ. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כּוֹרֵךְ עָלָיו פּוּנְדָּא אוֹ פָּסִקְיָא, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יַעַנְבֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from that which was taught in a *baraita*: With regard to **the rope of a bucket that was severed** on Shabbat, **one may not tie it** with a regular knot; **rather, he** may **tie a bow. And Rabbi Yehuda says: One may wrap a hollow belt around it or a sash as long as he does not tie it into a bow.**

The *Gemara* now introduces a *baraita* that creates a difficulty: *chevel dli shenifsak* — if the bucket's rope broke and you wish to reconnect the two ends, what do you do? The Rabbis say: *lo yikshrenu ela ovno* — don't tie a proper knot; rather, make a slipknot (*aniva*). Rabbi Yehuda says: you may wrap a belt or sash around it — *korech alav punda o paskia* — provided you do not make a bow. The *Gemara* identifies this as a contradiction: *kashya de'Rebbe Yehuda ad'Rebbe Yehuda, kashya de'Rabbanan ad'Rabbanan*.

The problem, as the *Gemara* unpacks it, is that everyone would presumably agree in principle that a *slipknot* is perfectly fine — it is thoroughly temporary and easy to undo. The assumed structure of the debate was that the Rabbis are concerned about visual confusion (*mizchilaf*) while Rabbi Yehuda is not. Yet here the positions appear reversed: the Rabbis permit the slipknot while Rabbi Yehuda disallows it. Why?

### Rope vs. Slipknot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, קַשְׁיָא דְּרַבָּנַן אַדְּרַבָּנַן. דְּרַבָּנַן אַדְּרַבָּנַן לָא קַשְׁיָא: חֶבֶל בְּחֶבֶל — מִיחַלַּף. עֲנִיבָה בִּקְשִׁירָה — לָא מִיחַלְּפָא.
>
> **Translation:** This is **difficult,** as there is a contradiction between one statement of **Rabbi Yehuda** and another statement of **Rabbi Yehuda,** as in the *baraita* he issued a decree prohibiting tying a bow due to tying a knot, and in the mishna he issued no decree. And this is also **difficult,** as there is a contradiction between one statement of the **Rabbis** and another statement of the **Rabbis,** as in the *baraita* they do not issue a decree and in the mishna they do issue a decree. The Gemara responds: The contradiction between one statement **of the Rabbis** and the other statement **of the Rabbis** is **not difficult,** as they are of the opinion that **a rope may be confused with** another **rope.** Therefore, the Rabbis issued a decree prohibiting a weaver's rope, because if it were permitted one might mistakenly come to tie a standard rope. However, **a bow is not confused with a knot,** and therefore they did not issue a decree prohibiting a bow.

The *Gemara* resolves the Rabbis' apparent contradiction cleanly: *chevel be'chevel michlaf* — one rope looks like another rope, so the Rabbis rightly worry about confusion in the bucket case. *Aniva be'kshira lo michlafa* — but a slipknot is obviously different from a regular knot; no one will confuse the two. Hence the Rabbis permit a slipknot and their position is consistent.

### Slipknot Permanence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָתָם — לָא מִשּׁוּם דְּמִיחַלְּפָא עֲנִיבָה בִּקְשִׁירָה, אֶלָּא עֲנִיבָה גּוּפָהּ קְשִׁירָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The contradiction between one statement **of Rabbi Yehuda** and the other statement **of Rabbi Yehuda** is **not difficult,** as **there,** where he prohibited tying a bow, it is **not because a bow may be confused with a knot,** but **because,** in his opinion, **a bow itself is a** full-fledged **knot** and is not prohibited due to a decree.

For Rabbi Yehuda, the resolution is more substantive. The *Gemara* says: *hasam lo mishum de'michlafa aniva be'kshira* — Rabbi Yehuda is not concerned that the slipknot will be confused with a permanent knot. That is not his reason for prohibiting it. *Ela aniva gufa kshira hi* — rather, Rabbi Yehuda holds that a slipknot is itself a genuine knot. Even though it can be undone easily, it can remain in place for a very long time. The ease of untying does not determine whether something qualifies as a knot; durability of result does. This is a meaningful principled disagreement about what constitutes a *kesher shel kayama*.

It is worth noting the connection to the earlier *mishnah* where Rabbi Meir held that if a knot can be undone with one hand it is not a problem. One reading of the *Gemara* there was that Rabbi Meir would permit even a well-tied slipknot if it could be undone one-handed — setting up a direct *bar plugta* between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda on exactly this question. The practical *halacha* is that a slipknot is not problematic, but the *Gemara*'s analysis makes clear that the issue is not merely one of confusion — it may independently implicate the question of permanence, even for an easily-released knot.

## Practical Examples

### Cow & Trough

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר רַב: מֵבִיא אָדָם חֶבֶל מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, וְקוֹשְׁרוֹ בַּפָּרָה וּבָאֵיבוּס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba said** that **Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi said** that **Rav said: A person may bring a rope from inside his house** on Shabbat **and tie** one end of **it to a cow and** the other end **to a trough.**

The *Gemara* cites a ruling in the name of Rav (via Rabbi Abba and Rabbi Hiyya bar Ashi): *mevi adam chevel mitoch beiso v'koshro b'parah u'va'eivus* — a person may bring a rope from his own home on *Shabbos* and tie it to his cow on one end and to the feeding trough on the other. The assumption is that since the cow needs to be released, both ends will eventually be untied, and there is no concern of a permanent knot.

### One vs. Both Ends

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי אַחָא אֲרִיכָא דְּהוּא רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר פָּפָּא לְרַבִּי אַבָּא: חֶבֶל שֶׁבָּאֵיבוּס — קוֹשְׁרוֹ בַּפָּרָה, וְשֶׁבַּפָּרָה — קוֹשְׁרוֹ בָּאֵיבוּס, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יָבִיא חֶבֶל מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ וְיִקְשׁוֹר בַּפָּרָה וּבָאֵיבוּס!
>
> **Translation:** That is not considered a permanent knot, because he will certainly untie the rope. **Rabbi Aḥa Arikha,** who was nicknamed Rabbi Aḥa the Long, due to his height, **who is** also called **Rabbi Aḥa bar Pappa, raised an objection to Rabbi Abba** from the *baraita*: With regard to **a rope that is** tied **to the trough, one may tie it to a cow, and** a rope that is tied **to the cow one may tie it to a trough, provided that one does not bring a rope from inside his house and tie it to the cow and to the trough.**

Rav Acha Aricha (also identified as Rabbi Acha bar Pappa) challenges this from a *baraita*: if the rope on the trough is already tied there, one may tie the other end to the cow, and vice versa — but *u'vilvad shelo yavi chevel mitoch beiso v'yikshor b'parah u'va'eivus* — one may not bring a new rope from home and tie *both* ends fresh. The concern is that when you are done, you might untie only one end, leaving the other as a permanent knot. This directly contradicts Rav's ruling.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם — חֶבֶל דְּעָלְמָא הָכָא חֶבֶל דְּגַרְדִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** where they permitted it, it is referring to **a weaver's rope,** which will certainly be untied.

Rav Abba resolves the tension: *hasam chevel d'alma* — the *baraita* is discussing an ordinary rope, for which the concern about leaving one end attached is real. *Hacha chevel d'garde* — Rav's ruling refers to a weaver's rope, which, as established in the *mishnah*, will not be left in place because it is needed for the weaver's professional use. The parallel with the bucket case is exact.

### Weavers' Muktzeh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כְּלֵי קִיוָּאֵי מוּתָּר לְטַלְטְלָן בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: A weaver's tools may be moved on Shabbat.**

The *Gemara* shifts to a related topic. Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel rules: *kol k'lei kivoi mutar l'taltalo b'Shabbos* — all of the weavers' vessels (*keilei kivoi*) are permitted to be carried on *Shabbos*. Since we have been discussing the weaver's rope, the *Gemara* now addresses the *muktzah* status of the various implements of weaving more broadly. These are tools used for *melachah* — they are *melachto l'issur* — yet as with any *k'lei she'melachto l'issur*, they may be moved *l'tzorech gufo o l'tzorech mekomo*: for their own use or to free up the space they occupy. The only prohibited reason to move such an item is to protect it from damage — *me'chamah l'tzel*.

The concern the *Gemara* raises goes beyond *melachto l'issur*: perhaps these implements are so valuable or delicate that one is never willing to let anyone touch or handle them — which would make them *muktzah machmas chisron kis*, a far more stringent category that cannot be moved for any purpose. The ruling is that this concern does not apply; the weavers' implements are movable.

### Loom Beams

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב יְהוּדָה: כּוֹבֶד הָעֶלְיוֹן וְכוֹבֶד הַתַּחְתּוֹן מַהוּ? אִין וְלָאו וְרַפְיָא בִּידֵיהּ. אִיתְּמַר אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כְּלֵי קִיוָּאֵי מוּתָּר לְטַלְטְלָן בְּשַׁבָּת, אֲפִילּוּ כּוֹבֶד הָעֶלְיוֹן וְכוֹבֶד הַתַּחְתּוֹן, אֲבָל לֹא אֶת הָעַמּוּדִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara records a question posed to Rav Yehuda: **What** is the ruling with regard to **the upper beam and the lower beam** of the weaver's loom? Does this leniency apply to them? Rav Yehuda did not provide a clear answer. He said: **Yes and no, and** the matter was **uncertain to him. It was stated: Rav Naḥman said** that **Shmuel said: A weaver's tools may be moved on Shabbat, even** the **upper beam and the lower beam, but not the posts.**

The *Gemara* records a question posed to Rav Yehuda: *koved elyon v'koved tachton mahu* — what about the upper and lower beams of the loom? These are substantial structural components, not small portable instruments — might they be *muktzah machmas chisron kis*? Rav Yehuda vacillated: *im v'lav v'rafya b'yadei* — he gave no firm answer. The question was subsequently resolved by a tradition from Rav Nachman citing Shmuel: the weavers' implements are indeed permitted to carry, *afilu koved elyon v'koved tachton* — even the upper and lower horizontal beams. However, *aval lo es ha'amudim* — not the vertical posts, which are set into the ground.

### Holes & Posts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: מַאי שְׁנָא עַמּוּדִים דְּלָא? אִילֵּימָא דְּקָעָבֵיד גּוּמּוֹת, גּוּמּוֹת מִמֵּילָא קָא הָוְיָין, דִּתְנַן: הַטּוֹמֵן לֶפֶת וּצְנוֹנוֹת תַּחַת הַגֶּפֶן, אִם מִקְצָת עָלָיו מְגוּלִּין — אֵינוֹ חוֹשֵׁשׁ לֹא מִשּׁוּם כִּלְאַיִם, וְלֹא מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִיעִית, וְלֹא מִשּׁוּם מַעֲשֵׂר, וְנִיטָּלִין בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Rav Naḥman: What is different** about the **posts that** one may **not** carry them? **If we say** it is because **one makes holes** when removing the post from the ground, **holes** come into **being on their own** and that is not prohibited, **as we learned** in a mishna: With regard to **one who stores a turnip or radish** in the ground **beneath a vine** for safekeeping, **if some of its leaves were exposed,** making it possible to grab the turnip or the radish and pull it out of the ground, **he need not be concerned, neither due to diverse kinds,** i.e., that he violated the prohibition of planting food crops in a vineyard, as there was no intent to plant them, **nor due to** concern that he violated the prohibition against working the land during **the Sabbatical Year, nor due to tithes,** i.e., that it would be considered as if he picked it from the ground and would be obligated to tithe it, **and they may be taken** from the ground **on Shabbat.**

Rava asks Rav Nachman: *mai shna amudim delo* — what distinguishes the vertical posts that they cannot be moved? If it is because removing them from the ground creates a hole — *mishum gumot* — that should not be a concern, since the holes are already there and any deepening of them is incidental (*gumot mimeila ka'avin*). The *Gemara* supports this from the *mishnah* about burying turnips and radishes beneath a vine: one may remove them on *Shabbos*, even though doing so disturbs the earth, because the displacement of dirt is merely incidental.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּשָׂדֶה — לָא אָתֵי לְאַשְׁווֹיֵי גּוּמּוֹת, הָכָא בְּבַיִת — אָתֵי לְאַשְׁווֹיֵי גּוּמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman answered him: The cases are not comparable. **In a field one will not come to fill holes;** however, **here,** where the holes are created **in the house,** the concern is that one may **come to smooth out** the **holes.**

The *Gemara* distinguishes: *b'sadeh lo asu l'ashvuyei gumos* — in a field, no one goes to fill in a hole in the ground. *Hacha b'bayis, asu l'ashvuyei gumos* — inside a house, once you have an exposed hole in the floor, you are likely to come and smooth it over. That act of filling and smoothing would constitute a form of *boneh*, at least rabbinically. The prohibition on moving the vertical posts is therefore not a *muktzah* concern at all — it is a concern that removing them will lead to a forbidden act of building.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מֵרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בַּר לֵיוַאי: כְּלֵי קִיוָּאֵי, כְּגוֹן כּוֹבֶד הָעֶלְיוֹן וְכוֹבֶד הַתַּחְתּוֹן, מַהוּ לְטַלְטְלָן בְּשַׁבָּת? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין. מַה טַּעַם? לְפִי שֶׁאֵין נִיטָּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised a dilemma before Rabbi Yehuda bar Liva'ei:** With regard to **weaver's tools, such as the upper beam and the lower beam** of the loom, **what** is the ruling in terms of **carrying them on Shabbat?** Rabbi Yehuda **said to him: One may not carry them.** Rabbi Yoḥanan asked him: **What is the reason** for this? Rabbi Yehuda bar Liva'ei answered: **Because they are not** usually **carried** even during the week, as they are extremely heavy and are considered fixed in place.

The parallel question posed by Rabbi Yochanan to Rabbi Yehuda bar Livai receives the same substantive answer: the upper and lower horizontal beams may not be moved, *l'pi she'ein nitlin* — because they are simply not moved. Rashi explains this to mean that since these items are never ordinarily relocated, they attain the status of *muktzah machmas chisron kis* — items so fixed in place by the owner's intention that they are treated as genuinely set aside.

## Folding & Hachana

### Folding Clothes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מְקַפְּלִין אֶת הַכֵּלִים אֲפִילּוּ אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה פְּעָמִים, וּמַצִּיעִין אֶת הַמִּטּוֹת מִלֵּילֵי שַׁבָּת לְשַׁבָּת, אֲבָל לֹא מִשַּׁבָּת לְמוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת. רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: מְקַפְּלִין אֶת הַכֵּלִים, וּמַצִּיעִין אֶת הַמִּטּוֹת מִיּוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים לְשַׁבָּת. וְחֶלְבֵי שַׁבָּת קְרֵיבִין בְּיוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים, אֲבָל לֹא שֶׁל יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים בְּשַׁבָּת. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא שֶׁל שַׁבָּת קְרֵיבִין בְּיוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים, וְלֹא שֶׁל יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים קְרֵיבִין בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may fold the garments** after removing them **even four or five times, and one may make the beds from Shabbat evening** in preparation **for Shabbat** day, **but not from Shabbat** in preparation **for the conclusion of Shabbat,** since one may not perform an action on Shabbat that is preparation for a weekday. **Rabbi Yishmael says: One may fold the garments and make the beds from Yom Kippur** in preparation **for Shabbat** if Yom Kippur occurs on Friday. **And the fats** of the offerings that were sacrificed on **Shabbat are offered on Yom Kippur, but not those of Yom Kippur on Shabbat,** because the sanctity of Shabbat is greater than the sanctity of Yom Kippur. **Rabbi Akiva says: Neither are** the fats **of** the offerings sacrificed on **Shabbat offered on Yom Kippur, nor are those of Yom Kippur offered on Shabbat.**

The *Gemara* now transitions to a new *mishnah*: *mekaplin es hakelim* — one may fold garments on *Shabbos*, even four or five times. One may also prepare the beds — spread out the sheets — *milelei Shabbos l'Shabbos*, from Friday night for the sake of *Shabbos* itself, meaning one is going to sleep in that bed on *Shabbos*. But *aval lo mi-Shabbos l'motzaei Shabbos* — one may not make the bed on *Shabbos* for Saturday night. The prohibition here is not connected to any specific *melachah* but to a distinct concern: *hachana*, preparing on one sacred day for the needs of a subsequent day or weekday.

Rabbi Yishmael adds: *mekaplin es hakelim u-matzi'in es hamitot mi-Yom Kippur l'Shabbos* — one may fold garments and prepare beds from *Yom Kippur* for *Shabbos*, in the case where *Yom Kippur* falls on a Friday. Since *Yom Kippur* is of somewhat lesser sanctity relative to *Shabbos*, it can serve *Shabbos* in this way. The *mishnah* also addresses the related case of *chelvei Shabbos*: if *Yom Kippur* falls on a Sunday, the fats of *Shabbos* offerings may be burned on *Yom Kippur* — again, the lesser-sanctified day serves the greater. But the fats of *Yom Kippur* offerings cannot be burned on *Shabbos*, since *Shabbos* does not serve *Yom Kippur*. Rabbi Akiva takes the most restrictive position: neither day's offerings may be processed on the other day — each day stands entirely on its own.

### Hachana Defined

The concern underlying the prohibition on folding garments for after *Shabbos* or making beds for *motzaei Shabbos* is what we call *hachana* — preparation from one day for the next. It is worth noting that some *Rishonim* do not frame the *issur* primarily as *hachana* but rather as *tore'ach b'Shabbos l'tzorech chol* — expending effort on *Shabbos* for weekday purposes. This reframing carries real practical implications. The question of whether one may study on *Shabbos* for an exam on Sunday, for instance, turns partly on which formulation one accepts.

### Intent & Context

A critical qualification is that the prohibition applies only when the action is genuinely done for tomorrow's benefit. Very often something done on *Shabbos* has the incidental effect of making things ready for *motzaei Shabbos*, but that is not the actual motivation. A common example: after *seudah shelishit*, well before *tzeit hakochavim*, clearing the table and removing the crumbs is not *hachana* for *motzaei Shabbos*. The motivation is simply that one does not want a messy room right now. The same logic applies to rinsing a bottle and placing it in recycling — one is not preparing for *motzaei Shabbos*; that is simply how one properly puts things away. The *Mishna Berura* and a growing number of contemporary *poskim* affirm this clearly.

Applying the same principle to folding: when one folds one's garments so that they do not become wrinkled — which is simply the way one treats clothing well — that is not *hachana*; it is attending to the garment for its own sake. The *Aruch HaShulchan* makes this point explicitly, as we will see. Similarly, carrying one's *tallis* home from *shul* after *Shabbos* davening is not *hachana l'machar* — one wants one's *tallis* at home, not left in *shul* where it might be lost or misplaced. The fact that it will then be available for next *Shabbos* does not transform the act into preparation for tomorrow.

## Folding & Tikun

### Folding Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּאָדָם אֶחָד, אֲבָל בִּשְׁנֵי בְּנֵי אָדָם — לֹא. וּבְאָדָם אֶחָד נָמֵי לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא בַּחֲדָשִׁים, אֲבָל בִּישָׁנִים — לָא. וָחֳדָשִׁים נָמֵי לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא בִּלְבָנִים, אֲבָל בִּצְבוּעִים — לָא. וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין לוֹ לְהַחֲלִיף, אֲבָל יֵשׁ לוֹ לְהַחֲלִיף — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Sages in **the school of Rabbi Yanai said: They only taught** that it is permitted **when one person** is folding the garments alone on Shabbat; **however, when two people** are folding the garments together, **no,** they may not do so, because when two people fold garments it is tantamount to repairing the garment. **And** with regard to **when a single person** is folding the garments, **we only said** it is permitted **when** he is folding a **new** garment, which is suitable to be worn even if it were not folded; **however,** with regard to **old** garments, **no,** he may not do so. **And** with regard to **new** garments, **we only said** that one may fold the garments **when** they are **white; however, when** they are **colored, no,** one may not do so. **And** with regard to white garments, **we only said** it is permitted to fold them on Shabbat when **one does not have** garments **into** which **one** could **change; however,** if **one has** other garments **into** which he could **change,** he may **not** fold them.

The *Gemara* introduces a significant additional restriction on folding that operates independently of the *hachana* concern. The school of Rabbi Yannai taught: *lo shanu ela b'adam echad* — folding is permitted only when done by a single person. With two people folding together — *b'shnei bnei adam lo* — it is forbidden. Furthermore, even with one person: *lo amran ela b'chadashim* — only new garments. Old garments, which are more creased and appear to require greater attention, are forbidden. And even new garments: *lo amran ela b'levanim* — only white ones. Colored garments are also forbidden. And even one person folding new white garments: *lo amran ela she'ein lo lehachalif* — only if one has no other garment available. If one has another garment to change into, the folding is forbidden because then one is plainly not folding for current use but for tomorrow — reverting to the *hachana* problem.

### Nireh Kemetaken

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל לֹא הָיוּ מְקַפְּלִים כְּלֵי לָבָן שֶׁלָּהֶן, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהָיָה לָהֶן לְהַחֲלִיף. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: אִם יֵשׁ לוֹ לְהַחֲלִיף — יַחֲלִיף, וְאִם אֵין לוֹ לְהַחֲלִיף — יְשַׁלְשֵׁל בִּבְגָדָיו. מַתְקִיף לַהּ רַב סָפְרָא: וְהָא מִיתְחֲזֵי כְּרָמוּת רוּחָא. כֵּיוָן דְּכׇל יוֹמָא לָא קָעָבֵיד, וְהָאִידָּנָא הוּא דְּקָא עָבֵיד — לָא מִיתְחֲזֵי כְּרָמוּת רוּחָא.
>
> **Translation:** And **it was taught** in the *Tosefta*: The members **of Rabban Gamliel's household did not** even **fold their white garments because they had** others **into** which they could **change. Rav Huna said: If one has** new garments **into** which he could **change** on Shabbat, **he will change** into those garments; **and if one does not have** garments **into** which he could **change, he** lets **his garments hang down** before Shabbat to beautify himself in deference to Shabbat as it used to be the custom of wealthy people to wear their clothes loosely. **Rav Safra strongly objects to this: Doesn't this appear as haughtiness?** The Gemara answers: **Since every day** he **does not do** so, **and now** in honor of Shabbat **he is doing so, it does not appear as haughtiness.** Rather it is apparent that he is acting in deference to Shabbat.

The reason for all these restrictions is stated by Rashi: *keshehem mekapplin otan mafashtin kimteihem venireh kemetakenin* — when garments are folded precisely, one smooths out the wrinkles, and this looks like one is fixing the garment, improving it, making it as good as new. The paradigmatic image is someone pressing their trousers precisely along the crease, smoothing everything perfectly — that is not merely putting a garment away, it is *nireh kemetaken*, it appears like a repair. The conditions enumerated — one person, new, white, no alternative — represent the cumulative threshold below which this appearance of fixing does not arise.

### No Alternative

The final condition — that there be no other garment available — works on two levels. First, if one has a different garment to wear for the rest of *Shabbos*, then one has no need for the folded garment today at all, which means the folding is inherently for tomorrow, raising the *hachana* problem once again. Second, the availability of an alternative also amplifies the appearance of *tikun*, since one is clearly investing care and effort in a garment for future use rather than immediate need.

### Tallit Practice

In practice, one can observe in many *shuls* a range of customs regarding *tallis* folding. Some people fold their *tallis* inside out — reversing the natural crease — precisely on account of this *Gemara*. Others simply roll it up rather than fold it. These practices reflect a concern about *nireh kemetaken*.

### Aruch HaShulchan View

The *Aruch HaShulchan*, however, takes a considerably more lenient position. He argues that this *Gemara* addresses a very specific and exacting type of folding — something akin to pressing a garment's crease with great precision, repeatedly, so that the crease becomes sharply defined. That is *nireh kemetaken*. Ordinary folding along the garment's natural folds, done simply to keep it from wrinkling, is not subject to this concern at all — it is merely how one properly stores a garment. Given this understanding, normal folding of a *tallis* on its natural folds presents no issue.

## Kavod Shabbat

### Shabbat Clothes

Rav Huna rules: *im yesh lo lehachalif yachalif* — if one has garments to change into, he should change into them for *Shabbos*. One is obligated to wear special *Shabbos* garments. This flows naturally from the prior discussion of folding: if one has a second set of clothes, one does not even need to fold the current ones — one changes into something designated for *Shabbos*.

### Lowering Hem

But what if one has no special *Shabbos* garments? Rav Huna continues: *yeshalshel bevagdav* — one should lower the hem of one's garment. In the world of the *Gemara*, the hem of working clothes was typically held up for ease of movement. Lowering it to near the ground was a mark of leisure or wealth — one who did not need to run about or labor. Even if one's garment is the same weekday garment, adjusting the hem signals that today is different; it is a day of rest and dignity. Rav Safra objects: *veha mitchazi keramut rucha* — doesn't this look arrogant, like one of the wealthy strutting about? The *Gemara* answers: *keivan dechol yoma lo ka'avid veha'idna hu deka'avid lo mitchazi keramut rucha* — since one does not normally do this and does it only now for *Shabbos*, it does not read as arrogance but as honor for the day.

### Vekibadto Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְכִבַּדְתּוֹ מֵעֲשׂוֹת דְּרָכֶיךָ״. ״וְכִבַּדְתּוֹ״ — שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא מַלְבּוּשְׁךָ שֶׁל שַׁבָּת כְּמַלְבּוּשְׁךָ שֶׁל חוֹל, וְכִי הָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן קָרֵי לְמָאנֵיהּ ״מְכַבְּדוֹתַי״. ״מֵעֲשׂוֹת דְּרָכֶיךָ״ — שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא הִילּוּכְךָ שֶׁל שַׁבָּת כְּהִילּוּכְךָ שֶׁל חוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** On a related note, the Gemara cites what we learned with regard to the following passage: "If you keep your feet from breaking, from pursuing your affairs on My holy day, and you call Shabbat a delight, the Lord's holy day honorable, **and you honor it by not going your own way,** from attending to your affairs and speaking idle words" (Isaiah 58:13). The Rabbis derived from the words **"and you honor it" that your dress on Shabbat should not be like your dress during the week, as Rabbi Yoḥanan** would **refer to his clothing as my honor,** indicating that appropriate clothing is a form of deference. The words **"going your own way"** mean **that your walking on Shabbat should not be like your walking during the week.**

The *Gemara* now expounds the verse from Yeshayahu: *vechibadto me'asot derachecha* — you shall honor it (*Shabbos*) by refraining from your normal ways. *V'chibadto* — you honor it: *shelo yehei malbushcha shel Shabbos kemalbushcha shel chol* — your *Shabbos* clothing should not be the same as your weekday clothing. The proof from practice: Rabbi Yochanan called his garments *mechabduyotai* — the things that give him honor. Clothing is how one expresses respect, for oneself and for the environment one inhabits. On *Shabbos*, the way one dresses is part of how one honors the day itself.

This raises an interesting tension for those who wear formal attire every weekday — suit and tie daily — since that makes it difficult to have distinctive *Shabbos* garments. One practical resolution is to reserve one's finest suits exclusively for *Shabbos*, or to wear formal items on *Shabbos* — such as French-cuff shirts — that one intentionally avoids on weekdays. The goal is differentiation, not merely formality.

### Gassah Walking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מֵעֲשׂוֹת דְּרָכֶיךָ״ — שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא הִילּוּכְךָ שֶׁל שַׁבָּת כְּהִילּוּכְךָ שֶׁל חוֹל. ״מִמְּצוֹא חֶפְצְךָ״ — חֲפָצֶיךָ אֲסוּרִין, חֶפְצֵי שָׁמַיִם מוּתָּרִין. ״וְדַבֵּר דָּבָר״ — שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא דִּבּוּרְךָ שֶׁל שַׁבָּת כְּדִבּוּרְךָ שֶׁל חוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The words **"going your own way"** mean it **is prohibited** to deal with **your** weekday **affairs** and to speak about them on Shabbat. However, **affairs of Heaven,** i.e., those pertaining to mitzvot, **are permitted. "And speaking idle words"** means **that your speech on Shabbat should not be like your speech during the week,** i.e., one should not discuss his weekday affairs on Shabbat.

The *Gemara* continues with the verse: *shelo yehei hiluchcha shel Shabbos ke'hiluchcha shel chol* — your walking on *Shabbos* should not be like your walking on the weekday. What does this mean practically? The *Gemara* answers with a ruling from Rav Huna citing Rav: if one is walking on *Shabbos* and encounters a stream, *im yachol l'haniach raglo rishona kodem she'te'aker shniyah* — if one can step across such that the second foot lands on the far bank before the first foot is lifted off the ground, it is permitted. If not, it is forbidden — since jumping or stretching dramatically is not the composed manner of *Shabbos* walking. Rava objects: must one then walk all the way around? That requires more effort. And if one wades through, one risks getting one's garments wet and then wringing them out. The *Gemara* concedes: *keivan d'lo efshar shapir dami* — when there is no practical alternative, it is fine.

### Cheftzei Shamayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִמְּצוֹא חֶפְצְךָ״ — חֲפָצֶיךָ אֲסוּרִין, חֶפְצֵי שָׁמַיִם מוּתָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **"From attending to your affairs"** means it **is prohibited** to deal with **your** weekday **affairs** and to speak about them on Shabbat. However, **affairs of Heaven,** i.e., those pertaining to mitzvot, **are permitted.**

The verse continues: *mimtzo cheftzecha* — from pursuing your desires. *Cheftzecha asurin* — your personal pursuits are forbidden. *Cheftzei Shamayim mutarin* — but pursuits for the sake of Heaven are permitted. This is the source for the well-known ruling that one may make announcements after *davening* for communal and religious purposes — those fall under *cheftzei Shamayim*. Similarly, one may speak about hiring a teacher for one's children — *l'tzorech mitzvah* — even though it involves planning for weekday activity, because it is a divine purpose.

### Speech Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּבּוּר אָסוּר, הִרְהוּר — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** However, it is only **speech** that they said is **prohibited,** whereas merely **contemplating** weekday affairs **is permitted.**

The final clause: *v'daber davar — shelo yehei diburcha shel Shabbos k'diburcha shel chol* — your speech on *Shabbos* should not be like your speech on the weekday. Rashi explains this as referring to business talk, market prices, financial accounting — the stuff of weekday conversation. This goes beyond the *mimtzo cheftzecha* prohibition, which concerns actually pursuing one's affairs; here we are told that even idle conversation about such matters — the kind of *shmoozing* one does about business without actively planning anything — is also inappropriate on *Shabbos*. *Tosafot* raises this distinction and cites Rabbeinu Tam, who notes the redundancy if *dover davar* merely repeats what *mimtzo cheftzecha* already covers.

*Tosafot* then cites a remarkable *Yerushalmi* in the name of Rabbeinu Tam: Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai had an elderly mother who talked a great deal on *Shabbos*. He said to her, "Mother, it is *Shabbos*," and she fell silent. The implication is that one simply should not be as generally talkative on *Shabbos* as on the weekday — a more sweeping disposition of quiet and restraint. The *Yerushalmi* also notes that even greeting one another on *Shabbos* required some leniency to be permitted, suggesting that there was a genuine sense among some that *Shabbos* was characterized by a quality of stillness. Nevertheless, the mainstream position — reflected in Rashi — focuses on content (business talk, planning, weekday pursuits) rather than on quantity of speech generally.

One final distinction: *dibur assur, hirhur muttar* — speaking is forbidden, but thinking is permitted. One may mentally plan for *motzaei Shabbos* even if one may not vocalize those plans. There is only so much one can reasonably regulate, and controlling one's thoughts entirely is not expected.

## Stream & Dirt

### Stream Crossing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כְּדִבְעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי מֵרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מַהוּ לִפְסוֹעַ פְּסִיעָה גַּסָּה בְּשַׁבָּת? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְכִי בַּחוֹל מִי הוּתְּרָה? שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: פְּסִיעָה גַּסָּה נוֹטֶלֶת אֶחָד מֵחֲמֵשׁ מֵאוֹת מִמְּאוֹר עֵינָיו שֶׁל אָדָם. וּמַהְדַּר לֵיהּ בְּקִידּוּשָׁא דְּבֵי שִׁמְשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather** say that walking that is defined as characteristic of weekday walking involves taking large steps. **As Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **raised a dilemma before Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei: What is** the ruling with regard to **taking large steps on Shabbat?** That is what the Gemara meant when it used the phrase: Your walking during the week. Rabbi Yishmael **said to him: And during the week are** large steps **permitted? As I say: A large step takes** away **one five-hundredth of a person's eyesight.** The Gemara comments: **And** his eyesight **is restored to him during *kiddush* on** Shabbat **evening.**

The *Gemara* returns to the question of walking on *Shabbos*: *mahu lifso'a pesiah gassah b'Shabbos* — may one take a large step on *Shabbos*? This was asked by Rav of Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yosi. The reply is striking: *ve-chi be-chol mi hutar* — who ever said it was permitted even on a weekday? *She-ani omer: pesiah gassah noteles echad mi-chamesh meos mi-me'or einav shel adam* — a large step removes one five-hundredth of a person's eyesight. The remedy, however, is available: *u-mahadra lei b'kiddusha d'bei shimshi* — it is restored through the *Kiddush* on Friday night. Rashi understands this as referring to drinking the wine of *Kiddush*. The precise meaning of this physiological claim is not entirely clear, and various more metaphorical interpretations have been proposed — among them, that someone rushing through life with great strides does not truly see what surrounds him, and that the slow, deliberate *Kiddush* of Friday night restores that awareness. Whatever the deeper meaning, the practical point is clear: an unnaturally large, rushing step is inappropriate and even harmful, both on *Shabbos* and in general.

The *Gemara* implicitly notes the best practical solution in the stream case: when there is truly no alternative, one takes the large step — *keivan d'lo efshar shapir dami*. The ruling about *pesiah gassah* similarly applies only when the large step is chosen unnecessarily; unavoidable circumstances are treated differently.

### Eating Dirt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי מֵרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מַהוּ לֶאֱכוֹל אֲדָמָה בְּשַׁבָּת? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְכִי בַּחוֹל מִי הוּתְּרָה? שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: אַף בַּחוֹל אָסוּר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מַלְקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** And **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **raised a dilemma before Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei: What is** the ruling with regard to **eating earth** for medicinal purposes **on Shabbat?** Rabbi Yishmael **said to him: And during the week is it permitted** to eat soil? **As I say: Even during the week it is prohibited because it is harmful.**

A parallel exchange: *mahu le'echol adamah b'Shabbos* — may one eat dirt on *Shabbos*? This was apparently practiced sometimes as a folk remedy. Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yosi responds identically: *ve-chi be-chol mi hutar* — who ever said this was permitted on a weekday? *She-ani omer: af be-chol assur mipnei she-hu malkeh* — even on a weekday it is forbidden, because it is injurious to health. The *Gemara* will continue with the specific hazards of eating dirt.

### Dirt Consequences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: כָּל הָאוֹכֵל מֵעֲפָרָהּ שֶׁל בָּבֶל — כְּאִילּוּ אוֹכֵל מִבְּשַׂר אֲבוֹתָיו. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: כְּאִילּוּ אוֹכֵל שְׁקָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami said: Anyone who eats the dust of Babylonia, it is as if he is eating the flesh of his ancestors** buried there. **And some say: It is as if he eats abominations and creeping creatures, as it is written: "And He wiped out all that existed** on the face of the earth, from humans to animals, to creeping creatures to the birds in the sky, and they were wiped off the land" (Genesis 7:23).

Rav Ami declares: *kol ha-ochel me-afra d'Bavel k'ilu ochel mi-bsar avosav* — anyone who eats from the soil of Bavel is as if he ate from the flesh of his ancestors. The Maharsha explains that in Bavel — unlike *Eretz Yisrael* where burial sites were well-known — people were interred throughout the land in the wake of the Babylonian exile, so one is likely consuming decomposed matter of the dead. A more severe formulation is also cited: *ve-yesh omrim k'ilu ochel shkatzim u-remasim* — some say it is as if one ate forbidden crawling creatures and rodents.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ ״שִׁנְעָר״ — שֶׁכׇּל מֵתֵי מַבּוּל נִנְעֲרוּ לְשָׁם. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ ״מְצוּלָה״ — שֶׁכׇּל מֵתֵי מַבּוּל נִצְטַלְּלוּ לְשָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos dead residue in the ground, **Reish Lakish said: Why is** Babylonia **called Shinar?** It is **because all those who died in** the **Flood were deposited there [*ninaru lesham*]. Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Why is** Babylonia **called Metzula?** It is **because all those who died in** the **Flood sank there [*nitztalelu lesham*].**

The rhetorical basis for this latter claim is traced through Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan. Reish Lakish asks: *lama nikra shema Shinar* — why was Bavel called *Shinar* in the Torah? *She-kol mesei mabul ninaru l'sham* — because all the dead of the Flood were shaken out there, since Bavel sits at a low elevation and everything flowed to it. Rabbi Yochanan similarly: *lama nikra shema Metzula* — why was it called *Metzula*? *She-kol metei mabul nitzlelu l'sham* — all the dead of the Flood floated there.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי כֵּיוָן דְּמַלְקֵי גְּזַרוּ בֵּיהּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, **since** soil **is harmful, the Sages issued a decree** not to eat it.

The *Gemara* objects to the second formulation: those dissolved remains have long since ceased to be *shkatzim v'remasim* in any halachic sense. The answer: *keivan d'malki, gazru beih Rabbanan* — since eating dirt is injurious to health, the Rabbis imposed this rhetoric as a safeguard, framing it as equivalent to having eaten forbidden animals. The language *gazru beih Rabbanan* is striking — it makes this not merely rhetorical hyperbole but almost a formal rabbinic equivalence, intended to deter the practice.

That such rhetoric was necessary suggests that eating dirt was genuinely practiced, which in turn is perhaps evidence of significant poverty in the population the *Gemara* is addressing.

### Clay Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּהָא הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דַּאֲכַל גַּרְגִּישְׁתָּא וַאֲכַל תַּחְלֵי, וּקְדַחוּ לֵיהּ תַּחְלֵי בְּלִבֵּיהּ, וּמִית.
>
> **Translation:** The decree was not issued due to the prohibition of eating creeping creatures; rather, it was issued **because a certain person ate soil** for medicinal purposes **and** also **ate cress.** The cress took root in the soil that was inside him and began to grow. **And the cress punctured his heart and he died.**

A concrete case: *hahu gavra d'achal gargashta v'achal tichli* — there was a man who ate gray clay and also ate cress. *V'kadchu tichlaya b'libeih u'meis* — the cress took root in the clay inside his body and caused a fatal fever. Rashi explains: *nikletu ha'tichlim b'adama v'gidlu* — the cress seeds lodged in the clay and literally sprouted, growing within the man and destroying him. This is precisely the kind of case — someone ingesting a seed which germinates internally — that has in fact been documented in modern medicine, lending the *Gemara*'s account an unexpected plausibility.

## Ruth Narrative

### Ruth's Preparation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְרָחַצְתְּ וָסַכְתְּ וְשַׂמְתְּ שִׂמְלֹתַיִךְ״ — אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אֵלּוּ בְּגָדִים שֶׁל שַׁבָּת. ״תֵּן לְחָכָם וְיֶחְכַּם עוֹד״ — אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: זוֹ רוּת הַמּוֹאֲבִיָּה וּשְׁמוּאֵל הָרָמָתִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss Shabbat. Naomi advised Ruth: **"And you shall bathe, and anoint yourself, and put on your robes,** and go down to the threshing floor. Do not make yourself known to the man until he has finished eating and drinking" (Ruth 3:3). **Rabbi Elazar said: These** robes **are Shabbat garments** that Naomi told her to wear in honor of the occasion. Apropos the book of Ruth, the Gemara cites additional statements of Rabbi Elazar with regard to Ruth: **"Give to the wise one and he will become wiser;** let the righteous one know and he will learn more" (Proverbs 9:9). **Rabbi Elazar said: This** refers to **Ruth the Moabite and Samuel of Rama,** who received advice and added to it with their wisdom.

Returning to the theme of *Shabbos* garments, the *Gemara* cites the verse from the Book of Ruth: *v'rachatzat v'sacht v'shamt simlotaich alaich v'yaradt ha'goren* — bathe and anoint yourself and put on your garments and go down to the threshing floor. These are Naomi's instructions to Ruth. Rabbi Elazar comments: *elu begadim shel Shabbos* — these are Shabbos garments. One might ask why the verse needs to say "put on your garments" at all — everyone gets dressed. The answer is that these are not merely any garments but specifically her finest, her special clothes, the equivalent of *Shabbos* dress. *Ten l'chacham v'yichkam od* — give to the wise and they will add wisdom. The *Gemara* now draws a comparison between Ruth and Shmuel as instances of someone who improved upon instructions given to them.

### Ruth's Modesty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רוּת, דְּאִילּוּ נָעֳמִי קָאָמְרָה לַהּ ״וְרָחַצְתְּ וָסַכְתְּ וְשַׂמְתְּ שִׂמְלֹתַיִךְ עָלַיִךְ וְיָרַדְתְּ הַגֹּרֶן״, וְאִילּוּ בְּדִידַהּ כְּתִיב: ״וַתֵּרֶד הַגֹּרֶן״, וַהֲדַר ״וַתַּעַשׂ כְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר צִוַּתָּה חֲמוֹתָהּ״. שְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאִילּוּ עֵלִי קָאֲמַר לֵיהּ ״שְׁכָב וְהָיָה אִם יִקְרָא אֵלֶיךָ וְאָמַרְתָּ דַּבֵּר ה׳ כִּי שׁוֹמֵעַ עַבְדֶּךָ״, וְאִילּוּ בְּדִידֵיהּ כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״וַיָּבֹא ה׳ וַיִּתְיַצַּב וַיִּקְרָא כְפַעַם בְּפַעַם שְׁמוּאֵל שְׁמוּאֵל וַיֹּאמֶר שְׁמוּאֵל דַּבֵּר כִּי שֹׁמֵעַ עַבְדֶּךָ״, וְלָא אֲמַר ״דַּבֵּר ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates. **Whereas Naomi said to Ruth: "And you shall bathe, and anoint yourself, and put on your robes, and go down to the threshing floor," but with regard to** Ruth **herself it is written, "And she went down to the threshing floor"** (Ruth 3:6), **and** only **afterward does it say, "And she did according to all that her mother-in-law commanded her."** Ruth decided to anoint herself at the threshing floor and not on the road so that people would not meet her on the way there and suspect her of immorality. **Whereas Eli said to Samuel:** "Go and **lie down and if He calls you, you say: Speak, Lord, for Your servant is listening"** (I Samuel 3:9), **but with regard to** Samuel **himself it is written: "And the Lord came and stood, and He called like He did the other times: Samuel, Samuel. And Samuel said: Speak, for Your servant is listening"** (I Samuel 3:10), **and he did not say: Speak, Lord,** since he would not assume it was God speaking to him until he was sure of it.

Naomi told Ruth to bathe, anoint herself, put on her garments, and then go down to the threshing floor — in that order. But the verse describing what Ruth actually did inverts the sequence: *v'tered ha'goren v'hadar vata'as k'chol asher tzivata chamota* — she went down first, and only then did all that her mother-in-law had commanded. Rashi explains that Ruth realized the wisdom of this reversal: had she prepared herself at home and then walked to the threshing floor in her finest clothes, she would have drawn attention and comment. By going down first and then adorning herself in a private place, she preserved her dignity and avoided the scrutiny of passersby.

The parallel case involves Shmuel. Eli instructed him: if the voice calls again, say *daber Hashem ki shomeia avdecha* — speak, Lord, for Your servant is listening. But when God called and Shmuel responded, he said only *daber ki shomeia avdecha* — speak, for I am listening — omitting God's name. Rashi suggests two reasons: either Shmuel was not entirely certain the voice was divine and therefore did not want to invoke God's name without certainty, or he was concerned about pronouncing the name unnecessarily. In either case, Shmuel improved upon his instructions, just as Ruth did.

### Ruth's Knowledge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתֵּלֶךְ וַתָּבֹא וַתְּלַקֵּט בַּשָּׂדֶה״ — אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שֶׁהָלְכָה וּבָאת, הָלְכָה וּבָאת, עַד שֶׁמָּצְאָה בְּנֵי אָדָם הַמְהוּגָּנִין לְיֵלֵךְ עִמָּהֶם. ״וַיֹּאמֶר בּוֹעַז לְנַעֲרוֹ הַנִּצָּב עַל הַקּוֹצְרִים לְמִי הַנַּעֲרָה הַזֹּאת״ — וְכִי דַרְכּוֹ שֶׁל בּוֹעַז לִשְׁאוֹל בְּנַעֲרָה? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: דְּבַר חָכְמָה רָאָה בָּהּ. שְׁתֵּי שִׁבֳּלִין — לוֹקֶטֶת, שָׁלֹשׁ שִׁבֳּלִין — אֵינָהּ לוֹקֶטֶת.
>
> **Translation:** And the verse in Ruth states: **"And she went, and she came, and she collected in the field** after the harvesters" (Ruth 2:3). **Rabbi Elazar said:** This verse teaches **that she went and came, went and came, until she found suitable people with whom to walk.** It also says: **"And Boaz said to his youth who was standing over the harvesters: To whom does this young woman belong?"** (Ruth 2:5). This is surprising: **And was it Boaz's habit to inquire about a young woman? Rabbi Elazar said: He saw in her a matter of wisdom** and Torah, and that is why he asked about her. What he saw was that **she collected two stalks,** but **she did not collect three stalks.** She thereby acted in accordance with the *halakha* that three stalks lying together are not considered to be gleanings left for the poor; rather, they remain in the possession of the owner of the field.

*V'telech v'tavo v'tilakeit ba'sadei* — she went and came and she gleaned in the field. Rabbi Elazar asks: the verse says both "went" and "came" — why not simply say she went and gleaned? *She-halcha u'vata halcha u'vata* — she went back and forth, back and forth, *ad she-matza b'nei adam ha'mehuganim la'yelech imahem* — until she found a group of properly behaved people to go with. She was careful from the outset about the company she kept.

Then Boaz noticed her. His foreman asked — or Boaz asked his foreman — *l'mi hana'ara hazot* — to whom does this young woman belong? One might wonder: was it Boaz's habit to inquire after young women? Rabbi Elazar explains: *dvar chochma ra'a bah* — it was a matter of wisdom he observed in her. *Shnei shibolim likta, shlosha shibolim eina likta* — when two stalks had fallen, she gathered them; when three fell together, she did not take them, because halachically *leket* (gleanings) applies only to two dropped stalks, not three. Boaz saw someone who knew the *halacha* and scrupulously observed it even when no one was watching.

### Boaz's Choice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: דְּבַר צְנִיעוּת רָאָה בָּהּ, עוֹמְדוֹת — מְעוּמָּד, נוֹפְלוֹת — מְיוּשָּׁב.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught in a *baraita*: He saw a matter of modesty in her** when she was collecting stalks. She picked stalks that were **upright** while she was **standing,** and stalks that had **fallen** she picked while **sitting;** due to her modesty she did not bend over to take them.

The *baraita* adds a parallel observation: *dvar tzniyut ra'a bah* — Boaz also saw a matter of *tzniut* (modesty) in her. *Omedet me'umad* — when she was gathering standing stalks, she stood upright. *Noflot meyoshav* — when she was picking up stalks that had fallen to the ground, she would not bend over; she crouched down instead to avoid immodest posture. It was the combination of halachic precision and refined modesty that captured Boaz's attention.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְכֹה תִדְבָּקִין עִם נַעֲרוֹתָי״ — וְכִי דַּרְכּוֹ שֶׁל בּוֹעַז לִדָּבֵק עִם הַנָּשִׁים? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזָא ״וַתִּשַּׁק עׇרְפָּה לַחֲמוֹתָהּ וְרוּת דָּבְקָה בָּהּ״, אֲמַר: שְׁרֵי לְאִידַּבּוֹקֵי בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** It also says: "And Boaz said to Ruth: Do you hear, my daughter? Do not go to glean in another field and do not leave from here, **but cling to my maidens"** (Ruth 2:8). This is also surprising. **And was it Boaz's habit to cling to women? Rabbi Elazar said: Since he saw "And Orpah kissed her mother-in-law and Ruth clung to her"** (Ruth 1:14), **he said: It is permitted to cling to** a woman like this.

Boaz then told Ruth: *vatishak Orpa lachamota v'Rut davka bah* — Orpa kissed her mother-in-law and left, but Ruth cleaved to her. Boaz says to Ruth that she should cleave to his young women — *vatidbaki im na'arotai*. The *Gemara* notes the apparent awkwardness of a man using the word "cleave" in addressing a woman. Rabbi Elazar explains: *shari l'idbuki bah* — since Ruth herself was defined by the act of *deveikut* (cleaving) — cleaving to Naomi when Orpa turned back — Boaz's use of that very word was an appropriate echo and expression of admiration.

### Kingdom Hints

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֹּאמֶר לָה בֹעַז לְעֵת הָאֹכֶל גֹּשִׁי הֲלֹם״ — אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, רֶמֶז רָמַז לָהּ: עֲתִידָה מַלְכוּת בֵּית דָּוִד לָצֵאת מִמֵּךְ, דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ ״הֲלֹם״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיָּבֹא הַמֶּלֶךְ דָּוִד וַיֵּשֶׁב לִפְנֵי ה׳ וַיֹּאמֶר מִי אָנֹכִי אֲדֹנָי ה׳ וּמִי בֵיתִי כִּי הֲבֵאתַנִי עַד הֲלֹם״. ״וְטָבַלְתְּ פִּתֵּךְ בַּחֹמֶץ״, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִכָּאן שֶׁהַחוֹמֶץ יָפֶה לַשָּׁרָב.
>
> **Translation:** It also says: **"And Boaz said to her at mealtime: Come here [*halom*]** and eat from the bread and dip your bread in vinegar. And she sat beside the harvesters and he gave her roasted grain and she ate, and she was satiated, and she left some over" (Ruth 2:14). **Rabbi Elazar** interpreted this and **said** that **he hinted to her** prophetically: **In the future the kingdom of David will come from you, as it is written with regard to it,** i.e., the kingdom of David: **"Here," as it is stated: "And King David came and sat before God and said: Who am I, Lord, God, and who is my family that You have brought me to here [*halom*]?"** (II Samuel 7:18). With regard to his saying: **"And dip your bread in vinegar"** (Ruth 2:14), **Rabbi Elazar said: From here** we see **that vinegar** is **good** to have **in hot weather.**

Boaz told Ruth: *vatisbat la kali vatochal vatisba* — he handed her parched grain and she ate and was satisfied. But first he said *goshi halom* — draw near. Rabbi Elazar comments: *remez ramaz lah* — he was hinting to her, though not necessarily consciously, that *atida malchut beit David latzet mimech* — the kingdom of the house of David would one day come from her. The word *halom* is the same word that appears in the verse where King David himself said: *mi anochi Hashem Elokim u'mi veiti ki hevi'atani ad halom* — who am I, Lord, that You have brought me to this point? The verbal echo links Ruth's future to David's acknowledgment of his own kingship.

When Boaz said *v'tavalt pitech bachometz* — dip your bread in vinegar — Rabbi Elazar sees a practical message: vinegar is beneficial in the heat. But Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani reads another hint: *atid ben latzet mimech she-ma'asav kashim k'chometz* — a descendant will come from you whose deeds are as harsh as vinegar. That is Menashe.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: רֶמֶז רָמַז לָהּ עָתִיד בֵּן לָצֵאת מִמֵּךְ שֶׁמַּעֲשָׂיו קָשִׁים כַּחֹמֶץ, וּמַנּוּ — מְנַשֶּׁה. ״וַתֵּשֶׁב מִצַּד הַקּוֹצְרִים״ — אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִצַּד הַקּוֹצְרִים וְלֹא בְּתוֹךְ הַקּוֹצְרִים, רֶמֶז רָמַז לָהּ שֶׁעֲתִידָה מַלְכוּת בֵּית דָּוִד שֶׁתִּתְחַלֵּק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **he hinted to her: A son will come from you in the future whose actions will be** as **sharp as vinegar, and who is he?** King **Manasseh. "And she sat beside the harvesters." Rabbi Elazar said with regard to this: Beside the harvesters, and not among the harvesters. He hinted to her that the kingdom of David will be divided in the future** and her children will not always be in the center of Israel.

When Ruth sat *mitzad hakotzrim* — by the side of the harvesters — Rabbi Elazar notes: *v'lo betoch hakotzrim* — not in their midst. Rashi's picture is that Boaz stood at one end and Ruth at the other, with all the harvesters between them. Rabbi Elazar sees in this spatial arrangement a hint that *atida malchut beit David shetehei metchaleket* — the kingdom of David would eventually be divided.

### Wealth Timeline

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּצְבׇּט לָהּ קָלִי וַתֹּאכַל״. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: ״וַתֹּאכַל״ — בִּימֵי דָוִד, ״וַתִּשְׂבַּע״ — בִּימֵי שְׁלֹמֹה, ״וַתֹּתַר״ — בִּימֵי חִזְקִיָּה. וְאִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: ״וַתֹּאכַל״ — בִּימֵי דָּוִד וּבִימֵי שְׁלֹמֹה, ״וַתִּשְׂבַּע״ — בִּימֵי חִזְקִיָּה, ״וַתֹּתַר״ — בִּימֵי רַבִּי. דְּאָמַר מָר: אֲהוּרְיָרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי הֲוָה עַתִּיר מִשַּׁבּוּר מַלְכָּא. בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: ״וַתֹּאכַל״ — בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״וַתִּשְׂבַּע״ — לִימוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ, ״וַתֹּתַר״ — לְעָתִיד לָבֹא.
>
> **Translation:** It also says in the verse: "And he gave her roasted grain and she ate, and she was satiated, and she left some over." The Gemara explains: **"And he gave her roasted grain and she ate";** this is also interpreted as a prophetic message. **Rabbi Elazar said: "And she ate"** was fulfilled by her children's children **in the days of David; "And she was satiated"** was fulfilled **in the days of Solomon; "And she left some over"** was fulfilled **in the days of Hezekiah. And some say** that there is a different interpretation: **"And she ate,"** was fulfilled **in the days of David and Solomon; "And she was satiated,"** was fulfilled **in the days of Hezekiah; "And she left some over"** was fulfilled **in the days of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **As the Master said: Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi's **horsekeeper [*ahuriyarei*] was richer than the king** of Persia. **It was taught in a *baraita*: "And she ate," in this world; "and she was satiated," in the days of the Messiah; "and she left some over," in the future,** at the end of days.

*Vayitzbat la kali vatochal vatisba* — he handed her parched grain and she ate and was satisfied. Rabbi Elazar expounds on the sequence of verbs in the verse. *Vatochal* — she ate: *bimei David* — this refers to the era of David, a time of basic sustenance and beginning. *Vatisba* — she was satisfied: *bimei Shlomo* — in the era of Shlomo, when there was genuine abundance. *Vattotar* — and there was leftover: *bimei Chizkiyahu* — in the time of Hezekiah, when wealth reached its fullest expression.

An alternative reading is also offered: *vatochal bimei David u'bimei Shlomo* — both David and Shlomo represent the first stage of simply eating, *vatisba bimei Chizkiyahu* — satisfaction came in Hezekiah's time, *vattotar bimei Rebbe* — and the surplus, the peak of wealth, corresponds to the era of Rebbe (Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi). This is a remarkable claim: the personal wealth of Rebbe exceeded that of the kingdoms of Shlomo and Chizkiyahu. The *Gemara* supports this: the one who managed Rebbe's stables was said to be wealthier than the Persian king Shabur Malka. Whether this is literal or metaphorical (some suggest it refers to Torah wealth rather than material wealth), the tradition is clear: Rebbe's household represented an extraordinary peak.

### Eschatology

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: ״וַתֹּאכַל״ — בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״וַתִּשְׂבַּע״ — לִימוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ, ״וַתֹּתַר״ — לְעָתִיד לָבֹא.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught in a *baraita*: "And she ate," in this world; "and she was satiated," in the days of the Messiah; "and she left some over," in the future,** at the end of days.

The *baraita* concludes with an eschatological reading of the same verse: *vatochal ba-olam ha-zeh* — she ate, referring to this world; *vatisba l'yemos ha-mashiach* — she was satisfied, referring to the messianic era; *vattotar l'asid lavo* — and there was leftover, referring to the world to come. The progression of eating, satisfaction, and surplus maps onto the three stages of Jewish eschatological history — the present world, the messianic age, and the ultimate future. With that expansive vision, the daf comes to a close.
