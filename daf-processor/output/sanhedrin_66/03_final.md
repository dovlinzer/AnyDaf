# Sanhedrin 66 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ba'al Ov & Yidoni

### Ba'al Ov Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בַּעַל אוֹב – אֶחָד הַמְדַבֵּר בֵּין הַפְּרָקִים וּמִבֵּין אַצִּילֵי יָדָיו. יִדְּעוֹנִי – זֶה הַמַּנִּיחַ עֶצֶם יָדוּעַ בְּפִיו, וְהוּא מְדַבֵּר מֵאֵלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: A necromancer is one who** causes the voice of the dead to be heard **speaking** from **between** his **joints or from his armpit. A sorcerer [*yideoni*] is one who places a bone of** an animal called **a *yadua* in his mouth, and** the bone **speaks on its own.**

We pick up in the middle of 65b, approximately fifteen lines down, at the *tanu rabbanan*. The discussion concerns *ba'al ov*, and it follows an extended treatment of what constitutes a *ma'aseh* — specifically whether speech qualifies as an act. The *gemara* had established that speech is characterized as *yeshna b'kol* (it exists by means of voice) and *yeshna b'lev* (it exists by means of thought or inner expression), meaning the physical movement of the lips is not what gives it its legal character; it is fundamentally an expression of thought. That reasoning generated various subsidiary questions, including notable exceptions such as *eidim zomemim* and the case of yelling at an animal to stop plowing. With that prior discussion as backdrop, the *gemara* now turns to the specific acts of witchcraft that carry capital liability — chiefly *ba'al ov* and *yidoni*.

The *tanu rabbanan* defines *ba'al ov* as encompassing two distinct forms: *echad ha-ma'aleh b'zichuro* — one who causes the spirit to ascend through the male organ, where, as Rashi explains, the spirit rests upon the practitioner's penis — and *echad ha-nish'al b'gugoles* — one who consults through a skull, in the manner of the famous Hamlet scene, causing the spirit to speak from within the skull. Either method constitutes *ba'al ov*.

### Two Forms of Ba'al Ov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה בֵּין זֶה לָזֶה? מַעֲלֶה בִּזְכוּרוֹ – אֵינוֹ עוֹלֶה כְּדַרְכּוֹ, וְאֵינוֹ עוֹלֶה בַּשַּׁבָּת. נִשְׁאָל בְּגוּלְגּוֹלֶת – עוֹלֶה כְּדַרְכּוֹ, וְעוֹלֶה בַּשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the difference **between this** type of necromancer **and that** type of necromancer? When one **raises** the dead **with his *zekhur*,** the dead **does not rise in its** usual **manner,** but appears upside-down, **and** it **does not rise on Shabbat.** By contrast, when one **inquires** about the future **from a skull,** the dead **rises in its** usual **manner, and** it **rises [*oleh*]** even **on Shabbat.**

The *gemara* then asks: *ma bein zeh l'zeh* — what is the practical difference between the two forms? When performed through the first method, the dead spirit does not ascend in the natural manner; it rises feet-first rather than head-first. Moreover, *eino oleh b'Shabbos* — it will not ascend on *Shabbos*. By contrast, when performed through the skull, *oleh k'darko* — the spirit ascends naturally, head-first, and *oleh b'Shabbos* — it will rise even on *Shabbos*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹלֶה? לְהֵיכָא סָלֵיק? הָא קַמֵּיהּ מַנַּח! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: עוֹנֶה כְּדַרְכּוֹ, וְעוֹנֶה בַּשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to the wording of the last statement: **Rises? To where does** it **rise? Isn't** the skull **lying before him? Rather, say** as follows: The dead **answers in its** usual **manner, and** it **answers [*ve'oneh*]** even **on Shabbat.**

At this point the *gemara* raises a pointed objection: *oleh*? What does it mean that it "rises" in the case of the skull? *L'heicha salik* — where is it going? *Hakamay manach* — it is right there in front of you, sitting in the skull. The notion of a body rising does not quite apply when you are simply making a skull speak. The *gemara* therefore emends: *ela eima oneh k'darko* — say rather that it *answers* naturally, *v'oneh b'Shabbos* — and it answers even on *Shabbos*. One interpretation offered is that in the first method, the spirit resides within the sorcerer's joints or knuckles, whereas in the second it resides directly within the skull — which perhaps explains why the skull method can function on *Shabbos* without the need for any additional sorcerous preparation.

### Turnus Rufus Exchange

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף שְׁאֵלָה זוֹ שָׁאַל טוֹרָנוּסְרוּפוּס הָרָשָׁע אֶת רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. אָמַר לוֹ: וּמָה יוֹם מִיָּמִים? אָמַר לוֹ: וּמָה גְּבַר מִגּוּבְרִין? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּמָרִי צָבֵי. שַׁבָּת נָמֵי דְּמָרִי צָבֵי.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the statement that the dead do not rise on Shabbat, the Gemara relates: **The wicked Turnus Rufus,** the Roman governor of Judea, **asked this question of Rabbi Akiva as well.** Turnus Rufus **said to him: And what** makes this **day,** Shabbat, different **from** other **days?** Rabbi Akiva **said to him: And what** makes this **man,** referring to his interlocutor, more distinguished **than** other **men?** Turnus Rufus **said to him:** I am more distinguished **because my master** the emperor **wants** it that way. Rabbi Akiva said to him: **Shabbat too** is unique **because my Master wants** it that way, as he has sanctified that day.

The *gemara* now introduces a famous exchange: *V'af she'ela zo sha'al Turnusrufus es Rabbi Akiva* — this same question was posed by Turnus Rufus to Rabbi Akiva. Turnus Rufus asked: *ma yom mi-yomayim* — why is *Shabbos* special? What distinguishes it from any other day of the week — why not Wednesday? Rabbi Akiva responded by turning the question back on his interlocutor: *u-ma gever mi-govrin* — why are you such a distinguished person? What sets you apart from any ordinary person? Turnus Rufus answered that his master, the Caesar, had appointed him. Rabbi Akiva replied: *Shabbos nami d'mari tzevi* — similarly, *Shabbos* is distinguished because God designated it so. There need not be anything intrinsically different about the day itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי קָאָמֵינָא לָךְ: מִי יֵימַר דְּהָאִידָּנָא שַׁבְּתָא? אָמַר לוֹ: נְהַר סַבַּטְיוֹן יוֹכִיחַ, בַּעַל אוֹב יוֹכִיחַ, קִבְרוֹ שֶׁל אָבִיו יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁאֵין מַעֲלֶה עָשָׁן בַּשַּׁבָּת. אָמַר לוֹ: בִּיזִּיתוֹ, בִּיַּישְׁתּוֹ, וְקִילַּלְתּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** Turnus Rufus **said to him: This** is what **I** mean to **say to you: Who is to say that now is Shabbat?** Perhaps a different day of the week is Shabbat. Rabbi Akiva **said to him: The Sabbatyon River can prove** that today is Shabbat, as it is calm only on Shabbat. **A necromancer can** also **prove** this, as the dead do not rise on Shabbat. **The grave of his father,** referring to Turnus Rufus's father, **can** also **prove** this, **as it does not emit smoke on Shabbat,** although smoke rises from it all week, as during the week he is being punished in Gehenna. Turnus Rufus **said to him: You have demeaned** my father, **you have** publicly **shamed him, and you have cursed him** by saying that he is being punished in Gehenna.

Turnus Rufus then clarified that this was not his real question. His question was: *mi yeimar d'idna Shabbasa* — who can say that what you call *Shabbos* today is actually *Shabbos*? Perhaps, over thousands of years, the count has been lost. Perhaps the day you observe is off by one, two, or more days. Rabbi Akiva replied: *d'mari tzevi* — and again, God confirmed it.

Rabbi Akiva then offered concrete evidence. *Nehar Sambatyon yochiach* — the Sambatyon River proves it. This is a river — apparently believed in antiquity to be a real river — that roars and churns throughout the entire week and rests silently on *Shabbos*. The very name *Sabbatyon* is embedded with *Shabbos*. That is one proof.

Then: *ba'al ov yochiach* — the *ba'al ov* also proves it, since we just established that a spirit raised through the first method cannot be raised on *Shabbos*, demonstrating that *Shabbos* is a day of special sanctity even in the realm of the dead. And finally: *kivro shel aviv yochiach* — your father's grave proves it. *She'ein ma'aleh ashan b'Shabbos* — smoke rises from it every day of the week but not on *Shabbos*, because those in *Gehennom* are granted a day of rest; the fires do not burn on *Shabbos*.

At this, Turnus Rufus was far from pleased. *Bizito, biyashto, v'kilalto* — you have degraded, disgraced, and cursed my father, he said, since the proof implies his father was burning in *Gehennom*. Luckily, Rabbi Akiva survived the exchange.

### Necromancer Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוֹאֵל אוֹב, הַיְינוּ ״וְדֹרֵשׁ אֶל הַמֵּתִים״!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **Isn't** one who **inquires** about the future from **a necromancer** the same as what is described in the verse: **"Or directs inquiries to the dead"** (Deuteronomy 18:11)? Why are they mentioned separately in the verse?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דּוֹרֵשׁ לַמֵּתִים, כִּדְתַנְיָא: ״וְדֹרֵשׁ אֶל הַמֵּתִים״ – זֶה הַמַּרְעִיב עַצְמוֹ וְהוֹלֵךְ וְלָן בְּבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת, כְּדֵי שֶׁתִּשְׁרֶה עָלָיו רוּחַ טוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: One who **directs inquiries to the dead** employs a different method to contact the dead, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **"Or directs inquiries to the dead"; this is one who starves himself and goes and sleeps** overnight **in a graveyard so that a spirit of impurity should settle upon him,** and he can listen to what the dead are saying.

Returning to *ba'al ov*, the *gemara* raises a conceptual difficulty: *sho'el ov haynu doreish el ha-meitim* — is not the *ba'al ov*, who raises and consults the spirit of the dead, identical to the category of *doreish el ha-meitim* mentioned in the Torah? And yet *doreish el ha-meitim* appears to be a mere *lav*, a negative prohibition, without capital punishment. How do they differ?

The *gemara* resolves this: *doreish el ha-meitim k'detanya* — *doreish el ha-meitim* is an entirely distinct category. It refers to *zeh ha-mar'iv atzmo v'holeich v'lan b'veit ha-kevarot* — one who starves himself, fasts for extended periods, and goes to sleep in a cemetery, in order to have the spirit of the dead rest upon him. Rashi explains that the purpose is *kidei she-tishreh alav ruach tum'ah* — so that a spirit of impurity will dwell upon him and assist him in his witchcraft. This is different from directly summoning and communicating with the dead; it is a more indirect form of contact, in which the spirit simply takes up residence in the practitioner.

## Doreish El HaMetim

### Cemetery Fasting Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְשֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מַגִּיעַ לְמִקְרָא זֶה הָיָה בּוֹכֶה, וּמָה הַמַּרְעִיב עַצְמוֹ כְּדַי שֶׁתִּשְׁרֶה עָלָיו רוּחַ טוּמְאָה – שׁוֹרָה עָלָיו רוּחַ טוּמְאָה, הַמַּרְעִיב עַצְמוֹ כְּדַי שֶׁתִּשְׁרֶה עָלָיו רוּחַ טׇהֳרָה – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה. אֲבָל מָה אֶעֱשֶׂה שֶׁעֲוֹנוֹתֵינוּ גָּרְמוּ לָנוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי [אִם] עֲוֹנוֹתֵיכֶם הָיוּ מַבְדִּלִים בֵּינֵיכֶם לְבֵין אֱלֹהֵיכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And when Rabbi Akiva would arrive at this verse he would weep** and say: **If one who starves himself so that a spirit of impurity will settle upon him** succeeds in doing so, and **a spirit of impurity settles upon him, all the more so one who starves himself so that a spirit of purity will settle upon him** should be successful, and a spirit of purity should settle upon him. **But what can I do, as our iniquities have caused us** not to merit the spirit of sanctity and purity, **as it is stated: "But your iniquities have separated between you and your God,** and your sins have hid His face from you, that He will not hear" (Isaiah 59:2).

The *gemara* elaborates on this definition of *doreish el ha-meitim*: self-imposed starvation in cemeteries, undertaken so that a spirit of impurity will rest upon the practitioner. There are parallel traditions of sleeping in cemeteries and hearing the dead speak, or even hearing heavenly voices from behind the divine curtain — but in this case, it is specifically about the *ruach tum'ah* taking up residence and facilitating acts of witchcraft, rather than direct necromantic communication.

### Akiva's Lament

When Rabbi Akiva would reach this verse — *doreish el ha-meitim* — *hayah bocheh*, he would weep. His reasoning, as the *gemara* presents it, is striking: *u-ma ha-mar'iv atzmo kidei she-tishreh alav ruach tum'ah shoreh alav ruach tum'ah* — if someone who goes through this effort in order to have an impure spirit rest upon him actually succeeds, then *ha-mar'iv atzmo kidei she-tishreh alav ruach taharah* — how much more so should someone who undergoes a similar process of self-abnegation in order to have the *ruach taharah*, the divine spirit of purity, rest upon him — such as to attain prophecy or a profound connection with the divine — succeed! *Al achas kamma v'khamma* — all the more so!

And yet it does not succeed. *Aval ma e'eseh she-avonoteinu garmu lanu* — but what can we do? Our sins have prevented this. The verse is cited: *ki im avonoteikhem hayu mavdilim beineikhem l'vein eloheikhem* — for your sins have created a separation between you and your God. Were it not for sin, Rabbi Akiva teaches, ascetic spiritual practice directed toward the divine would succeed.

### Rava's Creation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִי בָּעוּ צַדִּיקֵי, בָּרוּ עָלְמָא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי עֲוֹנוֹתֵיכֶם הָיוּ מַבְדִּלִים וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: If the righteous wish** to do so, **they** can **create a world, as it is stated: "But your iniquities have separated** between you and your God." In other words, there is no distinction between God and a righteous person who has no sins, and just as God created the world, so can the righteous.

Rava draws the implication further: *i ba'ei tzaddikei, baru alma* — if truly righteous individuals, those who are not separated from God by their sins, were to desire it, they could create worlds — for it is only the sin that divides. The verse is cited again: *ki avonoteikhem hayu mavdilim* — it is only the separation caused by sin that prevents this.

## Rabba's Golem

### Golem Creation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא בְּרָא גַּבְרָא. שַׁדְּרֵיהּ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא. הֲוָה קָא מִשְׁתַּעֵי בַּהֲדֵיהּ, וְלָא הֲוָה קָא מַהְדַּר לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִן חַבְרַיָּא אַתְּ, הֲדַר לְעַפְרָיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Indeed, **Rava created a man,** a golem, using forces of sanctity. Rava **sent** his creation **before Rabbi Zeira.** Rabbi Zeira **would speak to him but he would not reply.** Rabbi Zeira **said to him: You** were created **by** one of the members of **the group,** one of the Sages. **Return to your dust.**

In that spirit — Rava, or as some versions have it *Rabbah* — *bara gavra*, actually created a person. This is the *golem*. Rashi notes, and the *gemara* implies, that this was accomplished through the *Sefer Yetzirah*, a work attributed to Adam Harishon that is said to contain the secrets of creation through the combination of the letters of the divine name. Having created this being, *shadrei l'kamei d'Rabbi Zeira* — he sent him to Rabbi Zeira.

### Golem's Speechlessness

Rabbi Zeira did not initially recognize what he was dealing with. He assumed Rabba had sent him a student and began engaging the figure in conversation. *V'lo hava kama'ader lei* — but the *golem* could not respond. He was entirely speechless.

This is not incidental. Speech is considered the uniquely human characteristic. Onkelos, in his Aramaic translation of the verse in Bereishit where it says *va-yihi ha-Adam l'nefesh chayah* — and the man became a living soul — renders it as the man became a being of speech, *ruach memalela*. Even granting that a righteous sage could, through the powers of *Sefer Yetzirah*, construct a humanoid being, that being cannot possess genuine speech, because speech is not merely a physical act of moving the lips — it is an expression of divine breath, the very quality God breathed into the human being. Without that, no created being can speak. Intelligence, too, may be a distinguishing quality — philosophers have debated precisely what makes human beings unique — but even if degrees of intelligence exist in the animal kingdom, speech in this deeper sense remains distinctively human. A *golem* created without divine breath cannot cross that threshold.

### Return to Dust

Rabbi Zeira, now perceiving what stood before him, said to it: *min chavraya at* — you are one of those magicians, from those *chaveirim*, a term that itself suggests an indirect critique. And then: *hadar l'afra* — return to your dust. And the *golem* was dissolved.

Whether *min chavraya at* constitutes a critique is worth noting. Technically, Rabba did not perform this act through sorcery; he employed *Sefer Yetzirah*. And yet the phrase carries weight. There is a famous *teshuvah* of the *Chacham Tzvi* addressing whether a being created through *Sefer Yetzirah* could count toward a *minyan*. The contemporary novel *He, She, and It* by Marge Piercy, a work of Jewish feminist science fiction, engages precisely these questions — the creation of an android in a post-apocalyptic society, socialized through the legends of the Maharal's *golem*, with questions about what defines humanity — though the *Maharal*'s *golem* in that retelling turns out to be a woman. The conclusion of the halachic literature, it seems, was that the *golem* does not count.

## Weekly Golem Practice

### Friday Calf Creation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חֲנִינָא וְרַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא הֲווֹ יָתְבִי כׇּל מַעֲלֵי שַׁבְּתָא, וְעָסְקִי בְּסֵפֶר יְצִירָה, וּמִיבְּרֵי לְהוּ עִיגְלָא תִּילְתָּא, וְאָכְלִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another fact substantiating the statement that the righteous could create a world if they so desired: **Rav Ḥanina and Rav Oshaya would sit every Shabbat eve and engage in** the study of ***Sefer Yetzira*, and a third-born calf [*igla tilta*] would be created for them, and they would eat it** in honor of Shabbat.

The *gemara* then reports: *Rabbi Chanina v'Rabbi Oshaya hava yatvi kol ma'alei shabsa* — Rabbi Chanina and Rabbi Oshaya would sit together every *erev Shabbos* and *aski b'Sefer Yetzirah* — they would engage in the study of *Sefer Yetzirah*. And through this, *mivrei lehu igla taltsa* — they would create a third calf. The "third calf" is regarded as the choicest cut. Whether "third" means third of its age or the third born is debated: if it refers to its age, Rashi says they created the animal at that stage of development; if it refers to birth order, Rashi says they could not actually create a third-born animal but the one they created possessed the same taste and quality as one. In any case, *ve-achli lei* — and that was what they ate for *Shabbos*.

### Erev Shabbat Significance

The theological resonance here is striking, and somewhat unsettling, given the surrounding context. All the preceding material concerns forbidden practices of witchcraft, various categories of *chiyuv mita*, and yet here we read of *amoraim* apparently doing something that looks remarkably similar — and doing it as an act of *kavod Shabbos*. The juxtaposition seems intentional. Moreover, the specific timing of *erev Shabbos* is significant: humans and animals were created on *erev Shabbos*. Creating a living being on *erev Shabbos* thus mirrors the pattern of divine creation. There is evidently a distinction the *gemara* draws between the forbidden acts of witchcraft and the legitimate, if extraordinary, application of *Sefer Yetzirah* — but the text leaves that distinction more implied than articulated.

## Me'onein Forms

### Shimon's Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״מְעוֹנֵן״ – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: זֶה הַמַּעֲבִיר שִׁבְעָה מִינֵי זְכוּר עַל הָעַיִן. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: זֶה הָאוֹחֵז אֶת הָעֵינַיִם. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: זֶה הַמְחַשֵּׁב עִתִּים וְשָׁעוֹת וְאוֹמֵר ״הַיּוֹם יָפֶה לָצֵאת״, ״לְמָחָר יָפֶה לִיקַּח״, ״לִמּוּדֵי עַרְבֵי שְׁבִיעִיּוֹת חִטִּין יָפוֹת״, ״עִיקּוּרֵי קִטְנִיּוֹת מֵהֱיוֹת רָעוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** What is the definition of the **soothsayer** mentioned in the verse: "There shall not be found among you…a soothsayer" (Deuteronomy 18:10)? **Rabbi Shimon says: This is one who applies seven types of semen [*zekhur*] to** one's **eye** in order to perform sorcery. **And the Rabbis say: This is one who deceives the eyes,** as though he is performing sorcery. **Rabbi Akiva says: This is one who calculates** the fortune of **times and hours, and says,** for example: **Today is** a **propitious** day **for going away** on a journey; **tomorrow is propitious for purchasing** property successfully. Or he says that **on the eve of the Sabbatical** Years, the **wheat** harvest **is generally good; uprooting legumes** rather than cutting them from above the ground prevents them **from going bad.**

The *gemara* now turns to *me'onein*, one of the additional categories of prohibited practices that do not carry capital punishment. What is a *me'onein*? Rabbi Shimon offers a definition: *zeh ha-ma'avir shiva minei zachar al ha-ayin* — one who passes seven types of male semen over the eye. Rashi explains this as a means of giving someone an ability to perform witchcraft, connecting the word *me'onein* to *ayin* (eye).

### Illusionists Definition

The Sages, however, offer a different definition: *zeh ha-ochez et ha-einayim* — one who seizes the eyes, that is, an illusionist who uses hand motions and sleight of hand to make people believe they are witnessing genuine magic when in fact they are not. This raises an important contemporary question about the permissibility of stage magic and performance illusion. The consideration is that if everyone in the audience understands it is purely entertainment and no one genuinely believes real supernatural forces are at work, the practice may well be permissible. The concern would arise if the performance were so spectacular that, despite theoretical knowledge that it is an illusion, some part of the viewer's mind registers it as real — but arriving at that level of technical mastery is, as one might say, something we should all aspire to.

### Akiva Astrology

Rabbi Akiva offers yet another definition: *zeh ha-mechashev itim ve-sha'ot* — one who calculates times and hours. This connects *me'onein* to *onah*, meaning a period or season, and refers to astrological or sign-based prognostication: determining propitious or inauspicious moments for various activities.

### Omen Examples

The *gemara* elaborates with practical illustrations of what this looks like: *ha-yom yafe la-tzet* — today is a good day to go out, you will be successful in romance; *le-machar yafe li-kach* — tomorrow is good for doing business; *limudei arvei shevi'it chitim yafot* — those who plant on the eve of the seventh year will have good wheat. The final example — *ikurei kitniyot mi-or ra'ot* — pulling up one's legumes will prevent them from going bad — is not practical agrarian advice but rather a reading of mystical or astrological signs. None of these examples represent rational empirical observation; they are the sort of pronouncements based on what the practitioner claims to perceive in hidden cosmic indicators.

## Rambam-Ramban Debate

### Rambam Rationalism

At this juncture it is essential to note the fundamental dispute between the Rambam and Ramban regarding all of these categories. The Rambam takes an unambiguously rationalist position: all of this — witchcraft, necromancy, *ba'al ov*, the raising of spirits — is *narishkeit*, nonsense. None of it actually works. The entire reason the Torah imposes legal liability is because the practitioner presents himself as doing these things, creates the appearance of doing them, deceives people into believing they work. The *chiyuv* is essentially for the fraud and the social harm caused by that presentation. According to the Rambam, the Torah never endorsed the reality of these phenomena.

### Ramban Supernaturalism

The Ramban responds sharply. He acknowledges that the Rambam's view is intellectually appealing and that many readers will find it congenial — but he insists it is not the *peshat* of the Torah. The Torah, says the Ramban, prohibits these practices precisely because they actually work. The prohibition is not against claiming to do something that does not exist; it is against employing genuine supernatural forces that the Torah wishes to keep outside the domain of human practice. The narrative of the Witch of Ein Dor — where Shaul engages a necromancer who appears to raise the spirit of Shmuel — is cited as a clear case in point: the straightforward reading of that story is that it worked. The prohibition exists because the phenomenon is real, not because it is fake.

## Menachesh Superstitions

### Basic Omens

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״מְנַחֵשׁ״ – זֶה הָאוֹמֵר: ״פִּתּוֹ נָפְלָה מִפִּיו״, ״מַקְלוֹ נָפְלָה מִידּוֹ״, ״בְּנוֹ קוֹרֵא לוֹ מֵאַחֲרָיו״, ״עוֹרֵב קוֹרֵא לוֹ״, ״צְבִי הִפְסִיקוֹ בַּדֶּרֶךְ״, ״נָחָשׁ מִימִינוֹ״, וְ״שׁוּעָל מִשְּׂמֹאלוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** The **enchanter** mentioned in the verse (Deuteronomy 18:10) **is** one who relies on superstitious signs, e.g., **one who says:** If one's **bread fell from his mouth,** that is a bad sign for him; or: If one's **staff fell from his hand,** it is a bad sign; or: If one's **son calls him from behind,** it is a sign that he should return from his journey; or: If **a raven calls to him,** or if **a deer blocks him on the way,** or if **a snake is to his right, or** if **a fox is to his left,** all of these are bad signs. An enchanter is one who relies on these as bad signs and consequently changes his course of action.

The *gemara* next defines *menachesh*: *zehu omer pito nafla mipiv maklo nafla miyado* — this is someone who says, "The bread fell from his mouth — that is a sign of such-and-such; the stick fell from his hand — that is a sign of something else." The move here is from astrological calculation to the kind of superstitious omen-reading associated with everyday occurrences.

### Detailed Omens

The list continues: *beno koreh lo me-achorav* — his son called to him from behind; *orev koreh lo* — a raven called out to him; *tzvi hifsiko ba-derech* — a deer crossed his path; *nachash mi-yemino* — a snake appeared to his right; *ve-shu'al mi-smolo* — a fox to his left. Each such occurrence is treated as a portent of something. This is the domain of superstition as distinct from astrology or sleight of hand — the interpretation of natural, random events as meaningful signs.

## Daily Superstitions

### Morning Avoidance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אַל תַּתְחִיל בִּי״, ״שַׁחֲרִית הוּא״, ״רֹאשׁ חוֹדֶשׁ הוּא״, ״מוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת הוּא״.
>
> **Translation:** The term is also referring to one who says to a collector of charity: **Do not** collect from **me first,** as that is a bad sign for me; or: Do not collect from me now because **it is morning,** and it is a bad sign to begin the day with a loss; or: Do not collect from me now because **it is the New Moon,** and it is a bad sign to begin the month with a loss; or: **It is the conclusion of Shabbat** and I do not want to begin the week with a loss.

Closely related is the matter raised under the heading *al tatchil bi*: "don't start with me." Rashi illustrates with the example of a tax collector: if someone comes to collect from you, you say, "Don't start with me — ask someone else first, come to me second." The underlying concern is that being the first target of an unfortunate event early in the day will be a *siman ra* for the rest of the day. This represents the superstitious belief that one's fortune for a given period is set by what happens at its beginning.

## Me'onein Forms…

### Rosh Hashanah Simanim

This naturally raises a question with considerable practical relevance, particularly around this time of year: what about the *simanim* of Rosh Hashanah — the symbolic foods? Are those not precisely this kind of omen-reading? The distinction drawn in the *poskim* is fundamental. The *simanim* are not predictions and not signs in the operative sense; they are symbolic and aspirational. The formula *yehi ratzon* — may it be Your will — frames them explicitly as a prayer and an expression of hope, not as a reading of what the future will be based on what is happening now. The forbidden form of omen-reading is *causative*: "because this occurred, this outcome is predicted." The *simanim* of Rosh Hashanah are the opposite: they are acts of symbolic expression and aspiration toward a desired future, directed toward God.

## Daily Superstitions…

### Weekly Timing

The same pattern of *al tatchil bi* extends across other temporal thresholds: *Rosh Chodesh hu* — it is the new month, so bad things at this moment will be a *siman* for the entire month; *Motzaei Shabbos hu* — it is Saturday night, so something unfortunate at this moment sets a bad *siman* for the entire week. These are all prohibited as forms of the same superstitious omen-reading.

## Animal Divination

### Animal Charms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לֹא תְנַחֲשׁוּ וְלֹא תְעוֹנֵנוּ״ – כְּגוֹן אֵלּוּ הַמְנַחֲשִׁים בְּחוּלְדָּה, בְּעוֹפוֹת, וּבְדָגִים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** that the verse: **"Nor shall you practice divination nor soothsaying"** (Leviticus 19:26), is referring, **for example,** to **those who divine** and receive guidance according to what happens **to a weasel, to birds, or to fish.**

The *gemara* then cites *lo tenachashu v'lo te'onenu* and explains: *elu ha-menachashim be-chulda be-ofot u-ve-dagim* — these are people who give incantations or make prognostications using a weasel, birds, or fish. Rashi explains that this refers to when people go out on a journey or begin some undertaking and perform these acts of *nichush* in connection with these animals.

### Entrails Reading

One wonders whether this category might also encompass the practice of examining entrails — the inspection of the internal organs of slaughtered animals for omens — which was common throughout the ancient world. The *gemara* does not elaborate further on this point, and we move on.

## Chilul Shabbat

### Shabbat Violation Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְחַלֵּל אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת בְּדָבָר שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת, וְעַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Also liable to be executed by stoning is **one who desecrates Shabbat by** performing **a matter that for its intentional** performance one is liable to receive ***karet* and for its unwitting** performance one is obligated to bring **a sin-offering.**

The *mishnah* now transitions from witchcraft-related offenses and the cursing of parents to *chilul Shabbat* and then the cursing of parents — the second major grouping of capital offenses addressed in this section of the *perek*. The *mishnah* states: *ha-mechalel et ha-Shabbat be-davar she-zedonno kares ve-shigagato chatat* — one who desecrates *Shabbos* through an act whose intentional violation carries *kares* and whose unintentional violation requires a *chatat* offering. This is essentially the category of the thirty-nine *melachot*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא מִידֵּי דְּחִילּוּל שַׁבָּת הָוֵי, וְאֵין חַיָּיבִין לֹא עַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ חַטָּאת וְלֹא עַל זְדוֹנוֹ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **By inference, there is** another **matter that is a desecration of Shabbat,** and it is prohibited by the Torah, **but for its unwitting** performance **one is not obligated** to bring **a sin-offering, nor** does one receive ***karet* for its intentional** performance. Otherwise, the statement in the mishna that one is liable for desecrating Shabbat for performing an act for which one receives *karet* or is obligated to bring a sin-offering would have been superfluous.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הִיא? תְּחוּמִין, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. הַבְעָרָה, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the labor activity that desecrates Shabbat but whose punishment is not so severe? The Gemara answers: It is the prohibition against going outside the Shabbat **boundaries,** which are two thousand cubits beyond a person's location at the beginning of Shabbat, **and** it is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva,** who holds that although it is a Torah prohibition one is neither obligated to bring a sin-offering for violating it unwittingly nor liable to be executed for violating it intentionally. Alternatively, it may be referring to the prohibition against **kindling** a fire on Shabbat **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yosei,** who holds that contrary to other labors prohibited on Shabbat, this act is not punishable by stoning but by lashes.

The formulation implies, as the *gemara* notes, that there exist acts properly characterized as *chilul Shabbat* that do not fall into this capital category — acts that violate *Shabbos* without generating the full weight of *kares* and *chatat*. Not every *Shabbos* violation falls into this bin: failing to recite *Kiddush*, for instance, would not be called *chilul Shabbat* in this technical sense. The *gemara* asks what *chilul Shabbat* without these consequences looks like.

### Techumim Exception

The answer: *techumim ve-aliba de-Rabbi Akiva* — the prohibition of *techumim*, going beyond the *Shabbos* boundary, according to Rabbi Akiva. The verse *al yetze ish mi-mekomo be-yom ha-Shabbat* — let no man go out from his place on the *Shabbos* day — is, according to Rabbi Akiva, a biblical prohibition. Yet it is not a *melachah*; it does not carry *kares* or the requirement of a *chatat*. It is a biblical violation of *Shabbos* that falls outside the capital framework.

### Fire-Kindling Exception

Similarly: *ha-beirah ve-aliba de-Rabbi Yosi* — the kindling of fire according to Rabbi Yosi. The Torah singles out fire: *lo tiv'aru esh be-chol moshvoteichem be-yom ha-Shabbat* — you shall not kindle fire in any of your dwelling places on the *Shabbos* day. This could be read as the paradigmatic act of *chilul Shabbat*. But Rabbi Yosi holds that the Torah singled it out specifically to teach that it is a *lav* — a negative prohibition — and that one who transgresses it does *not* receive the death penalty. It is *chilul Shabbat* without the capital consequence.

## Cursing Parents

### Name Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְקַלֵּל אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיְּקַלְּלֵם בַּשֵּׁם. קִלְּלָם בְּכִנּוּי, רַבִּי מֵאִיר מְחַיֵּיב וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who curses his father or his mother is not liable** to be executed by stoning **unless he curses them with the name** of God. If **he cursed them with an appellation** of the name of God, **Rabbi Meir deems** him **liable, and the Rabbis deem** him **exempt.**

The *mishnah* then states: *ha-mekalel aviv ve-imo eino chayav ad she-yekalelam ba-shem* — one who curses his father or mother is not liable for capital punishment unless he curses them using God's explicit name. The mere use of an epithet or a non-explicit divine name is insufficient for the death penalty.

### Meir vs. Sages

*Kilelam be-kinui* — if he used a *kinui*, one of the non-explicit names of God such as *El* or *Elohim* (as opposed to the Tetragrammaton), *Rabbi Meir mechayev* — Rabbi Meir holds him liable for capital punishment. *Ve-chachamim potrin* — the Sages exempt him. This parallels a similar dispute in the context of blasphemy, where the same disagreement arises: must one use God's explicit name to be liable, or does a *kinui* suffice?

### Sages Identified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאן חֲכָמִים? רַבִּי מְנַחֵם בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הוּא. דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי מְנַחֵם בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר, ״בְּנׇקְבוֹ שֵׁם יוּמָת״ – מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״שֵׁם״? לִימֵּד עַל מְקַלֵּל אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ שֶׁאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיְּקַלְּלֵם בַּשֵּׁם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Who are the Rabbis** mentioned here? **It is Rabbi Menaḥem, son of Rabbi Yosei. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Menaḥem, son of Rabbi Yosei, says:** The verse states: "And he who blasphemes the name of the Lord shall be put to death; all the congregation shall stone him; the convert as well as the homeborn, **when he blasphemes the name he shall be put to death"** (Leviticus 24:16). **Why** must **the verse state "the name"** a second time, which appears to be a superfluous repetition? This term **taught concerning** one who transgresses other prohibitions of cursing, e.g., **one who curses his father or his mother, that he is not liable** to be executed by stoning **unless he curses them with the name** of God. If he merely uses an appellation, he is exempt.

The *gemara* asks: *man chachamim* — who are these Sages who require the explicit name? The answer: *Rabbi Menachem be-Rabbi Yosi hi* — it is Rabbi Menachem the son of Rabbi Yosi, as taught in a *baraisa*.

### Shem Derasha

Rabbi Menachem be-Rabbi Yosi derives this from the verse regarding blasphemy: *be-nokvo shem yumat* — when he articulates the Name, he shall be put to death. The question is: why does the verse say *shem* — the name — as an extra word? The verse could have simply said *be-nokvo yumat*. Earlier in the *gemara*, that word *shem* was used to teach that one must curse God's own name *with* God's name — meaning God must function as both subject and object. But here, Rabbi Menachem reads the extra *shem* as teaching something about an entirely different case: *limed al ha-mekalel aviv ve-imo she-eino chayav ad she-yekalelam ba-shem* — it instructs us concerning one who curses his parents that he is not liable unless he curses them with God's explicit name.

## Ish Ish Inclusivity

### Gender Inclusivity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אִישׁ״ – מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אִישׁ אִישׁ״? לְרַבּוֹת בַּת, טוּמְטוּם, וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** The verse (Leviticus 20:9) could have stated merely: For **a man [*ish*]** that curses his father and his mother shall be put to death; he has cursed his father and his mother; his blood shall be upon him. **Why** must **the verse state:** "For **any man [*ish ish*],"** repeating the term *ish*? It is **to include** not only a son, but also **a daughter, one whose sexual organs are indeterminate [*tumtum*], and a hermaphrodite [*ve'androginos*].**

The *gemara* now proceeds to unpack the full verse governing the cursing of parents: *ish ish ki yekalel aviv ve-imo mot yumat aviv ve-imo kilel damav bo*. The double formulation *ish ish* — any man, any man — serves to expand the category: *le-rabot bat, tumtum, ve-androginos* — it includes a female, a *tumtum* (a person of indeterminate sex), and an *androginos* (a person of mixed or dual sex characteristics). The doubling of the term *ish* signals inclusivity beyond the literal male.

### Both or Either

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֲשֶׁר יְקַלֵּל אֶת אָבִיו וְאֶת אִמּוֹ״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ. אָבִיו שֶׁלֹּא אִמּוֹ, אִמּוֹ שֶׁלֹּא אָבִיו – מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ קִלֵּל דָּמָיו בּוֹ״ – אָבִיו קִילֵּל, אִמּוֹ קִילֵּל. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** When the verse states: **"That curses his father and his mother," I have** derived **only** his liability for cursing both **his father and his mother. From where** do I derive that if one curses **his father but not his mother,** or **his mother but not his father,** that he is liable? The continuation of **the verse states: "His father and his mother he has cursed; his blood is upon him."** In the first part of the verse, the word "curses" is in proximity to "his father," and in the last part of the verse, it is in proximity to "his mother." This teaches that the verse is referring both to a case where **he cursed** only **his father** and to a case where **he cursed** only **his mother;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yoshiya.**

The verse continues: *asher yekalel et aviv ve-et imo* — who curses his father and his mother. Reading the *vav* as "and," one might conclude that the offense requires cursing *both* parents simultaneously. *Ein li ela aviv ve-imo* — I would know only the case of both together. *Aviv shelo imo, imo shelo aviv minayin* — but how do I know that cursing the father alone, without the mother, or the mother alone, without the father, is also prohibited?

The answer comes from the end of the verse: *aviv ve-imo kilel damav bo* — his father and his mother he cursed, his blood is upon him. From the structure of the verse — where *aviv* is juxtaposed to *kilel* in one clause and *imo* is juxtaposed to *kilel* in the same clause — we learn: *aviv kilel, imo kilel* — cursing either one alone is sufficient for liability.

### Vav Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן אוֹמֵר: מַשְׁמָע שְׁנֵיהֶן כְּאֶחָד, וּמַשְׁמָע אֶחָד בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ, עַד שֶׁיִּפְרוֹט לְךָ הַכָּתוּב ״יַחְדָּו״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yonatan says:** There is no need for this derivation, because the phrase "his father and his mother" **teaches** that one is liable if he curses **both of them together, and it** also **teaches** that he is liable if he curses either **one** of them **on their own, unless the verse specifies** that one is liable only when he curses both **together,** which it does not do in this case.

This is the position of Rabbi Yoshiyah. Rabbi Yonatan disagrees, arguing that no such elaborate derivation is necessary. *Mashma shem ke-echad u-mashma echad bifnei atzmo* — the *vav* carries both meanings simultaneously: "and" as well as "or." The default reading of a *vav* in the Torah is disjunctive — it means one *or* the other — and one would only read a *vav* as requiring both together when the text explicitly says *yachdav*, together, as in the prohibition of *shaatnez* (*lo tilbash shatnez tzemer u-fishtim yachdav*) or the prohibition against plowing with ox and donkey (*lo tacharosh be-shor u-va-chamor yachdav*). Absent the word *yachdav*, a *vav* means one or the other. Therefore, according to Rabbi Yonatan, no special derivation from the verse's structure is needed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מוֹת יוּמָת״ – בִּסְקִילָה. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בִּסְקִילָה, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בְּאַחַת מִכׇּל מִיתוֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בַּתּוֹרָה? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״דָּמָיו בּוֹ״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״דְּמֵיהֶם בָּם״. מָה לְהַלָּן בִּסְקִילָה, אַף כָּאן בִּסְקִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** From the phrase **"shall be put to death"** it is derived that his punishment is execution **by stoning.** The *baraita* asks: Do **you say** that that they are executed **by stoning, or is it rather by one of all the** other types of **death** penalty **stated in the Torah?** The *baraita* answers: **It is stated here: "His blood shall be upon him," and it is stated below,** with regard to a necromancer and a sorcerer: "They shall be put to death; they shall stone them with stones; **their blood shall be upon them"** (Leviticus 20:27). **Just as there** the verse states that a necromancer and a sorcerer are executed **by stoning, so too here,** with regard to one who curses his father or mother, he is executed **by stoning.**

The verse concludes: *mot yumat biskilah* — he shall be put to death by stoning. The *gemara* confirms that the death prescribed is *sekilah*, deriving this from a *gezeirah shavah*: *ne'emar kan damav bo ve-ne'emar le-halan dmeihem bam* — the expression "his blood is upon him" appears here and appears there (in another context established as *sekilah*), linking the two.

## Parent Curse Derash

### Damav Bo Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹנֶשׁ שָׁמַעְנוּ, אַזְהָרָה מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֱלֹהִים לֹא תְקַלֵּל וְגוֹ׳״. אִם הָיָה אָבִיו דַּיָּין – הֲרֵי הוּא בִּכְלַל ״אֱלֹהִים לֹא תְקַלֵּל״, וְאִם הָיָה אָבִיו נָשִׂיא – הֲרֵי הוּא בִּכְלַל ״וְנָשִׂיא בְעַמְּךָ לֹא תָאֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* asks: **We have learned** the **punishment** of one who curses his parent; **from where** is the **prohibition** against doing so derived? **The verse states: "You shall not blaspheme judges [*elohim*],** nor curse a king of your people" (Exodus 22:27). **If his father was a judge,** cursing him **is included in** the prohibition of: **"You shall not blaspheme judges,"** as it is prohibited for all people to curse a judge. **And if his father was a king,** cursing him **is included in** the prohibition of: **"Nor curse a king of your people."**

The *gemara* now addresses the question of the *azharah* — the negative prohibition — for cursing a parent. The punishment has been derived, but where does the Torah prohibit it? The verse is *Elohim lo tekalel* — do not curse *Elohim*. This refers to God or to judges. How do we get from there to ordinary parents?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵינוֹ לֹא דַּיָּין וְלֹא נָשִׂיא, מִנַּיִין? אָמְרַתְּ: הֲרֵי אַתָּה דָּן בִּנְיַן אָב מִשְּׁנֵיהֶן. לֹא רְאִי נְשִׂיא כִּרְאִי דַּיָּין, וְלֹא רְאִי דַּיָּין כִּרְאִי נָשִׂיא.
>
> **Translation:** If **he is neither a judge nor a king, from where** is it derived that it is prohibited to curse him? One can **say: You derive** it through **a paradigm** arrived at **from both of them** as follows: **The** defining **characteristic of a king is not like the** defining **characteristic of a judge, and the** defining **characteristic of a judge is not like the** defining **characteristic of a king.** The two cases are dissimilar.

The *gemara* reasons as follows: if the father were a *dayan* — a judge — he would be covered by the prohibition *Elohim lo tekalel*. If he were a *nasi* — a prince or king — he would be covered by *ve-nasi be-amcha lo ta'or* — do not curse a prince among your people. But what if he is neither a judge nor a king? Then the *gemara* attempts a *binyan av mi-shneihem* — a common-denominator argument drawing from both cases.

### Uniqueness Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא רְאִי דַּיָּין כִּרְאִי נָשִׂיא, שֶׁהֲרֵי דַּיָּין – אַתָּה מְצֻוֶּוה עַל הוֹרָאָתוֹ, כִּרְאִי נָשִׂיא – שֶׁאִי אַתָּה מְצֻוֶּוה עַל הוֹרָאָתוֹ. וְלֹא רְאִי נָשִׂיא כִּרְאִי דַּיָּין, שֶׁהַנָּשִׂיא – אַתָּה מְצֻוֶּוה עַל הַמְרָאָתוֹ, כִּרְאִי דַּיָּין – שֶׁאִי אַתָּה מְצֻוֶּוה עַל הַמְרָאָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* elaborates: **The** defining **characteristic of a judge is not like the** defining **characteristic of a king, as** with regard to **a judge, you are commanded with regard to** obeying **his** halakhic **ruling.** This is not **like the** defining **characteristic of a king, with regard to whose** halakhic **ruling you are not commanded** to obey. **And the** defining **characteristic of a king is not like the** defining **characteristic of a judge, as** with regard to **the king, you are commanded with regard to rebelling** against **him,** i.e., refusing to obey his order. This is not **like the** defining **characteristic of a judge, as you are not commanded with regard to rebelling** against **him.**

The challenge is that the unique characteristics of a *nasi* differ from those of a *dayan*. *Lo rei nasi ke-rei dayan ve-lo rei dayan ke-rei nasi* — the distinguishing feature of a king is not like that of a judge, and vice versa. A *dayan* carries the weight of obligatory compliance: you are commanded to follow the judge's ruling, as the Torah states that one must not deviate from the decision of the court in the Temple Mount to the right or to the left. A king carries a different weight: you are forbidden to rebel against him, to engage in acts of uprising or defiance — but you are not obligated to follow the legal pronouncements of a king in the way you are obligated to follow a judge's rulings. Each, in its own way, is weightier than the other. Therefore, one cannot simply derive from one to the other, or from both to an ordinary citizen, by means of a *binyan av*.

### Nation Commonality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶם שֶׁהֵן בְּעַמְּךָ, וְאַתָּה מוּזְהָר עַל קִלְלָתָן. אַף אֲנִי אָבִיא אָבִיךָ, שֶׁבְּעַמְּךָ, וְאַתָּה מוּזְהָר עַל קִלְלָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Their common denominator is that they are "of your people,"** i.e., they are members of the Jewish people, **and you are prohibited from cursing them. I too will include** the case of **your father, as** he is **"of your people," and** therefore **you are prohibited from cursing him.**

What is the common denominator? *She-hem be-amcha* — they are members of your people, your nation. And the verse itself provides this foothold through the word *be-amcha* in the verse *ve-nasi be-amcha lo ta'or*. From this, one could extend: *ve-af ani, avikha she-be-amcha ve-ata muzhar kilato* — and I will include also your father, who is among your people, and you are forbidden to curse him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶם, שֶׁכֵּן גְּדוּלָּתָן גָּרְמָה לָהֶם?
>
> **Translation:** This derivation is insufficient, as **what** is notable **about their common denominator,** i.e., another element that they share in common? It is notable **in that their prominence has caused them** to be entitled to an exceptional degree of respect; they are not merely "of your people." Therefore, perhaps the prohibition against cursing applies only to such prominent figures.

However, a counterargument is immediately raised: *ve-halo hatzad hashaveh she-beineihem she-ein ken gedulatam garma lahem* — the true common denominator between judge and king is that their *high status* is what generates the prohibition. Their greatness is what demands deference. That cannot straightforwardly extend to an ordinary person, including a parent.

### Cheresh Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא תְקַלֵּל חֵרֵשׁ״ – בְּאוּמְלָלִים שֶׁבְּעַמְּךָ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: Consequently, a third case is necessary to arrive at the required paradigm. **The verse states: "You shall not curse the deaf,** nor put a stumbling block before the blind" (Leviticus 19:14). **The verse speaks of the wretched of your people.** From the fact that it is prohibited to curse even those people, it can be derived that it is prohibited to curse anyone.

The *gemara* therefore invokes the third case: *lo tekalel cheresh* — do not curse a deaf person. *Ba-umlalin she-be-amcha ha-katuv medaber* — there the Torah is speaking not of people of high status but of society's most vulnerable — those who are marginalized, who cannot even hear the curse directed at them, people against whom one might feel free to act with impunity. The prohibition is designed to protect precisely them. Now we have two poles: on one end, those of the highest social status (judge and king), whose dignity demands protection; on the other, the most vulnerable (the deaf person), who need the Torah's protection precisely because they are powerless. Together, they span the full range of society.

One might ask: does the person cursed have to hear the curse? Clearly not — the case of the *cheresh* alone establishes that the curse is prohibited even when the object cannot hear it. More broadly, if a person went into a room with two witnesses and cursed his father or mother without the parent hearing, he would still be *chayav mita*.

### Logic Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶם, שֶׁכֵּן מְשׁוּנִּין?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* refutes this derivation: **What** is notable **about their common dominator?** It is notable **in that they are unusual,** i.e., they are not common people, but they are in some way different from others. Perhaps there is no prohibition against cursing an ordinary person.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אִם כֵּן נִכְתּוֹב קְרָא: אוֹ ״אֱלֹהִים וְחֵרֵשׁ״, אוֹ ״נָשִׂיא וְחֵרֵשׁ״. ״אֱלֹהִים״ לְמָה לִי? אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְגוּפוֹ, תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לְאָבִיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** this proof is insufficient, and the *baraita* states a different explanation: **If** it is **so** that it is prohibited to curse only unusual people, **let the verse write either** that it is prohibited to curse **judges and** the **deaf, or** that it is prohibited to curse **a king and** the **deaf,** and it would have been derived from these two cases that the prohibition applies to all unusual people. **Why do I** need the verse to state a special prohibition with regard to **judges?** It is superfluous, as judges are also extraordinary people. Rather, **if** the verse **is not** needed for **its own matter,** i.e., the prohibition against cursing judges, **apply it to the matter of** cursing one's **father.**

Despite spanning both extremes, the *gemara* resists the simple common denominator. *Ma le-nasi be-amcha she-ken gedulatan garma lahen* — one could object that the king and judge are prohibited to curse because of their elevated status, which is the unique factor that generates the prohibition. And *ma le-cheresh she-ken umlalin garma lo* — conversely, the deaf person is protected because of his vulnerability. Each has something unique about it. Therefore, the simplest common denominator — being a member of the Jewish nation — is not sufficient without the third case.

The *gemara* resolves this with the following logic: if the Torah had provided only two of the three cases — either judge plus deaf person, or king plus deaf person — we could have derived the prohibition for an ordinary person through a *binyan av*, despite the unique features of each, because together they would cover both extremes. But one of them would have been redundant given that we could have derived it from the other two. Since the Torah included all three, and two would have sufficed for the derivation, the third extra case — whichever one it is — is available to teach something further. *Im eino inyan le-gufo, tenahu inyan le-aviv* — if the extra case is not needed for its own subject matter (because the prohibition there could already be derived), apply it to teach about the father. And so the full prohibition against cursing one's father or mother is established.

It bears noting that this prohibition against cursing applies to all people — it is a general *lav* covering every member of the Jewish nation. What distinguishes the case of one's parents is not a separate prohibition but the penalty: cursing any ordinary person is a violation of a negative commandment; cursing one's father or mother is punishable by death.

## Elohim Meanings

### Elohim Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״אֱלֹהִים״ חוֹל, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר קוֹדֶשׁ, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to the one who says** that in the verse: "You shall not blaspheme *elohim*" (Exodus 22:27), the word **"*elohim*" is non-sacred,** as it is referring to judges. **But according to the one who says** that the word is **sacred,** as it is referring to God, **what can be said?** If the verse is not superfluous, as it is needed to state a special prohibition with regard to God, how is the prohibition against cursing one's parents derived from it?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״אֱלֹהִים״ – חוֹל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״אֱלֹהִים״ – קוֹדֶשׁ. וְתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: אַזְהָרָה לִמְבָרֵךְ אֶת הַשֵּׁם מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֱלֹהִים לֹא תְקַלֵּל״.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The word **"*elohim*"** in this verse is **non-sacred;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva says:** The word **"*Elohim*" is sacred. And it is taught** in a different *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: From where is the prohibition against one who blesses,** i.e., curses, **the name** of God, derived? **The verse states: "You shall not blaspheme God [*Elohim*]."**

The derivation just completed presupposed that *Elohim lo tekalel* refers to a judge — allowing the word *Elohim* to serve as the prohibition against cursing judges, from which the argument proceeded. But this leads to a fundamental question. As the *gemara* notes: *hani nicha le-man de-amar Elohim chol* — this works well for the position that *Elohim* in that verse is secular, meaning a judge. But *ela le-man de-amar kodesh* — what of the position that *Elohim* refers to God, and that the verse is the source for prohibiting blasphemy? A *baraisa* presents this explicitly: *Elohim chol divrei Rabbi Yishmael* — Rabbi Yishmael holds that *Elohim* in this verse is secular, referring to a judge, and it is the prohibition against cursing a judge. *Rabbi Akiva omer Elohim kodesh* — Rabbi Akiva holds it is sacred, referring to God, and it is the basis for prohibiting blasphemy.

According to Rabbi Akiva, then, we lack a verse explicitly prohibiting cursing a judge (we have only the verse about the king), and therefore the full derivation above — which required the judge as a separate pole — may be problematic. And yet another *baraisa* notes: *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov omer, azharah le-mevarech et ha-Shem minayin? Talmud lomar Elohim lo tekalel* — where is the prohibition against blasphemy? From *Elohim lo tekalel*. So according to that view (aligned with Rabbi Akiva), it is that verse that prohibits cursing God, not cursing a judge.

### Double Lamed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״אֱלֹהִים״ חוֹל – גָּמַר קוֹדֶשׁ מֵחוֹל, לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״אֱלֹהִים״ קוֹדֶשׁ – גָּמְרִינַן חוֹל מִקּוֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **According to the one who says** that **"*elohim*" is non-sacred, he derives** the prohibition according to the **sacred** meaning of the word **from** the **non-sacred** meaning, i.e., he derives the prohibition against cursing God from the prohibition against cursing judges. **According to the one who says** that **"*Elohim*" is sacred, we derive** the prohibition according to the **non-sacred** meaning of the word **from** the **sacred** meaning, i.e., he derives the prohibition against cursing judges from the prohibition against cursing God.

The *gemara* resolves this through the technique of the double *lamed* in the word *tekalel*. The verse could have said *lo takel* with a single *lamed*. Why the doubled form? *Shema mina tartei* — derive two things from it. The doubled *lamed* teaches that *Elohim* in this verse bears *both* meanings simultaneously — it refers both to God and to a judge. Therefore, the verse serves as the source for the prohibition against cursing God *and* as the source for the prohibition against cursing a judge, without needing to limit it to one or the other.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״אֱלֹהִים״ חוֹל, גָּמַר קוֹדֶשׁ מֵחוֹל. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״אֱלֹהִים״ קוֹדֶשׁ, גָּמַר חוֹל מִקּוֹדֶשׁ? דִּילְמָא אַקּוֹדֶשׁ אַזְהַר, אַחוֹל לָא אַזְהַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to the one who says** that **"*elohim*" is non-sacred, he can derive** the prohibition according to the **sacred** meaning of the word **from** the **non-sacred** meaning through an *a fortiori* inference. **But according to the one who says** that **"*Elohim*" is sacred,** can he **derive** the prohibition according to the **non-sacred** meaning **from** the **sacred** meaning? **Perhaps** the Torah **renders** it **prohibited** to curse God, in accordance with the **sacred** meaning of the word, but **it does not render** it **prohibited** to curse *elohim*, judges, in accordance with the **non-sacred** meaning.

But this raises a further difficulty: even if we accept both meanings, can one direction of derivation be accomplished by *kal va-chomer*? If *Elohim* means God and the prohibition applies to cursing the divine, can we derive from that, by an argument *a fortiori*, that cursing a human judge is also prohibited? Or conversely, if *Elohim* means a judge, can we then derive that cursing God is also forbidden? The problem is that *ein matzinin min ha-din* — one cannot derive a punishable prohibition by *kal va-chomer* alone. The double *lamed*, therefore, is not just a mnemonic; it is doing genuine legal work. *Dilma ha-kodesh az-har ha-chol lo az-har* — perhaps the verse only prohibits cursing the sacred (God) but not the profane (a judge). The doubling of the *lamed* forecloses that possibility and teaches both simultaneously.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, לִכְתּוֹב קְרָא: ״לֹא תָקֵל״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If so,** if it is prohibited to curse God but not judges, **let the verse write: You shall not treat lightly [*takel*],** instead of: "You shall not blaspheme [*tekallel*]"; it would have been derived from this wording that is prohibited to demean or curse God.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״לֹא תְקַלֵּל״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **What** *halakha* can be derived from the seemingly superfluous second letter *lamed*, which renders the expression: **"You shall not curse [*tekallel*]"? Conclude from it** that this expression includes **two** prohibitions: A prohibition against cursing God and a prohibition against cursing a judge.

## Na'arah Me'Orasah

### Four Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַבָּא עַל נַעֲרָה הַמְאוֹרָסָה אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב, עַד שֶׁתְּהֵא נַעֲרָה, בְּתוּלָה, מְאוֹרָסָה, וְהִיא בְּבֵית אָבִיהָ. בָּאוּ עָלֶיהָ שְׁנַיִם, הָרִאשׁוֹן בִּסְקִילָה וְהַשֵּׁנִי בְּחֶנֶק.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who engages in intercourse with a betrothed young woman is not liable** to be executed by stoning **unless she is a young woman,** i.e., neither a minor nor an adult; **a virgin; betrothed** but not yet married; **and she** lives **in her father's home,** having yet to move in with her husband. If **two** men **engaged in intercourse with her, the first** is liable to be executed **by stoning, and the second** is liable to be executed **by strangulation.** The second man is executed in this manner in accordance with the *halakha* of one who engages in intercourse with a married or non-virgin betrothed woman, as she was no longer a virgin when he engaged in intercourse with her.

The *gemara* now returns to the domain of sexual offenses, moving from the incest cases treated earlier to the case of adultery in its most aggravated form. Ordinary adultery — a married woman with a man who is not her husband — carries the death penalty of *chenek*, strangulation, the lightest of the four capital punishments. The case of *na'arah me'orasah*, a betrothed maiden, carries *sekilah*, the most severe. The *mishnah* establishes that one is not liable for *sekilah* in this category unless all of the following conditions are met: *ad she-tiyeh na'arah* — she must be a *na'arah*, between the age of twelve and approximately twelve and a half; *besulah* — she must be a virgin; *me'orasah* — she must be betrothed, neither single nor fully married; and *be-veit aviha* — she must still be under her father's jurisdiction, conceptually in his house.

This last condition requires elaboration. It is not about the physical four walls of the father's home. Once the father has handed her over to the husband's messengers who have come to bring her to her husband's house, even though the *chuppah* has not yet occurred and she has not yet arrived at her husband's home, she is conceptually no longer in her father's domain. *Prat le-shemasar av li-shlukhei ha-ba'al* — this excludes the case where the father has transferred her to the husband's agents; at that point she is in transition and the *sekilah* category no longer applies.

Similarly, the *mishnah* addresses the case of *ba alehah shenayim* — two men who had intercourse with her. One might immediately object that the second man would find her no longer a virgin. But the case under discussion is specifically one where the first act was anal intercourse, raising the question of how virginity is defined. Does virginity mean the hymen has not been ruptured? Or does it mean no act of intercourse has taken place, regardless of the anatomical consequence? This question recurs in other contexts — notably at the beginning of Kesuvos, where a woman's *kesubah* entitlement of 200 *shekel* (for a virgin) versus 100 (for a non-virgin) depends on this same definition, and where the same question arises for a woman whose hymen was ruptured through means other than intercourse. In the case of the *na'arah me'orasah*, the conclusion is: *ha-rishon bi-sekilah ve-ha-sheni be-chenek* — the first man receives *sekilah*, the second receives *chenek*, because even the act of anal intercourse renders her no longer a *besulah* for purposes of this category.

### Age Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״נַעֲרָה״ – וְלֹא בּוֹגֶרֶת, ״בְּתוּלָה״ – וְלֹא בְּעוּלָה, ״מְאוֹרָסָה״ – וְלֹא נְשׂוּאָה, ״בְּבֵית אָבִיהָ״ – פְּרָט לְשֶׁמָּסַר הָאָב לִשְׁלוּחֵי הַבַּעַל.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** with regard to the conditions stated in the mishna: The punishment of stoning applies only if the woman is **a young woman,** a category that applies until she is approximately twelve-and-a-half years of age, **but not a grown woman.** She must be **a virgin but not a non-virgin.** She must be **betrothed but not married.** She must be living **in her father's home, excluding** a case **where the father transferred** his daughter **to the husband's agents** to bring her to her husband's home for the purpose of marriage; in such a case, even though she has not arrived there yet, she is already considered a married woman according to *halakha*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר, אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: נַעֲרָה הַמְאוֹרָסָה – אֲפִילּוּ קְטַנָּה בַּמַּשְׁמָע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: This** mishna is **the statement of Rabbi Meir,** who holds that the *halakha* of a betrothed young woman applies to a young woman of the intermediate age between minority and adulthood. **But the Rabbis say: Even a minor is included in** the term: **A betrothed young woman;** only a grown woman is excluded.

Rav Yehudah says in the name of Rav: *zu divrei Rabbi Meir* — these are the words of Rabbi Meir, reflecting the view of our *mishnah* as requiring precisely that she be a *na'arah*, not younger. *Aval chachamim omrim na'arah me'orasah afilu ktanah be-mashma* — but the Sages say that *na'arah me'orasah* includes even a *ketanah*, a girl under twelve who has been legally betrothed by her father. According to the Sages, the word *na'arah* does not exclude the younger girl; it only excludes the *bogeres* who is too old.

This debate between Rabbi Meir and the Sages is not unique to the context of *sekilah*; it appears also in the law of the fine (*kenas*) for one who seduces a *na'arah besulah*, and in the law of the husband who falsely claims his wife was not a virgin. In all those contexts the same dispute surfaces: does *na'arah* restrict the category to girls precisely in that age range, or does it merely set the upper limit?

### Until Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא מִדִּפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: מִמַּאי דְּמַתְנִיתִין רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, וּלְמַעוֹטֵי קְטַנָּה נָמֵי? דִּילְמָא רַבָּנַן הִיא, וּלְמַעוֹטֵי בּוֹגֶרֶת וְתוּ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina: From where** is it indicated **that the mishna is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, and** the specification that she must be a young woman is not only to the exclusion of a grown woman but **also to the exclusion of a minor girl? Perhaps** the mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, and** the condition that she must be a young woman is **to the exclusion of a grown woman and nothing more.** Accordingly, one who engages in intercourse with an engaged minor girl is liable to be executed by stoning.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַאי ״אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁתְּהֵא נַעֲרָה בְּתוּלָה מְאוֹרָסָה״ – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל נַעֲרָה בְּתוּלָה מְאוֹרָסָה מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! וְתוּ לָא מִידִּי.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina **said to him: This** expression in the mishna: **Is not liable unless she is [*ad shetehe*] a young woman, a virgin, betrothed,** does not suit your suggested explanation. If the mishna is merely excluding a grown woman, **it should have** stated: **Is liable only for** engaging in intercourse with **a young woman** who is **a virgin** and **betrothed.** The expression *ad shetehe* indicates: Until she becomes, i.e., this *halakha* does not apply until she reaches the age of a young woman. **And** there is **nothing more** to be said on the matter, as this is clearly the intention of the mishna.

Rav Achai mi-Difti challenged Ravina: how do we know that our *mishnah* sides with Rabbi Meir and excludes a *ketanah*, rather than siding with the Sages and merely excluding a *bogeres*? The answer lies in the precise language of the *mishnah*: *einno chayav ad she-tiyeh na'arah* — he is not liable *until* she is a *na'arah*. The word *ad she-tiyeh* — "until she becomes" — carries temporal force: it means that at a younger stage (as a *ketanah*) she was *not* in this category, and only upon reaching the status of *na'arah* does the category apply. If the *mishnah* had intended to side with the Sages and include a *ketanah*, it would have phrased the condition differently: *einno chayav ela al na'arah me'orasah* — he is not liable except regarding a *na'arah me'orasah* — which would set the *na'arah* as a sufficient but not exclusive condition, leaving open the possibility that a younger girl is also included. The word "until" decisively indicates that our *mishnah* is Rabbi Meir, and that younger than *na'arah* is excluded.

## Ketanah Me'Orasah Case

### Punishment Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר אַדָּא מֵרַב: בָּא עַל הַקְּטַנָּה מְאוֹרָסָה לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, מַהוּ? לְגַמְרֵי מְמַעֵיט לֵיהּ, אוֹ מִסְּקִילָה מְמַעֵיט לֵיהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִסְּקִילָה מְמַעֵט לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ya'akov bar Adda asked Rav:** In the case of a man who **engaged in intercourse with a betrothed minor girl, what is** the *halakha* **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir? Does** Rabbi Meir **exclude him** from punishment **entirely, or does he exclude him** merely **from stoning** but he is executed by strangulation, like one who engages in intercourse with any other married woman? Rav **said to him: It stands to reason** that Rabbi Meir **excludes him from stoning** alone, but he is liable to be executed by strangulation.

Rav Yaakov bar Abba bar Adda posed the following question to Rav: if a man had intercourse with a *ketanah me'orasah* — a betrothed girl below the age of *na'arah* — according to Rabbi Meir, what is his punishment? The girl herself would not be liable, since she is a minor. But what of the man? Does he go completely free — *le-gamrei mi-pater lei* — because she falls between the categories, too young for the *sekilah* category and yet seemingly not covered by the ordinary adultery case with its *chenek* penalty? Or does he merely escape *sekilah* — *mi-sekilah mema'et lei* — and remain liable for *chenek* as in a standard case of adultery?

The answer offered is: *mistabra mi-sekilah mema'et lei* — it stands to reason that he is only exempted from *sekilah* specifically; he remains liable for *chenek*. He has committed what is effectively an act of adultery, even if the aggravated category of *na'arah me'orasah* does not apply.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכְתִיב: ״וּמֵתוּ גַּם שְׁנֵיהֶם״, עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ שְׁנֵיהֶן שָׁוִין? שְׁתֵיק רַב.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ya'akov bar Adda raised an objection to this answer: **But isn't it written:** "If a man is found lying with a woman married to a husband, **then they shall both die,** the man who lies with the woman, and the woman" (Deuteronomy 22:22), indicating that this punishment does not apply **unless they are both equally** subject to punishment? If so, one who engages in intercourse with a betrothed minor girl is exempt, as she is not halakhically competent. **Rav remained silent** and did not answer him.

The *gemara* then raises a complication from the verse *u-meitu gam sheneihem* — and they shall both die — *ad she-yihyu sheneihem shavin* — which seems to require that both parties be in the same status. Rashi reads this as suggesting that perhaps a man would not be liable at all if his partner is a minor and is not herself subject to punishment. Tosafot takes a different approach to avoid the difficulty, but following Rashi: the *gemara* is asking whether the symmetry demanded by *sheneihem* means that if she is not punishable, he is not either.

### Rav's Silence

Rav fell silent — *shasik Rav* — and had no immediate response. Shmuel then asked: *mai ta'ama shasik Rav* — why was Rav silent? The answer seems obvious.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מַאי טַעְמָא שְׁתֵיק רַב? וְנֵימָא לֵיהּ: ״וּמֵת הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר שָׁכַב עִמָּהּ לְבַדּוֹ״!
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel says: What is the reason** that **Rav remained silent? Why not say to** Rabbi Ya'akov that his premise is repudiated by the verse: "But if the man finds the betrothed young woman in the field, and the man takes hold of her, and lies with her, **then the man who lay with her alone shall die"** (Deuteronomy 22:25)? This verse proves that the punishment of stoning applies to the man even if the woman is exempt. The *halakha* of a man who engages in intercourse with a betrothed minor should be the same.

U-meitu ha-ish asher shakhav imah levado — the verse says "the man who lay with her shall die *alone*," explicitly indicating the case where the man is executed and the woman is not, because she was coerced and not culpable. This shows that the execution of one party does not require the punishment of the other. The *gemara* responds that this is indeed a *tannaitic* dispute.

## Equal Status Debate

### Yoshiyah Adult Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי: ״וּמֵתוּ גַּם שְׁנֵיהֶם״ – עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ שְׁנֵיהֶן שָׁוִין, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה. רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן אוֹמֵר: ״וּמֵת הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר שָׁכַב עִמָּהּ לְבַדּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that this matter is **subject to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as is taught in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"Then they shall both die"** (Deuteronomy 22:22), indicating that this *halakha* does not apply **unless they are both equally** subject to punishment. If one of them cannot be punished, e.g., because he or she is a minor, the other is not executed either. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yoshiya. Rabbi Yonatan says** that the phrase from the verse **"then the man who lay with her alone shall die"** indicates that in some cases only one of them is liable to receive the death penalty.

The verse *u-meitu gam sheneihem* — they shall both die — is interpreted by Rabbi Yoshiyah (following Rashi's reading) as requiring both parties to be adults. If one of them is a minor, the man would not be liable for capital punishment — a position strikingly at odds with the general principle that the age of one party does not determine the other's liability.

### Yonatan Counter

Rabbi Yonatan disagrees, relying precisely on the other verse: *u-meit ha-ish asher shakhav imah levado* — the man who lay with her shall die alone. This verse demonstrates that one can execute the man even when the woman is not punished. Therefore, even if she is a minor and not liable, he can still be put to death. Equal status is not required.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידָּךְ, הָהִיא ״וּמֵתוּ גַּם שְׁנֵיהֶם״ מַאי דָּרֵישׁ בֵּיהּ? אָמַר רָבָא: לְמַעוֹטֵי מַעֲשֵׂה חִידּוּדִים. וְאִידַּךְ? מַעֲשֵׂה חִידּוּדִים לָאו כְּלוּם הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what does the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Yonatan, who holds that this punishment applies even if only one party is subject to punishment, **derive from** the verse: **"Then they shall both die"? Rava says:** That verse is stated **to exclude an act of sharpening,** i.e., engaging in sexual contact with a woman without penetration. Since the man and woman do not equally receive pleasure from such an act, it is not considered sexual intercourse, and they are exempt. **And the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Yoshiya, holds that **an act of sharpening is nothing;** there is clearly no liability for such an act, and it is unnecessary to derive this *halakha* from the verse.

If so, what does *u-meitu gam sheneihem* teach? Rava answers: *le-ma'utei ma'aseh chidudin* — it comes to exclude acts that, while of a sexual character, involve only one living partner. Rashi understands this as acts that are pleasurable for only one party. But the more compelling reading, as the *gemara* suggests, is *ma'aseh Hordos* — the act associated with Herod, referring to necrophilia: intercourse with a woman who is already dead. In that case there are not truly two parties to the act, and the verse's emphasis on *both* dying excludes such a case from capital liability. It requires both parties to be alive — *sheneihem* — for the offense to be capital. But it does not require them to be of equal legal status in terms of age or capacity.

## Levado First Man

### Virginity Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתַנְיָא: בָּאוּ עָלֶיהָ עֲשָׂרָה בְּנֵי אָדָם, וַעֲדַיִן הִיא בְּתוּלָה – כּוּלָּם בִּסְקִילָה. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: הָרִאשׁוֹן בִּסְקִילָה, וְכוּלָּן בְּחֶנֶק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: He derives another *halakha*, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If **ten men engaged in intercourse with** a betrothed young woman, **and she is still a virgin,** as they engaged in anal intercourse with her, **they are all** liable to be executed **by stoning. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: The first** of them is executed **by stoning, and all** the rest **of them** are executed **by strangulation.** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi derives from the phrase in the verse "Then the man who lay with her alone shall die" that although the woman is still a virgin, only one man can be executed by stoning for engaging in intercourse with her. The others are executed by strangulation, in accordance with the *halakha* of one who engages in intercourse with a married woman or a betrothed non-virgin.

### Ten Men Case

A *baraisa* states: *ba al asara benei adam* — if ten men had intercourse (specifically anal intercourse) with this *na'arah me'orasah*, such that her hymen remained intact throughout. One position holds that *kulam bi-sekilah* — all of them receive *sekilah*, since she is technically still a *besulah* by the criterion of an unruptured hymen. Rabbi holds otherwise: *ha-rishon bi-sekilah ve-khulam be-chenek* — only the first receives *sekilah*; all subsequent ones receive *chenek*, because after the first act she is no longer considered a *besulah*, regardless of the anatomical state of the hymen. This is derived from *levado*: only one man — the first — is alone in the *sekilah* category for this woman. No subsequent man can achieve *sekilah* for her.

This resolves the question of how *levado* functions: according to one reading, the verse teaches that in the case of a *ketanah*, the man is punished alone (with *chenek* rather than *sekilah*); according to Rabbi's position, *levado* teaches that only the first man achieves *sekilah* and all others are limited to *chenek*.

## Bat Kohen Sreifah

### Parallel Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וּבַת אִישׁ כֹּהֵן כִּי תֵחֵל לִזְנוֹת״, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: תְּחִילָּה, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וּמֵת הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר שָׁכַב עִמָּהּ לְבַדּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** The verse states: **"And the daughter of any priest, if she profanes [*teḥel*] herself by playing the harlot,** she profanes her father; she shall be burned with fire" (Leviticus 21:9). **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The word *teḥel* means **first [*teḥila*]; and likewise it says: "Then the man who lay with her alone shall die"** (Deuteronomy 22:25).

The final *mishnah* in the passage concerns the *bat kohen*, the daughter of a kohen, who *zonet* — who commits sexual defilement. The Torah prescribes for her the penalty of *sreifah*, burning, rather than the *sekilah* of an ordinary *na'arah me'orasah*. Rabbi holds that this is precisely the parallel category: just as the ordinary *na'arah me'orasah* receives *sekilah*, the *bat kohen* in the equivalent situation receives *sreifah*. The same conditions that apply to one apply to the other; the only difference is the nature of the penalty.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: רַבִּי כְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר אֲרוּסָה יָצְאָה לִשְׂרֵיפָה וְלֹא נְשׂוּאָה. וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: אִם תְּחִילַּת בִּיאָה בִּזְנוּת – בִּשְׂרֵיפָה, אִידַּךְ – בְּחֶנֶק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **saying? Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, says: Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, who says** that only the **betrothed** daughter of a priest **is singled out for** execution by **burning, but not** one who is **married;** a married daughter of a priest who commits adultery is executed in the same manner as other women who commit adultery. **And this** is what Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **is saying: If** a woman's **first** act of **sexual intercourse** occurs **when** she commits **adultery,** as she is still betrothed, she is executed **by burning;** in **another** case, i.e., if she is already married and this is not the first time she engages in sexual intercourse, she is executed **by strangulation.**

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua states: *Rabbi ke-Rabbi Yishmael sevira lei* — Rabbi's position follows Rabbi Yishmael, *de-amar arusah yatzta li-sreifah ve-lo nesuah* — who holds that the *bat kohen* receiving *sreifah* is specifically when she is *arusah*, betrothed (as in the standard *na'arah me'orasah* case), not when she is *nesuah*, fully married.

### Techilah Defilement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְכֵן״? כִּי הָתָם: מָה הָתָם בִּתְחִילַּת בִּיאָה קָמִשְׁתַּעֵי קְרָא, הָכָא נָמֵי בִּתְחִילַּת בִּיאָה קָמִשְׁתַּעֵי קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the end of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's statement: **And likewise** it says: "Then the man who lay with her alone shall die"? What is the relevance of that verse to this issue? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi means to say that the *halakha* here, in the case of a betrothed young woman who is the daughter of a priest, is **like there. Just as there,** with regard to a betrothed young woman who is not the daughter of a priest, **the verse speaks of** her **first intercourse, here too, the verse speaks of** her **first intercourse.**

What does the verse *techel li-znot* mean? *V'hachi ka'amar* — this is what it is saying: *im techilat bi'atah be-znut bi-sreifah* — if it is the first act of intercourse that constitutes her defilement in the context of *zenut*, she receives *sreifah*. The first act of intercourse is the operative one. *Idach avatarah be-chenek* — all subsequent acts bring only *chenek*, not *sreifah*, because after the first act she is no longer a *besulah*.

The parallel verse *u-meit ha-ish asher shakhav imah levado* is cited here as well: just as we established for the ordinary *na'arah me'orasah* that *levado* means only the first man gets *sekilah*, so too here *levado* means only the first act of intercourse brings *sreifah* for the *bat kohen*. *Mah hatam bi-techilah bi'ah ka-mishtayei kra, hacha nami di-techilah bi'ah ka-mishtayei kra* — just as there the verse speaks of the beginning of intercourse, here too.

### Yosef Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי: מָר לָא הָכִי אֲמַר. וּמַנּוּ? רַב יוֹסֵף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Beivai bar Abaye said to** Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua: My **Master does not say,** i.e., does not explain the statement of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **like that.** The Gemara comments: **And who is** his Master? **Rav Yosef.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי, כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: נִשֵּׂאת לְאֶחָד מִן הַפְּסוּלִין – מִיתָתָהּ בְּחֶנֶק.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, he explains the statement of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi as follows: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who says:** In the case of the daughter of a priest who **married one of the** men **unfit** for her, e.g., a *mamzer*, and committed adultery while married to him, **her death** sentence is administered **by strangulation** and not by burning.

Ravibi bar Abaye then reports that Rav Yosef interpreted this differently. *Rabbi ke-Rabbi Meir sevira lei* — Rabbi's position actually follows Rabbi Meir, not Rabbi Yishmael. *De-amar niset le-echad min ha-pesulim mitatah be-chenek* — Rabbi Meir holds that if the *bat kohen* has been married to one of the men who would invalidate her for a *kohen* — a *mamzer*, a *chalal*, or others in that category — her penalty is *chenek*, not *sreifah*.

### Defilement Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: אִם תְּחִילַּת אַחָלָתָהּ בִּזְנוּת – בִּשְׂרֵיפָה, וְאִידַּךְ – בְּחֶנֶק. וּמַאי ״וְכֵן״?
>
> **Translation:** **And this** is what Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **is saying: If she first profanes herself,** i.e., disqualifies herself from the priesthood, **by** committing **adultery,** she is executed **by burning, but** in **another** case, where she was already disqualified from the priesthood by marrying a man unfit for her, she is executed **by strangulation. And what** is the meaning of the end of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's statement: **And likewise** it says: "Then the man who lay with her alone shall die"?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סִימָנָא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** It is **merely a mnemonic.** The verse is not relevant to this *halakha*, and it is cited merely as a sign indicating that just as the *halakha* of a betrothed young woman pertains to her first act of sexual intercourse, so too, the *halakha* of the daughter of a priest who committed adultery pertains to a case where it is her first disqualification from the priesthood.

On this reading, the distinction is not between the first and subsequent acts of intercourse, but between two types of defilement. *Im techilah achaltatah be-znut bi-sreifah* — if the first thing that defiles her is the act of sexual *zenut* itself, she receives *sreifah*. But *idach* — if she has already been defiled not through intercourse but through marriage to an invalidating partner (*chilul* through the marriage itself), then she receives *chenek*. The word *techel* is thus read as *chilul* — defilement — and *techilah* — beginning — simultaneously: it is the *beginning* of defilement, and that beginning must be through the act of intercourse, not through a prior disqualifying marriage.

The verse *levado* is cited on this reading not as a substantive derivation but as *simana be-alma* — merely a mnemonic device, a way of remembering the principle. It echoes the same idea — that it is the first, the singular, the beginning — without serving as the primary source for the legal rule.
