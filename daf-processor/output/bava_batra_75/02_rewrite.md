# Bava Batra 75 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rahav & Leviathan

### Rahav's Slaying

The *daf* opens with a statement of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav. At the moment when God sought to create the world, He said to the *sar shel yam* — the angel appointed over the sea — "Open your mouth and swallow all the waters of the world." This appears to be the mechanism behind the verse *yikavu ha-mayim el makom echad*, the gathering of the waters to one place so that the dry land should appear. The angel, however, refused. The Gemara relates: *amar lefanav ribbono shel olam, dai she-amod b-sheli* — Master of the Universe, it is enough for me to stand within what is mine. What exactly his objection was is not entirely clear, since one might think he would have welcomed more water. In any case, God immediately kicked him and killed him — *miyad be-at bo ve-harago*. The proof-text comes from the book of Iyov: *b-kocho raga ha-yam u-vi-tvunato machatz Rahav* — with His strength He stirred up the sea, and in His wisdom He severed *Rahav*.

### Rahav Identity

Rabbi Yitzchak draws the inference: *shema mina sar shel yam Rahav shemo* — we learn from this that the name of the master of the sea is *Rahav*. There is a certain circularity to the proof: we are reading the verse in light of the narrative we just told, and the only basis for reading the verse that way is the narrative itself. Nevertheless, that is how the *pasuk* is being employed.

### Rahav's Stench

*Rahav* has been slain and lies at the bottom of the sea. The Gemara adds: *ve-imalay mayim m-chasim oto, ein kol biryah y-cholah la-amod b-reicho* — were it not that water covers him, no creature could withstand the stench of the corpse. The proof-text is Isaiah 11: *lo ya-re'u ve-lo yash-chitu b-chol har kodshi ki mal'ah ha-aretz de'ah es Hashem ka-mayim la-yam m-chasim*. The verse reads literally that knowledge of God covers the earth *like water covers the sea*, which is linguistically awkward — water does not cover the sea, it *is* the sea. Therefore the Gemara reads it as *al tikrei la-yam m-chasim, ela le-saro shel yam m-chasim*: do not read it as "covers the sea," but rather "covers the master of the sea." The water conceals the corpse of *Rahav* and its stench.

### Creation Myths

This material resonates with a broader cultural context. In ancient Near Eastern mythology, creation accounts frequently involve a primordial battle between the creator-god and a divine power over the sea. The name of that sea-god, *Tiamat*, bears a striking resemblance to the Hebrew *tehomot*. What is remarkable about the Torah's account of creation in Bereshit is that it is entirely non-mythological — not even in the sense of engaging and rejecting these myths, but in simply ignoring them, treating them as though they do not exist. This is perhaps the most powerful form of rejection: to not even dignify the opposing view with a response.

Yet in the book of Iyov, these echoes come more explicitly to the fore. The *pesukim* there about God conquering *Rahav* and the *leviatan* appear to either allude to or actively engage these ancient traditions of divine conquest over the sea as the precondition for creation. The *midrash* here in the Gemara reinforces that connection: it is not a loose *aggadic* reading floating free of its *pesukim*, but rather an interpretation that takes seriously what those *pesukim* in Iyov are actually saying about God's primordial victory over the forces of the sea. The Torah's approach remains non-mythological, but those older traditions did not entirely disappear, and here, in Iyov and in this *aggadata*, their echoes surface.

## Jordan & Leviathan

### Jordan's Path

The Gemara continues with another statement of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: the Jordan River exits from the cave of *Pamyas* — possibly the Banias — and this is corroborated by a *baraita*. From there it flows through the *yam* of Sibchi, then the *yam* of Tiberias — the *Kinneret* — and then winds its way to the Mediterranean. From there it continues to flow until it reaches the mouth of the *leviatan*, as the verse in Iyov says: *yivtach ki yagiach yarden el pihu* — he will be confident because the Jordan will roar into his mouth. How the Jordan makes its way from the Mediterranean to the *leviatan* is not explained. The geography of these seas is also difficult to reconstruct, though one of them may have been the ancient marsh that once lay near the Banias area, which has since dried up.

### Beasts' Confidence

Rava bar Ulla objects: that verse in Iyov is not talking about the *leviatan* at all — it is written in the context of *behemot beharerei alef*, the great beasts of a thousand mountains. He therefore reinterprets: the verse indeed refers to those beasts — but when are the *behemot beharerei elef* confident, *betuchot*? When the Jordan pours into the *leviatan*'s mouth — that is, when the *leviatan* is well-fed and satisfied, the other great beasts feel secure. The *Rashbam* adds a further dimension: as long as God is actively sustaining the *leviatan*, the *behemot* are confident that they too will be sustained. As long as it lives, they feel confident they will live.

There is also a tradition in *Midrash Vayikra Rabbah* that in the future the *behemot* and the *leviatan* will battle each other as a kind of sport for the righteous — a *milchemet Gog u-Magog* in miniature — which would further explain the *behemot*'s interest in the *leviatan*'s state.

### Seven Seas

When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he cited Rabbi Yochanan's interpretation of the verse *ki hu al yamim yesada ve-al neharot yechoneneha* — God has established it upon seas and upon rivers. These seas and rivers, says Rabbi Yochanan, are the seven seas and four rivers that surround the Land of Israel. The seven seas are: the *yam* of Tiberias (the *Kinneret*), the *yam* of Sodom (the Dead Sea), the *yam* of Cheilat, the *yam* of Chilta, the *yam* of Sibchi, the *yam* of *Aspamya*, and the *yam hagadol* (the Mediterranean). Of these, three are readily identifiable; the *Yerushalmi* reads *Apamea* in place of *Aspamya*, which appears to refer to a town in Syria with an ancient lake. The four rivers are the *Yarden*, *Yarmokh*, *Kiromon*, and *Pigah*.

## Leviathan's Nature

### Gabriel's Hunt

The Gemara relates that in the future, *Gavriel* will make a *kenigya* — a hunting or sporting expedition — against the *leviatan*. The word *kenigya* appears to be a loan-word, likely Greek, meaning a hunt or chase. The *pasuk* cited is from Iyov: *Hatimshokh Livyatan be-chakkah, u-ve-chevel tashki'a leshono* — will you drag the *leviatan* with a hook and submerge its tongue with a rope? However, even *Gavriel* cannot prevail against the *leviatan* without God's help: *ve-imla Ha-Kadosh Barukh Hu ozro, ein yakhol lo*. The proof-text is: *Ha-oso yiggash charvo* — the one who made it brings the sword against it. That is God Himself.

The same pattern recurs here that we saw earlier with the *sar shel yam*: God's ultimate conquest over the sea monsters. These, too, carry the echo of those ancient creation myths — God slaughtering the female *leviatan*, God conquering the sea monsters. The *aggadata* consistently frames these primordial victories as requiring divine power that transcends what any angel can muster alone.

### Hunger & Steam

When Rav Dimi came, he cited Rabbi Yochanan with another teaching about the *leviatan*: when it is hungry, *motzi hevel mi-piv* — it releases steam from its mouth — *u-marthi'ach kol meimot she-ba-metzulah*, and this boils all the waters in the depths. Whether this is a side effect of its hunger or the mechanism by which it cooks its food is not entirely clear; the image of a dragon whose nostrils emit steam when it is angered is evocative, though cooking fish into soup is equally plausible. The verse cited from Iyov describes it vividly: *Yarti'ach ka-sir metzulah* — it will boil up the deep like a pot. These images are drawn directly from the *pesukim* in Iyov, where the *leviatan* is described in these dramatic, almost fantastical terms — this is not purely the Gemara's creative imagination, but rather an exposition of those remarkable verses.

### Eden Cooling

Were the *leviatan* not to insert its head into *Gan Eden* at such moments — *ve-ilmalei machnis rosho le-Gan Eden* — no creature could withstand the stench or heat that comes from its mouth. *Gan Eden* functions here as a place that sweetens or cools the creature. The verse cited is *yam yasim ke-merkacha* — the sea it will make like a *merkacha*, a sweet-smelling ointment — and the *Rashbam* or *Rashi* understands this as the effect of *Gan Eden* sweetening the *leviatan*'s odor.

### Thirst & Recovery

When the *leviatan* thirsts, it *oseh t'lamim t'lamim ba-yam* — makes furrows in the sea, great wave-like pathways. The verse is *acharayiv ya'ir nativ* — after it the path lights up. Rav Acha bar Yaakov adds: after the *leviatan* has drunk or boiled the waters, the depths do not return to their original strength for seventy years — *ein tehom chozer le-eitano ad shiv'im shanah*. The proof is from the verse *yachshov tehom le-seivah* — the deep will reckon itself old — and since *seivah* refers to old age, which begins at seventy, the restoration takes seventy years.

## Leviathan Feast

### The Feast

Rabbah in the name of Rabbi Yochanan states: in the future, God will make a *se'udah* for the righteous from the flesh of the *leviatan* — *atid Hakadosh Baruch Hu la'asot se'udah le-tzaddikim mi-besaro shel Livyatan*. This echoes what was mentioned earlier in this *aggadata* — that the female *leviatan* was salted away for the future feast. The verse cited is *yichru alav chaverim* — the friends will feast upon it — and the Gemara establishes that *keriyah* means feasting, as in the verse *vayichreh lahem kera gedolah*, he made for them a great feast.

### Friends & Markets

The "friends" (*chaverim*) in that verse are identified as *talmidei chachamim* — Torah scholars — from the verse in *Shir Hashirim*: *hayoshevet ba-ganim, chaverim makshivim le-koleich, hashmi'ini* — she who dwells in the gardens, the friends who listen to her voice, let me hear it. In *Shir Hashirim*, understood as an allegory of God and Israel, these friends are those who hear and study God's words — the Torah sages.

What remains after the feast is divided and sold in the marketplaces of Jerusalem. The *tzaddikim* appear to profit from the leftovers. The verse *yach'tzuhu bein Kena'anim* — it will be divided among the *Kena'anim* — is read through the Gemara's identification of *Kena'anim* with merchants, as in *Kena'an be-yado moznei mirmah* (Hoshea) and *asher sochareha sarim, kena'aneha nichbedei aretz* (Yeshayahu) — its merchants are princes, its traders the honored of the land. The *leviatan* thus provides both a feast and a commercial opportunity.

The deeper question of why specifically the *leviatan* serves as the meal of the righteous points toward the symbolic dimension of the whole narrative. The *leviatan* represents something large and ancient that only God's power can conquer. Its defeat and consumption by the righteous connects the future victory over evil with the reward of those who conquered their own *yetzer hara* in this world. As a metaphor it is remarkably apt: the greater the *leviatan* you overcame, the larger your portion.

### Merit Levels

Rabbah continues in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: God will make a *sukkah* for the righteous from the *leviatan*'s skin — *atid Hakadosh Baruch Hu la'asot sukkah la-tzaddikim me-oro shel Livyatan*. This resonates with the tradition in *Masechet Sukkah* that in the end all of Israel will dwell in one *sukkah*. The verse is *ha-timaleh be-sukkot oro* — will its skin be filled with huts — and the reading is that its skin will create the *sukkah* in which all dwell.

The reward is then graduated by merit. If the righteous person *zacha* — merits — he receives a full *sukkah*. If he does not fully merit, he receives *tzeltzel*, a shade — as in *u-ve-tzeltzel dagim rosho*, with the shade of fish over his head. If he does not merit even that, he receives *anak* — a necklace — from the bones, as in *va-anakim le-gargerotecha*. And if he does not merit even that, he receives a *kameia*, an amulet — as in *va-tikshreinu le-na'arotecha*. The skin of the *leviatan* thus provides protection, adornment, or at minimum a token of remembrance, each according to what the person achieved in this world.

### Wall Covering

Whatever skin remains after all of this, God will spread over the walls of Jerusalem — *u-vehash'ar, parso ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu al chomot Yerushalayim* — and its radiance will shine from one end of the world to the other. The verse is *ve-halchu goyim le-orech u-melachim le-nogah zarchach* — nations will walk by your light and kings by the brightness of your shining. The verse is often understood as a call to moral example — *or la-goyim*, a light unto the nations — but here its literal meaning is quite material: the luminous skin of the *leviatan* stretched across Jerusalem's walls will illuminate the entire world.

## Jerusalem Walls

### Gate Materials

The Gemara now cites the verse *ve-shamti kadkod shimshosayich* — and I will make your *shimshosayich* (a term interpreted as the walls or windows) like *kadkod*, a precious stone. Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani reports that two angels in heaven debated the meaning of this word: *Gavriel* and *Michael* — though *Tosafot* notes that *Eliyahu* must have been the one who revealed this heavenly debate to us. Alternatively, some say the dispute was between two *amora'im* in the West, namely Yehuda and Chizkiya the sons of Rabbi Chiya. One held that the material is *shoham* (onyx), the other that it is *yashfeh* (jasper, or according to some, carbuncle). God responded: *lehavei kedein ve-kedein* — it will be both. Hence the word *kadkod* encodes *kedein va-kedein*, both this and this. *Tosafot* adds that since both *shoham* and *yashfeh* appear earlier in the surrounding *pesukim*, the resolution of *kedein va-kedein* refers back to those specific stones.

### Block Dimensions

The verse continues: *u-she'arayich le-avnei ekdach* — and your gates like stones of *ekdach*. The word is connected by *derash* to *kodeach*, meaning to carve or pierce. Rabbi Yochanan was sitting and expounding: in the future, God will bring beautiful jewels and pearls of *shoham*, thirty by thirty *amot*, and will carve within them a gate ten *amot* wide and twenty *amot* high. The full block is thirty by thirty, and the gate carved within it is ten wide by twenty tall — meaning the gate occupies the center of the block, with the full block forming the wall through which one passes.

### Student's Mockery

A student mocked this vision: *hashta ke-veiyata de-tzitzela lo mishkachinan* — we cannot even find an onyx stone the size of a small bird's egg — *kulei hai mishkachinan*? You are claiming we will find one thirty by thirty *amot*? Many days later, the student's boat sailed far out to sea. He saw ministering angels sitting and hewing out precious jewels and stones — thirty by thirty — with a gate carved in them ten wide by twenty high. He asked them: for whom are these? They told him: these will stand in the gates of Jerusalem. He came before Rabbi Yochanan and said: Teach further, *Rebbi*, for you it is fitting to expound — *ka-asher amarta, ken ra'iti*, just as you said, so I have seen with my own eyes.

### Divine Punishment

Rabbi Yochanan's response was not warm. He said to him: *reika* — empty one! *Ilmalei ra'ita, lo he'emanta* — had you not seen it yourself, you would not have believed? *Nimtza melagleg al divrei chachamim ata* — you turn out to be a mocker of the words of the sages. He cast his eyes upon him and the student became a pile of bones — *natan einav bo ve-na'asa gal shel atzamot*.

This is a striking conclusion to the *aggadata*. The punishment seems to assert that one must take these teachings seriously — not necessarily that every detail is to be understood with wooden literalism, but that one must be able to believe that it *could* happen. If you genuinely believe in *techiyat ha-meitim* and in God's unlimited power, then the physical impossibility of a thirty-by-thirty *shoham* stone offers no real objection. One might wonder why the student was not punished when he first mocked — when he said there isn't even an egg-sized piece of onyx in the whole world. The story works better dramatically this way: the mockery is silently registered, the evidence is shown, and it is only the student's proud declaration that his belief required empirical confirmation that draws Rabbi Yochanan's rebuke.

## Future Heights

### Two Hundred Cubits

A *meitivei* is raised from the verse *ve-holakhtem komemiyut* — you will walk with upright stature. Rabbi Meir says: in the future, human beings will stand two hundred *amot* tall, *kishtei komot shel adam* — double the stature of *Adam HaRishon*, who was a hundred *amot* tall. The word *komemiyut* is read as a double *komah*, a double stature.

### Temple Height

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: humans in the future will be a hundred *amot* tall, corresponding to the height of the *heikhal* — *kineged heikhal ve-kotlav*. The verse cited is *asher baneinu ki-neti'im megudalim bi-nureihem, benoteinu ke-zaviyot mechutavot tavnit heikhal* — our sons like saplings grown in their youth, our daughters like cornerstones carved in the form of the *heikhal*. Since the *heikhal* was a hundred *amot* tall, the future human stature will match it.

### Height Paradox

A problem immediately surfaces. Whether the future human being will be a hundred or two hundred *amot* tall, how can such a person pass through a gate that is a mere twenty *amot* high? If anything, the problem makes the future vision even grander than it first appeared: a human being two hundred *amot* tall is an even more fantastical image than an onyx stone thirty *amot* square.

### Window Blocks

The Gemara resolves the difficulty: *ki ka'amar Rabbi Yochanan, le-shivei de-bei zika* — when Rabbi Yochanan spoke of the thirty-by-thirty blocks with the ten-by-twenty carving, he was referring not to the gates themselves, but to the windows carved into the gateposts for light and air. The gates themselves are far larger.

## Seven Canopies

### Canopy Merit

Rabbah in the name of Rabbi Yochanan teaches: in the future, God will make seven *chupot* for each and every righteous person — *atid Hakadosh Baruch Hu la-asot sheva chupot le-chol tzadik ve-tzadik*. Earlier we saw a *sukkah* from the *leviatan*'s skin; now each *tzadik* receives seven individual canopies of divine glory. The proof-text is Yeshayahu 4: *u-vara Hashem al kol mechon Har Tziyon ve-al mikraeha, anan yomam ve-ashan ve-nogah esh lehava lailah, ki al kol kavod chuppah* — God will create upon every place of Mount Zion and upon those called to it, a cloud by day and smoke and the brightness of flaming fire by night, for over all glory there shall be a *chuppah*.

### Isaiah's Seven

Counting the elements in the verse yields the seven *chupot*: *anan* (cloud) is one, *anan yomam* (cloud by day) could be read as elaborating, *ashan* (smoke) is two, *nogah* (brightness) is three, *esh* (fire) is four, *lehava* (flame) is five, *al kol kavod* is six — though the *Rashbam* notes this phrase is descriptive rather than a discrete *chuppah*, meaning each canopy is calibrated to the person's honor — and seven is the next verse, *ve-sukkah tihyeh le-tzel yomam*, which is also quoted at the opening of *Masechet Sukkah*. Each *tzadik* receives all seven, but just as every person can live in a house while houses vary enormously in grandeur, so each person's *chupot* are calibrated to their individual merit and deeds.

### Burning Jealousy

The Gemara asks: why would *ashan* — smoke — be part of a divine canopy? Rabbi Chanina answers: *she-kol mi she-einav tzarot be-talmidei chachamim ba-olam ha-zeh, mitmal'ot einav ashan le-olam ha-ba* — whoever begrudged the Torah scholars in this world will have his eyes filled with smoke in the world to come. This may represent a different type of *derasha* from the counting of canopies above.

Similarly: why would *esh* — fire — feature in a *chuppah*? Rabbi Chanina teaches the famous dictum: *kol echad ve-echad nichveh me-chuppato shel chaveiro* — every person is burned by the canopy of his companion. Even among the righteous, there are levels; and even having a magnificent *chuppah*, one can see that another's is still more glorious. The resulting feeling — a combination of jealousy, regret, and the painful recognition of what one could have achieved — burns. This is not merely envy in a base sense; it is the anguish of confronting one's own unrealized potential when the alternative stands before you. *Oy la-osa busha, oy la-osa kelimah* — woe to that shame, woe to that embarrassment.

### Moses & Joshua

A parallel is drawn. When God said to Moshe *ve-nasata me-hodcha alav* — put from your glory upon Yehoshua — the word is *me-hodcha*, from your glory, not *kol hodcha*, not all of your glory. The elders of Yehoshua's generation, who had known Moshe, would say: *penei Moshe ke-penei chamah, penei Yehoshua ke-penei levanah* — the face of Moshe was like the sun, the face of Yehoshua was like the moon. The moon, of course, only reflects the sun's light. *Oy la-osa busha, oy la-osa kelimah* — woe to such shame and embarrassment. The *Rashbam* explains this not solely as personal embarrassment for Yehoshua, but as the shame of an entire generation that had so quickly descended from such a level: *bi-zman mu'at nitma'et ha-kavod kol kach* — in such a short time, the honor has diminished so greatly.

## Adam's Canopies

### Eden's Treasures

Rabbi Chama bar Chanina teaches: God made ten *chupot* for *Adam HaRishon* in *Gan Eden* — *eser chupot asah Hakadosh Baruch Hu le-Adam HaRishon be-Gan Eden*. The *tzaddikim* of the future merit seven; the first human being had ten in paradise. The proof is the verse in Yechezkel addressed to *Chiram* the king of Tyre: *be-Eden gan Elohim hayita, kol even yekara* — you were in the Garden of Eden, every precious stone [was your covering]. Counting the ten types of precious things enumerated in that verse — stones and gold together — yields the ten *chupot*.

### Eleven Canopies

Mar Zutra holds that there are eleven *chupot*, not ten, because *kol even yekara* — "every precious stone" — is itself a separate, generalized item beyond the specific ten listed, making it the eleventh. Rabbi Yochanan adds: the least valuable of all these items in *Gan Eden* was gold — *ve-hagarua she-ba-kulam zahav* — because gold is listed last in the verse. The general principle is that the more important items are listed first.

### Chiram's Pride

The verse then asks: *mai melechet tupayecha u-nekevecha bach* — what is the work of your *tupayecha* and your *nekevecha* within you? This is all within Yechezkel's prophecy against *Chiram* of Tyre, who had grown profoundly arrogant. Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: God said to *Chiram* — *bach nistakalti u-varati nekavim nekavim be-adam* — it was in anticipation of someone like you that I made human beings full of orifices. From those orifices all bodily waste exits — a reminder that however grand a person's self-image, he is flesh and blood like all others. The *Rashbam* connects *tupayecha* to the word for a grave, *ha-kever she-chalul ke-tof* — hollow like a tambourine — meaning the awareness of mortality is built into human anatomy.

An alternative reading: *nistakalti ve-kanasti mita al ha-adam* — I saw that someone like you would arise, and that is why I decreed mortality upon human beings — not because of *Adam HaRishon*'s sin with the tree alone, but so that human beings would always know themselves to be mortal and not mistake themselves for gods. The whole sequence presents a meditation on humility: the righteous receive magnificent *chupot* but are burned by their companion's greater one; *Adam HaRishon* had ten *chupot* but was mortal; and *Chiram*, who thought himself divine, is reminded through the very structure of the human body that he is not.

## God's Name

### Two Jerusalems

Rabbah in the name of Rabbi Yochanan draws the distinction: *lo ke-Yerushalayim shel ha-olam ha-zeh Yerushalayim shel ha-olam ha-ba* — the Jerusalem of the world to come is not like the Jerusalem of this world. In this world, *kol ha-rotzeh la'alot oleh* — anyone who wishes can go up to Jerusalem. But in the world to come, *ein olim ela ha-mezumanim lah* — only those who have been summoned, invited to it, will ascend. Entry into the future Jerusalem requires, as it were, an engraved invitation.

### Three Names

Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan: three things are called by the name of God — *sheloshah nikra'u shemo shel Hakadosh Baruch Hu*: *tzaddikim*, *Mashiach*, and *Yerushalayim*. The *tzaddikim* are established from the verse already cited: *kol ha-nikra vi-shmi ve-li-chevodi berativ* — all who are called by My name and for My honor I have created. *Mashiach* is established from the verse in Yirmiyahu: *ve-zeh shemo asher yikre'u Hashem tzidkenu* — and this is his name by which he will be called, "God is our righteousness." *Yerushalayim* is established from Yechezkel: *saviv shemonah asar alef, ve-shem ha-ir mi-yom Hashem shamah* — the circumference is eighteen thousand, and the name of the city from that day shall be "God is there." *Al tikrei shamah ela shemah* — do not read it as "there" (*shamah*) but as "her name" (*shemah*): God is its name.

The theological audacity of this teaching is striking. The *tzaddikim* who dwell in Jerusalem are called by God's name; Jerusalem itself is called by God's name; and *Mashiach* bears God's name. If the righteous dwell in Jerusalem, they are dwelling, in a sense, in God — and they themselves bear His name. The image is extraordinary: God dwelling in God, the righteous called God, dwelling in a city named God.

### Called Holy

Rabbi Elazar teaches: in the future, it will be declared *kadosh* — holy — before the righteous, just as the angels declare *kadosh* before God. *Atidim tzaddikim she-omrim lifneihem kadosh.* The proof-text: *ve-hayah ha-nishar be-Tziyon ve-ha-notar bi-Yerushalayim kadosh ye'amer lo* — those who remain in Zion and in Jerusalem, holy will be said of him. The righteous, dwelling in Jerusalem — the extension of the Temple, the place where God's Presence rests — reach a level where the angels address them as they address God. The entire arc of this *aggadata* builds to this point: the conquest of the *leviatan*, the feast, the *sukkot*, the shining walls, the canopies calibrated to merit, and now — the righteous themselves called by God's name, declared holy before them.

## Jerusalem's Future

### Jerusalem's Measure

Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: God will raise Jerusalem three *parsaot* upward — *atid Hakadosh Baruch Hu le-hagbi'ah et Yerushalayim gimel parsa'ot le-maalah*. The proof is *ve-ramah ve-yashvah tachteha* — it will be raised and dwell beneath itself. *Tachteha* is read as *ke-tachteha* — by as much as its current depth below, meaning the elevation from its hilltop base; that same measure will be added upward. Whether this means it will be raised physically into the air — floating, approaching the heavenly Jerusalem — or simply elevated to a far greater height is debated. A floating Jerusalem fits the subsequent imagery of ascending like clouds.

Rabbah relates that a certain elder told him: *ledidi chazi li Yerushalayim kaimaita, ve-tlata parsei havya* — I saw the first Jerusalem, and it was three *parsaot* [high/elevated]. How Rabbah could have seen the first Jerusalem requires clarification — presumably it means before its destruction, or possibly a prophetic vision.

### No Walls

Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa teaches: God originally wished to give Jerusalem a fixed measure — *bikesh Hakadosh Baruch Hu latet et Yerushalayim be-mida* — which is a strange idea: why would God wish to limit Jerusalem? Perhaps even a vast but finite size represents a kind of limit on what is, by its nature, unlimited. The measuring angel of Zechariah's vision represents this impulse. But the ministering angels protested: *Ribbono shel Olam, harbeh krachim barata be-olamecha shel umot ha-olam, ve-lo natata midat orchan ve-midat rochban* — You have created many cities in Your world for the nations and gave none of them a fixed size; *Yerushalayim she-shimcha betochah, u-mikdashecha betochah, ve-tzaddikim betochah* — Jerusalem in which Your name rests, in which Your sanctuary stands, in which the righteous dwell — *ata noten bah midah*? You will give it a limit? Immediately: *prazot teshev Yerushalayim* — Jerusalem shall dwell as an unwalled city, spread out without limit, *me-rov adam u-behema betochah* — because of the multitude of people and animals within it.

### Added Structures

Resh Lakish teaches: in the future God will add to Jerusalem a thousand *tapaf* of gardens — the *Rashbam* reads *tapaf* as a *gematria*, yielding approximately 169,000 gardens — along with 210,000 towers, approximately 146,000 castles (*biranyot*), and vast numbers of a structure called *tutafra'ot*, also a type of castle. *Tosafot* takes a different approach, reading the terms as descriptive rather than numerical. In either reading, the multiplication is staggering.

### Building Sizes

Each one of these structures, Resh Lakish continues, will be as large as *Tzippori be-shalvatah* — Tzippori at its greatest height, at its peak of prosperity. A *baraita* provides testimony: Rabbi Yosei said: *ani ra'iti Tzippori be-shalvatah* — I myself saw Tzippori in its prosperity, and it was so abundant that even the remnants scraped from the bottom of the pot were sold in its markets.

### Size Expansion

The Gemara concludes with one final *derasha* on the phrase *ve-tzela el tzela, shalosh ve-sheloshim pa'amim* — side chamber by side chamber, thirty-three times — a reference to the chambers of the *Beit HaMikdash* in Yechezkel. What do thirty-three times mean in this context? In the name of Rav Yehoshua of Sachni: if the future Jerusalem will be three times the size of the current one, then each building will have thirty stories — high-rise Jerusalem. If it will be thirty times the size of the current one, then each building will have three stories. The number thirty-three encodes both possibilities: three Jerusalems each with thirty floors, or thirty Jerusalems each with three floors. With this the *aggadata* of Bava Batra concludes, and the *masechta* returns to *halakhic* discussion.
