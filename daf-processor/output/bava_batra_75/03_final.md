# Bava Batra 75 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rahav & Leviathan

### Rahav's Slaying

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁבִּיקֵּשׁ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לִבְראוֹת אֶת הָעוֹלָם, אָמַר לוֹ לְשַׂר שֶׁל יָם: פְּתַח פִּיךָ וּבְלַע כׇּל מֵימוֹת שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם. אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, דַּי שֶׁאֶעְמוֹד בְּשֶׁלִּי. מִיָּד בָּעַט בּוֹ וַהֲרָגוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּכֹחוֹ רָגַע הַיָּם וּבִתְבוּנָתוֹ מָחַץ רָהַב״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: At the time when the Holy One, Blessed be He, sought to create the world, He said to the minister of the sea: Open your mouth and swallow all the waters of the world,** so that there will be room for land. The minister of the sea **said before Him: Master of the Universe,** it is **enough that I will stay within my own** waters. God **immediately struck him and killed him; as it is stated: "He stirs up the sea with His power, and by His understanding He smites through Rahab"** (Job 26:12).

The *daf* opens with a statement of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav. At the moment when God sought to create the world, He said to the *sar shel yam* — the angel appointed over the sea — "Open your mouth and swallow all the waters of the world." This appears to be the mechanism behind the verse *yikavu ha-mayim el makom echad*, the gathering of the waters to one place so that the dry land should appear. The angel, however, refused. The Gemara relates: *amar lefanav ribbono shel olam, dai she-amod b-sheli* — Master of the Universe, it is enough for me to stand within what is mine. What exactly his objection was is not entirely clear, since one might think he would have welcomed more water. In any case, God immediately kicked him and killed him — *miyad be-at bo ve-harago*. The proof-text comes from the book of Iyov: *b-kocho raga ha-yam u-vi-tvunato machatz Rahav* — with His strength He stirred up the sea, and in His wisdom He severed *Rahav*.

### Rahav Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, שָׂרוֹ שֶׁל יָם ״רַהַב״ שְׁמוֹ; וְאִלְמָלֵא מַיִם מְכַסִּין אוֹתוֹ – אֵין כׇּל בְּרִיָּה יְכוֹלָה לַעֲמוֹד בְּרֵיחוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא יָרֵעוּ וְלֹא יַשְׁחִיתוּ בְּכׇל הַר קׇדְשִׁי וְגוֹ׳, כַּמַּיִם לַיָּם מְכַסִּים״ – אַל תִּקְרֵי ״לַיָּם מְכַסִּים״, אֶלָּא ״לְשָׂרָהּ שֶׁל יָם מְכַסִּים״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: Conclude from here** that **the name of the minister of the sea is Rahab, and were it not for waters** of the sea that **cover him, no creature could withstand his smell,** as his corpse emits a terrible stench. **As it is stated: "They shall not hurt nor destroy in all My holy mountain;** for the earth shall be full of the knowledge of the Lord, **as the waters cover the sea"** (Isaiah 11:9). **Do not read** this phrase as **"cover the sea"; rather** read it as: **Cover the minister of the sea,** i.e., the term sea is referring to the minister of the sea, not to the sea itself.

Rabbi Yitzchak draws the inference: *shema mina sar shel yam Rahav shemo* — we learn from this that the name of the master of the sea is *Rahav*. There is a certain circularity to the proof: we are reading the verse in light of the narrative we just told, and the only basis for reading the verse that way is the narrative itself. Nevertheless, that is how the *pasuk* is being employed.

### Rahav's Stench

*Rahav* has been slain and lies at the bottom of the sea. The Gemara adds: *ve-imalay mayim m-chasim oto, ein kol biryah y-cholah la-amod b-reicho* — were it not that water covers him, no creature could withstand the stench of the corpse. The proof-text is Isaiah 11: *lo ya-re'u ve-lo yash-chitu b-chol har kodshi ki mal'ah ha-aretz de'ah es Hashem ka-mayim la-yam m-chasim*. The verse reads literally that knowledge of God covers the earth *like water covers the sea*, which is linguistically awkward — water does not cover the sea, it *is* the sea. Therefore the Gemara reads it as *al tikrei la-yam m-chasim, ela le-saro shel yam m-chasim*: do not read it as "covers the sea," but rather "covers the master of the sea." The water conceals the corpse of *Rahav* and its stench.

### Creation Myths

This material resonates with a broader cultural context. In ancient Near Eastern mythology, creation accounts frequently involve a primordial battle between the creator-god and a divine power over the sea. The name of that sea-god, *Tiamat*, bears a striking resemblance to the Hebrew *tehomot*. What is remarkable about the Torah's account of creation in Bereshit is that it is entirely non-mythological — not even in the sense of engaging and rejecting these myths, but in simply ignoring them, treating them as though they do not exist. This is perhaps the most powerful form of rejection: to not even dignify the opposing view with a response.

Yet in the book of Iyov, these echoes come more explicitly to the fore. The *pesukim* there about God conquering *Rahav* and the *leviatan* appear to either allude to or actively engage these ancient traditions of divine conquest over the sea as the precondition for creation. The *midrash* here in the Gemara reinforces that connection: it is not a loose *aggadic* reading floating free of its *pesukim*, but rather an interpretation that takes seriously what those *pesukim* in Iyov are actually saying about God's primordial victory over the forces of the sea. The Torah's approach remains non-mythological, but those older traditions did not entirely disappear, and here, in Iyov and in this *aggadata*, their echoes surface.

## Jordan & Leviathan

### Jordan's Path

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: יַרְדֵּן – יוֹצֵא מִמְּעָרַת פַּמְיָיס. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: יַרְדֵּן יוֹצֵא מִמְּעָרַת פַּמְיָיס, וּמְהַלֵּךְ בְּיַמָּהּ שֶׁל סִיבְכִי וּבְיַמָּהּ שֶׁל טְבֶרְיָא, וּמִתְגַּלְגֵּל וְיוֹרֵד לַיָּם הַגָּדוֹל; וּמִתְגַּלְגֵּל וְיוֹרֵד עַד שֶׁמַּגִּיעַ לְפִיו שֶׁל לִוְיָתָן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יִבְטַח כִּי יָגִיחַ יַרְדֵּן אֶל פִּיהוּ״. מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבָא בַּר עוּלָּא: הַאי בִּבְהֵמוֹת בְּהַרְרֵי אֶלֶף כְּתִיב! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא בַּר עוּלָּא: אֵימָתַי בְּהֵמוֹת בְּהַרְרֵי אֶלֶף בְּטוּחוֹת? בִּזְמַן שֶׁמֵּגִיחַ יַרְדֵּן בְּפִיו שֶׁל לִוְיָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: The Jordan issues forth from the cave of Pamyas. That is also taught** in a *baraita*: **The Jordan issues forth from the cave of Pamyas, and travels in the Sea of Sivkhi,** i.e., the Hula Lake, **and in the Sea of Tiberias,** the Sea of Galilee, **and rolls down to the Great Sea, and rolls down until it reaches the mouth of the leviathan. As it is stated: "He is confident, though the Jordan rush forth to his mouth"** (Job 40:23). **Rava bar Ulla strongly objects to this** explanation of the verse, stating: But **this** verse **is written about the beasts on the thousand hills. Rather, Rava bar Ulla said** that this is the meaning of the verse: **When are the beasts on the thousand hills confident? When the Jordan rushes forth into the mouth of the leviathan.**

The Gemara continues with another statement of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: the Jordan River exits from the cave of *Pamyas* — possibly the Banias — and this is corroborated by a *baraita*. From there it flows through the *yam* of Sibchi, then the *yam* of Tiberias — the *Kinneret* — and then winds its way to the Mediterranean. From there it continues to flow until it reaches the mouth of the *leviatan*, as the verse in Iyov says: *yivtach ki yagiach yarden el pihu* — he will be confident because the Jordan will roar into his mouth. How the Jordan makes its way from the Mediterranean to the *leviatan* is not explained. The geography of these seas is also difficult to reconstruct, though one of them may have been the ancient marsh that once lay near the Banias area, which has since dried up.

### Beasts' Confidence

Rava bar Ulla objects: that verse in Iyov is not talking about the *leviatan* at all — it is written in the context of *behemot beharerei alef*, the great beasts of a thousand mountains. He therefore reinterprets: the verse indeed refers to those beasts — but when are the *behemot beharerei elef* confident, *betuchot*? When the Jordan pours into the *leviatan*'s mouth — that is, when the *leviatan* is well-fed and satisfied, the other great beasts feel secure. The *Rashbam* adds a further dimension: as long as God is actively sustaining the *leviatan*, the *behemot* are confident that they too will be sustained. As long as it lives, they feel confident they will live.

There is also a tradition in *Midrash Vayikra Rabbah* that in the future the *behemot* and the *leviatan* will battle each other as a kind of sport for the righteous — a *milchemet Gog u-Magog* in miniature — which would further explain the *behemot*'s interest in the *leviatan*'s state.

### Seven Seas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי הוּא עַל יַמִּים יְסָדָהּ וְעַל נְהָרוֹת יְכוֹנְנֶהָ״? אֵלּוּ שִׁבְעָה יָמִים וְאַרְבָּעָה נְהָרוֹת שֶׁמַּקִּיפִין אֶת אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן שִׁבְעָה יָמִים: יַמָּהּ שֶׁל טְבֶרְיָא, וְיַמָּהּ שֶׁל סְדוֹם, וְיַמָּהּ שֶׁל חֵילָת, וְיַמָּהּ שֶׁל חֵילָתָא, וְיַמָּהּ שֶׁל סִיבְכִי, וְיָם אַסְפַּמְיָא, וְיָם הַגָּדוֹל. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן אַרְבָּעָה נְהָרוֹת: יַרְדֵּן, וְיַרְמוּךְ, וְקֵירוּמְיוֹן, וּפֵיגָה.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "For He has founded it upon the seas, and established it upon the floods"** (Psalms 24:2)? **These are** the **seven seas and four rivers that surround Eretz Yisrael. And these are** the **seven seas: The Sea of Tiberias, the Sea of Sodom,** i.e., the Dead Sea, **the Sea of Ḥeilat, the Sea of Ḥeilata, the Sea of Sivkhi, the Sea of Aspamya, and the Great Sea,** i.e., the Mediterranean. **And these are the four rivers: The Jordan, the Jarmuth, and the Keiromyon, and the Piga,** which are the rivers of Damascus.

When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he cited Rabbi Yochanan's interpretation of the verse *ki hu al yamim yesada ve-al neharot yechoneneha* — God has established it upon seas and upon rivers. These seas and rivers, says Rabbi Yochanan, are the seven seas and four rivers that surround the Land of Israel. The seven seas are: the *yam* of Tiberias (the *Kinneret*), the *yam* of Sodom (the Dead Sea), the *yam* of Cheilat, the *yam* of Chilta, the *yam* of Sibchi, the *yam* of *Aspamya*, and the *yam hagadol* (the Mediterranean). Of these, three are readily identifiable; the *Yerushalmi* reads *Apamea* in place of *Aspamya*, which appears to refer to a town in Syria with an ancient lake. The four rivers are the *Yarden*, *Yarmokh*, *Kiromon*, and *Pigah*.

## Leviathan's Nature

### Gabriel's Hunt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָתִיד גַּבְרִיאֵל לַעֲשׂוֹת קְנִיגְיָא עִם לִוְיָתָן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַתִּמְשֹׁךְ לִוְיָתָן בְּחַכָּה וּבְחֶבֶל תַּשְׁקִיעַ לְשֹׁנוֹ״, וְאִלְמָלֵא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עוֹזְרוֹ – אֵין יָכוֹל לוֹ; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הָעֹשׂוֹ יַגֵּשׁ חַרְבּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **In the future, Gabriel will perform a hunt of the leviathan, as it is stated: "Can you draw out leviathan with a fish hook? Or press down his tongue with a cord?"** (Job 40:25). **And were the Holy One, Blessed be He, not assisting** Gabriel, **he would not be able to** hunt **it, as it is stated: "Only He Who made him can use His sword to approach him"** (Job 40:19).

The Gemara relates that in the future, *Gavriel* will make a *kenigya* — a hunting or sporting expedition — against the *leviatan*. The word *kenigya* appears to be a loan-word, likely Greek, meaning a hunt or chase. The *pasuk* cited is from Iyov: *Hatimshokh Livyatan be-chakkah, u-ve-chevel tashki'a leshono* — will you drag the *leviatan* with a hook and submerge its tongue with a rope? However, even *Gavriel* cannot prevail against the *leviatan* without God's help: *ve-imla Ha-Kadosh Barukh Hu ozro, ein yakhol lo*. The proof-text is: *Ha-oso yiggash charvo* — the one who made it brings the sword against it. That is God Himself.

The same pattern recurs here that we saw earlier with the *sar shel yam*: God's ultimate conquest over the sea monsters. These, too, carry the echo of those ancient creation myths — God slaughtering the female *leviatan*, God conquering the sea monsters. The *aggadata* consistently frames these primordial victories as requiring divine power that transcends what any angel can muster alone.

### Hunger & Steam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁלִּוְיָתָן רָעֵב, מוֹצִיא הֶבֶל מִפִּיו וּמַרְתִּיחַ כׇּל מֵימוֹת שֶׁבַּמְּצוּלָה – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יַרְתִּיחַ כַּסִּיר מְצוּלָה״. וְאִלְמָלֵא מַכְנִיס רֹאשׁוֹ לְגַן עֵדֶן – אֵין כׇּל בְּרִיָּה יְכוֹלָה לַעֲמוֹד בְּרֵיחוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יָם יָשִׂים כְּמֶרְקָחָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: When** the **leviathan is hungry, he produces breath from his mouth and** thereby **boils all of the waters in the depths** of the sea. **As it is stated: "He makes the deep boil like a pot"** (Job 41:23). **And if** the leviathan **did not place its head in the Garden of Eden, no creature could withstand his** foul **smell, as it is stated: "He makes the sea like a seething mixture [*merkaḥa*]"** (Job 41:23), and the term *merkaḥa* is also used to describe something with a smell (see Exodus 30:25).

When Rav Dimi came, he cited Rabbi Yochanan with another teaching about the *leviatan*: when it is hungry, *motzi hevel mi-piv* — it releases steam from its mouth — *u-marthi'ach kol meimot she-ba-metzulah*, and this boils all the waters in the depths. Whether this is a side effect of its hunger or the mechanism by which it cooks its food is not entirely clear; the image of a dragon whose nostrils emit steam when it is angered is evocative, though cooking fish into soup is equally plausible. The verse cited from Iyov describes it vividly: *Yarti'ach ka-sir metzulah* — it will boil up the deep like a pot. These images are drawn directly from the *pesukim* in Iyov, where the *leviatan* is described in these dramatic, almost fantastical terms — this is not purely the Gemara's creative imagination, but rather an exposition of those remarkable verses.

### Eden Cooling

Were the *leviatan* not to insert its head into *Gan Eden* at such moments — *ve-ilmalei machnis rosho le-Gan Eden* — no creature could withstand the stench or heat that comes from its mouth. *Gan Eden* functions here as a place that sweetens or cools the creature. The verse cited is *yam yasim ke-merkacha* — the sea it will make like a *merkacha*, a sweet-smelling ointment — and the *Rashbam* or *Rashi* understands this as the effect of *Gan Eden* sweetening the *leviatan*'s odor.

### Thirst & Recovery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְשָׁעָה שֶׁצָּמֵא – עוֹשֶׂה תְּלָמִים תְּלָמִים בַּיָּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אַחֲרָיו יָאִיר נָתִיב״. אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: אֵין תְּהוֹם חוֹזֵר לְאֵיתָנוֹ עַד שִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יַחְשֹׁב תְּהוֹם לְשֵׂיבָה״ – וְאֵין ״שֵׂיבָה״ פְּחוּתָה מִשִּׁבְעִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And when he is thirsty, he makes many furrows in the sea, as it is stated: "He makes a path to shine after him"** (Job 41:24). **Rav Aḥa bar Yaakov says:** After the leviathan drinks from the sea, **the depth** of the sea does **not return to its normal condition until seventy years** have passed, **as it is stated: "One would think the deep to be hoary"** (Job 41:24), **and hoary** indicates a person who is **no less than seventy** years old.

When the *leviatan* thirsts, it *oseh t'lamim t'lamim ba-yam* — makes furrows in the sea, great wave-like pathways. The verse is *acharayiv ya'ir nativ* — after it the path lights up. Rav Acha bar Yaakov adds: after the *leviatan* has drunk or boiled the waters, the depths do not return to their original strength for seventy years — *ein tehom chozer le-eitano ad shiv'im shanah*. The proof is from the verse *yachshov tehom le-seivah* — the deep will reckon itself old — and since *seivah* refers to old age, which begins at seventy, the restoration takes seventy years.

## Leviathan Feast

### The Feast

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עָתִיד הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לַעֲשׂוֹת סְעוּדָה לַצַּדִּיקִים מִבְּשָׂרוֹ שֶׁל לִוְיָתָן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יִכְרוּ עָלָיו חַבָּרִים״; וְאֵין ״כֵּרָה״ אֶלָּא סְעוּדָה – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּכְרֶה לָהֶם כֵּרָה גְדוֹלָה וַיֹּאכְלוּ וַיִּשְׁתּוּ״; וְאֵין ״חֲבֵרִים״ אֶלָּא תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַיּוֹשֶׁבֶת בַּגַּנִּים, חֲבֵרִים מַקְשִׁיבִים לְקוֹלֵךְ, הַשְׁמִיעִנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In the **future, the Holy One, Blessed be He, will make a feast for the righteous from the flesh of the leviathan, as it is stated: "The *ḥabbarim* will make a feast [*yikhru*] of him"** (Job 40:30). **And *kera*** means **nothing other than a feast, as it is stated: "And he prepared [*va'yikhreh*] for them a great feast [*kera*]; and they ate and drank"** (II Kings 6:23). **And *ḥabbarim*** means **nothing other than Torah scholars, as it is stated: "You that dwell in the gardens, the companions [*ḥaverim*] hearken for your voice: Cause me to hear it"** (Song of Songs 8:13). This verse is interpreted as referring to Torah scholars, who listen to God's voice.

Rabbah in the name of Rabbi Yochanan states: in the future, God will make a *se'udah* for the righteous from the flesh of the *leviatan* — *atid Hakadosh Baruch Hu la'asot se'udah le-tzaddikim mi-besaro shel Livyatan*. This echoes what was mentioned earlier in this *aggadata* — that the female *leviatan* was salted away for the future feast. The verse cited is *yichru alav chaverim* — the friends will feast upon it — and the Gemara establishes that *keriyah* means feasting, as in the verse *vayichreh lahem kera gedolah*, he made for them a great feast.

### Friends & Markets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְּׁאָר – מְחַלְּקִין אוֹתוֹ, וְעוֹשִׂין בּוֹ סְחוֹרָה בְּשׁוּקֵי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יֶחֱצוּהוּ בֵּין כְּנַעֲנִים״; וְאֵין ״כְּנַעֲנִים״ אֶלָּא תַּגָּרִים – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כְּנַעַן בְּיָדוֹ מֹאזְנֵי מִרְמָה לַעֲשֹׁק אָהֵב״. וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא, מֵהָכָא: ״אֲשֶׁר סֹחֲרֶיהָ שָׂרִים, כִּנְעָנֶיהָ נִכְבַּדֵּי אָרֶץ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to **the remainder** of the leviathan, they will **divide it and use it for commerce in the markets of Jerusalem, as it is stated: "They will part him among the *kena'anim*"** (Job 40:30). **And *kena'anim*** means **nothing other than merchants, as it is stated: "As for the merchant [*kena'an*], the balances of deceit are in his hand. He loves to oppress"** (Hosea 12:8). **And if you wish, say** that the proof is **from here: "Whose merchants are princes, whose traffickers [*kinaneha*] are the honorable of the earth"** (Isaiah 23:8).

The "friends" (*chaverim*) in that verse are identified as *talmidei chachamim* — Torah scholars — from the verse in *Shir Hashirim*: *hayoshevet ba-ganim, chaverim makshivim le-koleich, hashmi'ini* — she who dwells in the gardens, the friends who listen to her voice, let me hear it. In *Shir Hashirim*, understood as an allegory of God and Israel, these friends are those who hear and study God's words — the Torah sages.

What remains after the feast is divided and sold in the marketplaces of Jerusalem. The *tzaddikim* appear to profit from the leftovers. The verse *yach'tzuhu bein Kena'anim* — it will be divided among the *Kena'anim* — is read through the Gemara's identification of *Kena'anim* with merchants, as in *Kena'an be-yado moznei mirmah* (Hoshea) and *asher sochareha sarim, kena'aneha nichbedei aretz* (Yeshayahu) — its merchants are princes, its traders the honored of the land. The *leviatan* thus provides both a feast and a commercial opportunity.

The deeper question of why specifically the *leviatan* serves as the meal of the righteous points toward the symbolic dimension of the whole narrative. The *leviatan* represents something large and ancient that only God's power can conquer. Its defeat and consumption by the righteous connects the future victory over evil with the reward of those who conquered their own *yetzer hara* in this world. As a metaphor it is remarkably apt: the greater the *leviatan* you overcame, the larger your portion.

### Merit Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עָתִיד הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לַעֲשׂוֹת סוּכָּה לַצַּדִּיקִים מֵעוֹרוֹ שֶׁל לִוְיָתָן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַתְמַלֵּא בְשֻׂכּוֹת עוֹרוֹ״. זָכָה – עוֹשִׂין לוֹ סוּכָּה, לֹא זָכָה – עוֹשִׂין לוֹ צִלְצָל; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבְצִלְצַל דָּגִים רֹאשׁוֹ״. זָכָה – עוֹשִׂין לוֹ צִלְצָל, לֹא זָכָה – עוֹשִׂין לוֹ עֲנָק; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַעֲנָקִים לְגַרְגְּרֹתֶיךָ״. זָכָה – עוֹשִׂין לוֹ עֲנָק, לֹא זָכָה – עוֹשִׂין לוֹ קָמֵיעַ; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְתִקְשְׁרֶנּוּ לְנַעֲרוֹתֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In the **future, the Holy One, Blessed be He, will prepare a *sukka* for the righteous from the skin of** the **leviathan, as it is stated: "Can you fill his skin with barbed irons [*besukkot*]"** (Job 40:31). If one **is deserving** of being called righteous, an entire ***sukka* is prepared for him** from the skin of the leviathan; if one is **not deserving** of this honor, **a covering is prepared for his** head, **as it is stated: "Or his head with fish-spears"** (Job 40:31). If one is **deserving** at least of this reward, **a covering is prepared for him,** and if one is **not deserving, a necklace is prepared for him, as it is stated: "And necklaces about your neck"** (Proverbs 1:9). If one is somewhat **deserving, a necklace is prepared for him,** and if one is **not deserving** even of this, only **an amulet is prepared for him** from the skin of the leviathan, **as it is stated: "Or will you bind him for your maidens"** (Job 40:29), i.e., a small amulet is prepared for him, like the amulets tied on children's necks.

Rabbah continues in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: God will make a *sukkah* for the righteous from the *leviatan*'s skin — *atid Hakadosh Baruch Hu la'asot sukkah la-tzaddikim me-oro shel Livyatan*. This resonates with the tradition in *Masechet Sukkah* that in the end all of Israel will dwell in one *sukkah*. The verse is *ha-timaleh be-sukkot oro* — will its skin be filled with huts — and the reading is that its skin will create the *sukkah* in which all dwell.

The reward is then graduated by merit. If the righteous person *zacha* — merits — he receives a full *sukkah*. If he does not fully merit, he receives *tzeltzel*, a shade — as in *u-ve-tzeltzel dagim rosho*, with the shade of fish over his head. If he does not merit even that, he receives *anak* — a necklace — from the bones, as in *va-anakim le-gargerotecha*. And if he does not merit even that, he receives a *kameia*, an amulet — as in *va-tikshreinu le-na'arotecha*. The skin of the *leviatan* thus provides protection, adornment, or at minimum a token of remembrance, each according to what the person achieved in this world.

### Wall Covering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְּׁאָר – פּוֹרְסוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עַל חוֹמוֹת יְרוּשָׁלַיִם, וְזִיווֹ מַבְהִיק מִסּוֹף הָעוֹלָם וְעַד סוֹפוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָלְכוּ גוֹיִם לְאוֹרֵךְ, וּמְלָכִים לְנֹגַהּ זַרְחֵךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to **the remaining** part of the skin of the leviathan, **the Holy One, Blessed be He, spreads it on the walls of Jerusalem, and its glory radiates from** one **end of the world until the** other **end. As it is stated: "And nations shall walk in your light, and kings at the brightness of your rising"** (Isaiah 60:3).

Whatever skin remains after all of this, God will spread over the walls of Jerusalem — *u-vehash'ar, parso ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu al chomot Yerushalayim* — and its radiance will shine from one end of the world to the other. The verse is *ve-halchu goyim le-orech u-melachim le-nogah zarchach* — nations will walk by your light and kings by the brightness of your shining. The verse is often understood as a call to moral example — *or la-goyim*, a light unto the nations — but here its literal meaning is quite material: the luminous skin of the *leviatan* stretched across Jerusalem's walls will illuminate the entire world.

## Jerusalem Walls

### Gate Materials

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְשַׂמְתִּי כַּדְכֹד שִׁמְשֹׁתַיִךְ״ – אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי: פְּלִיגִי תְּרֵי מַלְאֲכֵי בִּרְקִיעָא – גַּבְרִיאֵל וּמִיכָאֵל; וְאָמְרִי לַהּ תְּרֵי אָמוֹרָאֵי בְּמַעְרְבָא, וּמַאן אִינּוּן – יְהוּדָה וְחִזְקִיָּה בְּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא; חַד אָמַר: שׁוֹהַם, וְחַד אָמַר: יָשְׁפֵה. אָמַר לְהוּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: לֶהֱוֵי כְּדֵין וּכְדֵין.
>
> **Translation:** **"And I will make your pinnacles of *kadkhod*"** (Isaiah 54:12). **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said: Two angels in heaven, Gabriel and Michael, disagree** with regard to the material that will be used to form the walls of Jerusalem. **And some say** that this dispute is between **two *amora'im* in the West,** i.e., Eretz Yisrael. **And who are they?** They are **Yehuda and Ḥizkiyya, the sons of Rabbi Ḥiyya. One said** they will be made of **onyx, and one said** of **jasper. The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to them: Let it be like this [*kedein*] and like that [*ukhedein*],** i.e., let them be formed from both together. This compromise is indicated by the word *kadkhod*, a combination of this [*kedein*] and that [*ukhedein*].

The Gemara now cites the verse *ve-shamti kadkod shimshosayich* — and I will make your *shimshosayich* (a term interpreted as the walls or windows) like *kadkod*, a precious stone. Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani reports that two angels in heaven debated the meaning of this word: *Gavriel* and *Michael* — though *Tosafot* notes that *Eliyahu* must have been the one who revealed this heavenly debate to us. Alternatively, some say the dispute was between two *amora'im* in the West, namely Yehuda and Chizkiya the sons of Rabbi Chiya. One held that the material is *shoham* (onyx), the other that it is *yashfeh* (jasper, or according to some, carbuncle). God responded: *lehavei kedein ve-kedein* — it will be both. Hence the word *kadkod* encodes *kedein va-kedein*, both this and this. *Tosafot* adds that since both *shoham* and *yashfeh* appear earlier in the surrounding *pesukim*, the resolution of *kedein va-kedein* refers back to those specific stones.

### Block Dimensions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּשְׁעָרַיִךְ לְאַבְנֵי אֶקְדָּח״ – כִּי הָא דְּיָתֵיב רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְקָא דָרֵישׁ: עָתִיד הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְהָבִיא אֲבָנִים טוֹבוֹת וּמַרְגָּלִיּוֹת שֶׁהֵם שְׁלֹשִׁים עַל שְׁלֹשִׁים, וְחוֹקֵק בָּהֶן עֶשֶׂר עַל עֶשְׂרִים, וּמַעֲמִידָן בְּשַׁעֲרֵי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם. לִגְלֵג עָלָיו אוֹתוֹ תַּלְמִיד: הַשְׁתָּא כְּבֵיעֲתָא דְצִיצְלָא לָא מַשְׁכְּחִינַן, כּוּלֵּי הַאי מַשְׁכְּחִינַן?!
>
> **Translation:** **"And your gates of precious stones"** (Isaiah 54:12). This should be understood **in** light of **that** incident **where Rabbi Yoḥanan sat and taught:** In the **future, the Holy One, Blessed be He, will bring precious stones and pearls that are thirty by thirty** cubits, **and He will hollow out in them** a hole of **ten by twenty** cubits **and set them in the gates of Jerusalem. A certain** unnamed **student sneered at him,** saying: **Now we do not find** precious stones even **of** the size of **an egg of a dove,** and yet **all of this we will find?**

The verse continues: *u-she'arayich le-avnei ekdach* — and your gates like stones of *ekdach*. The word is connected by *derash* to *kodeach*, meaning to carve or pierce. Rabbi Yochanan was sitting and expounding: in the future, God will bring beautiful jewels and pearls of *shoham*, thirty by thirty *amot*, and will carve within them a gate ten *amot* wide and twenty *amot* high. The full block is thirty by thirty, and the gate carved within it is ten wide by twenty tall — meaning the gate occupies the center of the block, with the full block forming the wall through which one passes.

### Student's Mockery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְיָמִים הִפְלִיגָה סְפִינָתוֹ בַּיָּם, חֲזָא מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת דְּיָתְבִי וְקָא מְינַסְּרִי אֲבָנִים טוֹבוֹת וּמַרְגָּלִיּוֹת שֶׁהֵם שְׁלֹשִׁים עַל שְׁלֹשִׁים, וְחָקוּק בָּהֶן עֶשֶׂר בְּרוּם עֶשְׂרִים. אֲמַר לְהוּ: הָנֵי לְמַאן? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: שֶׁעָתִיד הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְהַעֲמִידָן בְּשַׁעֲרֵי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּרוֹשׁ רַבִּי, לְךָ נָאֶה לִדְרוֹשׁ, כַּאֲשֶׁר אָמַרְתָּ כֵּן רָאִיתִי. אָמַר לוֹ: רֵיקָא! אִלְמָלֵא לֹא רָאִיתָ – לֹא הֶאֱמַנְתָּ; מְלַגְלֵג עַל דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים אַתָּה! נָתַן עֵינָיו בּוֹ, וְנַעֲשָׂה גַּל שֶׁל עֲצָמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **After** a period of **time** that student's **ship went to sea,** where **he saw ministering angels sitting and sawing precious stones and pearls that were thirty by thirty** cubits, **and hollowed out in them** were holes of **ten by twenty** cubits. **He said to** the angels: **For whom are these? They said to him that** in the **future, the Holy One, Blessed be He, will place them in the gates of Jerusalem.** Later, the student **came before Rabbi Yoḥanan** and **said to him:** Continue to **interpret, my teacher, it is fitting for you to interpret,** as **I saw** just **as you said.** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: Worthless** man, **if you had not seen, you would not have believed;** clearly, **you are mocking the statement of the Sages.** Rabbi Yoḥanan **set his eyes upon him, and** the student was instantly killed **and turned into a pile of bones.**

A student mocked this vision: *hashta ke-veiyata de-tzitzela lo mishkachinan* — we cannot even find an onyx stone the size of a small bird's egg — *kulei hai mishkachinan*? You are claiming we will find one thirty by thirty *amot*? Many days later, the student's boat sailed far out to sea. He saw ministering angels sitting and hewing out precious jewels and stones — thirty by thirty — with a gate carved in them ten wide by twenty high. He asked them: for whom are these? They told him: these will stand in the gates of Jerusalem. He came before Rabbi Yochanan and said: Teach further, *Rebbi*, for you it is fitting to expound — *ka-asher amarta, ken ra'iti*, just as you said, so I have seen with my own eyes.

Rabbi Yochanan's response was not warm. He said to him: *reika* — empty one! *Ilmalei ra'ita, lo he'emanta* — had you not seen it yourself, you would not have believed? *Nimtza melagleg al divrei chachamim ata* — you turn out to be a mocker of the words of the sages. He cast his eyes upon him and the student became a pile of bones — *natan einav bo ve-na'asu gal shel atzamot*.

This is a striking conclusion to the *aggadata*. The punishment seems to assert that one must take these teachings seriously — not necessarily that every detail is to be understood with wooden literalism, but that one must be able to believe that it *could* happen. If you genuinely believe in *techiyat ha-meitim* and in God's unlimited power, then the physical impossibility of a thirty-by-thirty *shoham* stone offers no real objection. One might wonder why the student was not punished when he first mocked — when he said there isn't even an egg-sized piece of onyx in the whole world. The story works better dramatically this way: the mockery is silently registered, the evidence is shown, and it is only the student's proud declaration that his belief required empirical confirmation that draws Rabbi Yochanan's rebuke.

## Future Heights

### Two Hundred Cubits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״וָאוֹלֵךְ אֶתְכֶם קוֹמְמִיּוּת״ – רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: מָאתַיִם אַמָּה, כִּשְׁתֵּי קוֹמוֹת שֶׁל אָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** against Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement, based on a *baraita*. The verse states: **"And I will make you go upright [*komemiyyut*]"** (Leviticus 26:13). **Rabbi Meir says:** In the future, the Jewish people will have the stature of **two hundred cubits, equivalent to two** times the **height [*komot*] of Adam the first** man, whose height was one hundred cubits. Rabbi Meir interprets the word *komemiyyut* as two *komot*.

A *meitivei* is raised from the verse *ve-holakhtem komemiyut* — you will walk with upright stature. Rabbi Meir says: in the future, human beings will stand two hundred *amot* tall, *kishtei komot shel adam* — double the stature of *Adam HaRishon*, who was a hundred *amot* tall. The word *komemiyut* is read as a double *komah*, a double stature.

### Temple Height

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מֵאָה אַמָּה, כְּנֶגֶד הֵיכָל וּכְתָלָיו – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר בָּנֵינוּ כִּנְטִעִים מְגֻדָּלִים בִּנְעוּרֵיהֶם, בְּנוֹתֵינוּ כְזָוִיֹּת מְחֻטָּבוֹת תַּבְנִית הֵיכָל״! כִּי קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן – לְכַוֵּוי דְּבֵי זִיקָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** They will have the stature of **one hundred cubits, corresponding to the Sanctuary and its walls, as it is stated: "We whose sons are as plants grown up in their youth; whose daughters are as corner-pillars carved after the fashion of the Sanctuary"** (Psalms 144:12). But if they are each one hundred cubits tall, how could the Jews enter the gates of Jerusalem, whose entrance gate will be ten by twenty cubits, as claimed by Rabbi Yoḥanan? The Gemara answers: **When Rabbi Yoḥanan stated** that idea, he was not referring to the gates themselves but **to** the **windows that** allow **wind** to enter.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: humans in the future will be a hundred *amot* tall, corresponding to the height of the *heikhal* — *kineged heikhal ve-kotlav*. The verse cited is *asher baneinu ki-neti'im megudalim bi-nureihem, benoteinu ke-zaviyot mechutavot tavnit heikhal* — our sons like saplings grown in their youth, our daughters like cornerstones carved in the form of the *heikhal*. Since the *heikhal* was a hundred *amot* tall, the future human stature will match it.

### Height Paradox

A problem immediately surfaces. Whether the future human being will be a hundred or two hundred *amot* tall, how can such a person pass through a gate that is a mere twenty *amot* high? If anything, the problem makes the future vision even grander than it first appeared: a human being two hundred *amot* tall is an even more fantastical image than an onyx stone thirty *amot* square.

### Window Blocks

The Gemara resolves the difficulty: *ki ka'amar Rabbi Yochanan, le-shivei de-bei zika* — when Rabbi Yochanan spoke of the thirty-by-thirty blocks with the ten-by-twenty carving, he was referring not to the gates themselves, but to the windows carved into the gateposts for light and air. The gates themselves are far larger.

## Seven Canopies

### Canopy Merit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עָתִיד הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לַעֲשׂוֹת שֶׁבַע חוּפּוֹת לְכׇל צַדִּיק וְצַדִּיק, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבָרָא ה׳ עַל כׇּל מְכוֹן הַר צִיּוֹן וְעַל מִקְרָאֶהָ עָנָן יוֹמָם, וְעָשָׁן וְנֹגַהּ אֵשׁ לֶהָבָה לָיְלָה, כִּי עַל כׇּל כָּבוֹד חֻפָּה״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁכׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד עוֹשֶׂה לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא חוּפָּה לְפִי כְּבוֹדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In the **future, the Holy One, Blessed be He, will fashion seven canopies for each and every righteous** individual, **as it is stated: "And the Lord will create over the whole habitation of Mount Zion, and over those who are invited to it, a cloud and smoke by day, and the shining of a flaming fire by night; for over all the glory shall be a canopy"** (Isaiah 4:5). This **teaches that** for **each and every** righteous individual, **the Holy One, Blessed be He, fashions for him a canopy** seven times over, **in accordance with his honor,** i.e., greater individuals receive grander and larger canopies.

Rabbah in the name of Rabbi Yochanan teaches: in the future, God will make seven *chupot* for each and every righteous person — *atid Hakadosh Baruch Hu la-asot sheva chupot le-chol tzadik ve-tzadik*. Earlier we saw a *sukkah* from the *leviatan*'s skin; now each *tzadik* receives seven individual canopies of divine glory. The proof-text is Yeshayahu 4: *u-bara Hashem al kol mechon Har Tziyon ve-al mikraeha, anan yomam ve-ashan ve-nogah esh lehava lailah, ki al kol kavod chuppah* — God will create upon every place of Mount Zion and upon those called to it, a cloud by day and smoke and the brightness of flaming fire by night, for over all glory there shall be a *chuppah*.

### Isaiah's Seven

Counting the elements in the verse yields the seven *chupot*: *anan* (cloud) is one, *anan yomam* (cloud by day) could be read as elaborating, *ashan* (smoke) is two, *nogah* (brightness) is three, *esh* (fire) is four, *lehava* (flame) is five, *al kol kavod* is six — though the *Rashbam* notes this phrase is descriptive rather than a discrete *chuppah*, meaning each canopy is calibrated to the person's honor — and seven is the next verse, *ve-sukkah tihyeh le-tzel yomam*, which is also quoted at the opening of *Masechet Sukkah*. Each *tzadik* receives all seven, but just as every person can live in a house while houses vary enormously in grandeur, so each person's *chupot* are calibrated to their individual merit and deeds.

### Burning Jealousy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָשָׁן בַּחוּפָּה לָמָּה? אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: שֶׁכׇּל מִי שֶׁעֵינָיו צָרוֹת בְּתַלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, מִתְמַלְּאוֹת עֵינָיו עָשָׁן לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא. וְאֵשׁ בַּחוּפָּה לָמָּה? אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁכׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד נִכְוֶה מֵחוּפָּתוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ. אוֹי לָהּ לְאוֹתָהּ בּוּשָׁה, אוֹי לָהּ לְאוֹתָהּ כְּלִימָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question with regard to the above verse: **Why** should there be **smoke in a canopy? Rabbi Ḥanina said:** It is **because anyone whose eyes are narrow,** i.e., is stingy, **toward Torah scholars in this world, his eyes fill with smoke in the World-to-Come. And why** should there be **fire in a canopy? Rabbi Ḥanina said:** This **teaches that each and every one is burned from** embarrassment at the size of **the canopy of the other,** and says: **Woe for this embarrassment, woe for this disgrace,** that I did not merit a canopy as large as his.

The Gemara asks: why would *ashan* — smoke — be part of a divine canopy? Rabbi Chanina answers: *she-kol mi she-einav tzarot be-talmidei chachamim ba-olam ha-zeh, mitmal'ot einav ashan le-olam ha-ba* — whoever begrudged the Torah scholars in this world will have his eyes filled with smoke in the world to come. This may represent a different type of *derasha* from the counting of canopies above.

Similarly: why would *esh* — fire — feature in a *chuppah*? Rabbi Chanina teaches the famous dictum: *kol echad ve-echad nichveh me-chuppato shel chaveiro* — every person is burned by the canopy of his companion. Even among the righteous, there are levels; and even having a magnificent *chuppah*, one can see that another's is still more glorious. The resulting feeling — a combination of jealousy, regret, and the painful recognition of what one could have achieved — burns. This is not merely envy in a base sense; it is the anguish of confronting one's own unrealized potential when the alternative stands before you. *Oy la-osa busha, oy la-osa kelimah* — woe to that shame, woe to that embarrassment.

### Moses & Joshua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּיּוֹצֵא בַּדָּבָר אַתָּה אוֹמֵר: ״וְנָתַתָּה מֵהוֹדְךָ עָלָיו״ – וְלֹא כׇּל הוֹדְךָ; זְקֵנִים שֶׁבְּאוֹתוֹ הַדּוֹר אָמְרוּ: פְּנֵי מֹשֶׁה כִּפְנֵי חַמָּה, פְּנֵי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ כִּפְנֵי לְבָנָה; אוֹי לָהּ לְאוֹתָהּ בּוּשָׁה, אוֹי לָהּ לְאוֹתָהּ כְּלִימָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **In a similar manner, you** can **say** that God said to Moses about Joshua: **"And you shall put of your honor upon him"** (Numbers 27:20), which indicates that you should put some of your honor, **but not all of your honor. The elders of that generation said: The face of Moses** was as bright **as the face of the sun; the face of Joshua** was **like the face of the moon. Woe for this embarrassment, woe for this disgrace,** that we did not merit another leader of the stature of Moses.

A parallel is drawn. When God said to Moshe *ve-nasata me-hodcha alav* — put from your glory upon Yehoshua — the word is *me-hodcha*, from your glory, not *kol hodcha*, not all of your glory. The elders of Yehoshua's generation, who had known Moshe, would say: *penei Moshe ke-penei chamah, penei Yehoshua ke-penei levanah* — the face of Moshe was like the sun, the face of Yehoshua was like the moon. The moon, of course, only reflects the sun's light. *Oy la-osa busha, oy la-osa kelimah* — woe to such shame and embarrassment. The *Rashbam* explains this not solely as personal embarrassment for Yehoshua, but as the shame of an entire generation that had so quickly descended from such a level: *bi-zman mu'at nitma'et ha-kavod kol kach* — in such a short time, the honor has diminished so greatly.

## Adam's Canopies

### Eden's Treasures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בַּר חֲנִינָא: עֶשֶׂר חוּפּוֹת עָשָׂה הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְאָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן בְּגַן עֵדֶן – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּעֵדֶן גַּן אֱלֹהִים הָיִיתָ, כׇּל אֶבֶן יְקָרָה וְגוֹ׳״. מָר זוּטְרָא אָמַר: אַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כׇּל אֶבֶן יְקָרָה״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְגָרוּעַ שֶׁבְּכוּלָּן – זָהָב, דְּקָא חָשֵׁיב לֵיהּ לְבַסּוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥama bar Ḥanina says: The Holy One, Blessed be He, fashioned ten canopies for Adam the first** man, **in the Garden of Eden; as it is stated** to Hiram, king of Tyre: **"You were in Eden the garden of God; every precious stone** was your covering, the carnelian, the topaz, and the emerald, the beryl, the onyx, and the jasper, the sapphire, the carbuncle, and the smaragd, and gold; the workmanship of your drums and of your holes was in you; they were prepared on the day that you were created" (Ezekiel 28:13). This verse mentions ten items, from carnelian to gold. **Mar Zutra said:** There were **eleven** canopies, **as it states: "Every precious stone,"** which is also part of the tally. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: And the worst of all of them** was **gold, as it is counted last,** which indicates that the other items are more valuable.

Rabbi Chama bar Chanina teaches: God made ten *chupot* for *Adam HaRishon* in *Gan Eden* — *eser chupot asah Hakadosh Baruch Hu le-Adam HaRishon be-Gan Eden*. The *tzaddikim* of the future merit seven; the first human being had ten in paradise. The proof is the verse in Yechezkel addressed to *Chiram* the king of Tyre: *be-Eden gan Elohim hayita, kol even yekara* — you were in the Garden of Eden, every precious stone [was your covering]. Counting the ten types of precious things enumerated in that verse — stones and gold together — yields the ten *chupot*.

### Eleven Canopies

Mar Zutra holds that there are eleven *chupot*, not ten, because *kol even yekara* — "every precious stone" — is itself a separate, generalized item beyond the specific ten listed, making it the eleventh. Rabbi Yochanan adds: the least valuable of all these items in *Gan Eden* was gold — *ve-hagarua she-ba-kulam zahav* — because gold is listed last in the verse. The general principle is that the more important items are listed first.

### Chiram's Pride

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״מְלֶאכֶת תֻּפֶּיךָ וּנְקָבֶיךָ בָּךְ״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְחִירָם מֶלֶךְ צוֹר: ״בְּךָ נִסְתַּכַּלְתִּי, וּבָרָאתִי נְקָבִים נְקָבִים בָּאָדָם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the phrase: **"The workmanship of your drums and of your holes [*nekavekha*]"** (Ezekiel 28:13)? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Hiram, king of Tyre:** Were you in the Garden of Eden when I created all of this for you? **I looked at you,** saw that you would one day claim divinity for yourself, **and created many orifices [*nekavim*] in man,** i.e., the excretory system, so that you would know that you are human and not a god.

The verse then asks: *mai melechet tupayecha u-nekevecha bach* — what is the work of your *tupayecha* and your *nekevecha* within you? This is all within Yechezkel's prophecy against *Chiram* of Tyre, who had grown profoundly arrogant. Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: God said to *Chiram* — *bach nistakalti u-varati nekavim nekavim be-adam* — it was in anticipation of someone like you that I made human beings full of orifices. From those orifices all bodily waste exits — a reminder that however grand a person's self-image, he is flesh and blood like all others. The *Rashbam* connects *tupayecha* to the word for a grave, *ha-kever she-chalul ke-tof* — hollow like a tambourine — meaning the awareness of mortality is built into human anatomy.

An alternative reading: *nistakalti ve-kanasti mita al ha-adam* — I saw that someone like you would arise, and that is why I decreed mortality upon human beings — not because of *Adam HaRishon*'s sin with the tree alone, but so that human beings would always know themselves to be mortal and not mistake themselves for gods. The whole sequence presents a meditation on humility: the righteous receive magnificent *chupot* but are burned by their companion's greater one; *Adam HaRishon* had ten *chupot* but was mortal; and *Chiram*, who thought himself divine, is reminded through the very structure of the human body that he is not.

## God's Name

### Two Jerusalems

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְעַל מִקְרָאֶהָ״? אָמַר רַבָּה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא כִּירוּשָׁלַיִם שֶׁל עוֹלָם הַזֶּה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם שֶׁל עוֹלָם הַבָּא; יְרוּשָׁלַיִם שֶׁל עוֹלָם הַזֶּה – כָּל הָרוֹצֶה לַעֲלוֹת עוֹלֶה, שֶׁל עוֹלָם הַבָּא – אֵין עוֹלִין אֶלָּא הַמְזוּמָּנִין לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the meaning of the phrase: **"And over those who are invited to it"? Rabba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Jerusalem of the World-to-Come is not like Jerusalem of this world.** With regard to **Jerusalem of this world, anyone who wants to ascend** there can **ascend.** With regard to Jerusalem **of the World-to-Come, only those who are invited to it** can **ascend.**

Rabbah in the name of Rabbi Yochanan draws the distinction: *lo ke-Yerushalayim shel ha-olam ha-zeh Yerushalayim shel ha-olam ha-ba* — the Jerusalem of the world to come is not like the Jerusalem of this world. In this world, *kol ha-rotzeh la'alot oleh* — anyone who wishes can go up to Jerusalem. But in the world to come, *ein olim ela ha-mezumanim lah* — only those who have been summoned, invited to it, will ascend. Entry into the future Jerusalem requires, as it were, an engraved invitation.

### Three Names

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עֲתִידִין צַדִּיקִים שֶׁנִּקְרָאִין עַל שְׁמוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כֹּל הַנִּקְרָא בִשְׁמִי וְלִכְבוֹדִי בְּרָאתִיו, יְצַרְתִּיו אַף עֲשִׂיתִיו״. וְאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁלֹשָׁה נִקְרְאוּ עַל שְׁמוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: צַדִּיקִים, וּמָשִׁיחַ, וִירוּשָׁלִַים.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In the **future, the righteous will be called by the name of the Holy One, Blessed be He; as it is stated: "Every one that is called by My name, and whom I have created for My glory, I have formed him, yea, I have made him"** (Isaiah 43:7). This indicates that one who was created by God and causes Him glory is called by His name. **And Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Three were called by the name of the Holy One, Blessed be He, and they are: The righteous, and the Messiah, and Jerusalem.**

Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan: three things are called by the name of God — *sheloshah nikra'u shemo shel Hakadosh Baruch Hu*: *tzaddikim*, *Mashiach*, and *Yerushalayim*. The *tzaddikim* are established from the verse already cited: *kol ha-nikra vi-shmi ve-li-chevodi berativ* — all who are called by My name and for My honor I have created. *Mashiach* is established from the verse in Yirmiyahu: *ve-zeh shemo asher yikre'u Hashem tzidkenu* — and this is his name by which he will be called, "God is our righteousness." *Yerushalayim* is established from Yechezkel: *saviv shemonah asar alef, ve-shem ha-ir mi-yom Hashem shamah* — the circumference is eighteen thousand, and the name of the city from that day shall be "God is there." *Al tikrei shamah ela shemah* — do not read it as "there" (*shamah*) but as "her name" (*shemah*): God is its name.

The theological audacity of this teaching is striking. The *tzaddikim* who dwell in Jerusalem are called by God's name; Jerusalem itself is called by God's name; and *Mashiach* bears God's name. If the righteous dwell in Jerusalem, they are dwelling, in a sense, in God — and they themselves bear His name. The image is extraordinary: God dwelling in God, the righteous called God, dwelling in a city named God.

### Called Holy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: עֲתִידִין צַדִּיקִים שֶׁאוֹמְרִים לִפְנֵיהֶן ״קָדוֹשׁ״ – כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁאוֹמְרִים לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָיָה הַנִּשְׁאָר בְּצִיּוֹן וְהַנּוֹתָר בִּירוּשָׁלִַם, קָדוֹשׁ יֵאָמֶר לוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says:** In the **future, the righteous will** have the name: **Holy, recited before them, as one recites before the Holy One, Blessed be He; as it is stated: "And it shall come to pass, that he who is left in Zion, and he who remains in Jerusalem, shall be called holy"** (Isaiah 4:3).

Rabbi Elazar teaches: in the future, it will be declared *kadosh* — holy — before the righteous, just as the angels declare *kadosh* before God. *Atidim tzaddikim she-omrim lifneihem kadosh.* The proof-text: *ve-hayah ha-nishar be-Tziyon ve-ha-notar bi-Yerushalayim kadosh ye'amer lo* — those who remain in Zion and in Jerusalem, holy will be said of him. The righteous, dwelling in Jerusalem — the extension of the Temple, the place where God's Presence rests — reach a level where the angels address them as they address God. The entire arc of this *aggadata* builds to this point: the conquest of the *leviatan*, the feast, the *sukkot*, the shining walls, the canopies calibrated to merit, and now — the righteous themselves called by God's name, declared holy before them.

## Jerusalem's Future

### Jerusalem's Measure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עָתִיד הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְהַגְבִּיהַּ אֶת יְרוּשָׁלַיִם שָׁלֹשׁ פַּרְסָאוֹת לְמַעְלָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְרָאֲמָה וְיָשְׁבָה תַחְתֶּיהָ״; מַאי ״תַּחְתֶּיהָ״ – כְּתַחְתֶּיהָ. וּמִמַּאי דְּהַאי ״תַּחְתֶּיהָ״ תְּלָתָא פַּרְסֵי הָוְיָא? אָמַר רַבָּה, אֲמַר לִי הָהוּא סָבָא: לְדִידִי חֲזֵי לִי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם קַמַּיְיתָא, וּתְלָתָא פַּרְסֵי הָוְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In the **future, the Holy One, Blessed be He, will raise Jerusalem three parasangs upward; as it is stated: "And it shall be lifted up and inhabited in its place"** (Zechariah 14:10). **What** is the meaning of the phrase **"in its place"?** It means **equivalent to its place,** i.e., Jerusalem will be raised to a height whose measure in parasangs corresponds to the measure of its area in square parasangs. The Gemara asks: **And from where** is it derived **that the place** of Jerusalem **was** an area of **three** square **parasangs? Rabba said** that **a certain elder said to me: I saw the initial Jerusalem,** when it was still extant, **and** its area **was three** square **parasangs.**

Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: God will raise Jerusalem three *parsaot* upward — *atid Hakadosh Baruch Hu le-hagbi'ah et Yerushalayim gimel parsa'ot le-maalah*. The proof is *ve-ramah ve-yashvah tachteha* — it will be raised and dwell beneath itself. *Tachteha* is read as *ke-tachteha* — by as much as its current depth below, meaning the elevation from its hilltop base; that same measure will be added upward. Whether this means it will be raised physically into the air — floating, approaching the heavenly Jerusalem — or simply elevated to a far greater height is debated. A floating Jerusalem fits the subsequent imagery of ascending like clouds.

Rabbah relates that a certain elder told him: *ledidi chazi li Yerushalayim kaimaita, ve-tlata parsei havya* — I saw the first Jerusalem, and it was three *parsaot* [high/elevated]. How Rabbah could have seen the first Jerusalem requires clarification — presumably it means before its destruction, or possibly a prophetic vision.

### No Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר פָּפָּא: בִּקֵּשׁ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לָתֵת אֶת יְרוּשָׁלַיִם בְּמִדָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וָאֹמַר אָנָה אַתָּה הֹלֵךְ, וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלַי: לָמֹד אֶת יְרוּשָׁלִַם, לִרְאוֹת כַּמָּה רׇחְבָּהּ וְכַמָּה אׇרְכָּהּ״ – אָמְרוּ מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, הַרְבֵּה כְּרַכִּים בָּרָאתָ בְּעוֹלָמְךָ שֶׁל אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם – וְלֹא נָתַתָּ מִדַּת אׇרְכָּן וּמִדַּת רׇחְבָּן; יְרוּשָׁלַיִם – שֶׁשִּׁמְךָ בְּתוֹכָהּ, וּמִקְדָּשְׁךָ בְּתוֹכָהּ, וְצַדִּיקִים בְּתוֹכָהּ – אַתָּה נוֹתֵן בָּהּ מִדָּה?! מִיָּד – ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלָו, רֻץ דַּבֵּר אֶל הַנַּעַר הַלָּז לֵאמֹר: פְּרָזוֹת תֵּשֵׁב יְרוּשָׁלִַם, מֵרֹב אָדָם וּבְהֵמָה בְּתוֹכָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappa says: The Holy One, Blessed be He, wished to give** a set **measure for Jerusalem, as it is stated** in a prophecy of Zechariah: "And I lifted up my eyes and saw, and behold a man with a measuring line in his hand. **Then I said: Where are you going? And he said to me: To measure Jerusalem, to see what is its breadth, and what is its length"** (Zechariah 2:5–6). **The ministering angels said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, You have created many cities in Your world for the nations of the world, and You did not give the measure of their length or the measure of their width,** i.e., they are not limited by any set measure, but expand as they develop. With regard to **Jerusalem, which has Your name in it, and Your Temple in it, and righteous people in it,** will **You give it a measure? Immediately:** "And, behold, the angel that spoke with me went forth, and another angel went out to meet him, **and said to him: Run, speak to this young man, saying: Jerusalem shall be inhabited without walls for the multitude of men and cattle within it"** (Zechariah 2:7–8).

God originally wished to give Jerusalem a fixed measure — *bikesh Hakadosh Baruch Hu latet et Yerushalayim be-mida* — which is a strange idea: why would God wish to limit Jerusalem? Perhaps even a vast but finite size represents a kind of limit on what is, by its nature, unlimited. The measuring angel of Zechariah's vision represents this impulse. But the ministering angels protested: *Ribbono shel Olam, harbeh krachim barata be-olamecha shel umot ha-olam, ve-lo natata midat orchan ve-midat rochban* — You have created many cities in Your world for the nations and gave none of them a fixed size; *Yerushalayim she-shimcha betochah, u-mikdashcha betochah, ve-tzaddikim betochah* — Jerusalem in which Your name rests, in which Your sanctuary stands, in which the righteous dwell — *ata noten bah midah*? You will give it a limit? Immediately: *prazot teshev Yerushalayim* — Jerusalem shall dwell as an unwalled city, spread out without limit, *me-rov adam u-behema betochah* — because of the multitude of people and animals within it.

### Added Structures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: עָתִיד הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְהוֹסִיף עַל יְרוּשְׁלֶם אֶלֶף טֶפֶף גִּינּוֹאוֹת, אֶלֶף קֶפֶל מִגְדָּלִים, אֶלֶף לִיצוֹי בִּירָנִיּוֹת, אֶלֶף וּשְׁנֵי שִׁילֹה טוֹטַפְרָאוֹת; וְכׇל אַחַת וְאַחַת הָוְיָא כְּצִפּוֹרִי בְּשַׁלְווֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says:** In the **future, the Holy One, Blessed be He, will add to Jerusalem one thousand** times the numerical value of ***tefaf*** of **gardens; one thousand** times the numerical value of ***kefel*** of **towers; one thousand** times the value of ***litzoy*** of **fortifications;** and **one thousand and two** times the value of ***shilo*** of **small houses [*totpera'ot*]. And each and every one** of these additions **will be like** the great city of **Tzippori in its prosperity.**

Resh Lakish teaches: in the future God will add to Jerusalem a thousand *tapaf* of gardens — the *Rashbam* reads *tapaf* as a *gematria*, yielding approximately 169,000 gardens — along with 210,000 towers, approximately 146,000 castles (*biranyot*), and vast numbers of a structure called *tutafra'ot*, also a type of castle. *Tosafot* takes a different approach, reading the terms as descriptive rather than numerical. In either reading, the multiplication is staggering.

### Building Sizes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אֲנִי רָאִיתִי צִפּוֹרִי בְּשַׁלְווֹתָהּ, וְהָיוּ בָּהּ מֵאָה וּשְׁמוֹנִים אֶלֶף שְׁוָוקִים שֶׁל מוֹכְרֵי צִיקֵי קְדֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the size of the city of Tzippori. **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei said: I saw Tzippori in its prosperity, and there were one hundred and eighty thousand markets of sellers of** meat **sauces in it.** On this basis, one can estimate the future size of Jerusalem.

Each one of these structures, Resh Lakish continues, will be as large as *Tzippori be-shalvatah* — Tzippori at its greatest height, at its peak of prosperity. A *baraita* provides testimony: Rabbi Yosei said: *ani ra'iti Tzippori be-shalvatah* — I myself saw Tzippori in its prosperity, and it was so abundant that even the remnants scraped from the bottom of the pot were sold in its markets.

### Size Expansion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהַצְּלָעוֹת צֵלָע אֶל צֵלָע, שָׁלוֹשׁ וּשְׁלֹשִׁים פְּעָמִים״ – מַאי ״שָׁלוֹשׁ וּשְׁלֹשִׁים פְּעָמִים״? אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי, אָמַר רַב פַּפֵּי מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ דְּסִכְנִי: אִם שָׁלֹשׁ יְרוּשָׁלַיִם הֵן, כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת יֵשׁ בָּהּ שְׁלֹשִׁים מְדוֹרִין לְמַעְלָה; אִם שְׁלֹשִׁים יְרוּשָׁלַיִם הֵן, כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת יֵשׁ בָּהּ שְׁלֹשָׁה מְדוֹרִין לְמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** In a similar manner, the Gemara interprets the verse: **"And the side-chambers were one over another, three and thirty times"** (Ezekiel 41:6). **What** is the meaning of: **"Three and thirty times"? Rabbi Levi says** that **Rav Pappi says in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua of Sikhnei: If** in the future **Jerusalem** will triple in size, so that it occupies **three** times its former area, then **each and every** dwelling will **contain thirty stories upward. If** the area of **Jerusalem** will be multiplied by **thirty, each and every** dwelling will **contain three stories above** every house.

The Gemara concludes with one final *derasha* on the phrase *ve-tzela el tzela, shalosh ve-sheloshim pa'amim* — side chamber by side chamber, thirty-three times — a reference to the chambers of the *Beit HaMikdash* in Yechezkel. What do thirty-three times mean in this context? In the name of Rav Yehoshua of Sachni: if the future Jerusalem will be three times the size of the current one, then each building will have thirty stories — high-rise Jerusalem. If it will be thirty times the size of the current one, then each building will have three stories. The number thirty-three encodes both possibilities: three Jerusalems each with thirty floors, or thirty Jerusalems each with three floors. With this the *aggadata* of Bava Batra concludes, and the *masechta* returns to *halakhic* discussion.
