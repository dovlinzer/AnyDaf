# Bava Batra 118 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three Division Approaches

### Yotzei Mitzrayim vs Ba'ei

The daf continues in the middle of a debate over the basis on which *Eretz Yisrael* was divided among the tribes. Two principal approaches were introduced previously: *yotzei Mitzrayim* and *ba'ei ha'aretz*. To illustrate the difference, consider two brothers — one with one child and one with ten. Under the *yotzei mitzrayim* approach, the number of plots is reckoned according to those who left Egypt. Those who left received virtual allotments that were never actualized in their lifetimes but were passed down to their children. The result is that the first family receives one unit and the second family divides one unit among ten children — a potentially dramatic disproportion between the size of a family and its share of the land.

The *ba'ei ha'aretz* approach is more nuanced than a simple per-capita division of the generation that entered. There remains a sense that the land is an inheritance, not merely a reward for personal achievement. Under this view, the number of plots is reckoned based on those who entered the land, but those portions are then passed virtually upward to the parents' generation and reinherited back downward. The division is thus proportional to the entering generation but retains the character of *nachalah*.

### Combined Approach

A third approach combines both *yotzei mitzrayim* and *ba'ei ha'aretz*: the total number of those who left Egypt and the total number of those who entered are both counted, portions are allocated accordingly, and each individual receives directly what he earns through *ba'ei ha'aretz* as well as what he inherits through *yotzei mitzrayim*. These are the three positions the Gemara works through as it examines various biblical narratives.

## Bnei Yosef Complaint

### B'shlama Yotzei Mitzrayim

The Gemara now tests these approaches against textual evidence from *Tanakh*, picking up several lines from the top of 118. The Gemara states: *b'shlama le-man d'amar le-yotzei mitzrayim* — it works out well according to the view that the land was divided among those who left Egypt, *haynu d'katzivchan B'nei Yosef*, that is why we understand why *Bnei Yosef* came and complained to Yehoshua. The verse states *vayidabru B'nei Yosef* — the sons of Yosef spoke to Yehoshua, declaring that they were a numerous people and their allotted territory was insufficient.

The Gemara's reasoning is this: if the division was based on *yotzei mitzrayim*, the allotments were fixed according to the population that left Egypt. The tribe of *Menashe* and *Ephraim* may have been small at the time of the Exodus but grew disproportionately large by the time they entered the land. A significant skewing could therefore arise between the size of their allotted territory — fixed by the earlier, smaller headcount — and their actual population at the time of settlement. Their complaint to Yehoshua thus makes perfect sense.

### Ba'ei Ha'aretz Challenge

The Gemara then turns to the challenge: *ella le-man d'amar le-ba'ei ha'aretz* — but according to the view that the land was divided among those who entered, what is the complaint? The allotment was calibrated precisely to the population at the moment of entry, with no time having passed for that population to change. *Mai katzivchu? Kulhu shaklu* — why are they complaining? Everyone received a share proportional to their numbers.

### Tfelim Explanation

The Gemara answers: *mishum tfelim* — because of *tfelim*, a term the *Rashbam* explains as referring to those under the age of twenty. The situation envisioned is that *Bnei Yosef* had a very large population of young people under twenty whose parents had died in the *midbar* and who therefore had no parent among the entering generation through whom to inherit. Since everyone over twenty would bequeath their portion to their own children, these orphaned young people under twenty had no one to bequeath to them. The complaint was that a substantial population segment would receive nothing proportional to their numbers. One may note that this would need to represent a significant demographic crisis to motivate the formal complaint, though this is the Gemara's resolution.

### Equal Tribal Lands

A different, and in some ways simpler, resolution is available: if one assumes that each tribe received an equal-sized territorial allotment regardless of its population — that is, that tribal lands were uniform in size rather than proportional to headcount — then *Bnei Yosef*'s complaint needs no special explanation and is independent of whether the division followed *yotzei mitzrayim* or *ba'ei ha'aretz*. They would simply be saying: our tribal land is the same size as every other tribe's land, but our population is far greater, and therefore each individual in our tribe receives far less than individuals in smaller tribes. This approach requires no reference to *tfelim* or demographic asymmetries.

However, this resolution is in tension with the historical record. The map of *Sefer Yehoshua* makes clear that tribal territories were not uniform — *Menashe* alone received a vast area spanning both sides of the Jordan, representing a substantial portion of the entire land. The Gemara at this point assumes equal individual portions (calibrated per person), while later the Gemara will shift to assuming equal tribal allotments, which will seem puzzling in light of the evident historical disproportion.

## Abaye's Inference

### Nobody Excluded Logic

Abaye now draws a broader inference from the narratives of *Bnot Tzelofchad* and *Bnei Yosef*: *shema mina lo havi chad dlo shakil* — we can learn from these stories that there was not a single person who failed to receive a portion in *Eretz Yisrael*.

One might imagine circumstances under which someone would be left without any share. Under *yotzei mitzrayim*, a child born in the *midbar* to a father who had left Egypt under the age of twenty would receive nothing — neither by virtue of entering the land himself nor by virtue of *yotzei mitzrayim*. Under *ba'ei ha'aretz*, a young person under twenty who entered the land without a living parent and without any surviving relative through whom to inherit in the manner of *Bnot Tzelofchad* could similarly find himself landless. Yet Abaye asserts this never occurred.

His reasoning: *d'ai salka daitach hava chad dlo shakil, ibaye leh limzvach* — if there had been even one person excluded from the land, he would surely have complained. *Bnot Tzelofchad* complained; *Bnei Yosef* complained. The cultural pattern of formal complaint was clearly operative and recorded. It follows that anyone who lacked a portion would have stepped forward. And since no additional complaints are recorded, no additional person was excluded.

### Bnei Yosef Counterpoint

The Gemara challenges this inference. Perhaps some people did complain, and their complaint simply went unheeded — and the Torah does not record every complaint that failed. The implied principle the Gemara tests is: *d'tzvach v'hani, kasvi kra; d'tzvach v'lo hani, lo kasvi kra* — that which was complained and succeeded was recorded; that which was complained and failed was not recorded. If so, Abaye's argument collapses, since we cannot infer from silence that no one complained.

Abaye responds by pointing to *Bnei Yosef* themselves: their complaint was recorded even though it ultimately did not succeed. The straightforward reading of *Sefer Yehoshua* seems to suggest their complaint did succeed — Yehoshua told them to go conquer the forests — but the Gemara cannot accept this *k'pshuto*. If the forests were already within *Bnei Yosef*'s tribal allotment, Yehoshua was telling them nothing new. If the forests belonged to another tribe, Yehoshua had no authority to reassign them. Therefore, the Gemara understands the complaint as having not actually been granted. Yet the Torah recorded it. This demonstrates that the Torah does record complaints that led nowhere, and therefore Abaye's argument is reinstated: since no other complaints are recorded, no one else was excluded.

### Ayin Hara Lesson

The Gemara then offers a different resolution to the *Bnei Yosef* episode, one that renders Abaye's inference moot regardless. The Gemara says: *eitza tova k'mashma lan* — the Torah recorded this narrative not as a halachic precedent regarding land distribution, but to convey a moral lesson. *D'ba'i l'inish l'izdaharu mei'ayna bisha* — a person must be vigilant against the *ayin hara*.

This, the Gemara explains, is what Yehoshua was actually telling *Bnei Yosef*: *im am rav ata alei l'cha hayara* — if you are a great nation, go into the forest. Not to conquer additional territory, but *lechu v'hachavu atzmechem b'yaarim shelo tishlot bachem ayina bisha* — go and conceal yourselves in the forests so that the *ayin hara* will not dominate you. Do not publicize your great numbers; remain hidden as fish are hidden in the sea.

*Bnei Yosef* replied: *anan m'zara d'Yosef, d'lo shalta bei ayina bisha* — we are the descendants of Yosef, over whom the *ayin hara* has no dominion. The verse states *ben porat Yosef, ben porat alei ayin* — a fruitful son is Yosef, fruitful by the wellspring. Rabbi Abbahu reads *alei ayin* as *olei ayin*, meaning that Yosef's descendants transcend the *ayin hara* rather than succumb to it. Rabbi Yosei the son of Rabbi Chanina derives the same from the verse *vayidgu larov b'kerev ha'aretz* — they shall multiply like fish within the land: just as fish are covered by water and unseen, and therefore free from the *ayin hara*, so too the descendants of Yosef are immune.

The Gemara thus establishes that the *Bnei Yosef* narrative was preserved for this moral lesson about *ayin hara*, not as evidence of a systematic pattern of recording all complaints. Once the narrative's purpose is reassigned, Abaye's inference from it — that all excluded persons would have been recorded — is undermined.

## Excluded Groups

### Meraglim & Spies

The Gemara now resumes working through the *braita* that catalogs which groups of sinners from the *midbar* did not receive a portion and what became of their shares. Regarding the *meraglim* — the spies who scouted the land and returned with a faithless report — the *braita* states: *Yehoshua bin Nun v'Calev ben Yefuneh natlu chelkam* — Yehoshua and Caleb took their portion.

### Chayu Derasha

The Gemara asks: *mena hani mili* — how do we know this? We know from another verse that Yehoshua and Calev were the sole survivors of that generation; that verse is redundant. The Gemara therefore derives the point from the verse *chayu min ha'anashim ha-hem* — they lived from among those men. Since the simple meaning of "they lived" is already known, the Gemara interprets *chayu* as *shechayu b'chelkam* — they were enriched through their portions, that is, they inherited the portions of the fallen spies.

### Mitlonenim Debate

The *braita* then states that the *mitlonenim* — the complainers — and *adat Korach* did not receive a portion in *Eretz Yisrael*. The Gemara notes a second *braita* that states these groups' portions went to Yehoshua and Calev as well, and resolves the contradiction as a *machloket*: one authority links the *mitlonenim* and *adat Korach* to the *meraglim* by analogy, concluding that just as Yehoshua and Calev inherited from the *meraglim*, so too from the *mitlonenim* and *adat Korach*; the other authority does not draw this analogy and holds that those portions were distributed among all of *Klal Yisrael*.

A further question arises: if the *mitlonenim* refers to all who ever complained against Moshe, then Yehoshua and Calev would have inherited an absurdly large share of the land, encompassing virtually all of *Klal Yisrael* at one point or another. The Gemara clarifies: *mitlonenim she-ba-adat Korach kamina* — the *mitlonenim* in question are specifically the complainers within *adat Korach*, not every instance of *vayilonu*.

### Bnot Tzelofchad Arg

The source from which we know that these three groups — *meraglim*, *mitlonenim*, and *adat Korach* — forfeited their portions is the argument of *Bnot Tzelofchad* themselves. The *braita* cites their declaration: *avinu met ba-midbar* — our father died in the wilderness, *ze Tzelofchad*. He was not among *adat ha-meraglim* — as derived from the verse *ad matai la-eda ha-ra'ah ha-zot*, from which we learn the *meraglim* constituted a formal *edah*. He was not among *ha-noadim al Hashem b'adat Korach* — derived from the verse *ve-chol adatcha ha-noadim al Hashem*. And the *mitlonenim* are equated with that same group.

By arguing that their father belonged to none of these categories, *Bnot Tzelofchad* implicitly confirmed that membership in any of these groups would have disqualified him from an inheritance. Their argument thus serves as the *braita*'s proof that the *meraglim*, *mitlonenim*, and *adat Korach* did not receive portions. The *machloket* that follows concerns not whether they were disqualified but rather to whom their notional shares were assigned.

A brief digression: a famous responsum of Rav Moshe Feinstein observes that *adat Korach*, from which we derive the minimum *minyan* of ten, included individuals who were in effect *apikorsim* — they denied God's ability to bring them to *Eretz Yisrael*. Since they nonetheless count toward the quorum from which we derive the *minyan*, this supports the position that even a *rasha* may be counted.

## Menashe's Ten Portions

### Why Ten Portions?

Rav Papa poses a question to Abaye: *v'amar lei Rav Pappa l'Abaye*. Under *yotzei mitzrayim*, the verse in *Sefer Yehoshua* that says *vayiplu chevlei Menashe asarah* — the portions of *Menashe* fell to ten — makes sense. *Menashe* had six sons who served as the heads of the *batei avot* of the tribe. One of them was *Chefer*, and *Tzelofchad* was *Chefer*'s son. The verse mentions *Tzelofchad* and *Bnot Tzelofchad* and then says the total came to ten portions.

### Yotzei Mitzrayim Count

Under *yotzei mitzrayim*, the portions attributed to *Bnot Tzelofchad* can be enumerated as follows. *Tzelofchad* himself, as one who left Egypt, receives his own individual portion: that is the first. As *Chefer*'s *bechor*, *Tzelofchad* receives a double portion from *Chefer*'s share: that accounts for two more. And if one or more of *Chefer*'s other sons died without male heirs, their portions would revert upward to *Chefer* and be redistributed among his surviving sons, including *Tzelofchad*. With one dead uncle, one arrives at three portions for *Tzelofchad*'s line — which matches the *mishnah*'s count of three. Adding this to six for the *batei avot* yields nine; to reach ten, a second dead uncle is posited, a conclusion the Gemara supports from the superfluous language *achuzat nachalah*.

### Ba'ei Ha'aretz Count

Under *ba'ei ha'aretz*, the picture changes significantly. Since the focus is on the generation that entered the land, *Tzelofchad* himself does not receive a personal portion — he was not among those who entered. All inheritance flows upward through *Chefer* and back down. *Tzelofchad*'s line thus receives only the double *bechor* portion from *Chefer* — two portions — plus whatever accrues from dead uncles. Six plus two yields only eight, leaving the verse's figure of ten unaccounted for without positing two dead uncles. The Gemara points out the asymmetry: *tamniya hu dahavu* — there would only be eight.

### Batei Avot & Bnot

The Gemara resolves the difficulty for *ba'ei ha'aretz* by the same device applied to *yotzei mitzrayim*: positing two dead brothers of *Tzelofchad*'s father rather than one. *Hacha nami trei achi d'abba hava lehu* — here too we say there were two brothers of the father who died, and their portions, redistributed through *Chefer* and back down to the surviving sons including *Tzelofchad*'s line, yield four portions for *Bnot Tzelofchad*, bringing the total to ten.

One must then ask: in what sense does the verse's figure of ten combine six major *batei avot* portions with four relatively minor *Bnot Tzelofchad* portions? These are portions of vastly different magnitude. The Gemara's answer, anticipated by Rav Papa's question, is addressed in the following segment.

## Naton Titein Braita

### Yotzei Mitzrayim Braita

The Gemara cites a *braita* that articulates the *yotzei mitzrayim* position through close reading of the relevant verse. *Naton titein lahem nachalah* — give, give them an inheritance. *Titein lahem* — this refers to *nachalat avihen*, the direct inheritance of their own father *Tzelofchad*, who himself left Egypt and is entitled as a *yotzei mitzrayim*. *Betoch achei avihen* — in the midst of their father's brothers, meaning what they receive through *Chefer* in parallel with their uncles. *Zu nachalat avi avihen* — this is the portion of their father's father, that is, the portion flowing through *Chefer*. And *ve-ha'avarta nachalat avihen lahem* — the additional transfer of their father's inheritance — yields the *chelek bechorah*, the double portion, since *Tzelofchad* was a *bechor*.

### Rabbi Eliezer Addition

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov adds a further element: *af chelek achei avihen natlu* — they also received the portion of their father's brothers, that is, the dead uncle or uncles whose shares were redistributed. This is derived from *naton titein* — the doubled language implying an additional grant beyond the straightforwardly inherited portions.

### Dead Brothers Logic

For the view that requires two dead brothers — which the Gemara needs to reach the figure of ten under *yotzei mitzrayim* — the additional exegetical basis comes from the phrase *achuzat nachalah*. The verse could have said either *nachalah* alone or *achuzah* alone; the combination signals yet another portion, providing the textual hook for a second dead brother's redistributed share.

### Rav Papa Question

Rav Papa's question, now addressed directly, is: *kra mai kachashiv* — what category of portions does the verse enumerate when it says ten? If it is counting *tfelim*, the subdivided portions of later generations, there would be far more than ten by the time of *Bnot Tzelofchad*. If it is counting *batei avot*, the major tribal divisions, there are six.

### Batei Avot Resolution

The Gemara resolves: *le'olam batei avot kachashiv* — the verse is always counting the major *batei avot* divisions. *Ve-ha kamashma lan d'Bnot Tzelofchad natlu chelek bechorah* — the reason the verse reaches ten, incorporating *Bnot Tzelofchad*'s portions alongside the six *batei avot*, is to teach that *Bnot Tzelofchad* received the *bechor* portion. The numerical exercise is thus an exegetical device to highlight the legal significance of their inheritance, and as noted, it simultaneously elevates their claim to a prominence comparable to the foundational tribal divisions themselves.

## Muchzeket Principle

### Bechor Paradox

A fundamental difficulty now surfaces. The Gemara has established that *Tzelofchad* received a *chelek bechorah* from *Chefer*. But the rule governing a *bechor*'s double portion — derived from *Devarim* — is that it applies only to property that was in the father's actual possession at the time of his death: *muchzak*, held and possessed. Property to which the father was merely *ra'uy* — entitled but not yet in hand — does not carry the double portion. *Eretz Yisrael* was never actually in *Chefer*'s possession; it was allocated to him only retroactively and conceptually. How, then, can *Tzelofchad* claim a *chelek bechorah*?

### Muchzeket Answer

The Gemara's answer is striking: *alma Eretz Yisrael muchzeket hi* — we see from this that *Eretz Yisrael* is regarded as *muchzeket*, as fully and actually possessed, not merely *ra'uy*. The entire enterprise of the allocation of *Eretz Yisrael* — passing portions up to the *yotzei mitzrayim* and back down — is treated not as a fictional entitlement but as genuine possession. The land of Israel carries a *chazakah* of ownership rooted in the divine promise and the ancestral inheritance, such that even retroactive conceptual assignment suffices to constitute *muchzak* for purposes of the *bechor*'s double portion.

## Children's Inheritance

### Braita Both Sides

A final *braita* addresses the children of those groups who forfeited their portions — the *meraglim*, *mitlonenim*, and *adat Korach*. The *braita* states: *ve-habanim natlu bizchut avi avihem u-vizchut avi imoteihem* — the children inherited through the merit of their paternal grandfathers and through the merit of their maternal grandfathers. The scenario is that the father, belonging to one of the disqualified groups, did not himself receive a portion, and yet his children were not left landless — they could receive through a grandfather on either the paternal or maternal side who was *yotzei mitzrayim* or *ba'ei ha'aretz* and thus did hold a valid portion.

### Contradiction Resolved

Against this, a second *braita* states that the children received *bizchut atzman* — by virtue of themselves. The apparent contradiction between inheriting through grandparents and receiving by one's own merit is resolved simply: *lo kashya, ha k'man d'amar l'yotzei mitzrayim, ha k'man d'amar l'ba'ei ha'aretz*. Under *yotzei mitzrayim*, a child of a disqualified father has no personal claim to the land and must rely on grandparents through whom to inherit; under *ba'ei ha'aretz*, the child who himself entered the land acquires a portion in his own right.

### Age Distinction

The Gemara offers an alternative resolution that keeps both *braitot* within the *ba'ei ha'aretz* framework: *ha d'havu ben esrim* — for those who were twenty years of age and entered the land, they received *bizchut atzman*, by their own standing as *ba'ei ha'aretz*; *ha d'lo havu ben esrim* — for those who were under twenty at the time of entry, they did not qualify on their own and therefore received only through their grandparents. On this reading, both *braitot* assume *ba'ei ha'aretz*, and the operative distinction is simply one of age at the time of entry into the land.
