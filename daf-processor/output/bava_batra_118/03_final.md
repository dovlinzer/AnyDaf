# Bava Batra 118 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three Division Approaches

### Yotzei Mitzrayim vs Ba'ei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאֵלּוּ וּלְאֵלּוּ נִתְחַלְּקָה הָאָרֶץ, כְּדֵי לְקַיֵּים שְׁנֵי מִקְרָאוֹת הַלָּלוּ – הָא כֵּיצַד? הָיָה מִיּוֹצְאֵי מִצְרַיִם – נוֹטֵל חֶלְקוֹ עִם יוֹצְאֵי מִצְרַיִם, הָיָה מִבָּאֵי הָאָרֶץ – נוֹטֵל חֶלְקוֹ עִם בָּאֵי הָאָרֶץ, מִכָּאן וּמִכָּאן – נוֹטֵל חֶלְקוֹ מִכָּאן וּמִכָּאן.
>
> **Translation:** **Eretz** Yisrael **was divided to these and to those, so as to uphold these two verses. How so?** If one **was from** among **those who left Egypt, he takes his portion** along **with those who left Egypt.** If one **was from** among **those who entered Eretz** Yisrael, **he takes his portion** along **with those who entered Eretz** Yisrael. If one was **from** both **here and there,** i.e., he was among those who left Egypt and also entered Eretz Yisrael himself, **he takes his portion from here and there,** as he is entitled to two portions.

The daf continues in the middle of a debate over the basis on which *Eretz Yisrael* was divided among the tribes. Two principal approaches were introduced previously: *yotzei Mitzrayim* and *ba'ei ha'aretz*. To illustrate the difference, consider two brothers — one with one child and one with ten. Under the *yotzei mitzrayim* approach, the number of plots is reckoned according to those who left Egypt. Those who left received virtual allotments that were never actualized in their lifetimes but were passed down to their children. The result is that the first family receives one unit and the second family divides one unit among ten children — a potentially dramatic disproportion between the size of a family and its share of the land.

The *ba'ei ha'aretz* approach is more nuanced than a simple per-capita division of the generation that entered. There remains a sense that the land is an inheritance, not merely a reward for personal achievement. Under this view, the number of plots is reckoned based on those who entered the land, but those portions are then passed virtually upward to the parents' generation and reinherited back downward. The division is thus proportional to the entering generation but retains the character of *nachalah*.

### Combined Approach

A third approach combines both *yotzei mitzrayim* and *ba'ei ha'aretz*: the total number of those who left Egypt and the total number of those who entered are both counted, portions are allocated accordingly, and each individual receives directly what he earns through *ba'ei ha'aretz* as well as what he inherits through *yotzei mitzrayim*. These are the three positions the Gemara works through as it examines various biblical narratives.

## Bnei Yosef Complaint

### B'shlama Yotzei Mitzrayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְיוֹצְאֵי מִצְרַיִם נִתְחַלְּקָה הָאָרֶץ, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״לָרַב תַּרְבֶּה נַחֲלָתוֹ וְלַמְעַט תַּמְעִיט נַחֲלָתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents the first question. **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: Granted, according to the one who says** that **Eretz** Yisrael **was divided among those who left Egypt, this is** the meaning of that **which is written: "To the more you shall give the more inheritance, and to the fewer you shall give the less inheritance"** (Numbers 26:54). The verse may be understood as teaching the novelty that even though the number of Jewish people entering Eretz Yisrael differed from the number who left Egypt, the land is nevertheless divided according to the numbers at the time of the Exodus, not according to the count at the time they received the land.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְבָאֵי הָאָרֶץ, מַאי ״לָרַב תַּרְבּוּ נַחֲלָתוֹ״? קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **But according to the one who says** that Eretz Yisrael was divided **among those who entered Eretz** Yisrael, **what** does the verse: **"To the more you shall give the more inheritance"** teach? It is obvious that larger families will receive more land due to their greater numbers. The Gemara concludes: This poses **a difficulty** to one who holds that opinion.

The Gemara now tests these approaches against textual evidence from *Tanakh*, picking up several lines from the top of 118. The Gemara's reasoning is this: if the division was based on *yotzei mitzrayim*, the allotments were fixed according to the population that left Egypt. The tribe of *Menashe* and *Ephraim* may have been small at the time of the Exodus but grew disproportionately large by the time they entered the land. A significant skewing could therefore arise between the size of their allotted territory — fixed by the earlier, smaller headcount — and their actual population at the time of settlement. Their complaint to Yehoshua thus makes perfect sense.

### Ba'ei Ha'aretz Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְיוֹצְאֵי מִצְרַיִם, הַיְינוּ דְּקָא צָוְוחָן בְּנוֹת צְלָפְחָד. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְבָאֵי הָאָרֶץ, אַמַּאי צָוְוחָן? הָא לֵיתֵיהּ דְּלִשְׁקוֹל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents the second question. **And Rav Pappa said to Abaye: Granted, according to the one who says** that Eretz Yisrael was divided **among those who left Egypt, this is** why **the daughters of Zelophehad cried out** in protest of the fact that they would be denied their father's portion, to which he was entitled as one who left Egypt. **But according to the one who says** that Eretz Yisrael was divided **among those who entered Eretz** Yisrael, **why did they cry out** in protest; after all, Zelophehad **was not** there **to take** his portion, so his daughters should have no claim to the land?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לַחֲזָרָה, וְלִיטּוֹל בְּנִכְסֵי חֵפֶר.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye answers: **Rather,** according to this opinion, the protest of Zelophehad's daughters was in reference **to the returning** of the portions from the generation that entered Eretz Yisrael to the generation that left Egypt, as described in the *baraita* above. **And** accordingly, Zelophehad's daughters demanded **to take** their portion **in** the **property of** their grandfather **Hepher,** who received land posthumously through his children, their uncles.

The Gemara then turns to the challenge: *ella le-man d'amar le-ba'ei ha'aretz* — but according to the view that the land was divided among those who entered, what is the complaint? The allotment was calibrated precisely to the population at the moment of entry, with no time having passed for that population to change. *Mai katzivchu? Kulhu shaklu* — why are they complaining? Everyone received a share proportional to their numbers.

### Tfelim Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם טְפָלִים דַּהֲווֹ נְפִישִׁי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: They protested **due to the children, as they had many** children who were not entitled to a portion of the land.

The Gemara answers: *mishum tfelim* — because of *tfelim*, a term the *Rashbam* explains as referring to those under the age of twenty. The situation envisioned is that *Bnei Yosef* had a very large population of young people under twenty whose parents had died in the *midbar* and who therefore had no parent among the entering generation through whom to inherit. Since everyone over twenty would bequeath their portion to their own children, these orphaned young people under twenty had no one to bequeath to them. The complaint was that a substantial population segment would receive nothing proportional to their numbers. One may note that this would need to represent a significant demographic crisis to motivate the formal complaint, though this is the Gemara's resolution.

### Equal Tribal Lands

A different, and in some ways simpler, resolution is available: if one assumes that each tribe received an equal-sized territorial allotment regardless of its population — that is, that tribal lands were uniform in size rather than proportional to headcount — then *Bnei Yosef*'s complaint needs no special explanation and is independent of whether the division followed *yotzei mitzrayim* or *ba'ei ha'aretz*. They would simply be saying: our tribal land is the same size as every other tribe's land, but our population is far greater, and therefore each individual in our tribe receives far less than individuals in smaller tribes. This approach requires no reference to *tfelim* or demographic asymmetries.

However, this resolution is in tension with the historical record. The map of *Sefer Yehoshua* makes clear that tribal territories were not uniform — *Menashe* alone received a vast area spanning both sides of the Jordan, representing a substantial portion of the entire land. The Gemara at this point assumes equal individual portions (calibrated per person), while later the Gemara will shift to assuming equal tribal allotments, which will seem puzzling in light of the evident historical disproportion.

## Abaye's Inference

### Nobody Excluded Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, לָא הֲוָה חַד דְּלָא שָׁקֵיל; דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ הֲוָה חַד דְּלָא שָׁקֵיל, אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְמִצְוַוח! וְכִי תֵּימָא: דִּצְוַוח וְאַהֲנִי – כַּתְבֵיהּ קְרָא, דִּצְוַוח וְלָא אַהֲנִי – לָא כַּתְבֵיהּ קְרָא; הָא בְּנֵי יוֹסֵף – דְּצָוְוחִי וְלָא אַהֲנִי, וְכַתְבִינְהוּ קְרָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Learn from** the fact that the Bible records the complaints of only the daughters of Zelophehad and the descendants of Joseph that **there was not one** other individual **who did not take** a portion of land; **as if it enters your mind** that **there was** even **one** other **who did not take** a portion of land, **he should have cried out** in protest. **And if you would say: The verse wrote** about one **who cried out and** his protest **was effective,** and **the verse did not write** about one **who cried out and** his protest **was not effective,** that is difficult. **But** there is the counterexample of **the descendants of Joseph, who cried out and** their protest **was not effective, and the verse wrote** about **them.**

Abaye now draws a broader inference from the narratives of *Bnot Tzelofchad* and *Bnei Yosef*: *shema mina lo havi chad dlo shakil* — we can learn from these stories that there was not a single person who failed to receive a portion in *Eretz Yisrael*.

One might imagine circumstances under which someone would be left without any share. Under *yotzei mitzrayim*, a child born in the *midbar* to a father who had left Egypt under the age of twenty would receive nothing — neither by virtue of entering the land himself nor by virtue of *yotzei mitzrayim*. Under *ba'ei ha'aretz*, a young person under twenty who entered the land without a living parent and without any surviving relative through whom to inherit in the manner of *Bnot Tzelofchad* could similarly find himself landless. Yet Abaye asserts this never occurred.

His reasoning: *d'ai salka daitach hava chad dlo shakil, ibaye leh limzvach* — if there had been even one person excluded from the land, he would surely have complained. *Bnot Tzelofchad* complained; *Bnei Yosef* complained. The cultural pattern of formal complaint was clearly operative and recorded. It follows that anyone who lacked a portion would have stepped forward. And since no additional complaints are recorded, no additional person was excluded.

### Bnei Yosef Counterpoint

The Gemara challenges this inference. Perhaps some people did complain, and their complaint simply went unheeded — and the Torah does not record every complaint that failed. The implied principle the Gemara tests is: *d'tzvach v'hani, kasvi kra; d'tzvach v'lo hani, lo kasvi kra* — that which was complained and succeeded was recorded; that which was complained and failed was not recorded. If so, Abaye's argument collapses, since we cannot infer from silence that no one complained.

Abaye responds by pointing to *Bnei Yosef* themselves: their complaint was recorded even though it ultimately did not succeed. The straightforward reading of *Sefer Yehoshua* seems to suggest their complaint did succeed — Yehoshua told them to go conquer the forests — but the Gemara cannot accept this *k'pshuto*. If the forests were already within *Bnei Yosef*'s tribal allotment, Yehoshua was telling them nothing new. If the forests belonged to another tribe, Yehoshua had no authority to reassign them. Therefore, the Gemara understands the complaint as having not actually been granted. Yet the Torah recorded it. This demonstrates that the Torah does record complaints that led nowhere, and therefore Abaye's argument is reinstated: since no other complaints are recorded, no one else was excluded.

### Ayin Hara Lesson

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם עֵצָה טוֹבָה קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּאִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְאִינִישׁ לְאִיזְדְּהוֹרֵי מֵעֵינָא בִּישָׁא. וְהַיְינוּ דְּקָאָמַר לְהוּ יְהוֹשֻׁעַ – דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֲלֵיהֶם יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, אִם עַם רַב אַתָּה, עֲלֵה לְךָ הַיַּעְרָה״. אֲמַר לְהוּ: לְכוּ וְהַחְבִּאוּ עַצְמְכֶם בִּיעָרִים, שֶׁלֹּא תִּשְׁלוֹט בָּכֶם עַיִן רַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects Abaye's inference: Generally, the verse would not record an instance where one cried out if his protest was not effective, and **there,** the verse includes the protest of Joseph's descendants in order to **teach us** a measure of **good advice: That a person should be wary of the evil eye. And this is what Joshua said to them, as it is written: "And Joshua said unto them: If you be a great people, go up to the forest,** and cut down for yourself there in the land of the Perizzites and of the Rephaim" (Joshua 17:15). Joshua **said to them: Go and conceal yourselves in the forests so that the evil eye will not have dominion over you.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אֲנַן מִזַּרְעָא דְּיוֹסֵף, דְּלָא שָׁלְטָא בֵּיהּ עֵינָא בִּישָׁא – דִּכְתִיב: ״בֵּן פֹּרָת יוֹסֵף, בֵּן פֹּרָת עֲלֵי עָיִן״, וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אַל תִּקְרֵי ״עֲלֵי עָיִן״, אֶלָּא ״עוֹלֵי עָיִן״.
>
> **Translation:** Joseph's descendants **said to him: We are of the descendants of Joseph,** upon **whom the evil eye had no dominion, as it is written: "Joseph is a fruitful vine, a fruitful vine by a fountain [*alei ayin*]"** (Genesis 49:22), **and Rabbi Abbahu states** a homiletic interpretation: **Do not read** it as **"*alei ayin*," rather** read it as ***olei ayin*,** above the eye, i.e., he transcended the influence of the evil eye. Joseph's descendants were saying that they also do not need to be wary.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא אֲמַר מֵהָכָא: ״וְיִדְגּוּ לָרוֹב בְּקֶרֶב הָאָרֶץ״; מָה דָּגִים שֶׁבַּיָּם – מַיִם מְכַסִּים עֲלֵיהֶם וְאֵין הָעַיִן שׁוֹלֶטֶת בָּהֶם, אַף זַרְעוֹ שֶׁל יוֹסֵף – אֵין הָעַיִן שׁוֹלֶטֶת בָּהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said** that a proof for the notion that the evil eye holds no sway over Joseph and his descendants, is **from here,** Jacob's blessing of Joseph's sons, Ephraim and Manasseh: "The angel who has redeemed me from all evil, bless the lads; and let my name be named in them, and the name of my fathers, Abraham and Isaac; **and let them grow into a multitude [*veyidgu*] in the midst of the earth"** (Genesis 48:16). *Veyidgu* is related etymologically to the word for fish [*dag*]. **Just as** with regard to **the fish in the sea, water covers them and the** evil **eye has no dominion over them, so too, the seed of Joseph, the** evil **eye has no dominion over them.**

The Gemara thus establishes that the *Bnei Yosef* narrative was preserved for this moral lesson about *ayin hara*, not as evidence of a systematic pattern of recording all complaints. Once the narrative's purpose is reassigned, Abaye's inference from it — that all excluded persons would have been recorded — is undermined.

## Excluded Groups

### Meraglim & Spies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְרַגְּלִים – יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְכָלֵב נָטְלוּ חֶלְקָם. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר עוּלָּא, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וִיהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן וְכָלֵב בֶּן יְפֻנֶּה חָיוּ מִן הָאֲנָשִׁים הָהֵם״ – מַאי ״חָיוּ״? אִילֵּימָא חָיוּ מַמָּשׁ, וְהָא כְּתִיב קְרָא אַחֲרִינָא: ״וְלֹא נוֹתַר מֵהֶם אִישׁ, כִּי אִם כָּלֵב בֶּן יְפֻנֶּה וִיהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן״! אֶלָּא מַאי ״חָיוּ״ – שֶׁחָיוּ בְּחֶלְקָם.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara analyzes the next section of the *baraita*, which states: With regard to the twelve **spies** sent to survey Eretz Yisrael prior to the Jewish people's entry into the land, **Joshua and Caleb took** all of the spies' **portions** of the land. The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived? **Ulla said:** It is **as the verse states: "But Joshua, son of Nun, and Caleb, son of Jephunneh, lived of those men** that went to spy out the land" (Numbers 14:38). **What** does the term **"lived"** mean? **If we say** that it means **literally** that **they lived, but** there is **another verse** that **states: "And there was not left a man of them, save Caleb, son of Jephunneh, and Joshua, son of Nun"** (Numbers 26:65), so why would the Torah state it twice? **Rather, what** does the term **"lived"** mean? **That** Joshua and Caleb **lived in** the other spies' **portion** of the land.

The Gemara now tests these approaches against textual evidence from *Tanakh*, picking up several lines from the top of 118. The Gemara states: *b'shlama le-man d'amar le-yotzei mitzrayim* — it works out well according to the view that the land was divided among those who left Egypt, *haynu d'katzivchan B'nei Yosef*, that is why we understand why *Bnei Yosef* came and complained to Yehoshua. The verse states *vayidabru B'nei Yosef* — the sons of Yosef spoke to Yehoshua, declaring that they were a numerous people and their allotted territory was insufficient.

### Chayu Derasha

The Gemara's reasoning is this: if the division was based on *yotzei mitzrayim*, the allotments were fixed according to the population that left Egypt. The tribe of *Menashe* and *Ephraim* may have been small at the time of the Exodus but grew disproportionately large by the time they entered the land. A significant skewing could therefore arise between the size of their allotted territory — fixed by the earlier, smaller headcount — and their actual population at the time of settlement. Their complaint to Yehoshua thus makes perfect sense.

### Mitlonenim Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִתְלוֹנְנִין וַעֲדַת קֹרַח – לֹא הָיָה לָהֶן חֵלֶק בָּאָרֶץ. וְהָתַנְיָא: מְרַגְּלִים, מִתְלוֹנְנִים וַעֲדַת קֹרַח – יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְכָלֵב נָטְלוּ חֶלְקָם! לָא קַשְׁיָא; מָר מַקֵּישׁ מִתְלוֹנְנִים לִמְרַגְּלִים, מָר לָא מַקֵּישׁ מִתְלוֹנְנִים לִמְרַגְּלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches that the **protesters and the assembly of Korah did not possess a portion of Eretz** Yisrael. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** otherwise in a *baraita*: With regard to the **spies,** the **protesters, and the assembly of Korah, Joshua and Caleb took their portions** of the land? Apparently, the protesters and the assembly of Korah were assigned portions in Eretz Yisrael, which were then given to Joshua and Caleb. The Gemara answers: It is **not difficult:** One **Sage,** the *tanna* of the *baraita* quoted earlier, **juxtaposes** the **protesters to the spies,** teaching that just as the spies were assigned a portion of Eretz Yisrael, so were the protesters. And one **Sage,** the *tanna* of the *baraita* quoted here, **does not juxtapose** the **protesters to the spies.** Although the spies were assigned a portion, the protesters were not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר מַקֵּישׁ מִתְלוֹנְנִים לִמְרַגְּלִים, וּמָר לָא מַקֵּישׁ מִתְלוֹנְנִים לִמְרַגְּלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: One **Sage juxtaposes** the **protesters to the spies,** so that Joshua and Caleb inherited the portions of both; **and** one **Sage does not juxtapose** the **protesters to the spies.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: וּלְמַאן דְּמַקֵּישׁ מִתְלוֹנְנִים לִמְרַגְּלִים, אִיכְּפוּל יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְכָלֵב וִירִתוּ לְכוּלַּהּ אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִתְלוֹנְנִים שֶׁבַּעֲדַת קֹרַח קָאָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents Rav Pappa's fourth question. **And Rav Pappa said to Abaye: And according to the one who juxtaposes** the **protesters to the spies,** is it reasonable that **Joshua and Caleb contested** the spies **and inherited all of Eretz Yisrael?** Many of the Jewish people protested in the wilderness at one point or another, and it cannot be that Joshua and Caleb received all of their portions by virtue of not participating in the sin of the spies. Abaye **said to him: We are referring to** the **protesters who were among the assembly of Korah.** This term is not referring to all those who protested, but rather to the 250 individuals who protested along with Korah, and it is their portions of land that Joshua and Caleb received.

A further question arises: if the *mitlonenim* refers to all who ever complained against Moshe, then Yehoshua and Calev would have inherited an absurdly large share of the land, encompassing virtually all of *Klal Yisrael* at one point or another. The Gemara clarifies: *mitlonenim she-ba-adat Korach kamina* — the *mitlonenim* in question are specifically the complainers within *adat Korach*, not every instance of *vayilonu*.

### Bnot Tzelofchad Arg

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״אָבִינוּ מֵת בַּמִּדְבָּר״ – זֶה צְלָפְחָד. ״וְהוּא לֹא הָיָה בְּתוֹךְ הָעֵדָה״ – זֶה עֲדַת מְרַגְּלִים. ״הַנּוֹעָדִים עַל ה׳״ – אֵלּוּ מִתְלוֹנְנִים. ״בַּעֲדַת קֹרַח״ – כְּמַשְׁמָעוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara quotes a related *baraita*. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that the verse quotes the daughters of Zelophehad: "Our father died in the wilderness, and he was not among the assembly of them that gathered themselves together against the Lord in the assembly of Korah, but he died in his own sin; and he had no sons" (Numbers 27:3), and the daughters of Zelophehad therefore claim they are entitled to his portion. **"Our father died in the wilderness," this** is referring to **Zelophehad. "And he was not among the assembly," this** is referring to the **assembly of spies. "That gathered themselves together against the Lord," these** are the **protesters. "In the assembly of Korah,"** this is **in accordance with its** straightforward **meaning.** It is clear from this verse that those in these categories were not entitled to a portion in Eretz Yisrael.

The source from which we know that these three groups — *meraglim*, *mitlonenim*, and *adat Korach* — forfeited their portions is the argument of *Bnot Tzelofchad* themselves. The *braita* cites their declaration: *avinu met ba-midbar* — our father died in the wilderness, *ze Tzelofchad*. He was not among *adat ha-meraglim* — as derived from the verse *ad matai la-eda ha-ra'ah ha-zot*, from which we learn the *meraglim* constituted a formal *edah*. He was not among *ha-noadim al Hashem b'adat Korach* — derived from the verse *ve-chol adatcha ha-noadim al Hashem*. And the *mitlonenim* are equated with that same group.

By arguing that their father belonged to none of these categories, *Bnot Tzelofchad* implicitly confirmed that membership in any of these groups would have disqualified him from an inheritance. Their argument thus serves as the *braita*'s proof that the *meraglim*, *mitlonenim*, and *adat Korach* did not receive portions. The *machloket* that follows concerns not whether they were disqualified but rather to whom their notional shares were assigned.

A brief digression: a famous responsum of Rav Moshe Feinstein observes that *adat Korach*, from which we derive the minimum *minyan* of ten, included individuals who were in effect *apikorsim* — they denied God's ability to bring them to *Eretz Yisrael*. Since they nonetheless count toward the quorum from which we derive the *minyan*, this supports the position that even a *rasha* may be counted.

## Menashe's Ten Portions

### Why Ten Portions?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְיוֹצְאֵי מִצְרַיִם נִתְחַלְּקָה הָאָרֶץ, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּפְּלוּ חַבְלֵי מְנַשֶּׁה עֲשָׂרָה״ – שִׁיתָּא דְּשִׁיתָּא בָּתֵּי אָבוֹת, וְאַרְבְּעָה דִּידְהוּ – הָא עֲשָׂרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the *baraita* and presents the fifth question. **And Rav Pappa said to Abaye: Granted, according to the one who says** that **Eretz** Yisrael **was divided among those who left Egypt, this is as it is written** with regard to the inheritance of the tribe of Manasseh: **"And ten parts fell to Manasseh,** beside the land of Gilead and Bashan, which is beyond the Jordan; because the daughters of Manasseh had an inheritance among his sons" (Joshua 17:5–6). **Six** were the portions **of the six fathers' houses** of Manasseh listed in a previous verse (Joshua 17:2), **and four** parts **of** the daughters of Zelophehad; **that** is **ten** parts.

Rav Papa poses a question to Abaye: *v'amar lei Rav Pappa l'Abaye*. Under *yotzei mitzrayim*, the verse in *Sefer Yehoshua* that says *vayiplu chevlei Menashe asarah* — the portions of *Menashe* fell to ten — makes sense. *Menashe* had six sons who served as the heads of the *batei avot* of the tribe. One of them was *Chefer*, and *Tzelofchad* was *Chefer*'s son. The verse mentions *Tzelofchad* and *Bnot Tzelofchad* and then says the total came to ten portions.

### Yotzei Mitzrayim Count

Under *yotzei mitzrayim*, the portions attributed to *Bnot Tzelofchad* can be enumerated as follows. *Tzelofchad* himself, as one who left Egypt, receives his own individual portion: that is the first. As *Chefer*'s *bechor*, *Tzelofchad* receives a double portion from *Chefer*'s share: that accounts for two more. And if one or more of *Chefer*'s other sons died without male heirs, their portions would revert upward to *Chefer* and be redistributed among his surviving sons, including *Tzelofchad*. With one dead uncle, one arrives at three portions for *Tzelofchad*'s line — which matches the *mishnah*'s count of three. Adding this to six for the *batei avot* yields nine; to reach ten, a second dead uncle is posited, a conclusion the Gemara supports from the superfluous language *achuzat nachalah*.

### Ba'ei Ha'aretz Count

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְבָאֵי הָאָרֶץ, תְּמָנְיָא הוּא דַּהֲווֹ – שִׁיתָּא דְּשִׁיתָּא בָּתֵּי אָבוֹת, וּתְרֵי דִּידְהוּ – הָא תְּמָנְיָא!
>
> **Translation:** **But according to the one who says** that Eretz Yisrael was divided **among** those **who entered Eretz** Yisrael, **there were eight** parts, as follows: **Six** were the portions **of the six fathers' houses, and two of theirs,** which they received from the estate of their grandfather Hepher, of whom Zelophehad was the firstborn; **that** is **eight.** Zelophehad himself, by contrast, was not entitled to a portion, as he did not enter Eretz Yisrael.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְיוֹצְאֵי מִצְרַיִם נִתְחַלְּקָה הָאָרֶץ, תִּשְׁעָה הֲווֹ! אֶלָּא מַאי אִית לָךְ לְמֵימַר – חַד אַחָא דְאַבָּא הֲוָה לְהוּ; הָכִי נָמֵי, תְּרֵי אַחֵי דְאַבָּא הֲוָה לְהוּ –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **And according to your reasoning, but** even **according to the one who says** that **Eretz** Yisrael **was divided among those who left Egypt, there are** only **nine** parts, as the mishna (116b) states that Zelophehad's daughters took three parts. **Rather, what have you to say?** How can the mishna be reconciled with the verse? One must say that according to the opinion that Eretz Yisrael was divided among those who left Egypt, **they had one** unmentioned **paternal uncle** who died without children, and Zelophehad's estate received a share of his portion. **So too,** according to the opinion that Eretz Yisrael was divided among those who entered Eretz Yisrael, it could be said that **they had two** unmentioned **paternal uncles,** so that they received two additional portions of land.

Under *ba'ei ha'aretz*, the picture changes significantly. Since the focus is on the generation that entered the land, *Tzelofchad* himself does not receive a personal portion — he was not among those who entered. All inheritance flows upward through *Chefer* and back down. *Tzelofchad*'s line thus receives only the double *bechor* portion from *Chefer* — two portions — plus whatever accrues from dead uncles. Six plus two yields only eight, leaving the verse's figure of ten unaccounted for without positing two dead uncles. The Gemara points out the asymmetry: *tamniya hu dahavu* — there would only be eight.

### Batei Avot & Bnot

The Gemara resolves the difficulty for *ba'ei ha'aretz* by the same device applied to *yotzei mitzrayim*: positing two dead brothers of *Tzelofchad*'s father rather than one. *Hacha nami trei achi d'abba hava lehu* — here too we say there were two brothers of the father who died, and their portions, redistributed through *Chefer* and back down to the surviving sons including *Tzelofchad*'s line, yield four portions for *Bnot Tzelofchad*, bringing the total to ten.

One must then ask: in what sense does the verse's figure of ten combine six major *batei avot* portions with four relatively minor *Bnot Tzelofchad* portions? These are portions of vastly different magnitude. The Gemara's answer, anticipated by Rav Papa's question, is addressed in the following segment.

## Naton Titein Braita

### Yotzei Mitzrayim Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״נָתֹן תִּתֵּן לָהֶם״ זוֹ נַחֲלַת אֲבִיהֶן. ״בְּתוֹךְ אֲחֵי אֲבִיהֶן״ – זוֹ נַחֲלַת אֲבִי אֲבִיהֶן. ״וְהַעֲבַרְתָּ אֶת נַחֲלַת אֲבִיהֶן לָהֶן״ – זוֹ חֵלֶק בְּכוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The fact that they received a portion from an uncle may be derived from a verse. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that the verse states: **"You shall give [*naton titten*] them** a possession of an inheritance" (Numbers 27:7). **This** is referring to the **inheritance of their father.** The verse continues: **"Among their father's brothers"; this** is referring to the **inheritance of their father's father.** The verse continues: **"And you shall pass the inheritance of their father to them"; this** is referring to the **portion of the firstborn** to which Zelophehad was entitled.

The Gemara cites a *braita* that articulates the *yotzei mitzrayim* position through close reading of the relevant verse. *Naton titein lahem nachalah* — give, give them an inheritance. *Titein lahem* — this refers to *nachalat avihen*, the direct inheritance of their own father *Tzelofchad*, who himself left Egypt and is entitled as a *yotzei mitzrayim*. *Betoch achei avihen* — in the midst of their father's brothers, meaning what they receive through *Chefer* in parallel with their uncles. *Zu nachalat avi avihen* — this is the portion of their father's father, that is, the portion flowing through *Chefer*. And *ve-ha'avarta nachalat avihen lahem* — the additional transfer of their father's inheritance — yields the *chelek bechorah*, the double portion, since *Tzelofchad* was a *bechor*.

### Rabbi Eliezer Addition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: אַף חֵלֶק אֲחִי אֲבִיהֶם נָטְלוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נָתֹן תִּתֵּן״. וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר תְּרֵי אַחֵי דְּאַבָּא הֲוָה לְהוּ, הַהוּא מֵ״אֲחֻזַּת נַחֲלָה״ נָפְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues. **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: They also took a portion** of **their father's brother, as it is stated: "You shall give [*naton titten*]."** The double expression indicates that they received an additional portion. The Gemara notes: **And according to the one who says** that **they had two paternal uncles, that** additional portion **is derived from** the phrase: "You shall give them **a possession of an inheritance,"** which would be superfluous were it not to indicate an additional portion.

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov adds a further element: *af chelek achei avihen natlu* — they also received the portion of their father's brothers, that is, the dead uncle or uncles whose shares were redistributed. This is derived from *naton titein* — the doubled language implying an additional grant beyond the straightforwardly inherited portions.

For the view that requires two dead brothers — which the Gemara needs to reach the figure of ten under *yotzei mitzrayim* — the additional exegetical basis comes from the phrase *achuzat nachalah*. The verse could have said either *nachalah* alone or *achuzah* alone; the combination signals yet another portion, providing the textual hook for a second dead brother's redistributed share.

### Rav Papa Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: קְרָא מַאי קָא חָשֵׁיב? אִי טְפָלִים קָא חָשֵׁיב, טוּבָא הֲווֹ! אִי בָּתֵּי אָבוֹת קָחָשֵׁיב, שִׁיתָּא הֲווֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents the sixth question. **And Rav Pappa said to Abaye:** In **the verse** quoted above, **what** portions **is it counting? If** the verse **is counting children,** i.e., the portions of those who inherited from their antecedents, as with the daughters of Zelophehad, **there were many** such portions, and the verse did not enumerate all portions inherited by all members of the tribe. And **if** the verse **is counting fathers' houses, there are** only **six,** as Hepher is included among the six enumerated in Joshua 17:2. Why, then, does the verse count the portions of the daughters of Zelophehad separately?

Rav Papa's question, now addressed directly, is: *kra mai kachashiv* — what category of portions does the verse enumerate when it says ten? If it is counting *tfelim*, the subdivided portions of later generations, there would be far more than ten by the time of *Bnot Tzelofchad*. If it is counting *batei avot*, the major tribal divisions, there are six.

### Batei Avot Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם בָּתֵּי אָבוֹת קָא חָשֵׁיב, וְהָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – דִּבְנוֹת צְלָפְחָד נָטְלוּ חֵלֶק בְּכוֹרָה; אַלְמָא אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל מוּחְזֶקֶת הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the verse **is counting fathers' houses. And** by also counting the inheritance of Zelophehad's daughters, the verse **teaches us this: That** the **daughters of Zelophehad took** the **portion of the firstborn** due their father. **Evidently, Eretz Yisrael is** considered already **in possession** of one who was entitled to his portion of the land, even before the land was assigned. Although a firstborn son does not take a double portion of the property due to his deceased father as he does the property his father possessed, it is considered that Hepher possessed the portion that would eventually be assigned to him, and that Zelophehad was entitled to a double portion.

The Gemara resolves: *le'olam batei avot kachashiv* — the verse is always counting the major *batei avot* divisions. *Ve-ha kamashma lan d'Bnot Tzelofchad natlu chelek bechorah* — the reason the verse reaches ten, incorporating *Bnot Tzelofchad*'s portions alongside the six *batei avot*, is to teach that *Bnot Tzelofchad* received the *bechor* portion. The numerical exercise is thus an exegetical device to highlight the legal significance of their inheritance, and as noted, it simultaneously elevates their claim to a prominence comparable to the foundational tribal divisions themselves.

## Muchzeket Principle

### Bechor Paradox

A fundamental difficulty now surfaces. The Gemara has established that *Tzelofchad* received a *chelek bechorah* from *Chefer*. But the rule governing a *bechor*'s double portion — derived from *Devarim* — is that it applies only to property that was in the father's actual possession at the time of his death: *muchzak*, held and possessed. Property to which the father was merely *ra'uy* — entitled but not yet in hand — does not carry the double portion. *Eretz Yisrael* was never actually in *Chefer*'s possession; it was allocated to him only retroactively and conceptually. How, then, can *Tzelofchad* claim a *chelek bechorah*?

### Muchzeket Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל מוּחְזֶקֶת הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Evidently, Eretz Yisrael is** considered already **in possession** of one who was entitled to his portion of the land, even before the land was assigned.

The Gemara's answer is striking: *alma Eretz Yisrael muchzeket hi* — we see from this that *Eretz Yisrael* is regarded as *muchzeket*, as fully and actually possessed, not merely *ra'uy*. The entire enterprise of the allocation of *Eretz Yisrael* — passing portions up to the *yotzei mitzrayim* and back down — is treated not as a fictional entitlement but as genuine possession. The land of Israel carries a *chazakah* of ownership rooted in the divine promise and the ancestral inheritance, such that even retroactive conceptual assignment suffices to constitute *muchzak* for purposes of the *bechor*'s double portion.

## Children's Inheritance

### Braita Both Sides

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: וְהַבָּנִים נָטְלוּ בִּזְכוּת אֲבִי אֲבִיהֶם, וּבִזְכוּת אֲבִי אִמּוֹתֵיהֶן. וְהָתַנְיָא: בִּזְכוּת עַצְמָן! לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר לְיוֹצְאֵי מִצְרַיִם, הָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר לְבָאֵי הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues its discussion of the *baraita*. **The Master says: And the sons** of the spies and of the protesters **took** portions of the land **in the merit of their paternal grandfathers and in the merit of their maternal grandfathers** if those grandfathers were among those who left Egypt. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 7:10): The sons of the spies and of the protesters took portions **in their own merit?** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult: This** *baraita*, which states they took portions in the merit of their grandfathers, is written **in accordance with** the opinion of the **one who says** that Eretz Yisrael was divided **among those who left Egypt;** and **that** *baraita*, which states that they took portions in their own merit, is written **in accordance with** the opinion of the **one who says** that Eretz Yisrael was divided **among those who entered Eretz** Yisrael.

A final *braita* addresses the children of those groups who forfeited their portions — the *meraglim*, *mitlonenim*, and *adat Korach*. The *braita* states: *ve-habanim natlu bizchut avi avihem u-vizchut avi imoteihem* — the children inherited through the merit of their paternal grandfathers and through the merit of their maternal grandfathers. The scenario is that the father, belonging to one of the disqualified groups, did not himself receive a portion, and yet his children were not left landless — they could receive through a grandfather on either the paternal or maternal side who was *yotzei mitzrayim* or *ba'ei ha'aretz* and thus did hold a valid portion.

### Contradiction Resolved

Against this, a second *braita* states that the children received *bizchut atzman* — by virtue of themselves. The apparent contradiction between inheriting through grandparents and receiving by one's own merit is resolved simply: *lo kashya, ha k'man d'amar l'yotzei mitzrayim, ha k'man d'amar l'ba'ei ha'aretz*. Under *yotzei mitzrayim*, a child of a disqualified father has no personal claim to the land and must rely on grandparents through whom to inherit; under *ba'ei ha'aretz*, the child who himself entered the land acquires a portion in his own right.

### Age Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָא וְהָא לְבָאֵי הָאָרֶץ, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא דַּהֲוָה בֶּן עֶשְׂרִים. הָא דְּלָא הֲוָה בֶּן עֶשְׂרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another answer. **And if you wish, say** instead: **This** *baraita* **and that** *baraita* are written in accordance with the opinion that asserts that Eretz Yisrael was divided **among those who entered Eretz** Yisrael, **and** it is **not difficult: This** *baraita* is discussing one **who was twenty** years **old** when the Jewish people entered Eretz Yisrael, and therefore he took a portion in his own merit, and **that** *baraita* is discussing one **who was not twenty** years **old.**

The Gemara offers an alternative resolution that keeps both *braitot* within the *ba'ei ha'aretz* framework: *ha d'havu ben esrim* — for those who were twenty years of age and entered the land, they received *bizchut atzman*, by their own standing as *ba'ei ha'aretz*; *ha d'lo havu ben esrim* — for those who were under twenty at the time of entry, they did not qualify on their own and therefore received only through their grandparents. On this reading, both *braitot* assume *ba'ei ha'aretz*, and the operative distinction is simply one of age at the time of entry into the land.
