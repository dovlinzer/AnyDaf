# Berakhot 39 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Cooked Vegetables

### Cooked Vegs & Bracha

The daf begins on 38b, and the discussion that carries us into this amud concerns whether cooked vegetables retain a *borei pri ha'adama* or are downgraded. Rav Yosef bar Shmuel raises an objection to the position that a cooked vegetable remains, in essence, the same object it was when raw.

### Maror Exception

His proof comes from a well-known halachic context: the vegetables with which one fulfills the *maror* obligation on Pesach. The Gemara teaches that one may fulfill the obligation through the vegetable itself or through its stalk — *u've-kelach she-lahen* — but not if they are pickled (*kevushim*), double-boiled (*shelukin*), or simply cooked (*mevushalim*). The implication seems devastating: if cooking a vegetable left it essentially unchanged, why should a cooked *maror* vegetable be disqualified?

### Identity & Taste

The Gemara deflects this challenge by drawing a distinction: *shani hatam de-va'inan ta'am maror ve-leika* — the case of *maror* is different, because we require specifically the taste of *maror*, and that taste is absent once the vegetable is cooked. The questioner might press further: how can we consider the object unchanged if its taste has changed entirely? The answer is that a change in taste does not alter the object's identity. If one were to add cinnamon to the vegetable, one would also not fulfill the *maror* obligation — yet the vegetable is still manifestly the same vegetable. The identity of an object, at least for purposes of *brachot*, is determined by its appearance and structure, not its taste. A change in taste is insufficient to declare it a different thing.

### Tosafot on Cooking

With that, the discussion of cooked vegetables reaches its *maskana*. Tosafot frames the analysis clearly: if a vegetable is essentially inedible raw, one makes *shehakol* on it raw and *borei pri ha'adama* once it is cooked and edible. The harder question is the reverse — what is the ruling when the vegetable is perfectly edible raw, and indeed perhaps ideal in its raw state, but one has chosen to cook it? Tosafot is inclined to say that only if the cooked form is equivalent to or better than the raw form does it retain *borei pri ha'adama*. If cooking actually makes it less desirable in the eyes of most people, the cooked form would receive only *shehakol*. However, Tosafot also cites the Rif, who holds that *borei pri ha'adama* is retained unless the vegetable has undergone a genuine transformation of form.

### Examples & Standards

In practice, most vegetables that retain their basic shape when cooked — carrots or tomatoes, for example — would still receive *borei pri ha'adama* even if many people prefer them raw. It is only when form is substantially lost that further considerations come into play. The case of garlic is worth noting, since the Gemara itself raised it as an example. Tosafot acknowledges the complication: most people eat garlic cooked rather than raw, but the reason they eat cooked garlic is not primarily to consume the garlic itself — it is to enjoy whatever it has flavored. Tosafot's observation is that eating something together with other things for the sake of those other things is not quite the same as eating that ingredient on its own terms.

## Olive-Pit & Kezayit

### Zeira & Olive

The Gemara now returns to an incident involving Rav Zeira. Rav Yirmiya asks him: how could he have recited a *bracha acharona* after eating a salted olive? The difficulty is that once the pit has been removed — *keivan de-shaklei le-gar'inei* — one no longer has a full olive's worth of food. Without a *ke-zayit*, how could he have obligated himself in a final blessing?

### No Shiur Before

Tosafot makes a critical clarificatory point at the outset of his comments: *batlei shiura* — the concern about measure — applies *davka* to the *bracha she-acharav*, the blessing recited afterward. For the *bracha she-lefanav*, the blessing before eating, there is no minimum measure whatsoever. The prohibition against deriving benefit from this world without a *bracha* applies to any amount of food or drink, however small. One who tastes food while cooking presents a somewhat distinct question — whether *tei'ima* constitutes *achila* — and many authorities hold that placing a small amount on the tip of one's tongue for tasting purposes may not require a *bracha* at all. But for the blessing before eating generally, no *shiur* is required.

### Kezayit Definition

Returning to Rav Zeira's case, the Gemara offers a resolution. Rav Zeira says to Rav Yirmiya: *mi savrat kezayit gadol beinan* — what makes you think we need a large olive? We need only an average-sized olive — *kezayit beinoni beinan*. The olive brought to Rabbi Yochanan, the Gemara notes, was a large one. So large, in fact, that even after the pit was removed, the remaining flesh still amounted to a medium-sized *ke-zayit*. A large olive minus its pit equals a standard olive. The source for this is a *mishna*: when the Sages speak of a *zayit* as a measure, they mean *lo katan ve-lo gadol* — neither a small olive nor a large one — *ela beinoni*, only a medium one.

### Water Displacement

The question of how the *ke-zayit* is measured is also addressed. The removal of the pit in this discussion implies that the measure is one of volume, not weight — and the volume in question excludes hollow space. The tannaitic sources that discuss measurement in this context speak explicitly of water displacement, making clear both that the Sages were aware of this method of measuring volume and that volume, not mass, is the operative standard. This principle underlies all the subsequent discussions about how much bread or *matzah* one must consume — and the debates about whether one must compress fluffy bread or *matzah* to determine its true volume.

### Aguri Olive Type

The Gemara identifies the olive that serves as the standard measure: *ve-zehu aguri* — this is the *aguri* olive. Rabbi Abbahu qualifies this: the name *aguri* is not actually its proper name; its true name is *avruti*, and some say *samrusi*. The reason it is called *aguri* is *she-shamno agur be-tokho* — its oil is, as it were, gathered within it, making it a particularly full and juicy variety. This specific species, then, is what the Sages had in mind when they established the average olive as the standard measure.

### Historical Olives

There is a striking and practically significant dimension to all of this. Natan Slifkin has written on the historical variability of olive sizes across generations and regions, noting that the *rishonim* of medieval Europe were working with Mediterranean olives that may have differed considerably from those available in the Talmudic period. The result is that the *shiur* of a *ke-zayit* as understood and practiced in later generations became, in certain contexts, disproportionately large relative to what the Gemara itself describes. The Gemara's own formulation here — that a *ke-zayit* should simply be an olive's worth — makes it all the more striking that the measure became unmoored from that concrete referent.

## Bar Kapra Story

### Setup & Foods

The Gemara now transitions to a narrative that will serve as the vehicle for working out the rules of *bracha* priority. *D'hanhu terei talmidei d'havu yatvei kamei de-Bar Kappara* — two students were seated in the presence of Bar Kappara. They were brought *kruv* — cabbage, presumably cooked — *ve-durmaskin* — a type of food that Rashi identifies as prunes, though Rashi himself is more comfortable reading it as a variety of cabbage given the *bracha* context, since the Gemara will treat its blessing as *adama* — and *pargyot*, young poultry, perhaps pigeon. Bar Kappara gave one of the students permission to make the *bracha*.

### First Mistake

The student immediately — *kafatz u'veirach al ha-pargyot* — jumped forward and made the *bracha* on the poultry. The use of *kafatz* here is significant. Earlier in the tractate, the same idiom appeared in the context of Rabbi Akiva making a *bracha* before Rabban Gamliel could intervene, with a certain ambiguity about whether the alacrity was excusable or not. Here, as a literary expression, *kafatz u'veirach* signals that something has gone wrong — the student acted without pausing to reflect.

### Second's Mock

The other student mocked him — *ligleig alav chaveiro*. His implicit point was that the *bracha* should have been made first on the vegetable, which carries a more specific *bracha*, before the poultry, which receives only *shehakol*.

### Anger & Rebuke

Bar Kappara became angry — *ikpad*. Initially he said: *lo al ha-mevareich ani ko'eis* — I am not angry at the one who made the *bracha*; *ela al ha-melagleig ani ko'eis* — I am angry at the one who mocked him. The mocker's condescension was misplaced: *im chavaracha domeh ke-mi she-lo ta'am ta'am basar me'olam* — if your friend looks like someone who has never tasted meat in his life, who was desperately eager for the poultry, *atah lamah liglagta* — why did you feel the need to mock him? Whatever the first student did may not have been ideal, but what gave the second student grounds for ridicule?

Then Bar Kappara reconsidered. *Chazar ve-amar*: now he reversed himself. *Lo al ha-melagleig ani ko'eis* — I am not, in the end, primarily angry at the mocker — *ela al ha-mevareich ani ko'eis* — I am angry at the one who made the *bracha*. Why? *Im chochma ein kan, ziknah ein kan* — even if you have no wisdom yourself, are there no elders in the room, no sages you could have consulted? You could simply have asked me. The student who acted impulsively, without pausing to seek guidance, bears the greater responsibility.

This brings us to the underlying halachic principle: when multiple foods are before a person, one makes the *bracha* with the more specific formulation before the one with the more general formulation. *Ha'eitz* precedes *adama*, *adama* precedes *shehakol*, and so on. The student who made the *bracha* on the poultry — *shehakol* — before the cooked vegetables — potentially *adama* — inverted this order. The parallel with the *kafatz u'veirach* of Rabbi Akiva is exact in language but distinct in meaning: there the phrase conveyed urgency without necessarily negative judgment, whereas here the idiom anticipates the rebuke that follows.

### Deaths & Aftermath

The postscript is difficult. *Tana u'shneihem lo hotziu shenatam* — it is taught in a *braisa* that neither of the two students lived out the year. The word *tana* suggests this is a separate source appended to the narrative — a later postscript from a different tradition, not part of the original story — and the Gemara leaves the reader to draw its own conclusions from the juxtaposition of Bar Kappara's displeasure and the students' deaths.

The deaths are disturbing from any modern vantage point. The *Maharsha* attempts to contextualize the severity by connecting it to the principle found elsewhere in the Gemara that one who renders a halachic ruling in the presence of his teacher is *chayav misah*. In a certain sense, both students were guilty of *moreh halacha bifnei rabbo*: one through the *bracha* he made, the other through the implicit halachic judgment embedded in his mockery. The first erred by acting; the second erred by presuming to correct — when the correction, if any, belonged to Bar Kappara alone.

There is a broader tension in the Gemara's approach to such narratives. The Gemara elsewhere urges that when one experiences suffering or misfortune, one should engage in self-examination — *cheshbon ha-nefesh*. Yet that same Gemara warns that telling another person their suffering is the result of their sins violates the prohibition of *ona'at devarim*, afflicting someone with words. The Gemara here does not explicitly attribute the deaths to Bar Kappara's rebuke; it places the facts alongside each other and allows the reader to draw conclusions. That subtlety is itself meaningful.

## Blessing Priority

### Desired First

The Gemara now attempts to reconstruct a formal halachic debate from the behavior of the two students. *Mai lav be-ha ka mipalgi* — perhaps this is the dispute between them. The student who made the *bracha* — *de-mevarech savar* — may have held that both the cooked vegetables and the poultry receive *shehakol*, and therefore when two foods carry the same *bracha*, one should prioritize the one that is more *chaviv*, more desired. Choosing to bless the poultry first would then be entirely defensible: he preferred it.

### Satiates First

The mocker — *de-melagleig savar* — may have held that cooked vegetables retain *borei pri ha'adama*, while the poultry receives *shehakol*, and therefore the more specific *bracha* should come first. *Ve-hilkach perat adif* — the more particular blessing takes precedence regardless of which food one desires more.

The Gemara, however, rejects this as the framing of their dispute. *De-kulei alma shelakos u-pargyot shehakol nihiya bi-dvaro* — one could say that all would agree both foods here received *shehakol*; the dispute is elsewhere. *Hacha be-hai sevara ka mipalgi*: the real debate is what to do when two foods carry equal *brachot*. One position holds *chaviv adif* — the more desired food takes priority. The other holds *kruv adif* — the cabbage is preferable, *di-zayin*, because it satiates more. When foods of equal *bracha* status are present, one should bless first the food that is more of a staple, not the food that is more of a delicacy.

### Specificity & Desire

There is something worth pausing over in this final formulation. The entire structure of *bracha* priority is oriented toward acknowledging the significance of what one eats. The principle that a more specific *bracha* precedes a more general one is one dimension of that. But this further rule — that between equals, the more satiating food takes precedence over the more desired one — carries a quiet message about the relationship between appetite and the act of blessing. The institution of *brachot* is meant to sanctify eating, not merely to ratify one's desires. Prioritizing the staple over the delicacy, the filling over the craved, reflects something of that orientation.

Tosafot raises an additional structural point in this context. When Bar Kappara gave one student permission to make the *bracha* on behalf of the others — *nasan Bar Kappara reshut le-echad me-hen le-vareich* — this raises the question of how one person can make a *bracha* for others in *birchat ha-nehenin*, blessings of pleasure, which are by nature intensely personal. Tosafot explains that this was possible because they were engaged in *heseiba* — a culturally significant form of communal reclining that constituted a meaningful shared meal experience. When people eat together in that mode, they can be considered a single unit, and one representative can bless on behalf of all.

In the absence of *heseiba* as a living institution, each person must make their own *bracha* in *birchat ha-nehenin*. This stands in contrast to *mitzvot*, where one person can generally discharge another's obligation. The difference is conceptual: a *bracha* of pleasure is rooted in the individual's personal *hana'ah*, the pleasure one is personally experiencing, and that is not readily transferable. A *mitzvah* has more of a participatory, collective dimension. This distinction governs many practical cases — including the question of a *mesader kiddushin* who recites *borei pri ha-gafen* under the *chuppah* and then passes the cup to the *chatan* and *kallah* without himself drinking. Tosafot holds that while *be-di'avad* one may be *motzi* another in *birchat ha-nehenin* provided one is also personally benefiting, *le-chatchila* each person should make their own blessing.

## Processing Vegetables

### Huna & Turnips

Rav Zeira reports: when I was in the house of Rav Huna, he taught us regarding the heads of turnips — *hanei gargelidi de-lifta*. If you cut them into large chunks — *parmuneihu parma rabba* — you recite *borei pri ha'adama*. If you mince them into very small pieces — *parma zuta* — you say *shehakol nihiya bi-dvaro*. The reasoning is that finely minced turnip no longer resembles a turnip; it has undergone a change of form — *nishtanah* — and its appearance has been substantially altered.

### Yehudah Revises

Rav Zeira continues: when we later came to Rav Yehudah, he told us — *idi ve-idi borei pri ha'adama* — you make *borei pri ha'adama* on both large and small pieces. However, that applies specifically when the vegetables have been minced sufficiently — *de-parminu tfei* — so that it *namtik ta'amei*, makes them sweeter and improves them. Even if they no longer look exactly like a turnip, if the change in form is a change for the better — *nishtanah le-meilyusa* — the blessing is not downgraded. This returns us to the governing principle from earlier in the tractate: a changed form that is equivalent or superior retains *borei pri ha'adama*; a changed form that represents a deterioration produces *shehakol*.

### Flour in Stew

Rav Ashi reports a teaching from Rav Kahana's house concerning a vegetable stew. If you have a beet stew — *tavshila de-silka* — into which you have not added much flour, you say *borei pri ha'adama*. But if you have a turnip stew — *de-lifta* — where the practice is to add a great deal of flour, you say *borei minei mezonot*, since the flour has become the *ikkar*, the dominant ingredient.

### Lidvukei Principle

Rav Kahana then reversed himself: *idi ve-idi borei pri ha'adama* — in fact, both receive *borei pri ha'adama*. The reason so much flour is added to the turnip stew is not to make flour the central ingredient — it is only *le-devukei be-alma*, to serve as a thickening or binding agent, to give the soup some body. The flour is entirely secondary. This gives us the important principle of *lidvukei*: when a grain product is introduced into a dish not as food in its own right but purely as a functional agent to thicken, bind, or coat, it does not transform the blessing of the dish into *mezonot*.

The parameters of *lidvukei* are not always easy to determine in practice. Tosafot here and elsewhere — often citing the *Maharam mi-Rothenburg* — tends to take a conservative approach: when the grain product is present *le-si'od*, to provide substance and fill, it is the *ikkar* and determines the *bracha*; when it serves a secondary culinary function — adding crunch, providing a coating, giving texture — it does not. In borderline cases, such as a dish with a substantial crumb topping, the question of which function the grain is primarily serving can be genuinely difficult to resolve. The *Maharam mi-Rothenburg*'s recurring advice in such situations is to eat the food in the context of a bread meal, thereby sidestepping the question entirely — an approach that one of the *Tosafot* here attributes to him explicitly.

## Vegetable Waters

### Papa & Waters

Rav Papa states: *peshita li* — it is obvious to me that *maya de-silka ke-silka* — water in which beets have been cooked is like the beet itself, and receives *borei pri ha'adama*. Similarly, *maya de-lifta ke-lifta* — turnip water is like the turnip, and also receives *borei pri ha'adama*.

### Tosafot's Problem

Tosafot immediately identifies the difficulty here. If the vegetable water contains nothing but water and the taste of the vegetable — *af al pi she-ein bo ela mayim ve-ta'am ha-yerek* — how does it merit *borei pri ha'adama*? We have already established earlier in the tractate that fruit juice — the liquid pressed from fruit — receives only *shehakol*, on the grounds that the juice is a secondary exudate of the fruit, *zei'a be-alma*. If even direct fruit juice falls to *shehakol*, why should water that has merely absorbed the taste of a vegetable retain *borei pri ha'adama*?

Tosafot acknowledges there is a distinction to be drawn but does not fully resolve it. One possible reading is that in Talmudic times drinking fruit juice was considered sufficiently unusual that it was treated as an unnatural use of the fruit — not because it lacked the essence of the fruit, but because it departed so far from the normal mode of consuming it that it warranted only *shehakol*. Vegetable broth, by contrast, may have been a natural and common way of deriving benefit from vegetables, such that the water was treated as an extension of the vegetable itself. But Tosafot himself recognizes the tension, and the underlying question — what gives infused liquids their halachic status — is a wide-ranging and unresolved one, with implications reaching well beyond blessings into areas such as the status of flavor-infused liquids on Pesach.

### Dill Question

Rav Papa then raises a further question: *maya de-shivsa mai* — what is the status of water in which *shivsa*, dill or a similar spice, has been cooked? The question turns on intent: *le-metukei ta'ama avdi* — was the dill added in order to improve the taste of the water, in which case the water takes on the *bracha* of the spice? Or *le-avurei zuhama avdi* — was it added only to remove some undesirable quality from the water, without being intended to impart positive flavor, in which case the *bracha* of the dill is irrelevant to the water?

### Terumah Precedent

The Gemara resolves this from a *terumah* precedent: if dill is placed in a pot and imparts its taste to what is cooking there, it has transferred its essence entirely and is no longer considered *terumah* — *ein ba mishum terumah* — and is no longer even classified as food, *ve-eina mitama tumas ochalin*, since all of its essence has gone out. *Shema mina le-metukei ta'ama avdi lei* — we can infer from this that the dill is placed there in order to add taste. If its taste has genuinely changed the contents of the pot, then clearly the purpose was to impart flavor, not merely to neutralize something unpleasant. This resolves Rav Papa's question: dill water would receive the *bracha* appropriate to the dill.

### Drinks General Rule

This discussion occasions an important general principle. Whatever one concludes about vegetable waters and their *brachot*, it is well established — and Tosafot addresses this explicitly in an earlier passage, on 48a — that for drinks, the default is *shehakol*, with the single exception of wine, which retains its special *borei pri ha-gafen*. Tosafot deals there with beer, *shichra*, noting that although beer is made from barley, what remains in the liquid is only the taste of the barley — *ta'ama be-alma* — and the beer is not itself the barley. *Ve-od bi-shtiya omer shehakol* — moreover, when one is drinking, one says *shehakol*. This is a longstanding position that our practice has internalized for coffee, tea, and other infused beverages: whatever their source, they receive only *shehakol*.

## Whole vs. Slice

### Chiya's View

Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi teaches: *pas tzenuma be-ke'ara* — dry bread placed in a bowl — *mevarchin alav hamotzi*. Since it is bread, the *bracha* is obviously *hamotzi*; the interest here lies not in which *bracha* is said but in a dispute about the procedure for making it. This teaching is set in tension with a different view of Rabbi Chiya: *tzarich she-tichleh bracha im ha-pas* — the *bracha* must conclude together with the bread. The implication is that one should not make the *bracha* on a slice; one should make it on a whole loaf. And the particular formulation — that the *bracha* must end *im ha-pas* — means that one begins the *bracha* while the bread is whole and completes it precisely as the piece separates, so that the blessing terminates simultaneously with the breaking.

### Rava's View

Rava challenges this position. *Mai shna tzenuma de-lo mishum de-ki chala bracha a-perusa chala* — why do you refuse to bless over the slice? Because when the *bracha* concludes, it concludes over a slice? But the same objection applies when you make the *bracha* over a whole loaf while breaking it: *ki ka-gamra a-perusa gamra* — by the time you finish the *bracha*, the piece has already been broken off, and you too are ending over a slice. Rabbi Chiya's method does not actually succeed in having the *bracha* end over a whole loaf. Therefore, Rava says: *mevarech ve-achar kach botze'a* — make the complete *bracha* first, while the loaf is intact, and only then break off the piece. This is the practice we follow.

### Practice Debate

*Nahardei avdi ke-Rabbi Chiya* — in Nehardea, the practice followed Rabbi Chiya, and people would break the bread as they were completing the *bracha*. *Ve-rabbanan avdi ke-Rava* — the rabbinic students followed Rava, completing the blessing before breaking. Ravina reports that his mother told him his father had followed Rabbi Chiya's practice. The Gemara's ruling is *hilcheta ke-Rava*: we rule like Rava. The blessing is made in full while the loaf is whole, and the bread is broken only afterward. This is the established *halacha*, confirmed in the Geonic literature.

### Shabbat Risk

Tosafot addresses an associated practical concern. Some have the practice of making *hamotzi* before cutting, so that the bread is fully intact throughout the *bracha* — and indeed, this is the straightforward application of Rava's view. Tosafot notes, however, that some are careful not to begin cutting until after the *bracha* is complete, and that a partial cut during the *bracha* would be a reasonable middle path. Yet on Shabbat, Tosafot cautions against this intermediate approach — *shelo tishmat yado* — lest one's hand slip and complete the separation accidentally before the *bracha* is finished, thereby ruining the whole loaf that is needed for *lechem mishneh*. If that whole loaf is compromised, one may have no intact loaf to fulfill the *mitzvah*. Therefore on Shabbat it is proper to be strict and make the complete *bracha* before any cutting begins. This accounts for the practice of scoring or lightly marking the *challah* as a compromise — though Tosafot suggests that even a partial cut left attached, where lifting the separating piece would still bring the whole loaf up with it, would be considered *shalem* for these purposes, following the *Rosh*'s definition.

## Multiple Loaves

### Huna on Pieces

The Gemara presents a new scenario: *hevi'u lifneihem pesisin u-shleimin* — they were brought both small broken pieces of bread and whole loaves. The obvious expectation would be to make the *bracha* on the whole loaf. Yet Rav Huna rules: *mevarech al ha-pesisin ve-poter et ha-shleimin* — one may make the *bracha* on the small pieces and thereby exempt the whole loaves. This is striking: it establishes that while whole is preferable, it is not an absolute requirement, and one who blesses on pieces has acted acceptably.

### Yochanan on Form

Rabbi Yochanan, however, insists: *shleima mitzvah min ha-muvchar* — a whole loaf is the more praiseworthy way of fulfilling the obligation. This does not mean the pieces are invalid, but that all else being equal, priority belongs to the whole. The question becomes what happens when all else is *not* equal.

### Wheat vs. Barley

The Gemara introduces the case of *perusa shel chitin u-shleima min ha-se'orin* — a slice of wheat bread and a whole loaf of barley bread. Here two competing considerations clash: wholeness favors the barley loaf, while species priority favors the wheat slice. Wheat — *chittah* — appears earlier than barley — *se'orah* — in the verse listing the seven species with which the Land of Israel is praised, and Rashi (though this formulation may not appear in our text of Rashi) identifies that priority as the operative factor. The Gemara rules: *divrei ha-kol mevarech al ha-perusa shel chitin ve-poter et ha-shleima shel se'orin* — unanimously, one blesses on the wheat slice and exempts the whole barley loaf. Scriptural order of mention overrides the preference for wholeness.

### Priority Hierarchy

Tosafot synthesizes these rulings into an ordered hierarchy of priorities. First comes specificity of *bracha*: a food whose *bracha* is more particular precedes one whose *bracha* is more general. If the *brachot* are equal, one then considers which species is mentioned earlier in the relevant verse. If that too is equal or inapplicable, the preference for a whole loaf over a slice governs. Finally, if all of those factors are equivalent, one follows one's preference — either the more desired food or the one of higher quality. One practical complication: these priorities assume that the foods in question are actually intended for consumption at this meal. If one has no interest in eating from the whole loaf and only wanted a slice, the loaf may effectively be removed from the calculus, since the formal priority of wholeness becomes relevant only when both are genuinely before one as eating options.

## Onion Debate

### Onion Dispute

The Gemara now imports a related debate from the laws of *terumah* in order to illuminate the question of whole versus partial. The *tanna'im* dispute: *tormin be-tzal katan shalem aval lo chatzi batzal gadol* — one should take *terumah* with a small whole onion rather than with half of a large onion. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *lo ki ela chatzi batzal gadol* — on the contrary, take the half of the large onion. The Gemara asks: *mai lav be-ha ka mipalgi* — is this not a debate between whether wholeness (*shalem adif*) or importance/quality (*chashuv adif*) takes precedence?

### With Kohen

The Gemara reframes this. *Heicha de-ika kohen* — when a *kohen* is present to receive the *terumah* immediately, *kulei alma lo pligi de-chashuv adif* — everyone agrees that importance takes precedence. When the *kohen* is there, the overriding concern is to give him what he will find most desirable or most valuable. Whether that means more quantity or better taste — Tosafot notes the ambiguity — it must override any formal preference for wholeness, since the purpose of *terumah* is the benefit of the *kohen*.

### Without Kohen

*Ki pligi de-leika kohen* — the dispute applies only when no *kohen* is present. The *mishna* clarifies: *kol makom she-yesh kohen torem min ha-yafeh* — wherever there is a *kohen*, one takes *terumah* from the better item. *Ve-chol makom she-ein kohen torem min ha-mitkayem* — where there is no *kohen*, one takes from what will last longer, so that if a *kohen* eventually arrives, something will still be available. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: even without a *kohen* present, one should take from the finer item, because the quality of the act of *teruma* taking itself is what one should focus on, independent of the *kohen*'s immediate presence.

The practical upshot for the onion question is therefore this: once you cut a vegetable, it will deteriorate faster. If a *kohen* is not immediately present, the whole small onion — which remains intact — may last longer and thus serve the *kohen* better when he eventually arrives, even if the half of the large onion would be preferable to him right now.

### Ravina's Solution

The Gemara then invokes the *yere shamayim* solution — the approach of one who is God-fearing and wishes to satisfy all opinions simultaneously. *Mar brei de-Ravina maniach perusa be-toch ha-shleima u-votze'a* — the son of Ravina would place the piece inside the whole loaf and make *hamotzi* on the combined unit. By doing so, he blessed on what was effectively both at once: the whole loaf provided the *shalem*, and the inserted piece addressed whatever consideration made the higher-quality item preferable. Rashi reads this as addressing simply the question of large-piece versus small-whole; Tosafot prefers to read it as continuing the earlier discussion about a wheat slice placed within a whole barley loaf — satisfying both the species-priority consideration and the wholeness consideration simultaneously. This interpretation makes the case halachically weightier and the solution more meaningful.

A *tanna* who was teaching this as a *braisa* before Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak was asked his name. He said: Shalman. Rav Nachman responded warmly: *shalom atta u-shleima mishnatecha* — peace to you and peace to your teaching, *she-samta shalom bein ha-talmidim* — for you have made peace among the students. The pun on *shalem* — whole, complete, peaceful — is unmistakable. Whether this *braisa* is authentically Tannaitic is a legitimate question; the Babylonian Talmud occasionally preserves as *braitot* what appear to be Amoraic formulations, and the tightly constructed wordplay here makes the authenticity somewhat questionable. Nevertheless, the content and the teaching stand. And there is a deliberate literary contrast with the Bar Kappara episode: there, an exchange over *brachot* ended with a harsh rebuke and tragic consequence; here, a teaching on the same theme elicits warmth, a name, and a blessing.

## Pesach Matzot

### Pesach Exception

Rav Papa teaches: *hakol modim be-Pesach she-maniach perusa be-toch shleima u-votze'a* — on Pesach, everyone agrees that one places the broken piece inside the whole *matzah* and makes the *bracha* on the combined unit. The context here is almost certainly *pita*-style flatbread rather than the soft *matzot* we use today, which is why placing one piece inside another was a practical option. In our practice, we hold the pieces together and make the blessing over both simultaneously.

### Lechem Oni

*Amar lei mai ta'ama* — what is the reason? *Lechem oni ktiv* — it is written "poor person's bread," and the poor person's bread is characteristically a broken loaf. On Pesach night, the broken *matzah* is not merely an accident of handling; it is itself halachically significant. The *mitzvah* of *matzah* on Pesach night specifically requires what is characterized as the bread of poverty, and a broken piece is that bread by definition.

This creates a genuine tension with the general principle that favors whole loaves. The resolution is the combined holding or stacking: one maintains the *shalem* — the whole *matzah* — for the general preference of wholeness, while incorporating the broken piece to fulfill the specific requirement of *lechem oni*.

### Ashkenazi Custom

The Ashkenazic practice is to hold all three *matzot* — two whole ones and the middle broken one — when making *hamotzi*. The *bracha* of *hamotzi* is directed toward the top whole *matzah*, satisfying the *lechem mishneh* requirement. Then, when reciting *al achilat matzah*, attention turns specifically to the middle broken *matzah*, which is the *lechem oni* and the one through which the *mitzvah* of eating *matzah* is most properly fulfilled. Though one is technically *yotzei* through any of the *matzot*, the broken middle piece is the one that the *mitzvah* specifically demands, and so it receives the focus of the second blessing.

### Multiple Blessings

Tosafot raises the question of whether making two blessings over the same object — *hamotzi* directed at the top whole *matzah*, then *al achilat matzah* — amounts to improperly piling up *mitzvot*. The answer, Tosafot concludes, is that *birchat ha-nehenin* — blessings of pleasure and enjoyment — are not subject to that concern. The principle against multiplying *mitzvot* on a single act applies to commanded obligations, not to blessings tied to personal enjoyment and *hana'ah*. The precedent is clear: we recite *borei pri ha-gafen* over wine at *kiddush* and then say all the blessings of *kiddush* over the same cup, without any concern that this constitutes inappropriate accumulation.

### Rambam Position

The *Rambam* takes a notably different position. He holds clearly that *hamotzi* on Pesach night should be made on the broken piece — the *perusa* — rather than the whole *matzah*. This accords strictly with the requirement of *lechem oni* and makes the broken piece, not the whole one, the primary vehicle of the *bracha*. In practice, the *Sefardic* communities follow the *Rambam*, though there is variation within those communities regarding whether three *matzot* are held to begin with or only two.

## Shabbat Lechem

### Double Manna

The Gemara now turns to the Shabbat obligation. Rav Abba teaches: *uvShabbat chayav adam livtzo'a al shtei kikarot* — on Shabbat one is obligated to break bread over two loaves. The reason: *lechem mishneh ktiv* — the verse says double bread, referring to the double portion of *man* that fell on Friday so that the Israelites would not need to gather on Shabbat. Therefore, we maintain two loaves on Shabbat to commemorate that double portion.

The straightforward implication of the Gemara's formulation is that this obligation applies specifically to Shabbat and not to *Yom Tov*. There is a debate among the *rishonim* on this point, partly tied to the question of whether the *man* fell double on *erev Yom Tov* as well. Our widespread practice is to observe *lechem mishneh* on *Yom Tov*, but the Gemara's language here does strongly suggest it is a Shabbat-specific institution. This also clarifies why on Pesach the practice of combining the whole and broken *matzah* is not driven by *lechem mishneh* concerns — the double-loaf requirement does not apply there in the same way — but rather by the competing interests of *shalem* and *lechem oni*.

A significant practical point: the *halacha* requires that two whole loaves be present for *lechem mishneh*, and wholeness is certainly the *lechatchila* standard. However, the absence of a whole loaf does not eliminate *lechem mishneh* entirely. One could technically fulfill the institution with two slices, ensuring that two loaves are represented even if neither is complete. This raises an interesting question left as a *she'ela*: if on Shabbat one possesses a single whole loaf and nothing else, does one make *hamotzi* on the whole loaf and forgo *lechem mishneh*, or does one break it in half to create two pieces and thereby achieve *lechem mishneh* at the cost of wholeness?

### Portion Requirement

Rav Ashi reports: I saw Rav Kahana hold two loaves on Shabbat — *denakit tartei* — and break only one — *u-vatza chada*. One is not obligated to eat from both loaves; having both present satisfies the institution. This is indeed the *halacha* we follow: *lechem mishneh* is a requirement of presence, not consumption.

### Zeira's Piece

Rav Zeira's practice is recorded: *hava batza a-kulei sheruta* — when he took his piece of bread for Shabbat, he would cut a very large chunk, sufficient to last him the entire meal.

### Etiquette Question

Ravina questioned Rav Ashi about this: *v'ha kamitchazi k'ra'avtanusa* — does this not look like gluttony or poor manners, the same quality for which Bar Kappara criticized the student who rushed to bless the poultry? Rav Ashi replied: *keivan de-chol yoma lo ka-avid hachi* — since this is not his normal practice throughout the week, and he does it specifically now, *lo mitchazi ke-ra'avtanusa* — it does not appear to be a failure of manners. He is not acting from habit or lack of self-control; there is a reason for this specific behavior in this specific context. The Gemara does not spell out that reason explicitly, though the connection to the institution of the *man* — which fed the Israelites for an entire day's portion — offers a compelling explanation for why a substantial Shabbat portion carries its own kind of significance.

### Thick Slices

Rav Ami and Rav Assi are reported to have cut very thick slices — *rifta* — sufficient for a person to be sustained for an entire meal. This is presented in the context of Shabbat eating, where the size of the portion connects meaningfully to the theme of the double *man* and the fullness that Shabbat meals are meant to embody.

## Eruv Food

### Ami & Assi

The Gemara concludes with a report about Rav Ami and Rav Assi: *ki hava misrami lehu rifta de-eruva* — when they happened to have bread left over from an *eruv* — whether *eruv chatzeirot* or the food set aside for *eruv tavshilin* — *mevarchin aleha Hamotzi Lechem Min Ha'aretz* — they would specifically make *hamotzi* on it and eat it themselves.

### Mitzvah Principle

*Amri* — they said: *ho'il ve-is'avid bah mitzvah chada, na'avid bah mitzvah achriti* — since this food has already been used for one *mitzvah*, let us use it for another. Rather than giving it away or setting it aside, they made a point of eating it themselves and reciting *hamotzi* over it, thereby involving it in the *mitzvah* of Shabbat or *Yom Tov* bread.

### Custom Connection

This principle has become the basis for the *minhag* of specifically eating the food designated for *eruv tavshilin* at the Shabbat or *Yom Tov* meal. Once an object has been sanctified for one *mitzvah* purpose, there is a value in connecting it to another. The Gemara continues with this theme, and this principle resonates with the broader spirit of Shabbat meal practice that the entire sugya has been elaborating — from the structure of blessings and the priority of loaves, through the significance of size and wholeness, to this final note on carrying forward the sanctity of one *mitzvah* into the next.
