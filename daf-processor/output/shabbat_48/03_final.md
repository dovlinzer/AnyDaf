# Shabbat 48 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hatmanah Overview

### Hatmanah Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בַּמֶּה טוֹמְנִין וּבַמָּה אֵין טוֹמְנִין? — אֵין טוֹמְנִין לֹא בַּגֶּפֶת וְלֹא בַּזֶּבֶל, לֹא בַּמֶּלַח וְלֹא בַּסִּיד וְלֹא בַּחוֹל — בֵּין לַחִין בֵּין יְבֵשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** When a pot is removed from the fire on Shabbat eve it may be insulated in materials that preserve its heat, but not in materials that increase its heat. Raising the temperature of a pot is tantamount to cooking. The *mishnayot* that follow list those materials in which such a pot may be insulated on Shabbat eve and those materials in which it may not be insulated. MISHNA: **In what may one insulate** a pot of cooked food on Shabbat eve, **and in what may one not insulate** it? **One may neither insulate** it **in the solid residue** of produce that has been pressed free of its oil, **nor in manure, nor in salt, nor in lime, nor in sand, whether** those materials are **moist** or **whether** they are **dry.** All of these materials spontaneously generate heat when piled for an extended period. Therefore, they add heat to a pot insulated in them.

The daf begins at the bottom of 47b, immediately following the *mishnah*, where a new *perek* opens. The previous *perek* dealt with leaving food on the fire — *shehiya* — touching along the way on questions of *bishul* and *muktzah*. This *perek* returns to the theme of putting food on the fire before *Shabbat*, but now in a different register: the food is already fully cooked, and the concern is insulating it so that it retains heat. This practice is called *hatmanah* — literally, burying or insulating.

The classic method of insulation was to store cooked food in hot ashes, which would preserve the heat most effectively. That very effectiveness, however, created a rabbinic concern parallel to the concern about *shehiya*: one might come to stir the ashes in order to reignite them and intensify the heat. Accordingly, the Rabbis ruled that one may not perform *hatmanah* — even before *Shabbat* — in any material that adds heat, including hot ashes. This prohibition applies even to materials unrelated to fire, so long as they actively add heat rather than merely retaining it.

### Heat-Adding Materials

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא בַּתֶּבֶן וְלֹא בַּזַּגִּין וְלֹא בַּמּוֹכִין וְלֹא בָּעֲשָׂבִין — בִּזְמַן שֶׁהֵן לַחִין, אֲבָל טוֹמְנִין בָּהֶן כְּשֶׁהֵן יְבֵשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** **And** one may **neither** insulate a pot **in straw, nor in the residue of grapes** that have been pressed for their juice, **nor in soft material,** e.g., from tattered clothing, **nor in grass, when these** materials **are moist. However, one may insulate** a pot **in them when they are dry.**

The *mishnah* enumerates a range of materials that add heat and are therefore prohibited for *hatmanah*. What all these materials appear to share, at least in significant part, is that they are organic substances whose decomposition releases heat — a process that constitutes the creation of new warmth rather than mere retention of stored warmth. Dung, for instance, releases heat through decomposition. Sand is different: it may retain heat if it was previously heated, but it does not inherently generate heat on its own.

### Gefet vs. Sesame

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: גֶּפֶת שֶׁל זֵיתִים תְּנַן, אֲבָל דְּשׁוּמְשְׁמִין שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי, אוֹ דִילְמָא דְּשׁוּמְשְׁמִין תְּנַן וְכָל שֶׁכֵּן דְּזֵיתִים?!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **Did we learn** with regard to **the residue of olives** in the mishna, **but** the residue **of sesame** seeds that were pressed for their oil, which produces less heat, may **well** be used for insulating food on Shabbat eve? **Or, perhaps, we learned** with regard to the residue **of sesame** in the mishna, **and all the more so** insulating food in the residue **of olives** is prohibited?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא מִשּׁוּם חַד דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: קוּפָּה שֶׁטָּמַן בָּהּ — אָסוּר לְהַנִּיחָהּ עַל גֶּפֶת שֶׁל זֵיתִים. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ שֶׁל זֵיתִים תְּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a resolution to this dilemma from what **Rabbi Zeira said in the name of one** of the Sages **of the school of Rabbi Yannai:** With regard to **a basket in which one insulated** food in a permissible manner, e.g., in dry soft material or the like, **it is prohibited to place it upon the residue of olives. Conclude from this** that **we learned** with regard to the residue **of olives** in our mishna; however, insulating food in the residue of sesame is permitted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ לְעִנְיַן הַטְמָנָה — דְּשׁוּמְשְׁמִין נָמֵי אָסוּר, לְעִנְיַן אַסּוֹקֵי הַבְלָא — דְּזֵיתִים מַסְּקִי הַבְלָא, דְּשׁוּמְשְׁמִין לָא מַסְּקִי הַבְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **Actually, I** can **say to you** that **with regard to** actual **insulation,** the residue **of sesame is also prohibited.** However, **with regard to** **causing heat to rise,** i.e., heating food that is not actually insulated in it, but merely resting upon it, the residue **of olives causes heat to rise.** Therefore, it is prohibited even to place cooked food upon it. However, the residue **of sesame does not cause heat to rise** to that extent. Therefore, it is permitted to place food upon it.

The *Gemara* opens with a formal question — *ibayah lehu* — about *gefet*. The term was initially understood to refer to the residue of grape pressing, but in fact it denotes the residue from olives: olive twigs or the refuse remaining after olive oil production. *Gefet* is one of the first items the *mishnah* identifies as prohibited for *hatmanah* because it adds heat.

The question the *Gemara* raises is whether the *mishnah*'s prohibition on *gefet shel zeitim* — olive refuse — is limited to olives specifically, with sesame refuse (*shumshumin*) being permitted, or whether the *mishnah* speaks of olives as its primary example while intending to include, *kal va-chomer*, even sesame refuse, which adds some heat though less than olives.

The *Gemara* adduces a proof from a statement of Rav Zeira in the name of one scholar from the academy of Rabbi Yannai. The ruling is as follows: if one has a container (*kupa*) into which a pot has been insulated — say, surrounded by towels or some other heat-retaining but not heat-adding material — and one then places that container on top of a box filled with *gefet shel zeitim*, this is prohibited. The *gefet* underneath functions like a heat-adding base, analogous to a fire below.

This scenario is directly relevant to contemporary practice, as it describes the functional equivalent of placing a wrapped pot on a *blech* — a metal sheet placed over a flame — where the *blech* adds heat and the towel retains it. The combined apparatus, in certain configurations, raises the question of *hatmanah b'mosif hevel*.

The *Gemara* resolves its initial question as follows: the prohibition on sesame refuse applies even to the surrounding configuration, since it does add heat, but only the olive refuse generates enough *hevel* — rising heat — to create a problem when placed underneath. Sesame refuse does not generate sufficient upward heat to constitute *hatmanah* in that configuration, whereas olive refuse does. Thus the two substances are distinguished not by whether they add heat in principle, but by how directionally and forcefully that heat rises.

## Hatmanah vs. Shehiya

### Blech Problem

At this point the discussion calls for a pause to address a fundamental practical question raised by Tosafot. We have learned throughout the previous *perek* about *shehiya*, and we know from the current *perek* that one may not perform *hatmanah* in a material that adds heat. Yet common practice involves placing food on a *blech* before *Shabbat* — a metal sheet over an open flame that clearly adds heat. If wrapping food in towels alongside the heat of a *blech* constitutes *hatmanah b'mosif hevel*, how is this permitted?

This is the foundational question Tosafot raises: what precisely is the line between *hatmanah* and *shehiya*? In Tosafot's time, the typical practice was to insert a pot into hot ashes such that the ashes would surround the lower portion of the pot — a partial immersion that could be considered a form of *hatmanah* even when the ashes had been swept and covered (*garuf ve-katum*). The problem is: if the surrounding material adds heat, why should that not implicate the prohibition of *hatmanah b'mosif hevel*?

### Tosafot's Distinction

Tosafot begins by drawing an inference from the case of *gefet*: since placing a pot-containing box atop olive refuse is prohibited because the refuse raises heat upward, one might conclude that placing coals beneath a pot — even covered with ashes — should likewise be prohibited. As Tosafot states: *sheharei gechalim ma'alim hevel lema'alah* — coals raise heat upward, just like *gefet shel zeitim*. This would seem to call into question not only the *blech* practice but the entire structure of the previous *perek*.

Tosafot raises the difficulty explicitly: *heicha anu matminim al kira shelanu* — how do we perform *hatmanah* on our own stoves and ovens? Placing a pot in an oven that one then turns off involves a heat-adding element. The *Ri* — one of the most important voices in the laws of *hatmanah* — provides the answer that becomes normative.

Tosafot does mention a second answer: that the heat in the case of a cooling oven is dissipating, meaning the oven is losing stored heat rather than generating new heat, whereas the organic materials in the *mishnah* are actively generating new heat through decomposition. Under this distinction, true *hatmanah b'mosif hevel* would require the creation of new heat, not merely the slow release of absorbed heat. However, this answer is not the one Tosafot ultimately focuses on, because if adopted it would force us to explain the entire previous *perek* as dealing exclusively with dissipating heat — an uncomfortable constraint — and would mean that any genuine new-heat scenario during *shehiya* would also be problematic.

The critical answer is that of the *Ri*, which draws the central line between *hatmanah* and *shehiya*:

> *Ve'lo dami l'hatmanah b'davar hamosif hevel, de'lo asru ela k'sh'matminu b'davik saviv hakedera, dumya d'remetz; aval tanur o chafira she'yesh avir bein hadafanos lakedera, ein le'esor yoser mish'ar sha'ah, af al pi shekol hakedera besoch hatanur.*

The prohibition of *hatmanah b'mosif hevel* applies only when the heat-adding material is *davik saviv hakedera* — pressed against and fully surrounding the pot on all sides, analogous to being buried in ashes. If there is an *avir* — an air space — between the pot and the surrounding heat source, it does not constitute *hatmanah* at all, regardless of how much heat is generated.

### Practical Application

This *Ri*'s distinction has far-reaching practical implications. Because a conventional oven has air space between the walls and the pot, placing food in an oven before *Shabbat* is not *hatmanah* — the pot is inside the oven but is not encased in a heat-adding material that presses against it from all sides. Similarly, a *blech* on a stovetop does not constitute *hatmanah* because the pot rests on the *blech* from below but is not surrounded by it.

The more nuanced contemporary question involves covering the pot with a towel while it sits on a *blech*. The towel retains heat; the *blech* adds heat. Does the combination create *hatmanah b'mosif hevel*? The answer depends entirely on whether the towel presses against the pot on all sides. If the towel is draped loosely — forming, as it were, a tent over the pot without compressing against it — there is air space and the arrangement does not constitute *hatmanah*. If, however, the towel is pressed tightly around the pot so as to encase it from all directions, that would be *hatmanah*, and since the *blech* below is adding heat, it would be *hatmanah b'mosif hevel*, which is prohibited.

A similar question arises regarding the *crock pot*: some rule that a crock pot constitutes *hatmanah* because the heating element surrounds the food vessel on all sides, while others permit it on the grounds that the lid is not in contact with the sides and thus the enclosure is incomplete. This Tosafot is the foundational source for that entire discussion, and the *Ri*'s distinction — full contact on all sides versus air space — is the operative principle in contemporary *halacha* for defining *hatmanah*.

## Reish Galuta Stories

### Flask Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה וְרַבִּי זֵירָא אִיקְּלַעוּ לְבֵי רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא. חַזְיוּהּ לְהָהוּא עַבְדָּא דְּאַנַּח כּוּזָא דְמַיָּא אַפּוּמָּא דְקוּמְקוּמָא. נַזְהֵיהּ רַבָּה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא: מַאי שְׁנָא מִמֵּיחַם עַל גַּבֵּי מֵיחַם? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם אוֹקוֹמֵי קָא מוֹקֵים, הָכָא אוֹלוֹדֵי קָא מוֹלֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates an anecdote somewhat relevant to the previous discussion: **Rabba and Rabbi Zeira happened** to come **to the house of the Exilarch** on Shabbat, and **saw this servant who placed a jug [*kuza*] of** cold **water on the mouth of a kettle** filled with hot water. **Rabba rebuked him** for having acted contrary to the *halakha*. **Rabbi Zeira said to** Rabba: **How is** this case **different** from placing **an urn on top of** another **urn,** which is permitted on Shabbat? Rabba **said to him: There,** when he places one urn on top of another urn, **he** merely **preserves** the heat in the upper urn; therefore, it is permitted. **Here,** in the case where he places the jug of cold water on the mouth of a kettle, **he is generating** heat in the water in the upper vessel; therefore, it is prohibited.

The *Gemara* now introduces two narratives set in the household of the *Reish Galuta*, the political head of the Jewish community in Babylonia. The *Reish Galuta*'s household was notable for wealth and political prominence but not always for *halachic* scrupulousness, and various improper practices are documented in the *Gemara* from that setting.

Rabba and Rabbi Zeira visit the house of the *Reish Galuta*. They observe a servant who places a flask of water (*kuza d'maya*) on the opening of a hot kettle (*kumkuma*). Rabba immediately rebukes the servant. Rabbi Zeira questions the rebuke: how is this different, he asks, from the common practice of placing one pot of water atop another hot pot to warm it — *maya agav maya*?

Rabba responds with a fundamental distinction: *hatam okumi kamokim* — there, you are merely maintaining the heat of something already warm; *hacha ulodi kamolid* — here, you are actively generating new heat in something cold. The servant was placing a flask of cold water directly atop a hot kettle, using the heat of the kettle to heat the flask.

One might initially read this as a concern analogous to *mosif hevel* in the context of *hatmanah*. But the *Rishonim* generally interpret this story as implicating *bishul* rather than *hatmanah*, given the analysis of Tosafot we have just reviewed. With the *Ri*'s distinction in place, this scenario — a pot resting atop another vessel — clearly lacks the full-contact enclosure that defines *hatmanah*.

Tosafot proposes that the straightforward reading of the case is that one flask will reach *yad soledet bo* while the other will not. If the flask placed on top of the hot kettle will ultimately be heated to *yad soledet bo*, that constitutes *bishul*; if it will not reach that threshold, there is no concern. However, Tosafot is not fully satisfied with this reading, because we already know from prior discussions that one may warm food that will not reach *yad soledet bo* — that in itself is not prohibited. So the mere addition of heat, absent *yad soledet bo*, should not be problematic.

Tosafot therefore presses the reading further and suggests the distinction may turn on the temperature of the contents: one flask contained warm water, the other cold. Even more significantly, Tosafot offers a principle that is *normative l'halacha*: one may not place a cold liquid — which is treated for these purposes like uncooked food even if it was previously cooked and cooled — in a position where it has the *potential* to reach *yad soledet bo*, even if one intends to remove it before that threshold is reached. The concern is the placement itself, not only the eventual outcome. This principle, while not derived explicitly from the words of the story, is how Tosafot reads the narrative in light of other *sugyot* and is practically significant.

One practical corollary: if one has a *hot plate* or heating surface that genuinely cannot raise food to *yad soledet bo* under any circumstances, it may be permissible to place food upon it on *Shabbat*, as the *bishul* concern would be absent — though one must still consider any applicable *chazarah* issues.

### Cloth & Squeezing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר חַזְיֵיהּ דִּפְרַס דַּסְתּוֹדַר אַפּוּמֵּיהּ דְּכוּבָּא וְאַנַּח נַטְלָא עִילָּוֵיהּ. נַזְהֵיהּ רַבָּה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא: אַמַּאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַשְׁתָּא חָזֵית. לְסוֹף חַזְיֵיהּ דְּקָא מְעַצַּר לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי שְׁנָא מִפְּרוֹנְקָא? אָמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם לָא קָפֵיד עִילָּוֵיהּ, הָכָא קָפֵיד עִילָּוֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: Rabba **then saw** that same servant **spread a kerchief [*dastodar*] over a vat** of water **and place a cup** used to draw water from the vat, **on** the kerchief. Once again, **Rabba rebuked him** for having acted improperly. **Rabbi Zeira said to him: Why** did you rebuke him? Rabba **said to him: Now, see** what will happen. **Ultimately, he saw that** the servant **was squeezing** out the water that was absorbed by the kerchief, thereby violating a Torah prohibition. Nevertheless, Rabbi Zeira **said to him: How is this** case **different from** that of a **cloth [*parvanka*],** which one is permitted to spread over a vat even on Shabbat? Rabba **said to him:** There is a distinction between the two cases: **There,** in the case of the cloth, **he is not particular about it;** even if it gets wet, he will not come to squeeze it dry. **Here,** with regard to the kerchief, **he is particular about it,** and he will wring it so that it will not remain wet.

The *Gemara* then records a second incident from the same visit. The servant spreads a cloth (*satudar*) over the mouth of a barrel used to store water, and places a cup (*natla*) on top of it. The cup would be used to draw water from the barrel, and the cloth was draped beneath it.

A brief digression on the word *natla* is in order. The *Rashba* notes that the term *natla* — the Aramaic word for a cup used to draw water — underlies the expression *netilat yadayim*. The *bracha* uses the term *al netilat yadayim* not merely in the sense of "lifting" the hands, but with specific reference to the act of pouring water over the hands from a cup. The *natla* is the instrument of that pouring.

Rava rebukes the servant for spreading the cloth over the barrel. Rabbi Zeira asks why, pointing out that in another *perek* there is a teaching permitting the spreading of a *parunka* — a cloth — over a barrel containing liquid on *Shabbat*. What is the difference between these two cases?

Rava replies: *hatam lo kapid ilavei, hacha kapid ilavei* — in the case of the *parunka*, the person is not particular about the cloth getting wet; here, the person is particular about it. In the *parunka* case, since the user does not mind the cloth becoming saturated, there is no concern that he will come to squeeze it out. Here, the cloth is one that its owner would not want wet, and were it to become wet — which is quite likely given that it covers a water barrel and a cup is placed upon it — he would squeeze it out in order to dry it.

The concern is *sechitah* — squeezing. Squeezing a cloth can implicate two distinct prohibitions: if done to extract the liquid for use, it resembles *dash* (threshing); if done to clean the cloth, it may implicate a form of laundering. In this case, the servant is not seeking to extract water for use, so the concern is more akin to the cleaning-laundering category. There is some discussion among the *Rishonim* regarding how clear the *d'oraita* concern is in this case, as the primary purpose is simply to dry the cloth, and the cleaning effect is incidental (*eino mitkaven*). Nonetheless, the risk is real enough that the arrangement is prohibited from the outset.

The broader point is that the anxiety around squeezing is pervasive in Shabbat law. The *Ashkenazi* custom to avoid full immersion in water on *Shabbat* similarly derives in significant part from concern about squeezing wet garments.

## Muktzah Materials

### Moving Muktzah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא בַּתֶּבֶן. בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר מַתְנָה מֵאַבָּיֵי: מוֹכִין שֶׁטָּמַן בָּהֶן, מַהוּ לְטַלְטְלָן בְּשַׁבָּת?
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **And** one may **neither** insulate a pot **in straw**, nor in the residue of grapes that were pressed for their juice, nor in soft material. **Rav Adda bar Mattana raised a dilemma before Abaye:** With regard to swatches of **soft material in which he insulated** a pot, **what is** the *halakha* with regard **to moving** that material **on Shabbat?** Ordinarily, swatches of materials of that kind are set-aside because they have no use. Therefore, moving them on Shabbat is prohibited. Do we say that since they are now being used to insulate a pot, they assume the legal status of a utensil, which may be moved on Shabbat?

The *Gemara* returns to the topic of *hatmanah* and turns to a related question raised by Rav Ada bar Matna before Abaye. The *mishnah* that the *Gemara* is discussing also prohibits *hatmanah* with *muchin* — small pieces of cloth — when they are moist, while allowing it when they are dry. These cloth pieces are ordinarily designated for sewing purposes; they sit in a sewing kit awaiting use as patches or similar applications, and as such, they are *muktzah* — set aside and lacking a permissible use for *Shabbat*.

The question is: if on *Erev Shabbat* one took these dry *muchin* and used them to perform permitted *hatmanah* around a pot, does that practical use change their status? Can they now be handled on *Shabbat*, their identity having shifted to "insulating material"?

There is a related procedure that the *Gemara* addresses elsewhere — *tiltul min ha-tzad*, incidental movement. Even if the *muchin* remain *muktzah*, one could reach into the insulation to grab the pot, and the cloth would be displaced incidentally rather than directly handled. The question here is more pointed: has the use of the *muchin* for *hatmanah* affirmatively stripped them of their *muktzah* status, making them directly movable?

### Identity Not Changed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְכִי מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ קוּפָּה שֶׁל תֶּבֶן, עוֹמֵד וּמַפְקִיר קוּפָּה שֶׁל מוֹכִין?
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to him:** Just **because he does not** now **have a basket of straw** in which to insulate his food, **does he stand up and renounce his basket of soft material?** Obviously, he would have preferred to insulate his food in straw, as it is less expensive. The only reason that he used that material was because there was no straw available at the time. However, he does not want the swatches of material to be used for any other purpose, lest it be ruined. Therefore, it remains set-aside.

Abaye's response is striking in its logic. He says: *ve-chi mipnei she-ein lo kupa shel teven omed u-mafkir kupa shel muchin?* — does the fact that one could not obtain straw for insulation mean that one is prepared to permanently relinquish one's cloth pieces and dedicate them to *hatmanah* every *Erev Shabbat*? Of course not. This was an ad hoc arrangement. Once *Shabbat* is over, the person will go and acquire proper insulating material. The *muchin* will return to the sewing kit. Their fundamental identity has not changed, and therefore they remain *muktzah*.

This principle is genuinely counterintuitive. Ordinarily, one can alter the *muktzah* status of an object by designating it for a *Shabbat*-permissible purpose before *Shabbat* begins — the classic example being a rock designated as a doorstop or paperweight, which ceases to be *muktzah* once assigned that function. The difference here is that the assignment to a new function was purely situational and temporary. The person's intent is clear: as soon as *Shabbat* ends, these pieces of cloth revert to their original role. There is no genuine reclassification of identity.

This raises a further question: what level of designation is required to remove an object from *muktzah* status? A verbal declaration is not strictly necessary; a practical act of designation before *Shabbat* can suffice. However, the more inherently *muktzah* an object is, the clearer and more practical the act of designation must be. And crucially, if the person's own mindset is that the object will return to its former use after *Shabbat*, then even a practical use during *Shabbat* preparation does not constitute a genuine change of identity.

### Baraita Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: טוֹמְנִין בְּגִיזֵּי צֶמֶר וּבְצִיפֵּי צֶמֶר וּבִלְשׁוֹנוֹת שֶׁל אַרְגָּמָן וּבְמוֹכִין, וְאֵין מְטַלְטְלִין אוֹתָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Let us say** that the following *baraita* **supports him: One may insulate** a pot of food on Friday afternoon **in woolen fleece, in combed wool, in tabs of** wool dyed **purple, and in** swatches of **soft material; however, he may not move them.** Apparently, this is in accordance with the opinion of Abaye.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מִשּׁוּם הָא — לָא אִירְיָא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִם לֹא טָמַן בָּהֶן אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין אוֹתָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **If that is the reason, there is no** conclusive **argument,** as **it is saying** in the *baraita* **as follows: If,** however, **he did not insulate** a pot **in them, he may not move them** on Shabbat. In that case, they remain earmarked for their own purpose and are therefore set-aside [*muktze*].

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי מַאי לְמֵימְרָא! מַהוּ דְתֵימָא חֲזֵי לְמִזְגָּא עֲלַיְיהוּ, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this last assertion: **If so, what** is the reason **to say** that? Obviously, those materials are set-aside. The Gemara explains: **Lest you say** that all these materials **are suitable** for one **to sit on them,** and, consequently, their legal status is that of utensils, which may be moved. Therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us** that this is not so, and they may not be moved due to the prohibition of set-aside.

The *Gemara* attempts to adduce support for Abaye's ruling from a *baraita*: *tomnin b'gizei tzemer* — one may insulate with raw wool shearings; *uvetzipei tzemer* — with partially combed wool; *uvilshonot shel argaman* — with crimson fabric strips; and *uvimuchin* — with small cloth pieces. All these materials are items ordinarily destined for sewing or textile production. The *baraita* then states: *ve-ein metaltelin otan* — one may not move them, as they are *muktzah*.

The *Gemara* observes that this would appear to provide clear support: the *baraita* mentions *muchin* used for *hatmanah* and then says they cannot be moved. But the *Gemara* deflects: *im lo taman bahen ein metaltelin otan* — the *baraita* is stating two independent rules: first, one may use these materials for *hatmanah*; second, under ordinary circumstances they are *muktzah*. It does not necessarily address the case where they were actually used for *hatmanah* — which was the specific question.

The *Gemara* then asks: if this is the reading, what is the *chiddush*? We already know these materials are *muktzah*. The *Gemara* answers: *mahu d'teima chazi le-mizga aleihu* — one might have thought that some of these materials, such as soft cloth pieces, could be used for lying upon and would therefore not be *muktzah*. The *baraita* teaches that no — they are sewing materials, not items with a functional *Shabbat* use, and they remain *muktzah*.

Thus the *baraita* cannot serve as proof for Abaye's specific point, but the overall conclusion of the *Gemara*'s discussion is that even after actual use in *hatmanah*, the *muchin* retain their *muktzah* status, since the use does not genuinely transform their identity.

## Making Vessels

### Pillow Stuffing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא שְׁרָא לְאַהְדּוֹרֵי אוּדְרָא לְבֵי סַדְיָא בְּשַׁבְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Ḥisda permitted returning stuffing to the pillow** from which it had fallen **on Shabbat.**

The *Gemara* now transitions to a series of topics connected thematically through the common thread of small cloth pieces and the question of what constitutes "making a vessel" (*melekhet keli*) on *Shabbat*. Rav Chisda ruled that one is permitted to replace the stuffing inside pillows on *Shabbat* — if cloth filler has come out, one may push it back in. The implicit reasoning is that this does not constitute a prohibited act of construction.

### Neck Hole Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב חָנָן בַּר חִסְדָּא לְרַב חִסְדָּא: מַתִּירִין בֵּית הַצַּוָּאר בְּשַׁבָּת, אֲבָל לֹא פּוֹתְחִין. וְאֵין נוֹתְנִין אֶת הַמּוֹכִין לֹא לְתוֹךְ הַכַּר וְלֹא לְתוֹךְ הַכֶּסֶת בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥanan bar Ḥisda raised an objection to** the opinion of **Rav Ḥisda** from a *baraita*: **One may untie the neck opening** of a shirt **on Shabbat** if it had been tied by the launderer; **however, one may not open** a new neck opening for the first time on Shabbat. **And one may not place soft material into a pillow or into a cushion on a Festival, and, needless to say,** one may not do so **on Shabbat.** This *baraita* contradicts the ruling issued by Rav Ḥisda.

Rav Chanan bar Chisda challenged his father Rav Chisda from a *baraita* that distinguishes between two similar acts: *matir beit ha-tzavar* — undoing the stitching at the neck hole of a garment — is permitted on *Shabbat*, while *poteach* — opening a neck hole for the first time — is not. The case of *matir* involves a garment that was stitched closed temporarily by a launderer or processor; the stitch was made for a transient purpose and was always intended to be removed. Undoing it on *Shabbat* is therefore permitted. But if the garment arrived from the weaver or vendor with the neck opening never having been made — an opening that is needed to complete the garment's function — then making that opening constitutes a completion of the garment, a form of *makeh b'patish*, and is prohibited.

The analogy to suit pockets sewn shut when purchased is apt: cutting open the pocket stitching is the completing act that makes the pocket usable, and this constitutes making a vessel on *Shabbat*.

### Cloth in Cushions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא בְּחַדְתֵי הָא בְּעַתִּיקֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. This,** the *baraita* is referring to **new** pillows, whereas **that,** the statement of Rav Ḥisda is referring to **old** pillows. Stuffing a pillow for the first time on Shabbat is prohibited because by so doing one fashions a new utensil. However, if the stuffing fell out of the pillow, refilling the pillow is permitted even on Shabbat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: אֵין נוֹתְנִין אֶת הַמּוֹכִין לֹא לְתוֹךְ הַכַּר וְלֹא לְתוֹךְ הַכֶּסֶת בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר בְּשַׁבָּת. נָשְׁרוּ, מַחֲזִירִין אוֹתָן בְּשַׁבָּת, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר בְּיוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **That** opinion **was also taught** in a *baraita*: **One may not place soft material** as stuffing **into a pillow or into a cushion on a Festival, and needless to say** one may not do so **on Shabbat.** However, if the stuffing **fell out, it may be replaced** even **on Shabbat, and needless to say** that doing so is permitted **on a Festival.**

The same *baraita* states: *ve-ein notnin et ha-muchin lo le-toch ha-kar ve-lo le-toch ha-kaset be-Yom Tov, ve-ein tzarich lomar be-Shabbat* — one may not place *muchin* into a cushion (*kar*) or pillow (*kaset*) on *Yom Tov*, and it goes without saying one may not do so on *Shabbat*. This ruling appears to contradict Rav Chisda's permission to replace stuffing.

The *Gemara* resolves the tension with a brief but critical distinction: *ha bachadti ha ba-atiki* — this refers to a new cushion, that to an old one. Inserting stuffing into a new cushion or pillow for the first time constitutes the making of a vessel — the *keli* did not previously exist in functional form. Re-inserting stuffing into an existing pillow from which it has fallen out is not making but restoring, and is permitted. A *baraita* confirms this explicitly: *nashru machzirin otan be-Shabbat, ve-ein tzarich lomar be-Yom Tov* — if the stuffing fell out, one may replace it on *Shabbat*, and certainly on *Yom Tov*.

### Breakage & Repair

This raises a question that is difficult to resolve with clean lines: at what point is something considered broken and its repair considered "making"? The degree to which stuffing has fallen out, the nature of the object, and the regularity with which such disassembly occurs all bear on the analysis.

A number of practical cases illustrate this difficulty. If the temple of eyeglasses falls off, reattaching it with a screw is prohibited — that constitutes a repair requiring a tool and deliberate reconstruction. For some people this happens frequently, which might alter the analysis, since a commonly recurring detachment may indicate that the parts are never truly considered permanently joined. A toothpick or other temporary fill is a different matter, and the permissibility of such improvised solutions depends on whether they genuinely restore function or merely approximate it.

The question of quantity is also raised: does it matter if half the pillow's stuffing fell out versus a small amount? The key variables remain the tightness of fit, the regularity of the occurrence, and whether what is being done constitutes a genuine act of reconstitution. An inflatable mattress or cushion presents an interesting variant: filling it with air for the first time on *Shabbat* might seem like *chadati* — making new — but since such objects are designed precisely to be filled and emptied repeatedly, the act of filling may not constitute the making of a new vessel.

A related question is raised about winding a stopped watch on *Shabbat*: is a non-functioning watch considered not-a-watch, such that restarting it is *makeh b'patish*? Since winding is a regular, recurring maintenance act, most authorities would treat it differently from genuine repair.

A puzzle presents its own set of issues. Assembling a puzzle may implicate *kotev* in the broad sense of creating an image, and if the puzzle is completed and preserved — framed or displayed — the completion could constitute *makeh b'patish*. There are additional concerns prior to reaching that threshold as well.

### Opening a Garment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: הַפּוֹתֵחַ בֵּית הַצַּוָּאר בְּשַׁבָּת — חַיָּיב חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** Having raised the issue of opening a collar, the Gemara cites that **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: One who opens** a new **neck opening** in a shirt **on Shabbat,** by cutting through the fabric and threads that kept it closed, **is liable to bring a sin-offering.** By creating the opening, he renders the shirt fit to wear, thereby fashioning a utensil on Shabbat.

Rav Yehuda then states in the name of Rav: *ha-poteach beit ha-tzavar be-Shabbat, chayav chatat* — one who opens the neck hole of a new garment on *Shabbat* is liable for a *chatat* offering. This is a *melacha d'oraita*, a violation of *makeh b'patish*.

## Chibbur Debate

### Rava's Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקִיף לַהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא: מָה בֵּין זוֹ לִמְגוּפַת חָבִית. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: זֶה חִיבּוּר, וְזֶה אֵינוֹ חִיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana strongly objects to this:** **What is** the difference **between this and the stopper of a** wine **barrel,** which the Sages permitted piercing on Shabbat in order to serve wine to guests? There, too, by piercing the stopper, he fashions a utensil. **Rava said to him:** The cases are not comparable: In **this** case, the neck opening of a shirt, it is considered a **connection,** i.e., it is an organic part of the weave of the fabric; whereas in **that** case, the stopper of the barrel, it is **not** considered a **connection.** Even though the stopper is sealed in place in the barrel, it is a separate entity. When the stopper is pierced, no new vessel is fashioned.

Rav Kahana challenges this ruling by asking: *mah bein zu le-megupat chavit* — how is opening the neck hole of a garment different from the seal of a barrel? The *Gemara* records in the context of barrel seals a ruling that one is permitted on *Shabbat* to open the barrel in order to access its wine. If that is permitted, why is opening the neck hole of a garment prohibited?

Rava answers: *zeh chibbur ve-zeh eino chibbur* — one is considered fully connected, the other is not.

### Rashi Explanation

Rashi explains the distinction as follows. The barrel cover (*megupah*) begins as an independent entity, separate from the barrel itself. Even though it is subsequently affixed to the barrel — *af al pi she-devuka bah* — it is not considered *chibbur* in the full sense, because it was always designed for removal: *she-harei lintol omedes*. It was placed on from the outside for a specific and temporary purpose and was never an integral part of the barrel. The garment, by contrast, was woven as a single unit — *aval ha-beged k'she-nearag kulo chibbur* — and even the closing of the neck opening was part of the initial construction of the garment, not a subsequent addition.

Tosafot offers an alternative and conceptually cleaner reading: the permitted act with the barrel is making a hole in the *cover*, not in the barrel itself. A hole in a cover does not constitute making a vessel; it makes the cover accessible, but the barrel — the actual vessel — is unaffected. Making a hole in the barrel itself would be prohibited. If the cover is not considered an integral part of the barrel as a vessel, then altering the cover is not making or completing a vessel.

### Opening Packages

This distinction has direct bearing on the question of opening packages and containers on *Shabbat*. The central principle emerging from the barrel-and-cover discussion is that making an opening in a separate cover that was not originally part of the main vessel as a single unit is different from making an opening in the vessel itself. If the top of a container — such as the sealed top of a can or the cap of a bottle — began as a separate unit that was subsequently fused or pressed on, there is more room to be lenient, since altering it is not making a hole in the vessel proper.

An additional important factor is disposability. If the container — or its cover — is designed for single use and will be discarded once emptied, it is harder to classify the opening as creating a *keli*, since a disposable container is not truly a vessel in the meaningful sense. Rav Moshe distinguishes further between a package that is discarded immediately upon being opened and one that is kept in use until its contents are exhausted. A glass jar that one washes out and reuses afterward is a stronger candidate for being considered a *keli*, while packaging that is immediately discarded is substantially more lenient.

Tearing packaging apart *derech kilkul* — in a destructive manner, with no interest in preserving the packaging as a functional container — is generally permitted, since the intent is to access the contents, not to fashion a vessel. Tearing open a bag of napkins, for instance, by ripping the plastic into pieces would not constitute making a *keli*.

### Barrel vs. Garment

To summarize the operative distinction: one who makes an opening in a garment that was woven as a single unit, completing for the first time a hole that is integral to the garment's function, has performed *makeh b'patish* and is liable. One who opens or punctures a separate cover that was attached to a vessel — particularly when the cover is not considered part of the vessel itself — has not made a *keli* and may be permitted to do so. The details of each case — whether the cover is truly separate, whether the object is disposable, and how the opening is made — determine the final ruling.

## Temporary Connections

### Three Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָמֵי לֵיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה לְרַבִּי זֵירָא: תְּנַן: שְׁלָל שֶׁל כּוֹבְסִין, וְשַׁלְשֶׁלֶת שֶׁל מַפְתְּחוֹת, וְהַבֶּגֶד שֶׁהוּא תָּפוּר בְּכִלְאַיִם חִיבּוּר לַטּוּמְאָה — עַד שֶׁיַּתְחִיל לְהַתִּיר. אַלְמָא שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת מְלָאכָה נָמֵי חִיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raised a contradiction before Rabbi Zeira. We learned** in a mishna: **The basting of launderers,** garments that a launderer sewed together with loose, temporary stitches to avoid losing them; **and a ring of keys; and a garment that was sewn with** a thread of **diverse kinds,** e.g., a woolen garment that was stitched with linen thread, which must be pulled out; even though they are attached only temporarily, as they will all eventually be separated, it is considered **a connection with regard to** issues of **ritual impurity.** If a source of ritual impurity comes into contact with one of the garments, they all become ritually impure, because they are treated as a single unit. This applies even though the connection is purely temporary.

Rabbi Yirmiyah raises a challenge to Rabbi Zeira from a *baraita* dealing with temporary connections. The *baraita* presents three cases. First, *kovasin* — bundles of laundered garments from multiple owners that were stitched together with a temporary thread during the laundering process, to be separated once the laundry was complete. Second, *shalshelet shel maftechot* — a keychain on which keys were bunched together temporarily, not as a permanent arrangement. Third, a garment that had been sewn together with another garment made of a different fiber combination for transport purposes, creating a temporary instance of *kilayim* — the prohibition against mixing certain fibers — with the understanding that it would be cut apart before being worn.

All three cases involve connections that are avowedly temporary: the thread will be cut, the keychain will be disassembled, and the *kilayim* joining will be undone.

### Tumah Before Undoing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* חִיבּוּר לַטּוּמְאָה — עַד שֶׁיַּתְחִיל לְהַתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** — **until one** actually **begins to untie them,** thereby indicating that he does not want them attached.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: מַקֵּל שֶׁעֲשָׂאוֹ יָד לְקוּרְדּוֹם — חִיבּוּר לַטּוּמְאָה בִּשְׁעַת מְלָאכָה. בִּשְׁעַת מְלָאכָה — אִין, שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת מְלָאכָה — לָא!
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a different mishna: With regard to **a stick that one made into an axe handle,** it is considered **a connection** between the stick and the axe **with regard to** issues of **ritual impurity when in use.** If the axe comes into contact with a source of ritual impurity, the stick also becomes ritually impure, and vice versa. By inference: Only **when** the axe is actually **in use, yes,** it is considered a connection; **when** the axe is **not in use, no,** it is not considered a connection.

The *halacha* in all three cases is: *chibbur l'tumah ad she-yatchil l'hatir* — they are considered a single connected unit for the purposes of *tumah* until one begins to undo the connection. The practical implication is that if *tumah* touches one garment in a laundered bundle, all the joined garments become *tamei*, because they are treated as a single unit. Normally, one garment cannot convey *tumah* to another separate garment, but if they are treated as one unit, contact with any part of the unit is contact with the whole. This applies even though the connection is purely temporary.

This ruling prompts a challenge from a seemingly parallel case. If one takes a stick found nearby and uses it as a makeshift handle for a hatchet head — a clearly temporary and improvised solution — that assembly is considered *chibbur l'tumah*, one unit for *tumah* purposes, but only *bish'at melacha* — during the time the hatchet is actively being used. *Shelo bish'at melacha* — once the work is done and the hatchet set aside — the stick is no longer considered connected to the hatchet head. It is as if the unit immediately reverts to its component parts once it is not in active use.

The challenge is: why does this case differ from the bundled laundry? In the laundry case, the connection is considered to persist even after the work is completed — *shelo bish'at melacha nami chibbur* — until one actively begins to undo the thread. In the hatchet case, once the work is done, the connection dissolves even without any active undoing.

### Intention Matters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת מְלָאכָה אָדָם עָשׂוּי לְזוֹרְקוֹ לְבֵין הָעֵצִים, הָכָא שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת מְלָאכָה נָמֵי נִיחָא לֵיהּ, דְּאִי מִיטַּנְּפוּ הָדַר מְחַוַּור לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira **said to** Rabbi Yirmeya: **There,** in the case of the axe, **when not in use, a person is likely to throw** the stick **into the wood** pile, as he is not particular about keeping them together. Therefore, it is not considered a connection with regard to ritual impurity. **Here,** with regard to the items listed in the first mishna, even **when not in use, he prefers** that they remain attached. In **that** way, **if they get dirty, he can launder them again,** as it is easier to wash one connected unit than several smaller swatches of fabric. Therefore, it is considered a connection with regard to ritual impurity.

Abaye resolves the contradiction by reference to intention and subjective relationship to the object. In the hatchet case, *adam asuy l'zorko l'vein ha-etzim* — a person is prepared to throw the stick into the woodpile the moment he is done working. His mind is already off the stick as a component of the hatchet as soon as he stops using it. It is mentally discarded, even if physically still attached.

In the laundry case, by contrast, *nami nicha lei* — the person actually wants the thread to remain on even after the laundering is complete. Why? Because *d'ei mitnafu, hadar mechaber lehu* — if any of the garments become soiled again, they will be laundered again as a bundle. The person has an ongoing interest in maintaining the connection. Therefore, the thread continues to be treated as a functional *chibbur* until the person actually begins to undo it.

The conceptual distinction, then, is not simply whether the work is currently being performed, but whether the person still has an active interest in maintaining the connection. Where the connection serves an ongoing potential purpose, it remains a *chibbur*. Where it has been mentally abandoned the moment the immediate task is complete, it dissolves without any physical undoing.

## Rabbi Meir Doctrine

### Oven Receptacles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּסוּרָא מַתְנוּ לַהּ לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא, בְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא מַתְנוּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: מַאן תְּנָא הָא מִלְּתָא דַּאֲמוּר רַבָּנַן כׇּל הַמְחוּבָּר לוֹ הֲרֵי הוּא כָּמוֹהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **In Sura, they taught this** following ***halakha* in the name of Rav Ḥisda; in Pumbedita, they taught** it **in the name of Rav Kahana, and some say,** it was taught **in the name of Rava: Who** is the *tanna* who **taught this matter stated by the Sages:** The status of **anything connected to** an object **is like** that of the object with regard to ritual impurity?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא. דִּתְנַן: בֵּית הַפַּךְ וּבֵית הַתַּבְלִין וּבֵית הַנֵּר שֶׁבַּכִּירָה מְטַמְּאִין בְּמַגָּע וְאֵין מְטַמְּאִין בָּאֲוִיר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מְטַהֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** The *tanna* in question **is Rabbi Meir, as we learned** in a mishna: **The receptacle** for **the cruse** of oil, **and the receptacle** for **the spices, and the receptacle** for **the lamp that are in the stove become ritually impure through contact,** i.e., if the wall of the stove becomes ritually impure through contact with a creeping animal, the receptacles also become ritually impure. However, these receptacles **do not become ritually impure through air** space, i.e., if the creeping animal were inside the stove but did not come into contact with its walls, the stove itself becomes ritually impure, but the receptacles do not; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And Rabbi Shimon deems** the receptacles **ritually pure,** even if the creeping animal came into actual contact with the stove.

The *Gemara* now asks: who is the *tanna* behind the doctrine that things which are physically connected are treated as a single unit for *tumah* purposes? This general principle — *kol ha-mechubar lo harei hu ka-moho* — underlies the entire preceding discussion. Rav Yehuda says in the name of Rav: it is Rabbi Meir.

The proof is from a *mishnah*. An oven (*kira*) had attached to it small holders or receptacles for oil (*beit ha-pach*), spices (*beit ha-tavlin*), and a lamp (*beit ha-ner*) — a design somewhat like small cup-holders affixed to the side of the oven. The question is whether these receptacles are considered part of the oven for *tumah* purposes.

Rabbi Meir rules: *mitam'in be-maga ve-ein mitam'in be-avir* — they become *tamei* through contact (i.e., if the *tamei* object physically touched either the oven or one of the receptacles, all become *tamei*), but they do not become *tamei* through *avir* (i.e., if an *impure* object was suspended in the air space of the oven without touching the base, the receptacles do not become *tamei* even though the oven itself does). Rabbi Shimon disagrees entirely, holding that the receptacles are always treated as separate from the oven and are never included in the oven's *tumah*.

### Meir vs. Shimon

The divide between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon tracks the basic question of whether physical connection of originally separate units creates a single halachic entity. Rabbi Meir holds that connection matters; Rabbi Shimon holds that the receptacles, being independently made and merely attached, remain independent.

### Air vs. Contact

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן — קָסָבַר לָאו כְּכִירָה דָּמוּ. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, אִי כְּכִירָה דָּמוּ — אֲפִילּוּ בַּאֲוִיר נָמֵי לִיטַּמּוּ. אִי לָאו כְּכִירָה דָּמוּ — אֲפִילּוּ בְּמַגָּע נָמֵי לָא לִיטַּמּוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this dispute: **Granted,** according **to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon; he holds** that these receptacles **are not** considered **like the stove** itself, and therefore they do not become ritually impure when the stove becomes ritually impure. **However,** according **to** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** it is difficult. **If** he holds that **they are** considered **like the stove** itself, then **even** if the creeping animal was **in the** stove's **air space,** the receptacles **should also become ritually impure. If** he holds that **they are not** considered **like the stove** itself, then **even** if the creeping animal came **into contact** with the stove, the receptacles **should also not become ritually impure.**

The *Gemara* raises the obvious objection: *mamma nafshach* — make up your mind. If the assembled scissors are one unit, the *haza'ah* should work by touching one component. If they are two separate units, contact with one should not convey *tumah* to the other. Why the split?

### Rabbinic Enactment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם לָאו כְּכִירָה דָּמוּ וְרַבָּנַן הוּא דִּגְזַרוּ בְּהוּ. אִי גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ, אֲפִילּוּ בָּאֲוִיר נָמֵי לִיטַּמּוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** by Torah law, the receptacles **are not** considered **like the stove** itself, **and the Sages are the ones who issued a decree** that **they** become ritually impure due to their proximity to the stove. The Gemara asks: **If** the Sages **issued a decree** that they become ritually impure, then **even** in the case where the creeping animal does not come into contact with the walls of the oven, but is merely **in** its **air** space, the receptacles **should also become ritually impure.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲבַדוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן הֶיכֵּרָא כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא אָתֵי לְמִשְׂרַף עֲלֵיהּ תְּרוּמָה וְקׇדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The Sages made a** conspicuous **distinction, so that** one will **not come to burn** his ***teruma* and** other **consecrated items because of it.** There is a severe prohibition to destroy *teruma* or consecrated items. If *teruma* becomes ritually impure, there is an obligation by Torah law to burn it; however, *teruma* that is ritually impure only by rabbinic decree is still fit by Torah law and may not be destroyed. Since there is concern that people will come to burn *teruma* even when doing so is prohibited, the Sages made a distinction, imposing ritual impurity on the receptacles only if the source of impurity came into physical contact with the walls of the stove, and not if it merely entered the stove's airspace. In that way, it is clear that the ritual impurity is by rabbinic decree, and one will not come to burn *teruma* and consecrated objects due to that impurity.

The *Gemara* resolves the difficulty: *le-olam lav ke-kira damu* — in truth, the receptacles are not biblically part of the oven — *ve-Rabbanan hu de-gazru behu* — it is the Rabbis who decreed that they be treated as part of the oven. And the Rabbis deliberately constructed this unusual split — contact creates *tumah*, *avir* does not — as a *heikher*, a marker, so that people would recognize that the connection is rabbinic, not biblical. The significance of the marker: if *terumah* comes into contact with these receptacles, one should not burn the *terumah* as if it had touched a biblically *tamei* object, since the underlying *chibbur* is only *d'rabbanan*. By making the law function in an anomalous way — different from how it would work for a genuinely single vessel — the Rabbis ensured that practitioners versed in *hilchot tumah va-taharah* would recall the rabbinic status.

This principle is notable: sometimes in the laws of *tumah* and *taharah*, a split ruling is introduced deliberately to preserve the memory that a given rule is only *d'rabbanan*, thereby preventing incorrect stringencies — such as burning *terumah* — that would be inappropriate if the *tumah* is only rabbinic in origin.

Returning to the broader principle: even Rabbi Meir, who holds that connected objects form a single unit for *tumah*, does not hold this biblically. He holds it as a rabbinic decree. It would be difficult to argue that ten sewn garments are one garment *d'oraita* — they plainly are not. The *chibbur* doctrine is rabbinic throughout, and Rabbi Meir is the *tanna* behind it.

Tosafot notes the contrast between the oven-and-receptacles case and the scissors or carpenter's plane cases to follow. The receptacles and the garments are functional independently of one another; they are combined into a single unit by physical connection. The components of scissors or a plane, by contrast, do not function independently at all — they only function when assembled together. Logically, the case for treating the assembled scissors as a single biblical unit is considerably stronger, yet even there the *Gemara* will reach a conclusion that involves rabbinic dimensions.

## Scissors & Plane

### Detachable Parts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַסְפּוֹרֶת שֶׁל פְּרָקִים וְאִיזְמֵל שֶׁל רָהִיטְנֵי חִיבּוּר לַטּוּמְאָה, וְאֵין חִיבּוּר לַהַזָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **scissors** made **of** component **parts** that are made to come apart **and the blade of a** carpenter's **plane,** which can be removed from its handle, it is considered **a connection** between the components **with regard to** contracting **ritual impurity.** If one part becomes ritually impure, the other part becomes ritually impure as well. **However,** it is **not** considered **a connection with regard to** the **sprinkling** of the water of a purification offering. When water of purification is sprinkled on these implements in order to purify them from ritual impurity contracted through contact with a corpse (see Numbers 19:17–19), the water must be sprinkled on each part individually.

A *baraita* introduces the case of *misporet shel perakim* — scissors made of detachable components, the sort that could be routinely taken apart and reassembled. Similarly, the carpenter's plane (*rehatni*) consists of a metal blade fitted into a wooden handle; both components are regularly disassembled for storage and reassembled for use.

The *halacha* stated in the *baraita* is: *chibbur l'tumah ve-ein chibbur le-haza'ah* — when assembled, these items are considered one unit for *tumah* (contact with one component conveys *tumah* to the whole), but they are not considered one unit for *haza'ah* (when sprinkling the waters of the *parah adumah* for purification, one must ensure the water contacts both components separately).

Recall that for pottery vessels, *tumah* can be conveyed through the *avir* — an impure object suspended in the air space of the vessel without touching the base renders the entire vessel *tamei*. For metal or other vessels, *tumah* is typically conveyed through contact. For the scissors or the plane, the principle is that contact with any part of the assembly renders the whole *tamei*, because the assembly is treated as one unit. But when the time comes to purify — by having the ash-water of the *parah adumah* sprinkled upon the object — the two components must each be sprinkled separately. It is insufficient to sprinkle only one.

### Tumah Haza'ah Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה נַפְשָׁךְ: אִי חִיבּוּר הוּא — אֲפִילּוּ לְהַזָּאָה נָמֵי, אִי לָאו חִיבּוּר הוּא — אֲפִילּוּ לְטוּמְאָה נָמֵי לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Whichever** way **you** look at it, there is a difficulty: **If it is** considered **a connection,** it should be so considered **even** with regard **to sprinkling;** and **if it is not** considered **a connection,** it should **not** be so considered **even** with regard **to ritual impurity.**

The *Gemara* raises the obvious objection: *mamma nafshach* — make up your mind. If the assembled scissors are one unit, the *haza'ah* should work by touching one component. If they are two separate units, contact with one should not convey *tumah* to the other. Why the split?

### Rava's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה בִּשְׁעַת מְלָאכָה חִיבּוּר — בֵּין לְטוּמְאָה בֵּין לְהַזָּאָה. וְשֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת מְלָאכָה — אֵינוֹ חִיבּוּר לֹא לְטוּמְאָה וְלֹא לְהַזָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: By Torah law, when in use,** it is considered **a connection, both** with regard **to ritual impurity** and with regard **to sprinkling. And when not in use,** even if the parts are now together, since they are made to eventually come apart and are typically dismantled, it is **neither** considered **a connection** with regard **to ritual impurity nor** with regard **to sprinkling.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְגָזְרוּ רַבָּנַן עַל טוּמְאָה שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת מְלָאכָה מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה שֶׁבִּשְׁעַת מְלָאכָה, וְעַל הַזָּאָה שֶׁבִּשְׁעַת מְלָאכָה מִשּׁוּם הַזָּאָה שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת מְלָאכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Sages issued a decree** that it should be considered a connection **with regard to ritual impurity** even **when not in use, due to ritual impurity when in use.** If one component becomes ritually impure, the other component becomes ritually impure as well. **And,** as a further stringency, they issued a decree that it is not considered a connection **with regard to sprinkling** even **when in use, due to sprinkling when not in use.** The water of purification must be sprinkled on each part individually.

Rava resolves the contradiction with a principled analysis. *Devar Torah, bish'at melacha chibbur, bein l'tumah uvein le-haza'ah* — biblically, when the scissors or plane are in active use, they are one unit, both for *tumah* and for purification. *Ve-shelo bish'at melacha, eino chibbur lo l'tumah ve-lo le-haza'ah* — when not in active use, since the components are disassembled, they are two separate units, neither for *tumah* nor for purification.

That is the biblical picture: one unit during use, two units when disassembled. But the Rabbis departed from this simple scheme in both directions simultaneously. Rabbinically, the assembled scissors are treated as one unit even *shelo bish'at melacha* — even when not in use — for the purpose of *tumah*. And rabbinically, the same scissors are treated as two separate units even *bish'at melacha* — even when in active use — for the purpose of *haza'ah*.

### Final Principle

> The result is a consistent asymmetry: *tumah* always treats the assembly as one unit, and *haza'ah* always treats it as two separate units, regardless of whether the scissors are currently in use. The logic, again, is that of rabbinic stringency applied in the direction appropriate to each law. Being strict about *tumah* means it is easier to become *tamei* — so the assembly is always one unit for *tumah*. Being strict about *taharah* means it is harder to become *tahor* — so the assembly always counts as two separate units, requiring that the purification water reach each component independently.

This produces a memorably clean final principle: always one for *tumah*, always two for *haza'ah*. The rabbinic enactment achieves this by extending each rule beyond its biblical scope in the direction of stringency and collapsing the biblical distinction based on *sha'at melacha*.

Tosafot observes that there is a meaningful conceptual difference between this case and the oven-and-receptacles case discussed earlier. The receptacles function independently of the oven; they are separately made objects that happen to be attached. The components of the scissors or plane, by contrast, have no independent function whatsoever — they are only useful together. This makes the case for treating them as a single biblical unit considerably more intuitive, and yet the *Gemara* still frames the full *chibbur* doctrine as rabbinic in its scope. The scissors case cannot serve as a basis for purification without addressing both components, because biblically when the tool is not in use, they are two objects, and the rabbinic stringency only operates to make *tumah* easier to acquire — not to make purification easier to achieve.

With this, the *Gemara*'s extended discussion of *chibbur*, *tumah*, and *haza'ah* — which branched out from the opening themes of *hatmanah* and the making of vessels — concludes. Tomorrow the discussion returns to *hatmanah* and the laws of *Shabbat*.
