# Rosh Hashanah 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Orlah & Planting

### Rosh Hashanah Intro

The daf opens with a *baraita* addressing the question of *Rosh Hashanah* for planting — *netia* — which is distinct from the role of the first of *Tishrei* in other agricultural contexts. As we have seen, the first of *Tishrei* serves as the *Rosh Hashanah* for *shmita* and *yovel* in the sense that those cycles are calibrated to the calendar year: what year of the *shmita* cycle is it? What year of the *yovel* cycle? Those are universal determinations that apply to everyone equally.

The question of *netia* is different. It concerns the age of a particular tree that a particular person planted: how many years old is this tree? The Torah in *Vayikra* establishes that the fruit of a tree is forbidden as *orlah* for its first three years, that the fourth year's fruit carries the status of *neta revai* — which must be redeemed and consumed in *Yerushalayim*, or the fruit itself brought and eaten there — and that only from the fifth year onward may the fruit be eaten without restriction. One might have assumed that "three years" means three years from the tree's planting date, counted day to day. The *Mishna*, however, teaches that *Tishrei* — *Rosh Hashanah* — is the point at which a tree's age increments. A tree planted even a month before *Rosh Hashanah* already enters its second year when *Rosh Hashanah* arrives. In theory, a tree planted on *Rosh Chodesh Elul* would complete its three years of *orlah* in a little over two calendar years. The fruit of *orlah* is not merely forbidden for consumption; it is forbidden even in benefit — one may do nothing with it whatsoever.

### Three Planting Methods

The *baraita* begins: *echad hanotea* — whether one plants directly; *ve'echad hamavrich* — or performs *mavrich*, the technique of bending a branch underground so that it emerges from the earth at a new location and effectively becomes a new tree, while still drawing nutrients from the original; *ve'echad hamarkiv* — or performs *markiv*, grafting, which joins a branch to a different root system. All three methods are treated as constituting a new planting for the purposes of *orlah*.

*Tosafot* notes a complexity regarding *markiv*: other sources suggest that bending a branch without severing it from the original tree does not constitute a new planting and that *orlah* does not apply. *Tosafot* resolves this by distinguishing between a case where the connection to the original tree is never cut — in which case the "new" growth is really just a branch extending in an unusual direction and *orlah* does not apply — and a case where the connection is eventually severed. When we say that *markiv* constitutes a new planting, we refer to the latter case, where the branch is ultimately cut off from its source.

### 30-Day Rule

The *baraita* continues: if one performs any of these three acts of planting *erev shevi'it* — the eve of the *shmita* year — at least thirty days before *Rosh Hashanah*, then *altah lo shana* — it counts as a year, meaning that when *Rosh Hashanah* arrives, the tree is already considered to have entered its second year for *orlah* purposes. Such a tree may also remain in the ground during *shvi'it*. But if the planting occurred fewer than thirty days before *Rosh Hashanah* — that is, from *Rosh Chodesh Elul* or later, since *Elul* has twenty-nine days — then it does not count as a year, the tree remains in its first year when *Rosh Hashanah* arrives, and it must be uprooted during *shmita*.

At this stage of the *Gemara's* analysis, the thirty-day requirement is provisionally understood as serving two purposes simultaneously: for *shmita*, it reflects the concept of *tosefet shvi'it* — one must add time to the *shmita* year and refrain from prohibited agricultural activity before *shmita* actually begins, and this *tosefet* period is thirty days. For *orlah*, it reflects the requirement that enough time elapse before *Rosh Hashanah* for *Rosh Hashanah* to meaningfully increment the tree's year. As we will see, the *Gemara* will ultimately revise this understanding.

### Fruit Restrictions

The *Gemara* then introduces a striking and counterintuitive *halakha* on the top of 11a. Although *Rosh Hashanah* increments the tree's year at the front end, there is a corresponding extension at the back end: *u'feiros netia zo asurim ad Tu B'Shvat* — the fruits of this planting remain forbidden until *Tu B'Shvat*, the fifteenth of *Shevat*, following the view of *Beis Hillel*. Specifically, if the tree is in its third year and the *orlah* prohibition is still operative, that prohibition extends beyond the *Rosh Hashanah* that nominally marks the entry into the fourth year and continues until *Tu B'Shvat*. Likewise, the *neta revai* status of the fourth year extends into what would otherwise be the fifth year, remaining in effect until the next *Tu B'Shvat*.

The logic is as follows. For its first three years, a tree has the status of *netia* — a sapling — and its years are counted by *Rosh Hashanah* in *Tishrei*. But once it has completed three years, it becomes an *ilan* — a full-grown tree — and for an *ilan*, the *Rosh Hashanah* is *Tu B'Shvat*. Thus there is a peculiar transitional moment: the tree is no longer a *netia* but not yet at its *Rosh Hashanah* as an *ilan*. The third year's *orlah* therefore continues until *Tu B'Shvat*, and the *revai* of the fourth year likewise continues to the following *Tu B'Shvat*.

## Orlah/Reva'i Calendar

### Tu B'Shvat Transition

To illustrate concretely: suppose a tree is planted roughly one month before *Rosh Hashanah*. When *Rosh Hashanah* arrives, the tree enters year two. The following *Rosh Hashanah* brings it to year three. The third *Rosh Hashanah* would, on a simple reading, bring it into year four and free it from *orlah*. But since the tree is now an *ilan* and its new year runs from *Tu B'Shvat*, the *orlah* of the third year continues until *Tu B'Shvat* of what is calendrically year four. Only at that *Tu B'Shvat* does the *revai* status take effect, and only at the following *Tu B'Shvat* does the tree become fully *mutar* — permitted without restriction.

### Misalignment Issue

One might observe that this arrangement seems less favorable than simply using *Tu B'Shvat* as the *Rosh Hashanah* for the tree throughout its entire life. If *Tu B'Shvat* governed from the beginning, the years would count cleanly from *Tu B'Shvat* to *Tu B'Shvat*, and the tree's fruit would become permitted at the same point it currently does. Moreover, the mixed system that begins with *Rosh Hashanah* and then extends to *Tu B'Shvat* is actually disadvantageous compared to a pure *Tu B'Shvat* system. Consider a tree planted between *Rosh Hashanah* and *Tu B'Shvat*: under the current system, *Rosh Hashanah* has already passed and cannot increment the tree's year until the following *Rosh Hashanah*, so the tree begins year two relatively late. Under a pure *Tu B'Shvat* system, however, the first *Tu B'Shvat* after planting would already bring the tree into year two, giving it more credit. Thus the current arrangement — counting from *Rosh Hashanah* at the front but extending to *Tu B'Shvat* at the back — is actually the least advantageous of the possible configurations. One suggested rationale is that *Tu B'Shvat* ensures that a full rainy season has been experienced; planting before *Tu B'Shvat* but well into the rainy season might not provide that assurance, and hence some extension makes sense.

### Biblical Sources

The *Gemara* asks: *minalan hani mili* — what is the source for all of this? Rabbi Chiya bar Abba says in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, with some uncertainty as to whether this tradition comes via the name of Rabbi Yanai, that the source is in *Vayikra*. The verse says: *shalosh shanim yihyeh lachem arelim lo ye'achel* — for three years it shall be *orlah*, it shall not be eaten — *u'vashanah ha'revi'it* — and in the fourth year. The *Gemara* reads the conjunction *u'* connecting the fourth year to the prior verse as indicating that the forbidden status does not simply end when the third year ends at *Rosh Hashanah*, but extends *into* the fourth year — specifically until *Tu B'Shvat*. Similarly, the verse continues: *u'vashanah ha'chamishit tochal et piryo* — and in the fifth year you shall eat its fruit — with the *revai* status similarly extending into the fifth year until its *Tu B'Shvat*. The *Gemara* summarizes: there are times when one is already calendrically within the fourth year — after the third *Rosh Hashanah* but before *Tu B'Shvat* — yet the fruit is still forbidden as *orlah*. And there are times when one is already calendrically within the fifth year yet the fruit still carries the status of *revai*.

## 30-Day Debate

### Rabbi Meir: One Day

The *Gemara* now turns to the thirty-day requirement and asks: *leima de-lo ke-Rabbi Meir* — shall we say this ruling does not accord with Rabbi Meir? For Rabbi Meir holds that *yom echad ba-shanah chashuv shanah* — one day within a year counts as a full year. We have already encountered this principle in the context of kings' regnal years. The *Gemara* adduces a parallel from a *baraita* concerning the ages of sacrificial animals.

The Torah uses several terms for cattle: *egel* — a calf — alone; *egel ben bakar* or simply *ben bakar*; and *par* or *par ben bakar*. Rabbi Meir holds that wherever the Torah says *egel* alone, the animal must be in its first year; where it says *ben bakar*, it must be in its second year; and where it says *par*, it must be in its third year. Accordingly, *par ha-amur ba-Torah stam* — a *par* mentioned in the Torah without further qualification — must be *ben esrim ve-arba'ah chodesh ve-yom echad*, twenty-four months and one day old. That is, according to Rabbi Meir, once the animal has entered its third year by even one day, it is halachically a three-year-old. Rabbi Eliezer, by contrast, requires *ben esrim ve-arba'ah chodesh u-shloshim yom* — twenty-four months and thirty days — before it may be called a three-year-old *par*. One day is insufficient.

It is worth noting a conceptual question that arises here: are we saying that the animal *is* three years old, which one day serves to establish, or are we saying that the defining criterion is simply being *into* one's third year? Rabbi Meir's position as understood by the *Gemara* is that the animal is defined as a *ben shalosh* — a three-year-old — and that one day into the third year is sufficient to confer that definition.

By analogy, if one day at the back end suffices to make an animal a three-year-old according to Rabbi Meir, then presumably, by the same logic, one day of planting before *Rosh Hashanah* should suffice to increment the tree's year. Yet the *Mishna* requires thirty days — apparently contradicting Rabbi Meir.

### Rava's Kal Vachomer

The *Gemara* responds: *afillu teima Rabbi Meir* — one may still attribute this ruling to Rabbi Meir. When did Rabbi Meir say one day counts? *Be-sof shanah* — at the end of a year. But *bit-chilat shanah lo amar* — he did not say so at the beginning of a year. It may be that a partial day at the end of a counting period, which marks the completion of that period, carries different weight than a partial period at the very start of a new count.

Rava challenges this distinction with a *kal vachomer*: *velav kal vachomer hu?* If anything, a partial period at the *beginning* should be easier to count than at the end, not harder. His proof is from the laws of *niddah*.

### Niddah Example

Rava's argument proceeds as follows. In the Torah's framework of *niddah*, a woman who begins menstruating counts seven days from the onset of bleeding, after which she immerses in a *mikvah* on the night following the seventh day. Consider a woman who begins bleeding at 1:00 PM on Sunday. That Sunday counts as day one of her seven days — even though only a portion of Sunday remained when the bleeding began. Yet when she reaches the beginning of the seventh day, *Shabbos*, the mere onset of *Shabbos* does not count as the completion of day seven; she must wait until *Shabbos* ends to immerse. The beginning of day seven does not discharge the final day.

Thus: *she'ein t'chilat ha-yom oleh lah be-sofah* — the start of a day does not count for her at the end — yet *sof ha-yom oleh lah bit-chilatah* — the end of a day *does* count for her at the beginning. A few hours of Sunday at the start of the count are sufficient to constitute day one, but the arrival of *Shabbos* is not sufficient to constitute day seven. This demonstrates that it is *easier* to count a partial unit at the beginning of a process than at the end. Therefore — *eino din* — if by cattle we allow one day at the end to count as a full year, how much more so by trees should we allow one day at the beginning to count as a year. Rabbi Meir should therefore not require thirty days.

*Rashi* cautions against a common misapplication of this passage. Some have erroneously inferred from the principle that *sof ha-yom oleh lah bit-chilatah* that the final hours of the day on which bleeding ceases can count as the first of the seven clean days. This is entirely incorrect; the *Gemara* here is discussing the biblical counting of seven days from the onset of bleeding, not the rabbinic system of seven clean days following the cessation of bleeding — two entirely distinct frameworks.

### Trees vs. Cows

One might ask: if the *kal vachomer* from *niddah* shows that beginnings are easier than ends, why do we not count a partial period at both the beginning *and* the end for cattle? The answer is that cattle and trees operate on fundamentally different calendrical systems. For cattle, the count runs from the animal's birthday: how many years have elapsed since the date of birth? The only opportunity for a partial-count credit is at the back end, when the animal has completed two full years and enters its third. There is no "beginning" in the same sense, because the count simply starts at birth. For trees, by contrast, the count is governed by a specific calendar date — *Rosh Hashanah* — which increments the tree's year. Here, the only opportunity for partial-count credit is at the front end: did the tree get planted before that date, and if so, does even a small amount of time before the date suffice to allow *Rosh Hashanah* to increment it into year two? The analogy from *niddah* is being used to establish the principle that partial periods are easier to credit at the start of a process than at its end, and this principle is then applied: if cattle get credit for one day at the end, trees should certainly get credit for one day at the beginning.

### Rabbi Eliezer: Rooting T…

The *Gemara* then turns to Rabbi Eliezer's position. If the ruling requiring thirty days before *Rosh Hashanah* accords with Rabbi Eliezer — who requires thirty days for the cattle to be considered a *par* — then, the *Gemara* objects, one would need *shloshim u-shloshim*, sixty days in total. Why? Because there is a separate consideration for trees that does not apply to cattle: a newly planted tree must first *take root* — *klita* — before it can be considered planted in any meaningful sense. Only after it has rooted does the clock begin running on its time in the ground.

This principle emerges from a *baraita* dealing with *shmita*: *ein note'in ve'ein mavrichin ve'ein markivin erev shvi'it pachos mi-lamed yom lifnei Rosh Hashanah* — one may not plant, bend, or graft within thirty days before *Rosh Hashanah* of *shmita*, and if one did, *ya'akor* — it must be uprooted. Rabbi Yehuda says: for *markiv*, only three days are required to take root; if it has rooted more than three days before *shmita*, it need not be uprooted. Rabbi Yossi and Rabbi Shimon say: two weeks suffice for rooting.

These opinions concern how long it takes for a planted tree to become genuinely rooted in the earth. *Tosafot* reads the *Gemara*'s conclusion as abandoning the earlier assumption that the thirty-day pre-*shmita* period reflects *tosefet shvi'it*. Rather, the concern is purely practical: one may not plant a tree in such a way that it will take root *on shmita* itself. The only question is therefore how long rooting takes. The first opinion holds thirty days; Rabbi Yehuda holds three days; Rabbi Yossi and Rabbi Shimon hold two weeks.

The same principle applies to *orlah*. Rav Nachman says in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha: *le-divrei ha-omer lamed* — according to the opinion that rooting takes thirty days — *tzarich lamed u-shloshim* — for *orlah* purposes one needs thirty days for rooting plus another thirty days to count as a year, a total of sixty days before *Rosh Hashanah*. According to the opinion that rooting takes two weeks, one needs two weeks plus thirty days before *Rosh Hashanah*. Even according to Rabbi Yehuda who requires only three days for rooting, one would need thirty-three days, not thirty. How then does the *Mishna* say that thirty days suffices?

## Reconciling 30 Days

### The 30-Day Problem

The problem is sharp: whatever opinion we adopt regarding rooting time, it would seem that thirty days alone cannot satisfy both the rooting requirement and the additional time needed for *Rosh Hashanah* to meaningfully increment the tree's year. Even the most lenient opinion — Rabbi Yehuda's three days for rooting — would require thirty-three days total before *Rosh Hashanah*. The *Mishna*'s thirty-day rule appears insufficient on any account.

### Double-Duty Solution

The *Gemara* resolves this by reversing the initial assumption. Rather than saying that the thirty-day requirement reflects *tosefet* or the time needed to count as a year, the *Gemara* concludes: *ela le-olam Rabbi Meir* — in fact, this *baraita* accords with Rabbi Meir. *Ve-chi ka'amar lamed li-klita* — when the *baraita* says thirty days before *Rosh Hashanah*, it refers entirely to the time needed for rooting. The first-opinion *baraita* holds that rooting takes thirty days; this is also the position assumed in the *shmita* context. And Rabbi Meir, who requires only one day to count as a year, provides the solution.

But if one needs thirty days for rooting *and* then at least one additional day to count as a year, one would need thirty-one days, not thirty. The *Gemara* answers: *kasavar yom sheloshim oleh le-kan u-le-kan* — the thirtieth day does double duty. Consider a tree planted on the last day of *Av*. Day thirty is the twenty-ninth of *Elul*. During the first portion of that thirtieth day, the rooting process completes — the tree is now genuinely planted. During the remaining hours of that same day, it has already begun its count as a rooted tree, and those hours constitute the "one day" before *Rosh Hashanah* that Rabbi Meir requires. When *Rosh Hashanah* arrives, the tree has both rooted and had at least one day of counted time, and *Rosh Hashanah* can therefore increment it into year two. The last day of the thirty-day period performs dual service: it is simultaneously the final day of the rooting process and the first day of the year-count.

### Shmita Conclusion

This resolution also clarifies the *shmita* ruling. The reason one must uproot a tree planted within thirty days of *shmita* is not *tosefet* — it is not that one has transgressed the prohibition of agricultural labor during the *tosefet* period. The reason is that the tree will take root *on shmita* itself. If a tree planted within thirty days has not yet rooted by *Rosh Hashanah*, it will root during *shvi'it*, which is prohibited. Accordingly, *tosefet* is a separate concern and does not drive the uprooting requirement; the uprooting requirement is driven solely by whether the tree roots before or during *shmita*. As *Tosafot* reads the *Gemara*, these two considerations — the *tosefet* prohibition and the rooting prohibition — are independent, and violating *tosefet* does not automatically trigger uprooting if the tree nevertheless roots before *shmita* begins.

## Prooftext Debate

### Rabbi Meir: Genesis

The *Gemara* now introduces a more fundamental textual basis for the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Eliezer. Rabbi Yochanan observes: *bi-shteihem mikra echad darshu* — both derived their positions from the same verse. The verse in question is from the flood narrative in *Bereishit*: *vayehi b'achat u-shesh me'ot shanah ba-rishon b'echad la-chodesh charevu ha-mayim me'al ha-aretz* — it came to pass in the six hundred and first year, in the first month, on the first of the month, that the waters dried from upon the earth.

Rabbi Meir derives his position from the phrase *b'achat* — on the first, the first day. The verse describes this as occurring in the six hundred and first year. From the fact that one day of the year — the very first day — is described as constituting or characterizing that year, Rabbi Meir infers *shema mina yom echad ba-shanah chashuv shanah* — one day within a year counts as a full year.

The logical force of this derivation is admittedly puzzling. The verse is simply saying that this occurred during the six hundred and first year — it is not claiming that 601 full years have elapsed. Nevertheless, Rabbi Meir reads it as though the single day mentioned is treated as though it represents the entire year, yielding the principle that one day in a year is counted as a year.

### Rabbi Eliezer: Genesis

Rabbi Eliezer derives from the same verse a very different lesson. He focuses on the phrase *ba-rishon b'echad la-chodesh* — in the first month, on the first of the month. His reading: *yom echad hu de-aya ba-chodesh ve-ka kara lei chodesh* — it is only one day into the month, yet the verse calls it a month. From this he infers *yom echad ba-chodesh chashuv chodesh* — one day within a month counts as a full month.

Here too the reading is strained: the verse is stating the date — the first of the month — not claiming that the month is complete. Yet Rabbi Eliezer reads it as conferring upon that one day the status of a full month.

### Day-Month-Year Analogy

Having established from the verse that one day counts as a month, Rabbi Eliezer proceeds by analogy. The relationship of a day to a month parallels the relationship of a month to a year. Just as a month is composed of days, and one unit (a day) suffices to count as the whole, so too a year is composed of months, and one unit (a month) suffices to count as the whole. Therefore *shloshim yom ba-shanah chashuvim shanah* — thirty days within a year count as a full year. The number thirty is used because a month always has at least thirty days — a month is by definition at least thirty days — and therefore a month, as the operative unit of a year, is represented by thirty days.

One might ask why we say thirty specifically rather than, say, twenty-nine, since a lunar month can be twenty-nine days. The implicit answer is that thirty days represents a complete, round month, the minimum period that is unambiguously a full month.

## Eliezer vs. Yehoshua

### Rabbi Eliezer Timeline

The *Gemara* has been working with verses from the flood narrative — the six hundred and first year, the first month, the first of the month — in establishing the debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Eliezer. This naturally raises the question of what month that "first month" refers to. The flood narrative pre-dates the establishment of *Nissan* as the first month in the context of the Egyptian exodus, so the question of whether the Torah's original month-count runs from *Tishrei* or from *Nissan* becomes directly relevant to how we read those very verses. The *Gemara* accordingly turns to a *baraita* in which this dispute is addressed directly.

Rabbi Eliezer says: *bi-Tishrei nivra ha-olam* — the world was created in *Tishrei*. *Bi-Tishrei noldu avos* — the forefathers were born in *Tishrei*. *Bi-Tishrei mesu avos* — the forefathers died in *Tishrei*, the assumption being that they lived out complete years and therefore died in the same month they were born. *Bi-Pesach nolad Yitzchak* — Yitzchak was born on *Pesach*, which constitutes an exception to the *Tishrei* pattern. *Bi-Tishrei asidin lehiga'el* — the future redemption will occur in *Tishrei*.

### Shared Remembrances

Rabbi Eliezer holds that *be-Rosh Hashanah nifkeda Sarah Rachel ve-Channah* — on *Rosh Hashanah*, Sarah, Rachel, and Hannah were *nifkadu* — remembered by God, meaning they became pregnant. This is why we read those passages on *Rosh Hashanah*: it is a day of divine remembrance and response. Sarah was remembered on *Rosh Hashanah*, conceived, and gave birth six months later on *Pesach* — hence Yitzchak's birth on *Pesach* rather than *Tishrei*. *Be-Rosh Hashanah yatza Yosef mi-beis ha-asurim* — on *Rosh Hashanah* Yosef was released from prison, as Pharaoh remembered him on that day. *Be-Rosh Hashanah batla avoda me-avoseinu be-Mitzrayim* — on *Rosh Hashanah* the labor imposed upon our ancestors in Egypt ceased, even before the actual redemption.

*Bi-Nissan nigalu* — they were redeemed in *Nissan*. *Bi-Tishrei asidin lehiga'el* — the future redemption will occur in *Tishrei*.

According to Rabbi Eliezer, then, *Tishrei* is the primary month for virtually everything: creation, the births and deaths of the forefathers, the future redemption, and the foundational acts of divine memory and liberation. The only exceptions that must be assigned to *Nissan* are the actual exodus itself and Yitzchak's birth — the latter being forced by the prior commitment to *Rosh Hashanah* as the time of Sarah's *pikida*.

### Rabbi Yehoshua Timeline

Rabbi Yehoshua disagrees, assigning primacy to *Nissan*. *Bi-Nissan nivra ha-olam* — the world was created in *Nissan*. *Bi-Nissan noldu avos* — the forefathers were born in *Nissan*. *Bi-Nissan mesu avos* — they died in *Nissan*. *Bi-Pesach nolad Yitzchak* — Yitzchak was born on *Pesach*. Both Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua agree on this point. And because Yitzchak was born on *Pesach*, Rabbi Yehoshua likewise agrees that: *be-Rosh Hashanah nifkeda Sarah Rachel ve-Channah* — Sarah, Rachel, and Hannah were remembered on *Rosh Hashanah*. Both Tannaim share this conclusion, since it follows necessarily from Yitzchak's birth on *Pesach*. Rabbi Yehoshua also agrees that *be-Rosh Hashanah yatza Yosef mi-beis ha-asurim* — Yosef was freed on *Rosh Hashanah* — and that *be-Rosh Hashanah batla avoda me-avoseinu be-Mitzrayim* — the forced labor in Egypt ceased on *Rosh Hashanah*. These are grounded in the idea of *Rosh Hashanah* as a day of *zikaron* — divine remembrance — which both Tannaim accept.

The key difference is at the cosmic and eschatological level: *bi-Nissan nigalu u-vi-Nissan asidin lehiga'el* — they were redeemed in *Nissan* and the future redemption will also occur in *Nissan*. Rabbi Yehoshua thus presents a more complex picture: the primary rhythms of creation, the lives of the forefathers, and the ultimate redemption all center on *Nissan*, yet certain specific events — the *pikida* of Sarah, Rachel, and Hannah; the release of Yosef; the cessation of Egyptian servitude — are nonetheless assigned to *Rosh Hashanah* in *Tishrei*, reflecting that month's enduring character as a time of divine attention and response. The full elaboration of how these positions are grounded in the verses of the flood narrative will be the subject of the next daf.
