# Gittin 69 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blood/Migraine Remedies

### Blood in Head

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִדְמָא דְּרֵישָׁא – לַיְתֵי שׁוּרְבִּינָא, וּבִינָא, וְאָסָא דָּרָא, וְזֵיתָא, וְחִילְפָא, וְחִילְפֵי דְיַמָּא, וְיַבְלָא; וְלִישְׁלוֹקִינְהוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי; וְלִנְטוֹל תְּלָת מְאָה כָּסֵי אַהַאי גִּיסָא דְרֵישָׁא, וּתְלָת מְאָה כָּסֵי אַהַאי גִּיסָא דְרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the discussion concerning the different remedies with which the chapter began: As a remedy **for** a headache caused by excessive **blood in the head, let him bring cypress [*shurvina*], willow, fresh myrtle [*asa dara*], olive, poplar, sea willow, and cynodon** grass **and boil them together. And he should pour three hundred cups** of this liquid **on one side of his head and three hundred cups on this,** the other **side of his head.**

The daf opens in the middle of an extended discussion of various *refuot* — medical treatments and remedies — that the Gemara presents in connection with a Mishnah dealing with a particular figure. We pick up following a remarkable *agadata* concerning Shlomo HaMelech and Ashmedai, and now arrive at the practical medical material beginning near the bottom of 68b.

*L'dama d'reisha* — for blood in the head — the Gemara prescribes an elaborate herbal preparation. One should bring *shorvina*, *vina*, fresh myrtle (*asa*), olives, poplar, sea willow, and cynodon, and boil them all together. The resulting mixture is then poured in 300 cups over one side of the head and 300 cups over the other side. Should this prove insufficient, one brings a white rose — specifically one where all the petals grow on a single side, as Rashi explains — boils it, and pours 60 cups of that mixture over each side of the head.

### Migraine Treatment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִצְלִיחְתָּא – לַיְתֵי תַּרְנְגוֹלָא בָּרָא; וְלִישְׁחֲטֵיהּ בְּזוּזָא חִיוָּרָא – אַהָהוּא גִּיסָא דְּכָיֵיב לֵיהּ, וְנִזְדְּהַר מִדְּמֵיהּ דְּלָא לְסַמִּינְהוּ לְעֵינֵיהּ; וְלִיתְלְיֵיהּ בְּסֵיפָא דְבָבָא – דְּכִי עָיֵיל חָיֵיף בֵּיהּ, וְכִי נָפֵיק חָיֵיף בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** As a remedy **for a migraine, let him bring a wild rooster and slaughter it using a silver dinar,** so that the blood flows **over the side** of his head **that hurts him. And he should be careful of its blood** so **as not to blind his eye. And he should hang it on the doorpost** of his house, **so that when he enters he rubs against it and when he exits he rubs against it.**

For *tzilchata* — which Rashi describes as a pain in half of the head, and which Koren translates as a migraine — the treatment is of a different character entirely. One takes a wild rooster and slaughters it with a sharpened silver coin (*zuza chivra*) on the side of the head where the pain is located. One must take great care that the blood does not enter the eyes, as Rashi warns it will cause blindness. The carcass is then hung on the doorpost so that one brushes up against it both upon entering and upon leaving the house, and that contact is understood to provide the therapeutic benefit.

## Eye Diseases

### Cataracts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִבְרוּקְתִּי – לַיְתֵי עַקְרַבָּא דְּשַׁב חוּמְרֵי, וּנְיַיבְּשֵׁיהּ בְּטוּלָּא; וְנִישְׁחוֹק תַּרְתֵּי מְנָתָא כּוּחְלָא, וַחֲדָא מְנָתָא מִינֵּיהּ; וְלִימְלֵי תַּלְתָּא מַכְחָלֵי בְּהַאי עֵינָא וּתְלָתָא מַכְחָלֵי בְּהַאי עֵינָא; טְפֵי לָא לִימַלֵּי, דְּאִי לָא – פָּקַע עֵינֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § As a remedy **for eye disease [*beroketi*], let him bring** the body of **a seven-colored scorpion, dry it in the shade, and grind** together **two portions of stibium and one portion of** the dried scorpion. **And let him place three eyebrushes full in this eye, and three eyebrushes** full **in that eye. But he should not place more, as if** he does **not** heed this advice and he does place more, **his eye will burst.**

At the top of 69a, the Gemara turns to eye diseases. For *frukita* — an eye ailment that Rashi identifies and which appears to correspond to something like cataracts — the treatment involves a scorpion. One takes a seven-colored scorpion, which Rashi understands to mean a very old one, and dries it out in the shade. One then grinds together two portions of *kuchla* — the substance used for eye application — and one portion of the powdered scorpion. Three applications of this mixture are placed in each eye using an eyebrush. The Gemara adds a clear warning: no more than three applications should be used, because too much will cause the eye to burst.

### Night Blindness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְשַׁבְרִירֵי דְלֵילְיָא – נֵיתֵי שׁוּדְרָא בָּרְקָא, וְנֵיסַר חֲדָא כַּרְעָא מִינֵּיהּ וַחֲדָא כַּרְעָא מִכַּלְבָּא; וְנִיטְרְפוּ יָנוֹקֵי חַסְפָּא אַבָּתְרֵיהּ, וְלֵימְרוּ לֵיהּ: "אַסָּא כַּלְבָּא אַכְסָא תַּרְנְגוֹלָא"; וְלִיגְבֵּי שַׁב אוּמְצֵי מִשִּׁבְעָה בָּתֵּי, וְלִיתְּבִינְהוּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ בְּצִינּוֹרָא דְּדַשָּׁא, וְנֵיכְלִינְהוּ בְּקִלְקְולֵי דְּמָתָא. בָּתַר הָכִי, לִפְשׁוֹט שׁוּדְרָא בָּרְקָא, וְנֵימְרוּ הָכִי: "שַׁבְרִירֵי דִּפְלוֹנִי בַּר פְּלוֹנִיתָא (שַׁבְקִינְהוּ) [לִישְׁבְּקוּהּ] לִפְלוֹנִי בַּר פְּלוֹנִיתָא, וְלִיחֲרוּ לְכַלְבָּא בְּבָבִיתָא דְּעֵינֵיה".
>
> **Translation:** As a remedy **for night blindness, he should bring a rope of** animal **hair [*shudra barka*], and he should tie one of his legs and one leg of a dog** together with the rope. **And let children throw** pottery **shards behind him, and let them say to him: The dog is old** and **the rooster is foolish. And let him take seven** pieces of **raw meat from seven houses and let him leave them for him in the socket of the door. And let him** then **eat them in the junkyard of the city. Afterward, he should untie the rope of hair and say as follows: The blindness of so-and-so, son of so-and-so; leave so-and-so, son of so-and-so; and** then they say: **Let them blow into the dog's eye.**

For *shavrire de-leilya* — night blindness — the Gemara prescribes an elaborate ritual that combines physical actions with incantation. One brings a rope made of animal hair, ties one end to one's own leg and the other end to the leg of a dog, and has a child follow behind banging on a pottery shard while reciting that the dog is old and the rooster is foolish. Seven pieces of raw meat are then collected from seven different houses, with the owners of each house placing the meat into the socket of their doorpost. The patient eats these pieces at the refuse heap of the city. Afterward, the rope is untied, and an incantation is recited in which the blindness of so-and-so son of so-and-so is declared to have departed from the patient and to have passed instead into the eyes of the dog. The treatment concludes by blowing into the pupils of the dog's eyes.

It is worth noting that the Gemara here clearly incorporates what it understood as incantation alongside physical remedies — presumably invoking some force associated with *shedim* or related phenomena. The Gemara evidently did not regard all of this as purely medical science in our sense of the term.

### Day Blindness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְשַׁבְרִירֵי דִימָמָא – לַיְתֵי שִׁבְעָה סוּמָּקֵי מִגַּוּוֹא דְחֵיוְתָא, וְנִיטְוִינְהוּ אַחַסְפָּא דְאוּמָּנָא; וְלִיתֵיב אִיהוּ מִגַּוַּאי וְאִינִישׁ אַחֲרִינָא מֵאַבָּרַאי, וְנֵימָא לֵיהּ עַוִּירָא: "הַב לִי דְּאֵיכוֹל", וְנֵימָא לֵיהּ הַאיְךְ פְּתִיחָא: "סַב אֵיכוֹל"; וּבָתַר דְּאָכֵיל לִיתְבְּרֵיהּ לְחַסְפָּא, דְּאִי לָא – הָדְרִי עִילָּוֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** As a remedy **for day blindness,** hemeralopia, which is the inability to see clearly in bright light, **let him bring seven animal spleens, and let** the patient **roast them on** the pottery **shard of a bloodletter. And let** the patient **sit on the inside** of the house **and** let **another person** sit **on the outside, and let** the patient **say to** himself: **Blind man, give me** something **to eat, and let the other** one **say to** the patient: **Seeing man, take** and **eat. And after** the patient **eats, let him break the shard, as if not,** the blindness **will follow him.**

For *shavrire de-yami* — day blindness — one takes seven animal spleens from the innards of animals and roasts them on the pottery vessel used by a bloodletter to collect blood. The patient sits inside the house while another person sits outside. The blind person calls out to the outside person asking for food, and the outside person responds, handing him the meat to eat. After eating, the pottery must be broken, because if it is not, the condition will return.

## Nosebleed Remedies

### Nosebleed Treatment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִדְמָא דְּאָתֵי מִנְּחִירָא – לֵיתֵי גַּבְרָא כֹּהֵן דִּשְׁמֵיהּ לֵוִי, וְלִיכְתּוֹב לֵיהּ לֵוִי לְמַפְרֵעַ. וְאִי לָא – לַיְתֵי אִינִישׁ מֵעָלְמָא, וְנִיכְתּוֹב לֵיהּ: "אֲנָא פַּפֵּי שֵׁילָא בַּר סוּמָּקֵי" – לְמַפְרֵעַ.
>
> **Translation:** As a remedy **for a nosebleed, let** the patient **bring a man named Levi** who is **a priest, and** that man **should write for** the patient the name **Levi, backward. And if** the patient is **not** able do this **let him bring an ordinary man, and let** that man **write for** the patient the following expression: **I am Pappi Sheila bar Summakei.** And that man should also write this **backward.**

For *dama de-asi mi-nichiri* — a nosebleed — the Gemara offers a graduated series of remedies of increasing complexity. First, a Kohen whose name is Levi should write the word *Levi* backwards. If that does not help, any ordinary man should write the phrase *ana Papi Shila bar Sumkei* — "I am Papi Shila bar Sumkei" — backwards. If that too fails, he should write the following words: *aton deli bemei chasaf, aton deli bemei pgam* — formulaic phrases about drawing from a bucket in silver water and in spoiled water.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא – נִיכְתּוֹב לֵיהּ הָכִי: טַעַם דְּלִי בְּמֵי כֶסֶף, טַעַם דְּלִי בְּמֵי פְגָם. וְאִי לָא – לַיְתֵי עִיקָּרָא דְאַסְפַּסְתָּא, וְאַשְׁלָא דְפוּרְיָיא עַתִּיקָא, וְקוּרְטְסָא, וּמוֹרִיקֵי, וְסוּמָּקָא דְלוּלִיבָּא; וְנִקְלִינְהוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי; וְלַיְתֵי גְּבָבָא דְעַמְרָא וְנִיגְדּוֹל תַּרְתֵּי פְּתִילָתָא, וְלִטְמִישׁ בְּחַלָּא, וְנִיגַדְבֵּל בְּקִיטְמָא הָדֵין, וְנִיתֵּיב בִּנְחִירֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** he is **not** able to do this **let** that man **write for** the patient **like this: Taste from a bucket in silver water, taste from a bucket in tainted water. And if** the patient is **not** able to do this **let him bring the root of fodder [*aspasta*], rope [*ashla*] from an old bed, and paper [*kurtesa*], and saffron, and the red** portion **of a palm branch, and let him burn them with each other. And let** the patient **bring a fleece of wool and spin** it into **two strings, and let him soak [*litmish*] them in vinegar, roll them in this ash** of the burned materials, **and place** them **in his nostrils.**

If none of these inscriptions succeeds, the treatment becomes more physically involved. One takes the root of fodder, a rope from an old bed, a type of paper, saffron, and the red of a palm branch, burns them all together, takes a cluster of wool, spins two threads from it, soaks them in vinegar, rolls them in the ashes of the burned mixture, and places them in the nostrils. This is the first remedy in the sequence that involves actually introducing something into the nose.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא – לִיחְזֵי אַמַּת הַמַּיִם דְּאָזְלָא מִמִּזְרָח כְּלַפֵּי מַעֲרָב; וְנִפְסַע וְנֵיקוּם חַד כַּרְעָא לְהַאי גִּיסָא וְחַד כַּרְעָא לְהַאי גִּיסָא; וְנִישְׁקוֹל טִינָא בִּידֵיהּ דְּיַמִּינָא מִתּוּתֵי כַּרְעָא דִשְׂמָאלֵיהּ, וּבִידֵיהּ דִּשְׂמָאלָא מִתּוּתֵי כַּרְעָא דְיַמִּינֵיהּ; וְנִיגְדּוֹל תַּרְתֵּי פְּתִילָתָא דְעַמְרָא וְנִיטְמִישׁ בְּטִינָא, וְנִיתֵּיב בִּנְחִירֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the patient is **not** able to do this **let him look at a stream of water flowing from** the **east toward** the **west, and let him spread** his legs **and stand** with **one foot on this side and one foot on that side. And let him take clay in his right hand from under his left foot, and** let him take clay **with his left hand from under his right foot. And let him spin two strings of wool, soak** them **in the clay, and place them in his nostrils.**

Should that also fail, one finds a water conduit flowing from east to west and straddles it — one foot on each bank. One picks up a piece of clay with the right hand and holds it under the left arm, and simultaneously picks up another piece of clay with the left hand and holds it under the right arm. Two threads of wool are spun, submerged in this clay, and placed in the nostrils.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא – לֵיתֵיב תּוּתֵי מַרְזָבָא, וְנַיְיתוֹ מַיָּא וְלִישְׁדּוֹ עֲלֵיהּ, וְלֵימְרוּ: "כִּי הֵיכִי דְּפָסְקִי הָנֵי מַיָּא, לִיפְסוֹק דְּמֵיהּ דִּפְלָנְיָא בַּר פְּלָנִיתָא".
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the patient is **not** able to do this **let him sit under the gutter and let them bring water and pour it on him. And let them say: Just as the water has ceased, let the blood of so-and-so, son of so-and-so, cease.**

And if even that proves ineffective, the patient should sit beneath a drainpipe. Water is poured through the pipe so that it hits him, and he recites: just as this water has stopped flowing, so too may the blood of so-and-so son of so-and-so cease.

### Mouth Blood Diagnosis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִדְמָא דְּאָתֵי מִפּוּמָּא – בָּדְקִינַן לֵיהּ בְּגִילָא דְחִיטְּתָא, אִי סָרֵיךְ – מֵרֵיאָה קָאָתֵי וְאִית לֵיהּ תַּקַּנְתָּא, וְאִי לָא – מִכַּבְדָּא קָאָתֵי וְלֵית לֵיהּ תַּקַּנְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** As a remedy **for blood that comes from the mouth we check** the patient **with a straw of wheat. If** the blood **sticks** to the straw then it comes **from his lungs and it has a remedy. But if** the blood does **not** stick to the straw, it means that the blood **comes from the liver and it has no remedy.**

For *dama de-asi mi-puma* — blood coming from the mouth — the Gemara first prescribes a diagnostic test before any treatment. A straw of wheat is introduced into the mouth to examine the blood. If the blood is viscous and sticky (*sarich*), it is coming from the *reeya* — the lungs — and there is a remedy. If it is not sticky and flows freely, it is coming from the *kavda* — the liver — and there is no remedy.

### Treifa Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַמֵּי לְרַב אָשֵׁי, וְהָאֲנַן אִיפְּכָא תְּנַן: נִיטָּל הַכָּבֵד וְלֹא נִשְׁתַּיֵּיר הֵימֶנָּה כְּלוּם, הָרֵאָה שֶׁנִּיקְּבָה אוֹ שֶׁחָסְרָה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כֵּיוָן דְּמִפּוּמֵּיהּ קָאָתֵי, אֵימַר אִיתְּמוֹחֵי אִיתְּמַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ami said to Rav Ashi: But didn't we learn the opposite** in a mishna (*Ḥullin* 42a): If **the liver was** completely **removed and none of it remains,** then it is considered to be an animal with a condition that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*]. But in the case of **a lung that is punctured or missing,** the animal is considered to be a *tereifa*, even if it has not been completely removed. This indicates that damage to the lung is more serious than damage to the liver. Rav Ashi **said to him: Since** the blood **comes out from** this person's **mouth,** one must **say that** the liver has **dissolved** and he will not survive.

This diagnostic conclusion prompts an important exchange. Rav Ami challenges Rav Ashi: you are saying that blood from the lung is more treatable than blood from the liver, but this seems to contradict what we know from the laws of *treifa*. Regarding an animal, if the liver is removed entirely and nothing remains, it is a *treifa*. But a punctured or partially missing lung also renders the animal a *treifa*, and relatively minor damage to the lung is more significant halakhically than partial damage to the liver. So it would seem the lung is the more vulnerable organ — how can you say that lung bleeding is more treatable?

Rav Ashi responds by distinguishing the cases. The law of *treifa* addresses what we find when we open the animal and examine it — and indeed, if some liver remains, the animal is not a *treifa*. But when a person is spitting blood from the mouth, we are concerned that the liver has already dissolved entirely, which is why there is no remedy. So the comparison is not to partial liver damage but to total dissolution. The two cases are not parallel.

### Lung Bleeding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: אִי מֵרֵיאָה קָאָתֵי, אִית לֵיהּ תַּקַּנְתָּא. מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ? לַיְתֵי שִׁבְעָה כּוּנֵי מִפִּירְמָא דְסִילְקָא, וְשִׁבְעָה כּוּנֵי פִּירְמָא דְכַרָּתֵי, וְחַמְשָׁה כּוּנֵי פְּרִידָא, וּתְלָתָא כּוּנֵי דִּטְלָפְחֵי, וְכוּנָא דְּכַמּוֹנָא, וְכוּנָא (דְּחַבְלֵי) [דְּתַבְלֵי]; וּכְנֶגְדָּן – כַּנְתָּא פְּטִירְתָּא; וְלִיבַשֵּׁיל וְלֵיכוֹל, וְלִישְׁתֵּי אַבָּתְרֵיהּ שִׁיכְרָא חֲרִיפָא דְּטֵבֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said: If the** blood **comes from the lungs** then **it has a remedy.** The Gemara asks: **What is its remedy?** The Gemara answers: **Let him bring seven fistfuls of sliced beets, and seven fistfuls of sliced leek, and five fistfuls of *perida*** grass, **and three fistfuls of lentils, and a fistful of cumin, and a fistful of ropes, and a corresponding** amount of **the intestines of a firstborn animal. And let him cook** all of it together **and let him eat** everything **and afterward let him drink good, strong beer.**

Now the Gemara returns to ask: what is the remedy when the blood is determined to be coming from the lungs? One brings seven handfuls of chopped beet (*silka*), seven handfuls of chopped leek (*karti*), five handfuls of a grass (*frada* according to Rashi), three handfuls of lentils (*talpachi*), a handful of cumin (*kamuna*), a handful of rope (*chavli*), and equal to the entire combined amount, the entrails (*kinsha petirta*) of a firstborn animal — Rashi explains this as the *peter rechem*. All of this is cooked together, eaten, and followed by drinking strong beer (*shichra charifa*).

## Tooth/Gum Care

### Toothache

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְכַכָּא – אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: לַיְתֵי תּוּמָא יְחִידָאָה וְנִימְרְסֵיהּ בְּמִישְׁחָא וּמִילְחָא, וְנִתְּבֵיהּ אַטֻּופְרָא דְאִלְיוֹנָא דְּהָהוּא גִּיסָא דְּכָיֵיב לֵיהּ, וְנַהְדַּר לֵיהּ גְּדָנְפָא דְלֵישָׁא. וְנִיזְדְּהַר לְבִישְׂרֵיהּ, דְּקָשֵׁי לְחִיוָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the remedy **for** pain in one's **teeth [*lekhakha*], Rabba bar Rav Huna said: Let him bring** an **individual garlic** that grew as a single clove **and grind it with oil and salt. And let him place it on the thumbnail of the side that hurts him, and let him surround it** with **a rim [*gedanpa*] of dough. And he should take care** that it does not touch **his flesh** because it is **harmful** and can cause **boils.**

For *kucha* — tooth pain — Rabba bar bar Chana states: take a single garlic bulb, specifically one in which only a single clove grew, as Rashi explains — though the practical difficulty of obtaining such a thing is evident. Grind it with olive oil and salt, and apply it to the thumbnail of the side of the body that is experiencing the pain. Then surround the thumbnail with a rim of dough as a barrier. One must be careful that the preparation does not touch the flesh directly, as it can cause boils.

### Gum Disease

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְחִינְכֵי – אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: חוּמְתִי – כִּי מַמְרוּ, וְעִיקָּרָא דְחוּמְתִי – עֲדִיף מִמַּמְרוּ, וְנִינְקוֹט בְּפוּמֵּיהּ. הָנֵי לְאוֹקוֹמֵי; לְבַשּׁוֹלֵי – לַיְיתֵי פָּארֵי דְּרֵישׁ נָפְיָיא, וּטְלָפְחֵי בְּעַפְרַיְיהוּ, וְשׁוּבְלִילְתָּא, וְחוּמַרְתָּא דִכְשׁוּתָא, וְנִינְקוֹט כְּאַמְגּוּזָא בְּפוּמֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the remedy **for gums, Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The **Spanish chamomile [*ḥomti*]** plant is **like** the ***mamru*** plant, **and the root** of the **Spanish chamomile is preferable to *mamru*, and let him take it in his mouth. This** is meant **to stabilize** the illness so that it does not intensify. **To boil them,** meaning to cause the wounds to open so that the pus can be removed from them, **let him bring the** coarsest **bran** found **at the upper portion of the sifter, and lentils in their dirt, and fenugreek [*shuvlilta*], and the flower of the hop** plant. **And let him take** an amount **about** the size of a **nut in his mouth.**

For *chinchei* — a problem with the gums — Rabbi Yochanan prescribes *chumsei*, a plant similar to *mamru*, and notes that the root of *chumsei* is even more effective than *mamru* itself. This is placed in the mouth, and it serves to prevent the gum problem from spreading. That is the formula for *le-ukmei* — containing the disease. However, if the goal is *le-vshulei* — to draw the pus together so that it can be drained, as Rashi explains — one takes bran from the top of a sieve, lentils with the dirt they came from, fenugreek (*tavlulita*), and a piece of the hop plant (*chumasta de-chashuta*), combines them, and places an amount the size of a nut in the mouth.

### Gum Blister

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאִיפְּתוֹחֵי – לִינְפַּח לֵיהּ חַבְרֵיהּ תַּחְלֵי חִיוָּרְתָּא בְּגִילָא דְחִיטְּתָא. לְאַסּוֹקֵי – לַיְתֵי עַפְרָא מִטּוּלָּא דְּבֵית הַכִּסֵּא, וְנִיגְבּוֹל בְּדוּבְשָׁא וְנֵיכוֹל, דִּמְעַלֵּי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** In order **to open** the blisters to remove the pus, **let his friend blow white cress** into his mouth **with a straw of wheat.** And if he wishes **to heal** the blisters **let him bring dirt found in the shade of the bathroom and knead it with honey and eat it, as this is effective** for curing the blisters.

If one then wishes to open the blister that has formed — *liftochi* — a friend blows a white seed through a straw of wheat, using it as a pea shooter aimed at the blister to cause it to burst. Once it has burst, for the purpose of healing and scabbing over — *le-asokei* — one takes dirt from the shade beneath an outhouse, kneads it with honey, and eats it, as this is said to be beneficial.

## Birsam Disease

### Birsam (Ammonia/Galbanum)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְבַרְסָם – לַיְתֵי כִּי פִיסְתְּקָא דְּנִישָׁדוּר, וְכִי אַמְגּוּזָא דְחֶלְבְּנִיתָא דּוּבְשָׁנִיתָא, וּמְלֵי תַּרְווֹדָא דּוּבְשָׁא חִיוָּרָא, וּמְלֵי נַטְלָא בַּת מָחוֹזָא חַמְרָא נְקִידָא; וְנִישְׁלוֹקִינְהוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי; וְכִי בְּשִׁיל נִשָׁדוּר – בְּשִׁיל לֵיהּ כּוּלֵּיהּ. וְאִי לָא – לַיְתֵי רְבִיעֵתָא דַּחֲלָבָא דְּעִיזָּא חִיַּורְתִּי
>
> **Translation:** **For a bronchial infection [*levarsam*], let him bring a nut-sized** amount **of ammonia [*nishdor*]** water **and a** nut-sized amount **of sweet galbanum, and a large spoonful of white honey, a vessel from Meḥoza,** i.e., one that holds a quarter-*log* of liquid, **full of clear [*nekida*] wine, and let him boil them together. And once the ammonia is cooked everything will have cooked. And if** he is **not** able to do this **let him bring a quarter-***log* **of milk from a white goat,**

For *birsam* — an ailment that Rashi describes as coming to the head through the nose — the primary remedy involves the following preparation: a nut-sized amount of *nishtor*, which Jastrow identifies as ammonia and Rashi calls *ammonica*; a nut-sized amount of sweet galbanum (*chalbinisa dushana*); a spoonful of white honey; and a full measure of a vessel from Mechuza — which Rashi says held a *revi'is* — of clear wine. All of these are cooked together. One knows everything is properly cooked when the ammonia is done, since that ingredient takes the longest.

### Birsam (Milk Alternative)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִיטַּיְיפֵיהּ אַתְּלָת קִלְחֵי כְּרָבָא, וְנִיבְחֲשֵׁיהּ בִּגְווֹזָא דְּמַרְמְהִין; וְכִי בְּשִׁיל גְּווֹזָא דְמַרְמָהִין – בְּשִׁיל לֵיהּ כּוּלֵּיהּ. וְאִי לָא – לַיְתֵי נִפְקָא דְּכַלְבָּא חִיוָּרָא, וְנִיגְבּוֹל בְּנִטְפָא. וְכַמָּה דְּאֶפְשָׁר – נִפְקָא לָא נֵיכוֹל, דִּמְפָרֵיק.
>
> **Translation:** **and let him drip it on three stalks of cabbage and stir it with a sprig** from **a marjoram** bush. **And when the sprig of marjoram** is **boiled all of it** will be **boiled. And if** he is **not** able to do that, **let him take the feces of a white dog and let him mix it with balm. And as much as possible let him not eat the feces because** it causes **the separation** of the limbs.

If that does not work, one takes a *revi'is* of milk from a white goat and stirs three stalks of cabbage into it, using a twig of *maharmin* — apparently marjoram — as the stirring implement. Again, the dish is considered fully prepared when the marjoram twig is cooked through.

### Birsam (Excrement)

If that alternative also proves ineffective, one takes the excrement of a white dog and mixes it with balm. The Gemara adds a notable warning: as much as possible, one should avoid relying on this remedy, because overuse of it causes a separation of the limbs. The Gemara clarifies that this means the remedy does work and is to be consumed, but one should exhaust every other option first, given the dangerous side effect of excess use.

## Internal Ailments

### Gira Pain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְגִירָא – לַיְתֵי גִּירָא דְלִילִיתָא, וְנֵיפְכֵיהּ, וְנִשְׁדֵּי מַיָּא עִלָּוֵיהּ וְנִשְׁתֵּי. וְאִי לָא – לַיְתֵי מִמַּיָּא דְּאִישְׁתִּי מִינַּיְיהוּ כַּלְבָּא בְּלֵילְיָא; וְנִיזְדְּהַר מִגִּילּוּיָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to a remedy **for a stinging pain [*gira*], let him bring** a stone called **an arrow of Lilith, and let him turn it upside down. And let him pour water on it and drink it. And if** he is **not** able to do that, **let him bring water from which a dog drank at night, and let him be careful about** leaving the water **uncovered** at night, in case a snake drank from it and left its venom in the water.

For *gira* — described as a stinging pain — one takes a *gira de-lilasa*, which Rashi explains is a hailstone shaped like an arrow associated with Lilith, some form of demonic being. One turns it so that the point faces downward, pours water over it, and drinks the water. If that does not suffice, one takes water that a dog drank from at night and uses that — though one must be particularly careful to keep this water covered, since leaving things uncovered is in general problematic, and in this case may be especially dangerous.

### Uncovered Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְגִילּוּיָא – אַנְפָּקָא דְחַמְרָא חַיָּיא.
>
> **Translation:** A remedy **for** drinking **uncovered** water is to drink **a quarter-***log* **[*anpaka*] of undiluted wine.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמוּרְסָא – אַנְפָּקָא דְּחַמְרָא, בְּאַהֲלָא תּוֹלָאנָא.
>
> **Translation:** As a remedy **for a pus-filled wound,** one should drink **a quarter-***log* **of wine** steeped **in red ice plant.**

If one has already drunk uncovered water — *giluya* — the remedy is to drink a measure of *chamra chaya*, undiluted raw wine. For a blister or pustular sore (*mursa*), one drinks a *revi'is* of wine mixed with the plant *ahala tulana*.

### Heart Palpitations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִירְחָא דְלִיבָּא – לַיְתֵי תְּלָת בְּרוֹשִׁיָּיאתָא דִשְׂעָרֵי, וְנִשְׁטְרִינְהוּ בְּכַמְכָּא דְּלָא עֲבַר עִילָּוֵיהּ אַרְבְּעִין יוֹמִין, וְנֵיכוֹל; וְנִשְׁתֵּי אַבָּתְרַיְיהוּ חַמְרָא מַרְקָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא מִדִּיפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן דְּפָרַח לִיבֵּיהּ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא – לְיוּקְרָא דְלִיבָּא אֲמַרִי;
>
> **Translation:** As a remedy **for palpitations of the heart,** i.e., if his heart is beating too fast, **let him bring three loaves of barley, and soak them in *kamka*** from **which forty days have not** yet **passed** since being made. **And let him eat** the loaves **and drink diluted wine after** consuming **them. Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina: All the more so his heart will palpitate** from these foods. Ravina **said to him:** You misheard what I had said; **I said** this as a remedy **for heaviness of the heart.**

For *pircha de-liba* — a fluttering or lightness of the heart — one takes three barley loaves, soaks them in *kimcha*, a yogurt-like substance that is no more than 40 days old, eats them, and then drinks diluted wine.

### Heart Remedy Dispute

Rav Acha of Difti raises an objection to Ravina: surely drinking diluted wine will only increase the fluttering of the heart, since such foods are considered light and would exacerbate rather than ground the condition. Ravina responds that the remedy he had described was actually for *yukra de-liba* — heaviness of the heart, not lightness. For heaviness, one wants precisely these lighter preparations — the barley loaves, the yogurt, and the diluted wine — to produce more lightness.

### Heart Pain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִירְחָא דְלִיבָּא – לַיְתֵי תְּלָת בְּרוֹשִׁיָּיאתָא דְחִיטֵּי, וְנִישְׁטְרִינְהוּ בְּדוּבְשָׁא וְנֵיכוֹל, וְנִישְׁתֵּי אַבָּתְרַיְיהוּ חַמְרָא חַיָּיא.
>
> **Translation:** As a remedy **for palpitations of the heart, let him bring three loaves of wheat, soak them in honey, and eat** them. **And let him drink undiluted wine after them.**

For *pircha de-liba* — the actual lightness — heavier foods are called for. One takes three wheat loaves rather than barley, soaks them in honey rather than yogurt, eats them, and then drinks *chamra chaya*, raw undiluted wine, rather than diluted wine.

### Heart Pain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְצִרְחָא דְלִיבָּא – לַיְתֵי תְּלָת בֵּיעֵי נִינְיָיא, וּבֵיעֲתָא דְכַמּוֹנָא, וּבֵיעֲתָא דְשֻׁומְשְׁמֵי, וְלֵיכוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** As a remedy **for heart pain, let him bring three egg**-bulks **of mint, and an egg**-bulk **of cumin, and an egg**-bulk **of sesame seeds, and let him eat** them together.

For *tzircha de-liba* — pain in the heart — one takes three egg-worths of mint (*ninya*), one egg-worth of cumin, and one egg-worth of sesame, and eats them.

### Stomach Ache

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִכְאֵב מְעֵי – לַיְתֵי תְּלָת מְאָה פִּלְפְּלֵי אֲרִיכָתָא, וְכֹל יוֹמֵי נִשְׁתֵּי מְאָה מִינַּיְיהוּ בְּחַמְרָא. רָבִין דְּמִן נָרֶשׁ עֲבַד לַהּ לְבַרְתֵּיהּ דְּרַב אָשֵׁי מְאָה וְחַמְשִׁין מֵהָנֵי דִּידַן, וְאִתְּסִיאַת.
>
> **Translation:** As a remedy **for pain of the intestines, let him bring three hundred long peppers, and every day let him drink one hundred of them with wine.** It is told: **Ravin of** the city of **Neresh prepared one hundred and fifty of our,** i.e., Babylonian, peppers **for the daughter of Rav Ashi,** who had this illness, **and she was healed.**

For *kev maya* — a stomach ache — one takes 300 *pilpali arichta*, long peppers, and each day drinks 100 of them prepared with wine, doing this for three days. The Gemara notes that Ravina of Narsh made this remedy for the daughter of Rav Ashi, using only 150 of the Babylonian variety of pepper — which were apparently more potent than those of Eretz Yisrael — and she was healed.

### Roundworm

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְכִירְצָא – אַנְפָּקָא דְּחַמְרָא בַּאֲטַרְפָּא דְעָרָאָה. לְכִירְצָא חִיוָּרָא – לַיְתֵי בִּיזְרָא גַּלְגִּילָא וְנִיצְיְירֵהּ בְּשִׁיסְתַּג, וְנִיתְיְירֵיהּ בְּמַיָּא וְנִשְׁתְּיֵהּ; וְנִזְדְּהַר מִבִּינְתָּא, דְּאִי לָא – מְנַקְּבָא לַהּ לְמַעְיָינֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** As a remedy **for roundworm,** he should drink **a quarter-***log* **of wine** steeped **in laurel leaves.** As a remedy **for white worm, let him bring cress and tie it with a piece of woven cloth. And let him soak it in water and drink it. And let him be careful with the seed, as if** he is **not** careful enough there is a danger that it will grow inside him **and puncture his intestines.**

For *kirtza* — roundworm — one drinks a *revi'is* of wine in laurel leaves. For *kirtza chivra* — white worm specifically — one brings *bizra galglai*, cress seeds, ties them in a piece of cloth, soaks the bundle in water, and drinks the water. One must take great care not to consume the seed itself, because if one swallows the seed it will puncture the intestine.

### Bowel Remedies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵיסַר – סִיסִין רַטִּיבָא בְּמַיָּא. לְמִישְׁרֵא – יַבִּישְׁתָּא בְּמַיָּא. וְסִימָנָךְ: אִיצָא רַטִּיבְ[תָּ]א דְּסָכַר נַהֲרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The remedy **to bind** the bowels, i.e., to cure diarrhea, is to consume the **chamomile [*sisin*]** plant when it is **wet with water.** And the remedy **to relax** the bowels and relieve constipation is to drink **dry** chamomile soaked **in water,** which acts as a laxative. **And your mnemonic** so as not to confuse these remedies is as follows: **Wet grass [*itza*] that is used to dam rivers,** and through this he will remember that moist chamomile is used to stop diarrhea.

For *maser* — the need to bind or stop diarrhea — one eats fresh, wet *sisin*, chamomile, in water. For *mishra* — the opposite problem, constipation requiring loosening of the bowels — one uses dried chamomile soaked in water rather than fresh. The Gemara offers a mnemonic: wet grass is used to dam rivers. Hence, wet chamomile stops flow, and dried chamomile releases it.

## Spleen Remedies

### Leeches Remedy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִטְחָלָא – לַיְתֵי שַׁב בִּינֵי דְמַיָּא וּנְיַיבְּשִׁינְהוּ בְּטוּלָּא, וְכֹל יוֹמָא נִישְׁתֵּי תַּרְתֵּי וְתִלְתָּא בְּחַמְרָא. וְאִי לָא – לַיְתֵי טְחָלָא דִצְיפִרְתָּא דְּלָא אִיפְּתַח, וְנִטְחֲיֵיהּ בְּתַנּוּרָא, וְנוֹקֵי לַהֲדֵיהּ, וְנֵימָא: "כִּי הֵיכִי דְּיַבִּישׁ הַאי טְחָלָא, נִיַּיבַּשׁ טְחָלֵיהּ דִּפְלוֹנִי בַּר פְּלוֹנִיתָא".
>
> **Translation:** As a remedy **for** illness of **the spleen, let him bring seven leeches, dry them in the shade, and every day drink two or three** of them **with wine. And if** he is **not** able to do this, **let him bring the spleen of a female goat that has not given birth, and place it in the oven, and stand across from it, and let him say: Just as this spleen is dried, so shall the spleen of so-and-so, son of so-and-so, be dried.**

For *techla* — a disease of the spleen — the Gemara prescribes a graduated series of treatments, paralleling the structure seen earlier for the nosebleed. One begins by taking seven water leeches (*bani de-maya*), drying them out in the shade, and drinking two and a third each day with wine for three days. The Gemara notes in passing that some of the remedies across this entire sugya involve non-kosher materials — water leeches being one example — but since these illnesses were understood to be potentially life-threatening, presenting a consideration of *sakanas nefashos*, the Gemara records these treatments as valid.

### Goat Spleen (Oven)

The treatment of drying the spleen in an oven continues the Gemara's emphasis on sympathetic magic — the idea that drying out a physical organ externally may help dry out the corresponding organ internally.

### Goat Spleen (Bricks)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא – נִיטְחֲיֵיהּ בֵּינֵי אוּרְבֵי דְבֵיתָא חַדְתָּא, וְנֵימָא הָכִי. וְאִי לָא, לִיבְקֵי שָׁכְבָא דִּשְׁכֵיב בְּשַׁבְּתָא, וְנִישְׁקְלֵיהּ לִידֵיהּ וְנוֹתְבַהּ אַטְּחָלֵיהּ, וְנֵימָא: "כִּי הֵיכִי דְּיַבִּישׁ הָא יְדָא, נִיַּיבַּשׁ טְחָלֵיהּ דִּפְלוֹנִי בַּר פְּלוֹנִיתָא".
>
> **Translation:** **And if** he is **not** able to do this, **let him place** the goat spleen **between the bricks of a new house, and let him say this** same statement. **And if not, let him search for someone who died on Shabbat and let him take the hand** of the deceased, **and let him place it on his spleen and say: Just as the hand of so-and-so was dried,** so too, **the spleen of so-and-so, the son of so-and-so, should be dried.**

If that proves insufficient, one places the goat spleen between the bricks of a new house and recites the same incantation — the same logic of sympathetic drying applied through a different medium.

### Dead Hand Remedy

The practice of using the hand of a deceased person who died on Shabbat represents an extreme form of sympathetic magic. The dried corpse serves as an effective symbol and means for transferring the condition of dryness.

### Blacksmith Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא – נַיְתֵי בִּינִיתָא וְנִיטְוְויֵיהּ בֵּי נַפָּחָא, וְנֵיכְלֵיהּ בְּמַיָּא דְּבֵי נַפָּחָא, וְנִישְׁתֵּי מִמַּיָּא דְּבֵי נַפָּחָא. הָהִיא עִיזָּא דַּהֲוָת שָׁתְיָא מַיָּא דְּבֵי נַפָּחָא, אִישְׁתְּחִיט – וְלָא אִישְׁתְּכַח לַהּ טְחָלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** he is **not** able to do this, **let him take a fish and roast it** in the **house of a blacksmith. And let him eat it with the water from the blacksmith's house** in which the red-hot metal is cooled. **And let him drink water from the blacksmith's house.** The Gemara brings corroborative evidence to this remedy: **A certain goat drank water from the blacksmith's house,** and when it **was slaughtered there was no spleen found** inside it.

If that does not work, one takes a fish, roasts it at the house of a blacksmith (*beit naphcha*), eats it with the water from the blacksmith's workshop, and drinks that water as well. The Gemara adduces an empirical proof for the efficacy of blacksmith water: there was a certain goat that regularly drank the runoff water from a blacksmith's shop, and when it was slaughtered, no spleen was found in it. The blacksmith water had evidently caused the spleen to disappear entirely — a striking piece of evidence that the Gemara brings to validate this remedy.

### Wine Barrel/Health

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא – לִיפְתַּח חָבִיתָא דְּחַמְרָא לִשְׁמֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אִי אִית לֵיהּ חָבִיתָא דְחַמְרָא, לָא אָתֵי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּמָר. אֶלָּא מַרְגַּל בְּפַת שַׁחֲרִית, דִּמְעַלְּיָא לְכוּלֵּי גּוּפֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** he is **not** able to do this, **let him open a barrel of wine for his sake,** i.e., let him drink a large amount of wine. **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi: If he has a barrel of wine he will not come before** my **Master** for a remedy, as the wine will already have healed him. **Rather,** what should he do to maintain his health? Let him become **accustomed** to eat **bread in the morning, because it benefits his entire body.**

If none of the preceding remedies succeeds, one should open a new barrel of wine (*chavita dechamra*) for the sake of the sick person and drink liberally from it. Rava bar Rav objects to Rav Ashi that this remedy is impractical: if the patient had an entire barrel of wine available to him, he would not have needed to seek out the other remedies in the first place, since wine was understood as a reliable cure but required a substantial quantity. The Gemara concludes with a more general piece of advice: eating bread in the morning is beneficial for the whole body — a remark that seems to transcend the specific treatment for the spleen and offers general counsel for maintaining health.
