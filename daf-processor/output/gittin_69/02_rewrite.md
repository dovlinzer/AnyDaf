# Gittin 69 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blood/Migraine Remedies

### Blood in Head

The daf opens in the middle of an extended discussion of various *refuot* — medical treatments and remedies — that the Gemara presents in connection with a Mishnah dealing with a particular figure. We pick up following a remarkable *agadata* concerning Shlomo HaMelech and Ashmedai, and now arrive at the practical medical material beginning near the bottom of 68b.

*L'dama d'reisha* — for blood in the head — the Gemara prescribes an elaborate herbal preparation. One should bring *shorvina*, *vina*, fresh myrtle (*asa*), olives, poplar, sea willow, and cynodon, and boil them all together. The resulting mixture is then poured in 300 cups over one side of the head and 300 cups over the other side. Should this prove insufficient, one brings a white rose — specifically one where all the petals grow on a single side, as Rashi explains — boils it, and pours 60 cups of that mixture over each side of the head.

### Migraine Treatment

For *tzilchata* — which Rashi describes as a pain in half of the head, and which Koren translates as a migraine — the treatment is of a different character entirely. One takes a wild rooster and slaughters it with a sharpened silver coin (*zuza chivra*) on the side of the head where the pain is located. One must take great care that the blood does not enter the eyes, as Rashi warns it will cause blindness. The carcass is then hung on the doorpost so that one brushes up against it both upon entering and upon leaving the house, and that contact is understood to provide the therapeutic benefit.

## Eye Diseases

### Cataracts

At the top of 69a, the Gemara turns to eye diseases. For *frukita* — an eye ailment that Rashi identifies and which appears to correspond to something like cataracts — the treatment involves a scorpion. One takes a seven-colored scorpion, which Rashi understands to mean a very old one, and dries it out in the shade. One then grinds together two portions of *kuchla* — the substance used for eye application — and one portion of the powdered scorpion. Three applications of this mixture are placed in each eye using an eyebrush. The Gemara adds a clear warning: no more than three applications should be used, because too much will cause the eye to burst.

### Night Blindness

For *shavrire de-leilya* — night blindness — the Gemara prescribes an elaborate ritual that combines physical actions with incantation. One brings a rope made of animal hair, ties one end to one's own leg and the other end to the leg of a dog, and has a child follow behind banging on a pottery shard while reciting that the dog is old and the rooster is foolish. Seven pieces of raw meat are then collected from seven different houses, with the owners of each house placing the meat into the socket of their doorpost. The patient eats these pieces at the refuse heap of the city. Afterward, the rope is untied, and an incantation is recited in which the blindness of so-and-so son of so-and-so is declared to have departed from the patient and to have passed instead into the eyes of the dog. The treatment concludes by blowing into the pupils of the dog's eyes.

It is worth noting that the Gemara here clearly incorporates what it understood as incantation alongside physical remedies — presumably invoking some force associated with *shedim* or related phenomena. The Gemara evidently did not regard all of this as purely medical science in our sense of the term.

### Day Blindness

For *shavrire de-yami* — day blindness — one takes seven animal spleens from the innards of animals and roasts them on the pottery vessel used by a bloodletter to collect blood. The patient sits inside the house while another person sits outside. The blind person calls out to the outside person asking for food, and the outside person responds, handing him the meat to eat. After eating, the pottery must be broken, because if it is not, the condition will return.

## Nosebleed Remedies

### Nosebleed Treatment

For *dama de-asi mi-nichiri* — a nosebleed — the Gemara offers a graduated series of remedies of increasing complexity. First, a Kohen whose name is Levi should write the word *Levi* backwards. If that does not help, any ordinary man should write the phrase *ana Papi Shila bar Sumkei* — "I am Papi Shila bar Sumkei" — backwards. If that too fails, he should write the following words: *aton deli bemei chasaf, aton deli bemei pgam* — formulaic phrases about drawing from a bucket in silver water and in spoiled water.

If none of these inscriptions succeeds, the treatment becomes more physically involved. One takes the root of fodder, a rope from an old bed, a type of paper, saffron, and the red of a palm branch, burns them all together, takes a cluster of wool, spins two threads from it, soaks them in vinegar, rolls them in the ashes of the burned mixture, and places them in the nostrils. This is the first remedy in the sequence that involves actually introducing something into the nose.

Should that also fail, one finds a water conduit flowing from east to west and straddles it — one foot on each bank. One picks up a piece of clay with the right hand and holds it under the left arm, and simultaneously picks up another piece of clay with the left hand and holds it under the right arm. Two threads of wool are spun, submerged in this clay, and placed in the nostrils.

And if even that proves ineffective, the patient should sit beneath a drainpipe. Water is poured through the pipe so that it hits him, and he recites: just as this water has stopped flowing, so too may the blood of so-and-so son of so-and-so cease.

### Mouth Blood Diagnosis

For *dama de-asi mi-puma* — blood coming from the mouth — the Gemara first prescribes a diagnostic test before any treatment. A straw of wheat is introduced into the mouth to examine the blood. If the blood is viscous and sticky (*sarich*), it is coming from the *reeya* — the lungs — and there is a remedy. If it is not sticky and flows freely, it is coming from the *kavda* — the liver — and there is no remedy.

### Treifa Dispute

This diagnostic conclusion prompts an important exchange. Rav Ami challenges Rav Ashi: you are saying that blood from the lung is more treatable than blood from the liver, but this seems to contradict what we know from the laws of *treifa*. Regarding an animal, if the liver is removed entirely and nothing remains, it is a *treifa*. But a punctured or partially missing lung also renders the animal a *treifa*, and relatively minor damage to the lung is more significant halakhically than partial damage to the liver. So it would seem the lung is the more vulnerable organ — how can you say that lung bleeding is more treatable?

Rav Ashi responds by distinguishing the cases. The law of *treifa* addresses what we find when we open the animal and examine it — and indeed, if some liver remains, the animal is not a *treifa*. But when a person is spitting blood from the mouth, we are concerned that the liver has already dissolved entirely, which is why there is no remedy. So the comparison is not to partial liver damage but to total dissolution. The two cases are not parallel.

### Lung Bleeding

Now the Gemara returns to ask: what is the remedy when the blood is determined to be coming from the lungs? One brings seven handfuls of chopped beet (*silka*), seven handfuls of chopped leek (*karti*), five handfuls of a grass (*frada* according to Rashi), three handfuls of lentils (*talpachi*), a handful of cumin (*kamuna*), a handful of rope (*chavli*), and equal to the entire combined amount, the entrails (*kinsha petirta*) of a firstborn animal — Rashi explains this as the *peter rechem*. All of this is cooked together, eaten, and followed by drinking strong beer (*shichra charifa*).

## Tooth/Gum Care

### Toothache

For *kucha* — tooth pain — Rabba bar bar Chana states: take a single garlic bulb, specifically one in which only a single clove grew, as Rashi explains — though the practical difficulty of obtaining such a thing is evident. Grind it with olive oil and salt, and apply it to the thumbnail of the side of the body that is experiencing the pain. Then surround the thumbnail with a rim of dough as a barrier. One must be careful that the preparation does not touch the flesh directly, as it can cause boils.

### Gum Disease

For *chinchei* — a problem with the gums — Rabbi Yochanan prescribes *chumsei*, a plant similar to *mamru*, and notes that the root of *chumsei* is even more effective than *mamru* itself. This is placed in the mouth, and it serves to prevent the gum problem from spreading. That is the formula for *le-ukmei* — containing the disease. However, if the goal is *le-vshulei* — to draw the pus together so that it can be drained, as Rashi explains — one takes bran from the top of a sieve, lentils with the dirt they came from, fenugreek (*tavlulita*), and a piece of the hop plant (*chumasta de-chashuta*), combines them, and places an amount the size of a nut in the mouth.

### Gum Blister

If one then wishes to open the blister that has formed — *liftochi* — a friend blows a white seed through a straw of wheat, using it as a pea shooter aimed at the blister to cause it to burst. Once it has burst, for the purpose of healing and scabbing over — *le-asokei* — one takes dirt from the shade beneath an outhouse, kneads it with honey, and eats it, as this is said to be beneficial.

## Birsam Disease

### Birsam (Ammonia/Galbanum)

For *birsam* — an ailment that Rashi describes as coming to the head through the nose — the primary remedy involves the following preparation: a nut-sized amount of *nishtor*, which Jastrow identifies as ammonia and Rashi calls *ammonica*; a nut-sized amount of sweet galbanum (*chalbinisa dushana*); a spoonful of white honey; and a full measure of a vessel from Mechuza — which Rashi says held a *revi'is* — of clear wine. All of these are cooked together. One knows everything is properly cooked when the ammonia is done, since that ingredient takes the longest.

### Birsam (Milk Alternative)

If that does not work, one takes a *revi'is* of milk from a white goat and stirs three stalks of cabbage into it, using a twig of *maharmin* — apparently marjoram — as the stirring implement. Again, the dish is considered fully prepared when the marjoram twig is cooked through.

### Birsam (Excrement)

If that alternative also proves ineffective, one takes the excrement of a white dog and mixes it with balm. The Gemara adds a notable warning: as much as possible, one should avoid relying on this remedy, because overuse of it causes a separation of the limbs. The Gemara clarifies that this means the remedy does work and is to be consumed, but one should exhaust every other option first, given the dangerous side effect of excess use.

## Internal Ailments

### Gira Pain

For *gira* — described as a stinging pain — one takes a *gira de-lilasa*, which Rashi explains is a hailstone shaped like an arrow associated with Lilith, some form of demonic being. One turns it so that the point faces downward, pours water over it, and drinks the water. If that does not suffice, one takes water that a dog drank from at night and uses that — though one must be particularly careful to keep this water covered, since leaving things uncovered is in general problematic, and in this case may be especially dangerous.

### Uncovered Water

If one has already drunk uncovered water — *giluya* — the remedy is to drink a measure of *chamra chaya*, undiluted raw wine. For a blister or pustular sore (*mursa*), one drinks a *revi'is* of wine mixed with the plant *ahala tulana*.

### Heart Palpitations

For *pircha de-liba* — a fluttering or lightness of the heart — one takes three barley loaves, soaks them in *kimcha*, a yogurt-like substance that is no more than 40 days old, eats them, and then drinks diluted wine.

### Heart Remedy Dispute

Rav Acha of Difti raises an objection to Ravina: surely drinking diluted wine will only increase the fluttering of the heart, since such foods are considered light and would exacerbate rather than ground the condition. Ravina responds that the remedy he had described was actually for *yukra de-liba* — heaviness of the heart, not lightness. For heaviness, one wants precisely these lighter preparations — the barley loaves, the yogurt, and the diluted wine — to produce more lightness.

### Heart Pain

For *pircha de-liba* — the actual lightness — heavier foods are called for. One takes three wheat loaves rather than barley, soaks them in honey rather than yogurt, eats them, and then drinks *chamra chaya*, raw undiluted wine, rather than diluted wine.

### Heart Pain

For *tzircha de-liba* — pain in the heart — one takes three egg-worths of mint (*ninya*), one egg-worth of cumin, and one egg-worth of sesame, and eats them.

### Stomach Ache

For *kev maya* — a stomach ache — one takes 300 *pilpali arichta*, long peppers, and each day drinks 100 of them prepared with wine, doing this for three days. The Gemara notes that Ravina of Narsh made this remedy for the daughter of Rav Ashi, using only 150 of the Babylonian variety of pepper — which were apparently more potent than those of Eretz Yisrael — and she was healed.

### Roundworm

For *kirtza* — roundworm — one drinks a *revi'is* of wine in laurel leaves. For *kirtza chivra* — white worm specifically — one brings *bizra galglai*, cress seeds, ties them in a piece of cloth, soaks the bundle in water, and drinks the water. One must take great care not to consume the seed itself, because if one swallows the seed it will puncture the intestine.

### Bowel Remedies

For *maser* — the need to bind or stop diarrhea — one eats fresh, wet *sisin*, chamomile, in water. For *mishra* — the opposite problem, constipation requiring loosening of the bowels — one uses dried chamomile soaked in water rather than fresh. The Gemara offers a mnemonic: wet grass is used to dam rivers. Hence, wet chamomile stops flow, and dried chamomile releases it.

## Spleen Remedies

### Leeches Remedy

For *techla* — a disease of the spleen — the Gemara prescribes a graduated series of treatments, paralleling the structure seen earlier for the nosebleed. One begins by taking seven water leeches (*bani de-maya*), drying them out in the shade, and drinking two and a third each day with wine for three days. The Gemara notes in passing that some of the remedies across this entire sugya involve non-kosher materials — water leeches being one example — but since these illnesses were understood to be potentially life-threatening, presenting a consideration of *sakanas nefashos*, the Gemara records these treatments as valid.

### Goat Spleen (Oven)

If that does not work, one takes the spleen (*techla*) of a female goat that has never given birth (*shakun* as Rashi understands it), places it in the oven to dry out, and stands near it reciting: just as this spleen is drying out, so may the spleen of so-and-so son of so-and-so dry out.

### Goat Spleen (Bricks)

If that proves insufficient, one places the goat spleen between the bricks of a new house and recites the same incantation — the same logic of sympathetic drying applied through a different medium.

### Dead Hand Remedy

If still ineffective, one locates someone who died on Shabbat (*shichiv be-Shabbat*), takes that person's hand (*yad shachufa*), places it on the spleen of the sick person, and recites: just as this hand has dried, so may the spleen of so-and-so son of so-and-so dry.

### Blacksmith Water

If that does not work, one takes a fish, roasts it at the house of a blacksmith (*beit naphcha*), eats it with the water from the blacksmith's workshop, and drinks that water as well. The Gemara adduces an empirical proof for the efficacy of blacksmith water: there was a certain goat that regularly drank the runoff water from a blacksmith's shop, and when it was slaughtered, no spleen was found in it. The blacksmith water had evidently caused the spleen to disappear entirely — a striking piece of evidence that the Gemara brings to validate this remedy.

### Wine Barrel/Health

If none of the preceding remedies succeeds, one should open a new barrel of wine (*chavita dechamra*) for the sake of the sick person and drink liberally from it. Rava bar Rav objects to Rav Ashi that this remedy is impractical: if the patient had an entire barrel of wine available to him, he would not have needed to seek out the other remedies in the first place, since wine was understood as a reliable cure but required a substantial quantity. The Gemara concludes with a more general piece of advice: eating bread in the morning is beneficial for the whole body — a remark that seems to transcend the specific treatment for the spleen and offers general counsel for maintaining health.
