# Zevachim 112 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blood in Two Cups

### Chatat Blood Divided

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בִּשְׁלָמָא בַּחוּץ וְחָזַר וְנָתַן בִּפְנִים – שֶׁכּוּלּוֹ רָאוּי לִהְיוֹת בִּפְנִים; אֲבָל בִּפְנִים וְהֶעֱלָן בַּחוּץ – שִׁירַיִים נִינְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara discusses the first clause of the mishna: **Granted** that one is liable in a case where he first placed the blood on an altar **outside** the courtyard **and then placed** the remaining blood on the altar **inside** the courtyard; that is because, as the mishna explains: **As** the blood in **its entirety is fit to be** placed **inside** the courtyard. **But** in a case where he first placed its blood on the altar **inside** the courtyard **and then offered up** the remaining blood on an altar **outside** the courtyard, why he is liable? That blood **is** merely **a remainder,** and one should not be liable for offering it up outside.

The Gemara at the top of 112 wraps up the conclusion of the thirteenth chapter, *Hashochet Veha'aleh*, returning briefly to the preceding *mishnah*. The *mishnah* had presented the case of a *korban chatat* whose blood was collected in a single cup, with part of the blood sprinkled outside the Temple and the remainder sprinkled inside. The Gemara opens with the observation: *bishlama bachutz vehazar venatan bifnim* — it is perfectly understandable why one would be *chayav* if the blood was first applied outside and the remainder then brought inside, since the entire quantity had been suitable to be applied inside; by performing the *zerikah* outside, one violated the prohibition of *zerikah bachutz*. But the reverse case — *aval bifnim veha'alah bachutz* — where the blood was first applied inside and then the remainder taken outside, is more difficult. The remainder is merely *shi'arayim*, the leftover blood, and having already fulfilled the *zerikah* obligation, one might expect that taking the *shi'arayim* outside carries no prohibition of substance.

### Rabbi Nechemya Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר: שְׁיָרֵי הַדָּם שֶׁהִקְרִיבָן בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna? It **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya, who says:** For the **remainder of the blood** of an offering that was supposed to be poured at the base of the altar and **that** instead **one sacrificed outside** the courtyard, one is **liable.**

To account for this case, the Gemara proposes: *ha mani Rabbi Nechemya hi* — the *mishnah* follows the opinion of Rabbi Nechemya, who holds that *she'arey dam she'hikrivan bachutz chayav*, even the leftover blood that does not effect the *kaparah* falls within the prohibition of *hakravah bachutz*. Rabbi Nechemya thus adopts an expansive understanding of the prohibition, extending it even to the *shi'arey hadam*.

### Reisha vs Seifa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: קִבֵּל דָּמָהּ בִּשְׁנֵי כּוֹסוֹת; נָתַן שְׁנֵיהֶם בִּפְנִים – פָּטוּר. שְׁנֵיהֶם בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב. אֶחָד בִּפְנִים וְאֶחָד בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר. וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה: שְׁיָרֵי הַדָּם שֶׁהִקְרִיבָן בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya,** then **say the latter clause:** If **one collected its blood in two cups** and **placed** the blood from **both of them** on the altar **inside** the courtyard, he is **exempt.** If he placed the blood from **both of them** on an altar **outside** the courtyard, he is **liable.** If he first placed the blood from **one** cup **inside and** then placed the blood from the other **one outside,** he is **exempt.** By using the blood of the first cup to perform the mitzva of placing the blood on the altar, he thereby rendered the blood in the second cup a mere remainder. The Gemara asks: How can this clause be attributed to Rabbi Neḥemya? **But doesn't Rabbi Neḥemya say:** For **the remainder of the blood** of an offering **that one offered outside** the courtyard, he is **liable?**

However, if one attributes the *mishnah* to Rabbi Nechemya, the *seifa* becomes problematic. The later clause of the *mishnah* addresses blood collected in two separate cups: if both cups are taken inside one is *patur*; if both are taken outside one is *chayav*; but if one is applied inside and one outside, one is *patur*. This sits uneasily with Rabbi Nechemya, who would presumably hold one *chayav* even for the second cup brought outside, since that blood — though now rendered *shi'arayim* after the first cup fulfilled the *zerikah* — was still brought beyond the Temple. The Gemara therefore concludes: *seifa ata l'Tana Kama d'Rabbi Elazar be'Rabbi Shimon* — while the *reisha* of the *mishnah* reflects Rabbi Nechemya's position, the *seifa* follows the opinion of Rabbi Elazar be'Rabbi Shimon, who articulates the principle *kos oseh dichei lechavero*: when blood is divided between two cups and one cup becomes disqualified, it renders the partner cup disqualified as well. The Rambam notes in his *Perush ha-Mishnayot* that this *sugya* makes clear that Rabbi Nechemya's position is rejected, and the Gemara is merely attempting to align parts of the *mishnah* with his view in order to account for the earlier clause.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֵיפָא אֲתָאן לְתַנָּא קַמָּא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר: כּוֹס עוֹשֶׂה דָּחוּי לַחֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: In **the latter clause we arrive at** the opinion of **the first *tanna*,** who disagrees with **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon. As** that *tanna* **says:** The placement of the blood from one **cup renders** the blood of **the other** cup as **disqualified.** Since it is actually disqualified and not merely a remainder, one is not liable for offering it up outside.

### Two Animals Designated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לְמַפְרִישׁ חַטָּאתוֹ וְאָבְדָה, וְהִפְרִישׁ אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִמְצֵאת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna presents an analogy for its ruling: **To what is this matter comparable?** It is comparable **to** a case where one **separated** an animal for **his sin offering and it was lost, and he separated another** animal **in its place, and thereafter, the first** animal **was found.**

The *mishnah* had then offered a *lema hadavar domeh* — an analogy comparing the case of blood divided between two cups to the following scenario: a person designated a *korban chatat*, it was lost, he designated a second animal as a replacement, and then the first animal was found. Now both animals stand as designated *korbanot chatat*. If both are slaughtered inside the *Beit HaMikdash* one is *patur*; if both are slaughtered outside one is *chayav*; if one is slaughtered inside and one outside one is *patur*. However, if the outside animal is slaughtered first and then the inside animal, one is *chayav* for the first, and the second still effects *kaparah*.

### Rebbi's Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה לְמָה לִי? הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי הִיא; דְּאָמַר: אֲבוּדָה בִּשְׁעַת הַפְרָשָׁה – מֵתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need to ask: **To what is this matter comparable,** and provide an analogy to the mishna's rulings? What does the analogy add? The Gemara explains: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna? It **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who says** (see *Temura* 22b): A sin offering that was **lost during the time of** the **separation** of a substitute, if it is later found and one of them is slaughtered as the person's sin offering, the other one is **put to death.** Accordingly, it is actually disqualified from being used as an offering, and one is therefore not liable for offering it up outside.

The Gemara asks: *lama li?* Why does the *mishnah* introduce this entire analogy at all? The answer is: *ha mani Rebbi hi* — this reflects the opinion of Rebbi, who holds *avudah beshas hafrasha meisah*: if the animal was lost at the time the replacement was designated, that first animal must die — it constitutes a sixth category of *chatas meisos* beyond the standard five. The purpose of the analogy is to explain why both animals are standing and must be addressed at all: the reason is precisely that the animal was lost in the interim, not that one tried to designate two *chatat* animals simultaneously from the outset. For if one had tried to designate two *korbanot* for the same liability at once, one of them would automatically revert to being a *korban olah*, as Rav Huna states in the name of Rav: an *asham* that was disqualified and sent to pasture, and then slaughtered *stam* without any particular intent, is valid as an *olah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִדְרַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: אָשָׁם שֶׁנִּיתַּק לִרְעִיָּיה, וּשְׁחָטוֹ סְתָם – כָּשֵׁר לְעוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this is **in accordance with** the statement that **Rav Huna** says that **Rav says, as Rav Huna says** that **Rav says: A guilt offering that was consigned to grazing** per the *halakha* to leave it to graze if its owner dies or achieves atonement through another guilt offering, **and** then instead of being left to develop a blemish, at which point it could be sold and the proceeds used to purchase a voluntary burnt offering, one **slaughtered it,** even with **unspecified** intent, the animal itself is **fit** to be sacrificed **as a burnt offering.** Similarly, the mishna assumes that in any case where an animal is consigned to grazing it is considered fit, and one would be liable for slaughtering it outside the courtyard.

### Rebbi's Limitations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי דָּמֵי?! הָתָם אָשָׁם זָכָר, וְעוֹלָה זָכָר; אֲבָל חַטָּאת – נְקֵבָה הִיא! אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא מִיּוֹסְתִּינְיָא: בִּשְׂעִיר נָשִׂיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Are** these cases **comparable? There,** in Rav's ruling, it is logical that the animal is considered fit, as **a guilt offering** is **a male** animal **and a burnt offering** is **a male** animal, so it is possible to bring an animal as the latter even if it had been designated as the former. Therefore, a guilt offering left to graze is still considered fit. **But** in the mishna's case, just because the animal is left to graze does not necessarily indicate that it itself is fit to be brought, as **a sin offering is a female** animal, which can never be brought as a burnt offering. Therefore, it should be considered unfit. **Rav Ḥiyya from Yostiniyya said:** The ruling of the mishna is **with regard to the goat of** the ***Nasi*,** which is a male sin offering. Therefore, in a case where it is left to graze it is still considered fit, as it can be brought as a burnt offering. This chapter discussed two distinct prohibitions: That of slaughtering an offering outside the Temple courtyard and that of offering up an offering by placing it upon an altar outside the Temple courtyard. Since they are considered two distinct prohibitions, one is liable even if he performs only one of them, and he is liable twice if he performs both.

However, the Gemara immediately challenges this: *mi dami? hatam asham zachar ve'olah zachar* — that case works because both the *asham* and the *olah* are male animals. But a *korban chatat* is a female animal, and a *korban olah* may only be brought from a male animal. Accordingly, one could not simply say that the second *chatat* animal reverts to an *olah*. Rabbi Chiyya of Vastinya therefore limits Rebbi's ruling to the very narrow case of *seir nasi* — the *chatat* of a ruler — which at the time of the *Beit Sheni* had perhaps highly limited application. Thus the *sugya* concludes that Rebbi's principle of *avudah beshas hafrasha meisah*, while compelling in theory, is confined by the *Amoraim* to a specific and uncommon case, since the general problem of the *chatat*'s being a female animal prevents it from reverting to any other category. With this, the Gemara closes the thirteenth chapter.

## Fourteenth Chapter Intro

### Parah & Seir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ פָּרַת חַטָּאת שֶׁשְּׂרָפָהּ חוּץ מִגִּתָּהּ, וְכֵן שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ שֶׁהִקְרִיב בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **the** red **heifer of purification that one burned outside its pit,** the pit being an excavation on the Mount of Olives designated for its slaughter and its burning, **and likewise the scapegoat that one sacrificed outside** the Temple courtyard rather than casting it off a cliff as prescribed, he is **exempt** from punishment for violating the transgression of slaughtering and sacrificing outside the Temple courtyard.

The fourteenth chapter, *Paras Chatas*, opens with the *mishnah* stating: *paras chatas she-serafah chutz mi-gitah* — if the *parah adumah* was burned outside its designated pit on Har ha-Zeitim, or if the *seir hamishtaleach* — which is supposed to be pushed off a cliff — was instead offered as a sacrifice outside the Temple, one is *patur* from the prohibition of *shechitat chutz*. The Scriptural basis is the verse *ve'el pesach ohel moed lo hevi'o*, from which the *mishnah* derives the principle: *kol she'ein ra'uy lavo el pesach ohel moed ein chayavin alav* — anything which has no obligation to be brought to the entrance of the *Ohel Moed* does not fall within the scope of the prohibition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֶל פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד לֹא הֱבִיאוֹ״ – כֹּל שֶׁאֵין רָאוּי לָבֹא אֶל פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד, אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The source for this is **as it is stated** with regard to slaughter of sacrificial animals outside the courtyard: "Whatever man…that slaughters outside the camp, **and to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting he did not bring it,** to present it as an offering to the Lord before the Tabernacle of the Lord" (Leviticus 17:3–4). From that verse it is derived: For **any** offering **that is not fit to come to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting** for sacrifice on the altar, e.g., the red heifer and the scapegoat, **one is not liable for its** slaughter and sacrifice outside its place.

### Inherently Disqualified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָרוֹבֵעַ, וְהַנִּרְבָּע, וְהַמּוּקְצֶה, וְהַנֶּעֱבָד, וְהַמְּחִיר, [וְהָאֶתְנַן], וְהַכִּלְאַיִם, וְהַטְּרֵיפָה, וְיוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן; שֶׁהִקְרִיבָן בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **an animal that** actively **copulated with a person, or an animal that was the object of bestiality, or** an animal **that** was **set aside** for idol worship, **or** an animal **that was worshipped** as a deity, **or** an animal given as **the price** of a dog that was purchased, **or** an animal that was given as **payment** to a prostitute, **or** an animal born of a mixture of **diverse kinds, or an animal with a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*], or** an animal **born by caesarean section,** any of **which one sacrificed outside** the Temple courtyard, he is **exempt.**

The following *mishnah* catalogs a series of animals whose disqualification is inherent to their bodies — *begufan*: an animal involved in an act of bestiality (*rove'ah* or *nirba'at*), an animal designated or worshipped for *avodah zarah* (*muktzeh* or *ne'evad*), an animal given as *etnan zonah* or *mechir kelev*, a crossbred animal (*kilayim*), a *treifah*, or an animal born through the abdominal wall (*yotzei dofen*). If any of these animals were slaughtered outside the Temple one is *patur* from *shechitat chutz*. The Scriptural basis cited here is slightly different from the previous *mishnah*: *lifnei mishkan Hashem*, suggesting that these animals are abhorrent to God, and their exclusion thus derives from a different dimension of the verse than the quasi-sacrificial cases addressed earlier.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לִפְנֵי מִשְׁכַּן ה׳״ – כֹּל שֶׁאֵין רָאוּי לָבֹא לִפְנֵי מִשְׁכַּן ה׳, אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The source for this is **as it is stated:** "And to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting he did not bring it to present it as an offering to the Lord **before the Tabernacle of the Lord."** From this verse, it is derived: For **any** animal **that is not fit to come to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting** for sacrifice on the altar, **one is not liable for its** slaughter and sacrifice outside the courtyard.

### Rabbi Shimon Lo Ta'aseh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין – בֵּין בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין קְבוּעִין, בֵּין בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין עוֹבְרִין; שֶׁהִקְרִיבָן בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין קְבוּעִין – פָּטוּר, בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין עוֹבְרִין – חַיָּיב בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** For **blemished** animals, **whether** they are **permanently blemished** or **whether** they are **temporarily blemished, which one sacrificed outside** the Temple courtyard, one is **exempt. Rabbi Shimon says:** For **permanently blemished** animals one is **exempt;** for **temporarily blemished** animals one is **liable for** violation of **a prohibition,** but it is not the type of prohibition for which he will receive *karet*, because ultimately the animal will be fit for sacrifice.

The next *mishnah* addresses *ba'alei mumim* — animals with physical deformities, whether permanent (*kevu'im*) or temporary (*overim*). The *tanna kamma* holds that slaughtering any such animal outside the Temple renders one *patur*. Rabbi Shimon introduces an intermediate category. For permanent deformities he agrees: *patur*. But for temporary deformities he says *ba'alei mumim overim belo ta'aseh* — one violates the prohibition without incurring *karet*. This intermediate position — neither fully *chayav* nor fully *patur* — is the signature of Rabbi Shimon's approach throughout this chapter. The category of *lo ta'aseh* here does not mean one receives lashes; rather, it means one has transgressed the prohibition in a manner that, due to some deficiency, does not carry the penalty to its fullest extent.

### Age Disqualifications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוֹרִים שֶׁלֹּא הִגִּיעַ זְמַנָּן, וּבְנֵי יוֹנָה שֶׁעָבַר זְמַנָּן, שֶׁהִקְרִיבָן בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: בְּנֵי יוֹנָה שֶׁעָבַר זְמַנָּן – פָּטוּר, וְתוֹרִים שֶׁלֹּא הִגִּיעַ זְמַנָּן – בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **doves whose time** of fitness for sacrifice **has not arrived,** as they are fit for sacrifice only when they are older, after their wings assume a golden hue; **and pigeons whose time** of fitness **has passed,** as they are fit only when they are young and their wings did not yet assume a yellowish tint, **that one sacrificed outside** the Temple courtyard, he is **exempt. Rabbi Shimon says:** For **pigeons whose time** of fitness **has passed** one is **exempt, and** for **doves whose time** of fitness **has not** yet **arrived** he is **in** violation of **a prohibition.**

The same structure applies to bird sacrifices. *Torim shelo higi'a zemanam* — turtledoves that have not yet reached their proper age — and *bnei yonah she-avar zemanam* — doves that have aged past their permissible stage — are invalid because turtledoves must be brought as older birds and doves must be brought as young birds. The *tanna kamma* holds that bringing either category outside the Temple renders one *patur*. Rabbi Shimon distinguishes: *bnei yonah she-avar zemanam patur* — doves that are too old, *patur* — but *torim shelo higi'a zemanam belo ta'aseh* — turtledoves that are too young incur a violation of the prohibition without *karet*. Rabbi Shimon's reasoning reflects a consistent principle: a disqualification that lies in the past cannot be corrected, while a disqualification that has not yet arrived can still be resolved with time.

### Future Disqualifications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ וּמְחוּסַּר זְמַן – פָּטוּר. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי זֶה בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה; שֶׁרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כׇּל שֶׁהוּא רָאוּי לָבֹא לְאַחַר זְמַן – הֲרֵי זֶה בְּלֹא תַּעֲשֶׂה וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כֹּל שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת – אֵין בּוֹ לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to an animal **itself and its offspring** that were slaughtered on the same day, where one violates a prohibition for slaughtering the second, **and** an animal **whose time has not yet** arrived, if one sacrificed it outside the Temple courtyard he is **exempt. Rabbi Shimon says:** For an animal whose time has not yet arrived, **that** person is **in** violation of a mere **prohibition, as Rabbi Shimon says:** With regard to **any** sacrificial animal **that is fit to come** and be sacrificed **after** the passage of **time,** if one sacrificed it outside the courtyard, **that** person is **in** violation of **a prohibition but there is no** liability for ***karet*. And the Rabbis say: In any** case **in which there is no** liability for ***karet* there is no** violation of **a prohibition.**

The *mishnah* extends Rabbi Shimon's principle to two further cases: *oto ve'et beno* — the prohibition against slaughtering a parent and its offspring on the same day — and *mechusar zeman* — an animal that has not yet reached its eighth day of life, as the Torah requires *shivat yamim yihyeh tachat imo*. Rabbi Shimon holds that both of these cases fall under *lo ta'aseh*: one violates the prohibition without incurring *karet*.

### Shimon's Principle

Rabbi Shimon's general principle is then articulated explicitly: *kol she-hu ra'uy lavo le-achar zeman harei zeh belo ta'aseh ve'ein bo karet* — anything which can be brought at a future time falls within the prohibition but without the punishment of *karet*. The logic is that because the time will eventually come when the offering may be brought legitimately, the transgression remains within the orbit of the prohibition even if full liability is not triggered.

### Chachamim Response

The *chachamim* reject this intermediate category entirely: *kol she'ein bo karet ein bo belo ta'aseh* — there is no such middle ground. Either one is liable with *karet* or one is wholly *patur*. This dispute represents a fundamental disagreement about the structure of the prohibition of *shechitat chutz*: whether it admits of gradations or whether it is binary in its application.

## Owner Deficiencies

### Mechussarei Kapparah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְחוּסַּר זְמַן – בֵּין בְּגוּפוֹ, בֵּין בִּבְעָלָיו. אֵיזֶהוּ מְחוּסַּר זְמַן בִּבְעָלָיו? הַזָּב וְהַזָּבָה וְהַיּוֹלֶדֶת וְהַמְּצוֹרָע, שֶׁהִקְרִיבוּ חַטָּאתָם וַאֲשָׁמָם בַּחוּץ – פְּטוּרִין. עוֹלוֹתֵיהֶן וְשַׁלְמֵיהֶן בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna adds: An animal is defined as one **whose time has not yet** arrived, **whether** it is **intrinsically** premature, e.g., doves whose wings have not yet assumed a golden hue or an animal less than seven days old (see Leviticus 22:27), or **whether** it is premature **for its owner.** **Which is** the animal **whose time has not yet** arrived because it is premature **for its owner?** It is the animal of **a man who experiences a gonorrhea-like discharge [*zav*], and a woman who experiences a discharge of uterine blood after her menstrual period [*zava*], and a woman after childbirth, and a leper** whose period of impurity is not yet complete, **where** these owners, who are ritually impure, **sacrificed their sin offerings or guilt offerings outside** the Temple courtyard. In this case **they are exempt,** as they are neither obligated nor permitted to bring those offerings. But if they sacrificed **their burnt offerings or their peace offerings outside** the courtyard **they are liable,** as those offerings may be brought as gift offerings even if their owner is ritually impure.

The *mishnah* now turns to *mechusar zeman bi-ve'alav* — a deficiency of time that pertains not to the animal itself but to its owners. The four cases of *mechussarei kapparah* are enumerated: a male *zav*, a female *zavah*, a *yoledet* — a woman who has given birth — and a *metzora*, whether male or female, who has recovered from *tzara'at*. All four must bring a *korban* as the final step of their purification before they may partake of other *korbanot*. Until that *korban* is brought, they are *mechussarei kapparah*. The *mishnah* will be addressed more fully in *Keritot*, but its application here is within the framework of *mechusar zeman* — it is not the animal that is not yet ready, but the owner who has not yet completed the process.

### Chatat/Asham Exempt

The *mishnah* states: *she-hikrivu chattatam ve-ashamam ba-chutz peturin* — if the owners of these four categories brought their *korban chatat* or *korban asham* outside the Temple, they are *patur* from the prohibition of *shechitat chutz*. The deficiency of the owner renders the *korban chatat* and *korban asham* in their hands analogous to disqualified sacrifices.

### Olah/Shelamim Liable

However, *oloteihen ve-shalmeihan ba-chutz chayavin* — if they brought their *korban olah* or *korban shelamim* outside the Temple, they are *chayav*. The *Gemara* will clarify the distinction, but the essential point is that the *olah* and *shelamim* are not inherently linked to the owner's deficiency in the same way that the *chatat* and *asham* are; those latter sacrifices are brought specifically to effect the *mechussarei kapparah*'s completion of *kaparah*, and in that capacity the owner's deficiency disqualifies them — whereas the *olah* and *shelamim* are not uniquely tied to the owner's incomplete status.

## Non-Central Avodot

### Burning Meat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּעֲלֶה מִבְּשַׂר חַטָּאת, מִבְּשַׂר אָשָׁם, מִבְּשַׂר קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, מִבְּשַׂר קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, וּמוֹתַר הָעוֹמֶר, וּשְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם, וְלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים, וּשְׁיָרֵי מְנָחוֹת;
>
> **Translation:** **One who offers up** outside the Temple courtyard a portion **of the meat of a sin offering** that is eaten; **of the meat of a guilt offering; of the meat of** other **offerings of the most sacred order** that are eaten, e.g., the sheep sacrificed on the festival of *Shavuot*, or **of the meat of offerings of lesser sanctity,** is exempt, as all these are eaten by the priests and not sacrificed on the altar. **And** for the same reason, one who sacrificed a portion of **the surplus of the *omer* offering,** a measure of barley brought as a communal offering on the sixteenth of the Hebrew month of Nisan, after the handful was removed; **or the two loaves,** i.e., the public offering on *Shavuot* of two loaves from the new wheat; **or the shewbread** arranged on the Table each Shabbat in the Sanctuary; **or the remainder of meal offerings,** is also exempt.

The *mishnah* continues with a long list of actions performed outside the Temple for which one is nonetheless *patur*. The first and most obvious category is burning the meat of a *korban chatat* or other *kodshei kodashim* — that is, meat which is designated for consumption by *kohanim* rather than for the *mizbe'ach* — and similarly meat of *kodashim kalim* such as *shelamim*. Burning that meat outside the Temple does not constitute a violation of *ha'alah bachutz*, since it is the portions meant to be consumed, not the portions consigned to the fire of the *mizbe'ach*.

### Permitted Leftovers

Similarly *patur* are: *motar ha-omer* — the remainder of the barley brought on Pesach; *shtei ha-lechem* — the wheat loaves brought on Shavuot; *lechem ha-panim* — the showbreads prepared each Shabbat; and *shirei menachot* — the portion of the *korban minchah* left for the *kohanim* to eat. In all of these cases, what is being burned or consumed outside the Temple is not a sacrificial act directed at the *mizbe'ach* and therefore does not trigger the prohibition.

### Minor Avodot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיּוֹצֵק, וְהַפּוֹתֵת, וְהַבּוֹלֵל, וְהַמּוֹלֵחַ, וְהַמֵּנִיף, וְהַמַּגִּישׁ; וְהַמְסַדֵּר אֶת הַשּׁוּלְחָן, וְהַמֵּטִיב אֶת הַנֵּרוֹת, וְהַקּוֹמֵץ, וְהַמְקַבֵּל דָּמִים בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** likewise with regard to **one who pours** oil onto a meal offering; **and one who breaks** the loaves of a meal offering into pieces; **and one who mixes** oil into the flour of a meal offering; **and one who salts** a meal offering or other offerings; **and one who waves** a meal offering; **and one who brings** a meal offering to the corner of an altar, if he performs these actions outside the courtyard; **and one who arranges** shewbread on **the table** outside the Sanctuary; **and one who removes the ashes** from **the lamps** of the Candelabrum; **and one who removes a handful** from a meal offering; **and one who collects** the **blood** of an offering in a vessel, if he did so **outside** the Temple courtyard: In all of these cases he is **exempt.** This is because one is liable only if he performs an action similar to sacrifice that completes the sacrificial service, while all of these actions are ones that are normally followed by additional sacrificial rites.

Also *patur*: *ha-yotzek ve-ha-bolel* — one who sprinkles or mixes oil onto the flour of a *minchah*; *ha-potet* — one who crumbles the *minchah* breads into the required pieces before they are placed on the *mizbe'ach*; *ha-nimlach* — one who applies salt; *ha-menif* — one who performs the *tenufah*, the waving of various sacrifices; and *ha-magish* — the act of bringing the offering forward, which is part of the process of the *korban minchah* as will be discussed at length in the fifth chapter of *Menachot*.

### Priestly Acts

The *mishnah* adds: *ha-mesader et ha-shulchan* — one who arranges the *lechem ha-panim* on the *shulchan*; *ha-metiv et ha-nerot* — one who cleans and relights the lamps of the *menorah*; *ha-kometz* — one who performs the *kemitzah* of the *korban minchah*; and *ha-mekabel damim ba-chutz* — one who receives the blood in a receptacle outside the Temple. All of these are *patur*.

It is worth noting that *ha-metiv et ha-nerot* and *ha-mesader et ha-shulchan* are among the more striking examples on this list, since the *menorah* and *shulchan* are located in the *Heichal*, a space no *Yisrael* is permitted to enter. One suggestion that has been raised is that perhaps a *Yisrael* could technically light the *menorah* from a great distance through the doorway — though the practical difficulties are formidable. The deeper point may be that the *avodah* itself carries no inherent requirement of *kehunah*, even if circumstantially one must be a *kohen* to access the location.

### Lesser Avodot Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו לֹא מִשּׁוּם זָרוּת, וְלֹא מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה, וְלֹא מִשּׁוּם מְחוּסַּר בְּגָדִים, וְלֹא מִשּׁוּם [שֶׁלֹּא] רְחוּץ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **And one is** likewise **not liable for** any of these actions, **neither due to** the prohibition against **a non-priest** performing the Temple service, **nor due to** the prohibition against performing the Temple service in a state of **ritual impurity, nor due to** the prohibition against a priest **lacking** the requisite priestly **vestments** while performing the Temple service, **nor due to** the prohibition against performing the Temple service **without washing** one's **hands and feet.**

Having enumerated all of these cases, the *mishnah* states: *ein chayavin alav lo mishum zarut ve-lo mishum tumah ve-lo mishum mechusar bgadim ve-lo mishum rechutz yadayim ve-raglayim* — none of the violations that ordinarily accompany improper performance of the Temple service apply here: neither *zarut* if a non-*kohen* performs them, nor *tumah* if a *tamei* person performs them, nor *mechusar bgadim* for lacking the *bigdei kehunah*, nor failure to wash hands and feet. The rationale is that all of these *avodot* are of a lesser rank — they are not among the most central and essential acts of the sacrificial service — and therefore they do not carry the full suite of prohibitions and penalties that attach to the primary *avodot*.

## Heter Bamot History

### Pre-Mishkan Period

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁלֹּא הוּקַם הַמִּשְׁכָּן – הָיוּ הַבָּמוֹת מוּתָּרוֹת, וַעֲבוֹדָה בִּבְכוֹרוֹת; וּמִשֶּׁהוּקַם הַמִּשְׁכָּן – נֶאֶסְרוּ הַבָּמוֹת, וַעֲבוֹדָה בַּכֹּהֲנִים. קׇדְשֵׁי קֳדָשִׁים נֶאֱכָלִין לִפְנִים מִן הַקְּלָעִים, וְקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים בְּכׇל מַחֲנֵה יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Until the Tabernacle was established,** private **altars were permitted and** the sacrificial **service** was performed **by the firstborn. And from** the time **that the Tabernacle was established,** private **altars were prohibited and** the sacrificial **service** was performed **by the priests. Offerings of the most sacred order were** then **eaten within the curtains** surrounding the courtyard of the Tabernacle in the wilderness **and offerings of lesser sanctity** were eaten **throughout the camp of Israel.**

The *mishnah* begins: *ad shelo hukam ha-mishkan, hayu ha-bamot mutarot, ve-avodah bi-vchorot* — before the *Mishkan* was ever built, outside altars were permitted and the sacrificial service was performed by the firstborn. This formulation is somewhat complex historically: the patriarchs brought *korbanot*, Moshe brought various *korbanot*, and not all of them were firstborns. The *mishnah* likely refers specifically to the *korban pesach* in Egypt and to the *korbanot* offered at Har Sinai in the period before the *Mishkan* was commanded or constructed, during which several months elapsed.

### Mishkan Established

*Mishehukam ha-mishkan ne'esru ha-bamot, ve-avodah b'kohanim* — once the *Mishkan* was erected, outside altars were prohibited and the service transferred to the *kohanim*. This is precisely why, at the beginning of *Sefer Bamidbar*, there are the elaborate arrangements for exchanging the tribe of Levi for all the firstborns among *Bnei Yisrael* — the right to perform the service was transferred from the *bechorim* to the *kohanim* and *Leviyim*, and the *bechorim* were thereby relieved of their original role. Some have suggested that this sheds light on why Yaakov was so eager to acquire the *bechorah* from Esav — to prevent Esav from serving in any sense in the future *Beit HaMikdash*.

The *mishnah* further describes that *kodshei kodashim* — like the *korban chatat*, *korban asham*, and the *zivchei shalmei tzibur* on Shavuot — were eaten *lifnim min ha-kela'im*, within the curtains of the *Mishkan*. Though this phrase appears anachronistically throughout *Zevachim* as an overlay on the *Beit HaMikdash*, here it is used literally, since we are speaking of the actual fabric curtains of the *Mishkan*. *Kodashim kalim* could be eaten throughout the entire *machaneh Yisrael*. The three concentric camps — *machaneh shekhinah* (the *Mishkan* itself), *machaneh leviyah* (surrounding the *Mishkan*), and *machaneh Yisrael* (the entire encampment of the Jewish people) — correspond respectively to the Temple precinct, the Temple Mount, and the halakhically designated city of Jerusalem. There is even a *sugya* in the Gemara elsewhere discussing whether the borders of Jerusalem might be greatly expanded in the future, as implied in *Sefer Yechezkel* toward its conclusion.

### Gilgal Period

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּאוּ לַגִּלְגָּל – הוּתְּרוּ הַבָּמוֹת; קׇדְשֵׁי קֳדָשִׁים נֶאֱכָלִין לִפְנִים מִן הַקְּלָעִים, וְקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים בְּכׇל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** When the Jewish people **arrived at Gilgal** private **altars were permitted, offerings of the most sacred order were** then **eaten within the curtains, and offerings of lesser sanctity** were eaten **anywhere.**

*Ba'u le-Gilgal hutteru ha-bamot* — when the *Mishkan* was brought to Gilgal in the period of Yehoshua, outside altars were once again permitted. *Kodshei kodashim* could be eaten within the curtains of the *Mishkan*, and *kodashim kalim* could be eaten *bekhol makom* — anywhere at all. In Gilgal there was no designation of *machaneh Yisrael* as a bounded area, since the Jewish people were now spread across the Land of Israel and the *Mishkan* was in one location with no corresponding camp structure. Therefore, only the *machaneh shekhinah* — the curtained interior — defined what counted as the place for *kodshei kodashim*, while for *kodashim kalim* there was no geographic boundary, which is precisely the nature of the *heter bamot*: one could bring a *korban shelamim* on a local *bamah* and eat it there.

### Shiloh Period

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּאוּ לְשִׁילֹה – נֶאֶסְרוּ הַבָּמוֹת. וְלֹא הָיָה שָׁם תִּקְרָה; אֶלָּא בֵּית אֲבָנִים בִּלְבָד מִלְּלַמַּטָּן, וְהַיְּרִיעוֹת מִלְּמַעְלָן; וְהִיא הָיְתָה ״מְנוּחָה״. קׇדְשֵׁי קֳדָשִׁים נֶאֱכָלִין לִפְנִים מִן הַקְּלָעִים, וְקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים וּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי בְּכׇל הָרוֹאֶה.
>
> **Translation:** When **they arrived at Shiloh,** private **altars were prohibited. And there was no roof** of wood or stone **there,** i.e., in the Tabernacle in Shiloh; **rather** there was **only a building of stone below and the curtains** of the roof of the Tabernacle were spread **above it. And** the period that the Tabernacle was in Shiloh **was** characterized in the Torah as **"rest"** in the verse: "For you have not as yet come to the rest and to the inheritance, which the Lord your God has given you" (Deuteronomy 12:9). **Offerings of the most sacred order were** then **eaten within the curtains** in the courtyard of the Tent of Meeting, **and offerings of lesser sanctity and second tithe** were eaten **in any** place **that overlooks** Shiloh.

*Ba'u le-Shilo ne'esru ha-bamot* — when the *Mishkan* was brought to Shiloh, outside altars were once again prohibited. At Shiloh a significant structural change occurred: rather than the wooden beams of the portable *Mishkan*, stone walls were built to support the structure, while the curtains and leather coverings were retained above. *Velo hayah sham tikrah, ella bayit shel avanim mil-mattan, yeri'ot mil-ma'alan* — there was no roof, just stone walls below and curtains on top. This more permanent physical structure gave Shiloh a more substantive and settled character.

The basis for the prohibition is the verse in Devarim 12:9: *ki lo vatem ad attah el ha-menuchah* — you have not yet come to the *menuchah*. Once the *Mishkan* reached Shiloh, which the Gemara identifies as the *menuchah*, the prohibition of *bamot* resumed. The structure at Shiloh was stable and designated as a central shrine, even if it was not yet the *Beit HaMikdash*, and that stability sufficed to reimpose *issur bamot*. *Kodashim kalim* and *ma'aser sheni* could be eaten *bekhol ha-ro'eh* — anywhere within sight of the *Mishkan* in Shiloh — not within the boundaries of a city, but within the visual lines of sight.

### Nov & Givon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּאוּ לְנוֹב וְגִבְעוֹן – הוּתְּרוּ הַבָּמוֹת. קׇדְשֵׁי קֳדָשִׁים נֶאֱכָלִין לִפְנִים מִן הַקְּלָעִים, וקדשים קַלִּים בְּכׇל עָרֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** When Shiloh was destroyed (see I Samuel 4:18), the Jewish people **arrived** with the Tabernacle **at Nov, and** later at **Gibeon,** and private **altars were permitted. Offerings of the most sacred order were** then **eaten within the curtains** in the courtyard of the Tent of Meeting, and **offerings of lesser sanctity** were eaten **in all the cities of** Eretz **Yisrael.**

*Ba'u le-Nov u-le-Giv'on hutteru ha-bamot* — when the *Mishkan* was brought to Nob and subsequently to Gibeon (with the *aron* absent from both locations), outside altars were once again permitted. *Kodshei kodashim* were eaten within the curtains, and *kodashim kalim bekhol arei Yisrael* — in any city of Israel. This is a more restricted formulation than the *bekhol makom* of the Gilgal period, where there was no geographic boundary at all; here the *mishnah* specifies *arei Yisrael*, restricting consumption to within city limits.

### Jerusalem Final

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּאוּ לִירוּשָׁלַיִם – נֶאֶסְרוּ הַבָּמוֹת וְלֹא הָיָה לָהֶן הֶיתֵּר, וְהִיא הָיְתָה ״נַחֲלָה״. קׇדְשֵׁי קֳדָשִׁים נֶאֱכָלִין לִפְנִים מִן הַקְּלָעִים, קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים וּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי לִפְנִים מִן הַחוֹמָה.
>
> **Translation:** When the Jewish people **arrived at Jerusalem** and built the Temple during the reign of Solomon, private **altars were prohibited, and** private altars **did not have** a subsequent period when they were **permitted. And** the Temple in Jerusalem **was** characterized as **"inheritance"** in the verse: "For you have not as yet come to the rest and to the inheritance, which the Lord your God has given you." **Offerings of the most sacred order were** then **eaten within the curtains,** i.e., in the Temple courtyard, and **offerings of lesser sanctity and second tithe** were eaten **within the walls** of the city, whose legal status was that of the Israelite camp in the wilderness.

*Ba'u li-Yerushalayim ne'esru ha-bamot, velo hayah lahem od hetter* — once everything was brought to Jerusalem and the first *Beit HaMikdash* was built, the period of *bamot* ended permanently. There was no further *hetter*. The Scriptural basis is the continuation of the verse from Devarim: *el ha-menuchah ve'el ha-nachalah* — the *menuchah* was Shiloh, and the *nachalah* is Jerusalem. Once the *nachalah* was established with the building of the *Beit HaMikdash*, the *heter bamot* was foreclosed entirely and irrevocably.

*Kodashim kalim* and *ma'aser sheni* could now be eaten only *lifnim min ha-chomah* — within the walls of Jerusalem. This provides a striking halachic context for the construction of Jerusalem's walls, as recounted in *Sefer Nechemiah*: beyond the obvious concerns of security and civic stature, there was a genuine halachic reason to define and maintain the city's walls.

## Sanctification Timing

### Both During Issur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַקֳּדָשִׁים שֶׁהִקְדִּישָׁן בִּשְׁעַת אִיסּוּר הַבָּמוֹת, וְהִקְרִיבָן בִּשְׁעַת אִיסּוּר הַבָּמוֹת מִבַּחוּץ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ בַּעֲשֵׂה וְלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **all offerings that one consecrated during a period of prohibition of** private **altars and sacrificed during a period of prohibition of** private **altars,** if he sacrificed them **outside** their designated area, for **these** animals he is **in** violation of both the **positive mitzva** to sacrifice the offering in the place chosen by God **and** the **prohibition** against sacrificing them on a private altar, **and he is liable** to receive ***karet* for** doing **it.**

The *mishnah* now addresses the question of sacrifices sanctified and offered during different periods of *issur bamot* and *heter bamot* — a scenario that could only have arisen historically during the transitions between these periods, and which has no future applicability, but which nonetheless defines the boundaries of the prohibition. The first case: *she-hikdishan bi-sha'at issur ha-bamot ve-hikrivan bi-sha'at issur ha-bamot bachutz* — if an animal was sanctified during a period when *bamot* were prohibited, and was then offered outside the Temple also during a period when *bamot* were prohibited, the full array of consequences applies: *harei elu b'aseh ve-lo ta'aseh ve-chayavin aleihem karet* — one has violated a *mitzvat aseh*, a *mitzvat lo ta'aseh*, and incurs *karet*.

### Sanctify Hetter Offer Is…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִקְדִּישָׁן בִּשְׁעַת הֶיתֵּר הַבָּמוֹת, וְהִקְרִיבָן בִּשְׁעַת אִיסּוּר הַבָּמוֹת בַּחוּץ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ בַּעֲשֵׂה וְלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה, וְאֵין חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** If **one consecrated** the animals **during a period of permitting of** private **altars and sacrificed them during a period of prohibition of** private **altars, outside** their designated area, for **these** animals he is **in** violation of **a positive mitzva and a prohibition, but he is not liable to** receive ***karet* for** sacrificing **them.**

The second case: *hikdishan bi-sha'at hetter bamot ve-hikrivan bi-sha'at issur ha-bamot* — if the animal was sanctified during a period when *bamot* were allowed, and then offered outside the Temple during a period when *bamot* were prohibited. This could occur if, for example, the animal was sanctified while *bamot* were allowed, and then the *Beit HaMikdash* was built in Jerusalem before the sacrifice was offered, and the owner was still in the countryside when he brought it as a sacrifice. In such a case: *harei elu b'aseh ve-lo ta'aseh ve-ein chayavin aleihem karet* — one violates both an *aseh* and a *lo ta'aseh*, but does not receive *karet*. This represents exactly the intermediate category that Rabbi Shimon had articulated earlier in the chapter, and it may suggest that even the *chachamim* agree to Rabbi Shimon's framework in these historical cases, even if they dispute his principle for the categories addressed at the beginning of the *perek*.

### Sanctify Issur Offer Het…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִקְדִּישָׁן בִּשְׁעַת אִיסּוּר הַבָּמוֹת, וְהִקְרִיבָן בִּשְׁעַת הֶיתֵּר הַבָּמוֹת – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ בַּעֲשֵׂה, וְאֵין בָּהֶן לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** If **he consecrated** the animals **during a period of prohibition of** private **altars and sacrificed them during a period of permitting of** private **altars,** outside their designated area, for **these** animals he is **in** violation of **a positive mitzva** for failure to bring it to the Tabernacle, **but these** animals **are not subject to a prohibition,** as it is permitted to sacrifice on a private altar.

The third case is the inverse: *hikdishan bi-sha'at issur ha-bamot ve-hikrivan bi-sha'at hetter bamot* — the animal was sanctified during a period of *issur bamot*, such as the time of Shiloh, but was then offered during a subsequent period of *heter bamot*, such as Nob. In this case: *harei elu b'aseh ve-ein bahem lo ta'aseh* — one violates a *mitzvat aseh* only, without any *lo ta'aseh*, since at the time of the offering one is in a period of *heter bamot*.

## Central vs Local

### Mishkan Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ קָדָשִׁים קְרֵבִין בַּמִּשְׁכָּן: קָדָשִׁים שֶׁהוּקְדְּשׁוּ לְמִשְׁכָּן, קׇרְבְּנוֹת צִיבּוּר קְרֵבִין בַּמִּשְׁכָּן, וְקׇרְבְּנוֹת הַיָּחִיד בְּבָמָה. קׇרְבְּנוֹת הַיָּחִיד שֶׁהוּקְדְּשׁוּ לַמִּשְׁכָּן – יַקְרִיבוּ בַּמִּשְׁכָּן, וְאִם הִקְרִיבָן בְּבָמָה – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And these are** the **sacrificial** items that **are sacrificed** only **in the Tabernacle** even when private altars are permitted: **Sacrificial** animals **that were** presumed to be **consecrated for** sacrifice in **the Tabernacle.** Therefore, **communal offerings are sacrificed in the Tabernacle, but offerings of an individual** may be sacrificed **on** a private **altar.** In addition, with regard to **offerings of an individual that were consecrated** expressly **for** sacrifice in **the Tabernacle, one must sacrifice** them **in the Tabernacle. But if he sacrificed them on** a private **altar,** he is **exempt.**

The *mishnah* proceeds to clarify which sacrifices must be brought specifically at the *Mishkan* and which may be brought at a local *bamah* during periods when *bamot* were permitted. *Elu kodashim krevin ba-Mishkan — Kodashim she'hukdeshu le-Mishkan* — those sacrifices that were sanctified explicitly for the *Mishkan* must be brought there. Similarly, *korbanot ha-tzibur krevin ba-Mishkan* — communal sacrifices, offered on behalf of the *tzibur*, must be brought at the central shrine regardless.

*Korbanot ha-yachid b'bamah* — individual sacrifices may be brought on a local *bamah* during the periods of *heter bamot*. However, *korbanot ha-yachid she-hukdeshu la-Mishkan, yikrevu ba-Mishkan* — if an individual specifically sanctified his animal with the intent of bringing it to the *Mishkan*, he is obligated to do so. *Ve'im hikrivan b'bamah patur* — but if he nonetheless brought it on a *bamah be-di'avad*, he is *patur*. He has not violated a prohibition, but he had a *le-chatchilah* obligation to bring it to the *Mishkan* as he had declared.

## Altar Differences

### Smicha & Direction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה בֵּין בָּמַת יָחִיד לְבָמַת צִיבּוּר? סְמִיכָה, וּשְׁחִיטַת צָפוֹן, וּמַתַּן סָבִיב, וּתְנוּפָה, וְהַגָּשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **And what** is the difference **between the** private **altar of an individual and the public altar** at the site of the Tabernacle when it was located in Gilgal, Nov, and Gibeon? It is that on a private altar there is no **placing** of **hands** on the head of an offering, no **slaughter in the north,** no **placement** of blood **around** all sides of the altar in offerings for which this is required, no **waving** of meal offerings, **and** no **bringing** of meal offerings to the corner of the altar prior to removal of the handful.

The *mishnah* enumerates the ways in which the *bamat ha-yachid* — an individual's private altar — differs from the *bamat ha-tzibur*, the central *Mishkan* which is itself called a *bamah gedolah* during periods of *heter bamot*. The following requirements apply only at the central shrine and not at a private *bamah*: *smicha* — the laying of hands on the animal before slaughter — does not apply at a *bamah*. Nor does *shechitat tzafon* — the requirement that *kodshei kodashim* such as *chatat*, *asham*, and *olah* be slaughtered on the north side of the *mizbe'ach*.

*Matan saviv* — going around the four corners of the *mizbe'ach* to apply the blood — does not apply at a *bamah*. *Tenufah* — the waving of the various portions of the *korban shelamim* — also does not apply at a *bamah*. Nor does *hagashah* — the bringing-forward that is part of the *korban minchah* process.

### Rabbi Yehuda Mincha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵין מִנְחָה בְּבָמָה, וְכִיהוּן, וּבִגְדֵי שָׁרֵת, וּכְלֵי שָׁרֵת, וְרֵיחַ נִיחוֹחַ, וּמְחִיצָה לְדָמִים, וְרִיחוּץ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: There is no meal offering** sacrificed **on an altar** outside the Temple. **And** requiring a member of the **priesthood** to perform the sacrificial rites, the priestly **service vestments,** the **service vessels,** the **pleasing aroma** to God, the **partition for the blood,** i.e., the red line dividing the upper and lower halves of the altar, **and** the priest's **washing of hands and feet** before his service all do not apply to sacrifice on private altars, as the service there need not be performed by priests nor follow all the protocols of the Temple service.

Rabbi Yehuda goes further: *ein minchah ba-bamah* — there is no *minchah* offering at a *bamah* at all. Additionally, Rabbi Yehuda holds *ve-kehun* — there is actually no requirement for *kohanim* at a *bamah*; even a *zar* could bring *korbanot* on a private *bamah*.

There is also no requirement for *bigdei sharet* — the priestly vestments are called *bigdei sharet* here rather than *bigdei kehunah* — and no requirement for *klei sharet*, sanctified vessels. There is no requirement to use formally designated containers for, for example, receiving the blood. And there is no *reiach nichoach* — the sacrifice does not achieve the same quality of spiritual acceptability as one brought at the central shrine. Rashi notes that *reiach nichoach* here refers to the principle established in the chapter of *Beit Shammai*: a sacrifice must be offered with the proper intent among the six purposes for which a sacrifice is brought (*leshem shishah devarim*), and there is no requirement for this mental awareness at a *bamah* because the offering does not achieve the same *reiach nichoach*.

### No Mizbe'ach Division

*Umechetza le-damim* — the *chut ha-sikra*, the red line that divides the *mizbe'ach* into its upper half (for blood applied above the line) and lower half (for blood applied below), does not apply at a *bamah*. According to Rabbi Yehuda, this is another element absent from the *bamah*. *Urechitzat yadayim ve-raglayim* — the washing of the hands and feet before performing the service is also not required at a *bamah*.

### Time & Notar Equal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הַזְּמַן, הַנּוֹתָר וְהַטָּמֵא – שָׁוִין בָּזֶה וּבָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **But the** intent to sacrifice or partake of the offering beyond its designated **time,** which renders the offering *piggul*; the *halakha* of portions of **the** offering **left over [*notar*]** beyond the time it may be eaten; **and the** prohibition against eating consecrated meat while **ritually impure are equal in this,** a private altar, **and that,** a public altar.

Despite all of these differences, certain requirements apply equally at both the central shrine and the private *bamah*: *hazeman ve-ha-notar* — the laws of improper intention regarding time (*piggul*) and the laws of *notar*, sacrificial portions that have remained beyond their permitted time. And *ha-tamei* — if the sacrifice or its portions become *tamei*, they are disqualified. These laws are *shavim ba-zeh u-va-zeh* — identical in both contexts. With that, the *mishnayot* of *Masechet Zevachim* are complete.
