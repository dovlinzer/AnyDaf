# Zevachim 112 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blood in Two Cups

### Chatat Blood Divided

The Gemara at the top of 112 wraps up the conclusion of the thirteenth chapter, *Hashochet Veha'aleh*, returning briefly to the preceding *mishnah*. The *mishnah* had presented the case of a *korban chatat* whose blood was collected in a single cup, with part of the blood sprinkled outside the Temple and the remainder sprinkled inside. The Gemara opens with the observation: *bishlama bachutz vehazar venatan bifnim* — it is perfectly understandable why one would be *chayav* if the blood was first applied outside and the remainder then brought inside, since the entire quantity had been suitable to be applied inside; by performing the *zerikah* outside, one violated the prohibition of *zerikah bachutz*. But the reverse case — *aval bifnim veha'alah bachutz* — where the blood was first applied inside and then the remainder taken outside, is more difficult. The remainder is merely *shi'arayim*, the leftover blood, and having already fulfilled the *zerikah* obligation, one might expect that taking the *shi'arayim* outside carries no prohibition of substance.

### Rabbi Nechemya Position

To account for this case, the Gemara proposes: *ha mani Rabbi Nechemya hi* — the *mishnah* follows the opinion of Rabbi Nechemya, who holds that *she'arey dam she'hikrivan bachutz chayav*, even the leftover blood that does not effect the *kaparah* falls within the prohibition of *hakravah bachutz*. Rabbi Nechemya thus adopts an expansive understanding of the prohibition, extending it even to the *shi'arey hadam*.

### Reisha vs Seifa

However, if one attributes the *mishnah* to Rabbi Nechemya, the *seifa* becomes problematic. The later clause of the *mishnah* addresses blood collected in two separate cups: if both cups are taken inside one is *patur*; if both are taken outside one is *chayav*; but if one is applied inside and one outside, one is *patur*. This sits uneasily with Rabbi Nechemya, who would presumably hold one *chayav* even for the second cup brought outside, since that blood — though now rendered *shi'arayim* after the first cup fulfilled the *zerikah* — was still brought beyond the Temple. The Gemara therefore concludes: *seifa ata l'Tana Kama d'Rabbi Elazar be'Rabbi Shimon* — while the *reisha* of the *mishnah* reflects Rabbi Nechemya's position, the *seifa* follows the opinion of Rabbi Elazar be'Rabbi Shimon, who articulates the principle *kos oseh dichei lechavero*: when blood is divided between two cups and one cup becomes disqualified, it renders the partner cup disqualified as well. The Rambam notes in his *Perush ha-Mishnayot* that this *sugya* makes clear that Rabbi Nechemya's position is rejected, and the Gemara is merely attempting to align parts of the *mishnah* with his view in order to account for the earlier clause.

### Two Animals Designated

The *mishnah* had then offered a *lema hadavar domeh* — an analogy comparing the case of blood divided between two cups to the following scenario: a person designated a *korban chatat*, it was lost, he designated a second animal as a replacement, and then the first animal was found. Now both animals stand as designated *korbanot chatat*. If both are slaughtered inside the *Beit HaMikdash* one is *patur*; if both are slaughtered outside one is *chayav*; if one is slaughtered inside and one outside one is *patur*. However, if the outside animal is slaughtered first and then the inside animal, one is *chayav* for the first, and the second still effects *kaparah*.

The Gemara asks: *lama li?* Why does the *mishnah* introduce this entire analogy at all? The answer is: *ha mani Rebbi hi* — this reflects the opinion of Rebbi, who holds *avudah beshas hafrasha meisah*: if the animal was lost at the time the replacement was designated, that first animal must die — it constitutes a sixth category of *chatas meisos* beyond the standard five. The purpose of the analogy is to explain why both animals are standing and must be addressed at all: the reason is precisely that the animal was lost in the interim, not that one tried to designate two *chatat* animals simultaneously from the outset. For if one had tried to designate two *korbanot* for the same liability at once, one of them would automatically revert to being a *korban olah*, as Rav Huna states in the name of Rav: an *asham* that was disqualified and sent to pasture, and then slaughtered *stam* without any particular intent, is valid as an *olah*.

### Rebbi's Limitations

However, the Gemara immediately challenges this: *mi dami? hatam asham zachar ve'olah zachar* — that case works because both the *asham* and the *olah* are male animals. But a *korban chatat* is a female animal, and a *korban olah* may only be brought from a male animal. Accordingly, one could not simply say that the second *chatat* animal reverts to an *olah*. Rabbi Chiyya of Vastinya therefore limits Rebbi's ruling to the very narrow case of *seir nasi* — the *chatat* of a ruler — which at the time of the *Beit Sheni* had perhaps highly limited application. Thus the *sugya* concludes that Rebbi's principle of *avudah beshas hafrasha meisah*, while compelling in theory, is confined by the *Amoraim* to a specific and uncommon case, since the general problem of the *chatat*'s being a female animal prevents it from reverting to any other category. With this, the Gemara closes the thirteenth chapter.

## Fourteenth Chapter Intro

### Parah & Seir

The fourteenth chapter, *Paras Chatas*, opens with the *mishnah* stating: *paras chatas she-serafah chutz mi-gitah* — if the *parah adumah* was burned outside its designated pit on Har ha-Zeitim, or if the *seir hamishtaleach* — which is supposed to be pushed off a cliff — was instead offered as a sacrifice outside the Temple, one is *patur* from the prohibition of *shechitat chutz*. The Scriptural basis is the verse *ve'el pesach ohel moed lo hevi'o*, from which the *mishnah* derives the principle: *kol she'ein ra'uy lavo el pesach ohel moed ein chayavin alav* — anything which has no obligation to be brought to the entrance of the *Ohel Moed* does not fall within the scope of the prohibition.

### Inherently Disqualified

The following *mishnah* catalogs a series of animals whose disqualification is inherent to their bodies — *begufan*: an animal involved in an act of bestiality (*rove'ah* or *nirba'at*), an animal designated or worshipped for *avodah zarah* (*muktzeh* or *ne'evad*), an animal given as *etnan zonah* or *mechir kelev*, a crossbred animal (*kilayim*), a *treifah*, or an animal born through the abdominal wall (*yotzei dofen*). If any of these animals were slaughtered outside the Temple one is *patur* from *shechitat chutz*. The Scriptural basis cited here is slightly different from the previous *mishnah*: *lifnei mishkan Hashem*, suggesting that these animals are abhorrent to God, and their exclusion thus derives from a different dimension of the verse than the quasi-sacrificial cases addressed earlier.

### Rabbi Shimon Lo Ta'aseh

The next *mishnah* addresses *ba'alei mumim* — animals with physical deformities, whether permanent (*kevu'im*) or temporary (*overim*). The *tanna kamma* holds that slaughtering any such animal outside the Temple renders one *patur*. Rabbi Shimon introduces an intermediate category. For permanent deformities he agrees: *patur*. But for temporary deformities he says *ba'alei mumim overim belo ta'aseh* — one violates the prohibition without incurring *karet*. This intermediate position — neither fully *chayav* nor fully *patur* — is the signature of Rabbi Shimon's approach throughout this chapter. The category of *lo ta'aseh* here does not mean one receives lashes; rather, it means one has transgressed the prohibition in a manner that, due to some deficiency, does not carry the penalty to its fullest extent.

### Age Disqualifications

The same structure applies to bird sacrifices. *Torim shelo higi'a zemanam* — turtledoves that have not yet reached their proper age — and *bnei yonah she-avar zemanam* — doves that have aged past their permissible stage — are invalid because turtledoves must be brought as older birds and doves must be brought as young birds. The *tanna kamma* holds that bringing either category outside the Temple renders one *patur*. Rabbi Shimon distinguishes: *bnei yonah she-avar zemanam patur* — doves that are too old, *patur* — but *torim shelo higi'a zemanam belo ta'aseh* — turtledoves that are too young incur a violation of the prohibition without *karet*. Rabbi Shimon's reasoning reflects a consistent principle: a disqualification that lies in the past cannot be corrected, while a disqualification that has not yet arrived can still be resolved with time.

### Future Disqualifications

The *mishnah* extends Rabbi Shimon's principle to two further cases: *oto ve'et beno* — the prohibition against slaughtering a parent and its offspring on the same day — and *mechusar zeman* — an animal that has not yet reached its eighth day of life, as the Torah requires *shivat yamim yihyeh tachat imo*. Rabbi Shimon holds that both of these cases fall under *lo ta'aseh*: one violates the prohibition without incurring *karet*.

### Shimon's Principle

Rabbi Shimon's general principle is then articulated explicitly: *kol she-hu ra'uy lavo le-achar zeman harei zeh belo ta'aseh ve'ein bo karet* — anything which can be brought at a future time falls within the prohibition but without the punishment of *karet*. The logic is that because the time will eventually come when the offering may be brought legitimately, the transgression remains within the orbit of the prohibition even if full liability is not triggered.

### Chachamim Response

The *chachamim* reject this intermediate category entirely: *kol she'ein bo karet ein bo belo ta'aseh* — there is no such middle ground. Either one is liable with *karet* or one is wholly *patur*. This dispute represents a fundamental disagreement about the structure of the prohibition of *shechitat chutz*: whether it admits of gradations or whether it is binary in its application.

## Owner Deficiencies

### Mechussarei Kapparah

The *mishnah* now turns to *mechusar zeman bi-ve'alav* — a deficiency of time that pertains not to the animal itself but to its owners. The four cases of *mechussarei kapparah* are enumerated: a male *zav*, a female *zavah*, a *yoledet* — a woman who has given birth — and a *metzora*, whether male or female, who has recovered from *tzara'at*. All four must bring a *korban* as the final step of their purification before they may partake of other *korbanot*. Until that *korban* is brought, they are *mechussarei kapparah*. The *mishnah* will be addressed more fully in *Keritot*, but its application here is within the framework of *mechusar zeman* — it is not the animal that is not yet ready, but the owner who has not yet completed the process.

### Chatat/Asham Exempt

The *mishnah* states: *she-hikrivu chattatam ve-ashamam ba-chutz peturin* — if the owners of these four categories brought their *korban chatat* or *korban asham* outside the Temple, they are *patur* from the prohibition of *shechitat chutz*. The deficiency of the owner renders the *korban chatat* and *korban asham* in their hands analogous to disqualified sacrifices.

### Olah/Shelamim Liable

However, *oloteihen ve-shalmeihan ba-chutz chayavin* — if they brought their *korban olah* or *korban shelamim* outside the Temple, they are *chayav*. The *Gemara* will clarify the distinction, but the essential point is that the *olah* and *shelamim* are not inherently linked to the owner's deficiency in the same way that the *chatat* and *asham* are; those latter sacrifices are brought specifically to effect the *mechussarei kapparah*'s completion of *kaparah*, and in that capacity the owner's deficiency disqualifies them — whereas the *olah* and *shelamim* are not uniquely tied to the owner's incomplete status.

## Non-Central Avodot

### Burning Meat

The *mishnah* continues with a long list of actions performed outside the Temple for which one is nonetheless *patur*. The first and most obvious category is burning the meat of a *korban chatat* or other *kodshei kodashim* — that is, meat which is designated for consumption by *kohanim* rather than for the *mizbe'ach* — and similarly meat of *kodashim kalim* such as *shelamim*. Burning that meat outside the Temple does not constitute a violation of *ha'alah bachutz*, since it is the portions meant to be consumed, not the portions consigned to the fire of the *mizbe'ach*.

### Permitted Leftovers

Similarly *patur* are: *motar ha-omer* — the remainder of the barley brought on Pesach; *shtei ha-lechem* — the wheat loaves brought on Shavuot; *lechem ha-panim* — the showbreads prepared each Shabbat; and *shirei menachot* — the portion of the *korban minchah* left for the *kohanim* to eat. In all of these cases, what is being burned or consumed outside the Temple is not a sacrificial act directed at the *mizbe'ach* and therefore does not trigger the prohibition.

### Minor Avodot

Also *patur*: *ha-yotzek ve-ha-bolel* — one who sprinkles or mixes oil onto the flour of a *minchah*; *ha-potet* — one who crumbles the *minchah* breads into the required pieces before they are placed on the *mizbe'ach*; *ha-nimlach* — one who applies salt; *ha-menif* — one who performs the *tenufah*, the waving of various sacrifices; and *ha-magish* — the act of bringing the offering forward, which is part of the process of the *korban minchah* as will be discussed at length in the fifth chapter of *Menachot*.

### Priestly Acts

The *mishnah* adds: *ha-mesader et ha-shulchan* — one who arranges the *lechem ha-panim* on the *shulchan*; *ha-metiv et ha-nerot* — one who cleans and relights the lamps of the *menorah*; *ha-kometz* — one who performs the *kemitzah* of the *korban minchah*; and *ha-mekabel damim ba-chutz* — one who receives the blood in a receptacle outside the Temple. All of these are *patur*.

It is worth noting that *ha-metiv et ha-nerot* and *ha-mesader et ha-shulchan* are among the more striking examples on this list, since the *menorah* and *shulchan* are located in the *Heichal*, a space no *Yisrael* is permitted to enter. One suggestion that has been raised is that perhaps a *Yisrael* could technically light the *menorah* from a great distance through the doorway — though the practical difficulties are formidable. The deeper point may be that the *avodah* itself carries no inherent requirement of *kehunah*, even if circumstantially one must be a *kohen* to access the location.

### Lesser Avodot Rationale

Having enumerated all of these cases, the *mishnah* states: *ein chayavin alav lo mishum zarut ve-lo mishum tumah ve-lo mishum mechusar bgadim ve-lo mishum rechutz yadayim ve-raglayim* — none of the violations that ordinarily accompany improper performance of the Temple service apply here: neither *zarut* if a non-*kohen* performs them, nor *tumah* if a *tamei* person performs them, nor *mechusar bgadim* for lacking the *bigdei kehunah*, nor failure to wash hands and feet. The rationale is that all of these *avodot* are of a lesser rank — they are not among the most central and essential acts of the sacrificial service — and therefore they do not carry the full suite of prohibitions and penalties that attach to the primary *avodot*.

## Heter Bamot History

### Pre-Mishkan Period

The *mishnah* now pivots to an extended historical excursus on the periods of *heter bamot* and *issur bamot* — times when outside altars were permitted versus prohibited. This material is, in a certain sense, highly anachronistic: it has no future practical application, since the prohibition of *bamot* has been in effect since the building of the first *Beit HaMikdash*. Nonetheless, it constitutes a coherent and historically significant area of *halachah*.

The *mishnah* begins: *ad shelo hukam ha-mishkan, hayu ha-bamot mutarot, ve-avodah bi-vchorot* — before the *Mishkan* was ever built, outside altars were permitted and the sacrificial service was performed by the firstborn. This formulation is somewhat complex historically: the patriarchs brought *korbanot*, Moshe brought various *korbanot*, and not all of them were firstborns. The *mishnah* likely refers specifically to the *korban pesach* in Egypt and to the *korbanot* offered at Har Sinai in the period before the *Mishkan* was commanded or constructed, during which several months elapsed.

### Mishkan Established

*Mishehukam ha-mishkan ne'esru ha-bamot, ve-avodah b'kohanim* — once the *Mishkan* was erected, outside altars were prohibited and the service transferred to the *kohanim*. This is precisely why, at the beginning of *Sefer Bamidbar*, there are the elaborate arrangements for exchanging the tribe of Levi for all the firstborns among *Bnei Yisrael* — the right to perform the service was transferred from the *bechorim* to the *kohanim* and *Leviyim*, and the *bechorim* were thereby relieved of their original role. Some have suggested that this sheds light on why Yaakov was so eager to acquire the *bechorah* from Esav — to prevent Esav from serving in any sense in the future *Beit HaMikdash*.

The *mishnah* further describes that *kodshei kodashim* — like the *korban chatat*, *korban asham*, and the *zivchei shalmei tzibur* on Shavuot — were eaten *lifnim min ha-kela'im*, within the curtains of the *Mishkan*. Though this phrase appears anachronistically throughout *Zevachim* as an overlay on the *Beit HaMikdash*, here it is used literally, since we are speaking of the actual fabric curtains of the *Mishkan*. *Kodashim kalim* could be eaten throughout the entire *machaneh Yisrael*. The three concentric camps — *machaneh shekhinah* (the *Mishkan* itself), *machaneh leviyah* (surrounding the *Mishkan*), and *machaneh Yisrael* (the entire encampment of the Jewish people) — correspond respectively to the Temple precinct, the Temple Mount, and the halakhically designated city of Jerusalem. There is even a *sugya* in the Gemara elsewhere discussing whether the borders of Jerusalem might be greatly expanded in the future, as implied in *Sefer Yechezkel* toward its conclusion.

### Gilgal Period

*Ba'u le-Gilgal hutteru ha-bamot* — when the *Mishkan* was brought to Gilgal in the period of Yehoshua, outside altars were once again permitted. *Kodshei kodashim* could be eaten within the curtains of the *Mishkan*, and *kodashim kalim* could be eaten *bekhol makom* — anywhere at all. In Gilgal there was no designation of *machaneh Yisrael* as a bounded area, since the Jewish people were now spread across the Land of Israel and the *Mishkan* was in one location with no corresponding camp structure. Therefore, only the *machaneh shekhinah* — the curtained interior — defined what counted as the place for *kodshei kodashim*, while for *kodashim kalim* there was no geographic boundary, which is precisely the nature of the *heter bamot*: one could bring a *korban shelamim* on a local *bamah* and eat it there.

### Shiloh Period

*Ba'u le-Shilo ne'esru ha-bamot* — when the *Mishkan* was brought to Shiloh, outside altars were once again prohibited. At Shiloh a significant structural change occurred: rather than the wooden beams of the portable *Mishkan*, stone walls were built to support the structure, while the curtains and leather coverings were retained above. *Velo hayah sham tikrah, ella bayit shel avanim mil-mattan, yeri'ot mil-ma'alan* — there was no roof, just stone walls below and curtains on top. This more permanent physical structure gave Shiloh a more substantive and settled character.

The basis for the prohibition is the verse in Devarim 12:9: *ki lo vatem ad attah el ha-menuchah* — you have not yet come to the *menuchah*. Once the *Mishkan* reached Shiloh, which the Gemara identifies as the *menuchah*, the prohibition of *bamot* resumed. The structure at Shiloh was stable and designated as a central shrine, even if it was not yet the *Beit HaMikdash*, and that stability sufficed to reimpose *issur bamot*. *Kodashim kalim* and *ma'aser sheni* could be eaten *bekhol ha-ro'eh* — anywhere within sight of the *Mishkan* in Shiloh — not within the boundaries of a city, but within the visual lines of sight.

### Nov & Givon

*Ba'u le-Nov u-le-Giv'on hutteru ha-bamot* — when the *Mishkan* was brought to Nob and subsequently to Gibeon (with the *aron* absent from both locations), outside altars were once again permitted. *Kodshei kodashim* were eaten within the curtains, and *kodashim kalim bekhol arei Yisrael* — in any city of Israel. This is a more restricted formulation than the *bekhol makom* of the Gilgal period, where there was no geographic boundary at all; here the *mishnah* specifies *arei Yisrael*, restricting consumption to within city limits.

### Jerusalem Final

*Ba'u li-Yerushalayim ne'esru ha-bamot, velo hayah lahem od hetter* — once everything was brought to Jerusalem and the first *Beit HaMikdash* was built, the period of *bamot* ended permanently. There was no further *heter*. The Scriptural basis is the continuation of the verse from Devarim: *el ha-menuchah ve'el ha-nachalah* — the *menuchah* was Shiloh, and the *nachalah* is Jerusalem. Once the *nachalah* was established with the building of the *Beit HaMikdash*, the *heter bamot* was foreclosed entirely and irrevocably.

*Kodashim kalim* and *ma'aser sheni* could now be eaten only *lifnim min ha-chomah* — within the walls of Jerusalem. This provides a striking halachic context for the construction of Jerusalem's walls, as recounted in *Sefer Nechemiah*: beyond the obvious concerns of security and civic stature, there was a genuine halachic reason to define and maintain the city's walls.

## Sanctification Timing

### Both During Issur

The *mishnah* now addresses the question of sacrifices sanctified and offered during different periods of *issur bamot* and *heter bamot* — a scenario that could only have arisen historically during the transitions between these periods, and which has no future applicability, but which nonetheless defines the boundaries of the prohibition. The first case: *she-hikdishan bi-sha'at issur ha-bamot ve-hikrivan bi-sha'at issur ha-bamot bachutz* — if an animal was sanctified during a period when *bamot* were prohibited, and was then offered outside the Temple also during a period when *bamot* were prohibited, the full array of consequences applies: *harei elu b'aseh ve-lo ta'aseh ve-chayavin aleihem karet* — one has violated a *mitzvat aseh*, a *mitzvat lo ta'aseh*, and incurs *karet*.

### Sanctify Hetter Offer Is…

The second case: *hikdishan bi-sha'at hetter bamot ve-hikrivan bi-sha'at issur ha-bamot* — if the animal was sanctified during a period when *bamot* were permitted, and then offered outside the Temple during a period when *bamot* were prohibited. This could occur if, for example, the animal was sanctified while *bamot* were allowed, and then the *Beit HaMikdash* was built in Jerusalem before the sacrifice was offered, and the owner was still in the countryside when he brought it as a sacrifice. In such a case: *harei elu b'aseh ve-lo ta'aseh ve-ein chayavin aleihem karet* — one violates both an *aseh* and a *lo ta'aseh*, but does not receive *karet*. This represents exactly the intermediate category that Rabbi Shimon had articulated earlier in the chapter, and it may suggest that even the *chachamim* agree to Rabbi Shimon's framework in these historical cases, even if they dispute his principle for the categories addressed at the beginning of the *perek*.

### Sanctify Issur Offer Het…

The third case is the inverse: *hikdishan bi-sha'at issur ha-bamot ve-hikrivan bi-sha'at hetter bamot* — the animal was sanctified during a period of *issur bamot*, such as the time of Shiloh, but was then offered during a subsequent period of *heter bamot*, such as Nob. In this case: *harei elu b'aseh ve-ein bahem lo ta'aseh* — one violates a *mitzvat aseh* only, without any *lo ta'aseh*, since at the time of the offering one is in a period of *heter bamot*.

## Central vs Local

### Mishkan Required

The *mishnah* proceeds to clarify which sacrifices must be brought specifically at the *Mishkan* and which may be brought at a local *bamah* during periods when *bamot* were permitted. *Elu kodashim krevin ba-Mishkan — Kodashim she'hukdeshu le-Mishkan* — those sacrifices that were sanctified explicitly for the *Mishkan* must be brought there. Similarly, *korbanot ha-tzibur krevin ba-Mishkan* — communal sacrifices, offered on behalf of the *tzibur*, must be brought at the central shrine regardless.

### Individual Permitted

*Korbanot ha-yachid b'bamah* — individual sacrifices may be brought on a local *bamah* during the periods of *heter bamot*. However, *korbanot ha-yachid she-hukdeshu la-Mishkan, yikrevu ba-Mishkan* — if an individual specifically sanctified his animal with the intent of bringing it to the *Mishkan*, he is obligated to do so. *Ve'im hikrivan b'bamah patur* — but if he nonetheless brought it on a *bamah be-di'avad*, he is *patur*. He has not violated a prohibition, but he had a *le-chatchilah* obligation to bring it to the *Mishkan* as he had declared.

## Altar Differences

### Smicha & Direction

The *mishnah* enumerates the ways in which the *bamat ha-yachid* — an individual's private altar — differs from the *bamat ha-tzibur*, the central *Mishkan* which is itself called a *bamah gedolah* during periods of *heter bamot*. The following requirements apply only at the central shrine and not at a private *bamah*: *smicha* — the laying of hands on the animal before slaughter — does not apply at a *bamah*. Nor does *shechitat tzafon* — the requirement that *kodshei kodashim* such as *chatat*, *asham*, and *olah* be slaughtered on the north side of the *mizbe'ach*.

### Blood & Waving

*Matan saviv* — going around the four corners of the *mizbe'ach* to apply the blood — does not apply at a *bamah*. *Tenufah* — the waving of the various portions of the *korban shelamim* — also does not apply at a *bamah*. Nor does *hagashah* — the bringing-forward that is part of the *korban minchah* process.

### Rabbi Yehuda Mincha

Rabbi Yehuda goes further: *ein minchah ba-bamah* — there is no *minchah* offering at a *bamah* at all. Additionally, Rabbi Yehuda holds *ve-kehun* — there is actually no requirement for *kohanim* at a *bamah*; even a *zar* could bring *korbanot* on a private *bamah*.

### No Priestly Items

There is also no requirement for *bigdei sharet* — the priestly vestments are called *bigdei sharet* here rather than *bigdei kehunah* — and no requirement for *klei sharet*, sanctified vessels. There is no requirement to use formally designated containers for, for example, receiving the blood. And there is no *reiach nichoach* — the sacrifice does not achieve the same quality of spiritual acceptability as one brought at the central shrine. Rashi notes that *reiach nichoach* here refers to the principle established in the chapter of *Beit Shammai*: a sacrifice must be offered with the proper intent among the six purposes for which a sacrifice is brought (*leshem shishah devarim*), and there is no requirement for this mental awareness at a *bamah* because the offering does not achieve the same *reiach nichoach*.

### No Mizbe'ach Division

*Umechetza le-damim* — the *chut ha-sikra*, the red line that divides the *mizbe'ach* into its upper half (for blood applied above the line) and lower half (for blood applied below), does not apply at a *bamah*. According to Rabbi Yehuda, this is another element absent from the *bamah*. *Urechitzat yadayim ve-raglayim* — the washing of the hands and feet before performing the service is also not required at a *bamah*.

### Time & Notar Equal

Despite all of these differences, certain requirements apply equally at both the central shrine and the private *bamah*: *hazeman ve-ha-notar* — the laws of improper intention regarding time (*piggul*) and the laws of *notar*, sacrificial portions that have remained beyond their permitted time. And *ha-tamei* — if the sacrifice or its portions become *tamei*, they are disqualified. These laws are *shavim ba-zeh u-va-zeh* — identical in both contexts. With that, the *mishnayot* of *Masechet Zevachim* are complete.
