# Zevachim 74 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ring of Avoda Zara

### Ring Falls in Sea

Rav Nachman transmits a ruling in the name of Rabbah bar Bar Hana in the name of Rav: a *taba'at shel avoda zara* — a ring that had been used in idolatrous worship, or that served as an ornament beautifying an altar (*noy avoda zara*) — becomes mixed with a hundred similar-looking rings. Such a ring is not *batel*: whether because idolatry is categorically weighty and resists nullification, because the ring is a significant object in its own right, or some combination of the two, the entire mixture is forbidden. Then one of the rings falls into the Mediterranean Sea (*Yam HaGadol*). The ruling: *hutru kulan* — all the remaining rings are now permitted, for we say that the one which fell away was the problematic one. This reasoning — *hach denafal hainu de-issura*, the one that fell, that is the forbidden one — operates as a kind of legal fiction, attributing the loss to the prohibited item and thereby freeing the remainder.

One immediate question presents itself: may one throw the ring into the sea *lechatchila*, deliberately, in order to trigger this permission? Presumably not, as that would constitute *ein mevatlin issur lechatchila* — one may not intentionally bring about the nullification of a forbidden item.

### Rabbi Eliezer Position

The ruling about the rings is attributed to the position of Rabbi Eliezer, as recorded in an earlier Mishnah: *Rabbi Eliezer omer im karev harosh shel echad mehen yikrevu kol haroshim kulam* — if the head of one of the mixed animals was already offered, all the remaining heads may be offered. The principle in both cases is the same: once something happens to one item — it was lost, destroyed, or used — we attribute the prohibition to that one and permit the rest. This is specifically Rabbi Eliezer's view, and as the Gemara will make clear, it is contested.

### Challenge to Rav Nachman

Rabbah challenges Rav Nachman directly from our Mishnah: *afilu achat be-ribo yamutu kulan* — even one forbidden animal mixed among ten thousand, they must all be left to die. Why, asks Rabbah, can we not apply the same logic? Inevitably one animal will die first — let us say *that* was the *shor haniskal*, the condemned ox, and permit the rest. Rav Nachman responds: Rav's ruling follows Rabbi Eliezer. Not everyone accepts the principle of blaming the loss on the prohibited item — it is a disputed position, and our Mishnah does not follow it. The debate is genuinely tannaitic.

### Two at a Time Requirement

The Gemara raises a further objection. Rabbi Elazar the Amora notes that Rabbi Eliezer — the Tanna whose principle underlies this ruling — did not give blanket permission when one item is lost or used. Rather, *lo hitar Rabbi Eliezer ela shenayim shenayim* — Rabbi Eliezer only permitted benefit when items are used two at a time. The reasoning is that when one takes two, at least one of them is certainly permitted, which creates a kind of doubled basis for permissibility: we assume the prohibited item was the one already lost, and of the two now being used, one is definitively acceptable. The response to this objection is: *ana nami tarti ka-amina* — the ruling about the rings was also stated with the understanding that they are used or sold two at a time. Rashi explains this in the context of sale: the rings are sold in pairs, so that the same two-at-a-time logic applies. One could similarly say they are worn two at a time — on a chain or together on a finger — though the precise mechanics matter less than the principle.

## Split Mixture Problem

### Forty/Sixty Split

Rav continues with a more complex case. A *taba'at shel avoda zara* was mixed into a hundred rings, and again the mixture is not *batel*. The pile of one hundred rings then splits: forty go off in one direction, sixty in another. Now, the ring of *avoda zara* is somewhere in one of these two groups, but we do not know which.

The question arising from this scenario is whether we can apply the principle of *kol de-parish me-rubba parish* — what separates from the majority is presumed to have come from the majority, and since the majority is permitted, the separated item is permitted. In theory, one might argue that each ring, wherever it ended up, came from the majority of the original hundred and is therefore acceptable. But the Gemara does not accept this reasoning here — just as it does not apply it to the case of a single animal that wanders away from a mixed herd. Once the items are part of an initial forbidden mixture, one cannot simply pick one up and say it came from the majority; this is the very same issue debated in the large *Tosafot* at the beginning of this sugya regarding when *kol de-parish me-rubba parish* does and does not apply. All forty rings remain forbidden, as do all sixty.

### One from Forty vs. Sixty

The ruling then turns to what happens when one ring from one of these sub-groups falls into a separate, new pile of rings. *Pirshah achat me-arba'im einah oseret* — if one ring from the group of forty falls into a new pile, it does not render that new pile forbidden. *Achat mi-shishim oseret* — if one ring from the group of sixty falls into a new pile, it does render it forbidden.

The logic is as follows. Even though both the forty and the sixty each contain the *ta'aroves* — the admixture of the forbidden ring — when a ring separates from the forty, we are prepared to say that the forbidden ring was more likely to have remained in the larger group of sixty. The sixty remain the "base" of the original forbidden mixture; the prohibited item is presumed to be there. By contrast, when something separates from the sixty, we assume it may well be the forbidden one, and the new pile into which it falls becomes forbidden.

This is acknowledged as a kind of fiction — but so is the original rabbinic ruling that even one in a hundred is not *batel*. The Rabbis created the stringency, and they also shaped the parameters of its application.

### Gemara's Question

The Gemara questions the asymmetry: *mai shna achat me-arba'im d'lo* — why is it that when one comes from the forty it does not render the new pile forbidden? *D'amrinan issura be-rubba isa* — we say the prohibited item remained with the majority. But then: *achat mi-shishim nami amrinan issura be-rubba isa* — when one comes from the sixty, we should equally say the forbidden item stayed with the majority (of the sixty that remains). The same reasoning should apply in both directions. If in each case we simply say the forbidden item stayed behind, then in neither case should a ring that separates render a new mixture forbidden.

### Entire Group Separation

The resolution: *ela im pirshu arba'im kulam le-makom echad ein oserot; shishim le-makom echad oserot* — the real operative distinction is not about a single ring separating, but about an entire sub-group moving as a whole into a new location. If all forty move together into a new pile, that pile is permitted. If all sixty move together into a new pile, the pile is forbidden. The reason is structural: when the entire larger group relocates together, it carries with it the probability that the forbidden ring is among them. When the smaller group relocates as a whole, the forbidden ring is presumed to have stayed with what was the majority.

This is, as the Gemara acknowledges, somewhat surprising. It seems to imply that even the single-ring case could be resolved by *kol de-parish me-rubba parish*, and the Gemara effectively concedes that point — though with the important caveat that specifically regarding *avoda zara*, Shmuel will shortly insist that these probabilistic moves are never permitted.

## Shmuel's Split Position

### Shmuel Never Batel

When Rav transmitted the ruling about the forty and sixty rings, he reports: *ki amrisa kamin d'Shmuel, amar li* — when I presented this analysis before Shmuel, Shmuel responded: *hanach le-avodat kochavim she-sfeika ve-sfek sfeika asurah ad sof ha-olam* — leave aside *avoda zara*, for by idolatry, even a doubt upon a doubt remains forbidden forever. By *avoda zara*, Shmuel holds, we never apply probabilistic reasoning across mixtures, no matter how many successive piles are involved. Not only does one ring from the minority group render a new mixture forbidden — even one ring from the majority group would render it forbidden. The chain of prohibition never breaks.

### Goblets Baraita

Against Shmuel's sweeping position, the Gemara raises a *Baraita*: *safek avodat kochavim asurah, ve-sfek sfeika muteret* — the first level of doubt by *avoda zara* is forbidden, but a *sfek sfeika* — a double doubt — is permissible. The illustrative case: a goblet (*kos*) of *avoda zara* fell into a warehouse (*otzar*) full of goblets — all are forbidden. One then fell from that mixture into a pile of ten thousand, and one from there into yet another pile of ten thousand: *mutarin* — at least by the third pile, they are permitted. That is at least a *sfek sfek sfeika*, and Tosafot notes that the lenient opinion might even permit the second pile. But the key point is that this *Baraita* explicitly applies *sfek sfeika* leniency to *avoda zara* — in direct tension with Shmuel's absolute position.

### Rabbi Yehuda/Shimon

The Gemara identifies the underlying tannaitic dispute by reference to a *Baraita* about *orlah* pomegranates from Badan — one of the categories not subject to standard *bittul*. Rabbi Yehuda says: *osrin bechol shehen* — even a single one in a large mixture is forbidden, and if one falls from the first mixture into a second mixture (*ribo*), and from there into a third, all remain forbidden. Rabbi Yehuda's position is maximally strict, and he does not limit it to *avoda zara* — this applies across the board to anything that lacks *bittul*.

Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda, citing Rabbi Shimon, takes a different view — and notably argues against his own father's position. Rabbi Shimon agrees that the first mixture (*leribo asurin*) and the second mixture also remain forbidden. But once we reach a third pile (*mi-shalosh le-makom acher*), even that is permitted. The Gemara initially questions why three are required: if the goal is a simple majority (*rubba*), two ought to suffice. The answer is that when the *Baraita* says "three," it means two were already there and the one item fell in to make three — so the majority of two out of three favors the permissible. An alternative reading is that Rabbi Shimon follows Rabbi Eliezer's principle of using things two at a time, and therefore requires a total of four — meaning one must be able to take two, and then two more — which would require more than just three items.

### Shmuel vs. Tannaim

The Gemara now turns to the question of how Shmuel fits within this tannaitic debate. Shmuel says that by *avoda zara* specifically, even a *sfek sfeika* is forever forbidden. If Shmuel is following Rabbi Yehuda, then Rabbi Yehuda says this applies to *all* prohibitions, not just *avoda zara* — and that is too broad for Shmuel's formulation, which singles out *avoda zara*. If Shmuel is following Rabbi Shimon, then Rabbi Shimon says pile three is permitted even by *orlah* — and by extension, presumably by *avoda zara* as well, which directly contradicts Shmuel. Moreover, if Rabbi Shimon would say pile three is permitted even by *avoda zara*, then the *Baraita* about the goblets that permits the third pile by *avoda zara* is Rabbi Shimon — and Shmuel's position aligns with neither Tanna consistently.

The Gemara resolves this: *v'Shmuel savar lah ke-Rabbi Yehuda be-chada u-falig alei be-chada* — Shmuel follows Rabbi Yehuda in one respect and disagrees with him in another. Rabbi Yehuda says no prohibition — *avoda zara* or otherwise — ever undergoes *bittul* across successive mixtures. Shmuel agrees with that conclusion specifically regarding *avoda zara*, but disagrees for other prohibitions, where he follows Rabbi Shimon's view that by the third pile even a simple majority suffices. The *Baraita* about the goblets, permitting the third mixture by *avoda zara*, follows Rabbi Shimon in full — who makes no distinction between *avoda zara* and other categories. Shmuel effectively votes with Rabbi Yehuda on *avoda zara* and with Rabbi Shimon on everything else.

The bottom line is that Shmuel does not follow any single Tanna across the board. The tannaitic debate itself makes no categorical distinction between *avoda zara* and other prohibitions: either you follow Rabbi Yehuda and nothing is ever *batel* across successive mixtures, or you follow Rabbi Shimon and by the third pile it is *batel* even by *avoda zara*. Shmuel carves out a middle position that is his own.

## Barrel Falls in Sea

### Barrel Case Setup

Reish Lakish presents a parallel case: *chavit shel trumah she-nit'areva be-me'ah chavyot* — a sealed barrel of *terumah* wine mixed with a hundred other sealed barrels. As established earlier in the tractate, sealed barrels constitute one of the categories of items that do not undergo *bittul*, even where liquid *terumah* would ordinarily be nullified in a hundred. So the mixture is forbidden. Then one of the barrels falls into the Dead Sea (*Yam HaMelach*). The ruling: *hutru kulan* — all are now permitted, because *de-amrinan hai nafalah de-isura nafalah* — we say the one that fell was the forbidden one. This parallels both the logic of Rabbi Eliezer's principle regarding the animal heads and Rav's ruling about the ring of *avoda zara*.

### Ring and Barrel Needed

The Gemara notes that both teachings are independently necessary — Rav Nachman's ruling about the ring and Reish Lakish's ruling about the barrel — and neither can be inferred from the other. Had we only had Rav Nachman's ruling about the ring of *avoda zara*, one might have argued that the leniency is specific to *avoda zara* — *de-ein lahem matirin* — because idolatrous objects have no path to become permitted under any circumstances. Since the prohibition is absolute, perhaps the Rabbis were more willing to allow this fiction as the only available relief. But *terumah* is different: *trumah de-yesh lah matirin* — *terumah* does have a form of permissibility, since a *kohen* may use it. Because there is someone for whom *terumah* is accessible, perhaps the same leniency should not apply, and we would not permit blaming the loss on the fallen barrel.

Conversely, had we only Reish Lakish's ruling about the barrel, one might have thought that the leniency is limited to barrels precisely because *minchar nefilasah* — the falling of a barrel is conspicuous and its absence is noticed. Everyone looks at the row of barrels and perceives the gap. That perceptibility makes it psychologically coherent — and halachically supportable — to attribute the loss to the problematic item. But a ring — *lo minchara nefilasah* — its fall and its absence go unnoticed. Perhaps then we cannot apply the same fiction to the ring. Since each case teaches something the other cannot, both are required.

### Rava Limits to Barrels

Rava proceeds to formalize exactly this limitation. Reish Lakish, says Rava, only permitted the fiction of blaming the fallen item when the loss is perceptible — as with a barrel. *Aval te'ena lo* — but if the forbidden item was a fig mixed among other figs, and one fig fell away, we would not apply the same principle. The small size and imperceptibility of the fig's absence means the event of its falling into the sea does not carry the same halachic weight. Whether Rava would also restrict the ring case by *avoda zara* — or whether the distinctive severity of idolatry justifies greater leniency there regardless — is left somewhat open, but his explicit ruling concerns *terumah* and similar categories.

### Rav Yosef Permits Fig

Rav Yosef disagrees: *afilu te'ena kenefilatah kach aliyatah* — even a fig follows the same logic. Just as when the fig fell into the group of figs it made the entire group forbidden despite its small size and inconspicuousness, so too when it falls away, it should be sufficient to render the remainder permitted. The same measure that makes a small item capable of creating a prohibition should make it capable of dissolving one when it is lost. Rav Yosef thus extends the permission to small items as well.

## Open Barrel Solution

### Remove Proportion

Rav Elazar proposes an independent method for dealing with the case of a *chavit shel terumah she-nafla be-kuf chavyot* — one sealed barrel of *terumah* mixed among a hundred others. The standard situation is that none of the barrels are *batel*, since sealed barrels resist nullification. Rav Elazar's solution: *pote'ach achat mehen ve-notel heimena kedei dameha ve-shoteh* — open one of the barrels and remove from it a proportional amount, corresponding to the one-hundredth share that would represent the forbidden portion, and then drink the rest.

The mechanism is as follows. Once a barrel is opened, it is no longer a sealed barrel and therefore loses its status as a significant, non-*batel* item. At the moment of opening, if that barrel happens to be the *terumah* one, it immediately becomes *batel* within the rest of the mixture, because it is no longer distinct. Rav Elazar's instruction to remove a proportional quantity is not because the opened barrel is literally a blend — it is either entirely *terumah* or entirely permitted wine. Rather, removing one one-hundredth serves as a kind of acknowledgment that a *ta'aroves* existed and that there is something to account for. One thereby "pays" the symbolic price of the mixture before drinking, rather than simply opening the barrel and drinking freely as if nothing had occurred.

The elegance of the method is that if one proceeds through all the barrels this way, removing one one-hundredth from each, the total removed will equal the volume of a full barrel — which is, of course, the amount of *terumah* that was at stake. There is a certain integrity to the outcome even if the path to it is admittedly a legal construction.

### Nachman's Objection

Rav Nachman objects sharply: *gama ve-shasa* — what, you're just swallowing and drinking? How can one open a barrel *lechatchila* for this purpose? Doing so would constitute *mevatlin issur lechatchila* — actively engineering the conditions for *bittul* to take effect, which is forbidden. Opening the barrel is itself the act that removes the sealed-barrel status and enables *bittul*, and one may not perform that act with that goal in mind.

### Natural Opening Only

The correction: *nifchatzah achat mehen* — only if one of the barrels happened to open on its own, or broke, or was opened for some other reason, may one then remove the appropriate proportion and drink. In that case, the opening was not done for the purpose of achieving *bittul*, and there is no violation of *ein mevatlin issur lechatchila*. The parallel is the ring that fell into the sea on its own — one cannot throw the ring in deliberately, but if it fell, the consequences may be used. Similarly here, one cannot deliberately open the barrel to trigger *bittul*, but if it opened, Rav Elazar's method applies.

### Multiple Openings

Rabbi Oshaya extends the ruling to a more complex case: *chavit shel terumah she-nit'areva be-me'ah va-chamishim chavyot* — one *terumah* barrel mixed among 150 barrels. Then *niftachu me'ah mehen* — a hundred of them opened. The ruling: *notel mimena kedei dimu'a ve-shoteh* — one removes the appropriate proportion from each of the hundred opened barrels and may drink from all of them, while *ve-she'ar asurin ad she-yipatechu* — the remaining fifty sealed barrels stay forbidden until they too are opened, at which point the same process applies.

One might have thought to object: if a hundred barrels all open simultaneously, perhaps we should say *issur be-rubba isa* — the forbidden barrel must be among the majority of those that opened, and therefore those hundred collectively remain suspect. Rabbi Oshaya rejects this: *chidush hu* — this is not how the principle operates. The moment a barrel opens, *bittul* applies to it regardless of how many others open at the same time. There is no rule requiring that the forbidden item remain within the majority of opened barrels; each opened barrel simply joins the category of non-significant, *batel* items. The appropriate proportion is removed, and all the opened barrels become permitted.
