# Sanhedrin 103 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Achav & Idolatry

### Three Evil Kings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַחְאָב: אָח לַשָּׁמַיִם, אָב לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. אָח לַשָּׁמַיִם, דִּכְתִיב: "אָח לְצָרָה יִוָּלֵד". אָב לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, דִּכְתִיב: "כְּרַחֵם אָב עַל בָּנִים".
>
> **Translation:** **Ahab [*Aḥav*],** although he was **a brother [*aḥ*] to Heaven,** he was **a father [*av*] for idol worship.** He was **a brother to Heaven, as it is written: "And a brother is born for adversity"** (Proverbs 17:17), and in desperate times, he turned to Heaven. He was **a father for idolatry.** This is the highest level of attachment, **as it is written: "As a father has compassion for his children"** (Psalms 103:13).

The present sugya continues in Perek Chelek, on 103a, amid the discussion of the three evil kings: *Yerovam*, *Achav*, and *Menashe*. *Yerovam* was dealt with previously — he split the kingdom, made the two golden calves, and led all the people into *avodah zarah*. Indeed, every subsequent king of Israel who did evil is described in terms of *Yerovam*: they continued to do evil as he had done. We now turn to *Achav*, one of the most terrible kings, notorious for his partnership with *Izevel*, the murder of *Nabot*, and his introduction of widespread idolatry. The *Gemara* notes, however, that he enjoyed a long reign — understood through the *midrash* as a reward for having treasured and protected the *Sefer Torah*.

### Achav's Merits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִפְּנֵי שֶׁכִּיבֵּד אֶת הַתּוֹרָה, שֶׁנִּיתְּנָה בְּעֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁתַּיִם אוֹתִיּוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיִּשְׁלַח מַלְאָכִים אֶל אַחְאָב מֶלֶךְ יִשְׂרָאֵל הָעִירָה. וַיֹּאמֶר לוֹ כֹּה אָמַר בֶּן הֲדַד כַּסְפְּךָ וּזְהָבְךָ לִי הוּא וְנָשֶׁיךָ וּבָנֶיךָ הַטּוֹבִים לִי הֵם". "כִּי אִם כָּעֵת מָחָר אֶשְׁלַח אֶת עֲבָדַי אֵלֶיךָ וְחִפְּשׂוּ אֶת בֵּיתְךָ וְאֵת בָּתֵּי עֲבָדֶיךָ וְהָיָה כׇּל מַחְמַד עֵינֶיךָ יָשִׂימוּ בְיָדָם וְלָקָחוּ". "וַיֹּאמֶר לְמַלְאֲכֵי בֶן הֲדַד אִמְרוּ לַאדֹנִי הַמֶּלֶךְ כֹּל אֲשֶׁר שָׁלַחְתָּ אֶל עַבְדְּךָ בָרִאשׁוֹנָה אֶעֱשֶׂה וְהַדָּבָר הַזֶּה לֹא אוּכַל לַעֲשׂוֹת".
>
> **Translation:** It is **due to** the fact **that he respected the Torah, which was given with twenty-two letters, as it is stated: "And he sent messengers to Ahab, king of Israel, into the city, and said to him: So says Ben-Hadad: Your silver and your gold are mine; your wives and also your good children, are mine.** And the king of Israel answered and said: It is according to your saying, my lord, O king: I am yours, and all that I have. And the messengers came again, and said: So speaks Ben-Hadad, saying: I sent to you, saying: You shall deliver me your silver, and your gold, and your wives, and your children. **Yet I will send my servants to you tomorrow about this time, and they shall search your house, and the houses of your servants, and it shall be, that they shall put in their hand all the delight of your eyes and take it…And he said to the messengers of Ben-Hadad, tell my lord the king: All that you did send for to your servant at the first I will do; but this thing I may not do"** (I Kings 20:2-6, 9).

Beyond his protection of the *Sefer Torah*, the *Gemara* records that while his wife *Izevel* was channeling funds toward *avodah zarah*, *Achav* was directing his own wealth toward the support of *talmidei chachamim*. These merits, however qualified, earned him some measure of divine favor during his lifetime.

### Nabot's Spirit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וַיֵּצֵא הָרוּחַ וַיַּעֲמֹד לִפְנֵי ה׳ וַיֹּאמֶר אֲנִי אֲפַתֶּנּוּ וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֵלָיו בַּמָּה. וַיֹּאמֶר אֵצֵא וְהָיִיתִי רוּחַ שֶׁקֶר בְּפִי כׇּל נְבִיאָיו וַיֹּאמֶר תְּפַתֶּה וְגַם תּוּכָל צֵא וַעֲשֵׂה כֵן". מַאי רוּחַ? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רוּחוֹ שֶׁל נָבוֹת הַיִּזְרְעֵאלִי.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to God's search for a volunteer to entice Ahab, it is written: **"And there came forth a spirit, and stood before the Lord, and said: I will entice him. And the Lord said to him: With what? And he said: I will go out, and I will be a lying spirit in the mouth of all his prophets. And He said: You shall entice him, and also prevail; go out, and do so"** (I Kings 22:21–22). The Gemara asks: **What spirit** was that? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It was **the spirit of Naboth the Jezreelite,** who sought to take revenge against Ahab.

The *Gemara* now picks up approximately ten lines from the bottom of the page, quoting the verse in which God addresses the spirits assembled before Him, asking who will go and seduce *Achav* into entering the battle in which he will fall. *Vayetze haruach vayaamod lifnei Hashem* — one spirit came and stood before God. *Vayomer ani apatenu* — I will seduce him. God asked: *bameh*, how? The spirit replied: *etze vehayiti ruach sheker befi kol nevi'av* — I will become a false spirit in the mouths of all of his false prophets, and they will all prophesy that he should go to war, and he will fall. God responds: *tepateh gam tuchal tze vaaseh ken* — go, seduce him, and you will succeed.

*Mai ruach?* What was this spirit? Rabbi Yochanan says: *rucho shel Nabot haYizre'eli* — the spirit of *Nabot haYizre'eli*, the man whose vineyard *Achav* had stolen and whose murder *Achav* had engineered. This spirit now finds his justice. It is worth pausing here: much of this discussion concerns *olam haba*, which in this context refers to the world following the Messianic era, though the more familiar notion of the afterlife — where the soul goes after death — also operates in fewer sources and becomes dominant in later tradition. Here, the disembodied soul of *Nabot* is obtaining his reckoning.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי "צֵא"? אָמַר רָבִינָא: "צֵא מִמְּחִיצָתִי", שֶׁכֵּן כְּתִיב: "דֹּבֵר שְׁקָרִים לֹא יִכּוֹן לְנֶגֶד עֵינָי". אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: דְּפָרַע קִינֵּיהּ מַחְרֵיב בֵּיתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the term **"go out"** that God instructed the spirit? **Ravina says** that it means: **Go out from** within **my partition** and do not return, **as so it is written: "He that speaks falsehoods shall not be established before My eyes"** (Psalms 101:7). The spirit that volunteered to lie may no longer be before God. **Rav Pappa says** that **this** is in accordance with the adage **that people say: One who avenges** due to **his zealotry destroys his** own **house.** The spirit of Naboth that sought revenge was expelled from before God.

*Mai tze?* Why did God need to say *tze*, "go out," if He had simply given the spirit his commission? *Amar Ravina: tze mimichitzati* — get out of my presence. As the verse says, *dover shekarim lo yikon leneged einai* — one who speaks falsehood will not stand before me. God essentially says: this is a necessary task, but once you are going out to speak falsehood, I no longer want you near me. It is worth noting the distinction: the original commission was *pitu'i*, seduction, which is not the same as outright lying — yet the spirit's execution of the task would involve falsehood, and for that God sends him away.

*Amar Papa: hainu de'amrei inshi, dipara kinei machriv beiteh* — that is what people say: the one who finally exacts vengeance for his jealousy ultimately destroys his own home. You may find satisfaction in acting on those feelings of revenge, but the outcome will be deeply destructive for yourself.

### Desecrating Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וַיַּעַשׂ אַחְאָב אֶת הָאֲשֵׁרָה וַיּוֹסֶף אַחְאָב לַעֲשׂוֹת לְהַכְעִיס אֶת ה׳ אֱלֹהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מִכֹּל מַלְכֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֲשֶׁר הָיוּ לְפָנָיו". אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֶׁכָּתַב עַל דַּלְתוֹת שֹׁמְרוֹן אַחְאָב כָּפַר בֵּאלֹהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, לְפִיכָךְ אֵין לוֹ חֵלֶק בֵּאלֹהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"And Ahab made the *ashera*; and Ahab did more to provoke the Lord, God of Israel, to anger than all the kings of Israel that were before him"** (I Kings 16:33), **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It means **that he wrote on the doors of Samaria: Ahab denies the** existence of the **God of Israel, therefore he has no share in the God of Israel.**

The *Gemara* then quotes the verse *vayaas Achav et ha'asheirah*, that *Achav* made the *asheira*, and *vayosef Achav laasot lehachis et Hashem elokei Yisrael mikol malchei Yisrael asher hayu lefanav* — he did more to anger God than all the kings who came before him. What did he do that so pointedly expressed his wickedness? Rabbi Yochanan says: *shekatav al daltot Shomron* — he wrote on the doors of *Shomron*: *Achav kafar be'elokei Yisrael* — *Achav* has rejected the God of Israel. He made it into a philosophical declaration: I reject you; I want nothing to do with you. And therefore, *ein lo chelek l'olam haba* — he has no portion in the world to come.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וַיְבַקֵּשׁ אֶת אֲחַזְיָהוּ וַיִּלְכְּדֻהוּ וְהוּא מִתְחַבֵּא בְשֹׁמְרוֹן". אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי: שֶׁהָיָה קוֹדֵר אַזְכָּרוֹת, וְכוֹתֵב עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה תַּחְתֵּיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"And he sought Ahaziah, and they apprehended him, for he was hiding in Samaria"** (II Chronicles 22:9), **Rabbi Levi says:** What would he do in hiding? **He would excise mentions** of God's name in Torah scrolls **and write** the name of an object of **idol worship in their stead.**

The *Gemara* then cites a verse about *Achazyahu* hiding in *Shomron* — though on its face the verse concerns *Achazyahu*, the *Gemara* appears ultimately to be reading it as referring to *Achav*. *Amar Rabbi Levi: shehayah koder azkarot* — what he would do is take a *Sefer Torah*, cut out or scrape away God's names, *vekotev avodat kokhavim tachtehen* — and write in their place the names of *avodah zarah*. He preserved the form of the *Sefer Torah* but replaced every divine Name with the name of an idol.

## Menashe's Repentance

### Menashe's Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנַשֶּׁה – שֶׁנָּשָׁה יָהּ. דָּבָר אַחֵר: מְנַשֶּׁה – שֶׁהִנְשִׁי אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל לַאֲבִיהֶם שֶׁבַּשָּׁמַיִם. וּמְנָלַן דְּלָא אָתֵי לְעָלְמָא דְּאָתֵי? דִּכְתִיב: "בֶּן שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה מְנַשֶּׁה בְמׇלְכוֹ וַחֲמִשִּׁים וְחָמֵשׁ שָׁנָה מָלַךְ בִּירוּשָׁלִָים וַיַּעַשׂ אֲשֵׁרָה כַּאֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה אַחְאָב מֶלֶךְ יִשְׂרָאֵל". מָה אַחְאָב אֵין לוֹ חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, אַף מְנַשֶּׁה אֵין לוֹ חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to discuss the third king enumerated in the mishna. He was called **Manasseh [*Menashe*] because he forgot God [*nasha yah*]. Alternatively,** he was called **Manasseh since he caused the** kingdom of **Israel to forget [*hinshi*] their Father Who is in Heaven. And from where do we** derive **that** Manasseh **does not enter into the World-to-Come?** The Gemara answers: It is derived **as it is written: "Manasseh was twelve years old when he began to reign, and he reigned fifty-five years in Jerusalem…**And he did that which was evil in the sight of the Lord…**And he made an *ashera*, as did Ahab king of Israel"** (II Kings 21:1–3). **Just as Ahab has no share in the World-to-Come, so too, Manasseh has no share in the World-to-Come.**

We now turn to the third king, *Menashe*. The *Gemara* begins with a play on his name: *shenashyah* — he forgot God. *Davar acher*: *shehishia et Yisrael l'avihen shebashamayim* — he caused Israel to be forgotten by their Father in heaven, or he caused Israel to forget God.

*Umanlan d'lo ati l'alma d'ati* — how do we know he has no portion in the world to come? The verse states: *ben shtem esreh shanah Menashe bemolcho vachamishim vechamesh shanah malach b'Yerushalayim* — he was twelve years old when he began to reign and he reigned for fifty-five years. *Vaya'as Asherah ka'asher asah Achav melech Yisrael* — and he made an *asheira* just as *Achav* king of Israel had done. The *Gemara* applies a *gezeirah shavah*: just as *Achav* has no portion in the world to come, so too *Menashe* has no portion in the world to come.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מְנַשֶּׁה יֵשׁ לוֹ חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיִּתְפַּלֵּל מְנַשֶּׁה אֶל ה׳ וַיֵּעָתֶר לוֹ וְגוֹ׳". אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וּשְׁנֵיהֶם מִקְרָא אֶחָד דָּרְשׁוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וּנְתַתִּים לְזַעֲוָה לְכֹל מַמְלְכוֹת הָאָרֶץ בִּגְלַל מְנַשֶּׁה בֶן יְחִזְקִיָּהוּ". מָר סָבַר: בִּגְלַל מְנַשֶּׁה שֶׁעָשָׂה תְּשׁוּבָה וְאִינְהוּ לָא עֲבוּד, וּמָר סָבַר:
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Yehuda says: Manasseh has a share in the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "And he prayed to him and He was entreated of him,** and heard his supplication and brought him back to Jerusalem unto his kingdom" (II Chronicles 33:13). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: And both of them,** Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis, who disagree with regard to whether Manasseh has a share in the World-to-Come, **interpreted one** and the same **verse, as it is stated: "And I will make them into a horror for all the kingdoms of the earth, on account of Manasseh, son of Hezekiah"** (Jeremiah 15:4). However, *Rabbi Yehuda omer Menashe yesh lo chelek l'olam haba* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that *Menashe* does have a portion in the world to come, as the verse says: *vayitpallel Menashe el Hashem vay'ater lo HaElohim* — *Menashe* prayed to God and God was importuned by him and responded. Rabbi Yehuda reads this as indicating genuine *teshuvah*, in contrast to *Achav*.

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan ushneihem mikra echad darshu* — both opinions are interpreting a single verse: *unatatim l'za'avah l'chol mamlechot ha'aretz biglal Menashe ben Yechizkiyahu* — I will make them a horror to all the kingdoms of the earth, because of *Menashe* son of *Yechizkiyahu*. *Mar savar*: one reads this as *biglal Menashe she'asah teshuvah v'inhu lo avid* — because *Menashe* demonstrated that even the most wicked person could do *teshuvah*, and this in turn, by contrast, indirectly brings judgment upon those who did not. *Umar savar* — and the other, which is closer to the simple meaning of the text, reads it as *biglal Menashe d'lo avid* — because of the evil that *Menashe* actually perpetrated, for which the people will ultimately pay.

## Achav & Idolatry (continued)

### No Share in Olam HaBa

The question arises regarding the *pasuk haraita ki nikhna Achav lifnai — yaan ki nikhna mipanai lo avia hara'ah beyamav*, the moment of *Achav*'s apparent humility before God. The assumption appears to be that this represented a moment of submission but not genuine *teshuvah*, and therefore it extended *Achav*'s days but did not reverse his ultimate judgment.

## Menashe's Repentance (continued)

### Rabbi Yehuda's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּגְלַל מְנַשֶּׁה, דְּלָא עֲבַד תְּשׁוּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **"on account of Manasseh"** means **because he did not repent,** and the people followed in his footsteps.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר מְנַשֶּׁה אֵין לוֹ חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא מְרַפֶּה יְדֵיהֶן שֶׁל בַּעֲלֵי תְשׁוּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Anyone who says** that **Manasseh has no share in the World-to-Come discourages penitents,** as Manasseh repented and according to them is nevertheless excluded from the World-to-Come.

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan*, at the top of 103a: *kol ha'omer Menashe ein lo chelek l'olam haba merappe yedeihen shel ba'alei teshuvah*. This is a striking statement. Rabbi Yochanan is not saying the view is factually wrong — he is making a point about which version of the story one chooses to tell. If one opts to emphasize the sheer wickedness of *Menashe* and declares that even after doing *teshuvah* it made no difference, that is not a religiously constructive message. The choice of interpretive framing carries moral consequences.

### Two Interpretations

The two views trace back to the same verse, and the debate reflects a genuine ambiguity: does *biglal Menashe* refer to the evil he committed, or does it refer to his *teshuvah* and what his repentance demonstrated? The *peshat* reading favors the former; the more theologically productive reading favors the latter.

One might raise the parallel of *Acher*, about whom the *Gemara* is extraordinarily harsh, with formulations like *chutz m'Acher* — even *teshuvah* would not help him. The answer is that Rabbi Yochanan is not asking anyone to claim that a person did *teshuvah* when he demonstrably did not. *Acher* showed no basis for *teshuvah*. The point here is specifically that when the verses genuinely permit a reading in which *teshuvah* occurred — when that is a legitimate interpretive option — one should adopt that version rather than the more damning one.

### Repentance Narrative

The account of *Menashe*'s *teshuvah* appears in *Divrei HaYamim* and is absent from *Sefer Melachim*, and scholars have noted the possibility of a difference in theological perspective between those two books. The *Gemara*, for its part, does not adjudicate the factual question but emphasizes the religious imperative: where *teshuvah* can reasonably be affirmed, affirm it.

### 33 Years of Good

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מְנַשֶּׁה עָשָׂה תְּשׁוּבָה (לִשְׁלֹשִׁים) [שְׁלֹשִׁים] וְשָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים, דִּכְתִיב: "בֵּן שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה מְנַשֶּׁה בְמׇלְכוֹ וַחֲמִשִּׁים וְחָמֵשׁ שָׁנָה מָלַךְ בִּירוּשָׁלִָים וַיַּעַשׂ (הָרַע) [אֲשֵׁרָה] וְכוּ׳ כַּאֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה אַחְאָב מֶלֶךְ יִשְׂרָאֵל". כַּמָּה מָלַךְ אַחְאָב? עֶשְׂרִין וְתַרְתֵּין שְׁנִין. מְנַשֶּׁה כַּמָּה מָלַךְ? חֲמִשִּׁים וְחָמֵשׁ. דַּל מִינַּיְיהוּ עֶשְׂרִים וְתַרְתֵּין, פָּשׁוּ לְהוּ תְּלָתִין וּתְלָת.
>
> **Translation:** **As** the ***tanna* taught** a *baraita* **before Rabbi Yoḥanan: Manasseh repented for thirty-three years, as it is written: "Manasseh was twelve years old when he began to reign, and he reigned fifty-five years in Jerusalem…And he did that which was evil…And he made an *ashera*, as did Ahab king of Israel"** (II Kings 21:1–3). **How many** years **did Ahab reign?** He reigned **twenty-two years. How many** years **did Manasseh reign?** He reigned **fifty-five** years. **Deduct from them** the **twenty-two** years during which he performed evil like Ahab, and **thirty-three** years **remain for him** to have repented.

A *tanna* taught before Rabbi Yochanan: *Menashe asa teshuvah shloshim v'shalosh shanim* — *Menashe* was on the righteous path for thirty-three years. The calculation proceeds from the verse: *ben shtaim esre shana Menashe b'molcho v'chamishim v'chamesh shana malach b'Yerushalayim* — he was twelve years old at his accession and reigned for fifty-five years. *Vaya'as hara kechol asher asu Achav melech Yisrael* — and he did evil like *Achav*. But *Achav* only reigned for twenty-two years. The *tanna* is asked: how long did *Achav* reign? Twenty-two years. How long did *Menashe* reign? Fifty-five. The years of evil — equated with the length of *Achav*'s reign by the *gezeirah shavah* — amount to twenty-two. *Pashu lei tlatin v'tlat* — that leaves thirty-three years of goodness. *Menashe* spent more than three-fifths of his life on the right path.

### Tunnel to Heaven

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: מַאי דִּכְתִיב "וַיִּשְׁמַע אֵלָיו וַיֵּחָתֶר לוֹ"? "וַיֵּעָתֶר לוֹ" מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! מְלַמֵּד שֶׁעָשָׂה לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא כְּמִין מַחְתֶּרֶת בָּרָקִיעַ, כְּדֵי לְקַבְּלוֹ בִּתְשׁוּבָה, מִפְּנֵי מִדַּת הַדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "And he prayed to Him; and He made an opening for him"** (II Chronicles 33:13)? Instead: **And He received his entreaty, should have been** written. Rather, this **teaches that the Holy One, Blessed be He, crafted for him a type of opening in Heaven in order to accept him in repentance.** It was necessary for Manasseh to enter the World-to-Come in a clandestine manner, **due to the attribute of justice** that sought to prevent his entry by claiming that his sentence was irreversible.

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan mishum Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai: mai dichtiv, vayishma elav vayichater lo* — what is the meaning of the phrase? The *pasuk* as it actually reads is *vaya'ater lo*, with an *ayin*, meaning God was importuned by or responded to him. But the version before Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai apparently had a *chet* instead of an *ayin*, reading *vayichater*, from the root meaning to dig. The *drasha* proceeds from this variant: *melamed she'asa lo HaKadosh Baruch Hu k'min machteret barakia* — God fashioned for *Menashe* something like a tunnel through the heavens, *kedei lekabelo b'teshuvah mipnei middat hadin* — in order to receive his *teshuvah*, circumventing, as it were, the *middat hadin*, the divine quality of strict justice, which would not otherwise have permitted it.

This is a striking image. One might ask: why should justice ever prevent *teshuvah*? The *Gemara*'s answer seems to be that while *teshuvah* in principle has the power to reverse even the gravest sin, there are cases where the *middat hadin* strains against accepting it — where it seems, from a rigorous standpoint of justice, almost inconceivable that all the evil a person has done could simply be wiped away. *Menashe* represents precisely such a case. And yet God does not simply reject the *teshuvah*; instead He tunnels through His own *middat hadin* to accept it. The imagery is extraordinarily powerful: God Himself is, as it were, sneaking around His own justice in order to embrace this repentance.

### Textual Variants

The question of the textual variant is addressed in the *Masoret HaShas*, which notes in brackets: *b'pasuk ketiv vaya'ater lo* — the actual verse reads *vaya'ater* with an *ayin*, and indeed the *Gemara* itself had quoted this form just moments earlier. *V'efshar d'beDivrei HaYamim shehaya lifnei Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai haya katuv vayichater* — it is possible that the text of *Divrei HaYamim* that lay before Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai actually read *vayichater* with a *chet*, representing a variant textual tradition. *Tosafot* in *Shabbat* likewise notes that the *Tannaim* and *Amoraim* sometimes had slightly different versions of the biblical text than the one we possess.

*Rashi* is also sensitive to this difficulty. He writes *vayichater lo u'mibachutz ketiv vaya'ater lo* — on the outside it says *vaya'ater*. It is not entirely clear what *Rashi* intends — whether he means the *drasha* is drawing on a hidden or secondary meaning beneath the surface reading — but he is evidently troubled by the discrepancy between the *darshan*'s text and the received version.

## Yehoyakim & Tzidkiyahu

### Resetting Creation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: מַאי דִּכְתִיב "בְּרֵאשִׁית מַמְלֶכֶת יְהוֹיָקִים בֶּן יֹאשִׁיָּהוּ", וּכְתִיב "בְּרֵאשִׁית מַמְלֶכֶת צִדְקִיָּה"? וְכִי עַד הָאִידָּנָא לָא הֲווֹ מַלְכֵי?
>
> **Translation:** § **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "In the beginning of the reign of Jehoiakim, son of Josiah"** (Jeremiah 26:1), **and it is written: "In the beginning of the reign of Zedekiah"** (Jeremiah 28:1). **But is it so** that **until now there were no kings?** Why did the prophet use the term "in the beginning" exclusively with regard to these two kings, as opposed to: During the first year of his reign, or a similar formulation?

*V'amar Rabbi Yochanan mishum Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai: mai dichtiv, bereshit mamlechut Yehoyakim ben Yoshiyahu* — what is meant by the phrase "the beginning of the reign of *Yehoyakim*"? And similarly: *u'chtiv bereshit mamlechut Tzidkiyahu* — and it also writes "the beginning of the reign of *Tzidkiyahu*." What is striking is the choice of the word *bereshit*, which obviously evokes the opening word of the Torah — *bereshit bara Elokim*. The text could simply have said *barishona* or *bashana rishona*. Why *bereshit*? *V'ad idna lo havu malchei?* Were there no kings before these? Was this truly a beginning?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, בִּקֵּשׁ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְהַחְזִיר אֶת הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ לְתוֹהוּ וָבוֹהוּ בִּשְׁבִיל יְהוֹיָקִים. נִסְתַּכֵּל בְּדוֹרוֹ, וְנִתְקָרְרָה דַּעְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the reason for the unusual formulation is that **the Holy One, Blessed be He, sought to restore the entire world to chaos and void,** as it was in the beginning before Creation, **on account of Jehoiakim** the wicked; but **He observed** Jehoiakim's **generation,** which included the righteous who had not yet been exiled to Babylonia, **and His mind was set at ease.** The term "in the beginning" is used to signify that it is as though the world had been created anew.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּקֵּשׁ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְהַחְזִיר אֶת הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ לְתוֹהוּ וָבוֹהוּ בִּשְׁבִיל דּוֹרוֹ שֶׁל צִדְקִיָּה. נִסְתַּכֵּל בְּצִדְקִיָּה וְנִתְקָרְרָה דַּעְתּוֹ. בְּצִדְקִיָּה נָמֵי כְּתִיב: "וַיַּעַשׂ הָרַע בְּעֵינֵי ה׳". שֶׁהָיָה בְּיָדוֹ לִמְחוֹת וְלֹא מִיחָה.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, **the Holy One, Blessed be He, sought to restore the entire world to chaos and void,** as it was in the beginning before Creation, **on account of the generation of Zedekiah,** as only the wicked remained after the exile of the righteous; but **He observed Zedekiah and His mind was set at ease.** The Gemara asks: **With regard to Zedekiah it is also written: "And he did evil in the eyes of the Lord"** (II Kings 24:19); why was God's mind set at ease? The Gemara answers: Zedekiah was not wicked; rather, **he had** it **in his power to rebuke** the people of his generation **and he did not rebuke** them. Therefore, their sins are attributed to him.

*Ela bikesh HaKadosh Baruch Hu lehachzir et ha'olam kulo l'tohu vavohu bishvil Yehoyakim* — rather, God wanted to return the entire world to void and nothingness, *bishvil Yehoyakim*, on account of the evil of *Yehoyakim*. *Mistakel bedoro venitkara daato* — but He looked at the generation and was appeased, for the people themselves were still relatively righteous, and so He stayed His hand.

*Bikesh HaKadosh Baruch Hu laasot olam kulo tohu vavohu bishvil doro shel Tzidkiyahu* — then, in the time of *Tzidkiyahu*, God again wanted to reverse the world to *tohu vavohu*, this time because of the wickedness of the entire generation. *Nistakel beTzidkiyahu venitkara daato* — but He looked at *Tzidkiyahu* and was appeased, for at least *Tzidkiyahu* himself was righteous.

### Silent Enablement

How did the situation reverse itself so quickly between *Yehoyakim* and *Tzidkiyahu*? *Rashi* explains: at the end of *Yehoyakim*'s reign — the period of the exile of *hachoresh vehamasger*, all the *talmidei chachamim* and righteous people — were taken to *Bavel* first. They were removed during *Yehoyakim*'s reign, so that by the time of *Tzidkiyahu*, none of them remained. That is how the two reigns present such mirror-image portraits: a righteous populace under a wicked king, followed by a wicked populace under a righteous king.

### Tzidkiyahu's Silence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: מַאי דִּכְתִיב "אִישׁ חָכָם נִשְׁפָּט אֶת אִישׁ אֱוִיל וְרָגַז וְשָׂחַק וְאֵין נָחַת"? אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: כָּעַסְתִּי עַל אָחָז וּנְתַתִּיו בְּיַד מַלְכֵי דַמֶּשֶׂק, זִיבַּח וְקִיטֵּר [לֵאלֹהֵיהֶם], שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיִּזְבַּח לֵאלֹהֵי דַרְמֶשֶׂק הַמַּכִּים בּוֹ וַיֹּאמֶר [כִּי] אֱלֹהֵי מַלְכֵי אֲרָם הֵם מַעְזְרִים אוֹתָם לָהֶם אֲזַבֵּחַ וְיַעְזְרוּנִי וְהֵם הָיוּ [לוֹ] לְהַכְשִׁילוֹ וּלְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל".
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "If a wise man contends with a foolish man, whether he is angry or laughs, there is no rest"** (Proverbs 29:9)? **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: I was angry with Ahaz and I delivered him into the hand of the kings of Damascus. He** then **sacrificed** offerings **and burned** incense **to their gods, as it is stated: "He sacrificed to the gods of Damascus that smote him; and he said: Because the gods of the kings of Aram help them, I will sacrifice to them, and they will help me. But they were the ruin of him, and of all Israel"** (II Chronicles 28:23). When the Holy One, Blessed be He, was angry with Israel and caused them to lose a war to prompt them to repent of their sins, their response was to worship idols.

The *Gemara* then notes: *uveTzidkiyahu nami ktiv vayaas hara be'einei Hashem* — but the verse also says that *Tzidkiyahu* did evil in the eyes of God. How can *Tzidkiyahu* be called righteous if the verse characterizes him as evil? *Shehaya beyado limchot velo micha* — because it was within his power to protest and he did not protest. His "evil" was passive enablement: he silently permitted the sins of those around him when he had the standing to intervene.

This principle — that the silent bystander who could have acted bears genuine moral responsibility — is treated with full seriousness by the *Gemara*. The failure to protest constitutes a form of complicity, and *Tzidkiyahu* is characterized accordingly.

### Gate Degraded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: מַאי דִּכְתִיב "אִישׁ חָכָם נִשְׁפָּט אֶת אִישׁ אֱוִיל וְרָגַז וְשָׂחַק וְאֵין נָחַת"? אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: כָּעַסְתִּי עַל אָחָז וּנְתַתִּיו בְּיַד מַלְכֵי דַמֶּשֶׂק, זִיבַּח וְקִיטֵּר [לֵאלֹהֵיהֶם], שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיִּזְבַּח לֵאלֹהֵי דַרְמֶשֶׂק הַמַּכִּים בּוֹ וַיֹּאמֶר [כִּי] אֱלֹהֵי מַלְכֵי אֲרָם הֵם מַעְזְרִים אוֹתָם לָהֶם אֲזַבֵּחַ וְיַעְזְרוּנִי וְהֵם הָיוּ [לוֹ] לְהַכְשִׁילוֹ וּלְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל".
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "If a wise man contends with a foolish man, whether he is angry or laughs, there is no rest"** (Proverbs 29:9)? **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: I was angry with Ahaz and I delivered him into the hand of the kings of Damascus. He** then **sacrificed** offerings **and burned** incense **to their gods, as it is stated: "He sacrificed to the gods of Damascus that smote him; and he said: Because the gods of the kings of Aram help them, I will sacrifice to them, and they will help me. But they were the ruin of him, and of all Israel"** (II Chronicles 28:23). When the Holy One, Blessed be He, was angry with Israel and caused them to lose a war to prompt them to repent of their sins, their response was to worship idols.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וַיָּבֹאוּ כֹּל [שָׂרֵי] מֶלֶךְ בָּבֶל (וַיָּבֹאוּ) [וַיֵּשְׁבוּ] בְּשַׁעַר הַתָּוֶךְ". אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: מָקוֹם שֶׁמְּחַתְּכִין בּוֹ הֲלָכוֹת. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי, בְּאַתְרָא דְּמָרֵיהּ תְּלָא לֵיהּ זְיָינֵיהּ, תַּמָּן קוּלְבָּא רָעֲיָא קוּלְּתֵיהּ תְּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"And all the princes of the king of Babylonia came in and sat in the middle gate [*sha'ar hattavekh*]"** (Jeremiah 39:3), **Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai:** *Sha'ar hattavekh* was **the place in which they** would **decide [*meḥattekhin*] *halakhot*. Rav Pappa says** that **this** is in accordance with the adage **that people say: In the place that the master** of the house **hangs his weapons, there the despicable shepherd hangs his jug.** In the place where the Sanhedrin convened, Nebuchadnezzar the wicked and his princes now assemble.

*Vayavo'u kol sarei melech bavel vayeshvu beshaar hatavech* — all the officers of the king of *Bavel* came and sat in the gate of *hatavech*, the middle gate. *Amar Rabbi Yochanan mishum Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai: makom shemechatchen bo halachos* — this is the place where legal rulings would be rendered, the seat of the *Sanhedrin* on the Temple Mount and in the *azarah*. *Rashi* notes: *hatavech*, from the root *choteich*, cutting — the *hei* and *chet* exchanged — because this was the place where matters of *halacha* were "cut," decided. *Rashi* further explains: *nehepach lemoshav leitzim* — it became a place of entertainment and mockery, a complete reversal of what had been.

*Amar Rav Papa: hainu de'amrei inshi, b'asra d'marei tali lei zainiei, taman kulba ra'aya kuzya tali* — that is what people say: in the same place where the master would hang his weapons, there a lowly shepherd now hangs up a water jug. This captures the outrage — not only of the mighty fallen, but of the sacred desecrated, the most solemn seat of judgment reduced to a resting place of foreign officers.

## Escalating Desecration

### Lazy Man's Field

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סִימַן: עַל שְׂדֵה, (בָתִּים) [כִּתִּים], לֹא תְּאוּנֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites **a mnemonic** for the following statements that Rav Ḥisda said that Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba said: **By the field, houses, shall not befall.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב "עַל שְׂדֵה אִישׁ עָצֵל עָבַרְתִּי וְעַל כֶּרֶם אָדָם חֲסַר לֵב וְהִנֵּה עָלָה כֻלּוֹ קִמְּשׂוֹנִים כׇּסּוּ פָנָיו חֲרֻלִּים וְגֶדֶר אֲבָנָיו נֶהֱרָסָה"? "עַל שְׂדֵה אִישׁ עָצֵל עָבַרְתִּי" – זֶה אָחָז, "וְעַל כֶּרֶם אָדָם חֲסַר לֵב" – זֶה מְנַשֶּׁה, "וְהִנֵּה עָלָה כֻלּוֹ קִמְּשׂוֹנִים" – זֶה אָמוֹן, "כׇּסּוּ פָנָיו חֲרֻלִּים" – זֶה יְהוֹיָקִים, "וְגֶדֶר אֲבָנָיו נֶהֱרָסָה" – זֶה צִדְקִיָּהוּ שֶׁנֶּחְרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ בְּיָמָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says** that **Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "I passed by the field of an indolent man, and by the vineyard of a man void of understanding; and behold, it was overgrown with thistles, and its surface was covered with nettles, and its stone wall was broken"** (Proverbs 24:30–31)? The verse describes the spiritual and political decline of the kings of Judea. **"I passed by the field of an indolent man"; this** is a reference to **Ahaz. "And by the vineyard of a man void of understanding"; this** is a reference to **Manasseh. "And behold, it was overgrown with thistles"; this** is a reference to **Amon. "And its surface was covered with nettles"; this** is a reference to **Jehoiakim. "And its stone wall was broken"; this** is a reference to **Zedekiah, in whose days the Temple was destroyed.**

*Amar Rav Chisda amar Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba*: what is meant by the verse *al sadeh ish atzel avarti* — I passed by the field of a lazy man — *v'al kerem adam chasar lev* — and by the vineyard of a man lacking understanding — *ve'hineh ala kulo kimshonim* — and behold it was all filled with thorns and thistles — *kasu fanav charullim* — its face covered with weeds — *v'geder avanav nehersa* — and the stone walls were broken through? The image of the broken wall is what anchors the *drasha*, evoking the destruction of the *Beit HaMikdash*.

### Stages of Ruin

The *Gemara* now works backwards through the verse, identifying each element with one of the evil kings. *Al sadeh ish atzel avarti zeh Achaz* — the lazy man is *Achaz*, who was passive in restoring the people to righteousness. *V'al kerem adam chasar lev zeh Menashe* — the vineyard and the man lacking understanding is *Menashe*. *Ve'hineh ala kulo kimshonim zeh Amon* — the thorns and thistles are *Amon*, because, as we will see, the *mizbe'ach* had been neglected until it was filled with what the *Gemara* will describe as cobwebs from disuse, as if weeds had grown up over everything. *Kasu fanav charullim zeh Yehoyakim* — the weeds covering the face correspond to *Yehoyakim*. *V'geder avanav nehersa zeh Tzidkiyahu* — and the broken walls are *Tzidkiyahu*, *shenechrav Beit HaMikdash beyamav* — for it was in his days that the *Beit HaMikdash* was destroyed. Even though *Tzidkiyahu* himself was not guilty of active wrongdoing, by his time the walls had already fallen.

### Four Excluded Groups

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא: אַרְבַּע כִּיתּוֹת אֵין מְקַבְּלוֹת פְּנֵי שְׁכִינָה: כַּת לֵצִים, כַּת שַׁקְרָנִים, כַּת חֲנֵיפִים, כַּת מְסַפְּרֵי לָשׁוֹן הָרָע. כַּת לֵצִים – דִּכְתִיב: "מָשַׁךְ יָדוֹ אֶת לֹצְצִים". כַּת שַׁקְרָנִים – דִּכְתִיב: "דֹּבֵר שְׁקָרִים לֹא יִכּוֹן לְנֶגֶד עֵינָי". כַּת חֲנֵיפִים – דִּכְתִיב: "כִּי לֹא לְפָנָיו חָנֵף יָבוֹא". כַּת מְסַפְּרֵי לָשׁוֹן הָרָע – דִּכְתִיב: "כִּי לֹא אֵל חָפֵץ רֶשַׁע אָתָּה לֹא יְגֻרְךָ רָע", צַדִּיק אַתָּה וְלֹא יִהְיֶה בִּמְגוּרְךָ רָע.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the previous statement, the Gemara cites an additional statement. **And Rav Ḥisda says** that **Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba says** that **four groups do not receive the Divine Presence:** This pertains to **the group of cynics, the group of liars, the group of flatterers,** and **the group of slanderers.** This pertains to **the group of cynics, as it is written: "He withdrew His hand with cynics"** (Hosea 7:5), indicating that God distanced Himself from them. This pertains to **the group of liars, as it is written: "He that speaks falsehoods shall not be established before My eyes"** (Psalms 101:7). This pertains to **the group of flatterers, as it is written: "That a flatterer shall not come before Him"** (Job 13:16). This pertains to **the group of slanderers, as it is stated: "For You are not a God who desires wickedness; evil shall not dwell with You"** (Psalms 5:5), which means: **You are righteous, and there will be no** form of **evil in Your dwelling.**

*V'amar Rav Chisda amar Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba: arba kitos ein mekablot pnei Shechinah* — four groups do not merit receiving the divine presence: *kat leitzim*, the mockers; *kat shakranim*, the liars; *kat chanifim*, the flatterers; and *kat mesaprei lashon hara*, those who speak evil of others. It is worth noting that all four of these categories concern speech — either the corruption of honest communication or its weaponization against others. There is something significant in that connection: encountering *Shechinah*, whether in prayer or in the ultimate redemption, requires a kind of integrity in language that these four groups have fundamentally violated.

*Kat leitzim* — the mockers — are excluded as the verse says, *dover shekarim lo yikon leneged einai*, one who speaks falsehood will not stand before me; this same verse was cited earlier in the context of the *ruach* of *Nabot*. *Kat chanifim* — flatterers — are excluded as the verse says *ki lo lefanav chanef yavo*, flattery will not come before Him. *Kat mesaprei lashon hara* — evil speakers — are excluded as the verse says *ki lo el chafetz resha atah*, you are not a God who desires evil; *rashi* reads the context as *lo yegurcha ra*, evil does not dwell with you.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב "לֹא תְאֻנֶּה אֵלֶיךָ רָעָה וְנֶגַע לֹא יִקְרַב בְּאׇהֳלֶךָ"? "לֹא תְאֻנֶּה אֵלֶיךָ רָעָה" – שֶׁלֹּא יִשְׁלוֹט בָּהֶן יֵצֶר הָרָע, "וְנֶגַע לֹא יִקְרַב בְּאׇהֳלֶךָ" – שֶׁלֹּא תִּמְצָא אִשְׁתְּךָ סְפֵק נִדָּה בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁתָּבֹא מִן הַדֶּרֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥisda says** that **Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "No evil shall befall you, nor shall any plague come near your tent"** (Psalms 91:10)? **"No evil shall befall you"** means **that the evil inclination shall not dominate them. "Nor shall any plague come near your tent"** means **that you will never find your wife** in a state of **uncertainty** whether she has the halakhic status of **a menstruating woman when you return from a journey.** After a period of separation, when a husband desires his wife, her uncertain status may prove more frustrating than a situation where the prohibition is clear-cut.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: "לֹא תְאֻנֶּה אֵלֶיךָ רָעָה" – שֶׁלֹּא יְבַעֲתוּךָ חֲלוֹמוֹת רָעִים וְהִרְהוּרִים רָעִים. "וְנֶגַע לֹא יִקְרַב בְּאׇהֳלֶךָ" – שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא לְךָ בֵּן אוֹ תַּלְמִיד שֶׁמַּקְדִּיחַ תַּבְשִׁילוֹ בָּרַבִּים, כְּגוֹן יֵשׁוּ הַנּוֹצְרִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** the phrase **"no evil shall befall you"** means **that you will be frightened neither by bad dreams nor by evil thoughts. "Nor shall any plague come near your tent"** means **that you will not have a child or student who overcooks his food in public,** i.e., sins in public and causes others to sin, **such as** in the well-known case of **Jesus the Nazarene.**

*V'amar Rav Chisda amar Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba: mai dichtiv, lo te'uneh elecha ra'ah v'nega lo yikrav b'oholecha* — what is the meaning of the verse "evil will not befall you and no plague will come near your tent"? *Lo te'uneh elecha ra'ah shelo yishlot becha yetzer hara* — the evil inclination, understood here primarily in its sexual dimension, will not have dominion over you; having it is human, but being controlled by it is the danger. *V'nega lo yikrav b'oholecha* — and no plague in your tent: *shelo timtza ishtecha safek niddah b'sha'ah shetavo min haderech* — that you will not find your wife in a status of doubtful *niddah* at the moment you return from a long journey. As *Rashi* observes, *safek niddah* is in some ways harder than *vadai niddah*, because with certainty one can simply accept the situation, whereas with doubt one is left grappling with rationalization.

*Davar acher*: *lo te'uneh elecha ra'ah shelo yeva'atucha chalomot ra'im v'hirhurim ra'im* — you will not be disturbed by bad dreams and troubling thoughts. *V'nega lo yikrav b'oholecha shelo yihiyeh lecha ben o talmid shemakdiach tavshilo barabbim* — you will not have a child or a student who publicly "burns his stew," that is, who goes off the path in a way that reflects badly upon you. The concern here is for one's tent understood not as the domestic space of marital life but as the tent of Torah — one's family and one's disciples.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כָּאן בֵּרְכוֹ אָבִיו, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ בֵּרְכַתּוּ אִמּוֹ. "כִּי מַלְאָכָיו יְצַוֶּה לָּךְ לִשְׁמׇרְךָ בְּכׇל דְּרָכֶיךָ. עַל כַּפַּיִם יִשָּׂאוּנְךָ וְגוֹ׳. עַל שַׁחַל וָפֶתֶן תִּדְרֹךְ וְגוֹ׳".
>
> **Translation:** **Until this point** in the psalm, Solomon's **father,** David, **blessed him,** as these are blessings appropriate for a father to bless his son. **From this** point **forward, his mother blessed him,** as these are blessings appropriate for a mother to bless her son. **"For he shall order His angels to preserve you in all your ways. They shall bear you on their hands,** lest they dash your foot against a stone. **You shall tread upon the lion and the adder;** the young lion and the crocodile shall you trample under foot" (Psalms 91:11–13).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כָּאן בֵּרְכַתּוּ אִמּוֹ, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ בֵּרְכַתּוּ שָׁמַיִם. "כִּי בִי חָשַׁק וַאֲפַלְּטֵהוּ אֲשַׂגְּבֵהוּ כִּי יָדַע שְׁמִי. יִקְרָאֵנִי וְאֶעֱנֵהוּ עִמּוֹ אָנֹכִי בְצָרָה אֲחַלְּצֵהוּ וַאֲכַבְּדֵהוּ. אוֹרֶךְ יָמִים אַשְׂבִּיעֵהוּ וְאַרְאֵהוּ בִּישׁוּעָתִי".
>
> **Translation:** **Until this point** in the psalm, **his mother blessed him. From this** point **forward,** God in **Heaven blessed him,** as the psalm proceeds to refer to God in the first person, as though speaking in His name: **"Because he has desired Me, therefore I will deliver him; I will set him on high, because he has known My name. He shall call upon Me, and I will answer him; I will be with him in trouble; I will deliver him, and honor him. With long life I will satisfy him, and show him My salvation"** (Psalms 91:14–16).

*Ad kan bercho aviv* — to this point are the blessings of the father. As *Rashi* notes, *David* is blessing *Shlomo*, and these are characteristically paternal concerns: the sexual dimension, the disciples, the continuity of righteous progeny.

*U'mikan v'ilech birchato imo* — and from this point begin the blessings of the mother. *Ki malachav yetzaveh lach lishmorcha bechol derachecha, al kapayim yisau'ncha, al shachal vafeten tidroch* — God will command His angels to guard you in all your paths, to carry you upon their palms, to allow you to tread upon snakes and serpents. A mother's blessing is for safety and protection. *Ad kan birchas imo* — to this point the blessings of the mother. *Mikan va'ilach birchas hashamayim* — and from here God Himself blesses: *ki bi chashak va'afalteihu, asagveihu, ki yada shemi, yikra'eini ve'eneihu, imo anochi betzarah, achaltzeihu va'achabdeihu, orech yamim asbi'eihu ve'ar'eihu bishu'asi* — because he trusted in Me I will deliver him; I will protect him because he knows My name; he will call to Me and I will answer; I am with him in his trouble; I will rescue him and honor him; I will satisfy him with long life and show him My salvation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: מַאי דִּכְתִיב "וְיִמָּנַע מֵרְשָׁעִים אוֹרָם וּזְרוֹעַ רָמָה תִּשָּׁבֵר"? מִפְּנֵי מָה עַיִן שֶׁל רְשָׁעִים תְּלוּיָה? כֵּיוָן שֶׁנַּעֲשֶׂה אָדָם רָשׁ מִלְּמַטָּה, נַעֲשֶׂה רָשׁ מִלְּמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "And He will withhold their light from the wicked [*resha'im*], and the high arm shall be broken"** (Job 38:15)? **For what** reason is the letter ***ayin* of** the word ***resha'im* suspended** slightly above the rest of the letters? It is suspended so that the word will be read *rashim*, meaning poor people. It means: **Once a person becomes poor** on earth **below** and the number of his enemies grows, **he becomes poor** in Heaven **above,** as he is certainly a sinner and that is why he is hated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא נִכְתְּבַיהּ כְּלָל? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: חַד אָמַר, מִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹדוֹ שֶׁל דָּוִד; וְחַד אָמַר, מִשּׁוּם כְּבוֹדוֹ שֶׁל נְחֶמְיָה בֶּן חֲכַלְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: If that is the meaning, **let** the *ayin* **not be written at all** and let the verse say: *Rashim*. **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Elazar** both explain why the *ayin* was not omitted. **One says:** It was **due to** the desire to preserve **the honor of David,** who had numerous enemies below despite the fact that he was a righteous person and had a place in the World-to-Come. **And one says:** It was **due to** the desire to preserve **the honor of Nehemiah, son of Hacaliah,** who also had numerous enemies below, despite his righteousness.

*Amar Reish Lakish: mai dichtiv, veyimana mersha'im oram vezroa ramah tishaber* — God withheld from the wicked their light, and the outstretched arm will be broken. *Mipnei mah ayin shel rasha'im teluyah?* — why is the *ayin* of *resha'im* suspended above the word in the text? Without the *ayin*, the word would read *rashim*, the poor. *Keivan shena'asah adam rash milmata* — when a person is impoverished below, meaning, as *Rashi* reads it, not materially poor but lacking in friends, with everyone arrayed against him — *na'asah rash milma'alah* — he becomes poor above as well; God's favor does not rest upon him either. The principle resonates with *matza chein veseichel tov be'einei Elokim ve'adam* — finding favor in the eyes of both God and man as interconnected realities.

*Velo nischivei klal* — but if so, why write the word with an *ayin* at all? Why not just write *rashim*? *Rabbi Yochanan veRabbi Elazar, chad amar mipnei kevodo shel David* — because the rule is not universal: righteous people can also be without friends. *David* himself faced constant opposition — from *Shaul*, from rebellions, from all sides — and the principle would be false as applied to him. *Vechad amar mipnei kevodo shel Nechemya ben Chachalyah* — and the other says because of *Nechemya*, who also faced tremendous opposition despite his righteousness. So the word is written with an *ayin* to signal that the pattern holds for the wicked, not as an iron rule about everyone lacking friends.

## Menashe's Repentance (continued again)

### Torah Scholar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מְנַשֶּׁה הָיָה שׁוֹנֶה חֲמִשִּׁים וַחֲמִשָּׁה פָּנִים בְּתוֹרַת כֹּהֲנִים, כְּנֶגֶד שְׁנֵי מַלְכוּתוֹ. אַחְאָב – שְׁמֹנִים וַחֲמִשָּׁה, יָרׇבְעָם – מֵאָה וּשְׁלֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Manasseh,** king of Judea, **would study fifty-five** different **aspects in** interpreting ***Torat Kohanim*,** the halakhic midrash on Leviticus, **corresponding to the years of his reign,** indicating that he possessed great knowledge. **Ahab** was greater and studied **eighty-five** aspects, and **Jeroboam** was greater still and studied **one hundred and three** aspects.

*Tana mna lan: Menashe hayah shoneh chamishim vachamishah panim beToras Kohanim* — a *tanna* taught: *Menashe* would expound fifty-five different interpretations of *Vayikra*, *keneged shenei malchuso* — corresponding to the years of his reign. *Achav shemonim vechamishah* — *Achav* had eighty-five different interpretations. *Yerovam me'ah ushloshah* — and *Yerovam* one hundred and three.

This is a remarkable acknowledgment: all three of these notoriously wicked kings were, simultaneously, towering *talmidei chachamim*. One message is that human beings are genuinely complex — they are never simply all evil or all good. But there is a harder religious message as well. One might be tempted to say: if people can be such great Torah scholars and still behave with such depravity, what is the point of Torah learning? The *Gemara* does not shy away from this tension. It does not pretend that Torah scholarship inevitably produces moral rectitude. One can have a person who is a *talmid chacham* of the highest order and simultaneously the wickedest of kings. We do Torah because God commanded it; we hope it transforms us — and often it does — but it is not a guarantee, and the *Gemara* is honest about that.

## Escalating Desecration (continued)

### Four-Faced Idol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב "פֶּסֶל", וּכְתִיב "פְּסִילִים". אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בַּתְּחִלָּה עָשָׂה לוֹ פַּרְצוּף אֶחָד, וּלְבַסּוֹף עָשָׂה לוֹ אַרְבָּעָה פַּרְצוּפִים, כְּדֵי שֶׁתִּרְאֶה שְׁכִינָה וְתִכְעוֹס.
>
> **Translation:** **It is written** that Manasseh crafted **"an idol"** (II Chronicles 33:7), **and it is written** that Manasseh crafted **"idols"** (II Chronicles 33:19). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Initially, he crafted one face [*partzuf* ] for** the idol, **and ultimately, he crafted four faces for it so that the Divine Presence would see** it from each side **and become angry.**

The *Gemara* addresses a textual question: one verse uses the singular *pessel* — a single idol — while another verse uses the plural *psilim* — multiple idols. Which is it? *Amar Rabbi Yochanan: b'techila asa lo partzuf echad* — at the beginning, *Menashe* made an idol with a single face, which he placed in the *Beit HaMikdash*. *U'vasof asa lo arba'ah partzufim* — but ultimately he gave it four faces, *kedei she'taknit Shechinah* — in order to provoke the *Shechinah*. God sees in all directions; *Menashe* wanted that no matter which direction God looked, he would see the face of the idol staring back at Him. This is *lehachis* — spite — taken to its architectural extreme.

### Narrow Bed Image

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָחָז הֶעֱמִידוֹ בַּעֲלִיָּיה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְאֵת הַמִּזְבְּחוֹת אֲשֶׁר עַל הַגָּג עֲלִיַּת אָחָז וְגוֹ׳". מְנַשֶּׁה הֶעֱמִידוֹ בַּהֵיכָל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיָּשֶׂם אֶת פֶּסֶל הָאֲשֵׁרָה אֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה בַּבַּיִת אֲשֶׁר אָמַר ה׳ אֶל דָּוִד וְאֶל שְׁלֹמֹה [בְנוֹ] בַּבַּיִת הַזֶּה וּבִירוּשָׁלִַים אֲשֶׁר בָּחַרְתִּי מִכֹּל שִׁבְטֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אָשִׂים אֶת שְׁמִי לְעוֹלָם". אָמוֹן הִכְנִיסוֹ לְבֵית קׇדְשֵׁי הַקֳּדָשִׁים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "כִּי קָצַר הַמַּצָּע מֵהִשְׂתָּרֵעַ וְהַמַּסֵּכָה צָרָה כְּהִתְכַּנֵּס".
>
> **Translation:** **Ahaz placed** that idol **in the upper chamber** in the Temple, **as it is stated: "And the altars that were on the roof of the upper chamber of Ahaz"** (II Kings 23:12). **Manasseh placed it in the Sanctuary** itself, **as it is stated: "And he set the graven image of the *ashera* that he had crafted, in that house of which the Lord said to David, and to Solomon his son: In this house, and in Jerusalem, which I have chosen from all the tribes of Israel, will I put My name forever"** (II Kings 21:7). **Amon introduced it into the Holy of Holies, as it is stated: "For the bed is too short for spreading, and the covering too narrow for when he gathers himself"** (Isaiah 28:20).

*Achaz he'emido ba'aliyah* — *Achaz* had placed an idol in the upper chamber, on the rooftops, as the verse says: *v'es hamizbechos asher al hagag aliyas Achaz* — the altars on the rooftop of the upper chamber of *Achaz*. It was somewhat concealed, elevated rather than displayed in the main space. *Menashe he'emido b'heichal* — *Menashe* placed the idol in the *Heichal* itself, as the verse says: *vayasem es pessel ha'asheirah asher asa babayis asher amar Hashem el David v'el Shlomo bno* — he set the carved image of the *asheira* in the very house about which God had said to *David* and to *Shlomo* his son: *babayis zeh uviYerushalayim asher bacharti mikol shivtei Yisrael asim es shmi l'olam* — in this house and in Jerusalem which I have chosen from all the tribes of Israel I will place My name forever. That is the house into which *Menashe* placed his idol.

*Amon hechniso l'veis kadshei hakodashim* — *Amon* brought it into the Holy of Holies. The verse that serves as the hook here is *ki katzar hamatza mehishtarei'a* — for the bed is too short to stretch out on — *v'hamaseichah tzarah k'hiskanes* — and the blanket too narrow to gather around oneself. The literal meaning refers to a couch and a cover, but the *drasha* builds on both.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי "כִּי קָצַר הַמַּצָּע מֵהִשְׂתָּרֵעַ"? אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: [כִּי] קָצַר הַמַּצָּע זֶה מִלְּהִשְׂתָּרֵר עָלָיו שְׁנֵי רֵעִים כְּאֶחָד. מַאי "וְהַמַּסֵּכָה צָרָה וְגוֹ׳"? אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי: רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן כִּי הֲוָה מָטֵי לְהַאי קְרָא הֲוָה קָא בָכֵי, מִי שֶׁכָּתַב בּוֹ "כֹּנֵס כַּנֵּד מֵי הַיָּם" תֵּעָשֶׂה לוֹ מַסֵּכָה צָרָה?
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the meaning of the phrase **"For the bed is too short for spreading"? Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says** that it means: **For his bed is too narrow for two colleagues to spread upon it together,** as it is impossible that service of God and idol worship will coexist there. **What** is the meaning of the phrase **"and the covering [*masekha*] too narrow [*tzara*]** for when he gathers himself [*kehitkanes*]"? **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says: When Rabbi Yonatan would reach this verse he would cry. He about whom it is written: "He gathers [*kones*] the waters of the sea like a rampart"** (Psalms 33:7), **shall a graven image [*masekha*] be a rival [*tzara*]?**

*Amar Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yonatan: ki katzar hamatza zeh m'lehishtareir alav shnei rei'im k'echad* — this bed, which is the *Beit HaMikdash* and the *Kodesh HaKodashim*, is too narrow for two masters to rule within it simultaneously, too narrow for two gods.

*Mai v'hamaseichah tzarah?* The word *maseichah* is the same term used for a cast idol. *Amar Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani Rabbi Yonatan ki havai mati l'hai kra havah bachi* — when he would reach this verse he would weep. *Mi shekasuv bo kones k'ned mei hayam* — the One of whom it is written that He gathers the waters of the sea like in a flask — *ta'aseh lo maseichah tzarah?* — that for Him there should be a *tzarah*, a competing wife, a rival, an idol? The word *tzarah* carries its full double meaning: the idol is simultaneously a rival deity and a *tzarah* in the sense of a co-wife, a competing claimant to God's own dwelling. *V'hamaseichah tzarah k'hiskanes* — the idol, the *tzarah*, when it was entered into the *Kodesh HaKodashim*. The imagery is devastating: the God who contains all the waters of the ocean has been given a rival who is crowded alongside Him in His innermost chamber.

### Achaz Seals Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* [אָחָז בִּטֵּל אֶת הָעֲבוֹדָה], וְחָתַם אֶת הַתּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "צוֹר תְּעוּדָה חֲתוֹם תּוֹרָה בְּלִמּוּדָי".
>
> **Translation:** **Ahaz nullified the** Temple **service and sealed the Torah,** prohibiting its study, **as it is stated: "Bind up the testimony, seal the Torah among my disciples"** (Isaiah 8:16).

Returning now to fill in the pattern of these kings' escalating violations: *Achaz bital es ha'avodah* — *Achaz* brought the Temple service to a halt. *V'chasam es haTorah* — and he sealed the Torah, preventing its study, as the verse says: *tzur te'udah chasum Torah belimudai* — seal the Torah among my learners.

### Menashe Cuts Names

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנַשֶּׁה קָדַר אֶת הָאַזְכָּרוֹת, וְהָרַס אֶת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Manasseh excised the mentions** of God's names from sacred books **and destroyed the altar.**

*Menashe kadar es ha'azkarot* — *Menashe* cut out or scraped away God's names from the *Sefer Torah*, as was noted earlier in a different connection, replacing them with names of *avodah zarah*. *V'haras es hamizbeiach* — and he destroyed the *mizbe'ach*, going beyond merely stopping the service to physically demolishing the altar.

### Amon's Violations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמוֹן שָׂרַף אֶת הַתּוֹרָה, וְהֶעֱלָה שְׂמָמִית עַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Amon burned the Torah and sacrificed a gecko,** an impure creeping animal, **upon the altar.**

*Amon saraf es haTorah* — *Amon* was not content with merely preventing Torah study; he burned the Torah scrolls. *V'he'ela shamamit al gabei hamizbeiach* — and he brought up a *shamamit* — apparently a type of lizard, perhaps a gecko or salamander — upon the altar. Even if the *mizbe'ach* had only been partially damaged rather than totally destroyed, the image is of total desolation: the altar of God overgrown, defiled, hosting vermin rather than offerings. This circles back to the earlier *drasha* of the lazy man's field, with its thorns and weeds: the *Beit HaMikdash* has become like an abandoned field.

## Spiting God

### Amon's Incest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָחָז הִתִּיר אֶת הָעֶרְוָה. מְנַשֶּׁה בָּא עַל אֲחוֹתוֹ. אָמוֹן בָּא עַל אִמּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "כִּי הוּא אָמוֹן הִרְבָּה אַשְׁמָה". רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, חַד אָמַר: שֶׁשָּׂרַף אֶת הַתּוֹרָה, וְחַד אָמַר: שֶׁבָּא עַל אִמּוֹ. אָמְרָה לוֹ אִמּוֹ: כְּלוּם יֵשׁ לְךָ הֲנָאָה מִמָּקוֹם שֶׁיָּצָאתָ מִמֶּנּוּ? אָמַר לָהּ: כְּלוּם אֲנִי עוֹשֶׂה אֶלָּא לְהַכְעִיס אֶת בּוֹרְאִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ahaz permitted** engaging in sexual intercourse with **forbidden relatives,** and announced that marriage between those relatives is permitted. **Manasseh** exploited that pronouncement and **engaged in sexual intercourse with his sister.** **Amon engaged in sexual intercourse with his mother, as it is stated: "But Amon increased his guilt"** (II Chronicles 33:23), indicating that he performed a greater and more disgraceful transgression than anyone else. **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Elazar** disagreed about his transgression; **one says that he burned the Torah, and one says that he engaged in sexual intercourse with his mother. His mother said to him: Do you have any pleasure** by engaging in intercourse **from the place from which you emerged? He said to her: I am doing** this **only to express insolence to my Creator,** not for my pleasure.

*Achaz hitir es ha'erva* — *Achaz* permitted forbidden sexual relations for the population. *Menashe ba'al achoso* — *Menashe* went further and actually had relations with his own sister. *V'Amon ba al imo* — and *Amon* had relations with his own mother. The verse says *ki hu Amon hirbah ashmah* — he multiplied his transgression. What makes *Amon*'s sin so particularly severe in the *Gemara*'s reading is not merely that it represents the outermost boundary of *arayos* but that it seems to have been entirely devoid of the ordinary pull of desire.

His mother said to him: *klum yesh lecha hana'ah mimakom sheyatzata mimenu?* — Do you have any pleasure from the place from which you emerged? The implication is that there is no erotic dynamic here, no *yetzer hara* in the ordinary sense. *Amar lah: klum ani oseh ela lehachis et bori* — I am only doing this to spite my Creator. The sin was not the product of temptation overcome but of pure, calculated contempt for God.

### Yehoyakim Blasphemes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא יְהוֹיָקִים אָמַר: קַמָּאֵי לָא יָדְעִי לְאַרְגּוֹזֵי, כְּלוּם אָנוּ צְרִיכִין אֶלָּא לְאוֹרוֹ? יֵשׁ לָנוּ זְהַב פַּרְוַיִים שֶׁאָנוּ מִשְׁתַּמְּשִׁין בּוֹ, יִטּוֹל אוֹרוֹ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: וַהֲלֹא כֶּסֶף וְזָהָב שֶׁלּוֹ הוּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "לִי הַכֶּסֶף וְלִי הַזָּהָב נְאֻם ה׳ צְבָאוֹת"? אָמַר לָהֶם: כְּבָר נְתָנוֹ לָנוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "הַשָּׁמַיִם שָׁמַיִם לַה׳ וְהָאָרֶץ נָתַן לִבְנֵי אָדָם".
>
> **Translation:** **When Jehoiakim came** along and reigned, **he said:** My **predecessors did not know** how **to express insolence to** God. **Do we need** God **even for his light?** Since **we have *parvayim* gold that we use** that shines, **let** God **take His light** from the world. **They said to him: Aren't the silver and the gold His, as it is stated: "The silver is mine and the gold is mine says the Lord of hosts"** (Haggai 2:8)? Jehoiakim **said to them: He has already given it to us, as it is stated: "The heavens are the heavens of the Lord, but the earth He has given to the children of men"** (Psalms 115:16).

*Ki ata Yehoyakim* — when *Yehoyakim* came, he said: *kama'ei lo yadu le'argezei klum* — the kings who came before didn't even know how to anger God properly. *Anu klum anu tzrichim le'oro?* — Do we even need God's light? *Hai it lanu zehav parvayim de'anan mishtamshin bei* — we have this beautiful gold of *Parvayim* that gives us light. Who needs God? *Lekatzatz et oro* — let God take away His light; it makes no difference to us.

Those around him pushed back: *velo kesef vezahav shelo hu?* — does not the gold itself belong to God? *She'ne'emar: li hakesef veli hazahav ne'um Hashem Tzeva'ot* — as the verse says, Mine is the silver and Mine is the gold, says the Lord of Hosts. *Amar lahem: kvar natnu lanu* — it has already been given to us, *she'ne'emar: hashamayim shamayim l'Hashem veha'aretz natan livnei adam* — the heavens are God's heavens, but the earth He gave to humanity.

### Relationship vs. Sin

The contrast between *Amon* and *Yehoyakim* on the one hand, and the earlier wicked kings on the other, raises a question worth examining carefully. *Amon* and those before him were doing genuinely terrible things — burning Torah, committing the most severe sexual violations, placing idols in the *Kodesh HaKodashim* — and they were doing it *lehachis*, to spite God. *Yehoyakim*, on the other hand, is in some sense just saying words: why do we need God, we have gold. This seems far less dramatic. Yet the *Gemara* treats *Yehoyakim*'s position as distinctively troubling.

The distinction appears to be this: *lehachis* — doing something to spite God — still presupposes a relationship. Even the most defiant act of spite is an act within a relationship; it acknowledges that the other party exists and can be hurt. *Amon* saying "I am doing this only to anger my Creator" is, paradoxically, still a form of engagement with God. *Yehoyakim* is doing something different: he is attempting to sever the relationship entirely, to declare that God is irrelevant, that we have no need of Him. It is better to be in a fighting relationship than in no relationship at all. The old observation applies: *lehachis mi* — to spite whom? — is the reply of one who has already stepped outside any relationship entirely.

### Yehoyakim Unlisted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַבָּה בַּר מָרִי: מִפְּנֵי מָה לֹא מָנוּ אֶת יְהוֹיָקִים? מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ: "וְיֶתֶר דִּבְרֵי יְהוֹיָקִים וְתֹעֲבֹתָיו אֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה וְהַנִּמְצָא עָלָיו". מַאי "וְהַנִּמְצָא עָלָיו"? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר – חַד אָמַר: שֶׁחָקַק שֵׁם עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה עַל אַמָּתוֹ, וְחַד אָמַר: שֶׁחָקַק שֵׁם שָׁמַיִם עַל אַמָּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava said to Rabba bar Mari: For what** reason **did** the *tanna'im* **not enumerate Jehoiakim** among the kings who have no share in the World-to-Come? One would imagine that he has no share in the World-to-Come, **because it is written concerning him: "And the rest of the acts of Jehoiakim and his abominations that he did, and that which was found on him"** (II Chronicles 36:8). The Gemara explains: **What** is the meaning of the phrase **"and that which was found on him"? Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Elazar** disagree; **one says that he etched the name of idols on his penis** due to his devotion to them, **and one says that he etched the name of Heaven on his penis** in a display of contempt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בִּמְלָכִים לֹא שָׁמַעְתִּי, בְּהֶדְיוֹטוֹת שָׁמַעְתִּי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba bar Mari **said to** Rava: **With regard to** the list of **kings, I did not hear** why Jehoiakim was excluded; but **with regard to** the list of **commoners, I heard** why a certain sinner was excluded.

*Amar lei Rava l'Rabba bar Mari: mipnei ma lo manu et Yehoyakim?* — Why, given everything discussed, is *Yehoyakim* not on the list of kings without a portion in the world to come? The verse says about him: *v'yeter divrei Yehoyakim v'to'avotav asher asa v'hanimtzah alav* — and the rest of the acts of *Yehoyakim* and his abominations which he committed and what was found upon him. *Mai v'hanimtzah alav?* What does "found upon him" mean?

*Rabbi Yochanan veRabbi Elazar, chada amar shechakak shem avodah zarah al amato* — one says he had the name of an idol tattooed on his membrum, expressing how thoroughly his desire for *avodah zarah* had consumed him. *V'chada amar shechakak shem shamayim al amato* — and the other says he had God's name tattooed there, as an act of contempt and desecration. Either way, *Yehoyakim* was extraordinarily wicked.

So why is he not on the list? *Amar lei: bimlachim lo shamati* — I have not heard an explanation for the kings. There are kings who seem as though they ought to be on the list and are not, and the *Gemara* acknowledges this openly. The Mishnah's list of kings who have no portion in the world to come is not exhaustive, and for the omissions among the kings, no satisfying explanation is available. *Behedyotot shamati* — for the list of non-royal figures, there are explanations for why some who might be expected to appear are absent.

## Michah's Kindness

### Michah's Bread

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִפְּנֵי מָה לֹא מָנוּ אֶת מִיכָה? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁפִּתּוֹ מְצוּיָה לְעוֹבְרֵי דְּרָכִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "כׇּל הָעוֹבֵר וְשָׁב אֶל הַלְוִיִּם".
>
> **Translation:** **For what** reason **did** the *tanna'im* **not enumerate Micah** among those with no share in the World-to-Come? After all, he crafted idols that the Jewish people worshipped (see Judges, chapter 17). It is **due to** the fact **that his bread** was **available for passersby, as it is stated: All those who pass by the Levites.** He would provide sustenance to all hosted in his house.

*Michdei maan lo manu et Michah?* — While we are on the question of unexpected omissions from the list, what about *Michah*, who erected an idol and practiced *avodah zarah* in the early period of Israelite history? Why is he not condemned? *Mipnei shepito metzuya l'ovrei drachim* — because his bread was always available to passersby. Even as he was engaged in his idolatrous worship, his home was open and his table was set for travelers. *Shene'emar kol over v'shav el ha'Leviyim* — anyone who came and went among the Levites was welcome. In the context of *Michah*'s *pesel v'avodah zarah*, this hospitality is what saves him from total condemnation.

### Gerev & Shiloh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וְעָבַר בַּיָּם צָרָה וְהִכָּה בַיָּם גַּלִּים". אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: זֶה פִּסְלוֹ שֶׁל מִיכָה. תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: מִגָּרֵב לְשִׁילֹה שְׁלֹשָׁה מִילִין, וְהָיָה עֲשַׁן הַמַּעֲרָכָה וַעֲשַׁן פֶּסֶל מִיכָה מִתְעָרְבִין זֶה בָּזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to that which is written: **"And He shall pass through the sea with affliction and shall strike the waves in the sea"** (Zechariah 10:11), **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This** affliction is a reference to **the idol of Micah,** as Micah passed through the sea during the exodus from Egypt. **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Natan says:** The distance **from Gerav,** where Micah resided, **to Shiloh,** where the Tabernacle was at that time, **was three *mil*, and the smoke from the arrangement** of wood on the altar in Shiloh **and the smoke** from the worship **of the idol of Micah would intermingle with each other.**

The *Gemara* offers a *drasha* on the verse *v'avar bayam tzarah v'hikah bayam galim* — he passed through the ocean and smote its waves. *Amar Rabbi Yochanan: zeh peslo shel Michah* — this refers to the idol of *Michah*, which was carried through the splitting sea when *Michah* left Egypt. According to this tradition, *Michah* was among those saved from the Egyptian slave labor — some say he had been built into the buildings the slaves were constructing — and he brought his idol with him through the *Yam Suf*.

*Tanya Rabbi Natan omer: miGerev l'Shiloh, gimel milin* — from *Gerev*, the location of *Michah*'s idolatrous sanctuary, to *Shiloh*, where the *Mishkan* stood, was only three *mil* — approximately three miles. *V'hayah ashan hama'aracha v'ashan pesel Michah mitarvim zeh b'zeh* — the smoke of the offerings on the *mizbe'ach* of the *Mishkan* and the smoke of *Michah*'s idol would mingle together. The proximity was not incidental; it was a direct affront, a competing worship placed within sight and smell of God's own dwelling.

### God's Mercy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּקְּשׁוּ מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת לְדוֹחְפוֹ. אָמַר לָהֶן הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: הַנִּיחוּ לוֹ, שֶׁפִּתּוֹ מְצוּיָה לְעוֹבְרֵי דְּרָכִים. וְעַל דָּבָר זֶה נֶעְנְשׁוּ אַנְשֵׁי פִּלֶגֶשׁ בַּגִּבְעָה. אָמַר לָהֶן הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: בִּכְבוֹדִי לֹא מְחִיתֶם, עַל כְּבוֹדוֹ שֶׁל בָּשָׂר וָדָם מְחִיתֶם!
>
> **Translation:** **The ministering angels sought to dismiss him** from the world. **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to them: Leave him, as his bread is available for travelers. And it is for this matter,** the sin of Micah's idol, that **the people** involved in the incident of **the concubine in Gibeah were punished.** The tribes of Israel waged war with the tribe of Benjamin and forty thousand of them were killed. **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to them: You did not protest for My honor** and took no action to destroy Micah's idol, but **you protested for the honor of** one of **flesh and blood** who was killed? Therefore, the other tribes were initially unsuccessful, and many of their soldiers were killed.

*Bikshu malachei hasharet l'dochfo* — the ministering angels wanted to push *Michah* away, to destroy him entirely. *Amar lahem HaKadosh Baruch Hu: hanichu lo, shepito metzuya l'ovrei drachim* — God said: leave him, because his bread is available to passersby. This is a remarkable statement. The same *talmidei chachamim* who were great scholars did not find their scholarship counterbalancing their sins. Yet *Michah*'s *hachnasat orchim* — his hospitality to travelers — tips the scales in his favor sufficiently that even the angels' desire to destroy him is overridden by divine mercy.

*V'al davar zeh ne'enash anshei Pilegesh b'Giva* — and it was because of *Michah*'s sin that the men of *Pilegesh b'Giva* were punished. *Amar lahem HaKadosh Baruch Hu: bichvodi lo mechitem v'al kvodo shel basar vadam mechitem?* — God said to them: You did not protest on behalf of My honor when the idol of *Michah* was erected right in your locale, and yet you came forward to defend the honor of a human being in the matter of the *pilegesh b'Giva*? That failure to protest against *Michah*'s idol — which stood so close to the *Mishkan* that their smokes mingled — is what ultimately brought punishment upon the men of that generation. The lesson parallels what was said of *Tzidkiyahu*: silence in the face of desecration is its own form of culpability.
