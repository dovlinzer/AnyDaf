# Bava Batra 168 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Document Writing Rules

### Opening & Overview

Daf 168 finds us in the closing stretch of *Bava Batra*, engaged with the *gemara*'s treatment of a *mishna* concerning the writing of various legal documents: loan documents, *shovarim* (receipts), *ketubot*, *shtarei erusin*, and related instruments. The underlying questions are structural: for whose benefit may a document be written in the absence of the other party, and who bears the cost of the *sofer*? Only the party who is obligating himself may have a document written without the other party present — not the reverse. These principles will carry through the entire daf.

### Shovar Case

The *gemara* opens mid-discussion concerning the writing of a *shovar* — a receipt acknowledging payment of a *ketubah*. The case before us: there was a certain *shovar* bearing the signature of Rabbi Yirmiya bar Abba. A woman declared, *lav ana havai* — this was not me, this was not my transaction. She was suggesting that her husband had arranged for some other woman to impersonate her and thereby obtain a fraudulent receipt attesting that her *ketubah* had been paid. Rabbi Yirmiya bar Abba responded, *ivra at havait* — no, in truth it was you. I know it was you, and you will not deceive me.

Abaye then contextualizes this: even though the *gemara* concluded at the end of yesterday's discussion that *rabanan* do not typically scrutinize women closely — *lav orcha lemadak* — and that Rabbi Yirmiya bar Abba could therefore have made an initial error of identification, here he is asserting positively that it was in fact this woman.

### Tosafot on Testimony

*Tosafot* raises a pointed question. We established yesterday that when Rabbi Yirmiya bar Abba *initially* thought a woman was not the one named in the document, he could retract his testimony — because scholars do not ordinarily look at women closely, such initial uncertainty is understandable. *Tosafot* frames the logical implication: *kal va-chomer*, if one can retract testimony that contradicts the *shtar*, then surely when a witness affirms precisely what the *shtar* says — *zu hi* — he is believed, even though his ordinary practice is not to look at women carefully, *af al pi she-ein darko lehistakel ba-hen*. That is exactly what is written in the document; what else would one think?

*Tosafot* resolves the question as follows: *kivan de-dikdak*, once Rabbi Yirmiya bar Abba stated definitively that it was her, *eino chozer umaggid* — he cannot subsequently claim he made a mistake. The point of the *gemara*'s discussion here is not to question his credibility but to establish that having made a firm identification, he is bound by it and may not retract.

## Talmidim & Document Costs

### Marriage Advice

Since the *gemara* has been discussing the fact that *talmidei chachamim* do not closely observe women, Abaye draws a practical lesson: *hai tzurba merabanan de-azal lekadesh iteta* — a Torah scholar who goes to betroth a woman should bring along an *am ha-aretz*, someone more familiar with the ordinary social world, *nidbar am ha-aretz bahadeih*. The concern is *dilma machlefu lah mineih* — perhaps they will swap the woman on him. Since the *talmid chacham* is not paying close attention, he may arrive at the *chuppah* and not recognize that a different woman has been substituted. The *am ha-aretz* will notice.

This is a striking sociological observation — one that inevitably evokes the *parshiyot* we are engaged with at this time of year, the story of Rachel and Leah, and the question of how such a substitution becomes possible at all. The *gemara* does not dwell on this, but the implication is significant.

### Get Costs

The *mishna* states that *ha-ba'al nosein sachar* — the husband pays the *sofer* to write the *get*. The reason is derived from the verse *ve-chasav ve-natan*: he must write it and give it. Since the *sofer* is acting as the husband's *shaliach*, his agent, the husband is naturally the one who pays. The one paying determines for whom the work is being done.

### Wife Pays Get

The *gemara* then asks: *veidach*, why does later practice diverge from this? By the time of the *gemara*, it had become standard for the wife to pay for the *get*. The explanation: *shadyu rabbanan a-isha ki heichi de-lo lishahai* — the Rabbis placed the financial burden on the wife so that the husband would have no monetary pretext to delay. This speaks directly to the tragedy of *agunah* situations: if the husband lacks funds to pay the *sofer* and is not eagerly seeking the divorce in the first place, the wife may be left waiting indefinitely. By having the wife absorb the cost, the impediment is removed.

What remains unresolved is how this squares with the principle that the *sofer* acts as the husband's agent. One might suggest it operates as a form of *hefker beis din hefker* — the rabbinic authority effectively assigns the payment in a way that is treated as though the husband is paying — but this is not entirely clear from the *gemara*'s language.

## Payment Responsibility

### Loan Docs

The *mishna* states: *kotvin shtar le-loveh af pi she-ein malveh immo* — you may write a loan document for the borrower even without the lender present, since the document only obligates the borrower himself. He may carry it and present it to the lender when ready. The *mishna* further states *ve-ha-loveh noten sechar* — the borrower pays the *sofer*. The *gemara* responds *pshita* — is this not obvious? The borrower is receiving a loan without paying interest; he is clearly the beneficiary of the transaction and should bear the cost.

### Iska Case

The *gemara* then says: *tzricha be-iska* — the ruling is actually needed for the case of an *iska*, a business investment arrangement that functions as half-loan and half-*pikadon*. Both parties benefit from the *iska*: the investor provides capital, the recipient invests it and shares the profit. One might have thought that since both benefit, both should share the cost. Nevertheless, since the recipient is the party without money who is more fundamentally in need of the arrangement, he is considered the primary beneficiary and pays the *sofer*'s fee — even though he currently lacks funds.

### Sale Docs

Similarly, *kotvin shtar le-mocher af pi she-ein lokeiach immo* — you may write a sale document for the seller without the buyer being present, since that document can only work against the seller's interests. The *mishna* states *ve-ha-lokeiach noten sechar* — the buyer pays. Again the *gemara* says *pshita*: who is benefiting? The *gemara* operates under the assumption that selling one's property — particularly ancestral land — is generally an unfortunate necessity rather than a commercial act. The buyer is therefore always the beneficiary.

### Field Quality

The *gemara* clarifies: *lo tzricha be-mocher sadehu mipnei ra'assa* — the ruling is needed for the case where someone is selling his field because it is of poor quality, a hardscrabble plot he is fortunate to unload. Here one might argue that the seller is the beneficiary, since he is rid of a liability. Nevertheless, the buyer still pays. The reasoning is either that in all cases of land acquisition the buyer is in the better position, or it reflects a principle of *lo plug* — once the rule has been established that the buyer pays for sale documents, we do not create distinctions between cases.

### Betrothal Docs

Regarding *shtarei erusin* and *nissuin*: *ein kotvin shtarei erusin* — you do not write betrothal documents or marriage contracts except when both parties are present, because it is a bilateral arrangement requiring mutual agreement. The *mishna* states *de-chassan noten sechar* — the groom pays. The *gemara* again says *pshita*. In a patriarchal framework, even though the groom takes on obligations of support, he is receiving a wife and is therefore considered the primary beneficiary. The *gemara* takes this so thoroughly for granted that it requires no elaboration.

The *gemara* then sharpens the ruling: *lo tzericha*, the ruling is needed even in the case of a *tzurba merabanan*, a Torah scholar, where *de-nichla ley le-chamuya le-kurvey* — the father-in-law actively desires him as a relative and derives significant social benefit from the match. Even so, the framing remains that the groom acquires the wife and he pays.

This passage invites substantial sociological reflection about how the *gemara* frames the question of who benefits in a marriage transaction — but the *gemara* itself moves on without dwelling on it.

### Sharecropping

*Ein kotvin shtarei arisut ve-kablanut* — one does not write sharecropping or rental agreements except with both parties present, and *ve-ha-mikabel noten sechar* — the one who receives the field to work it pays the *sofer*. That person is obtaining an opportunity to earn a livelihood; the field's owner merely has property. The *gemara* says *pshita* again, and notes it is needed for *bora* — a field requiring fallowing or heavy investment to be usable — where one might think the recipient is not clearly the beneficiary. Even so, he pays.

## Clarification Documents

### Berurin Definition

*Ein kotvin shtarei berurin ela mi-da'at shneihem* — one writes *shtarei berurin* only with the knowledge and consent of both parties. The *gemara* asks: *mai shtarei berurin* — what are these clarification documents? Two explanations are offered. In Babylonia it was explained as *shtarei ta'anata* — documents recording the claims and counterclaims of litigants that have been presented before the judges, a formal record of the positions each side has stated in court. Rabbi Yirmiya bar Abba offers a different explanation: *ze borer lo echad ve-ze borer lo echad* — it refers to the process by which each litigant selects one of the judges who will adjudicate his case, as we will see elaborated in *Sanhedrin*. The two chosen judges then select a third. Once those selections have been made, they must be recorded and formalized. Either way, both parties pay equally since they are symmetric sides of the litigation.

### Copy Dispute

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* rules that you write two copies of the *shtar berurin* — *l'ze bifnei atzmo u-l'ze bifnei atzmo* — each litigant receives his own copy rather than sharing a single document.

### Midat Sodom

The *gemara* asks whether this dispute turns on the principle of *chofin al midat Sodom* — whether we compel a person not to adopt the posture of *midat Sodom*. *Midat Sodom* is the attitude of *sheli sheli ve-shelcha shelcha* — I keep mine and you keep yours, with no concession to shared arrangement. As *Pirkei Avot* characterizes it, this is at best a middling quality and at its worst the defining sin of Sodom. The *Rashbam* frames the case as follows: one litigant says, you keep your copy of the *shtar*, I want my own copy. I am not willing to pay half to share one document — I want one for myself. Is this *midat Sodom*, an insistence on keeping things separate at needless cost to both parties? The *chachamim* would say yes — it is wasteful to produce two documents when one would serve both, and we do not indulge such stubbornness.

### Privacy Concern

The *gemara* rejects this framing, however. *Lo* — the debate is not about whether we tolerate *midat Sodom*. Rather, *d'kulei alma kofin* — all would agree we do not simply accommodate someone who insists on separate arrangements when a shared one is practically adequate. The real issue, according to the *gemara*, is that there is a legitimate reason why sharing a document is not practically adequate here. That is the basis of *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*'s position: *lo nicha li de-tehevei zechutach gabei zechuti* — I do not want my rights, my claims, sitting alongside your rights in your possession. *De-damit alai ke-ari arava* — you are like a lion lying in wait. If you hold the record of my claims while litigation is still ongoing, you will have the opportunity to examine my arguments and prepare your counterattack. I do not want you holding a court record that contains what I have said.

### Separate Copies

The solution, on this reading, is that each litigant receives only the record of his own claims — you see your claims, I see mine. An alternative reading in the *gemara* is that the concern is not about examining the opponent's claims but simply about physical control: even if I could theoretically request access to the shared document, the fact that you hold it gives you unequal practical control. In either case, *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*'s ruling — that separate copies protect each party against the other's potential for deception — is vindicated.

## Asmachta

### Mishna Setup

The next *mishna*: *mi she-para mikzat chovo ve-hishlish et shtaro* — someone who has repaid a portion of a debt and places the original *shtar* in the hands of a trusted third party. The situation: Reuven owes a thousand dollars, pays two hundred and fifty, and cannot yet pay the balance. He does not have a receipt for the partial payment, and the *sofer* is unavailable. But he also does not want the creditor holding a *shtar* stating he owes the full thousand. They therefore agree to deposit the *shtar* with a mutually trusted intermediary.

### Penalty Clause

The terms of the deposit: *ve-im lo natati lecha mikan ve-ad yom ploni ten lo shtaro* — if by a specified date I have not paid you the remaining balance, you are empowered to return the *shtar* to the creditor, and I will owe the full thousand. This arrangement substitutes for a formal receipt: the third party knows a partial payment was made, and the penalty for non-payment of the balance is the restoration of the full debt obligation.

### Yosi v. Yehuda

When the time arrives and the borrower has not paid: *Rabbi Yossi omer yiten* — Rabbi Yossi says the third party should hand over the document as agreed, and the full debt is enforceable. *Rabbi Yehuda omer lo yiten* — Rabbi Yehuda says no. That agreement does not bind the third party to hand over the document. This is the paradigm case of *asmachta*.

### Asmachta Defined

*Be-mai kamiflagi*? The dispute turns on whether *asmachta kanyia* — whether an *asmachta* is legally binding. An *asmachta* is, broadly, a commitment made under the assumption that the triggering condition will never actually arise. The person making the promise is overcommitting — promising something he does not genuinely intend to give, something he does not believe the other party really deserves — precisely because he is so confident the condition will never come about. Because of this, there is no real *gemirat da'at*, no genuine settled intent to transfer. The promise is hollow. *Rabbi Yossi* holds *asmachta kanyia* — such a commitment is nonetheless binding. *Rabbi Yehuda* holds *asmachta lo kanyia* — it is not.

### Rav Nachman Ruling

*Amar Rav Nachman amar Rabbah bar Avuha amar Rav*: the *halacha* follows *Rabbi Yossi* — *asmachta* is binding. It should be noted that *Tosafot*'s *girsa* reads *ein halachah ke-Rabbi Yossi*, the opposite ruling. This ambiguity in the transmitted text makes the ultimate *psak* genuinely uncertain.

### Rabbi Ami

When cases of *asmachta* came before *Rabbi Ami*, he expressed a kind of reluctant constraint: *ve-chi me-achar she-Rabbi Yochanan melamedenu pa'am rishonah u-sheniyah halachah ke-Rabbi Yossi* — since Rabbi Yochanan teaches again and again that the *halacha* follows *Rabbi Yossi*, what can I do? *Ani mah e'eseh* — I am bound by that ruling. People may have hoped he would rule otherwise, but he could not depart from Rabbi Yochanan's repeated and explicit position.

### Final Conclusion

The *gemara* concludes: *ve-ein halachah ke-Rabbi Yossi* — but the *halacha* does not follow *Rabbi Yossi*. *Asmachta* is not *kanyia*. This reversal then opens the extensive discussions in *Tosafot* and the *Rishonim* about whether *asmachta* is categorically never binding or whether certain types of conditional commitments do acquire legal force. That is a major topic in its own right.

## Erased Documents

### Replacing Erased

The next *mishna*: *mi she-nimchak shtar chovo* — someone whose loan document has been erased. This situation arises in practice, including with *ketubot*, where the witnesses' signatures — written with materials that fade — may become illegible over time. A replacement is needed, and critically, it must bear the original date, since the original date establishes the liens from which the holder may collect. A new document dated to the present would forfeit those earlier liens.

### Procedure

The *mishna*'s procedure: *ma'amido la-eidim* — you station witnesses before the document while it is still legible, so they can testify to its contents before the text fades entirely. They then come before *beis din*, and *beis din* — according to most *Rishonim*, as opposed to the *Rashbam* who holds it refers to the witnesses themselves — creates a substitute *shtar* based on that testimony.

The language of the substitute document identifies the original holder, the date, and the original witnesses: *ish ploni ben ploni nimchak shtaro be-yom ploni u-ploni u-ploni eidav*. One might ask: who exactly are these witnesses being named? *Tosafot* explains that the document names all the relevant witnesses — both the original witnesses to the loan and the secondary witnesses who testified before *beis din* about what was written. The full record would read something like: Levi and Yehuda came before us and testified that this person held a loan document of one thousand dollars dated a certain day, with Reuven and Shimon as the original witnesses — and now we as *beis din* are creating this substitute. This is also the framework behind the *ketubah d'irkasa* and *ketubah d'nimchaka* — the formalized versions of substitute *ketubot* used when the original is lost or erased.

### Kiyum Language

The *baraisa* provides the precise language the *beis din* uses to certify the replacement: *be-moshav tlasa havina* — we were convened as a court of three. The *dayanim* identify themselves, and then record that *ploni ben ploni hotzi shtar machuk le-faneinu be-yom ploni*, so-and-so brought an erased promissory note before us on this date, with such-and-such witnesses. The *beis din* then records whether it observed the document while still legible, or whether secondary witnesses testified to its content.

### Evidence Standard

The *baraisa* continues: *ve-nimtzas eidusam mechuvenes* — and their testimony was found to be consistent and reliable. In that case, *goveh ve-eino tzarich le-havi raya* — the holder may collect on the basis of the substitute document and need bring no further proof. The substitute establishes the original date and the original liens. If, however, the content of the original *shtar* cannot be adequately established — the witnesses' accounts are insufficient or the original text is entirely illegible — then *tzarich le-havi raya*, further proof is required and the substitute alone will not suffice for collection.

### Torn Documents

*Nikra pasul* — if a loan document or *ketubah* is torn, it is invalid. Tearing may indicate payment: the creditor may have torn the document upon receiving payment, as was the customary sign of discharge. One cannot simply present a torn document and demand a replacement, since the tearing itself is evidence that the debt was settled. However, *nitkarei kasher* — if the document was torn on its own, as opposed to clearly ripped by human hands, it remains valid and a replacement may be written. Similarly, *nimchak o nishtatash* — if erased or smudged — *im shemo nikar kasher*: if the content remains discernible, even if the ink has faded, the document is valid.

### Beis Din Tearing

The *gemara* asks: *heichi dami nikra heichi dami nitkarei* — what precisely distinguishes a humanly torn document from one torn on its own? *Amar Rav Yehuda*: *nikra kera shel beis din* — the invalidating tear is specifically the tear administered by a *beis din* to mark a document as paid and discharged. *Nitkarei kera she-eino beis din* — the tear that does not invalidate is one that does not follow the pattern of a *beis din* tear.

How does *beis din* tear a *shtar*? *Rav Yehuda* says: *makom eidim u-makom ha-zman u-makom ha-torof* — they tear through the critical sections: the witnesses' signatures, the date, and the *torof*, the substantive body of the document, the actual terms and amounts. *Abaye* says: *shesi va-erev* — they make a crisscross cut across the document. This crisscross tearing is recognizable as an intentional act of invalidation. It is also the practice today when a *ketubah* has been delivered and received — the officiants make a crisscross cut to mark it as no longer operative as an independent legal instrument.

## Pumbedisa Story

### Coercion Context

A story is now related. *Hanu arvaei* — there were certain Arabs who came to Pumbedisa, *de-havu ka-antsi a-arata de-inshei* — who were coercing people and forcing them to hand over their deeds to their land. Landowners, anticipating that they might soon be compelled to surrender their title documents under duress, came to Abaye before this occurred. They presented their deeds to establish their legitimate ownership and made a request: *ve-lichtav lan mar shtar achrina alei* — could the master write them a duplicate deed? *De-im mintsis chad netinan chad biyadan* — that if they are forced to hand over one copy under coercion, they will retain the other and be able to prove their ownership.

This is not an unreasonable request on its face. But the potential for abuse is obvious: with two valid deeds in hand, a person could sell the land, pocket the proceeds, and then produce the second deed to claim ownership again. Or in cases involving *achrayus*, he could collect reimbursement twice if the land were seized by a creditor. Abaye was therefore unwilling to comply.

### Abaye Refuses

*Amar lehu*: *mai avid lachon* — what can I do for you? I cannot help you. *De-amar Rav Safra: ein kotvin shnei shtaros al sada achas* — Rav Safra ruled that one may not write two deeds on a single field. *Dilma teref ve-hadar teref* — the fear is that the holder will collect and then collect again.

### Double Collection

The *gemara* elaborates the mechanism of abuse: suppose Reuven sold a field to a buyer with an *achrayus* clause — if a creditor of Reuven's subsequently seizes the field to collect a prior debt, the buyer is entitled to be reimbursed by Reuven. Now suppose the buyer has two copies of the deed. The creditor seizes the field; the buyer collects reimbursement from Reuven's other assets — say, from another purchaser of Reuven's land. That purchaser demands the deed as evidence that the buyer has received his compensation. The buyer hands over one copy. But he still holds the second copy. He then approaches yet another purchaser of Reuven's land and presents the second deed as though he has never been reimbursed, and collects a second time. The prohibition against two deeds on one field exists precisely to prevent this.

### Invalid Document

The landowners continued to pressure Abaye. *Hava katardy lay* — they were pressing him. Abaye then said to his scribe: *zil ktov lahu hu al ha-machak ve-eidim al ha-niyar de-pasul* — go write them a document, but make it an invalid one. Write the deed itself on *klaf* that has been previously erased — *al ha-machak* — and have the witnesses sign on clean, unerased *klaf* at the bottom. As we have already learned, this configuration is invalid: when the body of the document is on erased parchment, we are suspicious that the original text was scraped away and new content substituted. The witnesses are signing something whose top may have been altered.

This solution is deeply troubling. Abaye was directing his scribe to produce a document that the recipients would believe to be valid but that was in fact legally worthless. One might raise serious concerns about *zilusa de-bei dina* — the dignity and integrity of the court — in authorizing such a deception. The *gemara* does not offer a defense of this conduct, and one is left to surmise that Abaye found himself in an impossible position under sustained pressure, and chose this as the least harmful path available.

### Rav Aha Objection

*Amar lay Rav Aha bar Minyumi le-Abaye*: there is a problem with this plan. He understood Abaye's instruction to mean that a valid existing *shtar* would be erased and the new deed written on top of that erased surface. He pointed to the *mishna*: *nimchak o nishtatash — im shemo nikar kasher* — if a document was erased or smudged but the original lettering remains visible, even faintly, the document is still valid. If the original *shtar* beneath is still partially legible, the document the people are receiving might inadvertently be valid — not because of the new text written on top, which is on *machak*, but because the original text beneath is still operative. They would then be holding a valid document after all.

### Abaye Clarifies

Abaye replied: *mi kamina shtar ma'alya? Aleph-beis be-alma kamina* — I never said to erase an actual valid *shtar*. What I said was: write out nonsense, *aleph-beis gimel dalet*, meaningless letters, and then erase that. Then write the deed on top of the erased nonsense, with the witnesses on the clean parchment below. There is no underlying valid document whose lingering legibility could rescue the result. The entire thing — deed on erased nonsense — is invalid, and that is exactly what Abaye intended.

The troubling nature of this entire episode remains. Abaye was knowingly issuing an invalid document to deceive people who were being victimized by coercive forces, and the *gemara* records it without editorial comment.

## Lost Documents

### Lost Note Rule

The *gemara* now turns to a *baraisa*: *harei she-ba ve-amar avad shtar hovi* — someone who comes and says he has lost his promissory note. *Af al pi she-amru eidim anu kasavnu ve-hasamnu ve-nasatnu lo* — even if the original witnesses come forward and say, we wrote it, we signed it, we gave it to him — *ein kotvin lo et ha-shtar* — you do not write a replacement *shtar*. How does this differ from the *mishna*'s case where witnesses testify to the content of an erased document and a substitute is written? The distinction is critical: in the *mishna*'s case, the original document is present, visible, and can be destroyed before the substitute is issued. Here, the original document is nowhere to be found. The claimant says he lost it — but perhaps he did not. Perhaps he will find it. If a replacement is issued and he subsequently produces the original, he will be able to collect the same debt twice.

### Double Collection Risk

This is the same concern that animated the prohibition against two deeds on one field: *dilma teref ve-hadar teref* — we fear he will collect once on the replacement and again when the original surfaces. The asymmetry between the erased-document case and the lost-document case is therefore not about evidentiary sufficiency — the witnesses in both cases may be equally credible — but about the physical control of the original instrument.

### Sale Exception

*Bamei devarim amurim* — when does this prohibition apply? *Be-shtarei halva'ah* — with promissory notes, for the reason just given. *Aval shtarei mekach u-mimkar kotvin chutz min ha-achrayus she-bo* — but with bills of sale, one may write a replacement, provided the replacement does not contain an *achrayus* clause. The absence of *achrayus* means the holder cannot use the document to collect reimbursement from others if the field is seized. Since the double-collection mechanism depends on *achrayus*, removing it from the replacement document eliminates the practical risk.

This seems, at first glance, to conflict with the Pumbedisa story, where Abaye refused to write a duplicate deed even for parties acting in evident good faith. One might ask: why not simply issue them a second deed without *achrayus*? The *gemara* will need to address this tension — and indeed, the two cases may not be as parallel as they appear, since the *baraisa* is discussing a lost document that may yet resurface, while the Pumbedisa case involves a deliberate duplication.

### Rabban Shimon

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* takes a stricter position: *af be-shtarei mekach u-mimkar ein kotvin* — even for bills of sale, no replacement document is written. His reasoning emerges from his broader view of how documents function in property transactions.

### Document Return

*Ve-chein haya Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel omer*: if someone gave a gift to another and the recipient returned the document to the giver, *chazra matanaso* — the gift is reversed. Returning the deed reverses the transaction. The *chachamim* disagree: *matanaso kayemet* — the return of the document does not undo the transfer.

This disagreement illuminates Rabban Shimon's stricter stance on replacement documents. In his view, the document is not merely evidence of a transaction — it has a constitutive role. If a seller claims he no longer has his *shtar* but in fact he returned it to the original seller (perhaps because the sale was reversed and he received payment for the reversion), a replacement *shtar* would allow him to claim the sale was never undone — that he lost the document, not that he gave it back. Rabban Shimon therefore prohibits replacement sale documents entirely, since their potential for enabling precisely this deception is too great. This will be unpacked further in the continuation of the discussion.
