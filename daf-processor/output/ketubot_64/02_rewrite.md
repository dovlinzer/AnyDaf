# Ketubot 64 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Overview & Setup

### Opening

We begin near the bottom of 64a, picking up five lines from the end at the words *ad masai hu pocheit* — "until when does he reduce it."

### Marriage Troubles Review

The preceding *sugya* dealt with the situation in which a marriage has broken down and one of the partners is failing to fulfill his or her obligations. The discussion centered on whether the goal is to achieve compliance over time or to move directly toward a *get*, and what is done in the interim. Two significant issues emerged from that discussion. First, whether the husband can actually be compelled to go out and find work — he has an obligation to pay the bills, but does the principle of *v'ana aflach* mean he can be forced to seek employment? Second, and more consequential for *agunah* concerns, when the wife declares the marriage intolerable, does the court move immediately to compel a *get*, or does the woman at once forfeit her *ketubbah*? The *sugya* also touched on *Tosafot*'s discussion of whether a husband who refuses *chalitzah* can in some fashion be compelled or induced to grant it.

### Financial Penalties

The *Mishnah* established financial penalties for marital defiance in either direction. When the wife is a *moredes* — refusing to fulfill her obligations — the *ketubbah* is reduced by seven *dinarin* per week. When the husband is a *mored*, the *ketubbah* is increased by three *dinarin* per week for the wife's benefit. A parallel opinion substitutes *tarpeikin* for *dinarin* in both amounts.

## Currency & Math

### Dinar/Zuz/Sela

To understand these figures, one must first establish the currency equivalencies. A *dinar* equals a *zuz*, and four *zuz* equal a *sela*. The significance of the *sela* here is that the *Torah*'s *mohar* of fifty *shekel* equals 200 *zuz* — which is why the base *ketubbah* for a *betulah* is 200 *zuz*.

### Depletion Calculations

If the *ketubbah* stands at 200 *zuz* and is reduced at seven *zuz* per week, it will be exhausted in roughly 28 weeks. If instead it is increased for the husband at three *zuz* per week, that three-*zuz* increment would take approximately 67 weeks to accumulate an equivalent sum — though of course a *ketubbah* typically includes *tosefet*, a dowry, and other additions beyond the base 200.

The *Gemara* then asks: *mai tarpeikin* — what are *tarpeikin*? Rav Sheshet answers: an *astira*, which is *palga d'zuza* — half a *zuz*. This is confirmed by a *baraita*: Rabbi Yehudah says three *tarpeikin* equal nine *ma'in*, which works out to a *ma'ah* and a half per day (excluding Shabbat). The practical consequence is significant: seven *tarpeikin* per week equals three and a half *zuz*, so the second opinion in the *Mishnah* represents a rate half as severe as the first. At three and a half *zuz* per week, the base 200-*zuz ketubbah* would be depleted in roughly 56 weeks — twice as long as under the first opinion.

## Tarpeikin & Meals

### Tarpeikin Definition

To summarize the coinage: a *tarpeikin* is an *astira*, which equals half a *zuz*.

### Rabbi Yehudah Opinion

Rabbi Yehudah specifies that three *tarpeikin* equal nine *ma'in*, yielding a daily rate of a *ma'ah* and a half when Shabbat is excluded from the count.

### Penalty Comparison

Setting aside the *tarpeikin*-versus-*zuz* question, the structural differences between the two penalties can be stated clearly. For the *moredes* — the wife who refuses her obligations — the *ketubbah* is reduced by one unit per day, including Shabbat, for a total of seven per week. For the *mored* — the husband who refuses his — the *ketubbah* is increased by only half a unit per day, and Shabbat is not counted, yielding three units per week. These two asymmetries — full versus half, and Shabbat included versus excluded — are the focus of the *Gemara*'s next inquiry.

## Mored vs Moredes

### Half vs Full Fine

Rabbi Chiyya bar Yosef poses the question to Shmuel: *mah bein mored l'moredes* — why is there a difference between the *mored* and the *moredes*? Why is her penalty double his, and why is Shabbat counted against her but not against him?

### Shabbat Fining Logic

The *Gemara* addresses the Shabbat question first. When the wife is the defiant party and the *ketubbah* is being reduced, the fact that Shabbat is included does not create an appearance of payment for Shabbat labor — *lo michzi k'schar Shabbat* — because one is merely reducing a debt, not paying out wages. When the husband is the defiant party and the *ketubbah* is being increased, however, including Shabbat in the count would mean that the wife is effectively earning half a *zuz* on Shabbat — *michzi k'schar Shabbat* — which does create that problematic appearance. Hence Shabbat is excluded from his calculation.

A question was raised about *Yom Tov*: if there is concern about the appearance of *schar Shabbat*, why is *Yom Tov* not similarly excluded? *Tosafot* raises the related question of *havla'ah* — the practice of including Shabbat payment within a broader weekly wage so that it is not singled out — and whether that principle might apply here. The matter is left unresolved.

### Prostitute Market Evidence

On the question of why the wife's penalty is double the husband's, Shmuel tells Rabbi Chiyya bar Yosef: *tzei u'lemad mi-shuk shel zonos* — go and learn from the marketplace of prostitutes, *mi socher es mi* — who hires whom? It is men who hire women. The market for female prostitution is vastly larger than that for male prostitution. This is taken as empirical evidence that men experience greater sexual desire, and therefore the husband is the more aggrieved party when conjugal relations are withheld. Since the *mored* and *moredes* framework is understood primarily in terms of withholding sexual relations, the wife who withholds is penalized more heavily, because she is inflicting greater harm.

### Biological Arguments

A second argument follows: *davar acher* — *zeh yitzro mi-bachutz v'zeh yitzrah mi-bifnim* — one's genitalia is external, the other's internal. Rashi explains this in two ways. Symbolically, it reflects who harbors the greater innate desire. More concretely, *kishuyo nikar u-misganeh*: the husband's arousal is visible and thus more conspicuous in its frustration, whereas the wife's is not. Together, these two arguments — the evidence from the marketplace and the biological argument — establish that the husband is regarded as the more injured party when sexual relations are denied, which is why the penalty for the *moredes* is double that of the *mored*.

It bears noting that even though the husband has a positive obligation of *onah*, he is nonetheless regarded as the party more harmed by deprivation. The overall asymmetry — double penalty, plus Shabbat — compounds a broader imbalance already present in the framework, since the husband retains the unilateral power to initiate a *get*, which the wife does not. As for the timeline: whether one follows the seven-*zuz* or seven-*tarpeikin* rate, the process of depleting the base *ketubbah* runs between roughly half a year and a full year — far longer than the more rapid resolution the *Gemara* elsewhere envisions, where one wants to move quickly toward the *get* rather than drag out the proceedings.

## Food Minimums

### Hamshrah Setup

The *Mishnah* now turns to the counterpart of the *moredes* and *mored* discussion: the husband's affirmative obligation to support his wife. The particular case treated here is one of *hamshrah* — the husband is providing for his wife through an intermediary, meaning they are not sharing a domicile. Whether he is away on a business trip, or they are separated pending resolution of their situation, or for whatever other reason, she is not in his house and he is having someone pay her food costs. The *Mishnah* therefore specifies the minimum she must receive.

### Grain Allowance

He may not give her less than two *kav* of wheat per week, or, if she prefers barley, four *kav* of barley. The *kav* is a volumetric measurement; barley was considered a less desirable grain, which is why it requires double the quantity.

### Barley Exception

Rabbi Yosi objects: the only authority who said barley is permitted as an alternative was Rabbi Yishmael, who lived near Edom — *she-hayah samuch le-Edom*. The *Gemara* clarifies that Rabbi Yosi's objection is not that barley is never eaten elsewhere, but specifically that the rule requiring double the quantity of barley compared to wheat was particular to Rabbi Yishmael's region. The Edomite barley was of notoriously poor quality — *se'oreihen Adomiyot ra'ot hen* — so twice the wheat measure was required to produce equivalent sustenance. Elsewhere, the ratio between barley and wheat would differ.

### Legumes & Fruits

In addition to the grain, the husband must provide a half-*kav* of *kitnis* — legumes — weekly, along with a half-*log* of oil (a *log* is four *revi'iyot*, so a half-*log* is approximately seven ounces), and a *kav* of *grogeros* — dried figs — or, alternatively, a *maneh* (a weight measure) of pressed dates, *deveilah*. If none of these fruits are locally available, he substitutes an equivalent measure of whatever fruit is at hand.

### Durables

Beyond the weekly consumables, the *Mishnah* enumerates durable goods: a bed, a *mapatz* (mat), and a *machatzeles* (a form of cushion or mat). These items are presumably provided once at the outset rather than weekly. He also gives her a head-covering (*kippah le-roshah*), a belt for her waist (*chagor le-mosneha*), and shoes.

### Clothing Allowance

Shoes are replaced *mi-mo'ed le-mo'ed* — once each *regel*, three times a year. For clothing, he provides fifty *zuz* worth annually. There is, however, an important specification about timing: new clothing — which is heavier and warmer, not yet worn thin — should not be purchased in summer, and worn, thinned clothing should not be given in winter. Rather, the fifty *zuz* of new clothing should be bought in winter. She wears them throughout the cold months and, as they wear out, the thinned garments serve her well in summer. At the end of the year, when new clothing is purchased, she keeps the old worn garments — *ha-shechakim shelah*.

### Pin Money & Meals

She also receives a *ma'ah kesef* each week — a small personal stipend for incidental needs, what we might call pin money. And even when they are living apart, the husband is obligated to eat at least one meal together with his wife every Friday night: *ochelet immo mi-leilei Shabbat le-leilei Shabbat*. This is striking. The law already mandates *onah* — conjugal relations at regular intervals — and here extends the same logic to the shared meal: even when circumstances have separated them, the weekly Friday-night meal together is enforceable. The Friday night Shabbat table functions as a minimum unit of shared domestic life, a requirement of presence and not merely of financial provision.

If the husband fails to give the *ma'ah kesef* stipend, then *ma'aseh yadeha shelah* — whatever work she produces belongs to her rather than to him.

### Ma'aseh Yadayim

The *Mishnah* then asks: *u-mah hi osah lo* — and what work does she do for him? The *Gemara* earlier debated whether *ma'aseh yadayim* refers to the entirety of her domestic labor or only the surplus she earns beyond her basic obligations, *motar ma'aseh yadayim*. The plain sense of the *Mishnah* here, however, seems to single out a specific, quantifiable category of work — the weaving that she can actually earn income from outside the house. This is distinguished from the standard domestic duties (baking, laundry, cooking, etc.) enumerated earlier. Whether the *Gemara*'s two opinions on this question fully account for this distinction is not spelled out there; nonetheless, the *Mishnah*'s framing points toward the income-generating weaving as the primary content of *ma'aseh yadayim* in the strict sense.

### Weaving Amounts

The specific amounts are given by region. She must produce the equivalent of five *sla'im* of warp — *sheti* — as measured in Judah, which equals ten *sla'im* by Galilean weights (since Galilean weights were half the value of Judean ones). Alternatively, she must produce ten *sla'im* of woof — *erev* — by Judean measure, which equals twenty *sla'im* in Galilee. The woof is the easier of the two to prepare, which is why its required quantity is double.

### Nursing Adjustments

If she is nursing, her required work output is reduced — *pochtin lah mi-ma'aseh yadeha* — both because nursing itself constitutes significant labor and, more importantly, because excessive physical exertion could compromise her capacity to nurse well. Correspondingly, her food allowance is increased — *mosifin lah al mezonoteha* — since she is eating for two.

### Status Adjustments

All of these minimums apply specifically to the poor — *ba-ani she-be-Yisrael*. If the husband is of higher social standing, then *hakol le-fi kvodo* — everything is calibrated to his status. The minimums are a floor, not a universal standard.

## Meal Measures

### Eruv Techumin Reference

The *Gemara* now questions which Tanna authored this *Mishnah*, and concludes it can be neither Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka nor Rabbi Shimon. The basis for this analysis is a *Mishnah* in *eruv techumin*: the minimum *eruv* contribution is the amount of food constituting two meals — *mazon shtei se'udot* — per person.

### Meir vs Yehudah

Rabbi Meir says the reference meal is a weekday meal, not a Shabbat meal; Rabbi Yehudah says it is the Shabbat meal, not the weekday meal. The *Gemara* notes that each is being lenient — *zeh ve-zeh mitkavnim le-hakel* — but in opposite directions. Rabbi Meir holds that one eats more bread on Shabbat, so the weekday meal represents a smaller bread portion. Rabbi Yehudah holds that on Shabbat one eats more varied dishes and consequently less bread, so the Shabbat bread portion is smaller. Both are defining two meals by the smaller of the two bread quantities.

### R. Yochanan b. Broka

Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka defines the size of a two-meal loaf as the bread purchased for a *pundiyon* when four *se'ah* of grain are sold for a *sela*. Working through the arithmetic: a *sela* equals 48 *pundiyon*, and a *se'ah* equals six *kav*, so four *se'ah* equal 24 *kav*. This means 24 *kav* cost 48 *pundiyon*, which is one *kav* per two *pundiyon*, or half a *kav* per *pundiyon*. A loaf costing one *pundiyon* is therefore made from half a *kav* of wheat, and that loaf constitutes two meals.

### Rabbi Shimon

Rabbi Shimon takes a different measure: two-thirds of a loaf — *shtei yadot be-kikar* — when three loaves are made from one *kav*. If three loaves come from one *kav*, each loaf represents a third of a *kav*. Two-thirds of a loaf is therefore two-thirds of one-third of a *kav*, which equals two-ninths of a *kav* for two meals. This is considerably less than Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka's half-*kav*.

### Achilat Pras

Half of a two-meal loaf — that is, one meal's worth of bread — is called a *pras*, from the Aramaic for "half." Hence *kedei achilat pras* denotes the amount of time required to eat one meal's worth of bread, i.e., the time it takes to eat half a two-meal loaf. This becomes the standard measurement in a much broader range of halachic contexts. The *Gemara* notes related applications: *chatzi ha-pras* is the amount one must remain in a house afflicted with *tzara'at* to contract *tum'ah*; half of that is the amount of *tamei* food whose consumption renders one *tamei*; and half again — one-eighth of the full loaf — is *lekabbel tum'at ochlin*, the minimum food quantity susceptible to *tum'ah*, which Rashi identifies as a *beitzah*. These applications are noted but not pursued at length here.

## Two Kav Calculation

### Eight vs Eighteen

The *Gemara* now applies these two opinions to the *Mishnah*'s allowance of two *kav* of wheat. According to Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, half a *kav* yields two meals, so two *kav* yield eight meals. According to Rabbi Shimon, two-ninths of a *kav* yield two meals, meaning one *kav* yields nine meals, and two *kav* yield eighteen meals.

### Weekly Meal Mismatch

Neither figure matches the expected weekly meal count. A week contains fourteen meals — two per day, seven days — or fifteen if one counts *seudah shlishit* on Shabbat. Eight is far too few; eighteen is too many. This is the *Gemara*'s problem.

### Baker's Profit

Rav Chisda resolves the discrepancy for Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka's position by pointing out that the latter was not specifying the actual flour content of the loaf but rather its market price. The storekeeper-baker does not convert the entire purchase price into flour; he keeps a portion as profit. Rav Chisda says to subtract a third for the storekeeper — *tzei me-hen shelish la-chanvani* — meaning that only two-thirds of the money paid goes toward the wheat itself, while the baker pockets a third. Conversely, if one starts with the raw wheat — as the *Mishnah* does when it gives the wife two *kav* — one must add back the profit margin to calculate how many meals the raw grain represents. Adding a third to the eight meals derived from the market-price calculation yields twelve meals, not eight.

### Adjusted Totals

Twelve meals is still not fourteen. The *Gemara* resolves this by noting that the *Mishnah* specifies the wife eats one meal together with her husband on Friday night — *ochelet immo la-leil Shabbat* — bringing the total to thirteen. The *Gemara* then reconsiders: some interpret *achila* in the *Mishnah* not as literal eating but as a euphemism for *tashmish*, conjugal relations. If so, that Friday night provision adds no meal, and even with the literal interpretation one only reaches thirteen. The *Gemara* therefore adopts Rav Chisda's second formulation: the baker's profit is not a third but a half. Half of the purchase price goes to the wheat, half is the baker's profit. Applying the reverse: two *kav* of raw wheat, with a 100% markup factored in, yields sixteen meals. Sixteen exceeds fourteen — but now a different problem arises.

### Shabbat Meals

The *Gemara* asks: is this calculation aligned with Rabbi Chidka, who holds that one is obligated to eat four meals on Shabbat rather than three? With four Shabbat meals, the week contains sixteen meals total, and sixteen matches perfectly. The *Gemara* replies that one can maintain the Rabbinic view of three Shabbat meals as well: the extra portion — one meal's worth of bread — is set aside *le-orchei u-farchei*, for guests who might arrive unexpectedly. Hence the two *kav* covers fourteen regular meals plus one for Shabbat's Friday night meal together (whether literal or euphemistic) plus one extra for hospitality, reaching sixteen. Returning then to Rabbi Shimon's eighteen: under the Rabbinic view of three Shabbat meals (fifteen total), three portions are reserved for guests; under Rabbi Chidka's four Shabbat meals (sixteen total), two portions go toward hospitality.

## Missing Items

### No Protein/Veg

One striking feature of this minimum allowance is what it omits. The wife receives grain (wheat or barley), legumes, oil, and dried fruit or dates. What is entirely absent is protein — no meat, no fish, no eggs, no milk, no cheese. Equally absent are vegetables. This is characterized explicitly as the minimum for the poor — *ani she-be-Yisrael* — yet even granting that qualifier, the diet described is extremely bare: essentially starch supplemented by a small quantity of fruit. The *Gemara* is not troubled by the absence of protein or vegetables; as we will see, what the *Gemara* focuses on instead is the absence of wine.

## Wine Restrictions

### No Wine Rule

The *Mishnah*'s list of provisions includes no wine, and the *Gemara* takes this as support for the position of Rabbi Elazar, who states: *ein poskin yeinot le-ishah* — wine is not allocated to a woman living apart from her husband. Whether *beit din* is directing provision from a living but absent husband's assets or from a deceased husband's estate (since the obligation of *mezonot* for the widow from the estate follows the same framework as for the living separated wife), the same rule applies: wine is not part of the court-ordered food allocation.

### Hosea Derashah

Rabbi Yehudah of Kfar Nevirya (or, in another version, Kfar Nafor Chayya) offers a scriptural basis for this rule, deriving it from the verse in Hosea (2:7): *elchah acharei me'ahavai notnei lachmi u-maymai tzamri u-fishti shamni ve-shikuyai* — "I will go after my lovers who give me my bread and my water, my wool and my linen, my oil and my drink." The verse lists what a woman seeks from her lovers. The *Gemara* explains: *shikuyai* in this context does not mean wine but rather *devarim she-ha-ishah mishtokeket aleihen* — things a woman desires — namely jewelry and adornments. The verse, on this reading, implies that wine is emphatically not what should be provided for a woman from outside.

A further *derashah* is built on the narrative of Hannah at Shiloh. The verse reads: *va-takam Chanah acharei ochlo ve-Shilo ve-acharei shato* — "and Hannah arose after he ate in Shiloh and after he drank." The *drasha* notes *shato* — his drinking — but not *shatetah*, her drinking; the implication is that she did not drink wine. The *Gemara* challenges this: should *ochlo* similarly be read as his eating alone, implying she did not eat? The *Gemara* responds: *anan be-shinui kera be-dibura ka-amrinan* — the *drasha* rests on the deviation in language. The verse is speaking about Hannah in the third person throughout; when it shifts to speak of *shato* — his drinking — without a corresponding form for her drinking, that deviation is the exegetical point. The eating is stated in a form that encompasses her as well, but the drinking is stated only for him.

### Accustomed Exception

A *baraita* qualifies Rabbi Elazar's rule: *regila nosnin lah* — if a woman is accustomed to drinking wine, wine is allocated for her. *Regila shani* — an accustomed woman is treated differently, on the theory that she has developed a tolerance and the concern for licentious behavior is diminished. Rabbi Chanina bar Kahana in the name of Shmuel specifies: if she is accustomed, she receives one cup; if she is not accustomed, she receives two cups — a formulation that on its face seems counterintuitive and requires Abaye's clarification.

### Abaye Refinement

Abaye explains that Shmuel's statement must be read comparatively, distinguishing between her situation *bifnei ba'alah* (when living with her husband) and *she-lo bifnei ba'alah* (when living apart). The full structure is: if she customarily drinks two cups when with her husband, she receives one cup when apart from him; if she customarily drinks only one cup when with her husband, she receives nothing when apart from him. The reasoning is that even for a woman accustomed to wine, the absence of her husband removes a check on her behavior — both because her marital desires may go unfulfilled and because her husband is not present to moderate her conduct — so the allocation is reduced by one full cup. And if she was already at the minimum of one cup with her husband, there is no further reduction available, so she receives nothing.

### Cooking Wine

An alternative resolution is offered: even when drinking wine is prohibited, wine for cooking — *le-tsikei kdera* — is permitted. The concern about licentious behavior attaches to drinking; using wine as a cooking ingredient does not implicate that concern.

### Nakdimon Story

This principle is illustrated by the story of the daughter-in-law of Nakdimon ben Guryon. The Rabbis allocated for her two *se'ah* of wine per week — *me-erev Shabbat le-erev Shabbat* — specifically for cooking. She responded, "May you merit to apportion the same for your own daughters" — whether sincerely or ironically is unclear. The narrator adds: she was a *shomeret yavam*, awaiting *yibbum*, and the Sages did not respond *amen* to her blessing, lest it be read as an ill omen for their own daughters to be left widowed awaiting *yibbum*. The detail about *yibbum* also illuminates why it is *beit din* rather than a living husband allocating her food: she is a widow drawing sustenance from the deceased husband's estate, which is precisely the situation in which the *Mishnah*'s minimum provisions become most practically important for the *almanah*.

## Wine & Behavior

### One to Three Cups

The *Gemara* now states the *tanna*'s teaching that underlies all these restrictions. One cup of wine is beneficial for a woman — *kos echad yafeh le-ishah*. Two cups is unseemly — *nival hu*. Three cups, and she will verbally proposition men for sex — *tova'at be-peh*.

### Four Cups

Four cups, and she will solicit even a donkey in the marketplace without shame — *afilu chamor tova'at ba-shuk ve-eina ikpat lah*. This is, manifestly, a teaching composed from a male perspective, and reflects the assumption — already encountered in the *mored/moredes* discussion — that women's sexual self-control is more vulnerable to alcohol than men's. The social context is also relevant: women in this society had far less habitual exposure to wine, which would indeed have lowered their tolerance. Even granting all this, the extreme escalation is notable.

### Rava Clarification

Rava limits the entire teaching: *lo shanu ela she-ein ba'alah imah* — all of this applies only when the husband is not present. When the husband is with her — *ba'alah imah leit lan bah* — there is no restriction. This confirms the framework of the whole discussion: these wine-allocation rules operate only when *beit din* is the one providing food and wine, i.e., when she is living apart from her husband. When she is with her husband, what the two of them choose to drink together is their own business, and we assume the husband's presence serves as a sufficient check.

### Guest Exception

The *Gemara* raises a challenge from the story of Hannah, who was with her husband Elkanah at Shiloh, yet did not drink — and we derived from this that wine is restricted even when the husband is present. Rava's ruling seems to contradict this. The *Gemara* resolves it: *achsanai shani* — guests are different. Rav Huna teaches: how do we know that a guest couple should not engage in marital relations while staying in someone else's home? From the verse describing Elkanah and Hannah: *va-yashkimu ba-boker va-yishtachavu lifnei Hashem va-yashuvu va-yavo'u el beitam ha-Ramatah va-yeida Elkanah et Chana ishto* — they rose in the morning, bowed before God, returned to their home in Ramah, and *only then* Elkanah knew Hannah. The sequence implies that the marital intimacy occurred only after they returned home, not while guests at Shiloh. The same principle governs wine: when one is a guest in another's home, the usual latitude that applies between husband and wife in their own home does not apply. Greater decorum is required.

## Allocation Stories

### Homa's Request

Homa, who was Abaye's wife and apparently a woman of notable beauty, came before Rava. She requested that he allocate food for her — *psok li mezoni* — presumably either because Abaye was absent or, as later details suggest, possibly because he had died. Rava made the allocation.

### Private Wine Use

She then asked him to also allocate wine — *psok li chamra*. Rava replied: I know Nachmani — Abaye's other name — and he would not drink wine. She responded: by your life, he would drink wine in a *shufrizi* — a long horn-shaped cup — *this* big, and she extended her arms to demonstrate the size. This exchange itself illustrates a point made earlier in the *sugya*: people behave privately in ways they do not display publicly. Rava was under the impression that Abaye never drank wine; his wife knew otherwise.

### Rava's Response

As she extended her arms to demonstrate the size of the cup, her sleeve fell back and her arm became exposed. The *Gemara* describes it: *nafal nehora be-vei dina* — light filled the *beit din*, meaning her arm was of such luminous beauty that it illuminated the courtroom. Rava was profoundly stirred. He rose, went home, and immediately propositioned his wife — the daughter of Rav Chisda — seeking to be intimate with her in the middle of the day.

### Wife's Pursuit

When his wife asked what had happened in court, Rava told her that Homa, Abaye's wife, had appeared before him. His wife immediately ran after Homa and struck her with the strap from a chest — *be-kofei de-shida* — chasing her from the entire city of Mechuza. She shouted at Homa: *katlat leh* — you have already killed one man (meaning her husband), and now you want to kill another? The background is that Homa had been married three times, each husband having died, and Rava's wife feared that Homa's beauty would lead yet another husband — her own — to his ruin. Whether Abaye was indeed among the three deceased husbands is not entirely clear from the text; the narrative leaves open whether he was dead or merely absent, which is why Homa was seeking a food allocation in the first place.

### Rav Yosef's Wife

A parallel story follows. The wife of Rav Yosef the son of Rava came before Rav Nechemia the son of Rav Yosef. She too requested a food allocation — *psok li mezoni* — and he provided it. She then requested a wine allocation — *psok li chamra* — and he provided that as well. He explained his reasoning: *yadana behu bi-venei Mechuza de-shatu chamra* — I know the people of Mechuza, that they drink wine regularly. Because wine is a routine part of daily life in Mechuza — much as it would be in regions where wine accompanies every meal as a matter of course — allocating wine to a woman from that city does not implicate the concerns that motivated the restriction. Custom and local practice determine what counts as ordinary consumption and what constitutes dangerous excess.
