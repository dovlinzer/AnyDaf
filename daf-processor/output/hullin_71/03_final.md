# Hullin 71 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chayah & Behemah

### Intro: Inclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַיָּה בִּכְלַל בְּהֵמָה מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״זֹאת הַבְּהֵמָה אֲשֶׁר תֹּאכֵלוּ שׁוֹר שֵׂה כְשָׂבִים וְגוֹ׳ אַיָּל וּצְבִי וְיַחְמוּר וְגוֹ׳״, הָא כֵּיצַד? חַיָּה בִּכְלַל בְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** From where do we derive that according to the Torah, **a *ḥayya* is included** in the category of **a *behema*? As it is written: "These are the *behema* that you may eat: An ox, a sheep,** and a goat, **a deer, and a gazelle, and a fallow deer,** and a wild goat, and an oryx, and an aurochs, and a wild sheep" (Deuteronomy 14:4–5). Despite employing the term "*behema*," the verse also lists the deer and the gazelle, which are undomesticated animals. **How** is **this** possible? From here it is evident that **a *ḥayya* is included** by the Torah in the category of **a *behema*.**

The Gemara on 71a opens by establishing, through explicit *psukim*, that the categories of *chayah* and *behemah* are mutually inclusive — each can fall under the rubric of the other depending on context. The question *chayah bichlal behemah minalan* — how do we know that *chayah* is included within *behemah* — is answered from the verse *zos habehemah asher tocheilu* (Devarim 14:4–5), which opens with the heading of *behemah* and proceeds to enumerate species such as *shor*, *seh chesavim*, and *seh izim* — ox, sheep, and goat — but then continues to list *ayal*, *tzvi*, and *yachmur* (deer, gazelle, and fallow deer), which are plainly *chayah*. The fact that the verse subsumes these *chayos* under the heading of *behemah* demonstrates that *behemah* can function as a generic term inclusive of *chayah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּהֵמָה בִּכְלַל חַיָּה מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״זֹאת הַחַיָּה אֲשֶׁר תֹּאכְלוּ מִכׇּל הַבְּהֵמָה אֲשֶׁר עַל הָאָרֶץ כֹּל מַפְרֶסֶת פַּרְסָה״, הָא כֵּיצַד? בְּהֵמָה בִּכְלַל חַיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** From where do we derive that according to the Torah, **a *behema* is included** in the category of **a *ḥayya*? As it is written: "These are the *ḥayya* that you may eat, among all the *behema* that are on the earth. Whatever parts the hoof"** (Leviticus 11:2–3). **How** is **this** possible, that the verse mentions a *ḥayya* and then refers to a *behema*? This indicates that **a domesticated animal is included** in the category of **a *ḥayya*** by the Torah.

The converse is established from a separate verse: *v'zos hachayah asher tocheilu mikol habehemah asher al ha-aretz* — "and this is the *chayah* that you may eat from among all the *behemah* on the land" — which inverts the terminology, subsuming *behemah* under the heading of *chayah*. The *pshat* is clear: these words are not always category-specific; they function as general terms that can encompass each other. With this mutual inclusion established, the Gemara proceeds to identify four distinct halachic applications, two in each direction, encompassing both *tahor* and *tamei* species.

### Kosher Inclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַיָּה טְהוֹרָה בִּכְלַל בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה – לְסִימָנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The principle that **a kosher *ḥayya* is included** by the Torah in the category of **a kosher *behema*** is relevant **with regard to** the **distinguishing characteristics** of kosher animals. The Torah states with regard to a domesticated animal that only a species that chews its cud and has cloven hooves is kosher. Nevertheless, this requirement is understood as a reference to both domesticated and undomesticated animals.

The first application: *chayah tehorah bichlal behemah tehorah l'simanim* — a kosher *chayah* is subsumed under the category of kosher *behemah* with respect to the identifying signs of permitted consumption. The verse that specifies the *simanim* — split hooves and cud-chewing — frames them in terms of *behemah*. Since *chayah* is included within *behemah*, the same *simanim* apply to *chayah* as well, even though the verse does not enumerate them separately for wild animals.

### Non-Kosher Cross-breed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַיָּה טְמֵאָה בִּכְלַל בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה – לְהַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The principle that **a non-kosher *ḥayya* is included** by the Torah in the category of **a non-kosher *behema*** is relevant **with regard to** the prohibition against **mating** one species with another. The Torah states the prohibition with regard to a *behema*: "You shall not let your animal [*behema*] mate with a diverse kind" (Leviticus 19:19). Nevertheless, this is understood as referring to both a *behema* and a *ḥayya*.

The second application concerns non-kosher animals: *chayah temeiah bichlal behemah temeiah l'harva'ah* — a non-kosher *chayah* is included within *behemah* for the prohibition of cross-breeding (*kilayim*). The verse *behemtecha lo tarbi'ah kilayim* prohibits cross-breeding one's *behemah*, but says nothing explicit about *chayah*. Since *chayah* is included in *behemah*, the prohibition extends to wild animals as well, whether of kosher or non-kosher species.

### Gezeirah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה בִּכְלַל חַיָּה טְמֵאָה – לְכִדְרַבִּי. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אֶקְרָא אֲנִי ״חַיָּה״, בְּהֵמָה לָמָּה נֶאֶמְרָה? נֶאֶמְרָה כָּאן ״בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה״, מָה לְהַלָּן טוּמְאַת קֹדֶשׁ – אַף כָּאן טוּמְאַת קֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The principle that **a non-kosher *behema* is included** by the Torah in the category of **a non-kosher *ḥayya*** is relevant **with regard to that which Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi taught, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** Since a domesticated animal is also referred to as a *ḥayya*, it would be sufficient if **I would read,** i.e., the verse would write, only the clause about **a *ḥayya*,** and I would have known that one who touches a carcass of any non-kosher animal is impure. **Why,** then, **is** an explicit clause about **a *behema* stated?** It serves as the basis for a verbal analogy. **It is stated here,** with regard to one who is unaware that he touched an impure item: **"Non-kosher *behema*," and it is** likewise **stated below,** with regard to one who was aware that he touched an impure item: **"Non-kosher *behema*,"** in the following verse: "And when any one shall touch any impure thing, whether it is the impurity of man, or a non-kosher *behema*, and then eats of the flesh of a peace offering to the Lord, that soul shall be cut off from its people" (Leviticus 7:21). **Just as below,** the verse is referring to the **defiling of sacrificial** foods, **so too here,** the reference is to the **defiling of sacrificial** foods, and therefore one must bring a sliding-scale offering as an atonement.

The third application reverses the direction: a non-kosher *behemah* is included within *chayah* — *behemah temeiah bichlal chayah temeiah* — and this is relevant to what Rebbi taught in a *braisa*. Rebbi asks: the verse that addresses the case of one who touches the corpse of a non-kosher animal and then enters the *beis hamikdash* or eats consecrated food — why does that verse mention both *chayah* and *behemah*? Since *chayah* already includes *behemah*, the explicit mention of *behemah* is redundant and therefore available for a *gezeirah shavah*. Rebbi leverages this: the word *behemah temeiah* appears here and also later in *vayikra* in the context of prohibiting the eating of *korbanos* while in a state of *tumah*. The shared language creates a *gezeirah shavah* — *mah lehalan tumas kodesh, af kan tumas kodesh* — teaching that the transgression under discussion is specifically eating sacred food while *tamei*. The case thus requires the mutual inclusion not as a source of new law, but as the precondition that renders the explicit mention of *behemah* in the first verse formally superfluous and available for the hermeneutic derivation.

### Formation & Miscarriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה בִּכְלַל חַיָּה טְהוֹרָה, לִיצִירָה, דִּתְנַן: הַמַּפֶּלֶת מִין בְּהֵמָה חַיָּה וָעוֹף, בֵּין טְמֵאִין בֵּין טְהוֹרִין, אִם זָכָר – תֵּשֵׁב לְזָכָר. אִם נְקֵבָה – תֵּשֵׁב לִנְקֵבָה. אֵינוֹ יָדוּעַ – תֵּשֵׁב לְזָכָר וְלִנְקֵבָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ מִצּוּרַת אָדָם אֵינוֹ וָלָד.
>
> **Translation:** The principle that **a kosher *behema* is included** by the Torah in the category of **a kosher *ḥayya*** is relevant **with regard to** the **formation** of children. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Nidda* 21a): With regard to a woman **who miscarries** a fetus that appears to be in the form of **a type of *behema*, *ḥayya*, or bird, whether** it had the form of **a non-kosher** species **or a kosher** species, **if** the fetus is **a male,** then **she observes** the periods of impurity and purity required **for** giving birth to **a male. If** the fetus is **a female,** then **she observes** the periods required **for** giving birth to **a female.** After giving birth to a male, a woman is impure for seven days, during which it is prohibited for her to engage in intercourse. During the next thirty-three days she may immerse in a ritual bath at any time, after which she is permitted to engage in intercourse even should she then experience a discharge of uterine blood. Nevertheless, during the entire forty-day period she may not enter the Temple or be in contact with consecrated foods. After giving birth to a girl, the length of each of these periods is doubled to fourteen days and sixty-six days, respectively (see Leviticus 12:2–5). If the sex of the fetus is **unknown, she observes** the strictures of a woman who gave birth both **to a male and to a female.** Accordingly, it is prohibited for her to engage in intercourse for the fourteen days after birth, after which she immerses in a ritual bath. After that she is permitted to engage in intercourse despite any discharge of uterine blood until the fortieth day after birth, i.e., for thirty-three days following the seven days she would have been prohibited from engaging in intercourse if the fetus was male. The prohibition against entering the Temple, however, continues until eighty days have passed from the birth. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.** The Gemara elsewhere (*Nidda* 22b) explains that Rabbi Meir derives his opinion from the fact that the term "formation" is used to describe both the formation of man: "Then the Lord God formed man" (Genesis 2:7), and the formation of animals and birds: "The Lord God formed every animal [*ḥayya*] of the field, and every bird of the air" (Genesis 2:19). Furthermore, he holds that the term "*ḥayya*" is referring to both domesticated and undomesticated animals, and concludes that a woman observes these periods of purity and impurity if the fetus has the form of any of these types of animals. **And the Rabbis say: Any** fetus **that is not of human form is not** regarded as **an offspring** with regard to observance of these periods of purity and impurity, and she is permitted to engage in intercourse provided she does not experience a discharge of uterine blood.

The fourth application: *behemah tehorah bichlal chayah tehorah liyetzirah* — a kosher *behemah* is included within *chayah* with respect to *yetzirah*, the act of formation or creation. This rests on the verse in *bereishis*: *vayeitzer hashem elokim min ha'adamah kol chayas hasadeh v'es kol oph hashamayim* — God fashioned from the earth all the living creatures of the field and all the birds of the sky. The verse uses the word *chayah*, yet it plainly refers to all animals, including *behemah*. The halachic relevance emerges from a *mishnah* in *niddah*: *hamapeles min behemah chayah va'oph* — if a woman miscarries a fetus that resembles a *behemah*, a *chayah*, or a bird, whether of a *tahor* or *tamei* species, she is nonetheless treated as *temeiah leidah*. If the fetus appears male she observes the impurity of bearing a male child; if female, the longer impurity of a female; if indeterminate, she takes on both stringencies. This is *divrei Rebbi Meir*.

The reasoning of *rebbi meir* is that since all creatures — human, *chayah*, and *behemah* — are included together in the verse of *vayeitzer*, even a fetus resembling a non-human animal is considered a sufficient act of birth to impose *tumat leidah* on the mother. The question then arises: where does *rebbi meir* derive the inclusion specifically for a fetus that resembles a *behemah*, given that the *bereishis* verse mentions only *chayah* and *oph* explicitly? The answer is precisely the principle established above — *behemah bichlal chayah* — since *behemah* is encompassed within *chayah*, the verse in *bereishis* covers *behemah* as well.

The *chachamim* disagree, holding *kol she'eino mitzurat adam eino valad* — if it does not appear human, it does not impose *tumat leidah*. The Gemara notes that since the *chachamim* reject *rebbi meir's* ruling here, they must find another use for the verse demonstrating *behemah bichlal chayah* — and indeed, *rebbi* in the previous case (*lehalan*) uses it for the derivation concerning *tumah* of the non-kosher *behemah*.

What emerges from this entire sugya is that the mutual inclusion of *chayah* and *behemah* is not merely a lexicographic observation but has operative halachic consequences across domains ranging from the *simanim* of permitted eating, to the *kilayim* of cross-breeding, to the complex hermeneutics of *korbanos*, to the *tumah* status of a miscarried fetus. It also reflects a broader methodological disagreement between *rebbi yishmael*, who views the interchangeability of *chayah* and *behemah* as straightforward *pshat* — the *torah* simply speaks in general terms — and others who derive legal content from the same interchangeability through formal *drashos*.

## Tumah Bluah

### Fetus in Womb

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁמֵּת וְלָדָהּ בְּתוֹךְ מֵעֶיהָ, וּפָשְׁטָה חַיָּה אֶת יָדָהּ וְנָגְעָה בּוֹ – הַחַיָּה טְמֵאָה טוּמְאַת שִׁבְעָה, וְהָאִשָּׁה טְהוֹרָה עַד שֶׁיֵּצֵא הַוָּלָד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a woman whose fetus died in her womb and** the **midwife extended her hand** into the womb **and touched** the fetus, **the midwife is** thereby rendered **impure with the seven-**day **impurity** imparted by a corpse, **and the woman** remains **ritually pure until the offspring emerges** from the womb.

The *mishnah* that brings us into the next sugya introduces a case that initially appears straightforward but conceals one of the more technically involved principles in these chapters: *ha'isha shemeit valadah betoch me'eha* — a woman whose fetus died in her womb. *ufashtah chayah et yadah* — the midwife (the *chayah*, literally "the one who gives life") extended her hand inside to assist in delivery, *venag'ah bo hachayah tmeiah tumat shivah* — and touched the dead fetus; she contracts seven-day *tumah* from corpse contact. *veha'isha tehorah* — and the mother remains *tehorah* until the fetus exits the womb.

The operative principle here is *tumah bluah* — literally, swallowed or absorbed *tumah*. Something enclosed within a body is not considered to exist in the wider world as an independent source of *tumah*. To appreciate this, consider the analogous case that the Gemara will develop: a *neveilah* — the corpse of a non-kosher animal or any animal that died without proper *shechita* — that has been swallowed and is sitting in a person's stomach. Does it continue to cause *tumah*? The answer is no: absorbed within a body, it is no longer present in the world in a halachically operative sense.

This principle explains the asymmetry in the *mishnah*. From the mother's perspective — and from the perspective of everyone outside — the fetus is *bluah*, enclosed and absorbed, and thus does not function as a corpse causing *tumah*. The midwife, however, has inserted her hand into that space and is making direct contact with the fetus. From *her* perspective, it is not *bluah* at all — she is accessing it and touching it. Hence she contracts *tumat shivah* while the mother remains *tehorah*.

One might ask: what about the space itself — if the midwife's hand is within the womb but not yet touching the fetus, should not the proximity within that enclosed space itself create *tumah*, given that a corpse ordinarily projects *tumah* to all persons sharing an *ohel*? This is a genuine question, and the *mishnah*'s formulation — emphasizing *touching* — implies that the *bluah* status is only removed when there is actual handling contact, not mere presence in the same enclosed space. Until the fetus is physically handled, it retains the status of *bluah*.

The question of how a midwife could know with certainty that the fetus was already dead at the moment of contact is addressed practically: in any case of doubt, the same *halachah* applies *misafek* — she would be *temeiah tumat shivah misafek*.

A striking dimension of this *mishnah* is its implicit position on the status of the unborn. However varied the halachic opinions on abortion may be — ranging from those who view a fetus as having virtually no independent status, through positions that recognize partial life, to the stringent view of *rav moshe feinstein* based on the *rambam* that considers abortion akin to a form of killing — this *mishnah* treats a fetus that dies in the womb as a *met* in the full sense, capable of conveying *tumat meit*. It does not say the fetus lacks corpse-status because it was never born alive. The question of how early in development this applies is separately addressed in the *mishnah* in *niddah*, where the threshold of *tumat meit* from a fetus requires that it have reached a sufficient stage of development — specifically, a head of a size comparable to the warp-spool — and not merely the 40-day mark that governs *tumat niddah* in cases of questionable discharge.

### Rava's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבָּה: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁטּוּמְאָה בְּלוּעָה אֵינָהּ מְטַמְּאָה, כָּךְ טׇהֳרָה בְּלוּעָה אֵינָהּ מִיטַּמְּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabba says: Just as** a ritually **impure** item that is **encapsulated** within a body **does not impart impurity** to an item that comes in contact with it, **so too,** a ritually **pure** item that is **encapsulated** within a body **cannot be rendered impure** if it comes in contact with an impure item. For example, if one swallowed a ring that was ritually pure and then entered a tent containing a corpse, the ring would not be rendered impure.

*Amar Rava*: just as absorbed *tumah* does not cause *tumah* — *keshem she-tumah bluah einah metame* — so too *tahara bluah*, something *tahor* that is absorbed inside a person, does not become *tamei* — *kach tahara bluah eina mitame*. This is a paired principle: the absorption effect runs symmetrically in both directions.

### Neveilah Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טוּמְאָה בְּלוּעָה מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָאֹכֵל מִנִּבְלָתָהּ יְכַבֵּס בְּגָדָיו״, מִי לָא עָסְקִינַן דַּאֲכַל סָמוּךְ לִשְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה, וְקָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that **an encapsulated impure** item does not impart impurity? **As it is written: "And one who eats of its carcass shall wash his clothes,** and be impure until the evening" (Leviticus 11:40), i.e., he must immerse his body and garments in a ritual bath, and he then becomes pure at sunset. **Are we not dealing** even with a case **where one ate** from the carcass **close to sunset** and then immersed? **And** yet **the Merciful One states** that he is **pure** once the sun sets, despite the fact that his stomach still contains pieces of the carcass that have not yet been digested. If so, it is apparent that an encapsulated impure item does not impart impurity.

The Gemara then asks: *tumah bluah minalan* — from where do we derive that absorbed *tumah* does not cause *tumah*? The source is the verse *ve-hochel mi-nivlatah yechabes begadav* — one who eats from a *neveilah* must immerse his garments. The Gemara reasons: are we not discussing a case where a person ate this *neveilah* close to sunset — *samuch lishkias hachamah* — immersed in the *mikvah*, and emerged *tahor*, even though the *neveilah* is still inside his stomach? *ve-amar rachmana tahor* — the Torah declares him *tahor*. Despite the continued presence of the *neveilah* within him, he is not *tamei*. This is the proof that absorbed *tumah* does not cause *tumah*.

### Reuiah Le-Ger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּלָא חַזְיָא לְגֵר. הָנִיחָא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר: אַחַת זוֹ וְאַחַת זוֹ, עַד לְכֶלֶב – שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא לְבַר פְּדָא, דְּאָמַר: טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה לְגֵר, וְטוּמְאָה קַלָּה עַד לְכֶלֶב – מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא חַזְיָא לְגֵר הוּא! נְהִי דְּלָא חַזְיָא בְּפָנָיו, שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו מִיחַזְיָא חַזְיָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But perhaps** it **is different there,** as with regard to the prohibition against eating a carcass the Torah states: "You shall not eat any carcass; you may give it to the gentile resident who is in your cities" (Deuteronomy 14:21). The juxtaposition of the two parts of the verse teaches that the prohibition is limited to a carcass that is fit to be given to a gentile resident, i.e., it is fit for human consumption. Accordingly, perhaps the carcass that the person ate does not impart impurity, **as it is no** longer **fit for a gentile resident,** i.e., it is now unsuitable for human consumption. **This** proof **works out well according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, who says:** A carcass imparts impurity to **both this,** a person, **and that,** food, even if it is unfit for human consumption, **until** it is no longer fit **for** consumption by **a dog.** According to this opinion the proof is understood **well,** as the carcass in the person's stomach still has the ability to impart impurity to him because it is still fit for consumption by a dog. Therefore, it is apparent that the only reason it does not impart impurity is that it is encapsulated within his body. **But according to** the opinion of **bar Padda, who says** a carcass has the **severe form of ritual impurity,** which can impart impurity to people only while it is still fit to be eaten **by a gentile resident and** it has **the light form of ritual impurity** even **until** the point that it is no longer fit for consumption **by a dog,** this proof is flawed. This is because it is possible the carcass in the person's stomach does not render him impure simply because it is no longer fit for human consumption. Can one claim that the carcass cannot impart impurity **because it is no** longer **fit for a gentile resident? Granted that** the carcass **is not fit** to be eaten by someone when it was eaten **in his presence,** as he is aware that it was swallowed and considers it disgusting to eat a swallowed item, but **it is** still sometimes **fit** for a person to eat when it was eaten **not in his presence** and he is unaware that it had been previously swallowed. This is possible where one consumed a small piece of food and did not chew it prior to swallowing. The Gemara concedes this point and therefore accepts the proof.

Rashi makes a critical methodological observation here that will carry weight throughout the subsequent discussion. One might have thought the reason the person is not *tamei* is simply that he is not in contact with the *neveilah* — invoking the principle of *negiat beit hastarim*, that touching something with an internal part of the body (a hidden part not exposed externally) does not constitute halachic contact. If so, the proof would only establish the absence of *tumah* from non-contact, not the full principle of *tumah bluah*. Rashi explains, however, that this objection does not apply here, because a *neveilah* transmits *tumah* not only by touch but also by carrying — *masa*. If the *neveilah* in the stomach were still halachically operative, the person carrying it would be *tamei* without any touching at all. The fact that he is *tahor* therefore cannot be attributed to the absence of touch; it must reflect that the absorbed *neveilah* is simply no longer present in the world as a *neveilah*. Similarly, in our *mishnah*, the dead fetus projects *tumah* to all in the room if it is exposed — but while within the mother, it does not, precisely because it is *bluah*.

### From Below

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן טוּמְאָה בְּלוּעָה, טׇהֳרָה בְּלוּעָה מְנָלַן? קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה כְּלִי חֶרֶס הַמּוּקָּף צָמִיד פָּתִיל, שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַצִּיל עַל טוּמְאָה שֶׁבְּתוֹכוֹ מִלְּטַמֵּא, דְּאָמַר מָר: טוּמְאָה רְצוּצָה בּוֹקַעַת וְעוֹלָה עַד לָרָקִיעַ – מַצִּיל עַל טׇהֳרָה שֶׁבְּתוֹכוֹ מִלְּטַמֵּא, אָדָם שֶׁמַּצִּיל עַל טוּמְאָה שֶׁבְּתוֹכוֹ מִלְּטַמֵּא – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁמַּצִּיל עַל טׇהֳרָה שֶׁבְּתוֹכוֹ מִלִּיטַּמֵּא? מָה לִכְלִי חֶרֶס, שֶׁכֵּן אֵין מִטַּמֵּא מִגַּבּוֹ, תֹּאמַר בָּאָדָם שֶׁמִּטַּמֵּא מִגַּבּוֹ? אַטּוּ אֲנַן מִגַּבּוֹ קָאָמְרִינַן? מִתּוֹכוֹ קָאָמְרִינַן! אַדְּרַבָּה, כְּלִי חֶרֶס חָמוּר, שֶׁכֵּן מְטַמֵּא מֵאֲוִירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for the *halakha* that **an encapsulated impure** item does not impart impurity. **From where do we** derive that **a pure** item that is **encapsulated** within another body **cannot be rendered impure** if it comes in contact with an impure item? The Gemara explains: It is derived through **an *a fortiori*** inference: **If an earthenware vessel that is closed with a tightly sealed cover, which does not shield,** i.e., prevent, the **impure** piece of a corpse **contained within it from imparting impurity** to other items in the same tent as it; the Gemara interrupts the explication of the inference and clarifies: **As the Master said:** The **impure** piece of a corpse that is **in a tight space;** e.g., it is buried in the ground and there is not a handbreadth of hollow space above it, beneath the dirt, **breaks through** the earth above it **and ascends up to the heavens,** imparting impurity in that path. The Gemara resumes the explication of the inference: Although the tightly sealed earthenware vessel does not shield the impure item, it **does shield a pure** item **contained within it from being rendered impure** (see Numbers 19:15). Then with regard to **a person, who does shield** the **impurity** of a piece of a corpse **contained within him from imparting impurity** to other items in the same tent, as the Gemara derived above, **is it not logical that he shields a pure** item **contained within him from being rendered impure?** The Gemara challenges the inference: **What** is notable **about an earthenware vessel?** It is notable **in that it cannot become impure through** a source of impurity coming in contact with **its exterior. Can you say,** then, that it is comparable **to a person, who can become impure through** a source of impurity coming in contact with **his exterior?** Since here the *halakhot* concerning impurity of a person are more stringent than those concerning an earthenware vessel, one cannot apply to a person a leniency with regard to an earthenware vessel. The Gemara responds: **Is that to say we are speaking of** rendering an item impure through a source of impurity coming in contact with **its exterior? We are** in fact **speaking of** where the impurity comes in contact with **its interior.** For such cases, it is reasonable to compare the case of a person with an earthenware vessel. The Gemara adds: **On the contrary,** the contraction of impurity by **an earthenware vessel is more stringent** than that of a person, **as** it **becomes impure through** the presence of a source of impurity in **its airspace,** even if the source does not touch the vessel itself. By contrast, a person is rendered impure from a source of impurity in his airspace, e.g., in his mouth, only if it actually touches him, and the *a fortiori* inference remains valid.

Having established *tumah bluah* for the case of swallowing — absorption from above — the Gemara asks: *beluah dilmata minalan* — from where do we know that the same principle applies to absorption from below, i.e., something entering through the lower orifices, including the case of the fetus in the womb? The Gemara answers with a *kal vachomer*: if absorption from above — through the mouth, which is not the primary site of digestion — nevertheless grants *bluah* status and protects from *tumah*, then absorption from below — *she'oseh ichul*, where the digestive process is more intensive — certainly should. The Gemara anticipates the objection that digestion below only occurs because of what came from above, and responds that despite that dependency, *ichul dilmata nafish* — the degree of processing that occurs further down the digestive tract is in any case greater, making the *kal vachomer* stand.

### In Behemah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן בָּלוּעַ דְּאָדָם, בָּלוּעַ דִּבְהֵמָה מְנָלַן? קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה אָדָם שֶׁמִּטַּמֵּא מֵחַיִּים מַצִּיל בְּבָלוּעַ, בְּהֵמָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ מְטַמְּאָה מֵחַיִּים – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁתַּצִּיל בְּבָלוּעַ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for the *halakha* that an impure item **encapsulated** in the body **of a person** does not impart impurity; **from where do we** derive that an impure item **encapsulated** in the body **of an animal** does not impart impurity? The Gemara answers: It is derived through **an *a fortiori*** inference: **If** the body of **a person, who can impart impurity** even **while he is** still **alive,** e.g., if he is a leper or a *zav*, nevertheless **shields** the impure item **encapsulated** inside him from imparting impurity, then with regard to **an animal, which does not impart impurity while it is alive, is it not logical that it should shield** an impure item **encapsulated** inside it from imparting impurity?

With the principle of *tumah bluah* established for human beings — both from above and from below — the Gemara extends the inquiry: *beluah debeheimah minalan* — how do we know that something absorbed within an animal also has *bluah* status? This is directly relevant to the preceding *mishnah* about the fetus in an animal's womb, where the shepherd who inserted a hand and touched the fetus became *tamei* while the mother did not. The Gemara answers again with a *kal vachomer*: *umah adam shemetame mechayim matzil be-valua* — a human being, who is susceptible to *tumah* even while alive (through *zivah*, *baal keri*, *niddah*, *yoledet*, *metzora*, and the like), nevertheless protects things absorbed within him from operative *tumah*. *behemah she-einah metamma'ah mechayim* — an animal, which does not transmit *tumah* while alive, is far less susceptible to *tumah* overall. *eino din she-tatzil be-valua* — how much more so should it protect things absorbed within it.

### Tzaraat House

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְאָדָם, שֶׁכֵּן צָרִיךְ שְׁהִיָּיה בְּבַיִת הַמְנוּגָּע, תֹּאמַר בִּבְהֵמָה, שֶׁאֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה שְׁהִיָּיה בְּבַיִת הַמְנוּגָּע? בְּהֵמָה דְּאֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה שְׁהִיָּיה בְּבַיִת הַמְנוּגָּע, לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? לְכֵלִים שֶׁעַל גַּבָּהּ. אָדָם נָמֵי לָא בָּעֵי! דִּתְנַן: הַנִּכְנָס לְבַיִת הַמְנוּגָּע וְכֵלָיו עַל כְּתֵפָיו, וְסַנְדָּלָיו וְטַבְּעוֹתָיו בְּיָדָיו – הוּא וְהֵן טְמֵאִין מִיָּד. הָיָה לָבוּשׁ כֵּלָיו וְסַנְדָּלָיו בְּרַגְלָיו וְטַבְּעוֹתָיו בְּאֶצְבָּעוֹ – הוּא טָמֵא מִיָּד, וְהֵן טְהוֹרִין עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁהֶא בִּכְדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס, פַּת חִטִּים וְלֹא פַּת שְׂעוֹרִים, מֵיסֵב וְאוֹכֵל בְּלִיפְתָּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges the inference: **What** is notable **about** the impurity of **a person?** It is notable **in that** if he entered a leprous house, in order for his garment to be rendered impure **he needs to remain in** the **leprous house** for a minimal period of time (see Leviticus 14:33–53). **Can you say,** then, that it is comparable **to an animal, which does not need to remain in a leprous house** for a minimal period of time? Rather, any utensils placed upon it are rendered impure immediately upon its entry. Since here the *halakhot* concerning impurity of an animal are more stringent than those concerning a person, one cannot necessarily apply to an animal a leniency with regard to a person. The Gemara responds: When it is stated about **an animal that it does not need to remain in a leprous house** for a minimal period of time, **with regard to what *halakha*** was that said? A living animal itself cannot be impure. Perforce, the statement is referring **to utensils that are** placed **upon its back,** and they are rendered impure immediately upon entry. But in this regard there is no difference between animals and people: **A person as well does not need** to remain in the leprous house for a minimal period of time in order to render impure any utensils placed on his back. Rather, they are rendered impure immediately upon entry. **This is** **as we learned** in a mishna (*Nega'im* 13:9): With regard to **one who enters a leprous house with his clothes** resting **on his shoulder** but who is not wearing them in the normal manner, **and his sandals and his rings** are **in his hands,** both **he and they,** the clothes, sandals, and rings, are rendered **ritually impure immediately.** If **he was dressed in his clothes, and his sandals** were **on his feet and his rings** were **on his fingers, he** is rendered **impure immediately** upon entering the house, **but they,** the clothes, sandals, and rings, remain **pure until he remains** in the house **for** the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread. This calculation is made with **wheat bread and not** with **barley bread,** which takes more time to eat than wheat bread. In addition, the calculation is made with regard to one who is **reclining, and eating** the bread together **with a relish** usually consumed with bread, which hastens the eating.

The Gemara counters that there is one domain in which an animal is *more* susceptible to *tumah* than a human, which might undermine the *kal vachomer*. The case involves a *beit hamenuga* — a house afflicted with *tzara'at*. The Torah teaches that one who enters such a house and eats there — which the *chazal* understand as meaning one who tarries there the duration of eating a *pras* (roughly half a loaf of wheat bread, eaten while reclining with a dip, at a leisurely pace — *rashi* measures this as eight eggs' volume, the *rambam* as nine) — causes even his worn garments to become *tamei*. If however one entered but did not tarry, one becomes *tamei* personally but the clothes one is wearing remain *tahor* until the required duration has passed. This special protection of worn garments applies specifically to human beings. Garments being carried (on the shoulder, in the hand) become *tamei* immediately — as the *mishnah* teaches: *hanichnas le-veit hamenuga*, if one entered carrying one's clothes, one's sandals in hand, and one's ring in hand rather than wearing them, then both the person and all these objects become *tamei mi-yad*. Only when wearing them — *haya lavush kelav, ve-sandalav beraglav, ve-tabato be-etzbao* — are the garments protected initially, becoming *tamei* only after tarrying.

This protection of worn garments does not apply to animals. If a *behemah* enters a *beit hamenuga*, whether it is wearing anything or not, the animal and everything on it become *tamei* immediately. In this respect, the animal is *less* protected than a human, and one might argue that this lesser protection undermines the *kal vachomer* — if the *behemah* is less shielded in this domain, perhaps it also cannot generate *bluah* protection for what is inside it.

### Tosafot Resolution


The Gemara's response is that the *beit hamenuga* case is not truly a domain where the animal is conceptually more *susceptible* to *tumah* — it is rather a structural difference in how *tumah* is transmitted *through* a human subject versus objects. The Gemara notes that the difference between worn and carried garments applies equally to humans: carried garments become *tamei mi-yad* for humans as well. The unique protection of worn garments exists because *tumah* reaches them *through* the person who wears them; they are treated as an extension of his subjectivity. An animal, by contrast, does not function as a halachic subject through whom *tumah* is mediated — everything associated with it, worn or carried, is treated as a direct object of *tumah*.

*Tosafot* draws out this point precisely: even if a *behemah* were wearing garments — say, a collar or a saddle or whatever constitutes apparel for an animal — those garments still become *tamei mi-yad*, because the *tumah* is considered to reach them directly, not through the animal as an intermediary subject. This is not evidence that the *behemah* is more susceptible *internally*; it reflects only the fact that the animal is not a legal subject through whose personality *tumah* is mediated. Since the *bluah* principle concerns what is *inside* the body — what is absorbed and enclosed — and not how external garments are affected, the *beit hamenuga* asymmetry is irrelevant to the *kal vachomer*. The fundamental point remains: a human, who is more susceptible to *tumah* in the personal sense, protects what is absorbed within him. A *behemah*, which is less susceptible, does so equally.

### Mishnaiyot Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: תַּרְוַיְיהוּ תְּנַנְהִי, טוּמְאָה בְּלוּעָה תְּנֵינָא, טׇהֳרָה בְּלוּעָה תְּנֵינָא! טוּמְאָה בְּלוּעָה – דִּתְנַן: בָּלַע טַבַּעַת טְמֵאָה, טוֹבֵל וְאוֹכֵל בִּתְרוּמָתוֹ; הֱקִיאָהּ – טְמֵאָה, וְטִמְּאַתּוּ. טׇהֳרָה בְּלוּעָה תְּנֵינָא, דִּתְנַן: בָּלַע טַבַּעַת טְהוֹרָה, וְנִכְנַס לְאֹהֶל הַמֵּת, וְהִזָּה וְשָׁנָה וְטָבַל וֶהֱקִיאָהּ – הֲרֵי הִיא כְּמָה שֶׁהָיְתָה. כִּי קָאָמַר רַבָּה, כְּגוֹן שֶׁבָּלַע שְׁתֵּי טַבָּעוֹת, אַחַת טְמֵאָה וְאַחַת טְהוֹרָה, דְּלָא מְטַמְּיָא לַהּ טְמֵאָה לִטְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** § Rabba stated above that an encapsulated impure item cannot impart impurity and that an item that is ritually pure that is encapsulated within another body cannot be rendered impure. **Rava said:** Why is this statement necessary? **We learn both** *halakhot* in a mishna: **We learn** about **an encapsulated impure** item, and **we learn** about **an encapsulated pure** item. Rava elaborates: With regard to **an encapsulated impure** item, the *halakha* is **as we learned** (*Mikvaot* 10:8): If someone **swallowed a ring** that was **impure** due to having been in contact with a corpse, he is thereby rendered impure. To render himself pure **he immerses and** then **he may partake of his *teruma*,** despite the fact that the impure ring is still inside him. The reason this is permitted is that since the ring in encapsulated inside the person's body it cannot impart impurity. If **he vomited** out this ring it remains **impure,** as it was not purified by his immersion, **and** therefore **it renders him impure** upon its exit from his body. Likewise, **we learn** the *halakha* that **an encapsulated pure** item cannot be rendered impure, **as we learn** in the same mishna (*Mikvaot* 10:8): If one **swallowed a ritually pure ring and** then **entered a tent containing a corpse,** thereby contracting impurity, **and** then in order to render him pure someone **sprinkled** upon him the water that is mixed with the ashes of the red heifer, once on the third day **and again** on the seventh day as required, **and** then **he immersed,** thereby completing the purification process; **and** then **he vomited** out the ring, the ring **is** pure **as it** always **was,** i.e., it was not rendered impure from the corpse. The Gemara answers: The cases in the mishna concern the possibility of imparting impurity from the ring to the body within which it is contained and vice versa. But **when Rabba was speaking** he was referring to imparting impurity from one item to another where both are contained together inside a body, **such as where** a person **swallowed two rings, one impure and one pure,** and they came in direct contact with one another. Rabba taught that even in this case the **impure** ring **does not render the pure** ring **impure.**

The Gemara closes this section with a remark that cuts the discussion short in a different way. *rava* had stated both *tumah bluah* and *tahara bluah* as if they required demonstration. The Gemara responds: *tarvaihu taninu* — both of them are explicit in *mishnayot*. *rava* need not have derived either principle.

For *tumah bluah*: *bala tabaat teme'ah* — someone swallowed a ring that had contracted *tumat meit* (having touched a corpse, and being metal, it is treated as the corpse itself — *cherev ke-chalal* — such that touching it would ordinarily confer seven-day *tumah*). This person *tovel ve-ochel bi-terumah* — immerses in the *mikvah*, completes the process of *taharah*, and may eat *terumah*, with no concern that the *tamei* ring in his stomach continues to cause *tumah*. And if he subsequently regurgitates it — *hetia, temeia* — the ring is still *tamei*; it never lost its *tumah*. It simply did not project that *tumah* while absorbed within him. This is an explicit *mishnah* proving *tumah bluah*.

For *tahara bluah*: *bala tabaat tehorah* — one swallowed a *tahor* ring. *ve-nichnas le-ohel ha-met* — then entered a room containing a corpse, through which *ohel* contamination one normally contracts *tumah*. The person underwent the complete process of *taharah* — *ve-hiza ve-shina ve-taval* — the sprinkling of *mei chatat* on the third and seventh days, followed by immersion. Then *ve-hitia* — he regurgitated the ring — *harei hi ke-mah she-hayata* — and it remains *tahor*, just as it was. The ring was never *metamei*, because while inside his body it was insulated from the *tumah* of the *ohel*. That is *tahara bluah*. Both principles, *rava's* statement notwithstanding, are fully documented in the *mishnayot* of *mikvaot*. The Gemara will address tomorrow why *rava* found it necessary to articulate what is already explicit in *mishnah*.
