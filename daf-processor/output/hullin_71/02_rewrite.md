# Hullin 71 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chayah & Behemah

### Intro: Inclusion

The Gemara on 71a opens by establishing, through explicit *psukim*, that the categories of *chayah* and *behemah* are mutually inclusive — each can fall under the rubric of the other depending on context. The question *chayah bichlal behemah minalan* — how do we know that *chayah* is included within *behemah* — is answered from the verse *zos habehemah asher tocheilu* (Devarim 14:4–5), which opens with the heading of *behemah* and proceeds to enumerate species such as *shor*, *seh chesavim*, and *seh izim* — ox, sheep, and goat — but then continues to list *ayal*, *tzvi*, and *yachmur* (deer, gazelle, and fallow deer), which are plainly *chayah*. The fact that the verse subsumes these *chayos* under the heading of *behemah* demonstrates that *behemah* can function as a generic term inclusive of *chayah*.

The converse is established from a separate verse: *v'zos hachayah asher tocheilu mikol habehemah asher al ha-aretz* — "and this is the *chayah* that you may eat from among all the *behemah* on the land" — which inverts the terminology, subsuming *behemah* under the heading of *chayah*. The *pshat* is clear: these words are not always category-specific; they function as general terms that can encompass each other. With this mutual inclusion established, the Gemara proceeds to identify four distinct halachic applications, two in each direction, encompassing both *tahor* and *tamei* species.

### Kosher Inclusion

The first application: *chayah tehorah bichlal behemah tehorah l'simanim* — a kosher *chayah* is subsumed under the category of kosher *behemah* with respect to the identifying signs of permitted consumption. The verse that specifies the *simanim* — split hooves and cud-chewing — frames them in terms of *behemah*. Since *chayah* is included within *behemah*, the same *simanim* apply to *chayah* as well, even though the verse does not enumerate them separately for wild animals.

### Non-Kosher Cross-breed

The second application concerns non-kosher animals: *chayah temeiah bichlal behemah temeiah l'harva'ah* — a non-kosher *chayah* is included within *behemah* for the prohibition of cross-breeding (*kilayim*). The verse *behemtecha lo tarbi'ah kilayim* prohibits cross-breeding one's *behemah*, but says nothing explicit about *chayah*. Since *chayah* is included in *behemah*, the prohibition extends to wild animals as well, whether of kosher or non-kosher species.

### Gezeirah Shavah

The third application reverses the direction: a non-kosher *behemah* is included within *chayah* — *behemah temeiah bichlal chayah temeiah* — and this is relevant to what Rebbi taught in a *braisa*. Rebbi asks: the verse that addresses the case of one who touches the corpse of a non-kosher animal and then enters the *beis hamikdash* or eats consecrated food — why does that verse mention both *chayah* and *behemah*? Since *chayah* already includes *behemah*, the explicit mention of *behemah* is redundant and therefore available for a *gezeirah shavah*. Rebbi leverages this: the word *behemah temeiah* appears here and also later in *vayikra* in the context of prohibiting the eating of *korbanos* while in a state of *tumah*. The shared language creates a *gezeirah shavah* — *mah lehalan tumas kodesh, af kan tumas kodesh* — teaching that the transgression under discussion is specifically eating sacred food while *tamei*. The case thus requires the mutual inclusion not as a source of new law, but as the precondition that renders the explicit mention of *behemah* in the first verse formally superfluous and available for the hermeneutic derivation.

### Formation & Miscarriage

The fourth application: *behemah tehorah bichlal chayah tehorah liyetzirah* — a kosher *behemah* is included within *chayah* with respect to *yetzirah*, the act of formation or creation. This rests on the verse in *bereishis*: *vayeitzer hashem elokim min ha'adamah kol chayas hasadeh v'es kol oph hashamayim* — God fashioned from the earth all the living creatures of the field and all the birds of the sky. The verse uses the word *chayah*, yet it plainly refers to all animals, including *behemah*. The halachic relevance emerges from a *mishnah* in *niddah*: *hamapeles min behemah chayah va'oph* — if a woman miscarries a fetus that resembles a *behemah*, a *chayah*, or a bird, whether of a *tahor* or *tamei* species, she is nonetheless treated as *temeiah leidah*. If the fetus appears male she observes the impurity of bearing a male child; if female, the longer impurity of a female; if indeterminate, she takes on both stringencies. This is *divrei Rebbi Meir*.

The reasoning of *rebbi meir* is that since all creatures — human, *chayah*, and *behemah* — are included together in the verse of *vayeitzer*, even a fetus resembling a non-human animal is considered a sufficient act of birth to impose *tumat leidah* on the mother. The question then arises: where does *rebbi meir* derive the inclusion specifically for a fetus that resembles a *behemah*, given that the *bereishis* verse mentions only *chayah* and *oph* explicitly? The answer is precisely the principle established above — *behemah bichlal chayah* — since *behemah* is encompassed within *chayah*, the verse in *bereishis* covers *behemah* as well.

The *chachamim* disagree, holding *kol she'eino mitzurat adam eino valad* — if it does not appear human, it does not impose *tumat leidah*. The Gemara notes that since the *chachamim* reject *rebbi meir's* ruling here, they must find another use for the verse demonstrating *behemah bichlal chayah* — and indeed, *rebbi* in the previous case (*lehalan*) uses it for the derivation concerning *tumah* of the non-kosher *behemah*.

What emerges from this entire sugya is that the mutual inclusion of *chayah* and *behemah* is not merely a lexicographic observation but has operative halachic consequences across domains ranging from the *simanim* of permitted eating, to the *kilayim* of cross-breeding, to the complex hermeneutics of *korbanos*, to the *tumah* status of a miscarried fetus. It also reflects a broader methodological disagreement between *rebbi yishmael*, who views the interchangeability of *chayah* and *behemah* as straightforward *pshat* — the *torah* simply speaks in general terms — and others who derive legal content from the same interchangeability through formal *drashos*.

## Tumah Bluah

### Fetus in Womb

The *mishnah* that brings us into the next sugya introduces a case that initially appears straightforward but conceals one of the more technically involved principles in these chapters: *ha'isha shemeit valadah betoch me'eha* — a woman whose fetus died in her womb. *ufashtah chayah et yadah* — the midwife (the *chayah*, literally "the one who gives life") extended her hand inside to assist in delivery, *venag'ah bo hachayah tmeiah tumat shivah* — and touched the dead fetus; she contracts seven-day *tumah* from corpse contact. *veha'isha tehorah* — and the mother remains *tehorah* until the fetus exits the womb.

The operative principle here is *tumah bluah* — literally, swallowed or absorbed *tumah*. Something enclosed within a body is not considered to exist in the wider world as an independent source of *tumah*. To appreciate this, consider the analogous case that the Gemara will develop: a *neveilah* — the corpse of a non-kosher animal or any animal that died without proper *shechita* — that has been swallowed and is sitting in a person's stomach. Does it continue to cause *tumah*? The answer is no: absorbed within a body, it is no longer present in the world in a halachically operative sense.

This principle explains the asymmetry in the *mishnah*. From the mother's perspective — and from the perspective of everyone outside — the fetus is *bluah*, enclosed and absorbed, and thus does not function as a corpse causing *tumah*. The midwife, however, has inserted her hand into that space and is making direct contact with the fetus. From *her* perspective, it is not *bluah* at all — she is accessing it and touching it. Hence she contracts *tumat shivah* while the mother remains *tehorah*.

One might ask: what about the space itself — if the midwife's hand is within the womb but not yet touching the fetus, should not the proximity within that enclosed space itself create *tumah*, given that a corpse ordinarily projects *tumah* to all persons sharing an *ohel*? This is a genuine question, and the *mishnah*'s formulation — emphasizing *touching* — implies that the *bluah* status is only removed when there is actual handling contact, not mere presence in the same enclosed space. Until the fetus is physically handled, it retains the status of *bluah*.

The question of how a midwife could know with certainty that the fetus was already dead at the moment of contact is addressed practically: in any case of doubt, the same *halachah* applies *misafek* — she would be *temeiah tumat shivah misafek*.

A striking dimension of this *mishnah* is its implicit position on the status of the unborn. However varied the halachic opinions on abortion may be — ranging from those who view a fetus as having virtually no independent status, through positions that recognize partial life, to the stringent view of *rav moshe feinstein* based on the *rambam* that considers abortion akin to a form of killing — this *mishnah* treats a fetus that dies in the womb as a *met* in the full sense, capable of conveying *tumat meit*. It does not say the fetus lacks corpse-status because it was never born alive. The question of how early in development this applies is separately addressed in the *mishnah* in *niddah*, where the threshold of *tumat meit* from a fetus requires that it have reached a sufficient stage of development — specifically, a head of a size comparable to the warp-spool — and not merely the 40-day mark that governs *tumat niddah* in cases of questionable discharge.

### Rava's Principle

*Amar Rava*: just as absorbed *tumah* does not cause *tumah* — *keshem she-tumah bluah einah metame* — so too *tahara bluah*, something *tahor* that is absorbed inside a person, does not become *tamei* — *kach tahara bluah eina mitame*. This is a paired principle: the absorption effect runs symmetrically in both directions.

### Neveilah Proof

The Gemara then asks: *tumah bluah minalan* — from where do we derive that absorbed *tumah* does not cause *tumah*? The source is the verse *ve-hochel mi-nivlatah yechabes begadav* — one who eats from a *neveilah* must immerse his garments. The Gemara reasons: are we not discussing a case where a person ate this *neveilah* close to sunset — *samuch lishkias hachamah* — immersed in the *mikvah*, and emerged *tahor*, even though the *neveilah* is still inside his stomach? *ve-amar rachmana tahor* — the Torah declares him *tahor*. Despite the continued presence of the *neveilah* within him, he is not *tamei*. This is the proof that absorbed *tumah* does not cause *tumah*.

Rashi makes a critical methodological observation here that will carry weight throughout the subsequent discussion. One might have thought the reason the person is not *tamei* is simply that he is not in contact with the *neveilah* — invoking the principle of *negiat beit hastarim*, that touching something with an internal part of the body (a hidden part not exposed externally) does not constitute halachic contact. If so, the proof would only establish the absence of *tumah* from non-contact, not the full principle of *tumah bluah*. Rashi explains, however, that this objection does not apply here, because a *neveilah* transmits *tumah* not only by touch but also by carrying — *masa*. If the *neveilah* in the stomach were still halachically operative, the person carrying it would be *tamei* without any touching at all. The fact that he is *tahor* therefore cannot be attributed to the absence of touch; it must reflect that the absorbed *neveilah* is simply no longer present in the world as a *neveilah*. Similarly, in our *mishnah*, the dead fetus projects *tumah* to all in the room if it is exposed — but while within the mother, it does not, precisely because it is *bluah*.

### Reuiah Le-Ger

The Gemara raises a challenge: perhaps the reason the *neveilah* in the stomach does not cause *tumah* is not the principle of *tumah bluah* at all, but rather because it is no longer *reuiah lager* — fit for the *ger* (the stranger or non-Jew in one's community). The Torah instructs *lager asher bisharecha titnena* — give the *neveilah* to the stranger in your midst. The designation of an animal carcass as a *neveilah* is tied to its edibility; if it is no longer something a person would consume, perhaps it loses its halachic status as a *neveilah* entirely, not because it is hidden but because it is no longer edible.

The Gemara resolves this by invoking the position of *rabbi yochanan*, who holds that *neveilah* retains its status so long as it is fit for a dog — *achad zu ve-achad zu l'kelev*. Fitness for canine consumption is the operative threshold for *tumah*; human edibility is not required. Since a *neveilah* inside a person's stomach, if regurgitated, would certainly be eaten by a dog, it retains its status as *neveilah*. The only reason it does not cause *tumah* while in the stomach, therefore, must be *tumah bluah*.

The Gemara further notes that even for the position of *bar pada* — who holds that *neveilah* loses its severe *tumah* once unfit for human consumption, though it retains a lesser *tumah* until unfit even for a dog — the objection can still be deflected. The Gemara argues: *ledidei delo chazi be'anpei, aval shelo bifanav mechazi chazi leih* — while a person would not eat it if they saw it laid out before them and knew what it was, if they were unaware of what they were consuming, there is no reason to assume it is literally unpalatable. The repulsion is aesthetic, not intrinsic. Thus the *neveilah* in the stomach is still halachically edible to the person carrying it, and its failure to cause *tumah* can only be explained through *tumah bluah*.

### From Below

Having established *tumah bluah* for the case of swallowing — absorption from above — the Gemara asks: *beluah dilmata minalan* — from where do we know that the same principle applies to absorption from below, i.e., something entering through the lower orifices, including the case of the fetus in the womb? The Gemara answers with a *kal vachomer*: if absorption from above — through the mouth, which is not the primary site of digestion — nevertheless grants *bluah* status and protects from *tumah*, then absorption from below — *she'oseh ichul*, where the digestive process is more intensive — certainly should. The Gemara anticipates the objection that digestion below only occurs because of what came from above, and responds that despite that dependency, *ichul dilmata nafish* — the degree of processing that occurs further down the digestive tract is in any case greater, making the *kal vachomer* stand.

### In Behemah

With the principle of *tumah bluah* established for human beings — both from above and from below — the Gemara extends the inquiry: *beluah debeheimah minalan* — how do we know that something absorbed within an animal also has *bluah* status? This is directly relevant to the preceding *mishnah* about the fetus in an animal's womb, where the shepherd who inserted a hand and touched the fetus became *tamei* while the mother did not. The Gemara answers again with a *kal vachomer*: *umah adam shemetame mechayim matzil be-valua* — a human being, who is susceptible to *tumah* even while alive (through *zivah*, *baal keri*, *niddah*, *yoledet*, *metzora*, and the like), nevertheless protects things absorbed within him from operative *tumah*. *behemah she-einah metamma'ah mechayim* — an animal, which does not transmit *tumah* while alive, is far less susceptible to *tumah* overall. *eino din she-tatzil be-valua* — how much more so should it protect things absorbed within it.

### Tzaraat House

The Gemara counters that there is one domain in which an animal is *more* susceptible to *tumah* than a human, which might undermine the *kal vachomer*. The case involves a *beit hamenuga* — a house afflicted with *tzara'at*. The Torah teaches that one who enters such a house and eats there — which the *chazal* understand as meaning one who tarries there the duration of eating a *pras* (roughly half a loaf of wheat bread, eaten while reclining with a dip, at a leisurely pace — *rashi* measures this as eight eggs' volume, the *rambam* as nine) — causes even his worn garments to become *tamei*. If however one entered but did not tarry, one becomes *tamei* personally but the clothes one is wearing remain *tahor* until the required duration has passed. This special protection of worn garments applies specifically to human beings. Garments being carried (on the shoulder, in the hand) become *tamei* immediately — as the *mishnah* teaches: *hanichnas le-veit hamenuga*, if one entered carrying one's clothes, one's sandals in hand, and one's ring in hand rather than wearing them, then both the person and all these objects become *tamei mi-yad*. Only when wearing them — *haya lavush kelav, ve-sandalav beraglav, ve-tabato be-etzbao* — are the garments protected initially, becoming *tamei* only after tarrying.

This protection of worn garments does not apply to animals. If a *behemah* enters a *beit hamenuga*, whether it is wearing anything or not, the animal and everything on it become *tamei* immediately. In this respect, the animal is *less* protected than a human, and one might argue that this lesser protection undermines the *kal vachomer* — if the *behemah* is less shielded in this domain, perhaps it also cannot generate *bluah* protection for what is inside it.

### Tosafot Resolution

The Gemara's response is that the *beit hamenuga* case is not truly a domain where the animal is conceptually more *susceptible* to *tumah* — it is rather a structural difference in how *tumah* is transmitted *through* a human subject versus objects. The Gemara notes that the difference between worn and carried garments applies equally to humans: carried garments become *tamei mi-yad* for humans as well. The unique protection of worn garments exists because *tumah* reaches them *through* the person who wears them; they are treated as an extension of his subjectivity. An animal, by contrast, does not function as a halachic subject through whom *tumah* is mediated — everything associated with it, worn or carried, is treated as a direct object of *tumah*.

*Tosafot* draws out this point precisely: even if a *behemah* were wearing garments — say, a collar or a saddle or whatever constitutes apparel for an animal — those garments still become *tamei mi-yad*, because the *tumah* is considered to reach them directly, not through the animal as an intermediary subject. This is not evidence that the *behemah* is more susceptible *internally*; it reflects only the fact that the animal is not a legal subject through whose personality *tumah* is mediated. Since the *bluah* principle concerns what is *inside* the body — what is absorbed and enclosed — and not how external garments are affected, the *beit hamenuga* asymmetry is irrelevant to the *kal vachomer*. The fundamental point remains: a human, who is more susceptible to *tumah* in the personal sense, protects what is absorbed within him. A *behemah*, which is less susceptible, does so equally.

### Mishnaiyot Evidence

The Gemara closes this section with a remark that cuts the discussion short in a different way. *rava* had stated both *tumah bluah* and *tahara bluah* as if they required demonstration. The Gemara responds: *tarvaihu taninu* — both of them are explicit in *mishnayot*. *rava* need not have derived either principle.

For *tumah bluah*: *bala tabaat teme'ah* — someone swallowed a ring that had contracted *tumat meit* (having touched a corpse, and being metal, it is treated as the corpse itself — *cherev ke-chalal* — such that touching it would ordinarily confer seven-day *tumah*). This person *tovel ve-ochel bi-terumah* — immerses in the *mikvah*, completes the process of *taharah*, and may eat *terumah*, with no concern that the *tamei* ring in his stomach continues to cause *tumah*. And if he subsequently regurgitates it — *hetia, temeia* — the ring is still *tamei*; it never lost its *tumah*. It simply did not project that *tumah* while absorbed within him. This is an explicit *mishnah* proving *tumah bluah*.

For *tahara bluah*: *bala tabaat tehorah* — one swallowed a *tahor* ring. *ve-nichnas le-ohel ha-met* — then entered a room containing a corpse, through which *ohel* contamination one normally contracts *tumah*. The person underwent the complete process of *taharah* — *ve-hiza ve-shina ve-taval* — the sprinkling of *mei chatat* on the third and seventh days, followed by immersion. Then *ve-hitia* — he regurgitated the ring — *harei hi ke-mah she-hayata* — and it remains *tahor*, just as it was. The ring was never *metamei*, because while inside his body it was insulated from the *tumah* of the *ohel*. That is *tahara bluah*. Both principles, *rava's* statement notwithstanding, are fully documented in the *mishnayot* of *mikvaot*. The Gemara will address tomorrow why *rava* found it necessary to articulate what is already explicit in *mishnah*.
