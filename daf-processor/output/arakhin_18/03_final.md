# Arakhin 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Poverty Timing

### Tanna Kamma & R. Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָיָה עָנִי וְהֶעֱשִׁיר, עָשִׁיר וְהֶעֱנִי — נוֹתֵן עֵרֶךְ עָשִׁיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עָנִי וְהֶעֱשִׁיר וְחָזַר וְהֶעֱנִי — נוֹתֵן עֵרֶךְ עָשִׁיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If when one took a vow of valuation **he was destitute and he became wealthy,** or if **he was wealthy and became destitute, he gives the valuation** in accordance with the means of **a wealthy person. Rabbi Yehuda says:** This is the *halakha* not only in a case where one was wealthy either at the time he took the vow or at the time of payment; even if when one took a vow of valuation **he was destitute and he became wealthy and again became destitute, he gives** the **valuation** in accordance with the means of **a wealthy person.**

The *daf* begins on 17b, picking up a *machlokes* in the *Mishnah* about the *din* in *erchin* concerning the poor person's discount. Ordinarily, the *Torah* assigns a fixed amount when one declares an *erech* — a donation to the *Mikdash* based on the fixed price schedule for different age ranges. However, when the pledger cannot afford the fixed amount, the *kohen* assesses what the person can actually pay. The dispute in the *Mishnah* centers on *when* one must be poor in order to qualify for this reduced assessment. The *Tanna Kamma* holds that one must be poor both at the time the *erech* is declared and at the time of payment. Rabbi Yehuda, however, holds that one must be poor for the *entire interval* from the declaration until the payment in order to qualify for the discount.

### Vehu Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל בַּקׇּרְבָּנוֹת אֵינוֹ כֵּן, אֲפִילּוּ מֵת אָבִיו וְהִנִּיחַ לוֹ רִיבּוֹא, אוֹ סְפִינָתוֹ בַּיָּם וּבָאָה לוֹ בְּרִיבּוֹאוֹת — אֵין לַהֶקְדֵּשׁ בָּהּ כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **But with regard to** the **offerings** of a leper **that is not so,** as the offerings that one brings are determined by his status at the time he brings them. **Even** if it is common knowledge that **his father died and left him** an inheritance of **ten thousand** dinars, **or** that **his ship is at sea and** merchandise valued at **ten thousand** dinars **is coming into his** possession, **the Temple** treasury **has no** share **in it.** His payment is determined solely by his present situation.

The *Gemara* seeks the source for Rabbi Yehuda's position. The proposed source is the word *vehu* — "and he" — which appears in the relevant *pasuk* and which Rabbi Yehuda reads as indicating that the condition must persist across the entire interval. The *Gemara* then proceeds to examine every other instance in the *Torah* where the word *vehu* appears, asking whether Rabbi Yehuda would apply the same interval-spanning logic in each case.

### Shebuot Ha'edut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, ״וְהוּא עַד״, עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא כָּשֵׁר מִתְּחִלָּתוֹ וְעַד סוֹפוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **If that is so,** that Rabbi Yehuda maintains that the phrase: "And if he is poor," teaches that one is classified as destitute only if he is destitute from beginning to end, he should similarly derive from a verse that deals with testimony: **"And he is a witness,** whether he has seen or known, if he does not utter it, then he shall bear his iniquity" (Leviticus 5:1), that this applies **only if** the witness **is fit** to testify **from his beginning,** when he obtained the information about which he is testifying, **until his end,** when he testifies.

The case under examination at the opening of our *daf* is *shevu'at ha'edut* — the oath of testimony. If a litigant claims that someone possesses information relevant to his court case and that person denies it, the litigant may compel that person to take an oath declaring that he knows nothing. However, this mechanism only applies if the person is a *kosher* witness. The *Gemara* asks: following Rabbi Yehuda's logic, would *vehu* require that the potential witness be *kosher* for the entire interval from the moment he allegedly witnessed the event until the moment he would give testimony in court?

### Witness Disqualifications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא הָכִי נָמֵי, וְהָא תַּנְיָא: הָיָה יוֹדֵעַ לוֹ בְּעֵדוּת עַד שֶׁלֹּא נַעֲשָׂה חֲתָנוֹ, וְנַעֲשָׂה חֲתָנוֹ, פִּקֵּחַ וְנִתְחָרֵשׁ, פָּתוּחַ וְנִסְתַּמֵּא, שָׁפוּי וְנִשְׁתַּטָּה — הֲרֵי זֶה פָּסוּל. אֲבָל הָיָה יוֹדֵעַ לוֹ עֵדוּת עַד שֶׁלֹּא נַעֲשָׂה חֲתָנוֹ, וְנַעֲשָׂה חֲתָנוֹ,
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that **indeed,** that is the *halakha* with regard to the qualification of a witness, **but isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If someone **knew testimony about** another **before he,** the one who knew the testimony, **became** the other's **son-in-law, and** then he **became his son-in-law;** or when he was **able to hear, and** then he **became a deaf-mute;** or when he **could see, and** he subsequently **became blind;** or while he was halakhically **competent, and** he then **became an imbecile;** in all these cases, **he is disqualified** from testifying. **But** if someone **knew testimony about** another **before he became his son-in-law, and** he then **became his son-in-law,**

A *Braisa* is then cited that enumerates cases of disqualification. If a man knew testimony before becoming someone's son-in-law and then became that person's son-in-law — thereby rendering himself *posul le'edus* for that case — or if he was able to hear and speak and became deaf-mute, or was sighted and became blind (Rashi notes there is a special *gezeiras hakasuv* that a blind person is *posul le'edus*), or was sane and became insane, then in each of these cases he is invalid and cannot be compelled to take *shevu'as ha'edut*. So far, this appears consistent with Rabbi Yehuda's requirement of uninterrupted qualification.

### Temporary Disqualification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵתָה בִּתּוֹ, פִּקֵּחַ וְנִתְחָרֵשׁ וְחָזַר וְנִתְפַּקֵּחַ, פָּתוּחַ וְנִסְתַּמֵּא וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִתְפַּתֵּחַ, שָׁפוּי וְנִשְׁתַּטָּה וְחָזַר וְנִשְׁתַּפָּה — כָּשֵׁר. זֶה הַכְּלָל: כֹּל שֶׁתְּחִילָּתוֹ וְסוֹפוֹ בְּכַשְׁרוּת — כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **and afterward his** wife, who was the **daughter** of the father-in-law, **died,** which means that the witness is no longer related to the party involved; or when he was **able to hear, and** then **became a deaf-mute, and again became able to hear;** or when he **could see, and** subsequently **became blind, and afterward could see** again; or when he was halakhically **competent, and** then **became an imbecile, and again became** halakhically **competent;** in all these cases he is **fit** to testify.**This is the principle: Any** individual **whose beginning and end** is **in** a state of **qualification** to serve as a witness is **qualified** to testify, even if he was unfit in the interim. Evidently, it is not derived from the verse: "And he is a witness," that the witness must be fit from the beginning to the end.

However, the *Braisa* continues with more difficult cases. Suppose the person was *kosher* at the time he witnessed the event, then became disqualified — for instance, he became someone's son-in-law, or became a deaf-mute, or went blind, or became insane — and then the disqualification was removed: his son-in-law status ended because the daughter died, or he regained his hearing and speech, or his sight was restored, or he recovered his sanity. In all such cases, the *Braisa* rules *kosher* — the litigant may compel him to take the oath. The *Braisa* states the general principle: *kol shetechilaso v'sofo b'kashrus* — whenever the beginning and the end are in a state of qualification, the person is valid. This appears to pose a direct challenge to Rabbi Yehuda's position, since the person was not *kosher* throughout the entire interval.

### Gezeirat Hakasuv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״אוֹ רָאָה... אִם לֹא יַגִּיד״, בִּרְאִיָּיה וְהַגָּדָה תְּלָא רַחְמָנָא מִילְּתָא, וְהָא אִיכָּא. וְאֶלָּא ״וְהוּא״ לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: The *halakha* **is different there,** with regard to testimony, **as the verse states:** "He is a witness, **whether he has seen**…**if he does not utter"** (Leviticus 5:1). This formulation indicates that **the Merciful One renders the matter** of testimony **dependent on seeing and recounting** the content of his testimony. **And** in this instance **there is** both valid seeing and valid recounting, despite the fact that the witness was disqualified in the interim. The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **why do I** need the exclusion of the verse: **"And he** is a witness"?

The *Gemara* resolves the difficulty: *shani hatam*, the case of *shevu'as ha'edut* is different, because there is a special *gezeiras hakasuv*. The *pasuk* reads *o ra'ah o lo yagid* — the Gemara deduces from the juxtaposition: *b're'iyah v'hagadah talah rachmana milta*. The *Torah* specifically anchors the obligation to two moments — the act of witnessing (*re'iyah*) and the act of testifying (*hagadah*) — and only those two moments are halachically decisive. This is a particular scriptural decree unique to *shevu'as ha'edut*, and therefore one cannot draw any general inference about the word *vehu* from this case.

### Vehu & Specification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְכִדְתַנְיָא: רָאָה סִיאָה שֶׁל בְּנֵי אָדָם עוֹמְדִין, וְעֵידָיו בֵּינֵיהֶן, וְאָמַר ״מַשְׁבִּיעַנִי עֲלֵיכֶם אִם יוֹדְעִים אַתֶּם לִי עֵדוּת שֶׁתָּבוֹאוּ וּתְעִידוּנִי״, יָכוֹל יְהוּ חַיָּיבִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְהוּא עֵד״, וַהֲרֵי לֹא יִיחֵד עֵידָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The limiting clause is necessary **for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: If someone **saw a crowd of people standing, and his witnesses** were **among them, and he said: I** hereby **administer an oath to you, if you know any testimony** relating **to me, that you will come and testify for me,** one **might** have thought that this form of address suffices to single out the witnesses. This would mean that if the witnesses take a false oath that they do not know testimony with regard to the person who addressed them, they **would be obligated** to bring an offering of an oath of testimony. Therefore, **the verse states: "And he is a witness,"** to emphasize that the offering applies only to one who is singled out as a witness, **and** in this case the individual **did not single out his witnesses,** as he addressed a whole crowd of people. Consequently, the witnesses are exempt.

Since the rule in *shevu'as ha'edut* is derived entirely from the special *gezeiras hakasuv* of *o ra'ah*, the word *vehu* is freed from that context and must be teaching something else. The *Gemara* cites a *Braisa*: if someone sees a large group of people and says to all of them collectively, "I make all of you take an oath — if any of you knows testimony relevant to my case, come forward," one might have thought they are all thereby obligated. The word *vehu* — "and *he* is a witness" — teaches that one must specify the individual: the obligation of *shevu'as ha'edut* cannot be triggered by addressing a group indiscriminately. However, one might then think that even saying "whoever among you knows" is also insufficient specification. Therefore, the word *ed* after *vehu* teaches that if one does indicate "whoever among you," that is sufficient — what is prohibited is only addressing the group as a collective whole, but singling out any individual, even by implication, is adequate.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ אָמַר ״כׇּל מִי״? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְהוּא עֵד״, וַהֲרֵי יִיחֵד עֵידָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought that **even** if this individual **said** to the crowd: I adjure **whoever** knows testimony relating to me that he will come and testify for me, that even in the case of this more specific address the witnesses are likewise exempt from the offering of an oath of testimony. Therefore, **the verse states: "And he is a witness," and** by clarifying his appeal the individual **did single out his witnesses.** Consequently, in this situation the witnesses would be obligated to bring the offering.

## Korbanot vs Erchin

### Korban Metzora Rules

The *Mishnah* then states: *aval b'korbanos eino kein* — but for *korbanos*, the rule is not the same. The interval-based poverty assessment that governs *erchin* does not apply to *korbanos*. This refers specifically to *korban metzora*, which is the only *korban* that follows a tiered structure — the *korban oleh v'yored* — where a wealthier person brings a different offering than a poor person. For *korban metzora*, wealth is assessed solely at the moment of bringing the offering; one does not look at any prior interval. The *Mishnah* gives two illustrative cases: if one's father died and left a substantial inheritance, or if one has a ship at sea worth tens of thousands, neither of these counts toward the wealth assessment.

### Inheritance Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָבִיו מֵת וְהִנִּיחַ לוֹ רִיבּוֹא — עָשִׁיר הוּא! אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, אֵימָא: מַנִּיחַ לוֹ רִיבּוֹא.
>
> **Translation:** If the leper is destitute, even if his father died and left him ten thousand dinars, the Temple treasury has no share in it. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If his father** already **died and left him ten thousand** dinars, **he is wealthy.** How could he be considered destitute at all? **Rabbi Abbahu said** that one should **say** instead: His father is dying and **leaving him ten thousand** dinars, but he has not yet died.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! כְּשֶׁהָיָה אָבִיו גּוֹסֵס. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: רוֹב גּוֹסְסִין לְמִיתָה? קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a further difficulty: If so, it is **obvious** that with regard to the Temple treasury he remains destitute, as at the time he is in fact destitute. The Gemara answers: The *halakha* was stated with regard to a case **where his father is moribund. Lest you say** that as **the majority of moribund** people proceed **to die** the son should be considered wealthy even before the father dies, the mishna therefore **teaches us** that the son retains his status as destitute until the father actually dies.

The *Gemara* asks on the first case: *aviv meis v'hiniach lo ribo* — why state this? If your father is wealthy but you are not, is it not obvious that you are poor and cannot be assessed as wealthy? Rav Papa clarifies: the case must be discussing a father who is *goses* — in the final stages of dying. One might have thought that since *rov gosesin lemita* — virtually all *gosesim* die imminently, to the point where the *Gemara* notes that the correct formulation is essentially *kol gosesin lemita*, as a *goses* by definition is irreversibly dying within days — we should treat the inheritance as already effectively in the son's possession. The *Gemara* teaches *ka mashma lan* that we do not; we assess only what is presently in one's actual possession.

### Ship Rental Income

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְפִינָתוֹ בַּיָּם וּבָאָה לוֹ בְּרִיבּוֹאוֹת, עָשִׁיר הוּא! אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: כְּשֶׁהָיְתָה מוּחְכֶּרֶת וּמוּשְׂכֶּרֶת בְּיַד אֲחֵרִים. וְהָאִיכָּא שְׂכִירוּת? שְׂכִירוּת אֵינָהּ מִשְׁתַּלֶּמֶת אֶלָּא לְבַסּוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna likewise teaches: If **his ship is at sea and** merchandise valued at **ten thousand** dinars **is coming into his** possession, this money is not taken into consideration by the Temple treasury. The Gemara asks: But isn't **he wealthy? Rav Ḥisda said:** The mishna is referring to a case where his ship **was leased or rented to others,** and therefore the merchandise it contains belongs to someone else. The Gemara asks: **But isn't there** the **rent** money that will be paid to the leper? The Gemara answers: **Rent is paid only at the end** of the rental period, which means that the owner is destitute in the meantime.

The second case — a ship at sea generating tens of thousands in income — similarly seems obvious: your assets are simply inaccessible. Rav Chisda clarifies that the case is one where the ship is rented out to others and the tens of thousands refer not to cargo but to rental fees owed to the owner. The *Gemara* then asks: but does the person not in fact have the rental income? The *Gemara* answers: *schirut eina mishtalemet ela levasof* — rental fees were not payable until the conclusion of the rental period. Since the fees are not yet due, the person does not presently have them and cannot be assessed as wealthy on their account.

One might ask whether it matters that the fees will eventually accrue: if someone is legally owed money right now and could in theory pursue it immediately, should that not count as current wealth? The answer is that the present case involves fees that are not actionable until the end of the rental period — they simply have not come due yet. The related question of what the law would be for fees that are currently actionable but simply uncollected remains less clear, and the *Gemara* does not directly resolve it here. Note that the rule *schirut eina mishtalemet ela levasof* is itself a matter of *machloket* in other contexts, and the present ruling follows the opinion that rental payments accrue only at the end of the term.

### Boat Ownership Value

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם סְפִינָה! הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא, דִּתְנַן: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר, אִם הָיָה אִיכָּר — נוֹתֵן לוֹ צִמְדּוֹ, וְחַמָּר — נוֹתֵן לוֹ חֲמוֹרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another difficulty: **But let** the mishna **derive** and determine that the leper is wealthy **due to** the fact that he owns **a ship.** The Gemara answers: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** statement in the mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** who maintains that with regard to determining one's obligation to bring an offering as a wealthy or destitute individual, the property that he uses for business is not taken into account. **As we learned** in a mishna (23b): When someone's property is repossessed in order to pay his debt to the Temple treasury, **Rabbi Eliezer says: If he was a farmer,** the treasurer **gives him** permission to keep **his pair** of oxen with which he plows the field. If he was **a donkey driver,** the treasurer **gives him** permission to keep **his donkey.**

The *Gemara* then raises a further point: even if the rental fees are not yet owed, the owner still holds title to the boat itself. Should not the value of the boat as an asset render him wealthy?

The *Gemara* answers: *ha mani* — whose opinion does our *Mishnah* follow? It is that of Rabbi Eliezer, as we learn from a later *Mishnah* in the tractate: Rabbi Eliezer holds that when assessing a person's wealth for these purposes, we do not count a farmer's plow-animals or a donkey-driver's donkey. Rabbi Eliezer's principle is that the primary tools of one's livelihood are not included in the wealth assessment — we do not strip a person of the means by which he earns his living. Accordingly, the boat, as the owner's instrument of income, would similarly be excluded under Rabbi Eliezer's position, and the *Mishnah* reflecting that view would not count the ship's value toward the assessment.

## Age-Based Pricing

### Age of Valued Person

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשָּׁנִים בַּנִּידָּר, כֵּיצַד? יֶלֶד שֶׁהֶעֱרִיךְ זָקֵן — נוֹתֵן עֵרֶךְ זָקֵן, וְזָקֵן שֶׁהֶעֱרִיךְ אֶת הַיֶּלֶד — נוֹתֵן עֵרֶךְ יֶלֶד. הָעֲרָכִין בַּנֶּעֱרָךְ, כֵּיצַד? אִישׁ שֶׁהֶעֱרִיךְ הָאִשָּׁה — נוֹתֵן עֵרֶךְ אִשָּׁה, וְאִשָּׁה שֶׁהֶעֱרִיכָה אִישׁ — נוֹתֶנֶת עֵרֶךְ אִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The sum fixed by the Torah based on **the years** of age is **in accordance with** the age of **the subject of the vow; how** so? **A youth who valuated an elder gives the valuation of an elder, and an elder who valuated a youth gives the valuation of a youth. And the** distinction based on sex that is written in the *halakhot* of **valuations** is stated **with regard to the one valuated; how** so? **A man who valuated a woman gives the valuation of a woman, and a woman who valuated a man gives the valuation of a man.**

The *Mishnah* now turns to the mechanics of how *erchin* valuations are calculated. The *Torah* provides a price schedule based on age, and the *Mishnah* establishes that it is the age of the person being valued — the *ne'erach* — that determines the applicable price, not the age of the person making the pledge. The *Mishnah* illustrates: if a younger person (*yeled*, meaning here an adult between twenty and sixty) pledges the *erech* of an older person (*zaken*, meaning over sixty), he pays the price for the *zaken*; and if an older person pledges the *erech* of a younger person, he pays the price for the *yeled*.

### Gender Determines Price

The *Mishnah* similarly states: *ha-erchin be-ne'erach* — the *erchin* valuation follows the person being assessed. If a man pledges the *erech* of a woman, he pays the price for a woman; if a woman pledges the *erech* of a man, she pays the price for a man.

### Time of Pledge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָעֵרֶךְ בִּזְמַן הָעֵרֶךְ, כֵּיצַד? הֶעֱרִיכוֹ פָּחוּת מִבֶּן חָמֵשׁ, וְנַעֲשֶׂה יָתֵר עַל בֶּן חָמֵשׁ, פָּחוּת מִבֶּן עֶשְׂרִים, וְנַעֲשָׂה יָתֵר עַל בֶּן עֶשְׂרִים — נוֹתֵן בִּזְמַן הָעֵרֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And the** different **valuation** based on the age of the one valuated is determined **at the time** one takes **the** vow of **valuation; how** so? If **one valuated** another when he was **less than five years old,** when his valuation is five shekels, **and** before payment to the Temple treasury the subject of the vow **became more than five years old,** when his valuation is ten shekels; or if **one valuated** another when he was **less than twenty years old,** when his valuation is ten shekels, **and** before payment to the Temple treasury the subject of the vow **became more than twenty years old,** when his valuation is fifty shekels, in all these cases **he gives** payment according to the age of the subject of the valuation **at the time of the valuation.**

The *Mishnah* further rules: the valuation follows the moment the pledge was made, not the moment of payment. If someone pledged the *erech* of a person who was under five years old at the time, and by the time of payment that person had already turned five — or was between five and twenty at the time of pledge and turned twenty before payment, or was under sixty and turned sixty before payment — in each case the pledger pays based on the age category at the *time of the pledge*, not at the time of payment.

### Gezeirah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אַתָּה הִקַּשְׁיתָּ דָּמִים לַעֲרָכִין, מַרְגָּלִית לְקַלִּים, וְלִידּוֹן בִּכְבוֹדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the statement of the mishna: And the valuation is determined at the time one takes the vow of valuation, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **You** have **compared** pledges of **assessments** for the Temple treasury, i.e., one who takes a vow to donate a certain person's assessment, **to** vows of **valuations.** This comparison applies with regard to **a pearl** belonging **to light** people, i.e., the poor. In other words, just as the assessment of a pearl is determined by the pearl's location in a village of the poor rather than by the potential price of the pearl in the city market, so too, the valuation of a person is determined at the time of the valuation. **And** similarly, the comparison teaches that the court is required **to assess** the value of the limb **by its significance,** i.e., in the case of a limb that is vital to one's survival, the valuation of the limb is equivalent to the valuation of the whole person.

The *Gemara* introduces a *Braisa* that explains why this rule requires a scriptural source. The *Torah* elsewhere establishes a *hekesh* between *erchin* and other *nedarim* — specifically, *damim*, the type of vow where one pledges the market value of a person as opposed to the fixed *erech* schedule. From this *hekesh* we derive two laws: first, that when a person must sell property to fulfill a *neder*, we do not force him to travel to the city to obtain a higher price — he may sell locally, *margalit l'kalim*, the easiest way. Second, that if the *neder* concerned a limb that is indispensable to life — such as one's head — the entire value of the person is owed, *lidon b'chvodo*.

One might have thought to extend the *hekesh* further and conclude that just as with *nedarim* the obligation is assessed at the time of payment, so too with *erchin* the valuation should follow the time of payment. After all, a *neder* of market value is assessed based on what the person is worth at the time he pays, not at the time he made the vow.

### Keerkecha Yakum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל נַקִּישׁ עֲרָכִין לְדָמִים, שֶׁיִּתֵּן כִּשְׁעַת נְתִינָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כְּעֶרְכְּךָ כֵּן יָקוּם״, אֵינוֹ נוֹתֵן אֶלָּא בִּזְמַן הָעֵרֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that **we should compare valuations to assessments** in **that** the one pledging must **give** a person's valuation not corresponding to his value at the time of the pledge but **in accordance with** his value at **the time of giving,** as is the case with regard to vows of assessments. To counter this suggestion **the verse states: "According to your valuation it shall stand"** (Leviticus 27:17), which teaches that the one making the valuation **gives** his donation **only** according to the valuation of the person **at the time of the valuation,** as stated in the mishna.

The *Gemara* therefore cites the *pasuk*: *k'erkecha yakum* — "as your *erech*, so shall it stand." The word *k'erkecha* teaches that the *erech* is fixed at the moment the pledge is made: *eino noten ela bizman ha-erech*. The *erech* valuation, unlike a *neder*, does not shift with changing circumstances at the time of payment. This is why our *Mishnah*'s rule, though it might initially have appeared counterintuitive given the *hekesh* to *nedarim*, follows the time of the pledge.

## Boundary Age Cases

### Thirtieth Day Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ יוֹם שְׁלֹשִׁים כִּלְמַטָּה הֵימֶנּוּ, שְׁנַת חָמֵשׁ וּשְׁנַת עֶשְׂרִים כִּלְמַטָּה מֵהֶם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאִם מִבֶּן שִׁשִּׁים שָׁנָה וָמַעְלָה״, הֲרֵי אָנוּ לְמֵדִים בְּכוּלָּן מִשְׁנַת שִׁשִּׁים, מָה שְׁנַת שִׁשִּׁים כִּלְמַטָּה הֵימֶנָּה, אַף שְׁנַת חָמֵשׁ וּשְׁנַת עֶשְׂרִים כִּלְמַטָּה הֵימֶנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The Torah provides three age categories that determine the amount of the valuation: From the age of one month until age five, from age five until age twenty, and from age twenty until age sixty. For anyone less than one month old there is no valuation. The halakhic status of the **thirtieth day** is **like** that of the period **preceding** thirty days, and therefore the one who took the vow is exempt. Likewise, the halakhic status of the **fifth year and** the **twentieth year** is **like** that of the period **preceding them. As it is stated: "And if it is from sixty years old and upward"** (Leviticus 27:7), and **we derive all** the other age categories **from** the **sixtieth year: Just as** the halakhic status of the **sixtieth year,** where upward is written, is **like** that of the period **preceding it, so too,** the halakhic status of the **fifth year and** the **twentieth year** is **like** that of the period **preceding them.**

The *Mishnah* now addresses what happens at the boundary points between the age categories. The age categories for *erchin* are: under thirty days (for which there is no *erech* at all), thirty days to five years, five years to twenty years, and twenty years to sixty years, and above sixty. The *Mishnah* rules: *yom shloshim k'lemata heimena* — the thirtieth day itself counts with the prior period (i.e., below thirty days), not with the period that begins at thirty days. In practice, the thirty-first day is the first day of the next period.

### Birthdays with Younger

The *Mishnah* similarly rules that the fifth birthday and the twentieth birthday each count with the younger period. So the fifth birthday itself is still assessed at the lower rate, and only the day *after* the fifth birthday enters the higher category. The same applies at twenty.

### Shishim Pasuk Source

The *Mishnah* derives this principle from the *pasuk*: *v'im ben shishim shana v'ma'ala* — "and if he is sixty years and above." The *Mishnah* reads this as teaching that only from above sixty — meaning, the day after the sixtieth birthday — does one enter the higher (or in this case, lower-priced) category. The sixtieth birthday itself therefore counts with the younger period. This is read as a *chumra*: since the price drops at sixty, counting the birthday with the younger, higher-priced period means paying more.

### Binyan Av

From this ruling regarding the sixtieth birthday, the *Mishnah* makes a *binyan av* extending the same principle to all of the other age boundaries: just as the sixtieth birthday counts with the prior period, so too the twentieth and fifth birthdays count with their respective prior periods. However, the *Mishnah* immediately raises an objection to this *binyan av*: the sixty-year boundary goes with the younger period *lechumra* — you pay a higher price. But the twenty-year and five-year boundaries go with the younger period *lekula* — the younger price is lower. One cannot derive a general rule of "always count the birthday with the prior period" from a case where doing so is a *chumra* and then apply it to cases where doing so is a *kula*. Perhaps the correct rule is simply that the birthday always goes *lechumra*.

The *Mishnah* therefore abandons the *binyan av* approach and instead invokes a *gezeirah shavah*. Each of the relevant *pesukim* uses the word *shana* — "year." Since *esrim shana* and *shishim shana* both contain the word *shana*, we apply a *gezeirah shavah*: just as by the sixtieth year the word *shana* indicates that the birthday counts with the prior period, so too by the twentieth year and the fifth year the word *shana* carries the same implication. The *gezeirah shavah* thus extends the rule symmetrically, applicable both *lehachmir* and *lehakeil*, independent of which direction the price moves.

### Thirty Days Gap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵן? אִם עָשָׂה שְׁנַת שִׁשִּׁים כִּלְמַטָּה מִמֶּנָּה לְהַחְמִיר, נַעֲשֶׂה שְׁנַת חָמֵשׁ וּשְׁנַת עֶשְׂרִים כִּלְמַטָּה מִמֶּנָּה לְהָקֵל?
>
> **Translation:** The mishna asks: Is that **so?** Can one derive a *halakha* in this manner? **If** the Torah **rendered** the halakhic status of the **sixtieth year like** that of the period **preceding it** in order **to be stringent** and require one who valuated a sixty-year-old person to pay his valuation to the Temple treasury, **shall we render** the halakhic status of the **fifth year and** the **twentieth year like** that of the period **preceding** them in order **to be lenient** and pay a lower sum?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״שָׁנָה״ ״שָׁנָה״ לִגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה, מָה שָׁנָה הָאֲמוּרָה בִּשְׁנַת שִׁשִּׁים כִּלְמַטָּה, אַף שָׁנָה הָאֲמוּרָה מִשְׁנַת חָמֵשׁ וּשְׁנַת עֶשְׂרִים כִּלְמַטָּה מִמֶּנּוּ, בֵּין לְהָקֵל וּבֵין לְהַחְמִיר. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: עֵד שֶׁיְּהוּ יְתֵירוֹת עַל הַשָּׁנִים חֹדֶשׁ וְיוֹם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states "year"** with regard to the fifth and twentieth years (see Leviticus 27:3–6), and **"year"** with regard to the sixtieth year (Leviticus 27:7), **for a verbal analogy. Just as** the halakhic status of the **year stated with regard to** the **sixtieth year** is **like** that of the period **preceding** it, **so too,** the halakhic status of the **year stated with regard to** the **fifth year and** the **twentieth year** is **like** that of the period **preceding** them, **both** in order **to be lenient and** in order **to be stringent. Rabbi Eliezer says:** Their halakhic status remains like that of the period preceding it, **until they will be** aged one **month and one day beyond the** fifth, twentieth, and sixtieth **years.**

This approach, however, leaves the thirty-day threshold unaddressed. There is no word *shana* associated with the thirty-day boundary, and therefore the *gezeirah shavah* cannot reach it. The *Gemara* acknowledges that we need a *binyan av* to extend the boundary rule from the year-based categories to the thirty-day threshold.

### R. Eliezer Month+Day

Rabbi Eliezer dissents from the *Tanna Kamma*. He holds that one day past the birthday is not sufficient; rather, one needs *chodesh v'yom echad* — a full month and a day beyond each birthday. According to Rabbi Eliezer, the applicable thresholds are: one month and a day, five years and a month and a day, twenty years and a month and a day, and sixty years and a month and a day.

### Gezeirah Shavah Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מוּפְנֶה, דְּאִי לָא מוּפְנֶה — אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ כִּדְפָרְכִינַן. ״שָׁנָה״ ״שָׁנָה״ יַתִּירָא כְּתִיבִי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara notes: Evidently, the verbal analogy in the mishna is **free,** i.e., in each verse the word "year" is superfluous, and therefore can be used in the verbal analogy. This means that the *halakha* derived from the verbal analogy is considered as though it is written explicitly in the Torah. The significance of this fact is **that if** a verbal analogy **is not free,** it **can be refuted** by logical reasoning. In this case the refutation would be **as we refuted it** in the mishna, that if the fifth year and the twentieth year are considered the same as the respective periods preceding them, this generates a leniency. The Gemara confirms: The verbal analogy is free, as both the first **written** mention of **"year"** and the second written mention of **"year" are superfluous.**

The *Gemara* then introduces a further complication regarding the *gezeirah shavah* itself. The words *shana shana* that serve as the basis for the *gezeirah shavah* — appearing in *ben shishim shana* and *ben esrim shana* — raise the question of whether they are *mufneh*, free from other *derashos*. If they are not *mufneh*, the *gezeirah shavah* is vulnerable to challenge. However, the *Gemara* notes that *shana shana yitirei ketivi* — the words are in fact written as free, and furthermore, the structural symmetry of the *pesukim* supports this reading. Since the fifth birthday appears as both an endpoint (*v'ad ben chamesh shanim*) and a starting point (*miben chamesh*), the two instances of *shana* are equally balanced — *ishtaklelu* — and therefore neither can be *darshen*'d in the other direction. Rabbi's principle of *ad v'ad bichlal* (that every instance of *ad* includes the endpoint) would not apply here, because the same word is pulled equally in both directions. Consequently, even according to Rabbi, the *Mishnah*'s ruling stands.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי, דְּאִי רַבִּי — הָאָמַר: ״עַד״ ״וְעַד״ בַּכְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **as, if** it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **doesn't he say** that the word **"until"** means **until and including?** With regard to valuations, the Torah states: "From twenty years old until sixty years old…And if it is from five years old until twenty years old…And if it is from one month old until five years old" (Leviticus 27:3–6). Accordingly, in the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi there should be no need for a verbal analogy to include the fifth year and the twentieth year in the periods preceding them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״מִיּוֹם הָרִאשׁוֹן וְעַד יוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי״, יָכוֹל רִאשׁוֹן וְלֹא רִאשׁוֹן בַּכְּלָל, שְׁבִיעִי וְלֹא שְׁבִיעִי בַּכְּלָל,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara provides the source of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's opinion. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* concerning the festival of Passover: The verse states: "For whoever eats leavened bread **from the first day until the seventh day"** (Exodus 12:15). One **might** have thought that the prohibition against eating leaven applies from the **first** day and onward **but** the **first** day **is not included,** and likewise that the prohibition continues until the **seventh** day **but** the **seventh** day **is not included.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּעִנְיָן שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״מֵרֹאשׁוֹ וְעַד רַגְלָיו״, רֹאשׁוֹ וְלֹא רֹאשׁוֹ בַּכְּלָל, רַגְלָיו וְלֹא רַגְלָיו בַּכְּלָל?
>
> **Translation:** This is **similar to the matter that is stated** with regard to a leper: "And the leprosy covers all the skin of him who has the mark **from his head to his feet,** as far as the priest can see…it is all turned white: He is pure" (Leviticus 13:12–13). This verse is understood as follows: The mark reaches from **his head, but his head is not included;** it reaches to **his feet, but his feet are not included.** Therefore, one might have thought the prohibition against consuming leaven on the festival of Passover likewise does not apply to the endpoints mentioned in the verse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עַד יוֹם הָאֶחָד וְעֶשְׂרִים לַחֹדֶשׁ בָּעָרֶב״. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, ״רִאשׁוֹן״ וְ״רִאשׁוֹן״ בַּכְּלָל, ״שְׁבִיעִי״ וּ״שְׁבִיעִי״ בַּכְּלָל!
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states:** "You shall eat unleavened bread, **until the twenty-first day of the month in the evening"** (Exodus 12:18). This proves that the seventh day of the Festival is included. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: It is not necessary** to cite this verse, as the prohibition against consuming leaven is from the **first** day of Passover, **and** the **first** day **is included;** and the ban continues until the **seventh** day, **and** the **seventh** day **is included.** Accordingly, with regard to valuations, even without a verbal analogy Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi should maintain that the twentieth year is included in the preceding period.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי, שַׁקּוֹלֵי מְשַׁקְּלִי קְרָאֵי. מִכְּדֵי כְּתִיב: ״מִבֶּן חֹדֶשׁ וְעַד בֶּן חָמֵשׁ שָׁנִים״, תּוּ ״מִבֶּן חָמֵשׁ וְעַד בֶּן עֶשְׂרִים״ לְמָה לִי? הִילְכָּךְ אִישְׁתְּקִלוּ לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **You** may **even say** that the opinion in the mishna is in accordance with that of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, as **the verses offset each other,** and therefore their meaning is unclear without the verbal analogy. The Gemara elaborates: **Since it is written: "From one month old until five years old"** (Leviticus 27:6), this ostensibly includes the fifth year within the stated category. If so, **why do I** need **this** mention of five years **as well:** "And if it is **from five years old until twenty years old,** then your valuation shall be for the male twenty shekels" (Leviticus 27:5)? **Therefore,** with regard to the fifth year, as both verses mention that year, **the** verses **offset each other.** Consequently, even according to the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi the verbal analogy is necessary.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״לְמַעְלָה״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״חֹדֶשׁ וָמַעְלָה״, מָה לְהַלָּן מִבֶּן חוֹדֶשׁ וְיוֹם אֶחָד, אַף כָּאן מִבֶּן חוֹדֶשׁ וְיוֹם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this opinion, it **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says: It is stated here,** with regard to valuations, **"upward"** (Leviticus 27:7), **and it is stated there,** with regard to the census of the Levites in the wilderness: "From **one month old and upward** you shall number them" (Numbers 3:15). **Just as there,** in the case of the census, the verse means: **From one month and one day old, so too here,** with regard to valuations, the verse means that each respective category is counted **from** one **month and one day old** beyond the stated ages of five years, twenty years, or sixty years.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא כִּי הָתָם, מָה הָתָם חַד יוֹמָא, אַף כָּאן חַד יוֹמָא? אִם כֵּן, גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה מַאי אַהֲנִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** why doesn't Rabbi Eliezer **say** that the verse dealing with valuation should be understood **like** the verse written **there,** with regard to the census, in the following manner: **Just as there** it is **one day** more than the enumerated age of thirty days, **so too here,** it should be **one day** more than the enumerated ages of five years, twenty years, and sixty years. Why does Rabbi Eliezer add a month? The Gemara answers: **If so,** that only one day should be added, **what purpose does** this **verbal analogy** from the census **serve?** Even without any connection to the verse dealing with the census, it would be understood in the case of valuations that the new period begins from the day after the respective year is fully completed.

## Mei'et L'Et

### Mei'et L'Et Contexts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שָׁנָה הָאֲמוּרָה בְּקָדָשִׁים, שָׁנָה הָאֲמוּרָה בְּבָתֵּי עָרֵי חוֹמָה, שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים שֶׁבִּשְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה, וְשֵׁשׁ שָׁנִים שֶׁבְּעֶבֶד עִבְרִי, וְכֵן שֶׁבַּבֵּן וְשֶׁבַּבַּת — כּוּלָּן מֵעֵת לְעֵת.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to the period of one **year stated with regard to sacrificial** animals, e.g., "sheep in their first year" (Numbers 28:3); and the one **year stated with regard to houses of walled cities,** during which time it is permitted to redeem a sold house in a walled city (Leviticus 25:29); and the **two years** stated **with regard to an ancestral field,** during which one may not yet redeem an ancestral field he has sold (Leviticus 25:15); **and** the **six years** stated **with regard to a Hebrew slave** (Exodus 21:2); **and similarly,** the years stated **with regard to a son and with regard to a daughter,** as explained below; **all of** these are calculated **from** the **time** of day at the start of the period **to** the **time** of day at the end of the period, i.e., these periods are units of whole years; they do not expire on predetermined dates, such as at the end of the calendar year.

The *Gemara* now cites a *Braisa* that collects multiple halachic domains where time is measured precisely — *mei'et le'et* — meaning that we count exact intervals from the moment an event occurred, rather than using calendar-year cutoffs. The *Braisa* lists: the year specified for *kodashim* (sacrificial animals of a certain age), the year for houses in walled cities, the two years for a *sedeh achuza*, the six years of a *eved ivri*, and — as the *Gemara* will discuss — sons and daughters. In each case, the operative measurement is a full interval from the triggering event, not a partial year counted as a full one.

Rashi reads *mei'et le'et* as measuring down to the exact hour — *oto yom v'ota sha'ah*. The Rambam, however, holds that we measure only by the day and not by the hour. The *Kesef Mishneh* notes this *machloket* and suggests that the majority view among the *Rishonim* follows the Rambam.

### Kodashim Full Year

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁנָה הָאֲמוּרָה בְּקָדָשִׁים, מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַב אַחָה בַּר יַעֲקֹב, אָמַר קְרָא: ״כֶּבֶשׂ בֶּן שְׁנָתוֹ״, שְׁנָתוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ, וְלֹא שֶׁל מִנְיַן עוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to the one **year stated with regard to sacrificial** animals, **from where do we** derive that it is calculated by whole years rather than calendar years? **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov says** that **the verse states: "A sheep in its first year"** (Leviticus 12:6). Since the verse does not state: A sheep in the first year, it means **a year** based on the calculation of **its** own life, **and not** a year based **on the counting of the world,** i.e., the calendar year.

For *kodashim*, the source is the phrase *keves ben shnato* — "a lamb in its year." The *Gemara* expounds: *shnato shelo* — its own year, meaning a full twelve months from its birth, and not *shana l'minyan olam*, not a calendar year measured from Rosh Hashanah. Rav Acha bar Yaakov derives this from the possessive form of the word.

### Walled City Year

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁנָה הָאֲמוּרָה בְּבָתֵּי עָרֵי חוֹמָה — דִּכְתִיב: ״עַד תֹּם שְׁנַת מִמְכָּרוֹ״, מִמְכָּרוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ, וְלֹא שָׁנָה לְמִנְיַן עוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues clarifying the *baraita*: The *halakha* that the one **year stated with regard to houses of walled cities** is calculated by a whole year and not a calendar year is derived from the fact **that it is written:** "Then he may redeem it **within a whole year after it is sold,** for a full year he shall have the right of redemption" (Leviticus 25:29). The verse is referring to a year counted from the day **of its** own **sale, and not** the **year of the counting of the world.**

For houses in walled cities, the *pasuk* states *ad tom shnat mimkaro* — "until the completion of the year of his sale." The word *mimkaro* — "of his sale" — indicates a year measured from the date of the sale itself, not from the New Year.

### Field Redemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים שֶׁבִּשְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה, דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּמִסְפַּר שְׁנֵי תְבוּאֹת יִמְכׇּר לָךְ״, פְּעָמִים שֶׁאָדָם אוֹכֵל שָׁלֹשׁ תְּבוּאוֹת בִּשְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states: Concerning the **two years** stated **with regard to an ancestral field,** this is derived from the fact **that it is written: "According to the number of years of the crops he shall sell to you"** (Leviticus 25:15). The plural form of both "years" and "crops" indicates that the number of years does not necessarily correspond to the quantity of crops. Consequently, there are **times when a person** might **eat three** yields of **crops in two years.** If one purchased a field at the end of the calendar year when its yield had not yet been harvested, and he harvested that yield and subsequently grew and harvested two more crops before the completion of two whole years from the sale, he would have eaten three yields in less than two years. This is not possible if one follows the calendar years, as a new year would start soon after the purchase.

For the two-year restriction on redeeming a *sedeh achuza*, the *pasuk* states *b'mispar shnei t'vuot yimkar lach* — "according to the number of harvests shall it be sold to you." Rashi explains that *t'vuot* implies multiple harvests and is not limited to exactly two. The significance is that two calendar years could only ever include two harvests, but two full years measured from the time of sale can sometimes encompass three harvests — if the sale occurred partway through an agricultural cycle. This demonstrates that the measurement must be two full years *mei'et le'et*, not two calendar years.

Regarding leap years: these *halachot* are expressed in terms of years, not months, and therefore a full year is counted inclusive of any extra month added in a leap year. By contrast, *avelut* — which is twelve months — runs for twelve months regardless of whether the year is a leap year.

### Hebrew Servant Six Years

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֵׁשׁ שֶׁבְּעֶבֶד עִבְרִי — דִּכְתִיב: ״שֵׁשׁ שָׁנִים יַעֲבֹד וּבַשְּׁבִיעִית״, זִימְנִין דְּבִשְׁבִיעִית נָמֵי יַעֲבוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states: The *halakha* that the **six years** stated **with regard to a Hebrew slave** is calculated by whole years, not calendar years, is derived from the fact **that it is written: "Six years he shall work; and in the seventh** he shall go out free for nothing" (Exodus 21:2). The word "and" in the phrase: "And in the seventh," teaches that **sometimes** it turns out **that he shall also work** in the seventh calendar year, if six full years have not passed from when he was sold. For example, if he was sold in the month of Nisan, although five years and six months have passed when Tishrei, the first month of the seventh year, arrives, since he has not yet completed six years of service he must work in this seventh calendar year as well, until the day of the month in which he was sold.

For the *eved ivri*, the *pasuk* states *shesh shanim ya'avod u'vashvi'it yetzei lachofshi chinam* — "six years shall he serve, and in the seventh he shall go free." The *Gemara* reads the *pasuk* with an alternative punctuation: "six years shall he serve, and in the seventh" — meaning that there are times when the service will extend into the seventh calendar year. This is only possible if we measure six full years from the date servitude began, because a six-year calendar count would always end within the sixth year and never reach into the seventh. The fact that the *Torah* implies the servant sometimes works into the seventh year proves that we count six full years *mei'et le'et*.

### Mei'et L'Et Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁבַּבֵּן וְשֶׁבַּבַּת כּוּלָּן מֵעֵת לְעֵת. לְמַאי הִילְכְתָא? אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: לַעֲרָכִין. רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: לְפִרְקִין דְּ״יוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* also teaches: The years stated **with regard to a son and with regard to a daughter** are among these terms calculated from the time at the start of the period to the time at the end of the period. The Gemara asks: **With regard to what *halakha*** is this stated? **Rav Giddel said** that **Rav said: With regard to valuations,** i.e., that the age of a valuated male or female is calculated in whole years from the date of their birth, not by calendar years. **Rav Yosef said:** The *halakha* is stated **with regard to** the matters taught in the fifth **chapter of** tractate *Nidda*, which is called after its opening words: ***Yotze Dofen*,** i.e., an animal born by caesarean section. In other words, when a mishna in that chapter, which deals with various matters related to the ages of sons and daughters, mentions years, it means full years, even when it does not state this explicitly.

The final entry in the *Braisa* — "sons and daughters" — requires explanation: to what *halacha* does it refer? Rav Gidel in the name of Rav says it refers to *erchin*: when determining whether a child has reached a new age category for the purposes of an *erech* pledge, we measure from the child's actual birth date, not from the calendar year. Rav Yosef, by contrast, says the reference is to the examples in *Perek Yotzei Dofen* — the chapter in Tractate *Niddah* dealing with when a boy or girl becomes halachically an adult (*gadlut*). According to Rav Yosef, it is *bar* and *bat mitzvah* — reached at thirteen and twelve years respectively — that is measured *mei'et le'et* from the child's birthday, not from Rosh Hashanah.

### Abaye Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: מִי פְּלִיגִיתוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא, אֲנָא אֲמַרִי חֲדָא, וְהוּא אֲמַר חֲדָא. הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ פְּלִיגִי, מַאן דְּאָמַר לַעֲרָכִין לָא אָמַר לְ״יוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן״? וְהָאָמַר רַב: הִילְכְתָא בְּכוּלֵּהּ פִּירְקָא מֵעֵת לְעֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: Do you** and Rav **dispute** this matter, i.e., when you apply the mention of a son and a daughter to different cases, do each of you reject the opinion of the other? Rav Yosef **said to** Abaye: **No,** we do not disagree; **I said one** matter **and he said one** different matter. The Gemara adds: **This too stands to reason, as if it enters your mind** that they **disagree** on this matter, then with regard to **the one who says** full years are required **for** determining **valuations,** does he **not** also **say** that full years are used **for** the *halakhot* of ***Yotze Dofen*? But doesn't Rav say,** like Rav Yosef, that **the *halakha* in** that **entire chapter** is that the ages of the sons and daughters are determined **from** the **time** at the start of the period **to** the **time** at the end of the period, not by calendar years?

Abaye intervenes and asks Rav Yosef: do you disagree with Rav? Rav Yosef replies: not at all — *ana amri chada v'hu amar chada*, I cited one example and he cited another, and we both agree that the *halacha* is *mei'et le'et* in both cases. Abaye confirms that this is indeed the more sensible reading: *hacha nami mistabra*. For if they were truly in dispute — if the one who says *erchin* did not hold *mei'et le'et* for *Perek Yotzei Dofen*, and vice versa — it would be difficult to reconcile with the explicit statement of Rav, cited elsewhere, that *hilchata be-kula pirka me'et le'et* — the ruling throughout the entirety of *Perek Yotzei Dofen* is *mei'et le'et*. Since Rav himself stated this about the *perek*, and Rav is also the one cited by Rav Gidel as saying *erchin* is *mei'et le'et*, clearly Rav holds both. Rav Yosef and Rav Gidel were each giving *pshat* in the *Braisa*, not asserting an exclusive ruling.

### Opinion Consistency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לַעֲרָכִין, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר לְ״יוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן״? דּוּמְיָא דְּהָנָךְ, מָה הָנָךְ דִּכְתִיבָא, אַף הָנָךְ דִּכְתִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if that is the case, then **according to the one who says** that the reference to full years mentioned in the *baraita* is **for** determining **valuations,** i.e., Rav, **what is the reason** he **did not say** that the *baraita* is referring **to** the *halakhot* of ***Yotze Dofen*?** The Gemara answers: Rav would claim that the years of a son and a daughter in the *baraita* are **similar to these** other cases mentioned in the *baraita*: **Just as** those numbers of years **are explicitly **written** in the Torah, **so too these** years of the sons and daughters are referring to matters **where** the years **are written** in the Torah, i.e., the years of valuations, unlike the topics discussed in *Yotze Dofen*, where the years are not mentioned expressly in the Torah.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ, אִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ דִּכְתִיבָא, הַאי ״שֶׁבַּבֵּן וְשֶׁבַּבַּת״ —
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **the other,** Rav Yosef, how would he respond to this contention? He would maintain that **if it enters your mind** that the *baraita* is referring to the years of valuations, **which are written** in the Torah, then **this** phrase in the *baraita*: **With regard to a son and with regard to a daughter,** is unsuitable.

The *Gemara* then asks: why did the one who cited *erchin* not cite *Perek Yotzei Dofen* as his example, and why did the one who cited *Perek Yotzei Dofen* not cite *erchin*? The answer is *dumya de-hanach* — each sought an example that was parallel to the other cases listed in the *Braisa*. All of the other cases in the *Braisa* — *kodashim*, walled-city houses, *sedeh achuza*, *eved ivri* — have explicit *pesukim* from which *mei'et le'et* is derived. *Erchin* similarly has a scriptural basis for *mei'et le'et* and therefore fits naturally alongside those cases. Bar and bat mitzvah, by contrast, appear to be derived from reasoning (*misvara*) rather than from a direct *pasuk*; Tosafot notes that the *Gemara* never actually supplies an explicit verse for *erchin* being *mei'et le'et*, and suggests that the thirty-day threshold — for which *mei'et le'et* is clearer — extends by inference to the other *erchin* categories.

The one who cited *Perek Yotzei Dofen*, on the other hand, did not use *erchin* because the *Braisa*'s language of *ben u-vat* — "son and daughter" — does not naturally describe *erchin*, which uses the language of *zachar v'nekevah* — "male and female." The more natural reading of *ben u-vat* is *bar mitzvah* and *bat mitzvah*, the domain where a son and a daughter become adults. Each *amora* therefore chose the example that best fit both the language of the *Braisa* and the pattern of derivation.

## Gender Value Differences

### Age 60 Gender Gap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא נְקֵבָה, דְּכִי מִיזַּקְנָא קָיְימָא אַתִּילְּתָא, וּמַאי שְׁנָא זָכָר דְּלָא קָאֵי אַתִּילְּתָא? אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: אָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: סָבָא בְּבֵיתָא — פָּאחָא בְּבֵיתָא, סָבְתָא בְּבֵיתָא — סִימָא בְּבֵיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to valuations, the Gemara asks: **And what is different** with regard to **a female, that when she ages** past sixty years she **stands at** a valuation of ten shekels, **one-third** of her previous valuation of thirty shekels, **and what is different** with regard to **a male, that** when he ages past sixty, at which point he has a valuation of fifteen shekels, he does not **stand at** even **one-third** of his previous valuation of fifty shekels? **Ḥizkiya said** that **people say** a popular saying: If there is **an elderly man in the home,** there is **a burden [*paḥa*] in the home,** as he does not help with anything; if there is **an elderly woman in the home,** there is **a treasure in the home,**as she assists with various domestic labors.

A notable observation closes the *perek*. The *Gemara* asks: *mai shna nekeva de-chi mizkana kaima atilta* — why is it that when a woman ages past sixty, her *erech* remains relatively high compared to her prior value, whereas *mai shna zachar de-lo kaima atilta* — for a man, the drop in *erech* at sixty is far steeper? In concrete terms: the *erech* of a woman between twenty and sixty is thirty *shekalim*, and at sixty it drops to ten — a threefold decrease. The *erech* of a man between twenty and sixty is fifty *shekalim*, and above sixty it drops to fifteen — more than a threefold decrease. The proportional decline for men is sharper than for women. And this is worth noting alongside the broader picture: even a younger woman's *erech* is lower than a man's in the same age range, so this is not a simple question of relative honor.

### Old Age Proverbs

Chizkiyah answers with a popular saying: *amri inshi, saba be-veita pacha, savta be-veita sima be-veita* — people say, "an old man in the house is a burden in the house; an old woman in the house is a treasure in the house." The *erech* schedule reflects economic and social utility: the type of work associated with women — domestic labor, cooking, serving — remains productive and valuable into advanced age. The type of labor associated with men, being more physically demanding, loses value more sharply with age. Rashi explains the underlying logic in these terms. The Torah's fixed valuation schedule, counterintuitive as it may seem in some respects, tracks observable social realities as understood in the context of the time.

With this, we conclude *Perek HaSheg Yad*.
