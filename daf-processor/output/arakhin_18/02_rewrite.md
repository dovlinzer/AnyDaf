# Arakhin 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Poverty Timing

### Tanna Kamma & R. Yehuda

The *daf* begins on 17b, picking up a *machlokes* in the *Mishnah* about the *din* in *erchin* concerning the poor person's discount. Ordinarily, the *Torah* assigns a fixed amount when one declares an *erech* — a donation to the *Mikdash* based on the fixed price schedule for different age ranges. However, when the pledger cannot afford the fixed amount, the *kohen* assesses what the person can actually pay. The dispute in the *Mishnah* centers on *when* one must be poor in order to qualify for this reduced assessment. The *Tanna Kamma* holds that one must be poor both at the time the *erech* is declared and at the time of payment. Rabbi Yehuda, however, holds that one must be poor for the *entire interval* from the declaration until the payment in order to qualify for the discount.

### Vehu Source

The *Gemara* seeks the source for Rabbi Yehuda's position. The proposed source is the word *vehu* — "and he" — which appears in the relevant *pasuk* and which Rabbi Yehuda reads as indicating that the condition must persist across the entire interval. The *Gemara* then proceeds to examine every other instance in the *Torah* where the word *vehu* appears, asking whether Rabbi Yehuda would apply the same interval-spanning logic in each case.

### Shebuot Ha'edut

The case under examination at the opening of our *daf* is *shevu'at ha'edut* — the oath of testimony. If a litigant claims that someone possesses information relevant to his court case and that person denies it, the litigant may compel that person to take an oath declaring that he knows nothing. However, this mechanism only applies if the person is a *kosher* witness. The *Gemara* asks: following Rabbi Yehuda's logic, would *vehu* require that the potential witness be *kosher* for the entire interval from the moment he allegedly witnessed the event until the moment he would give testimony in court?

### Witness Disqualifications

A *Braisa* is then cited that enumerates cases of disqualification. If a man knew testimony before becoming someone's son-in-law and then became that person's son-in-law — thereby rendering himself *posul le'edus* for that case — or if he was able to hear and speak and became deaf-mute, or was sighted and became blind (Rashi notes there is a special *gezeiras hakasuv* that a blind person is *posul le'edus*), or was sane and became insane, then in each of these cases he is invalid and cannot be compelled to take *shevu'as ha'edut*. So far, this appears consistent with Rabbi Yehuda's requirement of uninterrupted qualification.

### Temporary Disqualificati…

However, the *Braisa* continues with more difficult cases. Suppose the person was *kosher* at the time he witnessed the event, then became disqualified — for instance, he became someone's son-in-law, or became a deaf-mute, or went blind, or became insane — and then the disqualification was removed: his son-in-law status ended because the daughter died, or he regained his hearing and speech, or his sight was restored, or he recovered his sanity. In all such cases, the *Braisa* rules *kosher* — the litigant may compel him to take the oath. The *Braisa* states the general principle: *kol shetechilaso v'sofo b'kashrus* — whenever the beginning and the end are in a state of qualification, the person is valid. This appears to pose a direct challenge to Rabbi Yehuda's position, since the person was not *kosher* throughout the entire interval.

### Gezeirat Hakasuv

The *Gemara* resolves the difficulty: *shani hatam*, the case of *shevu'as ha'edut* is different, because there is a special *gezeiras hakasuv*. The *pasuk* reads *o ra'ah o lo yagid* — the Gemara deduces from the juxtaposition: *b're'iyah v'hagadah talah rachmana milta*. The *Torah* specifically anchors the obligation to two moments — the act of witnessing (*re'iyah*) and the act of testifying (*hagadah*) — and only those two moments are halachically decisive. This is a particular scriptural decree unique to *shevu'as ha'edut*, and therefore one cannot draw any general inference about the word *vehu* from this case.

### Vehu & Specification

Since the rule in *shevu'as ha'edut* is derived entirely from the special *gezeiras hakasuv* of *o ra'ah*, the word *vehu* is freed from that context and must be teaching something else. The *Gemara* cites a *Braisa*: if someone sees a large group of people and says to all of them collectively, "I make all of you take an oath — if any of you knows testimony relevant to my case, come forward," one might have thought they are all thereby obligated. The word *vehu* — "and *he* is a witness" — teaches that one must specify the individual: the obligation of *shevu'as ha'edut* cannot be triggered by addressing a group indiscriminately. However, one might then think that even saying "whoever among you knows" is also insufficient specification. Therefore, the word *ed* after *vehu* teaches that if one does indicate "whoever among you," that is sufficient — what is prohibited is only addressing the group as a collective whole, but singling out any individual, even by implication, is adequate.

## Korbanot vs Erchin

### Korban Metzora Rules

The *Mishnah* then states: *aval b'korbanos eino kein* — but for *korbanos*, the rule is not the same. The interval-based poverty assessment that governs *erchin* does not apply to *korbanos*. This refers specifically to *korban metzora*, which is the only *korban* that follows a tiered structure — the *korban oleh v'yored* — where a wealthier person brings a different offering than a poor person. For *korban metzora*, wealth is assessed solely at the moment of bringing the offering; one does not look at any prior interval. The *Mishnah* gives two illustrative cases: if one's father died and left a substantial inheritance, or if one has a ship at sea worth tens of thousands, neither of these counts toward the wealth assessment.

### Inheritance Case

The *Gemara* asks on the first case: *aviv meis v'hiniach lo ribo* — why state this? If your father is wealthy but you are not, is it not obvious that you are poor and cannot be assessed as wealthy? Rav Papa clarifies: the case must be discussing a father who is *goses* — in the final stages of dying. One might have thought that since *rov gosesin lemita* — virtually all *gosesim* die imminently, to the point where the *Gemara* notes that the correct formulation is essentially *kol gosesin lemita*, as a *goses* by definition is irreversibly dying within days — we should treat the inheritance as already effectively in the son's possession. The *Gemara* teaches *ka mashma lan* that we do not; we assess only what is presently in one's actual possession.

### Ship Rental Income

The second case — a ship at sea generating tens of thousands in income — similarly seems obvious: your assets are simply inaccessible. Rav Chisda clarifies that the case is one where the ship is rented out to others and the tens of thousands refer not to cargo but to rental fees owed to the owner. The *Gemara* then asks: but does the person not in fact have the rental income? The *Gemara* answers: *schirut eina mishtalemet ela levasof* — rental fees were not payable until the conclusion of the rental period. Since the fees are not yet due, the person does not presently have them and cannot be assessed as wealthy on their account.

One might ask whether it matters that the fees will eventually accrue: if someone is legally owed money right now and could in theory pursue it immediately, should that not count as current wealth? The answer is that the present case involves fees that are not actionable until the end of the rental period — they simply have not come due yet. The related question of what the law would be for fees that are currently actionable but simply uncollected remains less clear, and the *Gemara* does not directly resolve it here. Note that the rule *schirut eina mishtalemet ela levasof* is itself a matter of *machloket* in other contexts, and the present ruling follows the opinion that rental payments accrue only at the end of the term.

### Boat Ownership Value

The *Gemara* then raises a further point: even if the rental fees are not yet owed, the owner still holds title to the boat itself. Should not the value of the boat as an asset render him wealthy?

### R. Eliezer Tools

The *Gemara* answers: *ha mani* — whose opinion does our *Mishnah* follow? It is that of Rabbi Eliezer, as we learn from a later *Mishnah* in the tractate: Rabbi Eliezer holds that when assessing a person's wealth for these purposes, we do not count a farmer's plow-animals or a donkey-driver's donkey. Rabbi Eliezer's principle is that the primary tools of one's livelihood are not included in the wealth assessment — we do not strip a person of the means by which he earns his living. Accordingly, the boat, as the owner's instrument of income, would similarly be excluded under Rabbi Eliezer's position, and the *Mishnah* reflecting that view would not count the ship's value toward the assessment.

## Age-Based Pricing

### Age of Valued Person

The *Mishnah* now turns to the mechanics of how *erchin* valuations are calculated. The *Torah* provides a price schedule based on age, and the *Mishnah* establishes that it is the age of the person being valued — the *ne'erach* — that determines the applicable price, not the age of the person making the pledge. The *Mishnah* illustrates: if a younger person (*yeled*, meaning here an adult between twenty and sixty) pledges the *erech* of an older person (*zaken*, meaning over sixty), he pays the price for the *zaken*; and if an older person pledges the *erech* of a younger person, he pays the price for the *yeled*.

### Gender Determines Price

Similarly, the *Mishnah* states: *ha-erchin be-ne'erach* — the *erchin* valuation follows the person being assessed. If a man pledges the *erech* of a woman, he pays the price for a woman; if a woman pledges the *erech* of a man, she pays the price for a man.

### Time of Pledge

The *Mishnah* further rules: the valuation follows the moment the pledge was made, not the moment of payment. If someone pledged the *erech* of a person who was under five years old at the time, and by the time of payment that person had already turned five — or was between five and twenty at the time of pledge and turned twenty before payment, or was under sixty and turned sixty before payment — in each case the pledger pays based on the age category at the *time of the pledge*, not at the time of payment.

### Gezeirah Shavah

The *Gemara* introduces a *Braisa* that explains why this rule requires a scriptural source. The *Torah* elsewhere establishes a *hekesh* between *erchin* and other *nedarim* — specifically, *damim*, the type of vow where one pledges the market value of a person as opposed to the fixed *erech* schedule. From this *hekesh* we derive two laws: first, that when a person must sell property to fulfill a *neder*, we do not force him to travel to the city to obtain a higher price — he may sell locally, *margalit l'kalim*, the easiest way. Second, that if the *neder* concerned a limb that is indispensable to life — such as one's head — the entire value of the person is owed, *lidon b'chvodo*.

One might have thought to extend the *hekesh* further and conclude that just as with *nedarim* the obligation is assessed at the time of payment, so too with *erchin* the valuation should follow the time of payment. After all, a *neder* of market value is assessed based on what the person is worth at the time he pays, not at the time he made the vow.

### Keerkecha Yakum

The *Gemara* therefore cites the *pasuk*: *k'erkecha yakum* — "as your *erech*, so shall it stand." The word *k'erkecha* teaches that the *erech* is fixed at the moment the pledge is made: *eino noten ela bizman ha-erech*. The *erech* valuation, unlike a *neder*, does not shift with changing circumstances at the time of payment. This is why our *Mishnah*'s rule, though it might initially have appeared counterintuitive given the *hekesh* to *nedarim*, follows the time of the pledge.

## Boundary Age Cases

### Thirtieth Day Rule

The *Mishnah* now addresses what happens at the boundary points between the age categories. The age categories for *erchin* are: under thirty days (for which there is no *erech* at all), thirty days to five years, five years to twenty years, and twenty years to sixty years, and above sixty. The *Mishnah* rules: *yom shloshim k'lemata heimena* — the thirtieth day itself counts with the prior period (i.e., below thirty days), not with the period that begins at thirty days. In practice, the thirty-first day is the first day of the next period.

### Birthdays with Younger

The *Mishnah* similarly rules that the fifth birthday and the twentieth birthday each count with the younger period. So the fifth birthday itself is still assessed at the lower rate, and only the day *after* the fifth birthday enters the higher category. The same applies at twenty.

### Shishim Pasuk Source

The *Mishnah* derives this principle from the *pasuk*: *v'im ben shishim shana v'ma'ala* — "and if he is sixty years and above." The *Mishnah* reads this as teaching that only from above sixty — meaning, the day after the sixtieth birthday — does one enter the higher (or in this case, lower-priced) category. The sixtieth birthday itself therefore counts with the younger period. This is read as a *chumra*: since the price drops at sixty, counting the birthday with the younger, higher-priced period means paying more.

### Binyan Av

From this ruling regarding the sixtieth birthday, the *Mishnah* makes a *binyan av* extending the same principle to all of the other age boundaries: just as the sixtieth birthday counts with the prior period, so too the twentieth and fifth birthdays count with their respective prior periods. However, the *Mishnah* immediately raises an objection to this *binyan av*: the sixty-year boundary goes with the younger period *lechumra* — you pay a higher price. But the twenty-year and five-year boundaries go with the younger period *lekula* — the younger price is lower. One cannot derive a general rule of "always count the birthday with the prior period" from a case where doing so is a *chumra* and then apply it to cases where doing so is a *kula*. Perhaps the correct rule is simply that the birthday always goes *lechumra*.

The *Mishnah* therefore abandons the *binyan av* approach and instead invokes a *gezeirah shavah*. Each of the relevant *pesukim* uses the word *shana* — "year." Since *esrim shana* and *shishim shana* both contain the word *shana*, we apply a *gezeirah shavah*: just as by the sixtieth year the word *shana* indicates that the birthday counts with the prior period, so too by the twentieth year and the fifth year the word *shana* carries the same implication. The *gezeirah shavah* thus extends the rule symmetrically, applicable both *lehachmir* and *lehakeil*, independent of which direction the price moves.

### Thirty Days Gap

This approach, however, leaves the thirty-day threshold unaddressed. There is no word *shana* associated with the thirty-day boundary, and therefore the *gezeirah shavah* cannot reach it. The *Gemara* acknowledges that we need a *binyan av* to extend the boundary rule from the year-based categories to the thirty-day threshold.

### R. Eliezer Month+Day

Rabbi Eliezer dissents from the *Tanna Kamma*. He holds that one day past the birthday is not sufficient; rather, one needs *chodesh v'yom echad* — a full month and a day beyond each birthday. According to Rabbi Eliezer, the applicable thresholds are: one month and a day, five years and a month and a day, twenty years and a month and a day, and sixty years and a month and a day.

The *Gemara* then introduces a further complication regarding the *gezeirah shavah* itself. The words *shana shana* that serve as the basis for the *gezeirah shavah* — appearing in *ben shishim shana* and *ben esrim shana* — raise the question of whether they are *mufneh*, free from other *derashos*. If they are not *mufneh*, the *gezeirah shavah* is vulnerable to challenge. However, the *Gemara* notes that *shana shana yitirei ketivi* — the words are in fact written as free, and furthermore, the structural symmetry of the *pesukim* supports this reading. Since the fifth birthday appears as both an endpoint (*v'ad ben chamesh shanim*) and a starting point (*miben chamesh*), the two instances of *shana* are equally balanced — *ishtaklelu* — and therefore neither can be *darshen*'d in the other direction. Rabbi's principle of *ad v'ad bichlal* (that every instance of *ad* includes the endpoint) would not apply here, because the same word is pulled equally in both directions. Consequently, even according to Rabbi, the *Mishnah*'s ruling stands.

Regarding the *tzara'at* discussion that arises in the context of *merosho v'ad raglav* — the *Gemara* explains that the head and feet are treated as separate regions from the body, either because the diagnostic criteria (*simanim*) for a *nega* on the head differ from those on the body (*shani simanim degufo misimanim derosho*), or alternatively because of the verse *lechol marei einei hakohen* — the assessment covers everything visible to the *kohen*'s eye, and certain areas of the head and feet are not ordinarily exposed to easy inspection.

### Chodesh Gezeirah

The source for Rabbi Eliezer's requirement of a month and a day beyond each age boundary is derived from a *Braisa*: *ne'emar kan lemalah v'ne'emar lehan chodesh ulemalah* — the word *lemalah* ("and above") appears both in connection with the year-based age categories and in the formulation *miben chodesh ulemalah* for the thirty-day threshold. Rabbi Eliezer applies a *gezeirah shavah*: just as the thirty-day period begins only from a month and a day after birth, so too all other age periods begin only from a month and a day beyond their threshold.

The *Gemara* challenges: why not read the *gezeirah shavah* the other way — just as the thirty-day threshold requires only one day above the threshold, so too all other thresholds require only one day? The *Gemara* responds: *im ken gezeirah shavah mai ahani* — if that were the intended reading, there would be no need for a *gezeirah shavah* at all, since we could derive "one day above each threshold" from straightforward reasoning (*misvara*). The *gezeirah shavah* must therefore be coming to teach us something beyond what reason alone would suggest — namely, a full month beyond each threshold, not merely one day.

## Mei'et L'Et

### Mei'et L'Et Contexts

The *Gemara* now cites a *Braisa* that collects multiple halachic domains where time is measured precisely — *mei'et le'et* — meaning that we count exact intervals from the moment an event occurred, rather than using calendar-year cutoffs. The *Braisa* lists: the year specified for *kodashim* (sacrificial animals of a certain age), the year for houses in walled cities, the two years for a *sedeh achuza*, the six years of a *eved ivri*, and — as the *Gemara* will discuss — sons and daughters. In each case, the operative measurement is a full interval from the triggering event, not a partial year counted as a full one.

Rashi reads *mei'et le'et* as measuring down to the exact hour — *oto yom v'ota sha'ah*. The Rambam, however, holds that we measure only by the day and not by the hour. The *Kesef Mishneh* notes this *machloket* and suggests that the majority view among the *Rishonim* follows the Rambam.

### Kodashim Full Year

For *kodashim*, the source is the phrase *keves ben shnato* — "a lamb in its year." The *Gemara* expounds: *shnato shelo* — its own year, meaning a full twelve months from its birth, and not *shana l'minyan olam*, not a calendar year measured from Rosh Hashanah. Rav Acha bar Yaakov derives this from the possessive form of the word.

### Walled City Year

For houses in walled cities, the *pasuk* states *ad tom shnat mimkaro* — "until the completion of the year of his sale." The word *mimkaro* — "of his sale" — indicates a year measured from the date of the sale itself, not from the New Year.

### Field Redemption

For the two-year restriction on redeeming a *sedeh achuza*, the *pasuk* states *b'mispar shnei t'vuot yimkar lach* — "according to the number of harvests shall it be sold to you." Rashi explains that *t'vuot* implies multiple harvests and is not limited to exactly two. The significance is that two calendar years could only ever include two harvests, but two full years measured from the time of sale can sometimes encompass three harvests — if the sale occurred partway through an agricultural cycle. This demonstrates that the measurement must be two full years *mei'et le'et*, not two calendar years.

Regarding leap years: these *halachot* are expressed in terms of years, not months, and therefore a full year is counted inclusive of any extra month added in a leap year. By contrast, *avelut* — which is twelve months — runs for twelve months regardless of whether the year is a leap year.

### Hebrew Servant Six Years

For the *eved ivri*, the *pasuk* states *shesh shanim ya'avod u'vashvi'it yetzei lachofshi chinam* — "six years shall he serve, and in the seventh he shall go free." The *Gemara* reads the *pasuk* with an alternative punctuation: "six years shall he serve, and in the seventh" — meaning that there are times when the service will extend into the seventh calendar year. This is only possible if we measure six full years from the date servitude began, because a six-year calendar count would always end within the sixth year and never reach into the seventh. The fact that the *Torah* implies the servant sometimes works into the seventh year proves that we count six full years *mei'et le'et*.

### Mei'et L'Et Dispute

The final entry in the *Braisa* — "sons and daughters" — requires explanation: to what *halacha* does it refer? Rav Gidel in the name of Rav says it refers to *erchin*: when determining whether a child has reached a new age category for the purposes of an *erech* pledge, we measure from the child's actual birth date, not from the calendar year. Rav Yosef, by contrast, says the reference is to the examples in *Perek Yotzei Dofen* — the chapter in Tractate *Niddah* dealing with when a boy or girl becomes halachically an adult (*gadlut*). According to Rav Yosef, it is *bar* and *bat mitzvah* — reached at thirteen and twelve years respectively — that is measured *mei'et le'et* from the child's birthday, not from Rosh Hashanah.

### Abaye Resolution

Abaye intervenes and asks Rav Yosef: do you disagree with Rav? Rav Yosef replies: not at all — *ana amri chada v'hu amar chada*, I cited one example and he cited another, and we both agree that the *halacha* is *mei'et le'et* in both cases. Abaye confirms that this is indeed the more sensible reading: *hacha nami mistabra*. For if they were truly in dispute — if the one who says *erchin* did not hold *mei'et le'et* for *Perek Yotzei Dofen*, and vice versa — it would be difficult to reconcile with the explicit statement of Rav, cited elsewhere, that *hilchata be-kula pirka me'et le'et* — the ruling throughout the entirety of *Perek Yotzei Dofen* is *mei'et le'et*. Since Rav himself stated this about the *perek*, and Rav is also the one cited by Rav Gidel as saying *erchin* is *mei'et le'et*, clearly Rav holds both. Rav Yosef and Rav Gidel were each giving *pshat* in the *Braisa*, not asserting an exclusive ruling.

### Opinion Consistency

The *Gemara* then asks: why did the one who cited *erchin* not cite *Perek Yotzei Dofen* as his example, and why did the one who cited *Perek Yotzei Dofen* not cite *erchin*? The answer is *dumya de-hanach* — each sought an example that was parallel to the other cases listed in the *Braisa*. All of the other cases in the *Braisa* — *kodashim*, walled-city houses, *sedeh achuza*, *eved ivri* — have explicit *pesukim* from which *mei'et le'et* is derived. *Erchin* similarly has a scriptural basis for *mei'et le'et* and therefore fits naturally alongside those cases. Bar and bat mitzvah, by contrast, appear to be derived from reasoning (*misvara*) rather than from a direct *pasuk*; Tosafot notes that the *Gemara* never actually supplies an explicit verse for *erchin* being *mei'et le'et*, and suggests that the thirty-day threshold — for which *mei'et le'et* is clearer — extends by inference to the other *erchin* categories.

The one who cited *Perek Yotzei Dofen*, on the other hand, did not use *erchin* because the *Braisa*'s language of *ben u-vat* — "son and daughter" — does not naturally describe *erchin*, which uses the language of *zachar v'nekevah* — "male and female." The more natural reading of *ben u-vat* is *bar mitzvah* and *bat mitzvah*, the domain where a son and a daughter become adults. Each *amora* therefore chose the example that best fit both the language of the *Braisa* and the pattern of derivation.

## Gender Value Differences

### Age 60 Gender Gap

A notable observation closes the *perek*. The *Gemara* asks: *mai shna nekeva de-chi mizkana kaima atilta* — why is it that when a woman ages past sixty, her *erech* remains relatively high compared to her prior value, whereas *mai shna zachar de-lo kaima atilta* — for a man, the drop in *erech* at sixty is far steeper? In concrete terms: the *erech* of a woman between twenty and sixty is thirty *shekalim*, and at sixty it drops to ten — a threefold decrease. The *erech* of a man between twenty and sixty is fifty *shekalim*, and above sixty it drops to fifteen — more than a threefold decrease. The proportional decline for men is sharper than for women. And this is worth noting alongside the broader picture: even a younger woman's *erech* is lower than a man's in the same age range, so this is not a simple question of relative honor.

### Old Age Proverbs

Chizkiyah answers with a popular saying: *amri inshi, saba be-veita pacha, savta be-veita sima be-veita* — people say, "an old man in the house is a burden in the house; an old woman in the house is a treasure in the house." The *erech* schedule reflects economic and social utility: the type of work associated with women — domestic labor, cooking, serving — remains productive and valuable into advanced age. The type of labor associated with men, being more physically demanding, loses value more sharply with age. Rashi explains the underlying logic in these terms. The Torah's fixed valuation schedule, counterintuitive as it may seem in some respects, tracks observable social realities as understood in the context of the time.

With this, we conclude *Perek HaSheg Yad*.
