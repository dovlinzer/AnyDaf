# Sanhedrin 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chizkiyahu & Tumah

### Chizkiyahu Overview

The daf opens at the bottom of 12b, continuing the discussion of Chizkiyahu and his controversial *korban Pesach*. The verse describes the people as having brought the *korban Pesach* *she'lo kakatuv* — not in accordance with what the Torah requires — and one of the reasons given is that Chizkiyahu added an extra month on account of *tuma*. The underlying question is whether *tuma* is a legitimate grounds for *ibur shana* in the first place, and whether the famous principle of *tuma hutrah betzibbur* or *dechuyah betzibbur* is relevant. An additional possibility is that Chizkiyahu acted on the thirtieth of Adar, which was already a day eligible to be designated as Nissan — and we encountered both of those positions earlier.

Now the Gemara continues, three lines into the wide-lined text. Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Shimon: the problem was that Chizkiyahu caused Israel to bring *Pesach Sheni*, delaying the *korban* to the second Pesach when they could have brought it on *Pesach Rishon* despite their *tuma*.

### Half Impure & Women

The Gemara asks: *heichi dami* — what exactly was the situation, and why was there any ambiguity? Rav Ashi explains: the case was one where Israel was exactly half *tamei* and half *tahor* — or perhaps slightly less than half *tamei*, since triggering *tuma hutrah betzibbur* requires a *rov tamei*. Chizkiyahu's error was factoring in the women. When women were included in the count, the *tahorim* became the majority, and Chizkiyahu concluded that since the majority was *tahor*, the *tamei* minority should be deferred to *Pesach Sheni*. But the rabbis disagreed: even if women bring the *korban Pesach*, the determination of whether *rov tzibbur* is *tamei* is made by counting men alone.

### Women's Status Shift

The Gemara explains that Chizkiyahu's position actually evolved. Initially — *me'ikara* — he held that women are *obligated* in *Pesach Rishon*, and therefore they are included in the count: when factored in, the *tamei* were a minority and were pushed to *Pesach Sheni*. Ultimately — *levasof* — he concluded that women have *reshut* to bring the *korban Pesach* on *Pesach Rishon* but are not obligated, and therefore they are not counted in determining the ratio. Under that reading, the *tamei* were in fact the majority, and the majority does not defer to *Pesach Sheni* but must bring on *Pesach Rishon*. The Gemara uses this as the *gufa* of the passage and then transitions to the separate topic of *ibur shana*.

## Ibur Shana Timing

### No Ibur on Adar 30

The Gemara, after closing out the Chizkiyahu discussion, records: *amar Shmuel*, you do not perform *ibur shana* on the thirtieth day of Adar, because that day is already eligible to be designated as Nissan. As we learned in the Yerushalmi, this day would normally become *Rosh Chodesh Nissan*.

### Ulla's De Facto

The Gemara then asks: *ibruha mai* — if *bedi'avad* you did carry out the intercalation on the thirtieth of Adar — say by not formally designating the month but simply treating it as a second Adar — would it take effect? One might think not, since this appeared to be the very mistake of Chizkiyahu according to one opinion. Ulla answers: you do not perform *kiddush hachodesh* — you do not formally declare "today is *Rosh Chodesh Adar Sheni*." Rashi explains this clearly: since the day is eligible to be Nissan, we say "this is Nissan and there is nothing after Nissan." Instead, one remains silent, and when the next *Rosh Chodesh* arrives, one sanctifies it as Nissan. So Ulla's solution is that *de facto* intercalation can be accomplished without a formal declaration. That resolves the *bedi'avad* question.

### Rava vs. Nachman

But what if one *did* formally declare — on the thirtieth of Adar — "today is *Rosh Chodesh Adar Sheni*"? Rava says: *batel ha'ibur* — the intercalation is void. This is, again, precisely what one opinion identified as Chizkiyahu's error. Rav Nachman, however, says: *me'ubar umekudash* — it is both intercalated and sanctified, and the declaration stands.

### Purim-Pesach Gap

Rava presses Rav Nachman with a practical objection. From Purim to Pesach is thirty days, and we begin teaching and asking about the laws of Pesach thirty days before Pesach — as the Baraita states: *sho'alin vedorshin be'hilchos haPesach kodem haPesach shloshim yom*, with Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel giving the opinion of two weeks. If *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* is only announced when it is already *Rosh Chodesh* — so late in the process — people will still be operating under the assumption that Pesach follows thirty days after Purim. When they suddenly hear a late announcement of *ibur*, they will not absorb it in time. They may already be treating chametz leniently (*mezalzel bechametz*) because they believe Pesach is a month later than it actually is.

### Computation Known

Rav Nachman responds: people know. The intercalation of the year depends on computation — *bechushbana talya milta* — and the public understands that the rabbis may not have completed their calculations until late in the month. People will not dismiss a late announcement of *ibur shana*; they will accept it and conduct themselves accordingly. There is therefore no practical concern.

## Tekufah & Ibur

### Majority Month Rule

The Gemara now introduces a ruling attributed to Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel — wrapping up the preceding subject and transitioning to a topic that will occupy the remainder of the daf: *ein me'abrin et hashana ela im ken hayta tekufah chaseira rubo shel chodesh* — one does not intercalate the year unless the *tekufah* is off by the majority of a month. The context here is not the agricultural consideration of *chodesh ha'aviv* and the ripening of grain, but the solar-calendrical requirement: *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* is supposed to coincide with the vernal equinox, and *Rosh Chodesh Tishrei* with the autumnal equinox. If the equinox is displaced far enough into the month — if the solar season is starting too late relative to the calendar — a month must be added to realign them. The question is how far off the *tekufah* must be before intercalation is required.

### Rabbi Yehuda: 16 Days

Shmuel's rule specifies a majority of a month, and the Baraita then asks: how much is the majority of a month? *Shishah asar yom* — sixteen days, according to Rabbi Yehuda. The reasoning is that Pesach must fall during the spring, and when the *omer* is brought, the season should be in effect. If the vernal equinox is displaced sixteen days past *Rosh Chodesh Nissan*, Pesach will no longer fall within the proper seasonal window, and intercalation becomes necessary.

### Rabbi Yose: 21 Days

Rabbi Yose says: *echad ve'esrim yom* — twenty-one days. Both opinions derive from the same verse, and the Bavli is striking in its choice of locus: rather than focusing on Nissan and Pesach as the Yerushalmi does, it examines the pasuk about Sukkot.

## Tekufah Interpretation

### Chag Ha-Asif Views

Both Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yose expound the same verse: *chag ha'asif tekufat hashana* — the festival of the ingathering, at the *tekufah* of the year. The dispute turns on what "the festival of the ingathering" requires with respect to the new season. One opinion holds that *kulo chag ba'inan bi'tekufah chadasha* — all of *chag ha'asif* must be within the new *tekufah*. As Rashi explains, *chag ha'asif* refers to *Chol HaMoed*, the intermediate days of Sukkot, when labor — including the gathering of the harvest — is actually permitted. *Chol HaMoed* spans seven days: the days between the first *yom tov* and *Shemini Atzeret*, running from the 16th through the 21st of Tishrei. The other opinion holds that only *miktzat chag*, some portion of *Chol HaMoed*, need fall in the new season.

To illustrate: picture the month of Tishrei, with the autumnal equinox meant to coincide with *Rosh Chodesh*. If the equinox has shifted forward — if the fall season, calendrically speaking, now begins on day 17 rather than day 1 — then only if it begins by day 16 is all of *Chol HaMoed* guaranteed to fall within the fall. If the equinox begins on day 17, the 16th of Tishrei — the first day of *Chol HaMoed* — was still part of the summer, and the requirement that all of *chag ha'asif* fall in the new season has been violated. That is the opinion requiring intercalation when the *tekufah* is sixteen days off. The other opinion requires only that some portion of *Chol HaMoed* begin within the new season: even if the first days of *Chol HaMoed* were in the old season, as long as the 21st of Tishrei — the last day of *Chol HaMoed* — begins in the fall, that suffices. That permits being up to twenty days off before intercalation is required.

### Yom Tekufah Debate

The Gemara probes the assumptions behind these positions. The equinox does not fall at the precise start of a calendar day; it can occur at any hour. The question therefore arises: does the day on which the equinox falls count as the final day of the outgoing season (*yom tekufah gomer*) or the first day of the incoming season (*yom tekufah matchil*)? The Gemara reasons that both opinions must hold *yom tekufah matchil* — the equinox day inaugurates the new season — because if they held *yom tekufah gomer*, neither position would work. If the equinox day still belongs to the old season, then even being fifteen days off would be problematic under the first opinion, since the sixteenth day of *Chol HaMoed* would still be in the prior season. The only way either threshold makes sense is if the day of the *tekufah* is already part of the new season.

A contradiction is then raised from a Baraita: *yom tekufah gomer — divrei Rabbi Yehuda; Rabbi Yose omer yom tekufah matchil*. This is a direct contradiction of the Gemara's assumption that both held *matchil*. Moreover, a second Baraita contradicts Rabbi Yehuda's position of sixteen days: there, Rabbi Yehuda says *shtei yadot* — two thirds of a month, which is twenty days — not sixteen.

### Rabbi Yehuda Contradicti…

The Gemara notes this second contradiction baldly. In the first Baraita, Rabbi Yehuda says sixteen days. In the second Baraita, Rabbi Yehuda says twenty days. The Gemara leaves this unresolved: *kashya*.

### Matchil/Gomer Resolution

However, having introduced the second Baraita, the Gemara now turns to explaining the relationship between its opinions. Rabbi Yehuda says twenty days; Rabbi Yose says that one calculates sixteen days before Pesach — if the *tekufah* is sixteen days off at Pesach time, one intercalates; but sixteen days before Sukkot, one does not intercalate. The Gemara notes that twenty and twenty-one seem to be saying essentially the same thing, and asks whether Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yose are simply agreeing. The answer: *yom tekufah gomer ve'yom tekufah matchil ika beinihu* — the difference between them is precisely the dispute about the equinox day. Rabbi Yose holds *matchil*: if the *tekufah* is twenty days off, the twenty-first day itself begins the new season and that last day of *Chol HaMoed* is fine. Rabbi Yehuda holds *gomer*: even at twenty days off, the twenty-first day is still the old season, so you can only tolerate being nineteen days off — i.e., twenty days is already a problem, which is why he says twenty.

### Five-Day Shift

The Gemara then addresses the seemingly anomalous language of Rabbi Yose, who speaks of sixteen days before Pesach rather than twenty-one days before Sukkot. The explanation involves a five-day differential between the lunar calendar and the solar seasons. There are ninety-one days to each *tekufah* (four times ninety-one equals three hundred sixty-four, approximately a solar year). But the distance from Tishrei to Nissan on the lunar calendar — six months averaging twenty-nine and a half days each — works out to approximately a hundred and seventy-seven days, while the seasonal distance is one hundred and eighty-two days (two times ninety-one). The five-day difference means that whatever the seasonal displacement is at Nissan time, it will have grown by five more days by Sukkot. If the *tekufah* is sixteen days off at Nissan, it will be twenty-one days off by Tishrei. Rabbi Yose is therefore expressing the same twenty-one-day threshold — but keyed to the Nissan calculation rather than the Tishrei one. His formulation says: if you are sixteen days off at Pesach time, you know the Sukkot *tekufah* will be twenty-one days off, which is beyond the threshold, and you intercalate. But if you are only sixteen days off *at Sukkot time*, that is within the twenty-one-day tolerance, and you do not intercalate. The reason he uses the number sixteen in both places is stylistic symmetry — the Baraita mentions sixteen before Pesach, and so it also says sixteen before the *chag*; but in reality, the threshold at Sukkot is anywhere up to twenty days off.

### Acheirim: 14 Days

*Acheirim* offer yet another threshold: *mi'uto* — even a minority of the month is sufficient to require intercalation. *Vechama mi'uto?* Fourteen days. The Gemara asks what the underlying reasoning is. To get to a threshold of fourteen, one must hold two things simultaneously: first, that all of Sukkot proper — including *yom tov rishon*, not merely *Chol HaMoed* — must fall within the new season; and second, that the equinox day belongs to the old season (*gomer*). If the *tekufah* is fourteen days off, it falls on the fifteenth of Tishrei — the first day of Sukkot itself. If that day is still part of the old season, the festival has not begun in the new season, which triggers the requirement to intercalate. The Gemara confirms this: *ei kasavri yom tekufah gomer ve'chulei chag ba'inan* — if we assume *gomer* and require the entire *chag* (including *yom tov rishon*) to be in the new season, then fourteen days off is indeed a problem.

### Pesach Reference

The Gemara turns back to Rabbi Yose's language about computing sixteen days before Pesach and asks: why does Rabbi Yose reference Pesach at all, if the underlying standard is about Sukkot? And, more sharply: if Sukkot is the operative framework, Rabbi Yose should say that up to twenty days off at Sukkot time is permissible — why does he say sixteen? The Gemara answers that the phrasing is driven by the parallel structure of the Baraita: because the opening clause says sixteen days before Pesach, the concluding clause uses sixteen before the *chag* as well. But substantively, Rabbi Yose's position permits being up to twenty days off at Sukkot without intercalating; it is only when the displacement reaches twenty-one — detectable already at Pesach as a sixteen-day offset — that intercalation is required. The number sixteen appears at the *chag* because it mirrors the formulation of the *reisha*, not because it independently represents a threshold there.

### Waxing Moon

Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak offers a distinct explanation for the *acheirim* position, one that does not depend on Sukkot at all but instead invokes the verse about Nissan: *shamor et chodesh ha'aviv* — guard the month of the ripening. He reads *chodesh* in the sense of *chadash* — renewal, the waxing of the moon. The injunction is that the *tekufah* of spring must occur while the moon is still *chadash*, still waxing. The moon waxes from the first of the month through the fourteenth, and becomes full on the fifteenth — at which point it is no longer waxing. If the spring *tekufah* is fourteen days off, it falls on the fifteenth of Nissan, when the moon is already full. That means the equinox arrives when the month is no longer waxing, violating the requirement of *chodesh ha'aviv*. Hence, if displaced by fourteen days, intercalation is necessary.

The Gemara then asks: but if you are only slightly off — say, one day — why must you add an entire extra month? Why not simply add one day to Adar? The Gemara answers through Rav Acha bar Yaakov: the *tanna*'s formulation counts downward rather than upward. When *acheirim* say *mi'uto*, fourteen days, they mean that *up to and including* fourteen days you do not intercalate; only from fifteen onward is intercalation required. This resolves the question about adding a single day: a single day's displacement does not trigger intercalation at all.

### Ravina & Tishrei

Ravina offers an alternative reading: *l'olam acheirim beTishrei kaimei* — the *acheirim* are actually speaking about Tishrei all along, not Nissan. One might ask: if the *tekufah* falls on the fifteenth of Tishrei, all of *Chol HaMoed* still falls within the new season — so why should being fourteen days off be a problem? Ravina answers: *acheirim* require not only that *Chol HaMoed* begin in the new season, but that *yom tov rishon* itself must begin in the new season — *kulei chag ba'inan ve'yom tov rishon*. If the *tekufah* is fourteen days off and falls on the fifteenth, and if we hold *yom tekufah gomer* — that the equinox day still belongs to the prior season — then the first day of Sukkot was the last day of the old season. The Gemara objects: can *yom tov rishon* really be included in *chag ha'asif*? One cannot do agricultural labor on *yom tov* itself. The Gemara answers: *chag haba biz'man asifah* — the verse means the *chag* that arrives during the time of gathering. Even if the gathering cannot actually occur on *yom tov*, the verse intends that the festival itself must fall within the season of the ingathering, which encompasses *yom tov rishon* as well.

The cumulative picture that emerges is this: there are three interlocking variables — which portion of Sukkot must fall in the new season (*yom tov rishon*, the first day of *Chol HaMoed*, or the last day of *Chol HaMoed*); whether *yom tekufah* is *matchil* or *gomer*; and whether the computation is keyed to Nissan or Tishrei. The interplay of these variables generates the range of thresholds: fourteen, sixteen, twenty, and twenty-one days.

## Smicha Zekenim

### Elders Defined

With the *tekufah* discussion concluded, the Gemara turns to the second topic of our *Mishnah*: *semichat zekenim*, the placing of hands by the elders on the *par he'elam davar shel tzibbur* — the communal *korban* brought when the Great Sanhedrin issued an erroneous ruling and the majority of the Jewish people acted upon it. The Baraita opens: *v'samchu ziknei* — "and the elders shall lay their hands." One might think the word *zekenim* simply means older men, perhaps communal figures one might encounter in the marketplace. The Baraita therefore derives from the word *edah* that these must be the elders of the congregation — the *Sanhedrin*. And since the plain meaning of *edah* could encompass even a small *Sanhedrin* of twenty-three, the Baraita adds *ha'edah hameyuchadim she'ba'edah* — the preeminent court over all of Israel, meaning the Great Sanhedrin of seventy-one.

### 5 vs. 3

How many of the Great Sanhedrin actually perform the *semichah*? The verse uses the word *v'samchu*, which is plural — implying two — and *ziknei*, also plural — implying another two. Since a court cannot be even-numbered, one additional member is added, yielding five. This is the position of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Shimon says three: *ziknei* implies two, an even court cannot stand, so a third is added. That gives three.

### Vayasmchu Debate

The dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon turns on whether the word *v'samchu* is exegetically available. Rabbi Yehuda reads it as adding two additional members beyond the two already derived from *ziknei*, yielding five. Rabbi Shimon objects: *v'samchu* is not extra — it is the essential verb of the sentence, telling us what the elders actually do. Had it been omitted, the text would have read "the elders of the congregation, their hands upon the head of the calf" — the verb *samach* would have been understood implicitly from context. Rabbi Shimon therefore says the word is necessary for its own meaning and cannot be used as a derivation.

Rabbi Yehuda rejoins: *lichtov ziknei ha'eidah yedeihem al rosh hapar* — the Torah could simply have written "the elders of the congregation, their hands on the head of the ox," and we would have understood from the context of *semichah* (as derived from *gezeirah shavah* of *rosh rosh* from an *olah*) that "their hands" means pressed upon the head. The verb *v'samchu* is therefore genuinely extra, and Rabbi Yehuda uses it to derive two additional elders. Rabbi Shimon counters that without the verb, he would have understood *al rosh* to mean merely in proximity to the head, not actually resting hands upon it. He does not accept the *rosh rosh* derivation as making the verb superfluous. The dispute ends there.

## Semichah Authority

### Two Types

A Baraita records: *semichah u'semichas zekeinim bishloshoh* — both *semichah* (the granting of rabbinic authority) and *semichas zekenim* (the placing of hands on the communal *korban*) require three.

### Semichah Definition

The Gemara asks: *mai semichah u'mai semichas zekeinim* — what distinguishes these two? Rabbi Yochanan answers: *mismach savi* — *semichah* here means the ordination of sages, the transmission of rabbinic authority. This is a distinct institution from the *semichah* of the elders on the *korban*, even though the same Hebrew root is used for both.

### Moshe & Yehoshua

Abaye then addresses Rav Yosef: where do we learn that this *semichah* of ordination requires three? He raises the obvious source: *vayismoch et yadav alav* — Moshe lays his hands upon Yehoshua. This is the paradigmatic model for the transmission of authority: Moshe received Torah at Sinai and transmitted it to Yehoshua, Yehoshua to the elders, and so down through the generations.

### One or 71?

But if Moshe is the model, the number derived is one — Moshe did it alone. If one argues that Moshe stood in the place of the entire Sanhedrin, then one needs seventy-one. How does one arrive at three? The Gemara leaves this unresolved: *kashya*.

### Semichah by Name

Rav Acha, son of Rava, asks Rav Ashi: is *semichah* performed by literally placing hands on the recipient's head, as Moshe did with Yehoshua? Rav Ashi responds: no — *samchinan lei bishmah*, it is done by conferring a name or title. The recipient is called *Rebbi*. This explains why some *tanna'im* and *amora'im* carry the title *Rebbi* and others do not — and this also reflects, in part, the distinction between sages ordained in Eretz Yisrael and those in Bavel — since this chain of *semichah* ultimately goes back to Moshe and is rooted in the land.

### Dinei Knasos

The practical legal effect of receiving this *semichah* is the authority to adjudicate *dinei knasos* — monetary fines. Rashi's comment is illuminating: the verse *ad ha'Elohim* — going back to the first *daf* of Sanhedrin — establishes that a *beis din* must *represent* God in some meaningful sense. To adjudicate cases involving penalties and compulsion — *knasos*, *malkus*, *misah* — rather than mere restitution, requires a *beis din* bearing this chain of authority traceable to Moshe Rabbeinu. These are matters of imposing force and punishment, not merely compensating a plaintiff. *Dayyanim mumchin*, ordained judges in this sense — *hainu semuchim* — are required for such cases. For ordinary monetary disputes, the rabbis were lenient, partly because of the principle of *ne'ilas deles* and partly through the mechanism of *shlichusayhu ka'avdinan* (acting as agents of ordained courts), which will be elaborated later. But fines and punishments remain reserved for those who carry this *semichah* tracing back to the *ad ha'Elohim* of the original divine mandate.

### One Person?

The Gemara then raises a new question: *vechad lo samich* — can a single individual grant *semichah* alone? This leads into a well-known narrative about *semichah* that the Gemara will take up beginning on the next daf.
