# Niddah 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Beis HaSesarim

### Hidden Places Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁרֶץ בְּקוּמְּטוֹ — טָהוֹר, נְבֵלָה בְּקוּמְּטוֹ — טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** If the carcass of a **creeping animal** is found **in the folds [*bekometo*] of** one's body, e.g., the armpit, he is **ritually pure.** If the flesh of **an animal carcass** is found **in the folds of** one's body, he is **impure.**

The daf picks up from the bottom of 42b, continuing the discussion of orifices and hidden places on the body and the distinctions that govern their role in *tumah*. Three categories are relevant. The first is *beis hasesarim*—a place on the body that is not exposed skin; this can include even an area as minor as *beis hakemattim*, the skin normally concealed under a fold of the body. The second is *balua*—something so deeply interior that it is halachically considered nonexistent. The third, and most contested, is the boundary between these two categories, which was the subject of the debate between Abaye and Rava treated in the previous session.

### Sheretz vs Neveilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁרֶץ בְּקוּמְּטוֹ, טָהוֹר — שֶׁרֶץ בִּנְגִיעָה הוּא דִּמְטַמֵּא, וּבֵית הַסְּתָרִים לָאו בַּר מַגָּע הוּא. נְבֵלָה בְּקוּמְּטוֹ, טָמֵא — נְהִי דִּבְמַגָּע לָא מְטַמֵּא, בְּמַשָּׂא מִיהָא מְטַמֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava elaborates: If the carcass of a **creeping animal** is found **in the folds of** one's body he is **ritually pure,** as **it is through contact that a creeping animal imparts impurity, and a concealed part** of the body **is not susceptible to** impurity through **contact.** By contrast, if the flesh of **an animal carcass** is found **in the folds of** one's body he is **impure,** as **although it does not impart impurity through contact,** since it is in a concealed part of the body, **it at least imparts impurity by carrying,** and he is considered to be carrying the animal carcass.

Rava opens with a foundational ruling: *sheretz bekumto tahor, neveilah bekumto tamei*—if one carries a dead rodent (*sheretz*) in a bodily fold, one is *tahor*; if one carries an animal carcass (*neveilah*) in such a fold, one is *tamei*. The reasoning turns on the distinct mechanisms by which these two sources of *tumah* operate. A *sheretz* conveys *tumah* only through contact (*negiah*), and contact through a concealed place—*beis hasesarim*—does not count as halachic contact. A *neveilah*, by contrast, conveys *tumah* also through *masa* (carrying), and carrying does not depend on the surface area being exposed. Even if one is not "touching" the *neveilah* in any halachic sense, one is still carrying it, and that suffices for *tumah*.

### Oven Airspace

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁרֶץ בְּקוּמְּטוֹ, וְהִכְנִיסוֹ לַאֲוִיר הַתַּנּוּר — טָמֵא. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: ״תּוֹכוֹ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלֹא תּוֹךְ תּוֹכוֹ — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rava further said: If there was the carcass of **a creeping animal in the folds of** one's body, **and he brought** the creeping animal that was in the fold **into the airspace of** a large earthenware vessel, such as **an oven,** the oven is thereby rendered **impure,** as is the *halakha* when a creeping animal is placed in its airspace. The Gemara asks: Isn't this **obvious?** The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that as **the Merciful One states** with regard to the impurity of creeping animals: "And any earthenware vessel into **whose interior** any of them fall, whatever is in it shall be impure, and it you shall break" (Leviticus 11:33), this teaches that impurity applies only if the creeping animal fell inside the vessel itself, **and not** if it was in **the interior of its interior,** i.e., contained within something else, such as a fold, which is inside the vessel, therefore Rava **teaches us** that a fold in one's body is not considered like the interior of the interior of a vessel. Rather, this definition applies only when the carcass of the creeping animal was actually inside another vessel whose opening was outside the oven.

The Gemara extends this logic to a further case: if one has a *sheretz* concealed in a bodily fold and enters the airspace of a pottery oven (*tanur*), the *tanur* becomes *tamei*. The Gemara notes that this might seem obvious, but a plausible objection exists: the Torah states that what is *inside* a *kli cheres* conveys *tumah* to it, and one might argue that something in a *beis hasesarim* is a kind of "inside within an inside," analogous to a vessel whose walls rise above the walls of the oven, in which case the item would be considered located within the inner container rather than within the oven's airspace. The Gemara rejects this: being in a *beis hasesarim* does not create that kind of insulation, and the *sheretz* is still considered to be within the oven's airspace. The *tanur*, at minimum, becomes *tamei*.

### Zav Moving Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: קָנֶה בְּקוּמְטוֹ שֶׁל זָב, וְהֵסִיט בּוֹ אֶת הַטָּהוֹר — טָהוֹר. קָנֶה בְּקוּמְטוֹ שֶׁל טָהוֹר, וְהֵסִיט בּוֹ אֶת הַזָּב — טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says:** If there was **a pole** or a stick placed **in the folds of** an individual impure with the impurity of **a *zav*, and he moved a ritually pure** person **with it,** that individual is **pure,** despite the fact that a *zav* imparts impurity by moving an item. If the **pole** was placed **in the folds of one who is pure, and he moved the *zav* with it,** the pure individual is thereby rendered **impure,** as is the *halakha* of one who carries a *zav*.

The Gemara pivots to a ruling of Reish Lakish bearing on the same distinction. If a *zav* has a reed (*kaneh*) concealed in his bodily fold and uses it to push a *tahor* person, that *tahor* person remains *tahor*. Conversely, if a *tahor* person has a reed concealed in his fold and uses it to move a *zav*, that *tahor* person becomes *tamei*.

### Moving Concepts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״וְכֹל אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע בּוֹ הַזָּב וְיָדָיו לֹא שָׁטַף בַּמָּיִם״ — זֶהוּ הֱסֵיטוֹ שֶׁל זָב, שֶׁלֹּא מָצִינוּ לוֹ טוּמְאָה בְּכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ. וְאַפְּקֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא בִּלְשׁוֹן נְגִיעָה, לְמֵימְרָא דֶּהֱיסֵט וּנְגִיעָה כְּיָדָיו, מָה הָתָם מֵאַבָּרַאי, אַף הָכָא מֵאַבָּרַאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** that if a *zav* moved another with a pole in his own folds he does not render the other person impure? **As the verse states: "And whoever a *zav* touches, without having rinsed his hands in water,** he shall wash his clothes, and bathe himself in water" (Leviticus 15:11). **This is** referring to the impurity imparted by **the movement of a *zav*, as we have not found an impurity** similar **to it in the entire Torah.** Only a *zav* imparts impurity to items by moving them. **And the Merciful One expresses** this impurity imparted **by** movement using **the language of touch,** in order **to say that** the **moving and touch** of a *zav* **are like his hands: Just as there,** with regard to the impurity imparted by contact with the hands, it occurs **external** to the body, **so too here,** impurity by means of movement applies only to moving an item with the **external** portions of the body of the *zav*.

The standard rule of *tumat heiset*—the *tumah* of moving—is understood as a derivative of *tumat masa*, carrying. Just as carrying a source of *tumah* makes one *tamei*, so too does moving it, because moving is treated as a form of carrying. This rule, however, applies when it is the *tahor* person who moves the *tamei* object. There is no general principle that a *tamei* object moving a *tahor* person conveys *tumah*. By a *zav*, uniquely, the Torah introduces just such a principle. The verse states: *vechol asher yiga bo hazav—veyadav lo shataf bamayim*, "anything that a *zav* touches, and he has not rinsed his hands in water." From the word *yiga* (touches), the Gemara derives not only literal contact but also movement caused by the *zav*: even when a *zav* moves something, he conveys *tumah* to it.

This explains why the *tahor* person who moves the *zav* with a reed is *tamei*: *tumat heiset* is a form of *masa*, and the concealment of the reed in a fold (*beis hasesarim*) is irrelevant when the mechanism is carrying rather than touching. But the case of the *zav* moving the *tahor* person through a concealed reed is different, because this *heiset*—the *zav*'s moving of the *tahor* object—is derived from the word of touching (*negiah*), not from carrying.

This analysis reveals a structural distinction between two concepts of *heiset*. The ordinary form—a *tahor* person moving a *tamei* object—is a species of *masa*, carrying, and therefore carries no concern of *beis hasesarim*. One can carry something on one's back, through a shirt, under layers of other objects; the medium is irrelevant. The second form—a *zav* moving a *tahor* object—is a special *chiddush* of the Torah, and because it is expressed through the language of *negiah* (touching), it inherits the constraints of touching: it must occur through exposed skin. If the *zav* moves something via a reed concealed in his fold, the contact is through *beis hasesarim* and does not convey *tumah*.

## Men's Fluids

### Zav/Baal Keri Exit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הַזָּב וּבַעַל קֶרִי אֵינָן מְטַמְּאִין וְכוּ׳. זָב — דִּכְתִיב ״כִּי יִהְיֶה זָב מִבְּשָׂרוֹ״ — עַד שֶׁיֵּצֵא זוֹבוֹ מִבְּשָׂרוֹ, בַּעַל קֶרִי — דִּכְתִיב ״וְאִישׁ כִּי תֵצֵא מִמֶּנּוּ שִׁכְבַת זָרַע״.
>
> **Translation:** **But the *zav* and one who experiences a seminal emission do not become ritually impure** until their emission of impurity emerges outside the body. The Gemara explains: This is the *halakha* with regard to **a *zav*, as it is written: "When** any man **has an issue out of his flesh"** (Leviticus 15:2). The verse teaches that a *zav* is not impure **until his issue emerges out of his flesh.** With regard to **one who experiences a seminal emission,** the reason is **that it is written: "And if the flow of seed goes out from a man"** (Leviticus 15:16), which indicates that the flow must exit his body.

The Gemara now returns to the Mishnah and shifts from the *beis hasesarim* discussion to a separate but related principle: by contrast to a woman's *tumah*, which attaches while the blood is still interior (*bivsara*), a man's *tumah* of *zivah* and seminal emission attaches only when the fluid exits the body. By a *zav*, this is derived from the phrase *ki yazov mibesaro*—"when it flows from his flesh"—as opposed to the woman's *bivsara*, "in her flesh." By a *baal keri*, it is derived from *v'ish ki tetzei mimenu shichvat zera*—"when a seminal emission goes out from him."

### Man Eating Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה אוֹכֵל בִּתְרוּמָה וְהִרְגִּישׁ וְכוּ׳. אוֹחֵז? וְהָתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הָאוֹחֵז בָּאַמָּה וּמַשְׁתִּין, כְּאִילּוּ מֵבִיא מַבּוּל לָעוֹלָם!
>
> **Translation:** **The mishna further states that if a priest** was partaking of *teruma* and sensed a quaking in his limbs, indicating that a seminal emission was imminent, he should firmly hold his penis to prevent the emission from leaving his body, and swallow the *teruma* while ritually pure. The Gemara asks: May one **hold** his penis? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says: Anyone who holds** his **penis and urinates** is considered **as though he is bringing a deluge to the world,** as masturbation was one of the sins that led to the flood?

The Mishnah presents a striking case: a *kohen* who is eating *terumah* and feels that his body is becoming sexually excited and that ejaculation is imminent. Since *tumah* attaches only upon exit from the body, he is not yet *tamei*. The Mishnah rules that he may hold himself down, swallow whatever *terumah* remains in his mouth, and only afterward allow the emission to occur—at which point he is no longer handling *terumah*.

The Gemara raises an immediate difficulty: does not a *baraita* cite Rabbi Eliezer as ruling that anyone who holds his penis while urinating is "as if he brings a flood upon the world," because doing so leads to *zera levatalah* and sexual excitation? The objection is twofold: first, such conduct is generally prohibited; second, and more practically, if holding oneself leads to greater excitement, it would seem to worsen rather than resolve the situation.

### Abaye/Rava Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּמַטְלִית עָבָה. רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא בְּמַטְלִית רַכָּה, כֵּיוָן דַּעֲקַר — עֲקַר. וְאַבָּיֵי חָיֵשׁ דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְאוֹסוֹפֵי, וְרָבָא — לְאוֹסוֹפֵי לָא חָיֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said,** in resolution of this difficulty, that the mishna is referring to one who holds his penis **with a coarse cloth. Rava said: You** may **even say** that the mishna is referring to a priest who holds his penis **with a soft cloth,** and the reason it is permitted is that **once** the semen **has** already **been uprooted** from his body, it is **uprooted,** and his subsequent holding of the penis, even with a soft cloth, does not increase the flow of semen. **And Abaye** prohibits the use of a soft cloth, as he is **concerned** that **perhaps** one might **come to increase** the emission of semen, due to the contact of this cloth. **But Rava is not concerned** that one might **come to increase** the emission.

Abaye resolves the difficulty: the Mishnah refers to holding oneself through a thick cloth (*matlit avah*). Since there is no direct skin contact, there is no sexual excitation, and the concern of Rabbi Eliezer does not apply.

Rava goes further: even if the cloth is thin (*matlit rakah*) and some degree of stimulation remains possible, there is no concern. *Keivan d'akar—akar*: once the semen has been uprooted—meaning the process of emission has already begun—it is going to come out regardless. Touching oneself at this stage does not cause additional semen to emerge; what will come out is already defined. The act of holding down is therefore not analogous to the prohibited form of self-stimulation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: לְמָה זֶה דּוֹמֶה? לְנוֹתֵן אֶצְבַּע בָּעַיִן, שֶׁכׇּל זְמַן שֶׁאֶצְבַּע בְּעַיִן — מַדְמַעַת וְחוֹזֶרֶת וּמַדְמַעַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty with regard to the opinion of Rava. **Isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **To what is this** holding of a penis **comparable?** It is comparable **to** one who **places a finger in** his **eye,** in **that as long as** the **finger** is **in the eye,** the eye **will tear and continue to tear.** Here too, the priest's action will lead to an increased emission of semen.

Abaye demurs. He fears that even at this advanced stage, insufficient insulation will lead to more semen being produced (*shema yarbe lesofa*). The Gemara presses: does Rava truly not share this concern? A *baraita* elsewhere compares a man touching himself to placing a finger in an eye—as long as the finger is there, the eye continues to tear. So too, as long as stimulation continues, more semen will continue to emerge.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא, כׁל אִחֲמוֹמֵי וַהֲדַר אִחֲמוֹמֵי בְּשַׁעְתָּא — לָא שְׁכִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **Rava** would maintain that if the priest's limbs were not quaking and the semen was coming out in drops, there is indeed a concern that holding the penis might increase the emission. But when he feels his limbs quaking, this concern does not apply. The reason is that **any** such event, i.e., **a heating** of the body that leads to a seminal emission **and** which is **then** followed by another **heating** of that kind **at the time** when the semen has been uprooted, is **uncommon.** Consequently, in this case the priest may hold his penis even with a soft cloth.

Rava responds: *kol ahmume ve-hadar ahmume be-sha'ata lo shekhiach*—to be heated up and then heated up again at virtually the same moment is not a common occurrence. Rashi understands this as Rava's position that we do not concern ourselves with renewed excitation happening immediately after emission. Whatever will emerge at the present moment is already determined, and subsequent stimulation leading to another emission is too remote a concern to be taken into account. The debate between Abaye and Rava thus turns on how much weight to give the possibility that touching at the very threshold of emission will cause the process to expand.

## Hargasha Criterion

### Sensation Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע שֶׁאֵין כׇּל גּוּפוֹ מַרְגִּישׁ בָּהּ אֵינָהּ מְטַמְּאָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? ״שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא — בִּרְאוּיָה לְהַזְרִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel says: Any** emission of **semen that is not felt by one's entire body does not render** him **impure. What is the reason? The Merciful One states: "The flow of seed"** (Leviticus 15:16), which indicates that it is referring to an emission **that is fit to fertilize,** i.e., it is referring only to the kind of emission which is felt as it exits the body.

Shmuel introduces a remarkable principle: *kol shichvat zera she-ein kol gufo margish bah eina metame'et*—any semen that exits the body without the entire body being in a state of excitation does not convey *tumah*. Even actual semen, if it merely dribbles out without any accompanying physical sensation, is *tahor*. The reason: the Torah uses the term *shichvat zera*, which Shmuel understands to mean semen that is *ra'uyah lehazria*—capable of causing conception. Semen that exits without the appropriate physiological process lacks that capacity, and therefore does not fall within the Torah's category of *shichvat zera* that conveys *tumah*.

The importance of the word *hargasha* here should not be understated. The first formulation uses the verb *margish*—"the body is not feeling"—a descriptive phrase about physiological state. This will shortly become the noun *hargasha*, sensation, a term of considerable halakhic weight. Shmuel applies an essentially parallel principle to women: blood that exits without *hargasha* does not convey *tumah*. That discussion lies ahead, but the grounding is already being established here. Both rulings by Shmuel reframe the question: it is not merely about a fluid exiting the body, but about a real physiological experience accompanying that exit. The locus shifts from purely objective—did the fluid leave?—to substantially subjective: did the appropriate sensation occur?

### Night Emission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הָיָה מְהַרְהֵר בַּלַּיְלָה, וְעָמַד וּמָצָא בְּשָׂרוֹ חַם — טָמֵא! תַּרְגְּמַאּ רַב הוּנָא: בִּמְשַׁמֵּשׁ מִטָּתוֹ בַּחֲלוֹמוֹ, דְּאִי אֶפְשָׁר לְשַׁמֵּשׁ בְּלֹא הַרְגָּשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a mishna (*Mikvaot* 8:3): If one **was having** sexual **thoughts at night and he arose and found** that **his flesh** was **warm, he is ritually impure,** despite the fact that he did not sense the emission of semen. This shows that the impurity of a seminal emission applies even if one did not feel it in his entire body. The Gemara answers: **Rav Huna interpreted** this mishna as referring **to** one who **engaged in intercourse in his dream. Since it is impossible to engage in intercourse without** the accompanying **sensation,** he certainly must have felt it, despite the fact that he was unaware of this when he awoke.

A challenge is raised from a ruling about nocturnal emission: if a man had sexual thoughts during the night, and upon waking finds his flesh warm, he is deemed *tamei*, having presumably ejaculated in his sleep—even though he was unconscious and experienced no waking sensation.

The Gemara resolves this through the explanation of Rav Huna: the case concerns one who dreamed that he was engaged in intercourse (*mishamshe mitato bachalomo*). Since it is possible to engage in intercourse in a dream without conscious awareness (*de'efshar leshamesh belo hargasha* in the waking sense), the sensation was in fact present—it was simply incorporated into the dream. The body did experience the appropriate physiological excitation; the dreamer was simply unaware of it as a waking experience. This *okimta* saves Shmuel's position: the *hargasha* requirement is satisfied, even if not consciously perceived, as long as the body underwent the relevant process, which was then reflected in the dream.

The significance of Shmuel's framing is underscored by the parallel to women. The same Shmuel who requires *hargasha* for a man's seminal emission to be *tamei* will require *hargasha* for a woman's blood to render her *teme'ah*. In both cases, Shmuel locates the determinative moment not in the objectified fluid but in the subjective experience of the body through which it passed.

### Arrow Ejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחְרִינָא, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע שֶׁאֵינוֹ יוֹרֶה כְּחֵץ אֵינָהּ מְטַמְּאָה. מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין הַאי לִישָּׁנָא לְהַאי לִישָּׁנָא? אִיכָּא בֵינַיְיהוּ: נֶעֶקְרָה בְּהַרְגָּשָׁה וְיָצְאָה שֶׁלֹּא בְּהַרְגָּשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites **another version** of the above statement. **Shmuel says: Any semen that is not shot like an arrow does not render** one **impure.** The Gemara asks: **What** practical difference **is there between this version** of Shmuel's ruling **and that version** of Shmuel's ruling? The Gemara answers that the difference **between them** is a case where the semen **was uprooted** accompanied **by a sensation, but it emerged without a sensation.** According to the first version the man is rendered impure, as he sensed the uprooting of the semen, whereas according to the second version he is not impure, as this is not considered semen shot like an arrow.

The Gemara presents an alternative version of Shmuel's ruling: *kol shichvat zera she-eino yoreh kacheitz eino metamei*—any semen that does not exit like an arrow, in a forceful stream, does not convey *tumah*. This version shifts the criterion from bodily sensation to the manner of exit itself. Even if sensation accompanied the process, if the semen did not emerge with sufficient force, it is *tahor*.

This is a still more striking *chiddush*. The first version distinguished between qualitatively different types of semen—semen capable of causing conception versus semen that cannot. The second version distinguishes not by quality of semen but by the mechanics of emission. Even semen that is in principle capable of conception does not convey *tumah* if it does not exit in the requisite manner. As the *Tosafot* clarify: the concern is not that such semen literally cannot impregnate, but that it will not reach where it needs to reach. This version places the emphasis entirely on the process and the act of emission rather than the intrinsic properties of the fluid.

### Process Stages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִילְּתָא דִּפְשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ לִשְׁמוּאֵל, מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ לְרָבָא. דְּבָעֵי רָבָא: נֶעֶקְרָה בְּהַרְגָּשָׁה וְיָצְתָה שֶׁלֹּא בְּהַרְגָּשָׁה, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that this **matter, which** is **obvious to Shmuel, is raised as a dilemma by Rava. As Rava raises a dilemma:** If semen **was uprooted** accompanied **by a sensation but it emerged without a sensation, what is** the *halakha*? Is the man ritually impure or not?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בַּעַל קֶרִי שֶׁטָּבַל וְלֹא הֵטִיל מַיִם, לִכְשֶׁיָּטִיל מַיִם טָמֵא. שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּרוּבַּהּ בְּהַרְגָּשָׁה נְפַק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a mishna (*Mikvaot* 8:3): With regard to **one who experienced a seminal emission,** and **who** subsequently **immersed but did not urinate** before doing so, **when he** later **urinates** he is rendered **impure,** as some semen will also be emitted. The reason that he is rendered impure by this emission, which he does not sense, must be because the uprooting of the semen was accompanied by a sensation. The Gemara refutes this proof: **There it is different, as the majority** of the semen **emerged** accompanied **by a sensation,** and therefore he is rendered impure by this small amount even without a sensation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחְרִינָא אָמְרִי לַהּ, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע שֶׁאֵינוֹ יוֹרֶה כְּחֵץ אֵינָהּ מַזְרַעַת. אַזְרוֹעֵי הוּא דְּלָא מַזְרְעָא, הָא טַמּוֹיֵי מְטַמְּיָא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי יִהְיֶה בְךָ אִישׁ אֲשֶׁר לֹא יִהְיֶה טָהוֹר מִקְּרֵה״ — אֲפִילּוּ קֶרִי בָּעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say another version** of the previous discussion. **Shmuel says: Any semen that is not shot like an arrow cannot fertilize,** i.e., impregnate a woman. The Gemara infers: **It cannot fertilize, but it does render** the man who emits it **ritually impure, as it is stated: "If there be among you any man who is not ritually pure by reason of that which happened to him** by night" (Deuteronomy 23:11). This teaches that **even mere semen** which cannot fertilize renders one impure.

What precisely is the difference between the two versions? The Gemara argues they are not identical: *ikka beinaihu ne'ekra behargasha veyatza shelo behargasha*—the case where the process was uprooted (the *akirah* began) with sensation, but the semen exited without sensation and without a forceful stream. According to the first version, which focuses on whether sensation accompanied the *akirah* (the beginning of the process), this would be *tamei*, since the *hargasha* was present at the outset. According to the second version, which requires the semen to exit like an arrow, this would be *tahor*, since the exit itself lacked that quality.

Rava, notably, regarded this case as an unresolved question: *ne'ekra behargasha veyatza shelo behargasha—mahu?* The Gemara notes that what Shmuel held as obvious (*pshita leh le-Shmuel*) remained a *she'eilah* for Rava. The Gemara offers an apparent resolution from a Mishnah: a *baal keri* who immersed in a *mikveh* but did not urinate beforehand becomes *tamei* when he later urinates, because the urine carries residual semen. That semen exits without the force of the original emission, yet it is *tamei*. The Gemara deflects: *shani hatam de-ruba behargasha nafik*—it is different there, because the bulk of the emission exited with *hargasha*, and the residual drops are considered part of that prior process. The *tumah* of the residual semen is therefore parasitic on the *hargasha* that attended the original emission. This resolution itself is revealing: the halakhic status of semen is determined not only by what it is at the moment of contact but by the process from which it originated—even if that process occurred the previous day.

A third version of Shmuel's statement is then presented: *kol shichvat zera she-eino yoreh kacheitz eino mazria—mazria hu de-la mazria, ha tamuyei matmei*—semen that does not exit like an arrow does not cause conception, but it does convey *tumah*. This version essentially rejects the halakhic significance of the *arrow* criterion for *tumah*: whatever the quality of the semen, and however it exits, the verse *ki yihyeh becha ish asher lo yihyeh tahor mikreh layla*—"if there is a man who is not *tahor* because of a nocturnal occurrence"—implies that any emission, in any manner, conveys *tumah*.

Three positions thus emerge: one that ties *tumah* to conception-capable semen (first version), one that ties it to forcefulness of ejaculation (second version), and one that rejects these limitations entirely (third version). Nonetheless, at least two of the three give significant weight to the concept of *hargasha* and the process of emission, rather than treating the mere presence of semen as the operative fact.

## Conversion Question

### Non-Jew Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: גּוֹי שֶׁהִרְהֵר, וְיָרַד וְטָבַל — מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises** a similar **dilemma:** With regard to **a gentile who had** sexual **thoughts,** on account of which semen was uprooted but not emitted from his body, **and he** subsequently **descended and immersed** for the purpose of conversion, which means that he is now Jewish, and he then emitted semen, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to his status of ritual purity?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר בָּתַר עֲקִירָה אָזְלִינַן, הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְחוּמְרָא, אֲבָל הָכָא דִּלְקוּלָּא — לָא אָמְרִינַן, אוֹ דִילְמָא לָא שְׁנָא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the dilemma: Even **if you say** that **we follow** the moment of **uprooting,** at which point he was still a gentile, one can maintain that **this statement** applies only when it entails **a stringency,** as is the case with regard to a born Jew. **But here, where** this would lead **to a leniency,** as the gentile would be ritually pure, perhaps **we do not say** that one follows the moment of uprooting. **Or perhaps** there **is no difference** in the application of this principle between a born Jew and a convert, but rather, one always follows the moment of uprooting. The Gemara concludes that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Rava poses a conceptual question: a non-Jew experienced sexual excitement, and the semen was already in the process of being uprooted (*akira*)—fully on its way but not yet exited—when he converted. When the semen subsequently exits, is he *tamei* as a *baal keri*?

The question is framed by the stakes: we have now established that the *akirah*, the beginning of the process, carries halakhic weight. If the *akirah* occurred while he was a non-Jew, it carried no *tumah* significance. Can the exit—now occurring while he is a Jew—nonetheless convey *tumah*? Or is the *akirah* essential to establishing the halakhic process such that, without a valid *akirah*, there is no *tumah* even upon exit?

The Gemara frames it carefully: *im timtza lomar batar akira azlinan*—if we say we follow the *akirah*, then we might distinguish between the case where the *akirah* is used *lechumra* (to be strict—even if the semen dribbles out, as long as the *akirah* was with *hargasha*, it is *tamei*) and the use of the *akirah* *lekula* (to be lenient—where the *akirah* was not halakhically significant, as here, perhaps the exit alone does not suffice). Alternatively, *o dilma lo shana*—perhaps the *akirah* and the exit are independent, and exit alone is determinative regardless. The Gemara concludes: *teiku*. The question remains unresolved.

## Women's Urine

### Zavah Urine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: זָבָה שֶׁנֶּעֶקְרוּ מֵימֵי רַגְלֶיהָ, וְיָרְדָה וְטָבְלָה — מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises** a further **dilemma:** With regard to **a woman who experienced a discharge of uterine blood after her menstrual period [*zava*], whose urine,** which imparts impurity like all liquids that she discharges vaginally, **was uprooted** but not emitted from her body, **and she descended** to the ritual bath **and immersed** to purify herself from her *ziva*, and urinated afterward, **what is** the *halakha*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר בָּתַר עֲקִירָה אָזְלִינַן, הָנֵי מִילֵּי שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע, דְּלָא מָצֵי נָקֵיט לַהּ, אֲבָל מֵימֵי רַגְלֶיהָ, דְּמָצֵי נָקֵיט לַהּ — לָא, אוֹ דִילְמָא לָא שְׁנָא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the sides of the dilemma: Even **if you say** that generally **we follow** the moment of **uprooting,** and therefore she should be impure, since the urine was uprooted when she was a *zava*, nevertheless one can claim that **this statement** applies only with regard to **semen, as** the man **cannot hold it** back from emission. **But** with regard to the **urine of** a *zava*, **which she can hold in,** one does **not** follow the moment of uprooting. **Or perhaps** there **is no difference** in the application of this principle between urine and semen, but rather, in both cases one follows the moment of uprooting. Here too, the Gemara concludes that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Rava poses a parallel question regarding a *zavah*. In general, a *zavah*'s *tumah* attaches while the blood is still within her body—a rule that, on its face, renders questions about the *akirah* less acute for women. However, a *zavah* also conveys *tumah* through her other bodily fluids, including urine (*me'ayanot*), and these presumably attach when the fluid exits. This creates the opening for the same type of question about process stages.

The case: a *zavah* feels the urge to urinate—the urine has, in the Gemara's language, been "uprooted" (*ne'ekru mimei raglehah*)—and she descends and immerses in a *mikveh*. When she later urinates, is that urine *tamei*? The question is whether the *tumah* of a *zavah*'s urine attaches at the moment of *akirah* (when she first feels the need to urinate, while still a *zavah* before immersion) or only at the moment of exit (after immersion, when she is no longer a *zavah*).

The Gemara notes an important asymmetry between this case and the parallel question about semen: *hani milei shichvat zera de-la mitzi naktei*—with semen, once the process is uprooted, it is effectively impossible to hold it back; the *akirah* and the exit are nearly continuous and clearly part of the same process. Urine, by contrast, *matzi nakit lah*—can be held back indefinitely. On a long road trip, one knows exactly how long this can persist. Since urine can be withheld, one might argue that the *akirah* is a more detached and preliminary stage, less integrated into the eventual exit, and therefore less likely to be determinative. Or, *o dilma lo shana*—perhaps the distinction is irrelevant, and we apply the same rule regardless of whether the fluid could have been held back. *Teiku*. The Gemara reaches no conclusion.

One should note an interesting inversion here. A woman's *tumah* of blood is internal—it attaches *bifnim*. A man's *tumah* of semen is external—it attaches only *bachutz*, upon exit. Yet it is in the man's case that *akirah* acquires significant halakhic weight, while for the woman's urine, the parallel question about *akirah* is left unresolved. The conceptual terrain is not as symmetrical as it might appear.

### Non-Jewish Zavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: גּוֹיָה זָבָה שֶׁנֶּעֶקְרוּ מֵימֵי רַגְלֶיהָ, וְיָרְדָה וְטָבְלָה — מַהוּ? אִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר בָּתַר עֲקִירָה אָזְלִינַן, אַף עַל גַּב דְּמָצֵי נָקֵיט לְהוּ — הָנֵי מִילֵּי יִשְׂרְאֵלִית דִּטְמֵאָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, אֲבָל גּוֹיָה זָבָה דִּטְמֵאָה דְּרַבָּנַן — לָא, אוֹ דִילְמָא לָא שְׁנָא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises** yet another **dilemma:** With regard to **a gentile *zava*,** who is not impure by Torah law, although by rabbinic law she is considered a *zava* in all regards, **whose urine was uprooted** when she was a gentile, **and she descended** to the ritual bath **and immersed** for the sake of conversion, **what is** the *halakha*? Once again, the Gemara explains the dilemma: **If you say** that **we follow** the moment of **uprooting even though she can hold in** the urine, nevertheless one can claim that **this statement** applies specifically to **a Jewish woman, who is impure by Torah law. But** with regard to **a gentile *zava*, who is impure by rabbinic law,** it is possible that one does **not** follow the moment of uprooting. **Or perhaps** there **is no difference** in the application of this principle between the case of a Jewish woman and a gentile woman, as in both cases one follows the moment of uprooting. The Gemara again concludes that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Rava adds a further layer: a non-Jewish woman who has the status of a *zavah*. By rabbinic decree, all non-Jewish women are treated as *zavaniyot*, and their urine is therefore *tamei* rabbinically. This woman urinated to the point of *akirah* and then immersed in a *mikveh* to convert. When the urine exits, is it *tamei*?

The Gemara identifies yet another variable: even if one were to resolve the prior question and say that for a Jewish *zavah* we follow the *akirah*—meaning the urine is *tamei* from the moment she felt the need to go, and therefore the *mikveh* does not sever the *tumah*—perhaps for a non-Jewish *zavah* the rule is more lenient. Her *zavah* status is only rabbinic, and her urine's *tumah* is therefore rabbinic. Perhaps the rabbis would not apply the *akirah* principle with equal stringency in a case that is only rabbinic to begin with. Or, *o dilma lo shana*—perhaps the rabbinic/biblical distinction is irrelevant here. *Teiku*. Again, no resolution.

## Zav Flow Size

### Orifice Filling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְטַמְּאִין בְּכֹל שֶׁהֵן. אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: זָב צָרִיךְ כַּחֲתִימַת פִּי הָאַמָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אוֹ הֶחְתִּים בְּשָׂרוֹ מִזּוֹבוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The mishna teaches that the emission of a *zav* and a seminal emission** impart impurity **in any amount. Shmuel says:** In order for **a *zav*** to become ritually impure, he **must** experience a discharge substantial enough **to** cause **a blockage of the tip of the penis, as it is stated:** "And this shall be his impurity in his issue: Whether his flesh run with his issue, **or his flesh be stopped from his issue,** it is his impurity" (Leviticus 15:3).

The Gemara turns to the last section of the Mishnah, which states that *tumah* of semen and blood conveys *tumah* in any amount (*bechol shehu*). Shmuel, whose voice has been dominant throughout this daf, adds a ruling that appears to stand in tension with that Mishnah: *zav tzarich kechatimat pi ha'amah*—by a *zav*, the flow must be sufficient in quantity to fill the orifice of the penis completely. The source, as Shmuel reads it, is the verse *o hechtim besaro mi-zovo*—"or his flesh is sealed through his flow"—which implies that the flow seals, i.e., fills, the orifice.

### Derivation Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאֲנַן תְּנַן: מְטַמְּאִין בְּכׇל שֶׁהֵן! הוּא דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי נָתָן, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: זָב צָרִיךְ כַּחֲתִימַת פִּי הָאַמָּה, וְלֹא הוֹדוּ לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that the emission of a *zav* and a seminal emission **impart impurity in any amount?** The Gemara answers that Shmuel **said** his ruling **in accordance with** the statement of **Rabbi Natan, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Natan says in the name of Rabbi Yishmael:** In order for **a *zav*** to become ritually impure, he **must** experience a discharge substantial enough **to** cause **a blockage of the tip of the penis, but** the Rabbis **did not concede to his** opinion, as they maintain that any amount is sufficient. The *tanna* of the mishna agrees with the opinion of the Rabbis, whereas Shmuel agrees with the statement of Rabbi Natan in the name of Rabbi Yishmael.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״אוֹ הֶחְתִּים בְּשָׂרוֹ מִזּוֹבוֹ״. וְרַבָּנַן? הַהוּא מִבָּעֵי לֵיהּ: לַח מְטַמֵּא, וְאֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא יָבֵשׁ. וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל? הָהוּא מֵ״רָר״ נָפְקָא. וְרַבָּנַן? הָהוּא לְמִנְיָנָא הוּא דַּאֲתָא: ״זוֹבוֹ״ — חֲדָא, ״רָר בְּשָׂרוֹ״ — תְּרֵי, ״אֶת זוֹבוֹ״ — תְּלָת, לִימֵּד עַל זָב בַּעַל שָׁלֹשׁ רְאִיּוֹת שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּקׇרְבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Yishmael,** i.e., what is the source in the Torah for his ruling? The Gemara answers: His source is, as mentioned above, **that the verse states:** "And this shall be his impurity in his issue: Whether his flesh run with his issue, **or his flesh be stopped from his issue,** it is his impurity" (Leviticus 15:3). This verse indicates that the emission must be enough to cause a blockage of his penis. The Gemara asks: **And the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Yishmael, what do they derive from this verse? The Gemara explains that according to the Rabbis, **that** verse **is necessary** to teach a different *halakha* with regard to a *zav*, that it is only a discharge which is **moist,** and which therefore could cause a blockage of the organ, that **imparts impurity, but a dry** discharge does **not impart impurity.** The Gemara asks: **And** from where does **Rabbi Yishmael** derive that only a moist discharge imparts impurity? The Gemara answers: **That** *halakha* **is derived from** the word **"run"** in the verse "And this shall be his impurity in his issue: Whether his flesh run with his issue." The Gemara asks: **And** what do **the Rabbis** derive from this word? The Gemara explains that according to the Rabbis, **that** verse **comes to** teach the **number** of emissions by which a *zav* is rendered impure, as follows: The term **"his issue"** is **one** emission, the term **"his flesh run"** is another emission so that there are **two** emissions, and the term **"with his issue"** makes a total of **three** emissions. The verse thereby **teaches with regard to a *zav* who experienced three emissions that** he **is obligated to** bring **an offering** as part of his purification process.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אוֹ הֶחְתִּים בְּשָׂרוֹ מִזּוֹבוֹ טָמֵא״, מִקְצָת זוֹבוֹ טָמֵא, לִימֵּד עַל זָב בַּעַל שְׁתֵּי רְאִיּוֹת שֶׁמְּטַמֵּא מִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב. וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, מִנְיָנָא מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִדְּרַבִּי סִימַאי. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי סִימַאי אוֹמֵר: מָנָה הַכָּתוּב שְׁתַּיִם וּקְרָאוֹ ״טָמֵא״, שָׁלֹשׁ וּקְרָאוֹ ״טָמֵא״. הָא כֵיצַד? שְׁתַּיִם לַטּוּמְאָה, וְשָׁלֹשׁ לַקׇּרְבָּן. וּלְמַאן דְּנָפְקָא לֵיהּ תַּרְוַיְיהוּ מִ״זֹּאת תִּהְיֶה טוּמְאָתוֹ בְּזוֹבוֹ״, ״אִישׁ אִישׁ כִּי יִהְיֶה זָב מִבְּשָׂרוֹ״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? מִבָּעֵי לֵיהּ עַד שֶׁיֵּצֵא מִבְּשָׂרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: From the last section of the verse: **"Or his flesh be stopped from his issue,** it is his **impurity,"** it is derived that one is **impure** even by means of **part of his issue,** i.e., even if he did not experience three emissions. Here the verse **teaches with regard to a *zav* who experienced two emissions that** although he is not obligated to bring an offering, he **renders** a surface designated for **lying and** a surface designated for **sitting ritually impure** by lying or sitting on them, even without touching them directly. The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi Yishmael, from where does he** derive this *halakha* of the requisite **number** of emissions? The Gemara answers that **he derives it from that** which **Rabbi Simai** said. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Simai says: The verse enumerated two** emissions **and called** the *zav* **impure:** "When any man has an emission out of his flesh, due to his issue he is impure" (Leviticus 15:2). And yet, another verse enumerates **three** emissions **and it** too **called him impure:** "And this shall be his impurity in his emission: Whether his flesh runs with his emission, or his flesh be stopped from his emission, it is his impurity" (Leviticus 15:3). **How** can **these** verses be reconciled? If one is impure after two emissions, for what purpose does the Torah mention three? It is to teach that **two** emissions are necessary **to** establish **impurity, and three** are necessary **to** render a *zav* liable to bring **an offering.** The Gemara asks: **And according to the one who derives both** the *halakha* of impurity and the obligation to bring an offering **from** the single verse: "And **this shall be his impurity in his issue," what do they,** i.e., the Rabbis, **do with** the other verse: **"When any man has an emission out of his flesh"?** The Gemara answers that this verse is **necessary** to teach the *halakha* that a *zav* is not impure **unless** the discharge **emerges from his flesh.** The Gemara further asks: **Why do I** need the last part of the previous verse, which can be read as: **His issue is impure** (Leviticus 15:2)? The Gemara explains that this **teaches with regard to the issue** itself **that it is impure,** i.e., not only does it render the man who emitted it impure, but the substance itself is impure and imparts impurity to others by contact.

The debate between Rabbi Yishmael and the sages turns on how to read the word *hechtim* in the verse *o hechtim besaro mi-zovo*. Rabbi Yishmael reads *hechtim* as requiring that the flow seal (fill) the orifice. The sages read *hechtim* differently: it refers not to a quantity that fills the hole, but rather to the quality of the flow—that it be a liquid (*lach*), not a solid (*yavesh*). The verse teaches that *tumah* attaches only when the flow is in liquid form; once dried, it no longer conveys *tumah* through the primary mechanism.

Rabbi Yishmael, by contrast, derives the liquid requirement from a different word in the verse: *rar*, which itself denotes a dribbling or liquid quality. If *rar* already tells us the flow must be liquid, then *hechtim* is freed to teach the additional requirement of filling the orifice.

The sages counter: they do not learn the liquid requirement from *rar* alone. They read *rar besaro et zovo* and other phrases as contributing to the count of *re'iyot* (episodes of flow) needed to establish *zav* status—two for the seven-day *tumah* and three for the obligation of a *korban*. The verse *o hechtim besaro mi-zovo*, by contrast, they read as an additional statement of *tumah* applying to a partial flow, i.e., one who has seen fewer than three times is already *tamei* for *mishkav u-moshav* even without the *korban*.

Rabbi Yishmael learns the count of two and three from the method of Rabbi Simai, who derives these numbers from the repetition of the words *tumato* ("his *tumah*") paired with different descriptions of the flow: twice the Torah says there is a flow and calls him *tamei*—those two instances correspond to the two *re'iyot* requiring *tumah*; three such formulations correspond to the three *re'iyot* requiring a *korban*. With the numerical derivation handled separately, *hechtim* is again freed to teach the quantity requirement.

The Gemara further notes that yet another position learns both the count of two and three from a single verse—*zot tihyeh tumato be-zovo*—freeing all remaining verses for other derivations, including the basic rule that the flow must exit the body (*ad sheyetzei mibesaro*), that the flow itself is *tamei* (*zovo tamei*), that the flow must be liquid, and—according to Rabbi Yishmael—that it must fill the orifice.

### Semen vs Touch Size

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חֲנִילַאי מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע — לְרוֹאֶה בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ, לְנוֹגֵעַ בְּכַעֲדָשָׁה. וְהָאֲנַן ״מְטַמְּאִין בְּכֹל שֶׁהֵן״ תְּנַן! מַאי לַָאו לְנוֹגֵעַ? לֹא, לְרוֹאֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥanilai says in the name of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon: Semen** imparts impurity **to** the man who **emits** it **in any amount,** whereas **with regard to one who touches** semen, it imparts impurity only **in** the amount of **a lentil-bulk.** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that the emission of a *zav* and a seminal emission **impart impurity in any amount? What, is it not** referring to **one who touches** semen? The Gemara answers: **No,** the mishna is referring **to** the man who **emits** it.

Rav Chanilai in the name of Rabbi Elazar the son of Rabbi Shimon rules: *shichvat zera laro'eh bechol shehu, lanogea ki-ke'adashah*—when semen exits a man's own body (*ro'eh*), any amount, however small, conveys *tumah*; but when another person touches semen (*nogea*), there is a minimum size requirement of a lentil bean (*ke'adashah*). Rav Chanilai derives the *ke'adashah* for touching from the parallel to *sheretz*, as will be developed in the following section.

## Semen Minimum Size

### Mishnah Perspective

The Gemara challenges: does not the Mishnah say *metameim bechol shehu*, the smallest amount causes *tumah*? Does this not include touching? The Gemara answers: no, the Mishnah refers to the *ro'eh* case—when the semen exits the man's own body—not to touching.

### Semen vs Rodent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: חוֹמֶר בְּשִׁכְבַת זֶרַע מִבְּשֶׁרֶץ, וָחוֹמֶר בְּשֶׁרֶץ מִבְּשִׁכְבַת זֶרַע. חוֹמֶר בְּשֶׁרֶץ — שֶׁהַשֶּׁרֶץ אֵין חֲלוּקָה טוּמְאָתוֹ, מָה שֶׁאֵין כֵּן בְּשִׁכְבַת זֶרַע. חוֹמֶר בְּשִׁכְבַת זֶרַע — שֶׁהַשִׁכְבַת זֶרַע מְטַמֵּא בְּכֹל שֶׁהוּא, מָה שֶׁאֵין כֵּן בְּשֶׁרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: There is an element of **stringency** which applies **to** the impurity of **semen that** does not apply **to** the impurity of the carcass of **a creeping animal, and** likewise there is an element of **stringency** which applies **to** the impurity of the carcass of **a creeping animal that** does not apply **to** the impurity of **semen.** The *baraita* elaborates: The **stringency** which applies **to** the carcass of **a creeping animal** but not to semen is **that** with regard to the carcass of **a creeping animal there is no differentiation in its impurity, which is not the case with regard to semen,** as certain types of semen impart impurity while others do not. The **stringency** which applies **to semen** but not to the carcass of a creeping animal is **that semen imparts impurity in any amount, which is not the case with regard to** the carcass of **a creeping animal,** which must be at least a lentil-bulk.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לַָאו לְנוֹגֵעַ? לָא, לְרוֹאֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this *baraita*: **What, is it not** referring **to one who touches** semen, and the *baraita* rules that he is rendered impure by any amount? The Gemara again answers: **No,** the *baraita* is speaking **of** the man who **emits** semen, whereas one who touches it becomes impure only if it is at least as large as a lentil-bulk.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דּוּמְיָא דְּשֶׁרֶץ קָתָנֵי, מָה שֶׁרֶץ בִּנְגִיעָה — אַף שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע בִּנְגִיעָה! אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: שׁוּם שֶׁרֶץ קָתָנֵי, וְשׁוּם שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty with regard to this answer: **But** the *baraita* **teaches** the case of semen as **similar to** that of the carcass of **a creeping animal,** which indicates that **just as** the impurity of the carcass of **a creeping animal** is imparted **by contact, so too,** the impurity discussed in the *baraita* with regard to **semen** is imparted **by contact. Rav Adda bar Ahava said** in response: The *baraita* **teaches the category of** the carcass of **a creeping animal, and** it likewise **teaches the category of semen,** i.e., it is referring to these types of impurity in general, but this does not mean that these types of impurity are contracted in the same manner.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁרֶץ לָא מְטַמֵּא בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ? וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: הָאֵבָרִים אֵין לָהֶם שִׁיעוּר, פָּחוֹת מִכְּזַיִת בְּשַׂר הַמֵּת, וּפָחוֹת מִכְּזַיִת בְּשַׂר נְבֵלָה, וּפָחוֹת מִכַּעֲדָשָׁה מִן הַשֶּׁרֶץ. שָׁאנֵי אֵבֶר — דְּכוּלֵּיהּ בִּמְקוֹם עֲדָשָׁה קָאֵי, דְּהָא אִילּוּ חָסַר פּוּרְתָּא אֵבֶר — מִי קָמְטַמֵּיא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does** the carcass of **a creeping animal not impart impurity in any amount? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Oholot* 1:7): **The limbs** of impure bodies that are whole **have no** minimum **measure** with regard to imparting ritual impurity. Even if a limb is **less than an olive-bulk of** a human **corpse, or less than an olive-bulk of an animal carcass, or less than a lentil-bulk of** the carcass of **a creeping animal,** it imparts ritual impurity. If so, how can the *baraita* state that there is a minimum measure of a lentil-bulk with regard to the impurity of a creeping animal? The Gemara answers that the *halakha* of **a limb is different, as** when **all of it** is intact **it stands in place of a lentil**-bulk, i.e., a whole limb is considered like a lentil-bulk of the carcass of a creeping animal, regardless of its actual size. The proof is **that if** it were **lacking** any slight **bit,** thereby causing this **limb** of a creeping animal to be less than a lentil-bulk, **would it impart impurity?** Certainly not. Clearly, then, the impurity of a limb is due to its wholeness, not its size.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע דַּחֲלוּקָה טוּמְאָתוֹ — מַאי הִיא? אִילֵימָא בֵּין יִשְׂרָאֵל לִדְגוֹיִם — הָכִי נָמֵי אִיכָּא עַכְבָּר דְּיָם וְעַכְבָּר דְּיַבָּשָׁה! אֶלָּא, בֵּין קָטָן לְגָדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches: The stringency that applies to the carcass of a creeping animal but not to semen is that with regard to a creeping animal there is no differentiation concerning its impurity, which is not the case with regard to semen. The Gemara asks: **What is the differentiation** with regard to **the impurity of semen? If we say** that this is referring to the difference **between** the semen of **Jews,** to which this impurity applies, **and** the semen **of gentiles,** to which it does not apply, **there is also** a differentiation with regard to the impurity of the carcass of a creeping animal between **a sea mouse,** which is not impure, and **a land mouse,** which is impure. **Rather,** the *baraita* is referring to the differentiation **between** the emission of **a minor,** which is not classified as semen and which does not render him impure, **and** that of **an adult,** which does render him impure. By contrast, there is no such differentiation with regard to the impurity of the carcass of a creeping animal, as it imparts impurity regardless of its age and size.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כְּתַנָּאֵי, מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת נוֹגֵעַ בְּשִׁכְבַת זֶרַע? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אוֹ אִישׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** The amount of semen which imparts impurity to one who touches it is **subject to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is stated in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **that** the Torah **includes one who touches semen,** in addition to one who emits semen, as impure? **The verse states** with regard to the impurity of the carcass of a creeping animal: **"Or whoever** touches any creeping animal, whereby he may become impure" (Leviticus 22:5). It is inferred from the inclusive phrase "or whoever" that one who touches semen is also impure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגִי תַּנָּאֵי בְּעָלְמָא, דְּאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: דּוּן מִינַּהּ וּמִינַּהּ, וְאִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: דּוּן מִינַּהּ וְאוֹקֵי בְּאַתְרַהּ. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר דּוּן מִינַּהּ וּמִינַּהּ — מָה שֶׁרֶץ בִּנְגִיעָה, אַף שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע בִּנְגִיעָה; וּמִינַּהּ — מָה שֶׁרֶץ בְּכַעֲדָשָׁה, אַף שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע בְּכַעֲדָשָׁה. וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר דּוּן מִינַּהּ וְאוֹקֵי בְּאַתְרַהּ, מָה שֶׁרֶץ בִּנְגִיעָה — אַף שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע בִּנְגִיעָה, וְאוֹקֵי בְּאַתְרַהּ — מָה שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע לָרוֹאֶה בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ, אַף לַנּוֹגֵעַ בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And in general, *tanna'im* disagree** with regard to the possibility of deriving a *halakha* in this manner. **As some say** with regard to a *halakha* that is inferred from another *halakha*: **Infer from it, and** derive the details of the *halakha* **from it** as well. **And some say** with regard to such a *halakha*: **Infer from it, but interpret** the *halakha* according **to its** own **place,** i.e., not all aspects of the source case are applied to this *halakha*. The Gemara explains how this general dispute applies to the case at hand. **According to the one who says** that one **infers from it and** again **from it,** the derivation is as follows: **Just as** the carcass of **a creeping animal** imparts impurity **by contact, so too, semen** imparts impurity **by contact. And** one again infers **from** the impurity of the carcass of a creeping animal that **just as** the carcass of **a creeping animal** imparts impurity **in** the amount of **a lentil-bulk, so too, semen** imparts impurity **in** the amount of **a lentil-bulk.** **And according to the one who says: Infer from it, but interpret** the *halakha* according **to** its own **place,** one derives as follows: **Just as** the carcass of **a creeping animal** imparts impurity **by contact, so too, semen** imparts impurity **by contact. But** one must **interpret** the *halakha* according **to** its own **place: Just as semen** imparts impurity **to the one who emits** it **in any amount, so too,** it imparts impurity **to the one who touches** it **in any amount.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נָתָן לְרַב פָּפָּא: מִמַּאי דְּמֵ״אוֹ אִישׁ״ דְּשֶׁרֶץ קָמְרַבֵּי לֵיהּ? דִּילְמָא מֵ״אוֹ אִישׁ אֲשֶׁר תֵּצֵא מִמֶּנּוּ שִׁכְבַת זָרַע״ קָמְרַבֵּי לֵיהּ, וּדְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא דּוּן מִינַּהּ וּמִינַּהּ? שַׁיְלִינְהוּ לְתַנָּאֵי, אִיכָּא דְּתָנֵי כְּרַב פָּפָּא, וְאִיכָּא דְּתָנֵי כְּרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Natan, said to Rav Pappa: From where** do you infer **that** the *tanna* of the *baraita* **derives** the impurity of one who touches semen **from** the phrase **"or whoever"** that is stated with regard to the impurity **of** the carcass of **a creeping animal? Perhaps he derives it from** the previous verse: **"Or from whoever the flow of seed goes out"** (Leviticus 22:4), **and everyone** agrees that when a *halakha* is derived from a verse dealing with the same matter, one should **infer from it and** derive the details of the *halakha* **from it** as well. If so, the amount of semen that imparts impurity by contact should be derived from the amount that renders the one who emitted it impure, which is any amount. The Gemara relates that the Sages **asked the *tanna'im*,** i.e., those who recite *mishnayot* and *baraitot*, whether the derivation of the *baraita* is from the verse dealing with the carcass of a creeping animal or from the one dealing with semen. They discovered that **there are those who teach** this *halakha* **in accordance with** the assumption of **Rav Pappa,** that it is derived from a creeping animal, **and there are those who teach** this *halakha* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Huna, son of Rav Natan,** that the source is the verse referring to semen.

A *baraita* is then cited that enumerates the ways in which semen is stricter than the *tumah* of a dead rodent (*sheretz*) and the ways in which the rodent is stricter than semen.

The *sheretz* is stricter in that its *tumah* is not divided (*ein chalukah tumato*)—there is no distinction between types of rodents within the eight species that convey *tumah*; a young rodent and an old one are equally *tamei*. Semen, by contrast, is categorically divided: semen from a male under the age of nine years does not convey *tumah*, whereas semen from an adult does. This asymmetry represents the way in which *sheretz* is stricter.

Semen is stricter in that it conveys *tumah* in any amount (*bechol shehu*), whereas the *sheretz* requires the minimum size of a lentil bean (*ke'adashah*). The *baraita* presents this as a direct comparison. The Gemara immediately asks: is this comparison made in the analogous case—touching semen versus touching a *sheretz*? And if so, does this not prove that even touching semen is *bechol shehu*?

The Gemara responds through Rav Adda bar Ahava: the comparison in the *baraita* is not between parallel cases. The *baraita* says *shem sheretz* and *shem shichvat zera*—each in its respective category. When the *baraita* says semen is stricter in being *bechol shehu*, it means in the *ro'eh* context (emission). The comparison with the *sheretz* (which requires *ke'adashah* for touching) is not asserted for the analogous touching case of semen; the *baraita* is comparing a non-analogous situation where, in that situation, semen is stricter.

The Gemara then asks: but is the *sheretz* truly not *bechol shehu* in any case? Does not a Mishnah state that limbs (*evarim*) have no minimum size—so even a fragment of a rodent limb, smaller than a *ke'adashah*, would convey *tumah*? The Gemara answers: a limb is not a case of *bechol shehu* in the relevant sense. When we say a limb conveys *tumah* regardless of size, it is because the limb itself constitutes a qualitative substitute for the *ke'adashah*—it possesses a form of inherent significance that replaces quantitative sufficiency. If any part of the limb is missing, that fragment no longer conveys *tumah*. The *ke'adashah* requirement for a *sheretz* is therefore either quantitative or qualitative; semen, by contrast, conveys *tumah* with genuinely no significance required, *bechol shehu* in the strictest sense.

Rav Pappa raises a further point: *ketannai*—this dispute about whether touching semen requires a minimum amount is in fact a debate among *Tannaim*. The *baraita* asks: from where do we know that touching semen (*nogea beshichvat zera*) conveys *tumah*? The answer: *talmud lomar o ish*—we learn it from the additional word *o ish* in a verse. The question is: which verse containing the words *o ish*?

Rav Pappa assumes the verse is *o asher yiga bechol sheretz asher yitma lo*—the verse about touching a *sheretz*, where an extra *o ish* is used to include touching semen. If so, a structural debate among *Tannaim* determines the *shiur*: some hold *don minah uminah*—learn from the source and retain its context, meaning the *shiur* of *ke'adashah* from *sheretz* is carried over to semen. Others hold *don minah ve'okei be'asra*—learn from the source but apply it in its natural context, meaning once we have derived that touching semen is *tamei*, we now apply the rules of semen: and semen when it exits the body is *bechol shehu*, so touching it is also *bechol shehu*.

Rav Huna the son of Rav Natan challenges Rav Pappa's assumption: perhaps the verse from which we learn that touching semen is *tamei* is *o ish asher yetzei mimenu shichvat zera*, the verse about *shichvat zera* itself, where the extra word *o ish* teaches an additional form of *tumah* via touching. If that is the source verse, then everybody agrees we apply the context of semen—*bechol shehu*—and there is no *tannaitic* dispute. The question of which *o ish* is meant is then put to the *Tanna'im*, the reciters who had memorized the *baraita*. The answer: some of those who had memorized the *baraita* held it referred to the verse of *sheretz*, and some held it referred to the verse of *shichvat zera*.

The upshot: it is undisputed that when semen exits a man's body, *tumah* attaches in any amount. When another person touches semen, there is a genuine dispute—some hold *bechol shehu*, others hold a minimum size of *ke'adashah* is required—and this dispute may trace back to which verse was the original source for deriving that touching semen conveys *tumah*, and whether the *shiur* of the source verse is carried forward or supplanted.
