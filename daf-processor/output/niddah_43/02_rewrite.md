# Niddah 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Beis HaSesarim

### Hidden Places Intro

The daf picks up from the bottom of 42b, continuing the discussion of orifices and hidden places on the body and the distinctions that govern their role in *tumah*. Three categories are relevant. The first is *beis hasesarim*—a place on the body that is not exposed skin; this can include even an area as minor as *beis hakemattim*, the skin normally concealed under a fold of the body. The second is *balua*—something so deeply interior that it is halachically considered nonexistent. The third, and most contested, is the boundary between these two categories, which was the subject of the debate between Abaye and Rava treated in the previous session.

### Sheretz vs Neveilah

Rava opens with a foundational ruling: *sheretz bekumto tahor, neveilah bekumto tamei*—if one carries a dead rodent (*sheretz*) in a bodily fold, one is *tahor*; if one carries an animal carcass (*neveilah*) in such a fold, one is *tamei*. The reasoning turns on the distinct mechanisms by which these two sources of *tumah* operate. A *sheretz* conveys *tumah* only through contact (*negiah*), and contact through a concealed place—*beis hasesarim*—does not count as halachic contact. A *neveilah*, by contrast, conveys *tumah* also through *masa* (carrying), and carrying does not depend on the surface area being exposed. Even if one is not "touching" the *neveilah* in any halachic sense, one is still carrying it, and that suffices for *tumah*.

### Oven Airspace

The Gemara extends this logic to a further case: if one has a *sheretz* concealed in a bodily fold and enters the airspace of a pottery oven (*tanur*), the *tanur* becomes *tamei*. The Gemara notes that this might seem obvious, but a plausible objection exists: the Torah states that what is *inside* a *kli cheres* conveys *tumah* to it, and one might argue that something in a *beis hasesarim* is a kind of "inside within an inside," analogous to a vessel whose walls rise above the walls of the oven, in which case the item would be considered located within the inner container rather than within the oven's airspace. The Gemara rejects this: being in a *beis hasesarim* does not create that kind of insulation, and the *sheretz* is still considered to be within the oven's airspace. The *tanur*, at minimum, becomes *tamei*.

### Zav Moving Objects

The Gemara pivots to a ruling of Reish Lakish bearing on the same distinction. If a *zav* has a reed (*kaneh*) concealed in his bodily fold and uses it to push a *tahor* person, that *tahor* person remains *tahor*. Conversely, if a *tahor* person has a reed concealed in his fold and uses it to move a *zav*, that *tahor* person becomes *tamei*.

The standard rule of *tumat heiset*—the *tumah* of moving—is understood as a derivative of *tumat masa*, carrying. Just as carrying a source of *tumah* makes one *tamei*, so too does moving it, because moving is treated as a form of carrying. This rule, however, applies when it is the *tahor* person who moves the *tamei* object. There is no general principle that a *tamei* object moving a *tahor* person conveys *tumah*. By a *zav*, uniquely, the Torah introduces just such a principle. The verse states: *vechol asher yiga bo hazav—veyadav lo shataf bamayim*, "anything that a *zav* touches, and he has not rinsed his hands in water." From the word *yiga* (touches), the Gemara derives not only literal contact but also movement caused by the *zav*: even when a *zav* moves something, he conveys *tumah* to it.

This explains why the *tahor* person who moves the *zav* with a reed is *tamei*: *tumat heiset* is a form of *masa*, and the concealment of the reed in a fold (*beis hasesarim*) is irrelevant when the mechanism is carrying rather than touching. But the case of the *zav* moving the *tahor* person through a concealed reed is different, because this *heiset*—the *zav*'s moving of the *tahor* object—is derived from the word of touching (*negiah*), not from carrying.

### Moving Concepts

This analysis reveals a structural distinction between two concepts of *heiset*. The ordinary form—a *tahor* person moving a *tamei* object—is a species of *masa*, carrying, and therefore carries no concern of *beis hasesarim*. One can carry something on one's back, through a shirt, under layers of other objects; the medium is irrelevant. The second form—a *zav* moving a *tahor* object—is a special *chiddush* of the Torah, and because it is expressed through the language of *negiah* (touching), it inherits the constraints of touching: it must occur through exposed skin. If the *zav* moves something via a reed concealed in his fold, the contact is through *beis hasesarim* and does not convey *tumah*.

## Men's Fluids

### Zav/Baal Keri Exit

The Gemara now returns to the Mishnah and shifts from the *beis hasesarim* discussion to a separate but related principle: by contrast to a woman's *tumah*, which attaches while the blood is still interior (*bivsara*), a man's *tumah* of *zivah* and seminal emission attaches only when the fluid exits the body. By a *zav*, this is derived from the phrase *ki yazov mibesaro*—"when it flows from his flesh"—as opposed to the woman's *bivsara*, "in her flesh." By a *baal keri*, it is derived from *v'ish ki tetzei mimenu shichvat zera*—"when a seminal emission goes out from him."

### Man Eating Terumah

The Mishnah presents a striking case: a *kohen* who is eating *terumah* and feels that his body is becoming sexually excited and that ejaculation is imminent. Since *tumah* attaches only upon exit from the body, he is not yet *tamei*. The Mishnah rules that he may hold himself down, swallow whatever *terumah* remains in his mouth, and only afterward allow the emission to occur—at which point he is no longer handling *terumah*.

The Gemara raises an immediate difficulty: does not a *baraita* cite Rabbi Eliezer as ruling that anyone who holds his penis while urinating is "as if he brings a flood upon the world," because doing so leads to *zera levatalah* and sexual excitation? The objection is twofold: first, such conduct is generally prohibited; second, and more practically, if holding oneself leads to greater excitement, it would seem to worsen rather than resolve the situation.

### Abaye/Rava Debate

Abaye resolves the difficulty: the Mishnah refers to holding oneself through a thick cloth (*matlit avah*). Since there is no direct skin contact, there is no sexual excitation, and the concern of Rabbi Eliezer does not apply.

Rava goes further: even if the cloth is thin (*matlit rakah*) and some degree of stimulation remains possible, there is no concern. *Keivan d'akar—akar*: once the semen has been uprooted—meaning the process of emission has already begun—it is going to come out regardless. Touching oneself at this stage does not cause additional semen to emerge; what will come out is already defined. The act of holding down is therefore not analogous to the prohibited form of self-stimulation.

Abaye demurs. He fears that even at this advanced stage, insufficient insulation will lead to more semen being produced (*shema yarbe lesofa*). The Gemara presses: does Rava truly not share this concern? A *baraita* elsewhere compares a man touching himself to placing a finger in an eye—as long as the finger is there, the eye continues to tear. So too, as long as stimulation continues, more semen will continue to emerge.

Rava responds: *kol ahmume ve-hadar ahmume be-sha'ata lo shekhiach*—to be heated up and then heated up again at virtually the same moment is not a common occurrence. Rashi understands this as Rava's position that we do not concern ourselves with renewed excitation happening immediately after emission. Whatever will emerge at the present moment is already determined, and subsequent stimulation leading to another emission is too remote a concern to be taken into account. The debate between Abaye and Rava thus turns on how much weight to give the possibility that touching at the very threshold of emission will cause the process to expand.

## Hargasha Criterion

### Sensation Requirement

Shmuel introduces a remarkable principle: *kol shichvat zera she-ein kol gufo margish bah eina metame'et*—any semen that exits the body without the entire body being in a state of excitation does not convey *tumah*. Even actual semen, if it merely dribbles out without any accompanying physical sensation, is *tahor*. The reason: the Torah uses the term *shichvat zera*, which Shmuel understands to mean semen that is *ra'uyah lehazria*—capable of causing conception. Semen that exits without the appropriate physiological process lacks that capacity, and therefore does not fall within the Torah's category of *shichvat zera* that conveys *tumah*.

The importance of the word *hargasha* here should not be understated. The first formulation uses the verb *margish*—"the body is not feeling"—a descriptive phrase about physiological state. This will shortly become the noun *hargasha*, sensation, a term of considerable halakhic weight. Shmuel applies an essentially parallel principle to women: blood that exits without *hargasha* does not convey *tumah*. That discussion lies ahead, but the grounding is already being established here. Both rulings by Shmuel reframe the question: it is not merely about a fluid exiting the body, but about a real physiological experience accompanying that exit. The locus shifts from purely objective—did the fluid leave?—to substantially subjective: did the appropriate sensation occur?

### Night Emission

A challenge is raised from a ruling about nocturnal emission: if a man had sexual thoughts during the night, and upon waking finds his flesh warm, he is deemed *tamei*, having presumably ejaculated in his sleep—even though he was unconscious and experienced no waking sensation.

The Gemara resolves this through the explanation of Rav Huna: the case concerns one who dreamed that he was engaged in intercourse (*mishamshe mitato bachalomo*). Since it is possible to engage in intercourse in a dream without conscious awareness (*de'efshar leshamesh belo hargasha* in the waking sense), the sensation was in fact present—it was simply incorporated into the dream. The body did experience the appropriate physiological excitation; the dreamer was simply unaware of it as a waking experience. This *okimta* saves Shmuel's position: the *hargasha* requirement is satisfied, even if not consciously perceived, as long as the body underwent the relevant process, which was then reflected in the dream.

The significance of Shmuel's framing is underscored by the parallel to women. The same Shmuel who requires *hargasha* for a man's seminal emission to be *tamei* will require *hargasha* for a woman's blood to render her *teme'ah*. In both cases, Shmuel locates the determinative moment not in the objectified fluid but in the subjective experience of the body through which it passed.

### Arrow Ejection

The Gemara presents an alternative version of Shmuel's ruling: *kol shichvat zera she-eino yoreh kacheitz eino metamei*—any semen that does not exit like an arrow, in a forceful stream, does not convey *tumah*. This version shifts the criterion from bodily sensation to the manner of exit itself. Even if sensation accompanied the process, if the semen did not emerge with sufficient force, it is *tahor*.

This is a still more striking *chiddush*. The first version distinguished between qualitatively different types of semen—semen capable of causing conception versus semen that cannot. The second version distinguishes not by quality of semen but by the mechanics of emission. Even semen that is in principle capable of conception does not convey *tumah* if it does not exit in the requisite manner. As the *Tosafot* clarify: the concern is not that such semen literally cannot impregnate, but that it will not reach where it needs to reach. This version places the emphasis entirely on the process and the act of emission rather than the intrinsic properties of the fluid.

### Process Stages

What precisely is the difference between the two versions? The Gemara argues they are not identical: *ikka beinaihu ne'ekra behargasha veyatza shelo behargasha*—the case where the process was uprooted (the *akirah* began) with sensation, but the semen exited without sensation and without a forceful stream. According to the first version, which focuses on whether sensation accompanied the *akirah* (the beginning of the process), this would be *tamei*, since the *hargasha* was present at the outset. According to the second version, which requires the semen to exit like an arrow, this would be *tahor*, since the exit itself lacked that quality.

Rava, notably, regarded this case as an unresolved question: *ne'ekra behargasha veyatza shelo behargasha—mahu?* The Gemara notes that what Shmuel held as obvious (*pshita leh le-Shmuel*) remained a *she'eilah* for Rava. The Gemara offers an apparent resolution from a Mishnah: a *baal keri* who immersed in a *mikveh* but did not urinate beforehand becomes *tamei* when he later urinates, because the urine carries residual semen. That semen exits without the force of the original emission, yet it is *tamei*. The Gemara deflects: *shani hatam de-ruba behargasha nafik*—it is different there, because the bulk of the emission exited with *hargasha*, and the residual drops are considered part of that prior process. The *tumah* of the residual semen is therefore parasitic on the *hargasha* that attended the original emission. This resolution itself is revealing: the halakhic status of semen is determined not only by what it is at the moment of contact but by the process from which it originated—even if that process occurred the previous day.

A third version of Shmuel's statement is then presented: *kol shichvat zera she-eino yoreh kacheitz eino mazria—mazria hu de-la mazria, ha tamuyei matmei*—semen that does not exit like an arrow does not cause conception, but it does convey *tumah*. This version essentially rejects the halakhic significance of the *arrow* criterion for *tumah*: whatever the quality of the semen, and however it exits, the verse *ki yihyeh becha ish asher lo yihyeh tahor mikreh layla*—"if there is a man who is not *tahor* because of a nocturnal occurrence"—implies that any emission, in any manner, conveys *tumah*.

Three positions thus emerge: one that ties *tumah* to conception-capable semen (first version), one that ties it to forcefulness of ejaculation (second version), and one that rejects these limitations entirely (third version). Nonetheless, at least two of the three give significant weight to the concept of *hargasha* and the process of emission, rather than treating the mere presence of semen as the operative fact.

## Conversion Question

### Non-Jew Case

Rava poses a conceptual question: a non-Jew experienced sexual excitement, and the semen was already in the process of being uprooted (*akira*)—fully on its way but not yet exited—when he converted. When the semen subsequently exits, is he *tamei* as a *baal keri*?

The question is framed by the stakes: we have now established that the *akirah*, the beginning of the process, carries halakhic weight. If the *akirah* occurred while he was a non-Jew, it carried no *tumah* significance. Can the exit—now occurring while he is a Jew—nonetheless convey *tumah*? Or is the *akirah* essential to establishing the halakhic process such that, without a valid *akirah*, there is no *tumah* even upon exit?

The Gemara frames it carefully: *im timtza lomar batar akira azlinan*—if we say we follow the *akirah*, then we might distinguish between the case where the *akirah* is used *lechumra* (to be strict—even if the semen dribbles out, as long as the *akirah* was with *hargasha*, it is *tamei*) and the use of the *akirah* *lekula* (to be lenient—where the *akirah* was not halakhically significant, as here, perhaps the exit alone does not suffice). Alternatively, *o dilma lo shana*—perhaps the *akirah* and the exit are independent, and exit alone is determinative regardless. The Gemara concludes: *teiku*. The question remains unresolved.

## Women's Urine

### Zavah Urine

Rava poses a parallel question regarding a *zavah*. In general, a *zavah*'s *tumah* attaches while the blood is still within her body—a rule that, on its face, renders questions about the *akirah* less acute for women. However, a *zavah* also conveys *tumah* through her other bodily fluids, including urine (*me'ayanot*), and these presumably attach when the fluid exits. This creates the opening for the same type of question about process stages.

The case: a *zavah* feels the urge to urinate—the urine has, in the Gemara's language, been "uprooted" (*ne'ekru mimei raglehah*)—and she descends and immerses in a *mikveh*. When she later urinates, is that urine *tamei*? The question is whether the *tumah* of a *zavah*'s urine attaches at the moment of *akirah* (when she first feels the need to urinate, while still a *zavah* before immersion) or only at the moment of exit (after immersion, when she is no longer a *zavah*).

The Gemara notes an important asymmetry between this case and the parallel question about semen: *hani milei shichvat zera de-la mitzi naktei*—with semen, once the process is uprooted, it is effectively impossible to hold it back; the *akirah* and the exit are nearly continuous and clearly part of the same process. Urine, by contrast, *matzi nakit lah*—can be held back indefinitely. On a long road trip, one knows exactly how long this can persist. Since urine can be withheld, one might argue that the *akirah* is a more detached and preliminary stage, less integrated into the eventual exit, and therefore less likely to be determinative. Or, *o dilma lo shana*—perhaps the distinction is irrelevant, and we apply the same rule regardless of whether the fluid could have been held back. *Teiku*. The Gemara reaches no conclusion.

One should note an interesting inversion here. A woman's *tumah* of blood is internal—it attaches *bifnim*. A man's *tumah* of semen is external—it attaches only *bachutz*, upon exit. Yet it is in the man's case that *akirah* acquires significant halakhic weight, while for the woman's urine, the parallel question about *akirah* is left unresolved. The conceptual terrain is not as symmetrical as it might appear.

### Non-Jewish Zavah

Rava adds a further layer: a non-Jewish woman who has the status of a *zavah*. By rabbinic decree, all non-Jewish women are treated as *zavaniyot*, and their urine is therefore *tamei* rabbinically. This woman urinated to the point of *akirah* and then immersed in a *mikveh* to convert. When the urine exits, is it *tamei*?

The Gemara identifies yet another variable: even if one were to resolve the prior question and say that for a Jewish *zavah* we follow the *akirah*—meaning the urine is *tamei* from the moment she felt the need to go, and therefore the *mikveh* does not sever the *tumah*—perhaps for a non-Jewish *zavah* the rule is more lenient. Her *zavah* status is only rabbinic, and her urine's *tumah* is therefore rabbinic. Perhaps the rabbis would not apply the *akirah* principle with equal stringency in a case that is only rabbinic to begin with. Or, *o dilma lo shana*—perhaps the rabbinic/biblical distinction is irrelevant here. *Teiku*. Again, no resolution.

## Zav Flow Size

### Orifice Filling

The Gemara turns to the last section of the Mishnah, which states that *tumah* of semen and blood conveys *tumah* in any amount (*bechol shehu*). Shmuel, whose voice has been dominant throughout this daf, adds a ruling that appears to stand in tension with that Mishnah: *zav tzarich kechatimat pi ha'amah*—by a *zav*, the flow must be sufficient in quantity to fill the orifice of the penis completely. The source, as Shmuel reads it, is the verse *o hechtim besaro mi-zovo*—"or his flesh is sealed through his flow"—which implies that the flow seals, i.e., fills, the orifice.

### Derivation Debate

The Gemara notes that Shmuel's ruling conflicts with the Mishnah, which includes a *zav* among those who convey *tumah* in any amount. The resolution: Shmuel follows the position of Rabbi Natan citing Rabbi Yishmael, as recorded in a *baraita*: *zav tzarich kechatimat pi ha'amah*—the flow must fill the orifice. The majority of sages do not accept this.

The debate between Rabbi Yishmael and the sages turns on how to read the word *hechtim* in the verse *o hechtim besaro mi-zovo*. Rabbi Yishmael reads *hechtim* as requiring that the flow seal (fill) the orifice. The sages read *hechtim* differently: it refers not to a quantity that fills the hole, but rather to the quality of the flow—that it be a liquid (*lach*), not a solid (*yavesh*). The verse teaches that *tumah* attaches only when the flow is in liquid form; once dried, it no longer conveys *tumah* through the primary mechanism.

Rabbi Yishmael, by contrast, derives the liquid requirement from a different word in the verse: *rar*, which itself denotes a dribbling or liquid quality. If *rar* already tells us the flow must be liquid, then *hechtim* is freed to teach the additional requirement of filling the orifice.

The sages counter: they do not learn the liquid requirement from *rar* alone. They read *rar besaro et zovo* and other phrases as contributing to the count of *re'iyot* (episodes of flow) needed to establish *zav* status—two for the seven-day *tumah* and three for the obligation of a *korban*. The verse *o hechtim besaro mi-zovo*, by contrast, they read as an additional statement of *tumah* applying to a partial flow, i.e., one who has seen fewer than three times is already *tamei* for *mishkav u-moshav* even without the *korban*.

Rabbi Yishmael learns the count of two and three from the method of Rabbi Simai, who derives these numbers from the repetition of the words *tumato* ("his *tumah*") paired with different descriptions of the flow: twice the Torah says there is a flow and calls him *tamei*—those two instances correspond to the two *re'iyot* requiring *tumah*; three such formulations correspond to the three *re'iyot* requiring a *korban*. With the numerical derivation handled separately, *hechtim* is again freed to teach the quantity requirement.

The Gemara further notes that yet another position learns both the count of two and three from a single verse—*zot tihyeh tumato be-zovo*—freeing all remaining verses for other derivations, including the basic rule that the flow must exit the body (*ad sheyetzei mibesaro*), that the flow itself is *tamei* (*zovo tamei*), that the flow must be liquid, and—according to Rabbi Yishmael—that it must fill the orifice.

### Semen vs Touch Size

Rav Chanilai in the name of Rabbi Elazar the son of Rabbi Shimon rules: *shichvat zera laro'eh bechol shehu, lanogea ki-ke'adashah*—when semen exits a man's own body (*ro'eh*), any amount, however small, conveys *tumah*; but when another person touches semen (*nogea*), there is a minimum size requirement of a lentil bean (*ke'adashah*). Rav Chanilai derives the *ke'adashah* for touching from the parallel to *sheretz*, as will be developed in the following section.

## Semen Minimum Size

### Mishnah Perspective

The Gemara challenges: does not the Mishnah say *metameim bechol shehu*, the smallest amount causes *tumah*? Does this not include touching? The Gemara answers: no, the Mishnah refers to the *ro'eh* case—when the semen exits the man's own body—not to touching.

### Semen vs Rodent

A *baraita* is then cited that enumerates the ways in which semen is stricter than the *tumah* of a dead rodent (*sheretz*) and the ways in which the rodent is stricter than semen.

The *sheretz* is stricter in that its *tumah* is not divided (*ein chalukah tumato*)—there is no distinction between types of rodents within the eight species that convey *tumah*; a young rodent and an old one are equally *tamei*. Semen, by contrast, is categorically divided: semen from a male under the age of nine years does not convey *tumah*, whereas semen from an adult does. This asymmetry represents the way in which *sheretz* is stricter.

Semen is stricter in that it conveys *tumah* in any amount (*bechol shehu*), whereas the *sheretz* requires the minimum size of a lentil bean (*ke'adashah*). The *baraita* presents this as a direct comparison. The Gemara immediately asks: is this comparison made in the analogous case—touching semen versus touching a *sheretz*? And if so, does this not prove that even touching semen is *bechol shehu*?

The Gemara responds through Rav Adda bar Ahava: the comparison in the *baraita* is not between parallel cases. The *baraita* says *shem sheretz* and *shem shichvat zera*—each in its respective category. When the *baraita* says semen is stricter in being *bechol shehu*, it means in the *ro'eh* context (emission). The comparison with the *sheretz* (which requires *ke'adashah* for touching) is not asserted for the analogous touching case of semen; the *baraita* is comparing a non-analogous situation where, in that situation, semen is stricter.

The Gemara then asks: but is the *sheretz* truly not *bechol shehu* in any case? Does not a Mishnah state that limbs (*evarim*) have no minimum size—so even a fragment of a rodent limb, smaller than a *ke'adashah*, would convey *tumah*? The Gemara answers: a limb is not a case of *bechol shehu* in the relevant sense. When we say a limb conveys *tumah* regardless of size, it is because the limb itself constitutes a qualitative substitute for the *ke'adashah*—it possesses a form of inherent significance that replaces quantitative sufficiency. If any part of the limb is missing, that fragment no longer conveys *tumah*. The *ke'adashah* requirement for a *sheretz* is therefore either quantitative or qualitative; semen, by contrast, conveys *tumah* with genuinely no significance required, *bechol shehu* in the strictest sense.

### Learning Context

Rav Pappa raises a further point: *ketannai*—this dispute about whether touching semen requires a minimum amount is in fact a debate among *Tannaim*. The *baraita* asks: from where do we know that touching semen (*nogea beshichvat zera*) conveys *tumah*? The answer: *talmud lomar o ish*—we learn it from the additional word *o ish* in a verse. The question is: which verse containing the words *o ish*?

Rav Pappa assumes the verse is *o asher yiga bechol sheretz asher yitma lo*—the verse about touching a *sheretz*, where an extra *o ish* is used to include touching semen. If so, a structural debate among *Tannaim* determines the *shiur*: some hold *don minah uminah*—learn from the source and retain its context, meaning the *shiur* of *ke'adashah* from *sheretz* is carried over to semen. Others hold *don minah ve'okei be'asra*—learn from the source but apply it in its natural context, meaning once we have derived that touching semen is *tamei*, we now apply the rules of semen: and semen when it exits the body is *bechol shehu*, so touching it is also *bechol shehu*.

Rav Huna the son of Rav Natan challenges Rav Pappa's assumption: perhaps the verse from which we learn that touching semen is *tamei* is *o ish asher yetzei mimenu shichvat zera*, the verse about *shichvat zera* itself, where the extra word *o ish* teaches an additional form of *tumah* via touching. If that is the source verse, then everybody agrees we apply the context of semen—*bechol shehu*—and there is no *tannaitic* dispute. The question of which *o ish* is meant is then put to the *Tanna'im*, the reciters who had memorized the *baraita*. The answer: some of those who had memorized the *baraita* held it referred to the verse of *sheretz*, and some held it referred to the verse of *shichvat zera*.

The upshot: it is undisputed that when semen exits a man's body, *tumah* attaches in any amount. When another person touches semen, there is a genuine dispute—some hold *bechol shehu*, others hold a minimum size of *ke'adashah* is required—and this dispute may trace back to which verse was the original source for deriving that touching semen conveys *tumah*, and whether the *shiur* of the source verse is carried forward or supplanted.
