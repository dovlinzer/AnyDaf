# Shevuot 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Heter Nedarim Timing

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: נִשְׁבַּע עַל כִּכָּר וַאֲכָלָהּ – אִם שִׁיֵּיר מִמֶּנָּה כְּזַיִת, נִשְׁאַל עָלֶיהָ; אֲכָלָהּ כּוּלָּהּ, אֵין נִשְׁאָל עָלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** If one **took an oath about a loaf and** then **ate it, if** he **left an olive-bulk of it,** he **may request** that his oath **about** the loaf be dissolved. If **he ate the whole** loaf, **he may no longer request** that his oath **about it** be dissolved.

The *daf* opens by continuing the discussion of *ein shvua chal al shvua* — the principle that a redundant oath does not take legal effect on top of an already binding one. The *Gemara* had already established that a second oath, if it adds some additional restriction beyond the first, does take effect, and that if the first oath is retroactively voided through *sheila l'chacham*, the second oath "descends" to fill its place. This sets the stage for a fundamental question about timing: how late in the game may one appeal to a *chacham* to void a *shvua*?

### Rava's Position

Rava addresses this at the bottom of 27b. The case involves someone who took an oath regarding a loaf of bread and then ate from it. Rava rules: *im shiyer heimenu kezayit, nishal aleha* — if a *kezayit* of the loaf still remains, the person may still go to a *chacham* and request *heter nedarim*, since the *shvua* retains current relevance with respect to that remaining portion. But *achala kula, ein nishal alav* — if the entire loaf has been eaten, the moment has passed, and the *chacham* cannot void what has no present application. The principle is clear: *hataras shvua* is a forward-looking institution. It functions only when there is something remaining upon which the *shvua* can still operate.

### Rav Acha Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּאָמַר ״שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״ – מִכְּזַיִת קַמָּא עַבְדֵּיהּ לְאִיסּוּרֵיהּ! אִי דְּאָמַר ״שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכְלֶנָּה״ – מַאי אִירְיָא כְּזַיִת?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi: What are the circumstances? If** one took an oath **where he said: I will not eat** this loaf, which Rava, as cited above, understands as rendering it prohibited for him to eat any part of the loaf, then **with the first olive-bulk** that he ate **he** already **committed his transgression. If** he took an oath **where he said: I will not eat it,** which Rava understands as rendering it prohibited for him to eat only the whole loaf, **why** does Rava mention **specifically** that he left over an **olive-bulk?**

Rav Acha the son of Rava challenges Rava's formulation before Rav Ashi. He notes that Rava's ruling is ambiguous depending on exactly how the oath was worded. If the person said *shelo ochel* — "I will not eat [from this loaf]" — then every *kezayit* constitutes an independent transgression, and someone who ate all but the final *kezayit* has already violated his *shvua* multiple times. Can we truly perform *heter nedarim* for someone who has already transgressed so severely? Alternatively, if the oath was *shelo ochlena* — "I will not eat it," meaning the entire loaf as a unit — then one does not transgress until consuming the whole thing, including the last *kezayit*. In that case, as long as even a tiny crumb remains, the person has not yet violated at all. Why then should the threshold specifically require a *kezayit*? Even less than a *kezayit* remaining should suffice to allow the *sheila*, since the *shvua* is still formally unviolated.

### Two Readings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא ״שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״, אִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא ״שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכְלֶנָּה״. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא ״שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״ – מִיגּוֹ דְּמַהְנְיָא לֵיהּ שְׁאֵלָה אַכְּזַיִת בָּתְרָא, מַהְנְיָא לֵיהּ שְׁאֵלָה נָמֵי אַכְּזַיִת קַמָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi answers: **If you wish, say** that the *halakha* stated by Rava is referring to a case where he took an oath, saying: **I will not eat** this loaf, and **if you wish, say** that it is referring to a case where he took an oath, saying: **I will not eat it.** The Gemara elaborates: **If you wish, say** that the *halakha* stated by Rava is referring to a case where he took an oath, saying: **I will not eat** this loaf. **Since a request** for dissolution **is** still **effective** even **for the last olive-bulk** of the loaf, **it is effective also for the first olive-bulk.**

The *Gemara* opens 28a with a dual response — *iba'it eima shelo ochel, iba'it eima shelo ochlena* — conceding that Rava's ruling is viable under either reading of the oath, and offering a separate justification for each.

Under the reading of *shelo ochel*, where each *kezayit* is a discrete transgression: yes, the person has already violated the oath multiple times over. But there remains one *kezayit* against which the *shvua* is still operative and enforceable. The *heter nedarim* is sought and granted for the sake of that remaining *kezayit* — not as a retroactive remedy for past sins. The institution of *hataras shvua* does not function to undo what has already been done; its purpose is to free the person from ongoing and future restriction. However, once the *heter* is legitimately granted for the remaining *kezayit*, it operates comprehensively: *migu d'mahani l'kezayit*, the voiding of the oath extends to cover the entire *shvua* retroactively as well. The critical point is that there must be *something* to which the *shvua* is currently relevant — a hook for the present — without which the process cannot begin.

### De Minimis Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא ״שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכְלֶנָּה״ – אִי שַׁיַּיר כְּזַיִת, חֲשִׁיב לְאִיתְּשׁוֹלֵי עֲלֵיהּ; וְאִי לָא, לָא חֲשִׁיב לְאִיתְּשׁוֹלֵי עֲלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** that the *halakha* stated by Rava is referring to a case where he took an oath, saying: **I will not eat it. If he left an olive-bulk,** that is a sufficiently **significant** quantity **for which to request** dissolution of the oath. **But if** he did **not** leave that much, it is **not** a sufficiently **significant** quantity **for which to request** dissolution of the oath.

The second reading, *shelo ochlena*, is somewhat harder but equally instructive. If the oath covered the loaf as a whole unit, then as long as any portion remains — even less than a *kezayit* — the person has technically not yet transgressed. So why should the requirement of a *kezayit* matter at all? The *Gemara*'s answer invokes what amounts to a *de minimis* principle: *hishir kezayit, chashiv lei tzaluyei alei; ve'i lo, lo chashiv lei tzaluyei alei* — if a *kezayit* remains, it is a matter of sufficient weight to bring before a *chacham*; if less than a *kezayit* remains, it is simply not important enough to justify the proceeding. The analogy is to bringing a claim before *beis din* for less than a *perutah* — the matter is beneath the threshold of legal concern. So even though the person has not yet violated, if what is at stake is the ability to eat only a fraction of a *kezayit*, the rabbis simply decline to entertain the appeal. The *shvua* remains technically in force, but the residual restriction is too trivial to warrant a *sheila l'chacham*.

### Tosafot Dispute

Tosafot dissents from the assumption embedded in the *Gemara*'s framing. Can one really eat an entire loaf of bread, the argument goes, without leaving behind any crumbs? Tosafot maintains that the practical threshold for transgressing a *shelo ochlena* oath is leaving less than a *kezayit* — anything below that amount is treated as negligible, and at that point the violation is considered to have occurred. Under Tosafot's reading, therefore, the dividing line is stark: if a *kezayit* remains, you have not yet violated, and *hataras shvua* is obviously available; if less than a *kezayit* remains, you have already violated, and it is too late.

This reading, however, sits uncomfortably with the *Gemara* itself, which does not seem to treat the distinction between "a *kezayit* remaining" and "less than a *kezayit* remaining" as representing a bright qualitative line between non-violation and violation. The *Gemara* reasons about both scenarios without suggesting that the former category is entirely permissible and the latter entirely violated — which is precisely what Tosafot's position would imply. Rashi, by contrast, holds that even with less than a *kezayit* remaining, the *shelo ochlena* oath has technically not yet been violated, but the remainder is too negligible to justify a *sheila*. The *Gemara*'s discussion is smoothly consistent with Rashi's approach, even if the exact practical threshold of "crumbs" requires further definition.

## Two Neziruyot Case

### Nazir Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: מִי שֶׁנָּדַר שְׁתֵּי נְזִירוֹת, וּמָנָה רִאשׁוֹנָה וְהִפְרִישׁ עָלֶיהָ קׇרְבָּן, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִשְׁאַל עַל הָרִאשׁוֹנָה – עָלְתָה לוֹ שְׁנִיָּה בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה!
>
> **Translation:** Rava assumes that once one has eaten the entire loaf, it is no longer possible to dissolve the oath. The Gemara **raises an objection** to this from a *baraita*: With regard to **one who took two vows of naziriteship, and counted the first** term and **separated an offering for it, and afterward requested** and received dissolution **of the first** vow from a halakhic authority, **the second** term **was counted for him in** the observance of **the first** term and he is not required to be a nazirite further. Although the first term of naziriteship was entirely finished, a halakhic authority could still dissolve the vow.

The *Gemara* now tests Rava's principle against a case involving a *nazir* who undertook two *neziruyot* in sequence. He completed the first *nezirut*, separated his *korban*, and then sought a *sheila* to void the first *nezirut* retroactively. The *Gemara* had already taught that if the first *nezirut* is voided, the second one "descends" to fulfill what was the first's slot. The question is whether this case poses a challenge to Rava — since the first *nezirut* appears to be entirely in the past at the time of the *sheila*.

### First Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּשֶׁלֹּא כִּיפֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **What are we dealing with here?** We are dealing with a case **where he has not** yet **atoned,** i.e., he has not yet brought the offerings that one brings at the conclusion of naziriteship.

The *Gemara*'s initial response is that *hacha mai askinan bishelo kipper* — the case is referring to one where the *nazir* has not yet brought his *korban*. Since the *korban* is still pending, the *shvua* retains present relevance, and there is no challenge to Rava at all.

### Baraita Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: כִּיפֵּר! בְּשֶׁלֹּא גִּלַּח – וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא, דְּאָמַר תִּגְלַחַת מְעַכְּבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that one can still dissolve his vow of naziriteship after **he has atoned?** The Gemara answers: It is a case **where he** has brought the offerings but **has not** yet **shaved** his hair, **and** this is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, who says: Shaving is indispensable** to the completion of naziriteship.

A more explicit difficulty then arrives: a *Baraita* teaches that the *sheila* may be performed even *after* the *korban* has been brought — *ha tanya kipper*. If the *korban* has already been offered, what future relevance remains? This appears to be a direct contradiction of Rava's principle.

### Shaving Component

The *Gemara* deflects by saying *bishelo gilach* — the *nazir* has not yet shaved his head, which is the concluding act of *nezirut*. According to Rabbi Eliezer, *tiglachas me'akeva* — the shaving is indispensable to the fulfillment of *nezirut*. Since something essential still remains undone, the *nezirut* has not been fully concluded, and the *sheila* therefore still operates upon something currently relevant. This preserves Rava's framework: the voiding is performed not on a purely concluded matter, but on something whose implications are still unresolved.

### Amemar Disagrees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: גִּלַּח! אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: נְזִירוֹת קָא רָמֵית? מִי גָּרַם לַשְּׁנִיָּה שֶׁלֹּא תָּחוּל – רִאשׁוֹנָה; וְאֵינָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that one can still dissolve his vow of naziriteship after **he has shaved? Rav Ashi said: Are you comparing naziriteship** to oaths? **What caused the second** naziriteship **to not take effect** until now? It was **the first** naziriteship, **and** once it has been dissolved, **it is no** longer a factor. Since the observance of the naziriteship term is the same whether it is counted for the second or the first, the first term of naziriteship can be regarded as not yet having started and that is why it can be dissolved. By contrast, in the case of the oath, once he ate the loaf, his oath is no longer extant at all.

A further *Baraita* pushes back even harder, indicating that the *sheila* works even after the shaving — *ha tanya gilach*. At this point, Amemar breaks with Rava's position altogether:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמֵּימָר אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ אֲכָלָהּ כּוּלָּהּ, נִשְׁאָל עָלֶיהָ; אִי בְּשׁוֹגֵג – מְחוּסָּר קׇרְבָּן, אִי בְּמֵזִיד – מְחוּסָּר מַלְקוֹת. אֲבָל כְּפָתוּהוּ עַל הָעַמּוּד – לָא; כְּדִשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כְּפָתוּהוּ עַל הָעַמּוּד וְרָץ מִבֵּית דִּין – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar said,** in contrast to the opinion of Rava: **Even** if **he ate the entire** loaf **he may still request** dissolution of the oath. **If** he ate it **unwittingly,** i.e., he forgot the oath, it is a situation where he **has not yet** brought the **offering** he is liable to bring. **If** he ate it **intentionally,** it is a situation where he **has not yet** received **lashes. But if he was** already **tied to the stake** in order to receive lashes, he can **no** longer request that his oath be dissolved, **in accordance with** the opinion **of Shmuel. As Shmuel says:** If one **had** already **been tied to the stake** in order to receive lashes, **and** he **ran** away **from the court** and escaped, he is **exempt** from receiving lashes, as being tied to the stake is regarded as the beginning of receiving the lashes; once he has escaped, he is treated as though he were already flogged.

His reasoning is that it is never truly "all in the past." If the violation was *beshogeig*, the person remains obligated to bring a *korban*, and having the *sheila* granted would exempt him from that obligation — that is present relevance. If the violation was *bemeizid*, he faces potential *malkos* from *beis din*, and the *sheila* has present relevance in staving off that punishment. Thus, Amemar expands the definition of present relevance rather than abandoning the requirement entirely.

### Rav Ashi Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הִיא; הָתָם רָץ, הָכָא לָא רָץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **And** that **is not so.** Even if he was tied to the stake he can still have his oath dissolved. **There,** with regard to his exemption from receiving lashes after **he ran** away, the original flogging is over and there is no need to initiate a new one. **Here,** with regard to dissolving the oath, **he did not run,** and since he is still subject to lashes, he can still have his oath dissolved.

Rav Ashi offers a more elegant answer that preserves Rava's position without Amemar's expansions. He argues that the case of two *neziruyot* is simply not analogous to the case of a single *shvua* that is entirely spent. The question to pose is: *mi garam lishniyah shelo tachol, rishonah ve'einah?* — what is it that prevented the second *nezirut* from taking effect in its own right? It was the first one. As long as the second *nezirut* is still ongoing, the first one retains living relevance — it is the direct cause of the present obligation. If the first *nezirut* is voided, the second one would have been the first all along, and the person is now free from the burden of a second term. There is thus a tangible and present reason to seek the *sheila*: the first *nezirut* is currently making its weight felt through the ongoing second one. This is entirely different from a person who took a single *shvua*, violated it fully, and can point to no present consequence whatsoever.

### Beating Post Limit

The discussion of Amemar's position raises a further boundary case. Suppose someone violated his *shvua bemeizid*, and has now been bound to the post in *beis din* to receive *malkos* — the witnesses have testified, the warning was given, and the lashing is about to begin. May he appeal to a *chacham* at that very moment? The *Gemara* cites Shmuel's ruling: *kaftuhu al ha'amud, veratz mibeis din, patur* — if one was bound to the post and managed to flee *beis din*, he is exempt from further lashing, since the humiliation of the flight (*venikla achicha l'einecha*) substitutes for the punishment. One might think that being bound to the post therefore has the status of having already received the *malkos*, and that it is accordingly too late to seek a *sheila*. The *Gemara* rejects this: *hacha lo ratz* — here, the person has not fled. The exemption Shmuel speaks of arises from the running, not from the binding itself. Until the lashes are actually administered, the punishment remains pending, the *shvua* retains present relevance through its threatened consequence, and the appeal to the *chacham* remains theoretically available — a striking parallel to the idea that *teshuva* remains possible until the very last moment.

## Conditional Vows

### Setup & Consciousness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל כִּכָּר זוֹ אִם אוֹכַל זוֹ״, וְאָכַל אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה בְּשׁוֹגֵג וְהַשְּׁנִיָּה בְּמֵזִיד – פָּטוּר. רִאשׁוֹנָה בְּמֵזִיד וּשְׁנִיָּה בְּשׁוֹגֵג – חַיָּיב. שְׁתֵּיהֶן בְּשׁוֹגֵג – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** If one says: On my **oath I will not eat that loaf if I eat this one, and** then **he ate the first** one, i.e., the loaf whose consumption was the condition for the oath taking effect, **unwittingly, and** ate **the second intentionally,** he is **exempt.** Since he fulfilled the condition unintentionally, the oath does not take effect, as it was without full intent. But if he ate the **first intentionally,** knowing that if he eats it it will be prohibited for him to eat the other loaf, **and** he then ate the **second unwittingly,** he is **liable** to bring an offering for breaking his oath unwittingly. If he ate them **both unwittingly** he is **exempt,** as the oath does not take effect when he fulfills the condition unwittingly.

Rava introduces a new case: *shevuah shelo ochal kikar zo im ochal zo* — an oath structured as "I will not eat loaf B if I eat loaf A." Loaf A functions as the *tnai* (the trigger condition) and loaf B is the object of the *shvua* itself. The oath does not restrict B automatically; it only becomes binding upon B when A is eaten. This conditional structure introduces a layer of complexity that the *Gemara* now unpacks, and the key analytical tool — articulated most clearly by Tosafot — concerns the requirement of conscious awareness at critical junctures.

### Tosafot Framework

The foundational premise, as Tosafot frames it, is this: since the *shvua* on B does not take effect automatically upon being uttered but only upon the eating of A, the act of eating A is not merely a behavioral trigger — it is constitutively part of the act of *taking* the *shvua*. The *shvua* is not fully formed without the trigger being consciously pulled. From this follows a remarkable requirement: to bring a *korban* for violating B, one must have been *beshogeg* — having forgotten the oath — when eating B. But to have the *shvua* take effect on B in the first place, one must have been *bemeizid*, i.e., fully conscious and aware, when eating A. If A was eaten inadvertently, with no awareness that doing so would activate the prohibition on B, the *shvua* simply never crystallized, and there is no violation of B regardless of the circumstances in which B is subsequently eaten.

### Four Combinations

Four combinations of *shogeg* and *meizid* across A (the trigger) and B (the forbidden act) exhaust the possibilities.

### Shogeg Trigger

If A was eaten *beshogeg* — without awareness that it was activating a *shvua* — the conditional *shvua* never takes effect. Regardless of what state of mind accompanied the eating of B, there is no liability: *patur*. The conversation ends there.

### Both Mezid

If A was eaten *bemeizid* (the trigger was consciously pulled) and B was eaten *beshogeg* (the person had forgotten his oath by the time he ate B), this is the paradigmatic case of liability: the *shvua* was properly activated, the violation was inadvertent, and a *korban* is required.

If both A and B were eaten *bemeizid*, the analysis becomes sensitive to order — specifically, which was eaten first.

### Order & Warning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁתֵּיהֶן בְּמֵזִיד – אַכְלֵיהּ לִתְנָאֵיהּ וַהֲדַר אַכְלֵיהּ לְאִיסּוּרֵיהּ, מִיחַיַּיב; אַכְלֵיהּ לְאִיסּוּרֵיהּ וַהֲדַר אַכְלֵיהּ לִתְנָאֵיהּ, פְּלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ – לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הַתְרָאַת סָפֵק שְׁמָהּ הַתְרָאָה, חַיָּיב; לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לָאו שְׁמָהּ הַתְרָאָה, פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where he ate **both of them intentionally,** if he **ate** the loaf whose consumption was **his condition and then ate the forbidden** loaf, **he is liable** to receive lashes. If he **ate the forbidden** loaf **and then ate** the loaf whose consumption was **his condition,** his liability is the subject of **a dispute between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish. According to the one who says that an uncertain forewarning is deemed** a valid **forewarning,** he is **liable** to receive lashes. **According to the one who says** that an uncertain forewarning **is not deemed** a valid **forewarning,** he is **exempt.** Since when he was forewarned for eating the forbidden loaf it was uncertain whether it would actually become forbidden, that forewarning is not sufficient for him to be liable to receive lashes.

If A (the trigger) was eaten first *bemeizid*, and then B was eaten *bemeizid* with full awareness that the *shvua* was already in effect, this is a straightforward willful violation, and *malkos* applies.

The more complex case arises when B is eaten first — while it is still entirely permissible — and only afterward is A eaten, thereby retroactively triggering the *shvua* and making the earlier eating of B into a transgression. If both acts were *bemeizid*, one might think a warning (*hasra'ah*) could have been issued when B was being eaten, saying: if you subsequently eat A, this eating of B will have constituted a violation. Whether such a *hasra'ah* is valid is the subject of the famous debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish regarding *hasra'as safek* — a conditional warning given when it is uncertain at the time whether a violation will actually occur. According to the opinion that *hasra'as safek shmah hasra'ah*, such a warning suffices for *malkos*; according to the opposing view, it does not, and the person is *patur* from lashes.

There is, however, something genuinely uncomfortable about applying Reish Lakish's position here. In the standard *hasra'as safek* case, the person is typically already in the process of an act that may or may not mature into a transgression — one can see how a warning at that moment captures the spirit of *hasra'ah*. Here, by contrast, when B was being eaten, the act was entirely permissible. The person had made no decision to violate anything. Giving a warning at that point — "if you later eat A, this eating of B will have been a sin" — does not seem to correspond to the usual function of *hasra'ah*, which is to confront the person at the moment of apparent transgression and elicit a conscious act of defiance. The person eating B is doing nothing wrong; it strains the concept of *hasra'ah* to treat that as the moment of violation. This remains a genuine analytical tension.

### Rashi Alternative

Rashi offers a different reading of the *Gemara* that does not require the trigger specifically to be *bemeizid*. Rather, Rashi holds that whichever of the two loaves is eaten *first* must be done with conscious awareness. This is a somewhat harder position to explain conceptually — Tosafot finds it difficult to justify why chronological priority should determine the consciousness requirement rather than the functional role of trigger versus forbidden act. The elegance of Tosafot's reading, however imprecise its handling of the term *rishona*, is that it maps neatly onto a coherent principle: the act that brings the *shvua* into being requires consciousness; the act that violates it requires *shegagah* for the *korban* to apply.

### Rabbeinu Tam

Rabbeinu Tam contributes an additional *chiddush*: one need not eat the trigger *bemeizid* for the *shvua* to take effect on B. His scenario involves eating A consciously and permissibly — since at the moment of eating A no transgression is occurring — and then eating B while having completely forgotten the entire conditional stipulation. Rabbeinu Tam rules that in such a case the person brings a *korban*, treating the entire sequence as *beshogeg*. The reasoning is that what rendered the eating of B a transgression was the prior eating of A, and since the person had no memory of the stipulation when B was eaten, the whole act is colored by that inadvertence and classified as *shogeg* — even though the eating of A itself was entirely intentional.

## Reciprocal Conditions

### Dual Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּלָאָן זוֹ בָּזוֹ – ״לֹא אוֹכַל זוֹ אִם אוֹכַל זוֹ״, ״לֹא אוֹכַל זוֹ אִם אוֹכַל זוֹ״; וְאָכַל זוֹ בִּזְדוֹן עַצְמָהּ וּבְשִׁגְגַת חֲבֶירְתָּהּ, וְזוֹ בִּזְדוֹן עַצְמָהּ וּבְשִׁגְגַת חֲבֶירְתָּהּ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** If one took an oath with regard to two loaves such that he **rendered them interdependent, this** one **on that** one, saying: **I will not eat that if I eat this,** and: **I will not eat this if I eat that, and he ate this one intentionally** with regard to **itself,** i.e., at the time he ate it he was aware that he had taken an oath that would render it forbidden if he ate the other, **but unwittingly** with regard to **the other,** i.e., he did not remember that in eating it he rendered the second one forbidden, **and** then he ate **that one intentionally** with regard to **itself but unwittingly** with regard to **the other,** he is **exempt,** as both conditions were fulfilled only unintentionally.

The *Gemara* now introduces a doubly interlocked case: *lo ochel zo im ochel zo, lo ochel zo im ochel zo* — "I will not eat A if I eat B, and I will not eat B if I eat A." This creates two superimposed conditional structures, each of which designates the other loaf as its trigger and as its object respectively. The analysis now runs through the same four-quadrant grid, but applied simultaneously to both conditions.

### Dual Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זוֹ בְּשִׁגְגַת עַצְמָהּ וּבְזָדוֹן חֲבֶירְתָּהּ, וְזוֹ בְּשִׁגְגַת עַצְמָהּ וּבִזְדוֹן חֲבֶירְתָּהּ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** If he ate **this** one **unwittingly** with regard to **itself,** having forgotten that it would be forbidden if he ate the other, **but intentionally** with regard to **the other,** understanding that with his action he rendered the other forbidden, **and that** one **unwittingly** with regard to **itself but intentionally** with regard to **the other,** he is **liable** to bring offerings for unwittingly breaking his oaths, as the conditions were fulfilled intentionally and the oaths took effect.

The variables are: the state of mind when eating each loaf in its capacity as trigger for the *other* loaf's prohibition, and the state of mind when eating each loaf in its capacity as the forbidden object.

### Both Shogeg

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁתֵּיהֶן בְּשׁוֹגֵג – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** If he ate **both of them unwittingly** he is **exempt,** as both conditions were fulfilled only unintentionally.

If in both instances the trigger was eaten *beshogeg* — without awareness that eating it would activate the prohibition on the other — neither conditional *shvua* takes effect, and there is no liability whatsoever.

### Both Mezid Triggers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁתֵּיהֶן בְּמֵזִיד – אַשְּׁנִיָּה מִיחַיַּיב, אַרִאשׁוֹנָה פְּלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** If he ate **both of them intentionally, he is liable** to receive lashes **for** eating **the second** loaf, while **for the first** loaf his status depends on the **dispute between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish** with regard to an uncertain forewarning.

If both triggers were eaten *bemeizid* but both prohibited acts were committed *beshogeg*, the person brings two separate *korbanos*, one for each violation.

### Mixed Intent

If the triggers were *meizid* in one direction and *shogeg* in the other, only the *shvua* activated by the *meizid* trigger takes effect. The *shvua* on the other side never crystallized, and only one *korban* or one set of consequences applies.

### Complete Mezid

If everything was *bemeizid* throughout, the analysis returns to the question of order. The eating that occurred *after* its respective trigger was activated constitutes a clear and contemporaneous willful violation — *malkos* applies there. The eating that occurred *before* its trigger was subsequently activated presents the same *hasra'as safek* problem: it is subject to the debate of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish as to whether that retroactive violation can carry *malkos* in its wake.

## Conditional Consciousness

### Four Vow Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מָרִי, אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: אַרְבָּעָה נְדָרִים הִתִּירוּ חֲכָמִים – נִדְרֵי זֵרוּזִין, נִדְרֵי הֲבַאי, נִדְרֵי שְׁגָגוֹת, נִדְרֵי אֳונָסִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mari said: We learn** in the mishna (*Nedarim* 20b) **as well** that if one takes an oath with a condition but then fulfills the condition only unwittingly, he is exempt: **The Sages dissolved four** types of **vows** without the requirement of a request to a halakhic authority: **Vows of exhortation, vows of exaggeration, unwitting vows, and vows** whose fulfillment is impeded by **circumstances beyond** one's **control.**

The *Gemara* brings support from the *Mishna* concerning *nidrei shegagos* — one of the four categories of vows that the Sages permit to be dissolved — alongside *nidrei ziruzin*, *nidrei havai*, and *nidrei onasin*.

### Forgotten Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִדְרֵי שְׁגָגוֹת כֵּיצַד? ״קוּנָּם אִם אָכַלְתִּי וְאִם שָׁתִיתִי״, וְנִזְכַּר שֶׁאָכַל וְשָׁתָה; ״שֶׁאֵינִי אוֹכֵל שֶׁאֵינִי שׁוֹתֶה״, שָׁכַח וְאָכַל וְשָׁתָה – מוּתָּר. וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁנִּדְרֵי שְׁגָגוֹת מוּתָּרִין, כָּךְ שְׁבוּעוֹת שְׁגָגוֹת מוּתָּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna elaborates (see *Nedarim* 25b): **Unwitting vows, how so?** If one says: A certain item is **forbidden** to me **like an offering [*konam*] if I ate or if I drank, and** he then **remembers that he ate or drank,** or if one says: This loaf is *konam* for me if **I** will **eat or** if **I** will **drink, and he** then **forgets and eats or drinks,** the item **is permitted. And it is taught** in a *baraita* **with regard to** that mishna: **Just as unwitting vows are dissolved, so are unwitting oaths dissolved,** since he fulfilled the condition while lacking awareness that he was doing so.

The *Mishna* illustrates *nidrei shegagos* with the following case: a person declares, "*konam* if I have eaten or drunk today," believing he had not eaten, and the bread he designated becomes forbidden contingent on that past condition. He then remembers that he did in fact eat or drink. The *neder* is treated as never having taken effect, because at the moment of the declaration, the speaker's understanding of reality was mistaken in a way that negates the binding intent. The *Mishna* then pivots to a forward-conditional version: *she'ani ochel she'ani shoteh* — "if I will eat or drink" — where the condition is yet to occur.

### Trigger Consciousness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁבוּעוֹת שְׁגָגוֹת הֵיכִי דָּמֵי – לָאו כִּי הַאי גַּוְונָא? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances** of **unwitting oaths? Is it not a case like this,** where he takes an oath with a condition and then fulfills the condition of the oath unwittingly? **Conclude from** that mishna that there is support for Rava's opinion.

Here is the *chiddush*: if the person subsequently forgot about his stipulation entirely and proceeded to eat and drink, the *neder* is nonetheless *muttar* — permissible. The conditional *neder* did not take effect, because the trigger was pulled without conscious awareness that it was a trigger. This supports the broader principle: when a *shvua* or *neder* is structured around a future condition, the person must be conscious of the stipulation at the moment the condition is fulfilled. If that awareness is absent, the vow never fully crystallizes into a binding prohibition.

### Baraita Proof

The *Baraita* confirms the application: *keshem shenidrei shegagos muttarim kach shvuos shegagos muttaros* — just as inadvertent *nedarim* are permissible, so too inadvertent *shvuos*. The *Baraita* then asks *heichi dami* — how exactly does a *shvuat shegagos* work? — and answers with precisely this scenario: a *shvua* conditioned on a future event, where the condition was triggered without the person's awareness of the stipulation. *Shema mina* — this is solid support for the principle that conscious triggering is required.

## Eifa's Story

### Identical Past Vows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֵיפָא תָּנֵי שְׁבוּעוֹת בֵּי רַבָּה. פְּגַע בֵּיהּ אֲבִימִי אֲחוּהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אָכַלְתִּי״ ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אָכַלְתִּי״, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִישְׁתַּבַּשְׁתְּ, הֲרֵי יָצְאָה שְׁבוּעָה לַשֶּׁקֶר!
>
> **Translation:** It is related that the Sage **Eifa learned** tractate ***Shevuot* in the academy of Rabba. His brother Avimi met him** and tested him concerning the *halakhot* of oaths. Avimi **said to him:** If one says: On my **oath I did not eat,** and then again: On my **oath I did not eat, what is** the *halakha*? Eifa **said to him: He is liable only once** if he ate. Avimi **said to him: You have confused** the issue. Since the oaths are about the past, it is not a question of whether the second oath takes effect. Each time, **a false oath was issued,** and each was a separate transgression.

The *Gemara* now presents a charming but halachically substantive anecdote. Eifa, a scholar who studied *Shevuot* in Rabba's academy, was tested by his brother Avimi on the material. Avimi asked: if someone takes a *shvua* twice that he did not eat in the past — "*shvua shelo achalti, shvua shelo achalti*" — is he liable for both? Eifa answered that he is liable for only one, applying the principle of *ein shvua chal al shvua*.

### Past Vows False

Avimi corrected him: *ishtabishata* — you have been sloppy. The principle of *ein shvua chal al shvua* applies only to future-oriented oaths, where the question is whether a second restriction can layer atop an already existing one. A past-oriented *shvua* is a different creature entirely. The moment the first false *shvua* is uttered, the person is liable for *shvuat shav*; the oath has no ongoing force, and when the second one is uttered, it is likewise an independent false *shvua*. There is no mechanism of "one riding on top of the other" since past *shvuos* impose no lasting prohibition — each is a discrete act of false swearing, and liability attaches to each one separately.

### Nine and Ten

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל תֵּשַׁע וְעֶשֶׂה״, מַהוּ? חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִישְׁתַּבַּשְׁתְּ, אִי תֵּשַׁע לָא אָכֵיל, עֶשֶׂה לָא אָכֵיל!
>
> **Translation:** Avimi asked him further: If one said: On my **oath I will not eat nine** pieces **and** on my oath I will not eat **ten, what is** the *halakha*? Eifa replied: **He is liable for each and every one** of the oaths, as the scope of the second oath is broader than that of the first. Avimi **said to him: You have confused** the issue: **If he may not eat nine, he may not eat ten.** The oath not to eat ten cannot take effect, since it is an action already prohibited by the oath not to eat nine.

Having corrected his brother, Avimi pressed further. He asked: if someone first takes a *shvua* not to eat nine olives, and then takes a *shvua* not to eat ten — what is the law? Primed by the previous correction to expect double liability, Eifa answered: *chayav al kol achas ve'achas* — liable for each one.

### Not Broader

Avimi corrected him: *ishtabishata*. Ten is not actually a broader restriction than nine. Since one cannot eat ten without having already eaten nine, the prohibition of nine entirely subsumes the prohibition of ten: *tesha lo ochal asara lo ochil* — if you cannot eat nine, you certainly cannot eat ten. The second oath adds nothing; it cannot take effect as a *chol al shvua* because it does not enlarge the scope of the prohibition at all.

### Ten Then Nine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל עֶשֶׂה וָתֵשַׁע״, מַהוּ? אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִישְׁתַּבַּשְׁתְּ, עֶשֶׂה הוּא דְּלָא אָכֵיל, הָא תֵּשַׁע מִיהָא אָכֵיל!
>
> **Translation:** Avimi asked him further: If one said: On my **oath I will not eat ten and** on my oath I will not eat **nine, what is** the *halakha*? Eifa replied: **He is liable** for **only one** oath. Avimi **said to him: You have confused** the issue. According to the first oath, it **is ten that he may not eat, but he may still eat nine,** so the second oath takes effect, in that it prohibits him from eating nine.

Now Avimi turned the tables. He asked about the reverse: first a *shvua* not to eat ten, then a *shvua* not to eat nine. Having been twice corrected toward single liability, Eifa answered: *eino chayav ella achas* — only one. Avimi corrected him a third time: *ishtabishata*.

### Nine More Restrictive

In the case of ten-then-nine, the second oath is actually *more* restrictive, not less. When the person swore not to eat ten, he could still eat nine freely — there is no violation until the tenth olive is consumed. The second oath, forbidding nine olives, now prohibits something the first oath left entirely permissible: eating nine olives without proceeding to the tenth. *Eser hu d'lo ochil, hatesha mi ochil?* — "It was ten I could not eat; who said anything about nine?" The second oath therefore genuinely adds a restriction and takes effect on its own terms. The brother had been tricked into overcorrecting in exactly the opposite direction — a lesson in thinking each case through on its own merits rather than applying a pattern from the previous case.

## Korban Separation

### Abaye Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: זִימְנִין דְּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ לְהָא דְּעֵיפָא, כִּדְמָר. דְּאָמַר רַבָּה: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל תְּאֵנִים וַעֲנָבִים״, וְחָזַר וְאָמַר: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל תְּאֵנִים״;
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** There are **times when you find that** the ruling **of Eifa** with regard to an oath not to eat ten followed by an oath not to eat nine applies, **as** in the case mentioned by **the Master. As Rabba says:** In the case of one who says: On my **oath I will not eat figs and grapes** together, **and then says:** On my **oath I will not eat figs,**

Abaye now intervenes to defend Eifa's final, apparently erroneous answer, at least in a narrow scenario. He says: *zimnin d'mishkachas lah, leha d'Eifa kedemar* — there are circumstances in which Eifa's statement, that the ten-then-nine structure yields only a single liability, would actually be correct. Abaye grounds this in a ruling of Rava.

### Figs & Grapes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָכַל תְּאֵנִים וְהִפְרִישׁ קׇרְבָּן, וְאַחַר כָּךְ אָכַל עֲנָבִים לְחוֹדַיְיהוּ – הָוְיָא לְהוּ עֲנָבִים חֲצִי שִׁיעוּר, וְאַחֲצִי שִׁיעוּר לָא מִיחַיַּיב קׇרְבָּן. הָכָא נָמֵי, כְּגוֹן דְּאָמַר: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל עֶשֶׂה״, וְחָזַר וְאָמַר: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל תֵּשַׁע״, וְאָכַל תֵּשַׁע וְהִפְרִישׁ קׇרְבָּן, וְאַחַר כָּךְ אָכַל עֲשִׂירִית; הָוְיָא לַהּ עֲשִׂירִית חֲצִי שִׁיעוּר, וְאַחֲצִי שִׁיעוּר לָא מִיחַיַּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **and he** unwittingly **ate figs and set aside an offering** for breaking the second oath, **and afterward he** unwittingly **ate grapes alone,** he is exempt from liability for breaking the first oath. **The grapes are** tantamount to **a half-measure** of his first oath, which was not to eat figs and grapes together, **and one is not liable** to bring **an offering for a half-measure. Here also, where he said:** On my **oath I will not eat ten, and then said:** On my **oath I will not eat nine, and he ate nine and set aside an offering** for breaking his second oath, **and later he ate** the **tenth,** this **tenth is** tantamount to **a half-measure** of his first oath, **and one is not liable** to bring an offering **for a half-measure.**

Rava's ruling addresses the following case: someone takes a *shvua* not to eat figs and grapes together — meaning a *kezayit* of figs *combined with* a *kezayit* of grapes. He then takes a second *shvua* not to eat figs alone. He eats the figs *beshogeg*, transgressing the second oath, and separates out a *korban* for that violation. He then eats the grapes separately. One might expect that the grapes, combined with the earlier eating of figs, would now constitute a violation of the first oath as well. But the *Gemara* rules otherwise: *havei lehu anavim chatzi shiur, v'chatzi shiur lo mechayeiv korban* — the grapes now constitute only a half-measure. The moment a *korban* was separated for the figs, the figs became exclusively identified with that specific violation. They are now "in their own box," categorically associated with the second *shvua* and its *korban*, and can no longer combine with other foods to create a new compound violation. The grapes alone are less than a *kezayit* toward any relevant threshold, and *chatzi shiur* does not generate *korban* liability.

### Half-Measure

The operative principle is that separating a *korban* segregates the violation. Once a *korban* is set aside for a specific transgression, the substance consumed in that transgression loses its capacity to function as part of a different combined violation. The remainder — whatever was not part of the act for which the *korban* was separated — now stands alone, and if alone it falls below the *shiur*, no additional liability follows.

### Nine & Ten Korban

Abaye applies the same logic to Eifa's case. Even though swearing not to eat ten and then not to eat nine should in principle create two distinct prohibitions — the second oath being the more restrictive one — there is a scenario in which only one *korban* results. If the person ate nine olives *beshogeg*, transgressing the second oath, and then separated a *korban* for that violation, and then ate the tenth olive, the tenth olive standing alone is *chatzi shiur* with respect to the first oath. It cannot combine with the now-segregated nine to form a compound violation of the first oath. In such a case, Eifa's answer of a single liability would be correct, even if he arrived at it by faulty reasoning.

### Tosafot Specificity

Tosafot adds a further nuance concerning the conditions under which a second oath takes effect at all. If the first oath forbids eating any nine out of a larger group, and the second oath forbids all ten, one can argue that the second oath adds nothing because one cannot eat ten without eating nine. But if the first oath was phrased to identify *specific* olives — "these nine" — and the second oath then includes a tenth olive that was previously excluded, the second oath explicitly encompasses something the first did not. Even though eating ten still requires eating nine, Tosafot argues that the specificity of the new inclusion is itself halachically significant: the second oath can be said to forbid a distinct object not referenced in the first, and on those grounds it takes effect even though the only path to transgressing it runs through the already-forbidden nine. This same principle would apply in the figs-and-grapes scenario where Tosafot reads the case differently than Rashi — if the first oath referred specifically to *zeitim* (olives) and the second added *anavim* (grapes), the addition of a specific new object may be sufficient for the second oath to bind, even though one cannot transgress the combined prohibition without eating the already-forbidden *zeitim*.
