# Bava Metzia 90 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Terumah/Ma'aser Exemption

### Terumah/Ma'aser Threshing

The sugya opens at the very end of 89b with a *tana rabbanan* and continues into 90a, which forms the primary focus of this shiur. The *braisa* introduces the case of *parot hameraksot batvu'ah* — cows that thresh grain by walking over it — and immediately raises the question of why the prohibition of muzzling would not apply. Rashi and Tosafot explain that the grain in question has already reached the stage of being obligated in *trumot umasrot*, that is, it has achieved *gmar melacha* with respect to the separation obligations. Since the prohibition of muzzling (*lo tachsom*) ceases to apply once the grain is at the *gmar melacha* stage, threshing such grain falls outside the scope of the verse. The Rambam offers a different reading of *meraksot* entirely, understanding it as a case where the animals are not being intentionally used for threshing but happen to be walking over the grain.

The *braisa* then states the central rule for our daf: *hadasot b'truma u'ma'aser* — cows threshing grain that already has the status of *truma* or *ma'aser* — *eino over mishum bal tachsom*, one does not transgress the muzzling prohibition. The background for this is important: it is possible to separate *trumot umasrot* before the grain reaches its formal stage of obligation. Normally, the obligation attaches when the grain has been threshed, winnowed, and piled. However, one can separate *truma* and *ma'aser* at an earlier stage — while the grain is still in the sheaves — and the separation takes effect, even though the grain was not yet obligated. Once that separation has occurred, a cow can thresh the grain that now bears the status of *truma* or *ma'aser*, and the question becomes whether *bal tachsom* applies. The *braisa*'s answer is that it does not.

The underlying rationale, drawn from Tosafot, turns on the word *bedisho* — "in its threshing" — which carries a possessive sense. The verse *lo tachsom shor b'disho* implies that the prohibition applies when the animal is threshing something to which it has access, something that is, in the relevant sense, fit for it to eat from. *Truma* grain does not satisfy that condition. A non-*kohen* cannot feed *truma* grain to his animal at all. Even for a *kohen* who owns the cow, Tosafot specifies that only vetch (*karshinin*), which is animal food by nature, may be fed to one's animal from *truma*, but not wheat, because feeding the wheat to an animal would constitute a loss of *truma*. Thus *truma* grain is not something over which the owner has full license, and it is not properly the *disho* — the threshing domain — of the animal.

### Maris Ayin Solution

Although one does not transgress *bal tachsom* in the *truma* and *ma'aser* case, the *braisa* adds that the owner must nonetheless address the concern of *maris ayin* — the appearance of wrongdoing. Passersby who see the cow threshing grain without being allowed to eat will think that the owner is violating the prohibition, or worse, will conclude from this spectacle that muzzling during threshing is acceptable. The remedy is: *mavi bul me'oto hamin v'tolo lo b'trakselin sheb'fiha* — bring a handful of grain of the same type and hang it in a basket near the animal's mouth, so that it is munching from the basket while it works. The assumption is that the animal will eat from the nearby basket rather than from the grain being threshed, and the appearance of wrongdoing is thus avoided.

Two practical questions arise. First, if the animal is eating from the basket, what is preventing it from also eating from the grain beneath its feet? Presumably the basket is positioned closely enough that the animal prefers it. Second, is it economically sensible for the owner to provide grain from the same batch that bears *truma* or *ma'aser* status? Perhaps the amount provided in the basket is kept limited enough that it remains manageable.

### Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai

Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai offers an alternative to the *maris ayin* remedy: rather than hanging grain of the same type, one should bring vetch (*karshinin*) and hang it for the animal, because *karshinin yafot lah min hakol* — vetch is better for the animal than anything else it could eat. This is a striking formulation. The stated purpose of the remedy is to address *maris ayin*, yet Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai introduces the consideration of what food is healthiest for the animal. If the entire point is to make it visually appear that the cow is eating from what it is threshing, one might expect the remedy to emphasize the visual similarity between what is in the basket and what is on the threshing floor. Why, when addressing a *maris ayin* concern, does Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai pivot to advocating for the optimal diet of the animal? This question will become significant later when the *gemara* uses this very passage to draw conclusions about the operative principle underlying the muzzling prohibition.

### Conflicting Braisas

At this point the *gemara* introduces a *v'reminhi* — a contradiction. The first *braisa* stated that cows threshing *truma* and *ma'aser* are exempt from *bal tachsom*. But a second *braisa* teaches precisely the opposite: *hadasot b'truma u'ma'aser over mishum bal tachsom* — when cows thresh *truma* and *ma'aser*, one does transgress the muzzling prohibition. The *gemara* thus has two explicit *braisas* directly contradicting one another on both *truma* and *ma'aser*.

The *gemara* identifies two separate contradictions: *kashya truma atruma, kashya ma'aser ama'aser*. We have a conflict about *truma* and a conflict about *ma'aser*, and they must be resolved independently.

## Reconciling Terumah

### Terumah vs. Gidulei

The *gemara* takes the *truma* contradiction first: *bishlama truma atruma lo kashya* — the contradiction about *truma* is easily resolved. *Kan b'truma, kan b'gidulei truma* — one *braisa* speaks of actual *truma* and the other of *gidulei truma*.

*Gidulei truma* is produce grown from *truma* seeds. If a *kohen* receives *truma* grain and, rather than consuming it, plants it in his field, the grain that grows from those seeds is called *gidulei truma*. From the Torah's standpoint, once the original seed has decomposed and a new stalk has grown from it, the resulting produce is not *truma* at all. The rabbis, however, decreed that *gidulei truma* must be treated like *truma* rabbinically — primarily to prevent the *kohen* from leaving *truma* seed lying around until the planting season, creating conditions for it to become impure or to be treated carelessly. Thus *gidulei truma* carries only a rabbinic status of *truma*.

The resolution follows directly: when the *braisa* says there is no prohibition of *bal tachsom* for *truma*, it means genuine *truma*, which is not *bedisho* because the animal has no claim to it. When the *braisa* says the prohibition does apply, it means *gidulei truma*, which is only rabbinically designated as *truma*. Since from the Torah's perspective this produce is ordinary grain, it *is bedisho*, and therefore the biblical prohibition of *lo tachsom* applies in full. A rabbinic status cannot exempt one from a biblical prohibition.

### Ma'aser Rishon/Sheni

The contradiction about *ma'aser* proves more difficult: *ela ma'aser ama'aser kashya*. The *gemara* considers suggesting a parallel distinction of *gidulei ma'aser*, but immediately rejects it. Unlike *gidulei truma*, which the rabbis treated as having the status of *truma*, produce grown from *ma'aser* seeds — *gidulei ma'aser* — is considered straightforward *chulin*. The rabbis never applied the status of *ma'aser* to such produce. A *mishna* is cited to confirm: *gidulei tevel v'gidulei ma'aser sheini chulin* — produce grown from *tevel* or from *ma'aser sheini* is considered *chulin*. Therefore, no *braisa* could have been referring to *gidulei ma'aser* as if it were *ma'aser*, and the parallel solution does not apply.

The *gemara* resolves the *ma'aser* contradiction differently: *ha b'ma'aser rishon, ha b'ma'aser sheini* — one *braisa* speaks of *ma'aser rishon* and the other of *ma'aser sheini*. *Ma'aser rishon* — the tithe given to a *Levi* — is the Levi's property outright, with no special restrictions on where it must be consumed or how it may be used. It is *bedisho* from the Torah's perspective, and thus the prohibition of *lo tachsom* applies. *Ma'aser sheini*, by contrast, must be brought to Jerusalem and eaten within its walls, and as we shall see, according to some opinions it is considered *mamon gavoha* — property belonging to God. Since the animal has no right to eat it in ordinary circumstances, it is not *bedisho*, and the prohibition does not apply.

### Mammon Gavoh Debate

The *gemara* then proposes an alternative resolution: *v'ibayit eima, ha v'ha b'ma'aser sheini, v'lo kashya — ha Rabbi Meir, ha Rabbi Yehuda*. Both *braisas* may be speaking of *ma'aser sheini*, and the contradiction between them reflects the famous dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda over the status of *ma'aser sheini*.

Rabbi Meir holds that *ma'aser sheini mamon gavoha hu* — *ma'aser sheini* is God's property. The owner has the right of access and consumption, but the property itself belongs to God. Rabbi Yehuda holds that *ma'aser sheini mamon hedyot* — it is ordinary private property of the owner, subject only to the special obligation to consume it in Jerusalem.

What practical difference does this distinction make? One well-known application involves *kiddushin*: if a man betroths a woman using *ma'aser sheini* produce, does the *kiddushin* take effect? The woman can certainly derive value from it — she can bring it to Jerusalem and eat it there. But according to Rabbi Meir, the man has not given her his own money; he has given her access to something that was already God's. The *kiddushin* therefore does not take effect. According to Rabbi Yehuda, it is his property, and the *kiddushin* is valid.

In our context, the same logic applies to *bedisho*: if *ma'aser sheini* is *mamon gavoha*, then even though the owner controls it, it is not truly his to allocate freely, and the animal's eating from it does not constitute the kind of *disha* that the verse has in mind. On Rabbi Meir's view, the prohibition does not apply; on Rabbi Yehuda's view, it does.

### Inside Jerusalem Walls

At this point the *gemara* raises a challenge: *ul'Rabbi Yehuda v'ha ba'ei choma*? Even according to Rabbi Yehuda, who considers *ma'aser sheini* to be private property, the owner is prohibited from consuming it outside Jerusalem's walls. If the animal cannot lawfully eat the *ma'aser sheini* because it is outside Jerusalem, why should it matter whether it is *mamon gavoha* or *mamon hedyot*? Either way, the animal has no right to eat it, and therefore it is not *bedisho* on any view. Rabbi Yehuda would thus also hold that the prohibition does not apply — and both *braisas* would be in agreement, leaving no contradiction to resolve!

The *gemara* answers: *kegon shedash lifnim mechomas Beit Pagi* — the case is where the threshing took place inside the walls of *Beit Pagi*, the outer wall of Jerusalem. In that setting, the *ma'aser sheini* is in the proper location for consumption, and the animal *would* have a right to eat it — if the produce belongs to the owner. Only then does the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda become operative: Rabbi Meir still excludes it from *bedisho* because even in Jerusalem it is *mamon gavoha*, while Rabbi Yehuda includes it because within Jerusalem it is both the owner's property and lawfully available for consumption.

### Demai Status

The *gemara* then offers yet another resolution — one that does not depend on the *ma'aser rishon/sheini* distinction or the *mamon gavoha* debate: *kan b'ma'aser vadai, kan b'ma'aser demai*.

*Demai* refers to produce purchased from an *am ha'aretz* — a person not known to be meticulous about the separation obligations. We assume that even an *am ha'aretz* separates *truma gedola* (the principal priestly portion), since it is a small quantity from the entire granary and even common people generally do so. We are concerned, however, that they did not separate *ma'aser*, which represents a full ten percent — and often twenty percent when both *ma'aser rishon* and *ma'aser sheini* are reckoned. The rabbis therefore decreed that one who purchases grain from an *am ha'aretz* must separate *ma'aser*, treating the produce as if it were *vadai* — certainly untithed. This is called *demai*. The institution of *demai* is attributed to Yochanan Kohen Gadol, who dispatched agents throughout the land of Israel and found that common people were separating only *truma gedola* but not *ma'aser*.

The rabbinic obligation of *demai* cannot override a biblical prohibition. From the Torah's standpoint, we can rely on the *rov* — the majority of *amei ha'aretz* do in fact tithe — and thus the produce is presumptively *chulin*. Therefore, if one treats the *demai* produce as *ma'aser sheini* and refrains from allowing the animal to eat it, that is only a rabbinic *chumra*. From the Torah's perspective, the produce is *chulin*, the animal has a right to eat it, and it is *bedisho*. The *braisa* that says one transgresses *bal tachsom* reflects a case of *ma'aser demai*, where the rabbinic stringency of treating it like *ma'aser sheini* cannot serve as a *kula* to exempt one from the biblical prohibition of *lo tachsom*.

The *gemara* then applies this same logic to *truma*. Having established the *demai* distinction for *ma'aser*, it suggests: *kan b'trumas vadai, kan b'trumas demai*. The *gemara* immediately objects: *trumas demai mi ika?* — there is no such thing as *trumas demai*. An *am ha'aretz* is assumed even by the rabbis to have separated *truma gedola*, so there is no rabbinic obligation to re-separate *truma* from *demai* produce. However, the *gemara* concludes, there is another category that functions analogously: *trumas ma'aser*. After *ma'aser rishon* is given to the *Levi*, the *Levi* must take one-tenth of that tithe and give it to the *kohen* as *trumas ma'aser*. Since an *am ha'aretz* who failed to take *ma'aser rishon* obviously also failed to take *trumas ma'aser*, once one separates the *ma'aser* of *demai*, the resulting *trumas ma'aser* carries only a rabbinic status. That rabbinic status cannot exempt from the biblical prohibition of *lo tachsom*, and thus the *braisa* that says one transgresses refers to this case of *trumas ma'aser* of *demai*.

## Animal Benefit Principle

### Eating Causes Harm

The *gemara* now records a question posed to Rav Sheshes: *hayisa ocheles u'merazes mahu?* — what is the law if an animal is of such a constitution that eating while threshing actually causes it distress, specifically diarrhea? Here, allowing the animal to eat is harmful to it, while muzzling it would actually be beneficial. Is one permitted — or perhaps even required — to muzzle the animal under these circumstances?

This is a striking *gemara*. There is no textual peg in the verse to which one can easily attach this case. The question is not about parsing a word or applying a *drasha*; it is a direct inquiry into the underlying rationale of the prohibition. The *gemara* implicitly assumes that if we can identify *why* the Torah prohibits muzzling, we can determine whether that rationale extends to this case or whether it excludes it.

### Benefit vs. Distress

The *gemara* formulates two competing principles. The first: *mishum d'ma'ale leihu* — the reason one may not muzzle the animal is because eating is beneficial to it, and the Torah requires that the animal derive benefit from its labor. If eating is not beneficial in this case — indeed, if it is harmful — then perhaps the prohibition does not apply. The second: *d'chazu mitztara* — the operative concern is not the animal's nutritional benefit but the distress it experiences when it sees grain in front of it and is prevented from eating. An animal does not reason abstractly that eating will cause it harm; it sees the grain, desires it, and suffers when restrained. This distress exists regardless of whether eating would ultimately be good or bad for its health.

### Animal Welfare Principle

Rav Sheshes resolves the question by citing the *braisa* of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai: *tanituha* — we have already learned the answer. When addressing the *maris ayin* problem in the case of *truma* and *ma'aser*, Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai said that one should provide vetch for the animal because vetch is the best food for it of all available options. The question is: why, in the context of addressing an appearance concern, does Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai introduce the consideration of the optimal diet for the animal? He could simply have said: hang some grain near its mouth. The fact that he specifies the *best* food indicates that the principle of animal welfare — what is most beneficial for the animal — is not incidental to his ruling but is operative within the framework of the muzzling prohibition itself.

The *gemara* draws the inference: *shema mina mishum d'ma'ale lah hu, shema mina* — we learn from this that the governing principle is indeed what is beneficial for the animal. Since eating causes harm in the case Rav Sheshes was asked about, the rationale of the prohibition does not apply, and one may muzzle the animal.

The depth of this ruling deserves emphasis. Without any explicit textual basis, the *gemara* determines — from a tangential remark of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai in a *maris ayin* context — that the entire prohibition of *lo tachsom* is grounded in animal welfare, specifically in what is beneficial for the animal. And on that basis, a seemingly clear biblical prohibition is set aside.

## Non-Jew Worker Issue

### Tell Non-Jew Question

The *gemara* now asks: *mahu she-yomar adam le-nokhri chasom parati v'dosh bah?* — may one instruct a non-Jew to muzzle one's ox and thresh grain with it? This question had already been foreshadowed by the earlier *braisa*, which stated that a non-Jew threshing with a Jew's cow does not cause the Jew to transgress *bal tachsom*. Rashi understood that *braisa* as speaking of the non-Jew threshing his own grain. On Rashi's reading, if the non-Jew were threshing the Jew's grain, a biblical transgression might apply. Tosafot disputes this and reads the *braisa* as speaking of the Jew's grain — since the parallel case involving *amira l'nokhri* on Shabbat concerns an act done for the Jew's benefit. The case before us, per Tosafot, is therefore: the non-Jew is my employee, threshing my grain, and I have instructed him to muzzle my ox. Do I transgress?

### Amira L'Nokhri

The *gemara* formulates the question around *amira l'nokhri*: is the rabbinic prohibition of instructing a non-Jew to perform a forbidden act limited to Shabbat — where the prohibition is particularly severe (*isura d'sekila*) and where there is a broader concern about *melacha* being performed on one's behalf — or does it extend to other prohibitions as well? Perhaps *amira l'nokhri* applies even to a simple negative commandment (*issur lav*) like the muzzling prohibition.

### Non-Jew Using Jew Cow

The *gemara* attempts to resolve this from the earlier *braisa*: *nokhri hadosh b'parato shel Yisrael eino over mishum lo tachsom* — a non-Jew threshing with a Jew's cow does not cause the Jew to transgress. If this is our case — the Jew instructed the non-Jew — and the *braisa* says the Jew does not transgress, perhaps we can infer that instructing a non-Jew is entirely permitted even for non-Shabbat prohibitions.

The *gemara* rejects this inference: *ma'avad hu de-lo avar* — the *braisa* says only that the Jew does not transgress *biblically*. It does not say the practice is permitted. The very fact that it says "he does not transgress" implies that there may remain a *rabbinic* prohibition of *amira l'nokhri*. The *gemara* counters this: *bedin hu de-isura nami leka* — perhaps it is entirely permitted, and the *braisa* merely specifies "he does not transgress" because it follows the contrasting case (*v'adai de-tani seifa*) of a Jew threshing with a non-Jew's cow, who does transgress. The first clause was formulated in parallel to the second, stating only the biblical ruling, but that does not imply a rabbinic prohibition in the first case.

The question therefore remains unresolved: the *braisa* addresses only the biblical dimension, and we do not know whether instructing a non-Jew to muzzle one's animal constitutes a rabbinic prohibition analogous to *amira l'nokhri* on Shabbat, or whether such a prohibition does not apply outside the Shabbat context.

## Castration Issue

### Bull Castration Trick

The *gemara* transitions to a related question involving a report sent to the father of Shmuel from the communities of Eretz Yisrael. They described a practice: *halin torei d'ganvin aramaei u'menakhniyan yathon* — non-Jews would "steal" bulls from Jewish farmers in the night, castrate them, and return them. The question was whether this arrangement — which clearly operated with the farmers' knowledge and tacit approval, even if no explicit instruction was given — was permitted.

The background is the biblical prohibition of *sirus* — castration of animals — derived from the verse *u've'artzechem lo ta'asu* ("in your lands you shall not do this"). The practical significance for agricultural communities was substantial: a castrated bull becomes an ox, which in ancient farming was far more valuable as a draft animal. Jewish farmers who needed their bulls castrated devised this workaround precisely to avoid direct transgression, having non-Jews come and perform the castration while maintaining the fiction that the animals were "taken."

The father of Shmuel sent back a clear response: *harama is'avid behu* — they were attempting a *harama*, a legal fiction, and it is not acceptable. *Irimu alaihu v'yizdabun* — act against their scheme and require them to sell the animals. The farmer who had his animals castrated through this scheme cannot continue using them; he must sell them and may not retain the benefit of the castration. This ruling establishes that the arrangement was prohibited. Remarkably, it also implies that even without a direct *amira* — even with merely a wink and a nod — the prohibition applies, at least in the context of *sirus*.

### Rabbi Chidka Position

Rav Pappa immediately qualifies the scope of this ruling: *benei ma'arava savri lah k'Rabbi Chidka*. The communities of Eretz Yisrael who raised this question, and the father of Shmuel who answered it, held like Rabbi Chidka. Rabbi Chidka maintains that *benei Noach metzuvin al ha-sirus* — non-Jews are themselves prohibited from performing castration as part of their seven Noahide obligations, presumably derived from the broader command of *pru u'revu*. On this view, a Jew who facilitates non-Jews in performing *sirus* transgresses *lifnei iver lo titein michshol* — placing a stumbling block before the blind — because the non-Jew himself commits a transgression by performing the act.

The ruling therefore does not necessarily establish anything about *amira l'nokhri* in general. It may have been driven entirely by the concern for *lifnei iver*: the Jew was causing the non-Jew to sin. If Rabbi Chidka is wrong and non-Jews are not prohibited on *sirus*, there might be no *lifnei iver* violation, and then the question of whether the Jew's indirect involvement constitutes a prohibited *amira* returns. The *gemara* thus does not provide a definitive answer on *amira l'nokhri* for prohibitions beyond Shabbat.

Two additional observations are worth noting. First, the fact that this case goes beyond even explicit *amira* — the animals were "stolen" without a direct instruction — and yet was treated as potentially problematic, suggests that for *sirus* there may be something more categorical at work. The verse *u've'artzechem lo ta'asu* applies to the *phenomenon* of castration occurring in one's domain, giving the prohibition a broader reach than a standard negative commandment. Second, the practical implications for our own times are significant: Jews who wish to have pets spayed or neutered must navigate these questions — whether non-Jews are prohibited on *sirus* (which would trigger *lifnei iver*), and whether even if non-Jews are permitted, *amira l'nokhri* applies to prohibitions outside Shabbat.

### Sale Requirement

Returning to the consequences of the *sirus* that has already occurred, Rava initially considered requiring the farmer to sell the castrated animal and to restrict even the sale to uses that would not allow him to benefit from the enhanced value of the animal as an ox. Abaye corrects this: the *knas* — the penalty — is only that the farmer must sell the animal and cannot use it himself. The farmer still retains the monetary benefit of the sale price; the penalty does not extend to stripping him of all financial benefit, only of direct personal use.

The *gemara* then asks: can the farmer sell the animal to his adult son? The answer is yes — an adult son is like any other person (*beno gadol ke-acher dami*), and a sale to him satisfies the requirement. Can he sell it to his minor son? This is disputed: Rav Acha prohibits it, while Rav Ashi permits it.

### Animal Swap Workaround

Mareimar and Mar Zutra — and some say two unnamed *chasidim* — resolved the problem by swapping castrated animals with one another. Each would "sell" his castrated animal to the other, satisfying the technical requirement to sell while ensuring that both parties ended up with functionally equivalent animals. This solution is reminiscent of arrangements made for other occasions, such as swapping *Purim seudot*.

Tosafot observes the difficulty in this entire picture, noting that the *gemara* seems to describe ongoing, structured arrangements among *amoraim* — or even *chasidim* — for managing animals that had been castrated. Tosafot attempts to read these cases as isolated, unanticipated events, but the language *machlifi ahadadi* — they would swap with each other — implies a recurring practice. One way to reconcile this is as follows: if non-Jews are not prohibited on *sirus*, and if there is no *amira l'nokhri* problem when the act is done without a direct instruction (such as when non-Jews came and took the animals without being explicitly told), then the prohibition would not apply, and there would be no *knas* to navigate. But if that were the case, the elaborate swap arrangement would be unnecessary. The internal tension in the passage remains, and the *gemara* ultimately leaves the broader question of *amira l'nokhri* for non-Shabbat prohibitions as unresolved.

## Muzzling Impediments

### Various Impediments

The *gemara* now presents a cluster of questions posed by Rami bar Chama: *hoshiv lah kotz b'fiyah mahu* — what if one placed a thorn in the animal's mouth, preventing it from eating? *Hirbitz lah ari mi-bachutz mahu* — what if one positioned a lion outside the field, causing the animal to be too frightened to eat? *Yashav lah kotz b'fiyah mahu* — what if a thorn happened to become lodged in the animal's mouth on its own? *Hirmitz lah ari mi-bachutz mahu* — what if a lion happened to lie down near the field? *He'emid bno mi-bachutz mahu* — what if one placed the animal's calf just outside the field, causing the mother to be so distressed with worry that she cannot eat? *Haysa tzme'a l'mayim mahu* — what if the animal was so thirsty that it had no appetite for food? *Peras lah katavlya al gabei deisha mahu* — what if one spread a leather mat over the grain so that the animal, while threshing, could not see or access it?

These questions form a spectrum. At one end are acts done directly to the animal — a thorn placed in its mouth, a lion positioned to frighten it — that seem clearly analogous to a physical muzzle. At the other end are indirect impediments — a mat over the grain, an animal's thirst — that are further removed from the classical act of muzzling.

### Effective Muzzles

The *gemara* moves quickly to resolve some of these. *Hoshiv lah kotz b'fiyah — chasima ma'alyata hi*: placing a thorn in the animal's mouth is obviously a full-fledged muzzle. The question of Rami bar Chama could not have been asking whether a thorn constitutes a muzzle. Similarly, *hirbitz lah ari mi-bachutz — chasima ma'alyata hi*: positioning a lion to terrify the animal is equally a muzzle. Anything that *effectively* prevents the animal from eating constitutes *lo tachsom* in terms of the active placement of such an impediment.

The *gemara* therefore reframes Rami bar Chama's questions. He was not asking whether these things constitute muzzles; he was asking whether the prohibition requires the owner to *remove* an impediment that already exists — a thorn that accidentally lodged itself, a lion that independently sat down near the field — or whether the prohibition is only violated by the active placement of a muzzle. Does *lo tachsom* prohibit only the act of muzzling, or does it also prohibit allowing the animal to thresh while in a state of being effectively muzzled?

### Hay Over Grain

On one question the *gemara* offers a partial answer. The *braisa* rules: *rashai ba'al habayis lehatir paki'a amir lifnei habehemah kedei shelo tochal min hadeisha* — the owner of the grain may spread straw in front of the threshing animal so that it eats the straw rather than the grain. The farmer who rents out his cow can arrive with a hungry animal — having fed it less the day before — in order to maximize how much it will eat from the grain during threshing. And the farmer using the cow can counter this by spreading straw over the grain.

The *gemara* initially wonders whether spreading straw is comparable to the problematic leather mat case — in both instances something is placed over the grain. The *gemara* rejects this analogy: *shani hatam d'ka'achlah* — there the animal is still eating, merely from straw rather than grain. The prohibition of *lo tachsom* requires that the animal be able to eat; as long as it is eating *something* while it works, there is no violation. Placing hay over the grain ensures the animal eats hay — that is not muzzling. Placing a leather mat that prevents access to any food would be a different matter.

### Timing of Muzzle

Rabbi Yona asked Rabbi Simai a further question: *chasama mibachutz mahu* — what if one muzzled the animal before it began threshing? Is the prohibition violated only by the act of muzzling *while* the animal is threshing — such that muzzling it beforehand and then putting it to work is not a direct transgression — or does the prohibition extend to any threshing done while the animal is in a muzzled state?

### State of Muzzling

Rabbi Simai resolves this with a *kal va-chomer* from the laws of priestly intoxication. The verse states: *yayin v'shechar al tisht ata u'vanecha itcha b'vo'achem* — "do not drink wine or intoxicating drink, you and your sons, when you come into the Tent of Meeting." One might read this literally: the prohibition is the act of *drinking* as you *enter*. But clearly a *kohen* who was already drunk before entering does not thereby avoid the prohibition. The verse *u'lehavdil bein hakodesh u'vein ha-chol* — "to distinguish between holy and ordinary" — indicates that the issue is being in a state of intoxication while serving. The act of drinking preceded the entry, but the state of being drunk is what matters.

The parallel to *lo tachsom* is exact: *mah hatam besha'at bi'ah lo tehe shichrut, hacha nami besha'at disha lo tehe chasima* — just as by the *kohen* the relevant moment is entry into the sanctuary and what matters is whether one is in a state of intoxication at that time, so too by *lo tachsom*, the relevant moment is the act of threshing, and what matters is whether the animal is in a state of being muzzled at that time. The prohibition is not only the act of placing a muzzle during threshing; it is the act of *threshing while the animal is in a muzzled state*. This is a significant broadening of the prohibition's scope.

This restatement of *lo tachsom* as a prohibition on *threshing while muzzled* rather than merely on *the act of muzzling during threshing* has broad implications for many of the cases discussed by Rami bar Chama — the thorn in the mouth, the frightening lion, the leather mat over the grain. If the prohibition is about the state of the animal during threshing, then all of these, to the extent they render the animal effectively muzzled, become relevant. Certain tensions remain — for instance, the earlier ruling that spreading hay *before* threshing begins is permitted, while the present teaching seems to focus on the animal's state *during* threshing regardless of how that state came about. These parameters will require further clarification, as the *gemara* continues with related questions on the following daf.
