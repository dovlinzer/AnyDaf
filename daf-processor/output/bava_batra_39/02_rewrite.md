# Bava Batra 39 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mechah Shelo Befanav

### Mechah & Chazakah

The central concern of the *gemara* on this *daf* is *mechah* — the formal protest an owner must lodge to prevent an adverse possessor from establishing *chazakah* — and in particular the principle of *mechah shelo befanav*: whether a protest made not in the occupant's direct presence constitutes a valid *mechah*. The two institutions are intertwined. If the owner cannot make an effective *mechah* from a distance, the entire system governing *chazakah* shifts accordingly.

### Tosafot's Framework

An important preliminary point, one that clarifies why the *gemara* focuses its scrutiny on the *mechah* rather than on the *chazakah* itself, is a framework offered by Tosafot. The assumption underlying *chazakah shelo befanav* — that is, adverse possession occurring while the original owner is away — is that word of the occupation will reach the original owner. If a stranger is on your land, you want to know what is happening with your property; you will ask questions, and the natural workings of the rumor mill will carry the news to you. This is Tosafot's approach; it is not the position of the Rashbam, but it is a helpful one. We therefore assume the message will reach the absent owner. The live question, then, is not whether the owner learns of the occupation, but rather: once he has learned of it and wishes to protest, can he do so *shelo befanav* — from where he is — and have that protest carry legal weight? Can we assume that his *mechah* will, in turn, reach the occupant?

### Rava's Position

Rava's explanation of the *mishna*'s assumptions is that *mechah shelo befanav* is in principle valid. One obvious advantage of *mechah befanav* — protesting directly in the occupant's presence — is that it creates unambiguous, direct evidence that the message was received. Yet Rava rules that this is not required. The owner may protest from wherever he is, and we assume the message will reach the occupant. Even if, in a given case, the occupant in fact did not hear of it, the halacha does not favor him on that account — a result that may sound counterintuitive, but reflects a considered policy judgment about whose interests the law protects.

### Geographic Limits

There is, however, a geographic limitation. If the owner is in a different country — a jurisdiction where communication cannot be assumed — then *mechah shelo befanav* will not be effective, because in those circumstances we cannot presume that the message will travel there. The consequence of *mechah shelo befanav* being unavailable is significant: if there is no realistic prospect of the message reaching the owner, we cannot treat his silence as meaningful acquiescence, and the occupant's *chazakah* correspondingly loses its evidentiary force.

### Core Principle

The governing principle, then, is this: one need not make a *mechah befanav*, but one must operate in conditions where communication is possible and where we can assume the message will arrive. Absent such conditions, the *chazakah* is not a valid *chazakah*. The point that the owner need only prove that he made the *mechah* properly — and need never demonstrate that the *machzik* actually heard it — is a principle that strongly favors the original owner. The *machzik*, for his part, carries real risk if he has not carefully preserved his *shtar*.

### Squatting Case

This raises an important question: what of someone who squats on land for three years without ever having had a *shtar*? Does the entire framework not break down, since there is no purchase to legitimate? The answer is that the system does work — but precisely as designed. The absence of any protest is probative only when the *machzik* is making a legitimate claim: that he purchased the property and no longer has the *shtar*. After three years of undisturbed possession, the absence of protest is evidence that he owns it — but only in that context. The *machzik* is not required to prove the occupant heard the *mechah*; the owner is not required to prove the *mechah* reached the occupant. What the owner must do is demonstrate that he made the *mechah* under conditions in which it could have reached its destination. Even if the *machzik* could somehow prove he spent the last three years in a silent retreat where no word reached him, it would not matter. The rules must be clear and administrable, and the rule is: make a proper *mechah*, and that suffices.

## Restrictions on Mechah

### Lo Temru Leh

The *gemara* now introduces a series of cases in which the protesting owner attempts, in various ways, to restrict the spread of information about his *mechah*. At the top of 39a, the *gemara* presents a case in which the owner says to the witnesses: *lo temru leh* — do not tell him. He issues his *mechah* before witnesses, declaring the occupant a *gazlan* who is unlawfully on his land and whom he intends to sue, but instructs the witnesses to keep the matter to themselves. This is, at minimum, suspicious. Perhaps the owner is acting in bad faith, hoping to prevent the occupant from holding onto his *shtar* so that he can reclaim the land more easily. Alternatively, perhaps the owner simply does not want the *gazlan* to begin shoring up a defense. In any case, the question is whether this instruction — *lo temru leh* — is enough to invalidate the *mechah* entirely. If the witnesses obey, after all, the message will certainly not reach the occupant; it begins to resemble the situation of a different country, where communication is presumed impossible.

### Rav Zvid Strict

Rav Zvid's position is consistent and strict: once you have said *lo temru leh*, the *mechah* is meaningless. If the witnesses comply, the word will not reach the occupant, and the protest has accomplished nothing.

### Rav Pappa Lenient

Rav Pappa disagrees. He reads the instruction narrowly: *ledidi lo temru leh* — don't tell him directly — but *leachrinei emru* — you may tell others. And once you tell others, the principle of *chavrach chavrah it leh vechavrach dechavrah chavrah it leh* — your friend has a friend, and your friend's friend has a friend — applies. The witnesses have no obligation in a normal *mechah* to pick up the telephone and notify the occupant directly; they are simply witnesses to a protest that will circulate through ordinary social channels. Saying *lo temru leh* only restricts direct communication, and since direct communication was never the mechanism to begin with, the *mechah* remains valid.

### Lo Tipku Shuta

What if the owner goes further? What if he says *lo tipku shuta* — do not let the word get out at all? This is broader than merely telling the witnesses not to speak to the occupant directly; it is an instruction to suppress the matter entirely. Here, Rav Zvid again rules that the *mechah* is nullified — this is entirely consistent with his position. And notably, Rav Pappa is silent. His silence is taken as implicit agreement: when the instruction is *lo tipku shuta*, even Rav Pappa concedes that the *mechah* fails, because the channels through which the message would have naturally traveled have been explicitly sealed off.

### Offered Silence

The *gemara* now introduces a further variation: what if the witnesses themselves, without being asked, volunteer that they will not tell the occupant — *amru leh lo amrinan leh*? There is a question whether this offer arises in response to the owner's instruction, or whether the witnesses proffer it entirely on their own initiative. The Rashbam suggests that the case may be *davka* where no instruction was given — because when one merely agrees to a restriction imposed by another, one tends to interpret it narrowly, looking for loopholes. If I tell you not to say anything, you might construe that as: I cannot tell *him*, but I can tell others. Whereas if you offer on your own that you will not say anything, the words may carry a broader intent, suggesting you mean to suppress the information entirely.

Rav Zvid is again consistent: if they have offered that they will not tell the occupant, the *mechah* is invalid. Rav Pappa, however, maintains that even here the *mechah* stands — because their offer, just as the owner's instruction, means only that they will not tell the occupant directly, not that the rumor mill will be stopped.

### Voluntary Offers

Now the *gemara* presents the broadest formulation: the witnesses offer, on their own, *lo mapkinan shuta* — we will not let the word get out. This is the equivalent of *lo tipku shuta*, but it comes as a voluntary offer rather than an instruction from the owner. One might suppose this is the same as the case where the owner issued the instruction, and therefore Rav Pappa would again concede the invalidity. And indeed, Rav Pappa does say that when the witnesses have volunteered *lo mapkinan shuta*, the *mechah* is not valid — because once the scope of the undertaking has been expanded from "we won't tell him" to "we won't let the word get out," even Rav Pappa agrees the *mechah* cannot stand.

### Voluntary Offers

But here Rav Huna brei deRav Yehoshua disagrees, and says that even in this last case the *mechah* remains valid. His reasoning is captured in the principle: *kol milta d'lo ramya alei d'inish amar lah v'lav ada'atei* — anything that is not an obligation formally placed upon a person, they will say and not really pay attention to it. When the witnesses volunteer "we won't let the word get out," no one has charged them with that responsibility; they have simply offered it freely. The weight of a freely offered promise is lighter than that of an obligation imposed from without. As a result, people will talk, and they will not even notice that they are violating their own pledge. The word will slip out, the rumor will spread, and the *mechah* will reach its destination.

This last ruling strikes as somewhat counterintuitive. As the cases multiply — don't tell him, don't let the word get out, they offer not to tell anyone — the likelihood that the *machzik* actually learns of the *mechah* diminishes with each step. One might have sympathy for Rav Zvid's consistent position, which tracks that decreasing likelihood honestly. Nevertheless, the trajectory of the *gemara* is toward the broadest possible definition of *mechah*, and the accepted halacha — as the Rashba confirms — follows the most expansive view, consistently favoring the original owner. This has a sobering practical implication: a *machzik* who has relied on three years of undisturbed possession as proof of ownership may be taking a significant legal risk, since all manner of purportedly restricted protests may still turn out to constitute valid *mechah*.

## Rava vs Rav Nachman

### Rav Nachman

Rava quotes Rav Nachman as stating: *mechah shelo befanav havya mecha* — a protest not in the occupant's presence constitutes a valid *mechah*. This, of course, is the position around which the entire discussion of *chavrach chavrah it leh* and the various restrictions has revolved. The alternative — that *mechah shelo befanav* does *not* work, that *lo havya mecha* — would mean the owner must appear before the occupant directly, serving him with notice personally or through an agent. The Rashbam adds that if one did hold such a position, *o hu o shlucho* — either he himself or his agent — would suffice, since the essential point is that the occupant receives the message unambiguously. But Rav Nachman's ruling dispenses with that requirement entirely.

### Rava's Question

Rava, however, challenges Rav Nachman from the position of Rabbi Yehuda in the *mishna*. Rabbi Yehuda explains the purpose of the three-year period as follows: *lo amru shalosh shanim ella kidei sheyehei be'Aspamia* — the three years were instituted to allow time for someone in Aspamia (a distant province) to be reached by news that someone is occupying his land, and to return and respond. The breakdown of the three years, as Rabbi Yehuda describes it, is: the occupant takes possession for one year, news travels to the owner for the second year, and the owner returns and protests in the third year — *veyavo leshana achereta*. The word *veyavo*, "he returns," implies that Rabbi Yehuda requires the owner to physically come back and issue the *mechah* directly. If *mechah shelo befanav* were sufficient, why would he need to return? Let him sit in Aspamia and protest from there — *leysev hatam be'duchtei velimchei*.

Tosafot notes a wrinkle in this question: we have already established that when one is in a different country, *mechah shelo befanav* does not work — because we cannot assume the message will travel there. So perhaps Rabbi Yehuda does not require *mechah befanav* at all; he only requires the owner to return to the same country, so that normal communication channels can carry the protest. In that case, the *gemara* should say he must return to the *medina*, not that he must actually appear before the *machzik*. The word *veyavo*, however, implies a more direct return and confrontation. The question thus retains some force at least within Rabbi Yehuda's own position.

### Good Advice

The *gemara* resolves Rava's challenge: *hasam eitzah tovah ka mashma lan* — there, Rabbi Yehuda is not stating a legal requirement; he is offering good advice. The *mishna* is counseling the owner: now that you have learned someone is on your land, go protect your interests. Return and sue the person; reclaim what is yours. It does not mean that you are legally obligated to do so, or that *mechah shelo befanav* is insufficient. Rabbi Yehuda's language describes the practically wise course of action, not a binding legal demand.

### Peiros Issue

The Rashbam makes an additional point that deepens the force of this advice, which bears emphasis: the sooner the owner returns, the sooner he can stop the occupant from consuming the produce — *peiros* — of the land. Produce already consumed is difficult to recover. The occupant may not have assets sufficient to reimburse the owner for fruits already eaten. Every additional year of delay means more consumed produce and a harder road to full recovery. This is why the *mishna* says, in practical terms, come back promptly — not because *mechah shelo befanav* is legally invalid, but because your financial interests demand swift action.

### Rava Accepts

The *gemara* then observes that since Rava himself had challenged Rav Nachman's position, it would appear that Rava did not initially accept that *mechah shelo befanav* is valid — yet we know from all the preceding discussion that Rava was a leading proponent of exactly that principle. The *gemara* resolves this: *basar d'shamei m'Rav Nachman savrah* — after he heard the ruling from Rav Nachman, challenged it, received a satisfying answer, and was persuaded, Rava accepted and adopted the position as his own. It was not his position from the outset; he came to it through this very exchange.

## Two vs Three Witness (II)

### Witness Number

The *gemara* now raises a different question about *mechah*: how many people must be present when the *mechah* is issued? We have assumed throughout that witnesses are required, and the cases above all describe the protest as made before multiple people — *lo temru leh* is in the plural. The question is whether the required number is two or three.

### Two Witnesses

Rabbi Abba bar Rabbi Chiya transmits a ruling in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *mechah bifnei shenayim* — a *mechah* requires two people.

### Three Witnesses

A different tradition records Rabbi Yochanan as ruling: *mechah bifnei shalosh* — three people are required.

### Lashon Hara Link

The *gemara* suggests that perhaps the dispute between these two versions tracks a teaching of Rabbah bar Rav Huna in Arachin: *kol milta demis'amra be'appei tlata leit bei mishum lishna bisha* — anything said in the presence of three people is not subject to the prohibition of *lashon hara*. The implication is that once something is said before three, it will become public knowledge; the assumption that it will spread is so strong that repeating it no longer carries the character of private gossip. This principle will require careful unpacking, and the *gemara* will return to it — but the initial suggestion is that the debate between two and three witnesses in *mechah* may reflect a debate about how many people are needed before a matter can be presumed to enter public circulation.

## Lashon Hara Issues

### Three & Lashon Hara

The teaching of Rabbah bar Rav Huna — that a matter said before three people is not subject to *lashon hara* — calls to mind what Benjamin Franklin is said to have observed: three people can keep a secret if two of them are dead. The underlying principle is that once three people know something, we presume it will spread. But the full meaning of this teaching requires examination, and there are several distinct interpretations in the *rishonim*.

## Two vs Three Witness (II)

### Testimony vs Public

The *gemara* proposes two ways of understanding the dispute between two and three witnesses for *mechah*. The first possibility: perhaps everyone agrees with Rabbah bar Rav Huna that three are needed to make something public, and the debate concerns a prior question. The one who says two holds *mechah shelo befanav lo havya mecha* — you cannot protest from a distance; you must deliver the *mechah* directly to the occupant. In that case, the witnesses need only testify that the protest was directly delivered — two witnesses suffice for testimony, just as in any other legal context. The one who says three holds *mechah shelo befanav havya mecha* — you do protest from a distance, the word must travel and reach the occupant, and for that you need three people to ensure the matter becomes public.

The second possibility is that everyone agrees *mechah shelo befanav* works, and the debate is about what mechanism makes it effective. The one who says two holds *sahadusa ba'inan* — we need testimony, formal witnesses to the act of protest. The one who says three holds *gillui milta ba'inan* — we need the matter to become public. This is a conceptually richer distinction that warrants further exploration.

### Tosafot's View

Tosafot reads the *sahadusa* position differently. Of course, Tosafot says, the message ultimately has to reach the occupant — that is the whole purpose of *mechah*. And in principle, rumors spread even from a single person: *ve'i lav mishum sahadusa hava sagi bechad dechad vechad ve'islei* — if not for the need for testimony, even one person would be enough, since word travels from person to person. But two are required for testimony to the fact that the *mechah* was made. The rumor itself will spread from those two, even without them being formally obligated to carry it. So what, then, is the difference between two and three if the rumor spreads from either number?

Tosafot answers with an important distinction: *ve'af al pi shelishon hara ein nishma, hainu lefi shema'alimin oso mibnei adam* — even though people talk, there is a distinction between whispering something privately and saying it openly. Two people may whisper — they will share the information in private, quietly, among themselves and perhaps one or two others. The rumor travels, but in the shadows. Three people, says Tosafot, makes it *gillui milta* — a public matter, akin to *lashon hara* which, by Rabbah bar Rav Huna's rule, ceases to be a private matter once said before three.

### Private vs Public

This distinction between private rumor and public knowledge is significant. Once something circulates only in whispers, whether it reaches any particular person depends on whether he is plugged into the right network. But when a matter is openly discussed, it enters general circulation and becomes something everyone knows. So the debate between two and three is, at its core, a debate about whether the *mechah* needs to reach the occupant through private rumor or must enter the domain of public knowledge.

### Public Perception

This points toward a deeper question about what *chazakah* is doing and what *mechah* is undoing. One account of *chazakah* focuses on the evidentiary inference from silence: because the owner did not protest, his silence constitutes implicit concession that the occupant is legitimate. On this account, the owner need only ensure that his protest reaches the occupant, breaking the inference of acquiescence and putting the occupant on notice to guard his *shtar*. But there is another account: by occupying the land publicly for three years, the *machzik* has established a kind of public status — in the community's eyes, he presents as the owner. What *mechah* must do, on this account, is not merely reach the occupant privately but recontextualize his public presence. If people are told publicly that this person is a *gazlan*, then his three years on the land no longer look like the occupancy of an owner — his public standing has been altered. Private whispers may not accomplish this. Only when the *mechah* becomes a public matter does it transform how the *machzik*'s presence is perceived by the community.

These two frameworks — the *mechah* as breaking evidentiary inference versus the *mechah* as reshaping public perception — are both operative in the *gemara* and help explain why *gillui milta* might demand more than *sahadusa* alone.

## Lashon Hara Issues (II)

### Rashbam's View

Several major *rishonim* offer distinct interpretations of Rabbah bar Rav Huna's teaching that something said before three is not subject to *lashon hara*. The Rashbam's interpretation is precise and narrow. The ruling does not grant license to say negative things about others merely because the statement is made before three people. Rather, it concerns what may be *reported about the speaker*. If I say something disparaging about David in front of three people, those three people are permitted to go to David and inform him of what I said about him — and that act of reporting is not itself *lashon hara* against me. The reasoning is that by making a statement publicly and willingly before three, I have implicitly accepted that the content of what I said, and the fact that I said it, will become known. Since I was willing to say it in a quasi-public setting, I cannot object to others repeating it. The statement becomes fair to report because I have, in effect, authorized its circulation.

A corollary of this reading: if someone does something embarrassing in public, consciously and not by accident, others may freely discuss it — because by acting openly, the person has chosen to make that act a public matter. The person who does the act is the one whose conduct is being discussed, and since they chose publicity, there is no violation in reporting it.

What this explicitly does *not* permit is spreading negative information about the subject of the original statement. The three listeners who heard something bad about David cannot take that information and spread it further, disparaging David to other people — David is the victim, and the rules of *lashon hara* protect him. The ruling only concerns reporting what the *speaker* said publicly, not repeating the underlying negative content about its target.

### Tosafot's View

Tosafot offers a different reading. There is a version of *lashon hara* that goes beyond saying outright negative things about someone — it encompasses the broader concern about discussing people at all, even in seemingly neutral terms. The *gemara* elsewhere notes that in conversations involving *lashon hara*, a person does not glance behind to see whether the subject is within earshot. The implication is that there is something problematic about discussing someone behind their back, even when the content is not obviously harmful, precisely because the absent person has no opportunity to respond.

Rabbah bar Rav Huna's ruling, on this reading, addresses that concern. When you say something about a person in front of three, we assume it will reach that person — word will get back to them. This being so, saying something about someone in front of three is roughly equivalent to saying it in their presence. And if you are willing to say it in their presence, the statement is presumptively the kind of thing that can be said. The test that the speaker clears — being prepared to speak openly before a group large enough to ensure the subject will hear — substitutes for the test of actually speaking in the subject's presence.

The common ground between the Rashbam and Tosafot is that neither reading permits spreading harmful information about the subject of the original statement. The distinctions concern what may be said about the speaker, and whether statements made quasi-publicly may be treated as statements made in the subject's presence.

### Rambam's View

The Rambam reads Rabbah bar Rav Huna's ruling as a broader license, particularly relevant in an era when information spreads with extraordinary speed. Once something has been said before three people, there is a presumption that it has become common knowledge. And things that are common knowledge — stories in the news, widely reported events — may be discussed and repeated, because there is no private confidence being violated and no private reputation being newly damaged by disclosure. The act of repeating commonly known information is simply what people do when they discuss current affairs.

This reading is, however, not without difficulty. The reason we presume something becomes public once said before three is that those three people will go and spread it. But spreading *lashon hara* is itself prohibited — so the presumption is built on an assumption that people will violate the prohibition. This circularity is uncomfortable: the matter becomes "public" only if the three listeners gossip, but gossip is forbidden.

The Rambam addresses this by qualifying his ruling carefully. One may treat something as public knowledge and discuss it conversationally, without attempting to make it more widely known. What remains prohibited is *targeting* the spread of negative information — going to people who might not know about it in order to make David look bad. The Chafetz Chaim adds a further qualification: one may assume something is common knowledge and treat it as such, but one may not repeat it with the intention of causing harm to the subject.

This still does not fully dissolve the circularity, since the only path from "said before three" to "common knowledge" runs through acts of repetition by those three. But the Rambam's practical reading is that once the threshold of three has been crossed, the social reality of information diffusion takes over and the legal status shifts accordingly.

### Rashbam Objects

The Rashbam raises and then rejects an interpretation of the *mechah* context that some of his colleagues proposed: perhaps the reason *mechah bifnei gimmel* is required is not merely by analogy to Rabbah bar Rav Huna's rule about public matters, but because repeating a *mechah* — telling people that so-and-so is a *gazlan* — would itself constitute *lashon hara*. If the witnesses may not repeat the protest without violating *lashon hara*, then the *mechah* can never effectively travel, unless there are three witnesses, in which case Rabbah bar Rav Huna's rule kicks in and the repetition is permitted.

The Rashbam dismisses this interpretation as fundamentally misguided: *delo dami lelashon hara klal de'ikar mecha'ah hainu mishum deba'inan sheyavo hadavar le'oznei hamachzik* — it is entirely unlike *lashon hara*, because the whole point of *mechah* is that the information must reach the occupant's ears. Moreover, it is a *mitzvah* to inform the *machzik* so that he may be careful with his *shtar*. The institution of *mechah* was specifically designed to spread: *tiknu lamar sheyimche bifnei edim kedei sheyotzi hakol* — the sages instituted that a *mechah* be made before witnesses so that the word would go out. Treating the spread of this information as a *lashon hara* concern inverts the entire institution.

### Lashon Hara LeToelet

Nonetheless, the underlying point of tension is real. When witnesses and their friends pass along the protest — "Did you hear? So-and-so says the person on his land is a *gazlan*" — there is at least a surface resemblance to *lashon hara*: negative information about an identifiable person is being spread through social networks. The resolution under the Rashbam's approach is that the spreading of *mechah* is a case of *lashon hara leto'elet* — speech that would otherwise be problematic is permitted, indeed mandated, when it serves a legitimate protective purpose. The *machzik* needs to know that someone is challenging his claim; the owner needs the legal mechanism of *mechah* to function. When the purpose of speech is to protect people's legal and financial interests, the ordinary restrictions yield.

## Renewing Mechah

### Gidol's Case

The *gemara* now presents the case of Gidol bar Minyumi, who needed to issue a *mechah*. He found Rav Huna, Chiya bar Rav, and Rav Hilkiya bar Tuvi — three prominent *rabbanim* who were sitting together — and issued his protest before them. Notably, he chose three, even though the presumed halacha follows the position of two witnesses. The following year, he returned, apparently believing that he was required to renew his *mechah* annually. They told him he was not: *lo tzerichat*.

### Rav: Once

Rav's ruling, as transmitted here, is: *keivan she'micha shanah rishonah shuv eino tzarich limchos* — once he made the *mechah* in the first year, he need never make it again. A single *mechah*, properly executed, is sufficient for the entire duration.

### Reish Lakish: Every 3 Ye…

Reish Lakish, citing Bar Kappara, disagrees: *v'tzarich limchos b'sof kol gimmel v'gimmel* — one is obligated to renew the *mechah* at the end of every three-year period. The reasoning is that after a *mechah*, the *machzik* is put on notice; he understands that his claim is contested and he must be careful with his *shtar*. But after three years of renewed silence — three years during which no further protest has been lodged — he may reasonably conclude that the earlier protest has been abandoned, and once again let down his guard. Therefore, the owner must renew the *mechah* within every three-year cycle.

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan found this troubling: *ki gazlan yesh lo chazakah* — does a *gazlan* have *chazakah*? If we have publicly declared this person a *gazlan*, how can he acquire any legal standing through continued possession?

### Permanent Notice

The *gemara* clarifies Rabbi Yochanan's concern and resolves it: the occupant is not literally a *gazlan* — that is only the claimant's allegation. But the practical thrust of Rabbi Yochanan's question is: once this person has been declared *ke'ein gazlan* — treated as one — he is permanently on notice that his claim to the land is disputed. Why should his *chazakah* be rehabilitated by three years of the owner's silence? He should always know he must guard his *shtar*.

### Rava Rules

Rava rules as a matter of halacha: *tzarich limchos b'sof kol gimmel v'gimmel* — the *mechah* must be renewed at the end of every three years. Despite the force of Rabbi Yochanan's argument — that once someone has been put on notice as a putative *gazlan* the *chazakah* clock should never run again — the halacha tips back toward the *machzik* on this point. If the owner allows three years to pass without renewing his protest, the *machzik* has effectively re-established his *chazakah*.

### Bar Kappara's Rule

Bar Kappara adds a further ruling about the quality of *mechah* renewal: *ir'er chozer v'ir'er chozer v'ir'er* — he challenges, returns, challenges again, and challenges again. *Im machamat ta'ana rishonah ir'er ein lo chazakah* — if each renewed protest is grounded in the same claim as the first, the series of *mechah* protests is valid and the occupant cannot establish *chazakah*. But *v'im lav yesh lo chazakah* — if the subsequent protests are based on different claims, the occupant does acquire *chazakah*. The reasoning is this: if I first protest that the occupant is a *gazlan*, three years later claim he is merely a sharecropper, and three years after that claim the arrangement was a *mashkon*, the constantly shifting story undermines all three protests. Each new claim is theoretically possible, and transitions between legal arrangements do occur. But an owner who cannot keep his story straight — whose claim shifts fundamentally from one *mechah* to the next — cannot sustain a coherent challenge, and his inconsistency gives the occupant the benefit of a consolidated *chazakah*.
