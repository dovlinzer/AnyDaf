# Chagigah 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Testimony Re: Kodesh

### Judah vs. Jerusalem

Today's *daf* is the last *daf* of *Chagigah*. We pick up in the middle of 26b and finish at the end of 27a. The passage under discussion concerns special circumstances in which people are believed — *ne'emanim* — regarding matters of *kodesh* and *terumah*.

The *mishnah* has already established a graduated framework of believability. People are believed about *kodesh* at all times, at least in *Yehudah*. They are believed about *terumah* at the time of the olive press, and about vessels of *terumah* related to *terumah* even seventy days before that season. Regarding *klei cheres*, they are believed even within fifteen *mil* of *Modi'in velifnim* — that is, within the radius surrounding *Yerushalayim*. A range of further cases is catalogued: we believe *gannavim*, tax collectors, and others about what they did or did not touch.

Now the *mishnah* reaches the line: *uv'Yerushalayim ne'emanim al ha-kodesh* — in *Yerushalayim* they are always believed regarding *kodesh*. This is similar to what was stated before, except that here it specifies *Yerushalayim* rather than *Yehudah*, which seems oddly more limiting. The *Gemara* will address this. And then: during the *regel*, everyone is believed even about *terumah*, even when it is not the season of the olive or grape press.

### Klei Cheres & Kilns

The *Tanna* continues: *ne'emanim al klei cheres gassim lekodesh* — in *Yerushalayim* proper, people are believed regarding the large, thick *klei cheres* designated for *kodesh* use. Outside *Yerushalayim*, within fifteen *mil* of *Modi'in*, they are believed about the thinner, more commonly used vessels. In *Yerushalayim* itself, they are believed about all *klei cheres* made for the purpose of *kodesh*.

The reason given is: *she'ein osin kivshonot b'Yerushalayim* — kilns are not built within *Yerushalayim*, and therefore there is a greater practical necessity to purchase *klei cheres* from the industrial outskirts. This policy necessity drives expanded believability. The basic distinction between the two categories seems to be that thin vessels are far more common, in much higher demand, and in much wider use — and therefore one must extend believability even further outside *Yerushalayim* for them. The thick, heavier vessels used specifically for *kodesh* are less common, and therefore greater believability is only extended within *Yerushalayim* proper. Rashi's explanation of this distinction is, as he often does, a matter of *svara* — reasoned speculation to reconcile a difficulty — rather than an explicit *mesorah* from his teachers.

### Sheat HaRegel Unity

The *mishnah* now arrives at its culminating statement: *uv'sha'as ha-regel af al ha-terumah* — during the *regel*, the distinctions fall away entirely and everyone is believed even about *terumah*. This is what the entire *perek* has been building toward.

One might have supposed this was simply a practical policy — ensuring *kohanim* have adequate opportunity to receive *terumah* during the festival, when the bulk of *klal Yisrael* is gathered. But the *Gemara*'s derivation reveals that the logic is far broader. The *regel* policy is not primarily about receiving *terumah* at all; it is about creating a fundamental sense of unity among *Bnei Yisrael*. The normal stratifications — *am ha'aretz* versus *chaver*, trusted versus untrusted — are completely dissolved at this time.

Think about what it would mean practically if all of *klal Yisrael* were gathered in *Yerushalayim* and everyone remained guarded against everyone else, suspicious about who was a *chaver* and who was an *am ha'aretz*, who had touched what. At home you can navigate those distinctions naturally, because you know who is who. In a crowd of hundreds of thousands of pilgrims, you would have to avoid virtually everyone. *Davka* at this moment, therefore, in contrast to the distinctions that opened this *masechet*, all distinctions are eradicated to create *achdut*.

### Chaveirim Pasuk

The *Gemara* asks: *mina hanei milei?* — what is the scriptural source? Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi cites the verse: *vaye'asef kol ish Yisrael el ha-ir k'ish echad chaveirim* — all of *Yisrael* gathered to the city as one man, *chaveirim*. The *Gemara* draws a play on words: *ha-kasuv asam kulan chaveirim* — Scripture made them all *chaveirim*, treating all of *Yisrael* as having the status of a *chaver*.

It is worth stepping back to appreciate the arc of the *masechet* as a whole. The opening chapter established which categories of people were exempt from *aliyah l'regel* — non-males, those with various disabilities, and so on. In one sense, this created social divisions at the very outset: only some are obligated to come, and others — like people engaged in deeply malodorous occupations such as tanning — are effectively unwelcome in the group. The *Yerushalmi*, as noted, actually understands that everyone was obligated to be *oleh l'regel* based on the *parasha* of *Hakhel*, where the text says *b'alot kol Yisrael lera'ot et p'nei* and then specifies *ha'anashim veha'nashim vehataf* — men, women, and children without distinction. The *Bavli* does not appear to share this position, but the contrast remains instructive.

The *masechet* ends, then, by saying that at the time of *aliyah l'regel* we do everything possible to unify. The whole enterprise of *aliyah l'regel* is about coming together — and the *Gemara* teaches that even the ordinary social hierarchies of purity and impurity must be set aside in service of that unity.

A further note: the *Yerushalmi* cites a different verse — *Yerushalayim ir shechubra lah yachdav* — and interprets *ha-ir shekulo na'aseh chaverim*, that in *Yerushalayim* all become friends. The basic question of whether *b'sha'as ha-regel* applies specifically within *Yerushalayim* or more broadly is worth considering in connection with how one reads the *mishnah*, and though it is most naturally read as referring to *Yerushalayim*, it remains an open point.

## Food Post-Regel

### Hapotei'ach Chavito

Now the *mishnah* turns to a striking question: if we suspend our concerns about *tum'ah* during the *regel*, what happens to food that was opened or begun during the *regel* but not finished by its end? Imagine a store owner in *Yerushalayim* who opened a barrel of wine during the *regel*. During the *regel* itself, anyone might come in and touch it — perhaps taste a little — and all of that is permissible because we suspend our concerns about who is an *am ha'aretz* and who is a *chaver*. Once the *regel* ends and you are left with a half-open barrel of wine, what do you do?

The case: *hapote'ach et chavito umatchil be'isato agav haregel* — one who opened his barrel and one who began kneading his dough during the *regel*. *Rabbi Yehudah omer yigmor* — Rabbi Yehudah says you may finish it even after the *regel*, since you would not have opened it during the *regel* if it were going to lose its status of *tahor* afterwards. *V'chachamim omrim lo yigmor* — the Sages say you may not complete it; once the *regel* ends, the status changes.

### Rav Ami's Debate

*Yasiv Rav Ami v'Rav Yitzchak Nafcha* — Rav Ami and Rav Yitzchak Nafcha were sitting on the porch of Rav Yitzchak Nafcha. One opened and asked: *ma'u sheyanichena l'regel acher?* — according to the *chachamim* who say *lo yigmor*, could one perhaps do as is done with wine pressed during *sha'as haggitot*, and simply set it aside to be used at the next *regel*?

The other replied: *yada hakol memash'shin bah v'at amrat yanichena l'regel acher?* — everyone is handling it and you suggest waiting until the next *regel*? The point is that once the *regel* is over, we must revert to our actual concerns: it is almost certainly *tamei*. We may have been willing to suspend that reality during the *regel*, but surely we cannot suspend it indefinitely until the next *regel* comes around.

### Rachmana Tihara

The first continues: *atu ad idna lav yada hakol memash'shin bah?* — but was not everyone also handling it during the *regel* itself? You were willing to suspend the concern then — why not suspend it again at the next *regel*? 

The second responds: *hachi hashta, bishlama ad idna tumas am ha'aretz b'regel rachmana tahara* — during the *regel*, the *Rachmana* declared the *tum'ah* of the *am ha'aretz tahor*. He invokes the verse cited by Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi — *vaye'asef kol ish Yisrael el ha-ir k'ish echad chaveirim* — which is not from the *Torah* itself and was originally spoken about *Pilegesh b'Givah*, and is therefore not technically *de'oraita*. All these stringencies are in any case rabbinic. Yet the language of *Rachmana tihara* is invoked, perhaps because it was felt so deeply problematic to be this lenient across the board that the authority of the *Torah* is summoned to sanction it. During the *regel*, then, we are in a state of what one might call suspended disbelief — the *tum'ah* of the *am ha'aretz* is simply declared not to exist.

*Ela hashta tmeia hi* — but once the *regel* ends, the status of *tamei* takes hold. The moment we acknowledge that *amei ha'aretz* touched it and there is no longer any reason to treat it as *tahor*, the food assumes a status of *tamei*. It cannot then revert to *tahor* by waiting for the next *regel*, because a *tamei* status cannot be suspended a second time by the same mechanism that prevents it from applying in the first place.

The contrast to the case of *sha'as haggitot* is instructive. During the season of the oil and wine pressing, there was at least a factual basis for trust — it was genuinely understood that people made an effort to be *metaher* everything at that time. So it made sense to say: after the pressing season, we lose that basis for trust; when the next pressing season comes around, we regain it. Here, the suspension during the *regel* rests not on a positive assumption that people purified themselves, but simply on the Torah's declaration overriding the concern. Once that declaration no longer applies, what remains is an acknowledged *tum'ah* with nothing to counter it.

### Yanichena Debate

*Neima ketannai* — the *Gemara* suggests this is in fact a *tannaic* dispute. One *beraita* says *yanichennah l'regel acher* — leave it for the next *regel* — and another says *lo yanichennah l'regel acher* — do not leave it.

The *Gemara* initially suggests this is a straightforward *machlokes tannaim* parallel to the dispute of Rabbi Yehudah and the *chachamim*. But it quickly notes that *tisbara* — this does not hold up: Rabbi Yehudah says *yigmor*, you can finish it right away, so he would have no need to say leave it for the next *regel*. The *Gemara* therefore reverses the attributions: the *beraita* saying *lo yanichennah* — he need not leave it — represents Rabbi Yehudah, meaning one need not wait until the next *regel*; the *beraita* saying *yanichennah* — leave it for the next *regel* — represents the *Rabbanan*, meaning that is the only option they permit. In any event, one opinion does hold that you may leave it for the next *regel*, drawing a parallel to the wine and oil pressed during *sha'as haggitot*, and whether that parallel holds is itself a *machlokes*.

## Tevila Post-Regel

### Mishe'avar Haregel

Now the *mishnah* turns to something even more striking: the possible touching of the *keilim* of the *Beit HaMikdash* by those who may have been *tamei*. *Mishe'avar haregel* — once the *regel* is over — *ma'avirin al taharat azarah*: you take out all the vessels from the *azarah*, the Temple courtyard, and immerse them.

*Avar haregel beyom shishi* — if the *regel* ended on a Friday, *lo ma'avirin mipnei kevod haShabbat* — you would not perform the immersion due to *Shabbat*, since immersing all the Temple vessels while also needing to prepare for *Shabbat* would leave no time for the *kohanim* to make their own preparations. *Rabbi Yehudah omer af lo beyom chamishi* — Rabbi Yehudah says even if the *regel* ended on Thursday you would not immerse, *she'ein kohanim penuyim* — the *kohanim* have no time. The *Gemara* adds via a *beraita*: they were preoccupied with removing the enormous accumulation of ashes — *hadeshen she'al hatapuach* — the pile at the center of the *mizbe'ach* that had built up over the entire *regel*. Removing that ash was the primary task on the night after the *regel*, consuming all of Thursday. By Friday, preparations for *Shabbat* were already pressing. So in such a case, the immersion of the vessels would be deferred until the following Sunday.

What this reveals is twofold. First, we must be assuming that the *keilim* in the *azarah* became *tamei* — from whom and how, we will discuss below. Second, not only do we suspend that concern throughout the *regel* itself, we are willing to continue suspending it for an additional two or three days — through Thursday, Friday, and *Shabbat* — if circumstances demand. The concern is real enough that we immerse the vessels once we can, but not so urgent that we disrupt *Shabbat* or prevent the *kohanim* from clearing the *mizbe'ach*.

### Hizaheru Shelo Tigu

*Keitzad ma'avirin al taharat azarah* — how precisely was this done? *Matbilin et hakeilim shehayu baMikdash* — they would immerse the vessels that had been in the *Mikdash*. And they would announce during the *regel* itself: *hizaheru shelo tigu bashulchan* — be careful not to touch the *shulchan*.

This is a remarkable statement. The *shulchan* is not in the *azarah* but in the *heichal*, the interior sanctuary. The *kohanim* would enter the *azarat kohanim*, and beyond the *sha'ar Nikanor* only *kohanim* could generally go. The *heichal* proper was accessible only to the handful of *kohanim* who lit the *menorah* or arranged the bread on the *shulchan*. So who exactly is being warned not to touch it?

Rashi reads this as addressed to *kohanim am ha'aretz* — *kohanim* who did not maintain the standards of a *chaver*. But even then, only one or two *kohanim* per day entered the *heichal*, and the concern about which of them was an *am ha'aretz* seems manageable. The *Gemara* does not fully spell out the mechanism of access, and we will have to work through the clues provided.

### Lechem Panim Tamid

The *Gemara* explains why the *shulchan* is the specific vessel singled out: *shulchan ksiv bei tamid* — regarding the *shulchan*, the *Torah* writes *lechem panim lefanai tamid*, the bread of presence before Me at all times. Therefore, the *shulchan* cannot be moved to be immersed. All other vessels in the *Mikdash* had *shenayim ushlishim* — backup duplicates, seconds and thirds — *she'im nitme'u harishonim yavi'u shenayim tachteihem*: if one became *tamei* they would simply use the replacement. *Kol hakeilim shehayu baMikdash te'unim tevila* — all vessels, if they became *tamei*, required immersion — *chutz mimizbei'ach hazahav umimizbei'ach hanechoshet* — except for the gold altar and the copper altar, which are considered like the ground, *kekarka*, and therefore do not become *tamei*. This is *divrei Rabbi Eliezer*. *Vachachamim omrim mipnei shehein metzupin* — the *chachamim* say it is because they are coated, which is puzzling, since coating with metal would ordinarily make an object *more* susceptible to *tum'ah*, not less. We will return to this below.

The *Gemara* notes that the *tanna* of the *beraita* says the warning was about both the *shulchan* and the *menorah*: *hizaheru shelo tigu bashulchan u-vimenorah*. Regarding the *shulchan*, the *tamid* clause is explicit. Regarding the *menorah*, the *Gemara* observes: *lo ksiv ba tamid* — the *menorah* does not have a *tamid* written about it in the same sense. Rashi clarifies: the *menorah* does say *le'ahlos ner tamid*, but that *tamid* refers only to the nighttime burning. The *menorah* is not required to burn during the day, so its *tamid* is a *tamid milailah l'lailah*, not a perpetual day-and-night obligation. Therefore it could theoretically be moved and immersed during daylight hours.

The other opinion in the *Gemara* responds: *at hamenorah nochach hashulchan* — the verse places the *menorah* opposite the *shulchan*, treating them as if equivalently *tamid*. The first position rejects this: that verse merely fixes the *menorah*'s location opposite the *shulchan*; it does not create a *tamid* obligation for the *menorah* itself.

### Kli Etz Asui Lenachat

The *Gemara* now raises a pointed question: *v'teipok lei d'kli etz ha'asui lenachat hu* — and furthermore, should we not say that the *shulchan* cannot become *tamei* in the first place, because it is a wooden vessel made to remain in its place, and *kol kli etz ha'asui lenachat lo mitamei* — any wooden vessel made to remain in place does not become *tamei*?

The principle: *dumya d'sak be'inan* — we require an analogy to a *sak*, a sack. The *Torah* speaks about a *sak* becoming *tamei*, and we learn that only something that can be carried both full and empty — *mitaltel malei v'reikam* — is susceptible to *tum'ah*. Something that cannot be carried when full is not a vessel in the relevant sense. And the *shulchan* — precisely because it is not permitted to be moved, because it has a *tamid* obligation — should count as *asui lenachat*, made to remain in its place, and therefore should not be susceptible to *tum'ah* at all.

The logic is almost circular: the reason they are worried about touching the *shulchan* is that they cannot immerse it. But the very reason they cannot move it — its permanent, fixed, *tamid* status — is the same reason it should not become *tamei* to begin with. So why the concern?

## Magbihim Oto

### Reish Lakish's Drash

The *Gemara* resolves the difficulty: *ha nami mitaltel malei v'reikam hu, kideresh Lakish* — it can in fact be carried full and empty, as Reish Lakish teaches. *D'amar Reish Lakish, mai d'ksiv al hashulchan hatahor* — what does Scripture mean by calling it the *tahor shulchan*, the pure table? *Miklal shehu tamei* — the very need to declare it *tahor* implies that it is capable of being *tamei*. If it were inherently immune to *tum'ah*, why would Scripture need to say it is *tahor*?

*Ela melamed shemagbihin oto umarin bo l'olei regalim* — rather, this teaches that they would lift the *shulchan* up and display it to the pilgrims ascending for the *regel*. *V'omrim lahem reu chibatchem lifnei hamakom* — and they would say to them: see how beloved you are before God.

### Nes Gadol Lechem

And what would they show? *Siluko kesiduro* — that the removal of the bread was like its placement: the *lechem hapanim* remained fresh and warm when taken off the *shulchan* just as it was when placed upon it, even after a full week. *D'amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: nes gadol na'asa belechem hapanim, kesiduro kach siluko* — a great miracle was performed with the bread of presence: just as it was placed warm and fresh, so it was removed warm and fresh. *Shenemar lasum lechem cham beyom hilakcho* — as it is written, to place warm bread on the day of its taking.

So the *shulchan* was indeed moved — lifted — during the *regel*. It was not permanently fixed in the sense of never being touched. The *Rachmana* calls it *tahor* precisely because they would lift it and move it, which means it was capable of receiving *tum'ah*.

### Parochet Aside

Now the question becomes: where were the people shown the *shulchan*? The answer has to do with the remarkable physical access granted to pilgrims during the *regel*. The gates of the *azarah*, the *ulam*, the *heichal*, and even the *kodesh kodashim* were aligned opposite one another. During the *regel*, the *parochot* — the curtains separating these chambers — were pushed aside, allowing the people to see straight through. How exactly the *mizbe'ach* situated in the way was visible is itself a subject of *machlokes* regarding where the *mizbe'ach* was positioned. But the *Gemara* presents a picture of remarkable, almost scandalous, access: the very entrance to which only the *kohen gadol* on *Yom Kippur* had access was made visible to the *olei regalim*.

In this context, *magbihim oto* — they would lift the *shulchan* — may mean only that they moved it to a more central position within the *heichal* so that those looking through the aligned gates could see it. That would explain why the warning *shelo tigu bashulchan* was issued: even a slight repositioning exposes the *shulchan* to the possibility of being touched, and it is precisely because they could not then immerse it that the warning was necessary.

There is an interesting further question of how the people could verify that the bread was warm. One cannot easily perceive warmth from a distance, though the fragrance of fresh bread might perhaps carry. This remains somewhat unclear.

What is evident is that this practice of physical access fits a broader pattern. On *Sukkot*, for instance, the *hakafa* with the *aravot* appears from the *mishnah* to have been performed by all of *Yisrael*, entering even the space between the *ulam* and the *mizbe'ach*, which is ordinarily off-limits even to *kohanim* unless specifically required. The people were being given a level of proximity to the *keilim* of the *Beit HaMikdash* that under ordinary circumstances they would never have had. That is why the post-*regel* immersion of vessels was necessary.

This connects the *masechet*'s closing movement back to its opening. The verse says *yeira'eh kol zechurcha et p'nei Adonai*, but its plain meaning in the other direction — to *see* God — animates the entire enterprise. People ascending the *regel* sought not merely to appear and bring their *korban* but to have some genuine encounter. The *chachamim* responded by pulling aside the *parochot*, displaying the *shulchan* and the *lechem hapanim*, allowing the people in. The *Mikdash* was brought out to the people and the people were brought into the *Mikdash*, at a moment when all the stratifications of *chaver* and *am ha'aretz*, all the ordinary purity hierarchies, were set aside. That, the *masechet* says, is what *aliyah l'regel* truly meant.

## Tzipui & Etz

### Coating vs. Substance

The *Gemara* now raises a different reason why the *shulchan* might be susceptible to *tum'ah*, independent of whether it is moved: *v'teipok lei mishum tzipui* — perhaps it becomes *tamei* simply because it is coated with gold, making it a metal vessel.

The *Gemara* cites a *mishnah*: *hasshulchan vehaddufki shenifchatu* — if a table or a *dufki* (a type of folding seat, according to Rashi) broke, or was covered with marble — *shechiipan b'shayish* — and there remains enough exposed surface *mekom hanachat kossos*, sufficient space to hold cups, it is still *tamei*. The implication: coating a vessel can change its legal status. If coating with marble can make a wooden vessel effectively neutral (though here a functional remnant remains), how much more might coating with gold change the vessel's character and render it a metal vessel — metal being susceptible to *tum'ah* even when *asui lenachat*. The concern then is not whether the *shulchan* is moved, but whether its gold coating transforms its halachic category from wood to metal.

One might respond: perhaps acacia wood — *atzei shittim* — from which the *shulchan* was made, is considered sufficiently significant and important that even if coated with gold, the wood retains its primary status and the gold is secondary to it. This is admittedly a counterintuitive suggestion — that wood might be considered more important than gold — but as a halachic category it is worth examining.

### Reish Lakish & Yochanan

*Hanicha le-Reish Lakish* — this approach works well for Reish Lakish, who says: *lo shanu ela bichlei achsalgin haba'im mimedinas hayam* — the rule that coating changes the status of the underlying wood applies only to a certain lower-quality wood that comes from overseas. *Aval bichlei mesamim lo bateili* — but with more important, stronger wood (one might compare this to oak or similar), the coating does not nullify the wood's status. If acacia wood falls into this latter category, then even the gold coating does not convert the *shulchan* into a metal vessel; it remains legally a wooden vessel.

*Ella le-Rabbi Yochanan de-amar afilu bichlei mesamim nami bateili* — but according to Rabbi Yochanan, who holds that even with important wood the coating transforms the vessel's status, what can be said? According to Rabbi Yochanan, the gold coating of the *shulchan* should make it a metal vessel, susceptible to *tum'ah* not because it is moved but because of the coating itself.

A further distinction is then raised: perhaps the rule that coating changes status depends on whether the coating is *tzipui omed* — a permanent, solid plating — or *tzipui she'eino omed* — a plating that is not durable or well-secured. Rashi explains: *she'eino mechuzzak be-masmerim yafeh* — not fastened well with nails. And he adds: *v'tzipui de-Mikdash lav omed hu* — the plating on the *Mikdash* vessels is not of the *omed* type. The implication is that the gold plates are applied like separate panels nailed onto the surface rather than being truly fused or embedded, such that they could conceivably come off. If the plating is not fully permanent, perhaps it does not change the vessel's halachic category.

### Rachmana Kra'ah Etz

Reish Lakish then poses a direct question to Rabbi Yochanan: *betzipui omed o betzipui she'eino omed?* — does the rule that coating changes the vessel's status apply even when the coating is not permanent? *Bechofeh et halizbez o beshe'eino chofeh et halizbez?* — and does it matter whether the coating covers the entire rim and border of the vessel or not? Rabbi Yochanan answers: none of these distinctions matter. The coating changes the status regardless of whether it is permanent or removable, and regardless of whether it covers the trim completely.

If so, one cannot exempt the *shulchan* on grounds of the coating being impermanent. According to Rabbi Yochanan, the *shulchan* should halachically be a gold vessel, susceptible to *tum'ah* by virtue of the coating alone.

The *Gemara* resolves this: *ela shani Shulchan de'Rachmana krayei etz* — the *shulchan* is different because the *Rachmana* calls it wood. Even though all the halachic logic would classify it as a metal vessel by virtue of its gold plating, the *Torah* itself overrides that classification and designates the *shulchan* as a wooden vessel. The proof text is: *dechtiv hamizbe'ach etz shalosh amot* — the altar, wood, three *amot* high — and immediately following: *vayedaber elai zeh hashulchan asher lifnei Hashem* — and he said to me: this is the table that is before God. The *Gemara* notes a textual curiosity: the verse opens with the *mizbe'ach* and closes with the *shulchan*. This will receive its own *drash* momentarily.

### Mizbeach to Shulchan

The *Gemara* observes: *patach bemizbe'ach vesiyeim beshulchan* — the verse opens with the *mizbe'ach* and ends with the *shulchan*. Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish both say: *bizman she'Beit HaMikdash kayam mizbe'ach mechaper al adam* — when the *Beit HaMikdash* stood, the *mizbe'ach* atoned for a person. *Achshav shulchano shel adam mechaper alav* — now, a person's table atones for him.

Rashi reads this as referring to *hachnasat orchim* — welcoming guests. But there is a broader resonance. The *Gemara* elsewhere speaks of bringing an *olah* to God and *shalmei chagigah* for oneself — and refers to the *mizbe'ach* itself as *shulchan rabecha*, your Master's table. Now, in the absence of the *Beit HaMikdash*, the sanctity of eating migrates from the *mizbe'ach* into the home. This is enacted through washing one's hands and reciting *brachot*, transforming the domestic table into a form of sanctified eating. It imports the reality of the *Mikdash* into every Jewish home — without the divisions, without the hierarchies. Anyone has a table; anyone can eat with awareness of God's presence. The *kedushah* that previously required proximity to the *mizbe'ach* becomes universally accessible.

This is the note on which the halachic portion of the *masechet* closes. We began with a *Mikdash* in which access was stratified — who had to come, who could come, who was welcome — and we end with the *mizbe'ach*'s role transferred to the domestic table, available to all of *Yisrael* without distinction.

## Mizbachot Exemption

### Kol Hakeilim Shniim

The *mishnah* states: *kol hakeilim shehayu baMikdash yesh lahem shenayim ushlishim* — all vessels in the *Mikdash* had seconds and thirds as replacements, so that if the first became *tamei*, a backup could be substituted. The exception is the *shulchan*, for the reason already established: it could not be moved without violating the *tamid* requirement.

### Mizbeach HaNechoshet

Similarly, the copper *mizbe'ach* had no replacement — not because of a *tamid* requirement, but because of its categorization as *karka*. The *Gemara* derives this from the verse: *mizbeach adama ta'aseh li* — make for Me an altar of earth. The copper *mizbe'ach* is called *adama*, ground, and is therefore halachically treated as attached to and part of the earth. Even if one wished to argue that it is made of *nechoshet* and is therefore a metal vessel, the *Torah*'s own designation as *adama* overrides that classification.

### Mizbeach HaZahav

The gold *mizbe'ach*, which was located inside the *heichal* and was considerably more portable than the copper *mizbe'ach* of the outer courtyard, is also exempt. The *Gemara* derives this through a *hekesh*: the verse mentions *hamenorah v'hamizbechos* — the *menorah* and the altars — connecting the two altars to one another. The *menorah* was susceptible to *tum'ah*, but the altars are treated together, and the gold *mizbe'ach* is also classified as *kekarka*, part of the ground.

### Metzupin Disagreement

The *chachamim* in the *mishnah* stated that the altars are exempt *mipnei shehein metzupin* — because they are coated. The *Gemara* objects: *aderaba kivan d'metzupin ninhu mitamu* — on the contrary, because they are coated with metal, they should be *more* susceptible to *tum'ah*, not less! Perhaps, then, the *chachamim* are saying the opposite: *eima d'chachamim mitamim mipnei shehein metzupin* — the *chachamim* hold that the altars *do* become *tamei* by virtue of their coating.

The *Gemara* resolves this by reframing: *v'iba'is eima Rabbanan l'Rabbi Eliezer ka'amri* — the *chachamim* are speaking to Rabbi Eliezer's logic. Rabbi Eliezer quoted a *pasuk* to show the altars are *kekarka*. The *chachamim* ask: why do you need a *pasuk*? The altars are coated with metal, and you might have thought that makes them metal vessels — but in fact *mevatel batil tzipuyeihu gabeihu*, their coating is considered secondary and nullified relative to the substance beneath. Without the gold coating being halachically significant, the altars remain in the category of wooden vessels made to remain in place, and require no *pasuk* to establish their exemption from *tum'ah*. Either way — whether through Rabbi Eliezer's *pasuk* or through the *chachamim*'s reasoning that the coating is nullified — all agree that the altars do not become *tamei*.

## Aggadata Gehinnom

### Talmidei Chachamim

The *masechet* closes with *aggadata*. Rabbi Abahu said in the name of Rabbi Elazar: *talmidei chachamim ein or shel Gehennom sholetes bahen* — *talmidei chachamim*, scholars of Torah, the fire of *Gehennom* has no dominion over them.

### Kal Vachomer

The proof is a *kal v'chomer* from the salamander. The ancients believed that the salamander was born of fire and that one who smeared oneself with its blood would be immune to fire. *U'mah salamandra shetoldos esh hi, v'hasach midamah ein or sholetes bo* — if the salamander, which is a product of fire, confers fireproofing upon one who anoints himself with its blood, then *talmidei chachamim shekol gufan esh* — scholars whose very essence is fire —how much more so must they be protected.

### Devarai Kaesh

*Dechtiv halo ko devarai kaesh neum Hashem* — as it is written: are not My words like fire, says God. The words of God are fire, and those who have made those words their entire being are, in a sense, fire themselves. *Al achat kama v'chama* — how much more so are they protected from *Gehennom*.

### Poshei Yisrael Karimon

Reish Lakish adds: *ein or shel Gehennom sholetes beposhei Yisrael* — the fire of *Gehennom* has no dominion even over the sinners of *Yisrael*. Here the *kal v'chomer* is drawn from the gold *mizbe'ach* — and this is likely why the aggadic passage is appended here, following the discussion of the *mizbe'ach*: *mah mizbe'ach hazahav she'ein alav ela ke'ovi dinar zahav, kama shanim ein haesh sholetet bo* — the gold *mizbe'ach* had a coating only as thick as a gold *dinar*, yet the fire burned upon it for years without damaging it. *Poshei Yisrael shemillei mitzvos karimon* — sinners of *Yisrael* who are filled with *mitzvot* like a pomegranate — how much more so will the fire not harm them.

The proof-text: *dechtiv kefelach harimon rakatekh* — like a slice of pomegranate are your temples. *Al tikri rakatekh ela rekanim shebach* — do not read *rakatekh* but *rekanim shebach*, the empty ones among you — even the apparently empty ones are filled with *mitzvot* like the seeds of a pomegranate. *Al achas kama v'chama* — all the more so will they be protected from the fires of *Gehennom*.

### Seder Moed Conclusion

*Hadran alach chomer bakodesh vesalika lah maseches Chagigah* — and with that, *Seder Moed* is complete.

It is worth pausing over what the *masechet* has accomplished. The opening chapters dealt with who was obligated for *aliyah l'regel*, creating categorical distinctions: who had to come, who was excused, who was effectively excluded. The middle chapters built an elaborate framework of *tum'ah* and *taharah* that stratified *klal Yisrael* into *chaverim* and *amei ha'aretz*. And yet the closing movement of the *masechet* — and of *Seder Moed* — systematically dismantles each of those divisions.

During *sha'as haregel*, all of *Yisrael* is treated as *tahor*. The access ordinarily reserved for *kohanim* alone is extended toward the entire people. The *Mikdash* is brought to them; they are brought toward it. The *parochot* are pushed aside. The *shulchan* is lifted and shown. And then, when the *Beit HaMikdash* is no longer standing, even that residual hierarchy dissolves: the *mizbe'ach* that once stood as the singular site of atonement is replaced by *shulchano shel adam*, every person's table, available in every home.

The *masechet* began with the alternative to *aliyah l'regel* being *oleh lehakbil penei rabbo baregel* — ascending to greet one's teacher during the festival — a mode of encounter with the divine mediated through *Torah* rather than *korban*, and one available to all regardless of physical or social condition. It ends with *talmidei chachamim* protected from *Gehennom* by their Torah, and *poshei Yisrael* — even sinners — protected by their *mitzvot*, filled like the seeds of a pomegranate. Both *Torah* and *mitzvot* are universal. They do not require access to the *Mikdash*; they do not depend on being a *chaver* rather than an *am ha'aretz*; they create no division within *klal Yisrael*. With that theme of democratic embrace of all *Yisrael*, *Seder Moed* comes to its close.
