# Bava Kamma 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ze Neheneh Ve-Lo Chaser

### Intro: Benefit/No Loss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אִיתְהֲנִית! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַתְנִיתִין הִיא. הֵי מַתְנִיתִין? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְכִי תְּשַׁמֵּשׁ לִי. שְׁקַל סוּדָרֵיהּ כְּרַךְ לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִם נֶהֱנֵית – מְשַׁלֶּמֶת מַה שֶּׁנֶּהֱנֵית.
>
> **Translation:** **You have derived benefit** from my property, as by living there you saved the money you otherwise would have had to pay in order to rent out a different courtyard, and therefore you must pay me for the benefit you derived. Rami bar Ḥama **said to him:** This dilemma is not new; rather, **it is** discussed in **the mishna,** and the mishna already provided a solution. Rav Ḥisda asked him: To **which mishna** are you referring? Rami bar Ḥama **said to him: After you serve me,** I will tell you. Rav Ḥisda **took** hold of Rami bar Ḥama's **scarf [*suderei*] and folded it**, as an act of service. Rami bar Ḥama then **said to him:** This is the mishna: **If** the animal **derives benefit,** the owner of the animal **pays** for **the benefit that** the animal **derived.** This demonstrates that one who derives benefit must pay for the benefit he derives, even if the injured party is not entitled to payment for his loss.

The Gemara on the top of 21b takes up one of the central and most consequential discussions in this chapter: the principle of *ze neheneh ve-ze lo chaser* — one party receives a benefit while the other party incurs no loss whatsoever. The Mishna had already established that when an animal eats food belonging to another in *reshut ha-rabim*, the owner cannot be held liable for *mazik*, yet must nonetheless pay for the benefit his animal received, since that benefit came at someone else's expense. But what of a case where the benefit truly costs the other party nothing?

The Gemara's guiding scenario is a person who moves into someone else's apartment while the owner is away and has no interest in renting it out — the owner could not have made money from it in any case, yet the occupant has clearly received a substantial benefit. Is any payment owed? This question drives the entire *sugya*.

### Mishna: Three Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַמַּקִּיף חֲבֵירוֹ מִשָּׁלֹשׁ רוּחוֹתָיו, וְגָדַר אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה וְאֶת הַשְּׁנִיָּה וְאֶת הַשְּׁלִישִׁית – אֵין מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ. הָא רְבִיעִית – מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to cite conclusive evidence from another case: **We learned** in a mishna (*Bava Batra* 4b): If **one's** fields **surround** the fields of **another on three sides, and he fenced in the first, the second, and the third** field, thereby providing protection also to the other man's field, the court **does not obligate** the owner of the inner field to share in the costs of the fence, as he can claim that he does not derive benefit from it, since his field remains exposed on the fourth side. The Gemara infers: **But** this indicates that if his fields surrounded the inner field on all four sides, and the owner of the outer fields fenced the field on the **fourth** side as well, the court **does obligate** the owner of the inner field to share in the expenses.

The Gemara introduces the discussion with a *tnan* — a citation from the Mishna. The case: *ha-makif chavero mi-shalosh ruchosav* — Reuven has fields surrounding Shimon's field on three sides. Reuven then builds fences. According to Rashi, these are the dividing walls between Reuven's and Shimon's fields; according to Tosafot, they are the outer perimeter walls of Reuven's own fields. For present purposes, the Tosafot reading is more instructive, since it produces the greater *chiddush*: once Reuven builds his outer walls on all four sides, Shimon's field, sitting in the middle, becomes protected from the outside world — from animals, from intruders, from everything — even though Shimon contributed nothing. Reuven has done Shimon an enormous favor.

Yet the Mishna rules: *ein mechayvin oso* — Shimon is not obligated to pay anything after only three walls have been built, because his field remains exposed on the fourth side.

### Fourth Wall Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: זֶה נֶהֱנֶה וְזֶה לֹא חָסֵר – חַיָּיב! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַתְּ גְּרַמְתְּ לִי הֶקֵּיפָא יַתִּירָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Conclude from** the mishna that where **this one derives benefit and that one does not suffer a loss,** the one who derives benefit is **obligated** to pay for that benefit. In this case, the owner of the inner field benefits from the fence while the owner of the outer field does not suffer a loss, because in any event he would have needed to build those fences, and the owner of the inner field is obligated to share in the expense. The Gemara rejects this: It **is different there** because the owner of the outer field **can say to** the owner of the inner field: **Your** field **caused me to have to** build the fence with **a larger circumference** than I would have otherwise needed to had your field not been there, and therefore your field caused me an additional expense. This case is therefore similar to those where this one derives benefit and that one suffers a loss.

Once the fourth wall is in place, however, the Mishna's ruling shifts: *mechayvin oso* — Shimon is made to pay. The reasoning appears straightforward: Shimon is clearly *neheneh* — he has benefited — and Reuven was going to build the wall regardless of Shimon's presence, so it is a case of *ze lo chaser*. From this the Gemara initially draws the inference: *shma mina ze neheneh ve-ze lo chaser chayav* — even pure benefit-without-loss obligates payment.

### Perimeter/Extra Cost

The Gemara immediately pushes back: *shani hasam* — this case is different. Reuven can say to Shimon, *at garamta li hikefa yeseira* — you caused me to build more wall. The geometry is decisive: had Reuven's four fields been contiguous without Shimon's field interrupting the middle, his perimeter fence would have been smaller. Shimon's presence increases the perimeter and therefore increases Reuven's cost. It is thus not a true case of *ze lo chaser* at all — the benefit Shimon received did come at some expense to Reuven, even if no one is being blamed for wrongdoing. The point is structural: because you were there, I had to spend more money. This case therefore cannot serve as a proof for the general principle.

### Want/Assumption Question

This raises a further difficulty: how do we know Shimon even wants the fence? The answer the Gemara works with is that we operate from a general presumption — a farmer with fields exposed to animals and trespassers would ordinarily want protection. If one could genuinely establish that a particular farmer never would have hired a watchman, never would have erected a fence, and had no desire for protection, then there would be no *hana'ah* — no benefit — to speak of, and no payment could be demanded. Just as one need not pay for a free animal ride if the animal happened to eat food one was actively trying to keep it from, so too here: if the supposed benefit was genuinely unwanted, it is not a benefit. But in the ordinary case, protection of one's field is treated as self-evidently desirable.

### Rabbi Yosi Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אִם עָמַד נִיקָּף וְגָדַר אֶת הָרְבִיעִית – מְגַלְגְּלִין עָלָיו אֶת הַכֹּל. טַעְמָא דְּגָדַר נִיקָּף, הָא מַקִּיף – פָּטוּר;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts another resolution: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the continuation of that mishna. **Rabbi Yosei said: If** the owner of **the surrounded,** i.e., inner, field **arose and enclosed the fourth** side on his own, the court **imposes upon him** the obligation to pay his share of **all** of it, as through his actions he demonstrated that he was interested in having the fence installed. The Gemara infers from this: The **reason** the court imposes upon him the obligation to pay his share of all of it is because the owner of **the surrounded** field arose and **enclosed** the fourth side himself. By inference, had the owner of **the surrounding** field built the fourth fence, the owner of the inner field would be **exempt.**

The Gemara then cites a *ta shema* from Rabbi Yosi: *im amad nikef ve-gadar es ha-revi'is, megalgelin alav es ha-kol* — if Shimon himself got up and built the fourth wall, he demonstrates conclusively that he wanted the protection, and he is then made to pay his share of the entire cost of all four walls. Whether this is calculated as *hana'ah* or as something closer to retroactive contractual participation is a question the Gemara leaves open, but the result is clear: by acting, Shimon obligates himself.

### Initial Inference

From Rabbi Yosi's statement, the Gemara attempts to draw a negative inference: *ta'ama de-gadar nikef* — the reason Shimon pays is only because *he* built the fourth wall. *Ha makif patur* — if it was Reuven who built the fourth wall, Shimon would be exempt. This appears to contradict the *tanna kamma* and to prove that even in a case that costs Reuven something (the extra perimeter), Shimon is not obligated to pay — all the more so in a genuine case of *ze lo chaser*. The provisional inference: *shma mina ze neheneh ve-ze lo chaser patur*.

### Loss Rebuttal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, זֶה נֶהֱנֶה וְזֶה לֹא חָסֵר – פָּטוּר! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: לְדִידִי סַגִּי לִי בִּנְטִירָא בַּר זוּזָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Conclude from** the mishna that where **this one derives benefit and that one does not suffer a loss,** the one who derives benefit is **exempt.** The Gemara rejects this: It **is different there** because the owner of the inner field **can say to** the owner of the surrounding fields: **For me, the protection** of a fence **worth a dinar would have been sufficient;** I did not wish to pay for such an expensive fence.

The Gemara immediately qualifies this. Even granting that Rabbi Yosi exempts Shimon when Reuven built the fourth wall — that does not prove a *patur* for *ze neheneh ve-ze lo chaser* — because this case still involves actual *chisaron*. The extra perimeter represents a real cost. The exemption here, if there is one, must have a different explanation.

### Watchman/Value

That explanation is supplied: *shani hasam de-amar lei ledidi sagi li be-netira bar zuza* — Shimon can say to Reuven, I never needed a wall in the first place. A watchman watching my field for a nominal daily wage would have been sufficient for me. This goes to the heart of measuring *hana'ah*: the wall may have cost Reuven ten thousand dinars, but the benefit to Shimon is not ten thousand dinars' worth. It is worth only what Shimon himself would have paid to achieve equivalent protection — perhaps a trivial sum. This is the correct way to quantify the benefit in ambiguous cases, and it explains why even with the perimeter cost in play, Rabbi Yosi might exempt Shimon: not because there is no loss, but because the claimed *hana'ah* is wildly disproportionate to any real benefit Shimon would have paid for.

## House/Upper Story

### Scenario Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַבַּיִת וְהָעֲלִיָּיה שֶׁל שְׁנַיִם, שֶׁנָּפְלוּ; אָמַר בַּעַל הָעֲלִיָּיה לְבַעַל הַבַּיִת לִבְנוֹת, וְהוּא אֵינוֹ רוֹצֶה – הֲרֵי בַּעַל הָעֲלִיָּיה בּוֹנֶה בַּיִת וְיוֹשֵׁב בָּהּ, עַד שֶׁיִּתֵּן לוֹ יְצִיאוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts another resolution: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a mishna (*Bava Metzia* 117a): If **a house and its upper story,** which belonged **to two** separate people, **collapsed,** necessitating that the entire structure be rebuilt, and **the owner of the upper story told the owner of the** lower story of the **house to build** the lower story again so that he could rebuild the upper story, but the owner of the lower story **does not want to** do so, **the owner of the upper story may build** the lower story of the **house and live in it until** the owner of the lower story **will pay him for his expenses,** and only then will he be required to vacate the lower story of the house and build the upper story.

The Gemara brings another *ta shema*. The scenario: *ha-bayis ve-ha-aliyah shel shnayim* — a ground floor and an upper story belonging to two different people. The house collapses. The owner of the upper story says to the owner of the ground floor: rebuild, so that I can rebuild on top of you. The ground floor owner refuses and leaves. The upper story owner, with nowhere to live, hires contractors, builds the ground floor, and takes up residence in it while awaiting reimbursement. The Mishna rules that he may remain there *ad she-yiten lo yetzi'osav* — until the ground floor owner repays his construction expenses.

### Payment Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְצִיאוֹתָיו הוּא דִּמְחַיֵּיב לֵיהּ בַּעַל הַבַּיִת, הָא שְׂכָרוֹ – לָא; שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, זֶה נֶהֱנֶה וְזֶה לֹא חָסֵר – פָּטוּר! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּבֵיתָא לַעֲלִיָּיה מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: It **is** all of **his expenses that the owner of the** lower story of the **house is obligated to** pay **him, and** he **does not** subtract from the amount compensation **for rent** for the time that the owner of the upper story lived in the lower story of the house. **Conclude from** the mishna that where **this one derives benefit and that one does not suffer a loss,** the one who derives benefit is **exempt,** as the owner of the upper story derived benefit from living in the lower story, while the owner of the lower story did not suffer a loss, since he said he did not need it. The Gemara rejects this proof: It **is different there because the** lower story of the **house is subjugated to the upper story,** because it also serves as the foundation of the upper story, and therefore the owner of the lower story of the house is obligated to provide lodging to the owner of the upper story in the interim.

The Gemara draws a consequential inference from this ruling: *yetzi'osav hu de-michayiv lei baal ha-bayis* — when the ground floor owner finally decides to return and pay, he owes the full construction costs. *Ha sacharo lo* — but he does not deduct from that payment any rent that the upper story owner owed for having lived in the ground floor during the interim. This, the Gemara notes, is a case of *dar be-chatzer chaveiro she-lo bi-reshut* — living in another's space without permission — since the upper story owner had rights only to the second floor, not the ground floor. And yet no rent is charged. Can we infer from here *shma mina ze neheneh ve-ze lo chaser patur*?

### First Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף זֶה הַדָּר בַּחֲצַר חֲבֵירוֹ שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעְתּוֹ – צָרִיךְ לְהַעֲלוֹת לוֹ שָׂכָר. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: זֶה נֶהֱנֶה וְזֶה לֹא חָסֵר – חַיָּיב! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, מִשּׁוּם שַׁחְרוּרִיתָא דְאַשְׁיָיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts another resolution. **Come** and **hear** a proof from the continuation of the mishna, in which Rabbi Yehuda disagrees with the first *tanna* and holds that the owner of the upper story may not reside in the lower story of the house without payment. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** A proof that the owner of the upper story must pay rent is from the *halakha* that **even this** one **who resides in another's courtyard without his knowledge must pay him rent. Conclude from** the mishna that when **this one derives benefit and that one does not suffer a loss,** the one who derives benefit is **obligated** to pay. The Gemara rejects this: It **is different there,** as there it is possible to claim that the owner of the lower story suffers a loss **due to the blackening of the walls,** as when a person lives in a home, the value of the house depreciates due to the use.

The Gemara declines to draw that inference: *shani hasam de-bayis ve-aliyah mishtabed* — the case is fundamentally different. There is a special relationship of mutual dependence between the ground floor and the upper story: the upper story owner is being actively prevented from living in his rightful space precisely because the ground floor owner refuses to rebuild. Since the ground floor owner is the obstacle, he cannot in good conscience demand rent for the period during which his obstruction forced the upper story owner to occupy the ground floor.

Then the Gemara pivots. It cites: *Rabbi Yehuda omer al ze haya omer — dar be-chatzer chaveiro she-lo mida'ato tzarich le-haalos lo sachar* — Rabbi Yehuda explicitly rules that one who lives in another's house without his knowledge must pay rent. This is precisely the formulation of our central question. And if Rabbi Yehuda says you must pay, perhaps that proves: *shma mina ze neheneh ve-ze lo chaser chayav*.

### Blackening Walls

That explanation is supplied. When a person lives in a house, the walls darken — from candles, from cooking, from ordinary habitation. That constitutes a real, if small, *chisaron* — an actual loss to the owner. So it is not a pure case of *ze lo chaser*.

### Rashi: New House

One might object: if the walls of any inhabited house darken, the problem of *ze lo chaser* would never arise at all — every case of unauthorized dwelling would be a case of *chisaron*, and the entire legal question would be mooted. Rashi addresses this directly: the case in the *bayis ve-aliyah* scenario involves a house that the upper story owner himself just built. A brand new house losing its freshness the moment someone moves in is analogous to a new car depreciating the instant it leaves the lot. In a newly constructed building, the deterioration from first habitation is real and immediate. In an older building that has been lived in for years, the incremental damage from one more occupant may be imperceptible and the case thus remains a genuine *ze lo chaser*.

### Trivial Loss/Full Pay

This leads to a striking principle: even a trivial *chisaron* appears to be sufficient to trigger the full obligation. If a tenant receives one thousand dinars' worth of benefit from living in an apartment but causes only five dinars' worth of darkening damage per month, the Gemara nonetheless treats it as a case of *ze chaser* — and the full obligation to pay for the *hana'ah* received follows. Crucially, the payment is not calculated as *mazik* — as compensation for damage — but as *neheneh*, the value of the benefit received. These are different measures: the court does not assess retail rental value mechanically, but rather what this particular person actually received as benefit. Nevertheless, the principle holds: once the *lav lo chaser* falls away — even minimally — the receiving party cannot claim free enrichment. Conversely, where there is purely *ze lo chaser*, the party who gave the benefit appears to have no legal standing to demand payment, because the enrichment did not come at his expense.

## Amoraim Dispute

### Rav Ami: No Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחוּהָ בֵּי רַבִּי אַמֵּי, אָמַר: וְכִי מָה עָשָׂה לוֹ, וּמָה חִסְּרוֹ, וּמָה הִזִּיקוֹ? רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר: נִתְיַישֵּׁב בַּדָּבָר. הֲדַר שַׁלְּחוּהָ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא, אָמַר: כּוּלֵּיהּ הַאי שָׁלְחוּ לִי וְאָזְלִי! אִילּוּ אַשְׁכַּחִי בַּהּ טַעְמָא, לָא שָׁלַחְנָא לְהוּ?!
>
> **Translation:** This dilemma was not successfully resolved so **they sent it** to the **academy of Rabbi Ami** to ask his opinion. Rabbi Ami **said: And what did** this squatter **do to** the owner? **What loss did he cause him? How did he damage him?** Rav Ami holds that if this one derives benefit and that one does not suffer a loss, no payment is necessary. When asked about this dilemma, **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said: Let us consider the matter.** After waiting and not receiving a response **they sent** the question **to Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba again. He said: They keep sending me this** dilemma; **had I found a response** to their question, **would I not have sent them** a reply? They should have realized that Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba did not have an answer for them.

The Gemara records that the question was sent to the academy of Rav Ami. His response was blunt: *vechi mah asah lo u-mah chasro u-mah hiziko?* — what exactly did this person do to the owner? What loss did he cause? What injury did he inflict? If the owner suffered no harm, there is simply no legal basis for a claim. He concluded: *patur* — the unauthorized occupant is exempt.

One might also ask whether the *chisaron* should include non-monetary harm — the sheer fact that a stranger is living in one's home without permission, which many homeowners would find deeply objectionable even if it cost them nothing financially. The Gemara does not raise this as a cognizable legal category, and presumably it is not actionable — but it remains an ongoing background question in the *sugya*.

### Rabbi Yochanan Conflict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר, רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְהַעֲלוֹת לוֹ שָׂכָר. רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: צָרִיךְ לְהַעֲלוֹת לוֹ שָׂכָר.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that the *amora'im* discussed this matter: **Rav Kahana** says that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: He need not pay him rent,** and **Rabbi Abbahu** says that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: He must pay him rent.**

The Gemara records that Rabbi Chiya bar Abba was asked and said he was unsure: *nistapakti bah* — the case was genuinely difficult, with serious arguments on both sides. When pressed again for an answer, he replied with remarkable candor: *kulei hai shalchu li ve-azlu? Ei ashkachi bah ta'ama lo shachana lachem* — you keep sending me the same question? If I had found a reason to come down on one side, do you think I wouldn't have told you? The Gemara then records a direct conflict of traditions. Rav Kahana transmitted in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *eino tzarich le-haalos lo sachar* — no payment is required. Rabbi Abbahu transmitted in the name of the same Rabbi Yochanan: *tzarich le-haalos lo sachar* — payment is required.

### Rav Pappa: Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הָא דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ – לָאו בְּפֵירוּשׁ אִתְּמַר, אֶלָּא מִכְּלָלָא אִתְּמַר. דִּתְנַן: נָטַל אֶבֶן אוֹ קוֹרָה שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא מָעַל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: This** statement **of Rabbi Abbahu,** citing Rabbi Yoḥanan, **was not stated explicitly; rather,** it **was stated from an inference** that Rabbi Abbahu drew from something that Rabbi Yoḥanan said in a different context. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Me'ila* 5:4): If the treasurer of consecrated property **took** for himself **a stone or** a cross **beam** that had been **consecrated, he has not** violated the prohibition of **misuse** of consecrated property. This is because his act of taking the stone or the beam has not effectively removed it from within the jurisdiction of Temple property, as any item under his control is in the possession of the Temple treasury.

Rav Pappa resolves the contradiction: *ha de-Rabbi Abbahu lo be-ferush itmar ela mi-klala itmar* — Rabbi Abbahu's version, that payment is required, was not an explicit statement of Rabbi Yochanan but rather an inference Rabbi Abbahu drew from something Rabbi Yochanan said. It was not a direct ruling.

### Me'ilah Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְתָנָהּ לַחֲבֵירוֹ – הוּא מָעַל, וַחֲבֵירוֹ לֹא מָעַל. בְּנָאָהּ לְתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא מָעַל, עַד שֶׁיָּדוּר תַּחְתֶּיהָ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה.
>
> **Translation:** If the treasurer **gave it to another** as a gift, **he** has then **misused** consecrated property, as he removed it from the possession of the Temple and conveyed it into the possession of another. But **the other** man, who received the item, has **not misused** a consecrated item until he actually uses the item. If the treasurer himself **built it into his house, he has not** violated the prohibition of **misuse** of consecrated property **until he has lived beneath it** for an amount of time **worth one *peruta*.**

What did Rabbi Abbahu infer from? The Gemara explains via a Mishna: *natal even o korah shel hekdesh* — one who takes a stone or beam belonging to *hekdesh*. In this scenario, the one taking the beam is the *gizbar* — the temple treasurer — who has legitimate custody of *hekdesh* property. Having the beam in his possession is therefore not yet an act of *me'ilah*; he has not yet taken it away from *hekdesh*'s domain. If he transfers it to a private individual — *netanah le-chaveiro* — then *hu ma'al ve-chaveiro lo ma'al* — he has committed *me'ilah* by taking it out of *hekdesh*'s domain, while the recipient, who was an unwitting participant, has not.

If, however, he incorporates the beam into his own house — placing it in the roof — the Gemara rules *harei zeh lo ma'al ad she-yadur tachteha shaveh perutah* — he has not yet committed *me'ilah* until he sits beneath it and derives a *perutah*'s worth of *hana'ah*. Rashi explains that the beam was merely laid loosely rather than fully hammered into place. At the moment he derives that minimal benefit, however, *me'ilah* is triggered — not through an act of physical taking but through the enjoyment of *hana'ah*. In other words, *me'ilah* operates in two modes: as a form of *gezeilah*, taking *hekdesh* property away from its domain; or as a form of *hana'ah*, deriving benefit from *hekdesh*.

This, Rabbi Abbahu argued, proves the principle in civil law as well: *zot omeret hadar be-chatzer chaveiro she-lo mida'ato tzarich le-haalos lo sachar* — just as deriving *hana'ah* from *hekdesh* without its knowledge triggers obligation, so too one who lives in another's property without permission must pay rent. In the *me'ilah* case, *hekdesh* is the *chaver* as it were — the owner — and the benefit was derived *she-lo mida'ato*, and it is *ze lo chaser* since *hekdesh* does not lose from one sitting beneath a beam. And yet *me'ilah* applies. Rabbi Yochanan was silent after this inference was stated; Rabbi Abbahu took the silence as agreement.

### Me'ilah Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיָתֵיב רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְקָאָמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת, הַדָּר בַּחֲצַר חֲבֵירוֹ שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעְתּוֹ – צָרִיךְ לְהַעֲלוֹת לוֹ שָׂכָר; וּשְׁתֵק לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Abbahu sat before Rabbi Yoḥanan and** was **saying in the name of Shmuel: That is to say, one who resides in another's courtyard without his knowledge must pay him rent,** similar to the treasurer who resides beneath the consecrated beam without detracting from the value of the beam in any way but has nevertheless violated the prohibition of misuse since he has derived personal benefit from it. **And** Rabbi Yoḥanan **remained silent** and offered **him** no reply.

The Gemara, however, rejects this reasoning: *olo hi* — it is not so. Rabbi Yochanan's silence was not agreement but dismissal. The inference was simply not compelling. The standard cases of *me'ilah* — using a golden goblet belonging to *hekdesh*, drinking from *hekdesh* wine — are all cases of *ze neheneh ve-ze lo chaser*: *hekdesh* does not lose the cup when you drink from it. But the fact that benefiting from *hekdesh* triggers *me'ilah* does not mean that benefiting from a private person's property triggers civil obligation. *Me'ilah* operates on a completely different standard than civil monetary law. One cannot import the laws of *me'ilah* to establish a monetary claim in ordinary disputes.

### Raba: Hekdesh Known

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיהוּ סָבַר – מִדִּשְׁתֵיק, מוֹדֶה לֵיהּ. וְלָא הִיא, אַשְׁגּוֹחֵי לָא אַשְׁגַּח בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abbahu **thought** that **since he was silent,** this must mean **he concedes to his** conclusion, and therefore, from then on he would cite this opinion in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan. **But** that is **not so.** The reason Rabbi Yoḥanan was silent was because **he did not pay attention** to Rabbi Abbahu and did not bother to contradict his opinion on the issue. In truth Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion is as was reported in his name by Rav Kahana, that a squatter living on someone else's premises without his permission and without causing him any loss does not need to pay the owner any rent.

Why is the inference so obviously flawed? Raba explains: *hekdesh she-lo mida'at ke-hedyot mida'at dami* — using *hekdesh* property without its knowledge is treated as equivalent to using it with full knowledge. Rashi explains this by noting that God is omniscient. Since *hekdesh* is always treated as if fully aware, the *she-lo mida'ato* formulation does not apply to it the way it applies to a private owner. Therefore, deriving benefit from *hekdesh* is inherently governed by *me'ilah* law, not by the logic of *ze neheneh ve-ze lo chaser* in ordinary civil law.

### Tosafot: Different Standard

Tosafot is uncomfortable with Rashi's reasoning. If *she-lo mida'ato* means only that no formal agreement was made — not that the owner was literally unaware — then pointing to divine omniscience seems beside the point. A private owner who discovers someone in his house mid-stay is still a case of *she-lo mida'ato* in the legal sense, because no permission was granted. Tosafot therefore reads Raba's statement differently: all it means is that *me'ilah* is not measured by whether civil monetary law would also create an obligation. One can be liable for *me'ilah* in situations where, had the property belonged to a private person, no court would have ordered payment. The two regimes operate on entirely different standards and cannot be cross-applied. The inference of Rabbi Abbahu therefore fails not because *hekdesh* is different in a subtle metaphysical sense, but because *me'ilah* is simply a different category of law altogether.

## Settled Halakha

### Rabbi Abba's Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר זַבְדָּא לְמָרֵי בַּר מָר, בְּעִי מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב הוּנָא: הַדָּר בַּחֲצַר חֲבֵירוֹ שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעְתּוֹ, צָרִיךְ לְהַעֲלוֹת לוֹ שָׂכָר, אוֹ לָא? אַדְּהָכִי – נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba bar Zavda sent** a message **to Mari bar Mar** saying: **Raise** the following **dilemma before Rav Huna:** Does **one who resides in another's courtyard without his knowledge need to pay him rent or not? In the meantime,** before he was able to respond to the question, **Rav Huna died.**

At the top of 21a, the Gemara records: Rabbi Abba bar Zavda sent a message to Mari bar Mar asking him to inquire of Rav Huna — does one who lives in another's house without the owner's knowledge need to pay rent or not?

### Raba's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא, הָכִי אָמַר אַבָּא מָרִי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְהַעֲלוֹת לוֹ שָׂכָר, וְהַשּׂוֹכֵר בַּיִת מֵרְאוּבֵן – מַעֲלֶה שָׂכָר לְשִׁמְעוֹן. שִׁמְעוֹן מַאי עֲבִידְתֵּיהּ? הָכִי קָאָמַר: נִמְצָא הַבַּיִת שֶׁל שִׁמְעוֹן – מַעֲלֶה לוֹ שָׂכָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba, son of Rav Huna, said to him** in response to the question that was asked of his father: **So did my father, my Master, say in the name of Rav: He does not need to pay him rent. And** he also stated another *halakha*: **One who rents a house from Reuven must pay rent to Shimon.** The Gemara is puzzled: **Shimon? What does he** have to **do** with this? The Gemara explains: **This** is what he is **saying,** i.e., what he means: If it **is discovered that the house** he rented did not actually belong to Reuven but rather it was **Shimon's,** he **must pay rent to** Shimon.

Before the question could be posed, Rav Huna died. Raba bar Rav Huna then transmitted: *hachi amar Abba Mari mi-shmeih de-Rav* — this is what my father said in the name of Rav — *eino tzarich le-haalos lo sachar* — no payment is required. This appears to settle the question on the side of *patur*.

### Rental Fraud Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּרְתֵּי?! הָא – דְּקָיְימָא לְאַגְרָא, הָא – דְּלָא קָיְימָא לְאַגְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this statement: Did Rav Huna state **two** contradictory *halakhot*? On the one hand he says that one who resides in a courtyard without the owner's knowledge does not need to pay rent, but on the other hand he says that if it is discovered that the true owner of a rented house was someone else, and therefore the tenant was living in another's courtyard without the owner's knowledge, he is obligated to pay him rent. The Gemara resolves the difficulty: **This** second *halakha*, which stated that he is obligated to pay rent, is referring to a courtyard that **stands to be rented out,** while **that** *halakha*, which stated that he is not obligated to pay rent, is referring to a courtyard that **does not stand to be rented out.**

Immediately, however, the Gemara introduces a complication. Rav teaches: *ha-socher bayis mi-Reuven ma'aleh sachar le-Shimon* — if one rents a house from Reuven and it turns out the house actually belongs to Shimon, the tenant must pay Shimon. This seems puzzling: from the tenant's perspective, he entered into a contract with Reuven, not with Shimon. With respect to Shimon, he is simply *dar be-chatzer chaveiro she-lo mida'ato* — living in Shimon's house without his knowledge or agreement. If the *halakha* is that such a person need not pay, why does he have to pay Shimon?

### Rental Status Distinction

The Gemara's initial answer: *ha de-kaima le-agra ha de-lo kaima le-agra* — one case involves property that was functioning as a rental property, the other does not. When Reuven was actively renting out the property, that demonstrates the house was the kind of asset from which income was being generated. Shimon, the true owner, lost a rental opportunity — so the case is really one of *chisaron*, not *ze lo chaser*.

### Agreement Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר נָמֵי: אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַב, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הַדָּר בַּחֲצַר חֲבֵירוֹ שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעְתּוֹ – אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְהַעֲלוֹת לוֹ שָׂכָר, וְהַשּׂוֹכֵר בַּיִת מִבְּנֵי הָעִיר – מַעֲלֶה שָׂכָר לַבְּעָלִים. בְּעָלִים מַאי עֲבִידְתַּיְיהוּ? הָכִי קָאָמַר: נִמְצְאוּ לוֹ בְּעָלִים – מַעֲלִין לָהֶן שָׂכָר.
>
> **Translation:** **It was also stated: Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin says** that **Rav says, and some say** that **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin says** that **Rav Huna says: One who resides in another's courtyard without his knowledge does not need to pay him rent, and one who rents a house from the residents of the city must pay rent to the owners.** The Gemara is puzzled: **Owners? What do they** have to **do** with this? The case concerns one who rents a property from the residents of the city, i.e., the house is public property. The Gemara explains: **This is** what he **said:** If it is **discovered that** the house **did have owners** and was not public property, the renters **must pay rent to** those owners.

The Gemara's alternative account — and the one the lecturer finds more analytically compelling — is that the decisive factor is not the nature of the property but the nature of the tenant's commitment. When a tenant agrees to pay rent, even if he made that agreement with the wrong party, he has already positionally obligated himself. He is no longer someone who merely happened to receive an incidental benefit; he has contracted to pay. That contractual posture then shifts to the rightful owner. This is analogous to the *nikaf* who gets up and builds the fourth wall himself: by that act, he demonstrates his willingness to participate and pay, and the obligation crystallizes. The same logic applies to a tenant who negotiated and agreed to rent a property — he cannot later claim to be a passive beneficiary who owes nothing.

A parallel case reinforces this: *ve-im sachar bayit mi-vnei ha-ir ma'aleh sachar la-ba'alim* — if one rented a house from the city administration of a town (which assumed the property was communal) and it subsequently emerged that the property had a private owner, the tenant must pay the owner. There was no scammer here — the city acted in good faith. And yet, because the tenant agreed to pay rent, that obligation is redirected to the rightful owner. Here again, the analysis is the same: it is the tenant's prior agreement to pay, not merely the character of the property, that creates the obligation.

## Desolation/Sheidim

### Rav Sechora Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב סְחוֹרָה אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: הַדָּר בַּחֲצַר חֲבֵירוֹ שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעְתּוֹ – אֵין צָרִיךְ לְהַעֲלוֹת לוֹ שָׂכָר, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּשְׁאִיָּה יוּכַּת שַׁעַר״. אָמַר מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי: לְדִידִי חֲזֵי לֵיהּ, וּמְנַגַּח כִּי תוֹרָא. רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: בֵּיתָא מְיַתְּבָא – יָתֵיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Seḥora says** that **Rav Huna says** that **Rav says: One who resides in another's courtyard without his knowledge does not need to pay him rent because it is stated:** "Desolation remains in the city, **and the gate is stricken unto ruin"** (Isaiah 24:12), i.e., a house that is not lived in will collapse at some point due to neglect. Consequently, one who lives inside an otherwise uninhabited house is providing a service to the homeowner, as he maintains the house and prevents it from falling apart. **Mar bar Rav Ashi said: I saw this** ruin **and it gores like an ox,** i.e., it is devastating. **Rav Yosef stated** a similar idea: **A home that is lived in is settled** and safeguarded, while a home that is not lived in has no one to look after it and maintain it.

Rav Sechora then transmits a teaching of Rav Huna that reinforces the *patur* ruling: *ha-dar be-chatzer chaveiro she-lo mida'ato eino tzarich le-haalos lo sachar* — the unauthorized occupant owes nothing.

### Deterioration Prevention

The reason given takes an unexpected form. It is said in the name of Rav: *mishum she-ne'emar ve-she'iyah yukat sha'ar* — because the verse states that desolation will strike the gates. Leaving a house vacant causes it to deteriorate — or, in the idiom of the Gemara, to be afflicted by *shedim*. The unauthorized occupant, by living in the house, is actually doing the owner a service, preserving the property from the damage that vacancy brings. The benefit, then, is not unidirectional.

### Sheidim Evidence

Mar bar Rav Ashi added: *ledidi chazi li* — he personally witnessed what happens in desolate spaces: the *shedim* would gore one another like oxen. Notably, the very image of the goring ox appears here once more. The point is that an occupied house is measurably better off than a vacant one.

### Rav Yosef: Settlement

Rav Yosef offers a more rationalistic framing: *beita meitava yativ* — a house is simply better off when someone is living in it. When someone inhabits a property, they attend to emerging issues — leaks, damage, wear — in ways that a vacant property cannot self-correct.

### Storage vs. Habitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ – דְּקָא מִשְׁתַּמַּשׁ בֵּיהּ בְּצִיבֵי וְתִיבְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the difference **between** what Rav said and what Rav Yosef said? The Gemara answers: The difference **between them** is with regard to a house that the homeowner **uses** to store **wood and straw.** The house is not empty and desolate but there is nobody living in it. According to Rav Yosef's reasoning, a squatter there would not have to pay rent to the homeowner.

The Gemara then distinguishes between the two positions. The practical difference: *ika beina'ihu de-ka mishtamesh beih be-tzivei u-vetivna* — if the property is being used as a storehouse for wood and hay, it is not desolate in the sense that would attract *shedim*, so the supernatural argument does not apply. But Rav Yosef's practical argument — that human presence and habitation itself preserves and maintains the property — would still be operative. Someone storing goods in a house is not attending to it as a habitation and does not provide the ongoing maintenance benefit that a true occupant provides.

## Castle/Orphans

### Castle Construction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דִּבְנָה אַפַּדְנָא אַקִּילְקַלְתָּא דְיַתְמֵי. אַגְבְּיֵהּ רַב נַחְמָן לְאַפַּדְנֵיהּ מִינֵּיהּ. לֵימָא קָסָבַר רַב נַחְמָן: הַדָּר בַּחֲצַר חֲבֵירוֹ שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעְתּוֹ, צָרִיךְ לְהַעֲלוֹת לוֹ שָׂכָר? הָהוּא – מֵעִיקָּרָא קַרְמְנָאֵי הֲווֹ דָּיְירִי בֵּיהּ, וְיָהֲבִי לְהוּ לְיַתְמֵי דָּבָר מוּעָט. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל פַּיְּיסִינְהוּ לְיַתְמֵי, וְלָא אַשְׁגַּח. אַגְבְּיֵהּ רַב נַחְמָן לְאַפַּדְנֵיהּ מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who built a mansion [*apadna*] on a garbage heap [*akilkalta*] belonging to orphans,** and **Rav Naḥman confiscated his mansion from him** as he did not pay the owners of the property. The Gemara asks: **Shall we say** that **Rav Naḥman holds** that **one who resides in another's courtyard without his knowledge must pay him rent?** The Gemara rejects this: No, there is no proof taken from this case, because it was a unique situation. **In that** case **Carmanians,** nomadic tribes, **were initially living on** the property, **and they would pay the orphans a small amount** for the use of the land, and when this man built his mansion he removed the Carmanians from there. Rav Naḥman had **said to** the man who built the mansion: **Go and appease the orphans** with regard to their lost income, **but he did not pay attention** to the ruling. Therefore, **Rav Naḥman confiscated his mansion from him.**

The Gemara presents a final case study. A certain person built a substantial structure — described as an *apadna*, a kind of castle or grand building — on a *kilkalta de-yatmei*, what was essentially a dunghill belonging to orphans, a parcel of marginal, unclaimed-looking land.

### Rav Nachman Seizure

Rav Nachman's response was decisive: *agbayeh Rav Nachman le-apadna mineih* — he had the entire structure seized on behalf of the orphans as compensation for the unauthorized use of their land.

### Prior Payment

However, the Gemara clarifies: this is not simply a case of *ze lo chaser* being treated as *chayav*. In fact, *ha-hu me-ikara karmuna'ei havu dayrei beih* — previously, certain nomads or itinerant people had been living on that land and *yehavu le-yatmei devar mu'at* — they had paid the orphans a nominal rent. The land was thus established as a property that generated income, even if only a small amount.

### Huna Demand

Rav Huna therefore ruled: since you drove those paying tenants off and built your castle there, you must continue to pay the orphans rent at least at the rate the prior tenants were paying. *Amar leih zil payisinhu le-yatmei* — go and pay the orphans.

### Penalty Seizure

The builder refused — *velo ashgach* — he ignored the ruling. It was in response to this refusal that Rav Nachman had the entire structure seized. The seizure was therefore not a statement about *ze neheneh ve-ze lo chaser* as a general principle. It was a penalty for flouting a legitimate ruling to pay even nominal rent in a case that involved *chisaron* — real, established loss to the orphans.

## Payment Determination

### Payment Calculation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד מְשַׁלֶּמֶת מַה שֶּׁנֶּהֱנִית וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב: וּבִמַחְזֶרֶת. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ מַחְזֶרֶת נָמֵי פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: Under **what** circumstances does the owner of the animal **pay** for **the benefit that** his animal **derived?** If the animal ate produce in the public square in the area before the storefronts, the owner of the animal pays for the food from which it benefits. If the animal ate from food placed at the side of the public square, which is not a public thoroughfare, the owner of the animal pays for what it damaged, as the legal status of that area is like that of the property of the injured party. **Rav said:** When the mishna says that the owner of the animal pays for what it damaged, it is referring to a case **where** the animal **turns** its head to reach the food but the animal itself is standing completely inside the public domain and it eats while standing there. **And Shmuel said: Even if** it is standing in the public domain and it **turns** its head to eat from food placed at the side of the public square, its owner is **also exempt,** as the animal itself is in the public domain.

With the theoretical *sugya* largely resolved, the Gemara turns to the practical question: *keitzad meshalemet mah she-nehenit* — how exactly is payment calculated for the *neheneh*? The answer that emerges from the preceding analysis is that the standard is the value of the benefit actually received — not the retail cost of renting comparable property, not the extent of the damage caused, but what this particular person derived from the use. As established earlier, a farmer who would have hired a watchman for a nominal fee owes only the equivalent of that watchman's wage, not the full cost of the wall Reuven built. The *hana'ah* measure is thus sensitive to the actual benefit in the specific circumstances, which may differ substantially from market rates.

## Reshut Debate

### Mishna: Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִשְׁמוּאֵל – הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ דִּמְחַיֵּיב? כְּגוֹן דִּשְׁבַקְתַּהּ לִרְחָבָה, וַאֲזַלָה וְקָמָה בְּצִידֵּי רְחָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But according to** the opinion of **Shmuel, how can you find** a case in which the owner is **liable** to pay for all of the damage caused when his animal ate from food placed at the side of the public square, as indicated by the mishna? The Gemara answers: **For example,** when an animal **leaves the** public **square and goes and stands at the side of the** public **square,** and eats the food stored there. In that case its owner certainly pays for what it damaged, as this area is comparable to the property of the injured party.

The Gemara now shifts to a distinct but related topic: the *Mishna*'s rule regarding where an animal ate and how that determines liability. The *Mishna* had established that an animal eating food in *reshut ha-rabim* creates only a *neheneh* obligation, while food on the *tzidei reshut ha-rabim* — the sides of the public thoroughfare — is treated like the *chatzer ha-nizak*, placing full liability on the animal's owner.

### Rav: Head Turn

Rav's position: *u-vi-mechazeret* — the Mishna's case of *tzidei reshut ha-rabim* applies when the animal has to turn its head. An animal walking straight through *reshut ha-rabim* and eating food directly in its path is eating in *reshut ha-rabim*. But if it must turn its head to reach the food — diverting from its normal forward movement — that food is considered to be in *reshut ha-yachid* or *chatzer ha-nizak*, and full liability follows.

### Shmuel: Accessible

Shmuel disagrees: *afilu mechazeret nami patur* — even if the animal turned its head, it is still *patur*. For Shmuel, turning the head is still a use of *reshut ha-rabim*; it is simply not the *mezid* deviation that would take the animal out of the public domain. The only case in which Shmuel would hold the owner *chayav* for eating from the *tzidei reshut ha-rabim* is where the animal actually left the thoroughfare — *shavkah le-orchah ve-azlah ve-kamah be-tzidei rechavah* — abandoned the road entirely and stood on the side.

### Functional Definition

The deeper question here is how one defines the boundary of *reshut ha-rabim* — not formally, by legal title, but functionally, by the animal's ordinary mode of travel. The parallel to the first *Mishna* in Shabbat, where the laws of *hotza'ah* are defined functionally rather than geometrically, is noted explicitly: the definition is about the normal, expected use of the space. For Rav, anything requiring even a head-turn is already a deviation from the animal's normal public-domain usage. For Shmuel, the animal is still operating within the functional scope of *reshut ha-rabim* so long as the food is accessible from where it walks, even if a minor adjustment of direction is involved.

### Rav Nachman Clarify

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּמַתְנֵי לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא בְּאַפֵּי נַפְשָׁהּ: מַחְזֶרֶת – רַב אָמַר: חַיֶּיבֶת, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: פְּטוּרָה. וְלִשְׁמוּאֵל, ״מְשַׁלֶּמֶת מַה שֶּׁהִזִּיקָה״ הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ דִּמְחַיְּיבָא? כְּגוֹן דִּשְׁבַקָה לִרְחָבָה, וַאֲזַלָה וְקָמָה בְּצִידֵּי רְחָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are those who teach this *halakha*** as **an independent** dispute and not as an explanation of the mishna: If an animal is standing in the public domain and **turns** its head to eat from food placed at the side of the public square, **Rav says:** Its owner is **liable, and Shmuel says:** Its owner is **exempt.** The Sages asked: **But according to** the opinion of **Shmuel,** with regard to that which was said in the **mishna, that its owner pays for what it damaged, how can you find a case in which its owner will be liable?** The Gemara answers: **For example,** when an animal **leaves the** public **square and goes and stands at the side of the** public **square,** and eats the food stored there.

An alternative tradition teaches this debate independently of the Mishna, simply as a stand-alone dispute: if the animal turned its head — *rav amar chayavet, Shmuel amar pturah*. And for Shmuel, the question is posed again: when would the *Mishna*'s case of payment apply? The answer is the same: only when the animal left the road entirely and stood on the side.

### Store Front Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מִפֶּתַח הַחֲנוּת – מְשַׁלֶּמֶת מַה שֶּׁנֶּהֱנֵית. הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ – פְּשִׁיטָא בְּמַחְזֶרֶת; וְקָאָמַר (מָר) ״מַה שֶּׁנֶּהֱנֵית״, מַה שֶּׁנֶּהֱנֵית – אִין, מַה שֶּׁהִזִּיקָה – לָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak raises an objection** to this explanation of Rav's opinion: The mishna says that if the animal ate produce **from the entrance of the store,** its owner **pays** for **the benefit that** the animal **derived,** as the status of a store entrance is like that of the public domain. **How can you find these** circumstances? It is **obvious** that the discussion in this case concerns an animal that **turns** its head from the public domain to the entrance of the store, **and** the Master **says** that the owner of the animal **pays** for **the benefit that** the animal **derived.** Evidently, for **the benefit that** the animal **derived, yes,** this is what the owner pays, but he does **not** pay for that **which it damaged.**

The Gemara then revisits *mipetach ha-chanot mishalemet mah she-nehenit* — the *Mishna*'s ruling that an animal eating from the front of a store pays the value of what it benefited. This is treated as *reshut ha-rabim*, hence only *neheneh* and not *mazik*. But wait: by Rav's logic, if the food is off to the side of the road, the animal would have had to turn its head to reach it, and it should be *chayav* as *chatzer ha-nizak*! Why does the *Mishna* say only *neheneh*?

The Gemara poses this as a question the disputant raises and then answers himself: *de-kaima be-keren zavis* — the food is at a corner location. In certain configurations — Rashi illustrates with a diagram of a store positioned at a bend in the road — the animal traveling along its normal path would encounter the food directly in front of it without needing to turn. In that case, even though it is technically in front of a private store, it is straight in the animal's path. The *Mishna*'s case of *neheneh* applies to this configuration.

### Corner Location

The practical upshot: the distinction between *reshut ha-rabim* and *reshut ha-yachid* for purposes of animal eating is not geometric but functional. If the food is directly in the animal's path — whatever the geometry of the road — it is *reshut ha-rabim*. If reaching it requires the animal to deviate from its normal path, it is *reshut ha-yachid*. The corner location is therefore the paradigm case where food in front of a private store can still be functionally *reshut ha-rabim*.

## Private/Public Allocation

### Head Turn Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא מוֹתֵיב לַהּ וְהוּא מְפָרֵק לַהּ: דְּקַיְימָא בְּקֶרֶן זָוִית.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak **raised the objection, and he resolved it** by explaining that the case in the mishna is one where a store **is located on a corner** and it is situated in a way that some of the produce there would be in an animal's path as it turns the corner, and therefore the animal would not need to turn its head away from the public domain in order to eat the produce.

The Gemara's concluding section introduces yet another version of the Rav-Shmuel dispute. According to this version — *ika de-amri* — the two opinions actually agree that if the animal turned its head to eat, it is *chayav* in all cases. That is, turning the head always means leaving *reshut ha-rabim* and entering *reshut ha-yachid*, and full liability follows. This is the position of *kulei alma* on that question.

### Allocation Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: מַחְזֶרֶת – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּחַיֶּיבֶת. כִּי פְּלִיגִי, בְּמַקְצֶה מָקוֹם מֵרְשׁוּתוֹ לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who state** a different explanation of the dispute between Rav and Shmuel. In a case **where it turns** its head to eat from the sides of the public square, **everyone agrees that** its owner is **liable** to pay the full cost of the damage. **When they disagree** it is with regard to a case **where one allocates space from his property,** as he does not have a use for it, and he adds this space **to the public domain** by leaving it accessible for the public to use, and the damage took place in that area. The dispute is about whether the area is categorized as private property or the public domain.

The real dispute, according to this reading, concerns a different scenario: *miktze makom me-reshuto le-reshut ha-rabim* — one who voluntarily opens up part of his private property to public use. Imagine a property owner whose land abuts *reshut ha-rabim* who sets his fence back and allows the public to walk through part of what was his *chatzer*. He does not formally transfer title — he retains legal ownership — but he functionally allocates the space to public use.

### Rav: Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכִי אִתְּמַר, אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא מַחְזֶרֶת, אֲבָל מַקְצֶה מָקוֹם מֵרְשׁוּתוֹ לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – פְּטוּרָה. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ מַקְצֶה מָקוֹם מֵרְשׁוּתוֹ לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – חַיֶּיבֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is what was stated,** meaning this was their dispute: **Rav says** that in the mishna, **they taught** that one is liable **only** in a case where his animal **turns** its head to the sides of the public square, as this area is categorized as private property, **but if one allocated some space from his property** and added it **to the public domain** and the damage happened there, the owner is **exempt,** as that area is treated as part of the public domain. **And Shmuel says: Even if he allocates space from his property** and adds it **to the public domain,** the owner of the animal is **liable** as the damage took place in an area with the legal status of private property.

Rav rules: *aval miktze makom me-reshuto le-reshut ha-rabim paturah* — if an animal eats food that was placed in this allocated space, the owner is *patur*. By allowing public use of the space, the owner has effectively converted it into *reshut ha-rabim* for these purposes. The animal was functioning within what is operationally a public thoroughfare; the private title the owner retains does not change the functional reality.

### Shmuel: Title Retained

Shmuel rules: *afilu mekadesh makom bi-reshuto le-reshut ha-rabim chayavet* — even if the owner allocated his space to public use, the animal is still *chayav*. Because the owner retains legal title, the space remains, in law, *reshut ha-yachid* — *reshut ha-nizak* — and the owner of the animal is liable for any damage done there. Functional public use cannot override legal ownership for purposes of these *halakhot*. The analogy to Rockefeller Center — which periodically asserts its property rights over its publicly accessible plaza to prevent acquisition of a public easement — illustrates precisely the tension Rav and Shmuel are debating: can functional dedication to public use be treated as legal transformation of the domain's status?

The Gemara reaches the end of the *daf* at this point, and the question of what *chayavet* means in Shmuel's formulation — and what the full practical implications of his position are — will be taken up in the continuation.
