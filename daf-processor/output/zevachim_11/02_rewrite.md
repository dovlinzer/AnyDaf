# Zevachim 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blood Invalidation

### Three Opinions

The daf opens by citing a *mishnah* from elsewhere in *Masechet Zevachim* — specifically from 91b — which records a three-way dispute concerning bloods that entered the *heichal*, the inner sanctuary. Rabbi Akiva holds that *kol damim shenichnasu laheichal l'chaper psulim* — any blood whatsoever that entered the sanctuary to effect atonement renders the offering *pasul*. The Rabbis restrict this rule to the *chatat* alone. And Rabbi Eliezer includes both the *chatat* and the *asham*, deriving this from the *hekesh* between them expressed in the verse *kachatat k'asham* (Vayikra 7:7).

### Rabbi Akiva's Logic

Rabbi Akiva draws his expansive position from a verse in *Parshat Tzav* (Vayikra): *v'chol chatat asher yuvah midamah el ohel moed lo teachal ba-esh tisaref* — every *chatat* whose blood is brought into the Tent of Meeting shall not be eaten but burned in fire. The double formulation — *v'chol chatat*, "and every sin offering" — reads to Rabbi Akiva as a kind of double plural, a linguistic amplification that extends the disqualification beyond the *chatat* itself to all bloods that enter the sanctuary.

### Rabbis vs. R. Eliezer

The Rabbis confine the rule to the *chatat* alone, while Rabbi Eliezer extends it to include the *asham* on the basis of the *hekesh* linking *chatat* and *asham*. The tension between these positions — when the *chatat* and *asham* are treated as equivalent and when they are not — runs as a central thread through much of the discussion that follows. As will become clear, the question of which direction legal inferences may travel between these offerings is not settled so simply.

---

## Rava's Reasoning

### Rava's Answer

The *Gemara* asks: *bishlama* Rabbi Eliezer, his reason is stated — but what is the Rabbis' reason for excluding the *asham*? Rava explains: we cannot derive from a *kal vachomer* from the *olah* that the *asham*'s blood entering the sanctuary renders it *pasul*.

### Failed Kal va-Chomer

The attempted *kal vachomer* runs as follows: if the *olah*, which is completely burned — *kalil* — and yet its blood entering the sanctuary does not disqualify it, could we not argue *kal vachomer* that the *asham*, which is not burned completely, would similarly not be disqualified when its blood enters? The argument collapses immediately on inspection, however: this proves the *asham* is *kasher* when its blood enters, not *pasul* — the exact opposite of what we were trying to establish.

### Refuting Olah Logic

A possible objection to the *olah* as the basis for this reasoning is then raised: *mah l'olah sheken einah mechaperet* — what is special about the *olah* is that it does not atone for a specific transgression. Since the *olah* is not an atonement offering in the way the *chatat* is, the comparison breaks down. The *olah* belongs to a fundamentally different category, and legal inferences across such a divide are suspect.

### Meal Offering Attempt

The *Gemara* therefore attempts to construct the argument from the *minchat choteh*, the meal offering of a sinner — an offering that, like the *chatat* and *asham*, does serve an atonement function. But this too is rejected: *mah leminchat choteh sheken einah min hazevach* — the *minchah* is simply not in the category of animal sacrifice (*zevach*), and the analogy therefore fails for a different reason. The *Gemara* briefly considers whether the *chatat ha'of*, the bird sin offering, might serve instead, but that too is rejected — the *chatat ha'of* follows the ruling of Rabbi Avin and involves a different process; the blood is collected differently, with a finger rather than through standard *shechitah* and *kabbalah*.

### Kal va-Chomer Fails

Having exhausted these alternatives, the *Gemara* reverts to the *olah*. Now both possible analogies have failed on their own terms. As the formulation goes: *lo rei zeh k'rei zeh v'lo rei zeh k'rei zeh* — this case is not fully like that one, and that one is not fully like this one. The *kal vachomer* cannot be constructed from either direction.

## Kodshei Kodashim

### Holiness Level

Since no single offering provides an adequate basis for a *kal vachomer*, the *Gemara* seeks instead the *tzad hashaveh* — the common feature shared by the *olah* and the *chatat* that might allow an inference to the *asham*. The answer: both the *olah* and the *chatat* are *kodshei kodashim*, offerings of the highest degree of sanctity. From this shared characteristic one might seek to derive: *af ani avi asham shehu kodesh kodashim* — I will similarly bring the *asham*, which is also *kodshei kodashim*, into the same legal category.

### Asham Classification

The *asham* does indeed qualify as *kodshei kodashim*. The attempt to group it with the *olah* and *chatat* on this basis initially seems promising. However, a distinction is then introduced that cuts against the inference.

### Asham's Minimum

Rava of Barnish points out to Rav Ashi: *mah l'tzad hashaveh shebahen sheken ein lahen kitzvah* — what is distinctive about the common feature of the *olah* and *chatat* is that neither has a prescribed minimum monetary value. *Tomar b'asham sheyesh lo kitzvah* — but you cannot make the same inference regarding the *asham*, which does have a minimum value (*kitzvah*). The Torah was more stringent with the *asham* in requiring a minimum, and this distinguishing feature prevents the inference from succeeding. This, the *Gemara* concludes, is the fundamental reason (*ta'amei d'Rabbanan*) underlying the Rabbis' position.

---

## Verse Interpretation

### Damah Pronoun

The *Gemara* now turns to the precise derivation from the verse itself. The relevant verse (Vayikra) reads: *v'chol chatat asher yuvah midamah el ohel moed* — every *chatat* from whose blood is brought into the Tent of Meeting. The word *damah* — "its blood" — is now subjected to close analysis. The *Gemara* first reads it as *damah shel zu v'lo damah shel acher*: the verse speaks of the blood of this specific offering and not the blood of a different one. That is, it is only the *chatat*'s own blood entering the sanctuary that disqualifies it; blood from another offering entering does not disqualify the *chatat*.

### R. Eliezer's Read

Rabbi Eliezer takes the word *damah* differently. For him, the reference to blood — *damah v'lo besarah* — teaches that it is the blood of the offering that triggers disqualification, not its flesh. He is not concerned with whether it is the blood of this offering or another's; his reading addresses a different question entirely.

### Rabbis' Limit

The Rabbis, by contrast, learn two separate lessons from *dam damah*. First, as Rabbi Eliezer does, that the verse speaks of blood and not flesh. Second — and this was already noted at the bottom of 10b — that the rule applies only to the *chatat* and does not extend to other offerings. The double formulation (*dam... damah*) yields both restrictions simultaneously for the Rabbis.

### R. Eliezer Differs

Rabbi Eliezer does not read the verse in this doubled fashion. Since the word *damah* is the grammatically correct form for "its blood," he treats it as a single, necessary term — not an amplification from which additional rulings may be derived — and uses it solely to teach that blood, not flesh, is the operative element. He therefore cannot draw from the same verse the Rabbis' limiting conclusion.

## Mincha Connection

### Asham Intent

Returning to the broader question, the *Gemara* notes: *bishlama l'Rabbanan d'amri asham sheshachato shelo lishmo kasher* — according to the Rabbis, an *asham* that was slaughtered *shelo lishmo*, not for its designated purpose, remains *kasher*. It does not fulfill the owner's obligation, but the offering itself is valid. This sets up the question of how the *minchah* is linked, by *hekesh*, to both the *chatat* and the *asham*.

### Mincha Dual Nature

The *Gemara* observes that the *minchat choteh* — the meal offering brought by a sinner — can in some respects resemble a *chatat* and in other respects resemble an *asham*, depending on the context. This dual character follows from a specific verse.

### Rabbi Shimon Quote

Rabbi Shimon cites the verse from *Parshat Tzav* (Vayikra 6:10): *kodesh kodashim hi kachatat v'ka'asham* — it is most holy, like the *chatat* and like the *asham*. This verse, he explains, is the source for the *minchat choteh* being treated as analogous to both.

### Two Mincha Types

The practical consequence runs as follows: *minchat choteh harei hi kachatat* — the *minchat choteh* is like the *chatat* — therefore, if the *kemitzah*, the removal of the handful, was performed *shelo lishmah*, not for its intended purpose, it is *psulah*, invalid. By contrast, *minchat nedavah harei hi ka'asham* — the voluntary *minchah* is like the *asham* — therefore, if its *kemitzah* was performed *shelo lishmah*, it remains *kasher*. The *asham* slaughtered *shelo lishmo* is still valid; so too the voluntary *minchah* whose handful was removed without the requisite intent.

## Four Blood Works

### Blood Services

The *Gemara* now identifies the four *avot ha-dam*, the four primary blood services performed in connection with animal sacrifices: *shechitah* (slaughter), *kabbalah* (collection of the blood), *holachah* (conveying the blood to the altar), and *zrikah* (sprinkling the blood).

### Mincha Parallels

Each of these four services has a parallel in the *minchah*. In place of *shechitah* stands the *kemitzah* — the removal of the handful. In place of *kabbalah* stands the placing of the *minchah* into a *kli sharet*, a sacred service vessel. *Holachah* corresponds directly to *holachah* — carrying the handful to the altar. And in place of *zrikah* stands the placing of the *kometz* upon the fire.

### Kemitza Role

The analogy between *kemitzah* and *shechitah* is worth pausing over. Just as *shechitah* is the initial act of preparation that enables all subsequent blood service, the *kemitzah* is the first act that sets the *minchah* service in motion. And just as any valid *shochet* may perform *shechitah* in the context of sacrifices that require a *kohen* for the blood service, the *kemitzah* must be performed specifically by a *kohen*. This shared requirement of priestly performance reinforces the parallel.

### Kli Sharet Need

The requirement that the *minchah* be placed into a *kli sharet* parallels the *kabbalah* of the blood into a vessel. There is a deeper principle at work here as well. Animal sacrifices possess an intrinsic *kedushat haguf* — a sanctity of the body — that attaches to the animal from the moment of designation. The *minchah*, however, acquires this sanctity of the body only through the act of placing it into the *kli sharet*. Without the *kli sharet*, the *minchah* has not yet received the full measure of *kedushah* that enables the subsequent service to be valid. The teaching recorded in the *mishnah* — *shelo bichli sharet psulah* — that a *minchah* processed without a sacred vessel is *psulah* — reflects this principle, though Rabbi Shimon disagrees and validates it.

---

## Rav Yehuda Explains

### Kodesh Kodashim

Rav Yehuda the son of Rav Chiyya explains the reason behind Rabbi Shimon's position. The verse states: *kodesh kodashim hi kachatat v'ka'asham* — most holy it is, like the *chatat* and like the *asham*. This single verse, he argues, contains within it two distinct teachings about how the *minchah* resembles each of these offerings in turn.

### Hand Distinction

The parallel to the *chatat* emerges from the right-handed performance of the *minchah*'s service: *ba le'ovda beyad, ovda be-yamin kachatat* — when it comes to performing the service by hand, performing it with the right hand makes it analogous to the *chatat*. The parallel to the *asham* emerges from the left-handed dimension: *ovda be-smol ka'asham* — when performed with the left hand, the service resembles that of the *asham*. This yields a coherent framework: the same offering may behave like a *chatat* in one register and like an *asham* in another, depending on the specific act being analyzed.

### Dual Extraction

Rabbi Shimon extracts both of these lessons from the single verse. The verse *kodesh kodashim hi kachatat v'ka'asham* is not redundant; each element of the comparison — *kachatat*, *ka'asham* — carries its own distinct legal meaning, and Rabbi Shimon deploys both.

---

## Kemitza Intent

### Purpose Requirement

The *Gemara* now specifies the conditions under which the *minchat choteh* is rendered invalid on account of improper intent. If the *kemitzah* — the removal of the handful — was performed *shelo lishmah*, not for the purpose of the *minchat choteh*, the offering is *psulah*. The requirement of *lishmah* is not merely aspirational; a deviation in intent at this stage invalidates the service entirely.

### Specificity from 'Hi'

The source for this ruling is the word *hi* — "it is" — appearing in the verse: *kodesh kodashim hi kachatat v'ka'asham*. The word *hi* is not syntactically necessary; the verse could have read *kodesh kodashim kachatat v'ka'asham* without it. Its presence teaches that the offering must be specifically what it is — *davka* this *minchah*, for this purpose. The word *hi* introduces a requirement of particularity: the handful must be removed with the *minchah choteh*'s own identity in mind.

### Chatat Parallel

The same word *hi* appears in the verse governing the *chatat* itself (Vayikra 4:24), and the Rabbis read it there identically: the *chatat* too must be slaughtered specifically for its own purpose. The parallel structure of the two verses — each containing the word *hi* in an apparently superfluous position — allows the *Gemara* to derive the same purpose requirement for both offerings from the same linguistic device.

---

## Semichah Requirement

### Hekesh Logic

The *Gemara* then asks how the Rabbis employ the *hekesh* linking *asham* to *chatat*, given that they reject Rabbi Eliezer's use of it to extend the blood-invalidation rule to the *asham*. The answer: *mah chatat te'unah semikhah af asham ta'un semikhah* — just as the *chatat* requires *semikhah* — the owner's leaning upon the animal's head prior to slaughter — so too the *asham* requires *semikhah*. This, and not the extension of blood-disqualification, is the legal lesson the Rabbis extract from the *hekesh* between the two offerings.

## Wrong Purpose

### Yosef & R. Eliezer

The *Gemara* now returns to the *mishnah* from the beginning of the tractate (2a), where Yosef ben Choni states: *hanishchatim leshem pesach v'leshem chatat psulim* — animals slaughtered with the intent of a *pesach* or a *chatat* are *psulim*. The reasoning is apparent: the *chatat* is an atonement offering tied to a specific sin and a specific individual; intent cannot be freely redirected between atonement offerings. And the *pesach* carries its own cluster of stringencies — a fixed time, a fixed group of designated eaters, a fixed sacrificial identity — that make it uniquely resistant to substitution.

### Yochanan Expansion

Rabbi Yochanan reads Yosef ben Choni's position as equivalent to that of Rabbi Eliezer, and goes further: the agreement between them, he argues, extends beyond *chatat* and *pesach* themselves. Not only are those specific offerings *pasul* when done *shelo lishmam*, but all *korbanot* performed for the sake of a *chatat* or *pesach* are likewise *pasul*.

### Raba's Limit

Raba offers a more restrained reading. He holds that Yosef ben Choni and Rabbi Eliezer agree regarding the *pesach* — an *olah* slaughtered for the sake of the *pesach* is *pasul* — but they diverge when it comes to other offerings slaughtered for the sake of a *chatat*. For Yosef ben Choni, such an offering is *pasul*; for Rabbi Eliezer, it is *kasher*, though it does not fulfill the owner's *chiyuv*. The sacrifice itself remains valid; only its capacity to discharge the obligation is lacking. This disagreement between Raba and Rabbi Yochanan — whether they are truly in agreement or differ on the question of *bacharim leshem chatat* — is noted as a matter of dispute, with the *Tosafot* on 11b arguing that Raba is not in fact disputing Rabbi Yochanan but elaborating upon him.

## Pesach Dispute

### Baraita Setup

A *baraita* is now introduced that sets out the positions of Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua more fully. Two cases are considered together: (1) a *pesach* that passed its year — the animal designated for *pesach* grew older than one year before being brought, and is now slaughtered at the time of *pesach* with *pesach* intent; and (2) an animal that was not designated as *pesach* but is slaughtered at the time of *pesach* for the sake of *pesach*. In both cases, Rabbi Eliezer declares the offering *pasul* and Rabbi Yehoshua declares it *kasher*.

### Three Categories

Before entering the debate, it is important to note that the *Gemara* works with three distinct legal statuses, not two. An offering can be *kasher* — valid and accepted. It can be *pasul* — invalid. Or it can occupy an intermediate position: *kasher* but *lo yotzei hachiyuv* — valid as a sacrifice in that it may be eaten, but not fulfilling the owner's religious obligation. This third category is crucial to understanding the dispute. A *pesach* offering done with imperfect intent does not necessarily become forbidden; the question is whether it achieves anything beyond producing a meal.

### Yehoshua's Kal

Rabbi Yehoshua advances a *kal vachomer*. During the rest of the year, the *pesach* offering is not valid for its own sake — it cannot be a *pesach* when offered outside of its time — yet other animals offered for its sake are *kasher* (they become *shelamim*). How much more so, then, at the time of *pesach* itself — when the *pesach* is valid for its own sake — should other animals slaughtered for its sake also be *kasher*?

### Eliezer's Counter

Rabbi Eliezer refuses this *kal vachomer* and offers the reverse. During the rest of the year, the *pesach* offering is not *kasher* for its own sake, yet it is *kasher* for the sake of other offerings — meaning a *pesach*-designated animal offered outside its time becomes *shelamim* and is valid. If so, at the time of *pesach* itself — when it is *kasher* for its own sake — *kal vachomer* it should be *kasher* for the sake of other offerings as well. But this conclusion — that a *pesach* slaughtered on the fourteenth of Nisan *shelo lishmo* is *kasher* — directly contradicts established *halacha*. Since the reverse *kal vachomer* produces an absurd result, Rabbi Eliezer uses it to refute Rabbi Yehoshua's original reasoning.

### Yehoshua Responds

Rabbi Yehoshua pushes back sharply: if Rabbi Eliezer insists on this reading, then he has diminished the power of the *pesach* and artificially elevated the power of the *shelamim*. The argument Rabbi Eliezer is making would make the *pesach* — in some respects — weaker than the *shelamim*, which is a counterintuitive and legally uncomfortable result.

### One-Way Logic

Rabbi Eliezer then presents a different principle. *Matzinu motar pesach ba-shelamim v'ein motar shelamim ba-pesach* — we find that the surplus designated for a *pesach* offering may become a *shelamim*, but the surplus designated for a *shelamim* may never become a *pesach*. The *pesach* is a subcategory within the broader family of *kodashim kalim* — and like any subcategory, it can be absorbed upward into the general category (*shelamim*), but the general category cannot be pressed downward into it. If a person set aside funds for a *pesach* and had money left over, those surplus funds may be directed toward a *shelamim*; but funds designated for a *shelamim* cannot be redirected to acquire a *pesach*. From this asymmetry Rabbi Eliezer argues: *u'mah pesach shemotaro ba-shelamim shechato bizmano leshum shelamim pasul* — even the *pesach* whose surplus becomes *shelamim*, if it is slaughtered at its proper time with the intent of *shelamim*, is *pasul*. How much more so would *shelamim*, whose surplus can never become *pesach*, be *pasul* if slaughtered at the time of *pesach* for the sake of *pesach*.

### Chatat-Olah

Rabbi Yehoshua counters with a parallel case. *Matzinu motar chatat ba-olah v'ein motar olah ba-chatat* — we find that the surplus designated for a *chatat* may become an *olah*, but the surplus designated for an *olah* can never become a *chatat*. (The *olah* is completely burned; there is no surplus left to redirect, and furthermore the *chatat* is female while the *olah* is typically male — they cannot even be the same animal.) If the *chatat*, whose surplus goes to an *olah*, is nonetheless *psulah* when slaughtered for the sake of an *olah*, then by the same logic, the *olah*, whose surplus cannot come to a *chatat*, would certainly be *psulah* if slaughtered for the sake of a *chatat*. Rabbi Yehoshua is testing whether Rabbi Eliezer's framework yields consistent and defensible results when applied to other pairs of offerings.

### Time-Specific Rule

Rabbi Eliezer offers his final and decisive principle. *Lo, im amarta b'chatat sheken k'sheirah lishmah kol hashanah kulah, tomar b'pesach she'eino kasher lishmah ela bizmano* — you cannot reason from the *chatat* to the *pesach*, because the *chatat* is valid for its own sake throughout the entire year. The *pesach*, by contrast, is valid for its own sake only at its designated time. Since the *pesach* offering is *pasul* for its own sake during the rest of the year, it follows logically that other offerings done for its sake at its designated time are equally *pasul*. The *pesach*'s identity is inseparable from its time; only what belongs to that time may participate in it.

## Offering Hierarchy

### Hierarchy Rule

The *Gemara* now returns to the *mishnah* and the position of Shimon *achi* Azariah, which was cited at the opening of the tractate (2a). His rule: *shechatan l'shem gavoha meihen k'sheirim, l'shem namuch meihen psulim* — if lower-sanctity offerings are slaughtered for the sake of higher-sanctity offerings, they are *kasher*; if higher-sanctity offerings are slaughtered for the sake of lower-sanctity offerings, they are *pasul*. The hierarchy of sanctity operates as a one-way street: ascending is permitted, descending is not.

### Desecration Verse

The source for this rule is a verse in Vayikra 22:15: *v'lo yechalelu et kodshei bnei Yisrael et asher yarimu l'Hashem* — and they shall not desecrate the holy things of the children of Israel that they shall lift up (*yarimu*) for *Hashem*. The word *yarimu*, from the root of *terumah* — to lift up — is read to teach: what is desecrated (*mechullal*) is that which is brought downward (*namuch*); what is lifted upward (*gavoha*) is not subject to desecration. The verse is transmitted in two versions: Rav Ashi teaches it in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, and Rav Acha son of Rava teaches it in the name of Rabbi Yannai.

### Shmuel's Use

The same verse, however, is also the basis for a teaching of Shmuel in an entirely different context. Shmuel derives from it that one who eats *tevel* — produce from which *terumot* and *ma'asrot* have not yet been separated — is liable to the death penalty. The verse reads: *v'lo yechalelu et kodshei bnei Yisrael asher yarimu l'Hashem* — the holy things of Israel which they *will* separate for *Hashem*. Shmuel reads *yarimu* as a future tense referring to what is *destined* to be set apart; the *tevel* is already designated in principle for *terumah*, and one who eats it before that separation has been made has desecrated something consecrated. The *Gemara* notes that the verse is needed for Shmuel's derivation and asks how it can simultaneously be available for Shimon *achi* Azariah's hierarchy principle.

### Tense Lesson

The resolution lies in the verb tense. The verse uses the future form *yarimu* — "they will lift up" — when, if it were speaking only of a past act, it should have said *hurmu*, "that which has been lifted." The use of the future tense is therefore intentional and carries extra meaning. *Shema mina tartei* — we learn two things from it: one governing past consecrated items (Shimon *achi* Azariah's hierarchy rule applied to offerings already sanctified) and one governing future ones (Shmuel's ruling about *tevel*, which is destined for sanctification but not yet separated). A single verb in a single tense is thus pressed into service for two distinct *halachic* domains.

## Valid Not Accepted

### Zeira's Query

Rabbi Zeira raises a pointed question regarding Shimon *achi* Azariah's position. When Shimon holds that lower-sanctity offerings slaughtered for the sake of higher-sanctity ones are *kasher*, does he mean they are *kasher v'ein mirtzin* — valid but not accepted, meaning the owner has not fulfilled his obligation — and that Shimon is disagreeing with the Rabbis on only one matter (the *kasher* status of such an offering)? Or does Shimon hold that such offerings are both *kasher u'mirtzin* — valid and accepted — meaning they fully discharge the owner's obligation, and Shimon therefore disagrees with the Rabbis on two matters?

### Two Positions

The question opens up a three-part logical space. An offering can be *kasher* and fulfill the obligation (*kasher u'mirtzin*); it can be *kasher* but not fulfill the obligation (*kasher v'ein mirtzin*); or it can be *pasul* entirely. Whether Shimon's leniency extends to acceptance and obligation-fulfillment, or only to the technical validity of the sacrifice, determines the full scope of his disagreement with the Rabbis.

### Bechor Example

Abaye — or, as some attribute it, Rabbi Zrika — brings a *ta shema* to probe the question. The *mishnah* teaches: a *bechor*, firstborn animal, or *ma'aser beheimah*, tithe animal, that was slaughtered with the intent of *shelamim*, is *kasher*. These are lower in the holiness hierarchy than *shelamim* proper, and slaughtering them for the sake of the higher offering is permitted.

### Reverse Invalid

The same *mishnah* continues: *shelamim* slaughtered for the sake of a *bechor* or *ma'aser* are *pasul* — the higher for the lower is disqualified, consistent with Shimon's rule. The *Gemara* then asks: if we were to entertain the possibility that the lower-for-higher case yields both *kasher* and *meratzeh* — fully acceptable — then is the *bechor* the kind of offering that can achieve *ritzuy*, acceptance? The *bechor* is not an offering chosen by the owner for the purpose of pleasing *Hashem* in the sense of fulfilling an elected obligation; it is an offering mandated by the firstborn status of the animal. It therefore does not function as a vehicle of *ritzuy* in the relevant sense. The conclusion follows: the *bechor* slaughtered for the sake of *shelamim* is *kasher v'ein mirtzin* — valid but not accepted.

### Hierarchy Only

From the concluding clause, the *Gemara* infers that the opening clause must be read consistently: the *bechor* slaughtered for *shelamim* is also *kasher v'ein mirtzin*. The principle that emerges is that the hierarchy rule governs validity (*kashrus*) but does not automatically generate full acceptance (*ritzuy*). Shimon *achi* Azariah's leniency, on this reading, concerns only whether the sacrifice is *pasul* — not whether it discharges the owner's obligation.

## Order Precedence

### Shelamim Priority

The *Gemara* then raises another *mishnah*, which teaches that *shelamim* take precedence over *bechor* and *ma'aser* when a *kohen* must decide which offering to perform first. The reason: *shelamim* require additional services that *bechor* and *ma'aser* do not.

### Shelamim Services

The services unique to *shelamim* are enumerated: *matan arba'* — the four applications of blood to the corners of the altar; *semikhah* — the leaning of hands upon the offering; *nesachim* — the wine libations; and *tenufat chazeh v'shok* — the waving of the chest and thigh. Because *shelamim* require this more elaborate service, they are given priority so that the *kohen* may complete their more demanding *avodah* while fully attentive.

### Hierarchy Primary

The *Gemara* notes that the primary and authoritative statement of the hierarchy principle between higher and lower offerings appears in the earlier *mishnah* addressed in the main discussion, not in this later one. The second *mishnah*, which addresses the order of precedence among offerings of comparable status, invokes the hierarchy principle only incidentally — in the course of explaining why *shelamim* precede *bechor* in the queue. The foundational rule, however, is the one derived from Shimon *achi* Azariah and grounded in the verse of *yarimu*, and it is to that earlier *mishnah* that the primary statement properly belongs.
