# Sanhedrin 75 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yeihareg Ve'al Ya'avor

### Three Cardinal Sins

Today's daf is 75. We pick up at the very bottom of 74b, having just concluded the extended discussion of *yeihareg ve'al ya'avor* — when one is obligated to surrender one's life rather than transgress. The basic conclusion is that in private, this obligation applies only to the three cardinal sins: *avodah zarah*, *gilluy arayot*, and *shefichut damim*. In public, however, and for a particular reason — namely, when the transgression is demanded specifically as an act of undermining the faith — or during a general period of *sha'as hashmad*, the act becomes one of *kiddush Hashem*, and even a minor prohibition triggers the obligation to give one's life. There was also a nuanced discussion regarding whether the public context requires that the coercion be specifically *l'ha'avir* — intended to make one transgress — as opposed to *hana'as atzmoh*, where the persecutor acts for personal benefit, not ideological subversion. And we examined at length the question of women, *karka olam*, and whether the formulation is *yeihareg ve'al ya'avor* or *teihareg ve'al ta'avor*.

### Bnei Noach Kiddush Hashem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוּ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַבִּי אַמֵּי: בֵּן נֹחַ מְצוֶּּוה עַל קְדוּשַּׁת הַשֵּׁם, אוֹ אֵין מְצוֶּּוה עַל קְדוּשַּׁת הַשֵּׁם?
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **raised a dilemma before Rabbi Ami:** Is **a descendant of Noah,** who is commanded to refrain from idol worship, also **commanded about the sanctification of God's name, or is he not commanded about the sanctification of God's name?**

We now turn to a different angle on this topic. The Gemara records: *bei'u minei mei-Rabbi Ami* — they asked Rabbi Ami: is a *ben Noach* obligated in *kiddush Hashem*, or is he not so obligated? In other words, must a non-Jew give up his life rather than violate one of the *sheva mitzvos bnei Noach*?

### Tosafot's Question

Tosafot raises a preliminary question: why do we begin by assuming that a non-Jew is *not* obligated to die for his commandments, and then seek proof that he is? For Jews, we know from the verse *v'chai bahem* — "and he shall live by them" — that the Torah's commandments are intended to sustain life, not to require one to forfeit it. But non-Jews have no such verse. Might we not assume, even without a verse, that there is no obligation to die for one's commandments? Tosafot concludes that perhaps *v'chai bahem* is not independently necessary to establish this point; perhaps even without any verse we would have assumed that one does not surrender life for *mitzvos*. That seems to be the baseline assumption for *bnei Noach* as well — and the question is whether they are nonetheless obligated in *kiddush Hashem*.

It should be noted that the Gemara does not appear to be asking specifically about the three cardinal sins, even though *bnei Noach* are also prohibited in *avodah zarah*, *gilluy arayot*, and *shefichut damim*. Those three were previously framed not through the language of *kiddush Hashem* but through specific verses — *rotzeach* through the logic of whose blood is redder, *gilluy arayot* derived from that reasoning, and *avodah zarah* grounded in *ahavas Hashem*. The concept of *kiddush Hashem* specifically pertains to contexts of *sha'as hashmad* or being coerced to transgress *b'farhesya* with the intent to make one violate. The present question, then, seems to concern all *sheva mitzvos* — one must imagine a case, for instance, of a pagan government compelling observant non-Jews to violate their seven commandments.

## Seven Mitzvot Abizrayhu

### Abaye's Seven Mitzvot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, תָּא שְׁמַע: שֶׁבַע מִצְוֹת נִצְטַוּוּ בְּנֵי נֹחַ. וְאִם אִיתָא, תַּמְנֵי הָוְיָין! אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִינְהוּ וְכֹל אַבְזְרַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: Come** and **hear** an answer to this question from a *baraita* in which it was taught: **Descendants of Noah were commanded** to observe **seven mitzvot:** To establish courts of law, to refrain from cursing God, idol worship, adultery, bloodshed, robbery, and from eating the limb of a living animal. **And if it is so** that they are commanded about the sanctification of God's name, then **there would be eight** mitzvot in which they are commanded. **Rava said to him:** There is no proof from here, as when the *baraita* speaks of seven mitzvot it means the seven mitzvot **themselves with all their associated [*avzaraihu*]** obligations. The mitzva to sanctify God's name can be understood as a detail of the prohibition of idolatry.

The Gemara now addresses this question. Abaye says: *tashma* — come and hear a proof — *sheva mitzvos nitztavu bnei Noach*: non-Jews were commanded in seven commandments. If there were also an obligation of *kiddush Hashem*, that would make eight. The very enumeration of seven implies that *kiddush Hashem* is not an independent eighth obligation.

### Rava's Abizrayhu

Rava responds: *inhu v'chol avizrayu* — when the tradition says seven, it means the seven commandments along with all their appurtenances, the things connected to them. The word *avizrayu* — cognate to the modern Hebrew *avizarim*, meaning accessories or peripherals — denotes the ramifications and extensions of each commandment. So if there is a commandment not to steal, the question of whether one must submit to death rather than steal under threat to one's life is a dimension of the scope of that prohibition, not an independent eighth commandment. Similarly, for each of the *sheva mitzvos*, the question of whether *kiddush Hashem* applies is part of that commandment's inner structure. The fact that *kiddush Hashem* is not listed as an eighth obligation is therefore not a counter-proof.

### Naaman Introduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הָוֵי עֲלַהּ? אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: אָמְרִי בֵּי רַב, כְּתִיב: ״לַדָּבָר הַזֶּה יִסְלַח ה׳ לְעַבְדֶּךָ בְּבוֹא אֲדֹנִי בֵית רִמּוֹן לְהִשְׁתַּחֲוֹת שָׁמָּה וְהוּא נִשְׁעָן עַל יָדִי וְהִשְׁתַּחֲוֵיתִי״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר לוֹ לֵךְ לְשָׁלוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** halakhic conclusion **was** reached **about** this matter? **Rav Adda bar Ahava says** that **they say** in **the school of Rav: It is written** that Naaman, commander of the army of the king of Aram, said to the prophet Elisha: **"For this matter may the Lord pardon your servant, that when my master goes into the house of Rimmon to bow down there and he leans on my hand, and I bow myself down** in the house of Rimmon" (II Kings 5:18). That is, he was forced to bow down before an idol out of fear of his master, the king of Aram. **And it is written** in the following verse: **"And he said to him: Go in peace,"** indicating that Elisha did not criticize him for acting in this manner.

The Gemara then asks: *mai hava alah* — what was the conclusion? Adda bar Ahava said in the name of the school of Rav: it is written, *ladavar hazeh yislach Hashem l'avdecha* — "For this thing may the Lord forgive your servant." This is the statement of Naaman, who had been cured of his leprosy and now addresses Elisha. He says: when my master goes to the house of Rimon to prostrate himself, and he leans upon my arm, and I too bow down — for this act may God forgive me. And the verse continues: *vayomer lo lech l'shalom* — and Elisha said to him, "Go in peace."

## Naaman Case

### Naaman Source

Adda bar Ahava uses this episode as a source bearing on the question of *kiddush Hashem* for non-Jews. Elisha gives Naaman his blessing — apparently endorsing, or at least not prohibiting, Naaman's bowing in the house of idolatry.

### Bowing Analysis

This is striking in its own right. Naaman is not bowing as an act of worship; he does not believe in the idol, and he intends no act of devotion. He is merely physically supporting the king, who is prostrating himself. The Gemara elsewhere discusses a case of someone who bends down to pick up coins in front of an idol — that is not *avodah zarah b'shogeg*, it merely looks like it from the outside. Similarly, Naaman's bowing is not an act of worship at all; it merely has the outward appearance of one.

### Private vs Public

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אִיתָא, לָא לֵימָא לֵיהּ? הָא בְּצִנְעָה, הָא בְּפַרְהֶסְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if it is so** that a descendant of Noah is commanded about the sanctification of God's name, **he should not have said to him:** "Go in peace." The Gemara answers: **This** situation, where Elisha permitted Naaman's conduct, happened **in private.** When Naaman bowed down in the house of Rimmon, he did not do so in the presence of ten Jews. Whereas **that** question that was raised is whether or not a descendant of Noah must sanctify God's name **in public,** in the presence of ten Jews. Consequently, the question remains without a solution.

Given that Elisha simply bade him go in peace, the Gemara reasons: if there were a problem of *kiddush Hashem* for non-Jews, Elisha should have said something to him rather than giving his unqualified blessing. The Gemara, however, pushes back: perhaps the distinction is *ha b'tzina ha b'farhesya* — perhaps this took place in private, and therefore *kiddush Hashem* was not at issue; had it been public, the obligation might have applied.

### Rashi's Definition

But in what sense was this "private"? The king publicly goes to the house of idolatry — how could this be private? Rashi explains that the definition of *farhesya*, "public," in the context of *kiddush Hashem*, requires the presence of ten Jews. So if we were to say that *kiddush Hashem* applies to non-Jews, it would mean only that they may not transgress their *sheva mitzvos* in the presence of ten Jews — but transgression in front of an entirely non-Jewish public would not implicate *kiddush Hashem*. This is admittedly quite strange: a non-Jew's *kiddush Hashem* would be defined relative to a Jewish audience.

### Tosafot's Alternative

Tosafot offers a different reading. His *girsa* of the Gemara reads *v'im issa leima lei ha b'tzina ha b'farhesya* — if there were a problem of *kiddush Hashem*, why didn't Elisha say to Naaman: this is acceptable in private, but do not do it in public? The point is that since Elisha gave Naaman a blanket blessing without qualification, it seems he was declaring the matter entirely permissible in all contexts, whether private or public. According to Tosafot's reading, the Gemara's conclusion is that there is no obligation of *kiddush Hashem* for non-Jews at all.

One additional oddity bears noting. Recall that in the previous discussion, *kiddush Hashem* among Jews was defined not merely as a public transgression, but specifically as one in which the coercer acts *l'ha'avir* — to make one transgress — and not *l'hana'as atzmoh*, for personal benefit. The Naaman case is a perfect instance of *hana'as atzmoh*: there is no attempt to undermine anyone's faith; this is simply a political ritual. It is thus somewhat strange that the Gemara treats this as a potential scenario of *kiddush Hashem* at all, unless it presupposes that the question is specifically about *avodah zarah* — which, even in a diluted form, may operate by different rules.

## Lust Story

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁנָּתַן עֵינָיו בְּאִשָּׁה אַחַת, וְהֶעֱלָה לִבּוֹ טִינָא. וּבָאוּ וְשָׁאֲלוּ לָרוֹפְאִים, וְאָמְרוּ: אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה עַד שֶׁתִּבָּעֵל. אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: יָמוּת וְאַל תִּבָּעֵל לוֹ. תַּעֲמוֹד לְפָנָיו עֲרוּמָּה: יָמוּת וְאַל תַּעֲמוֹד לְפָנָיו עֲרוּמָּה. תְּסַפֵּר עִמּוֹ מֵאֲחוֹרֵי הַגָּדֵר: יָמוּת וְלֹא תְּסַפֵּר עִמּוֹ מֵאֲחוֹרֵי הַגָּדֵר.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the discussion of the obligation to allow oneself to be killed rather than engage in forbidden sexual intercourse, the Gemara notes that **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** There was **an incident involving a certain man who set his eyes upon a certain woman and passion rose in his heart,** to the point that he became deathly ill. **And they came and asked doctors** what was to be done with him. **And** the doctors **said: He will have no cure until she engages in sexual intercourse** with him. **The Sages said: Let him die, and she may not engage in sexual intercourse with him.** The doctors said: **She should** at least **stand naked before him.** The Sages said: **Let him die, and she may not stand naked before him.** The doctors suggested: The woman **should** at least **converse with him behind a fence** in a secluded area, so that he should derive a small amount of pleasure from the encounter. The Sages insisted: **Let him die, and she may not converse with him behind a fence.**

The Gemara continues with a different case related to the theme of *yeihareg ve'al ya'avor*. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: there was once a certain man who set his eyes upon a particular woman. His heart was consumed with lust — an overwhelming, uncontrollable desire. They came and consulted doctors, who concluded: *ein lo takana ad shettibael* — there is no cure for him unless he has intercourse with this woman.

### Sages' Response

The Sages ruled: let him die rather than have intercourse with her. The doctors then suggested a lesser remedy: perhaps if she were merely to stand before him naked, that would satisfy his desire. The Sages again ruled: let him die rather than have her stand before him naked. A third proposal was raised: perhaps she could speak with him from behind a fence — out of sight, but in conversation, perhaps offering him some relief. And again the Sages ruled: let him die rather than have this conversation.

### Woman's Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר אִידֵּי וְרַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי: חַד אָמַר: אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ הָיְתָה, וְחַד אָמַר: פְּנוּיָה הָיְתָה. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ הָיְתָה – שַׁפִּיר, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר פְּנוּיָה הָיְתָה – מַאי כּוּלֵּי הַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Rabbi Ya'akov bar Idi and Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani disagree about** this issue. **One** of them **says:** The woman in question **was a married woman, and** the other **one says: She was unmarried.** The Gemara tries to clarify the issue: **Granted, according to the one who says** that **she was a married woman,** the matter is **properly** understood. Since the case involved a severely prohibited forbidden relationship, the Sages did not allow any activity hinting at intimacy. **But according to the one who says** that **she was unmarried, what is** the reason for **all this** opposition? Why did the Sages say that the man must be allowed to die, rather than have the woman do as was requested?

Rav Yitzchak bar Idi and Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani debated the identity of this woman. One said she was a married woman, an *eishet ish*; the other said she was an unmarried *pnuyah*. If she was married, the ruling is understandable — but the Gemara then asks: if she was single, why were the Sages so insistent? His life, at least according to the doctors, was genuinely at stake. What justifies such stringency for a *pnuyah*?

### Pgam Mishpacha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם פְּגַם מִשְׁפָּחָה. רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא אָמַר: כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל פְּרוּצוֹת בַּעֲרָיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says:** This is **due to** the potential **family flaw,** i.e., harm to the family name, as it is not permitted to bring disgrace to the entire family in order to save the lovesick man. **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, says:** This is **so that the daughters of Israel should not be promiscuous with regard to forbidden sexual relations.** Were they to listen to the doctors' recommendations, Jewish women might lose moral restraint.

Rav Pappa answers: *mishum pgam mishpacha* — out of concern for the disgrace to the family. It is worth noting the contrast with an earlier context where the concept of *pgam* was raised: in the laws of the *rodef*, the one pursuing a woman to violate her, the *pgam* was only invoked in cases of biblically serious forbidden relations — those carrying *kares* or the death penalty. Here there is no such weight, yet we still speak of *pgam*. It is somewhat striking that the concern is framed as a *pgam* to the family, with the woman's own dignity apparently subsumed within it — though the implication may simply be that such a concern extends even to lesser cases.

### Societal Protection

Rava adds *mishum pgam mishpacha* as well, but Rav Acha the son of Rav Ika offers a different rationale: *kedei shelo yiheyu bnot Yisrael prutzos ba'arayos* — so that Jewish women should not become accustomed to licentiousness. Even if in this specific case there is no formal *halachic* prohibition at the level of biblical law, the Sages cannot countenance a social license for this kind of behavior. If it were permitted for a man to claim that his illness requires such conduct, it would open the door to the degradation of women in Jewish society.

One might ask: why not simply have the man marry her? The Gemara answers: *lo miyasva da'ateih* — that would not settle his mind or resolve his pathological state.

### Stolen Pleasures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי טַעְמָא? כִּדְרַבִּי יִצְחָק. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִיּוֹם שֶׁחָרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, נִיטְּלָה טַעַם בִּיאָה וְנִיתְּנָה לְעוֹבְרֵי עֲבֵירָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״מַיִם גְּנוּבִים יִמְתָּקוּ וְלֶחֶם סְתָרִים יִנְעָם״.
>
> **Translation:** [The Gemara continues its explanation:] **And what is the reason** for his condition? **In accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Yitzḥak. As Rabbi Yitzḥak says: Since the day the Temple was destroyed, sexual pleasure was taken away** from those who engage in permitted intercourse **and given to transgressors, as it is stated: "Stolen waters are sweet, and bread eaten in secret is pleasant"** (Proverbs 9:17). Therefore, the man could have been cured only by engaging in illicit sexual interaction.

This is explained in light of a teaching of Rabbi Yitzchak: *miyom shecharav Beit HaMikdash, nital ta'am biyah venitnah le'ovrei aveirah* — from the day the Temple was destroyed, the genuine pleasure of marital intimacy was taken away and given over exclusively to those who transgress sexually. There is a heightened gratification in forbidden acts, as the verse says: *mayim gnuvim yimtaku v'lechem setarim yinam* — "Stolen waters are sweet and bread eaten in secret is pleasant" (Mishlei 9:17). Bread, in many contexts, serves as a metaphor for sexual intimacy.

### Analysis

This *sugya* raises a number of important questions. On one level, the Sages are insisting on the prohibition even at the cost of a man's life — and yet the Gemara makes clear that at least when she is a *pnuyah*, this is not because of the technical laws of *yeihareg ve'al ya'avor*. The second answer — Rav Acha's societal concern — is explicitly framed as a rabbinic and societal protective measure, not a formal *halachic* ruling about *yeihareg ve'al ya'avor*. The first answer, regarding *pgam mishpacha*, occupies an intermediate position.

When the woman is a married *eishet ish*, however, some *rishonim* derive from this case the principle that *yeihareg ve'al ya'avor* for the three cardinal sins extends not only to the act itself but to *avizraihu* — its appurtenances. Even though no actual act of adultery is being discussed — merely standing naked before him or conversing from behind a fence — because these are connected to and arouse the forbidden relationship, they too fall under the obligation of *yeihareg ve'al ya'avor*. This principle may also illuminate the earlier discussion of Naaman: perhaps even *maris ayin* of *avodah zarah*, something that merely resembles idolatrous prostration, also triggers *yeihareg ve'al ya'avor*. By extension, one might ask whether *yichud* — forbidden seclusion — falls under this rubric; *yichud* is not even *maris ayin* but rather something that may *lead to* the forbidden act, and the questions expand considerably: what if the connected prohibition is *de'oraisa* but not the grave sin itself? What if it is merely *derabbanan*? These are precisely the questions that flow from this case, and the matter generates serious *halachic* debate, even in modern contexts — the question of whether mixed swimming falls under *avizraihu* of *gilluy arayot* has been posed in earnest.

There is also an additional dimension to the case: the man's desire originates from within himself. No one is holding a weapon to his head — the illness is self-generated through his own lust. This raises the question of whether the calculus is genuinely the same as a case of external coercion. Furthermore, the Sages' firmness reflects an awareness that tolerating this behavior would encourage others to claim similar exemptions for similar desires, which would undermine the very fabric of social and moral restraint.

## Ben Sorer Conclusion

### Reflection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַרַן עֲלָךְ בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה.
>
> **Translation:** 

This *sugya* leads naturally to a reflection on where we are in the chapter. The entire *perek* began with the concept of *ben sorer u'moreh* — the rebellious son — which is itself framed around the principle of *al shem sofo*, punishing based on where a trajectory leads. There is a thematic resonance in ending the chapter with a case about protecting society from licentiousness: in both instances, the Torah and the Sages are not responding solely to the immediate act but to what that act portends for the individual and for the community. Extra precautions are warranted not because this single act is necessarily catastrophic in isolation, but because of what it opens up. Just as *ben sorer u'moreh* is about preemptive societal protection, so too is the ruling here — particularly in Rav Acha's formulation — about maintaining boundaries that prevent society from unraveling.

### Hadran

*Hadran al ben sorer u'moreh.* We conclude the chapter.

## Nisrafin

### Order Review

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְאֵלּוּ הֵן הַנִּשְׂרָפִין: הַבָּא עַל אִשָּׁה וּבִתָּהּ, וּבַת כֹּהֵן שֶׁזִּנְּתָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And these are** the transgressors **who are burned** in the implementation of the court-imposed death penalty: **One who engaged in intercourse with a woman and her daughter, and** one who is **the daughter of a priest** and **who committed adultery.**

We now move on to the next chapter. The preceding material dealt with those executed by *sekilah* — stoning — of which *ben sorer u'moreh* was one, and that framework gave rise to the extended discussions of *yeihareg ve'al ya'avor*, *ba bamachteres*, and related cases. From *sekilah* we proceed to *sereifah* — burning — the next category in the order of the four death penalties, an order familiar from the *vidui* of Yom Kippur: *sekilah, sereifah, hereg, chenek*.

### Mishnah List

The Mishnah states: *ve'eilu hanisrafin* — and these are the ones who are executed by burning. The primary case is *haba al isha u'vittah* — one who has intercourse with a woman and her daughter. As we will see in the Gemara, the explicitly stated case in the Torah is a man who has intercourse with his mother-in-law — which is itself an instance of a woman and her daughter (the wife and her mother). The additional case is *bat kohen shezinisah* — a *kohen*'s daughter who commits fornication, and there is a well-known debate about exactly which case of the *bat kohen* is meant.

## Isha U'Bita Categories

### Case List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יֵשׁ בִּכְלַל אִשָּׁה וּבִתָּהּ: בִּתּוֹ, וּבַת בִּתּוֹ, וּבַת בְּנוֹ, וּבַת אִשְׁתּוֹ, וּבַת בִּתָּהּ, וּבַת בְּנָהּ, חֲמוֹתוֹ, וְאֵם חֲמוֹתוֹ, וְאֵם חָמִיו.
>
> **Translation:** Included **in the category of** the prohibition of engaging in intercourse with **a woman and her daughter** and the resulting execution by burning, **there are: His daughter, and the daughter of his daughter, and the daughter of his son.** Likewise, the following are also included in this category: Intercourse with **the daughter of his wife,** even though she is not his daughter, **and the daughter of her daughter, and the daughter of her son,** as well as intercourse with **his mother-in-law, and the mother of his mother-in-law, and the mother of his father-in-law.** The prohibition and punishment apply both in cases where a man marries a woman and then engages in intercourse with her daughter, and in cases where a man marries a woman and then engages in intercourse with her mother.

The Mishnah enumerates the cases included within the category of *isha u'bita*: his own daughter (*bitto*), his daughter's daughter and son's daughter (*vat bitto u'vat bino*) — that is, his granddaughter, even from a relationship that was not with his wife. Also included is his wife's daughter (*vat ishto*), even if she is not his biological daughter — his stepdaughter — and his step-granddaughter. And then: *chamoto* — his mother-in-law, the case explicit in the Torah as carrying *sereifah*, *em chamoto* — his grandmother-in-law on the maternal side, and *em chamiv* — his grandmother-in-law on the paternal side.

### Biblical Sources

We now examine the relevant verses. The first is Vayikra 18:17: *ervat ishah u'vittah lo tegaleh, et bat binah v'et bat bittah lo tikkah l'galot ervatah, she'erah henmah zimah hi* — "You shall not uncover the nakedness of a woman and her daughter; you shall not take her son's daughter or her daughter's daughter to uncover her nakedness, they are her kindred — it is *zimah*." This verse establishes the *issur* — the prohibition — regarding the wife's daughters and granddaughters, and it contains the critical word *zimah*.

The second verse is Vayikra 20:14: *v'ish asher yikkah et ishah v'et immah zimah hi, ba'esh yisrefu oto v'etahen* — "And if a man takes a woman and her mother, it is *zimah*; they shall be burned in fire, both he and they." Here we have both the word *zimah* and the explicit punishment of *sereifah*.

The structure of the *derashah* becomes clear: the prohibition is stated in one verse, the punishment in another, and they must be linked. The verse in chapter 18 gives us the *issur* for the wife's daughter and granddaughter; the verse in chapter 20 gives us the *onesh* — burning — for the case of woman and her mother. We must now determine how to extend *sereifah* to all the cases listed in the Mishnah, and how to ensure that each case has both a stated prohibition and an established punishment.

A third verse, Vayikra 18:10, addresses a man's own granddaughters: *ervat bat binkha o vat bittkha lo tegaleh ervatan ki ervatkha henmah* — "The nakedness of your son's daughter or your daughter's daughter — do not uncover their nakedness, for they are your own nakedness." Notably, this verse mentions granddaughters but skips over the daughter herself; we will later derive the prohibition regarding one's own daughter through a *kal va-homer*. The word *hena* — "they" — in this verse will also prove exegetically significant.

The general task before us: the only case that explicitly receives *sereifah* in the Torah is the mother-in-law. The other forbidden relationships are explicitly stated as prohibitions, but their punishment must be derived. We will need to connect the prohibition of 18:17 with the punishment of 20:14 through the shared word *zimah*, and then extend the *sereifah* upward one additional generation to reach the grandmother-in-law.

## Mishnah Formulation

### Mishnah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הַבָּא עַל אִשָּׁה שֶׁנָּשָׂא בִּתָּהּ – לָא קָתָנֵי, אֶלָּא הַבָּא עַל אִשָּׁה וּבִתָּהּ. מִכְּלָל דְּתַרְוַיְיהוּ לְאִיסּוּרָא, וּמַאן נִינְהוּ? חֲמוֹתוֹ וְאֵם חֲמוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The *tanna* **does not teach** the case of **one who engaged in intercourse with a woman whose daughter he** previously **married. Rather,** the *tanna* teaches the case of **one who engaged in intercourse with a woman and her daughter. By inference,** one may conclude **that both** the woman and her daughter are mentioned in the mishna **for** the purpose of establishing that there is **a prohibition** of intercourse with either of them, and when he engages in intercourse with the first of them he is liable to be executed. **And who are these** women? The reference is to **his mother-in-law and the mother of his mother-in-law.**

The Gemara now examines the Mishnah's language. The Mishnah opens with the case of *haba al isha u'vittah* — one who comes upon a woman and her daughter. But the primary case explicit in the Torah is a man who has relations with his mother-in-law — that is, *haba al isha she'nasa bittah*, the woman whose daughter he has married. The Gemara notes that the Mishnah does not say this; it says simply "a woman and her daughter," without specifying that this is his wife's mother. What, then, is the Mishnah's primary case?

### Concrete vs Abstract

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָתָנֵי: ״יֵשׁ בִּכְלַל אִשָּׁה וּבִתָּהּ״, מִכְּלָל דְּתַרְוַיְיהוּ כְּתִיבִי בְּהֶדְיָא, וְהָנָךְ מִדְּרָשָׁא אָתְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the *tanna* **teaches:** Additional women are included **in the category of** the prohibition of and the punishment for engaging in intercourse with **a woman and her daughter. By inference,** one may conclude **that** with regard to **both** his mother-in-law and the mother of his mother-in-law, the prohibition and punishment **are written explicitly** in the Torah, **and** with regard to **those** additional women enumerated in the mishna, the prohibition and punishment **are derived by** means of **interpretation.**

One might read "a woman and her daughter" as an abstraction: any woman and her daughter, and then the Mishnah proceeds to list specific instantiations. But the Gemara resists this reading. It insists on reading the Mishnah as beginning with a concrete case found in the Torah. Since the Mishnah describes a woman and her daughter without specifying that one of them is married to the man, the most natural concrete reading that fits the Torah's explicit case of *sereifah* is: the grandmother-in-law (*em chamoto*) is the "woman," and the mother-in-law (*chamoto*) is the "daughter." The man is not married to either of them — he is married to the daughter of the mother-in-law, i.e., his wife — and both the grandmother-in-law and mother-in-law are therefore described abstractly as "a woman and her daughter." The Gemara thus reads the Mishnah's opening not as a broad abstract category but as pointing to two specific women mentioned explicitly in the verses as carrying *sereifah*: *miklal d'trivaihu ksivi b'hedya* — it implies that both are written explicitly in the Torah. Everything else in the Mishnah is derived through *drasha*.

### Abaye Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְאַבָּיֵי, דְּאָמַר: מַשְׁמָעוּת דּוֹרְשִׁין אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. מַתְנִיתִין מַנִּי? רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **This works out well according to Abaye, who says** that with regard to the dispute between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva cited later in the Gemara (76b), the difference **between their** opinions is only concerning **the interpretation of the meaning** of the verse, but there is no practical difference between their opinions. According to Abaye, **whose** opinion is expressed in **the mishna? It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva.** Abaye explains that Rabbi Akiva maintains that the prohibition of engaging in intercourse with the mother of one's mother-in-law is stated explicitly in the Torah.

This reading follows Abaye, who holds that the Mishnah's primary case is grandmother-in-law — *masnisin mane Rabbi Akiva* — and according to Abaye's reading of that debate, Rabbi Akiva maintains that the grandmother-in-law is implicit in the *psukim* as well. The end of the Mishnah, which lists grandmother-in-law, would then according to Abaye be adding only *em chamiv* — the grandmother-in-law on the father's side — since the grandmother-in-law on the mother's side is, in his view, already explicit.

### Rava Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרָבָא דְּאָמַר: חֲמוֹתוֹ לְאַחַר מִיתָה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ, מַתְנִיתִין מַנִּי? אָמַר לָךְ רָבָא: תְּנִי ״הַבָּא עַל אִשָּׁה שֶׁנָּשָׂא בִּתָּהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **But according to Rava, who says** that the difference **between their** opinions is with regard to the prohibition of engaging in intercourse with one's **mother-in-law after** the **death** of his wife, **whose** opinion is expressed in **the mishna?** The opinion of the *tanna* of the mishna corresponds neither to the opinion of Rabbi Akiva nor to the opinion of Rabbi Yishmael, as they both maintain that the prohibition of engaging in intercourse with the mother of one's mother-in-law is not stated explicitly in the Torah. The Gemara answers: **Rava** could **say to you:** Emend the mishna and **teach: One who engaged in intercourse with a woman whose daughter he** previously **married.**

Rava, however, offers a simpler reading: *tanya haba al isha she'nasa bittah* — simply read the Mishnah as saying "one who comes upon a woman whose daughter he has married." The opening of the Mishnah refers directly to the concrete biblical case of the mother-in-law. There is no need to interpret it as grandmother-in-law and mother-in-law simultaneously. According to Rava, the debate he identifies elsewhere concerns a different question — whether the mother-in-law remains forbidden after the wife dies — and no one maintains that grandmother-in-law appears explicitly in the *psukim*. The end of the Mishnah is therefore doing the full work of extending the category to include grandmother-in-law on both sides, and when it also mentions mother-in-law again, this is because it is enumerating all the derived cases comprehensively.

## Zimah Gezeirah Shava

### Baraita Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יִקַּח אֶת אִשָּׁה וְאֶת אִמָּהּ״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אִשָּׁה וְאִמָּהּ. בַּת אִשָּׁה, וּבַת בִּתָּהּ, וּבַת בְּנָהּ מִנַּיִן?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna enumerates several women with whom intercourse is forbidden who are included in the prohibition of engaging in intercourse with a woman and her daughter, which is punished by execution by burning. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? They are derived **as the Sages taught:** "And **if a man takes a woman and her mother,** it is lewdness; they shall be burned with fire both him and them, and there shall be no lewdness among you" (Leviticus 20:14). **I have** derived **only** that this punishment applies to one who engages in intercourse with **a woman and** with **her mother. From where** is it derived that one who engages in intercourse with **the daughter of** the **woman** married to him, **or** with **the daughter of her daughter, or** with **the daughter of her son,** is also liable to be executed by burning?

The Gemara now turns to the derivation of *sereifah* for the other cases. A *baraita* is cited: *v'ish asher yikkah et ishah v'et immah* — a man who takes a woman and her mother. The *baraita* asks: I know only that this specific case — the mother-in-law, the woman and her mother — carries *sereifah*. How do I know that the wife's daughter, her daughter's daughter, and her son's daughter also receive *sereifah*?

### Zimah Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״זִמָּה״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״זִמָּה״. מָה לְהַלָּן – בִּתָּהּ וּבַת בִּתָּהּ וּבַת בְּנָהּ, אַף כָּאן – בִּתָּהּ וּבַת בִּתָּהּ וּבַת בְּנָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Lewdness is stated here,** with regard to the punishment: "There shall be no lewdness among you" (Leviticus 20:14), **and lewdness is stated there,** with regard to the prohibition: "You shall not uncover the nakedness of a woman and her daughter; you shall not take her son's daughter, or her daughter's daughter, to uncover her nakedness; they are near kinswomen, it is lewdness" (Leviticus 18:17). It is derived by means of a verbal analogy that **just as there** the prohibition applies to the woman's **daughter, and the daughter of her daughter, and the daughter of her son, so too here,** the punishment of burning applies to one who engages in intercourse with the woman's **daughter, and** to **the daughter of her daughter, and** to **the daughter of her son.**

The *baraita* answers: *ne'emar kan zimah v'ne'emar le-halan zimah* — the word *zimah* is said here (in 20:14, the verse establishing *sereifah*) and the word *zimah* is said there (in 18:17, the verse prohibiting the wife's daughters and granddaughters). The *gezeirah shava* links the two: just as "there" — in 18:17 — the prohibition extends to the daughter and granddaughter, so too "here" — in 20:14 — the punishment of *sereifah* extends to the daughter and granddaughter. Thus the *zimah*-*zimah* link allows us to carry the punishment of burning from the mother-in-law case to all the cases governed by 18:17.

The *baraita* continues: *minayin la'asot zecharim ki-nekevot* — how do we know to make males like females? And: *minayin la'asot lemata ke'lema'ala* — how do we know to make below like above? These formulations are, for now, cryptic. The rest of the *amud* is devoted to unpacking them.

### Application Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנַּיִן לַעֲשׂוֹת זְכָרִים כִּנְקֵבוֹת? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״זִמָּה״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״זִמָּה״. מָה לְהַלָּן זְכָרִים כִּנְקֵבוֹת, אַף כָּאן זְכָרִים כִּנְקֵבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **From where** is it derived **to render** the status of **males like** that of **females** with regard to this punishment? **Lewdness is stated here and lewdness is stated there. Just as there,** the Torah renders the status of **males like** that of **female** relatives, **so too here,** the Torah renders the status of **males like** that of **females.**

But first, a difficulty. The *baraita* says the *zimah*-*zimah* connection extends *sereifah* to the daughters and granddaughters. The Gemara asks: the cases of a man's own daughters and granddaughters — which are listed in the Mishnah — do not contain the word *zimah* in their verse (18:10). So how can we apply the *gezeirah shava* of *zimah*-*zimah* to extend *sereifah* to them?

## Hena Hena Solution

### Three Generations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָכִי קָאָמַר, מִנַּיִן לַעֲשׂוֹת שְׁאֵר הַבָּא מִמֶּנּוּ כִּשְׁאֵר הַבָּא מִמֶּנָּה? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״זִמָּה״ וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״זִמָּה״ וְכוּ׳. וְהָא בִּשְׁאֵר דִּידֵיהּ לָא כְּתִיבָא בֵּיהּ ״זִמָּה״!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: This** is what the *baraita* **is saying. From where** is it derived **to render a relative who comes from his** family, e.g., the daughter of his daughter and the daughter of his son, **like a relative who comes from her** family, e.g., the daughter of his wife and the daughter of his wife's daughter? **Lewdness is stated here and lewdness is stated there.** Just as there, the Torah renders the status of male relatives like that of female relatives, so too here, the Torah renders the status of males like that of females. The Gemara asks: **But isn't** it so that **with regard to a relative from his** family the term **lewdness is not written,** as the verse states: "The nakedness of your son's daughter, or of your daughter's daughter, their nakedness you shall not expose; for theirs is your own nakedness" (Leviticus 18:10)?

Abaye offers his interpretation of the *baraita*. First he addresses *zecharim ki-nekevot* — "males like females." This does not mean granddaughter through a son versus granddaughter through a daughter (which are both explicit in the same verse). Rather, it means: descendants of the wife (*she'er haba mimena*) — her daughters and granddaughters — are already covered by the *zimah* verses. How do we know that descendants of the husband (*she'er haba mimenu*) — his own daughters and granddaughters from other relationships — also receive *sereifah*? That is the question of *zecharim ki-nekevot*: making the male line like the female line.

### Hena Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אֲמַר לִי רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אֲבוּדִימִי, אָתְיָא ״הֵנָּה״ ״הֵנָּה״, אָתְיָא ״זִמָּה״ ״זִמָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: Rav Yitzḥak bar Avudimi said to me:** The equation of his relative to her relative is **derived** by means of a verbal analogy between the word **"*henna*"** written with regard to his relative: "The nakedness of your son's daughter, or of your daughter's daughter, their nakedness you shall not expose; for theirs [*henna*] is your own nakedness" (Leviticus 18:10), and the word **"*henna*"** written with regard to her relative: "You shall not uncover the nakedness of a woman and her daughter; you shall not take her son's daughter, or her daughter's daughter, to uncover her nakedness; they [*henna*] are near kinswomen; it is lewdness" (Leviticus 18:17). Additionally, it is **derived** from a verbal analogy between the word **"lewdness"** (Leviticus 18:17) and the word **"lewdness"** in the verse: "And if a man takes a woman and her mother, it is lewdness; they shall be burned with fire both him and them, and there shall be no lewdness among you" (Leviticus 20:14).

The *baraita* answers that this too is derived from *zimah*-*zimah*. But the Gemara objects: *uv'hai she'er didei lo ktiv bei zimah* — the verse governing a man's own daughters and granddaughters (18:10) does not contain the word *zimah*. So the *zimah*-*zimah* connection cannot directly apply. Rava then says in the name of Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi: *atya hena hena, atya zimah zimah* — there is a dual link. The verse 18:17 contains both *she'erah hena* — "they are her kindred" — and *zimah hi*. And the verse 18:10, governing a man's own descendants, contains *ki ervatkha hena* — "for they are your own nakedness." The shared keyword *hena* links 18:10 to 18:17, and the *zimah* in 18:17 then links to the *zimah* of 20:14 which carries *sereifah*. So the *hena*-*hena* connection bridges the prohibition of 18:10 into the *zimah* network, and from there *sereifah* applies.

### Zimah Teaches Both

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: מִנַּיִן לַעֲשׂוֹת לְמַטָּה כִּלְמַעְלָה. מַאי ״לְמַטָּה כִּלְמַעְלָה״? אִילֵּימָא בַּת בְּנָהּ וּבַת בִּתָּהּ כְּבִתָּהּ, בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי קָאָתְיָאן!
>
> **Translation:** **The Master says: From where** is it derived **to render** the status of a relative **below like** the status of a relative **above?** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of: The status of a relative **below like** the status of a relative **above? If we say** this means that the status of second-generation relative, i.e., **the daughter of the son of** his wife **and the daughter of her daughter** is **like** the status of first-generation relative, i.e., **her daughter,** this is difficult, as **they are derived together,** as all of them are mentioned in the same verse (see Leviticus 18:17).

The *baraita*'s second cryptic phrase is *minayin la'asot lemata ke'lema'ala* — how do we know to make below like above, or above like below? Abaye now explains this as follows: *minayin la'asot shalosh dorot lema'ala kishlosh dorot lemata* — how do we know to go three generations upward as we go three generations downward? (The count includes the wife as the first generation.) The verse governing the wife's daughters and granddaughters (18:17) goes two generations down from the wife. By parallel, the law of the mother-in-law, which is one generation up from the wife, should extend two generations upward — reaching the grandmother-in-law. The *zimah*-*zimah* link enables this: the same connection that carries *sereifah* downward to two generations of descendants carries the principle upward to two generations of forebears. And when it says *ne'emar lemata zimah v'ne'emar lema'ala zimah, mah lemata shalosh dorot af lema'ala shalosh dorot* — the *zimah* below (the descendants) goes three generations, so too the *zimah* above (the forebears) goes three generations.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: מִנַּיִן לַעֲשׂוֹת לְמַטָּה כִּלְמַעְלָה. מַאי ״לְמַטָּה כִּלְמַעְלָה״? אִילֵּימָא בַּת בְּנָהּ וּבַת בִּתָּהּ כְּבִתָּהּ, בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי קָאָתְיָאן!
>
> **Translation:** **The Master says: From where** is it derived **to render** the status of a relative **below like** the status of a relative **above?** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of: The status of a relative **below like** the status of a relative **above? If we say** this means that the status of second-generation relative, i.e., **the daughter of the son of** his wife **and the daughter of her daughter** is **like** the status of first-generation relative, i.e., **her daughter,** this is difficult, as **they are derived together,** as all of them are mentioned in the same verse (see Leviticus 18:17).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אֵם חָמִיו וְאֵם חֲמוֹתוֹ כַּחֲמוֹתוֹ. הַאי ״לְמַטָּה כִּלְמַעְלָה״? ״לְמַעְלָה כִּלְמַטָּה״ הוּא! תְּנִי: ״לְמַעְלָה כִּלְמַטָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it means that the status of the **mother of his father-in-law and the mother of his mother-in-law is like** that of **his mother-in-law.** The Gemara challenges: If so, is **this** an example of: A relative **below is like** the status of a relative **above?** On the contrary, **it is** an example of: A relative **above is like** the status of a relative **below.** The Gemara responds: Emend the *baraita* and **teach:** The status of a relative from the generation **above is like** the status of a relative from the generation **below** them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״זִמָּה״ וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״זִמָּה״, וּמָה הַשְׁתָּא: אִינְהִי לָא כְּתִיבָא, זִמָּה דִּידְהוּ כְּתִיבָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** as for the continuation of the *baraita*: **Lewdness is stated here,** with regard to the prohibition, **and lewdness is stated there,** with regard to the punishment, **now, if** the mother of his father-in-law and the mother of his mother-in-law **are not written** in the Torah and are derived through interpretation, is the term **lewdness written concerning them?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָכִי קָאָמַר, מִנַּיִן לַעֲשׂוֹת שְׁלֹשָׁה דּוֹרוֹת לְמַעְלָה כִּשְׁלֹשָׁה דּוֹרוֹת לְמַטָּה? נֶאֱמַר לְמַטָּה ״זִמָּה״ וְנֶאֱמַר לְמַעְלָה ״זִמָּה״. מָה לְמַטָּה שְׁלֹשָׁה דּוֹרוֹת, אַף לְמַעְלָה שְׁלֹשָׁה דּוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: This** is what the *baraita* **is saying: From where** is it derived **to render** the status of a relative for **three generations above,** his wife, his mother-in-law, and the mother of his mother-in-law, **like** the status of a relative for **three generations below,** his wife, his daughter, and the daughter of his daughter? **Lewdness is stated** with regard to relatives **below,** the offspring of his wife (Leviticus 18:17), **and lewdness is stated** with regard to relatives **above,** the ancestors of his wife (Leviticus 20:14). **Just as** with regard to relatives **below,** one is liable for engaging in intercourse with **three generations** of women, **so too** with regard to relatives **above,** one is liable for engaging in intercourse with **three generations** of women.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה בָּעוֹנֶשׁ עָשָׂה לְמַטָּה כִּלְמַעְלָה, אַף בָּאַזְהָרָה נָמֵי עָשָׂה לְמַעְלָה כִּלְמַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And just as with regard to punishment,** the verse **rendered** the status of relatives **below like** that of relatives **above, so too, with regard to** the **prohibition,** the verse **rendered** the status of relatives **above like** that of relatives **below.**

What remains is to establish not merely the punishment but the prohibition for the grandmother-in-law, since *ein onshin ela im ken mazhirin* — we do not punish without a preceding warning, i.e., an explicit prohibition. The *baraita*'s final phrase — *u-mah ba-ones asar lemata ke'lema'ala, af be'azharah nami asar lema'ala ke'lemata* — addresses this: just as in terms of punishment, the law below is like the law above, so too in terms of the prohibitive warning, the above is like the below. The *zimah*-*zimah* *gezeirah shava* thus teaches both the prohibition and the punishment for the grandmother-in-law.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם כִּדְקָתָנֵי, וּמַאי ״לְמַטָּה״? לְמַטָּה בְּאִיסּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says: Actually,** it is not necessary to emend the *baraita*, and the *baraita* may be interpreted **as it is taught. And what is** the meaning of the term **below?** The reference is to **below** in terms **of** the **prohibition,** i.e., below refers to more distant relatives, e.g., the mother of his mother-in-law, with regard to whom the prohibition is less severe, and above refers to closer relatives, e.g., his mother-in-law, with regard to whom the prohibition is more severe.

Rav Ashi offers an alternative reading of the phrase *lemata ke'lema'ala*, one that does not reverse the terms. He understands *lemata* as denoting the lesser-severity relationship (the more distant relative, the grandmother-in-law) and *lema'ala* as denoting the heavier relationship (the closer relative, the mother-in-law). The point remains the same — how do we know that the grandmother-in-law is forbidden and receives *sereifah* just like the mother-in-law? — but the terminology is applied differently. The underlying conclusion, however, is identical regardless of which reading one adopts.

## Man's Grandmother

### Analogy Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי: מָה הִיא – אֵם אִמָּהּ אֲסוּרָה, אַף הוּא – אֵם אִמּוֹ אֲסוּרָה?
>
> **Translation:** Based on that interpretation of the *baraita*, the Gemara asks: **If** so, why not say: **Just as the mother of the mother of** his wife **is forbidden** to him, **so too the mother of his mother is forbidden** to him, and say that he would be liable to be executed by burning?

Having established that the wife's grandmother is forbidden biblically and carries *sereifah*, the Gemara poses a parallel question: since we have drawn an analogy between the wife's lineage (downward to her descendants, upward to her ancestors) and the husband's lineage (his own daughters and granddaughters), should we not also apply the analogy upward for the husband? That is: just as the wife's grandmother is forbidden and carries *sereifah*, should we not say that the husband's own grandmother (*em imo*) is similarly forbidden to him? The Gemara notes that in fact a man's own biological grandmother is only forbidden to him *rabbinically*, not biblically — which is ironic and counterintuitive given that his *wife's* grandmother is a biblical prohibition.

### Abaye Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אָמַר קְרָא ״אִמְּךָ הִיא״, מִשּׁוּם אִמּוֹ אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ, וְאִי אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ מִשּׁוּם אֵם אִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says** that it is not possible that one is liable to be burned for engaging in intercourse with the mother of his mother, as **the verse states:** "The nakedness of your father and the nakedness of your mother you shall not expose; **she is your mother,** you shall not expose her nakedness" (Leviticus 18:7). Infer from this: **Due to** his intercourse with **his mother you deem him liable** to be executed, **but you do not deem him liable** to be executed **due to** his intercourse with **the mother of his mother.**

Abaye answers from the verse itself. The Torah says *imecha hi, lo tegaleh ervatah* — "she is your mother; you shall not uncover her nakedness." The emphasis on *imecha hi* — "she is your mother" — signals that the prohibition stops at the mother. One is liable for his mother but not for his mother's mother. The verse itself draws the line.

### Rava Methodologies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: בֵּין לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״דּוּן מִינַּהּ וּמִינַּהּ״, וּבֵין לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״דּוּן מִינַּהּ וְאוֹקֵי בְאַתְרַהּ״ – לָא אָתְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: Both according to the one who says** with regard to the method of derivation by means of a verbal analogy: **Infer** the *halakha* **from it,** i.e., from the source of the verbal analogy, **and** derive the details **from it,** i.e., from that source, **and according to the one who says: Infer** the *halakha* **from it but interpret** the *halakha* according **to its** own **place,** i.e., the derived *halakha* is subject to the principles that govern that which is derived by means of the verbal analogy, it **is not derived** that one is liable to be burned for engaging in intercourse with the mother of his mother.

Rava offers a different approach, independent of the limiting verse. He says: regardless of whether you employ the methodology of *don minah u'minah* or *don minah v'oki b'atrah*, you cannot successfully learn that a man's own grandmother should be biblically forbidden by analogy to his wife's grandmother.

These are two fundamental methodologies in *talmudic* legal derivation. When we use an analogy to borrow a *halacha* from category B and apply it to category A, do we import that *halacha* together with its full original context from B — in which case we apply both the rule and its attendant parameters as they exist in B? That is *don minah u'minah* — learn from it and from it. Alternatively, do we import only the principle itself and then allow it to be governed by the normal rules of category A? That is *don minah v'oki b'atrah* — learn from it but establish it in its new location according to local norms.

### Punishment Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: דּוּן מִינַּהּ וּמִינַּהּ, מָה הִיא אֵם אִמָּהּ אֲסוּרָה – אַף הוּא נָמֵי אֵם אִמּוֹ אֲסוּרָה. וּמִינַּהּ: מָה הִיא בִּשְׂרֵיפָה – אַף הוּא נָמֵי בִּשְׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **According to the one who says: Infer** the *halakha* **from it and** derive the details **from it,** one might infer: **Just as** with regard to relatives of his wife, **the mother of her mother is forbidden** to him, **so too** with regard to **his** relatives, **the mother of his mother is forbidden** to him. **And** derive again **from it: Just as** with regard to relatives of his wife, he is liable to be executed **by burning** due to his intercourse with the mother of her mother, **so too** with regard to **his** relatives, he is **also** liable to be executed **by burning** due to his intercourse with the mother of his mother.

According to *don minah u'minah*: we would learn that just as the wife's grandmother is forbidden, the man's own grandmother is forbidden. But we would also have to import the full context — meaning, the grandmother carries *sereifah*, just as the wife's grandmother does. This creates an absurdity: a man's own *mother* is punished by *sekilah* (stoning), while by this analogy his *grandmother* would be punished by *sereifah* (burning). If *sereifah* is the weightier punishment, we cannot even begin the analogy, because the wife's grandmother being subject to the more severe punishment demonstrates that her category is inherently more serious — which means we cannot automatically transfer the prohibition to the husband's grandmother. Moreover, the logical contradiction is glaring: how can a man's grandmother carry a more severe punishment than his own mother?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: שְׂרֵיפָה חֲמוּרָה, אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ. מָה לְהִיא – שֶׁכֵּן אִמָּהּ בִּשְׂרֵפָה, תֹּאמַר בְּהוּא – שֶׁאִמּוֹ בִּסְקִילָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to explain why one cannot derive that one is liable to be burned for engaging in intercourse with the mother of his mother. **According to the one who says:** Execution by **burning is** more **severe** than execution by stoning, the inference from the *halakha* of relatives of his wife **can be refuted** as follows: **What** is notable **about her** relatives, where he is liable to be burned for engaging in intercourse with the mother of her mother? They are notable **in that** one who engages in intercourse with **the mother of** his wife is liable to be executed **by** the more severe punishment of **burning,** not the less severe, more common, punishment of stoning. **Will you** then **say** that the same *halakha* should apply **with regard to his** relatives, **where** one who engages in intercourse with **his mother** is liable to be executed **by** the less severe punishment of **stoning?**

Furthermore — and this is a fatal consistency problem — by the wife, the daughter and granddaughter, and correspondingly the mother and grandmother, all carry the *same* punishment of *sereifah*. There is no distinction between generations on her side. But on the husband's side, if we were to apply this analogy, we would have the mother receiving *sekilah* and the grandmother receiving *sereifah*. The analogy breaks down because it does not preserve the internal consistency that makes the original case coherent.

### Consistency Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד: אִמּוֹ בִּסְקִילָה, אֵם אִמּוֹ בִּשְׂרֵיפָה?
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** as one who engages in intercourse with **his mother** is executed **by** the less severe punishment of **stoning,** is it possible that one who engages in intercourse with **the mother of his mother,** a more distant relative, is executed **by** the more severe punishment of **burning?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד: מָה הִיא – לֹא חִלַּקְתָּ בָּהּ בֵּין אִמָּהּ לְאֵם אִמָּהּ, אַף הוּא – נָמֵי לָא תַּחְלוֹק בּוֹ בֵּין אִמּוֹ לְאֵם אִמּוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, just as** with regard to **her** relatives, **you did not distinguish between her mother and the mother of her mother,** and one who engages in intercourse with either is executed by burning, **so too** with regard to **his** relatives, **you should not distinguish between his mother and the mother of his mother.** Since one who engages in intercourse with his mother is punished by stoning, not burning, it follows that one who engages in intercourse with the mother of his mother is punished by stoning, not burning.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר סְקִילָה חֲמוּרָה, מֵהַאי קוּשְׁיָא לָא נְידוּנַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And according to the one who says** that **stoning is** more **severe** than burning, although the first two refutations of the derivation, based on the fact that execution by burning is more severe, are not relevant, **due to this** third **difficulty,** this *halakha* **is not derived.** Just as with regard to her relatives, you did not distinguish between her mother and the mother of her mother, so too with regard to his relatives, you should not distinguish between his mother and the mother of his mother.

The conclusion is therefore clear: whether one applies *don minah u'minah* or *don minah v'oki b'atrah*, the analogy fails. A man's own grandmother cannot be derived biblically from the prohibition of the wife's grandmother, and she remains only rabbinically forbidden. This stands in contrast to the wife's grandmother, who is forbidden and punishable by *sereifah* on a biblical level. The one remaining item — how a man's own *daughter* (as distinct from his granddaughter, which is stated explicitly in 18:10) is derived — will be addressed subsequently.
