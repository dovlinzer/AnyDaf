# Sanhedrin 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Witness vs Judge Disqual…

### Rav Dimi Intro

The daf opens at the bottom of 23b, with the *gemara* presenting two distinct traditions — brought by *tanna'im* who traveled from Eretz Yisrael to Bavel — regarding Rabbi Yochanan's interpretation of the *mishna*'s ruling that *zeh posel dayano shel zeh ve-zeh posel dayano shel zeh*, "this one disqualifies the other's judge and the other disqualifies his judge." Yesterday's discussion established the interpretation of Rav Dimi, read through the Rambam's *pshat*: the *mishna* is addressing a case where a litigant brings two sets of witnesses. Since he has done so, it is apparent that he himself knows at least one set is *pasul*, and therefore if the opposing litigant challenges one of the witnesses as invalid, the burden of proof falls on the one who brought four witnesses to demonstrate that all of them are *kosher*.

### Ravin Migo Logic

A second tradition arrives with Ravin, who likewise cites Rabbi Yochanan: *ki ata Ravin amar Rabbi Yochanan: reisha be-edim pesulin ve-dayanim kesherin, migo de-paslei edim paslei nami dayane* — the *reisha* of the *mishna*, "this one disqualifies the other's judge," is a case where the witnesses are *pasul* and the judges are *kosher*, and yet, because (*migo*) we disqualify the witnesses, we disqualify the judges as well. Conversely, the *seifa* — *migo de-paslei dayane paslei nami edim* — addresses a case where the judges are *pasul* and the witnesses are *kosher*, and since we disqualify the judges, we disqualify the witnesses as well, because the entire proceeding has been rendered *pasul* and must begin from scratch.

This requires explanation, because at first glance the logic seems strange: what does the *pasul* status of witnesses have to do with the judges, who perform an entirely different function? Rashi addresses this under the heading of Ravin: the underlying principle is *migo be-alma*, here meaning not the familiar *migo* of monetary law (where we believe a claim because a stronger claim was available), but simply "since" or "because." The mechanism, as Rashi explains in the *seifa* of the *dibbur hamatchil*, is that once the challenging litigant has proven the truth of one of his claims — say, that the *edim* are *pasul* — we extend credibility to his remaining claim, namely that the *dayanim* are likewise *pasul*. The principle is that *she-nimtzu devarav emet*, since his words have proven true about one matter, we believe him regarding the other. This is the position of Rabbi Meir. If, conversely, the opposing party brings proof that the *dayanim* or *edim* in question are in fact *kosher*, that proof might suffice to rebut the claim even according to Rabbi Meir — but the *gemara* does not press that point.

### Rava Challenge

Rava challenges the *migo* logic: *bishlama migo de-paslei edim paslei nami dayane* — it is understandable that disqualifying the witnesses would also disqualify the judges, because *ika bei dina achrina*, one can always find other judges. The case is analogous to a mistrial in secular law: the proceeding is voided and begins anew, perhaps with a different panel, as the judges are not the evidentiary foundation of the case. But *ela migo de-paslei dayane paslei nami edim, ve-ha edim tafi leika* — how can we say that because the judges are *pasul*, the witnesses are also *pasul*, when there may simply be no other witnesses available? Everything rests on the witnesses; they are the *ikar* of the case. If the witnesses are disqualified solely by virtue of this *migo*, the claimant may be left with no legal recourse whatsoever.

The *gemara* resolves this: *lo tzricha, d'ika kat acher* — this ruling applies only where there is another set of witnesses available. The disqualification of the witnesses via the *migo* of the judges' *pasul* status is only operative when replacement witnesses exist. The question is then asked: *ha leika kat acher, mai?* — what is the law if no other set of witnesses exists? The answer: *hachi nami d'lo matzi paslei* — in that case as well, even if one of the judges is proven *pasul*, the witnesses are not automatically disqualified.

### Dimi vs Ravin

The *gemara* then observes: *hainu d'Rav Dimi* — this is essentially the same position as Rav Dimi, who held that Rabbi Meir's ruling (that a litigant can disqualify the other's witnesses or judges) only applies in a case involving two sets of witnesses. The *gemara* concludes: *ika beiniyahu migo* — the practical difference between Rav Dimi and Ravin lies precisely in whether we invoke this *migo* principle. Rav Dimi holds *lo amrinan migo* in this context; Ravin, citing Rabbi Yochanan, holds *amrinan migo*. According to Ravin, the *migo* is operative and therefore one litigant's proof of *pasul* status regarding one element of the proceeding extends credibility to his claim about the other element as well.

## Reish Lakish Solo Witness

### Reish Lakish Intro

The *gemara* returns (*gufa*) to a statement cited earlier. One interpretive approach to the *mishna* renders it as *zeh posel edo shel zeh ve-zeh posel edo shel zeh* — "this one disqualifies the other's witness," referring to a single witness. In other words, if one litigant brought only one witness, the opposing litigant, even after having accepted that witness as valid, may still declare him *pasul*. This reading was attributed to Reish Lakish.

### Reish Lakish Challenge

The *gemara* challenges this attribution: *ini?* — is this really what Reish Lakish said? The challenge arrives via a statement of Ulla: *ha-ro'eh et Reish Lakish be-veit ha-midrash ke-ilu oker harim ve-tochenan zeh ba-zeh* — one who observed Reish Lakish in the *beit midrash* was as though watching someone uproot mountains and grind them against one another. Rashi explains this as meaning that Reish Lakish rendered everything coherent and straightforward — *matiach u-makish zeh ba-zeh*, he would strike his arguments together with such force and clarity that nothing remained unclear. Rashi adds that Reish Lakish was known to be exceedingly *charif*, sharp in his analysis, yet it was said of him *derech anavah*, out of humility, that he praised Rabbi Meir with the phrase *peh kadosh*, "a holy mouth." The *gemara*'s *ini* is therefore not about the substance of *tani edo* but about the phrase *peh kadosh yomar davar zeh* — the surprise is that one as formidable as Reish Lakish would speak with such deferential praise of another. The transition into the aggadic passage that follows emerges from this observation.

## Eretz Yisrael vs Bav (II)

### Mutual Respect Comparison

Ravina resolves the rhetorical surprise of Reish Lakish's humble praise by reframing the *gemara*'s *ini* entirely: the point is not that Reish Lakish said something surprising, but that both Reish Lakish and Rabbi Meir — about whom Ulla said the same thing, *ke-ilu oker harei harim ve-tochenan zeh ba-zeh* — praised one another so extravagantly. *Hachi ka'amar*: come and see, *bo u-re'eh kama mechabevin zeh et zeh*, how much the scholars of Eretz Yisrael love and respect one another — unlike the scholars of Bavel, who are *charif* and harsh with one another.

### Rabbi Yossi Humble Defer…

The *gemara* illustrates *mechabevin zeh et zeh* with a concrete case. There was a time when Rabbi — referring to Rebbe, the *Nasi* — declared that it is *asur* to *lehatmin*, to insulate, cold food before *Shabbat*. Rashi explains the reasoning: insulating cold food might lead to insulating hot food, which is *asur de-rabbanan*. Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yossi then said to him: my father, Rabbi Yossi, *hitir lehatmin et ha-tzonen* — explicitly permitted the insulation of cold food. Upon hearing this, Rebbe immediately retracted: *kvar hora zaken* — "a *zaken* has already ruled on this matter," and he accepted Rabbi Yossi's position. This is the model of *mechabevin zeh et zeh*: even though Rebbe held his own ruling, out of respect for Rabbi Yossi and love for his fellow scholars, he deferred.

Rav Pappa adds a further observation: *bo u-re'eh kama mechabevin zeh et zeh* — and here is additional proof of how they respected one another. *De-ilu Rabbi Yossi kayam, haya kafuf ve-yoshev lifnei Rabbi* — had Rabbi Yossi still been alive, he himself would have sat before Rebbe in a posture of deference, bent and subordinate, *de-ha Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yossi memale makom avotav* — as is demonstrated by the fact that Rabbi Yishmael, who filled his father's place (*chashuv ke-aviv*, as Rashi says, he was regarded as important as his father), himself sat humbly before Rebbe. Yet here is what is striking: according to one reading, Rabbi Yossi was in fact a greater Torah scholar than Rebbe, and yet he would have deferred to Rebbe because Rebbe held the position of *Nasi*. The deference was not a function of inferior learning but of respect for institutional position. And still, when Rabbi Yishmael conveyed his father's ruling, Rebbe accepted it without contest — *kvar hora ha-zaken*.

### Noa'im vs Chovelim

Rav Oshaya opens a *midrashic* reading of a verse from Zechariah: *va-ekach li et shenei maklot, karati le-no'am u-le-echad karati le-chovelim* — "I took for myself two staffs; one I called *no'am* and one I called *chovelim*." *No'am*, pleasantness: *elu talmidei chachamim she-be-Eretz Yisrael, she-man'imin zeh la-zeh ba-halacha* — these are the Torah scholars of Eretz Yisrael, who are pleasant to one another in *halacha*. *Chovelim*, destructive or harsh: *elu talmidei chachamim she-be-Vavel, she-mechavelim zeh la-zeh ba-halacha* — these are the Torah scholars of Bavel, who are harsh with one another in *halacha*. Rashi notes that this harshness — *de-kamnatzchi zeh la-zeh be-divrei baraita u-shemu'ata* — because they compete and contend with one another over *baraitot* and *shemu'ot*, they actually lose something in their learning. The productive tension of genuine dialogue is replaced by combative disputation, and Torah is thereby diminished.

A second verse from Zechariah is then read: *u-shnei zeitim aleha* — the two olive trees alongside the menorah — interpreted by Rabbi Yitzchak as *elu talmidei chachamim she-be-Eretz Yisrael, she-nochin zeh la-zeh ba-halacha ke-shemen zayit* — the scholars of Eretz Yisrael are gentle with one another like olive oil. By contrast, *elu talmidei chachamim she-be-Vavel she-meririn zeh la-zeh* — the scholars of Bavel are bitter to one another, like a raw olive eaten directly from the tree, whose bitterness clings in the mouth for many minutes.

A third passage from Zechariah describes two women carrying a tub (*eifah*) between sky and earth, with stork's wings and wind within them. The destination of this tub is *Eretz Shinar*. Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai interprets: *zo chanufah ve-gasut ruach she-yardu le-Vavel* — this is flattery and arrogance that descended to Bavel. The *gemara* immediately challenges: there is a tradition that *asarah kabin gasut yardu la-olam, tishah natlah Elam ve-achat kol ha-olam kulo* — ten measures of arrogance descended to the world, nine were taken by Elam (Persia) and one by the rest of the world combined. This seems to contradict the claim that *gasut ruach* went to Bavel. The *gemara* resolves: *ve-ishtarbovei de-ishtarbav le-Elam* — they became confused in transit and ended up in Elam. The proof is in the verse itself: *livnot lah bayit be-Eretz Shinar*, the *heh* in *lah* lacks a *mapik heh*, hinting that the destination was not firmly established — they were headed for *Shinar* but ended up in Elam. A further tradition is cited: *siman le-gasut ha-ruach, aniyut* — the marker of arrogance is poverty — and *aniyut le-Vavel nachit*, poverty did descend to Bavel. The *gemara* resolves: *mai aniyut? aniyut Torah* — this is poverty of Torah, as the verse says *achot lanu ktana ve-shadayim ein lah* — "we have a little sister and she has no breasts," referring, as Rashi indicates, to Daniel in Elam: *she-zachata lilmod ve-lo zachata lelamed* — she merited to learn but not to teach, meaning Daniel received Torah but had no disciples to whom he could transmit it.

## Eretz Yisrael vs Bav (II)

### Bavel Belula Meaning

The *gemara* asks: *mai Bavel?* — what is the meaning of the word *Bavel* itself? Rabbi Yochanan answers: *belula be-Mikra, belula be-Mishna, belula be-Talmud* — "mixed together" with Scripture, *Mishna*, and *Talmud*. The very name *Bavel* contains a pun on *belula*, meaning mixed or combined, and this is now offered as a compliment: the *Talmud Bavli* integrates all three sources of Torah — *pesukim*, *Mishna*, and *Talmud* — simultaneously. Rabbeinu Tam, cited in *Tosafot*, develops this further: the Sages taught that one should divide one's learning time three ways — one third *Mikra*, one third *Mishna*, one third *Talmud*. Because the *Talmud Bavli* incorporates all three, one who learns it is *yotzei* all three obligations without needing to divide one's time separately.

Against this stands Rabbi Yirmiya's reading of *ba-machshakim hoshivani ke-metei olam* — "He made me sit in darkness like the eternal dead" — which he applies to the *Talmud* of Bavel: *she-ein nochin zeh im zeh ve-talmuda safek be-yadam* — because the scholars are harsh with one another and in constant contradiction, their Torah is consequently diminished, sitting in darkness.

## Creditor Accepts Invalid

### Mishna Setup

The *gemara* now transitions to the next *mishna*, which had already been cited in the previous discussion. The *mishna* teaches: if one litigant says to the other, *ne'eman alai avikha, ne'emanin alai shlosha ro'ei bakar* — "your father is trusted by me," or "these three cowherd are trusted by me" — to serve as witnesses or judges even though *mi-ikar ha-din* they are *pasul*, Rabbi Meir says *yachol lachzor bo*, he may retract his acceptance, while the Sages say *eino yachol lachzor bo*, he may not retract. A second clause teaches: if one litigant is required to take a *shevua* and the other says *dor li be-chayei roshecha* — "swear to me on your life" — rather than the more severe *shevua* invoking the name of God, Rabbi Meir says he may retract and the Sages say he may not.

### Shmuel Creditor Debtor

The *gemara* opens with a statement of Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel: *machloket be-machul lach, aval be-eten lach divrei hakol yachol lachzor bo* — the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Sages concerns *machul lach*, the case where the creditor says "I forgive your debt" conditional on the *pasul* witnesses testifying. But in the case of *eten lach* — where the debtor says "I will pay you" upon the testimony of the *pasul* witnesses — everyone agrees he can retract, because *mi-de'oraita*, the rule of *ha-motzi me-chaveiro alav ha-ra'aya* means the burden of proof rests with the one extracting the money. Since the *pasul* witnesses cannot truly discharge that burden *mi-de'oraita*, the debtor's acceptance is not binding and all agree he may go back.

### Rabbi Yochanan Both Cases

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *be-eten lach machloket* — the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Sages applies specifically to the case of *eten lach*, where the debtor says "I will pay you" upon the testimony of the *pasul* witnesses. It is that acceptance — of the debtor himself — that is contested.

### Question Both or One

This raises a further question: according to Rabbi Yochanan, who places the *machloket* in the case of *eten lach*, what is the rule regarding *machul lach*? Is there also a *machloket* there, or does everyone agree that the creditor's acceptance is binding and he cannot retract? Or perhaps, as the other possibility holds, there is a *machloket* in both cases — both *eten lach* and *machul lach*.

## Rava Three Positions

### Rava Oath Case

Rava teaches: *machloket be-eten lach, aval be-machul lach divrei hakol ein yachol lachzor bo* — the *machloket* exists in the case of *eten lach*, but in the case of *machul lach* everyone agrees that one cannot retract. If one accepts this as Rabbi Yochanan's position — that the *machloket* is in *eten lach* alone, while *machul lach* is universally binding — then Rava's ruling aligns with Rabbi Yochanan. But if one says the *machloket* applies in both cases, then *Rava de-amar ke-man?* — whose opinion does Rava follow? In that case, *Rava ta'ama de-nafshei ka'amar* — Rava is expressing his own independent position, yielding a three-way dispute: some hold the *machloket* applies only to *machul lach* (Shmuel); some hold it applies only to *eten lach* (Rabbi Yochanan); and some hold it applies to both.

### Rav Acha Objection

Rav Acha bar Tachlifa raises a challenge to Rava from the second clause of the *mishna*: if one is obligated to take a *shevua* and the other says *dor li be-chayei roshecha*, Rabbi Meir says he may retract and the Sages say he cannot. Rava had said the *machloket* is in *eten lach* but not in *machul lach*. The question: does not this case of the *shevua* involve *machul lach* — the creditor effectively saying "take this lighter oath and I release you" — which would mean there *is* a *machloket* by *machul lach*, contradicting Rava?

### Nishba'in Cases

The *gemara* resolves by distinguishing two types of oath-takers. There are those who are *nishba'in ve-lo meshalmin* — they take an oath and are thereby exempted from payment. In that case, the creditor is saying: swear on your life and I will forgive the debt — which is structurally *machul lach*. But Rava's ruling in the *shevua* case concerns *nishba'in ve-notlin* — those who take an oath and then collect, such as a paid guardian (*shomer sachar*) from whom the object was stolen, who swears that it was stolen and thereby collects. The debtor in such a case is saying: I accept this lighter oath, and *eten lach* — I will pay you — which is structurally *eten lach*. Therefore the *shevua* case poses no contradiction to Rava.

### Both Clauses Needed

The *gemara* then addresses a potential redundancy: if the *seifa* of the *mishna* (the *shevua* clause) is also about *eten lach*, why do we need it when the *reisha* already established the *machloket* in *eten lach*? The answer is that *tanna tole be-da'at acherim* and *tanna tole be-da'at atzmo* — we need both because they differ in a critical way. The *reisha* (*avikha*, *avicha*, the cowherd witnesses) involves an acceptance that depends on others' intentions — the litigant is accepting witnesses or judges who are not himself, and Rabbi Meir might say he can retract because *mi yeimar de-mazkei lei*, perhaps he assumed they would testify in his favor and was not truly *gomer* in his commitment. But in the *seifa*, the case is *tole be-da'at atzmo* — the debtor himself decides to swear on his own life, which is his own *da'at*, and one might think he is fully committed and even Rabbi Meir would agree he cannot retract. Conversely, if we only had the *seifa*, we might think the Sages agree he can retract in the *reisha* because the acceptance was based on others' intentions and therefore was never fully binding. Both clauses are therefore necessary, *tzericha*.

## Before vs After Judgment

### Reish Lakish Position

A parallel structural debate now emerges, this time concerning the temporal dimension of the *machloket*. Reish Lakish says: *machloket lifnei gmar din, aval le-achar gmar din divrei hakol ein yachol lachzor bo* — the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Sages concerns what happens *before* the judgment is finalized. Once *gmar din* has occurred, everyone agrees that neither party can retract their acceptance of the *pasul* witnesses or judges. Concretely: if Reuven brings his father as a witness, Shimon accepts him, the father testifies, and Shimon loses the case, according to Reish Lakish Shimon can no longer invoke Rabbi Meir's position to challenge the father's testimony — the judgment is final.

### Rabbi Yochanan Dispute A…

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *le-achar gmar din machloket* — even after the judgment has been rendered, the *machloket* between Rabbi Meir and the Sages remains live. That is, Rabbi Meir would hold that even after the judgment is finalized, a party could still challenge the *pasul* witnesses or judges he had previously accepted.

### Rava Temporal Proof

The *gemara* raises a question about Rabbi Yochanan's position: *le-achar gmar din machloket, aval lifnei gmar din divrei hakol yachol lachzor bo?* — does Rabbi Yochanan mean the *machloket* applies only after *gmar din*, while before *gmar din* everyone agrees one can retract? Or perhaps there is a *machloket* both before and after?

Rava provides an answer: *kibel alav karov o pasul lifnei gmar din yachol lachzor bo, le-achar gmar din ein yachol lachzor bo* — if someone accepted a relative (*karov*) or otherwise *pasul* witness before the conclusion of the judgment, he may retract; after *gmar din*, he cannot.

### Question Temporal Scope

This teaching of Rava now becomes a proof. The *gemara* reasons: if one takes the position that the *machloket* is only after *gmar din*, while before *gmar din* everyone agrees one can retract — then Rava's ruling reflects Rabbi Yochanan's position, specifically the opinion of the Sages (*aliba de-rabbanan*). Before *gmar din*, even the Sages agree one can retract (*yachol lachzor bo*); after *gmar din*, the Sages hold one cannot (*ein yachol lachzor bo*), and it is only there that Rabbi Meir would disagree. Rava's ruling — before *gmar din* one can retract, after *gmar din* one cannot — fits precisely with Rabbi Yochanan as understood according to the Sages.

But if one says the *machloket* exists both before and after *gmar din*, then *Rava de-amar ke-man?* — Rava's ruling cannot be attributed to any consistent position, since he holds one can retract before *gmar din* but not after, which would mean he follows the Sages on one side and no one in particular on the other.

### Conclusion After Judgment

The *gemara* concludes: *ela lav, shema mina le-achar gmar din machloket* — we must therefore conclude, according to Rabbi Yochanan (as demonstrated by Rava), that the *machloket* between Rabbi Meir and the Sages applies only after *gmar din*. Before *gmar din*, the rule is *divrei hakol yachol lachzor bo* — all agree that one may retract his acceptance of a *pasul* witness or judge. *Shema mina*.

## Geonic Rulings Kinyan

### Nachman Correspondence

The *gemara* records a correspondence: Rav Nachman bar Rav Chisda sent a question to Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak: *yelamdenu rabbeinu* — teach us, our master — *lifnei gmar din machloket o le-achar gmar din machloket?* Is the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Sages located before *gmar din*, as Reish Lakish holds, or after *gmar din*, as Rabbi Yochanan holds? And what is the practical *halakha*?

### Halakha Chachamim

The reply: *le-achar gmar din machloket*, the *machloket* is after *gmar din* — following Rabbi Yochanan. *Ve-halakha ke-divrei chachamim*: the *halakha* follows the Sages, which means that after *gmar din* one may not retract the acceptance of a *pasul* witness or judge.

### Rav Ashi Question

Rav Ashi reports that the question sent was actually framed differently: *be-eten lach machloket o be-machul lach machloket?* — is the *machloket* in the case of *eten lach* (the debtor accepting the *pasul* testimony as grounds for payment) or in the case of *machul lach* (the creditor accepting it as grounds for forgiveness)? And who is the *halakha* like?

### Rav Ashi Answer

The reply: *be-eten lach machloket* — the *machloket* is in the case of *eten lach*, following Rabbi Yochanan. *Ve-halakha ke-divrei chachamim* — the *halakha* follows the Sages, meaning that once the debtor has said *eten lach* and the case has proceeded, he cannot retract.

### Kinyan Exception Final

The *gemara* then notes a distinction between the academies of Sura and Pumbedita. In Sura they taught as above. In Pumbedita, Rav Chanina bar Shalmaya relates that a question was sent from the *bei rav* to Shmuel: *yelamdenu rabbeinu, lifnei gmar din ve-kanu mi-yado, mai?* — if all of this occurred before *gmar din* but an act of *kinyan* had been performed — what is the *halakha*? The reply: *ein le-achar kinyan klum* — after a *kinyan* has taken place, nothing can be undone. Once a *kinyan* is performed, even Rabbi Meir agrees with the Sages that the acceptance is fully binding and there is no further possibility of retraction. The reasoning becomes clear: *kinyan* constitutes *gmar da'at* — a decisive, finalized act of will — and once the debtor has both declared his acceptance and performed an act of acquisition, his commitment is unambiguous and irrevocable.
