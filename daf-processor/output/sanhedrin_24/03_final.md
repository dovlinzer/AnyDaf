# Sanhedrin 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Witness vs Judge Disqual…

### Rav Dimi Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב דִּימִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף: כְּגוֹן דְּקַבְּלֵיהּ עֲלֵיהּ בְּחַד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Dimi, son of Rav Naḥman, son of Rav Yosef, says:** The case of a litigant who accepts his father or the father of the other litigant as a judge is referring to **where** the litigant **accepted** this relative **upon himself as one** of the judges in a court of three, where the other two judges are fit. It is not referring to where he accepted him as the sole judge.

The daf opens at the bottom of 23b, with the *gemara* presenting two distinct traditions — brought by *tanna'im* who traveled from Eretz Yisrael to Bavel — regarding Rabbi Yochanan's interpretation of the *mishna*'s ruling that *zeh posel dayano shel zeh ve-zeh posel dayano shel zeh*, "this one disqualifies the other's judge and the other disqualifies his judge." Yesterday's discussion established the interpretation of Rav Dimi, read through the Rambam's *pshat*: the *mishna* is addressing a case where a litigant brings two sets of witnesses. Since he has done so, it is apparent that he himself knows at least one set is *pasul*, and therefore if the opposing litigant challenges one of the witnesses as invalid, the burden of proof falls on the one who brought four witnesses to demonstrate that all of them are *kosher*.

### Ravin Migo Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רֵישָׁא בְּעֵדִים פְּסוּלִין וְדַיָּינִין כְּשֵׁרִין, מִיגּוֹ דְּפָסְלִי עֵידֵי – פָּסְלִי נָמֵי דַּיָּינֵי. סֵיפָא: בְּדַיָּינִין פְּסוּלִין וְעֵדִים כְּשֵׁרִין, דְּמִיגּוֹ דְּפָסְלִי דַּיָּינֵי – פָּסְלִי נָמֵי עֵידֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **When Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** a different interpretation of the dispute: **The former clause,** is stated **with regard to** a case of **disqualified witnesses and fit judges,** i.e., the litigant claims that both the witnesses and the judges are disqualified and proves his claim only with regard to the witnesses. Rabbi Meir holds that **since the witnesses are disqualified the judges are also disqualified,** as the litigant's entire claim is deemed credible. **The latter clause,** where Rabbi Meir rules that a litigant can disqualify witnesses, is stated **with regard to** a case of **disqualified judges and fit witnesses,** i.e., the litigant proves his claim only with regard to the judges. **Since the judges are disqualified the witnesses were also disqualified.**

A second tradition arrives with Ravin, who likewise cites Rabbi Yochanan: *ki ata Ravin amar Rabbi Yochanan: reisha be-edim pesulin ve-dayanim kesherin, migo de-paslei edim paslei nami dayane* — the *reisha* of the *mishna*, "this one disqualifies the other's judge," is a case where the witnesses are *pasul* and the judges are *kosher*, and yet, because (*migo*) we disqualify the witnesses, we disqualify the judges as well. Conversely, the *seifa* — *migo de-paslei dayane paslei nami edim* — addresses a case where the judges are *pasul* and the witnesses are *kosher*, and since we disqualify the judges, we disqualify the witnesses as well, because the entire proceeding has been rendered *pasul* and must begin from scratch.

This requires explanation, because at first glance the logic seems strange: what does the *pasul* status of witnesses have to do with the judges, who perform an entirely different function? Rashi addresses this under the heading of Ravin: the underlying principle is *migo be-alma*, here meaning not the familiar *migo* of monetary law (where we believe a claim because a stronger claim was available), but simply "since" or "because." The mechanism, as Rashi explains in the *seifa* of the *dibbur hamatchil*, is that once the challenging litigant has proven the truth of one of his claims — say, that the *edim* are *pasul* — we extend credibility to his remaining claim, namely that the *dayanim* are likewise *pasul*. The principle is that *she-nimtzu devarav emet*, since his words have proven true about one matter, we believe him regarding the other. This is the position of Rabbi Meir. If, conversely, the opposing party brings proof that the *dayanim* or *edim* in question are in fact *kosher*, that proof might suffice to rebut the claim even according to Rabbi Meir — but the *gemara* does not press that point.

### Rava Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבָא: בִּשְׁלָמָא, מִיגּוֹ דְּפָסְלִי עֵדֵי פָּסְלִי נָמֵי דַּיָּינֵי, אִיכָּא בֵּי דִינָא אַחֲרִינָא. אֶלָּא מִיגּוֹ דְּפָסְלִי דַּיָּינֵי פָּסְלִי נָמֵי עֵדֵי? וְהָא עֵדִים תּוּ לֵיכָּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava objects to this** interpretation: **Granted,** in the former clause, it is reasonable that **since the witnesses are disqualified the judges are also disqualified,** as, since **there is** the option of going to **another court,** disqualifying these specific judges has no irreversible effect on the outcome of the case. **But** in the latter case, how can Rabbi Meir hold that **since the judges are disqualified, the witnesses are also disqualified** without proof? This disqualification nullifies the entire case, as **there are no more witnesses.**

Rava challenges the *migo* logic: *bishlama migo de-paslei edim paslei nami dayane* — it is understandable that disqualifying the witnesses would also disqualify the judges, because *ika bei dina achrina*, one can always find other judges. The case is analogous to a mistrial in secular law: the proceeding is voided and begins anew, perhaps with a different panel, as the judges are not the evidentiary foundation of the case. But *ela migo de-paslei dayane paslei nami edim, ve-ha edim tafi leika* — how can we say that because the judges are *pasul*, the witnesses are also *pasul*, when there may simply be no other witnesses available? Everything rests on the witnesses; they are the *ikar* of the case. If the witnesses are disqualified solely by virtue of this *migo*, the claimant may be left with no legal recourse whatsoever.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאִיכָּא כַּת אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** the mishna is not referring to a case where there are no other witnesses. The dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis is **necessary** only in a case **where there is another set** of witnesses, which the litigant did not disqualify. Since disqualifying this set will not predetermine the outcome, the litigant's claim that these witnesses are disqualified is accepted.

The *gemara* resolves this: *lo tzricha, d'ika kat acher* — this ruling applies only where there is another set of witnesses available. The disqualification of the witnesses via the *migo* of the judges' *pasul* status is only operative when replacement witnesses exist. The question is then asked: *ha leika kat acher, mai?* — what is the law if no other set of witnesses exists? The answer: *hachi nami d'lo matzi paslei* — in that case as well, even if one of the judges is proven *pasul*, the witnesses are not automatically disqualified.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לֵיכָּא כַּת אַחֶרֶת, מַאי? הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלָא מָצֵי פָּסְלִי? הַיְינוּ דְּרַב דִּימִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if **there is no other set** of witnesses, **what** is the *halakha*? Is it **indeed** true **that** the litigant **cannot disqualify** them? If so, **this is** identical to **Rav Dimi's** interpretation that the mishna is referring to a case where there are two sets of witnesses.

### Dimi vs Ravin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ מִיגּוֹ, דְּמָר סָבַר: אָמְרִינַן מִיגּוֹ, וּמָר סָבַר: לָא אָמְרִינַן מִיגּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There is** a practical difference **between them** with regard to the principle that **since [*miggo*]** one of the litigant's claims is found to be correct, it can be assumed that other claims of his are correct as well. **As** one **Sage,** Ravin, **holds** that according to Rabbi Meir, **we say *miggo*,** i.e., this principle should be followed, **and** one **Sage,** Rav Dimi, **holds** that **we do not say *miggo*,** but rather the litigant is required to prove every claim he makes.

The *gemara* then observes: *hainu d'Rav Dimi* — this is essentially the same position as Rav Dimi, who held that Rabbi Meir's ruling (that a litigant can disqualify the other's witnesses or judges) only applies in a case involving two sets of witnesses. The *gemara* concludes: *ika beiniyahu migo* — the practical difference between Rav Dimi and Ravin lies precisely in whether we invoke this *migo* principle. Rav Dimi holds *lo amrinan migo* in this context; Ravin, citing Rabbi Yochanan, holds *amrinan migo*. According to Ravin, the *migo* is operative and therefore one litigant's proof of *pasul* status regarding one element of the proceeding extends credibility to his claim about the other element as well.

## Reish Lakish Solo Witness

### Reish Lakish Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: פֶּה קָדוֹשׁ יֹאמַר דָּבָר זֶה? תְּנִי: ״עֵדוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to discuss **the** matter **itself: Reish Lakish says: Would a holy mouth,** i.e., that of Rabbi Meir, **say this** strange **statement,** that a litigant can prevent a witness from testifying against him? Rather, emend the text of the mishna and **teach: His witness,** in the singular, meaning that a litigant can disqualify only a witness who testifies alone.

The *gemara* returns (*gufa*) to a statement cited earlier. One interpretive approach to the *mishna* renders it as *zeh posel edo shel zeh ve-zeh posel edo shel zeh* — "this one disqualifies the other's witness," referring to a single witness. In other words, if one litigant brought only one witness, the opposing litigant, even after having accepted that witness as valid, may still declare him *pasul*. This reading was attributed to Reish Lakish.

### Reish Lakish Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָאָמַר עוּלָּא: הָרוֹאֶה אֶת רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ בְּבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ, כְּאִילּוּ עוֹקֵר הָרִים וְטוֹחֲנָן זֶה בָּזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so?** Was it in character for Reish Lakish to speak of Rabbi Meir with such reverence when disagreeing with his ruling? **But doesn't Ulla say:** When **one sees Reish Lakish** studying Torah **in the study hall** it is **as though he is uprooting mountains and grinding them into each other?** Reish Lakish was evidently very sharp in his analyses.

The *gemara* challenges this attribution: *ini?* — is this really what Reish Lakish said? The challenge arrives via a statement of Ulla: *ha-ro'eh et Reish Lakish be-veit ha-midrash ke-ilu oker harim ve-tochenan zeh ba-zeh* — one who observed Reish Lakish in the *beit midrash* was as though watching someone uproot mountains and grind them against one another. Rashi explains this as meaning that Reish Lakish rendered everything coherent and straightforward — *matiach u-makish zeh ba-zeh*, he would strike his arguments together with such force and clarity that nothing remained unclear. Rashi adds that Reish Lakish was known to be exceedingly *charif*, sharp in his analysis, yet it was said of him *derech anavah*, out of humility, that he praised Rabbi Meir with the phrase *peh kadosh*, "a holy mouth." The *gemara*'s *ini* is therefore not about the substance of *tani edo* but about the phrase *peh kadosh yomar davar zeh* — the surprise is that one as formidable as Reish Lakish would speak with such deferential praise of another. The transition into the aggadic passage that follows emerges from this observation.

## Eretz Yisrael vs Bav (II)

### Mutual Respect Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: וַהֲלֹא כׇּל הָרוֹאֶה רַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ, כְּאִילּוּ עוֹקֵר הָרֵי הָרִים וְטוֹחֲנָן זֶה בָּזֶה! הָכִי קָאָמַר: בָּא וּרְאֵה כַּמָּה מְחַבְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said** in response: What is the difficulty? **But is it not** so that when **anyone sees Rabbi Meir** studying Torah **in the study hall,** it is **as though he is uprooting the highest of mountains and grinding them into each other?** Rabbi Meir was a greater scholar than Reish Lakish, so it was fitting for Reish Lakish to speak of him with reverence. The Gemara answers: The question: Is that so, was not stated to raise a difficulty; rather, **this** is what he **is saying,** i.e., this is what the Gemara was noting: **Come and see how much** the Sages **love each other.** Although Reish Lakish was himself very sharp and a great Torah scholar, he spoke of Rabbi Meir with reverence.

Ravina resolves the rhetorical surprise of Reish Lakish's humble praise by reframing the *gemara*'s *ini* entirely: the point is not that Reish Lakish said something surprising, but that both Reish Lakish and Rabbi Meir — about whom Ulla said the same thing, *ke-ilu oker harei harim ve-tochenan zeh ba-zeh* — praised one another so extravagantly. *Hachi ka'amar*: come and see, *bo u-re'eh kama mechabevin zeh et zeh*, how much the scholars of Eretz Yisrael love and respect one another — unlike the scholars of Bavel, who are *charif* and harsh with one another.

### Rabbi Yossi Humble Defer…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּיָתֵיב רַבִּי וְקָאָמַר: אָסוּר לְהַטְמִין אֶת הַצּוֹנֵן. אָמַר לְפָנָיו רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אַבָּא הִתִּיר לְהַטְמִין אֶת הַצּוֹנֵן. אָמַר לָהֶם: כְּבָר הוֹרָה זָקֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another example of Torah scholars who spoke of each other with reverence. It is **like that** incident **where Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **sat and said: It is prohibited to insulate cold** food on Shabbat to keep it cold, as this may lead one to insulate hot food on Shabbat to keep it hot. **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, said before him: My father** ruled that it is **permitted to insulate cold** food on Shabbat. There is no concern that this will lead one to insulate hot food on Shabbat. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi subsequently **said to** those who asked him about this issue: I retract my previous statement; **the elder,** Rabbi Yosei, **has already issued a ruling** on this topic, and I defer to his ruling.

The *gemara* illustrates *mechabevin zeh et zeh* with a concrete case. There was a time when Rabbi — referring to Rebbe, the *Nasi* — declared that it is *asur* to *lehatmin*, to insulate, cold food before *Shabbat*. Rashi explains the reasoning: insulating cold food might lead to insulating hot food, which is *asur de-rabbanan*. Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yossi then said to him: my father, Rabbi Yossi, *hitir lehatmin et ha-tzonen* — explicitly permitted the insulation of cold food. Upon hearing this, Rebbe immediately retracted: *kvar hora zaken* — "a *zaken* has already ruled on this matter," and he accepted Rabbi Yossi's position. This is the model of *mechabevin zeh et zeh*: even though Rebbe held his own ruling, out of respect for Rabbi Yossi and love for his fellow scholars, he deferred.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בֹּא וּרְאֵה כַּמָּה מְחַבְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה, דְּאִילּוּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי קַיָּים הָיָה, כָּפוּף וְיוֹשֵׁב לִפְנֵי רַבִּי. דְּהָא רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי מְמַלֵּא מְקוֹם אֲבוֹתָיו הֲוָה, וְהָיָה כָּפוּף וְיוֹשֵׁב לִפְנֵי רַבִּי, וְקָא אָמַר: כְּבָר הוֹרָה זָקֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says: Come and see how much they loved each other. As, had Rabbi Yosei** still **been alive, he would have been subordinate** to **and sitting before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi as his student, **as Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, was his fathers' replacement,** i.e., he was as great a Torah scholar as his forebears, **and he was subordinate** to **and sitting before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi as his student. **And,** nevertheless, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said: The elder has already issued a ruling** on this topic, and he deferred to Rabbi Yosei's ruling.

Rav Pappa adds a further observation: *bo u-re'eh kama mechabevin zeh et zeh* — and here is additional proof of how they respected one another. *De-ilu Rabbi Yossi kayam, haya kafuf ve-yoshev lifnei Rabbi* — had Rabbi Yossi still been alive, he himself would have sat before Rebbe in a posture of deference, bent and subordinate, *de-ha Rabbi Yishmael bar Rabbi Yossi memale makom avotav* — as is demonstrated by the fact that Rabbi Yishmael, who filled his father's place (*chashuv ke-aviv*, as Rashi says, he was regarded as important as his father), himself sat humbly before Rebbe. Yet here is what is striking: according to one reading, Rabbi Yossi was in fact a greater Torah scholar than Rebbe, and yet he would have deferred to Rebbe because Rebbe held the position of *Nasi*. The deference was not a function of inferior learning but of respect for institutional position. And still, when Rabbi Yishmael conveyed his father's ruling, Rebbe accepted it without contest — *kvar hora ha-zaken*.

### Noa'im vs Chovelim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״וָאֶקַּח לִי אֶת שְׁנֵי מַקְלוֹת לְאַחַד קָרָאתִי נוֹעַם וּלְאַחַד קָרָאתִי חוֹבְלִים״? ״נוֹעַם״ – אֵלּוּ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁבְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁמַּנְעִימִין זֶה לָזֶה בַּהֲלָכָה. ״חוֹבְלִים״ – אֵלּוּ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁבְּבָבֶל, שֶׁמְּחַבְּלִים זֶה לָזֶה בַּהֲלָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** This demonstrates what **Rabbi Oshaya says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "And I took for myself two staves; the one I called Graciousness, and the other I called Binders"** (Zechariah 11:7)? **"Graciousness"; these are** the **Torah scholars in Eretz Yisrael, who are gracious to one another in** discussions of ***halakha*.** They treat each other with honor and love, as demonstrated in the statements of Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. **"Binders [*ḥovelim*]"; these are** the **Torah scholars in Babylonia, who injure [*shemeḥabbelim*] each other in** discussions of ***halakha*,** i.e., they speak harshly to each other when they disagree.

Rav Oshaya opens a *midrashic* reading of a verse from Zechariah: *va-ekach li et shenei maklot, karati le-no'am u-le-echad karati le-chovelim* — "I took for myself two staffs; one I called *no'am* and one I called *chovelim*." *No'am*, pleasantness: *elu talmidei chachamim she-be-Eretz Yisrael, she-man'imin zeh la-zeh ba-halacha* — these are the Torah scholars of Eretz Yisrael, who are pleasant to one another in *halacha*. *Chovelim*, destructive or harsh: *elu talmidei chachamim she-be-Vavel, she-mechavelim zeh la-zeh ba-halacha* — these are the Torah scholars of Bavel, who are harsh with one another in *halacha*. Rashi notes that this harshness — *de-kamnatzchi zeh la-zeh be-divrei baraita u-shemu'ata* — because they compete and contend with one another over *baraitot* and *shemu'ot*, they actually lose something in their learning. The productive tension of genuine dialogue is replaced by combative disputation, and Torah is thereby diminished.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלַי אֵלֶּה [שְׁנֵי] בְנֵי הַיִּצְהָר הָעֹמְדִים וְגוֹ׳ וּשְׁנַיִם זֵיתִים עָלֶיהָ״. ״יִצְהָר״ – אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: אֵלּוּ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁבְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנּוֹחִין זֶה לָזֶה בַּהֲלָכָה כְּשֶׁמֶן זַיִת. ״וּשְׁנַיִם זֵיתִים עָלֶיהָ״ – אֵלּוּ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁבְּבָבֶל, שֶׁמְּרוֹרִין זֶה לָזֶה בַּהֲלָכָה כְּזַיִת.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, it is stated: **"Then he said to me: These are the two anointed ones, that stand** by the Lord of the whole earth" (Zechariah 4:14), and it is stated: **"And two olive trees by it,** one upon the right side of the bowl, and the other upon its left side" (Zechariah 4:3). With regard to the expression **"anointed ones," Rabbi Yitzḥak says: These are** the **Torah scholars in Eretz Yisrael, who are pleasant to each other in** discussions of ***halakha* like olive oil,** which is not bitter. The verse **"and two olive trees by it"** should be interpreted as follows: **These are** the **Torah scholars in Babylonia, who are bitter to each other in** discussions of ***halakha* like an olive.**

A second verse from Zechariah is then read: *u-shnei zeitim aleha* — the two olive trees alongside the menorah — interpreted by Rabbi Yitzchak as *elu talmidei chachamim she-be-Eretz Yisrael, she-nochin zeh la-zeh ba-halacha ke-shemen zayit* — the scholars of Eretz Yisrael are gentle with one another like olive oil. By contrast, *elu talmidei chachamim she-be-Vavel she-meririn zeh la-zeh* — the scholars of Bavel are bitter to one another, like a raw olive eaten directly from the tree, whose bitterness clings in the mouth for many minutes.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וָאֶשָּׂא עֵינַי וָאֵרֶא וְהִנֵּה שְׁתַּיִם נָשִׁים יוֹצְאוֹת וְרוּחַ בְּכַנְפֵיהֶם וְלָהֵנָּה כְנָפַיִם כְּכַנְפֵי הַחֲסִידָה וַתִּשֶּׂאנָה הָאֵיפָה בֵּין הַשָּׁמַיִם וּבֵין הָאָרֶץ. וָאֹמַר אֶל הַמַּלְאָךְ הַדֹּבֵר בִּי אָנָה הֵמָּה מוֹלִכוֹת אֶת הָאֵיפָה. וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלַי לִבְנוֹת לָה בַיִת בְּאֶרֶץ שִׁנְעָר״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי: זוֹ חֲנוּפָּה וְגַסּוּת הָרוּחַ שֶׁיָּרְדוּ לְבָבֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interprets another verse in Zechariah: **"Then I lifted my eyes and saw, and behold there came forth two women, and the wind was in their wings, for they had wings like the wings of a stork. And they lifted up the measure between the earth and the heaven. Then I said to the angel that spoke with me: To where do they take the measure? And he said to me: To build her a house in the land of Shinar"** (Zechariah 5:9–11). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: This** measure refers to **flattery and arrogance that descended to Babylonia,** i.e., Shinar.

A third passage from Zechariah describes two women carrying a tub (*eifah*) between sky and earth, with stork's wings and wind within them. The destination of this tub is *Eretz Shinar*. Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai interprets: *zo chanufah ve-gasut ruach she-yardu le-Vavel* — this is flattery and arrogance that descended to Bavel. The *gemara* immediately challenges: there is a tradition that *asarah kabin gasut yardu la-olam, tishah natlah Elam ve-achat kol ha-olam kulo* — ten measures of arrogance descended to the world, nine were taken by Elam (Persia) and one by the rest of the world combined. This seems to contradict the claim that *gasut ruach* went to Bavel. The *gemara* resolves: *ve-ishtarbovei de-ishtarbav le-Elam* — they became confused in transit and ended up in Elam. The proof is in the verse itself: *livnot lah bayit be-Eretz Shinar*, the *heh* in *lah* lacks a *mapik heh*, hinting that the destination was not firmly established — they were headed for *Shinar* but ended up in Elam. A further tradition is cited: *siman le-gasut ha-ruach, aniyut* — the marker of arrogance is poverty — and *aniyut le-Vavel nachit*, poverty did descend to Bavel. The *gemara* resolves: *mai aniyut? aniyut Torah* — this is poverty of Torah, as the verse says *achot lanu ktana ve-shadayim ein lah* — "we have a little sister and she has no breasts," referring, as Rashi indicates, to Daniel in Elam: *she-zachata lilmod ve-lo zachata lelamed* — she merited to learn but not to teach, meaning Daniel received Torah but had no disciples to whom he could transmit it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְגַסּוּת הָרוּחַ לְבָבֶל נְחִית? וְהָאָמַר מָר: עֲשָׂרָה קַבִּין גַּסּוּת יָרְדוּ לְעוֹלָם, תִּשְׁעָה נָטְלָה עֵילָם וְאַחַת כׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ! אִין, לְבָבֶל נְחִית, וְאִישְׁתַּרְבּוֹבֵי [הוּא] דְּאִישְׁתַּרְבַּב לְעֵילָם. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״לִבְנוֹת לָה בַיִת בְּאֶרֶץ שִׁנְעָר״. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ. וְהָאָמַר מָר: סִימָן לְגַסּוּת הָרוּחַ – עֲנִיּוּת, וַעֲנִיּוּת לְבָבֶל נְחִית! מַאי עֲנִיּוּת? עֲנִיּוּת תּוֹרָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״אָחוֹת לָנוּ קְטַנָּה וְשָׁדַיִם אֵין לָהּ״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: זוֹ עֵילָם, שֶׁזָּכְתָה לִלְמוֹד וְלֹא זָכְתָה לְלַמֵּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did arrogance descend to Babylonia? But doesn't the Master say: Ten *kav*** of **arrogance descended to the world; Eilam took nine and all** the rest of **the world in its entirety** took **one?** The Gemara answers: **Yes, it descended to Babylonia, and it made its way to Eilam.** The language of the verse **is also precise, as it is written: "To build her a house in the land of Shinar,"** which indicates that the original intention was to build a house in Babylonia, but it was not built there. The Gemara comments: **Conclude from it** that arrogance did not remain in Babylonia. The Gemara asks: **But doesn't the Master say: A sign of arrogance** is **poverty? And poverty descended to Babylonia,** not to Eilam! The Gemara answers: To **what** kind of **poverty** is this referring? It is **poverty** with regard to **Torah,** which was characteristic of Eilam. **As it is written: "We have a little sister, and she has no breasts"** (Song of Songs 8:8), and **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This** refers to **Eilam, whose** inhabitants **merited to learn** Torah **but did not merit to teach** it. They did not produce Torah scholars capable of imparting their wisdom to others.

## Eretz Yisrael vs Bav (II)

### Bavel Belula Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בָּבֶל? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּלוּלָה בַּמִּקְרָא, בְּלוּלָה בַּמִּשְׁנָה, בְּלוּלָה בַּתַּלְמוּד. ״בְּמַחֲשַׁכִּים הוֹשִׁיבַנִי כְּמֵתֵי עוֹלָם״ – אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: זֶה תַּלְמוּדָהּ שֶׁל בָּבֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the homiletic interpretation of the word **Babylonia? Rabbi Yoḥanan says,** as a tribute to the Jewish community of Babylonia and its Torah scholars: It means **mixed with Bible, mixed with Mishna,** and **mixed with Talmud.** Other Sages had a different opinion of the Torah in Babylonia: With regard to the verse: **"He has made me dwell in dark places, as those that have been long dead"** (Lamentations 3:6), **Rabbi Yirmeya says: This is the Talmud of Babylonia,** which is not as clear as the Talmud of Eretz Yisrael.

The *gemara* asks: *mai Bavel?* — what is the meaning of the word *Bavel* itself? Rabbi Yochanan answers: *belula be-Mikra, belula be-Mishna, belula be-Talmud* — "mixed together" with Scripture, *Mishna*, and *Talmud*. The very name *Bavel* contains a pun on *belula*, meaning mixed or combined, and this is now offered as a compliment: the *Talmud Bavli* integrates all three sources of Torah — *pesukim*, *Mishna*, and *Talmud* — simultaneously. Rabbeinu Tam, cited in *Tosafot*, develops this further: the Sages taught that one should divide one's learning time three ways — one third *Mikra*, one third *Mishna*, one third *Talmud*. Because the *Talmud Bavli* incorporates all three, one who learns it is *yotzei* all three obligations without needing to divide one's time separately.

Against this stands Rabbi Yirmiya's reading of *ba-machshakim hoshivani ke-metei olam* — "He made me sit in darkness like the eternal dead" — which he applies to the *Talmud* of Bavel: *she-ein nochin zeh im zeh ve-talmuda safek be-yadam* — because the scholars are harsh with one another and in constant contradiction, their Torah is consequently diminished, sitting in darkness.

## Creditor Accepts Invalid

### Mishna Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אָמַר לוֹ: ״נֶאֱמָן עָלַי אַבָּא״, ״נֶאֱמָן עָלַי אָבִיךָ״, ״נֶאֱמָנִים עָלַי שְׁלֹשָׁה רוֹעֵי בָּקָר״. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר בּוֹ, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר בּוֹ. הָיָה חַיָּיב לַחֲבֵירוֹ שְׁבוּעָה, וְאָמַר לוֹ: ״דּוֹר לִי בְּחַיֵּי רֹאשֶׁךָ״. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר בּוֹ, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one litigant **says to** the other: **My father is trusted** to adjudicate **for me,** or: **Your father is trusted** to adjudicate **for me,** or: **Three cattle herders,** who are not proficient in *halakha*, **are trusted** to adjudicate **for me,** all of whom are disqualified from serving as judges, **Rabbi Meir says:** The one who made the offer **can retract it, and the Rabbis say: He cannot retract it,** but must accept their verdict. Similarly, one who **was obligated** by Torah law to take **an oath to another,** which is done while grasping a sacred object, **and** the latter **said to him:** Instead of taking an oath, merely **vow to me by the life of your head** that what you claim is true, **Rabbi Meir says:** The one who made the offer **can retract it,** and demand that the other litigant take an oath, as he is obligated to do by Torah law. **And the Rabbis say: He cannot retract** his offer. Once he has agreed to accept a vow, which is of less severity than an oath, he cannot retract his agreement.

The *gemara* now transitions to the next *mishna*, which had already been cited in the previous discussion. The *mishna* teaches: if one litigant says to the other, *ne'eman alai avikha, ne'emanin alai shlosha ro'ei bakar* — "your father is trusted by me," or "these three cowherd are trusted by me" — to serve as witnesses or judges even though *mi-ikar ha-din* they are *pasul*, Rabbi Meir says *yachol lachzor bo*, he may retract his acceptance, while the Sages say *eino yachol lachzor bo*, he may not retract. A second clause teaches: if one litigant is required to take a *shevua* and the other says *dor li be-chayei roshecha* — "swear to me on your life" — rather than the more severe *shevua* invoking the name of God, Rabbi Meir says he may retract and the Sages say he may not.

### Shmuel Creditor Debtor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּ״מָחוּל לָךְ״, אֲבָל בְּ״אֶתֵּן לָךְ״ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The **dispute** between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis is **with regard to** a case where the claimant had said to the defendant: The money I claim you owe me is **forgiven you** if my father or your father rules as judge to that effect, and the claimant subsequently wishes to retract his offer. **But in** a case where it is the defendant who said: **I will give you** what you claim if that is the ruling of this judge, **everyone agrees** that **he can retract** his offer.

The *gemara* opens with a statement of Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel: *machloket be-machul lach, aval be-eten lach divrei hakol yachol lachzor bo* — the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Sages concerns *machul lach*, the case where the creditor says "I forgive your debt" conditional on the *pasul* witnesses testifying. But in the case of *eten lach* — where the debtor says "I will pay you" upon the testimony of the *pasul* witnesses — everyone agrees he can retract, because *mi-de'oraita*, the rule of *ha-motzi me-chaveiro alav ha-ra'aya* means the burden of proof rests with the one extracting the money. Since the *pasul* witnesses cannot truly discharge that burden *mi-de'oraita*, the debtor's acceptance is not binding and all agree he may go back.

### Rabbi Yochanan Both Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: בְּ״אֶתֵּן לָךְ״ – מַחְלוֹקֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The **dispute** is **with regard to** a case where the defendant says: **I will give you, but in** a case where the claimant says: The money I claim you owe me is **forgiven you, everyone agrees** that **he cannot retract** his offer.

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *be-eten lach machloket* — the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Sages applies specifically to the case of *eten lach*, where the debtor says "I will pay you" upon the testimony of the *pasul* witnesses. It is that acceptance — of the debtor himself — that is contested.

### Question Both or One

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בְּ״אֶתֵּן לָךְ״ – מַחְלוֹקֶת, אֲבָל בְּ״מָחוּל לָךְ״ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֵין יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר בּוֹ? אוֹ דִילְמָא: בֵּין בָּזוֹ וּבֵין בָּזוֹ מַחְלוֹקֶת?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages with regard to the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan: Is the **dispute** only **with regard to** a case where the defendant says: **I will give you, but in** a case where the claimant says: The money I claim you owe me is **forgiven you, everyone agrees** that **he cannot retract** his offer? **Or perhaps** Rabbi Yoḥanan maintains that the **dispute is both with regard to this** case **and with regard to that** case?

This raises a further question: according to Rabbi Yochanan, who places the *machloket* in the case of *eten lach*, what is the rule regarding *machul lach*? Is there also a *machloket* there, or does everyone agree that the creditor's acceptance is binding and he cannot retract? Or perhaps, as the other possibility holds, there is a *machloket* in both cases — both *eten lach* and *machul lach*.

## Rava Three Positions

### Rava Oath Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּ״אֶתֵּן לָךְ״, אֲבָל בְּ״מָחוּל לָךְ״ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֵין יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution from that **which Rava says:** The **dispute is with regard to** a case where the defendant says: **I will give you, but in** a case where the claimant says: The money I claim you owe me is **forgiven you, everyone agrees** that **he cannot retract** his offer.

Rava teaches: *machloket be-eten lach, aval be-machul lach divrei hakol ein yachol lachzor bo* — the *machloket* exists in the case of *eten lach*, but in the case of *machul lach* everyone agrees that one cannot retract. If one accepts this as Rabbi Yochanan's position — that the *machloket* is in *eten lach* alone, while *machul lach* is universally binding — then Rava's ruling aligns with Rabbi Yochanan. But if one says the *machloket* applies in both cases, then *Rava de-amar ke-man?* — whose opinion does Rava follow? In that case, *Rava ta'ama de-nafshei ka'amar* — Rava is expressing his own independent position, yielding a three-way dispute: some hold the *machloket* applies only to *machul lach* (Shmuel); some hold it applies only to *eten lach* (Rabbi Yochanan); and some hold it applies to both.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא בְּ״אֶתֵּן לָךְ״ – מַחְלוֹקֶת, אֲבָל בְּ״מָחוּל לָךְ״ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֵין יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר בּוֹ, רָבָא דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ בֵּין בָּזוֹ וּבֵין בָּזוֹ מַחְלוֹקֶת, רָבָא דְּאָמַר כְּמַאן?
>
> **Translation:** **Granted,** this makes sense **if you say** that according to Rabbi Yoḥanan too, the **dispute** is **with regard to** a case where the defendant says: **I will give you, but in** a case where the claimant says: The money I claim you owe me is **forgiven you, everyone agrees** that **he cannot retract** his offer; this means that **Rava is stating** the *halakha* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan. But if you say** that according to Rabbi Yoḥanan the **dispute is both with regard to this** case **and with regard to that** case, then **in accordance with whose** opinion **is Rava stating** the *halakha*? His statement is in accordance with neither Shmuel's opinion nor Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion.

### Rav Acha Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר תַּחְלִיפָא לְרָבָא: הָיָה חַיָּיב לַחֲבֵירוֹ שְׁבוּעָה, וְאָמַר לוֹ: ״דּוֹר לִי בְּחַיֵּי רֹאשְׁךָ״. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר בּוֹ, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Taḥlifa raised an objection to** the opinion of **Rava** from the latter clause in the mishna: With regard to one who **was obligated** by Torah law to take **an oath to another, and** the latter **said to him: Vow to me by the life of your head** that what you claim is true, **Rabbi Meir says:** The one who made the offer **can retract it; and the Rabbis say: He cannot retract** his offer.

Rav Acha bar Tachlifa raises a challenge to Rava from the second clause of the *mishna*: if one is obligated to take a *shevua* and the other says *dor li be-chayei roshecha*, Rabbi Meir says he may retract and the Sages say he cannot. Rava had said the *machloket* is in *eten lach* but not in *machul lach*. The question: does not this case of the *shevua* involve *machul lach* — the creditor effectively saying "take this lighter oath and I release you" — which would mean there *is* a *machloket* by *machul lach*, contradicting Rava?

### Nishba'in Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו בְּאוֹתָן הַנִּשְׁבָּעִין וְלֹא מְשַׁלְּמִין, דַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ כְּ״מָחוּל לָךְ״? לָא, בְּאוֹתָן הַנִּשְׁבָּעִין וְנוֹטְלִין, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ כְּ״אֶתֵּן לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **What,** is it **not** referring **to those who take an oath and do not pay,** i.e., cases where the defendant is obligated to take an oath that the claim against him is false, and he is thereby exempt from paying? Accordingly, it is the claimant who suggests that the defendant take a vow instead, **which is the same as** saying: If you take a vow, the money I claim you owe **is forgiven you.** The Gemara rejects this objection: **No,** it is referring **to those who take an oath and collect** a debt. There are cases where it is the claimant who is obligated to take an oath, and he then collects the money that he claims. In a case where the defendant suggests that the claimant take a vow instead, **it is the same as** saying: If you take a vow **I will give you** what you claim.

The *gemara* resolves by distinguishing two types of oath-takers. There are those who are *nishba'in ve-lo meshalmin* — they take an oath and are thereby exempted from payment. In that case, the creditor is saying: swear on your life and I will forgive the debt — which is structurally *machul lach*. But Rava's ruling in the *shevua* case concerns *nishba'in ve-notlin* — those who take an oath and then collect, such as a paid guardian (*shomer sachar*) from whom the object was stolen, who swears that it was stolen and thereby collects. The debtor in such a case is saying: I accept this lighter oath, and *eten lach* — I will pay you — which is structurally *eten lach*. Therefore the *shevua* case poses no contradiction to Rava.

### Both Clauses Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תְּנָא לֵיהּ רֵישָׁא! תְּנָא תּוֹלֶה בְּדַעַת אֲחֵרִים, וּתְנָא תּוֹלֶה בְּדַעַת עַצְמוֹ. וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי תְּנָא תּוֹלֶה בְּדַעַת אֲחֵרִים – בְּהָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר דְּמָצֵי הָדַר בֵּיהּ, מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא גָּמַר וּמַקְנֵי, דְּאָמַר: ״מִי יֵימַר דִּמְזַכֵּי לֵיהּ?״ אֲבָל תּוֹלֶה בְּדַעַת עַצְמוֹ, אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בְּהָא, בְּהָא קָאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן, אֲבָל בְּהַהִיא אֵימָא מוֹדוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But** according to Rava's interpretation, the mishna already **taught** the *halakha* in a case where the defendant says: I will give you, in **the first clause,** where the defendant accepts unfit judges. The Gemara answers: The mishna **taught** in the former clause a case where the defendant **makes** the outcome **dependent on the decision of others,** namely, his father or the father of the claimant; **and** the mishna **taught** in the latter clause a case where **he makes** the outcome **dependent on** the claimant's **own decision.** The Gemara explains: **And it is necessary** for the mishna to teach both cases, **since if** the mishna **taught** only the case where **he makes** the outcome **dependent on the decision of others,** one might reason that specifically **in this** case **Rabbi Meir says that he can retract** his acceptance of the unfit judges. This is **because he** presumably **did not resolve to transfer** the money if these judges deem him liable, **as he** must have **said** to himself: **Who will say that** the judges **will rule in favor of** the claimant? **But** in a case where **he makes** the outcome **dependent on** the claimant's **own decision** by suggesting that he take a vow, **say** that Rabbi Meir **concedes to the Rabbis** that the defendant cannot retract his commitment to pay. **And** conversely, **if** the mishna **would teach us** only **this** latter case, where the defendant suggests that the claimant take a vow, one might reason that specifically **in this** case **the Rabbis say** that he cannot retract his offer; **but in that** former case, where he makes the outcome dependent on the decision of others, **say** that **the Rabbis concede to Rabbi Meir** that he can retract his acceptance of the unfit judges. Therefore, **it is necessary** for the mishna to teach both cases.

The *gemara* then addresses a potential redundancy: if the *seifa* of the *mishna* (the *shevua* clause) is also about *eten lach*, why do we need it when the *reisha* already established the *machloket* in *eten lach*? The answer is that *tanna tole be-da'at acherim* and *tanna tole be-da'at atzmo* — we need both because they differ in a critical way. The *reisha* (*avikha*, *avicha*, the cowherd witnesses) involves an acceptance that depends on others' intentions — the litigant is accepting witnesses or judges who are not himself, and Rabbi Meir might say he can retract because *mi yeimar de-mazkei lei*, perhaps he assumed they would testify in his favor and was not truly *gomer* in his commitment. But in the *seifa*, the case is *tole be-da'at atzmo* — the debtor himself decides to swear on his own life, which is his own *da'at*, and one might think he is fully committed and even Rabbi Meir would agree he cannot retract. Conversely, if we only had the *seifa*, we might think the Sages agree he can retract in the *reisha* because the acceptance was based on others' intentions and therefore was never fully binding. Both clauses are therefore necessary, *tzericha*.

## Before vs After Judgment

### Reish Lakish Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מַחְלוֹקֶת לִפְנֵי גְּמַר דִּין, אֲבָל לְאַחַר גְּמַר דִּין – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֵין יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Reish Lakish says:** The **dispute** between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis concerns whether or not one can retract his acceptance of unfit judges **before the verdict** is issued. **But after the verdict** is issued, **everyone agrees** that **one cannot retract** his acceptance, as he has already been deemed liable.

A parallel structural debate now emerges, this time concerning the temporal dimension of the *machloket*. Reish Lakish says: *machloket lifnei gmar din, aval le-achar gmar din divrei hakol ein yachol lachzor bo* — the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Sages concerns what happens *before* the judgment is finalized. Once *gmar din* has occurred, everyone agrees that neither party can retract their acceptance of the *pasul* witnesses or judges. Concretely: if Reuven brings his father as a witness, Shimon accepts him, the father testifies, and Shimon loses the case, according to Reish Lakish Shimon can no longer invoke Rabbi Meir's position to challenge the father's testimony — the judgment is final.

### Rabbi Yochanan Dispute A…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: לְאַחַר גְּמַר דִּין מַחְלוֹקֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The **dispute** concerns whether or not one can retract his acceptance **after the verdict** is issued.

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *le-achar gmar din machloket* — even after the judgment has been rendered, the *machloket* between Rabbi Meir and the Sages remains live. That is, Rabbi Meir would hold that even after the judgment is finalized, a party could still challenge the *pasul* witnesses or judges he had previously accepted.

### Rava Temporal Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: לְאַחַר גְּמַר דִּין מַחְלוֹקֶת, אֲבָל לִפְנֵי גְּמַר דִּין דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר בּוֹ? אוֹ דִילְמָא: בֵּין בָּזוֹ וּבֵין בָּזוֹ מַחְלוֹקֶת?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages with regard to the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan: Is the **dispute** over whether one can retract his acceptance **after the verdict** is issued, **but before the verdict** is issued, **everyone agrees** that **he can retract** his acceptance? **Or perhaps** Rabbi Yoḥanan maintains that the **dispute is both with regard to this** case **and with regard to that** case.

The *gemara* raises a question about Rabbi Yochanan's position: *le-achar gmar din machloket, aval lifnei gmar din divrei hakol yachol lachzor bo?* — does Rabbi Yochanan mean the *machloket* applies only after *gmar din*, while before *gmar din* everyone agrees one can retract? Or perhaps there is a *machloket* both before and after?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: קִיבֵּל עָלָיו קָרוֹב אוֹ פָּסוּל – לִפְנֵי גְּמַר דִּין יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר בּוֹ, לְאַחַר גְּמַר דִּין אֵין יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution to the dilemma, **as Rava says:** If one **accepted a relative upon himself** as a judge or witness, **or** if he accepted **one who is disqualified** for another reason from serving as a judge or a witness, **he can retract** his acceptance **before the verdict** is issued. **He cannot retract** his acceptance **after the verdict** is issued.

Rava provides an answer: *kibel alav karov o pasul lifnei gmar din yachol lachzor bo, le-achar gmar din ein yachol lachzor bo* — if someone accepted a relative (*karov*) or otherwise *pasul* witness before the conclusion of the judgment, he may retract; after *gmar din*, he cannot.

### Question Temporal Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא: לְאַחַר גְּמַר דִּין מַחְלוֹקֶת, אֲבָל לִפְנֵי גְּמַר דִּין דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר בּוֹ – רָבָא דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ: בֵּין בָּזוֹ בֵּין בָּזוֹ מַחְלוֹקֶת – רָבָא דְּאָמַר כְּמַאן?
>
> **Translation:** **Granted,** this makes sense **if you say** that according to Rabbi Yoḥanan too, the **dispute** concerns whether one can retract his acceptance **after the verdict** is issued, **but before the verdict** is issued **everyone agrees** that **one can retract** his acceptance; then this means that **Rava is stating** the *halakha* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, and according to** the opinion **of the Rabbis. But if you say** that according to Rabbi Yoḥanan, the **dispute is both with regard to this** case **and with regard to that** case, then **in accordance with whose** opinion **is Rava stating** the *halakha*? His statement is in accordance with the opinion of neither Reish Lakish nor Rabbi Yoḥanan.

This teaching of Rava now becomes a proof. The *gemara* reasons: if one takes the position that the *machloket* is only after *gmar din*, while before *gmar din* everyone agrees one can retract — then Rava's ruling reflects Rabbi Yochanan's position, specifically the opinion of the Sages (*aliba de-rabbanan*). Before *gmar din*, even the Sages agree one can retract (*yachol lachzor bo*); after *gmar din*, the Sages hold one cannot (*ein yachol lachzor bo*), and it is only there that Rabbi Meir would disagree. Rava's ruling — before *gmar din* one can retract, after *gmar din* one cannot — fits precisely with Rabbi Yochanan as understood according to the Sages.

But if one says the *machloket* exists both before and after *gmar din*, then *Rava de-amar ke-man?* — Rava's ruling cannot be attributed to any consistent position, since he holds one can retract before *gmar din* but not after, which would mean he follows the Sages on one side and no one in particular on the other.

### Conclusion After Judgment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: לְאַחַר גְּמַר דִּין מַחְלוֹקֶת? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** must we **not conclude from** Rava's statement that the **dispute** is over whether one can retract his acceptance **after the verdict** is issued? The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from** Rava's statement that this is so.

The *gemara* concludes: *ela lav, shema mina le-achar gmar din machloket* — we must therefore conclude, according to Rabbi Yochanan (as demonstrated by Rava), that the *machloket* between Rabbi Meir and the Sages applies only after *gmar din*. Before *gmar din*, the rule is *divrei hakol yachol lachzor bo* — all agree that one may retract his acceptance of a *pasul* witness or judge. *Shema mina*.

## Geonic Rulings Kinyan

### Nachman Correspondence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר רַב חִסְדָּא לְרַב נַחְמָן בַּר יַעֲקֹב: יְלַמְּדֵנוּ רַבֵּינוּ, לִפְנֵי גְּמַר דִּין מַחְלוֹקֶת אוֹ לְאַחַר גְּמַר דִּין מַחְלוֹקֶת? וַהֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי מִי? שְׁלַח לֵיהּ: לְאַחַר גְּמַר דִּין מַחְלוֹקֶת, וַהֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman, son of Rav Ḥisda, sent** the following question **to Rav Naḥman bar Ya'akov: Our teacher, instruct us:** Does the **dispute** concern whether or not one can retract his acceptance **before the verdict** is issued, **or** does the **dispute** concern whether or not one can retract his acceptance **after the verdict** is issued? **And** furthermore, **in accordance with whose statement** is **the *halakha*?** Rav Naḥman bar Ya'akov **sent to him** in response: The **dispute** concerns whether one can retract his acceptance **after the verdict** is issued, **and the *halakha*** is **in accordance with the statement of the Rabbis.**

The *gemara* records a correspondence: Rav Nachman bar Rav Chisda sent a question to Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak: *yelamdenu rabbeinu* — teach us, our master — *lifnei gmar din machloket o le-achar gmar din machloket?* Is the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Sages located before *gmar din*, as Reish Lakish holds, or after *gmar din*, as Rabbi Yochanan holds? And what is the practical *halakha*?

### Halakha Chachamim

The reply: *le-achar gmar din machloket*, the *machloket* is after *gmar din* — following Rabbi Yochanan. *Ve-halakha ke-divrei chachamim*: the *halakha* follows the Sages, which means that after *gmar din* one may not retract the acceptance of a *pasul* witness or judge.

### Rav Ashi Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: הָכִי שְׁלַח לֵיהּ, בְּ״אֶתֵּן לָךְ״ מַחְלוֹקֶת, אוֹ בְּ״מָחוּל לָךְ״ מַחְלוֹקֶת? וַהֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי מִי? שְׁלַח לֵיהּ: בְּ״אֶתֵּן לָךְ״ מַחְלוֹקֶת, וַהֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says** a different version of the question and answer: **This** is the question that Rav Naḥman, son of Rav Ḥisda, **sent to** Rav Naḥman bar Ya'akov: Is the **dispute with regard to** a case where the defendant says: **I will give you, or** is the **dispute with regard to** a case where the claimant says: The money I claim you owe me is **forgiven you? And** furthermore, **in accordance with whose statement** is **the *halakha*?** Rav Naḥman bar Ya'akov **sent** this response **to him:** The **dispute is with regard to** a case where the defendant says: **I will give you, and the *halakha*** is **in accordance with the statement of the Rabbis.**

Rav Ashi reports that the question sent was actually framed differently: *be-eten lach machloket o be-machul lach machloket?* — is the *machloket* in the case of *eten lach* (the debtor accepting the *pasul* testimony as grounds for payment) or in the case of *machul lach* (the creditor accepting it as grounds for forgiveness)? And who is the *halakha* like?

### Rav Ashi Answer

The reply: *be-eten lach machloket* — the *machloket* is in the case of *eten lach*, following Rabbi Yochanan. *Ve-halakha ke-divrei chachamim* — the *halakha* follows the Sages, meaning that once the debtor has said *eten lach* and the case has proceeded, he cannot retract.

### Kinyan Exception Final

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּסוּרָא מַתְנוּ הָכִי. בְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא מַתְנוּ הָכִי, אָמַר רַב חֲנִינָא בַּר שֶׁלֶמְיָה: שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ מִבֵּי רַב לִשְׁמוּאֵל, יְלַמְּדֵנוּ רַבֵּינוּ: לִפְנֵי גְּמַר דִּין וְקָנוּ מִיָּדוֹ, מַאי? שְׁלַח לְהוּ: אֵין לְאַחַר קִנְיָין כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **In Sura they teach that** previous version of the question and the answer. **In Pumbedita they teach this** following version: **Rav Ḥanina bar Shelemya says** that **they sent** the following question **from Rav's study hall to Shmuel: Our teacher, instruct us:** In a case where one litigant wishes to retract his acceptance of the judges **before the verdict** is issued, **and** the other litigant **had performed** a formal **act of acquisition with him** giving legal power to the acceptance, **what** is the *halakha*? Shmuel **sent them** in response: **Nothing** can be retracted **after an act of acquisition** was performed.

The *gemara* then notes a distinction between the academies of Sura and Pumbedita. In Sura they taught as above. In Pumbedita, Rav Chanina bar Shalmaya relates that a question was sent from the *bei rav* to Shmuel: *yelamdenu rabbeinu, lifnei gmar din ve-kanu mi-yado, mai?* — if all of this occurred before *gmar din* but an act of *kinyan* had been performed — what is the *halakha*? The reply: *ein le-achar kinyan klum* — after a *kinyan* has taken place, nothing can be undone. Once a *kinyan* is performed, even Rabbi Meir agrees with the Sages that the acceptance is fully binding and there is no further possibility of retraction. The reasoning becomes clear: *kinyan* constitutes *gmar da'at* — a decisive, finalized act of will — and once the debtor has both declared his acceptance and performed an act of acquisition, his commitment is unambiguous and irrevocable.
