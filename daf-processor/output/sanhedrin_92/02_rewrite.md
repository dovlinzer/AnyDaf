# Sanhedrin 92 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Textual Notes

### Daf 92 Overview

Daf 92 continues within *Perek Chelek*, one of the most expansive loci of *aggadata* in the entire *Gemara*, devoted to *olam haba* and the many theological and eschatological questions surrounding it.

### Rashi Attribution

Before resuming the *sugya*, two points that arose in the previous session warrant brief mention. The first concerns whether the Rashi commentary on this section is genuine. It was Y. N. Epstein who first raised the question and proposed that the commentary was composed by a student of Rashi — the *Rivas* — rather than Rashi himself. Based on scholarship from roughly four decades ago, however, the overwhelming weight of evidence appears to favor authentic Rashi attribution, and nothing in the commentary itself reads in a way that feels distinctively different from Rashi's characteristic approach. The debate exists, but the strong position is that this is indeed Rashi's work.

### Minim/Kuthim Variant

The second point concerns a *Gemara* from the previous daf that discussed a verse in the context of *minim* — sectarians — where the phrase *vi-af-tem toraschem* ("you have forged your Torah") appeared awkward in context. A student noted that the Koren Steinsaltz edition reads *kuthim* — Samaritans — which fits the passage far more naturally. What is surprising upon consulting the manuscripts, which are available online in their entirety, is that every extant manuscript reads *kuthim* as well. It appears that the Samaritan reading was the original text. How the word *minim* found its way into printed editions — when normally the censorship process runs in the opposite direction, replacing *minim* with *kuthim* so as to avoid a politically charged term — remains unclear.

## Rabbi-Antoninus Dialogue

### Intro to Dialogue

The *Gemara* returns to the dialogue between Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi and Antoninus, whom the tradition identifies with Marcus Aurelius — though how historically accurate this identification is remains an open question. The preceding discussion had addressed the body-soul relationship and how divine judgment can hold each accountable: since the soul and body each could claim that the other was responsible for sin, God reunites them and judges them together. This sits well with the understanding that *yom hadin* follows *techiyas hameisim*, when body and soul are rejoined. The broader framework of body-soul dualism, which pervades this section, is itself worth noting: it is more originally a Greek idea than a distinctively Jewish one, though by the Talmudic period it had become thoroughly integrated into rabbinic discourse.

### Soul Entry Timeline

Antoninus poses a question to Rabbi: *neshama mishe'as nitna ba-adam* — from when is the soul placed within a person? Is it *mish'at pekida*, from the moment of conception, or *mish'at yetzira*, from the moment of formation — that is, the point forty days after conception when the fetus begins to take on human form?

Rabbi initially answers: *mish'at yetzira*, from the time of formation. Before this stage, the Gemara elsewhere describes the embryo as merely water, and Rabbi's intuition is that the soul enters only once the body begins to acquire some human shape.

This exchange touches on a broader question embedded in the *Bereishit* narrative. The Torah describes *nishmat chayim* breathed into Adam, yet the same expression *nefesh chaya* appears in relation to animals and even sea creatures. The precise distinction between *neshama* and *nefesh* is not fully resolved, though the breathing of life by God into the human being does suggest something qualitatively distinct. The question of whether the *neshama* under discussion is the distinctively human divine spark — the *tzelem Elokim* — or simply the animating life-force present in all creatures, bears on how one reads the entire exchange.

### Flesh Spoilage Argument

Antoninus challenges Rabbi's position with a compelling logical argument: *efshar chatichat basar omedet shlosha yamim belo melach ve'eina masracha?* Can a piece of raw flesh sit for three days without salt and not spoil? By implication, if the soul only enters at forty days, what prevents the developing embryo from decomposing in the womb during those first forty days? Presumably it is the presence of the soul that preserves the flesh from decay. But then the soul must be present from the very beginning — from the moment of conception.

### Soul at Conception

Rabbi's response is immediate: *davar zeh limadani Antoninus* — this point Antoninus taught me. And once he recognized the argument's force, he found a verse that supports it: *upkudat'cha shamra ruchi* — "your visitation has guarded my spirit." The word *pekida* — visitation or conception — appears in the same root as *v'Hashem pakad et Sarah*, God's quickening of Sarah, and denotes the initial moment of divine engagement. From the moment of *pekida*, the spirit is already present, preserving the flesh.

The *Maharsha* reads the verse to mean that the spirit actively guards the flesh from the moment of conception — it is the soul's presence that prevents decay, which is precisely the logic Antoninus had employed.

### Philosophical Context

One might ask whether this discussion carries any practical *halachic* implications — particularly with respect to the question of abortion and the legal standing of an embryo before forty days. Some have sought to draw a line from here, arguing that if the soul enters at conception, then perhaps the embryo's legal standing should reflect that even from the earliest stage.

This argument is, however, largely dismissed by the *poskim*. The reason is that there exists a substantial body of *halachic* literature — actual *Gemarot* bearing directly on abortion law — and the *poskim* treat this passage as a philosophical discussion, not as a *halachic* ruling about the legal status of the embryo. It is worth noting that in contemporary *halachic* questions where no direct *Gemarot* exist — such as questions of maternity and paternity in cases of artificial insemination or surrogate motherhood — authorities do sometimes turn to *aggadic* sources. But because explicit *halachic* *Gemarot* exist on abortion, those take precedence and this passage remains in the domain of philosophy.

One might press: if the soul is already in the flesh, surely that should have some legal significance. One possible response is that the *neshama* discussed here may not refer to the distinctively human divine soul — the *tzelem Elokim* — but rather to the animating life-force shared with animals. One could equally ask when the soul of an animal enters the womb during gestation, and why that flesh does not decay either. The verse *nishmat chayim* may be operating at a different level than the *neshama* at stake in Antoninus's biological argument.

### Yetzer Hara Timeline

Antoninus then turns to a second question: *me'eimatai yeitzer hara sholeit ba-adam* — from when does the *yetzer hara* exercise dominion over a person? Is it from *mishat yetzira*, the time of formation, or from *mishat yetzia*, the time the infant exits the womb?

It is worth noticing that the three key terms in this passage — *yeitzer*, *yetzira*, and *yetzia* — share a common root, a linguistic resonance that may or may not be deliberate. Notably, the question here does not include *mishat pekida* as a possibility, unlike the previous exchange about the soul. The *yetzer hara* in this context should probably not be understood in its most extreme sense of an evil drive toward immorality, but more broadly as the animating desires and drives of a person — the raw impulse-life that motivates behavior.

Rabbi's initial answer is again: *mishat yetzira*, from the time of formation.

### Fetal Motion Objection

Antoninus challenges this: *im kein boeit be-mei imo ve-yotzei* — if that were so, the fetus would already be trying to kick its way out of the womb. If the *yetzer hara* — understood as drives and desires and the will to assert oneself — were already present in the fetus, why would the fetus remain placid inside the womb rather than pushing insistently to be free? Of course, some women would argue that this is precisely what their babies seemed to be doing for nine months. But the force of Antoninus's objection is that a being governed by drives should not be content in such a constrained state.

The *Gemara* on *ki yeitzer leiv ha-adam ra mi-ne'urav* — which appears in the passage of Noach — is relevant here. Rashi there, citing *Bereishit Rabbah*, interprets *mi-ne'urav* as *mi-shenina'ar latzeit mi-mei imo* — from the time it stirs to exit the womb. That formulation places the onset of the *yetzer hara* not at actual birth but at the moment the infant is pushing its way out. This is somewhat in tension with the *Yaakov* and *Esav midrashim*, in which Esav's *yetzer hara* manifests already in the womb, though the *Maharsha* addresses some of that tension.

### Yetzer at Birth

The resolution follows the now-familiar pattern: *ela mishat yetzia* — it must be from the moment of exit. And again Rabbi acknowledges: *davar zeh limadani Antoninus u-mikra mesaye'o* — Antoninus taught me this, and the verse supports it: *l'fetach chatat roveitz* — sin crouches at the doorway. The doorway is understood as the opening of the womb. The *yetzer hara* enters the moment a person enters the world.

## Resurrection Concepts

### Reish Lakish Paradox

The *Gemara* now shifts to a series of apparent contradictions about the nature of the future world, beginning with Reish Lakish. Two verses seem to present opposing pictures. One verse states: *bam iver u-fise'ach harah ve-yoledet yachdav* — among them are the blind and the lame, the pregnant and the birthing together. Another verse declares: *az yidaleig ka-ayal pise'ach*, then the lame will leap like a hart, and *ve-taron leshon ileim*, the tongue of the mute will sing forth. The first verse implies that disability will persist into the messianic age; the second implies complete healing. How are these reconciled?

This type of question — will the dead be resurrected in their prior physical condition or already healed — is precisely the sort of inquiry Rambam criticized as beneath the dignity of serious theological discussion. Yet the *Gemara* engages it directly, which itself demonstrates that Rambam's position was not the consensus.

### Resurrection with Healing

The resolution: *omdim be-mumam u-mitrapin* — they are resurrected with their blemishes and then they are healed. One can reflect on why this sequence should be theologically significant. Perhaps the continuity between the person who died and the person who is resurrected requires that they arise in recognizable form — as themselves, with their physical history intact — before restoration takes place.

### Ula's Death Paradox

Ula similarly points to a contradiction between two verses. One verse reads: *bila hamavet lanetzach umacha Hashem dima me'al kol panim* — death will be swallowed up forever, and God will wipe away the tears from every face. This appears to promise a future world entirely without death. Yet another verse reads: *ki hana'ar ben me'ah shanah yamut* — one who dies at a hundred years will be considered to have died young, and: *lo yihyeh misham od ul yamim* — there will no longer be a burden of days. This verse envisions vastly extended lifespans, but still envisions death.

### Jewish vs. Non-Jewish Et…

The resolution: *lo kashya — kan be'Yisrael kan be'ovdei kochavim*. The verse promising the complete elimination of death applies to Jews; the verse describing century-long lifespans as youthful death applies to non-Jews. In the future world, Jews will live forever, while non-Jews will live for extraordinary lengths of time but will not be immortal.

This resolution immediately raises a broader question: what is the status of non-Jews in the future world at all? Several possibilities present themselves. One view holds that the distinction between Jews and non-Jews will be maintained, with non-Jews recognizing God but retaining their own distinctive identity. A second possibility is that all non-Jews will become Jewish. A third possibility flows from the opinion that *mitzvot betelot le'atid lavo* — commandments will no longer be operative in the future era — in which case the very category of religious particularism may dissolve, and the distinction between Jew and non-Jew may cease to exist. All three frameworks remain live options within the tradition.

### Non-Jews' Role Future

The *Gemara* presses the question: *ve'ovdei kochavim mai ba'u hatam?* — who invited the non-Jews? What are they doing in the future world at all? The answer draws on a verse: *ve'amdu zarim ve-ra'u tzonchem u-venei nechar ikareichem ve-chormeichem* — foreigners will stand and tend your flocks, and the children of strangers will be your farmers and your vintners. The non-Jews will be present not as recipients of divine reward in their own right, but as the labor force that enables the Jewish people to be fully engaged in their transcendent existence. This framing, striking as it is, sets up a broader discussion about the role of non-Jews in the future world that will continue further in the *Gemara*.

## Yemos HaMashiach vs. Ola…

### Light Paradox

Rav Chisda points to another contradiction, this one concerning the luminaries. One verse states: *ve-chafra halevana u-vosha hachama ki malach Hashem tzeva'ot* — the moon will be shamed and the sun will be embarrassed, for God will reign as king. In the radiance of divine presence, the light of the sun and moon will be overwhelmed and humbled. Another verse, however, states: *va-yhi or halevana ke-or hachama ve-or hachama yihye shiv'atayim ke-or shivat ha-yamim* — the light of the moon will be like the light of the sun, and the light of the sun will be sevenfold, like the light of the seven original days of creation. This verse envisions a vast amplification of natural light.

### Two Periods Resolved

The resolution: *lo kashya — kan li-yemot ha-mashiach kan le-olam haba*. These verses describe two distinct periods. In *yemot hamashiach*, the messianic era, the sun and moon will shine with dramatically increased brilliance. In *olam haba*, by contrast, the physical existence associated with natural light may no longer obtain at all, and the divine radiance will overwhelm whatever luminaries remain.

### Samuel's Naturalism

Now the *Gemara* introduces Shmuel's famous position: *ein bein olam hazeh li-yemot hamashiach ela shib'ud galuyot bilvad* — there is no difference between this world and the messianic era except the subjugation of exile. Rambam's version of this formulation reads *shib'ud malchuyot*, the subjugation of foreign kingdoms, but the substance is the same. Messianic times, according to Shmuel, will not involve any miraculous transformation of nature. It is critical to note that this opinion is presented in the *Gemara* as the view of Shmuel alone. Rambam famously cites it as if it represents the consensus of the sages — *ameru chachamim* — but in its original context it is a single, dissenting position.

How does Shmuel's view reconcile the verses Rav Chisda raised? *Lo kashya — kan bi-machaneh tzadikkim kan bi-machaneh shechina*. The difference lies in proximity to the divine presence, borrowing from the concentric structure of the wilderness camp: those in the innermost circle, close to the *Mishkan*, experience God's radiance overwhelming all natural light, while those at greater distance still perceive the amplified light of sun and moon.

Crucially, Shmuel's naturalism about *yemot hamashiach* does not mean he denies a miraculous *olam haba*. Shmuel is arguing only that the messianic period itself will be continuous with this world, minus exile. The otherworldly transformation — the *machaneh shechina* overwhelming natural light — belongs to *olam haba*, which even Shmuel affirms.

### Wounding & Healing

Rava points to yet another apparent contradiction. One verse states: *ani amis ve-achayeh* — I will kill and bring to life. Another verse states: *machatzti va-ani erpa* — I will wound and I will heal. The tension between them: if God kills and revives, what is the role of wounding and healing? The resolution: *mah she-ani meimis ani mechayeh, va-hadar mah she-machatzti va-ani erpa* — first God will resurrect the dead, and then, as we established earlier, they will be healed. The sequence is: death, resurrection with blemishes, and then healing — consistent with what was established in the resolution of Reish Lakish's contradiction.

## Techiyas Hametim Proofs

### Kill & Revive Proof

The *Gemara* now turns to a sustained *tannaitic* effort to establish *techiyas hameisim min ha-Torah*. The verse *ani amis ve-achayeh* is brought as a primary source. A *baraita* raises the obvious objection: perhaps the verse simply means that one person dies and another comes to life — the ordinary course of the world, in which each generation replaces the one before. The response comes from the parallel phrase in the same verse: *machatzti va-ani erpa*. Just as wounding and healing apply to the same individual — a person is wounded and that same person is healed — so too death and revival must apply to the same individual. The one who died is the one who is brought back to life. This is *mikan teshuvah la-omrim ein techiyas hameisim min ha-Torah* — a response to those who deny that resurrection has any basis in the Torah.

### Future Tense Argument

Rabbi Meir asks: where is the basis for *techiyas hameisim* in the Torah? He cites the Song of the Sea: *az yashir Moshe u-vnei Yisrael et ha-shira ha-zot l'Hashem*. The word is *yashir* — will sing — not *shar*, sang. The future tense indicates that Moshe will sing again in the future. *Mikan l'techiyas hameisim min ha-Torah*. A parallel proof: *az yivneh Yehoshua mizbe'ach l'Hashem* — *yivneh*, will build, rather than *bana*, built. Yehoshua will build an altar in the future.

### Solomon Idol Problem

The *Gemara* raises an immediate objection: if the future tense in these verses proves resurrection, consider: *az yivneh Shlomo bama l'Chemosh shikutz Moav* — Solomon will build a high place for Chemosh, the abomination of Moab. Does this mean Solomon will be resurrected to engage in idolatry? The resolution: regarding idolatry, the principle applies that Scripture ascribes to a person the intention to sin as if the sin were committed. The future tense there means Solomon desired to build it, and the verse therefore treats it as though he did. By contrast, regarding Moshe and Yehoshua, we know from context that these are real future events — Moshe will actually sing praise again. The future tense carries a different valence depending on context.

### Dwelling in God's House

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi derives the proof from: *ashrei yoshvei veitecha od yehalelucha sela* — praised are those who dwell in Your house; they will yet praise You. The verse says *yehalelucha* — will praise — not *hilelucha*, have praised. *Mikan l'techiyas hameisim min ha-Torah*.

### Singing Continuity

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi draws a corollary: *kol ha-omer shira ba-olam hazeh, zocheh ve-omra le-olam haba* — whoever sings God's praises in this world will merit to sing them in the world to come. The proof text is the same verse: *ashrei yoshvei veitecha od yehalelucha sela*. Those who dwell in God's house — whether this means the *beit knesset* or some broader sense of sacred space — and who praise God now, will praise God again in the future. The pattern across these proofs is consistent: the future tense of praise implies that those who sang will sing again, which implies their continued or renewed existence.

### Prophets' Unified Voice

Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan adds: *kol tzofayich nas'u kol yachdav yeranenu* — the sound of your visionaries lifted their voice together and sang forth. The proof again lies in the future tense: *yeranenu*, they will sing — not *rinnenu*, they sang. *Mikan l'techiyas hameisim min ha-Torah*.

Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba further states in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *atidin kol ha-nevi'im kulam omrim shira be-kol echad* — in the future, all the prophets will sing praise in a single voice. This remarkable statement connects to the *Gemara* encountered earlier regarding *signon echad* — that different prophets could perceive the same divine vision yet articulate it in their individual styles. In the future, the clarity of divine perception will be so complete that all individual variation dissolves, and all the prophets will speak, and sing, in perfect unison.

## Teaching Torah Obligation

### Withholding Theft

Rabbi Yehuda in the name of Rav states: *kol ha-mone'a halacha mi-pi talmid ke-ilu gozlo mi-nachalat avotav* — whoever withholds a *halacha* from a student is as though he stole from that student's ancestral inheritance. The proof: *Torah tziva lanu Moshe morasha kehilat Yaakov* — the Torah that Moshe commanded us is the inheritance of the congregation of Jacob. Since Torah is the national inheritance of all Israel from the six days of creation, it cannot be withheld from any member of that nation.

### Withholding Curses

Rav Chana bar Bizna in the name of Rabbi Shimon Chasida intensifies this: *kol ha-mone'a halacha mi-pi talmid, afilu ubarin she-be-mei imma mekalelin oto* — even the fetuses in their mothers' wombs curse out anyone who withholds Torah from a student. The prooftexts work through a chain of associations: *mone'a bar yikvuhu le'om* — one who withholds grain (*bar*) will be cursed (*yikvuhu*) by nations (*le'om*). *Ve-ein le'om ela ubarin*, as the verse *u-le'om mi-le'om ye'ematz* — one nation shall prevail over the other — uses *le'om* to mean unborn children. *Ve-ein kavo ela klala* — to *kovo* means to curse, as in *mah ekov lo kavo El*. And *ve-ein bar ela Torah* — grain (*bar*) means Torah, as in *nashku bar pen ye'enaf*.

### Pierced Like Sieve

Ulla bar Shmuel adds a vivid image: *menakvin oto ke-khivara* — such a person will be pierced through like a sieve. The wordplay: *yikvuhu* in the verse can be read not only as cursing but as piercing, as in *vayikov chor be-dalto*, to bore a hole in a door. Abaye adds that this is like the perforated implement used by launderers to sprinkle water. The resonance is striking: the one who tries to hoard Torah and hold it inside himself will find himself punctured through — everything he tried to keep for himself will drain away.

### Teaching Reward

If one does teach, what is the reward? Rava in the name of Rav Sheshet states: *zocheh le-vrachot ke-Yosef* — one merits blessings like Joseph. The verse: *u-vracha le-rosh mashbir* — blessing is upon the head of the one who provides sustenance. And *ein mashbir ela Yosef*, as in *Yosef hu ha-shalit al ha-aretz hu ha-mashbir le-chol am ha-aretz* — Joseph provided sustenance to all the people of the land. Just as Joseph provided for all who came to him, so the teacher of Torah is rewarded with blessing.

### Extended Teaching Effort

The *Maharsha*'s interpretation of this entire passage is particularly powerful. He reads *kol ha-mone'a halacha mi-pi talmid* not merely as a prohibition against outright refusal to teach, but as an obligation to extend oneself in teaching — specifically, to persist until the student has genuinely mastered the material. The *Maharsha* writes: *she-eino lomed im ha-talmid ke-Rav Preida, she-haya lamed im talmido arba me'ot pe'amim ad she-hayta shegurah lo ba-peh* — one is obligated to learn with a student like Rav Preida, who would teach a student 400 times until the material was fluent in that student's mouth. The prohibition is not only against refusing to teach the easy students; it is against refusing to invest the effort required for the difficult ones.

The *Maharsha* continues: *u-fi she-omer ha-rav she-ein ha-talmid hagun*, when a teacher dismisses a student as unworthy or unteachable — *harei gozlo mi-nachalat avotav*, he steals that student from the inheritance of the fathers. The basis: *she-ha-Torah hi morasha le-chol Yisrael mi-sheshet yemei bereishit* — Torah is the inheritance of all Israel from the six days of creation, and *le-fi beri'utam ve-tiv'am mi-sheshet yemei bereishit mukhanim kol Yisrael lilmod Torah* — by their very created nature, all of Israel are prepared and capable of learning Torah. This *Gemara* speaks with particular force to the communal obligation to ensure that every child has genuine access to Torah education — including those children for whom learning requires extraordinary effort and accommodation.

### Teaching in Olam HaBa

Rav Sheshet extends the theme: *kol ha-melamed Torah ba-olam ha-zeh, zocheh u-melamdah le-olam haba* — whoever teaches Torah in this world merits to teach it in the world to come. The verse: *u-marveh gam hu yoreh* — the one who saturates will also teach. This continues the pattern observed throughout this section: the activities one excels in in this world are precisely the activities one will continue in *olam haba*. Teaching Torah is not merely rewarded with something external; it becomes the very substance of one's existence in the world to come.

## Righteous Attributes

### Da'at Meaning

Rabbi Elazar states: *gedolah de'ah she-nitnah bein shtei otiyot* — great is *de'ah*, knowledge or intellect, for it was placed between two of God's names. The verse: *ki El de'ot Hashem* — for God is a God of knowledge. The divine name *El* precedes, and the name *Hashem* follows, and between them stands *de'ot*. The *Maharsha* emphasizes that this positioning is not merely formal but substantive: *El* represents *midat ha-din*, the divine attribute of strict judgment, while *Hashem* represents *midat ha-rachamim*, the attribute of mercy. True *de'ah* — authentic intellectual and moral understanding — therefore encompasses the capacity to know when *din* is called for and when *rachamim* is appropriate. The wisdom to navigate between these two divine qualities is itself what *de'ah* means at its deepest level.

### Da'at & Beis HaMikdash

Rabbi Elazar makes a parallel claim: *gadol Mikdash she-nitan bein shtei otiyot* — great is the *Beit HaMikdash*, for it too was placed between two of God's names. The verse: *pa'alta Hashem mikdash Hashem konenu yadecha* — You fashioned, O God, the sanctuary of God, your hands established it. Examining the names of God in the verse reveals that the first *Hashem* is *Alef-Dalet-Nun-Yud*, God's *midat hadin*, and the second is the *yud-keh-vav-keh*, the *shem hameforash* associated with *midat harachamim*. The *Beit HaMikdash* stands between these two qualities, just as *de'ah* does.

The *Maharsha* connects this to the teaching that Betzalel knew how to *letzaref et ha-otiyot* — to combine the letters of God's name — and through that capacity he fashioned the *Beit HaMikdash*. The *de'at* to understand and harness the interplay of divine names is what made the *Mishkan* possible. This creates a theologically rich equivalence: *de'ah* in the human being corresponds to what the *Beit HaMikdash* achieves in the world. The implication is significant — in the absence of the *Beit HaMikdash*, the person who has developed genuine *de'ah* represents a living point of divine connection.

### Vengeance Question

Rav Adda Karchinaos challenges: *ela me'ata gedola nekama she-nitnah bein shtei otiyot* — if the criterion for greatness is appearing between two of God's names, then vengeance too should be considered great, since the verse reads *Kel nekamot Hashem, Kel nekamot hofi'a* — God of vengeance, O God, appear — with *nekamot* appearing between two divine names.

The response: *le-milta hachi nami* — yes, in its proper context, vengeance too is a divine quality. When the exercise of *nekama* is genuinely appropriate, it partakes of the same divine character as *de'ah* and *mikdash*. The *Maharsha* invokes Pinchas here as the paradigm case — an act of zealous intervention that the rabbis regarded with significant ambivalence (the *Yerushalmi* states that the sages actually wished to place Pinchas in *cherem*, and only a divine voice declaring *hineni noten lo et briti shalom* stayed their hand). The point is that *nekama* as a *midah* is not simply rejected — but *midat harachamim* will almost always counsel against it, leaving only an extraordinarily narrow space in which it is appropriate.

Ulla elaborates: *shtei hofa'ayot halalu lama?* — why does the verse mention *hofi'a* twice in connection with *nekamot*? *Achat le-midah tovah ve-achat le-midat pur'anut* — one for reward and one for punishment. Rashi understands the rewarding manifestation as referencing *hofi'a meHar Paran*, the revelation of Torah at Sinai, in which the nations who refused the Torah were implicitly judged while Israel received it — though the connection to *nekama* in that context is admittedly indirect.

### Intellect Wealth

Rabbi Elazar continues: *kol adam she-yesh lo de'ah le-sof mitasher* — anyone who has *de'ah* will ultimately become wealthy. The verse: *u-ve-da'at chadarim yimale'u, kol hon yakar ve-na'im* — through knowledge, rooms are filled with all precious and pleasant treasure. This can be read literally — a sharp intellect provides the tools for material success — or figuratively, as a description of a richly lived inner life in which one possesses the things truly worth having.

### No Compassion Lack Da'at

Rabbi Elazar further states: *kol adam she-ein bo de'ah assur le-rachem alav* — it is forbidden to show compassion to someone who lacks *de'ah*. The verse: *ki lo am binot hu — al ken lo yerachamenu osehu ve-yotzro lo yechunenu* — for they are not a people of understanding; therefore their Creator will not have compassion on them, and their fashioner will not show them grace.

This requires careful interpretation. Taken at face value, it raises challenging questions: does Rabbi Elazar mean that anyone who is not intellectually gifted forfeits the right to compassion? Almost certainly not. What is being described is a person who has the capacity to develop their intellect and moral reasoning but chooses not to — who squanders opportunities for growth. The verse is about willful non-engagement, not congenital limitation. Even so, the statement raises important practical questions about where communal resources and compassion should be directed. Notably, even in cases where someone has acted with profound self-destructiveness — endangering their own life — one is still obligated to violate Shabbat to save them. The statement about withholding compassion is likely about orientation and priorities rather than a literal prohibition on assistance.

### Bread to Foolish

Rabbi Elazar also states: *kol ha-noten pito le-mi she-ein bo de'ah, yissurin ba'in alav* — one who gives their bread to someone lacking understanding will be visited with suffering. The verse: *lachmecha yasim mazor tachtecha ein tevunah* — your bread will become a *mazor* beneath you, there being no understanding. And *ein mazor ela yissurin*, as the verse *vayar Efraim et cholyo vi-Yehuda et mazoro* uses *mazor* to mean affliction.

The verse's logic implies that indiscriminate provision to someone who refuses to develop their capacities is not an act of generosity but of enabling — it entrenches the very pattern that leads them toward ruin. The practical analogy is to the question of enabling destructive behavior: sometimes genuine compassion requires withholding easy support. This connects back to the interplay between *midat hadin* and *midat harachamim* — sometimes what looks like mercy is in fact the greater cruelty.

### Exile from Ignorance

Finally: *kol adam she-ein bo de'ah, le-sof goleh* — anyone who lacks *de'ah* will ultimately go into exile. The verse: *lachen gala ami mi-bli da'at* — therefore my people went into exile for lack of knowledge. The loss of rootedness, of home, of stability — all these are the natural consequence of failing to cultivate understanding.

## Torah Support & Conduct

### Night Torah Study

Rabbi Elazar states: *kol bayit she-ein divrei Torah nishma'in bo va-lailah, esh ochalto* — any house in which words of Torah are not heard at night, a fire shall consume it. The verse: *kol choshech tamun li-tzfunav, tochalehu esh lo nupach, yera sarid be-ohalo* — all darkness is stored up for what is hidden away; an unblown fire consumes it; evil is upon the remnant in one's tent. *Ve-ein sarid ela talmidei chachamim*, as the verse *u-vaseridim asher Hashem koreh* uses *sarid* to mean those whom God calls out — and therefore the absence of Torah scholars from the home signals this darkness.

The *Maharsha* observes that night is the crucial time precisely because it is free from the legitimate demands of livelihood. During the day, one must work; that is understood. But at night, when there are no external obligations and one's true priorities are revealed in how one uses discretionary time, the absence of Torah study is a statement about character. The question of whether one opens a book that develops the mind or settles into purely passive entertainment is, in the *Maharsha*'s reading, what this *gemara* is addressing.

### Support Scholars

Rabbi Elazar adds: *kol she-eino mehaneh talmidei chachamim mi-nechesav, eino ro'eh siman bracha le'olam* — anyone who does not benefit Torah scholars from their property will see no sign of blessing. The *Maharsha* frames this as the complementary obligation for those who genuinely cannot devote their own time to Torah study: even if one's livelihood fully occupies one's days, the obligation to support those who do learn remains. To spend one's working life accumulating property and share none of it with those who dedicate themselves to Torah study is to court the withdrawal of divine blessing.

### Leftover Bread

Rabbi Elazar further states: *kol she-eino meshayer pat al shulchano, eino ro'eh siman bracha le'olam* — anyone who does not leave some bread on the table sees no sign of blessing. The verse: *ein sarid le-ochlo al ken lo yachil tuvo* — there is no remnant left to eat; therefore his goodness will not endure. Leaving bread on the table is a sign that God has given one more than enough — *achalnu ve-hoteir*, we ate and there was still something left over. The *Maharsha* adds that leaving crumbs also creates the opportunity for the poor to benefit afterward.

A seeming contradiction: does not Rabbi Elazar elsewhere state that one who leaves crumbs (*pesitim*) on the table is considered as if engaging in idolatry? The verse: *ha-orchim la-gad shulchan ve-ha-memalim li-mni mimsach* — those who set a table for the idol *gad* and fill vessels with libations. The resolution: *ha de-ika sheleima ba-hadeih* — the prohibition applies when a whole loaf of bread is left alongside the crumbs, which mimics an idolatrous ritual. *Ha de-leika sheleima ba-hadeih* — when only some remaining bread is left over from the meal, without the ceremonial character of a full loaf, this is the proper sign of blessing, not idolatry.

### Proper Conduct

Rabbi Elazar states: *kol ha-machlif be-diburo ke-ilu oved avoda zara* — anyone who changes his words, meaning misrepresents himself, is as if engaging in idolatry. The verse from the *Yaakov* and *Esav* narrative: *ve-hayisi be-einav ke-mtatei'a* — I will be in my father's eyes like a deceiver. And the parallel: *hevel heima ma'aseh tatu'im* — they are nothingness, they are pure deception — used of idolatry itself. The essential falseness of idolatry and the essential falseness of misrepresentation of oneself partake of the same spiritual category.

Rabbi Elazar adds: *kol ha-mistakel be-erva kashto ninarat* — one who gazes at forbidden nudity will have his bow shaken out. Rashi offers two interpretations: the literal — a man gazing at a woman's nakedness — or, in Rashi's preferred reading, a man who fixates mentally on sexual sin. The punishment is the loss of virility. The verse: *arya te'or kashtecha* — nakedness (*arya*, read as *erva*) will rouse your bow — the bow being understood as the instrument of masculine power.

### Humility & Privacy

Rabbi Elazar concludes this series: *le-olam hevei kavel ve-kayam* — always be accepting and enduring. The *Maharsha* offers two readings of *kavel*: either "submissive" or, from the Aramaic, "dark" — that is, not drawing attention to oneself, remaining in the background. Do not publicize your success. Do not be a complainer. Stay quiet and you will survive.

Rabbi Zeira supports this from a *mishnah*: *bayit afel ein potchin lo chalonot lir'ot nigo* — a dark house, one does not open windows in it to examine the blemishes. If one remains humble and unassuming, one is not inviting scrutiny of one's own imperfections. *Shma minna* — the principle is confirmed.

## Resurrection Proofs Natu…

### Never Satisfied Three

Rabbi Tavi in the name of Rabbi Yoshiya asks about the verse: *she'ol ve-otzer rachem eretz lo save'ah mayim* — the grave, the womb, and the thirsty earth are never satisfied. What connection is there between the depths of the underworld and the womb?

### Womb & Underworld

The *Gemara* draws the parallel: *mah rachem machnis u-motzi*, just as a womb receives and releases — it receives the semen and releases the child — so too the underworld receives the dead and releases them. *Af she'ol machnis u-motzi* — the underworld too receives souls in and releases them again. This is another proof of *techiyas hameisim*, arrived at through the structural parallel between two things that both receive and both release.

### Kal Vachomer Womb

The *Gemara* adds a *kal va-chomer*: *mah rachem she-machnisin lo be-chashai, motzi'in mimeno be-kolei kolos* — what goes into the womb goes in privately, in intimacy, yet what comes out does so with loud cries, publicly, as every birth is announced to the world. The underworld is the opposite: *she-ol she-machnisin bo be-kolei kolos* — what goes into the underworld does so loudly, with the entire procession of a funeral, publicly observed. *Eino din she-motzi'in mimeno be-kolei kolos?* — how much more so should what comes out of the underworld be announced with great fanfare and miracle. *Mikan techiyas hameisim min ha-Torah*.

## Righteous Future State

### No Return to Dust

A *tanna de-vei Eliyahu* teaches: *tzadikkim she-atid ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu le-hachayotan einan chozrin la-afaram* — the righteous whom God will revive in the future will not return to dust. This settles a question about what follows *techiyas hameisim*: it is not a temporary resurrection that eventually ends in a second death, but a permanent one for the righteous. The verse: *ve-haya ha-nishar be-Tzion ve-ha-notar bi-Yerushalayim kadosh ye'amer lo, kol ha-kasuv la-chayim bi-Yerushalayim* — all who are inscribed for life in Jerusalem will be called holy. *Mah kodesh le-olam kayam, af heim le-olam kayamin* — just as what is holy endures forever, so do they.

### Thousand Year Renewal

But a practical question arises: if there is a transitional period in which God is remaking the world — a process that will take a thousand years, corresponding to the verse *va-nisgav Hashem levado ba-yom hahu* — what will happen to the resurrected righteous during that interval? The world will be in a state of *tohu va-vohu*, dissolution and primordial chaos. The righteous, having been promised that they will not return to dust, must somehow persist through this cosmic transition.

### Eagle Wings Period

The answer: *ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu oseh lahem knafayim ke-nesharim, la-shut al pnei ha-mayim* — God will give them wings like eagles, and they will soar over the primordial waters. The image is a deliberate echo of *ve-ruach Elokim merachefet al pnei ha-mayim* — the spirit of God hovering over the waters at creation. The bodies of the righteous, sustained by divine power, will hover over the waters of the re-forming world. The verse: *al ken lo nira be-hamir aretz, be-mot harim be-lev yamim* — therefore we will not fear when the earth is transformed, when the mountains fall into the heart of the sea.

### Believer Strength

The *Gemara* addresses a possible objection: will this hovering existence be painful or exhausting? *Talmud lomar: ve-kovei Hashem yachalifu koach, ya'alu ever ke-nesharim, yarutzu ve-lo yig'u, yelchu ve-lo yi'afu* — those who trust in God will renew their strength; they will rise with wings like eagles; they will run and not grow weary; they will walk and not grow tired.

## Ezekiel Dry Bones

### Ezekiel as Proof

The *Gemara* now raises the question: *ve-nilaf mi-meitim she-hechiya Yechezkel* — why not derive the proof for *techiyas hameisim* from the dead that Yechezkel revived? If God actually revived dry bones through Yechezkel, this would serve as perhaps the most dramatic and direct evidence that resurrection is possible — and, at least for the bones that were revived, as an actual instance of it. The *Maharsha* frames this as the question: even if Yechezkel's revival does not prove that there will be universal *techiyas hameisim* in the messianic future, it at least proves that God has the power to raise the dead from dust and bones. Why is that not invoked as evidence?

### Miracle vs. Metaphor

The answer: the *Gemara* here follows the opinion that *be-emet mashal haya* — in truth, it was only a *mashal*. The vision of the dry bones in the valley was not an actual miracle but a parable for Israel's national restoration: the people felt that their hope was lost and they were as good as dead, and the vision reassured them that God would restore them to national life. Since it was not a real event, it cannot serve as evidence for anything.

The phrase is striking: *be-emet mashal haya* — in truth it was a *mashal*. The word *be-emet* sitting next to *mashal* is almost paradoxical. Rabbi Nechemiah indeed presses on exactly this: *im emet lama mashal, ve-im mashal lama emet?* — if it was true, why call it a *mashal*, and if it was a *mashal*, why say in truth? The resolution: *ela be-emet mashal haya* — it simply means: truly, in reality, it was only a parable. Nothing more than that.

### Eliezer: Actual Event

Rabbi Eliezer, however, holds that it actually happened: *meisim she-hechiya Yechezkel amdu al ragleihem va-amru shira u-meisu* — they stood on their feet, sang praises, and then died. They were revived for the purpose of the lesson, and then died again. What *shira* did they sing? *Hashem meimis be-tzedek u-mechayeh be-rachamim* — God caused us to die with justice and revives us with compassion, for we did not deserve the resurrection.

Rabbi Yehoshua adds an alternative version of the *shira*: *Hashem meimis u-mechayeh, morid she'ol va-ya'al* — God causes death and gives life, brings down to the depths and raises up again.

### Yehuda: Pure Metaphor

Rabbi Yehuda holds: *emet mashal haya* — it was truly just a *mashal*. The vision referred to the state of the Jewish people in exile, as the verse itself confirms: *ha-atzamot ha-eleh kol beit Yisrael heimah* — these bones represent the entire house of Israel. The people said *yavshu atzmoteinu ve-avda tikvatenu nigzarnu lanu* — our bones are dried out and our hope is lost. The vision is an extended metaphor for national desolation and the promise of restoration.

### Actual Aliyah & Children

Rabbi Eliezer son of Rabbi Yose HaGlili goes furthest: *meisim she-hechiya Yechezkel alu le-Eretz Yisrael ve-nasu nashim va-olidu banim u-vanot* — the dead whom Yechezkel revived went up to the Land of Israel, married, and had children. Remarkably, Rabbi Yehuda ben Beseira rose to his feet and testified: *ani mi-bnei veneihem, ve-halu tefillin she-hiniach li avi avi me-hem* — I am among their descendants, and here are the *tefillin* that my grandfather's grandfather bequeathed to me from them.

### Ephraim Identity

Who were these dead? Rav identifies them as *benei Ephraim she-manu la-keitz ve-ta'u* — the children of Ephraim who calculated the timing of the redemption and erred, leaving Egypt prematurely and being killed in battle. The verse supports this: the genealogy in *Divrei HaYamim* traces the descendants of Ephraim through Shuthelach and records that *Ezer and Elad were killed by the men of Gat who were born in the land* — meaning that these children of Ephraim had somehow made it to the Land of Israel before the Exodus. And: *va-yitabbel Ephraim avihem yamim rabbim va-yavo'u echav le-nachamo* — Ephraim mourned for them for many days. There may also be a subtle warning embedded in this identification: so much of the surrounding discussion concerns anticipating when the *keitz* will be. The *benei Ephraim* are the paradigm case of what happens when one tries to force the end prematurely.

### Alternative Identities

Shmuel identifies them differently: *elu benei adam she-kafru bi-techiyas ha-meitim* — these were people who denied the resurrection of the dead. The entire two dappim of the Gemara have been building proofs for *techiyas hameisim*; here are the paradigmatic deniers. Why were they resurrected if denial of resurrection forfeits one's portion? Precisely to demonstrate the truth to them — they were revived as a proof, not as a reward.

Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba identifies them as *benei adam she-ein bahem lachluchit shel mitzvah* — people who have not even a trace of moisture, not even a trace of *mitzvot* within them. Completely dried out morally. And yet God revived them — perhaps demonstrating that *kol Yisrael yesh lahem chelek le-olam haba*, or perhaps they were resurrected only for the moment of judgment.

Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha identifies them as the people who covered the entire *Heichal* with images of *shekatizm u-remasim* — vermin and creeping things. The verse from Yechezkel's own vision: *va-avo va-ereh, ve-hineh kol tavnis remes u-veheimah sheketz ve-chol gillulei beit Yisrael mechukkah al ha-kir saviv saviv* — he entered and saw carved on the wall all manner of creeping things and abominations surrounding the sanctuary. The verbal parallel *saviv saviv* connects Yechezkel's vision of the defiled *Beit HaMikdash* to his vision of the valley of bones.

## Divine Vindication Narra…

### Dura Valley Resurrection

Rabbi Yochanan identifies the dead of the valley as *meitim she-be-bikat Dura* — those who died in the valley of Dura. The valley of Dura stretched from *Nahar Eshel* to *Ravat*. At the time when Nevuchadnezzar exiled Israel, there were among the exiles young men *she-hayu meganin et ha-chamah be-yofyan* — so extraordinarily beautiful that they made the sun itself look dim by comparison.

### Dead Strike King

The *Gemara* then provides the narrative framework: when Nevuchadnezzar was about to cast Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah into the furnace, God commanded Yechezkel to go and resurrect the dead in *bikat Dura*. Once he revived them, *ba'u atzamot ve-tapchu lo le-oto rasha al panav* — the resurrected dead came and slapped Nevuchadnezzar in the face, right at the moment he was presiding over the furnace. Nevuchadnezzar was bewildered: *ma tivan shel elu?* — who are these people who have walked into my presence and struck me? He was told: *chavreihen shel elu, mechayei meitim be-bikat Dura* — the friends of these three — meaning Yechezkel — has revived the dead you killed in the valley of Dura. The implicit warning was clear: you are about to throw their companions into the fire, and it will not end in your favor.

### Nebuchadnezzar Conversion

Nevuchadnezzar opened his mouth and said: *atvohi kama ravrevin, ve-timhohi kama takiffin, malchuteh malchut alam ve-shaltaneh im dar ve-dar* — how great are God's miracles, how wondrous are God's wonders, His kingdom is an eternal kingdom and His dominion endures for all generations. A wicked king, confronted with the undeniable demonstration of divine power through the resurrection of those he had slaughtered, burst forth in praise.

### Angel Intervention

Yet this moment of praise was not to be completed. Rabbi Yitzchak states: *yutzak zahav roteach le-toch piv shel oto rasha* — molten gold should have been poured down the throat of that wicked man. For *she-ilmale ba malach u-staro al panav* — had an angel not come and slapped him on the face, he would have gone on to sing God's praises so extensively and so powerfully that he would have *bikeish le-ganot kol shirot ve-tishbachot she-amar David be-sefer Tehillim* — his praises would have surpassed and thereby embarrassed all the psalms and praises that David composed in the book of Tehillim. Nevuchadnezzar was such a *rasha* that he did not deserve that opportunity. The angel intervened to stop him. His recognition of God's greatness was real — but it was cut short precisely because a man of his deeds should not be the one to attain that ultimate level of praise.
