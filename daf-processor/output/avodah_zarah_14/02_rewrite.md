# Avodah Zarah 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Prohibited Items Intro

### Business Prohibition

Daf 14 picks up with the *mishnah* at the bottom of 13b. Having addressed the prohibition of doing business with non-Jews on their holidays and market days — including the consequences of violations, such as the destruction of goods acquired through such transactions, and the related questions of deriving and conferring benefit — the *mishnah* now shifts to an entirely separate concern: selling items throughout the year that non-Jews might employ in their idolatrous practice. While the issue of animals potentially being sacrificed had already surfaced in the *gemara*'s framing of the holiday prohibition, here the concern is far more general and explicit. This section was also of considerable practical relevance to the *Rishonim*, particularly the Ashkenazi *Rishonim*, for whom trade in Christian religious objects — sold directly to the Church — was a significant commercial activity.

### Selling Idolatry Items

The *mishnah* opens: *aylu devarim assurim limkor l'ovdei kochavim* — the following things are prohibited to sell to an idolater. The items listed raise an underlying question that runs throughout the discussion: what precisely is the nature of the prohibition being enabled? Is the concern that the seller facilitates an actual act of *avodah zarah* — a forbidden act of worship — or merely that he participates in some ritual that adorns or honors the idol? For a Jew, even beautifying an idol would carry severe consequences, but it would not technically constitute the capital offense of *avodah zarah* itself; it might instead violate a *lav*. This distinction matters for understanding what the seller is enabling.

### Mishnah Items List

The prohibited items are as follows. First, *itzrublin* — a term the *gemara* will identify. Second, *bnot shuach* — which the *gemara* will explain are white figs. Third, *uptotrot* — their stems; the *gemara* will clarify that this means one may not sell these items with their stems attached, since the stems allow the buyer to string them up more elegantly before the idol. Fourth, *u-levonah* — frankincense, which constitutes an actual act of offering. Fifth, *tarnegol halvavan* — a white rooster, which was apparently used as some form of ritual sacrifice or offering.

### Frankincense & Roosters

The prohibition on frankincense is readily understood as facilitating a genuine act of worship, since burning incense is an offering in the conventional sense. The white rooster is more complex. The precise nature of what is being done with it matters: is it being brought as a sacrifice, or employed in some other ritual capacity? This connects to the broader question of whether the non-Jew is transgressing the *Bnei Noach* prohibition of *avodah zarah* at all, since the nature of what constitutes a forbidden act of worship for a non-Jew in a pagan ritual context is not entirely straightforward.

### Rav Yehuda Lenient

Rabbi Yehuda takes a more lenient position regarding white roosters: *mutar limkor lo tarnegol lavan bein ha-tarnegolim* — it is permitted to sell a white rooster mixed together with other roosters, since if the buyer is not specifically selecting the white one he may simply want it for ordinary use. However, *bizman she-hu bifnei atzmo kotei'a et etzba'o u-mochro lo* — if you are selling him only the white rooster by itself, you must first cut off its finger before selling it. The rationale: *lefi she-ein makrivin chaser l'avodat kochavim* — they do not offer up a blemished animal to their idol. Since pagans observe the same standard of an unblemished offering, removing a limb renders it unsuitable for sacrifice and therefore permissible to sell. This confirms that the concern with the white rooster is specifically about it being brought as an actual sacrifice.

### Rabbi Meir Stringent

Regarding other items, the *mishnah* states: *v'sha'ar kol ha-devarim staman mutar, peirushan assur* — for all other items, if purchased generically it is permitted; if purchased explicitly for idolatrous purposes it is forbidden. Rabbi Meir goes further and adds that one may not sell even a good palm tree (*dekel tav*), *chatzav*, and *niklas*. The identification of these items and the basis for their prohibition will be taken up in the *gemara*.

## Achutzrublin Analysis

### Achutzrublin ID

The *gemara* opens at the top of 14a with the question: *mai itztrublin?* — what is *itztrublin*? The answer given is *turnisa*. Rashi understands this to mean a cedar tree, while Tosafot identifies it as some type of mineral. An additional valuable note points out that the word can refer to rounded objects including pine cones — which were indeed closely associated with Asclepius, the Greek god of medicine, whose iconography typically depicts him holding a staff topped with a pine cone. Pine cones are large, symmetrical, and visually striking, which makes them fitting objects to hang in a ritual context. This identification also helps explain the subsequent *gemara*.

### Shemitah Rules

The *gemara* raises a contradiction: *u-rminhu* — a *baraita* teaches that the following four items were later added to the list of things to which *shemitah* applies: *alachsin*, *itztrublin*, *machsasin*, and *bnot shuach*. Now, if *itztrublin* is a mineral, as Tosafot suggests, it cannot be subject to *shemitah*, since *shemitah* applies only to things that grow from the ground. A *baraita* states: *zeh ha-klal, kol she-yesh lo ikar yesh lo shvi'it, v'chol she-ein lo ikar ein lo shvi'it* — anything that has roots is subject to *shemitah*; anything that does not, is not. A mineral has no roots. The *gemara* therefore concludes: *ela amar Rav Safra peirei d'arza* — rather, *itztrublin* is the fruit of the cedar tree. This is confirmed: *v'chen ki ata Ravin amar Rabbi Elazar peirei d'arza*. Rashi identifies this as *golna*. The question of why it was not initially included in the *shemitah* list likely relates to uncertainty about when exactly it grew — whether it was sufficiently edible, or whether its growth spanned years in a way that made its *shemitah* status unclear. Tosafot discusses this at length. In the end, whatever its original status, it was eventually included. If this is indeed pine cones, one can understand why they might not have been initially included — pine cones are not truly edible — yet their eventual inclusion reflects their use in ritual contexts, including the stringing discussed in the *mishnah*.

### Bnot Shuach ID

Regarding *bnot shuach*: *amar Rabbah bar bar Chana amar Rabbi Yochanan te'enei chivarta* — white figs. These appear frequently in the *gemara*, always accompanied by lengthy Tosafot discussions, because different passages treat them differently: some suggest they were unpalatable or not truly edible, while others describe them as a delicacy. Tosafot endeavors to reconcile these by positing that there may be different varieties. The relevance of their marginal edibility is that it strengthens the presumption of idolatrous use: if these items are not commonly purchased for food, the most plausible reason someone is buying them is for their ritual use before an idol.

### Stems Prohibition

The *mishnah*'s mention of *ptutrot* is clarified by Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *biptutroteihen shanu* — the *mishnah* does not refer to stems as a separate prohibited item, but rather teaches that these items — *itztrublin* and *bnot shuach* — may not be sold with their stems attached.

### Worship Act

Rashi explains why: *ein mochrin lahen kol eilu itztrublin u-venot shuach be-uktzeihen, lefi she-tolin otam bifnei avodat kochavim* — they would string these objects before the idol. One might ask: is stringing items before an idol merely a decorative ritual, or does it constitute an actual act of worship? Rashi addresses this with three decisive words: *ve-darkam le-ovdan be-chach* — for that particular form of *avodah zarah*, this stringing was considered an act of worship, not merely an aesthetic gesture. This is significant: the framework being applied is not just that the seller participates in some loosely religious act, but that he actively facilitates a specific recognized mode of idol worship.

## Frankincense & Bulk

### Frankincense Pure

The *gemara* records that Rabbi Yitzchak said in the name of Rabbi Shimon Lakish: *levonah zakkah* — pure frankincense is what is prohibited.

### Bulk Sales

The *tanna* teaches: *u-mikulan mochrin lahen chavilah* — but from all of these items one may sell in bulk. The rationale is that a large wholesale quantity is being sold to a middleman who will then retail it; the seller is not directly supplying someone who will use it for *avodah zarah*. The *gemara* asks: *ve-kamah chavilah?* — how large is a *chavilah*? Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira specifies: *ein chavilah pachot mi-shlosha manin* — no less than three *manin*, a substantial quantity. But then the *gemara* presses: *ve-chuish shema azil u-mazvin le-achrin kach?* — should we not be concerned that he will go and sell it to others who will then use it? In other words, the ultimate consumer will still acquire the incense for *avodah zarah*. Is the wholesaler not still causally connected to that sin?

### Lifnei Iver Degrees

Abaye resolves this with a principle that is foundational for the entire sugya: *alifnei mefaktinan, alifnei de-lifnei lo mefaktinan* — we are commanded regarding the prohibition of *lifnei iver*, do not place a stumbling block before the blind; we are not commanded regarding one step further removed — that is, we are not prohibited from placing a stumbling block before someone whose own wrongdoing will be to place a stumbling block before someone else. The prohibition of *lifnei iver* extends only one degree; it does not reach two degrees of separation.

The *acharonim* raise an interesting refinement of this principle. The bulk-sale scenario involves two non-Jews: the wholesaler who buys from the Jew, and the end-user who buys from that wholesaler. Non-Jews are not included in the prohibition of *lifnei iver* vis-à-vis other non-Jews — the seven Noahide laws do not prohibit a non-Jew from causing another non-Jew to sin in this way. One might therefore suggest that a Jew could sell to another Jew who would then sell to a non-Jew, arguing that the Jew's violation of *lifnei iver* by selling to the non-Jew places the original seller only two degrees removed. But this logic fails: while the non-Jewish intermediary does not transgress *lifnei iver*, the Jewish intermediary does. Therefore, selling to a Jew who will then facilitate a non-Jew's sin may constitute a *lifnei iver* violation after all — not with respect to the non-Jew's sin, but with respect to the Jew's transgression of *lifnei iver*. The operative rule — that two degrees of removal are permissible — applies when both intermediaries are non-Jews. This is the accepted *halacha*.

## White Roosters

### Generic vs Specific

Returning to the white rooster: Rabbi Yochanan, cited through a chain of *amoraim*, clarifies the basic rule. *Tarnigol le-mi* — if the non-Jew goes around asking generically, "Who has a rooster? Anyone selling roosters?" — then one may sell him a white rooster. *Tarnigol lavna mi?* — if he goes around specifically asking, "Who has white roosters?" — then *assur limkor lo tarnegol lavan*, it is forbidden to sell him a white rooster.

### Zeh V'zeh Case

This framework raises a difficulty with the *mishnah* itself. The *mishnah* presents Rabbi Yehuda's ruling that one may sell a white rooster *bein ha-tarnegolim* — mixed together with other roosters. The *gemara* asks: *heichi dami?* — what is the case? If the non-Jew is explicitly asking for white roosters (*tarnegol lavan le-mi*), then even selling it mixed with others should be forbidden — the fact that other roosters accompany the sale does not diminish the clarity of his intent. But if he is asking generically (*tarnegol le-mi*), then according to the framework just established, it should be permitted for everyone, not just Rabbi Yehuda. So what is the *mishnah*'s intermediate case? The *gemara* concludes: *kegon de-amar zeh v'zeh* — the case is where he said "this one and this one."

### Tosafot Reading

Rashi explains *zeh v'zeh* as referring to a buyer who asks for roosters of multiple colors — white, black, and red — such that his mention of white does not indicate a specific ritual focus. Since he is seeking variety, Rabbi Yehuda reasons one may assume he is not interested in white for *avodah zarah* purposes. The *Tanna Kamma* disagrees, holding that once white is explicitly named, the sale is forbidden even mixed with others. Tosafot offers what is arguably a more compelling reading of the language, and one that fits more naturally with the *gemara*'s continuation. According to Tosafot, the buyer does not verbally request colors at all. Instead, he walks up to the seller, looks over the roosters, and says: "I'll take that one — and that one — and that one," pointing to individual birds. Among those he points to, one happens to be white. The question is whether the fact that he selects a white rooster by pointing is sufficiently concerning when he has simultaneously pointed to other roosters. The *Tanna Kamma* holds that since he specifically pointed to the white one, even with the others, nothing may be sold to him. Rabbi Yehuda holds that since he pointed to multiple roosters without any verbal expression of preference for white, one may sell him the whole group together. This reading preserves the structure: explicitly asking for white is *assur* for everyone; asking generically is *mutar* for everyone; pointing to this one and that one is the disputed middle case.

### Context Exceptions

A *baraita* reinforces Rabbi Yehuda's position and introduces contextual factors. *Amar Rabbi Yehuda eimatai* — when is selling a white rooster forbidden? *Bizman she-amar tarnegol zeh lavan* — when he points to a specific rooster and says "that white one." *Aval im amar zeh v'zeh* — but if he says "this one and this one," even if one is white, it is *mutar* according to Rabbi Yehuda. The *baraita* then adds that even when a non-Jew does say "that specific one" and it happens to be white, context can render the sale permissible: *oved kochavim she-asa mishta livno* — if you know he is making a feast for his son, or *o she-haya lo choleh betoch beito* — if there is a sick person in his house, then even an explicit selection of a white rooster is *mutar*. The rationale is that a plausible alternative purpose exists — food for a celebration or for an ill person — and when the context clearly points to a non-ritual use, the sale is permitted. This is within Rabbi Yehuda's more lenient framework, and the question of how certain one needs to be, and how much weight alternative explanations carry, is left with some ambiguity.

### Tavzig Definition

The *gemara* then questions this: is not a *mishta livno* — a feast for one's son — itself associated with *avodah zarah*, since betrothing or marrying off a son was listed among the occasions on which doing business with a non-Jew is restricted? A *mishnah* specifies that for such an occasion only that individual on that day is restricted: *oto hayom ve-oto ish*. Rabbi Yitzchak bar Abba in the name of Rabbah resolves this: *be-tavzig tnan* — the *mishnah* here is speaking of a *tavzig*, which Rashi identifies as a generic informal festive meal, not a wedding feast. The word itself is of Middle Iranian origin and refers to something like a casual celebratory meal or picnic. Since such a meal is not the kind of event that carries an *avodah zarah* dimension, the contextual inference of non-idolatrous use is valid.

## Generic vs Explicit

### Other Items Rule

The *mishnah* states: *u-v'she'ar kol ha-devarim staman mutar, peirushan assur* — regarding all other items, generic purchases are permitted and explicit ones are forbidden.

### Gemara Challenge

The *gemara* asks: *mai stam u-mai perush?* — what exactly constitutes a generic purchase and what constitutes an explicit one in this context? It considers an initial reading: *stam* means the buyer asks simply for something ordinary without specifying type (analogous to asking for generic wheat), while *perush* means he explicitly declares his intention to use the item for *avodah zarah*. But under this reading, says the *gemara*, neither ruling would be a *chiddush*. If someone merely asks for wheat, it is obviously permissible to sell it — there is no reason to suspect idolatrous intent from an unspecified request. And if someone explicitly announces that he is purchasing for *avodah zarah*, it is obviously forbidden — no teaching is needed. *Lo stam tzricha lemeimar de-mezabninan, ve-lo perush tzricha lemeimar de-lo mezabninan* — neither the permission nor the prohibition is novel under this reading.

### Wheat Inference

The *gemara* therefore proposes a different reading: *stam* refers to a generic request (simply "wheat"), while *perush* refers to a suspiciously specific request — asking for something unusual in its detail without verbally invoking *avodah zarah*, such as asking specifically for white wheat. Under this reading, the *mishnah* teaches that even such a specific, peculiar request is permitted so long as *avodah zarah* is not explicitly invoked. That is the *chiddush* of the permissive ruling. And then: *miklal de-tarnigol afilu stama nami lo* — from this it follows that regarding a white rooster, even a generic request should be forbidden. Here is the logic: if the *mishnah*'s prohibition for non-rooster items requires something specific but not fully explicit, and that is what is forbidden, then for a rooster — which is mentioned as a special case — even a generic request must be sufficient to trigger the prohibition. Otherwise, why would the *mishnah* treat roosters differently?

### Rooster Always Forbidden

This analysis seems to contradict the earlier rule that a generic request for a rooster is permitted. The *gemara* resolves the contradiction: *amar lei l'olam d'stama d'ka'amar chitei chivarta* — actually, *stam* in the *mishnah*'s general rule does mean a fully generic unspecific request (just "wheat"), and *perush* does mean being truly explicit about *avodah zarah*. The *chiddush* of the prohibition, however, is not obvious: *salka datach amina, hai gavra lav l'avodah zarah ka ba'ei* — one might have thought that even when a person says "I need this for my *avodah zarah* ritual," perhaps he does not really mean it. After all, why would he tell the Jewish storekeeper this?

### Really Explicit

The answer involves a striking psychological observation. *Ella mivzach hu d'ka vazich b'avodah zarah* — this person is utterly consumed by his *avodah zarah*, he is passionately cleaved to his idol-worship.

### Obsessed Logic

*Ki heichi d'hu gavra avik bah, kuli alma nami aviki* — because he himself is so obsessed with his idolatrous practice, he assumes everyone else is equally enthusiastic. Just as a person deeply consumed by any obsession tends to project that enthusiasm onto others, this idolater figures the Jewish seller must be equally enamored of his god. He therefore assumes that announcing his *avodah zarah* purpose will ingratiate him with the seller, or perhaps earn him a discount: *ki heichi d'lozeil lei* — so that the seller will give it to him cheaply, since surely everyone loves the idol as much as he does. The *gemara* therefore rules that we do not invoke this psychological theory to dismiss his explicit declaration. If he says *avodah zarah*, we take him at his word.

The underlying structure of the *mishnah*'s rules can then be summarized: a generic request for a white rooster is *assur*, a generic request for an unspecified item is *mutar*, and only when the buyer truly explicitly declares an idolatrous purpose does the prohibition on non-rooster items apply — and even that declaration, despite its apparent transparency, requires a ruling not to explain it away.

## Context & Broken Limbs

### Broken Leg Case

Rav Ashi poses a case: *tarnigol lavan katu'a li* — a non-Jew requests a white rooster with a broken leg. This is an unusual specification. On one hand, asking for a damaged bird suggests he is not using it for *avodah zarah*, since blemished animals are disqualified from ritual sacrifice. On the other hand, he is still asking specifically for a white one. The question: *mahu limkor lo tarnigol lavan shalem* — may one then sell him a whole, unblemished white rooster?

### Scheming Concern

The *gemara* formulates the two sides: *mi amrina mikdi amar katu'a, le-avodah zarah lo kaba'ei lei* — perhaps since he asked for one with a broken leg, he clearly does not intend to use it for *avodah zarah*, and one may sell him a whole one. *O dilma arumay hu de-ka-arim* — or perhaps he is scheming: he knows that if he asks for a blemished rooster, the seller will be reassured and offer him a whole one. The broken-leg request is a stratagem to circumvent the prohibition.

### Multiple Purchases

A related scenario follows. A non-Jew goes around asking for a white rooster. One seller offers him a black one — he takes it. Another offers him a red one — *ve-yahavu lei adu ve-shaklu* — he takes that too. Having accepted non-white roosters, may one now sell him a white rooster? *Mi amrina keivan de-yahavi shahor ve-shakal, adu ve-shakal, lo le-avodah zarah kaba'ei* — since he accepted red and black roosters without objection, perhaps his interest in white is merely aesthetic rather than ritual. *O dilma arumay hu de-ka-arim* — or perhaps here too he is scheming: he is buying whatever is offered, working his way through the market, until someone finally sells him a white one.

### Teiku Unresolved

The *gemara* leaves both questions unresolved: *teiku*. It remains uncertain whether these contextual indicators — asking for a blemished animal or accepting non-white alternatives — are sufficient to permit selling a whole white rooster. The cases illustrate the broader principle, raised earlier in discussion, that contextual clues can cut in both directions: they can indicate non-idolatrous intent and thereby permit a sale, or they can raise suspicion of scheming and thereby forbid one.

## Rabbi Meir Stringency

### Meir's Items

Returning to the *mishnah*: *Rabbi Meir omer af dekel tav, chatzav ve-niklas assur limkor le-ovdei kochavim* — Rabbi Meir prohibits selling even *dekel tav*, *chatzav*, and *niklas* to idolaters, beyond what the *Tanna Kamma* prohibits.

### Hisda Statement

Rav Hisda said to Avimi: *gmiri de-avodah zarah de-Avraham Avinu arba me'ah pirkai hava* — there is a tradition that Avraham Avinu's *masechet Avodah Zarah* had four hundred chapters. We have a mere five chapters in our tractate, *ve-lo yadainan mai ka'amrinan* — and we do not even properly understand what our five chapters are describing.

### Ignorance Theme

The remark is triggered by the very prohibitions Rabbi Meir lists: *u-mai kashya de-tnan Rabbi Meir omer af dekel tav chatzav ve-niklas assur limkor le-ovdei kochavim* — what is Rav Hisda's exasperation about? He cannot make sense of the *dekel tav* prohibition: *dekel tav u-dela mizdavni mizvninan?* — can we sell them a bad palm tree but not a good one? And *ve-hatnan ein mochrin lahem be-mechubar le-karka* — but we have already taught that one may not sell them anything attached to the ground, which covers all trees regardless. The inability to explain *why* specifically these items are singled out reflects a broader ignorance of the specific idolatrous practices involved. If we had four hundred chapters of detailed knowledge of pagan ritual as Avraham Avinu did, we would presumably understand exactly which practices required which items and why.

### Fruits Not Trees

The *gemara* resolves the *dekel tav* difficulty: *mai dekel tav, peiros dekel tav* — *dekel tav* does not mean a palm tree itself; it means the fruit of a *dekel tav* — a particular species of palm tree, or a particularly fine variety. Rav Huna confirms: *peiros dekel tav*. This resolves the apparent tension with the prohibition of selling anything attached to the ground, which applies to the tree itself. What Rabbi Meir prohibits is the *fruit* of this species, not the tree.

### Translation Problem

Regarding *chatzav*: the *gemara* identifies it as *kispa*, which Rashi suggests may be some type of grass. Regarding *niklas*: when Abaye pressed Rav Dimi on the matter, saying *tnan niklas ve-lo yadainan mahu* — the *mishnah* says *niklas* and I do not know what it is — and Rav Dimi responded *kuraiti*, Abaye replied: *ve-lo yadainan mai ahanis lan* — how does it help me? You have translated one unknown term into another unknown term. Rav Dimi explains the practical value: *de-chi azlas hasam amras lehu niklas ve-lo yadai, ve-amras lehu kuraiti, ve-yadai, u-machavu lach* — when you travel to Eretz Yisrael and ask people what *niklas* is, they may not know. But if you then say *kuraiti*, they will recognize it and show it to you. Having two names for an unknown item doubles your chance of identifying it through local knowledge.

### Niklas Dates Origin

A note clarifies: *niklas* refers to a superior species of date named after a philosopher — Niklas — who used to send these dates from Syria to Augustus Caesar in Rome. The *Rambam* may have had a variant reading *niklavon* in place of *niklas*.

## Tosafot Christian

### Frankincense Priests

Tosafot (the *tosafot* beginning with the asterisk that reads *de-avodas kochavim de-Avraham Avinu*) quotes Rabbeinu Baruch, who draws the following practical application. Since the *mishnah* establishes that items explicitly associated with *avodah zarah* worship are forbidden, it follows that: *assur le-adam limkor le-komer oved kochavim levonah, de-havei kemo perusham* — it is forbidden to sell frankincense to a Christian priest, because doing so is equivalent to an explicit case. *De-davar barur hu de-likrovas ba'ei lei* — it is obvious that he intends to use it as an offering, for burning incense. *Ve-over mishum lifnei iver* — the seller thereby transgresses *lifnei iver*. One might note that the *mishnah* itself already lists *levonah* as a prohibited item, so why does Rabbeinu Baruch need to say this? The answer may be that one might have thought the *mishnah*'s prohibition reflects the practices of ancient paganism, not necessarily applicable to contemporary Christian ritual. Alternatively, the point is that even ordinary individuals who sell incense might assume that since people commonly burn incense for fragrance in their homes, the prohibition does not apply to a general sale. The priest's context, however, removes all ambiguity: when the buyer is a priest, the purpose is clear, and the sale falls squarely within the prohibition.

### Wax Candles

Tosafot continues: *ve-gam assur limkor sha'avah le-shum oved kochavim be-yom eid ha-hu* — it is also forbidden to sell wax to any non-Jew on their particular holiday, since on that occasion wax candles are burned before icons and statues in a form of worship. *Aval vadai be-sha'ar yamim mutar* — but on other days it is plainly permitted. The operative principle is that the known use to which the item will be put determines permissibility: on days when the purpose is certain, the sale is forbidden; on ordinary days, when no such certainty exists, it is allowed.

### Damaged Vessels

Tosafot then addresses used ritual vessels: *ve-chein otam klei avodat kochavim she-Yisrael koneh le-achar she-pigmo oved kochavim u-bitlo* — if a Christian has damaged or invalidated a ritual vessel such as a chalice and a Jew purchases it, the Jew may not then resell it to another Christian as-is. *Assur limkor le-oved kochavim kemo she-hu, ad she-yinatei'ach kulo* — it is forbidden to resell it in its current state; it must first be completely melted down. *U-vishvil pgimah achat eino mani'ach le-hativ yayin le-khales avodas kochavim* — a single dent will not prevent them from using it as a chalice in their ritual. The formal act of invalidation by the prior owner is insufficient; only total destruction of the vessel's form removes it from the category of ritual object.

### Sacred Texts

Tosafot extends the principle further: *ve-hachi nami sefarim pesulim ha-re'uyim le-tefillah be-veit avodat kochavim* — books suitable for use in church prayer are similarly prohibited. Tosafot uses the dysphemistic *beit tiflah* — house of nonsense — rather than *beit tefillah*, a standard Talmudic practice when referring to houses of idolatrous worship. One might think that a Bible or *Tehillim* could be sold to a Christian layman for personal study at home. Tosafot rules this too is forbidden: *de-afilu oved kochavim she-eino komer assur, de-vaday yehenenu o yimkerenu le-komer* — even a Christian who is not a priest may not be sold such books, because he will certainly either donate it or sell it to a priest for use in church. The *lifnei iver* prohibition therefore applies even through the layman-to-priest chain, not merely in direct sales to clergy. This encompasses incense, candles, chalices, and liturgical texts — all items used in Christian worship — as the subject of a unified prohibition.

### Christianity AZ

The framework underlying all of these rulings is explicit: Tosafot is treating Christianity as *avodah zarah*. The items listed — frankincense, votive candles, the chalice used in the Eucharist, books chanted or read in church — are all understood as instruments of actual idolatrous worship, not merely cultural or aesthetic objects. This raises the obvious question of how Ashkenazi *Rishonim* conducted trade in precisely these objects throughout the medieval period. Two answers developed in the *Rishonim*. One is the position — not reflected in this Tosafot — that Christianity does not constitute *avodah zarah* for non-Jews; since Christians are not commanded against *avodah zarah* in the same way Jews are, facilitating their worship does not constitute *lifnei iver* in the relevant sense. The second answer emerges from the very next *mishnah*, and from the subsequent *gemara* and Tosafot discussion.

## Small Animals

### Custom Determines

The *mishnah* states: *makom she-nahagu limkor behema daka le-ovdei kochavim, mochrin; makom she-nahagu shelo limkor, ein mochrin* — where local custom permits selling small animals (sheep and goats) to non-Jews, one may sell; where custom prohibits it, one may not. In every place, however, large animals (*behema gassa*) — cows and horses, as well as calves (*agalim*) and foals (*sayachim*), whether whole or injured — may not be sold to non-Jews. Rabbi Yehuda permits selling an animal with a broken limb, and Ben Beteira permits selling a horse. The *gemara* will explain that the concern with large animals relates to the risk that permitting sale will lead to lending or renting them to non-Jews, who may then use them for labor on *Shabbat*, violating the prohibition against a Jew's animal working on *Shabbat*. The concerns around horses and broken-limbed animals relate to whether those cases present a genuine *Shabbat* risk.

### No Issurah

The *gemara* states directly: *de-issura leika* — there is no inherent prohibition on selling small animals to non-Jews. *Minhaga eika* — the only operative factor is *minhag*. Where the custom is restrictive, it applies; where it is permissive, the permissive practice governs.

### Bestiality Baraita

This requires reconciliation with a *baraita*: *ein ma'amidin behema be-pundeka'ot shel ovdei kochavim, mipnei she-chashudim al ha-revia* — one may not leave one's animal in the inn-stable of non-Jews, because they are suspected of committing acts of bestiality. This implies an inherent prohibition on facilitating contact between non-Jews and Jewish-owned animals, which would seem to preclude selling small animals on the same grounds. If they will do this to animals left at an inn, why would selling them an animal outright be any less problematic?

### Rava Connection

Rava resolves the apparent contradiction: *makom she-hitiru limkor, hitiru leyached; makom she-asru leyached, asru limkor* — the two prohibitions are linked and geographically determined. In places where small animals may be sold to non-Jews, it is because those communities do not suspect local non-Jews of bestiality, and in those same places it is also permitted to leave one's animal unattended with a non-Jew. In places where leaving animals unattended with non-Jews is prohibited — because the local population is suspected of such conduct — selling small animals to them is also prohibited. The *minhag* governing sales and the *minhag* governing unattended animals are one and the same in each locale.

### Eliezer Lenient

Rabbi Eliezer takes a more nuanced position: *af makom she-asru leyached, mutar limkor* — even in places where leaving an animal alone with a non-Jew is forbidden, selling it to him is permitted. The distinction: *akum chas al behemto shelo te'aker* — a non-Jew is protective of his own animal, fearing that acts of bestiality will render the animal sterile. He will do such acts to your animal, not to his own. Therefore, selling an animal to him transfers ownership to him, and he will then guard it as his own — which is precisely what inhibits the act. Leaving your animal with him is different, since your animal remains yours and his restraint does not operate. Rabbi Eliezer thus permits sale everywhere while maintaining the universal prohibition on leaving animals unattended with non-Jews.

## Tosafot Bestiality

### Tosafot LI

Tosafot on *makom she-nahagu shelo limkor ein mochrin* notes: *peirush mishum chashash revia, ve-amra al lifnei iver* — the prohibition on selling is grounded in the concern for bestiality, and the seller transgresses *lifnei iver*. Tosafot then observes that the *mishnah* draws no distinction between cases where the non-Jew already has his own animal and cases where he does not. This is notable: *alma mashma de-afilu it lei le-akum behema achrinita, assur* — even if the non-Jew already possesses other animals of his own, selling him another is still prohibited as *lifnei iver*.

### No Distinction

This is a striking ruling. The standard analysis of *lifnei iver* holds that if someone can commit the transgression without your assistance — because they already possess the means to do so — you do not transgress by providing them with additional means, since you have not truly placed a stumbling block before them. Yet here the *mishnah* seems to prohibit the sale regardless.

### Chananel Q

Rabbenu Chananel poses this challenge explicitly: *mai shena de-hacha gabi tikrovet de-it lei de-hava behema shari, ve-leish mishum lifnei iver?* — in the prior discussion regarding bulk sales of *avodah zarah* items, it appeared that if the non-Jew already has access to what he needs, there is no *lifnei iver* violation. Why should the animal case differ?

### Desire Answer

Rav Yehuda answers: *d'shani revia she-mitaveh le-chol behema va-behema* — bestiality is different, because the desire attaches to each individual animal. A non-Jew's possession of his own animals does not eliminate his desire for every additional animal he encounters. The inhibition that operates regarding his own animals — the concern for sterility — is precisely what redirects that desire toward others' animals. Therefore, selling him yet another animal does place an additional stumbling block before him, since his desire is not satisfied by what he already has.

### Access Logic

This analysis becomes directly relevant to the question of selling Christian ritual objects. The *lifnei iver* framework suggests that if a person can access a forbidden item independently — because other sellers supply it freely — then one does not transgress by selling it to him. If Rabbenu Chananel's question is valid — that *lifnei iver* does not apply when the person already has the means available — then it follows that selling a chalice or frankincense to a Christian priest is permissible if he can readily obtain these items from other sources, whether other Jewish merchants or non-Jewish suppliers. One is not placing a stumbling block before the blind if the path is already open to him.

### Irony Explained

The irony of this logic, however, is not lost. If the entire Jewish merchant community dealing in a particular market all justify their individual sales on the grounds that the buyer could simply purchase from any of the others, then collectively they are supplying the entire demand — yet each one individually maintains his exemption from *lifnei iver* by pointing to the others. Every seller can say: if not from me, he'll buy from you. But if all the sellers in the market are Jewish, no one is truly creating the availability that each cites as his justification; they are all creating it together while each one disclaims responsibility. This becomes the second major basis on which the *Rishonim* permitted trade in Christian ritual objects — distinct from, and arguably in tension with, the approach that denies Christianity the status of *avodah zarah* altogether for purposes of this prohibition.
