# Tamid 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Prayer & Korban

### Review & Context

We resume from the bottom of 32b, picking up at the beginning of the fifth *perek*. To orient ourselves: the *kohanim* have already completed the *terumat hadeshen*, the *dishun* of the *mizbe'ach hapnimi* and the *menorah* — removing ashes and performing the initial cleaning — and then proceeded to the *shechita* of the *tamid*, the sprinkling of the blood, the binding and dismembering of the animal, and finally the bringing of its parts halfway up the ramp. With those stages complete, the *kohanim* gathered in the *lishkat hagazit* for a further lottery — but before that lottery, they first paused to daven.

### Kriat Shema

That brief service is what the first *mishnah* described: *kriat shema*, followed by three *brachot* — *emet ve-yatziv*, which is the blessing of *ga'al yisrael*, and two blessings drawn from the *shemoneh esrei*, one concerning the *avodah* and one concerning *birkat kohanim* (not the actual *birkat kohanim* itself). There was also a special blessing recited on *Shabbat* by the departing priestly division for the entering one.

### Prayer Framing

This interweaving of *tefillah* with *korbanot* is theologically significant and deserves careful attention. The *mefaresh* here — the Pseudo-Rashi — emphasizes at the close of the previous *perek* that the reason they went to the *lishkat hagazit* to recite *shema* was, in his words, *kedei she-yitpallelu kodem she-yaktiru shum haktar ya'alu k'vanam le-rei'ach nicho'ach* — that it is fitting they should pray before any *haktar* ascends, so that their intention should rise up as a pleasing fragrance. The blood of the *tamid* had already been sprinkled, but the smoke had not yet gone up; the *ketoret* had not yet been offered. All of that is still ahead. And they wanted to frame it with *tefillah*.

What does it mean to frame a *korban* with *tefillah*? The simple reading is that the *korban* is the center of gravity, and the words of prayer give it meaning — just as one who brings a *chatat* lays his hands on it and recites a *viduy*, or as the Rambam suggests that one who brings a *shelamim* would lay hands and speak words of praise. Words frame the significance of the act. And the two *brachot* the *kohanim* recited — beyond *ga'al yisrael*, which accompanies the *shema* — were specifically about the *avodat hakorbanot* being accepted and about the *birkat kohanim* they were about to perform. These are not independent spiritual exercises; they are precisely calibrated to the service at hand.

Yet even granting all of this, even if the *tefillah* here is "merely" a frame, its framing role is itself of tremendous consequence. The *korbanot* do not, as it were, speak for themselves. Their meaning, and the manner in which they ascend, is bound up with the words one says and the intention one brings. The recognition that *tefillot* preceded *korbanot* — that almost the first thing that happens is prayer — is a critical point.

### Antiquity of Prayer

This further implies something about the antiquity of *tefillot* themselves. The *nusach* of *emet ve-yatziv* already reflects the classical form of that *bracha*, and the structure mirrors the *shemoneh esrei*. It was only the *kohanim* themselves who were praying here — they were the ones bringing the *korbanot* — but the institution of *tefillah* as something that corresponds to and accompanies the *avodah* is clearly ancient. In *Ta'anit*, the discussion of the *ma'amadot* — the representatives of *yisrael* who traveled to the *mikdash* in rotation, twenty-four groups corresponding to the twenty-four priestly divisions — shows that even the *yisraelim* who remained behind had special Torah readings and forms of communal expression. *Tehillim*, for its part, functioned as an ancient prayer-book of sorts: before *tefillot* were formalized, one would select the appropriate psalm for the moment. Prayer as a profound human spiritual need goes back to *Avraham*, or earlier still to *Noach*. What this *mishnah* implicitly addresses is when it became more statutory, when it acquired a fixed form — and that question is very much alive in these lines.

## Ketoret Lottery

### Lottery Call

The *mishnah* continues. The *memuneh* — the appointed officer, the floor manager responsible for distributing and overseeing all of the assignments — now addresses the assembled *kohanim* in the *lishkat hagazit* following their *davening*: *chadashim la-ketoret* — anyone who has not yet had the merit of offering the *ketoret*, come and participate in the lottery. The offering of *ketoret* was regarded as a *segulah* for wealth, and as a result, a *kohen* was permitted to do it only once. Given that most *kohanim* came to the *mikdash* only twice a year outside of the *regalim*, and that there were a very large number of *kohanim*, many would never have had the opportunity even once. So the lottery was called among those who had not yet had their turn, and *zacha mi shezacha* — whoever won it, won it.

### Sequential Lotteries

Then the *memuneh* called for a second lottery: *chadashim im yeshanim* — both those new to the task and those who had done it before — *mi ma'aleh eivarim min hakeves lamizbe'ach?* Who will bring the limbs of the *tamid* from the midpoint of the ramp to the top? The pieces had been laid at the lower half of the ramp; a fresh lottery now determined who would bring them the rest of the way. The *meforshim* explain that this second lottery, layered atop the first, was specifically a matter of *kavod*. By splitting the task into stages and conducting separate lotteries for each, the process drew attention to itself and gave it weight. Had the entire carrying of the limbs up the ramp been done in a single unbroken motion, it would have been over before one realized it. The multiplication of lotteries and participants transforms what might be a brief act into something charged with honor.

### R. Eliezer View

One might naturally wonder whether this arrangement is not rather forced — surely whoever began the task should finish it? Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov holds precisely this view: *ha-ma'aleh eivarim la-keves hu ma'aleh otam al gabei hamizbe'ach* — whoever brought the limbs to the halfway point of the ramp is the one who brings them the rest of the way. He does acknowledge two distinct stages, but he holds that a separate lottery for the second stage is unnecessary. The principle he invokes is: *hitchil be-mitzva omrim lo gemor* — if one has begun a *mitzva*, we tell him to complete it.

## Garment Storage

### Change of Clothes

The next *mishnah* presents what is, frankly, a puzzling arrangement. Now that various *kohanim* have completed their assigned tasks, the *mishnah* says: *masru la-chazzanim* — they were handed over to the *chazzanim*. The precise status of the *chazzanim* is not entirely clear — they may be a position of authority or, given that they are here assisting the *kohanim* in undressing, perhaps more of an attendant role. What is clear is that the *chazzanim* would undress the *kohanim* who were not currently assigned to the next stage, removing their *bigdei kehunah*, and leaving them with only their *michnasayim* — the priestly breeches. Then, in a sequence described by the *gemara*, the *kohen* would put on his non-priestly garment — a cloak or toga — and remove his *michnasayim* from underneath, subsequently changing into his ordinary undergarments. So there would be no moment of actual undress.

This raises an immediate question: why are they changing out of their *bigdei kehunah* at all? They are not done for the day. They will need to return. The most plausible answer is connected to the principle of *bigdei kehunah nitnu li-henot bahem* — whether *kohanim* are permitted to wear their vestments for personal enjoyment or leisure. If they were going to take a break, perhaps walk around *har habayit*, they could not do so in *bigdei kehunah*. It is, nonetheless, a striking arrangement — one would have expected them simply to stand and wait.

### Kevach System

*V'hachalonot hayu sham* — and there were small cubbies, *chalomot*, in that space. *V'chatuv aleihem tashmish ha-keilim* — and written on them was the designation of use: one marked for hats, one for breeches, one for cloaks. So there was an organized storage system for the priestly garments of those not currently serving.

### Cubby Details

A closer look raises interesting practical questions. The *gemara* establishes the principle *mido bad she-hi ke-midato* — that priestly garments had to be fitted to the *kohen's* individual measurements, at least in the case of the tunic and its length. If each garment was custom-fitted, some system of individual assignment would have been necessary. Perhaps the cubbies were organized by size — like, one might imagine, a bowling alley shoe counter where one requests a particular size. The hats may be another matter; whether the principle of individual measurement applied equally to headgear is less clear. In any case, each *kohen* retained his own undergarments — and whatever the precise system, the interval between the change of clothes and the next stage of service was apparently not very long, given that we will soon see the *kohanim* summoned back by the sound of the *magreifa*.

## Ketoret Vessels

### Kaf Description

The *mishnah* now turns to the person who won the lottery for the *ketoret*: *mi she-zacha ba-ketoret haya notel et ha-kaf* — he would take the *kaf*, a ladle or deep vessel. *V'ha-kaf domeh le-tarkav gadol* — it resembled a large *tarkav*, which is a two-*kav* carrying container. Imagining it as something between a ladle and a pot — with depth and a handle — captures the idea. *Shel zahav* — it was made of gold. *U-machazeket shloshet kavin* — its capacity was three *kavin* of *ketoret*.

### Bazach Structure

The *ketoret* itself, however, was not placed directly in this larger vessel. *V'ha-bazach haya be-tocho* — the *bazach*, a smaller vessel, was nested inside it. *Malei ve-gadush ketoret* — the *bazach* was filled and overflowing, heaped up with a mound of *ketoret*. The nesting arrangement served a clear purpose: the *bazach* was piled so high that the *ketoret* might spill during transport, and the larger *kaf* caught any overflow. One might ask why the *ketoret* was not simply placed in the larger vessel in the first place. The answer is *kavod*: it is more impressive to display a vessel entirely full and overflowing than to have a modest quantity lying at the bottom of a larger container. The aesthetics of abundance and fullness were themselves a form of honor.

*V'chisuy haya lo* — there was also a cover. Exactly which vessel the cover belonged to is not entirely clear from the text. And one might ask how a cover can fit over a vessel that is overflowing — perhaps it was a flat cover, or perhaps some form of dome.

### Metultelet

*U-che-min metultelet haya alav mi-lma'alah* — and there was something called a *metultelet* on top. What is a *metultelet*? Opinions differ. Some say it was a metal ring handle, analogous to the handle on a pot lid. Others say it was a cloth covering — perhaps what we might call a pot-holder, though the *ketoret* was not itself hot. The Rambam reads the text differently from either of these: he understands the *chisuy* and the *metultelet* as two distinct items — a cover for the inner *bazach*, and then a cloth covering over the larger *kaf*. On the Rambam's reading, the *ketoret* was entirely concealed beneath layers of covering, and no one would have seen the *ketoret* itself during transport.

### Eyewitness Detail

These details are remarkably specific — the exact capacity, the nesting structure, the nature of the handle or covering. One is struck by the observation that these descriptions read like eyewitness accounts. How else would one know these precise particulars about vessels used in the *heichal*? The specificity suggests that those who transmitted these *mishnayot* had direct familiarity with the service, not merely inherited abstractions.

## Coal Procedure

### Silver Shovel

*Mi she-zacha ba-machtah natal machtat ha-kesef* — whoever merited the *machtah*, the shovel, took a silver shovel, *ve-alah le-rosh hamizbe'ach* — and ascended to the top of the outer *mizbe'ach*. There he moved aside the coals, *u-finah et ha-gechalim hilech ve-hilech*, to expose the best coals in the center. The outer *mizbe'ach* maintained a special fire specifically for producing coals for the *ketoret* — positioned toward the west, closer to the *heichal*, symbolizing its purpose. He scooped the coals — *chatah* — and then descended, *yarad ve-iram le-toch shel zahav*, pouring them from the silver shovel into a gold shovel.

The reason for using silver first and gold second: gold is precious, and the rough action of scooping coals directly would wear it down. So the initial scooping was done in silver, and the coals were then transferred to gold. One might note in passing that pure gold, being highly malleable, would in practice require some alloy — but in any case, the intention is clear.

One practical question arises: how does a *kohen* handle a shovel full of burning coals without injury? A long handle would provide some insulation, and the contact is brief — the scooping takes only moments. But if there were any kind of cloth handle or intermediate material, one might ask whether that constitutes a *chatzitzah* between the *kohen* and the *kli shareit*. There is a broader discussion of whether something done *le-kavod* avoids this problem, but here the reason is purely practical rather than honorific. The question is left open; a sufficiently long handle may simply be the answer.

### Shovel Transfer

The fire used for the *ketoret* coals had a capacity of roughly five *kavin*. He scooped four *kavin* from it in his silver shovel. The gold shovel, however, had a capacity of only three *kavin*. When he poured the four *kavin* of coals from the silver shovel into the gold one, one *kav* of coals inevitably spilled out. So both vessels are, in a sense, comparable in size to a large soup pot — the three-*kav* gold shovel, and the three-*kav kaf* that holds the nested *bazach* of *ketoret*. Two *kohanim*, each carrying something roughly that size in gold, approach the *heichal* together: one with coals, one with *ketoret*.

### Spilled Coals

*Ve-haya mechabdan la-amah* — the spilled *kav* of coals was swept into the *amah*. The *amah* was a channel through which water flowed, running through the *beit hamikdash* and draining out beyond it. It served as the receptacle for blood from the various *korbanot* as well, swept in by a kind of squeegee action and carried away. The spilled coals were handled similarly.

### Psachtar Vessel

On *Shabbat*, however, sweeping was not permitted — the coals were *muktzah*, and extinguishing them (*kibuy*) was forbidden. So *u-ve-Shabbat haya kofeh aleihen psachtar* — they would place a large cauldron, the *psachtar*, over the spilled coals. The principle that there are no *d'rabananm* in the *mikdash* applies specifically in the context of the *avodah*; but covering coals that have spilled is not itself part of the *avodah*, and so *muktzah* and the prohibition of *kibuy* apply.

The *psachtar* itself was a very large vessel — *machazekes letach*, containing a *letach*, which is half a *kor*, equivalent to fifteen *se'ah* or ninety *kavin*. Compared to the three-*kav* capacity of our "soup pot," the *psachtar* is thirty times as large — not a pot at all, but something more like a massive metal vat or cauldron.

One might ask why so large a vessel was needed merely to cover a *kav* of spilled coals. The answer is likely practical: a small cover could be tripped over and dislodged. A cauldron thirty times the size of the surrounding items cannot be easily tripped over or overturned. And a secondary function on *Shabbat* is noted as well: if a dead rodent were found in the *beit hamikdash* on *Shabbat*, it too was *muktzah* and could not be removed. The *psachtar* would be placed over it as well — both to prevent accidental contact with something *tamei* and to mark clearly that something was there. It is striking that even the symbolically disturbing presence of a dead creature in the *mikdash* was left in place until after *Shabbat*, covered by the cauldron.

### Chains System

*U-shtei sharsheros hayu vah* — the *psachtar* had two chains. *Achat she-hu moshech vah ve-yored* — one for drawing it down, *ve-achat she-hu ochetz mi-lma'alah* — and one for holding it from above, *bishvil she-lo yisgalgel* — so it would not roll.

This explains how the large ash removal was accomplished. When the main ash pile on the outer *mizbe'ach* needed to be cleared — not the symbolic *terumat hadeshen*, which was done daily, but the full removal done less frequently — the *psachtar* would be filled with ashes at the top of the *mizbe'ach* and then needed to be brought down the ramp. With a cauldron of that size, carrying was impossible. One *kohen* would hold the chain from above to keep it stable; another would pull the chain from below, walking down the ramp. The two chains allowed them to lower the cauldron in a controlled descent without it rolling or tipping.

*U-vishlosha devarim hayeta meshamashes* — this large cauldron thus served three functions: covering the spilled coals on *Shabbat*, covering a dead rodent found in the *mikdash* on *Shabbat*, and serving as the vessel for removing the main ash pile from the *mizbe'ach*.

## Magreifa Gong

### Throw Action

*Higiu bein ha-ulam ve-la-mizbe'ach* — they arrived at the space between the *ulam* and the *mizbe'ach*. The *ulam* is the vestibule — the entry hall before the *heichal* proper — and the space between it and the *mizbe'ach* is a charged and liminal zone. It is still within the *azara*, yet it stands directly at the threshold of the *heichal*. The *kiyor*, the laver, was located there as well.

Upon reaching this space, *natal echad et ha-magreifa ve-zarka bein ha-ulam ve-la-mizbe'ach* — one of them took the *magreifa* and threw it into that space. The *magreifa* could simply mean a shovel, and there is a known *kli shareit* by that name. But what is being described here is better understood as a large percussive instrument — something that, when thrown, produced an enormous sound, akin to striking a gong.

### Sound Signal

*Ein adam shomeia kol chaveiro bi-Yerushalayim mi-kol ha-magreifa* — no person could hear his fellow's voice in Yerushalayim over the sound of the *magreifa*. We have already heard that the opening of the doors of the *heichal* was audible as far as Yericho; this sound, produced at close range within the *azara*, was of such magnitude that ordinary conversation was impossible throughout the city.

### Three Functions

*U-lishlosha devarim hayeta meshamashes* — and this sound served three purposes.

First: *kohen she-shomea et kolah yodea she-echav ha-kohanim nichnasim le-hishtachavot* — a *kohen* hearing this knew that his fellow *kohanim* were about to enter for the *hishtachavaya*, the prostration, and *ve-hu ratzu va* — he ran to take his place. This raises a question worth pausing over: the *mishnah* has just told us that the *kohanim* who were not currently assigned took off their *bigdei kehunah* and changed into their ordinary garments. Yet now, only minutes later — after the scooping of coals and the brief transit to this space — they are being summoned to run and get ready? The tension between the clothing interlude and the immediacy of the next stage is genuinely puzzling.

Second: *u-ven levi she-shomea et kolah yodea she-echav ha-leviyim nichnasim le-daber be-shir* — a *levi* hearing this knew that his fellow *leviyim* were about to take their positions on the *duchan* to perform their music and song, *ve-hu ratzu va* — and he too ran to prepare.

Third: *ve-rosh ha-maamad haya ma'amid et ha-tameim be-sha'ar mizrach* — the head of the *maamad* would position the *tameim* at the eastern gate.

It is worth pausing here to note what this signal represents in the broader rhythm of the morning. The first audible mark of the day's service was the opening of the *heichal* doors, heard as far as Yericho. That initiated the *kohanim*'s preparatory work — *terumat hadeshen*, *shechita* of the *tamid*, all the steps of dismembering and arrangement. The *azara* gates had not yet been opened to the public; the *beit hamikdash* was not yet "open for business." This second signal — the *magreifa* — indicates that the final preparatory stages are underway, and that very soon the *azara* will be open. It is the equivalent of the store clerk announcing that the doors are about to open, that everything is nearly ready for the public. Interestingly, unlike a fixed-time alarm, this signal is triggered by reaching a particular stage of the *avodah*. The time is marked not by the clock but by the progress of the service itself.

There is also a connection here to the bells of the *kohen gadol*'s robe: *ve-nishma kolo be-vo'o el ha-kodesh ve-lo yamut* — his sound shall be heard when he enters the holy place, so that he does not die. There is something symbolically consistent between the bells that announce the *kohen gadol*'s entry into the *kodesh* and the *magreifa* thrown into the space between the *ulam* and the *mizbe'ach* to announce the imminent entry for *ketoret*. In both cases, the act of entering a sacred inner space is preceded by a sound that announces it.

### Tameim Placement

*Ve-rosh ha-maamad haya ma'amid et ha-tameim be-sha'ar mizrach* — the head of the *maamad* would stand the *tameim* at the eastern gate, which is the gate of the *azara*. The word *tameim* here refers to those — whether *kohanim* or the *yisraelim* of the *maamad* — who were in a state of *tuma* that required them to bring a *korban* before their purification process could be complete.

### Tameim Debate

There is a debate about the purpose of this placement. Most commentators understand it as one of two things. The first reading is that it was a public shaming — placing the *tameim* visibly at the gate so that all could see: look, these *kohanim* were so negligent that they became *tamei* on the very day they were scheduled to serve. This is a harsh reading. The second reading, which appears to be the view the Rambam prefers, is precisely the opposite: this was a kindness. When people saw that certain *kohanim* were absent from the service, they might wonder or be troubled. Positioning the *tameim* visibly at the gate explained their absence without suspicion or complaint.

The Rambam, however, offers a third and quite different explanation. The *tameim* were positioned at the gate so that they could be brought to the front of the line. Certain states of *tuma* end only when the individual brings a *korban*; the *maamad* head, hearing the *magreifa* and knowing that the *azara* would soon open, assembled those who needed to bring their *korbanot* and placed them at the head of the queue — like giving priority boarding to those with special needs. This is by far the most generous interpretation and has an appealing practical logic.

One practical question here: how was the *maamad* head already at the eastern gate of the *azara* before it was opened? To reach the eastern gate of the *azara* one would have had to pass through the *ezrat nashim*. Whether the gates of the *ezrat nashim* had already been opened at this point is not stated explicitly in the *mishnah*, but the question is a fair one and worth examining further.

## Ash Removal

### Ascending Ulam

*Hitchilu olim be-ma'alot ha-ulam* — they began ascending the steps leading up to the *ulam*. The *ulam* itself had no gate — it was an open vestibule, a covered space extending before the *heichal* door. One might compare it to the small antechamber that many synagogues place before their main sanctuary: a transitional space that allows one door to close before the next opens. The steps leading to the *ulam* are visible in the architectural reconstructions. This entire structure — the *ulam* together with the main hall — was under one roofed unit, though one still passed through a doorway before entering the *heichal* proper.

### Inner Altar

*Mi she-zacha be-dishun mizbe'ach hapnimi ve-ha-menorah* — the ones who had merited the cleaning of the inner *mizbe'ach* and the *menorah* preceded the others. The first cleaning had been done earlier in the morning; this was the completion of that work. *Mi she-zacha be-dishun hamizbe'ach hapnimi nichnas* — the one assigned to the inner *mizbe'ach* entered, *ve-natal et ha-teni* — and took the basket filled with ashes, *hishtachavah* — he prostrated himself, *ve-yatza* — and left.

Why the prostration? The *heichal* is the inner sanctum, the throne room of the King. When a servant enters and then prepares to leave such a space, he bows in a gesture of reverence and permission to depart. Whether an analogous prostration was performed upon entry is a separate question; what the *mishnah* records is the bow upon leaving.

### Menorah Cleaning

*Mi she-zacha be-dishun ha-menorah* — the one who merited cleaning the *menorah* entered. Earlier, when the *menorah* cleaning had begun, he had left two of the lamps still burning. *Nichnas u-matza shtei nerot maaraviyim dolakim* — he found the two western lamps still burning.

A note on the term "western": there is a broader debate about precisely which lamp is called the *ner ha-maaravi*, whether it is literally the westernmost lamp or refers to a lamp that stands west relative to the next one in sequence. But regardless of the precise identification, the operative point is that two lamps had been left burning, and he now had two lamps remaining to clean.

*Midashen et ha-mizrachim* — he cleaned out the more eastern of the two. *U-maniach et ha-maaravi dolek* — and he left the western one burning. *She-mimmennu haya madlik et ha-menorah she-bein ha-arbayim* — because this was the lamp from which the *menorah* would be lit in the afternoon. Whichever lamp the *ner ha-maaravi* was, it was preserved so it could serve as the source of flame for lighting the other lamps at the evening service. He did clean one lamp — completing part of the *dishun* — but left this one to continue burning for that purpose.

What if the *ner ha-maaravi* was found extinguished? *Matzo she-kava midashno u-madliko mi-mizbe'ach ha-olah* — he would clean it out and relight it from the outer *mizbe'ach*. The *gemara* derives this from a *pasuk*: the *olah* altar is described with the phrase *esh tamid tukad al ha-mizbe'ach*, and the *menorah* is similarly described as burning *tamid* before God — *lifnei Hashem tamid*. One lights the *tamid* from the *tamid*, drawing fire from the outer *mizbe'ach* whose fire never ceases.

### Koz Pitcher

*Natal et ha-koz* — he took the *koz*, a small pitcher that held the oil, wicks, and other materials needed for the *menorah*. *Mi-ma'alah shniyah* — it had been placed on the second step of the *menorah* stand. *Hishtachava ve-yatza* — he prostrated himself and departed.

## Ketoret Offering

### Spreading Coals

*Tziber et ha-gechalim al gabei ha-mizbe'ach* — the *kohen* who had won the lottery for the *machtah* now placed the coals on top of the golden inner *mizbe'ach*. This was done after the ashes had been cleared and the prostrations made. *Ve-ridedan be-shulei ha-machtah* — he flattened them with the underside of the shovel, because spreading the coals evenly was essential: one does not want a heaped mound but a distributed layer on which the *ketoret* will be placed. *Hishtachava ve-yatza* — he prostrated himself and left.

### Bazach Transfer

Now the one who had won the *ketoret* lottery entered. *Haya notel et ha-bazach mi-toch ha-kaf* — he took the inner *bazach* from within the larger *kaf*, and *ve-natno le-ohavo o le-krovo* — he gave it to his friend or relative. Now, this seems surprising — he has just won the lottery for the *ketoret*, and he is handing it to someone else? The *meforshim* explain that what he gave away was not the *ketoret* but the larger outer *kaf*: here, you hold this large vessel, I need my hands free for the actual offering. The presence of this third person — a friend or relative who simply holds the larger pot — means that what appeared to be a two-person ceremony actually involves at least three: the one who spread the coals, the one doing the *ketoret*, and this helper.

### Hand Pouring

*Nitpazru mimenu le-tochah* — some of the *ketoret* may have scattered from the *bazach* into the larger outer *kaf* during transit. *Ve-natno lo be-chofnav* — and so the helper would gather this scattered *ketoret* and place it back into the hands of the one performing the offering.

This explains the logic of the arrangement. The *kohen* doing the *ketoret* is trying to pour the contents of the *bazach* into his cupped hands — this was considered one of the most physically demanding *avodot*. He is manipulating a vessel while trying to receive its contents in his palms, without a flat surface to work on. He performs this over the larger *kaf* so that whatever spills is caught. The helper then takes whatever landed in the larger *kaf* and returns it to his hands, enabling him to have the full quantity without loss.

### Burning Warning

*Melamdin oto* — those who had previously performed the *ketoret* would instruct the *kohen* about to do it. The key warning: *hevei zahir she-ma tatchil mi-lefanecha she-lo tik-aveh* — be careful not to begin scattering from close to your body moving outward, for if you do, the rising smoke and heat will burn your hands. Instead, begin from far away and move inward. The *ketoret* placed on hot coals begins to smoke immediately; one must be positioned and moving in the right direction from the very first moment.

### Performance

*Hitchil meradded ve-yatza* — he began to pour it out in a smooth, spread layer — *meradded* carries the sense of flattening or sifting, letting it fall in a distributed, even covering rather than in a lump — and then departed. The mechanics of the pouring are clear: the hands are held open, and the *ketoret* is allowed to fall gradually across the coals.

### Authorization

*Lo haya ha-maktir maktir ad she-ha-memuneh omer lo hakter* — the *maktir*, the one performing the *ketoret*, would not begin until the *memuneh*, the appointed officer, gave the word: *hakter* — do the incense. It is not entirely clear where the *memuneh* was positioned at this moment — was he inside with them, or calling from outside? — but the point is that the performance did not begin at the performer's own discretion.

*Ve-im haya kohen gadol, ha-memuneh omer lo: ishi kohen gadol, hakter* — and if it was the *kohen gadol* performing, the *memuneh* addressed him accordingly, with the honorific *ishi kohen gadol* — "my lord *kohen gadol*" — and only then gave the command. The sudden mention of the *kohen gadol* here is striking; it looks ahead to *Yom Kippur*, when the *kohen gadol* himself performs the *ketoret* in the *kodesh kodashim*.

### People Separate

*Pirshu ha-am* — the people separated and withdrew. *Ha-maktir* performed the *ketoret*, *ve-hishtachaveh ve-yatza* — prostrated himself, and left.

What does *pirshu ha-am* mean, and why was it necessary? The answer lies in a principle derived from the verse *kol adam lo yihyeh be-ohel mo'ed be-vo'o lechaper ba-kodesh ad tzeito* — no person shall be in the *ohel mo'ed* when he enters to effect *kapara* in the *kodesh*, until he goes out. The *ketoret* is understood as a form of *kapara* — recall that when *Aharon* used the *ketoret* to halt the plague, it was *mechaper al benei yisrael*. Whenever a *kapara* is performed inside the *heichal*, no one else may be present — not only on *Yom Kippur*, but anytime: for example, *par helam davar shel tzibur*, when the *beit din* rules erroneously and the community follows, the *zerikat hadam* inside the *heichal* similarly requires the area to be cleared.

Where exactly did they have to withdraw? Other *gemarot* specify that they had to leave not only the *heichal* and the *ulam*, but the space *bein ha-ulam ve-la-mizbe'ach* — the very transitional zone from which the *magreifa* had been thrown. That space, which represents the threshold of entry into the *heichal*, is treated as an extension of the *heichal's* sanctity. When the *kapara* is performed within the *heichal*, the zone of required separation extends outward to include even that liminal corridor. Thus the space *bein ha-ulam ve-la-mizbe'ach* functions on multiple levels: it is the place from which approach to the *heichal* is announced, and it is the boundary that must be cleared before the *heichal*'s most sacred acts are performed.

On *Yom Kippur*, when the *ketoret* is offered in the *kodesh kodashim*, it suffices for people to withdraw from the *heichal*, since the *kapara* is being done one level deeper. But when the *kapara* is done in the *heichal* itself, withdrawal must extend further out — to and including the *bein ha-ulam ve-la-mizbe'ach*. The sequencing — *memuneh* gives the word, all clear, everyone withdraws, he performs, prostrates, and exits — captures this carefully calibrated protocol.

## Tamid Opening

### Middot Conclusion

We conclude with the *hadran* for Masechet Middot: *hadran alach Masechet Middot ve-da'atan alach Masechet Middot, lo titnashi minan Masechet Middot ve-lo nitnashi minach, lo be-alma ha-dein ve-lo be-alma de-atei.*

### Guard Stations

With Masechet Middot behind us, we open Masechet Tamid. The first *mishnah* of *perek* 1 reads: *bi-shlosha mekomot ha-kohanim shomrim be-veit ha-mikdash* — in three places the *kohanim* stand guard in the *beit hamikdash*: in the *beit avtinas*, the *beit hanitzotz*, and the *beit hamoked*. *Ve-ha-leviyim be-esrim ve-echad makom* — and the *leviyim* in twenty-one places: five at the five gates of the *beit*, four at its four inner corners, five at the five gates of the *azara*, four at its four outer corners, one in the chamber of the sacrifice, one in the chamber of the curtain, and one behind the *beit ha-kapores*.

### Honor Guards

What is the purpose of these guards? There are no thieves to fear in the *beit hamikdash*. The answer is already given in the fifth *perek* of Masechet Middot, at 36a: the *shemira* of the *beit hamikdash* is only *le-kavod* — for honor. A palace with no guards is not an honorable palace. The guards were stationed not out of security necessity but to manifest the greatness and dignity of the place — which is precisely why both *leviyim* and *kohanim* were assigned, in separate roles, to separate stations. *Eino domeh palatin she-yesh aleha shomrim le-palatin she-ein aleha shomrim* — a palace that has guards is simply not comparable to one that does not. The more guards, the greater the honor; and so twenty-four stations were established throughout the *mikdash* complex, three held by *kohanim* and twenty-one by *leviyim*.
