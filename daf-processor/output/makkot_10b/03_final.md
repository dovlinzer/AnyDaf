# Makkot 10b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Roads to Arei Miklat

### Crossroads & Signs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: ״מִקְלָט״ הָיָה כָּתוּב עַל פָּרָשַׁת דְּרָכִים, כְּדֵי שֶׁיַּכִּיר הָרוֹצֵחַ וְיִפְנֶה לְשָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** It is taught in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: "Refuge" was written on** signs at every **crossroads** marking the path to a city of refuge, **so that the** unintentional **murderer would identify** the route to the city of refuge **and turn to** go **there.**

The daf opens at the very last line of 10a with a *baraita* concerning the infrastructure of the *arei miklat*. The *Mishna* had described paths interconnecting the various cities of refuge with one another — a system that, on reflection, is not the most practical arrangement. What a fleeing manslaughterer truly needs are paths leading from every point in the land *toward* the *arei miklat*, not merely between them. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov addresses precisely this concern: *miklat haya katuv al parashat derakhim* — at every crossroads throughout the country, a sign would be posted reading "*miklat*," indicating the direction to turn. In this way, wherever the *rotzeach* found himself, he could immediately orient himself and make his way to safety. It is worth pausing on the Gemara's consistent use of the term *rotzeach* for the person who killed *beshogeg* — a striking choice, and one the Gemara does not explain away.

### Prepare the Way

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: מַאי קְרָא? ״תָּכִין לְךָ הַדֶּרֶךְ״ – עֲשֵׂה [לְךָ] הֲכָנָה לַדֶּרֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said: What is the verse** from which this is derived? **"Prepare for you the road"** (Deuteronomy 19:3), meaning: **Perform for you preparation of the road.**

Rav Kahana identifies the scriptural basis for this entire infrastructure: *takhin lekha ha'derekh* — "prepare the way" (Devarim 19:3). The verse is read actively and expansively: one must make the way directed and prepared so that it is genuinely accessible. Implicit in this, as the Gemara notes, is not only the erection of signs but the ongoing maintenance of the roads themselves.

### Opening Homily

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חָמָא בַּר חֲנִינָא פָּתַח לַהּ פִּתְחָא לְהַאי פָּרַשְׁתָּא מֵהָכָא: ״טוֹב וְיָשָׁר ה׳ עַל כֵּן יוֹרֶה חַטָּאִים בַּדָּרֶךְ״ – אִם לַחַטָּאִים יוֹרֶה, קַל וָחוֹמֶר לַצַּדִּיקִים.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos that *halakha*, the Gemara cites that **Rav Ḥama bar Ḥanina introduced this portion** with regard to the *halakhot* of exile **with an introduction from here: "Good and upright is God; therefore He directs sinners along the way"** (Psalms 25:8). He said: **If He directs sinners** by commanding the placing of signs directing them to the city of refuge, it may be inferred ***a fortiori*** that He will assist and direct **the righteous** along the path of righteousness.

Rav Chama bar Chanina, when delivering a *derasha* on the *parasha* of *arei miklat*, would open with a classic rabbinic *ptichta* — that form of homiletical introduction found throughout *Midrash Rabbah*, in which one begins with an apparently distant verse and gradually winds one's way to the topic at hand. His *ptichta* verse was: *tov ve'yashar Hashem al ken yoreh chata'im baderekh* — "God is good and just, and therefore He will show sinners the path" (Tehillim 25:8). The point of departure is that even someone who bears some moral culpability — for negligence that led to a death is not pure accident — is nonetheless shown the path by God. And if this is what God does for *chata'im*, sinners, then *kal vachomer* how much more so does He do for the righteous.

### Evil Produces Evil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ פָּתַח לַהּ פִּתְחָא לְהַאי פָּרַשְׁתָּא מֵהָכָא: ״וַאֲשֶׁר לֹא צָדָה וְהָאֱלֹהִים אִנָּה לְיָדוֹ וְגוֹ׳״, ״כַּאֲשֶׁר יֹאמַר מְשַׁל הַקַּדְמֹנִי מֵרְשָׁעִים יֵצֵא רֶשַׁע וְגוֹ׳״,
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish introduced this portion with an introduction from here:** It is stated with regard to an unintentional murderer: **"And one who did not lie in wait, but God caused it to come to his hand,** and I will appoint you a place where he may flee" (Exodus 21:13). Now this is puzzling. Why would God cause one to sin in this manner? The verse states: **"As the ancient parable says: From the wicked comes forth wickedness"** (I Samuel 24:13). Evil incidents befall those who have already sinned.

Resh Lakish's *ptichta* takes a different approach. Rather than beginning from a distant verse in *Nach*, he opens directly within the *parasha* itself: *ve'asher lo tzadah ve'ha'elohim ina leyado* — "and one who did not lie in wait, but God delivered him into his hand" (Shemot 21:13). This raises an immediate difficulty: if God orchestrated the death, if the killer was merely God's instrument, why must he go into exile at all? Resh Lakish weaves in the verse *ka'asher yomar meshal ha'kadmoni me'resha'im yetze resha* — "as the ancient proverb says, from the wicked comes wickedness" (Shmuel I 24:14). The point, as Rashi explains, is that the killer was already a person of moral failing, and therefore even though divine providence shaped the circumstances, the exile is still deserved.

## Providence & Justice

### Rashi Resolution

The apparent contradiction — that God caused it and yet the killer is liable — is precisely what the Gemara elaborates upon through the famous parable. The scenario described is one of two people who each committed killing, one *bemezid* and one *beshogeg*, neither of whom had witnesses. Both escaped human judgment. Divine justice, the Gemara explains, then arranges the resolution.

### Inn Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמָּה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר – בִּשְׁנֵי בְּנֵי אָדָם שֶׁהָרְגוּ אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ, אֶחָד הָרַג בְּשׁוֹגֵג וְאֶחָד הָרַג בְּמֵזִיד, לָזֶה אֵין עֵדִים וְלָזֶה אֵין עֵדִים. הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מַזְמִינָן לְפוּנְדָּק אֶחָד, זֶה שֶׁהָרַג בְּמֵזִיד יוֹשֵׁב תַּחַת הַסּוּלָּם, וְזֶה שֶׁהָרַג בְּשׁוֹגֵג יוֹרֵד בַּסּוּלָּם, וְנֹפֵל עָלָיו וְהֹרְגוֹ, זֶה שֶׁהָרַג בְּמֵזִיד – נֶהֱרָג, וְזֶה שֶׁהָרַג בְּשׁוֹגֵג – גּוֹלֶה.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish explains: In this light, the verse "But God caused it to come to his hand" may be understood. **With regard to what** scenario **is the verse speaking?** It is **with regard to two people who killed a person,** where **one killed unintentionally** while **the other killed intentionally. For this** person **there are no witnesses** to his action, **and for that** person **there are no witnesses** to his action; therefore, neither received the appropriate punishment of exile and execution, respectively. **The Holy One, Blessed be He, summons them to one inn. This** person **who killed intentionally sits beneath a ladder, and that** person **who killed unintentionally descends the ladder, and** he **falls upon him and kills him.** There were witnesses to that incident and therefore, **that** person **who killed intentionally is killed, and that** person **who killed unintentionally is exiled,** each receiving what he deserved.

The parable unfolds as follows: *HaKadosh Baruch Hu mazminam le'pundak echad* — God arranges that both individuals arrive at the same inn. The intentional murderer sits beneath a ladder, while the one who killed unintentionally is descending that same ladder — and note that the direction matters, he is coming *down* — and falls upon the murderer and kills him. Justice is thus served on both accounts: *zeh she'harag bemezid neherag* — the intentional killer is killed as he deserved to have been by a court, *ve'zeh she'harag beshogeg goleh* — the one who killed accidentally goes into exile as he deserved for his original act. Divine providence has arranged the circumstances, but each party receives precisely what justice required of him. One student noted that the verse *me'resha'im yetze resha* appears in the context of David's address to Shaul — David's refusal to kill Shaul despite the opportunity — which enriches the resonance of Resh Lakish's citation.

## Divine Guidance

### Three Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִן הַתּוֹרָה וּמִן הַנְּבִיאִים וּמִן הַכְּתוּבִים – בַּדֶּרֶךְ שֶׁאָדָם רוֹצֶה לֵילֵךְ בָּהּ – מוֹלִיכִין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the path upon which God leads people, the Gemara cites a statement that **Rabba bar Rav Huna says** that **Rav Huna says, and some say** it was a statement that **Rav Huna says** that **Rabbi Elazar says: From the Torah, from the Prophets, and from the Writings** one learns that **along the path a person wishes to proceed, one leads** and assists **him.**

The Gemara transitions to a broader principle articulated by Rabbah bar Rav Huna in the name of Rav Huna — or according to some versions, Rav Huna in the name of Rav Elazar: *b'derech she'adam rotzeh lalechet bah molichin oto* — in the path that a person wishes to travel, heaven facilitates his going. This principle is derived from three distinct parts of *TaNaCh*.

### Bilam Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִן הַתּוֹרָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא תֵלֵךְ עִמָּהֶם״, וּכְתִיב: ״קוּם לֵךְ אִתָּם״.
>
> **Translation:** One learns this **from the Torah, as it is written** that initially God said to Balaam with regard to the contingent dispatched by Balak: **"You shall not go with them"** (Numbers 22:12). After Balaam implored Him and indicated his desire to go with them, **it is written: "Arise, go with them"** (Numbers 22:20).

From the Torah: when Bilam wished to go with the Moabite princes, God initially said *lo telech imahem* — do not go with them. But Bilam persisted, and God relented: *kum lech itam* — arise and go with them. This is the principle made concrete: since Bilam insisted on going, God did not continue to obstruct him. One student pressed the point that God here does not merely *permit* but actively *instructs* Bilam to go, which is somewhat more than simply not standing in one's way. Rashi's formulation in this context is *ein me'akvin b'yado* — one does not hold him back.

### Isaiah's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִן הַנְּבִיאִים – דִּכְתִיב: ״אֲנִי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ מְלַמֶּדְךָ לְהוֹעִיל מַדְרִיכְךָ בְּדֶרֶךְ (זוּ) תֵּלֵךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** One learns this **from the Prophets, as it is written: "I am the Lord your God, Who teaches you for your profit, Who leads you on the path that you go"** (Isaiah 48:17), indicating that along the path that one seeks to go, God will direct him.

From the Prophets: *ani Hashem elohecha m'lamdecha l'ho'il madrichacha b'derech telech* — "I am the Lord your God who teaches you for your benefit, who guides you in the path you will walk" (Yeshayahu 48:17). The key word is *telech* — the path *you will walk*, not the path *you ought to walk*. God guides a person along the road that person has already chosen.

### Proverbs Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִן הַכְּתוּבִים – דִּכְתִיב: ״אִם לַלֵּצִים הוּא יָלִיץ וְלַעֲנָוִים יִתֶּן חֵן״.
>
> **Translation:** One learns this **from the Writings, as it is written: "If one seeks the cynics, He will cause him to join the cynics, but to the humble He will give grace"** (Proverbs 3:34), indicating that if one chooses cynicism God will direct him there and if he opts for humility God will grant him grace.

From *Ketuvim*: *im laleitzim hu yalitz ve'la'anavim yitein chein* — "if one deals with mockers, He will mock, but to the humble He gives grace" (Mishlei 3:34). Rashi reads *hu yalitz* to mean that if a person wishes to go ahead and mock or pursue evil, God will enable that as well — *ein me'akvin b'yado*, one does not obstruct him. The Gemara uses this as a metaphor for the *rotzeach* running toward the *ir miklat*: the Torah facilitates his reaching it, because God directs people along the paths they have chosen.

## Go'el HaDam Liability

### Rav Huna View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: רוֹצֵחַ שֶׁגָּלָה לְעִיר מִקְלָט וּמְצָאוֹ גּוֹאֵל הַדָּם וַהֲרָגוֹ – פָּטוּר. קָסָבַר: ״וְלוֹ אֵין מִשְׁפַּט מָוֶת״ – בְּגוֹאֵל הַדָּם הוּא דִּכְתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara resumes its discussion of the *halakhot* of exile. **Rav Huna says:** In the case of an unintentional **murderer who was exiled to a city of refuge, and the blood redeemer found him** on the way **and killed him,** he is **exempt.** The Gemara notes: **Rav Huna holds** that the verse: "Lest the blood redeemer pursue the murderer…and strike him fatally…**and for him there is no sentence of death,** as he did not hate him from before" (Deuteronomy 19:6), **is written with regard to the blood redeemer,** teaching that the blood redeemer is not liable to be executed for killing the murderer.

Rav Huna rules: *rotzeach she'galah l'ir miklat u'metza'o go'el hadam v'harago — patur* — a manslaughterer who has fled toward the city of refuge and is killed along the way by the *go'el hadam*, the *go'el hadam* is exempt from liability. The basis is a reading of the verse in Devarim (19:6): *pen yirdof go'el hadam acharei harotzeach ki yeicham levavo v'harago baderech v'lo ein mishpat mavet* — lest the *go'el hadam* pursue the killer while his heart is hot and kill him on the way, and *v'lo ein mishpat mavet* — and he will not deserve the death penalty. The plain reading of the verse seems to be that the manslaughterer does not deserve to die, since *ki lo sonei hu lo mitmol shilshom* — he did not hate the deceased from the past, i.e., it was accidental. But Rav Huna reads the verse differently: *v'lo ein mishpat mavet* refers not to the *rotzeach* but to the *go'el hadam* — it is the *go'el hadam* who will not be liable for having killed the *rotzeach* before he reached the city. And it is precisely because the *go'el hadam* can act with impunity during this window that the Torah commands *tachin lekha ha'derekh* — you must ensure the road is prepared so the *rotzeach* can arrive as quickly as possible.

### Braita Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״וְלוֹ אֵין מִשְׁפַּט מָוֶת״ – בָּרוֹצֵחַ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בָּרוֹצֵחַ, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בְּגוֹאֵל הַדָּם? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְהוּא לֹא שֹׂנֵא לוֹ מִתְּמוֹל שִׁלְשׁוֹם״ – הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר בָּרוֹצֵחַ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Rav Huna from a *baraita*: **"And for him there is no sentence of death"; the verse is speaking with regard to the** unintentional **murderer,** teaching that the unintentional murderer is not liable to be executed. That is why the Jewish people were commanded to establish cities of refuge to protect him. The *baraita* proceeds to prove that the verse is written with regard to the murderer. **Do you say** that it is speaking **with regard to the** unintentional **murderer, or** is it speaking **only with regard to the blood redeemer? When it states** in an earlier verse: **"And he did not hate him from before"** (Deuteronomy 19:4), it is clear that the reference is to the unintentional murderer, and therefore, **you must say** that in the phrase: "And for him there is no sentence of death," **the verse is speaking with regard to the** unintentional **murderer.**

A *braita* is cited in objection: *v'lo ein mishpat mavet berotzeiach hakatuv medaber* — the verse is speaking about the killer himself, not about the *go'el hadam*. The proof is that the following phrase, *ki lo sonei hu lo mitmol shilshom* — he did not hate him from the past — is manifestly speaking about the *rotzeach* and his lack of prior malice toward the victim. Since that phrase clearly refers to the *rotzeach*, the immediately preceding phrase, *v'lo ein mishpat mavet*, must refer to him as well. The *go'el hadam* reading of Rav Huna is therefore challenged.

### Braita Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא דְּאָמַר כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְלוֹ אֵין מִשְׁפַּט מָוֶת״ – בְּגוֹאֵל הַדָּם הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בְּגוֹאֵל הַדָּם הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בָּרוֹצֵחַ? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״כִּי לֹא שׂוֹנֵא הוּא לוֹ מִתְּמוֹל שִׁלְשׁוֹם״ – הֲרֵי רוֹצֵחַ אָמוּר, הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים: ״וְלוֹ אֵין מִשְׁפַּט מָוֶת״? בְּגוֹאֵל הַדָּם הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rav Huna **states** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **that** following ***tanna*, as it is taught** in another *baraita*: **"And for him there is no sentence of death"; the verse is speaking with regard to the blood redeemer.** The *baraita* clarifies: **Do you say** that it is speaking **with regard to the blood redeemer, or** is it speaking **only with regard to** the unintentional **murderer? When it states: "As he did not hate him from before,"** the unintentional **murderer** is already **stated,** as that phrase certainly is referring to him. **How do I realize** the meaning of the verse: **"And for him there is no sentence of death"?** It is **with regard to the blood redeemer** that **the verse is speaking.**

The Gemara resolves this by showing that Rav Huna's position is in fact supported by another *tanna*. A second *baraita* argues the inverse: since the phrase *ki lo sonei hu lo mitmol shilshom* unambiguously refers to the *rotzeach* — and would be redundant if the entire verse were about him — *v'lo ein mishpat mavet* must refer to a *different* subject, namely the *go'el hadam*. The *baraita* works through the logic explicitly: *ata omer b'go'el hadam hakatuv medaber o eino ela berotzeiach* — one might say it refers to the *go'el hadam* or perhaps to the *rotzeach*; but since the closing phrase *ki lo sonei* is already clearly about the *rotzeach*, the opening phrase *v'lo ein mishpat mavet* must be about the *go'el hadam*. This is accordingly a genuine *tannaitic* dispute, and Rav Huna holds in accordance with the *tanna* who reads the verse as referring to the *go'el hadam*'s exemption. The *pshat* of the broader passage — that the Torah establishes the *arei miklat* lest the killer be caught on the road — remains consistent with this reading: the killer is exposed until he arrives, which is precisely why the road must be prepared.

## Talmidei Chachamim

### Mishna Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: מוֹסְרִין לוֹ שְׁנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, שֶׁמָּא יַהַרְגֶנּוּ בַּדֶּרֶךְ, וִידַבְּרוּ אֵלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites proof concerning Rav Huna's ruling from the mishna. **We learned** in the mishna: **And they would provide** the unintentional murderer fleeing to a city of refuge with **two Torah scholars,** due to the concern that **perhaps** the blood redeemer **will** seek to **kill him in transit, and** in that case **they will talk to** the blood redeemer.

The *Mishna* states: *mosrin lo shnei talmidei chachamim shema yehorgenu baderech v'yedabru elav* — two *talmidei chachamim* are appointed to accompany the manslaughterer as he flees, lest the *go'el hadam* kill him on the road, and they are to speak to the *go'el hadam*.

### Persuasion vs Halacha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, כִּדְתַנְיָא: וִידַבְּרוּ אֵלָיו – דְּבָרִים הָרְאוּיִים לוֹ. אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: אַל תִּנְהַג בּוֹ מִנְהַג שׁוֹפְכֵי דָמִים, בִּשְׁגָגָה בָּא מַעֲשֶׂה לְיָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **No,** the statement of the Torah scholars to the blood redeemer can be explained **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **And they will speak to him** about **matters appropriate to him. They say to** the blood redeemer: **Do not accord him treatment** appropriate for **murderers,** as it was **unintentionally** that **he came to be involved in the incident.**

What precisely are these scholars to say? One might assume — given that they are *talmidei chachamim* — that their role is to convey *halacha*: to inform the *go'el hadam* that if he kills the *rotzeach* he will himself be liable to the death penalty, since the *rotzeach* is now under legal protection. This would also confirm that the manslaughterer is indeed protected while still in transit. But the Gemara raises an alternative: perhaps they are simply functioning as persuaders, not as *halachic* instructors. The *baraita* spells out what they would say: *al tinhag bo minhag shofchei damim bisshgagah ba miyado* — do not treat this person as a murderer; what happened was an accident. One might question how persuasive this is likely to be when addressed to a *go'el hadam* running in the heat of passion, but the Gemara takes the position seriously.

### Emissary Value

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: הוּא מְדַבֵּר עַל יְדֵי עַצְמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְזֶה דְּבַר הָרֹצֵחַ״. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: הַרְבֵּה שְׁלִיחוּת עוֹשָׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The unintentional murderer too **speaks [*medabber*] on his own behalf** to dissuade the blood redeemer, **as it is stated: "And this is the matter [*devar*] of the murderer,** who shall flee there and live" (Deuteronomy 19:4), indicating that the murderer himself apologizes and speaks to the blood redeemer. The Sages **said to** Rabbi Meir: **Many** matters **are performed** more effectively through **agency.**

Rabbi Meir holds that the manslaughterer can and even should speak on his own behalf: *hu medaber al yedei atzmo* — he speaks for himself, as implied by the verse *ve'zeh devar ha'rotzeach* — "and this is the matter of the killer" (Devarim 19:4). The Rabbis respond: *harbeh shelichuto oseh* — an emissary often accomplishes what direct speech cannot. A person defending his own life before the family member of the one he killed is unlikely to be the most effective advocate for himself; an intermediary carries a different kind of authority and neutrality.

### Intent Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: בִּשְׁגָגָה בָּא מַעֲשֶׂה לְיָדוֹ. פְּשִׁיטָא! דְּאִי בְּמֵזִיד, בַּר גָּלוּת הוּא? אִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*. **The Master says** in the *baraita*: It was **unintentionally** that **he came to be involved in the incident.** The Gemara asks: Isn't this **obvious? As, if it were intentionally** that he killed a person, **is he liable to be exiled?** The Gemara answers: **Yes,** even intentional murderers flee to a city of refuge on occasion.

One might ask: if their role is merely persuasion — telling the *go'el hadam* the death was accidental — why is this not obvious? If the man is fleeing to the *ir miklat*, is it not self-evident that he killed *beshogeg*? The Gemara responds: *peshita d'bemezid bar galut hu?* — obviously an intentional killer would not be heading to the *ir miklat* for refuge! So what is the information the scholars are conveying? The Gemara answers that this is not as obvious as it seems, because according to the following *tanna*, it is not only the *shogeg* who flees to the *ir miklat*.

### Rabbi Yose View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בַּתְּחִלָּה, אֶחָד שׁוֹגֵג וְאֶחָד מֵזִיד מַקְדִּימִין לְעָרֵי מִקְלָט, וּבֵית דִּין שׁוֹלְחִין וּמְבִיאִין אוֹתָם מִשָּׁם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And** so **it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: Initially, either** one who killed another **unintentionally or** one who killed another **intentionally** would **hurry** and flee **to the cities of refuge, and the court** in his city would **send** for him **and** would **bring him from there** to stand trial.

Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda teaches: *batchila echad shogeg v'echad mezid makdimin l'ir miklat* — initially, both the inadvertent killer and the intentional murderer proceed to the *ir miklat*. The *ir miklat* functions at first as a holding place for all who killed, pending the convening of *beit din*. The court then summons them: *u'veit din sholchin u'mevi'in otam misham* — *beit din* sends and retrieves them from there. The one found liable for intentional murder is executed; the one found entirely innocent is freed (*v'hitzilu ha'edah et harotzeach miyad go'el hadam*); and the one found liable for manslaughter is returned to the *ir miklat*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב מִיתָה – הֲרָגוּהוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְשָׁלְחוּ זִקְנֵי עִירוֹ וְלָקְחוּ אֹתוֹ מִשָּׁם וְנָתְנוּ אֹתוֹ בְּיַד גֹּאֵל הַדָּם וָמֵת״. מִי שֶׁלֹּא נִתְחַיֵּיב – פְּטָרוּהוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהִצִּילוּ הָעֵדָה אֶת הָרֹצֵחַ מִיַּד גֹּאֵל הַדָּם״. מִי שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב גָּלוּת – מַחְזִירִין אוֹתוֹ לִמְקוֹמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהֵשִׁיבוּ אֹתוֹ הָעֵדָה אֶל עִיר מִקְלָטוֹ אֲשֶׁר נָס שָׁמָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: With regard to **one who was** found **liable** to receive the **death** penalty for intentional murder, after the trial the court **would execute him, as it is stated: "And the elders of his city shall send and take him from there and deliver him into the hands of the blood redeemer and he shall die"** (Deuteronomy 19:12). And with regard to **one who was not** found **liable** to receive the **death** penalty, e.g., if they deemed that it was due to circumstances beyond his control, **they freed him, as it is stated: "And the congregation shall rescue the murderer from the hands of the blood redeemer"** (Numbers 35:25). With regard to **one who was** found **liable to be exiled,** the court would **restore him to his place** in the city of refuge, **as it is stated:** "And the congregation shall judge between the murderer and the blood redeemer…**and the congregation shall restore him to his city of refuge, that he fled there"** (Numbers 35:24–25).

Since, according to Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda, an intentional killer might also be found in the city, the *talmidei chachamim* serve a real informational function: they are conveying to the *go'el hadam* that this particular person killed *beshogeg*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: מֵעַצְמָן הֵן גּוֹלִין. כִּסְבוּרִין הֵן אֶחָד שׁוֹגֵג וְאֶחָד מֵזִיד – קוֹלְטוֹת, וְהֵן אֵינָן יוֹדְעִין שֶׁבַּשּׁוֹגֵג קוֹלְטוֹת, בְּמֵזִיד אֵינָן קוֹלְטוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The Torah does not command intentional murderers to flee to a city of refuge; rather, the Torah is cognizant of the fact that in practice, intentional murderers **would exile** themselves **on their own,** as **they thought** that they would be **admitted** to these cities, which would provide refuge for **both unintentional and intentional** murderers, **and they do not know that** only those who murder **unintentionally are admitted** to these cities, but those who murder **intentionally are not admitted.**

*Rebbi* disagrees with Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda. He reads the *pesukim* describing *beit din* retrieving a *mezid* from the *ir miklat* not as reflecting a *beit din*-administered system but as describing cases where the *mezid* went there *voluntarily*: *me'aleihen hen golin* — they go on their own initiative. Why would an intentional murderer run to the *ir miklat*? Because *kasvorin hen echad shogeg v'echad mezid kolelet* — they mistakenly believed the *ir miklat* protects everyone, whether *shogeg* or *mezid*. *V'einan yodim she'bishgagah kolelet u'vemezid einah kolelet* — they did not know that in fact only the *shogeg* is protected there, not the intentional killer. This also raises the pointed question of how the *go'el hadam* — or anyone — can determine before trial whether the *rotzeach* is a *shogeg* or *mezid*, since the trial has not yet occurred. One must presumably act on the available presumption, with the *talmidei chachamim* conveying what is known about the circumstances at the time.

## City Requirements

### Majority Murderers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: עִיר שֶׁרוּבָּהּ רוֹצְחִים – אֵינָהּ קוֹלֶטֶת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְדִבֶּר בְּאׇזְנֵי זִקְנֵי הָעִיר הַהִיא אֶת דְּבָרָיו״ – וְלֹא שֶׁהוּשְׁווּ דִּבְרֵיהֶן לִדְבָרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Elazar says:** An unintentional murderer **is not admitted to a city** of refuge **whose majority** consists of unintentional **murderers, as it is stated** with regard to an unintentional murderer who fled to a city of refuge: **"And he shall speak his matters in the ears of the elders of that city"** (Joshua 20:4), indicating that there is some novel element in the matters that he seeks to convey to the elders of the town, **but not when their matters are equal to his matters,** as those elders made the same statements when they arrived at the city of refuge as unintentional murderers.

Rav Elazar rules: *ir she'rubbah rotzchim einah koletet* — a city whose population is predominantly composed of those who killed does not function as a city of refuge. The source is a verse in Yehoshua (*va'yedaber b'oznei ziknei ha'ir hahi et devarav*), read as implying that the population of the *ir miklat* must be distinct from the *rotzeach* who takes refuge there — *v'lo shehushevu devarav l'devarav* — their words and his words are not equivalent; they are a separate civic population, not a community uniformly composed of killers. The *ir miklat* requires a functioning, normal civic body to confer its status of sanctuary upon the minority who are the refugees.

### No Elders

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: עִיר שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ זְקֵנִים אֵינָהּ קוֹלֶטֶת, דְּבָעֵינַן ״זִקְנֵי הָעִיר״ וְלֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar says:** An unintentional murderer **is not admitted to a city in which there are no elders, as we require** the fulfillment of the verse: "And he shall speak in the ears of **the elders of the city"** (Joshua 20:4), **and there are none.**

Rav Elazar further rules: *ir she'ein bah zekenim einah koletet* — a city without elders cannot function as an *ir miklat*. The Yehoshua verses specify that the *rotzeach* speaks before the elders of that city, and that requirement is understood as substantive, not merely descriptive.

### Ammi vs Assi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: עִיר שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ זְקֵנִים, רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי – חַד אוֹמֵר: קוֹלֶטֶת, וְחַד אוֹמֵר: אֵינָהּ קוֹלֶטֶת. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵינָהּ קוֹלֶטֶת – בָּעֵינַן ״זִקְנֵי הָעִיר״, וְלֵיכָּא. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר קוֹלֶטֶת – מִצְוָה בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **It was stated: A city in which there are no elders** is the subject of a dispute between **Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi. One says:** An unintentional murderer **is admitted** there, **and one says:** An unintentional murderer **is not admitted** there. The Gemara explains: **According to the one who says** that an unintentional murderer **is not admitted to a city in which there are no elders,** his reasoning is due to the fact that **we require** the presence of **the elders of the city and there are none. According to the one who says** that an unintentional murderer **is admitted** there, his reasoning is that he holds that speaking to the elders is **merely a mitzva** *ab initio*, but it does not affect the city's status as a city of refuge.

A direct *machlokes* between Rav Ammi and Rav Assi follows. One holds *kolelet* — the city still provides protection even without elders — and the other holds *einah kolelet* — it does not. The one who rules it does not protect reads the elders requirement as *me'akev*, indispensable. The one who permits it reads the mention of elders as *mitzvah b'alma* — describing the ideal situation, but not a condition that invalidates the city's function if absent.

### Related Extensions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעִיר שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ זְקֵנִים – רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי, חַד אָמַר: נַעֲשֶׂה בָּהּ בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה, וְחַד אָמַר: אֵין נַעֲשֶׂה בָּהּ בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין נַעֲשֶׂה בָּהּ בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה – בָּעֵינַן ״זִקְנֵי עִירוֹ״, וְלֵיכָּא. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר נַעֲשֶׂה בָּהּ בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה – מִצְוָה בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And a city in which there are no elders** is the subject of another dispute between **Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi. One says:** One **can become a wayward and rebellious son in it. And one says:** One **cannot become a wayward and rebellious son in it.** The Gemara explains: **According to the one who says** that one **cannot become wayward and rebellious son in it,** it is due to the fact that **we require** the presence of the elders of the city, as it is written: "And his father and mother shall take hold of him and bring him out to **the elders of his city** and the gate of his place" (Deuteronomy 21:19), **and there are none. According to the one who says** that one **can become a wayward and rebellious son in it,** the presence of the elders is **merely a mitzva** *ab initio*.

The same dispute between Rav Ammi and Rav Assi is applied analogously to *ben sorer u'moreh* and *eglah arufah*. Regarding *ben sorer u'moreh*: one holds that such a case can be adjudicated in a city without elders, reading the requirement of *ziknei iro* as merely the ideal procedure; the other holds it cannot, since the Torah explicitly requires the parents to bring him before *the elders*. Regarding *eglah arufah*: one holds that the ritual can be performed even in a city without elders, and one holds it cannot.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעִיר שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ זְקֵנִים, רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי – חַד אָמַר: מְבִיאָה עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה, וְחַד אָמַר: אֵינָהּ מְבִיאָה עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵינָהּ מְבִיאָה עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה – בָּעֵינַן ״זִקְנֵי הָעִיר הַהִיא״, וְלֵיכָּא. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מְבִיאָה עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה – מִצְוָה בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And a city in which there are no elders** is the subject of another dispute between **Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi. One says:** If a corpse was discovered proximate to that city, the inhabitants of the city **bring a heifer whose neck is broken. And one says:** The inhabitants of the city **do not bring a heifer whose neck is broken.** The Gemara explains: **According to the one who says** that the inhabitants of the city **do not bring a heifer whose neck is broken,** it is due to the fact that **we require** the presence of the elders of the city, as it is written: "And **the elders of that city** shall bring the calf down to a rough valley" (Deuteronomy 21:4), **and there are no** elders. **According to the one who says** that the inhabitants of the city **bring a heifer whose neck is broken,** the presence of the elders is **merely a mitzva** *ab initio*.

Tosafot raises a question here: there is a *Gemara* elsewhere that when the closest town lacks elders and a more distant town has them, the more distant town performs the *eglah arufah* — which would seem to confirm that elders are genuinely required. Tosafot resolves this by distinguishing the case: when there is a choice between a town with elders and one without, one assigns the responsibility to the town that has them. But if no surrounding town has elders, the ritual proceeds regardless. The *eglah arufah* case is particularly compelling for requiring elders, since the entire *inyan* is the city taking collective responsibility — a function that cannot easily be delegated to an external institution the way *ben sorer u'moreh* might be transferred to the *beit din* of another town.

## Harsh Language

### Question Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בַּר חֲנִינָא: מִפְּנֵי מָה נֶאֶמְרָה פָּרָשַׁת רוֹצְחִים בְּלָשׁוֹן עַזָּה,
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Ḥama bar Ḥanina says: For what** reason **was the portion** discussing **murderers stated with harsh language,**

Rabbi Chama bar Chanina asks: *mipnei mah ne'emrah parashat rotzchim b'lashon azah* — why is the section concerning the *rotzeach* and the *arei miklat* in Yehoshua expressed in harsh language? Elsewhere in *Sefer Yehoshua*, when God speaks to Yehoshua, the text consistently uses *vayomer* — "and He said." But when it comes to this passage, the verb shifts repeatedly to *vayedaber*, *daber*, *dibarti* — forms of *dibur*, which *Chazal* understood as connoting a harder, more severe register of speech.

### Torah Seriousness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיְדַבֵּר ה׳ אֶל יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לֵאמֹר דַּבֵּר אֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל לֵאמֹר תְּנוּ לָכֶם אֶת עָרֵי הַמִּקְלָט אֲשֶׁר דִּבַּרְתִּי אֲלֵיכֶם וְגוֹ׳״? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן שֶׁל הַתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **as it is written: "And the Lord spoke [*vayedabber*] to Joshua saying: Speak [*dabber*] to the children of Israel, saying: Assign you the cities of refuge of which I spoke [*dibbarti*] to you** by means of Moses" (Joshua 20:1–2). Why does the Torah repeatedly employ a term of *dibbur*, connoting harsh speech, as opposed to the term of *amira*, connoting neutral speech? It is **due to** the fact **that** the cities of refuge **are** a mitzva **of the Torah,** and therefore they warrant emphasis.

The first answer offered is *mipnei shehi Torah* — because this is the fulfillment of a *mitzvah* of the Torah. The repeated use of *vayedaber* and its cognates evokes the formulaic *Vayedaber Hashem el Moshe lemor* of the Chumash, signaling that what Yehoshua is now doing carries the weight and gravity of divine command. The harsher language conveys the seriousness and import of what is being enacted.

### Vayedaber Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּכׇל דִּיבּוּר לָשׁוֹן קָשָׁה? אִין, כְּדִכְתִיב: ״דִּבֶּר הָאִישׁ אֲדֹנֵי הָאָרֶץ אִתָּנוּ קָשׁוֹת״. וְהָתַנְיָא: ״נִדְבְּרוּ״, אֵין ״נִדְבְּרוּ״ אֶלָּא לְשׁוֹן נַחַת, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר ״יַדְבֵּר עַמִּים תַּחְתֵּינוּ״! ״דִּבֶּר״ לְחוּד, ״יַדְבֵּר״ לְחוּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that to say that all** instances of **speaking [*dibbur*]** indicate **harsh language?** The Gemara answers: **Yes, as it is written** with regard to Joseph's brothers: **"The man, the lord of the land, spoke [*dibber*] harshly to us"** (Genesis 42:30). The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: "Then **they** who feared the Lord **spoke [*nidberu*]** with one another" (Malachi 3:16), that the term **"they spoke" is nothing other than a term of gentleness, and likewise,** the same is true of the verse which **states: "He subdues [*yadber*] peoples under us"** (Psalms 47:4), meaning that God will calmly and gently conduct the nations under the influence of the Jewish people? The Gemara answers: The meaning of ***dibber* is discrete** and the meaning of ***yadber* is discrete.** There is a difference between the two conjugations of the same root.

The Gemara then asks: *leimar dechol dibur lashon kasheh* — does *dibur* always indicate harshness? It cites a counter-example: *diber ha'ish adonei ha'aretz itanu kashot* — the man, the lord of the land, spoke harshly with us (Bereishit 42:30), where the harshness is explicit. But then it cites *az nidberu yirei Hashem* — then those who feared God spoke with one another (Malachi 3:16), where the *baraita* says *ein nidberu ela lashon nachat* — *nidberu* means pleasant speech. And *yadber amim tachteinu* is similarly cited as a form of *dibur* that connotes leading or guiding rather than harshness. The Gemara's resolution is *dibur lachud yadber lachud* — *dibur* as a noun-form connotes harshness, while *yadber* in the sense of "to lead" or "to guide" is a distinct usage with a softer valence.

### Rav Yehuda Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבָּנַן, חַד אוֹמֵר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁשִּׁיהָם. וְחַד אוֹמֵר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן שֶׁל תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resumes the discussion of the harsh language employed in the portion discussing murderers in the book of Joshua. **Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis disagree with regard to this** matter. **One says** harsh language was employed **because** Joshua **delayed** fulfilling the mitzva of designating cities of refuge, **and one says** it is **because** the cities of refuge **are** a mitzva **of the Torah,** and therefore they warrant emphasis.

Rav Yehuda and the Rabbis dispute the underlying reason for the harsh language. One holds *mipnei sheshicham* — because Yehoshua delayed in fulfilling the *mitzvah* of establishing the cities of refuge, so the harsh divine speech represents a rebuke. The other holds the reason is simply *mipnei shehein shel Torah* — as stated above, because it is biblical in character and must be expressed with commensurate gravitas.

## Joshua's Writing

### Dispute on Content

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּכְתֹּב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אֶת הַדְּבָרִים הָאֵלֶּה בְּסֵפֶר תּוֹרַת אֱלֹהִים״, פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה – חַד אוֹמֵר: שְׁמֹנָה פְּסוּקִים, וְחַד אוֹמֵר: עָרֵי מִקְלָט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites an additional dispute with regard to the portion of the cities of refuge in the book of Joshua. It is written: **"And Joshua wrote these matters in the scroll of the Torah of God"** (Joshua 24:26). **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Neḥemya disagree with regard to this** matter. **One says:** The reference is to the final **eight verses** in the Torah that record the death of Moses and were recorded by Joshua in the scroll of the Torah, in addition to the rest of the Torah that was written by Moses (see *Bava Batra* 15a). **And one says:** The reference is to the portion of the **cities of refuge** that appears in the book of Joshua.

The Gemara now addresses a related question prompted by a verse in Yehoshua: *vayichtov Yehoshua et hadevarim ha'eleh besefer Torat Elohim* — "and Yehoshua wrote these words in the book of the Torah of God" (Yehoshua 24:26). What exactly did Yehoshua write? Rav Yehuda and Rav Nechemiah dispute this. One holds he wrote the *shemoneh pesukim* — the last eight verses of the Torah in *Devarim*, which according to one opinion in *Bava Batra* were written by Yehoshua rather than Moshe, since they describe Moshe's death. The word *hadevarim* in the verse is particularly apt, evoking *Sefer Devarim* itself (*eleh hadevarim*). The other opinion holds that what Yehoshua wrote was the *parshat arei miklat* — the section concerning the cities of refuge as it appears in *Sefer Yehoshua* itself.

### Rashi Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר שְׁמֹנָה פְּסוּקִים, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּסֵפֶר תּוֹרַת אֱלֹהִים״. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר עָרֵי מִקְלָט, מַאי ״בְּסֵפֶר תּוֹרַת אֱלֹהִים״? הָכִי קָאָמַר: ״וַיִּכְתּוֹב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בְּסִפְרוֹ אֶת הַדְּבָרִים הָאֵלֶּה הַכְּתוּבִים בְּסֵפֶר תּוֹרַת אֱלֹהִים״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses these two opinions: **Granted, according to the one who says that** the reference is to the final **eight verses** in the Torah, **that is** the reason **that it is written:** "And Joshua wrote these matters **in the scroll of the Torah of God,"** as he wrote those verses and they were included in the Torah. **But according to the one who says** that the reference is to the portion of the **cities of refuge** in the book of Joshua, **what** is the meaning of the phrase **"in the scroll of the Torah of God"?** They appear in the book of Joshua, not in the Torah. The Gemara answers: **This** is what the verse **is saying: And Joshua wrote in his book these matters that are** also **written in the scroll of the Torah of God.**

The Gemara asks: if the second opinion is correct, in what sense was it written *besefer Torat Elohim* — in the Torah scroll of God? Rashi's reading resolves this elegantly: *sefer Torat Elohim* in this verse refers not to the *Sefer Torah* of Moshe but to *Sefer Yehoshua* itself. The book of Yehoshua is here called *sefer Torat Elohim* — the book of God's Torah — and what Yehoshua wrote within it was the *parshat arei miklat*. The Gemara then offers a further harmonization: one could also read the verse as saying that Yehoshua wrote *in his own book* the words that are already written *in the Sefer Torah of God* — meaning he recorded the fulfillment of the Mosaic command, with the phrase *besefer Torat Elohim* modifying *hadevarim ha'eleh* (those words which are in the Torah), not describing where he wrote them.

## Torah Stitching

### Flax Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֵפֶר שֶׁתְּפָרוֹ בְּפִשְׁתָּן, פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר – חַד אוֹמֵר: כָּשֵׁר, וְחַד אוֹמֵר: פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to cite another dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and one of the Sages in which it is not clear which opinion is attributable to which Sage. In the case of a Torah **scroll where one sewed its** sheets **with linen** threads, **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir disagree with regard to this** matter. **One says:** The Torah scroll is **fit** for use, **and one says:** The Torah scroll is **unfit** for use.

Since the Gemara has been presenting a series of disputes structured as *chad amar* / *chad amar*, it now appends another such dispute. If a *Sefer Torah* is sewn together with flax rather than with *gidin* — sinews derived from animal intestine — is it *kasher* or *pasul*? Rav Yehuda and Rav Meir disagree.

### Tefillin Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאן דְּאָמַר פָּסוּל, דִּכְתִיב: ״לְמַעַן תִּהְיֶה תּוֹרַת ה׳ בְּפִיךָ״, וְאִיתַּקַּשׁ כׇּל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ לִתְפִילִּין: מָה תְּפִילִּין הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי לְתוֹפְרָן בְּגִידִין – אַף כֹּל לְתׇפְרָן בְּגִידִין. וְאִידַּךְ: כִּי אִיתַּקַּשׁ – לַמּוּתָּר בְּפִיךָ, לְהִלְכוֹתָיו לָא אִיתַּקַּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **According to the one who says** that the Torah scroll is **unfit** for use, the reason is **as it is written** with regard to phylacteries: "And it shall be for you a sign on your hand and a memorial between your eyes, **in order that the Torah of God shall be in your mouth"** (Exodus 13:9). **And** in this verse **the entire Torah is juxtaposed** and likened **to phylacteries: Just as** with regard to **phylacteries,** there is **a *halakha*** transmitted **to Moses from Sinai to sew them with sinews, so too,** with regard to **all** sheets of the Torah scroll, there is a requirement **to sew them with sinews. And the other** Sage holds: **When** the Torah scroll **is juxtaposed** and likened to phylacteries, it is only **with regard to** the principle that the sheets of the Torah scroll may be prepared only from a species of animal **that is permitted to your mouth,** i.e., that it is permitted for a Jew to eat; but with regard **to its** other ***halakhot*, it is not juxtaposed** and likened to phylacteries.

The one who rules *pasul* invokes the verse *lema'an tihyeh Torat Hashem beficha* — "so that the Torah of God shall be in your mouth" (Shemot 13:9), by which the *Gemara* connects *tefillin* to *Sefer Torah*. There is a *Halacha l'Moshe miSinai* that *tefillin* must be sewn together with *gidin*. Since the verse creates a *hekesh* between *tefillin* and *Sefer Torah*, the same requirement applies: *mah tefillin Halacha l'Moshe miSinai litforan b'gidin, af kol litforan b'gidin* — just as *tefillin* requires sinews by received tradition, so too *Sefer Torah*. The one who rules *kasher* argues that the *hekesh* between *tefillin* and *Sefer Torah* extends only to the specific *derasha* of *b'ficha* — that the parchment must come from a *mutar b'ficha*, an animal whose flesh is permitted for consumption — but does not extend to all the subsidiary *halachos* of *tefillin*. Since the *gidin* requirement is a detail of *tefillin*, it does not transfer via this *hekesh* to *Sefer Torah*.

### Rav Chazina Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: חֲזֵינַן לְהוּ לִתְפִילִּין דְּבֵי חֲבִיבִי דִּתְפִירִי בְּכִיתָּנָא, וְלֵית הִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: I saw that the phylacteries of the house of my uncle,** Rabbi Ḥiyya, **were sewn with linen. But the *halakha* is not in accordance with his** opinion; phylacteries may be sewn only with sinews.

Rav Chazina adds a striking piece of testimony: *l'didi chazi li tefillin d'bei chavivi d'tzafiri b'kitna* — I myself saw the *tefillin* of the household of my beloved (Rabbi Chiyya), *d'tzafiri b'kitna* — that they were sewn with flax. This is a remarkable report, since even those who rule *kasher* for a *Sefer Torah* stitched with flax do not say the same for *tefillin*, for which the *gidin* requirement is unambiguous. The Gemara concludes: *v'leit hilchata k'vateih* — and the *halacha* is not in accordance with this practice.

## High Priest Death

### High Priest Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֶחָד מָשׁוּחַ בְּשֶׁמֶן הַמִּשְׁחָה, וְאֶחָד הַמְרוּבֶּה בִּבְגָדִים, וְאֶחָד שֶׁעָבַר מִמְּשִׁיחוּתוֹ – מַחֲזִירִין אֶת הָרוֹצֵחַ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף מְשׁוּחַ מִלְחָמָה מַחֲזִיר אֶת הָרוֹצֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The Torah states that an unintentional murderer is required to remain in the city of refuge to which he fled until the death of the High Priest. The mishna elaborates: With regard to High Priests, who were appointed in several different manners, **one anointed with the anointing oil,** which was the method through which High Priests were consecrated until the oil was sequestered toward the end of the First Temple period; **and one** consecrated by donning **multiple garments,** the eight vestments unique to the High Priest, which was the practice during the Second Temple period; **and one** who received a temporary appointment due to the unfitness of the serving High Priest, **who departed from his anointment** with the restoration of the serving High Priest to active service, their deaths **facilitate** the **return of the murderer** from the city of refuge to his home. **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even** the death of a priest **anointed for war** to address the soldiers (see Deuteronomy 20:1–7) **facilitates** the **return of the murderer.**

The *Gemara* now transitions to the *Mishna* dealing with which *Kohen Gadol*'s death releases the manslaughterer from the *ir miklat*. The *Mishna* enumerates: the *Kohen Gadol* anointed with the *shemen hamishcha* (as was the case in the first *Beit HaMikdash*); the one who became *Kohen Gadol* through the wearing of the eight special garments alone, without the oil of anointing (as occurred toward the end of the first *Beit HaMikdash* when the *shemen hamishcha* was lost); and even a *Kohen Gadol* who had been removed from his office — one who, for example, had a *pesul* that temporarily disqualified him from serving on *Yom Kippur*, requiring a substitute to serve, so that one now has both a sitting and an ex-*Kohen Gadol*. Even the death of such a former *Kohen Gadol* would free the manslaughterer. Rabbi Meir adds a fourth category: even the *mashuach milchama* — the Kohen anointed to address the troops before battle (Devarim 20:2-4), who is accorded a status of distinction above ordinary *Kohanim* — counts as well, and his death would also free the *rotzeach*.

### Care Packages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִיכָךְ אִימּוֹתֵיהֶן שֶׁל כֹּהֲנִים מְסַפְּקוֹת לָהֶן מִחְיָה וּכְסוּת, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יִתְפַּלְּלוּ עַל בְּנֵיהֶם שֶׁיָּמוּתוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **Therefore, the mothers of** High **Priests** would **provide** those exiled to cities of refuge with **sustenance and garments so that they would not pray that their sons would die.** The more comfortable their lives in the city of refuge, the less urgency they would feel to leave, and the less likely it would be that they would pray for the death of the High Priests.

The *Mishna* records a remarkable social practice consequent upon this law: *imoteihem shel Kohanim mesafkot lahen michya u'chsut* — the mothers of the *Kohanim Gedolim* would provide food and clothing to the residents of the *arei miklat*. The reason given is *k'dei shelo yitpallelu al b'neihem sheyamutu* — so that those confined in the *ir miklat* should not pray for their sons to die. The logic runs in two directions: gratitude for the care packages might prevent the residents from wishing death upon the benefactor's son, and, more practically, if the *Kohen Gadol* dies and the manslaughterer is freed, the care packages stop as well. The entire arrangement reflects a deep assumption about the efficacy of prayer.

### Prayer Efficacy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְיָשַׁב בָּהּ עַד מוֹת הַכֹּהֵן הַגָּדוֹל״,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters,** that the death of these High Priests facilitates the return of the murderer, derived? **Rav Kahana said** they are derived from a verse, **as the verse states: "And he shall dwell there until the death of the High Priest**

The Gemara sharpens this into a question. If the concern is that the manslaughterers not pray for the *Kohen Gadol*'s death, there is a verse that seems to make such prayers futile: *ke'tzippor lanud ke'dror la'uf ken kilelat chinam lo tavo* — like a bird that wanders, like a swallow that flies, so too a causeless curse shall not come to rest (Mishlei 26:2). Why should a prayer for an innocent person's death accomplish anything at all?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא סָבָא: מִפִּירְקֵיהּ דְּרָבָא שְׁמִיעַ לִי, שֶׁהָיָה לָהֶן לְבַקֵּשׁ רַחֲמִים עַל דּוֹרָן וְלֹא בִּקְּשׁוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A certain elder said to him: I heard in the lecture** delivered **by Rava** that it is not a baseless curse, as the High Priests share the blame for the unintentional murders performed by these people, **as they should have pleaded for mercy for their generation,** that no murder should transpire, even unintentionally, **and they did not plead.** Due to their share in the blame, prayers for their death could be effective.

A certain elder (*hahu saba*) cites the teaching of Rav's academy: *she'hayah lahem levakesh rachamim al doram v'lo bikshu* — the *Kohanim Gedolim* were culpable because they *should have* prayed for their generation, for the safety of the people under their watch, and they failed to do so. This failure means that when tragedy strikes — even accidental deaths — the leadership bears a share of the responsibility. Thus, when those confined in the *ir miklat* pray for the *Kohen Gadol* to die, it is not entirely a causeless curse; the *Kohen Gadol* himself carries some culpability for not having done more to prevent the conditions that led to the tragedy.

### Leadership Responsibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּמַתְנֵי: כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּתְפַּלְּלוּ עַל בְּנֵיהֶם שֶׁלֹּא יָמוּתוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And some teach** a variant reading of the mishna: Therefore, the mothers of High Priests would provide those exiled to cities of refuge with sustenance and garments, **so that** those exiled **would pray that their sons will not die.**

An alternate version of the *Mishna* reads the mothers' motivation differently: not *shelo yitpallelu al b'neihem sheyamutu* — that they should not pray for their sons to die — but *k'dei she'yitpallelu aleihem shelo yamutu* — so that the residents would actively pray for their sons to *live*. The Gemara finds this version even more demanding. If the reason the *Kohanim Gedolim* don't die is only because the *rotze'achim* are praying actively for them to live, the implication is that absent such prayers, the *Kohanim Gedolim* would in fact die. But what would they have done to deserve death?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא סָבָא: מִפִּירְקֵיהּ דְּרָבָא שְׁמִיעַ לִי, שֶׁהָיָה לָהֶן לְבַקֵּשׁ רַחֲמִים עַל דּוֹרָן וְלֹא בִּקְּשׁוּ. כִּי הָא דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּאַכְלֵיהּ אַרְיָא בְּרָחוֹק תְּלָתָא פַּרְסֵי מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, וְלָא אִישְׁתַּעִי אֵלִיָּהוּ בַּהֲדֵיהּ תְּלָתָא יוֹמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **A certain elder said to him: I heard in the lecture** delivered **by Rava** that the High Priests share the blame, **as they should have pleaded for mercy for their generation and they did not plead.** Consequently, they required the exiles to pray on their own behalf. The Gemara illustrates the concept of the responsibility held by the spiritual leadership: This is **like** in **this** incident **where a certain man was eaten by a lion at a distance of three parasangs from** the place of residence of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, and Elijah** the prophet **did not speak with him** for **three days** because of his failure to pray that an incident of this kind would not transpire in his place of residence.

Again the elder's answer applies: *she'hayah lahem levakesh rachamim al doram v'lo bikshu* — they should have prayed for their generation and did not. A leader who fails to carry the welfare of the community in prayer, who does not function as an advocate before God for those under his authority, bears a degree of culpability when harm befalls them. The Gemara reinforces this with the account of the lion that attacked a man within three *parsangs* of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi — a great *amora* who regularly had conversations with Eliyahu HaNavi — and Eliyahu did not speak with him for three days afterward, implicitly holding Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi accountable for what occurred in his vicinity.

## Power of Curses

### Wise Person Curse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: קִלְלַת חָכָם – אֲפִילּוּ בְּחִנָּם הִיא בָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos curses that are realized, **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** With regard to **the curse of a Sage, even** if it is **baseless,** i.e., based on a mistaken premise, **it** nevertheless **comes** to fruition and affects the object of the curse.

Rav Yehuda teaches in the name of Rav: *kilat chacham afilu bechinam hi ba'ah* — the curse of a *chacham*, even if it was issued without cause — *bechinam* — will still come to pass. The word *chinam* here is critical: it is the same word that appears in *kilelat chinam lo tavo* — a causeless curse shall not come to rest — yet even this protection does not apply when the one who issued the curse is a *chacham*.

### Achitofel Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָלַן? מֵאֲחִיתוֹפֶל, שֶׁבְּשָׁעָה שֶׁכָּרָה דָּוִד שִׁיתִין, קְפָא תְּהוֹמָא, בְּעָא לְמִישְׁטְפָא לְעָלְמָא. אֲמַר: מַהוּ לִכְתּוֹב שֵׁם אַחַסְפָּא וּמִישְׁדֵּא בִּתְהוֹמָא דְּלֵיקוּ אַדּוּכְתֵּיהּ? לֵיכָּא דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ מִידֵּי. אֲמַר: כׇּל הַיּוֹדֵעַ דָּבָר זֶה וְאֵינוֹ אוֹמְרוֹ – יֵחָנֵק בִּגְרוֹנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The proof comes from Achitofel. When David was excavating the foundations for the *Beit HaMikdash*, he dug so deep that he tapped into the subterranean waters — the *tehom* — and the waters began rising, threatening to inundate the world. David called out: **Who knows how to write God's name on a shard and throw it into the depths?** And he added: **Whoever knows this and does not come forward, let him be choked in his throat.**

The proof comes from Achitofel. When David was excavating the foundations for the *Beit HaMikdash*, he dug so deep that he tapped into the subterranean waters — the *tehom* — and the waters began rising, threatening to inundate the world. David called out: *man yadei'a lichtov shem achaspa u'mishda bitehoma* — who knows how to write God's name on a shard and throw it into the depths? And he added: *kol hayodeia davar zeh ve'eino omro yechanek bigrono* — whoever knows this and does not come forward, let him be choked in his throat.

### Achitofel Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָשָׂא אֲחִיתוֹפֶל קַל וָחוֹמֶר בְּעַצְמוֹ, אָמַר: וּמָה לַעֲשׂוֹת שָׁלוֹם בֵּין אִישׁ לְאִשְׁתּוֹ, אָמְרָה הַתּוֹרָה: שְׁמִי שֶׁנִּכְתַּב בִּקְדוּשָּׁה יִמָּחֶה עַל הַמַּיִם, לְכׇל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁרֵי. כְּתַב שֵׁם אַחַסְפָּא, שְׁדָא אַתְּהוֹמָא, נְחַת וְקָם אַדּוּכְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Then **Ahithophel raised an *a fortiori*** inference **on his own** and **said: And if** in order **to make peace between a man and his wife** in the case of a *sota*, when the husband suspects his wife of having committed adultery, **the Torah says: My name that was written in sanctity shall be erased on the water,** then, in order **to establish peace for the whole world in its entirety, is it not all the more so** permitted? Ahithophel **said to** David: **It is permitted.** David **wrote** the sacred **name on an earthenware shard** and **cast it into the depths,** and the water in the depths **subsided and stood in its place.**

The *kal vachomer* that Achitofel constructed was this: *umah la'asot shalom bein ish le'ishto amrah Torah shemi shehichtav bikedusha yimacheh al hamayim* — in order to make peace between a husband and wife, the Torah permits God's name, written in holiness, to be erased into the water of the *sotah* ritual. *Lechol ha'olam kulo lo kol sheken* — certainly for the sake of the entire world, the same should be permitted. One might ask what the *hava amina* even was — why would there be a prohibition? Perhaps it was understood in terms of *Lo ta'asun ken l'Hashem Elokechem*, or perhaps even as approaching the category of *yehareg ve'al ya'avor*. Regardless, Achitofel ruled: *shari* — it is permitted.

### Achitofel Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי כְּתִיב: ״וַאֲחִיתֹפֶל רָאָה כִּי לֹא נֶעֶשְׂתָה עֲצָתוֹ וַיַּחֲבֹשׁ אֶת הַחֲמוֹר וַיָּקׇם וַיֵּלֶךְ אֶל בֵּיתוֹ (וְ)אֶל עִירוֹ וַיְצַו אֶל בֵּיתוֹ וַיֵּחָנַק וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And even so it is written** that during the rebellion of Absalom: **"And Ahithophel saw that his counsel was not taken, and he saddled his donkey and he arose and went to his house, to his town, and he commanded his household and strangled himself"** (II Samuel 17:23). Although David stipulated that his curse would take effect only if one who knows the answer fails to share it with him, and Ahithophel did not fail to share it with him, the curse was realized.

Despite having provided this crucial service, Achitofel met his end shortly thereafter. The verse records: *va'yar Achitofel ki lo na'astah atzato vayachavosh et hachamor vayakum vayelech el beito el iro vayetzav el beito vayechanek* — Achitofel saw that his counsel had not been followed — in the context of the succession dispute — and he went to his city, set his affairs in order, and hanged himself. The self-imposed end is striking. Nevertheless the Gemara's point stands: the curse of David, *even though it was bechinam*, came into effect because a *chacham* issued it, and Achitofel — the foremost *chacham* of the generation — was caught within its scope.

## Conditional Curses

### Nidui Conditional

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: קִלְלַת חָכָם – אֲפִילּוּ עַל תְּנַאי הִיא בָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a similar statement: **Rabbi Abbahu says:** With regard to **the curse of a Sage, even** if it is stated **conditionally, it comes** to realization.

Rabbi Abahu teaches: *kilat chacham afilu al tnai tzarich hafara* — the curse of a *chacham* takes effect even when issued conditionally, i.e., even if the condition upon which it was suspended was ultimately not met, or was met in such a way that the curse ought not to apply. This leads Rav Yehuda to articulate a consequent *halacha*: *nidui al tnai tzarich hafara* — excommunication issued conditionally still requires formal release (*hafara*), even if the condition was never triggered.

### Eli Curse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָלַן? מֵעֵלִי, דְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ [עֵלִי] לִשְׁמוּאֵל: ״כֹּה יַעֲשֶׂה לְּךָ אֱלֹהִים וְכֹה יוֹסִיף אִם תְּכַחֵד מִמֶּנִּי דָּבָר״. וְאַף עַל גַּב דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּגֶּד לוֹ שְׁמוּאֵל אֶת כׇּל הַדְּבָרִים וְלֹא כִחֵד מִמֶּנּוּ״, [וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי] כְּתִיב: ״וְלֹא הָלְכוּ בָנָיו בִּדְרָכָיו וְגוֹ׳״.

### Moses Blessing

### Tosafot's Distinction

Tosafot raises a sharply practical question: in the medieval period, the primary mechanism by which rabbinic authorities secured communal compliance was the *cherem al tnai* — the conditional excommunication. If one does not do such-and-such, one is in *cherem*. But if even a *nidui al tnai* requires formal *hafara*, this would seem to undermine the entire apparatus, since presumably no *hafara* is issued every time a member of the community complies with a rabbinic enactment. Tosafot resolves this by drawing a distinction based on the nature of the condition. In Yehuda's case, the condition — bringing Binyamin safely back and presenting him before Yaakov — was not fully within his control: Yosef might have prevented it, Binyamin might have died along the way. Because the condition was partially outside Yehuda's ability to guarantee, the *cherem* took effect from the outset regardless of outcome. But when the condition of a *cherem* is entirely within the person's own control — for instance, the rabbinic enactment that says "if you do such-and-such, you are in *cherem*" — then from the moment the *cherem* is issued, we presume the person has no intention of violating it, and therefore it does not take effect at all at the outset. It takes effect, if ever, only at the moment the person actually chooses to violate the condition. This distinction preserves the rabbinic authority that depended on the *cherem al tnai* as an enforcement tool.

## Practical Application

### Gmar Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְיָשַׁב בָּהּ עַד מוֹת הַכֹּהֵן הַגָּדוֹל״, וּכְתִיב: ״כִּי בְעִיר מִקְלָטוֹ יֵשֵׁב עַד מוֹת הַכֹּהֵן הַגָּדוֹל״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְאַחֲרֵי מוֹת הַכֹּהֵן הַגָּדוֹל״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters,** that the death of these High Priests facilitates the return of the murderer, derived? **Rav Kahana said** they are derived from a verse, **as the verse states: "And he shall dwell there until the death of the High Priest** who was anointed with the sacred oil" (Numbers 35:25), **and it is written: "For in his city of refuge he shall dwell until the death of the High Priest"** (Numbers 35:28), **and it is written: "And after the death of the High Priest** the murderer shall return to his ancestral land" (Numbers 35:28).

The *Mishna* establishes that the operative moment determining which *Kohen Gadol*'s death will free the manslaughterer is *gmar din* — the conclusion of his trial and the formal pronouncement of sentence. It is not sufficient that the manslaughterer has simply arrived at the *ir miklat*; there must be a completed judicial proceeding in which *beit din* formally adjudicated his guilt and sentenced him to exile. The *beit din* taking control of the process — sentencing, returning people from the *ir miklat* for trial, and assigning them back — is foundational to how this institution functions. Once *gmar din* has occurred, the manslaughterer's fate is tied to the *Kohen Gadol* who is in office at that moment.

### Succession Rule

If a *Kohen Gadol* dies during the *proceedings* but before *gmar din*, and a new *Kohen Gadol* is appointed in the interim, and *gmar din* then occurs during the new *Kohen Gadol*'s tenure — *chozer bemitato shel sheni* — it is the death of the second *Kohen Gadol* that will free the manslaughterer. The logic is that the operative *Kohen Gadol* is the one in office at the moment of *gmar din*.

### No High Priest

If there is simply no *Kohen Gadol* in office at the time of *gmar din* — *nigmar dino belo Kohen Gadol* — then *eino yotze misham le'olam*, the manslaughterer is never freed. There is no mechanism by which his release can be triggered. Similarly, if a *Kohen Gadol* himself committed an act of manslaughter and there is a new *Kohen Gadol* in office by the time of his *gmar din*, he too is never freed, since the current *Kohen Gadol* is himself. The source for extending the release to multiple categories of *Kohen Gadol* — anointed, invested by garments, retired — is the three-fold repetition of *Kohen Gadol* in the relevant verses of Bemidbar (35:25, 28, 32), each repetition coming to include one further category. Rabbi Yehuda's fourth category, the *mashuach milchama*, is derived from a fourth verse that mentions only *haKohen* without the qualifier *haGadol* — and the other opinion holds that this verse is simply one of the three already counted.

### Absolute Restrictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחָזַר לִמְקוֹמוֹ עַד מוֹת הַכֹּהֵן הַגָּדוֹל, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנִּשְׁאַל לְהָעִיד בְּעֵדוּת שֶׁל מִצְוָה, וְנִשְׁאַל לְהָעִיד בְּעֵדוּת שֶׁל מָמוֹן, וְנִשְׁאַל לְהָעִיד בְּעֵדוּת שֶׁל קַפִּין – אֵינוֹ יוֹצֵא, וְנִשְׁאַל עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִשְׁאַל לְהָעִיד בְּעֵדוּת שֶׁל קַפִּין – אִי שׁוּם דָּבָר שֶׁהוּא יוּצִיא? אַשְׁכַּחְנָא שֶׁנִּשְׁאַל לְהָעִיד בְּעֵדוּת שֶׁל קַפִּין וַהֲתוּמִים בַּמִּדְבָּר, וְרַבִּי יַעְקֹב בֶּן קוֹרְחָה שׁוֹמְרוֹ בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** From the context of Rav Kahana's teaching, the verse shows: Once confined to the *ir miklat*, the manslaughterer may not leave for any reason whatsoever: not to give testimony even in the context of a *mitzvah*, not for monetary testimony, not even for capital testimony.

Even if all of Israel has need of him — *v'afilu sar tzva Yisrael k'Yoav ben Tzeruyah* — even if he is a military commander as distinguished as Yoav ben Tzeruyah, he may not go forth. The verse *asher nas shamah* — "to which he fled" — is read expansively: *sham tehei diraso, sham tehei misaso, sham tehei kevuraso* — there shall be his dwelling, there his death (if the *Kohen Gadol* does not die in time), and there his burial. The force of *pikuach nefesh* and other overriding considerations might seem to challenge this, and the *Gemara* acknowledges the strangeness of forbidding someone of Yoav's stature from leading an army, yet the prohibition is absolute.

### Environs Protection

Not only the city itself but its *techum* — its surrounding boundary, defined as 2,000 *amos*, the same measure used for the *techum Shabbat* — provides equivalent protection. If the manslaughterer steps outside this boundary, however, the protection lapses immediately.

## Outside City Killing

### Yose HaGalili

The *Mishna* concludes with a debate about the status of the manslaughterer who has left the *ir miklat* or its *techum* and is found by the *go'el hadam*. Rabbi Yose HaGalili rules: *mitzvah b'yad go'el hadam* — there is a *mitzvah* for the *go'el hadam* to kill the manslaughterer who has left the protected area. Furthermore, *urshut b'yad kol adam* — any other person has *permission* to kill him. This is a striking position: the person killed *beshogeg*, yet once he has violated the boundaries of his city of refuge, he is open not merely to the *go'el hadam* but to all. One might read Rabbi Yose HaGalili less as expressing a judgment that the manslaughterer *deserves* death and more as a mechanism to ensure that the conditions of confinement are taken with absolute seriousness — the existential risk of leaving is what keeps the person within the *ir miklat*. Nevertheless, the ruling remains troubling.

### Akiva Position

Rabbi Akiva disagrees: *reshut b'yad go'el hadam* — the *go'el hadam* has permission but no *mitzvah* to kill the manslaughterer outside the city. The *pshat* of the *pesukim* indeed seems to favor Rabbi Akiva's position: the Torah establishes the *arei miklat* precisely to protect the *rotzeach* from the *go'el hadam*, and there is no indication that it *wants* the *go'el hadam* to act when the opportunity presents itself. The Torah cannot entirely eradicate all elements of vigilante justice — it must tolerate them to a degree — but it confers no positive mandate upon them.

### Third Party Liability

The final question concerns a third party — someone other than the *go'el hadam* — who kills the manslaughterer outside the *ir miklat*. Two versions of the *Mishna*'s text exist here. According to the standard reading: *kol adam chayavin alav* — any person who kills him would be liable for murder. According to the alternative *girsa*: *ein chayavin alav* — one would not be liable for having killed him. This remains a deeply unsettled question about how far the Torah intended to reach in tolerating, permitting, or indeed sanctioning the logic of the blood avenger — and how much it meant to constrain it absolutely, tolerating only the narrowest exception in favor of the *go'el hadam*'s permission, while treating any broader extension as murder.
