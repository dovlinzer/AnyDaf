# Hullin 108 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Mishna

### Transition to Basar

With Daf 108a, the tractate transitions away from the rabbinic concerns about eating forbidden and permitted foods together and moves into the weighty biblical prohibitions of *basar bechalav* — meat and milk cooked together. Notably, the Gemara has not yet introduced the question of which types of meat, which types of milk, or which species of animals are subject to this prohibition; that discussion belongs to a later Mishna. For now, the focus is the core biblical concern: the prohibition of cooking meat and milk together.

### Tipat Chalav

The Mishna at the top of 108a opens with the following case: *tipat chalav shenafela al hachaticha* — a drop of milk falls onto a chunk of meat sitting in a stew pot. This Mishna will sound familiar to anyone who studied the Mishna in the chapter of *Gid HaNasheh*. There, however, the *gid* itself was the forbidden substance falling among permitted pieces; here the meat is entirely permitted and the drop of milk is what triggers the concern.

### Taste Determines Status

The Mishna rules: *im yesh ba benotein ta'am b'ota hachaticha asura* — if the milk is detectable by taste in that piece of meat, the piece becomes forbidden as *basar bechalav*. The underlying general rule, which the Mishna expresses qualitatively rather than numerically, is that if the milk constitutes more than one-sixtieth of the meat it imparts a detectable taste and the meat is forbidden; if less than one-sixtieth, the taste is not detectable and the meat remains permitted. How detectability is assessed in practice — whether by estimation from an analogous case or by asking a non-Jewish taster — is a separate matter. What the Mishna establishes is that taste is the operative criterion. Strikingly, even though meat and milk have technically been cooked together, if the milk cannot be tasted in the result, the Torah does not treat it as though they were cooked together, and the meat is not forbidden.

### Stirred Pot

The Mishna continues: *ni'er et hakedira* — if the pot was stirred, then *im yesh ba benotein ta'am b'ota kedira asura* — if the taste is detectable throughout the entire pot, the entire pot is forbidden.

### Two Readings

The natural reading of this clause is that we are measuring whether the taste of the milk has spread throughout the pot. On this reading, the Mishna is making essentially the same point twice: milk must be tasteable in the meat for it to be forbidden, and after stirring, milk must be tasteable throughout the pot for the whole pot to be forbidden.

There is, however, a second way to read the Mishna. On this reading, after the milk fell and was absorbed into the original piece of meat, that piece of meat became *basar bechalav* — forbidden in its entirety, as if it were *chazir*. Now, when this piece of forbidden meat is stirred into the rest of the pot, it is not the taste of milk that is spreading throughout the pot, but the taste of a fully forbidden piece of meat. The forbidden substance being measured is now the entire piece of meat, not merely the original drop of milk. On this reading, the pot becomes forbidden if the taste of that forbidden piece of meat spreads throughout, and the taste in question is the taste of the meat — measured in full units of that piece — not just the taste of the original drop of milk. The Gemara will develop this reading further.

## Abaye vs. Rava

### Abaye's Position

The Gemara opens with a statement of Abaye: *ta'amo v'lo mamasho be-alma d'oraita* — the principle that taste without substance is a biblical concern. This is the principle known as *ta'am k'ikar*, that taste is like the essence of the forbidden thing. The paradigmatic case of *ta'amo v'lo mamasho* is when a piece of *chazir* falls into a kosher pot, the taste disperses, and then the piece is removed. One is left with taste of *chazir* but not the substance itself. Is this a biblical prohibition or only rabbinic? Abaye argues it is biblical, and he derives this from the laws of *basar bechalav*.

His proof: *d'ee salka datach d'rabbanan* — were you to suppose that taste-without-substance is only a rabbinic concern, why can we not prove that taste is a biblical concern from *basar bechalav*? After all, in the case of *basar bechalav*, a drop of milk gives taste to the meat and that makes the meat forbidden — demonstrating that taste is what the Torah cares about.

### Learning from Basar

This argument, however, is already known to the Gemara elsewhere (in tractates *Nazir* and *Pesachim*, among others) and is rejected: *dechidush hu* — *basar bechalav* is an exceptional case, a *chiddush*, and we cannot derive general principles from exceptional cases.

### Exception Theory

The reason *basar bechalav* is exceptional, the objection runs, is that the prohibition is not simply about taste — it is about having cooked them together. Consider: if one soaks cold meat in cold milk until the meat absorbs the milk's taste, this would not be biblically forbidden, even though the taste of milk is thoroughly present in the meat. This demonstrates that *basar bechalav* is not merely about taste but about the act of cooking. Since it is not purely a taste-based prohibition, one cannot cite it as proof that taste is a biblical concern in general.

### Abaye's Proof

Abaye responds to this objection directly. He grants that cooking is a necessary condition for the biblical prohibition. But, he argues, if the focus were entirely on the act of cooking rather than on taste, then *ei chidush hu* — if that were the logic — *af al gav d'leika notein ta'am nami* — even if the milk gave no detectable taste to the meat, you should still be biblically forbidden, because you cooked them together. Yet our Mishna demonstrates otherwise: if the milk is not detectable by taste, the meat is not forbidden. This means that the biblical prohibition is not simply triggered by the bare act of cooking; taste must be present. Since taste is what determines whether the prohibition applies, Abaye concludes that taste is indeed a biblical concern.

### Intermingling Concept

There is a genuine conceptual distinction at stake here. When meat and milk are cooked together, a chemical transformation occurs — the two substances merge into something that is genuinely a new combination, a true intermingling of tastes. By contrast, cold meat soaking in cold milk produces two tastes sitting alongside each other, not genuinely merged. As Rav Soloveitchik famously frames it: the cold scenario produces *basar vechalav* — meat *and* milk, with a *vav* — two separate things coexisting. The cooking scenario produces *basar bechalav* — meat *in* milk, a genuine mixture. Abaye's position is that the Torah requires cooking because cooking effects this true intermingling of tastes, but it is ultimately the intermingling of the tastes that is the problem.

### Rava's Position

Rava disagrees: *derech bishul asra Torah* — the Torah forbade the way of cooking, the act of cooking together, and taste is not itself the prohibited matter. Why then is the prohibition limited to cases where the milk is detectable by taste? Because without detectable taste, the act of cooking was not a *classic* act of cooking — you would not normally cook something with an ingredient that fails to impart any taste. So the taste threshold functions as a limiting condition that defines what counts as genuine cooking, not as evidence that taste per se is what is prohibited. On Rava's account, what is forbidden is not the presence of the intermingled taste but the historical act of having cooked them together.

It should be noted that although Rava appears to reject the biblical status of taste, many *Rishonim* suggest he may be doing so purely for the sake of argument. The overwhelming consensus of *Rishonim* is that *ta'am k'ikar* is indeed a biblical concern. Rashi, however, does read Rava as genuinely rejecting the biblical status of taste.

### Definition of Cooking

Beyond the question of taste, this debate has significant practical consequences for how one defines *bishul* at the biblical level. If, like Rava, the emphasis is on the act of cooking itself, one might be inclined to define cooking formally and narrowly. Other methods of applying heat — roasting, steaming, smoking — might then fall short of the biblical prohibition of *bishul*, at least at the Torah level. If, like Abaye, the emphasis is on the intermingling of tastes effected by heat, the formal category of cooking matters less; what matters is whether the heat produced a genuine merging of tastes. This debate between the *Rishonim* and *Acharonim* about which forms of heat application constitute biblical *bishul* in the context of *basar bechalav* flows directly from this disagreement between Abaye and Rava.

## Chaticha Neveila

### Rav's Ruling

The Gemara now opens what will be the central *sugya* of this section: *chaticha naaseit neveilah* — the piece becomes forbidden. Rav rules: *keivan shenatan ta'am b'chaticha, otah chaticha naaseit neveilah* — once the milk gives taste to that original piece of meat, that piece of meat becomes entirely forbidden, as if it were *neveilah*, as if it were *chazir*. The piece transforms in its entirety.

### Min Bemino

Rav continues: *v'oser kol hachatichot kulam mipnei shehen minah* — and if this piece is then cooked along with the other pieces in the pot, all the other pieces are forbidden, because they are of the same *min*, the same type. This invokes the principle of *min bemino lo batel* — a forbidden substance does not become *batel* among substances of the same type, no matter how many there are. The practical consequence is striking. Suppose you had one unit of milk and fifty units of meat. Ordinarily, sixty times the milk would suffice to render the milk *batel*. But once the milk gives taste to the meat, you no longer have one unit of forbidden milk among fifty units of kosher meat. You now have fifty units of forbidden *basar bechalav*. If that forbidden meat now continues cooking with other pieces of meat, the other pieces of meat are the same *min* as the forbidden piece, and *min bemino lo batel* applies — you cannot negate one piece of meat among a thousand other pieces of meat.

### Forbidden as Meat

There is an additional and subtle dimension to Rav's ruling. One might have supposed that meat cooked with milk becomes forbidden not as meat and not as milk but as a new, hybrid category — something in between. If that were so, the forbidden piece would be *eino mino*, a different type, relative to the plain kosher meat in the pot, and *bitul* might apply in the normal fashion. Rav is telling us otherwise: meat cooked with milk is forbidden *as meat*. Its *min* is meat. The forbidden piece is not some third category; it remains meat, simply meat that has been rendered forbidden. Consequently, it and all the other pieces of meat in the pot are the same *min*, and *min bemino lo batel* renders all the other pieces forbidden as well. This, incidentally, is fully consistent with Rava's understanding of *basar bechalav*: what is forbidden is the act of having cooked them together, not some new hybrid taste. The meat remains meat, categorically.

### Negation Question

This raises an obvious question. The forbidden piece falls into the pot. The pot contains not only other pieces of meat but also vegetables, water, and other ingredients. Even if the other pieces of meat are the same *min* as the forbidden piece and cannot negate it, why can't the *other* ingredients — which are a different *min* — negate it? According to Rava's principle stated in the context of *Gid HaNasheh*: *salek et mino k'mi she'eino, u'she'eino mino rabah alav u'mevatlo* — set aside the same-type items as if they weren't there, and if the different-type items are sufficient in quantity, they can negate the forbidden piece. So why, in a stew full of vegetables and water, can the vegetables not negate the forbidden piece of meat?

### Thick Stew Answer

The Gemara answers: *ei d'nafal berotev rakah hachi nami* — if it fell into a *thin* stew, you would be right. *Hacha bemai askinan d'nafal berotev avah* — the case under discussion is one in which it fell into a *thick* stew. Rashi explains that a *rotev avah* here means a stew that is predominantly meat with very little liquid or other ingredients. There is simply not enough of the non-meat ingredients to constitute sixty times the forbidden piece and negate it. But the Gemara explicitly acknowledges: had there been sufficient vegetables and water, they could indeed negate the forbidden piece, notwithstanding the meat's *min bemino* status relative to the other pieces.

## Efshar Lesochto

### Reversal Question

The Gemara now raises a fundamental conceptual question about Rav's ruling: *u'mai kasavar* — what does Rav hold regarding the theoretical possibility of reversing the forbidden status of the piece of meat? The question is: *ei kasavar efshar lesochto mutar* — if Rav held that were you to squeeze the milk back out of the piece of meat, the piece would revert to being permitted, then how can he maintain that the piece becomes a full *neveilah* and remains forbidden?

### Two Positions

The Gemara formulates the dilemma sharply. *Efshar lesochto mutar* — if in principle the milk could be removed and the status reversed, then the moment the piece of meat continues cooking in the pot and the milk disperses out of it, the original piece of meat should revert to being permitted, and the entire pot should be fine. *Efshar lesochto assur* — only if even theoretically squeezing the milk back out would not restore the piece to being permitted can Rav's ruling stand.

### Status Stickiness

There are two distinct conceptual frameworks for understanding why the piece might remain forbidden even after the milk is removed. The first framework is what might be called the stickiness of status: once a piece of meat has acquired a forbidden status, that status adheres and is not easily reversed. As long as any trace of milk remains — even if it is too small to have originally caused the prohibition — the status persists. Only if every last trace were removed, *efshar lehafrido*, might the piece conceivably revert; even then, the *Acharonim* debate whether it would.

The second framework connects directly to the conceptual debate between Abaye and Rava. If one holds, like Abaye, that the prohibition is about the mixture of tastes, then in principle reversing the mixture — removing the taste — might restore the status. But if one holds, like Rava, that the prohibition is the historical act of having been cooked together, that historical fact cannot be undone. The meat was cooked with milk. That is a fact that persists regardless of the current state of the milk in the pot. On this view, the meat remains forbidden not because of what it currently contains but because of what was done to it.

These three ideas — *chaticha naaseit neveilah*, that the piece remains forbidden as *meat* (not as a hybrid), and *efshar lesochto assur* — are mutually reinforcing and all point toward the same underlying understanding: the problem in *basar bechalav* is the act of cooking, not the resulting mixture of tastes.

### Majority View

The Gemara records that this debate is between major Amoraic authorities. Rav, Rabbi Chanina, and Rabbi Yochanan all hold *efshar lesochto assur* — even if the milk is squeezed out, the piece remains forbidden. Shmuel, Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi, and Resh Lakish hold *efshar lesochto mutar* — if the milk is removed, the piece reverts to being permitted. The more authoritative line — Rav and Rabbi Yochanan — takes the stricter position, and this is the ruling that governs the *sugya* going forward.

## Meat in Milk Pot

### Meat Falls in

The Gemara now reverses the scenario. Until now, the case was milk falling into a pot of meat. Now consider: *kezayit basar shenafal l'tokh yoreh shel chalav* — a *kezayit*-sized chunk of meat falls into a pot of hot milk.

### Rav's Ruling

Rav rules: *basar assur v'chalav mutar* — the meat is forbidden, and the milk is permitted. This ruling is logical on its face. The meat has absorbed the taste of milk and is therefore forbidden as *basar bechalav*. The milk, however, contained a thousand units of milk and only one unit of meat. The taste of meat in the milk is negligible — one part in a thousand — and the milk remains permitted.

---

## Meat in Milk Pot…

### Status Problem

This ruling, however, appears to be in tension with Rav's own position that *efshar lesochto assur*. If the status of the forbidden piece does not revert even when the forbidden ingredient disperses out of it, then consider what is happening here. The milk enters the meat. The meat becomes forbidden. The meat also imparts taste to the milk, but that is trace and not a problem. But now: the milk that entered the meat — which became, as it were, forbidden milk, *basar bechalav* as milk — when that milk goes back out of the piece of meat and disperses back into the pot of milk, why doesn't that forbidden milk render the entire pot of milk forbidden? After all, milk among milk is *min bemino*, and *min bemino lo batel*. So why does Rav say the milk pot is permitted?

---

## Efshar Lesochto…

### Milk Challenge

This challenge is precisely the parallel of the one raised against *chaticha naaseit neveilah*. There, the forbidden meat remained forbidden even as the milk dispersed out of it. Here, the forbidden milk that entered the meat and then dispersed back into the pot — if status is sticky and the milk retains its forbidden character — should render the entire pot forbidden through *min bemino*. The parallel seems exact: just as the forbidden meat cannot become permitted when the milk leaves it, so too the forbidden milk should not become permitted when it disperses back into the pot.

### Torah's Focus

The Gemara resolves this with a ruling that is striking in its precision. *D'amar kra: lo tvashel gdi b'chalav imo* — the Torah says: do not cook a kid in its mother's milk. *Gdi asra Torah v'lo chalav* — the Torah forbade the *gdi*, the meat, and not the milk. This is a remarkable statement. The Torah's focus in the prohibition of *basar bechalav* is on the meat. What is forbidden is cooking the meat in the milk. What becomes prohibited as a result is the meat. The milk is never the primary subject of the Torah's prohibition. Therefore, while the milk that entered the meat does contribute to the meat's forbidden status, that same milk, when it disperses back into the pot, does not itself become a new unit of *basar bechalav* that can in turn forbid the milk pot. The milk is simply not the thing the Torah is addressing.

This ruling is again deeply consistent with the underlying conceptual position: it is the act of cooking the meat that is prohibited, and it is the meat that bears the consequence. The milk is merely the medium. One might note how radical this sounds — milk cooked with meat being entirely permitted — yet the internal logic is coherent given the framing.

The Gemara then immediately raises an objection to the more extreme version of this position. If the Torah truly only forbids the *gdi* and not the milk at all, consider: *chatzi zayit basar v'chatzi zayit chalav shebishlan zeh im zeh* — if one cooked a half-*kezayit* of meat with a half-*kezayit* of milk. Now there is a full *kezayit* of *basar bechalav* — half meat, half milk. *Amar Rav loke al achilato* — Rav says one receives lashes for eating it. But if the Torah only forbids the meat and not the milk, then the person has only consumed a half-*kezayit* of meat, which is *chatzi shiur* and does not incur lashes. The Gemara concludes: *ela olam kasvar Rav chalav nami assur* — in fact, Rav holds that the milk is also forbidden. The statement that the Torah forbade the *gdi* and not the milk is not absolute; it explains why, in the specific case of meat falling into a milk pot, the milk pot is not rendered forbidden by *min bemino*. It is not a general statement that milk can never be forbidden as *basar bechalav*.

## Half-Kezayit Debate

### Half-Kezayit Case

The Gemara now examines the ruling about half-a-*kezayit* of each more carefully. The case: *chatzi zayit basar v'chatzi zayit chalav shebishlan zeh im zeh* — half a *kezayit* of meat and half a *kezayit* of milk cooked together.

### Rav's Position

Rav rules: *loke al achilato, v'eino loke al bishulo* — one receives lashes for eating the combined *kezayit*, but does not receive lashes for the act of cooking it. The reasoning: for eating, one has consumed a full *kezayit* of *basar bechalav* — it is true that it is half meat and half milk, but both halves are *basar bechalav* and they combine to a full *kezayit*, incurring lashes. For cooking, however, Rav does not impose lashes.

---

## Meat in Milk Pot…

### Boiling Pot Answer

Returning to the challenge about the milk pot: the Gemara resolves it by noting that the scenario involves a *yora rotachat* — a boiling vat of milk. *Demivla bala miplat lo palit* — in a boiling vessel, the meat absorbs the milk but does not release it back out. The milk flows in but does not flow back out while the vat is actively boiling. Therefore, the problematic scenario — forbidden milk dispersing back into the pot and rendering it forbidden through *min bemino* — simply does not occur. The meat absorbs the milk but retains it.

The Gemara then raises a follow-up: *ki nayach hadar palit* — surely when the pot cools and stops boiling, the meat will then release the milk back out. The Gemara answers: *kadam vesilko* — one removes the piece of meat from the pot before it cools. This is the practical scenario: the meat falls into the boiling pot, absorbs milk but does not release it while boiling, and is then removed. The milk in the pot has only trace amounts of meat taste — one in a thousand — and remains permitted. The milk that entered the meat has not been released back into the pot. The meat is forbidden. The milk is permitted.

---

## Half-Kezayit Debate…

### Inconsistency

The Gemara returns to the half-*kezayit* ruling and presses an internal inconsistency in Rav's position. *Mima nafshach* — make up your mind. *I mitztarfin abishul nami lilki* — if the half-*kezayit* of meat and half-*kezayit* of milk combine, then they should combine for cooking as well, and one should receive lashes for the act of cooking. *V'ei lo mitztarfin, achila nami lo lilki* — and if they do not combine because they are fundamentally distinct things, then one should not receive lashes for eating either.

### Combination Logic

The Gemara resolves: *leolam lo mitztarfin* — in fact, they do not combine. *Uvaba miyora gedola* — the ruling about lashes for eating refers not to a case of half-a-*kezayit* of each cooked in isolation, but to a different scenario: *b'ba miyora gedola* — coming from a large pot. Rashi explains that this means the following. The Gemara's challenge was not merely an abstract analogy; it was more pointed. If in the act of cooking a half-*kezayit* of meat with a half-*kezayit* of milk one has not transgressed — because neither constitutes a full *kezayit* of the relevant substance — then how does the *product* become forbidden? Given Rav's position that the prohibition is about the act of cooking, if no transgression occurred, the cooking act did not create forbidden food.

The Gemara's answer: Rav's ruling that one receives lashes for eating applies only where the forbidden act of cooking had in fact occurred. Specifically: imagine pot B, which contains a large quantity of meat into which sufficient milk fell — enough to constitute a full act of cooking and create forbidden *basar bechalav*. And imagine pot C, which contains a large quantity of milk into which sufficient meat fell — similarly a full and forbidden act of cooking. From pot B one takes a half-*kezayit* of forbidden meat; from pot C one takes a half-*kezayit* of forbidden milk. Combining and eating these two halves gives one lashes, because each half is already fully forbidden *basar bechalav* from its respective pot in which a complete, prohibited act of cooking occurred. In such a case, the two halves do combine for eating.

By contrast, if one takes a half-*kezayit* of raw meat and a half-*kezayit* of raw milk and cooks them together — where neither constitutes a full *kezayit* of its respective substance — no complete forbidden act of cooking occurred, the product is not forbidden, and one would receive neither lashes for cooking nor lashes for eating.

The essential principle: what makes *basar bechalav* forbidden is the prohibited act of cooking. Where that act was complete, the product is forbidden and can combine with other forbidden products for the purpose of eating. Where that act was incomplete — because neither substance reached the threshold of a *kezayit* — the product carries no forbidden status at all.

### Levi's View

Levi disagrees with Rav's limiting condition. *Af lo ke'al bishulo* — even in the case of a half-*kezayit* of each cooked together, one does receive lashes, and one need not reach the threshold of a full *kezayit* of each. Levi taught this in a *Mishna* (*matnisin*): just as one receives lashes for eating a half-*kezayit* of each, so too for cooking a half-*kezayit* of each.

### Cooking Standard

The Gemara adds one further qualification, attributed in context to this discussion: *ve'eizeh bishul amru — bishul she'hem ochlin oto machmat bishulo* — and what cooking did they mean? They meant cooking to a degree that others would actually eat it. This is significant. Even according to the view that the prohibition is about the act of cooking rather than merely the mixture of tastes, there is a qualitative standard for what constitutes a prohibited act of cooking. Initial heating that begins to mix tastes but does not produce food that others would eat does not qualify. This minimum standard of *bishul* — producing something edible — must be met before a biblical prohibition is incurred and before lashes apply. Rabbinic prohibition may of course begin earlier.

## Baraita & Tannaim

### R. Yehuda's View

The Gemara now demonstrates that the debate over *efshar lesochto* and *chaticha naaseit neveilah* is rooted in a Tannaitic dispute. A *Baraita* is cited: *tipat chalav shenafela al hachaticha* — a drop of milk falls on a piece of meat in the pot. *Keivan shenatan ta'am bachaticha, hachaticha atzmah naaseit neveilah ve'oseret kol hachatichot kulam mipnei shehen minah, divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — once it gives taste to that piece, the piece becomes entirely forbidden and renders all the other pieces of meat in the pot forbidden, because they are of the same *min*. These are the words of Rabbi Yehuda. This is word for word the ruling attributed to Rav, establishing that Rav's position follows Rabbi Yehuda.

### Sages' View

*Vachachamim omrim* — the Sages say otherwise. *Ad shetitein ta'am berotev uvakippa uvachatichot* — the pot is not forbidden unless the taste of the milk spreads and is detectable throughout the entire pot: in the liquid, in the residue (*kippa*), and in the pieces of meat. According to the Sages, the piece of meat does not transform into a new unit of forbidden food that then renders all other pieces of meat forbidden through *min bemino*. Rather, one measures only whether the original drop of milk is detectable throughout the pot.

One might assume that the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Sages is simply about *min bemino* — whether same-type items can negate each other. But the Gemara does not read the dispute that way, because the next statement of Rebbi suggests that the deeper question is about the status of the piece of meat itself.

### Rebbi's Split

Rebbi — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi — offers a nuanced ruling: *nirin divrei Rabbi Yehuda beshelo ni'er veshelo kissa, vedivrei chachamim besheni'er vekhissa* — Rabbi Yehuda's position appears correct in a case where the pot was not stirred and not covered; the Sages' position applies where the pot was stirred and covered.

Rebbi is effectively ruling like Rabbi Yehuda, but limiting the scenario in which Rabbi Yehuda's ruling applies. The distinction is about whether the milk initially concentrates into the original piece of meat or immediately disperses throughout the pot. If the pot was stirred and covered from the outset, the milk never settles on a single piece — it disperses immediately throughout the pot, and one measures only whether the original drop of milk gives taste to the whole pot. If the pot was not stirred and not covered, the drop of milk first settles on and absorbs into the original piece of meat, which then becomes fully forbidden as *basar bechalav*, and only subsequently is that forbidden piece stirred into the rest of the pot.

### Timing Question

The Gemara clarifies what Rebbi must mean. One might think *lo ni'er velo kissa* means the pot was never stirred and never covered at any stage. But then the original piece of meat becomes forbidden, yet no taste ever spreads from it to the other pieces, since nothing is ever stirred. In that case, why should the rest of the pot be forbidden? The Gemara explains: Rebbi must mean that one did not stir initially but did stir later — *lo ni'er bitchila ela besof* — the pot was not stirred at the beginning, allowing the milk to absorb into the original piece and make it forbidden, and only afterward was the pot stirred, causing the taste of that now-forbidden piece to spread to the rest of the pot.

### Gemara Challenge

With this reading in place, the Gemara raises an objection. Once the pot is stirred, the milk that was absorbed into the original piece of meat disperses back out into the pot. The proportions then reverse: the piece of meat, which originally had a full unit of milk giving it taste, now has only a tiny fraction of milk remaining. The milk has mostly left the piece. Should the piece of meat not then revert to being permitted? If so, it should not render the rest of the pot forbidden, and Rebbi's ruling is difficult to maintain.

### Final Answer

The Gemara answers: *kasavar efshar lesochto assur* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that *efshar lesochto assur*. Even when the milk disperses out of the original piece of meat and the piece now has only trace amounts of milk, the piece does not revert to being permitted. It acquired a forbidden status at the moment the milk gave it taste; that status is permanent. The piece remains forbidden as *basar bechalav*, forbidden as meat, and when stirred into the pot, it renders all the other pieces of meat forbidden through *min bemino*. The ruling of Rav, which follows Rabbi Yehuda, and the principle of *efshar lesochto assur* are thus mutually entailing: both are required for Rabbi Yehuda's position to be coherent, and both are confirmed by the *Baraita*. The *sugya* will continue on the first day of Yom Tov.
