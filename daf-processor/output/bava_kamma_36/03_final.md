# Bava Kamma 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hamotzi vs. Sumchos

### Intro: Burden of Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָיוּ הַנִּיזָּקִין שְׁנַיִם – אֶחָד גָּדוֹל וְאֶחָד קָטָן, וְהַמַּזִּיקִין שְׁנַיִם – אֶחָד גָּדוֹל וְאֶחָד קָטָן; הַנִּיזָּק אוֹמֵר: ״גָּדוֹל הִזִּיק אֶת הַגָּדוֹל, וְקָטָן אֶת הַקָּטָן״, וְהַמַּזִּיק אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא כִי, אֶלָּא קָטָן אֶת הַגָּדוֹל, וְגָדוֹל אֶת הַקָּטָן״; אֶחָד תָּם וְאֶחָד מוּעָד, הַנִּיזָּק אוֹמֵר: ״מוּעָד הִזִּיק אֶת הַגָּדוֹל, וְתָם אֶת הַקָּטָן״, וְהַמַּזִּיק אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא כִי, אֶלָּא תָּם אֶת הַגָּדוֹל, וּמוּעָד אֶת הַקָּטָן״ – הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If the **injured** animals **were two** oxen, **one large and** the other **one small, and the ones that caused the damage** were also **two** oxen, **one large and one small,** and **the injured** party **says: The large one injured the large one and the small one injured the small one, and the one liable for damage says: No; rather, the small one** injured **the large one,** in which case, if half the value of the belligerent ox does not cover half the damage, he is not required to pay more, **and the large one** injured **the small one;** or, similarly, if **one** of the belligerent oxen was **innocuous and one forewarned,** and **the injured** party **says:** The **forewarned** ox **injured the large** one, **and** the **innocuous** ox injured **the small one, and the one liable for damage says: No; rather,** the **innocuous** ox injured **the large one and** the **forewarned** ox injured **the small one;** here too, **the burden of proof rests upon the claimant.**

The present sugya continues from the bottom of 35b and engages a *mishna* that establishes the principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav haraya*: the *nizak* who seeks to claim that a higher liability applies bears the burden of proof. The *gemara* notes that this ruling stands in direct tension with the position of *Sumchos*, who holds that in any case of objective doubt — *safek* — the disputed amount is split between the parties. The *gemara* therefore asks whether *Sumchos* would maintain his position of *chokim* even in a case of *bari ve-bari*, where both the *nizak* and the *mazik* each make definite, unequivocal claims — no, that happened; no, this happened — and concludes that he would. Even in *bari ve-bari*, *Sumchos* says you split, because the *safek* is objective regardless of how confident each party sounds. Our *mishna*, which insists on *hamotzi mechavero alav haraya*, is therefore in direct conflict with *Sumchos*.

### Bari vs. Shema

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: [זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת] חֲלוּקִים עָלָיו חֲבֵירָיו עַל סוֹמְכוֹס, דְּאָמַר: מָמוֹן הַמּוּטָּל בְּסָפֵק – חוֹלְקִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: אָמַר סוֹמְכוֹס אֲפִילּוּ בָּרִי וּבָרִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, אָמַר סוֹמְכוֹס אֲפִילּוּ בָּרִי וּבָרִי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna rules that in a case where there is uncertainty with regard to whether it was the pursuing ox that caused the injury to the other ox, the burden of proof rests upon the claimant. **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says: That is to say** that **the colleagues of Sumakhos, who says** that **property of uncertain ownership is divided, disagree with his** opinion. **Rabbi Abba bar Memel said to Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba: Does Sumakhos state** this principle **even** in the case of **a certain** claim **and a certain** claim, i.e., where both parties maintain the certainty of their claims, as appears to be the case in the mishna? Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba **said to him: Yes, Sumakhos says** this **even** in a case of **a certain** claim **and a certain** claim.

Once the *gemara* establishes that the *mishna* opposes *Sumchos* even in *bari ve-bari*, it turns to a related implication of the *mishna*'s language. The *mishna* seems to suggest that even if the *nizak* cannot prove his larger claim, he is at least entitled to collect whatever the *mazik* concedes. The *gemara* asks: if this is a case of *bari ve-bari* — where each party flatly rejects the other's account — why would the *nizak* be entitled to anything at all? If the *nizak* is claiming the *mazik*'s larger ox gored his ox, and the *mazik* says it was definitively the smaller one, then the *nizak* is actively rejecting the *mazik*'s own admission. This leads directly to the principle of *tanu chitin ve-hodo lo bise'orin*.

### Chitin v. Se'orin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא – אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר נָתָן: טְעָנוֹ חִטִּין וְהוֹדָה לוֹ בִּשְׂעוֹרִין – פָּטוּר. מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? תְּנֵינָא: טְעָנוֹ חִטִּין וְהוֹדָה לוֹ בִּשְׂעוֹרִין – פָּטוּר!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to discuss **the** matter **itself** that was mentioned above. **Rabba bar Natan says:** If one **claims** that another owes him **wheat, and** the other **concedes** that he owes **him barley,** the latter is **exempt** from payment. The Gemara asks: **What** novel element **is** this **teaching us? We** already **learned** it in a mishna: If one **claims** that another owes him **wheat, and** the other **concedes** that he owes **him barley,** he is **exempt**.

*Rava bar Natan* teaches: if a claimant says "you owe me wheat" and the defendant says "I owe you only barley," the defendant is entirely exempt — *patur* — even from paying barley. The *gemara* here applies this principle: since the claimant is rejecting the defendant's concession, he cannot then turn around and collect based on it. The *tanu chitin ve-hodo lo bise'orin* case is taught explicitly in a *braita*, and *Rava bar Natan*'s innovation is that the defendant is *patur le-gamri* — completely exempt. One might have thought the ruling only meant the claimant cannot collect the wheat; he should still be entitled to the barley which was conceded. *Rava bar Natan* teaches that this is not so: since the claimant denied that he was owed barley, his concession is rejected and nothing is owed.

### Shor Tam Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: אָמַר סוֹמְכוֹס אֲפִילּוּ בָּרִי וּבָרִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, אָמַר סוֹמְכוֹס אֲפִילּוּ בָּרִי וּבָרִי. וּמִדְּרֵישָׁא בָּרִי וּבָרִי – סֵיפָא נָמֵי בָּרִי וּבָרִי; אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: הָיָה אֶחָד גָּדוֹל וְאֶחָד קָטָן, נִיזָּק אוֹמֵר: ״גָּדוֹל הִזִּיק״, וּמַזִּיק אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא כִי, אֶלָּא קָטָן הִזִּיק״... הָא לָא מַיְיתֵי רְאָיָה – שָׁקֵיל כִּדְאָמַר מַזִּיק; נֵימָא תִּהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבָּה בַּר נָתָן! אֶלָּא בְּבָרִי וְשֶׁמָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **From** the suggestion **that** the case in **the first clause** of the mishna is one where there is **a certain** claim **and a certain** claim, it may be inferred that **the latter clause,** the subsequent clause in the mishna, **also** is referring to a case where there is **a certain** claim **and a certain** claim. If so, **say the latter clause:** If **one was large and** the other **one small,** and **the injured** party **says** that **the large** ox **caused the damage, and the one liable for damage says: No; rather, the small** ox **caused the damage...**  This indicates that **if he does not bring proof** for his claim, **he takes** compensation **according to what the one liable for damage says.** If it is a case where both parties maintain certainty of their claims, **let us say** that this mishna **is a conclusive refutation of** the statement of **Rabba bar Natan.** **Rather,** this clause must be referring to a case where there is **a certain** claim **and an uncertain** claim, i.e., one party maintains certainty of his claim, while the other merely states his claim as a possibility.

The *gemara* applies this logic to the *shor tam* cases in the *mishna*. If the *mazik* claims it was his smaller ox that gored and the *nizak* says no, it was definitely the larger one, the *nizak* cannot collect the smaller amount because his *bari* claim constitutes a rejection of the *mazik*'s admission. The *mishna*'s implication that the *nizak* collects at least based on what the *mazik* conceded must therefore be read as applying only when the *nizak* himself is making a *shema* claim — an uncertain claim — rather than a definite one.

The *gemara* asks: if the *mishna*'s implication of entitlement to the lesser amount requires the *nizak* to be saying *shema*, then who is saying *bari* and who is saying *shema*? The *gemara* considers and rejects the possibility that the *nizak* is saying *bari* and the *mazik* is saying *shema*, because in that configuration the *nizak* would still be saying definitively that he is owed the larger amount, while the *mazik* says only "maybe" — and in that case the *chitin ve-se'orin* problem resurfaces: if the *mazik* is saying *shema* about the large ox, has he truly conceded anything about the small one that the *nizak* can collect?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּקָאָמַר בָּרִי מַאן, דְּקָאָמַר שֶׁמָּא מַאן? אִי נֵימָא דְּקָאָמַר נִיזָּק בָּרִי וְקָאָמַר מַזִּיק שֶׁמָּא, אַכַּתִּי לֵימָא תִּהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבָּה בַּר נָתָן! אֶלָּא דְּקָאָמַר נִיזָּק שֶׁמָּא, וְקָאָמַר מַזִּיק בָּרִי. וּמִדְּסֵיפָא נִיזָּק שֶׁמָּא וּמַזִּיק בָּרִי, רֵישָׁא נָמֵי נִיזָּק שֶׁמָּא וּמַזִּיק בָּרִי;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who is** the one **who states a certain** claim, and **who is** the one **who states an uncertain** claim? **If we say that** it is **the injured** party who **states** with **a certain** claim that the large ox caused the injury, **and** it is **the one liable for damage** who **states an uncertain claim** that it was the small one, **let us still say that** the mishna **is a conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Rabba bar Natan.** **Rather,** it must be a case **where the injured** party **states an uncertain** claim that it was the large ox that caused the injury, **and the one liable for damage states a certain** claim that it was the small one. Since the claimant does not contradict the defendant's admission, the latter is liable to pay the amount that he admitted he owes. The Gemara notes: **And from** the fact **that the latter clause** of the mishna is referring to a case where **the injured** party states **an uncertain** claim **and the one liable for damage** states **a certain** claim, it may be inferred that **the first clause** is **also** referring to a case where **the injured** party states **an uncertain** claim that it was the defendant's ox that injured his ox, **and the one liable for damage** states **a certain** claim that it was injured by a stone. It is not referring to a case of two certain claims as inferred above.

The *gemara* concludes: the case where the *mishna* implies the *nizak* collects at least the smaller amount must be where the *nizak* is saying *shema* — "I don't know, perhaps it was your larger one" — and the *mazik* is saying *bari*, definitively claiming it was the smaller one. In that configuration, the *nizak* is not rejecting the *mazik*'s admission; he is only uncertain about whether he is owed more. He therefore does not trigger the *chitin ve-se'orin* exemption and may collect based on what the *mazik* concedes.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: הֲרֵי זֶה מִשְׁתַּלֵּם עַל הַקָּטָן מִן הַגָּדוֹל, וְלַגָּדוֹל מִן הַקָּטָן! דִּתְפַס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that the injured party **receives payment for** his **small ox from the** liable party's **large** ox, **and for** his **large** ox **from the** liable party's **small** ox? Evidently he does receive payment. The Gemara answers: The *baraita* is referring to a case **where** the injured party **seized** the defendant's ox, in which case the court allows it to remain in his possession, since his taking it was appropriate, although the court cannot compel the defendant to pay him *ab initio*.

A further wrinkle: the *gemara* notes a *braita* that states the *nizak* collects — *mishtalem al ha-katan min ha-gadol* — and resolves this with the answer *de-tafas*: the *nizak* had seized the ox. This is a somewhat difficult resolution. If *tefisa* — seizure — is sufficient to switch who is considered the *muchzak*, one might expect the *nizak* to be entitled to his full larger claim. The *gemara* limits recovery to the smaller conceded amount even after *tefisa*, which raises the question of precisely what *tefisa* accomplishes here.

### Sumchos Extreme Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר סוֹמְכוֹס אֲפִילּוּ בְּהָא – דְּאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן דְּלָא?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does Sumakhos state** his ruling that the disputed property is divided between the two parties **even with regard to this** case, where the claimant states an uncertain claim, so **that it was necessary** for the mishna **to teach us that** according to the other Rabbis it is **not** divided? Certainly Sumakhos would concede that in this case the burden of proof rests upon the claimant.

Having established that the *mishna*'s *seyfa* — where the *nizak* collects the lesser conceded amount — involves a case of *nizak shema* and *mazik bari*, the *gemara* asks whether *Sumchos* would say *chokim* even in this configuration. This is actually a more extreme version of the *Sumchos* position: the *mazik* is the *muchzak* — he has the money in his possession — and he is making a definite claim, while the *nizak* who seeks to extract money from him is only saying *shema*, he is not even certain of his own claim. Would *Sumchos* really say to split the disputed amount in a case where the claimant himself is uncertain and the one in possession is confident? That is a striking and counterintuitive position.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; סֵיפָא נִיזָּק שֶׁמָּא וּמַזִּיק בָּרִי, רֵישָׁא נִיזָּק בָּרִי וּמַזִּיק שֶׁמָּא. וְהָא לָא דָּמְיָא רֵישָׁא לְסֵיפָא! אָמְרִי: בָּרִי וְשֶׁמָּא, שֶׁמָּא וּבָרִי – חַד מִילְּתָא הִיא; בָּרִי וּבָרִי, שֶׁמָּא וּבָרִי – תְּרֵי מִילֵּי נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No;** although **the latter clause** is referring to a case where **the injured** party states **an uncertain** claim **and the one liable for damage** states **a certain** claim, **the first clause** is referring to a case where **the injured** party states **a certain** claim **and the one liable for damage** states **an uncertain** claim. It is with regard to this case that the Rabbis disagree with the opinion of Sumakhos that the property is divided. The Gemara questions this interpretation: **But** according to this interpretation, **the first clause is not similar to the last clause.** Why not explain the first clause as referring to a case of two certain claims? The Sages **said** in response: A case of **a certain** claim **and an uncertain** claim, and a case of **an uncertain** claim **and a certain** claim, **are one matter.** Therefore, the two clauses are congruent. By contrast, a case of **a certain** claim **and a certain** claim, and a case of **an uncertain** claim **and a certain** claim, **are two matters.** Therefore, the first clause of the mishna cannot be referring to a case of two certain claims.

The *gemara* says yes, *Sumchos* would go that far. And the argument makes sense within *Sumchos*'s framework: if the *safek* is objective, the relative confidence of the parties is irrelevant to the question of how to distribute property in doubt.

### Mishna's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּמוּעָדִין. אִי בְּמוּעָדִין, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: הָיָה אֶחָד גָּדוֹל וְאֶחָד קָטָן, הַנִּיזָּק אוֹמֵר: ״גָּדוֹל הִזִּיק״, וְהַמַּזִּיק אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא כִי, אֶלָּא קָטָן הִזִּיק״ – הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה. אִי בְּמוּעָדִין, מַאי נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִינֵּיהּ? סוֹף סוֹף, דְּמֵי תּוֹרָא מְעַלְּיָא בָּעֵי לְשַׁלּוֹמֵי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: סֵיפָא בְּתַמִּין, וְרֵישָׁא בְּמוּעָדִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא סָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אִי בְּמוּעָדִין, ״חַיָּיבִים״?! ״חַיָּיב גַּבְרָא״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! וְתוּ, מַאי ״שְׁנֵיהֶם״?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi rejected this inference: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing **with forewarned** oxen, in which case the injured party does not collect damages exclusively from the belligerent ox but rather from any of its owner's property. Therefore, he can collect from either ox as compensation. Rava of Parzika replied: **If** the mishna is referring **to forewarned** oxen, **say the latter clause** of the mishna: If **one** of the belligerent oxen **was large and** the other **one** was **small,** and **the injured** party **says** that **the large** ox **caused the damage, but the one liable for damage says: No; rather, the small** ox **caused the damage, the burden of proof rests upon the claimant. If** this is referring **to forewarned** oxen, **what difference does it make to him** which one caused the injury? **Ultimately,** the liable party **is required to pay** the **value of a full ox** in any case. Rav Ashi **said to him: The latter clause** of the mishna is referring **to innocuous** oxen, **and the first clause** is referring **to forewarned** oxen. **Rav Aḥa the Elder said to Rav Ashi: If** it is referring **to** a case of **forewarned** oxen, where compensation is not collected specifically from the belligerent ox, why does the mishna state: **They are liable,** in the plural form? **It should have** stated: The **man is liable. And furthermore, what** is the word **both** in the statement: Both are liable, referring to?

The *gemara* now resolves the full picture. The *mishna* contains cases in both directions: some where the *nizak* is saying *shema* and the *mazik* is saying *bari* (*nizak shema, mazik bari*), and others where the *nizak* is saying *bari* and the *mazik* is saying *shema* (*nizak bari, mazik shema*). The *gemara* notes that these are not two entirely different genres — in each, one party is *bari* and the other is *shema* — they are the same genre with the parties reversed, and *chada milta hi*, it is one type of case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְעוֹלָם בְּתַמִּין; וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא, דְּאָמַר: שׁוּתָּפִין נִינְהוּ; וְטַעְמָא דְּאִיתַנְהוּ לְתַרְוַיְיהוּ – דְּלָא מָצֵי מְדַחֵי לֵיהּ, אֲבָל לֵיתַנְהוּ לְתַרְוַיְיהוּ – מָצֵי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל אַיְיתִי רְאָיָה דְּהַאי תּוֹרָא אַזְּקָךְ, וַאֲשַׁלֵּם לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the first clause of the mishna is **actually** referring **to innocuous** oxen, **and it is** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who says** that the injured party and the liable party **are partners,** as they share ownership of the belligerent ox. **And** therefore, **the reason** that the owner of the belligerent oxen is liable is that **the two of them are** available, **so he cannot dismiss** the injured party by claiming that this ox is not the one that caused the injury. **But** if **the two of them are not** available, e.g., if one died or was lost, **he can say to** the injured party: **Go bring proof that** it was **this ox** that **caused you damage, and I will pay you** compensation.

The *mishna*'s purpose in spanning both configurations is to make the most powerful possible case against *Sumchos*. The *reysha* — where the *nizak* is *bari* and the *muchzak* is saying *shema* — is precisely the scenario where *Sumchos*'s position has the strongest logical support: the *nizak* makes a definite claim, the person in possession is only uncertain, and yet the *mishna* still rules *hamotzi mechavero alav haraya*. Even there, the *nizak* bears the burden of proof and gets nothing if he cannot meet it.

At the same time, the *mishna*'s implication that in some cases the *nizak* does collect the lesser amount — where he himself was saying *shema* and is not rejecting the *mazik*'s concession — is preserved. The *mishna* thus navigates between rejecting *Sumchos* on one end and avoiding the full harshness of *chitin ve-se'orin* on the other, with the key variable being whether the *nizak*'s own claim is *bari* or *shema*.

## Shnayim Chayavim

### Both Oxen Present

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם שֶׁל אִישׁ אֶחָד – שְׁנֵיהֶם חַיָּיבִים.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where an ox was injured by one of two oxen that were pursuing it, if **both** oxen **belonged to one person, both are liable.**

The *mishna* states: if both oxen belonged to one person, *shnayhem chayavim* — both are liable. At first glance the meaning seems obvious: the owner of both oxen is clearly liable for the goring. But the precise phrasing — *shnayhem chayavim*, both of them are *chayav* — is curious, since in this case we do not know which of the two oxen actually gored. Saying both are liable requires explanation, because at most one of them did the damage.

The *gemara* draws a *diyuk* from the phrase *shnayhem chayavim*: the *mishna* is not simply saying the owner owes money. *Chayavim* refers to the oxen themselves, implying that both oxen must be present and available in order for the *nizak* to collect. *Tosafot* notes the *diyuk* — that the *mishna*'s language points to a rule about requiring both oxen to be on hand, even though in this particular case it is not literally true that both gored.

### Tam vs. Muad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּמוּעָדִין. אִי בְּמוּעָדִין, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: הָיָה אֶחָד גָּדוֹל וְאֶחָד קָטָן, הַנִּיזָּק אוֹמֵר: ״גָּדוֹל הִזִּיק״, וְהַמַּזִּיק אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא כִי, אֶלָּא קָטָן הִזִּיק״ – הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה. אִי בְּמוּעָדִין, מַאי נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִינֵּיהּ? סוֹף סוֹף, דְּמֵי תּוֹרָא מְעַלְּיָא בָּעֵי לְשַׁלּוֹמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi rejected this inference: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing **with forewarned** oxen, in which case the injured party does not collect damages exclusively from the belligerent ox but rather from any of its owner's property. Therefore, he can collect from either ox as compensation. Rava of Parzika replied: **If** the mishna is referring **to forewarned** oxen, **say the latter clause** of the mishna: If **one** of the belligerent oxen **was large and** the other **one** was **small,** and **the injured** party **says** that **the large** ox **caused the damage, but the one liable for damage says: No; rather, the small** ox **caused the damage, the burden of proof rests upon the claimant. If** this is referring **to forewarned** oxen, **what difference does it make to him** which one caused the injury? **Ultimately,** the liable party **is required to pay** the **value of a full ox** in any case.

The *gemara* raises the question of whether this case involves *tamim* or *muadim*. The *gemara* initially considers whether the *mishna* might be discussing *muad* oxen in the opening case. However, this is rejected: the *seifa* of the *mishna* debates whether the big ox or the small ox gored, and that debate would be irrelevant if the oxen were *muad*, since a *muad* owner pays full damages regardless of which ox caused the harm — what matters for a *muad* is the size of the damage suffered by the *nizak*, not which ox caused it. Since the *mishna* clearly cares which ox gored, it must be discussing *tamim*. The *gemara* therefore reads the opening case as *muadim* and the later case as *tamim*, then ultimately concludes the whole *mishna* addresses *tamim* oxen and invokes Rabbi Akiva's *shutfin* model.

### Akiva's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: סֵיפָא בְּתַמִּין, וְרֵישָׁא בְּמוּעָדִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא סָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אִי בְּמוּעָדִין, ״חַיָּיבִים״?! ״חַיָּיב גַּבְרָא״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! וְתוּ, מַאי ״שְׁנֵיהֶם״? אֶלָּא לְעוֹלָם בְּתַמִּין; וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא, דְּאָמַר: שׁוּתָּפִין נִינְהוּ; וְטַעְמָא דְּאִיתַנְהוּ לְתַרְוַיְיהוּ – דְּלָא מָצֵי מְדַחֵי לֵיהּ, אֲבָל לֵיתַנְהוּ לְתַרְוַיְיהוּ – מָצֵי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל אַיְיתִי רְאָיָה דְּהַאי תּוֹרָא אַזְּקָךְ, וַאֲשַׁלֵּם לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said to him: The latter clause** of the mishna is referring **to innocuous** oxen, **and the first clause** is referring **to forewarned** oxen. **Rav Aḥa the Elder said to Rav Ashi: If** it is referring **to** a case of **forewarned** oxen, where compensation is not collected specifically from the belligerent ox, why does the mishna state: **They are liable,** in the plural form? **It should have** stated: The **man is liable. And furthermore, what** is the word **both** in the statement: Both are liable, referring to? **Rather,** the first clause of the mishna is **actually** referring **to innocuous** oxen, **and it is** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who says** that the injured party and the liable party **are partners,** as they share ownership of the belligerent ox. **And** therefore, **the reason** that the owner of the belligerent oxen is liable is that **the two of them are** available, **so he cannot dismiss** the injured party by claiming that this ox is not the one that caused the injury. **But** if **the two of them are not** available, e.g., if one died or was lost, **he can say to** the injured party: **Go bring proof that** it was **this ox** that **caused you damage, and I will pay you** compensation.

The *gemara* concludes: *ela le-olam be-tamim ve-Rabbi Akiva hi de-amar shutfin ninhu* — the *mishna* always speaks of *tamim* oxen, and it follows Rabbi Akiva who holds that the *nizak* becomes a *shutaf* — a partner — in the goring ox. *Ve-taama de-itnayhu le-tarvayhu*: the reason the *nizak* is entitled to collect at all is because both oxen are still present. If both are alive and available, the *nizak* can say to the owner: one of these is partially mine, I have rights to one of them — you decide which one to give me, but you cannot deny that one of them is mine.

This is the correct reading of *shnayhem chayavim*: it is not that both oxen are each independently *chayav*. Rather, the presence of both together is what generates the obligation. If one of the oxen died or ran away, the *mazik* can say: perhaps the one that is gone was the one that gored you, and your lien — your partnership interest — was in that one. Since you cannot prove otherwise, *matzi amar lei zil ayisi raya de-hai tora azikach ve-ashalem lach* — go and prove that this living ox is the one that gored you, and I will pay. Without that proof, the *nizak* cannot collect.

### Yishmael vs. Akiva

The *gemara*'s attribution of the *mishna* to Rabbi Akiva rather than Rabbi Yishmael flows from a fundamental structural difference between their positions. Rabbi Yishmael holds that the relationship between the *nizak* and the *mazik* is one of *ba'al chov* — creditor and debtor. The *nizak* has a monetary claim, and the goring ox serves as the vehicle through which that claim is collected; the *nizak* holds a lien focused on the ox. Rabbi Akiva holds that the *nizak* becomes a *shutaf* — an actual co-owner — in the goring ox itself, up to the value of the half-damage owed.

The question of why the *mishna* cannot follow Rabbi Yishmael is sharpened here. If the *nizak* has a debt claim against the *mazik* — not merely an interest in the ox — then even if one of the two oxen dies or disappears, the debt remains. Why should the *nizak* lose his claim? The *mazik* still owes him money. The *gemara* therefore says the *mishna* must be Rabbi Akiva: under the partnership model, the *nizak*'s entire entitlement is tied to his co-ownership of a specific ox, and if that ox is gone, there is nothing left to collect from.

### Tosafot Analysis

*Tosafot* engages a significant dispute here with *Rashi*. *Rashi* understands that according to Rabbi Yishmael, the *nizak* is not limited to collecting from the ox alone; since the relationship is one of debt, the *nizak* retains the right to collect money from the *mazik* even if the ox is no longer available. Under *Rashi*'s reading, losing the ox does not extinguish the *nizak*'s rights — he can simply demand payment.

*Tosafot* disagrees and marshals a powerful proof from elsewhere in the *gemara*, which states that according to Rabbi Yishmael, if the *mazik* slaughtered the goring ox — *shachto* — the act stands and the *nizak* loses his money. *Tosafot* asks: if the *nizak* truly has an independent debt claim under Rabbi Yishmael, why is he left empty-handed when the ox is slaughtered? Let him simply demand cash payment instead. The fact that the *gemara* does not propose this answer indicates that under Rabbi Yishmael as well, the *nizak*'s right is fundamentally tied to the ox. The *mazik* may choose to pay cash in order to buy out the *nizak*'s lien, but the *nizak* cannot independently demand cash — his right runs against the ox and only against the ox.

*Tosafot* therefore concludes that it is *pashita* — self-evident — that under Rabbi Yishmael, if one of the two oxen is gone, the *nizak* loses out, because the *mazik* can simply assert that the departed ox was the one that gored. The *gemara*'s attribution of the *mishna* to Rabbi Akiva is needed not because Rabbi Yishmael would produce a different result in this case, but because Rabbi Akiva's *shutfin* model might have suggested a different outcome — namely that the *nizak*'s established co-ownership creates a *chazaka* — a presumption — entitling him to collect even if one ox is gone. The *mishna* therefore needs to specify that Rabbi Akiva's rule too requires both oxen to be present.

## Multiple Gorings

### R. Meir: Last First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שׁוֹר שֶׁנָּגַח אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה שְׁוָורִים זֶה אַחַר זֶה – יְשַׁלֵּם לָאַחֲרוֹן שֶׁבָּהֶם, וְאִם יֵשׁ בּוֹ מוֹתָר – יַחֲזִיר לְשֶׁלְּפָנָיו, וְאִם יֵשׁ בּוֹ מוֹתָר – יַחֲזִיר לְשֶׁלִּפְנֵי פָנָיו, וְהָאַחֲרוֹן אַחֲרוֹן נִשְׂכָּר; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to an innocuous **ox that gored four or five** other **oxen one after the other,** its owner **shall pay** the owner of **the last** one **of them** half of the damages from the proceeds of the sale of the belligerent ox; **and if there is surplus** value left **in** his ox after he pays that owner, **he shall return** it **to** the owner of **the previous** ox that was gored; **and if there is** still **surplus** value left **in** his ox after he pays that owner, **he shall return** it **to the** one **prior to the previous** one. The principle is that the owner of **the latest** of the oxen gored in succession **gains.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

A new *mishna* introduces a case of sequential gorings: an ox gores four or five oxen one after another — *ze achar ze*. The *tanna kamma*, identified as Rabbi Meir, rules *acharon acharon nishkar*: the last victim collects first. Whatever remains in the value of the goring ox after the last victim is paid goes to the second-to-last, then to the one before that, and so on, working backward. The logic and structural justification of this position become the subject of extensive *gemara* analysis below.

### R. Shimon: Partners

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה מָאתַיִם שֶׁנָּגַח לְשׁוֹר שָׁוֶה מָאתַיִם, וְאֵין הַנְּבֵלָה יָפָה כְּלוּם – זֶה נוֹטֵל מָנֶה, וְזֶה נוֹטֵל מָנֶה. חָזַר וְנָגַח שׁוֹר אַחֵר שָׁוֶה מָאתַיִם – הָאַחֲרוֹן נוֹטֵל מָנֶה; וְשֶׁלְּפָנָיו – זֶה נוֹטֵל חֲמִשִּׁים זוּז, וְזֶה נוֹטֵל חֲמִשִּׁים זוּז. חָזַר וְנָגַח שׁוֹר אַחֵר שָׁוֶה מָאתַיִם – הָאַחֲרוֹן נוֹטֵל מָנֶה, וְשֶׁלְּפָנָיו חֲמִשִּׁים זוּז, וּשְׁנַיִם הָרִאשׁוֹנִים – דִּינַר זָהָב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says** that the division of the compensation is as follows: With regard to an innocuous **ox worth two hundred** dinars **that gored an ox worth two hundred** dinars, thereby killing it, **and the carcass is worthless,** the injured party **takes one hundred dinars,** i.e., half the cost of the damage, from the proceeds of the sale of the belligerent ox, **and** the owner of the belligerent ox **takes** the remaining **one hundred dinars.** If the ox, after goring the first ox but before compensation had been paid, **again gored another ox worth two hundred** dinars, and the carcass is worthless, the owner of **the last** ox that was gored **takes one hundred dinars; and** with regard to payment for **the previous** goring, the owner of **this** ox that was gored **takes fifty dinars,** which is half the remaining value of the belligerent ox after one hundred dinars were paid to the last injured party, **and** the owner of **that** belligerent ox **takes** the remaining **fifty dinars.** If the ox, after goring the first two oxen but before compensation had been paid, again **gored another ox worth two hundred** dinars, and the carcass is worthless, **the last** injured party **takes one hundred dinars, the previous one** takes **fifty dinars, and the first two,** i.e., the first injured party and the owner of the belligerent ox, divide the remainder, each receiving **one gold dinar,** which is worth twenty-five silver dinars.

Rabbi Shimon takes a fundamentally different approach, one rooted in the partnership model of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Shimon presents the case numerically: an ox worth 200 gores an ox worth 200, and the carcass of the victim is worth nothing. The *nizak* takes 100 — half the damage — and becomes a partner in the goring ox to the extent of 100. Now this jointly owned ox — belonging to both the original owner and the first victim — turns and gores another ox worth 200. The last victim takes 100 from the jointly owned ox. Whatever remains of the joint ownership is split proportionally. The second goring is collected equally from both co-owners of the ox at the time of the second goring. And so on: each subsequent goring is distributed proportionally according to each party's ownership share in the ox at the moment of impact.

Concretely, in a case of three sequential gorings where all oxen are worth 200 and all carcasses are worth nothing: the third victim gets a full 100; the second victim, who owns half the ox, and the first victim and original owner who each own a quarter, each receive 50 and 25 and 25 respectively from the third goring's distribution — but accounting as well for what each had already collected. The *mishna* states that after three gorings the last victim takes 100, the one before him takes 50, and the first two each take a *dinar zahav* — 25 *zuz*.

### Awareness Caveat

*Tosafot* raises an important qualification to Rabbi Shimon's model. The *mishna* presents a situation where each successive *nizak* automatically becomes a part-owner of the ox and thereby assumes responsibility for its future conduct. But *Tosafot* asks: how can a person become liable for an ox's future gorings if he had no knowledge that he had become a co-owner, or if he had no practical ability to watch the animal?

*Tosafot* admits this creates a *teima* — a difficulty — and offers a somewhat forced answer: since the new co-owner has the ability in principle to bring witnesses and prove his ownership and win in court, his responsibility for the ox begins from the moment the goring occurs. This is so even though the court case has not yet been adjudicated and the co-ownership has not yet been formally established — *kivan she-yesh be-yado le-havi edim le-altar*, since he has the capacity to bring witnesses immediately, his liability attaches now. The retroactive legal establishment of ownership creates retroactive liability.

Crucially, however, *Tosafot* makes clear that this reasoning applies only when the new co-owner had actual knowledge of what occurred and had the practical ability to watch the ox. If he was not in a position to guard it — if he was elsewhere, or unaware — then the subsequent goring cannot be charged to his portion. In that case, the full liability falls on *Reuven*, the original owner. The new co-owner is in the position of an *oneis* — someone facing an unavoidable circumstance — and bears no liability for what he could not have prevented.

### Attribution to Amoraim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַתְנִיתִין מַנִּי? דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וּדְלָא כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא! אִי כְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל דְּאָמַר בַּעֲלֵי חוֹבוֹת נִינְהוּ, הַאי ״אַחֲרוֹן אַחֲרוֹן נִשְׂכָּר״?! ״רִאשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן נִשְׂכָּר״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אִי כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּאָמַר תּוֹרָא דְשׁוּתָּפֵי הוּא, הַאי ״יֵשׁ בּוֹ מוֹתָר – יַחֲזִיר לְשֶׁלְּפָנָיו״?! ״לְכוּלָּם״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Whose** opinion is expressed in the **mishna?** Apparently the mishna is **neither in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, nor in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva** (see 33a). The Gemara explains: **If** the mishna had been **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, who says** that the owners of the gored oxen **are** considered **creditors** of the owner of the belligerent ox, then instead of stating **this** *halakha* that the owner of **the latest** of the oxen gored in succession **gains,** the mishna **should have** stated that the owner of **the earliest** of the oxen gored in succession **gains,** since the creditor to whom the ox is initially liened collects first. And **if** the mishna had been **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who says** that **the** belligerent **ox belongs to** its owner and to the injured party, who are considered **partners** in the ownership of the ox, then instead of stating **this** ruling that **if there is surplus** value remaining in the ox after he pays that owner, **he shall return** it **to** the owner of the **previous** ox, the mishna **should have** ruled that the surplus shall be returned **to all of them,** i.e., all the prior injured parties, since they all share joint ownership of the belligerent ox.

The *gemara* asks whose opinion is reflected in the opening case of the *mishna* — the *acharon acharon nishkar* rule of Rabbi Meir. It observes that this case fits neither Rabbi Yishmael nor Rabbi Akiva cleanly. Under Rabbi Yishmael's debt model, the first victim should have the earliest lien and therefore the strongest priority claim — *rishon rishon nishkar* would be the expected rule, not *acharon acharon nishkar*. Under Rabbi Akiva's partnership model, whenever there is a remainder after paying the most recent victim, it should be distributed to all prior co-owners proportionally — not funneled entirely to the immediately preceding victim.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לְעוֹלָם כְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל – דְּאָמַר בַּעֲלֵי חוֹבוֹת נִינְהוּ; וּדְקַשְׁיָא לָךְ: ״אַחֲרוֹן אַחֲרוֹן נִשְׂכָּר״?! ״רִאשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן נִשְׂכָּר״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁתְּפָסוֹ נִיזָּק לִגְבּוֹת הֵימֶנּוּ, וְנַעֲשָׂה עָלָיו כְּשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר לִנְזָקִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Actually,** the mishna is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, who says** that all the injured parties **are creditors. And as for the difficulty you** pose, that instead of stating that the owner of **the latest** of the oxen gored in succession **gains,** the mishna **should have** stated that the owner of **the earliest** of the oxen gored in succession **gains,** that can be answered. **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where the** first **injured** party **seized** the ox **to collect** payment **from it, and** consequently **became like a paid bailee** with regard **to damage** it causes. Therefore, he is responsible for any subsequent attacks by the ox. Similarly, if the next injured party seizes the ox from the first as compensation, he becomes responsible for any subsequent attacks.

*Rava* resolves this by saying the opening of the *mishna* — Rabbi Meir's position — follows Rabbi Yishmael, but with a specific mechanism: *tafas nizak ligbot heimenu ve-na'asa lo ke-shomer sachar le-nezikin*. Each *nizak*, upon being damaged, seizes the goring ox in order to secure his lien — analogous to a creditor taking collateral. By taking possession of property in which he has a monetary interest and from which he benefits by being able to secure his debt, the *nizak* becomes a *shomer sachar* — a paid watchman — with respect to the ox. As a *shomer sachar*, he bears liability for damage the ox causes while under his watch.

The closing of the *mishna* — Rabbi Shimon's position — follows Rabbi Akiva: the victims become actual partners, and the distribution flows accordingly.

### Seizure & Shomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, ״יֵשׁ בּוֹ מוֹתָר – יַחֲזִיר לְשֶׁלְּפָנָיו״?! ״יַחֲזִיר לַבְּעָלִים״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אָמַר רָבִינָא, הָכִי קָתָנֵי: אִם יֵשׁ בּוֹ מוֹתָר בִּנְזָקָיו – יַחֲזִיר לְשֶׁלְּפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** instead of the mishna stating that **if there is surplus** value left **in** his ox after he pays that owner, **he shall return** it **to** the owner of **the previous** ox that was gored, **it should have** stated that **he shall return it to** its **owner,** since half the value of the belligerent ox belongs to its owner, who is not responsible for any later damage it causes. **Ravina said** that **this is what** the mishna **is teaching: If there is surplus** value **with regard to its damages,** i.e., the latter injured party sustained less of a loss than the previous one, that injured party **shall return** this surplus value **to the previous** injured party.

The mechanism *Rava* describes requires careful unpacking. Under Rabbi Yishmael's model, when *Shimon*'s ox is gored by *Reuven*'s ox, *Shimon* acquires a lien on *Reuven*'s ox. *Shimon* then physically seizes that ox — *tafas* — to prevent *Reuven* from removing it before *Shimon* can collect. By holding a piece of property in which he has a legal interest and from which he derives the benefit of secured collection, *Shimon* takes on the legal status of a *shomer sachar*. A *shomer sachar* is liable for damage the object under his care causes to others.

When the seized ox subsequently gores *Levi*'s ox, *Levi* collects his full amount from *Shimon* — who, as *shomer sachar*, was responsible for the ox at the time of the second goring. Whatever portion of the ox's value *Shimon* held by virtue of his lien, *Levi* collects from that. Then *Levi* seizes the ox, becomes the new *shomer sachar*, and when the ox gores *Yehuda*, *Yehuda* collects from *Levi*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, ״יֵשׁ בּוֹ מוֹתָר – יַחֲזִיר לְשֶׁלְּפָנָיו״?! ״יַחֲזִיר לַבְּעָלִים״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אָמַר רָבִינָא, הָכִי קָתָנֵי: אִם יֵשׁ בּוֹ מוֹתָר בִּנְזָקָיו – יַחֲזִיר לְשֶׁלְּפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** instead of the mishna stating that **if there is surplus** value left **in** his ox after he pays that owner, **he shall return** it **to** the owner of **the previous** ox that was gored, **it should have** stated that **he shall return it to** its **owner,** since half the value of the belligerent ox belongs to its owner, who is not responsible for any later damage it causes. **Ravina said** that **this is what** the mishna **is teaching: If there is surplus** value **with regard to its damages,** i.e., the latter injured party sustained less of a loss than the previous one, that injured party **shall return** this surplus value **to the previous** injured party.

The *gemara* challenges this account: if there is any remainder after *Levi* collects, why does that remainder go to *Shimon* (the immediately preceding victim) rather than all the way back to *Reuven* (the original owner)? The *gemara* answers — as articulated by *Ravina* — that the remainder goes back to whoever held it. The original 150 that *Reuven* owned and was never seized by *Shimon* always belonged to *Reuven*. But the 100 that *Shimon* seized — that is what gets swapped through the chain. Each successive victim collects his portion from the immediately preceding party's seized share, and any excess of that seized share returns to the party who held it, not to *Reuven*.

### Remainder Rules

To fully appreciate the distribution mechanics, one must track carefully the size of each victim's damage relative to the ox's value. When each goring produces the same damage, the shares simply pass down the chain and any remainder flows back in strict sequence. When damages differ — when, say, the first goring entitles the *nizak* to 150 and the second goring entitles the subsequent *nizak* to only 100 — then the second *nizak* takes his 100 from the first *nizak*'s 150, and the first *nizak* retains the remaining 50. The third *nizak* then collects from whoever holds shares at the moment of the third goring, and any leftover from their portions returns to them.

The critical point *Ravina* adds is that *im yesh motar be-nizakav yachzir le-shel lifanav* — if there is a leftover within the damage distribution, it returns to the one who preceded. This is because the chain of *tefisa* and *shomer sachar* liability creates a sequential trail of ownership: each *nizak* takes over the seized portion from the one before him, and excess beyond what was owed returns to the source. Whatever *Reuven* owned and was never touched in the *tefisa* chain returns to *Reuven* throughout.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: אִין, דְּהָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב יְהוּדָה: שִׁינָּנָא, שְׁבוֹק מַתְנִיתִין וְתָא בָּתְרַאי – רֵישָׁא רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל וְסֵיפָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said** that **yes,** this is the case, **as Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda: *Shinnana*, leave** the presumption that **the** entire **mishna** follows one opinion **and follow my** interpretation: **The first clause** is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael and the latter clause** is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva.**

The *gemara* concludes by confirming that the *reysha* of the *mishna* reflects Rabbi Yishmael via the *tefisa*-and-*shomer sachar* mechanism, while the *seifa* reflects Rabbi Akiva's partnership model. Although it is slightly surprising that Rabbi Meir — himself a student of Rabbi Akiva — sides with Rabbi Yishmael's framework, and Rabbi Shimon — also a student of Rabbi Akiva — sides with Rabbi Akiva, *Shmuel* already anticipated this kind of difficulty: *shevok masnisin ve-ta batrai* — set aside the expectation that students must follow their teacher's framework, and follow the analysis as it leads.
