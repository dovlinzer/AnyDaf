# Bava Kamma 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hamotzi vs. Sumchos

### Intro: Burden of Proof

The present sugya continues from the bottom of 35b and engages a *mishna* that establishes the principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav haraya*: the *nizak* who seeks to claim that a higher liability applies bears the burden of proof. The *gemara* notes that this ruling stands in direct tension with the position of *Sumchos*, who holds that in any case of objective doubt — *safek* — the disputed amount is split between the parties. The *gemara* therefore asks whether *Sumchos* would maintain his position of *chokim* even in a case of *bari ve-bari*, where both the *nizak* and the *mazik* each make definite, unequivocal claims — no, that happened; no, this happened — and concludes that he would. Even in *bari ve-bari*, *Sumchos* says you split, because the *safek* is objective regardless of how confident each party sounds. Our *mishna*, which insists on *hamotzi mechavero alav haraya*, is therefore in direct conflict with *Sumchos*.

### Bari vs. Shema

Once the *gemara* establishes that the *mishna* opposes *Sumchos* even in *bari ve-bari*, it turns to a related implication of the *mishna*'s language. The *mishna* seems to suggest that even if the *nizak* cannot prove his larger claim, he is at least entitled to collect whatever the *mazik* concedes. The *gemara* asks: if this is a case of *bari ve-bari* — where each party flatly rejects the other's account — why would the *nizak* be entitled to anything at all? If the *nizak* is claiming the *mazik*'s larger ox gored his ox, and the *mazik* says it was definitively the smaller one, then the *nizak* is actively rejecting the *mazik*'s own admission. This leads directly to the principle of *tanu chitin ve-hodo lo bise'orin*.

### Chitin v. Se'orin

*Rava bar Natan* teaches: if a claimant says "you owe me wheat" and the defendant says "I owe you only barley," the defendant is entirely exempt — *patur* — even from paying barley. The *gemara* here applies this principle: since the claimant is rejecting the defendant's concession, he cannot then turn around and collect based on it. The *tanu chitin ve-hodo lo bise'orin* case is taught explicitly in a *braita*, and *Rava bar Natan*'s innovation is that the defendant is *patur le-gamri* — completely exempt. One might have thought the ruling only meant the claimant cannot collect the wheat; he should still be entitled to the barley which was conceded. *Rava bar Natan* teaches that this is not so: since the claimant denied that he was owed barley, his concession is rejected and nothing is owed.

### Shor Tam Application

The *gemara* applies this logic to the *shor tam* cases in the *mishna*. If the *mazik* claims it was his smaller ox that gored and the *nizak* says no, it was definitely the larger one, the *nizak* cannot collect the smaller amount because his *bari* claim constitutes a rejection of the *mazik*'s admission. The *mishna*'s implication that the *nizak* collects at least based on what the *mazik* conceded must therefore be read as applying only when the *nizak* himself is making a *shema* claim — an uncertain claim — rather than a definite one.

The *gemara* asks: if the *mishna*'s implication of entitlement to the lesser amount requires the *nizak* to be saying *shema*, then who is saying *bari* and who is saying *shema*? The *gemara* considers and rejects the possibility that the *nizak* is saying *bari* and the *mazik* is saying *shema*, because in that configuration the *nizak* would still be saying definitively that he is owed the larger amount, while the *mazik* says only "maybe" — and in that case the *chitin ve-se'orin* problem resurfaces: if the *mazik* is saying *shema* about the large ox, has he truly conceded anything about the small one that the *nizak* can collect?

The *gemara* concludes: the case where the *mishna* implies the *nizak* collects at least the smaller amount must be where the *nizak* is saying *shema* — "I don't know, perhaps it was your larger one" — and the *mazik* is saying *bari*, definitively claiming it was the smaller one. In that configuration, the *nizak* is not rejecting the *mazik*'s admission; he is only uncertain about whether he is owed more. He therefore does not trigger the *chitin ve-se'orin* exemption and may collect based on what the *mazik* concedes.

A further wrinkle: the *gemara* notes a *braita* that states the *nizak* collects — *mishtalem al ha-katan min ha-gadol* — and resolves this with the answer *de-tafas*: the *nizak* had seized the ox. This is a somewhat difficult resolution. If *tefisa* — seizure — is sufficient to switch who is considered the *muchzak*, one might expect the *nizak* to be entitled to his full larger claim. The *gemara* limits recovery to the smaller conceded amount even after *tefisa*, which raises the question of precisely what *tefisa* accomplishes here.

### Sumchos Extreme Case

Having established that the *mishna*'s *seyfa* — where the *nizak* collects the lesser conceded amount — involves a case of *nizak shema* and *mazik bari*, the *gemara* asks whether *Sumchos* would say *chokim* even in this configuration. This is actually a more extreme version of the *Sumchos* position: the *mazik* is the *muchzak* — he has the money in his possession — and he is making a definite claim, while the *nizak* who seeks to extract money from him is only saying *shema*, he is not even certain of his own claim. Would *Sumchos* really say to split the disputed amount in a case where the claimant himself is uncertain and the one in possession is confident? That is a striking and counterintuitive position.

The *gemara* says yes, *Sumchos* would go that far. And the argument makes sense within *Sumchos*'s framework: if the *safek* is objective, the relative confidence of the parties is irrelevant to the question of how to distribute property in doubt.

### Mishna's Position

The *gemara* now resolves the full picture. The *mishna* contains cases in both directions: some where the *nizak* is saying *shema* and the *mazik* is saying *bari* (*nizak shema, mazik bari*), and others where the *nizak* is saying *bari* and the *mazik* is saying *shema* (*nizak bari, mazik shema*). The *gemara* notes that these are not two entirely different genres — in each, one party is *bari* and the other is *shema* — they are the same genre with the parties reversed, and *chada milta hi*, it is one type of case.

The *mishna*'s purpose in spanning both configurations is to make the most powerful possible case against *Sumchos*. The *reysha* — where the *nizak* is *bari* and the *muchzak* is saying *shema* — is precisely the scenario where *Sumchos*'s position has the strongest logical support: the *nizak* makes a definite claim, the person in possession is only uncertain, and yet the *mishna* still rules *hamotzi mechavero alav haraya*. Even there, the *nizak* bears the burden of proof and gets nothing if he cannot meet it.

At the same time, the *mishna*'s implication that in some cases the *nizak* does collect the lesser amount — where he himself was saying *shema* and is not rejecting the *mazik*'s concession — is preserved. The *mishna* thus navigates between rejecting *Sumchos* on one end and avoiding the full harshness of *chitin ve-se'orin* on the other, with the key variable being whether the *nizak*'s own claim is *bari* or *shema*.

## Shnayim Chayavim

### Both Oxen Present

The *mishna* states: if both oxen belonged to one person, *shnayhem chayavim* — both are liable. At first glance the meaning seems obvious: the owner of both oxen is clearly liable for the goring. But the precise phrasing — *shnayhem chayavim*, both of them are *chayav* — is curious, since in this case we do not know which of the two oxen actually gored. Saying both are liable requires explanation, because at most one of them did the damage.

The *gemara* draws a *diyuk* from the phrase *shnayhem chayavim*: the *mishna* is not simply saying the owner owes money. *Chayavim* refers to the oxen themselves, implying that both oxen must be present and available in order for the *nizak* to collect. *Tosafot* notes the *diyuk* — that the *mishna*'s language points to a rule about requiring both oxen to be on hand, even though in this particular case it is not literally true that both gored.

### Tam vs. Muad

The *gemara* raises the question of whether this case involves *tamim* or *muadim*. The *gemara* initially considers whether the *mishna* might be discussing *muad* oxen in the opening case. However, this is rejected: the *seifa* of the *mishna* debates whether the big ox or the small ox gored, and that debate would be irrelevant if the oxen were *muad*, since a *muad* owner pays full damages regardless of which ox caused the harm — what matters for a *muad* is the size of the damage suffered by the *nizak*, not which ox caused it. Since the *mishna* clearly cares which ox gored, it must be discussing *tamim*. The *gemara* therefore reads the opening case as *muadim* and the later case as *tamim*, then ultimately concludes the whole *mishna* addresses *tamim* oxen and invokes Rabbi Akiva's *shutfin* model.

### Akiva's Logic

The *gemara* concludes: *ela le-olam be-tamim ve-Rabbi Akiva hi de-amar shutfin ninhu* — the *mishna* always speaks of *tamim* oxen, and it follows Rabbi Akiva who holds that the *nizak* becomes a *shutaf* — a partner — in the goring ox. *Ve-taama de-itnayhu le-tarvayhu*: the reason the *nizak* is entitled to collect at all is because both oxen are still present. If both are alive and available, the *nizak* can say to the owner: one of these is partially mine, I have rights to one of them — you decide which one to give me, but you cannot deny that one of them is mine.

This is the correct reading of *shnayhem chayavim*: it is not that both oxen are each independently *chayav*. Rather, the presence of both together is what generates the obligation. If one of the oxen died or ran away, the *mazik* can say: perhaps the one that is gone was the one that gored you, and your lien — your partnership interest — was in that one. Since you cannot prove otherwise, *matzi amar lei zil ayisi raya de-hai tora azikach ve-ashalem lach* — go and prove that this living ox is the one that gored you, and I will pay. Without that proof, the *nizak* cannot collect.

### Yishmael vs. Akiva

The *gemara*'s attribution of the *mishna* to Rabbi Akiva rather than Rabbi Yishmael flows from a fundamental structural difference between their positions. Rabbi Yishmael holds that the relationship between the *nizak* and the *mazik* is one of *ba'al chov* — creditor and debtor. The *nizak* has a monetary claim, and the goring ox serves as the vehicle through which that claim is collected; the *nizak* holds a lien focused on the ox. Rabbi Akiva holds that the *nizak* becomes a *shutaf* — an actual co-owner — in the goring ox itself, up to the value of the half-damage owed.

The question of why the *mishna* cannot follow Rabbi Yishmael is sharpened here. If the *nizak* has a debt claim against the *mazik* — not merely an interest in the ox — then even if one of the two oxen dies or disappears, the debt remains. Why should the *nizak* lose his claim? The *mazik* still owes him money. The *gemara* therefore says the *mishna* must be Rabbi Akiva: under the partnership model, the *nizak*'s entire entitlement is tied to his co-ownership of a specific ox, and if that ox is gone, there is nothing left to collect from.

### Tosafot Analysis

*Tosafot* engages a significant dispute here with *Rashi*. *Rashi* understands that according to Rabbi Yishmael, the *nizak* is not limited to collecting from the ox alone; since the relationship is one of debt, the *nizak* retains the right to collect money from the *mazik* even if the ox is no longer available. Under *Rashi*'s reading, losing the ox does not extinguish the *nizak*'s rights — he can simply demand payment.

*Tosafot* disagrees and marshals a powerful proof from elsewhere in the *gemara*, which states that according to Rabbi Yishmael, if the *mazik* slaughtered the goring ox — *shachto* — the act stands and the *nizak* loses his money. *Tosafot* asks: if the *nizak* truly has an independent debt claim under Rabbi Yishmael, why is he left empty-handed when the ox is slaughtered? Let him simply demand cash payment instead. The fact that the *gemara* does not propose this answer indicates that under Rabbi Yishmael as well, the *nizak*'s right is fundamentally tied to the ox. The *mazik* may choose to pay cash in order to buy out the *nizak*'s lien, but the *nizak* cannot independently demand cash — his right runs against the ox and only against the ox.

*Tosafot* therefore concludes that it is *pashita* — self-evident — that under Rabbi Yishmael, if one of the two oxen is gone, the *nizak* loses out, because the *mazik* can simply assert that the departed ox was the one that gored. The *gemara*'s attribution of the *mishna* to Rabbi Akiva is needed not because Rabbi Yishmael would produce a different result in this case, but because Rabbi Akiva's *shutfin* model might have suggested a different outcome — namely that the *nizak*'s established co-ownership creates a *chazaka* — a presumption — entitling him to collect even if one ox is gone. The *mishna* therefore needs to specify that Rabbi Akiva's rule too requires both oxen to be present.

## Multiple Gorings

### R. Meir: Last First

A new *mishna* introduces a case of sequential gorings: an ox gores four or five oxen one after another — *ze achar ze*. The *tanna kamma*, identified as Rabbi Meir, rules *acharon acharon nishkar*: the last victim collects first. Whatever remains in the value of the goring ox after the last victim is paid goes to the second-to-last, then to the one before that, and so on, working backward. The logic and structural justification of this position become the subject of extensive *gemara* analysis below.

### R. Shimon: Partners

Rabbi Shimon takes a fundamentally different approach, one rooted in the partnership model of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Shimon presents the case numerically: an ox worth 200 gores an ox worth 200, and the carcass of the victim is worth nothing. The *nizak* takes 100 — half the damage — and becomes a partner in the goring ox to the extent of 100. Now this jointly owned ox — belonging to both the original owner and the first victim — turns and gores another ox worth 200. The last victim takes 100 from the jointly owned ox. Whatever remains of the joint ownership is split proportionally. The second goring is collected equally from both co-owners of the ox at the time of the second goring. And so on: each subsequent goring is distributed proportionally according to each party's ownership share in the ox at the moment of impact.

Concretely, in a case of three sequential gorings where all oxen are worth 200 and all carcasses are worth nothing: the third victim gets a full 100; the second victim, who owns half the ox, and the first victim and original owner who each own a quarter, each receive 50 and 25 and 25 respectively from the third goring's distribution — but accounting as well for what each had already collected. The *mishna* states that after three gorings the last victim takes 100, the one before him takes 50, and the first two each take a *dinar zahav* — 25 *zuz*.

### Awareness Caveat

*Tosafot* raises an important qualification to Rabbi Shimon's model. The *mishna* presents a situation where each successive *nizak* automatically becomes a part-owner of the ox and thereby assumes responsibility for its future conduct. But *Tosafot* asks: how can a person become liable for an ox's future gorings if he had no knowledge that he had become a co-owner, or if he had no practical ability to watch the animal?

*Tosafot* admits this creates a *teima* — a difficulty — and offers a somewhat forced answer: since the new co-owner has the ability in principle to bring witnesses and prove his ownership and win in court, his responsibility for the ox begins from the moment the goring occurs. This is so even though the court case has not yet been adjudicated and the co-ownership has not yet been formally established — *kivan she-yesh be-yado le-havi edim le-altar*, since he has the capacity to bring witnesses immediately, his liability attaches now. The retroactive legal establishment of ownership creates retroactive liability.

Crucially, however, *Tosafot* makes clear that this reasoning applies only when the new co-owner had actual knowledge of what occurred and had the practical ability to watch the ox. If he was not in a position to guard it — if he was elsewhere, or unaware — then the subsequent goring cannot be charged to his portion. In that case, the full liability falls on *Reuven*, the original owner. The new co-owner is in the position of an *oneis* — someone facing an unavoidable circumstance — and bears no liability for what he could not have prevented.

### Attribution to Amoraim

The *gemara* asks whose opinion is reflected in the opening case of the *mishna* — the *acharon acharon nishkar* rule of Rabbi Meir. It observes that this case fits neither Rabbi Yishmael nor Rabbi Akiva cleanly. Under Rabbi Yishmael's debt model, the first victim should have the earliest lien and therefore the strongest priority claim — *rishon rishon nishkar* would be the expected rule, not *acharon acharon nishkar*. Under Rabbi Akiva's partnership model, whenever there is a remainder after paying the most recent victim, it should be distributed to all prior co-owners proportionally — not funneled entirely to the immediately preceding victim.

*Rava* resolves this by saying the opening of the *mishna* — Rabbi Meir's position — follows Rabbi Yishmael, but with a specific mechanism: *tafas nizak ligbot heimenu ve-na'asa lo ke-shomer sachar le-nezikin*. Each *nizak*, upon being damaged, seizes the goring ox in order to secure his lien — analogous to a creditor taking collateral. By taking possession of property in which he has a monetary interest and from which he benefits by being able to secure his debt, the *nizak* becomes a *shomer sachar* — a paid watchman — with respect to the ox. As a *shomer sachar*, he bears liability for damage the ox causes while under his watch.

The closing of the *mishna* — Rabbi Shimon's position — follows Rabbi Akiva: the victims become actual partners, and the distribution flows accordingly.

### Seizure & Shomer

The mechanism *Rava* describes requires careful unpacking. Under Rabbi Yishmael's model, when *Shimon*'s ox is gored by *Reuven*'s ox, *Shimon* acquires a lien on *Reuven*'s ox. *Shimon* then physically seizes that ox — *tafas* — to prevent *Reuven* from removing it before *Shimon* can collect. By holding a piece of property in which he has a legal interest and from which he derives the benefit of secured collection, *Shimon* takes on the legal status of a *shomer sachar*. A *shomer sachar* is liable for damage the object under his care causes to others.

When the seized ox subsequently gores *Levi*'s ox, *Levi* collects his full amount from *Shimon* — who, as *shomer sachar*, was responsible for the ox at the time of the second goring. Whatever portion of the ox's value *Shimon* held by virtue of his lien, *Levi* collects from that. Then *Levi* seizes the ox, becomes the new *shomer sachar*, and when the ox gores *Yehuda*, *Yehuda* collects from *Levi*.

The *gemara* challenges this account: if there is any remainder after *Levi* collects, why does that remainder go to *Shimon* (the immediately preceding victim) rather than all the way back to *Reuven* (the original owner)? The *gemara* answers — as articulated by *Ravina* — that the remainder goes back to whoever held it. The original 150 that *Reuven* owned and was never seized by *Shimon* always belonged to *Reuven*. But the 100 that *Shimon* seized — that is what gets swapped through the chain. Each successive victim collects his portion from the immediately preceding party's seized share, and any excess of that seized share returns to the party who held it, not to *Reuven*.

### Remainder Rules

To fully appreciate the distribution mechanics, one must track carefully the size of each victim's damage relative to the ox's value. When each goring produces the same damage, the shares simply pass down the chain and any remainder flows back in strict sequence. When damages differ — when, say, the first goring entitles the *nizak* to 150 and the second goring entitles the subsequent *nizak* to only 100 — then the second *nizak* takes his 100 from the first *nizak*'s 150, and the first *nizak* retains the remaining 50. The third *nizak* then collects from whoever holds shares at the moment of the third goring, and any leftover from their portions returns to them.

The critical point *Ravina* adds is that *im yesh motar be-nizakav yachzir le-shel lifanav* — if there is a leftover within the damage distribution, it returns to the one who preceded. This is because the chain of *tefisa* and *shomer sachar* liability creates a sequential trail of ownership: each *nizak* takes over the seized portion from the one before him, and excess beyond what was owed returns to the source. Whatever *Reuven* owned and was never touched in the *tefisa* chain returns to *Reuven* throughout.

The *gemara* concludes by confirming that the *reysha* of the *mishna* reflects Rabbi Yishmael via the *tefisa*-and-*shomer sachar* mechanism, while the *seifa* reflects Rabbi Akiva's partnership model. Although it is slightly surprising that Rabbi Meir — himself a student of Rabbi Akiva — sides with Rabbi Yishmael's framework, and Rabbi Shimon — also a student of Rabbi Akiva — sides with Rabbi Akiva, *Shmuel* already anticipated this kind of difficulty: *shevok masnisin ve-ta batrai* — set aside the expectation that students must follow their teacher's framework, and follow the analysis as it leads.
