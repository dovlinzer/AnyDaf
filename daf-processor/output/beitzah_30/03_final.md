# Beitzah 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Measuring Spices

### Measuring Spices

The daf we study today is 30, which opens the next *perek*. Before turning to that new chapter, a loose end from the previous daf deserves attention. The *gemara* there discussed measuring on *yom tov* — measuring out flour and similar ingredients — and noted that a *nachtom*, a professional cook, may measure spices in order to ensure a dish comes out properly. The question left open was whether the same latitude extends to an ordinary person.

The *halacha* is that even a regular *ba'al habayis* or *ba'alas habayis* who is cooking on *yom tov* may use measuring spoons — a teaspoon of this, a half-teaspoon of that — because this is the kind of normal kitchen measuring that is necessary to ensure the dish comes out right. The one qualification the *poskim* add is that if a person habitually does not measure but simply adds a dash or a pinch of salt, that person should not begin measuring on *yom tov*. But for those who ordinarily measure, this is entirely permitted.

### New Perek: uvda d'chol

The new *perek* opens with a cluster of issues that sit just before the activity of cooking itself — matters of transporting items to the house — and the central concerns are *uvda d'chol*, the appearance of weekday activity, and the related concern of *tircha*, undue effort.

## Wine/Straw Transport

### Mishnah: Wine & Straw

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמֵּבִיא כַּדֵּי יַיִן מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם — לֹא יְבִיאֵם בְּסַל וּבְקוּפָּה, אֲבָל מֵבִיא הוּא עַל כְּתֵפוֹ אוֹ לְפָנָיו. וְכֵן הַמּוֹלִיךְ אֶת הַתֶּבֶן — לֹא יַפְשִׁיל אֶת הַקּוּפָּה לַאֲחוֹרָיו, אֲבָל מְבִיאָהּ הוּא בְּיָדוֹ. וּמַתְחִילִין בַּעֲרֵמַת הַתֶּבֶן, אֲבָל לֹא בָּעֵצִים שֶׁבַּמּוּקְצֶה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who brings wine jugs from** one **place to** another **place may not bring** a large number of **them in a basket or in a tub** in the usual weekday manner on a Festival, as this is disrespectful of the Festival; **but he may bring** one or two barrels **on his shoulder or** carry them **in front of himself. Similarly, one who brings straw** for kindling or for feeding animals **may not place the tub behind him** while carrying it, as this is the usual weekday manner; **but he may transport it** in front of him **in his hand,** in an unusual fashion. **And one may begin** taking straw **from the pile of straw,** although he did not designate the pile for this purpose the day before; **but one may not** begin to take from **the wood in the** wood **storage,** a small yard behind the house where people store various items that they do not intend to use in the near future.

The *mishnah* at the bottom of 29b begins: *hamayvee kadey yayin mimakom lemakom* — one who brings jugs of wine from one place to another, say from a storage shed to the house. The *mishnah* rules: *lo yavi besal uv'kuppa* — do not bring them in a large basket or box, carrying twenty jugs at once. Rather, *aval mayvee hu al ketayfo o lefanav* — bring them on your shoulder or holding them in front of you, but do not haul a large quantity all at once.

Similarly, *vechein hamolich es hateven* — one who brings straw to his animals: *lo yafshil es hakuppa lachora* — should not take a huge container of straw and carry it on his back; rather, *aval mayveehu beyado* — carry it in your hand, in front of you.

The *mishnah* continues: *umatchilin ba'arimat hateven* — one may break into a pile of straw that had not yet been opened before *yom tov*, and the assumption is that the straw will be used as fuel. However, *aval lo b'etzim shebemuktza* — not from wood stored in the *muktza*, which Rashi explains refers to the alleyway behind the house used as a storage area. This is the same root as *muktza* in the sense of items set aside and off-limits: things stored away and not designated for use. So one may break into a haystack of straw to begin using it as firewood, but not into a storage shed of wood not yet opened for that purpose.

### Rashi: uvda d'chol

What exactly is the concern behind the first part of the *mishnah*? Rashi explains — in a comment several lines into the *perek* — *d'nireh k'masa dechol laseis masa'os*: it looks like a thoroughly weekday activity to be carrying large burdens the way one does during the week. On the other hand, Rashi continues, *aval mayvee al ketayfo achas o shtayim d'mucha d'letzorech yom tov*: if you carry only a small amount, it is obvious why — you need wine for the *yom tov* meal, you need some straw for your animals. Large quantities, by contrast, suggest commercial transport or farm management of the sort associated with weekday life. Rashi's concern is thus squarely one of *uvda d'chol*, and he does not emphasize the *tircha* dimension.

### Tosafot: Shabbat/YomTov

Tosafot raises a challenge. A *mishnah* in *Shabbos* in the chapter *mafnin* appears to be more lenient: *mafnin arba kupot o chameish* — you may move out four or five boxes of straw. If on *Shabbos* one may haul several boxes at once, why does our *mishnah* restrict even a single basket on *yom tov*?

Tosafot's resolution is illuminating. He argues that on *Shabbos*, in the absence of an *eiruv* encompassing the city, most carrying takes place *mizavit l'zavit b'oto bayis* — from one corner to another within the house, or within a courtyard sharing an *eiruv*. In that confined context, there is no concern of *uvdin d'chol*: nobody watching you shuffle items within your courtyard thinks you are engaged in weekday commerce. The concern on *Shabbos* is therefore primarily *tircha*, undue effort, and since fewer trips means less effort, the *gemara* there prefers you carry more at once even if it is a heavier load.

*Yom tov* is different. Since carrying in the street is permitted on *yom tov*, transport genuinely takes place over longer distances where it is visible to others. The dominant concern therefore shifts to *uvdin d'chol*, and the principle becomes: *adif tfei l'afushei b'hilucha v'ya'aseh derech shinui ki heichi d'lo litchazei k'uvdin d'chol* — better to make more trips and carry less at a time, even at the cost of greater effort, so long as it does not look like weekday commercial transport.

These are genuinely competing values. By *Shabbos*, minimizing effort takes priority and we prefer fewer trips. By *yom tov*, once carrying is permitted at all, the visual reality of that carrying creates a stronger *uvdin d'chol* concern, and so we insist on small quantities per trip even if that multiplies the number of journeys.

### Exception: No Other Way

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: אִם אִי אֶפְשָׁר לְשַׁנּוֹת — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** A *tanna* **taught** in a *baraita*: **If it is impossible to modify** the manner in which one carries a vessel, whether due to the vessel or due to time constraints, **it is permitted** to act in the typical weekday manner.

A *baraita* qualifies the rule: *im ee efshar l'shanot muttar* — if there is no feasible way to carry the items differently — say the item is bulky and can only be transported in the normal manner — it is permitted to do so as-is.

### Rava's Takanah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתְקִין רָבָא בְּמָחוֹזָא: דְּדָרוּ בְּדוּחְקָא — לִדְרוֹ בְּרִגְלָא. דְּדָרוּ בְּרִגְלָא — לִדְרוֹ בְּאַגְרָא. דְּדָרוּ בְּאַגְרָא — לִדְרוֹ בְּאַכְפָּא. דְּדָרוּ בְּאַכְפָּא — נִפְרוֹס סוּדָרָא עִלָּוֵיהּ, וְאִם לָא אֶפְשָׁר — שְׁרֵי. דְּאָמַר מָר: אִם אִי אֶפְשָׁר לְשַׁנּוֹת — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rava instituted** the following **in** his city, **Meḥoza:** One **who** usually **carries** his burden **with difficulty** on a weekday **should** modify his habit on a Festival and **carry it on a pitchfork.** One **who** usually **carries it on a pitchfork should carry it on a carrying pole** held by two people on their shoulders. One **who carries it on a carrying pole** held by two people on their shoulders **should carry it** on a carrying pole **in his hands,** although he is not thereby making it easier for himself. One **who carries** burdens on a carrying pole **in his hands should spread a scarf [*sudara*] over it. And if it is not possible** to make these modifications due to time constraints, **it is permitted** to proceed in the usual manner, as **the Master said above: If it is impossible to modify, it is permitted.**

Rava established a set of guidelines in Mechuza, his city, for situations where some form of *shinui* is required. Items normally carried on the shoulder (*d'daru b'katfa*) should be carried with a pitchfork-like implement (*d'daru b'rigla*) — Rashi understands *rigla* not as "leg" literally but as such a tool. Items normally carried that way should instead be carried on a beam shared between two people (*d'daru b'igra* — *b'mote bishnayim*, a rod borne on shoulders together). And for items where no other mode is available, *nifra sudra iluya* — drape a cloth over the items, so they are covered and the transport is less conspicuous.

Here the logic is clearly visual: covering items does not reduce the weight, but it reduces the *uvda d'chol* appearance. This is precisely where Rashi and Tosafot diverge. Rashi holds that a *shinui* should ideally also lighten the burden and certainly not increase it. Tosafot holds that since the concern is *uvda d'chol* and not effort per se, even a *shinui* that does nothing to reduce the burden is valid. Finally, *ve'im lo efshar shari* — if no alternative whatsoever is feasible, one may carry in the normal way, as the *baraita* stated at the outset.

## Water Drawing Issue

### Women Drawing Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חָנָן בַּר רָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אֲמוּר רַבָּנַן כַּמָּה דְּאֶפְשָׁר לְשַׁנּוֹיֵי מְשַׁנֵּינַן בְּיוֹמָא טָבָא. וְהָא הָנֵי נְשֵׁי דְּקָא מָלְיָין חַצְבַיְיהוּ מַיָּא בְּיוֹמָא טָבָא, וְלָא קָא מְשַׁנְּיָין, וְלָא אָמְרִינַן לְהוּ וְלָא מִידֵּי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥanan bar Rava said to Rav Ashi: The Sages said: As much as it is possible to modify** the weekday manner, **one should modify on a Festival.** A question was asked of Rav Ashi: **But don't those women fill their jugs with water on a Festival without modifying, and we say nothing to them** by way of protest; why do we not instruct them to alter their usual manner?

Rav Chanan bar Rava said to Rav Ashi: the rabbis have said that to the extent possible we require a *shinui* on *yom tov*. Yet *de-kamalyan khatzivayhu maya beyoma tova* — the women go and fill their buckets with water on *yom tov* from the river or well, *velo kamashanyian* — and they do not draw the water any differently than on a weekday. *Velo amrinan lehu velo midi* — and we say nothing to them. How can we tolerate this without comment?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר. הֵיכָא לֶיעְבַּד? דְּמָלְיָא בְּחַצְבָּא רַבָּה תִּמְלֵי בְּחַצְבָּא זוּטָא — קָא מַפְּשָׁא בְּהִלּוּכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to him:** It is **because it is not possible** for them to fill their jugs any other way. **How should they act?** She **who** is accustomed to **filling a large jug, should** she instead **fill a small jug?** Won't this mean that **she increases** her **walking,** since she has to make more than one trip to bring home more than one jug, and she will thereby perform unnecessary labor on the Festival?

The response is: this falls into the category of things one cannot reasonably change. What practical *shinui* would you demand? *Heichi liyavid* — what are they supposed to do? If those who normally use a large bucket (*d'daru b'khatzba raba*) are told to use a small bucket (*d'malyi b'chatzva zuta*), the result is *kamaphish behiluha* — they will have to make multiple extra trips. That alone would make it more burdensome.

Now, Tosafot had just argued in connection with the *mishnah* that multiple trips are exactly what we prefer when it reduces *uvda d'chol* — and Tosafot is aware of the tension. His resolution is that in the *mishnah*, hauling small quantities is itself unusual enough to signal that this is for an immediate *yom tov* need. With water, the situation is different: some people always carry large buckets, others always carry small ones. Whichever way a particular woman switches, it will not look different from the weekday practice of someone else in the community. The *shinui* accomplishes nothing cosmetically, while imposing real cost.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּמָלְיָא בְּחַצְבָּא זוּטָא תִּמְלֵי בְּחַצְבָּא רַבָּה — קָא מַפְּשָׁא בְּמַשּׂוֹי. תְּכַסְּיֵיהּ בְּנִכְתְּמָא — זִמְנִין דְּנָפֵיל וְאָתֵי לְאֵתוֹיֵי. תִּקְטְרֵיהּ — זִמְנִין דְּמִפְּסִיק וְאָתֵי לְמִקְטְרֵיהּ. תִּפְרוֹס סוּדָרָא עִלָּוֵיהּ — זִמְנִין דְּמִטְּמִישׁ בְּמַיָּא וְאָתֵי לִידֵי סְחִיטָה. הִלְכָּךְ לָא אֶפְשָׁר.
>
> **Translation:** If one were to suggest the opposite, **that one who fills a small jug should fill a large jug,** won't this mean that **she increases** her **load?** Furthermore, if one were to suggest that **she should cover** the jug **with** a wooden **cover, sometimes** it **falls and she might come to bring it** by hand, in the manner of a burden. **Should she tie** the cover to the jug, the rope might **occasionally break, and she might come to tie it,** a prohibited labor. Finally, **should she spread a scarf over it,** it **occasionally** falls off and **becomes soaked in water, and she might come to** transgress the prohibition against **squeezing. Therefore, it is not possible** to make a modification, and those women may act in the regular manner.

Conversely, if those who normally use small buckets (*d'malyi b'chatzva zuta*) are told to switch to large buckets (*t'malyi b'chatzva rabba*), *ka mafish b'masoi* — you are increasing the weight they must carry. Every option carries a cost without producing a meaningful *uvda d'chol* benefit.

What about covering the bucket with a wooden plank? *Zimin d'nafel v'ati l'asuyei* — sometimes the cover will fall and there will be an impulse to carry it; and *tikatrei* — they will want to tie the cover down, but *zimin d'mafsik* — the knot will sometimes break, and they will end up tying a new knot on *yom tov*, which is forbidden. Covering with a cloth scarf instead? *Zimin d'mitatmei b'maya v'ati l'dey s'chita* — the cloth will become soaked with water and one will come to wring it out, which is forbidden. The concern about *s'chita* here is specifically because the liquid absorbed is water: wringing water from a cloth implicates the prohibition of laundering, whereas wringing out a cloth that absorbed wine or juice — where the liquid would go to waste — presents less of a problem. Because it is water, and because the bucket has a wide open mouth making saturation likely, this is a genuine concern.

*Hilkach lo efshar* — therefore, there is no feasible alternative, and the women draw water in the normal manner.

This discussion also points to something worth pausing over: the language of the *mishnah* itself is instructive. It does not say *assur l'havi* — it is forbidden to bring. It says *lo yavi'eim b'sal u'v'kupah* — in the style of guidance about how one should go about it. The entire domain of *uvda d'chol* is in many ways amorphous and contextual. It resists quantification precisely because it is about the experiential texture of the day — what does it feel like? What does it look like? On the one hand, this makes it one of the most important concerns in the spirit of *shabbos* and *yom tov*. On the other hand, it rarely generates a hard and formally articulated *issur d'rabbanan* in the way other areas of *halacha* do. The *gemara* here reads more like principled guidance than strict prohibition, and that tension plays out in practice whenever a *shailah* involves *uvda d'chol* as the primary concern.

### Rav Chanan's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא בַּר רַב חָנִין לְאַבָּיֵי, תְּנַן: אֵין מְטַפְּחִין וְאֵין מְסַפְּקִין וְאֵין מְרַקְּדִין, וְהָאִידָּנָא דְּקָא חָזֵינַן דְּעָבְדָן הָכִי, וְלָא אָמְרִינַן לְהוּ וְלָא מִידֵּי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava bar Rav Ḥanin said to Abaye: We learned** in a mishna: The Rabbis decreed that **one may not clap, nor strike** a hand on his thigh, **nor dance** on a Festival, lest he come to repair musical instruments. **But nowadays we see that** women **do so, and** yet **we do not say anything to them.**

Rava bar Rav Hanin said to Abaye: we have learned in a *mishnah* (to be studied in about a week) *ein metapchin bein masapkin u-vein merakdin* — one does not clap, slap the thigh, or dance on *yom tov*. The reason, as the *gemara* understands it, is a *gezerah* lest one come to fix a musical instrument. Interestingly, the parallel source in the *Tosefta* frames this quite differently, presenting these as activities — clapping, jumping — performed out in the field to scare birds away from crops, and framing the prohibition as *al yaaseh ke-derech she-hu oseh be-chol*, do not do it the way you do on a weekday. That parallel is directly about *uvda d'chol*. The *Bavli*, however, reframes it around the concern of repairing musical instruments.

Whatever the underlying concern, *ve-ha-idna de-chazinan de-avdi hachi* — we observe that people go ahead and do these things, *velo amrinan lehu velo midi* — and we say nothing. How do we permit ourselves this silence?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וּלְטַעְמָךְ הָא דְּאָמַר (רָבָא): לָא לֵיתֵיב אִינִישׁ אַפּוּמָּא דְלִחְיָא, דִּלְמָא מִגַּנְדַּר לֵיהּ חֵפֶץ וְאָתֵי לְאֵתוֹיֵי (אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים), וְהָא הָנֵי נְשֵׁי דְּשָׁקְלָן חַצְבַיְיהוּ, וְאָזְלָן וְיָתְבָן אַפּוּמָּא דִמְבוֹאָה, וְלָא אָמְרִינַן לְהוּ וְלָא מִידֵּי!
>
> **Translation:** **He said to him: And according to your reasoning,** how do you explain **that** which **Rava said: A person should not sit at the entrance** to an alleyway, next to **the side post** that has been placed at the edge of an alleyway in order for it to be considered a private domain, as **perhaps an object** will **roll** away **from him and he will come to carry it four cubits in the public domain,** thereby transgressing a biblical prohibition? **But don't these women take their jugs, and go, and sit at the entrance to an alleyway, and we do not say anything to them?**

A parallel is cited: Rava ruled that a person should not sit at the mouth of a courtyard right beside the *lechi*, the post that demarcates the boundary. The reason is *dilma migandar lei chefetz v'asi l'asuyei* — perhaps an object will roll away and the person, sitting right at the threshold with his back to the marker, will chase after it and carry it out without noticing. Here too the concern is what the act might lead to. And yet: *ve'ha hani nashi deshaklan chatzuvaihu v'azlan v'yavlan apuma d'mavoi* — women take their buckets and sit right at the mouth of the *mavoi*. This is itself a violation of Rava's rule. *Velo amrinan lehu velo midi* — and we say nothing.

### Shogeg vs. Mezid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: הַנַּח לָהֶם לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, מוּטָב שֶׁיִּהְיוּ שׁוֹגְגִין וְאַל יִהְיוּ מְזִידִין. הָכָא נָמֵי: הַנַּח לָהֶם לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, מוּטָב שֶׁיִּהְיוּ שׁוֹגְגִין וְאַל יִהְיוּ מְזִידִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the accepted principle is: **Leave** the **Jews** alone; **it is better that they be unwitting sinners and not be intentional sinners.** If people engage in a certain behavior that cannot be corrected, it is better not to reprove them, as they are likely to continue regardless of the reproof, and then they will be sinning intentionally. It is therefore preferable for them to be unaware that they are violating a prohibition and remain merely unwitting sinners. **Here, too,** with regard to clapping and dancing, **leave** the **Jews** alone; **it is better that they be unwitting sinners and not be intentional sinners.**

Abaye's response is the principle: *ela hanoch lahem l'Yisrael mutav sheyihyu shoggin v'al yihyu mezidin* — leave the Jewish people alone; better they sin unintentionally than sin deliberately. This principle has two dimensions. At the individual level, if one tells them and they ignore the rebuke, they have moved from *shogeg* to *mezid*. At the communal level, if a rabbinic instruction is openly flouted, it undermines rabbinic authority itself. One must therefore judge whether speaking will do any good, and if it clearly will not, silence is sometimes the more responsible choice. This logic applies, the *gemara* continues, to the case of the dancing and clapping as well: *hacha nami hanoch lahem l'Yisrael mutav sheyihyu shoggin v'al yihyu mezidin*.

### Tochacha Discussion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי בִּדְרַבָּנַן, אֲבָל בִּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא — לָא. וְלָא הִיא, לָא שְׁנָא בִּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא וְלָא שְׁנָא בִּדְרַבָּנַן, לָא אָמְרִינַן לְהוּ וְלָא מִידֵּי, דְּהָא תּוֹסֶפֶת יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הוּא, וְאָכְלִי וְשָׁתוּ עַד שֶׁחָשְׁכָה, וְלָא אָמְרִינַן לְהוּ וְלָא מִידֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: There were those who understood that **this** principle **applies only to rabbinic** prohibitions **but** not to **Torah** prohibitions, with regard to which the transgressors must be reprimanded. **However, this is not so;** it is **no different** whether the prohibition is **by Torah** law **or** whether it is **by rabbinic** law, **we do not say anything to them.** For example, on the eve of Yom Kippur, there is an obligation **that** one begin the fast while it is still day, before sunset, as **the extension of Yom Kippur.** During this time, one must observe all the *halakhot*. This mitzva of extending Yom Kippur **is by Torah** law, **and** yet people **eat and drink until darkness** falls **but we do not say anything to them,** as we know they will pay no attention.

A natural question follows: how far does this principle extend? Is there ever a case where we must speak even when it will do no good?

The *gemara* initially suggests a distinction: *v'hani mili b'drabbanan* — this tolerance of silence applies to rabbinic violations. And indeed, both cases discussed — the concern about fixing a musical instrument and the concern about carrying past the *lechi* — are themselves *gezerot*, rabbinic safeguards against potential violations. *Aval b'doraisa lo* — but for a biblical violation, perhaps we are required to speak up regardless.

### D'Oraisa Distinctions

The *gemara* then rejects this distinction: *lo shena d'oraisa velo shena d'rabbanan* — it makes no difference; even for a biblical violation, if the rebuke will not be heeded, we say nothing. The proof: *tosefes Yom HaKippurim* — adding to *Yom Kippur* is a biblical concept derived from *me'erev ad erev*, the idea of beginning the fast before sunset. Yet *ve'achlu veshassu ad shechashecha* — people eat right up to sunset, and *velo amrinan lehu velo midi* — we say nothing. This real-world rabbinic practice demonstrates that silence is sometimes appropriate even regarding a *d'oraisa*.

Tosafot notes that this cannot literally mean eating until the very last instant before sunset, since no one can time it so precisely and people would then surely eat on *Yom Kippur* itself. There must be some interval before sunset. That observation leads the *rishonim* to discuss what minimum duration constitutes *tosefes Yom HaKippurim* — opinions range from a half-hour down to considerably less.

The *gemara*'s conclusion, as developed *lehalacha*, is that the case where one must speak even if confident the rebuke will not be heeded is where the prohibition is both biblical and *meforash bakra* — explicitly stated in the Torah. The reasoning operates on several levels. First, one is never truly a prophet: perhaps repeated remonstration will eventually reach the person. Second, when a prohibition is explicit, the status of *shogeg* is weaker — it is harder to claim genuine ignorance of something plainly written. The *shogeg* argument therefore carries less exculpatory weight, making silence less justified.

A further dimension of *tochacha* is worth noting: sometimes the obligation to speak has less to do with the individual violator and more to do with the communal witness. If violations occur and no one speaks, the implicit message is that the conduct is tolerated. Even when the particular transgressor will not listen, articulating that something is wrong ensures that everyone else present understands there is a problem. This public dimension of *tochacha* may itself be an independent consideration, particularly regarding explicit biblical prohibitions.

## Straw vs. Wood

### Storage Shed Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַתְחִילִין בַּעֲרֵמַת הַתֶּבֶן. אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת, מַתְחִילִין בָּאוֹצָר תְּחִלָּה. מַנִּי — רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּלֵית לֵיהּ מוּקְצֶה.
>
> **Translation:** It is taught in the mishna: **And one may begin** taking straw **from the pile of straw. Rav Kahana said: That is to say** that **one may begin** removing items **from a storeroom** on a Festival ***ab initio*.** Although the items in this storeroom are designated for other purposes, it is not assumed that one put them out of his mind. If so, in accordance with **whose** opinion is this mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who is not** of the opinion that there is a prohibition of **set-aside [*muktze*].** According to him, on Shabbat and Festivals it is not prohibited to handle items that one has removed from his mind.

The *gemara* now returns to the *mishnah*'s distinction between opening a pile of straw (*matchilin ba'areimas hateven*) and opening the storage shed of wood (*aval lo b'etzim shebemuktza*).

### Rav Kahana: Muktzah

Rav Kahana reads the *risha*, the opening clause, as teaching: *zos omeres matchilin b'otzar techilah* — one is permitted to break into a storage supply, a closed cache of goods, and begin using it on *yom tov*, without this constituting a *muktza* problem. *Mani?* — whose view does this follow? *Rabbi Shimon d'leis lei muktza* — it follows Rabbi Shimon, who has a significantly lower threshold for *muktza*. For Rabbi Shimon, the mere fact that something is stored away does not render it off-limits if it is fundamentally usable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא סֵיפָא: אֲבָל לֹא בָּעֵצִים שֶׁבַּמּוּקְצֶה. אֲתָאן לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאִית לֵיהּ מוּקְצֶה! הָכָא בְּאַרְזֵי וְאַשּׁוּחֵי עָסְקִינַן, דְּמוּקְצֶה מֵחֲמַת חֶסְרוֹן כִּיס, וַאֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מוֹדֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **Say the latter clause** of the same mishna as follows: **But not wood in the** wood **storage.** If so, **we have come to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who is** of the opinion that there is a prohibition of ***muktze*.** The Gemara answers: **Here, we are dealing with** wood of **cedars and firs,** which are expensive and used only in the construction of important buildings, not for kindling; the wood storage is therefore considered ***muktze* due to** potential **monetary loss.** With regard to an item that one removes from his mind due to the financial loss he might suffer were he to use it, but not due to any prohibition involved, **even Rabbi Shimon concedes** that it may not be handled due to the prohibition of *muktze*.

*Eima seifa* — but look at the end of the *mishnah*: *aval lo b'etzim shebemuktza* — one may not use the wood in the storage shed. *Atan l'Rabbi Yehuda d'is lei muktza* — this shifts to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that setting items aside in storage is itself sufficient to create *muktza* status. The *mishnah* thus appears to contain the positions of both *tanna'im*, and the question is what distinguishes the straw from the wood.

### Straw/Cedar Diff

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַסֵּיפָא: אֲבָל לֹא בָּעֵצִים שֶׁבַּמּוּקְצֶה. אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת, אֵין מַתְחִילִין בָּאוֹצָר תְּחִלָּה. מַנִּי — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּאִית לֵיהּ מוּקְצֶה. אֵימָא רֵישָׁא: מַתְחִילִין בַּעֲרֵמַת הַתֶּבֶן, אֲתָאן לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּלֵית לֵיהּ מוּקְצֶה! הָתָם בְּתִבְנָא סַרְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who taught** the statement of Rav Kahana as referring **to the latter clause** of the mishna, as follows: **But not wood from the** wood **storage** area. **Rav Kahana said: That is to say** that **one may not begin** removing items **from a storeroom *ab initio*.** If so, in accordance with **whose** opinion is the mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who ** maintains that there is a prohibition of ***muktze*.** The Gemara challenges: **Say the first clause** of the mishna, which states that **one may begin** taking **from the pile of straw.** If so, **we have come to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who is not** of the opinion that there is a prohibition of ***muktze*.** The Gemara answers: **There,** in the first clause of the mishna, it is dealing **with straw** that has rotted **and become rancid.** Since it is no longer fit as animal fodder, even Rabbi Yehuda concedes that it will be used for kindling and is not *muktze*.

The *gemara* resolves the tension: *hacha b'arzei d'ashuchei askinan* — the wood prohibited in the storage shed is not ordinary firewood but valuable cedar beams. This is a case of *muktza machmas chisaron kis* — *muktza* by reason of financial loss, meaning the item is so valuable that one would never consider using it as fuel under normal circumstances. Even Rabbi Shimon agrees that this category of *muktza* is genuine.

An alternative reading (*ika d'masni lah asseifa*) starts from the prohibition of the wood and asks about the permission for the straw. Under this reading, the prohibition follows Rabbi Yehuda: *zos omeres ein matchilin b'otzar techilah* — one may not initially break into stored goods on *yom tov*, because in Rabbi Yehuda's view, setting items aside in storage renders them *muktza*. The permission for the straw then follows Rabbi Shimon. But how, under this reading, does Rabbi Yehuda's position accommodate the straw? The *gemara* answers: *hasham b'tivna sarya* — the straw there is somewhat spoiled. Once straw is no longer usable for animals, one would naturally be thinking about burning it, and it therefore no longer has the psychological character of something fully stored away and reserved.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּבְנָא סַרְיָא, הָא חֲזֵי לְטִינָה! דְּאִית בֵּיהּ קוֹצִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Isn't rancid straw fit for clay** in the making of bricks; why can one assume that it will be used as fuel? The Gemara answers: The mishna is referring to straw **that has thorns,** which cannot be kneaded into clay. It will certainly be used only for kindling.

One might object: perhaps the spoiled straw can still be used for making plaster (*hachazi l'tina*). The *gemara* responds: *d'ika kotzim* — the straw is mixed with thorns and therefore not even suitable for plaster. At that point, one would obviously be mentally prepared to begin using it as fuel, so it does not carry the *muktza* character of something genuinely reserved in storage.

The core debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon thus concerns: when something is usable but has been set aside and stored, does that storage alone create *muktza*? Rabbi Yehuda says yes; Rabbi Shimon says no — only when the item is inherently off-limits does *muktza* apply. The cedar beams represent a case that even Rabbi Shimon agrees is *muktza*, not by virtue of storage but by virtue of the item's value.

Tosafot notes an interesting wrinkle: the straw being used for firewood rather than for animals — its designated storage purpose — seems like an additional factor. One would think it is actually more permissible to use something for the purpose you stored it for, and stranger to divert it to a different use. Tosafot raises this as a genuine difficulty: using the straw for firewood is in one sense further from the storage designation, yet in another sense the straw was "in your mind" insofar as you always knew you might burn it. This does not receive a clean resolution, but Tosafot acknowledges the tension.

## Sukkah Sticks

### Mishnah: Sukkah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין נוֹטְלִין עֵצִים מִן הַסּוּכָּה, אֶלָּא מִן הַסָּמוּךְ לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may not take wood from a *sukka*** on any Festival, not only on the festival of *Sukkot*, because this is considered dismantling, **but** one may take **from near it.**

The *mishnah* states: *ein notlin eitzim min hasukkah ela min hasamukh lah* — one may not take sticks from the *sukkah*, but only from what is near the *sukkah*. In context, this appears to be another *muktza* question — parallel to the wood-in-the-shed issue — though as we will see, further considerations are involved.

### Gemara: Sukkah Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי שְׁנָא מִן הַסּוּכָּה דְּלָא — דְּקָא סָתַר אֻהְלָא, מִן הַסָּמוּךְ לָהּ נָמֵי — קָא סָתַר אֻהְלָא?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara poses a question with regard to the mishna: In **what** way **is** this case **different?** Why did the mishna teach that **from the *sukka*** itself one may **not** remove wood? It is because **one** thereby **dismantles a tent,** which is a prohibited labor. But if so, if one takes wood **from near it, too,** doesn't **he** thereby **dismantle a tent?** Why, then, does the mishna permit him to do so?

The *gemara* immediately presses: what is the distinction? *Mai shna min hasukkah de-lo* — why can you not take from the *sukkah* itself? Is it because of *muktza*, or is it *soter* — the prohibition of dismantling a structure? Taking a beam from a roof, even without demolishing the entire building, constitutes *soter*, the *melacha* of demolition, which is prohibited on *yom tov*. And yet, *min hasamukh lah nami de-ka-assar aleh* — Rashi's initial reading is that "near the *sukkah*" means sticks lying on top of the *sechach*, and those too would become incorporated into the *sechach* and be subject to the same *soter* prohibition. The distinction therefore collapses.

### Rav Yehuda: Wall Sticks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מַאי סָמוּךְ — סָמוּךְ לַדְּפָנוֹת. רַב מְנַשְּׁיָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא בְּשֶׁאֵין סָמוּךְ לַדְּפָנוֹת, כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא — בְּאִסּוּרְיָיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: What is** the meaning of: **Near it?** It means **near the walls.** Wood placed near the walls may be removed because it is not part of the *sukka* itself; the walls themselves may not be removed. **Rav Menashya said: Even** if **you say** that it is referring **to** a case **where** the wood **is not near the walls** but is part of the roof of the *sukka* itself, **when that** *baraita* **was taught, it was** with regard **to bundles** of reeds that are not considered part of the roof of the *sukka*, as they have not been untied. Therefore, one may remove them.

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *mai samukh? samukh ledofanos* — "near the *sukkah*" does not mean on top of the *sechach*, but sticks leaning against the wall of the *sukkah* from outside. Sticks lying on the *sechach* become incorporated into it and removing them is *soter*. Sticks leaning against the outside wall, by contrast, are clearly external to the structure — they are not part of the wall — and therefore neither are they *muktza* nor does their removal constitute *soter*.

The sticks that form the walls themselves, *min hadofanos*, cannot be removed either — that would be dismantling the structure. But something merely resting against the outside of the wall is a separate matter entirely.

### Bundle Distinct

Rav Menashya offers a different resolution: *afilu teima b'she-ein samukh ledofanos* — one may say we are even speaking of something on the *sechach* itself, not just leaning against the wall. *Ki tanya hi ba-asurayta* — when the *baraita* permits taking from "near the *sukkah*," it refers specifically to a distinct bundle of wood resting on top of the *sechach*. A bundle is self-evidently separate — it never merges into the *sechach*, which consists of scattered branches and thatch. The bundle retains its identity as a discrete object on top of the roof and therefore does not become part of the structure. Removing it raises neither a *soter* problem nor a *muktza* problem.

## Huktzah L'Mitzvah

### Fallen Sukkah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר יוֹסֵף קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין נוֹטְלִין עֵצִים מִן הַסּוּכָּה אֶלָּא מִן הַסָּמוּךְ לָהּ, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר. וְשָׁוִין בְּסוּכַּת הַחַג בֶּחָג — שֶׁאֲסוּרָה. וְאִם הִתְנָה עָלֶיהָ — הַכֹּל לְפִי תְנָאוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Yosef taught** the following *baraita* **before Rabbi Yoḥanan: One may not take wood from the *sukka*** itself **but** only **from the nearby** wood. **And Rabbi Shimon permits** one to take wood from the *sukka* as well. **And** all **agree,** even Rabbi Shimon, that with regard **to the *sukka*** that was built for the **festival** of *Sukkot*, **during the Festival it is prohibited** to remove wood from it. **But if** at the outset **one stipulated** a condition **with regard to it** allowing him to use it for other purposes, it is **all according to his stipulation.**

The *mishnah* and *baraita* then note a further point: *veshavin be-sukkas he-chag be-chag she-asurah* — both Rabbi Shimon and the *tanna kamma* agree that a *sukkah* used for the *mitzvah* of *sukkot* on *sukkot* is absolutely forbidden, for a reason beyond *muktza* and beyond *soter*. And: *ve-hisnah aleiha ha-kol lefi tina'o* — if one stipulated beforehand, the stipulation is effective. We will need to unpack both of these points.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר? וְהָא קָא סָתַר אֻהְלָא! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: הָכָא בְּסוּכָּה נוֹפֶלֶת עָסְקִינַן, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּלֵית לֵיהּ מוּקְצֶה. דְּתַנְיָא: מוֹתַר הַשֶּׁמֶן שֶׁבַּנֵּר וְשֶׁבַּקְּעָרָה — אָסוּר. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this *baraita*: **And** does **Rabbi Shimon permit** one to take wood from the *sukka* itself? **But isn't one dismantling a tent,** which is a prohibited labor? The Gemara answers that **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Here, we are dealing with a *sukka*** that has already **collapsed.** Therefore, the only potential concern is *muktze*, not dismantling. **And Rabbi Shimon** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as he is not** of the opinion that there is a prohibition of ***muktze*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If a wick in oil was lit before Shabbat, and it went out on Shabbat, **the remainder of the oil in a lamp or in a bowl is prohibited** for use, as it is *muktze*. **And Rabbi Shimon permits** one to use it. Consequently, Rabbi Shimon also permits one to take wood from the *sukka*.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak answers the question about Rabbi Shimon: *hacha be-sukkah nufeles askinan* — we are dealing with a *sukkah* that has fallen down. Once the *sukkah* has collapsed, there is no longer a *soter* concern — there is nothing left to demolish. The question that remains is only *muktza*: the wood was off-limits when *yom tov* began, since at that moment removing it would have constituted *soter*. Rabbi Shimon, however, does not recognize *muktza* of this kind — since the wood is fundamentally usable, now that the structure has fallen it may be used.

This parallels the case of leftover oil in a lamp: *mosar shemen she-ba-ner she-be-ke'arah asur* — oil remaining in the dish after the flame goes out is *muktza* because it was off-limits when *shabbos* began (removing it would have diminished the flame — *kibbu'i*). *Ve-Rabbi Shimon matir* — Rabbi Shimon permits using it once the flame has naturally gone out. Similarly, once the *sukkah* collapses, Rabbi Shimon permits using the wood.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם אָדָם יוֹשֵׁב וּמְצַפֶּה אֵימָתַי תִּכְבֶּה נֵרוֹ, הָכָא אָדָם יוֹשֵׁב וּמְצַפֶּה אֵימָתַי תִּפּוֹל סוּכָּתוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this claim: **Is it comparable? There,** in the case of oil in a lamp, **a person sits and anticipates when his lamp will be extinguished.** It is clear to him that it will be extinguished, and he can safely assume that a certain amount of oil will remain in the lamp or the bowl. **Here,** however, can it be said that **a person sits and anticipates when his *sukka* will fall?** He cannot know ahead of time that his *sukka* will collapse.

The *gemara* objects: *mi dami?* Are these cases truly comparable? *Hasam adam yoshev u-metzapeh eimasai tichbeh neiro* — in the oil case, a person anticipates that the candle will burn down and leave oil; the prospect of leftover oil was always in mind. *Aval hacha adam yoshev u-metzapeh eimasai tipol sukkaso?* — does a person actually expect his *sukkah* to collapse? If not, even Rabbi Shimon should not permit it, since nothing about the outcome was anticipated when *yom tov* began.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: הָכָא בְּסוּכָּה רְעוּעָה עָסְקִינַן, דְּמֵאֶתְמוֹל דַּעְתֵּיהּ עִלָּוַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Here, we are dealing with a *sukka*** that is **not sturdy, as from yesterday,** the Festival eve, **one** already **had his mind on it.** He thought it might collapse, and therefore he did not remove the possibility of using its wood from his mind.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves this: *hacha be-sukkah re'u'ah askinan, dem-e'asmol da'ato alaveih* — we speak of a rickety, fragile *sukkah*. Since the person was already expecting it might fall, the eventuality was in mind from before *yom tov*. Under those conditions, Rabbi Shimon permits using the wood when it does fall.

### Pesukim Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשָׁוִין בְּסוּכַּת הֶחָג בֶּחָג שֶׁהִיא אֲסוּרָה, וְאִם הִתְנָה עָלֶיהָ — הַכֹּל לְפִי תְנָאוֹ. וּמִי מַהֲנֵי בַּהּ תְּנַאי? וְהָאָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: מִנַּיִן לַעֲצֵי סוּכָּה שֶׁאֲסוּרִין כׇּל שִׁבְעָה — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״חַג הַסּוּכּוֹת שִׁבְעַת יָמִים לַה׳״, וְתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָא אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן שֶׁכְּשֵׁם שֶׁחָל שֵׁם שָׁמַיִם עַל הַחֲגִיגָה, כָּךְ חָל שֵׁם שָׁמַיִם עַל הַסּוּכָּה — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״חַג הַסּוּכּוֹת שִׁבְעַת יָמִים לַה׳״, מָה חַג לַה׳ — אַף סוּכָּה לַה׳.
>
> **Translation:** § The above *baraita* states: **All agree with regard to the *sukka*** that was built for the **festival** of *Sukkot*, that **during the Festival it is prohibited** to remove wood from it, **but if one stipulated** a condition **with regard to it,** it is **all according to his condition.** The Gemara asks: **And is a condition effective with** regard to **it?** **But didn't Rav Sheshet say in the name of Rabbi Akiva: From where** is it derived **that the wood of a *sukka*** is **prohibited** to be used for any other use **all seven** days of the Festival? It is **as it is stated: "The festival of *Sukkot* to the Lord, seven days"** (Leviticus 23:34). **And it is taught** in a different *baraita* in explanation of this that **Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira says: From where** is it derived **that just as the name of Heaven takes effect upon the Festival peace-offering, so too, does the name of Heaven take effect upon the *sukka*? The verse states: "The festival of *Sukkot* to the Lord, seven days"** (Leviticus 23:34), from which it is learned: **Just as the Festival** offering is consecrated **to the Lord, so too, the *sukka*** is consecrated **to the Lord.** Since the wood of the *sukka* is compared to consecrated objects, how may one stipulate a condition with regard to it?

Now the *gemara* turns to the second part of the *mishnah*: the agreement that a *sukkat he-chag* on *sukkot* is forbidden. The *gemara* cites Rav Sheshes in the name of Rabbi Akiva: *minayin l'atzei sukkah she'asurin kol shiva?* — from where do we know that the wood of the *sukkah* (according to some opinions, including the walls; according to others, only the *sechach*) is forbidden for the entire week of *sukkot*? This is not ordinary *muktza*, which does not normally apply on *chol ha-moed*. It is a separate category.

The source: *shene'emar Chag haSukkos shivat yamim l'Hashem* — "a celebration of *sukkot* seven days to God." A *baraita* teaches: just as God's name, so to speak, descends upon the *korban chagiga* — sanctifying it — so too does it descend upon the *sukkah* itself. *Talmud lomar: Chag haSukkos shivat yamim l'Hashem* — the *sukkah* is "to God." *Ma chag l'Hashem af sukkah l'Hashem* — just as the *chagiga* is dedicated to God, so is the *sukkah*. This sanctity does not prohibit sitting in the *sukkah* — obviously one must sit in it — but it does prohibit using the *sukkah*'s materials for any other purpose throughout the seven days. Some hold this to be a genuine *d'oraisa* concept.

Given this level of sanctity, what good can a *tnai* — a prior stipulation — do? A stipulation affects one's mental state and designation, but it cannot undo an objective *kedushah* that takes effect by Torah law.

### Stipulation & Noy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מְנַשְּׁיָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: סֵיפָא אֲתָאן לְסוּכָּה דְעָלְמָא, אֲבָל סוּכָּה דְמִצְוָה — לָא מַהֲנֵי בַּהּ תְּנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Menashya, son of Rava, said:** In **the latter clause,** where the stipulation is mentioned, **we have arrived at** the case of **a regular *sukka*,** a hut used throughout the year, not specifically for the Festival. With regard to such a *sukka*, one may stipulate to use the wood as he wishes; **but** as for **a *sukka* of mitzva,** used for the Festival, **a condition is not effective with regard to it.**

Rav Menashya resolves the contradiction: *seifa aslan l'sukkah d'alma* — the clause permitting stipulation does not refer to a *sukkat he-chag* on *sukkot* at all. It refers back to the hut used on *pesach* — the fallen *sukkah* case. Even according to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that *muktza* applies, if one explicitly declared before *yom tov* "should this *sukkah* fall, I intend to use it for firewood," the stipulation removes the *muktza* status. *Aval sukkah d'mitzvah lo mahani bah tnai* — but for the *mitzvah*-*sukkah* of *sukkot*, no stipulation will help. Even if the *sukkah* falls, the wood remains forbidden, because the prohibition is not about mental designation but about inherent sanctity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסוּכָּה דְּמִצְוָה לָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: סִכְּכָהּ כְּהִלְכָתָהּ וְעִטְּרָהּ בִּקְרָמִים וּבִסְדִינִין הַמְצוּיָּירִין, וְתָלָה בָּהּ אֱגוֹזִים שְׁקֵדִים אֲפַרְסְקִים וְרִמּוֹנִים וּפַרְכִּילֵי עֲנָבִים, יֵינוֹת שְׁמָנִים וּסְלָתוֹת וְעַטְרוֹת שִׁבֳּלִים — אָסוּר לְהִסְתַּפֵּק מֵהֶן עַד מוֹצָאֵי יוֹם טוֹב הָאַחֲרוֹן שֶׁל חַג. וְאִם הִתְנָה עֲלֵיהֶם — הַכֹּל לְפִי תְנָאוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question from a different angle: **And is** a condition **not** effective for **a *sukka* of mitzva? But isn't it taught** in the *Tosefta*: In the case of a *sukka* that **one roofed in accordance with its *halakha*, and decorated it with embroidered** clothes **and with patterned sheets, and hung on it nuts, almonds, peaches, pomegranates, and vines [*parkilei*], of grapes** and glass containers filled with **wine, oil, and flour, and wreaths of ears of corn** for decoration, **it is prohibited to derive benefit** from any of these **until the conclusion of the last Festival day. But if one stipulated** a condition **with regard to them** whereby he allows himself to use them, it is **all according to his condition.** This shows that conditions are effective even with regard to a *sukka* of mitzva.

But the *gemara* immediately confronts an apparent contradiction: a *baraita* teaches that if one decorated the *sukkah* — *sichcha k'hilchasa, v'itrah b'kramim u'v'sadinim hametzuyarim* — covered it properly with *sechach* and adorned it with illustrated sheets and cloths, hung from it nuts, almonds, peaches, pomegranates, clusters of grapes, wine, oil, flour, and decorated wheat sheaves — then *asur l'histapeik meihen ad motzaei yom tov acharon shel chag*: all these are forbidden for use until after the final day of *sukkot*. However, *v'im hitna aleihem hakol l'fi tna'o* — if one stipulated beforehand, the stipulation is effective. So here, stipulation does work for the *sukkah* of the *mitzvah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: בְּאוֹמֵר אֵינִי בּוֹדֵל מֵהֶם כׇּל בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת, דְּלָא חָלָה קְדוּשָּׁה עֲלַיְיהוּ. אֲבָל עֲצֵי סוּכָּה, דְּחָלָה קְדוּשָּׁה עֲלַיְיהוּ — אִתַּקְצַאי לְשִׁבְעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers based on the opinion of **Abaye and Rava, who both say** that this is referring to a case **where one says: I am not removing** myself **from them throughout twilight.** In other words, he announces from the outset that he will not set them aside as *sukka* decorations, but rather he will use them for other purposes as well. In that case, **no sanctity devolves upon them** at all, and he may therefore use them throughout the Festival. **However,** as for the actual **wood of a *sukka*, sanctity devolves upon it** through the very construction of the *sukka*, and **it** has therefore been **set aside** from use **for** the entire **seven** days.

The resolution is that the *baraita* is speaking not of the *sukkah*'s structural materials but of its *noi* — the decorations. Abaye and Rava both rule: *b'omer eini boded meihem kol bein hashmashos* — if one declares at the onset of *sukkot*, "I am not fully separating myself from these decorations throughout *bein hashmashos*," meaning he does not wholly surrender his rights to the fruit and adornments as *sukkot* begins — then *lo chala kedushah alaihu*: the sanctity of the *sukkah* never takes full hold of the *noi*, and one's stipulation retains effectiveness. *Aval atzei sukkah, de-chala kedushah alaihu, itkatsai le-shiva* — but the wood of the *sukkah* itself, upon which the sanctity does fully descend, remains off-limits for the entire seven days. No stipulation can undo that.

This introduces the broader principle of *huktza le-mitzvah*, designation for a *mitzvah*: an item committed to the performance of a *mitzvah* — like the oil of a *chanukah* menorah — is off-limits for other uses, independent of the sanctity of the day and independent of ordinary *muktza*. For the *sukkah*'s structural wood, this principle applies in its fullest form. The *noi*, by contrast, can be kept outside that designation through a prior stipulation, so long as the stipulation is made before the *kedushah* takes hold.

### Esrog Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא מֵהָא דְּאִתְּמַר: הִפְרִישׁ שִׁבְעָה אֶתְרוֹגִים לְשִׁבְעַת הַיָּמִים, אָמַר רַב: כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת יוֹצֵא בָּהּ וְאוֹכְלָהּ לְאַלְתַּר. וְרַב אַסִּי אָמַר: כׇּל אַחַת יוֹצֵא בָּהּ וְאוֹכְלָהּ לְמָחָר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** in **what** way is it **different from that which was stated** with regard to a different *halakha*: In the case of one who **separated seven *etrogim* for** each of **the seven** Festival **days,** one for each day, **Rav said: He fulfills** his obligation **through each and every one** of them when he recites the blessing over the *lulav* and *etrog*, **and** if he so desires **he may eat it immediately** after the blessing. **And Rav Asi said: He fulfills** his obligation **through each one, and he may eat it the following day,** as it retains its sanctity for the duration of that entire day. In any case, all agree that the sanctity of each *etrog* does not extend to the following day. If so, why does the sanctity of the *sukka* extend through all seven days?

The *gemara* presses further: if the *sukkah* can be designated for only one day at a time — perhaps one could stipulate "I am designating this *sukkah* for day one, and that *sukkah* for day two," similar to a case involving *esrogim*. The case: if one sets aside seven *esrogim* for the seven days of *sukkot*, Rav rules *kol achas ve-achas yotzei bah ve-ocheil le-alter* — each *esrog*, once the *mitzvah* has been performed with it, may be eaten immediately. Rav Assi rules *kol achas ve-achas yotzei bah ve-ocheil le-machar* — one must wait until the following day to eat it, since the *esrog* remains designated for the entire day on which it is used. Yet both agree the *esrog* is not designated beyond its own day. If so, why couldn't one cycle through *sukkot* the same way, with each day's *sukkah* becoming permissible the following day?

### Seven Days Continuous

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם, דְּמַפְסְקוּ לֵילוֹת מִיָּמִים — כׇּל חַד וְחַד יוֹמָא מִצְוָה בְּאַפֵּי נַפְשֵׁיהּ הוּא. הָכָא, דְּלָא מַפְסְקוּ לֵילוֹת מִיָּמִים — כּוּלְּהוּ יוֹמֵי כַּחֲדָא יוֹמָא אֲרִיכְתָּא דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: There is a difference between an *etrog* and a *sukka*. **There,** with regard to an *etrog*, the **nights are divided from the days,** as the mitzva of *etrog* applies only during the day and not at night. This means that **each and every day is its own mitzva,** and therefore an item that is sanctified for one day is not necessarily sanctified for the following day. However, **here,** with regard to a *sukka*, **where** the **nights are not divided from the days,** as the mitzva of *sukka* applies at night as well, **all** seven **days are considered as one long day.** Throughout the Festival, there is no moment during which the sanctity of *sukka* leaves the wood; it leaves only at the conclusion of the Festival.

The *gemara* distinguishes: *hasam de-mafski leilos mi-yamim* — with the *lulav* and *esrog*, night interrupts day. Since the *mitzvah* of *lulav* is not performed at night, each day is a discrete and self-contained *mitzvah* period. *Kol chad ve-chad yoma mitzvah be-anpei nafsheih hu* — each day stands as its own independent *mitzvah*. The *esrog* designated for Monday is not designated for Tuesday; they are seven separate *mitzvos*.

*Hacha de-lo mafski leilos mi-yamim* — with *sukkah*, by contrast, night does not interrupt. One sleeps in the *sukkah*; the *mitzvah* continues through the night. *Kulei yoma ke-chad yoma arichta dami* — the entire seven-day period is like one single extended day. Once the *sukkah* is designated for the beginning of this long unified period, it remains designated — and therefore off-limits for other use — for the entire week.
