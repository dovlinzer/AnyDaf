# Bava Batra 54 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chazakah Fundamentals

### Three Modes of Acquisiti…

Today's daf is 54. We are in the middle of the discussion of *chazakah* — not the *chazakah* of three years' uncontested possession, but the *chazakah* more closely related to Masechet Kiddushin: the *chazakah* of taking possession, the specific act that enables a transfer of property. The Mishnah in Kiddushin establishes that land is acquired through *kesef*, *shtar*, and *chazakah*. Our *sugya* concerns what constitutes a valid *chazakah* as a mode of *kinyan*.

### Improving vs. Benefiting

The primary definition of *chazakah* is *na'al*, *gadar*, *ufaratz* — locking a gate, building a fence, or breaching a wall — acts that in some way improve the land. The Gemara on the preceding daf introduced a significant *chidush*: there exists a category of *chazakah* that consists not in improving the land but in deriving direct benefit from it. Sleeping directly on the ground, for instance, constitutes a valid *chazakah*, because the benefit is immediate and flows from the land itself. Harvesting fruit, by contrast, does not qualify: one's benefit comes not from the land directly but from the tree, and is realized only later through the eating of the fruit. This same principle explains why harvesting fruit does not constitute a *chazakah* of ownership — even though the three-year *chazakah* of presumptive ownership is established precisely by harvesting fruit year after year. The two types of *chazakah* operate on entirely different logics.

### Palm Tree Pruning

The Gemara extends this analysis to the pruning of a palm tree. If the pruning is evidently performed for the purpose of obtaining the branches or fronds — that is, to benefit from the tree — rather than to improve the tree itself, it does not constitute a valid *chazakah*. It is functionally no different from harvesting fruit. However, if the pruning is done on both sides of the tree in a manner that reasonably indicates it was done for the tree's benefit, it does work as a *chazakah*. This example also illustrates that intent — to the extent it can be inferred from the nature of the act — is a component of a valid *chazakah*.

## Shmuel's Context Rules

### Weeds & Twigs Case

The Gemara now presents a series of rulings attributed to Shmuel, each exploring how the purpose behind an act determines whether it constitutes a valid *chazakah*. The Gemara introduces the first: *hai man d'zachi zichaya* — someone who clears the land by pulling weeds and picking up twigs. The Rashbam explains that this refers to cleaning debris from the ground. If one does so *adaata d'ara* — for the sake of improving the land — then *kanei*, one acquires it. If one does so *adaata d'tzivi* — for the sake of the twigs themselves, say, to use as firewood — then *lo kanei*, one does not acquire. The distinguishing criterion is selectivity: *shakal ravrevi v'zutri* — if one takes both the large twigs and the small ones, one is evidently trying to clean the ground, and this constitutes a *chazakah*. *Shakal ravrevi v'shavik zutri* — if one takes only the large twigs and leaves the small ones, one is evidently interested only in the firewood and not in improving the land, and no acquisition results. The parallel to the palm tree discussion is precise.

### Leveling Ground

Shmuel adds another case: *hai man d'atkil tikla* — one who removes obstacles from the land, leveling out mounds and filling in holes. If done *adaata d'ara*, to improve the land for plowing, *kanei* — this is a valid *chazakah*. If done *adaata d'bei dari*, for the purpose of using the surface as a threshing floor, *lo kanei* — it does not work.

This ruling requires explanation, because at first glance it seems puzzling. If one levels the ground to use it as a threshing floor, one has surely made the land usable in a particular way — why should that not count as a *tikkun*? The Rashbam explains that the case must be understood in context: the land is ordinarily used for plowing, and one is leveling it only temporarily in order to thresh grain, after which one intends to vacate and allow the land to revert to its plowing use. A temporary intervention that is not oriented toward the ongoing productive value of the land does not constitute a *tikkun*. That said, the difficulty does not entirely dissolve, because even a partial leveling does not appear to damage the land's plowing capacity — it has simply been made somewhat flatter than before.

The Rashbam himself goes so far as to say *venimtza she'ein zeh kan ki im kilkul, ve'ein zeh metaken* — that this amounts to a *kilkul* rather than a *tikkun* — though it is genuinely hard to understand how leveling constitutes damage. Perhaps the act of threshing on the ground, rather than the leveling itself, is what harms the soil for future planting. In any case, the Rashbam also states that if it were clear the person intended to use the land permanently as a threshing floor, it would indeed constitute a valid *tikkun* — the ruling is specific to the scenario where the regular intended use is plowing.

The Gemara then provides the diagnostic criterion: *shakil mulya veshadya benatza* — if one takes a mound and throws the dirt into a hole, producing complete leveling, this is *adaata d'ara*, for the sake of plowing, and constitutes a *chazakah*. *Mulya bemulya venatza benatza* — by contrast, if one merely smooths the mounds and partially fills the holes, reducing but not eliminating the unevenness, this is *adaata d'bei dari*: sufficient for a threshing floor but not for plowing. The implicit assumption is that plowing requires a more thoroughly level surface than threshing does.

It is here that Tosafot enters with a pointed objection. Rav Yosef of Orleans (*hiksha Rav Yosef de'Orleans*) challenges this assumption directly: *mashma she'tzarich karka shaveh lacharisha yoter milidisha* — the Gemara seems to assume that plowing requires flatter ground than threshing. Yet, he says, *ve'anan sahadi delidisha tzarich harbeh yoter shaveh vechalak* — in our experience, threshing requires a considerably flatter and smoother surface than plowing. How, then, does this Gemara make sense?

Tosafot further notes the difficulty with the Rashbam's approach of distinguishing between temporary and permanent use of a threshing floor: *heichi adinan iy avid adaata degaren kavua o eino kavua?* — how would one ever know whether the person intended a permanent or temporary threshing floor? That criterion is unworkable in practice.

Rav Yosef of Orleans therefore proposes an entirely different reading of the Gemara. When the text says *adaata d'bei dari*, it does not mean a threshing floor (*goren*) in the usual sense. Rather, *debei daray* means using the space as a pen or barn for animals — *hainu lehamid sham beheimot*. He adduces support from elsewhere in the Talmud where *bei daray* refers to an animal enclosure. On this reading, the Gemara is saying that merely preparing the ground so that animals can stand on it does not constitute a *tikkun* sufficient for a *chazakah*, and the problem of requiring a flatter surface for threshing than for plowing disappears entirely.

Rabbeinu Tam responds to Rav Yosef of Orleans by defending the plain reading. He explains that in the Talmudic period threshing was performed with a *morag* — the implement described in the verse in Yeshayahu (*bamorag charutz chadash ba'al pipiyot*), a kind of spiked board dragged by animals over the grain. This technology did not require the same degree of surface flatness as later methods of threshing. One should not retroject the requirements of one's own agricultural technology onto the Gemara's assumptions. In their era, plowing required a flatter surface than threshing did.

### Water Diversion

Shmuel offers a third parallel case: *hai man d'pasach maya b'ara* — one who opens water into a plot of land, diverting a stream so that water flows onto it. If done *adaata d'ara* — to irrigate the land — *kanei*, it constitutes a *chazakah*. If done *adaata d'chivri* — to catch fish — *lo kanei*, it does not. The distinguishing sign: *pasach terei bavei* — if one opens two channels, one bringing water in and one taking it out, so that water merely passes through the land, this is evidently for fish-catching. *Pasach chad bava* — if one opens only a single inlet so the water enters and remains, this is irrigation, *adaata d'ara*, and constitutes a valid *chazakah*.

## Woman & Palm Tree

### 13-Year Harvesting

The Gemara now presents a concrete case. *Hahu itata d'achla dikla b'tafsicha telisar shanin* — a certain woman had been harvesting the branches and fronds of a palm tree for thirteen years, taking benefit from it. Recall that the word *pashach* appeared earlier in the daf in connection with palm tree pruning. Then a certain man came and dug a small amount at the base of the tree, turning over a little soil. He brought the case before Levi, and some say before Mar Ukva.

### Harvesting ≠ Improving

The ruling was *okmei b'yadei* — they awarded possession to the man who had improved the ground at the base of the tree. The woman, it appears, had been pruning in the manner that indicates she was simply taking the fronds for her own use, not pruning in the bilateral manner that would demonstrate she was doing it for the sake of the tree. Her activity therefore amounted to deriving benefit from the tree rather than improving it — precisely the category that the Gemara has established does not constitute a valid *chazakah*. The man, by contrast, had turned over the earth, performed an act of improvement to the land, and thereby acquired the tree.

### Ownership Demonstration

The woman protested before the court. The judge responded: *d'lo achzikat k'dmachziki inshi* — you did not establish *chazakah* in the manner that people normally establish *chazakah*. This phrase is worth pausing on, because on its surface it sounds as though the standard is merely communal practice — whatever people happen to do — rather than the halachic definition of a *kinyan*. The phrase *kidmachziki inshi* seems to invoke social norms rather than legal categories.

The tension is real and illuminating. The woman plainly assumed she had been *machzik* the tree all along, because in the popular understanding, occupying and using a tree for thirteen years ought to demonstrate ownership. Yet the court says that is not what constitutes *chazakah*. Perhaps the phrase is best understood as follows: what makes *chazakah* function as a *kinyan* is that it is an act which demonstrates ownership. And what demonstrates ownership is not taking benefit but taking responsibility — caring for a thing, investing in it. A sharecropper takes fruit. A laborer harvests. An owner is the one who fences, waters, and maintains. The primary *chazakah* of ownership is therefore an act of giving benefit to the land, not extracting it. From this perspective, *kidmachziki inshi* does not mean "whatever people happen to do" but rather "the act by which people demonstrate they are the owner." Harvesting fruit does not demonstrate ownership; it is what non-owners do. Digging at the base of the tree, improving the ground — that is what an owner does.

One might note the apparent irony: a woman who worked this tree for thirteen years is told she has no claim, while a man who turned over a small amount of dirt acquires it entirely. The law here is technically rigorous and practically striking.

## Drawing on Walls

### Tzura on Walls

The Gemara records that Rav said: *hatzad tzura b'nichsei hager kaneh* — one who draws a decorative design (*tzura*) on the wall of a house belonging to a *ger* — here meaning property that has become ownerless, *nichsei hager* — acquires it. By drawing on the wall, one has improved the structure, and this constitutes a valid *kinyan*.

### Rav Huna's Garden

The Gemara specifies: *d'Rav lo kanei l'ginta d'bei Rav ela b'tzurata* — Rav acquired the garden adjacent to his *yeshiva* only by means of its design. The garden was evidently *nichsei hager*, ownerless property, and the act of drawing designs on its walls was considered an improvement and thus a *kinyan*.

It is worth noting the contrast here with the preceding case. A woman who harvested a palm tree for thirteen years acquired nothing; a person who draws a picture on a wall acquires the property. The distinction is technically precise — taking benefit versus giving benefit — but the juxtaposition is arresting.

The Rashbam adds a relevant nuance. Earlier in the Gemara, when discussing plastering and drawing on a wall, the Gemara required that the drawing be at least an *amah* in length and positioned opposite the doorway. Here, no such requirement is mentioned. The Rashbam explains that the earlier rule applied to ordinary decorative work — generic curling designs, flowers, simple patterns (*tziyurin b'alma*, patterns of creatures and flowers). For more significant artistic representations — an actual figure of an animal or bird, something representationally meaningful — the mere act of drawing constitutes a more substantial improvement, and one is not required to meet the criterion of an *amah* opposite the doorway. The quality of the artistry, in other words, affects the legal threshold. A minor decorative flourish must meet stricter locational requirements; a more impressive work does not.

## Field Scope Dispute

### Rav: Whole Field

The Gemara now shifts from discussing the nature of the act that constitutes *chazakah* to asking about the spatial scope of its effect. The question: when one performs a single act of *chazakah* on part of a field, does the entire field thereby become acquired, or only the specific spot on which the act was performed?

*Itmar. Sadeha mesuyemet bemetzareha* — a field that is demarcated and defined, with clear boundary markers establishing exactly where the field begins and ends. Rav Huna said in the name of Rav: *keivan shehikish bah maka achat kanah kulah* — once one has struck it with a single blow, one acquires the entire field. The implicit assumption throughout the preceding discussion has been along these lines: one performs a single act of improvement on a portion of the field and thereby acquires the whole.

### Shmuel: Location Only

Shmuel disagrees: *lo kanah ela mekom makosho bilvad* — one acquires only the precise location where one dug or turned the soil.

### Boundaries & Scope

This immediately raises a difficulty. We have learned that building a fence, locking a gate, or making an opening — the classic *na'al gadar ufaratz* — constitutes a *chazakah* on the entire field. If Shmuel says one acquires only the spot of one's act, how can those acts work for the whole field?

The resolution lies in the nature of the act. A fence or wall surrounds the entire field; building or improving it necessarily touches the whole perimeter that defines the field. Locking a gate or making a breach relates to the wall, which itself defines the entire enclosed area. These acts are, by their nature, acts performed with respect to the whole field. By contrast, turning over a small patch of soil in the middle of a field is an act done to that particular patch. It does not inherently relate to the rest of the field.

This distinction also connects to an important limiting condition: the case of *nichsei hager* — ownerless property — where, unlike in a purchase, there is no money paid and no seller whose intent designates the entire field as a single unit. In a sale, the seller's intention to transfer a defined parcel, or the money that is given in exchange for a defined area, can extend the transaction to encompass the whole field, even fields separated by some distance. When a field comes into one's possession from *hefker*, the act of *chazakah* must itself define the scope of the acquisition, and a single shovelful of dirt does not inherently define the whole.

### No-Boundary Cases

The Gemara then turns to a related question: *veshe'einah mesuyemet bemetzareha* — a field that lacks physical boundary markers. According to Shmuel, we do not know how much one would need to do to acquire the entire field. Even according to Rav, when there are no external markers to define the field's identity as a unit, the question arises: how much of the field must one's act of *chazakah* encompass?

*Ad kamah* — how much? Rav Pappa says: *ki de'azal tayara deshurei vehadar* — like a team of oxen goes in one direction and returns, plowing a furrow the length of the field and back.

The Rashbam and Tosafot disagree about what question Rav Pappa is answering. The Rashbam understands him to be answering: how much must one do in order to acquire the entire unmarked field? The answer is that one must go across one full dimension — back and forth, the width of the field. Even though one has not traversed the length, the dimensions of the field are known from the deed, even if not physically marked. One traversal of one dimension suffices to constitute a *chazakah* on the whole. Tosafot reads the question differently: when one digs a single hole in a field without markers, how much does one thereby acquire? The answer is the area of a single furrow's length and return — the span of one plowing pass. Yet Tosafot essentially agrees with the Rashbam's underlying principle: that to acquire the entire unmarked field, one would need to traverse its full length, without necessarily having to go both length and width.

One might have thought logically that one would need to traverse both the length and the width — to define the entire two-dimensional space. The resonance with *Avraham Avinu* walking the land *le'orchah ulerochbah* — traversing it lengthwise and widthwise — is suggestive: to take possession of land one walks the whole thing. But the *halacha* as articulated here requires at minimum one full dimension to link the act to the totality of the plot.

## Non-Jew Property

### Desert Principle

The Gemara now opens an entirely new and far-reaching discussion. *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel: nichsei ovdei chochavim harei hen kemidbar* — the property of non-Jews is like the wilderness, like ownerless property. *Kol hamachzik bahen zachah bahen* — anyone who takes possession acquires it. This is a striking statement on its face: can one simply walk over to one's non-Jewish neighbor and perform a *chazakah* on his house? No. As the Gemara will clarify, this applies specifically when property is in the process of being transferred — and there arises a gap between when the non-Jew relinquishes ownership and when the Jew acquires it, during which a third party may seize the property.

### Kesef vs. Shtar

*Mai ta'ama?* What is the reason? *Ovdei chochavim miki matu zuzai lideih istalak leih* — for the non-Jew, the payment of money (*kesef*) is sufficient to complete the transfer; the moment he receives the money, he relinquishes his ownership.

There is a well-known debate in Bava Metzia about whether the standard modes of *kinyan* for Jews and non-Jews are identical or inverse. The Rashbam here assumes that *kesef* is the operative *kinyan* for non-Jews in all domains. Tosafot takes a more nuanced position, suggesting that perhaps for moveable goods the non-Jew's *kinyan* is *meshichah*, but at minimum for real property (*karka*), the *kinyan* is by *kesef* — as demonstrated by the biblical precedent of Efron's sale of the cave of Machpelah to Avraham. Whatever the general rule, it is established that for land, a non-Jew's transfer is effected through money.

So when a Jew pays money to a non-Jew for land, the non-Jew's ownership immediately terminates. However, in a locale where the accepted practice between Jews is to write a *shtar* — and there are many such places, since while *kesef* is theoretically sufficient for Jews acquiring land, the established norm in most places was to insist on a deed to ensure legal certainty — the Jew does not yet take possession until the *shtar* is written. *Yisrael lo kanei ad demati shtar leyadei* — the Jew has not acquired until the deed reaches his hand.

The logic is as follows: since the standard in this locale requires a *shtar* between Jews, and since the Jew's default expectations and customary practice are shaped by that norm, even when purchasing from a non-Jew those same expectations apply. One does not regard the deal as concluded until a *shtar* is written. In the interim — after the non-Jew has received the money and relinquished his ownership, but before the *shtar* has been delivered — the land exists in a kind of legal no-man's land. It is neither owned by the non-Jew nor yet by the Jew. And during that gap, *harei hein kemidbar* — it is ownerless, and *kol hamachzik bahen zachah bahen*, anyone who performs an act of *chazakah* on it acquires it.

The Rashbam notes, though he does not resolve, the moral dimension: the person who swoops in and seizes this land is called a *rasha*. The principle of *ani hamahapech b'chararah* — the poor man who is about to take possession of a found object and is beaten to it by someone else — establishes that such a person is morally condemned even if technically permitted. Here the situation is worse: the Jew had actually paid money to the non-Jew. If a third party seizes the property, the original buyer has lost his money and has no recourse — the non-Jew has no obligation to return the payment, and the Gemara indicates the third party need not compensate him either. The seizure is halachically effective and morally reprehensible simultaneously.

Tosafot raises a substantive challenge. Since *kesef* can in principle work for a Jew acquiring land, and it only fails in this locale because of the customary expectation that a *shtar* will be written, one would think that in a transaction where the non-Jew's ownership ends with *kesef*, the Jew would similarly want his acquisition to take effect with *kesef* — to avoid precisely the dangerous gap being described. Why would any reasonable person maintain the expectation of needing a *shtar* when doing so leaves the property temporarily ownerless, exposing himself to losing everything he paid?

Tosafot's answer: *ve'tzarich lomar d'anan sahadi shelo haya beda'ato ela kemoshehu ragil liknot miYisrael dehainu beshtar* — we attest that the buyer's default assumption is that the transaction works the same way it would if he were buying from a Jew, i.e., not concluded until a *shtar* is written. Even though this leaves a gap, and even though *kesef* could theoretically protect him, the force of habit and customary expectation means the buyer does not fully intend to complete the transaction without a *shtar*. Tosafot acknowledges this answer is less satisfying than the question.

## Dina D'Malchusa

### Abaye's Challenge

Against this entire framework, Abaye poses a powerful objection to Rav Yosef. *Mi amar Shmuel hachi?* — how could Shmuel possibly have held this position? For Shmuel himself is the author of the famous principle *dina d'malchusa dina* — the law of the kingdom is the law. And the king had explicitly ruled that land does not transfer except with a deed — *lo tikni ara ela be'igarta*. If we follow the law of the secular government, then the non-Jew's ownership does not terminate merely upon receiving money; it terminates only when a deed is issued. And correspondingly, the Jew takes possession precisely when the deed is issued. There is no gap, no ownerless interval, no opening for a third party to seize the land. How, then, can the same Shmuel who taught *dina d'malchusa dina* also teach that this land is available for seizure?

## Non-Jew Property…

### Dura d'Reusa Case

Rav Yosef replies: I cannot resolve the apparent contradiction between Shmuel's two teachings, but here is how I know Shmuel himself held the position that a third party can seize. *Be'uvda d'hava beDura diReusa* — there was an actual case in Dura diReusa (which the Rashbam explains means either a place name or a village of shepherds, from *raya*, shepherd). A Jew purchased land from a non-Jew. A second Jew came and turned over a small amount of that land — *rafik bepurtah*. The case came before Rav Yehuda, a student of Shmuel, and Rav Yehuda awarded the land to the second Jew who had performed the *chazakah*. Since Rav Yehuda was ruling in keeping with his teacher Shmuel, this demonstrates that Shmuel held the third party can acquire.

## Dina D'Malchusa…

### Back Taxes Explanation

Abaye responds: *Dura diReusa kamart?* You want to bring a proof from that case? *Hasam bagei d'mitmari havai* — in that case the lands in question were plots being concealed from the government. The original non-Jewish owners had not been paying their back taxes to the king — *de'inhu gufaiyu lo havai yahavi taska lemalchah*. And following the very principle of *dina d'malchusa dina* — which Abaye himself insists must govern — the king had declared: *man d'yahiv taska lachol ara* — whoever pays the outstanding taxes on a piece of land may claim it. The second Jew, therefore, was not seizing ownerless property in the *halachic* sense described by Shmuel's ruling. Rather, he was entitled to the land under the law of the king because he stepped forward and paid the back taxes that the non-Jew had failed to pay. The case was a distressed-land situation, akin to tax forfeiture. Abaye's reconstruction of the story thus shows that the entire episode was governed by *dina d'malchusa dina*, not by any *halachic* gap of ownership. Rav Yosef's proof collapses: the two statements of Shmuel remain unreconciled.

## Non-Jew Property……

### Rav Huna v. Nachman

A parallel case is then recorded. Rav Huna purchased a plot of land from a non-Jew. A second Jew came and turned over a small amount of it — *rafik bepurtah*. The case came before Rav Nachman, who awarded the property to the second Jew — *okmei b'yadei*. Rav Huna challenged Rav Nachman: on what basis are you ruling this way? If you are relying on Shmuel's teaching that *nichsei ovdei chochavim harei hen kemidbar* and a third party can seize it, then at least be consistent with Shmuel's other ruling: *lo kanah ela mekom makosho bilvad* — the second Jew should acquire only the precise spot where he dug. Let him have his little hole; the rest of the field remains mine.

Here we see the connection back to the earlier discussion in this daf. Shmuel's two teachings — that one acquires only the spot of one's act, and that property of non-Jews in transfer is like ownerless wilderness — are now brought into direct confrontation with each other in a practical case.

Rav Nachman replied: *baha ana kishmai'atich svira li* — on that question, I rule in accordance with your own teaching, i.e., in the name of Rav: *keivan shenikesh bemakom echad kanah kulah* — a single blow acquires the entire defined field. The result: Rav Nachman ruled against Rav Huna on both counts simultaneously. He ruled that a third party can seize — following Shmuel's *kemidbar* principle — and that one act of *chazakah* acquires the entire field — following Rav, as taught by Rav Huna himself. Rav Huna loses the field entirely.

## Dina D'Malchusa……

### Public vs. Civil Law

The Gemara has left unresolved how to reconcile Shmuel's two teachings. The Rashbam offers what he acknowledges to be an unsatisfying answer: the secular law of that locale was simply not the kind that required a *shtar*, and therefore there was in fact no tension with *dina d'malchusa dina*. This amounts to claiming that the disputants were simply mistaken about what the actual secular law required — a resolution that is difficult to accept given the apparent deliberateness of the Talmudic debate.

Assuming the secular law genuinely did require a *shtar*, the question of how to reconcile the two Shmuelian teachings opens into one of the most consequential discussions in halachic literature: what are the limits of *dina d'malchusa dina*? How far does secular law go in overriding *halacha*?

The concern is real and urgent. Obviously we cannot extend *dina d'malchusa dina* to invalidate a *get* because a civil divorce has been obtained. But what about *dinei mamonos*? Consider laws of inheritance. The Rambam is unequivocal that *dina d'malchusa dina* does not apply there: the Torah's laws of inheritance carry the designation of *chok* — they are statutory in a fundamental sense, not merely transactional arrangements between parties. More broadly, the Rashba, in a famous *teshuvah*, takes vehement exception to any suggestion that *dina d'malchusa dina* could wholesale replace *halachic* civil law. His retort: if that were so, why do we need all of *Nezikin*? Why do we need the entire corpus of *Choshen Mishpat*? Who needs all the works of Ravina and Rav Ashi in the domain of *dinei mamonos*? Nearly a quarter of Shas addresses civil law — one cannot simply sweep it aside on the grounds that secular law governs. That this very claim is sometimes implicitly operative in contemporary *batei din* — which frequently resolve cases based on some combination of *dina d'malchusa dina*, compromise, and equitable considerations rather than strict *Choshen Mishpat* — does not alter the theoretical force of the Rashba's position.

So what are the limits? One approach holds that *dina d'malchusa dina* applies only where *halacha* has not addressed a particular area — it fills in the gaps. Intellectual property is a frequently cited example: since *halacha* contains no concept of intellectual property rights, secular law may govern in that domain. A counterargument is that *halacha*'s silence is not a gap but a statement — if *halacha* does not recognize intellectual property, that itself is the *halachic* position. Nevertheless, this approach would potentially harmonize the two statements of Shmuel: the gap of ownership between a non-Jew's transfer and the Jew's acquisition is precisely the kind of legal lacuna that *dina d'malchusa dina* could fill — except that Shmuel himself evidently did not fill it here.

A more satisfying resolution is associated with the *Geonim*. Their position is that *dina d'malchusa dina* applies specifically to what might be called public law — the body of law that governs the relationship between citizens and the government. Taxes, government levies, regulations pertaining to one's obligations to the state: these fall under *dina d'malchusa dina*, as seen from the primary *sugya* in Bava Kamma, where the principle was first applied to taxation. If the government takes wood from one's property to build roads or bridges, one cannot claim the builder of the bridge has stolen from one's neighbor — *dina d'malchusa dina*. But the laws governing relationships between private individuals — classic civil law, the domain of *Choshen Mishpat* — those remain governed by *halacha*.

This division has considerable intuitive force. We have an elaborate body of *halachic* literature governing interpersonal obligations. We have essentially nothing governing the relationship of individual Jews to sovereign governments — no *masechet* dedicated to that question. The reason is historically plain: until modern times, Jews lived under non-Jewish sovereigns, and the laws defining their obligations to those sovereigns were necessarily the laws of those sovereigns themselves. *Dina d'malchusa dina* fills that vacuum. But for the purely interpersonal domain, *halacha* was always present and complete.

Crucially, this resolution works well for our *sugya*. The question of when precisely a land transaction transfers ownership between private parties — a Jew buying from a non-Jew — is a matter of interpersonal civil law, not public law. *Dina d'malchusa dina* does not apply. The *halachic* analysis of *kesef* and *shtar* governs, and the gap of ownerless property remains possible.

### Consent Theory

The Rashbam pauses at the phrase *dina d'malchusa dina* to offer what is in effect a political theory — an explanation of why the law of the government should be binding at all. His account is grounded in the consent of the governed. He writes that *kol bnei hamalchut mekablim aleihem mirtsonam chukei hamelech umishpatav* — all the subjects of the kingdom accept upon themselves, willingly, the laws and statutes of the king. Therefore it constitutes *din gamur*, fully valid law — *ve'ein lemachzik bemamon chaveiro al pi chok hamelech hanehug ba'ir mishum gezel* — and one who retains another's property in accordance with the king's established law is not regarded as committing theft. The basis of governmental authority, on the Rashbam's account, is the implicit consent of those governed. This is a recognizable political theory, and one that appears to predate its most famous later Western formulations by several centuries.

### Feudal vs. Modern

The Ran offers a contrasting political theory, one that reflects a feudal conception of land tenure: the king owns all the land. Because the king is the fundamental proprietor of the territory, he may set the conditions under which subjects occupy and use that land. His authority to make law follows from his status as ultimate landowner. This theory was congruent with the feudal reality of medieval Europe but carries significant implications when extended to *Eretz Yisrael*.

The Ran himself noted that his theory might not apply in *Eretz Yisrael*, because in *Eretz Yisrael* the land belongs to the Jewish people — each family has a Torah-mandated portion. The land is not fundamentally the king's. If the basis of *dina d'malchusa dina* is the king's ownership of the land, that basis is absent in *Eretz Yisrael*.

This observation opens a significant contemporary question. How does one conceptualize, halachically, the authority of the State of Israel? Two broad approaches emerge among the *poskim*. One holds that the State of Israel is simply another secular government, and *dina d'malchusa dina* applies to it just as it applied to the governments of England or France. This is a non-Zionist posture, and it faces the difficulty that if the Ran's theory underlies *dina d'malchusa dina*, it would actually apply *less* to Israel than to other states, since in *Eretz Yisrael* the king-owns-the-land rationale is absent. Some have indeed used this reasoning to argue that the laws of the State of Israel carry less binding force than those of other governments — an argument deployed by those who wish to contest state authority on halachic grounds.

The alternative is to conceptualize the State of Israel not through the lens of *dina d'malchusa dina* at all, but through the category of *melech* in the Torah's framework — a halachically grounded Jewish polity. This is the Zionist aspiration, and it carries its own enormous challenges: the *halacha* of Jewish governance — of a police force, a military, a judicial system, a tax code — was never fully developed precisely because, for two millennia, Jews did not govern themselves. There was no need, and no opportunity. With the founding of the State, many envisioned that a new body of *halacha* would emerge to meet those needs. That project is ongoing, and the theoretical frameworks established in this Gemara and in the *rishonim* who wrestled with them remain directly relevant to it.
