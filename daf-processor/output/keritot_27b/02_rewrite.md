# Keritot 27b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Korban Designation

### Son & Father's Chattat

The *mishnah* opens with a seemingly straightforward case: if a person sets aside a *chattat* and then dies, his son cannot bring that *chattat* in his father's place. This ruling launches an extended discussion about the specificity with which a *korban* is designated—not merely for a particular person, but for a particular sin.

### No Kapara for Dead

Two distinct reasons underlie this prohibition. First, there is no formal halachic *kapara* for the dead—whatever other traditions exist regarding *kaddish* or learning *mishnayot* on behalf of the deceased, no *korban* can achieve atonement for someone who has already died. Second, and more fundamentally, a *chattat* cannot be used for a person other than the one it was designated for, nor can it be used for a sin other than the one it was designated to atone for. The *mishnah* presses this point to its logical extreme: even the person himself cannot bring a *chattat* designated for one act of eating *chelev* to atone for a different act of eating *chelev* on a different day. The specificity is absolute—the *chattat* is tied to the person and to the precise sin.

### Peshat from Vayikra

The biblical source for this principle is the phrase *korbano al chataso*—his *korban* for his sin. The word *korbano* indicates that it must be his own *korban*, not his father's; *chataso* indicates that it must be for his specific sin, not a different one.

### Father's Money Question

The *gemara* then probes further. If an animal is too specifically designated to be transferred, what about money? After all, unlike an animal, money is more abstract—perhaps money sanctified by a father for a *chattat* could be used by the son. The *tana rabbanan* works through several hypothetical cases: perhaps a son cannot use his father's sanctified animal, but could he use his father's sanctified money? And could he perhaps use it even across different categories of sin—light to light, or heavy to heavy?

### Animal vs. Money Kedusha

The *gemara* explains why there is a conceptual distinction between an animal and money, and why that distinction ultimately does not permit reassignment in either case. The key concept is the nature of *kedusha*. When an animal is designated as a *chattat*, its *kedusha* is fixed absolutely—even if one were to commit *me'ila* with that animal, it would not lose its sanctity and exit to *chullin*. This immovability is evidence that its identity is wholly fixed and cannot be switched. Money, by contrast, has a more transferable *kedusha*: if one commits *me'ila* with designated money, that money does become desanctified and exit to *chullin*. One might therefore think that because money's *kedusha* is less fixed, the designation could be reassigned. Similarly, an unblemished animal cannot be redeemed to transfer its *kedusha*, whereas money can be transferred—another indication that the animal's *kedusha* is categorically more rigid.

### Me'ila & Fixed Identity

The *gemara* pursues this logic carefully. For an animal, the reasoning is: *lo ma'al velo kiper*—since *me'ila* would not dislodge the animal's *kedusha*, there is no avenue by which its identity could ever shift, and therefore reassignment is impossible. For money, the argument is more nuanced. Since if one *were* to commit *me'ila* with the money it would become desanctified and one could then use it for a different *korban*—even if illicitly—this shows in principle that the money's designation is not entirely immovable. One might therefore conclude that even *lechatchila* the money could be reassigned. The *gemara* rejects this: the *pasuk* says *korbano al chataso*, establishing that even money designated for a *chattat* cannot be reassigned to a different sin. The identity is fixed regardless.

Rav Pappa clarifies the precise meaning of *lo ma'al velo kiper* in the animal case: since there is no way, even through transgression, to dislodge the animal's *kedusha*, the animal can never serve another purpose—and therefore its designation is absolutely immovable. With money, since transgression *would* in theory result in the money becoming *chullin* and then being used for a different *korban*, one might have thought reassignment should be permitted. The *pasuk* teaches otherwise.

The discussion closes with a note that the same rule governs an *asham talui*: the final ruling follows the *chattat*—it too is specific to person and sin, and cannot be reassigned.

## Ola VeYored Switches

### Sheep to Goat Switch

The *mishnah* proceeds to a separate category of cases involving flexibility within the same *korban*. If a person sanctified money to purchase a sheep for a *chattat*, he may change his mind and use the money instead for a goat—and vice versa. This is not considered reassigning the *korban* to a different sin; the money remains *chattat* money. It simply was not specifically sheep money, and the shift from one animal to another of the same halachic category is permitted.

### Up & Down Scale

The more dramatic flexibility concerns the *olah veyored*—the small set of sins for which the Torah mandates a korban scaled to the sinner's economic standing. The *olah veyored* obligates a wealthy person to bring a sheep or goat, a person of middle means to bring two birds (one *chattat*, one *olah*), and a very poor person to bring a *minchah*. The *mishnah* rules that if one's financial standing shifts after setting aside money, one may move along this scale accordingly. If you set aside money for a sheep and became poorer, you may redirect the money to birds. If you became poorer still, you may redirect it to a *minchah*, pocketing any surplus. Conversely, if you set aside money for a *minchah* and became richer, you must add to it and bring a bird; richer still, a sheep or goat.

The *Rambam*'s explanation of the mechanics is instructive: you purchase the birds with your own money and then declare that the *kedusha* of the original sum is transferred onto the birds. While ordinarily one may not transfer *kedusha* from a more valuable item to a lesser one, if one does so it nonetheless takes effect—and here, because the *korban* obligation has itself shifted, this transfer is permitted even *lechatchila*. The remainder of the original sanctified money becomes *chullin* and may be used for personal needs.

It is worth emphasizing that the critical moment for determining one's financial standing is *not* the moment of sanctification. As the *mishnah* makes plain, if you sanctified money for a sheep and later became richer, you are obligated to bring a sheep—you cannot avail yourself of the more lenient category simply because you happened to sanctify the money when you were poorer.

### Mem vs. Alef Derasha

The *gemara* derives the rules of the *olah veyored* scale from two phrases in the *pasuk*. When the Torah speaks of bringing a sheep or goat it uses the term *mechataso*—with a *mem*, meaning "from his sin," indicating a subset of the larger amount. When speaking of the *minchah* it uses the term *al chataso*—with an *alef lamed*, meaning "upon his sin," indicating addition. The *mem* teaches that one may draw *down* from what was designated—i.e., use part of the sheep-money for a bird or part of the bird-money for a *minchah*. The *al* teaches that one may add *up*—supplementing the *minchah* money to bring a bird, or supplementing the bird money to bring a sheep.

### Repetition Defense

One might ask: why does the Torah need to say *mechataso* both by the sheep-or-goat case and again by the bird case? Would it not suffice to state it once and let us infer the rest? The *gemara* explains that without the second statement, one would have thought that moving down from sheep to birds is permitted—since both are blood offerings placed on the altar—but moving down from birds to a *minchah* is not, since a *minchah* is a fundamentally different type of *korban* that involves no blood at all. Therefore the Torah had to state *mechataso* explicitly by the bird case as well, to teach that one may move down even from birds to a *minchah*.

Symmetrically, why state *al chataso* by the *minchah* rather than by the bird? Had the Torah stated it by the bird, one might have inferred that moving up from birds to sheep-or-goat is permitted—since both are blood offerings—but moving up from a *minchah* to a bird might not be, since they are categorically different. By stating *al chataso* at the *minchah* level, the Torah teaches that even from a *minchah* one may move upward to a bird and from a bird to a sheep. Both phrases are thus indispensable.

### Bird Redemption Limit

There is one important limitation in moving down the scale: if one has sanctified the birds themselves—not merely money for birds—and they acquire a blemish, one cannot redeem them and redirect the proceeds to a *minchah*. The reason is that birds have no *pidyon*—there is no halachic mechanism to redeem a sanctified bird. This means that if one has sanctified actual birds rather than money, one is in a very constrained position. The birds cannot be redeemed, they cannot move down to a *minchah*, and one is essentially stuck with them.

## Dichuy Principle

### Rich Sets Aside Birds

Rav Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya addresses the following case: a wealthy person who contracted *tum'at mikdash* (entering the Temple while impure or eating *kodashim* while impure)—one of the classic *olah veyored* categories—is obligated to bring a sheep. Instead, this person sets aside birds. Subsequently, the person becomes poor, and birds are now their proper *korban*. Can they now use those birds?

The ruling is *hova'il venidcha yidacheh*—since those birds were pushed away and unacceptable at the moment they were designated, they remain pushed away permanently. When the person was rich, those birds were not their proper *korban*. They were labeled unacceptable at that moment, and that rejection is permanent even though they have now become the proper *korban*. This is the principle of *dichuy*: once a *korban* is stamped as rejected, that stigma does not lift.

Note that this case involves the actual birds, not money. The question of whether the same principle would apply to sanctified money—which is conceptually more fungible—is left open. But once the birds themselves are sanctified, they carry *kedusha* (specifically *kedushat damim*), and the *dichuy* attaches to them with full force. One cannot even redeem them, since birds have no *pidyon*, leaving the birds in an essentially irresolvable state.

### Shema Mina Telat

Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua draws three conclusions from this ruling—a *shema mina telat*. First, *ba'alei chayim nidchin*: live animals can be subject to *dichuy*. Some hold that this is impossible—that a living creature cannot be permanently rejected—but this case implies they can. Second, *kedushat damim nidchah*: even when the *kedusha* was only *kedushat damim* (monetary sanctity) from the outset—since the birds were never fit as this person's proper *korban* from the moment of their designation—this form of *kedusha* is nonetheless subject to *dichuy*. Third, *dichuy mei'ikara havei dichuy*: even rejection from the very beginning, where the object was never once fit, constitutes valid *dichuy*. It is worth noting that these latter two conclusions overlap considerably, and the *gemara* itself acknowledges that the last two tend to collapse into each other when discussing *ba'alei chayim nidchin*—the *gemara* notes that it always seeks three and that the final two tend to be closely related.

### Rabbi Shimon Dispute

Rav Ukva bar Chama challenges from a *braita*: if someone designates a female animal for the *Pesach* offering before Passover (a *Pesach* requires a male), the animal must wait to acquire a blemish, be redeemed, and the proceeds used to purchase a proper *Pesach*. If before that redemption the female gives birth to a male, that male must likewise wait to acquire a blemish and be redeemed—it cannot simply be brought as a *Pesach* directly. But Rabbi Shimon disagrees and says the male offspring *can* be brought as a *Pesach*. 

From Rabbi Shimon's ruling, we see that he holds *ba'alei chayim einan nidchin*—live animals are not subject to *dichuy*. The mother's unacceptability does not invalidate the male offspring; the offspring carries *kedushat Pesach* and, being male, is fit to be brought. This directly contradicts the three-fold conclusion drawn above. The *gemara* resolves the tension by noting that the ruling of Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua was stated in accordance with the majority view that disputes Rabbi Shimon. Rabbi Shimon's view—that *ba'alei chayim einan nidchin*—is a known minority position.

### Hagralah Case

Rav Shimi bar Ashi adds a further nuance regarding Rabbi Shimon's position. Rabbi Shimon also holds that the *hagralah*—the drawing of lots over the two Yom Kippur goats—is not indispensable (*einah me'akevet*). These two positions of Rabbi Shimon are linked. In the case where one of the pair of Yom Kippur goats dies after the lots are drawn, the surviving goat was temporarily unfit—it had no pair. Rabbi Shimon says that the surviving goat is not thereby rendered *nidcheh*, since *ba'alei chayim einan nidchin*, and furthermore that when a replacement is found the lots need not be redrawn. For the majority, who hold that *ba'alei chayim nidchin*, the remaining goat is stigmatized by its period of unfitness and cannot simply be reused with a replacement.

## Bird Designation Timing

### Rav Chisda Rule

Rav Chisda establishes a foundational principle for the tractate of *Kinnim*: the birds of a *kan* (a pair of birds obligated as one *chattat* and one *olah*) cannot acquire their individual designations—which is the *chattat* and which is the *olah*—except at one of two specific moments. Either the owner designates them at the time of *lekichah*, purchase, or the *kohen* designates them at the time of performing the service. Any designation attempted in between these two moments is halachically inoperative.

### Vayikra Designation

Rav Shimi bar Ashi provides the textual basis for this principle. One *pasuk* says *ve-lakcha shtei torim*—"and she shall take two birds"—and then *echad le-chatas ve-echad le-olah*, "one for a *chattat* and one for an *olah*." The phrase "one for a *chattat* and one for an *olah*" appears at the moment of *ve-lakcha*, the taking or purchase. A second *pasuk* says *ve-asah ha-kohen*—"and the *kohen* shall perform"—followed again by *echad le-chatas ve-echad le-olah*. The designation phrase thus appears exclusively at the moment of purchase and at the moment of the *kohen*'s action, nowhere between.

### Hagralah Challenge

The *gemara* challenges this from the *hagralah* of Yom Kippur. By the two Yom Kippur goats, both goats have already been sanctified, and the question of which is the *chattat* and which is the *la-Azazel* goat is resolved only by drawing lots—which occurs at a middle stage, neither at the time the goats were purchased nor at the time the *kohen* performs the service. This is a form of designation occurring at an intermediate time, seemingly contradicting Rav Chisda's rule.

### Kal VaChomer Analysis

The *gemara* considers a *kal vachomer*. For a normal *korban*, a lot would not sanctify an animal—verbal designation (*shem*) is what creates *kedusha* and identity. By the Yom Kippur goats, the lot *does* create the designation. One might therefore argue: just as lots work at their appointed time for the Yom Kippur goats—a time that is neither purchase nor service—so too should verbal designation work at some intermediate time for birds, and at an even greater degree since verbal designation is the more standard form.

### Verse Override

The *gemara* resolves the challenge by clarifying that the comparison to the Yom Kippur goats is an analogy, not an identity. The *pasuk* states *ve-asahu chatas*—"and it shall make it a *chattat*"—from which the *drashah* derives that only the lot makes it a *chattat*, not verbal designation, and not any action of the *kohen* alone in a non-standard way. The *kal vachomer* would have been persuasive, but the *pasuk* overrides it explicitly. Moreover, the analogy is more subtle: for birds, wherever the right time is—purchase or the *kohen*'s action—verbal designation works at that right time; lots do not. For the Yom Kippur goats, the designated time is the drawing of lots, and verbal designation does not work even then. The two cases operate by different rules, and the *pasuk* for the Yom Kippur goats is explicit that only the lot governs.

## Bird Money Subdivision

### Poor to Rich Case

The *gemara* now examines a *braita* related to the *mishnah*'s teaching about moving up the scale. A poor person who incurred *tum'at mikdash* sets aside money for a *kan*—a pair of birds. Subsequently, the person becomes rich, so the bird offering is no longer the correct *korban*; a sheep or goat is required. The person then declares: this portion of the money is for my *chattat* and this portion for my *olah*. He then adds funds to the *chattat* portion and brings a sheep or goat *chattat*. The *braita* permits this addition for the *chattat* portion. However, he cannot add to the *olah* portion and bring a separate *olah* from it, since the obligation of a *chattat-olah* pair applies specifically when bringing birds—when bringing a sheep or goat, only a *chattat* is required, not an *olah*. The *olah* bird money therefore remains designated as *olah* and goes toward a *nedavah*.

This *braita* clarifies the *mishnah*: the permission to add to bird money and redirect it to a sheep or goat applies only when the bird money is undivided and generic. Once it has been subdivided—with specific amounts assigned to the *chattat* and the *olah*—only the *chattat* portion may be supplemented and redirected. The *olah* portion is fixed in its designation.

### Rav Sheshes Challenge

Rav Sheshes objects to the *braita* as it appears to read. The *braita* states that after becoming rich, the person declares which portion is for what. But once a person is rich, birds are categorically not their *korban*. How can such a person, now firmly in the sheep-or-goat category, validly declare which portion of the bird money is for a *chattat* and which for an *olah*? This subdivision is irrelevant to a rich person's obligation.

### Rich Cannot Use Bird

This objection is reinforced by the ruling of Rav Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya stated earlier: if a rich person who incurred *tum'at mikdash* brought a bird offering, he would not be *yotzei* even *bedi'eved*. If a rich person cannot fulfill the obligation with birds even after the fact, then any subdivision of bird money by a rich person is legally meaningless—the designation of which birds would serve as *chattat* and which as *olah* has no operative effect on someone for whom birds are entirely outside the applicable *korban* category.

### Original Designation

The resolution is that the *braita* does not mean the person declared the subdivision *after* becoming rich. Rather, the *braita* must be read as saying the person had already declared which portion was for the *chattat* and which for the *olah* back when he was still poor and originally set aside the money. The subdivision was established at the original moment of sanctification. This also resolves the earlier question about timing: just as one may designate which bird is the *chattat* and which is the *olah* at the moment of purchase, so too may one designate which portion of money serves which purpose at the moment of original sanctification.

### Post-Facto Option

Rav Chaga in the name of Rav Oshaya, however, holds a different position: *bedi'eved*, a rich person who brought a bird offering is *yotzei*. If that is so, then even after becoming rich the person is still in principle within the purview of the bird *korban*, and a declaration of which portion serves as *chattat* and which as *olah* would have legal force. According to this view, the *braita* can indeed be read literally—the person designated after becoming rich—because even a rich person retains a residual connection to the bird *korban bedi'eved*. The *gemara* suggests reading the *braita* accordingly: when it says the person "declared" which is which, it may mean the person *purchased* the birds at that point (the moment of *lekichah*), which is one of the two valid moments of designation identified by Rav Chisda.

## Scale Movement Post-Facto

### Metzora Scale

The *gemara* now turns to the question of scale movement in the *olah veyored* after the fact, focusing on the *metzora* as a test case. A *braita* states: a poor *metzora* who brought the richer offering—the more expensive *korban*—is *yotzei*. But a rich *metzora* who brought the poorer offering is not *yotzei*. This establishes that for a *metzora*, going up on the scale is valid even *bedi'eved*, but going down is not.

### Zos vs. Toras

The textual basis for this ruling lies in the language the Torah uses by the *metzora*. The word *zos*—"this is"—implies exactitude: the law is precisely as stated, neither more lenient nor more stringent. By itself, *zos* would prohibit both going up and going down. However, the Torah also uses the word *toras*—"the teaching of"—which is inclusive and broadens the category. The *braita* derives from *toras* that a poor person who brings a richer offering is *yotzei*, but derives from *zos* that a rich person who brings a poorer offering is not.

### Metzora Unique

One might ask: why not derive from the *metzora* case that going down on the scale is forbidden for all *olah veyored* situations? The *gemara* explains that the limitation on going down is unique to the *metzora*, because the Torah uses a limiting word there—*hu*, "he," applied to the poor person—which confines the leniency of the lower-tier offering specifically to a poor person. By contrast, by other *olah veyored* cases (such as *tum'at mikdash*), no such limiting language appears, leaving open the debate whether going down *bedi'eved* is valid.

### Up Down Summary

The conclusion of this discussion is that going up the scale—a poorer person bringing a more expensive offering—is apparently universally valid *bedi'eved* across all *olah veyored* categories. Going down—a richer person bringing a cheaper offering—is explicitly forbidden for the *metzora* by the word *zos*, and is the subject of debate for other *olah veyored* cases. According to the view of Rav Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya, going down does not fulfill the obligation even *bedi'eved*; according to Rav Chaga in the name of Rav Oshaya, going down is valid *bedi'eved* in cases other than *metzora*.

## Hierarchies & Rankings

### Sheep vs. Goat

The final *mishnah* of the tractate is taught by Rabbi Shimon. He observes that everywhere in the Torah, sheep (*kevasim*) are mentioned before goats (*izim*). One might therefore conclude that sheep are superior and preferable for *korbanot*. Rabbi Shimon shows that this cannot be the correct reading: in one place the Torah says *ve-im keves yavi korbano le-chatat*—"and if he brings a sheep as his sin offering"—reversing the usual order and mentioning a sheep in a context where a goat had just been discussed. This reversal teaches that the two are entirely equal—the standard ordering is a stylistic convention, not an expression of hierarchy.

### Other Reversals

Rabbi Shimon applies the same reasoning to two other standard pairings. The Torah consistently mentions *torim* (turtledoves) before *bnei yonah* (pigeons), but in one place reverses the order—*ven yonah o tor le-chatat*—teaching that turtledoves and pigeons are equal. Similarly, the Torah consistently mentions the father before the mother, as in *kabed et avicha ve-et imecha*, but in another verse reverses it: *ish imo ve-aviv tira'u*—teaching that both parents are equal in the obligations of honor and reverence.

### Father Precedence

Despite the equivalence established by Rabbi Shimon, the Sages add a practical qualification: in all places, the father takes precedence over the mother. The reasoning given is not that the father is categorically superior, but that the son's mother herself is obligated in honoring her husband. When there is a conflict and a choice must be made, the father takes precedence because the mother too stands in a position of deference to him. This applies, it should be noted, specifically within the context of a marriage; if the parents are divorced or otherwise not in that relationship, no such hierarchy between them would apply.

### Rav Priority

The *mishnah* adds a parallel ruling regarding the primacy of a *rav* over a father in the context of Torah study. If a son has merited to study Torah before a *rav*, that *rav* takes precedence over the father in matters of honor. The *mishnah*'s stated reason is that both the son *and the father* are obligated in honoring the *rav*—meaning the *rav*'s claim on honor supersedes even the father's claim, since the father himself owes honor to the *rav*. Elsewhere in the *gemara* (developed more fully in *Kiddushin*) a deeper reason is given—the father brings a child into this world while the *rav* brings a student into the life of the World to Come—but here the *mishnah* grounds the ruling in the fact that the father himself participates in the obligation of *kavod* toward the *rav*.

## Temple Cryings

### First & Second

The tractate concludes with an *aggadic* passage. The *gemara* records that the Temple courtyard cried out four times—personifying the *azarah* as expressing either revulsion or welcome. The first crying was an expulsion: "Remove from here the sons of Eli, Hofni and Phinehas, who defiled the *Heichal*." Their misdeeds are well known from *Tanakh*. The second was a welcome: "Open the gates and let Yochanan ben Nidbai, student of Pinkai, enter and fill his belly from the sacrifices of heaven." Far from being a compliment to his character, this is a tribute to his utility: he was said to eat four *se'ah* of birds as a dessert course, and as long as he was present, there was never meat left over in the *azarah*—the prohibition of *notar* was never violated on his watch.

### Third & Fourth

The third crying was another welcome: "Lift your heads, O gates, and let Elishama ben Phikai, student of Pinchas, enter and serve in the *kehuna gedolah*." The fourth was an expulsion: "Open the gates and remove Issachar ish Kfar Barkai, who honors himself while dishonoring the sacrifices of Heaven."

### Silk Gloves Sin

What was Issachar's offense? He would wrap his hands in silk before performing the *avodah*—he wore what amounted to silk gloves in order to protect his hands. The concern was clearly for his own dignity and comfort rather than for the integrity of the service. This not only expressed contempt for the *korbanot* but also technically invalidated the *avodah*, since contact through an intervening material is *pasul*.

### Hands Cut Off

The story of Issachar's end is told with stark irony. King Yannai and his queen were debating whether goats or sheep are superior meat. They sent the question to Issachar ish Kfar Barkai—he was always at the *korbanot*, they said, so he would know. Issachar replied: if goats were superior, they would have been brought as the *korban tamid*; clearly since sheep are brought daily, sheep are better. Not only did this side him with the queen against the king, but as he made his argument, he accompanied it with a dismissive wave of his hand toward the king. The king ordered that his right hand be cut off—the very hand he had wrapped in silk to protect it. Issachar bribed the executioner, who cut off only his left hand. When the king discovered this, he ordered the right hand cut off as well. Issachar lost both hands. Rav Yosef declares: blessed is God who brought Issachar ish Kfar Barkai his *metafsarei*—his comeuppance.

### Ignorance Added

Rav Ashi adds a final dimension to Issachar's disgrace. The man apparently never even learned the *mishnayot*—specifically the very *mishnah* taught at the end of this tractate. Had he learned it, he would have known that sheep and goats are declared equal by the Torah itself. He could have answered the king's question without siding with anyone: both are the same. Instead, his ignorance compounded his arrogance. Ravina adds: he did not even know the basic *pesukim*, since the Torah's phrasing—*im keves im ez*, "if a sheep, if a goat"—in perfectly parallel construction already signals their equivalence to anyone who has read *Chumash*. The dismissive gesture with the silken hands was the visible expression of a deeper contempt rooted in ignorance.

## Peace Through Torah

### Talmidei Chacham

The tractate ends, as is fitting, on a note of reconciliation. After a story about arrogance, contempt, and violence, the *gemara* closes with Rabbi Elazar quoting Rabbi Chanina: *talmidei chachamim marbim shalom ba-olam*—students of the wise increase peace in the world—as it says, *ve-chol banayich limudei Hashem ve-rav shalom banayich*, "and all your children shall be students of God, and great will be the peace of your children," on which the *drashah* is applied: *al tikrei banayich ela bonayich*—do not read "your children" but "your builders." The connection to what precedes it is two-fold: it balances the destructive tendencies on display in the story of Issachar, and it ties into the final *mishnah*'s teaching about the precedence of the *rav* and the centrality of *talmud torah*, suggesting that genuine Torah learning—far from producing the arrogance of Issachar ish Kfar Barkai—is the source of *shalom*.

*Hadran alach masechet Keritot ve-silka lach masechet Keritot.*
