# Gittin 71 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Testing Non-Speakers

### False Nodding Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִשְׁתַּתֵּק, וְאָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״נִכְתּוֹב גֵּט לְאִשְׁתְּךָ?״ וְהִרְכִּין בְּרֹאשׁוֹ, בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה פְּעָמִים, אִם אָמַר עַל לָאו – ״לָאו״, וְעַל הֵן – ״הֵן״, הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ יִכְתְּבוּ וְיִתְּנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where the husband became mute, and the members of the court said to him: Shall we write a bill of divorce for your wife, and he nodded his head indicating his agreement, they examine him with various questions three times. If he responded to questions that have a negative answer: No, and responded to questions that have a positive answer: Yes, indicating his competence, they shall write the bill of divorce and give it to his wife based on the nod of his head.

The daf opens by continuing a discussion from the preceding amud concerning a husband who has lost the ability to speak — *nishtatek* — and whether those around him may write a *get* on his behalf based on his nodding responses. The *mishna* had established that one may pose yes-or-no questions and, if he responds correctly and coherently, his nodded instructions may be followed. But the *gemara* immediately raises a difficulty: *v'lechosh d'lamma shita d'lav lav naktay* — perhaps the man has been seized by a fit of head-shaking and is simply nodding or shaking reflexively. If all the questions put to him happen to align with his involuntary movement, his responses prove nothing.

### Alternating Questions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְעוֹלָם לֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא שִׁיחְיָא דְּ״לָאו לָאו״ נַקְטֵיהּ, אִי נָמֵי שִׁיחְיָא דְּ״הֵן הֵן״ נַקְטֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** But let there be a concern that perhaps the involuntary movement of: No, no, took hold of him, and he continuously nods his head as if he were signaling no, even though he does not intend it? Alternatively, perhaps the involuntary movement of: Yes, yes, took hold of him?
>
> אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: דְּאָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ בְּסֵירוּגִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef bar Minyumi says that Rav Naḥman says: The meaning of the mishna is that we, the court, say alternating questions to him, switching between asking him questions to which he must answer yes and questions to which he must answer no.
>
> וְלֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא שִׁיחְיָא דְּסֵירוּגִין נַקְטֵיהּ! דְּאָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ חַד ״לָאו״ וּתְרֵין ״הֵן״, וּתְרֵין ״לָאו״ וְחַד ״הֵן״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But let there be a concern that perhaps alternating involuntary movements took hold of him, such that he switches between nodding his head yes and nodding his head no. The Gemara answers: The meaning of the mishna is that we, the court, say to him questions to which he must answer no once and yes twice, and twice no and one time yes. If he nevertheless answers each question correctly there is no concern that his movements were involuntary.

Rav Papa's solution is to ask the questions *b'sirugin* — alternating between questions that should elicit a "yes" and questions that should elicit a "no." Rashi understands this to mean waiting an interval between each question; the approach suggested here, however, is that the questions themselves alternate in the type of answer they require, so that a man simply locked into a repetitive motion would immediately be exposed. But the *gemara* presses further: perhaps he is seized by a fit of *alternating* — first nodding, then shaking, then nodding again. The answer is that one should vary the pattern unpredictably: *chad lav u'trei hen, u'trei lav u'chad hen* — one question requiring "no," then two requiring "yes," then two requiring "no," then one requiring "yes," in no fixed sequence, so that no mechanical repetition could accidentally produce the correct results throughout.

### Fruit Test Questions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנָא: אוֹמְרִים לוֹ דְּבָרִים שֶׁל יְמוֹת הַחַמָּה בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים, וְשֶׁל יְמוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: The court says to him questions concerning matters of the summer in the rainy season, and concerning matters of the rainy season in the summer, in order to check if he is answering coherently.
>
> מַאי נִיהוּ? אִילֵּימָא גְּלוּפְקְרֵי וּסְדִינֵי – לֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא קוֹרָא אַחְדֵּיהּ, אִי נָמֵי חַמָּה אַחְדֵּיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: What are these matters that the court asks him? If we say that the court asks him if he wants a warm coat in the summer or a thin sheet in the rainy season, and he answers that he does, this cannot serve as proof of his intellectual capabilities, as the following is possible: Let there be a concern that perhaps he has the chills even though it is the summer and needs a warm coat. Alternatively, that the heat afflicted him during the rainy season and he requires a thin sheet, in which case his response does not demonstrate a lack of comprehension.
>
> אֶלָּא בְּפֵירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, it means that the court asks him questions with regard to produce that is not found during that season.

A *baraita* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael adds a further method: *amrinan leih devarim shel yemos hachama b'yemos hageshamim v'shel yemos hageshamim b'yemos hachama* — one asks him about things characteristic of summer during the winter, and about things characteristic of winter during the summer. The *gemara* questions what precisely this means. If you ask whether he wants a winter coat or a summer coat, that proves nothing, since a person might be cold in summer or warm in winter. Rather, says the *gemara*, the test is conducted through fruit — *b'peiri* — asking about seasonal produce that is simply unavailable out of season. Tosafot notes that some fruits can be preserved, and therefore suggests the question be posed in terms of going out to the orchards to pick fruit, which clearly cannot be done in the wrong season.

## Deaf-Mute Writing

### Writing = Speech

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר רַב: חֵרֵשׁ שֶׁיָּכוֹל לְדַבֵּר מִתּוֹךְ הַכְּתָב, כּוֹתְבִין וְנוֹתְנִין גֵּט לְאִשְׁתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Kahana says that Rav says: With regard to a deaf-mute who can express himself through writing, the judges of the court may write and give a bill of divorce to his wife based on his written instructions.

A statement of Rav Kahana in the name of Rav now introduces one of the most important and far-reaching *chiddushim* on the daf: *cheresh she'yachol l'daber mitoch haksav* — if a person who is deaf can "speak" through writing, then *kosvin venosin get le'ishto* — a *get* may be written and given to his wife.

To appreciate the full weight of this ruling, one must recall what the term *cheresh* means in the *gemara*. It refers not simply to a person who cannot hear, but specifically to the deaf-mute — someone who is both deaf and unable to speak intelligibly. The *gemara* operated under the presumption that most people born deaf never acquired intelligible speech, and were therefore classified as lacking *bar da'as* status. This is a categorical *halakhic* designation: such a person is considered to lack the intellectual competence required for binding legal transactions at the *d'oraisa* level. He may marry and be married *d'rabbanan*, but a marriage contracted before he became a *cheresh* — when he was a *pikeach*, a person of full faculties — is a full *d'oraisa* marriage, which would ordinarily require a valid *get* for dissolution.

Rav Kahana's ruling addresses precisely this case. If such a person can communicate through writing, he is no longer halakhically a *cheresh* at all. One might understand this in one of two ways. The formal reading is that writing constitutes an act of speech — *yachol l'daber mitoch haksav*, he can "speak" through writing — and therefore he is definitionally not mute. The functional reading is that the designation *cheresh* was always a proxy for lacking *da'as*, and that writing demonstrates unambiguously that he possesses *da'as*, rendering the category inapplicable. As will become clear, the distinction between these two framings is not merely theoretical.

### Rav Yosef Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? תְּנֵינָא: נִשְׁתַּתֵּק, וְאָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״נִכְתּוֹב גֵּט לְאִשְׁתְּךָ?״ וְהִרְכִּין בְּרֹאשׁוֹ, בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה פְּעָמִים, אִם אָמַר עַל לָאו – ״לָאו״, וְעַל הֵן – ״הֵן״, הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ יִכְתְּבוּ וְיִתְּנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef said: What is he teaching us? We already learned in the mishna: In a case where the husband became mute, and the members of the court said to him: Shall we write a bill of divorce for your wife, and he nodded his head indicating his agreement, they examine him with various questions three times. If he responded to questions that have a negative answer: No, and responded to questions that have a positive answer: Yes, indicating his competence, they shall write the bill of divorce and give it to his wife based on the nod of his head.

Rav Yosef challenges the novelty of this ruling: *tanina* — we already know this from a *braita* concerning a man who became silent (*nishtatek*), was asked whether a *get* should be written, nodded, was tested three times, and answered yes-and-no questions correctly, whereupon it was ruled that a *get* may be written. Is it not obvious that non-verbal communication suffices?

### Ilem vs. Cheresh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא: אִלֵּם קָאָמְרַתְּ? שָׁאנֵי אִלֵּם – דְּתַנְיָא: מְדַבֵּר וְאֵינוֹ שׁוֹמֵעַ – זֶהוּ חֵרֵשׁ, שׁוֹמֵעַ וְאֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר – זֶהוּ אִלֵּם, וְזֶה וָזֶה הֲרֵי הֵן כְּפִקְחִין לְכׇל דִּבְרֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira said to him: A mute, you say? Is your challenge based on the mishna which discusses one who is mute? A mute is different because it is clear that his mind is intact. As it is taught in a *baraita*: With regard to one who speaks but cannot hear, this individual is categorized as a deaf person. With regard to one who hears but cannot speak, this individual is categorized as a mute person, and both this one and that one are considered to be like halakhically competent people with regard to all their matters. Rav Kahana stated his ruling with regard to one who can neither hear nor speak. This goes beyond the *halakha* of the mishna, as Rav Kahana says that the written statement of a deaf-mute is an indication of his mental competence.
>
> וּמִמַּאי דִּמְדַבֵּר וְאֵינוֹ שׁוֹמֵעַ זֶהוּ חֵרֵשׁ, שׁוֹמֵעַ וְאֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר זֶהוּ אִלֵּם? דִּכְתִיב: ״וַאֲנִי כְחֵרֵשׁ לֹא אֶשְׁמָע וּכְאִלֵּם לֹא יִפְתַּח פִּיו״. וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא, כִּדְאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: אִישְׁתְּקִיל מִילּוּלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: And from where do we learn that one who speaks but cannot hear, this individual is categorized as a deaf person, and that with regard to one who hears but cannot speak, this individual is categorized as a mute person? As it is written: "And I am like a deaf man, I do not hear, and like the mute man who will not open his mouth" (Psalms 38:14). And if you wish, say as people say: The word for a mute [*ilem*] is a contraction of the expression his speech has been taken [*ishtakil millulei*].

Rav Zeira responds that the *braita* is no precedent at all, because it speaks of an *ilem* — a person who is mute but can hear — and that case is entirely different. As a *braita* establishes: *medaber ve'eino shomea zehu cheresh* — one who can speak but not hear is a *cheresh*; *shomea ve'eino medaber zehu ilem* — one who can hear but not speak is an *ilem*. And *ve'ze ve'ze harei hen kepikechin lekhol divreihem* — both the person who is only deaf and the person who is only mute are treated as fully competent, *kepikechin*, in all respects. An *ilem* is unquestionably a *bar da'as*; the only question regarding him is whether his nodding constitutes a valid instruction. The *gemara* notes a scriptural support for the terminology from the verse *va'ani kacheresh lo eshma, ukhe'ilem lo yiftach piv* — "I was like a deaf person who did not hear, and like a mute person who did not open his mouth" — showing that the deaf person and the mute are designated by distinct terms in *Tanakh* as well.

The *chiddush* of Rav Kahana amar Rav, therefore, is not about a *bar da'as* who happens to be unable to speak. It concerns someone the *halakha* would categorize as an *eino bar da'as* — a *cheresh* — who ordinarily cannot engage in binding *halakhic* transactions. The ruling is that if this person can write, that recategorizes him entirely. That is a genuinely revolutionary position.

### Writing Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: אִי קַשְׁיָא לִי – הָא קַשְׁיָא לִי, דְּתַנְיָא: ״אִם לֹא יַגִּיד״ – פְּרָט לְאִלֵּם, שֶׁאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְהַגִּיד. אַמַּאי? הָא יָכוֹל לְהַגִּיד מִתּוֹךְ הַכְּתָב!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira said: If Rav Kahana's statement is difficult for me, this is difficult for me, as it is taught in a *baraita* with regard to the punishment of one who was called to testify and failed to do so: "If he does not utter his testimony then he shall bear his iniquity" (Leviticus 5:1). The Sages derive from this that the verse serves to exclude a mute who is unable to utter his testimony, and he is exempt from bringing the offering of one who refuses to testify. And it is possible to raise the following question: Why is he exempt from bringing the offering; but he is able to utter his testimony through writing? Apparently, written testimony is not valid testimony.
>
> אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: עֵדוּת קָאָמְרַתְּ? שָׁאנֵי עֵדוּת, דְּרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר: ״מִפִּיהֶם״ – וְלֹא מִפִּי כְתָבָם.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye said to him: You say testimony? Testimony is different, as the Merciful One states: "From their mouths" (Deuteronomy 17:6), which emphasizes that testimony must come from the witnesses' mouths and not from their writing.

Rav Zeira raises a further question from a different angle. The verse in *Vayikra* states *im lo yagid venasa avono* — if a person who was adjured to testify fails to come forward, he bears his sin and must bring a *korban shvua*. The verse adds *perat le'ilem she'eino yachol lehagid* — excluding a mute person who cannot speak. But according to Rav Kahana, writing is a form of speaking — *yachol lehagid mitoch haksav* — so why should a mute person who can write be excluded from the laws of testimony and the *korban shvua*?

Abaye responds: *shani eidus derachmana amar mipihem velo mipi ksavam* — testimony is different, because the Torah specifically requires *al pi shenayim eidim*, "by the mouth of two witnesses," and the *gemara* derives from this that testimony must come from their mouths and not from their writing. Even if writing constitutes a form of speech for other *halakhic* purposes, the specific demand of *mipihem* — from their mouths — requires actual verbal articulation. One might further note that this principle also underlies the requirement that a *beis din* know the seventy languages — *shivim lashon* — since testimony cannot pass through an interpreter, for *mipihem velo mipi hameturgeman*.

This exchange actually clarifies the formal nature of Rav Kahana's position. The *gemara* is not merely saying that writing is a reliable indicator of *da'as*; it is making the stronger claim that writing counts as speech *halakhically*. This is why the objection from testimony is worth raising at all — if writing were merely a *de facto* test of competence, there would be nothing to ask. But because writing is being declared a formal act of speech, Rav Zeira can legitimately ask whether it should satisfy *lehagid*, and Abaye must answer that the particular requirement of *mipihem* is more exacting than the general category of speech.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁבּוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ לְגִיטִּין, כָּךְ בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ לְמַשָּׂאוֹת, וּלְמַתָּנוֹת, וּלְעֵדִיּוֹת, וְלִירוּשּׁוֹת. קָתָנֵי מִיהַת – ״עֵדִיּוֹת״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection to this based on that which is taught in a *baraita*: Just as the judges of the court investigate him, one who lost his ability to speak, with regard to bills of divorce, so too, the judges investigate him with regard to business transactions, testimonies, and inheritances. In any event it teaches: Testimonies, which proves that even one unable to speak can give testimony.
>
> אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: בְּעֵדוּת אִשָּׁה, דַּאֲקִילּוּ בַּהּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef bar Minyumi said that Rav Sheshet said: This is not referring to testimony in general. Rather, it is referring to testimony with regard to a woman whose husband died, as the Sages were lenient with her and allowed testimony that would normally not be accepted, in order to permit her to remarry.
>
> וְהָא קָתָנֵי: ״יְרוּשּׁוֹת״! אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: יְרוּשַּׁת בְּנוֹ הַבְּכוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: But isn't it also taught: Inheritances. This seems to be referring to monetary matters, where proper testimony is required. Rabbi Abbahu said: The *baraita* is referring to the inheritance of his own firstborn son, meaning that he is not testifying about other people's property but testifying which one of his sons is the firstborn. Since this testimony is merely considered to be a division of the property that belongs to him, the testimony of one who is unable to speak is valid.
>
> קָתָנֵי מִיהַת – ״לְמַשָּׂאוֹת וּלְמַתָּנוֹת״, מַאי, לָאו לְעָלְמָא? לָא, לְדִידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to challenge: In any event it teaches in the *baraita*: With regard to business transactions, what, does it not mean that a mute may testify with regard to business transactions for everyone else? The Gemara answers: No, he may testify for himself only. The *baraita* is saying that when a mute conducts business the court must first check if he is mentally capable.

A subsequent *braita* cited in the *gemara* states: *keshem shebodkin oso l'gittin, kach bodkin oso l'massa u'mattanot, ul'eduyos, ul'yerushos* — the same testing procedure used for *gittin* applies to business transactions, testimony, and bequests. This seems to reintroduce the problem, since *eduyos* is listed. The *gemara* resolves: Rav Yosef bar Minyumi in the name of Rav Sheshet explains that the mention of testimony refers to *edus isha* — testimony concerning whether a woman's husband has died — where the rabbis were lenient and did not require the full standards of formal testimony. As for bequests (*yerushos*), Rabbi Abahu explains that it refers to a case where the person is declaring which of his sons is the firstborn — again not a case of formal *eidus*. And *massa u'mattanot*, business transactions, refers to his own transactions, not to his testifying about someone else's. So the principle that formal testimony requires verbal speech remains intact.

## Braita Contradiction

### Braita Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: חֵרֵשׁ, לֹא הָלְכוּ בּוֹ אַחַר רְמִיזוֹתָיו וְאַחַר קְפִיצוֹתָיו וְאַחַר כְּתַב יָדוֹ, אֶלָּא בְּמִטַּלְטְלִין; אֲבָל לֹא לְגִיטִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection from a *baraita*: With regard to a deaf-mute, the judges of the court follow his signals, and follow the movement of his lips, and follow his handwriting only for matters of buying and selling movable property but not for bills of divorce. This appears to contradict Rav Kahana's statement that it is permitted for a deaf-mute husband to give written instructions to divorce his wife.

The *gemara* now introduces a *braita* that directly challenges Rav Kahana's position. The *braita* states that when it comes to a *cheresh*, the *chachamim* did not follow his signaling, his jumping, or his writing — *v'halchu bo achar remizosav v'achar k'fitzosav v'achar k'sav yado* — except for *mitaltelin*, transactions involving movable property, where a lower standard of *da'as* has always been accepted. But *aval lo l'gittin* — not for *gittin*. This directly contradicts Rav Kahana's ruling that a *cheresh* who can write may divorce his wife through that writing.

One might ask at this point: does this difficulty not arise only regarding a marriage that is *d'oraisa*? After all, the principle *keshem shekones bi'remiza kach motzi bi'remiza* — just as he married through signaling, so too he may divorce through signaling — seems to already allow a *cheresh* who married rabbinically to be divorced in the same mode. The *gemara* will confirm that this principle applies only to a woman he married as a *cheresh*, where the original marriage was *d'rabbanan*. The hard case is a woman he married before becoming a *cheresh*, or — as will emerge — a *yevama* who fell to him from a *pikeach* brother, where the relationship carries *d'oraisa* force.

### Tannaitic Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא – דְּתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּחֵרֵשׁ מֵעִיקָּרוֹ, אֲבָל פִּיקֵּחַ וְנִתְחָרֵשׁ – הוּא כּוֹתֵב וְהֵן חוֹתְמִין.
>
> **Translation:** It is a dispute between *tanna'im*, as it is taught in a *baraita* that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said: In what case is this statement that the court may not rely on the written testimony of a deaf-mute with regard to a bill of divorce said? Only in the case of a deaf-mute who was deaf from the outset, i.e., from birth. But if he had been halakhically competent, i.e., he could previously hear, but became a deaf-mute later, then he may write instructions to give his wife a bill of divorce and they, the witnesses, should sign, in accordance with the opinion of Rav Kahana.

The *gemara* resolves the contradiction: *tanna'ei hi* — it is a dispute between *tannaim*. The *braita* that denies writing any power to redefine a *cheresh* represents the position of the *chachamim*, while Rav Kahana's ruling follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.

### Rabban Shimon Caveat

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel thus draws a distinction: a person who began life with full faculties and then lost them may be recategorized if he can communicate through writing. A person who was always a *cheresh* cannot be recategorized no matter how clearly he writes. The *chachamim* disagree and make no such distinction — for them, writing helps in neither case.

This also raises the question of how a *cheresh* who was *always* a *cheresh* could ever be in a situation where he needs a *get* from a *d'oraisa* marriage. If he was always a *cheresh*, any marriage he contracted himself was only *d'rabbanan*, and he could dissolve it the same way it was contracted. The answer is *yibbum* — levirate marriage. If his brother, a *pikeach*, died, leaving a wife, and this *cheresh* then had relations with her, the act of *yibbum* itself — which does not require *da'as* — would create a *d'oraisa* bond between them. Now he has a *d'oraisa* marriage he cannot dissolve through writing, according to the *chachamim*.

The implications for the *halachic* history of deaf people are significant. This is one of the most consequential *sugyot* on the matter, and the *gemara* — through this *braita* — has pushed back hard against Rav Kahana's liberating position. This deserves to be noted. The concern for *agunos* that Rav Kahana's ruling might have alleviated remains in tension with the outcome of the *sugya*.

## Yibum Case

### Rabbinic Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחֵרֵשׁ מֵעִיקָּרוֹ – לֹא?! כְּשֵׁם שֶׁכּוֹנְסָהּ בִּרְמִיזָה, כָּךְ מוֹצִיאָהּ בִּרְמִיזָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: And one who is a deaf-mute from the outset cannot give written instructions with regard to a bill of divorce? Isn't it true that just as he marries her with intimation, i.e., without speaking, so too, he divorces her with intimation.

The *gemara* now addresses the question head-on. If a *cheresh* was always a *cheresh*, then *keshem shekone'sa beremiza kach motzi'a beremiza* — the same way he married through signaling, he can divorce through signaling. His marriage was only *d'rabbanan*, so his divorce need only satisfy a *d'rabbanan* standard as well. This is not in dispute.

### Yibum Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי בְּאִשְׁתּוֹ, הָכִי נָמֵי; הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בִּיבִמְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: If the *baraita* is referring to his wife, indeed this would be the case and he could divorce her through intimations, because such a marriage is not a fully valid marriage by Torah law. But with what are we dealing here? With his *yevama*, his sister-in-law whose husband, his brother, died childless to whom he performed levirate marriage, and whom he subsequently wishes to divorce. This marriage is a fully valid marriage.

The problematic case is *yevamto* — his *yevama*, his sister-in-law who falls to him for *yibbum*. If his brother was a *pikeach* and was *d'oraisa* married to a woman, and that brother died, then this *cheresh* has a *d'oraisa* obligation of *yibbum*. The act of *yibbum* is effected through sexual relations, and that act does not require *da'as* to be binding at the *d'oraisa* level. Once the act occurs, she is his wife *d'oraisa*, and he cannot divorce her — not through signaling and not through writing, according to those who reject Rav Kahana.

### Wife vs. Sister

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְבִמְתּוֹ מִמַּאן? אִילֵימָא דִּנְפַלָה לֵיהּ מֵאָחִיו חֵרֵשׁ – כְּשֵׁם שֶׁכְּנִיסָתָהּ בִּרְמִיזָה, כָּךְ יְצִיאָתָהּ בִּרְמִיזָה! אֶלָּא דִּנְפַלָה לֵיהּ מֵאָחִיו פִּיקֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: His *yevama* from whom? If we say that she fell to him from his brother who was also a deaf-mute, then just as her marriage to the brother was through intimation, so too, her divorce from the *yavam* can be through intimation. Rather, it must be that she fell to him from his halakhically competent brother. Consequently, the bond of the levirate marriage is by Torah law, whereas the deaf-mute man's intimations that he desires to divorce her are valid only by rabbinic law.
>
> וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם דִּנְפַלָה לֵיהּ מֵאָחִיו חֵרֵשׁ, וּגְזֵירָה אָחִיו חֵרֵשׁ אַטּוּ אָחִיו פִּיקֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** And if you wish, say instead: Actually, explain that she fell to him from his deaf-mute brother, and the Sages instituted a rabbinic decree with regard to a woman bound in a levirate marriage from his deaf-mute brother due to the death of his halakhically competent brother.

The *gemara* then asks: if his brother was himself a *cheresh* and had only a *d'rabbanan* marriage to this woman, then the entire framework remains rabbinic — her marriage to the brother was *d'rabbanan*, and consequently her status as a *yevama* is also only *d'rabbanan*. In that case, *keshem shek'nisata b'remiza kach yetziata b'remiza* should apply even to the *yibbum* scenario.

The *gemara* offers an alternative answer: *gzeira achiv chereish atu achiv pikeach* — we make a decree that even the case where the brother was a *cheresh* is treated like the case where the brother was a *pikeach*, because the two situations — a *yevama* from a *cheresh* brother and a *yevama* from a *pikeach* brother — might be confused with one another.

### Two Deaf Siblings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אִשְׁתּוֹ נָמֵי! יְבִמְתּוֹ בִּיבִמְתּוֹ מִיחַלְּפָא, אִשְׁתּוֹ בִּיבִמְתּוֹ לָא מִיחַלְּפָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: If so, and the Sages made such a decree with regard to a case where a deaf-mute brother died due to the case where a brother who is halakhically competent died, then with regard to his own wife as well, they should have made a decree that a deaf-mute husband cannot divorce her with a bill of divorce if he married her as a deaf-mute. The Gemara answers: His *yevama* who was married to his halakhically competent brother might be confused with his *yevama* who was married to his deaf-mute brother. But people would not confuse his own wife with his *yevama*. Therefore, there was no need to institute such a decree.
>
> וּמִי גָּזְרִינַן חֵרֵשׁ אַטּוּ פִּיקֵּחַ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: And do we, the Sages, decree with regard to his deaf-mute brother due to confusion between him and his halakhically competent brother?
>
> וְהָתְנַן: שְׁנֵי אַחִין חֵרְשִׁין – נְשׂוּאִים שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת פִּיקְּחוֹת, אוֹ שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת חֵרְשׁוֹת, אוֹ שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת אַחַת פִּיקַּחַת וְאַחַת חֵרֶשֶׁת; וְכֵן שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת חֵרְשׁוֹת – נְשׂוּאוֹת לִשְׁנֵי אַחִים פִּיקְּחִים, אוֹ לִשְׁנֵי אַחִים חֵרְשִׁין, אוֹ לִשְׁנֵי אַחִים אֶחָד פִּיקֵּחַ וְאֶחָד חֵרֵשׁ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ פְּטוּרוֹת מִן הַחֲלִיצָה וּמִן הַיִּבּוּם.
>
> **Translation:** But didn't we learn in a mishna: In a case where there were two deaf-mute brothers, whose marriage is valid by rabbinic law, married to two halakhically competent sisters, or to two deaf-mute sisters, or to two sisters, one of whom was halakhically competent and one of whom was a deaf-mute; and similarly, if there were two deaf-mute sisters, whose marriage is valid by rabbinic law, married to two halakhically competent brothers, or to two deaf-mute brothers, or to two brothers, one of whom was halakhically competent and one of whom was a deaf-mute; and in each case one brother dies without children, then all these women are exempt from *ḥalitza* and from levirate marriage. Each sister is exempt, as she is the sister of the wife of the *yavam*.
>
> וְאִם הָיוּ נׇכְרִיּוֹת, יִכָּנְסוּ; וְאִם רָצוּ לְהוֹצִיאָן – יוֹצִיאוּ.
>
> **Translation:** And if they were unrelated women the men may marry them in levirate marriage, and if they wanted to divorce them later via a bill of divorce they may divorce them. Since this is stated as a general *halakha* it indicates that the deaf-mute brother can also give a bill of divorce to his *yevama* who was previously married to his deaf-mute brother, and the Sages did not institute a decree in order not to create confusion with the similar case of a deceased halakhically competent brother.
>
> אֶלָּא מְחַוַּורְתָּא כִּדְשַׁנִּין מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, it is clear as we initially answered, that it is referring to a *yevama*, previously married to a halakhically competent brother, who fell before a *yavam* who was a deaf-mute from birth. The alternative answer given on the previous *amud* is rejected.

The *gemara* now challenges the *gzeira* from a different direction. A *mishna* discusses the case of two brothers who are both *charashim*, married to two sisters who may be *pikchot*, *cherashot*, or one of each. In all these combinations, the marriages are *d'rabbanan*, and consequently the *gemara* rules that *harei elu peturot min hachalitza umin hayibbum* — they are entirely exempt from both *yibbum* and *chalitza*, because we treat the situation as analogous to a case of *arayos* in a full *d'oraisa* framework: just as two brothers married to two sisters cannot do *yibbum* when everything is *d'oraisa*, so too when everything is operating on the same *d'rabbanan* plane, we apply the same logic. Conversely, *v'im hayu nochriyot yichnesu* — if the two women are unrelated, *yibbum* is performed, and since the *yibbum* itself is only *d'rabbanan*, *v'im ratzu lehotzi yotzi'u* — the husband may afterward divorce her with a *get*, since the marriage is only rabbinic.

What is significant about this *mishna* for the present argument is that it treats the *cheresh* brother's case in its own right, without any concern that it will be confused with the *pikeach* brother's case. This directly undermines the *gzeira* reasoning. The *gemara* acknowledges this: *umi gazrinan chereish atu pikeach?* — are we really going to decree that a *cheresh* case be treated like a *pikeach* case? The *mishna* plainly does not do so.

The *gemara* therefore concludes: *ela michvarta kideshanin me'ikara* — the better answer is the original one. The case where the *cheresh* who was always a *cheresh* cannot divorce his *yevama* is specifically the case where his brother was a *pikeach*, making the *yibbum* a genuine *d'oraisa* bond. Where the brother was also a *cheresh*, there is no need for a *gzeira* because the two cases are not confused with each other, and divorce remains available.

## Recategorization Debate

### Rejection Clarified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: חֲלוּקִין עָלָיו חֲבֵירָיו עַל רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan says: The colleagues of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagree with him, and hold that written instructions from a deaf-mute have no halakhic validity.

Rav Yochanan makes explicit what was already implied: *chalukin alav chaveirav d'Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — the *chachamim* disagree with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. This is not a case where Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's leniency can be taken as a clarification of the majority view. His position stands alone, and the majority reject it entirely.

### Can Never Divorce

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: נִישַּׁטֵּת, לֹא יוֹצִיא. נִתְחָרֵשׁ הוּא אוֹ נִשְׁתַּטָּה – לֹא יוֹצִיא עוֹלָמִית. מַאי ״עוֹלָמִית״? לָאו – אַף עַל גַּב דְּיָכוֹל לְדַבֵּר מִתּוֹךְ הַכְּתָב?
>
> **Translation:** Abaye said that we, too, learn in the mishna that non-verbal instructions are insufficient to initiate a divorce even for one who was born with the ability to hear and subsequently became a deaf-mute: If a woman became an imbecile after her wedding the Sages instituted that the husband must not divorce her. If the husband became a deaf-mute or an imbecile he can never divorce her, as he does not have the legal competence to grant a bill of divorce. Abaye explains: What is the reason that the mishna emphasizes that he can never divorce her? Isn't this teaching that even though he can communicate through writing, he is unable to divorce her?

Abaye adds that this rejection can be seen from the *mishna* itself: *nishtatek lo yotzi, nitcharesh... lo yotzi olamit* — if a man becomes silent he may not divorce his wife, and if he becomes a *cheresh* or *shoteh* he may *never* divorce her. The word *olamit* — forever — is doing important work here.

### Forever = Writing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אִי לָאו דְּאַשְׁמְעִינַן רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל לְפָרוֹשֵׁי טַעְמָא דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא הוּא דַּאֲתָא; וּמַאי ״עוֹלָמִית״? אַף עַל גַּב דַּחֲזֵינָא לֵיהּ דְּחָרִיף.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa said: If not for the fact that Rabbi Yoḥanan teaches us that there is a dispute between Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the Rabbis, then I would say that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel comes only to explain the reason of the first *tanna*, not to disagree with him. If that were so, all agree that one who was born with the ability to hear and subsequently became a deaf-mute can issue written instructions to write and give a bill of divorce for his wife. And according to this explanation, what is the meaning of the word never? It is not referring to issuing written instructions. It means that even though I see that he is sharp by means of examining his gestures, these indications are not sufficient to warrant the giving of a bill of divorce. Despite this, if he were to issue written instructions to divorce his wife, they would be followed.

Rav Papa explains what he would have said had Rav Yochanan not clarified the matter: he would have read *olamit* as meaning that no matter how intelligent the man appears — *af al gav d'chazina d'charif* — as long as he cannot communicate, he can never divorce. But once he can write, he would no longer be classified as a *cheresh* and could divorce. In other words, Rav Papa would have read the *mishna* as consistent with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel — "*never*" means never *as a cheresh*, but writing exits the category. Only Rav Yochanan's explicit statement that the *chachamim* disagree with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel forecloses this reading. According to the *chachamim*, *olamit* means that even if he can write, it does not change his status, and he can never divorce her.

### Shotah Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי, לְכִדְרַבִּי יִצְחָק – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה, שׁוֹטָה מִתְגָּרֶשֶׁת – מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַפִּיקַּחַת בְּעַל כׇּרְחָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Alternatively, the mishna emphasized that the only situation where the husband can never divorce his wife is where her husband became a deaf-mute or an imbecile, but not if the husband remained healthy and the wife became a deaf-mute or imbecile, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yitzḥak, as Rabbi Yitzḥak says: By Torah law a woman who is an imbecile may be divorced even though she is unable to give her consent, just as it is permitted to divorce a halakhically competent woman against her will.
>
> וּמָה טַעַם אָמְרוּ אֵינָהּ מִתְגָּרֶשֶׁת – שֶׁלֹּא יִנְהֲגוּ בָּהּ מִנְהַג הֶפְקֵר.
>
> **Translation:** And what is the reason that the Rabbis said she may not be divorced? So that she will not be treated as ownerless property. If she has no husband to protect her, and she is unable to protect herself, she may be treated as ownerless property by anyone who wishes to engage in sexual intercourse with her. By contrast, if the husband is a deaf-mute or an imbecile then he cannot divorce her by Torah law. This is why the mishna stresses only that the husband may never divorce his wife in the case where he becomes halakhically incompetent, but not when it is the wife who becomes a deaf-mute or an imbecile, in order to demonstrate the difference between the two cases in terms of Torah law.

An alternative reading of *olamit* is offered in the name of Rav Yitzchak: *d'var Torah shota mitgereshes* — biblically, a *shotah* — a woman who is not of sound mind — can in principle be divorced. The reasoning is *midi d'hava a'pikeches ba'al korcha* — just as a woman who is fully competent can be divorced against her will, the same applies to a *shotah*, since the *get* takes effect regardless of her mental state, provided she can retain custody of it (*shomeres es gitah*). Why then did the rabbis prohibit divorcing a *shotah*? *Shelo yinhagu ba minhag hefker* — so that people should not treat her as ownerless property, exploiting her vulnerability. This concern reflects a sober awareness of the dangers to which cognitively impaired individuals are exposed.

Rav Yitzchak's reading thus proposes that *olamit* was said specifically about the *man* to emphasize that when *he* becomes a *cheresh* or *shoteh*, the prohibition is *d'oraisa* — unlike the case where the *woman* becomes a *shotah*, where the prohibition is only *d'rabbanan*. The word "forever" signals the absolute, *d'oraisa* nature of his incapacity to grant a *get*, in contrast to her case, where the incapacity is only rabbinic.

## Modern Challenge

### Logical Problem

The *sugya* leaves us with a genuine *halakhic* challenge that has only grown more acute in modern times. The *gemara* — according to the *chachamim* who reject even Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel — holds that a *cheresh* who was born a *cheresh* cannot grant a *get* even if he can write clearly and intelligently. This is a striking conclusion. We are not dealing with a person whose competence is ambiguous. We are dealing with a person who is demonstrably communicating precisely in writing — and yet the *gemara* still classifies him as a *cheresh*, lacking *bar da'as* status at the *d'oraisa* level.

The logical tension is acute. How can a person who writes clearly, reasons clearly, and communicates precisely be described as lacking *da'as*? This is not merely a formal inconvenience — it has profound human consequences, affecting the person's ability to give a *get*, receive an *aliyah*, serve as a *shaliach tzibbur*, or perform any *mitzvah* at a *d'oraisa* level. The *gemara* seems to be applying a rigidly formal category rather than a genuinely cognitive one.

### Tech Solutions

One interesting technological consideration that arises in this context is text-to-speech software. If a person types text that is then vocalized by a device — as Stephen Hawking famously did — one might argue that the result is actual audible speech, not merely writing. This could potentially provide a basis for satisfying even those authorities who require actual *mipihem*, verbal articulation, rather than merely writing, at least for purposes other than formal *eidus* where the *d'oraisa* requirement of *mipihem* applies.

### Poskim Redefine

In practical *halakha*, this *sugya* stands at the center of a major debate among contemporary *poskim* regarding the status of deaf individuals in the modern era. The development of schools for the deaf — dating back roughly two and a half centuries — and above all the emergence of sign language as a fully structured linguistic system with its own grammar, has prompted extensive *halakhic* reassessment. Many prominent *poskim* have ruled that a *cheresh* who can communicate through sign language is no longer classified as a *cheresh* for *halakhic* purposes. This is both a logically compelling and a practically important ruling.

At the same time, intellectual honesty requires acknowledging the difficulty this *gemara* poses. The *gemara* specifically discusses a *cheresh* who communicates through *writing* — precise, articulate, unambiguous communication — and yet still classifies him as a *cheresh* at the *d'oraisa* level if he was born that way. Any contemporary ruling in the direction of recategorization must engage with this *gemara* directly, whether by distinguishing sign language from writing on formal grounds, or by arguing that changed social circumstances alter the underlying presumptions about cognitive development that originally grounded the category.

## Direct Instruction

### Direct Instruction Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״נִכְתּוֹב גֵּט לְאִשְׁתֶּךָ?״ וְאָמַר לָהֶן: ״כְּתוֹבוּ״; אָמְרוּ לַסּוֹפֵר וְכָתַב, וְלָעֵדִים וְחָתְמוּ; אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁכְּתָבוּהוּ וַחֲתָמוּהוּ וּנְתָנוּהוּ לוֹ, וְחָזַר וּנְתָנוֹ לָהּ – הֲרֵי הַגֵּט בָּטֵל, עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר לַסּוֹפֵר ״כְּתוֹב״ וְלָעֵדִים ״חֲתוֹמוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If people said to the husband: Shall we write a bill of divorce for your wife? And he said to them: Write the document, and those people told the scribe to write it, and he wrote it and instructed the witnesses to sign it, and they signed it; even if they wrote it, and signed it, and gave it to him, and he then gave it to his wife, the bill of divorce is void unless he himself says to the scribe: Write the document, and he himself says to the witnesses: Sign the document.

The *mishna* now turns to a different but related problem of *gittin*: the requirement that the husband give direct instructions to the *sofer* and witnesses. The case presented is: a man is told, "Should we write a *get* for your wife?" He answers, "Write it." They relay this to a *sofer*, who writes it, and to witnesses, who sign it. Even though the *get* is written, signed, and delivered to the husband who then gives it to his wife — *harei ha'get batel* — the *get* is invalid, *ad she'yomar le'sofer ktov ve'le'edim chasomu* — until he himself directly instructs the *sofer* to write and the witnesses to sign.

### Invalidity Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ טַעְמָא דְּלֹא אָמַר ״תְּנוּ״, הָא אָמַר ״תְּנוּ״ – נוֹתְנִין, מַנִּי? רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: מִילֵּי מִימַּסְרָן לְשָׁלִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara infers from the mishna: The reason that the bill of divorce is void is because he told the people only to write the document, but he did not say: Give a bill of divorce to my wife. But if he said to them: Give a bill of divorce to my wife, and they told the scribe to write the document and the witnesses to sign it, those people give the document to his wife and it is valid. In accordance with whose opinion is this statement? It is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir, who says: Verbal directives can be delegated to an agent. Everyone agrees that an agent can be appointed to perform an action on behalf of another, but Rabbi Meir holds that an agent can be appointed to give instructions to others on behalf of another.

The reason, the *gemara* states, is *taima de'lo amar tenu* — his only word was "write it," not "give it." The word *ktovu* — write — is a more limited instruction; *tenu* — give — implies broader authorization and somewhat more latitude. The *gemara* will explore whether saying *tenu* would have made any difference.

### Rabbi Meir Latitude

The *gemara* asks who the *reisha* — the beginning of the *mishna* — is consistent with, in light of the implication that the *get* might have been valid had a different word been used. The answer: *Rebbi Meir, de'amar mili nimseran le'shaliach* — Rabbi Meir, who holds that verbal appointment can be transmitted through an agent. Under Rabbi Meir's view, if a husband says *tenu* to a *beis din* — three people — those three are empowered to pass on the appointment to others, including instructing a *sofer* to write and witnesses to sign. In that case, the *get* might be valid even without the husband's direct instruction to the *sofer*. The *reisha*'s implication that the defect was specifically the use of *ktovu* rather than *tenu* resonates with Rabbi Meir's position.

### Rabbi Yosi Strict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא סֵיפָא: ״עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר לַסּוֹפֵר כְּתוֹב וְלָעֵדִים חֲתוֹמוּ״ – אֲתָאן לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּאָמַר: מִילֵּי לָא מִימַּסְרָן לְשָׁלִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: Say the latter clause of the mishna: The bill of divorce is void unless he himself says to the scribe: Write the document, and he himself says to the witnesses: Sign the document. In the latter clause of the mishna, we arrive at the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, who said: Verbal directives cannot be delegated to an agent.

The *seifa* — the conclusion of the *mishna*, which requires that the husband personally instruct the *sofer* and witnesses — sounds like Rabbi Yosi, *de'amar mili lo nimseran le'shaliach* — who holds that verbal appointment is categorically non-transferable. According to Rabbi Yosi, there is no formulation — not *tenu*, not *beis din*, nothing — that can substitute for direct verbal instruction from the husband to the *sofer* and witnesses.

## Verbal Transmission

### Rabbi Meir Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישָׁא רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְסֵיפָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי?! אִין, רֵישָׁא רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְסֵיפָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is that to say that the first clause of the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir and the latter clause is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei? The Gemara answers: Yes, the first clause is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir and the latter clause is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei. Although unusual, it is possible for a single mishna to represent two contrary opinions.

Rabbi Meir's position, *mili nimseran le'shaliach*, holds that words of appointment — verbal instructions — can be relayed through an agent. This means that when the husband says *tenu* to three people and designates them as a *beis din*, those three can themselves appoint a *sofer* and instruct witnesses, and the *get* produced through that chain is valid. The verbal appointment given to them can be "carried" and transmitted.

### Rabbi Yosi Position

Rabbi Yosi's position, *mili lo nimseran le'shaliach*, is the opposite: verbal appointment cannot be relayed. Only the husband himself can instruct the *sofer* and witnesses. No intermediary, regardless of the formulation used, can substitute for the husband's direct word.

### Reconciliation Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: כּוּלָּהּ רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – דְּלָא אֲמַר ״תְּנוּ״. אִי הָכִי, ״עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר תְּנוּ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** Abaye said: Actually, the mishna in its entirety is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir. And with what are we dealing here? With a situation whereby the husband did not say: Give the bill of divorce to my wife, but said only to write it. In that case he must instruct the scribe and the witnesses directly. If he issued instructions to give the bill of divorce, it would also have been effective. The Gemara asks: If so, the mishna should have said: The bill of divorce is void unless he says: Give the bill of divorce to my wife.
>
> אֶלָּא הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – דְּלָא אֲמַר לְבֵי תְלָתָא. אִי הָכִי, ״עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר לִשְׁלֹשָׁה״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** Rather, with what are we dealing here? With a situation whereby the husband did not say his instructions to three people, who constitute a court with the authority to appoint others to write a bill of divorce. Rather, he instructed two people, who do not constitute a court, and therefore do not have the authority to appoint others to write a bill of divorce, even if the husband told them to give the bill of divorce to his wife. Witnesses may only write and give the document themselves. The Gemara challenges: If so, the mishna should have said: Unless he says his instructions to three people.
>
> אֶלָּא כּוּלַּהּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – דְּלָא אֲמַר ״אִמְרוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, the mishna in its entirety is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, and with what are we dealing here? With a situation whereby the husband did not explicitly say to his agents: Say my instructions to the scribe to write the document.
>
> אִי הָכִי, ״עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר אִמְרוּ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: If so, the expression: Until he says to the scribe, is imprecise. Rather, the mishna should have said: Until the husband instructs the agents to tell.

The *gemara* struggles to read the *mishna* as consistent with a single *tanna*. If the *reisha* reflects Rabbi Meir's latitude, it should have said that the *get* is invalid specifically because he did not say *tenu* — pointing to the fix. But the *seifa* says the *get* is invalid until he directly instructs the *sofer*, with no mention of *tenu* as an alternative. That is pure Rabbi Yosi.

Abaye attempts to read the entire *mishna* as Rabbi Meir, arguing that the *mishna* is addressing a case where only *ktovu* was said, and that *tenu* would still allow for indirect instruction. The *gemara* objects: if so, the *mishna* should have said the fix is to say *tenu*, not to directly instruct the *sofer*. The *gemara* then tries to read the *mishna* as Rabbi Meir but in a context of only two people — since Rabbi Meir's latitude applies only to a *beis din* of three. But the *gemara* again objects that in that case the *mishna* should have said the fix is to have three people present. The *gemara* ultimately prefers reading the *seifa* as Rabbi Yosi, leaving the *reisha* and *seifa* as representing different *tannaim*, or attributing the entirety to Rabbi Yosi with a specific reading of the *reisha*.

## Scribe Signature

### Signature Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, מִי מוֹדֶה רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּאוֹמֵר: ״אִמְרוּ״?! וְהָתְנַן: ״כָּתַב סוֹפֵר וְעֵד – כָּשֵׁר״; וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: ״חָתַם סוֹפֵר״ שָׁנִינוּ. וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מַתְנִיתִין מַנִּי –
>
> **Translation:** And does Rabbi Yosei concede in the case of one who says: Tell another to write it? But didn't we learn in a mishna: If a bill of divorce has the writing of a scribe, and the scribe identifies his handwriting, and one witness verifies his signature, it is valid as though two witnesses testified to ratify their signatures. And Rabbi Yirmeya said: We learned in the mishna that this is the *halakha* with regard to the scribe's signature and not the scribe's writing. And Rav Ḥisda said: Whose opinion is expressed in the mishna?
>
> רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא, דְּאָמַר: מִילֵּי לָא מִימַּסְרָן לְשָׁלִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** It is the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, who says: Verbal directives cannot be delegated to an agent, and there is no concern that the scribe signed the document without the husband instructing him to do so.

A *braita* is now cited: *ksav sofer ve'ed kasher* — if a *get* bears the handwriting of the *sofer* and one witness, it is valid. Rabbi Yirmiyah adds that the *mishna* means specifically *chasam sofer* — the *sofer*'s signature, not merely his penmanship throughout the document. The *sofer*'s own signature on the *get* is sufficient evidence that he was validly appointed.

### Yosi No Fear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מוֹדֶה רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּאוֹמֵר ״אִמְרוּ״, נָפֵיק מִינַּהּ חוּרְבָּא – דְּזִימְנִין דְּאָמַר לְהוּ לִשְׁנַיִם ״אִמְרוּ לְסוֹפֵר וְיִכְתּוֹב, וְלִפְלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי וְיַחְתֹּמוּ״; וּמִשּׁוּם כִּיסּוּפָא דְּסוֹפֵר – חָיְישִׁי וּמַחְתְּמִי לֵיהּ, וּבַעַל לָא אֲמַר הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** And if it enters your mind to say that Rabbi Yosei concedes in the case of one who says: Tell a scribe and he will write the document and tell so-and-so and so-and-so and they will sign it. And due to the shame of the scribe, who asks: Don't you consider me a sufficiently upright person to sign the document as a witness, the agents are concerned to avoid that disgrace and they will have one of those witnesses and the scribe sign together with him, and the husband did not say to do so. This bill of divorce is invalid because it was signed contrary to the husband's instructions, and the agents will mistakenly think it is valid.

Rav Chisda explains: *masnisin mani, Rabbi Yosi hi, de'amar mili lo mimseran le'shaliach* — our *mishna* follows Rabbi Yosi, who holds that verbal appointment cannot be transmitted. Under Rabbi Yosi's framework, one need not fear that the *sofer* signed simply because the messengers pressured him or were embarrassed to have hired him and then ask him to sign even though the husband never appointed him. Since Rabbi Yosi requires that the *sofer* hear directly from the husband, if the *sofer* has signed, we may presume the husband indeed appointed him directly.

### Embarrassment Risk

The *gemara* raises a concern: *mishum kissufa de'sofer* — the problem of the scribe's embarrassment. The scenario is that the husband tells two people: "Tell the *sofer* to write, and tell Reuven and Shimon to sign." These people then approach the *sofer*, and out of embarrassment — not wanting to have brought him all this way for nothing, or not wanting to exclude him — they ask him to sign as well, even though the husband said no such thing. If there is any avenue under Rabbi Yosi for indirect appointment — even through an explicit *omer imru*, telling the agents to tell the *sofer* — this *kissufa* risk makes the *sofer*'s signature unreliable as evidence of valid appointment.

The *gemara* therefore argues that the very fact that Rabbi Yirmiyah and Rav Chisda treat the *sofer*'s signature as reliable under Rabbi Yosi's framework demonstrates that Rabbi Yosi recognizes no *omer imru* exception at all. There is no pathway under Rabbi Yosi for indirect appointment. The only way the *sofer* could legitimately have signed is if he heard directly from the husband, and the *kissufa* dynamic simply cannot arise in Rabbi Yosi's world.

### Unresolved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מְחַוַּורְתָּא, רֵישָׁא רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְסֵיפָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The reason that this is not a concern must be because Rabbi Yosei holds that even if the husband says to the agents: Tell the scribe to write, the bill of divorce is not valid. Rather, it is clear that the first clause of the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir and the latter clause is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei.

At this point, the *gemara* has not fully resolved how the *reisha* and *seifa* of the *mishna* can both be read coherently under any single *tannaic* position. The *reisha*'s implication that *ktovu* was the problem — suggesting *tenu* might have worked — sits uneasily alongside a *seifa* that, read according to Rabbi Yosi, leaves no room for any alternative to direct instruction. The question of how to harmonize the two halves of the *mishna* is carried forward into the next day's *sugya*.
