# Gittin 71 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Testing Non-Speakers

### False Nodding Problem

The daf opens by continuing a discussion from the preceding amud concerning a husband who has lost the ability to speak — *nishtatek* — and whether those around him may write a *get* on his behalf based on his nodding responses. The *mishna* had established that one may pose yes-or-no questions and, if he responds correctly and coherently, his nodded instructions may be followed. But the *gemara* immediately raises a difficulty: *v'lechosh d'lamma shita d'lav lav naktay* — perhaps the man has been seized by a fit of head-shaking and is simply nodding or shaking reflexively. If all the questions put to him happen to align with his involuntary movement, his responses prove nothing.

### Alternating Questions

Rav Papa's solution is to ask the questions *b'sirugin* — alternating between questions that should elicit a "yes" and questions that should elicit a "no." Rashi understands this to mean waiting an interval between each question; the approach suggested here, however, is that the questions themselves alternate in the type of answer they require, so that a man simply locked into a repetitive motion would immediately be exposed. But the *gemara* presses further: perhaps he is seized by a fit of *alternating* — first nodding, then shaking, then nodding again. The answer is that one should vary the pattern unpredictably: *chad lav u'trei hen, u'trei lav u'chad hen* — one question requiring "no," then two requiring "yes," then two requiring "no," then one requiring "yes," in no fixed sequence, so that no mechanical repetition could accidentally produce the correct results throughout.

### Fruit Test Questions

A *baraita* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael adds a further method: *amrinan leih devarim shel yemos hachama b'yemos hageshamim v'shel yemos hageshamim b'yemos hachama* — one asks him about things characteristic of summer during the winter, and about things characteristic of winter during the summer. The *gemara* questions what precisely this means. If you ask whether he wants a winter coat or a summer coat, that proves nothing, since a person might be cold in summer or warm in winter. Rather, says the *gemara*, the test is conducted through fruit — *b'peiri* — asking about seasonal produce that is simply unavailable out of season. Tosafot notes that some fruits can be preserved, and therefore suggests the question be posed in terms of going out to the orchards to pick fruit, which clearly cannot be done in the wrong season.

## Deaf-Mute Writing

### Writing = Speech

A statement of Rav Kahana in the name of Rav now introduces one of the most important and far-reaching *chiddushim* on the daf: *cheresh she'yachol l'daber mitoch haksav* — if a person who is deaf can "speak" through writing, then *kosvin venosin get le'ishto* — a *get* may be written and given to his wife.

To appreciate the full weight of this ruling, one must recall what the term *cheresh* means in the *gemara*. It refers not simply to a person who cannot hear, but specifically to the deaf-mute — someone who is both deaf and unable to speak intelligibly. The *gemara* operated under the presumption that most people born deaf never acquired intelligible speech, and were therefore classified as lacking *bar da'as* status. This is a categorical *halakhic* designation: such a person is considered to lack the intellectual competence required for binding legal transactions at the *d'oraisa* level. He may marry and be married *d'rabbanan*, but a marriage contracted before he became a *cheresh* — when he was a *pikeach*, a person of full faculties — is a full *d'oraisa* marriage, which would ordinarily require a valid *get* for dissolution.

Rav Kahana's ruling addresses precisely this case. If such a person can communicate through writing, he is no longer halakhically a *cheresh* at all. One might understand this in one of two ways. The formal reading is that writing constitutes an act of speech — *yachol l'daber mitoch haksav*, he can "speak" through writing — and therefore he is definitionally not mute. The functional reading is that the designation *cheresh* was always a proxy for lacking *da'as*, and that writing demonstrates unambiguously that he possesses *da'as*, rendering the category inapplicable. As will become clear, the distinction between these two framings is not merely theoretical.

### Rav Yosef Challenge

Rav Yosef challenges the novelty of this ruling: *tanina* — we already know this from a *braita* concerning a man who became silent (*nishtatek*), was asked whether a *get* should be written, nodded, was tested three times, and answered yes-and-no questions correctly, whereupon it was ruled that a *get* may be written. Is it not obvious that non-verbal communication suffices?

### Ilem vs. Cheresh

Rav Zeira responds that the *braita* is no precedent at all, because it speaks of an *ilem* — a person who is mute but can hear — and that case is entirely different. As a *braita* establishes: *medaber ve'eino shomea zehu cheresh* — one who can speak but not hear is a *cheresh*; *shomea ve'eino medaber zehu ilem* — one who can hear but not speak is an *ilem*. And *ve'ze ve'ze harei hen kepikechin lekhol divreihem* — both the person who is only deaf and the person who is only mute are treated as fully competent, *kepikechin*, in all respects. An *ilem* is unquestionably a *bar da'as*; the only question regarding him is whether his nodding constitutes a valid instruction. The *gemara* notes a scriptural support for the terminology from the verse *va'ani kacheresh lo eshma, ukhe'ilem lo yiftach piv* — "I was like a deaf person who did not hear, and like a mute person who did not open his mouth" — showing that the deaf person and the mute are designated by distinct terms in *Tanakh* as well.

The *chiddush* of Rav Kahana amar Rav, therefore, is not about a *bar da'as* who happens to be unable to speak. It concerns someone the *halakha* would categorize as an *eino bar da'as* — a *cheresh* — who ordinarily cannot engage in binding *halakhic* transactions. The ruling is that if this person can write, that recategorizes him entirely. That is a genuinely revolutionary position.

### Writing Testimony

Rav Zeira raises a further question from a different angle. The verse in *Vayikra* states *im lo yagid venasa avono* — if a person who was adjured to testify fails to come forward, he bears his sin and must bring a *korban shvua*. The verse adds *perat le'ilem she'eino yachol lehagid* — excluding a mute person who cannot speak. But according to Rav Kahana, writing is a form of speaking — *yachol lehagid mitoch haksav* — so why should a mute person who can write be excluded from the laws of testimony and the *korban shvua*?

Abaye responds: *shani eidus derachmana amar mipihem velo mipi ksavam* — testimony is different, because the Torah specifically requires *al pi shenayim eidim*, "by the mouth of two witnesses," and the *gemara* derives from this that testimony must come from their mouths and not from their writing. Even if writing constitutes a form of speech for other *halakhic* purposes, the specific demand of *mipihem* — from their mouths — requires actual verbal articulation. One might further note that this principle also underlies the requirement that a *beis din* know the seventy languages — *shivim lashon* — since testimony cannot pass through an interpreter, for *mipihem velo mipi hameturgeman*.

This exchange actually clarifies the formal nature of Rav Kahana's position. The *gemara* is not merely saying that writing is a reliable indicator of *da'as*; it is making the stronger claim that writing counts as speech *halakhically*. This is why the objection from testimony is worth raising at all — if writing were merely a *de facto* test of competence, there would be nothing to ask. But because writing is being declared a formal act of speech, Rav Zeira can legitimately ask whether it should satisfy *lehagid*, and Abaye must answer that the particular requirement of *mipihem* is more exacting than the general category of speech.

A subsequent *braita* cited in the *gemara* states: *keshem shebodkin oso l'gittin, kach bodkin oso l'massa u'mattanot, ul'eduyos, ul'yerushos* — the same testing procedure used for *gittin* applies to business transactions, testimony, and bequests. This seems to reintroduce the problem, since *eduyos* is listed. The *gemara* resolves: Rav Yosef bar Minyumi in the name of Rav Sheshet explains that the mention of testimony refers to *edus isha* — testimony concerning whether a woman's husband has died — where the rabbis were lenient and did not require the full standards of formal testimony. As for bequests (*yerushos*), Rabbi Abahu explains that it refers to a case where the person is declaring which of his sons is the firstborn — again not a case of formal *eidus*. And *massa u'mattanot*, business transactions, refers to his own transactions, not to his testifying about someone else's. So the principle that formal testimony requires verbal speech remains intact.

## Braita Contradiction

### Braita Rule

The *gemara* now introduces a *braita* that directly challenges Rav Kahana's position. The *braita* states that when it comes to a *cheresh*, the *chachamim* did not follow his signaling, his jumping, or his writing — *v'halchu bo achar remizosav v'achar k'fitzosav v'achar k'sav yado* — except for *mitaltelin*, transactions involving movable property, where a lower standard of *da'as* has always been accepted. But *aval lo l'gittin* — not for *gittin*. This directly contradicts Rav Kahana's ruling that a *cheresh* who can write may divorce his wife through that writing.

One might ask at this point: does this difficulty not arise only regarding a marriage that is *d'oraisa*? After all, the principle *keshem shekones bi'remiza kach motzi bi'remiza* — just as he married through signaling, so too he may divorce through signaling — seems to already allow a *cheresh* who married rabbinically to be divorced in the same mode. The *gemara* will confirm that this principle applies only to a woman he married as a *cheresh*, where the original marriage was *d'rabbanan*. The hard case is a woman he married before becoming a *cheresh*, or — as will emerge — a *yevama* who fell to him from a *pikeach* brother, where the relationship carries *d'oraisa* force.

### Tannaitic Dispute

The *gemara* resolves the contradiction: *tanna'ei hi* — it is a dispute between *tannaim*. The *braita* that denies writing any power to redefine a *cheresh* represents the position of the *chachamim*, while Rav Kahana's ruling follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.

### Rabban Shimon Caveat

The *braita* records: *bameh devarim amorim b'cheresh mei'ikaro* — when is it true that writing does not help? When the person was *always* a *cheresh*. *Aval pikeach venischaraish* — but if he was originally a *pikeach* and then became deaf-mute — *hu kosev vehein chosmin* — he writes and others cosign the *get*, and it is valid.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel thus draws a distinction: a person who began life with full faculties and then lost them may be recategorized if he can communicate through writing. A person who was always a *cheresh* cannot be recategorized no matter how clearly he writes. The *chachamim* disagree and make no such distinction — for them, writing helps in neither case.

This also raises the question of how a *cheresh* who was *always* a *cheresh* could ever be in a situation where he needs a *get* from a *d'oraisa* marriage. If he was always a *cheresh*, any marriage he contracted himself was only *d'rabbanan*, and he could dissolve it the same way it was contracted. The answer is *yibbum* — levirate marriage. If his brother, a *pikeach*, died, leaving a wife, and this *cheresh* then had relations with her, the act of *yibbum* itself — which does not require *da'as* — would create a *d'oraisa* bond between them. Now he has a *d'oraisa* marriage he cannot dissolve through writing, according to the *chachamim*.

The implications for the *halachic* history of deaf people are significant. This is one of the most consequential *sugyot* on the matter, and the *gemara* — through this *braita* — has pushed back hard against Rav Kahana's liberating position. This deserves to be noted. The concern for *agunos* that Rav Kahana's ruling might have alleviated remains in tension with the outcome of the *sugya*.

## Yibum Case

### Rabbinic Marriage

The *gemara* now addresses the question head-on. If a *cheresh* was always a *cheresh*, then *keshem shekone'sa beremiza kach motzi'a beremiza* — the same way he married through signaling, he can divorce through signaling. His marriage was only *d'rabbanan*, so his divorce need only satisfy a *d'rabbanan* standard as well. This is not in dispute.

### Yibum Obligation

The problematic case is *yevamto* — his *yevama*, his sister-in-law who falls to him for *yibbum*. If his brother was a *pikeach* and was *d'oraisa* married to a woman, and that brother died, then this *cheresh* has a *d'oraisa* obligation of *yibbum*. The act of *yibbum* is effected through sexual relations, and that act does not require *da'as* to be binding at the *d'oraisa* level. Once the act occurs, she is his wife *d'oraisa*, and he cannot divorce her — not through signaling and not through writing, according to those who reject Rav Kahana.

### Wife vs. Sister

The *gemara* then asks: if his brother was himself a *cheresh* and had only a *d'rabbanan* marriage to this woman, then the entire framework remains rabbinic — her marriage to the brother was *d'rabbanan*, and consequently her status as a *yevama* is also only *d'rabbanan*. In that case, *keshem shek'nisata b'remiza kach yetziata b'remiza* should apply even to the *yibbum* scenario. 

The *gemara* offers an alternative answer: *gzeira achiv chereish atu achiv pikeach* — we make a decree that even the case where the brother was a *cheresh* is treated like the case where the brother was a *pikeach*, because the two situations — a *yevama* from a *cheresh* brother and a *yevama* from a *pikeach* brother — might be confused with one another.

But then the *gemara* asks: *ishto nami* — if we are concerned about such confusion, why not similarly prohibit divorcing one's wife when the marriage was *d'rabbanan*, lest it be confused with a *d'oraisa* marriage? The *gemara* answers: *yevamto b'yevamto michalfa, ishto b'yevamto lo michalfa* — a person can confuse one *yibbum* case with another *yibbum* case, since both involve the same category of relationship. But no one will confuse a wife with a *yevama*, as these are categorically distinct situations in the mind of anyone dealing with them.

### Two Deaf Siblings

The *gemara* now challenges the *gzeira* from a different direction. A *mishna* discusses the case of two brothers who are both *charashim*, married to two sisters who may be *pikchot*, *cherashot*, or one of each. In all these combinations, the marriages are *d'rabbanan*, and consequently the *gemara* rules that *harei elu peturot min hachalitza umin hayibbum* — they are entirely exempt from both *yibbum* and *chalitza*, because we treat the situation as analogous to a case of *arayos* in a full *d'oraisa* framework: just as two brothers married to two sisters cannot do *yibbum* when everything is *d'oraisa*, so too when everything is operating on the same *d'rabbanan* plane, we apply the same logic. Conversely, *v'im hayu nochriyot yichnesu* — if the two women are unrelated, *yibbum* is performed, and since the *yibbum* itself is only *d'rabbanan*, *v'im ratzu lehotzi yotzi'u* — the husband may afterward divorce her with a *get*, since the marriage is only rabbinic.

What is significant about this *mishna* for the present argument is that it treats the *cheresh* brother's case in its own right, without any concern that it will be confused with the *pikeach* brother's case. This directly undermines the *gzeira* reasoning. The *gemara* acknowledges this: *umi gazrinan chereish atu pikeach?* — are we really going to decree that a *cheresh* case be treated like a *pikeach* case? The *mishna* plainly does not do so.

The *gemara* therefore concludes: *ela michvarta kideshanin me'ikara* — the better answer is the original one. The case where the *cheresh* who was always a *cheresh* cannot divorce his *yevama* is specifically the case where his brother was a *pikeach*, making the *yibbum* a genuine *d'oraisa* bond. Where the brother was also a *cheresh*, there is no need for a *gzeira* because the two cases are not confused with each other, and divorce remains available.

## Recategorization Debate

### Rejection Clarified

Rav Yochanan makes explicit what was already implied: *chalukin alav chaveirav d'Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — the *chachamim* disagree with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. This is not a case where Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's leniency can be taken as a clarification of the majority view. His position stands alone, and the majority reject it entirely.

### Can Never Divorce

Abaye adds that this rejection can be seen from the *mishna* itself: *nishtatek lo yotzi, nitcharesh... lo yotzi olamit* — if a man becomes silent he may not divorce his wife, and if he becomes a *cheresh* or *shoteh* he may *never* divorce her. The word *olamit* — forever — is doing important work here.

### Forever = Writing

Rav Papa explains what he would have said had Rav Yochanan not clarified the matter: he would have read *olamit* as meaning that no matter how intelligent the man appears — *af al gav d'chazina d'charif* — as long as he cannot communicate, he can never divorce. But once he can write, he would no longer be classified as a *cheresh* and could divorce. In other words, Rav Papa would have read the *mishna* as consistent with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel — "*never*" means never *as a cheresh*, but writing exits the category. Only Rav Yochanan's explicit statement that the *chachamim* disagree with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel forecloses this reading. According to the *chachamim*, *olamit* means that even if he can write, it does not change his status, and he can never divorce her.

### Shotah Status

An alternative reading of *olamit* is offered in the name of Rav Yitzchak: *d'var Torah shota mitgereshes* — biblically, a *shotah* — a woman who is not of sound mind — can in principle be divorced. The reasoning is *midi d'hava a'pikeches ba'al korcha* — just as a woman who is fully competent can be divorced against her will, the same applies to a *shotah*, since the *get* takes effect regardless of her mental state, provided she can retain custody of it (*shomeres es gitah*). Why then did the rabbis prohibit divorcing a *shotah*? *Shelo yinhagu ba minhag hefker* — so that people should not treat her as ownerless property, exploiting her vulnerability. This concern reflects a sober awareness of the dangers to which cognitively impaired individuals are exposed.

Rav Yitzchak's reading thus proposes that *olamit* was said specifically about the *man* to emphasize that when *he* becomes a *cheresh* or *shoteh*, the prohibition is *d'oraisa* — unlike the case where the *woman* becomes a *shotah*, where the prohibition is only *d'rabbanan*. The word "forever" signals the absolute, *d'oraisa* nature of his incapacity to grant a *get*, in contrast to her case, where the incapacity is only rabbinic.

## Modern Challenge

### Logical Problem

The *sugya* leaves us with a genuine *halakhic* challenge that has only grown more acute in modern times. The *gemara* — according to the *chachamim* who reject even Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel — holds that a *cheresh* who was born a *cheresh* cannot grant a *get* even if he can write clearly and intelligently. This is a striking conclusion. We are not dealing with a person whose competence is ambiguous. We are dealing with a person who is demonstrably communicating precisely in writing — and yet the *gemara* still classifies him as a *cheresh*, lacking *bar da'as* status at the *d'oraisa* level.

The logical tension is acute. How can a person who writes clearly, reasons clearly, and communicates precisely be described as lacking *da'as*? This is not merely a formal inconvenience — it has profound human consequences, affecting the person's ability to give a *get*, receive an *aliyah*, serve as a *shaliach tzibbur*, or perform any *mitzvah* at a *d'oraisa* level. The *gemara* seems to be applying a rigidly formal category rather than a genuinely cognitive one.

### Tech Solutions

One interesting technological consideration that arises in this context is text-to-speech software. If a person types text that is then vocalized by a device — as Stephen Hawking famously did — one might argue that the result is actual audible speech, not merely writing. This could potentially provide a basis for satisfying even those authorities who require actual *mipihem*, verbal articulation, rather than merely writing, at least for purposes other than formal *eidus* where the *d'oraisa* requirement of *mipihem* applies.

### Poskim Redefine

In practical *halakha*, this *sugya* stands at the center of a major debate among contemporary *poskim* regarding the status of deaf individuals in the modern era. The development of schools for the deaf — dating back roughly two and a half centuries — and above all the emergence of sign language as a fully structured linguistic system with its own grammar, has prompted extensive *halakhic* reassessment. Many prominent *poskim* have ruled that a *cheresh* who can communicate through sign language is no longer classified as a *cheresh* for *halakhic* purposes. This is both a logically compelling and a practically important ruling.

At the same time, intellectual honesty requires acknowledging the difficulty this *gemara* poses. The *gemara* specifically discusses a *cheresh* who communicates through *writing* — precise, articulate, unambiguous communication — and yet still classifies him as a *cheresh* at the *d'oraisa* level if he was born that way. Any contemporary ruling in the direction of recategorization must engage with this *gemara* directly, whether by distinguishing sign language from writing on formal grounds, or by arguing that changed social circumstances alter the underlying presumptions about cognitive development that originally grounded the category.

## Direct Instruction

### Direct Instruction Rule

The *mishna* now turns to a different but related problem of *gittin*: the requirement that the husband give direct instructions to the *sofer* and witnesses. The case presented is: a man is told, "Should we write a *get* for your wife?" He answers, "Write it." They relay this to a *sofer*, who writes it, and to witnesses, who sign it. Even though the *get* is written, signed, and delivered to the husband who then gives it to his wife — *harei ha'get batel* — the *get* is invalid, *ad she'yomar le'sofer ktov ve'le'edim chasomu* — until he himself directly instructs the *sofer* to write and the witnesses to sign.

### Invalidity Reason

The reason, the *gemara* states, is *taima de'lo amar tenu* — his only word was "write it," not "give it." The word *ktovu* — write — is a more limited instruction; *tenu* — give — implies broader authorization and somewhat more latitude. The *gemara* will explore whether saying *tenu* would have made any difference.

### Rabbi Meir Latitude

The *gemara* asks who the *reisha* — the beginning of the *mishna* — is consistent with, in light of the implication that the *get* might have been valid had a different word been used. The answer: *Rebbi Meir, de'amar mili nimseran le'shaliach* — Rabbi Meir, who holds that verbal appointment can be transmitted through an agent. Under Rabbi Meir's view, if a husband says *tenu* to a *beis din* — three people — those three are empowered to pass on the appointment to others, including instructing a *sofer* to write and witnesses to sign. In that case, the *get* might be valid even without the husband's direct instruction to the *sofer*. The *reisha*'s implication that the defect was specifically the use of *ktovu* rather than *tenu* resonates with Rabbi Meir's position.

### Rabbi Yosi Strict

The *seifa* — the conclusion of the *mishna*, which requires that the husband personally instruct the *sofer* and witnesses — sounds like Rabbi Yosi, *de'amar mili lo nimseran le'shaliach* — who holds that verbal appointment is categorically non-transferable. According to Rabbi Yosi, there is no formulation — not *tenu*, not *beis din*, nothing — that can substitute for direct verbal instruction from the husband to the *sofer* and witnesses.

## Verbal Transmission

### Rabbi Meir Position

Rabbi Meir's position, *mili nimseran le'shaliach*, holds that words of appointment — verbal instructions — can be relayed through an agent. This means that when the husband says *tenu* to three people and designates them as a *beis din*, those three can themselves appoint a *sofer* and instruct witnesses, and the *get* produced through that chain is valid. The verbal appointment given to them can be "carried" and transmitted.

### Rabbi Yosi Position

Rabbi Yosi's position, *mili lo nimseran le'shaliach*, is the opposite: verbal appointment cannot be relayed. Only the husband himself can instruct the *sofer* and witnesses. No intermediary, regardless of the formulation used, can substitute for the husband's direct word.

### Reconciliation Problem

The *gemara* struggles to read the *mishna* as consistent with a single *tanna*. If the *reisha* reflects Rabbi Meir's latitude, it should have said that the *get* is invalid specifically because he did not say *tenu* — pointing to the fix. But the *seifa* says the *get* is invalid until he directly instructs the *sofer*, with no mention of *tenu* as an alternative. That is pure Rabbi Yosi.

Abaye attempts to read the entire *mishna* as Rabbi Meir, arguing that the *mishna* is addressing a case where only *ktovu* was said, and that *tenu* would still allow for indirect instruction. The *gemara* objects: if so, the *mishna* should have said the fix is to say *tenu*, not to directly instruct the *sofer*. The *gemara* then tries to read the *mishna* as Rabbi Meir but in a context of only two people — since Rabbi Meir's latitude applies only to a *beis din* of three. But the *gemara* again objects that in that case the *mishna* should have said the fix is to have three people present. The *gemara* ultimately prefers reading the *seifa* as Rabbi Yosi, leaving the *reisha* and *seifa* as representing different *tannaim*, or attributing the entirety to Rabbi Yosi with a specific reading of the *reisha*.

## Scribe Signature

### Signature Evidence

A *braita* is now cited: *ksav sofer ve'ed kasher* — if a *get* bears the handwriting of the *sofer* and one witness, it is valid. Rabbi Yirmiyah adds that the *mishna* means specifically *chasam sofer* — the *sofer*'s signature, not merely his penmanship throughout the document. The *sofer*'s own signature on the *get* is sufficient evidence that he was validly appointed.

### Yosi No Fear

Rav Chisda explains: *masnisin mani, Rabbi Yosi hi, de'amar mili lo mimseran le'shaliach* — our *mishna* follows Rabbi Yosi, who holds that verbal appointment cannot be transmitted. Under Rabbi Yosi's framework, one need not fear that the *sofer* signed simply because the messengers pressured him or were embarrassed to have hired him and then ask him to sign even though the husband never appointed him. Since Rabbi Yosi requires that the *sofer* hear directly from the husband, if the *sofer* has signed, we may presume the husband indeed appointed him directly.

### Embarrassment Risk

The *gemara* raises a concern: *mishum kissufa de'sofer* — the problem of the scribe's embarrassment. The scenario is that the husband tells two people: "Tell the *sofer* to write, and tell Reuven and Shimon to sign." These people then approach the *sofer*, and out of embarrassment — not wanting to have brought him all this way for nothing, or not wanting to exclude him — they ask him to sign as well, even though the husband said no such thing. If there is any avenue under Rabbi Yosi for indirect appointment — even through an explicit *omer imru*, telling the agents to tell the *sofer* — this *kissufa* risk makes the *sofer*'s signature unreliable as evidence of valid appointment.

The *gemara* therefore argues that the very fact that Rabbi Yirmiyah and Rav Chisda treat the *sofer*'s signature as reliable under Rabbi Yosi's framework demonstrates that Rabbi Yosi recognizes no *omer imru* exception at all. There is no pathway under Rabbi Yosi for indirect appointment. The only way the *sofer* could legitimately have signed is if he heard directly from the husband, and the *kissufa* dynamic simply cannot arise in Rabbi Yosi's world.

### Unresolved

At this point, the *gemara* has not fully resolved how the *reisha* and *seifa* of the *mishna* can both be read coherently under any single *tannaic* position. The *reisha*'s implication that *ktovu* was the problem — suggesting *tenu* might have worked — sits uneasily alongside a *seifa* that, read according to Rabbi Yosi, leaves no room for any alternative to direct instruction. The question of how to harmonize the two halves of the *mishna* is carried forward into the next day's *sugya*.
