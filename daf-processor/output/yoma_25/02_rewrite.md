# Yoma 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Context & Setup

### Daf Overview

Daf 25 begins approximately ten lines from the bottom of the preceding page — just before the passage where the lines widen and the words *lama mafisim* appear. In some editions of the Gemara this passage is set off by two dots, indicating a new discussion, while in others it is not. The reason for this ambiguity is that the Gemara is not returning to quote a new Mishnah; rather, it is resuming an earlier thread. The bulk of the material on daf 24b had been devoted to the question of what constitutes an *avoda*, whether a *kohen gadol* or a non-*kohen* performed it, and the attendant question of *chiyuv mita*. That detour addressed the *trumat hadeshen* from multiple angles — its timing, the requirement to change garments, whether the *hotzat hadeshen* and the *trumat hadeshen* are themselves *avodot*, how much ash is removed, and whether a *zar* who performs the *trumat hadeshen* incurs the death penalty. Now, having concluded that discussion, the Gemara returns to the original subject: the lottery held in connection with the *trumat hadeshen* during the ordinary days of the year — not on Yom Kippur.

It is worth noting in passing that the *rishonim* debate whether on Yom Kippur itself the *kohen gadol* performs the *trumat hadeshen* or whether another *kohen* does so, since the *trumat hadeshen* is performed at night and the question is whether the requirement that all the day's *avodot* be done by the *kohen gadol* extends to this nighttime service. That debate, however, pertains to Yom Kippur; the present discussion concerns the rest of the year.

### Lottery Context

The prior material established that a *payis* — a lottery — was held to determine who would perform the *trumat hadeshen*. That lottery touched off a long chain of associated *halachot*. Now the Gemara returns: *lama mafisim* — why would they conduct the lottery at all? The Gemara swiftly pivots: the real question is not why a lottery exists but why it is done four separate times rather than once at the beginning of the day.

---

## Purpose of Lotteries

### Four Lotteries

The deeper question the Gemara raises is: given that several fixed services are assigned by lottery each day — one lottery for the *trumat hadeshen*, another for the *tamid*, a third for the *ketores*, and so forth — why not conduct a single lottery at the outset of the day and assign all the regular daily duties in one stroke? Why are there four separate lottery events?

### Commotion & Excitement

Rabbi Yochanan answers: *kedai lehargish kol ha'azara* — in order to create a commotion throughout the entire Temple courtyard. The purpose of repeating the lottery process multiple times is to generate excitement, movement, and a sense of heightened significance. Each new lottery would send a wave of activity through the *Beis HaMikdash*: priests running, officers assembling, the entire *azara* stirred into motion. Rabbi Yochanan derives this from the verse in Psalms (55:15): *asher yachdav namtik sod, b'veis Elohim nehalech b'ragesh* — "together we would share sweet counsel; in the house of God we would walk with commotion." The *ragesh*, the commotion, is understood to reflect the animated, purposeful energy that was meant to pervade the Temple.

### Verse Analysis

It is worth observing the *pshat* of the verse alongside the *drash*. The word *sod* in the verse — which can mean "secret" or "counsel" — is parallel to *yachdav*, "together," suggesting that *sod* here refers to a gathering of wise and understanding people, a *sod chachamim v'nevonim*. That is the *pshat*: a group convening together in the house of God, moving with commotion. The *drash* that the Gemara invokes focuses on the *ragesh*, using it to explain the halakhic requirement for multiple lotteries.

There is also something worth observing in the juxtaposition of *sod* and *ragesh* even in the *drash*: the combination of something mysterious or esoteric alongside all this movement and commotion creates a powerful sense of importance and gravity. While the Gemara itself does not draw that comparison, the contrast is striking — unlike the secret rituals of the surrounding nations, which derived their mystique from deliberate concealment, the *Beis HaMikdash* achieved a related effect through transparency paired with animated ceremony. Indeed, the lottery ceremony was conducted inside a chamber rather than in the open *azara*, so it was not entirely visible to all — a point that reinforces the element of *sod* even while the *ragesh* was public.

## Clothing Dispute

### Weekday vs. Priestly

The Gemara next addresses the question *bameh mafisim* — in what garments did the *kohanim* stand when they participated in the lottery? Rav Nachman holds they wore *bigdei chol*, their ordinary weekday clothes. Rav Sheshet holds they wore *bigdei kodesh*, their priestly vestments. The dispute is not framed around a principled question of whether *bigdei kehuna* are required or permitted for non-*avoda* activity; the arguments on both sides are grounded entirely in human psychology.

### Rav Nachman Rationale

Rav Nachman reasons: if all the *kohanim* were already standing in their *bigdei kehuna*, then a bully — *ba'alei zero'ot* — who did not win the lottery could simply push forward and perform the service before anyone could stop him. Being already dressed and ready, he could race to the *mizbeach* and perform the *trumat hadeshen* before the proper winner could assert himself. Having everyone in weekday garments creates a necessary delay: the winner must first go change, and during that interval the officers can ensure the correct person proceeds. The weekday clothing, in other words, serves as a practical safeguard against unauthorized seizure of the *avoda*.

### Rav Sheshet Rationale

Rav Sheshet reasons the opposite way. If the *kohanim* are in their weekday garments, then the winner of the lottery — because the *avoda* is so precious and dear to him, *agav chavivuteih* — will be too excited to wait to change and will run up to perform the service without putting on his *bigdei kehuna*. To prevent this, everyone should already be dressed in *bigdei kehuna* from the outset. The two positions thus represent two different analyses of human nature: Rav Nachman worries about the aggressive usurper; Rav Sheshet worries about the overeager winner.

### Tamid Source

Rav Nachman cites a source from the Mishnah in Tamid. As Tosafot notes, that Mishnah appears immediately after the passage describing the lottery for the *trumat hadeshen*, making it contextually relevant. The Mishnah states: *masru l'chazzanim* — they handed them over to the *chazzanim* (the coordinating officers); *hayu mafshitim otan et bigdeihem* — they would strip them of their garments; *v'lo hayu manichim ela michnasayim bilvad* — leaving them with nothing but their breeches. Rav Nachman reads this as referring to the winners of the lottery: they are handed over, stripped of their weekday garments, and dressed in *bigdei kehuna*. This implies they had been wearing weekday clothing.

### Alternative Reading

Rav Huna bar Yehuda in the name of Rav Sheshet contests this reading. He argues the Mishnah refers not to those who won the lottery but to those who did *not* win. The lottery was conducted before daybreak, and those who did not win the *trumat hadeshen* would remove their *bigdei kehuna* — there being no need to wear them in the middle of the night — and wait in their *michnasayim* until the morning service began, at which point they would re-dress fully. The winners, already in *bigdei kehuna*, proceeded immediately to perform the service.

### Undergarment Issue

This reading faces a difficulty. If the Mishnah is speaking of those who *did* win and were to be stripped of weekday garments and dressed in *bigdei kehuna*, the text's mention of leaving them only in their *michnasayim* is puzzling. The *michnasayim* — the linen undergarment — is itself one of the *bigdei kehuna*. If you strip a man of his weekday garments and leave him in his weekday undergarment before putting on the priestly vestments, you face a problem: the *michnasayim* of the *bigdei kehuna* must be placed directly against the flesh, as derived from the verse (Lev. 16:4): *umichnesei vad yihyu al besaro* — "the linen breeches shall be upon his flesh." You cannot put the *bigdei kehuna* undergarment on top of a weekday undergarment and then remove the latter from beneath, because at the moment the priestly *michnasayim* are donned, they must already be directly on the skin.

### Garment Solution

The Gemara resolves this with a creative reading of the procedure. The text, correctly understood, describes the following sequence: *ad she'odan aleihem bigdei chol* — while the *kohen* is still wearing his weekday cloak (people then wore togas or cloaks rather than trousers), *malbishin otan michnesei kodesh* — attendants first have him remove his weekday undergarment and put on the priestly *michnasayim* beneath the cloak, directly against the flesh; *ve'achar hayu mafshitin otan bigdei chol* — only then is the outer cloak stripped away, after which the remaining *bigdei kehuna* are put on. In this sequence the *michnasayim* of the *bigdei kehuna* are indeed placed on bare flesh — the cloak is an outer garment, not an undergarment in the relevant sense — and no violation of the requirement occurs. This reading imports a great deal into the text that is not stated explicitly, but it is the only way to reconcile the Mishnah with the *baraita* requiring the *michnasayim* to be placed on bare flesh.

## Mitznefet & Gazis

### Headgear Problem

Rav Sheshet now marshals his own evidence from a *baraita* describing the lottery procedure in the *Lishkat Hagazit*, the Chamber of Hewn Stone. The *baraita* states that the appointed officer (*memuneh*) would come and take the *mitznefet* — the priestly headgear — from the head of one of the *kohanim*, and the count for the lottery would begin from that *kohen*. The Gemara asks: if the *kohanim* were in their weekday garments, as Rav Nachman maintains, who would be wearing a *mitznefet*? A *mitznefet* is a component of *bigdei kehuna*; it has no place in ordinary weekday dress.

The response is: *ain* — this is not a problem — *k'detani Rav Yehuda*: Rav Yehuda (and some say Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda) teaches that if a *kohen*'s mother made him a *ketonet* — using the same term applied to the priestly tunic — he may use it to perform his service, specifically *avodat yachid*. In other words, garments resembling *bigdei kehuna* but not formally consecrated as such could be made by private individuals and worn in certain contexts, and these garments would use the same terminology — *ketonet*, *mitznefet* — without actually being *bigdei kehuna*.

### Mother's Garment

The concept introduced here is that the *ketonet* a *kohen*'s mother made for him could be used for *avodat yachid*. When the same *baraita* is quoted later in the tractate (35b), it adds the qualification *uvalvad shetimsrena latzibur* — provided she transfers ownership to the communal funds — suggesting that if the garment is to be used for actual *avoda*, it must be consecrated and publicly owned. But even before that formal transfer, the garment would be fashioned to resemble *bigdei kehuna* and would employ the same terminology.

### Rashi's View

Rashi's explanation here, which he connects to the present Gemara, is that mothers would make their *kohen* sons beautiful garments styled after the *bigdei kehuna* — garments that were not formally *bigdei kehuna* but evoked them visually and nominally. This fits the Gemara's logic: the *mitznefet* visible on a *kohen*'s head during the lottery would not be a formal priestly *mitznefet* but a garment resembling one, made by his mother. A *kohen* standing in his everyday attire might still be wearing such a garment on his head. Rashi's approach aligns with a reading of *avodat yachid* as referring to the *Kohen Gadol*'s reading of the Torah on Yom Kippur, which does not require formal *bigdei kehuna* — the *ketonet*-like garment would be worn for that occasion. Rashi himself prefers a different understanding of *avodat yachid* (the *Kohen Gadol*'s solitary entry into the *Kodesh Kodashim* for the *ketores*), but that reading creates tension with the requirement at 35b that the garment be transferred to public ownership, since that requirement implies it must function as actual *bigdei kehuna*. For the present passage, however, the reading that the garment is *not* actual *bigdei kehuna* — only resembling it in appearance and name — serves the Gemara's argument most cleanly: the *mitznefet* visible during the lottery need not be a formal priestly article.

## Mitznefet & Gazis…

### Chamber Layout

The *baraita* cited by Rav Sheshet describes the *Lishkat Hagazit* — the Chamber of Hewn Stone, which served as the seat of the *Sanhedrin* — as shaped *kemin basiliki gedola*, like a large basilica: an expansive chambered hall. The lottery (*payis*) was conducted in the eastern section of the chamber, while the elder of the court sat in the western section. The *kohanim* stood arranged in a circular formation described as *kemin bukhliyar* — or *kukhliyar* — a term apparently derived from the Greek word for a snail or cochlear spiral, suggesting a curved or roughly circular arrangement rather than a perfect circle.

### Lottery Procedure

The procedure, as described in the *baraita* and explained by Rashi, worked as follows. The *memuneh*, the officer appointed to oversee the lottery, would approach and remove the *mitznefet* from the head of one of the *kohanim* standing in the circle. This action designated that *kohen* as the starting point. A number would then be called out at random, and the *kohanim* would be counted off from that starting point — one, two, three — until the called number was reached. The *kohen* at whom the count ended would be designated the winner, and the distribution of tasks would proceed from him outward to the adjacent *kohanim* in sequence.

## Chamber Status

### Half & Half

From the description in the *baraita* — that the lottery was held in the eastern portion of the chamber and the elder of the court was seated in the western portion — Abaye draws an inference about the chamber's physical and legal status: *Lishkat Hagazit chetzya b'kodesh v'chetzya b'chol* — the Chamber of Hewn Stone was half in the sanctified *azara* and half outside it.

### Seating Problem

The reasoning unfolds as follows. If the chamber were located entirely within the *azara*, then *zaken yoshev b'maarava* — the elder sitting in the western section — presents an insuperable problem. The Gemara cites the principle: *ein yeshiva ba'azara ela l'malchei beis Dovid bilvad* — only kings of the Davidic dynasty may sit within the *azara*. The *Sanhedrin* elder was not a king; therefore he could not be seated in the *azara*.

### Pasuk Requirement

But if the chamber were located entirely outside the *azara* — in the *chol*, the non-sanctified space — the opposite problem arises. The lottery (*payis*) was conducted in the eastern portion of the chamber, and the verse cited earlier — *b'veis Elohim nehalech b'ragesh* — establishes that the *payis* must take place within the sanctified precinct, the *Beis Elohim*. If the entire chamber lacks *kedushah*, the lottery cannot validly be held there.

### Two Doors

Both constraints — the necessity for the lottery to be in the *kodesh* and the prohibition on sitting in the *azara* — can only be reconciled if the chamber straddles the boundary. Abaye concludes: *shenei petachim hayu lah* — the chamber had two doors. *Echad patuach bakodesh v'echad patuach bachol* — one opening into the *azara* and one opening into the non-sanctified space.

### Sanctity Principle

The Gemara invokes a well-established principle: the sanctity of a closed chamber (*lishka*) is determined not by the ground it is built on but by the space into which its door opens. *Ha-lishkot ha-benuyot bachol ufetuchot la-kodesh — tochan kodesh*: a chamber built in non-sanctified space but opening into the *azara* has the sanctity of the *azara*. Conversely, *benuyot bakodesh ufetuchot lechol — tochan chol*: a chamber built within the *azara* but opening into the non-sanctified space does not have the sanctity of the *azara*.

Given this principle, a single door would determine the entire chamber's status in one direction or the other — and either direction creates one of the two problems identified above. Two doors, with one opening into the *azara* and one opening outside, allow the chamber to be divided: the section oriented toward the *azara* door has *kedushah* and is suitable for the lottery; the section oriented toward the outer door does not have *kedushah* and is suitable for the *Sanhedrin*'s seating. And for this division to work coherently, the chamber must itself straddle the physical boundary line — built half in and half out — so that the two doors and the two sections correspond to two genuinely distinct zones.

One might ask: if the chamber had two doors, why was it also necessary for it to be physically built on the boundary? Could it not be built entirely within the *azara* and simply have one door opening outward? The Gemara's logic implies that with two doors in a single contiguous space, the default status might revert to the nature of the ground the chamber stands on, or produce some indeterminate status. The division into two halves with two corresponding doors, combined with being physically situated on the dividing line itself, is what allows the space to be cleanly and practically divided.

### Tosafot Analysis

Tosafot raises a point of conceptual precision that connects to a similar discussion elsewhere — including the question of where the *Kohen Gadol* slept on Yom Kippur eve. There are two distinct considerations: one is the *kedushah* of a space, i.e., whether it carries the sanctified status of the *azara*; the other is the actual physical ground, i.e., whether the space is literally situated within the temple precincts. Tosafot observes that when a chamber is built in the *chol* with a doorway into the *azara*, it may have the *kedushah* of the *azara* while being physically situated on non-temple ground; and vice versa.

Applied here: one might have thought that the verse *b'veis Elohim nehalech b'ragesh* requires the lottery to take place on actual temple ground — physical territory of the *Beis HaMikdash* — regardless of sanctity status. In that case, the location of the door would be irrelevant; what would matter is where the chamber is built. But Abaye's entire analysis focuses on where the door opens. This indicates, as Tosafot argues, that for *both* concerns at stake — the prohibition on sitting in the *azara* and the requirement to hold the lottery in the *Beis Elohim* — what is decisive is the *kedushah* of the space rather than the physical ground. The prohibition on sitting applies where the *azara*'s sanctity is present; the obligation to hold the lottery there also depends on *kedushah*, not on being on temple soil. The door therefore matters for both questions.

This principle applies specifically to a closed chamber — a *lishka* — which by its nature as a room is defined by the space it opens into, much as an interior room in a building is understood to belong to the apartment whose door it shares. It does not extend to large open areas, which are defined by their own physical parameters.

### Decorum Concern

Tosafot in a later passage (*sof ha-perek Elu Ne'emarin*) raises a further question: given that the *payis* had to be held in *kodesh* — in the *azara* — why was it held inside the chamber at all? Why not perform it in the open *azara* itself, which is certainly sanctified? Rabbenu Tam answers: since the lottery procedure required removing the *mitznefet* from one *kohen*'s head, it would be inappropriate for that *kohen* to stand uncovered in the *azara*. Conducting the lottery within the enclosed chamber resolved this problem.

### Circle Arrangement

Rav Yitzchak offers a complementary explanation. Because the *kohanim* were arranged in a circle — the *bukhliyar* formation — some of them would inevitably have their backs to the *Heichal*. Facing away from the sanctuary in the open *azara* would be improper; the enclosed chamber provided a setting where this concern did not apply in the same way.

An additional observation: while reviewing the physical arrangement, it is worth noting a point of orientation. The *baraita* states that the lottery was in the east of the chamber and the elder sat in the west. Given that the *Beis HaMikdash* faces east — the sanctuary is to the west — having the *Sanhedrin* seated in the western portion of a chamber straddling the boundary, with the lottery in the eastern portion, requires careful mapping against the actual layout of the *azara*. The precise geometry is complex, and different schematic reconstructions arrange the two sections somewhat differently; the key structural point — half in *kodesh*, half in *chol*, two doors — is what the Gemara establishes.

## Second Lottery

### Thirteen Tasks

The Mishnah now turns to the second lottery — the *payis* held for the *tamid*, the daily communal offering. Thirteen *kohanim* would win assignments through this lottery. The enumeration is as follows: one *kohen* performs the *shechita*; one performs the *zrika* (casting the blood on the altar); one cleans the inner altar (the incense altar); one cleans the *menora*; and nine *kohanim* carry the various parts of the animal up the ramp to the altar. These nine assignments cover: the head and right hind leg (one *kohen*); the two forelegs (one *kohen*); the tail and left hind leg (one *kohen*); the chest and windpipe (one *kohen*); the two flanks (one *kohen*); the inner organs (one *kohen*); the flour of the *minchat nesachim* (one *kohen*); the *chavitin* — the *Kohen Gadol*'s daily meal offering, half brought in the morning and half with the afternoon *tamid*, offered from his own funds but not necessarily by him personally (one *kohen*); and the wine libation (one *kohen*). That totals thirteen.

### Dispute Order

Ben Azzai, speaking before Rabbi Akiva in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua, offers a dissenting view: *derech hilucho haya karev* — the limbs should be brought up to the altar in the order corresponding to how the animal would naturally walk. The Gemara will analyze what this means, but the apparent implication is that Ben Azzai disagrees with the Mishnah's ordering of the anatomical assignments.

### One Lottery

A question is then raised: when the lottery is conducted for all thirteen *tamid* assignments, does it take the form of thirteen separate lottery draws — one per task — or a single draw in which the winner and the twelve *kohanim* adjacent to him in the circle each receive a consecutive assignment? In other words, is there one lottery with thirteen outcomes, or thirteen independent lotteries?

### Four Gatherings

The initial attempt to resolve this from the statement *arba peisos hayu sham* — there were four lotteries — proves insufficient. One might have thought: if there were thirteen separate draws within the *tamid* lottery session, the total across all four sessions would far exceed four. But Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak points out that "four" refers only to the number of *gatherings* — *arba pe'amim nichnasim lehafis* — not the number of individual draws. Within each gathering, multiple rounds could in principle occur. A definitive proof comes from the *baraita* of Rabbi Chiyya: *lo lechol avoda va'avoda mepisin* — they do not hold a separate draw for each and every task. Rather, the *kohen* who wins the *tamid* lottery draws along with him the next twelve *kohanim* in the circle — *sheneim asar echav ha-kohanim nimshachim imo* — one winner plus twelve, all assigned in sequence. *Shema mina*.

## Ketores Exception

### No Lottery

The Mishnah addresses the third lottery — for the *ketores*, the incense offering. Rabbi Yehuda rules that there was no separate lottery for the *machta*, the fire pan used to carry coals from the outer altar to the inner altar. Instead, the *kohen* who won the *ketores* lottery would say to the *kohen* standing next to him: *zacha imi bamachta* — you have won along with me the *machta*. The adjacent *kohen* was simply designated by the *ketores* winner without any additional draw.

### One Service

The Gemara explains this ruling: *shani machta u-ketores d'chada avoda hi* — the fire pan and the *ketores* are different from separately enumerated tasks because they together constitute a single unified service. The *machta* is not an independent *avoda* that happens to follow the *ketores*; it is an integral component of how the *ketores* is performed. Tasks that form one composite act do not receive a separate lottery.

### Ketores Wealth

One might have expected that precisely because the *ketores* was so extraordinarily prized, a separate lottery for the *machta* would be introduced to give more *kohanim* an independent chance. The tradition was that a *kohen* who performs the *ketores* becomes wealthy — it was considered a *segula* for prosperity. As the *Rashash* notes, this tradition apparently extended to the *kohen* who performed the *machta* as well, as the *Yerushalmi* states explicitly. Given this prestige, one might think each *kohen* deserved his own independent opportunity at either component of the service.

### Rule Confirmed

The Gemara entertains this reasoning — *salka da'atach amina natkin lah payis bifnei atzmah*, one might have thought a separate lottery should be held for each component — and then rejects it. The *baraita* of Rabbi Chiyya comes as the clinching proof: the rule is categorical. They do not hold separate lotteries for each task. The *machta*-and-*ketores* ruling is not an exception driven by their unity; it is a specific illustration of the general principle. The reason the Mishnah needed to state it explicitly for the *machta* is that one might have been moved by the exceptional desirability of the *ketores* service to institute a separate lottery in this case specifically — *kamashma lan* that no such exception was made.

## Blood Reception

### Missing Task

The Mishnah for the second lottery lists the *shochet* (the one who slaughters) and the *zorek* (the one who casts the blood on the altar) as distinct lottery assignments. But a question arises: who performs the *kabbalat hadam* — the receiving of the blood in the vessel immediately after the *shechita*? This task appears nowhere in the lottery list. It must have been bundled with one of the two named roles, but with which?

### Shochet Theory

The first possibility is that the *shochet* also performs the *kabbalah*. But the Gemara rejects this: *agav chavivuteih* — because the *avoda* is so precious to the *zorek*, he will be impatient. If the *shochet* is catching the blood in the vessel while also performing the *shechita*, there is no practical problem — but the Gemara reasons along the lines of an earlier theme: the one who does the *zrika* is so eager for his honored moment at the *mizbeach* that if the *kabbalah* is left to the *shochet*, the *zorek* will not wait for all the blood to be properly collected. He will seize the vessel before the collection is complete and rush to the altar, resulting in an incomplete *kabbalah*.

### Zorek Theory

The alternative is that the *zorek* performs the *kabbalah*. But this also raises a concern: *zimnin d'shachit zar* — sometimes a non-*kohen* performs the *shechita*, since *shechita* is one of those *avodot* that a *zar* may validly perform. If the practice were that the *shochet* receives the blood, and a *zar* happened to be the *shochet* that day, the *kabbalah* — which absolutely requires a *kohen* — would be performed by a non-*kohen*, rendering the service invalid. Assigning the *kabbalah* to the *zorek*, who is always a *kohen*, prevents this possibility.

### Twelve Faucets

The Gemara introduces a remarkable piece of evidence. The Mishnah records that Ben Katin installed twelve faucets on the *kiyor* — the laver used for *kidush yadayim v'raglayim*, the washing of hands and feet required before *avoda*. He did this so that the twelve *kohanim* engaged in the *tamid* service could all wash simultaneously, without queuing.

### Faucet Proof

The difficulty is that the Mishnah lists thirteen *kohanim* for the *tamid* — not twelve. Twelve faucets for thirteen *kohanim* seems one short. The resolution: the *shochet* does not need to wash his hands before performing *shechita*. Since *shechita* may be performed by a *zar*, it does not require *kidush yadayim v'raglayim* even when performed by a *kohen*. Therefore only twelve of the thirteen *tamid kohanim* require a faucet — the *shochet* does not. Twelve faucets for twelve washers: the numbers align.

But here is the proof for the *kabbalah* question: if the *shochet* were also performing the *kabbalah*, he would be performing a task that requires *kohen* status, and accordingly would need to wash his hands before the *kabbalah*. He would then need a faucet — and the count would be thirteen. The fact that Ben Katin built exactly twelve faucets demonstrates that the *shochet* does not perform the *kabbalah*. The *kabbalah* must be performed by the *zorek*, who in any case washes his hands for the *zrika*. *Ella lav shema mina zorek mekabel* — you must conclude that the *zorek* performs the *kabbalah*. This is also practically sensible: the *zorek* stands beside the altar with the vessel, catches the blood, and then proceeds directly to cast it.

### Tamid Source

The definitive textual proof comes from the Mishnah in Tamid itself. Rav Acha the son of Rav Ulla cites it before Rav Ashi: *shachat hashochet v'kibel hamekabel, uba lo lizrok* — the *shochet* slaughtered, the *mekabel* (receiver) received the blood, and then *he* — the same *mekabel* — went to cast it. The one who caught the blood is the one who went to throw it. The *zorek* and the *mekabel* are one and the same person. *Shema mina*. The discussion will continue on the following daf with the question of the order in which the parts of the *tamid* were brought up to the altar.
