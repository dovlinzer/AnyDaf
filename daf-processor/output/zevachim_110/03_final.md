# Zevachim 110 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kezayit Ketores & Kli

### Kezayit Ketores Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמָנָא. מָר סָבַר: קְבִיעוּת מָנָא מִילְּתָא הִיא, וּמָר סָבַר: לָאו מִילְּתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** by placing them **in a vessel.** One **Sage,** Rabbi Eliezer, **holds** that the **designation of** a measure of incense larger than an olive-bulk by placing it in **a vessel is** a significant **matter** that renders one obligated to burn all the incense that was placed there. Therefore, one who then burned only an olive-bulk of that incense outside the courtyard is exempt. **And** one **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds** that it **is nothing** and does not render one obligated to burn all the incense that was placed in the vessel. Therefore, one who then burned an olive-bulk of that incense outside the courtyard is liable.

The *daf* opens in the midst of a *gemara* debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim* concerning one who offers a *kezayit* of *ketores* outside the *Beis Hamikdash* when it does not constitute the full *shiur*. The *gemara* has been working through various formulations of this dispute, attempting to locate its precise parameters.

### Two Kezayisim in Vessel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָבַע שִׁשָּׁה לְפַר, וּמָשַׁךְ מֵהֶן אַרְבָּעָה וְהִקְרִיבָן בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב; שֶׁרְאוּיִין לְאַיִל.
>
> **Translation:** if **one designated** in a vessel **six** *log* of wine **as** a libation to accompany the sacrificing of **a bull,** which is the required amount, **and** then **removed four** *log* **from** it **and brought those** four *log* as a libation **outside** the courtyard, he would be **liable, as** a libation of four *log* of wine is **fit for** the sacrificing of **a ram** (see Numbers 28:14).

The most recent explanation settled upon is a case involving two *kezaisim* placed together in a single *kli*. Under normal circumstances, Rabbi Eliezer would agree with the *chachamim* that a single *kezayit* is sufficient for *haktara* — for offering incense on the *Mizbeach Hazahav* — and that one would be *chayav* for performing that act outside. The one exception where Rabbi Eliezer requires more than a *kezayit* is *Yom Kippur*. The question, however, is whether placing two *kezaisim* together in a *kli* alters the *shiur*. Rabbi Eliezer holds that the *kli* now unifies them into a single unit, such that a *kezayit* is no longer sufficient if taken from this combined quantity; liability outside would require the full amount. The *chachamim*, by contrast, hold that the mere fact of being placed in a vessel together does not redefine the operative *shiur*.

### Kviusa Demana

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָבַע אַרְבָּעָה לְאַיִל, וּמָשַׁךְ מֵהֶן שְׁלֹשָׁה וְהִקְרִיבָן בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב, שֶׁרְאוּיִין לְכֶבֶשׂ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, if one **designated** by placing in a vessel **four** *log* of wine **for** a libation to accompany the sacrificing of **a ram and** then **removed three** *log* **of** wine from it **and brought those** three *log* as a libation **outside** the courtyard, he would be **liable, as** three *log* of wine is a **fit** libation **for** the sacrificing of **a lamb** (see Numbers 28:14).

The *gemara* encapsulates this dispute: *mar savar kviusa demana milta hi* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that fixing the material within the vessel is halakhically significant and establishes it as a new unit — *umar savar lav milta hi* — the *chachamim* hold that it is not significant, and a *kezayit* remains sufficient regardless.

## Wine Nesachim & Vessel

### Rava Six Log Wine

The application continues with a final test case: if one filled a vessel with less than the full required measure, the analogy reverses. The point is consistent: in the view of the *chachamim*, placing a larger quantity in a vessel does not elevate the minimum threshold for liability.

### Rams & Lambs Shiur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָסְרוּ כׇּל שֶׁהוּא, וְהִקְרִיבָן בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** But if those three *log* **were lacking any amount, and one brought them** as a libation **outside** the courtyard, he would be **exempt** because less than three *log* of wine is never a fit libation.

The same logic extends further: if one filled a vessel with four *log* for a ram and withdrew three *log* and offered them outside, one is likewise *chayav* — *reuyin lakeves* — because three *log* is the valid measure for a lamb. The point is consistent: in the view of the *chachamim*, placing a larger quantity in a vessel does not elevate the minimum threshold for liability.

### Chasar Minimum

There is, however, a limit. If the full minimum amount was lacking — *chasar kol shehu v'hikrivan bachutz* — meaning not merely that a portion was withdrawn but that the required minimum of three *log* was not present to begin with, then one would be *patur*. Rashi clarifies that this refers to falling short of the minimum three *log* threshold, not merely to falling short of the six; anything less than three renders one exempt. This sets the stage for the more complex discussion of *chasar* that follows.

## Ketores vs Wine

### Rav Ashi Ketores

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: נִיסּוּךְ מֵהַקְטָרָה לָא יָלְפִי רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** in defense of Abaye's opinion: Indeed, as is indicated by the *baraita*, **the Rabbis do not derive** the measure for liability for **pouring a libation** outside the courtyard **from** the measure for liability for **burning** incense, which are two different rites performed in the outer sanctum.

Rav Ashi now addresses a point raised in yesterday's *gemara*. The *gemara* had proposed that one rationale for holding someone *chayav* for a *kezayit* of *ketores* of *Yom Kippur* offered outside is an analogy: since one is *chayav* for a *kezayit* of ordinary *ketores* offered outside, the same should apply to the *ketores* of *Yom Kippur*. This reasoning was challenged on the grounds that we do not generally apply *shiurim* across categories of outside offerings. We do not say that because a *kezayit* suffices for meat or *ketores*, the same *kezayit* suffices for wine — for wine the minimum is three *log*. Rav Ashi now makes this point explicit: *nisuch mehaketores lo yalpi rabbanan* — the *rabbanan* do not derive the *shiur* for *nisuch* from *haktara* of *ketores*. Even though both are assessed in the context of outside offerings, the categories differ too fundamentally for the *shiur* of one to govern the other.

### Ketores to Ketores

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַקְטָרָה מֵהַקְטָרָה יָלְפִי, אַף עַל גַּב דְּחוּץ מִפְּנִים.
>
> **Translation:** they do derive the measure for liability for **burning** the incense of the outer sanctum **from** the identical rite of **burning** of the incense of the inner sanctum, **even though** it involves deriving the *halakhot* of a rite performed in the **outer** sanctum **from** one performed in the **inner** sanctum.

Nevertheless, and this is the resolution, *haketores mehaketores yalfei af al gav de-chutz mi-pnim* — one is prepared to derive the minimum *shiur* for *Yom Kippur* *ketores* from the *shiur* for ordinary *ketores*, even though one is offered in the *Heichal* and the other in the *Kodesh Hakodashim*. The distinction between the outer and inner categories is far less significant than the distinction between *ketores* and *nisuch*. Both are *ketores*, and therefore the cross-application is defensible. This confirms that the answer offered previously remains viable.

## Chasar Kometz

### Kometz Levona Lacking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלָּן שֶׁחָסְרוּ. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: חֶסְרוֹן דְּחוּץ, שְׁמֵיהּ חֶסְרוֹן אוֹ לָא שְׁמֵיהּ חֶסְרוֹן?
>
> **Translation:** § After enumerating various items that are burned entirely on the altar, the mishna states: **And** with regard to **any of these** offerings **that were lacking** any amount, if one sacrifices it outside the courtyard, he is exempt. Concerning this ruling, **a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Is **a lack that** occurs to an offering **outside** the courtyard **considered a lack** in order to exempt one who sacrifices the remainder outside the courtyard? **Or** is it **not considered a lack?**

The *gemara* now turns to a new *mishna*. The *mishna* states: *ve-chulan she-chasru* — in all cases where the *matir* was lacking. The context is the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the *chachamim* regarding outside offerings. Rabbi Eliezer holds that one is *chayav* only when offering the complete *matir* outside; the *chachamim* hold that a *kezayit* of the *kometz* or *levona* suffices for liability. The *mishna* then records that the *chachamim* agree that if any of the *matir* was lacking — if a portion of the *kometz* was missing — then even they concede that one is not *chayav* for offering a *kezayit* of what remains. This is because without the integrity of the full *kometz*, the *matir* is no longer meaningful; it no longer functions as a *matir* in any legitimate sense. Similarly, Rashi notes, if the *shirayim* of the *mincha* were lacking, one would not be *chayav* for offering the *kometz* outside.

One might find this initially puzzling. After all, *chasar* seems to fall within the category of *pesulo ba-Kodesh*, and we have established as a general principle that if *pesulo ba-Kodesh* — if a *korban*'s disqualification can occur within the *Beis Hamikdash* — then *im alu lo yerdu*, it cannot be removed from the altar, and one is *chayav* for offering it outside. Why should *chasar* be treated more severely than other *pesulim*? This question is precisely what the *gemara* will address.

### Chasar Chutz vs Pnim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי אָמְרִינַן: כֵּיוָן דִּנְפַק – אִיפַּסְלוּ לְהוּ, מָה לִי חָסֵר מָה לִי יָתֵר; אוֹ דִילְמָא, יוֹצֵא דְּאִיתֵיהּ בְּעֵינֵיהּ – אִין, דְּלֵיתֵיהּ בְּעֵינֵיהּ – לָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Do we say** that **once** an offering **emerges** from the courtyard **it is** in any event **disqualified,** and yet the Torah deems one liable for offering it there, so **what** difference is there **to me** if there is an additional disqualification of being **lacking** and **what** difference is there **to me** if it is still **complete? Or perhaps** it is only with regard to **emerging** from the courtyard, **where it is** still **in its original state,** that **yes,** one is liable despite the fact that it was disqualified by emerging from the courtyard, but where it is **not in its original state,** one would **not** be liable.

The *gemara* raises the question: *chisaron de-chutz shme chisaron o lo shme chisaron?* What if the *kometz* was not lacking before it was taken outside, but became lacking only after it was removed from the *Beis Hamikdash*? Does that *chasar* disqualify it in the same way? Is one still *patur* for offering a *kezayit* of what remains?

### Yotzei & Pesul

The *gemara* presents two ways of framing the question. On one side: *mi amrinan keivan d-nafik ipsalu lehu* — once the *kometz* is taken outside, it is already disqualified by virtue of *yotzei*. And yet one is *chayav* for bringing it outside — that is the very nature of the *chiyuv* of *ha'alas chutz*. So if we do not disqualify it for the *pesul* of *yotzei*, why should we disqualify it for the *pesul* of *chasar*? *Mah li yotzei mah li chasar* — what is the operative distinction between one *pesul* and another, once we have already crossed that threshold?

On the other side, one might argue: *yotzei de-isaya be-einaya* — in the case of *yotzei*, the object is still intact and present; the *pesul* is a label attached to it, but the entity as a whole remains. *Chasar*, by contrast, does not merely label the *kometz* as defective — it changes the physical reality of what exists. Once something is missing, the entity is fundamentally altered, and perhaps that is why one cannot be *chayav* for bringing what remains.

### Abaye & Rav Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, תָּא שְׁמַע: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר פּוֹטֵר עַד שֶׁיַּקְרִיב אֶת כּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Come** and **hear** a resolution from the mishna: **Rabbi Eliezer deems** him **exempt unless he sacrifices the whole of** any one of these items outside the Temple. It is apparent then that the offering must remain complete.

Abaye attempts to resolve the question from the *mishna*'s statement that Rabbi Eliezer exempts until the full *matir* is brought: *Rabbi Eliezer poter ad she-yakriv es kulan*. However, Rabbah bar bar Chana challenges Abaye: how can one derive anything from Rabbi Eliezer when the question pertains to the *chachamim*'s position? Abaye responds that he is not relying on a deduction from Rabbi Eliezer himself, but on an explicit tradition received from Rav: *b'feirush shmia li minei d'Rav* — the *chachamim* dispute Rabbi Eliezer only in a case where the *kometz* is intact, *de-isya b'inei*. If the *kometz* has a *chisaron*, however, *modei lei* — the *chachamim* agree with Rabbi Eliezer that one would not be *chayav*, because one no longer has a functional *matir*. Tosafot notes that this tradition from Rav is at least implicit in the *mishna* itself.

### When Lacking Occurred

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאו דַּחֲסַר בַּחוּץ? לָא, דַּחֲסַר בִּפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to bring a proof from here: Was Rav **not** referring to a case **where it became lacking outside** the courtyard? If so, it is evident that even according to the Rabbis a lack that occurs outside is considered a lack. The Gemara rejects this: **No,** he was referring to a case **where it became lacking inside** the courtyard. Accordingly, this mishna cannot serve as a proof.

Yet even this tradition does not settle the original question. The *gemara* asks: *lav d'chasar b'chutz?* — Is this tradition perhaps referring to a case where the *chasar* occurred outside the *Beis Hamikdash*? The *gemara* answers: *lo, d'chasar b'pnim* — no, we are speaking of a *chasar* that occurred inside, within the *Beis Hamikdash*. In that case the *kometz* was already compromised as a *matir* before being taken out. The question of *chasar b'chutz* — where the *kometz* was intact when removed and only became lacking outside — remains unresolved. The *gemara* attempts once more to press the language of the *mishna*, *ve-chulan she-chasru v'hikrivan bachutz patur*, asking whether this refers to *chasar b'chutz* — but again the response is that the *mishna* is clearly describing *chasar b'pnim*, and the case of *chasar* that occurs only after removal from the *Beis Hamikdash* remains an open question.

## Mincha & Kometz Mix

### Mincha Not Komtzed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִנְחָה שֶׁלֹּא נִקְמְצָה, וְהִקְרִיבָהּ בַּחוּץ – פָּטוּר. קְמָצָהּ, וְחָזַר קוּמְצָהּ לְתוֹכָהּ, וְהִקְרִיבוֹ בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If there is **a meal offering** from **which a handful was not removed, and one sacrificed it outside** the Temple courtyard, he is **exempt** from liability, because until the handful is actually removed it is not fit to be burned on the altar inside the Temple. But if a priest **took a handful from it and** then **returned its handful into** the remainder of the meal offering, **and one sacrificed** the entire mixture **outside** the courtyard, he is **liable,** as once the handful has been removed it is fit to be burned on the altar inside the Temple, and one is liable for offering it up outside even though it is mixed into the remainder.

The *gemara* transitions to the next *mishna*. *Mincha shelo nikmetza v'hikriva bachutz patur* — a *mincha* that was not *komtzed* and was brought outside in its entirety renders one exempt. The reason is that an uncomtzed *mincha* is not *rauy l'pesach ohel moed* — it is not fit for offering at the entrance of the *Ohel Moed* — and thus does not meet the threshold required for liability outside.

Before returning to the next clause, the *gemara* completes a loose end from the previous *mishna*. The case there involved one who wrapped the *imurin* in meat and offered them outside on the altar. The *mishna* held one *chayav*, and the *gemara* asked: *amai v'ha'ika chatzitza* — why should one be *chayav* if the meat interposes between the *imurin* and the fire? Three answers are given. Shmuel says: the case is where one turned them over, so the *imurin* came into direct contact with the fire. Rabbi Yochanan says: even if they were not turned over, the *mishna* follows Rabbi Shimon, who holds that even placing something on a rock constitutes a valid act of offering on a *bama*, and therefore direct contact with fire is not required. Rava says: meat and meat do not constitute a *chatzitza* for one another — *min bemino eino chotetz* — and therefore the act is valid and one is *chayav*.

### Kometz Nullification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְאַמַּאי? (לִיבַטְּלִי) [לִיבַטְּלוּ] שִׁירַיִם לְקוֹמֶץ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks about the final clause: **But why** is he liable? **Let** the **remainder** of the meal offering, which is certainly the majority of the mixture, **nullify the handful.**

Returning to the *mishna*: *kimtza v'chozar kimtza l'tocha v'hikriva bachutz chayav* — if one took the *kometz* from the *mincha*, then dropped it back in and mixed it together, and subsequently offered the combined mixture outside, one is *chayav*. Why? Because although this mixed *mincha* would be irregular in the *Beis Hamikdash*, if it were placed on the *mizbeach* it would not be removed — and therefore it is considered as though the *kometz* had been placed there. One would be *yotzei* in the *Beis Hamikdash*, and correspondingly *chayav* for doing so outside.

The *gemara* raises an obvious challenge: *v'amai livatlu shirayim l'kometz?* — why should the *kometz* not be nullified by the majority of the *mincha* once it is dropped back in? If the *shirayim* constitute a majority, they should nullify the *kometz*, which would mean one has not effectively brought the *matir* at all and should be exempt.

### Haktara Linked

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: נֶאֶמְרָה הַקְטָרָה בַּקּוֹמֶץ, וְנֶאֶמְרָה הַקְטָרָה בַּשִּׁירַיִם; מָה הַקְטָרַת קוֹמֶץ – אֵין קוֹמֶץ מְבַטֵּל חֲבֵירוֹ, אַף הַקְטָרַת שִׁירַיִם – אֵין שִׁירַיִם מְבַטְּלִין קוֹמֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said:** A term of **burning is stated with regard to the handful** removed from the meal offering, **and** a term of **burning is stated with regard to the remainder** of the meal offering. With regard to the handful, referred to by the Torah as "the memorial part," it is written: "And the priest shall burn the memorial part upon the altar" (Leviticus 2:2), and with regard to the remainder of the meal offering it is written: "Do not burn it as a fire to the Lord" (Leviticus 2:11). This provides a verbal analogy that teaches that **just as** with regard to the **burning of** the **handful,** if two handfuls are mixed together one **handful does not nullify another, so too,** with regard to the **burning of** the **remainder,** if the remainder and the handful are mixed together, the **remainder does not nullify** the **handful.**

Rabbi Zeira resolves this: *ne'emar haktara b'kometz v'ne'emar haktara b'shirayim* — the Torah uses the language of *haktara* both with respect to the *kometz* and with respect to the *shirayim*, the latter in the sense of a prohibition against burning them. This shared language creates a halakhic linkage between the two.

### Ein Olin Mevatlin

The underlying principle is *ein olin mevatlin zeh es zeh* — things that go up on the altar do not nullify one another. When two *kometzos* from different *minchos* are mixed together and placed on the *mizbeach*, even if one is larger, neither is nullified, because both are designated for the altar. The same logic, extended by the linguistic link to *haktara* of *shirayim*, means that the *shirayim* cannot nullify the *kometz* either. The *kometz* therefore retains its identity within the mixture, and offering it outside — even in this mixed state — makes one *chayav*.

## Kometz & Levona

### One Matir Chayav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַקּוֹמֶץ וְהַלְּבוֹנָה, שֶׁהִקְרִיב אֶת אֶחָד מֵהֶן בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר פּוֹטֵר עַד שֶׁיַּקְרִיב אֶת הַשֵּׁנִי. אֶחָד בִּפְנִים וְאֶחָד בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The burning of both the handful and the frankincense permits the consumption of the remainder of the meal offering by the priests. With regard to **the handful and the frankincense,** in a case **where one sacrificed** only **one of them outside** the Temple courtyard, he is **liable. Rabbi Eliezer exempts** from liability one who burns only one of them **until he** also **sacrifices the second.** Since the remainder of the meal offering becomes permitted only once both have been burned, he considers each one alone to be an incomplete offering, and he holds one is not liable for sacrificing only one of them. Rabbi Eliezer concedes that if one sacrificed **one inside** the courtyard **and one outside** the courtyard, he is **liable.**

The next *mishna* states: *hakometz v'halevona shehikriv es echad mehen bachutz chayav* — if one offered outside either the *kometz* or the *levona*, which together serve as the two *matirin* for a *mincha*, one is *chayav*. This is the classic case of *chatzi matir*. The *chachamim* do not require the full *matir* and therefore hold one *chayav* for bringing even one of the two components outside.

### Full vs Partial Matir

Rabbi Eliezer, predictably and consistently, rules otherwise: *Rabbi Eliezer poter ad sheyakriv es hasheini* — Rabbi Eliezer exempts until the second *matir* is also brought. He requires the complete *matir* before liability attaches. The *mishna* then addresses the case of one *matir* inside and one outside: *echad bifnim v'echad bachutz chayav* — if one *matir* was already offered inside the *Beis Hamikdash*, leaving only the second, and one then brings that second one outside, even Rabbi Eliezer agrees one is *chayav*. At that point, what is brought outside constitutes the entirety of what remains of the *matir*, and thus the full *matir* has been engaged.

### Two Levona Spoons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנֵי בְּזִיכֵי לְבוֹנָה, שֶׁהִקְרִיב אֶת אֶחָד מֵהֶם בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר פּוֹטֵר, עַד שֶׁיַּקְרִיב אֶת הַשֵּׁנִי. אֶחָד בִּפְנִים וְאֶחָד בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The burning of two bowls of frankincense permits the consumption of the shewbread. With regard to the **two bowls of frankincense,** in a case **where one sacrificed** only **one of them outside** the courtyard, he is **liable. Rabbi Eliezer exempts** from liability one who burns only one of them **until he** also **sacrifices the second,** since the shewbread becomes permitted only once both bowls of frankincense are burned. Rabbi Eliezer concedes that if one sacrificed **one inside** the courtyard **and one outside** the courtyard, he is **liable.**

The *mishna* applies the same debate to the *Lechem Hapanim*. The two spoons (*bezichin*) of *levona* that are burned on *Shabbos* serve as the *matir* permitting the *kohanim* to eat the showbread. If one burns one of the two spoons outside: the *chachamim* hold one *chayav*, while Rabbi Eliezer, consistent with his position, exempts until both are offered — *Rabbi Eliezer poter ad sheyakriv es hasheini*. Again, if one spoon was already burned inside the *Beis Hamikdash*, bringing the second one outside renders one *chayav* even according to Rabbi Eliezer. It is worth noting parenthetically that these *bezichin* may be less than a *kezayit* in quantity; however, the identity of each as a full spoon — a distinct, complete vessel — may itself confer sufficient halakhic significance to substitute for the *shiur* of *kezayit*.

## Matirin Status Question

### Rav Yitzchak Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא: קוֹמֶץ, מַהוּ שֶׁיַּתִּיר כְּנֶגְדּוֹ בַּשִּׁירַיִם? מִישְׁרָא שָׁרֵי, אוֹ (קלושי) [אִקְּלוֹשֵׁי] מִיקְּלֵשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna is based on the fact that it is permitted for the priests to consume the remainder of the meal offering only once both the handful and the frankincense have been burned. With regard to this, **Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa raises a dilemma:** If one burned the **handful** but not the frankincense, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to **whether** this **will permit** the consumption of **the corresponding** half **of the remainder?** Since the burning of both the handful and the frankincense permits the entire remainder, it seems that each one of them affects half of the remainder. Accordingly, it is unclear whether burning one of them will entirely **permit** half of the remainder, **or** whether it will merely **weaken** the prohibition concerning the remainder, and it will still be prohibited to eat any of it.

Rav Yitzchak bar Nafcha poses a penetrating conceptual question: *kometz mahu sheyatir k'negdo b'shirayim?* — when one of the two *matirin* of a *mincha* is offered on the *mizbeach*, what is the halakhic status of the *mincha* at that moment? Has it become half-permitted? Or does the first *matir* merely weaken the overall prohibition without actually permitting any portion?

One might frame the question this way, reading as Tosafot does: if the *kometz* is burned on the *mizbeach* and the *levona* has not yet been burned, is the *mincha* now in a state where half of it is permitted and half is not — though one cannot identify which half — such that eating two *kezaisim* might trigger liability of *malkos*? Or alternatively, does the first *matir* operate not by carving out half but by diminishing the overall prohibition, such that only when both are brought is the *mincha* fully permitted?

### Rabbi Meir Half Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר: מְפַגְּלִין בַּחֲצִי מַתִּיר – מִישְׁרֵא שָׁרֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion did Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa raise this dilemma? **If** it is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Meir, who says: One can render** an offering ***piggul*** by having *piggul* intention **during half** of two acts that together **permit** the offering for consumption, i.e., during either one of them, then it should be obvious that the offering of the handful alone should entirely **permit** half of the remainder. The fact that Rabbi Meir holds that intention during just one of the acts can render the offering *piggul* demonstrates that he holds that each act alone has the power to permit part of the offering. It is apparent, then, the dilemma was not raised according to Rabbi Meir's opinion.

The *gemara* asks: according to whom is this question being posed? If it is according to Rabbi Meir, who holds *mapiglin bechatzi matir* — that a *piggul* intention during one of the two *matirin* renders the *korban piggul* — then the very concept of *piggul* being operative through half a *matir* implies that the half-*matir* actually accomplishes something. It must effect some permissive change on at least part of the *mincha*, for otherwise the *piggul* thought at that *avoda* would be meaningless. Thus, for Rabbi Meir, it seems that bringing one *matir* does indeed make half the *mincha* permitted — *mishra shari*.

### Rabbanan No Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן, דְּאָמְרִי: אֵין מְפַגְּלִין בַּחֲצִי מַתִּיר – לָא מִישְׁרָא שָׁרֵי, וְלָא [אִקְּלוֹשֵׁי] מִקְּלֵשׁ (קָלֵישׁ)!
>
> **Translation:** **If** it is **in accordance with** the opinion **of the Rabbis, who say: One cannot render** an offering ***piggul*** by having *piggul* intention **during half** of two acts that together **permit** the offering for consumption; rather, one must have *piggul* intention during both acts, then it is evident that neither act alone has the power to affect the offering, and so the burning of the handful alone should **neither permit** any of the remainder **nor weaken** the prohibition that applies to it. It is apparent, then, the dilemma was not raised according to the Rabbis' opinion.

If the question is posed according to the *rabbanan*, who hold *ein mapiglin bechatzi matir* — that a *piggul* thought during only one of the two *matirin* does not make the *korban piggul* — then there is no evidence from *piggul* that the partial *matir* accomplishes anything at all. One cannot determine from their position whether the first *matir* permits half, weakens the prohibition, or has no effect whatsoever until both are brought — *lo mishra shari v'lo michlush kalish*.

### Rabbi Eliezer Tayku

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן דְּהָכָא. מַאי? מִישְׁרָא שָׁרֵי, אוֹ (קָלֵישׁ) [אִקְּלוֹשֵׁי] מִקְּלֵשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it must be that the dilemma was raised **in accordance with** the opinion **of the Rabbis that** is taught **here** in the mishna. They hold that one is liable even for sacrificing outside only the handful or only the frankincense. Evidently, they hold that each one alone has the power to affect the status of the remainder. Accordingly, Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa asked concerning a case where one of them is burned on the altar in the Temple, **what** is the *halakha*? Does it entirely **permit** half of the remainder, **or** does it **weaken** the prohibition concerning the entire remainder?

The *gemara* suggests that the question might be posed according to Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that one is not *chayav* for bringing outside until both *matirin* are offered — a position structurally parallel to the *rabbanan*'s view that *ein mapiglin bechatzi matir*. For Rabbi Eliezer, there is similarly no basis to conclude that either *matir* alone accomplishes anything prior to the completion of both. The *gemara* then concludes: the question is really meaningful only according to the *rabbanan* of the *mishna* — those who hold one is *chayav* for bringing a single *matir* outside. For them it seems clear that one *matir* does have some effect; the unresolved issue is the nature of that effect. And on that the *gemara* leaves the question standing: *tayku*.

## Dam & Shirayim

### Partial Dam Chayav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַזּוֹרֵק מִקְצָת דָּם בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: אַף הַמְנַסֵּךְ מֵי הֶחָג בֶּחָג בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב. רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁיָרֵי הַדָּם (שֶׁהִקְרִיבוּ) [שֶׁהִקְרִיבָן] בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who sprinkles part of** the **blood** of an offering, e.g., one sprinkling instead of four, **outside** the Temple courtyard is **liable. Rabbi Elazar says: So too, one who pours as a libation water** consecrated for the libation **of the festival** of *Sukkot*, **during the Festival, outside** the courtyard, is **liable. Rabbi Neḥemya says:** For the **remainder of the blood** of an offering that was supposed to be poured at the base of the altar and **that** instead **one sacrificed outside** the courtyard, one is **liable.**

The final *mishna* of the page opens with a ruling that is intuitively straightforward: *hazorek miktzas dam bachutz chayav* — one who throws even part of the blood outside the *Beis Hamikdash* is *chayav*. This does not mean all the blood of the animal, but rather that even performing only some of the required blood applications — even just one corner of the altar — renders one liable.

### Rabbi Eliezer Dam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רָבָא: וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּדָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rava says:** Rabbi Elazar generally holds that one is liable for sacrificing outside only when he sacrifices a complete offering, **but Rabbi Elazar concedes with regard to** the sprinkling of the **blood** of an offering, that one is liable for sprinkling outside even part of the blood, e.g., one sprinkling instead of four. This is clearly the *halakha* with regard to offerings whose blood is sprinkled on the external altar, as a single sprinkling renders such an offering valid (see 36b), and it can be regarded as a complete offering. Rabbi Elazar concedes that this is the *halakha* even with regard to offerings whose blood is sprinkled on the inner altar, despite the fact that such offerings are valid only once all the sprinklings have been completed.

Notably, Rabbi Eliezer does not dispute this *mishna*, even though he generally requires the full *matir* before assigning liability. The explanation is that for blood, even in a *chatas* which has four required applications, we rule that any single application of blood is itself a *matir*. Since one application is *bediavad* sufficient as a *matir*, Rabbi Eliezer agrees that one is *chayav* for even a single application outside. Rava makes this point explicit — *u'modeh Rabbi Eliezer bedamim* — though it is largely implicit in the *mishna*'s silence on Rabbi Eliezer's dissent.

### Rava Chiddush Dam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמְרִים: מִמָּקוֹם שֶׁפָּסַק הוּא מַתְחִיל.
>
> **Translation:** The fact that Rabbi Elazar concedes this point is apparent from that **which is taught** in a mishna (*Yoma* 60a) with regard to the numerous sprinklings of blood performed in the Holy of Holies, upon the Curtain, and on the inner altar, as part of the Yom Kippur Temple service: If during the sprinklings the blood spills and it is necessary to bring the blood of a second animal in order to complete them, **Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon say: From the place that** the High Priest **stopped** sprinkling the blood of the first animal, there **he begins** to sprinkle the blood of the second animal; it is unnecessary to repeat any of the sprinklings that have already been performed. From this ruling it is apparent that each sprinkling is considered an independent and complete act of service, and one will be liable for even a single act of sprinkling done outside the Temple.

However, Tosafot identifies Rava's true *chiddush* as the following: one might have thought that on *Yom Kippur*, where every individual blood application is *meakev* — where each and every application is indispensable to the atonement — Rabbi Eliezer would require all of the applications to have been done before assigning liability for a single one outside, since without all of them the *matir* is never complete. This would be a compelling argument. Rava's innovation is that even in that case, Rabbi Eliezer would hold one *chayav* for a single application offered outside.

### Where Spilling Begins

The source for this is a *braita*: Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon say *mimakom shepasak hu matchil* — if some of the blood spilled during the service on *Yom Kippur*, one does not start over from the beginning; one resumes from where one left off. This demonstrates that even an incomplete set of applications accomplishes something — each application achieves its own portion of the *avoda* and need not be redone. This is why even one application is meaningful, even though the full *matir* has not yet been completed: it has permanently discharged that portion of the service. Accordingly, one would be *chayav* for bringing even a single application outside.

The *mishna* also records Rabbi Nechemiah's position: *sheyarei hadam shehikrivan bachutz hayav* — one who pours the *shirayim* hadam — the remnant blood poured at the base of the altar — outside the *Beis Hamikdash* is also *chayav*. Rabbi Nechemiah holds that the pouring of the *shirayim* is *meakev*, an indispensable component of the service. It is therefore still a form of *hakravah*, still falls within the category of *rauy l'pesach ohel moed*, and thus incurs liability when performed outside. This point will be developed further in the following *daf*.

## Nisuch Hamayim

### Water Libation Chayav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: אַף הַמְנַסֵּךְ מֵי הֶחָג [בֶּחָג] בַּחוּץ. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מְנַחֵם יוֹדְפָאָה: רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר – בְּשִׁטַּת רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא רַבּוֹ אֲמָרָהּ, דְּאָמַר: נִיסּוּךְ הַמַּיִם דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״וּנְסָכֶיהָ״ – בִּשְׁנֵי נִיסּוּכִים הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, אֶחָד נִיסּוּךְ הַמַּיִם וְאֶחָד נִיסּוּךְ הַיַּיִן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Rabbi Elazar says: So too, one who pours as a libation water** consecrated for the libation **of the festival** of *Sukkot*, **during the Festival, outside** the courtyard, is liable. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Menaḥem Yodfa'a: Rabbi Elazar said that** *halakha* **in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, his teacher, who says: The water libation** on *Sukkot* is a mitzva **by Torah law. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Akiva says** concerning the verse: "Beside the daily burnt offering, its meal offering, **and its libations"** (Numbers 29:31), the fact that the Torah makes reference to "libations" in the plural indicates that **the verse is speaking of two** types of **libations. One** is **the water libation,** which is unique to the festival of *Sukkot*; **and** the other **one** is **the wine libation,** which always accompanies the daily offering. If the water libation was not a mitzva by Torah law, one would not be liable for pouring it as a libation outside the Temple courtyard.

Rabbi Eliezer adds his own ruling in the *mishna*: *af hamenasech mehachag bachutz chayav* — one who pours the water of *Sukkos* outside the *Beis Hamikdash* is also *chayav*. The question immediately arises: what is the *chiddush*? If one is already *chayav* for *nisuch hayayin* — for the wine libation offered outside — why would water be any different? The *gemara* will explain.

### Rabbi Akiva Source

Rabbi Yochanan, citing Rabbi Menachem of Yudfaya, explains: *Rabbi Eliezer beshitas Rabbi Akiva rabbo amar* — Rabbi Eliezer is following the position of his teacher Rabbi Akiva. A *braita* records that Rabbi Akiva derives from the word *u'nesacheha* — written in the plural regarding the libations of the sixth day of *Sukkos* — that the Torah is speaking of two libations: one of wine and one of water. *Bishnei nesachim hakasuv medaber echad nisuch hamayim v'echad nisuch hayayin.* The *chiddush* of Rabbi Eliezer, then, is that *nisuch hamayim* is *d'oraysa* — a Torah obligation — and because it is *d'oraysa*, one is *chayav* for performing it outside the *Beis Hamikdash*.

This raises a striking point noted by Tosafot: the very framing of this derivation implies that there is an opinion holding *nisuch hamayim* to be merely Rabbinic. Tosafot finds this astonishing. Even setting aside the problem of *chullin ba'azarah* — the prohibition against bringing non-sacred items into the Temple precincts — the very idea that the Rabbis would institute a major and elaborate ritual within the *Beis Hamikdash*, *lechatchilah*, without any Torah warrant is deeply striking. A ritual that is not recognized by the Torah would render the water *chullin*, and yet it would be poured as though it were a sacred libation. Tosafot suggests that perhaps the centrality of water to the themes of *Sukkos* — it being a time of prayer for rain — was so compelling that the Rabbis felt it necessary to establish a ritual that would cause the Jewish people to be remembered before God in the days of rain. The theological implications of instituting an unsanctioned ritual in the *Mikdash* precisely to attract divine attention are, as Tosafot's language implies, not without difficulty.

### Reish Lakish Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אִי – מָה לְהַלָּן שְׁלֹשֶׁת לוּגִּין, אַף כָּאן שְׁלֹשֶׁת לוּגִּין?! וְהָא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר ״מֵי הֶחָג״ קָאָמַר! אִי – מָה לְהַלָּן בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה, אַף כָּאן בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה?! [וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר] ״בֶּחָג״ קָאָמַר!
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: If** Rabbi Elazar derives the mitzva of the water libation through the derivation taught by Rabbi Akiva, according to which both the wine libation and water libation are derived from the same word, then he should hold that **just as there,** with regard to wine, one is liable only if he pours **three *log*, so too here,** with regard to water, one should be liable only if he pours **three *log*. But** in the mishna, **Rabbi Elazar says** simply: **Water of the festival** of *Sukkot*, which seems to include any amount. Furthermore, he should hold that **just as** with regard to pouring a libation of wine outside the Temple, one is liable for pouring a libation **during the rest of the days of the year** and not only on *Sukkot*, **so too,** with regard to pouring a libation of water, one should be liable for pouring a libation **during the rest of the days of the year. But** in the mishna **Rabbi Elazar says** that one is liable only if he pours the water libation **during the Festival.** It is apparent that Rabbi Elazar derived the mitzva of the water libation from a different source.

Reish Lakish presses Rabbi Yochanan with two challenges. The first: *im al hallan shloshah lugin af kan shloshah lugin* — if Rabbi Eliezer is basing himself on Rabbi Akiva, who derives *nisuch hamayim* from the analogy to wine, then the minimum *shiur* for the water should be three *log*, just as for wine. Yet Rabbi Eliezer implies one is *chayav* even for a minimal amount — *kol shehu* — which cannot be consistent with the analogy to wine.

### Sukkot Only Question

The second challenge: *v'im al hallan bish'ar yemot ha-shanah af kan bish'ar yemot ha-shanah* — if Rabbi Eliezer bases the obligation on Rabbi Akiva's derivation, which derives from the wine libation that applies throughout the year, then *nisuch hamayim* should likewise apply throughout the year, and one should be *chayav* for pouring water outside at any time. Yet Rabbi Eliezer says one is *chayav* only on *Sukkos*. Tosafot has an alternative textual reading here: the word *u'nesacheha* appears in the context of the sixth day of *Sukkos*, so according to Rabbi Akiva's derivation, the obligation would logically begin only on the sixth day, or perhaps only apply to the sixth and seventh days. The *gemara*'s challenge, in Tosafot's reading, is therefore *sh'ar yemot ha-hag* — why is Rabbi Eliezer *chayav* on all the days of *Sukkos* if Rabbi Akiva's derivation would limit it to day six and onwards? Either way, Rabbi Eliezer's position cannot be consistently explained by reference to Rabbi Akiva.

### Halakha Lemoshe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִישְׁתְּמִיטְתֵּיהּ הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי; דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי נְחוּנְיָא אִישׁ בִּקְעַת בֵּית חוֹרְתָן: עֶשֶׂר נְטִיעוֹת, עֲרָבָה וְנִיסּוּךְ הַמַּיִם – הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי.
>
> **Translation:** **That which Rabbi Asi says escaped him, as Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Neḥunya, a man of the valley of Beit Ḥortan:** The *halakha* of **ten saplings,** the practice of taking **a willow** in the Temple during *Sukkot*, **and** the obligation to perform **the water libation** during *Sukkot*, each of these is **a *halakha*** transmitted **to Moses from Sinai.** According to Rabbi Asi, the mitzva of the water libation is not derived from the same source as the wine libation, and it is possible that there will be differences in the *halakhot* that apply to them.

The *gemara* resolves both challenges with the word *ishtamitayh* — Rabbi Yochanan forgot. Specifically, he forgot another statement taught in his own name, as cited by Rabbi Assi: *ha d'amar Rabbi Assi amar Rabbi Yochanan mishum Rabbi Nehunia ish Bik'at Beis Hortan — esser nityos, aravah, v'nisuch hamayim halakhah l'Moshe mi-Sinai*. The water libation — together with the *aravah* ritual of *Sukkos* and a ruling regarding young plantings — is a *halakhah l'Moshe mi-Sinai*. It is not derived from *u'nesacheha* and is not dependent on Rabbi Akiva's analogy to wine. Rabbi Eliezer, according to this, holds that *nisuch hamayim* is *d'oraysa* because it is a *halakhah l'Moshe mi-Sinai* — an independent received tradition. Under this model, the minimum amount need not be three *log*, since the tradition itself may specify a smaller quantity; and the obligation applies throughout *Sukkos*, since that is how the tradition was received. Rabbi Yochanan, when he attributed Rabbi Eliezer to Rabbi Akiva, forgot that he himself had transmitted this alternative source.

## Water Minimum & Kli

### Three Log Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמְנַסֵּךְ שְׁלֹשֶׁת לוּגִּין מַיִם בֶּחָג בַּחוּץ – חַיָּיב. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר (בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן) אוֹמֵר: אִם מִלְּאָן לְשֵׁם חַג – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One who pours as a libation three *log* of water during the festival** of *Sukkot* **outside** the courtyard is **liable. Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says: If he filled** a service vessel with the three *log* in order to consecrate **them for the sake of** the **Festival,** he is **liable.**

A *braita* now states: *ha-menasekh shloshah lugin mayim be-hag ba-hutz hayyav* — one who pours three *log* of water on *Sukkos* outside the *Beis Hamikdash* is *chayav*. Rabbi Eliezer, in Tosafot's textual reading, concedes this: he says *im la'an l'sheim chag chayav* — if one filled the vessel with three *log* designated for the *chag*, then one would indeed be *chayav* for three *log*. His normal position is that even a *kol shehu* is sufficient, but he concedes the three *log* threshold when the vessel was filled with three *log* for the purpose of the *chag*.

### Kli Sharet Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: בְּיֵשׁ שִׁיעוּר בְּמַיִם קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the difference **between** these two opinions? **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: They disagree with regard to** whether **there is** a precise **measure of water** that can be consecrated as a libation. The first *tanna* holds that even if one fills a service vessel with more than three *log*, the water is thereby consecrated. Therefore, if one then pours at least three *log* of that water outside the courtyard, he is liable. Rabbi Elazar holds that if one attempts to consecrate more than three *log*, the consecration of the water is ineffective. Therefore, if one then pours three *log* of that water as a libation outside the courtyard, he is not liable.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak explains the underlying dispute: *b'yesher b'mayim kamiflegi* — they debate the minimum *shiur* of the water libation itself. The *chachamim* hold, perhaps following Rabbi Akiva's analogy to wine, that the minimum is three *log*, and therefore one is not *chayav* outside for less than three. Rabbi Eliezer holds that even a *kol shehu* suffices for *nisuch hamayim* inside the *Beis Hamikdash*, and therefore even the smallest amount incurs liability outside. However, Rabbi Eliezer's own principle of *kviusa demana* — that placing a quantity in a vessel establishes it as the halakhic unit — applies here as well. If one filled a vessel with three *log* intending that amount for *nisuch hamayim*, then three *log* is what is now established, and one would be *chayav* only for that full amount. The positions thus dovetail neatly: the *chachamim* maintain three *log* as an absolute minimum; Rabbi Eliezer says *kol shehu* in general, but agrees that three *log* becomes the binding *shiur* when the vessel was filled with that amount.

### Midbar Nesachim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: בְּקָרְבוּ נְסָכִים בַּמִּדְבָּר פְּלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** They disagree with regard to whether one is liable for pouring a libation outside the courtyard that was not first consecrated in a service vessel. This dispute is based on a disagreement **with regard to** whether wine **libations were offered** in the Tabernacle **in the wilderness** before the Jewish people entered Eretz Yisrael. The Gemara will soon explain the logical connection between the two issues.

Rav Pappa offers an alternative explanation, which is admittedly cryptic on its face: *b'kirbu nesachim bamidbar pligi* — the dispute concerns whether *nesachim* were brought in the *midbar*. Rashi bridges this to the practical question as follows. The Torah, in commanding the *nesachim* accompanying animal offerings, says *ki tavo'u el ha'aretz* — when you come to the land. The plain reading suggests that *nesachim* were not brought in the *midbar* and were reserved for *Eretz Yisrael*, implying that they belong to a centralized *Beis Hamikdash* context and were not offered on private *bamot*. If, however, one holds that *nesachim* were in fact brought in the *midbar*, then *ki tavo'u el ha'aretz* must mean something else: it instructs that upon entering the land, even when there is no central *Mishkan* and private *bamot* are permitted, *nesachim* should still be brought — even on those private altars.

This matters halakhically because offerings that are valid on a private *bama* do not require a *kli sharet* for sanctification. If *nesachim* were brought in the *midbar* — and hence on private *bamot* — then *nesachim* are valid without a *kli sharet*. Since what is valid on a *bama* during a time when *bamot* are prohibited generates *chiyuv* outside, one would be *chayav* for *nisuch* even without a *kli sharet*. Rabbi Eliezer holds that a *kli sharet* is required — the water must be filled in a sacred vessel, *mil'an l'sheim chag* — because without that sanctification it is not *rauy l'pesach ohel moed* and there is no liability. The *chachamim* hold that since *nesachim* were brought in the *midbar* and hence on private *bamot* without a *kli sharet*, a *kli sharet* is not required, and one is *chayav* even without it.

### Bamot Question

On this reading, the dispute is not primarily about three *log* versus *kol shehu* at all. Both sides might agree on the minimum amount; the disagreement concerns the role of the *kli sharet* as a prerequisite for liability. Whether *nesachim* were ever offered on private *bamot* thus becomes the axis of the entire debate.

### Ravina Water Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: בִּלְמֵדִין נִיסּוּךְ הַמַּיִם מִנִּיסּוּךְ הַיַּיִן פְּלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said:** Everyone agrees that wine libations are valid even if they are not first consecrated in a sacred service vessel. Therefore, one who pours a wine libation outside the courtyard is liable even if it was not first consecrated in a service vessel. **They disagree with regard to** whether the liability for pouring **a water libation can be derived from** that of **a wine libation.** The first *tanna* holds that it can; Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, holds that it cannot.

Ravina offers yet a third explanation of the dispute: *b'limadim nisuch hamayim mi-nisuch hayayin pligi* — the debate concerns whether *nisuch hamayim* can be derived from *nisuch hayayin* in the relevant respect. Everyone agrees that *nisuch hayayin* was practiced on private *bamot* without a *kli sharet*, and that it therefore generates *chiyuv* outside even without one. The question is whether *nisuch hamayim* follows the same rule. *Nisuch hamayim* was never offered on a private *bama* — it is a communal *Beis Hamikdash* ritual. One might therefore argue that it requires a *kli sharet*, since we cannot draw the parallel to wine. The *chachamim* hold that just as wine generates liability without a *kli sharet*, the same applies to water: one is *chayav* even without it. Rabbi Eliezer holds that water cannot be so derived from wine, and without a *kli sharet* there is no liability. Thus on Ravina's reading, both the question of *kli sharet* and the question of whether water follows wine stand at the heart of the disagreement, and the *daf* concludes here.
