# Shabbat 147 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Marit Ha'ayin

### Forbidden in Private

The daf opens at the very bottom of 146b, transitioning from the laws of food and opening packages to a range of practical *shabbat* applications. The final point of the *mishna* addressed a case where someone's clothes became soaking wet — whether from rain or from walking through water — and upon returning home wishes to remove them. He may lay them out in the sun to dry, but not in a visible location, since passersby will think he did laundry on *shabbat*.

Building on this, the *gemara* cites a ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: *kol makom she-asru Chachamim mipnei marit ha'ayin, afilu be-chadrei chadarim asur* — wherever the Sages forbade something on account of appearances, it is forbidden even in one's innermost private chambers. The concern for how an action appears is not dependent on whether anyone is actually watching.

### Tannaitic Dispute

The *gemara* immediately questions this from our *mishna*, which says one may spread out the garments in the sun *but not in front of people* — implying that the prohibition is specifically in public. The *gemara* resolves this by noting that it is in fact a *tannaitic* dispute: a *baraita* records that while the first opinion limits the prohibition to public settings, Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon extend the prohibition even to private chambers. The ruling we follow, requiring the prohibition even in private, is thus the more stringent *tannaitic* position.

It is worth noting, however, that this is not without dissent in the *rishonim*. The *Hilchos Gedolos* (*Behag*) rules that the prohibition applies only in public. We rule like the overwhelming majority position — even in private — but there is an interesting contemporary discussion regarding a case where the worst a bystander could think is that one is violating a *rabbinic* prohibition, as opposed to a biblical one. Some *poskim* hold that in such a case the concern for appearances applies only in public.

### Context & Intent

A critical distinction in the laws of *marit ha'ayin* is that the operative question is not whether anyone happens to be watching, but rather: *were* someone to observe this, what would it appear to be? Context can therefore dissolve the concern entirely. If one's raincoats are dripping wet and one hangs them in the bathroom to dry, no one would think laundry was being done on *shabbat* — that simply is not a *marit ha'ayin* problem. The context makes the situation self-explanatory. This is qualitatively different from a leniency based on the mere absence of observers: we do not permit something just because no one happens to be looking, but we do not prohibit it when context makes clear that no violation is being committed or suggested.

### Personal Sensibilities

One might ask why *marit ha'ayin* should be prohibited even in genuinely private circumstances where no human observer is present. One answer is pragmatic: one can never be entirely certain of privacy, and the concern applies as a precautionary matter. But there is a deeper reason as well. The concern for appearances is not solely about what other people will think, nor is it only about preventing others from drawing incorrect conclusions or becoming lenient about *aveiros*. It is also about one's own sensibilities and sense of personal boundaries. Performing an act that has all the outward appearance of being forbidden can itself compromise one's own relationship to the limits of *halacha*, regardless of whether any external observer is present. This is likely among the reasons why the prohibition holds even in private.

## Laundering & Garments

### Shaking Tallit

The *gemara* continues at the bottom of the page. Rav Huna rules: *hamenayer talit beShabbat chayav chatat* — one who shakes out his *tallit* on *shabbat* is liable for a *chatat* offering. This is the *melacha* of *kibus*, laundering, and the ruling is unambiguous.

Rashi explains this as *hamenayer talit min ha'afar* — shaking dust out of the garment; that, he says, constitutes its laundering (*libun*). Tosafot disagrees and cites Rabbeinu Chananel's reading that the reference is to dew (*tal*) — shaking the moisture out of the garment. It should be noted that in our printed text of Rabbeinu Chananel the reading actually aligns with Rashi's, referring to dust rather than dew, even if Tosafot cites him otherwise.

### Laundering Without Water

The deeper debate between Rashi and Tosafot concerns whether *libun* — laundering — can occur without the use of water. According to Rashi, vigorous shaking that effectively removes embedded dust or dirt from a garment constitutes *kibus* even without water. According to Tosafot, without water there is no *kibus*, and therefore shaking off dew is the better illustration because the water component is present.

Interestingly, Rashi himself elsewhere seemed to require water as part of *kibus*, and Rabbi Akiva Eiger points to the apparent tension. Here, however, Rashi takes the position that the effective removal of embedded dirt constitutes laundering regardless of whether water is involved.

From the perspective of Tosafot, one might genuinely ask: if a garment is soaking wet and you shake the moisture off, in what sense has it been laundered? The *gemara* elsewhere discusses *sechita* — squeezing — and there are opinions that squeezing water out of a garment constitutes *kibus* even without asking whether the garment is cleaner afterward. This may reflect a view that laundering is defined not only by the result but by participation in the stages of the laundering process: submerging in water, squeezing, drying. Each stage, on this view, is prohibited in its own right even if the garment does not emerge cleaner. Yet if one were to compare the two: taking soaking wet clothes and spreading them out in the sun to dry — as in our *mishna* — seems more clearly an act within the laundering process than simply shaking off surface moisture. The practical ruling follows Rashi overwhelmingly, which carries implications in both directions: *kibus* does not require water, but by the same token, a simple shaking off of surface moisture may not be the classic case.

### Qualifications

The *gemara* then introduces several important qualifications that substantially narrow the scope of the prohibition. First, *velo amru ella bechadti* — this applies only to new garments. Old garments (*atikei*) are not subject to the same concern. The reasoning is that the significance of the cleaning process is diminished when the garment itself is worn and unremarkable; there is no dramatic transformation, and the act does not rise to the level of meaningful *kibus*. This is not simply a matter of intent — even if one did not care much, that does not generally permit a prohibited act — but rather that without the typical laundering process (water, detergent, wringing), the prohibition only applies where one produces a dramatic, meaningful improvement in the garment's appearance. An old, worn garment does not meet that threshold.

Second, *velo amrin ela be-uchmin* — the prohibition applies particularly to black garments. White or red garments (*chivrei ve-sumkei*) are not subject to the same concern, because dust and dirt are far more visible on black, and thus the contrast between soiled and cleaned is far more pronounced. One might add that if the dirt itself were dark, the analysis would presumably shift even for a light-colored garment.

Third, *vehu dekpid alei* — all of this applies only where one is genuinely particular about cleanliness. Here too, the category of *kapid* is doing significant work: if one is not sufficiently *machpid* that the garment be clean to a meaningful degree, the act does not rise to the level of *kibus*. One might suggest that *kapid* means: one would not wear the garment in its dirty state, such that cleaning it constitutes a genuine transformation of use. The question of how these qualifications map onto contemporary fabrics, garment types, and types of soiling is one the *gemara* does not fully resolve.

### Ulla's Challenge

The *gemara* then relates that Ulla came to Pumbedita and observed rabbis shaking out their garments on *shabbat*. He declared: *kama machalin rabanan shabata* — the rabbis are desecrating *shabbat*! Rav Yehuda responded by instructing the students to go shake out their garments right in front of Ulla — demonstrating publicly that the local practice held this to be permitted. The justification: *anan lo kapdinan midi* — we are not particularly *machpid* about it. The implication is that they fell below the threshold of *kapid* that would render the act prohibited.

### Lo Kapdin

Similarly, Abaye was standing before Rav Yosef and asked for his hat. Rav Yosef saw that there was *tal* — either dew or, according to Rabbeinu Chananel, a stain of some kind — on the hat and hesitated to hand it over, apparently concerned that Abaye might shake it off on *shabbat*. Rav Yosef then said: *nefoz shadi* — shake it off, throw it. *Anan lo kapdinan midi* — we are not *machpid* about such things. Again, some middle level of concern that falls short of *kapid* does not trigger the prohibition.

### Surface vs. Deep

As a general principle, it emerges that lightly brushing something off the surface of a garment — where it is merely resting and not embedded — is not *kibus*. The prohibition begins when one is extracting dirt that is embedded within the fabric. The language of the *gemara*, however — *hamenayer*, shaking out — does sound like something more surface-level than deep scrubbing, a point that is not fully resolved.

## Carrying vs. Wearing

### Folded Tallit

Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *hayotzei betallis mekupeles munachas al kesefo beShabbos chayav chatat* — one who goes out on *shabbat* with a *tallit* folded up and placed on his shoulder is liable for carrying. It should be noted that *tallit* here does not refer to the ritual four-cornered garment with *tzitzis*, but to a general type of cape or cloak that was worn in that era. The normal manner of wearing this garment was to drape it so that it hung low behind the back. If instead one bundles it all up and has it sitting around one's neck or draped entirely over one shoulder — not flowing down in its normal configuration — that is no longer considered wearing; it is considered carrying. Even though the garment is technically on the body, the configuration determines the *halakhic* category.

### Merchants & Pouches

A *baraita* confirms this: *socharei kesus hayotzin betallisos mekupallos umunachos al kispheihem beShabbos chayavin chatas* — garment merchants who go out with their cloaks folded over and resting on their shoulders on *shabbat* are liable for *chatat*. The *baraita* clarifies that it is not only merchants who are subject to this rule — it is anyone who wears a *tallit* in this configuration. The merchants are singled out because it is their characteristic manner of transporting goods, which illustrates that the folded-over configuration is inherently one of carrying rather than wearing.

Similarly, a storekeeper who goes out with coins bound up in his cloak — essentially a money pouch fashioned from the garment — is liable. Again, the rule extends to anyone who carries objects in this fashion, not only storekeepers. This manner of bearing objects is defined as carrying, not wearing.

### Runners Exception

There is an exception: *rattanim* — messengers or runners — may go out on *shabbat* with their *sudar* (a type of scarf or head covering) folded up and resting on their shoulders. And again, the *baraita* notes this is not limited to runners — anyone may do so — but runners are cited as the characteristic case because this is their normal manner of wearing such a garment.

Why is this different from the merchants' case? The distinction lies in whether the configuration constitutes a normal mode of *wearing* or merely a mode of *transporting* the object on the body. For runners, wearing the *sudar* in this gathered or rolled configuration is a recognized manner of wearing it — analogous to the different ways one might wrap a scarf — and therefore it qualifies as wearing, not carrying. One might have thought that because runners specifically adopt this configuration to keep the garment from falling off during rapid movement, it is a functional choice rather than a genuine wearing position. The *chiddush* is that even so, if this is a recognized way in which people wear such a garment, it is defined as wearing.

### Horkenos Story

Rav Yehuda related that Horkenos the son of Rabbi Eliezer ben Horkenos once went out on *shabbat* with his *sudar* on his shoulder, with a string wound around his finger — apparently to help secure the garment. When the matter came before the Sages, they ruled: even without a string wound around the finger, this is a recognized manner of wearing the *sudar* and requires no additional securing. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak stated in the name of Rav Chisda that this is the accepted *halacha*: one need not wind any string around one's finger, for simply wearing the *sudar* in this folded configuration is sufficient.

### Folding & Creasing

The *gemara* then records that Ulla came to the house of Assi bar Hini, and those present asked: *mahu laasot marzev beShabbat* — what is the law regarding making a *marzev* on *shabbat*? In the context of garments, Rashi understands *marzev* to mean creating permanent creases or folds in a garment — deliberately shaping it in a lasting way. Tosafot, together with the Rambam, understand the question differently: it concerns whether folding a garment in a particular configuration constitutes a manner of *wearing* it or whether it is merely a way of *bundling* it so it does not fall off, which would make it carrying. The discussion, on Tosafot's reading, is entirely continuous with the preceding analysis.

Ulla replied that Rebbi had ruled it is forbidden to make a *marzev* on *shabbat*. The *gemara* then asks: what is a *marzev*? Rav Zera explains: *kisei bavlayita* — the pleated pouches of the Babylonians; i.e., pleated or creased folds in the fabric.

A vivid illustrative exchange follows. Rav Yirmiyah was sitting before Rav Zera and asked: what if I do it *this* way — is it permitted? Forbidden, said Rav Zera. What about *this* way? Also forbidden. The precise configurations are lost to us since the text only records the exchange without the visual demonstration.

Rav Papa then offers a unifying principle: *kol adaata delechnufei, assur; kol deleitnaot, shari* — anything done with the intent to bundle the garment up is forbidden; anything done to make it look attractive is permitted. According to Rashi, *lechnufei* means creating permanent creases in the garment as an end in itself, as opposed to simply arranging it attractively for one's appearance at that moment. According to the Rambam and Tosafot, *lechnufei* means bundling the garment up to keep it off the ground or to prevent it from falling off while moving — which, as argued above, renders it carrying rather than wearing. If the purpose is that the garment looks its best in that configuration on one's body, it is wearing; if the purpose is to bundle or secure it, it is carrying.

The point at which a garment goes from being worn to being carried on one's body is genuinely difficult to define visually. One may still be wearing the garment while bearing it in a non-standard configuration — the question is when that configuration crosses the line. The Rambam's view, for instance, is that lifting a hem to avoid a puddle is not problematic, because one is still wearing the garment. But rolling the entire garment up and changing its fundamental configuration — so that it no longer hangs and drapes in any normal sense — may be carrying. The analogy to a *tallit* worn to *shul* in a place without an *eruv* is instructive: wearing it draped in its normal manner is permissible; wearing it bundled up around the neck is not.

### Rebbi's Folded Tallit

Rav Dimi reported: once Rebbi — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi — went out to the field with the two edges of his *tallit* folded and resting on his shoulders. Yehoshua ben Ziruz — identified as the son of the father-in-law of Rabbi Meir — said to Rebbi with diplomatic indirection: would not Rabbi Meir render one liable for a *chatat* in this situation? Rebbi was apparently taken aback — *dikdik Rebbi Meir ad kan?* — Rabbi Meir would be so precise as to require a *chatat* even in this case? Nevertheless, *shilshel Rebbi talliso* — Rebbi lowered his *tallit* and redraped it in its normal configuration, thus conforming to Rabbi Meir's position.

When Ravin later came, he reported an alternate version of the story: it was not Yehoshua ben Ziruz but Yehoshua ben Kupsai, and the challenge was not invoking Rabbi Meir but Rabbi Akiva — *b'zu lo chiyyeiv Rebbi Akiva chattas?* Rebbi's reaction was the same — *dikdik Rebbi Akiva ad kan?* — and he again redraped his *tallit*.

A third version, brought by Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yehuda, holds that the story was not about Rebbi actually wearing his *tallit* this way, but about Rebbi posing the question in the abstract — whether this would be permitted. This version apparently addresses the discomfort of the narrative in the other versions, which could be read as implying that Rebbi had unwittingly violated *shabbat* according to Rabbi Meir or Rabbi Akiva.

## Bathing & Towels

### Hot Water Immersion

The *gemara* next addresses bathing on *shabbat*. The *mishna* mentions bathing in cave water or in the hot springs of Tiberias. The *gemara* notes that cave water is compared to the springs of Tiberias — both are hot — which suggests that immersion in hot water is being discussed. The general rabbinic rule is that immersion in hot water heated by fire is forbidden on *shabbat*, lest it lead to going to bathhouses and heating water on *shabbat*. Hot springs that are naturally heated — like Mei Tverya — are permitted. Accordingly, the *gemara* explains that *harochetz* — "one who bathed" — is formulated in the past tense (*bediavad*) to indicate that one was not supposed to have immersed in fire-heated hot water, even if it was heated before *shabbat*.

### Single Person Towel

The *mishna* then addresses drying off: *afilu be-eser aluntiyos, lo yevi'em b'yado* — even if one used ten towels to dry off, a single person may not bring them home. The concern is *sechita*: carrying a wet towel creates a risk that one will come to squeeze it. Even ten towels, each of which absorbs less water individually, do not resolve the concern when a single person is carrying them.

### Multiple People Towel

However: *aval asara benei adam, mistapkim b'aluntis achas* — ten people who dry themselves with a single towel, which becomes thoroughly wet, *mevi'in otan b'yadam* — may bring it home in their hands. The reason: since many people are involved, they will remind one another not to squeeze the towel. This is a general principle in *halakha* — when a group acts together, we are less concerned about inadvertent violations, because the collective serves as a check on individual lapse. This principle parallels the teaching at the end of the first *perek* of the tractate, where placing the *korban pesach* in the oven near *shabbat* is permitted in part because a group is involved and they remind one another.

## Oil & Body Care (III)

### Anointing Oil

The *mishna* continues: *sakhnin u'memashmeshin* — one may anoint the body with oil and spread it gently. This is permitted on *shabbat*.

### Vigorous Massage

However, *aval lo misamlin* — vigorous rubbing or massage is forbidden. Such intense physical manipulation is either associated with *refuah* — healing — or constitutes *uvda d'chol*, a weekday-type activity. Even absent oil, a vigorous massage would similarly be problematic.

A *baraita* specifies: one may anoint the stomach (*bnei mei'in*) and massage it gently on *shabbat*, provided one does not do it in the manner one does on a weekday — so as to avoid *uvda d'chol*. The question of which activities fall into the category of *uvda d'chol* is not always transparent; here, what exactly constitutes the weekday manner is ambiguous. If the concern were *refuah*, the framing as *uvda d'chol* would be puzzling; and if it is *uvda d'chol*, the specific mention of the stomach — which has medicinal connotations — is noteworthy.

### Scraping Skin

*Velo misgarerin* — one may not use a scraper on the skin. After anointing with oil, it was common to use a metal scraper to remove the oil from the body. Such scrapers, illustrated vividly in ancient archaeological finds, resembled small spoon-shaped implements in various sizes mounted on a ring. Using them on *shabbat* is forbidden, apparently because of *refuah* or *uvda d'chol*. An exception applies, as Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules, if the legs are caked with dried mud or excrement (*tit uvetzoah*) — in that case, *gored kedarko* — one may scrape it off in the normal manner without concern, since this is simply cleaning rather than a therapeutic act on the skin itself.

The *gemara* notes that the mother of Shmuel bar Yehuda had a silver scraper made for him, specifically for use on *shabbat*. Rashi understands this as a specially designated *shabbat* implement. The reasoning may be that a dedicated, ornamental silver scraper is less evocative of the weekday professional procedure, thus reducing the *uvda d'chol* concern — though one may fairly observe that the act itself remains similar regardless of the instrument's material.

## Bathing Locations

### Muddy Rivers

*Ein yordin leKardumisa* — one may not descend into the river Kardumisa. The reason: *mishum pika* — because of the mud, one may slip and fall, causing one's garments to become entirely soaked with muddy water. The concern is the resulting need to wring them out or the general dishevelment and damage that would follow.

## Medical Treatments

### Broken Bones

*Ve'ein machzirin es hashever* — one may not reset a broken bone on *shabbat*. This falls under the prohibition of *refuah* on *shabbat*. Strikingly, the *mishna* categorizes this as a standard *issur refuah* rather than as a life-threatening emergency requiring the overriding of *shabbat*. In practice today, however, we would immediately classify such a patient as a *choleh kol haguf*, and all the relevant prohibitions of *refuah* would not apply. Our scope of applying *issur refuah* is far narrower than the *mishna*'s categorical treatment would suggest.

### Dislocated Limbs

*Mi shenifreka yado veraglo* — someone with a dislocated arm or leg may not plunge the limb into cold water, which would apparently reduce swelling and pain. However, *aval rochetz hu kedarko* — one may wash normally. And: *ve'im nisrape nisrape* — if one heals in the process, so be it — provided the act itself does not take on the overt character of a therapeutic procedure. Again, practically, we would consider any person with a dislocated limb to fall well within the category of *choleh kol haguf*, and the analysis would proceed accordingly. The *mishna*'s placement of these cases within the category of *issur refuah* remains somewhat difficult to explain within modern *halakhic* practice.

## Bathing & Towels (II)

### Shower vs. Immerse

The *gemara* now draws out a distinction from the *mishna*'s phrasing. The *mishna* speaks of bathing in cave water *similar to* the hot springs of Tiberias; the *gemara* notes: just as Mei Tverya is hot, so too here. This implies hot water is involved, yet immersion in fire-heated hot water is rabbinically forbidden on *shabbat*. The *gemara* resolves: *harochetz diavad in, lechatchila lo* — "one who bathed" implies that this was *bediavad*; one should not have immersed in fire-heated hot water to begin with.

From this a further inference follows: *mikal d'lehishtatef kol gufo afilu lechatchila shapir dami* — pouring or showering hot water over one's entire body would be permitted even *lechatchila*. The rabbinic decree (*gezeirat merchata'ot*) was directed at immersing in hot water in a bathhouse context, which would lead to heating water on *shabbat*. It was not directed at showering.

A *baraita* records the dispute explicitly. Rabbi Meir forbids showering (*lehishtatef*) in either hot or cold water. Rabbi Shimon permits it entirely. Rabbi Yehuda holds that hot water is forbidden and cold water is permitted. The practical *halakha* follows the view that showering — as opposed to immersing — is technically not included in the original decree. This is the basis on which contemporary discussions of hot water use in apartment buildings on *shabbat* proceed, with various qualifications depending on the specific setup. The prohibition as applied to showering is a separate question involving concerns about *sechita* (hair), the use of liquid soap, and related issues — but the fundamental *gezeirah* of *merchata'ot* relates to immersion.

The *gemara* then returns to the drying question: *venistapeg afilu be-asara alunti'ot* — one may dry off even with ten towels. The *gemara* explains that both the opening clause and the closing clause of the *mishna* teach a *chiddush*. The opening clause teaches that even with ten towels — where each one holds relatively little water — since it is one individual, there remains a concern he will come to squeeze them, and he may not carry them home. The closing clause teaches that even with one towel shared by ten people — which becomes quite wet — since they are many, they remind one another, and there is no concern they will come to squeeze it.

### Bringing Towels Home

A *baraita* is cited: *mistapeg adam be-aluntit u-menichah be-chalon* — one may dry off with a towel and place it in the window to dry. This is not considered *kibus* (laundering), and people understand the towel became wet from use on *shabbat* rather than from washing. The *baraita* adds that one may not hand the towel to bathhouse attendants (*ulyarin*), because they are suspect to wring it out.

### Rabbi Shimon Lenient

Rabbi Shimon takes a more permissive position: *mistapeg be-aluntit achat u-mevia be-yado le-toch beito* — one may dry off with a single towel and carry it home. This disagrees with the *mishna*'s ruling that one person may not carry towels home. Abaye asked Rav Yosef: how do we rule? Rav Yosef responded by listing the authorities who permit it: Rabbi Shimon, Rebbi, Shmuel, and Rabbi Yochanan. Given such a consensus, the ruling is *muttar*.

The *gemara* then questions how Rabbi Yochanan can rule against a *stam mishna* — Rabbi Yochanan's general principle being that we rule in accordance with an anonymous *mishna*. The resolution: Rabbi Yochanan's version of the *mishna* attributed the stringent ruling to Ben Hachinai by name, not as an anonymous ruling. Having it attributed removes it from the status of *stam mishna*, and the majority view permits bringing the towel home.

### Students Story

Rebbi himself is cited from a *baraita*: when we were learning Torah from Rabbi Shimon in Tekoa — presumably Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai — we would carry oil and towels from courtyard to roof and from roof to an enclosed yard until reaching the wellspring where we bathed on *shabbat*. The *gemara* understands this as evidence that each individual could carry his own towel. Tosafot note that the reasoning may be connected to the fact that students of a rabbi are assumed to follow their master's practice, and therefore Rebbi's personal involvement implies that each individual was permitted to do so. Shmuel's ruling is recorded via Rav Yehuda: one may dry oneself and bring the towel home. Rabbi Yochanan's ruling is recorded by Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba: the *halacha* is that one may dry oneself and bring the towel home.

## Carrying vs. Wearing (II)

### Scarf Below Shoulders

The *gemara* returns to the question of wearing a *sudar* in a way that does not constitute carrying. Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *lemata mikispayim* — it must be tied or arranged below the shoulders (specifically, below the shoulder blades). If the garment is not secured below that point, it is not considered properly worn.

Rava applied this practically to the inhabitants of Mechuza. When they were required to bring garments to the troops on *shabbat* — a compulsory service that created an unavoidable carrying concern — Rava told them: *sharvivu behu lemata mikispayim* — drape the garments down below your shoulders. By ensuring the garments hang below the shoulder blades, they are being worn rather than carried, thus minimizing the *chillul shabbat* as much as possible.

## Oil & Body Care (III)

### Method Debate

The *gemara* returns to the question of anointing on *shabbat* and the dispute about how to do so in a manner that is not *uvda d'chol*. Rabbi Chama bar Chanina rules: *sach ve-achar kach memashmesh* — first anoint and then rub, because on weekdays the order is reversed — first rub and then anoint. By reversing the order one avoids doing it in the weekday manner. Rabbi Yochanan rules: *sach u'memashmesh be-vas achas* — anoint and rub simultaneously, rather than doing them sequentially as one does during the week.

The nature of the *uvda d'chol* concern here is admittedly unclear. The classic cases of *uvda d'chol* involve either activities associated with *melachos* or activities associated with professional labor. What exactly makes the sequential anointing-and-rubbing process resemble weekday labor is not self-evident. It may be that specifically regarding the stomach (*bnei mei'in*), the concern shades into *refuah*, which would explain the emphasis on doing it differently than on a weekday. But if the concern were *refuah*, one might expect the language of *uvda d'chol* to be replaced by or supplemented with *refuah* language. The category remains somewhat opaque.

## Bathing Locations (II)

### Hot Springs Healing

Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: it is forbidden to stand on the floor of a *dyumeset* on *shabbat*, because it causes one to sweat (*me'amelet*) and thereby heals. The concern is not the sweating per se but the deliberate use of the *dyumeset*'s properties as a therapeutic process — which constitutes a *maaseh refuah* on *shabbat*. Whether something that can also be done in a non-therapeutic context would be permitted even by a sick person if done without medicinal intent is a legitimate question; context and intent would presumably be relevant.

### Dyumeset Waters

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: the period during which the *dyumeset* is therapeutically beneficial is twenty-one days, and *Atzeret* — *Shavuos* — is included in that count. The *gemara* asks: is *Shavuos* the beginning or the end of the twenty-one day period? A proof is attempted from a statement of Shmuel: all beverages are salubrious from the period of the *Zevach* — i.e., from Pesach — until *Shavuos*. This implies that *Shavuos* is the end-point of the beneficial period. However, the *gemara* deflects the proof: perhaps beverages are more beneficial when the world is cooler, and *Shavuos* marks the end of the cool season. But the *dyumeset* is effective because of its steam and heat, and therefore it may be more beneficial as the world gets hotter — meaning *Shavuos* could be the beginning of its effective period rather than the end. The question is left unresolved.

### Rabbi Elazar Story

Rabbi Chelbo then introduces an *aggadic* observation: the wine of Prugeita and the waters of Dyumeset *kipchu aseret hashvatim mi'Yisrael* — they caused the ten tribes to be cut off from Israel, meaning that these great indulgences led to a life of excess that caused them to abandon Torah and their spiritual heritage.

Immediately illustrating this principle, the *gemara* relates: Rabbi Elazar ben Arach happened to come to Prugeita and was drawn after its exceptional wine. *V'imshich batreihu* — he was pulled in its wake. *I'akar talmudo* — his learning was uprooted. He forgot everything he had learned. When he finally returned, he arose to read from the *Sefer Torah* and wished to read the *pasuk* *Hachodesh hazeh lachem* — "this month is to you," the very first commandment given to *Klal Yisrael*. Instead, he read *Hacheresh haya libam* — "their hearts became stopped up," like a deaf-mute. The words look similar in script, and his processing of the text had so deteriorated that he misread even this foundational verse.

The rabbis prayed for him, and his learning was miraculously restored. Yet the story is a stark illustration of the dangers of indulgence and isolation. It is noteworthy that what he read — *hacheresh haya libam* — is not merely a misreading but an unwitting self-description: his heart had become blocked, unable to receive Torah.

### Torah Community

The *gemara* connects this to the *mishna* in Pirkei Avos where Rabbi Nehorai teaches: *hevei gole le'makom Torah* — exile yourself to a place of Torah. Do not remain where it is comfortable and indulgent; be prepared to give up creature comforts for an environment of Torah learning. And: *ve'al tomar she'hi tavo acharecha* — do not say that Torah will follow you wherever you go, that students will come to you. Why? *She'chaveirecha yekaymuha beyadecha* — your colleagues are what maintain your Torah in your hands. One cannot be self-sufficient; one needs the environment, the peers, the constant engagement of a Torah community to remain sharp. *Ve'al binatcha al tish'en* — do not rely on your own intellect alone. You need your friends, your colleagues, the place of Torah.

A *baraita* then adds that Rabbi Nehorai was not actually named Nehorai — his real name was Rabbi Nechemia, and some say Rabbi Elazar ben Arach himself. This is striking: the person who issued the warning about the necessity of the Torah community may have been the same one who experienced its loss most devastatingly. His name *Nehorai* — from the root meaning light — was given because *sheme'ir einei chachamim bahalacha* — he would illuminate the eyes of the sages in *halacha*. He was the great luminary. And yet when he went to a place where the wine was too good and the Torah sages were absent, even he lost his Torah.

## Oil & Body Care (III)…

### Silver Scraper

The *gemara* records that Shmuel bar Yehuda's mother had a silver scraper made for him. Rashi understands this as a *shabbat*-specific implement — a dedicated silver tool for use on *shabbat*. The apparent reasoning is that an ornamental silver scraper is less evocative of the weekday professional procedure and thus may mitigate the *uvda d'chol* concern. As one might fairly note: the act itself is the same regardless of the implement's material. The silver designation signals that this is a *shabbat* implement and thus perhaps shifts the framing from weekday-type activity to something fitting the sanctity of the day — though the concern does not disappear entirely.

## Medical Treatments (II)

### Purgatives

*Ve'ein osin apiktvizin beShabbat* — one may not take a purgative on *shabbat*. Rabbah bar bar Chana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: this prohibition applies specifically to a medicinal substance (*sam*) that one drinks to induce vomiting. However, sticking one's finger in one's throat to induce vomiting is *muttar*. This distinction is initially puzzling — why is the manual method not equally a *maaseh refuah*?

### Waste Concern

The answer is suggested by the next line. Rav Nechemia taught: in fact, even on a weekday it is forbidden to vomit in order to eat more — because it constitutes *hefsed ochlin*, wasteful destruction of food. The fact that the concern is about wasting food — rather than about healing — reveals that inducing vomiting with one's finger is not categorized as *refuah* at all. It is a gluttonous practice, done to enable further eating at a banquet. A liquid purgative, by contrast, is associated with a medical process and thus falls under *issur refuah* on *shabbat*. That the *gemara* frames the concern solely as food waste without reference to the moral dimension of gluttony is itself noteworthy.

### Minor's Limbs

*Ve'ein me'atzvin et hakatan* — one may not *me'atzvim* (arrange or align) the limbs of a minor. Rabbah bar bar Chana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: however, *lelapufi yanuka beShabbata shapir dami* — a different type of alignment or bundling of a baby's limbs (*lipuf*) is permitted. The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: the *mishna*'s prohibition specifically concerns aligning the spine — *shatzedei bechumrei shidra demichzi kevoneh* — which resembles the act of building, structuring the skeletal frame. Other arrangements or bundling of a minor's limbs that do not rise to that level remain permitted.

### Choleh Leniency

Here again, the *gemara*'s categorical treatment is striking in light of contemporary practice. We would immediately classify any patient requiring bone-setting, limb-resetting, or spinal manipulation as at minimum a *choleh kol haguf* — and in most such cases the patient would likely qualify as *choleh sheyesh bo sakana*. In either case, the *issur refuah* does not apply. Even for a *choleh she'ein bo sakana*, we are *matir* even rabbinic prohibitions based on the circumstances. The practical upshot is that the *mishna*'s categorical prohibitions in this area are far narrower in application than they appear, and the question of how to explain what the *mishna* was addressing — given the narrowness of how we apply *issur refuah* today — remains genuinely difficult.
