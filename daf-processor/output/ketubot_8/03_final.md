# Ketubot 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sheva Brachos Intro

### Previous Review

The daf begins embedded in the classic *sugya* concerning *birchas chasanim* — what we commonly call *sheva brachos* — and *birchas erusin*. The previous day's discussion had centered on the language of *te'una bracha*, the sense in which the *kalla* requires a *bracha*. Rashi and Tosafos, quoting Maseches Kalla, introduced the formulation *kalla blo bracha asura le'ba'ala* — that the *kalla* is in some sense forbidden and the *bracha* effects her permissibility. The *bracha* can thus be reframed as a sanctification of the act of marital relations, though the language also focuses quite specifically on the woman as the object being rendered permissible.

### Ruth & Boaz Source

The *gemara* also raised the question of whether the requirement of a *minyan* derives from the *pasuk* concerning Ruth and Boaz — a blessing of their union by the assembled people — or from the verse *bemakhelos barchu Hashem mi'mekor Yisrael*, where *mekor* appears from the *gemara* to refer specifically to the sexual dimension rather than merely fertility. What emerges clearly is that this is a blessing given by the community to the couple for their union, with the sanctification of the sexual act as a central element. The *bracha* functions as a *matir* of sorts — not necessarily in a strictly technical sense, but as a framing that sanctifies and thereby enables the couple's union.

Tosafos also quotes a passage from Maseches Kalla concerning the blessing that Rivka's mother and brother gave her — a *bracha* recited before only two people, not a *minyan*, and directed to the *kalla* alone rather than the couple. As one participant astutely noted, that blessing — *achoseinu at hayi l'alfei revava* — is also a blessing of fertility rather than of the sexual union. Whether it constitutes a serious *makor* for *birchas chasanim* or for *birchas erusin* is therefore uncertain. Tosafos raises the possibility that it may serve as a source for *birchas erusin*, though that too seems somewhat tangential.

### Nisuin vs Erusin

What the *gemara* has established so far about *sheva brachos* is that it concerns *birchas nisuin* — the blessing of the couple's union, related to the sanctification of their shared life together. That the *minyan* is required for *birchas chasanim* makes sense precisely because this is a *bracha* of the *tzibur* to the *chasan* and *kalla*. The *gemara* had also noted the case of Yehuda, where the possibility of relations before *nisuin* — following *erusin* alone — might also require a *bracha*, which is somewhat ironic given that the text of the blessing clearly envisions the beginning of married life together. Yet that case focuses the mind on the sexual act as such, which is precisely what the blessing addresses. With that context established, the *gemara* now turns its attention to *birchas erusin*, a concept not found in any *mishna* — this is the first we hear of it.

## Birchat Erusin

### Tanya Idach Formula

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: מְבָרְכִין בִּרְכַּת חֲתָנִים בְּבֵית חֲתָנִים, וּבִרְכַּת אֵרוּסִין בְּבֵית הָאֵרוּסִין. בִּרְכַּת הָאֵרוּסִין מַאי מְבָרֵךְ? רָבִין בַּר רַב אַדָּא וְרַבָּה בַּר רַב אַדָּא, תַּרְוַיְיהוּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה אָמְרִי: ״בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו, וְצִוָּנוּ עַל הָעֲרָיוֹת, וְאָסַר לָנוּ אֶת הָאֲרוּסוֹת, וְהִתִּיר לָנוּ אֶת הַנְּשׂוּאוֹת עַל יְדֵי חוּפָּה וְקִדּוּשִׁין״. רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא מְסַיֵּים בַּהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה: ״בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳, מְקַדֵּשׁ יִשְׂרָאֵל עַל יְדֵי חוּפָּה וְקִדּוּשִׁין״.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in **another** *baraita*: **One recites the benediction of the grooms in the house of the grooms, and the benediction of the betrothal in the house of the betrothal.** With regard to **the benediction of the betrothal, what** formula **does one recite? Ravin bar Rav Adda and Rabba bar Rav Adda both said in the name of Rav Yehuda: Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, Who sanctified us through His mitzvot, and commanded us concerning the forbidden relatives, and prohibited to us those women who are betrothed, and permitted to us** those women **who are married by means of the wedding canopy and betrothal. Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, concludes** the blessing **in the name of Rav Yehuda: Blessed are You, Lord, Who sanctifies Israel by means of the wedding canopy and betrothal.**

The *gemara* introduces this with a *baraisa*: *mevarchin birchas chasanim be'beis chasan u'virchas erusin be'beis erusin* — we recite *birchas chasanim* in the home of the *chasan* and *birchas erusin* in the home of the *erusin*. What is the text of *birchas erusin*? Ravin bar Rav Adda and Rabba bar Rav Adda both say in the name of Rav Yehuda: *Baruch atta Hashem Elokeinu melech ha'olam asher kideshanu be'mitzvosav ve'tzivanu al ha'arayos ve'assar lanu es ha'arusos ve'hitir lanu es ha'nesuos*. Rabbeinu Tam added the word *lanu* to clarify that the only *nesua* with whom a man is permitted to have relations is the one married to him — *al yedei chupa ve'kiddushin*. Rav Acha the son of Rava concludes it in the name of Rav Yehuda with the *chasima*: *Baruch atta Hashem mekadesh Yisrael* — some versions have *amo Yisrael* — *al yedei chupa ve'kiddushin*.

### Asur/Hitir Language

What is immediately striking about this formulation is what it does *not* say. It could have said *asher kideshanu be'mitzvosav ve'tzivanu al ha'kiddushin* or *al ha'erusin*, yet instead it chooses to frame the entire blessing around the language of *isur* and *heter*: *ve'assar lanu es ha'arusos ve'hitir lanu es ha'nesuos al yedei chupa ve'kiddushin*. Once again, the focus falls squarely on marital relations.

What is notable is that the *bracha* does not say *assar lanu penuyos* — it does not address the general prohibition of relations outside of marriage. What it says is that the *arusa* specifically is forbidden to us at this stage, and that she will become permitted when we proceed to *nisuin*. In a remarkable way, then, this is a *bracha* that openly acknowledges the present incompleteness of the marriage: we are not there yet. The *kiddushin* is a preparatory stage, and this blessing frames it as pointing forward toward the *nisuin* when the full married life will begin. The institution is described as one that operates in stages, and the *bracha* itself is constructed around that staging.

### Rambam 2 Stages

This is an appropriate place to note what the Rambam writes at the opening of Hilchos Ishus. Before *matan Torah*, he explains, the practice for a man and woman who wished to marry was simply to begin living together — marriage was a single, immediate act. This remains the structure for a non-Jew. The Torah introduced the innovation of two distinct stages: first *kiddushin*, then *nisuin*, after which they commence their shared life. The Torah divided into two what had previously been one.

### Kiddushin Contract

What is the profound significance of this two-stage structure? If marriage begins entirely with the reality of living together, then the marriage is defined by that lived reality at any given moment. And reality fluctuates — there will be periods of tension, distance, and estrangement, times when the couple is not living as a married couple in any practical sense. Without something that precedes and transcends that lived reality, such moments would simply unravel the marriage itself.

*Kiddushin* is best understood as a binding contractual commitment that precedes the reality of married life precisely because there will be times when that reality falters. It establishes a legal and spiritual framework — not one that cannot be dissolved, but one that creates a genuine obligation to work through difficult periods. The binding nature of *kiddushin* is not contingent on the day-to-day happiness of the couple. It precedes that happiness and persists even when it is absent. That is the point of separating the two stages.

### Geonic Variants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּלָא חָתֵים, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַבִּרְכַּת פֵּרוֹת וְאַבִּרְכַּת מִצְוֹת. וּמַאן דְּחָתֵים, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַקִּידּוּשָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** **One who does not conclude** the benediction of the betrothal in accordance with the opinion of Rav Aḥa, but instead recites it without a concluding blessing, deems the formula of this blessing **just as** the formula **is in the blessing** recited over **fruits and the blessing** recited over **mitzvot,** in which the words: Blessed are You, Lord, appear only at the beginning of the blessing. **And one who concludes** the benediction of the betrothal in accordance with the opinion of Rav Aḥa, deems the formula of this blessing **just as** the formula **is** in the blessing **of *kiddush*,** in which the words: Blessed are You, Lord, appears both at the beginning and the conclusion of the blessing.

Returning to the text of the *bracha*: the formulation *mekadesh Yisrael al yedei chupa ve'kiddushin* raises an obvious question. We say *mekadesh Yisrael ve'hazmanim* in *kiddush*, but we do not ordinarily say *mekadesh Yisrael al yedei X* for any particular *mitzvah* — we do not say *mekadesh Yisrael al yedei tzitzis* and so on. The *geonim* therefore proposed an alternative reading: the *chasima* is simply *mekadesh Yisrael*, without the *al yedei*. That is a powerful statement on its own — that this *mitzvah* in particular is connected to *kedushas Yisrael*. Marriage and family life taking place within a framework of *kedusha* is what defines and hallows the people.

As for the phrase *al yedei chupa ve'kiddushin* — in which *chupa* precedes *kiddushin* despite *kiddushin* coming first chronologically — the *geonim* offer an ingenious explanation. Originally, they suggest, the text read *be'kiddushin* with a *bet* rather than *ve'kiddushin* with a *vav*. Due to the phonological rules of *dagesh* following a *hey*, the *bet* loses its *dagesh* and becomes a *vet*, which was subsequently misread as a *vav*. The original meaning would therefore have been *chupa bi'kiddushin* — the *chupa* effected through the act of *kiddushin*. Regardless of whether one accepts that reading, the ordering does accomplish something important. By naming *chupa* first, the *bracha* points toward the ultimate destination of married life. And by concluding with *kiddushin*, the *bracha* ends on the word that describes what is actually happening now. Had it read *al yedei kiddushin ve'chupa*, a *birchas erusin* would have ended on the word *chupa* — and that would have been incongruous.

### Chasima Question

The *gemara* addresses the question of whether *birchas erusin* requires a *chasima* — a closing *baruch atta* at the end. Two positions emerge: *man de'lo chasim* holds that it is like *birchas hapeiros* and *birchas hamitzvos*, which are short *brachot* that do not require a *chasima*. *Man de'chasim* holds that it is like *kiddush*, which does conclude with a *chasima*.

### Rashi Kedusha

Tosafos frames the question as purely technical: the *bracha* is longer than a standard *birchas hamitzvah* but shorter than *kiddush*, so it is genuinely unclear which category applies. Rashi, by contrast, begins from the position that the *bracha* belongs in the short category — it does not introduce multiple themes the way *kiddush* does — but explains why one might nonetheless treat it like *kiddush*. His answer: *keivan de'vilashon kedusha hu ke'kiddush hayom chasminan beih* — since it employs the language of *kedusha*, with *kiddushin* and *mekadesh* throughout, it is structured like *kiddush* and therefore takes a *chasima*. For Rashi, then, the issue is not merely a matter of length but of the nature of the *bracha* itself.

### 3 Models Erusin

This discussion opens up three genuinely distinct ways of understanding the function of *birchas erusin*.

The first is that it is a *birchas hamitzvah* — a *bracha* recited before performing a *mitzvah*, specifically the act of *kiddushin*. This is the most straightforward reading: *asher kideshanu be'mitzvosav ve'tzivanu*, just as one recites before taking a *lulav* or immersing in a *mikveh*. This raises an immediate question among the *rishonim*: is there actually a *mitzvah* of *kiddushin* per se, distinct from *peru urevu*? That is a major debate. If it is a *birchas hamitzvah*, the *bracha* should be recited by the *chasan* himself and should precede the act — consistent with our practice of reciting it before the *mesiras tabaas*. However, our actual practice is a hybrid: the *bracha* is recited by the rabbi, which reflects not a *birchas hamitzvah* but a *birchas hashevach* — a praise of God for the institution of marriage, recited by one who witnesses the act rather than performs it. A *birchas hashevach* need not be recited before the act; indeed, it would typically be recited afterwards, in response to what one has witnessed. Some *rishonim* do hold that *birchas erusin* is recited after the *mesiras tabaas*. At a wedding where the rabbi forgot to recite it beforehand, one can rely on those *rishonim* and have it recited afterwards, precisely because it can be understood as a *birchas hashevach*.

The third model draws the comparison to *kiddush*. *Kiddush* involves not merely acknowledging that *Shabbos* is holy but actively participating in declaring it so. By reciting *kiddush*, one adds a human layer of sanctification. Similarly, one might understand *birchas erusin* not only as praise for a holy institution but as the act by which one sanctifies the union — the human element that consecrates the couple's shared life and their intimacy. That is the *kiddush* dimension.

The comparison to *birchas hapeiros*, finally, connects to the previous day's discussion: it frames the sexual dimension of marriage as an act of pleasure that is elevated by directing it toward God through a *bracha*, just as one elevates physical enjoyment of food. Each of these models carries different *nafka minos*, and taken together they reflect a richly layered understanding of what *birchas erusin* is doing.

## Birchat Chasanim

### Week Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מְבָרְכִין בִּרְכַּת חֲתָנִים בַּעֲשָׂרָה כׇּל שִׁבְעָה. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: וְהוּא שֶׁבָּאוּ פָּנִים חֲדָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: One recites the benediction of the grooms in** a quorum of **ten** men **all seven** days of the wedding celebration. **Rav Yehuda said: And that is** the case only **when new faces** who did not previously participate in the festivities **came** to join the celebration.

The *gemara* states: *mevarachin birchas chasanim be'asara kol shiva* — we recite *birchas chasanim* with ten people throughout the seven days. Note that *birchas erusin* carries no requirement of *borei peri hagafen*. The *sheva brachos*, by contrast, do eventually incorporate wine — though this addition comes from the *geonic* period rather than the *gemara* itself. The reason *borei peri hagafen* became so intrinsic to *sheva brachos*, while the same is not true of *birchas erusin*, is likely because *sheva brachos* is anchored in the context of meals, and wine is the natural accompaniment of a *seudah*.

Rav Yehuda adds: *v'hu shebo panim chadashos* — this applies when new guests have arrived. Tosafos explains that *panim chadashos* refers specifically to people whose presence genuinely adds to the *simcha* — those whose arrival generates a qualitatively greater joy. The underlying logic is that the *brachos* are an expression of joy: either they are a continuation of the existing *simcha*, or they reflect something new. The presence of new guests who increase the *simcha* provides that new element.

### Shehakol/Yotzer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מְבָרֵךְ? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: ״בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, שֶׁהַכֹּל בָּרָא לִכְבוֹדוֹ״. וְ״יוֹצֵר הָאָדָם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What blessings does one recite? Rav Yehuda said** that these are the seven blessings: **Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, Who has created all for His glory.** **And** the second blessing is: Blessed are You…**Creator of mankind.**

Rav Yehuda then transmits the text of the *brachos* themselves. The first is *shehakol bara lichvodo* — God created everything for His honor. The second is *yotzer ha'adam* — You have created the human being.

### Divine Image

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַ״אֲשֶׁר יָצַר אֶת הָאָדָם בְּצַלְמוֹ, בְּצֶלֶם דְּמוּת תַּבְנִיתוֹ, וְהִתְקִין לוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ בִּנְיַן עֲדֵי עַד. בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳, יוֹצֵר הָאָדָם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the third blessing is: Blessed are You…**Who made humanity in His image, in the image of the likeness of His form, and out of His very self formed a building** (see Genesis 2:22) **for eternity. Blessed are You, Lord, Creator of mankind.**

The third is *asher yatzar es ha'adam betzalmo betzelem demus tavniso ve'hisin lo mimenu binyan adei ad* — who formed the human in His image, in the image of His likeness, and fashioned from him an everlasting structure. The *binyan adei ad* points to the woman, created from man, and the *adei ad* indicates human continuity — children and generations proceeding from that original creation.

### Sos Tasis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שׂוֹשׂ תָּשִׂישׂ וְתָגֵל הָעֲקָרָה, בְּקִבּוּץ בָּנֶיהָ לְתוֹכָהּ בְּשִׂמְחָה. בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳, מְשַׂמֵּחַ צִיּוֹן בְּבָנֶיהָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The fourth blessing is: **May the barren** city of Jerusalem **greatly rejoice and delight with the ingathering of her children within her in joy. Blessed are You, Lord, Who gladdens Zion through her children.**

The next *bracha* shifts register: *sos tasis ve'tagel ha'akara bekibbutz baneha le'socha be'simcha* — the barren one will greatly rejoice and exult as her children are gathered joyfully within her. This is *Yerushalayim*. The *chasima* is *Baruch atta Hashem mesameach Tzion bivneha*.

### National Themes

This is a notable pivot. We have moved from the creation of the human being and the fashioning of woman from man, and now — before returning to the couple themselves — we introduce the national dimension. This does not strike the note of mourning (*Im eshkachech*), but rather the positive one: the gathering of the children of Israel to Jerusalem will be cause for joy. This presages the redemption and frames the couple's joy within a larger messianic horizon. The reference to the *akara* carries the suggestion of fertility as well, though this remains implicit.

### Gan Eden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שַׂמֵּחַ תְּשַׂמַּח רֵיעִים הָאֲהוּבִים, כְּשַׂמֵּחֲךָ יְצִירְךָ בְּגַן עֵדֶן מִקֶּדֶם, בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳, מְשַׂמֵּחַ חָתָן וְכַלָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The fifth blessing is: **Bring great joy to these loving friends, as You gave joy to Your creations in Eden in ancient times. Blessed are You, Lord, Who brings joy to the groom and bride.**

The *bracha* of *sameach tesamach re'im ha'ahuvim ke'samechacha yetzircha be'Gan Eden mikedem* returns to the couple directly. The language is striking: may this couple rejoice as their Creator rejoiced with them — or rejoiced for them — in the Garden of Eden. *Gan Eden mikedem* may mean Eden in the east or Eden in the primordial past. In either case, the image is of the original union of man and woman, *zot hapa'am etzem me'atzami uvasar mibsari* — the moment of recognition and joy at coming together. The *chasima* is *Baruch atta Hashem mesameach chatan ve'kallah*.

### Sasson Brachos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳, אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, אֲשֶׁר בָּרָא שָׂשׂוֹן וְשִׂמְחָה, חָתָן וְכַלָּה, גִּילָה, רִינָּה, דִּיצָה, חֶדְוָה, אַהֲבָה וְאַחְוָה וְשָׁלוֹם וְרֵיעוּת. מְהֵרָה ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ יִשָּׁמַע בְּעָרֵי יְהוּדָה וּבְחוּצוֹת יְרוּשָׁלַיִם קוֹל שָׂשׂוֹן וְקוֹל שִׂמְחָה, קוֹל חָתָן וְקוֹל כַּלָּה, קוֹל מִצְהֲלוֹת חֲתָנִים מֵחוּפָּתָם וּנְעָרִים מִמִּשְׁתֵּה נְגִינָתָם, בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳, מְשַׂמֵּחַ חָתָן עִם הַכַּלָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The sixth blessing is: **Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, Who has created joy and gladness, groom and bride, delight, exultation, happiness, jubilation, love and brotherhood, and peace and friendship. Soon, Lord our God, may there be heard in the cities of Judea and in the streets of Jerusalem the sound of joy and the sound of gladness, the sound of the groom and the sound of the bride, the joyous sound of grooms from their wedding canopy and of young people from their feast of song** (see Jeremiah 33:11). **Blessed are You, Lord, Who makes the groom rejoice with the bride.** Together with the blessing over the wine, these are the seven wedding blessings.

The next *bracha* is *Baruch atta Hashem Elokeinu melech ha'olam asher bara sasson ve'simcha chatan ve'kallah gilah rinah ditzah ve'chedvah ahavah ve'achvah shalom ve're'us* — who created all these forms of rejoicing: joy and gladness, groom and bride, delight, song, mirth, and exultation, love, brotherhood, peace, and companionship. This is then followed by: *mehera Hashem Elokeinu yishama be'arei Yehuda u've'chutzos Yerushalayim kol sasson ve'kol simcha kol chatan ve'kol kallah* — may there soon be heard in the cities of Judah and the streets of Jerusalem the voice of joy and the voice of gladness, the voice of the groom and the voice of the bride, concluding with *Baruch atta Hashem mesameach chatan im hakallah*.

The structure here is chiastic. We moved from the creation of man and woman, to the national-messianic theme of Zion and her children, back to the couple rejoicing in Gan Eden. Now the final *bracha* unifies both themes: the joy God created — *sasson ve'simcha chatan ve'kallah* — is the same joy that will ring out in the streets of Yerushalayim at the time of redemption, echoing the *pesukim* in Yirmiyahu. The couple's wedding joy and the national redemptive joy are woven together. This is one reason the *brachos* carry such power when recited *be'asara* — as a communal act. The blessing declares that this is not merely a private *simcha*. It is a *simcha* of the entire community, intertwined with national hope, and the presence of ten is what gives it that communal dimension.

Rashi notes the distinction between the *chasima* of the penultimate *bracha* — *mesameach chatan ve'kallah* — and the final one — *mesameach chatan im hakallah*. The former, he says, expresses the wish that each of them individually should have success and blessing — *besipuk mezonos ve'chol tuv*. The latter, *chatan im hakallah*, refers specifically to the joy they find *with one another* — that the *couplehood* itself should be a source of joy. Both dimensions matter, but they are distinct.

### Baruch Opening

The *gemara* states: *mevarachin birchas chasanim be'asara kol shiva* — we recite *birchas chasanim* with ten people throughout the seven days. A technical question arises: why do some of the *brachos* open with *Baruch atta* while others do not? Tosafos provides a clear answer. The general rule is that a *bracha* which follows immediately upon another — *samuch la'chavertah* — does not require its own opening *Baruch atta*. However, when two consecutive *brachos* are very short, they must each open with *Baruch atta* or they will simply sound like a single continuous *bracha*. If one were to say *Baruch atta Hashem Elokeinu melech ha'olam shehakol bara lichvodo yotzer ha'adam*, without a new *Baruch atta* at *yotzer ha'adam*, the listener would take it for one strange undifferentiated *bracha*. Similarly, if *asher yatzar es ha'adam betzalmo* did not open with *Baruch atta*, one would hear *Baruch atta Hashem yotzer ha'adam asher yatzar* as a single unit. The brevity of those *brachos* requires the explicit opening to signal that a new *bracha* has begun.

Once *asher yatzar es ha'adam* is clearly a distinct, somewhat longer *bracha*, the shift to *sos tasis* with its entirely new theme is obviously a different *bracha*, and therefore *sos tasis* does not require its own *Baruch atta*.

As for the final *bracha* — *asher bara sasson ve'simcha chatan ve'kallah* — it does open with *Baruch atta*, and Tosafos explains why: because this *bracha* may sometimes be recited independently, without the preceding *brachos*. Since it can stand alone — as we will see below in the discussion of the week following the wedding — it must be formulated as a self-contained *bracha* and therefore opens with *Baruch atta*. Rav Moshe Feinstein rules that once the *nusach* was established in this form, the opening *Baruch atta* or its absence is fixed regardless of how the *brachos* are distributed among different participants.

### Community Simcha

The *brachos* as a whole intertwine the joy of the couple with the joy of the community and the joy of national redemption. The making of these *brachos be'asara* is not incidental — the community is not merely an audience. The assembled ten are participants in a *simcha* that transcends the private moment of the wedding.

### Rashi Am Assembled

Rashi's reading of *shehakol bara lichvodo* is particularly striking. Rashi says — and the text reads *einah min haseder ela asifas ha'am hane'esafim sham ligmol chesed, zecher chasdei haMakom she'nahag im Adam HaRishon shena'asu lo shoshvinin ve'nit'asek bo, ve'asifah zo mihu kevod haMakom* — that *shehakol bara lichvodo* is not primarily a cosmological statement but a *bracha* for the assembled people themselves. All who have gathered here to gladden the *chasan ve'kallah* are performing an act of *chesed*, following in the ways of God, who Himself attended to Adam HaRishon and was *mesameach chatan ve'kallah*. The gathering itself is *kevod haMakom* — an honor to God — because the assembled are imitating God's own conduct. Whether this is *peshat* in the *bracha* is debatable — *shehakol bara lichvodo* most naturally reads as a statement about creation and God's purpose — but Rashi's reading gives the opening *bracha* a remarkable social and ethical valence, framing the entire ceremony as a communal act of *chesed* modeled on the divine.

On the question of whether the six *brachos* include *borei peri hagafen*: the *gemara* records only six. The *borei peri hagafen* was added in the *geonic* period and is not part of the *talmudic* text. For *birchas erusin*, the addition of a cup of wine is clearly later and universally recognized as not intrinsic. The *borei peri hagafen* of *sheva brachos*, while also *geonic*, came to be seen as far more integral to the *brachos* — likely, as noted, because *sheva brachos* is so thoroughly anchored in the context of the meal.

## Count & Creation

### Levi/Rav Ashi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵוִי אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי רַבִּי בְּהִלּוּלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרֵיהּ, בָּרֵיךְ חֲמֵשׁ. רַב אַסִּי אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי רַב אָשֵׁי בְּהִלּוּלֵיהּ דְּמָר בְּרֵיהּ, בָּרֵיךְ שֵׁית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Levi happened** to come **to the house of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **during the wedding celebration of Rabbi Shimon, his son, and recited five** of these **blessings. Rav Asi happened** to come **to the house of Rav Ashi during the wedding celebration of Mar, his son, and recited six** of these **blessings.**

The *gemara* records two incidents. Levi visited the household of Rebbi at the wedding celebration of Rav Shimon, Rebbi's son, and recited five *brachos*. Rav Ashi visited the household of Rav Ashi at the wedding celebration of Mar, his son — Mar bar Rav Ashi — and recited six. The *gemara* proposes that this reflects a *machlokes*:

### Chad vs Shtei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: דְּמָר סָבַר חֲדָא יְצִירָה הֲוַאי, וּמָר סָבַר שְׁתֵּי יְצִירוֹת הֲוַאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that they disagree about this:** One **Sage holds: It was one** act of **creation** with which man was created. **And** one **Sage holds: It was two** acts of **creation** with which man was created. The first opinion, that there was one act of creation, is based on the concept that man was created with two sides, one male and one female. There was no additional act of creation. Man and woman were subsequently separated into two independent beings. Therefore, there is no need for the two blessings: Who created mankind, and: Who created mankind in His image. The second opinion is that there were in fact two separate acts of creation. Therefore, it is appropriate to recite two blessings with regard to the creation of mankind.

*Leima b'hai kamifligi: de'mar savar chada yetzira havai u'mar savar shtei yetziros havai* — one holds that *yotzer ha'adam* and *asher yatzar es ha'adam betzalmo* describe a single act of creation and should be collapsed into one *bracha*, while the other holds that they describe two distinct acts and therefore require two *brachos*. What is the basis for this question?

### Together/Separate

Rashi connects this to the familiar reconciliation of Bereishis 1 and Bereishis 2. In Bereishis 1, the human is created male and female simultaneously — *zachar u'nekeva bara osam*. One reading of this, familiar from the *midrash*, is that the first human was created as a single being comprising both male and female, joined back-to-back, subsequently divided into two. Under this reading, *chada yetzira havai* — it was always one creation, always male-and-female, and the separation was not a conceptually distinct second act but merely the division of what was already present. The two *brachos* can therefore be collapsed into one.

### Plan vs Execution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא חֲדָא יְצִירָה הֲוַאי. מָר סָבַר בָּתַר מַחְשָׁבָה אָזְלִינַן, וּמָר סָבַר בָּתַר מַעֲשֶׂה אָזְלִינַן. כִּי הָא דְּרַב יְהוּדָה רָמֵי, כְּתִיב: ״וַיִּבְרָא אֱלֹהִים אֶת הָאָדָם בְּצַלְמוֹ״, וּכְתִיב: ״זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה בְּרָאָם״, הָא כֵּיצַד? בַּתְּחִלָּה עָלָה בְּמַחְשָׁבָה לִבְראוֹת שְׁנַיִם, וּלְבַסּוֹף נִבְרָא אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that suggestion: **No, everyone agrees** that **it was** only **one act of creation.** However, one **Sage holds:** It is **according to** the initial **thought** that **we proceed. And** one **Sage holds:** It is **according to** the **action** that **we proceed.** God's initial thought was to create man and woman as separate entities. Ultimately, they were created as one entity. That explanation is **like the following. Rav Yehuda raises a contradiction.** In one verse **it is written: "And God created man in His own image"** (Genesis 1:27), indicating one act of creation, **and** in another verse **it is written: "Male and female He created them"** (Genesis 5:2), indicating two acts. **How** can **this** apparent contradiction be resolved? **Initially, the thought entered** God's mind **to create two, but ultimately only one was** actually **created.**

The *gemara* rejects the premise of this *machlokes*. *De'kulei alma chada yetzira havai* — everyone agrees there was one creation. The real question is *mar savar basar machshava azlinan u'mar savar basar ma'aseh azlinan* — do we follow the original intention or the actual execution? And this is *ki ha d'Rav Yehuda*, who raised a contradiction: it says *vayivra Elokim es ha'adam betzalmo betzelem Elokim bara oso*, a single male figure, and elsewhere *zachar u'nekeva bara am* — male and female. *Ha keitzad? Bet'chila ala be'machshava lehivaros shnayim — the original plan was to create two — u'le'vasof nivra echad* — but ultimately they were created as one, then divided.

The conclusion that *chada yetzira havai* — reflecting the reading that male and female were already present together from the outset — carries a theological resonance that commends it to our contemporary sensibilities: woman is not an afterthought or appendage of man, but part of the original and complete creation. The two accounts of creation read together convey something profound: the individual human being bears *tzelem Elokim* and full dignity, while simultaneously being incomplete without the other. The original human comprised both; division into two created not lack but the possibility of wholeness through reunion. And there is something to the observation that the *machshava* envisioned two distinct beings while the *ma'aseh* began with one — the separation itself grounds a kind of autonomy and individual wholeness that is distinct from the wholeness of union.

## Week Conditions

### Day 1 Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי רַב כָּהֲנָא. יוֹמָא קַמָּא בָּרֵיךְ כּוּלְּהוּ, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ אִי אִיכָּא פָּנִים חֲדָשׁוֹת — בָּרֵיךְ כּוּלְּהוּ, וְאִי לָא — אַפּוֹשֵׁי שִׂמְחָה בְּעָלְמָא הוּא, מְבָרֵךְ ״שֶׁהַשִּׂמְחָה בִּמְעוֹנוֹ״ וַ״אֲשֶׁר בָּרָא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Ashi happened** to come **to the house of Rav Kahana** to attend a wedding. **The first day he recited all** seven **blessings. From that** point **forward, if there were new faces** present, **he recited all the blessings, and if not,** he would say: **It is merely an extension of the** original **celebration, and he would recite the blessing: In Whose dwelling is joy,** in the *zimmun* prior to Grace after Meals, **and** the sixth blessing after Grace after Meals: **Who has created.**

Having established the *brachos* themselves, the *gemara* now turns to the conditions under which they are recited throughout the seven days. Rav Ashi came to the home of Rav Kahana. On the first day — *yoma kama barech kulhu* — he recited all of the *brachos*. From that point forward: *ei ika panim chadashot barech kulhu* — if there are *panim chadashot*, new guests, he recited all of them. If not — *afushei simcha be'alma hu* — it is simply more of the same *simcha*, not a qualitatively new one.

### Shehasimcha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשִּׁבְעָה וְעַד שְׁלֹשִׁים, בֵּין אָמַר לְהוּ מֵחֲמַת הִלּוּלָא, וּבֵין לָא אָמַר לְהוּ מֵחֲמַת הִלּוּלָא — מְבָרֵךְ ״שֶׁהַשִּׂמְחָה בִּמְעוֹנוֹ״. מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ, אִי אָמַר לְהוּ מֵחֲמַת הִלּוּלָא — מְבָרֵךְ ״שֶׁהַשִּׂמְחָה בִּמְעוֹנוֹ״, וְאִי לָא — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the wedding blessings, the Gemara continues: **From seven** days after the wedding **until** the **thirtieth** day, **whether** the groom **said** to the guests that he is inviting them **due to the wedding celebration or whether he did not say to them** that he is inviting them **due to the wedding celebration, he recites the blessing: In Whose dwelling is joy. From this** point, thirty days after the wedding, **forward, if he said to them** that he is inviting them **due to the wedding celebration [*hillula*], he recites the blessing: In Whose dwelling is joy, and if not, he doesn't.**

In that case, *mivarech she'hasimcha bimono* — one recites the addition of *she'hasimcha bimono* in the *zimmun*, the opening of *birchas hamazon*: *Baruch Elokeinu she'hasimcha bimono ve'she'achalnu mishelo*. And together with that, *asher bara* — the final *bracha* of the *sheva brachos* — is recited on its own. This is precisely why *asher bara sasson ve'simcha* opens with *Baruch atta*: it can be recited independently, as it is in this situation during the week when *panim chadashot* are absent. The fact that *she'hasimcha bimono* functions as an insertion in the *zimmun* anchors the whole structure in the context of the meal, and makes explicit what the *gemara* had previously left implicit: *sheva brachos* is a practice of the meal.

### Week 2 Month

*Mishiva ve'ad shloshim* — from after the first week until thirty days: *bein amruhu machmat hilula bein lo amruhu machmat hilula* — whether or not one specifies that the meal is in honor of the wedding, one adds *she'hasimcha bimono* in the *zimmun*. There is no *asher bara* recited independently during this period — at most, the *zimmun* addition. It is worth noting the parallel structure with *aveilus*: the stages of seven days, thirty days, and twelve months mirror the structure of mourning. Whether this reflects a general pattern of how significant life events recede over time, or whether the parallels are more pointed, the *gemara* will make explicit by comparing *birchas chasanim* and *birchas aveilim* directly.

### Twelve Months

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי אָמַר לְהוּ מֵחֲמַת הִלּוּלָא, עַד אֵימַת? אָמַר רַב פַּפִּי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: עַד תְּרֵיסַר יַרְחֵי שַׁתָּא. וּמֵעִיקָּרָא מֵאֵימַת? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: מִכִּי רְמוּ שְׂעָרֵי בַּאֲסִינְתָּא. אִינִי? וְהָא רַב פָּפָּא אִיעֲסַק לְאַבָּא מָר בְּרֵיהּ, וּבָרֵיךְ מִשְּׁעַת אֵירוּסִין! שָׁאנֵי רַב פָּפָּא, דַּהֲוָה טְרִיחַ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And when** the groom **said to them** that he is inviting them **due to the wedding celebration, until when** is this blessing recited? **Rav Pappi said in the name of Rava: Until twelve months of the year** have passed since the wedding. Since his legal status remains that of a groom, the blessing: In Whose dwelling is joy, may be recited. The Gemara asks: **And initially,** prior to the wedding, **from when** is that blessing recited? **Rav Pappa said: From when they cast barley into the mortar** to prepare beer for the wedding. The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But didn't Rav Pappa involve** himself in preparations **for** the wedding of **his son, Abba Mar, and** begin **reciting the blessing from the time of betrothal? Rav Pappa is different, because** the wedding preparations had already been **prepared for him,** and it was merely a matter of waiting for the designated time to arrive. Therefore, the wedding celebration began for him from the time of betrothal.

After the first month: *ei amar lehu machmat hilula* — if one explicitly frames the meal as being in honor of the *chasan ve'kallah*, as when celebrating with guests who were unable to attend the wedding, *mivarech she'hasimcha bimono*. If not — one does not. And to what point can one still frame a meal as *machmat hilula*? Rav Papa says in the name of Rava: *treisar yarchei shata* — twelve months, the first year.

### Early Erusin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אִיעֲסַק לֵיהּ לִבְרֵיהּ בֵּי רַב חֲבִיבָא, וּבָרֵיךְ מִשְּׁעַת אֵירוּסִין. אָמַר: קִים לִי בְּגַוַּיְיהוּ דְּלָא הָדְרִי בְּהוּ. לָא אִסְתַּיַּיע מִילְּתָא וְהָדְרִי בְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Ravina arranged for his son** to marry a woman from **the house of Rav Ḥaviva and recited the blessing from the time of betrothal. He said: I am certain with regard to them, that they will not retract** their commitment and terminate the betrothal, and the wedding will take place on time. Nevertheless, **the matter was not to be, and** ultimately **they retracted** their commitment, and the wedding was canceled.

The *gemara* then asks: from the earlier side, what is the earliest point from which one may begin reciting *she'hasimcha bimono*? The *gemara* considers whether it can be said even before the wedding, in anticipation of it. Rav Papa married off Abba Mar his son, and he recited *she'hasimcha bimono* from the time of *erusin* — at the engagement celebration, which was explicitly *leshem chatan ve'kallah*. The *gemara* responds: *shani Rav Papa de'hava tari'ach lei* — Rav Papa was different because he was wealthy and had people to manage all the preparations; there was no perceived obstacle between *erusin* and *nisuin*, and he was therefore genuinely already in the mode of *simcha*. Similarly, Ravina married off his son to the daughter of Rav Chaviva, and he too recited *she'hasimcha bimono* from the time of *erusin*, saying *kim li be'gavaihu de'la hadri behu* — I know they will not back out. As long as no obstacle is perceived, one is already in the mindset of *simcha* and may say *she'hasimcha bimono*. The *gemara* then adds with wry economy: *le'vasof lo istai'a milsa ve'hadru vehu* — in the end it did not work out and they did back out. The lesson: do not be overconfident.

### Bris Milah Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חֲבִיבָא אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי מָהוֹלָא, בָּרֵיךְ ״שֶׁהַשִּׂמְחָה בִּמְעוֹנוֹ״. וְלֵית הִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ — מִשּׁוּם דִּטְרִידִי דְּאִית לֵיהּ צַעֲרָא לְיָנוֹקָא.
>
> **Translation:** It is further related: **Rav Ḥaviva happened** to come **to the house** where **a circumcision** was taking place. He **recited the blessing: In Whose dwelling is joy.** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha* is not in accordance with his** opinion. **Since** the parents of the baby **are anxious, as the baby is** experiencing **pain,** it is not appropriate to recite the blessing under those circumstances.

Rav Chaviva came to a *bris milah* and added *she'hasimcha bimono* in the *zimmun*, reasoning that any *seudas mitzvah* might warrant the addition. *Ve'leis hilchasa ke'vosei* — we do not rule that way — *mishum de'tridi, de'is tza'ara le'yanuka* — because the participants are disturbed: the baby is in pain. This *gemara* is the source for the *Ashkenazi minhag* of not reciting *shehecheyanu* at a *bris milah*. Though a *bris* is undeniably a *simcha*, the baby's suffering introduces a dimension of distress that is incompatible with the full declaration of joy that *she'hasimcha bimono* — or *shehecheyanu* — would represent. *Sefardim* do recite *shehecheyanu* at a *bris*, treating it primarily as a *bracha* on a new *mitzvah*, but the *gemara* here reflects a more ambivalent assessment.

## Minyan Rules

### Groom Counts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַב: חֲתָנִים מִן הַמִּנְיָן, וְאֵין אֲבֵלִים מִן הַמִּנְיָן. מֵיתִיבִי: חֲתָנִים וַאֲבֵלִים מִן הַמִּנְיָן! מַתְנִיתָא קָא רָמֵית עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַב? רַב תַּנָּא הוּא, וּפְלִיג. אִיתְּמַר, אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: חֲתָנִים מִן הַמִּנְיָן, וְאֵין אֲבֵלִים מִן הַמִּנְיָן. מֵיתִיבִי: חֲתָנִים וַאֲבֵלִים מִן הַמִּנְיָן!
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Naḥman said** that **Rav said:** With regard to the quorum of ten required to recite the wedding blessings, **grooms are included in the tally. And mourners are not included in the tally** for the blessing of the mourners. The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **Grooms and mourners are included in the tally.** The Gemara responds: **Are you raising a contradiction from a *baraita* against** the opinion of **Rav? Rav himself** had ***tanna*** status **and** therefore, unlike later *amora'im*, could **disagree** with opinions of *tanna'im*. **It was stated: Rabbi Yitzḥak said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Grooms are included in the tally, but mourners are not included in the tally. The Gemara raises an objection** from the *baraita* cited above: **Grooms and mourners are included in the tally.**

Rav Nachman says in the name of Rav: *chassanim min haminyan* — the *chasan* counts toward the ten required for *birchas chasanim*. This may seem obvious, but it is not. If the *minyan* is conceptualized as a *tzibur* giving a blessing to the couple, one might argue that the recipients of the blessing cannot simultaneously constitute the quorum of blessers. The answer is that at a wedding, everyone present is part of the *simcha* — there is no division between the one who rejoices and those who cause his rejoicing. The *chasan* is not a passive recipient; he too is part of the *kehilla* assembled in joy, and therefore counts.

### Aveilim Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא — בְּבִרְכַּת הַמָּזוֹן, כִּי קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן — בְּשׁוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **When that** *baraita* **is taught,** it is **with regard to** combining with others to form a quorum of three to recite **Grace after Meals,** as a mourner is obligated to recite Grace after Meals. However, **when Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that mourners are not included in the tally, it is **with regard to** the quorum of ten men required to form **a line** to comfort the mourners following the burial.

The *gemara* introduces a parallel: *ve'ein aveilim min haminyan* — but for *birchas aveilim*, the *avel* does not count toward the required ten. Here the logic is the inverse: at a house of mourning, there are clearly distinct roles — the *avel* is the mourner, and everyone else is there to console. The *menachamim* constitute the quorum; the *avel* is its recipient.

### Rav vs Braisa

The *gemara* raises an objection: a *baraisa* states *chassanim ve'aveilim min haminyan* — that even the *avel* counts. The *gemara* resolves this by invoking the principle that *Rav tanna u'palig* — Rav, who belongs to the transitional generation between *tannaim* and *amoraim*, may dispute a *baraisa*.

### Yitzchak/Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מְבָרְכִים בִּרְכַּת חֲתָנִים בַּעֲשָׂרָה, וַחֲתָנִים מִן הַמִּנְיָן. וּבִרְכַּת אֲבֵלִים בַּעֲשָׂרָה, וְאֵין אֲבֵלִים מִן הַמִּנְיָן. בְּרָכָה בְּשׁוּרָה מִי אִיכָּא? אֶלָּא כִּי קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בִּרְחָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara raises an objection: **However, that which Rabbi Yitzḥak said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One recites the blessing of the grooms in** a quorum of **ten, and the grooms are included in the tally, and one recites the blessing of the mourners in** a quorum of **ten, and the mourners are not included in the tally. Is there a blessing** recited **in the line** formed to comfort the mourners? Clearly, this statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan cannot be explained as referring to the line. **Rather, when Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that mourners are not included in the tally, it was **with regard to** the blessing recited **in the square** adjacent to the cemetery, where the meal of comfort takes place and various blessings are recited to comfort the mourners. Mourners are not included in the requisite quorum of ten.

The same positions are then attributed to Rav Yitzchak in the name of Rav Yochanan: *chassanim min haminyan ve'ein aveilim min haminyan*. Here too the *gemara* raises the *baraisa* stating that *aveilim* count. But since Rav Yochanan is an *amora*, the solution of *tanna u'palig* is less available, and the *gemara* must find another resolution.

### Shurah Context

The *gemara* distinguishes between two contexts. The *baraisa* that counts *aveilim* in the *minyan* refers to *birchas hamazon* — there is a form of *birchas aveilim* that occurs in the context of *birchas hamazon* at the meals during the week of *aveilus*, and there the *avel* does count. When Rav Yochanan says the *avel* does not count, he refers to the *shurah* — the two lines formed by the consolers when the mourners return from the graveside, through which the *avel* passes to receive words of *nichum*. In the *shurah*, the *avel* is clearly distinct from the participants and cannot count.

### First vs Later

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מְבָרְכִין בִּרְכַּת חֲתָנִים בַּעֲשָׂרָה כׇּל שִׁבְעָה, וַחֲתָנִים מִן הַמִּנְיָן. וּבִרְכַּת אֲבֵלִים בַּעֲשָׂרָה כׇּל שִׁבְעָה, וְאֵין אֲבֵלִים מִן הַמִּנְיָן. בִּרְכַּת רְחָבָה כׇּל שִׁבְעָה מִי אִיכָּא? מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּפָנִים חֲדָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **However,** with regard to **that which Rabbi Yitzḥak said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One recites the blessing of the grooms in** a quorum of **ten all seven** days of celebration, **and the grooms are included in the tally; and one recites the blessing of the mourners all seven** days of mourning **in** a quorum of **ten, and the mourners are not included in the tally, is there a blessing** recited **in the square all seven** days? The meal of comfort and the associated blessings take place directly after the burial, not throughout the seven days of mourning. The Gemara answers: **You find** blessings recited throughout the seven-day mourning period **in** a case where **new faces** who did not attend the burial are present. In that case, eulogies and words of comfort are repeated, and the blessing of the mourners is recited again.

The *gemara* then asks: but the *baraisa* that excludes *aveilim* from the *minyan* specifically in the context of *birchas aveilim* — if *birchas aveilim* occurs at *birchas hamazon* and *aveilim* count there, where does this exclusion apply? The *gemara* answers: *ki ka'amar Rabbi Yochanan be'rachava* — Rav Yochanan refers to the *rachava*, the open space or street where the first meal is served to the mourners upon returning from the cemetery. At that first meal, the roles are maximally differentiated — those who feed and console on one side, the *avel* on the other — and therefore the *avel* does not count toward the ten. This is a distinct *bracha* associated with that initial meal, not the *birchas aveilim* of later meals during the week.

### Mourner Panim

The *gemara* then asks: can there really be a *birchas rachava* throughout all seven days? The *baraisa* says *ein aveilim min haminyan* for *birchas aveilim kol shiva* — all seven days. But the *rachava* is only the first meal. The *gemara* answers: *meshkachas la be'panim chadashot* — you find it through *panim chadashot*. Just as *sheva brachos* continues throughout the week when new guests arrive to augment the *simcha*, so too *birchas aveilim* can recur throughout the week when new *menachamim* arrive — new consolers who were not present before. Their arrival creates a new instance of the *mitzvah* of *nichum aveilim*, and a new occasion for the *bracha*. In those cases, the *avel* does not count toward the ten, for the same reason as always: the *avel* and the *menachamim* occupy fundamentally distinct roles, and the quorum must consist of those performing the *mitzvah* of consolation, not its recipient.
