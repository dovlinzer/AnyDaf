# Bava Kamma 87 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Suma & Boshet

### Mishnah Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְבַיֵּישׁ אֶת הֶעָרוֹם, הַמְבַיֵּישׁ אֶת הַסּוֹמֵא, וְהַמְבַיֵּישׁ אֶת הַיָּשֵׁן – חַיָּיב. וְיָשֵׁן שֶׁבִּיֵּישׁ – פָּטוּר. נָפַל מִן הַגָּג, וְהִזִּיק וּבִיֵּישׁ – חַיָּיב עַל הַנֶּזֶק, וּפָטוּר עַל הַבּוֹשֶׁת עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא מִתְכַּוֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who humiliates a naked person,** or **one who humiliates a blind person, or one who humiliates a sleeping person** is **liable, but a sleeping person who humiliates** another is **exempt.** If one **fell from the roof** onto another person, **and** thereby **caused** him **damage and humiliated** him, then the one who fell is **liable for the** indemnity of **damage,** since a person is always considered forewarned, **and exempt from the** indemnity of **humiliation,** since a person is not liable for humiliation **unless he intends** to humiliate the other person.

The *mishnah* on 86b, beginning with the words *hamvayesh et hasuma*, establishes that one who embarrasses a blind person or a naked person is still *chayav* in *boshet* — embarrassment damages are owed regardless of the victim's condition. The only exemption the *mishnah* articulates in its second clause is for one who causes embarrassment while asleep, because *boshet* requires *kavanah*, and a sleeping person lacks the requisite intent. By mentioning only the sleeping person as exempt, the *mishnah* implies that all other actors — including a blind person who embarrasses someone — remain liable.

### Suma Embarrassing Others

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: סוֹמֵא – אֵין לוֹ בּוֹשֶׁת, וְכָךְ הָיָה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה פּוֹטֵר מֵחַיָּיבֵי גָלִיּוֹת, וּמֵחַיָּיבֵי מַלְקִיּוֹת, וּמֵחַיָּיבֵי מִיתוֹת בֵּית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says: A blind person does not have** liability for **humiliation. And** in **this way Rabbi Yehuda deemed** a blind person **exempt from** being among **those liable to be exiled** for killing unintentionally, **and from** being among **those liable** to receive **lashes, and from** being among **those liable** to receive **court**-imposed **capital punishment.**

The *gemara* picks up on this implication and asks whether everyone agrees that a blind person who embarrasses another is *chayav*. Both Rashi and Tosafot understand the *gemara*'s question not to be whether one is liable *for* embarrassing a blind person — that much is clear from the *mishnah* — but rather whether a *suma* who himself causes embarrassment would be *chayav* or exempt. The *gemara* answers: *matnisin d'lo k'Rabbi Yehuda* — our *mishnah*, which implies that a blind person who embarrasses is liable, does not accord with the view of Rabbi Yehuda. For as a *baraisa* teaches: Rabbi Yehuda says that a blind person has no *boshet* — he is exempt when he causes embarrassment. Moreover, Rabbi Yehuda would similarly exempt a *suma* from *galut* if he killed someone unintentionally, from *malkut* if he violated a negative prohibition, and from *mitas beis din* if he committed a capital offense. This is a sweeping, categorical exemption — not a case-by-case inquiry into whether this particular blind person could have been more careful.

### Gezeirah Shavah Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? גָּמַר "עֵינֶךָ"–"עֵינֶךָ" מֵעֵדִים זוֹמְמִין; מָה הָתָם סוֹמִין לָא, אַף הָכָא סוֹמִין לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi Yehuda?** He **derives** a verbal analogy to: **"Your eye** shall not pity" (Deuteronomy 25:12), stated with regard to humiliation, **from: "Your eye** shall not pity" (Deuteronomy 19:21), stated with regard to **conspiring witnesses.** The analogy teaches that **just as there,** with regard to the *halakha* of conspiring witnesses, **blind people** are **not** included, as blind people cannot see events occur in order to testify, **so too here,** with regard to the *halakha* of compensation for humiliation, **blind people** are **not** included.

The *gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama d'Rabbi Yehuda* — what is the scriptural basis for his position regarding *boshet*? The answer is a *gezeirah shavah*: the word *einecha* ("your eye") appears both in the passage concerning *eidim zomemin* (conspiring witnesses) and in the passage concerning *boshet* — the verse *vekatzota kapa lo tachos einecha*. This verbal parallel links the two laws. And since the entire *parshah* of *eidim zomemin* applies only to witnesses who can see — for the simple reality is that a blind person cannot serve as a valid witness — so too the obligation of *boshet* applies only to one who can see. The shared language of *einecha* creates the link, and the structural logic of *eidim zomemin* being categorically closed to the blind imports that same categorical exclusion into *boshet*.

### Broader Exemptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵחַיָּיבֵי גָלִיּוֹת – דְּתַנְיָא: "בְּלֹא רְאוֹת" – פְּרָט לַסּוֹמֵא, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַסּוֹמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its explanation: Why did Rabbi Yehuda deem a blind person exempt **from** being among **those liable to be exiled** for killing unintentionally? **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that the verse states with regard to an unintentional killing: "Or with any stone, whereby one may die, **seeing him not"** (Numbers 35:23), this formulation serves to **exclude a blind person;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Meir says:** The verse serves **to include a blind person.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אָמַר לָךְ: "וַאֲשֶׁר יָבֹא אֶת רֵעֵהוּ בַיַּעַר לַחְטֹב עֵצִים" – וַאֲפִילּוּ סוֹמֵא; כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא "בְּלֹא רְאוֹת" – לְמַעוֹטֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the dispute: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi Yehuda?** He would **say to you:** The verse states with regard to an unintentional killing, without any further limiting clause: **"As when one goes into the forest with his neighbor to hew wood"** (Deuteronomy 19:5), **and** this includes **even a blind person;** therefore, **the Merciful One wrote** in the Torah: **"Seeing him not"** (Numbers 35:23), **to exclude** a blind person.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר – כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא "בְּלֹא רְאוֹת" לְמַעוֹטֵי, וּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא "בִּבְלִי דַעַת" לְמַעוֹטֵי; הָוֵי מִיעוּט אַחַר מִיעוּט, וְאֵין מִיעוּט אַחַר מִיעוּט אֶלָּא לְרַבּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** what is the reasoning of **Rabbi Meir? The Merciful One wrote** in the Torah: **"Seeing him not"** (Numbers 35:23), apparently **to exclude** one who happened to not see the one he killed; **and the Merciful One wrote** in the Torah: "Who kills his neighbor **unawares"** (Deuteronomy 19:4), apparently **to exclude** a blind person, who is not aware of where others are standing. **This is a restriction following a restriction, and** there is a hermeneutical principle that **a restriction following a restriction** serves **only to amplify** the *halakha* and include additional cases. Therefore, a blind person is included in the *halakha* of those liable to be exiled for unintentional killing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – הָהוּא "בִּבְלִי דַעַת", פְּרָט לְמִתְכַּוֵּין הוּא דַּאֲתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what would **Rabbi Yehuda** respond to this reasoning? The Gemara explains: **That** verse: "Who kills his neighbor **unawares,"** does not exclude one who could not see, but rather, **it comes to exclude** one who was **intending** to kill a particular person and unintentionally killed someone else. That killer is not exiled.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַיָּיבֵי מִיתוֹת בֵּית דִּין – אָתְיָא "רֹצֵחַ"–"רֹצֵחַ" מֵחַיָּיבֵי גָלִיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to explain the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda. Why did he deem a blind person exempt from being among **those liable** to receive **court**-imposed **capital punishment?** That *halakha* is **derived** by means of a verbal analogy to: "But if he smote him with an instrument of iron so that he died, he is **a killer;** the killer shall be put to death" (Numbers 35:16), written with regard to capital punishment, **from:** "You shall appoint for yourselves cities, to be cities of refuge for you, that **a killer** who killed any person unintentionally may flee there" (Numbers 35:11), written with regard to **those liable to be exiled.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַיָּיבֵי מַלְקִיּוֹת – אָתְיָא "רָשָׁע"–"רָשָׁע" מֵחַיָּיבֵי מִיתוֹת בֵּית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** Why did Rabbi Yehuda deem a blind person exempt from being among **those liable** to receive **lashes?** That *halakha* is **derived** by means of a verbal analogy to: "Then it shall be, if the **guilty** one deserves to be beaten" (Deuteronomy 25:2), written with regard to lashes, **from:** "Moreover you shall take no ransom for the life of a murderer, that is **guilty** of death" (Numbers 35:31), written with regard to **those liable** to receive **court**-imposed **capital punishment.**

The *gemara* then works through the rest of Rabbi Yehuda's exemptions. His exclusion of the *suma* from *galut* is derived from the verse *belo re'ot* — "without seeing" — in the context of one who kills another in the forest. Rabbi Yehuda reads this to mean that the *parshah* of exile applies only to one who normally could have seen his victim but on this occasion did not; a blind person, who categorically cannot see, is therefore excluded entirely. Rabbi Meir disagrees and reads *belo re'ot* as *including* the blind person — meaning even one who cannot see is liable for *galut*. Since another verse uses the phrase *bivli da'at* ("without awareness"), which could also be read as excluding the *suma*, Rabbi Meir invokes the rule that *mi'ut achar mi'ut* — two consecutive exclusions — actually serves to *include*. Tosafot notes the peculiarity here: removing both exclusions merely returns one to the default position of inclusion, so the double *mi'ut* seems to accomplish nothing new. Regardless, for Rabbi Meir the *suma* is included in *galut*. Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, holds that *belo re'ot* genuinely excludes the blind, and *bivli da'at* serves the separate and more obvious purpose of excluding the intentional killer — who faces execution, not exile.

The exemption from *mitas beis din* is derived through a *gezeirah shavah* of *rotze'ach rotze'ach* — the word appears both in the context of *galut* and in the context of capital liability, linking them so that the *suma*'s categorical exclusion from one extends to the other. The exemption from *malkut* is then derived through a *gezeirah shavah* of *rasha rasha* — the verse by *mitas beis din* reads *asher hu rasha lamut*, and the verse establishing *malkut* uses the same word *rasha*. Since a *suma* cannot be executed, by the verbal parallel he also cannot receive lashes.

### Rabbi Meir Disagrees

The full structure of Rabbi Meir's position becomes clear from his reading of *belo re'ot*. For Rabbi Meir, no blanket categorical exclusion of the *suma* follows from that phrase; he reads it as including the blind person. The double-exclusion argument is his mechanism for restoring the *suma* to the default of liability. Rabbi Yehuda, however, uses each scriptural peg — *belo re'ot* for *galut*, the *gezeirah shavah* of *einecha* for *boshet*, the *rotze'ach rotze'ach* for capital liability, and the *rasha rasha* for *malkut* — to build a comprehensive categorical exclusion from all court-administered penalties.

### Total Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן הָיָה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה פּוֹטְרוֹ מִכׇּל דִּינִים שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אָמַר קְרָא: "וְשָׁפְטוּ הָעֵדָה בֵּין הַמַּכֶּה וּבֵין גֹּאֵל הַדָּם עַל הַמִּשְׁפָּטִים הָאֵלֶּה" – כֹּל שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בְּמַכֶּה וּבְגוֹאֵל הַדָּם, יֶשְׁנוֹ בְּמִשְׁפָּטִים; כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ בְּמַכֶּה וּבַגּוֹאֵל הַדָּם, אֵינוֹ בְּמִשְׁפָּטִים.
>
> **Translation:** **and so did Rabbi Yehuda exempt** a blind person **from all judgments** of civil law **that are in the Torah.** The Gemara explains: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi Yehuda? The verse states** with regard to an unintentional killing: **"Then the congregation shall judge between the smiter and the avenger of blood, according to these laws"** (Numbers 35:24), to teach that **anyone who is subject to** the *halakha* of **a smiter and to** the *halakha* of **an avenger of blood is subject to** civil **laws, and anyone who is not subject to** the *halakha* of **a smiter or to** the *halakha* of **an avenger of blood,** including a blind person, **is not subject to** civil **laws.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: סוֹמֵא אֵין לוֹ בּוֹשֶׁת. וְכֵן הָיָה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה פּוֹטְרוֹ מִכׇּל מִצְווֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בַּתּוֹרָה. אָמַר רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אָמַר קְרָא: "וְאֵלֶּה הַמִּצְוֹת הַחֻקִּים וְהַמִּשְׁפָּטִים" – כֹּל שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בְּמִשְׁפָּטִים, יֶשְׁנוֹ בְּמִצְווֹת וְחֻקִּים; וְכֹל שֶׁאֵינוֹ בְּמִשְׁפָּטִים, אֵינוֹ בְּמִצְווֹת וְחֻקִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents another statement of Rabbi Yehuda. It **is taught** in **another** *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says: A blind person does not have,** i.e., receive, compensation for **humiliation, and so did Rabbi Yehuda exempt** a blind person **from all mitzvot that are stated in the Torah. Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi, said: What is the reasoning of Rabbi Yehuda? The verse states: "And this is the commandment, statutes, and laws"** (Deuteronomy 6:1), to teach that **anyone who is subject to** civil **laws is** also **subject to** the **commandments and statutes, and anyone who is not subject to** civil **laws,** including a blind person, **is** also **not subject to** the **commandments and statutes.**

A second *baraisa* extends this even further. Rabbi Yehuda not only exempts the *suma* from all punishments but, as the *gemara* on 87a begins, declares: *vechen haya Rabbi Yehuda potro mikol dinim shebatorah* — Rabbi Yehuda would exempt him from all civil law as well. This is a qualitatively different category. The earlier exemptions concerned intentional conduct and court-administered penalties; civil law governs monetary obligations that do not depend on intention in the same way. The scriptural basis offered for this expansion comes from the verse in the context of *galut*: *veshaftu ha'edah bein hamakeh uvein go'el hadam al hamishpatim ha'eleh* — the congregation shall judge between the killer and the blood-redeemer according to "these laws." The *gemara* derives: whoever is in the category of *galut* — that is, subject to exile — is also in the category of civil *mishpatim*; whoever is excluded from *galut* is excluded from civil law entirely. For Rabbi Yehuda the *suma* is excluded from *galut*, and therefore excluded from civil law entirely.

A third *baraisa* takes the final step: *vechen haya Rabbi Yehuda potro mikol mitzvos ha'amuros batorah* — Rabbi Yehuda would exempt him from all *mitzvos* in the Torah. Rav Sheshes the son of Rav Idi supplies the scriptural basis: the verse *ve'eleh hamitzvos hachukim vehamishpatim* links *mitzvos* and *mishpatim* in a single formulation. Whoever is in the category of *mishpatim* is in the category of *mitzvos* and *chukim*; whoever is excluded from *mishpatim* — as Rabbi Yehuda has shown the *suma* to be — is excluded from *mitzvos* as well. Tosafot, struck by the radical sweep of this position, maintains that *Chazal* must have obligated the *suma* at least *miderabbanan*, and that other *gemaros* make clear that a *suma* may recite *Krias Shema* and daven. But the position of Rabbi Yehuda at the level of *de'oraisa* is nevertheless a sweeping categorical exemption that functionally places the blind person in the same legal category as a *cheresh* and *shoteh*.

### Rav Yosef's Stance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, מֵרֵישׁ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מַאן דְּאָמַר הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – דְּאָמַר: סוֹמֵא פָּטוּר מִן הַמִּצְווֹת, קָא עָבֵדְינָא יוֹמָא טָבָא לְרַבָּנַן, מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּלָא מִפַּקַּדְנָא, וְקָא עָבֵדְינָא מִצְווֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef,** who was blind, **said: At first, I would say:** If I hear **one who says that** the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who says: A blind person** is **exempt from the mitzvot,** then **I will host a festive day for the Sages. What is the reason?** It is **that I am not commanded and** nevertheless **I perform mitzvot.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דִּשְׁמַעִית לְהָא דְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: גָּדוֹל הַמְצֻוֶּוה וְעוֹשֶׂה – מִמִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְצֻוֶּוה וְעוֹשֶׂה; מַאן דְּאָמַר לִי: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, עָבֵידְנָא יוֹמָא טָבָא לְרַבָּנַן. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּכִי מִפַּקַּדְינָא – אִית לִי אַגְרָא טְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef continues. **But now that I heard this** statement **of Rabbi Ḥanina, as Rabbi Ḥanina says:** One who is **commanded and performs** a mitzva **is greater than** one who **is not commanded and performs** it, I say: If I hear **one who says to me** that the ***halakha*** is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** then **I will host a festive day for the Sages. What is the reason?** It is **that as I am commanded, I have more reward.**

The *gemara* then records a poignant personal statement by Rav Yosef, who was himself blind. He said: initially I would say that if anyone holds the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda — that a *suma* is exempt from *mitzvos* — I would make a *yom tov* for the rabbis. Why? Because I am not commanded yet I do, and I assumed that performing *mitzvos* without being obligated would be regarded as an act of exceptional voluntarism deserving great reward. But now, having heard the teaching of Rabbi Chanina — that greater is one who is commanded and does than one who is not commanded and does — I have reversed course. Now I would make a *yom tov* if someone tells me the *halacha* does *not* follow Rabbi Yehuda, because if I am commanded, my reward is greater.

### Metzuve vs. Eino Metzuve

The principle of *metzuve ve'oseh* versus *eino metzuve ve'oseh* that Rabbi Chanina articulates is well established, and two distinct reasons are given for why the commanded one receives greater reward. Rashi's approach is psychological: if you are obligated, the *yetzer hara* is more active, the pull to find an exemption is stronger, and therefore overcoming that resistance to fulfill the command is a greater achievement. A second approach is theological: if God commanded certain people to perform a given act and did not command others, this reflects that performing it is more fully aligned with *ratzon Hashem* for those who are commanded. If God had wanted the voluntary performer's act to be equally fundamental to His will, He would have commanded it. This theological framework has direct implications for how the *rishonim* of Ashkenaz understood women's *brachos* on *mitzvos asei shehazman gerama*: just as Rav Yosef, even if exempt, would have continued performing *mitzvos* and reciting the *bracha* of *asher kidshanu bemitzvotav vetzivanu*, so too women who are exempt may recite that *bracha* when performing those *mitzvos*.

## Five Damages

### Adam vs. Shor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ זֶה חוֹמֶר בָּאָדָם מִבַּשּׁוֹר – שֶׁהָאָדָם מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק, צַעַר, רִיפּוּי, שֶׁבֶת וּבוֹשֶׁת, וּמְשַׁלֵּם דְּמֵי וְלָדוֹת; וְשׁוֹר אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא נֶזֶק, וּפָטוּר מִדְּמֵי וְלָדוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **This** *halakha* **is a stringency with regard to a person** who caused injury, **compared to** the *halakha* **with regard to an ox** that caused injury: The *halakha* is **that the person pays** compensation for **damage, pain, medical costs, loss of livelihood, and humiliation; and** if he caused a woman to miscarry he also **pays compensation for** miscarried **offspring,** as the verse states (see Exodus 21:22). **But** in the case of **an ox** that caused injury, the owner **pays only** compensation for **damage, and** he is **exempt from** paying **compensation for** miscarried **offspring.**

The *gemara* transitions to the next *mishnah*: *zeh chomer b'adam mibshor* — this is a stringency by a human over an ox. A human who injures another human must pay all five standard categories of damage: *nezek* (permanent injury), *tza'ar* (pain), *ripui* (medical expenses), *sheves* (lost work), and *boshet* (embarrassment). Furthermore, a human who caused a woman to miscarry must pay *dmei velados*, an assessed value for the fetuses. By contrast, an ox that gores a human pays only *nezek*, and if it causes a woman to miscarry, it pays nothing for the lost fetuses.

### Kiyum Lei Doctrine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּכֶּה אֶת אָבִיו וְאֶת אִמּוֹ וְלֹא עָשָׂה בָּהֶן חַבּוּרָה, וְחוֹבֵל בַּחֲבֵירוֹ בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים – חַיָּיב בְּכוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **One who strikes his father or his mother but did not cause them** to have **a bruise,** and therefore is not liable to receive court-imposed capital punishment, **and one who injures another on Yom Kippur,** the punishment for which is not court-imposed capital punishment, is **liable** to pay **for all of** the five types of indemnity.

The *mishnah* then introduces cases touching on the principle of *kim lei b'drabba minei* — when one's conduct simultaneously incurs capital liability, the lesser monetary obligation is subsumed. The *mishnah* presents: one who struck his father or mother but did not actually draw blood (*chavurah*) — which is required for the capital offense — and one who wounded another on *Yom Kippur*, which carries *kares* but not *mitas beis din* — in both these cases the injurer is *chayav b'kullan*, obligated in all five payments, because neither case triggers actual capital liability. The principle of *kim lei b'drabba minei* does not apply where the more severe liability (*mitas beis din*) is absent; *chiyuv kares* alone does not displace the monetary obligation.

### Eved Ivri Injury

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַחוֹבֵל בְּעֶבֶד עִבְרִי – חַיָּיב בְּכוּלָּן, חוּץ מִן הַשֶּׁבֶת בִּזְמַן שֶׁהוּא שֶׁלּוֹ. הַחוֹבֵל בְּעֶבֶד כְּנַעֲנִי שֶׁל אֲחֵרִים – חַיָּיב בְּכוּלָּן. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵין לַעֲבָדִים בּוֹשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** **One who injures a Hebrew slave** is **liable** to pay **for all of** the five types of indemnity. This is **except for** compensation for **loss of livelihood** suffered **during the time that** the injured slave **belongs to** the one that injured him. Since the right to the slave's labor belongs to his master, his inability to work is his master's loss. **One who injures a Canaanite slave belonging to others is liable** to pay **for all of** the five types of indemnity. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** Canaanite **slaves do not have humiliation,** so the one who injures the slave pays only the other four types of indemnity.

One who injures a *eved ivri* must pay all five payments — *nezek*, *tza'ar*, *ripui*, *boshet*, and *sheves* — with one exception. An *eved ivri* is a full Jewish person; the master holds only rights to his labor, not to his person. Therefore *nezek*, *tza'ar*, *ripui*, and *boshet* all go to the slave himself. The sole exception is *sheves*, the compensation for time out of work: if it is your own *eved ivri*, that payment is exempt, because the master is the one losing the benefit of the slave's labor during his recovery — paying *sheves* would simply be paying oneself. If, however, it is someone *else's* *eved ivri* who is injured, the third-party injurer must pay *sheves* to the master, because the master is the party actually sustaining the loss of labor.

### Eved Kena'ani Injury

A *Canaanite slave* is legally the full property of his master, not merely his labor. Therefore one who injures another person's *eved kena'ani* must pay all five damages, but pays them to the master, who owns the slave entirely. Tosafot discusses at length the question of the medical bill: since payment for *ripui* goes to the master, the master remains obligated to ensure the slave is actually healed, or alternatively that sum is paid directly to the slave for his treatment. The *mishnah* also records that Rabbi Yehuda holds *ein l'avadim boshet* — slaves do not collect *boshet*. Rather than examining case by case whether a particular slave experienced embarrassment, Rabbi Yehuda applies a categorical exclusion: slaves do not collect embarrassment damages.

## Categorical Exempts

### Cheresh Shoteh Katan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֵרֵשׁ, שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן פְּגִיעָתָן רָעָה, הַחוֹבֵל בָּהֶן – חַיָּיב, וְהֵם שֶׁחָבְלוּ בַּאֲחֵרִים – פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: With regard to **a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor, an encounter with them is disadvantageous.** In other words, no favorable outcome is possible for someone involved in an incident with one of these people, since **one who injures them** is **liable. But** if **they were** the ones **who injured others, they** are **exempt.** This is because they lack awareness and are not responsible for their actions.

The *mishnah* states: *cheresh shoteh v'katan pgi'atan ra'ah* — it is bad to have a violent encounter with a deaf-mute, a mentally ill person, or a minor. If you injure them you are fully *chayav*: they can suffer, they incur medical expenses, they experience pain and embarrassment. But if they injure others, they are exempt — *patur* — because they do not bear legal responsibility for their actions. The *cheresh* as understood by the *gemara* is one who, lacking hearing and speech, did not develop full intellectual capacity; the *shoteh* is one with mental illness; the *katan* is a minor. None of them are held liable for their tortious conduct.

### Woman & Slave Exempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָעֶבֶד וְהָאִשָּׁה פְּגִיעָתָן רָעָה, הַחוֹבֵל בָּהֶם – חַיָּיב, וְהֵם שֶׁחָבְלוּ בַּאֲחֵרִים – פְּטוּרִין, אֲבָל מְשַׁלְּמִין לְאַחַר זְמַן. נִתְגָּרְשָׁה הָאִשָּׁה, נִשְׁתַּחְרֵר הָעֶבֶד – חַיָּיבִין לְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, with regard to **a slave and** a married **woman, an encounter with them is disadvantageous,** since **one who injures them** is **liable. But** if **they** were the ones **who injured others, they** are **exempt,** because they do not have money with which to pay compensation. **But they pay** compensation **at a later time.** The exemption is only temporary, as, if **the woman becomes divorced** or **the slave becomes emancipated,** and they then have their own money, they are **liable to pay** compensation.

A woman and a slave (*eved kena'ani*) present a related but distinct case. *Ha'eved v'ha'ishah pgi'atan ra'ah* — it is also bad to have a violent encounter with them, because if you injure them you are *chayav*. But if they injure others, they are *patur* — not because they lack legal capacity, as the *cheresh shoteh v'katan* do, but because they have no collectible property. A woman's property is largely under her husband's control; a slave owns nothing, as *mah she'kanah eved kanah rabo* — whatever a slave acquires is acquired by his master. There is therefore nothing to collect from them, even though in principle they bear legal responsibility.

### Future Liability

Nevertheless, this exemption is not permanent. *Aval m'shalmim l'achar zman* — they will pay at a later time. Once a woman is divorced (*nitgarshah ha'ishah*) and comes into her own property, and once a slave is freed (*nishtachrer ha'eved*), they become *chayavim l'shalem* — obligated to pay for damages they caused while exempt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּכֶּה אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ וְעָשָׂה בָּהֶן חַבּוּרָה, וְהַחוֹבֵל בַּחֲבֵירוֹ בְּשַׁבָּת – פָּטוּר מִכּוּלָּן, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא נִדּוֹן בְּנַפְשׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **One who strikes his father or his mother and causes them** to have **a bruise, or one who injures another on Shabbat,** is **exempt from** paying **all of** the five types of indemnity, **because he is judged with** losing **his life.** The court imposes capital punishment for these acts, so there is no additional monetary punishment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַחוֹבֵל בְּעֶבֶד כְּנַעֲנִי שֶׁלּוֹ – פָּטוּר מִכּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **And one who injures his own Canaanite slave** is **exempt from** paying **all of** the five types of indemnity, because his slave is his property.

The *mishnah* then loops back in a chiastic structure to a case implicating *kim lei b'drabba minei*: one who strikes his father or mother and actually draws blood (*asah bahen chavurah*), or one who wounds another on *Shabbos* and draws blood — in those cases the injurer is *patur mikulan mipnei shehu nidon b'nafsho*: exempt from all monetary payments because he faces capital liability, and *kim lei b'drabba minei* applies. Similarly, one who injures his own *eved kena'ani* — since he owns the slave entirely, there is no one to pay; he is *patur mikulan*.

## Minor Daughter

### Father's Rights Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מֵרַב: הַחוֹבֵל בְּבַת קְטַנָּה שֶׁל אֲחֵרִים, חֲבָלָה לְמִי?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara discusses a case of compensation for injury. **Rabbi Elazar raised a dilemma before Rav:** In a case of **one who injures the minor daughter of others, to whom** does he pay compensation for the **injury?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי אָמְרִינַן: כֵּיוָן דְּאַקְנִי לֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא שֶׁבַח נְעוּרִים לְאָב – חֲבָלָה נָמֵי דַּאֲבוּהּ הָוֵי, מַאי טַעְמָא – דְּהָא אַפְחֲתַהּ מִכַּסְפַּהּ; אוֹ דִילְמָא, שֶׁבַח נְעוּרִים הוּא דְּאַקְנִי לֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא, דְּאִי בָּעֵי לְמִמְסַר לַהּ לְמוּכֵּה שְׁחִין – מָצֵי מָסַר, אֲבָל חֲבָלָה – כֵּיוָן דְּאִי בָּעֵי מִ(ת)חְבַּל בַּהּ לָא מָצֵי חָבֵיל, לָא (קַנְיֵיהּ) [אַקְנִי] לֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Elazar explains the dilemma: **Do we say** that **since the Merciful One granted the profits of** her **youth to the father,** as he receives her betrothal money, compensation for **injury also belongs to her father? What is the reason** that he should receive it? The reason is **that** the one who injured her **lowered her monetary** value, resulting in her father receiving gifts of lesser value when she is betrothed. **Or perhaps it is** only **the profits of** her **youth that the Merciful One granted to** her father, **because if he desires to give her** in marriage **to a** man **afflicted with boils, he can give** her to him, **but** with regard to **injury, since if he desires to injure her he may not injure her,** just as he may not injure any other person, **the Merciful One did not grant him** the right to receive the compensation when she is injured.

Rav Elazar posed a question to Rav: *ha'chovel b'vas ktanah shel acherim chavalah le'mi* — if someone injures the minor daughter of another man, who collects the injury payments? The question turns on the nature of the father's rights over his minor daughter. On one hand, the Torah granted the father *shevach neurim* — the benefits of his daughter's minority. He may sell her as a slave and he may betroth her, even to someone entirely unsuitable, such as one covered with boils. Since he may thus extract significant monetary benefit from her person, perhaps the *Torah*'s grant of *kol shevach neurim le'aviah* is broad enough to encompass the proceeds of an injury to her as well — for what is such a payment but compensation for reducing her value, *afchata mi'kaspa*, diminishing her worth? On the other hand, perhaps the father's rights are limited precisely to what he can achieve through the powers the Torah explicitly granted him: selling her or betrothing her. He cannot lawfully injure her himself, and therefore the Torah's grant of rights over her does not extend to the proceeds of another's wrongful injury to her body. Her body, at some fundamental level, remains her own.

Tosafot approaches the question at the *derabbanan* level: to the extent that the father receives benefits beyond the explicit biblical ones — for example, when his daughter finds a lost object — that is a rabbinic enactment designed to prevent ill-will between father and daughter, lest he grow resentful and marry her off to someone harmful. The question then becomes whether the Rabbis extended that same enactment to cover injury payments. But the *gemara* itself frames the issue in more fundamental conceptual terms: is the Torah's grant to the father structural and limited, or sweeping and categorical?

### Rav's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לֹא זִכְּתָה תּוֹרָה לָאָב, אֶלָּא שֶׁבַח נְעוּרִים בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** Rav **said to** Rabbi Elazar in response: **The Torah granted the father only the profits of** her **youth** and nothing else. Therefore, compensation for the injury goes to the daughter.

Rav answered: *lo zichsah Torah le'av ella shevach neurim bilvad* — the Torah gave the father only ordinary *shevach neurim*, and did not include *chavalah*, injury payments, in that grant. He receives the money from selling her as a slave and from betrothing her, but does not receive the proceeds of injury to her body. This makes intuitive sense: despite the remarkable power the Torah grants a father over his minor daughter, rights to the consequences of injury done to her own person are, at their core, hers.

### Eved Ivri Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: הַחוֹבֵל בְּעֶבֶד עִבְרִי – חַיָּיב בְּכוּלָּן, חוּץ מִן הַשֶּׁבֶת בִּזְמַן שֶׁהוּא שֶׁלּוֹ! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מוֹדֶה רַב בְּשֶׁבֶת, דְּמַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ עַד שְׁעַת בַּגְרוּת – דַּאֲבוּהּ הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Elazar **raised an objection to** Rav's statement based on the mishna: **One who injures a Hebrew slave** is **liable for all of** the five types of indemnity. This is **except for** compensation for **loss of livelihood** occurring **during the time that** the injured slave **belongs to** the one that injured him. This should be the *halakha* with regard to a minor daughter as well, that since the father has the right to the earnings of his minor daughter, he should receive the compensation for her loss of livelihood. **Abaye said** in response: **Rav concedes with regard to** compensation for her **loss of livelihood** that it is paid to the father, **since her earnings belong to her father until the time of** her **adulthood,** and therefore her inability to work is her father's loss.

The *gemara* challenges Rav's ruling from the *baraisa* concerning a *eved ivri*. That *baraisa* states: one who injures a *eved ivri* pays all five payments — except for *sheves* when it is your own *eved ivri*, because the master is entitled to the slave's labor and therefore the lost work time is a loss the master has already absorbed. The implication is pointed: just as the master who owns the labor rights is exempt from paying *sheves* because he is entitled to that labor, the father — who can sell his daughter into servitude and is therefore entitled to her labor — should likewise not have to pay *sheves* when he injures her. And correspondingly, if others injure her, they should pay *sheves* to the father. This seems to undercut Rav's ruling that all injury payments go to the daughter.

### Abaye Resolves

Abaye resolves the tension: Rav's ruling was never intended to address *sheves* at all. *Modeh rav besheves dema'aseh yadeha ad sha'as bagrus de'avuha havei* — Rav concedes that with respect to *sheves*, the father does collect. The father can sell his daughter into servitude until she reaches *bagrus* — approximately twelve and a half years of age. Therefore, the compensation for lost work during the period she would have been in that service belongs to the father. But *nezek*, *tza'ar*, *ripui*, and *boshet* — the payments that concern her body itself — go to the daughter. Rav's ruling thus stands, appropriately narrowed: only *sheves*, reflecting the labor rights the Torah granted the father, goes to him; all other payments remain hers.

## Minor Daughter…

### Second Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: הַחוֹבֵל בִּבְנוֹ גָּדוֹל – יִתֵּן לוֹ מִיָּד, בִּבְנוֹ קָטָן – יַעֲשֶׂה לוֹ סְגוּלָּה. הַחוֹבֵל בְּבִתּוֹ קְטַנָּה – פָּטוּר, וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא אֲחֵרִים שֶׁחָבְלוּ בָּהּ – חַיָּיבִין לִיתֵּן לְאָבִיהָ! הָכִי נָמֵי בְּשֶׁבֶת.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Elazar **raised an objection to** Rav's statement based on a *baraita*: **One who injures his adult son** must **give him** his compensation **immediately.** If one injured **his minor son he** must **make a safe investment [*segulla*] for him** with the compensation money. **One who injures his minor daughter** is **exempt, and moreover,** if there were **others who injured her,** they are **liable to give** compensation **to her father.** The Gemara answers: **So too** here, the *baraita* is speaking **with regard to** the daughter's **loss of livelihood** alone, which is paid to the father. The other types of indemnity are paid to the daughter.

The *gemara* raises a second challenge from a *baraisa* that states: one who injures his own minor daughter is *patur* — exempt; and not only that, but if others injure her, they are *chayavim liten le'aviha* — obligated to pay the father. This sounds like a blanket ruling granting the father all injury proceeds: he pays nothing when he himself injures her, and everyone else must pay him. This would seem to entirely negate Rav's position that *nezek*, *tza'ar*, *ripui*, and *boshet* belong to the daughter herself.

### Sheves Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִבְנוֹ גָּדוֹל יִתֵּן לוֹ מִיָּד? וּרְמִינְהוּ: הַחוֹבֵל בְּבָנָיו וּבִבְנוֹתָיו שֶׁל אֲחֵרִים, גְּדוֹלִים – יִתֵּן לָהֶם מִיָּד, קְטַנִּים – יַעֲשֶׂה לָהֶם סְגוּלָּה. בְּבָנָיו וּבִבְנוֹתָיו שֶׁלּוֹ – פָּטוּר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the first ruling of the *baraita*: **And** is it so that if a father injured **his adult son he** must **give him** his compensation **immediately? And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from that which is taught in another *baraita*: In a case of **one who injures the sons or daughters of others,** if they are **adults he** must **give them** their compensation **immediately;** if they are **minors he** must **make a safe investment for them.** If one injures **his own sons or daughters,** he is **exempt** from paying them compensation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: לָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן כְּשֶׁסְּמוּכִים עַל שֻׁלְחָנוֹ, כָּאן כְּשֶׁאֵין סְמוּכִין עַל שֻׁלְחָנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: This is **not difficult. Here,** where the *baraita* states that the father is exempt, it is dealing with a case **where** the children **are dependent on** their father's **table** for support. **There,** where the *baraita* states that the father is liable, it is dealing with a case **where** the children **are not dependent on his table.**

The *gemara* reinterprets this *baraisa* in a remarkably narrow fashion. *Hachi nami besheves* — here too, the *baraisa* is speaking only about *sheves*, only about lost labor. The father's exemption when he injures his daughter, and others' obligation to pay him when they injure her, refers exclusively to *sheves*. The payments tied to her own body — permanent injury, pain, medical expenses, embarrassment — remain hers. The *gemara*'s reading is striking: an apparently sweeping statement about the father being exempt and others being *chayav* to pay him is compressed entirely into the single category of labor compensation.

### Unsupported Daughter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתַּהּ לְקַמַּיְיתָא – בְּשֶׁאֵין סְמוּכִים עַל שֻׁלְחָנוֹ? אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: הַחוֹבֵל בְּבִתּוֹ הַקְּטַנָּה – פָּטוּר; וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא אֲחֵרִים שֶׁחָבְלוּ בָּהּ – חַיָּיבִין לִיתֵּן לְאָבִיהָ. לְדִידַהּ בָּעֵי לְמִיתַּב לַהּ – דְּבַעְיָא מְזוֹנֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this resolution of the two *baraitot*: **In what** manner **did you interpret the first** *baraita*? You interpreted it as dealing with a case **where** the children **are not dependent on his table? If so, say the latter clause** of that *baraita*: **One who injures his minor daughter** is **exempt, and moreover,** if there were **others who injured her,** they are **liable to give** compensation **to her father.** If this is a case where the daughter is not dependent upon her father for support, then the one who injured her would be **required to give** the compensation **to her, because she needs** to provide her own **sustenance.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: יָכוֹל הָרַב לוֹמַר לָעֶבֶד ״עֲשֵׂה עִמִּי וְאֵינִי זָנָךְ״; הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּעֶבֶד כְּנַעֲנִי – דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: עֲבֵיד עֲבִידְתָּא כּוּלֵּי יוֹמָא, וּלְאוּרְתָּא זִיל סְחַר וֶאֱכוֹל; אֲבָל עֶבֶד עִבְרִי, דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי טוֹב לוֹ עִמָּךְ״ – עִמְּךָ בְּמַאֲכָל, עִמְּךָ בְּמִשְׁתֶּה – לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן בִּתּוֹ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its challenge. **And even according to the one who says** that **a master can say to** his **slave: Work for me but I will not feed you,** i.e., a master is not legally obligated to provide sustenance for his slave, **this matter applies** only **to a Canaanite slave, as** the master can **say to him: Work** for me **the entire day, and at night go around,** beg, **and eat. But** in the case of **a Hebrew slave, as it is written** with regard to him: **"Because he fares well with you"** (Deuteronomy 15:16), indicating that the Hebrew slave must be **"with you" in food** and **"with you" in drink,** i.e., the Hebrew slave is entitled to live with his master as an equal, the master may **not** compel the slave to serve him unless he feeds him. **All the more so** is it not the case with regard to **his daughter,** that she is not required to beg for her sustenance and should receive the compensation herself?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְאָמַר רָבָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב עוּלָּא: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לְהַעְדָּפָה; הָכָא נָמֵי, לָא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לְהַעְדָּפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **as Rava, son of Rav Ulla, said** concerning a similar matter (*Ketubot* 43a): This *halakha* is **necessary only for** the **surplus,** i.e., money that a woman earns beyond what she needs for her essential sustenance. **Here too,** this *halakha* is **necessary only for** the **surplus.** If the compensation for injury is more than the daughter requires for her sustenance, the additional sum is paid to her father.

One further complication arises: what if the father is not supporting his daughter? The *gemara* observes that if he does not feed her, *ledida baei lemeisav lah debaei mezonei* — he should give her the *sheves* payment because she needs food. If he will not support her, she retains rights to her own earnings. The *gemara* draws on the analogy of an *eved ivri*, for whom the Torah requires equality — *imach bema'achal imach bemishteh* — and concludes that a *kol sheken* applies to the father's daughter: one cannot demand labor while refusing sustenance.

However, the *gemara* resolves this through the principle stated by Rava the son of Rav Ulla in a different context: *lo nitzrecha ela le'adafa* — we are speaking only about the *surplus* labor beyond what the daughter needs to feed herself. Even if the father does not support her, she retains the right to earnings sufficient for her own food; everything beyond that still belongs to the father. So if she earns substantially more than her food costs, the excess goes to the father — and therefore, with respect to the *sheves* corresponding to that excess, he remains entitled: he is *patur* when he injures her, and others pay him when they injure her. The portion covering her own sustenance, however, she keeps.

## Sons & Daughters

### Sons Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתַּהּ לְבָתְרָיְיתָא – בִּסְמוּכִים עַל שֻׁלְחָנוֹ? ״גְּדוֹלִים – יִתֵּן לָהֶם מִיָּד, קְטַנִּים – יַעֲשֶׂה לָהֶם סְגוּלָּה״, אַמַּאי? לַאֲבִיהֶם בָּעֵי לְמִיתַּב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara again challenges the resolution of the two *baraitot*: **In what** manner **did you interpret the latter** *baraita* discussing one who injures the children of another? You interpreted it as dealing with a case **where** the sons **are dependent on** their father's **table.** But the *baraita* also stated: If they are **adults, he** must **give them** their compensation **immediately;** if they are **minors, he** must **make a safe investment for them. Why** does he make a safe investment for them? Shouldn't he be **required to give** the compensation **to their father?**

A *baraisa* addresses the case of sons. One who injures his adult son must pay him immediately — *yiten lo miyad*. One who injures his minor son must set aside the funds as a *segulah* — a trust or sacred investment held for the child's benefit, since paying immediately risks the child squandering the money. One who injures his own sons and daughters, whether adult or minor, is *patur* — exempt.

### Contradiction

This creates an immediate difficulty: how can a father be exempt for injuring his own children, whether minors or adults? There is no principle comparable to *kim lei b'drabba minei* at work here, and the blanket exemption seems inexplicable.

### Support = Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: כִּי קָא קָפֵיד – בְּמִידֵּי דְּקָא חָסַר, בְּמִידֵּי דַּאֲתָא מֵעָלְמָא – לָא קָפֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: **Where** the father **is particular** about receiving money that would go to his child is specifically **in a matter that causes** him **a loss,** as in a case where the father himself injured his child and would need to pay compensation. But **in a matter that comes from elsewhere,** as in a case where someone else injured his child, he **is not particular** about receiving the money, and it is paid to the child.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מְצִיאָה, דְּמֵעָלְמָא קָאָתֵי לְהוּ, וְקָא קָפֵיד! אָמְרִי: רַוְוחָא דְּקָאָתֵי לְהוּ מֵעָלְמָא, וְלֵית לְהוּ צַעֲרָא דְּגוּפַיְיהוּ בְּגַוַּוהּ – קָפֵיד; אֲבָל חֲבָלָה, דְּאִית לְהוּ צַעֲרָא דְּגוּפַיְיהוּ, וּמֵעָלְמָא קָאָתֵי לְהוּ – לָא קָפֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges that response: **But** isn't **a found** item a matter **that comes to** the children **from elsewhere, and** the father **is particular** about receiving it? The Sages **say** in response: With regard to **profit that comes to** the children **from elsewhere, and they do not** suffer **physical pain in** obtaining **it,** the father **is particular** about receiving the money, as he does not feel that the children deserve it. **But** in the case of **an injury, where they** suffer **physical pain and it comes to them from elsewhere,** the father **is not particular** about receiving the compensation paid to his child.

The *gemara* resolves: *lo kashya — kan keshemuchim al shulchano, kan keshein smuchim al shulchano*. It depends entirely on whether the father is supporting his children. When they eat at his table — *smuchim al shulchano* — he is exempt. When he does not support them, he is *chayav*. The entire framework, as Tosafot explains, concerns only *sheves*. It would be unthinkable to suggest that just because a father feeds his children, he need not pay for the pain, suffering, embarrassment, or permanent injury he causes them. The operative question is whether he has rights to their labor.

### Father's Labor Rights

The logic proceeds as follows. When a father supports his children, he is — as a *Chazal*-structured reciprocity — entitled to their *ma'aseh yadayim*, their labor. He is also entitled to their *metziah*, any found objects they discover. If someone else injures his supported children, their *sheves* payment technically belongs to him, since he holds rights to their work. But if *he* injures them, he does not pay *sheves* because paying *sheves* would simply mean paying himself — he would receive the labor compensation that is already his.

### Unsupported Daughter

The underage daughter presents a special case even when the father does not support her. As established above, when he does not feed her, she is entitled to retain her earnings up to the cost of her own sustenance. But the father retains rights to the *surplus* beyond her food costs. He is therefore still *chayav* in the role of a *gavra kapdana* — a demanding person who insists on his rights even from the outside — because those surplus earnings are genuinely his. Correspondingly, others who injure her pay the *sheves* of that surplus to him, not to her.

### Windfall Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הָתָם, דְּאִית לַהּ צַעֲרָא דְּגוּפָא, וּמֵעָלְמָא קָאָתֵי לַהּ, וְקָא קָפֵיד! דְּקָתָנֵי: וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא (אֲפִילּוּ) אֲחֵרִים שֶׁחָבְלוּ בָּהּ – חַיָּיבִין לִיתֵּן לְאָבִיהָ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But there,** in the first *baraita*, isn't it discussing a case **where** the daughter suffers **physical pain, and** it is a matter that **comes to her from elsewhere, and** the father **is particular** about receiving the money, **as it teaches: And moreover,** if there were **others who injured her,** they are **liable to give** compensation **to her father?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הָתָם, דְּגַבְרָא קַפְדָנָא הוּא, דְּהָא אֵין סְמוּכִין עַל שֻׁלְחָנוֹ – אֲפִילּוּ בְּמִידֵּי דְּאָתֵי לְהוּ מֵעָלְמָא קָפֵיד. הָכָא, דְּלָאו גַּבְרָא קַפְדָנָא הוּא, דְּהָא סְמוּכִין עַל שֻׁלְחָנוֹ; כִּי קָא קָפֵיד – בְּמִידֵּי דְּקָא חָסַר לֵיהּ, בְּמִידֵּי דְּאָתֵי לְהוּ מֵעָלְמָא – לָא קָפֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: **There,** the *baraita* discusses a case **where** the father **is a captious man,** which is evident from the case of the *baraita* itself, **as** his children **are not dependent on his table.** A man like this **is particular even about matters that come to them from elsewhere.** By contrast, **here,** in the latter *baraita*, it discusses a case **where** the father **is not a captious man,** which is evident from the case of the *baraita* itself, **as** his children **are dependent on his table. When he is particular** about receiving the money, it is **in a matter that causes him a loss,** but **in a matter that comes to them from elsewhere,** he **is not particular.**

The *gemara* then confronts an apparent inconsistency: we know that when a father supports his children, he also takes their *metziah* — found objects — even though those too are windfalls from the outside. If he takes a *metziah* even though it comes from the outside, why does he yield on *sheves*? The *gemara* answers: *revacha dekati me'alma veleit lehu tza'ara degufaihu begavah kapid* — for a pure windfall that involves no suffering on their part, such as finding a lost object, the father demands and receives it. *Aval chavala de'it lehu tza'ara degufaihu* — but for injury payments, where they are themselves suffering bodily pain, and the money is coming from the outside, *lo kapid* — he does not demand it, and they keep it.

The full picture thus resolves into three distinct situations. First, when the father does not support his children: for adult children and sons generally, they are fully independent and collect everything themselves; if he injures them, he pays them fully. For his minor daughter, even without supporting her, he retains rights to her surplus earnings; he does not pay *sheves* for the surplus, and others pay him that *sheves* — because this father, having abandoned his support obligation, is indeed a *gavra kapdana* who insists on what is his even when it is a windfall and his daughter is suffering. Second, when he supports his children: if he injures them, he pays nothing in *sheves* because he owns their labor — paying himself would be circular. If others injure them, the children keep the *sheves* payment because the father, who is generous enough to support them, is not *makpid* on a windfall that accompanies their suffering. Third, the *metziah* distinction: found objects, being pure gain with no associated suffering, the father does insist on receiving.

### Segula Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״סְגוּלָּה״? רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה. רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: דִּיקְלָא דְּאָכֵיל מִינֵּיהּ תַּמְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The two *baraitot* stated that the father makes a safe investment for his minor children with the compensation paid to them. The Gemara asks: **What** is meant by **a safe investment? Rav Ḥisda says:** The father should purchase **a Torah scroll** for his child. **Rabba bar Rav Huna, says:** The father should purchase **a date palm, from which** the child **will consume dates.**

Finally, the *gemara* turns to the question of what precisely constitutes the *segulah* — the trust arrangement used when a minor child is to receive an injury payment. Rav Chisda says: a *Sefer Torah*. A *Sefer Torah* retains its value, carries religious significance, can be used for learning, and represents both a sound spiritual and financial investment. Rabba bar Rav Huna offers a more practical alternative: *dikla de'achil minei tamrei* — a palm tree from which they can eat the dates. This is essentially a productive asset: the principal is preserved and generates ongoing income. Reish Lakish concurs with this latter approach.
