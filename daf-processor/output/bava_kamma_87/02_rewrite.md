# Bava Kamma 87 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Suma & Boshet

### Mishnah Overview

The *mishnah* on 86b, beginning with the words *hamvayesh et hasuma*, establishes that one who embarrasses a blind person or a naked person is still *chayav* in *boshet* — embarrassment damages are owed regardless of the victim's condition. The only exemption the *mishnah* articulates in its second clause is for one who causes embarrassment while asleep, because *boshet* requires *kavanah*, and a sleeping person lacks the requisite intent. By mentioning only the sleeping person as exempt, the *mishnah* implies that all other actors — including a blind person who embarrasses someone — remain liable.

### Suma Embarrassing Others

The *gemara* picks up on this implication and asks whether everyone agrees that a blind person who embarrasses another is *chayav*. Both Rashi and Tosafot understand the *gemara*'s question not to be whether one is liable *for* embarrassing a blind person — that much is clear from the *mishnah* — but rather whether a *suma* who himself causes embarrassment would be *chayav* or exempt. The *gemara* answers: *matnisin d'lo k'Rabbi Yehuda* — our *mishnah*, which implies that a blind person who embarrasses is liable, does not accord with the view of Rabbi Yehuda. For as a *baraisa* teaches: Rabbi Yehuda says that a blind person has no *boshet* — he is exempt when he causes embarrassment. Moreover, Rabbi Yehuda would similarly exempt a *suma* from *galut* if he killed someone unintentionally, from *malkut* if he violated a negative prohibition, and from *mitas beis din* if he committed a capital offense. This is a sweeping, categorical exemption — not a case-by-case inquiry into whether this particular blind person could have been more careful.

### Gezeirah Shavah Source

The *gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama d'Rabbi Yehuda* — what is the scriptural basis for his position regarding *boshet*? The answer is a *gezeirah shavah*: the word *einecha* ("your eye") appears both in the passage concerning *eidim zomemin* (conspiring witnesses) and in the passage concerning *boshet* — the verse *vekatzota kapa lo tachos einecha*. This verbal parallel links the two laws. And since the entire *parshah* of *eidim zomemin* applies only to witnesses who can see — for the simple reality is that a blind person cannot serve as a valid witness — so too the obligation of *boshet* applies only to one who can see. The shared language of *einecha* creates the link, and the structural logic of *eidim zomemin* being categorically closed to the blind imports that same categorical exclusion into *boshet*.

### Broader Exemptions

The *gemara* then works through the rest of Rabbi Yehuda's exemptions. His exclusion of the *suma* from *galut* is derived from the verse *belo re'ot* — "without seeing" — in the context of one who kills another in the forest. Rabbi Yehuda reads this to mean that the *parshah* of exile applies only to one who normally could have seen his victim but on this occasion did not; a blind person, who categorically cannot see, is therefore excluded entirely. Rabbi Meir disagrees and reads *belo re'ot* as *including* the blind person — meaning even one who cannot see is liable for *galut*. Since another verse uses the phrase *bivli da'at* ("without awareness"), which could also be read as excluding the *suma*, Rabbi Meir invokes the rule that *mi'ut achar mi'ut* — two consecutive exclusions — actually serves to *include*. Tosafot notes the peculiarity here: removing both exclusions merely returns one to the default position of inclusion, so the double *mi'ut* seems to accomplish nothing new. Regardless, for Rabbi Meir the *suma* is included in *galut*. Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, holds that *belo re'ot* genuinely excludes the blind, and *bivli da'at* serves the separate and more obvious purpose of excluding the intentional killer — who faces execution, not exile.

The exemption from *mitas beis din* is derived through a *gezeirah shavah* of *rotze'ach rotze'ach* — the word appears both in the context of *galut* and in the context of capital liability, linking them so that the *suma*'s categorical exclusion from one extends to the other. The exemption from *malkut* is then derived through a *gezeirah shavah* of *rasha rasha* — the verse by *mitas beis din* reads *asher hu rasha lamut*, and the verse establishing *malkut* uses the same word *rasha*. Since a *suma* cannot be executed, by the verbal parallel he also cannot receive lashes.

### Rabbi Meir Disagrees

The full structure of Rabbi Meir's position becomes clear from his reading of *belo re'ot*. For Rabbi Meir, no blanket categorical exclusion of the *suma* follows from that phrase; he reads it as including the blind person. The double-exclusion argument is his mechanism for restoring the *suma* to the default of liability. Rabbi Yehuda, however, uses each scriptural peg — *belo re'ot* for *galut*, the *gezeirah shavah* of *einecha* for *boshet*, the *rotze'ach rotze'ach* for capital liability, and the *rasha rasha* for *malkut* — to build a comprehensive categorical exclusion from all court-administered penalties.

### Total Exemption

A second *baraisa* extends this even further. Rabbi Yehuda not only exempts the *suma* from all punishments but, as the *gemara* on 87a begins, declares: *vechen haya Rabbi Yehuda potro mikol dinim shebatorah* — Rabbi Yehuda would exempt him from all civil law as well. This is a qualitatively different category. The earlier exemptions concerned intentional conduct and court-administered penalties; civil law governs monetary obligations that do not depend on intention in the same way. The scriptural basis offered for this expansion comes from the verse in the context of *galut*: *veshaftu ha'edah bein hamakeh uvein go'el hadam al hamishpatim ha'eleh* — the congregation shall judge between the killer and the blood-redeemer according to "these laws." The *gemara* derives: whoever is in the category of *galut* — that is, subject to exile — is also in the category of civil *mishpatim*; whoever is excluded from *galut* is excluded from *mishpatim* as well. For Rabbi Yehuda the *suma* is excluded from *galut*, and therefore excluded from civil law entirely.

A third *baraisa* takes the final step: *vechen haya Rabbi Yehuda potro mikol mitzvos ha'amuros batorah* — Rabbi Yehuda would exempt him from all *mitzvos* in the Torah. Rav Sheshes the son of Rav Idi supplies the scriptural basis: the verse *ve'eleh hamitzvos hachukim vehamishpatim* links *mitzvos* and *mishpatim* in a single formulation. Whoever is in the category of *mishpatim* is in the category of *mitzvos* and *chukim*; whoever is excluded from *mishpatim* — as Rabbi Yehuda has shown the *suma* to be — is excluded from *mitzvos* as well. Tosafot, struck by the radical sweep of this position, maintains that *Chazal* must have obligated the *suma* at least *miderabbanan*, and that other *gemaros* make clear that a *suma* may recite *Krias Shema* and daven. But the position of Rabbi Yehuda at the level of *de'oraisa* is nevertheless a sweeping categorical exemption that functionally places the blind person in the same legal category as a *cheresh* and *shoteh*.

### Rav Yosef's Stance

The *gemara* then records a poignant personal statement by Rav Yosef, who was himself blind. He said: initially I would say that if anyone holds the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda — that a *suma* is exempt from *mitzvos* — I would make a *yom tov* for the rabbis. Why? Because I am not commanded yet I do, and I assumed that performing *mitzvos* without being obligated would be regarded as an act of exceptional voluntarism deserving great reward. But now, having heard the teaching of Rabbi Chanina — that greater is one who is commanded and does than one who is not commanded and does — I have reversed course. Now I would make a *yom tov* if someone tells me the *halacha* does *not* follow Rabbi Yehuda, because if I am commanded, my reward is greater.

### Metzuve vs. Eino Metzuve

The principle of *metzuve ve'oseh* versus *eino metzuve ve'oseh* that Rabbi Chanina articulates is well established, and two distinct reasons are given for why the commanded one receives greater reward. Rashi's approach is psychological: if you are obligated, the *yetzer hara* is more active, the pull to find an exemption is stronger, and therefore overcoming that resistance to fulfill the command is a greater achievement. A second approach is theological: if God commanded certain people to perform a given act and did not command others, this reflects that performing it is more fully aligned with *ratzon Hashem* for those who are commanded. If God had wanted the voluntary performer's act to be equally fundamental to His will, He would have commanded it. This theological framework has direct implications for how the *rishonim* of Ashkenaz understood women's *brachos* on *mitzvos asei shehazman gerama*: just as Rav Yosef, even if exempt, would have continued performing *mitzvos* and reciting the *bracha* of *asher kidshanu bemitzvotav vetzivanu*, so too women who are exempt may recite that *bracha* when performing those *mitzvos*.

## Five Damages

### Adam vs. Shor

The *gemara* transitions to the next *mishnah*: *zeh chomer b'adam mibshor* — this is a stringency by a human over an ox. A human who injures another human must pay all five standard categories of damage: *nezek* (permanent injury), *tza'ar* (pain), *ripui* (medical expenses), *sheves* (lost work), and *boshet* (embarrassment). Furthermore, a human who caused a woman to miscarry must pay *dmei velados*, an assessed value for the fetuses. By contrast, an ox that gores a human pays only *nezek*, and if it causes a woman to miscarry, it pays nothing for the lost fetuses.

### Kiyum Lei Doctrine

The *mishnah* then introduces cases touching on the principle of *kim lei b'drabba minei* — when one's conduct simultaneously incurs capital liability, the lesser monetary obligation is subsumed. The *mishnah* presents: one who struck his father or mother but did not actually draw blood (*chavurah*) — which is required for the capital offense — and one who wounded another on *Yom Kippur*, which carries *kares* but not *mitas beis din* — in both these cases the injurer is *chayav b'kullan*, obligated in all five payments, because neither case triggers actual capital liability. The principle of *kim lei b'drabba minei* does not apply where the more severe liability (*mitas beis din*) is absent; *chiyuv kares* alone does not displace the monetary obligation.

### Eved Ivri Injury

One who injures a *eved ivri* must pay all five payments — *nezek*, *tza'ar*, *ripui*, *boshet*, and *sheves* — with one exception. An *eved ivri* is a full Jewish person; the master holds only rights to his labor, not to his person. Therefore *nezek*, *tza'ar*, *ripui*, and *boshet* all go to the slave himself. The sole exception is *sheves*, the compensation for time out of work: if it is your own *eved ivri*, that payment is exempt, because the master is the one losing the benefit of the slave's labor during his recovery — paying *sheves* would simply be paying oneself. If, however, it is someone *else's* *eved ivri* who is injured, the third-party injurer must pay *sheves* to the master, because the master is the party actually sustaining the loss of labor.

### Eved Kena'ani Injury

A *Canaanite slave* is legally the full property of his master, not merely his labor. Therefore one who injures another person's *eved kena'ani* must pay all five damages, but pays them to the master, who owns the slave entirely. Tosafot discusses at length the question of the medical bill: since payment for *ripui* goes to the master, the master remains obligated to ensure the slave is actually healed, or alternatively that sum is paid directly to the slave for his treatment. The *mishnah* also records that Rabbi Yehuda holds *ein l'avadim boshet* — slaves do not collect *boshet*. Rather than examining case by case whether a particular slave experienced embarrassment, Rabbi Yehuda applies a categorical exclusion: slaves do not collect embarrassment damages.

## Categorical Exempts

### Cheresh Shoteh Katan

The *mishnah* states: *cheresh shoteh v'katan pgi'atan ra'ah* — it is bad to have a violent encounter with a deaf-mute, a mentally ill person, or a minor. If you injure them you are fully *chayav*: they can suffer, they incur medical expenses, they experience pain and embarrassment. But if they injure others, they are exempt — *patur* — because they do not bear legal responsibility for their actions. The *cheresh* as understood by the *gemara* is one who, lacking hearing and speech, did not develop full intellectual capacity; the *shoteh* is one with mental illness; the *katan* is a minor. None of them are held liable for their tortious conduct.

### Woman & Slave Exempt

A woman and a slave (*eved kena'ani*) present a related but distinct case. *Ha'eved v'ha'ishah pgi'atan ra'ah* — it is also bad to have a violent encounter with them, because if you injure them you are *chayav*. But if they injure others, they are *patur* — not because they lack legal capacity, as the *cheresh shoteh v'katan* do, but because they have no collectible property. A woman's property is largely under her husband's control; a slave owns nothing, as *mah she'kanah eved kanah rabo* — whatever a slave acquires is acquired by his master. There is therefore nothing to collect from them, even though in principle they bear legal responsibility.

### Future Liability

Nevertheless, this exemption is not permanent. *Aval m'shalmim l'achar zman* — they will pay at a later time. Once a woman is divorced (*nitgarshah ha'ishah*) and comes into her own property, and once a slave is freed (*nishtachrer ha'eved*), they become *chayavim l'shalem* — obligated to pay for damages they caused while exempt.

The *mishnah* then loops back in a chiastic structure to a case implicating *kim lei b'drabba minei*: one who strikes his father or mother and actually draws blood (*asah bahen chavurah*), or one who wounds another on *Shabbos* and draws blood — in those cases the injurer is *patur mikulan mipnei shehu nidon b'nafsho*: exempt from all monetary payments because he faces capital liability, and *kim lei b'drabba minei* applies. Similarly, one who injures his own *eved kena'ani* — since he owns the slave entirely, there is no one to pay; he is *patur mikulan*.

## Minor Daughter

### Father's Rights Query

Rav Elazar posed a question to Rav: *ha'chovel b'vas ktanah shel acherim chavalah le'mi* — if someone injures the minor daughter of another man, who collects the injury payments? The question turns on the nature of the father's rights over his minor daughter. On one hand, the Torah granted the father *shevach neurim* — the benefits of his daughter's minority. He may sell her as a slave and he may betroth her, even to someone entirely unsuitable, such as one covered with boils. Since he may thus extract significant monetary benefit from her person, perhaps the *Torah*'s grant of *kol shevach neurim le'aviah* is broad enough to encompass the proceeds of an injury to her as well — for what is such a payment but compensation for reducing her value, *afchata mi'kaspa*, diminishing her worth? On the other hand, perhaps the father's rights are limited precisely to what he can achieve through the powers the Torah explicitly granted him: selling her or betrothing her. He cannot lawfully injure her himself, and therefore the Torah's grant of rights over her does not extend to the proceeds of another's wrongful injury to her body. Her body, at some fundamental level, remains her own.

Tosafot approaches the question at the *derabbanan* level: to the extent that the father receives benefits beyond the explicit biblical ones — for example, when his daughter finds a lost object — that is a rabbinic enactment designed to prevent ill-will between father and daughter, lest he grow resentful and marry her off to someone harmful. The question then becomes whether the Rabbis extended that same enactment to cover injury payments. But the *gemara* itself frames the issue in more fundamental conceptual terms: is the Torah's grant to the father structural and limited, or sweeping and categorical?

### Rav's Answer

Rav answered: *lo zichsah Torah le'av ella shevach neurim bilvad* — the Torah gave the father only ordinary *shevach neurim*, and did not include *chavalah*, injury payments, in that grant. He receives the money from selling her as a slave and from betrothing her, but does not receive the proceeds of injury to her body. This makes intuitive sense: despite the remarkable power the Torah grants a father over his minor daughter, rights to the consequences of injury done to her own person are, at their core, hers.

### Eved Ivri Challenge

The *gemara* challenges Rav's ruling from the *baraisa* concerning a *eved ivri*. That *baraisa* states: one who injures a *eved ivri* pays all five payments — except for *sheves* when it is your own *eved ivri*, because the master is entitled to the slave's labor and therefore the lost work time is a loss the master has already absorbed. The implication is pointed: just as the master who owns the labor rights is exempt from paying *sheves* because he is entitled to that labor, the father — who can sell his daughter into servitude and is therefore entitled to her labor — should likewise not have to pay *sheves* when he injures her. And correspondingly, if others injure her, they should pay *sheves* to the father. This seems to undercut Rav's ruling that all injury payments go to the daughter.

### Abaye Resolves

Abaye resolves the tension: Rav's ruling was never intended to address *sheves* at all. *Modeh rav besheves dema'aseh yadeha ad sha'as bagrus de'avuha havei* — Rav concedes that with respect to *sheves*, the father does collect. The father can sell his daughter into servitude until she reaches *bagrus* — approximately twelve and a half years of age. Therefore, the compensation for lost work during the period she would have been in that service belongs to the father. But *nezek*, *tza'ar*, *ripui*, and *boshet* — the payments that concern her body itself — go to the daughter. Rav's ruling thus stands, appropriately narrowed: only *sheves*, reflecting the labor rights the Torah granted the father, goes to him; all other payments remain hers.

## Minor Daughter (II)

### Second Challenge

The *gemara* raises a second challenge from a *baraisa* that states: one who injures his own minor daughter is *patur* — exempt; and not only that, but if others injure her, they are *chayavim liten le'aviha* — obligated to pay the father. This sounds like a blanket ruling granting the father all injury proceeds: he pays nothing when he himself injures her, and everyone else must pay him. This would seem to entirely negate Rav's position that *nezek*, *tza'ar*, *ripui*, and *boshet* belong to the daughter herself.

### Sheves Only

The *gemara* reinterprets this *baraisa* in a remarkably narrow fashion. *Hachi nami besheves* — here too, the *baraisa* is speaking only about *sheves*, only about lost labor. The father's exemption when he injures his daughter, and others' obligation to pay him when they injure her, refers exclusively to *sheves*. The payments tied to her own body — permanent injury, pain, medical expenses, embarrassment — remain hers. The *gemara*'s reading is striking: an apparently sweeping statement about the father being exempt and others being *chayav* to pay him is compressed entirely into the single category of labor compensation.

### Unsupported Daughter

One further complication arises: what if the father is not supporting his daughter? The *gemara* observes that if he does not feed her, *ledida baei lemeisav lah debaei mezonei* — he should give her the *sheves* payment because she needs food. If he will not support her, she retains rights to her own earnings. The *gemara* draws on the analogy of an *eved ivri*, for whom the Torah requires equality — *imach bema'achal imach bemishteh* — and concludes that a *kol sheken* applies to the father's daughter: one cannot demand labor while refusing sustenance.

However, the *gemara* resolves this through the principle stated by Rava the son of Rav Ulla in a different context: *lo nitzrecha ela le'adafa* — we are speaking only about the *surplus* labor beyond what the daughter needs to feed herself. Even if the father does not support her, she retains the right to earnings sufficient for her own food; everything beyond that still belongs to the father. So if she earns substantially more than her food costs, the excess goes to the father — and therefore, with respect to the *sheves* corresponding to that excess, he remains entitled: he is *patur* when he injures her, and others pay him when they injure her. The portion covering her own sustenance, however, she keeps.

## Sons & Daughters

### Sons Payment

A *baraisa* addresses the case of sons. One who injures his adult son must pay him immediately — *yiten lo miyad*. One who injures his minor son must set aside the funds as a *segulah* — a trust or sacred investment held for the child's benefit, since paying immediately risks the child squandering the money. One who injures his own sons and daughters, whether adult or minor, is *patur* — exempt.

### Contradiction

This creates an immediate difficulty: how can a father be exempt for injuring his own children, whether minors or adults? There is no principle comparable to *kim lei b'drabba minei* at work here, and the blanket exemption seems inexplicable.

### Support = Rights

The *gemara* resolves: *lo kashya — kan keshemuchim al shulchano, kan keshein smuchim al shulchano*. It depends entirely on whether the father is supporting his children. When they eat at his table — *smuchim al shulchano* — he is exempt. When he does not support them, he is *chayav*. The entire framework, as Tosafot explains, concerns only *sheves*. It would be unthinkable to suggest that just because a father feeds his children, he need not pay for the pain, suffering, embarrassment, or permanent injury he causes them. The operative question is whether he has rights to their labor.

### Father's Labor Rights

The logic proceeds as follows. When a father supports his children, he is — as a *Chazal*-structured reciprocity — entitled to their *ma'aseh yadayim*, their labor. He is also entitled to their *metziah*, any found objects they discover. If someone else injures his supported children, their *sheves* payment technically belongs to him, since he holds rights to their work. But if *he* injures them, he does not pay *sheves* because paying *sheves* would simply mean paying himself — he would receive the labor compensation that is already his.

### Unsupported Daughter

The underage daughter presents a special case even when the father does not support her. As established above, when he does not feed her, she is entitled to retain her earnings up to the cost of her own sustenance. But the father retains rights to the *surplus* beyond her food costs. He is therefore still *chayav* in the role of a *gavra kapdana* — a demanding person who insists on his rights even from the outside — because those surplus earnings are genuinely his. Correspondingly, others who injure her pay the *sheves* of that surplus to him, not to her.

### Windfall Exception

The *gemara* then asks: if the father is supporting his children and is therefore entitled to their labor and their *metziah*, why do *others* who injure them pay *sheves* to the *children* rather than to the father? The answer: *ki ka kapid bemidi dekachasar, bemidi de'ati me'alma lo kapid* — the father demands and is *makpid* when it requires him to reach into his own pocket. When he injures his children, he does not pay *sheves* because doing so would mean paying out of his own funds for something he is owed. But when the money comes from the outside — from a third party who injured them — and his children are suffering physical pain, he is not *makpid*. He lets them keep it. In that case, even though he is technically entitled to their labor, he relinquishes the *sheves* payment because it is both a *windfall* and compensation for their personal suffering.

The *gemara* then confronts an apparent inconsistency: we know that when a father supports his children, he also takes their *metziah* — found objects — even though those too are windfalls from the outside. If he takes a *metziah* even though it comes from the outside, why does he yield on *sheves*? The *gemara* answers: *revacha dekati me'alma veleit lehu tza'ara degufaihu begavah kapid* — for a pure windfall that involves no suffering on their part, such as finding a lost object, the father demands and receives it. *Aval chavala de'it lehu tza'ara degufaihu* — but for injury payments, where they are themselves suffering bodily pain, and the money is coming from the outside, *lo kapid* — he does not demand it, and they keep it.

The full picture thus resolves into three distinct situations. First, when the father does not support his children: for adult children and sons generally, they are fully independent and collect everything themselves; if he injures them, he pays them fully. For his minor daughter, even without supporting her, he retains rights to her surplus earnings; he does not pay *sheves* for the surplus, and others pay him that *sheves* — because this father, having abandoned his support obligation, is indeed a *gavra kapdana* who insists on what is his even when it is a windfall and his daughter is suffering. Second, when he supports his children: if he injures them, he pays nothing in *sheves* because he owns their labor — paying himself would be circular. If others injure them, the children keep the *sheves* payment because the father, who is generous enough to support them, is not *makpid* on a windfall that accompanies their suffering. Third, the *metziah* distinction: found objects, being pure gain with no associated suffering, the father does insist on receiving.

### Segula Definition

Finally, the *gemara* turns to the question of what precisely constitutes the *segulah* — the trust arrangement used when a minor child is to receive an injury payment. Rav Chisda says: a *Sefer Torah*. A *Sefer Torah* retains its value, carries religious significance, can be used for learning, and represents both a sound spiritual and financial investment. Rabba bar Rav Huna offers a more practical alternative: *dikla de'achil minei tamrei* — a palm tree from which they can eat the dates. This is essentially a productive asset: the principal is preserved and generates ongoing income. Reish Lakish concurs with this latter approach.
