# Bava Metzia 74 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Deferred Payment Fix

### Watchmen Payment

Today's daf is 74, though we actually pick up near the bottom of 73a with the words *amar lei Rava l'hanei d'mantrei bagi* — Rava said to those who watched the valleys where the wheat was growing. These watchmen were employed to guard the grain fields, but their payment was deferred until after the harvest had been cut, threshed, and brought to market — that is, when the owners actually had money to pay them. In the meantime, the owners would throw in a little extra: instead of the wage that was strictly owed, they would pay somewhat more to compensate for the wait. This creates a problem of *ribbis*: the additional payment functions as interest on the deferred salary.

### Nominal Work Solution

Rava's solution is elegant and practical. He told these watchmen: *puku hafichu b'hadei dari* — go out and turn over the grain at the threshing floor. Continue doing some nominal, trivial work for the owner right up until the moment he is ready to pay you. The operative principle is *d'sechar eino mishtalem ela l'vasof* — payment for service is only discharged at the end of the service. By extending their involvement, even in a perfunctory way, through the threshing period, the watchmen's service is technically not concluded until the moment of payment.

### Service Extension

Once the service formally extends to the point of payment, the extra compensation the owner gives is no longer characterized as interest for waiting. The Gemara expresses this with the phrase *azulei hu d'ka mozil gabaichu* — he is simply selling his grain to you cheaply, or equivalently, being overly generous with you. Once there is no gap between the end of service and the moment of payment, whatever excess he gives you is merely generosity, not deferred *ribbis*. It is only when a worker has already stopped working some time ago, and then receives extra payment later, that the additional amount is cast as compensation for the delay — which constitutes *ribbis*. The practical instruction, then, is clear: keep doing some nominal work until the time of payment, and then if the owner wishes to be generous, that is entirely his prerogative.

### Payment Delay Q&A

This analysis raises a broader question: why can a worker ever agree to defer payment in the first place? The answer is that the prohibition of *halanat sechar sachir* — withholding a worker's wages — can be contractually waived; a worker may arrange in advance to receive payment at a later date. That said, the arrangement carries real risk of abuse. If the employer's market gives him leverage, he might systematically pre-arrange deferred payment and then add extra, effectively charging interest on the wage debt. The further observation that follows naturally from this is that in any commercial society, the prohibition of *ribbis* creates acute difficulties. Purchasing an automobile on installment payments from a Jewish seller, with an interest charge built into the monthly payments, is a paradigm case of *ribbis*. The entire framework of modern consumer credit, and indeed the structure of bank deposits bearing interest, falls within these concerns — hence the reliance in modern Israel and elsewhere on instruments such as the *heter iska*.

## Sharecropper Rights

### Rabbis Challenge

The Gemara then records a challenge directed at Rava himself. The Rabbis said to him: *ka-akhil mar ribbis* — it appears that the master is eating *ribbis*. The practice under discussion was land rental: *de-khulei alma shakli arba u-maslaki le-hu be-Nisan* — everyone else who rents out fields to sharecroppers charges four units per measure and removes the sharecroppers in Nisan. Rava, by contrast, waits until Iyar — an additional month — and collects six units. Since he defers collection and collects more, it looks as though the increment is compensation for the delay: a form of *ribbis*.

### Rava's Defense

Rava's response is sharp and unexpected: *attun ka avdatun shelo ke-din* — it is not I who am acting improperly, it is you. The reasoning: *ara le-arisa mishta'abeda* — the land is under the rights and jurisdiction of the sharecropper. The sharecropper has a legal entitlement to remain on the land, and the other landlords who eject their sharecroppers in Nisan are harming them. *Ei attun mesalki le-hu be-Nisan mafsidtu le-hu be-khamma* — when you remove them in Nisan, you cause them to lose profit, because the grain is not yet fully ready for harvest.

### Profit Justification

Rava continues: *ana natarna le-hu ad Iyar u-mavkhana le-hu be-khamma* — I allow them to remain through Iyar, and they therefore reap a larger harvest and earn more money. It is precisely because the sharecropper derives greater benefit from the land under Rava's arrangement that Rava charges more. The higher rental fee is not compensation for a deferral of payment; it is proportionate to the greater profit the sharecropper actually realizes. Rava's arrangement is thus not only free of *ribbis* — it is arguably more equitable than the prevailing practice.

## Mashkon House

### Rav Mari Family

The Gemara introduces *Rav Mari bar Rachel*, and Rashi's comment here is fascinating. The name itself is unusual — in a patriarchal society, why identify someone by his mother? Rashi explains: *bat Shmuel havya ve-ishtabai* — this Rachel was Shmuel's daughter, who was taken captive. *U-nesava oved kokhavim* — she was taken by a non-Jew. *Ve-nitgayyer u-shmey Issur Giyyora* — and this non-Jew eventually converted, taking the name Issur Giyyora, an appropriately literal name for a *ger*. Rav Mari's status was therefore: *horato shelo bi-kdusha ve-leidato bi-kdusha* — conceived before his father's conversion, born after it. *U-mekhamat kevodo lo karu leh al shmey de-avuha* — out of respect for Rav Mari, they did not name him after his father, since at the time of conception the father was still a non-Jew; hence he was named after his mother. Rav Mari bar Rachel and his father Issur Giyyora appear in a number of places throughout *Shas*.

### House Collateral

*Rav Mari bar Rachel amshakhne leh hahu nokhri beita* — a certain non-Jew pledged his house to Rav Mari as collateral on a loan. Rav Mari was living in the house — effectively his interest payment, which is permissible when dealing with a non-Jew. *Hadar zabna le-Rava* — the non-Jew then sold the house to Rava while Rav Mari was still residing in it. This raises interesting questions about the transfer of title over property currently occupied by someone with possessory rights; in practice, such transfers do occur, and the Gemara proceeds on that basis.

### Rent-Free Year

*Natar taresar yarkhei sheta* — Rav Mari waited twelve months. *Shakil agar beita amti leh le-Rava* — and then he took rent money and brought it to Rava. He explains his reasoning: *hai de-la amti leh le-mar agar beita ad haidanna — d'stama mashkanta shatta* — the reason he had not paid rent for the entire first year is that, as the Gemara established earlier, a *stama mashkon* — a standard collateral arrangement over a house or field — carries with it a full year during which the creditor cannot be displaced. That year of exclusive possession was effectively his interest payment on the loan with the non-Jew. When the non-Jew sold the house to Rava, Rav Mari's rights for that year were not extinguished. *Ei bei l'salukei lo hava matzi l'salukei* — even had the original owner wished to remove him during that year, he could not have done so. The sale to Rava did not change that.

### Payment After Year

Once the year expired, however, Rav Mari recognized a change in his legal position. *Hashta* — now — if the non-Jew were to pay off the loan, he would have the right to reclaim the house and remove Rav Mari. This means that Rav Mari's rights are no longer absolute; he can be displaced upon repayment of the debt. In the language of the analysis: before the year was up, the non-Jew could not have removed him under any circumstances — his occupation was as secure as ownership. Now, the situation is different: Rav Mari is residing on land that is effectively Rava's, subject to a lien. To continue living there rent-free would resemble receiving a benefit from Rava — and that benefit is connected, however indirectly, to money he has out on loan to the non-Jew. Technically, it is not *ribbis* in the strict sense, because Rava is not the debtor — the non-Jew is. One might recall from an earlier passage in the Gemara the discussion of someone else paying for a loan made to a third party; the same logic applies here. But it does not look right. Accordingly, Rav Mari decides he should begin paying rent.

### Rava Refuses Rent

Rava's response is remarkable. *Ei hava yadana d'hava memashken lei l'mar lo hava zavnina lei* — had I known the house was under your collateral arrangement, I never would have purchased it from the non-Jew; I would not have interfered with your relationship. *Hashta kedineihem avdina lach* — now, I will follow, for these purposes, the terms that would have governed your relationship with the non-Jew: *kol eimas d'lo misalkana lach b'zuzei lo shakilna agar bayta* — as long as the debt has not been repaid, I will not accept rent from you. Rashi clarifies that this means: until the non-Jew pays off the loan and the debt is settled, Rava refuses to take any rent from Rav Mari. The spectacle of each party declining what the other is offering — one insisting on paying rent, the other refusing to accept — reflects the *tzidkut* of both parties. The underlying halachic structure is: Rav Mari is entitled to remain in the house by virtue of his lien on the non-Jew's debt; Rava, recognizing this, refuses to extract rent during the period in which that lien remains in force.

## Wine & Grain Ribbis

### Wine Selection Ribbis

*Amar lei Rava miBarnish l'Rav Ashi: chazi mar rabanan d'ka achli ribis* — Rava miBarnish said to Rav Ashi: look at what the rabbis are doing — they appear to be eating *ribbis*. The practice described is: *yahavi zuzei achamra b'Tishrei u'meshachali lei b'Teves* — they give money for wine in Tishrei but only select which wine they will actually take in Teves. In the interval between Tishrei and Teves, some wine turns to vinegar while other wine remains good and clear. Because they are not taking immediate possession, the arrangement functions as a loan to the wine seller — they allow him to hold his wine longer, and in return they receive the right to choose the best wine. That right of selection, obtained in exchange for the delay, is a form of *ribbis*.

### Breira Defense

Rav Ashi's response turns on a conceptual point: *inhu nami achamar kayahavi, achala lo kayahavi* — when they pay for wine, they pay at the rate of wine, not vinegar. They never agreed to purchase vinegar. The underlying claim is: *me'ikara dechamar chamar dechala chala* — what was wine from the beginning was wine, and what was vinegar from the beginning was vinegar. Even at the moment of purchase in Tishrei, the inferior wine already carried within it the quality that would make it turn sour by Teves. The wine that remains good was always good wine; it was not transformed by the passage of time. This is a form of *breira* — a retroactive determination that clarifies what was always the case: the good wine was always the wine they purchased, because they paid for wine, not for vinegar. At the moment of selection in Teves, they are simply identifying which barrels were, all along, the ones they had actually bought.

One might push back: even granting the *breira* argument, a normal purchaser who takes immediate delivery still bears the risk that his wine might turn. By waiting and selecting, these buyers are insulated from that risk entirely — and being freed from commercial risk is itself a benefit. *Hahi sha'ata hu dekamivchari* — it is only at that later moment that the determination is made as to which wine was chosen. Even if the quality differential was latent from the beginning, the buyers are still enjoying a benefit that a standard purchaser, who must accept the goods and bear the risk, would not have. This is what makes the case genuinely difficult, and the *breira* argument — while perhaps technically defensible — does not fully dissolve the intuition that something valuable is being received in exchange for the delay.

### Ravina's Grain

*Ravina hava yahiv zuzei livnei akra deshanvata* — Ravina would give money to the people living by the riverbank plots called Shanvata, pre-purchasing their grain. *Veshafchi lei tefei kufisa* — when it came time to deliver, they would pour a little extra into his bag — a baker's dozen arrangement, as it were. Since the delivery was later than payment, and they gave him extra at the time of delivery, the question arises whether this constitutes *ribbis*. *Azal lekamei deRav Ashi; amar lei: mi sharei? Amar lei: in* — he went before Rav Ashi, who said it was permissible. *Achulei hu deka machli gabach* — they are simply being *mochel*, waiving something in your favor, giving you a gift.

### Rav Ashi Approval

The obvious difficulty is that they are forgiving something in Ravina's favor precisely because he lent them money — or more precisely, because he pre-paid and thus forwarded them funds. One recalls earlier discussions in the Gemara about arrangements where a lender may not live rent-free in the borrower's house, or receive flour with an extra scoop added. The principle in those cases was that even without explicit acknowledgment, the benefit is presumed to flow from the loan relationship. Why is this different?

### Tosafot Conditions

Tosafot addresses this carefully. *Ein zeh ribbis gemurah* — the extra grain does not constitute full *ribbis*. The reasoning proceeds on two grounds. First, *shelo hayu mazkirim shenotnim bishvil me'otav shehayu beteilim etzlam kidefairash bekontres* — they did not say, "here is a little extra because you pre-paid us and your money was tied up with us." The connection to the loan was never made explicit. Second, and more significantly, Tosafot distinguishes this from the case of living rent-free in a borrower's house: *velo dami lehalevehu vadar bechatzeiro shetzarich leha'alot lo sachar* — that case is different because *deshani hasham she'adayan chayav lo* — there the loan is still active, the debt is still outstanding. Here, by the time the extra grain is delivered, the transaction has been completed. The grain has been delivered; there is no longer an outstanding loan.

This generates a precise and important set of conditions. If the debt is concluded and no explicit connection is made to the original loan — "I am grateful for the money you lent me" — then giving a gift or extra measure does not constitute *ribbis*. The counterintuitive *chiddush* is that the gift need not be temporally distant from the delivery; the juxtaposition alone does not render it *ribbis*, so long as the two conditions are met: the loan is over, and the connection is not verbalized. If, on the other hand, one were to say "thank you for waiting the two months" or "thank you for paying up front, here is a little something" — that is *ribbis*. If one simply hands over the grain with a bit extra and says nothing about the earlier arrangement, it is not.

## Dina D'Malchusa

### Tax-Seized Land

A new problem emerges. *Amar lei ha'ara lav didhu hi* — suddenly the question arises: how could he even accept delivery of grain from these people? It turns out that the land on which their grain was grown may not have belonged to them at all — they were apparently working land that had been seized from others who had defaulted on their taxes to the government. If that is the case, then the grain itself might be stolen property, making the entire earlier *ribbis* discussion beside the point.

### Government Authority

*Amar lei: ara l'taska meshabda* — the land is subjugated to the tax obligation; by virtue of the tax debt, the government has rights to the land. *U'malka amar: man d'yahiv taska leichol ara* — and the king declares that whoever pays the tax may benefit from the land. The government, through its tax authority, has the power to reassign use of the land to those willing to pay the outstanding taxes. This is a commercially functioning economic system, not arbitrary theft, and it has halachic implications.

### Legal Principle

Why should Jewish law care what the government declares? Because *dina d'malchusa dina* — the law of the kingdom is law. Rashi explicitly invokes this principle here. The government has liens on land for unpaid taxes; it can reassign productive use of that land. That economic and legal reality is recognized within *halacha*. Some *rishonim* and *geonim* observe that *dina d'malchusa dina* applies specifically to public law — areas in which the government has a systemic interest, such as taxation and public land rights — as distinguished from government regulation of purely private relationships between individuals. The case here is paradigmatically a matter of public law: "If you fail to pay your taxes, the government acquires rights over your land." That is precisely the domain where *dina d'malchusa dina* operates.

## Poll Tax Slavery

### Enslavement Question

*Amar lei Rav Pappa l'Rava: chazi mar hanei rabbanan d'yahvi zuzei akarga* — Rav Pappa said to Rava: look at this troubling practice. These rabbis of the *yeshiva* — and it is worth noting that it is the anonymous scholars who are apparently engaging in this — are paying the poll tax of individual citizens. *D'akarga d'inish, umeshabbdi behu tfei* — they pay someone else's poll tax to the government, and then they subjugate that person. In practical terms: Charlie owes the government a hundred dollars in poll tax; I pay the government on Charlie's behalf; I then seize Charlie and put him to work as my personal servant until he reimburses me.

### Prepaid Labor

The *ribbis* dimension of this arrangement: Charlie will eventually pay the government his poll tax — or reimburse me — and at that point I will no longer have a claim on his service. So what have I done in the interim? I effectively forwarded Charlie the hundred dollars, and while he owes it, I am working him. My gain for having advanced the money is his labor. Everyone can see why this is structurally a form of *ribbis*: I lend money, and I receive service in return until the loan is repaid.

### Rav Sheshet's

Rava's response is almost theatrical. *Hashta ichu schivna, lo amri lichu hani miltha* — had I died just now, I would have departed this world without teaching you this important *halacha*. What Rav Sheshet taught: *moharkayu de-hani be-tapsa de-malka monach* — the enslavement document of these citizens sits, conceptually, in the king's treasury. Just as the king holds fundamental rights over the land through the tax structure — recalling the story of Pharaoh and Yosef — the king holds fundamental rights over the service of his subjects.

### Public Law

*U-malka amar: man de-lo yaiv karga lishhtabeid le-man de-yaiv karga* — the king declares that whoever fails to pay his poll tax, and someone else pays it on his behalf, that other person acquires rights to the defaulter's service. This is *dina d'malchusa dina*: a public law that governs the relationship between the citizen and the state, and between citizens whose financial obligations to the state are discharged by others. The government's legal framework is recognized as creating enforceable entitlements within *halacha*, just as it does in the case of tax-seized land.

### Ribbis Tension

Yet the problem is not fully dissolved. *Dina d'malchusa dina* may establish my right to Charlie's service in the sense of: what entitles me to seize him at all, given that Jewish law does not ordinarily permit enslaving a debtor? But it does not obviously answer the separate question of why receiving his labor in return for having forwarded his poll tax is not *ribbis*. Saying that the government permits it does not address whether I am gaining something extra for having effectively extended Charlie a loan.

One resolution suggested is a reconceptualization of the transaction: rather than framing it as "I lent Charlie money and I receive his service as interest," one could frame it as "I purchased from the government the rights to Charlie's labor." Under this reading, there is no loan and no debtor-creditor relationship; it is a purchase of a service right. A further resolution — and this may be the cleaner one — is that Charlie is working off his debt through his labor rather than repaying a monetary loan. If the service itself is understood as discharging the obligation, there is no gap between the loan and its repayment; there is simply a sale of labor in satisfaction of a debt. Under this reading, there is no *ribbis* at all, and the sole question is the one *dina d'malchusa dina* answers: what entitles a private person to enslave another for non-payment of taxes? Ordinary *halacha* would not permit this — a *eved ivri* arises only from theft, not from ordinary debt — and so the government's law is doing all the necessary work.

Tosafot, it should be noted, reads this Gemara as a *ribbis* case: *veshafchi lei tefei kufisa... she-levisof hayu machazirim lahem ma'os hakarga she-notnim ba'avoram, v'chol mah shehayu mishtabdin bahen hayu bischar halva'ah* — in the end they would return the poll-tax money to those who had paid it on their behalf, and all the labor they performed during that period was compensation for the loan. Under Tosafot's reading, the *ribbis* problem is real and the answer via *dina d'malchusa dina* is doing even more work — which makes the answer more interesting and more difficult simultaneously.

## Rav Sura Seizure

### Criminal Seizure

The Gemara records a related practice. *Rav Sura Ram, achui d'Rava, havay takeish insha d'lo malei* — Rav Sura, Rava's brother, would seize people who were not good — petty thieves or others injurious to society — *u'malyu b'goharka d'Rava* — and put them to work carrying Rava's palanquin. This is distinct from the poll-tax case: here there is no government law, no outstanding tax, and no debtor-creditor relationship. The basis for this seizure is purely communal control.

### Biblical Source

*Amar lei Rava: shappir ka'avidat* — Rava said to him: you did well, this is acceptable. The source is a *braita*: *ra'isa she-eino noheig kashura — minayin she-ata rashai l'hishtabeid bo? Talmud lomar: l'olam bahem ta'avodu* — if you see someone behaving improperly, how do you know you may enslave him as a measure of control? Because the verse says "you may enslave them forever."

### Improper Conduct

The *derasha* reads the verse with a different pause. The full verse continues: *u'v'acheichem b'nei yisrael ish b'achiv lo tirdeh bo b'farech* — but among your brother Israelites, you shall not work one another with harshness. The *derasha* first reads the text as if it includes a Jew who behaves improperly alongside the foreigners who may be enslaved: *l'olam bahem ta'avodu u'v'acheichem* — you may enslave them and your fellow Jew. The *talmud lomar* qualification then restores the verse's actual division, limiting the permission to those who are *not* acting properly. The conclusion: a community has the authority to subject those whose conduct is injurious to society to compelled service as a form of social control — not as permanent enslavement, but as a corrective measure. This is a striking Gemara: prior to this point, the daf had established that enslavement under Jewish law arises only from theft (the *eved ivri* case) or self-sale. Here we find two additional grounds: government authority via *dina d'malchusa dina* in the poll-tax case, and communal authority over those who do not conduct themselves properly.

## Negligent Agent

### Wine Purchase

*Amar Rav Chama: hai man d'yahiv zuzei l'chavrei l'mizban lei chamra* — someone gave money to his friend to purchase wine on his behalf. The market timing was right; prices were low. The agent was given the money and a fee for his efforts. *U'pasha v'lo zavin lei* — the agent was negligent and did not make the purchase. The opportunity passed and the prices rose. What does the agent owe?

The preliminary objection is that this seems to be a case of *grama b'nezikin patur* — indirect damage, for which one is ordinarily exempt. No physical property was damaged; there was simply a lost opportunity caused by negligence.

### Compensation Rule

Nevertheless, Rav rules: *meshaleim lei k'ka'azla afarvata d'zushafat* — the agent must pay at the lowest market rate at which wine was available at the best market, the place called Zushafat where wine went cheapest. Despite the absence of direct damage, the negligent agent must make his principal whole at the prices the principal would have secured.

### Standard Wine

*Amar Meimar amritah l'shma'ata kmaih d'Rav Zevid mi-Neharde'a* — when this ruling was reported before Rav Zevid of Nehardea, he qualified it: *hani mili b'yayin stam* — this applies only when the instruction was to buy generic wine. *B'yayin zeh*, however — if the principal specified a particular seller — *lo*: the agent is not liable. Why? *Mi yeimar d'mizabni lei nihalei* — who is to say that seller would have sold to him? Perhaps the specified seller would have declined. Since the success of the mission was not guaranteed even had the agent performed his duty, the causal link between the negligence and the loss is insufficiently certain to impose liability. When the instruction is simply "buy wine" and wine is universally available, there is no such uncertainty; the agent's negligence definitively cost the principal the opportunity. When the instruction specifies a particular source, the outcome was never certain.

### Asmachta Issue

*V'Rav Ashi amar: afillu yayan stam nami* — Rav Ashi goes further and says that even in the case of generic wine, the agent owes nothing. *Mai ta'ama? Asmachta hi* — the reason is that any obligation the agent would have to compensate rests on an implicit penalty clause, and penalty clauses of this type are *asmachta*, which is not legally binding. The agent did not sell anything to the principal, did not rent him anything, did not create a formal exchange. The implicit understanding that "if I fail to do the job I will compensate you for the loss" is a penalty commitment — and penalty commitments, under conditions we will shortly define, are not seriously meant and therefore not legally enforceable.

### Asmachta Definition

*Asmachta* is the concept of a commitment, typically a penalty or forfeiture clause, that one does not expect to have to honor and therefore never truly commits to. Because the obligation arises not from an exchange of property or services but from a contingency the party expects not to occur, it lacks the *gemirat da'at* — the full mental commitment — necessary to create a binding obligation. *V'asmachta lo kanya* — an *asmachta* does not effect acquisition or liability.

Under Rav Ashi's analysis, then, the reason a negligent agent would owe anything at all is not damages in the conventional sense — this is plainly *grama*, and *grama b'nezikin* is ordinarily exempt. The only conceivable basis for liability is an implicit penalty clause, and that penalty clause is *asmachta* and therefore unenforceable.

### Sharecropper Penalty

*V'Rav Ashi mai shna mei'ha d'tnan: im ovir v'lo a'avid ashalem b'meitava* — but what distinguishes the classic sharecropper case, where the contract provides: "if I leave the land fallow and do not work it, I will pay you with the best of the crops" — which is precisely a penalty clause for non-performance, and yet is enforceable? If all penalty clauses were *asmachta*, that clause too should fail.

### Control Factor

*Hatam b'yado, hacha lav b'yado* — the distinction is control. In the sharecropper case, the decision whether to work the land or leave it fallow is entirely within the sharecropper's control. He is choosing to neglect his duty; no external factor prevents him from working. When you fully control whether the penalty-triggering event occurs, the *asmachta* argument collapses: you cannot say you did not seriously commit to the penalty, because you had every ability to avoid it. Here, however — in the agent's case — according to Rashi, the argument is that even with generic wine, there may have been factors outside the agent's control preventing him from securing the exact prices or the exact wine. Since his success was not entirely within his hands, the penalty clause was never fully and seriously meant.

Tosafot presses on this point, noting that it seems odd to classify a case of purchasing freely available generic wine as "not in his hands." Tosafot suggests that perhaps it was not entirely certain that the agent could have obtained the wine at those particular low prices. The deeper question Tosafot raises is whether the *b'yado/lav b'yado* distinction is really the defining factor for *asmachta*, and this opens into a broader discussion: is gambling *asmachta*? What about competitive games? The analysis that emerges is that at the extremes the question is clearer. When something is entirely within one's control — as in the sharecropper case — committing to a penalty is a genuine commitment, and one is held to it. When something is entirely random — as with a roulette wheel — one bets knowing full well that the outcome is beyond control, and the bet is accepted with clear eyes. The more difficult cases are intermediate ones: situations involving both an element of skill or control and an element of chance, where one might genuinely believe one can control the outcome while in fact one cannot fully do so. In those cases, the argument that one never seriously committed to the penalty is most compelling.

## Situmta Purchase

### Joint Purchase

*Amar Rava: hani bei tlata d'yahavi zuzei l'chad l'mizban lehu midi* — three people give money to one agent to purchase something on their behalf. *V'zavan l'chad mineihu* — if he ends up buying for one of them — or perhaps, using one person's money — *zavan l'kulu*: since they all gave their money together, he has effectively purchased for all three. He cannot allocate the purchase to only one of the three principals; the collective contribution creates collective ownership.

### Separate Bundles

*V'la amran ela d'la tzar v'chatumi inish inish l'chudei* — this rule applies only if the money was not sealed and individually signed by each person. If each of the three persons handed the agent a separately sealed, signed bundle of money, then clearly there is no collective here — there are three distinct individuals engaging the agent for three separate transactions. In that case, *l'man d'zavan zavan u'l'man d'la zavan la zavan*: whatever he purchased with A's money belongs to A, and B has no claim on it, and vice versa. Here one can see, in embryonic form, something resembling the concept of a corporation — the pooling of capital into a single undifferentiated fund creates collective rights and obligations.

### Situmta Mark

*Amar Rav Pappa mishmei d'Rava: hai situmta* — in the name of Rava, Rav Pappa teaches regarding the *situmta*. According to Rashi, a *situmta* works as follows: I go to a wine merchant and wish to purchase ten barrels, but I cannot take immediate delivery of all of them. I place my mark — my identifying sign — on ten specific barrels. I have not performed *meshicha*, the act of physical pulling or moving that ordinarily effects transfer of movable property. *Hai situmta kanya* — does that mark effect acquisition?

### Full Ownership

*L'mai hilchata? Rav Chaviva amar l'miknei mamash* — for what purpose does it matter? Rav Chaviva holds that the mark effects full, real ownership even without a standard *kinyan*. The moment the mark is placed, those barrels belong to the buyer.

### Commitment Only

*V'rabanan amri l'kabulei alei mi shepara* — the Rabbis disagree. The mark is not a full *kinyan*; it is analogous to the payment of money in a sale, which under rabbinic law does not consummate the transfer of movable goods. It creates a quasi-*kinyan* — a level of commitment such that if either party backs out, the *mi shepara* curse applies. But it is not full ownership.

### Custom Determination

*V'hilchata l'kabulei alei mi shepara* — the ruling follows the Rabbis: the mark alone creates only a commitment, not full ownership. However: *u'v'atra d'nahigi l'miknei mamash kani* — in a place where the accepted practice is that placing one's mark constitutes a final, binding sale, that communal practice is halachically recognized as effecting full transfer of ownership. Where a community or industry operates on the understanding that the mark is the conclusive act of sale, *halacha* honors that understanding.

### Mazal U'Bracha

This is a foundational principle in halachic commercial law, and the most commonly cited contemporary illustration is from the diamond industry, where the declaration *mazal u'vracha* is understood by all parties as the moment the deal is final and irrevocable. In some contexts a handshake functions similarly. Various industries and marketplaces have their own conventional acts that signal binding completion — eBay's formulation that all bids are final binding contracts is a modern parallel. *Halacha* recognizes these conventions. The theoretical underpinning is the concept of *gemirat da'at* — full mental commitment to the transaction. The standard *kinyanim* defined by *halacha* are themselves expressions of *gemirat da'at*, and when a community has developed its own acts that equally express that finality of commitment, *halacha* can recognize those acts as effecting the transfer, even if they fall outside the formally defined *kinyanim*. It is, in a sense, a do-it-yourself *kinyan* — grounded in the same underlying concept but expressed through communally recognized custom rather than formally legislated form.

## Early Grain Sales

### Possession Rule

With the foundational rule in view — that one may pre-purchase goods from an early seller only if the seller actually has the goods in some form of possession — the Gemara examines the debate between Rav and Shmuel regarding how far removed from the final product the goods may be.

### Rav Two Stages

*Rav* holds: *mechusar shtayim posek, shalosh eino posek* — if the product is only two processing stages away from its deliverable form, the seller may pre-sell it. If it is three or more stages removed, he may not, because it is considered as though he does not yet have it.

### Shmuel Control

*Shmuel* offers a fundamentally different criterion. *B'yedei adam afilu me'ah posek* — if all the remaining stages are under human control, the seller may pre-sell even if there are a hundred steps remaining. The reasoning ties back to the *asmachta* discussion: when the process is entirely within human hands, the seller genuinely has the product in a meaningful sense, because nothing stands between him and delivery except his own effort.

### Divine Dependency

Conversely: *b'yedei shamayim afilu achat eino posek* — if even one remaining step depends on divine intervention — rain, wind, sun — then even a single step is too much. The seller cannot guarantee delivery; his ownership of the product is too contingent. Rav's quantitative criterion and Shmuel's qualitative criterion thus diverge sharply, and as we shall see, all the cases in the *Mishna* work out better for Shmuel.

## Produce Applications

### Wheat Bundle

The *Mishna* permits pre-purchasing on the basis of a *gadish* — a bundle of wheat — even though what will ultimately be delivered is grain. The Gemara notes: *v'ha mechusar misht'cha b'chama l'mevesh u-l'midash u-midra* — the bundle is still lacking: drying in the sun, complete desiccation, threshing, and winnowing. That is four steps. The Gemara resolves this by stipulating: *kegon d'shadya b'chama v'yavesh* — the case in the *Mishna* is where the bundle has already been dried in the sun and desiccated. We are now only two steps removed — threshing and winnowing — which satisfies Rav's criterion.

### Sieve Solution

For Shmuel's criterion, the remaining challenge is winnowing, which appears to depend on wind — a divine factor. *Efshar b'nafavata* — it is possible to accomplish the winnowing with a sieve, without relying on wind at all. The process is thereby entirely within human hands, and Shmuel's condition is satisfied.

### Wine Processing

The *Mishna* also permits pre-purchasing wine on the basis of a *avit* — a barrel of grapes. But wine requires: *michmar* — a heating process applied to the grapes, bringing them to the pressing floor, pressing, and having the wine flow into the pit. This is a substantial number of steps. The Gemara resolves the issue by invoking the language of Rav Chiyya's *braita* about olives, which uses the term *komar* — a vessel in which the grapes sit after the heating process has been completed. *Hacha nami al ha-komar shel anavim* — here too, the purchase is made at the stage of the *komar* of grapes, after the heating has occurred.

### Buyer Final Step

Even so, three steps remain: transport to the pressing floor, pressing, and having the wine flow into the pit. *B'atra d'zavin hu d'nagid* — the Gemara explains that in the locale under discussion, the buyer himself performs the final step of causing the wine to flow into the pit. This is admittedly difficult to picture as a standard commercial practice — one imagines a merchant purchasing wine and being informed that he must show up on pressing day to participate in the process. But the Gemara's solution requires it: by having the buyer perform the final step, the number of steps the seller must complete is reduced to two, satisfying Rav.

### Oil Extraction

*V'al ha-ma'atan shel zeitim* — a vessel of olives, where what is being purchased is olive oil. As with the wine, the process is lengthy: the heating, bringing to the pressing floor, pressing, and extracting the oil. The same resolution applies: *tni Rav Chiyya al ha-komar shel zeitim* — Rav Chiyya's formulation positions the purchase at the stage after heating, in the *komar* vessel, reducing the remaining steps. And again, *de'ihu dezavin hu demangid* — the buyer performs the final extraction step himself.

### Potter's Clay

*Ve'al habeytzim shel yotzer* — the "eggs" of the potter, i.e., balls of clay. What is being pre-purchased is not the clay but the finished pottery. The clay in its raw ball form must still be shaped, dried, placed in the kiln, fired, and removed from the kiln. *Kegon demelafefa veyivusha* — the Gemara stipulates that the clay has already been shaped and dried; it now remains only to fire it. This is described as still being called a "ball of clay" even though it is technically an unfired vessel.

### Pottery Removal

Even so, three steps remain for the firing stage: placing in the oven, firing, and removing. *Ve'azdu leta'amei de'ihu dezavin hu demaphik* — the buyer again performs the final step, removing the fired pottery from the kiln. The buyer here receives the honor of the final act of production. Across all these cases, it is striking that the evidence consistently points in Shmuel's direction: the *Mishna* permits pre-purchasing in situations where many steps of processing remain, which works straightforwardly under Shmuel's criterion (all steps are within human control) but requires considerable ingenuity — stipulating that prior stages are already completed and that buyers perform final steps themselves — to fit Rav's two-stage limit.

### Plaster Grinding

*Ve'al hasid misheshika'o bekivshan* — plaster after it has been placed in the kiln. Raw stone is placed in a kiln, fired, and then ground into powder. The Gemara notes three remaining steps: firing, removing, and grinding. *Ve'azdu leta'amei de'ihu dezavin hu dedayyek* — the buyer performs the final grinding step. For Shmuel, the Gemara raises the question: *lama li nishishikao bekivshan?* — why does the *Mishna* specify the stage after placement in the kiln? Under Shmuel's logic, since all steps are under human control, the pre-sale should be permitted even before the stone is placed in the kiln. The answer: *eima mishedaroy leshakao bekivshan* — read the condition as "when it is fit to be placed in the kiln," meaning when the stone has been prepared and is ready for firing, even if it has not yet been put in.

## Meir vs. Yosi

### Meir's Rule

A *braita* presents a dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosi specifically regarding the potter's clay case. *Ein poskin al habeytzim shel yotzer ad sheye'asu divrei Rabi Meir* — Rabbi Meir holds that one cannot pre-purchase the potter's clay until it has actually been shaped into balls. The underlying principle is that the seller must have the product in his possession in a meaningful sense, and unformed clay does not yet constitute the product being sold.

### Yosi's Rule

*Amar Rabi Yosi: bameh devarim amorim be'afar lavan* — Rabbi Yosi qualifies: when is Rabbi Meir's requirement applicable? When dealing with white clay, which is rarer and not widely available. In that case, the seller must actually own and possess the shaped clay before a pre-sale is valid. *Aval be'afar shachor* — but black clay, of the type produced in the villages of Kefar Chananya and Kefar Shichin and their environs, is different. *Poskin af al pi she'ein lazeh yesh lazeh* — one may pre-purchase it even if the seller does not yet own it, because it is available everywhere, making it equivalent to *yatza hasha'ar* — a commodity with a fixed, publicly known price, which may be pre-sold even without the seller having immediate possession.

### Ameymar Practice

*Ameymar yahiv zuzei miki maytu afra* — Ameymar would pay for the clay from the moment the dirt was brought in to the workshop, before it had even been shaped into balls. The Gemara asks: *keman?* — like whom does this practice accord? It fits neither Rabbi Meir, who requires the shaped balls, nor Rabbi Yosi as usually understood, since Rabbi Yosi's leniency on black clay would seemingly permit payment before possession entirely — not require possession of the raw dirt.

### Compromise Position

*Le'olam keRabi Yosi uv'atra de'Ameymar ashik afra* — the resolution is that this practice does accord with Rabbi Yosi, but reflects a local condition: in Ameymar's locale, dirt of this type was not so freely available as in Kefar Chananya and Kefar Shichin. It was not rare enough to require shaped balls as Rabbi Meir demands, but neither was it so universally available that one could pre-sell it without any possession whatsoever. Once the dirt was brought into the workshop — *v'yahavu zuzei, v'd'lo lo* — that was sufficient possession to permit the pre-sale. The case thus represents an intermediate position: not as stringent as Rabbi Meir (who requires shaped balls) and not as lenient as Rabbi Yosi at his most permissive (who would allow pre-sale with no possession at all when the commodity is widely available), but somewhere in between, calibrated to local conditions of availability.

## Dung Availability

### Year-Round Dung

*U'poskin al ha'zevel kol yemot hashana* — the *Mishna* permits pre-purchasing dung throughout the entire year, since dung is always available even if the seller does not have it in hand. Dung is never truly scarce; some owner's animals are always producing it, and one can rely on obtaining it at any time.

### Rainy Season Issue

The Gemara introduces a challenge: during the rainy season, dung is less readily available. Apparently, because of rain and wet conditions, the dung that accumulates becomes less serviceable as a commodity, or its collection is disrupted. This calls into question whether pre-purchasing dung truly can be permitted year-round.

### Three Positions

*Amar Rava yemot hageshamim ika beinyahu* — Rava identifies that the difference between the positions in the *Mishna* turns on the rainy season. Three distinct views emerge. The first position holds that one may pre-purchase dung at any time of year, even during the rainy season when it is less available — because it is sufficiently available even then to permit pre-sale without the seller having possession. The second position holds that pre-purchasing is permissible only during the summer months, when dung is at peak availability; during the rainy season, the seller must actually own it. The third position holds that during the rainy season even ownership is insufficient — the seller must have it physically in hand — while during the summer months, pre-purchasing without possession is freely permitted. The dispute thus mirrors the broader framework of the daf: how available must a commodity be, and what degree of possession is required at various levels of availability, in order to validate a pre-sale transaction?
