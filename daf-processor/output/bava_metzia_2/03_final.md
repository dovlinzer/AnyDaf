# Bava Metzia 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Tallis Case

### Masechet Overview

*Bava Metzia* means "middle gate," reflecting its position as the second of the three sections into which *Nezikin* is divided — *Bava Kamma*, *Bava Metzia*, and *Bava Batra*, meaning first section, middle section, and final section. Together these thirty *prakim* comprise the bulk of civil law in the Talmud.

Unlike *Bava Kamma*, which coheres around a single dominant theme — torts, whether damages, robbery, theft, or personal injury — *Bava Metzia* is considerably more varied in its subject matter. The masechet ranges across contested ownership and *metziot* (found objects), through two of the most technically demanding *prakim* in all of *Shas* (*Eizehu Neshech* and *HaZahav*, dealing with *ribbis* and the nature of money), multiple chapters on *shomrim* (the third *perek* and the eighth *perek* both return to this topic), and an extended treatment of workers' rights. One should not expect a single unifying theme; *Bava Metzia* is, in the most direct sense, the middle ten *prakim* of *Nezikin*, covering a broad sweep of civil law.

It is a classic Yeshivish *masechet* with rich treatment by the major *rishonim*, and as will become evident, many of its discussions open onto broader *Shas*-wide questions of principle. Tosafot notes that this *perek* also connects naturally to what immediately precedes it: the final *perek* of *Bava Kamma* dealt with the division of wood shavings and sawdust between a craftsman and his employer — not a question of contested ownership as such, but one of dividing objects between two parties. That may provide the logical transition into our opening *mishnah*.

### Two Holding Tallis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנַיִם אוֹחֲזִין בְּטַלִּית, זֶה אוֹמֵר: ״אֲנִי מְצָאתִיהָ״, וְזֶה אוֹמֵר: ״אֲנִי מְצָאתִיהָ״. זֶה אוֹמֵר: ״כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי״, וְזֶה אוֹמֵר: ״כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי״. זֶה יִשָּׁבַע שֶׁאֵין לוֹ בָּהּ פָּחוֹת מֵחֶצְיָהּ, וְזֶה יִשָּׁבַע שֶׁאֵין לוֹ בָּהּ פָּחוֹת מֵחֶצְיָהּ – וְיַחְלוֹקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **two** people came to court **holding a garment,** and **this** one, the first litigant, **says: I found it, and that** one, the second litigant, **says: I found it; this** one **says: All of it is mine, and that** one **says: All of it is mine;** how does the court adjudicate this case? **This** one **takes an oath that he does not have** ownership of **less than half of it, and that** one **takes an oath that he does not have** ownership of **less than half of it, and they divide** it.

The *mishnah* opens: *shnayim ochazim b'talit* — two people are holding onto a *talit*, a cloak (not a ritual garment with *tzitzit*, but an ordinary cloak). Each one says *ani metzatiha* — I found it. Each one says *kulo sheli* — it is all mine. The ruling is *yachloku*: they divide it. But before simply dividing, each one swears: *she'ein lo pachos mechetzyo* — that he has no less than half of it.

The image of two people clutching opposite ends of a garment is perhaps the defining image of *Bava Metzia*. The situation presents a genuine adjudicative puzzle: both parties have equal claims, both are physically holding the object, and there is no witness or other evidence to privilege one claim over the other. The natural resolution is to split it — not literally with scissors, but by having one party purchase the other's half, or by selling the object and dividing the proceeds. What requires explanation is not the division itself but the accompanying oath and, in particular, the precise formulation of that oath.

### Oath Formulation

One might ask: why does each claimant swear only that he has *no less than half*, rather than swearing outright that the whole garment belongs to him? After all, each party is claiming the whole thing.

The answer lies in the principle that *beit din* must not suborn false oaths. If each party were required to swear *kulo sheli* — it is all mine — then we would be compelling at least one person, and quite possibly both, to swear something false. Equally, if each were required to swear only *chetzi sheli* — half is mine — that formulation would effectively force a claimant to retract his own claim before the oath is even adjudicated. The oath as crafted — *she'ein lo pachos mechetzyo*, that he has no less than half — neither overstates beyond what a truthful claimant could honestly affirm nor undermines the claimant's own position. It is a carefully engineered formulation.

There is an additional elegance to this structure: the oath maps precisely onto the ultimate ruling. Each swears he has no less than half; each receives half. When *beit din* announces its decision, it is calibrated exactly to what each party swore. Justice appears to have been served, and the integrity of the proceedings is preserved.

### Oath Function

The deeper question is: what is the *function* of the oath? Why do we think it will help?

The basic assumption — which will recur throughout the *masechet* — is that a person who might be willing to steal is nonetheless unwilling to take a false oath. I might grab your cloak and claim it as mine, but when confronted with the obligation to swear before *beit din*, I will back down and concede rather than go through with the false declaration. The oath thus serves as a mechanism for drawing out the truth: if I know I am lying, the oath might deter me from proceeding.

### Chashud Amamona

This assumption is crystallized in the principle *chashud amamona lo chashud ashvuasa* — one who is suspect in monetary matters is not necessarily suspect of swearing falsely. The two categories of moral failure are treated as distinct: a person may be the sort who steals but still recoil from the gravity of a false oath. The oath thus operates as a filter that a thief may decline to pass through.

### Rationalization

Yet the more psychologically precise explanation — and one with considerable *mussar* resonance — is the phenomenon of *mori ve-amar*, self-rationalization. Most of the time, a person who takes something that is not fully his is not a brazen thief who knows exactly what he is doing. He is someone who has talked himself into believing it is acceptable. He convinces himself that he saw the object first, or that the other party owes him money, or that he needs it more — and on that basis he acts. In his own mind, he is an honest person.

It is precisely for such a person that the oath is most effective. When he is brought before *beit din* and required to swear, the formal gravity of the moment cuts through the rationalization. He can no longer hide behind his self-justification; he must look squarely at what he is about to affirm before *Heaven*. At that point he may well admit, or at least withdraw his claim. The oath works not because it deters a committed criminal, but because it forces a rationalizer to confront the gap between what he has told himself and what he actually knows to be true.

## Variant Claims

### One All, One Half

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה אוֹמֵר: ״כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי״, וְזֶה אוֹמֵר: ״חֶצְיָהּ שֶׁלִּי״, הָאוֹמֵר כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי – יִשָּׁבַע שֶׁאֵין לוֹ בָּהּ פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה חֲלָקִים, וְהָאוֹמֵר חֶצְיָהּ שֶׁלִּי – יִשָּׁבַע שֶׁאֵין לוֹ בָּהּ פָּחוֹת מֵרְבִיעַ. זֶה נוֹטֵל שְׁלֹשָׁה חֲלָקִים, וְזֶה נוֹטֵל רְבִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** If **this** one **says: All of it is mine, and that** one **says: Half of it is mine,** since they both agree that half of the cloak belongs to one of them, the conflict between them is only about the other half. Therefore, **the one who says: All of it is mine, takes an oath that he does not have** ownership of **less than three parts,** i.e., three-fourths, **of it, and the one who says: Half of it is mine, takes an oath that he does not have** ownership of **less than one-quarter of it. This** one **takes three parts, and that** one **takes one-quarter.**

The *mishnah* proceeds to a variant case: *ze omer kulo sheli ve-ze omer chetzi sheli* — one claims the whole garment and the other claims only half. By saying "half is mine," the second claimant effectively concedes that the other half belongs to his opponent. The dispute is therefore confined to the remaining half. That contested half is divided equally between them, yielding three-quarters to the one who claimed everything and one-quarter to the one who claimed half.

The oaths are calibrated accordingly: the one claiming the whole swears *she'ein lo bo pachos mishlosha chalakim* — he has no less than three-quarters; the one claiming half swears *she'ein lo bo pachos mirvia* — he has no less than a quarter. Each receives precisely what he swore for. The result is not only legally coherent but, as noted above, aesthetically satisfying from the perspective of *beit din*: the ruling tracks the oaths exactly.

### Migo Question

Tosafot raises a pointed question about this ruling. If the half-claimant could have said "it is all mine" — and, had he done so, would have received half — then why does his more modest claim of "half is mine" not earn him that full half via a *migo*? The logic of *migo* is that since a claimant could have made a stronger claim and chose not to, his actual claim deserves credence. Here, the half-claimant voluntarily made the weaker claim; should that not at minimum secure him the half he claims?

### Muchzak Doctrine

Tosafot's answer turns on the concept of *muchzak* — presumptive possession. In *halacha*, the person who is considered in possession of an object has the default status of owner, and the principle *hamotzi mechaveiro alav hara'ayah* applies: whoever seeks to take from another bears the burden of proof. Physical possession, when combined with a claim, establishes *muchzakut*.

In the equal case — each claiming everything — both parties are equally *muchzak* in the entire object, since they are both physically holding it and each asserts full ownership. That equal *muchzakut* is what compels the division. But when one claims all and the other claims half, the analysis shifts: the full-claimant is *muchzak* in the whole; the half-claimant is *muchzak* only in half. The half-claimant's *migo* argument, if accepted, would allow him to use his stronger potential claim to dislodge the other party's *chazakah* over that disputed quarter. That, explains Tosafot, is a *migo le-hotzi* — a *migo* deployed to extract something from another's established possession — and such a *migo* is generally not accepted. The claimed half already has someone else's *chazakah* resting on it, and a *migo* alone cannot overcome that.

It is worth pausing on what this teaches about the underlying logic of the entire *perek*. The division of the contested object is not arbitrary; it is generated by the interplay of physical possession and the claims that frame it. Where neither party has a stronger default claim — because both are equally holding and equally asserting — the only principled resolution is equal division. In a case where one of the parties is not physically holding the object at all (as the *gemara* will discuss regarding a boat in the harbor, where neither party is in possession), the result might be entirely different — not a split, but a standoff, with *beit din* declining to intervene at all.

### Animal Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ שְׁנַיִם רוֹכְבִין עַל גַּבֵּי בְּהֵמָה, אוֹ שֶׁהָיָה אֶחָד רוֹכֵב וְאֶחָד מַנְהִיג, זֶה אוֹמֵר: כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי, וְזֶה אוֹמֵר: כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי – זֶה יִשָּׁבַע שֶׁאֵין לוֹ בָּהּ פָּחוֹת מֵחֶצְיָהּ, וְזֶה יִשָּׁבַע שֶׁאֵין לוֹ בָּהּ פָּחוֹת מֵחֶצְיָהּ, וְיַחְלוֹקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** If **two** people **were sitting** in a riding position **on** the back of **an animal,** e.g., a donkey or camel, **or one was sitting** in a riding position on the animal **and one was leading** it by its halter, and **this** one **says: All of it is mine, and that** one **says: All of it is mine,** how does the court adjudicate this case? **This** one **takes an oath that he does not have** ownership of **less than half of it, and that** one **takes an oath that he does not have** ownership of **less than half of it, and they divide** it.

The *mishnah* extends the same ruling to animals: *hayu shenayim rochvin al gabi behema* — two people were riding on an animal — *o she-hayah echad rochev ve-echad manhig* — or one was riding and one was leading it. Each claims full ownership, and the ruling is identical: each swears he has no less than half, and they divide.

The novelty of this case, which the *gemara* will explore, is the question of what degree of physical engagement with an object suffices to constitute the kind of possession that generates *muchzakut*. Riding an animal and leading an animal are each forms of control, but they differ from simply gripping a garment. The *mishnah* establishes by example — rather than by articulating a general principle — that both modes of engagement are sufficient.

### Agreement & Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּזְמַן שֶׁהֵם מוֹדִים אוֹ שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶן עֵדִים – חוֹלְקִין בְּלֹא שְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **When they admit** to the validity of each other's claims **or when they** each **have witnesses** attesting to their claims, **they divide** the disputed item **without** taking **an oath,** as an oath is administered only in a case where the parties have no other way to prove their claims.

The *mishnah* concludes with an obvious but important provision: *u-vizman she-shneihem modim*, if both parties agree about the facts, *o she-yesh sham eidim*, or there are witnesses, *cholkin belo shevuah* — they divide without an oath. The oath was introduced precisely because there is no other means of getting at the truth. Where agreement or testimony supplies that function, the oath is unnecessary.

## Why Two Cases?

### Double Phrasing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה לִי לְמִתְנָא זֶה אוֹמֵר אֲנִי מְצָאתִיהָ וְזֶה אוֹמֵר אֲנִי מְצָאתִיהָ, זֶה אוֹמֵר כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי וְזֶה אוֹמֵר כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי? לִיתְנֵי חֲדָא! חֲדָא קָתָנֵי: זֶה אוֹמֵר אֲנִי מְצָאתִיהָ וְכוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי, וְזֶה אוֹמֵר אֲנִי מְצָאתִיהָ וְכוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need the *tanna* **to teach** two separate claims made by each party? Why does the *tanna* say both: **This** one **says: I found it, and that** one **says: I found it;** and in addition: **This** one **says: All of it is mine, and that** one **says: All of it is mine? Let** the *tanna* **teach one** case. The Gemara answers: The correct understanding of the mishna is that **it teaches one** claim of each party, as their claims were as follows: **This** one **says: I found it and all of it is mine, and that** one **says: I found it and all of it is mine.**

The *gemara* opens with a structural question about the *mishnah* itself. The *mishnah* states two formulations — *ze omer ani metzatiha ve-ze omer ani metzatiha* ("I found it, I found it") and then *ze omer kulo sheli ve-ze omer kulo sheli* ("it is all mine, it is all mine"). Why are both formulations needed? *Litni chada* — let it state just one of them.

The *gemara*'s first answer is *chada katani* — both phrases constitute a single case. Each claimant is saying: I found it and therefore it is all mine. The compound statement — *ani metzatiha ve-kulo sheli* — is the full claim of each party; the *mishnah* simply presents the two components in sequence.

### Finding = Possession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיתְנֵי ״אֲנִי מְצָאתִיהָ״, וַאֲנָא יָדַעְנָא דְּכוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי! אִי תְּנָא ״אֲנִי מְצָאתִיהָ״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מַאי ״מְצָאתִיהָ״ – רְאִיתִיהָ. אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא אֲתַאי לִידֵיהּ – בִּרְאִיָּה בְּעָלְמָא קָנֵי, תְּנָא: ״כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי״ – דְּבִרְאִיָּה לָא קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But let** the *tanna* **teach** a case where each one merely claims: **I found it, and I would know that** the intention of each litigant is to claim: **All of it is mine.** The Gemara answers: **If** the *tanna* **would teach** only that each one claimed: **I found it, I would say** that **what** is the meaning of the claim: I **found it?** It means: I **saw it.** In other words, he is claiming that he saw the item first, and he believes that **even though it did not reach his possession, he acquired** it **through mere sight.** Since it would have been possible to think that this is an effective claim, the *tanna* **teaches** that the litigant states definitively: **All of it is mine,** to teach **that** one **does not acquire** a lost item **through sight** alone.

But if that is the answer, the *gemara* presses further: *ve-litni ani metzatiha ve-ana yadana de-kula sheli* — let the *mishnah* simply have them say "I found it," since the claim of finding already implies full ownership. Why bother adding the phrase *kulo sheli* at all?

The *gemara* answers: *i tana ani metzatiha, hava amina mai metzatiha re'itiha* — had the *mishnah* only said "I found it," one might have understood *metzatiha* to mean merely "I spotted it," and concluded that spotting a *metziah* is sufficient to acquire it. The phrase *kulo sheli* is therefore added to forestall that misunderstanding: the claim of ownership requires actual physical acquisition, not mere sighting.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ מַאי ״מְצָאתִיהָ״ – רְאִיתִיהָ, וְהָא אָמַר רַבְנַאי ״וּמְצָאתָהּ״ – דַּאֲתַאי לִידֵיהּ מַשְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this explanation: **But how can you say** that **what** the term: I **found it,** means is actually: I **saw it? But didn't Rabbenai say** in interpreting the verse: "And so shall you do with every lost item of your brother's, which he has lost, and you have found it" (Deuteronomy 22:3), that **"and you have found it" indicates that it came into his possession?** The term find in the Torah refers exclusively to a situation where the item is in the possession of the finder.

This is the interpretation associated with Ravina, who in *Bava Kamma* derived from the verse *asher tavad mimenu u-metzatah* that finding means coming into possession — *ata lidei*. The *gemara* here affirms Ravina's exegesis while adding a critical nuance: the biblical text may indeed imply physical acquisition, but ordinary colloquial speech does not. When people say "I found it," they typically mean "I spotted it first." The *mishnah* anticipates this potential misreading of colloquial language and corrects it by appending *kulo sheli*, signaling that mere sighting is insufficient — *re'iya b'alma lo kani*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין ״וּמְצָאתָהּ״ דִּקְרָא – דַּאֲתָא לִידֵיהּ מַשְׁמַע, וּמִיהוּ תַּנָּא לִישָּׁנָא דְעָלְמָא נָקֵט, וּמִדְּחָזֵי לֵיהּ אָמַר: אֲנָא אַשְׁכַּחִית, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא אֲתַאי לִידֵיהּ בִּרְאִיָּה בְּעָלְמָא קָנֵי, תָּנֵי: ״כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי״ – דְּבִרְאִיָּה בְּעָלְמָא לָא קָנֵי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Indeed,** the phrase **"and you have found it" in the verse** certainly **indicates that it came into his possession. But** one might say that the ***tanna* employed colloquial language** in the mishna. And in colloquial language, **once** a person **sees** an item, **he says: I found it, even if it did not** yet **come into his possession,** because he believes that **he acquired** the item **through mere sight.** Since it would have been possible to understand the claim of: I found it, in this manner, the *tanna* **teaches** that the litigant states definitively: **All of it is mine,** to teach **that** one **does not acquire** a lost item **through sight** alone.

### Let Him State Only Mine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיתְנֵי ״כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי״ וְלָא בָּעֵי ״אֲנִי מְצָאתִיהָ״! אִי תָּנֵי ״כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי״ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: בְּעָלְמָא דְּקָתָנֵי ״מְצָאתִיהָ״ בִּרְאִיָּה בְּעָלְמָא קָנֵי, תְּנָא ״אֲנִי מְצָאתִיהָ״ וַהֲדַר תְּנָא ״כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי״ דְּמִמִּשְׁנָה יַתִּירָה אַשְׁמְעִינַן דִּרְאִיָּה לָא קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if that was the objective of the *tanna*, **let him teach** that each party need only state: **All of it is mine, and** the litigant would **not need** to say: **I found it.** The Gemara answers: **If** the *tanna* had **taught** that it is sufficient for each party to claim only: **All of it is mine, I would say** that **in general, when** the *tanna* **teaches** that one claims: I **found it,** he means that the finder **acquires** the item **through mere sight.** Therefore, he **taught** that the litigants claimed: I **found it, and** he **then taught** that the litigants claimed: **All of it is mine,** to teach that only when the litigants each make both of these claims does the court divide the item, **as** from **the superfluous** expression in the **mishna he teaches us that one does not acquire** the item through **sight** alone.

The *gemara* then asks the converse: if so, let the *mishnah* say only *kulo sheli* and drop the *metzatiha* entirely. The answer is that the *mishnah* deliberately invokes the scenario of *metziah* — found objects — because it wishes to establish the rule that spotting does not constitute acquisition. Without *metzatiha*, the *mishnah* would simply present two people disputing ownership with no discussion of the *metziah* context, and the teaching about *re'iya* would be lost. Both phrases are therefore necessary: *tana ani metzatiha* to raise the *metziah* scenario, *ve-hadar tana kula sheli* to clarify that spotting alone does not suffice.

### Metzia vs. Purchase

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ חֲדָא קָתָנֵי? וְהָא ״זֶה״ וְ״זֶה״ קָתָנֵי: ״זֶה אוֹמֵר אֲנִי מְצָאתִיהָ וְזֶה אוֹמֵר אֲנִי מְצָאתִיהָ, זֶה אוֹמֵר כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי״ וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** After explaining the viability of this interpretation, the Gemara asks: **But how can you say** that the mishna **is teaching one** claim of each party? **But doesn't** the mishna **teach: This** one says, **and** again: **This** one says? In other words, the mishna writes: **This** one **says: I found it, and that** one **says: I found it;** and it states additionally: **This** one **says: All of it is mine,** and that one says: All of it is mine. From the fact that the *tanna* introduced each of the claims with the phrase: This one says, it is apparent that they are two separate claims, not one compound claim.

The *gemara* then rejects the *chada katani* reading entirely, on the grounds that the *mishnah*'s phrasing — *ze omer ... ve-ze omer ... ze omer ... ve-ze omer* — presents two clearly distinct cases, not a single case with two phrases. Had the *mishnah* intended to present one case, it would have formulated it as *ze omer ani metzatiha ve-kulo sheli*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ כְּדִי: רֵישָׁא בִּמְצִיאָה, וְסֵיפָא בְּמִקָּח וּמִמְכָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said, and some say** it was said by **Rav Shimi bar Ashi, and some say** it was **an unattributed [*kedi*]** statement: **The first clause,** where each party says: I found it, is referring **to** a case of **a found item,** where two people found one item. **And the latter clause,** where each party says: All of it is mine, is referring **to** a case of **buying and selling,** where each party claims that he is the one who bought the item from its seller.

The authoritative answer is given by Rav Pappa (and attributed also to Rav Shimi bar Ashi, or anonymously): *reisha bi-metziah ve-seifa be-mekach u-memkar* — the first case concerns a found object (who picked it up first), and the second case concerns a purchase (who bought the object from a merchant). These are genuinely two distinct scenarios, and the *gemara* now explains why both must be stated — *tzricha*, each is necessary.

### Metzia Rationalization

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא: דְּאִי תְּנָא מְצִיאָה הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מְצִיאָה הוּא דִּרְמוֹ רַבָּנַן שְׁבוּעָה עֲלֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם דְּמוֹרֵי וְאָמַר, חַבְרַאי לָאו מִידֵּי חָסַר בָּהּ, אֵיזִל אֶתְפִּיס וְאִתְפְּלֵיג בַּהֲדֵיהּ! אֲבָל מִקָּח וּמִמְכָּר, דְּלֵיכָּא לְמֵימַר הָכִי – אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** it is **necessary** for the mishna to teach its ruling both with regard to a found item and with regard to a purchase. **As had** the *tanna* **taught** the case of **a found item** alone, **I would say** that **it is** only in the case of **a found item that the Sages imposed an oath upon him, as** in that case **one can rationalize** his actions **and say: The other** party, the one who in fact found the item, **is not losing anything** by not keeping all of **it,** as it was not his to begin with. **I will go seize** it from him **and divide** it **with him. But** in the case of **buying and selling, where that cannot be said, say** that the Sages did **not** impose an oath upon him.

Had the *mishnah* stated only the *metziah* case, one might have thought: *metziah hu de-ramu rabbanan shevua alei mishum de-mori ve-amar* — it is specifically in the found-object case that the Rabbis imposed an oath, because that is the case most prone to rationalization. The reasoning: my opponent did not own the object a moment ago, he was not looking for it, he was not planning on it — it simply came his way by good fortune. I can easily convince myself that I need it more, that I deserve it more, that he is not truly losing something he had invested in. The rationalization writes itself: *chaverai lav midi chasar bah* — my friend is not really losing anything he had before.

The oath in that case is maximally effective, because the person making the false claim is doing so through self-justification rather than brazen dishonesty. The *shevuah* cuts through the rationalization and forces honesty.

But if only the *metziah* case were taught, one might think: *aval mekach u-memkar* — but in a purchase dispute — *de-leika le-meimar hachi* — you cannot say that. The object was something the other party deliberately sought out, paid for, and clearly wanted. It is much harder to rationalize taking it from him. If one cannot rationalize, one is either a brazen thief who knowingly lies — in which case a *shevuah* is of questionable value — or else genuinely believes his own claim, in which case a *shevuah* will not produce an admission either. *Eima lo* — perhaps in the *mekach u-memkar* case there should be no oath.

### Purchase Rationalization

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי תְּנָא מִקָּח וּמִמְכָּר, הוּא דִּרְמוֹ רַבָּנַן שְׁבוּעָה עֲלֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם דְּמוֹרֵי וְאָמַר: חַבְרַאי דְּמֵי קָא יָהֵיב וַאֲנָא דְּמֵי קָא יָהֵיבְנָא, הַשְׁתָּה דִּצְרִיכָא לְדִידִי אֶשְׁקְלֵיהּ אֲנָא, וְחַבְרַאי לֵיזִיל לִטְרַח לִיזְבַּן, אֲבָל מְצִיאָה, דְּלֵיכָּא לְמֵימַר הָכִי – אֵימָא לָא. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And had** the *tanna* **taught** the case of **buying and selling** alone, one might say that **it is** specifically in this case **that the Sages imposed an oath upon him, because he could rationalize** his actions, **saying** to himself: **The other** party **gave money** to the seller **and I gave money** to the seller; **now that I need** it **for myself, I will take it and let the other one go to the trouble to buy** another item like the first item. **But** in the case of **a found item, where that cannot be said, say** that the Sages did **not** suspect that he seized an item that did not belong to him, and therefore there is no need to impose an oath upon him. Therefore, both cases are **necessary.**

To counter this, the *gemara* presents the opposite argument. Had only the *mekach u-memkar* case been stated, one might have concluded that it is specifically the purchase case where the oath is necessary and effective, because there too rationalization is very much available. The reasoning runs: *demeika yahiv ve-ana demeika yahiv* — we both paid the merchant. Whoever loses the case will get his money back. So I am not truly taking anything of lasting value from my opponent — he will be reimbursed, he will find another animal, and I need this one more urgently. *Ashkelei ana ve-chaverai leizel litrach ve-lizban* — I'll take it, and he can go to the trouble of buying another one. That entire chain of self-justification constitutes *mori ve-amar*, and the oath is effective precisely at disrupting it.

But in the *metziah* case, there is no such comfortable rationalization available in the other direction: if I claim a lost object that my opponent found, he is not getting it back. He is simply out the value of whatever he found. The loss is real and irreversible. If I claim it anyway, I am either lying outright — knowing I am stealing — or sincerely mistaken. Neither scenario is one where a *shevuah* is the most effective instrument. *Eima lo* — perhaps in the *metziah* case there should be no oath.

### Merchant Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִקָּח וּמִמְכָּר, וְלִחְזֵי זוּזֵי מִמַּאן נָקֵט? לָא צְרִיכָא דְּנָקֵט מִתַּרְוַיְיהוּ, מֵחַד מִדַּעְתֵּיהּ וּמֵחַד בְּעַל כָּרְחֵיהּ, וְלָא יָדַעְנָא מִי הוּא מִדַּעְתֵּיהּ, וּמִי הוּא בְּעַל כָּרְחֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: How can the mishna be referring to a case of **buying and selling? But let us see from whom** the seller **took the money.** Obviously, the one who gave him the money is the one who bought it. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** in a case **where he took** money **from both of them.** He accepted the money **willingly from** the **one** to whom he wanted to sell the item, **and** he received the money **against his will from** the **one** to whom he did not want to sell the item, **and I do not know who is** the one from whom he took it **willingly, and who is** the one from whom he received it **against his will.** Consequently, the matter is clarified by means of an oath.

If two parties are disputing who purchased an object, why not simply ask the merchant whom he sold it to? *Ve-lechzei zuzei mimi nakat* — let us see whose money he accepted.

The answer is *tzricha* for the specific case of *de-nakat mi-tarveihu* — where the merchant took money from both of them. *Michad mida'ato u-michad ba'al karcho* — one he agreed to accept payment from, and from the other he was compelled to accept it, but he no longer remembers which was which. Both parties pressed money upon him simultaneously; he knows he concluded a sale with one of them, but he cannot say which. In this scenario, the merchant's testimony is unavailing, and the dispute returns to the baseline situation of contested, equally-supported claims — which is precisely where the oath becomes relevant.

## Ben Nanas & Sumchus

### Ben Nanas Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּבֶן נַנָּס, דְּאִי בֶּן נַנָּס, הָאָמַר: כֵּיצַד אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ בָּאִין לִידֵי שְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **ben Nannas, as, if** it were in accordance with the opinion of **ben Nannas, doesn't he say** that an oath is not administered to two parties in court when one of them is certainly lying? As it is stated in a mishna (*Shevuot* 45a): With regard to a case where a man said to his laborer: Go to the storekeeper and he will give you food in lieu of your salary, and sometime later the laborer claimed that the storekeeper did not give him anything while the storekeeper claimed that he did, the Rabbis say: The storekeeper and the laborer must each take an oath to support their claims, and the employer must pay them both. Ben Nannas says in response: **How** can you allow **these** people, i.e., the laborer, **and those** people, i.e., the storekeeper, to **come to** take **an oath in vain?** Since one of them is definitely lying, the Sages would not impose the taking of an oath that by definition must be false. Similarly, in the case of the mishna here, since the found item is divided between the parties in any case, according to the opinion of ben Nannas they should receive their portions without taking an oath.

Having established the ruling of the *mishnah*, the *gemara* asks whether it is consistent with all Tannaic opinions. *Leima matnitin d'lo ke-Ben Nanas* — let us say our *mishnah* does not follow Ben Nanas.

Ben Nanas poses an objection based on a different case. If a person instructs a shopkeeper to extend credit to his workers, and at the end the shopkeeper says the workers took a hundred dinars' worth of goods while the workers say the shopkeeper never gave them anything — both the shopkeeper and the workers swear, and the employer pays both. Ben Nanas objects: *keitzad elu va-elu ba'in lidei shvuat shav* — how can we bring both of them to a false oath? In that case, one of the two groups is definitely lying, and we are causing a *shvuat shav* — a false oath — to be taken.

### Possible Honest Oath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא בֶּן נַנָּס, הָתָם וַדַּאי אִיכָּא שְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא. הָכָא אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר דְּלֵיכָּא שְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא, אֵימוֹר דְּתַרְוַיְיהוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי אַגְבְּהוּהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: It is **even** possible for **you to say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **ben Nannas. There,** in the case of the laborer and the storekeeper, **an oath is certainly** taken in **vain,** as it is clear that one of them is lying. **Here, there is** room **to say that there is no oath** taken in **vain. Say that they** are **both** taking an oath truthfully, as they **lifted** the item **together,** and therefore each of them owns half of it. In this case, ben Nannas would agree that they both take an oath.

The *gemara* answers by distinguishing the two cases. *Hatam vadai ika shvuat shav* — in Ben Nanas's case, there is definitely a false oath being taken; the shopkeeper and the workers cannot both be telling the truth. *Hacha ika leimeir d'leika shvuat shav* — here, in our *mishnah*, it is possible that there is no false oath at all. *Eima d'tarveihu bahadi hadadi agbeu* — perhaps they both picked it up at the same time, or the merchant agreed to sell it to both simultaneously. In that scenario, each one genuinely owns half, and each one's oath — *she'ein lo pachos mechetzyo* — is entirely true.

The scenario of joint simultaneous acquisition may not be the most likely explanation for most such disputes, but it is a genuine possibility. And because it is possible that both oaths are truthful, the *mishnah*'s procedure does not violate Ben Nanas's principle. Ben Nanas objects only where a false oath is *certain*; where a truthful oath is at least possible, he does not object.

### Sumchus on Doubt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּסוֹמְכוֹס, דְּאִי כְּסוֹמְכוֹס, הָאָמַר: מָמוֹן הַמּוּטָּל בְּסָפֵק – חוֹלְקִין בְּלֹא שְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests further: **Let us say that the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Sumakhos, as, if** it were **in accordance with** the opinion of **Sumakhos, doesn't he say** the following principle: In a case of **property of uncertain ownership,** the parties **divide** it **without** taking **an oath.**

The *gemara* then raises a more pointed challenge. *Leima matnitin d'lo ke-Sumchus* — let us say our *mishnah* does not follow Sumchus.

Sumchus is known from *Bava Kamma* for the ruling: *mamon ha-mutal be-safek cholkin* — money or property whose ownership is in doubt is divided. The classic case involves a cow that was sold, and a calf was found beside it after the sale. The seller claims the calf was born before the sale and therefore belongs to him; the buyer claims it was born after and belongs to him. The *chachamim* say: the seller retains the *chazakah* — he was the owner before the sale — and the burden of proof falls on the buyer. Sumchus says: since there is genuine doubt, we split it.

The *gemara* adds a crucial detail: Sumchus rules *belo shevuah* — without an oath. He requires neither party to swear. The *mishnah*, however, requires an oath. *Leima matnitin d'lo ke-Sumchus* — our *mishnah* seems to be at odds with Sumchus.

### Rabbanan Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מַאי, רַבָּנַן? הָא אָמְרִי הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵרוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **Rather, what** opinion does the mishna follow? Does the mishna follow the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who disagree with Sumakhos? **Don't they say** that in a case of property of uncertain ownership **the burden of proof rests upon the claimant?** In the case of the mishna neither side offers proof.

The *gemara* asks: and if not Sumchus, is our *mishnah* the position of the *rabbanan* who dispute him? But that creates its own problem — *ha amri*, the *rabbanan* say *hamotzi mechaveiro alav hara'ayah*. They do not say split at all; they say whoever is the default owner retains the object until the other party brings proof. So our *mishnah*, which does split the object, cannot be the *rabbanan* either. *Hai mai* — what kind of question is this?

### Muchzak Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מַאי? אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא רַבָּנַן: הָתָם דְּלָא תָּפְסִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ אֲמַרוּ רַבָּנַן הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה, הָכָא דְּתַרְוַיְיהוּ תָּפְסִי [פָּלְגִי] לַהּ בִּשְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **What** is **this** comparison? **Granted, if you say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis** there, in the case of property of uncertain ownership, there is room to distinguish between two cases: **There,** in the case of property of uncertain ownership, **where both** parties **are not grasping** the property, **the Rabbis say** that **the burden of proof rests upon the claimant** since the one with possession of the property ostensibly has the right to that property. **Here,** in the case of the mishna, **where both are grasping** the property and neither has exclusive possession of the item, **they divide it with** the proviso that they take **an oath.**

The *gemara* answers: the *rabbanan*'s position makes perfect sense as applied to the calf case. *Hatam de-lo tafsi tarvaihu* — there, only one party is the *muchzak*, and the other is the *motzi* seeking to extract from that *chazakah*; the *rabbanan* therefore require proof. *Hacha de-tarvaihu tafsi* — here, both are equally holding the garment and equally *muchzak*; the *rabbanan* would agree to split, and given that splitting is necessary but the claims are contested, they would also require a *shevuah* to try to get at the truth.

### Shema vs. Bari

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא סוֹמְכוֹס: כִּי אָמַר סוֹמְכוֹס שֶׁמָּא וְשֶׁמָּא, אֲבָל בָּרִי וּבָרִי – לָא אָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **You may even say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Sumakhos. When Sumakhos states** that in a case of property of uncertain ownership, the parties divide it without taking an oath, that is in the case of **an uncertain** claim **and an uncertain** claim, i.e., when the circumstances are such that neither party can state definitively that he is entitled to the property. **But** in a case of **a certain** claim **and a certain** claim, where each party states definitively that he is entitled to the property, Sumakhos **does not say** that they divide the property without taking an oath.

The *gemara* now proposes that Sumchus could still agree with the *mishnah*: *ki amar Sumchus shema va-shema* — Sumchus's ruling of split-without-oath applies specifically when both parties speak in terms of uncertainty (*shema*). In the calf case, neither party actually *knows* when the calf was born; each is expressing what he believes or hopes but cannot assert with certainty. In such a case, what *shevuah* could one even formulate? One cannot swear definitively about something one does not know.

*Aval bari va-bari* — but when both parties make definite (*bari*) claims, as in our *mishnah* where each says flatly "it is mine," Sumchus would concede that an oath is called for. A definite claim can support a definite oath, and the possibility that the oath will induce an admission — or at least stop rationalization — remains live. Sumchus's exception therefore does not extend to the case of two equally definite, competing *bari* claims.

### Drara Demamona

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא, דְּאָמַר: אָמַר סוֹמְכוֹס אֲפִילּוּ בָּרִי וּבָרִי, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to** the opinion of **Rabba bar Rav Huna, who said** that **Sumakhos says** that **even** in a case of **a certain** claim **and a certain** claim the parties divide the property without an oath, **what is there to say** to establish the mishna in accordance with the opinion of Sumakhos?

The *gemara* asks what to do if Rabba bar Rav Huna's interpretation is correct — that Sumchus does *not* even require an oath in the *bari-bari* case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא סוֹמְכוֹס, כִּי אָמַר סוֹמְכוֹס – הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא דְּרָרָא דְמָמוֹנָא, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּלֵיכָּא דְּרָרָא דְמָמוֹנָא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **You may even say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Sumakhos. When Sumakhos says** that property of uncertain ownership is divided, he is referring to a case **where** the litigants **have a financial association** with the item independent of their claims to it. **But where** the litigants **do not have a financial association** with the item beyond their claims, they do not divide it without taking an oath.

The *gemara* then introduces an additional limiting principle for Sumchus's position. *Ki amar Sumchus heicha de-ika drara demamona* — Sumchus says split without oath only where there is *drara demamona* — an objective monetary stake or objective uncertainty arising from the facts themselves. *Aval heicha de-leika drara demamona lo* — where there is no such objective stake, he would not say so.

The term *drara demamona* is the subject of significant dispute among the *rishonim*, but Tosafot's understanding is that it refers to a situation where, setting aside the parties' claims entirely, the known and agreed-upon facts themselves generate an objective question about ownership. In the calf case: everyone agrees the cow was sold, everyone agrees a calf was born, and the simple passage of time creates an objective factual uncertainty — when exactly did the birth occur? That is *drara demamona*. The doubt is not manufactured by the litigation; it inheres in the facts.

### Sumchus Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָאו קַל וָחוֹמֶר הוּא? וּמָה הָתָם דְּאִיכָּא דְּרָרָא דְמָמוֹנָא לְמָר, וְאִיכָּא דְּרָרָא דְמָמוֹנָא לְמָר, וְאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר כּוּלָּהּ לְמָר, וְאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר כּוּלָּהּ לְמָר, אָמַר סוֹמְכוֹס: מָמוֹן הַמּוּטָּל בְּסָפֵק חוֹלְקִין בְּלֹא שְׁבוּעָה, הָכָא, דְּלֵיכָּא דְּרָרָא דְמָמוֹנָא, דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר דְּתַרְוַיְיהוּ הִיא, לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But is it not an *a fortiori*** inference? **If there,** in a case **where** this **Master has a financial association** with the item, **and** that **Master has a financial association** with the item, **and there is** room **to say that it** belongs **entirely to one** of them, **and there is** also room **to say that it** belongs **entirely to** the other **one,** and nevertheless **Sumakhos says** that since it is **property of uncertain ownership they divide** it **without** taking **an oath,** then **here, where** the litigants have **no financial association** with the item, **as there is** room **to say that it** belongs to **both of them, all the more so** is it **not** clear that they should divide it without taking an oath?

In our *mishnah*, by contrast, the entire dispute is generated by the parties' claims and their act of grabbing the garment. If one were to set aside the claims and look only at agreed-upon facts, there would be very little — only the bare fact that both are holding opposite ends of a *talit*. The uncertainty arises from the competing assertions, not from any objective factual puzzle that exists independently of those assertions. In Tosafot's formulation: *drara demamona* exists where the doubt would be recognizable to an outside observer examining only agreed-upon facts; it does not exist where the doubt is entirely a product of the parties' claims before *beit din*.

Where there is no *drara demamona*, Sumchus would agree that a *shevuah* is called for — because the mechanism of the oath, which pushes back against rationalization and forces claimants to confront their own honesty, is precisely what is needed when the only source of uncertainty is the claims themselves.

## Kal Vachomer

### Kal Vachomer Setup

The *gemara* immediately challenges the *drara demamona* distinction with a *kal vachomer*. If Sumchus rules split-without-oath even in the calf case — where one party is taking from the other's established *chazakah* and the split is therefore, by any reckoning, a significant departure from the default legal position — then why should he require an oath in the garment case, where both parties are equally holding and the split is a far more natural outcome?

### Unfaithful Psak

The force of the *kal vachomer* turns on a pointed observation: in the calf case, the *psak* of splitting is admittedly not *k'fi ha-emet* — not according to the truth. The calf is either entirely the seller's or entirely the buyer's. It cannot genuinely belong to both. Yet Sumchus is willing to issue that ruling without even attempting to use a *shevuah* to get closer to the truth.

### More Truthful Here

In the garment case, by contrast, the *psak* of splitting *could* be *k'fi ha-emet*. As the *gemara* established earlier, it is genuinely possible — *eima d'tarvaihu bahadi hadadi agbeu* — that both parties picked it up simultaneously and each legitimately owns half. The ruling of *yachloku* is not inherently false; it may reflect the actual state of ownership.

The *kal vachomer* therefore runs: if Sumchus is willing to issue a ruling he knows may be wrong, without seeking to refine it through a *shevuah*, then all the more so he should be willing to issue a ruling that may be entirely correct, also without a *shevuah*. The lesser departure from truth (or no departure at all) should require less resort to the oath, not more.

### Unresolved Tension

The *gemara* raises this *kal vachomer* as a genuine challenge and does not resolve it within this *amud*. Why Sumchus might nonetheless agree that the garment case requires a *shevuah* — despite the *kal vachomer* suggesting otherwise — is a question that remains open here and will be taken up in the continuation.
