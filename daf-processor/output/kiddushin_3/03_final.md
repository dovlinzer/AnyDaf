# Kiddushin 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kinyan vs. Bi'ah Models

### Nikneit vs. Mekadesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִיתְנֵי הָתָם ״הָאִישׁ קוֹנֶה״! מֵעִיקָּרָא תָּנֵי לִישָּׁנָא דְאוֹרָיְיתָא, וּלְבַסּוֹף תָּנֵי לִישָּׁנָא דְרַבָּנַן, וּמַאי לִישָּׁנָא דְרַבָּנַן? – דְּאָסַר לַהּ אַכּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא כְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But let** the mishna **teach there,** in the next chapter: **A man acquires.** The Gemara explains: **Initially,** the mishna **taught** using **the language of the Torah,** in which betrothal is called taking. **And ultimately,** in the next chapter, **it taught** using **the language of the Sages. And what** is the reason that betrothal is called *kiddushin*, literally, consecration, in the **language of the Sages?** The reason is **that** through betrothal the husband **renders her forbidden to everyone like consecrated** property. Therefore, this act is referred to as consecration.

Today we take up *Kiddushin* daf 3, picking up towards the bottom of amud 2. The first *daf* of the *masechet* laid critical groundwork, and the *Gemara* continues here with what is, on its surface, a technical grammatical introduction — yet embedded within it are some of the most fundamental conceptual questions of the entire *masechet*.

The opening discussion centered on the verb *nikneit* — *ha'isha nikneit* — rather than what we find later in the *masechet*, where the language shifts to *mekadesh* or *mekudeshet*. This seemingly minor terminological difference opens onto a major conceptual fault line: is *kiddushin* to be understood as a type of *kinyan*, a property acquisition, in which the woman is essentially the object being purchased? The *Gemara* identified this as *lishna d'kra*, the Torah's own framing of the institution — rooted, as we saw, in the *gezeira shava* from the purchase of Ephron's field.

### Lishna D'Kra vs. Status

On the other side stands the rabbinic understanding encoded in the term *mekudeshet*. As *Tosafot* explains it, *mekudeshet* is not merely a passive status of being forbidden to others — it is *mekudeshet li*, forbidden to others specifically as a result of the relationship between this particular man and this particular woman. This is a status model rather than a property model, and the distinction could not be more consequential. These two frameworks — *kinyan* and *kiddushin* as status — will compete with one another throughout the first half of the first *perek*.

### Object vs. Subject

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִיתְנֵי הָכָא ״הָאִישׁ קוֹנֶה״! מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא בָּעֵי לְמִיתְנָא סֵיפָא: ״וְקוֹנָה אֶת עַצְמָהּ״ בְּדִידַהּ, תְּנָא נָמֵי רֵישָׁא בְּדִידַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks another question with regard to the difference in wording between the two *mishnayot*: **And let it teach here,** as in the following chapter: **A man acquires.** Why does this mishna teach: The woman is acquired, with the woman as the subject of the sentence? The Gemara answers: This is **because** the *tanna* **wanted to teach** in **the latter clause** of the mishna: **And she acquires herself,** which is stated **with regard to her.** Therefore, the *tanna* **also taught** the *halakha* stated **with regard to her** in **the first clause.**

What makes the *Gemara*'s introduction even more striking is the way it reads the *Mishnah*'s language against itself. The *Mishnah* does indeed treat the woman as a kind of grammatical object — *ha'isha nikneit* sits alongside *yivama nikneit*, *eved ivri*, *eved kna'ani*, *nechasim she'yesh lahem achrayut*, the list moving between persons and things being acquired. And yet the *stama d'Gemara* insists that the choice of *nikneit* rather than *ha'ish koneh* is precisely to foreground her role as a subject — to emphasize that *kiddushin* cannot be done against her will, that she must be a consenting party. This is notably not the Torah model; in the Torah, the woman as an adult acting for herself is nowhere explicit. The *Gemara* here is registering a shift: from the Torah's model, in which the father gives her as a minor, to the world of *Chazal*, in which she is an adult participant.

## Drachim vs. Devarim

### Gender Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי אִירְיָא דְּתָנֵי ״שָׁלֹשׁ״? לִיתְנֵי ״שְׁלֹשָׁה״! מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא בָּעֵי לְמִיתְנֵי ״דֶּרֶךְ״ וְ״דֶרֶךְ״ לְשׁוֹן נְקֵבָה הוּא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהוֹדַעְתָּ לָהֶם אֶת הַדֶּרֶךְ יֵלְכוּ בָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to analyze the style of the mishna: **And why does** the *tanna* **specifically teach: Three [*shalosh*]** ways, formulated in the feminine? **Let it teach: Three [*shelosha*]** ways, formulated in the masculine. The Gemara explains: The mishna uses this form **because it wants to teach** the word **way [*derekh*], and *derekh* is formulated** in the **feminine, as it is written: "And you shall show them the way [*derekh*] in which [*bah*] they must walk"** (Exodus 18:20). The term *bah*, which is referring to *derekh*, is formulated in the feminine.

Continuing along the bottom of the *daf*, the *Gemara* raises the question of gender agreement in the phrase *shlosha drachim*. Since *derech* can be either masculine or feminine in biblical Hebrew, and in fact appears both ways in different *mishnayot*, the *Gemara* asks *mai ta'ama tani shalosh* — why the feminine form *shalosh* rather than *shlosha*? The answer offered is that *derech* takes on a feminine character here because the woman, who is the subject of the *Mishnah*, inflects the gender of the word. This is, admittedly, a point that is difficult to get especially animated about. But behind it lies a more interesting question, to which the *Gemara* immediately pivots.

### Derech and Bi'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא תָּנֵי ״שָׁלֹשׁ״ – מִשּׁוּם ״דְּרָכִים״? נִיתְנֵי ״דְּבָרִים״ וְנִיתְנֵי ״שְׁלֹשָׁה״! מִשּׁוּם דְּקָבָעֵי לְמִיתְנֵי ״בִּיאָה״, וּבִיאָה אִיקְּרִי ״דֶּרֶךְ״, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְדֶרֶךְ גֶּבֶר בְּעַלְמָה כֵּן דֶּרֶךְ אִשָּׁה מְנָאָפֶת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks another question with regard to the language of the mishna: **What is the reason** that the mishna **teaches: Three [*shalosh*],** formulated in the feminine? This is **because** it wanted to teach: **Ways. But** if so, **let it teach** instead the word: **Matters,** i.e., a woman can be acquired through three matters, **and** as this term is masculine, **let it teach three [*shelosha*],** in the masculine. The Gemara answers: The mishna did do so **because it wanted to teach intercourse** as one of these ways, **and intercourse is called a way** in the Torah, **as it is written: "And the way of a man with a young woman, so is the way of an adulterous woman"** (Proverbs 30:19-20). For this reason the mishna used the term ways rather than matters.

The deeper question is: why say *drachim* at all? Why not simply say *b'shlosha devarim* — in three things, or three matters? In English the difference between "a woman is acquired in three ways" and "a woman is acquired by three things" is not trivial, and the *Gemara* takes the distinction seriously. The answer: *mishum d'ka ba'ei l'mitni bi'ah* — because the *Mishnah* is going to list *bi'ah*, sexual union, as one of the three modes, and *bi'ah* is linguistically and conceptually associated with the word *derech*. As the *pasuk* says, *v'derech gever b'alma* — the way of a man with a young woman. And similarly, *ken derech isha menafet* — the way of an adulterous woman. The word *derech* is bound up with the concept of *bi'ah* in the biblical imagination.

### Derech Natural Way

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא תִּינַח ״בִּיאָה״, ״כֶּסֶף״ וּ״שְׁטָר״ מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? מִשּׁוּם ״בִּיאָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **This works out well** with regard to **intercourse,** which is referred to as a way. But **what is there to say** concerning **money and a document?** The mishna could have used the word matters with regard to these modes of betrothal. The Gemara answers: **Because** it was necessary to mention **intercourse,** which is called a way, the mishna used the word way in reference to the other two modes as well.

But then the *Gemara* poses the obvious challenge: very well, *derech* may be the right word for *bi'ah*, but what about *kesef* and *shtar*? Why should those also be called *drachim*? The answer: *hanahu nami tzorech bi'ah ninhu* — those two also ultimately exist in service of *bi'ah*. All of *kiddushin*, on this reading, is oriented toward and framed by the eventual union of the couple. The word *derech*, applied to all three modes, reflects the fact that the entire institution is pointing toward *bi'ah* as its telos. This is a striking claim, and it parallels the earlier move with *nikneit*: just as the term *nikneit* frames the whole institution even though it is strictly appropriate only to *kesef*, so *derech* frames all three modes even though it is strictly appropriate only to *bi'ah*.

## Derech Framing

### Man Seeks Woman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי אַטּוּ חֲדָא? הָנָךְ נָמֵי צוֹרֶךְ בִּיאָה נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** would the mishna **teach two** cases in a particular manner **due to one?** Since the word way suits only one of the three modes of betrothal, why didn't the mishna use the term: Matters, on account of the other two? The Gemara answers: **These, too, are for the sake of sexual intercourse.** Since the marital relationship, in which intercourse is paramount, is the ultimate purpose of betrothal, the mishna considers this clause as the most important part of the *halakha*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה ״כִּי יִקַּח אִישׁ אִשָּׁה״, וְלֹא כָּתַב ״כִּי תִּלָּקַח אִשָּׁה לְאִישׁ״? – מִפְּנֵי שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ שֶׁל אִישׁ לְחַזֵּר עַל אִשָּׁה וְאֵין דַּרְכָּהּ שֶׁל אִשָּׁה לְחַזֵּר עַל אִישׁ. מָשָׁל לְאָדָם שֶׁאָבְדָה לוֹ אֲבֵידָה – מִי חוֹזֵר עַל מִי? בַּעַל אֲבֵידָה מְחַזֵּר עַל אֲבֵידָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna, which teaches *derekh*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says: For what** reason **did the Torah say: "When a man takes a woman"** (Deuteronomy 22:13) **and did not write: "When a woman is taken by a man? Because it is the way [*derekh*] of a man to pursue a woman, and it is not the way of a woman to pursue a man.** The Gemara cites **a parable of a man who lost an item. Who searches for what?** Certainly **the owner of the lost item searches for his lost item,** not the other way around. Since woman was created from man's lost side, the man seeks that which he has lost. To allude to this statement of Rabbi Shimon, the mishna employs the term *derekh* in this context.

This is where the *Gemara* turns from grammatical observation to something considerably more profound. The *stama d'Gemara* offers a second explanation for the word *drachim*, introduced with *ve'iba'it eima ha mani Rabbi Shimon hi* — an alternative reading attributed to Rabbi Shimon. The *Gemara* cites a *baraita*: *Rabbi Shimon omer, mipnei mah amrah Torah ki yikach ish isha v'lo k'tiv ki tilakach isha le'ish* — why does the Torah say "when a man takes a woman" and not "when a woman is taken by a man"? Why is the man framed as the active party in *kiddushin*?

The answer: *mipnei shedarko shel ish lachazor achar haishah v'ein darkah shel ishah lachazor achar haish* — because it is the way of a man to seek out a woman, and it is not the way of a woman to seek out a man. Even in today's more egalitarian social landscape, this dynamic has not entirely disappeared — the expectation that the man is more often the initiator in courtship remains, however contested, a recognizable pattern. And this dynamic also has a direct *halachic* implication: the man is the active party in the act of *kiddushin* itself.

### Aveidah Metaphor

What follows is a beautiful and theologically rich *mashal*: *mashal l'adam she'avdah lo aveidah* — it is like a person who has lost an object. Why does he go looking for it? Because the object was lost from *him*. So too, *mi chozer al mi? Ba'al aveidah mechazer al aveidato* — the one who suffered the loss is the one who goes searching. The man seeks the woman because she is his lost half — the part of him taken away in *maaseh bereishit*, when woman was formed from man. The reference is to *al ken ya'azov ish et aviv ve'et imo v'davak b'ishto v'hayu l'vasar echad* — the two halves rejoining as one flesh.

This metaphor deserves careful attention, because it is doing something quite different from the *kinyan* model. Yes, the *aveidah* metaphor still contains a possessive element — it is something of his that he is reclaiming. But the emotional and conceptual valence is entirely different from the acquisition of an object. One's *aveidah* is not a commodity; it is something one is incomplete without. The resonance is with *bashert* — with the idea that the union is a restoration of something originally whole, not the purchase of something external.

### Beyond Kinyan

The *Gemara* is therefore introducing, right at the outset of the *masechet*, several competing models of what is actually happening in *kiddushin*. On one side sits the *kinyan* framework, with its emphasis on acquisition, property transfer, and the woman as object. On the other side sits the *bi'ah* framework, with its emphasis on natural union, biological and existential complementarity, and the man not as purchaser but as the one searching for his lost half. It is worth pausing to appreciate how powerful it is that both of these framings appear simultaneously in the *Gemara*'s introduction. Saying that all of *kiddushin* is ultimately about the *kinyan* model, or all about the *bi'ah* model, would be too simple. The *Gemara* is presenting both, letting them sit in tension.

There is a related and important observation about the man's role as active party. One might have said: of course the man is the active one, because she is merely the object being purchased. But Rabbi Shimon's answer reframes this entirely. The man is the active one not because she is a passive object, but because he is the one who has lost something and must go searching. This moves the dynamic from domination to longing.

### Natural Operation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דִּתְנַן: ״בְּשִׁבְעָה דְּרָכִים בּוֹדְקִין אֶת הַזָּב״, לִיתְנֵי ״דְּבָרִים״! הָתָם הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּדַרְכָּא דְּמֵיכְלָא יַתִּירָא לְאֵתוֹיֵי לִידֵי זִיבָה, וְדַרְכָּא דְּמִישְׁתְּיָא יַתִּירָא לְאֵתוֹיֵי לִידֵי זִיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** with regard to **that which we learned** in a mishna: **One examines a *zav* in seven ways,** why does it use this phraseology? **Let it teach** the word: **Matters.** The Gemara answers that the mishna **there teaches us this** *halakha*, **that it is the way of excessive eating to lead to *ziva*, and** likewise **it is the way of excessive drinking to lead to *ziva*.** Therefore, the mishna uses the phrase: Seven ways, to emphasize that there are ways of behavior that can cause the emission of a *zav*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דִּתְנַן: ״אֶתְרוֹג שָׁוֶה לָאִילָן בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה דְּרָכִים״, לִיתְנֵי ״דְּבָרִים״! מִשּׁוּם דְּבָעֵינַן מִתְנֵי סֵיפָא: ״וְלַיָּרָק בְּדֶרֶךְ אֶחָד״. סֵיפָא נָמֵי, נִיתְנֵי ״דָּבָר״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further challenges: **And** with regard to **that which we learned** in a mishna (*Bikkurim* 2:6): The *halakhot* of **an *etrog*** tree **correspond to** those of **a tree in three ways. Let it teach** instead: Three **matters.** The Gemara answers: **Because it wants to teach in** the **latter clause: And** the *halakhot* of an *etrog* tree correspond **to** those of **a vegetable in one way,** therefore the mishna uses the term: Ways, in the first clause as well. The Gemara asks: In the **latter clause too, let** the mishna **teach: Matter,** rather than: Way.

The *Gemara* then broadens its inquiry into the word *drachim* by testing it against other *mishnayot* that use the same term. It asks: *vehasnan, be-zayin drachim bodkim et hazav* — we examine a *zav* in seven *drachim* to determine whether his flow results from natural causes. Why say *drachim* rather than *devarim*? The answer: *hasam kamashma lan de'orcha demichla yasira la'asuyei lidei ziva* — the word *derech* conveys that excessive eating naturally leads to a discharge; this is the way things work. Similarly with drink: *ve'orcha demishtaya yasira la'asuyei lidei ziva*. The word *derech* signals not merely a list of items but a natural causal relationship — these are the ways in which the body operates.

The *Gemara* then turns to the *mishnah*: *etrog shaveh le'ilan be-gimmel drachim* — the *etrog* resembles a tree in three *drachim*. Why not say *devarim*? The answer: *mishum de'ba'inan l'mitni seifa, v'leyarak be-derech echad* — because the *Mishnah* also needs to say that it resembles a vegetable in one *derech*. But this just shifts the question: why say *derech* there either? And the *Gemara*'s answer in both places is *de'orcha de'etrog keyarak* — the *way* of the *etrog* is like the *way* of a vegetable, in that it grows on all waters and goes by the time of its picking for *maaser* purposes.

Here it is worth stepping back to explain the underlying *halacha*. By trees, the year that determines which *maaser* applies is the year of blossoming. By vegetables, it is the year of picking. An *etrog* behaves like a tree for *shemita* purposes — we go by when it blossoms. But for *maaser* purposes, it behaves like a vegetable, because like a vegetable it grows continuously throughout the year and can be picked at any time; it is not fixed to one season. *Rashi* explains that the *etrog*'s similarity to a tree includes the *halachot* of *orlah* and *neta revai*, while its similarity to a vegetable includes that its *maaser* obligation follows the time of picking. The use of the word *derech* in both cases signals that these are not arbitrary classifications but reflect something about how each organism naturally operates — how it grows, how it fruits, what it requires.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם הָא קָמַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּדַרְכֵּיהּ דְּאֶתְרוֹג כְּיָרָק – מָה יָרָק דַּרְכּוֹ לִיגְדַּל עַל כׇּל מַיִם וּבִשְׁעַת לְקִיטָתוֹ עִישּׂוּרוֹ, אַף אֶתְרוֹג דַּרְכּוֹ לִיגְדַּל עַל כׇּל מַיִם וּבִשְׁעַת לְקִיטָתוֹ עִישּׂוּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **There,** the mishna **teaches us this, that** the **way an *etrog*** grows is **like a vegetable: Just as it is the way of a vegetable to grow by** being watered by **all water,** i.e., in addition to rainwater it requires irrigation, **and its tithing is according to when it is harvested,** i.e., its tithing is based on when it is collected from the field, **so too, it is the way of an *etrog* to grow by** being watered by **all water,** as it requires more water than rain provides, **and its tithing is according to the time when it is harvested.** If, for example, a vegetable is collected during a year when poor man's tithe is given, that tithe is separated from it, even if it formed a bud during the previous year, when second tithe was separated. The same applies to an *etrog*. Therefore, the mishna specifically uses the term: Way, to allude to this reason.

This carries back to *kiddushin*. The insight that *drachim* is used when natural operation or natural causation is being invoked is significant. *Bi'ah* as a mode of *kiddushin* is not arbitrary — there is something deeply natural about physical union as the expression and actualization of marriage. There is a compelling argument, indeed, that among the three modes, *bi'ah* represents the most primordially natural one. The *Rambam* notes, based on the *Gemara*, that for *Bnei Noach* before the Torah was given, a man and woman who wished to marry would come together in sexual union and thereby be married. This is the *derech* in its most natural sense — not a societal formalism, not a written document, but the physical coming together that *Bereishit* itself describes in the words *v'davak b'ishto*.

This observation opens onto a further question raised in the course of the discussion: if *bi'ah* is the most natural and perhaps the most primordially valid mode of *kiddushin*, does that affect its *halachic* status relative to the others? *Rashi* already cites, in the name of his *rabotai*, a *shita* — associated also with the *Geonim* and reflected in a *sugya* at the beginning of *Ketubot* regarding *hafka'at kiddushin* — that *kiddushei kesef* is *d'rabbanan*. The *Rambam* himself says in several places that *kiddushei kesef* is *midivrei sofrim*. The most explicitly *d'oraissa* mode of *kiddushin* in the Torah may well be *bi'ah*, given the phrase *ki yikach ish isha u-ve'alah* — though we noted yesterday that *ki yikach ish isha* in the plain reading of the Torah refers to *nissuin* rather than *kiddushin*, and that *kiddushin* in the Torah is rooted in *asher eirastiv*. Still, the *Rambam*'s position invites the reading that *bi'ah* is the primary mode and *kesef* is in some sense derived or secondary. If so, then the *Gemara*'s framing — that *kesef* and *shtar* are themselves in service of *bi'ah*, that the whole institution is shadowed by *bi'ah* — is not merely a *drasha* about word choice but a substantive claim about the architecture of *kiddushin*.

One participant in the shiur raised a sharp point: the *Gemara*'s framing may not be merely that marriage is ultimately oriented toward *bi'ah* in the sense of physical intimacy within the marriage, but that *bi'ah* is the primary act of *kiddushin* itself — and the other two modes are in service of that primary act, not merely in service of the eventual marital union. This reading has real *halachic* weight given the positions of *Rashi*'s *rabotai* and the *Rambam*.

There is something else worth noting about how *derech* functions across these cases. As one sharp observation during the shiur put it: the *Gemara* opens by making strong claims about natural order — the natural way of a man is to seek a woman, the natural way of *bi'ah* is framed by the word *derech* — and then proceeds to bring cases of in-between things, like the *etrog* that is partly like a tree and partly like a vegetable. It is almost as if the *Gemara* is subverting its own invocations of natural order by immediately showing that nature is full of hybrids and edge cases. The *koi* — that creature the *Gemara* elsewhere identifies as a hybrid of uncertain classification — is *shaveh l'chaya* in some ways and *shaveh l'behema* in others, and in some ways like neither. *Derech* as natural order is the framework, and then immediately the *Gemara* illustrates how complex natural order really is.

## Drachim vs. Devarim (II)

### Drachim Alternatives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא פְּלוּגְתָּא תָּנֵי ״דְּרָכִים״, וְכֹּל הֵיכָא דְּלֵיכָּא פְּלוּגְתָּא תָּנֵי ״דְּבָרִים״. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: ״רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֶתְרוֹג שָׁוֶה לָאִילָן לְכׇל דָּבָר״. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara rejects the previous explanations, as the phraseology is not dependent on the nature of the topic at hand. Instead, **anywhere that there is a distinction** with regard to an issue the mishna **teaches** the term: **Ways,** as there are different ways or possibilities available. **And anywhere that there is no distinction it teaches** the word: **Matters.** The Gemara comments: The language of the mishna **is also precise** in this regard, **as it teaches in the latter clause** of that mishna that **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The *halakhot* of **an *etrog*** tree **correspond to** those of **a tree with regard to every matter.** This indicates that when no distinctions apply, the mishna will use the term matter. The Gemara concludes: **Learn from** this clause in the mishna that this explanation is correct.

After surveying all these cases, the *Gemara* arrives at a clean resolution of the *drachim* versus *devarim* question. The rule is: *kol haycha d'ika plugta tani drachim* — wherever there are distinctions being drawn, wherever the point is that certain ways are specified as against alternatives, the *Mishnah* uses *drachim*. When one is listing matters without implying that alternative possibilities are being excluded, the word used is *devarim*. The proof: the *seifa* of the *mishnah* about *etrog* says *Rabbi Eliezer omer eser shaveh le'ilan l'chol davar* — similar to a tree in all matters — not *l'chol derech*. When the point is not contrast but comprehensive similarity, *devarim* is the right word. When the point is here are the paths as opposed to other possible paths, *drachim* is right.

This maps cleanly onto the usage in our *Mishnah*: a woman is acquired in three *drachim* — these three, and not others. The very phrasing signals that alternatives are being excluded. And this, of course, sets up the *Gemara*'s next move: what are those alternatives being excluded?

It is worth pausing on what these grammatical *sugyot* at the opening of a *masechet* actually accomplish. *Rav Sherira Gaon*, in his famous letter, notes that these opening passages are often characterized by an accumulation of Tannaitic material organized around seemingly trivial questions of grammar or terminology. The tendency in *yeshiva* study is to hyperfocus on every fine distinction; the opposite tendency, in survey learning, is to gloss over these passages as introductory filler. Neither extreme serves well. What the *Gemara* is doing, through these grammatical questions, is introducing the key conceptual vocabulary of the *masechet* — and, more than that, placing several competing models in tension with one another before the *halachic* analysis proper begins. The same move occurs at the opening of *Bava Kama*, where the question of whether *toladot* are equivalent to *avot* for purposes of liability serves as the frame for introducing every major category of damage in the first half of the tractate.

## Excluded Modes

### Minyana Resha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנְיָינָא דְרֵישָׁא לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי, מִנְיָינָא דְסֵיפָא לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues to analyze the language of the mishna: With regard to **the number in the first clause** of the mishna, i.e., a woman is acquired in three ways, this serves **to exclude what?** The fact that the mishna mentions a number indicates that other modes of acquisition do not apply to betrothal. What mode is excluded? Similarly, **the number in the latter clause** of the mishna, that a woman acquires herself in two ways, serves **to exclude what?**

Having established that the counting in the *Mishnah* is designed to exclude alternatives, the *Gemara* now asks directly: *minyana derisha lema'utei mai* — what does the number three in the opening clause exclude? And correspondingly, what does the number two in the closing clause exclude?

### Chuppah Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנְיָינָא דְרֵישָׁא – לְמַעוֹטֵי חוּפָּה. וּלְרַב הוּנָא, דְּאָמַר חוּפָּה קוֹנָה מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר, לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **The number in the first clause** serves **to exclude** a wedding **canopy,** which does not effect betrothal. The Gemara asks: **And according to** the opinion of **Rav Huna, who said:** A wedding **canopy acquires** a woman, and it alone could be a mode of betrothal, as derived **by an *a fortiori*** inference (see 5a), this number serves **to exclude what?**

The *Gemara* answers the first question: *minyana derisha lema'utei chuppah* — the three modes of acquisition exclude *chuppah* as a means of *kiddushin*. The case imagined is one where the father hands over his daughter, or perhaps where a man and woman proceed directly to *chuppah* without any prior *kiddushin*, and the question is whether standing under *chuppah* alone could effect *kiddushin*. *Rashi* here introduces the father specifically, perhaps because the act of *chuppah* alone — simply standing beneath it — is difficult to conceptualize as a significant legal act. What precisely is being done? *Rashi* may find it easier to frame the case if the father actively hands over his daughter, making the *chuppah* feel like something more concrete is occurring. Still, the question stands: might *chuppah* alone, as a symbol of marriage, suffice to create *kiddushin*?

The *Gemara* immediately notes: *u-lerav Huna de'amar chuppah konah* — Rav Huna in fact holds that *chuppah* does effect *kiddushin*, deriving it through a *kal vachomer*. We will encounter his position in the coming days. For Rav Huna, then, the question becomes: what does the number three exclude? To which the *Gemara* responds —

### Chalipin Excluded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַעוֹטֵי חֲלִיפִין. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא, הוֹאִיל וְגָמַר ״קִיחָה״ ״קִיחָה״ מִשְּׂדֵה עֶפְרוֹן – מָה שָׂדֶה מִקַּנְיָא בַּחֲלִיפִין, אַף אִשָּׁה נָמֵי מִקַּנְיָא בַּחֲלִיפִין, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The number serves **to exclude** acquisition through symbolic **exchange,** i.e., a *pro forma* act of acquisition effecting the transfer of ownership of an article. Although a woman can be betrothed by means of money or an item of monetary value, she is not betrothed if she is given an item by symbolic exchange. The Gemara explains why this needs to be excluded: It might **enter your mind to say** that **since** the Sages **derive** the acquisition of a woman by verbal analogy between the term expressing **taking** stated with regard to betrothal **from** the term expressing **taking** with regard to **the field of Ephron,** it can be suggested that **just as a field can be acquired through** symbolic **exchange, so too, a woman can be acquired through** symbolic **exchange.** Therefore, the *tanna* **teaches us** that this is not the case.

— *lema'utei chalipin*. It would exclude *chalipin*, exchange or barter, as a mode of *kiddushin*. The *hava amina* is serious: *salka deitach amina, ho'il u'gamar kikha kikha misdei Ephron* — since we derive *kiddushin* through a *gezeira shava* of *kicha kicha* from the acquisition of Ephron's field, we might have thought that all modes of acquisition that work for a field should work for a woman. Since *chalipin* works for acquiring a field, perhaps it should work for *kiddushin* as well. *Kamashma lan* — the *Mishnah* tells us no.

### Chalipin Perutah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא הָכִי נָמֵי! חֲלִיפִין אִיתַנְהוּ בְּפָחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, וְאִשָּׁה בְּפָחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** perhaps one should **say** that **so too,** it is possible to betroth a woman by means of symbolic exchange. The Gemara answers: This cannot be the case, as symbolic **exchange is** effective **using** an item worth **less than the value of one *peruta*, and with** an item worth **less than the value of one *peruta*, a woman**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא מַקְנְיָא נַפְשַׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **does not render herself acquired** by a man.

But the *Gemara* does not stop there. It asks *ve'eima hachi nami* — why not indeed? What is actually wrong with the *hava amina*? The *Gemara* answers: *chalipin itnehu b'fachot mishaveh perutah* — *chalipin* can be effected with an object worth less than a *perutah*. A *keli* of any value, even a toothpick, suffices. *Ve'ishah b'fachot mishaveh perutah lo mikanya nafshah* — a woman will not agree to give herself over for less than a *perutah*. *Rashi* adds: *degnai hu lah* — it is demeaning to her to be party to such a transaction.

Two distinct types of *chalipin* must be held in mind here. The first is classic barter — I give you my donkey, you give me your cow; objects are exchanged on the assumption of rough equivalence. This is in some ways more ancient and more *natural* than *kesef*, since commerce likely began as barter before abstract monetary value was established. The second type of *chalipin* — often called *kinyan* — is a formal legal act: one party takes an object (classically a handkerchief) as a symbolic gesture that expresses *gemar da'at*, the finalization of a commitment. This second type is more like a handshake than an exchange; in the context of a wedding, for example, the groom takes the rabbi's watch or handkerchief to obligate himself to the terms of the *ketubah*.

### Exchange Model Fails

For the exchange model, the inappropriateness for *kiddushin* is readily felt. To frame *kiddushin* as a swap — my pen for your daughter — thoroughly commodifies the woman. *Rashi*'s language of *degnai hu lah* maps cleanly onto this: it is demeaning because it turns *kiddushin* into barter. And this demeanor quality inheres in the transaction type itself, not merely in the value of the object. Consider: if a man were to say *harei at mekudeshet li* while handing a woman an iPhone worth several hundred dollars, that works perfectly well as *kiddushei kesef*. But if he were to say *be-chalifei iPhone zeh harei at mekudeshet li*, framing the same object as *chalipin*, it does not work. The object is identical; what changes is the framing. When the transaction is framed as *chalipin*, it is framed as exchange — we are swapping — and that framing is what makes it inappropriate. The *kesef* model, by contrast, allows the object to function symbolically: you give something of value, and that effectuates the institution, without the connotation of a commercial transaction.

The *gemar da'at* model of *chalipin* presents a harder case. Why should a formal act of commitment-expression be inappropriate for *kiddushin*? In some ways, *kiddushin* as *gemar da'at* seems conceptually apt — it expresses finality, mutual commitment, the completion of a decision. Yet even the *gemar da'at* form of *chalipin* ultimately hearkens back to the root concept of exchange and barter. Even if the handkerchief is purely symbolic, the institutional logic of *chalipin* remains that of swap. And it is that root logic — exchange, commodity, barter — that is incompatible with *kiddushin*.

### Tosafot Chalipin

*Tosafot* offers a narrower and more technically precise reading. According to *Tosafot*, the question was never whether all modes of field acquisition should work for a woman; it was specifically whether *chalipin* might be subsumed under *kesef* — that is, whether *chalipin* is simply a form of *kesef*. After all, in both cases you give something and the transaction is effected. Why not call *chalipin* a species of *kesef*?

The *Gemara*'s answer, on *Tosafot*'s reading, is that *chalipin* operates on fundamentally different parameters. *Kesef* works because of the *value* of what is given; it requires *shaveh perutah*, monetary worth. *Chalipin* works because of the *keli* — the object itself, regardless of value. Fruit, for example, would not work for *chalipin*. These are categorically distinct mechanisms. Once *chalipin* is shown to work *b'fachot mishaveh perutah* — with objects worth less than the minimum monetary unit — it is proven that *chalipin* is not a form of *kesef*. And since only *kesef*, *shtar*, and *bi'ah* effectuate *kiddushin*, *chalipin*, having been shown to be none of these, does not work.

## Woman's Freedom

### Minyana Sifa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנְיָינָא דְסֵיפָא לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי – לְמַעוֹטֵי חֲלִיצָה. סָלְקָא דַּעְתְּךָ אָמֵינָא: תֵּיתֵי בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר מִיְּבָמָה, מָה יְבָמָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ יוֹצְאָה בְּגֵט – יוֹצְאָה בַּחֲלִיצָה, זוֹ, שֶׁיּוֹצְאָה בְּגֵט – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיּוֹצְאָה בַּחֲלִיצָה? קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **The number in the latter clause** of the mishna, that a woman acquires herself in two ways, serves **to exclude what?** The Gemara answers that it serves **to exclude *ḥalitza*,** i.e., a woman is not released from marriage through *ḥalitza*. Without this exclusion it might **enter your mind to say** that this **can be derived through an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the *halakha* of **a *yevama*,** as follows: **Just as a *yevama*, who is not released** from the *yavam* **through a bill of divorce, is** nevertheless **released** from him **through *ḥalitza*,** with regard to **this** married woman, **who can be released** from her husband **through a bill of divorce, is it not logical that she can be released** from him **through *ḥalitza*?** Therefore, the *tanna* **teaches us** that a married woman cannot be released from her husband by means of *ḥalitza*.

The *Gemara* turns from the first clause to the second: *minyana de-seifa lema'utei mai* — what does the number two exclude in the clause that says a woman acquires her freedom in two ways?

### Chalitza Exclusion

The answer: it excludes *chalitza*. The *hava amina* is constructed as a *kal vachomer*: *mah yevama she-eina yotza b'get yotza b'chalitza* — a *yevama*, a widow awaiting *yibum*, cannot be released from her obligation to the *yavam* by means of a *get* prior to *yibum*, and yet *chalitza* does release her. *Zu she'yotza b'get eino din she'yotza b'chalitza* — a regular married woman, whom a *get* does free, should *kal vachomer* be freeable by *chalitza* as well. The *Gemara* answers: *amar kra, sefer keritut* — the verse says a *sefer keritut*, a written bill of severance. Only a *get* dissolves a regular marriage.

This *kal vachomer*, it must be said, is one of a series of formally constructed but intuitively strained arguments that appear throughout the opening of *Kiddushin*. The whole force of *chalitza* in the context of *yibum* has everything to do with the specific bond created by the *yibbum* obligation — it is *kacha ye'aseh la'ish asher lo yivneh et beit achiv*, a specific ceremony for a specific relationship. To suggest via *kal vachomer* that *chalitza* might therefore dissolve a regular marriage is, as a practical matter, almost absurd. And yet the *Gemara* follows the formal logic of the argument to its conclusion, requiring a *pasuk* to rule it out. This is characteristic of how the *Gemara* operates in this section: it applies the structure of *kal vachomer* with full rigor even when the conclusion seems intuitively obvious. The need for *sefer keritut* — a verse that explicitly requires a written document — closes the argument definitively.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא הָכִי נָמֵי! אָמַר קְרָא: ״סֵפֶר כְּרִיתֻת״ – סֵפֶר כּוֹרְתָהּ, וְאֵין דָּבָר אַחֵר כּוֹרְתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** perhaps one should **say** that **so too,** this is the case, i.e., a married woman can be released from marriage through *ḥalitza*. The Gemara answers: **The verse states** with regard to divorce: **"A scroll of severance"** (Deuteronomy 24:3), which teaches: **A scroll,** i.e., a written document, **severs her** from her husband, **and nothing else severs her** from him.

*Tosafot* continues the analysis by exploring further *kal vachomers* that might have been constructed and were not, asking why certain arguments were or were not raised. This mode of analysis reaches its apex in the *acharon* known as the *Atzmos Yosef*, who effectively performs *Tosafot* to the second power, raising hypotheticals upon hypotheticals. The point is not to mock the exercise but to recognize that the formal structure of *kal vachomer* as applied here is being pushed to its limits, and the limiting factor is always either a *pasuk* or an intrinsic incompatibility in the comparison.

## Kesef Source

### Menalan Kesef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּכֶסֶף. מְנָא לַן? וְתוּ, הָא דִּתְנַן: ״הָאָב זַכַּאי בְּבִתּוֹ בְּקִדּוּשֶׁיהָ, בְּכֶסֶף בִּשְׁטָר וּבְבִיאָה״, מְנָלַן דְּמִיקַּנְיָא בְּכֶסֶף, וְכֶסֶף דַּאֲבוּהּ הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that a woman can be acquired **with money.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that a woman can be acquired through money? **And furthermore,** with regard to **that which we learned** in a mishna (*Ketubot* 46b): **A father has authority** over **his daughter with regard to her betrothal,** whether it is **through money, through a document, or through sexual intercourse, from where do we** derive **that** she **is acquired** by her husband **with money, and** that this **money is her father's?**

Having established what *kiddushin* does not include, the *Gemara* now asks the foundational question: *menalan* — how do we know that *kesef* works for *kiddushin* at all? This might seem surprising given how much has already been built on this assumption, but the *Gemara* now turns to ground it properly.

### Father's Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״וְיָצְאָה חִנָּם אֵין כָּסֶף״ – אֵין כֶּסֶף לְאָדוֹן זֶה, אֲבָל יֵשׁ כֶּסֶף לְאָדוֹן אַחֵר. וּמַאן נִיהוּ – אָב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** The reason is **that the verse states** with regard to a Hebrew maidservant acquiring freedom from her master: **"Then shall she go out for nothing, without money"** (Exodus 21:11). The extraneous phrase: Without money, indicates that **there is no money for this master,** i.e., in this case the master she leaves loses the money he paid for her, **but there is money for a different master,** i.e., another master receives money for her when she leaves his authority. **And who is** the other master who can transfer her to someone else and receives money for her? This is her **father.**

The *Gemara* approaches this question through the lens of the father's rights, citing a *mishnah* in *Ketubot*: *zakai b'vito b'kiddusheha b'kesef b'shtar u-v'viah* — the father has rights over his daughter's *kiddushin* and can marry her off through *kesef*, *shtar*, or *bi'ah*. This is significant because you might have thought that only *kesef* would work when it is the father acting — since the transaction model, with money passing from the groom to the father, fits most naturally with *kesef*. The *mishnah* establishes that all three modes apply equally when the father acts on his daughter's behalf.

The emphasis on the father also carries a deeper conceptual weight. As noted above, the Torah's presentation of *kiddushin* is fundamentally the father's act — the woman in the Torah is a minor whose marriage is arranged by her father. The father model is where the *kinyan* framework is most at home: she is the object, he is the seller, the money comes to him. By beginning the derivation of *kesef* from the father's rights, the *Gemara* is in some sense returning to the foundational framework.

### Adon Zeh Adon

The actual derivation of *kesef* comes from a *pasuk* in the context of a Jewish slave-girl, the *amah haivrit*: *veyatza chinam ein kesef* — she goes free without money. The *Gemara* reads this as follows: *ein kesef le'adon zeh, aval yesh kesef le'adon acher* — there is no money going to *this* master when she leaves his control, but there is money going to *another* master. The verse cannot be referring merely to the explicit case of the slave-girl's sale — that is already stated explicitly. There must be another case in which a woman leaves the father's control and money is paid to him. That case must be *kiddushin*. Hence: when a man marries the daughter, money goes to the father.

### Father Active Agent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא לְדִידַהּ! הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! אָבִיהָ מְקַבֵּל קִידּוּשֶׁיהָ, דִּכְתִיב: ״אֶת בִּתִּי נָתַתִּי לָאִישׁ הַזֶּה״, וְאִיהִי שָׁקְלָה כַּסְפָּא?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **say** that this money is given **to her?** The Gemara rejects this: **How can** one suggest **this? Her father receives her betrothal,** i.e., the money or document of betrothal, when he marries her off to her husband, **as it is written: "I gave my daughter to this man"** (Deuteronomy 22:16), **and** shall **she take the money?** Since he is the one who marries her off, he is certainly entitled to the money of her betrothal.

But how do we know the father is the one who receives the money, rather than the daughter herself? The *Gemara* cites: *et biti natati l'ish hazeh* — "I have given my daughter to this man." This verse, from the case of *motzi shem ra*, establishes that the father is the active agent who gives over his daughter in *kiddushin*. He performs the act; he hands her over. *Rashi* and *Tosafot* both note that the verse technically speaks of a *na'arah* rather than a *ketanah*, but the *Gemara* reads it as applying to a case where he married her off when she was a minor. In any event, if the father is the one performing the act of *kiddushin* — *et biti natati* — then obviously the money placed in his hand is his to keep.

### Ketanah Na'arah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי קְטַנָּה, דְּלֵית לַהּ יָד לְקַבֵּל קִידּוּשִׁין, אֲבָל נַעֲרָה, דְּאִית לַהּ יָד לְקַבֵּל קִידּוּשִׁין – תְּקַדֵּישׁ אִיהִי נַפְשָׁהּ וְתִשְׁקוֹל כַּסְפָּא! אָמַר קְרָא: ״בִּנְעֻרֶיהָ בֵּית אָבִיהָ״ – כׇּל שֶׁבַח נְעוּרִים לְאָבִיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **say** that **this** *halakha* **applies** only when she is **a minor girl, as she does not have the power to receive** her **betrothal.** Since she lacks the intellectual capacity, she likewise does not have the legal right to conduct this transaction. **But** with regard to **a young woman, who does have the power to receive** her **betrothal,** as a female older than twelve years is considered an adult, **let her betroth herself and take the money.** The Gemara answers that **the verse states: "Being in her youth, in her father's house"** (Numbers 30:17), which teaches: **Any profit** she gains in her **youth** belongs **to her father.**

The *Gemara* then introduces a complication. One might have argued: all of the above — that the father acts, that the money goes to him — applies clearly when she is a *ketanah*, a minor, because as a minor she has no legal personality of her own and no capacity to receive *kiddushin* herself. But what about when she is a *na'arah* — between twelve and twelve and a half, the stage at which she has attained some degree of her own personhood in *halacha*? At that stage she does have some capacity to accept *kiddushin* on her own. Perhaps, then, when she is a *na'arah*, she could accept the *kesef* herself and retain it: *tikadesh hi nafshah v'tishkol kasba* — she could betroth herself and pocket the money.

This question — how do we know the father retains his rights and his claim to the money when she is a *na'arah* — is where the *Gemara* leaves us. The status of the *na'arah* is the middle ground: not the *ketanah* over whom the father has total control, and not the *bogeret*, the adult woman twelve and a half and older who is entirely beyond the father's control. In the *na'arah* stage, *halacha* recognizes her as possessing *yad* — a hand, an agency — even while the father also retains certain rights. How those rights are defined, and how we know the father is entitled to the *kiddushin* money even when she has some independent standing, is the question that will open tomorrow's discussion.
