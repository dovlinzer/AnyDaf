# Sanhedrin 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Character & Integrity

### Intro & Tisha B'Av Review

We begin today in the *Bavli* on daf 18, at the opening of the second *perek*. By way of introduction, it is worth noting that a pre-recorded shiur on Sanhedrin for Tisha B'Av has been posted, covering material from both the *Bavli* and the *Yerushalmi* on the qualifications for membership on the *Sanhedrin gedolah*. Two points from that recording bear mention here.

The first concerns a requirement discussed in the *Bavli*: to serve on the *Sanhedrin*, one must be fluent in multiple languages so as to hear the testimony of witnesses and litigants directly, without relying on an interpreter. The concern is not merely about conscious misrepresentation but about the subtle loss of nuance and emphasis that even a well-intentioned translation inevitably introduces. The second point, equally famous, is that a judge cannot sit on a *beit din* unless he is capable of constructing an argument that a *sheretz* — an otherwise quintessentially impure creeping creature — is *tahor*. *Tosafot* famously asks: *mah lanu ledrashot shel hevel* — what is the value of such demonstrably hollow argumentation? What does it prove?

### Integrity > Intellect

The capacity to demonstrate intellectual virtuosity — to prove a counterfactual conclusion through sheer command of texts and logic — is indeed part of what this requirement tests. But the deeper question it raises is precisely that intellectual prowess alone is not sufficient, and is perhaps even dangerous. If a person is clever enough and knows the sources well enough, he can reach virtually any conclusion he sets out to reach. The ability to win an argument does not establish the integrity of a decision.

What is truly striking, when one examines the criteria for who may serve on the *Sanhedrin* in both the *Bavli* and the *Yerushalmi*, is that it is not intellectual ability or breadth of knowledge that leaps out at you — it is *character*. Humility, probity, *yiras shamayim*, moral integrity: these are the qualities that dominate the lists of qualifications. This remains relevant to the question of how one evaluates *poskim* today. In theory, one could construct a halakhic argument leading to virtually any conclusion. The degree to which we can trust a ruling is bound up with our confidence in the decisor's integrity and fidelity — not merely in his erudition.

### Torah Models

The second noteworthy point from the Tisha B'Av recording concerns a famous and lengthy *Tosefta* discussed in the *Yerushalmi*, which describes an era when there was never any unresolved debate. Whenever a *beit din* did not know the answer to a question, it would refer the matter to the next higher court — and so on, up through the hierarchy, until the question reached the *Sanhedrin*. The *Tosefta* describes each level of the process with the same formula: *im shamu amru lahem* — if they had a tradition, they transmitted it; *ve-im lo shamu* — if they did not have a tradition, they referred the matter upward. At the level of the *Sanhedrin* itself: *im shamu amru lahem, ve-im lo shamru amdu leminyan* — if they had a tradition they transmitted it, and if they had no tradition, they took a vote. What is conspicuously absent at every stage is any notion that a court, lacking a received tradition, would turn to its own creative analysis to work out the answer.

This reflects a particular model of *Torah she-ba'al peh*: it is entirely transmission. Debate is not a sign of intellectual vitality but of uncertainty — a failure in the chain of tradition. The *Tosefta* praises the era when there was never any debate precisely because it meant the tradition was intact and the *halacha* was always known.

This stands in sharp contrast to what might be called the *Hillel* or the *Rabbi Akiva* model of *Torah she-ba'al peh*, in which any tradition and any text is inherently limited and cannot be expected to address every future question. In that model, debate is not a symptom of failure but a natural and indeed positive feature of a living tradition — a space for human intellect, creativity, and development to operate. These two visions of the nature of *Torah she-ba'al peh* are worth holding in mind as we proceed, for we will encounter the same *Tosefta* discussed at length in the *Bavli* as well.

## Kohen Gadol Status

### Perek 2 Opening

The second *perek* opens with the topic of the *kohen gadol* and the *melech*. One might note that this represents a degree of digression from the organizational logic of the first *perek*, which was organized around the question of court sizes. But it is not really such a great digression, since the underlying question is one of fundamental importance: is anyone above the law, and who receives special treatment within the legal system? It is worth noting, however, that even within the *Mishnah* of the first *perek* there were puzzling digressions — including a discussion of *ir nidachat* that is difficult to account for. The digressions here, as we will see, are more understandable.

### Kohen Gadol Judged

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל דָּן וְדָנִין אוֹתוֹ, מֵעִיד וּמְעִידִין אוֹתוֹ, חוֹלֵץ וְחוֹלְצִין לְאִשְׁתּוֹ, וּמְיַיבְּמִין אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ. אֲבָל הוּא אֵינוֹ מְיַיבֵּם, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא אָסוּר בָּאַלְמָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The **High Priest judges** others if he is sufficiently wise, **and** others **judge him** when he transgresses. He **testifies** before the court **and** others **testify concerning him. He performs *ḥalitza*** with his brother's widow **and** his brother **performs *ḥalitza* with his wife; and** his brother **consummates levirate marriage with his wife. But he does not consummate levirate marriage** with his brother's widow, **because it is prohibited for him to** marry **a widow** (see Leviticus 21:14), and can therefore never fulfill the mitzva of levirate marriage, as a *yevama* is by definition a widow.

The *Mishnah* opens: *kohen gadol dan vedanin oto* — a *kohen gadol* may serve as a judge, and he himself may be judged. As we saw in the previous *perek*, when he is judged, it is before a *beit din* of seventy-one — *kol hadavar hagadol yavi'u eilecha*, the cases involving great personages are brought before the highest court. Nevertheless, he is judged. He is not above the law.

### Testimony Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵעִיד וּמְעִידִין אוֹתוֹ

This clause appears as part of the same Mishnah quoted above.

*Me'id ume'idin oto* — he may testify, and others may testify against him. This is perhaps obvious: if he can be brought to court and judged, it follows that testimony must be admissible against him.

### Family Law

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹלֵץ וְחוֹלְצִין לְאִשְׁתּוֹ, וּמְיַיבְּמִין אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ. אֲבָל הוּא אֵינוֹ מְיַיבֵּם, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא אָסוּר בָּאַלְמָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **He performs *ḥalitza*** with his brother's widow **and** his brother **performs *ḥalitza* with his wife; and** his brother **consummates levirate marriage with his wife. But he does not consummate levirate marriage** with his brother's widow, **because it is prohibited for him to** marry **a widow** (see Leviticus 21:14), and can therefore never fulfill the mitzva of levirate marriage, as a *yevama* is by definition a widow.

The *Mishnah* continues: *choletz vecholtzin le'ishto* — he may perform *chalitzah*, and even though that involves his sister-in-law spitting at him, this applies. And if he dies, his wife may undergo *chalitzah* with his brother. *Umeyabmim et ishto* — if he dies, his brother may perform *yibum* with his wife. *Aval hu eino meyabem* — but he himself cannot perform *yibum* with his brother's widow, *mipnei shehu asur be'almanah* — because a *kohen gadol* is forbidden to marry a widow.

This immediately raises a question: if he is categorically forbidden from *yibum*, why is he obligated in *chalitzah* at all? He should seemingly be excluded from the entire institution. The answer relates to the status of his brother, who is a *kohen* but not a *kohen gadol*. A standard *kohen* is forbidden to marry a *gerushah* but is permitted to marry an *almanah*. It is only the *kohen gadol* who is uniquely forbidden to marry a widow — contrary to what a surface reading of *Yechezkel* might suggest, since *Yechezkel* implies that *kohanim* generally cannot marry a widow. But that is one of those places where the *Yerushalmi* notes that *Yechezkel* appears to contradict received *halacha*. The operative *halacha* is that an ordinary *kohen* may marry an *almanah* but not a *gerushah*. So his brother, being a regular *kohen*, is eligible for *yibum*, and because *yibum* is available through the brother, *chalitzah* remains relevant.

### Mourning Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵת לוֹ מֵת, אֵינוֹ יוֹצֵא אַחַר הַמִּטָּה, אֶלָּא הֵן נִכְסִין וְהוּא נִגְלֶה, הֵן נִגְלִין וְהוּא נִכְסֶה, וְיוֹצֵא עִמָּהֶן עַד פֶּתַח שַׁעַר הָעִיר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** If a relative **of** the High Priest **dies, he does not follow the bier** carrying the corpse, since it is prohibited for the High Priest to become ritually impure even for immediate relatives (see Leviticus 21:11). **Rather,** once the members of the funeral procession **are concealed** from sight by turning onto another street, **he is revealed** on the street they departed, and when **they are revealed,** then **he is concealed, and** in this way, **he goes out with them until the entrance of the gate of the city,** from where they would take out the corpse, since the dead were not buried in Jerusalem. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

*Mes lo mes* — if an immediate relative of the *kohen gadol* dies: *eino yotzei achar hamitah* — he does not directly follow the coffin. Rather, *ela hen nichnasim vehu nigleh, hen niglin vehu nichseh* — as the procession turns from one street onto another, he appears on the street they have just left; when they appear on a new street, he withdraws from it. He is always at a distance, never on the same street as the coffin. *Veyotzei imahen ad petach sha'ar ha'ir* — in this manner he accompanies the procession, maintaining this careful distance, until it reaches the city gate, since the burial takes place outside the city. This is the view of *Rabbi Meir*.

The reason for this protocol is that even though an ordinary *kohen* is permitted — and indeed has a *mitzvah* to become *tamei* for his immediate relatives — a *kohen gadol* is not. The verse in *Emor* states explicitly: *ve'al kol nafshot met lo yavo, le'aviv ule'imo lo yitama* — he may not become *tamei* even for his father or his mother. Similarly, the *nazir* shares this same restriction. The *kohen gadol* must therefore keep his distance to avoid accidentally incurring *tumah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ יוֹצֵא מִן הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמִן הַמִּקְדָּשׁ לֹא יֵצֵא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: He does not emerge from the Temple** at all for the burial of his relatives, **as it is stated: "And from the Temple he shall not emerge** and will not desecrate the Temple of his God; for the separateness of the oil of the anointment of his God is on him" (Leviticus 21:12).

*Rabbi Yehuda* takes a stricter and more literal position: *eino yotzei min hamikdash* — he does not leave the *Beit HaMikdash* at all, based on a literal reading of the verse *u'min hamikdash lo yetzei*. According to *Rabbi Yehuda*, the *kohen gadol* continues performing the *avodah* even while his relative is being buried. *Rabbi Meir* reads the verse differently — not as a prohibition on physically leaving the *Beit HaMikdash*, but as a prohibition on compromising his *kedushah* — *min hakedusha lo yetzei* — and therefore allows him to leave, provided he avoids *tumah*.

### Consolation Rituals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְשֶׁהוּא מְנַחֵם אֲחֵרִים, דֶּרֶךְ כׇּל הָעָם עוֹבְרִין בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה, וְהַמְמוּנֶּה מְמַצְּעוֹ בֵּינוֹ לְבֵין הָעָם. וּכְשֶׁהוּא מִתְנַחֵם מֵאֲחֵרִים, כׇּל הָעָם אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: אֲנַחְנוּ כַּפָּרָתְךָ, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר לָהֶן: תִּתְבָּרְכוּ מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם. וּכְשֶׁמַּבְרִין אוֹתוֹ, כׇּל הָעָם מְסוּבִּין עַל הָאָרֶץ, וְהוּא מֵיסֵב עַל הַסַּפְסָל.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **And when he consoles others** in their mourning when they return from burial, the **way of all the people** is that **they pass by one after another** and the mourners stand in a line and are consoled, **and the appointed** person **stands in the middle, between** the High Priest **and the people. And when he is consoled by others** in his mourning, **all the people say to him: We are your atonement. And he says to them: May you be blessed from Heaven. And when they comfort him with the** first **meal** after the burial of one of his relatives, **all the people recline on the ground** as if they are taking his mourning on themselves, **and he reclines on the bench** out of respect for his status as High Priest.

The *Mishnah* then describes the protocols that apply to the *kohen gadol* in the context of consolation — both when he is consoling others and when others are consoling him.

When he consoles others: *derech kol ha'am ovrim ze achar ze* — all the people pass before him in a line, and *vehamemuna memutza beino uvein ha'am* — the *memuna*, his deputy, stands as a kind of buffer between him and the passing mourners. *Rashi* explains that this means the *memuna* stands to his right while the people pass on his left.

When he himself is the mourner receiving consolers: *kol ha'am omrim lo anu kaparat'cha* — all the people say to him, "We are your atonement," meaning: let whatever bad that might befall you befall us instead. This formula reflects a theological assumption that a death represents the need for atonement — whether for the sins of the deceased, or because the loss itself is a sign that something must be spiritually rectified. One encounters a similar formula in the practice of saying *hareini kaparat mishkavo* upon the death of a relative. Here it is the community that takes upon itself, as it were, to serve in the *kohen gadol*'s stead.

The *kohen gadol* responds: *titvarchu min hashamayim* — may you be blessed from heaven.

When the *kohen gadol* is served the *havra'ah* — the meal given to a mourner following the burial: *kol ha'am mesubin al ha'aretz vehu meisev al hasafsal* — all the people sit on the ground as mourners would, while he sits on a bench. This is a kind of reversal of the usual practice, in which the mourner sits below and the consolers sit above. Here, out of honor for the *kohen gadol* and as an expression of *anu kaparat'cha*, the community takes on the posture of mourning while he is elevated above them.

## King's Exemption

### King Not Judged

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמֶּלֶךְ לֹא דָּן, וְלֹא דָּנִין אוֹתוֹ, לֹא מֵעִיד וְלֹא מְעִידִין אוֹתוֹ, לֹא חוֹלֵץ וְלֹא חוֹלְצִין לְאִשְׁתּוֹ, לֹא מְיַיבֵּם וְלֹא מְיַיבְּמִין לְאִשְׁתּוֹ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִם רָצָה לַחֲלוֹץ אוֹ לְיַיבֵּם – זָכוּר לַטּוֹב. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues, enumerating the *halakhot* pertaining to the king in similar matters: **The king does not judge** others as a member of a court **and** others **do not judge him, he does not testify and** others **do not testify concerning him, he does not perform *ḥalitza*** with his brother's widow **and** his brother **does not perform *ḥalitza* with his wife,** and **he does not consummate levirate marriage** with his brother's widow **and** his brother **does not consummate levirate marriage with his wife,** as all these actions are not fitting to the honor of his office. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** These are not restrictions, but his prerogative: **If he desired to perform *ḥalitza* or to consummate levirate marriage,** he is **remembered for good,** as this is to the benefit of his brother's widow. The Sages **said to him: They do not listen to him** if he desires to do so, as this affects not only his own honor but that of the kingdom.

In stark contrast to the *kohen gadol*, the *Mishnah* states: *hamelech lo dan velo danin oto* — the king does not judge, and he is not judged. It should be noted that in an earlier discussion we mentioned that the phrase *kol hadavar hagadol yavi'u eilecha* extends a *beit din* of seventy-one to the heads of tribes — and at that point it was perhaps suggested, implicitly, that a king might be included. But this *Mishnah* makes clear that a king stands outside the judicial system entirely. This is, in some respects, troubling. The *Torah* explicitly requires the king to write a *Sefer Torah* and carry it with him — *velo yasur levavo mei'achav* — to constantly reinforce that he is not above his fellows. He is clearly bound by *Torah* law. Yet here he is excluded from the court system.

The *Gemara* will resolve this by explaining that this exclusion applies specifically to *malchei Yisrael* — the kings of the northern kingdom, who were not seen as consistently submissive to *Torah* authority. Bringing such kings to court would only cause harm to the judicial system; the confrontation would not end well. But the *Gemara* will go on to say that this does not apply to *malchei Yehuda*. So the earlier suggestion was not entirely wrong. But from the *Mishnah*'s plain presentation, the king is excluded both from judging and from being judged.

There is something worth noting here: the exclusion cuts both ways. If the king cannot be judged, he also cannot judge. He is not operating a parallel system in which he enjoys immunity while wielding authority over others. He is simply outside the system altogether. This is at least partially analogous to certain modern conventions — such as the question of whether a sitting head of state can be subpoenaed as a witness in a civil dispute, a matter that courts have engaged with in various ways. The underlying tension between sovereign authority and judicial accountability is not unique to the *halacha*.

### King Restrictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא דָּן, וְלֹא דָּנִין אוֹתוֹ, לֹא מֵעִיד וְלֹא מְעִידִין אוֹתוֹ, לֹא חוֹלֵץ וְלֹא חוֹלְצִין לְאִשְׁתּוֹ, לֹא מְיַיבֵּם וְלֹא מְיַיבְּמִין לְאִשְׁתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** This appears as part of the same Mishnah passage quoted above.

*Lo dan velo danin oso, lo me'id velo me'idin oso* — the king does not testify and you cannot testify against him. *Lo choletz velo choltzin le'ishto* — he does not perform *chalitzah*, which would require his sister-in-law to spit at him, and if he dies, *chalitzah* is not performed on his wife. *Lo meyabem velo meyabemin le'ishto* — he does not perform *yibum* with his brother's widow, and if he dies, *yibum* is not performed with his wife.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין נוֹשְׂאִין אַלְמְנָתוֹ, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: נוֹשֵׂא הַמֶּלֶךְ אַלְמְנָתוֹ שֶׁל מֶלֶךְ, שֶׁכֵּן מָצִינוּ בְּדָוִד שֶׁנָּשָׂא אַלְמְנָתוֹ שֶׁל שָׁאוּל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וָאֶתְּנָה לְךָ אֶת בֵּית אֲדֹנֶיךָ וְאֶת נְשֵׁי אֲדֹנֶיךָ בְּחֵיקֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And no one may marry** a king's **widow,** due to his honor. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** Another **king may marry the widow of a king, as we found that** King **David married the widow of** King **Saul, as it is stated: "And I have given you the house of your master and the wives of your master in your bosom"** (II Samuel 12:8).

*Rabbi Yehuda* says: *im ratza lachlotzo leyabem zachur letov* — if he voluntarily wishes to perform *chalitzah* or *yibum*, he will be remembered favorably. This implies, according to *Rabbi Yehuda*, that the restriction is merely that we do not compel him, but that voluntary participation is praiseworthy. The *tanna kamma*, however, holds that we do not permit it even if he wishes to waive his honor: *shemachal al kevodo, ein kevodo machul* — even when he waives his own honor, his honor as king is not his to waive, for it represents the dignity of the entire nation. The troubling implication of the *tanna kamma*'s view, of course, is that his brother's widow would remain an *agunah*.

### Royal Marriage

*Ve'ein nosin almanaso* — one may not marry the king's widow. This reflects a reality familiar from *Tanach*, where a king's widows are treated as off-limits to others. *Rabbi Yehuda* adds: *nosei hamelech almanaso shel melech* — a king may marry the widow of a previous king, since we find that David married Saul's widow, as the verse states: *va'etna lecha et beit adonecha ve'et neshei adonecha becheikecha*.

## Talmudic Analysis

### Self-Judgment First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל דָּן. פְּשִׁיטָא! דָּנִין אוֹתוֹ אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ. הָא נָמֵי פְּשִׁיטָא: אִי לָא דָּיְינִינַן לֵיהּ, אִיהוּ הֵיכִי דָּיֵין? וְהָכְתִיב ״הִתְקוֹשְׁשׁוּ וָקוֹשּׁוּ״, וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: קְשֹׁט עַצְמְךָ וְאַחַר כָּךְ קְשֹׁט אֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that the **High Priest judges** others as a member of a court. The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious?** Why would one think that he would be unfit to serve as a judge? The Gemara answers: **It was necessary for** the mishna to mention the latter clause: And others **judge him,** and therefore, it taught the related *halakha* with it. The Gemara objects: **This** is **also obvious; if** others **do not judge him, how can he judge** others? **But isn't it written: "Gather yourselves together, yea, gather together [*hitkosheshu vakoshu*]"** (Zephaniah 2:1); **and Reish Lakish says:** This verse teaches a moral principle: **Adorn [*kashet*] yourself** first, **and then adorn others,** i.e., one who is not subject to judgment may not judge others.

The *Gemara* opens its analysis with what appears to be an obvious question: *kohen gadol dan* — of course a *kohen gadol* can serve as a judge. Why would we think otherwise? Perhaps, one might suggest, because the next *Mishnah* will exclude the king from judging, and we might have assumed the same of the *kohen gadol* by analogy. But then comes the real question: *vedanin oto* — that he himself may be judged — is that not equally obvious? Why does the *Mishnah* need to state it?

The *Gemara* answers: *ei lo dayninan lei, ei ihu heichi dayan* — if we would not allow him to be judged, how could he himself judge others? The verse states *hitkosheshu vakoshu*, which *Resh Lakish* expounds by reading *hitkosheshu* through the root *kshot* — to adorn or perfect. The *Gemara* renders: *kshot atzmecha, ve'achar kach kshot acherim* — first perfect yourself, and only then set about perfecting others. The ethical principle embedded here is a powerful one: you cannot stand in judgment over others unless you yourself are open to being judged. One must first be subject to the law before exercising authority within it. As the *kohen gadol* must atone for himself before atoning for the nation on *Yom Kippur*, so too a judge must be open to judgment before he can legitimately judge.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אַיְּידֵי דְּקָא בָּעֵי לְמִיתְנֵי מֶלֶךְ לֹא דָּן וְלֹא דָּנִין אוֹתוֹ, תְּנָא נָמֵי כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל דָּן וְדָנִין. וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא, הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן כִּדְתַנְיָא: כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל שֶׁהָרַג אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ בְּמֵזִיד – נֶהֱרָג, בְּשׁוֹגֵג – גּוֹלֶה, וְעוֹבֵר עַל עֲשֵׂה וְעַל לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה, וַהֲרֵי הוּא כְּהֶדְיוֹט לְכׇל דְּבָרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** neither *halakha* concerning the High Priest is a novelty. But **since** the *tanna* **wants to teach** that the **king does not judge** others **and** others **do not judge him, he also taught** that the **High Priest judges** others **and** others **judge him. And if you wish, say** instead that **this teaches us** the *halakha* **as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 4:1): **A High Priest who killed a person intentionally is killed;** if he killed **unintentionally, he is exiled** to a city of refuge, **and he transgresses a positive mitzva and a prohibition, and he is like an ordinary** person **concerning all of his** *halakhot*.

Since the *Gemara* establishes that the *kohen gadol*'s being judged is logically entailed by his being a judge, it adds: *ella aidi dikba'ei lemitni melech lo dan velo danin oto* — the reason both sides are stated explicitly (*dan vedanin oto*) is that since the *Mishnah* must state both elements by the king in the negative, it states both explicitly by the *kohen gadol* as well, for symmetry.

Alternatively, the *Gemara* offers a further reason: *kedetanya* — as taught in a *baraita*: *kohen gadol sheharag et hanefesh, bemezid neherag, beshogeg goleh* — a *kohen gadol* who kills someone is executed if he acted intentionally, and is sent into exile if he did so unintentionally. *Ve'over al asei ve'al lo ta'aseh, vaharei hu kehedyot lechol devarav* — and he transgresses a positive and a negative commandment, and he is treated as a commoner in all other matters.

### Murder Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמֵזִיד נֶהֱרָג – פְּשִׁיטָא! בְּשׁוֹגֵג גּוֹלֶה אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*: A High Priest who killed another **intentionally is killed.** Isn't that **obvious?** Why would he be punished differently than other murderers? **It was necessary for it** to mention the latter clause: If he killed **unintentionally, he is exiled,** so it taught the related case with it.

The *Gemara* proceeds to analyze each clause. That he is executed for intentional murder — *pshita*, that is obvious, once we have established that he can be judged. The *chiddush* is specifically that *beshogeg goleh* — he is exiled for unintentional killing. Why might we have thought otherwise?

### Permanent Exile

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא נָמֵי פְּשִׁיטָא! אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּכְתִיב ״וְיָשַׁב בָּהּ עַד מוֹת הַכֹּהֵן הַגָּדוֹל״, אֵימָא: כׇּל דְּאִית לֵיהּ תַּקַּנְתָּא בַּחֲזָרָה – לִיגְלֵי, דְּלֵית לֵיהּ תַּקַּנְתָּא בַּחֲזָרָה – לָא לִיגְלֵי, דִּתְנַן: הַהוֹרֵג כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, אוֹ כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל שֶׁהָרַג אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ – אֵינוֹ יוֹצֵא מִשָּׁם לְעוֹלָם; אֵימָא לָא לִיגְלֵי – קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. וְאֵימָא הָכִי נָמֵי? אָמַר קְרָא ״לָנוּס שָׁמָּה כׇּל רֹצֵחַ״ – אֲפִילּוּ כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל בַּמַּשְׁמָע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **This** is **also obvious.** The Gemara responds: **It was necessary** for the *baraita* to state the latter clause, lest it **enter your mind to say: Since it is written** about one who kills unintentionally and is exiled to a city of refuge: **"And he should dwell in it until the death of the High Priest"** (Numbers 35:25), **say: Anyone for whom there is a rectification by returning** from the city of refuge **is exiled,** but one **for whom there is no rectification by returning is not exiled.** The High Priest has no such rectification, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Makkot* 11b): **one who kills a High Priest or a High Priest who killed a person,** he **never departs from** the city of refuge. Therefore, **I will say** that a High Priest **is not exiled** at all. For that reason, the *baraita* above **teaches us** that he is exiled. The Gemara suggests: Why not **say that** the *halakha* is **indeed** that the High Priest would not be exiled, since he would not be able to return home? The Gemara responds: The **verse states** concerning the city of refuge: **"That every manslayer may flee there"** (Deuteronomy 19:3), and **even a High Priest is indicated** by the inclusive term "every."

*Salka datach amina*: I might have thought that since the release from exile is conditioned on the death of the *kohen gadol* — *veyashav bah ad mot hakohen hagadol* — and since this *kohen gadol* is himself the *kohen gadol*, there is no death of a *kohen gadol* that will ever free him. And therefore, *kol de'it lei takanta bachazara lighlot* — exile applies only to those for whom there is a mechanism of release; *vechol deleit lei takanta bachazara lo lighlot* — those for whom there is no mechanism of release should not be exiled at all.

This brings up a remarkable question: can a *kohen gadol* continue to serve in that capacity even from exile, after having committed an unintentional killing? It sounds very much from the *Gemara* here as if the answer is yes — that he would remain *kohen gadol*, and that this is precisely the source of the problem, since his own death would be required to free himself. 

The *Mishnah* at the beginning of daf 18b states: *ha-horeg kohen gadol o kohen gadol she-harag et hanefesh, eino yotzei misham le-olam* — both one who kills a *kohen gadol* and a *kohen gadol* who kills someone are never released. *Rashi* explains the two cases somewhat differently. In the first case — one who kills the *kohen gadol* — since at the time of exile there was no *kohen gadol* in office (the position had just been vacated by the very killing), there is no one whose death can anchor the exilee's release. Even if a new *kohen gadol* is subsequently appointed, *Rashi* maintains that it is the *kohen gadol* in office *at the time of exile* that determines release — and that position was empty. In the second case — the *kohen gadol* who commits the killing — the problem is simply that it is his own death we would be talking about.

*Tosafot* refines the first case: *eino yotzei misham le-olam* applies specifically when no replacement *kohen gadol* was appointed before the judgment was concluded. Had a new *kohen gadol* been installed before the exilee's sentence was finalized, that *kohen gadol*'s eventual death would serve as the release mechanism. *Tosafot* further notes that in addition to a sitting *kohen gadol*, a *mashuach she-avar* — a former *kohen gadol* who had vacated the position — or a *mashuach milchama* can also function as the relevant figure, meaning there may be other quasi-equivalents whose death could free the exilee.

In the case of the *kohen gadol* who himself commits the killing, the *Rashi* is somewhat ambiguous: *bemisas mi yashuv? Halekha Kohen Gadol* — by whose death would he return? He is himself the *kohen gadol*. This could be read either as meaning that he remains *kohen gadol* throughout his exile and hence there is literally no one else's death that could free him, or that he has effectively ceased functioning as *kohen gadol* but no replacement has yet been appointed. The question of whether a *kohen gadol* who commits an unintentional killing would be removed from office or retained is not definitively resolved here, though the plain sense of the *Gemara* is that he remains *kohen gadol*.

The *Gemara* then asks: could we simply say he should not be exiled at all? *Amar kra: venas shama kol rotzeiach* — the verse says "every murderer shall flee there," and *afilu kohen gadol bemashma* — even a *kohen gadol* is included in "every."

### Non-Capital Crimes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹבֵר עַל עֲשֵׂה וְעַל לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה. לָא סַגִּי דְּלָא עָבַר? הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִם עָבַר עַל עֲשֵׂה וְעַל לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה, הֲרֵי הוּא כְּהֶדְיוֹט לְכׇל דְּבָרָיו. פְּשִׁיטָא! סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא, הוֹאִיל וּתְנַן: אֵין דָּנִים לֹא אֶת הַשֵּׁבֶט וְלֹא אֶת נְבִיא הַשֶּׁקֶר וְלֹא אֶת כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל אֶלָּא עַל פִּי בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל שִׁבְעִים וְאֶחָד, וְאָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: ״כׇּל הַדָּבָר הַגָּדוֹל יָבִיאוּ אֵלֶיךָ״ – דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל גָּדוֹל, אֵימָא כׇּל דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל גָּדוֹל, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches that a High Priest **transgresses a positive mitzva and a prohibition.** The Gemara understands the *baraita* as an independent statement asserting that the High Priest must transgress a positive mitzva and a prohibition, and asks: Is it **not possible that he doesn't transgress** a positive mitzva and prohibition; i.e., must the High Priest transgress a positive mitzva and a prohibition? The Gemara responds: **This is what the *baraita* is saying:** This is not an independent statement asserting that the High Priest must transgress a positive mitzva and a prohibition; rather, the *baraita* teaches that **if he transgressed a positive mitzva and a prohibition,** then **he is** considered to be **like an ordinary** person **concerning all of his** *halakhot*, judged just as anyone else for common transgressions. Isn't that **obvious?** The Gemara responds: No, as it could **enter your mind to say** that **since we learned** in the mishna (2a): The court **judges** cases involving an entire **tribe** that sinned, **or a false prophet, or a High Priest, only on the basis of a court of seventy-one** judges, i.e., the Great Sanhedrin, **and** since **Rav Adda bar Ahava says** that the phrase **"every great matter they shall bring unto you"** (Exodus 18:22), refers to the **matters of a great** person, the High Priest, who is judged in a court of seventy-one, therefore, one might **say** that this means **all the matters of a great** person, including non-capital transgressions, are judged in a court of seventy-one. Therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us** that for non-capital transgressions, he is treated like an ordinary person, and judged even by a court of three.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא הָכִי נָמֵי: מִי כְּתִיב דִּבְרֵי גָדוֹל? ״הַדָּבָר הַגָּדוֹל״ כְּתִיב, דָּבָר גָּדוֹל מַמָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **But** why not **say** that **indeed,** the *halakha* is that all cases involving the High Priest must be adjudicated in a court of seventy-one? The Gemara rejects this. **Is it written: The matters of a great person?** "Every **great matter" is written,** meaning **actually a great matter,** one involving a capital transgression, not only one related to a great person.

*Baraita* concludes: *ve'over al asei ve'al lo ta'aseh, vaharei hu kehedyot lechol devarav* — he is treated as a commoner in all his affairs. The *Gemara* asks: is that not obvious? But no — one might have thought otherwise, given the rule established in the first *perek* that *ein danin lo et hashevet velo et navi hasheker velo et kohen gadol ela al pi beit din shel shivim ve'echad* — only a *beit din* of seventy-one may judge the *kohen gadol*. And *Rabbah bar bar Chana* derived this from *kol hadavar hagadol yavi'u eilecha* — all matters relating to this great personage come before the highest court. One might therefore have thought that even monetary disputes or cases of *malkus* require a *beit din* of seventy-one for the *kohen gadol*.

The *Mishnah* therefore teaches that for anything short of a capital crime, he is treated entirely as a commoner and receives a standard *beit din* of three. The textual basis for limiting the elevated *beit din* to capital matters is the phrase *hadavar hagadol* — the *great* matter — which implies not merely matters *relating to* a great person, but matters that are themselves of great import. A non-capital case, even involving the *kohen gadol*, does not meet that threshold.

It is worth noting that the *Yerushalmi* actually takes a different view: it holds that for non-capital crimes, the *kohen gadol* is given a *beit din* of twenty-three rather than three. The *Yerushalmi* thus maintains an intermediate position — he is not treated entirely as a commoner in non-capital matters, even if he does not receive the full *beit din* of seventy-one.

## Testimony Exceptions

### Lost Property Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵעִיד וּמְעִידִין אוֹתוֹ. מֵעִיד? וְהָתַנְיָא: ״וְהִתְעַלַּמְתָּ״ – פְּעָמִים שֶׁאַתָּה מִתְעַלֵּם, וּפְעָמִים שֶׁאִי אַתָּה מִתְעַלֵּם. הָא כֵּיצַד? כֹּהֵן וְהוּא בְּבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת, זָקֵן וְאֵינָהּ לְפִי כְּבוֹדוֹ, אוֹ שֶׁהָיְתָה מְלָאכָה שֶׁלּוֹ מְרוּבָּה מִשֶּׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – לְכָךְ נֶאֱמַר ״וְהִתְעַלַּמְתָּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that the High Priest **testifies** before the court **and** others **testify concerning him.** The Gemara expresses surprise: **He testifies? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that in the verse: "You shall not see your brother's ox or his sheep wandering and ignore them; you shall return them to your brother" (Deuteronomy 22:1), the use of the unusual term **"and ignore** them," as opposed to the more direct: Do not ignore them, indicates that there are **times when you ignore** lost items **and** there are **times when you do not ignore** them. **How so?** If the one who found the item was **a priest, and** the lost item **is in the cemetery,** where priests are prohibited from entering; or if he was **an elderly person and it is not** in keeping **with his dignity** to tend to a lost item of that kind; **or** if **his work,** which he would need to suspend in order to tend to and return the item, **is** of **greater** value **than** the lost item **of the other,** one might think that he must nonetheless return it. **Therefore,** with regard to those cases, **it is stated: "And ignore** them." Since a distinguished individual may ignore a mitzva that is incumbent on others if it is not in keeping with his dignity, perhaps the High Priest would be allowed to ignore the obligation to testify, due to his honor.

The *Mishnah* states that a *kohen gadol* may testify, *me'id ume'idin oso*. But the *Gemara* raises a challenge from a *baraita*: *vehisalamta meihem* — the verse says "and you shall hide from them," implying that there are times when one is actually supposed to look away, to passively not fulfill certain positive *mitzvot*, when fulfilling them would compromise one's dignity. The specific model is the *mitzvah* of returning a lost object. The verse states *lo tireh et shor achicha o et seyo nidachim vehisalamta meihem* — do not see your brother's ox or sheep going astray and hide from it. The Rabbis, in their characteristically precise reading, focus on *vehisalamta meihem*: there are times when you should hide from it. When? When it would not be befitting your dignity — when stooping to recover and return the object would represent a genuine compromise of a person's station.

This principle of *kevod habriyot* — human dignity — is capable of overriding rabbinic enactments and even, in the case of the passive non-performance of a positive *mitzvah*, potentially biblical obligations. Three cases are enumerated in the *baraita* for when one may be exempt from returning a lost object: a *kohen* who would have to enter a cemetery to retrieve it; an elder for whom the physical act of retrieving it would not be befitting his dignity (*zaken ve'eino lefi kevodo*); and a case where the monetary value of the time one would lose from one's own work exceeds the value of the object (*melachto merubah mishel chaveiro*).

This last case reflects a broader and critically important principle in *bein adam lachaveiro*: *chayecha vechayei achicha chayecha kodmim* — your life and wellbeing take precedence over your fellow's. This is not merely about life-or-death situations; it applies to economic interests as well. My primary obligation is to my own needs before those of my fellow. Why should protecting your property come at a greater cost to me than the property is worth to you? If there are two lost objects and I can only retrieve one, I am permitted to prioritize my own. This principle has significant implications for domains well beyond *aveida* — including, crucially, questions about *kibbud av va'em* in the context of difficult or harmful parental relationships. The genuine exemption in such cases is not derived from characterizing the parent as a *rasha* — a categorization that has traditionally been applied only to those who reject *Torah* observance, not simply those who behave cruelly — but from the fundamental principle that a person's own psychic and emotional wellbeing takes precedence over the obligation to honor a parent. That message is too often lost, but it is an authentic and important *halachic* position.

Returning to the *Gemara*'s question: if testifying is a positive obligation, and if *kevod habriyot* can exempt a person from a positive obligation when its performance would compromise his dignity, should a *kohen gadol* not be exempt from appearing to testify? The *Gemara* is raising this as a genuine challenge to the *Mishnah*'s statement that the *kohen gadol* may testify.

### Royal Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מֵעִיד לַמֶּלֶךְ. וְהָתְנַן: לֹא דָּן וְלֹא דָּנִין אוֹתוֹ, לֹא מֵעִיד וְלֹא מְעִידִין אוֹתוֹ? אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: מֵעִיד לְבֶן מֶלֶךְ. בֶּן מֶלֶךְ הֶדְיוֹט הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** A High Priest **testifies** only **about a king,** since such testimony would not compromise his dignity. The Gemara objects: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna above that the king **does not judge and is not judged,** and **he does not testify and** others **do not testify concerning him? Rather, Rabbi Zeira said:** The meaning of Rav Yosef's statement is that a High Priest **testifies about a son of a king.** The Gemara objects: **The son of a king is an ordinary** person, without special status in *halakha*, so it is not in keeping with the High Priest's dignity to testify concerning him.

*Amar Rav Yosef*: the *kohen gadol* testifies before a king — meaning in a case involving a king, that would be befitting his dignity. But the *Gemara* immediately objects: how does a king get into a court case at all? The *Mishnah* said *lo dan velo danin oso, lo me'id velo me'idin oso* — a king is neither judged nor testified against. So in what scenario would the *kohen gadol* be testifying in a matter involving a king?

### Prince Halakhic Status

*Ela amar Rav Zeira*: perhaps the *kohen gadol* is testifying in a case involving the king's son — the prince. That would be a person of sufficient dignity that appearing as a witness would be befitting the *kohen gadol*'s honor.

But the *Gemara* objects immediately and strikingly: *ben melech hedyot hu* — the king's son is a commoner. This is a genuinely arresting statement. There is no *halachic* status of prince. The person may stand next in line to the throne, but from a *halachic* standpoint he carries no special standing. The *Gemara* therefore holds that testifying in a case involving a prince would not be sufficiently dignified to bring out the *kohen gadol*. One might ask: surely *kavod* is a social reality, not a purely technical *halachic* one — and who would say that a prince commands no special respect? Yet the *Gemara* insists on a *halachically* recognized status as the threshold.

### King's Brief Attendance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, מֵעִיד בִּפְנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ. וְהָא אֵין מוֹשִׁיבִין מֶלֶךְ בְּסַנְהֶדְרִין! מִשּׁוּם יְקָרָא דְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל אֲתָא וְיָתֵיב. מְקַבְּלִי נִיהֲלֵיהּ לְסָהֲדוּתֵיהּ, קָאֵי הוּא וְאָזֵיל, וּמְעַיְּינִינָא לֵיהּ אֲנַן בְּדִינֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the meaning of Rav Yosef's statement is that **he testifies before the king,** and it is not in keeping with the dignity of the High Priest to testify if the king is a presiding judge. The Gemara objects: **But** didn't we learn in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 2:8) that **a king is not seated on the Sanhedrin?** The Gemara explains: Rav Yosef is referring to a special arrangement: **Due to the honor of** the **High Priest,** the king **comes and sits** as one of the judges, **they receive his testimony, he rises and goes, and we deliberate about the case.**

*Ela meid bifnei hamelech* — the *Gemara* revises: perhaps what is meant is that the *kohen gadol* testifies in a case being held *in the presence of* the king. The king is not serving as a judge — that would be excluded by the *Mishnah* — but the proceedings are taking place before him.

The *Gemara* then notes: *ve'ein moshivin melech be'Sanhedrin* — but you cannot have a king sitting in the *Sanhedrin*, even in a non-judicial, presiding role. The reason, which will be stated explicitly in the next passage, is the principle of *lo sa'aneh al rav* — one cannot freely argue once a higher authority has expressed a view. The presence of the king, even as a passive observer, would effectively silence debate and compromise the *Sanhedrin*'s independence. The separation of judicial and royal authority is not merely formal but functional.

The *Gemara* then resolves the tension: *mishum yikra d'kohen gadol asa veyasiv* — in order to honor the *kohen gadol* and enable him to appear as a witness without it being beneath his dignity, the king would come and briefly sit — because it is his son who is involved in the case. His attendance is not a participation in the judicial process but a courtesy. *Mekabli minei sahadusa* — the court accepts the testimony of the *kohen gadol*. *Kai ve'azil* — and then the king immediately departs, leaving the court to conduct its deliberations without royal presence. *U'mashkali u'matrinan be'dina* — and the judges then engage in the normal course of judicial deliberation.

The net effect of this entire discussion is that the *Gemara* has effectively narrowed to near-vanishing the circumstances in which a *kohen gadol* would actually testify. The only viable scenario turns out to be a highly unusual and orchestrated one: a case involving the king's son, in which the king makes a brief formal appearance to lend sufficient honor to the proceedings that the *kohen gadol*'s appearance is not beneath his dignity. In any ordinary case, the *kohen gadol* could decline to testify on grounds of dignity, just as an elder is exempt from returning a lost object when it would compromise his station.

## Sanhedrin Exclusions

### King on Sanhedrin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא: אֵין מוֹשִׁיבִין מֶלֶךְ בַּסַּנְהֶדְרִין, וְלֹא מֶלֶךְ וְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל בְּעִיבּוּר שָׁנָה. מֶלֶךְ בַּסַּנְהֶדְרִין – דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא תַעֲנֶה עַל רִב״ – לֹא תַעֲנֶה עַל רַב.
>
> **Translation:** § Having mentioned this *baraita*, the Gemara turns its attention to **the** matter **itself: A king is not seated on the Sanhedrin, nor** is **a king or a High Priest** seated **on** a court for **intercalating the year.** The Gemara explains: With regard to **a king on the Sanhedrin,** the source is **as it is written: "Do not answer in a cause [*riv*]"** (Exodus 23:2), which is explained to mean: **Do not answer to a great person [*rav*].** Therefore, one whose stature will make the other judges afraid to contradict him may not be appointed to the Sanhedrin.

*Gufah* — taking up the statement itself: *ein moshivin melech be'Sanhedrin* — a king may not sit on the *Sanhedrin*. This means not only that he does not serve formally as a judge but that he should not even be present in a presiding or supervisory role during deliberations. The reason is the verse *lo sa'aneh al riv* — do not respond against a *rav*, a greater authority. The presence of the king would suppress honest debate. This is precisely why in capital cases the *Sanhedrin* begins deliberations from the most junior members — so that no one is silenced by deference to authority. A king in the room would fatally compromise that independence. The principle here is, in modern terms, one of separation of powers — judicial independence requires that the royal presence be entirely excluded from the deliberative process.

### Leap Year Conflicts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא מֶלֶךְ וְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל בְּעִיבּוּר שָׁנָה. מֶלֶךְ – מִשּׁוּם אַפְסַנְיָא, כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל – מִשּׁוּם צִינָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its explanation of the *baraita*: **Nor** is **a king or a High Priest** seated **on** a court for **intercalating the year. A king** does not serve as a judge concerning this matter **due to** the **sustenance** of soldiers. **A High Priest** does not serve as a judge concerning this matter **due to the cold.** If a month is added to the calendar and the High Priest must perform his Yom Kippur immersions and walk barefoot on the floor of the Temple deeper into the cold autumn, he too will have a vested interest in not adding a month.

*Ve'lo melech ve'kohen gadol be'ibur shana* — neither the king nor the *kohen gadol* may participate in the *beit din* that determines whether a leap year should be declared. The concern regarding the *kohen gadol*: *mishum tzina* — because of cold. *Rashi* explains that the concern is that he will be cold on *Yom Kippur*, when he must immerse five times. *Tosafot* disagrees, noting that the *mikvah* water would be heated, and suggests instead that the concern is that the stone floor of the *Beit HaMikdash*, which he must traverse barefoot, would be unbearably cold. Either way, the *kohen gadol* has a personal, self-interested stake in ensuring the leap year is not declared in a way that places *Yom Kippur* in a cold month — and that self-interest disqualifies him from the decision.

### King's Money Interest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, שַׁתָּא בָּתַר יַרְחָא אָזֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says: Learn from** the ruling concerning the High Priest that the weather of **the year follows the months,** meaning that changes in the seasons are in accordance with the sequence of the months in the standard, non-intercalated calendar. The High Priest would not want the year to be intercalated, because then the colder weather of Marḥeshvan would occur during Tishrei.

The king's disqualification is similarly rooted in self-interest: *mishum afsanya* — because of the wages paid to his troops. An intercalary year gives the king an extra month of labor from the soldiers and officials on his payroll, since they are paid an annual salary. The king would therefore have a financial stake in declaring a leap year, and that conflict disqualifies him from participating in the decision. This is a striking example of how rigorously the *halacha* guards the integrity of decisions of public moment against even structural conflicts of interest.

### Year Calculations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָא הָנָךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה רוֹעֵי בָקָר דַּהֲווֹ קָיְימִי, וְשַׁמְעִינְהוּ רַבָּנַן דְּקָאָמְרִי: חַד אָמַר: אִם בַּכִּיר וְלַקִּישׁ כַּחֲדָא יֵינֵץ – דֵּין הוּא אֲדָר, וְאִם לָאו – לֵית דֵּין אֲדָר. וְחַד אָמַר: אִם תּוֹר בִּצְפַר בִּתְלַג יְמוּת, וּבְטִיהֲרָא בְּטוּל תְּאֵינָה יִדְמוֹךְ יַשְׁלַח מַשְׁכֵּיהּ – דֵּין הוּא אֲדָר, וְאִם לָאו – לֵית דֵּין אֲדָר. וְחַד אָמַר: אִם קִידּוּם תַּקִּיף לַחֲדָא יְהֵא, יִפַּח בְּלוֹעָךְ נָפֵיק לְקִיבְלֵיהּ – דֵּין הוּא אֲדָר, וְאִם לָאו – לֵית דֵּין אֲדָר. וְעַבְּרוּהָ רַבָּנַן לְהַהִיא שַׁתָּא. וְתִסְבְּרָא רַבָּנַן אַרָעֲווֹתָא סְמוּךְ? אֶלָּא, רַבָּנַן אַחוּשְׁבָּנַיְיהוּ סְמוּךְ, וְרוֹעֵי בָקָר אִיסְתַּיּוֹעֵי הוּא דְּאִיסְתַּיַּיעָא מִילְּתַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **But** isn't that contradicted by the incident of **those three cattle herders that were standing** around, **and the Sages heard them saying** various statements about annual weather signs? **One** of them **said: If the first grains and the late-ripening grains sprout at the same** time, **that is Adar, but if not, that is not Adar** but still Shevat. **And one** of them **said: If an ox dies in the morning in the snow,** indicating that it is still very cold, **and at noon in the shade of a fig tree it sleeps,** rubbing against it **to strip off its skin** because of the heat, **that is Adar, but if not, that is not Adar. And one** of them **said: If there is a very strong east wind,** which is cold, yet the warm air that **you blow with your jaw goes out** and **toward it** and overcomes its effect, **that is Adar, but if not, that is not Adar. And** since those signs had not yet come to pass, **the Sages intercalated** the calendar **that year.** And can **you understand** that the **Sages relied on cattle herders** to determine *halakha*? **Rather, the Sages relied on their own calculations, and** those **cattle herders were supported** inasmuch **as their statements were supported** by the Sages' independent conclusion.

*Amar Rav Pappa: shma mina* — we may infer from this discussion that *shata basar yarcha azla* — the year follows the months. *Tosafot* explains that this means the primary basis for declaring a leap year is the calculation of the *tekufah* — the solar season, the position of the sun — rather than the agricultural state of the crops. This is why the *kohen gadol*'s concern about cold is relevant: cold versus warm is fundamentally a function of where the sun is, i.e., the *tekufah*. The question of *aviv* — the actual agricultural ripeness of the grain — is, in this framework, secondary.

This inference is supported by a story in the *Gemara*. Three cowherds were overheard conversing, and the Rabbis who heard them subsequently declared an intercalary year based on what they had heard. The three men offered different criteria for identifying the month of *Adar*. The first said: if the early grain and the late grain sprout simultaneously, then it is *Adar*; if not, it is still *Shevat*. The second said: if in the morning an ox might freeze to death, and by midday the same ox can lie down in the shade of a fig tree to escape the heat, then it is *Adar*; otherwise it is still *Shevat*. The third said: if the east wind is so strong that when you breathe out you can see your breath, then it is *Adar*; otherwise it is not yet *Adar*.

The Rabbis, having heard these observations, then declared that year a leap year — inferring that it was not yet truly *Adar* but still *Shevat*, and that an additional *Adar* was needed.

The *Gemara* immediately asks: *vetisbera rabanan arevusa samchi* — do you really think the Rabbis relied on the observations of shepherds? *Ela rabanan achushbenayehu samchi* — no, the Rabbis relied on their own calculations of the *tekufah*. *Vero'ei bakar isyuhi de'istaye milta* — the observations of the cowherds merely served to support and confirm what the Rabbis had already determined through calculation. The testimony of the shepherds was corroborative, not determinative.

### Shepherds & Tekufah

The conclusion, as *Tosafot* understands it, is that the *tekufah* — the solar calculation — is primary in determining whether a leap year is needed, while agricultural observation serves a secondary, confirmatory role. This also illuminates the reason for the *kohen gadol*'s disqualification from leap year deliberations: since the decision turns primarily on questions of heat and cold, i.e., the solar season, the *kohen gadol*'s personal stake in avoiding cold at *Yom Kippur* time creates a direct and disqualifying conflict of interest.
