# Keritot 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mother-in-Law Case

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַבָּא עַל חֲמוֹתוֹ חַיָּיב עָלֶיהָ מִשּׁוּם חֲמוֹתוֹ, וְכַלָּתוֹ, וְאֵשֶׁת אָחִיו, וְאֵשֶׁת אֲחִי אָבִיו, וַאֲחוֹת אִשְׁתּוֹ, וְאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, וְנִדָּה. וְכֵן הַבָּא עַל אֵם חָמִיו, וְעַל אֵם חֲמוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: It is possible for **one who engages in intercourse with his mother-in-law** to be **liable** to bring seven sin offerings **for** doing so, **due to** the unwitting violation of the prohibitions of engaging in intercourse with **his mother-in-law, and his daughter-in-law, and the wife of his brother, and the wife of his father's brother, and his wife's sister, and a married woman, and a menstruating woman. And likewise,** the same applies with regard to **one who engages in intercourse with the mother of his father-in-law or with the mother of his mother-in-law.**

The *mishna* at the bottom of 14b presents one further case of multiple sins arising from a single act of sex, before the discussion broadens considerably. The case opens: *haba al chamoto* — a man has sex with his mother-in-law — and the *mishna* states that it is possible, though not in every case, for that one act to carry obligations under multiple designations simultaneously.

### Genealogy Explained

How could a man's mother-in-law also be his daughter-in-law? The construction is less complicated than it first appears. Suppose the man is married to a woman — call her Rachel — and Rachel's mother is Sarah. Sarah is his mother-in-law. Now, suppose the man had previously had a son, and that son married Sarah, and they later divorced. Sarah was therefore once his daughter-in-law. After the divorce, she remarried, had a child, and eventually — through the family tree — Rachel was born from that line. The man then marries Rachel, making her former mother-in-law Sarah simultaneously his current mother-in-law and his former daughter-in-law. The *mishna* then layers on further designations: *v'eshet achiv* — this same woman was then married to his brother; *v'eshet achi aviv* — and then to his uncle; *v'achot ishto* — and she is somehow also his wife's sister, which requires their having the same father; *v'eshet ish* — she is a married woman; *v'niddah* — and she is a *niddah*.

### Multiple Relations

The *mishna* continues: *v'chen haba al em chamiv v'al em chamoto* — the same construction applies if one replaces "mother-in-law" with "grandmother-in-law." The prohibition of a man marrying a woman extends not only to her mother but to her granddaughter as well, so an analogous list of overlapping designations can be generated with the grandmother-in-law as its center.

### R. Yochanan b. Nuri

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי אוֹמֵר: הַבָּא עַל חֲמוֹתוֹ חַיָּיב עָלֶיהָ מִשּׁוּם חֲמוֹתוֹ, וְאֵם חֲמוֹתוֹ, וְאֵם חָמִיו. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: שְׁלׇשְׁתָּן שֵׁם אֶחָד הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri says:** It is possible for **one who engages in intercourse with his mother-in-law** to be **liable due to** the unwitting violation of the prohibitions of engaging in intercourse with **his mother-in-law, and the mother of his mother-in-law, and the mother of his father-in-law.** The Rabbis **said to him: Those three** prohibitions **are** all **one category** of prohibition, derived from the same verse: "You shall not uncover the nakedness of a woman and her daughter; you shall not take her son's daughter, or her daughter's daughter" (Leviticus 18:17). Consequently, one is not liable to bring separate sin offerings for violating these prohibitions.

*Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri omer*: a man who has sex with his mother-in-law can be *chayav* for three violations in that single act — *mishum chamoto*, *v'em chamoto*, *v'em chamiv* — mother-in-law, mother-in-law's mother, and father-in-law's mother. The scenario: the man is married to three women — call them Rachel, Rivka, and Sarah — and this one woman is the mother of one, the paternal grandmother of another, and the maternal grandmother of the third. She had one daughter and two granddaughters, one through a son and one through a daughter, and the man married all three. In having sex with this one woman, he has committed three sins: *isha u'bitah*, a woman and her daughter; *bat bitah*, a woman and her granddaughter on the mother's side; and *bat beno*, a woman and her granddaughter on the father's side. The sages respond: *shlosha shemot echad* — although there are three labels, these are all expressions of the single sin of mother and daughter, and therefore only one *chatat* is incurred. Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri says three *chatatot*; the rabbis say one.

## Sex vs. Slaughter

### Sumchus Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי הוֹשַׁעְיָא: רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי וְסוֹמְכוֹס אָמְרוּ דָּבָר אֶחָד. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי – הָא דַּאֲמַרַן. סוֹמְכוֹס מַאי הִיא? דִּתְנַן:
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Elazar says** that **Rabbi Hoshaya says: Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri and Sumakhos said the same thing.** With regard to **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri,** this is referring to **that which we stated** in the mishna. With regard to **Sumakhos, what is** the equivalent ruling that he stated? It is the following ruling, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Ḥullin* 82a):

*Amar Rabbi Elazar amar Rabbi Oshaya*: Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri and Sumchus said the same thing — *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri veSumchus amru davar echad*.

### Slaughter Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁחָטָהּ וְאֶת בַּת בִּתָּהּ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ שָׁחַט אֶת בִּתָּהּ – סוֹפֵג אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים. סוֹמְכוֹס אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: סוֹפֵג שְׁמוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** If **one slaughtered** an animal **and its daughter's daughter and afterward slaughtered** the original animal's **daughter, he incurs the forty** lashes for transgressing the prohibition against slaughtering an animal and its offspring on a single day (see Leviticus 22:28). **Sumakhos says in the name of Rabbi Meir: He incurs eighty** lashes for slaughtering the third animal, as its mother and its daughter had already been slaughtered on that day. This indicates that Sumakhos also maintains that when a person commits a single act and thereby commits two transgressions that are derived from a single verse, he is liable to receive punishment for both violations.

What did Sumchus say? A *mishna* on 17a records the following case of *oto v'et beno*, the prohibition against slaughtering a mother animal and its offspring on the same day. Suppose someone slaughtered the grandmother sheep, then the granddaughter, and then the middle generation — the daughter of the grandmother and the mother of the granddaughter. When the middle animal was slaughtered, two violations of *oto v'et beno* were committed simultaneously: she was the daughter of the first and the mother of the third, so slaughtering her was at once *oto v'et beno* in relation to the animal above her and *oto v'et beno* in relation to the animal below her. The question is how many sets of lashes this incurs. The first view is *sofeg arba'im* — only forty lashes, a single transgression committed in two directions. *Sumchus omer mishum Rabbi Meir sofeg shemonim* — no, two sets of forty. One act, two variations of the same prohibition. The parallel to Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri is evident: in both cases it is one act with multiple instances of what appears to be the same underlying sin, and in both cases the question is whether this generates multiple obligations.

### Distinct Labels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: דִּלְמָא לָא הִיא, עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי הָכָא אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם שֵׁמוֹת מוּחְלָקִין, דְּמִיקַּרְיָא חֲמוֹתוֹ, וּמִיקַּרְיָא אֵם חֲמוֹתוֹ, וּמִיקַּרְיָא אֵם חָמִיו. אֲבָל גַּבֵּי אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ, דְּכוּלְּהוּ ״אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ״ מִיקַּרְיָין, דְּאֵין שֵׁמוֹת מוּחְלָקִין – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Perhaps it is not so,** as Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri would not agree with the ruling of Sumakhos. It is possible that **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri says** that one is liable to bring multiple sin offerings **only here,** in the case of one who engaged in intercourse with his mother-in-law, **only due to** the **differentiated names** of the prohibitions, **as she is called his mother-in-law, and she is called his mother-in-law's mother, and she is called his father-in-law's mother,** and the Torah prohibits each of these relationships separately. **But with regard to** the case of slaughtering **a mother and its offspring** in a single day, **where all of** the permutations **are called: A mother and its offspring, as there are no differentiated names** given in the Torah for slaughtering a mother animal and then its offspring or for slaughtering the offspring and subsequently its mother, Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri might maintain that one is **not** liable to receive multiple sets of lashes.

Rava intervenes to argue that perhaps the two cases are not as parallel as they appear. *Amar Rava dilma lo hi* — maybe they are not the same. When did Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri say that sleeping with this one woman incurs three *chatatot*? *Ela mishum shemot muchlakim* — because the designations are conceptually distinct. The woman carries three different labels vis-à-vis the man: *chamoso*, *em chamoso*, *em chamiv* — mother-in-law, mother-in-law's mother, father-in-law's mother. Although the underlying prohibition in each case is that of a woman and her daughter or granddaughter — articulated in a single *pasuk* — the Torah gives each relationship a different name. One is *isha u'vitah*, another *bat vitah*, another *bat beno*. The labels are *shemot muchlakim*, distinct designations. *Aval gabei oto v'et beno*, by contrast, *d'khulhu oto v'et beno mikayim* — the Torah never distinguishes between slaughtering a mother-and-child versus a child-and-mother. There is only one label: *oto v'et beno*. So he may well say multiple obligations where the Torah provides distinct labels, but not where the Torah uses only a single designation throughout.

### Zimah Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: דִּלְמָא עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר סוֹמְכוֹס אֶלָּא גַּבֵּי אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ, דְּגוּפִין מוּחְלָקִין. אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּאֵין גּוּפִין מוּחְלָקִין, אֵימָא כְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״שַׁאֲרָה הֵנָּה זִמָּה הִיא״, הַכָּתוּב עֲשָׂאָן לְכוּלָּן זִמָּה אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Perhaps Sumakhos says** that one is liable to receive two sets of lashes **only with regard to** the case of slaughtering **a mother and its offspring** on the same day, **as** there are **differentiated bodies.** Although he violated the same prohibition twice, he did so by slaughtering three different animals. **But here,** with regard to a mother-in-law, **where** there are **not** several **differentiated bodies,** as the man engaged in intercourse with only one woman, one might **say** that Sumakhos **holds in accordance with** the statement that **Rabbi Abbahu says** in the name of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, as Rabbi Abbahu says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The verse that introduces the prohibitions against engaging in intercourse with one's mother-in-law, one's mother-in-law's mother, and one's father-in-law's mother concludes with the phrase: **"They are near kinswomen; it is lewdness"** (Leviticus 18:17). This teaches that although these are different relationships, **the verse rendered them all one** single prohibition of **lewdness,** for which only a single sin offering is brought.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak presses the distinction further in the other direction. Perhaps Sumchus would not agree with Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri either. When does Sumchus say the sheep case yields two sets of lashes? *Ela gabei oto v'et beno d'gufin m'chulakin* — because there the animals are different bodies: the grandmother and the granddaughter are two distinct animals, and each one provides the middle animal with a different identity. *Aval hacha d'ein gufin m'chulakin* — here in the case of the mother-in-law there is only one body, one woman. Perhaps Sumchus would say it is all one. The Gemara then resolves the matter through Rabbi Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: the *pasuk* discussing sex with a mother and her daughters or granddaughters closes with the phrase *she'erah henah zimah hi* — "it is the same flesh, it is *zimah*" — using *zimah hi* in the singular. *Hakatuv asan l'khulan zimah achat* — the Torah itself declares all of these acts to be a single *zimah*. That verse is precisely why even Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's case, where the labels are distinct, might ultimately collapse into one sin — yet it is equally the verse that distinguishes this case from *oto v'et beno*, where no such unifying declaration exists. The Gemara does note, though, that the case with the mother-in-law is arguably the stronger candidate for multiple obligations, since not only do different people give her each designation — just as two different sheep give the middle one its identity — but additionally the Torah provides distinct labels for each relationship.

## R. Akiva's Questions

### Market Setting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: שָׁאַלְתִּי אֶת רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְאֶת רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בָּאִיטְלֵיס שֶׁל עֵימָאוּם, שֶׁהָלְכוּ לִיקַּח בְּהֵמָה לְמִשְׁתֵּה בְנוֹ שֶׁל רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, הַבָּא עַל אֲחוֹתוֹ וְעַל אֲחוֹת אָבִיו וְעַל אֲחוֹת אִמּוֹ, מַהוּ? חַיָּיב אַחַת עַל כּוּלָּן, אוֹ חַיָּיב עַל כָּל אַחַת וְאֶחָת?
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Akiva said: I asked Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua in the meat market [*itlis*] in Emmaus, where they went to purchase an animal for the** wedding **feast of the son of Rabban Gamliel:** In the case of **one who** unwittingly **engages in intercourse with his sister, and the sister of his father, and the sister of his mother,** during one lapse of awareness, **what is** the *halakha*? Is he **liable** to bring **one** sin offering **for all** three prohibitions, **or** is he **liable** to bring a separate sin offering **for each and every one** of the prohibitions?

*Amar Rabbi Akiva*: Rabbi Akiva relates that he posed a question to Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua *b'itliz shel Amos* — in the meat market of Emmaus — while they were going to purchase an animal for Rabban Gamliel's son's wedding feast. The detail is noted by Tosafot: *l'hodia shivchan* — to convey the greatness of these sages, who *af al pi shehayu trudim*, even though they were occupied with a practical errand, *hayu oskim b'divrei Torah*, were engaged in Torah discussion throughout.

### The Question

The question Rabbi Akiva posed: *haba al achoto v'al achot aviv v'al achot imo* — a man who has sex with a woman who is simultaneously his sister, his father's sister, and his mother's sister. *Mahu* — is it one *chatat* for all, *chayav achat al kulan*, or is he obligated for each designation separately, *o chayav al kol achat v'achat*? This question is the conceptual foundation of all the previous *mishnayot*, which had assumed without explicit derivation that multiple designations in a single act generate multiple *chatatot*. Here Rabbi Akiva is trying to establish that principle with the sages.

### Sages' Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לוֹ: לֹא שָׁמַעְנוּ, אֲבָל שָׁמַעְנוּ: הַבָּא עַל חָמֵשׁ נָשָׁיו נִדּוֹת בְּהֶעְלֵם (אַחַת) [אֶחָד], שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאֶחָת, (וְרוֹאֶה אֲנִי) [וְרוֹאִין אָנוּ] שֶׁהַדְּבָרִים קַל וָחֹמֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **They said to** Rabbi Akiva: **We did not hear** a ruling from our teachers about that case, **but we heard** the following ruling: **One who engages in intercourse with** each of **his five wives** while they are **menstruating, during one lapse of awareness,** we heard **that he is liable** to bring a separate sin offering **for** having engaged in intercourse with **each and every one** of them. **And it appears to me that these matters** can be derived from **an *a fortiori*** inference: If he is liable to bring separate sin offerings for having engaged in intercourse with five menstruating women, who are forbidden by one prohibition, he should certainly be liable to bring separate sin offerings for having engaged in intercourse with his sister, the sister of his father, and the sister of his mother, who are forbidden by three separate prohibitions.

*Amru lo lo shamanu* — they had no tradition about this. What they did have a tradition about was the following: *haba al chamesh nashim niddot uvehelem achat shehu chayav al kol achat v'achat* — a man who slept with five wives who were all *niddot* in a single state of forgetfulness brings a separate *chatat* for each one. That is, the same sin — *niddah* — with multiple different women, generates multiple sin offerings.

### Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָאִינוּ שֶׁהַדְּבָרִים קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה הַבָּא עַל חָמֵשׁ נָשָׁיו נִדּוֹת בְּבַת אַחַת, שֶׁהִיא שֵׁם אַחַת – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת, אֲחוֹתוֹ שֶׁהִיא אֲחוֹת אָבִיו שֶׁהִיא אֲחוֹת אִמּוֹ, שֶׁהֵן שְׁלֹשָׁה שֵׁמוֹת – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיְּהֵא חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת?
>
> **Translation:** **And it appears to us that these matters** can be derived from **an *a fortiori*** inference: **If one who engages in intercourse with his five wives** while they are **menstruating, at the same time, which is** only **one name** of a prohibition, is nevertheless **liable** to bring a sin offering **for each and every one** of them, then in the case of one who engages in intercourse with **his sister who is** also **the sister of his father** and **the sister of his mother, which are three** separate prohibition **names, is it not logical that he should be liable for each and every one** of them?

*V'ro'im anu shehadvarim kal vachomer* — it seems to us, they said, that a *kal vachomer* follows. If the same sin — *niddah* — committed with multiple different people requires multiple sin offerings, then surely different sins — distinct designations — committed against one person in a single act should require multiple sin offerings, since the sins are even more fundamentally distinct. The hidden premise of this *kal vachomer*, however, is precisely what is at stake: it assumes that distinct designations — *shemot mechulakot* — are a stronger ground for multiple obligations than distinct bodies — *gufim mechulakot*. That assumption is exactly what can be challenged.

## Names & Bodies

### One Person

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא כִּדְקָתָנֵי, מַאי תִּיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ? הֲרֵי שֵׁמוֹת מוּחְלָקִין, הֲרֵי גּוּפִין מוּחְלָקִין!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara raises a difficulty with regard to Rabbi Akiva's question: **What are the circumstances** about which Rabbi Akiva inquired? **If we say** that the circumstances are precisely **as** the mishna **teaches** them, that one unwittingly engaged in intercourse with his sister, and the sister of his father, and the sister of his mother, then for **what** purpose did Rabbi Akiva **raise this dilemma** before Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua? **Aren't they differentiated names** of prohibitions and **aren't they** also **differentiated bodies?** Since the man engaged in intercourse with three women, who are forbidden by three distinct prohibitions, it is obvious that he is obligated to bring three separate sin offerings.

The Gemara clarifies the structure of the *mishna*. One might initially read the case as involving three different women — a sister, a father's sister, and a mother's sister — in which case the question would be trivial. *Eileima kidekatani, mai tibei lei* — if it were three different women, what is the question? *Harei shemot mecholakim harei gufi mecholakim* — it would be both different sins and different bodies. *Ela hachi katani* — no, the case is one woman who is simultaneously *achoto shehi achot aviv shehi achot imo* — his sister, who is also his father's sister, who is also his mother's sister. One person, three designations, one act.

### Core Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכִי קָתָנֵי: הַבָּא עַל אֲחוֹת אָבִיו שֶׁהִיא אֲחוֹת אִמּוֹ, חַיָּיב אַחַת עַל כּוּלָּן, אוֹ חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאֶחָת, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן: הֲרֵי שֵׁמוֹת מוּחְלָקִין, אוֹ דִלְמָא הֲרֵי אֵין גּוּפִין מוּחְלָקִין?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it must be that **this** is what the mishna **is teaching:** Rabbi Akiva asked: In the case of **one who** unwittingly **engages in intercourse with** his sister who is also **the sister of his father** and **who is** also **the sister of his mother,** is he **liable** to bring only **one** sin offering **for** violating **all of those** prohibitions, **or is he liable** to bring a separate sin offering **for each and every one? What is** the *halakha*? **Do we say** that **they are differentiated names** of prohibitions, and since he violated distinct prohibitions, he is liable to bring separate sin offerings? **Or perhaps** we say: **It is not** a case of **differentiated bodies;** since he engaged in intercourse with only one woman, he is liable to bring only a single sin offering.

The Gemara then formulates the question precisely: *mi amrinan harei shemot mecholakim* — do we say it is distinct sins, distinct labels, and therefore three *chatatot*? *O dilma harei ein gufin mecholakim* — or, since there is only one body involved, is that sufficient reason to say it is one *chatat*? As all the previous *mishnayot* have established, the answer is that distinct designations are indeed sufficient. But here the question is being asked explicitly for the first time.

### Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְחָמֵשׁ נִדּוֹת – שֶׁכֵּן גּוּפִין מוּחְלָקִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: This line of reasoning **can be refuted,** as follows: **What** is notable **about** the case of **five menstruating women** that would render one liable to bring a separate sin offering for having intercourse with each one of them? It is notable **in that** they are **differentiated bodies,** which is not the case with regard to one's sister who is also the sister of his father and the sister of his mother, as she is a single person.

The *kal vachomer* can be challenged: *mah l'hanei niddot she'kein gufim m'chulakin* — what makes the case of five *niddot* yield multiple obligations is that it is five different bodies, five distinct acts with five distinct people. One cannot straightforwardly infer from there to the case of one person with multiple designations, because perhaps *gufim mechulakot* — different bodies, different acts — is the operative principle, and without different bodies there is no ground for multiple obligations.

### Verse Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר קְרָא: ״עֶרְוַת אֲחוֹתוֹ גִלָּה״, לְחַיֵּיב עַל אֲחוֹתוֹ שֶׁהִיא אֲחוֹת אָבִיו שֶׁהִיא אֲחוֹת אִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the reason one is liable to bring three separate sin offerings for engaging in intercourse with his sister who is also the sister of his mother and the sister of his father is that **the verse states:** "And if a man takes his sister…and they shall be cut off [*venikhretu*] in the sight of the children of their people; **he has uncovered his sister's nakedness"** (Leviticus 20:17). The final clause in this verse serves **to render him liable** to receive three separate penalties of *karet* **for** intentional intercourse, or to bring three separate sin offerings for unwitting intercourse, with **his sister who is** also **the sister of his father** and **who is the sister of his mother.**

The Gemara therefore resolves the matter not by *kal vachomer* but by verse. *Ela amar kra ervas achoto gilah* — the Torah in describing the prohibition of a sister uses what was already noted as a seemingly redundant formulation, specifying *ervas achoto* with the word *gilah*. The reason for this apparent redundancy is precisely *l'chaiv al kol achat v'achat* — to teach that for every distinct designation, even with a single body in a single act, a separate *chatat* is incurred. The *pasuk* teaches the case of *l'chaiv al achoso she'hi achos aviv she'hi achos imo* — a sister who is also the father's sister and the mother's sister — that one is obligated for each designation separately. The principle is thus established not by inference but by explicit *drashah*: both *gufim mechulakot* and *shemot mechulakot* independently require multiple sin offerings.

## Genealogy of Evil

### Rasha Bar Rasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּרַשִּׁיעָא בַּר רַשִּׁיעָא, שֶׁבָּא עַל אִמּוֹ וְהוֹלִיד שְׁתֵּי בָּנוֹת, וְחָזַר וּבָא עַל אַחַת מֵהֶן וְהוֹלִיד בֵּן, וּבָא בְּנוֹ עַל אֲחוֹת אִמּוֹ, שֶׁהִיא אֲחוֹתוֹ, שֶׁהִיא אֲחוֹת אָבִיו, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ רַשִּׁיעָא בַּר רַשִּׁיעָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains how there could be a case where one's sister is also the sister of his father and the sister of his mother: **Rav Adda bar Ahava says: You find it in** the case of **a wicked man, the son of a wicked man.** The case applies if a man **engaged in intercourse with his mother and fathered two daughters, and then engaged in intercourse with one of those** daughters **and fathered a son, and his son engaged in intercourse with his mother's sister who is** also **his** own **sister,** because they share a father, and who **is** also **his father's sister.** This individual **is a wicked man, the son of a wicked man,** as both he and his father engaged in incestuous relationships.

The Gemara now addresses how the scenario of a sister who is simultaneously a father's sister and a mother's sister can actually arise. *Amar Rav Adda bar Ahava*: *mashkachas lah b'rashia bar rashia* — one finds this case in a wicked person who is the son of a wicked person.

### Genealogy Built

The construction proceeds as follows. A man — call him X — has sex with his mother and fathers two daughters, Rachel and Leah. These two daughters are therefore both his daughters and his sisters, since they share his mother. He then has sex with Rachel, and from her a son is born — call him Reuven. Reuven is thus X's son, though also his grandson through the maternal line. The *gemara* then states: *v'chazar ba'al achman v'holid ben uva beno al achot imo she'hi achoto she'hi achot aviv* — the son Reuven then has sex with Leah, who is: his father's sister, because she is the daughter of X and X's mother; his mother's sister, because she is the daughter of X and X's mother who is also Reuven's grandmother through Rachel; and his sister, because she is the daughter of his father X. She is simultaneously *achoto*, *achot aviv*, and *achot imo* — his sister, his father's sister, and his mother's sister. This is the *rasha bar rasha* construction: a wicked person who had relations with his mother, followed by a wicked son who compounded the genealogical violations.

## Multiple Acts

### Niddah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בָּא עָלֶיהָ, וְחָזַר וּבָא עָלֶיהָ, וְחָזַר וּבָא עָלֶיהָ – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאֶחָת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אֶחָת.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If one unwittingly **engages in intercourse with** a woman who is forbidden to him under the penalty of excision from the World-to-Come [*karet*], **and then** again **engages in intercourse with her, and then** yet again **engages in intercourse with her,** all during one lapse of awareness, he is **liable** to bring a separate sin offering **for each and every one** of the acts of intercourse with her. This is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer. But the Rabbis say: He is liable** to bring **only one** sin offering.

The Gemara now turns to a *baraita* that directly addresses the question of the same sin with the same person committed multiple times. The case: a husband and wife have sex multiple times while she is a *niddah*, without either of them remembering between acts — *ba'al v'chazar ba'al v'chazar ba'al*.

### R. Eliezer

*Chayav al kol achat v'achat divrei Rabbi Eliezer* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that each distinct act requires its own *chatat*, even when the sin and the person are identical throughout.

### Sages' View

*V'chachamim omrim eino chayav ela achat* — the sages hold that the same sin with the same person, however many times repeated without intervening awareness, generates only a single *chatat*. This is the normative ruling.

### Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדִים חֲכָמִים לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּבָא עַל חָמֵשׁ נָשָׁיו נִדּוֹת בְּבַת אַחַת, שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת, הוֹאִיל וְהוּא גָּרַם לָהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis concede to Rabbi Eliezer with regard to one who engages in intercourse with his five wives** while they are **menstruating,** where the prohibition was violated unwittingly by both the man and the woman, **at the same time,** i.e., during one lapse of awareness, **that he is liable for each and every one** of them, **since he caused** each of **them** to be liable to bring a sin offering.

Nevertheless, *umodim l'Rabbi Eliezer b'ba al chamesh niddot b'vat achat shehu chayav al kol achat v'achat* — the sages agree with Rabbi Eliezer that if there are five different women, each a *niddah*, and he had sex with all of them in a single state of forgetfulness, he brings a *chatat* for each one. The sin is the same — *niddah* — but the persons are different.

### Causation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹאִיל וְהוּא גָּרַם לָהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **since he caused** each of **them** to be liable to bring a sin offering.

The *baraita* explains this with the phrase *ho'il v'hu garam lahem* — since he caused them to bring sin offerings. The reasoning: each of his five wives will herself be obligated to bring a *chatat* for this act. Since each woman brings a separate sin offering, he cannot be treated as having committed a single undivided act when the women's obligations are five distinct ones. Therefore he too brings separately for each.

## Causation Challenged

### Hypothetical

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: מִי אָמְרִינַן ״הוֹאִיל וְהוּא גָּרַם לָהֶן״? וְהָתַנְיָא: הוּא בְּהֶעְלֵם (אַחַת) [אֶחָד] וְהִיא בַּחֲמִשָּׁה הֶעְלֵמוֹת – הוּא אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת וְהִיא חַיֶּיבֶת עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאֶחָת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Rav Naḥman: Do we** really **say** that **since he caused** each of **them** to be liable to bring a sin offering, he himself is liable to bring five separate sin offerings? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If one unwittingly engages in intercourse five times with a woman who is forbidden to him, and **he** does so **during one lapse of awareness,** without realizing over the course of that time period that the act is forbidden, **but she** does so **during five lapses** of awareness, as she repeatedly became aware of the prohibition and then forgot it before the next transgression, **he is liable** to bring **only one** sin offering, **but she is liable** to bring a separate sin offering **for each and every one** of the five transgressions? In this case, although he caused her to bring several sin offerings, he himself brings only one sin offering.

Rava challenges this causation principle before Rav Nachman. *Mi amrinan hov hu garam lahem?* — is that truly the operative reason? Consider the following case: *hu b'he'elam achat v'hi b'he'elamot* — he had sex with his wife five times and his state of forgetfulness was continuous throughout, but she remembered between each act and then forgot again. Each time she remembered and then forgot, a new state of forgetfulness began for her.

### Independent Judgment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אֵימָא: הוֹאִיל וְגוּפִין מוּחְלָקִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** one must **say** that this is the reason he brings five sin offerings for engaging in intercourse with his five wives while they were each menstruating, during one lapse of awareness: It is **because** they are **differentiated bodies.**

How many sin offerings does each party bring? She brings five — one for each interval of forgetfulness. He brings one — his forgetfulness was never interrupted. *Hu eino chayav ela achat v'hi chayavet al kol achat v'achat*. Now if the principle were truly that his obligation is determined by her obligations — that because she brings five, he must bring five — then in this case he should also bring five. But he does not. You judge each party independently; the number of *korbanot* one party brings does not determine the other's obligation.

### Real Principle

The correct principle is simply that different bodies, different people involved in distinct acts, is what creates distinct obligations. The causation language of the *baraita* is not the true underlying reason; the true reason is *gufim muchlakim*. When a man has sex with five different women, there are five distinct acts involving five distinct people, and that alone is sufficient to require five sin offerings regardless of what the women bring.

## Shabbat Question

### Picking Apples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ, קָצַר וְקָצַר – מָה לִי אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer that one who repeatedly engages in intercourse with a woman who is forbidden to him is liable to bring a separate sin offering for each act of intercourse, even if this all occurred in a single lapse of awareness. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If one **reaped** a fig-bulk of grain on Shabbat, which is the measure for which one is liable to bring a sin offering, **and** then **reaped** another fig-bulk, in a single lapse of awareness, **what** would **Rabbi Eliezer say?**

The Gemara now raises a new question by analogy. *Iba'ya lehu: katzar v'katzar mai l'amar Rabbi Eliezer?* — what would Rabbi Eliezer say if someone forgot it was *Shabbat* and harvested an apple, and then an hour later harvested another apple? Same sin, same type of act, multiple times — would Rabbi Eliezer require two *chatatot* here as he did for multiple acts of sex?

### First View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הָתָם מִשּׁוּם דְּהוּא דַּעֲבַד תַּרְתֵּין, וְאַמְּטוּל (ל)הָכִי אָמַר: חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת, וְהָכָא נָמֵי דַּעֲבַד תַּרְתֵּין,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the sides of the dilemma: Is **the reason for** the ruling of **Rabbi Eliezer there,** with regard to forbidden relations, **that** the sinner **performed two** separate prohibited acts, **and therefore** Rabbi Eliezer **says** he is **liable** to bring a separate sin offering **for each and every one** of the acts? If so, **here too, where he performed two** separate acts of reaping, he is liable to bring a separate sin offering for each act.

One possibility: Rabbi Eliezer's reasoning is simply that he did two acts — *de'avid tartei* — and therefore two *chatatot* are incurred. On this reading, multiple distinct acts always require multiple sin offerings regardless of what the act involves, and by *Shabbat* harvesting twice would yield two obligations.

### Second View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִּלְמָא טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הָתָם מִשּׁוּם דְּאִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹ לְעָרְבָן לְבִיאוֹת זוֹ בָּזוֹ, אַמְּטוּל (לְ)הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת, אֲבָל קָצַר כִּגְרוֹגֶרֶת וְחָזַר וְקָצַר כִּגְרוֹגֶרֶת בְּהֶעְלֵם (אַחַת) [אֶחָד], כֵּיוָן דְּאֶפְשָׁר לוֹ לְעָרֵב שְׁתֵּי גְּרוֹגְרוֹת בְּבַת אַחַת אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אֶחָת, מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **Or, perhaps the reason** for the ruling **of Rabbi Eliezer there,** in the case of forbidden relations, is **that it is impossible for** the sinner **to combine the** acts of **intercourse with one another,** and **therefore Rabbi Eliezer says** that he is **liable** to bring a separate sin offering **for each and every one** of the acts of intercourse. **But** if one **reaped a fig-bulk** of grain, **and then reaped** another **fig-bulk, in a single lapse of awareness, since it is possible for him to combine the two fig-bulks** of grain together and reap them **at the same time, he** would be **liable** to bring **only one** sin offering. **What** is the correct explanation for Rabbi Eliezer's ruling?

*Dilma ta'ama d'Rabbi Eliezer hatam mishum d'i efshar lo l'arvan bi'ah zo b'zo* — alternatively, the reason Rabbi Eliezer requires multiple *chatatot* for sex is specifically that it is impossible to perform two acts of sex simultaneously. There is no conceptual way to combine them into a single act. *Amtu l'hachi amar Rabbi Eliezer chayav al kol achat v'achat* — it is precisely because simultaneity is impossible that each act is irreducibly distinct.

### Why Different?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הָתָם – מִשּׁוּם דַּעֲבַד תַּרְתֵּי, הָכָא נָמֵי הָא עֲבַד תַּרְתֵּי. וְרַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הָתָם – דְּאִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹ לְעָרְבָן, אֲבָל אֶפְשָׁר לוֹ לְעָרְבָן – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אֶחָת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: The reason** for the ruling **of Rabbi Eliezer there,** in the case of forbidden relations, is **that** the sinner **performed two** separate prohibited acts; and **here too, he has performed two** separate acts of reaping and is liable to bring two sin offerings. **And Rav Yosef said: The reason** for the ruling **of Rabbi Eliezer there,** in the case of forbidden relations, is **that it is impossible for him to combine** the two transgressions into a single act; **but** if it is **possible for him to combine them,** e.g., by reaping two fig-bulks of grain, **he is liable** to bring **only one** sin offering.

The contrast then becomes clear. *Aval katzar ke-grogoret v'katzar ke-grogoret*, if one harvested the minimum quantity — a *grogoret* — and then harvested another *grogoret*, *kivan d'efshar lo l'arvei shtei grogorot b'vat achat* — since it would in theory have been possible to harvest both quantities in a single act, perhaps even sequential acts of the same type are considered one act when they could in principle have been combined. The *grogoret* is merely a minimum measure; eating a large loaf of bread is not multiple acts of eating just because it contains multiple *kezayitim*. Similarly, harvesting several apples at once is one act of harvesting that met the minimum size, not multiple acts. On Rav Yosef's reading, then, Rabbi Eliezer would not require multiple *chatatot* for harvesting twice, since the acts could have been combined.

## Shiurim Logic

### Minimum Size

The underlying principle being clarified here is that a *shiur* — a minimum measure — defines the threshold of an act; it does not define the unit of repetition. If one harvests a *grogoret*-sized quantity, that is one act of harvesting that has reached the minimum size for liability. The question of whether two such acts are really two acts or one depends not on the *shiur* but on whether the actions were genuinely distinct.

### Simultaneous Acts

The illustration: if one picks three apples in a single motion — hand closes around all three at once — that is one act of picking. The fact that three apples were taken does not multiply the act into three, any more than eating a large piece of bread constitutes multiple acts of eating. The *grogoret* is the standard for what counts, not the dividing line between acts.

### Distinction

Sex acts, however, cannot be performed simultaneously by their very nature. Each act of sex is necessarily sequential, and no act of sex can be subsumed into another. Therefore, for Rabbi Eliezer, each act of sex is irreducibly distinct, and multiple acts require multiple sin offerings. For acts like harvesting, where simultaneity is physically possible, Rabbi Eliezer on Rav Yosef's reading would not impose the same multiplication — because if it could have all been done at once, the sequential version may still be viewed as one act in different moments.

## Rava vs. Yosef

### Rava's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הָתָם – מִשּׁוּם דַּעֲבַד תַּרְתֵּי, הָכָא נָמֵי הָא עֲבַד תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: The reason** for the ruling **of Rabbi Eliezer there,** in the case of forbidden relations, is **that** the sinner **performed two** separate prohibited acts; and **here too, he has performed two** separate acts of reaping and is liable to bring two sin offerings.

*Amar Rava ta'ama d'Rabbi Eliezer hatam mishum d'avid tartei hacha nami d'avid tartei* — Rava holds that the reason Rabbi Eliezer requires multiple *chatatot* is simply that two distinct acts were committed. This applies everywhere. Whether it is sex or harvesting or any other category, if the acts were distinct, Rabbi Eliezer would require separate sin offerings for each.

### Yosef's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הָתָם – דְּאִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹ לְעָרְבָן, אֲבָל אֶפְשָׁר לוֹ לְעָרְבָן – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אֶחָת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yosef said: The reason** for the ruling **of Rabbi Eliezer there,** in the case of forbidden relations, is **that it is impossible for him to combine** the two transgressions into a single act; **but** if it is **possible for him to combine them,** e.g., by reaping two fig-bulks of grain, **he is liable** to bring **only one** sin offering.

*Rav Yosef amar: ta'ama d'Rabbi Eliezer hatam d'i efshar l'arvan* — Rav Yosef holds that the reason is specifically the impossibility of simultaneity. Where acts cannot be combined — as with sex — Rabbi Eliezer requires multiple *chatatot*. Where they can be combined, *aval efshar l'arvan eino chayav ela achat* — even Rabbi Eliezer would say one.

### Shabbat Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַבָּה: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מְחַיֵּיב עַל וַלְדֵי מְלָאכוֹת בִּמְקוֹם אֲבוֹת מְלָאכוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to** the opinion of **Rabba** from a *baraita*: Each type of labor prohibited on Shabbat comprises acts that are primary categories of that labor and acts that are subcategories of that labor, both prohibited by Torah law. **Rabbi Eliezer deems** one **liable** to bring separate sin offerings **for** the performance of **subcategories** of prohibited **labor** on Shabbat **in a situation** where he also performed the corresponding **primary categories of** prohibited **labor** during the same lapse of awareness. For example, if one planted grain on Shabbat and then watered the field, which is a subcategory of the prohibited labor of planting, he is liable to bring two sin offerings.

Abaye challenges Rava from Rabbi Eliezer's known position on *Shabbat*: *Rabbi Eliezer mechayev al toldot melachot bimkom avot melachot* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that performing an *av melachah* and its *toldah* in one act still yields two *chatatot*, because they carry distinct labels. This is a *chiddush*. But it implies the converse: if two instances of the *same* *av melachah* were performed, *patur* — meaning one *chatat* only, not two. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אָב וְאָב, בְּבַת אַחַת – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** It may be inferred from this statement that: **But** if one performed **a primary category** of labor **and** then performed the same **primary category at the same time,** i.e., during the same lapse of awareness, he is **exempt** from bringing a second sin offering.

*Mar berei d'Ravina* and Rav Nachman bar Zechariah explain: the case of the same *av* twice incurring only one *chatat* refers to a specific scenario — *b'dalit ha-mudelet al gabi te'enah* — a vine wrapped around a fig tree, where both were cut in a single act. There it is literally one motion. The case of *av* and *toldah* incurring two *chatatot* is where it is a conceptually distinct type of action — such as cutting a fig (*kotzer*, to take its produce) while simultaneously pruning a vine for growth (*zeriah*, a derivative of planting). Those are different labels and different objects. But two instances of harvesting performed at the exact same moment remain one act.

## Vine & Fig

### Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְווֹתַהּ קָצַר וְקָצַר, הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ דְּמִיפְּטַר? כְּגוֹן שֶׁקָּצַר שְׁתֵּי גְּרוֹגְרוֹת בְּבַת אַחַת, אֲבָל קָצַר כִּגְרוֹגֶרֶת וְחָזַר וְקָצַר כִּגְרוֹגֶרֶת – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **In the corresponding** situation, where one **reaped** a fig-bulk of grain on Shabbat, which is the measure for which one is liable to bring a sin offering, **and** then **reaped** another fig-bulk in a single lapse of awareness, **how can you find** circumstances in **which he is exempt** from bringing a second sin offering? You find this in a situation **where he reaped two fig-bulks simultaneously,** in which case he performed only a single prohibited act. **But** if one **reaped a fig-bulk** of grain **and then reaped** another **fig-bulk,** he is **liable** to bring two sin offerings, as he performed two separate acts.

The case of *dalit ha-mudelet al gabi te'enah*: a vine is growing on a trellis around a fig tree, and one wishes to cut a fig to eat it while simultaneously pruning the vine to encourage growth. A single cut of the shears accomplishes both.

### Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם הָכִי מְחַיֵּיב רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, הוֹאִיל וְשֵׁמוֹת מוּחְלָקִין וְגוּפִין מוּחְלָקִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Due to that** reason **Rabbi Eliezer deems** him **liable** to bring two sin offerings, **since** it is a case of **differentiated names** of prohibitions, as he violated both the primary category of the labor and a subcategory, **and** it is also a case of **differentiated physical entities,** as he detached figs and part of a grapevine.

*Mishum hachi mechayev Rabbi Eliezer ho'il u'shemot muchlakim v'gufin muchlakim* — Rabbi Eliezer requires two *chatatot* here because two conditions are met: the *melachot* have distinct labels — *kotzer* for the harvest action and *zeriah* for the pruning action — and they are performed on distinct objects — the fig and the vine. Both *shemot muchlakim* and *gufin muchlakim* are present.

### Counter

The contrasting scenario: one cuts two apples from adjacent trees in a single motion. This is *kotzer v'kotzer* — the same *melachah* on different but similar objects. *Heichi mashkachat lah d'chavateih katzar v'katzar d'miptar* — the case of the same *melachah* twice where one would be exempt from multiple obligations is *katzar shtei grogerot b'vat achat* — harvesting two *grogoret*-sized quantities at the exact same moment. That is one act.

### Conclusion

*Aval katzar ke-grogeret v'chazar v'katzar ke-grogeret chayav* — but if one harvested one quantity and then harvested again separately, that is two distinct sequential acts and one is obligated for each. The conclusion: literal simultaneity — one motion, one instant — makes multiple objects part of one act. Sequential acts, even of the same type, remain distinct. For Rava, sequential distinct acts always matter and Rabbi Eliezer would require multiple *chatatot*. For Rav Yosef, Rabbi Eliezer's principle applies only to sex, where simultaneity is inherently impossible; by other *melachot*, acts that could theoretically have been simultaneous are treated as one even if they were not.

## Dangling Limb

### Limb Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְעוֹד שָׁאַל רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: אֵבֶר הַמְדֻולְדָּל בַּבְּהֵמָה, מַהוּ? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לֹא שָׁמַעְנוּ, אֲבָל שָׁמַעְנוּ אֵבֶר הַמְדֻולְדָּל בָּאָדָם שֶׁהוּא טָהוֹר, וְכָך הָיוּ
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And furthermore, Rabbi Akiva asked** Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua in the meat market of Emmaus: **What is** the status of **a dangling limb of an animal?** Does it impart ritual impurity like a severed limb? **They said to** Rabbi Akiva: **We have not heard** a ruling from our teachers in that specific case, **but we have heard** with regard to **a dangling limb of a person that it is ritually pure. And** in **this** manner **would**

The Gemara records a further question Rabbi Akiva posed, perhaps in the same context of the meat market. *Eiver hameduldal biveheimah mahu* — what is the status of a limb dangling from a living animal? Once a limb detaches from a living being, it is *eiver min hachai* and causes *tumah*. But if it has not yet fully detached — if it is still hanging by some connection — does it already carry the status of a detached limb for purposes of *tumah*?

### Human Case

*Amru lo lo shamanu* — no tradition about animals. *Aval shamanu: eiver hameduldal b'adam shehu tahor* — they did have a tradition that a limb dangling from a living person is *tahor*, even though once it fully detaches it would be *eiver min hachai* and *tamei*.

### Pesach Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכָךְ הָיוּ מוּכֵּי שְׁחִין עוֹשִׂין בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, הוֹלֵךְ לוֹ עֶרֶב פֶּסַח אֵצֶל הָרוֹפֵא, וְחוֹתְכוֹ עַד שֶׁמַּנִּיחַ בּוֹ כְּשַׂעֲרָה, וְתוֹחֲבוֹ בְּסִירָא, וְנִמְשָׁךְ מִמֶּנּוּ,
>
> **Translation:** the people **afflicted with boils,** whose limbs were dangling due to their affliction, **act in Jerusalem:** Each of them **would go on Passover eve to the doctor,** who **would cut** the affected limb almost completely **until he would leave** it connected **by a hairbreadth** of flesh, so that neither the doctor nor the afflicted would be rendered ritually impure by a severed limb. Then, the doctor would **impale** the limb **on a thorn** attached to the floor or the wall, **and** the afflicted **would pull away from** the thorn, thereby completely severing the limb.

*V'kach hayu mokei shechin osim biYerushalayim* — and this is what people with leprosy would do in Jerusalem. The scenario is specifically tied to *erev Pesach*. Such a person had a limb dangling. He wished to bring the *korban Pesach*, and he wanted to deal with his limb before *Pesach* without incurring *tumah*. So he would go *erev Pesach* to the doctor.

### Technique

*V'chotcho ad shemaniyach bo kisa'arah* — the doctor would cut nearly through the limb, leaving it attached by only a thread of skin. The doctor himself does not become *tamei* because the limb is not yet fully detached. Now the patient must sever that last thread without touching the limb — because if he holds it while pulling, he contacts the detached limb at the moment of separation. So *v'tochvo besirah* — he fixes the dangling limb to a wall with a nail or peg while it is still attached by its thread, and then *v'nimshach mimenu* — he pulls away from it, so that the limb is yanked off while he is not holding it. *Venimshach mimenu v'hala oseh pischo v'harofeh oseh pischo* — both the patient and the doctor can then go and bring their *korban Pesach*.

### Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַלָּה, עוֹשֶׂה פִּסְחוֹ וְהָרוֹפֵא עוֹשֶׂה פִּסְחוֹ, וְרוֹאֶה אֲנִי שֶׁהַדְּבָרִים קַל וָחוֹמֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **And that** person afflicted with boils **would perform** the rite of **his Paschal offering, and the doctor would perform** the rite of **his Paschal offering,** as neither had come into contact with the limb once it was severed. In any case, as long as it was dangling, the limb did not impart impurity. **And I consider that these matters** can be derived from **an *a fortiori*** inference. If a person's limb, the impurity of which when amputated is severe, does not impart impurity when it is dangling, it is all the more so logical that an animal's limb, the impurity of which when amputated is lenient, does not impart impurity when it is dangling.

*V'ro'im anu shehadvarim kal vachomer* — the sages offer a *kal vachomer*. A human being is a greater source of *tumah* than an animal. If a dangling limb from a human is *tahor*, how much more so must a dangling limb from an animal be *tahor*.

## Separation Tumah

### Leek Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תְּנַן הָתָם: הַמּוֹחֵק אֶת הַכְּרֵישָׁה, וְהַסּוֹחֵט בִּשְׂעָרוֹ וּבִכְסוּתוֹ – מַשְׁקִין שֶׁבְּתוֹכוֹ אֵינָם בְּ״כִי יוּתַּן״, וְהַיּוֹצְאִין מִמֶּנּוּ הֲרֵי הֵן בְּ״כִי יוּתַּן״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **We learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Makhshirin* 1:5): With regard to **one who** forcefully **wipes** rainwater from the surface of **a leek or squeezes out** water absorbed **in his hair or in his clothing, the liquid that** remains **within it is not** included **under** the rubric of the verse: **"If** water **be put** upon the seed, and some of their carcass fall on it, it is impure for you" (Leviticus 11:38). Therefore, it cannot render food susceptible to contracting ritual impurity. **But** the liquid **that is released from it,** in this process of wiping or squeezing, **is** included **under** the rubric of the verse: **"If** water **be put,"** and therefore it renders any food it touches susceptible to ritual impurity, as the person has accorded it significance.

*Tnan hatam*: the *mishna* elsewhere discusses a case of *hamochek et hakrisha* — one who squeezes a leek that has absorbed rainwater. Rainwater that has merely been absorbed — and not actively desired — does not yet have the status of a liquid that makes food susceptible to *tumah* (*be-ki yutan*). The liquid inside the leek is *mah shebetocho einah be-ki yutan* — it does not count. *V'yotzei mimenu harei hein be-ki yutan* — but as it is being squeezed out, it acquires liquid status. *Amar Shmuel: d'kalcha atzmah hukhsharah* — Shmuel adds that as the liquid comes out, it makes the leek itself susceptible to *tumah*, because at the moment of separation, while still in contact with the leek, it has already acquired its liquid status.

### Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: עֶרֶב פֶּסַח הוֹלֵךְ לוֹ אֵצֶל כּוּ׳. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁעַת פְּרִישָׁתָן הוּכְשַׁר, הָא אִיכָּא נָמֵי הָהוּא אֵבֶר הַמְדוּלְדָּל, בִּשְׁעַת פְּרִישָׁתוֹ מֵאָדָם – נְטַמְּיֵיהּ לְאָדָם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **But we learned** in the mishna: A person afflicted with boils **would go on Passover eve to** the doctor to sever his dangling limb, and the doctor and the afflicted individual would remain pure. **And if you say** that **at the moment of** the water's **separation** from the leek, the leek **is rendered susceptible** to ritual impurity, **there is also** a parallel situation in **that** case of the **dangling limb; at the moment of its separation from the person** with boils **it should render that person ritually impure.**

*B'sha'as perishah mimena hukhsharah* — the principle is that at the moment of separation the liquid activates and affects the object it is leaving. But how does that square with the *mishna*'s ruling about the dangling limb? If at the moment the dangling limb finally detaches from the person, should it not transmit *tumah* to him at that exact instant of separation?

### Force Factor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְקָאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: בְּנִיתָּזִין בְּכׇל כֹּחָן, הָכָא נָמֵי, בְּנִיתָּזִין בְּכׇל כֹּחָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The explanation is **as Rav Yosef says** in a different context, that it is referring **to** a situation where the liquids **are removed with great force. Here too,** in the case of the dangling limb, it can be explained as referring **to** a situation where the limbs **are removed with great force.** Since the limb is pulled off in one powerful motion it is not considered to have touched the person after it was detached. By contrast, the water wiped off the leek is not discharged all at once, and therefore as the drops of water separate from the leek they render it susceptible to ritual impurity.

*Ki d'amar Rav Yosef b'nitzin b'khol kokhan* — Rav Yosef resolves the contradiction by distinguishing on the basis of force. When liquid is *nitzin b'khol kokhan* — squirted out powerfully — it separates cleanly without lingering. There is no moment of extended contact at the boundary. When it is squeezed out slowly, the liquid adheres momentarily before falling away, and it is in that moment of adherence that it touches and activates.

### Application

The same principle applies to the limb. *Hacha nami b'nitzin b'khol kokhan* — the reason the yanking technique works is precisely because it is done forcefully. When the limb is yanked off sharply and quickly, the separation is clean, and there is no moment of lingering contact at the point of detachment. Therefore no *tumah* is transmitted at the moment of separation. Had the limb been removed slowly, the contact at the moment of detachment might well have transmitted *tumah*. This is why the technique prescribed is a forceful, sharp pull away from the wall.

## Zav Meis Case

### Rain Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְהֵיכָא אִיתְּמַר דְּרַב יוֹסֵף? אַהָא: זָב וּטְמֵא מֵת שֶׁהָיוּ מְהַלְּכִים וְיָרְדוּ עֲלֵיהֶם גְּשָׁמִים, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁסּוֹחֲטִין זֶה אֶת זֶה, מַשְׁקִין הַיּוֹרְדִין מִצַּד הָעֶלְיוֹן לְצַד הַתַּחְתּוֹן – טְהוֹרִים, שֶׁאֵין מְחַשְּׁבִין אֶלָּא שֶׁיָּצְאוּ מִכּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **And where,** i.e., in connection to which case, **was** this explanation **of Rav Yosef stated?** It was stated **with regard to this** *baraita*: If **a man who experiences a gonorrhea-like discharge [*zav*] and one** who is **ritually impure** through contact with **a corpse were walking** along **and rain fell on them, although they squeeze** the garments of **one another** in order to remove the water, those **liquids that flow down from the upper section** of a garment **to the lower section** remain **pure.** They do not become impure from contact with the impure person or his garments, **as these liquids **are not considered** liquids that can become ritually impure **unless they have emerged from** those garments **entirely.**

*Rav Yosef*'s statement about forceful separation was originally made in connection with a different *mishna*: the case of a *zav* or *tamei meis* — both primary sources of *tumah* — walking in the rain. Rain falls on their clothes, and the water becomes absorbed. The question is whether the absorbed water, once on their body, becomes *tamei* or gains liquid status to make other things susceptible. *Af al pi she-sokhtin mitzad ha-elyon l'tzad ha-tachton, tehorin* — even if they squeeze the water along their garment from top to bottom, the water running along the garment remains *tahor*.

### Full Separation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצְאוּ מִכּוּלָּן – הֲרֵי מַכְשִׁירִין, שֶׁאֵין נֶחְשָׁבִין אֶלָּא לְאַחַר שֶׁיָּצְאוּ מִגּוּפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Once the liquids **have emerged from** those garments **entirely they render** food **susceptible** to ritual impurity, as the people have accorded it significance by squeezing it out of their garments. But the water itself is still not impure, **as it is considered** liquid that is susceptible to ritual impurity **only after it has** completely **emerged from the body** of those garments.

*Yeitzu mikulan harei makhshirin* — only upon full departure does it become a liquid that can make other things susceptible or itself become *tamei*. This is the source for Rav Yosef's principle: complete separation is what activates status, not the boundary moment. *She'ein hukhshar ela l'achar she-yeitzu migufo* — only after full separation from the body. The apparent tension with the leek case, where status activates at the moment of separation, is what Rav Yosef resolves by distinguishing between forceful and gradual separation.

### Proof

The water only attains liquid status once it has completely separated from them — from their hair, their clothing, their body entirely. As long as it is running along their garments, it remains absorbed and has no status.

## Five Pots Case

### Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְעוֹד (שָׁאֲלוּ) [שְׁאֵלָן] רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: הַשּׁוֹחֵט חֲמִשָּׁה זְבָחִים בַּחוּץ בְּהֶעְלֵם (אַחַת) [אֶחָד] מַהוּ? חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת, אוֹ אַחַת עַל כּוּלָּן? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לֹא שָׁמַעְנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And furthermore, Rabbi Akiva asked** Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua: With regard to **one who** unwittingly **slaughters five offerings outside** the Temple **during one lapse of awareness, what is** the *halakha*? Is he **liable** to bring five sin offerings, one **for each and every** act of slaughter, **or** is he liable to bring **one** sin offering **for all** the acts of slaughter? **They said to** Rabbi Akiva: **We have not heard** a ruling from our teachers in that specific case.

*V'od sha'alan Rabbi Akiva* — Rabbi Akiva posed a further question. *Ha-shocheit chamishah zevachim bachutz b'he'elam achat* — someone slaughtered five different sacrificial animals outside the *Beit HaMikdash* in a single state of forgetfulness. *Mahu* — is it one *chatat* for all five, or a separate *chatat* for each? This is, in a sense, the mirror image of the previous question: where the earlier question was one person carrying multiple designations, here it is multiple animals but the same sin. The presumptive answer based on *gufim mechulakot* would be multiple *chatatot*. *Amru lo lo shamanu* — no tradition.

### R. Yehoshua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: שָׁמַעְתִּי בְּאוֹכֵל מִזֶּבַח אֶחָד מֵחֲמִשָּׁה תַּמְחוּיִין בְּהֶעְלֵם (אַחַת) [אֶחָד] שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת מִשּׁוּם מְעִילָה, וְרוֹאֶה אֲנִי שֶׁהַדְּבָרִים קַל וָחוֹמֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua said: I have heard with regard to one who eats** meat **from one offering from five** different **pots** in which they were prepared, **during one lapse of awareness, that he is liable** to bring separate guilt offerings **for** the meat prepared in **each and every** pot, **due to misuse** of consecrated property. **And I consider that these matters** can be derived from **an *a fortiori*** inference: If one is liable to bring five guilt offerings for one offering prepared in five pots, all the more so is he liable to bring five sin offerings for slaughtering five offerings outside the Temple.

*Amar Rabbi Yehoshua: shama'ti* — but Rabbi Yehoshua said he did have a tradition about an analogous eating case. *Be'ocheil mizevach echad meichamesh tanchuyin be'helem echad* — one who ate from a single sacrifice that had been prepared in five different ways — five different pots — in one state of forgetfulness, and since the blood had not yet been sprinkled this would be *me'ilah*, is *chayav al kol achat v'achat mishum me'ilah*. One sacrifice, five preparations, five separate *me'ilah* obligations. What is striking is that this is the same animal, the same sin, and the only distinction is that it was prepared in different ways. If that suffices for multiple *me'ilah* obligations, *ro'eh ani shehadvarim kal vachomer* — then surely five entirely distinct animals slaughtered outside should generate five obligations.

## Me'ilah Notar

### R. Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: לֹא כָּךְ שָׁאַל(ו) רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אֶלָּא בְּאוֹכֵל נוֹתָר מֵחֲמִשָּׁה זְבָחִים בְּהֶעְלֵם (אַחַת) [אֶחָד] מַהוּ? חַיָּיב אַחַת עַל כּוּלָּן, אוֹ חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאֶחָת? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לֹא שָׁמַעְנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Shimon said:** It was **not that** question that Rabbi Akiva **asked** them. **Rather,** it was **with regard to one who eats *notar* from five offerings during one lapse of awareness. What is** the *halakha*? Is he **liable** to bring **one** sin offering **for all** the offerings from which he ate *notar*, **or** is he **liable** to bring five sin offerings, one **for each and every one** of the offerings from which he ate *notar*? **They said to** Rabbi Akiva: **We have not heard** a ruling from our teachers in that specific case.

*Amar Rabbi Shimon: lo kasha'al Rabbi Akiva* — Rabbi Shimon clarifies that this was not actually the structure of Rabbi Akiva's question. Rabbi Akiva was not comparing slaughtering to eating; he was comparing eating to eating, and specifically asking about *notar* — the leftovers of a sacrifice that become forbidden once the time for consumption has passed.

### Refined Q

The refined question: *ela b'ocheil notar mechamesh zevachim b'helem echad* — one who ate *notar* from five different sacrifices in a single state of forgetfulness. *Mahu* — is it one *chatat* or five? This is precisely the question of how *gufim mechulakot* applies to sacrificial meat: are five sacrifices five distinct bodies the way five women are five distinct bodies, or is it all the same type of object and therefore one undivided act?

## Five Pots Case…

### R. Yehoshua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: שָׁמַעְתִּי בְּאוֹכֵל מִזֶּבַח אֶחָד בַּחֲמִשָּׁה תַּמְחוּיִין בְּהֶעְלֵם (אַחַת) [אֶחָד] שֶׁחַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת מִשּׁוּם מְעִילָה, וְרוֹאֶה אֲנִי שֶׁהַדְּבָרִים קַל וָחוֹמֶר – מָה מִזֶּבַח אֶחָד שֶׁאֵין גּוּפִין מוּחְלָקִין חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת מִשּׁוּם תַּמְחוּיִין מוּחְלָקִין, חֲמִשָּׁה זְבָחִים דְּגוּפִין מוּחְלָקִין לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua said: I have heard with regard to one who eats** meat **from one offering** prepared in five pots, **during one lapse of awareness, that** he is **liable** to bring a separate guilt offering **for** the meat prepared in **each and every** pot, **due to misuse** of consecrated property. **And I consider that these matters** may be derived from **an *a fortiori*** inference: **Just as** one who eats **from one offering** prepared in five pots, **in** which case the different parts of the offering do **not** come from **differentiated physical entities,** is **liable** to bring a separate guilt offering **for** the food prepared in **each and every** pot **because** he ate from **differentiated pots,** with regard to one who eats *notar* from **five** separate **offerings, which** are **differentiated physical entities,** is it **not all the more so** logical that he is liable to bring a separate sin offering for each and every offering?

### Kal Vachomer

If one sacrifice prepared five ways generates five obligations, then certainly five distinct sacrifices should generate five obligations, since they are even more physically distinct. The *kal vachomer* now operates within the same category of eating, not across categories.

## Me'ilah Notar…

### R. Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: אִם הֲלָכָה נְקַבֵּל, אִם לָדִין יֵשׁ תְּשׁוּבָה. אָמַר לוֹ: הָשֵׁב. אָמַר: וְלֹא אִם אָמַרְתָּ בִּמְעִילָה, שֶׁעָשָׂה בָּהּ אֶת הַמַּאֲכִיל כָּאוֹכֵל וְאֶת הַמְהַנֶּה כַּנֶּהֱנֶה,
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said to** Rabbi Yehoshua: **If** you are reporting **a *halakha*** that you received from your teachers, **we will accept it,** but **if** it is based merely **on** the *a fortiori* **inference** from misuse of consecrated property, **there is a response** that refutes the inference. Rabbi Yehoshua **said to** Rabbi Akiva: **Respond.** Rabbi Akiva **said:** And **no;** one cannot derive the *halakha* of *notar* through an *a fortiori* inference from misuse of consecrated property: **If you said with regard to misuse** of consecrated property that one is liable to bring five guilt offerings, perhaps that is because there are additional stringent elements unique to misuse. **As, with regard to** misuse, the Torah **established** that the status of **one who feeds** another person sacrificial meat is **like** that of **one who eats** sacrificial meat, **and** the status of **one who gives benefit** to another from consecrated property that is not food is **like** that of **one who derives benefit** himself, in that each is liable to bring a guilt offering for misuse.

*Amar lo Rabbi Akiva: im halacha nekabel* — Rabbi Akiva responds: if you have a tradition that eating *me'ilah* from one sacrifice five ways is five *chatatot*, I will accept it on its own terms. *Im ledin yesh teshuvah* — but if you want to reason logically, I have a response. The *kal vachomer* does not work, because *me'ilah* and *notar* are fundamentally different in their stringencies.

### Me'ilah Features

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צֵירֵף אֶת הַמְּעִילָה לִזְמַן מְרוּבֶּה, תֹּאמַר בְּנוֹתָר, שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ אַחַת מֵאֵלּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** In addition, the Torah **joined the misuse** of consecrated property that was performed **over an extended period,** i.e., if one derived benefit worth half a *peruta* one day and half a *peruta* the next, he is liable to bring a guilt offering for misuse. **Would you say** the same **with regard to *notar*, which has none of these** *halakhot*?

Three stringencies of *me'ilah* distinguish it from *notar*. First, *oseh ham'achal k'ochel* — the one who causes another to eat sanctified food is treated as if he himself ate it; even the feeder incurs *me'ilah*. Second, *v'hamahane k'nehane* — one who causes another to benefit from *hekdesh* is treated as if he himself benefited, even without eating. Third, *tziruf etzel me'ilah lizman merubeh* — by *me'ilah*, benefit that accrues over an extended period of time can combine toward the minimum measure for liability. This is unlike eating prohibitions, where the act must occur within the time of *khdei achilat pras*.

### Conclusion

Because of these stringencies, one could argue that *me'ilah* treats one sacrifice prepared five ways as five distinct acts of misuse, each with its own character. The law may be more stringent and more finely parsed with *me'ilah* than with other prohibitions. *Tomar b'notar she'ein bo achat me'elu* — but *notar* has none of these stringencies. Therefore one cannot infer from the *me'ilah* ruling that five sacrifices of *notar* yield five obligations. Perhaps five sacrifices of *notar* is still one act repeated five times with respect to one undivided prohibition.

## Summary

### Central Issue

The concluding discussion highlights the central conceptual problem of this entire sugya: how do we define *gufim mechulakot* as we move from the domain of people to the domain of animals to the domain of meat and sacrifices? When a man has sex with five different women, the distinctness of the acts is obvious — five people, five acts. But when one eats a *kezayit* of *cheilev* from one animal and later a *kezayit* of *cheilev* from a different animal, does that constitute *gufim mechulakot*? Or is it all *cheilev* — all the same act, the same sin, simply repeated?

### Piece vs. Piece

The question sharpens when applied to *notar*: is eating *notar* from one sacrifice distinct from eating *notar* from another sacrifice, the way sleeping with one woman is distinct from sleeping with another? Or is it all "eating leftover sacrificial meat," and the fact that the meat came from different animals no more creates *gufim mechulakot* than eating from different cuts of the same animal?

### Parameters

The entire discussion of five pots, five animals, *me'ilah* versus *notar*, hinges on where exactly the parameters of *gufim mechulakot* end. The question is left without a fully definitive resolution — the sugya has demonstrated the principle and tested its boundaries, but the extension from persons to animals to sacrificial meat to pieces of meat remains genuinely difficult and open to dispute.

## R. Akiva Method

### Sages' Approach

A pattern runs through the entire set of exchanges: the sages consistently respond *lo shamanu* or *shama'ti* — we have no tradition, or here is our tradition. Their engagement with the questions is through the medium of received knowledge. They do not reason from first principles or engage in independent logical construction; they either have a *mesorah* or they do not.

### R. Akiva

Rabbi Akiva's mode is conspicuously different. When Rabbi Yehoshua offers a tradition and builds a *kal vachomer* from it, Rabbi Akiva responds: *im halacha nekabel* — if you have a tradition, I accept it. But *im ledin yesh teshuvah* — if you want to argue logically, I have a counter-argument. He insists on reasoning through the cases independently, distinguishing *me'ilah* from *notar* on their logical merits, not merely deferring to whatever tradition happens to apply.

### Historical Shift

This represents something significant in the history of *Torah Shebe'al Peh*. Rabbi Akiva stands at the watershed between two modes of transmission: the older model in which knowledge is entirely a matter of *mesorah* — passed down, received, cited — and the newer model in which the scholar engages the tradition creatively, reasons independently, builds arguments, and challenges received applications with logical counter-proposals. Rabbi Akiva was not the only figure in this shift, but he exemplifies it sharply throughout this sugya.

### Two Trends

Both trends are legitimate and constitute authentic expressions of *Torah Shebe'al Peh*. One is the passive faithful transmission of what was received — *lo shamanu*, *shamanu*. The other is the active intellectual engagement with the material — *im ledin yesh teshuvah*. What the encounter between Rabbi Akiva and the sages in the meat market of Emmaus dramatizes is precisely that tension, and the recognition that both are part of what it means for Torah to be carried forward in each generation.
