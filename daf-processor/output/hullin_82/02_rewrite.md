# Hullin 82 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Parah Adumah & Eglah

### Parah Adumah Intro

The *daf* opens by continuing the *Gemara*'s examination of the *mishnah* that catalogues cases of *shechitah she'einah re'uyah* — slaughter that is not considered "fit" — a category debated by Rabbi Shimon and the *chachamim*. Having already explored the larger theoretical framework and addressed the case of one who slaughters for *avodah zarah*, the *Gemara* now turns to the next case in the *mishnah*: slaughtering the cow designated for use as a *parah adumah*. The *parah adumah* is a sanctified animal that is slaughtered, its blood is sprinkled toward the *Beis Hamikdash*, and it is then burned; its ashes are used for purification. The meat is not eaten — it is entirely burned. There is no permitted consumption and no benefit to be derived from it. On the face of it, then, slaughtering such an animal would seem to be a paradigmatic case of *shechitah she'einah re'uyah* according to Rabbi Shimon.

### Parah Status

The *Gemara* challenges this assumption at the bottom of 81b: *u'parat chatas shechitah she'einah re'uyah?* — Is slaughtering the *parah adumah* really an instance of unfit slaughter? The challenge is mounted from a *baraita* in which Rabbi Shimon himself states that the *parah adumah* is capable of becoming *tamei* as food — *tumat ochlim* — on the grounds that it had a *sha'at ha-kosher*, a moment when it was fit to be eaten. This is a striking claim. The *parah adumah* is sanctified, slaughtered, and burned. When could it possibly have been considered fit for consumption?

The answer requires understanding the *parah adumah*'s ambiguous halachic status. The *Gemara* elsewhere debates whether the *parah adumah* carries *kedushat mizbe'ach* — the sanctity of an altar offering, a genuine *korban* — or *kedushat bedek ha-bayis* — the sanctity of something dedicated to the Temple for its monetary value, not as a sacrifice. The distinction matters enormously for the question of redemption. A *korban* cannot be redeemed once slaughtered; redemption of a living unblemished sacrifice is itself prohibited. But something with *kedushat bedek ha-bayis* can be redeemed at any time, since the very purpose of such dedication is that the Temple will eventually sell or exchange it for its value.

### Redemption Theory

This is precisely what Reish Lakish teaches at the top of 82a in the name of Rabbi Shimon: *parah nifdeit al gabei ma'arachtah* — the *parah adumah* can be redeemed even after it has been slaughtered, even as it lies upon the wood of its pyre, right up until the moment it is actually burned. According to Rabbi Shimon, the *parah adumah* possesses enough of the character of *kedushat bedek ha-bayis* that it remains redeemable at every stage prior to burning. Were one to redeem it at that point — substituting a superior *parah adumah*, for instance — the slaughtered animal would no longer be sanctified, and since it was slaughtered *halachically*, its meat would become permissible to eat. It is this theoretical possibility of redemption that allows Rabbi Shimon to say the *parah adumah* can receive *tumat ochlim*: it had, at least in principle, a moment where it was fit to become food.

### Challenge to Status

This leads directly to the *Gemara*'s challenge. If the *parah adumah* can theoretically become edible through redemption, why does the *mishnah* classify slaughtering it as *shechitah she'einah re'uyah*? The theoretical path to edibility exists — so should the *shechitah* not be considered a qualified, fit slaughter? One might answer that there is a difference in standards: what counts as a *sha'at ha-kosher* for the purposes of *tumat ochlim* may be less demanding than what is required to make a *shechitah* count as *re'uyah* for Rabbi Shimon's principle. Indeed, as discussed in the preceding context, Rabbi Shimon holds that slaughtering a *korban* does not constitute *shechitah re'uyah* until the blood is sprinkled — the animal cannot be eaten until that further act is performed. By the same logic, the *parah adumah* cannot be eaten unless one additionally performs the act of redemption. Why, then, should the theoretical possibility of redemption suffice to make the *shechitah* fit? The *Gemara* does not offer this rejoinder, however. It treats the challenge as fully compelling.

### Parah Deleted

The *Gemara*'s resolution, delivered by Rabbi Shimon bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, is striking: *parat chatat einah mishnah* — the *parah adumah* does not belong in the *mishnah*. Cross it out. Because the *parah adumah* can theoretically become edible through redemption, its slaughter is, in fact, a *shechitah re'uyah* — a qualified slaughter — and Rabbi Shimon would not have included it as a case of *shechitah she'einah re'uyah*. Its presence in our *mishnah* is an error. The willingness of the *Gemara* to emend the text of the *mishnah* here is itself notable, particularly given that defensible answers to the challenge seem available.

### Eglah Status

The *Gemara* proceeds to the next case in the *mishnah*: the *eglah arufah*, the calf whose neck is broken in the valley when a murder victim is found outside a city and the killer is unknown. The *mishnah* treats slaughtering this calf — rather than breaking its neck in the prescribed manner — as another instance of *shechitah she'einah re'uyah*, implying that such a slaughter does not render the animal permissible to eat. The *Gemara* challenges this: *ve-eglah arufah lav shechitah re'uyah hi?* — Is it really true that slaughtering the *eglah arufah* yields unfit meat?

### Eglah Challenge

The challenge is drawn from a *mishnah* elsewhere: *nimtza ha-horeg* — if the murderer is found before the ritual is completed — *ad shelo te'aref ha-eglah, tetzei ve-tireh ba-eder* — if the calf's neck has not yet been broken, it goes back to the flock. The calf is not forbidden in *hana'ah* until the actual moment of neck-breaking. If this is so, and the animal retains its permissible status right up until the neck is broken, then slaughtering it rather than breaking its neck should render it entirely edible. Why, then, does the *mishnah* treat this as *shechitah she'einah re'uyah*?

### Yanai's Boundary

Just as Rabbi Yochanan crossed out the *parah adumah*, the *Gemara* introduces a teaching attributed to Reish Lakish in the name of Rabbi Yanai: *eglah arufah ein hamishna* — cross the *eglah arufah* out of the *mishnah* as well. If it is not forbidden until its neck is broken, then slaughtering it ought to be a valid *shechitah* and the meat ought to be permissible.

But the *Gemara* immediately questions whether Rabbi Yanai himself could have said this. For Rabbi Yanai is on record as having taught: *gvul shamati bah* — I have heard that there is a boundary, a point at which the *eglah arufah*'s status becomes irrevocably fixed, even before its neck is broken. He acknowledged forgetting the precise demarcation, but the assumption of the scholars — *nasvi chavraya le-meimar* — was that the descent of the calf to the *nachal eitan*, the hard wadi prescribed by the Torah as the site of the ritual, is what fixes its status. Once the calf is brought down into the wadi, it becomes defined as an *eglah arufah*, it is forbidden in benefit, and no subsequent *shechitah* can render it permissible.

### Before/After Descent

With this teaching in hand, the contradiction resolves cleanly. The *mishnah* that says the calf can return to the flock if the murderer is found — *ad shelo te'aref* — is speaking of a case before the calf has been brought down to the wadi. At that stage its status is not yet fixed and it remains permissible. The *mishnah* in our *perek* that treats slaughtering the *eglah arufah* as *shechitah she'einah re'uyah* is speaking of a case after the descent into the wadi, at which point its status as *eglah arufah* is fixed and it is forbidden in benefit. Once that threshold is crossed, *shechitah* cannot help. According to Rabbi Yanai, then, there is no problem with the *mishnah* — *kan kodem yeridah, kan le-achar yeridah*, here before the descent, here after the descent — and no need to cross anything out.

### Attribution

The *Gemara* therefore clarifies the attribution: *anan mishmeih de-Reish Lakish matninan lah* — the tradition held by Rav Pinchas the son of Rav Ami did not include Rabbi Yanai's name. The teaching that the *eglah arufah* must be crossed out of the *mishnah* was attributed directly to Reish Lakish himself, not to Rabbi Yanai. According to Rabbi Yanai, the *mishnah* presents no difficulty: once the calf descends to the wadi, its status is fixed. It is Reish Lakish who apparently holds that the *eglah arufah*'s status is never fixed prior to the actual breaking of the neck — meaning that *shechitah* at any earlier point would render the animal permissible — and it is therefore Reish Lakish who crossed it out of the *mishnah*.

### Ashi Questions

Rav Ashi now raises a pointed objection: *mi amar Reish Lakish hachi?* — Could Reish Lakish really maintain that the *eglah arufah*'s status is never fixed until the neck is broken? This seems difficult to reconcile with what Reish Lakish says elsewhere.

### Birds Precedent

The *Gemara* cites a teaching concerning the *tzipporei metzora* — the two birds used in the purification ritual of a person afflicted with *tzara'at*. One bird is slaughtered, its blood collected, and the other is dipped in the blood and sent free. The *Gemara* records a dispute: Rabbi Yochanan holds that the birds become forbidden in benefit only from the moment of *shechitah*, whereas Reish Lakish holds they become forbidden from the moment of *lekichah* — from the moment one takes possession of them for this ritual purpose, before any act of slaughter.

### Gezeirah Shava

The *Gemara* then asks: what is Reish Lakish's reason? The answer is that he applies a *gezeirah shava* — an analogy based on identical language — between the taking of the birds for the *metzora* ritual and the taking of the calf for the *eglah arufah*. Both are described with the word *kicha* — taking. Just as the *eglah arufah* becomes forbidden before its neck is broken — upon its descent to the wadi — so too the birds of the *metzora* become forbidden before they are slaughtered, from the moment of acquisition. This *gezeirah shava* presupposes that Reish Lakish agrees the *eglah arufah* does become forbidden before the neck is broken. His reasoning for the *tzipporei metzora* case depends upon it.

### Resolution

This completely undermines the earlier attribution. If Reish Lakish holds that the *eglah arufah* becomes forbidden upon descent to the wadi — which is precisely the basis of his *gezeirah shava* — he cannot be the one who said the *eglah arufah* must be crossed out of the *mishnah*. It must therefore be Rabbi Yochanan who crossed it out, operating on the view that the *eglah arufah* is not forbidden until its neck is actually broken. Just as Rabbi Yochanan crossed out the *parah adumah*, he also crossed out the *eglah arufah*. Rabbi Yochanan evidently holds that the *eglah arufah*'s status is not irrevocably fixed until the neck-breaking itself, which means that a prior *shechitah* would render the animal permissible — and the case therefore does not belong among the instances of *shechitah she'einah re'uyah*.

This concludes the *Gemara*'s treatment of *shechitah she'einah re'uyah* and its application to the *parah adumah* and *eglah arufah*. The *Gemara* then pivots to the next *mishnah*, which addresses a different set of questions arising from the prohibition of *oto ve-et beno*.

## Rights & Ownership

### First Buyer Right

The *mishnah* presents the following scenario: two people each purchase one of a mother-child pair of animals — say, one buys the mother and the other the offspring — from the same seller on the same day. Both intend to slaughter their animal that day. A question arises: who has the right to slaughter first? The *mishnah* answers: *eizeh she-lakach rishon yishchot rishon* — whoever made the first purchase is entitled to slaughter first.

### Seller Logic

Rashi explains the logic as follows. When the seller sold the first animal to the first buyer, there was an implicit understanding that the buyer could slaughter it immediately. The seller, at the moment of the first sale, would not simultaneously slaughter the related animal, since he was actively selling it — one does not undercut the utility of what one is selling. The first buyer therefore purchased his animal with an implicit entitlement to slaughter it that very day. When the second buyer subsequently purchased the other animal, he bought it knowing that the related animal already belonged to someone else who had that prior implicit right. The second buyer thus acquired no such entitlement to go first.

This case is fundamentally about regulatory entitlement, not about the prohibition of *oto ve-et beno* as an *issur*. As Rav Yosef will make explicit, the question of who has the right to slaughter first is a *din* — a matter of adjudicating rights — not a question of *issur ve-heter*. If the second buyer simply goes ahead and slaughters his animal before the dispute is resolved by a *beis din*, he has not violated any prohibition of *oto ve-et beno*. The first buyer cannot compel the second to wait by invoking a prohibition — rather, a court, if applied to, will enforce the first buyer's prior entitlement. The *issur* of *oto ve-et beno*, in its broader application to multiple parties and multiple acts of ownership, is a matter the subsequent *mishnah* will address; the present *mishnah* already takes that application as a given and focuses on the regulatory question of priority.

### Second Buyer Acts

What happens if the second buyer goes ahead and slaughters first? The *mishnah* states: *zakha* — he has what he has. His meat is his, he may eat it, and he has not transgressed the prohibition of *oto ve-et beno*. The first buyer is left in a difficult position — one of the pair has now been slaughtered on this day and he cannot slaughter the other. The second buyer acted out of turn according to the regulatory framework, but his action is not legally reversible and he faces no punishment.

### Regulatory Nature

The *Gemara* confirms this explicitly. Rav Yosef states: *le-inyan dina tenan* — what the *mishnah* is teaching here is about a *din*, a regulatory matter of entitlement. It is not a statement about the framework of *issur ve-heter*, the prohibition-and-permission structure of *oto ve-et beno* itself. The first buyer's priority is a right recognized by the court, not a prohibition binding the second buyer.

### Zariz Merit

A *Tosefta* elaborates on this theme in a striking way. If the second buyer acts first, *harei zeh zariz ve-niskar* — he is *zariz*, expeditious and eager, and he benefits. He is *zariz de-la avar issura* — his eagerness ensured that he would not transgress the prohibition, since he who slaughters first is never at risk of violating *oto ve-et beno*; it is only the one who slaughters second who faces that risk. And he is *niskar*, he profits, *de-ka-akhal bisra* — he gets to eat his meat today, without waiting. The *Tosefta*'s language here clearly describes the second buyer's preemptive act in positive terms — *zariz* being a word normally associated with zealousness in *mitzvot* — even while the underlying regulatory framework grants priority to the first buyer. The *Tosefta* is perhaps descriptive rather than prescriptive, cataloguing the practical advantages that accrue to the one who acts first, but it does create a somewhat mixed message.

## Order & Multiple Actors

### Mishnah Lashes

The next *mishnah* addresses permutations of the *oto ve-et beno* prohibition involving multiple acts and, implicitly, multiple actors. If someone slaughters the mother and then two of her offspring, he receives eighty lashes — two sets — because each child represents a distinct prohibition: "it and its child" for the first offspring, "it and its child" for the second. If he slaughters the two offspring first and then the mother, the *mishnah* says *safek arba'im* — he receives a doubtful forty lashes, meaning at most one set, since it is unclear whether slaughtering the two offspring and then the mother constitutes two prohibitions or one, but in any event because it is one act, only one set of lashes is given. This ruling implicitly demonstrates that the order — mother-child or child-mother — makes no difference to the existence of the prohibition.

### Three Generations

The *mishnah* continues with a further scenario. If one slaughters a grandmother, then a grandchild, and then the middle generation — the mother — the count is: grandmother then mother yields one violation of "it and its child," and the middle cow then grandchild yields a second violation of "it and its child." Two separate prohibitions were committed in a single act, yielding *safek shemonim* — a doubtful eighty lashes. Slaughtering the grandmother and the grandchild alone, before the middle generation, creates no violation; the grandmother and grandchild do not stand in a direct parent-child relationship to each other. It is only the slaughter of the middle cow that triggers two simultaneous violations, as she is both a child of the grandmother and a mother of the grandchild.

### Sumchus/Meir

Sumchus, in the name of Rabbi Meir, rules that in the last case described — where the middle cow is both child and mother — one receives *safek shemonim*, two sets of lashes, because two distinct prohibitions were committed. This is the position of Sumchus even though both violations were accomplished in a single act with no interruption between them.

### Plural Analysis

The *Gemara* then turns to a foundational textual question raised by the *mishnah*'s implicit assumption that order is irrelevant and that different actors can trigger the prohibition. The Torah uses the formulation *lo tishchatu* — "you [plural] shall not slaughter" — rather than the singular *lo tishchat*. The *Gemara* asks: why the plural?

### Order Interpretation

The *Gemara* cites a *baraita*: *oto ve-et beno* — "it and its child" — might suggest only the order of mother first, then child. How do we know that child first, then mother, is equally prohibited? The verse says *lo tishchatu* — you plural shall not slaughter — indicating, according to the *baraita*, *harei kan shnayim* — there are two modes of slaughter, two orders, each independently prohibited. The plural subject implies two different types of actors, each performing one of the two possible orderings, thereby teaching that both mother-child and child-mother are prohibited. The last actor who completes the pair — *shnayim acharonim chayavim*, the last of the two to slaughter — is the one who transgresses.

### Multiple Actors

With the order question addressed, the *Gemara* pivots to the next implication of the plural. If the Torah had simply meant to prohibit one person from slaughtering both animals, it would have used the singular. The plural *lo tishchatu* implies that even where two separate people are involved — one slaughters the mother, another slaughters the child — the prohibition still applies. *Lo tishchatu va-afilu trei* — "you plural shall not slaughter" encompasses even two people acting in sequence.

### Primary Function

The *Gemara* refines the analysis. If the plural's purpose were only to teach that the order doesn't matter, the Torah could have achieved that differently. What the plural in the active voice most directly communicates is precisely that two different actors can trigger the prohibition between them — this is the primary implication of "you [plural] shall not slaughter." The order-irrelevance teaching is then derived alongside it.

### Passive Alternative

How would the Torah have expressed order-irrelevance without implying two actors? The *Gemara* suggests: *lo yishachetu* — the passive voice, "they shall not be slaughtered on the same day." A passive formulation would have taught that regardless of who is doing the slaughtering — one person or two — simply ensuring that both animals are not slaughtered on the same day is the requirement, without specifying anything about the actor. Rashi's reading confirms this: *lo yishachetu d'mashma lo al yedei echad ve-lo al yedei shnayim lo yihu nishchatim* — the passive would mean it doesn't matter whether it's done by one or by two, they simply shall not be slaughtered. Because the Torah instead used the active plural *tishchatu*, it teaches specifically that two actors together can transgress the prohibition. From *shema mina tartei* — we derive both teachings from the one formulation: order is irrelevant, and two separate actors can each bear responsibility.

## Same Prohibition Lashes

### Abaye Questions

Abaye poses a question to Rav Yosef: what is the underlying rationale of Sumchus's ruling that in the case of the middle cow — who is both mother and child — one receives two sets of lashes?

### Same Sin Doubled

The first possibility Abaye raises is that Sumchus holds the most expansive position: *achal shenei zeitei chelev be-helem echad chayav shtei chataot* — if a person ate two *kezaytim* of forbidden fat (*chelev*) in a single state of unawareness, without any interruption, he would be obligated to bring two sin offerings rather than one. Ordinarily, the rule is that a single act of forgetting, encompassing multiple violations of the same prohibition, generates only one obligation. Separate obligations arise only when the person remembered between acts and then forgot again, or when he was warned between acts. Sumchus, on this reading, would hold that even without any such interruption — even when the same prohibition is doubled within one continuous act — each iteration generates its own obligation for lashes or a sin offering. Under this view, it would not matter whether the doubled act involves conceptually distinct violations or the very same violation repeated; repetition alone is sufficient to multiply the consequence.

### Objection

If this is Sumchus's position, however, the *Gemara* points out an immediate difficulty. Sumchus should have stated his ruling in the first case of the *mishnah* as well — the case where the mother and two children are slaughtered. There, the violator slaughters one child (one violation) and then another child (the same violation again), and only then the mother. That is the exact same prohibition doubled — "it and its child" committed twice in succession with respect to the same mother. If Sumchus holds that even identical prohibitions doubled without interruption yield multiple obligations, he should have intervened there.

### Conceptual Unity

The *Gemara* answers that the Rabbis in the first case exempt for a specific reason: conceptually, the two violations are one thing done twice. It is "the mother of animal A" and "the mother of animal B" — the same relationship, the same prohibition, applied to the same mother animal. The Rabbis say that because it is the same prohibition with the same subject done twice, it is treated as one act generating one obligation. In the last case of the *mishnah*, by contrast, the middle cow is simultaneously two conceptually distinct things — she is a child with respect to the grandmother, and a mother with respect to the grandchild. Even the Rabbis might have recognized those as two distinct violations. Yet even there, the Rabbis exempt from a second set of lashes. Sumchus chose to articulate his position in the last case — where the Rabbis' exemption is most surprising — in order to reveal the full strength of the Rabbis' contrary position: even when the violations are conceptually distinct (*gufin mechulakin*), the Rabbis still say one set of lashes.

### Kilayim Proof

Rav Yosef asserts that Sumchus does in fact hold the more extreme position — that even the same prohibition doubled without interruption can yield two obligations — and seeks to prove this from a *mishnah* about *kilayim*. The *mishnah* states that one who plants *kilayim kilayim* — *kilayim* twice — receives lashes. The double language suggests two sets of lashes. If this referred merely to sequential acts with a warning between them, it would be obvious and unremarkable; that principle is already established elsewhere regarding a *nazir* who drinks wine repeatedly, where each act following a warning generates its own set of lashes. The *mishnah*'s use of the doubled *kilayim kilayim* must therefore be conveying something additional — namely, that even simultaneous or uninterrupted planting of *kilayim* twice yields two sets of lashes, thus proving the extreme position.

### Kilayim Reinterpretation

The *Gemara* rejects this reading of the *kilayim mishnah*. The doubling of *kilayim kilayim* is not teaching anything about multiple sets of lashes for repeated acts. It is telling us, *le-apokei mi-divrei Rabbi Yoshiya*, to exclude the position of Rabbi Yoshiya: Rabbi Yoshiya holds that to transgress *kilei ha-kerem*, the prohibition of mixed species in a vineyard, one must plant wheat, barley, and a grape seed together — a double mixture. The *mishnah* teaches that there are in fact two independent ways to transgress *kilei ha-kerem*: wheat with a grape seed (*chartzan*), or barley with a grape seed. These are two distinct forms of *kilayim*, and each independently constitutes a violation. The repeated language conveys this typological point, not a claim about multiple lashes for repeated acts.

### Gid HaNasheh

The *Gemara* then seeks proof from the laws of *gid ha-nasheh*, the forbidden sinew of the thigh. There is a dispute about whether the prohibition applies to the sinew of one thigh or both. If it applies to both, and a person eats a *kezayit* from the sinew of one thigh and a *kezayit* from the sinew of the other, the Rabbis say he receives eighty lashes — two sets. Rabbi Yehuda says forty — one set — because only one thigh is actually forbidden, though he does not know which one.

### Simultaneous Case

The *Gemara* establishes that this case must be simultaneous — both *kezaytim* eaten at once. If the eating were sequential, with a warning between each act, Rabbi Yehuda's position would be inexplicable: why would he deny lashes when the person has undeniably eaten the forbidden sinew, even if sequentially? Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning is that *hatra'at safek* — a doubtful warning — is not a valid warning, and since at each sequential act one cannot know with certainty whether that particular sinew is the prohibited one, no warning can be definitive. This position — that a doubtful warning does not count as a warning — is attested independently in the name of Rabbi Yehuda from a *baraita* about a child who strikes or curses two putative fathers when paternity is uncertain: *bezeh achar zeh patur*, Rabbi Yehuda exempts him from punishment if done sequentially, though *be-vat achat chayav*, if done simultaneously he is liable. Thus the case of *gid ha-nasheh* must involve eating both *kezaytim* simultaneously.

### Rabbis' Position

On this reading, the Rabbis who say two sets of lashes are ruling in a case where the same prohibition — eating the forbidden sinew — is doubled simultaneously in a single act. This would constitute proof that there exists a position, namely that of the Rabbis, holding that even the same prohibition doubled yields two obligations.

### Sequential Counter

The *Gemara* deflects this proof. The case need not be simultaneous at all. *Le-olam bezeh achar zeh* — it can indeed be sequential, and the Rabbis award two sets of lashes in the sequential case because there were two separate warnings and two separate acts. This is wholly unproblematic and requires no extreme position about simultaneous identical violations.

### Doubtful Warning

Under this sequential reading, however, Rabbi Yehuda's exemption requires explanation. If the acts were sequential with warnings between them, and the person clearly ate the forbidden sinew by the end of the sequence, why does Rabbi Yehuda deny lashes? The *Gemara* resolves this by pointing to an alternative tradition of Rabbi Yehuda: *hatra'at safek shmah hatra'ah* — a doubtful warning does count as a warning. According to this second opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, since at each sequential act the warning was uncertain — one did not know definitively which sinew was the prohibited one — the warning is nevertheless effective, and Rabbi Yehuda would award lashes even in the sequential case, though only one set, since he holds only one sinew is actually forbidden. The discussion of whether the extreme position of Sumchus can be proven from these sources — that even the same act doubled in one continuous moment yields two obligations — remains open and will be taken up in the subsequent *daf*.
