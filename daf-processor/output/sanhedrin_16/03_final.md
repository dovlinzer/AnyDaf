# Sanhedrin 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wild Animals & Beis Din

### Opening & Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאֲרִי וְהַזְּאֵב כּוּ׳. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: וְהוּא שֶׁהֵמִיתוּ, אֲבָל לֹא הֵמִיתוּ – לָא. אַלְמָא קָסָבַר: יֵשׁ לָהֶן תַּרְבּוּת וְיֵשׁ לָהֶן בְּעָלִים. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא הֵמִיתוּ. אַלְמָא קָסָבַר: אֵין לָהֶם תַּרְבּוּת וְאֵין לָהֶם בְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna records a dispute with regard to the judgment of **a lion or a wolf:** Does this judgment require twenty-three judges? **Reish Lakish says: And that** dispute concerns a lion or wolf **that has killed** a person. **But** if **they have not killed,** then **no,** they may not be executed. **Apparently,** Reish Lakish **holds** that **they have** the capability of being **tamed** and domesticated, **and** consequently **they** might **have owners,** so it is not permitted to kill them without due cause. And **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The dispute applies **even if they have not killed. Apparently,** Rabbi Yoḥanan **holds** that **they do not have** the capability of being **tamed, and** therefore **they do not have owners.**

Daf 16 opens in the middle of a discussion that begins on 15b, concerning the case of *ha'ari vehaze'ev* — the lion and the wolf. The Gemara has been treating the *shor haniskal*, the ox condemned to stoning, as requiring a *beis din* of twenty-three. This leads the Mishnah to extend the discussion to wild animals more broadly, a topic also addressed in Bava Kamma.

### R. Eliezer Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַקּוֹדֵם לְהוֹרְגָן זָכָה. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, לְמַאי זָכָה? זָכָה לְעוֹרָן. אֶלָּא לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, לְמַאי זָכָה? כֵּיוָן שֶׁהֵמִיתוּ, שַׁוִּינְהוּ רַבָּנַן כְּמַאן דִּגְמַר דִּינַיְיהוּ, וְאִיסּוּרֵי הֲנָאָה נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: **Rabbi Eliezer says: Anyone who kills them first merits. Granted, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, what does he merit?** It can be explained that he **merits,** i.e., acquires, **their hides;** since these animals are by definition ownerless, whoever kills them first may take the hide for himself. **But according to** the opinion of **Reish Lakish, what** does **he merit? Since** according to Reish Lakish the mishna is discussing a case where **they have** already **killed, the Sages made their** status **equivalent to those whose verdicts have been issued** in court, **and** oxen that are stoned **are items from which** deriving **benefit is prohibited.**

The Mishnah presents two views. Rabbi Eliezer says: *kol hakodem lehorgam zachah* — whoever kills these dangerous animals first has thereby merited. Rabbi Akiva says: *misasan be-beis din shel sheloshah* — their death requires a court of three. The underlying premise of Rabbi Eliezer's opinion is that these creatures — lions, bears, and similarly fierce animals — are not like an ox that has gored three times and thus acquired a dangerous history. They are fundamentally, inherently dangerous, and therefore one need not wait for a *beis din* at all.

### Reish Lakish Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״זָכָה״? זָכָה לַשָּׁמַיִם. תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אֶחָד שׁוֹר שֶׁהֵמִית וְאֶחָד בְּהֵמָה וְחַיָּה שֶׁהֵמִיתוּ – בְּעֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: שׁוֹר שֶׁהֵמִית – בְּעֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה, וּשְׁאָר בְּהֵמָה וְחַיָּה שֶׁהֵמִיתוּ – כׇּל הַקּוֹדֵם לְהוֹרְגָן זָכָה בָּהֶן לַשָּׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **What** is the meaning of: **Merits,** according to Reish Lakish? It means that **he merits according to Heaven,** meaning that he has performed a mitzva by destroying a harmful animal. The Gemara relates: It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Reish Lakish**: **Both an ox that killed and** a different **domesticated animal or undomesticated animal that killed** are sentenced **by twenty-three** judges. **Rabbi Eliezer says: An ox that killed** is sentenced **by twenty-three** judges, **but** with regard to **other domesticated animals and undomesticated animals that killed, anyone who kills them first merits, with that** act, **according to Heaven.**

Reish Lakish qualifies: *vehu shehemitu* — this dispensation to kill without a court applies only if the animal has already killed. If the animal has not yet taken a human life, one may not simply destroy it. The underlying rationale, the Gemara explains, is *al-ma kasavar yesh lahem tarbus veyesh lahem be'alim* — Reish Lakish holds that these animals can, in principle, be domesticated. It is exceedingly difficult, but it is possible to keep a lion or a bear as a pet, and therefore such animals can have owners. Until they have demonstrated by actually killing that they are reverting to their wild nature, an owner's rights must be respected and one cannot simply kill the animal.

Once the animal has killed, however, its danger is considered to exceed even that of a *shor haniskal*, and one may proceed to kill it without waiting for a court proceeding.

### R. Yochanan Position

Rabbi Yochanan takes a more sweeping position: *af al pi she-lo hemitu* — even if the animal has not yet killed, one may go ahead and kill it. *Al-ma kasavar ein lahem tarbus ve-ein lahem be'alim* — Rabbi Yochanan holds that these animals cannot truly be domesticated, and therefore no one is genuinely their owner in any meaningful legal sense. At their core they are wild, and killing them is simply a service to society.

### Merit Explained

This dispute generates a precise question about what Rabbi Eliezer means when he says the first to kill *zachah* — merits. The Gemara works this out according to each position. According to Rabbi Yochanan, the answer is straightforward: *zachah le-oran* — the killer merits the hide of the animal. Since the animal cannot truly be owned, the one who kills it acquires its skin, an established principle that the killer of such an animal keeps its hide. This provides a clear incentive to perform the socially beneficial act of eliminating a dangerous creature.

According to Reish Lakish, however, the question is sharper. Since Reish Lakish maintains that these animals do have owners, killing the animal would normally leave the skin with the owner, not the killer. So what does the first killer merit? The Gemara answers: *kevan shehemitu* — since Reish Lakish requires that the animal have already killed before it may be slain, we are in effect treating these animals within the conceptual framework of a *shor haniskal*. And a *shor haniskal* is forbidden in benefit — *assur be-hana'ah*. Consequently, the killer cannot acquire the hide as property. *Mai zachah?* What then does one merit? *Zachah lishmaya* — one merits the spiritual reward of having performed a *mitzvah*.

### Tanya & Reish Lakish

A *baraisa* is then cited in support of Reish Lakish's position: *echad shor shehemit ve-echad behemah vechayah shehemitu be-esrim ushloshah* — both an ox that killed and other domesticated or wild animals that killed require a court of twenty-three. Rabbi Eliezer adds, in a notably expansive formulation, that for all animals other than an ox — including any *behemah* or *chayah* — once they have killed, *kol hakodem lehorgam zachah bahen lishmaya*, whoever kills them first merits, and that merit is explicitly *lishmaya*, meaning one does not acquire the hide. The *tanya* thus confirms the Reish Lakish position, and also broadens the category considerably beyond what was initially stated.

### R. Akiva & Beis Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר וְכוּ׳. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הַיְינוּ תַּנָּא קַמָּא? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ נָחָשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states that with regard to these dangerous animals, **Rabbi Akiva says:** Their death is decreed by twenty-three judges. The Gemara asks: The opinion of **Rabbi Akiva is** identical to the opinion of the **first *tanna*;** what is the novelty of his statement? The Gemara answers: The difference **between them** relates to the *halakha* concerning **a snake.**

Rabbi Akiva's position is that even these animals require a *beis din* of twenty-three. The Gemara asks: is this not identical to the view of the *tana kamma* of the Mishnah? The answer is that there is a practical difference between them regarding a snake. The *tana kamma* would require a *beis din* of twenty-three even for a snake, whereas Rabbi Akiva — consistent with other *mishnayos* in Bava Kamma — might concede that a snake, being particularly and distinctively lethal, does not require a court at all.

## Shevet & Avodah Zarah

### Shevet Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין דָּנִין כּוּ׳. הַאי שֵׁבֶט דַּחֲטָא בְּמַאי? אִילֵימָא שֵׁבֶט שֶׁחִלֵּל אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת, אֵימַר דְּפַלֵּיג רַחֲמָנָא בֵּין יְחִידִים לִמְרוּבִּין לְעִנְיַן עֲבוֹדַת כּוֹכָבִים, בִּשְׁאָר מִצְוֹת מִי פְּלִיג? אֶלָּא בְּשֵׁבֶט שֶׁהוּדַּח.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that the court **judges** cases involving an entire tribe that sinned only in the Great Sanhedrin, consisting of seventy-one judges. The Gemara asks: With regard to **this tribe that sinned, in what** way did it sin? **If we say** that it was **a tribe that** transgressed most prohibitions, e.g., they **desecrated Shabbat,** this is difficult. There is a source to **say that the Merciful One distinguishes between individuals and multitudes with regard to idol worship,** as there is a special *halakha* with regard to an idolatrous city. But **with regard to other mitzvot, does He distinguish** between individuals and multitudes? **Rather,** it must be that the mishna is discussing **a tribe that was subverted** and which engaged in idol worship.

The Mishnah teaches that a *shevet* — an entire tribe of Israel — that sins requires a *beis din* of seventy-one to adjudicate the matter. The Gemara now seeks to understand this ruling in detail.

### Tribe's Sin

The first question is: *hai shevet dechata be-mai?* — in what sin did this tribe engage? The Gemara considers whether an ordinary transgression, such as *chilul Shabbos*, could be the subject of this ruling. The answer is that the Torah distinguishes between individuals and multitudes specifically in the context of *avoda zara*, idolatry — that is the realm in which the Torah treats a community as a corporate religious entity rather than a mere aggregation of individuals. By other *mitzvos*, the Torah does not apply this communal framing. Therefore the tribe in question must have committed *avoda zara*.

### Ir Hanidachas Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דִּבְדִינָא דְּרַבִּים דָּיְינִינַן לֵיהּ? כְּמַאן: לָא כְּרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה וְלָא כְּרַבִּי יוֹנָתָן! דְּתַנְיָא: עַד כַּמָּה עוֹשִׂין עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת? מֵעֲשָׂרָה וְעַד מֵאָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה. רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן אוֹמֵר: מִמֵּאָה וְעַד רוּבּוֹ שֶׁל שֵׁבֶט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that we judge** such a tribe **with the *halakha* of a multitude,** meaning that an entire idolatrous tribe is subject to the same treatment as an idolatrous city? If so, in accordance **with whose** opinion is the mishna? It is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoshiya, and not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yonatan. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **How many** people in a city must engage in idol worship for it to be **designated** as **an idolatrous city? From ten until one hundred** people; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yoshiya. Rabbi Yonatan says: From one hundred** people **until a majority of a tribe.**

Having established that the tribe committed *avoda zara*, the Gemara considers the obvious interpretation: the tribe is simply being treated as a large *ir hanidachas*, a wayward city, and since *ir hanidachas* requires a court of seventy-one, a tribe that similarly commits mass idolatry would also require seventy-one. But the Bavli presses further and demonstrates that this reading is not only obvious — it is in fact legally impossible. The *ir hanidachas* framework cannot actually apply to a whole tribe, because there are size limits beyond which a city loses its status as an *ir hanidachas*.

### City Size Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן לָא קָאָמַר אֶלָּא רוּבּוֹ, אֲבָל כּוּלּוֹ לָא. אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה: הָכָא
>
> **Translation:** **And** it is therefore apparent that **even Rabbi Yonatan,** who allows a larger community to be declared an idolatrous city, **said** this **only** with regard to **a majority of** the tribe at most, **but** if it was **all of** the tribe that was idolatrous, then the *halakha* of an idolatrous city does **not** apply.

A *baraisa* records a debate between Rabbi Yoshiya and Rabbi Yonasan. Rabbi Yoshiya holds that an *ir hanidachas* must have between ten and one hundred inhabitants. A community smaller than ten or larger than one hundred does not qualify. Rabbi Yonasan holds that the range extends from one hundred up to the majority of a tribe, *rubo shel shevet* — but once the city encompasses a majority of a tribe, it is no longer treated as a city at all, and reverts to the status of individuals who happened to commit *avoda zara*. Tosafos notes that the figures of ten and one hundred appear in a verse in Amos.

Critically, even Rabbi Yonasan only goes as far as a majority — *rubo*. If it is literally the entire *shevet*, he would not apply the *ir hanidachas* model at all. One might invoke the principle of *rubo ke-kulo*, majority as equivalent to totality, but a literal entire tribe would fall outside Rabbi Yonasan's formulation. The result is a pointed irony: the case of the whole tribe committing *avoda zara* would paradoxically revert to being judged as individuals, which is clearly not what the Mishnah intends. The Gemara therefore rejects the *ir hanidachas* model entirely as the basis for the Mishnah's ruling, and seeks alternative explanations.

### Rav Masnah Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּנְשִׂיא שֵׁבֶט שֶׁחָטָא עָסְקִינַן. מִי לָא אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: ״כׇּל הַדָּבָר הַגָּדֹל יָבִיאוּ אֵלֶיךָ״ – דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל גָּדוֹל? הַאי נָמֵי גָּדוֹל הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **we are dealing with the *Nasi* of a tribe who has sinned. Doesn't Rav Adda bar Ahava say:** The verse states: **"They shall bring every great matter to you"** (Exodus 18:22), meaning: **Matters of a great one,** i.e., in any case where a great person is accused of a transgression whose punishment is death, he is tried by the Great Sanhedrin of seventy-one judges. And **this** *Nasi* of the tribe **is also a great one,** so his trial is by seventy-one judges.

Rav Masnah offers the first answer: *hacha be-nasi shevet she-chata askinan* — the Mishnah is dealing not with the entire tribe but with the *nasi* of the tribe, its leader, who sinned.

### Moses as Source

The basis for this is drawn from the principle already stated elsewhere by Rabbi Adda bar Ahava: the verse in Yisro says *kol hadavar hagadol yavi'u eilecha* — every great matter shall be brought to you, Moses. This is read as *devarav shel gadol* — matters pertaining to a great person. Moses represents the *beis din* of seventy-one, so matters involving a great personage go to a court of seventy-one. Accordingly, the head of a tribe — a *nasi* — is such a great personage, and any capital matter involving him goes before the *sanhedrin* of seventy-one.

### Ulla: Boundaries

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: בְּבָאִין עַל עִסְקֵי נְחָלוֹת, וְכִתְחִילָּתָהּ שֶׁל אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל. מָה תְּחִילָּתָהּ שִׁבְעִים וְאֶחָד, אַף כָּאן – שִׁבְעִים וְאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said** that **Rabbi Elazar** said a different explanation: The mishna discusses a case **where** two tribes **came** to adjudicate a dispute **about matters of inheritance,** i.e., territory claimed by each tribe on the border between them, **and** this dispute is handled by the Great Sanhedrin consisting of seventy-one judges, **as** was done at **the beginning of** the settlement in **Eretz Yisrael** during the time of Joshua. **Just as the beginning,** the initial division, was performed by **seventy-one** Elders of the congregation, **so too here,** when there is a dispute about the borders determined by that initial division, the case is adjudicated by the **seventy-one** judges of the Great Sanhedrin.

Ulla, citing Rabbi Elazar, offers a second answer: the Mishnah refers to disputes *al iskei nachalos*, concerning tribal inheritances and the boundary lines of the land of Israel. When *shevatim* dispute which territory belongs to whom, a court that stands above the *shevatim* is required, and since the original apportionment of the land was conducted with a *beis din* of seventy-one, boundary disputes among tribes similarly require seventy-one. The Gemara then pushes back: if we are reconstructing the original division, should we not also require the *kalfi* — the lottery — the *urim ve-tumim*, and the presence of all of Israel? That would obviously be absurd. The Gemara therefore rejects the extreme form of this argument and concludes that Rav Masnah's formulation is the cleaner and more well-established answer.

### Ravina Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם בְּשֵׁבֶט שֶׁהוּדַּח, וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ – בְּדִינָא דְּרַבִּים דָּיְינִינַן לֵיהּ? אִין, אַף עַל גַּב דְּקָטְלִינַן בְּיָחִיד, בֵּי דִינָא דְּרַבִּים דָּיְינִינַן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said:** The mishna is **actually** discussing **a tribe that was subverted** and which engaged in idol worship, **and** with regard to that **which poses a difficulty for you,** the question of whether **we judge** such a tribe **with the *halakha* of a multitude,** it can be answered: **Yes, although we execute** them **as individuals** by stoning, and their money is not confiscated, nevertheless **we judge them** in **a court of the multitude,** i.e., each one of them is tried by the Great Sanhedrin.

Ravina offers a third answer: *le-olam be-shevet she-huda'ach* — in fact the Mishnah does refer to a tribe that committed *avoda zara*. And the earlier objection — that we cannot treat them as a corporate entity because they would not qualify as an *ir hanidachas* — is met as follows. *In* — yes, indeed we are judging them *bedina de-rabim*, with the framework applicable to a mass collective. *Af al gav de-katlinan be-yachid* — even though the actual mode of execution is as individuals, not as a collective destruction of a city, nevertheless the *type of court* that hears the case is the court that would apply to a corporate entity. The judgment is individual; the court is communal.

### R. Chama Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי לָא אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: ״וְהוֹצֵאתָ אֶת הָאִישׁ הַהוּא אוֹ אֶת הָאִשָּׁה הַהִוא וְגוֹ׳״? אִישׁ וְאִשָּׁה אַתָּה מוֹצִיא לִשְׁעָרֶיךָ, וְאִי אַתָּה מוֹצִיא כָּל הָעִיר כּוּלָּהּ לִשְׁעָרֶיךָ. הָכָא נָמֵי: אִישׁ וְאִשָּׁה אַתָּה מוֹצִיא לִשְׁעָרֶיךָ, וְאִי אַתָּה מוֹצִיא כׇּל הַשֵּׁבֶט כּוּלּוֹ לִשְׁעָרֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina continues: **Doesn't Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Yosei, say** that **Rabbi Oshaya says:** The verse that discuses an individual who serves idol worship states: **"And you shall take out that man or that woman** who did that evil thing to your gates, even the man or the woman, and you shall stone them until they die" (Deuteronomy 17:5), and it is inferred: **You take out a man or a woman to your gates** to judge them in the court that is located at the gates of the city, which is a lesser Sanhedrin, **but you do not take out an entire city to your gates;** rather they are to be judged by the large court. **Here also** with regard to a tribe that has sinned: **You take out a man or a woman to your gates, but you do not take out the entire tribe to your gates;** rather they are judged by a court of seventy-one.

This is supported by a teaching of Rabbi Chama bar Yosei citing Rabbi Oshaya, which explains how we know that an *ir hanidachas* requires a *beis din* of seventy-one. The verse concerning *avoda zara* by an individual says *vehotzeisa es ha-ish hahu oh es ha-ishah hahi el she'arecha* — you shall bring that man or that woman out to your gates, meaning the *beis din* of the city. The Gemara derives: *ish ve-ishah ata motzi lish'arecha* — individuals you bring to the city gates; *ve-i ata motzi kol ha-ir kulo lish'arecha* — but if the entire city has sinned, you do not bring them to the city's own *beis din*. Instead they go before the *beis din* of seventy-one, the court of *Klal Yisrael*. By the same token, *ish ve-ishah ata motzi lish'arecha ve-i ata motzi kol shevet kulo lish'arecha* — if the entire tribe has sinned, it too requires a different and higher court, even though the judgment rendered is individual in nature. This also suggests that the rule is not limited to *avoda zara* specifically: once one adopts the principle that a sufficiently large portion of the population demands a court of seventy-one, that logic could extend to any mass communal sin. The Gemara leaves this as a point worth noting.

## Navi Sheker & Zakein

### Navi Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא אֶת נְבִיא הַשֶּׁקֶר. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי?
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that **a false prophet** may be judged only by the Great Sanhedrin, consisting of seventy-one judges. The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived?

The Mishnah teaches that a *navi sheker*, a false prophet, is also judged before a *beis din* of seventy-one. The Gemara seeks the scriptural basis for this ruling.

### Hazada Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אַתְיָא ״הֲזָדָה״ ״הֲזָדָה״ מִזָּקֵן מַמְרֵא, מָה לְהַלָּן בְּשִׁבְעִים וְאֶחָד – אַף כָּאן בְּשִׁבְעִים וְאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says:** It is **derived** by means of a verbal analogy, with the meaning of **presumptuousness** stated in the context of a false prophet learned **from presumptuousness** stated in the context of **a rebellious elder.** With regard to a false prophet the verse states: "But the prophet who shall speak a word presumptuously in My name, which I have not commanded him to speak, or who shall speak in the name of other gods, that prophet shall die" (Deuteronomy 18:20), and with regard to a rebellious elder it states: "And the man that acts presumptuously, by not listening to the priest that stands to minister there before the Lord your God, or to the judge, that man shall die" (Deuteronomy 17:12). **Just as there,** with regard to a rebellious elder, he is presumptuous **against** a court of **seventy-one** judges, **so too here,** with regard to a false prophet, he is judged **by** a court of **seventy-one** judges.

Rabbi Yosei berabbi Chanina says: this is derived through a *gezeirah shavah* — a verbal analogy — of *hazada hazada* from *zakein mamre*. The verse concerning a false prophet uses the word *yazid*, a form of *zadon* — presumption or willful rebellion. The verse concerning a *zakein mamre* likewise uses the word *bezadon*. Just as the *zakein mamre* is associated with a court of seventy-one, so too the false prophet is judged by seventy-one.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״הֲזָדָה״ כִּי כְּתִיבָא, בִּקְטָלָא הוּא דִּכְתִיבָא, וּקְטָלָא בְּעֶשְׂרִין וּתְלָתָא הוּא! אֶלָּא, אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: גָּמַר ״דָּבָר״ ״דָּבָר״ מֵהַמְרָאָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But when presumptuousness is written** with regard to a rebellious elder, **it is written with regard to** the **death** penalty, **and a death** sentence may **be** issued **by** a court of **twenty-three** judges. **Rather, Reish Lakish said:** It is **derived** by means of a verbal analogy, with the meaning of the term **word** stated in the context of a false prophet learned **from** the term **word,** used when describing the rebellious elder's **rebellion.**

At this point the Bavli, departing from the simpler treatment in the Yerushalmi, raises an important problem. The verse using *zadon* in the context of *zakein mamre* — *veha-ish asher ya'aseh bezadon levilti shmo'a el ha-kohein... u-meis ha-ish hahu* — is referring specifically to the moment of his *execution*. And the execution of a *zakein mamre* takes place before a *beis din* of *twenty-three*, not seventy-one. A *zakein mamre* must rebel against the *sanhedrin* of seventy-one, but he is actually tried and executed by a *beis din* of twenty-three. This is a striking and counterintuitive point: the *zakein mamre* rejects the authority of seventy-one but is himself judged by twenty-three. Thus the word *zadon* in the verse about *zakein mamre* maps onto a court of twenty-three, not seventy-one. Linking the false prophet's *zadon* to the *zakein mamre*'s *zadon* would therefore yield twenty-three — the opposite of what is intended.

### Gezeirah Shavah

Reish Lakish therefore proposes a different *gezeirah shavah*: *davar davar*, a link through the word *davar* — matter. In the passage dealing with the *zakein mamre*, the verse reads *ve-asisa al pi ha-davar asher yagidu lecha* — you shall act according to the matter that they tell you. This *davar* situates itself in the context of the *zakein mamre* defying the *sanhedrin* of seventy-one. By linking the word *davar* in the false prophet's verse — *asher yazid ledaber davar bishmi* — to the *davar* in the *zakein mamre* passage, we connect the false prophet not to the *zakein mamre*'s execution (which involves twenty-three) but to the *zakein mamre*'s act of rebellion against seventy-one. From this we derive that the false prophet is judged by seventy-one.

### No Reverse Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֶהְדַּר זָקֵן מַמְרֵא, וְלִגְמַר ״הֲזָדָה״ ״הֲזָדָה״ מִנְּבִיא הַשֶּׁקֶר? ״דָּבָר״ ״דָּבָר״ גְּמִיר, ״הֲזָדָה״ ״הֲזָדָה״ לָא גְּמִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let** the *halakha* of **a rebellious elder return, and let it be derived** by means of a verbal analogy, with the meaning of **presumptuousness** stated in the context of a rebellious elder learned **from presumptuousness** stated in the context of **a false prophet,** to indicate that a rebellious elder is also sentenced by a court of seventy-one judges. The Gemara answers: This *tanna* **derives** *halakhot* through the verbal analogy comparing the terms **word** and **word,** but he **does not derive** *halakhot* through a verbal analogy between the terms **presumptuousness** and **presumptuousness,** as he did not receive it as an authentic tradition.

A natural follow-up question arises: now that we have the false prophet pegged at seventy-one via the *davar davar* link to *zakein mamre*, can we close the circle? Can we take the *hazada hazada* link and use the false prophet's seventy-one to elevate the *zakein mamre* to seventy-one as well? The Gemara answers: *davar davar gamir, hazada hazada lo gamir* — we have a received tradition of the *davar davar* analogy operating in one direction only. We do not have a tradition for the *hazada hazada* link to operate in the other direction. A *gezeirah shavah* works only where there is a received tradition, and we cannot simply reverse its direction at will.

### Zakein Irony

The Gemara thus arrives at a formally ironic position: the *zakein mamre* defines his sin by rejecting the authority of the *sanhedrin* of seventy-one, yet he himself is judged by a *beis din* of only twenty-three. The *halacha* of a *zakein mamre* is that after his case is heard by the *sanhedrin* of seventy-one, he returns to his city and continues to rule according to his dissenting opinion. The sin that seals his death warrant is his continuing to rule and teach in his city in defiance of the Great Court. It is therefore the *beis din* of his own city — a court of twenty-three — that ultimately hears his case. The logic of the *halacha* carries its own internal coherence, even if the result is striking.

## Kohen Gadol & 71

### KG Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא אֶת כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״כׇּל הַדָּבָר הַגָּדֹל יָבִיאוּ אֵלֶיךָ״, דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל גָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that **the High Priest** may be judged only by the Great Sanhedrin, consisting of seventy-one judges. The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived? **Rav Adda bar Ahava says that the verse states: "They shall bring every great matter to you"** (Exodus 18:22). This means that Moses, or the Great Sanhedrin with seventy-one judges, which served the parallel role to that of Moses, adjudicates all **matters** relating **to a great one,** i.e., the High Priest.

The Mishnah also teaches that a *kohen gadol* is judged only before a *beis din* of seventy-one. The Gemara again asks for the scriptural source.

### Gadol vs. Kashe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״דָּבָר גָּדֹל״ – דָּבָר קָשֶׁה. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר דָּבָר קָשֶׁה, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל גָּדוֹל? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״אֶת הַדָּבָר הַקָּשֶׁה יְבִיאוּן אֶל מֹשֶׁה״, הֲרֵי דָּבָר קָשֶׁה אָמוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to this from a *baraita*: The verse states: "They shall bring every **great matter** to you," which is interpreted to mean that **a difficult matter** is judged by the Great Sanhedrin. The *baraita* asks: **Do you say** that the verse is actually referring to **a difficult matter, or is it only** referring to **the matters** relating **to a great one?** The *baraita* answers: **When it states** in a different verse that Moses implemented Yitro's advice: **"They brought the difficult matter to Moses"** (Exodus 18:26), **a difficult matter is stated** explicitly.

A *baraisa* then challenges this reading. Perhaps *davar gadol* simply means a difficult matter — *davar kashe* — which is the plain sense of the verse. After all, the *baraisa* itself entertains the question: *atah omer davar kashe o eino ela devarav shel gadol?* A later verse confirms that the people brought *davar kashe* — the difficult matters — to Moses. Since this parallel verse uses *davar kashe* to describe the same practice, and since both verses describe Moses's role, one might conclude that *davar gadol* and *davar kashe* are synonyms, both meaning simply difficult matters. The interpretation of *devarav shel gadol* would then be excluded.

### Two Concepts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא דְּאָמַר כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״דָּבָר גָּדֹל״ – דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל גָּדוֹל. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל גָּדוֹל, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא דָּבָר הַקָּשֶׁה? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״הַדָּבָר הַקָּשֶׁה״, הֲרֵי דָּבָר קָשֶׁה אָמוּר. הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״דָּבָר גָּדוֹל״? דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל גָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rav Adda bar Ahava **states** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **that** other ***tanna*, as it is taught** in a different *baraita*: The meaning of **"great matter"** in the verse is **matters** relating **to a great one.** The *baraita* asks: **Do you say** that it is referring to **matters** relating **to a great one, or is it only** referring to **a difficult matter?** The *baraita* explains: **When it says** further on: "They brought **the difficult matter** to Moses," **a difficult matter is stated,** so **how do I realize** the meaning of **"great matter"?** It is referring to **matters** relating **to a great one.**

The Gemara resolves this by appealing to another *baraisa* that reads the two terms in precisely the opposite way: *davar gadol* means *devarav shel gadol*, matters of a great personage, while *davar kashe* means a difficult matter. The two verses use different words deliberately, and the divergence in terminology communicates two distinct legal concepts: difficult matters go to Moses — that is, to the *sanhedrin* of seventy-one — and matters involving great personages also go to Moses. One verse communicates one principle; the other communicates the other.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי תַּנָּא, תְּרֵי קְרָאֵי לְמָה לִי? חַד לְצַוָּאָה בְּעָלְמָא, וְחַד לַעֲשִׂיָּיה. וְאִידַּךְ? אִם כֵּן, לִכְתּוֹב אוֹ ״גָּדוֹל״ ״גָּדוֹל״, אוֹ ״קָשֶׁה״ ״קָשֶׁה״. מַאי ״גָּדוֹל״ וּמַאי ״קָשֶׁה״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **this *tanna*** in the first *baraita* who maintains that "great matter" and "difficult matter" are referring to the same *halakha*, **why do I** need **two verses** to express the same idea? The Gemara answers: **One** is **for the command in general, and one** is **to** state that **the execution** of the matter was carried out correctly. **And** why does **the other** *tanna* not accept this explanation? The Gemara explains: He would claim that **if so,** if both verses were actually referring to the same thing, **let it write either "great"** in one verse and **"great"** in the other, **or "difficult"** in one verse and **"difficult"** in the other. **What** is the significance of writing **"great"** in one verse **and what** is the significance of writing **"difficult"** in the other? **Conclude two** conclusions **from it;** one verse is referring to difficult matters and the other to matters relating to a great one.

The Gemara presses further: if both terms convey the same point, why change terms at all? The answer that both verses refer to the same instruction but one is the command and one is its execution is formally adequate but does not explain the lexical variation. The better answer is *shema mina tartei* — infer from this two separate teachings. Both concepts are intentionally embedded in the Torah's use of different language.

### Abaye: Monetary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שׁוֹרוֹ שֶׁל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, בְּכַמָּה? לְמִיתַת בְּעָלִים דִּידֵיהּ מְדַמֵּינַן לֵיהּ, אוֹ דִילְמָא לְמִיתַת בְּעָלִים דְּעָלְמָא מְדַמֵּינַן לֵיהּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִדְּקָא מִבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ שׁוֹרוֹ, מִכְלָל דְּמָמוֹנוֹ פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar asked:** If **the ox of a High Priest** killed a person, **by how many** judges is it sentenced? Do **we compare it to** a trial that could result in **the death of its owner,** the High Priest, which would have to be judged by seventy-one judges, **or perhaps we compare it to** a trial that could result in **the death of owners in general,** which could be judged by twenty-three judges? **Abaye said: Since he asked** the question only with regard to the High Priest's **ox,** where there is a specific reason to say that its judicial proceedings should have the same halakhot as those concerning its owner, **by inference** it can be derived that **it was obvious to him that** court hearings related to the High Priest's other **property** may be deliberated by an ordinary court.

The Gemara then raises a question attributed to Rabbi Eliezer about the *kohen gadol*: if *kol ha-davar ha-gadol* means all matters involving a great personage, does this include monetary cases? Would even a routine financial dispute involving a *kohen gadol* require a *beis din* of seventy-one? Abaye responds: the very fact that the question focuses on an ox — *shoro shel kohen gadol* — and notes that monetary matters are *peshita*, obviously not requiring seventy-one, already concedes that a straightforward monetary claim against a *kohen gadol* would go to an ordinary court. The matter pertains to his property, not to his person.

### Open Questions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הוֹאִיל וְכָתַב ״כׇּל הַדָּבָר הַגָּדוֹל״, כׇּל דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל גָּדוֹל – קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious?** Why should it not be so? The Gemara answers: Abaye needed to clarify this, **lest you say** that **since it writes: "Every great matter,"** one might have thought that the verse is referring to **all matters** relating **to a great one,** meaning that any case involving the High Priest is adjudicated by the Great Sanhedrin. Therefore, Abaye **teaches us** that this is not the *halakha*.

The Gemara notes that the question is not fully resolved, however, regarding the broader range of cases that fall between monetary matters and capital crimes. What about *malkus* — lashes? What about *knas* — a financial penalty that is punitive in nature? The scope of the *beis din* of seventy-one's exclusive jurisdiction over the *kohen gadol* beyond capital crimes remains an open question at this stage.

## Milchemet Reshut

### War Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֵין מוֹצִיאִין וְכוּ׳. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״וְלִפְנֵי אֶלְעָזָר הַכֹּהֵן יַעֲמֹד״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that the king may **bring** the nation **out** to an optional war **only** on the basis of a court of seventy-one judges, i.e., the Great Sanhedrin. The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived? **Rabbi Abbahu says:** It is **as the verse states** with regard to the appointment of Joshua: **"And he shall stand before Elazar the priest,** and he shall ask counsel of the Urim before the Lord; by his word they shall go out, and by his word they shall come in, he and all of the children of Israel with him and all of the congregation" (Numbers 27:21).

The Mishnah states that going out to an optional war — *milchemet reshut* — requires the authorization of a *beis din* of seventy-one. It is notable that this halakhic requirement is not addressed in the Yerushalmi. One might speculate — though the Gemara does not say so explicitly — that in the wake of the Bar Kochva revolt and the Hadrianic persecution, the Sages of the land of Israel may have been reluctant to engage with topics touching on Jewish sovereignty, kingship, and the prospect of going to war. The Bavli addresses the topic directly.

### Joshua & Urim

Rabbi Abbahu cites a verse from Bamidbar concerning Joshua: *ve-lifnei Elazar ha-kohen ya'amod ve-sha'alu be-mishpat ha-urim lifnei Hashem al piv yetze'u ve-al piv yavo'u* — he shall stand before Elazar the priest, who shall inquire for him through the judgment of the *urim* before God; at his word they shall go out and at his word they shall come in. This verse describes Joshua consulting the *kohen* who bears the *urim ve-tumim*, clearly establishing the role of divine consultation in going to war.

### Verse Parsing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הוּא״ – זֶה מֶלֶךְ, ״וְכׇל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אִתּוֹ״ – זֶה מְשׁוּחַ מִלְחָמָה, ״וְכׇל הָעֵדָה״ – זֶה סַנְהֶדְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abbahu analyzes the end of the verse. With regard to the word **"he," this** is the **king,** referring to Joshua and to any other leader who brings the nation out to war. With regard to the word "him" in the verse **"And all of the children of Israel with him," this** is the priest **anointed for war,** who was anointed specially to stand and instruct the people before the war (see Deuteronomy 20:2). **"And all of the congregation"; this** is the **Sanhedrin.**

The Gemara then parses additional phrases from the surrounding verses to identify each component required for *milchemet reshut*. *Ya'amod* — he shall stand — refers to Joshua, representing the king. *Ve-chol bnei Yisrael ito* — all of Israel with him — is read as referring to the *mashuach milchama*, the kohen anointed for war, whose role it is to lead and address the troops. *Ve-chol ha-edah* — and all the congregation — the word *edah* being a key term that the Gemara consistently reads as signifying the *sanhedrin*, represents the seventy-one.

### Urim Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא לְסַנְהֶדְרֵי הוּא דְּקָאָמַר לְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא, דְּלִישַׁיְּילוּ בְּאוּרִים וְתוּמִּים?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But perhaps** this mention of the Sanhedrin simply means **that the Merciful One says that the Sanhedrin may ask** a question **of the *Urim VeTummim*,** as may the king or the priest anointed for war, as opposed to an ordinary person; but with regard to the decision to go to war, perhaps the king may do so without the agreement of the Sanhedrin.

The Gemara raises an alternative reading: perhaps the verse is not specifically about the requirements for going to war, but rather about who has standing to consult the *urim ve-tumim* in general — whether it is a king or the *sanhedrin* that has this authority. The Gemara sets this aside and moves to an alternative derivation.

### David Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, כִּי הָא דְּאָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר בִּיזְנָא אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן חֲסִידָא: כִּנּוֹר הָיָה תָּלוּי לְמַעְלָה מִמִּטָּתוֹ שֶׁל דָּוִד. כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ חֲצוֹת לַיְלָה, רוּחַ צְפוֹנִית מְנַשֶּׁבֶת בּוֹ וְהָיָה מְנַגֵּן מֵאֵלָיו. מִיָּד הָיָה דָּוִד עוֹמֵד וְעוֹסֵק בְּתוֹרָה עַד שֶׁעָלָה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר. כֵּיוָן שֶׁעָלָה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר, נִכְנְסוּ חַכְמֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶצְלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the proof is **like that which Rav Aḥa bar Bizna says** that **Rabbi Shimon Ḥasida says: A lyre hung above David's bed, and once midnight arrived, the northern** midnight **wind would blow on it and** cause the lyre to **play on its own. David would immediately rise** from his bed **and study Torah until the dawn arrived. Once dawn arrived, the Sages of Israel** would **enter to** advise **him** with regard to the various concerns of the nation and the economy.

The alternative is a well-known *midrash* from Berachot. Rav Yitzchak bar Bizna cites Rabbi Shimon Chasida: David had a *kinor* hanging above his bed. At midnight, a northern wind would blow through it and cause it to play on its own — his natural alarm. David would arise immediately and engage in Torah study until the first light of dawn. When dawn broke, the sages of Israel would come before him and say: *Adoneinu ha-melech, amcha Yisrael tzrichim le-farnasa* — our lord the king, your people Israel need sustenance. He would tell them to sustain themselves from one another through commerce. They would respond: *ein ha-kometz masbia et ha-ari* — a handful does not satisfy a lion. The resources are insufficient for internal trade alone to meet the people's needs. *Ve-ein ha-bor mitmale me-chulyato* — and a pit does not fill from its own cavity. Rashi interprets this phrase as meaning that dirt excavated from a pit, if replaced, does not fully fill it back in. Tosafos suggests instead that a well does not fill with water from its own opening. In either case, the point is that recirculating insufficient resources among the people yields nothing. David would then say: *lechu pish'tu yadeichem bi-gdud* — go extend your hands in battle, go to war.

### David Breakdown

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֲדוֹנֵינוּ הַמֶּלֶךְ, עַמְּךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל צְרִיכִין לְפַרְנָסָה. אָמַר לָהֶן: לְכוּ וְהִתְפַּרְנְסוּ זֶה מִזֶּה. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵין הַקּוֹמֶץ מַשְׂבִּיעַ אֶת הָאֲרִי, וְאֵין הַבּוֹר מִתְמַלֵּא מֵחוּלְיָיתוֹ. אָמַר לָהֶם: לְכוּ פִּשְׁטוּ יְדֵיכֶם בִּגְדוּד.
>
> **Translation:** One time **they said to him: Our master the king, your nation, Israel, requires sustenance.** King David **said to them: Go and sustain one another,** i.e., provide each other with whatever is lacking. The Sages **said to him** in response, citing a parable: A single **handful [*hakometz*]** of food **does not satisfy a lion, and a cistern** will **not be filled** merely **from** the rain that falls directly into **its mouth,** but other water must be channeled in. So too, the nation cannot sustain itself using its own resources. King David then **told them: Go and take up arms with the troops** in battle in order to expand our borders and provide our people with the opportunity to earn a livelihood.

The Gemara then shows that what follows in the verse describing David's court maps onto the three required elements. Rav Yosef cites the verse: *ve-acharei Achitofel Benayahu ben Yehoyada ve-Evyatar ve-sar tzava la-melech Yoav* — after Achitofel came Benayahu ben Yehoyada and Evyatar, and the commander of the king's army was Yoav. The breakdown is as follows: *Achitofel zeh yoetz* — Achitofel was the king's personal advisor. *U-Bnayahu ben Yehoyada zo Sanhedrin* — Benayahu ben Yehoyada represents the *sanhedrin*. *Evyatar elu urim ve-tumim* — Evyatar represents the *urim ve-tumim*, the divine consultation. Only after all three — advisor, *sanhedrin*, and *urim ve-tumim* — had been consulted did the king proceed to war under the command of Yoav.

### Kereti & Peleti

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיָּד יוֹעֲצִין בַּאֲחִיתוֹפֶל, וְנִמְלָכִין בְּסַנְהֶדְרִין, וְשׁוֹאֲלִין בְּאוּרִים וְתוּמִּים. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי קְרָא? ״וְאַחֲרֵי אֲחִיתֹפֶל בְּנָיָהוּ בֶּן יְהוֹיָדָע וְאֶבְיָתָר וְשַׂר צָבָא לַמֶּלֶךְ יוֹאָב״. ״אֲחִיתוֹפֶל״ – זֶה יוֹעֵץ, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וַעֲצַת אֲחִיתֹפֶל אֲשֶׁר יָעַץ וְגוֹ׳״. וּ״בְנָיָהוּ בֶּן יְהוֹיָדָע״ – זוֹ סַנְהֶדְרִין. ״אֶבְיָתָר״ – אֵלּוּ אוּרִים וְתוּמִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **immediately sought advice from Ahithophel** to determine whether or not it was appropriate to go to war at that time and how they should conduct themselves; **and they consulted the Sanhedrin** in order to receive the requisite permission to wage a war under those circumstances; **and they asked the *Urim VeTummim*** whether or not they should go to war, and whether or not they would be successful. **Rav Yosef says: What** is **the verse** from which this *aggada* is derived? It is: **And after Ahithophel was Benaiah, son of Jehoiada; and Ebiathar; and the general of the king's army, Yoav** (see I Chronicles 27:34). The individuals named in this verse correspond to the roles in the *aggada* as follows: **Ahithophel is** the **advisor** whose counsel they sought first with regard to going to war, **and so it says: "Now the advice of Ahithophel, which he counseled** in those days, was like that of a man who inquires of the word of God; so was the counsel of Ahithophel both with David and with Absalom" (II Samuel 16:23). **And Benaiah, son of Jehoiada** corresponds to **the Sanhedrin,** since he was the head of the Sanhedrin, and **Ebiathar** corresponds to **the *Urim VeTummim*,** as Ebiathar, son of Ahimelech the priest would oversee inquiries directed to the *Urim VeTummim* (see I Samuel 23:9).

The identification of Evyatar with the *urim ve-tumim* is buttressed by another verse: *u-Bnayahu ben Yehoyada al ha-Kereti ve-al ha-Peleti* — Benayahu ben Yehoyada was over the *Kereti* and the *Peleti*. For *Chazal*, *Kereti* and *Peleti* are designations for the *urim ve-tumim*. *Kereti she-korin divreihem* — they cut their pronouncements with precision, like a definitive divine word. *Peleti she-mufla'im ma'aseihem* — their workings are wondrous, describing the miraculous way the divine word emerges from the *urim ve-tumim*. Having established that *Kereti* and *Peleti* refer to the *urim ve-tumim*, and that Benayahu is juxtaposed to them in one verse, while Evyatar is paired with Benayahu in the other, the Gemara maps Evyatar onto the *urim ve-tumim* — thus confirming that all three elements (advisor, *sanhedrin*, divine oracle) were present before David went to war.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וּבְנָיָהוּ בֶּן יְהוֹיָדָע עַל הַכְּרֵתִי וְעַל הַפְּלֵתִי״. וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָן כְּרֵתִי וּפְלֵתִי? כְּרֵתִי – שֶׁכּוֹרְתִין דִּבְרֵיהֶן, וּפְלֵתִי – שֶׁמּוּפְלָאִין מַעֲשֵׂיהֶן. וְאַחַר כָּךְ ״שַׂר הַצָּבָא לַמֶּלֶךְ יוֹאָב״.
>
> **Translation:** **And so it says** with regard to the position of Benaiah, son of Jehoiada, as head of the Sanhedrin: **"And Benaiah, son of Jehoiada, was over the *Kereti* and over the *Peleti*"** (II Samuel 20:23). **And why was** the Sanhedrin **called *Kereti* and *Peleti*?** It was called ***Kereti*** because **they were decisive [*shekoretin*] in their pronouncements.** It was called ***Peleti*** because **their actions** and wisdom **were wondrous [*shemufla'in*],** as *Peleti* and *mufla'in* share the same root. According to the order of the verse, upon being instructed by King David to go to war, the Sages first consulted with Ahithophel, then with the Sanhedrin, and then they would ask the *Urim VeTummim*; **and** only **thereafter** was **the general of the king's army, Yoav,** given the command to ready the army for battle.

### David's Harp

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אַדָּא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר אֲבוּדִימִי: מַאי קְרָא? ״עוּרָה כְבוֹדִי עוּרָה הַנֵּבֶל וְכִנּוֹר אָעִירָה שָּׁחַר״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak, son of Rav Adda, and some say Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Avudimi, said: What is the verse** from which it is derived that David's lyre would awaken him at midnight? **"Awake, my glory; awake, harp and lyre; I will awaken the dawn"** (Psalms 57:9). This means that the self-playing lyre has already awoken, and now I must engage in Torah study until dawn.

Rav Yitzchak bereih de-Rav Adda — and some say Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi — provides the scriptural basis for David's harp waking him at midnight. The verse reads: *urah kviudi, urah ha-nevel ve-chinor, a'irah shachar* — awake my glory, awake harp and lyre, I will wake the dawn. David does not say that the dawn woke him; he says he will wake the dawn — indicating that it is he who rises before first light, prompted by the instrument that plays on its own.

## City Expansion & Judges

### City Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מוֹסִיפִין עַל הָעִיר. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב שִׁימִי בַּר חִיָּיא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״כְּכׇל אֲשֶׁר אֲנִי מַרְאֶה אוֹתְךָ אֵת תַּבְנִית הַמִּשְׁכָּן וְכֵן תַּעֲשׂוּ״ – לְדוֹרוֹת הַבָּאִים.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **They may extend the city** of Jerusalem or the courtyards of the Temple only on the basis of a court of seventy-one judges. The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived? **Rav Shimi bar Ḥiyya says: The verse states: "According to all that I show you, the pattern of the Tabernacle** and the pattern of all its vessels, **and so shall you do"** (Exodus 25:9). "And so shall you do" means **for future generations;** just as the Tabernacle was fashioned in all of its details according to Moses' instructions, so too later, the Temple is fashioned according to the instructions of the Great Sanhedrin, whose members stand in place of Moses.

The Mishnah teaches that one may not expand the walls of the city — *ein mosifin al ha-ir* — without the authority of a *sanhedrin* of seventy-one. The Gemara asks for the source.

### Mosaic Model

Rav Shimi bar Chiyya cites the verse: *ke-chol asher ani mareh otcha et tavnit ha-mishkan ve-chen ta'asu le-dorot ha-ba'im* — precisely as I show you the pattern of the Tabernacle, so shall you do for future generations. The construction of the *mishkan* was supervised by Moses, and Moses represents the *sanhedrin* of seventy-one. The verse's instruction to replicate the process for future generations thus implies that analogous acts of national significance — such as expanding a city — require that same level of authority.

### Mishkan Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָבָא: כׇּל הַכֵּלִים שֶׁעָשָׂה מֹשֶׁה, מְשִׁיחָתָן מְקַדַּשְׁתָּן. מִיכָּן וְאֵילָךְ, עֲבוֹדָתָן מְחַנַּכְתָּן. וְאַמַּאי? נֵימָא ״וְכֵן תַּעֲשׂוּ״ לְדוֹרוֹת הַבָּאִים!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises an objection** from a *baraita*: With regard to **all of the utensils that Moses fashioned, their anointment** with the sacred oil is what **consecrates them,** rendering them fit for service in the Tabernacle. **From that** point **forward,** i.e., in future generations, there is no need for anointment, but rather **their service** in and of itself **dedicates them,** meaning that when they are used for the first time in sacred service they become consecrated. Rava explains the objection: **And why** is this so? **Let us say** instead that since the verse states: **"And so shall you do,"** this teaches that it must be done **for future generations** as in the Tabernacle, and therefore anointment with sacred oil should be required in the Temple as in the Tabernacle.

Rava challenges this derivation from a *baraisa*: *kol ha-kelim she-asah Moshe meshichatan mekadashtan* — all the vessels that Moses made for the sanctuary, their anointing with the *shemen ha-mishchah* consecrated them. But *mikan va-eilach* — from that point on and for future generations — *avodatan mechanchtan*, they are consecrated through being put to use in the service of the sanctuary. This raises a question: if the verse says *ve-chen ta'asu le-dorot*, meaning do as Moses did for all generations, then future generations should also have to anoint their vessels with *shemen ha-mishchah* — just as Moses did.

### Vessel Sanctification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וַיִּמְשָׁחֵם וַיְקַדֵּשׁ אֹתָם״ – אוֹתָם בִּמְשִׁיחָה, וְלֹא לְדוֹרוֹת בִּמְשִׁיחָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It **is different there, as the verse states:** "And it came to pass on the day that Moses completed erecting the Tabernacle that he anointed it and sanctified it and all its vessels, and the altar and all its vessels, **and he anointed them and he sanctified them"** (Numbers 7:1). The verse emphasizes that he sanctified **"them,"** and from this it is inferred that only those utensils need sanctification **by anointment, but for** future **generations** there is **not** a requirement of sanctification **by anointment.**

The Gemara resolves this from another verse: *va-yimshachem va-yikadesh otam* — and he anointed them and sanctified them. The word *otam* — them, specifically — implies that it was those particular vessels that were consecrated through anointing, and not the vessels of future generations. *Otam bi-meshichah ve-lo le-dorot bi-meshichah* — those vessels through anointing, but not for future generations through anointing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: אוֹתָם בִּמְשִׁיחָה, וּלְדוֹרוֹת – אִי בִּמְשִׁיחָה אִי בַּעֲבוֹדָה? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אָמַר קְרָא ״אֲשֶׁר יְשָׁרְתוּ בָם בַּקֹּדֶשׁ״, הַכָּתוּב תְּלָאָן בְּשֵׁירוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And say** instead: **Those** vessels require sanctification specifically **by anointment, but for** future **generations** it could be done **either by anointment or by service. Rav Pappa says: The verse states** with regard to this: "And they shall take all service vessels **with which they shall serve in the sanctuary"** (Numbers 4:12). **The verse renders it dependent upon service,** meaning that the service is what sanctifies them.

The Gemara pushes back: perhaps *otam* is not exclusive, and in future generations one has the option of consecrating through anointing *or* through use? Another verse resolves this: *asher yeshartu bam ba-kodesh* — that they will be used in sanctity — implying that use is the operative mechanism for future consecration. The Gemara then asks: if a separate verse already establishes that future vessels are consecrated through use, why is *otam* needed at all?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא ״אֹתָם״ לְמָה לִי? אִי לָאו ״אֹתָם״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: לְדוֹרוֹת בִּמְשִׁיחָה וּבַעֲבוֹדָה, דְּהָא כְּתִיב ״וְכֵן תַּעֲשׂוּ״. כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״אֹתָם״ – אוֹתָם בִּמְשִׁיחָה, וְלֹא לְדוֹרוֹת בִּמְשִׁיחָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **why do I** need the extra word **"them"?** This emphasis seems superfluous. The Gemara answers: **Had** the verse **not** added the word **"them," I would say: For** future **generations** the sanctification is accomplished **by anointment and by service** together, **as it is written: "And so shall you do." Therefore, the Merciful One writes "them,"** to teach: **They** alone are consecrated **by anointment, but for** future **generations** the vessels are **not** consecrated **by anointment.**

The answer is that without *otam*, one might have thought — given the instruction of *ve-chen ta'asu* — that future generations require *both* anointing and use, since they are told to follow Moses's model. *Otam* comes to teach that future vessels require anointing specifically in their generation, not going forward; and going forward, use alone is sufficient. This excursion into the laws of *mishkan* vessels arises directly from the effort to establish the limits of the principle that future generations must replicate Moses's process.

## Judge Hierarchy

### Judges Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין סַנְהֶדְרָאוֹת כּוּ׳. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? כִּדְאַשְׁכְּחַן בְּמֹשֶׁה, דְּאוֹקִי סַנְהֶדְרָאוֹת, וּמֹשֶׁה בִּמְקוֹם שִׁבְעִים וְחַד קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **They may appoint** a lesser **Sanhedrin** for the tribes only on the basis of a court of seventy-one judges. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this matter? The Gemara answers: It is **as we find with regard to Moses, who established** lesser **courts** for all of the people (see Exodus 18:25–26), **and Moses stands in place of** the **seventy-one** judges on the Great Sanhedrin.

A *baraisa* establishes the scriptural basis for the entire system of appointing judges throughout Israel. *Minayin she-ma'amidin shoftim le-Yisrael* — how do we know there is a *mitzvah* to appoint judges for Israel? *Talmud lomar shoftim titen* — the verse says you shall appoint judges.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִנַּיִין שֶׁמַּעֲמִידִין שׁוֹפְטִים לְיִשְׂרָאֵל? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שֹׁפְטִים תִּתֵּן״. שֹׁטְרִים לְיִשְׂרָאֵל מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שֹׁטְרִים תִּתֵּן״. שׁוֹפְטִים לְכׇל שֵׁבֶט וְשֵׁבֶט מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שֹׁפְטִים לִשְׁבָטֶיךָ״. שׁוֹטְרִים לְכׇל שֵׁבֶט וְשֵׁבֶט מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שֹׁטְרִים לִשְׁבָטֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **that** society must **establish judges for** the **Jewish people? The verse states: "You shall place judges** and officers over you in all of your gates that the Lord your God gives you for your tribes, and they shall judge the people with righteous judgment" (Deuteronomy 16:18). **From where** is it derived that society must also establish **officers for** the **Jewish people? The** same **verse states: "You shall place** judges and **officers." From where** is it derived that society must also establish **judges** not only for the entire Jewish people but also **for each and every tribe? The verse states:** "You shall place **judges** and officers…**for your tribes." From where** is it derived that society must also establish **officers for each and every tribe? The** same **verse states:** "You shall place judges and **officers**…**for your tribes."**

### Police Force

*Shotrim le-Yisrael minayin* — how do we know there is an obligation to establish a police force, officers who enforce the law? *Talmud lomar* — the verse says *shotrim*, officers, alongside the judges.

### Tribal & City Courts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוֹפְטִים לְכׇל עִיר וָעִיר מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שֹׁפְטִים לִשְׁעָרֶיךָ״. שׁוֹטְרִים לְכׇל עִיר וָעִיר מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שֹׁטְרִים לִשְׁעָרֶיךָ״. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֶחָד מְמוּנֶּה עַל כּוּלָּן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תִּתֶּן לְךָ״. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: ״לִשְׁבָטֶיךָ וְשָׁפְטוּ״ – מִצְוָה בַּשֵּׁבֶט לָדוּן אֶת שִׁבְטוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **From where** is it derived that society must also establish **judges for each and every city? The verse states:** You shall place **judges** and officers…**for your gates,** as the gate of the city is the seat of the elders of the city and its judges. **From where** is it derived that society must also establish **officers for each and every city? The verse states:** You shall place Judges and **officers**…**for your gates. Rabbi Yehuda says:** You must also have **one** court **appointed over all of them, as it is stated: "You shall place over you,"** meaning that there must be a single institution that is responsible for all of these appointments. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** Another *halakha* is derived from the verse: **"For your tribes, and they shall judge."** This teaches that it is **a mitzva for a tribe to judge** the sinners from within **its tribe,** and not to delegate the responsibility to other tribes.

*Shoftim le-chol shevet ve-shevet minayin* — how do we know that each individual tribe must have its own judges and its own *sanhedrin* standing over it, rather than just a single federal-level court? The *baraisa* derives this from the full verse: *shoftim ve-shotrim titein lecha be-chol she'arecha asher Hashem Elokecha notein lecha le-shivtecha* — you shall appoint judges and officers in all your gates that the Lord your God gives you, according to your tribes. The word *le-shivtecha* — for your tribes — indicates that each tribe is to have its own judicial structure. Similarly, *shotrim le-chol shevet ve-shevet* — a police force for each tribe — and *shoftim le-chol ir ve-ir* — judges for each and every city — and *shotrim le-chol ir ve-ir* — officers for each city — are each derived from the relevant terms in the verse. The word *she'arecha*, your gates, means every city, requiring each city to maintain its own local *beis din* of twenty-three.

### R. Yehudah Head

Rabbi Yehudah adds: *echad memuneh al kullan* — there is one appointed over all of them. Rashi understands this as referring to the *sanhedrin gedolah* that presides over the entire court system. Tosafos interprets it more modestly, as indicating that each *beis din* has a single head or presiding officer, derived from the verse's phrase *titein lecha*, which implies a singular appointment at each level.

### RSbG Tribal Authority

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel concludes: *leshivtecha ve-shaftu — mitzvah be-shevet ladun es shivto* — the verse *leshivtecha* teaches that it is a *mitzvah* for each tribe to judge matters pertaining to its own tribe. Each tribe, as it were, handles its own internal affairs. Only specific matters of national import — those requiring the *sanhedrin gedolah* of seventy-one — are brought before the highest court. The question of who originally appointed the members of the *sanhedrin gedolah* itself is left without a formal answer.
