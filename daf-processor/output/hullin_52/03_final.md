# Hullin 52 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Fallen Animals & Bedikah

### Amemar: Bedikah Focus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַמֵּימָר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב דִּימִי מִנְּהַרְדְּעָא: נְפוּלָה שֶׁאָמְרוּ – צְרִיכָה בְּדִיקָה כְּנֶגֶד בְּנֵי מֵעַיִים. אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר זוּטְרָא: הָכִי אָמְרִינַן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא: צְרִיכָה בְּדִיקָה כְּנֶגֶד בֵּית הֶחָלָל כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar said in the name of Rav Dimi of Neharde'a: A fallen** animal **that** the Sages **said requires inspection** must be inspected **around the intestines** to see whether the organs there have been perforated or torn, rendering the animal a *tereifa*. **Mar Zutra said to him: This** is what **we say in the name of Rav Pappa:** Such an animal **requires inspection around the entire** space of the body **cavity,** in case the ribs or spine have been damaged.

The daf opens at the bottom of 51b, picking up from a discussion that had introduced a principle not found in earlier treatments: if an animal that fell from a roof gets up and walks around afterward, it is presumed unharmed. If, however, it merely stood without walking, or if twenty-four hours elapsed, it may still be permissible but requires an internal inspection — a *bedikah*. The Gemara now turns to the precise scope of that *bedikah*.

Amemar said in the name of Rav Dimi from Nehardea: when we say that a *nefulah* — a fallen animal — requires *bedikah*, what must be examined? *Keneged bnei mei'ayim* — the internal organs, to verify that the fall did not puncture or tear them. This is intuitive: a fall might cause a rupture that is not externally visible, and such damage would render the animal a *treifah*.

Rashi, it should be noted, had understood the *bedikah* differently at an earlier stage of the discussion: he assumed the examination was directed at the bones, specifically to check whether the ribs had shattered or the spinal cord severed — each of which constitutes an independent *treifah*. On Rashi's reading, the concern for *piruk atzamot* (dislocation or loosening of the bones as a general category) is resolved by the fact that the animal stood up; what remains is to rule out those more specific skeletal *treifot*. Amemar's formulation, by contrast, focuses on the organs.

### Mar Zutra & Rav Pappa

Mar Zutra responded with a tradition in the name of Rav Pappa: the *bedikah* must cover the entire inner cavity — *kol beit halal kulo*. Two questions arise from this formulation. First, does *beit halal* mean the organs throughout the cavity, including heart and lungs, or does it refer — as Rashi suggests — to the bones, ribs, and vertebrae that form the structure of the cavity? Second, is Rav Pappa supplementing Amemar or contradicting him — broadening the scope of inspection beyond the digestive organs, or redirecting it entirely?

Neither question yields a clean resolution. Rashi's reading of *beit halal* as emphasizing the skeletal structure connects to the *treifot* of shattered ribs and a severed spinal cord; the alternative reading, that it means every organ within the cavity, is perhaps more natural linguistically. The relationship between the two opinions remains open.

### Rav Ashi: Simanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הוּנָא מָר בַּר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נְחֶמְיָה לְרַב אָשֵׁי: סִימָנִין מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: סִימָנִין קָשִׁין הֵן אֵצֶל נְפִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Huna Mar, grandson of Rav Neḥemya, said to Rav Ashi: What** about the **two organs that must be severed in ritual slaughter [*simanim*],** i.e., the windpipe and the gullet? Do they require inspection as well? Rav Ashi **said to him:** The ***simanim* are hard** and resistant to damage **in falling.** Therefore, one need not inspect them.

A question was then posed to Rav Ashi by Huna Mar the son of Rav Nechemiah: what of the *simanim* — the trachea and esophagus? Must one also check whether these were damaged in the fall?

Rav Ashi replied: *simanim kashim heim eitzel nefilah* — the *simanim* are resistant, hardy with respect to falling. They are not the kind of structures that sustain damage from the impact of a fall. One need not check them. The concern for a *bedikah* after a fall is directed at the bones and internal organs, not the trachea or esophagus, which are not vulnerable to that type of immediate trauma.

## Bird Impact Types

### Bird in Water Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: עוֹף שֶׁנֶּחְבַּט עַל פְּנֵי הַמַּיִם, כֵּיוָן שֶׁשָּׁט מְלֹא קוֹמָתוֹ – דַּיּוֹ. וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא מִמַּטָּה לְמַעְלָה, אֲבָל מִלְּמַעְלָה לְמַטָּה – מַיָּא הוּא דְּאַשְׁפִּלוּ. וְאִי מַיָּא קָיְימִי – לֵית לַן בַּהּ, וְאִי שְׁדָא צִיבֵי וְקַדְמֵיהּ – הָא קַדְמֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: If a bird** fell and **hit the surface of the water, once it swims the full length of its** body, this **is sufficient** to indicate that its limbs have not been shattered, similar to an animal that walks after falling. **And we said** this **only** in cases where it swam **from below to above,** i.e., upstream. **But** if it swam **from above to below,** i.e., downstream, **it is** only **the water that carried it down,** and it must be inspected. **And if the water is standing,** e.g., in a pond, **we have no** problem **with it,** as it is clear that the bird is swimming on its own strength, and it need not be inspected. **And** even if the bird swims downstream, **if straw was scattered** in front of it **and** the bird **overtook it** with its swimming, **then** the bird **overtook it** on its own strength and need not be inspected.

The Gemara transitions to birds, drawing on a principle articulated by Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel. For large animals, the test of walking resolves the concern for *risuk eivarim* — internal organ rupture from impact. For birds, an analogous test is needed.

If a bird was hurled against the surface of the water — *nichbat al pnei hamayim* — and one cannot readily perform the walking test, the Gemara provides a substitute: *keivan she'shat melo komato dayo* — as long as the bird swims the length of its own body, that is sufficient, equivalent to a large animal walking. However, a critical qualification applies: this works only when the bird is swimming *against* the current — *mi-mata l'ma'ala*. If it is carried along *with* the current, the movement proves nothing, as the water is simply moving it. If the water is still, the question of current is moot, and any swimming is probative.

An additional nuance: if there are pieces of wood floating downstream with the current, and the bird is moving faster than those pieces, that is evidence of the bird's own locomotion — and that suffices.

### Taut Cloaks & Nets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּלִימָא מְתִיחַ – חָיְישִׁינַן; דְּלָא מְתִיחַ – לָא חָיְישִׁינַן; עוּף וּמְעוּפָּף – לָא חָיְישִׁינַן; אִיזְלָא וּמְקָרְבִי קִיטְרֵי – חָיְישִׁינַן; לָא מְקָרְבִי קִיטְרֵי – לָא חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** If the bird fell on **a garment** spread out **taut** over poles, **we must be concerned** that its limbs may have been shattered from the impact. If it fell on a garment **that was not taut, we** need **not be concerned.** In any event, if the garment was **folded, we** need **not be concerned,** since it presumably was not taut enough to injure the bird. If the bird fell on **a net whose knots were** woven **closely** together, **we must be concerned** that its limbs may have been shattered from the impact. If the **knots were not close** together, **we** need **not be concerned.**

The Gemara now surveys which surfaces, when a bird strikes them, constitute a harsh impact sufficient to create concern for *risuk eivarim*, and which do not. A cloak spread taut — whether hung like laundry perpendicular to the ground or otherwise stretched — creates concern (*chaishinan*) if the bird smashes into it, because the surface is unyielding. If the cloak is not taut, we are not concerned, even if it arrested the bird's flight. A cloak that is folded double, even if taut, similarly does not produce a harsh enough impact — *lo chaishinan*.

A tightly-knotted net — one where the knots are close together — creates concern: the bird may strike directly against a knot, producing a hard impact. If the knots are spread apart, we are not concerned.

### Flax & Bundle Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּיתָּנָא דַּעֲבִיד בְּטוּנֵי – חָיְישִׁינַן; דְּהַאי גִּיסָא וּדְהַאי גִּיסָא – לָא חָיְישִׁינַן; אִסּוּרְיָיתָא – חָיְישִׁינַן; כִּיתָּנָא דִּדְיִיק וּנְפִיץ – לָא חָיְישִׁינַן; דְּיִיק וְלָא נְפִיץ – חָיְישִׁינַן; דַּעֲבִיד בִּיזְרֵי כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא בֵּיהּ קִטְרֵי – חָיְישִׁינַן; דַּקְתָּא – חָיְישִׁינַן; דַּקְדַּקְתָּא – לָא חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** If the bird fell directly on to **flax arranged into bundles, we must be concerned** that its limbs may have been shattered from the impact, because the bundles are hard. If it fell on **this side or that side** of the bundles, **we** need **not be concerned,** because it did not fall directly onto the bundles and the impact was dampened. If it fell on **bundles of reeds, we must be concerned.** If it fell on **beaten and combed flax,** with the impurities removed, **we** need **not be concerned,** since it is soft. If it fell on flax that was **beaten but not combed, we must be concerned** due to the residue of flax stalks in the bundles. If it fell on flax **that was bundled** after it was beaten and combed, **since it has knots** in it **we must be concerned.** If the bird fell on flax **tow,** a coarse bundle of unspun fiber, **we must be concerned.** If it fell on **fine tow, we** need **not be concerned,** because it is soft.

Flax arranged in bundles (*d'avid bituni*) creates concern, because the bundle is firm. However, if the bird strikes at the edges of the bundle — on either side — where the structure is less rigid, we are not concerned.

A bundle of wood similarly creates concern. Flax that has been beaten and combed — with the hard seed vessels removed — is soft, and we are not concerned. Flax that has been soaked but not yet had its outer layers removed retains those harder components, and we are concerned. If the flax is bundled together with knots (as opposed to being loose), the knots themselves create the hard surface of impact, and we are concerned. If the outer husks themselves have been removed and then crushed finely, they are no longer a hard surface — *lo chashinan*.

A processed vine-like fiber (*nivra*) that has not yet been worked into its final form remains hard and creates concern; once processed into its finished, soft state (*timchasa*), it does not.

### Sand, Ash, Straw

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נַבְרָא – חָיְישִׁינַן; תִּימַחְתָא – לָא חָיְישִׁינַן; קִיטְמָא נְהִילָא – חָיְישִׁינַן; לָא נְהִילָא – לָא חָיְישִׁינַן. חוֹל הַדַּק – לָא חָיְשִׁינַן; חוֹל הַגַּס – חָיְשִׁינַן; אֲבַק דְּרָכִים – חָיְשִׁינַן; תִּיבְנָא וַעֲבִיד בִּזְגָּא – חָיְשִׁינַן; לָא עָבֵיד בִּזְגָּא – לָא חָיְשִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** If the bird fell on ***nevara*,** the fibers that grow around a palm tree, **we must be concerned** that its limbs may have been shattered. If it fell on ***timaḥta*,** palm bark cut into strips, **we** need **not be concerned.** If it fell on **sifted ashes, we must be concerned,** because the ashes harden. If it fell on **unsifted** ashes, **we** need **not be concerned,** because they are soft and scatter on impact. If the bird fell on **fine sand, we** need **not be concerned,** because the sand slides on impact, cushioning the fall. If it fell on **coarse sand, we must be concerned,** because there are large stones mixed into it. If it fell on **dust of the road, we must be concerned,** because the dust is compact and hard. If the bird fell on **bundled straw, we must be concerned,** because it is compact and hard. If the straw **was not bundled, we** need **not be concerned.**

Sifted ashes (*kitma nehila*) create concern, because the sifting process results in a compressed, densely packed pile; unsifted ashes, which contain more air and are looser, do not. Fine sand (*chol ha-dak*) does not create concern; coarse, pebbly sand (*chol ha-gas*) does. Dust compacted on a roadway (*avak drachim*) creates concern — Rashi explains this as dust that has been gathered and compressed underfoot. Straw formed into bundles is a concern; loose straw piled without being bundled is not.

Wheat and harder grains create concern. Barley and similar softer grains do not — notably, the Gemara here breaks the alternating *chaishinan / lo chaishinan* pattern by listing them separately, implying a meaningful distinction in hardness.

### Legumes & Slip Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חִיטֵּי וְכֹל דְּמִינַיְיהוּ – חָיְשִׁינַן; שְׂעָרֵי וְכֹל דְּמִינַיְיהוּ – חָיְשִׁינַן; כׇּל מִינֵי קִטְנִיּוֹת – אֵין בָּהֶם מִשּׁוּם רִיסּוּקֵי אֵבָרִים, לְבַר מִן רוּבְּיָא. חִימְצֵי – אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם רִיסּוּק אֵבָרִים, חִפְצֵי – יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם רִיסּוּק אֵבָרִים. כְּלָלָא דְּמִלְּתָא: כֹּל מִידֵּי דְּמַשְׁרֵיק – אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם רִיסּוּק אֵבָרִים, לָא מַשְׁרֵיק – יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם רִיסּוּק אֵבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** If the bird fell on **wheat and all** similar **types of** grain, such as spelt or rye, whose kernels are hard, **we must be concerned.** If it fell on **barley and all** similar **types of** grain, such as oats, **we must be concerned.** With regard to **all types of beans, there is no** concern **due to** possible **shattered limbs** if a bird fell **on them,** since their round shape causes the bird to slide when it hits them, **except for fenugreek.** With regard to **peas, there is no** concern **due to** possible **shattered limbs,** but **with regard to chickpeas, there is** a concern **due to** possible **shattered limbs. The principle of the matter** is: **With regard to anything that slips** to the sides on impact, **there is no** concern **due to** possible **shattered limbs.** And with regard to anything that **does not slip, there is** a concern **due to** possible **shattered limbs.**

All types of *kitniyot* — legumes — do not involve *risuk eivarim*. The Gemara's phrasing here shifts tellingly from the *chaishinan / lo chaishinan* idiom to the more absolute *ein bahem risuk eivarim*. Rashi explains this is not merely because legumes are soft, but because they do not hold together — they slip against one another, such that impact against them is scarcely an impact at all.

An exception is noted for *rubaya* (fenugreek), which does hold its shape and compact. Another substance, *chamtzei* (likely peas), also slips and does not stack, and therefore is not a concern. *Chaptzei* (chickpeas, according to Artscroll), which is not circular and does stack, does present concern for *risuk eivarim*.

The Gemara then articulates the general principle: *kol midi demishrak ein bo mishum risuk eivarim* — anything that slips does not produce *risuk eivarim*. Anything that does not slip does.

### Glued Trap Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבוֹק – רַב אָשֵׁי שָׁרֵי, אַמֵּימָר אָסַר. בְּחַד גַּפָּא – דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דִּשְׁרֵי, כִּי פְּלִיגִי בִּתְרֵי גַּפֵּי. מַאן דְּאָסַר אָמַר לָךְ: הֵיכִי נֵיקוּם? וּמַאן דְּשָׁרֵי אָמַר לָךְ: אֶפְשָׁר דְּנֵיקוּם אַעִיקְבֵי דְּגַפֵּי. וְאִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: בִּתְרֵי גַּפֵּי כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דַּאֲסִיר, כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּחַד גַּפָּא. מַאן דְּשָׁרֵי אָמַר לָךְ: אֶפְשָׁר דְּפָרַח בְּחַד גַּפָּא, וּמַאן דְּאָסַר: כֵּיוָן דִּבְהַאי לָא מָצֵי פָּרַח, בְּהַאי נָמֵי לָא מָצֵי פָּרַח. וְהִילְכְתָא: בִּתְרֵי גַּפֵּי אֲסִיר, בְּחַד גַּפָּא שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** If the bird's wings became stuck to **a *davuk*,** a board covered with glue set as a trap, and in trying to escape it fell to the ground while stuck to the board, **Rav Ashi deemed** the bird **permitted,** while **Ameimar deemed** it **prohibited.** The Gemara explains: **In** a case where only **one wing** was stuck to the board, **everyone agrees that it is permitted,** because the bird flaps with the other wing, lessening the impact of the fall. **They disagree when both wings** are stuck to the board. The one **who deemed** it **prohibited** could have **said to you: How will it stand** itself up so that it might dampen the impact? **And** the one **who deemed** it **permitted** could have **said to you: It is possible for** it to **stand** itself up **by** using **the tips of** its **wings. And there are** those **who say** the details of this exchange differently: After raising an objection to the statement of Rav Ḥisda from the *baraita* that states that a cat cannot effectively claw kids and lambs, **In** a case where **two wings** were stuck to the board, **everyone agrees that it is prohibited,** because it cannot dampen the impact. **They disagree when** only **one wing** is stuck to the board. **The one who deemed** it **permitted** could have **said to you: It is possible for** it to **fly with one wing** and dampen the fall. **And the one who deemed** it **prohibited** could say: **Since it cannot fly with this** wing that is stuck to the *davuk*, **it also cannot fly with that** untrapped wing. The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is: **In** a case where **two wings** were stuck, it is **prohibited. In** a case where only **one wing** was stuck, it is **permitted.**

The Gemara addresses a particular type of bird trap in which pieces of wood were coated with glue. When a bird landed, its wing — or wings — would stick to the wood, and when it tried to fly off, it would crash to the ground. The question is whether such a fall constitutes *risuk eivarim*.

Rav Ashi permitted; Rav Amimar forbade. A first version of the dispute holds that if only one wing is immobilized, everyone agrees the bird is permitted — one free wing cannot fly the bird but can cushion its fall enough that the impact is insufficient to cause concern. The dispute applies when *both* wings are stuck: the one who forbids argues that the bird cannot control its descent at all; the one who permits argues that the bird can still brace itself on the heel of the wing where it joins the body, and this partially breaks the fall.

A second version of the dispute inverts the structure: when both wings are immobilized, everyone agrees the bird is forbidden — it has no means to soften its crash. The dispute applies when only *one* wing is stuck. The permitting opinion holds that one free wing allows the bird to flap and control its fall sufficiently; the forbidding opinion holds that since it cannot fly with the stuck wing, the one free wing also becomes functionally useless, as the bird cannot coordinate flight — *keivan debehai lo matzya parchah, behai nami lo matzya parchah*.

The *halacha* is: two wings stuck — forbidden; one wing stuck — permitted.

## Broken Ribs Debate

### Amemar: Rib Majority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִשְׁתַּבְּרוּ רוֹב צַלְעוֹתֶיהָ. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵלּוּ הֵן רוֹב צְלָעוֹת – שֵׁשׁ מִכָּאן וְשֵׁשׁ מִכָּאן, אוֹ אַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה מִכָּאן וְאַחַת מִכָּאן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that if **most of** an animal's **ribs were fractured,** it is a *tereifa*. **The Sages taught: These are most of the ribs: Six from here and six from there,** i.e., six on each side, **or eleven from here and one from there.** Twenty-two ribs are significant for matters of *tereifot*, eleven on each side. Twelve fractured ribs constitutes a majority.

The Gemara returns to the *mishnah*, which speaks of *rov tzaloteha* — the majority of the ribs being broken as a *treifah*. The ribs relevant to this discussion are the large ribs that form the ribcage — those that have marrow. Rashi holds that there are eleven such ribs on each side, for a total of twenty-two. The majority of twenty-two is twelve. It may be noted that anatomists count thirteen pairs of ribs in these animals, but Rashi's number apparently reflects only those with marrow — possibly the top eleven pairs — with the remaining ones excluded as non-qualifying.

The *Tanna'im* teach: *eilu hein rov tzalot* — what constitutes the majority? Either six ribs from each side, or eleven from one side and one from the other — and by implication, any distribution in between. What matters is that twelve ribs in total, drawn from these eleven pairs, are broken. The distribution between the two sides is irrelevant, so long as the overall number reaches twelve.

### Zeiri: Upper Half Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר זְעֵירִי: וּמֵחֶצְיָין כְּלַפֵּי שִׁדְרָה. אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וּבִצְלָעוֹת גְּדוֹלוֹת שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן מוֹחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ze'eiri says: And** this applies only when the ribs were fractured **from the half of** the rib **toward the spine,** but not if they were fractured on the other half. **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: And** this applies only to fractures occurring **in large ribs that contain marrow;** fractures in small ribs do not render the animal a *tereifa*.

Zeiri adds a qualification: the break must occur in the half of the rib *toward the spinal cord* — *mechetzayan klapei shidra*. A break in the lower half of the rib, toward the front of the animal and away from the spine, does not constitute a full break for the purposes of this *treifah*. Only fractures in the upper, spinal half of the rib count.

Rabba bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan clarifies that throughout this discussion, we are speaking of *tzalot gedolot* — the large ribs that have marrow — eleven on each side.

### Ben Zakkai vs. R. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא, בֶּן זַכַּאי אָמַר: נֶעְקְרוּ – בְּרוֹב צַד אֶחָד, נִשְׁתַּבְּרוּ – בְּרוֹב שְׁנֵי צְדָדִין. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: בֵּין נֶעְקְרוּ בֵּין נִשְׁתַּבְּרוּ – בְּרוֹב שְׁנֵי צְדָדִין.
>
> **Translation:** § **Ulla said** that **ben Zakkai says:** If the ribs were **dislocated** from the spine, even **a majority of one side,** i.e., six dislocated ribs, is enough to render the animal a *tereifa*. Only if the ribs were **broken** is **a majority of both sides** necessary. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Whether they were dislocated or broken,** the animal is a *tereifa* only **with a majority of both sides.**

Ulla cites Ben Zakkai (not to be confused with the Tanna Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai, given the generational distance, but some other Sage of that name): *ne'ekru b'rov tzad echad* — if the ribs are not merely broken but *uprooted* — fully detached from where they join the spinal column — then a majority of *one side* suffices. Six uprooted ribs from one side would render the animal a *treifah*. If, however, the ribs are merely *broken* (*nishtabru*), the majority must span *both* sides — twelve ribs total.

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: whether uprooted or broken, one always requires the majority of both sides combined — twelve ribs. The uprooting of ribs is not inherently more severe in terms of the threshold required.

### Rav: Rib + Vertebra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: נֶעֶקְרָה צֵלָע וְחוּלְיָא עִמָּהּ – טְרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says:** If **a rib was dislocated and** the attached **vertebra** was torn out **with it,** the animal is **a *tereifa*,** even if the spinal cord remains intact.

Rav goes further: *ne'ekra tzela v'chulya imah* — if a rib is uprooted together with its vertebra, *v'afilu achat treifah* — even a single such rib renders the animal a *treifah*. Until now we have been discussing ribs broken or uprooted from their articulation with the spine; here Rav speaks of a rib removed entirely along with the vertebra to which it is attached.

### Challenge: Opposing Ribs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי לֵיהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא וְרַב אַסִּי לְרַב: נֶעֶקְרָה צֵלָע מִכָּאן וְצֵלָע מִכָּאן, וְחוּלְיָא קַיֶּימֶת, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: גִּיסְטְרָא קָאָמְרִיתוּ?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana and Rav Asi said to Rav:** If **a rib was dislocated from here and** another **rib from there,** i.e., both ribs connected to a single vertebra were dislocated, **but** the **vertebra** itself remains **intact, what is** the *halakha*? Rav **said to them: Are you saying** that an animal that was **sliced** in half is a *tereifa*? This animal is considered to have been sliced and already has the status of a carcass, as it is already considered dead (see 21a).

Rav Kahana and Rav Assi challenged Rav: what of a case in which a rib from each side — an opposing pair — is uprooted, but the vertebra in the middle remains intact? They assumed this was a case Rav had not yet addressed — more severe than just a rib, but perhaps less than a rib with its vertebra, since the vertebra itself is present.

Rav's response was striking: *gistera ka ramitu* — "You are presenting me with a *gistera*?" The *gistera* is an animal split in half widthwise; even while still alive, such an animal is considered a *neveilah* — not merely a *treifah* but a carcass outright. Rav apparently regarded the removal of an opposing pair of ribs as equivalent to splitting the animal in half — a remarkable and, one might say, counterintuitive leap.

### Gemara Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רַב נָמֵי גִּיסְטְרָא קָאָמַר? כִּי קָאָמַר רַב – צֵלָע בְּלֹא חוּלְיָא. וְהָא צֵלָע וְחוּלְיָא קָאָמַר? צֵלָע וַחֲצִי חוּלְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't Rav also say** that **a sliced** animal is a *tereifa*, since he said that if a rib was dislocated along with the attached vertebra, the animal is a *tereifa*? In such a case, the opposite rib is inevitably detached. If so, the animal should be considered a carcass. The Gemara responds: **When Rav said** that such an animal is a *tereifa*, he was referring to a case where the **rib** was dislocated **without the vertebra. But didn't** Rav explicitly **say** his statement with regard to **a rib and vertebra** that were dislocated? The Gemara responds: Rav meant that if **a rib and half** its attached **vertebra** were dislocated, the animal is a *tereifa*. The opposite rib, however, is intact and connected to the remainder of the vertebra.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא וְרַב אַסִּי, צֵלָע בְּלֹא חוּלְיָא אָמְרִי, וַאֲמַר לְהוּ: ״גִּיסְטְרָא קָאָמְרִיתוּ״? וְהָאָמַר עוּלָּא: בֶּן זַכַּאי אָמַר: נֶעְקְרוּ – בְּרוֹב צַד אֶחָד, נִשְׁתַּבְּרוּ – בְּרוֹב שְׁנֵי צְדָדִין. אָמַר לָךְ: הָתָם זֶה שֶׁלֹּא כְּנֶגֶד זֶה, הָכָא זֶה כְּנֶגֶד זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **By inference,** one may conclude **that Rav Kahana and Rav Asi stated** their question with regard to a less serious case, i.e., where **a rib** from each side was torn out **without** the **vertebra** being damaged at all. **And** yet, Rav **said to them: Are you saying** that an animal that was **sliced** in half is a *tereifa*? Rather, this animal is a carcass and is certainly prohibited. **And** how could Rav say this? **But didn't Ulla say** that **ben Zakkai says:** If the ribs were **dislocated,** even **a majority of one side** is enough to render the animal a *tereifa*, while if the ribs were **broken, a majority of both sides** is necessary? If so, any animal with fewer than six dislocated ribs should be kosher, as long as the spine is undamaged. The Gemara responds: Rav could have **said to you: There,** where ben Zakkai says that six dislocated ribs on one side render the animal a *tereifa*, this is referring only to ribs **not** dislocated **one opposite the other. Here,** where Rav says that two dislocated ribs render the animal a carcass, this is referring to ribs dislocated **one opposite the other.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּרוֹב שְׁנֵי צְדָדִין, וּבְרוֹב שְׁנֵי צְדָדִין אִי אֶפְשָׁר דְּלָא קָיְימָא (חד) [חֲדָא] מִינַּיְיהוּ זֶה כְּנֶגֶד זֶה! הָתָם – בּוּכְנָא בְּלָא אֲסִיתָא, הָכָא – בּוּכְנָא וַאֲסִיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But doesn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say** that the animal is a *tereifa* only if **a majority of both sides** was dislocated, **and in a majority of both sides, it is impossible that one of them is not situated opposite another?** Still, Rabbi Yoḥanan says that the animal is a *tereifa* and not a carcass. The Gemara responds: **There,** Rabbi Yoḥanan is referring to a case where **the pestle,** i.e., the end of the rib, was torn out **without the mortar,** the socket of the vertebra in which it sits, leaving the spine completely intact. In such a case, the animal is a *tereifa* only if twelve ribs were dislocated, even though this necessarily includes one rib opposite another. **Here,** where Rav says that two dislocated ribs opposite one another render the animal a carcass, that is referring to a case where the **pestle and mortar** were torn out together, damaging the spine.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ דְּרַב? לָא שְׁמִיעַ לְהוּ דְּרַב. וְלִיבְעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ כִּדְרַב? סָבְרִי: לִיבְעֵי מִינֵּיהּ חֲדָא דְּפָרֵישׁ לַן תַּרְתֵּי, דְּאִי בָּעֵינַן מִינֵּיהּ חֲדָא, הָנִיחָא אִי אֲמַר לַן ״טְרֵפָה״ – כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן תַּרְתֵּי, אִי אֲמַר לַן ״כְּשֵׁרָה״ – אַכַּתִּי תַּרְתֵּי קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לַן. אִי הָכִי, הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי דְּקָא בָּעֵי מִינֵּיהּ תַּרְתֵּי, הָנִיחָא אִי אֲמַר לְהוּ ״כְּשֵׁרָה״ – כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן חֲדָא; וְאִי אֲמַר לְהוּ ״טְרֵפָה״, אַכַּתִּי חֲדָא מִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ. סָבְרִי: אִם כֵּן מִירְתָּח קָא רָתַח. חֲדָא טְרֵפָה, תַּרְתֵּי מִיבַּעְיָא? וְהָא קָא אָמְרִי לֵיהּ וְלָא רָתַח, כֵּיוָן דְּקָאָמַר לְהוּ ״גִּיסְטְרָא קָאָמְרִיתוּ״ – הַיְינוּ רִיתְחֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** why did Rav Kahana and Rav Asi ask anything of Rav? Their case in question **is** identical to the statement of **Rav.** If Rav says that the animal is a *tereifa* if a rib and part of its vertebra were dislocated, that should be the *halakha* all the more so if ribs opposite one another were dislocated in addition to part of the attached vertebra. The Gemara responds: Rav Kahana and Rav Asi **did not hear** that statement **of Rav. And let them ask** about the simpler case of one dislocated rib, **like that** which **Rav** himself discussed. Why ask specifically about two ribs opposite one another? The Gemara responds: **They reasoned: Let us ask him one** question through **which he** will **explain to us two** different cases. **As, if we ask him** about only **one** dislocated rib, **this works out well if he says to us** that the animal is **a *tereifa*,** because we can infer that **all the more so** if **two** ribs opposite one another are dislocated, the animal is a *tereifa*. But **if he says to us** that it is **kosher, we** must **still ask** with regard to a case of **two** dislocated ribs. The Gemara objects: **If that is so,** then **now, too, when they ask him** about **two** ribs, they may not receive an answer with regard to both cases. Granted, **this works out well if he says to them** that an animal with two dislocated ribs opposite one another is **kosher,** since they can infer that **all the more so** it is kosher if only **one** rib is dislocated. **But if he says to them** that it is **a *tereifa*, they** must **still ask** with regard to a case of only **one** dislocated rib. The Gemara responds: **They reasoned** that it is better to ask about a case where two ribs opposite one another were dislocated, because **if** it is **so** that Rav holds that the animal is a *tereifa* if even one rib is dislocated, then **he would become angry** and respond: If an animal with even **one** dislocated rib is **a *tereifa*, is** it **necessary** to ask about an animal with **two** dislocated ribs? The Gemara objects: **But they did say** this question **to him and he did not become angry,** despite the fact that he holds that even one dislocated rib renders the animal a *tereifa*. The Gemara responds: **When he said to them: Are you saying** that an animal that was **sliced** in half is a *tereifa*, **this is his anger.** Rav Kahana and Rav Asi understood from this response that an animal with two dislocated ribs opposite one another is a carcass, but an animal with one dislocated rib is a *tereifa*.

## Amoraic Treifot

### Shmuel's Three Treifot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב שֵׁילָא אָמַר רַב מַתְנָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: נֶעֶקְרָה צֵלָע מֵעִיקָּרָהּ, וְגוּלְגּוֹלֶת שֶׁנֶּחְבְּסָה בְּרוּבָּה, וּבָשָׂר הַחוֹפֶה אֶת רוֹב הַכָּרֵס בְּרוּבּוֹ – טְרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabba bar Rav Sheila says** that **Rav Mattana says** that **Shmuel says:** If **a rib was ripped from its root, or** if the **skull was crushed in its majority, or** if **the flesh that envelops the majority of the rumen** was damaged **in its majority,** the animal is **a *tereifa*.**

The Gemara now records three *treifot* transmitted by Rabba bar bar Shila in the name of Rav Matna in the name of Shmuel. These belong to the corpus of Amoraic *treifot* that supplement the *mishnah*'s list. The three are: *ne'ekra tzela me'ikara* — a rib fully uprooted from its base; *gulgoles shenichbesa beruba* — a skull bashed in over the majority of its surface (without necessarily cracking through); and *basar hofe et rov hakeres beruba* — the abdominal muscle covering the majority of the *keres* (the rumen), if the majority of that muscle is torn.

### Rib vs. Vertebra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶעֶקְרָה צֵלָע מֵעִיקָּרָהּ טְרֵפָה? וּרְמִינְהוּ: כַּמָּה חִסָּרוֹן בְּשִׁדְרָה? בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: שְׁתֵּי חוּלְיוֹת, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: חוּלְיָא אַחַת, וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: וְכֵן לִטְרֵפָה. הָכָא – צֵלָע בְּלֹא חוּלְיָא, הָתָם – חוּלְיָא בְּלֹא צֵלָע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Did Shmuel really say that if **a rib was ripped from its root,** the animal is **a *tereifa*? But raise a contradiction** from a mishna (*Oholot* 2:3): **How much** is considered **a deficiency in the spine** of a corpse so that it will not be considered a full corpse that would render one impure in a tent? **Beit Shammai say: Two** missing **vertebrae, and Beit Hillel say: One vertebra. And Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: And** just as Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel disagree with regard to ritual impurity, **so too** they disagree with regard **to a *tereifa*,** i.e., according to Beit Hillel an animal missing only one vertebra is a *tereifa*. Evidently, Shmuel holds that a missing vertebra renders the animal a *tereifa*, but a dislocated rib does not. The Gemara responds: This is not difficult. **Here,** where Shmuel says that a rib ripped from its root renders the animal a *tereifa*, he is referring to a case where the **rib** was torn out **without the vertebra. There,** where he says that a missing vertebra renders the animal a *tereifa* according to Beit Hillel, he is referring to a case where the **vertebra** was missing **without** the **rib** being dislocated. But if the rib itself was ripped from its root, the animal is a *tereifa*.

The Gemara immediately raises a challenge to the first item — one uprooted rib as a *treifah* — from a *baraita* concerning *tumah* in an *ohel*. How much of the spinal column must be missing for a human skeleton to lose its status as a complete skeleton (and therefore no longer convey *tum'at ohel*)? Beit Shammai: two vertebrae. Beit Hillel: one vertebra. Shmuel himself is reported to have taught that the same threshold applies to *treifah* in an animal: whatever level of skeletal incompleteness prevents the skeleton from conveying *tumah* also means an animal cannot survive — and is thus a *treifah*. That ruling is about vertebrae, not ribs. How then does Shmuel add "one uprooted rib" to the list?

The Gemara resolves this cleanly: the two rulings address different cases. *Hacha tzela belo hulya* — here we speak of a rib without a vertebra: a rib uprooted from its joint, carrying no portion of the vertebra. *Hatam hulya belo tzela* — there we speak of a vertebra without a rib: a vertebra removed from a location where there is no rib attached (as in the lower portion of the spinal column). Either alone renders the animal a *treifah*. Two new additions to the list thus emerge: an uprooted rib (without the vertebra), and a removed vertebra (without a rib).

Rashi reads *ne'ekra tzela me'ikara* as meaning a rib with no part of the vertebra — but this conflicts with what was established on 52a, where even half a vertebra sufficed for Rav's ruling. Tosafot therefore reads the phrase as meaning a rib with *part* of the vertebra, not the full vertebra.

The Gemara also notes: it is straightforward to imagine a rib uprooted without taking the vertebra entirely; but how does one remove a vertebra without the rib coming with it? The answer is that in areas of the spine where no ribs are attached — the floating vertebrae lower in the spinal column — a vertebra can indeed be removed without any rib.

### Beit Shammai vs. Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא צֵלָע בְּלֹא חוּלְיָא – מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ, אֶלָּא חוּלְיָא בְּלֹא צֵלָע – הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? בְּשִׁילְהֵי כַּפְלֵי. מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא: וְלִתְנְיַיהּ גַּבֵּי קוּלֵּי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וְחוּמְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל? אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב: כִּי אִיתְּשִׁיל – לְעִנְיַן טוּמְאָה אִיתְּשִׁיל, דְּהָווּ לְהוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי לְחוּמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** with regard to a case where **a rib** was dislocated **without the vertebra, you find this** commonly. **But how can you find** a case of a missing **vertebra without** a dislocated **rib?** The Gemara responds: This can occur **at the ends of the flanks,** where there are vertebrae with no ribs attached to them. Shmuel said that just as Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel disagree with regard to ritual impurity, so too they disagree with regard to a *tereifa*. **Rav Oshaya objects to this** statement: **But** if that is so, then the opinion of Beit Hillel is the more stringent one, as they hold that even one missing vertebra renders the animal a *tereifa*. Accordingly, **let** the *tanna* **teach** this dispute **along with** the list of **leniencies of Beit Shammai and stringencies of Beit Hillel** in tractate *Eduyyot*. **Rav said to him: When** the question **was asked, it was asked with regard to** the **ritual impurity** of a corpse. That is the source of the dispute between Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai, and on that matter, **Beit Shammai constitute** the **stringent** opinion. The parallel disagreement with regard to *tereifot* is not mentioned explicitly in any mishna or *baraita*, and it was therefore omitted from the list in tractate *Eduyyot*.

An objection is raised by Rav Hoshaya: given that Shmuel's teaching about vertebrae and *treifah* derives from the *tumah* debate between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, why is this not listed among the well-known cases of *kulei Beit Shammai* and *chumrei Beit Hillel* — the leniencies of Beit Shammai and the stringencies of Beit Hillel? In the context of *tumah*, Beit Shammai is *machmir* (two vertebrae must be removed before the skeleton loses its status) and Beit Hillel is *meikel* (one suffices). But if we apply the same logic to *treifah*, the positions reverse: Beit Hillel becomes *machmir* (one vertebra already renders the animal a *treifah*) and Beit Shammai becomes *meikel* (two must be removed). This would be a case of Beit Hillel being strict — a reversal of the usual pattern.

Rava responds: the original debate between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel was framed entirely in the context of *tumah* — specifically, the *tum'at ohel* of a human skeleton. In that framing, Beit Shammai is indeed stringent and Beit Hillel lenient. Shmuel then extrapolated the same principle to *treifah*, at which point the positions with respect to stringency and leniency swap. But the debate itself was not framed as a *treifah* debate, and therefore it does not appear in the canonical lists of *kulei* and *chumrei*. This, incidentally, is the classic model of a ruling that is consistent in its internal logic even as it appears to produce reversed stringency across two domains.

### Skull Height or Circumference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְגוּלְגּוֹלֶת שֶׁנֶּחְבְּסָה בְּרוּבָּהּ. בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: רוֹב גּוֹבְהָהּ, אוֹ רוֹב הֶיקֵּיפָהּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to Shmuel's statement: **Or** if the **skull was crushed in its majority,** the animal is a *tereifa*. **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma:** Is this referring to **the majority of its height or the majority of its circumference?** The Gemara responds: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The second item on Shmuel's list — *gulgoles shenichbesa beruba*, the skull bashed in over its majority — prompts a question from Rami bar Ami: when we say "majority," do we measure *rov govha* — the majority of the skull's height — or *rov heikeifa* — the majority of its circumference? Rashi notes that the circumference is larger than the height; the two measures therefore yield meaningfully different thresholds. Rashi locates the midpoint of the height at approximately the level of the eyes — a consideration familiar from the anatomy of the face, where the eyes fall at the midpoint of the skull's vertical dimension. The Gemara offers no resolution: *teiku*.

### Abdominal Muscle Torn

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבָשָׂר הַחוֹפֶה רוֹב הַכָּרֵס בְּרוּבּוֹ. בָּעֵי רַב אָשֵׁי: בְּרוֹב קָרוּעַ, אוֹ בְּרוֹב נָטוּל? תִּפְשׁוֹט לֵיהּ מִדִּתְנַן: כָּרֵס הַפְּנִימִית שֶׁנִּיקְּבָה, אוֹ שֶׁנִּקְרַע רוֹב הַחִיצוֹנָה, וְאָמְרִי בְּמַעְרְבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: כׇּל הַכָּרֵס כּוּלּוֹ זוֹ הִיא כָּרֵס הַפְּנִימִי, וְאֵי זֶהוּ כָּרֵס הַחִיצוֹנָה? בָּשָׂר הַחוֹפֶה אֶת רוֹב הַכָּרֵס. מִידֵּי הוּא טַעְמָא, אֶלָּא לִשְׁמוּאֵל, הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מָקוֹם שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מֵילָת.
>
> **Translation:** Shmuel also said: **Or** if **the flesh that envelops the majority of the rumen** was damaged **in its majority,** the animal is a *tereifa*. **Rav Ashi raises a dilemma:** Is this referring even to a case **where** the **majority** of the length of the flesh was **torn? Or** is it referring only to a case **where** the **majority** of the flesh was **removed,** but if it was torn along the majority of its length the animal remains kosher? The Gemara responds: **Resolve** the dilemma **from that which we learned** in the mishna: If **the internal rumen was perforated or most of the external rumen was torn,** the animal is a *tereifa*. **And in the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **they said in the name of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina: The entire rumen is the internal rumen. And** if so, **which is the external rumen?** It is the **flesh that envelops the majority of the rumen.** Apparently, the animal is a *tereifa* even if the flesh is torn in its majority. The Gemara rejects the proof: **This explanation is** meant **only to** clarify the statement of **Shmuel,** and Shmuel does not agree with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, as **this** is what **Rabbi Ya'akov bar Naḥmani said** that **Shmuel said:** The case of: Most of the external rumen was torn, mentioned in the mishna, where the animal is a *tereifa*, is not referring to where the flesh enveloping the majority of the rumen was torn, but rather where the majority of the rumen itself was torn. The internal rumen is the **place** in the rumen **that has no wool,** i.e., downy projections on the inside of the rumen. Accordingly, one cannot prove anything about the statement of Shmuel based on an explanation of the mishna offered by Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina.

The third item — the abdominal muscle covering the majority of the *keres* — prompts a parallel question from Rav Ashi: does *beruba* mean the majority of the muscle is *torn* (*rov karua*), or the majority is *removed* (*rov natul*)? The latter would be a far more expansive standard.

The Gemara attempts to resolve this from the *mishnah*, which speaks of the outer *keres* being torn over its majority. A tradition from Eretz Yisrael in the name of Rabbi Yochanan bar Chanina identifies the entire stomach as the *keres hapnimis* — the inner stomach — and the *keres hachitzona* as the abdominal muscle itself. If that reading is correct, the *mishnah* already addresses this case, and "majority" there clearly means *torn*, not *removed*.

However, Shmuel himself explains the outer *keres* differently: it refers to the part of the stomach wall where the felt-like inner lining ends. On Shmuel's own reading, the abdominal muscle is not mentioned in the *mishnah* at all — it is a genuinely new *treifah* he is introducing. If it is a new category, the *mishnah*'s language about "torn" cannot resolve the question of what "majority" means for this muscle. The question remains unresolved.

## Animal Attack Treifot

### Rav Yehuda: Wolf & Netz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּדְרוּסַת הַזְּאֵב. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: בִּבְהֵמָה – מִן הַזְּאֵב וּלְמַעְלָה, וּבְעוֹפוֹת – מִן הַנֵּץ וּלְמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **And** an animal that was **clawed by a wolf**  is a *tereifa*. **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: In** the case of **an animal,** it is a *tereifa* if it was clawed by any predator **from the** size of a **wolf and upward. And with regard to birds,** they are *tereifot* if they were clawed by any predator **from the** size of **a hawk and upward.**

The *mishnah* turns to *derusah* — the damage caused by an animal attack. The Gemara understands the concern here to involve not simply the physical wound but something transmitted through the attack — perhaps a venom-like substance, or more plausibly some form of infection introduced through violence to the body. It is worth noting that the later *mishnah* will establish that poison alone, without bodily assault, does not render an animal a *treifah*; what is at issue here is a combination of attack and toxic consequence.

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav establishes: for large animals (*behemah*), the attacker must be a wolf or larger to create a *treifah* concern. For birds, it must be a *netz* — a sparrow hawk — or larger.

### R. Yehuda: Lion vs. Wolf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? אִילֵּימָא לְמַעוֹטֵי חָתוּל – תְּנֵינָא: ״וּדְרוּסַת הַזְּאֵב״, וְכִי תֵּימָא הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דִּזְאֵב בְּגַסָּה נָמֵי דָּרֵיס – וְהָא תְּנַן: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: דְּרוּסַת הַזְּאֵב בַּדַּקָּה, וּדְרוּסַת אֲרִי בַּגַּסָּה! וְכִי תֵּימָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מִפְלָג פְּלִיג, וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי בִּנְיָמִין בַּר יֶפֶת אָמַר רַבִּי אִלְעָא: לֹא בָּא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אֶלָּא לְפָרֵשׁ דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים! גַּבְרָא אַגַּבְרָא קָא רָמֵית? אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם לְמַעוֹטֵי חָתוּל, מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא אוֹרְחָא דְּמִלְּתָא קָתָנֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What does** this statement of Rav, that an animal is a *tereifa* if it was clawed by a predator at least as large as a wolf, **serve to exclude? If we say** it serves **to exclude a cat** that clawed an animal, since it is smaller than a wolf, **we** already **learned** in the mishna: **And** an animal **clawed by a wolf** is a *tereifa*. One can infer from this that cats do not render an animal a *tereifa*. **And if you would say: This** mishna **teaches us that a wolf** can **also** effectively **claw a large** animal, e.g., cattle, but it may still be that a cat can render a small animal a *tereifa*, this cannot be; **didn't we learn** in the mishna that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** If it was **clawed by a wolf in** the case of **a small** animal, **or clawed by a lion in** the case of **a large** animal, the animal is a *tereifa*? **And if you would say** that **Rabbi Yehuda disagrees** with the Rabbis, and they hold that a wolf can also render large livestock *tereifa* by clawing, this too is impossible, as **doesn't Rabbi Binyamin bar Yefet say** that **Rabbi Ela says:** The statement of **Rabbi Yehuda** in the mishna **comes only to explain the statement of the Rabbis?** The Gemara responds: **Are you setting** the statement of one **man against** the statement of another **man?** Even if Rabbi Ela holds that Rabbi Yehuda comes only to explain the statement of the Rabbis, Rav may still hold that Rabbi Yehuda disagrees with the Rabbis. Or, **if you wish, say** instead: **Actually,** everyone agrees that a wolf can render only a small animal a *tereifa* through clawing. Nevertheless, the statement of Rav serves **to exclude a cat** from the ability to render even a small animal a *tereifa*. **Lest you say** that the mishna **taught** about a wolf only because this is **the manner** in which **the matter** typically occurs, but a cat may in fact render a small animal a *tereifa*, Rav **teaches us** that it is referring specifically to a wolf.

The Gemara asks what novel ruling this adds, since the *mishnah* already establishes that a wolf is the relevant attacker. One suggestion: perhaps Rav Yehuda is teaching that a wolf is sufficient for *all* large animals, including oxen — one might have thought a wolf's attack on a large bovine would be inconsequential. But this suggestion is challenged by Rabbi Yehuda's position in the *mishnah* itself: *drusas haze'ev bedaka* — a wolf is only the threshold for small animals like sheep and goats; for large animals, one requires a lion (*ari*).

A tannaitic tradition from Rabbi Binyamin bar Yafes in the name of Rabbi Ila holds that Rabbi Yehuda was not disputing the *Tanna Kamma* but merely clarifying: the *Tanna Kamma*'s wolf applies only to small animals, and Rabbi Yehuda is specifying what is required for large ones. If that is so, Rav Yehuda's ruling simply aligns with that clarification — stating explicitly that a wolf suffices for all animals, *against* Rabbi Yehuda's position.

The Gemara offers a second resolution: Rav Yehuda's ruling excludes not a large animal from the wolf's reach, but a *cat* from below the wolf. The *mishnah* mentions a wolf — perhaps because wolves were the typical attacker of sheep, one might generalize that any large predator capable of injuring sheep would suffice. Rav Yehuda teaches that the threshold is specifically the wolf, no smaller.

### Rav Chisda: Cat & Weasel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב עַמְרָם אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: דְּרוּסַת חָתוּל וּנְמִיָּיה בִּגְדָיִים וּטְלָאִים, דְּרוּסַת חוּלְדָּה בְּעוֹפוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Amram says** that **Rav Ḥisda says:** If an animal was **clawed by a cat or a mongoose, in** the case of **kids or lambs,** which are very small, it is a *tereifa*. If it was **clawed by a weasel in** the case of **birds,** it is a *tereifa*.

Rav Amram in the name of Rav Chisda adds further detail: an attack by a *chatul* (cat) or *nemiyah* (weasel or marten) on *kivshim u'gedayim* — young lambs or kid goats — does constitute a *derusah* concern. The relevant variable is not only the species of attacker but the size of the prey. Against the adult versions of these animals, a cat or weasel would not suffice; but against their young, these smaller predators can inflict the kind of attack that creates the *treifah* concern. Similarly, an attack by a *chuldah* (weasel) against birds is sufficient.

### Baraita: Organ Piercing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: דְּרוּסַת חָתוּל, נֵץ וּנְמִיָּיה עַד שֶׁתִּינָּקֵב לֶחָלָל, אֲבָל דְּרוּסָה לֵית לְהוּ! וְתִסְבְּרָא נֵץ לָא דָּרֵיס? וְהָתְנַן: וּדְרוּסַת הַנֵּץ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: An animal **clawed by a cat, hawk, or mongoose** is not rendered a *tereifa* **until** one of its internal organs **is perforated to** its **recesses.** One may infer: **But they,** i.e., a cat, hawk, and mongoose, **do not have** the ability to render an animal a *tereifa* through the **clawing** itself. The Gemara questions the inference: **And can you understand** that **a hawk does not** effectively **claw** an animal? **But didn't we learn** in the mishna: **And** a small bird **clawed by a hawk** is rendered a *tereifa*?

A *baraita* is cited that appears to contradict this: *drusas chatul netz u'nemiyah* — attacks by a cat, *netz*, or *nemiyah* — are not a concern for *derusah* unless they also pierce an inner organ (*tinakev l'chalal*). The attack alone, without such piercing, does not render the animal forbidden. The *baraita* seems to say explicitly that these animals do not create *derusah*.

The Gemara notes one apparent internal problem: the *mishnah* includes the *netz* among those that do create *derusah* by birds, yet the *baraita* places the *netz* in the list of animals that do not. This is resolved cleanly: the *mishnah* addresses the *netz* attacking birds; the *baraita* addresses the *netz* — and cat and *nemiyah* — attacking other animals. But the ruling of Rav Chisda remains in tension with the *baraita*, since Rav Chisda says cats and weasels do create *derusah* against young goats and lambs, while the *baraita* says they do not.

### Birebbi: Rescue Factor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּעוֹפוֹת, כָּאן בִּגְדָיִים וּטְלָאִים. מִכׇּל מָקוֹם, לְרַב חִסְדָּא קַשְׁיָא, הוּא דְּאָמַר כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא: בְּרִיבִּי אוֹמֵר: לֹא אָמְרוּ אֵין דְּרוּסָה אֶלָּא בְּמָקוֹם שֶׁאֵין מַצִּילִין, אֲבָל בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ מַצִּילִין – יֵשׁ דְּרוּסָה.
>
> **Translation:** That** is **not difficult. Here,** where the mishna states that a hawk can effectively claw, it is **with regard to birds,** while **there,** where the *baraita* states that a hawk does not effectively claw, it is **with regard to kids and lambs.** The Gemara returns to its initial objection: **In any case,** the *baraita* poses **a difficulty for Rav Ḥisda,** who says that a cat may render kids and lambs *tereifot* through clawing. The Gemara responds: Rav Ḥisda **states** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **that *tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **The Distinguished One says: They said** that a kid is **not** effectively **clawed** by a cat **only in a place where there are none** present to **save** it. **But in a place where there are** bystanders trying to **save** the kid, it **is** effectively **clawed,** since the cat is angered and injects venom into the wound.

The Gemara resolves this by citing a *tanna* known as *Birebbi*, who offers a principle that reframes the entire discussion. *Lo amru ein drusa ela b'makom she'ein matzilim* — the ruling that these animals do not create *derusah* applies only in a situation where there is no prospect of rescue intervention. When a cat, weasel, or similar predator attacks and is not afraid that anyone will intervene to save its prey, it attacks in a relatively measured, controlled way — and that kind of attack does not produce the *drusa* effect that creates a *treifah*.

*Aval b'makom she'yesh matzilim* — but where there is prospect of intervention, the predator attacks more urgently and viciously, knowing it may be deprived of its prey. In that case, *yesh drusa* — there is *derusah*. The violence of the attack, and whatever toxic or infectious consequence flows from it, is directly proportional to the threat perceived by the attacker.

This distinction elegantly reconciles Rav Chisda with the *baraita*. When Rav Chisda says cats and weasels create *derusah* against young goats and lambs, he speaks of a domestic or farm setting — precisely the context where human rescue is likely, prompting a more ferocious attack. The *baraita*'s ruling that they do not create *derusah* applies to an open field or unguarded setting, where no rescuer is anticipated and the attack is less severe.

The Gemara breaks off at this point, with the full elaboration of this principle to be taken up in the following session.
