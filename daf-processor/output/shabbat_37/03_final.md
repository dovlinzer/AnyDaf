# Shabbat 37 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Garuf vKatum: Shehiya vs…

### Mishna Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כִּירָה שֶׁהִסִּיקוּהָ בְּקַשׁ וּבִגְבָבָא — נוֹתְנִים עָלֶיהָ תַּבְשִׁיל. בְּגֶפֶת וּבְעֵצִים — לֹא יִתֵּן עַד שֶׁיִּגְרוֹף, אוֹ עַד שֶׁיִּתֵּן אֶת הָאֵפֶר. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: חַמִּין, אֲבָל לֹא תַּבְשִׁיל. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: חַמִּין וְתַבְשִׁיל. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נוֹטְלִין, אֲבָל לֹא מַחֲזִירִין. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אַף מַחֲזִירִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **a stove that was lit** on Shabbat eve **with straw** or **with rakings,** scraps collected from the field, one may **place** a pot of **cooked food atop it** on Shabbat. The fire in this stove was certainly extinguished while it was still day, as both straw and rakings are materials that burn quickly. However, if the stove was lit **with pomace,** pulp that remains from sesame seeds, olives, and the like after the oil is squeezed from them, **and** if it was lit **with wood,** one may **not place** a pot atop it on Shabbat **until he sweeps** the coals from the stove while it is still day **or until he places ashes** on the coals, so that the fire will not ignite on Shabbat. **Beit Shammai say:** Even after one has swept away the coals, it is only permitted to place **hot water** on it, as it is sufficiently hot and does not require additional cooking, **but not cooked food.** Since, in general, one prefers that food will cook more, there is concern lest he come to ignite the fire by stoking the coals. **And Beit Hillel say:** Both **hot water and cooked food** may be placed. **Beit Shammai say: One may remove** a pot from the stove on Shabbat **but** may **not return** it. **And Beit Hillel say:** One may **even return it.**

The second chapter of *Masechet Shabbat*, known as *Kira*, addresses the fundamental questions of keeping food on a flame before *Shabbos* and of removing and returning food to that flame. The *Mishna* establishes that in order to leave food on a stove or oven from before *Shabbos*, the oven must be *garuf o katum* — either swept out of its coals or have ashes placed upon them. This requirement, expressed by the *Gemara* as *garuf ve'katum*, functions as a safeguard against the concern that one might come to stir the coals on *Shabbos* in order to intensify the heat. In contemporary practice, the *blech* — a metal sheet placed over the burners — serves as our translation of this concept.

### Chananya's Lenient View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: הַאי ״לֹא יִתֵּן״ — לֹא יַחֲזִיר הוּא, אֲבָל לְשַׁהוֹת — מְשַׁהִין, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ גָּרוּף וְאֵינוֹ קָטוּם. וּמַנִּי — חֲנַנְיָה הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא, חֲנַנְיָה אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁהוּא כְּמַאֲכָל בֶּן דְּרוּסַאי — מוּתָּר לְשַׁהוֹתוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי כִּירָה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ גָּרוּף וְאֵינוֹ קָטוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The students **raised a dilemma** with regard to the explanation of the mishna. **That** which we learned in the mishna: One may **not place, does it** mean that **one may not return** a pot that he took off the fire and wishes to return on Shabbat; **however, to leave** the pot from Shabbat eve into Shabbat, **one may leave** it **even though** this stove **is not swept** of its coals **and** its coals **are not covered with ashes? And,** according to this, **whose** opinion **is it** in this mishna? **It** is the opinion of **Ḥananya. As it was taught** in a *baraita*, **Ḥananya says: Any** food **that** has already been cooked to the extent **of the food of ben Drosai,** who would only cook his food the minimum amount necessary, **one is permitted to leave it atop a stove** on Shabbat **even though** the stove **is not swept and not covered with ashes.**

The *Gemara*, however, raises the possibility that the *Mishna*'s requirement of *garuf ve'katum* applies only when one wishes to return food to the flame on *Shabbos* (*chazara*), but not merely to leave food on the flame from before *Shabbos* (*shehiya*). This reading draws support from the position of Chananya, who holds that once food has reached the level of *ma'achal ben drusai* — roughly half-cooked — one may leave it on an untreated flame from before *Shabbos* without sweeping out the coals. As will become clear, this is precisely the ruling adopted by *Ashkenazim*: once food is partially cooked, no *blech* is required merely to leave food on the flame. The *blech* is required only when one wishes to return food to the flame on *Shabbos* itself.

### Gemara's Core Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִילְמָא — לְשַׁהוֹת תְּנַן, וְאִי גָרוּף וְקָטוּם — אֵין, אִי לָא — לָא, וְכָל שֶׁכֵּן לְהַחֲזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps,** that which **we learned** in the mishna: One may not place, means one may not **leave** it on the fire from Shabbat eve. **And if** the coals in the stove were **swept** or **covered with ashes, yes,** one may leave the pot on the stove. **And if not, no,** one may not leave it, **and all the more so** one may not **return** it to the stove on Shabbat under any circumstances.

The central question of this entire sugya, then, is whether the *Mishna*'s demand for *garuf ve'katum* — and by extension our requirement of a *blech* — applies equally to *shehiya* (leaving food on from before *Shabbos*) or only to *chazara* (returning food to the flame on *Shabbos*). The *Gemara* works through a series of proofs and counter-proofs in order to resolve this question.

### Tocha vs Al Gaba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֶלְבּוֹ אָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא עַל גַּבָּהּ, אֲבָל לְתוֹכָהּ — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥelbo said** that **Rav Ḥama bar Gurya said** that **Rav said: They only taught** that placing is permitted with regard to a stove as far as placing a pot **atop it** is concerned. **However,** placing a pot **inside it** is **prohibited.**

About ten lines into 37a, the *Gemara* introduces a teaching of Rabbi Chelbo. The text reads: *de'amar Rabbi Chelbo amar Rabbi Chama bar Guria amar Rav: lo shanu ela al gaba aval letocha assur* — "we only taught the leniency with respect to on top of the oven, but inside the oven it is forbidden." The distinction being drawn is between *tocha* — placing food inside the oven, suspended in the hollow — and *al gaba* — placing it on top of or against the outer rim or roof of the oven. 

Rashi explains that *al gaba* may refer either to placing a pot on the outer edge of the oven's rim, or to setting a covering over the opening and resting the pot on top of that covering, making it function much like a stovetop. In either case, the point is that *al gaba* is not the normal mode of cooking; it involves far less direct heat and is far less experiential of an act of cooking than placing something inside the oven proper. The significance of this distinction lies in the concern underlying the *garuf ve'katum* requirement: namely, that one might come to stir the coals. When something is inside the oven in the usual cooking position, both the heat and the psychological experience of cooking are more intense, and therefore the concern about stirring is more acute.

### Applying the Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא ״לְהַחֲזִיר״ תְּנַן — הַיְינוּ דְּשָׁנֵי בֵּין תּוֹכָהּ לְעַל גַּבָּהּ. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ ״לְשַׁהוֹת״ תְּנַן, מַה לִּי תּוֹכָהּ מַה לִּי עַל גַּבָּהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if you say** that **we learned returning** in the mishna, **that is why there is a** halakhic **difference between** placing a pot **inside it and** placing a pot **atop it.** If one returns it on Shabbat, placing it inside a stove that might have burning coals, there is concern that Shabbat would be desecrated. Therefore, it was only permitted to place cooked food atop the stove. **However, if you say** that **we learned leaving** in the mishna, **what is** the difference **to me** whether it is **inside** the stove and **what is** the difference **to me** whether it is **atop it?**

The *Gemara* now asks how this distinction between *tocha* and *al gaba* bears on our central question. The argument runs as follows: *ee amrat bishlama lehachzir tenan* — if you say the *Mishna* is speaking about returning food to the flame, then Rabbi Chelbo's distinction makes perfect sense. The concern driving *chazara* is not only that one might stir the coals, but also that the act of putting something back on the flame feels experientially like cooking. Placing food inside an open oven is far more reminiscent of cooking than placing it on the outer rim or on a cover. One could therefore understand why, even in a case where the oven has been treated (*garuf ve'katum*), we would still be more permissive about placing food on top than about placing it inside — the latter simply looks more like cooking. In our contemporary terms, putting food into the oven interior is a more cooking-like act than putting it on the stovetop, even if the stovetop is over a live flame.

But if the *Mishna* is speaking about *shehiya* — leaving food on from before *Shabbos* — then the entire concern is whether one will come to stir the coals, not about what the act looks like experientially. In that case, *ma li tocha ma li al gaba* — what difference does it make whether the pot is inside or on top? The concern about stirring is the same either way. Since Rabbi Chelbo does draw this distinction, the *Gemara* initially reasons that his teaching must be addressing *chazara*, the act of returning food, and this in turn would suggest that our *Mishna* too is about *chazara*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי סָבְרַתְּ רַבִּי חֶלְבּוֹ אַרֵישָׁא קָאֵי? אַסֵּיפָא קָאֵי: ״וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים אַף מַחֲזִירִין״ — וְאָמַר רַבִּי חֶלְבּוֹ אָמַר רַו חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא עַל גַּבָּהּ, אֲבָל תּוֹכָהּ — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Do you think** that **Rabbi Ḥelbo is referring to the first clause** of the mishna? No, he **is referring to the latter clause** of the mishna, which states: **And Beit Hillel say that one may even return. And** with regard to this **Rabbi Ḥelbo said** that **Rav Ḥama bar Gurya said** that **Rav said: They only taught** that one may return the cooked food **atop** the stove; **however, inside it** is **prohibited.**

The *Gemara*, however, immediately deflects this inference. *Mee savrat Rabbi Chelbo areisha kaee?* — Who says Rabbi Chelbo is addressing the first clause of the *Mishna* (concerning the conditions for leaving food on)? *Aseifa kaee* — he is addressing the concluding clause, where *Beit Hillel* says *af machzirin*, that one may even return food to the flame. Rabbi Chelbo's distinction between *tocha* and *al gaba* pertains to the end of the *Mishna*, which unambiguously concerns *chazara*. Our question about the beginning of the *Mishna* — whether *garuf ve'katum* is required for *shehiya* — therefore remains open.

### Double Oven Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שְׁתֵּי כִּירוֹת הַמַּתְאִימוֹת, אַחַת גְּרוּפָה וּקְטוּמָה, וְאַחַת שֶׁאֵינָהּ גְּרוּפָה וְאֵינָהּ קְטוּמָה: מְשַׁהִין עַל גַּבֵּי גְּרוּפָה וּקְטוּמָה וְאֵין מְשַׁהִין עַל שֶׁאֵינָהּ גְּרוּפָה וְאֵינָהּ קְטוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a resolution to this dilemma from that which was taught in the *Tosefta*: In the case of **two adjoining stoves** that share a common wall, in **one** of them, the coals were **swept** or **covered with ashes, and** in **one** the coals **were not swept and not covered with ashes;** the ruling with regard to leaving a pot atop them on Shabbat is as follows: **One may leave** food **atop** the one that was **swept** or **covered with ashes, and one may not leave** food **atop the** one **that was not swept and not covered with ashes.**

The *Gemara* introduces a new source: *be kirot hamat'imot* — a double oven, two cylindrical hollows side by side within a single unit. The *Tosefta* teaches: *achat gerufa u-ketuma ve-achat she-eina gerufa ve-eina ketuma, mash'hin al gabei gerufa u-ketuma ve-ein mash'hin al she-eina gerufa u-ketuma* — one of the paired ovens is swept and has ashes, the other does not; you may leave food on the treated one but not on the untreated one. 

The *chiddush* here is that even though the two ovens are a single unit and the untreated one is generating heat right beside the treated one, this does not disqualify the treated side. Each compartment is judged independently. Crucially for our question, the language is explicitly *mash'hin* — you may leave food on. This is unambiguously *shehiya*, not *chazara*. And the *Tosefta* says you may not do so on the untreated side. This constitutes a clear proof that *garuf ve'katum* is required even for merely leaving food on from before *Shabbos*, not only for returning it.

## Baraita: What Foods Allo…

### Rabbi Meir's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה הֵן מְשַׁהִין? — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: וְלֹא כְלוּם. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: חַמִּין, אֲבָל לֹא תַּבְשִׁיל. עָקַר — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל לֹא יַחֲזִיר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** to the crux of the matter, **what may one leave? Beit Shammai say: Nothing at all.** They dispute the *halakha* cited above. **And Beit Hillel say:** One may leave **hot water but not cooked food.** However, if one **removed** the cooked dish from atop the stove, **everyone,** Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, **agrees** that **one may not return** it atop the stove; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

Continuing the double-oven *baraita*, the text asks *u'ma hein mash'hin* — what exactly may one leave on the treated oven? Here the *Gemara* presents two versions of the *Beit Shammai*–*Beit Hillel* debate that are considerably stricter than what our *Mishna* records.

According to Rabbi Meir's version: *Beit Shammai omrim ve-lo klum* — *Beit Shammai* says nothing at all may be left on the flame. This is a strikingly severe position: it would mean that on *Shabbos* there could be no hot food whatsoever, a position that begins to resemble the *Karaite* rejection of any Shabbos fire, though there is no evidence the *Tzedukim* took this view. *Beit Hillel*, in Rabbi Meir's version, permits only hot water (*chamin*) to be left on the treated flame, but not cooked food (*tavshil*). And if one removed the pot from the flame, everyone — both *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* — agrees it may not be returned. According to this reading, neither school would have hot food on *Shabbos*; at best, *Beit Hillel* would have hot water.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: חַמִּין, אֲבָל לֹא תַּבְשִׁיל. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: חַמִּין וְתַבְשִׁיל. בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נוֹטְלִין, אֲבָל לֹא מַחֲזִירִין. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אַף מַחְזִירִין.
>
> **Translation:** According to the tradition that he received, that is the issue disputed between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel. **Beit Shammai say:** One may leave **hot water** on it **but not cooked food. And Beit Hillel say:** One may leave both **hot water and cooked food.** Furthermore, **Beit Shammai say: One may remove** a pot from the stove on Shabbat **but may not return** it. **And Beit Hillel say: One may even return** it.

Rabbi Yehuda presents a substantially more lenient and familiar version. In his account, *Beit Shammai* permits hot water but not cooked food; *Beit Hillel* permits both hot water and cooked food. Furthermore, *Beit Shammai* holds that one may remove the pot (*notlin*) but may not return it (*lo machzirin*), while *Beit Hillel* adds that one may even return it (*af machzirin*). This entire structure — the debate about water versus food, the debate about returning — maps exactly onto our *Mishna*, making Rabbi Yehuda the natural candidate for whose opinion our *Mishna* follows.

### Which Opinion?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא ״לְשַׁהוֹת״ תְּנַן, מַתְנִיתִין מַנִּי — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ ״לְהַחֲזִיר״ תְּנַן, מַתְנִיתִין מַנִּי? לָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְלָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר. אִי רַבִּי מֵאִיר — קַשְׁיָא לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי בַּחֲדָא, וּלְבֵית הִלֵּל בְּתַרְתֵּי. אִי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, קַשְׁיָא גְּרוּפָה וּקְטוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if you say** that the case **we learned** in our mishna was with regard to **leaving** the pot on the stove, in accordance with **whose** opinion is **our mishna? It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda. However, if you say** that the case **we learned** in our mishna was with regard to **returning** the pot to the stove, in accordance with **whose** opinion is **our mishna?** It is **neither** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda nor** with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir. If** you say that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** it is **difficult for Beit Shammai in one** respect. In our mishna, Beit Shammai permit some use of a stove on Shabbat; while according to Rabbi Meir in the *baraita*, Beit Shammai prohibit any use. **And for Beit Hillel** it is difficult **in two** respects. According to our understanding of the mishna, Beit Hillel permit both hot water and cooked food, contrary to Rabbi Meir's version of their opinion as stated in the *baraita*. Similarly, in our mishna, Beit Hillel permit returning the pot to the stove, contrary to Rabbi Meir's version of their opinion. **If** you explain that our mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda** in the *baraita*, it is **difficult** with regard to the issue of **sweeping** the coals **and covering** them **with ashes.**

The *Gemara* now uses this *baraita* to press the question of *shehiya* versus *chazara* further. *Ee amrat bishlama lish'hos tnan* — if our *Mishna* is about leaving food on from before *Shabbos*, then it aligns perfectly with Rabbi Yehuda. The *baraita* in Rabbi Yehuda's version is explicitly about what foods may be left on (*shehiya*) and requires *garuf ve'katum* for doing so. Our *Mishna* would simply be Rabbi Yehuda's position.

But *ela ee amrat lehachzir tnan* — if our *Mishna* is about returning food, and the requirement of *garuf ve'katum* applies only to *chazara* while *shehiya* is entirely unrestricted, then our *Mishna* cannot align with either Rabbi Meir or Rabbi Yehuda.

It cannot be Rabbi Meir, because according to Rabbi Meir even *Beit Hillel* only allows hot water on a treated flame. If our *Mishna* is read to mean that *shehiya* needs no *garuf ve'katum* at all, then the *Mishna* implies everyone agrees food may be left freely on an untreated flame — which flatly contradicts Rabbi Meir's *Beit Shammai* (nothing allowed) and *Beit Hillel* (only water, not food, and no returning). The *Gemara* formulates this as: *kashya le-Beit Shammai be-chada u-Beit Hillel be-tarti* — it contradicts *Beit Shammai* in one respect and *Beit Hillel* in two. According to *Beit Shammai* in Rabbi Meir's version, you can leave nothing on any flame, yet our *Mishna* (read as concerning only *chazara*) implies you can freely leave food on an untreated flame. According to *Beit Hillel* in Rabbi Meir's version, you can leave only water on a treated flame and you cannot return food at all — yet our *Mishna* permits both food and returning. In any case, that our *Mishna* does not follow Rabbi Meir is clear regardless of how one reads it.

The problem with saying our *Mishna* is Rabbi Yehuda is more pointed: even Rabbi Yehuda explicitly requires *garuf ve'katum* for *shehiya*. If our *Mishna*'s requirement of *garuf ve'katum* is being read as applying only to *chazara*, that directly contradicts Rabbi Yehuda's position that *garuf ve'katum* is needed even to leave food on.

### Our Mishna's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ ״לְהַחֲזִיר״ תְּנַן, וְתַנָּא דִידַן סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בַּחֲדָא וּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא. סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בַּחֲדָא — בְּחַמִּין וְתַבְשִׁיל, וְנוֹטְלִין וּמַחְזִירִין. וּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא — דְּאִילּוּ תַּנָּא דִידַן סָבַר לְשַׁהוֹת וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ גָּרוּף וְקָטוּם, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר בִּלְשַׁהוֹת נָמֵי גָּרוּף וְקָטוּם — אִין, אִי לָא — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** This claim is rejected: **Actually, you can say** that the case **we learned** in our mishna was with regard to **returning** the pot to the stove, **and our *tanna*** in the mishna **holds in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda in one** matter, **and disagrees with him in one** matter. **He holds in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda in one** matter, with regard to the matter of **hot water and cooked food, and** what may be **taken** from the stove **and** what may even be **returned. And** he **disagrees with him in one** matter: **While our *tanna*** in the mishna **held** that **to leave** a pot on a stove is permitted **even though it is not swept or covered with ashes, Rabbi Yehuda held: With regard to** permitting one **to leave** a pot on the stove **as well,** if the stove was **swept or covered with ashes, yes,** it is permitted; **if** it was **not** swept or covered with ashes, **no,** it is prohibited.

The *Gemara* resolves this by positing that our *Mishna* represents a third, independent position: *tana didan savar lah ke-Rabbi Yehuda be-chada u-falig aleih be-chada* — our *Mishna* agrees with Rabbi Yehuda in one respect but differs in another. It agrees in the general structure of the debate: *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* dispute whether water alone or food as well may be left on, and they dispute whether food may be returned. In this, it follows Rabbi Yehuda precisely. But it differs on the question of when *garuf ve'katum* is required. *D'ilu tana didan savar lish'hos ve-af al pi she-eino garuf ve-katum* — our *Mishna* holds that for *shehiya*, no *garuf ve'katum* is needed; food may be left on an untreated flame. *Ve-Rabbi Yehuda savar be-sh'hiyya nami grufa u-ketuma in, ee lo lo* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that even for *shehiya*, one must have a treated flame.

This is acknowledged to be a strained reading of our *Mishna*. As Tosafot notes, the plain meaning of the *Mishna* seems clearly to require *garuf ve'katum* for *shehiya* as well. The only reason to push the forced reading of the *Mishna* as concerning *chazara* is, as Tosafot explains, because the *halacha* is ultimately ruled in accordance with Chananya — and that is the *Ashkenazi* position.

## Somech (Leaning Against)

### Somech Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מַהוּ לִסְמוֹךְ בָּהּ? תּוֹכָהּ וְגַבָּהּ אָסוּר, אֲבָל לִסְמוֹךְ בָּהּ — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי, אוֹ דִילְמָא לָא שְׁנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: With regard to a stove that was neither swept nor covered with ashes, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to permitting one **to lean** a cooked dish **against it,** so that it may be heated from the sides of the stove? The dilemma is: Was it only placing a pot **inside it and atop it** that **is prohibited,** but **to lean** the pot **against it** he may **well** do so? **Or, perhaps,** leaning **is no different** and it is prohibited in every case.

The *Gemara* now turns to a distinct but related question: *mahu lismoch bah* — what is the rule about placing a pot not inside the oven or on top of it, but against its outer wall, adjacent to it? The question is whether this mode — *somech*, leaning the pot against the oven — is treated like placing food directly on the flame (and thus subject to the same restrictions) or whether it is inherently more lenient because it is not placed on the heat source itself.

The practical stakes are significant: if *somech* requires no *garuf ve'katum*, then even in cases where one could not otherwise leave food on the flame, one could place it against the outside of the oven and benefit from the radiant heat.

### Somech Allowed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שְׁתֵּי כִּירוֹת הַמַּתְאִימוֹת, אַחַת גְּרוּפָה וּקְטוּמָה וְאַחַת שֶׁאֵינָהּ גְּרוּפָה וּקְטוּמָה, מְשַׁהִין עַל גַּבֵּי גְּרוּפָה וּקְטוּמָה. וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּקָא סָלֵיק לֵיהּ הַבְלָא מֵאִידָךְ! דִּילְמָא שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּכֵיוָן דְּמִידַּלְּיָא שָׁלֵיט בָּהּ אַוֵּירָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a resolution to this dilemma from that which was taught in a *baraita*: If there are **two adjoining stoves, one** that was **swept** or **covered with ashes and one that was not swept and covered with ashes, one may leave** cooked food **atop** the stove that is **swept and covered with ashes** on Shabbat. Apparently, it is permitted to lean a pot on a stove that was not swept, **even though heat rises to it from the other** stove. The Gemara rejects this: **Perhaps that** case of two adjoining stoves **is different. Since** the pot **is elevated, the air affects it** and cools it. Therefore, it is not comparable to actually leaning it against the stove.

The *Gemara* returns to the double-oven *baraita* as a source for resolving the *somech* question. The two ovens are a single unit, placed side by side, and therefore the pot left in the treated oven is effectively right up against the wall of the untreated oven — yet the *baraita* permits it. The *Gemara* draws from this that even when heat is communicated through an adjoining wall (*af al gav de-ka salik lei havla me-idach* — even though the hot air rises from the other oven), the arrangement is permitted.

The *Gemara* attempts to deflect this proof by noting that *keivan de-madlya shalat beih avira* — in the double-oven case, the pot is suspended with air circulating beneath it and is not directly pressed against the wall, which might render it different from a pot physically leaning against an oven wall. Nonetheless, the *Tosefta* settles the matter directly: *kira she-hesikuha be-gefet u-ve-etzim, somchin lah ve-ein mekaymin ela im ken grufa u-ketuma* — in an oven lit with wood, one may lean food against it (*somchin*), but one may not place food on the flame itself unless it has been treated. This makes explicit that *somech* — placing food against the oven — does not require *garuf ve'katum*, while placing food directly on the flame does.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַב סָפְרָא אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא: קְטָמָהּ וְנִתְלַבְּתָה, סוֹמְכִין לָהּ וּמְקַיְּימִין עָלֶיהָ, וְנוֹטְלִין מִמֶּנָּה וּמַחְזִירִין לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** another resolution to this dilemma from that which **Rav Safra said** that **Rav Ḥiyya said:** If there is a stove whose coals **one covered** with ashes on Shabbat eve **and it** subsequently **reignited** on Shabbat, **one may lean** a pot **against it, and leave** cooked food **on it, and remove** food **from it, and** even **return** food **to it.**

This ruling has direct practical relevance: even without a *blech*, one may warm food by placing it near the oven, provided there is no independent *bishul* concern and the food is not going to overheat. The area of the *blech* not directly over the burners raises a similar question — whether that region counts as *somech* or whether the *blech*'s heat distribution makes even that area equivalent to being directly over the flame.

## Ashes & Reignition

### Tosefta on Ashes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הָוֵי עֲלַהּ? תָּא שְׁמַע: כִּירָה שֶׁהִסִּיקוּהָ בְּגֶפֶת וּבְעֵצִים סוֹמְכִין לָהּ, וְאֵין מְקַיְּימִין אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן גְּרוּפָה וּקְטוּמָה. גֶּחָלִים שֶׁעָמְמוּ אוֹ שֶׁנָּתַן עָלֶיהָ נְעוֹרֶת שֶׁל פִּשְׁתָּן דַּקָּה — הֲרֵי הִיא כִּקְטוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What conclusion was reached** with regard to this dilemma? **Come** and **hear** a resolution to this from that which was taught in the *Tosefta*: With regard to **a stove that was lit with pomace** or **with wood, one may lean** a pot of cooked food **against it; however, one may not leave** a pot inside it **unless** the stove is **swept out** or **covered with ashes. Coals that dimmed or on which a strip of** thinly **beaten flax was placed** and the fire did not ignite, **it is as** if it were **covered with ashes,** and one need not add more ashes to it. In any case, the conclusion is drawn from here that one is permitted to lean a dish of cooked food against a stove, even though it is not covered with ashes or swept out.

The *Tosefta* continues by addressing two additional scenarios that are treated as equivalent to *garuf ve'katum*: *gechalim she-amemu* — embers that have burned themselves out entirely — and placing thin flax strings (*ne'oret shel pishtan daka*) upon the coals. Either of these is described as *harei hi ke-ktuma* — comparable to having placed ashes. 

This is noteworthy on two levels. First, the case of embers that simply burned out involves no deliberate act on the person's part — the flame extinguished itself, yet this suffices. Second, the case of the flax strings suggests that even a relatively minor act of dampening the flame qualifies, even if it does not fully extinguish it. These two tracks — spontaneous extinction and deliberate-but-minimal intervention — will become important when translating the *garuf ve'katum* concept into the contemporary *blech*, which represents a deliberate action that nonetheless still permits a vigorous flame underneath.

### Reignition Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא: קְטָמָהּ וְהוּבְעֲרָה מְשַׁהִין עָלֶיהָ חַמִּין שֶׁהוּחַמּוּ כׇּל צוֹרְכָּן וְתַבְשִׁיל שֶׁבִּישֵּׁל כׇּל צוֹרְכּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Naḥmani said** that **Rav Oshaya said:** With regard to a stove that **he covered with ashes and** that **reignited** on Shabbat, **one may leave hot water that was** already **completely heated and cooked food that was** already **completely cooked upon it.** In that case, there is no need for additional cooking, and therefore there is no concern that one might come to stoke the coals and ignite the fire.

With this, Rav Safra's teaching in the name of Rabbi Hiyya becomes critical: *kitma ve-nisla'ava, somchin lah u-mekaymin aleha ve-notlin heimena u-machzirin lah* — if one placed ashes on the coals and the coals then reignited into a full flame, one may nevertheless lean food against it, leave food on it, remove food from it, and return food to it.

This is a striking ruling. One might have thought that reignition would nullify the ashes entirely, as though they had never been placed. Rav Safra teaches that this is not so: the prior placement of ashes retains its legal significance even after the flame reignites. This principle is foundational for understanding the contemporary *blech*, where a fully vigorous flame burns beneath a metal cover. The *blech* is not extinguishing the flame; it is a *heker*, a marker or reminder, and that suffices — just as the ashes, even when the flame reignites through them, retain their status.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינָּה לִסְמוֹךְ נָמֵי, קְטָמָהּ — אִין, לֹא קְטָמָהּ — לָא. וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, ״נוֹטְלִין מִמֶּנָּה״ דְּקָתָנֵי, קְטָמָהּ — אִין, לֹא קְטָמָהּ — לָא?! אֶלָּא תְּנָא נוֹטְלִין מִשּׁוּם מַחְזִירִין, הָכָא נָמֵי תְּנָא סוֹמְכִין מִשּׁוּם מְקַיְּימִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Conclude from this** the following with regard to **leaning, as well:** If **he covered** them **with ashes, yes,** if **he did not cover** them **with ashes, no,** as the Gemara is speaking about a stove whose ashes were covered properly during the day. The Gemara rejects this proof too. **And according to your opinion, that which was taught: One may remove** the food **from it,** would you say there too that if **he covered them, yes,** and if **he did not cover them, no?** Everyone agrees that it is permitted to take the pot off of the stove even if it is not swept or covered with ashes. **Rather,** it must be understood that **he taught** permission **to remove** the pot **due to** the fact that it taught permission **to return** it. **Here too, it taught** permission **to lean** the pot **due to** the fact that it taught permission **to leave** the pot on the stove. Consequently, a conclusion cannot be drawn that leaning a pot on an unswept stove is prohibited.

The *Gemara* notes that Rav Safra's list includes *somchin lah* — leaning food against the oven. One might infer that this is listed because without the ashes, *somech* would have been forbidden. The *Gemara* immediately deflects this inference: *notlin* (taking food off) is also listed, yet one can obviously remove food from the flame without any *garuf ve'katum*. The reason *notlin* is listed is because the *Tosefta* wanted to convey the idea that once you take it off, you can put it back — *notlin* is listed for the sake of *machzirin*. Similarly, *somchin* may be listed merely as a rhetorical companion to *mekaymin*, not as evidence that *somech* normally requires *garuf ve'katum*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם נוֹטְלִין וּמַחְזִירִין בְּחַד מָקוֹם הוּא, תְּנָא נוֹטְלִין מִשּׁוּם מַחְזִירִין. אֶלָּא הָכָא — סוֹמְכִין בְּחַד מָקוֹם הוּא וּמְקַיְּימִין בְּחַד מָקוֹם הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara is astonished by this comparison. **How can you compare** them? **There, one removes** the pot from **and returns it** to **one** and the same **place.** Therefore, **it taught removing due to returning,** as one cannot return a pot before he removes it. **However, here,** where **one leans** the pot **is in one place and** where **one leaves** the pot **is in one,** another, **place,** there is no connection between the two. If the *tanna* did not intend to teach that leaning is permitted only on a swept stove, there would be no reason to mention permission to lean in conjunction with permission to leave. In any event, this is not an absolute proof, and the dilemma has not been resolved.

The *Gemara* pushes back: the analogy of *notlin* to *somchin* is imperfect. Taking off and putting back (*notlin u-machzirin*) are linked because they describe a single process in the same location — you take the pot from the flame and return it to the same flame. *Somchin* and *mekaymin*, by contrast, describe two different places: leaning against the outside wall and placing on the flame itself. There is no inherent reason to mention *somchin* merely for the sake of *mekaymin*. Hence, the inference stands that *somech* without ashes might indeed be problematic — though the *Gemara* ultimately concludes otherwise, as the *Tosefta* clarifies.

## Somech (Leaning Agains…

### Tosafot's Caveat

Tosafot introduces an important limitation on the permissibility of *somech*. The *Tosefta*'s ruling that *somchin* is permitted — even without *garuf ve'katum* — applies specifically to leaning food against the *wall* of the oven, where the oven's own structure intervenes between the pot and the fire: *deshani hacha de-dofnei kira mafsikin beino u-vein ha-esh* — here the walls of the oven separate the pot from the flame. This is categorically different from placing a pot directly against an exposed, open flame, such as a burner on a stovetop. In that case, with no oven wall mediating between the pot and the fire, *somech* may not be treated so leniently. This caveat from Tosafot is directly relevant to how these principles translate into contemporary kitchen arrangements.

## Ashes & Reignition…

### Reignition = Fully Cooked

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינָּה מִצְטַמֵּק וְיָפֶה לוֹ מוּתָּר! שָׁאנֵי הָכָא דְּקַטְמַהּ. אִי הָכִי מַאי לְמֵימְרָא?! הוּבְעֲרָה אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ, מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא כֵּיוָן דְּהוּבְעֲרָה הָדְרָא לָהּ לְמִילְּתָא קַמַּיְיתָא, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Conclude from this** *halakha* that even when it is food that **shrivels and improves** by remaining on the fire, it is nevertheless **permitted** to leave it. The food is already completely cooked and there is no concern lest one come to stoke the coals and ignite the fire. Rabbi Oshaya did not distinguish between different types of foods in permitting this. The Gemara rejects this conclusion: **Here,** it **is different** because **he covered** the coals in the stove **with ashes,** and that is the reason that he is permitted to leave food on the stove. The Gemara asks: **If so, what** purpose was there **to say** this *halakha*? Is it to teach that if the coals are covered with ashes, there is no room for concern? That is obvious. The Gemara answers: The case where he covered it with ashes and it **reignited** on Shabbat **was necessary** and required additional articulation. **Lest you say** that **since it reignited, it returns to its original status** and is prohibited, therefore **it taught us** that this is not the case.

Rabbi Yitzchak bar Nachmani, citing Rabbi Yehoshua, addresses the reignition scenario directly: *ktama ve-huv'ara, mash'hin aleha chamim she-huchmu kol tzarchan ve-tavshil she-bishal kol tzarcho* — if one placed ashes on the coals and they reignited, one may leave on the flame water that has been fully heated and food that has been fully cooked.

This is the position that stands in opposition to Chananya, and it is the ruling adopted by the *Shulchan Aruch* (the *Mechaber*, not the *Rema*). The implicit question is: since ashes were placed, why is only *fully* cooked food permitted? If the ashes retained their legal significance after reignition, one might expect the oven to be treated as a fully kosher *katum* oven, on which even less-than-fully-cooked food could remain. The fact that only fully cooked food is permitted suggests that the reignition does diminish the ashes' effect to some degree.

This is the appropriate point to clarify the underlying principle at stake. The question of whether to leave something on an untreated flame is not a *de'oraita* question of *bishul* — the prohibition of cooking on *Shabbos*. Once food is *mevushal kol tzarcho* — fully cooked — the principle of *ein bishul achar bishul* applies: cooking something already cooked is not an act of *bishul* for Torah-law purposes, even if the food continues to improve (*mitztemek ve-yafeh lo*). The question of whether to permit leaving food on a live flame is a *rabbinic* concern — the fear that one will stir the coals to accelerate cooking. This is important because practitioners sometimes focus exclusively on the *shehiya* and *hatmanah* questions and overlook the independent *bishul de'oraita* dimension, which must always be considered separately.

With the rabbinic concern in view, the distinction between *mitztemek ve-yafeh lo* (the food shrinks and improves) and *mitztemek ve-ra lo* (the food shrinks and deteriorates) becomes central. If the food continues to improve as it cooks, there remains a motivation to stir the coals — one benefits from the continued cooking. If the food only deteriorates with continued cooking, the concern evaporates: the only reason to leave it on the flame is to keep it hot, not to cook it further, and so there is no reason one would come to stir.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: קְטָמָהּ וְהוּבְעֲרָה, מְשַׁהִין עָלֶיהָ חַמִּין שֶׁהוּחַמּוּ כׇּל צוֹרְכָּן וְתַבְשִׁיל שֶׁבִּישֵּׁל כׇּל צוֹרְכּוֹ, וַאֲפִילּוּ גֶּחָלִים שֶׁל רוֹתֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** With regard to a stove that **he** swept out or **covered with ashes** before Shabbat **and** subsequently **reignited** on Shabbat, **one may leave hot water that** was already **completely heated and cooked food that** was already **completely cooked upon it, even if** the **coals** were **from** the wood of **a broom** tree, which are very hot and long-burning.

Thus, the key question being explored is: for the position that opposes Chananya — which requires the food to be fully cooked before leaving it on an untreated flame — is it sufficient that the food is fully cooked, or must one additionally establish that continued cooking makes the food worse (*mitztemek ve-ra lo*)? The *Gemara* notes that Rabbi Yitzchak bar Nachmani does not explicitly distinguish between the two cases, which might suggest he permits *mitztemek ve-yafeh lo* as well.

The *Gemara* deflects this inference: *shani hacha di-katma* — here is different because ashes were placed. Perhaps his leniency is specific to the case where ashes were applied. If so, the *chiddush* of his ruling is not that *mitztemek ve-yafeh lo* is generally permitted on an untreated flame, but rather that the reignition does not render the ashes entirely meaningless. *Mahu de-teima keivan de-huv'ara hadra lah le-milsa ke-me'ikara* — one might have thought that the reignition returns the situation to its original untreated state, as if no ashes had been applied. The ruling teaches us that even after reignition, the ashes retain some significance.

Tosafot here presents two readings. One possibility is that even after reignition the ashes are fully effective, such that even not-fully-cooked food would in principle be permitted, and Rabbi Yitzchak bar Nachmani's language is explained on other grounds. The other possibility is that reignition creates an intermediate category: had the ashes never been applied, not even fully cooked food could be left on; had the ashes been applied without reignition, even not-fully-cooked food could be left on; with ashes applied and subsequent reignition, it is a middle case — fully cooked food yes, not-fully-cooked food no.

### Retam Embers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינָּה מִצְטַמֵּק וְיָפֶה לוֹ מוּתָּר! — שָׁאנֵי הָכָא דְּקַטְמַהּ. אִי הָכִי מַאי לְמֵימְרָא?! הוּבְעֲרָה אִצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ. הַיְינוּ הָךְ! — גֶּחָלִים שֶׁל רוֹתֶם אִצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Conclude from this** that even if food **shrivels and improves** when left on the stove, it **is permitted**. The Gemara rejects this: **Here,** in this case, **it is different** because **he covered it with ashes.** Therefore, it is permitted to leave it on the stove. The Gemara asks: **If so, what** was the purpose **of saying** this *halakha*? The Gemara answers: Mention of the case where he covered it with ashes and it **reignited** on Shabbat **was necessary.** The Gemara challenges that explanation: **This** case **is identical to the** previous **one.** Why did Rabbi Yoḥanan find it necessary to repeat what was already said? The Gemara responds that there is a novel element in his statement. It **was necessary** to teach the case of **coals** from the wood of **a broom** tree. Even in a case of especially hot coals it is permitted.

Rabbah bar Bar Chana citing Rabbi Yochanan restates the same rule: *kitmah ve-huv'arah, mash'hin aleha chamim she-huchmu kol tzarchan ve-tavshil she-bishal kol tzarcho* — after ashes and reignition, only fully cooked food may remain on the flame. He adds, *va-afilu gachalim shel rotam* — even if the embers are those of the *rotam* plant, known for retaining exceptional heat for an extraordinarily long time even after appearing extinguished. One might have thought that since *rotam* embers remain intensely hot, any ashes placed upon them are rendered trivially ineffective by the re-ignition. He teaches that even in this case, the ashes retain their significance. The *Gemara* notes that this is essentially the same teaching as what came immediately before, and distinguishes the two: the first ruling teaches that reignition in general does not nullify the ashes, while the *rotam* addendum teaches that even particularly vigorous reignition through unusually persistent embers does not nullify them.

## Lenient View: Shehiya On…

### Rav Sheshet's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כִּירָה שֶׁהִסִּיקוּהָ בְּגֶפֶת וּבְעֵצִים מְשַׁהִין עָלֶיהָ חַמִּין שֶׁלֹּא הוּחַמּוּ כׇּל צוֹרְכָּן וְתַבְשִׁיל שֶׁלֹּא בִּישֵּׁל כׇּל צוֹרְכּוֹ. עָקַר — לֹא יַחֲזִיר עַד שֶׁיִּגְרוֹף אוֹ עַד שֶׁיִּתֵּן אֵפֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** With regard to **a stove that was lit with pomace** or **with wood, one may leave hot water on it even if** the water **has not** yet **been completely heated, and** the same is true for **cooked food** even **if it was not** yet **completely cooked.** However, if **one removed** the food from the stove, **one may only return** it **if he sweeps** the coals out of the stove while it is still day, **or if he places ashes** on the coals.

Rav Sheshet, citing Rabbi Yochanan, now presents the lenient opinion in its clearest form: *kura she-sikuha be-gefset u-ve-eitzim, mash'hin aleha chamim shelo huchmu kol tzarchan ve-tavshil shelo bishal kol tzarcho* — if an oven was lit with wood, one may leave on it water that has not yet been fully heated and food that has not yet been fully cooked. And if one removed the pot from the flame — *akar, lo yachazir ad she-yigrof o ad she-yiten eifer* — one may not return it until the oven has been swept out or ashes have been placed.

This is precisely the position of Chananya. The teaching of Rav Sheshet in the name of Rabbi Yochanan makes explicit what had been implied throughout the earlier discussion: *kasavar matnitin lehachzir tnan* — our *Mishna*'s requirement of *garuf ve'katum* pertains only to the act of returning food to the flame. For *shehiya* — leaving food on from before *Shabbos* — no treatment of the flame is required, even if the food is only partially cooked. The *blech* is necessary only when one wishes to return food to the flame on *Shabbos* itself.

### Rava's Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: תַּרְוַויְיהוּ תְּנַנְהִי. לְשַׁהוֹת תְּנֵינָא: אֵין נוֹתְנִין אֶת הַפַּת בְּתוֹךְ הַתַּנּוּר עִם חֲשֵׁיכָה וְלֹא חֲרָרָה עַל גַּבֵּי גֶּחָלִים אֶלָּא כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּקְרְמוּ פָּנֶיהָ. הָא קָרְמוּ פָּנֶיהָ — שְׁרֵי. לְהַחֲזִיר נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: בֵּית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים אַף מַחְזִירִין. וְעַד כָּאן לָא קָשָׁרוּ בֵּית הִלֵּל אֶלָּא בִּגְרוּפָה וּקְטוּמָה, אֲבָל בְּשֶׁאֵינָהּ גְּרוּפָה וּקְטוּמָה לָא! וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת נָמֵי דִּיּוּקָא דְמַתְנִיתִין קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: We** already **learned both** aspects of Rav Sheshet's *halakha* and there is no need to teach us something that was already stated explicitly in the mishna. **We** already **learned** that it is permitted **to leave** a pot on the stove. **One may not put bread into the oven at nightfall and** may **not** place **cake on top of coals unless** there is enough time before Shabbat **that its surface will form a crust.** However, if **its surface already formed a crust** before Shabbat, **it is permitted** to leave it even in an oven that was not swept and not covered with coals. Likewise, **we also** already **learned** in our mishna the second aspect of Rav Sheshet's *halakha* that it is permitted **to return** the pot to the fire, as **Beit Hillel say: One may even return. And** it is clear that **Beit Hillel only went so far as permitting** the return of the pot **in** a stove that is **swept** or **covered with ashes; however, in one that is not swept** or **covered with ashes,** they did **not** permit doing so. If so, Rav Sheshet's statement is superfluous. The Gemara answers: **Rav Sheshet also** only came to **teach us the inference from the mishna** and not to introduce new *halakhot*.

Rava observes that Rabbi Yochanan's teaching should not have been necessary, because the same principle is already evident from a *Mishna* at the end of the first chapter (*perek*). That *Mishna* states: *ein notnin et ha-pas be-toch ha-tanur im hasheichah* — one may not place bread in the oven right before *Shabbos* begins, *ela kedei she-yikremu paneha* — unless it has already formed a crust. The implication is that once the bread has formed a crust — not fully baked, but with some initial cooking — it may be left in the oven even over *Shabbos* on an untreated flame. This *Mishna* from the first chapter already demonstrates that *shehiya* on an untreated flame is permitted for partially cooked food. Rava adds that the requirement of *garuf ve'katum* for returning food is equally obvious from our *Mishna*, since *Beit Hillel*'s permission to return food (*af machzirin*) is predicated on the flame having been treated.

### Our Mishna Reread

In light of this, the role of Rav Sheshet's teaching (*diyuka de-matnitin ka mashma lan*) is to provide the correct reading of our *Mishna* in the second chapter. On its face, the *Mishna* there appeared to require *garuf ve'katum* for *shehiya* as well. Rav Sheshet teaches that the *Mishna* should be understood as addressing only *chazara* — the condition of *garuf ve'katum* in its first clause is a condition for returning food, not for leaving it. The *Mishna* from the first chapter meanwhile establishes that *shehiya* is unrestricted. Together, Rav Sheshet, Rabbi Yochanan, and Rava converge: there is no requirement of *garuf ve'katum* if one only wishes to leave food on the flame from before *Shabbos*.

### Ashkenazi Position

Rashi makes this explicit in his commentary. He writes: *ve-anan de-mash'hainan kedira agav kira she-eina grufa* — we *Ashkenazim* who leave our pot on an untreated flame from before *Shabbos*, *a-Chananya samchinan* — we rely on Chananya, *ho'il u-tnan stam mishna kevateih* — because the *stam mishna* at the end of the first chapter follows his view, *ve-af al gav de-lo bishal kol tzorko* — even when the food is not fully cooked, *ve-amar Rav Sheshet mishmeih de-Rabbi Yochanan kevateih* — and Rav Sheshet said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan the same ruling, and *de-matnitin de-pirkin mishum chazara hu* — our *Mishna* in this chapter concerns returning, not leaving.

Tosafot states the same position. All of these *Amoraim* are understood to agree that *shehiya* requires no treatment of the flame. The opposing *Amoraim* who hold that the *Mishna*'s demand for *garuf ve'katum* applies to *shehiya* as well are ruled against, and the *Ashkenazi* practice is built on the convergence of the *stam mishna*, Chananya, and Rav Yochanan as cited by Rav Sheshet.

The *Sephardim*, by contrast, rule in accordance with the stricter positions, because the plain meaning (*pshat*) of our *Mishna* clearly requires *garuf ve'katum* for *shehiya*, and all the other opinions are read as a *lo shanu* against Chananya. Accordingly, the *Sephardi* requirement is that food be fully cooked (*mevushal kol tzarcho*) before it may remain on an untreated flame — and even then, as we shall see, an additional condition applies.

The *Rosh*, who began in *Ashkenaz* and moved to *Sepharad*, navigates between the two positions and ultimately leans toward the *Sephardi* ruling, while framing the *Ashkenazi* practice charitably: *hanichu lahem li-vnei Ashkenaz she-mitoch she-machmirim al oneg Shabbat hem somchin a-Chananya* — allow the *Ashkenazim* their practice, for since they are so scrupulous about *oneg Shabbat* and wish to have hot food that has not become overcooked, they rely on Chananya.

## Mitztemek v'Yafeh Lo Deb…

### Stringent Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כִּירָה שֶׁהִסִּיקוּהָ בְּגֶפֶת וּבְעֵצִים מְשַׁהִין עָלֶיהָ תַּבְשִׁיל שֶׁבִּישֵּׁל כׇּל צוֹרְכּוֹ וְחַמִּין שֶׁהוּחַמּוּ כׇּל צוֹרְכָּן וַאֲפִילּוּ מִצְטַמֵּק וְיָפֶה לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** With regard to **a stove that was lit with pomace** or **with wood,** on Shabbat eve **one may leave a cooked dish that was** already **completely cooked, as well as hot water that was** already **completely heated, upon it and even if** it is the type of food that when left for a prolonged period of time on the fire it **shrivels and improves.** There is no concern lest one come to stoke the coals.

The *Gemara* now records a version of Rabbi Yochanan's opinion that is considerably stricter. Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda, citing Rabbi Yochanan, teaches: *kira she-hisikuha be-gefet u-ve-eitzim, mash'hin aleha tavshil she-bishal kol tzorko ve-chamin she-huchmu kol tzorkan* — one may leave on such an oven only food that is fully cooked and water that is fully heated. Furthermore, *va-afilu mitztemek ve-yafe lo* — even though the food continues to improve as it remains on the flame, this is still permitted once the food is fully cooked.

This is the opinion that opposes Chananya: no untreated flame may hold not-fully-cooked food. But on the question of *mitztemek ve-yafeh lo*, Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda's version of Rabbi Yochanan is lenient — once fully cooked, the fact that continued cooking improves the food does not create a new restriction.

### The Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִדְּרַבָּנַן לְרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יְהוּדָה: הָא רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַויְיהוּ מִצְטַמֵּק וְיָפֶה לוֹ אָסוּר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **one of the Sages said to Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda: Isn't it Rav and Shmuel who both say,** contrary to your opinion, that if food **shrivels and improves** when placed on the stove, leaving it on there on Shabbat is **prohibited?**

This leniency regarding *mitztemek ve-yafeh lo* is immediately challenged. Other Rabbis cite the position of Rav and Shmuel, both of whom hold *mitztemek ve-yafeh lo assur* — once fully cooked food continues to improve with further cooking, it may not be left on an untreated flame. Only *mitztemek ve-ra lo* — food that continues to deteriorate — may be left on. Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda responds: he is well aware of what Shmuel says on this matter (*tika mina lach Rav Yochanan ka'amina*) — but he is reporting Rabbi Yochanan's view, which is that once food is fully cooked, it may be left on the flame even if it continues to improve.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַטּוּ לֵית אֲנָא יָדַע דְּאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִצְטַמֵּק וְיָפֶה לוֹ — אָסוּר? כִּי קָאָמֵינָא לָךְ, לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן קָאָמֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda **said to him: Is that to say** that **I do not know** that **Rav Yosef said** that **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: If** food **shrivels and improves** when left on the fire for an extended period, it is **prohibited** to leave it there? **When I said to you** that it is permitted to leave it, **I said** it **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan.**

### Regional Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב עוּקְבָא מִמֵּישָׁן לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אַתּוּן דִּמְקָרְבִיתוּ לְרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל — עֲבִידוּ כְּרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל, אֲנַן נַעֲבֵיד כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ukva from Meishan said to Rav Ashi: You, who are close to** the place where **Rav and Shmuel** lived, **act in accordance with** the ruling of **Rav and Shmuel; we will act in accordance with** the ruling of **Rabbi Yoḥanan.**

Rav Ukva mi-Meishan addresses this dispute to Rav Ashi directly, framing it in terms of regional practice: *atun de-mikravitu le-Rav u-Shmuel* — you who are close to where Rav and Shmuel lived and taught, *avidu ke-Rav u-Shmuel* — follow their ruling. In your locale, fully cooked food may only be left on an untreated flame if it is *mitztemek ve-ra lo*. *Anan, ne-ivid ke-Rabbi Yochanan* — we, who are farther away, follow Rabbi Yochanan and permit leaving fully cooked food on the flame even if it is *mitztemek ve-yafeh lo*. Abaye then asks Rav Yosef for a practical ruling on *shehiya*, and Rav Yosef responds that Rav Yehuda himself used to leave food on the flame and eat it. Abaye deflects this: Rav Yehuda's case cannot serve as a precedent for ordinary people, since he had ongoing health concerns (*mesukan*) that would have permitted him to act on *Shabbos* in ways that might otherwise be restricted. Rav Yosef then notes that the practice in *Sura* was to leave food on the flame — presumably even if *mitztemek ve-yafeh lo* — citing Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, a man of great deeds, as a practitioner of this leniency.

### Rav Ashi's Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: קָאֵימְנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא וְשַׁהִין לֵיהּ כָּסָא דְהַרְסָנָא וָאֲכַל, וְלָא יָדַעְנָא אִי מִשּׁוּם דְקָסָבַר מִצְטַמֵּק וְיָפֶה לוֹ מוּתָּר, אִי מִשּׁוּם דְּכֵיוָן דְּאִית בֵּיהּ מִיחָא — מִצְטַמֵּק וְרַע לוֹ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: I stood before Rav Huna and** saw that **they left fish fried in oil [*kasa deharsena*]** for him atop the stove on Shabbat, **and he ate** the fish on Shabbat. **And I do not know if** his reason for doing so is **because he holds** that **it is permitted** to leave food that **shrivels and improves** when left on the stove for a long time. Or, **if** it is **because this** dish **has flour** in it, and therefore it **shrivels and deteriorates.** Everyone agrees that it is permitted to leave food atop the stove that shrivels and deteriorates.

Rav Ashi recounts that while in the presence of Rav Huna, *shav lei kasa de-harsena* — they left on the flame some fried or battered fish — *ve-lo yadana* — and he did not know whether to infer from this that Rav Huna held *mitztemek ve-yafeh lo mutar* (since a fully cooked dish was left on), or whether the case fell under *mitztemek ve-ra lo* — because the dish contained flour, and floury preparations generally deteriorate with extended cooking. The uncertainty itself is telling: the presence of flour in the batter introduced ambiguity as to whether this particular food was classified as improving or worsening on the flame.

### Rav Nachman's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מִצְטַמֵּק וְיָפֶה לוֹ — אָסוּר, מִצְטַמֵּק וְרַע לוֹ — מוּתָּר. כְּלָלָא דְּמִלְּתָא: כׇּל דְּאִית בֵּיהּ מֵיחָא — מִצְטַמֵּק וְרַע לוֹ, לְבַר מִתַּבְשִׁיל דְּלִיפְתָּא דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּאִית בֵּיהּ מֵיחָא — מִצְטַמֵּק וְיָפֶה לוֹ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said:** Food that **shrivels and improves** when left on the stove, **it is prohibited** to leave it on the stove; if it **shrivels and deteriorates, it is permitted. The principle in this matter** is as follows: **Any** food **that has flour in it shrivels and deteriorates, except for a cooked turnip** dish, **which, even though it has flour, shrivels and improves.**

Rav Nachman closes the discussion with a clear ruling: *mitztemek ve-yafeh lo assur, mitztemek ve-ra lo mutar* — if the food continues to improve with further cooking, it is forbidden to leave it on an untreated flame; if it continues to deteriorate, it is permitted. This is the definitive *Sephardi* position: fully cooked food that is *mitztemek ve-ra lo* may remain on an untreated flame; fully cooked food that is *mitztemek ve-yafeh lo* may not.

## Practical Applications

### General Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי דְּאִית בֵּיהּ בִּשְׂרָא, אֲבָל לֵית בֵּיהּ בִּשְׂרָא — מִצְטַמֵּק וְרַע לוֹ הוּא. וְכִי אִית בֵּיהּ בִּשְׂרָא נָמֵי לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא קָבָעֵי לֵהּ לְאוֹרְחִין, אֲבָל קָבָעֵי לֵהּ לְאוֹרְחִין — מִצְטַמֵּק וְרַע לוֹ. לַפְדָּא דַּיְיסָא וְתַמְרֵי מִצְטַמֵּק וְרַע לָהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **And this applies only** when **there is meat in it, but** when **there is no meat in it, it shrivels and deteriorates. And when there is meat in it, too, we only said** that it shrivels and improves **when** one **does not need it for guests, but** when one **needs it for guests,** it **shrivels and deteriorates** because it is not polite to serve guests overcooked food, which is not aesthetic. Furthermore: Leaving dishes made **of figs [*lafda*], porridge,** or **dates** on the stove causes them to **shrivel and deteriorate.**

The *Gemara* concludes with practical guidance on which foods fall into which category. The general principle: *kol de-is bei mikha, mitztemek ve-ra lo* — anything that contains flour tends to deteriorate as it continues to cook once fully prepared. Such foods are generally classified as *mitztemek ve-ra lo* and may be left on the flame. The one exception noted is *tavshil de-lifta* — a stew made with turnips (or a similar vegetable), whose behavior depends on further conditions.

### Stews with/without Meat

With respect to the turnip stew: *aval leis bei bisra, mistameich ve-ra lo* — if there is no meat in the stew, it deteriorates with continued cooking and is therefore *mitztemek ve-ra lo*, permitting it to be left on the flame. *Ve-ki is bei bisra nami, lo amrin ela de-lo kavya lei le-orchin* — even when the stew contains meat, this leniency applies only when one is not expecting guests. If guests are expected, *kavya lei le-orchin, mistameich ve-ra lo* — the food's shrinkage is considered harmful because there will be less to serve, and thus it is again classified as *mitztemek ve-ra lo* from the standpoint of quantity if not quality.

### Specific Foods

The *Gemara* closes with three specific examples: *lafta, daisa, ve-tamri* — a fig preparation, an oatmeal-type dish, and a date preparation. All three are classified as *mistameich ve-ra lahen* — they deteriorate with continued cooking. These may therefore be left on an untreated flame once fully cooked, according to the *Sephardi* ruling of Rav Nachman.

The *Gemara* here is clearly operating within the framework of the *Sephardi* conclusion: the permissibility of leaving food on an open flame is conditioned on the food being *mevushal kol tzarcho* and *mitztemek ve-ra lo*. As noted throughout, the *Ashkenazi* position relies on Chananya and does not require full cooking for *shehiya*, rendering these distinctions largely academic in *Ashkenazi* practice for the *shehiya* question — though they remain essential for *chazara*, which even *Ashkenazim* agree requires a *blech*.
