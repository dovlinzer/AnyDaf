# Shabbat 141 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sweeping/Moving Straw

### Intro: Sweeping Dung

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ גּוֹרְפִין מִלִּפְנֵי הַפַּטָּם וּמְסַלְּקִין לִצְדָדִין מִפְּנֵי הָרְעִי, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי דּוֹסָא, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹסְרִין. נוֹטְלִין מִלִּפְנֵי בְּהֵמָה זוֹ וְנוֹתְנִין לִפְנֵי בְּהֵמָה זוֹ בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may sweep** hay **from before an animal that is being fattened, and one may move** hay **to the sides for an animal that grazes** on its own in the field (Rabbeinu Ḥananel); this is **the statement of Rabbi Dosa. And the Rabbis prohibit** doing so. **One may take** hay **from before this animal and place it before that animal on Shabbat.**

The daf begins at the bottom of 140, picking up with the *mishnah*: *gorfin milifnei hapatam umesalkin litzdadim* — one may sweep away the dung from in front of an ox or cow that is being fattened, because one does not want the animal walking into its own waste. The basic concern is maintaining cleanliness in the feeding area.

### Rashi: Food Cleanliness

Rashi clarifies that the sweeping need not be understood as removing dung per se. Rather, one is sweeping the area in front of the animal because one is about to place straw there for the animal to eat, and one does not want that food to become soiled or mixed with waste. The permission is thus framed around ensuring the cleanliness of the animal's food supply.

### Moving Straw Aside

The *mishnah* continues: *umesalkin litzdadim mipnei hare'i* — one may also move the straw to the side so that it does not become mixed with dung and rendered inedible. These are the words of Rabbi Dosa. *Vachachamim osrin* — the Rabbis, however, forbid this. The precise scope of the Rabbis' objection remains to be determined.

The *mishnah* then states: *notlin milifnei behema zu venotnin lifnei behema zu beShabbat* — one may remove food from in front of one animal and place it in front of another. Although this might seem obvious — if one may feed an animal, surely one may redistribute its food — one might have thought that once an animal has begun eating from a portion, that portion becomes unfit for another animal and would therefore acquire the status of *muktzah*. The *mishnah* teaches that this is not a concern.

### Gemara: When Dispute?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: רַבָּנַן אַרֵישָׁא פְּלִיגִי, אוֹ אַסֵּיפָא פְּלִיגִי, אוֹ אַתַּרְוַיְיהוּ פְּלִיגִי?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Do **the Rabbis,** who are stringent, **disagree with the first clause** of Rabbi Dosa's statement, **or** do **they disagree with the latter** clause of the mishna, **or** do **they disagree with both** clauses?

A *baraita* is cited: *vachachamim omrim echad ze ve'echad ze lo yesalkenu litzdadim* — the Rabbis say that in either case, one may not move it to the side. This establishes that the dispute applies to both.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא: וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה — לֹא יְסַלְּקֶנּוּ לִצְדָדִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a resolution from that which **was taught** in a *baraita*. **And the Rabbis say:** With regard to **both this,** hay placed before an animal set aside for fattening, **and that,** hay placed before an animal that grazes on its own, **one may not move** it **to the sides.** Apparently, the Rabbis rule stringently in both cases.

Rashi, anticipating what is to come, explains the two possible concerns underlying the prohibition. In the first case, *gorfin* — the sweeping — if one is dealing with a dirt floor rather than a wooden trough, then sweeping will smooth the ground, constituting a form of *boneh*. In the second case, once the animal has walked on the straw and eaten from it, the straw may already be *muktzah* by virtue of no longer being fit. These are the two categories of concern.

### Rav Chisda: Dirt Floor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מַחֲלוֹקֶת בְּאֵיבוּס שֶׁל קַרְקַע, אֲבָל בְּאֵיבוּס שֶׁל כְּלִי — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מוּתָּר. וְאֵיבוּס שֶׁל קַרְקַע מִי אִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּשָׁרֵי?! הָא קָא מַשְׁוֵי גּוּמּוֹת! אֶלָּא, אִי אִיתְּמַר הָכִי אִיתְּמַר: אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא מַחֲלוֹקֶת בְּאֵיבוּס שֶׁל כְּלִי, אֲבָל בְּאֵיבוּס שֶׁל קַרְקַע — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said:** This **dispute** is **with regard to a trough** formed **in the ground; however, with regard to a trough which is a vessel, everyone agrees that it is permitted.** The Gemara expresses surprise: **Is there anyone who permits** doing so in **a trough** formed in **the ground? Isn't one leveling holes** and thereby performing the prohibited labors of building or plowing? **Rather, if it was stated, it was stated as follows: Rav Ḥisda said:** This **dispute** applies only **to a trough that is a vessel; however, with regard to a trough** formed in **the ground, everyone agrees that it is prohibited,** due to the concern lest one level holes.

Rav Chisda resolves the matter: *machlokes be'evus shel kli* — the dispute between Rabbi Dosa and the Rabbis applies when the trough is made of a vessel, that is, some form of wooden or constructed receptacle. *Aval be'evus shel karka divrei hakol asur* — but if it is a dirt-floor situation, everyone agrees it is forbidden, because sweeping a dirt floor constitutes smoothing the ground.

The *gemara* initially presents Rav Chisda's statement in the reverse — that the dispute concerns a dirt floor — but immediately rejects this: if it were a dirt floor, how could Rabbi Dosa ever permit it? Sweeping a dirt floor would inevitably constitute *hashvaat gumot*, leveling the ground. The statement is therefore corrected: the dispute is over a vessel-trough, and the dirt-floor case is universally prohibited.

### Moving Food Between

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנוֹטְלִין מִלִּפְנֵי בְּהֵמָה. תְּנָא חֲדָא: נוֹטְלִין מִלִּפְנֵי בְּהֵמָה שֶׁפִּיהָ יָפֶה וְנוֹתְנִין לִפְנֵי בְּהֵמָה שֶׁפִּיהָ רַע. וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: נוֹטְלִין מִלִּפְנֵי בְּהֵמָה שֶׁפִּיהָ רַע וְנוֹתְנִין לִפְנֵי בְּהֵמָה שֶׁפִּיהָ יָפֶה!
>
> **Translation:** We also learned in the mishna: **One may take** hay **from before** this **animal** and place it before that animal. **It was taught in one** *baraita*: **One may take** hay **from before an animal whose mouth is fine and place it before an animal whose mouth is foul. And it was taught in another** *baraita*: **One may take** hay **from before an animal whose mouth is foul and place it in front of an animal whose mouth is fine.** There is an apparent contradiction between the two *baraitot*.

The *mishnah*'s final clause — that one may take food from in front of one animal and place it before another — prompts the *gemara* to examine the matter more carefully. Presumably these are herbivores that will eat from one another's portions. The concern one might raise is that once an animal's saliva has been mixed into the food, another animal would not want it — potentially rendering the food unfit and hence *muktzah*.

### Mouths & Spittle

Two *baraitot* address this tension, apparently in contradiction. One teaches: *notlin milifnei behema shepiha yafe venotnin lifnei behema shepiha ra* — one may take from an animal with a "nice mouth" and give to one with a "bad mouth." The other states the reverse: one takes from a "bad mouth" and gives to a "good mouth." The meaning of these characterizations requires explanation.

### Abaye: Donkey/Cow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי מִקַּמֵּי חַמְרָא לְקַמֵּי תוֹרָא — שָׁקְלִינַן, מִקַּמֵּי תוֹרָא לְקַמֵּי חַמְרָא — לָא שָׁקְלִינַן. וְהָא דְּקָתָנֵי ״נוֹטֵל מִלִּפְנֵי בְּהֵמָה שֶׁפִּיהָ יָפֶה״ — בַּחֲמוֹר, דְּלֵית לֵיהּ רִירֵי. ״וְנוֹתְנִין לִפְנֵי בְּהֵמָה שֶׁפִּיהָ רַע״ — בְּפָרָה,
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** Both **this** *baraita* **and that** *baraita* hold that **one may take** hay **from before a donkey** and place it **before an ox.** However, **one may not take** hay **from before an ox** and place it **before a donkey.** The formulation of the *baraitot* can be explained as follows: **That which was taught: One may take** hay **from before an animal whose mouth is fine,** is referring to **a donkey,** and the reason the *baraita* says its mouth is fine is **because it has no spittle.** Therefore, the donkey does not dampen the remaining hay with its saliva. **And** the statement: **And one may place it before an animal whose mouth is foul,** is referring to **a cow,**

Abaye resolves the apparent contradiction: *mi-chamra le-kamei tura shaklinan* — everyone agrees one may take from in front of a donkey and place it before a cow. *Mi-tura le-kamei chamra lo shaklinan* — but to take from a cow and give to a donkey, everyone agrees one may not. The two *baraitot* are simply different ways of expressing this single directional rule, using the terms "nice mouth" and "bad mouth" to describe donkeys and cows respectively, depending on which characteristic is being highlighted.

### Donkey Habits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִית לַהּ רִירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **which has spittle.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דְּקָתָנֵי ״נוֹטְלִין מִלִּפְנֵי בְּהֵמָה שֶׁפִּיהָ רַע״ — בַּחֲמוֹר, דְּלָא דָּיֵיק וְאָכֵיל. ״וְנוֹתְנִין לִפְנֵי בְּהֵמָה שֶׁפִּיהָ יָפֶה״ — בְּפָרָה, דְּדָיְיקָא וְאָכְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And that which was taught** in the other *baraita*: **One may take** hay **from before an animal whose mouth is foul,** is referring to **a donkey.** Its mouth is foul **because it does not discern** between different foods **and** it **eats** everything. **And** the statement: **One may place it before an animal whose mouth is fine,** is referring **to a cow, which discerns** between different foods **and** only then **it eats.**

The first *baraita*, which speaks of a "nice mouth," describes the donkey as *de-la reyrei* — it does not drool or produce much spittle as it eats. The cow is therefore described as having a "bad mouth" — *de-it leh reyrei* — because it does produce significant amounts of *reyrei*, spittle. Since the cow drools, food it has eaten from would be objectionable to another animal. But since the donkey does not, food a donkey has eaten from remains acceptable to a cow.

The second *baraita* uses the same terms in reverse: the donkey is called "bad mouth" — *de-lo dayek ve-achil* — because it is not particular about what it eats, consuming thorns and thistles indiscriminately. The cow is "good mouth" because it is more selective. One might have thought that precisely because the donkey is less particular, one could transfer from cow to donkey — since the donkey would not mind the cow's saliva. Abaye teaches that this reasoning is mistaken: the determinative factor is the degree to which *reyrei* contaminates the food, and it is the cow's excessive saliva that causes the concern, not the receiving animal's level of fastidiousness.

## Straw on Bed/Muktzah

### Mishnah: Straw Bed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַקַּשׁ שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּטָּה — לֹא יְנַעְנְעֶנּוּ בְּיָדוֹ, אֶלָּא מְנַעַנְעוֹ בְּגוּפוֹ. וְאִם הָיָה מַאֲכַל בְּהֵמָה, אוֹ שֶׁהָיָה עָלָיו כַּר אוֹ סָדִין — מְנַעַנְעוֹ בְּיָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **straw that is on top of a bed,** if a person wishes to lie on it, **he may not move it with his hand** to smooth it, as the straw is set aside for kindling; **rather, he may move it with his body.** Since moving straw with one's body is not the usual manner, it is permitted. **And if** the straw **was** designated as **animal food, or a pillow or sheet was on it,** which would clearly indicate that the straw was placed on the bed so one could sleep on it, the straw is not considered set-aside, and **one may move it** even **with his hand.**

The next *mishnah* addresses straw (*kash*) that is found lying on a bed. The question is at what point such straw acquires the status of a mattress — and hence is not *muktzah* — versus remaining ordinary straw, which would be *muktzah*. The underlying premise is that straw occupying an ambiguous position, neither clearly designated as animal feed nor as bedding, presents a genuine *muktzah* question.

### Straw as Fuel

Rashi explains that while straw can serve as animal food (in which case it would not be *muktzah*) or as a mattress (in which case it would not be *muktzah* either), the default assumption is that straw is designated for fuel. It is therefore *muktzah*. The scenario is one where the straw somehow ends up on the bed — perhaps one intended to fashion it into a mattress — but the question is whether merely placing it there is sufficient to remove its *muktzah* status. Rashi implies it is not, and the straw retains its presumptive designation for firewood.

### Hand vs. Body

Given this *muktzah* status, the *mishnah* rules: *lo yenanenu beyado* — one may not move it with one's hand. *Ela menanenu begufo* — but one may move it with one's body. Presumably this permits even an intentional displacement of the straw from the bed, so long as it is accomplished without using one's hand.

### Types of Movement

Two distinct concepts must be distinguished here. *Tiltul min hatzad* is the incidental movement of a *muktzah* item with one's hand in the course of handling a permitted item — as when reaching for a permissible object causes a *muktzah* item to be pushed aside. *Tiltul begufo* is different: it refers not to incidental movement but to movement accomplished using a part of the body other than the hand — elbows, for instance, or simply pressing one's body against the object.

The practical difference between the two is significant. *Tiltul min hatzad* is permitted when one is in the act of handling a *davar hamutar* — a permitted item — and the *muktzah* object moves incidentally in that process. It does not permit directly and deliberately displacing the *muktzah* item, even without one's hands, in isolation from any handling of a permitted item. *Tiltul begufo*, by contrast, is permitted even when one is directly focused on the *muktzah* item itself — for instance, kicking one's wallet (if it were *muktzah*) under a couch with one's foot. While the upcoming *gemara* will appear to equate these two categories, the distinction is maintained *lehalacha*.

### Exceptions to Muktzah

The *mishnah* continues: *im haya maachal behema* — if the straw was designated as animal food — or *o shehaya alav kar o sadin* — if there was a pillow or blanket on it, thereby designating it as one's mattress — then *menanenu beyado*, one may move it directly by hand, since in those cases it is not *muktzah* at all.

### Clothing Press

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַכְבֵּשׁ שֶׁל בַּעֲלֵי בָתִּים — מַתִּירִין, אֲבָל לֹא כּוֹבְשִׁין. וְשֶׁל כּוֹבְסִין — לֹא יִגַּע בּוֹ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִם הָיָה מוּתָּר מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת — מַתִּיר אֶת כּוּלּוֹ וְשׁוֹמְטוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **A press which belongs to a homeowner, one may loosen it** on Shabbat. This press is used to dry and press clothing after laundering. One loosens it to remove clothing from it. **However, one may not press** clothing with it on Shabbat. **And** in the case of a press that **belongs to a launderer,** which is made specifically for pressing and requires professional expertise for its operation, **one may not touch it. Rabbi Yehuda says: If** the launderer's press **was loosened** somewhat **on Shabbat eve, he may loosen it completely** on Shabbat **and remove** the garment.

The *mishnah* next addresses a *machbash shel baale batim* — a home clothing press, used to remove wrinkles from garments by pressing a heavy weight down upon them. Rashi describes a frame-and-peg mechanism in which a large weight is held secure over the garment by means of a peg inserted into a frame.

The rule is *matirin aval lo kovshin* — one may undo the press (remove the peg and lift the weight to retrieve one's garments), but one may not press new garments on *Shabbat*. Rashi explains that pressing garments on *Shabbat* would be objectionable both as a potential *tikkun* of the garment and because it would constitute preparation for after *Shabbat*.

### Pro vs. Home Press

*Veshel kovesin lo yigga bo* — a professional launderer's press, however, one may not even touch. Rashi explains that the professional press involves much tighter construction, and engaging with it even to release it would resemble a significant act of construction or deconstruction. One might alternatively have framed the prohibition in terms of *muktzah machmat chisron kis* — a professionally-valued tool — but Rashi frames the issue around building and unbuilding rather than *muktzah*.

### Rabbi Yehuda

Rabbi Yehuda says: *im haya muttar me'erev Shabbat matir et kulo umeshamto* — if it was already undone before *Shabbat* (i.e., the peg was removed, leaving the weight resting loosely rather than locked in place), then even from a professional press one may remove the weight and extract the garment on *Shabbat*. Rashi explains *muttar* here not as "permitted" but in its literal sense of "loosened" — the peg was already taken out before *Shabbat* began. This reinforces Rashi's view that the *tanna kamma*'s objection is not rooted in *muktzah* but in the act of constructing and deconstructing the press mechanism itself.

## Turnip/Dirt Moving

### Rav Nachman: Turnip

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הַאי פּוּגְלָא, מִלְּמַעְלָה לְמַטָּה — שְׁרֵי. מִלְּמַטָּה לְמַעְלָה — אֲסִיר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to moving items that are set aside, the Gemara cites that which **Rav Naḥman said: This radish,** which was buried in the earth, if it was buried **from top to bottom,** with its wide end at the top and its narrow end at the bottom, **it is permitted** to remove it on Shabbat. When the radish is buried in that manner, one does not move the earth when he removes the radish. However, if the radish was buried in the earth **from bottom to top,** with the wide end at the bottom, **it is prohibited.**

Rav Nachman addresses a case discussed previously in the *gemara*: *hai pugla* — a turnip (or radish) that has been buried in dirt for storage, not grown there. *Milmala lmata* — if the wide end is at the top and the narrow end points downward, *shari* — it is permissible to pull it out on *Shabbat*.

### Extraction Direction

The reason, as Rashi explains, is that when the wide end is uppermost, pulling the turnip upward causes the dirt to collapse naturally around it. The dirt's movement is entirely incidental — analogous to *tiltul min hatzad* — and one is focused entirely on retrieving the turnip itself. *Milmata lmala* — if, however, it is inverted, with the wide end below and the narrow end up, *assur* — it is forbidden. In that configuration, as one pulls the turnip upward, the wide base must force its way through a narrower opening, actively displacing the dirt. Since one is gripping the turnip, that grip is an extension of one's hand, and the dirt is being directly pushed away — this is no longer incidental movement.

### Rav Adda Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַבָּא, אָמְרִי בֵּי רַב: תְּנֵינָא דְּלָא כְּרַב נַחְמָן. הַקַּשׁ שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּטָּה — לֹא יְנַעַנְעֶנּוּ בְּיָדוֹ, אֲבָל מְנַעַנְעוֹ בְּגוּפוֹ. וְאִם הָיָה מַאֲכַל בְּהֵמָה, אוֹ שֶׁהָיָה עָלָיו כַּר אוֹ סָדִין — מְנַעַנְעוֹ בְּיָדוֹ. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ טִלְטוּל מִן הַצַּד — לָא שְׁמֵיהּ טִלְטוּל. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Abba said: They say** in **the school of Rav: We already learned** in the mishna that the *halakha* is **not** in accordance with the opinion of **Rav Naḥman:** With regard to **straw on top of a bed, one may not move it with his hand, but he may move it with his body. And if it is animal food, or a pillow or sheet is on it, he may move it** even **with his hand. Conclude from here** that **moving** an item **in an atypical manner is not considered moving.** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **conclude from here** that this is indeed the case.

Rav Adda bar Ahava challenges Rav Nachman from the *mishnah* just studied: *tanina delo k'Rav Nachman* — our *mishnah* contradicts Rav Nachman. The *mishnah* ruled that straw on a bed may not be moved with the hand but may be moved with the body. This implies that *tiltul begufo* is always permitted, without distinguishing between cases where the incidental movement is more or less direct — seemingly contradicting Rav Nachman's distinction between the two orientations of the turnip.

### Equating Movement Types

The *gemara* derives from the *mishnah* that since movement of a *muktzah* item with one's body is permitted, *tiltul min hatzad* is similarly not considered *tiltul* at all. The *gemara* treats these two categories as equivalent in their permissibility.

### Incidental Not Movement

The governing principle established here is: *tiltul min hatzad lo shmei tiltul* — incidental movement is not considered movement. The *gemara* understands the *mishnah*'s permission of *tiltul begufo* as proof that when one is focused on a permitted object and a *muktzah* item moves as a result, that movement carries no legal weight.

It bears noting that *lehalacha*, the distinction between the two categories is maintained even if the *gemara* appears here to equate them. *Tiltul min hatzad* is permitted when one is handling a *davar hamutar* and the *muktzah* item is displaced in the process of doing so. *Tiltul begufo* is permitted even when one is deliberately focused on the *muktzah* item itself, so long as one does not use one's hand. These describe two different phenomena; it does not necessarily follow that because one is permitted the other must be equally permitted in all respects, and yet once both categories are defined, the *gemara*'s equation here is its operative ruling.

One might ask: is this not a case of *psik reisha*? When pulling the turnip out of the ground, one certainly knows the dirt will be displaced. The answer is that we are not speaking of a *melacha* in the classic sense but rather of the act of *tiltul* itself — specifically whether what occurs constitutes a prohibited form of moving. It is the character of the movement, incidental versus direct, that is being defined, not whether the consequence was inevitable.

## Grinding Pepper

### Rav Yehuda: Peppers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הָנֵי פִּלְפְּלֵי, מֵידַק חֲדָא חֲדָא בְּקַתָּא דְסַכִּינָא — שְׁרֵי, תַּרְתֵּי — אֲסִיר. רָבָא אָמַר: כֵּיוָן דִּמְשַׁנֵּי — אֲפִילּוּ טוּבָא נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a somewhat similar case. **Rav Yehuda said:** In the case of **these peppers, crushing them one by one with the handle of a knife is permitted.** However, crushing **two** at a time **is prohibited,** because it appears to be a prohibited labor. **Rava said: Since he alters** the manner in which he performs this activity, **even** crushing **many** at a time **is also** permitted.

Rav Yehuda addresses the grinding of peppercorns — here meaning actual peppercorn spice as known in Talmudic times. He rules: *hanei pilpeli midek chada chada bekata desakina* — one may grind them one at a time using the handle of a knife. This is *shari* — permissible.

### Two at a Time

However, *tarti asiri* — grinding two at a time is forbidden. By grinding one at a time with the handle rather than the blade of a knife, a double *shinui* is introduced: the instrument is used unconventionally (*bekata desakina*) and only one peppercorn is processed at a time. This combination apparently places the act sufficiently outside the normal manner of grinding to permit it.

### Rava: Shinui Permits

Rava disagrees with the upper limit: *keivan demishani afilu tuva nami* — once one has introduced any *shinui*, even many peppercorns at once are permissible. Since the peppercorns are not being ground in the normal manner (using a pepper mill), the act of grinding has been sufficiently altered.

### Biblical Melacha

This is a striking *gemara*. Grinding spices is a *melacha* — and apparently a *melacha de'oraita*. The suggestion that a *shinui* renders biblical labor permissible on *Shabbat* demands explanation. Tosafot grapples with this at length, considering whether the discussion might actually pertain to *Yom Tov* rather than *Shabbat*. Tosafot concludes, however, that the text is clearly speaking of *Shabbat* — were it *Yom Tov*, the text would have said so explicitly. Moreover, Tosafot notes that on *Yom Tov* one might actually be permitted to use a conventional pepper mill, since spices lose potency if ground in advance and one may not have anticipated the need, so *Yom Tov* presents its own *heter* without recourse to the *shinui* argument.

The *gemara* thus appears to permit a biblical *melacha* on *Shabbat* through the mechanism of *shinui*. Without resolving the *halacha* definitively, it may be noted that there are contexts in which certain labors are permitted on *Shabbat* with a *shinui* — sometimes requiring a double *shinui* — particularly in areas like *losh* (kneading), where the back of a spoon and an unconventional mixing motion are discussed. The ruling here is nonetheless quite remarkable.

## Swimming/Bathing

### Rav Yehuda: Drying

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: מַאן דְּסָחֵי בְּמַיָּא — לִינַגֵּיב נַפְשֵׁיהּ בְּרֵישָׁא וַהֲדַר לִיסְלֵיק, דִילְמָא אָתֵי לְאֵתוֹיֵי אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בְּכַרְמְלִית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda** also **said: One who bathes in water should first dry himself** immediately upon exiting, **and then ascend** to the coast, **lest he come to carry** the remaining drops of water on his body **four cubits in a *karmelit*.**

Rav Yehuda turns to a case of bathing: *man desachi bemaya lingiv nafshei berisha vehadar lislok* — one who bathes in water (in a permissible context) should first dry oneself and only then step out of the bath.

### Karmelit Water

The reason is *dilma ati le'atuyei arba amot bekarmelit* — one might otherwise come to carry water on one's body for four *amot* through a *karmelit*. Even though a *karmelit* is only a rabbinic prohibition — it is not a full *reshut harabbim* — the concern is still sufficient to require the safeguard of drying oneself first.

### Ashkenazi Custom

This concern about swimming-related *Shabbat* violations underlies the *Ashkenazi* custom of prohibiting immersion in cold water on *Shabbat* altogether. While there is nothing in the *gemara* that categorically prohibits swimming in cold water in a permissible location, the *minhag* — which developed approximately 1,400 to 1,700 years ago — reflects the accumulation of several concerns: carrying water on one's body through a *karmelit*, squeezing out a towel (*sechita*), and squeezing water from one's hair. Because of these various secondary concerns converging around the act of swimming, the *Ashkenazi* practice emerged to avoid full immersion in cold water on *Shabbat* entirely.

The *Sephardic* practice does not share this prohibition — in cold water, in a swimming pool, with none of the concerns about heated water (which raises a separate *gezeirah* that one will come to heat water on *Shabbat*), the *gemara* provides no basis for prohibition.

### Mikvah Exception

The case of *mikvah* is always excluded from the swimming restrictions. Women are required to immerse in a *mikvah* on *Shabbat*, and therefore *mikvah* was never subject to the concerns about carrying water or squeezing. Indeed, *mikvah* was even exempted from the prohibition against heated water that ordinarily applies — because of its necessity, it always received a pass.

### Kocho: Energy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, כִּי קָא נָחֵית נָמֵי, קָא דָּחֵי כֹּחוֹ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, וַאֲסִיר! כֹּחוֹ בְּכַרְמְלִית — לָא גְזַרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** if there is a concern about carrying water, there should be concern **when one descends** into the water **as well.** In that case, **his force propels** water **four cubits** into the river, **and** it should be **prohibited.** The Gemara answers: With regard to movement that results from **his force in a *karmelit*,** the Sages **did not** issue a **decree.** Since one does not directly propel the water, but the water moves only as an extension of his motion, and since the river is a *karmelit*, and the prohibition to carry there is only a rabbinic law, the Sages did not issue a decree for one who descends into the water.

The *gemara* raises an objection: when one enters the water, one's body displaces water and sends waves traveling four *amot* through the *karmelit* — should this not itself be forbidden as a form of carrying? The *gemara* responds: *kocho b'karmelit lo gazru* — the Rabbis did not make a *gezeirah* against the indirect force exerted by one's body, meaning the waves and energy displacement produced by entering water. This is one's *koach*, one's indirect force, and that was not included in the prohibition.

## Scraping Mud/Plaster

### Plaster Scraping

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב יְהוּדָה: טִיט שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי רַגְלוֹ — מְקַנְּחוֹ בַּקַּרְקַע, וְאֵין מְקַנְּחוֹ בַּכּוֹתֶל.
>
> **Translation:** In a similar decree, **Abaye said, and some say** that it was stated by **Rav Yehuda:** If one has **mud on his foot, he may wipe it on the ground** on Shabbat, **but he may not wipe it on a wall.**

Abaye — and some attribute this to Rav Yehuda — rules: *tina de'al gabei raglo mikancho b'karka ve'ein mikancho b'kosel* — if one has plaster on one's legs, one may scrape it off on the ground, but not on the wall.

### Rava: Wall/Ground

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא בַּכּוֹתֶל לָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּמֶיחְזֵי כְּבוֹנֶה? הָא בִּנְיָן חַקְלָאָה הוּא! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: מְקַנְּחוֹ בַּכּוֹתֶל וְאֵין מְקַנְּחוֹ בַּקַּרְקַע — דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְאַשְׁווֹיֵי גּוּמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: What is the reason** that he may **not** wipe it off **on a wall?** Is it **because it appears like building,** as he is adding plaster to the wall? **That is the building of a field laborer,** which is not an actual building. There is no concern in that case because in adding plaster to that building, one does not perform the prohibited labor of building. **Rather, Rava said:** On the contrary, **He may wipe it on a wall, but he may not wipe it on the ground.** This is due to the concern **lest one come to level holes** in the ground while wiping his foot.

Rava explains the rationale: scraping plaster onto a wall is forbidden because *d'mechze k'boneh* — it looks like building, as though one is applying plaster to the wall and adding to its structure. The *gemara* characterizes this as the kind of building done by field hands — not professional construction, but building nonetheless.

Rava then inverts the ruling: one should scrape on the wall — *mikancho b'kosel* — and not on the ground — *ve'ein mikancho b'karka* — because *dilma ati le'ashvei gumos* — one might come to level the ground, which constitutes a form of *boneh* or *choresh*. So the two statements — Abaye/Rav Yehuda and Rava — appear to contradict each other.

### Contradiction: Allowed/Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר. מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבִינָא אָמַר: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה — אָסוּר. רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **It is stated** that other *amora'im* disputed this issue. **Mar, son of Ravina, said: Both this,** wiping the mud on a wall, **and that,** wiping the mud on the ground, **are prohibited. Rav Pappa said: Both this and that are permitted.**

Mar brei d'Ravina rules that neither is permissible: *echad ze ve'echad ze assur*. Rav Papa rules that both are permissible: *echad ze ve'echad ze mutar*.

### Kora Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבִינָא בְּמַאי מְקַנְּחִי לֵיהּ? מְקַנְּחִי לֵיהּ בְּקוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **According to** the opinion of **Mar, son of Ravina, with what may he wipe** his foot? The Gemara answers: Even according to his opinion, there is a permitted manner to clean his foot; **he wipes it on a beam** on the ground.

According to Mar brei d'Ravina, who forbids both, one must ask how the plaster is to be removed at all. The answer: *mikanchei lei b'kora* — one scrapes it on a beam (*kora*) resting on the ground. This avoids both problems simultaneously: one is neither adding to a wall structure nor leveling the ground surface. One might add, with some humor, that going swimming would also solve the problem — though that creates its own difficulties on *Shabbat*.

## Lechi & Carrying

### Rava: Lechi Danger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לָא לִיתִּיב אִינִישׁ אַפּוּמֵּיהּ דְּלִיחְיָיא, דִילְמָא מִיגַּנְדְּרָא לֵיהּ חֵפֶץ וְאָתֵי לְאֵתוֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: A person should not sit** on Shabbat right **at the entrance** to a closed alleyway where **a side post** is placed as a symbolic partition enabling one to carry inside the alleyway. The reason for this is that **perhaps an object will roll** into the public domain **and one will come to get it,** as there is no conspicuous demarcation between inside and outside the alleyway.

Rava warns: *lo laseiv inish appuma d'leichaya* — a person should not sit right up against the *lechi* post. A *mavoi* — a semi-enclosed alleyway into which several courtyards open — may be used for carrying on *Shabbat* if it possesses a *lechi* at its open end. The function of the *lechi* is to serve as a *heicher* — a marker alerting one that one is about to exit the enclosed area and enter a domain where carrying is prohibited.

### Rolling Objects

If one sits with one's back pressed against the *lechi* post, one is effectively covering the marker. *Dilma megandara lei chefetz* — an object may roll away from that person and out of the *mavoi* into the *reshut harabbim*, and since the person is not attending to the *lechi* as a boundary marker, he may instinctively retrieve the object without realizing he has crossed into the *reshut harabbim*.

## Heavy Objects

### Barrel Adjustment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: לָא לִיצַדֵּד אִינִישׁ כּוּבָּא, דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְאַשְׁווֹיֵי גּוּמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava** also **said** a similar decree: **A person may not position a barrel** on a dirt floor, **lest he come to level holes** in the ground while smoothing the surface upon which he is positioning the barrel.

Rava further rules: *lo litzaded inish kuba* — a person should not adjust or settle a large barrel of wine into position on *Shabbat*.

### Smoothing Ground

The concern is *dilma ati le'ashvei gumos* — the barrel is large and heavy, and dragging or adjusting it along the ground may cause one to smooth out the ground surface in the process, constituting a form of *boneh*.

### Cloth Tightening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: לָא לִיהַדֵּק אִינִישׁ אוּדְרָא בְּפוּמָּא דְשִׁישָׁא, דִילְמָא אָתֵי לִידֵי סְחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** furthermore, **Rava said: A person may not stuff a rag into the mouth of a jug [*shisha*]** on Shabbat, **lest he come to** violate the prohibition of **squeezing** liquid from the cloth.

Rava adds another warning: *lo lehadik inish udra bepuma deshisha* — one should not tighten a cloth over the opening of a pitcher.

### Squeezing Oil

The concern here is *dilma ati lidei sechita* — one may come to squeeze out the cloth. The cloth is resting over the opening of a vessel containing oil, and as one tightens it, the cloth absorbs the oil and the act of tightening may cause the oil to be squeezed back out. Whether the resulting prohibition is biblical or rabbinic — and whether *sechita* is prohibited only when one desires the liquid being squeezed, or regardless — is a discussion among the *Rishonim*, though clearly if the squeezed oil flows back into the vessel, there is good reason to consider it a genuine concern.

## Plaster from Clothes

### Rav Kahana: Inside/Out

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: טִיט שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי בִּגְדּוֹ — מְכַסְכְּסוֹ מִבִּפְנִים וְאֵין מְכַסְכְּסוֹ מִבַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said:** With regard to **mortar that is on one's garment** on Shabbat, **one may rub it** off **from the inside, but one may not rub it** off **from the outside,** because that is comparable to the prohibited labor of laundering.

Rav Kahana rules: *tit al gabei begado mechaschecho mibifnim ve'ein mechaschecho mibachutz* — if one has plaster on one's garment, one may rub it away from the inside of the garment, but not from the outside. The image is one of working from behind the fabric rather than pressing and rubbing directly on the surface — a less forceful and less conventional method of removing the stain.

### Why the Distinction

The question is what precisely distinguishes the two techniques. The underlying concern is laundering — *libun* — specifically the prohibition of removing an embedded substance from a cloth. The issue hinges on when one crosses from removing surface matter to dislodging something that has become embedded in the fibers. Rubbing from the outside, in the conventional manner of treating a stain, more readily embeds the action in the category of laundering. Rubbing from the inside is either less effective in dislodging embedded material, or simply does not resemble the prohibited act. Whether the distinction is about the degree of resemblance to ordinary laundering (*uvda d'chol*) or about the *shinui* introduced by working from the inside is not entirely resolved.

### Rashi: No Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: טִיט שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי מִנְעָלוֹ — מְגָרְרוֹ בְּגַב סַכִּין, וְשֶׁעַל בִּגְדּוֹ — מְגָרְרוֹ בְּצִפּוֹרֶן, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יְכַסְכֵּס. מַאי לָאו, שֶׁלֹּא יְכַסְכֵּס כְּלָל! לָא שֶׁלֹּא יְכַסְכֵּס מִבַּחוּץ, אֶלָּא מִבִּפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from that which we learned: With regard to **mortar that is on one's shoe, he may scrape it** off **with the back of a knife** as a departure from the typical manner of scraping. **And** mortar **which is on one's clothes, he may scrape** off **with his fingernail, as long as he does not rub it.** The Gemara asks: **What, is it not** saying **that he may not rub** it **at all?** The Gemara rejects that premise: **No,** it is saying **that he may not rub** it **from the outside, rather from the inside.**

Rashi offers an important observation at the bottom of the relevant passage: *umelabben mamash lo havei de'eika de'ein sham mayim* — whatever one does here, it does not constitute actual *libun*, because there is no water involved. According to Rashi, biblical laundering cannot be transgressed without the use of water — even if one succeeds in removing an embedded substance from a cloth by rubbing alone, the absence of water means no biblical violation has occurred. This is a notable position, as many *Rishonim* and *Poskim* disagree and hold that the use of water is not a prerequisite for the biblical prohibition of *libun*.

## Mud from Shoes

### New vs. Old Shoes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: מְגָרְרִין מִנְעָל חָדָשׁ, אֲבָל לֹא יָשָׁן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu said** that **Rabbi Elazar said** that **Rabbi Yannai said: One may scrape** mud off of **a new shoe** on Shabbat, **but not** off of **an old** shoe, because a layer of the shoe will be removed, which constitutes the prohibited labor of smoothing.

Rav Ivya cites Rav Elazar citing Rav Yanai: *megarin mina chadasha velo yeshana* — one may scrape mud off a new shoe but not an old one. Rashi explains that on an old shoe, the leather may no longer be in good condition, and scraping it could smooth out the leather surface, potentially treating it in a way that amounts to tanning or refining.

### Knife Tool

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה מְגָרְרוֹ? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: בְּגַב סַכִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **With what does one scrape it off? Rabbi Abahu said: With the back of a knife,** which is a departure from the typical manner of doing so.

The *gemara* asks how one scrapes: Rav Abbahu answers *begav sakkin* — with the back of a knife, as stated earlier. The back of a knife is narrow and effective for scraping surface mud without cutting into or deeply affecting the material beneath.

### Rabbi Chiya: No Scraping

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא סָבָא: סְמִי דִּידָךְ מִקַּמֵּי הָא דְתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: אֵין מְגָרְרִין לֹא מִנְעָל חָדָשׁ וְלֹא מִנְעָל יָשָׁן, וְלֹא יָסוּךְ אֶת רַגְלוֹ שֶׁמֶן וְהוּא בְּתוֹךְ הַמִּנְעָל אוֹ בְּתוֹךְ הַסַּנְדָּל. אֲבָל סָךְ אֶת רַגְלוֹ שֶׁמֶן, וּמַנִּיחַ בְּתוֹךְ הַמִּנְעָל אוֹ בְּתוֹךְ הַסַּנְדָּל. וְסָךְ כׇּל גּוּפוֹ שֶׁמֶן, וּמִתְעַגֵּל עַל גַּבֵּי קַטְבֻלְיָא, וְאֵינוֹ חוֹשֵׁשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **A certain Elder said to** Rabbi Abbahu: **Delete your** teaching **before this** statement **taught by Rabbi Ḥiyya: One may not scrape** at all; **neither a new shoe nor an old shoe, and one may not smear oil on one's foot while it is inside the shoe or inside the sandal,** as the oil is absorbed by the leather of the shoe and strengthens it, which constitutes performance of the prohibited labor of tanning. **However, one may smear oil on his foot** in the typical manner **and place** it afterward **in a shoe or in a sandal,** and he need not be concerned that this oil will enhance the shoe leather. **And he may** likewise **smear oil on his entire body with oil and roll on a leather carpet, and** he need **not be concerned.**

An elder challenges this teaching by citing a *baraita* of Rabbi Chiya: *ein megarin lo min hachadash velo min hayashan* — one may not scrape mud from either new or old shoes. This appears to contradict Rav Abbahu's ruling entirely. The two positions are not explicitly reconciled here, though it may be that Rabbi Chiya's *baraita* takes a more stringent view across the board.

### Oil in Shoe

Rabbi Chiya's *baraita* adds a further prohibition: *velo yasuch et raglo shemen vehu betoch hamin'al o betoch hasandal* — one may not apply oil to one's leg while one's leg is already inside the shoe or sandal. The concern is that the oil will trickle down from the leg onto the leather of the shoe, thereby treating the leather — *me'abed* being one of the *avot melachot*.

### Pre vs. During

However, the *baraita* permits the reverse sequence: *aval sach et raglo shemen umani'ach betoch hamin'al* — one may first apply oil to one's leg and then place the leg into the shoe. In that case, what subsequently occurs to the leather is entirely incidental — one applied the oil to one's own skin, not to the leather, and any contact with the shoe is a secondary consequence. Similarly, *vesach kol gufo shemen umitgalgel al gabei katavliya* — one may oil one's entire body and then roll on a leather mat, again because the oiling of the leather is incidental to the primary act of oiling one's body.

### Rav Chisda: Oil Amount

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שִׁיעוּר לְצַחְצְחוֹ, אֲבָל שִׁיעוּר לְעַבְּדוֹ — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: They only taught** that it is permitted to do so on Shabbat in a case where one smears **a measure** sufficient only **to polish it; however,** if one smears **a measure** sufficient **to tan it, it is prohibited,** even if he did not intend to tan the leather.

Rav Chisda qualifies the entire framework: *lo shanu ela shiur letzach-tzecho aval le'avdo assur* — all these permissions apply only when the amount of oil on one's body is sufficient to give the leather a shine (*tzach-tzacho*) but not enough to actually treat and tan it (*le'avdo*). If one has merely enough oil to produce a shine, then even if incidentally transferred to the leather, the act may remain at a rabbinic level of concern — and in that case, the incidental occurrence is permissible. But if there is enough oil to actually cause full leather treatment, *me'abed*, then even incidental transfer is forbidden, because the biblical *melacha* could be triggered regardless of intent.

The *gemara* initially presents Rav Chisda's statement as distinguishing between intent to shine versus intent to treat, but immediately corrects itself: who would ever permit intentionally applying oil to leather even to shine it? The statement is reformulated to hinge on quantity: the question is whether the *shiur* of oil present is sufficient only for *tzach-tzacho* or sufficient for *me'abed*. This makes the ruling far more workable — the permissibility of incidental leather contact depends entirely on how much oil is present on the skin, not on one's stated intention.

## Shoe Restrictions

### Tanu Rabbanan: Shoes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לֹא יֵצֵא קָטָן בְּמִנְעָל גָּדוֹל, אֲבָל יוֹצֵא הוּא בְּחָלוּק גָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **A small** person **may not go out in** a too **large shoe,** due to concern lest the shoe fall off and he will come to carry it in the public domain; **but he may go out in** a too **large cloak.** Even if it does not fit him properly, it will certainly not fall off.

A *baraita* teaches: *lo yeitzei katan bemin'al gadol* — a small person should not go out wearing an oversized shoe on *Shabbat*. However, *aval yotzei hu bechaluk gadol* — one may go out wearing oversized clothing.

### Removal Concern

The reason for the shoe restriction is the concern that the poorly fitting shoe will be removed and then carried in a *reshut harabbim*. A garment, even an oversized one, does not generate the same concern.

### Women's Sensitivity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא תֵּצֵא אִשָּׁה בְּמִנְעָל מְרוּפָּט, וְלֹא תַּחֲלוֹץ בּוֹ, וְאִם חָלְצָה — חֲלִיצָתָהּ כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And a woman may not go out** on Shabbat **in a shoe that is torn on top,** lest she be mocked, remove it, and carry it on Shabbat. **And** a shoe of that kind **may not be used to perform *ḥalitza*,** as it is not a suitable shoe. *Ḥalitza* is performed by a widow bound in a levirate bond with her brother-in-law. **However, if she performed *ḥalitza*** with it, **her *ḥalitza* is valid,** since ultimately it is a shoe.

*Velo teitzei isha bemin'al merufat* — a woman should not go out wearing a shoe that is *merufat* — damaged or torn at the top — because women are more likely to be sensitive to wearing items that are damaged or unflattering, and are therefore more likely to remove such a shoe in public and carry it. The concern for men with a clearly oversized shoe is lesser, in keeping with the general principle that men are less bothered by the appearance of what they wear, whereas women are more sensitive to such matters.

### Chalitza Issue

*Velo tachloz bo* — a *merufat* shoe may not be used for *chalitza* because it is not a complete, intact shoe. However, *ve'im chaltza chalitza kesheira* — if such a shoe was in fact used for *chalitza*, the *chalitza* is nonetheless valid *bedi'eved*, since it still qualifies as a shoe in some sense.

### New Shoe Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין יוֹצְאִין בְּמִנְעָל חָדָשׁ. בְּאֵיזֶה מִנְעָל אָמְרוּ — בְּמִנְעָל שֶׁל אִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **And one may not go out** on Shabbat wearing **a new shoe,** due to the concern that it will not fit properly, and then one will remove it and carry it. The Gemara comments: In **what case** did **they say** that one may not wear a new **shoe?** They said this **with regard to a woman's shoe,** as women are very particular about having their shoes fit properly.

*Ve'ein yotzim bemin'al chadash* — one may not go out wearing a new shoe. The question is what "new" means in this context. Rashi explains that a new shoe has not yet been broken in and does not conform to the exact shape of the foot (*im einena mechuyevet lemidas ragla*). Because of this poor fit, a woman is more likely to remove the shoe in public, triggering the concern of carrying.

### Bar Kappara: Pre-Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי בַּר קַפָּרָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא יָצְאָה בּוֹ שָׁעָה אַחַת מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם, אֲבָל יָצְאָה בּוֹ מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Bar Kappara taught: They only taught** that she may not go out wearing a new shoe on Shabbat **if she did not** yet **go out** wearing **it for any** length of **time while** it was still **day. However, if she went out** wearing **it on Shabbat eve,** at which point she would have ascertained if it fits her, she is **permitted** to go out wearing it on Shabbat.

Bar Kappara qualifies: *lo shanu ela shelo yatza bo sha'ah achat mibode yom* — the prohibition of a new shoe applies only if it has never been worn even for a single hour before *Shabbat*. *Aval yatza bo me'erev Shabbat mutar* — if one wore it outside even briefly on *Erev Shabbat*, it is permissible to wear on *Shabbat*, as the minimal prior use suffices to break it in.

## Shoe from Last

### Shoe Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי חֲדָא: שׁוֹמְטִין מִנְעָל מֵעַל גַּבֵּי אֵימוּס, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: אֵין שׁוֹמְטִין. לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, הָא — רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **One** *baraita* **taught: One may remove a shoe from the** shoemaker's **last,** the frame on which a shoe is shaped, on Shabbat. **And another** *baraita* **taught** the opposite: **One may not remove it.** The Gemara explains that this is **not difficult: This** *baraita*, which prohibits doing so, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** and **that** *baraita*, which permits doing so, is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis.** Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis dispute the applicability of the *halakhot* of ritual purity and impurity in a similar case.

The *gemara* cites two contradictory *baraitot*. One states that one may remove a shoe from the *imos* (the last — the wooden form on which the shoe is constructed) on *Shabbat*. The other states that one may not. This contradiction needs to be resolved.

### Kelim Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: מִנְעָל שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי אֵימוּס, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מְטַהֵר, וַחֲכָמִים מְטַמְּאִים.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna: **A shoe that** remains **on a last, Rabbi Eliezer deems** it **pure,** i.e., unable to become impure, as in his opinion the shoe is not yet complete, and therefore it is not yet a vessel and cannot become impure. **And the Rabbis deem it** capable of becoming **impure,** as in their opinion, the shoe is completed, and any vessel whose work is complete can become ritually impure. Correspondingly, the Rabbis, who hold that a shoe on a last is a completed vessel, hold that it may be moved on Shabbat. Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that it is an incomplete vessel, holds that it may not be moved.

The source of the dispute is a *mishnah* in *Kelim*: *minal she'al gabei imos Rabbi Eliezer metaher* — a shoe that is still on the last, Rabbi Eliezer rules it is *tahor*, because it is not yet considered a complete vessel. *Vachachamim metamin* — the Rabbis say it is *tamei*, because they consider the shoe complete once its construction is finished, regardless of whether it has been removed from the last.

This dispute has direct implications for *Shabbat*: according to Rabbi Eliezer, the shoe still on the last has not yet acquired the status of a shoe-vessel, and is therefore *muktzah*. According to the Rabbis, the shoe is complete and is not *muktzah*. The first *baraita* follows the Rabbis' view; the second follows Rabbi Eliezer.

### Raba: Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְרָבָא, דְּאָמַר: דָּבָר שֶׁמְּלַאכְתּוֹ לְאִיסּוּר, בֵּין לְצוֹרֶךְ גּוּפוֹ, בֵּין לְצוֹרֶךְ מְקוֹמוֹ — מוּתָּר, שַׁפִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question: **It works out well according to** the opinion of **Rava, who said:** Moving **an object whose** primary **function is for a prohibited** use, **whether for the purpose** of utilizing the object **itself** to perform a permitted action **or for the purpose of** utilizing **its place, is permitted. It is well** understood that one may move the last slightly while removing the shoe, since removing the shoe is considered utilizing the last's place.

The *gemara* notes that the ability to remove the shoe from the last depends not only on the shoe's status but on that of the last itself. The last is a tool used for shoemaking — it is a *kli shemlachto l'issur*, a tool whose primary use involves a prohibited labor. Raba rules that a *kli shemlachto l'issur* may be moved *bein l'tzorech gufo bein l'tzorech mekomo* — both for the purpose of its own use and to free up the space it occupies. According to Raba, therefore, the last may be moved to facilitate removal of the shoe — this is *tzorech mekomo*, freeing up space — and the *baraita* permitting removal works out well.

### Abaye: Broader View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְאַבָּיֵי, דְּאָמַר: לְצוֹרֶךְ גּוּפוֹ — מוּתָּר, לְצוֹרֶךְ מְקוֹמוֹ — אָסוּר. מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** **However, according to Abaye,** who **said** that **for the purpose** of utilizing the object **itself** to perform a permitted action, **it is permitted;** however, **for the purpose of utilizing its place, it is prohibited, what can be said?** It is prohibited to move the last, which is clearly a utensil whose primary function is for a prohibited use. How is it possible to remove the shoe without moving the last?

Abaye, however, disagrees on the rule for *kli shemlachto l'issur*: *l'tzorech gufo mutar l'tzorech mekomo assur* — it may be moved for its own designated purpose but not merely to clear its space. This creates a problem: if removing the last from the shoe is *tzorech mekomo* — clearing the space inside the shoe so one can use it — then according to Abaye, one cannot move the last at all.

One might initially have thought that pulling the last out of the shoe in order to use the shoe is *tzorech gufo* — one is, in some sense, using the last in the process of extracting the shoe. The *gemara*, however, understands this as *tzorech mekomo* — the last is simply in the way, occupying a space that needs to be cleared. And according to Abaye, that is not a sufficient reason to move a *kli shemlachto l'issur*.

### Loose vs. Tight

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — בְּרָפוּי. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִם הָיָה רָפוּי — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** It is **with** a shoe placed **loosely** on the last, so the shoe can be removed without moving the last. **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says: If it was loose, it is permitted.**

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty: *hakha b'mai askinan — be'rafui* — the case concerns a shoe that is loosely (*rafui*) resting on the last, not tightly fitted. When the shoe is loose, one can remove the shoe by handling the shoe itself, without having to grip or directly move the last at all. The last simply stays behind. There is therefore no problem with the last as a *kli shemlachto l'issur*, since it is not being moved; only the shoe is handled.

A *baraita* confirms this: *Rabbi Yehuda omer im haya rafui muttar* — Rabbi Yehuda says if it is loose, it is permissible.

### Rabbi Yehuda Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּרָפוּי, הָא לֹא רָפוּי — לָא. הָנִיחָא לְאַבָּיֵי, דְּאָמַר: דָּבָר שֶׁמְּלַאכְתּוֹ לְאִיסּוּר, לְצוֹרֶךְ גּוּפוֹ — מוּתָּר, לְצוֹרֶךְ מְקוֹמוֹ — אָסוּר, שַׁפִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: **The reason** it is permitted is only **because it is loose; however,** if it is **not loose, no,** it is prohibited. **It works out well according to** the opinion of **Abaye,** who **said** that moving **an object** whose primary **function** is **for** a **prohibited** use **for the purpose** of utilizing the object **itself** to perform a permitted action **it is permitted;** however, **for the purpose of utilizing its place, it is prohibited** to move it. **It is well** understood that it is prohibited to move the last if the shoe is tightly attached to it.

Having established that looseness resolves the problem according to Abaye, the *gemara* asks how this teaching integrates with Raba's view. According to Raba, who permits moving the last for *tzorech mekomo* in any case, why does Rabbi Yehuda specifically require that it be loose? If it is tight, Raba would still permit moving the last. The *gemara* answers: *hee d'Rabbi Yehuda mishum d'Rabbi Eliezer hee* — Rabbi Yehuda's statement is not advancing his own independent position on the last; rather, it is addressing the concern of Rabbi Eliezer regarding the shoe itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרָבָא, דְּאָמַר: בֵּין לְצוֹרֶךְ גּוּפוֹ, בֵּין לְצוֹרֶךְ מְקוֹמוֹ — מוּתָּר, מַאי אִירְיָא רָפוּי? אֲפִילּוּ לֹא רָפוּי נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **However, according to Rava, who said** that moving **an object whose** primary **function** is **for a prohibited** use, **whether for the purpose** of utilizing the object **itself** to perform a permitted action **or for the purpose of** utilizing **its place, is permitted, why** discuss **specifically** a case where it is **loose? Even if it were not loose,** it should **also** be permitted to move it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מִשּׁוּם דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הוּא. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: אִם הָיָה רָפוּי — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **That** *baraita* **is** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Eliezer,** and it is not a clarification of the opinion of the Rabbis. Although Rabbi Eliezer holds that the shoe is not yet completed, nevertheless, Rabbi Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Eliezer that it is permitted to carry it. **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Eliezer: If it was** already **loose** and no longer requires a last, **it is permitted** to move it, since it is a completed vessel and no longer needs the last to shape it.

The *baraita* is explicit: *Rabbi Yehuda omer mishum Rabbi Eliezer im haya rafui muttar* — Rabbi Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Eliezer: if it is loose, it is permissible. Rabbi Eliezer holds that the shoe is not yet a vessel so long as it remains on the last. But if the shoe has been loosened from the last — the last is no longer tightly embedded within it, and it has begun to separate — then even Rabbi Eliezer will acknowledge that the shoe has achieved its independent identity as a shoe. The shoe is therefore no longer *muktzah* even according to Rabbi Eliezer, and one may handle it.

Thus the distinction between tight and loose in Rabbi Yehuda's statement is not about the *last* at all — it is about defining at what point, in Rabbi Eliezer's view, the shoe crosses the threshold into *vessel* status.

## Carrying Infants

### Taking a Child

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נוֹטֵל אָדָם אֶת בְּנוֹ וְהָאֶבֶן בְּיָדוֹ. וְכַלְכַּלָּה וְהָאֶבֶן בְּתוֹכָהּ. וּמְטַלְטְלִין תְּרוּמָה טְמֵאָה עִם הַטְּהוֹרָה, וְעִם הַחוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A person may take his son** in his hands on Shabbat, **and** even though there **is a stone,** which is a set-aside item, **in** the child's **hand,** it is not prohibited to pick up the child. **And** it is permissible to take **a basket with a stone inside it** on Shabbat. **And one may move ritually impure *teruma*,** which may not be eaten and is set-aside, **with ritually pure** *teruma,* **as well as with non-sacred** produce.

The new *perek* returns to *muktzah*. The *mishnah* opens: *notel adam et bno ve'even beyado* — one may pick up one's child even though the child is holding a stone. The stone is *muktzah*, yet because one is fundamentally handling the child, the stone's movement is incidental.

### Raba: Live Infant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הוֹצִיא תִּינוֹק חַי וְכִיס תָּלוּי בְּצַוָּארוֹ — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם כִּיס. תִּינוֹק מֵת וְכִיס תָּלוּי לוֹ בְּצַוָּארוֹ — פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rava said:** If one **carried out a living baby** to the public domain on Shabbat, **and** the baby had **a purse** that **was hanging** around **his neck, he is liable for** carrying out **the purse.** However, one who carried out **a dead baby, with a purse hanging** around **his neck, is exempt.**

The *gemara* now brings a teaching of Raba that connects to this *mishnah*: *hotzi tinok chai ve'kis tali b'tzavaro chayav mishum kis* — one who carries a live infant into a *reshut harabbim* while the infant has a money pouch hanging around its neck is liable for carrying the money pouch. This is not *muktzah* but the domain-crossing prohibition of carrying on *Shabbat*, though the *gemara* treats the two contexts as governed by analogous principles of primary and secondary objects.

### Self-Carrying

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* תִּינוֹק חַי וְכִיס תָּלוּי לוֹ בְּצַוָּארוֹ — חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם כִּיס: וְלִיחַיַּיב נָמֵי מִשּׁוּם תִּינוֹק!
>
> **Translation:** Rava said: If one **carried out a living baby** to the public domain on Shabbat, **and a purse was hanging** around the baby's **neck, he is liable for** carrying out **the purse.** The Gemara asks: **And let him be liable for** carrying out the **baby as well.**

The *gemara* asks: why is one not liable also for carrying the infant? Because Raba holds like Rabbi Noson: *chai nosei et atzmo* — a living person carries himself. When one carries a living, mobile person, one is not considered to be doing the prohibited act of carrying that person; their own vitality renders them self-carried. At minimum this requires that they be capable of some form of movement, though how much mobility is necessary is a discussion Tosafot addresses.

### Pouch Independence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא כְּרַבִּי נָתָן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר חַי נוֹשֵׂא אֶת עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Rava holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Natan, who said: A living being carries itself.** Therefore, one who carries a living being from one domain to another is not liable.

But given this, why is the money pouch not considered secondary to the infant and therefore also exempt? The *mishnah* itself teaches: *ha-motzi et ha-chai b'mitah patur af al ha-mitah* — one who carries a live person in a bed is exempt even for the bed, because the bed is *tefeilah lo* — secondary to the person. If the bed is nullified by the live person, why is the money pouch not similarly nullified?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיבְטַל כִּיס לְגַבֵּי תִּינוֹק, מִי לָא תְּנַן אֶת הַחַי בַּמִּטָּה — פָּטוּר אַף עַל הַמִּטָּה, שֶׁהַמִּטָּה טְפֵילָה לוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let** the **purse be negated relative to** the **baby;** and he should be exempt for carrying out the purse as well. **Didn't we learn in a mishna:** One who carries out a **living** person **on a bed is exempt even for** carrying out **the bed, because the bed is secondary to** the person? The same should be said with regard to the purse, relative to the baby.

The *gemara* distinguishes: *mitah l'gabe chai mivtali lei* — the bed in relation to a live person is indeed secondary, because the bed is there solely to serve the person, in the manner of something that clothes or supports him. *Kis l'gabe tinok lo mivtali lei* — but a money pouch hanging around a child's neck retains its independent identity. It is not there to serve the child in the way a bed serves its occupant; it remains an independent object that one also has in mind. Therefore one is liable for it separately.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִטָּה לְגַבֵּי חַי — מְבַטְּלִי לֵיהּ, כִּיס לְגַבֵּי תִּינוֹק — לָא מְבַטְּלִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where **a bed** is **relative to a living being,** the living being **negates it,** as the bed is needed to carry the person and is secondary to him. However, in a case where **a purse** is **relative to a baby,** the baby does **not negate it,** since it is independently significant.

### Dead Infant Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינוֹק מֵת וְכִיס תָּלוּי לוֹ בְּצַוָּארוֹ — פָּטוּר: וְלִיחַיַּיב מִשּׁוּם תִּינוֹק! רָבָא כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ דְּאָמַר: כׇּל מְלָאכָה שֶׁאֵין צָרִיךְ לְגוּפָהּ — פָּטוּר עָלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** And Rava said: One who carried out **a dead baby with a purse hanging** around the baby's **neck is exempt.** The Gemara asks: **And let him be liable for** carrying out the **baby.** The Gemara answers: **Rava holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who said:** With regard to **any labor that is not needed for its own sake, one is exempt for** performing **it** on Shabbat. One who carries out a corpse does not do so because he needs it; rather, he does so for the sake of the corpse, i.e., to bury it or to move it from a degrading place. Therefore, he has not performed a labor prohibited by Torah law. Similarly, he is also exempt for carrying out the purse because due to his distress and mourning he negates the purse, as it is insignificant relative to the baby.

*Tinok meit ve'kis tali lo b'tzavaro patur* — if the infant is dead and has the money pouch hanging from its neck, one is exempt.

### Work Not Needed

The *gemara* asks why one is not liable for carrying the dead infant. Raba holds like Rabbi Shimon: *kol melacha she'eino tzricha l'gufo patur aleha* — for any labor that is not needed for its own intrinsic purpose, one is exempt. Taking a dead body out of the house is not done because one wants the body in a particular place; it is done because one wants the body out of the current place — precisely the definition of *melacha she'eino tzricha l'gufo*. One is therefore exempt. As for the money pouch, it is either similarly *melacha she'eino tzricha l'gufo*, or — per Rashi — in this case the pouch is more emotionally connected to the dead infant (perhaps one intends to bury the child with it), making it more secondary to the infant than in the live-infant case.

### Mishnah Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: נוֹטֵל אָדָם אֶת בְּנוֹ וְהָאֶבֶן בְּיָדוֹ? אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי בְּתִינוֹק שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ גַּעֲגוּעִין עַל אָבִיו.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: **A person may take his son** in his hands on Shabbat; **and** this is permitted even though there **is a stone in** the child's **hand.** As it can be inferred from this mishna that the stone is negated relative to the child, why, then, is he liable in the case of a purse hanging around a live baby's neck? Let the purse be negated relative to the baby. The Sages **of the school of Rabbi Yannai say:** You cannot infer from this mishna that the stone is negated and therefore it is permitted to move it. Rather, the mishna is referring **to a baby who has longings for his father.** It is permitted for the father to move the stone because if the father does not lift him, the baby might take ill.

The *gemara* now returns to the *mishnah*'s case of picking up a child who is holding a stone. The connection is important: the *mishnah* appears to treat the stone as secondary to the child — analogous to the way one might expect the money pouch to be secondary to the infant. This is precisely what prompted Raba's discussion of why, by the live-infant case, the money pouch nonetheless retains independence.

### Child's Yearning

The house of Rabbi Yannai offers an explanation for the *mishnah*'s leniency: *b'tinok sheyesh lo ga'agu'in al aviv* — the case involves a child who has a yearning (*ga'agu'in*) for his father. The stone is not truly nullified or secondary; technically, picking up a child carrying a stone does involve moving *muktzah*. The reason it is permitted is that refusing to pick up a child in such a state of longing and distress would effectively render the child a *choleh* — ill from emotional anguish — and for a *choleh* we permit *issur d'rabonon*. This is a limited justification that depends on the specific emotional circumstances.

### Muktzah Exception

This answer, while internally consistent, is a narrow one — it does not establish a broad principle that objects held by children are always secondary and *batel*. The basket-and-stone case and the *terumah* cases mentioned in the *mishnah* will require their own analysis. The *gemara* will continue to work through these implications in the discussions that follow.
