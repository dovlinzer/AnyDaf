# Nedarim 65 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Vows for Another Person

### Baraita: Benefit Vows

The daf opens at the top of 65a, continuing the discussion of the *mishnah* concerning *petach* — the "opening" used to release someone from a vow. The *gemara* had already addressed whether one may use *kvod ha-makom* or *kvod aviv ve-imo* as the basis for a *petach*, and whether a *nolad* — an unforeseen new circumstance — may serve as the grounds for retroactive *charata*. Before returning to the *mishnah*'s treatment of *nolad*, the *gemara* introduces an important digression concerning *hatarat nedarim* and the role of a third party when a vow is made for another person's sake.

The *baraita* states: *ha-mudar hana'ah me-chaveiro* — one who has vowed not to derive benefit from a friend — *ein matirin lo ella be-fanav*: the vow cannot be released except in the presence of that friend. The *baraita* does not immediately explain the reason, and the *gemara* proceeds to illuminate it through scriptural analysis.

### Moshe & Yitro Story

The *gemara* asks *minani milei* — from where do we derive this rule? Rav Nachman answers that it is sourced from the verse: *va-yomer Hashem el Moshe be-Midyan lekh shuv Mitzrayim ki meitu kol ha-anashim ha-mevakshim et nafshekha* — "And God said to Moshe in Midyan: go, return to Egypt, for all the men who sought your life have died" (Shemot 4:19). The *gemara* had already discussed on the previous daf that this verse provided Moshe with the *petach* for the vow he had made to Yitro — more precisely a *shevua*, as indicated by the use of the word *va-yo'el*, which the *gemara* reads as an expression of oath. Now the focus turns to the phrase *be-Midyan*: God told Moshe that he had taken his vow *in Midyan*, and therefore must go and release his vow *in Midyan* — that is, before Yitro himself. The basis for the release already existed — *ki meitu kol ha-anashim ha-mevakshim et nafshekha* — but that release could not take effect unless Moshe returned to Yitro and appeared before him.

The underlying logic begins to emerge. When a vow is made as an expression of commitment to another person — as Moshe's *shevua* to Yitro was an undertaking made *for Yitro's benefit*, as a promise to remain with him — that other person is not merely background context. The vow is, in a meaningful sense, a commitment *to* that person, much as a *teshuvah* for a sin *bein adam le-chaveiro* requires not only reconciliation with God but with the person wronged. Even after the *beit din* provides a technical *heter*, the interpersonal dimension demands that the other party be brought into the process.

### Nebuchadnezzar Case

The *gemara* then cites the verse from Divrei Hayamim: *ve-gam be-melech Nevuchadnezzar marad asher hishbio be-Elohim* — "and he also rebelled against King Nevuchadnezzar, who had made him swear by God." The Talmud here unfolds a dramatic narrative around this verse. Tzidkiyahu came upon Nevuchadnezzar eating a live rabbit — something deeply embarrassing — and Nevuchadnezzar compelled Tzidkiyahu to swear an oath not to reveal it. Tzidkiyahu took the oath, but later became tormented by his desire to publicize it: according to Rashi, he wanted to damage Nevuchadnezzar's reputation. He therefore went before a *beit din* and obtained a *heter* for his *shevua*. The *beit din* released him — perhaps on the grounds that he could not have anticipated how anguished he would be by the oath — and Tzidkiyahu proceeded to reveal the embarrassing episode, causing Nevuchadnezzar to be publicly denigrated.

Nevuchadnezzar, upon hearing what had happened, summoned the *Sanhedrin* and Tzidkiyahu. He confronted them: had Tzidkiyahu not sworn in the name of Heaven not to reveal this? The *Sanhedrin* replied that he had obtained a *heter*. Nevuchadnezzar was stunned — he had not known that oaths could be released — and then asked the critical question: *be-fanav o afilu shelo be-fanav?* — must this release be done in the presence of the person to whom the oath was made, or may it be done even without his presence? When told *be-fanav* — in his presence — Nevuchadnezzar turned on the *Sanhedrin* and demanded: why did you not tell Tzidkiyahu that he could not obtain a release without appearing before *me*?

*Mi-yad yashvu la-aretz yadmu ziknei bat Tziyon* — immediately the elders of Zion sat on the ground in silence. Rav Yitzchak explains that they removed the cushions from beneath them and sat on bare ground in a posture of mourning, recognizing that their failure had set catastrophic events in motion.

This *gemara* is genuinely striking. Until this point, the entire machinery of *heter nedarim* had been presented as a matter between a person and God — a technical halachic mechanism, like other rules of *Shabbos* or *issur ve-heter*, where the law has its own internal logic and the fact that one found a release was simply the operation of that system. But this narrative ruptures that framing. When a vow or *shevua* functions as a commitment to another person, the ease with which one can extract a release becomes morally troubling. How can one make a binding oath to another party and then simply slip away through a technical *heter*? The *gemara* insists that in such cases we must, at a minimum, require that the other person be present — both to force the one seeking release to confront the person to whom the commitment was made, and to impose a degree of social weight and embarrassment that puts the brakes on too easy an escape.

### Ran's Analysis

The Ran engages in a careful analysis of the rule and its underlying rationale. He begins by citing one view: the requirement of *be-fanav* applies only when the vow was made *le-tzorech chaveiro* — specifically for the sake of the other person, as in Moshe's vow to Yitro or Tzidkiyahu's oath to Nevuchadnezzar. A *neder* that was not made for another person's sake could, according to this position, be released even in that person's absence.

The Ran then notes that the question turns on a dispute found in the Yerushalmi regarding the reason for the rule. The first rationale offered there is *mipnei ha-busha* — on account of embarrassment. Since the vow was made for the other person's benefit and now the vow-maker wants to be released from it, he must appear before that person and be embarrassed. This embarrassment functions as a deterrent, adding gravity to any such release. Crucially, this rationale applies only when the vow was made for the sake of the other person — only then does embarrassment before *him specifically* make sense.

The second rationale in the Yerushalmi is *mipnei ha-chashad* — on account of suspicion. If the other person, before whom I vowed not to derive benefit, later sees me deriving benefit from his property, he will suspect me of violating my vow, not knowing I obtained a *heter*. Under this rationale, the requirement has nothing to do with whether the vow was made for the other person's sake; it applies any time the vow concerns the other person's property or domain, simply because he might see the vow-maker acting in what appears to be a prohibited manner.

The Ran draws out the practical differences. According to the *busha* rationale, *be-fanav* is strictly required — only genuine personal presence produces the embarrassment that the rule demands. According to the *chashad* rationale, the requirement can be satisfied in other ways: if the *beit din* sends word to the other party notifying him that the vow has been released, the suspicion is dispelled and physical presence is unnecessary.

The Ran then turns to the question that lies at the heart of the matter: what is the other person's actual legal status? If the vow was made for his sake, does he have the power to *refuse* to release the vow-maker? This would mirror the structure of *teshuvah bein adam le-chaveiro*, where the wronged party must grant *mechila*. The Ran cites Rabbeinu Tam's position that where the *neder* was made for another person's benefit, a release obtained *shelo mi-da'ato* — without that person's consent — is in question, and he raises the possibility that *bedi'avad* it may not work at all.

The Ran examines the Tzidkiyahu narrative for guidance. One reading is that since the *Sanhedrin* released Tzidkiyahu's oath and he was then able to reveal the secret, the *heter* must have worked *bedi'avad* — which would suggest the rule is only *lechatchila*. But the opposing view cites the very verse that introduces the story — *asher hishbio be-Elohim* — as implying that Tzidkiyahu was indeed punished for violating his oath, suggesting the *heter* did not work and the oath remained binding. The Ra'avad supports this stricter reading, pointing to the full catastrophe of the *churban* that followed as evidence of the gravity of what was done.

The Ran concludes without fully resolving the question, though his analysis tends toward the stricter position — that when a *neder* or *shevua* is made as a commitment to another person, that person becomes in some meaningful sense a party to it, and their consent may be required for any release to be effective even *bedi'avad*. This represents a remarkable extension: the ordinarily bilateral world of *nedarim*, which operates between a person and God, has been opened to admit a third party — the human being to whom the commitment was made.

Tosafot adds a further observation on the mechanism. The *baraita* says *be-fanav* — in his presence — but does not say *bi-reshuto* — with his permission. This suggests that what is required is presence, not consent. Moreover, Tosafot raises a logical difficulty: if the vow-maker initiated the vow entirely on his own, on what basis does the other person acquire any legal power over its release? The mechanism does not easily support the conclusion that another person can hold a vow in place against the vow-maker's will. The distinction between *shevua* and *neder* is relevant here as well: a *shevua* is by its nature interpersonally oriented — one can be *mashbia* another, compel another to swear — whereas a *neder* is always self-initiated. To import the logic of one into the other is a significant conceptual step, and while the *gemara* does so, the Rishonim flag the tension.

## Kenolad v'Einan Kenolad

### Rabbi Meir Position

The *gemara* now returns to the *mishnah* and its treatment of *nolad*. The next *mishnah* introduces a category that Rabbi Meir calls things that are *ke-nolad ve-einam ke-nolad* — they resemble a *nolad* event but are not truly *nolad* — and may therefore serve as valid grounds for release even though the Sages disagree.

The *mishnah* gives examples. A man says: *konam* — the fruit of the world is forbidden to me — were I to marry this woman, because her father is wicked. They then tell him: her father died, or he did *teshuvah*. Or: *konam* — I will not enter this house — because it contains a vicious dog, or a snake. They then tell him: the dog died, or the snake was killed. In all these cases, Rabbi Meir rules that release is permissible: *harei hen ke-nolad ve-einan ke-nolad*.

The reason these cases qualify under Rabbi Meir's view, despite resembling *nolad*, is that the vow-maker explicitly articulated his reason within the vow itself. He didn't simply make a general prohibition — he stated *why* he was making the vow. Since he embedded the reason in the vow's formulation, Rabbi Meir reads the vow as if it were *conditional*: he forbade the thing only insofar as the stated reason remained applicable. When the reason disappears — the father dies, the dog is killed — the *konam* lapses on its own.

The Sages, however, refuse to read the vow as a *tenai* simply because a reason was articulated. In their view, the person stated his mindset at the time, but made an unconditional vow. The subsequent change of circumstances is a *nolad*, and if *nolad* is not a valid basis for release, this case cannot be permitted.

### Nolad or Error

The *gemara* asks: what is the precise nature of Rabbi Meir's ruling? Is this a case of *nolad* — the circumstances genuinely changed after the vow — or is it a case of *taut* — error — meaning the facts were already different from what the vow-maker believed at the time he made the vow? The distinction matters for how we understand the debate and for how the *mishnah* relates to a later *mishnah* that everyone agrees governs a case of clear *taut*.

### Rav Huna: Conditional

Rav Huna answers: *na'aseh ke-mi she-hisnah nidro be-davar* — the vow is treated as though he made it conditional on the stated circumstance. This means the case in our *mishnah* is indeed one of *nolad*: the father genuinely was wicked when the vow was made, the conditions truly changed afterward. Rabbi Meir's innovation is simply to read the vow *as if* he had formulated it as a *tenai*, because he articulated the reason explicitly. The Sages decline to read it that way. On Rav Huna's account, the later *mishnah* — which addresses a case of clear *taut* — represents an entirely different scenario and is not in tension with our *mishnah*, since each addresses its own category.

### Rabbi Yochanan: Died Alr…

Rabbi Yochanan, however, reads the case differently: *u-kvar meis* — the father had *already* died before the vow was made. The vow-maker simply did not know it. Under this reading, the case is one of *taut* even in our *mishnah*, not *nolad*, since the facts were wrong from the outset.

This creates an obvious difficulty: if it is a plain *taut*, why would the Sages disagree? A clear factual error should be uncontroversial grounds for release. Tosafot addresses this by offering a reading of Rabbi Meir's position under Rabbi Yochanan's interpretation: Rabbi Meir is not simply saying the case is *taut* and permissible. Rather, Rabbi Meir is responding to a concern that one might apply a *gezeirah* and prohibit even this case, lest people confuse it with a scenario where the father *died after* the vow — which would be a genuine *nolad* and should not serve as grounds for release. Rabbi Meir says: we need not make such a *gezeirah* here, because the difference between the two cases is clear enough that confusion is unlikely. The Sages, by contrast, hold that since the scenarios are superficially similar — in both cases the vow was made because of the father's wickedness and the father is now dead — people *will* confuse them, and we therefore prohibit even the *taut* case to prevent it from being mistaken for *nolad*. This is how Tosafot reconciles Rabbi Yochanan's reading with the existence of a genuine Rabbinic dispute.

### Woman's Beauty Case

The *gemara* then raises a challenge from a later *mishnah*: *konam she-eini nosei le-plonis ke-urah* — a *konam* that I will not marry this woman, who is ugly — and it turns out she was beautiful; or he thought she was dark-skinned and she was fair-skinned; or thought she was short and she was tall. In such cases the *halacha* is *mutar bah* — he may marry her. The *mishnah* explicitly states: *ella she-ha-neder taut* — because the vow was an error.

The *gemara* notes that on Rav Huna's reading, this works out neatly: our *mishnah* addresses cases where the vow-maker treated the articulated reason as a *tenai*, and the later *mishnah* addresses a straightforward case of *taut* — two distinct scenarios requiring two distinct rulings. But on Rabbi Yochanan's reading, since both *mishnayot* address *taut*, the repetition becomes difficult: *lama li le-mitni tartei zimnei neder taut* — why state the rule of erroneous vows twice? The *gemara* marks this as a *kashya* and does not resolve it cleanly.

One observation worth noting: according to Rabbi Yochanan, the *gezeirah* against *nolad* that the Sages invoke would presumably be limited to cases where confusion with a genuine *nolad* scenario is a realistic concern — for instance, where the stated condition could plausibly change over time. The later *mishnah*, by contrast, involves mistaking someone's appearance, which admits of no *nolad* analogue, and would therefore be *divrei ha-kol*. The *gemara* does not explicitly draw this inference, but it follows naturally from the logic of the dispute.

## Petach: Prohibited Acts

### Rabbi Meir's Opening

The *mishnah* continues: *ve-od amar Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi Meir further stated that one may open a *petach* for the release of a vow by invoking the subject's violation of a prohibition written in the *Torah*. The *gemara* cites the rule: *potchin lo min ha-katuv she-ba-Torah* — one opens based on what is written in the *Torah*.

The *beit din* asks the person: had you known that by taking this vow you were transgressing *lo tikom ve-lo titor* — the prohibitions against vengeance and bearing a grudge (Vayikra 19:18) — or the prohibition *ve-ahavta le-rei'acha kamocha*, or *lo tisna et achicha bi-lvavecha*, or *ve-chai achicha imach* — the obligation to sustain one's impoverished fellow Jew — would you still have made this vow? If he answers that he would not, *harei ze mutar* — the vow is released.

This is a contextually significant ruling. The *mishnah* earlier in the tractate had established that one may not use certain universal openings — *kvod ha-makom* or *kvod aviv ve-imo* — because they are so broad that everyone would be able to release their own vows, or because the framing would compel a dishonest answer. Rabbi Meir permits this category, however, because the focus on specific prohibitions that directly relate to the content and motivation of the vow is narrower and more honest. The person taking a vow of spite or revenge genuinely might not have considered that he was thereby violating *lo tikom* or *lo titor*, and the question is therefore a real one that can elicit a sincere response.

### Objection to Opening

One might ask: is it truly plausible to claim ignorance of Torah prohibitions? Can a person genuinely say he would not have taken a vow had he known it constituted a transgression? This is a subtle point. The concern with certain earlier *petachim* was that the framing would essentially guarantee a particular answer, generating dishonest responses and turning the process into a formality. But here, the argument runs, people sometimes act impulsively in moments of anger or spite without consciously connecting their actions to the prohibitions they are violating. The question *ilu hayita yodea she-atah over* — had you known you were transgressing — is therefore genuinely informative and capable of receiving an honest answer.

One might still ask: shouldn't anyone who fears heaven already know he is violating these prohibitions? And if he knows it and did it anyway, is the *petach* still legitimate? The *gemara* leaves the ruling in Rabbi Meir's name without explicitly indicating that the Sages dispute it here, though the attribution to Rabbi Meir alone may imply a dispute. The force of the ruling rests on the recognition that people act in moments of passion without deliberate weighing of halachic consequences, and the question invites them to step back and reflect honestly.

### Gabbayim Objection

Rav Huna bar Avdimi raises a challenge against the use of *ve-chai achicha imach* as a *petach*. He suggests that the person seeking release from his vow might simply say: even granting that this person has become poor, my obligation does not fall to me alone. Whatever falls to me proportionally, along with the rest of the community, I will contribute through the communal *gabbayim*. I do not have a personal, individualized responsibility to support this particular person — let him receive his share from the general charitable distribution.

### Newly Poor Person

The *gemara* responds to this argument: *ani omer kol ha-nofel eino nofel li-dei gabbai techilah* — a person who descends into poverty for the first time does not first turn to the communal *gabbay*. Someone already established in the charitable system may rely on it, but when a person first becomes poor, he turns to his friends and relatives — those who know him personally. In this case, given that there was clearly a prior relationship between the vow-maker and the person against whom the vow was directed, the newly impoverished individual would naturally have turned to him first. If the vow-maker is bound by his *neder* and cannot help him at that critical moment, he would thereby transgress *ve-chai achicha imach*. This closes the objection and restores the legitimacy of using that prohibition as a *petach*.

## Opening: Ketubah

### Ketubah Opening Rule

The *mishnah* now states: *potchin le-adam bi-ktuvatat ishto* — one may open a release from a vow by confronting the vow-maker with the implications of his wife's *ketubah* obligation. When a person's vow leads toward divorce, the *beit din* may ask: had you known what you would be required to pay out under her *ketubah*, would you still have made this vow?

### Man's Vow Case

The *mishnah* then presents a concrete case: a man vowed that his wife could derive no benefit from him — *nadar me-ishto hana'ah*. Her *ketubah* was worth four hundred *dinarim*, an exceptionally large sum.

### Rabbi Akiva's Ruling

He came before Rabbi Akiva, and Rabbi Akiva ruled: *chiyavo liten lah kesuvatah* — you are obligated to pay her full *ketubah*. The man protested: his father had left him only eight hundred *dinarim*, of which his brother took four hundred and he took four hundred. Was it not enough, he asked, that she should receive two hundred and he keep two hundred? Rabbi Akiva was unmoved: *afilu atah mocher se'ar roshecha* — even if you had to sell the hair of your head, *atah noten lah kesuvatah* — you must pay her full *ketubah*.

### Vower Released

The man then said: *ilu hayiti yodea she-hu kein, lo hayiti noder* — had I known this would be the consequence, I would never have made this vow. Rabbi Akiva thereupon released him from his vow. The case is remarkable because the *petach* was not arranged artificially by the *beit din* in anticipation of seeking a release; it emerged organically from the man's direct encounter with the halachic ruling about his *ketubah* obligation, producing a genuinely sincere expression of regret.

### Chattels & Ketubah

The *gemara* raises a technical difficulty with the narrative: *metaltelin mi mishta'abdei li-ktuva?* — are chattels encumbered for the *ketubah*? The standard rule is that a *ketubah*, like a debt, creates a lien only on *karka* — real property — and not on *metaltelin* — movable goods. If so, why did Rabbi Akiva say the man would have to sell the hair of his head? Hair is chattel, not real estate.

The *gemara* therefore suggests that the figure of eight hundred *dinarim* left by the father refers not to liquid cash but to *karka* worth eight hundred *dinarim*. The brother took land worth four hundred and the man retained land worth four hundred — exactly the value of the *ketubah*. This accounts for the *ketubah*'s collection from real property.

But the *gemara* then presses: the *mishnah* explicitly mentions selling the hair of one's head. Does this not imply that chattel must also go toward paying the *ketubah*? The *gemara* works through several approaches, including the general principle that *avadim hukshu le-karkaot* — servants are legally analogous to real property — but this does not directly resolve the question of hair.

### Hair Sale Explained

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty with a clarification offered by Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak: *lomar she-ein mekori'in shetar ketubah* — the purpose of the phrase is to convey that the *ketubah* must be paid in full without reduction, not that the man's hair itself would literally be sold to pay it. The expression *afilu atah mocher se'ar roshecha* means: even if full payment would leave you so impoverished that you would need to sell whatever you had — the hair off your head — just to put food on the table, the obligation to pay her complete *ketubah* nonetheless stands. Rabbi Akiva was not saying that chattel is encumbered for the *ketubah*; he was expressing the absoluteness of the obligation in vivid, hyperbolic terms.

This also addresses a further inference the *gemara* considered: *shema mina ein mesadrin le-ba'al chov* — perhaps Rabbi Akiva's ruling implies that we do not provide the debtor with an installment arrangement, allowing him to retain enough to sustain himself while paying off the debt gradually. This principle, known as *mesadrin le-ba'al chov*, generally governs ordinary creditors. But here too, Rabbi Akiva's language is not establishing a rule about installments; he is emphasizing that the *ketubah* amount itself cannot be negotiated downward. The man's impoverishment after full payment is his own consequence to bear; what cannot happen is a reduction of the *ketubah* amount that she is owed.
