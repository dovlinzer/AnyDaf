# Nedarim 58 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Core Negation Question

### Forbidden Growth Setup

Today's *daf* is 58, and we pick up at the bottom of 57b, in the middle of a discussion that will carry through to the end of the tractate. The question the *gemara* is grappling with is this: if something is forbidden and continues to grow — a plant or produce that carries a certain *halachic* status — can the new growth negate the forbidden core?

The cases are varied. An onion that is *kilayim*, or a fruit that is *orlah*, or produce that carries the status of *terumah* or *shemittah* — in each of these situations, one can imagine a scenario in which the original forbidden core continues to grow, but the new growth itself does not share that forbidden status. Perhaps the vineyard was uprooted, so the onion is no longer growing in a *kilayim* context. Perhaps it is now the eighth year, and the onion continues to grow after *shemittah* has ended. Perhaps seeds of *terumah* were planted, and the produce that sprouts is not itself *terumah*. In all of these scenarios, the question is the same: can the new growth — which is *hetter* — apply the principles of *bittul* and nullify the original forbidden core?

The *gemara* never explicitly articulates the reason one might say no, but several possibilities present themselves. One is that because the new growth derives entirely from the forbidden source, it is not sufficiently distinct or neutral to carry the nullifying power of *bittul*. The argument would run: the whole thing is simply a continued growth of the core — it is always defined by the core, and how can what is merely an extension of the core turn around and declare the core nonexistent?

### Ran's Framework

The *Ran* takes this one step further and proposes an alternative framing entirely. Perhaps the question is not really about *bittul* at all. Perhaps what grows from a forbidden source simply takes on the status of that source. If the original was *terumah*, what grows from it is *terumah*. If the original was *orlah*, what grows from it is *orlah*. In that case, the question is not whether the new growth can nullify the core — it is a question about what *halachic* status attaches to the new growth in the first place.

The *gemara*, however, seems to be asking a *bittul* question: can the new growth have the power to negate the core? That framing shapes everything that follows.

### Amoraic Positions

The *gemara* has already established that this is an *amoraic* dispute. *Rabbi Yitzchak* held that the new growth can negate the core — that the permissible growth can apply *bittul* and render the mixture permitted. *Rabbi Yochanan* and *Rabbi Yonasan*, by contrast, held that it cannot — the core remains forbidden regardless of how much grows around it.

## Davar Sheyesh Matirin

### Baraita Examples

We now pick up at the bottom of 57b, where the *gemara* introduces a *baraita* that will anchor the discussion. *Rabbi Shimon* states: *kol davar sheyesh lo matirin* — anything that has a path to permissibility — is never subject to *bittul*, regardless of the quantities involved. What does he mean by a path to permissibility?

He gives four examples. First, *tevel* — grain from which *terumos u'maasros* have not yet been separated. It is forbidden, but it will become permitted the moment those separations are made. Second, *maaser sheni* — the second tithe, which carries a special status requiring it to be eaten in *Yerushalayim*, but which can very naturally be redeemed onto money and the money then brought to *Yerushalayim*. Third, *hekdesh* — property consecrated to the Temple treasury. This does not refer, according to most *rishonim*, to an animal designated as a *korban*, but rather to ordinary property — one's possessions donated to the Temple. The Temple treasury has no use for the object itself; it will sell it, and the *kedusha* will transfer to the proceeds. Thus the consecration will naturally be lifted in the ordinary course of things. Fourth, *chadash* — the new grain that grows before *Pesach*, which is forbidden but becomes permitted once the *omer* is brought on the second day of *Pesach*. All four of these things are currently forbidden, but all four have a natural or common path to becoming permitted.

### Why No Bittul

Regarding all of these, *Rabbi Shimon* rules that *lo nasnu bahem chachamim shiur* — the rabbis did not establish a quantity threshold. These things can never be nullified in a mixture. Even the tiniest amount renders the entire mixture problematic.

The general logic is compelling: *bittul* is a mechanism for situations of true unavoidability — the forbidden item fell in, and discarding the entire mixture would impose an unacceptable loss. But here there is another option. You can simply wait until the second day of *Pesach*. You can redeem the *maaser sheni*. You can separate *terumos u'maasros* from other grain and thereby fix the status of the *tevel*. *Bittul* is a last resort; when a preferable route exists, the rabbis declined to make that last resort available. This is the principle of *davar sheyesh lo matirin*: *afilu b'elef lo batel* — even one in a thousand, even the most infinitesimal presence, is not nullified.

It is worth noting that while *Rabbi Shimon* presents this principle here as a point of dispute, it ultimately becomes accepted as the undisputed *halachic* ruling.

One might observe something philosophically striking about this principle. An item whose status can actually change through some formal process turns out to be, in a sense, more fixed and permanent than an item whose identity is simply forbidden with no exit. Counterintuitively, the more flexible item — the one that *can* become permitted — is less susceptible to dissolution through *bittul*. Its very capacity for transformation through a legitimate channel closes off the back door of nullification.

### No Path to Permissibility

*Rabbi Shimon* continues: *v'chol davar she'ein lo matirin* — anything that has no path to permissibility. Here he lists *terumah*, *trumas maaser*, *challah*, *orlah*, and *kilay hakerem*. None of these has any mechanism by which it ceases to be what it is. *Terumah* will always be *terumah*; *orlah* will always be *orlah* from those trees; *kilayim* will always retain its status. In these cases, the rabbis *did* establish a quantity threshold — and it is worth noting that for agricultural forbidden items, the threshold is higher than the standard sixty-to-one ratio. For *terumah* the ratio is one hundred to one; for *orlah* and *kilayim* it is two hundred to one. Nevertheless, once the mixture crosses that threshold, the forbidden item is *batel*.

A brief technical footnote: all of the above applies when the mixture is *min b'mino* — the same species mixed with the same species, apples with apples, grain with grain. If the forbidden item falls into something of a different species — *eino mino* — then even a *davar sheyesh lo matirin* would be *batel* at the standard sixty-to-one ratio. But all of *Rabbi Shimon*'s analysis pertains to the case of like mixed with like.

### Shevi'it Challenge

The rabbis respond to *Rabbi Shimon*: *v'halo shevi'it ein la matirin* — *shevi'it* has no path to permissibility. Once produce carries the status of the seventh year, that status never goes away. You must eat it in accordance with its sanctity; you cannot treat it casually or dispose of it; and once the time of *bi'ur* arrives — when that species of produce is no longer available in the fields for animals to eat — you must actually destroy it. There is no mechanism by which *shevi'it* produce simply loses its status. And yet, says the *gemara*, *velo nasnu bah chachamim shiur* — even *shevi'it* is not subject to *bittul*. The *mishnah* in *shevi'it* states: *hashevi'it oseret kol shehu beminah* — produce with *shevi'it* status, when mixed with its own kind, renders the entire mixture forbidden even in the most minute quantity.

So this seems to disprove half of *Rabbi Shimon*'s principle. He said that things with no path to permissibility *are* subject to *bittul* — yet here is *shevi'it*, which has no path to permissibility, and it is nevertheless not *batel*. At most, the rabbis have only disproven one half of his statement — the half that said *she'ein lo matirin* is always *batel*. They have not touched his claim that *sheyesh lo matirin* is never *batel*. But they have shown that his framework does not map neatly onto all cases.

### Shimon's Response

*Rabbi Shimon* replies: *af ani lo amarti ela levi'ur* — even I would only say that *shevi'it* is not *batel* with respect to the *halacha* of *bi'ur*. But *aval l'achilah* — regarding the obligation of eating it — the standard rule of *nosein ta'am* would apply.

This requires explanation. *Shevi'it* produce carries two distinct *halachic* dimensions. During the period while the produce is still available in the fields and animals can still eat from it, there is an obligation to eat the produce and handle it with *kedushat shevi'it* — one cannot simply discard it or destroy it. After that period ends and the species is no longer available in the fields, the time of *bi'ur* arrives, and it becomes obligatory to destroy the produce rather than eat it.

The difficulty is that neither of these statuses seems to constitute *yesh lo matirin* in any obvious sense. Neither one provides a mechanism by which the *shevi'it* status simply dissolves.

### Rishonim Reframe

This is where the *rishonim* significantly reinterpret what *Rabbi Shimon* is saying. The *Ran* reads the passage as follows. *Rabbi Shimon*'s answer is that the rabbis who challenged him were only correct with respect to *bi'ur* — that is, regarding the *halacha* of eating the produce before *bi'ur* arrives. During that period, even though the produce has *shevi'it* status and you cannot simply flush it away, you *can* eat it. It is, in a sense, workable — you can operate within its parameters. And that, says the *Ran*, constitutes a kind of *yesh lo matirin*: not that the status will disappear, but that there is a permissible way to engage with the item. That is why, during the eating period, *bittul* does not apply — since just as with classic *yesh lo matirin*, you should simply eat it in the proper manner rather than dissolve it into a mixture.

After the time of *bi'ur* arrives, however, when one is forbidden to eat the produce and obligated to destroy it, that is a genuine *ein lo matirin* situation — there is no permissible path. In that case, *Rabbi Shimon* would actually agree that *bittul* could apply, and the standard rule of *nosein ta'am* would govern.

This is a notably flexible deployment of the concept of *davar sheyesh lo matirin*. Elsewhere one can find a similar approach: the *Rif* in a complicated passage discusses bread baked in an oven with meat, and invokes the category of *davar sheyesh lo matirin* — not in the sense that the bread will stop being *fleishig*, but in the sense that one can perfectly well eat it with *fleishig* food. The principle is being applied wherever there exists a workable path within the item's status, not only where the status itself will eventually dissolve.

Returning to the text: *amru lo: velo shevi'it ein lo matirin, detnan: hashevi'it oseret kol shehu beminah. Amar lei: af ani lo amarti ela levi'ur* — which the *rishonim* understand to mean: I would only say it is not *batel* with respect to the period in which you must eat it, because there you can simply eat it (*yesh lo matirin*). *Aval achilah benosen ta'am* — but after *bi'ur*, when eating is forbidden and destruction is required, in that case it follows the standard rule of *nosein ta'am* and would be *batel*.

The *Ran* makes explicit what the words seem to invert: *af heim lo amru she'hashevi'it oseret b'chol shehu b'minah ella l'inyan she'tzarich le'echol hata'aroves kodem habi'ur* — they only said that *shevi'it* is not *batel* regarding the requirement to eat the mixture before *bi'ur*. *V'keivan she'efshar le'ochla, havei lei k'davar sheyesh lo matirin* — since one can eat it, it functions like *yesh lo matirin*, and therefore *bittul* does not apply.

To sharpen the logic: *bittul* functions by declaring the forbidden component nonexistent, effectively eliminating the need to comply with its *halachic* demands. But if the item can simply be eaten — if compliance is easy and available — then *bittul* does not dissolve the status; instead, the mixture must be treated with *kedushat shevi'it*, handled properly, and eaten before *bi'ur*. It cannot be discarded as if the *shevi'it* component were not there.

With this, the *gemara*'s engagement with *Rabbi Shimon*'s principle is complete, and we turn to the central question of whether new growth can negate a forbidden core.

## Onion Growth Case

### Onion Color Test

The *gemara* now turns to a *mishnah* to test the principle. *Betzalim sheyardu aleihem geshamim* — onions upon which rain fell. The scenario is as follows: onions that were growing in the sixth year continue into the seventh year. The question is whether any significant growth occurred during *shemittah* or whether the onion essentially completed its growth in the sixth year. *Im hayu alim shelahem shchorim, assurim* — if the leaves were dark or black-colored, the onion is forbidden, indicating that enough growth occurred during *shemittah* to give it *shevi'it* status. *Horiku, mutarim* — if they turned bright green, the onion is permitted, indicating that the growth had essentially concluded in the sixth year, or that any additional growth was driven by rainfall rather than by the *shemittah* ground.

The precise mechanism by which leaf color indicates the nature of growth is not fully transparent, and the *rishonim* offer various readings. The *mishnah* also works in the reverse direction — from the seventh year into the eighth — and the same indicators apply with opposite consequences: what was permitted in the sixth-to-seventh scenario is analogous to what would be forbidden in the seventh-to-eighth scenario.

### Leaf Strength Test

*Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos* offers an alternative diagnostic criterion: *im yecholim lehitaresh ba'alim shelahem, assurim* — if the leaves are strong enough that one could pull the onion up by them, the onion is forbidden, as this demonstrates that significant growth occurred during *shemittah* and that the leaves are robustly connected in a way that reflects continued growth during the seventh year.

### Growth Negates Core

The *gemara* draws an inference from the *mishnah*. In the case of an onion growing from the seventh year into the eighth: if the color of the leaves or their strength indicates that growth continued into the eighth year, the onion becomes *permitted*. But wait — the core of the onion grew during *shemittah*. How is it permitted? Does this not show that *gidulei heter ma'alin et ha'issur* — permissible new growth negates the forbidden core? This would appear to vindicate *Rabbi Yitzchak*'s lenient position and contradict *Rabbi Yochanan* and *Rabbi Yonasan*.

### Ground-Up Objection

The *gemara* deflects: *vedilma bimduchanim* — perhaps the case of the *mishnah* involves onions that have been ground up. In other words, perhaps once the onion is processed into a ground form, the usual logic of *bittul* applies by virtue of the mixture's processed state, and this is not a case where the new growth in its whole form is negating the original whole core. It is not immediately obvious why being ground up changes the equation, but the *gemara*'s suggestion seems to be that when the onion retains its original form — one continuous object, growing from the very core that was forbidden — it is difficult to say that the same onion that was forbidden is now permitted simply because it is larger. But if it has been ground up and turned into a processed mixture, then ordinary *bittul* ratios can apply without the conceptual difficulty of claiming that the same object has negated itself.

## Samaritan Weeding

### Field Worker Scenario

The *gemara* tries again from a *baraita*: *hamnakeish im hakuti bechasyos* — someone who is weeding together with a Samaritan among leafy vegetables. The underlying assumption is that the Samaritan does not observe the laws of *terumos u'maasros*, and therefore the produce he planted likely did not have those separations taken from it. Nevertheless, workers are permitted under certain conditions to snack on the produce they are working with — *achilat arai*. The ruling is that one may eat in that manner, but if one receives a portion of the vegetables as wages, those must be treated as definite *tevel* and separated accordingly, since we have no reason to assume the Samaritan separated.

### Shimon ben Elazar

*Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* introduces the case directly relevant to our question. *Im Yisrael chashud al hashevi'it* — if instead of a Samaritan, one is working with a Jew who is suspect of not observing *shemittah* properly, such that the seeds or bulbs he planted may have been *shemittah* produce — *lemotzei shevi'it mutar* — once *shemittah* is over and it is the eighth year, one may still eat freely from the vegetables growing in his field.

### Permits Mixture

The *gemara* draws the inference: *lemimra degidulei heter ma'alin et ha'issur* — this seems to demonstrate conclusively that permissible new growth negates the forbidden core. Here you are, eating from onions in the eighth year, knowing that the core of those onions may have been *shemittah* produce. And yet it is permitted. The new growth in the eighth year appears to negate the original *shemittah* core.

### Wheat Objection

The *gemara* attempts a first deflection: *vedilma bedavar shezaro kalah* — perhaps we are not dealing with onions or similar vegetables at all. Perhaps the *baraita* concerns wheat or another grain in which the original seed disintegrates into the ground. If so, there is no surviving "core" to speak of — what grows is entirely new, and there is no issue of the new growth negating a present forbidden core, because the forbidden core has long dissolved and is gone. The question of *gidulei heter ma'alin et ha'issur* only arises when the original core remains — something like an onion, where the bulb is still there and continues to grow. In the case of wheat, the original kernel is gone, and what grows is simply new produce.

### Vegetables Confirmation

The *gemara* rejects this evasion: *ha tanya — elu hein chasayos: kegon haluf vehashum vehabetzalim* — the *baraita* itself defines what *chasayos* are: the *luf* (some type of onion-family vegetable, possibly a leek), garlic, and onions. These are all vegetables that do not dissolve when planted; the original bulb remains and continues to grow. So we are definitively in the category of *ayin zaro kalah* — the seed does not disintegrate — and the original core is present. The *baraita*'s permissive ruling cannot be explained away by appealing to the disintegration of the original.

### Ground Again

The *gemara* tries the earlier deflection: *vedilma bimduchanim* — perhaps the scenario involves ground-up, processed vegetables rather than whole ones, and that is why *bittul* applies.

### Direct Weeding

The *gemara* refutes this as well: *hamnakeish katani* — the *baraita* explicitly describes someone who is *weeding* in the field. He is not eating from processed or ground vegetables; he is eating directly from vegetables growing in the ground as he works. There is no way to read a scenario of processed food into a description of field weeding. The *baraita* thus seems to present an unambiguous case: one eats from whole vegetables growing in the field, the core of which may have been *shemittah* produce, and it is nevertheless permitted. This appears to decisively demonstrate that the new growth negates the core.

## Shemittah Unique

### Shanya Shemittah

Having apparently established a clear proof that *gidulei heter ma'alin et ha'issur*, the *gemara* turns to *Rabbi Yitzchak* for the resolution: *shanya shevi'it ho'il v'issurah al yedei karka bitulah nami al yedei karka* — *shemittah* is different. Since its prohibition operates by virtue of the land, its nullification likewise operates by virtue of the land. This is the *gemara*'s decisive answer. What grows from *shemittah* produce in the eighth year can negate the *shemittah* core — but only because *shemittah* is a unique category. This does not establish a general principle that new growth negates a forbidden core in other domains.

### Year-Based Logic

What does it mean that *shemittah*'s prohibition operates through the land? The insight is that *shemittah* asks a fundamentally different question than other forbidden categories. When one encounters an *orlah* fruit, the question is: is this object forbidden? The focus is on the fruit as an object — it carries the *orlah* status and that is what defines it. When one encounters an onion that may or may not have grown during *shemittah*, the question one naturally asks is: *which year is this from?* It is a year-based, land-based question. Did this grow from the land of the seventh year or the land of the eighth year?

Because the question is framed that way, one does not necessarily subdivide the onion itself and ask: how many grams grew in year seven versus how many grams grew in year eight, and does the latter quantity cross the threshold needed to nullify the former? That would be a standard *bittul* analysis applied to components of a single object. Instead, since *shemittah* is defined by which year the land belongs to, one can make a global assessment: if the majority of this onion grew in the eighth year, it is an eighth-year onion, and that single designation applies to the whole. The year-based framing allows for a holistic determination of status in a way that other *issurim* do not.

By contrast, an *orlah* fruit cannot be characterized holistically as "belonging to" a permitted year simply because more of it grew after the three-year *orlah* period. The analysis there focuses on the object and its components, not on a year-based label.

### Tosafos Active/Passive

*Tosafos* offers a different approach to understanding *shanya shevi'it*. Looking back at the cases cited by *Rabbi Yochanan* and *Rabbi Yonasan* — grafting a branch from an *orlah* tree onto a non-*orlah* tree, and uprooting the vineyard in which a *kilayim* onion was growing — *Tosafos* observes that in both of those cases, a person actively intervened to create the *hetter*. The *orlah* status was dissolved by the act of grafting; the *kilayim* status was dissolved by the act of uprooting the vineyard. This active human intervention, says *Tosafos*, is problematic: it resembles *mevatlin issur l'chatchila* — the prohibition against deliberately arranging for a forbidden item to be nullified. Since the person went out and actively did something to convert the *issur* into *hetter*, we do not credit the resulting *bittul*.

In the *shemittah* case, by contrast, nothing was done. The onion simply continued to grow naturally after *shemittah* ended. There was no deliberate manipulation to engineer permissibility. Therefore, the natural growth in the eighth year is credited and can define the onion's status.

This reading is not the straightforward *pshat* of the *gemara*, which grounds the distinction in the land-focused nature of *shemittah* rather than in the active-versus-passive distinction. But *Tosafos*'s reading is worth noting as an alternative conceptual framework.

## Maaser Countercase

### Maaser Parallel

Having established that *shemittah* is exceptional because its prohibition focuses on the land, the *gemara* immediately raises a challenge: *harei maaser de'issura al yedei karka* — *maaser* is also a prohibition that arises from the land. Grain that grows from the land becomes obligated in *maaser* by virtue of having grown from that land. If the land-focus of *shemittah* is what allows the new growth to negate the core, then by the same logic, new growth of *maaser* grain should negate the original untithed core. But the *gemara* is about to show that it does not.

### Grain Growth Case

A *baraita* is cited: *litra maaser tevel* — a measure of *maaser* grain that is *tevel*. How does one have *maaser* grain that is *tevel*? When a *levi* receives his *maaser* from the *Yisrael*, the *levi* must in turn separate *trumas maaser* — a tenth of that portion — and give it to a *kohen*. If he fails to do so, his *maaser* grain retains the status of *tevel*: it is forbidden to eat. If this *levi* takes his untithed *maaser* grain — or onion bulbs, or whatever the produce may be — and plants them in the ground before separating *trumas maaser*, and they then grow tenfold, we have a case with a clear forbidden core surrounded by substantial new growth.

### Original Remains

The ruling of the *baraita* is: *chayaves bemaaser uvishvi'it* — the new produce is fully obligated in *maaser*, and if the year is a *shemittah* year it carries *shevi'it* status, just like any produce that grows that year. So far this is unremarkable. But then: *ve'osah litra* — what of the original single unit that was *tevel*? It has not been negated. One must go and separate *trumas maaser* for that original portion from other, unambiguous *maaser* grain elsewhere. One cannot take the *trumas maaser* from the mixture itself — everything is commingled and indistinguishable — so one separates from a separate known source on behalf of that original unit. The original unit retains its status and requires independent rectification.

This is striking in light of what was just demonstrated about *shemittah*. There we said the new growth negates the core. Here we are saying it does not. And *maaser* grain also grows from the land.

### Maaser Digun

The *gemara* resolves this: *maaser digun hu dekagrim lah* — the defining moment of *maaser* obligation is not the land itself but the *digun*, the processing. Grain becomes fully obligated in *terumos u'maasros* only after it has been threshed, winnowed, piled, and the pile smoothed over. That final act of processing is the trigger. Therefore, even though *maaser* grain grows from the land, the essential focus of *maaser* is not "which land did this come from" or "which year did this grow from" — it is "has this grain been processed in the manner that triggers the obligation?" Because the obligation is ultimately keyed to human processing rather than to the identity of the land or the year, the analysis of the *maaser* grain focuses on the grain as an object with its own processing history. And when the analysis focuses on the object rather than on the land-year question, the result is the same as with *terumah* or *orlah*: there is an original component with a problematic status, and new growth around it does not negate that original core.

## General Rule

### Synthesis

Pulling back from the details, the *gemara*'s conclusion can be stated as follows: the general rule is that new growth does not negate the original forbidden core. *Shemittah* is an exception to this general rule.

### Why Shemittah Works

*Shemittah* is exceptional because it poses a global, land-based, year-based question. When determining whether produce is *shevi'it*, one asks: which year is this from? If the majority grew in the eighth year, the holistic answer is that it is an eighth-year product. The framework is not about the components of the object and their individual *halachic* histories, but about a single characterization of the produce as a whole.

### Why Others Fail

All other categories — *terumah*, *orlah*, *kilayim*, and even *maaser* despite its connection to the land — ultimately require analysis that focuses on the object itself and on human processing components. For *maaser*, the trigger is *digun*, not the land. For *terumah* and *orlah* and *kilayim*, the question is whether a particular component carries a particular status. In none of these cases can one simply step back and ask "which category does this whole thing belong to?" — because the analysis invariably requires accounting for the original core as a distinct component. Therefore, in all of these cases, the original core is never negated by new growth, and one must always address it independently.
