# Niddah 46 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishna Redundancy

### Challenge & Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ' וְכֵיוָן דִּתְנָא ״בַּת אַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה וְיוֹם אֶחָד נְדָרֶיהָ נִבְדָּקִין״, ״בַּת שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה וְיוֹם אֶחָד נְדָרֶיהָ קַיָּימִין״ לְמָה לִי? סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: בּוֹדְקִין לְעוֹלָם, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **But since** the mishna **teaches:** With regard to a girl who is **eleven years and one day old her vows are examined, why do I** need the mishna to further state: Once she is **twelve years and one day old her vows are in effect?** After all, by this stage she is already an adult. The Gemara answers that this ruling is necessary, as it might **enter your mind to say** that **one examines** her vows **forever,** even when she is an adult. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that the vows of an adult are valid even without examination.

The Gemara on 46b opens by scrutinizing what appears to be a redundancy in the Mishna. The Mishna established two parallel rules: that from eleven years and a day — that is, throughout the twelfth year — a girl's vows are subject to examination (*bedikah*), and that from twelve years and a day her vows are fully binding without examination. The Gemara challenges: since we have already been told that at twelve years and a day the vows are unqualifiedly valid, why bother stating this at all? The answer is *salka da'atach amina* — one might have supposed that even after she becomes an adult, at twelve years and a day, we continue to check whether she understands the nature of her vow. The Mishna therefore teaches that once she has crossed the threshold of adulthood, we assume competence and she cannot even claim ignorance retroactively.

### Checking Whole Year

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵיוָן דְּתָנֵי בַּת ״שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה וְיוֹם אֶחָד נְדָרֶיהָ קַיָּימִין״, ״בּוֹדְקִין כׇּל שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה״ לְמָה לִי? סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וְאָמַר מָר ״שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם בַּשָּׁנָה חֲשׁוּבִים שָׁנָה״, הֵיכָא דִּבְדַקְנָא שְׁלֹשִׁים וְלֹא יָדְעָה לְהַפְלוֹת, אֵימָא: תּוּ לָא לִיבְדּוֹק, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **And since** the mishna **teaches:** Once she is **twelve years and one day old her vows are in effect, why do I** need it to further state: **One examines** her vows throughout **the entire twelfth** year? The Gemara answers that this ruling is necessary, as it might **enter your mind to say: Since the Master says** that **thirty days in a year are considered** equivalent to **a year,** in a case **where we examine her** for **thirty** days after she turned eleven **and she did not know** how **to utter** a vow **properly,** i.e., she did not have a clear understanding of the meaning of the vow, one might **say** that **one should examine** her **no further** until she reaches the age of twelve. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that she is examined throughout her twelfth year.

Having established why both the eleven-and-a-day and twelve-and-a-day boundaries are necessary, the Gemara raises a further difficulty. Since we now know that at twelve and a day the vow is binding regardless, and we knew already from the eleven-and-a-day rule that the twelfth year involves examination — why does the Mishna separately teach that one checks throughout the entire twelfth year? Surely that follows from the combination of the two endpoints.

The Gemara answers with *salka da'atach amina*: one might have thought that the principle *lamed yom b'shana chashuv shana* — thirty days in a year counts as a year, a rule associated with Rabbi Meir — could apply here as well. One might therefore have supposed that if a girl made vows during the first thirty days of her twelfth year and demonstrated ignorance at that time, that ignorance would define her status for the remainder of the year, disqualifying her vows for the entire period. The Mishna therefore teaches explicitly that she is entitled to be examined at any point throughout her twelfth year, regardless of earlier ignorance. At whatever moment she demonstrates understanding, her vow is valid.

### Both Boundaries

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִתְנֵי הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי בָּבֵי: ״בַּת שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה וְיוֹם אֶחָד נְדָרֶיהָ קַיָּימִין, וּבוֹדְקִין כׇּל שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה״. ״בַּת אַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה וְיוֹם אֶחָד נְדָרֶיהָ נִבְדָּקִין״ לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let** the mishna **teach** only **these two clauses:** Once she is **twelve years and one day old her vows are in effect, and one examines** her vows throughout **the entire twelfth** year. Once both of these have been taught, **why do I** need the ruling: With regard to a girl who is **eleven years and one day old, her vows are examined?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: סְתָמָא — בִּשְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה בָּעֲיָא בְּדִיקָה, בְּאַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה לָא בָּעֲיָא בְּדִיקָה, וְהֵיכָא דְּחָזֵינַן לַהּ דַּחֲרִיפָא טְפֵי — (מִיבַּדְקָה) [לִיבְדְּקַהּ] בְּאַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that this clause **was necessary,** as it might **enter your mind to say:** In **an ordinary** case, a girl **requires examination in** her **twelfth** year, whereas **in** her **eleventh** year she **does not require examination. But** in a case **where we discern about her that she** has **a very sharp** mind, perhaps **she should be examined** already **in** her **eleventh** year. Therefore, the *tanna* **teaches us** that she is not examined in her eleventh year irrespective of how intelligent she is, as she is too young.

The Gemara then asks: given that we now know the twelfth year is entirely a period of examination, and given that we know the terminal point is twelve and a day, why does the Mishna need to state the starting point — eleven years and a day — separately? The answer is again *salka da'atach amina*: without the explicit starting point, one might have thought that in the case of an unusually precocious girl, checking could begin even earlier, during the eleventh year, from ten years and a day onward. The Mishna therefore teaches that the starting point is inviolable — no examination is meaningful before eleven years and a day, no matter how sharp the girl appears to be.

### Further Redundancy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קוֹדֶם הַזְּמַן הַזֶּה וְאַחַר הַזְּמַן הַזֶּה, לְמָה לִי? סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּלָא קָאָמְרִי אִינְהוּ, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּקָאָמְרִי אִינְהוּ — נִסְמוֹךְ עֲלַיְיהוּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need the mishna to teach that **prior to that time** their vows and consecration are always not valid **and after that time** they are always valid? These *halakhot* can be inferred from the previous statements of the mishna. The Gemara answers that these rulings are necessary, as it might **enter your mind to say: These matters** apply only in a case **where they do not say:** We know in Whose name we vowed, when they are younger than the periods mentioned in the mishna, or: We do not know in Whose name we vowed, when they are older. **But** in a case **where they do say** such statements, perhaps **we rely on their** claim. Therefore, the *tanna* **teaches us** that when they are younger than the periods stated in the mishna their vows are never valid, and when they are older, their vows are always valid.

With all three components now justified — the starting point, the ending point, and the checking throughout the year — the Gemara asks yet again: once those three rules are established, why does the Mishna go on to restate explicitly that before eleven and a day nothing is valid, and after twelve and a day checking is unnecessary? These consequences seem logically implied.

### Contrary Claims

The Gemara answers that the explicit restatement is necessary to forestall the argument that these rules apply only in the default case, where no one is affirmatively claiming otherwise. One might have thought that if a girl under eleven years and a day claimed she knew precisely what she was doing, or if a girl over twelve and a day claimed she had no idea, those explicit statements could shift the *halacha*. The Mishna therefore teaches that these boundaries are categorical and not subject to contrary claims: below eleven and a day, nothing is valid regardless of claimed competence; above twelve and a day, everything is valid regardless of claimed ignorance; and within the intermediate period, the test governs. While the Gemara itself acknowledges that some degree of redundancy remains, it demonstrates that every formulation in the Mishna serves to foreclose a possible misreading.

## Age Differences

### Reversed Ages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵלּוּ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: דְּבָרִים הָאֲמוּרִים בְּתִינוֹקֶת — בְּתִינוֹק אֲמוּרִים, דְּבָרִים הָאֲמוּרִים בְּתִינוֹק — בְּתִינוֹקֶת אֲמוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna indicates that the intellectual development of a girl is faster than that of a boy. In this regard, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **This** opinion, with regard to the periods of vows for girls and boys, is in accordance with **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. But **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says** the opposite, that the **matter stated** here **with regard to a girl is** actually **stated with regard to a boy,** whereas the **matter stated with regard to a boy is** in fact **stated with regard to a girl,** as the intellectual development of males is faster than that of females.

A *baraita* is now introduced: "these are the words of Rabbi" — that a girl reaches majority at twelve and a boy at thirteen, which is, of course, how we rule. But then comes a position that would strike anyone who had not studied the Gemara as completely bizarre. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: what you say about a girl applies to a boy — a boy reaches majority at twelve. And what you say about a boy applies to a girl — a girl reaches majority at thirteen. The ages are entirely reversed.

### Rabbi's Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי? דִּכְתִיב ״וַיִּבֶן ה׳ [אֱלֹהִים] אֶת הַצֵּלָע״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנָּתַן הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא בִּינָה יְתֵירָה בָּאִשָּׁה יוֹתֵר מִבָּאִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: What is the reason of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi? **As it is written,** with regard to the creation of woman: **"And the rib,** which **the Lord God** had taken from the man, **He made [*vayyiven*]** a woman, and brought her to the man" (Genesis 2:22). This **teaches that the Holy One, Blessed be He, granted a woman a greater understanding [*bina*] than** that **of a men.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ, הַהוּא מִבָּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן מְנַסְיָא: ״וַיִּבֶן ה׳ [אֱלֹהִים] אֶת הַצֵּלָע אֲשֶׁר לָקַח מִן הָאָדָם לְאִשָּׁה וַיְבִאֶהָ אֶל הָאָדָם״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁקִּלְּעָהּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְחַוָּה וֶהֱבִיאָהּ אֵצֶל אָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן, שֶׁכֵּן בִּכְרַכֵּי הַיָּם קוֹרִין לְקַלָּעִיתָא — בַּנָּיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what does **the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, derive from this verse? The Gemara answers: **He requires that** verse **for that which Reish Lakish** taught, **as Reish Lakish said in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya** with regard to the verse: **"And the rib, which the Lord God had taken from the man, He made a woman, and brought her to the man."** This **teaches that the Holy One, Blessed be He, braided** the hair of **Eve, and** then **brought her to Adam the first** man. **As in the cities overseas [*bikhrakei hayyam*] they call braiding** hair, **building [*benayita*].**

The Gemara asks: what is Rabbi's reason for the standard ruling? Rav Hisda explains based on a verse. The Torah says *vayiven Hashem Elokim et hatzela* — God "built" the side taken from Adam into a woman. The word *vayiven* shares its root with *binah*, understanding. The Gemara derives from this that God granted women a superior or more precocious form of *binah* than men. The implication here is not merely a general statement about intellectual capacity or feminine intuition, though that reading is sometimes given, but specifically that women's minds mature earlier. This earlier mental maturity, combined with earlier physical maturity, is why girls reach the age of majority a full year before boys.

One might ask what Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar does with this verse if he cannot use it for this derivation. The Gemara explains that he reserves the verse for a teaching of Reish Lakish, cited in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya: *vayiven* — from the same root as *binyata*, the word used in certain Mediterranean locales (*charkei hayam*) for braided hair — teaches that God braided Chava's hair and brought her to Adam. The verse is thus understood as describing the divine preparation of the bride, with particular emphasis on *hachnasat kallah*, the honoring of the bride before the wedding. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar therefore has no use for the *binah* derivation and cannot apply it to establish the earlier majority age for women.

### Education Factor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר מַאי טַעְמֵיהּ? אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁהַתִּינוֹק מָצוּי בְּבֵית רַבּוֹ, נִכְנֶסֶת בּוֹ עַרְמוּמִית תְּחִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, what is the reason** that he maintains that the intellectual development of males is faster than that of females? **Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak says: Since a boy frequents his teacher's house, cleverness enters his** mind **first.**

What then is Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's reason for his reversed position? Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak explains: *mitoch she-tinok matzui be-veit rabo, nichneset bo armumit techilah* — because a boy is found regularly in his teacher's house, a certain worldly shrewdness (*armumit*) enters him first. This is a striking observation. It is not a claim about innate intellectual superiority of boys. Quite the contrary — it is an explicit acknowledgment that the difference is attributable to a societal reality: boys received formal education while girls generally did not, and therefore boys developed a certain kind of practical acuity at an earlier age.

### Peer Association

Tosafot adds an important clarification. Even if a particular boy does not himself have a teacher, he regularly associates with schoolmates who do — *ragil lehitchaber ulavo* — and through that association he too develops more quickly. The implication is that the educational advantage is transmitted not only through direct instruction but through the peer environment itself. We do not rule like Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, of course. But what is particularly striking is the acknowledgment, embedded within a halachic discussion, that observed differences in intellectual development between boys and girls may reflect differences in societal opportunity rather than innate capacity.

## Pre-Mitzvah Status

### Question Framed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: תּוֹךְ זְמַן — כְּלִפְנֵי זְמַן, אוֹ כִּלְאַחַר זְמַן?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that there are three periods in the development of girls and boys: When their vows are examined, i.e., the twelfth year for a girl and the thirteenth year for a boy, which will be termed below: During the time; the period beforehand, when their vows are entirely invalid, called: Before the time; and after that period, when their vows are always valid, known as: After the time. But the mishna does not address the issue of their physical development during these periods, with regard to the appearance of two pubic hairs. In this regard, **a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If a boy or girl developed pubic hairs **during** the **time,** is this year considered **like** the development of signs indicating puberty **before** the **time** that the child reaches majority, and therefore they are not treated as signs indicating puberty, **or** is it considered **as after** the **time?**

The Gemara now raises a broader question: *tosefet zman ki-fene zman o ke-le-achar zman* — does the year leading up to *bar* or *bat mitzvah* count as belonging to the period before, or to the period after? That is, does this transitional year carry the legal status of childhood or of adulthood? The question seems almost paradoxical on its face — surely the whole significance of crossing the *bar mitzvah* threshold is that you become an adult only upon reaching it, not before. Yet given everything the Gemara has said about this year being a distinctive period, it presses the question seriously.

### Vows: In-Between

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אִי לִנְדָרִים — לָאו כְּלִפְנֵי זְמַן דָּמְיָא, וְלָאו כִּלְאַחַר זְמַן דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what *halakha*** is this dilemma raised? **If** it is **with regard to vows,** the development of pubic hairs **is not considered as before** the **time, but** it **is not considered as after** the **time** either. Instead, the status of the vow is determined in accordance with the examination of the child's understanding, as stated in the mishna.

For vows, the Gemara notes, the question cannot be answered simply, because the twelfth or thirteenth year has a unique intermediate status. It is neither fully before — in which case checking would be pointless — nor fully after — in which case checking would be unnecessary. For vow purposes, this year occupies a sui generis category.

### Punishment Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, לָעֳונָשִׁין מַאי? רַב וְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא דְּאָמְרִי תַרְוַויְיהוּ: תּוֹךְ זְמַן כְּלִפְנֵי זְמַן, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי דְּאָמְרִי תַרְוַויְיהוּ: תּוֹךְ זְמַן כִּלְאַחַר זְמַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the dilemma is raised **with regard to punishments,** i.e., whether such a boy or girl is punished like an adult for violating the prohibitions of the Torah. **What,** then, is the *halakha*? The Sages disagree. **Rav and Rabbi Ḥanina both say:** The development of pubic hairs **during** that **time** is considered **as before** the **time,** and therefore the boy or girl is not liable to receive punishment for his or her actions. **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi both say:** The development of pubic hairs **during** that **time** is considered **as after** the **time,** and they are punished.

The Gemara therefore asks specifically about criminal punishment: *le-onshin mai* — what is the rule regarding liability to court-imposed punishment? One might have thought this is the one area where the bar mitzvah threshold is most clearly decisive; yet the Gemara treats this as an open question. Rav and Rabbi Chanina both hold that the year before is like the earlier period — no punishable liability until the actual birthday of majority. Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, by contrast, hold the surprising position that even the year leading up already carries adult status for purposes of punishment.

### Sign Reference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, וְסִימָנָיךְ ״וְזֹאת לְפָנִים בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: And your mnemonic,** to remember which Sages said which ruling, is the verse: **"Now this [*vezot*] was the custom in former times in Israel"** (Ruth 4:7). The Sage whose name has a feminine form like the word *vezot*, namely, Rav Ḥanina, maintains that the development of pubic hairs during the time is considered as before the time, like the former times mentioned in the verse.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers a mnemonic: *ve-zos lefanim be-Yisrael* — "this is as before in Israel," suggesting the position that the year before is like *lifnei*, the earlier period. Rashi explains that the word *ve-zos* is grammatically feminine, and that Rabbi Chanina's name sounds feminine in its ending. This connects *zos* to Rabbi Chanina, and the word *lefanim* — earlier — signals that his position, shared with Rav, is that the year before does not carry adult status.

### Hamnuna Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב הַמְנוּנָא: אַחַר זְמַן הַזֶּה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרוּ ״אֵין אָנוּ יוֹדְעִים לְשֵׁם מִי נָדַרְנוּ״, ״לְשֵׁם מִי הִקְדַּשְׁנוּ״ — נִדְרֵיהֶם נֶדֶר וְהֶקְדֵּשָׁן הֶקְדֵּשׁ. הָא תּוֹךְ זְמַן — כְּלִפְנֵי זְמַן!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hamnuna raises an objection** to the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi from the mishna: **After that time,** twelve years and one day for a girl and thirteen years and one day for a boy, **even if they say: We do not know in Whose name we vowed** and **in Whose name we consecrated, their vow is** a valid **vow and their consecration is** a valid **consecration.** Rav Hamnuna infers from this ruling that if they issued this statement **during** the **time,** it is considered **as before** the **time,** even if they had developed two hairs.

Rav Hamnuna challenges from a *baraita*: after the relevant age — twelve for a girl, thirteen for a boy — even if the individual says "we do not know in whose name we made our vow or in whose name we consecrated," the vow and the consecration are nonetheless fully valid. The implication (*ha toch zman ki-lifnei zman*) is that within the prior year — *toch zman* — the period has the character of *lifnei*, the earlier period.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אֵימָא רֵישָׁא — קוֹדֶם הַזְּמַן הַזֶּה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרוּ ״יוֹדְעִים אָנוּ לְשֵׁם מִי נָדַרְנוּ״, ״לְשֵׁם מִי הִקְדַּשְׁנוּ״ — אֵין נִדְרֵיהֶם נֶדֶר וְאֵין הֶקְדֵּשָׁן הֶקְדֵּשׁ. הָא תּוֹךְ זְמַן — כִּלְאַחַר זְמַן!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Rav Hamnuna, in rejection of this proof: **Say the former clause** in the mishna: **Prior to that time,** eleven years and one day for a girl and twelve years and one day for a boy, **even if they said: We know in Whose name we vowed** and **in Whose name we consecrated, their vow is not** a valid **vow and their consecration is not** a valid **consecration.** One can infer the opposite from here, that if they issued this statement **during** the **time,** it is considered **as after** the **time.**

Rava pushes back: you cannot infer from the end of the rule, because the beginning of the rule says that before eleven and a day, even if they claim full knowledge, nothing counts. By parallel inference, that would imply that *toch zman* — the intermediate year — is like *le-achar zman*, the later period. The two inferences cancel each other out.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הִיא, רָבָא קָטָעֵי. הוּא סָבַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא מִמִּשְׁנָה יְתֵירָה קָדָיֵיק, וְאַדְּדָיֵיק מִסֵּיפָא — לֵידוּק מֵרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **And** that **is not so,** as **Rava erred. He thought** that **Rav Hamnuna inferred from the superfluous** statement of the **mishna,** i.e., that the clause Rav Hamnuna cites is unnecessary for the *halakha* it states, which is why Rav Hamnuna inferred his conclusion from it. **And** therefore Rava responded that **rather than inferring from the latter clause** of the mishna that if the boy or girl claims not to know in Whose name he or she vowed during the time, it is considered as before the time, **let him infer from the former clause** that it is considered as after the time, as Rava demonstrated.

The Gemara, however, rejects Rava's response as a misreading of Rav Hamnuna's proof. Rava thought Rav Hamnuna was making a *diyuk* from a superfluous phrase in the Mishna. But in fact Rav Hamnuna's proof was from the substance of the *halacha* itself. When the Mishna says that at twelve years and a day the vow is binding even if the girl claims ignorance — what case is this? It cannot be the case where she lacks the two pubic hairs (*shtei se'arot*), because then she would be a minor regardless. It must therefore be the case where she does have the two pubic hairs. And the Gemara draws from this that it is precisely *after* twelve years and a day that these matters — the pubic hairs — acquire their legal significance. Before that point they are legally meaningless.

### Hair Significance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְטַעְמָא דִּלְאַחַר זְמַן הוּא דְּגָמְרָה לַהּ לְמִילְּתֵיהּ? הָא תּוֹךְ זְמַן — כְּלִפְנֵי זְמַן.
>
> **Translation:** **and the reason** that the development of two hairs renders him an adult is **that** the onset of **his matter,** i.e., his reaching puberty, **was completed for him after** the **time?** Rav Hamnuna infers from here that if the child developed two hairs **during** the **time,** it is considered **as before** the **time,** and he or she is not classified as an adult.

This leads to a critical conceptual point. When we speak of adulthood requiring two pubic hairs and a minimum age, these are not simply two independent parallel criteria. One might have thought age and physical maturity are entirely separate requirements that happen to both be necessary. But the Gemara now suggests a different relationship: the age threshold is what gives meaning to the pubic hairs. Hairs that appear before the age of majority may be mere *shuma* — some kind of bodily growth unrelated to genuine puberty — and they acquire their legal character as signs of maturity only when they appear in conjunction with the attainment of the proper age. The age, on this reading, is not merely an additional criterion but the interpretive context that determines whether the physical sign counts at all.

### Zeira Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד מֵתִיב רַבִּי זֵירָא: ״אִישׁ כִּי יַפְלִא לִנְדּוֹר נֶדֶר״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אִישׁ״? לְרַבּוֹת בֶּן שְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה וְיוֹם אֶחָד, שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ לְהַפְלִיא — נְדָרָיו קַיָּימִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, Rabbi Zeira raises an objection** to the opinion that the development of signs indicating puberty during the time is equivalent to their development after the time. It is taught in a *baraita* which deals with the verse: "Speak to the children of Israel and say to them: **When** either **man** or a woman **shall clearly utter a vow"** (Numbers 6:2). **What** is the meaning when **the verse states "man,"** after it has already stated "the children of Israel"? This serves **to include** anyone who is **thirteen years and one day old, that even if he does not know** how **to utter clearly** and articulate the meaning of his statements, **his vows are in effect.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּלָא אַיְיתִי שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת — קָטָן הוּא! אֶלָּא לָאו דְּאַיְיתִי שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת, וְטַעְמָא דְּבֶן שְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה וְיוֹם אֶחָד הוּא דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ ״אִישׁ״, הָא תּוֹךְ זְמַן כְּלִפְנֵי זְמַן! תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira analyzes this *baraita*. **What are the circumstances? If** it is referring to a case **where** the boy **has not** yet **developed two** pubic **hairs,** then **he is a minor,** and the *halakha* with regard to him cannot be derived from the word "man." **Rather, is it not** referring to a case **where** the boy **has developed two** pubic **hairs? And** by inference, **the reason that he is** considered **a man** due to his development of pubic hairs **is that he is thirteen years and one day old, but** if the boy developed two hairs **during** the **time,** it is considered **as before** the **time.** The Gemara concludes: This is indeed **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi that developing pubic hairs during the time is equivalent to developing hairs after the time.

Rav Zeira adds a further proof from a verse. The Torah says *ish ki yafli lindor neder* — if a man enunciates a vow. Why does the Torah say *ish*, a man? To include a boy of thirteen years and a day — teaching that once he has crossed that threshold, his vow is binding even if he claims he did not know what he was doing. Again the Gemara asks: what case is this? If he lacks two pubic hairs, he is a minor regardless. It must therefore be the case where he has two pubic hairs. And the reason his vow counts now — *d'hava lei ish*, because he is now considered a man — is precisely because he is thirteen years and a day. This proves definitively that before that point, even the presence of the hairs carries no legal significance.

### Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכַסָא תוֹךְ זְמַן כִּלְפְנֵי זְמַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara declares *tiyuvta* — an absolute refutation — of the view that the year before carries the status of the later period. The conclusion (*ve-hilchasa toch zman ki-lifnei zman*) is that the year leading up to *bar* or *bat mitzvah* is legally equivalent to the period before — it has no adult status. Notably, the proof comes from the realm of vows, even though the Gemara had initially framed the question as specifically about punishment. The Gemara's implicit answer is that the question of when pubic hairs acquire legal meaning is fundamental and uniform across all areas of *halacha*. If the hairs carry no significance before the age threshold even for vows, then certainly the year before cannot confer adult status for any other purpose.

## Hair by Age

### Before Nine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: כְּתַנָּאֵי, בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים שֶׁהֵבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת — שׁוּמָא, מִבֶּן תֵּשַׁע וְעַד שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה וְיוֹם אֶחָד — שׁוּמָא. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: סִימָן. בֶּן שְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה וְיוֹם אֶחָד — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל סִימָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said** that the *baraita* is not a refutation of the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, as this matter is **subject to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** since there is another *baraita* which teaches the following: Everyone agrees with regard to **a nine-year-old** boy **who developed two hairs** that this is not considered a sign of puberty, as they are treated as hairs that grow on **a mole. From nine** years **of age until** the age of **twelve years and one day,** even if the hairs have not fallen out, this is still considered **a mole. Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says:** At this stage it is **a sign** indicating puberty. If he is **thirteen years and one day old** and has grown two hairs, **all agree** that it is **a sign** indicating puberty.

The Gemara introduces a *baraita* that maps the significance of pubic hair across specific age ranges. Before nine years of age, any pubic hair that appears is classified as *shuma* — some form of incidental bodily growth, perhaps fatty tissue or random hair unconnected to puberty — and carries no legal weight whatsoever.

From nine years and a day until twelve years and a day (in the context of a girl), any pubic hair that grows is still classified as *shuma*. It is random, not reflective of genuine pubescent development, and cannot serve as a sign of maturity.

### Yosei Dissent

Rabbi Yosei b'Rabbi Yehuda dissents sharply: from nine years onward, pubic hair *can* count as a sign of maturity (*siman*). This is a striking position that would seem to imply adulthood is potentially achievable long before the established ages of twelve or thirteen.

### Internal Tension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא גּוּפַאּ קַשְׁיָא, אָמְרַתְּ: ״מִבֶּן תֵּשַׁע וְעַד שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה וְיוֹם אֶחָד״ — שׁוּמָא, הָא שְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה גּוּפָא — סִימָן, וַהֲדַר תָּנֵי: ״בֶּן שְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה וְיוֹם אֶחָד״ — סִימָן, הָא שְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה גּוּפָא — שׁוּמָא!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman analyzes the *baraita*. **This** *baraita* **itself** is **difficult,** as **you** initially **said** that **from nine** years **of age until** the age of **twelve years and one day** it is **a mole,** from which it can be inferred that if he developed two pubic hairs in **the thirteenth year itself,** it is **a sign** indicating puberty. **And then** the *baraita* **teaches** that if he is **thirteen years and one day old** and has grown two hairs, this is **a sign** indicating puberty, which indicates that if he developed the hairs in **the thirteenth year itself,** it is **a mole.**

The Gemara immediately notices an internal contradiction within the *baraita* itself. The first part states that from nine to twelve it is *shuma*. But then the *baraita* later says that at thirteen years and a day, *divrei hakol siman* — everyone agrees it is a valid sign. What happened to the period from twelve to thirteen? If nine-to-twelve is *shuma*, one might have inferred that the thirteenth year itself — from twelve to thirteen for a boy — would already count. But the *baraita*'s final clause says it takes until thirteen and a day. This creates an apparent gap: the thirteenth year itself is neither covered by the *shuma* clause nor by the *divrei hakol* clause.

### Multiple Readings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לַָאו תַּנָּאֵי הִיא: דְּמָר סָבַר תּוֹךְ זְמַן כִּלְאַחַר זְמַן, וּמָר סָבַר תּוֹךְ זְמַן כְּלִפְנֵי זְמַן?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman concludes: **What, is it not** correct to say that there **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as** one **Sage,** the *tanna* who states the first line of the *baraita*, **holds** that **during** that **time** is considered **as after** the **time, and** one **Sage,** the *tanna* of the last line of the *baraita*, **holds** that **during** that **time** is considered **as before** the **time?** If so, the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi is one side of a dispute between *tanna'im*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא תּוֹךְ זְמַן כְּלִפְנֵי זְמַן, וְאִידִי וְאִידִי בְּתִינוֹקֶת, וְרֵישָׁא רַבִּי וְסֵיפָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No, everyone,** i.e., the *tanna'im* of both clauses of the *baraita*, agrees that **during** that **time** is considered **as before** the **time, and this** clause **and that** clause of the *baraita* are both referring **to a young girl. And** the difference between them is that **the first clause** of the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, who maintains that the thirteenth year for a female is considered after the time, and therefore the development of two pubic hairs at this stage is a sign of maturation; **and the last clause** is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar,** who holds that the thirteenth year for a female is considered before the time.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָא וְהָא בְּתִינוֹק, וְרֵישָׁא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר וְסֵיפָא רַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** that **this** clause **and that** clause are both dealing **with a young boy, and the first clause** is in accordance with **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar and the last clause** is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, who maintains that the thirteenth year for a boy is considered before the time.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָא וְהָא רַבִּי, הָא — בְּתִינוֹק, הָא — בְּתִינוֹקֶת. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָא וְהָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר, הָא — בְּתִינוֹק, הָא — בְּתִינוֹקֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** that both **this** clause **and that** clause are in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, and the difference between them is that **this** last clause of the *baraita* is referring **to a young boy,** whereas **that** first clause is referring **to a young girl. And if you wish, say** that both **this** clause **and that** clause are in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar,** and **that** first clause of the *baraita* is referring **to a young boy,** whereas **this** last clause is referring **to a young girl.**

The Gemara proposes several ways to resolve this. One possibility is that this represents a genuine dispute between *tanna'im*: one holds the year before counts (*tosefet zman ke-zman*), the other holds it does not (*tosefet zman ki-lifnei zman*). But the Gemara rejects this as the necessary conclusion, offering three alternative readings. The *baraita* may be discussing only girls throughout — the first clause following Rabbi's view that girls go by twelve, and the final clause following Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's view that girls go by thirteen. Alternatively, both clauses may be discussing boys — the first clause following Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar that boys go by twelve, the final clause following Rabbi that boys go by thirteen. Or the two clauses may simply address different subjects without contradiction: the first is about girls (going by twelve), the second about boys (going by thirteen). A fourth reading inverts this: the first clause is about boys going by twelve (Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar), and the second about girls going by thirteen (Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar again). In any case, the apparent contradiction does not necessarily prove anything about whether the year before counts or not — it may simply reflect which *tanna*'s view is operative for which gender.

### Krispida Clarifies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: סִימָן. אָמַר רַבִּי כְּרוּסְפָּדַאי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי שַׁבְּתַאי: וְהוּא שֶׁעוֹדָן בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* further teaches that **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says** with regard to hairs from nine years of age until the age of twelve years and one day, that it is **a sign** indicating puberty. In explanation of this opinion, **Rabbi Keruspedai, son of Rabbi Shabbtai, says: And** this **is** the *halakha* only **when** the hairs **are still upon him,** i.e., they had not fallen out when he reached the age of puberty, as otherwise they are considered a mole.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד שֶׁהֵבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת — שׁוּמָא, מִבֶּן תֵּשַׁע וְעַד שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה וְיוֹם אֶחָד, וְעוֹדָן בּוֹ — שׁוּמָא. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: סִימָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that **this** opinion **is also taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to a boy **nine years and one day old who developed two hairs,** this is considered **a mole.** If the boy is **from nine** years **of age until** the age of **twelve years and one day, and** the hairs **are still upon him,** it is still considered **a mole. Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says:** It is **a sign** indicating puberty.

Returning to Rabbi Yosei b'Rabbi Yehuda's position that pubic hair from age nine can count, the Gemara cites Rav Krispida son of Rav Yehuda son of Rav Shabsai with a critical qualification: *ve-hu she-odan bo* — this applies only if the hairs are still present at the time of majority. Rabbi Yosei b'Rabbi Yehuda does not mean that a nine-year-old can actually be an adult. He means that if hairs appeared at age nine and remained through the age of *bar mitzvah*, they may count together with the attainment of the proper age.

A *baraita* is cited in support: if a boy nine years and a day old has two hairs, it is *shuma*. If hairs grew between nine and twelve years and a day and they are still present at majority, Rabbi Yosei b'Rabbi Yehuda says they count; the *chachamim* say they do not.

### Independent Criteria

This disagreement reflects a deeper conceptual divide. According to the *chachamim*, age does not merely function as a parallel independent criterion — it serves to define whether the hairs are genuine signs of puberty in the first place. Hairs that were identified as *shuma* at an earlier age retain that classification even if they remain on the body at the time of majority. They were already categorized as legally meaningless, and that categorization does not change. According to Rabbi Yosei b'Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, age and pubic hair are genuinely independent criteria. Both are required, but neither depends on the other for its meaning. A pubic hair that grows at nine is real pubic hair; if it persists until age thirteen and combines with the attainment of the proper age, the two criteria are jointly satisfied.

The Rambam rules in accordance with the *chachamim*, holding that hair which grew before *bar mitzvah* does not count — only hair that grew after the age of majority can serve as a valid sign. This leads to the striking implication that virtually no boy would actually become a *bar mitzvah* on the day of his birthday, since the likelihood that hair grew precisely on that day is negligible. This is why we do not routinely rely on age alone for biblical obligations such as *parshat zachor* without additional evidence of pubic hair. Visible facial hair, the Gemara notes, serves as an indication that pubic hair is also present. The tension between this ruling and our practical assumptions about *bar mitzvah* is something to sit with.

## Meeun & Chalitzah

### Meeun Defined

The Gemara now turns to the practical application of these principles through two contrasting legal categories: *meeun* and *chalitzah*. *Meeun* — refusal — is the institution by which a minor girl who was married off by her mother or brothers (in the absence of a living father) in a rabbinically enacted quasi-marriage may verbally withdraw from that marriage without a *get*. The ability to perform *meeun* belongs exclusively to a minor. Once she reaches *bat mitzvah*, she can no longer exercise this option.

### Chalitzah Defined

*Chalitzah*, by contrast, is valid only when performed by a definitively adult woman. If a married woman's husband dies, she becomes subject to *yibum* — levirate marriage — with the deceased's brother. If she does not wish to enter such a marriage, she performs *chalitzah*, the formal release from that bond. A minor cannot perform valid *chalitzah*; she must be established as a full adult.

### Rava Uncertain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, הִילְכְתָא: תּוֹךְ זְמַן — כְּלִפְנֵי זְמַן. רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר זוּטְרָא מַתְנֵי לַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתָּא דְּרָבָא בְּהַאי לִישָּׁנָא, אָמַר רָבָא: קְטַנָּה כׇּל שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה מְמָאֶנֶת וְהוֹלֶכֶת, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ אֵינָהּ מְמָאֶנֶת וְאֵינָהּ חוֹלֶצֶת.
>
> **Translation:** § In summary of the rulings cited above, **Rava said: The *halakha*** is that the development of two hairs **during** the **time** is considered **as before** the **time,** and it does not render one an adult. **Rav Shmuel bar Zutra teaches this *halakha* of Rava in this formulation: Rava says:** With regard to **a minor girl** whose father has passed away and whose mother or brothers accepted betrothal on her behalf, a form of betrothal instituted by the Sages, throughout her **entire twelfth year she** has the **continuous** right to **perform refusal with regard to** this marriage and thereby annul it. **From that** point **forward,** when she is already an adult, **she may no** longer **perform refusal, and she may not perform *ḥalitza*** with the brother of her husband, if he died without children.

Rava states: throughout the entire twelfth year, a girl may continue to perform *meeun*. But from twelve years and a day onward, she can neither perform *meeun* nor perform *chalitzah*. The reason is that at twelve years and a day, her status with respect to pubic hair is uncertain — she may or may not have the signs of adulthood. She might be an adult (which would invalidate *meeun*) or she might be a minor (which would invalidate *chalitzah*). Rava thus places her in a liminal state where neither option is available.

### Apparent Paradox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא גוּפַאּ קַשְׁיָא: אָמְרַתְּ ״אֵינָהּ מְמָאֶנֶת״, אַלְמָא גְּדוֹלָה הִיא, אִי גְּדוֹלָה הִיא — תַּחְלוֹץ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This** statement of Rava **itself** is **difficult: You** first **said** that once she is twelve **she may not perform refusal. Evidently, she is an adult woman.** But **if she is an adult woman, let her perform *ḥalitza*,** like any other adult woman.

The Gemara presses the apparent paradox in Rava's position. He says she cannot perform *meeun* — which implies he treats her as possibly an adult. But he also says she cannot perform *chalitzah* — which implies he treats her as possibly a minor. How can the same individual be subject to both restrictions simultaneously? This is not a contradiction but a recognition of genuine uncertainty: her pubic hair status is unknown, so she must be treated with stringency in both directions.

### Rava's Assumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ, וּמִי מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ? וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: קְטַנָּה שֶׁהִגִּיעָה לִכְלַל שְׁנוֹתֶיהָ אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה בְּדִיקָה, חֲזָקָה הֵבִיאָה סִימָנִין!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that Rava **is uncertain** whether a twelve-year-old girl is presumed to have developed two hairs and is therefore an adult, or whether it is presumed that she has not yet grown two hairs and remains a minor, and consequently he is stringent on both counts, that she may not perform refusal, like an adult, but she may also not perform *ḥalitza*, like a minor, this suggestion is problematic, as **is** Rava actually **uncertain** in this regard? **But doesn't Rava say: A minor girl who reached her full age** of maturity, i.e., twelve years and one day, **does not require examination** to determine whether she has grown two hairs, as there is **a presumption** that **she has developed signs** indicating puberty.

But this raises a further difficulty. Rava himself states elsewhere: *ketana she-higia li-chlal shenosehah einah tzricha bdikah* — once a minor girl reaches her age, she does not require examination. We operate on the presumption (*chazaka*) that she has brought forth the signs of maturity. This is the well-known *chazaka de-Rava*. If Rava assumes pubic hairs are present at this age, how does he have any doubt?

### Hairs Checked

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי מִילֵּי בִּסְתָמָא, אֲבָל הָכָא דִּבְדַקוּ וְלָא אַשְׁכַּחוּ — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **this statement,** that it is presumed that a twelve-year-old girl has developed two hairs, applies only **in an ordinary** situation. **But here** Rava is referring to a case **where they examined** her **and did not find** hairs. In such an instance, Rava did **not** say that the presumption is in effect.

The Gemara considers whether the doubt arises specifically in the case where she was checked and no hairs were found. If the hairs are absent on examination, perhaps the presumption is overcome. But this raises another issue: maybe the hairs were there and fell out.

### Rabbinic vs. Biblical

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, תְּמָאֵן! חוֹשְׁשִׁין שֶׁמָּא נָשְׁרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** that she was actually examined, she should be considered a minor in all regards and **she should** be able to **perform refusal.** The Gemara answers: **We are concerned** that **perhaps** the girl had already developed pubic hairs but **they fell out.** Consequently, although the girl is not treated with the presumption that she is an adult, she does not have the certain status of a minor either, and cannot perform refusal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר חוֹשְׁשִׁין, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? דְּאִיתְּמַר: רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין שֶׁמָּא נָשְׁרוּ, רַב פַּפֵּי אָמַר: חוֹשְׁשִׁין. הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְעִנְיַן חֲלִיצָה, אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן מֵיאוּן — חוֹשְׁשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **This works out well according to the one who says** that **we are concerned** that the pubic hairs fell out. **But according to the one who says** that **we are not concerned** that they have fallen out, **what is there to say? As it was stated** that *amora'im* disagreed with regard to this matter. **Rav Pappa says: We are not concerned** that **perhaps** the pubic hairs **fell out; Rav Pappi says: We are concerned** that they might have fallen out. The Gemara answers that **this statement** of Rav Pappa, that there is no concern that perhaps the pubic hairs fell out, applies only **with regard to *ḥalitza*, but with regard to refusal** everyone agrees that **we are concerned** that they might have fallen out.

The resolution is that *chazaka de-Rava* — the presumption that pubic hairs are present upon attaining the proper age — applies to rabbinic laws, not to biblical laws. *Meeun*, being a rabbinic institution, can be foreclosed by the presumption that she may be an adult (i.e., that hairs are present). *Chalitzah*, being a biblical act, requires more than a presumption — one cannot perform a biblical act of release on the basis of a mere *chazaka* when there is genuine uncertainty. Therefore, for *meeun* the presumption of hairs operates to deny her the option, while for *chalitzah* the absence of confirmed pubic hair means she cannot validly perform it.

This distinction between rabbinic and biblical law has broad ramifications. The vast majority of synagogue ritual — *davening*, *krias haTorah*, *megillat Esther*, *zimmun*, and the like — are rabbinic in character. For all such matters, *chazaka de-Rava* applies and one may assume majority upon attaining the proper age without independent verification of pubic hair. For the small category of biblical obligations — *parshat zachor*, *tekiyat shofar*, *davening* on Yom Kippur — the *chazaka* is insufficient and additional evidence of physical maturity is required.

### Fallen Hair Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב דִּימִי מִנְּהַרְדְּעָא: הִלְכְתָא, חוֹשְׁשִׁין שֶׁמָּא נָשְׁרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the issue of whether there is concern that hairs might have fallen out, **Rav Dimi of Neharde'a said: The *halakha*** is that if a girl reached the age of twelve and she was examined and the signs of puberty were not found, **we are concerned** that **perhaps** the pubic hairs **fell out.** Consequently, if her mother or brothers had accepted betrothal for her when she was a minor, she cannot perform refusal at that stage.

Rav Dimi mi-Neharde'a rules: the *halacha* is *choshshin shema nashru* — we are concerned that the hairs may have been present and fallen out. Even a visual examination that reveals no pubic hair does not definitively establish minority status once the girl has reached the age of *bat mitzvah*. This ruling reinforces the broader principle: after the age threshold has been crossed, the presumption of physical maturity is robust and not easily displaced by negative evidence.

## Marriage Aftermath

### Post-Mitzvah Sex

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּקִדְּשָׁהּ בְּתוֹךְ זְמַן, וּבָעַל לְאַחַר זְמַן, דְּאִיכָּא סְפֵיקָא דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, אֲבָל מֵעִיקָּרָא — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And this statement** applies only in a case **where** her husband **betrothed her during** the **time,** before she reached the age of twelve years and one day, **and engaged in intercourse** with her **after** the **time,** when she was already twelve years and one day old. This is a situation **where there is an uncertainty** with regard to **Torah law,** since if she had developed two hairs and is an adult, the marriage applies by Torah law, due to the intercourse. **But** if he engaged in intercourse with her only **at the outset,** before she turned twelve, there is **no** concern that perhaps she developed pubic hairs and they fell out, as this marriage applies by rabbinic law.

The Gemara refines these principles through a specific scenario. Suppose a girl was given in a rabbinic marriage — by her mother or brothers — while still a minor, during her twelfth year. Suppose further that sexual relations occurred after she reached twelve years and a day. She now claims she is still a minor and wishes to perform *meeun*. She also notes that no pubic hairs are visible.

### Biblical Marriage

The problem is that if she was in fact an adult at the time of those post-majority relations — that is, if she did have pubic hairs that have since fallen out — then those relations would have constituted a biblically valid act of marriage (*kiddushin* effected through intercourse). She would be biblically married. Since we are now dealing with a potential biblical marriage, we cannot rely on the absence of pubic hairs to declare her a definitive minor and permit *meeun*. The concern about fallen hairs (*chashash nashru*) means we cannot assume minority for the purpose of a biblical determination.

### No Post-Mitzvah Sex

If, however, there were no sexual relations after she reached twelve years and a day, then no act could have created a biblical marriage, and the relationship remains purely rabbinic in character throughout. In that case, since the question is entirely about a rabbinic institution, we may rely on the absence of visible pubic hairs to treat her as a definitive minor for *meeun* purposes. For rabbinic matters, the absence of visible hairs — combined with the absence of any act that could have generated a biblical consequence — is sufficient to establish minority status and permit *meeun*.

## Vows & Adult Status

### Huna: Lashes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הִקְדִּישׁ וְאָכַל — לוֹקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to a minor who vowed, **Rav Huna says:** If the minor is aware of the meaning of his vow and in Whose name he or she vowed, and the minor's age is during the time, i.e., the twelfth year for a girl or the thirteenth year for a boy, and he **consecrated** an item of food **and** subsequently **ate** it, **he is flogged,** which is the punishment for one who eats consecrated food.

The Gemara now returns to the question of what consequences flow from the *mufla samuch le-ish* status — the year before majority during which vows are binding if the minor understands what he or she is doing. The critical question is: if the vow takes effect, does that mean the minor is treated as a full adult for all purposes of vow law? Specifically, if this minor consecrates an object (*hekdesh*) through a vow and then proceeds to eat it or use it, do they receive lashes for the violation?

Rav Huna says: *hikdish ve-achal lokeh* — he consecrated it and ate it, he receives lashes, even during this year before majority.

### Yafli Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִישׁ כִּי יַפְלִא לִנְדּוֹר״, וְ״לֹא יַחֵל דְּבָרוֹ״ — כֹּל שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בְּהַפְלָאָה יֶשְׁנוֹ בְּבַל יַחֵל, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵינוֹ בְּהַפְלָאָה אֵינוֹ בְּבַל יַחֵל.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna explains: **As it is stated: "When** either **a man** or a woman **shall clearly utter a vow"** (Numbers 6:2), from which it is derived that if one on the brink of adulthood is able to articulate that his vow is in the name of God, his vows are valid. **And** another verse states: **"He shall not profane his word"** (Numbers 30:3). This indicates that **any** person **who is** included **in explicitness** of intent **is** also included **in** the prohibition: **"He shall not profane** his word," **and anyone who is not** included **in explicitness** of intent **is not** included **in** the prohibition: **"He shall not profane** his word."

The biblical source is *ish ki yafli lindor neder* — if a man enunciates a vow. The word *yafli* — to enunciate, the same root as *mufla* — is understood as extending the scope of the verse to include one who is *mufla samuch le-ish*, on the cusp of manhood. And since the same passage includes the prohibition *ve-lo yachel devaro* — he shall not violate his word — the Gemara derives: *kol she-yeshno be-hafla'ah yeshno be-lo yachel* — whoever can make a binding vow is also subject to the prohibition against violating it. Rav Huna thus holds that the year-before minor is fully subject to both the capacity to make vows and the punishable consequences of violating them.

### Baraita Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב הוּנָא בַּר יְהוּדָה (לְרָבָא) לְסַיּוֹעֵי לְרַב הוּנָא:
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna bar Yehuda raises an objection** to Rava, **in support** of the opinion **of Rav Huna:**

Rav Huna bar Yehuda brings a *baraita* in apparent support of this position. The *baraita* states: the Torah treats a minor like an adult with respect to willful violation of an oath (*zadon shevuah*), with respect to the prohibition, and with respect to *lo yachel*. However, the *baraita* continues: one might have thought the minor is also obligated to bring a *korban* (sacrifice) for an unwitting violation (*shogeg*). Therefore, the Torah writes *zeh ha-davar* — "this is the matter" — limiting the parallel to the area of prohibitions, but not extending it to *korbanos*. The minor is treated as an adult for the oral acts and their prohibitions, but not for the sacrificial consequences.

### Bal Yachel Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִי שֶׁמָּצִינוּ שֶׁהִשְׁוָה הַכָּתוּב הַקָּטָן כַּגָּדוֹל לִזְדוֹן שְׁבוּעָה וּלְאִיסָּר וּלְבַל יַחֵל, יָכוֹל יְהֵא חַיָּיב עַל הֶקְדֵּשׁוֹ קׇרְבָּן?
>
> **Translation:** **Since we find that the verse equates a minor,** i.e., one on the brink of adulthood, **to an adult with regard to an intentional** violation of **an oath and with regard to** a vow of **prohibition,** where one renders an item prohibited to himself through a vow, **and with regard to** the prohibition of **he shall not profane** his word, one **might** have thought that this minor, like an adult, **should** also **be liable** to bring **an offering for** misuse of **his consecrated** property, e.g., if he ate an item that he consecrated.

The Gemara, however, immediately questions whether this *baraita* actually proves what Rav Huna wants it to prove. The *baraita* says the minor is subject to *issur Bal Yachel* and the prohibition of the oath. But what does that mean exactly? The Gemara poses a dilemma: if the *mufla samuch le-ish* status is truly biblical (*d'Oraita*), then not only does the prohibition apply but lashes should follow (*milka nami lilki*). If, on the other hand, the status is not truly biblical (*lav d'Oraita*), then there should be no prohibition at all (*issur nami leika*). Where does a vague intermediate prohibition — binding enough to be meaningful but not sufficient to warrant lashes — come from?

### Adults Transgress

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת לְאִיסָּר וּלְ״בַל יַחֵל״ חַיָּיב. אֵימָא: לְאִיסּוּר ״בַּל יַחֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*. **In any event,** the *baraita* **teaches** that a discriminating minor on the brink of adulthood is considered an adult **with regard to** a vow of **prohibition and with regard to** the prohibition of **he shall not profane** his word, which indicates that he is **liable** for violating this prohibition. This supports the opinion of Rav Huna that a minor is flogged for eating food he consecrated. The Gemara refutes this proof: There is room to **say** that the word: And, in the phrase: With regard to a vow of prohibition and with regard to the prohibition of he shall not profane his word, should be omitted, and the *baraita* is comparing a minor to an adult **with regard to the prohibition** of **he shall not profane** his word, but it does not indicate that he is liable to receive lashes for violating this prohibition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיסּוּר ״בַּל יַחֵל״ — מָה נַפְשָׁךְ: אִי מוּפְלָא סָמוּךְ לְאִישׁ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא — מִילְקָא נָמֵי לִילְקֵי, וְאִי מוּפְלָא סָמוּךְ לְאִישׁ לָאו דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא — אִיסּוּר נָמֵי לֵיכָּא, לְאוֹתָן הַמּוּזְהָרִים עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Can the *baraita* actually mean that a minor is compared to an adult with regard to **the prohibition** of **he shall not profane** his word, but he is not flogged? **Whichever way you** look at it, this is problematic: **If a discriminating** minor **on the brink of adulthood** is considered an adult **by Torah law, he should be flogged too,** for his violation. **And if a discriminating** minor **on the brink of adulthood** is **not** considered an adult **by Torah law, there is no prohibition** violated here **either.** The Gemara answers that according to the *baraita* the prohibition does not apply to the minor himself, but **to those who are warned to** keep **him** away from the prohibited item.

The Gemara resolves this by reframing who bears the prohibition. *L'otan ha-muzharim alav* — it is not the minor who is the subject of the prohibition, but the adults around him. When the minor consecrates an object through his vow, the vow may take effect (at least to some degree, even if only rabbinically), and it is the adults who subsequently use or eat the consecrated object who transgress *Bal Te'acher* — the prohibition against misappropriating consecrated property — and who may receive lashes. The minor himself, as a minor, does not bear adult criminal liability. The *baraita*'s statement that a minor is treated like an adult for these prohibitions thus refers to the obligations imposed on others by virtue of the minor's act, not to the minor's own liability.

### Kahana: Rabbinic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: קָטָן אוֹכֵל נְבֵלוֹת — בֵּית דִּין מְצוִּּוין עָלָיו לְהַפְרִישׁוֹ. הָכָא בְמַאי עָסְקִינַן? כְּגוֹן שֶׁהִקְדִּישׁ הוּא, וְאָכְלוּ אֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: If so, one can **conclude from** the *baraita* that if **a minor eats** meat from unslaughtered **animal carcasses** or violates other prohibitions, **the court is commanded to prevent him** from doing so. This is problematic, as elsewhere it is stated that this matter is subject to dispute (see *Yevamot* 114a). The Gemara explains: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where** the minor **consecrated** the food item **and others ate** it. They are liable to receive lashes for their consumption, but if he ate it he is not liable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: ״הִקְדִּישׁ הוּא וְאָכְלוּ אֲחֵרִים — לוֹקִין״, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: ״אֵין לוֹקִין״ — מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? דְּאִיתְּמַר: הִקְדִּישׁ הוּא וְאָכְלוּ אֲחֵרִים, רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר: אֵין לוֹקִין, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַויְהוּ: לוֹקִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another difficulty: **This works out well according to the one who said** that if a minor **consecrated** a food item **and others ate** it, **they are flogged. But according to the one who said** that in such a case **they are not flogged, what can be said? As it was stated** that *amora'im* disagreed with regard to this issue: If a minor **consecrated** a food item **and others ate** it, **Rav Kahana says** that **they are not flogged; Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish both say** that **they are flogged.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּרַבָּנַן, וּקְרָא אַסְמַכְתָּא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara therefore reverts to the interpretation that the *baraita* is referring to the prohibition of he shall not profane his word, not the punishment for violation of the vow. And the reason lashes are not administered is that the prohibition is **by rabbinic law. And** as for the **verse** mentioned in the *baraita*, when it states that the verse equates a minor to an adult, which indicates that it is dealing with Torah law, this verse is **a mere support** for a rabbinic law.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא: הִקְדִּישׁ וְאָכְלוּ אֲחֵרִים — רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר: אֵין לוֹקִין, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: לוֹקִין. בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? מָר סָבַר: מוּפְלָא סָמוּךְ לְאִישׁ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, וּמָר סָבַר: מוּפְלָא סָמוּךְ לְאִישׁ מִדְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses **the** matter **itself,** i.e., the dispute cited above. If a minor **consecrated** a food item **and others ate** it, **Rav Kahana says** that **they are not flogged; Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish both say** that **they are flogged. With regard to what** principle **do** these Sages **disagree?** One **Sage,** i.e., Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish, **holds** that **a discriminating** minor **on the brink of adulthood** is considered an adult **by Torah law,** which is why others are liable for eating an item he consecrated; **and** one **Sage,** Rav Kahana, **holds** that **a discriminating** minor **on the brink of adulthood** is considered an adult **by rabbinic law.**

One might then ask whether this implies a general principle that *Beit Din* is obligated to intervene whenever a minor is engaged in a transgression. The Gemara deflects this by clarifying that the scenario is more specific: *hikdish hu ve-achalu acherim* — the minor consecrated the object, and other adults ate it. It is the adults' own act of eating consecrated food that triggers their liability under *Bal Te'acher*. The issue is not one of a communal duty to restrain the minor, but of the adults' own obligations with respect to an object that has acquired sanctified status.

A three-way position is now in view. Rav Huna holds the most expansive view: the year-before minor is treated as a full adult for vow law, and if he violates his own vow he receives lashes. An intermediate position holds that the vow is fully binding and adults who transgress the consecration receive lashes, but the minor himself does not. And now Rav Kahana presents the most restrictive position: *ein lokin* — even the adults do not receive lashes for eating what the minor consecrated. Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish disagree with Rav Kahana and say the adults do receive lashes. According to Rav Kahana's position, the entire institution of *mufla samuch le-ish* is essentially rabbinic in character, without biblical force. The Gemara indicates that according to Rav Kahana the *baraita*'s language about prohibition is ultimately only rabbinic in authority, with the verse serving as a mere *asmachta* — a textual peg — rather than a genuine biblical source. This three-way debate will be taken up in the following session.
