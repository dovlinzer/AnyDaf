# Hullin 58 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Treifa Lifespan

### Rav Huna's Standard

The daf begins at the bottom of 57b, where the Gemara takes up a question that had been alluded to earlier in the chapter: in what timeframe does a *treifa* die? The issue had already surfaced when empirical evidence arose that an animal had not died, and the response offered was that it must have died within the year — that being sufficient for *treifa* status. Now the Gemara addresses the question directly.

*Amar Rav Huna: siman le'treifa yud-beis chodesh* — Rav Huna teaches that twelve months is the indicator for a *treifa*. This does not mean that any animal dying within twelve months was a *treifa*, since it may have died from other causes entirely. Rather, the ruling operates in the reverse direction: if there is a *safek* whether an animal is a *treifa* and it survives for twelve months, one may conclude it is not a *treifa* — and proceed to *shecht* it. The twelve-month threshold thus has practical relevance specifically in cases of *safek treifa*.

### Tosefta Debate

The Gemara immediately challenges this with a *meteiv* from a *tosefta* that presents multiple different indicators for determining *treifa* status. The first opinion in the *tosefta* states: *siman le'treifa kol she'eina yoledes* — an indicator of a *treifa* is that it does not give birth. This formulation is admittedly imprecise, since many animals cannot give birth for reasons entirely unrelated to being a *treifa* — old age or independent fertility problems, for instance. The teaching must therefore be read in its positive form: when an animal *does* give birth, that is evidence it is *not* a *treifa*.

### Rabban Gamliel

Rabban Gamliel offers a different indicator: *meshabbachas ve'holechet beyadua she'hi keshaira* — if after an injury the animal's health progressively improves, it is kosher. Conversely, *misnavveves ve'holechet beyadua she'hi treifa* — if the animal's health continues to decline, it is a *treifa*. Notably, Rabban Gamliel does not specify that the animal will die, which raises a broader question about the underlying logic of these opinions.

One must ask: are these indicators — inability to give birth, declining health — simply diagnostic tools for identifying that a wound was indeed fatal? That reading would be consistent with the principle that a *treifa* cannot survive. Alternatively, perhaps these opinions do not accept that principle at all, and instead define *treifa* by functional criteria: an injury that causes infertility, or that causes progressive deterioration. The difficulty with the infertility criterion, however, is that the Gemara elsewhere is explicit that an animal missing a womb is *not* a *treifa*. It therefore cannot be that any injury leading to infertility constitutes a *treifa*. The simpler reading remains that all opinions agree in principle that a *treifa* will die; what they debate is how to determine whether a particular doubtful injury is fatal, and these physiological markers — fertility and health trajectory — serve as the practical tests.

### Rabbi Meir

A third opinion in the *tosefta* is attributed to *rebbe* or, in some versions, to Rabbi Meir: *siman le'treifa shloshim yom* — if the animal does not die within thirty days, it is not a *treifa*. This raises an obvious difficulty.

### Empirical Challenge

The Gemara records the objection directly: *ve-lo harbeh miskayyemes shtayim ve-shalosh shanim?* — are there not many animals that survive two or three years? This is the empirical challenge. Importantly, those raising this objection are not proposing two or three years as an alternative timeframe; they are arguing that since animals with such injuries sometimes survive that long, no single fixed period can serve as a reliable threshold. The implication may be that one should rely instead on qualitative indicators — does the animal's health improve, can it give birth — rather than on a specific duration.

This brings the problem back to Rav Huna. Even if thirty days is too short, how can Rav Huna assert twelve months when some animals demonstrably survive their injuries for two or three years?

### Seasonal Theory

The Gemara answers: *tannai hi* — it is a dispute among *tannaim*, meaning yet another *braita* is brought to bear. The *braita* records a case cited by Rabbi Yosi ben HaMeshullam: in a place called Invo, a person suffered a perforated skull. Medical intervention was applied — Rashi explains a hardened gourd was used as a kind of covering, a plaster of sorts — and the person survived. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar dismisses the proof: *misham raya?* — that was in the summer, *yemos hachamah haya*, and *de'keivan de'asa alav yemos hageshamim miyad mes* — once winter came upon him, he died immediately.

This is the basis for Rashi's explanation in support of Rav Huna's twelve-month standard: any injury that constitutes a *treifa* will necessarily prove fatal when the seasons turn. A particular season may not exacerbate the wound, but over the course of a full year every season will arrive, and one of them will. After twelve complete months, if the animal has survived, it has weathered every seasonal condition and thus demonstrated it is not a *treifa*.

This explanation gives rise to a noteworthy question raised by later *poskim*: if the twelve-month rule is about seasonal exposure, then in a leap year (*ibur*) with thirteen months, should one wait thirteen months rather than twelve? The Gemara consistently says twelve months, not a *shanah*, suggesting the number itself — rather than the concept of a full seasonal cycle — is determinative. What the answer would be in a climate with effectively no seasonal variation, like certain consistently temperate regions, is left unresolved.

## Treifa Fertility

### Rav Acha Position

*Amar Rav Acha bar Yaakov: halacha treifa yoledes u'meshabbachas* — the ruling is that a *treifa* can give birth and can improve in health in other respects. This is a striking ruling. It means that observing an animal giving birth or seeing its health improve cannot serve as evidence that it is not a *treifa*, since these phenomena are consistent with *treifa* status.

Before proceeding, it is worth clarifying what this discussion does and does not mean. It does not mean that when an animal's health improves or it gives birth with no evidence of injury, one should suspect it of being a *treifa*. Nor — and this is crucial — does it mean that if an animal clearly has a *treifa* injury (a punctured lung, for instance) and then survives twelve months, gives birth, or improves in health, one can retroactively declare it kosher. The *rishonim* themselves were confronted with empirical evidence of animals with established *treifa* conditions surviving, and no authority suggested that such evidence could override the established category. A *ma'aseh nissin* — a miraculous exception — does not alter the *halacha*. (The Maharshal does suggest that perhaps ninety-nine percent of animals with *treifa* injuries will die, and there is a small percentage of outliers, which reconciles the principle with contrary evidence without disturbing the ruling.)

The only context in which these tests are halachically operative is where there is genuine doubt — a *safek treifa* — such as when an animal was clawed (*drusa*) and it is unknown whether the membrane was punctured. In such cases, if the animal survives or gives birth, that evidence resolves the *safek*.

There is also a significant and somewhat surprising ruling that emerges from the *poskim*: if there is a dispute among halachic authorities (*machlokes haposkim*) whether a particular type of injury constitutes a *treifa*, one may invoke empirical evidence — that animals with this injury consistently survive twelve months — to rule in favor of those authorities who hold it is not a *treifa*. This is not overriding an established Talmudic *treifa* category; it is using evidence to adjudicate between positions in an unresolved halachic dispute. What one cannot do is use such evidence to override something that has been established by tradition as definitively a *treifa*.

### Ameimar Eggs

*Amar Ameimar: hanei beiei d'treifa* — Ameimar addresses the case of eggs from a hen that is a *treifa*. The general principle is that a food product from an animal that is not kosher — even a kosher species rendered non-kosher by being a *treifa* — is itself not kosher. Milk from a *treifa* cow and eggs from a *treifa* hen are both forbidden.

### First Cluster

*Shachala kama asira* — the first cluster of eggs is forbidden. The reasoning: when the hen was attacked and became a *treifa*, it was already carrying eggs. Those eggs, carried within the hen at the moment she became a *treifa*, are treated as part of her body and therefore take on her status. The first cluster is forbidden.

### Later Eggs Permitted

*Mikan v'eilach havi lei zeh v'zeh goreim u'mutar* — from that point forward, however, subsequent eggs fall under the principle of *zeh vezeh goreim* and are permitted. This principle applies to fertilized eggs — where the concern would be the developing chick. What causes the chick to develop? Both the contribution of the mother and the contribution of the father. Even though the mother is a *treifa*, the father is not, and when there are two contributing causes — one forbidden, one permitted — the product is not considered exclusively a product of the forbidden source. This is *zeh vezeh goreim mutar*: two causes, and therefore permissible.

One might object that the mother remains a *treifa* throughout and continues producing *treifa* ova. But the point is that when fertilized eggs are in question and one is interested in the developing chick, what creates the chick is the combined contribution of both parents. Having one *treif* contributor and one non-*treif* contributor is not the same as mixing a *treif* ingredient into a non-*treif* food. This is something entirely new, caused by both a *treifa* origin and a non-*treifa* origin, and the ruling of *zeh vezeh goreim* is that it is permitted.

The counterintuitive element here is worth noting. One might have thought the logic runs the other way: the eggs the hen was carrying *before* becoming a *treifa* should be fine, since they were in a kosher hen; the eggs produced *after* she became a *treifa* should be forbidden, since they are growing in a *treifa* hen. The Gemara rejects this intuition, applying something analogous to *ubar yerech imo*: the egg being carried at the moment the hen became a *treifa* is treated as part of the mother, just as a *shechita* of the mother covers the fetus. But what comes afterward is viewed in the context of the developed chick and the father's contribution — hence *zeh vezeh goreim* applies and those later eggs are permitted.

### Rav Ashi Challenge

*Aisi Rav Ashi l'Amemar* — Rav Ashi challenges Ameimar from the well-known agreement between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel regarding the egg of a *treifa* hen. Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel disagree about an egg found in a *neveilah* — a hen that was killed without *shechita* and is therefore *neveilah*. When such a hen is opened and a fully formed egg is found inside — complete enough that, as the Gemara describes, *kemosa nimkeres bashuk*, it could be sold in a marketplace — is it considered part of the mother and thus *neveilah*, or is it so complete that it is already independent of the mother and therefore kosher? That is the *machlokes*.

However, they agree — *veshavin beveitzas treifa she'asura* — that the egg of a *treifa* hen is forbidden. Why is the *treifa* case worse than the *neveilah* case? *mipnei shegadla be'issur* — because it grew while the hen was in a state of prohibition. Meaning: when the hen was attacked and became a *neveilah* — dead on the spot — the egg stopped developing at that moment. But when the hen became a *treifa* and then continued living for days or weeks before being slaughtered, the egg continued to grow inside a *treifa* hen. It is precisely that continued growth during the period of prohibition that makes it forbidden.

But if *zeh vezeh goreim* applies to later eggs, why does it not apply here as well? Why can one not invoke the father's contribution to permit these eggs?

### Egg Types

*Hasam desafna me'ara* — the Gemara answers that the case where Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel agree the egg is forbidden involves an *unfertilized* egg. The Gemara's idiom is that the egg "became fertilized from the ground" — a circumlocution for an egg that has no paternal contribution. An unfertilized egg has only one cause — the mother alone — and *zeh vezeh goreim* has no application where there is only a single source. *d'chad gorem hu* — it is only one cause, and therefore forbidden.

This also resolves a question about the formulation. The Gemara says the egg is forbidden because it *grew* when the hen was already *asura*. One might have assumed the relevant case is an egg she was already carrying when she became a *treifa* — which, as we said, is part of the mother anyway. But the language "*gadla* be'issur" — that it *grew* in the prohibited state — implies the egg came into being after she became a *treifa*. The Gemara presses this point: if the intended case were an egg already present at the time of the injury, the *braita* should have said *gamra be'issur* — that it *completed* growing — not that it grew during the prohibition. The use of *gadla* therefore indicates even a newly generated egg, and the reason it is forbidden despite arguably having a paternal contribution is that it is unfertilized, making it a case of only one cause.

## Treifa Offspring

### Sacrifice Question

The Gemara now raises the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua about *vlad treifa* — the offspring of a *treifa* animal — and whether such offspring may be offered on the *mizbei'ach*. Rabbi Eliezer holds *lo yikarev l'gabei mizbei'ach* — it may not be brought as a *korban*. Rabbi Yehoshua holds *yikarev* — it may.

The question is: what is the case? *b'shenitrefa u'levasof ibra* — the animal first became a *treifa* and afterward became pregnant. And then the question becomes one of *zeh vezeh goreim*: the mother is a *treifa*, the father is not, and the offspring results from both. Rabbi Eliezer holds *zeh vezeh goreim assur*: when there are two contributing causes — one forbidden — the product is forbidden. Rabbi Yehoshua holds *zeh vezeh goreim mutar*: two causes renders it permissible.

The Gemara then asks: *ad d'mifligi l'gavoa, liflego l'hedyot?* — why do they only debate whether it may be used as a *korban*? Why not debate whether a layperson may eat it? The Gemara answers: *koach d'heiteira adif lei* — it is a greater *chiddush* to declare something permitted than to declare it forbidden. Forbidding something requires no special justification — it is always the cautious position. It takes real halachic courage, so to speak, to go so far as to permit something. Therefore the *tanna* chose to teach the ruling of Rabbi Yehoshua in its most expansive form: that the offspring is *so* unproblematic it may even be offered on the *mizbei'ach*. Symmetrically, one might have framed Rabbi Eliezer's ruling in terms of a layperson to demonstrate how far his stringency extends — that not only may it not be a *korban*, a layperson may not eat it either. But the Gemara's answer is that the *tanna* always prefers to demonstrate the reach of the permissive position.

Implicitly, then, there is no middle ground: the Gemara frames the debate as all-or-nothing. An offspring of a *treifa* via *zeh vezeh goreim* is either fully permissible (permitted even as a *korban*) or fully forbidden (even for a layperson). A middle position — forbidden as a *korban* but permitted for a layperson — is not entertained.

## Ravina Position

### Ravina Rejects

*Ravina lo savar lah k'Rav Acha bar Yaakov* — Ravina does not accept Rav Acha bar Yaakov's position. Ravina holds that a *treifa* cannot give birth at all. This fundamentally reframes the halachic application of the fertility test: rather than *zeh vezeh goreim*, the question of whether an animal produces subsequent eggs becomes a diagnostic tool for determining whether the animal was ever a *treifa* in the first place.

### Ameimar Doubt Test

Accordingly, Ravina presents an entirely different version of Ameimar's teaching. In Ravina's version, Ameimar was speaking about *safek treifa* — a doubtful *treifa*. The formulation is: *hanei bei d'safek treifa* — eggs of a hen that is a *doubtful* *treifa*.

### First Cluster Test

*D'shichla kama* — the first cluster of eggs poses a problem, because the hen may have been carrying them before the doubtful injury occurred, making it impossible to determine from those eggs alone whether she is a *treifa*. The Hebrew *shichla* (which Rashi renders *pushta*) appears to refer to a cluster of developing eggs at various stages — a hen carrying multiple eggs at once, depositing them successively as each reaches completion.

So how does one resolve the *safek*? *mashinan lehu* — one puts those eggs aside and waits. *ei hadra v'tana sharainan* — if the hen subsequently produces another cluster of eggs after the injury, then one permits even the first cluster, since her continued egg production demonstrates she was not a *treifa*. *v'ei lo asiran* — if she never produces more eggs, the first cluster is forbidden as well, because the absence of subsequent eggs suggests she was indeed a *treifa*.

### Safek Resolution

This version of Ameimar's ruling uses fertility not as *zeh vezeh goreim* but as evidence to resolve a *safek treifa* from the outset. The halachic force is entirely different: it is not that the offspring of a *treifa* is permissible because the father also contributed; rather, subsequent egg-laying demonstrates that the hen was never a *treifa*, making even the eggs from the first cluster permissible in retrospect.

## Treifa Offspring…

### Case Reconstruction

Rav Ashi challenges Ameimar (in this second version, per Ravina's reading) from the ruling of Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel that the egg of a *treifa* is forbidden. If a *treifa* cannot give birth, how is it possible for a *treifa* hen to have an egg that grew while she was already forbidden? The Gemara answers: *hasam b'shichla kama* — that ruling speaks not of new eggs produced after she became a *treifa*, but of the cluster she was already carrying when she became one. Those eggs continued developing inside a *treifa* hen, and are therefore forbidden.

The Gemara presses further: the *braita* says the egg is forbidden because it *grew* while she was *asura*, which sounds as though the egg came into being during the period of prohibition. If it were merely an egg already present at the time, the *braita* should have said *gamra* — it *completed* growing — not *gadla*, it grew. The Gemara accepts this: *tani gamra* — read the *braita* as saying *gamra*, or understand *gadla* to mean *gamra* in this context. The case is eggs already present when she became a *treifa*, and their continued development inside a *treifa* hen is what makes them forbidden.

### Rabbi Eliezer

Now the Gemara returns to the debate of Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua about *vlad treifa* — but the case must be reconstructed differently under Ravina's position that a *treifa* cannot become pregnant. If she cannot become pregnant after becoming a *treifa*, then the case of Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua cannot be *b'shenitrefa u'levasof ibra* (first became *treifa*, then became pregnant). That scenario is impossible according to Ravina. The case must instead be *k'she'ibra u'levasof nitrefa* — she was first pregnant, and then became a *treifa*.

Under this reconstruction, *zeh vezeh goreim* drops out entirely — the father's contribution occurred before the *treifa* status arose, and he is out of the picture by the time she becomes a *treifa*. The question is solely: does the mother's *treifa* status extend to the fetus she is carrying? Rabbi Eliezer holds *ubar yerech imo hu* — the fetus is like a limb of the mother. Since the mother became a *treifa*, the fetus became a *treifa*.

### Rabbi Yehoshua

Rabbi Yehoshua holds *ubar lav yerech imo hu* — the fetus is not part of the mother. The mother's becoming a *treifa* does not extend to the fetus. The debate, on this reading, has nothing to do with *zeh vezeh goreim* and is entirely about the principle of *ubar yerech imo*.

### Egg Agreement

The Gemara then notes that even though Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua debate the status of the fetus, they agree regarding the egg of a *treifa*: *gufa hi* — it is simply part of her body. The egg does not have its own life force. Whereas the fetus, even inside the womb, manifests its own independent vitality — and that is what makes *ubar yerech imo* debatable — the egg has no such independent existence. Tosafot elaborates further on this distinction: physically, the egg is entirely embedded within the hen; the fetus in a mammal is connected to the mother through the placenta and has visible signs of its own life. It is therefore more intuitive to treat the egg as part of the mother than to treat the fetus as part of the mother, and accordingly the egg's status is undisputed by both Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua.

To summarize the two frameworks that have emerged: According to Rav Acha bar Yaakov, a *treifa* can give birth, and the question of whether the offspring of a *treifa* is permitted involves *zeh vezeh goreim*. According to Ravina, a *treifa* cannot give birth, and egg-laying after an injury serves not as *zeh vezeh goreim* but as a diagnostic test for *safek treifa*. Where all agree is that unfertilized eggs — exclusively the product of the mother — are forbidden, as are eggs carried within the hen at the time she became a *treifa*, on the grounds that they are effectively part of her body.

## Final Ruling

### Halacha Summary

*Ve-hilchesa: bezachar kol shneim asar chodesh; benekeva kol she'eina yoledes* — the *halacha* follows: for a male, the standard is twelve months; for a female, the indicator is failure to give birth — or more precisely, giving birth demonstrates it is not a *treifa*.

### Phrasing Issues

The formulation is admittedly imprecise. As stated, *kol she'eina yoledes* — all that does not give birth — would be the negative indicator, which is not quite the operative rule; the positive form, *kol she'yoledes*, all that does give birth, is what matters practically. The Gemara articulates it in the negative, which creates some ambiguity. Furthermore, the ruling as stated does not make explicit that for females as well, surviving twelve months is sufficient. One would assume this, but it goes unstated until the *shulchan aruch*, and the *shach* is the one who makes it explicit: of course twelve months for a female is also sufficient, but the Gemara means to add that giving birth within twelve months suffices as well, even before the full year has elapsed.

### Poskim Debates

The *halacha* is that we rule a *treifa* cannot give birth, and therefore a female's producing offspring resolves a *safek treifa* in her favor. This generates two related questions debated among the later *poskim*. First, since we rule *treifa aina yoledes*, do we also rule *treifa eino molid* — that a male *treifa* cannot impregnate? This parallel is not discussed in the Gemara and is disputed among the *poskim*. Second, when the Gemara says *aina yoledes*, does it mean a *treifa* cannot become pregnant at all (*aina me'uberes*), or that it cannot carry to term and give birth? The impression from the Gemara is the more expansive reading — she will not even conceive — but this too is somewhat debated.

### Evidence Use

The *poskim* also address the use of empirical evidence more broadly. As noted above, if there is a *machlokes haposkim* about whether a particular injury constitutes a *treifa* — not an established Talmudic category but an unresolved halachic question — and there is empirical evidence that animals with this injury consistently survive twelve months or give birth, one may invoke that evidence to rule in accordance with the lenient *poskim*. One cannot use such evidence to override what has been definitively established as a *treifa* by Talmudic tradition. But for unresolved questions, the evidence is admissible.

The practical context for these tests — twelve months for a male, fertility for a female — is primarily cases of *safek drusa*, *nefula*, and similar situations where the animal sustained an injury of uncertain severity and the internal examination is inconclusive.

### Rambam Exception

The Rambam makes a notable remark in this context. As a general rule, *safek* in *treifos* is treated leniently — animals have a *chazaka* of being permitted, and various grounds exist to resolve doubts favorably. The one area where the *poskim* are distinctly stricter about *safek* is *safek drusa* — an animal that may have been clawed, with possible internal injection of venom. The Rambam explains that *drusa* is the paradigmatic *treifa* of the Torah itself. The classical *treifa*, after all, is an animal mauled by a predator — the Torah's own example. The Rambam's point is that this foundational case carries a stringency that other *safek treifa* situations do not, and therefore greater scrutiny applies to *safek drusa* than to other doubtful *treifa* categories.

## Invertebrates

### No Bones Rule

*Amar Rav Huna: kol biryah she'ein bo etzem eino miskayeim yud-beis chodesh* — any creature that has no bones cannot survive twelve months. The Gemara presents this as a related teaching from Rav Huna, extending his twelve-month principle beyond *treifos* to the biological domain of invertebrates generally.

### Wormy Fruit

*Amar Rav Pappa: shma mina mi'deRav Huna* — Rav Pappa derives a practical halachic ruling from Rav Huna's principle, based on a teaching of Shmuel.

The background: the Torah forbids *sheretz hashoretz al ha'aretz* — a creeping creature that crawls on the ground. This is understood to mean that insects found in fruits or vegetables are only forbidden if they have at some point been on the ground. If an insect develops inside a fruit after it has been picked from the tree and never contacts the ground, it is permissible. However, if the insect developed while the fruit was still attached to the tree and growing, then the insect is considered to have been moving in something connected to the ground — and it is forbidden.

Shmuel's ruling: *kishus she'hisli'ah be'avi'ah asura* — a gourd that became wormy while it was still growing on the vine is forbidden, because the worm's movement occurred while the fruit was connected to the ground. The mechanism of detection: since invertebrates cannot survive twelve months (per Rav Huna), any live worm found in an already-harvested fruit must have developed after it was picked, and therefore never was on the ground.

### Date Test

*Hanei tamrei de-chada* — if one has dates that have been sitting in a bowl for more than twelve months (*le-basar treisar yarchei shata*) and then discovers live worms inside them, those worms must have developed after the dates were picked — since any worm present at the time of harvesting could not have survived the intervening year. Therefore *sharyin* — they are permissible.

### Fly Dispute

*Amar Rav: leit baka bar yoma* — Rav states that a *baka* (a type of fly or gnat) does not survive even a single day. *v'leit didva bar shata* — a *didva* (another type of flying insect) does not survive a year.

Rav Pappa then cites a popular folk-tale to challenge the first claim. The story: *sheva shani de'amrah bakta l'bakra* — for seven years, the female *baka* was arguing with the male. She had seen a wealthy man from *mechuza* bathe in the river, emerge, wrap himself in sheets — and the male *baka* had sat upon him and drawn his blood without telling her. How could he have deprived her of this feast? The story implies the *baka* lives for seven years. Is this not a challenge to Rav's claim that it does not survive a day?

The Gemara is unimpressed: *u'letaameich*, if you want to bring proofs from folk-tales, the same tradition says sixty *mane* of iron hung from a mosquito's stinger. *mi ika ihu gufa kama hvei?* — how much does the mosquito itself weigh? Such tales cannot be taken literally. Rather, *b'shani didei* — the seven years is in "mosquito years," and the sixty *mane* is in "mosquito measures," analogous to noting that an ant can lift one hundred times its own weight. In the mosquito's frame of reference, it is seven years; in human terms, it does not live a day.

## Extra Limbs

### Hind Leg Rule

*Tnan hatam: behema ba'alat hamesh raglayim, o she'ein la ela shalosh, harei ze mum* — the Mishnah elsewhere rules that an animal with five legs or only three legs has a *mum* and may not be brought as a *korban*. Rav Huna now extends this: *lo shanu ela shechisar v'yiter b'yad* — this *mum* ruling applies only to the foreleg. *aval chisar v'yiter b'regel* — if the hind leg is missing or extra, it is not merely a *mum* but a *treifa*.

A missing hind leg making an animal a *treifa* is already established, since a dislocated hind leg does so. But an *extra* hind leg as a *treifa*? That requires explanation.

### Yiter Principle

*Kol yiter k'natul dami* — anything extra is considered as if it were missing. Therefore, an animal with an extra hind leg is treated as though it were missing a hind leg, and a missing hind leg is a *treifa*.

### Rishonic Problem

This ruling generated significant difficulty for the *rishonim*, who had clear empirical evidence of animals with extra limbs living and not dying as *treifos* are supposed to die. The Rashba suggests a possible resolution: perhaps the principle of *yiter k'natul* does not operate on the same basis as other *treifos*. All other *treifos* rest on the empirical claim that such animals cannot survive. *yiter k'natul* is instead a legal-conceptual principle — extra counts as missing — which does not necessarily carry the prediction of death. Under this reading, the animal may well survive; it is still forbidden as a *treifa* because the legal construct applies. The Rashba himself, however, is not fully satisfied with this resolution and acknowledges the empirical problem remains serious.

### Sani D'Avi

*Hahi chayuta d'havu lah tartei sani d'evi* — there was an animal with two of what is called *sani d'evi*, a chamber near the first stomach where waste accumulates. The case was brought before Ravina, who declared it a *treifa* — *mi-dei d'Rav Huna*, on the basis of Rav Huna's principle: since a missing *sani d'evi* would make it a *treifa*, an extra one does as well.

### Stomach Tubes

However: *v'ei shaphichan l'hadadi, kesherah* — if the fluids flow between the two chambers, it is kosher. When the two chambers are hydraulically continuous, they are effectively one, and there is no extra chamber to invoke *yiter k'natul*.

A subsequent case: *hahu gufsa d'hava nafka mibei kassi l'huvelila* — there was a tube connecting the *beit hakosot* (the second or third stomach) to the next stomach. Rav Ashi wanted to declare it a *treifa*. Huna Mar bar Chiya told him: *kol hanei chivei b'varai hachi it lehu* — this is common in animals; it does not indicate a defect.

## Dual Rectums

### Nassan Testimony

*Heid Nassan bar Shila Rav Tavchaya di'Tzippori lifnei Rebbi* — Rav Nassan bar Shila, who served as head butcher in Tzippori, testified before Rebbe: *al shenei benei me'ayim hayotzim min habeheimah ke'echad she'hi tereifah* — if an animal has two large intestines whose waste exits at once, meaning the animal has two rectums, it is a *treifa*. This is striking given that the Gemara elsewhere establishes that certain problems with the rectum — holes in it, for instance — do not constitute a *treifa*. Nevertheless, a fully duplicated exit is treated differently. *u'kenegedam b'of kesherah* — the equivalent in a bird, however, is kosher.

### Exit Proximity

*Bameh devarim amurim sheyotzim bishnei mekomot* — this *treifa* ruling applies only when the two intestines exit through two separate openings. *aval yotzim bemakom echad vekalyan ad ke'etzba kesherah* — but if they exit through the same place and converge to the width of one *etzba*, it is kosher.

### Fusion Debate

*Palgi bah Rav Ami ve'Rav Assi* — Rav Ami and Rav Assi debate what it means for the two to share a single exit. One opinion: *ha d'hadrei ve'arbei* — the two tubes actually reconnect internally, inside the animal, before reaching the exterior, and therefore constitute a single continuous passage. The other opinion: *af al gav d'lo hadrei ve'arbei* — even if they do not reconnect internally, if they exit in such proximity that together they span only an *etzba* in width, that suffices to render it kosher.

The Gemara notes the internal tension: the first opinion integrates naturally with the language *ad ke'etzba* — the tubes reconnect inside and thus exit as a single passage of *etzba* width. The second opinion must interpret *ad ke'etzba* differently: the two separate exits lie side by side — not one below the other — and together measure no more than an *etzba* across. The case of the tube connecting the *beit hakosot* to the *kares* (first stomach) — *hahu gufsa d'hava ma'avra mibei kassi l'karsa* — Mar bar Rav Ashi wanted to permit this by analogy to the previously permitted tube connecting the second and third stomachs. Rav Oshaya rejected the analogy: *atu kulhu b'chada machta machtinhu?* — do you think all cases are decided in the same batch? *heicha d'itmar itmar, heicha d'lo itmar lo itmar* — where it was taught, it was taught; where it was not taught, it was not taught. The first tube was explicitly identified as common and therefore kosher; this tube was not, and the absence of such identification means it is treated as an aberration and constitutes a *treifa*.

The discussion of the principle that extra is like missing — *yiter k'natul* — thus continues to generate challenging applications, and as noted, it has posed significant difficulties for the *rishonim* that remain without fully satisfying resolution.
