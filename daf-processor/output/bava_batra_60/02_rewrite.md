# Bava Batra 60 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Perek Shift

### Daf 60 Intro

Daf 60 is the final daf of *Chazkas Habatim*. Whatever one's feelings about leaving it behind, *Chazkas Habatim* is almost certainly the most intensive of the *prakim* in Bava Batra — the one that is classically studied in the *yeshivos* — and it has been a demanding and rewarding unit.

### Chazakah to Rights

The *mishnayot* at the opening of daf 60 represent something of a thematic shift. Rather than the third *perek*'s central concern with the three-year *chazakah* — demonstrating ownership through sustained possession — these *mishnayot* belong more naturally to the second *perek*'s concerns: the *chazakah* of rights of use, questions about neighbors, and whether one person's activities invade another's space. The underlying dynamic is familiar: if I am doing something that potentially encroaches upon your space or affects you, does your silence constitute acquiescence, effectively waiving your rights and granting me those privileges?

## Doors & Courtyards

### Lakach Bayis Rule

The *mishnah* addresses the rules governing a shared courtyard among several houses. In particular, it prohibits opening a house from another courtyard directly into the shared courtyard — for instance, if the back of a house in one courtyard faces a second courtyard, one may not cut a back door that creates new access into that shared space. The members of the courtyard can clearly object: you are not entitled to increase the foot traffic through their shared domain.

The *mishnah* also specifies what one *may* do. The end of the *mishnah* states: *ella im ratzah boneh et hachedar lifnim mibeito boneh aliyah al gabei beito uphotchah letoch beito* — if one wishes, one may build a back addition to one's house, and then build a second story upon that addition, as long as the second story opens into one's own house rather than into the courtyard. The immediate difficulty is obvious: why does this help anything? Whoever lives in the second story will come down into the addition and then exit through the original door into the courtyard. The number of people using the courtyard may well increase regardless.

### Foot Traffic

The *gemara* opens by asking *mai ta'ama* — what is the reason for the prohibition? The answer: *mipnei shemarbeh aleihem et haderech* — because you increase the foot traffic, the *drichas haregel*, through the courtyard. The *gemara* then notes the apparent internal tension: *eima seifa* — consider the end of the *mishnah*. Even in the permitted case of building a back addition and a second story, does one not equally increase the foot traffic? *Ve-halo marbe alav et haderech* — is one not still increasing the burden of passage? The shift from the plural *aleihem* to the singular *alav* is slightly puzzling; following the Rashbam, the phrase is read as referring to increased traffic in the courtyard.

### Addition & Loft

Rav Huna resolves the tension by proposing a fundamentally different reading of the *mishnah*. He argues that the *mishnah* does not actually permit building a new back addition at all. *Mai cheder shechilko bishnayim* — when the *mishnah* speaks of building a "back room," it means subdividing one's existing house in two, not constructing a new addition. And *umai aliyah, aliyah aftaya* — when the *mishnah* speaks of building a "second story," it does not mean literally constructing a new upper floor, but rather subdividing the existing interior space vertically — something like a loft, dividing a high-ceilinged room into two levels rather than erecting an entirely new structure.

### Rav Huna Subdivision

The logic of Rav Huna's distinction is subtle but coherent. There is no rule in a shared courtyard that limits how many guests one may invite into one's home. If I choose to host my extended family for six months, no one in the courtyard can complain that additional foot traffic will result — that is within my rights as a resident. What I am *not* permitted to do is introduce new *residents* of the courtyard, new right-holders with independent claims to the shared space.

Given this, the question becomes: what is the legal line between guests and residents? Rav Huna's answer is structural rather than intentional. If I subdivide my existing house, I am not doing anything beyond what I could theoretically have done by filling my house with guests. The total capacity of the space is fixed; I am merely rearranging it. But if I construct a new addition or a true second story, that represents the creation of new residential capacity beyond anything that could have been treated as guest space — and that is prohibited.

### Doorways vs Intent

One might have supposed the *pshat* of the *mishnah* operates differently. The *mishnah* seems to focus most naturally on doorways: the concern is whether you are creating new points of entry into the courtyard, not whether you are adding square footage. On this reading, as long as all residents — however many — use the same door, the courtyard partners have no grounds for complaint, because the number of doors is the relevant legal measure of traffic exposure. That measure is admittedly somewhat arbitrary, but it is simple and workable. It also maps naturally onto practical reality: people generally do not subdivide and rent space without providing a separate entrance.

Rav Huna, by contrast, frames the distinction as subdivision versus new construction. A third possible line — whether you are charging rent or not — was not the approach the *gemara* adopted, though it might have seemed the most intuitive basis for distinguishing guests from tenants.

What emerges from the discussion is a three-way set of possible criteria: the number of doors (the apparent *pshat* of the *mishnah*), the nature of the construction (Rav Huna's reading), or the economic relationship of the new occupants. The *gemara* seems to favor Rav Huna's structural criterion, even if the Rashbam's reading of the *mishnah* — that subdivision of any kind is permitted regardless of intent or scale — leads to results that seem counterintuitive when pushed to extremes.

## Windows & Privacy

### Facing Doors/Windows

The next *mishnah* addresses a different dimension of the shared courtyard: *lo yiftach adam lachatzar hashutafin petach keneged petach vechalon keneged chalon* — one may not open a doorway or window directly facing the doorway or window of a neighbor in the shared courtyard. This *mishnah* apparently assumes a situation in which one does have a right to open windows into the courtyard — perhaps by virtue of a *chazakah* or an arrangement among the courtyard's residents. Even within that permission, however, directly opposing windows are forbidden. The concern is *hezek re'iyah* — the harm of visual intrusion, the damage caused by being seen in one's private domain.

Interestingly, the prohibition extends even to doorways. One might have thought that doorways present little privacy concern — by the time someone opens a door, they are presumably about to leave, and their body fills the frame. Yet the *mishnah* prohibits facing doorways as well, since a person standing just inside an open door can observe a significant portion of the courtyard without being easily noticed.

### Door Size & Division

The *mishnah* further rules: *haya katan lo ya'asenu gadol* — if one had a small doorway, one may not enlarge it; and *echad lo ya'asenu shnayim* — one may not divide a single doorway into two. These prohibitions raise immediate questions: what exactly is the harm? The *gemara* will provide two competing frameworks for understanding them.

### Reshut Harabim Exception

An important exception applies when houses open directly onto the *reshut harabim*: in that case, *petach keneged petach vechalon keneged chalon* is permitted, as is enlarging or multiplying doorways. The reasoning is that a house already opening onto the public domain cannot assume any privacy in the first place — the *reshut harabim* carries constant foot traffic and public exposure. One additional neighbor across the street with a facing door adds nothing to the visual exposure that already exists. Practically, this reasoning assumes single-story structures and the absence of electric lighting, since in a pre-modern context, once darkness fell, the *reshut harabim* ceased to function as a public thoroughfare and privacy was naturally restored. In taller modern buildings with artificial lighting, the calculus would clearly differ.

### Bilam & Tents

The *gemara* asks *mena hani mili* — what is the scriptural source for the prohibition of facing windows and doors? Rabbi Yochanan cites the verse: *vayisa Bilam et einav vayar Yisrael shochen lishvatav* — Bilam lifted his eyes and saw Israel dwelling according to their tribes. And he exclaimed, *ma tovu ohalecha Yaakov* — how goodly are your tents, O Jacob. What exactly did Bilam see that moved him to this praise? *Ra she'ein pitchei ohaleihem mechovanim zeh lazeh* — he saw that their tent doorways were not aligned facing one another, that they had arranged their encampment so as to protect one another's privacy. *Amar re'uyim halalu shetishreh aleihem Shechina* — he declared: these people are worthy of having the Divine Presence rest upon them.

Tosafot notes the connection to the end of the *pasuk*, which records that the *ruach Elokim* rested upon Bilam at that moment — as though his perception of Israel's *tzniyut* drew down upon him, too, a measure of that same divine inspiration. There is something deeper here as well: the *Beit HaMikdash* itself — the dwelling place of the *Shechina* — is characterized above all by careful gradations of access and privacy, from the *parochet* inward to the *kodesh kodashim*. A community whose homes embody the same principle of protected interiority becomes, in a sense, worthy of that divine indwelling. Each tent, arranged to honor its neighbor's privacy, becomes in its own right a place where the *Shechina* might rest.

### Rabbah's Property View

The *gemara* then turns to Rabbah bar Bar Chana's interpretation of the prohibition against enlarging a doorway. He understood the concern as a property rights issue rather than a privacy issue, drawing on the rule (established earlier in the tractate) that when a shared courtyard is divided, each resident receives a portion proportional to the width of his doorway — with a minimum of four by four *amos*. On this view, if one had a doorway four *amos* wide, one received a four-by-four allocation; enlarging to eight *amos* would mean claiming an eight-by-four share of the courtyard upon division. The prohibition against enlargement thus protects the other partners from having their shares diminished. Consequently, Rabbah bar Bar Chana reasoned, enlarging a doorway from two *amos* to four would be permitted — since even with a two-*amah* doorway one was already entitled to a four-by-four minimum, so no additional property claim was being asserted.

### Rava Privacy Concern

Rava rejected this property-based framework. He argued: *matzina litznu'ei minach bepitcha zutra; bepitcha raba lo matzina litznu'ei minach* — with your narrow doorway, I could conduct myself privately in the courtyard, knowing the limits of your line of sight; with a wider doorway, you can survey a larger portion of the courtyard from within your home without my being aware that you are doing so. This is a straightforward *hezek re'iyah* argument: the width of the doorway determines not just who passes through, but the angle and extent of view from inside. Any widening, even from four *amos* to five, partially diminishes the privacy available to others in the courtyard. This reading is also far more consonant with the plain meaning of the *mishnah*, which is clearly concerned with *hezek re'iyah* throughout.

### One vs Two Doors

The same debate plays out regarding the prohibition against converting one doorway into two. Rami bar Chama read this too as a property rights issue: if one's doorway was four *amos* wide and one divided it into two doorways of two *amos* each, one would claim two separate four-by-four allocations upon division — eight by four in total rather than the original four by four, effectively doubling one's property claim. He therefore concluded that dividing an eight-*amah* doorway into two doorways of four each would be permitted, since the total property claim (*tamnei* by four) remains identical.

Rava again objected on privacy grounds: *bechad pitcha matzina itznu'ei minach; bitrei pitchi lo matzina itznu'ei minach* — with a single large doorway, one knows when it is open and can adjust accordingly; with two smaller doorways, one may be watching through one while the other appears closed. Two separate openings provide superior ability to observe the courtyard covertly, and therefore any such division is prohibited regardless of the symmetry of the property claims involved. The exception for houses opening onto the *reshut harabim* follows naturally: since no privacy can be assumed in a space already exposed to the public domain, there is no *hezek re'iyah* to protect against.

## Tunnels & Safety

### No Tunnels

The *mishnah* now shifts to a different category of restriction involving the *reshut harabim*: *ein osin chalal tachat reshut harabim* — one may not dig a tunnel beneath the public domain. Even if one somehow holds property rights to the space underground, the excavation creates a hazard to those passing above: the ceiling of the tunnel may collapse under the weight of traffic. Similarly, one may not dig a *bor*, *shich*, or *me'arah* — a cistern, trench, or cave — beneath the *reshut harabim*, even if one's excavation begins within one's own property and continues under the public domain in order to access water.

### Eliezer Exception

Rabbi Eliezer permits such construction under one condition: *kedei shetehei agalah mehalechet utuna avanim* — the ceiling of the tunnel must be structurally capable of supporting a fully loaded stone wagon passing overhead. If the structure can bear that weight, the safety concern is addressed and the excavation is permitted.

### Maintenance Issue

The Rabbis' response, as the *gemara* elsewhere elaborates, is that even a ceiling sufficiently strong at the time of construction may become problematic over time: *zimnin demifchit velav adateih* — cracks and fissures develop, and no one is necessarily monitoring the structure's integrity. Since there is no appointed guardian of the tunnel's continued safety, the initial robustness provides insufficient assurance over the long term. It is better not to create the hazard at all than to rely on a structural standard that may not be maintained.

## Wall Protrusions

### Zizim Prohibition

The *mishnah* continues: *ein motzin zizin vegustra'ot lireshut harabim* — one may not extend beams and protrusions from the wall of one's house or courtyard into the *reshut harabim*. Such protrusions were functionally useful — for hanging items, for example — but they constitute a hazard to passersby.

### Inset Wall Solution

If one wishes to have protrusions, the *mishnah* offers a solution: *kones letokh shelo umotzi* — draw your wall inward from the property line, build it set back within your own land, and then extend the protrusions outward, keeping them entirely within your own space rather than allowing them to reach into the *reshut harabim*.

### Bought Courtyard

A practical question arises: *lakach chatzer uvah zizin vegustra'ot* — what if one purchases a courtyard whose walls already feature protrusions that extend toward the *reshut harabim*? In such a case the *halacha* is *harei zo bechezkatah* — it is presumed to be in order. Perhaps the original builder set the wall inward and the protrusions remain within the property boundary; since the facts are unknown to the purchaser, the existing situation is assumed to be legitimate.

The Rashbam notes, however, that this presumption applies only to the *purchaser*. The original builder cannot avail himself of the same logic by arguing that since no one complained for several years, the arrangement must be valid — a *chazakah* of silence. The Rashbam explains: the silence of the *rabbim* does not constitute *mechilah*. The *reshut harabim* belongs to the public collectively, and any individual member may raise an objection. To establish genuine consent would require the entire community to act in concert — a *shiva tuvei ha-ir* or equivalent communal body acting formally. Silent acquiescence by diffuse members of the public is insufficient to create rights. Therefore the original owner retains the burden of proof that the protrusions remain within his boundary. But the purchaser, who acquired in good faith with the situation as he found it, may rely on the presumption of legitimacy.

### Yannai & Ammi

The *gemara* presents two parallel stories that illuminate the distinction between a *mavoi* and the *reshut harabim*. Rebbi Ammi had a protrusion (*ziza*) extending from his wall into a *mavoi* — a semi-private alley serving a defined number of courtyards. Another man had a protrusion extending into the full *reshut harabim*. The people of the *reshut harabim* brought a complaint against this second man — and the case came before Rebbi Ammi himself. One might question whether he should have recused himself. He did not.

Rebbi Ammi ruled: *zil kots* — go cut down your protrusion. The man protested: but you also have a protrusion, into the *mavoi*. Rebbi Ammi replied: *didi lemavoi mafik; benei mavoi machlin gabai* — mine extends into the *mavoi*, and the residents of the *mavoi* are a defined, bounded group who have acquiesced. There, *mechilah* is possible, because the population is known and their collective silence is meaningful. *Didach lireshut harabim mafik; man machil gabach* — yours extends into the *reshut harabim*, which is open to all; there is no bounded group capable of granting consent, and no individual's silence can waive the rights of the public.

### Tree Story

A parallel story: Rebbi Yannai had a tree whose branches overhung the *reshut harabim*. Another man similarly had an overhanging tree, and the people of the *reshut harabim* brought a complaint against him. The man came before Rebbi Yannai — and one notices the *gemara* uses the singular *ata*, suggesting the defendant chose his venue deliberately, aware that Rebbi Yannai was in the same position.

Rebbi Yannai said to him: I am occupied today — come back tomorrow. That night, Rebbi Yannai sent someone to cut down his own tree. The next morning, he ruled: *zil kots* — go cut down yours. The man objected: you also had a tree overhanging the *reshut harabim*. Rebbi Yannai replied: *zil chazei — ei ka-its didi, kots didach; ve-i lo ka-its didi, lo tikots* — go and see: if my tree has been cut, cut yours; if not, do not. They went and looked, and his had been cut.

The *gemara* then asks: why did Rebbi Yannai have to resort to this strategy? Why not simply say: cut yours, and I will cut mine immediately afterward? And further, why did Rebbi Yannai have the tree to begin with?

On the latter point: *me'ikara savar nicha lehu livenei reshut harabim d'yasvi betula* — originally he had assumed the public preferred having a shaded walkway and would appreciate the tree's canopy. When he saw the complaint against the other man, he realized that their silence toward him had been only out of deference, not genuine preference — *keivan de-chaza de-ka me'akevei, shada vekatzya* — once he saw they were in fact objecting, he cut it down.

### Self-Criticism First

As for the stratagem of cutting his own tree overnight before ruling on the other man's: this was out of deference to the principle stated by Reish Lakish — *hitkosheshu vekhoshu*. Reish Lakish reads the verb *hitkosheshu* as related to *kashot*, to adorn or beautify: *kashot atzmecha* — beautify yourself first — *ve'achar kashot acheirim* — and only then tell others to do the same. Before criticizing or directing someone else to correct their behavior, ensure that your own conduct is in order. The Rashbam's formulation — *haseir kash mibein einecha techilah* — draws on the same image of removing the splinter from one's own eye before attending to another's.

Rebbi Yannai had not acted in bad faith — he had genuinely believed the public appreciated the shade. But before directing someone else to remove what was improperly in *reshut harabim*, he wanted first to have visibly put his own situation right. The principle is a powerful one: not merely avoiding hypocrisy, but ensuring one's credibility and moral authority before exercising it on others.

### Konas Debate

The *gemara* then raises a further question: *konas ve-lo hotzi mahu sheyachzor ve-yotzi* — if one drew one's wall inward from the property line (the *konas* step, setting the wall back within one's own land) but then did not proceed to install any protrusions, has one waived one's right to later extend protrusions? Or does one retain that right since the space is still one's property?

### Restore Wall?

This debate is the occasion for a broader disagreement between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish, though — as the *gemara* notes — the attributions are reversed from what one might initially expect. The question at the heart of the dispute is not actually about the protrusions but about whether, having drawn the wall inward and thereby allowed the public to walk on the exposed strip, one may later restore the wall to its original position.

### Yaakov Resolution

Rabbi Yaakov clarifies the terms of the debate for Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Tachlifa: *le-hotzi, kulei alma lo pleigi demotzi* — everyone agrees that if one drew the wall inward with the intention of extending protrusions, one may install those protrusions at a later time; that is still one's property and no rights have been waived. *Ki pleigi, le-hachzir ktalim limkonam* — the dispute concerns whether one may fully restore the wall to its original position, reclaiming the strip of land over which the public has been walking.

### Public Passage Rights

Rabbi Yochanan rules *eino machzir* — one may not restore the wall. His reasoning is based on the principle of Rabbi Yehuda: *meitzar shehecheziku bo rabbim assur lekalkelo* — a path over which the public has established passage rights may not be destroyed, even if it is technically one's own property. The wall was drawn in; the public began traversing the newly opened strip; they thereby acquired a right of passage over it. The owner retains the right to have protrusions (since the space is still his property), but cannot take back what the public's established use has effectively encumbered. The result is an unusual coexistence: property rights sufficient for protrusions, but passage rights sufficient to prevent full reclamation.

### Reish Lakish View

Reish Lakish rules *machzir* — one may restore the wall. He reads Rabbi Yehuda's principle as applying only when the public has no alternative passage: *hani mili heicha de-leika revacha* — the prohibition against destroying an established path applies when, without that path, passage is impossible. *Hacha ika revacha* — here, the full *reshut harabim* remains available; one is merely reclaiming one's own strip of land without blocking anyone's passage. Since the public retains adequate thoroughfare, the owner's full property rights may be reasserted.

## Zecher L'Churban

### Plaster Prohibition

The *gemara* now transitions to a *baraita* about *zecher le-churban* — the obligation to maintain a tangible memorial to the destruction of the *Beit HaMikdash*: *lo yasud adam et beito besid* — one may not plaster one's house with clean white *sid* (lime plaster), since this constitutes beautifying one's home in a manner incompatible with ongoing mourning for the *Mikdash*. However, *ve-im erev bo chol o teven mutar* — if one mixes sand or straw into the plaster, dulling and roughening its appearance, that is permitted. Rabbi Yehuda adds a nuance: mixing sand produces a harder, superior finish (*tarsid*) and is therefore prohibited; mixing straw merely dulls the color and is permitted.

### Existing Plaster OK

A parallel case: *lakach chatzer mesuyedet mechuyeret mefuyachet, harei zo bechezkatah* — if one purchases a courtyard whose walls already bear fine plaster, carvings, or decorative finishes, one need not destroy them; the existing state is presumed valid and may be maintained. But *nafla* — if the structure falls, *eino chozer u-voneh otah* — one may not rebuild it with the same level of ornamentation. Building anew carries the fresh obligation of *zecher le-churban*. The *gemara* draws an explicit distinction between this case and the earlier one of courtyard protrusions: for the protrusions, the question was one of monetary property rights, and rights survive even after reconstruction. For the plastering, the question is one of religious prohibition (*issura*), and the obligation applies fresh whenever one undertakes new construction.

### Ascetics

The *baraita* continues: after the destruction of the second *Beit HaMikdash*, *rabu prushim beYisrael* — ascetics multiplied among the Jewish people, who resolved *shelo le'echol basar veshelo lishtot yayin* — not to eat meat or drink wine. Rabbi Yehoshua engaged with them.

### Yehoshua's Logic

He asked: *mipney mah i atem ochlin basar ve-ein atem shosin yayin* — why have you adopted this practice? They replied: should we eat meat, which was once offered on the *mizbe'ach* and is now no longer brought? Should we drink wine, which was poured as a *nisuch* on the *mizbe'ach* and is now ceased? Their practice was a living *zecher le-churban* — every day was, for them, the equivalent of the Nine Days.

Rabbi Yehoshua pressed the reductio: if so, *lechem nami lo nochal* — we should not eat bread either, since the *menachot* of flour have also ceased. They replied: we can manage on fruit. He continued: *peiros lo nochal* — we should not eat fruit, since *bikkurim* are no longer brought. They replied: we can eat fruits of other species not included in the *shivas haminim*. He pressed further: *mayim lo nishteh* — should we not drink water, since the *nisuch hamayim* on *Sukkot* has also ceased? *Shatku* — they were silent. There was no answer. Tosafot notes the possibility of surviving on fruit juice, but the rhetorical point stands: carried to its logical conclusion, the ascetic program is unsustainable.

### Balanced Mourning

Rabbi Yehoshua therefore articulated the proper balance: *shelo lehitabel kol ikkar i efshar* — not to mourn at all is impossible; the decree has been given and must be acknowledged. *Ule-hitabel yoter midai i efshar* — but to be overwhelmed by mourning is equally impossible, *she-ein gozrin gezeirah al hatzibbur ela im ken rov hatzibbur yecholin la'amod bah* — for a communal enactment is only valid if the majority of the community can bear it. The verse cited — *bim'eira atem ne'arim ve-oti atem kovim hagoy kullo* — indicates that communal acceptance requires that *kol hagoy* be capable of compliance.

### Specific Measures

The right approach, the *Sages* teach, is: *sad adam et beito besid umeshayer bo davar mu'at* — plaster your house, but leave a small space unplastered. Rav Yosef: the unplastered area should be one *amah* by one *amah*. Rav Chisda: it should be *keneged hapetach* — opposite the doorway, where it will be visible to all who enter. Similarly, one may prepare a full and generous meal, but leave over a small amount — *kasa deharsana*, a small portion of fish hash, suggests Rav Papa, just something left on the table as a reminder. A woman may wear all her jewelry and adornments, but leave one small thing undone — *bat tzida*, the temples, leaving the customary application of cosmetics there incomplete.

### Wedding Ashes

These practices are anchored in the verse: *im eshkachech Yerushalayim tishkach yemini; tidbak leshoni lechikki im lo ezkerechi* — if I forget you, Jerusalem, let my right hand lose its cunning; let my tongue cleave to my palate if I do not remember you. The point is not permanent self-denial but structured remembrance: we get on with the fullness of life and joy, but build into every aspect of that joy a moment of conscious memory. *Al rosh simchati* — at the very apex of my rejoicing, Jerusalem stands. Rav Yitzchak identifies this as the practice of placing *eifer mikleh* — ashes from burning — at the head of the groom at his wedding, at the very moment of greatest personal joy. Rav Papa asked Abaye: where precisely is this placed? *Bimkom tefillin* — where the *tefillin* are worn, as the verse indicates: *lasum le-aveilei Tziyon latet lahem pe'er tachat eifer* — to give the mourners of Zion glory (*pe'er*, which is the word for *tefillin*) in place of ashes. At the apex of a wedding — the moment of *rosh simchah*, the head of joy — the ashes stand where glory will one day return.

This is, of course, the same impulse that lies behind the breaking of the glass at the wedding's conclusion: a moment of *zecher le-churban* precisely as the joy reaches its height.

### Mourning Promise

The *gemara* cites the verse: *simchu et Yerushalayim ve-gilu vah kol ohaveha; sisu itah masos kol hamitablim aleha* — rejoice with Jerusalem and be glad for her, all who love her; exult with her in joy, all who mourn for her. *Ve-chol hamitabel al Yerushalayim, zocheh ve-ro'eh besimchatah* — whoever mourns for Jerusalem will merit to witness her rejoicing. The mourning is not futile or merely retrospective; it is a form of investment in the future redemption.

### Stricter vs Capable

A *tanna* then pushes the other direction: Rabbi Yishmael ben Elisha states that in principle, from the day the *Beit HaMikdash* was destroyed, *din hu she-nigzor al atzmenu she-lo le'echol basar veshelo lishtot yayin* — it would be right to decree upon ourselves the abstention from meat and wine. Yet the law stands: *ein gozrin gezeirah al hatzibbur ela im ken rov hatzibbur yecholin la'amod bah* — a decree the community cannot bear is not binding. The community cannot sustain full asceticism, and therefore the moderate approach is the law.

### Persecution View

Rabbi Yishmael ben Elisha goes further, turning to the particular darkness of Roman persecution: *mi-yom she-pashtah malchut ha-risha'ah* — from the time the evil kingdom spread its decrees, forbidding Torah study and *bris milah*, it would logically follow — *din hu she-nigzor al atzmenu she-lo lisa isha ve-she-lo leholid banim* — that we should decree upon ourselves not to marry and not to have children, since children born under such conditions cannot be raised in Torah and *mitzvot*, and *nimtza zaro shel Avraham kaleh me-eilav* — the seed of Avraham would thus end by itself rather than by the enemy's hand.

The parallel to the *midrash* about Amram — who ceased having children under Pharaoh's decree, until Miriam pointed out that his own decree was harsher than Pharaoh's — is striking. Here, however, the *gemara* appears to endorse the sentiment, at least in principle, before drawing back. *Ela hanichu lahem le-Yisrael mutav she-yihyu shogegin ve-al yihyu mezidin* — let Israel be; better they transgress unknowingly than that they transgress deliberately. Since people will marry regardless of any such decree, it is better not to make the decree formal, lest everyone become a willful transgressor.

This is a strange and sobering conclusion to the *perek*. The *gemara* seems to accept the logic that brings one to the brink of recommending communal self-dissolution, while pulling back only on practical grounds. The underlying tension — between the imperative of *zecher le-churban* and the imperative of communal continuity — is left unresolved, as perhaps it must be. The *perek* ends here, and the next begins.
