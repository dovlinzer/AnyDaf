# Taanit 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Crop Blight & Immediacy

### New Topics Begin

The nineteenth daf of *Ta'anit* opens a new *perek*, beginning at the very bottom of 18b. Having dealt at length with the laws surrounding delayed rainfall, the *mishnah* now shifts to related but distinct causes for communal fasting and prayer — situations that follow a similar halakhic structure but arise from different sources of distress.

### Tzmachim She-shanu

The *mishnah* opens with the rule of *tzmachim she-shanu*: if sprouts or crops have visibly changed in their growth pattern — some form of blight, infestation, or aberration in development — this is not a situation where one waits patiently through the graduated process of three weeks of *yechidim* fasting, then three weeks of *tzibur* fasting, and then the seven-week period of *matri'in*. Rather, *matri'in aleihem miyad* — you respond immediately with the full intensified prayer service. As noted earlier in the tractate, there is a debate about what *matri'in* means: whether it refers to the *shofar* blast, the *anenu* prayer, or both. But whatever its precise content, the term carries with it the full package of the special twenty-four-*brachah* version of *shemoneh esrei*, the taking of the *aron* into the courtyard, and the accompanying penitential liturgy. All of that is done at once, without delay.

### Arbaim Yom & Batzoret

Similarly, if rain fell at its proper time but there then follows a forty-day gap — *bein geshem l'geshem arbaim yom* — during which no further rain descends, this too is immediately severe. The crops have begun to grow, they are now dependent on continued moisture, and a prolonged dry spell at that stage may be even more damaging than if rain had never come at all. The *mishnah* designates this a *makat batzoret* — a smiting of *batzoret*, a condition of drought and insufficiency — and the response is likewise *matri'in aleihem* without waiting.

### Batzoret vs Ra'av

The *mishnah* draws a distinction between *batzoret* and *ra'av*. Both relate to a shortage of rain and its agricultural consequences, but *ra'av* — famine — is the more severe condition. The *gemara* will elaborate on this distinction later, but already the *mishnah* signals that there is a spectrum of severity, and that the forty-day gap without rain, while serious enough to trigger an immediate response, falls into the category of *batzoret* rather than the more extreme *ra'av*.

### Timeline Calculation

A natural question arises regarding the three-three-and-seven structure described earlier in the tractate. If one calculates the timeline: three weeks of *yechidim* fasting is roughly ten and a half days of actual fast days, followed by another three weeks of *tzibur* fasting, and then the seven-week intensified period — the total comes to approximately sixty days from the start of the process to the end. More precisely, the arrival at the *matri'in* stage occurs after roughly thirty-five days of accumulated fast days. The point being that a forty-day gap between rains, occurring after rain had already fallen, reaches the same threshold of severity — indeed arguably exceeds it in urgency — that the ordinary calendar-based process only reaches after many weeks. Hence the *miyad*. One sharp observation worth noting: if the three-three-and-seven sequence begins in the appropriate season, it would in some years arrive squarely at *Chanukah*, raising interesting but unresolved questions about how the sequence interacts with the festival calendar. The *gemara* does not appear to address this directly.

## Localized Drought

### Ir Shelo Yardu

The *mishnah* then introduces the case of *ir shelo yardu aleiha geshamim* — a city upon which rain did not fall, even while surrounding cities received rainfall. This scenario is not merely theoretical; the *pasuk* in *Amos* explicitly describes God sending rain upon one city but withholding it from another, even upon one portion of a field while another portion goes dry. One might think that if the neighboring cities have adequate rain, the affected city can simply purchase grain from them, and the situation, while uncomfortable, is not so dire as to require a communal response from the region as a whole.

### Tanna Kamma Position

The *tanna kamma* rules otherwise. *Ota ha-ir mit'ana u'matra'at* — that city itself fasts and does the blasts, engaging the full intensified response. *V'chol svivoteha mit'anot v'lo matriot* — the surrounding cities fast as well, but do not engage in the special *tfilot*, the elevated *matri'in* service. Pseudo-*Rashi* explains this partial participation: if one city in a region lacks rain, it will be compelled to purchase grain from the surrounding cities. This creates scarcity and economic pressure throughout the region, even if the neighboring cities received ample rain. The affected city's crisis becomes a regional concern.

### Rabbi Akiva Omer

*Rabbi Akiva* takes a striking contrary position: *matriot v'lo mit'anot*. The surrounding cities perform the elevated *tfilot* — the *matri'in* service — but do not fast. This is counterintuitive given the entire structure of the tractate, where fasting always precedes and underlies the *matri'in* service, and where *matri'in* appears only at the most extreme stage of the process. How could the surrounding cities do the higher-level response without the lower-level one?

### Akiva's Logic

The key to understanding *Rabbi Akiva* lies in the nature of the *ta'anit* itself. Fasting is not merely a technique for moving God; it is an expression and intensification of one's own suffering. The community in the affected city is already suffering — their crops are failing, their livelihood is threatened — and fasting is the bodily enactment of that distress: *we have no rain, look how we suffer, we are not even eating*. The fast amplifies and renders visible the reality of the affliction they are already enduring. For the surrounding cities, however, there is as yet no suffering. A *ta'anit* would be a false expression — they have not been struck by anything. What they can appropriately do is pray, because they are genuinely concerned about what may befall them and about the situation of their neighbors. According to *Rabbi Akiva*, *tefilla* and *ta'anit* are separable, and the latter is only appropriate where there is actual suffering to express. This offers a speculative but coherent reading of his position. Notably, it is not until this point in the *mishnah* that we encounter the possibility of *tefilla* without *ta'anit* as a legitimate halakhic category.

### Dever O Mapolet

The *mishnah* extends the same framework to other local disasters: *ir she'yeish bah dever o mapolet* — a city afflicted with pestilence or with collapsed structures. Again, the affected city performs the full response, and the surrounding cities participate in a lesser way — and again, *Rabbi Akiva* maintains his position that the surrounding cities do the *tfilot* without the fasting. What might explain why collapsing structures affect surrounding cities at all? One possibility, offered tentatively, is that if there is a medical emergency with many people dead or injured, demands will be made on the resources and personnel of neighboring communities. Alternatively — and this is a genuinely intriguing suggestion raised in discussion — collapsed structures in the ancient Near Eastern context may well reflect seismic activity, and earthquakes in *Eretz Yisrael* have historically been followed by successive tremors, making the threat very much a regional one. The tractate of *Berachot* does deal with blessings recited over earthquakes, which suggests that seismic events were familiar enough to warrant their own liturgical category.

### Pestilence Definition

The *mishnah* asks: *eizehu dever*? How does one determine that a city is actually afflicted with pestilence, as opposed to experiencing a series of unrelated deaths? The answer: a city that *hamotzia chamesh me'ot ragli* — that produces five hundred able-bodied soldiers, meaning a city of that population size — where three people have died on three successive days, *zeh achar zeh*, that is *dever*. The imagery is pointed: instead of the living and vigorous (*ragli*, foot soldiers) emerging from the city, it is the dead who come out. *Pachot mikan ein zeh dever* — fewer deaths relative to population, and it does not constitute pestilence. Similarly, even three deaths in three days would not qualify if the city were significantly larger, because the threshold is proportional.

This is, as one participant aptly noted, arguably the earliest recorded use of a mortality incidence rate in the history of epidemiology. The rabbis understood that a few deaths by themselves mean nothing; it is the ratio to population, combined with the concentration in time and the unusual nature of the deaths, that signals a communicable epidemic. Presumably, too, the rabbinic standard assumed additional observable signs — physical symptoms consistent with a recognized disease — not merely three unexplained deaths in isolation. The parallel to contemporary public health thresholds, and to the work of institutions like the CDC in determining when isolated deaths constitute a reportable outbreak, is striking.

## Universal Plagues

### Crop Diseases

Having established the framework of local disasters with regional participation, the *mishnah* now introduces a third category: *al elu matri'in b'chol makom* — afflictions that require a response not merely in the affected area and its surroundings, but everywhere. The first group consists of agricultural threats: *al hashidafon v'al hayerakon* — *shidafon* is some form of blight, and *yerakon* here refers not to the human disease of jaundice but to the yellowing and withering of crops. To these are added *al haarbeh v'al hachasil* — different species of locusts, and caterpillars. All are crop-destroying forces that do not remain stationary.

### Human Threats

The *mishnah* then shifts to threats against human life and safety: *v'al hachaya hara'ah* — dangerous wild animals — and *v'al hacherev* — the sword, meaning military forces marching through, besieging or ravaging populated areas. One cannot help noticing the resonance with the *makkot b'mitzrayim*: pestilence, crop destruction, wild animals, all appear in both lists.

### Mahalachet Concept

The unifying rationale for this universal response is *mipnei shehi makkah mehallechet* — because it is a plague that moves, that spreads. Locusts devastate one field and then move to the next. Crop diseases spread from plant to plant, region to region. Armed forces march through one territory and then advance to the next. Whatever strikes one location today will strike another tomorrow. This is why the response must be universal: the threat is not local, it is in transit toward everyone.

This immediately raises an interesting question about *dever*, pestilence, which appeared in the prior category. If anything should qualify as *mehallechet* — spreading — it would seem to be a communicable disease. Perhaps the rabbis of the *mishnah* period did not fully conceptualize the contagious mechanism of *dever* in the same way they recognized the visible spread of crop blight or a locust swarm. Or perhaps the category of *dever* as they defined it was understood differently from what we would call a pandemic pathogen. In any case, the *mishnah* places *dever* in the local category, not the universal one.

### Ashkelon Story

The *mishnah* offers a historical example: elders descended from *Yerushalayim* to their towns and decreed a *ta'anit* because *shidafon* — crop blight — the size of an oven's opening had been observed in *Ashkelon*. Depending on how one reads the narrative, either the elders were in *Yerushalayim* when they heard the news and implemented the fast upon returning to their home communities, or they traveled from *Ashkelon* to *Yerushalayim* and back. In either case, the *ta'anit* was declared throughout the region in response to what had appeared in *Ashkelon*. The geographic scope here is still within *Eretz Yisrael*, raising the question of where exactly the limits of *b'chol makom* lie.

### Wolves Story

A second example: a *ta'anit* was decreed because wolves had devoured two children in *Ever HaYarden*. *Rabbi Yose* adds that even if the wolves had merely been *sighted* in the settled areas — without yet attacking anyone — that alone would be sufficient cause to respond, since wild animals in human settlements represent a clear and present danger. Here again, the geographic reference is *Ever HaYarden*, which while across the Jordan is still closely connected to the Land of Israel and not distant in the way that Babylonia or elsewhere would be.

### Bechol Makom Limits

What precisely are the limits of *b'chol makom*? The *mishnah* does not define them, and this is a genuine lacuna. The question has two dimensions. The first is practical: at what distance does the probability of a threat reaching you become negligible? Something happening in one corner of a continent may simply never realistically arrive where you live. The second dimension is perceptual and relational: to what community do you understand yourself as belonging? How far does your sense of shared fate and shared responsibility extend? New Yorkers and Americans generally, it was observed, are often criticized for a provincial dismissiveness toward events happening elsewhere in the world — sometimes even when those events will demonstrably affect them economically or otherwise. The rabbis, working in a world of dramatically slower communication and more limited geographic awareness, nonetheless built into their halakha a principle that afflictions elsewhere concern you. But they did not specify how far elsewhere extends.

There is also a conceptual point that sharpens the distinction between the categories. In the local case — the city without rain, the city with *dever* or *mapolet* — the disaster is understood to be contained. It will not spread to the surrounding villages. Yet those surrounding villages still participate, at a reduced level, because they are proximate and their regional economy and communal fabric is affected. When the affliction is *mehallechet*, spreading, the surrounding communities participate at the full level because the threat will likely arrive at their doorstep. The gradation of response thus maps onto a gradation of both relatedness and realistic exposure to risk.

## Shabbat Exceptions

### Emergencies Listed

The *mishnah* now identifies a fourth and most extreme category: *al elu matri'in b'shabbat* — situations so urgent that the *matri'in* response is performed even on *Shabbat*. This is remarkable. The *shofar* is not blown on *Rosh Hashanah* when it falls on *Shabbat*, yet here, in certain emergencies, the *matri'in* service is performed on *Shabbat*. This may have been one reason the *gemara* earlier in the tractate was inclined to interpret *matri'in* as referring to *tefilla* rather than *shofar* blast — one cannot easily imagine blowing *shofar* on *Shabbat* even in an emergency that is not *pikuach nefesh* in the most immediate sense.

The three cases are: *ir shehekifuah nochrim* — a city surrounded by foreign troops, under siege; *o nahar* — a river threatening to overflow its banks; and *v'al hasfina hamitorefet bayam* — a ship caught in a storm at sea.

### Rabbi Yossi Omer

*Rabbi Yossi* draws a sharp distinction: *l'ezra v'lo l'tzeaka* — you may sound the alarm on *Shabbat* to call people to practical aid, but not as a *tzeaka*, a crying out to God. According to *Rabbi Yossi*, what distinguishes these three cases is not simply their severity, but the possibility of immediate human intervention. People can rally to defend a besieged city, can pile sandbags against a flooding river, can send rescue boats to a storm-tossed vessel. The *matri'in* on *Shabbat* is thus permitted as a practical alarm — *l'ezra* — not as an elevated prayer ritual.

### Tanna Kamma View

The *tanna kamma* apparently permits the full *matri'in* on *Shabbat* even as a prayer directed to God — *l'tzeaka* — in these cases. If so, what is unique about them that justifies this beyond all the other severe afflictions listed earlier? The most plausible reading is that these situations share an extreme, moment-by-moment urgency that the other categories do not. A famine is terrible but develops over time. A besieged city, a flooding river, a ship in a storm — each of these can become catastrophic within minutes or hours. The degree of emergency is qualitatively different, and it is that quality of immediacy that the *tanna kamma* regards as sufficient to override the ordinary *Shabbat* restrictions on communal prayer.

### Shimon HaTimni

*Shimon HaTimni* adds: even *dever*, pestilence, should warrant the *matri'in* on *Shabbat* — *af al hadever*. The *chachamim* did not accept his position. The rejection is interesting in light of what we know about communicable disease. If one truly understood the spreading, urgent nature of a communicable plague, the logic for treating it with the same urgency as a siege would be compelling. But either the rabbis understood *dever* differently, or they were unconvinced that the kind of immediate human response that justifies the *Shabbat* exception was possible in the case of pestilence.

### All Afflictions Rule

The *mishnah* concludes with a general statement: *al kol tzara shelo tavo al hatzibur matri'in aleihem* — on any affliction — the phrasing *shelo tavo* functioning as a kind of rhetorical hedge, a *kein ayin hara* — one performs the *matri'in*, with the full package of *ta'anit* and *tefilla*.

### Excessive Rain Logic

The one exception: *chutz mirov geshamim*, excessive rain. Even when torrential rains are causing damage — flooding homes, ruining crops, waterlogging the soil — one does not perform this extreme communal response. The reason is that rain is fundamentally a *bracha*. We spend so much of this tractate worrying about insufficient rain that we are not going to treat an overabundance with the same severity we would treat a plague or famine. We should only be so troubled. Some smaller acknowledgment might be appropriate, but the full *matri'in* package is reserved for genuine curses, not for blessings that have become, in a particular season, excessive.

## Choni Ha-Ma'agel

### Ovens Request

The *mishnah* now introduces the famous story of *Choni Ha-Ma'agel*, the circle drawer. Before entering the narrative itself, several contextual observations are worth making. The story reaches back to the period of the *Chashmonaim*. *Yosefus* already records traditions about *Choni Ha-Ma'agel* in connection with that era — not this story, but a story in which one of the warring Hasmonean brothers sought him out and asked him to pray for the defeat of the other side. This tells us that by the Hasmonean period, the legend of *Choni* was well established: a figure whose *tefillot* were uniquely efficacious, whom people sought out specifically to leverage his special access to divine response. The *mishnah* is recording this story approximately three hundred years after *Choni* would have lived.

The *mishnah* opens: *she-amru lo l'Choni Ha-Ma'agel*, they told *Choni Ha-Ma'agel*, *hitpallel she-yerdu geshamim* — pray that the rains should come. An important question immediately presents itself: at what stage in the process did people go to *Choni*? Had the community already gone through the entire graduated sequence of *ta'aniyot* and *tfilot* that the *mishnah* prescribes? Or were some people bypassing the institutional process entirely — going directly to a charismatic wonder-worker rather than engaging the communal penitential system? These may represent two distinct and competing theological paradigms operating simultaneously: the institutional model, in which the moral and religious quality of the entire community is what God responds to, and the charismatic model, in which a single extraordinary individual with a special relationship to the divine can move heaven. The latter is clearly threatening to rabbinic authority, as the end of the story will make plain. Who were the people going to *Choni*? Almost certainly not the *chachamim* themselves.

If the ovens for roasting the *korban pesach* are being brought out and protected — the concern *Choni* expresses — this suggests the story takes place at the very end of *Adar*, just before *Pesach*. Consistent with this, the *gemara* later in the text will confirm that the events occurred at the end of *Adar*. If so, the context is stark: it has been five or six months since the expected rains of *Marcheshvan*, and still nothing. The community has presumably exhausted normal channels. *Choni* is approached as a last resort. His response is immediate and breathtakingly confident: *tze'u v'hakhnisu tannurei psachim bishvil she-lo yimmoku* — go bring in the Pesach ovens so that they do not dissolve in all the rain that is about to fall. Pottery dissolves only under extraordinary quantities of water; *Choni* is announcing a downpour before he has even prayed.

### Circle & Demand

He prays — and nothing falls. *Hitpallel v'lo yardu geshamim*. One might read in this a small measure of irony at the expense of his earlier bravado. *Mah asah*? What did he do? He drew a circle, stood inside it — *ag uga v'amad betokha* — and addressed God directly: *Ribbono shel olam, banecha samu pneihem alay* — Master of the Universe, Your children have turned their faces toward me, *she-ani k'ven bayit lefanekha* — for I am like a *ben bayit* before You. *Nishba ani b'shimcha hagadol she'eini zaz mikan ad sheterachem al banecha* — I swear by Your great name that I will not move from this spot until You have mercy on Your children.

The expression *ben bayit* requires unpacking. It can mean a literal member of the household — a child in his father's home, with all the intimacy and presumption that implies. Some commentators, however, prefer to read it as a household servant or attendant, one who has constant access to the king's chambers but is not actually family. This reading draws a comparison to the figure of the Shakespearean fool or court jester — the one person in the palace permitted a certain latitude of speech precisely because of his lowly station. On this reading, *Choni* is claiming not royal status before God but rather the particular license of the intimate attendant. In either case, the audacity is remarkable: he is making a demand of God, threatening, in effect, a sit-in.

### Rain Escalation

The rain begins to drip — *menatfin*, a mere drizzle. *Amar lo kach sha'alti* — that is not what I asked for. He specifies: *gishmei borot shichin u'me'arot* — enough rain to fill the wells and the cisterns. The rain then comes down *beza'af* — in fury, torrential. Again: *lo kach sha'alti*. He asks for *gishmei ratzon, bracha u'nedava* — rain of goodwill, blessing, and generosity. And finally the rain falls properly — *yardu ketiknan* — as it should.

### Flood & Stone Check

But now it rains too much. The people of *Yerushalayim* are forced to abandon their homes and ascend to *Har HaBayit* to escape the flooding. They return to *Choni*: just as you prayed for rain to come, pray now for it to stop. His response: *tze'u u're'u im nimcheta even hato'im* — go see whether the Stone of the Lost has dissolved. This was the stone in *Yerushalayim* where public announcements were made, where people who had lost objects would post notices and where others could come to reclaim what they had found — a central gathering point, particularly during the *regalim*. Only if that stone has been worn away by the rain, *Choni* implies, has it truly rained enough to stop.

The comment is telling. Before the rain came, *Choni* had said the ovens would dissolve from all the coming rain; now that it is raining too much, he sets the bar of adequacy at the dissolution of the announcement stone. There is a pattern here of a man who is never immediately responsive — who always pushes further, demands more, sets a new threshold.

### Shetach Rebuke

*Shimon ben Shetach*, one of the leading figures of that generation, sends him a message: *ilmalei Choni ata gozrani alecha nidui* — were you not *Choni*, I would place you under *nidui*, excommunication. The justification for *nidui* would presumably be the disrespect shown to God — demanding, escalating, refusing to accept what was given. But *Shimon ben Shetach* acknowledges that he cannot enforce this: *aval ma e'eseh lach she'ata mitchatei lifnei haMakom v'oseh lach retzonecha* — but what can I do, for you *mitchatei* before God and God does your will?

The word *mitchatei* is difficult to translate precisely. *Rashi* in another context explains it as a kind of misbehavior. *Shimon ben Shetach* elaborates: *k'ven shemitchatei al aviv v'oseh lo retzono* — like a child who misbehaves toward his father and yet the father indulges him. And upon you the verse applies: *yismach avicha v'imecha v'tagel yoladtecha* — your father and mother will rejoice in you.

### Choni Critique

This ending is layered and somewhat paradoxical. *Shimon ben Shetach* is ostensibly praising *Choni* — God did his bidding — and yet the entire rebuke frames *Choni* as a misbehaving child who got away with something he should not have. Furthermore, the facts of the story do not quite bear out *Shimon ben Shetach*'s claim that God did *Choni*'s will: what *Choni* got was technically responsive to his requests, but God was clearly not cooperating in a straightforward way — first a drizzle, then a torrent, and then rain so excessive that the city flooded. This is not God cheerfully granting the prayer of a beloved child; it is something closer to ironic compliance.

The deeper agenda of the passage, it seems, is the undercutting of charismatic authority. *Chazal* cannot simply deny that *Choni* had a special relationship with the divine — the popular perception was too strong, and the text itself would not support a flat denial. But they can do several things: they can show that *Choni*'s arrogance and presumptuousness were theologically inappropriate; they can show that God's responses were not in fact what *Choni* demanded; and they can show that the rabbinic establishment — *Shimon ben Shetach* — regarded this behavior as potentially worthy of *nidui*. The contrast with the earlier portrait of the *yechidim* — anonymous individuals chosen for the *ta'anit* prayers precisely on the basis of their moral character and piety, not any charismatic wonder-working ability — is sharp and almost certainly intentional.

## Fasts After Rain

### Before Sunrise Rule

The *mishnah* turns to a different question: what happens if rain falls on a day when the community is already fasting? If rain falls *kodem hanetz hachamah* — before sunrise — then *lo yashlimu*, they need not complete the fast. The fast has not yet fully begun; the *bracha* has arrived; there is no obligation to continue.

### After Sunrise Rule

If rain falls *le'achar hanetz hachamah* — after sunrise — then *yashlimu*, they must complete the fast. Once the day has been formally constituted as a fast day, the obligation attaches. That it rains after sunrise does not dissolve the obligation already incurred.

### Rabbi Eliezer

*Rabbi Eliezer* disputes the cutoff point: *kodem chatzot lo yashlimu, le'achar chatzot yashlimu*. The dividing line for *Rabbi Eliezer* is not sunrise but *chatzot*, midday. If rain comes before noon, there is no obligation to complete the fast; if after, there is.

### Lod Story

The *mishnah* supports *Rabbi Eliezer*'s position with a *ma'aseh*: a *ta'anit* was decreed in *Lod*, and rain fell before *chatzot*. *Rabbi Tarfon* told the community: *tze'u v'ichlu u-she'tu v'asu yom tov* — go out, eat, drink, and celebrate. And they did — and they returned in the *bein ha'arbayim* and recited *Hallel HaGadol*. Note that *Hallel HaGadol* is not the *Hallel HaMitzri* familiar from festivals; it refers to the extended passage of *Hodu la-Shem ki tov*, which is added into the *Pesukei d'Zimra* on *Shabbat*.

### Institutional Nature

This *mishnah* raises a striking contrast to the *Choni* narrative that immediately precedes it. In the *Choni* story, the relationship between prayer and divine response is immediate and dynamic — *Choni* prays, adjusts his prayer, God responds in kind, and the exchange has a living, transactional quality. Here, however, we find the formalized, institutional face of the fast: a community that has accepted upon itself a *ta'anit* may be obligated to continue fasting even after their prayers have been answered, because the formal structure of the day as a fast overrides the logic of immediate responsiveness. The fast is no longer simply a vehicle for prayer; it has become an independent obligation with its own rules of inception and completion. This contrast — between the charismatic immediacy of *Choni Ha-Ma'agel*'s encounter with God and the institutional formalism of the fast-day laws — is one of the most illuminating juxtapositions in the chapter.

## Gemara: Famine Timeline

### Prayer vs Fasting

The *gemara* opens its discussion with an apparent contradiction. The *mishnah* we have been learning seems to suggest that the three-three-and-seven fasting sequence begins with the passing of the first *revi'ah* — the first appointed rain date. But another source states that the first and second *revi'ot* are occasions only for prayer — *lish'ol* — and only the third unanswered *revi'ah* triggers fasting. *Rav Yehuda* resolves the contradiction: the three-three-and-seven sequence refers not to the first date passing, but to the situation where the first rain was delayed beyond all three *revi'ah* dates and has still not arrived. It is only at that point — once all three dates have passed without rain — that the community enters the graduated fasting process. *Aval yardu geshamim birevi'ah rishonah* — but if rain came at the first date when it should have, and the crops were planted but failed to sprout, or sprouted and then changed abnormally — in that case one responds *matri'in aleihem miyad*, immediately, even before the three *revi'ah* dates would have passed.

### Nishtanu Debate

*Rav Nachman* then adds a qualification: *davka nishtanu* — the immediate response is triggered only if the crops have actually undergone a visible change, some form of recognizable blight or disease. But if the crops simply *yavshu* — dried out — that alone does not necessarily trigger the immediate response. *Tosafot* explains that drying out is less definitive; crops can recover from dryness if rain comes. The *gemara* then asks: isn't this obvious? Didn't the *mishnah* itself say *nishtanu*? The answer: *lo tzericha* — the case is specifically where the crops dried out but have now begun to show signs of partial recovery. One might think: now that they are reviving somewhat, this is precisely the moment when prayer will help — add rain now and the partial recovery will complete itself. The *gemara* answers that this partial recovery is not significant enough; if the crops truly dried out, they are effectively ruined regardless.

### Batzoret Definition

The *mishnah* mentioned *makat batzoret* in the context of the forty-day rain gap. The *gemara* asks: *mai makat batzoret*? *Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Rav* says it refers to *baka hamevi'ah lidei batzoret* — something that brings about a condition of insufficient grain, whatever that may specifically mean in context. *Rav Nachman* then offers his famous definition of the difference between *batzoret* and *kafna* (the Aramaic equivalent of *ra'av*, famine): *nahara anahera batzorta* — if you can travel by river to a neighboring locale and procure grain there, even if locally there is scarcity, that is *batzoret*, a drought condition, but not yet full famine. *Medinta amedinta kafna* — if you have to travel from one country to another country to obtain grain, that is *kafna*, genuine famine, the Aramaic equivalent of the *ra'av* of which the Torah speaks — *vayehi ra'av ba'aretz*, the famine in the days of *Avraham*.

### Price vs Availability

*Rabbi Chanina* adds another dimension: *se'ah besela ush'chicha* — if a *se'ah* of grain costs a *sela*, which is very expensive, but it is readily available in the market, that is still only *batzorta* — high prices, but not famine. *Arba'a velo sh'chicha* — if four *se'ah* can be had for a *sela*, meaning the price is remarkably cheap, but the grain is simply unavailable — nobody is selling — then that is *kafna*, genuine famine. The critical variable is not price but availability. A commodified shortage with sustained low prices is theoretically puzzling — markets generally drive prices up when supply is constrained — but *Rabbi Chanina* posits the scenario, and the halakhic principle is clear: it is the inability to obtain food, not its cost, that defines the severity of the condition. As an analogy offered in discussion: during the Irish famine, food was being produced and exported, and prices may not have reflected the internal scarcity experienced by the poor, yet a million people starved — because availability and distribution failed, not price alone.

### Money Scarcity

*Rabbi Yochanan* adds a further qualification: *lo shanu ela bizman shema'ot bezol uperot beyoker* — all of the above analysis applies when money is plentiful and food is expensive. In that case, expensive but available food is *batzoret* and unavailable food (even if cheap) is *kafna*. But *aval ma'ot beyoker uperot bezol* — if money is scarce and food is cheap, if prices are low but nobody has any money to spend, then *matri'in aleihem miyad*, one responds immediately with the full severity. *Rabbi Yochanan* himself recalls: *nehirna kad havah kaimei arba se'in besela* — I remember when four *se'ah* could be had for a *sela*, dirt cheap, yet *nefishei nefishei kafna b'Tveria* — there were many people bloated from famine in *Tiberias*, because *leis isa* — nobody had even a penny with which to purchase the cheap food available. The distinction between a price crisis and a money crisis, while economically interesting, requires further analysis to fully understand the halakhic significance of distinguishing them.

## Rain for Crops

### Rain Type Effects

The *gemara* now works through the agricultural categories mentioned in the earlier *mishnah* — rain good for crops but not trees, or vice versa. *Bishlama l'tzmachim v'lo l'ilan* — rain that is good for crops but not trees: this is possible when the rain falls gently, pleasantly — *nicha* — because gentle rain is absorbed gradually into the soil and nourishes grain crops, but does not penetrate deeply enough to reach tree roots. *L'ilan v'lo l'tzmachim* — good for trees but not crops: this occurs when rain falls strongly and forcefully — *razya* — driving deep into the soil to reach tree roots, but falling too hard to benefit the delicate grain crops. Rain that is *l'zeh v'l'zeh* — good for both trees and crops — but insufficient for wells and cisterns: this happens when there is some pleasant rain and some strong rain, but not enough of either to fill the underground cisterns.

### Torrential Rain

A *braita* is then cited introducing a fourth category: rain good for the wells and cisterns but not for the crops or trees. The *gemara* asks how this is possible — *heichi mashkachat lah*? The answer: *d'atei b'shfichuta* — it comes in torrents. Torrential downpours run off the surface and fill underground water sources, but they damage the plants rather than nourishing them.

### Trees Half Pesach

The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: *matri'in al ha'ilanot b'fros haPesach* — one performs the *matri'in* for the trees half a month before *Pesach*, meaning from *Rosh Chodesh Nissan*. The timing makes sense: trees require their water needs met by spring, roughly two months after *Tu B'Shvat*. If by *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* there has been insufficient rain for the trees, the fast and prayer response is initiated for them specifically — a different calendrical threshold than what applies to grain crops.

### Wells Tishrei

For wells, cisterns, and caves — *borot shichin u'me'arot* — the threshold is even earlier: *afilu b'fros hachag*, even from *Rosh Chodesh Tishrei*. This is surprising. By *Rosh Chodesh Tishrei* the rainy season has not yet typically begun in *Eretz Yisrael*. Perhaps the anticipation is driven by the need to prepare for the masses of *olei regalim* who will arrive for *Sukkot* and require water, creating an unusual early-season demand. The precise reasoning behind this early threshold remains somewhat unclear.

### Drinking Water

For drinking water, however, there is no seasonal calendar at all: *im ein lahem mayim lishtot, matri'in aleihem miyad* — if people have no drinking water, the response is immediate regardless of the time of year. The urgency of basic human survival overrides all calendrical frameworks. And the *gemara* specifies what *miyad* means here in practice: *sheni v'chamishi v'sheni* — Monday, Thursday, and Monday, the three-fast-day cycle, which is the response even for this immediate case.

## Specific Diseases

### Askara Disease

The *gemara* addresses *askara*, a disease affecting the throat — perhaps croup, perhaps diphtheria, the precise ancient medical identification remaining uncertain. Whatever it was, it was known to be agonizing and potentially fatal. The rule: *bizman sheyesh bah mita, matri'in aleiha* — when *askara* is causing death, one initiates the communal response. *U'vizman she'ein bah mita, ein matri'in aleiha* — when it does not lead to death, there is no such response. Even if the disease were contagious, mere illness without a high mortality rate is apparently not sufficient grounds for the full *matri'in*. The threshold, as with *dever* earlier, is death, not sickness.

### Locusts Response

For *govai*, a species of locust, *matri'in al hagovai b'chol shehu* — even the smallest appearance of *govai* triggers the immediate response. The rationale is implied: no locust swarm begins small and remains small. A few *govai* today means devastation tomorrow. *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* extends this to *chagav* as well, another species of locust or grasshopper, which the *tanna kamma* apparently did not include.

### Shmitta Trees

A *baraita* addresses the case of trees during the *shmitta* cycle: *matri'in al ha'ilanot b'shar shnei shavua* — one prays for the trees in all the years of the seven-year cycle other than *shmitta* itself, because during *shmitta* the trees are not being harvested in the usual sense. *Tosafot* takes the opposite view — *shmitta* is precisely when one needs the trees to bear fruit, since people depend on the produce that grows on its own. This is a genuine tension in the *rishonim*.

### Sifichin Shmitta

*Rabban Shimon Gamliel* goes further: one prays *af al ha'ilanot bishvi'it* — even for the trees during *shmitta* — because the trees provide sustenance for the poor. The entire institution of *shmitta* is oriented toward making the land's produce accessible to everyone, and particularly to the poor who have no other resources. If the trees bear no fruit because of insufficient rain, the poor suffer. *Rabban Shimon Gamliel* then adds *al hasifichin bishvi'it* as well — even for *sifichin*, wild vegetation that grows without being planted during the *shmitta* year, *matri'in*, because *sifichin* too provide *parnasa la'aniyim*, sustenance for the poor. *Rashi* reads *Rabban Shimon Gamliel*'s position as encompassing both trees and wild vegetation: not only cultivated fruit trees but even the spontaneous growth of the field is worth praying for during *shmitta*, given the dependence of the poor upon it. The question of whether *sifichin* may be eaten in *shmitta* — a separate halakhic discussion — is left here in the background.
