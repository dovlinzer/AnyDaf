# Bava Batra 121 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bnot Tzelofchad & Tu B'Av

### Bnot Tzelofchad Setup

The daf opens mid-page — the line begins with the word *mumche* — with the Gemara continuing its treatment of *Bnot Tzelofchad*. By way of background: the Gemara has just established that the restriction on intertribal marriage applied only to that particular generation, specifically to women who, like *Bnot Tzelofchad*, inherited a share of the land through their father. Future generations were free to marry whomever they wished. Moreover, even *Bnot Tzelofchad* themselves were not strictly prohibited from marrying outside the tribe — they were advised against it, but chose to follow that advice. With that background in place, the Gemara proceeds to quote a famous *Mishna* in Taanit, because it intersects directly with what has just been discussed.

### Mishna: Tu B'Av & Yom Ki…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם, אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: לֹא הָיוּ יָמִים טוֹבִים לְיִשְׂרָאֵל כַּחֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר בְּאָב וּכְיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים, שֶׁבָּהֶן בְּנוֹת יְרוּשָׁלַיִם יוֹצְאוֹת בִּכְלֵי לָבָן שְׁאוּלִין, שֶׁלֹּא לְבַיֵּישׁ אֶת מִי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned over **there** (*Ta'anit* 26b): **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said: There were no days as joyous for the Jewish people as the fifteenth of Av and as Yom Kippur, as on these** days **the daughters of Jerusalem would emerge in white garments,** which each woman **borrowed** from another. Why did they borrow garments? They did this **so as not to embarrass one who did not have** her own white garments.

The Gemara introduces the passage with the formula *tnan hatam* — "we learned over there," in Taanit. Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel declares: *lo hayu yamim tovim leYisrael kechamisha asar b'Av ukYom HaKippurim* — there were never days as joyous for Israel as the fifteenth of *Av* and *Yom Kippur*. The *Mishna* explains that on these days the young women of Israel would go out wearing borrowed white garments — *chelei lavan she'ulin* — so that anyone who needed to borrow would not be embarrassed. Even those who owned their own clothes would borrow, so that the practice became universal. The Gemara elsewhere describes that the daughter of the *Cohen Gadol* would borrow from a princess, and people would borrow from friends of similar station. The young women would then go out and dance, and the day served as an occasion for courtship.

### Women's Sayings: Beauty…

A *Braita* preserves what the young women would call out to the young men watching. The beautiful ones said: young man, look toward beauty, for a woman is prized for her appearance. Those from distinguished families said: look toward *yichus*, for a woman is for her children. And those who were neither beautiful nor well-born said: choose a wife *leshem Shamayim* — for the sake of Heaven. The *Brisker Rav* is said to have quipped: and what did those who were both beautiful and *meyuchasot* say? They were already married.

## Reasons for Tu B'Av Joy

### Yom Kippur Joy & Tablets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים – יוֹם סְלִיחָה וּמְחִילָה, יוֹם שֶׁנִּתְּנוּ בּוֹ לוּחוֹת אַחֲרוֹנוֹת. אֶלָּא חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר בְּאָב – מַאי הִיא?
>
> **Translation:** **Granted** that **Yom Kippur** is a day of joy, because it is **a day of pardon and forgiveness,** and moreover, it is the **day on which the last Tablets** of the Covenant **were given. But what is** the special joy of the **fifteenth of Av?**

The Gemara now asks: what made these days so joyous? Regarding *Yom Kippur* the answer is relatively straightforward — *bishlama Yom HaKippurim*, one can understand it. It was *yom slicha umechila*, a day of forgiveness, and historically it was the day Moshe came down with the second set of *luchot*. The arithmetic works out as follows: *matan Torah* was on *Shavuot*, the sixth of *Sivan*. Forty days later is the seventeenth of *Tammuz*, when Moshe descended and broke the tablets. Although the narrative in Shemot and the account in Devarim each seem to describe only one subsequent forty-day period, *Chazal* harmonize the two accounts and posit three distinct forty-day periods. From the seventeenth of *Tammuz* plus forty days arrives at approximately the twenty-eighth of *Av*, and then another forty days brings one to *Yom Kippur*. On that day Moshe descended with the second *luchot*, which is why *Yom Kippur* carries the designation *yom slicha umechila* — it marked a new beginning, as if starting over.

### Husband-Wife Metaphor

What *Yom Kippur* has to do with courtship and the dancing of young women is not made explicit by the Gemara itself, but a suggestive parallel may be drawn. The *Nevi'im* employ the metaphor of husband and wife to describe the relationship between God and *Bnei Yisrael*, and *Chazal* apply a version of this metaphor to the breaking and restoration of the *luchot*. The breaking of the tablets was, as it were, the tearing up of the marriage contract; the giving of the second *luchot* on *Yom Kippur* represented the reconciliation and reunion. There is an added dimension: in the *Nevi'im*, it is often the woman — here, *Bnei Yisrael* — who takes the initiative in seeking the reconciliation, just as in the episode of the Golden Calf it was Moshe's prayers that moved God to agree to restore the relationship. Something of that dynamic may be reflected in the women taking the initiative on these days of courtship. The Gemara in Taanit also touches on this theme of reunion, though the connection between *Yom Kippur* and the specific custom of courtship remains more implicit than explicit.

### Rav Yehuda: Tribes Permi…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: יוֹם שֶׁהוּתְּרוּ שְׁבָטִים לָבֹא זֶה בָּזֶה. מַאי דְּרוּשׁ? ״זֶה הַדָּבָר״ – דָּבָר זֶה לֹא יְהֵא נוֹהֵג אֶלָּא בְּדוֹר זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** This was the **day when** the members of different **tribes were permitted to marry** into **one another's** tribe. Such marriages were restricted for the first generation to enter Eretz Yisrael, as discussed above (120a). **What** verse **did** the sages of that time **interpret** in support of their conclusion that this *halakha* was no longer in effect? The verse states: **"This is the matter"** (Numbers 36:6), meaning, **this matter shall be practiced only in this generation,** in which Eretz Yisrael is being divided among the tribes.

The question now turns to *Tu B'Av*: *u'v'chamisha asar b'Av mai hi* — what is the significance of the fifteenth of *Av*? Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel answers: *yom she'hutru shevatim lavo zeh ba'zeh* — the day that the tribes were permitted to intermarry with one another. This is precisely the context that brings the *Mishna* from Taanit into the discussion of *Bnot Tzelofchad*. The restriction that a woman who inherited a share of the land could not marry outside her tribe was limited to that generation. When that generation died out — perhaps fifty years or so after the entry into *Eretz Yisrael* — the restriction lapsed entirely, and all were free to marry whom they chose. That day of freedom is identified with the fifteenth of *Av*.

This answer connects elegantly to the courtship theme of the *Mishna*. The women going out and calling *bachur sa na einecha* — lift your eyes, young man — mirrors the verse from our passage, *hatov b'eineihem tihyena l'nashim*. The initiative of women in selecting their own husbands also resonates with the story of *Bnot Tzelofchad* throughout. No specific *pasuk* identifies the fifteenth of *Av* as the date; rather, it reflects a received tradition that this generation had by then fully died out. The *drasha* that grounds the ruling itself is *zeh hadavar* — *davar zeh lo yehe noheg ela b'dor hazeh*, the matter shall apply only to that generation.

### Binyamin Rejoins via Mar…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: יוֹם שֶׁהוּתַּר שֵׁבֶט בִּנְיָמִן לָבֹא בַּקָּהָל, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאִישׁ יִשְׂרָאֵל נִשְׁבַּע בַּמִּצְפָּה לֵאמֹר: אִישׁ מִמֶּנּוּ לֹא יִתֵּן בִּתּוֹ לְבִנְיָמִן לְאִשָּׁה״. מַאי דְּרוּשׁ? ״מִמֶּנּוּ״ – וְלֹא מִבָּנֵינוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan** offered another explanation: The fifteenth of Av was the **day when** the **tribe of Benjamin was permitted to enter into the congregation** of the other tribes of Israel through marriage, after the other tribes found a way to dissolve the vow that had prohibited them from marrying a member of the tribe of Benjamin in the aftermath of the episode of the concubine in Gibeah (Judges, chapters 19–20). **As it is written: "And the men of Israel had taken an oath in Mizpah, saying: None of us shall give his daughter to Benjamin as a wife"** (Judges 21:1). The Gemara asks: **What** verse **did** the sages of that time **interpret** that enabled them to dissolve this vow? The verse states: "None **of us," and not:** None **of our children;** therefore, the oath applied only to the generation that had taken the oath.

Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan offers a second answer: *yom she'hutru shevet Binyamin lavo b'kahal* — the day that the tribe of Binyamin was permitted to rejoin in marriage with the rest of Israel. After the affair of *Pilegesh b'Givah*, the other tribes had sworn at Mitzpah: *ish mimenu lo yiten bito l'Binyamin l'isha* — no man from among us shall give his daughter to Binyamin as a wife. This led to the extraordinary episode in which the men of Binyamin seized women for themselves, since no one would give voluntarily.

What *drasha* indicates that this oath had an expiration date? *Mimenu v'lo mibaneinu* — from *us*, but not from *our sons*. The oath was binding on that generation alone; the next generation was free to give their daughters to Binyamin. Binyamin survived that one difficult generation, and from that point the restriction dissolved. Here too the theme of freedom of marriage and the restoration of inter-tribal bonds is directly connected to the courtship customs of *Tu B'Av*.

### Dor Hamidbar Deaths End

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב דִּימִי בַּר יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: יוֹם שֶׁכָּלוּ בּוֹ מֵתֵי מִדְבָּר; דְּאָמַר מָר: עַד שֶׁלֹּא כָּלוּ מֵתֵי מִדְבָּר, לֹא הָיָה דִּיבּוּר עִם מֹשֶׁה – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי כַּאֲשֶׁר תַּמּוּ כׇּל אַנְשֵׁי הַמִּלְחָמָה לָמוּת מִקֶּרֶב הָעָם״, וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ: ״וַיְדַבֵּר ה׳ אֵלַי לֵאמֹר״ – אֵלַי הָיָה הַדִּיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Dimi bar Yosef says** that **Rav Naḥman** says: The fifteenth of Av was the **day on which those designated to perish in the wilderness stopped** dying, as the entire generation that had left Egypt had died due to the sin of the spies (Numbers 14:29–30). **As the Master says: As long as those designated to perish** in the **wilderness had not stopped** dying, God **did not speak with Moses, as it is stated: "So it came to pass, when all the men of war were consumed and dead from among the people"** (Deuteronomy 2:16), **and juxtaposed to** that verse it is written: **"That the Lord spoke to me, saying"** (Deuteronomy 2:17). Moses indicates: Only after the last of that generation had died, **was the speech** of God directed **to me.** When the Jewish people realized that the decree had been lifted, the day was established as a permanent day of rejoicing.

Rav Dimi bar Yosef in the name of Rav Nachman offers a third answer: *yom she'kalu vo metei midbar* — the day on which the deaths of the wilderness generation finally came to an end, before the entry into *Eretz Yisrael*. The reason this constitutes a day of joy is that after the dying stopped, a new generation could begin fresh.

The Gemara cites a striking teaching: *ad she'lo kalu metei midbar, lo haya dibur im Moshe* — until the wilderness generation had finished dying out, God did not speak directly to Moshe. The proof-text juxtaposes the verse describing the completion of the deaths — *vayehi ka'asher tamu kol anshei hamilchama lamut mikerev ha'am* — with the very next verse: *vay'dabber Hashem elai lemor*, God spoke to me. The immediate juxtaposition implies that the divine speech to Moshe was conditional on the death of that generation.

This is a striking teaching. After all, the Torah records God speaking to Moshe extensively during those thirty-eight years — *vayomer Hashem el Moshe* appears throughout. The Rashbam resolves this by drawing a distinction between *dibur* and *amira*. The term *vay'dabber*, with its connotation of directness and intimacy — the kind of speech described as *peh el peh adabber bo*, face to face — was suspended during this period. God spoke only when necessary, but the full, intimate, restored relationship was not resumed until the generation had passed.

The deeper message is powerful, and in some ways counterintuitive. The episode of the *Meraglim* seems to portray Moshe's relationship with God as something entirely independent of the people — God even says He would destroy them all and make Moshe into a great nation. Yet *Chazal* here communicate precisely the opposite: Moshe's special access to God was a function of his role as leader of *Bnei Yisrael*. While that people was in a state of divine disfavor — described in Taanit as *menudeh lashamayim*, placed under a kind of ban — Moshe's intimacy with God was correspondingly diminished. Only when the next generation stood ready to enter the land was the relationship fully restored. The connection between this answer and the courtship theme of *Tu B'Av* is, admittedly, less direct than the preceding answers, though one can point to themes of intimacy and the restoration of relationship.

### Midrash: Grave-Digging S…

The Rashbam cites a *Midrash* from *Midrash Eichah* that attempts to connect the answer of *kalu metei midbar* specifically to the date of the fifteenth of *Av*. The *Midrash*, brought in the name of Rabbi Levi, describes the following scene: every year on the eve of *Tisha B'Av*, a proclamation would go out — *hakol yetzu lachpor chafirim*, everyone go out and dig graves — and the people would go and lie down in the graves they had dug. In the morning, another proclamation: *yibadlu hachayim min hameitim*, let the living separate themselves from the dead. Each year approximately fifteen thousand people would remain in their graves — since forty times fifteen thousand equals six hundred thousand, roughly the number counted — and the rest would rise and go on. This continued for forty years.

In the final year, they all went into their graves on the ninth of *Av* — and in the morning, no one was dead. Uncertain whether they had miscounted the date, they tried again the following night, and the night after that, repeating this through the tenth, eleventh, twelfth, thirteenth, and fourteenth of *Av*, until the full moon appeared on the fifteenth. At that point they understood: *bitel HaKadosh Baruch Hu osah gezeira kasha me'aleinu* — the Holy One had annulled the harsh decree against them. And they made a feast and a celebration and a *yom tov*.

### Tosafot Rejects Midrash

Tosafot objects to this *Midrash* sharply, and in particular to the way the Rashbam reads it. The *pesukim* are unambiguous: *lo notar mehem ish ki im Kalev ben Yefuneh* — none of them remained except Calev ben Yefuneh. According to the Rashbam's reading of the *Midrash*, some fifteen thousand people from the wilderness generation had their decree waived and actually entered *Eretz Yisrael* — a reading that directly contradicts the plain meaning of the verses.

Moreover, Tosafot notes that even on its own terms, the *Midrash* does not actually support the Gemara's statement that this was the day *kalu* — that they finished dying. To the contrary, it is the day they discovered they were *not* dying — the day the decree was lifted for the survivors, not the day the generation concluded. The *Midrash* thus does not fit what the Gemara is saying. Tosafot's own reading, without the *Midrash*, is simpler: *Tu B'Av* was simply the day the last member of the wilderness generation died out, and that is all.

## Further Tu B'Av Reasons

### Hoshea: Removes Guard Po…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אָמַר: יוֹם שֶׁבִּיטֵּל בּוֹ הוֹשֵׁעַ בֶּן אֵלָה פַּרְדְּסָאוֹת שֶׁהוֹשִׁיב יָרׇבְעָם עַל הַדְּרָכִים, שֶׁלֹּא יַעֲלוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל לָרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla says:** The fifteenth of Av was the **day when** King **Hoshea, son of Ela, removed** the **guards [*pardesaot*] that Jeroboam,** son of Nevat, **placed on the roads so that Israel** would **not ascend** to Jerusalem **for the pilgrimage Festival.** By doing so, King Hoshea renewed the access to Jerusalem for pilgrims.

Ulla offers yet another answer: *yom she'bitel bo Hoshea ben Elah pardesa'os she'hoshib Yeravam al hadrachim shelo ya'alu Yisrael leregel* — the day that Hoshea ben Elah removed the guard posts that Yeravam ben Nevat had placed on the roads to prevent the northern kingdom from making the *aliya laregel* to Yerushalayim.

The background is as follows: when the kingdom split and Yeravam established the golden calves in Dan as an alternative center of worship, he feared that if his subjects continued to make pilgrimage to Yerushalayim, their political and religious loyalties would remain with the Davidic kingdom in the south. He therefore stationed guards on the highways to physically block travel to Yerushalayim during the pilgrimage festivals. Every subsequent king of Israel is described in the formula *vayelech b'derech Yeravam ben Nevat uvachat'aso asher hichti et Yisrael* — he walked in the ways of Yeravam and in his sin by which he caused Israel to sin. But of Hoshea ben Elah the verse says only *rak lo kemalchei Yisrael asher hayu lefanav* — only, he was not like the kings of Israel who preceded him. The Rashbam, citing *Seder Olam*, explains that what distinguished Hoshea was precisely this act: upon seeing that the golden calves had been carried off by the enemy, he removed these roadblocks and allowed free movement to Yerushalayim for the festivals.

One might ask: with the golden calves gone, would there not be even more reason to block access to Yerushalayim, lest the northerners fully return to its orbit? Nevertheless, the tradition identifies Hoshea's removal of these barriers as occurring on the fifteenth of *Av*. The connection to the courtship theme here is perhaps the weakest of all the answers — one might suggest it is about removing barriers between people, allowing the two kingdoms to draw closer, though the Gemara does not make this connection explicitly.

### Beitar Burial & Bracha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב מַתְנָה אָמַר: יוֹם שֶׁנִּתְּנוּ הֲרוּגֵי בֵיתָר לִקְבוּרָה. דְּאָמַר רַב מַתְנָה: אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם שֶׁנִּתְּנוּ הֲרוּגֵי בֵיתָר לִקְבוּרָה – תִּקְנוּ בְּיַבְנֶה ״הַטּוֹב וְהַמֵּטִיב״; ״הַטּוֹב״ – שֶׁלֹּא הִסְרִיחוּ, ״וְהַמֵּטִיב״ – שֶׁנִּתְּנוּ לִקְבוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mattana says:** The fifteenth of Av was the **day when the slain** victims **of Beitar were afforded burial,** several years after they were killed and the Roman emperor Hadrian decreed that they were not to be buried (see *Gittin* 57a). **As Rav Mattana says:** On **the day that the slain of Beitar were afforded burial,** the Sages **in Yavne instituted** the blessing: Blessed is He **Who is good and Who does good.** The term: **Who is good,** is to give thanks **that** the corpses **did not decompose** despite the long delay; and the term: **And Who does good,** is to give thanks **that** the slain ones **were** ultimately **afforded burial.**

Rav Masna offers still another answer: *yom she'nisnu harugei Beitar likvura* — the day that permission was granted to bury those slain at Beitar in the aftermath of the destruction of the second *Beis HaMikdash*. The bodies had lain unburied, and when they were finally permitted to be interred, the rabbis at Yavneh instituted a new *bracha* — *HaTov veHaMeitiv* — the fourth blessing of *Birkas Hamazon*.

### Hatov Vehameitiv Bracha

This fourth *bracha* is in some ways structurally distinct from the first three. The first three *brachos* of *Birkas Hamazon* function as a unit — one ordinarily does not answer *amen* to one's own *bracha*, and the absence of an intervening *amen* after the third *bracha* signals their unity. *HaTov veHaMeitiv* then begins as a separate, later addition, opening with its own *baruch ata*.

What does *HaTov veHaMeitiv* mean in this context? *Hatov shelo hisrichu* — it was good that God caused the bodies not to decompose or stench; *veHaMeitiv she'nisnu likvura* — and He did good in that they were permitted to be buried. The standard understanding of *veHaMeitiv* — that the good extends beyond the immediate beneficiaries to others as well — applies here in that the entire people was able to feel that these dead had been properly treated and honored.

The broader architecture of *Birkas Hamazon* is worth pausing on in this context. The first *bracha*, *Hazan es Hakol*, concerns food in a universal sense. The second, *Al Ha'aretz ve'Al Hamazon*, reflects the *pasuk* in the Torah — *uveirachta et Hashem Elokecha al ha'aretz hatovah asher natan lach* — situating gratitude for food within the larger context of living in, farming, and harvesting the land of Israel; it is not merely thanks for sustenance but acknowledgment of divine gift of the land itself, reminiscent of the bringing of *bikkurim*. The third *bracha*, *Boneh Yerushalayim*, naturally follows once the theme of *Eretz Yisrael* has been introduced — though it strikes an elegiac note, looking back toward a Yerushalayim that is destroyed and forward toward its restoration. Then *HaTov veHaMeitiv*, linked to the tragedy of Beitar — an episode in which people arguably pressed too hard, *dachaku et haketz*, for national restoration before its time — closes the sequence. The question of what all this is doing in the framework of a blessing over food is itself a rich one, but the thematic thread running from food, to land, to Yerushalayim, to Beitar, has its own internal logic.

### Wood-Cutting Ends

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: יוֹם שֶׁפּוֹסְקִין בּוֹ מִלִּכְרוֹת עֵצִים לַמַּעֲרָכָה. תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הַגָּדוֹל אוֹמֵר: כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר בְּאָב – תָּשַׁשׁ כֹּחָהּ שֶׁל חַמָּה, וְלֹא הָיוּ כּוֹרְתִין עֵצִים לַמַּעֲרָכָה. אָמַר רַב מְנַשֶּׁה: וְקָרוּ לֵיהּ ״יוֹם תְּבַר מַגָּל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba and Rav Yosef both say:** The fifteenth of Av is the **day when they stop cutting wood for the arrangement** of wood on the altar. It **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer the Great says: Once the fifteenth of Av came, the force of the sun would weaken, and** from this date **they would not cut** additional **wood for the arrangement,** because wood cut from then on would not dry properly and would be unfit for use in the Temple. **Rav Menashe said: And** the people **called** the fifteenth of Av: The **day of the breaking of the sickle [*maggal* ],** as they did not need the lumbering tools until the following year.

Rava and Rav Yosef both say: *yom shepaskin bo milichot etzim l'ma'arakha* — the day on which the cutting of wood for the altar fires ceased for the season. Throughout the summer, wood was cut and dried for the *ma'arakha*, the arrangement of wood atop the altar. Dry wood was necessary to minimize smoke and prevent worm infestation, which moisture would encourage. After *Tu B'Av*, with the summer waning and conditions becoming less favorable, the wood-cutting season ended.

Why was the completion of this task a cause of celebration? The Rashbam explains simply that they had been engaged in this significant *mitzvah* throughout the summer, and its completion was marked with joy. As for the connection to the broader courtship theme of *Tu B'Av*, this answer is the most difficult to connect directly, though the association of harvest-time celebrations with communal dancing and courtship is attested in the ancient world more broadly. One might note that the verse describing the fourth year's produce — *kodesh hillulim l'Hashem* — is understood by some as *hillulim* deriving from *chalulim*, circular dances, suggesting a connection between harvest and the kind of festive dancing described in the *Mishna*.

### Breaking of the Hatchet

Breaking of the Hatchet: Rabbi Eliezer the Great states: *kivan she'higi'a chamisha asar b'Av, tashash kocha shel chamah* — from the fifteenth of *Av* onward, the strength of the sun wanes. And Rav Menashe adds: *karu lei yom tavar magal* — they called it the day of the breaking of the hatchet, because the hatchet used for the summer's wood-cutting was put away.

### Increase Torah Study

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ, דְּמוֹסִיף – יוֹסִיף, שֶׁאֵינוֹ מוֹסִיף – יְסִיף.
>
> **Translation:** **From this** point **forward,** when the nights lengthen, **one who adds [*demosif* ]** to his nightly Torah study **will add [*yosif* ]** to his life, **and he who does not add,** that person is ***yesif*.

From this point, the Gemara states: *de'mosif yosif, she'eino mosif ye'asef* — one who adds will be added to, and one who does not add will be gathered in. The Rashbam explains: as the nights lengthen after *Tu B'Av*, one must add time for Torah study in the evening hours to compensate for the shorter days. The one who takes advantage of this opportunity will be blessed; the one who fails to do so — *tikberei imei*, as the expression goes, his mother should bury him. A rather discordant coda to a discussion of a joyous day, but characteristic of the Gemara's tonal range.

### Seven Lifespans Span His…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שִׁבְעָה קִפְּלוּ אֶת כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ – מְתוּשֶׁלַח רָאָה אָדָם, שֵׁם רָאָה מְתוּשֶׁלַח, יַעֲקֹב רָאָה אֶת שֵׁם, עַמְרָם רָאָה אֶת יַעֲקֹב, אֲחִיָּה הַשִּׁילוֹנִי רָאָה אֶת עַמְרָם, אֵלִיָּהוּ רָאָה אֶת אֲחִיָּה הַשִּׁילוֹנִי – וַעֲדַיִין קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: Seven** people **spanned** in their lifetimes **the whole world in its entirety,** i.e., their lives have spanned all of human history. **Methuselah saw Adam** in his lifetime; **Shem saw Methuselah; Jacob saw Shem; Amram saw Jacob; Ahijah the Shilonite saw Amram; Elijah saw Ahijah the Shilonite; and** Elijah **is still alive.**

The Gemara then introduces a seemingly unrelated teaching: *shiva kiflu et kol ha'olam kulo* — seven people whose combined lifespans encompass the entire history of the world. The Rashbam suggests the connection may lie in the preceding saying about *de'mosif yosif*: these individuals lived to such extraordinary ages because they added to their Torah study, and their days were therefore added to them. The connection is admittedly tenuous, but the tradition is introduced here.

## Witnesses & Desert Decree

### Chain: Adam to Elijah

The seven individuals whose overlapping lives span all of world history are presented as follows. Metushelach saw Adam — their lifespans overlap, as the reader of Bereishit can verify by calculating the years. Shem saw Metushelach. Yaakov saw Shem — again, the mathematics of the genealogies in the Torah bear this out. Amram saw Yaakov. Achiyah HaShiloni saw Amram. And Eliyahu saw Achiyah HaShiloni — this is attested in the *pesukim* themselves. And Eliyahu, of course, is still alive.

Regarding Amram and Yaakov: this overlap is not stated explicitly in the Torah, but the Rashbam derives it by inference. Yaakov descended to Mitzrayim together with Kehat, and lived there for seventeen years before his death. Amram was Kehat's eldest son. If Kehat was alive when Yaakov died and was at least seventeen or eighteen years old by that point, it is reasonable to assume he had already fathered his firstborn son Amram. Thus Amram could have seen Yaakov, even if the Torah does not say so explicitly.

### Achiyah & Amram Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲחִיָּה הַשִּׁילוֹנִי רָאָה אֶת עַמְרָם? וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״וְלֹא נוֹתַר מֵהֶם אִישׁ, כִּי אִם כָּלֵב בֶּן יְפֻנֶּה וִיהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן״!
>
> **Translation:** **And** is it true that **Ahijah the Shilonite saw Amram? But it is written: "And no man among them remained, save Caleb, son of Jephunneh, and Joshua, son of Nun"** (Numbers 26:65). Since Amram died well before the Jewish people left Egypt, in order to have lived at the time of Amram, Ahijah would have had to be an adult at the time of the Exodus. How, then, could he have lived through the generation of those who died in the wilderness?

The more difficult link is between Achiyah HaShiloni and Amram. Achiyah HaShiloni was a prophet in the time of Yeravam ben Nevat — considerably later in history. For him to have seen Amram, who died in Mitzrayim, Achiyah would have had to be present in Mitzrayim while Amram was alive, then left with the Exodus — presumably at an advanced age — then traversed the wilderness, entered *Eretz Yisrael*, and lived for hundreds of years thereafter until the time of Yeravam. Moreover, if Amram died at 137, and the people were in Mitzrayim for approximately 210 years, then anyone who could have seen Amram alive would have been roughly seventy years old at the time of the Exodus. This compounds the difficulty: not only must we posit an extraordinary lifespan for Achiyah, but also that he survived the period of the *midbar* — when, after all, the verse says *lo notar mehem ish ki im Kalev ben Yefuneh v'Yehoshua bin Nun*.

### Levi Tribe Exempted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: לֹא נִגְזְרָה גְּזֵרָה עַל שִׁבְטוֹ שֶׁל לֵוִי, דִּכְתִיב: ״בַּמִּדְבָּר הַזֶּה יִפְּלוּ פִגְרֵיכֶם וְכׇל פְּקֻדֵיכֶם לְכׇל מִסְפַּרְכֶם מִבֶּן עֶשְׂרִים שָׁנָה וָמָעְלָה״ – מִי שֶׁפְּקוּדָיו מִבֶּן עֶשְׂרִים; יָצָא שִׁבְטוֹ שֶׁל לֵוִי, שֶׁפְּקוּדָיו מִבֶּן שְׁלֹשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hamnuna says:** The **decree** of death pronounced for the generation of the spies **was not decreed upon the tribe of Levi, as it is written: "Your carcasses shall fall in this wilderness, and all those who were counted among you, according to your whole number, from twenty years old and upward"** (Numbers 14:29). The verse is interpreted: The decree applies to **one whose count** in the census **is from the age of twenty** and up, **excluding the tribe of Levi, whose count is from the age of thirty** and up. Achiyah was a Levite, and he was not subject to the decree.

The Gemara answers the second difficulty first, through Rav Hamnuna: *lo nigzera gezeria al shivto shel Levi* — the decree of dying in the wilderness did not apply to the tribe of Levi. Achiyah HaShiloni was a Levi, and therefore not subject to the decree.

### Levi Counted at Thirty

The basis for this exemption is derived from the language of the verses themselves. The decree is formulated: *bamidbar hazeh yiflu pigreichem v'chol pkudeichem l'chol misparchem miben esrim shanah vama'alah* — in this wilderness your corpses shall fall, all of your counting, to all your numbers, from twenty years and upward. The phrase *mi she'pkudav miben esrim* — those who are counted from age twenty — excludes Levi, *she'pkudav miben shloshim* — for the Levites are counted from the age of thirty, as the verse states *yavo litzvo tzava ba'avodas Ohel Moed*. The Rashbam observes that even this formulation borrows the language of military service — *tzava* — parallel to *kol haba latzava* used for the general census, but for the Levites the threshold is thirty rather than twenty. Since the decree was framed in terms of those counted from twenty, and Levi was counted from thirty, Levi falls outside the decree's scope. Thus the entire tribe of Levi — not merely Yehoshua and Kalev — could have survived the desert period and entered *Eretz Yisrael*.

### Yair & Machir Survive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִשְּׁאָר שְׁבָטִים לָא עֲיֻיל? וְהָתַנְיָא: יָאִיר בֶּן מְנַשֶּׁה וּמָכִיר בֶּן מְנַשֶּׁה נוֹלְדוּ בִּימֵי יַעֲקֹב, וְלֹא מֵתוּ עַד שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל לָאָרֶץ – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּכּוּ מֵהֶם אַנְשֵׁי הָעַי כִּשְׁלֹשִׁים וְשִׁשָּׁה אִישׁ״, וְתַנְיָא: שְׁלֹשִׁים וְשִׁשָּׁה מַמָּשׁ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה: וְכִי נֶאֱמַר ״שְׁלֹשִׁים וְשִׁשָּׁה״? וַהֲלֹא לֹא נֶאֱמַר אֶלָּא ״כִּשְׁלֹשִׁים וְשִׁשָּׁה״! אֶלָּא זֶה יָאִיר בֶּן מְנַשֶּׁה, שֶׁשָּׁקוּל כְּרוּבָּהּ שֶׁל סַנְהֶדְרִין!
>
> **Translation:** **And** is it true that **no** one **from the other tribes entered** Eretz Yisrael? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Yair, son of Manasseh, and Machir, son of Manasseh, were born in the days of Jacob, and they did not die until the Jewish people entered Eretz Yisrael, as it is stated: "And the men of Ai killed of them about thirty-six men"** (Joshua 7:5). **And** concerning this verse, **it is taught: Literally thirty-six** people were killed, this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.** **Rabbi Neḥemya said to him: But is it stated** that **thirty-six** were killed? **Isn't it stated nothing other than: "About thirty-six"** were killed? **Rather, this** unusual term is referring to **Yair, son of Manasseh, who** was **equivalent to the majority of the Sanhedrin,** whose maximum number of judges is seventy-one, of which thirty-six is a slight majority. Evidently, then, Yair, son of Manasseh also survived the time in the wilderness.

Having opened this door, the Gemara immediately asks: *u'mishar shvatim lo hayu* — were there really no exceptions from the other tribes? The *Braita* is cited: Ya'ir ben Menashe and Machir ben Menashe were born in the days of Yaakov — *noldu bi'mei Yaakov* — yet they did not die until *Bnei Yisrael* entered the land — *v'lo meisu ad she'nichnesu Yisrael la'aretz*.

The proof that Ya'ir ben Menashe made it into *Eretz Yisrael* comes from the battle of Ha'Ai. The verse says *vayaku mehem anshei ha'Ai kishloshim v'shisha ish* — and the men of Ha'Ai struck down approximately thirty-six of them. A *Braita* records the debate: Rabbi Yehuda says it means literally thirty-six. Rabbi Nechemia objects: the verse says *kishloshim v'shisha*, *like* thirty-six, not exactly thirty-six. Rather, says Rabbi Nechemia, this refers to Ya'ir ben Menashe, who was *shakul k'rubah shel Sanhedrin* — equivalent in weight to the majority of the Sanhedrin. The Sanhedrin consists of seventy-one members; a majority requires thirty-six votes. The verse's formulation of "like thirty-six" is thus a reference to Ya'ir's singular importance. The assertion is that Ya'ir survived from the time of Yaakov all the way through the desert and into *Eretz Yisrael* — raising the same question as with Achiyah HaShiloni: how did he survive the desert decree?

One sharper question arises here as well: Tzelofchad — whose daughters are central to this entire sugya — was himself from the tribe of Menashe. If Ya'ir ben Menashe survived the desert, the question of how Tzelofchad did not, and why his daughters inherited from him, becomes more intricate.

### Ages 20-60 Decree Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: לֹא נִגְזְרָה גְּזֵירָה לֹא עַל פָּחוּת מִבֶּן עֶשְׂרִים, וְלֹא עַל יָתֵר מִבֶּן שִׁשִּׁים. לֹא עַל פָּחוּת מִבֶּן עֶשְׂרִים – דִּכְתִיב: ״מִבֶּן עֶשְׂרִים שָׁנָה וּמַעְלָה״. וְלֹא עַל יָתֵר מִבֶּן שִׁשִּׁים – גָּמַר ״וּמַעְלָה״–״וּמַעְלָה״ מֵעֲרָכִין; מָה לְהַלָּן – יָתֵר מִבֶּן שִׁשִּׁים כְּפָחוּת מִבֶּן עֶשְׂרִים, אַף כָּאן – יָתֵר מִבֶּן שִׁשִּׁים כְּפָחוּת מִבֶּן עֶשְׂרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said:** The **decree** of death **was not decreed either upon** those **less than twenty** years **old or upon** those **more than sixty** years **old** at the time of the sin of the spies. The Gemara explains: **Not upon** those **less than twenty** years **old, as it is written: "From twenty years old and upward"** (Numbers 14:29). **And not upon** those **more than sixty** years **old,** because he **learns** that there is a verbal analogy between **"and upward"** in the verse cited, and **"and upward" from** the *halakhot* of **valuations,** in the phrase: "From sixty years old and upward" (Leviticus 27:7). **Just as there,** concerning valuations, **more than sixty** years **old is comparable to less than twenty** years **old,** as there is a distinct category of those between the ages of twenty and sixty, **so too here, more than sixty** years **old is comparable to less than twenty** years **old** insofar as those older were not subject to the sentence. Yair, son of Manasseh, who was already older, did not die in the wilderness.

Rav Acha bar Yaakov provides the resolution: *lo nigzera gezeira lo al pachot miben esrim v'lo al yeser miben shishim* — the decree was not issued against those under twenty years old, and not against those over sixty years old. It applied only to the range of twenty to sixty.

### Over Sixty: Exempt

The textual basis for the lower bound is clear: the verse itself says *miben esrim shanah vama'alah*. The upper bound is derived through a *gezeira shava*: the word *ma'alah* appears both in the desert decree and in *Erchin*, where the Talmud treats those over sixty as a separate category, distinct from the twenty-to-sixty range. *Mah lehallan yeser miben shishim kepachot miben chaf* — just as in *Erchin* a person over sixty is treated differently from one in the twenty-to-sixty range, so too here, a person over sixty falls outside the scope of the decree. The Rashbam clarifies that this does not mean the over-sixty and under-twenty categories are identical to one another — they are not — but only that both stand outside the category against which the decree was directed.

This resolution now makes sense of both Achiyah HaShiloni and Ya'ir ben Menashe. If Amram died at 137, and the descent to Mitzrayim was roughly 210 years before the Exodus, then someone who could have seen Amram alive would have been approximately seventy at the Exodus — over sixty, and therefore exempt from the desert decree. Achiyah HaShiloni could thus have entered *Eretz Yisrael* and lived the additional centuries needed to reach the time of Yeravam. Similarly, Ya'ir ben Menashe, born in Yaakov's time, would have been well over sixty at the Exodus and likewise exempt.

As Rashi notes in the *Chumash*, this resolution considerably qualifies the apparent meaning of the verse that *only* Kalev and Yehoshua survived. In addition to the exemption of the entire tribe of Levi, and possibly the fifteen thousand whose decree was waived according to the Rashbam's reading of the *Midrash* — a reading Tosafot rejects — anyone over sixty was also exempt from the decree and could enter *Eretz Yisrael*. The categorical statement of the *pesukim* thus applies strictly to those in the twenty-to-sixty range who were counted in the original census.

## Division of Eretz Yisrael

### Equal vs. Proportional

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל – לִשְׁבָטִים אִיפְּלוּג, אוֹ דִּלְמָא לְקַרְקַף גַּבְרֵי אִיפְּלוּג?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Was **Eretz Yisrael divided according to** the **tribes,** meaning that each of the twelve tribes received an equal portion, and the members of each tribe divided these tribal portions according to their numbers; **or perhaps** Eretz Yisrael was **divided according to men's skulls,** meaning that a certain area of land was given to each individual, and the size of the tribal allotments was a function of the tribe's population?

The Gemara now transitions to a new question concerning the division of *Eretz Yisrael* among the tribes: *Eretz Yisrael lishvatim iplog o dilma lekarkaf gavrei iplog* — was the land divided into twelve equal sections, with the size of each individual's portion depending on whether his tribe was numerous or small? Or was each tribe's section proportional to its population, so that all individuals received equal per-capita portions regardless of their tribe?

### Territory Map Evidence

The intuitive answer — and the one strongly supported by visual evidence — is the second option. The map of tribal territories in Yehoshua shows sections of clearly varying sizes, suggesting that the division was proportional to population. The evidence from the geography of *Eretz Yisrael* itself seems to favor this reading decisively.

### Torah Verses on Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״בֵּין רַב לִמְעָט״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: "According to the lot shall their inheritance be divided **between the more and the fewer"** (Numbers 26:56). Evidently, whether the tribe had many or few people, the tribe as a whole received a portion equal to that of every other tribe, and each individual within the tribe received a different amount of land than those in other tribes.

Yet the Gemara brings two apparently contradictory *pesukim* and uses them to reach the opposite conclusion. One verse states *larav tarbeh nachalato velamaat tamit nachalato* — to the many you shall increase their portion and to the few you shall decrease their portion. Another verse states *bein rav lemaat* — whether many or few. How are these reconciled?

### Interpretation: Equal La…

The Gemara's resolution is that *bein rav lemaat* means each tribe receives the same amount of land regardless of its size. The former verse, which speaks of increasing and decreasing portions, refers to the distribution within each tribe — individuals within a more numerous tribe receive smaller personal allotments, while individuals within a smaller tribe receive larger ones. But the tribal sections themselves were of equal size.

### Division by Value Not Ar…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, תַּנְיָא: עֲתִידָה אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁתִּתְחַלֵּק לִשְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר שְׁבָטִים – שֶׁבַּתְּחִלָּה לֹא נִתְחַלְּקָה אֶלָּא לִשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר שְׁבָטִים. וְלֹא נִתְחַלְּקָה אֶלָּא בְּכֶסֶף – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בֵּין רַב לִמְעָט״. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: סְאָה בִּיהוּדָה, שָׁוָה חָמֵשׁ סְאִין בַּגָּלִיל.
>
> **Translation:** **And** another proof can be seen from that which **is taught** in a *baraita*: **Eretz Yisrael is destined to be divided among thirteen tribes** during the messianic era, unlike the division in the time of Joshua. **As, initially** the land was **divided only among twelve tribes,** as the Torah does not allot a portion to the tribe of Levi. The *baraita* continues: **And** the land was **divided only with money,** such that each tribe that received a portion more valuable than average compensated another tribe that had received a portion less valuable than average, **as it is stated: "Between the more and the fewer." Rabbi Yehuda said:** The area of land whose yield is **a *se'a*** of grain **in Judea** is so valuable that it is **equal** in value to the area necessary to produce **five *se'a*** of grain **in the Galilee.**

The *Braita* adds a further layer: *v'lo nitchalkah ela b'kesef* — it was divided not simply by area but by value. Rav Yehuda illustrates: *seah biYehudah shaveh chamesh sein baGalil* — one *seah* of land in Judah equals five *seah* in the Galilee in value. The land in Judah was far more valuable than equivalent acreage in the Galilee. The division thus aimed at equalization of value rather than equalization of area. Two mechanisms could achieve this: giving people different sizes of land but of equal worth, or giving everyone the same size and then compensating the difference financially.

### Urim v'Tummim & Lots

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא נִתְחַלְּקָה אֶלָּא בְּגוֹרָל – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אַךְ בְּגוֹרָל״. וְלֹא נִתְחַלְּקָה אֶלָּא בְּאוּרִים וְתוּמִּים – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עַל פִּי הַגּוֹרָל״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And** the land was **divided only by a lottery, as it is stated: "Only by lot** shall the land be divided" (Numbers 26:55). **And** the land was **divided only with** the ***Urim VeTummim*, as it is stated: "By the pronouncement of the lot"** (Numbers 26:56).

The *Braita* further states: *v'lo nitchalkah ela b'goral* — it was divided by lot, as the verse says *ach b'goral techalek ha'aretz*; and *v'lo nitchalkah ela b'Urim v'Tummim* — it was also conducted through the *Urim v'Tummim*, as the verse says *al pi hagoral*, by the mouth of the lot. The word *pi* — mouth — implies that there was speech, an oracle, emerging from the *Urim v'Tummim*, not merely a silent drawing.

### Drawing Lots Procedure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כֵּיצַד? אֶלְעָזָר מְלוּבָּשׁ אוּרִים וְתוּמִּים, וִיהוֹשֻׁעַ וְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל עוֹמְדִים לְפָנָיו; וְקַלְפִּי שֶׁל שְׁבָטִים וְקַלְפִּי שֶׁל תְּחוּמִין מוּנָּחִים לְפָנָיו; וְהָיָה מְכַוֵּין בְּרוּחַ הַקֹּדֶשׁ וְאוֹמֵר: זְבוּלֻן עוֹלֶה, תְּחוּם עַכּוֹ עוֹלֶה עִמּוֹ. טָרַף בְּקַלְפִּי שֶׁל שְׁבָטִים – וְעָלָה בְּיָדוֹ זְבוּלֻן, טָרַף בְּקַלְפִּי שֶׁל תְּחוּמִין – וְעָלָה בְּיָדוֹ תְּחוּם עַכּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **How** can **these** texts be reconciled? One indicates that the land was divided by lottery and the other indicates that the land was divided with the *Urim VeTummim*. The *baraita* explains: **Elazar** the High Priest was **dressed** with the ***Urim VeTummim*, and Joshua and all the Jewish people** were **standing before him, and** a lottery **receptacle** containing the names **of** the **tribes and** another lottery **receptacle** containing the names **of** the **boundaries** of the twelve different regions of Eretz Yisrael were **placed before him.** **And** Elazar **would ascertain** the assignments of land **with the Divine Spirit and say,** in accordance with the notification of the *Urim VeTummim*: The name of the tribe **Zebulun** now **emerges** from the receptacle in the lottery, and the region whose **boundary** is **Akko emerges with it** from the other receptacle. After stating this, he would **mix** the lots **in the receptacle of the tribes and** the lot of **Zebulun** would **emerge in his hand.** He would then **mix** the lots **in the receptacle of the boundaries, and** the **boundary** of **Akko** would **emerge in his hand.**

How could the division have been accomplished by both lot and prophetic oracle simultaneously? The *Braita* describes the procedure in detail. Elazar the High Priest stood dressed in the *Urim v'Tummim*, with Yehoshua and all of Israel assembled before him. Two boxes were placed before him: one containing placards bearing the names of the twelve tribes, the other containing lots bearing the names of the territorial sections of *Eretz Yisrael*. Before reaching into either box, Elazar would first prophesy through the *Urim v'Tummim*, declaring what he was about to draw. He would say, for example: *Zevulun oleh, tchum Akko oleh imo* — Zevulun is going to come up, and the boundary of Akko will come up with it. He would then mix the lots in the tribal box and draw — and Zevulun would emerge. He would then mix the lots in the territorial box and draw — and the boundary of Akko would emerge. And so on for each tribe in turn.

### Continuation: Naftali Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחוֹזֵר וּמְכַוֵּין בְּרוּחַ הַקֹּדֶשׁ וְאוֹמֵר: נַפְתָּלִי עוֹלֶה, וּתְחוּם גִּינּוֹסַר עוֹלֶה עִמּוֹ. טָרַף בְּקַלְפִּי שֶׁל שְׁבָטִים – וְעָלָה בְּיָדוֹ נַפְתָּלִי, טָרַף בְּקַלְפִּי שֶׁל תְּחוּמִין – וְעָלָה בְּיָדוֹ תְּחוּם גִּינּוֹסַר. וְכֵן כׇּל שֵׁבֶט וָשֵׁבֶט.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Elazar **would repeat** the process **and ascertain** the assignments **with the Divine Spirit and say:** The name of the tribe **Naftali** now **emerges, and** the region whose **boundary** is **Ginnosar emerges with it** from the other receptacle. After stating this, he would **mix** the lots **in the receptacle of the tribes and** the lot of **Naftali** would **emerge in his hand.** He would then **mix** the lots **in the receptacle of the boundaries, and** the **boundary** of **Ginnosar** would **emerge in his hand. And so** he would proceed for **each and every tribe.**

The Rashbam explains the purpose of this dual procedure: so that the people would recognize that the outcome was divinely ordained and not the product of chance. If lots alone were drawn without prophecy, an unhappy tribe might attribute its portion to bad luck. But when the outcome was declared in advance and then confirmed by the draw, all could see that God Himself had assigned each tribe its inheritance. One might note the inverse concern as well: if the draw always matched the prophecy, a skeptic could suspect the process was rigged. But the combination of public prophecy, witnessed drawing, and consistent confirmation served to establish the divine character of the division.

### Future Division: 13 Sect…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא כַּחֲלוּקָּה שֶׁל עוֹלָם הַזֶּה, חֲלוּקָּה שֶׁל עוֹלָם הַבָּא. הָעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, אָדָם יֵשׁ לוֹ שְׂדֵה לָבָן – אֵין לוֹ שְׂדֵה פַרְדֵּס, שְׂדֵה פַרְדֵּס – אֵין לוֹ שְׂדֵה לָבָן. לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, אֵין לָךְ כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד שֶׁאֵין לוֹ בָּהָר וּבַשְּׁפֵלָה וּבָעֵמֶק, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: שַׁעַר רְאוּבֵן אֶחָד, שַׁעַר יְהוּדָה אֶחָד, שַׁעַר לֵוִי אֶחָד״. הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מְחַלֵּק לָהֶן בְּעַצְמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֵלֶּה מַחְלְקֹתָם נְאֻם ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And unlike the division in this world,** i.e., in the time of Joshua, will be the **division** of portions **in the World-to-Come,** i.e., in the messianic era. In **this world,** if **a person has a field of grain, he does not have a field** for **an orchard;** if he has **a field** for **an orchard, he does not have a field of grain.** This is so because each climate and variety of soil is suitable for a different type of produce. But **in the World-to-Come, you do not have any person who does not have** a portion in Eretz Yisrael **in the mountain, and in the lowland, and in the valley, as it is stated: "The gate of Reuben, one; the gate of Judah, one; the gate of Levi, one"** (Ezekiel 48:31), which is to say that everyone's portion will be the same. And **the Holy One, Blessed be He, will distribute it to them personally, as it is stated: "And these are their portions, says the Lord"** (Ezekiel 48:29). This is the conclusion of the *baraita*.

The *Braita* contrasts this with the division that will take place in the future, at the time of *Olam HaBa*. It is worth noting that *Olam HaBa* in Talmudic usage typically refers not to some post-mortem spiritual realm but to the future messianic era, when all of Israel will be living in *Eretz Yisrael* on this physical earth. That future division will differ from the present one in several respects.

First, it will encompass thirteen portions rather than twelve — the tribe of Levi will receive its own section along with the others. Second, the inequities of the present world will be remedied: *adam yesh lo sadeh lavan ein lo sadeh pardes* — in this world one might have a wheat field but no orchard, or an orchard but no wheat field. *B'Olam HaBa ein lecha kol echad v'echad she'ein lo bahar u'vashefeilah u'va'emek* — in the world to come, every individual will have a portion that includes mountain, lowland, and valley. Third, the division will be performed by God directly: *veHaKadosh Baruch Hu m'chalek lahen b'atzmo* — the Holy One will distribute it Himself, as the verse says *ve'eleh machlekotam neum Hashem*.

### Conclusion: 12 Equal Por…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת: ״שֶׁבַּתְּחִלָּה לֹא נִתְחַלְּקָה אֶלָּא לִשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר שְׁבָטִים״; שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, לִשְׁבָטִים אִיפְּלוּג! שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states its proof: **In any event,** the *baraita* **teaches: As initially,** the land was **divided only among** the **twelve tribes. Conclude from** the *baraita* that the land **was divided according to** the **tribes,** and not apportioned directly to each person. The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from** the *baraita* that this is the case.

The Gemara concludes from the *Braita*: *katani mi-ha she'b'tchilla lo nitchalkah ela lishneim asar shevatim* — the tradition states explicitly that originally it was divided into twelve. The Gemara reads this as establishing that the division was into twelve equal sections, thereby proving *lishvatim iplog* — that the tribal sections themselves were of equal size. Individuals in a numerous tribe therefore received smaller personal portions than individuals in a small tribe.

This conclusion is in some tension with the visual evidence of the territorial map, where the sections clearly vary in size. Yet the Gemara consistently returns to the principle of *bein rav lemaat* — whether many or few, each tribe received an equal share — and the conclusion the Gemara draws is that equality operated at the tribal level. Each of the twelve tribes received a section of equal value, and within that section, the size of each individual's personal allotment was inversely proportional to the tribe's population.
