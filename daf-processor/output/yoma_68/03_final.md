# Yoma 68 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Par V'Sa'ir Nisrafim

### Ba Lo Etzel Par V'Sa'ir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בָּא לוֹ אֵצֶל פַּר וְשָׂעִיר הַנִּשְׂרָפִין, קְרָעָן, וְהוֹצִיא אֶת אֵימוּרֵיהֶן, נְתָנָן בְּמָגֵיס וְהִקְטִירָן עַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: After the High Priest passed the goat to the man tasked with dispatching the goat, **he came next to the bull and the goat that were to be burned. He tore their** flesh but was not required to cut it into pieces, **and removed the portions** of the offering consumed on the altar **and placed them in a large bowl [*magis*] and burned them on the altar.**

The *mishna* at the bottom of 67b brings us to the next stage of the *Kohen Gadol*'s *avoda* on Yom Kippur. Having completed the blood service — slaughtering the ox, performing the *ketoret*, sprinkling the blood of the ox and the goat in the *Kodesh HaKodashim*, on the *parochet*, in the *Ohel Mo'ed*, on the golden *mizbe'ach*, and at the base of the outer *mizbe'ach* — and having dispatched the *sa'ir la-Azazel* to the wilderness, the *Kohen Gadol* now turns his attention to the carcasses of the ox and the goat whose blood was used in the inner service. The *mishna* states: *ba lo etzel par v'se'ir hanisrafin* — he came to the ox and the goat that are to be burned, for that is what is done with their carcasses.

### Hotzi Es Imureihem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְלָעָן בְּמִקְלָעוֹת וְהוֹצִיאָן לְבֵית הַשְּׂרֵיפָה. וּמֵאֵימָתַי מְטַמְּאִין בְּגָדִים — מִשֶּׁיֵּצְאוּ חוּץ לְחוֹמַת הָעֲזָרָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מִשֶּׁיַּצִּית הָאוּר בְּרוּבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **He interwove** and bound the bull and the goat together **into braids** as one mass. They were placed in that way upon two poles and carried by four people who **took them out to the place** designated for **burning,** outside of Jerusalem. **And at what point do the** bull and goat **render** the **garments** of those who carried them **impure,** as it is written: "And he who burns them shall wash his garments" (Leviticus 16:28)? They render the garments impure **from** the moment **that they emerged outside the wall of the** Temple **courtyard. Rabbi Shimon says:** They render the garments impure **from** the moment **that the fire has ignited in most of** the bull and the goat.

The *mishna* continues: *kera'an v'hotzi es imureihem* — he split them open and removed their inner organs, the fats and the internal parts designated for the *mizbe'ach*. Although the blood of these animals was offered in the inner precincts rather than on the outer *mizbe'ach*, the *imurim* — the fats and inner organs — are treated as in all *korbanot* and are burned on the outer *mizbe'ach*. He then placed them in a vessel, a *magis*, a bowl of some kind, for the purpose of later burning them on the *mizbe'ach*. One might ask why the *Kohen Gadol* himself tears open the carcasses; the subject of the verb is somewhat ambiguous in the *mishna*, and when we reach the phrase *v'hotzi'an l'veis hasreifa* — he took them out to the place of burning — it becomes clear that this was not done personally by the *Kohen Gadol* at all. Whether he himself actually cut them open is therefore a reasonable question, though the text does not resolve it definitively.

### Kela'an B'miklaot

The *mishna* describes a further step: *kela'an b'miklaot* — he twisted them, or as Rashi explains, braided them, onto a type of wooden carrying frame. This allowed the carcasses to be transported securely, interlocked so that nothing would slip free, and then they were taken out to the place where they would be burned. The actual removal is carried out by others, not by the *Kohen Gadol* himself, since the Torah mandates that these carcasses be burned outside the *Beis HaMikdash*.

### Mimasi Mitamim B'gadim

The *mishna* then raises the parallel question to what was asked regarding the *ish iti* who escorted the *sa'ir* to the wilderness: *u'mei'emasi mitam'in bgadim* — from when do the garments become *tamei*? The Torah states *v'hasoref osam yechabes bgadav* — the one who burns them must launder his garments, and he and his garments become *tamei*. The question is when precisely this *tumah* takes effect.

The *tanna kamma* rules: *misheyatze'u chutz l'chomas azara* — as soon as the carcasses leave the wall of the *azara*, the courtyard. This is notably different from the parallel ruling regarding the *sa'ir*, where the *tanna kamma* said the relevant threshold is leaving the walls of Yerushalayim. Here, leaving the *azara* already triggers the *tumah* of the *begadim*, even though the Torah's language refers to the act of burning, which takes place much farther away.

Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *mishetetzat ha'or beruban* — only once the fire has seized the majority of the carcass does the *tumah* begin. This is a consistent position. In the previous *mishna*, Rabbi Shimon held that the person escorting the *sa'ir* becomes *tamei* only when the goat actually goes over the cliff. Here he similarly requires the completion of the act — the fire taking hold of the majority of the carcass — before *tumah* begins. The *tanna kamma*, by contrast, holds that leaving the relevant precincts is sufficient to trigger *tumah*, even before the burning is accomplished.

This discussion also connects to a broader set of three distinct questions that arise regarding the *sa'ir*: when was the *avoda* minimally performed, such that one is considered to have fulfilled the service even *bedi'avad* — perhaps even if the goat died after the *vidui*; when is the *mitzvah* considered fulfilled, *yatza mitzvatah* — perhaps as soon as it leaves Yerushalayim; and when is the full *kappara* achieved, corresponding perhaps to when it goes over the cliff. These questions, and their relationship to when the red string turned white, will be revisited later in the *daf*.

## Vehiktiran Salka Da'atach

### Lo Okim Lah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ ״וְהִקְטִירָן״ סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: לְהַקְטִירָן עַל גַּבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: It was taught in the mishna that the High Priest removed the portions of the offering consumed on the altar and burned them on the altar. The Gemara expresses surprise: **Would it enter your mind** to say **he burned them?** He would not burn them right away, since he had to perform other services first. **Rather, say** that he placed them in a large bowl in order **to burn them on the altar** later.

The *Gemara* immediately raises a difficulty with the *mishna*'s phrase *netanam b'magis v'hiktiran al gabei ha'mizbe'ach* — he placed them in a vessel and burned them on the *mizbe'ach*. The *Gemara* asks: *vehiktiran salka da'atach?* — do you really think he burned them at this point? The burning of the inner organs does not occur at this stage in the *seder avoda*. It happens later, after other services, after the *musaf*, after the removal of the *kaf*. The text cannot literally mean that the *Kohen Gadol* offers them immediately at this moment.

The *Gemara* therefore emends the reading: *ella eima lehaktiran* — the *mishna* means he placed them there *for the purpose* of later burning them on the *mizbe'ach*. The act being described is the preparation and holding of the *imurim*, not their immediate offering.

### Magis V'Kli Sherut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״קְלָעָן בְּמִקְלָעוֹת״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּמִין קְלִיעָה. תָּנָא: לֹא הָיָה מְנַתְּחָן נִיתּוּחַ בְּשַׂר עוֹלָה, אֶלָּא עוֹר עַל גַּבֵּי בָּשָׂר. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן עוֹר וּבָשָׂר וָפֶרֶשׁ, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן עוֹר וּבָשָׂר וָפֶרֶשׁ,
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna that the High Priest **interwove** and bound the bull and the goat together **into braids** as one mass before taking them to be burned. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** He made them **like a braid,** whose parts are interwoven. **It was taught: He would not dissect** the bull and goat in the manner of the **dissection of the flesh of a burnt-offering,** where the animal is skinned and then cut into parts. **Rather,** he would leave the **skin on top of the flesh** and would cut the animal into pieces without skinning it. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **As it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: It is stated here** with regard to the bull and goat, **skin and flesh and dung** (Leviticus 16:27), **and it is stated below,** with regard to the bull sin-offerings that are burned, **skin and flesh and dung** (Leviticus 4:11).

This gives rise to a further question addressed by Rashi and a large *Tosafot*, which touches on a parallel *Gemara* elsewhere: why was a bowl, a *magis*, used at all? In the case of the *tamid*, for example, the limbs were carried directly without a vessel. The *imurim* could simply be held by hand and placed on the *mizbe'ach*. The answer is practical: unlike a *mincha*, where placing the *kometz* in a vessel is part of the formal act of sanctification, here no such requirement exists. However, since the *imurim* will not be offered immediately — there will be a wait of some time before they are placed on the fire — they need to be stored somehow. Placing them on the ground is not a reasonable option, particularly given the substantial quantity of material from an ox. The bowl serves as interim storage. When the moment comes to actually place them on the *mizbe'ach*, one does not need to hold the bowl the way one does with the blood; one can simply take them out by hand. The vessel here is a practical necessity, not a halakhic requirement of the offering itself.

### Lo Hayu M'natchin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְהַלָּן, עַל יְדֵי נִיתּוּחַ וְלֹא עַל יְדֵי הֶפְשֵׁט, אַף כָּאן, עַל יְדֵי נִיתּוּחַ וְלֹא עַל יְדֵי הֶפְשֵׁט.
>
> **Translation:** **Just as below** it is prepared for burning **by means of dissection and not by means of skinning, so too here** it is **by means of dissection and not by means of skinning.**

A *baraita* is introduced: *lo hayu m'natchin nituach basar basar olah* — they would not dismember the carcasses the way one dismembers the meat of an *olah*. The contrast is: *or al gabei basar* — by the *olah*, one flays the skin, and that leather is given to the *kohanim*, who benefit from it significantly. Here, however, the carcasses are burned with the skin still on the flesh. They are dismembered but not flayed.

## Or V'basar B'yachad

### Netzar Kan V'Nizchar L'h…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתָם מְנָא לַן? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְקִרְבּוֹ וּפִרְשׁוֹ וְהוֹצִיא״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמּוֹצִיאוֹ שָׁלֵם. יָכוֹל יִשְׂרְפֶנּוּ שָׁלֵם? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן: ״רֹאשׁוֹ וּכְרָעָיו״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן: (״רֹאשׁ וּכְרָעַיִם״), מָה לְהַלָּן עַל יְדֵי נִיתּוּחַ, אַף כָּאן עַל יְדֵי נִיתּוּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And there,** with regard to bull sin-offerings that are burned, **from where do we** derive that they are cut but not skinned? The Gemara answers: **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: "And the skin of the bull and all its flesh, with its head and with its legs, **and its innards, and its dung,** even the whole bull **shall he carry** outside the camp to a clean place, where the ashes are poured out, and burn it on wood with fire" (Leviticus 4:11–12). This **teaches that he brings it out whole.** One **might** have thought **that he should burn it whole. It is stated here: Its head and its legs, and it is stated there,** with regard to burnt-offerings, **head and legs. Just as there,** the burnt-offering is performed **by means of dissection,** as explicitly stated in the verse, **so too here it is by means of dissection.**

The *Gemara* asks: *minalan de-ha?* — what is the source for this rule? A *baraita* is cited in the name of Rebbe: *ne'emar kan or basar u-feresh* — it says here, in the context of Yom Kippur, that you burn them with their skin, their flesh, and their *peresh*, their excrement or undigested matter. *V'ne'emar l'hallan or u-vasar u-feresh* — and it says elsewhere, by the other cases of animals whose blood is brought inside the *Heichal*, such as the *par kohen gadol* and the *par he'elam davar* described in Vayikra 4, that their skin, flesh, and dung are taken outside and burned.

The connection works as follows. In the *parasha* of the *olah* in Vayikra, the Torah describes the burning of the *olah* and mentions *rosho v'al k'ra'av* — its head and its legs — as well as *v'kirbo u'chra'av*, the inner organs and the legs. Those same terms, *rosho* and *k'ra'av*, appear in our context as well. Because the language parallels that of the *olah*, we derive from the *olah* the law of *nituach* — dismemberment. From the *olah* we learn that the carcass is cut into pieces before burning.

However, from the *olah* we do not derive *hifshit* — flaying. The *olah* is flayed (*v'hifshit es ha'olah*) and dismembered (*v'nitach osah l'nesacheha*), but here we apply only the *nituach* and not the *hifshit*. The reason is grounded in the logic of keeping the carcass as a unified whole.

### Nitach V'Lo Hifshit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מָה לְהַלָּן עַל יְדֵי הֶפְשֵׁט, אַף כָּאן עַל יְדֵי הֶפְשֵׁט, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְקִרְבּוֹ וּפִרְשׁוֹ״. מַאי תַּלְמוּדָא? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁפִּרְשׁוֹ בְּקִרְבּוֹ, כָּךְ בְּשָׂרוֹ בְּעוֹרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** so, derive the following from the same verbal analogy: **Just as there,** in the case of burnt-offerings, the dissection is performed **with skinning, so too here** it is **with skinning,** and he must remove the skin of the animals before burning them. The Gemara answers: Therefore, **the verse states "and its innards, and its dung."** The Gemara asks: **What is the derivation** from this phrase? **Rav Pappa said: Just as its dung is within its innards** and they do not remove it, **so too, its flesh is in its skin** and they do not separate the skin from the flesh.

The *Gemara* explains why the flaying is excluded even though the dismemberment is learned from the *olah*. The Torah says *v'kirbo u'firsho v'hotzi* — and its innards and its dung, take it outside. The *peresh*, the excrement or undigested contents, remains *inside* the carcass as it is taken out. It is not removed and separated. This teaches us that the carcass goes out as a unit — the contents and the container together. The same logic applies to the skin and the flesh: just as the *peresh* is not separated from the inner cavity, so too the *or* and the *basar* remain together. You flay a carcass when you want to separate skin from flesh; here, since the whole animal goes as a unit, you do not flay it.

Thus, the *gezeirah shavah* from the *olah* teaches *nituach* — that you cut the carcass into pieces — while the internal reasoning of the *parasha* itself, from the principle of keeping the *peresh* inside, teaches us that the skin remains attached to the flesh throughout.

### Kemin Klia V'Or

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דְּשַׁוְּיֵהּ חֲרוֹכָא.
>
> **Translation:** There is a difference between them when he turned it into a charred mass and the form of the animal has already become distorted but has not actually become ash.

Rabbi Yochanan had already offered a description of the braiding: *kemin klia* — like a braid. This interlacing on the carrying frame, as noted earlier, allowed the dismembered pieces of both the ox and the goat to be held together securely on the frame for transport. The logic of keeping skin and flesh united is thus further reflected in the physical manner in which the carcasses were transported: wrapped and interlocked, the skin functioning as a container for the flesh, much as it does for the *peresh*, so that the whole goes out and burns as one.

## Chutz L'shalosh Machanot

### Michutz Lamachaneh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״יוֹצִיא אֶל מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה וְשָׂרְפוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** a *baraita* based on the verse: "The bull of the sin-offering and the goat of the sin-offering whose blood was brought in to gain atonement in the sacred place, **shall be taken outside the camp and they shall burn"** (Leviticus 16:27).

A *tanna* taught: *v'hotzi el michutz lamachaneh v'sarafu* — you shall take them out of the camp and burn them. This is the verse from the Yom Kippur context. The *baraita* then raises the tension: *l'halan atah nosein lahem shalosh machanot* — in other cases of animals burned outside, you apply three camps; *v'kan atah nosein lahem machaneh achat* — here the Torah says only *michutz lamachaneh*, suggesting a single camp.

To understand this properly, one must recall the structure of the three camps. *Machaneh shechinah* is the *Beis HaMikdash* itself, corresponding in the desert to the area of the *mishkan*. *Machaneh leviyah* corresponds in Temple times to *Har HaBayit*. *Machaneh Yisrael* corresponds to Yerushalayim itself. When the Torah says *el michutz lamachaneh* — take it outside the camp — a single reference would seem to indicate outside only the first camp, meaning you burn the carcasses on *Har HaBayit*, just outside the *azara*. Does that mean you could burn them right there on *Har HaBayit* rather than outside Yerushalayim?

The *baraita* takes for granted that the normal case — such as the *par he'elam davar* in Vayikra 4 — requires burning outside all three camps, outside Yerushalayim. The question is therefore what function the single *michutz lamachaneh* in our Yom Kippur passage serves. The answer: *lomar lach kivan sheyatza chutz lemachaneh achat mitam'in begadim* — to tell you that as soon as it exits the first camp, as soon as it leaves the *Beis HaMikdash*, that is when the garments become *tamei*. The Torah does not intend to limit the burning to one camp; rather, this single reference to leaving the camp establishes the moment at which *tumah* begins.

### Lehalan At Noheg

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתָם מְנָא לַן? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְהוֹצִיא אֶת כׇּל הַפָּר״ — חוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ מַחֲנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And there,** with regard to other bull sin-offerings that are burned, **from where do we** derive that they are removed from all three camps? The Gemara explains: **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: It is stated about the bull sin-offering of the High Priest: **"Even the whole bull shall he carry outside** the camp…and burn it" (Leviticus 4:12), meaning that he should take it **outside of three camps.**

The *Gemara* then asks: how do we know that in the other cases — the *par kohen gadol* and the *par he'elam davar* — the burning takes place outside all three camps, outside Yerushalayim? A *baraita* teaches the derivation systematically.

By the *par kohen gadol*, the Torah says *el michutz lamachaneh* — take it outside the camp. One might have said this means only outside the first camp, burning it on *Har HaBayit*. But the *par he'elam davar* also says *michutz lamachaneh*, and additionally it says *ka'asher asah et hapar harishon* — just as you did with the first ox. If the Torah already instructs you to treat them identically, why repeat *michutz lamachaneh*? The additional mention teaches a second camp — you must go further out. That gives us two camps.

Then, when the Torah discusses the *trumat hadeshen* — the removal of ashes from the *mizbe'ach* — it says the ashes are to be taken *el michutz lamachaneh*, outside the camp. But we already know from the burning of the *parim* that the ashes go outside the camp; the place of burning, *shefech hadeshen*, already implies outside the camp. Why repeat it? *Tein lo machaneh shlishit* — this gives us a third camp. The burning of all these animals, and similarly the carcasses of the Yom Kippur animals, must be performed outside all three camps — outside Yerushalayim.

### Lifnei Ma Nemar Michutz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתָּה אוֹמֵר חוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ מַחֲנוֹת, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא חוּץ לְמַחֲנֶה אַחַת? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר בְּפַר הָעֵדָה ״אֶל מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״, שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר, שֶׁהֲרֵי כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״וְשָׂרַף אוֹתוֹ כַּאֲשֶׁר שָׂרַף אֵת הַפָּר הָרִאשׁוֹן״, וּמָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אֶל מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״ — לִיתֵּן לוֹ מַחֲנֶה שְׁנִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Do **you say** that he takes it **outside of three camps, or is** he required to take it **outside of only one camp? When it says** with regard to **the bull** sin-offering brought for the sin of **the community:** "He shall carry the bull **outside the camp"** (Leviticus 4:21), that verse requires explanation, **as** there is **no** need for **the verse to state** this, **since it was already stated: "And burn it as he burned the first bull"** (Leviticus 4:21), which indicates that all the *halakhot* of the bull sin-offering of a High Priest apply to the bull sin-offering of the community as well. **And what** is the meaning when **the verse states outside the camp? To give it a second camp,** i.e., to indicate that it must be removed from the Levite camp in addition to the camp of the Divine Presence.

Given that we derive from the other cases that the burning is outside Yerushalayim, the single-camp language in the Yom Kippur *parasha* cannot be telling us the location of burning. As established, it comes to teach the moment at which *tumah* takes hold: *kivan sheyatza chutz lemachaneh achat mitam'im begadim* — as soon as it exits the first camp, the *Beis HaMikdash*, the garments become *tamei*. The full burning takes place outside Yerushalayim, but the *tumah* begins the moment the carcasses leave the *azara*.

According to the *chachamim*, the burning takes place to the north of Yerushalayim, since the principle for any *chatat* is that it is associated with the north — the *chatat* is slaughtered on the north side of the *mizbe'ach*, and the north is the directional association for sin offerings generally.

### Rabbi Shimon V'Parah Adu…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, הַאי ״מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״ וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה״, מָה כָּאן חוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ מַחֲנוֹת, אַף לְהַלָּן חוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ מַחֲנוֹת. וּמָה לְהַלָּן בְּמִזְרָחָהּ שֶׁל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם — אַף כָּאן בְּמִזְרָחָהּ שֶׁל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what does Rabbi Shimon do with** the phrase **outside the camp,** as he holds that those carrying the bull and goat are rendered impure only once they leave all three camps? The Gemara answers: **He needs it for that which was taught** in a *baraita*, that **Rabbi Eliezer says: It is stated here,** with regard to the bull of Yom Kippur, **outside the camp, and it is stated there,** with regard to the red heifer, **"outside the camp"** (Numbers 19:3). **Just as here,** it is burned **outside three camps, so too there** the heifer, it is burned **outside three camps. And just as there** it is burned **east of Jerusalem,** since the heifer must be burned "toward the front of the Tent of Meeting" (Numbers 19:4), opposite the entrance to the Temple to its east, **so too here** the bull and goat of Yom Kippur are burned **east of Jerusalem.**

Rabbi Shimon, who holds that *tumah* begins only at the moment of actual burning, has no need for the single-camp language to teach the onset of *tumah*. He therefore uses it differently: *ne'emar kan michutz lamachaneh v'ne'emar l'hallan michutz lamachaneh* — it says here *michutz lamachaneh* and it says there, in Bamidbar, *michutz lamachaneh* by the *parah adumah*. This is the teaching of Rabbi Eliezer, which Rabbi Shimon accepts: just as the burning here is outside all three camps, so too the *parah adumah* is performed outside all three camps, outside Yerushalayim — specifically on *Har HaZeitim*, to the east. This is confirmed by the requirement that the blood of the *parah adumah* be sprinkled toward the opening of the *Heichal*, which means the officiant must stand east of *Har HaBayit*, looking westward.

From this linkage Rabbi Shimon derives a reciprocal lesson: *u'mah l'hallan bemizrachah shel Yerushalayim* — just as the *parah adumah* is performed to the east of Yerushalayim, *af kan bemizrachah shel Yerushalayim* — so too the burning of our carcasses is to the east of Yerushalayim. Thus Rabbi Shimon uses the extra *michutz lamachaneh* to forge a *gezeirah shavah* with the *parah adumah*, teaching both that the *parah adumah* is outside Yerushalayim and that the burning of the Yom Kippur animals is specifically to the east.

The *chachamim*, by contrast, do not derive their knowledge of the *parah adumah*'s location from this verse — presumably from another source — and use the single-camp mention to teach the onset of *tumah* rather than to establish direction. They hold the burning is to the north.

## Al Beis Hadeshen

### Sheyadim Sham Deshen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לִצְפוֹנָהּ שֶׁל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם, וְחוּץ לְשָׁלֹשׁ מַחֲנוֹת. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אַבֵּית הַדֶּשֶׁן נִשְׂרָפִין.
>
> **Translation:** **North of Jerusalem, and outside of three camps. Rabbi Yosei says: They are burned in the place of the ashes.**

The *baraita* records the opinion of Rabbi Yossi: *al beis hadeshen nisrafin* — they are burned at the *beis hadeshen*, the place where the ashes are deposited. This is consistent with the earlier derivation that the Torah says *al shefech hadeshen yisareif* — it shall be burned at the place where the ashes are poured. The *chiddush* in Rabbi Yossi's opinion, as it is understood, is that there must actually be ashes present at the site — not merely that the site is designated as a place for ashes, but that physical ashes must be there at the time of the burning. Since the Torah says *shefech hadeshen*, the word *shefech* implies active presence of ashes, not merely a designated location.

### Makom Meshupach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאן תְּנָא דִּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי — רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב הוּא. דְּתַנְיָא: ״אֶל שֶׁפֶךְ הַדֶּשֶׁן יִשָּׂרֵף״, שֶׁיְּהֵא לְשֵׁם דֶּשֶׁן, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: שֶׁיְּהֵא מְקוֹמוֹ מְשׁוּפָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Who is** the ***tanna* who disagrees with Rabbi Yosei** on this issue? **It is Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, as it was taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: **"Where the ashes are poured out [*shefekh hadeshen*] shall it be burned"** (Leviticus 4:12), which means **that there shall** already **be ash there** in that place, so that it is known as the ash heap even before this animal is burned there. **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says:** This verse indicates **that its place should be slanted [*meshupakh*]** so that ash that is deposited there will roll downhill. Rava understood that whereas Rabbi Yosei requires that there already be ash present when the bull is burned, Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov does not.

Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov disagrees. He reads *shefech hadeshen* not as requiring the literal presence of ashes but as requiring that the terrain be *meshupach* — sloped, so that ashes poured there would naturally flow downward. The site must have the physical characteristic of a place where things pour and cascade, but it need not have actual ashes present at the time of the burning.

### Dilma Makom Meshupach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְדִילְמָא בִּמְקוֹמוֹ מְשׁוּפָּךְ הוּא דִּפְלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him:** There is no proof from here, as **perhaps they disagree** only **about whether the place must be slanted.** Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov may agree there should be ash there to begin with, but he adds that the place must also be slanted. Therefore, there is no proof to support Rava's statement.

Abaye raises a subtle point: perhaps Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov is not actually contradicting Rabbi Yossi on the question of whether ashes must be present. Perhaps he is adding an additional criterion. *Dilma b'makom meshupach hu d'paligi* — perhaps the dispute is specifically about whether the terrain must be sloped. Rabbi Yossi requires ashes but may be silent on the question of slope; Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov requires the sloped terrain and may agree that ashes are also needed. The two positions would then not be mutually exclusive but complementary, with Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov adding a condition that the *tanna kamma* did not specify.

## Hasoreif Metamei Begadim

### Velo Hamatzis Es Ha'or

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְהַשּׂוֹרֵף״ — הַשּׂוֹרֵף מְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים וְלֹא הַמַּצִּית אֶת הָאוּר וְלֹא הַמְסַדֵּר אֶת הַמַּעֲרָכָה. וְאֵי זֶהוּ הַשּׂוֹרֵף? זֶה הַמְסַיֵּיעַ בִּשְׁעַת שְׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** It states: **"And he who burns** them shall wash his garments" (Leviticus 16:28), to indicate that only the **garments** of the one **who burns** the bull and goat of Yom Kippur **are rendered impure, but not** the garments of the one **who kindles the fire, and not** the garments of the one **who arranges the pile** of wood. **And who is the one who burns? It is the one who assists at the** actual **time of burning.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the detailed laws of who becomes *tamei* during the burning. A *baraita* teaches: *v'hasoref osam yechabeis begadav* — the one who burns them becomes *tamei*. But *hasoref metamei begadim velo hamatzis es ha'or* — it is only the one who actively burns who becomes *tamei*, not the one who merely lights the fire. Nor does *velo hamasader es hamarachah* — the one who builds up the woodpile and arranges the pyre — become *tamei*. Only the one engaged in the act of burning itself becomes *tamei*.

### Zeh Hamisayeya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל אַף מִשֶּׁנַּעֲשׂוּ אֵפֶר מְטַמְּאִין בְּגָדִים — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹתָם״, אוֹתָם מְטַמְּאִין בְּגָדִים, וְלֹא מִשֶּׁנַּעֲשׂוּ אֵפֶר מְטַמְּאִין בְּגָדִים. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: הַפָּר מְטַמֵּא, נִיתַּךְ הַבָּשָׂר אֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that **garments would be rendered impure even** after the bull and goat have **become ash.** Therefore, **the verse states: Them,** to indicate that **they** themselves, the bull and goat of Yom Kippur, **render garments impure, but they do not render garments impure once they become ash. Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says: The bull causes ritual impurity** before it is burned, but once **the flesh is burned it no longer renders garments impure.**

Who qualifies as the one who burns? *Zeh hamisayeya b'shas sreifah* — anyone who participates during the time of the burning. This formulation is precise: it is not about preparatory acts, but about involvement during the actual burning. One who is moving the carcass on the woodpile is clearly the *soreif*. What of one who adds a log to the fire while the carcass is burning? That too appears to constitute *misayeya b'shas sreifah* — participation during the burning — and would render that person *tamei*. The criterion is temporal and participatory: are you actively involved at the moment the burning is taking place?

### Yachol Af Mishe'nasu Eif…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דְּשַׁוְּיֵהּ חֲרוֹכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between the** opinion of the first *tanna* and the opinion of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon? The Gemara answers: **There is** a difference **between them when he turned it into a charred** mass and the form of the animal has already become distorted but has not actually become ash. According to Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, it no longer causes impurity.

One might have thought that if someone continues to tend the fire even after the carcass has become ashes, they too become *tamei*. The *Gemara* rejects this: *talmud lomar osam* — the verse says *osam*, referring to the animals themselves. The *tumah* applies when you burn *them*, when they still have their identity as a carcass. *Osam metamei begadim velo mishe'nasu eifer metamei begadim* — once they have become ashes they no longer exist as carcasses, and continued burning of the ashes does not render one *tamei*.

Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon takes a stricter position: *nizach habasar* — once the flesh has dissolved, even if it has not yet become ash, the *tumah* no longer applies. The *Gemara* asks: *mai beinaihu?* — what is the practical difference between the *chachamim* and Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon? *Ika beinaihu d'shavei charuchah* — the case of charcoal. A piece of meat that is totally charred and blackened but has not yet crumbled into ash: according to Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon, since the flesh has essentially been destroyed, there is no longer *tumah*; according to the *chachamim*, so long as it has not fully become ash, it retains its identity as burnt flesh and renders one *tamei*.

It bears noting that although Rashi reads this entire passage as proceeding according to Rabbi Shimon's view — that *tumah* begins only at the moment of actual burning — this need not be the case. Even the *tanna kamma*, who holds that *tumah* begins when the carcasses leave the *Beis HaMikdash*, would presumably agree that anyone directly involved in the burning also becomes *tamei*, since the Torah explicitly says *v'hasoref osam yechabeis begadav*. The *tanna kamma* does not deny the *tumah* of burning; he simply holds that *tumah* begins earlier as well. The detailed laws of who qualifies as *hasoref* and at what point the *tumah* lapses are therefore relevant to all opinions.

## Higia Sa'ir L'midbar

### Amru Lo L'Kohen Gadol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל: הִגִּיעַ שָׂעִיר לַמִּדְבָּר. וּמִנַּיִין הָיוּ יוֹדְעִין שֶׁהִגִּיעַ שָׂעִיר לַמִּדְבָּר? דִּירְכָּאוֹת הָיוּ עוֹשִׂין וּמְנִיפִין בְּסוּדָרִין. וְיוֹדְעִין שֶׁהִגִּיעַ שָׂעִיר לַמִּדְבָּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **They said to the High Priest: The goat has reached the wilderness. And how did they know** in the Temple **that the goat reached the wilderness? They would build platforms [*dirkaot*]** all along the way and people would stand on them **and wave scarves [*sudarin*]** to signal when the goat arrived. **And** therefore **they knew that the goat reached the wilderness.**

The *mishna* now brings the thread of the *sa'ir la-Azazel* back into view: *amru lo l'Kohen Gadol higia sa'ir la-midbar* — they informed the *Kohen Gadol* that the goat had reached the wilderness.

### Eino Rashai Lehatchil

Rashi explains the significance of this announcement: *she'eino rashai lehatchil b'avodah acheres ad she'yagia sa'ir la-midbar* — the *Kohen Gadol* is not permitted to begin the next stage of service until the goat has reached the wilderness. He can finish handling the carcasses — cutting them open, removing the *imurim*, and sending them out — but he cannot proceed to something fundamentally new from the *sa'ir* and *par* process until the goat's arrival at the wilderness is confirmed. The source is the verse: *v'shilach es ha-sa'ir ha-midbarah ve-achar kach v'es chelev ha-chatat yaktir ha-mizbeicha* — he shall send the goat to the wilderness, and only afterward burn the fats of the sin offering on the *mizbe'ach*. Even though the immediate next act is not the burning of the fats, there is nonetheless a structural dependence: the goat must reach the wilderness before the *Kohen Gadol* moves forward.

### Uminayin Hayu Yodim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וַהֲלֹא סִימָן גָּדוֹל הָיָה לָהֶם: מִירוּשָׁלַיִם וְעַד בֵּית חֲדוֹדוֹ, שְׁלֹשָׁה מִילִין, הוֹלְכִין מִיל וְחוֹזְרִין מִיל, וְשׁוֹהִין כְּדֵי מִיל וְיוֹדְעִין שֶׁהִגִּיעַ שָׂעִיר לַמִּדְבָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said:** Why did they need these platforms? **Didn't they** already **have a reliable indicator? From Jerusalem to Beit Ḥiddudo,** the edge of the wilderness, where the mitzva of dispatching the goat was performed, was a distance of **three *mil*.** Since the nobles of Jerusalem **walked a *mil*** to escort the dispatcher **and returned a *mil**, and waited** the time **equivalent** to the time it takes to walk **a *mil**, they knew that the goat reached the wilderness.** There was no need for the platforms.

How did anyone know when the goat reached the wilderness? *Dirka'ot hayu osim u-menifin bi-sudarin* — appointed individuals were stationed at intervals and would wave cloths or flags. As the goat reached its destination, the person nearest to it would wave, the next person in the chain would see and wave in turn, and so the signal would travel back to the *Beis HaMikdash* like a relay. This was clearly a daytime visual communication system, analogous to the fire-signals once used to announce the *kiddush hachodesh*, though since this occurs on Yom Kippur fire-signals were naturally not an option.

### Siman Gadol Hayah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: וַהֲלֹא סִימָן אַחֵר הָיָה לָהֶם: לָשׁוֹן שֶׁל זְהוֹרִית הָיָה קָשׁוּר עַל פִּתְחוֹ שֶׁל הֵיכָל, וּכְשֶׁהִגִּיעַ שָׂעִיר לַמִּדְבָּר הָיָה הַלָּשׁוֹן מַלְבִּין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִם יִהְיוּ חֲטָאֵיכֶם כַּשָּׁנִים כַּשֶּׁלֶג יַלְבִּינוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael says: Didn't they have a different indicator? There was a strip of crimson tied to the entrance to the Sanctuary, and when** the **goat reached the wilderness** and the mitzva was fulfilled **the strip would turn white, as it is stated: "Though your sins be as scarlet, they will become white as snow"** (Isaiah 1:18).

Rabbi Yehuda observes: *v'halo siman gadol hayah* — but they had a simpler sign available. *Miyerushalayim v'ad Beis Chidudo shlosha milin* — from Yerushalayim to the place called *Beis Chidudo*, which marks the edge of the wilderness, is three *mil*. Rashi takes *Beis Chidudo* to refer to the cliff, though its more precise meaning appears to be the beginning of the wilderness boundary. The *Yerushalmi*'s *girsa* reads *Beis Choron*, a known location outside Yerushalayim, which if accurate would also indicate the direction in which the goat was taken.

### Shlosha Milin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ בֵּית חֲדוֹדוֹ בַּמִּדְבָּר קָיְימָא, וְהָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּקָסָבַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ שָׂעִיר לַמִּדְבָּר — נַעֲשֵׂית מִצְוָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Abaye said: Learn from this** that **Beit Ḥiddudo is located in the wilderness, and this** comes to **teach us that Rabbi Yehuda holds that once the goat has reached the wilderness, its mitzva is complete** even before it is pushed off the cliff, and there is no need to wait any longer.

Rabbi Yishmael offers a time-based method: the escort party accompanies the goat for one *mil* out of Yerushalayim, then returns — that accounts for two *mil* of travel time. One then waits the equivalent of a third *mil* — the time it takes to travel that distance — and at that point one may conclude that the goat has arrived at the wilderness. No eyewitness relay is needed; a simple calculation of elapsed time suffices. *Ve-yod'im she-higia sa'ir la-midbar* — and in this way they knew the goat had reached the wilderness.

Abaye draws a halakhic inference from this: *shma mina Beis Chidudi bamidbar kayma* — we learn from this that *Beis Chidudi* is considered to be within the wilderness, or at its threshold. Once the goat reaches that boundary, *keivan she-higia la-midbar na'aseit mitzvatah* — the *mitzvah* of *shiluach hase'ir* is considered fulfilled. Although one is ideally supposed to push the goat over the cliff, and although one might be minimally *yotzei* the *avoda* even if the goat died immediately after the *vidui*, the full performance of the *mitzvah* — including the *kappara* — is achieved once the goat reaches the wilderness, before it goes over the cliff. And it is at this moment that the *begadim* of the person who escorted it become *tamei*, and at this moment the *Kohen Gadol* may proceed.

## Ba Lo Kohen Gadol Likros

### Ratza Likros B'vagdei Vu…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בָּא לוֹ כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל לִקְרוֹת, אִם רָצָה לִקְרוֹת בְּבִגְדֵי בוּץ — קוֹרֵא, וְאִם לָאו — קוֹרֵא בְּאִצְטְלִית לָבָן מִשֶּׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The High Priest came to read** the Torah. **If he wished to read** the Torah while still dressed **in** the **fine linen garments,** i.e., the priestly vestments he wore during the previous service, **he** may **read** wearing them; **and if not he** is permitted to **read in a white robe of his own,** which is not a priestly vestment.

With the goat having reached the wilderness, the *Kohen Gadol* now proceeds to the next stage. The *mishna* states: *ba lo Kohen Gadol likros* — the *Kohen Gadol* came to read from the Torah. He has a choice regarding his garments: *im ratza likros bivgadei vutz korei* — if he wishes to read in his linen *bigdei kehunah*, the same garments he wore for the *avoda*, he may do so. *Ve-im lav, korei be-itztalit lavan mishelo* — if he prefers not to use those garments, he may wear his own white tunic instead, changing out of the priestly garments and then, when necessary, changing back. The question of *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim* in connection with this change of garments will be addressed in the following *mishna*.

### Chazzan Haknesset Notel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַזַּן הַכְּנֶסֶת נוֹטֵל סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה, וְנוֹתְנוֹ לְרֹאשׁ הַכְּנֶסֶת, וְרֹאשׁ הַכְּנֶסֶת נוֹתְנוֹ לַסְּגָן, וְהַסְּגָן נוֹתְנוֹ לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, וְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל עוֹמֵד וּמְקַבֵּל, וְקוֹרֵא בְּ״אַחֲרֵי מוֹת״ וְ״אַךְ בֶּעָשׂוֹר״, וְגוֹלֵל סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה וּמַנִּיחוֹ בְּחֵיקוֹ וְאוֹמֵר: יוֹתֵר מִמַּה שֶּׁקָּרָאתִי לִפְנֵיכֶם כָּתוּב כָּאן. ״וּבֶעָשׂוֹר״ שֶׁבְּחוֹמֶשׁ הַפְּקוּדִים קוֹרֵא עַל פֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **The synagogue attendant takes a Torah scroll and gives it to the head of the synagogue** that stood on the Temple Mount; **and the head of the synagogue gives it to the deputy** High Priest, **and the Deputy gives it to the High Priest, and the High Priest stands and receives** the scroll from his hands. **And he reads** from the scroll the Torah portion beginning with the verse: **"After the death"** (Leviticus 16:1) **and** the portion beginning with the verse: **"But on the tenth"** (Leviticus 23:26), **and furls the Torah scroll and places it on his bosom and says: More than what I have read before you is written here.** The Torah portion beginning with the verse: **"And on the tenth," from the book of Numbers** (29:7), **he** then **reads by heart.**

The *Sefer Torah* is passed to the *Kohen Gadol* through an elaborate chain of intermediaries. *Chazzan Haknesset notel Sefer Torah* — the attendant of the synagogue takes the *Sefer Torah*. Rashi explains that there was a *beis haknesset* — a small synagogue — situated on *Har HaBayit*, adjacent to the *azara*. The *Sefer Torah* was kept there. The attendant gives it to the *rosh haknesset* — the head of the congregation, the one responsible for allocating synagogue honors, what Rashi describes as equivalent to the *gabbai*. He in turn gives it to the *segan*, the deputy *Kohen Gadol*, and the *segan* delivers it to the *Kohen Gadol* himself, who stands and receives it. The entire ceremony builds toward this climactic moment of reception.

The existence of a *beis haknesset* on *Har HaBayit* itself is a striking detail, though Rashi is clear that this is where the *chazzan* brings the *Sefer Torah* from — the *Kohen Gadol* himself does not leave the *Beis HaMikdash* for this purpose.

### V'korei Acharei Mot

*Ve-korei Acharei Mot ve-ach be-asor* — he reads the *parasha* of *Acharei Mot*, which describes the Yom Kippur *avoda*, and the *parasha* of *Ach be-asor* from *Emor*, which contains the commandment of fasting and self-affliction and relates to the *musaf* service.

### Yosser Mimah Shekarasi

After reading, the *Kohen Gadol* rolls the *Sefer Torah* closed, holds it against his chest, and announces: *yosser mi-mah she-karasi lifneichem kasuv kan* — more than what I have read to you is written here. This declaration has a specific purpose: he is about to recite a passage from Bamidbar by heart, and he wants the assembled congregation to understand that he is not continuing to read from this scroll.

### Korei Al Peh

*Korei al peh* — he then recites from memory the section *be-asor lachodesh* from the *parasha* of Pinchas in Bamidbar, which contains the *musafim*. It would be an undue burden on the congregation — *tircha de-tzibura* — to roll the *Sefer Torah* all the way from the middle of Vayikra to the end of Bamidbar. Therefore, the *Kohen Gadol* closes the scroll, makes it obvious he is setting it aside, announces that there is more written there, and then reads this additional passage *ba'al peh*, demonstrating clearly that he is not reading from the scroll but from memory. The congregation should not imagine that the two sections are adjacent in the Torah.

### Shemoneh Berachot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְבָרֵךְ עָלֶיהָ שְׁמוֹנֶה בְּרָכוֹת: עַל הַתּוֹרָה, וְעַל הָעֲבוֹדָה, וְעַל הַהוֹדָאָה, וְעַל מְחִילַת הֶעָוֹן, וְעַל הַמִּקְדָּשׁ בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ, וְעַל יִשְׂרָאֵל בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן, וְעַל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ, וְעַל הַכֹּהֲנִים בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן, וְעַל שְׁאָר הַתְּפִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And he recites after** the reading the following **eight blessings:**  **Concerning the Torah:** Who has given us the Torah of truth;  **and concerning the** Temple **service:** Find favor in Your people Israel and accept the service in Your most holy House... for You alone do we serve with reverence;  **and concerning thanksgiving:** We give thanks to You;  **and concerning pardon of iniquity:** Pardon our iniquities on this Yom Kippur;  **and concerning the Temple in and of itself,** which concludes: Blessed…Who chose the Temple;  **and concerning the Jewish People in and of itself,** which concludes: Blessed…Who chose Israel;  **and concerning Jerusalem in and of itself,** which concludes: Blessed…Who chose Jerusalem;  **and concerning the priests in and of themselves,** which concludes: Blessed…Who chose the priests;  **and concerning the rest of the prayer,** which concludes: Blessed…Who listens to prayer.

After the reading, the *Kohen Gadol* recites eight *berachot*. These are: *al ha-Torah* — over the Torah, a blessing for the gift of Torah; *ve-al ha-avoda* — a blessing resembling *retzeh*, thanking God for the *avoda* he has just completed, closing with words like *ve-otcha levadcha b'yira na'avod*; *ve-al ha-hodaya* — thanksgiving, parallel to *modim*; *ve-al mechilat ha-avon* — for the forgiveness of sin, the central theme of Yom Kippur, closing as Rashi describes: *mocheil avonotenu b'yom hakippurim melech mocheil ve-solei'ach la-avonotenu vela-avonot amo beit Yisrael*; *ve-al ha-mikdash bifnei atzmo* — a blessing for the *Mikdash*, that God should continue to dwell there, closing *baruch she-bachar ba-mikdash*; *ve-al Yisrael bifnei atzman* — a blessing for the people of Israel; *ve-al Yerushalayim bifnei atzmo* — for Yerushalayim; and *ve-al ha-kohanim bifnei atzman* — a blessing specifically for the *kohanim*, which Rashi describes as closing *she-bachar be-zaro shel Aharon*. It is notable that the blessing for the *kohanim* follows the blessing for Yerushalayim rather than immediately following the blessing for Israel — a structural choice that perhaps reflects a view of the *kohanim* as associated primarily with the institution of the *Beis HaMikdash* rather than with the general community. Finally, *ve-al she'ar ha-tefilla* — for the remainder of the prayer, *binichuta*, which will be elaborated in subsequent discussion.

### Derech Rechoka V'melacht…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָרוֹאֶה כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל כְּשֶׁהוּא קוֹרֵא — אֵינוֹ רוֹאֶה פַּר וְשָׂעִיר הַנִּשְׂרָפִין, וְהָרוֹאֶה פַּר וְשָׂעִיר הַנִּשְׂרָפִין — אֵינוֹ רוֹאֶה כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל כְּשֶׁהוּא קוֹרֵא. וְלֹא מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי, אֶלָּא שֶׁהָיְתָה דֶּרֶךְ רְחוֹקָה, וּמְלֶאכֶת שְׁנֵיהֶן שָׁוָה כְּאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Mishna comments: **One who sees the High Priest reading** the Torah **does not see the bull and goat that are burned; and one who sees the bull and goat that are burned does not see the High Priest reading** the Torah. The Mishna explains: **And** this is **not due to** the fact **that one is not permitted** to see both, **but because there was a distant path** between them, **and** the **performance of both of them** is undertaken **simultaneously.**

The *mishna* closes with a striking observation: *haro'eh Kohen Gadol kshehu korei eino ro'eh par v'sa'ir hanisrafim* — one who is present to witness the *Kohen Gadol* reading the Torah cannot simultaneously witness the burning of the *par* and *sa'ir*. And conversely, one who witnesses the burning cannot witness the Torah reading. *V'lo mipnei she'eino rashai* — this is not because one is forbidden to witness both; there is no prohibition. Rather, *ela she'hayeta derech rechoka* — they were simply far apart: one was occurring inside Yerushalayim on *Har HaBayit*, the other outside Yerushalayim entirely. *U-melachtan sheteihen ke'echad* — and both were happening simultaneously. It is simply impossible to be in two places at once.

This simultaneity is itself significant. The *Kohen Gadol*, having completed the central *avoda* and confirmed that the *sa'ir* has reached the wilderness, now engages in the public, communal dimension of the day — reading the Torah and pronouncing eight *berachot* — while, at the same moment, the carcasses are being consumed by fire outside the city. The question of the purpose and nature of this *krias haTorah* will be taken up at the start of the next session.
