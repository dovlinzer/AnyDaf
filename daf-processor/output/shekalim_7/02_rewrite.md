# Shekalim 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Leftover Funds Rules

### Shekel Leftovers

The daf we pick up on is 6b, which opens with a *mishnah* that extends the discussion from the previous session concerning what happens when someone sets aside extra money for their *shekel*. The prior discussion focused on whether such a designation constitutes *hekdesh* in error — *hekdesh ta'us* — or a legitimate sanctification. The present *mishnah* broadens the inquiry to the general question of leftover funds sanctified for various *korbanos*: when such funds cannot be applied to their original purpose, what becomes of them?

The *mishnah* opens: *mosar shekalim chullin* — leftover money for a *shekel* is *chullin*. This reflects the position of *Beis Hillel* that since the *shekel* obligation has a fixed amount, any excess was contributed inadvertently — a *ta'us* — and therefore never achieved *kedushas hekdesh* in the first place.

### Asirit HaEifah

The *mishnah* continues: *mosar asirit ha'eifah* — leftover funds designated for the *asirit ha'eifah* offering. This refers specifically to the *minchat choteh* brought within the framework of the *korban oleh v'yored*, the sliding-scale *chatas* applicable to a defined set of transgressions enumerated at the end of *Parshas Vayikra*. For someone too poor to bring an animal, the minimum permissible offering is a tenth of an *eifah* of flour, which functions as a *chatas* substitute. Leftover funds for this, together with leftover funds for *kinei zavim*, *kinei zavos*, and *kinei yolodos* — the bird-pairs brought by a *zav*, *zavah*, and a woman who has given birth — as well as for *chataos* and *ashamos* generally, all carry the same ruling: *mosran nedavah*, the surplus goes to the *nedavah* fund.

It is worth pausing here. In the cases of *kinei zavim* and *kinei yolodos*, not both birds constitute a *chatas*. As the *pasuk* in *Tazria* specifies: one bird is an *olah* and one is a *chatas*. Yet the *mishnah* treats the entire fund as falling under the *chatas* rule. The simpler reading is that since a *chatas* is present within the package, and since the entire offering serves a *taharah* purpose — for *zav*, *zavah*, and *yolodes* alike — the whole fund is treated with the stringency of *chatas* funds. The *mishnah* emphasizes the *chatas* dimension because that is what drives the *halacha*.

The *nedavah* referred to here is not a personal voluntary offering but rather a communal fund — *kaitz hamizbeach* — drawn upon by the *Beis HaMikdash* to bring *olahs* when the *mizbeach* would otherwise stand idle.

### Chatat & Asham Rule

The *mishnah* states the general principle: *zeh haklal — kol sheba leshem chatat umishum ashmah, mosran nedavah* — anything designated as a *chatas* or an *asham*, its surplus goes to *nedavah*. This is understood to be *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*.

### Voluntary Offerings

The *mishnah* then addresses the voluntary offerings. *Mosar olah le'olah* — leftover money set aside for a personal *olah* goes toward another *olah*. The distinction between this and *nedavah* is significant: *nedavah* is a communal contribution to the *Beis HaMikdash*'s standing fund, whereas here the individual retains the surplus and applies it to his next personal *olah*. An *olah* is a free-will sacrifice, not an obligatory or sin-related one, and therefore the funds are not locked into a specific obligation but may be carried forward.

Similarly, *mosar minchah laminchah* — surplus *minchah* funds are saved for the individual's next *minchah*. And *mosar shelamim shelamim* — leftover *shelamim* funds go toward a future personal *shelamim*.

### Pesach to Shelamim

*Mosar hapesach lishlamim* — leftover money for a *korban pesach* goes toward a *shelamim*. One might ask: why not simply hold those funds for the following year's *pesach*? The answer lies in what was learned in *Masechet Pesachim*: after *Pesach*, both the animal itself and any funds designated for it undergo a conversion of status to *shelamim*. The leftover money therefore cannot remain designated as *pesach* funds.

### Nazirite Leftovers

The *mishnah* closes with the *nazir*. *Mosar nezirim l'nezirim* — if funds were collected communally for multiple *nezirim*, any surplus is held for the next *nazir* who requires assistance, since the funds are not specific to any one individual. However, *mosar nazir lindavah* — if an individual *nazir* set aside money for his own *korbanos*, any surplus goes to *nedavah*. The *nazir*'s obligation is highly particular to his specific period of *nezirus*, much like a *chatas*, and therefore the leftover money cannot simply be carried forward.

## Shekel Death Question

### Rav Yehuda's Query

The *gemara* opens with a vivid report. *Amar Rabbi Yosi: ad d'anan taman* — while I was still there, in *Bavel* — *shami'a kal Rav Yehuda sha'il l'Shmuel*: I heard the voice of Rav Yehuda asking Shmuel. The phrasing is striking: not merely "I heard that Rav Yehuda asked," but rather "I heard the voice of Rav Yehuda asking" — an almost graphic recollection that perhaps suggests he was nearby but unseen, or that the exchange made a vivid impression.

The question Rav Yehuda posed to Shmuel: *hefrish shiklo u'mes* — someone sanctified his *shekel* and then died. What becomes of the money? Shmuel's answer: *yiplu l'nedavah* — it falls to the *nedavah* fund.

### Ta'us Doctrine

This ruling requires unpacking. Our *mishnah* opened by stating *mosar shekalim chullin* — surplus *shekel* money is *chullin*. But that ruling applies to the case of *hekdesh ta'us*: the person was dropping coins into a container and inadvertently exceeded the half-*shekel* threshold without realizing it. That excess was never intentionally sanctified; it was a mistake.

Here the case is entirely different. The person properly and deliberately sanctified his *shekel* — the sanctification was legitimate — and then he died. This is not *ta'us*. The funds are genuine *hekdesh*. Since *Beis Shammai* does not accept the *ta'us* doctrine and holds that even accidentally over-sanctified *shekel* funds are genuine *hekdesh* going to *nedavah*, and since our case presents deliberate sanctification followed by death, the ruling is that such funds go to *nedavah*. The *ta'us* element that would render the funds *chullin* is entirely absent.

## Asirit HaEifah Dispute

### Yam HaMelach

The *gemara* now raises a dispute concerning the ruling by the *asirit ha'eifah*. The pronoun *shelo* in the *mishnah*'s phrase *mosar asirit ha'eifah shelo* — the surplus of *his* *asirit ha'eifah* — points toward a specific individual. The *gemara* identifies this as the *Kohen Gadol*, who is obligated to bring an *asirit ha'eifah* every day, half in the morning and half in the afternoon, as a daily *minchah*. This is a *chovah* — an obligatory offering — but it is categorically different from the *minchat choteh* of the *oleh v'yored* discussed in the *mishnah*.

Rabbi Yochanan rules: *yolichem l'Yam HaMelach* — you cast them into the Dead Sea. There is nothing that can be done with them.

### Mishnah Contradiction

The *gemara* immediately presses: *masnisa peligah al Rabbi Yochanan* — our *mishnah* contradicts Rabbi Yochanan directly. The *mishnah* lists *mosar asirit ha'eifah* among those whose surplus goes to *nedavah*. How can Rabbi Yochanan rule that it must be disposed of?

### Minchat Choteh

Rabbi Yochanan resolves this: *patar lah* — he explains the *mishnah* as referring specifically to the *asirit ha'eifah sheminchat choteh shel kol Yisrael* — the *asirit ha'eifah* that functions as the *minchat choteh* for the general population, the offering brought within the sliding-scale *chatas* system. That is what the *mishnah* addresses. But the *Kohen Gadol*'s daily *asirit ha'eifah* is an obligatory *minchah* of an entirely different character — it is not a *chatas* substitute at all.

This distinction, though, invites scrutiny. The general principle observed throughout the *mishnah* is that obligatory funds with no remaining designated use go to *nedavah*. This applies to *shekalim*, to *nazir* korbanos, to the birds of a *zavah* even though one is an *olah*. Why would the *Kohen Gadol*'s *minchah* be different? The rule of *nedavah* does not appear to be limited to *chatas*-category offerings. If surplus obligatory funds generally go to *nedavah*, Rabbi Yochanan's position is difficult to understand.

One possible resolution: the *Kohen Gadol*'s *asirit ha'eifah* occupies an unusual intermediate position. On one hand, the *Kohen Gadol* is an individual bringing what functions as a private obligation; on the other, his daily service is also bound up with the general operations of the *Beis HaMikdash* in a way that sets it apart from a typical private *chovah*. Perhaps Rabbi Yochanan views this unique status as removing the *minchah* from the ordinary *nedavah* framework. Still, this remains difficult, and Rabbi Eliezer's position — *yiplu l'nedavah* — seems to follow naturally from the broader principle.

## Pesach Conversion

### Bavli Derasha

The *gemara* reports another teaching transmitted by Rabbi Yosi: *ad d'anan taman* — while I was still in *Bavel* — he heard a *derasha* that they make there. *Abba bar Ba* raised the following question about a Babylonian exegesis: *minayin shehapesach mishtaneh l'shem shelamim?* — from where do we know that a *pesach* converts to a *shelamim*? The verse cited is *v'im min hatzon korbano l'zevach shelamim* (the opening of the *shelamim* section in *Vayikra*). The Babylonian *derasha* reads: *kol shehu min hatzon ba shelamim* — anything that comes from the flock, if not used for its original purpose, becomes a *shelamim*.

### Olah Challenge

Abba bar Ba then challenges this *derasha*: *harei olah* — but consider an *olah*! An *olah* also comes from the flock. Why, then, would surplus *olah* funds not also become a *shelamim*? And of course we know they do not — surplus *olah* funds go toward another *olah*.

The response: *davar she'eino ba ela min hatzon* — something that comes *only* from flocks. *Yatzat olah shehi ba'ah af min habakar* — an *olah* is excluded because it may be brought from cattle as well, not exclusively from the flock. The phrase *v'im min hatzon* therefore encompasses only offerings that are exclusively flock-animals. A *pesach* qualifies; an *olah* does not.

### Min Hatzon Parse

But this does not end the analysis. *Hayatzivun? Harei asham?* — is this really correct? What of an *asham*? An *asham* comes only from sheep — specifically from male sheep, rams — and not from cattle. If the principle is that *min hatzon* indicates an exclusively flock-based offering, the *asham* should also be included, and surplus *asham* funds should become *shelamim*. We know they do not.

Rav Bun bar Kahana answers: *davar shehu ba mikol hatzon* — the verse points to something that comes from *all types* of flocks. *Tzon* encompasses both sheep and goats. A *shelamim* may come from either sheep or goats; a *pesach* similarly may come from either. But an *asham* comes only from rams — not from goats. Therefore, *yatza asham she'eino ba ela min ha'eilim bilvad* — the *asham* is excluded because it does not come from the full range of *tzon*.

At this point, however, a counter-pressure emerges within the *gemara*. In all other contexts, the prefix *min* is understood as *l'ma'et* — to exclude, to indicate a subset. Yet here we are reading *min hatzon* as inclusive of both sheep and goats. How can *min* be expansive?

Rav Mana answers: *hachi nami min l'ma'et* — yes, *min* here is also excluding, but what it excludes is not goats but rather certain characteristics of sheep themselves. Specifically, it excludes females — a *pesach* may only be male — and it excludes animals older than one year. The *min* narrows *within* the category of sheep-and-goat flocks by excluding females and two-year-old animals, thereby pointing us specifically toward the *pesach*. Meanwhile, *tzon* itself still encompasses both sheep and goats, which excludes the *asham*.

The *gemara* then presses: *v'gabei asham nami min l'ma'et hu she'eino ba ela min ha'eilim* — could we not equally read *min* with regard to the *asham* as excluding goats? The *gemara* resists this reading; the *pshat* of *tzon* as covering the full range of flocks is what excludes the *asham*, and the *min* is reserved to narrow within that range toward the specific parameters of a *pesach*.

### Logical Reasons

The *gemara* then returns to why surplus *pesach* funds should become *shelamim* rather than *olah*, even though a textual argument could in principle be constructed from the *olah* verse as well. Rabbi Avun offers: *meshanim davar shehu l'achila l'davar shehu l'achila, v'ein meshanim davar shehu l'achila l'davar she'eino l'achila* — we convert something that is eaten into something that is also eaten; we do not convert something that is eaten into something that cannot be eaten. A *pesach* is eaten; a *shelamim* is eaten; an *olah* is entirely consumed on the *mizbeach*. We therefore read the *pesach* into the *shelamim* passage, not the *olah* passage.

Rabbi Yose bar Abun adds a parallel consideration: *meshanim kodashim kalim l'shem kodashim kalim, v'ein meshanim kodashim kalim l'shem kodashei kodashim* — we convert *kodashim kalim* into *kodashim kalim*, not *kodashim kalim* into *kodashei kodashim*. A *pesach* is *kodashim kalim*; a *shelamim* is likewise *kodashim kalim*; but an *olah* is *kodashei kodashim*. We therefore prefer the *shelamim* reading.

What is striking here is the interplay between *derasha* and *sevara*. One might assume that a *drasha* from a *pasuk* is determinative on its own, operating mechanically. But here the *gemara* reveals that there was a genuine choice between two possible *pesukim* — either the *olah* passage or the *shelamim* passage could have served as the basis for the conversion of *pesach* funds. The *sevaros* of Rabbi Avun and Rabbi Yose bar Abun are what tilt the reading toward *shelamim*. Similarly, the earlier argument about whether the *asham* or the *pesach* is the subject of the *drasha* reflects a choice in how to parse the verse, guided by substantive legal logic. This illustrates how *derasha* and *sevara* function together rather than in isolation.

## Shechita Intent

### Shelamim Intent

The *gemara* now addresses the conditions required for an actual *pesach* animal — not merely surplus funds — to convert to a *shelamim* after the holiday has passed. Rabbi Yosi reports that in *Bavel* they held: *ein hapesach mishtaneh l'shem shelamim ela im ken shechato l'shem shelamim* — the *pesach* does not convert to a *shelamim* unless it was specifically slaughtered with the intent of a *shelamim*. This is more restrictive than what was taught in *Pesachim*, where any *she'lo lishmo* intent — slaughtering it for something other than *pesach* — sufficed for the conversion.

### Any Non-Pesach

Rabbi Chanina disagrees: *afilu shechato l'shem olah* — even if you slaughtered it with the intent of an *olah*, any intent other than *pesach* suffices to convert it to a *shelamim*.

### Any Korban Intent

Rav Illa explains the basis for Rabbi Yochanan's position that even slaughter for an *olah* converts the animal to a *shelamim*: the *drasha* of *v'im min hatzon korbano l'zevach shelamim* — once it is slaughtered *l'zevach*, for any other *korban*, it becomes a *shelamim*. The word *zevach* is read broadly: any act of slaughter directed toward *some* sacrificial purpose — not necessarily *shelamim* specifically — triggers the conversion.

The *gemara* then raises the question: *u'mishtaneh l'machshevet pasul* — does the animal also change with respect to *machsheves pasul*, invalid intent? The specific case is where someone slaughtered the *pesach* with the intent of an *olah* and simultaneously entertained the thought of throwing the blood the following day — a *pigul* thought. Does the *olah* intent interact with the *pigul* thought in any legally significant way?

The *gemara* notes, *miko makom pasul hu* — regardless, it would be invalid. But the *gemara* refines this: if you say *mishtaneh l'machshevet pasul* — if the *olah* intent gives the animal a kind of dual identity such that the slaughter is not purely *shelamim* but is somehow colored by the *olah* designation — then adding a *pigul* thought produces *pasul* but not *pigul*. The compounding of the *olah* designation alongside the improper intent keeps it from achieving full *pigul* status. However, if you say *ein mishtaneh l'machshevet pasul* — the *olah* intent carries no independent weight and the slaughter is treated as straightforward *shelamim* slaughter — then the *pigul* thought takes full effect and produces *pigul*.

The *nafka mina* is significant. *Pigul* entails a separate Torah violation committed at the moment of the improper slaughter, and one who eats *pigul* is *chayav kareis*. *Pasul* merely invalidates the offering without the additional consequences.

## Pesach Post-Holiday

### She'lo Lishmo Rule

The *gemara* now addresses the case of *lishmo u'she'lo lishmo* — where the slaughterer declares a dual intent simultaneously, both for *pesach* and for *shelamim*, during the rest of the year (*bish'ar yemos hashana*). For the conversion of *pesach* to *shelamim* to take effect, the animal must be slaughtered *she'lo lishmo* — not for *pesach*. What happens when the intent is split?

### Neutralization

Rabbi Avin bar Bun bar Chiya in the name of Shmuel bar Abba reasons: *mikeivan she'ein lo shem* — since the *pesach* during the rest of the year has no designated status, the dual declaration of *lishmo u'she'lo lishmo* effectively cancels itself out, as if the slaughter were performed *bishitika* — in silence, without any verbal designation. And if performed in silence, the animal is *kasher*, since once the *Pesach* holiday has passed, the animal's original *pesach* identity is understood to have lapsed without requiring active uprooting.

### Dual Intent

This raises the question: if *lishmo u'she'lo lishmo* neutralize one another and produce the same result as silence, what would be the *halacha* if someone slaughtered it *lishmo* but then intended to sprinkle its blood *she'lo lishmo*? Could one argue that the separated *lishmo* and *she'lo lishmo* — one at *shechita* and one at *zrika* — also neutralize? The *gemara* resists this extension. One might argue that when the acts are separated temporally, the first act (*shechita lishmo*) already defines the animal and takes precedence, precluding any subsequent neutralization.

### Challenge to Neutralizat…

A challenge follows: if we say that *lishmo u'she'lo lishmo* in combination neutralize and produce the result of silence, then the same logic could presumably extend to cases where the two intents were somehow articulated sequentially rather than simultaneously. Why does neutralization apply only to the combined declaration?

### Silence Question

Rabbi Abba Mari raises a more fundamental challenge: *ma nomar bishitika kasher o nomar bishitika pasul?* — where do we derive the premise that silence produces a *kasher* result in the first place? Can we not equally say that slaughtering the *pesach* in silence during the rest of the year is *pasul* — that it retains its *pesach* identity and requires active redefinition?

This maps precisely onto the question in the *Bavli* of whether *pesach bish'ar yemos hashana ba'ei akira o lo ba'ei akira* — whether the *pesach* requires active uprooting of its old name during the rest of the year. The *Yerushalmi* frames this as: does silence produce *kasher* or *pasul*? The *gemara* leaves this unresolved.

## Nazirite Leftovers

### Order Rule

The *mishnah* ruled that surplus funds of an individual *nazir* go to *nedavah*. The *gemara* now probes a foundational ambiguity: a *nazir* brings three *korbanos* — an *olah*, a *shelamim*, and a *chatas*. If a *nazir* set aside a combined sum for all three and there was money left over, how do we characterize the surplus? Is it surplus *chatas* money? Surplus *olah* money? Surplus *shelamim* money? The answer matters because the rules differ.

Rav Chisda rules: *v'hu shekarva chatato besof* — it depends on which *korban* was offered last. Since the funds are understood to flow toward the last offering brought, the surplus takes on the identity of that *korban*. If the *chatas* was the last offered, the surplus is *chatas* surplus, going to *nedavah*. If the *shelamim* was brought last, *mosar shelamim shelamim* — the surplus goes toward a future *shelamim*.

### Chatat Identity

Rav Zeira disagrees: *afilu karvu shelamim l'besof, halacha achas hi benazir* — even if the *shelamim* was brought last, there is one unified *halacha* for the *nazir*: since a *chatas* is part of the *nazir*'s package of obligations, any surplus is treated as *chatas* surplus and goes to *nedavah*. The same logic underlies the *mishnah*'s ruling about *kinei zavim* and *kinei zavos*: the presence of a *chatas* within the bundle stamps the identity of the entire fund.

### Maos Setumim

A *baraisa* is cited in support of Rav Zeira: *elu hein maos setumim — kol shedmei chatas mesos meuravas bahen* — these are the undesignated funds: any fund into which *chatas* money was ever mixed. The *chiddush* of the *baraisa* follows: *va'afilu hifrish dmei chatas mitokhan maos setumim hein* — even if someone later separated out the *chatas* money from the pool and set it aside distinctly, the remaining funds are still treated as *maos setumim*, undesignated funds with *chatas* identity. Since *chatas* money was once part of this pool, that status persists regardless of subsequent separation. Applied to the *nazir*: since *chatas* funds were always part of the *nazir*'s designated pool, any surplus is treated as *chatas* surplus, regardless of which *korban* was offered last.

### Meila Principle

A *baraisa* is then cited in support of Rav Chisda. If someone designated funds specifically for a *chatas* and separately designated the remainder for his other *nazir* korbanos, and then died: *moalin b'kulan v'ein moalin b'miktzasan* — one who uses *all* of these funds has committed *me'ilah*, but one who uses only *some* of the funds has not. The reason: the remaining fund is a mixture of *olah* and *shelamim* designations. *Me'ilah* applies to *kodshei kodashim* such as an *olah*, but not to *kodashim kalim* such as a *shelamim*. If you use only part of the fund, perhaps those coins were going toward the *shelamim* portion, and *me'ilah* does not apply. Only if you take the entire amount can you be certain you have used funds designated for an *olah* and thereby committed *me'ilah*. Significantly, the *baraisa* does not say that if the man died the funds go to *nedavah* because the *chatas* money was once mixed in — which supports Rav Chisda's position that the *chatas* does not automatically dominate the identity of the entire fund when clearly separated.

## Bread & Wine

### Bread Rots

The *gemara* now turns to the non-animal components of the *nazir*'s obligation. Rav Chisda rules: *mosar lachmo shel Nazir yirkav* — surplus funds designated for a *nazir*'s bread offering must be left to rot. This parallels the earlier ruling about the *Kohen Gadol*'s *asirit ha'eifah* according to Rabbi Yochanan — certain surplus funds that cannot be used for their original purpose and do not fit the *nedavah* framework must simply be disposed of.

### Unique Offering

The *gemara* explains why the bread cannot be redirected. One might suggest using it for a similar offering — the way surplus *olah* funds go to another *olah* — or saving it for another period of *nezirus*. Neither works. As for the first option: *she'ein l'cha lechem kareiv l'atzmo* — there is no such thing as a bread offering brought independently. The *nazir*'s bread has no parallel that could receive the funds. And as for applying it to a future *nezirus*: *she'ein l'cha nezirus belo lechem* — every period of *nezirus* requires its own independent sanctification for the bread. Funds designated for one *nezirus* cannot serve another. Therefore: *mosar lachmo shel Nazir yirkav* — the surplus must rot.

### Wine Rots Too

Rav Yosef initially reasoned by analogy: just as the bread must rot, so too the surplus wine funds — *mosar niscav* — should also be left to rot, for the same reasons: the wine cannot be brought independently and cannot be transferred to another *nezirus*.

### Wine to Nedavah

Rav Yossi bar Rabbi Bun disagrees: *mosar niscav kodshei kodashim inun v'yiplu l'nedavah* — the surplus wine funds have the status of *kodshei kodashim* and therefore fall to *nedavah*. The *nesachim* — wine libations — are entirely consumed on the *mizbeach* without being eaten, which gives them *kodshei kodashim* status. Unlike the bread, which is *kodashim kalim*, the wine shares the *kodshei kodashim* character that makes *nedavah* the appropriate disposition for surplus funds.

## Kodashei Kodashim

### Three Positions

The *gemara* draws together three *amora'im* who have each articulated, in their own domain, the principle that surplus *kodshei kodashim* funds go to *nedavah*:

First, Shmuel, as reported by Rabbi Yosi who heard it in *Bavel* — *hefrish shkalov u'meis yiplu l'nedavah*: surplus *shekel* funds of a man who died go to *nedavah*. The *shekel* is not a *chatas*, yet its surplus goes to *nedavah*.

Second, Rabbi Eliezer — *mosar asirit ha'eifah shelo yiplu l'nedavah*: surplus funds of the *Kohen Gadol*'s *asirit ha'eifah* go to *nedavah*. This is the *asirit ha'eifah* of the *Kohen Gadol* — a *minchah* obligation, not a *chatas* — yet Rabbi Eliezer holds it goes to *nedavah*. (Rabbi Yochanan, recall, disagreed and ruled *Yam HaMelach*.)

Third, Rabbi Yossi bar Rabbi Bun — through his interpretation of Rav Chisda's position — holds that surplus *nazir* wine funds, as *kodshei kodashim*, go to *nedavah*.

The *gemara* aligns these three: *shloshtan amru davar echad* — all three expressed one principle. What ties them together is precisely the *kodshei kodashim* character of their respective surpluses. The rule of *nedavah* for leftover sanctified funds is thus not confined to the *chatas* category alone. Rather, the broader principle emerges: when obligatory funds of *kodshei kodashim* status have no remaining designated application, they go to *nedavah*. The distinction between what *yirkav* (rots) and what becomes *nedavah* tracks the distinction between *kodashim kalim* — such as the *nazir*'s bread — which cannot easily be redirected, and *kodshei kodashim*, which finds its natural home in the *nedavah* framework.

## Charitable Funds

### Ransom Rules

The *mishnah* now shifts from *hekdesh* to *tzedakah* funds designated for specific *mitzvah* purposes — the analogy to sanctified funds being structurally significant. *Mosar shvuyim l'shvuyim* — surplus funds collected generally for *pidyon shvuyim* are retained for future captives. *Mosar shavuy l'oto shavuy* — if funds were collected for a particular captive and there was a surplus after his ransom, that surplus belongs to him.

### Poor Funds

Similarly, *mosar aniyim l'aniyim* — general surplus funds for the poor are saved for future cases. *Mosar ani l'oto ani* — surplus funds raised for a specific poor person go to that individual. The parallel to *hekdesh* is evident: funds designated for a specific purpose or a specific person follow that designation.

### Burial Funds

*Mosar hametim lametim* — general surplus burial funds are saved for future burial needs. *Mosar hamet* — surplus raised for a specific deceased person is disputed. The *tanna kamma* holds it goes *l'yorshav* — to his heirs, since the funds were designated for him and what he would have owned passes to his heirs. Rabbi Meir holds the surplus must remain in escrow — *yehei munach ad she-yavo Eliyahu* — since it is unclear whether it should be used for additional honor to the deceased or passed to his heirs. Rabbi Natan holds the surplus is used for additional honor of the deceased — building a tombstone and providing for the dignity of his burial.

The underlying question is unique to the *met*: unlike the *ani* or the *shavuy*, the deceased cannot express a preference. The question is whether we assume the deceased would have wanted the funds to benefit his heirs, or to be applied to additional honor for his grave.

## Burial Excess

### Mistaken Collection

The *gemara* presents a specific case: *gavu lo be-chezkas she-ein lo v'nimtza she-yesh lo* — funds were collected for burial on the assumption that the deceased was penniless, and it then emerged that he had his own resources and did not need the collected funds. What becomes of the money?

### Heirs vs Mistake

Rav Yirmiyah initially inclined to say: *motar hamet l'yorshav* — apply the standard rule and give the surplus to the heirs, since the money was designated for the deceased regardless of the circumstances. Rav Idi of *Chutra* challenged him: *de-lo kiven ela leh* — the collectors only gave because they believed the deceased needed it. This is a fundamentally different case from the standard *mosar hamet*. There, the funds were genuinely needed and something was left over; here, the entire collection was predicated on a mistaken assumption. The whole enterprise was a *ta'us*, and the funds should be returned to the donors. Rav Yirmiyah pushed back: *ana lo amari — at mena lach?* — "I didn't assert that position — where do you get yours?" The *gemara* here turns on a question of *sevara*: who bears the burden of proof in characterizing the collection as either a legitimate designation-with-surplus or an error ab initio?

### Tombstone Use

A *baraisa* in the name of Rabbi Natan expands on his ruling from the *mishnah*: *motar hamet yibaneh lo nefesh al kivro* — the surplus is used to build a tombstone at the grave site. Additionally, *v'ya'aseh lo ziluf al gabei mitato* — some of the funds may be used to sprinkle wine over the coffin during transport, which served as a practical measure to address the odor of decomposition in an era without refrigeration. The *baraisa* also rules: *ein podin shavuy be-shavuy* — you do not redeem one captive with funds designated for another captive, and *ein govin tallit be-tallit* — one does not collect a cloak for a cloak, meaning funds collected for one specific item may not simply be redirected to a comparable item. The exception: *v'ein memachin be-yad parnasin le-kach* — one does not object to the charity officers making such redirections, because when funds are given to communal charity administrators, there is an implicit understanding that they have discretion over allocation.

### Righteous Memorial

A *baraisa* adds the famous teaching of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: *ein osin nefashot la-tzaddikim* — one does not build tombstones for the righteous. *Divreihem hen hen zichronam* — their teachings, their words, their legacy: that is their memorial. The righteous do not require physical monuments; what they leave behind in Torah and deed constitutes their lasting remembrance. This teaching also implicitly cautions against the veneration of graves as physical sites, redirecting the instinct toward honoring the substance of what the *tzaddik* transmitted.

## Yochanan & Eliezer

### Hiding Encounter

The *gemara* closes with a narrative. Rabbi Yochanan *hava masmach ve-azal* — was being supported as he walked, leaning on Rabbi Chiya bar Abba, apparently due to his considerable weight. Rabbi Eliezer — a Babylonian who had come to *Eretz Yisrael* — saw Rabbi Yochanan approaching. *U-mitmar leh mi-kamei* — he went and hid.

### Yochanan's Complaint

Rabbi Yochanan, observing this through Rabbi Chiya bar Abba, remarked: *hani tarti milayu hadein Bavla avad bi* — this Babylonian has done two things to me. *Chada d'lo sha'al bishlomei* — first, he did not ask after my wellbeing, he offered no greeting. *V'chada d'mitmar* — and second, he fled into hiding, as if he could not bear to be in my presence.

### Babylonian Custom

Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi, who happened to be present, explained: *kach minhagin gav'hon* — that is simply their practice over there in *Bavel*. *D'zeira lo sha'al bishlomeih d'rabba* — a person of lesser stature does not ask after the wellbeing of someone of greater stature. In Babylonian practice, greeting a *rav* as one greets a friend — *shalom alecha* — would itself be inappropriate, a kind of presumptuous familiarity. The absence of a greeting, far from being discourteous, is the proper expression of deference.

### Scripture Basis

Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi supports this practice from the verse: *re'uni ne'arim venichba'u, veyshishim kamu amadu* — "Young ones saw me and hid; elders rose and stood." The verse distinguishes two forms of honor: the elders rise and stand, an active demonstration of respect; the young ones — those of lesser standing — see the great figure and *hide*. Their concealment is itself the expression of awe. They cannot simply walk by; they cannot presume to offer a greeting; and so they withdraw entirely, an act that paradoxically manifests reverence through absence.

### Idol Analogy

To illuminate Rabbi Eliezer's behavior, Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi posed a pointed question to Rabbi Yochanan: *mahu me'avar kamei d'hadein tzalmaya?* — is it permitted to walk in front of this idol? Rabbi Yochanan replied: *ma palig lei y'kar?* — what honor are you showing it by avoiding it? *Avor kumei v'samei eynech* — walk right past it and close your eyes. By refusing to walk in front of an idol, one inadvertently confers upon it a kind of gravity and significance. The correct response is to walk past it with eyes averted — demonstrating indifference rather than awe. Treating the idol like something so weighty that you cannot pass before it is itself a form of reverence.

### Respect Shown

The analogy is then applied directly: *ah, ya'os Rabbi Eliezer avad* — you see, Rabbi Eliezer did right. Since Babylonian practice holds that a lesser person does not greet a greater one — because greeting is what you do with a peer, not with one before whom you stand in awe — to walk before Rabbi Yochanan and remain silent would have been the most conspicuous disrespect. Rabbi Eliezer was simply not prepared to do that. And so he hid. *D'lo avar kamach* — he did not walk past you. His concealment was not avoidance; it was reverence expressed in the only form Babylonian protocol permitted him.

The *gemara* will continue this encounter in the following *daf*, where Rabbi Yochanan's further concerns will be addressed.
