# Rosh Hashanah 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nissan vs Tishrei

### Intro & Shlomo Review

Today's *daf* is 3, and we pick up at the bottom of 2b, in the middle of the discussion of *Nissan* being *Rosh Hashanah* for kings. To clarify a point from the previous session: the evidence used was in the context of Shlomo's Temple construction, because the counting of years for the building of the *Beis Hamikdash* was reckoned as the 480th year from *Yetzias Mitzrayim* and the fourth year of Shlomo's reign — and from this we learn that the counting from *Yetzias Mitzrayim* begins from *Nissan*. Similarly, the count for *Jewish* kings begins from *Nissan*. As we will see today, the count for non-Jewish kings runs from *Tishrei*.

This actually makes a great deal of sense. The command *hachodesh hazeh lachem rosh chodashim* — "this month shall be for you the first of the months" — was the *chiddush* of the *Torah*. Naturally, in the agricultural cycle upon which the ancient calendar was based, *Tishrei* was the beginning: it marks the onset of the planting season and thus, organically, the start of the year. The *Torah* came along and declared that the Jewish people would instead count from the month of *geulah*, from the month of redemption. *Nissan* became *rosh chodashim*. And now the *Gemara* is establishing that not only the months but also the years — at least for Jewish kings — are counted from *Nissan*.

### Torah's Nissan Mandate

The *Gemara* takes it for granted that *Nissan* is month number one in terms of the *Torah*'s numbering of the months. The *Torah* itself never uses the familiar names — *Nissan*, *Tishrei*, *Cheshvan*, *Kislev*, *Iyar* — those names are in fact non-Jewish, Persian in origin, and only begin appearing in *Tanach* after the first *churban bayis*, which is why we find them in *Ezra*, *Nechemia*, and *Megillas Esther*. The *Torah* instead refers to the months as *hachodesh harishon*, *hachodesh hasheini*, and so on. Yet it is clear from the *Torah* itself that this numbering begins from *Nissan* — as for example when it instructs the *korban pesach* in the second year, it specifies *bachodesh harishon*, and it is obvious that *Nissan* is the first month.

The *Gemara*'s question, which was introduced at the end of the previous *daf*, is distinct from the question of month-numbering. Granted that the months are numbered from *Nissan* — are the *years* also counted from *Nissan*? That is, when the *Torah* counts the years from *Yetzias Mitzrayim*, does the year increment when *Nissan* arrives? In principle one might expect the year-count to follow a different rhythm from the month-count — and indeed, we ourselves today do precisely that: we increment the year at *Tishrei* for *Rosh Hashanah* purposes, while still numbering the months from *Nissan*. So the question is a genuine one.

### Aharon & Moshe Evidence

The *Gemara* attempts to prove that years are counted from *Nissan* by means of the following argument. The *Torah* records that Aharon died at *Hor Hahar* in the fifth month — counting from *Nissan*, that is *Av* — *bachodesh hachamishi*. Then, the book of *Devarim* opens with Moshe delivering his great address, which begins in the eleventh month — that is, *Shevat*. Both events are stated to belong to year forty. We know the death of Aharon came before Moshe's speech — and we will demonstrate this explicitly in the next segment — so the two events, in month five and month eleven of the same year, bracket the period during which no year-increment occurred.

Now *Tishrei* is the seventh month. It falls precisely between month five and month eleven. If *Tishrei* were the *Rosh Hashanah* for counting years — meaning the year would increment when *Tishrei* arrived — then Moshe's speech in month eleven would have to be year forty-one, not year forty. Since the *Torah* places both events in year forty, nothing between month five and month eleven can be *Rosh Hashanah* for the year-count. Months six through eleven are all eliminated. The logical conclusion is that year forty-one begins only after month eleven — pointing to *Nissan*, month one, as the point of year-increment.

### Shevat Alternative

The *Gemara* immediately raises a seemingly absurd counter-suggestion: who says the death of Aharon in month five came *before* Moshe's speech in month eleven? Perhaps it was the other way around — Moshe spoke in *Shevat* first, and Aharon died six months later. In that case, one could place the year-increment anywhere from month six through month eleven, since the gap between the two events would have run in the opposite direction. Needless to say, such a reading of *Sefer Devarim* is virtually inconceivable — the entire structure of the book presupposes that Moshe is giving his farewell address on the eve of entering the land, and it contains retrospective references to events including Aharon's death. Nevertheless, the *Gemara* entertains the logical possibility and proceeds to refute it by means of an independent proof establishing the correct chronological sequence — which it will address in the following section.

## Aharon Death Sequence

### Canaanite King = Sichon

The *Gemara* establishes that Aharon's death preceded the battle with Sichon, and that Moshe was still alive when Sichon was defeated. The opening of *Sefer Devarim* explicitly states that Moshe delivered his address *acharei hakoso es Sichon Melech ha'Emori* — "after he struck Sichon king of the Amorites." So Sichon's defeat was a precondition for Moshe's speech.

Now the question becomes: was Sichon still alive when Aharon died? The *Gemara* answers by pointing to a verse in *Bamidbar* that reads: *Vayishma haKena'ani Melech Arad yoshev haNegev* — "and the Canaanite king of Arad who dwelt in the south heard." This verse appears immediately following the account of Aharon's death. The natural reading of the juxtaposition is that this king "heard" about Aharon's death and attacked on account of it — meaning he was alive and active at that point. And if this king can be identified with Sichon, then we know Sichon was still alive after Aharon died, which in turn means Moshe's speech — delivered after Sichon's defeat — necessarily came *after* Aharon's death. The normal sequence of the *Torah* is thus confirmed.

### Rashi: Clouds Departed

Rashi explains that the verse *mah shmua shama* — "what report did he hear?" — is answered as follows: he heard that Aharon had died, *v'nistalku ananei hakavod*, and that the clouds of divine glory had consequently departed. With the protective *ananei hakavod* gone, the nation was exposed and vulnerable, and the king therefore concluded that *nitna reshu lalechem b'Yisrael* — there was now an opening to wage war against Israel. This reading is not merely an inference from juxtaposition; it reads the very motivation of the attack as caused by Aharon's death, making clear that Aharon was already dead at the time.

This interpretation is then supported from the verse itself: *Vayir'u kol ha'eda ki gava Aharon* — "and the whole congregation saw that Aharon had died," which appears immediately before the king's attack. The connection between seeing and the departure of the clouds is unpacked in the following passage.

### Ki's Four Functions

Rabbi Abbahu teaches: *al tikri vayir'u ela vayeira'u* — do not read "they saw" (*vayir'u*) but rather "they were made visible" or "exposed" (*vayeira'u*). Rashi explains with the single word *nitgalu ha'eda* — the congregation became exposed. The shift in reading hinges on a *derashah* that the departure of Aharon, whose merit sustained the *ananei hakavod*, left the people unprotected and literally uncovered before their enemies.

This *derashah* is introduced by reference to a principle stated by Resh Lakish: the word *ki* functions in four different ways — *ei* (if), *dilma* (lest), *ela* (rather/but), and *de'ha* (because). The point here is that the *ki* in *vayir'u kol ha'eda ki gava Aharon* is being read not as a simple "that" — not as "they saw *that* Aharon died" — but in its causative sense: they were exposed *because* Aharon died.

It is worth pausing on these four functions. *Ei* means "if," as in *ki tifga shor oyevcha* — "if you encounter your enemy's ox." *Dilma* means "lest" — and this differs from "if" in a crucial way. Rashi's example is *ki tomar bilvavcha rabbim ha-goyim ha-eleh mimenni* — "lest you say in your heart, these nations are more numerous than I." The word "lest" is not conditional: "if" implies that there would be a contrary consequence in the opposite case, whereas "lest" implies no such conditionality — you simply should not entertain this thought, full stop. *Ela* means "rather" or "but," and Rashi illustrates this with a single *pasuk* containing two different uses: *Vatomer lo tzachakti ki yare'ah — Vayomer lo ki tzachakt* — "she said I did not laugh because she was afraid — he said, no, *rather* you did laugh." *De'ha* means "because" or "for the reason that."

There is actually a fifth common function of *ki* — namely "when," which operates similarly to *ka'asher*, as in *ki tissa et rosh benei Yisrael*. Rashi tends to subsume this under *ei*, since *im* in Hebrew can also function as "when" — as in *im kesef talveh et ammi* — and the distinction between "if" and "when" can be treated as a nuance within the same usage.

Interestingly, the straightforward *peshat* of *ki* in the *pasuk* under discussion — the simple meaning of "that," as in "they saw *that* Aharon had died" — does not fit neatly into any of the canonical four functions of *ki* enumerated by Resh Lakish. Tosafot notes that this sense of "that" can be understood under the function of "when," which Rashi places under *ei* since *im* sometimes means "when." But the observation stands that the plain reading is not one of the four listed functions, which makes the enumeration somewhat incomplete as a comprehensive account of the word's range.

### Sichon ID Baraita

Having established the reading of the Canaanite king's attack from a *derashah* perspective, the *Gemara* now addresses the identification itself. The *Gemara* objects: *mi dami* — how can we compare? The verse speaks of *Kena'an*, whereas we are trying to prove something about Sichon. A *baraita* resolves this by establishing that this figure bore multiple names: *Sichon* because he resembled a wild colt (*ayyar*) in the desert; *Kena'an* was the name of his kingdom (*al shem malchuto*); and his personal name was *Arad*. A second version of the *baraita* gives *Arad* the etymology of resembling a wild ass (*arohd*) in the desert, while maintaining that *Kena'an* was his kingship and his personal name was Sichon. Tosafot cites an interesting *midrash* suggesting that Sichon actually *disguised* himself as a Canaanite king, perhaps so that the Israelites would pray for salvation from the Canaanites — which would be ineffective, since he was actually an Amorite.

With considerable effort, then, what might seem like an obvious reading of the *Torah* has been established through textual proofs and a *baraita*: Aharon died before Sichon's defeat, and Moshe's speech in *Shevat* came after Aharon's death in *Av*, both within year forty. The year-increment between months five and eleven is thus ruled out, which eliminates *Tishrei* as *Rosh Hashanah* for this counting.

## Month Elimination

### Mishkan/Cloud Proof

Having eliminated *Tishrei* and all months between *Av* and *Shevat*, the *Gemara* still needs to eliminate the months that remain — those earlier in the year. Perhaps *Rosh Hashanah* falls in *Iyar* (month two), *Sivan* (month three), *Tammuz* (month four), or *Av* (month five) itself? And what about *Adar* (month twelve)?

The *Gemara* first dismisses *Iyar*: *lo salka da'atach* — this is inconceivable. The verse states that the *Mishkan* was erected on the first of the first month — *Nissan* — in the second year: *vayehi bachodesh harishon bashanah hashenis b'echad lachodesh hukam hamishkan*. And the very next passage states that in the second year in the second month — *Iyar* — the cloud departed from above the *Mishkan*: *vayehi bashanah hashenis bachodesh hasheni na'alah he'anan me'al mishkan ha'edus*. Both events are designated as occurring in the second year. If *Iyar* were *Rosh Hashanah*, the departure of the cloud would have been in year three. Since it is called the second year — *midekai b'Nissan v'karei shanah shenis, v'kai b'Iyar v'karei shanah shenia* — clearly the year did not increment at *Iyar*. *Iyar* is therefore not *Rosh Hashanah*.

### Matan Torah Proof

*Sivan* is likewise eliminated. The verse introducing *Matan Torah* reads: *bachodesh hashlishi l'tzeis Bnei Yisrael me'Eretz Mitzrayim* — "in the third month of the going out of the Children of Israel from Egypt." It does not say "in the third month, in the *second* year of the going out." If *Sivan* were *Rosh Hashanah*, the year would have incremented by the time they stood at *Har Sinai*, and the verse would have needed to say "in the second year." Since it says no such thing, *Sivan* is eliminated as well.

### Shlomo Temple Proof

After *Sivan* is ruled out, the *Gemara* still faces three remaining candidates: *Tammuz*, *Av*, and *Adar*. Rather than eliminating each individually, Rabbi Eliezer resolves all of them at once by returning to the verse about Shlomo. The verse states: *Vayachel livnot bachodesh hasheni b'sheni b'shnas arba l'malchuto* — "and he began to build in the second month, on the second, in the fourth year of his reign." The word *sheini* — second — appears twice in close succession. The first *sheini* clearly refers to the second month, counted in the standard manner from *Nissan*: that is, *Iyar*. The second *sheini* must also refer to the second month — but now, the second month of that year of Shlomo's kingship. This means the year of Shlomo's kingship is itself counted from *Nissan*. The verse thus provides direct textual proof that years — and not just months — are numbered from *Nissan*. This eliminates *Tammuz*, *Av*, and *Adar* simultaneously.

### Ravina Day Challenge

Ravina challenges this reading: perhaps *b'sheni* means the second *day* of the month, not the second month? The *Gemara* rejects this on two grounds. First, whenever the *Torah* wants to specify a day of the month, it always uses the construction *bachodesh* — "of the month" — such as *b'asor bachodesh* or *b'chamisha lachodesh*. A bare *basheini* without *bachodesh* would be anomalous as a day-reference. Second, one might suggest that *basheini* means Monday — the second day of the week — but this too is rejected: we never find the days of the week mentioned in *Tanach* except in the context of Creation, and apart from references to *Shabbos* and *erev Shabbos*, the day of the week is never specified. Finally, a *gezeirah shavah* is applied: the second *sheini* in the verse is linked to the first *sheini*, and just as the first *sheini* unambiguously refers to a month, so too the second *sheini* refers to a month — specifically, the second month from the point at which the king's year is incremented. The year of Shlomo's kingship is thus conclusively shown to be counted from *Nissan*.

## Jewish vs Non-Jewish

### Complete Baraita

The *Gemara* now cites a *baraita* that collects and formalizes all the proofs assembled above: *minayin she'ein monim lahem l'mlachim ela miNissan?* — from where do we know that we count for kings only from *Nissan*? The *baraita* marshals each proof in turn. It cites the verse connecting Shlomo's construction of the Temple to the 480th year from *Yetzias Mitzrayim*, establishing the parallel between the two counting systems. It then cites the verse about Aharon's death, *vaya'al Aharon hakohen el Hor Hahar al pi Hashem*, and the verse that places Moshe's address in year forty, month eleven — *vayehi b'arbaim shanah b'ashti asar chodesh* — demonstrating that *Tishrei* is not *Rosh Hashanah*. It then invokes the passage *acharei hakoso es Sichon* and the story of the Canaanite king's attack — *vayishma hakena'ani... vayiru kol ha'eda ki gava Aharon* — to establish the correct chronological sequence. Finally it proceeds through the elimination proofs: the Mishkan-cloud evidence ruling out *Iyar*, the *Matan Torah* verse ruling out *Sivan*, and the Shlomo verse providing the positive proof for *Nissan*.

### Rav Chisda Ruling

Rav Chisda qualifies the entire preceding discussion: *lo shanu ela l'malchei Yisrael* — all of the above applies only to Jewish kings. When counting the regnal years of non-Jewish kings, the year increments not at *Nissan* but at *Tishrei*: *aval l'malchei umos ha'olam miTishrei mininan*.

This distinction raises an interesting question about the nature of this counting system. Is the system for Jewish kings something the *Sages* created and imposed, or are they simply observing and codifying the system that was actually in practice? In the case of non-Jewish kings, it seems clear that the *Gemara* is simply adopting their own counting convention. The case of Jewish kings is somewhat more ambiguous — and in practical terms, how long this system remained operative is itself uncertain, given the disrupted succession of kings through the *Chashmonaim* period and beyond.

## Nehemiah Year Twenty

### Kislev to Nissan

The proof that non-Jewish kings are counted from *Tishrei* derives from the book of *Nechemia*. The *Gemara* cites two verses: the opening of the book, *vayehi bachodesh Kislev shnas esrim* — "and it was in the month of *Kislev* in year twenty"; and a verse from the second chapter, *vayehi bachodesh Nissan shnas esrim l'Artachshashta hamelech* — "and it was in the month of *Nissan* in year twenty of King Artaxerxes." *Kislev* is the ninth month and *Nissan* is the first month of the following spring. Yet between these two events — one in *Kislev* and the other in *Nissan* — the year number remains unchanged at twenty. If *Nissan* were *Rosh Hashanah* for non-Jewish kings, the *Nissan* event should have been called year twenty-one. Since it is still year twenty, *Nissan* is evidently not the *Rosh Hashanah* for non-Jewish kings. The conclusion, by elimination, is that the year increments at *Tishrei*.

At this stage of the discussion, the *Gemara* notes that the month names used in *Nechemia* are Babylonian-Persian names, reflecting the post-exilic reality in which the Jewish community had absorbed the nomenclature of the surrounding culture. This was the cultural context in which names like *Kislev*, *Tammuz*, and *Adar* entered Jewish usage — names that in some cases carry pagan associations (such as *Tammuz*, a Babylonian deity), yet which became embedded in the Jewish calendar.

### Event Sequence Logic

Before accepting the proof, the *Gemara* raises a preliminary objection: how do we know that the event in chapter one of *Nechemia* (*Kislev*) preceded the event in chapter two (*Nissan*)? Perhaps the *Nissan* event happened first, and the fact that the year had not yet incremented is simply because *Nissan* had just passed or the *Kislev* event came later in the same year after *Nissan*.

A further objection: the verse in *Kislev* merely says "year twenty" without specifying *whose* year twenty. Perhaps this "year twenty" refers to a different counting system entirely — perhaps some general era-based count — whereas only the *Nissan* verse explicitly ties the count to Artaxerxes. How do we know both verses are counting the same king's regnal years?

Rav Papa resolves the second question via a *gezeirah shavah*: both passages use the identical phrase *shenas esrim* — "year twenty." The repetition of the exact phrase in both places establishes that both are counting the same system, and specifically the same king's year. *Mah hatam l'Artachshashta af hacha l'Artachshashta* — just as the *Nissan* verse explicitly counts Artaxerxes's years, so too the *Kislev* verse counts Artaxerxes's years.

### Nehemiah Narrative

The *Gemara* then addresses the sequence question directly via a *baraita*: the things that Chanani said to Nechemia were said in *Kislev*, and those were the things that Nechemia subsequently reported to the king in *Nissan*. The *baraita* is supported by an extended close reading of the relevant *pesukim*.

Chapter one of *Nechemia* opens: *Divrei Nechemia ben Chachalyah — vayehi bachodesh Kislev shnas esrim va'ani hayiti b'Shushan habirah* — Nechemia was in the capital city of Shushan. *Vayavo Chanani echad me'achai hu v'anashim miYehudah* — and Chanani, one of his brothers, came along with other men from *Yehudah*. Nechemia asked them about the condition of the Jewish remnant in the land and about *Yerushalayim*. They reported: the survivors left from the exile are there in the land in great misery and shame, *v'chomat Yerushalayim meporetzet ushe'areha nitzetu va'esh* — the walls of *Yerushalayim* are broken down and its gates have been consumed by fire.

This account is the event of *Kislev*. Then in chapter two, *Nechemia* reports this to the king in *Nissan*: he is serving wine before the king, *vayehi bachodesh Nissan shnas esrim l'Artachshashta hamelech yayin lefanav*, and the king notices his distress — *madua panecha ra'im v'ata eincha choleh* — "why do you look so troubled, since you are not sick?" — and diagnoses it: *ein ze ki im roa lev* — "this must be bitterness of heart." Nechemia, after first offering the obligatory salutation *hamelech l'olam yichyeh*, explains that the city of his forefathers' burial lies desolate and its gates have been consumed by fire. The king asks what he seeks; Nechemia offers a silent prayer to God and then petitions: *im al hamelech tov* — if it pleases the king — to be sent to *Yehudah* to rebuild the city of his forefathers. The king, with the *shagal* seated beside him, agrees and asks how long the journey will take. Nechemia provides a timeframe, the king is pleased, and Nechemia is sent forth.

### Tishrei Confirmed

The sequence is thus plain from the *peshat* of the text itself: Nechemia received the report from Chanani in *Kislev*, was distressed by it, and months later — in *Nissan* of the same year — the king noticed his distress and the matter came to a head. One event is clearly the cause and precondition of the other. Both are called year twenty of Artaxerxes. Since *Nissan* — the first month of spring — arrived between *Kislev* and the later event without triggering a year increment, the year for non-Jewish kings does not increment at *Nissan*. The conclusion stands: for non-Jewish kings, the year increments at *Tishrei*.

## Darius Contradiction

### Chaggai Adar-Av

An apparent contradiction now emerges from the book of *Chaggai*. The *Gemara* cites a verse: *u'veyom esrim v'arba'ah lachodesh bashishi bishnas shtayim l'Daryavesh* — on the twenty-fourth day of the sixth month in the second year of Darius — and then a later verse referring to the seventh month without specifying a new year number. Rashi notes that from the context of the preceding *pasuk* it is clear that the seventh month is still year two of Darius. Since the seventh month is *Tishrei*, and we have said that *Tishrei* is *Rosh Hashanah* for non-Jewish kings, the year should have incremented. Yet both events — month six and month seven — are counted as year two. This suggests that for Darius, the year *did not* increment at *Tishrei*, implying instead that *Nissan* was his *Rosh Hashanah*.

Furthermore, the *Gemara* draws on a *baraita* that records: the completion of the Temple building took place on the third of *Adar* in year six of Darius — *v'shitzia beita dena ad yom talata leyarch Adar dehi shenas sheis lemalkus Daryavesh malka*. And a separate tradition records that at that same time, the following year, Ezra began to bring back a major wave of *aliyah*. The verse in *Ezra* then states: *vayavo Yerushalayim bachodesh hachamishi hi shenas hashvi'is lamelech* — he arrived in *Yerushalayim* in the fifth month, which is year seven of the king. The fifth month of year seven is *Av*. Between the *Adar* of year six and the *Av* of year seven, *Nissan* intervened. If *Nissan* were the increment point for Darius, *Av* of that year would have been year *eight*, not year seven. Since it is called year seven, *Nissan* evidently did not function as *Rosh Hashanah* for Darius in this period — which coheres with the principle that non-Jewish kings count from *Tishrei*.

### Rav Yosef Challenge

Rav Yosef raises the core difficulty. The evidence from *Chaggai* — where month seven is still called year two — seems to require that *Nissan*, not *Tishrei*, was the *Rosh Hashanah* for Darius. This directly contradicts the rule established for non-Jewish kings, and stands in tension with the *Ezra* evidence just cited, which fits *Tishrei* as the increment point.

### Kasher King Theory

Rabbi Abbahu proposes a resolution: *Coresh Melech Kasher haya* — Coresh was a righteous king. And therefore, *lepichach manu lo kemalchei Yisrael* — they accordingly counted his years as one counts for Jewish kings, that is, from *Nissan*. This would explain why in *Chaggai* the year did not increment at *Tishrei* — because for a king of this moral stature, *Nissan* was the operative *Rosh Hashanah*.

This resolution is striking, but it raises an immediate practical difficulty. The entire discussion of non-Jewish kings has operated on the assumption that "non-Jewish kings count from *Tishrei*" is a stable rule. To introduce an exception based on the personal righteousness of a king — so that Jewish scribes and prophets would be using a fundamentally different calendar counting for this particular king — is hard to picture as a practical reality. Would the community really have maintained a parallel and divergent year-count for this king while everyone around them was counting differently?

### King Name Identity

There is also the textual problem that the suggestion invokes *Coresh*, while the *Chaggai* passages are about Darius. The *Gemara* therefore cites a *baraita*: *hu Coresh hu Daryavesh hu Artachshashta* — this king bore all three names. *Coresh* because he was a *kasher* king — and Rashi notes the wordplay between *Coresh* and *kasher*. *Artachshashta* was his royal title, *al shem malchuto*. And *Daryavesh* was his actual personal name. Rashi qualifies this identification, noting that this is not the original *Coresh* — the famous Cyrus who issued the initial decree permitting the rebuilding of the Temple — but a later king who happened to share the name *Coresh*, given that royal names were reused in antiquity. The identification of the original *Coresh* as a righteous figure is not outlandish — certain traditions even invoke a phrase like *Coresh avdi*, "Coresh my servant," in prophetic contexts — but the claim that his successors operated under a distinct year-counting regime remains difficult.

## Unresolved Difficulty

### Calendar Change Problem

Rav Yosef presses the objection. Even granting that Darius-Coresh was a *melech kasher* and was therefore counted like Jewish kings from *Nissan*, this creates a self-contradiction within the *pesukim* themselves. The *Chaggai* evidence implies that during the earlier period of Darius's reign — when the year did not increment at *Tishrei* — he was being counted from *Nissan*, consistent with the *kasher* designation. But the later *Ezra* evidence — where *Av* of year seven follows *Adar* of year six without the intervening *Nissan* triggering a new year — implies that by that later point the year was *not* incrementing at *Nissan*, which means the counting had reverted to *Tishrei*. One cannot have it both ways. If he was a *melech kasher* who counted from *Nissan*, then the *Ezra* passage should read year eight; if he counted from *Tishrei*, the *Chaggai* passage should show an increment at *Tishrei*. The two sets of *pesukim*, drawn from the same king's reign, appear to operate under mutually inconsistent calendar assumptions.

Furthermore, Rav Yosef objects: *mi dami? hasam Coresh hacha Daryavesh* — how can you simply conflate them? One king is called *Coresh* and the other *Daryavesh*. The *baraita*'s identification that they are one and the same person addresses this, but the underlying contradiction in the calendar assumptions remains.

### Soured King Theory

The *Gemara* proposes an answer: *lo kashya — kan kodem she-hichmitz, kan achar she-hichmitz* — there is no difficulty. The earlier *Chaggai* evidence dates from the period *before* Darius soured — when he was a righteous king and was counted from *Nissan*. The later *Ezra* evidence dates from after he had *hichmitz* — a term drawn from the imagery of wine turning to vinegar, or dough souring — when he had deteriorated morally. At that later stage, the counting reverted to *Tishrei* as for all non-Jewish kings.

The connection between *chametz* and moral deterioration is noted — the *chametz* imagery of *yetzer hara* is embedded in the very term *hichmitz* here.

### Kahana Objection

Rav Kahana challenges this: who says Darius soured? The *pasuk* from *Ezra* records that when Ezra was returning to the land, the king — identified as Artaxerxes/Darius — issued a remarkably generous directive to the treasury officials: *u'mah chashchan* — whatever is needed — whether rams, bulls, lambs, grain, salt, wine, or oil — *kelamar kahanya di b'Yerushalem* — whatever the priests in *Yerushalayim* request for the sacrifices — *leheve mityahiv lahem yom b'yom* — let it be provided to them on a daily basis from the royal treasury, *u'd'la shalu* — without them even needing to ask. A king who voluntarily funds the *Beis Hamikdash* from his own treasury, unsolicited, hardly seems like a king who has "soured."

### Self-Interest Motive

Rav Yitzchak responds to Rav Kahana directly: *matuncha* — your very proof contains the answer. What does the end of the verse say? *Di lehevon mehakarvin nichochin l'Eloah shemaya u'matzlin l'chayei malka u'vnohei* — "that they may offer pleasing aromas to the God of heaven and pray for the life of the king and for his sons." The king's generosity was not altruistic; it was entirely self-interested. He was purchasing prayers for his own life and the lives of his children. The *Gemara* notes with gentle irony that perhaps one should not be too critical of a patron whose self-interested contributions result in prayers and sacrifices — and the discussion of whether self-interested generosity constitutes genuine virtue will be taken up on the following *daf*.
