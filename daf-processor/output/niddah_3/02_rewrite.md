# Niddah 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mikvah & Retroactive

### Mikvah Unknown Status

The sugya opens by situating us within a broader series of comparisons the Gemara has been drawing between the case of a *niddah* and other instances in which an object or person possesses a known status that subsequently changes — and the question is when precisely that change occurred. Among those comparisons is the case of a *mikvah* found to be deficient in its required volume of water. When such a *mikvah* is discovered lacking, what is the status of vessels or persons who immersed in it without knowing at the time that it was inadequate? Were their immersions valid? Are they *tahor* or *tamei*?

### Rabbis: Definitely Impure

The *rabbanan* hold that such vessels or persons are definitively *tamei*. Their reasoning rests on the original status of the object in question: it began as *tamei*, and unless we can establish with certainty that a valid purification occurred, that status of *tumah* remains fully in force.

### R. Shimon: Doubtful

Rabbi Shimon disagrees and holds that the matter is a *safek* — a doubt. He draws on the model of *sotah*, from which the Gemara derives general principles about how to treat doubtful cases of *tumah*. The principle, as classically formulated, is: *safek tumah b'reshut hayachid* — *tamei*; *safek tumah b'reshut harabim* — *tahor*. Rabbi Shimon applies this model, but not fully. He holds that in a *reshut harabim* the vessels are *tahor*, yet in a *reshut hayachid* they are merely *safek* — doubtful — rather than definitively *tamei*. This already signals that his application of the *sotah* model is not straightforward.

### Sotah Analogy Issue

The Gemara notes that the opening claim was that both the *rabbanan* and Rabbi Shimon derived their positions from *sotah*. It then challenges each in turn. Regarding the *rabbanan*: if *sotah* is truly your model, then why do you not follow it consistently and distinguish between *reshut hayachid* and *reshut harabim* in the *mikvah* case? Why do you say definitively *tamei* in all circumstances, without regard to domain?

The Gemara's initial response is striking. It says: *hatam mishum stirah hu* — in the case of *sotah*, the significance of the private domain is not formalistic at all. The reason a *sotah* is treated as *tamei* is because she secluded herself with the man — *stirah*, seclusion — and seclusion cannot occur in a public domain. So the real operative question is not "was it in a *reshut hayachid*?" but rather "is there a basis to suspect that something happened?" If we were truly logical about the *sotah* model rather than formalistic, we would say that whenever there is a genuine basis for suspicion, the person is *tamei* — regardless of domain. And here, in the *mikvah* case, the basis for doubt is clear: the *mikvah* was found lacking. Why should the domain matter at all?

### Logic vs. Formalism

This is a genuinely bold analytical move, and the Gemara is aware of it. If we follow this logic rigorously, the rule should be that *safek tumah* is *tamei* whenever there is a real-world basis for the doubt — which is the case here with the deficient *mikvah* — and the formalistic distinction between *reshut hayachid* and *reshut harabim* would be irrelevant. Yet this is precisely what we do *not* say throughout *Shas*. The established rule everywhere is the formalistic one: private domain is *tamei*, public domain is *tahor*. So the Gemara pulls back: granted, we apply the formalistic rule. Then the question returns — if *sotah* is your model and you apply it formalistically, why does the *mikvah* case not follow the *sotah* distinction between domains?

This exchange also offers an opportunity to reflect on the general phenomenon of formalistic rules versus case-by-case logical assessment in *halacha*. Legal systems, including *halacha*, often prefer quantifiable formal rules over individualized assessments, because formal rules allow the system to function with predictability. In matters of *tumah* and *taharah*, which already carry a more ritualistic character, this degree of formalism may be less troubling than in areas like *kashrut*, where one might want the rules to more closely track reality. When a formal rule generates both stringencies and leniencies independent of the underlying logic, that is a feature of the formalism itself — and the *sotah* case, as one of the questioners here noted, is particularly striking because the *tumah* it creates is not *tumah* in the ordinary sense but a relational prohibition toward her husband, rooted not in what objectively happened but in the husband's jealousy acted upon in formal legal ways, involving warning (*kinui*) in front of witnesses and subsequent seclusion witnessed by others.

### Tarti L'Reiusa

The Gemara resolves the question about the *rabbanan* with the principle of *tarti l'reiusa* — two bad things. The *mikvah* case is not analogous to standard *sotah*-model cases because it involves two compounding negative factors. First, the *mikvah* was found lacking. Second — and this is the critical point — the vessels or person began with a status of *tumah*. In the standard application of the *sotah* model, one begins from a position of *taharah* and the question is whether *tumah* supervened. There, it is reasonable to say: in a public domain, we will not assume *tumah* occurred; in a private domain, we are stricter. But when the person or object began as *tamei*, the question is the reverse: did a valid purification occur? In that case, we will not rely on doubt. Unless we know for certain that the purification was effective, the original status of *tumah* remains unchanged.

## Status Quo Distinction

### Impure Starting Point

The principle of *tarti l'reiusa* stated at the end of the previous section is now developed more precisely. The core insight is that the *sotah* model and its derivative rule — *safek tumah b'reshut hayachid tamei* — apply only when the question is whether *tumah* came into being in the first place. That is *techilas tumah*: the person started *tahor*, and we are uncertain whether they became *tamei*. In such cases, the domain matters: in a *reshut hayachid*, we rule *tamei*; in a *reshut harabim*, we rule *tahor*. But the *mikvah* case is structurally different. There the person or vessel began as *tamei*, and the question is whether a valid *tevilah* successfully removed that *tumah*. This is *sof tumah* — the end of the process of *tumah*, not its beginning. The *rabbanan* hold that you cannot apply the *sotah* model to *sof tumah*. The status quo of *tumah* holds: *mispika lo mafkinan leih mitumaso* — out of doubt we do not extract a person from their established status of *tumah*.

This principle is symmetric: the law always favors the status quo. If your status quo is *tahor* and there is a doubt whether you became *tamei*, we will not impose *tumah* upon you without the domain-based rules providing a clear basis. If your status quo is *tamei* and there is a doubt whether you became *tahor*, we will not remove that *tumah* based on doubt alone. In both directions, the law is inclined to retain your existing status rather than assume it changed.

### R. Shimon Alternative

Rabbi Shimon holds differently. He is willing to apply the *sotah* model even to the *sof tumah* case — *gamar sof tumah mitechilas tumah* — learning the end of the process from the beginning. Just as in *techilas tumah* we say *b'reshut harabim tahor*, so too in *sof tumah*, if the *tevilah* occurred in a *reshut harabim* and there is a doubt whether it was valid, we say *tahor*. Rabbi Shimon is not moved by the fact that the person began *tamei*. At the end of the day, the operative question is whether the *tevilah* was valid, and he applies the *sotah* model to answer that question.

### Raglayim L'Davar

The Gemara then returns to a challenge against Rabbi Shimon's position: if *sotah* is your model, why in a *reshut hayachid* do you say *safek* rather than *vadai tamei*, as we find by *sotah* itself? The Gemara's answer here is important. By *sotah*, the reason she is treated as definitively *tamei* — meaning forbidden to her husband — even though it is only a doubt is that *yesh raglayim ladavar*: there is a concrete basis for the suspicion. He warned her (*kinui*), and she then went into seclusion (*stirah*). Those external, witnessed facts give real-world grounding to the suspicion. It is not a free-floating doubt.

### Mikvah Lacks Basis

By contrast, in the *mikvah* case, there is no such basis for assuming the worst. The *mikvah* was found lacking at some point, but we have no particular reason to think this occurred earlier rather than now. There is no *raglayim ladavar* — no concrete circumstance pointing to a specific time or suggesting that the *tevilah* was in fact invalid. Hence, even in a *reshut hayachid*, Rabbi Shimon would not treat it as *vadai tamei*; he says *safek*.

### Rashi on R. Shimon

Rashi draws a striking conclusion from this analysis. Since Rabbi Shimon holds that the only reason *sotah* is treated as *vadai tmeiah* — even in a *reshut hayachid* — is the *raglayim ladavar* specific to that case, it follows that he rejects the general principle we otherwise derive from *sotah*: that *safek tumah b'reshut hayachid* is definitively *tamei*. According to Rabbi Shimon, the rule for *sotah* is that a *reshut harabim* yields *tahor*, but a *reshut hayachid* in ordinary cases yields only *safek*, not *vadai tumah*. The *vadai* ruling by *sotah* is unique to that case because of the *raglayim ladavar*. In every other case of *safek tumah*, even in a *reshut hayachid*, the result is *safek* — and practically, one would need to immerse again and the *terumah* handled in the interim would carry a *tumas safek*.

An alternative formulation, introduced by *ve'iba'is eima*, says that Rabbi Shimon's point is precisely the structural one about beginning versus end of the *tumah* process. The *rabbanan* insist the two are incomparable: when you begin *tahor*, a *reshut hayachid* doubt yields *tamei*; when you begin *tamei*, you remain *tamei* out of doubt, because *mispika lo machtinan leih letumah* and *mispika lo mafkinan leih mitumaso* — the law favors the status quo in each direction. Rabbi Shimon holds that *sof tumah* can indeed be learned from *techilas tumah*, and the *sotah* model applies throughout.

The practical upshot is that the debate between the *rabbanan* and Rabbi Shimon concerns whether the status quo of *tumah* is an independent, overriding factor (*rabbanan*) or whether it is simply one element in a broader *safek* analysis to which the *sotah* rules apply (*Rabbi Shimon*).

## Mavoi & Niddah Special

### Mavoi Dead Rodent

The Gemara introduces one more comparison before turning fully to the unique features of *niddah*. A *mishnah* teaches: if a dead *sheretz* — one of the creeping creatures that cause *tumah* — is found in a *mavoi* (a courtyard), then whoever was in that courtyard is retroactively rendered *tamei*. The contamination is assumed to have begun at some earlier point — *metamei l'mafrei'a* — and one cannot assume the *sheretz* arrived only just now, until one can say: "I checked this courtyard and there was no *sheretz* there," or until the last time the courtyard was swept, since the sweeping also served as a de facto inspection for *tumah* concerns.

This is clearly analogous to the approach of Beit Hillel regarding *niddah*: we are concerned retroactively until the last known point of verification. The question naturally arises: would Shammai disagree with this ruling about the *mavoi*, and if not, what distinguishes the *mavoi* from *niddah*?

### Two Bad Things

The Gemara initially applies the *tarti l'reiusa* framework here as well, though in a more generic sense than before. In the *mavoi* case, there are multiple potential sources of *sheratzim*: rodents indigenous to the courtyard itself and rodents that may have come from elsewhere. The multiplicity of possible origins gives additional reason to suspect the *sheretz* may have been there earlier. This functions as "two bad things" in a looser, more general sense — several factors compound to make retroactive *tumah* the appropriate ruling — and the ruling stands for the *mavoi* just as it does for the deficient *mikvah*.

### Hargasha Awareness

But the Gemara then pivots. Rather than continuing to multiply formalistic distinctions between *niddah* and these other cases, it introduces an entirely different mode of reasoning. The Gemara says: *ho'il ve'ishah margeshes be'atzmah* — a woman feels within herself when she gets her period. This is the first and most important reason why *niddah* is a genuinely distinct case. Rather than treating the woman as an inert vessel whose status is determined by external, objective rules, the Gemara here restores the woman as a subject with her own inner experience. The *hargashah* — the physical sensation that accompanies menstruation — means that a woman knows when her period begins. And therefore, Shammai holds, we need not assume it happened earlier: if it had happened, she would have known. Since she did not report experiencing it, we do not have to concern ourselves with the retroactive period.

This is a conceptually important move. The whole previous discussion — comparing *niddah* to a *mikvah*, a barrel of wine turning to vinegar, a *mavoi* with a *sheretz* — treated the woman as an object whose status is determined by the same abstract rules governing vessels and spaces. The *hargashah* argument says: no, *niddah* is different, because here we have a person with subjective awareness of what is happening in her own body, and that subjective reality is halachically significant.

Tosafot raises an obvious objection: if *hargashah* is the reason we do not concern ourselves with the past, what about the case before us where presumably the woman is *asking* — suggesting she does not have clear *hargashah* at this moment? Tosafot's answer is that this is a matter of statistical reliability. As a rule, a woman will be aware when her period begins. We therefore do not impose retroactive concern, because the working assumption is that she would have known. Exceptional cases where she lacked awareness do not override the general rule.

### Hillel's Objection

Hillel counters: *kesavur margeshes mei raglayim hi* — perhaps she did have a sensation, but she discounted it, attributing it to the feeling of urination rather than menstruation. The two sensations may overlap or be confused, and so one cannot rely on the *hargashah* to rule out retroactive bleeding. Hillel does not deny that women have a sensation — he merely argues that the sensation is not sufficiently distinctive to reliably distinguish between the two, and therefore Shammai's inference is unsound.

### Sleeping Woman Wake

The Gemara then presses Shammai: what about a woman who was asleep when her period began? She could not have consciously experienced the *hargashah*. The Gemara answers on Shammai's behalf: *yeshena nami agav tzara mit'ara* — even while asleep, the discomfort accompanying the onset of menstruation will wake her. The analogy is to the sensation of needing to urinate, which also wakes people from sleep. According to Shammai, even in sleep a woman will be aware that her period has begun, and so the argument from *hargashah* remains intact.

The question of whether the *hargashah* refers to the sensation of the flow itself or to the pressure and urge preceding it is not definitively settled in this passage. Hillel's argument about confusion with *mei raglayim* could apply either to the sensation of actual flow or to the feeling of pressure, and Shammai's response that the sensation is reliable does not specify precisely what kind of sensation he has in mind.

### Shotah Exception

The Gemara raises a further challenge: what about a *shotah* — a woman who is not of sound mind and not fully cognizant of her surroundings? Such a woman would not be reliably aware of her own *hargashah*. Here the Gemara acknowledges: *modeh Shammai b'shotah* — Shammai concedes this case. A *shotah* cannot be expected to register and report her *hargashah*, and so for her, the retroactive concern does apply.

But the Gemara notes that the Mishnah says *kol hanashim* — all women — have this status of being *tamei* only from the time of discovery according to Shammai. If Shammai concedes the *shotah* case, how can the Mishnah say "all women"? The answer is that *kol hanashim* here means all women under normal circumstances — it refers to women who are *pikchot*, possessed of ordinary cognitive function. The word *kol* is not meant to be literally exhaustive; rather, it is meant to indicate that Shammai's leniency applies to a far broader category than Rabbi Eliezer would allow. Rabbi Eliezer identifies only four specific categories of women who are *dayyan sha'atan* — assessed only from the moment they see — whereas Shammai extends this to all women of ordinary awareness, not just exceptional cases.

### Ketamim Paradox

The Gemara raises a genuinely challenging question from the laws of *ketamim* — bloodstains discovered on clothing whose origin is unknown. The halacha is that if there is no other plausible explanation for the stain, the woman is *tamei* at least rabbinically. But the very definition of the *ketamim* case is that the woman had no awareness: she discovered the stain after the fact, without any felt experience of bleeding. If Shammai's entire position rests on the woman's *hargashah*, then the category of *ketamim* should be impossible under his framework — for how can she be *tamei* from a stain if he holds that bleeding is always accompanied by a felt sensation?

Conversely, one might think that Shammai would reject the entire institution of *ketamim*, since according to him it is essentially impossible to bleed without awareness. The Gemara inverts this: since Shammai holds the *hargashah* always occurs, he should say that a discovered stain is actually evidence she did not bleed — for if she had, she would have felt it.

One of the questioners here raises a genuinely important point: the *hargashah* argument pertains to menstrual blood, but there are forms of uterine bleeding that are not menstruation and that could nonetheless cause *tumah*. Shammai's argument from *hargashah* does not necessarily account for non-menstrual bleeding, and the Gemara's treatment of *ketamim* seems to elide this distinction.

### Abaye on Stains

Abaye resolves the tension: *modeh Shammai b'kesamim* — Shammai does agree to the category of *ketamim*, but only under a specific condition: when there is no other plausible source for the blood. *Be-tzippor lo nit'aska* — she was not slaughtering birds; *u-ve-shuk shel tabbachim lo avra* — she did not pass through the butcher's market. When every other possible explanation for the stain has been eliminated, and the blood can only have come from her, then even Shammai acknowledges she is *tamei*. This is not because he retracts his claim about *hargashah*; rather, the *ketamim* ruling is applied precisely in those cases where the alternative explanations are ruled out, making it halachically necessary to attribute the stain to her. Shammai's *hargashah* principle does not prevent the *ketamim* category from operating — it merely ensures that it applies only when no other attribution is available.

## Blood Flow Biology

### Blood Comes Out

A second, independent reason is offered for why *niddah* is a special case distinct from the *mavoi* and *mikvah* comparisons. The Gemara states: *d'im isa d'hava dam meikara hava atei* — if there had been blood earlier, it would have come out already. This is Shammai's biological argument. A woman becomes *tamei* as a *niddah* once the blood passes through the cervix into the vaginal canal. But Shammai holds that once that occurs, the blood will exit the body immediately — there is no mechanism to retain it. Therefore, if she sees blood now, it must have emerged now. If it had been present earlier, we would have seen it earlier. The conclusion is the same as before: we are not concerned that she was *tamei* retroactively.

This argument shifts the basis for Shammai's position from the woman's subjective experience to an objective biological claim. Even setting aside whether she would have felt a *hargashah*, the physical reality is such that blood cannot linger. The two arguments — *hargashah* and immediate flow — are thus distinct, and the Gemara will later identify practical differences between them.

### Blood Retention

Hillel disagrees with the biological premise: *kotlei beit harechem he'emiduhu* — the walls of the *beit harechem* retained it. While *beit harechem* literally means uterus, in this context it must refer to the vaginal walls, since the *tumah* of *niddah* attaches once the blood has already crossed the cervix. Hillel holds that blood can collect along the vaginal walls and remain there without flowing out immediately. This is why he is concerned that a woman may have bled earlier without it being externally visible: the blood was present internally, retained by the vaginal walls, without yet exiting the body.

This is the foundation of so much of the broader *halacha* of *niddah*: the laws of *bedikot*, the entire question of when precisely *tumah* attaches and when it can be detected — all of this depends on the reality that blood may be present internally in a way that is felt by the woman but not externally visible. Hillel is acknowledging this biological reality and building his halachic position upon it.

### Moch Device

The Gemara raises a challenge to Shammai's biological argument from the case of a woman who uses a *moch* — a sponge or diaphragm-like device — during intercourse. Certain women are required or permitted to use a *moch* because pregnancy poses a danger to them. If such a device is present, it could physically obstruct the flow of blood, giving concrete physical reason to think blood might have been present earlier without flowing out. This is exactly the case Shammai's argument cannot account for: here there is a reason blood would not have exited immediately.

Abaye holds: *modeh Shammai b'meshameshes b'moch* — Shammai concedes the *moch* case. When a *moch* is present, the blood-comes-out-immediately argument does not hold, and retroactive *tumah* concern applies.

Rava, however, disagrees. He holds that even with a *moch* in place, *agav zei'a michvatz* — because of the body's internal heat and moisture, the *moch* will shrivel and compress, and blood will still flow out even with the device present. Therefore, even in the *moch* case, Shammai would not concede. However, *modeh Rava moch dachus* — Rava agrees that if the *moch* is tightly packed and firmly in place, then even he would say Shammai concedes. A tightly fitted *moch* genuinely obstructs the flow, and in that case Shammai's biological argument fails.

### Practical Difference

The Gemara draws out the practical implications of the two approaches — *hargashah* versus immediate blood flow — and why it matters which one is the operative reason behind Shammai's position. If Shammai's reasoning is the *hargashah*, then the cases where he would concede are those where the *hargashah* is unreliable: the *shotah*, who lacks normal awareness. If his reasoning is the immediate blood-flow argument, then the cases where he would concede are those where blood might not flow immediately: the *moch* case according to Abaye, or the *moch dachus* case according to Rava. The two rationales, though both leading to the same general leniency regarding retroactive *tumah*, diverge precisely in their exceptional cases. The *hargashah* approach is about the woman's subjective experience; the blood-flow approach is about objective biological mechanism. And these generate different exceptions: a woman with impaired cognition (*shotah*) on one approach, a woman with a physical obstruction (*moch*) on the other.

## Policy: Priya V'Rivya

### Box Analogy

The Gemara now records a *baraita* that preserves the actual exchange between Hillel and Shammai. Hillel challenges Shammai with the case of a *kuppa* — a box filled with *terumah* — used in one corner of a space, in which a *sheretz* is subsequently found in another corner. The *sheretz* could have been there the entire time the *terumah* was being handled, in which case the *terumah* was rendered *tamei*. Hillel asks: would you not admit that in that case we are concerned retroactively? And how is *niddah* different?

The Gemara explains that Hillel was operating under the assumption that Shammai's rationale concerned the biological reality — that blood exits immediately. Hillel thought: if that is your argument, then I will challenge you with a physical analogy where something found now should have been visible earlier if it was there. How is your *niddah* case different from my *kuppa* case?

### Rav: Policy Override

Rav answers: *ta'ama de'Shammai mishum bitul pirya ve'rivya* — Shammai's real reason is a policy concern. If women must always be concerned that they might have been *tamei* retroactively — since we can never be sure that blood was not present earlier without flowing out — the practical consequence is that couples will become afraid to engage in sexual relations. The ongoing uncertainty about whether a woman might be internally bleeding without visible evidence will create a pervasive anxiety that will discourage intercourse and thereby lead to a suppression of procreation. Shammai's leniency is therefore not primarily a logical derivation from biological or psychological facts, but a *gezeirat hakatuv* in effect — an overriding policy decision that the presumption of *taharah* must be maintained in order to safeguard *pirya ve'rivya*.

This is a striking attribution, and more than one observer has noted that such a concern — for the practical ability of families to sustain conjugal life — sounds more typically Hillelite in temperament. Yet here it is Shammai who invokes it.

### Baraita Debate

The *baraita* presents Shammai's challenge to Hillel directly: *im ken bitalta bnot Yisrael mi'pirya ve'rivya* — "if so, you have abolished [the capacity of] the daughters of Israel for procreation." Shammai is addressing Hillel's inclination to be concerned retroactively, just as we are in the *kuppa* and *mavoi* cases. He says: I understand your logic. But think through its implications. If women are always potentially *tamei* retroactively, then every act of intercourse is shadowed by the possibility that she was *tamei* at the time. This is not a manageable concern — it will lead people to refrain from sexual relations altogether.

### Reconciliation

The Gemara reconciles the *baraita* with the earlier presentation of Shammai's rationale. The *baraita* shows that Hillel thought Shammai's reasoning was about the biological reality — that blood exits immediately — and he challenged Shammai on that basis with the *kuppa* analogy. Shammai's response in the *baraita* is twofold: first, my real reason is *bitul pirya ve'rivya* — the policy concern. Second, even if you want to argue on the biological level, I can answer you there too: *l'zu yesh lah shulayim, l'zu ein lah shulayim* — a box has a floor; the woman's body does not. A box has a contained interior where a *sheretz* can sit undetected. The body does not — blood will flow out and not be retained, so if she is bleeding now, it began now. Thus Shammai's biological argument and his policy argument are complementary, and he can invoke both.

### Hillel's Safeguard

The Gemara then asks: given this presentation of the two rationales, how does the *baraita* read if we take the blood-comes-out argument as primary? In that reading, Hillel is not just challenging Shammai — he is also conceding the underlying logic: yes, if there was blood earlier, it would have come out. Granted, the strict law says she is not *tamei* retroactively. But, says Hillel to Shammai: *asay seyag le'devarekha* — make a rabbinic safeguard nonetheless. Even if the strict law is on your side, the Sages regularly erect safeguards, and here too we should add a layer of rabbinic concern for the period prior to the discovery. This, notably, is the Hillelite move — to propose adding a *seyag* — even though one might have expected it more from Shammai.

### Shammai Rejects

Shammai refuses. *Im kein bitalta bnot Yisrael mipriya urviya* — even a rabbinic safeguard is unacceptable, because even rabbinic concern will, in practice, discourage intercourse. Once you establish as a matter of ongoing religious practice that a woman's retroactive status must be investigated, people will internalize the message that she may have been *tamei* — and that anxiety will permeate the entire conjugal relationship, not only the question of *terumah*.

### Hillel Final

Hillel's final response is to draw a distinction: *mipriya ve'rivya mi kamina — letoharos dekamina*. I am not talking about prohibiting intercourse retroactively — how could one even give such a warning after the fact? What I am proposing is a concern only for *taharot*: we would say that the *terumah* she handled during the retroactive period is suspect. Nobody is suggesting that the intercourse itself was prohibited. The two are separable, and retroactive concern for *tumah* in the domain of *taharot* need not generate the same psychological impact on conjugal life.

Shammai rejects this distinction as well: *libo nokufo u'porosh* — his heart will strike him and he will withdraw. The concern for *taharot* cannot be cleanly compartmentalized. Once a man knows that halacha regards his wife's retroactive status as doubtful — even only for the purposes of *terumah* — the implicit message is that she may have been *tamei* during that period. That message cannot be confined to one domain. It will inevitably create anxiety about the relationship as a whole, and lead to avoidance of intercourse.

The Gemara identifies a nuance between the two versions of Shammai's reasoning. In the first version — where *pirya ve'rivya* is the primary and overriding consideration — Shammai appears to be invoking policy to override what strict logic would otherwise demand, meaning the law as a matter of principle would have inclined toward retroactive concern, but he is overriding it for policy reasons. In the second version — where the blood-comes-out argument is primary — the strict law itself supports Shammai's leniency, and Hillel is the one proposing to add something beyond the law via a *seyag*. Shammai's rejection in the second version is therefore not about overriding the law but about refusing to create a new stringency whose policy consequences he finds unacceptable. These are meaningfully different positions, and the distinction between them bears on how we understand the relationship between Shammai's halachic logic and his policy sensibility.
