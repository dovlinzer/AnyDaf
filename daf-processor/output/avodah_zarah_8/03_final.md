# Avodah Zarah 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nachum HaMadi Intro

### Opening Context

The present daf continues from the sugya begun at the end of 7b, where the Mishnah addressed the question of precisely when, in relation to the holidays of idolaters, one is forbidden from conducting business with them. Rabbi Yishmael's position was three days before and three days after. The Gemara explored whether the holiday day itself is included, and whether "three days" encompasses the days surrounding a Sunday — which, when counted in both directions together with the day itself, would effectively prohibit commerce every week of the year, amounting to a blanket prohibition on doing business with non-Jews at any time. In the course of that discussion, the opinion of Nachum HaMadi was introduced, and since his name has entered the sugya, the Gemara now proceeds to survey several of his other rulings.

### Nachum: One Day Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גוּפָא, נַחוּם הַמָּדִי אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ אָסוּר אֶלָּא יוֹם אֶחָד לִפְנֵי אֵידֵיהֶן. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: נִשְׁתַּקַּע הַדָּבָר וְלֹא נֶאֱמַר. וְהָאִיכָּא רַבָּנַן בָּתְרָאֵי דְּקָיְימִי כְּוָותֵיהּ! מַאן חֲכָמִים? נַחוּם הַמָּדִי הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** Having mentioned Naḥum the Mede's opinion, the Gemara discusses **the** matter **itself. Naḥum the Mede says:** It is **prohibited only** on **the day before their festival.** The other Sages **said to him:** It would be best if this **matter were lost and not stated,** as it is not the *halakha*. The Gemara asks: **But aren't there the later Rabbis, who hold in accordance with his** opinion? The Gemara answers: **Who are** these **Rabbis?** This **is** referring to **Naḥum the Mede** himself, but the other Sages disagree.

The *gufah* opens with Nachum HaMadi's position on the core question of the sugya: *Nachum HaMadi omer eino assur ela yom echad lifnei ihen* — it is only forbidden the single day before their holidays. This is a considerably more lenient position than that of Rabbi Yishmael.

### Nachum's Background

Nachum HaMadi is a *tanna* from the first century, contemporaneous with the period just before the destruction of the Temple. His epithet *HaMadi* indicates that he originates from Madai — he is essentially a foreigner, someone who lived among non-Jews in a different cultural context from the mainstream rabbinic milieu of the Land of Israel. This background plausibly informs his somewhat more permissive views on the question of proximity to non-Jewish practice. It is worth noting that Rav Acha bar Manyumi, who will enter the discussion shortly, also traces his origins to that same region.

### Rejection Formula

The Rabbis respond to Nachum HaMadi's position with a formulaic phrase that will recur throughout this brief section: *nishtaka hadavar velo ne'emar* — either this ruling was never authoritatively stated, or it was circulated only within the *beit midrash* as a position under discussion and was subsequently lost without ever being accepted as normative law. The Gemara then raises an objection: are there not later Rabbis who hold the same view as Nachum HaMadi? To this the Gemara responds: *man chachamim? Nachum HaMadi hu* — the "Sages" who are said to share this view are none other than Nachum HaMadi himself. Having noted his position on the holidays, the Gemara proceeds to adduce several other rulings of his that were similarly rejected.

## Nachum: Animals & Tithes

### Horse Sale Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: נַחוּם הַמָּדִי אוֹמֵר: מוֹכְרִין לָהֶן סוּס זָכָר וְזָקֵן בַּמִּלְחָמָה. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: נִשְׁתַּקַּע הַדָּבָר וְלֹא נֶאֱמַר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites additional statements of Naḥum the Mede. It **is taught** in **another** *baraita* that **Naḥum the Mede says:** Although it is prohibited to sell large livestock to gentiles, **one may sell a male and elderly horse to them during a war,** as it cannot be used for battle. The Sages **said to him:** It would be best if this **matter were lost and not stated,** as it is not the *halakha*.

A *baraita* is cited: *Nachum HaMadi omer mocherin lahem sus zachar vezaken bamilchama* — Nachum HaMadi ruled that one may sell non-Jews a male, elderly horse during wartime. The Rabbis responded with the same formula: *nishtaka hadavar velo ne'emar*.

### Animal Sales Mishna

This ruling relates to a Mishnah that will be treated in greater detail when the Gemara reaches 14b. That Mishnah states that in a place where it is customary to sell small animals to *ovdei kochavim*, one may do so; where the custom is not to sell, one may not. Regardless of custom, however, large animals — *behema gassa* — as well as calves and foals, whether whole or injured, may not be sold anywhere. Rabbi Yehuda permits the sale of an injured animal, and Ben Beteira permits the sale of a horse — the horse being treated as a distinct category, as it was considered primarily a riding and military animal rather than a work or slaughter animal.

### Ben Beteira Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאִיכָּא בֶּן בְּתִירָא דְּקָאֵי כְּוָותֵיהּ, דִּתְנַן: בֶּן בְּתִירָא מַתִּיר בַּסּוּס. בֶּן בְּתִירָא לָא מְפַלֵּיג בֵּין זְכָרִים לִנְקֵבוֹת, אִיהוּ מִדְּקָא מְפַלֵּיג בֵּין זְכָרִים לִנְקֵבוֹת, כְּרַבָּנַן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ. וּלְרַבָּנַן נִשְׁתַּקַּע הַדָּבָר וְלֹא נֶאֱמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't there ben Beteira, who holds in accordance with his** opinion? **As we learned** in a mishna (14b): **Ben Beteira permits** selling **a horse** to gentiles. The Gemara answers: **Since ben Beteira does not differentiate between male and female** horses, and Naḥum the Mede **does differentiate between male and female** horses, it is clear that Naḥum the Mede **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** that one is generally not permitted to sell a horse to a gentile, only he permits it in this specific circumstance. **And according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** it would be best if this **matter were lost and not stated.**

The Gemara challenges the blanket rejection of Nachum HaMadi on the horse question: surely Ben Beteira holds the same position, since he permits the sale of horses. The Gemara distinguishes between them: Ben Beteira does not differentiate between male and female horses — he permits the sale of horses generally. Nachum HaMadi, by contrast, introduces a distinction between male and female animals. Since his leniency turns on a gender-based distinction that no other authority draws, his position is not parallel to Ben Beteira's, and the response remains: *de-rabbanan nishtaka hadavar velo ne'emar* — the Rabbis do not hold by this.

### Shevet Tithing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא [אִידַּךְ], נַחוּם הַמָּדִי אוֹמֵר: הַשֶּׁבֶת מִתְעַשֵּׂר זֶרַע וְיָרָק וְזִירִין. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: נִשְׁתַּקַּע הַדָּבָר וְלֹא נֶאֱמַר! וְהָאִיכָּא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר דְּקָאֵי כְּוָותֵיהּ, דִּתְנַן: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: הַשֶּׁבֶת מִתְעַשֶּׂרֶת זֶרַע וְיָרָק וְזִירִין! הָתָם בִּדְגִנּוּנִיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a similar dispute. It **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Naḥum the Mede says: The dill** plant **is subject to tithes,** whether it is used as **a seed, or a vegetable, or a pod.** The Sages **said to him:** It would be best if this **matter were lost and not stated,** as it is not the *halakha*. The Gemara asks: **But isn't there Rabbi Eliezer, who holds in accordance with his** opinion? **As we learned** in a mishna (*Ma'asrot* 4:5) that **Rabbi Eliezer says: The dill** plant **is subject to tithes** whether it is used as **a seed, or a vegetable, or a pod.** The Gemara answers: **There,** Rabbi Eliezer is referring **to the garden** variety of dill, which is of such a high quality that its seeds, vegetable, and pods are eaten. By contrast, in the case of wild dill, only its seeds and vegetable are eaten, not the pods.

The Gemara cites a further *baraita*: *Nachum HaMadi omer ha-shevet mit'aser zera ve-yerek ve-zirim* — Nachum HaMadi ruled that the *shevet* plant — identified by Rashi as dill, possibly also anise — is subject to *ma'aser* in all three components: its seeds, its leaves, and its stems. The Rabbis again responded: *nishtaka hadavar velo ne'emar*.

The background to this ruling requires brief clarification. The obligation of *ma'aser* on vegetables is *mi-de-rabbanan* only; the Torah's tithing obligation, *de-oraita*, applies exclusively to *dagan, tirosh ve-yitzhar* — grain, wine, and oil. And even the rabbinic extension of *ma'aser* to vegetables applies only to items that are ordinarily eaten. The question, then, is whether all three components of the *shevet* plant — seeds, leaves, and stems — are eaten regularly enough to fall under the tithing obligation.

### Wild vs Garden Plant

The Gemara again challenges the rejection: does not Rabbi Eliezer hold the same view? A *mishnah* is cited in which Rabbi Eliezer states that the *shevet* is tithed in its seeds, leaves, and stems. The Gemara resolves this: *hatam be-dignunita* — Rabbi Eliezer was speaking of the cultivated, garden variety of the plant, which is indeed eaten in all its parts. Nachum HaMadi, by contrast, was referring to the wild variety, which does not have that status. The two rulings therefore address different plants, and Nachum HaMadi's position on the wild *shevet* remains rejected.

## Rav Acha Dispute

### Rav Acha Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי לְאַבָּיֵי: גַּבְרָא רַבָּה אֲתָא מֵאַתְרִין, כֹּל מִילְּתָא דְּאָמַר, אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: "נִשְׁתַּקַּע הַדָּבָר וְלֹא נֶאֱמַר". אֲמַר [לֵיהּ]: אִיכָּא חֲדָא דְּעָבְדִינַן כְּוָותֵיהּ, דְּתַנְיָא: נַחוּם הַמָּדִי אוֹמֵר: שׁוֹאֵל אָדָם צְרָכָיו בְּ״שׁוֹמֵעַ תְּפִלָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the discussion between Naḥum the Mede and the other Sages, **Rav Aḥa bar Minyumi said to Abaye: A great man came from our place,** i.e., from Media, and in response to **every matter that he said,** the Sages **said to him** that it would be best if this **matter were lost and not stated.** Do they wish to erase all the *halakhot* taught by the Sages of Media? Abaye **said: There is one** instance in **which we act in accordance with his** opinion, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Naḥum the Mede says:** During the *Amida* prayer **a person may request his** personal **needs** that are not included in the standard formulation of the *Amida* prayer, **in** the blessing that ends: **Who listens to prayer.**

At this point, Rav Acha bar Manyumi addresses Abaye directly, invoking his shared regional identity with Nachum HaMadi: *gavra rabba ata mi-atrin* — a great man has come from our place — *kol milta de-amar amritun leh nishtaka hadavar velo ne'emar?* — and everything he says, you dismiss with this formula? Rav Acha bar Manyumi's objection is pointed: surely there is at least one opinion of Nachum HaMadi's that the halacha accepts.

### Shomea Tefilla Opinion

Abaye concedes: *ika chada de-avdinan ke-vateih* — there is one thing we do in accordance with his view. A *baraita* is cited: *Nachum HaMadi omer sho'el adam tzorachav be-shomei'a tefillah* — a person may insert requests for his personal needs within the blessing of *shomei'a tefillah*, the sixteenth blessing of the *Amidah*. However, Rav Acha bar Manyumi adds a qualification: *amar mar, minei de-ha'i de-talya be-ashlei ravrevei* — this particular ruling hangs on great ropes, meaning that more authoritative figures than Nachum HaMadi have made the same ruling, and it is from them — not from him — that the halacha is properly derived. The Gemara then opens a full discussion of the underlying dispute about when and how personal petitions may be integrated into structured prayer.

### Rejection Formula

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר [לֵיהּ] בַּר מִינַּהּ דְּהַהִיא, דְּתַלְיָא בְּאַשְׁלֵי רַבְרְבֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Aḥa bar Minyumi **said** to Abaye: **Apart from this** *halakha*, i.e., this does not serve as proof that a statement of Naḥum the Mede was ever accepted by the other Sages, **as** this *halakha* **is dependent on** the dispute between **great trees,** i.e., great authorities, who expressed their opinions with regard to the *halakha* before Naḥum the Mede.

## Prayer Order Dispute

### Rabbi Eliezer View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: שׁוֹאֵל אָדָם צְרָכָיו וְאַחַר כָּךְ יִתְפַּלֵּל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "תְּפִלָּה לְעָנִי כִי יַעֲטֹף וְלִפְנֵי ה׳ יִשְׁפֹּךְ שִׂיחוֹ וְגוֹ׳", אֵין שִׂיחָה אֶלָּא תְּפִלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיֵּצֵא יִצְחָק לָשׂוּחַ בַּשָּׂדֶה".
>
> **Translation:** Rav Aḥa bar Minyumi elaborates: **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says: A person should request his own needs** first **and afterward recite** the *Amida* **prayer, as it is stated: "A prayer of the afflicted, when he is faint and pours out *siḥo* before the Lord.** O Lord, hear my prayer" (Psalms 102:1–2). These verses indicate that one first requests help concerning his afflictions and pains, and only afterward pours forth his *siḥa*. And ***siḥa*** means **nothing other than prayer, as it is stated: "And Isaac went out to meditate [*lasuaḥ*] in the field"** (Genesis 24:63).

A *baraita* is cited: *Rabbi Eliezer omer sho'el adam tzorachav ve-achar kach yitpallel* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that a person should first articulate his personal needs and only afterward pray the *Shemoneh Esrei*. His proof is from Tehillim: *tefillah le-ani ki ya'atof ve-lifnei Hashem yishpoch sicho* — the prayer of the poor man when he is faint, he pours his *sicha* before God. The word *sicha* is elsewhere connected to *tefillah* through the verse describing Yitzchak going out *lasuach ba-sadeh* — his meditation in the field being, per tradition, an act of prayer. Rabbi Eliezer's reading is that the *ani* first articulates his distress and then turns to formal prayer.

### Rabbi Yehoshua View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: יִתְפַּלֵּל, וְאַחַר כָּךְ יִשְׁאַל צְרָכָיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "אֶשְׁפֹּךְ לְפָנָיו שִׂיחִי צָרָתִי לְפָנָיו אַגִּיד".
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** One **should pray** first **and afterward request his own needs, as it is stated: "I pour out *siḥi* before Him, I declare before Him my trouble"** (Psalms 142:3), which teaches that first one pours forth his *siḥa*, and only afterward speaks of his own troubles.

Rabbi Yehoshua, by contrast, holds the opposite: *yitpallel ve-achar kach yish'al tzorachav* — one should first pray the *Shemoneh Esrei* and only afterward request personal needs. His proof is from the continuation of that same psalm: *eshpoch lefanav sichi, tzarati lefanav aggid* — I pour out my *sicha* before Him, and then I declare my distress before Him. In Rabbi Yehoshua's reading, the *sicha* — the formal prayer — precedes the declaration of personal suffering.

### Verse Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב: "אֶשְׁפֹּךְ לְפָנָיו שִׂיחִי"! הָכִי קָאָמַר: "אֶשְׁפּוֹךְ לְפָנָיו שִׂיחִי" — בִּזְמַן שֶׁצָּרָתִי לְפָנָיו אַגִּיד. וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב: "תְּפִלָּה לְעָנִי כִי יַעֲטֹף"! הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֵימָתַי תְּפִלָּה לְעָנִי? בִּזְמַן שֶׁלִּפְנֵי ה׳ יִשְׁפּוֹךְ שִׂיחוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Rabbi Eliezer as well, isn't it written: "I pour out *siḥi* before Him,** I declare before Him my trouble"? The Gemara answers: According to Rabbi Eliezer, **this** is what the verse **is saying: I pour out my *siḥa* before Him when I** have already **declared before Him my trouble.** The Gemara asks with regard to Rabbi Yehoshua's opinion: **And** according to **Rabbi Yehoshua as well, isn't it written: "A prayer of the afflicted, when he is faint** and pours out *siḥo* before the Lord"? The Gemara answers: According to Rabbi Yehoshua, **this** is what the verse **is saying: When is there a prayer of the afflicted? When** he has already **poured out *siḥo* before the Lord,** and now requests his own needs.

The Gemara notes that each *amora* must account for the verse that anchors the other's position. Rabbi Eliezer explains *eshpoch lefanav sichi* as referring to the formal prayer that follows the prior articulation of distress: *eshpoch lefanav sichi bizman she-tzarati lefanav aggid* — I will pour out my prayer at the time when I have already declared my troubles before Him. Rabbi Yehoshua, for his part, reads *tefillah le-ani ki ya'atof* as describing the proper occasion for prayer: *eimatai tefillah le-ani, bizman she-lifnei Hashem yishpoch sicho* — when is the prayer of the poor man effective? When he has first directed his formal *sicha* before God, and only then gives voice to personal petition. Since each authority can plausibly read his opponent's proof-text in support of his own position, the Gemara asks: given that neither set of verses conclusively supports one view over the other, what exactly is the underlying principle on which they disagree?

### Rav Simlai Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְדָרֵישׁ רַבִּי שִׂמְלַאי [דְּדָרֵישׁ רַבִּי שִׂמְלַאי]: לְעוֹלָם יְסַדֵּר אָדָם שִׁבְחוֹ שֶׁל מָקוֹם וְאַחַר כָּךְ יִתְפַּלֵּל, מְנָלַן? מִמֹּשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ, דִּכְתִיב: "ה׳ אֱלֹהִים אַתָּה הַחִלּוֹתָ לְהַרְאוֹת אֶת עַבְדְּךָ וְגוֹ׳", וּכְתִיב בָּתְרֵיהּ: "אֶעְבְּרָה נָּא וְאֶרְאֶה אֶת הָאָרֶץ הַטּוֹבָה".
>
> **Translation:** They disagree **with regard to that which Rabbi Simlai taught. As Rabbi Simlai taught: A person should always set forth praise of God and** only **then pray** for his own needs. **From where do we** derive this? We derive it **from Moses our teacher, as it is written: "O Lord God, You have begun to show Your servant** Your greatness, and Your strong hand; for what god is there in heaven or on earth, that can do according to Your works, and according to Your mighty acts?" (Deuteronomy 3:24). Here Moses praises God, **and it is written afterward,** in the following verse, that then Moses requested from God: **"Let me go over, I pray You, and see the good land"** (Deuteronomy 3:25).

The answer comes from a teaching of Rav Simlai: *l'olam yesader adam shivcho shel makom ve-achar kach yitpallel* — a person should always arrange the praise of the Omnipresent first, and only then pray. This principle is already embedded in the structure of the *Amidah* itself: the opening three blessings are entirely doxological — God of our fathers, God the Mighty, God the Holy — and only afterward does the sequence of personal petitions begin, with *da'at*, *selichah*, *refuah*, and so forth. The question, then, is from where this principle is derived.

### Moshe Example

Rav Simlai answers: from Moshe Rabbeinu. The verse at the opening of Moshe's great petition in Devarim reads: *Hashem Elokim, Ata hachilota le-harot et avdecha* — You have begun to show Your servant Your greatness — followed by praise of God's unparalleled might and dominion. Only after this extended doxology does Moshe make his personal request: *evra na ve-ereh et ha-aretz ha-tova* — please let me cross over and see the good land. The sequence in Moshe's prayer — praise first, petition second — establishes the normative order.

### Learning from Moshe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ סָבַר: יָלְפִינַן מִמֹּשֶׁה, וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר: לָא יָלְפִינַן מִמֹּשֶׁה, שָׁאנֵי מֹשֶׁה דְּרַב גּוּבְרֵיהּ. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא כְּדִבְרֵי זֶה וְלֹא כְּדִבְרֵי זֶה, אֶלָּא שׁוֹאֵל אָדָם צְרָכָיו בְּ״שׁוֹמֵעַ תְּפִלָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua holds** that **we derive from** the case of **Moses** that one should first praise God in prayer and only afterward issue personal requests. **And Rabbi Eliezer holds** that **we do not derive from Moses** how to act, since **Moses is different, as his might is great,** i.e., he knew how to pray to God in this order. **And the Rabbis say:** The *halakha* **is not in accordance with the statement of this** Sage, who says that one should issue personal requests before praying, **nor** is it **in accordance with the statement of that** Sage, who says that personal requests should follow prayer. **Rather, a person requests his own needs in** the blessing ending: **Who listens to prayer.** Therefore, when Naḥum the Mede stated that this is the *halakha*, he was merely concurring with the opinion of the Rabbis.

The Gemara identifies this as the point of dispute between Rabbi Yehoshua and Rabbi Eliezer: Rabbi Yehoshua holds that we derive the proper order of prayer from Moshe's example; Rabbi Eliezer holds that we do not.

### Moshe Uniqueness

Rabbi Eliezer's objection is that Moshe is *shani* — exceptional. Rashi explains: Moshe's deeds were great and his wisdom extraordinary, and it was therefore fitting and becoming for him to preface his petition with lavish praise of the King of kings. But an ordinary person who does so risks the appearance of *yohara* — presumption or arrogance — as if he genuinely believes himself capable of adequately praising God before asking for something. Rabbi Eliezer's psychological realism is striking: a person in need cannot authentically arrange praises first; like a child who wants candy, the need comes first and the formalities come after, whether one likes it or not.

## Personal Requests Rules

### Shomea Tefilla Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, הֲלָכָה: שׁוֹאֵל אָדָם צְרָכָיו בְּ״שׁוֹמֵעַ תְּפִלָּה״. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר שִׁילַת מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרוּ שׁוֹאֵל אָדָם צְרָכָיו בְּשׁוֹמֵעַ תְּפִלָּה״, אֲבָל אִם בָּא לוֹמַר בְּסוֹף כׇּל בְּרָכָה וּבְרָכָה מֵעֵין כׇּל בְּרָכָה וּבְרָכָה — אוֹמֵר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the halakhic ruling, **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The ***halakha*** is that **a person requests his own needs** during the *Amida* prayer **in** the blessing ending: **Who listens to prayer. Rav Yehuda, son of Rav Shmuel bar Sheilat, says in the name of Rav: Although** the Sages **said** that **a person requests his own needs in** the blessing ending: **Who listens to prayer,** that is not the only option. **Rather, if he wishes to recite at the conclusion of each and every blessing** personal requests that **reflect the nature of each and every blessing, he may recite** them.

The *chachamim* stake out a middle position: *sho'el adam tzorachav be-shomei'a tefillah* — neither before the *Amidah* nor after it, but within the *Amidah* itself, in the blessing of *shomei'a tefillah*, which is the blessing designated as the receptacle for personal petition.

### Rav Yehuda Halacha

Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel rules definitively: *halacha sho'el adam tzorachav be-shomei'a tefillah* — the accepted halacha is that a person inserts his personal needs within the blessing of *shomei'a tefillah*.

### End of Blessing Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר רַב: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרוּ שׁוֹאֵל אָדָם צְרָכָיו בְּ״שׁוֹמֵעַ תְּפִלָּה״, אִם יֵשׁ לוֹ חוֹלֶה בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ — אוֹמֵר בְּבִרְכַּת חוֹלִים, וְאִם צָרִיךְ לְפַרְנָסָה — אוֹמֵר בְּבִרְכַּת הַשָּׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi says** that **Rav says: Although** the Sages **said** that **a person requests his own needs in** the blessing ending: **Who listens to prayer, if he has a sick person in his house he recites** a special prayer for him **during the blessing of the sick. And if he is in need of sustenance, he recites** a request **during the blessing of the years.**

Rav Yehuda bar Rav Shmuel bar Shilat citing Rav adds a further ruling: although the general principle locates personal petitions in *shomei'a tefillah*, if a person wishes to add a petition that is thematically relevant to a particular blessing, he may do so at the conclusion of that blessing. The *siddur* preserves traces of this principle, and many are familiar with the practice of inserting personal additions within the blessing that corresponds to the nature of the request.

### Relevant Blessing Rule

Rav Chiya bar Ashi citing Rav specifies this concretely: if one has a sick person in his household, he should add that prayer within *birkat cholim* — the blessing for healing; if one needs livelihood, he should insert that petition within *birkat hashanim* — the blessing for the seasons and sustenance.

### After Amidah Option

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרוּ: שׁוֹאֵל אָדָם צְרָכָיו בְּ״שׁוֹמֵעַ תְּפִלָּה״, אֲבָל אִם בָּא לוֹמַר אַחַר תְּפִלָּתוֹ, אֲפִילּוּ כְּסֵדֶר יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים — אוֹמֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: Although** the Sages **said** that **a person requests his own needs in** the blessing ending: **Who listens to prayer; but if one wishes to recite** prayers and supplications **after** finishing **his** *Amida* **prayer, even** if his personal requests **are as** long as **the order** of the confession of **Yom Kippur, he may recite** them.

Rav Yehoshua ben Levi extends the latitude further: although the established rule locates personal requests within *shomei'a tefillah*, if a person wishes to elaborate his personal prayers at greater length, he may do so after completing the *Amidah* — and even if those prayers are as lengthy as the order of *Yom Kippur* confessions, he may recite them in full. This opinion reflects a deeply important strand of rabbinic thought: alongside the fixed liturgical structure, the tradition consistently recognized and valued a personal, individualized conversation with God. The tension between the written structure and this more intimate *hitbodedut* is real, and the gradual dominance of the fixed text at the expense of personal prayer may represent a genuine loss in the practice of *tefillah*.

## Non-Jewish Holidays

### Holiday Names List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְאֵלּוּ אֵידֵיהֶן שֶׁל גּוֹיִם — קָלֶנְדָּא, וּסְטַרְנוּרָא, וְקַרְטֵיסִים, וְיוֹם גְּנוּסְיָא שֶׁל מַלְכֵיהֶם, וְיוֹם הַלֵּידָה, וְיוֹם הַמִּיתָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל מִיתָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ שְׂרֵיפָה — יֵשׁ בָּהּ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְשֶׁאֵין בָּהּ שְׂרֵיפָה — אֵין בָּהּ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. אֲבָל יוֹם תִּגְלַחַת זְקָנוֹ וּבְלוֹרִיתוֹ, וְיוֹם שֶׁעָלָה בּוֹ מִן הַיָּם, וְיוֹם שֶׁיָּצָא מִבֵּית הָאֲסוּרִין, וְגוֹי שֶׁעָשָׂה מִשְׁתֶּה לִבְנוֹ — אֵינוֹ אָסוּר אֶלָּא אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם וְאוֹתוֹ הָאִישׁ בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And these are the festivals of gentiles: Kalenda, Saturnalia, and Kratesis, and the day of the festival of their kings, and the birthday** of the king, **and** the anniversary of **the day of the death** of the king. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: Every death that includes** public **burning** is a festival that **includes idol worship, and** any death **that does not include** public **burning** is **not** a festival that **includes idol worship. But** in the case of **the day of shaving his,** i.e., a gentile's, **beard and his locks, and the day of** his **ascent from the sea, and the day that he left prison, and** also in the case of **a gentile who prepared** a wedding **feast for his son** and celebrates on that day, engaging in business **is prohibited only** on **that day and** with **that man.**

The Gemara returns to the primary subject of the *masechta* with a Mishnah enumerating the holidays of *ovdei kochavim* regarding which the prohibition on commercial dealings applies. The holidays listed are: *Kalenda*, *Saturnalia*, *Cartisim*, *yom genosya shel malkeichem* — the festival of the kings — and *yom haleida veyom hamita* — the day of a king's birth and the day of his death. This is the view of Rabbi Meir.

It is worth noting that the word *Kalenda* and the English word "calendar" share the same Latin root, *Calendae*, the first day of the Roman month. *Saturnalia* takes its name from the planet Saturn. *Cartisim* will be explained by the Gemara shortly.

### Death Celebration Rule

The *chachamim* qualify the inclusion of the day of a king's death: *kol mita sheyesh bah sereifa yesh bah avodat kochavim, veshein bah sereifa ein bah avodat kochavim* — any death that is accompanied by the burning of idolatrous items involves idol worship and is therefore prohibited; a death without such burning does not involve idol worship and is permitted.

### Private Celebration Rule

The Mishnah then distinguishes between public, universal holidays and private, personal celebrations. Private occasions include: the day a person shaves his beard and hair — in that era, a shaved beard was itself a celebratory event rather than a routine matter; the day a person returns from a sea voyage; the day a person is released from prison; and when an idolater makes a feast for his son — understood to mean a wedding or similar celebration. For all of these private occasions, only that specific day and only that specific individual are subject to the prohibition on commercial dealings. His servants, associates, or others connected to him are not included.

## Winter Holidays

### Kalenda & Saturnalia

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב חָנָן בַּר רָבָא: קָלֶנְדָּא — שְׁמוֹנָה יָמִים אַחַר תְּקוּפָה, סְטַרְנוּרָא — שְׁמוֹנָה יָמִים לִפְנֵי תְּקוּפָה, וְסִימָנָךְ — ״אָחוֹר וָקֶדֶם צַרְתָּנִי וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Ḥanan bar Rava says:** When are these festivals celebrated? **Kalenda** is celebrated during the **eight days after** the winter **solstice,** and **Saturnalia** is celebrated during the **eight days before** the winter **solstice. And your mnemonic** to remember which festival is that the one that occurs after the solstice is mentioned first in the mishna, and the festival that takes place before the solstice is mentioned after, as in the verse: **"You have hemmed me in behind and before,** and laid Your Hand upon me" (Psalms 139:5), where the word "before" appears after the term "behind."

Rav Chanan bar Rava explains the calendrical relationship between the two major Roman festivals and the winter solstice. *Kalenda* falls eight days after *tekufat Tevet* — the winter solstice — while *Saturnalia* falls eight days before it. A mnemonic from Tehillim is provided: *achor vakedem tzartani* — "behind and before You have formed me" — evoking the symmetrical positioning of the two festivals around the solstice.

### Adam Midrash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לְפִי שֶׁרָאָה אָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן יוֹם שֶׁמִּתְמַעֵט וְהוֹלֵךְ, אָמַר: אוֹי לִי! שֶׁמָּא בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁסָּרַחְתִּי עוֹלָם חָשׁוּךְ בַּעֲדִי וְחוֹזֵר לְתוֹהוּ וָבוֹהוּ, וְזוֹ הִיא מִיתָה שֶׁנִּקְנְסָה עָלַי מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם. עָמַד וְיָשַׁב שְׁמוֹנָה יָמִים בְּתַעֲנִית [וּבִתְפִלָּה].
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the dates of these festivals, **the Sages taught: When Adam the first** man **saw** that **the day was progressively diminishing,** as the days become shorter from the autumnal equinox until the winter solstice, he did not yet know that this is a normal phenomenon, and therefore he **said: Woe** is **me; perhaps because I sinned the world is becoming dark around me and** will ultimately **return to** the primordial state of **chaos and disorder. And this is the death that was sentenced upon me from Heaven,** as it is written: "And to dust shall you return" (Genesis 3:19). **He arose and spent eight days in fasting and in prayer.**

The Gemara provides the theological and historical context for these eight-day celebrations through a remarkable *midrash*. Adam HaRishon, observing the days growing shorter and shorter as winter progressed, was seized with terror: he concluded that because of his sin, the world was being darkened and reverting to *tohu va-vohu* — primordial chaos — and that this was the death sentence imposed upon him from heaven. He sat for eight days in fasting — according to some opinions, in prayer — mourning the apparent unraveling of creation. Then, at *tekufat Tevet*, when he observed the days beginning to lengthen, he understood: *minhago shel olam hu* — this is simply the way of the world, the natural cycle. In response to this revelation, he established eight days of celebration. The following year, he established the same eight days both before and after the solstice — and those celebrations were *leshem shamayim*, for the sake of heaven. The non-Jewish nations subsequently adopted the same festivals, but redirected them *leshem avodat kochavim* — as occasions of idol worship.

### Adam Creation Date

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: "בְּתִשְׁרִי נִבְרָא הָעוֹלָם״, יוֹמֵי זוּטֵי חֲזָא, יוֹמֵי אֲרִיכֵי אַכַּתִּי לָא חֲזָא. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: "בְּנִיסָן נִבְרָא הָעוֹלָם״, הָא חֲזָא לֵיהּ יוֹמֵי זוּטֵי וְיוֹמֵי אֲרִיכֵי! (דְּהָוֵי) זוּטֵי כּוּלֵּי הַאי לָא חֲזָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Granted, according to the one who says** that **the world was created in** the month of **Tishrei,** one can understand why Adam believed that the days were becoming shorter as part of his punishment, as **he saw the short days** of the winter and **had not yet seen the long days** of summer. **But according to the one who says** that **the world was created in** the month of **Nisan, he had** already **seen** the difference between **the short days and the long days,** as the days in the month of Nisan become progressively longer with the passage of time. The Gemara answers: Although Adam had experienced short days, **he had not seen days that were this short,** as in the days before the winter solstice.

The Gemara notes a tension with respect to when Adam was created. According to the opinion that the world was created in Tishrei, Adam would have experienced days growing progressively shorter from the outset, but would not yet have witnessed the lengthening of days. According to the opinion that the world was created in Nisan, Adam would have experienced both the shortening and the lengthening of days over the course of his first year — giving him the full experiential arc that underlies the *midrash*. However, the extreme darkness of a winter solstice — *kulei hai lo chaza* — the depth of winter darkness he had not yet seen. The Gemara leaves both opinions in place, noting that the entire discussion presupposes a northern hemisphere setting.

## Adam First Sunset

### Adam's Fear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יוֹם שֶׁנִּבְרָא בּוֹ אָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן, כֵּיוָן שֶׁשָּׁקְעָה עָלָיו חַמָּה, אָמַר: אוֹי לִי, שֶׁבִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁסָּרַחְתִּי עוֹלָם חָשׁוּךְ בַּעֲדִי, וְיַחְזוֹר עוֹלָם לְתוֹהוּ וָבוֹהוּ, וְזוֹ הִיא מִיתָה שֶׁנִּקְנְסָה עָלַי מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם. הָיָה יוֹשֵׁב בְּתַעֲנִית וּבוֹכֶה כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה, וְחַוָּה בּוֹכָה כְּנֶגְדּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** On **the day that Adam the first** man **was created, when the sun set upon him he said: Woe** is **me, as because I sinned, the world is becoming dark around me, and the world will return to** the primordial state of **chaos and disorder. And this is the death that was sentenced upon me from Heaven. He spent all night fasting and crying, and Eve was crying opposite him.**

A parallel *baraita* offers a related but distinct account. On the very first day of Adam's creation, when the sun set for the first time, Adam said: woe to me — because of my sin the world is going dark and returning to *tohu va-vohu*, and this is the death that was decreed upon me from heaven. He sat fasting and weeping throughout the night, and Chavah wept alongside him. This account raises a host of questions that the Gemara does not pursue at length: what precisely is "the first day" they were created — if it was a Friday, was Adam sitting in *ta'anit* on *Shabbat*? When exactly did the sin occur in relation to this experience? What had happened to their life in the Garden? The *midrash* is best understood in its metaphorical register: the human experience of first darkness as guilt, first terror as divine judgment, and first dawn as grace and restored order.

### Adam's Sacrifice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן שֶׁעָלָה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר, אָמַר: מִנְהָגוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם הוּא. עָמַד וְהִקְרִיב שׁוֹר שֶׁקַּרְנָיו קוֹדְמִין לְפַרְסוֹתָיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְתִיטַב לַה׳ מִשּׁוֹר פָּר מַקְרִן מַפְרִיס".
>
> **Translation:** **Once dawn broke, he said:** Evidently, the sun sets and night arrives, and **this is the order of the world. He arose and sacrificed a bull whose horns preceded its hoofs** in the order that they were created, **as it is stated: "And it shall please the Lord better than a bullock that has horns and hoofs"** (Psalms 69:32). This verse is referring to the one particular bull whose horns preceded its hoofs.

When dawn broke and *amud ha-shachar* arose, Adam declared: *minhago shel olam hu* — this is the way of the world. He rose and offered a sacrifice — an ox whose horns preceded its hooves, as attested by the verse from Tehillim: *ve-titav la-Shem mi-shor par makrin mafris* — "and it pleased God more than an ox, a bull with horns and hooves." The unusual formulation *makrin mafris* — emphasizing both the horns and the hooves — anchors the description of this primordial sacrifice. One interpretive note: the animals created during the six days of creation were brought into existence already whole and fully formed, not born gradually limb by limb, so the formulation that its horns "preceded" its hooves reflects the sequence of its miraculous creation.

### Unicorn Ox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שׁוֹר שֶׁהִקְרִיב אָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן, קֶרֶן אַחַת הָיְתָה לוֹ בְּמִצְחוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְתִיטַב לַה׳ מִשּׁוֹר פָּר מַקְרִן מַפְרִיס". "מַקְרִין" תַּרְתֵּי מַשְׁמַע! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: "מַקְרָן" כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The **bull that Adam the first** man **sacrificed had one horn in its forehead, as it is stated: "And it shall please the Lord better than a bullock that has horns [*makrin*] and hooves."** The Gemara raises a difficulty: Isn't ***makrin*** plural, which **indicates two** horns? **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: *Mikkeren* is written,** i.e., the letter *yod* is missing from the word, indicating that there was only one horn.

Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel adds a striking tradition: the ox that Adam HaRishon sacrificed had a single horn in its forehead — making it, in effect, a unicorn. The verse *makrin mafris* is cited. The Gemara raises an objection: *makrin* appears to be in the plural, implying two horns. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves this: *mikeren ktiv* — the word is written in the Torah text as *mikeren*, in the *ktiv chaser* form, without the *yud*, indicating the singular. The tradition of the single-horned ox thus has a textual anchor in the defective spelling.

## Rome's Kalenda

### Dependent Towns Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה: רוֹמִי שֶׁעָשְׂתָה קָלֶנְדָּא, וְכׇל הָעֲיָירוֹת הַסְּמוּכוֹת לָהּ מִשְׁתַּעְבְּדוֹת לָהּ, אוֹתָן עֲיָירוֹת אֲסוּרוֹת אוֹ מוּתָּרוֹת? רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמַר: קָלֶנְדָּא אֲסוּרָה לַכֹּל הִיא, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֵין אֲסוּרָה אֶלָּא לְעוֹבְדֶיהָ בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Mattana says:** Since **Rome established** the festival of **Kalenda** on a specific date, **and all of the nearby towns are ruled by** Rome, i.e., they pay their tax to Rome and provide its needs but do not themselves celebrate the festival, is it **prohibited or permitted** to engage in business transactions with the gentile residents of **those towns? Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: It is prohibited** to engage in business during the time of the **Kalenda with everyone. Rabbi Yoḥanan says: It is prohibited** to engage in business **only with its worshippers,** whereas it is permitted to engage in business transactions with gentiles who do not celebrate the festival.

Rav Matna raises a practical question: if Rome celebrates *Kalenda* and all the surrounding towns that are subjugated to Rome are effectively subject to the same celebration — are those towns forbidden for purposes of commercial dealings, or not? The question is whether the prohibition follows the festival as a Roman imperial institution to which these towns are politically bound, or whether it follows only those individuals who actively worship and celebrate the holiday.

### Kalenda Scope

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi rules that *Kalenda asura lechol hi* — the prohibition of *Kalenda* applies universally, to all who are in any way connected to Rome's celebration. Rabbi Yochanan rules more narrowly: *ein asura ela le'ovdeha bilvad* — it is forbidden only with respect to those who actually worship and celebrate it.

### Halacha Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרוּ, רוֹמִי עָשְׂתָה קָלֶנְדָּא, וְכׇל עֲיָירוֹת הַסְּמוּכוֹת לָהּ מִשְׁתַּעְבְּדוֹת לָהּ, הִיא עַצְמָהּ אֵינָהּ אֲסוּרָה אֶלָּא לְעוֹבְדֶיהָ בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Sage **taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan: Although they said** that **Rome** has **established** the festival of **Kalenda and all of the nearby towns are ruled by** Rome, **it is prohibited** to engage in business **only with its worshippers.**

A *tanna* is cited in support of Rabbi Yochanan: even though Rome has established *Kalenda* and all surrounding subject towns are subjugated to it, the prohibition applies only to the actual worshippers of the holiday. The halacha follows Rabbi Yochanan's more restricted position. The practical significance of this ruling cannot be overstated. The stricter view — that any town in any way associated with Rome is subject to the prohibition — would make ordinary commerce nearly impossible in the Roman world. The underlying tension in the sugya is precisely this: how does a Jewish community maintain economic viability while adhering to the prohibitions of *avodah zarah*? Figures like Nachum HaMadi, living in the diaspora, presumably confronted this tension in its most acute form, which may explain the more permissive character of his rulings throughout.

## Diaspora Jews

### Unwitting Idolatry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁבְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ עוֹבְדֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בְּטָהֳרָה הֵן, כֵּיצַד? גּוֹי שֶׁעָשָׂה מִשְׁתֶּה לִבְנוֹ וְזִימֵּן כׇּל הַיְּהוּדִים שֶׁבְּעִירוֹ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאוֹכְלִין מִשֶּׁלָּהֶן וְשׁוֹתִין מִשֶּׁלָּהֶן, וְשַׁמָּשׁ שֶׁלָּהֶם עוֹמֵד לִפְנֵיהֶם, מַעֲלֶה עֲלֵיהֶם הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ אָכְלוּ מִזִּבְחֵי מֵתִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְקָרָא לְךָ וְאָכַלְתָּ מִזִּבְחוֹ".
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yishmael says: Jews who are outside of Eretz** Yisrael **are** considered to **engage in idol worship in purity,** i.e., unwittingly. **How** does this occur? In the case of **a gentile who prepared a feast for** the marriage of **his son, and invited all of the Jews in his town, even though they eat of their own** kosher food **and drink of their own** kosher beverages, **and their own attendant stands before them, the verse ascribes** guilt **to them as though they ate of** the **offerings to the dead,** i.e., idols, **as it is stated:** "And sacrifice to their gods, **and they call you, and you eat of their sacrifice"** (Exodus 34:15). Since Jews participate in a feast in which the gentile sacrifices offerings to his idol, it is as though they partook of the offering themselves.

Rabbi Yishmael teaches: *Yisrael shebechutz la'aretz ovdei avodat kochavim betahara hen* — Jews who live outside the Land of Israel are, in a sense, worshippers of idol worship *betahara*, which here means *beta'ut* — inadvertently, without awareness or intent. The formulation is striking and deliberately provocative: simply by living in the diaspora, Jews are drawn into situations that constitute participation in idolatry, even without any conscious act of worship.

### Wedding Feast Case

The case that illustrates this principle: a non-Jew makes a wedding feast for his son and invites all the Jews of his city. Even if the Jews bring their own food, drink their own wine, and have their own server standing before them — *ma'aleh alav ha-katuv ke'ilu achlu zivchei meitim* — the text regards them as if they had eaten from sacrifices to the dead, i.e., idolatrous offerings. This is a remarkably severe ruling: their food is kosher, their wine is their own, they have not eaten a single bite of the non-Jew's food — and yet the act of participating in the celebration itself is treated as a form of complicity in idol worship.

### Verse Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: עַד דְּאָכֵיל! אָמַר רָבָא: אִם כֵּן, נֵימָא קְרָא "וְאָכַלְתָּ מִזִּבְחוֹ", מַאי "וְקָרָא לְךָ"? מִשְּׁעַת קְרִיאָה! הִלְכָּךְ, כֹּל תְּלָתִין יוֹמִין, בֵּין אָמַר לֵיהּ מֵחֲמַת הִלּוּלָא וּבֵין לָא אָמַר לֵיהּ מֵחֲמַת הִלּוּלָא — אָסוּר, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ, אִי אָמַר לֵיהּ מֵחֲמַת הִלּוּלָא — אָסוּר, וְאִי לָא אָמַר לֵיהּ מֵחֲמַת הִלּוּלָא — שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **say** that the verse is criticizing the Jews only **once they eat** from the sacrifice? **Rava said: If** that **is** what is meant, **let the verse say** only: **And you eat of their sacrifice. What** is meant by the additional phrase: **"And they call you"?** This indicates that the prohibition occurs **from the time of the call. Therefore,** during **all** the **thirty days** that follow the wedding celebration, if the gentile invites a Jew to a feast, **whether he said** to the Jew that the feast is **due to the wedding celebration or whether he did not say to him** that the feast is **due to the wedding celebration,** it is **prohibited** to attend, as it is assumed the festivity is part of the wedding celebration. **From this** point **forward, if he said to him** that the feast is **due to the wedding celebration,** it is **prohibited** to participate, **but if he did not say to him** that the feast is **due to the wedding celebration,** it is **permitted** to do so.

The biblical source is a verse from Shemot: *vekara lecha ve'achalta mizivcho* — "and he will call to you and you will eat from his sacrifice." One might have expected the operative prohibition to be formulated as *ve'achalta mizivcho* alone — you ate from his sacrifice — which would limit the violation to actual consumption of idolatrous food. But the verse begins with *vekara lecha* — and he called to you, and he invited you. From this the Gemara derives that the moment of invitation is itself the moment of complicity. *Mishe'at kriya* — from the time of the summons, the Jewish guest is already enmeshed in the idolatrous celebration, regardless of what he eats. The Gemara had entertained the possibility of limiting the prohibition to actual eating — *ad de'achil* — but the inclusion of *vekara lecha* forecloses that reading.

## Thirty-Day Rule

### Thirty Days

The practical halacha that follows from this principle: within thirty days of a celebration — whether the celebrant explicitly mentions that the meal is connected to that celebration or not — meals provided by the idolater are forbidden.

### Beyond Thirty

Beyond thirty days, the prohibition is more nuanced: if the idolater explicitly states that the meal is *machamat hilula* — on account of the wedding celebration — it remains forbidden. If he does not mention the connection, it is permitted.

### Twelve Months

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי אָמַר לֵיהּ מֵחֲמַת הִלּוּלָא, עַד אֵימַת? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: עַד תְּרֵיסַר יַרְחֵי שַׁתָּא. וּמֵעִיקָּרָא מֵאֵימַת אָסוּר? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: מִכִּי רָמוּ שְׂעָרֵי בַּאֲסִינְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** in a case **where he said to him** that the feast is **due to the wedding celebration, until when** is the feast assumed to be connected to idol worship? **Rav Pappa said: Until twelve months of the year** have passed since the wedding. The Gemara asks: **And initially,** before the wedding, **from when is it prohibited? Rav Pappa said in the name of Rava: From** the time **when they cast barley into the mortars [*ba'asintei*]** to prepare beer for the wedding.

Rav Papa rules, citing Rava, that if the celebration is explicitly invoked, the prohibition can extend up to twelve full months after the wedding. As for the period before the wedding: Rav Papa in the name of Rava rules that the prohibition begins from the time they cast barley into the vessels — the moment preparations for the celebration commence, whether that means the brewing of beer or the preparation of garlands and other festive items.

### Rav Yitzchak Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְבָתַר תְּרֵיסַר יַרְחֵי שַׁתָּא שְׁרֵי? וְהָא רַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי הָהוּא גּוֹי לְבָתַר תְּרֵיסַר יַרְחֵי שַׁתָּא, וְשַׁמְעֵיהּ דְּאוֹדִי, וּפֵירַשׁ וְלָא אֲכַל! שָׁאנֵי רַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא, דְּאָדָם חָשׁוּב הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And after the twelve months of the year** have passed since the wedding, is it always **permitted** to participate in a feast? **But Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Mesharshiyya, happened** to come **to the house of a certain gentile after twelve months of the year** had passed since his son's wedding, **and he heard** the gentile **giving thanks** to his idol for the marriage of his son, **and he withdrew** from the feast **and did not eat** there. The Gemara answers: **Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Mesharshiyya, is different, as he is an important person** and therefore his presence caused the gentile to rejoice.

A case is then cited: Rav Yitzchak bar Rav Mesharsheya happened to be at the home of a certain non-Jew more than twelve months after a celebration had taken place. While there, he heard the host giving thanks to his idol in connection with his son's wedding. Rav Yitzchak withdrew from the feast and did not eat.

### Important Person

The Gemara's response is telling: *shani Rav Yitzchak brei d'Rav Mesharsheya de-adam chashuv hu* — Rav Yitzchak bar Rav Mesharsheya is different, because he was an *adam chashuv*, a person of distinction. The presence of an important person lends weight and significance to an event. What would be a purely private meal if attended by an ordinary Jew becomes, when attended by a prominent rabbinic figure, a form of public endorsement. His withdrawal was therefore appropriate not because the halacha would require it of everyone — twelve months had passed and no explicit mention had been required — but because of the specific dynamics created by his prominence.

## Cartisim & Rome

### Cartisim Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּקְרָטֵסִים וְכוּ׳. מַאי קְרָטֵסִים? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: יוֹם שֶׁתָּפְסָה בּוֹ רוֹמִי מַלְכוּת. וְהָתַנְיָא: קְרָטֵסִים וְיוֹם שֶׁתָּפְסָה בּוֹ רוֹמִי מַלְכוּת! אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: שְׁתֵּי תְּפִיסוֹת תָּפְסָה רוֹמִי, אַחַת בִּימֵי קְלֵפַּטְרָא מַלְכְּתָא, וְאַחַת שֶׁתָּפְסָה בִּימֵי יְוָנִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And Kratesis,** and the day of the festival of their kings. The Gemara asks: **What** is the festival of **Kratesis? Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** It commemorates **the day when Rome seized** control of **an empire.** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: Two festivals are **Kratesis and the day when Rome seized** control of **an empire?** This indicates that Kratesis and the day when Rome seized control of an empire are two separate festivals. **Rav Yosef said:** On **two** separate occasions **Rome seized** control of **an empire. One** occurred **in the days of Queen Cleopatra,** when they conquered Egypt, **and one** happened much earlier, **when** Rome **seized** control **in the days of the Greeks.**

Returning to the Mishnah's list of holidays, the Gemara asks: *mai Cartisim?* — what is *Cartisim*? Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel answers: it is the day that Rome seized imperial power — *yom shetafsa bo Romi malchut*.

### Two Conquests

A *baraita* is cited that seems to list *Cartisim* and "the day Rome became the ruling empire" as two separate entries, suggesting they are not identical. Rav Yosef resolves this: Rome had two distinct episodes of seizing power — one in the era of Cleopatra, queen of Egypt, representing Rome's conquest of the south, and one in the wars against the Greeks.

### Thirty-Two Battles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּכִי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר: תְּלָתִין וּתְרֵים קְרָבֵי עֲבַדוּ רוֹמָאֵי בַּהֲדֵי יַוְנָאֵי, וְלָא יְכַלוּ לְהוּ, עַד דְּשַׁתְּפִינְהוּ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ, וְהָכִי אַתְנוֹ בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ: אִי מִינַּן מַלְכֵי — מִנַּיְיכוּ הִפַּרְכֵי, אִי מִנַּיְיכוּ מַלְכֵי — מִינַּן הִפַּרְכֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **As when Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael **he said: The Romans waged thirty-two battles with the Greeks but were unable to** defeat **them, until they formed a partnership with the Jewish people** and finally vanquished the Greeks. **And this is the condition that they stipulated with** the Jewish people: **If the kings** come **from among us, the governors [*hiparkhei*]** will come **from among you;** and **if the kings** come **from among you, the governors** will come **from among us.**

Rav Dimi elaborates: the Romans fought thirty-two battles against the Greeks and were unable to prevail — *velo yaklu lehu* — until they struck a partnership with the Jewish people. It is the circumstances of this partnership that occupy the next several paragraphs.

### Negotiation Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁלַחוּ לְהוּ רוֹמָאֵי לְיַוְנָאֵי: עַד הָאִידָּנָא עָבֵידְנָא בִּקְרָבָא, הַשְׁתָּא נַעֲבֵיד בְּדִינָא. מַרְגָּלִית וְאֶבֶן טוֹבָה, אֵיזוֹ מֵהֶן יֵעָשֶׂה בָּסִיס לַחֲבֵירוֹ? שְׁלַחוּ לְהוּ: מַרְגָּלִית לְאֶבֶן טוֹבָה. אֶבֶן טוֹבָה וְאִינָךְ, אֵיזוֹ מֵהֶן יֵעָשֶׂה בָּסִיס לַחֲבֵירוֹ? אֶבֶן טוֹבָה לְאִינָךְ. אִינָךְ וְסֵפֶר תּוֹרָה, אֵיזוֹ מֵהֶן יֵעָשֶׂה בָּסִיס לַחֲבֵירוֹ? אִינָךְ לְסֵפֶר תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Romans sent** the following message **to the Greeks: Until now, we** attempted to resolve our conflict **through** fighting **battles; now, let us** settle the matter **by** means of **judgment.** In the case of **a pearl and a precious stone, which** one **of them should serve as a base for the other?** The Greeks **sent them** in response: The **pearl** should serve as the base **for** the **precious stone,** which has a greater value. The Romans further inquired: If there was **a precious stone and an onyx [*innakh*],** a particularly valuable precious stone, **which** one **of them should serve as a base for the other?** The Greeks answered: The **precious stone** should serve as the base **for** the **onyx.** Once again, the Romans asked: In the case of **an onyx and a Torah scroll, which** one **of them should be serve as a base for the other?** The Greeks responded: The **onyx** should serve as the base **for the Torah scroll.**

The Gemara presents this partnership through a remarkable parable of negotiation. The Romans and Greeks resolve their conflict through a test of judgment rather than force. Rome sends a pearl and a precious stone and poses the question: which should serve as the base for the other? The Greeks reply that the precious stone should serve as the base for the pearl — meaning the precious stone is the lesser, and the pearl is the greater. Then Rome poses a second question: which of two precious stones — one ordinary, one of the variety called *inach* (perhaps onyx) — should serve as the base? The Greeks reply that the ordinary precious stone is the base for the *inach*. Then Rome poses the final and decisive question: which should serve as the base, the *inach* or a *Sefer Torah*? The *inach* serves as the base for the *Sefer Torah*.

### Jacob-Esau Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחוּ לְהוּ: [אִם כֵּן] אֲנַן סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה גַּבַּן וְיִשְׂרָאֵל בַּהֲדַן. כְּפוֹ לְהוּ עֶשְׂרִין וְשֵׁית שְׁנִין, קָמוּ לְהוּ בְּהֵימָנוּתַיְיהוּ בַּהֲדֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ אִישְׁתַּעְבַּדוּ בְּהוּ. מֵעִיקָּרָא מַאי דְּרוּשׁ, וּלְבַסּוֹף מַאי דְּרוּשׁ? מֵעִיקָּרָא דְּרוּשׁ "נִסְעָה וְנֵלֵכָה וְאֵלְכָה לְנֶגְדֶּךָ״, וּלְבַסּוֹף דְּרוּשׁ "יַעֲבׇר נָא אֲדֹנִי לִפְנֵי עַבְדּוֹ".
>
> **Translation:** The Romans **sent** this response **to them: If** that is **so,** then you should submit to us, as **we have** the **Torah scroll with us, and the Jewish people** are **with us.** The Romans are akin to the precious stone, and they are allied with the Jewish people who are akin to the onyx, and they possess the Torah scroll. The Romans therefore **forced** the Greeks to surrender and took over their world dominance. For **twenty-six years** the Romans **stood faithfully with the Jewish people; from that** point **forward, they subjugated them.** The Gemara asks: **Initially,** when the Romans acted faithfully, **what** verse **did they interpret, and ultimately,** when they subjugated the Jews, **what** verse **did they interpret? Initially, they interpreted** the verse where Esau said to Jacob upon their meeting: **"Let us take our journey, and let us go, and I will go before you"** (Genesis 33:12). In this verse, Esau equates himself to Jacob, prefiguring the initial Roman treatment of the Jews. **And ultimately, they interpreted** the verse that recites Jacob's response to Esau: **"Let my lord, I pray you, pass over before his servant"** (Genesis 33:14), demonstrating Jacob's subjugation to Esau, and by extension that of the Jews to Rome.

Rome then sends the Greeks the conclusion: *anan Sefer Torah gavan ve-Yisrael bahadan* — we have the *Sefer Torah* with us, and we have the Jewish people with us. Therefore, you must capitulate to us, for the Torah — and the people who bear it — outrank everything else in the hierarchy that has just been established. The Greeks are compelled by this logic, and Rome's supremacy is established.

The Gemara maps this historical dynamic onto the relationship of Yaakov and Esav — understood throughout the tradition as the prototypes of Israel and Rome respectively. Two verses from the reunion narrative in Bereishit are brought to bear. Initially, the relationship was one of partnership: *nisa'ah ve-nelecha ve-elecha lenegdecha* — "let us travel and go, and I will go alongside you" — a formulation of equal partnership, walking together. Subsequently, the relationship shifted: *ya'avor na adoni lifnei avdo* — "let my lord pass before his servant" — in which Yaakov cedes the lead to Esav and assumes the role of subordinate. These two verses are read as encoding the arc of the Rome-Israel relationship: an initial period of genuine partnership and mutual benefit, followed by the subjugation of Israel under Roman imperial power.

The twenty-six years during which the partnership was honored is the subject of a further question — *minalanu?* — from where do we know this figure? — which introduces the historical chronology that follows.

## Roman Suppression

### 180 Years

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֶשְׂרִין וְשֵׁית שְׁנִין דְּקָמוּ בְּהֵימָנוּתַיְיהוּ בַּהֲדֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מְנָא לַן? דְּאָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: כְּשֶׁחָלָה רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בַּר יוֹסֵי, שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ: רַבִּי! אֱמוֹר לָנוּ שְׁנַיִם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים שֶׁאָמַרְתָּ לָנוּ מִשּׁוּם אָבִיךָ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: מֵאָה וּשְׁמֹנִים שָׁנָה קוֹדֶם שֶׁנֶּחֱרַב הַבַּיִת פָּשְׁטָה מַלְכוּת הָרְשָׁעָה עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל, שְׁמוֹנִים שָׁנָה עַד לֹא חָרַב הַבַּיִת גָּזְרוּ טוּמְאָה עַל אֶרֶץ הָעַמִּים וְעַל כְּלֵי זְכוּכִית, אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה עַד לֹא חָרַב הַבַּיִת גָּלְתָה סַנְהֶדְרִין וְיָשְׁבָה לָהּ בַּחֲנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to the **twenty-six years during which** the Romans **stood faithfully with the Jewish people, from where do we** know that this was the case? The Gemara cites a proof. **As Rav Kahana says: When Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, fell ill,** the Sages **sent** the following message **to him:** Our **teacher, tell us two or three statements that you** once **told us in the name of your father,** Rabbi Yosei ben Ḥalafta, as we do not remember the statements precisely. Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, **said to them** the following statements that were passed down to him by his father: **One hundred and eighty years before the** Second **Temple was destroyed, the evil** Roman **Empire stretched forth over Israel** and ruled over them. **Eighty years before the Temple was destroyed,** the Sages **decreed impurity on the land of the nations and on glass vessels. Forty years before the Temple was destroyed, the Sanhedrin was exiled** from the Chamber of Hewn Stone **and sat in the store** near the Temple Mount.

Rav Kahana cites a tradition: when Rabbi Yishmael bar Yosi fell ill, the Sages sent a message requesting that he transmit two or three teachings he had taught in the name of his father. Rabbi Yishmael bar Yosi relayed a series of historical chronological traditions. The first: one hundred and eighty years before the destruction of the Temple, *malchut ha-rishah* — the wicked empire, i.e., Rome — spread its dominion over Israel.

### Eighty Years: Tumah Decree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי: לוֹמַר, שֶׁלֹּא דָּנוּ דִּינֵי קְנָסוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what *halakha*** is it necessary to know where the Sanhedrin would convene? **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Avdimi said:** It is necessary in order **to say that they no** longer **judged cases of fines.**

Eighty years before the destruction of the Temple, the Sages decreed *tumah* — ritual impurity — upon *eretz ha-amim*, the lands of the nations, i.e., the diaspora, and upon vessels of glass. The practical implications of this decree are extensive but are not developed here.

### Sanhedrin Relocation

Forty years before the destruction of the Temple, the Sanhedrin was exiled from *Lishkat HaGazit* — the Chamber of Hewn Stone within the Temple complex, the seat of the Great Sanhedrin — and relocated to *Chanot*, a place understood to be in the marketplace area of the Temple Mount.

### Kenasos Suspension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּינֵי קְנָסוֹת סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? וְהָאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: בְּרַם זָכוּר אוֹתוֹ הָאִישׁ לַטּוֹב, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא שְׁמוֹ, שֶׁאִלְמָלֵא הוּא נִשְׁתַּכְּחוּ דִּינֵי קְנָסוֹת מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל! "נִשְׁתַּכְּחוּ"? לִגְרְסִינְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Does it enter your mind** that at this point the Sanhedrin no longer judged **cases of fines? But doesn't Rav Yehuda say** that **Rav says: Indeed [*beram*], that man will be remembered favorably, and Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava is his name, as had it not been for him the laws of fines would have been forgotten from** among **the Jewish people.** The Gemara challenges that assertion: **Would** the laws of fines actually **have been forgotten? Let** the scholars **study them,** so they will not be forgotten.

The Gemara asks: *lemai hilchata* — what is the halachic significance of this relocation? Rav Yitzchak bar Rav Avdimi answers: to teach that from that point forward, the Sanhedrin no longer adjudicated *dinei kenasos* — cases involving monetary penalties and fines. The authority to impose such penalties was tied to the Sanhedrin's proper seat, and once that was lost, the authority lapsed. The Gemara initially presses: surely *dinei kenasos* is itself a matter of Torah law — how can one simply say it was forgotten? The resolution is that it was not forgotten in the sense of being unknown, but *batlu* — suspended, rendered inoperative — due to the circumstances that now follow.

### Smicha Persecution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בָּטְלוּ דִּינֵי קְנָסוֹת מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁגָּזְרָה מַלְכוּת הַרְשָׁעָה שְׁמָד עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל: כָּל הַסּוֹמֵךְ יֵהָרֵג, וְכׇל הַנִּסְמָךְ יֵהָרֵג, וְעִיר שֶׁסּוֹמְכִין בָּהּ תֵּחָרֵב, וּתְחוּם שֶׁסּוֹמְכִין בּוֹ יֵעָקֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** his intention was to say that **the laws of fines would have ceased** to be implemented **from** among **the Jewish people,** as they would not have been able to adjudicate cases involving these *halakhot* due to a lack of ordained judges. This is **because** at one time **the wicked kingdom** of Rome **issued decrees of religious persecution against the Jewish people** with the aim of abolishing the chain of ordination and the authority of the Sages. They said that **anyone who ordains** judges **will be killed, and anyone who is ordained will be killed, and the city in which they ordain** the judges **will be destroyed, and** the areas around **the boundary** of the city **in which they ordain** judges **will be uprooted.** These measures were intended to discourage the Sages from performing or receiving ordination due to fear for the welfare of the local population.

When the wicked empire issued its decree, it proclaimed: anyone who confers rabbinic *smicha* shall be killed; anyone who receives *smicha* shall be killed; the city in which *smicha* is conferred shall be destroyed; and the *techum* within which *smicha* is given shall be uprooted. The purpose of the decree was to sever the chain of rabbinic ordination entirely, thereby preventing the perpetuation of the authority required to adjudicate *dinei kenasos* and other matters dependent on ordained judges.

### Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה עָשָׂה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא? הָלַךְ וְיָשַׁב בֵּין שְׁנֵי הָרִים גְּדוֹלִים, וּבֵין שְׁתֵּי עֲיָירוֹת גְּדוֹלוֹת, בֵּין שְׁנֵי תְּחוּמֵי שַׁבָּת, בֵּין אוּשָׁא לִשְׁפַרְעָם, וְסָמַךְ שָׁם חֲמִשָּׁה זְקֵנִים: רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן שַׁמּוּעַ, וְרַב אַוְיָא מוֹסִיף: אַף רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **What did** Rabbi **Yehuda ben Bava do? He went and sat between two large mountains, and between two large cities,** and **between two Shabbat boundaries: Between Usha and Shefaram,** i.e., in a desolate place that was not associated with any particular city so that he would not endanger anyone not directly involved, **and there he ordained five Elders,** namely: **Rabbi Meir, and Rabbi Yehuda, and Rabbi Shimon, and Rabbi Yosei, and Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua. And Rav Avya adds** that **Rabbi Neḥemya** was **also** among those ordained.

Into this breach stepped Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava, who is recalled with the formula *zachur oto ha'ish le-tov* — that man is remembered for good. Without his intervention, *dinei kenasos* would have been lost from Israel entirely. Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava is among the Ten Martyrs of the period of the Bar Kochba rebellion and its aftermath, and the full account of his heroism in preserving the chain of *smicha* — transmitting ordination to five students between two mountains while Roman soldiers closed in — will be taken up in the continuation of the daf.

### Death of Rabbi Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִכִּירוּ בָּהֶם אוֹיְבִים, אָמַר לָהֶם: בָּנַי, רוּצוּ! אָמְרוּ לוֹ: רַבִּי, וְאַתָּה מָה תְּהֵא עָלֶיךָ? אָמַר לָהֶם: הֲרֵינִי מוּטָל לִפְנֵיהֶם כְּאֶבֶן שֶׁאֵין לָהּ הוֹפְכִין. אָמְרוּ: לֹא זָזוּ מִשָּׁם עַד שֶׁנָּעֲצוּ לְגוּפוֹ שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת לוּלְנִיאוֹת שֶׁל בַּרְזֶל, וַעֲשָׂאוּהוּ לְגוּפוֹ כִּכְבָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **When** their **enemies discovered them,** Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava **said to** the newly ordained rabbis: **My sons, run** for your lives. **They said to him:** Our **teacher, and what will be with you?** Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava was elderly and unable to run. He **said to them:** In any case, **I am cast before them like a stone that cannot be overturned;** even if you attempt to assist me I will not be able to escape due to my frailty, but if you do not escape without me you will also be killed. People **said** about this incident: The Roman soldiers **did not move from there until they had inserted three hundred iron spears [*lulniot*] into his body, making his body** appear **like a sieve** pierced with many holes. It can be inferred from this episode that there were ordained judges who could hear cases of fines for many years after the destruction of the Temple, in contrast to Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Avdimi's statement.

**Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says** in explanation: **Do not say** that after the Sanhedrin was exiled from the Chamber of Hewn Stone they no longer judged cases of **fines; rather,** emend the statement to say **that they no** longer **judged** cases of **capital law,** as a court does not have the authority to hear capital cases when the Sanhedrin is not sitting in the Chamber of Hewn Stone.
