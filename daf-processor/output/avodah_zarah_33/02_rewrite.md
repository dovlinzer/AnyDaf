# Avodah Zarah 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Returning Pilgrims

### Vhaba'im Mutarin

The daf opens twelve lines into 33a, at the break in the *mishnah* marked by the two dots. The *mishnah* had been discussing various scenarios involving foods, moneys, and other matters associated with *avodah zarah*. One such scenario concerned doing business with those traveling to an idolatrous pilgrimage site — which is forbidden, even if it is not within the three-day period before a holiday, since the pilgrim will experience some joy from the transaction and carry that into his worship. The *mishnah* continues, however: *vhaba'im mutarin* — those returning from such a pilgrimage are permitted. They are simply going home, and one may do business with them.

### Kshurim Zeh Bazeh

Reish Lakish qualifies this ruling: *lo shanu ela she'ein kshurin zeh bazeh* — this permission applies only when the returning pilgrims are coming back separately from one another. But if they are returning *kshurin zeh bazeh* — bonded together, traveling as a group — then it is forbidden. The reasoning is *eima daito lachzor*: when they return as an organized group, it suggests they are deeply committed and intend to go back. The implication is that they are still in some meaningful sense engaged with their worship. Admittedly, even if they plan to return next year, that does not directly affect a business transaction conducted now — the precise logic here is not fully clear, but Reish Lakish evidently regards the group dynamic as indicative of an ongoing relationship with the idolatrous practice.

## Leather Flasks

### Nodot Grudim Mutarin

The *gemara* turns to an entirely different issue: the absorption of wine by vessels, and particularly the question of whether vessels can absorb taste even in cold conditions. The normal principle is that the transfer of taste requires heat. Yet when pottery vessels or leather flasks are used with wine over a long period, and the wine remains cold, there is still a concern that the vessel has absorbed the wine's taste. This becomes halachically significant in the context of *yayin nesech* and *stam yeinam*.

The *mishnah* records a dispute about leather flasks (*nodot*) and pottery vessels (*kankanim*) that absorbed non-kosher wine: Rabbi Meir held the wine that absorbed and came back out renders one's own wine forbidden even in *hana'ah*, while the *chachamim* said it is only forbidden to drink but not forbidden in *hana'ah*. The *gemara*, however, does not pursue that dispute. Instead, it focuses on which types of vessels absorb in the first place.

*Tannu rabbanan*: leather flasks of non-Jews — if they are *grudim chadashim*, peeled and new, meaning the inside has never been lined with pitch and they have only been used briefly, they are permitted. They do not absorb the taste, especially given that all contact is cold. But if they are *yeshanim*, old — meaning used by the non-Jew for an extended period — or *mezufafim*, tarred on the inside (Rashi reads "or" rather than "and"), they are forbidden. Old flasks have had ample time to absorb, and tarred ones present additional concern because the tar lining is highly porous.

### Shikhur Bishvil Pariput

The *braisa* continues: if a non-Jew applies the pitch lining and also pours wine into the flask — which was apparently standard practice to season the pitch and neutralize its bad taste — and a Jew is observing, one need not be concerned. The *gemara* immediately asks: if the non-Jew is the one pouring in the wine, what difference does it make that a Jew is watching? It is still the non-Jew pouring wine, which ought to render it *yayin nesech*.

### Rav Papa's Reinterpretat…

Rav Papa resolves this by reinterpreting the *braisa*: the non-Jew applies the pitch, but it is the Jew who pours in the wine. And it is yet another Jew who stands and observes. Now the *gemara* asks again: if a Jew is the one pouring in the wine, why does he need a second Jew to observe? The answer is *dilma agav tirdei minaseich velav adaitei* — perhaps, because the Jew pouring is occupied and focused on his professional task of adding the wine at precisely the right moment during the pitch application, he is not paying close attention to the non-Jew's movements. The non-Jew might exploit that distraction to touch or sprinkle the wine in a manner constituting *nisuch*. A second Jew stands watch specifically to prevent that. This is a re-reading of the *braisa*, but it resolves the difficulty.

### Zorkeik Mayim Letit

Rav Zvid offers a different resolution. He maintains the original reading of the *braisa* — the non-Jew himself pours the wine — and explains why it is nevertheless permitted. *Be'idna dekeshadi lei*, at the moment he pours the wine in, *naasei kezoreik mayim letit* — it is as though one is throwing water into plaster. The wine is effectively going to waste, being swallowed up into the pitch. Rashi explains that since the wine becomes mixed with the pitch, the pitch absorbs and destroys the wine so thoroughly that no taste can later migrate back into wine stored in the flask. Others, citing the *Yerushalmi*, read the point as one about intent: pouring something into pitch is an act of destruction, not an act of *nisuch*. That rationale would be more relevant when it is the Jew's wine being poured — though admittedly, if it were the non-Jew's own wine, the concern would be different.

### Salt Processing

Rav Papa then draws an inference from Rav Zvid's teaching: *hai oved kochavim deshadi chamra lebei milchei* — if a non-Jew pours wine into a Jew's salt, perhaps the salt similarly burns away and destroys the taste, making it permitted. Rav Ashi challenges this: *mi dami?* The cases are not comparable. In the case of pitch, the wine actively goes to waste — *ka'azil le'ibud*. In the case of salt, no such active destruction occurs; the taste remains present in the salt.

Tosafot observes that the comparison would never have been drawn to ordinary salt — the contrast is obvious. Tosafot therefore explains that the comparison was to salt-processing facilities, where fires burn during production and the claim was that the intense heat destroys the wine poured in. Rav Ashi's response is that even in that context, the wine can still be absorbed more than in the case of pitch. Importantly, Tosafot cites Rabbeinu Tam, who notes that Rav Ashi's challenge is merely a *dechuyah* — it shows only that one cannot derive the ruling from Rav Zvid's case, but Rav Papa's underlying position remains correct. On the basis of this, Rabbeinu Tam rules that one may use salt commercially processed by non-Jews, even though it is common practice during the boiling phase to add a small amount of blood to whiten it. Since the processing occurs at such high temperatures, the blood is destroyed. This is, incidentally, consistent with the observation that even in certain modern food industries, small amounts of blood are used for particular processing effects.

## Kashering Method

### Bar Addi Taya

The *gemara* presents a case involving Bar Addi Taya, an Arab, who forcibly seized leather wine flasks from Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef, filled them with wine for his own use, and then returned them. The question was brought to the *beit midrash*: how does one restore these flasks to a permitted status?

### Memalim Mayim

Rabbi Yirmeya reported that Rav Ami had issued a practical ruling: *memal'im mayim shlosha yamim u-m'aran* — fill the flask with water for three days and pour it out. Rava adds the specification: *tzricha l'aran me'et l'et* — the water must be poured out every twenty-four hours. The procedure is thus three cycles of twenty-four hours, each time replacing the water. The logic is that the fresh water draws out the absorbed wine taste, but after twenty-four hours the water itself has absorbed what it can and must be replaced with clean water to continue the process.

### Aran Me'et L'et

Two assumptions underlie this ruling. First, that the flask absorbed the wine — which is not entirely obvious, since the earlier *gemara* indicated that new or briefly used vessels do not absorb. Here, though, the flask had been used by the Jew previously and was evidently considered capable of further absorption when seized by the non-Jew. Second, that a cold-water method can effect *kashering* — a notable departure from the standard heat-based methods. This is the source for the practice some observe of *kashering* glasses for *Pesach* through immersion in water changed over seventy-two hours, though whether that practice is warranted in other contexts is a separate discussion.

### Didan vs. Didehu

Following Rava's ruling, the *gemara* records that some thought this water method applied only to Jewish-owned flasks (*didan*) that had been temporarily used by a non-Jew — since such flasks had already been heavily conditioned by Jewish wine, the additional absorption during the non-Jew's use was minimal and could be extracted by water. But if the flask was one that had been used by a non-Jew for years (*didehu*), this method would not suffice. The *mishnah* never provides a method to *kasher* such vessels; it simply forbids them.

### Echad Shelanu

When Ravin arrived from Eretz Yisrael, he reported in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish: *echad shelanu ve'echad shelahem* — the water method works for both. Whether the flask is one that had been in Jewish use or one used entirely by non-Jews, the principle that cold absorption can be reversed by cold extraction applies equally.

### Kankanin Question

Rav Acha son of Rava then raised a question before Rav Ashi: perhaps this water method is effective for leather flasks (*nodot*), even if they have pitch lining — but what of pottery vessels (*kankanin*)? Pottery is highly porous. Would the three-day water method succeed there as well?

### Rav Ashi Response

Rav Ashi replied: *lo shena nodot velo shena kankanin* — there is no distinction. Just as taste was absorbed through cold, so too it can be extracted through cold. One might note the tension: absorption occurred over many years of use, while extraction is claimed to occur in seventy-two hours. But that is the ruling.

## Pottery Vessels

### Kankanin Chadashim

*Tanu rabbanan*: pottery vessels (*kankanin*) of non-Jews — if they are new and untarred (*chadashim grudim*), they are permitted, as brief use has not resulted in meaningful absorption. If they are old (*yeshanim*) or tarred (*mezufafim*) — Rashi reads these as alternative conditions rather than cumulative — they are forbidden. Old vessels have had time to absorb; tarred ones present additional absorption through the porous pitch lining.

### Non-Jew Wine, Jewish Wat…

If a non-Jew has used such a vessel for wine and a Jew then fills it with water, the vessel is *kashered*. Rashi reads this as a reference to the three-day water process described earlier — *noten letochan mayim* does not by itself specify the full procedure, but that is how Rashi understands the *braisa*. This passage also explicitly addresses earthenware pottery vessels, providing confirmation that the water method applies to them as well, which had been the subject of Rav Acha's question above.

### Tzir U'Moris

The *braisa* continues: if a non-Jew used the vessel for wine, a Jew may subsequently use it for *tzir umoris* — brine and fish sauce — without further concern. Rashi explains that these sharp, pungent liquids actively negate and counteract the taste of the absorbed wine. This is a stronger claim than simply the water method: rather than gradually drawing the absorbed taste out, the brine neutralizes it immediately. One might also consider the principle of *noten ta'am lifgam* — that a taste which imparts a detrimental flavor is halachically disregarded — as an alternative rationale, though Rashi focuses on the counteracting mechanism. This is presented as distinct from the water method, which requires the three-day process, whereas the brine works immediately.

### Lechatchila or Bedi'avad

A question is then raised: is using brine and fish sauce in such vessels permitted only *bedi'avad* — after the fact, if one already used them — or is it permitted *lechatchila*, from the outset?

### Rav Zevid Permits

The *gemara* resolves this by citing Rav Zevid bar Oshaya at the top of 33b: one who purchases pottery vessels from non-Jews — if new, he may simply use them for wine; if old, he may fill them with *tzir umoris lechatchila*. This explicitly permits the use of brine from the outset, not merely *bedi'avad*. It is a form of counteraction, not *kashering*, but it is fully permitted at the outset for old vessels.

## Fire Kashering

### Fire Method Question

Rav Yehuda Nesi'ah, grandson of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, posed a question to Rabbi Ami: *hechziran lechivshan ha'esh venitlabnu mahu* — if one places these pottery vessels back into the kiln and heats them until they become white-hot, does that constitute a valid method of destroying the absorbed taste?

### Fire Negates Taste

Rabbi Ami responded with a *kal vachomer*: if we have already permitted even simple brine — which merely counteracts the taste of the wine — then certainly real fire would be considered to destroy it. The underlying premise, which Rashi makes explicit, is that the brine does not merely permit use while the absorbed wine taste remains; rather, it goes in and destroys that taste, rendering the vessel fully permitted for any subsequent use. On that reading, the *kal vachomer* to fire is straightforward.

### Tar Melts Off

It was also taught: Rabbi Yochanan (and some say Rabbi Assi reported it in his name) ruled that once the inner tar of such vessels has melted off in the fire, the vessels become permissible. This makes sense: if the absorbed wine was primarily held within the tar lining, once the tar is gone, the vessel is restored to its untarred state.

### Softening Suffices

Rav Ashi takes this further: *lo teima ad denasran* — one need not wait for the tar to fully melt and slough off. *Afilu rifuy mirpu* — even if the tar only softens, that too is sufficient. The point is not literally about the tar being gone, but about the degree of heat applied. Softening of the tar is indicative of the vessel having been sufficiently heated to destroy the absorbed wine.

### Kinsa Debate

The *gemara* then raises the question of *kinsa* — placing small pieces of wood or twigs inside the vessel and burning them on the inside rather than firing the vessel from the outside in a kiln. Rav Acha and Ravina dispute this: one permits it and one forbids it. The *halacha* is ruled like the one who forbids.

### Outside vs. Inside Fire

Rashi explains the distinction carefully. When a vessel is placed in a kiln and fired from the outside: since the heat is applied externally, and the tar on the inside eventually melts, that means the pottery body itself became white-hot — *tanyas levana achris*. It is the pottery that was heated through. In the case of *kinsa*, however, putting fire on the inside causes the tar to soften immediately, but the pottery body itself is not meaningfully heated. The softening of the tar in the kiln case was merely a marker for the pottery reaching sufficient heat. Here, one has achieved softened tar without heating the pottery, and that does not *kasher* the absorbed wine in the pottery itself.

### Wine Barrels Kashering

Rashi draws a direct practical application: wine barrels and pottery jugs used for long-term storage are the classic case of *yeshanim* — they have genuinely absorbed wine over extended use. Such vessels require *kashering*. *Lo sagi behachsher bifnim* — firing from the inside is insufficient, as established. And since *kinsa* itself is forbidden, *kol shekein bemayim chamim* — certainly ordinary *kashering* with hot water will not work either. The options that remain are firing from the outside as in a kiln, or the three-day water method with the water changed every twenty-four hours. Rashi also mentions waiting twelve months as an additional possibility, though this does not appear explicitly in the *gemara* at this point.

It is worth pausing to note the underlying paradox: ordinarily, pottery cannot be *kashered* at all because the Torah indicates that absorbed matter cannot be extracted from it. On the other hand, the absorption here occurred through cold contact. The result is that standard heat-based *kashering* methods do not work — because it is pottery — but the cold water method does, precisely because the absorption was cold.

## Beer vs. Wine

### Beer in Wine Vessels

Having established that *tzir umoris* can counteract absorbed wine, the *gemara* raises the parallel question: *mahu lates lesochan shechar* — what about using beer in vessels previously used for wine? Can beer similarly neutralize the absorbed wine taste, or must one use only the specific substances mentioned?

### Rav Nachman vs. Rava

Rav Nachman and Rav Yehuda both forbid using beer for this purpose. Rava, however, permits it. Rashi gives an explanation similar to the brine case: the beer counteracts the taste of the wine. Whether the vessel is then fully permitted for any subsequent use is not entirely clear from the discussion.

### Ravina Permits

Ravina ruled leniently, permitting Rav Chiya son of Rav Yitzchak to use these vessels for beer. That person subsequently put wine in the vessels instead. Ravina, upon learning of this, was unconcerned.

### Teaching Correct Law

One might have expected Ravina to respond by tightening his ruling — if telling people beer is permitted leads them to use wine, perhaps one should simply rule beer forbidden to prevent the confusion. Ravina rejected this approach entirely: *lo chash lamiltei amikrei be'alma* — it was a pure coincidence that the mistaken substitution occurred. It does not justify altering the correct *halacha*. The correct ruling must be taught, and incidental errors do not change that.

## Temporary Use

### Dung Vessels Burst

Rav Yitzchak bar Bisna had vessels made from dried dung (*Pakusna*) that had absorbed wine. He attempted to *kasher* them using the water method but placed them out in the sun. The vessels burst.

### Issura D'Olam

Rav Abba rebuked him sharply: *assarta tinu alaich issura de'olam* — you have rendered them permanently forbidden, a prohibition for all time. By destroying the vessels through this improvised method, he made it impossible to ever *kasher* them properly. When did the Rabbis prescribe the water method? *Memal'im mayim* — simply filling with water and leaving them. *Achinu beshimsha mi amrei* — leaving them in the hot sun was never part of the prescription. The sun's heat caused the vessels to burst, and that was simply an error.

### Vessels of Nitron

Rav Hisda said in the name of Rav Ami: *klei netara ein lo tahara olamit* — vessels of nitron can never be fully *kashered*; they have no enduring remedy. When asked what *klei netara* are, Rav Yosef bar Avin explained that they are vessels made from the excavated earth around alum deposits — nitron being a mixture of sodium carbonate occurring naturally in desert terrain, often produced in antiquity from seaweed. The precise reason these cannot be *kashered* is not fully spelled out in the *gemara*, though the most natural understanding is that they absorb wine without being capable of releasing it.

### Temporary Use Rinsing

The house of Parzik — an official of some governmental authority — forcibly seized vessels from Pumbedita, filled them with wine, and then returned them. This closely parallels the earlier case of Bar Addi Taya. In this instance, the question was brought to Rav Yehuda.

### Lo Machnisan Lekiyomam

Rav Yehuda ruled: *keivan delo machnisan lekiyomam* — since they did not keep the wine in the vessels for long-term storage but only used them temporarily, *meshachshechan bemayim ve'in mutarin* — simply rinsing with water until no drips remain on the inside is sufficient. This contrasts with the earlier case, where the full three-day water method was required. The distinction may be that Bar Addi Taya's case involved longer use, constituting genuine *machnisan lekiyomam*, while here the use was plainly temporary. The *gemara* does not place these two cases in explicit dialogue with one another, but that resolution is available.

### Porous Assessment

Rav Avira taught: *hani chatzvei shachimei d'armaya* — these brown earthenware vessels of the Arameans, since *delo bal'u tuva* — they are not particularly porous — *meshachshechan bemayim ve'in mutarin* — rinsing with water suffices. Even though they are earthenware, the halachic concern is not a formal categorical one about the material but about the actual degree of absorption. Rav Pappi makes the same point regarding the *pasvasa* vessels of the region of Michsi, which were made from especially hard earth and were similarly not porous — rinsing suffices.

This reflects a *metzius*-oriented approach to the laws of absorption: the question is whether a given vessel actually absorbs, not merely what category of material it belongs to. This approach has implications for contemporary *kashering* questions as well. Some *poskim* take a formalist view — if a material resembles pottery it is treated like pottery, if it resembles glass it is treated like glass — while others assess actual porosity. The *gemara* here clearly endorses the latter framework, at least for cold absorption.

## Glazed Vessels Wine

### Glaze Types

Rav Zpidi taught: glazed vessels (*mani dekunya*) — if the glaze is white (*chivra*) or black (*uchma*), it is permissible. The glaze renders the vessel non-porous and prevents absorption, particularly since we are dealing with cold contact. However, green-glazed (*yeroka*) vessels are forbidden, *mishum demitzrif* — because green glaze contains alum, which is highly absorbent. This connects back to the earlier discussion of *klei netara*: alum absorbs and retains in a way that cannot be reversed. Therefore, even though the vessel appears glazed, the green glaze allows absorption.

### Cracked Glaze

Even for white and black glazed vessels, if the glaze has cracks (*kartufnei*), all are forbidden. Cracked glaze loses its protective function, allowing wine to penetrate and be retained by the earthenware beneath.

### Mareimar Permits All

Mareimar ruled more broadly: glazed vessels — *bein uchma bein chivra bein yeroka*, whether black, white, or green — are all permissible. This is a more lenient position than Rav Zpidi's, dispensing with the distinction based on glaze color. Rashi notes that these glazes were lead-based — a reminder of the ubiquity of lead in the ancient world's material culture.

## Pesach Glazed Vessels

### Pesach Question

The *gemara* then poses a striking challenge to Mareimar's leniency. The question was brought directly to him: *hani mani dekunya, mahu le'ishtamushi behu bePesach* — can these glazed vessels be used for *Pesach*? In this context, of course, the vessels would have been used with heat — *chametz* is cooked or baked, unlike wine which is consumed cold. So we are now in a different domain of absorption: hot rather than cold.

Mareimar's questioner lays out the parameters precisely. Green-glazed vessels need not even be asked about — *demitzrif uvala*, the alum-based glaze absorbs regardless, and it is certainly forbidden. Similarly, if the glaze has cracks (*kartufnei*), the answer is obvious — *devadai bal'u*, they certainly absorbed, and they are forbidden. The real question concerns white and black glazed vessels with smooth, intact glaze: what is the *halacha* for *Pesach*?

### Sweating Shows Absorption

Mareimar responded: *ana chazina lehu demadaytzi* — I see that they sweat. Even the smooth, white or black glazed vessels, after being used, exude moisture from their surface. *Vekivan demadaytzi, ve'azli bal'ei* — since they sweat, that demonstrates they absorb. *Va'asiri* — they are forbidden. The sweating is empirical evidence that liquid permeates through even smooth glaze when heat is involved. Mareimar, who had been entirely lenient regarding wine, is here fully stringent regarding *Pesach*.

### Torah's Testimony

Why is absorption through glaze concerning for *Pesach* but not for wine? The *gemara* invokes a broader principle: *datorah eidah al kli cheres she'eino yotzei miyedei dofno le'olam* — the Torah itself testifies that pottery vessels cannot release absorbed matter. The source is the *halacha* of cooking a *chatas* in a pottery vessel, which must be destroyed afterward — because once absorbed, the taste cannot be expelled. If the sweating demonstrates that the glazed vessel absorbed, then the Torah's rule applies: that absorbed matter stays in permanently.

### Heat vs. Cold

The *gemara* now resolves the apparent contradiction between Mareimar's leniency regarding wine and his stringency regarding *Pesach*. The response is: *zeh tashmisho bechamin vezehu tashmisho betzonen* — these are two different modes of use. *Chametz* is used with heat; *yayin nesech* is used cold. When contact is hot, the absorbed material penetrates through even intact glaze and embeds itself in the pottery beneath — whereupon the Torah's rule applies and it can never be fully expelled. When contact is cold, the glaze itself serves as an effective barrier and no meaningful absorption occurs at all.

This is one of the central distinctions running through the entire daf: cold absorption is a genuine phenomenon — vessels used with wine over extended periods do absorb even without heat — but it is categorically different from hot absorption. Cold absorption can, under certain conditions, be reversed; hot absorption into pottery cannot. Cold absorption may be blocked by an intact glaze; hot absorption will penetrate through it. And cold absorption permits more flexible methods of remedy — the three-day water process, brine, and other approaches — whereas hot absorption into pottery admits of no remedy at all, as the Torah itself attests.
