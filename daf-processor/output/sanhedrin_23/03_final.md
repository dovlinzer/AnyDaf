# Sanhedrin 23 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Perek III Intro (II)

### Context & Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה. זֶה בּוֹרֵר לוֹ אֶחָד, וְזֶה בּוֹרֵר לוֹ אֶחָד, וּשְׁנֵיהֶן בּוֹרְרִין לָהֶן עוֹד אֶחָד, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: שְׁנֵי דַיָּינִין בּוֹרְרִין לָהֶן עוֹד אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Cases of **monetary law** are adjudicated **by three.** They are chosen in the following manner: **This** litigant **chooses one for himself and that** litigant **chooses one for himself, and the two of them choose one more for themselves;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say:** The **two judges** that were chosen **choose one more** judge **for themselves.**

Sanhedrin 23a opens the third *perek* of the tractate, and in doing so circles back to the very beginning of *Masechet Sanhedrin*. The first three words of the new *mishna* — *dinei mamonot bishlisha*, monetary cases require three judges — are of course familiar, since that is precisely how the entire tractate began. The *Rambam* explains that the reason this phrase recurs here is that the second *perek* took us on something of a detour, addressing the relationship of the *kohen gadol* and the *melech* to the *beit din*. Now we return to *dinei mamonot bishlisha*, and this *perek* will focus on a different question: how does one actually constitute such a court? Who gets to be among the three who sit in judgment over these monetary cases?

### Mishna Translation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה פּוֹסֵל דַּיָּינוֹ שֶׁל זֶה, וְזֶה פּוֹסֵל דַּיָּינוֹ שֶׁל זֶה – דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵימָתַי? בִּזְמַן שֶׁמֵּבִיא עֲלֵיהֶן רְאָיָה שֶׁהֵן קְרוֹבִין אוֹ פְּסוּלִין. אֲבָל אִם הָיוּ כְּשֵׁרִין אוֹ מוּמְחִין מִפִּי בֵּית דִּין – אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְפוֹסְלָן.
>
> **Translation:** **This** litigant can **disqualify the judge** chosen **by that** litigant **and that** litigant can **disqualify the judge** chosen **by this** litigant; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: When** can one of the litigants disqualify the judges? Only **when he brings evidence about them that they are related** to one of the litigants or to each other, **or** that they are **disqualified** from serving as judges for another reason. **But if they are fit** to serve as judges **or are experts** ordained **by the court, he cannot disqualify them.**

The *mishna* itself is not entirely clear, and as it turns out, the *gemara* will spend considerable time — across multiple *amudim* — simply trying to determine what the *mishna* means. For that reason, it is worth reading and translating it first before attempting any deeper analysis.

*Dinei mamonot bishlisha* — monetary cases require three *dayanim*. *Zeh borer lo echad vezeh borer lo echad* — this litigant chooses one, and the other litigant chooses one. The *gemara* will address what exactly "one" refers to. *Ushneihem borerim lahen od echad* — and the two of them choose one more. The question of who precisely "the two of them" refers to will likewise depend on how the *mishna* is read. *Divrei Rabbi Meir* — this is Rabbi Meir's position. *Vachachamim omrim shnei dayanim borerim lahen od echad* — the Sages say that the two *dayanim* choose one additional *dayan*. From this formulation, it becomes clear that in the *reisha*, "the two of them" who each choose one *dayan* must be the two litigants — since the *Chachamim* explicitly say it is the two *dayanim* (not the litigants) who choose the third.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה פּוֹסֵל עֵדָיו שֶׁל זֶה, וְזֶה פּוֹסֵל עֵדָיו שֶׁל זֶה. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵימָתַי? בִּזְמַן שֶׁמֵּבִיא עֲלֵיהֶן רְאָיָה שֶׁהֵן קְרוֹבִין אוֹ פְּסוּלִין, אֲבָל אִם הָיוּ כְּשֵׁרִין – אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְפוֹסְלָן.
>
> **Translation:** **This** litigant can **disqualify the witnesses of that** litigant **and that** litigant can **disqualify the witnesses of this** litigant; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: When** can one litigant disqualify the other's witnesses? Only **when he brings evidence about them that they are related** to one of the litigants or to each other, **or** that they are **disqualified** from bearing witness for another reason. **But if they are fit** to serve as witnesses, **he cannot disqualify them.**

*Zeh posel dayno shel zeh vezeh posel dayno shel zeh, divrei Rabbi Meir* — this litigant can invalidate the other's *dayan*, and vice versa; this is Rabbi Meir's view. *VeChachamim omrim eimatai bizman shemevi aleihem ra'ayah shehen kerovim o pesulim* — the Sages say: when can he do this? Only when he brings proof that the *dayan* is related to the other litigant, or is *pasul* for some other reason. *Aval im hayu kesherim, eino yachol leposlan* — but if they are *kosher*, he cannot disqualify them.

The *mishna* continues with a parallel ruling regarding witnesses. *Zeh posel ido shel zeh vezeh posel ido shel zeh, divrei Rabbi Meir* — each litigant can invalidate the other's witness; this is Rabbi Meir's view. The *Chachamim* repeat their position: only when he can prove they are related or *pesulim* for another reason; but if they are *kesherim*, he cannot *posel* them.

## Mai Zeh Borer

### Gemara Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״זֶה בּוֹרֵר לוֹ אֶחָד וְזֶה בּוֹרֵר לוֹ אֶחָד״? בִּתְלָתָא סַגִּי!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara assumes that the statement: This litigant chooses one and that litigant chooses one and the two of them choose one more, means that each litigant chooses a court of three judges and the two courts together choose one more court, for a total of nine judges. The Gemara asks: For **what** purpose does **this** litigant **choose one** court **for himself and that** litigant **choose one** court **for himself;** isn't it **sufficient** to judge the case **with three** judges?

The *gemara* opens with the question *mai zeh borer lo echad vezeh borer lo echad, bitlata sagi* — what does this mean, "this one chooses one and this one chooses one"? Three *dayanim* would suffice; why does the *mishna* speak of this elaborate procedure of choosing? According to Rashi's reading, the *gemara*'s initial assumption is that *echad* in the *mishna* means one entire *beit din* of three judges — meaning each litigant selects a full *beit din*, yielding six judges, and then together they choose a third *beit din*, yielding nine. The *gemara* finds this puzzling: we only need three.

## Rashi and Tosafot

### Rashi: Full Beit Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: כְּשֶׁזֶּה בּוֹרֵר לוֹ בֵּית דִּין אֶחָד, וְזֶה בּוֹרֵר לוֹ בֵּית דִּין אֶחָד, שְׁנֵיהֶן בּוֹרְרִין לָהֶן עוֹד אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This** is what the mishna **is saying:** In a case **where this** litigant **chooses one court for himself,** i.e., he requests to be judged before three specific judges, **and that** litigant **chooses one** other **court** of three judges **for himself,** as he does not wish to be judged before the court that the first litigant requested, in this case, **both of them choose one more** court **for themselves,** i.e., they must reach a compromise and decide about the composition of the court that will judge them.

Rashi explains the *pshat* of the *mishna* as follows: *zeh borer lo beit din echad shehem shlosha, vezeh echad, harei shisha* — this one chooses one full *beit din* of three, and the other chooses another, giving six. *Ushneihem borerim lahen od echad, harei tisha* — and together they choose a third *beit din*, giving nine. And that, says Rashi, is precisely the force of the *gemara*'s surprised question: *mai zeh borer, ha bitlata sagi* — why go through all of this when three judges are sufficient?

### Tosafot Dispute

Tosafot disagree with Rashi's reading of the *mishna*'s *pshat*. They maintain that the *gemara* knows from the outset that *echad* simply means one *dayan*, not one *beit din*, and that the straightforward reading of the *mishna* is that each litigant chooses one judge and together they choose a third. According to Tosafot, the *gemara*'s question is not asked out of genuine puzzlement about the *mishna*'s meaning; rather, it is posed as a kind of leading question in order to introduce an additional ruling — namely, the scenario in which one litigant selects a *beit din* and the other rejects it, and they must then choose a third *beit din* together. The *gemara* asks the question in order to bring that case as an answer, even though it already knows that is not the primary *pshat* of the *mishna*.

## Mai Zeh Borer (II)

### Beit Din Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ לֹוֶה מָצֵי מְעַכֵּב? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא מַלְוֶה, אֲבָל לֹוֶה כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ וְדָן בְּעִירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And can even a debtor restrict** the choice of the court? **But doesn't Rabbi Elazar say:** The Sages **taught** that **only a creditor** can refuse a court chosen by the debtor, due to his desire to be judged by a prominent court, **but** that as for **a debtor, they compel him** to appear in the court that **presides in his** own **city.**

With this framework in place, the *gemara* proceeds to answer its own question. *Hachi ka'amar* — this is what the *mishna* means: when Reuven selects a *beit din* and Shimon objects, and then Shimon proposes a different *beit din* and Reuven objects, neither of those first two courts is used. Instead, *shneihem borerim lahen od echad* — together they choose a third *beit din*. This is the scenario the *gemara* had in mind when it asked *mai zeh borer*.

### Debtor Power Issue

The *gemara*, however, immediately raises a difficulty against this proposed answer. It notes the apparent contradiction with a ruling of Rav Elazar cited later in the tractate: *lo shanu ela malve, aval love kofin oto vedan be'iro* — the rule that a litigant may reject the other's *beit din* applies only to the creditor (*malve*), but the debtor (*love*) is compelled to appear before the court in his own city. If the *love* has no power to reject the *malve*'s *beit din*, how can the *mishna* contemplate a scenario in which the *love* does exactly that?

### Rabbi Elazar Ruling

Rav Elazar's ruling thus creates a problem for the reading of the *mishna* just proposed: the *mishna* seems to assume that either litigant may reject the other's court, while Rav Elazar limits that power to the creditor alone.

### R. Yochanan: Non-Expert

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּעַרְכָּאוֹת שֶׁבְּסוּרְיָא שָׁנוּ. הָכָא נָמֵי, בְּעַרְכָּאוֹת שֶׁבְּסוּרְיָא שָׁנוּ. אֲבָל מוּמְחִין – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is **as Rabbi Yoḥanan says** concerning a different matter, that the Sages **taught** that other matter **with regard to** the laymen's **courts [*be'arkaot*] in Syria. Here too,** the Sages **taught** the *halakha* in the mishna **with regard to** the laymen's **courts in Syria,** which allow even the debtor to refuse to be judged before the court chosen by the creditor, claiming that they are not worthy judges. **But** if the creditor chooses a court of **experts,** the debtor does **not** have the right to refuse to be judged before them.

The *gemara* resolves this tension through Rabbi Yochanan's ruling in a different context: *be'arkaot shebe'Surya shanu* — our *mishna* is speaking specifically about non-professional courts. When the *beit din* consists of three *dayanim* who are not *mumchin* — who are not well-versed experts in *dinei mamonot* — then even the *love* can legitimately object, because there is valid grounds for preferring a different court. The *love* is not acting capriciously; he has a substantive reason to reject *dayanim* who are not professionally competent. *Aval mumchin lo* — but if the *dayanim* are expert professionals, the *love* cannot reject the creditor's court, and we revert to Rav Elazar's ruling.

### Rav Pappa Clarif.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא מוּמְחִין, כְּגוֹן בֵּי דִינָא דְּרַב הוּנָא וּדְרַב חִסְדָּא, דְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ: מִי קָא מַטְרַחְנָא לָךְ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: You** may **even say** that the mishna is referring to courts of **experts** in the same city, **such as the courts of Rav Huna and of Rav Ḥisda. As** in that case, each litigant **can say to** the other: **Am I burdening you** by requesting that you be judged by a different court?

Rav Pappa offers a further nuance. Even if one assumes the *mishna* is speaking of expert courts — *afilu teima mumchin*, such as the courts of Rav Huna and Rav Chisda — the *love* may still object, on the grounds that *mi ka matrachna lach* — I am not causing you a significant burden (*tircha*) by asking you to go to the other *beit din*, because both courts are equally accessible. Rashi explains that Rav Elazar's ruling — that the *love* cannot impose on the *malve* — is premised on the *love* causing undue *tircha*, such as requiring the *malve* to travel to a different city. But where both courts are located in the same city, no such *tircha* is imposed, and even the *love* may insist on a different court. Tosafot extend this somewhat, suggesting that even courts within a certain distance — less than three *parsaot* — would satisfy this condition.

## Reject Beit Din Interp.

### Logic Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים, שְׁנֵי דַיָּינִין בּוֹרְרִין לָהֶן עוֹד אֶחָד. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ כִּדְקָאָמְרִינַן בֵּית דִּין – בֵּית דִּין בָּתַר דְּפָסְלִי לְהוּ, אָזְלוּ וּבָרְרוּ לְהוּ בֵּי דִינָא אַחֲרִינֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: **And the Rabbis say:** The **two judges** that were chosen **choose one more for themselves. And if it enters your mind** to interpret the mishna **as we said** before, that each litigant chose **a court** of three judges, this statement is difficult; **after** each **court was disqualified** by one of the litigants, shall the members of both courts **go and choose another court for themselves?**

Having explored the reading of the *mishna* as referring to *batei din*, the *gemara* now returns to reject this interpretation entirely. The *mishna* states: *vachachamim omrim shnei dayanim borerim lahen od echad* — the Sages say the two *dayanim* choose a third. If the earlier part of the *mishna* meant that each litigant chose an entire *beit din*, it would make no sense for those two *batei din* — having rejected each other — to then convene together and choose a third *beit din*. *Beit din batar depasli lehu azlu uvararu lehu beit dina achrina* — two courts that have just invalidated each other are not going to collaborate on selecting a third. The scenario is logically absurd.

### One Judge Each

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְעוֹד, מַאי ״זֶה בּוֹרֵר לוֹ אֶחָד וְזֶה בּוֹרֵר לוֹ אֶחָד״?
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** for **what** reason would the mishna state that the standard procedure for choosing judges is that **this** litigant **chooses one** court **for himself and that** litigant **chooses one** court **for himself,** each disqualifying the court that the other chose?

There is also a linguistic problem with the *beit din* reading. The *mishna* uses the word *echad* — one. One does not mean one *beit din*; *echad* means one *dayan*. The *gemara* therefore concludes: *ela hachi ka'amar* — this is what the *mishna* actually means. *Keshezeh borer lo dayan echad vezeh borer lo dayan echad, shneihem borerim lahen od echad* — when this litigant chooses one *dayan* and the other litigant chooses one *dayan*, the two of them together choose a third.

### Fairness Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: כְּשֶׁזֶּה בּוֹרֵר לוֹ דַּיָּין אֶחָד, וְזֶה בּוֹרֵר לוֹ דַּיָּין אֶחָד, שְׁנֵיהֶן בּוֹרְרִין לָהֶן עוֹד אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, this** is what the mishna **is saying: When this** litigant **chooses one judge for himself** before whom he requests to be judged, **and that** litigant **chooses one** other **judge for himself** before whom he requests to be judged, **the two** litigants then **choose one more** judge **for themselves,** and the case is adjudicated by these three judges.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא דְּעָבְדִי הָכִי? אָמְרִי בְּמַעְרְבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁזֶּה בּוֹרֵר לוֹ דַּיָּין אֶחָד, וְזֶה בּוֹרֵר לוֹ דַּיָּין אֶחָד, וּשְׁנֵיהֶן בּוֹרְרִין לָהֶן עוֹד אֶחָד – יֵצֵא הַדִּין לַאֲמִיתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about this procedure; i.e., **that** the selection of the judges is **performed** specifically **in this** manner? The Sages **in the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **say in the name of Rabbi Zeira: As a result of** the fact **that this** litigant **chooses one judge for himself, and that** litigant **chooses one judge for himself, and the two** litigants **choose one more** judge **for themselves, the true judgment will emerge.** Each litigant assumes that the judges he chose do not bear a grudge against him, and he will accept their ruling.

The *gemara* then asks *mai shena de'avdi hachi* — why is this the proper procedure? The answer comes from the *amora'im* in the West (*amri bema'arava*) in the name of Rabbi Zeira: *mitoch shezeh borer lo dayan echad vezeh borer lo dayan echad ushneihem borerim lahen od echad, yetzei hadin la'amito* — because this arrangement produces the truest and most reliable judicial outcome. Both Rashi and the Rambam emphasize that this structure is the surest guarantee of a fair result, and it also puts the losing litigant's mind at ease. Whoever loses will be able to say: I chose my own *dayan*, and he tried his best to rule in my favor; the fact that the court ruled against me means the *din* must be correct.

## Rabbi Meir Dispute

### Judges' Knowledge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים כּוּ׳. נֵימָא בִּדְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב קָמִיפַּלְגִי?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And the Rabbis say:** The two judges that were chosen choose one more judge. The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis **disagree with regard to what Rav Yehuda** says that **Rav says.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אֵין הָעֵדִים חוֹתְמִין עַל הַשְּׁטָר אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן יוֹדְעִין מִי חוֹתֵם עִמָּהֶם. רַבִּי מֵאִיר לֵית לֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, וְרַבָּנַן אִית לְהוּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: Witnesses do not sign a document unless they know who is signing with them.** One does not sign a document unless he recognizes that those signing with him are fit to bear witness. According to the Gemara's suggestion, **Rabbi Meir does not accept that which Rav Yehuda** says that **Rav says.** Therefore, the litigants can choose the third judge, as the first two judges will be amenable to signing the court order without knowing the third judge. **And the Rabbis accept that which Rav Yehuda** says that **Rav says;** and the judges would not sign the court order without themselves knowing the third judge.

With the *pshat* of the *mishna* now established — each litigant chooses one *dayan*, and together they choose a third — the *gemara* turns to the *machloket* between Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* regarding who selects that third *dayan*. The *gemara* wonders whether this dispute might be rooted in an earlier *machloket* between Rabbi Yehuda in the name of Rav: *ein ha'eidim chotmin al hashtar ela im ken yod'im mi chotam imahen* — witnesses should not sign a document unless they know who else is signing with them. Rashi explains the reason: *shema yachtimu imahem ed echad pasul* — lest an invalid witness sign alongside them, thereby invalidating the entire testimony and causing them embarrassment.

This ruling is supported by a *baraita*: *kach hayu niki'ei hada'at shebe'Yerushalaim osin* — this was the practice of those of refined sensibility in Jerusalem. They would not sign a document unless they knew who was signing with them; they would not sit in judgment unless they knew who their fellow *dayanim* would be; and they would not enter a *se'udah* unless they knew with whom they would be *meisav*. The first two practices follow naturally from legal and halakhic concerns. The third — not dining without knowing one's company — reflects something beyond the strictly legal: the importance of the company one keeps, the dignity associated with a formal meal in rabbinic culture. Rashi's comment suggests this reflects concern for the honor of Torah scholars, though the text uses the term *niki'ei hada'at* rather than *chachamim*, which raises the question of whether this is a distinct category with only partial overlap.

### R. Meir: Full Consent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אֵין הָעֵדִים חוֹתְמִין עַל הַשְּׁטָר כּוּ׳. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: כָּךְ הָיוּ נְקִיֵּי הַדַּעַת שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם עוֹשִׂין – לֹא הָיוּ חוֹתְמִין עַל הַשְּׁטָר אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן יוֹדְעִין מִי חוֹתֵם עִמָּהֶן, וְלֹא הָיוּ יוֹשְׁבִין בְּדִין אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן יוֹדְעִין מִי יוֹשֵׁב עִמָּהֶן, וְלֹא הָיוּ נִכְנָסִין בִּסְעוּדָה אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן יוֹדְעִין מִי מֵסֵב עִמָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** Returning to **the** matter **itself, Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: Witnesses do not sign a document** unless they know who is signing with them. **That is also taught** in a *baraita*: **This is what the scrupulous people of Jerusalem would do: They would not sign a document unless they knew who was signing with them, and they would not sit in judgment unless they knew who was sitting with them, and they would not join a meal unless they knew who was reclining,** i.e., eating, **with them.**

The *gemara* proposes that the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* tracks precisely this earlier *machloket*. Rabbi Meir — who holds that both litigants together choose the third *dayan* — would *not* hold like Rabbi Yehuda's ruling in the name of Rav. In other words, Rabbi Meir is not concerned that the two *dayanim* might be embarrassed by the third turning out to be *pasul*. Rather, Rabbi Meir's position reflects a different concern: that the litigants themselves must have full buy-in (*da'at ba'alei dinim*) into the composition of the *beit din*. According to Rabbi Meir, a *beit din* requires the consent of both litigants to all three *dayanim*; without that, the court lacks proper authority.

### Chachamim: Judges Agree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אִית לְהוּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, וְדַעַת הַדַּיָּינִין – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּבָעֵינַן. כִּי פְּלִיגִי – דַּעַת בַּעֲלֵי דִינִים: רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר דַּעַת בַּעֲלֵי דִינִים נָמֵי בָּעֵינַן, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי דַּעַת הַדַּיָּינִין בָּעֵינַן, דַּעַת בַּעֲלֵי דִינִים לָא בָּעֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No, everyone accepts that which Rav Yehuda** says that **Rav says; and everyone agrees** with the premise **that we need the consent of the** first two **judges** for the appointment of the third. Rather, **when they disagree,** it is about whether **the consent of the litigants** is necessary. **Rabbi Meir holds** that **we need the consent of the litigants as well, and the Rabbis hold** that although **we need the consent of the** other two **judges, we do not need the consent of the litigants.**

The *gemara* rejects this proposed alignment. *De'chulei alma is lehu deRabbi Yehuda amar Rav* — everyone agrees with Rabbi Yehuda's ruling in the name of Rav that witnesses (and by extension *dayanim*) must know who their counterparts are. The dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* is not about whether the *dayanim* need to know who the third *dayan* is — all agree they do — but rather about whether the *litigants* also need to know and consent to the third *dayan*. *Rabbi Meir savar da'at ba'alei dinim nami ba'inan* — Rabbi Meir holds that the litigants' consent is also required. *VeRabbanan savri da'at hadayanim ba'inan da'at ba'alei dinim lo ba'inan* — the *Chachamim* hold that the *dayanim*'s agreement on the third is sufficient; the litigants need not consent.

This surfaces a broader theme that runs throughout this entire *sugya*: whence does a *beit din* derive its authority? If three people simply sit down and declare themselves a *beit din*, does that automatically grant them judicial power? The emerging principle seems to be that a *beit din* of recognized professionals (*mumchin*) established in a town possesses authority automatically. But where the *dayanim* are not *mumchin*, the court requires the buy-in of the litigants — without it, the *beit din* has no real power over them.

### Language Analysis

The *gemara* then cites the later part of the *mishna* to further examine this question — *gufah*, returning to the *baraita* quoting Rabbi Yehuda's ruling — *ein ha'eidim chotmin al hashtar*, and confirming that this is cited as support. At this point the *gemara* begins addressing the second part of the *mishna* — the disqualification of witnesses — a subject which introduces its own complexities.

## Witness Disqualif.

### Disqualif. Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה פּוֹסֵל דַּיָּינוֹ כּוּ׳. כֹּל כְּמִינֵּיהּ דְּפָסֵיל דַּיָּינֵי?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **This** litigant can **disqualify the judge** chosen by that litigant and that litigant can disqualify the judge chosen by this litigant. The Gemara asks: **Is it in his** power **to disqualify judges?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּעַרְכָּאוֹת שֶׁבְּסוּרְיָא שָׁנוּ, אֲבָל מוּמְחִים – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The Sages **taught** this matter **with regard to** the laymen's **courts in Syria.** In those courts, each litigant can refuse to be judged by the judge the other litigant chose. **But** this *halakha* is **not** stated with regard to a court of **expert** judges.

The *mishna* states, according to Rabbi Meir, that *zeh posel dayno shel zeh* — one litigant may invalidate the other's *dayan*. The *gemara* asks: *kol keminei depasil dayanei* — can anyone simply come along and disqualify a judge at will? This seems untenable. The *gemara* invokes Rabbi Yochanan's answer: *be'arkaot shebe'Surya shanu* — the *mishna* is speaking of non-professional courts, courts in which the *dayanim* are not *mumchin*. In those cases, a litigant may object. *Aval mumchin lo* — but for professional *dayanim*, no, one cannot simply disqualify them.

However, the *gemara* immediately challenges this reading from the *seifa* — the latter clause — of the *mishna* itself: *VeChachamim omrim eimatai bizman shemevi aleihem ra'ayah shehen kerovim o pesulim, aval im hayu kesherim umumchin mipi beit din eino yachol leposlan* — the *Chachamim* say that one may only disqualify where he brings proof that the *dayanim* are *kerovim* or *pesulim*, but if they are *kesherim* and *mumchin*, he cannot. This formulation implies that Rabbi Meir is indeed talking about *mumchin* — for the *Chachamim* respond specifically that *mumchin* cannot be disqualified. If Rabbi Meir were only talking about non-*mumchin*, the *Chachamim*'s response would be oddly framed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים, אֵימָתַי? בִּזְמַן שֶׁמֵּבִיא רְאָיָה שֶׁהֵן קְרוֹבִין אוֹ פְּסוּלִין. אֲבָל אִם הָיוּ כְּשֵׁרִין, אוֹ מוּמְחִין מִפִּי בֵּית דִּין – אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְפוֹסְלָן, מִכְּלָל דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר מוּמְחִין נָמֵי קָאָמַר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **From** the fact **that** the mishna **teaches** in **the latter clause: And the Rabbis say: When** can one of the litigants disqualify the judges? **When he brings evidence that they are related** to one of the litigants or to each other, **or** that they are **disqualified** from serving as judges for another reason; **but if they are fit** to serve as judges, **or are experts** ordained **by the court, he cannot disqualify them. By inference, Rabbi Meir is also speaking** of a court of **experts** when he says that one can refuse to be judged.

The *gemara* attempts to defend the *arkaot shebe'Surya* reading by reinterpreting the *Chachamim*'s clause: *aval im hayu kesherim na'asu kemumchin mipi beit din ve'eino yachol leposlan* — if they are otherwise *kosher*, they become *like* certified *mumchin* by virtue of a *beit din*'s endorsement, and therefore cannot be disqualified. Under this reading, Rabbi Meir is saying that even technically *kosher* non-*mumchin dayanim* can be invalidated by a litigant, while the *Chachamim* say that once a *beit din* has endorsed them as *like mumchin*, they cannot be touched.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֲבָל אִם הָיוּ כְּשֵׁרִין, נַעֲשׂוּ כְּמוּמְחִין מִפִּי בֵּית דִּין, וְאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְפוֹסְלָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This** is what the mishna is **saying: But if they are fit** to serve as judges **they are treated like experts** ordained **by the court, and he cannot disqualify them.**

The *gemara* proceeds to reject even this reading. A *baraita* is cited: *ta shema* — Rabbi Meir's opponents say to him, *lo kol heimenu sheposel dayan shemumche larabbim* — not everyone may disqualify a judge who is publicly acknowledged as an expert. This implies that Rabbi Meir is indeed speaking about *mumchin*, and even so he allows disqualification. A further formulation — *lo kol heimenu sheposel dayan shehimchu rabbim aleihem* — clarifies: this refers not to the most elite expert, but to one whom the community has collectively invested with judicial authority, even without formal expertise in *dinei mamonot*. The *baraita* then states explicitly: *le'olam posel vehole'ch ad sheyekabel alav beit din shemumche larabbim, divrei Rabbi Meir* — according to Rabbi Meir, a litigant may continue disqualifying proposed *dayanim* until a *beit din* that is publicly recognized as authoritative is agreed upon.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, לֹא כׇּל הֵימֶנּוּ שֶׁפּוֹסֵל דַּיָּין שֶׁמּוּמְחֶה לְרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a *baraita* that contradicts this interpretation: The Rabbis **said to Rabbi Meir: It is not within** a litigant's power **to disqualify a judge who** is accepted as **an expert for the public.** Apparently, Rabbi Meir disagrees with the Rabbis with regard to an expert judge.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: לְעוֹלָם פּוֹסֵל וְהוֹלֵךְ, עַד שֶׁיְּקַבֵּל עָלָיו בֵּית דִּין שֶׁמּוּמְחֶה לְרַבִּים. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **That** interpretation **is also taught** in a *baraita*, which teaches: A litigant **can continuously disqualify** the judges chosen by the other litigant **forever, until** the latter **accepts** to be judged by **a court that** is accepted as **an expert** court **for the public;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.** Clearly, Rabbi Meir concedes that one cannot refuse to have the case judged by an expert court.

This now makes it clear that the *arkaot shebe'Surya* reading cannot account for Rabbi Meir's position in the *mishna*. Rabbi Meir is speaking precisely about *mumchin*, and still permitting disqualification. The problem then sharpens when we turn to witnesses, since witnesses are always treated as *mumchin* by default — *eidim ke'mumchin dami* — and yet Rabbi Meir says *zeh posel edav shel zeh*: each litigant may invalidate the other's witnesses. If witnesses are automatically *mumchin*, and Rabbi Meir still allows invalidation, it cannot be that Rabbi Meir's *chiddush* is confined to non-*mumchin*. Even by *mumchin* — even by *dayanim* — Rabbi Meir must be saying the same thing.

### Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְהָא עֵדִים כְּמוּמְחִין דָּמֵי? וְאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: זֶה פּוֹסֵל עֵדָיו שֶׁל זֶה, וְזֶה פּוֹסֵל עֵדָיו שֶׁל זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But aren't witnesses like experts,** in that their standing is independent of their being accepted by the litigants? **And** yet **Rabbi Meir says: This** litigant **can disqualify the witnesses of that** litigant **and that** litigant **can disqualify the witnesses of this** litigant.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הָא אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: פֶּה קָדוֹשׁ יֹאמַר דָּבָר זֶה? תְּנִי: ״עֵדוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It was stated with regard to that** statement of Rabbi Meir that **Reish Lakish says: Would a holy mouth,** i.e., that of Rabbi Meir, **say this** strange **statement,** that a litigant can prevent a witness from testifying against him? Rather, emend the text of the mishna and **teach: His witness,** in the singular, meaning that a litigant can disqualify only a witness who testifies alone.

This leads to the striking intervention of Reish Lakish: *peh kadosh yomar davar zeh* — could a holy mouth such as Rabbi Meir say something so extreme, that a litigant may simply invalidate the other's *dayanim* and witnesses at will? Reish Lakish offers a radical re-reading: *tani edo edo* — read the *mishna* as saying *edo* (his one witness) rather than *edav* (his two witnesses). The *mishna* is not speaking about disqualifying multiple witnesses; it is speaking about one witness.

### One vs. Two Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* עֵדוֹ לְמַאי? אִילֵימָא לְמָמוֹן, רַחֲמָנָא פַּסְלֵיהּ! אִי לִשְׁבוּעָה, הֵימוֹנֵי מְהֵימַן כְּבֵי תְּרֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **For what** purpose does **his** single **witness** testify? **If we say** it is **to** testify about **monetary matters, the Merciful One** already **disqualifies him,** as by Torah law the testimony of only one witness is not accepted. **If** it is **to** render the defendant liable to take **an oath** that he does not owe the money being claimed, in this regard the testimony of one witness **is deemed credible like two** witnesses; the Torah states that if one witness supports the claim of the claimant, the defendant is required to take an oath that he does not owe the money.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* לְעוֹלָם לְמָמוֹן. לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּקַבְּלֵיהּ עֲלֵיהּ כְּבֵי תְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the purpose of the testimony is **to** testify about **monetary matters;** and **no,** one should not raise that challenge, as this ruling is **necessary** only in a case **where** the litigant initially **accepted upon himself** the single witness **as** equivalent to **two** witnesses. Afterward, he retracted his agreement to accept the single witness's testimony as if it were that of two witnesses. Rabbi Meir rules that his retraction is valid, and the testimony is not accepted.

The *gemara* immediately asks: *lemai* — to what end? *Ilima lemamon, Rachmana paslei* — if we are talking about monetary cases, a single witness is already invalidated by the Torah; one witness accomplishes nothing in a *din* of *mammon*. The *gemara* therefore suggests: *i lishvuah, heimnu heman ke'vei terei* — if we are talking about the case where a single witness obligates an oath (*shvuah*), then once the witness swears, he is believed like two witnesses — and in that case there would be no power to renege. The *gemara* therefore concludes: *le'olam lemamon, lo tzricha dekablei alei kevei terei* — we are in fact talking about a monetary case, but one in which the opposing litigant voluntarily accepted the single witness as though he were two witnesses. Just as a litigant may accept a *beit din* of non-*mumchin*, he may also accept a single witness in lieu of two. The *chiddush* of Rabbi Meir, according to Reish Lakish, is that even after making such an acceptance, and even after that witness has testified, the litigant may retract his acceptance. The *Chachamim* disagree: once the acceptance has been made, it cannot be revoked.

The scenario unfolds as follows: Reuven brings one witness. Shimon says, "I accept your single witness as valid." The witness testifies. Shimon then says, "I have changed my mind; I no longer accept him." Rabbi Meir says this retraction is effective. The *Chachamim* say it is not. One might observe that reneging in this situation is not without cost: the *dayanim* may well draw negative inferences from a litigant who rejects a witness only after hearing unfavorable testimony. Nevertheless, Rabbi Meir maintains the retraction is legally operative.

### Judge Acceptance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: דְּמָצֵי הָדַר בֵּיהּ? תְּנֵינָא: אָמַר לוֹ ״נֶאֱמָן עָלַי אַבָּא״, ״נֶאֱמָן עָלַי אָבִיךָ״, ״נֶאֱמָנִים עָלַי שְׁלֹשָׁה רוֹעֵי בָּקָר״ – רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר בּוֹ, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is this teaching us, that one can retract** this kind of agreement? **We learn** this in the following mishna (24a): If one of the litigants **says to** the other: **My father is trusted** to adjudicate **for me,** or: **Your father is trusted** to adjudicate **for me,** or: **Three cattle herders,** who are not proficient in *halakha*, **are trusted** to adjudicate **for me,** all of whom are legally disqualified from serving as judges, **Rabbi Meir says** that the one who made the offer **can retract it; and the Rabbis say** that **he cannot retract it,** and must accept their verdict.

The *gemara* asks: *mai kamashma lan* — what is the *chiddush* here, given that this principle seems already to have been taught in an earlier *mishna*? That *mishna* states: *amar lo ne'eman alai aba, ne'eman alai avicha, ne'emanin alai shlosha roei vakar* — if one litigant says to the other, "I accept your father as a *dayan*," or "I accept my father," or "I accept these three cattle-herders" (who are ordinarily unqualified), *Rabbi Meir omer yachol lachazor bo* — Rabbi Meir says he may retract. *VeChachamim omrim eino yachol lachazor bo* — and the *Chachamim* say he may not. This is identical in structure to what Reish Lakish read in our *mishna*. What, then, does our *mishna* add?

### Both Mishnayot

The *gemara* answers that both *mishnayot* are necessary. If we only had the *mishna* of *aba ve'avicha* — the father cases — we might have thought the *Chachamim* said he cannot retract specifically there because *aba ve'avicha*, for everyone else in the world who is not their child, would be *kesher* witnesses. Perhaps the *Chachamim* only restrict retraction when the accepted witness is one who is *kosher le'alma* — universally valid — just not for this particular litigant due to their familial relationship. But one might then think that for a single witness accepted as two — who is *pasul de'Oraita* for the entire world, not just for this litigant — even the *Chachamim* would agree with Rabbi Meir that retraction is possible. The case in our *mishna* is therefore necessary to teach that the *Chachamim* disallow retraction even there.

Conversely, if we only had our *mishna* — the case of one witness accepted as two — we might think Rabbi Meir only permits retraction in that case, since a single witness is universally *pasul* and the acceptance is inherently tenuous. But in the *aba ve'avicha* case, where the accepted individual would be *kosher* for everyone else, we might have thought Rabbi Meir agrees with the *Chachamim* that retraction is unavailable. The earlier *mishna* teaches that Rabbi Meir permits retraction even there. Both *mishnayot* are thus indispensable.

## Two Sets Witnesses

### Reject Reish Lakish

Ha midiktani reisha dayano vesifa eidav, alma davka katani — the *reisha* of the *mishna* uses the word *dayano* (his *dayan*) and the *seifa* uses *eidav* (his witnesses). The *mishna* is clearly deliberate and precise in its language, shifting terms intentionally. If Reish Lakish were correct that *eidav* really means *edo* — one witness — the *mishna*'s precision in distinguishing *dayano* from *eidav* would be undermined. The linguistic evidence points against Reish Lakish's emendation.

### Family Flaw

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: בְּבָא הוּא וְאַחֵר לְפוֹסְלָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said:** The mishna is referring to a case **where** the litigant **and another** person **come to disqualify** the witnesses, testifying that they are disqualified from bearing witness.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* כֹּל כְּמִינֵּיהּ? נוֹגֵעַ בְּעֵדוּתוֹ הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is it in his** power to testify that the witnesses of the other litigant are disqualified from bearing witness? **His testimony is** tainted by **a conflict of interest;** how can the court accept it?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁקָּרָא עָלָיו עַרְעָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, said:** The mishna is referring to a case **where he raised a challenge against** the witness's fitness to testify, which cannot be disregarded due to his conflict of interest.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* עַרְעָר דְּמַאי? אִילֵּימָא עַרְעָר דְּגַזְלָנוּתָא, כֹּל כְּמִינֵּיהּ? נוֹגֵעַ בְּעֵדוּתוֹ הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **A challenge over which** matter? **If we say** that he raises **a challenge** by testifying that the witness is guilty **of theft** or some other transgression, **is it in his** power to testify to that effect? **His testimony is** tainted by **a conflict of interest.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֶלָּא: עַרְעָר דִּפְגַם מִשְׁפָּחָה. רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: הָנֵי אַמִּשְׁפָּחָה קָמַסְהֲדִי, וְאִיהוּ מִמֵּילָא קָפָסֵיל. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: סוֹף סוֹף נוֹגֵעַ בְּעֵדוּתוֹ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is **a challenge over a family flaw.** The litigant testifies that the witness's lineage prevents him from testifying, as he is from a family of Canaanite slaves. **Rabbi Meir holds** that the litigant's testimony is accepted, as **these** witnesses, the litigant and the witness who testifies with him, **are testifying about the family** of the witness, **and** the witness **is disqualified indirectly.** The litigant is therefore not disqualified from giving this testimony due to his conflict of interest. **And the Rabbis hold** that since **his testimony is ultimately** tainted by **a conflict of interest,** it is not accepted.

Rabbi Elazar offers yet another explanation of our *mishna*. He says: *bevah hu ve'echad leposlan* — the litigant comes together with one other witness in order to disqualify the other's witnesses. That is, Shimon himself, along with a second witness, testifies that Reuven's witnesses are *pasul*. The immediate problem: *kol deminei nogea be'eduto* — Shimon is a *nogea badavar*, a party with a direct interest in the case. He stands to benefit from the disqualification of Reuven's witnesses, so how can he serve as one of the witnesses to their invalidity?

Rav Acha the son of Rav Ika answers: *kegon shekara alav arar* — this applies where there is already a public claim (*arar*) circulating that there is some flaw in the witness's status. Rashi defines *arar* here as a rumor — *shemetz shel pasul* — a hint or suggestion of *pesul* already in the air. The *gemara* then asks: *arar demai* — a rumor about what? If the rumor concerns theft — *arar degezelanuta* — Shimon is still *nogea be'eduto*, still a party with an interest. But if it is *arar depgam mishpacha* — a rumor concerning the witness's family status, perhaps that he is a slave, or that his lineage is not fully Jewish — then perhaps Shimon can bring a second witness to testify about this man's family.

### Family Bias Dispute

The *machloket* between Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* in this explanation turns on whether Shimon is still considered *nogea badavar* when he testifies about the witness's family. *Rabbi Meir savar hanei amishpacha ka mashadei vehu mimela ka pasil* — Rabbi Meir holds that Shimon is only testifying about the family; the disqualification of the witness follows automatically, as a byproduct. Since his testimony is about a matter external to the case itself — the man's lineage — he is not *nogea be'eduto* in a meaningful sense. *VeRabbanan savrei sof sof nogea be'eduto hu* — the *Chachamim* hold that at the end of the day, the practical effect of his testimony is to alter the outcome of his own case, and therefore he remains *nogea badavar* regardless of what he is formally testifying about.

### Rav Dimi Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַחֲלוֹקֶת בִּשְׁתֵּי כִּיתֵּי עֵדִים.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: The dispute is with regard to** a case in which one of the litigants claims that there are **two sets of witnesses** who can testify on his behalf, and the other litigant responds that one of the sets of witnesses is disqualified. In this case, the second litigant's claim is not tainted by a conflict of interest, as there is another set of witnesses who can testify against him in any event.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵר, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵר. אֲבָל בְּכַת אַחַת – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֵין יָכוֹל לְפוֹסְלָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir** maintains that the litigant cannot give this testimony, **as** Rabbi Meir **holds** that a litigant who claims in court to have a certain number of witnesses ready to testify on his behalf **is required to substantiate** that they exist. Therefore, there is no concern that the other set of witnesses will not be brought to court, causing that litigant to lose the case due to the disqualification of the first set. **And the Rabbis hold** that one **is not required to substantiate** the existence of witnesses he claims to have. As the disqualification of the first set of witnesses can cause the first litigant to lose the case, the second litigant is consequently unable to disqualify that first set, due to his conflict of interest. **But in** a case of **one set** of witnesses, **everyone agrees** that a litigant **cannot disqualify them,** due to his conflict of interest.

At this point, Rav Dimi arrives from Eretz Yisrael with yet another reading of the *mishna*. He brings in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *machloket bishtei kitei eidim* — the *machloket* between Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* concerns a case involving two sets of witnesses. *DeRabbi Meir savar tzarich levarer veRabbanan savrei eino tzakich levarer* — Rabbi Meir holds that examination and verification (*levarer*) is required, while the *Chachamim* hold it is not. There are several ways to understand this, and the *gemara* will proceed to elaborate through the framework of the Rambam's reading.

## Rambam: Four Witnesses

### Multiple Witnesses

The Rambam's explanation — which is the clearest and most coherent reading of this *sugya* — sets up the case as follows. Reuven and Shimon have a monetary dispute. Reuven brings two witnesses who testify in his favor. Subsequently, Reuven brings two additional witnesses who corroborate the first two. Shimon then claims that one or more of these four witnesses is *pasul*.

### R. Meir: Prove Kosher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמְרוּ לְפָנָיו רַב אַמֵּי וְרַב אַסִּי: אֵין שָׁם אֶלָּא כַּת אַחַת, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Dimi continued: **Rav Ami and Rav Asi said before** Rabbi Yoḥanan: If there **is only one set** of witnesses **there, what is** the *halakha*?

According to Rabbi Meir, *shomin lo* — we listen to Shimon's claim — *vene'emar leReuven hava re'ayah shehem kesherim* — and we require Reuven to prove that all four of his witnesses are *kosher*. The rationale: if Reuven had confidence in his witnesses, why would he bring two full sets? The very fact that he brought four witnesses testifying to the same matter suggests he knew one set was potentially compromised. Shimon's suspicion is therefore credible, and Reuven bears the burden of establishing the validity of his witnesses.

### Chachamim: No Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֵין שָׁם אֶלָּא כַּת אַחַת? וְהָאָמְרַתְּ: אֲבָל בְּכַת אַחַת דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֵין יָכוֹל לְפוֹסְלָן! אֶלָּא, נִמְצֵאת כַּת שְׁנִיָּה קְרוֹבִין אוֹ פְסוּלִין – מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interrupts: If there **is only one set** of witnesses **there,** what is the dilemma? **But didn't you** already **say: But in** a case of **one set** of witnesses, **everyone agrees** that a litigant **cannot disqualify them? Rather,** the question should be understood as follows: According to Rabbi Meir, who holds that a litigant can disqualify one of the other litigant's two sets of witnesses, if **the second set is** ultimately **found to be related** to one of the litigants or to each other, **or is disqualified** from bearing witness for other reasons, **what is** the *halakha*? Can the litigant whose witnesses were disqualified then claim that the other litigant was not capable of disqualifying the first set, as it turned out that they were the only witnesses who would be testifying against him?

The *Chachamim* are not troubled by this inference. They hold *eino tzakich levarer* — Reuven need not prove the *kashrus* of his witnesses. The fact that he brought two sets of witnesses does not imply knowledge of a defect in one of them; a litigant may simply wish to strengthen his case with additional testimony. Reuven is presumed to have brought four witnesses in good faith, and Shimon's unsupported claim cannot compel an investigation. *Aval bekat achat divrei hakol eino yachol leposlan* — if Reuven brought only one set of witnesses, everyone agrees that Shimon cannot challenge their *kashrus* without proof.

### R. Yochanan Clarif.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר לָהֶן: כְּבָר הֵעִידוּ עֵדִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: כְּבָר הֵעִידוּ עֵדִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to them: The first** pair of **witnesses,** i.e., the second litigant, who disqualified the other litigant's first set of witnesses, **already testified.** Since his testimony was valid at the time it cannot be disqualified afterward. **Some say** this statement in the name of a different *amora*: **Rav Ashi says: The first** pair of **witnesses already testified.**

The discussion continues in the context of Rav Dimi's report from Eretz Yisrael. Rav Ami and Rav Assi pose a question to Rabbi Yochanan: *ein sham ella kat achat mahu* — if only one set of witnesses is present, what is the *din*? Rabbi Yochanan notes that this has already been answered: with only one *kat*, everyone agrees the litigant cannot disqualify. The question is then refined: *ella nimtzeit kat sheniya krovim o psulim mahu* — what if Reuven brings two sets of witnesses, and one set turns out to be *pasul* or *karov* — can Shimon still press his claim and compel Reuven to prove the remaining two witnesses are *kosher*?

The Rambam suggests two possible readings of Rabbi Meir's position here. On one view, Rabbi Meir would say yes — since Reuven initially brought four witnesses, Shimon's suspicion remains operative even after one set is found to be invalid; Reuven must still prove the remaining two are *kesherim*. Alternatively, one might say that once we have identified *which* witnesses were *pasul*, the concern is resolved, and Reuven need not prove the validity of the remaining *kat*. The *gemara*'s resolution — *amar lahem kvar he'idu eidim harishonim*, the first witnesses have already testified — leaves some ambiguity, and Rashi notes it could be read either way. A parallel statement is attributed to Rav Ashi as well: *ika de'amri amar Rav Ashi kvar idu edut rishonim*.

## Bava Batra Parallel

### Document vs. Possess.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* נֵימָא, בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי וְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל קָמִיפַּלְגִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Shall we say** that Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis **disagree with regard to** the issue that is the subject of **the dispute between Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel?**

The *gemara* now proposes that the *machloket* between Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* in our *sugya* might parallel an older dispute from *Bava Batra*. *Nima biplugta deRabbi veRabban Shimon ben Gamliel ka miflegi* — perhaps Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* are disputing the same underlying principle as Rabbi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. The case from *Bava Batra*: a litigant comes to court with both a *shetar* (document of sale) and a claim of *chazaka* (three years of uninterrupted possession). These are analogous to two sets of witnesses: the *shetar* functions as one set of witnesses, and the *chazaka* as another.

### Rabbi: Document Wins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* דְּתַנְיָא: הַבָּא לִידּוֹן בִּשְׁטָר וּבַחֲזָקָה, נִידּוֹן בִּשְׁטָר – דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: נִידּוֹן בַּחֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who comes** to court **to be judged** concerning a claim against land that is in his possession, if in order to support his claim of ownership he comes to court both **with a deed** of purchase proving that the land is his **and with** a claim of **presumptive ownership** of the land stating that it had been in his possession for the past three years, which would render the deed superfluous, in this case his claim **is judged on the basis of the deed.** The court examines whether or not the deed is valid. This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** His claim **is judged on the basis of** his **presumptive ownership.**

*Haba lidon be'shetar uve'chazaka, nidon be'shetar, divrei Rabbi* — Rabbi holds that when a litigant presents both a *shetar* and *chazaka*, he must adjudicate through the *shetar*. The *shetar* is the stronger and more primary form of proof. By claiming both, the litigant implicitly concedes the *shetar* is his fundamental basis, and he must rely on it.

### Rabban Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְהָוֵינַן בַּהּ: בַּחֲזָקָה וְלֹא בִּשְׁטָר? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: אַף בַּחֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And we discussed** this dispute, asking: Can Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel possibly mean that the defendant's claim is judged **on the basis of** only his **presumptive ownership and not on the basis of** his **deed,** i.e., is his deed of purchase ignored? Clearly, there is no reason to ignore the deed of purchase. **Rather, say** that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel means that the defendant's claim is judged not only on the basis of the deed of purchase, but **even on the basis of** his **presumptive ownership.** In other words, either claim suffices.

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel nidon be'chazaka* — Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says he may use *chazaka*. The *gemara* asks: does Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel really mean the litigant *cannot* use the *shetar* if he has one? That would be strange. *Ela eima af be'chazaka* — rather, read it as: even *chazaka* suffices. He may use either the *shetar* or the *chazaka*; he is not compelled to produce the *shetar* simply because he mentioned it. The parallel to our *sugya* is thus: according to this reading, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds *eino tzarich levarer* — once the litigant has one valid form of proof, there is no obligation to verify the other. *VeRabbi Meir keRabbi* — Rabbi Meir in our case holds *tzarich levarer*, like Rabbi in *Bava Batra*: the existence of two sets of evidence creates an obligation to examine them both.

### Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* לָא. אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי. כִּי פְּלִיגִי, אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי. דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר כְּרַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No. According to** the opinion **of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, everyone agrees** that there is no need to substantiate the existence of all the types of evidence. **When** Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis **disagree** in the mishna it is **according to** the opinion **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **as Rabbi Meir** holds that one is required to substantiate the existence of all of his types of evidence, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְרַבָּנַן אָמְרִי לָךְ: עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי הָתָם אֶלָּא בַּחֲזָקָה, דְּמִכֹּחַ שְׁטָרָא קָאָתֵי. אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּהָנֵי עֵדִים לָאו מִכֹּחַ עֵדִים אַחֲרִינֵי קָאָתוּ, אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי מוֹדֶה דְּאֵין צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis** could have **said to you** that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says there** that one is required to substantiate the existence of his types of evidence **only vis-à-vis** a claim of **presumptive ownership** of the land. If one claims that he possesses the deed of sale for his land he cannot rely on his presumptive ownership, **as** the power of his presumptive ownership **comes from the power of the deed;** mere possession without an accompanying claim of how one became the owner is disregarded (see *Bava Batra* 41a). **But here,** in the case of two sets of witnesses, **since** the power of **these witnesses does not come from the power of the other witnesses, even Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **concedes that one is not required to substantiate** the existence of the other set of witnesses.

The *gemara* ultimately rejects this proposed alignment. *Lo, aliba deRabban Shimon ben Gamliel kuli alma lo peligi* — both Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* in our case agree with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. *Peligi aliba deRabbi* — the *machloket* between Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* is entirely within the framework of Rabbi's position. *DeRabbi Meir keRabbi* — Rabbi Meir agrees with Rabbi that *tzarich levarer*. *VeRabbanan amri lach, ad kan lo ka'amar Rabbi hatam, ela be'chazaka demikoach shtara ka'atei* — the *Chachamim* say: Rabbi only required verification in *Bava Batra* because the *chazaka* there *emerged from* the *shetar*. There is an A-then-B relationship: the *chazaka* is grounded in and derived from the *shetar*, and therefore when a litigant claims *chazaka*, he is implicitly relying on the *shetar* as its source. Rabbi says we must therefore examine the *shetar* directly.

### Independent Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּהָנֵי עֵדִים לָאו מִכֹּחַ עֵדִים אַחֲרִינֵי קָאָתוּ, אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי מוֹדֶה דְּאֵין צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **But here,** in the case of two sets of witnesses, **since** the power of **these witnesses does not come from the power of the other witnesses, even Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **concedes that one is not required to substantiate** the existence of the other set of witnesses.

*Aval hakha, dehanei eidim lav mikoach eidim achrinei ka'atei, afilu Rabbi modeh de'eino tzarich levarer* — but in our case, two sets of witnesses are not A-then-B. One set of witnesses did not produce or generate the other. They are two independent, parallel testimonies. Therefore, even Rabbi — say the *Chachamim* — would agree that when a litigant brings two independent sets of witnesses, there is no obligation to verify both sets. The existence of one valid *kat* is sufficient; the other can simply be disregarded. There is no structural dependency between them that would obligate further examination.
