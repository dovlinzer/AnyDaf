# Sanhedrin 23 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Perek III Intro

### Context & Opening

Sanhedrin 23a opens the third *perek* of the tractate, and in doing so circles back to the very beginning of *Masechet Sanhedrin*. The first three words of the new *mishna* — *dinei mamonot bishlisha*, monetary cases require three judges — are of course familiar, since that is precisely how the entire tractate began. The *Rambam* explains that the reason this phrase recurs here is that the second *perek* took us on something of a detour, addressing the relationship of the *kohen gadol* and the *melech* to the *beit din*. Now we return to *dinei mamonot bishlisha*, and this *perek* will focus on a different question: how does one actually constitute such a court? Who gets to be among the three who sit in judgment over these monetary cases?

### Mishna Translation

The *mishna* itself is not entirely clear, and as it turns out, the *gemara* will spend considerable time — across multiple *amudim* — simply trying to determine what the *mishna* means. For that reason, it is worth reading and translating it first before attempting any deeper analysis.

*Dinei mamonot bishlisha* — monetary cases require three *dayanim*. *Zeh borer lo echad vezeh borer lo echad* — this litigant chooses one, and the other litigant chooses one. The *gemara* will address what exactly "one" refers to. *Ushneihem borerim lahen od echad* — and the two of them choose one more. The question of who precisely "the two of them" refers to will likewise depend on how the *mishna* is read. *Divrei Rabbi Meir* — this is Rabbi Meir's position. *Vachachamim omrim shnei dayanim borerim lahen od echad* — the Sages say that the two *dayanim* choose one additional *dayan*. From this formulation, it becomes clear that in the *reisha*, "the two of them" who each choose one *dayan* must be the two litigants — since the *Chachamim* explicitly say it is the two *dayanim* (not the litigants) who choose the third.

*Zeh posel dayno shel zeh vezeh posel dayno shel zeh, divrei Rabbi Meir* — this litigant can invalidate the other's *dayan*, and vice versa; this is Rabbi Meir's view. *VeChachamim omrim eimatai bizman shemevi aleihem ra'ayah shehen kerovim o pesulim* — the Sages say: when can he do this? Only when he brings proof that the *dayan* is related to the other litigant, or is *pasul* for some other reason. *Aval im hayu kesherim, eino yachol leposlan* — but if they are *kosher*, he cannot disqualify them.

The *mishna* continues with a parallel ruling regarding witnesses. *Zeh posel ido shel zeh vezeh posel ido shel zeh, divrei Rabbi Meir* — each litigant can invalidate the other's witness; this is Rabbi Meir's view. The *Chachamim* repeat their position: only when he can prove they are related or *pesulim* for another reason; but if they are *kesherim*, he cannot *posel* them.

## Mai Zeh Borer

### Gemara Question

The *gemara* opens with the question *mai zeh borer lo echad vezeh borer lo echad, bitlata sagi* — what does this mean, "this one chooses one and this one chooses one"? Three *dayanim* would suffice; why does the *mishna* speak of this elaborate procedure of choosing? According to Rashi's reading, the *gemara*'s initial assumption is that *echad* in the *mishna* means one entire *beit din* of three judges — meaning each litigant selects a full *beit din*, yielding six judges, and then together they choose a third *beit din*, yielding nine. The *gemara* finds this puzzling: we only need three.

---

## Perek III Intro…

### Rashi: Full Beit Din

Rashi explains the *pshat* of the *mishna* as follows: *zeh borer lo beit din echad shehem shlosha, vezeh echad, harei shisha* — this one chooses one full *beit din* of three, and the other chooses another, giving six. *Ushneihem borerim lahen od echad, harei tisha* — and together they choose a third *beit din*, giving nine. And that, says Rashi, is precisely the force of the *gemara*'s surprised question: *mai zeh borer, ha bitlata sagi* — why go through all of this when three judges are sufficient?

### Tosafot Dispute

Tosafot disagree with Rashi's reading of the *mishna*'s *pshat*. They maintain that the *gemara* knows from the outset that *echad* simply means one *dayan*, not one *beit din*, and that the straightforward reading of the *mishna* is that each litigant chooses one judge and together they choose a third. According to Tosafot, the *gemara*'s question is not asked out of genuine puzzlement about the *mishna*'s meaning; rather, it is posed as a kind of leading question in order to introduce an additional ruling — namely, the scenario in which one litigant selects a *beit din* and the other rejects it, and they must then choose a third *beit din* together. The *gemara* asks the question in order to bring that case as an answer, even though it already knows that is not the primary *pshat* of the *mishna*.

---

## Mai Zeh Borer…

### Beit Din Rejection

With this framework in place, the *gemara* proceeds to answer its own question. *Hachi ka'amar* — this is what the *mishna* means: when Reuven selects a *beit din* and Shimon objects, and then Shimon proposes a different *beit din* and Reuven objects, neither of those first two courts is used. Instead, *shneihem borerim lahen od echad* — together they choose a third *beit din*. This is the scenario the *gemara* had in mind when it asked *mai zeh borer*.

### Debtor Power Issue

The *gemara*, however, immediately raises a difficulty against this proposed answer. It notes the apparent contradiction with a ruling of Rav Elazar cited later in the tractate: *lo shanu ela malve, aval love kofin oto vedan be'iro* — the rule that a litigant may reject the other's *beit din* applies only to the creditor (*malve*), but the debtor (*love*) is compelled to appear before the court in his own city. If the *love* has no power to reject the *malve*'s *beit din*, how can the *mishna* contemplate a scenario in which the *love* does exactly that?

### Rabbi Elazar Ruling

Rav Elazar's ruling thus creates a problem for the reading of the *mishna* just proposed: the *mishna* seems to assume that either litigant may reject the other's court, while Rav Elazar limits that power to the creditor alone.

### R. Yochanan: Non-Expert

The *gemara* resolves this tension through Rabbi Yochanan's ruling in a different context: *be'arkaot shebe'Surya shanu* — our *mishna* is speaking specifically about non-professional courts. When the *beit din* consists of three *dayanim* who are not *mumchin* — who are not well-versed experts in *dinei mamonot* — then even the *love* can legitimately object, because there is valid grounds for preferring a different court. The *love* is not acting capriciously; he has a substantive reason to reject *dayanim* who are not professionally competent. *Aval mumchin lo* — but if the *dayanim* are expert professionals, the *love* cannot reject the creditor's court, and we revert to Rav Elazar's ruling.

### Rav Pappa Clarif.

Rav Pappa offers a further nuance. Even if one assumes the *mishna* is speaking of expert courts — *afilu teima mumchin*, such as the courts of Rav Huna and Rav Chisda — the *love* may still object, on the grounds that *mi ka matrachna lach* — I am not causing you a significant burden (*tircha*) by asking you to go to the other *beit din*, because both courts are equally accessible. Rashi explains that Rav Elazar's ruling — that the *love* cannot impose on the *malve* — is premised on the *love* causing undue *tircha*, such as requiring the *malve* to travel to a different city. But where both courts are located in the same city, no such *tircha* is imposed, and even the *love* may insist on a different court. Tosafot extend this somewhat, suggesting that even courts within a certain distance — less than three *parsaot* — would satisfy this condition.

---

## Reject Beit Din Interp.

### Logic Problem

Having explored the reading of the *mishna* as referring to *batei din*, the *gemara* now returns to reject this interpretation entirely. The *mishna* states: *vachachamim omrim shnei dayanim borerim lahen od echad* — the Sages say the two *dayanim* choose a third. If the earlier part of the *mishna* meant that each litigant chose an entire *beit din*, it would make no sense for those two *batei din* — having rejected each other — to then convene together and choose a third *beit din*. *Beit din batar depasli lehu azlu uvararu lehu beit dina achrina* — two courts that have just invalidated each other are not going to collaborate on selecting a third. The scenario is logically absurd.

### One Judge Each

There is also a linguistic problem with the *beit din* reading. The *mishna* uses the word *echad* — one. One does not mean one *beit din*; *echad* means one *dayan*. The *gemara* therefore concludes: *ela hachi ka'amar* — this is what the *mishna* actually means. *Keshezeh borer lo dayan echad vezeh borer lo dayan echad, shneihem borerim lahen od echad* — when this litigant chooses one *dayan* and the other litigant chooses one *dayan*, the two of them together choose a third.

### Fairness Purpose

The *gemara* then asks *mai shena de'avdi hachi* — why is this the proper procedure? The answer comes from the *amora'im* in the West (*amri bema'arava*) in the name of Rabbi Zeira: *mitoch shezeh borer lo dayan echad vezeh borer lo dayan echad ushneihem borerim lahen od echad, yetzei hadin la'amito* — because this arrangement produces the truest and most reliable judicial outcome. Both Rashi and the Rambam emphasize that this structure is the surest guarantee of a fair result, and it also puts the losing litigant's mind at ease. Whoever loses will be able to say: I chose my own *dayan*, and he tried his best to rule in my favor; the fact that the court ruled against me means the *din* must be correct.

---

## Rabbi Meir Dispute

### Judges' Knowledge

With the *pshat* of the *mishna* now established — each litigant chooses one *dayan*, and together they choose a third — the *gemara* turns to the *machloket* between Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* regarding who selects that third *dayan*. The *gemara* wonders whether this dispute might be rooted in an earlier *machloket* between Rabbi Yehuda in the name of Rav: *ein ha'eidim chotmin al hashtar ela im ken yod'im mi chotam imahen* — witnesses should not sign a document unless they know who else is signing with them. Rashi explains the reason: *shema yachtimu imahem ed echad pasul* — lest an invalid witness sign alongside them, thereby invalidating the entire testimony and causing them embarrassment.

This ruling is supported by a *baraita*: *kach hayu niki'ei hada'at shebe'Yerushalaim osin* — this was the practice of those of refined sensibility in Jerusalem. They would not sign a document unless they knew who was signing with them; they would not sit in judgment unless they knew who their fellow *dayanim* would be; and they would not enter a *se'udah* unless they knew with whom they would be *meisav*. The first two practices follow naturally from legal and halakhic concerns. The third — not dining without knowing one's company — reflects something beyond the strictly legal: the importance of the company one keeps, the dignity associated with a formal meal in rabbinic culture. Rashi's comment suggests this reflects concern for the honor of Torah scholars, though the text uses the term *niki'ei hada'at* rather than *chachamim*, which raises the question of whether this is a distinct category with only partial overlap.

### R. Meir: Full Consent

The *gemara* proposes that the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* tracks precisely this earlier *machloket*. Rabbi Meir — who holds that both litigants together choose the third *dayan* — would *not* hold like Rabbi Yehuda's ruling in the name of Rav. In other words, Rabbi Meir is not concerned that the two *dayanim* might be embarrassed by the third turning out to be *pasul*. Rather, Rabbi Meir's position reflects a different concern: that the litigants themselves must have full buy-in (*da'at ba'alei dinim*) into the composition of the *beit din*. According to Rabbi Meir, a *beit din* requires the consent of both litigants to all three *dayanim*; without that, the court lacks proper authority.

### Chachamim: Judges Agree

The *gemara* rejects this proposed alignment. *De'chulei alma is lehu deRabbi Yehuda amar Rav* — everyone agrees with Rabbi Yehuda's ruling in the name of Rav that witnesses (and by extension *dayanim*) must know who their counterparts are. The dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* is not about whether the *dayanim* need to know who the third *dayan* is — all agree they do — but rather about whether the *litigants* also need to know and consent to the third *dayan*. *Rabbi Meir savar da'at ba'alei dinim nami ba'inan* — Rabbi Meir holds that the litigants' consent is also required. *VeRabbanan savri da'at hadayanim ba'inan da'at ba'alei dinim lo ba'inan* — the *Chachamim* hold that the *dayanim*'s agreement on the third is sufficient; the litigants need not consent.

This surfaces a broader theme that runs throughout this entire *sugya*: whence does a *beit din* derive its authority? If three people simply sit down and declare themselves a *beit din*, does that automatically grant them judicial power? The emerging principle seems to be that a *beit din* of recognized professionals (*mumchin*) established in a town possesses authority automatically. But where the *dayanim* are not *mumchin*, the court requires the buy-in of the litigants — without it, the *beit din* has no real power over them.

### Language Analysis

The *gemara* then cites the later part of the *mishna* to further examine this question — *gufah*, returning to the *baraita* quoting Rabbi Yehuda's ruling — *ein ha'eidim chotmin al hashtar*, and confirming that this is cited as support. At this point the *gemara* begins addressing the second part of the *mishna* — the disqualification of witnesses — a subject which introduces its own complexities.

---

## Witness Disqualif.

### Disqualif. Problem

The *mishna* states, according to Rabbi Meir, that *zeh posel dayno shel zeh* — one litigant may invalidate the other's *dayan*. The *gemara* asks: *kol keminei depasil dayanei* — can anyone simply come along and disqualify a judge at will? This seems untenable. The *gemara* invokes Rabbi Yochanan's answer: *be'arkaot shebe'Surya shanu* — the *mishna* is speaking of non-professional courts, courts in which the *dayanim* are not *mumchin*. In those cases, a litigant may object. *Aval mumchin lo* — but for professional *dayanim*, no, one cannot simply disqualify them.

However, the *gemara* immediately challenges this reading from the *seifa* — the latter clause — of the *mishna* itself: *VeChachamim omrim eimatai bizman shemevi aleihem ra'ayah shehen kerovim o pesulim, aval im hayu kesherim umumchin mipi beit din eino yachol leposlan* — the *Chachamim* say that one may only disqualify where he brings proof that the *dayanim* are *kerovim* or *pesulim*, but if they are *kesherim* and *mumchin*, he cannot. This formulation implies that Rabbi Meir is indeed talking about *mumchin* — for the *Chachamim* respond specifically that *mumchin* cannot be disqualified. If Rabbi Meir were only talking about non-*mumchin*, the *Chachamim*'s response would be oddly framed.

The *gemara* attempts to defend the *arkaot shebe'Surya* reading by reinterpreting the *Chachamim*'s clause: *aval im hayu kesherim na'asu kemumchin mipi beit din ve'eino yachol leposlan* — if they are otherwise *kosher*, they become *like* certified *mumchin* by virtue of a *beit din*'s endorsement, and therefore cannot be disqualified. Under this reading, Rabbi Meir is saying that even technically *kosher* non-*mumchin dayanim* can be invalidated by a litigant, while the *Chachamim* say that once a *beit din* has endorsed them as *like mumchin*, they cannot be touched.

The *gemara* proceeds to reject even this reading. A *baraita* is cited: *ta shema* — Rabbi Meir's opponents say to him, *lo kol heimenu sheposel dayan shemumche larabbim* — not everyone may disqualify a judge who is publicly acknowledged as an expert. This implies that Rabbi Meir is indeed speaking about *mumchin*, and even so he allows disqualification. A further formulation — *lo kol heimenu sheposel dayan shehimchu rabbim aleihem* — clarifies: this refers not to the most elite expert, but to one whom the community has collectively invested with judicial authority, even without formal expertise in *dinei mamonot*. The *baraita* then states explicitly: *le'olam posel vehole'ch ad sheyekabel alav beit din shemumche larabbim, divrei Rabbi Meir* — according to Rabbi Meir, a litigant may continue disqualifying proposed *dayanim* until a *beit din* that is publicly recognized as authoritative is agreed upon.

This now makes it clear that the *arkaot shebe'Surya* reading cannot account for Rabbi Meir's position in the *mishna*. Rabbi Meir is speaking precisely about *mumchin*, and still permitting disqualification. The problem then sharpens when we turn to witnesses, since witnesses are always treated as *mumchin* by default — *eidim ke'mumchin dami* — and yet Rabbi Meir says *zeh posel edav shel zeh*: each litigant may invalidate the other's witnesses. If witnesses are automatically *mumchin*, and Rabbi Meir still allows invalidation, it cannot be that Rabbi Meir's *chiddush* is confined to non-*mumchin*. Even by *mumchin* — even by *dayanim* — Rabbi Meir must be saying the same thing.

### Reish Lakish

This leads to the striking intervention of Reish Lakish: *peh kadosh yomar davar zeh* — could a holy mouth such as Rabbi Meir say something so extreme, that a litigant may simply invalidate the other's *dayanim* and witnesses at will? Reish Lakish offers a radical re-reading: *tani edo edo* — read the *mishna* as saying *edo* (his one witness) rather than *edav* (his two witnesses). The *mishna* is not speaking about disqualifying multiple witnesses; it is speaking about one witness.

### One vs. Two Witnesses

The *gemara* immediately asks: *lemai* — to what end? *Ilima lemamon, Rachmana paslei* — if we are talking about monetary cases, a single witness is already invalidated by the Torah; one witness accomplishes nothing in a *din* of *mammon*. The *gemara* therefore suggests: *i lishvuah, heimnu heman ke'vei terei* — if we are talking about the case where a single witness obligates an oath (*shvuah*), then once the witness swears, he is believed like two witnesses — and in that case there would be no power to renege. The *gemara* therefore concludes: *le'olam lemamon, lo tzricha dekablei alei kevei terei* — we are in fact talking about a monetary case, but one in which the opposing litigant voluntarily accepted the single witness as though he were two witnesses. Just as a litigant may accept a *beit din* of non-*mumchin*, he may also accept a single witness in lieu of two. The *chiddush* of Rabbi Meir, according to Reish Lakish, is that even after making such an acceptance, and even after that witness has testified, the litigant may retract his acceptance. The *Chachamim* disagree: once the acceptance has been made, it cannot be revoked.

The scenario unfolds as follows: Reuven brings one witness. Shimon says, "I accept your single witness as valid." The witness testifies. Shimon then says, "I have changed my mind; I no longer accept him." Rabbi Meir says this retraction is effective. The *Chachamim* say it is not. One might observe that reneging in this situation is not without cost: the *dayanim* may well draw negative inferences from a litigant who rejects a witness only after hearing unfavorable testimony. Nevertheless, Rabbi Meir maintains the retraction is legally operative.

### Judge Acceptance

The *gemara* asks: *mai kamashma lan* — what is the *chiddush* here, given that this principle seems already to have been taught in an earlier *mishna*? That *mishna* states: *amar lo ne'eman alai aba, ne'eman alai avicha, ne'emanin alai shlosha roei vakar* — if one litigant says to the other, "I accept your father as a *dayan*," or "I accept my father," or "I accept these three cattle-herders" (who are ordinarily unqualified), *Rabbi Meir omer yachol lachazor bo* — Rabbi Meir says he may retract. *VeChachamim omrim eino yachol lachazor bo* — and the *Chachamim* say he may not. This is identical in structure to what Reish Lakish read in our *mishna*. What, then, does our *mishna* add?

### Both Mishnayot

*Tzricha* — both *mishnayot* are necessary. If we only had the *mishna* of *aba ve'avicha* — the father cases — we might have thought the *Chachamim* said he cannot retract specifically there because *aba ve'avicha*, for everyone else in the world who is not their child, would be *kesher* witnesses. Perhaps the *Chachamim* only restrict retraction when the accepted witness is one who is *kosher le'alma* — universally valid — just not for this particular litigant due to their familial relationship. But one might then think that for a single witness accepted as two — who is *pasul de'Oraita* for the entire world, not just for this litigant — even the *Chachamim* would agree with Rabbi Meir that retraction is possible. The case in our *mishna* is therefore necessary to teach that the *Chachamim* disallow retraction even there.

Conversely, if we only had our *mishna* — the case of one witness accepted as two — we might think Rabbi Meir only permits retraction in that case, since a single witness is universally *pasul* and the acceptance is inherently tenuous. But in the *aba ve'avicha* case, where the accepted individual would be *kosher* for everyone else, we might have thought Rabbi Meir agrees with the *Chachamim* that retraction is unavailable. The earlier *mishna* teaches that Rabbi Meir permits retraction even there. Both *mishnayot* are thus indispensable.

## Two Sets Witnesses

### Reject Reish Lakish

Having explored Reish Lakish's reading, the *gemara* now formally rejects it. *Ha midiktani reisha dayano vesifa eidav, alma davka katani* — the *reisha* of the *mishna* uses the word *dayano* (his *dayan*) and the *seifa* uses *eidav* (his witnesses). The *mishna* is clearly deliberate and precise in its language, shifting terms intentionally. If Reish Lakish were correct that *eidav* really means *edo* — one witness — the *mishna*'s precision in distinguishing *dayano* from *eidav* would be undermined. The linguistic evidence points against Reish Lakish's emendation.

### Family Flaw

Rabbi Elazar offers yet another explanation of our *mishna*. He says: *bevah hu ve'echad leposlan* — the litigant comes together with one other witness in order to disqualify the other's witnesses. That is, Shimon himself, along with a second witness, testifies that Reuven's witnesses are *pasul*. The immediate problem: *kol deminei nogea be'eduto* — Shimon is a *nogea badavar*, a party with a direct interest in the case. He stands to benefit from the disqualification of Reuven's witnesses, so how can he serve as one of the witnesses to their invalidity?

Rav Acha the son of Rav Ika answers: *kegon shekara alav arar* — this applies where there is already a public claim (*arar*) circulating that there is some flaw in the witness's status. Rashi defines *arar* here as a rumor — *shemetz shel pasul* — a hint or suggestion of *pesul* already in the air. The *gemara* then asks: *arar demai* — a rumor about what? If the rumor concerns theft — *arar degezelanuta* — Shimon is still *nogea be'eduto*, still a party with an interest. But if it is *arar depgam mishpacha* — a rumor concerning the witness's family status, perhaps that he is a slave, or that his lineage is not fully Jewish — then perhaps Shimon can bring a second witness to testify about this man's family.

### Family Bias Dispute

The *machloket* between Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* in this explanation turns on whether Shimon is still considered *nogea badavar* when he testifies about the witness's family. *Rabbi Meir savar hanei amishpacha ka mashadei vehu mimela ka pasil* — Rabbi Meir holds that Shimon is only testifying about the family; the disqualification of the witness follows automatically, as a byproduct. Since his testimony is about a matter external to the case itself — the man's lineage — he is not *nogea be'eduto* in a meaningful sense. *VeRabbanan savrei sof sof nogea be'eduto hu* — the *Chachamim* hold that at the end of the day, the practical effect of his testimony is to alter the outcome of his own case, and therefore he remains *nogea badavar* regardless of what he is formally testifying about.

### Rav Dimi Intro

At this point, Rav Dimi arrives from Eretz Yisrael with yet another reading of the *mishna*. He brings in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *machloket bishtei kitei eidim* — the *machloket* between Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* concerns a case involving two sets of witnesses. *DeRabbi Meir savar tzarich levarer veRabbanan savrei eino tzarich levarer* — Rabbi Meir holds that examination and verification (*levarer*) is required, while the *Chachamim* hold it is not. There are several ways to understand this, and the *gemara* will proceed to elaborate through the framework of the Rambam's reading.

---

## Rambam: Four Witnesses

### Multiple Witnesses

The Rambam's explanation — which is the clearest and most coherent reading of this *sugya* — sets up the case as follows. Reuven and Shimon have a monetary dispute. Reuven brings two witnesses who testify in his favor. Subsequently, Reuven brings two additional witnesses who corroborate the first two. Shimon then claims that one or more of these four witnesses is *pasul*.

### R. Meir: Prove Kosher

According to Rabbi Meir, *shomin lo* — we listen to Shimon's claim — *vene'emar leReuven hava re'ayah shehem kesherim* — and we require Reuven to prove that all four of his witnesses are *kosher*. The rationale: if Reuven had confidence in his witnesses, why would he bring two full sets? The very fact that he brought four witnesses testifying to the same matter suggests he knew one set was potentially compromised. Shimon's suspicion is therefore credible, and Reuven bears the burden of establishing the validity of his witnesses.

### Chachamim: No Proof

The *Chachamim* are not troubled by this inference. They hold *eino tzarich levarer* — Reuven need not prove the *kashrus* of his witnesses. The fact that he brought two sets of witnesses does not imply knowledge of a defect in one of them; a litigant may simply wish to strengthen his case with additional testimony. Reuven is presumed to have brought four witnesses in good faith, and Shimon's unsupported claim cannot compel an investigation. *Aval bekat achat divrei hakol eino yachol leposlan* — if Reuven brought only one set of witnesses, everyone agrees that Shimon cannot challenge their *kashrus* without proof.

### R. Yochanan Clarif.

The discussion continues in the context of Rav Dimi's report from Eretz Yisrael. Rav Ami and Rav Assi pose a question to Rabbi Yochanan: *ein sham ella kat achat mahu* — if only one set of witnesses is present, what is the *din*? Rabbi Yochanan notes that this has already been answered: with only one *kat*, everyone agrees the litigant cannot disqualify. The question is then refined: *ella nimtzeit kat sheniya krovim o psulim mahu* — what if Reuven brings two sets of witnesses, and one set turns out to be *pasul* or *karov* — can Shimon still press his claim and compel Reuven to prove the remaining two witnesses are *kosher*?

The Rambam suggests two possible readings of Rabbi Meir's position here. On one view, Rabbi Meir would say yes — since Reuven initially brought four witnesses, Shimon's suspicion remains operative even after one set is found to be invalid; Reuven must still prove the remaining two are *kesherim*. Alternatively, one might say that once we have identified *which* witnesses were *pasul*, the concern is resolved, and Reuven need not prove the validity of the remaining *kat*. The *gemara*'s resolution — *amar lahem kvar he'idu eidim harishonim*, the first witnesses have already testified — leaves some ambiguity, and Rashi notes it could be read either way. A parallel statement is attributed to Rav Ashi as well: *ika de'amri amar Rav Ashi kvar idu edut rishonim*.

## Bava Batra Parallel

### Document vs. Possess.

The *gemara* now proposes that the *machloket* between Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* in our *sugya* might parallel an older dispute from *Bava Batra*. *Nima biplugta deRabbi veRabban Shimon ben Gamliel ka miflegi* — perhaps Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* are disputing the same underlying principle as Rabbi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. The case from *Bava Batra*: a litigant comes to court with both a *shetar* (document of sale) and a claim of *chazaka* (three years of uninterrupted possession). These are analogous to two sets of witnesses: the *shetar* functions as one set of witnesses, and the *chazaka* as another.

### Rabbi: Document Wins

*Haba lidon be'shetar uve'chazaka, nidon be'shetar, divrei Rabbi* — Rabbi holds that when a litigant presents both a *shetar* and *chazaka*, he must adjudicate through the *shetar*. The *shetar* is the stronger and more primary form of proof. By claiming both, the litigant implicitly concedes the *shetar* is his fundamental basis, and he must rely on it.

### Rabban Shimon

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel nidon be'chazaka* — Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says he may use *chazaka*. The *gemara* asks: does Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel really mean the litigant *cannot* use the *shetar* if he has one? That would be strange. *Ela eima af be'chazaka* — rather, read it as: even *chazaka* suffices. He may use either the *shetar* or the *chazaka*; he is not compelled to produce the *shetar* simply because he mentioned it. The parallel to our *sugya* is thus: according to this reading, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds *eino tzarich levarer* — once the litigant has one valid form of proof, there is no obligation to verify the other. *VeRabbi Meir keRabbi* — Rabbi Meir in our case holds *tzarich levarer*, like Rabbi in *Bava Batra*: the existence of two sets of evidence creates an obligation to examine them both.

### Reconciliation

The *gemara* ultimately rejects this proposed alignment. *Lo, aliba deRabban Shimon ben Gamliel kuli alma lo peligi* — both Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* in our case agree with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. *Peligi aliba deRabbi* — the *machloket* between Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* is entirely within the framework of Rabbi's position. *DeRabbi Meir keRabbi* — Rabbi Meir agrees with Rabbi that *tzarich levarer*. *VeRabbanan amri lach, ad kan lo ka'amar Rabbi hatam, ela be'chazaka demikoach shtara ka'atei* — the *Chachamim* say: Rabbi only required verification in *Bava Batra* because the *chazaka* there *emerged from* the *shetar*. There is an A-then-B relationship: the *chazaka* is grounded in and derived from the *shetar*, and therefore when a litigant claims *chazaka*, he is implicitly relying on the *shetar* as its source. Rabbi says we must therefore examine the *shetar* directly.

### Independent Witnesses

*Aval hakha, dehanei eidim lav mikoach eidim achrinei ka'atei, afilu Rabbi modeh de'eino tzarich levarer* — but in our case, two sets of witnesses are not A-then-B. One set of witnesses did not produce or generate the other. They are two independent, parallel testimonies. Therefore, even Rabbi — say the *Chachamim* — would agree that when a litigant brings two independent sets of witnesses, there is no obligation to verify both sets. The existence of one valid *kat* is sufficient; the other can simply be disregarded. There is no structural dependency between them that would obligate further examination.
