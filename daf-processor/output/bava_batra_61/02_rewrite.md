# Bava Batra 61 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Context

### Perek Overview

With today's daf we begin a new *perek*, *Hamocher Et Habayit* — "One Who Sells a House." This chapter represents a notable shift in character from the previous one. Where the preceding *perek* engaged with a range of abstract concepts — *chazaka*, standards of proof, rules of *eidut* — the present chapter is considerably more concrete and practical: when you say you are selling your house, what exactly is included in that sale? When you say you are selling a cow or a horse, what comes along with it, absent any specification to the contrary? This is the animating question of the chapter, and it generates a richly detailed body of law.

### Agadeta Reflection

Before proceeding, it is worth pausing briefly on the *agadeta* at the end of the previous *perek*, which we had to rush through. What emerged from it was, for the most part, a remarkably balanced vision: we are obligated to maintain *aveilus* on the *Beit HaMikdash*, yet we are equally obligated to live our lives fully. The teaching does not demand a life of obsessive mourning — perhaps ninety-nine percent of one's life is lived fully, with a small *zeicher* woven in. The very end of the *agadeta*, however, struck a far more somber and almost nihilistic note, one that is genuinely difficult to reckon with. But before that jarring conclusion, the balance articulated there is, in fact, the one we actually follow in practice.

### Chazka & Churban

There is an interesting thematic resonance worth noting as we transition into this new chapter. The entire previous *perek* was about *chezkas habatim* — establishing a kind of permanence of ownership, asserting that this land is mine, that I have been here three years, that I have a right to it. And yet, as we noted right at the opening of that *perek*, one of the foundational ideas surrounding *chazaka* — including the very question of whether one had a *shtar* — was derived from the *nevua* in *Yirmiyah*: go ahead and write *shtaros* and place them in a *kli cheres*, *lema'an ya'amdu yamim rabbim*, for you are going into *galus*. You are going to be living in *chutz la'aretz*, and who knows when you will return to *Eretz Yisrael*.

There is something worth sitting with there. All of these weighty discussions about proving whose land is whose, about asserting ownership through possession — this is fundamentally a *chutz la'aretz* conversation. This is not where we belong. And so after such an intense *perek* built around the sense of owning property and proving that ownership through a kind of semi-permanence, the *agadeta* at its conclusion reminds us: this is not our permanent place. When you build your house, you leave a *zeicher l'churban*. *Im lo a'aleh es Yerushalayim al rosh simchasenu* — that is the verse the *agadeta* itself quoted. That sense of longing for permanence in the right place is the note on which the previous chapter ends, and it lends a particular resonance to a chapter that is now going to dig deeply into what it means to own, sell, and transfer property in this world.

## Excluded Items

### Mishnah Text

The *Mishnah* opens: *lo machar yatzia* — one who sells a house does not sell the *yatzia*, the veranda or attached add-on structure, even though it opens into the house. *Ve-lo et hacheder she-liphnim heimenu* — nor does the sale include the inner room located within or behind it. *Ve-lo et hagag bizman she-yesh lo ma'akeh gavoah asara tefachim* — nor does it include the roof, provided the roof has a surrounding wall of ten *tefachim*. Rabbi Yehuda adds: *im yesh tzurat hapetach af al pi she'eina gevoha asara tefachim eino machur* — if the fence around the roof has a *tzurat hapetach*, a doorway-type structure, then even without a wall of ten *tefachim* the roof is not included in the sale.

### Yetzia Definition

What exactly is a *yatzia*? The word seems to denote some kind of structure exterior to the main wall of the house — either an enclosed porch, something resembling a veranda, or an add-on structure of some kind. The parallel usage in the context of *Sukkah* involves a type of balcony, but here the structure appears to be exterior to the wall of the house rather than interior to it.

What is immediately striking about this *Mishnah*, however, is not the definition of *yatzia* so much as the underlying assumption: that one person could own the veranda while another owns the house proper, or that a seller would retain the veranda even while transferring ownership of the house. This is a difficult concept to absorb intuitively. The *yatzia*, after all, may only be accessible through the house itself — and yet, the *Mishnah* treats it as something potentially separable. Perhaps the key is recognizing that the word "house" in a bill of sale cannot be expected to carry more weight than its narrowest, most natural referent, and anything not captured by that referent remains with the seller unless explicitly transferred. Even so, there is something genuinely puzzling about envisioning a division of ownership of this kind.

One might suggest that the difficulty stems from reasoning too much from a modern, New York real estate context. In the ancient world, where structures were built organically — flat roofs with add-on rooms, extensions behind the main living space, separate quarters accessible from a shared courtyard — the division of ownership between different parts of a single complex may have been a far more ordinary social reality than it appears to us. There is something to this point.

### Inner Room

The *Mishnah*'s next exclusion is the *cheder* — the inner room. Here again, one's first instinct is to ask: what does it mean that the bedroom or back room of a house is not included when you sell the house? The more sensible reading, it seems, is that the *cheder* here refers not to an ordinary bedroom but to a separate, self-contained living facility — what we might call a mother-in-law suite or a back unit with its own character as a distinct domicile. We have seen elsewhere the concept that one person owns the ground floor and another owns the upper floor, that one person owns the front and another the back. To the extent that a *cheder* could plausibly belong to a different tenant or function as a separate residential unit, it makes sense that the word "house" in a bill of sale would not automatically encompass it.

The *Rashbam*, however, explains the *cheder* quite differently — as a storage room, essentially an attic or back storeroom where one keeps one's belongings, not as an alternative living space. His language is clear: *de-keivan de-eino asui ela lehatznia sham chafatzav lishmor k'mo betevah*, since it is made only for storing one's possessions like a box, it is therefore not included in the category of *bayit*. This is, to say the least, counterintuitive. The idea that I am selling you my house while retaining the storage room in the back — not because it is a separate dwelling but simply because it is a storage facility — is very difficult to understand. Why should a back storeroom not be considered an integral part of what one is selling? The *Rashbam*'s reading stands, but it is hard to make entirely natural sense of it.

### Roof Conditions

The *Mishnah*'s third exclusion concerns the *gag* — the roof — under certain conditions. If the roof has a surrounding *ma'akeh*, a perimeter wall of ten *tefachim*, then the roof is not included in the sale. The *ma'akeh* in question echoes the *mitzvah* of *asita ma'akeh l'gagecha*, and a roof enclosed by such a wall becomes, in effect, a usable living space in its own right. Anyone familiar with certain neighborhoods in Jerusalem will recognize the phenomenon: what appears to be a flat expanse of concrete turns out to be the rooftops of houses below, functioning as open courtyards or gathering spaces. In densely built urban environments, the roof of one person's house could be effectively the outdoor living space of another. The *Mishnah* treats such a roof as independently functional and therefore not automatically included in the sale.

Rabbi Yehuda holds that even without a full ten-*tefach* wall, if the boundary structure has the form of a *tzurat hapetach* — a post-and-lintel doorway structure — then the roof is similarly understood as its own independent space and is not sold along with the house.

## Yetzia Etymology

### Three Terms

The *Gemara* opens its analysis of the *Mishnah* by exploring the semantics of the word *yatzia* itself. *Tani Rav Yosef* — Rav Yosef taught: this structure that the *Mishnah* calls *yatzia* is known by three different names: *yatzia*, *tzeila*, and *ta*. Each of these terms appears in a different classical text, and the *Gemara* proceeds to identify the source of each.

In *Eretz Yisrael*, the Gemara notes, it is translated as *afta* — a low-built structure attached to the side or the back of the house. Rav Yosef identifies it as *bidka chalila*, a latticed or windowed structure, more open to the air, built onto the house. The *Gemara* then draws out the logical relationship between these two readings: if even the fully enclosed *afta* is considered independent of the house and is not sold along with it, then *a fortiori* the open-aired, latticed *bidka chalila* would not be sold with the house. Conversely, according to the view that the *Mishnah* is referring to the *bidka chalila*, the enclosed *afta* would indeed be sold along with the house, since it functions more as an extension of the interior space.

### Tanach Sources

The *Gemara* then traces each of the three terms to its source in *Nach*. *Yatzia dichtiv* — the word *yatzia* appears in the book of Kings in connection with the walls of the *Beit HaMikdash*: *hayatzia hatachtona chamesh ba'ama rachava* — "the lowest *yatzia* was five *amot* wide." This refers to the exterior chambers built onto the outside of the *Beit HaMikdash*'s walls.

*Tzeila dichtiv* — the word *tzeila* appears in *Yechezkel*, in his description of the *Beit HaMikdash*: *vehatzela'ot tzela el tzela shalosh ushloshim pa'amim* — "the *tzela'ot*, *tzela* by *tzela*, thirty-three times." *Yechezkel*'s description differs from that in Kings in various respects, including the number of these exterior chambers — thirty-three rather than three — but structurally the same phenomenon is being described: chambers built exterior to the wall of the *Beit HaMikdash*.

*Ta dichtiv* — the word *ta* appears in *Yechezkel* as well: *vehata kaneh echad orech vekaneh echad rochav uvein hata'im chamesh amot* — describing the dimensions of these exterior chambers and the spaces between them.

### Beit Mikdash Structure

The *Gemara* adds that if one prefers a source from *Mishnah* rather than *Nevi'im*, there is one in *Middot*: *kotel haheichal shesh vehata shesh kotel hata chamesh ama* — "the wall of the *heichal* was six [*amot*], the *ta* was six, and the outer wall of the *ta* was five *amot*." This describes, in architectural terms, the layered structure around the *heichal*: the inner wall, then a six-*amah* chamber built onto its exterior, then a five-*amah* outer wall enclosing that chamber.

The architecture behind this deserves a moment's attention. The wall of the *Beit HaMikdash* was built with a series of stepped indentations along its base, progressively narrowing as it rose. This meant that as the three tiers of exterior chambers were stacked on top of one another going upward, each successive tier was actually wider than the one below — because each could rest on a broader ledge cut into the wall's indented base. This is why the verse in Kings specifies that the *lowest* chamber was five *amot* wide, rather than the widest: counter-intuitively, the upper chambers were wider because of the progressive indentation of the wall below. According to the *Rashbam*, these indentations existed precisely so that the chambers could be supported without the beams being inserted into the sacred walls themselves.

There is something strikingly thematic here. Once again — as was true at the very beginning of the previous *perek* — the textual paradigm for the structures and terms under discussion is the *Beit HaMikdash* itself. The *Mishna* on *chezkas habatim* opened with a *nevua* from *Yirmiyah* about the exile; now, the vocabulary of *yatzia*, *tzeila*, and *ta* — the attached chambers that give legal identity to structures adjacent to a house — is drawn from Kings, *Yechezkel*, and *Middot*, all describing the exterior chambers of the *Beit HaMikdash*. The textual paradigm for ownership and building is the Temple. Whatever legal category we are trying to understand about ordinary houses in exile, it is the *Beit HaMikdash* that teaches us the language.

## Four Amot Rule

### Mar Zutra's Rule

The *Gemara* records: *amar Mar Zutra vehu dehazei v'hu d'havei arba amos* — Mar Zutra teaches that the *yatzia* is considered an independent entity, separate from the house and therefore not automatically sold along with it, only if it has minimum dimensions of four *amot* by four *amot*. A *yatzia* smaller than that is simply too trivial a space to carry independent standing; it is functionally an appendage of the house and goes along with it.

### Ravina's Question

Ravina poses a challenge to Mar Zutra: if we require four *amot* as the threshold of independence, does this same standard apply to the case of a well (*bor*) or a cistern (*dos*)? A *Mishnah* later in the chapter will establish that even if one sells a house and specifies in the bill of sale that all rights extend to the full depth below and the full height above — *af al pi she'kasav lo umka v'ruma* — the sale still does not include any well or cistern on the property. Does Mar Zutra hold that a well is only independently excluded if it is four *amot* wide, and that a smaller well would be considered part of the house?

### Tashmishta Distinction

Mar Zutra's response is that the two cases are simply not comparable. By a well and a house, *ha tashmishta l'chud v'ha tashmishta l'chud* — each serves an entirely distinct function. A house is a living space; a well is a water source. Nobody could mistake one for an extension of the other; they are categorically different in their *tashmishta*, their mode of use. The four-*amot* threshold therefore has no relevance there.

The case of the *yatzia* is different: *hacha idi v'idi chad tashmishta hi*. Here, the *yatzia* and the house proper are both enclosed spaces used for habitation. The only meaningful question is whether we are dealing with one living space or two. It is precisely because they share the same *tashmishta* that a size threshold becomes the relevant criterion: below four *amot*, the space is too small to function as an independent habitation and is absorbed into the house; at four *amot* or more, it qualifies as its own living space and is therefore not automatically included in the sale. This distinction is coherent and, once stated, makes considerable sense.

## Castle Example

### Castle Setup

Rav Nachman cites a ruling in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha: *ha-mocher bayit le-chavero be-birah gedolah* — one who sells a house to his fellow within a large castle (*birah*). The *Rashbam* explains the structure of a *birah* as something resembling a castle in its architectural organization: a large enclosed complex with a shared open space at its center, ringed by individual houses or chambers that each open onto the shared courtyard — separate living units contained within one overarching physical structure, sharing a perimeter wall.

Now imagine: I own one such chamber within this castle complex, and I wish to sell it. I draw up a bill of sale, I call the property a *bayit*, and when I record the dimensions, I give not the dimensions of my individual chamber but the dimensions of the outer wall of the entire castle. The ruling is: *af al pi she-metzar lo metzarim ha-chitzoniyim, metzarim hirchiv lo* — even though I bounded the property with the exterior dimensions of the castle, those dimensions are merely an expansion of the boundaries, not a definition of what was sold. The buyer acquires only my individual chamber; the rest of the castle does not transfer.

### Naming vs. Dimensions

The *Gemara* interrogates this ruling carefully. If the chamber is universally called *bayit* and the whole castle is universally called *birah*, then the answer is obvious — *peshita*, *bayit zavan lei birah lo zavan lei* — I sold you a *bayit*, not a *birah*. Even combined with exterior dimensions, the naming convention conclusively determines what was sold. Conversely, if the word *bayit* can sometimes refer to the whole castle — as occasionally happens in colloquial usage — then combined with the exterior dimensions, one might have thought the entire complex was sold.

The *Gemara* resolves that the ruling is necessary precisely for the intermediate case: the majority of speakers use *bayit* to mean only the individual chamber and *birah* for the whole complex, but there exists a minority who do refer to the whole castle as their *bayit* — much as someone might casually say "come to my little place in Connecticut" when referring to a large estate. In such a case one might have thought that the combination of a word that could apply to the whole and dimensions that match the whole should be read as conveying the whole. The ruling tells us otherwise.

### Shayarti Line

The reason why the narrow reading prevails becomes clear with the key legal principle now introduced: *mi-de-hava lei le-michtav ve-lo shayarti bi-zvinai illeyn klum* — because the seller should have written, and did not write, "I do not retain any ownership in what I am selling." This is the crucial line. The entire subsequent chapter, in a sense, reduces to this: every time the *Mishnah* teaches that selling a house does not include the *yatzia*, the *cheder*, the roof, the well — the practical upshot is that if you want to sell your house and genuinely convey all of these components, you must include in your *shtar* the declaration that you are not retaining any portion of what you are selling. Without that line, the bill of sale will be read as narrowly as possible.

### Narrow Reading

Therefore, when the seller used the word *bayit* and gave the dimensions of the castle, but did not include the *shayarti* declaration, the document must be interpreted in the most restrictive way. The word *bayit* is read at its narrowest, referring only to the individual chamber, and the dimensions are treated as providing nothing more than a locating context — a rough address, as it were — rather than as a definition of what was conveyed. Without the *shayarti* line, the default presumption is that the seller retained anything not unambiguously transferred. *Shema mina shiyurei shiyer* — we infer from the absence of that line that the seller retained ownership of those parts.

## Field in Valley

### Valley Setup

A parallel ruling follows, again cited by Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha: *ha-mocher sade le-chavero be-vika gedolah* — one who sells a field to his fellow within a large valley. The seller owns many fields within this broad valley, intends to sell one of them, and draws up a *shtar* using the word *sadeh*. When recording the dimensions, however, he gives not the dimensions of the particular field being sold but those of the entire surrounding valley. *Af al pi she-metzar lo metzarim hitzonim, metzarim hirchiv lo* — even so, the dimensions are only an expansion of the boundary context, and the buyer acquires only the one field, not the whole valley.

### Address Theory

The *Rashbam*'s explanation of this case is instructive. Without the kind of precise locational reference systems we take for granted today — no longitude, no latitude, no street addresses — identifying a particular field among many in a large valley could be genuinely difficult. The *Rashbam* describes a situation in which the parties are actually standing in the field at the time of the transaction; the *eidim* and the parties present know exactly which field is being sold. The valley dimensions serve not as a description of what is being sold but as an identifying address — "the valley to the south of town that runs five hundred *amot* by seven hundred *amot*" is something people could locate and recognize, whereas "the third field from the left" would mean nothing to anyone not present. In this reading, the dimensions of the valley function the way we would use a neighborhood name or a zip code: context for identification, not a definition of the property conveyed.

One might then ask: if the document uses valley dimensions as an address, does it not at least create ambiguity about which particular field within the valley is being sold? The *Rashbam*'s answer, it seems, is that the physical context of the transaction — where the parties were standing when the document was written — was itself sufficient identification, and the question the *Gemara* is addressing is not which field but how much of the valley.

### House vs. Field

The *Gemara* then asks why both rulings are necessary — the castle case and the valley case — since they seem to cover the same principle. The answer is that each case independently might have been ruled differently, and only by stating both do we preclude error in either direction.

Had we known only the castle case, one might have argued: of course a single *bayit* within a *birah* does not convey the whole *birah*, because *ha tashmishta l'chud v'ha tashmishta l'chud* — the individual chambers each serve as separate living spaces; no one lives in the entire castle as a single dwelling. People inhabit individual chambers; the castle as a whole is nobody's living unit. Given that naturally differentiated *tashmishta*, it makes perfect sense to read the word *bayit* narrowly and not to be swayed by the castle's exterior dimensions.

But one might have thought the valley case should work differently: *dechula chada tashmishta hi* — the entire valley is, in principle, one unified agricultural unit. It can be plowed and cultivated as a whole; buying the whole valley to farm it as one expanse is a perfectly intelligible transaction. Given that unified *tashmishta*, combined with the broad dimensions recorded, perhaps we should read the *shtar* as conveying the whole valley.

### Could Be Specific

Conversely, had we known only the valley case, one might have thought: of course you don't convey the whole valley, because the seller had no practical way to describe the specific field other than by reference to the valley in which it sat. The use of valley dimensions was effectively forced by the absence of any better descriptive vocabulary; it tells us nothing about intent to convey the whole.

But in the castle case, the seller *could* have been more specific. One could identify a particular chamber within a castle by its location — the one in the northeast corner, the one with the red door, the one beside the well. The failure to be specific when specificity was available, and the use of castle-wide dimensions instead, might seem more deliberate — perhaps indicating that the seller intended to convey the full complex. Therefore, without the ruling of Rav Nachman covering both cases explicitly, one might have erred in different directions for each. *Utzricha* — both statements are necessary.

## Shayarti Principle

### Rav Mari

The *Gemara* now draws the thread together with a universal statement: *amar Rav Mari bra debat Shmuel mishmeih de'Abaye* — Rav Mari, the son of Shmuel's daughter, citing Abaye: *hai man demazbin leh midi lechavereih, tzarich lemichtav leh lo shayerit bizvini ilin kadmai klum* — anyone who sells property to another is required to include in the bill of sale the declaration "I do not retain any ownership in what is being sold before me." This is the universal rule, and it follows directly from Rav Nachman's rulings. Both the castle ruling and the valley ruling can only be coherent — that is, the narrow reading of a document that gives broad dimensions can only be legally defensible — if we accept as a background norm that the *shtar* should have contained this line and did not.

### Presumption Logic

The underlying logic is one of burden of proof in the context of documentary ambiguity. This touches on an interesting *halachah* question: even in the presence of a *shtar*, if the terms of that *shtar* are ambiguous, who bears the burden? The *shtar* is, in a sense, a form of *eidut* — testimony that a transaction took place — but the question of *what* the transaction included remains open when the language is unclear. In such circumstances, the principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'ayah* inclines the resolution toward the seller, who was originally in possession. If the seller had included the *shayarti* declaration, the *shtar* would be interpreted maximally, and the buyer would receive everything. Without that line, the document's ambiguity resolves in the seller's favor: we assume the seller retained ownership of anything not unambiguously conveyed. *Shema mina shiyurei shiyer* — the absence of the line is itself evidence of retention.

## Multiple Plots

### Case Setup

The *Gemara* now introduces a specific factual scenario. A seller says to a buyer: *ar'a deRabbi Chiya mezavanna lach* — "I am selling you the land of Rabbi Chiya," referring to parcels he had acquired from Rabbi Chiya. A *shtar* is drawn up accordingly. It then emerges that the seller owned two separate plots of land, both of which were commonly referred to as "the land of Chiya," having been purchased from him at different times and located in different places. The document says "the land" (*ar'a*), in the singular. Which field was sold?

### Singular/Plural Rule

Rav Ashi rules: *chada amar lei tarti lo amar lei* — he said "land" (singular), not "lands"; therefore only one plot was conveyed. Grammatically, *ar'a* is singular and *ar'ata* is the plural form — a point that emerges from the *Gemara*'s own usage, where it describes the seller as having *tarti ar'ata*, "two plots," using the plural. So *ar'a* means one.

But the ruling extends further. If the seller had said *ar'ata*, the plural, this conveys a minimum of two — *mi'ut ar'ata shtayim*. It does not, however, convey all five fields if the seller owned five. To convey all of them, the seller would need to have said *kol ar'ata de'is lei* — "all of the lands that I own" — and even then, this does not include orchards or gardens. The word *ar'a*, even in its plural and even with the universal *kol*, refers to cultivated grain fields; orchards (*bustana*) and gardens (*pardisa*) require a different designation.

If, however, the seller uses the word *zeira* — a more generic Aramaic term for cultivated land — this is broad enough to encompass any form of cultivated property, *afilu bebustana ufardisa*, including orchards and gardens. The one thing it still does not include is houses (*batei*) or Canaanite slaves (*avdei*), since those belong to a categorically different domain than land used for cultivation, even though they are obviously connected to the land in various ways.

### Ambiguity Resolution

Returning to the case of the two plots both called "land of Chiya": since the document says *ar'a* in the singular and only one plot was therefore sold, but neither party can prove which of the two plots was the one intended, the matter resolves according to *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'ayah* — the burden of proof falls on the one seeking to extract from the other. The seller retains the right to determine which plot was meant, and the buyer cannot establish a claim to a specific field without proof. This, again, reflects the consistent principle running through this entire section: where a document is ambiguous, the presumption favors the party who was previously in possession.

## Property Semantics

### Ar'a Terms

To summarize the terminological distinctions established by Rav Ashi: *ar'a* in the singular conveys one plot of cultivated land; *ar'ata* in the plural conveys a minimum of two; *kol ar'ata*, "all my lands," conveys all grain fields but excludes orchards and gardens.

### Zeira

The term *zeira* is broader in scope, functioning as a generic word for any cultivated land. A sale or transfer denominated using *zeira* therefore includes orchards and gardens in addition to grain fields, since the word does not carry the implicit specialization of *ar'a*. The one category *zeira* does not encompass is houses and Canaanite slaves, which belong to a qualitatively different legal category from cultivated land, regardless of how closely associated with a given piece of land they might be.

### Nichsei

The broadest term is *nichsei* — property in the most general sense. A sale framed in terms of *nichsei* includes all possessions: grain fields, orchards, gardens, houses, and even Canaanite slaves. When the word chosen is *nichsei*, nothing is excluded by virtue of being a different *type* of asset, because *nichsei* does not carry any implicit restriction to one category of holding. This terminological hierarchy — from the narrow *ar'a* through *ar'ata*, *kol ar'ata*, *zeira*, and finally *nichsei* — provides the practical vocabulary for drafting documents that accurately convey the intended scope of a transaction.
