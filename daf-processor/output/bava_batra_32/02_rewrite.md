# Bava Batra 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ziluta D'Bei Dina

### Witnesses & Property

The daf opens by continuing a case involving conflicting witnesses over property ownership. Two parties each produced witnesses to establish *chazaka* over a parcel of land, but one party had the additional advantage of witnesses testifying that the land had belonged to his father. Rav Nachman had originally awarded the land to that party. Subsequently, however, new witnesses came forward testifying that the land had in fact belonged to the other party's father, which returned the dispute to equal footing. Rav Nachman responded by reversing his earlier ruling — not to award the land to the opposing party, but simply to withdraw the earlier award and leave the matter unresolved, with the Gemara's principle of *kol de'alim gavar* applying until the matter could be properly adjudicated.

### Court Reversal

This reversal prompted a challenge: was Rav Nachman not concerned about *ziluta d'bei dina* — the ill repute that falls upon a court that reverses its own rulings? The Gemara had raised a position suggesting that once a *beit din* issues a ruling, it should not walk it back, lest the court appear inconsistent and its authority be undermined. Rav Nachman seemed at first to concede this concern, yet then proceeded to reverse himself in practice. The Gemara's observation is pointed: one who witnessed this might have thought Rav Nachman simply forgot that his position had been challenged. But that is not the case. Rather, as the Gemara puts it, *ela mishum detalya be'ashelai ravrevei* — he had hung his ruling on very large oak trees, meaning he had strong tannaitic support for the position that we are not concerned with *ziluta d'bei dina*, and that the pursuit of true justice outweighs the reputational concern of a court reversing itself.

This is noteworthy in its own right. Even in a case where the reversal does not produce a clear winner — where withdrawing an earlier award merely returns the dispute to contested status — Rav Nachman maintains that *ziluta d'bei dina* is no impediment to correct adjudication. One might argue more plausibly for leaving things in place when there is no obvious correct outcome to achieve. Yet Rav Nachman holds firm: if there is no longer a valid reason to have awarded the property to one party, we withdraw that award regardless of how it reflects on the court.

### Tannaitic Support

The Gemara cites a *mishna* dealing with the status of a *kohen* whose lineage has come under question — specifically, whether he might be a *ben grusha* or *ben chaluza*, rendering him a *chalal* disqualified from priestly service. Rabbi Yehuda rules that one witness is not sufficient to establish his legitimacy as a *kohen* when doubt exists. Rabbi Elazar qualifies this: it is only when there are genuine *orerin* — an established formal challenge to his status — that a single witness cannot restore him. But absent such a challenge, one witness suffices. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, citing Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya, also holds that one witness can establish the *kohen*'s status. The Gemara initially notes that this appears to be the same position as Rabbi Elazar's, raising the question of what exactly the dispute is.

## Single Witness

### Priest Status

The question is what constitutes *orerin* — the threshold of opposition against which a single witness becomes insufficient. Rabbi Yochanan is cited as stating that there is no valid *ir* (ill report) with fewer than two witnesses. This means that if only one witness is claiming the *kohen* is disqualified, that single witness claim cannot suffice to establish *orerin*, and therefore everyone would agree that a single witness could counter it and restore the *kohen* to status. Conversely, if there are two witnesses attesting to disqualification, everyone agrees a single witness cannot counter two. The question then becomes: where exactly is the debate between Rabbi Elazar and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel?

### Two vs One

The Gemara proposes that the debate concerns whether a single witness can counter another single witness. According to this reading, Rabbi Eliezer holds that *orerin* can be established even by a single opposing witness, such that one witness for validity cannot overcome even one witness against. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, by contrast, holds that true *orerin* requires two witnesses, meaning a single witness for validity can counter and neutralize a single witness against, restoring the *kohen* to his *chezkat kashrus*.

### Rumor Case

The Gemara then constructs the specific case that underlies this debate. We begin with an established *chazaka* that this person's father was a *kohen*. A rumor then circulates that he is a *ben grusha* or *ben chaluza*. Based on this rumor, he is placed on what amounts to administrative leave — not a definitive ruling of disqualification, but a suspension pending clarification. One witness then comes forward and attests to his legitimacy as a *kohen*. This is accepted, and he is restored to service. Then two witnesses come forward and testify that he is indeed a *ben grusha* and *ben chaluza*. This constitutes *orerin* in the full sense, since Rabbi Yochanan established that *orerin* requires two witnesses. He is accordingly deposed again, and this time there is a definitive ruling — not merely a suspension — that he is a *pasul kohen* who may not perform the *avodah*. Then yet another witness comes forward and attests to his being a valid *kohen*. The structure is thus: rumor, one witness good, two witnesses bad, one witness good.

The key observation is that the first *ed echad* who testified in his favor and the final *ed echad* who testifies in his favor can potentially be combined: together they would constitute two witnesses attesting to his legitimacy, which could then stand against the two witnesses who attested to his disqualification. If they cancel each other out, we return to the *chezkat kashrus* with which we began, and he should be restored to his priestly post.

The *Rashbam* and *Tosafot* note the obvious question: why complicate the case with the initial rumor and first *ed echad*? Could we not simply have begun with two witnesses saying bad and then two witnesses saying good, without any of the preliminary stages? They explain that the rumor and first witness are included only to explain why any witness came forward at all — there had to be something to respond to. And the initial deposition based on the rumor is not considered a real *psak*, only a provisional suspension, so it does not generate its own *ziluta d'bei dina* concern. The genuine *psak* comes only when the two witnesses attest to his disqualification, and the question is whether reversing *that* ruling implicates *ziluta d'bei dina*.

### Combining Testimony

Given this structure, the Gemara reads the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel as turning on *ziluta d'bei dina*. Rabbi Eliezer holds *keivan denachtinan lo maskinan lei* — once we have deposed him, we do not restore him. Not because one witness cannot match one witness in principle, but because of the concern for *ziluta d'bei dina*: the *beit din* cannot keep reversing itself. Rabban Gamliel holds the opposite: *anan nachtinan lei ve'anan maskinan lei* — we took him off and we can put him back on. *U'lezilusa d'bei dina lo chaishinan* — we are not concerned for the ill repute of the *beit din*.

This reading would give Rav Nachman a tannaitic dispute that at least includes one position — Rabban Gamliel's — supporting his view that *ziluta d'bei dina* is not a concern. However, it remains a *machloket tanaim*, not a definitive source.

## Rav Ashi's Frame

### Rav Ashi Challenges

Rav Ashi presses the point. If the entire debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabban Gamliel is really about *ziluta d'bei dina*, why does the case need to be framed as one witness, then two witnesses, then one witness? The concern of *ziluta d'bei dina* would be equally present — and more directly illustrated — if the case were simply: two witnesses said he is disqualified, we ruled accordingly, and then two witnesses came and said he is valid. Why introduce the complication of separate, individual witnesses whose testimonies must be combined?

### Everyone Agrees

Rav Ashi therefore reframes the entire sugya. *Dekulei alma lo chaishinan lezilusa d'bei dina* — everyone, in fact, agrees that we are not concerned with *ziluta d'bei dina*. That is the constant, not the variable. If there were two witnesses saying he is valid after two witnesses said he was invalid, everyone would agree we reverse the ruling and restore him, with no concern for how the court appears. The variable — the actual dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabban Gamliel — is whether the two separate *ed echad* testimonies can be combined into a valid *eidut* of two. This is the question of *tzeiruf eidut*: can testimony given by two individuals who came separately, at different times and in different proceedings, be joined to constitute the halakhic unit of two witnesses?

According to Rav Ashi's reading, Rabbi Eliezer holds they cannot be combined and Rabban Gamliel holds they can. And since both positions assume that *ziluta d'bei dina* is not a concern — the debate being only about *tzeiruf* — this provides far stronger support for Rav Nachman. It is not merely one side of a tannaitic dispute that supports him; rather, *all* tannaitic opinions implicitly agree with his position on *ziluta d'bei dina*.

### Baraita Support

The Gemara then cites a *beraita* that further clarifies the question of combining separate testimonies. One position holds *leolam ein eidus mitztarefes ad sheyihyu sheneihem ke'echad* — testimony can only be combined if both witnesses testify together as a unit. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha permits combination even *bezeh achar zeh* — even when each witness testifies to a different moment or circumstance, so long as the underlying claim is the same.

The Gemara distinguishes between two sub-cases in this *beraita*. The first involves two witnesses who each observed a different event (e.g., a loan made on different days) but both attest to the same underlying obligation — that is a question of *tzeiruf* regarding what was observed. The second, which is directly relevant to our case, involves witnesses who came to *beit din* at different times: one witness on Sunday, whose testimony is heard and recorded, and a second witness who arrives Wednesday, and whose separate testimony is then heard. Can these two testimonies be combined retroactively into a single *eidut* of two? Rabbi Natan permits this — *shomin devarav shel zeh hayom u'khesheyavo chavero lemachar shomin devarav* — we hear each witness separately and then combine them. The *tanna kamma* disagrees. This is the precise debate reflected in the *kohen* case: can the first *ed echad* who testified before the deposition be combined with the *ed echad* who testified afterward to form two valid witnesses for his legitimacy? And regardless of how that debate resolves, *ziluta d'bei dina* plays no role — supporting Rav Nachman's position on both views.

## Forged Document

### Land Migo

The Gemara introduces a new case. Reuven challenges Shimon: what right do you have to this land? Shimon responds that he purchased it from Reuven and produces a *shtar* as proof. Reuven declares the *shtar* to be a forgery. Shimon then approaches Rava — who was serving as the *dayan* — and whispers to him: yes, the *shtar* is indeed a forgery, and he himself produced it. However, he explains, he did originally possess a legitimate *shtar* for the sale, which he subsequently lost. He created the forgery as a substitute, relying on it to serve in place of the genuine document.

Rava rules in Shimon's favor on the basis of *migo*. The *shtar* was such a convincing forgery that, had Shimon wished to deceive the court, he could simply have maintained that it was genuine and would have prevailed. The fact that he voluntarily admitted the forgery — when he had no need to do so — lends credibility to his explanation that he had a genuine *shtar* that was lost. This is the classic structure of *migo*: since Shimon could have successfully maintained the stronger lie, his admission of a lesser impropriety is believed.

### Yosef Challenge

Rav Yosef challenges Rava sharply: *amai samacht ahai shtra? hai shtra chaspa be'alma hu* — how can you rely on this *shtar*? It is nothing but a shard of pottery. The challenge has real force, and it is worth unpacking carefully.

In an ordinary *migo*, the claim is: believe me, because if I wanted to lie I could have told a more advantageous lie, yet I am telling you the truth instead. The entire logic rests on the premise that the claimant is presenting himself as an honest person, and the *migo* confirms that honesty. Here, however, Shimon has openly confessed to committing forgery. He is not saying "I could have lied but chose not to" — he is saying "I did in fact fabricate a document, but believe me that I had a real one." Having already demonstrated himself to be someone capable of and willing to forge legal documents, he now asks the court to credit his word. Rav Yosef finds this untenable: a *migo* is grounded in the trustworthiness of the claimant, but this person has, by his own admission, destroyed any claim to trustworthiness. The *shtar* on which his entire *migo* is predicated is worthless — not because it is forged, but because the person wielding it has revealed himself as a liar. To grant him relief on the strength of such a *migo* is to reward the act of forgery with legal standing.

*Tosafot* offers a related but somewhat different framing: perhaps it is a valid *migo* in principle, but a *migo* grounded in a fraudulent act is too weak to serve the function of *hotza'a* — extracting property or money from someone else's possession. It may be sufficient to maintain what one already holds, but not to compel the other party to surrender what is in their possession. This reading anticipates the Gemara's ultimate ruling.

### Money Case

The Gemara then presents a parallel case involving money rather than land. Reuven claims that Shimon owes him a hundred *zuz* and produces a *shtar* as proof. Shimon responds that the *shtar* is a forgery. Reuven then approaches Rabba privately and admits: yes, the *shtar* is forged, but he did originally hold a valid *shtar* for the loan and lost it, creating the forgery in its place. Rabba again applies *migo*: since Reuven could have insisted on the authenticity of the document and likely prevailed, his voluntary admission of forgery is credible, and the underlying loan should be recognized. Rav Yosef again objects in identical terms: *hai shtra chaspa be'alma hu* — this *shtar* is a mere shard. The person has confessed to forgery and cannot be believed on the strength of a *migo* derived from that very act.

### Land/Money Rule

Rav Idi bar Avin then articulates the ruling that reconciles these two cases: *hilcheta kevasiah de-Rava be-ara*, *ve-hilcheta kevasiah de-Rav Yosef be-zuzi* — we rule like Rava in the case of land, and like Rav Yosef in the case of money. The guiding principle is: *de-heicha de-kaima ara tiku* — wherever the land stands, let it remain; *de-heicha de-kaimi zuzi lokmi* — wherever the money sits, let it remain.

In the land case, Shimon is currently in possession of the land, holds the (forged) *shtar*, and has the *migo*. Even though the *migo* is weak — being grounded in an admitted falsehood — it is sufficient to sustain what he already holds. The *migo* is not being used to extract anything from Reuven; it is being used to preserve the status quo in Shimon's favor. In the money case, the creditor is asking to compel extraction from the borrower — to force money out of the borrower's pocket on the basis of an invalid *shtar* and a *migo* derived from forged documents. This the law will not permit. Whether the reasoning is that *migo* is categorically insufficient to extract money (*lehotzi*), or that this particular *migo* is too compromised by the underlying forgery to carry such weight, the practical outcome is the same: a *migo* rooted in admitted fraud can preserve but cannot compel.

## Guarantor Case

### Re-Loan Setup

A further case is introduced. A guarantor (*ariv*) — call him Reuven — had stood surety for Shimon's debt to a creditor. The creditor proceeded to collect from the guarantor, and Reuven paid the creditor a hundred *zuz* on Shimon's behalf. Reuven then approached Shimon and demanded reimbursement. Shimon paid him back the hundred *zuz*. So far so good. But then Shimon turned around and asked to borrow that same hundred *zuz* from Reuven — a new loan. Reuven lent him the money. Now Reuven has come to collect the hundred *zuz* of the new loan, and he is doing so by presenting the original *shtar* — the document the creditor had given him as a receipt when the original debt was discharged.

Shimon challenges him: I already paid you back. Reuven acknowledges this but clarifies: yes, you repaid me for the original debt I covered on your behalf, but then you borrowed the hundred *zuz* from me again, and it is *that* new loan I am now collecting. He is, in other words, attempting to use the old *shtar* as proof of a new and distinct obligation.

### Old Receipt

Rav Idi bar Avin sends this question to Abaye: *ki hai gavna mai?* What is the law in such a situation? The complexity is clear: the *shtar* Reuven is presenting does not reflect the current debt. It is the receipt for the original loan — a loan that Shimon has already repaid. Reuven is now using it to stand in for a new, undocumented loan. There is a *migo* of sorts: Reuven did not have to disclose that the original debt was repaid; he could have simply presented the *shtar* as valid and demanded payment. The fact that he explained the situation openly might seem to lend credibility to his account of the re-loan.

### Abaye Answer

Abaye's response is terse and direct. He sends back: *mai tibei lach?* — what is your question? You yourself are the one who articulated the rule — *hilchata kevasiah de-Rabba be-ara ve-hilchata kevasiah de-Rav Yosef be-zuzi*. This is a case of money. The money is in Shimon's possession. The *shtar* is not a valid instrument for the current debt, and the *migo* grounded in that invalid *shtar* cannot be used to extract money from Shimon. We rule like Rav Yosef: *heicha de-kaimi zuzi lokmi* — where the money is, let it remain.

### Bad Coin

The Gemara adds one important qualification. The above ruling — that the guarantor cannot collect using the old *shtar* — applies specifically in the case where the re-loan scenario is as described: Shimon repaid Reuven, and Reuven then lent him the money again as a fresh loan, such that the original *shtar* is truly spent. However, if the situation was different — if Shimon attempted to repay Reuven with coins that were worn and discolored (*shaifei ve-sumkei*), rendering them unacceptable currency, and Reuven therefore rejected the payment and returned the coins — then the original debt was never actually discharged. The coins were tendered but not accepted as valid payment, and so the underlying obligation of the original loan remains intact. In that case, *acheti ita le-shibuda de-shtara* — the *shibud* of the original *shtar* is still in force, the debt is still legally owed under the original instrument, and Reuven may collect with that *shtar* without any difficulty. The entire analysis of *migo* and the invalid use of an old document simply does not apply, because in this scenario the document was never rendered invalid to begin with.
