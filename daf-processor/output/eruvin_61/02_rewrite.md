# Eruvin 61 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## City in 2000 Amot

### Mishnah Contradiction

The daf opens with an apparent contradiction between two *mishnayot*. The first *mishnah* teaches that when one places an *eiruv* outside of a city, what one gains on one side one loses on the other — meaning the city does not count against one's 2000 *amot* at all. Yet a second *mishnah*, appearing at the bottom of today's *amud*, states that when a person begins measuring from outside a city and his measurement reaches into the city, he must stop where his 2000 *amot* end, even in the middle of the city. This implies that the city does count against him. The two *mishnayot* thus appear to flatly contradict one another on the question of whether a city is counted within the 2000 *amot* boundary.

### Rav Yehoshua Solution

The *Gemara* resolves the contradiction through a distinction introduced by Rav Idi in the name of Rav Yehoshua ben Levi: if the city is completely contained within the 2000 *amot*, it does not count against the traveler at all — he traverses the entire city and continues from its far edge. But if his 2000 *amot* end in the middle of the city, the city does count against him and he may go no further. This distinction reconciles the two *mishnayot* by showing they address different geometric scenarios.

### Divrei Neviyut

Rav Idi himself, having transmitted this teaching in Rav Yehoshua ben Levi's name, then described it as *divrei neviyut* — words of prophecy. The force of this remark is that this crucial distinction was nowhere made explicit in the *Mishnah* itself; it required inspired inference to identify it.

### Gemara Demonstration

The *Gemara* then attempts to demonstrate whether this distinction is in fact derivable from the *mishnayot*. It quotes the *mishnah* at the bottom of today's page: *ulmodeh she'amru nosnin lo alpayim amah, she'afilu sof midato kalah bim'arah* — one who measures from outside is given 2000 *amot*, and even if his measurement ends in the middle of a cave, he stops there. The *Gemara* concedes that it is indeed clear from the text that a city counts against you when your 2000 end in its interior. What was not explicit in the *mishnayot*, and what thus merited the designation *divrei neviyut*, is the converse principle — that when the entire city falls within the 2000 *amot*, it does not count against you at all. That positive half of the distinction was Rav Yehoshua ben Levi's original contribution. And it is this ruling that governs how we *pasken*.

## Big & Small Cities

### Two Girsaot

The *Gemara* now turns to the next *mishnah*, which concerns the relationship between a large city and a small city when they are within range of one another. Rav Nachman notes that there are two textual versions — *girsaot* — of the *mishnah*, and he argues that neither version is necessarily in error. One version reads *anshei ir ketanah* — people of the small city can traverse the big city. The other reads *ein anshei ir ketanah* — the people of the small city cannot traverse the big city. He proceeds to explain how each reading can be correct, depending on which scenario the *mishnah* is describing.

### Measuring Scenario

The version that reads *ein anshei ir ketanah* — the small city cannot go through the big — describes a scenario where each person begins measuring from his own native city. Take as an example a big city of 3000 *amot* and a small city of 1000 *amot* with 900 *amot* between them. The people of the big city travel 900 *amot* to the small city and then traverse its entire 1000 *amot*, all of which falls within their 2000 *amot*, so none of it counts against them, leaving them another 1100 *amot* beyond. The people of the small city, however, travel the 900 *amot* across and then continue 1100 *amot* further — stopping exactly at the middle of the big city, since their 2000 *amot* end there and the big city was not fully contained within them. Hence, *ein anshei ir ketanah mahalchin et kol ir gedolah* — the small city cannot traverse the entirety of the big city.

### Eiruv Membership

The version that reads *anshei ir ketanah* — the people of the small city can go through the big — describes an entirely different scenario: a person who places his *eiruv* in the other city effectively becomes a member of that city. Under this reading, the *mishnah* is not about measuring from one's home city at all, but about the power of the *eiruv* to transfer one's legal affiliation. If the person from the big city places his *eiruv* in the small city, he becomes a member of the small city and is entitled to traverse it entirely and 2000 *amot* beyond. Conversely, a person from the small city who places his *eiruv* in the big city becomes a member of the big city and similarly gets the whole city plus 2000 *amot*. Both can thus traverse each other's cities — not because of their geographic relationship, but because of their *eiruv*-based membership.

### Textual Reconstruction

The *Gemara* notes that the *mishnah* text is incomplete — *chisurai mechasra* — and must be supplemented to make the full contrast explicit. The reconstructed reading inserts a *bameh devarim amurim* clause: when is it that the small city cannot traverse the big? *Bimdud* — when one is simply measuring from one's native city without having placed an *eiruv* in the other. But — and this is how the *mishnah* actually continues — *mi she-hayah b'ir gedolah v'hiniach et eruvo b'ir ketanah*, one who was in the big city and placed his *eiruv* in the small, or was in the small and placed his *eiruv* in the big, may travel the whole of it and 2000 *amot* beyond. The version that reads *ein anshei ir ketanah* thus sets up a contrast: without an *eiruv* in the other city, you are limited; with one, you gain full membership and the complete range of that city.

## Retaining Walls

### Wadi Cities

Rav Yosef, quoting Rami bar Abba quoting Rav Huna, introduces a new principle: a city built at the edge of a river or *wadi* — *ir she-yoshevet al sfat ha-nachal* — requires a *dakah* of four before it is treated as a city for purposes of *techum* measurement. If such a barrier exists, the city is measured from the edge of the wadi as any other city. If it does not, *ein modedin lah ela mipetach beito* — the city is not treated as a unified entity, and each individual must measure from the doorway of his own house. Rashi explains this by analogy to an earlier *sugya* about temporary dwellings: if the settlement is too precarious or impermanent, it cannot be considered a city. A community perched at the edge of a wadi, with no retaining wall to stabilize it, is not considered a sufficiently stable settlement, and so it lacks the legal status of a city.

### Functional vs Symbolic

One might ask why the barrier must be four *amot* rather than four *tefachim*, as is standard in other contexts where a *dakah* or minimal partition suffices. Abaye raises precisely this question with Rav Yosef — you told us originally that the barrier must be four *amot*, not four *tefachim*. Why the difference? Rav Yosef explains: in other cases, *hatam lo bi'ita tashmishta* — one is not afraid to use the space, and so a minimal symbolic barrier suffices to achieve the needed legal separation. Here, however, *hacha bi'ita tashmishta* — without a serious wall, residents are genuinely afraid to use the space at the edge of the wadi. The barrier is therefore not merely symbolic but must be a genuinely functional structure. The requirement of four *amot* reflects this practical, rather than formal, purpose.

### Slope Cities

Rav Yosef then adduces a *baraita* to support this principle. The *baraita* records that *Rebbi* — Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi — permitted the people of Gadgader to descend to Chamtan, but did not permit the people of Chamtan to ascend to Gadgader. Rashi provides a helpful illustration: the two cities sit on a slope, with Gadgader at the top and Chamtan below. Both were within the *techum* of the other, so in principle travel in either direction should have been permitted. Yet *Rebbi* allowed only one direction. The *baraita* offers no explanation, leaving considerable room for interpretation.

### Wall Explanation

Rav Yosef himself proposes the following explanation: *lav mishum dehani avdu dakah v'hani lo avdu dakah* — Gadgader built a retaining wall while Chamtan did not. As a result, Gadgader has the status of a stable city, and its outer edge serves as the point from which its *techum* is measured, bringing it within range of Chamtan. Chamtan, lacking a retaining wall, does not have city status, and its residents must each measure from their own doorways. Only those whose houses happen to be within 2000 *amot* of Gadgader can actually reach it — and those who can benefit from the rule of squaring off can extend their range further. But because Chamtan has no city status, its people do not uniformly reach Gadgader the way Gadgader's people uniformly reach Chamtan. This is how *Rebbi*'s asymmetric ruling reflects the asymmetric physical and legal situation of the two cities.

## Slope Explanations

### Political Takana

Rav Dimi, when he came from Eretz Yisrael, offers a completely different explanation — one that has nothing to do with retaining walls or city status. He explains that the people of Gader were attacking (*mitatrogi*) the people of Chamtan. *Rebbi*'s ruling was therefore not a *pesak* in *hilchos Shabbat* at all, but rather a *takana* — a political decree — issued in his capacity as communal leader to prevent a dangerous situation.

### Power Dynamics

This raises an immediate question: if *Rebbi* feared violence, why did he only restrict travel on *Shabbat*, and why only forbid movement in one direction? The *Gemara* answers the first question by explaining *de'shchichi beshichrus* — on *Shabbat*, when people drink more wine, aggression is more likely to be acted upon. As for the directionality: *kalba belo mataye shev shanin lo navach* — a dog not on its own turf will not bark for seven years. Even though the people of Gader are the aggressors, when they are visitors in Chamtan they will not act out. But when the people of Chamtan enter Gader's territory, they place themselves in genuine danger, since Gader's people on their home ground, drinking on *Shabbat*, become a serious threat. The *Gemara* then anticipates a further question: should we not also be afraid that the people of Chamtan would attack Gader's visitors on their own territory? The answer is *kulei hai lo kaifi lehu* — the people of Chamtan are not powerful enough to harm the stronger Gadgerites even on home turf. The asymmetry of power explains the asymmetry of the ruling.

### Rainbow City

Rav Safra proposes a third explanation, dispensing with the retaining wall and the political considerations alike. He says it was a case of *ir ha'asuya k'keshet* — a city shaped like a rainbow, which creates a complex geometric situation affecting who can reach whom based on where they start.

### Squaring Off

Tosafot's explanation of Rav Safra's view is considerably clearer than Rashi's. Imagine a rainbow-shaped city and a second city nearby. Because the arc of the rainbow is within 4000 *amot* from end to end, the entire arc is treated as a single city. For those who reside within the rainbow city, the rule of squaring off applies — the city is treated as one unit, and from its far side they gain the full benefit of measuring outward. Residents of the upper arc can thus reach the neighboring city and traverse it entirely, all within their 2000 *amot*. But the residents of the neighboring city, starting from outside the rainbow, do not benefit from the same squaring off. When they travel toward the rainbow city, they end up somewhere in its interior — their 2000 *amot* run out before they reach the upper arc. They do not gain access to the full extent of the rainbow city. This is, in essence, a variant of the same underlying principle established at the outset: if the entire city is contained within your 2000 *amot*, it does not count against you; if your 2000 end in the city's interior, it does count against you.

### Simple Big/Small

Rav Dimi bar Chinnena offers the simplest explanation of all: it was a straightforward case of a big city and a small city, where the big city was not fully contained within the *techum* of the small city. Those starting in the big city could traverse the small city entirely since it fit within their 2000 *amot*. Those starting in the small city ended up stranded in the middle of the big city. No retaining walls, no political tensions, no unusual geometry — just the standard rule applied to an ordinary size disparity.

The *Gemara* then records a dispute about attribution: Rav Kahana and Rav Tavyumi disagree about who said which of the final two explanations, whether it was Rav Safra who spoke of the rainbow city and Rav Dimi bar Chinnena who gave the big-city-small-city explanation, or the reverse. What emerges from the full discussion is that *Rebbi*'s asymmetric ruling in the *baraita* about Gadgader and Chamtan admits of multiple readings — a dispute about walls, a political decree, an unusual city geometry, and a simple size disparity — and that the *baraita* itself is sufficiently open-ended to sustain all of them.

Tosafot also notes a textual curiosity: some had the *girsa* in the *Gemara* of *v'od hitir Rebbi* — "and furthermore *Rebbi* permitted." Tosafot explains this as a misreading. The original context, reflected in the *Tosefta*, had an elder testifying before *Rebbi*: *zakhur ani she-hayu bnei Migdal yordim l'Chamtsan ad hachatzer hachitzonah hasmucha l'gesher v'od* — I remember that the people of Migdal would descend to Chamtsan until the outermost courtyard near the bridge and a bit further. The word *v'od* properly belonged to the end of that testimony, indicating they could go slightly beyond. It was subsequently misread as the opening of the next sentence, *v'od hitir Rebbi*, suggesting a new ruling. According to Tosafot's reading, the elder's testimony about how far people previously traveled served as the evidentiary basis upon which *Rebbi* then established the parameters of who could go where.

## Eiruv Membership

### Eiruv Integration

The *mishnah* that we now reach — and which we have already encountered in fragments throughout the preceding discussion — states its rule directly: one who was in a big city and placed his *eiruv* in a small city, or was in a small city and placed his *eiruv* in a big city, may travel the entire city in which his *eiruv* was placed and 2000 *amot* beyond it. The principle is that placing one's *eiruv* in a city makes one, in a meaningful legal sense, a member of that city. This is what allows the person to treat the entire city as his base of operations for *Shabbat*, gaining the full benefit of city membership including the rule that a city completely within one's 2000 *amot* does not count against the measurement.

### Rabbi Akiva

Rabbi Akiva disputes this. He holds that placing one's *eiruv* — one's bread — in another city does not transform one into a member of that city. *Ein lo ela mimakom eiruvo alpayim amah* — he receives only 2000 *amot* from the location of the *eiruv*, not the full extent of the city and 2000 *amot* beyond. To benefit from complete city membership, one must actually have been present in the city when *Shabbat* began. The leniency whereby an entire city within 2000 *amot* does not count against the measurer was stated for actual residents of that city — real city-dwellers — not for those who have only nominal or virtual membership through the placement of an *eiruv*. Rabbi Akiva thus draws a sharp distinction between *me'arev beraglo* — one who establishes his *Shabbat* location through his own physical presence — and one who relies solely on the placement of bread.

### Cave Precedent

Rabbi Akiva then attempts to secure agreement from the *Chachamim* by citing the case of a cave: *ei atem modim li benoten eruvo beme'arah she'ein lo mimakom eruvo alpayim amah* — don't you agree that if one placed his *eiruv* in a cave, he does not receive the whole cave, only 2000 *amot* from the point of placement? The *Chachamim* respond: *eimatai* — when is that so? Only *bezman she'ein bah diyurin* — when there are no inhabitants in the cave. But if people actually live there, *mahalech es kullah vechutzah lah alpayim amah* — one traverses the entire cave and 2000 *amot* beyond it. The logic of the *Chachamim* is that an inhabited space, whether cave or city, functions as a city, and placing one's *eiruv* among its residents makes one legally part of that community.

### Physical Presence

The *Gemara* draws a striking corollary: *nimtza kaltah mima'al gabah* — it is more lenient to place one's *eiruv* inside the cave than on top of it. If one's *eiruv* is placed inside the cave where people live, one gains the entire length of the cave and 2000 *amot* in all directions. If it is placed on the ground above the cave, one is not within any inhabited space, and the benefit of city-like membership does not apply — only 2000 *amot* from that point. This is a clean illustration of the principle: it is inhabited space, not walls alone, that confers the advantage of collective membership.

The final line of the *mishnah* then restates the principle established at the beginning of the daf: one who starts measuring from outside a city receives 2000 *amot*, and even if his measurement ends in a cave — and now we understand why the *mishnah* chose the cave example, since the discussion immediately above involved a cave — he stops precisely where his 2000 *amot* end, regardless of whether anyone lives in the cave. This is the uncontested rule, agreed upon by both Rabbi Akiva and the *Chachamim*. What divides them is the intermediate case: one who places his *eiruv* in an inhabited city but does not himself begin *Shabbat* there.

## Desolate Cities

### Physical Presence Requir…

Rav Yehudah in the name of Shmuel now introduces the case of a *ir chareiva* — a desolate, formerly inhabited city, a ghost town. The question is whether such a place retains enough of its former status to confer the benefits of city membership. Shmuel rules: if someone actually began *Shabbat* physically present in a desolate city, *mehalech es kullah vechutzah lah alpayim amah* — he may traverse the entire city and 2000 *amot* beyond it. Physical presence in the ghost town, when *Shabbat* begins, is sufficient to invoke city-level entitlements — even though nobody else lives there.

### Eiruv Insufficient

However, if one merely places one's *eiruv* in a desolate city without being personally present, *ein lo mimakom eruvo ela alpayim amah* — he receives only 2000 *amot* from the location of his *eiruv*. Unlike an inhabited city, where the *Chachamim* hold that placing one's *eiruv* among the residents is sufficient to confer membership, a desolate city has no residents among whom to join. The *eiruv* thus cannot anchor one's membership in a community that no longer exists.

### Rabbi Eliezer

Rabbi Eliezer disputes this limitation. He holds that *echad Shabbat ve'echad heiniach* — whether one physically rested in the desolate city when *Shabbat* began, or merely placed one's *eiruv* there, *mehalechet kullah vechutzah lah alpayim amah* — one receives the entire city and 2000 *amot* beyond. For Rabbi Eliezer, the walls of the former city are sufficient to confer its benefits regardless of current habitation or personal presence.

### Scope Dispute

The *Gemara* raises a challenge from the *mishnah*'s earlier exchange between Rabbi Akiva and the *Chachamim*. Rabbi Akiva had asked: don't you agree that placing one's *eiruv* in a cave yields only 2000 *amot* from that spot? The *Chachamim* replied: only if the cave is uninhabited; if it is inhabited, one gets the whole cave. The *Gemara* infers: *ha be'eina bat diyurin modi leh* — the *Chachamim* concede that when a place is not fit for habitation, the *eiruv* alone does not suffice. Is a desolate city not in exactly the same position? The *Gemara* distinguishes: *ein re'uyah lediyurah* — the cave is not fit for habitation at all. A ghost town is different; it was once a city, its walls still stand, and it remains in principle habitable. Whether that distinction is sufficient to bring a desolate city within the rule of the inhabited city, or whether it is assimilated to the uninhabited cave, is precisely the question the *Gemara* presses.

### City-Cave Analogy

The *Gemara* then introduces a *baraita* to test this question. The *baraita* states: if one spent *Shabbat* in a city — *afilu hi gedolah k'Antiocha*, even as large as Antioch — or in a cave — *afilu hi k'me'arat Tzidkiyahu*, even as vast as the cave of Tzidkiyahu, the underground tunnels through which the last king of Yehudah attempted to flee — one traverses the entire space and 2000 *amot* beyond. The *Gemara* notes that the *baraita* juxtaposes city and cave: *katani ir dumya d'me'arah* — a city is stated analogously to a cave. Just as a cave is desolate, so too the city is desolate. And it specifies *shavat* — that you must actually begin *Shabbat* there; *aval hiniakh lo* — merely placing one's *eiruv* there does not suffice.

The *Gemara* then asks: *mani* — whose opinion is this *baraita*? If it reflects Rabbi Akiva's view, there is no particular novelty in requiring personal presence in a desolate city, since Rabbi Akiva requires personal presence even in an inhabited city. The *baraita* must therefore reflect the position of the *Rabbanan*, for whom this is a genuine *chiddush*: even though the *Rabbanan* ordinarily allow an *eiruv* to confer membership in an inhabited city, a desolate city is different — only personal presence works there. At this point the *Gemara* challenges the identification of the cave as desolate, since the *baraita* compared it to the cave of Tzidkiyahu, which was hardly inhabited. The *Gemara* resolves this: *k'me'arat Tzidkiyahu v'lo k'me'arat Tzidkiyahu* — the comparison is only to the size of the cave of Tzidkiyahu, not to its desolate character. The cave in the *baraita* is, like the city, assumed to be inhabited; it is comparable to Tzidkiyahu's cave only in its enormous dimensions.

The upshot of the full discussion: according to the *Rabbanan*, one who is personally present in a walled space — inhabited or desolate — at the onset of *Shabbat* gets the full benefit of that space. One who places only an *eiruv* in an inhabited city also gets the full benefit, because the *eiruv* anchors one among real residents. But placing an *eiruv* alone in a desolate city, where there are no residents among whom to join, does not suffice. Whether to extend the *halacha k'divrei hameikel* principle to resolve this last case in the lenient direction is left somewhat open.

## Synagogue Eiruv

### Mar Yehudah Case

A practical case is now related. Mar Yehudah found the people of Mafrachta placing their *eiruv* — *eruvai-hu* — in the synagogue of the house of Agogva. He advised them: *gavu batfei ki hechi di-lish-ru le-chu tfei* — place it further inside, so that more will be permitted to you. The idea is that by placing the *eiruv* deeper into the synagogue — further in the direction of the city they wished to reach — they would gain a greater range of permitted movement into that direction. Mar Yehudah was concerned about Rabbi Akiva's position, under which placing an *eiruv* in a location where one is not physically present does not make one a full member of that space. If that view is taken seriously, the placement of the *eiruv* at the near edge of the synagogue would be less advantageous than placing it deeper inside.

### Inhabited Synagogue

One might ask why the synagogue in this case is treated as a city-like space at all, rather than as bare walls surrounding an uninhabited area. The answer, apparent from Rashi, is that people actually live or regularly gather in this synagogue — *dayarei*, residents. Because the space is inhabited, placing one's *eiruv* there functions like placing it in a city: it makes one a legal member of the community of inhabitants. The key distinction from the desolate city case is precisely this: an inhabited space, whatever its form, allows the *eiruv* to work. It is the desolate, uninhabited space — like the empty cave — for which the *eiruv* alone is insufficient and personal presence is required.

### Lenient Ruling

Rava, however, objects to Mar Yehudah's counsel. *Pag'a ba-eiruvin* — he was acting contrary to the established practice regarding *eruvin*. *Leit lach l'had d'Rabbi Akiva* — we do not give weight to Rabbi Akiva's stringent position in the domain of *eruvin*. Rashi states this explicitly: *d'kaima lan halacha k'divrei hameikel be'eruv* — the established ruling is that we follow the lenient view in all matters of *eruv*. Tosafot similarly affirms this principle. The practical consequence is that Mar Yehudah's instruction to move the *eiruv* deeper inside — motivated by solicitude for Rabbi Akiva's view — was unnecessary. The majority position, and the operative *halacha*, allows an *eiruv* placed in an inhabited location to fully confer membership, without requiring physical presence in that space.

## Non-Jews in Courtyard

### Mishnah Rule

With the conclusion of *Perek Keitzad Me'arvin* — which dealt with *eruv techumin* — the *Gemara* turns to the opening *mishnah* of the next *perek*, which returns to the domain of *eruv chatzeros*, the communal *eiruv* of the courtyard. This topic is more legally intricate, and the *mishnah* opens with a striking rule: *hadar im ha'akum bechatzer* — if one lives in a courtyard with a non-Jew, *o im mi she'eino modeh be'eruv* — or with a Jew who does not accept the validity of *eruv*, *harei zeh oser alav* — that person renders it forbidden. The effect is that the other residents of the courtyard cannot make a valid *eruv chatzeros*.

The underlying logic of *eruv chatzeros* is worth pausing on. The institution is fundamentally about creating a unified community: all the residents of a courtyard join together, pooling their symbolic ownership of the shared space, and are thereby permitted to carry within it on *Shabbat*. The *eiruv* creates a sense of communal cohesion. When a non-Jew or a Jew who rejects the premise of *eruv* shares that courtyard, the possibility of full communal unification is broken. They cannot be meaningfully incorporated into the *eiruv* community — the non-Jew because he is not Jewish, the dissident because he excludes himself. Their presence therefore makes the *eiruv* invalid and carrying in the courtyard forbidden.

### Rabbi Eliezer Exception

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov disagrees with a partial exception. He holds that *le'olam eino oser ad she'yehu shnei Yisraelim osrim zeh al zeh* — the prohibition applies only when there are two Jews in the courtyard who are each restricting the other. A single Jew, even when cohabiting with non-Jews or with those who reject *eruv*, does not create a problem. This is somewhat counterintuitive: one might have assumed that the more Jews present, the better the prospects for a valid *eiruv*, and that reducing the number of Jewish participants to one would make things worse, not better. The *Gemara* will explore this further, but the premise appears to be that if there is only one Jewish householder with legal standing over the courtyard, there is no conflict of membership to resolve — the issue only arises when at least two Jews are in a position to obstruct one another.

### Sadducee Story

Rabban Gamliel then reports a case that he saw his father handle. There was a Sadducee — a *Tzeduki*, one who did not accept rabbinic authority and its institutions, including *eruv* — who lived with them in the same *mavoi* in Jerusalem. Rabban Gamliel's father instructed his household: *maharu vehotziu et hakelim lamavoi ad shelo yotzi veye'aser aleichem* — hurry and bring your vessels out into the *mavoi* before he comes out and makes it forbidden to you.

### Waiving Rights

Rashi explains the legal logic underlying this instruction. A *Tzeduki* is treated differently from a non-Jew. A non-Jew, as the *Gemara* will develop, must have his portion formally rented from him in order for the *eiruv* to function — the rental effectively removes his legal standing in the shared space for that *Shabbat*. But a *Tzeduki*, because he is Jewish, can instead be *mevattel reshut* — he can waive or renounce his rights in the shared space. He cannot be an active participant in the *eiruv*, but he can voluntarily surrender his claim over his portion, thereby removing the obstacle his presence creates.

The problem is that *bitul reshut* — the waiver of rights — is revocable. Unlike a formal rental, which transfers rights definitively for the duration of the agreement, a waiver can be retracted. The *Tzeduki* might change his mind and reclaim his portion, at which point the *eiruv* would again be invalid. The strategy Rabban Gamliel's father employed was therefore to assert the community's rights over the shared space *before* the *Tzeduki* could reclaim his — going out and using the *mavoi* first, thereby establishing the others' possession of the space for that *Shabbat*. Once they had used it, his subsequent reclamation could not undo what had already been established for this *Shabbat*.

### Retractable Waiver

Rabbi Yehudah presents an alternative version of the story. In his account, the instruction was: *maharu ve'asu tzorcheichem bamavoi ad shelo yotzi veye'aser aleichem* — hurry and attend to whatever you need to do in the *mavoi* before he comes out and makes it forbidden. The *Gemara* explains the difference: according to Rabbi Yehudah, the *Tzeduki*'s waiver is always retractable. At any moment, he may reassert his rights, and once he does, the *eiruv* is gone. The advice is therefore not to secure the space permanently for the *Shabbat*, but simply to make use of it for as long as he has not yet objected. The practical implication is that there is no mechanism for the other residents to lock in the benefit of the waiver — they must simply act quickly and make whatever use of the *mavoi* they can before he changes his mind.

What emerges from this final *mishnah* and its accompanying narrative is a richly layered set of issues: the distinction between a non-Jew and a Jew who rejects *eruv* with respect to the remedy available; the nature of *bitul reshut* and its revocability; the asymmetry between one Jewish dissenter and two; and the practical legal maneuvering that the rabbis employed to navigate a shared courtyard with a non-cooperative neighbor. The *Gemara* will continue to develop all of these themes in the *dappim* ahead.
