# Pesachim 53 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shemita & Biur

### Ownerlessness Principle

The daf begins in the middle of a discussion concerning several aspects of *shemita*, specifically the obligations that flow from the fundamental ownerlessness of *shemita* produce. During the *shemita* year, all food is treated as ownerless: it is a free-for-all, and both the poor and the wealthy alike are free to collect it from the fields. One may bring produce home, but keeping it stored for an extended period contradicts the essential ethos of *shemita*. Hoarding food in one's home — treating it as exclusively one's own — runs counter to the principle that this produce belongs to everyone.

### Biur Requirement

The Rabbis therefore understood that once a particular type of produce is no longer available out in the field, one may no longer keep it stored at home. The verse states *v'lachaya asher b'artzecha* — "for the wild animal that is in your land" — which is read as the governing principle: as long as there are, say, apples out in the field for any animal to take, one may maintain apples at home. But once nothing of that type remains available in the field, one must perform *biur* — removing the produce from one's possession. Rashi understands *biur* as putting the produce out for the animals, while Tosafot cites other sources indicating that *biur* means distributing the produce equally among the poor and among all comers. One may place one's stored produce outside, declare it available to all, allow everyone to take from it — and then take back a portion for oneself. What one cannot do is continue to hoard it alone.

### Geographic Regions

The Gemara had established by the end of the previous daf that *biur* operates according to three distinct geographic regions: Yehuda, Galil, and Ever HaYarden — Judea, the Galilee, and the Transjordan. So long as any produce of the relevant type remains available somewhere within one's region — even if not in one's immediate locale — one need not perform *biur*. Only when the produce has disappeared from the entire region does the obligation take effect.

### Transporting Produce

This question of regional availability had served as the backdrop for the prior discussion of a traveler leaving Israel with *shemita* produce. If one departs the Land with, for instance, apples, and the time of *biur* arrives while one is abroad, what does one do? A debate ensued: some hold that one distributes the produce wherever one happens to be, while others maintain that the verse *b'artzecha* — "in your land" — requires one to return to the Land of Israel and distribute the produce there. The Gemara had concluded with a memorable story: Rav Safra was leaving Israel when he realized he had a *miflakh* of wine with him. He asked the two students traveling with him how to rule. One cited a tradition requiring return to Israel; the other cited a tradition to the contrary. Rav Safra declared that he would follow the second, more lenient ruling — claiming it was because that student had the more reliable tradition. Rav Yosef then offered a pointed remark, quoting the verse *ami b'atzo yishal umaklo yagid lo* — "My people ask their stick, and their staff tells them" — a verse associated with necromancy and divination. The wordplay: *kol hamekel magid lo* — whoever gives him the lenient ruling, that is the one he wants to tell him the *halacha*.

## Rabbi Ilai & Fruit Status

### Unripe Dates Case

The Gemara now introduces a new episode. Rabbi Ilai cut down a palm branch during *shemita*, and that branch bore *kasnisa* — unripe dates that were not yet edible and were going to go to waste. The question immediately arises: *heichi avid hachi* — how could he do this? The Torah states that *shemita* produce is designated *l'ochla* — for eating — *v'lo l'hefseid* — and not for destruction. By cutting down the branch with unripe dates still on it, was he not wasting the produce?

One might suggest that the prohibition against waste applies only once the item *nachis l'peira* — has descended to the status of fruit, that is, once it is recognizable and developed as fruit. If the dates are still at such an early, unripe stage that they have not yet attained the legal status of fruit, then perhaps there is no issue with cutting down the branch even if those immature dates are discarded. This is a plausible reading — and, as will emerge, ultimately the correct one — but the Gemara does not accept it immediately, first exploring other angles.

Rashi makes explicit what might otherwise be left implicit: Rabbi Ilai cut the palm branch in order to use it for firewood, leaving behind the unripe dates. The question, therefore, is not one of *bal tashchit* with respect to the wood — using branches for firewood is a perfectly legitimate purpose — but rather what one does about the fruit on those branches.

### Protective Shell

The Gemara introduces the teaching of Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha: these *matchli* — the shell-like protective coverings that grow around unripe dates — are subject to the laws of *orlah*. The reason: *hoil v'nasu shomer l'pira* — since they serve as a protective covering for the fruit, they are treated as an extension of the fruit itself and thus bear *orlah* status. The Gemara then observes that this protective function is operative specifically *bekufri* — when the dates are still in their unripe stage, for it is then that the shell exists; as the fruit ripens, the shell falls away. The implication is striking: if the *matchli* qualifies as a *shomer l'pira* and thereby falls under the laws of *orlah* — which apply only to fruit — then the unripe date itself must already be considered a fruit, even at this early stage. Since *orlah* only applies to fruit, and since the shell is treated as an extension of the fruit, the Gemara concludes that *v'ka kari lehu peiri* — he is treating even the unripe date as fruit. If this is true regarding *orlah*, one would presume it should apply equally to *shemita*.

### Fruit Definition

However, this position is attributed to a specific authority: *hi d'amar k'Rabbi Yosi* — this follows the view of Rabbi Yosi, who holds that *smadar* — the very earliest emergence of the grape, before it has developed into anything recognizable — is already forbidden as *orlah* because it is already considered fruit: *smadar assur mipnei shehu peiri*. But *u'pligi rabbanan alei d'Rabbi Yosi* — the Rabbis dispute Rabbi Yosi. According to the Rabbis, produce is not considered fruit for purposes of *orlah* or *shemita* until a later stage of development. This would then explain how Rabbi Ilai could cut down that branch — he follows the more lenient position that the unripe dates have not yet attained legal fruit status, and therefore there is no issue with allowing them to go to waste.

### Beit Shammai/Hillel

The Gemara then asks: do the Rabbis really dispute Rabbi Yosi across the board, including by trees other than grapevines? The answer is found in a Mishnah that addresses precisely the case at hand. *Me'eimatai ein kotzetzin es ha'ilanos bishvi'is* — from when is it prohibited during *shemita* to cut down branches because of the fruit on them? Beit Shammai say: *kol ha'ilanos misheyotziu* — for all trees, as soon as any fruit emerges at all. Beit Hillel say: the carob from the time *misheyishaleshalu* — they begin to form into a chain-like cluster; the grapevines from *misheyagri'u* — a somewhat later stage of development; olives from the time they flower; and all other trees from when the fruit emerges. Thus Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel disagree only with respect to carob, grapevine, and olive. For all other trees, both agree that once the fruit emerges, it is considered fruit and may not be wasted.

### Smadar vs. Boser

Rav Assi then draws an explicit connection: *hu boser, hu girua* — the stage called *boser*, the intermediate unripe grape that is nonetheless somewhat developed, is the same stage referred to by Beit Hillel's phrase *misheyagri'u*. Furthermore, *hu pul halavan* — this corresponds in size to the white bean, a standard halakhic measurement. The Gemara asks, somewhat incredulously: can a grape really be compared to a white bean? The answer is that the comparison is to size: *shehu kepul halavan* — it is the size of the white bean.

This then clarifies the positions. Beit Shammai, who says even *smadar* constitutes fruit, parallels Rabbi Yosi. Beit Hillel, who says not until the *boser* / *girua* stage, parallels the Rabbis who argue on Rabbi Yosi. And crucially, for all other trees — those not in the carob-grapevine-olive group — Beit Hillel agrees with Beit Shammai that the fruit is considered fruit from the moment it emerges.

### Male Palms Resolution

This leads back to the difficulty: how could Rabbi Ilai cut down that palm branch? For palm trees, which are not among the three trees in dispute, everyone — including Beit Hillel — agrees that once the fruit emerges, it is considered fruit. So how could he discard the unripe dates without concern?

The Gemara's answer: *ella Rabbi Ilai bediniskhani katz* — Rabbi Ilai cut down male palm trees, or branches from male palm trees. Palm trees do in fact come in male and female varieties, and the fruit on male palms never fully ripens on the tree. Rashi explains that if one wishes to ripen the dates from male palms, one picks them and places them in baskets woven from palm fronds, where they then ripen off the tree. This is entirely analogous to fruits such as pears, which also fully ripen only after being picked. Thus Rabbi Ilai's action involved no waste at all: he cut down the branch for firewood, and the fruit from the male palm — which would not ripen on the tree in any case — could be picked and brought to ripen separately. There was no destruction of fruit, and the resolution precisely mirrors the intuition that had been suggested earlier in the discussion.

A related question was raised concerning *bal tashchit* more broadly. The halakhic parameters of *bal tashchit* are worth clarifying: the prohibition applies to wanton destruction without any constructive purpose. Using a branch for firewood — where there is a legitimate need and no waste of the wood itself — does not constitute *bal tashchit*, because the act serves a genuine purpose. The *bal tashchit* prohibition looks at the macro question of whether the use is legitimate, not at whether every incidental item in the transaction is maximally preserved. The concern about the unripe fruit, by contrast, was a more focused, micro-level question about *kedushat shevi'it* — the sanctity of *shemita* produce and whether that produce was fulfilling its purpose. These are distinct concerns, and the resolution of the second (that male palm fruit can ripen off the tree) addresses the *shemita* difficulty directly.

## Consumption Seasons

### Grapes & Avel

The Gemara now returns to the central theme of *biur* and the question of how long one may continue consuming stored *shemita* produce of various types. A *baraita* teaches: *ochlin ba'anavim ad sheyichlu dalyos shel* Avel — one may eat grapes as long as there are still grapes on the trellises in the place called Avel, which was apparently known as the region where grapes could be found latest in the season. The underlying principle is the one established earlier: as long as produce of a given type remains available somewhere within the region — even if not in one's own locale — one may continue to keep and consume it at home.

But the *baraita* makes an important qualification: *im yesh me'ucharos meihem, ochlin aleihem* — if there are grapes that remain available even later than those in Avel, one relies on those later grapes. The name of the place is not a limit; it is a rule of thumb. The true governing principle remains subjective observation — as long as something is actually still available out in the field. These geographic names serve as practical shorthand, not as absolute cutoff points.

### Olives/Figs/Dates

The *baraita* continues: *ochlin bazayisim ad sheyichleh acharon shebiTkoa* — one may eat olives until there are no more olives in Tekoa. Rabbi Eliezer says: until there are none left in Gush Chalav, since he holds that olives were available there even later. A quantitative standard is also provided: *kedei she'ani yotzei v'eino motzei lo benofo v'lo be'ikaro rova* — once a poor person going out could not find even a *rova* (roughly a quart) of olives, neither on the branches nor fallen to the ground, olives are considered to have ceased being available.

For *grogeros* — pressed or dried figs — one may eat them until those in Beit Hini are no longer available.

### Dried Figs

A subtle difficulty: the *baraita* speaks of eating dried figs, yet what remains in the field are fresh figs, not dried ones. Why is the rule for dried figs determined by the availability of fresh figs in the field?

The answer lies in a distinctive characteristic of figs: unlike most fruits, which ripen all at once within a single season, figs ripen progressively over an extended period. As a result, one might have dried figs at home from figs picked months earlier, and those dried figs might still be edible long after the main season has passed. The *baraita* therefore finds it necessary to specify the rule for dried figs — because figs alone present the scenario where one is eating preserved produce from one harvest period while figs continue ripening in the field through a much later season. The clock for *biur* of those dried figs runs until the fresh figs of Beit Hini are no longer available.

### Small Figs Debate

Rav Yehuda then raises a challenge: *lo hutru pagei Beit Hini* — the small figs of Beit Hini were only deemed relevant in the context of *ma'aser*, not *shemita*. The original ruling had been *pagei Beit Hini v'ahini deTuvina chayavin b'ma'aser* — the small figs of Beit Hini and the dates of Tuvina are obligated in *ma'aser* despite their small size. One might have inferred that since these small figs are halakhically significant as fruit (sufficient to require *ma'aser*), their presence in the field extends the period during which one may continue eating one's stored larger figs at home. Rav Yehuda rejects this inference: the small figs of Beit Hini may be large enough to be considered fruit for *ma'aser* purposes, but that determination in the domain of *ma'aser* does not carry over to establish that their continued availability in the field extends the *shemita* eating window for the larger varieties one has stored at home. The two categories — *ma'aser* and *shemita biur* — are governed by distinct criteria.

### Holiday Markers

A second *baraita* presents the same rules in terms of calendar dates rather than geographic markers: *ochlin be'anavim ad haPesach* — one may eat grapes until Pesach; olives until Shavuot; dried figs until Chanukah; and dates until Purim. It is notable, and not without pedagogical value, that these seasonal cutoffs align conveniently with the major holidays — a natural mnemonic.

The tension between these two *baraitot* — one giving geographic markers, the other giving calendar dates — is apparent. What, for instance, is the relationship between "until the last olive in Tekoa" and "until Shavuot"? The Gemara resolves: *idi v'idi chad shiura hu* — both formulations are describing the same measure. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who spoke descriptively ("until there are none left in Tekoa"), and the second *baraita*, which translates that into a calendar date, are both expressing the same practical reality: that is approximately when the olives of Tekoa run out.

### Rav Bibi's Alternative

Rav Bibi reports that Rabbi Yochanan reversed the last two items: he held that dried figs could be eaten until Purim and dates until Chanukah — a striking difference, given the large gap of time between the two holidays. The Gemara takes note but does not resolve the discrepancy by distinguishing between the positions. Rather, since the *baraita* itself had already acknowledged that the calendar dates are only approximations — *im yesh me'ucharos meihem, ochlin aleihem* — the discrepancy may simply reflect the inherent imprecision in translating observation-based rules into fixed calendar markers.

### Observation Principle

This pattern raises a broader halakhic question about the tension between subjective observation and fixed, quantifiable standards. The most rigorous standard is direct observation: is this produce still available in the field? But people want measurable, calendar-based rules. The Gemara here, as in other contexts — the parallel case of the eighteen-minute standard for *chametz* being one prominent example — acknowledges both the practical utility of fixed markers and the ultimate primacy of the observational principle. The fixed dates on the calendar are heuristics; the underlying rule is always whether produce of that type is still genuinely available somewhere in the field.

## Geographic Characteristi…

### Mnemonic Markers

The Gemara cites a teaching of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: *siman leharim milin* — the characteristic of mountains is *milin* (a type of gallnut); *siman la'amakim dkalim* — the characteristic of valleys is palm trees, as anyone who has traveled to the low-lying areas near the Dead Sea can observe; *siman lenachalim kanim* — the characteristic of wadis is bullrushes; *siman lishfela shikma* — the characteristic of the lowlands (*shfela*) is the sycamore tree. A Scriptural support is adduced: *vayiten hamelech et hakesef biYerushalayim ka'avanim v'et ha'arazim natan kashikmim asher bashfela* — Solomon made silver in Jerusalem as plentiful as stones, and cedar trees as plentiful as the sycamores in the *shfela*. From this verse one sees that the *shfela* is identified by its sycamore trees. Although Rabban Shimon acknowledges that this verse is not a formal proof but a *zecher ladavar* — a textual hint or allusion — it supports the mnemonic association.

### Bikurim Application

The practical significance of these geographic identifications is spelled out. With respect to *bikurim*: *ein mevi'im bikurim ela mishivat haminim* — one brings *bikurim* only from the seven species. Even when bringing dates, one may not bring from palm trees that grow in the mountains — *v'lo midkalim shebeharim* — because those are not the paradigmatic palm trees. The true date palms, yielding the finest fruit, grow in the valleys. Conversely, the finest grains do not grow in the valleys. Each species must come from its optimal natural habitat, and these geographic markers identify which trees and crops qualify as the finest exemplars of their kind for *bikurim* purposes.

Similarly, the wadi (*nachal*) identification by bullrushes has relevance for the law of *egla arufa*: the *nachal eitan* to which the heifer of *egla arufa* is brought must be a wadi in the halakhic sense, and a wadi without bullrushes does not fulfill the criteria of a paradigmatic wadi.

### Business Application

The *shfela* identification by sycamore trees has relevance for commercial transactions: *nafka mina lemikach umimkar* — if one contracts to sell property described as being in the *shfela*, and the land lacks sycamore trees, it may not halakhically qualify as *shfela* at all. Once this business application is raised, the Gemara generalizes: *kulhu nami lemikach umimkar* — all of these geographic identifiers could similarly be relevant to commercial law. If a seller represents land as "mountain" and it has no *milin* trees, one might question whether it truly meets the halakhic definition of mountain. These botanical-geographic markers thus serve not merely as mnemonics but as legal definitions of terrain types, with consequences across multiple areas of *halacha*.

## Animal Sales Customs

### Custom Variations

The Gemara now returns to the broader theme of place-based customs, presenting a Mishnah that is treated more extensively in Masechet Avodah Zarah: *makom shenagu limkor behema daka lenochrim mocherin* — in a place where the custom was to sell small grazing animals (sheep and goats) to non-Jews, one may sell them; *makom shenagu shelo limkor, ein mocherin* — in a place where the custom was not to sell even these, one does not sell them.

The Bavli understands the primary concern underlying these restrictions to be a *Shabbat* concern: if a cow or horse is loaned or rented to a non-Jew and that non-Jew uses it for labor on *Shabbat*, the Jewish owner has permitted his animal to perform *melacha* on *Shabbat* — a violation of *behemtecha* ("your animal" shall rest). Selling creates a risk that the transaction might devolve into a lending arrangement before the sale is finalized, and one's animal would then be performing *melacha* on *Shabbat* while still halakhically in Jewish ownership. Small grazing animals present somewhat less of this concern, which is why some communities permitted their sale while restricting large animals.

However, one might also read these restrictions through a different lens — not purely *Shabbat*-based, but connected to broader concerns about maintaining Jewish control over the agricultural assets of the Land of Israel: who owns the land and who controls the animals that work it.

The Mishnah concludes with a rule that applies in all places universally: *behema gassa* — large animals such as cows and horses — may not be sold to non-Jews. This restriction extends even to *agalim vesiyachin* — calves and foals — even though they cannot yet perform labor. Once the Rabbis established the prohibition, they applied it to the entire category. Nor does it matter whether the animal is *shalem* (uninjured) or *shavur* (with a broken limb), since the entire category was prohibited. Rabbi Yehuda permits the sale of an injured animal, reasoning that it will be slaughtered immediately and will never be used for *melacha*. Ben Beteira permits the sale of horses specifically, on the grounds that riding an animal does not constitute *melacha* in the halakhic sense — because of the principle *chai nosei et atzmo* (a living being supports its own weight), such that one does not technically violate the *Shabbat* prohibition by having a non-Jew ride one's horse, since the horse's living body provides its own balance and support. The Gemara in Avodah Zarah develops these positions in full.

## Roasted Meat on Pesach

### Eating Custom

The Mishnah now arrives at a topic of direct relevance to Pesach: *makom shenagu le'echol tzali beleilei Pesachim ochlim* — a place where the custom is to eat roasted meat on the night of Pesach, one eats it; *makom shenagu shelo le'echol, ein ochlim* — where the custom is not to eat roasted meat, one does not.

This passage presents a striking ambiguity about what the normative baseline is and what constitutes the special *minhag*. From an Ashkenazic perspective, the familiar practice is not to eat roasted meat on the *Seder* night, lest it appear that one is eating a *korban* outside of Yerushalayim — *shelo yochlu d'mechzi ke'ochel Pesach bachutz*, as Rashi frames the concern. On this reading, the *minhag* is restraint, and those who eat roasted meat are following a place-based exception. But Sephardic practice runs in the opposite direction: many Sephardic communities eat roasted meat — some specifically roasted lamb or goat — *davka* on the *Seder* night, as a *zecher lePesach*. On that reading, the *minhag* is to have roasted meat as a commemorative act, and the restraint is the departure from the norm.

Both orientations find expression in the Mishnah, and both are grounded in the same underlying tension: there is a push toward commemorating the *korban Pesach* through roasted meat, and simultaneously a push to keep one's distance from anything that too closely resembles the actual *korban* being brought outside its proper context. This tension plays out directly in the Gemara that follows.

### Rav Yehuda Restriction

Rav Yehuda Amar Rav: *assur le'adam sheyomar basar zeh lePesach* — it is prohibited for a person to designate meat on the *Seder* night by saying "this meat is for the *Pesach*," because *mipnei shenireh kemakdish behemato v'ochel kodshim bachutz* — it appears as though he has sanctified his animal and is eating consecrated meat outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. This is why at the *Seder*, when we recite *Pesach matzah maror*, we say *Pesach shehayu avoteinu ochlim* — "the *Pesach* offering that our ancestors ate" — rather than pointing to the meat and calling it *Pesach zeh* — "this Pesach."

### Rav Pappa Clarification

Rav Pappa refines the prohibition: *davka basar* — this applies specifically to meat. *Aval chitey lo* — but one may say "this wheat is for *Pesach*," because wheat cannot be construed as a *korban Pesach* in any sense. The word *lePesach* in connection with wheat is immediately understood as referring to the festival, not the offering. Meat is different precisely because it is capable of being interpreted as a reference to the *korban Pesach*, and that ambiguity is what the prohibition targets.

### Theodosius Case

The Gemara now cites a *baraita* that introduces Theodosius of Rome. Rabbi Yosi (or, in an alternate version, Rabbi Shimon) reports: *Tudos Ish Romi hinhig et bnei Romi le'echol gedayim mekulasin beleilei Pesachim* — Theodosius of Rome instituted the practice for the Jews of Rome to eat whole roasted goats on the nights of Pesach. *Mekulasin* means the goats were roasted whole over an open flame, exactly as the *korban Pesach* was prepared — *rosho al kra'av v'al kirbo* — with the head over the legs and the innards, precisely replicating the form of the offering. The Rabbis sent him a message: *ilmaleh Tudos attah, gazarnu alecha niduy* — were you not who you are, Theodosius, we would place you in *cherem* — *she'atah ma'achil et Yisrael kodshim bachutz* — for causing Israel to eat *kodshim* outside the *Beit HaMikdash*.

The Gemara immediately clarifies: *kodshim salta da'atach?* — could it literally be *kodshim*? Obviously not; these are ordinary animals. *Ella eima karov le'hachil Yisrael kodshim bachutz* — rather, it is as if he was causing them to eat *kodashim* outside, because the whole-roasted form so closely resembles the actual *korban Pesach* that the distinction becomes dangerously blurred.

From here the Gemara draws a distinction: *mekulas in, she'eino mekulas lo* — if the animal is roasted whole (*mekulas*), the prohibition applies regardless of whether one verbally designates it, because the form alone already creates an impermissible resemblance to the *korban*. If it is not roasted whole, the prohibition is triggered only by explicit verbal designation — *pireish in, lo pireish lo*. Thus, either the visual resemblance alone (whole roasting) or the verbal declaration alone (saying "this is for the Pesach") creates the problem; absent both, there is no issue with roasted meat per se.

It is worth noting that while this Gemara presents Theodosius as having been censured, one should not conclude too quickly that the practice of eating roasted meat was universally rejected. The Mishnah itself attests to communities that maintained the custom, and other Tannaitic sources indicate that some *tanna'im* were specifically careful to have roasted meat on Pesach. The *minhag Sefardim* of eating roasted meat — and in some communities specifically roasted lamb — reflects a legitimate reading of the same Mishnah that the Ashkenazic tradition reads in the opposite direction.

### Rav Sheshet Challenge

The Gemara then reports that Remi bar Chama taught this *baraita* — the criticism of Theodosius — in the name of Rabbi Shimon (rather than Rabbi Yosi). Rav Sheshet challenges this attribution. If the *baraita* is reported in the name of Rabbi Yosi, the criticism is intelligible. But if it is in the name of Rabbi Shimon, there is a difficulty: *v'hatnan, Rabbi Shimon poter shelo hitnadev kederech ha-mitnadvim* — we have a Mishnah in which Rabbi Shimon exempts a person who made a vow that does not conform to the normal manner of vowing. The case involves someone who declares an obligation to bring a *mincha* from barley. Since vowed *minchot* can only be of wheat (barley *mincha* is the *sotah* offering, not a vowed offering), the statement is self-contradictory. Some authorities would truncate the statement and treat it as a valid vow to bring a wheat *mincha*. Rabbi Shimon says: we take the complete statement as it was spoken, and because the whole statement is internally contradictory and meaningless, the person is exempt — he need not bring anything.

How is this relevant to Theodosius? Because, according to Tosafot's understanding of the issue, the concern with Theodosius was that his followers might be thought to have sanctified their animals as *korban Pesach* offerings and then consumed them outside Yerushalayim. But according to Rabbi Shimon, such a declaration would be legally meaningless — there is no such thing as a *korban Pesach* brought outside of Yerushalayim, just as there is no such thing as a vowed *mincha* of barley. A statement that is inherently impossible to fulfill is not a valid declaration at all. If one explicitly says "I am consecrating this animal to bring as a *korban Pesach* here in Rome," the animal acquires no *kedusha* — the declaration is void. Note: there is an *issur* in bringing a *korban* outside the *Beit HaMikdash* (*makriv bachutz*), but if the consecration itself is void *ab initio* because the stated purpose is impossible, the concern evaporates. And if we cannot even construct a scenario in which Theodosius's practice constituted a real violation, why would Rabbi Shimon have criticized him?

### Rava's Resolution

Ravina then turns the question around: even if it is taught in the name of Rabbi Yosi, is it unproblematic? The same challenge should apply — if the declaration is impossible to fulfill, why is any criticism warranted?

Rava resolves this: *Reb Shimon b'shitat Reb Yosi amrah* — Rabbi Shimon's ruling draws on a position of Rabbi Yosi. There is a broader debate about what to do when someone makes a statement that contains an internal contradiction: do you truncate the statement at the point where it first becomes coherent, or do you accept the full statement including its final words? Rabbi Yosi holds *bi-gmar devarav adam nitpas* — a person is bound by the full conclusion of his words. In the case of an *olah shelamim* — where someone says an animal is simultaneously an *olah* and a *shelamim* — Rabbi Yosi accepts the whole statement and resolves it by dividing the proceeds between both types of offering. Rabbi Shimon goes even further: he too accepts the full statement (agreeing with Rabbi Yosi that one does not truncate), but unlike the *olah shelamim* case — which can be satisfied by a splitting of proceeds — the *mincha* of barley cannot be satisfied in any way, and so Rabbi Shimon is prepared to throw out the entire statement and exempt the person entirely.

Thus the relationship between the two authorities: *Reb Shimon savar lah k'Rebbi Yosi* — Rabbi Shimon holds like Rabbi Yosi, that one looks at the full statement. *V'Rebbi Yosi lo savar lah k'Reb Shimon* — but Rabbi Yosi does not hold like Rabbi Shimon; Rabbi Yosi, when the full statement can be partially satisfied, seeks to make it work rather than void it entirely. Only Rabbi Shimon is willing to void an impossible statement in its entirety.

It follows that according to Rabbi Shimon, even a verbal declaration consecrating an animal as a *korban Pesach* in Rome would be entirely void — and therefore Rabbi Shimon, consistently, should have had no problem with Theodosius's practice. According to Rabbi Yosi, by contrast, there is no analogous principle voiding the declaration, and the criticism stands.

## Theodosius Identity

### Derasha on Martyrdom

Having raised the question of whether Theodosius was a great scholar or merely a powerful patron, the Gemara adduces evidence. A *baraita* records: *od zu darash Tudos Ish Romi* — Theodosius of Rome expounded the following additional *derasha*. (The "additional" implies he was known for multiple teachings.) What was his *drasha*?

*Mah rau Chananiah Mishael va-Azariah she-masru atzmam al kedushat ha-Shem le-kivshan ha-eish?* — What moved Chananiah, Mishael, and Azariah to give up their lives and allow themselves to be thrown into Nevuchadnezzar's fiery furnace? *Nas'u kal va-chomer b'atzman mi-tzfarde'im* — they reasoned by *kal va-chomer* from the frogs of the plague of Egypt. The logic: *u-mah tzfarde'im she-ein mitzuavin al kedushat ha-Shem* — frogs are not commanded to sanctify God's name, yet the verse says *u-vau b'tanurecha u-v'misharosecha* — they came into the ovens and the kneading troughs. *Eimasai misharos metzuyot eitzel tannur* — when are kneading troughs found next to an oven? *B'sha'ah she-ha-tannur cham* — when the oven is hot. So the frogs threw themselves into a burning oven simply because God commanded them to enter it. *Anu she-mitzuavin al kedushat ha-Shem, al achas kamma v'khamma* — we who are commanded to sanctify God's name, how much more so must we be prepared to give our lives.

Rashi understands the question as how they knew they should give their lives rather than bow to an idol. Tosafot raises a more nuanced reading: perhaps the bowing demanded by Nevuchadnezzar was not technically *avoda zara* — perhaps it was merely bowing to a statue without real idolatrous intent — and even so, Chananiah, Mishael, and Azariah chose to sanctify God's name by entering the fire. Or, according to Tosafot's second suggestion, perhaps they could have escaped — and they chose not to. In either case, they went beyond the strict *yehareg v'al ya'avor* requirement.

This reading of the story is historically and intellectually significant. As scholars such as Chaim Soloveitchik have noted, Tosafot's reading cannot be divorced from the historical realities of the Crusades, during which Ashkenazic Jews martyred themselves — sometimes for acts that were not halakhically among the three cardinal sins that require one to die rather than transgress, and sometimes when escape was possible. Tosafot reads into the story of Chananiah, Mishael, and Azariah a model for this kind of voluntary *kiddush Hashem*: that there exists a category of optional *kiddush Hashem*, a willingness to give one's life for God's honor even when not strictly commanded to do so. Whether such voluntary martyrdom is permitted or is equivalent to suicide is itself a subject of halakhic debate, but Tosafot's position is that such a category exists and is laudable.

The very fact that Theodosius could formulate and transmit this sophisticated *drasha* — one with deep halakhic, aggadic, and existential dimensions — establishes his credentials as a serious Torah scholar, not merely a wealthy patron.

### Financial Support Reason

Having established his scholarship, the Gemara now explains why the Rabbis held back from actually placing him in *cherem*: *matya malya le'kisa de'talmidei chachamim havah* — Theodosius was someone who filled the wallets of Torah scholars. He provided financial support for the *talmidei chachamim*, managing investments on their behalf and securing their economic wellbeing. One does not publicly censure one's major benefactors, even when they cross a line halakhically.

### Reward for Support

The Gemara closes with a teaching of Rabbi Yochanan that provides the theological underpinning for the high regard in which such a supporter is held: *kol hamatil melai le'kis talmidei chachamim, zoche v'yoshev bi'yeshiva shel ma'ala* — whoever places "filler" into the wallet of Torah scholars — that is, whoever invests in their financial wellbeing — merits to sit in the *yeshiva shel ma'ala*, the heavenly academy, in the world to come. The wealthy person who supports Torah scholars will ultimately sit alongside them and learn Torah together with them in the realm above. The Scriptural support: *ki betzel ha-chochma betzel ha-kesef* — "in the shade of wisdom is the shade of money." Provide the shade of money, and you will ultimately dwell in the shade of wisdom.
