# Rosh Hashanah 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## World Creation Debate

### Rabbi Eliezer Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בְּתִשְׁרִי נִבְרָא הָעוֹלָם, בְּתִשְׁרִי נוֹלְדוּ אָבוֹת, בְּתִשְׁרִי מֵתוּ אָבוֹת, בַּפֶּסַח נוֹלַד יִצְחָק, בְּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה נִפְקְדָה שָׂרָה רָחֵל וְחַנָּה, בְּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה יָצָא יוֹסֵף מִבֵּית הָאֲסוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that the *tanna'im* disagreed about this point: **Rabbi Eliezer says: In Tishrei the world was created; in Tishrei the Patriarchs were born; in Tishrei the Patriarchs died; on Passover Isaac was born; on Rosh HaShana Sarah, Rachel, and Hannah were remembered** by God and conceived; **on Rosh HaShana Joseph came out from prison;**

The *gemara* opens with a *baraita* that frames one of the great calendrical disputes of rabbinic literature: when precisely was the world created? The *baraita* is found at the very bottom of 10b, and serves as the foundation for the extended discussion on 11b.

*Rabbi Eliezer omer*: *be-Tishrei nivra ha-olam* — the world was created in *Tishrei*. Following from this premise, *be-Tishrei noldu avot* and *be-Tishrei metu avot* — the forefathers were born and died in *Tishrei*. There are, however, notable exceptions to the *Tishrei* pattern within Rabbi Eliezer's own position: *be-Pesach nolad Yitzchak* — Yitzchak was born on Pesach — and *be-Rosh Hashanah nifkeda Sarah Rachel ve-Chanah* — Sarah, Rachel, and Chanah were remembered (that is, became pregnant) on Rosh Hashanah. As will become clear from the *gemara*, these two items are related: if Sarah was remembered on Rosh Hashanah, the calculation requires that Yitzchak be born approximately six to seven months later, which points toward Pesach. Rabbi Eliezer further holds that *be-Rosh Hashanah yatza Yosef mi-beit ha-asurim* — Yosef was freed from prison on Rosh Hashanah — and *be-Rosh Hashanah batlah avodah me-avoteinu be-Mitzrayim* — the forced labor in Egypt ceased on Rosh Hashanah, even though the actual redemption, *be-Nissan nigalu*, took place in Nissan. And crucially: *be-Tishrei atidin lehigael* — the future redemption will occur in *Tishrei*.

### Rabbi Yehoshua Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: בְּנִיסָן נִבְרָא הָעוֹלָם, בְּנִיסָן נוֹלְדוּ אָבוֹת, בְּנִיסָן מֵתוּ אָבוֹת, בְּפֶסַח נוֹלַד יִצְחָק, בְּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה נִפְקְדָה שָׂרָה רָחֵל וְחַנָּה, בְּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה יָצָא יוֹסֵף מִבֵּית הָאֲסוּרִין, בְּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה בָּטְלָה עֲבוֹדָה מֵאֲבוֹתֵינוּ בְּמִצְרַיִם, בְּנִיסָן נִגְאֲלוּ בְּנִיסָן עֲתִידִין לִיגָּאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua** disagrees and **says: In Nisan the world was created; in Nisan the Patriarchs were born; in Nisan the Patriarchs died; on Passover Isaac was born; on Rosh HaShana Sarah, Rachel, and Hannah were remembered** by God and conceived sons; **on Rosh HaShana Joseph came out from prison; on Rosh HaShana our forefathers' slavery in Egypt ceased; in Nisan** the Jewish people **were redeemed** from Egypt; **and in Nisan in the future** the Jewish people **will be redeemed** in the final redemption.

Rabbi Yehoshua's position is a systematic inversion. *Be-Nissan nivra ha-olam* — the world was created in Nissan. *Be-Nissan noldu avot*, *be-Nissan metu avot* — the forefathers were born and died in Nissan. He concedes the same exceptions: *be-Pesach nolad Yitzchak*, and *be-Rosh Hashanah nifkeda Sarah Rachel ve-Chanah*. He likewise agrees that Yosef was freed and the labor ceased on Rosh Hashanah. Where he sharply diverges from Rabbi Eliezer is on the future redemption: *u-be-Nissan nigalu u-be-Nissan atidin lehigael* — just as they were redeemed in Nissan, so too will the future redemption come in Nissan.

### Textual Analysis

The *gemara* now proceeds to unpack the textual proofs on both sides, focusing on the verses from the creation narrative itself. Each position turns on close readings of the vegetation imagery in the first chapter of *Bereishit*, and it is to those proofs that the *gemara* immediately turns.

---

## Eliezer Vegetation Proof

### Vegetation Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן שֶׁבְּתִשְׁרִי נִבְרָא הָעוֹלָם — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֱלֹהִים תַּדְשֵׁא הָאָרֶץ דֶּשֶׁא עֵשֶׂב מַזְרִיעַ זֶרַע עֵץ פְּרִי״. אֵיזֶהוּ חֹדֶשׁ שֶׁהָאָרֶץ מוֹצִיאָה דְּשָׁאִים וְאִילָן מָלֵא פֵּירוֹת — הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר זֶה תִּשְׁרִי. וְאוֹתוֹ הַפֶּרֶק זְמַן רְבִיעָה הָיְתָה, וְיָרְדוּ גְּשָׁמִים וְצִימֵּחוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֵד יַעֲלֶה מִן הָאָרֶץ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains these matters in detail: **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says: From where** is it derived **that the world was created in** the month of **Tishrei? As it is stated: "And God said: Let the earth bring forth grass, herb yielding seed, and fruit tree yielding fruit after its kind"** (Genesis 1:11). **Which is the month in which the earth brings forth grass and the trees are full of** ripe **fruit? You must say** that **this is Tishrei.** And a further proof that the world was created in Tishrei is that when the world was first created, it needed rain so that the plants would grow, **and the period** beginning with Tishrei **is a time of rain, and rain fell and** the plants **grew, as it is stated: "But there went up a mist from the earth,** and watered the whole face of the ground" (Genesis 2:6).

The *gemara* cites the *baraita* presenting Rabbi Eliezer's proof from vegetation: *mina'in she-be-Tishrei nivra ha-olam?* — from where do we know the world was created in *Tishrei*? *She-ne'emar*: *vayomer Elohim tadshe ha-aretz deshe esev mazria zera etz peri oseh peri* — God said, let the earth be covered with grass, seed-bearing herbage, and fruit trees making fruit. *Eizu chodesh she-ha-aretz motzia deshaim ve-ilan maleh perot?* — what month is it when the earth brings forth grass and the tree is filled with fruit? *Hevei omer zeh Tishrei* — it must be *Tishrei*.

### Tishrei Fruit Season

The intuition behind Rabbi Eliezer's proof is straightforward: apple-picking season falls in September, in the beginning of autumn. That is when trees in the Northern Hemisphere — and, as the *gemara* assumes, in the Mediterranean climate of *Eretz Yisrael* — are filled with ripe fruit. The key word in the verse is *maleh perot*: the tree is already full with fruit. This points to *Tishrei*, not to spring.

### Grass Growth Climate

There is, however, a complication regarding the grass portion of the verse. *Rashi* draws attention to the difference between two possible *girsaot*. Our standard text reads *motzia deshaim* — the earth brings forth, or sprouts, its grass — which would seem to suggest a spring context, since in the Mediterranean climate grass sprouts in the spring, not the fall. But *Rashi* appears to have had the *girsa* of *mele'ah deshaim* — that the earth is already coated and covered with grass — consistent with his comment that *tadshe ha-aretz titkaseh ve-titlabesh bi-deshaim*, the earth should be clothed and covered in grass. This is also the reading the *Bach* explicitly adopts in his textual emendation.

The distinction matters because it bears on the contrast the *gemara* will draw with Rabbi Yehoshua's proof. The verb *tadshe* in Rabbi Eliezer's verse, under this reading, implies a full coating of grass already in place, whereas Rabbi Yehoshua's verse uses *vatotzei* — the earth *brought forth* — which connotes initial sprouting. Under the *Bach*'s *girsa*, then, Rabbi Eliezer's verse describes a world already blanketed with grass and laden with fruit — both signs of *Tishrei* fullness — while Rabbi Yehoshua's verse describes a world just beginning to produce, which fits Nissan.

The *gemara* does not, it should be noted, dwell at length on the grass question; its primary focus is on the contrast between *etz peri* and *etz oseh peri* — whether the tree is already a full fruit-bearing tree or merely beginning to produce. That contrast carries the weight of the dispute.

### Tishrei Rains

Rabbi Eliezer's proof is further reinforced by an observation about the rainy season. *Ve-oto ha-perek zman revia hayta* — that period is the time of *revia*, the beginning of the rains. The rains begin to fall in *Cheshvan*, shortly after *Tishrei*. *Ve-yardu geshamim ve-tzamchu* — the rains descend and the vegetation sprouts. The *gemara* cites the verse from *Bereishit* 2: *ve-ed ya'aleh min ha-aretz ve-hishka et kol pnei ha-adamah* — a mist rose from the earth and watered the entire surface of the ground — as an allusion to this cycle.

It is worth pausing on the term *revia* itself. The word does not mean "a fourth" or "a quarter" in this context; it is connected rather to imagery of fertility and penetration — the rain that waters and fertilizes the earth, a motif running through the agricultural imagination of *Chazal*. It is because of this seasonal reality that we begin reciting *mashiv ha-ruach u-morid ha-gashem* and *ve-ten tal u-matar* in the *Tishrei*-*Cheshvan* period. The world's fertility cycle, on Rabbi Eliezer's view, begins in *Tishrei*.

## Yehoshua Vegetation Proof

### Sprouting Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן שֶׁבְּנִיסָן נִבְרָא הָעוֹלָם — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַתּוֹצֵא הָאָרֶץ דֶּשֶׁא עֵשֶׂב מַזְרִיעַ זֶרַע וְעֵץ עוֹשֶׂה פְּרִי״. אֵיזֶהוּ חֹדֶשׁ שֶׁהָאָרֶץ מְלֵיאָה דְּשָׁאִים וְאִילָן מוֹצִיא פֵּירוֹת — הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר, זֶה נִיסָן. וְאוֹתוֹ הַפֶּרֶק, זְמַן בְּהֵמָה וְחַיָּה וָעוֹף שֶׁמִּזְדַּוְּוגִין זֶה אֵצֶל זֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לָבְשׁוּ כָרִים הַצֹּאן וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua says: From where** is it derived **that the world was created in** the month of **Nisan? As it is stated: "And the earth brought forth grass, herb yielding seed** after its kind, **and tree yielding fruit"** (Genesis 1:12). **Which is the month in which the earth is full of grass and the trees** begin to **bring forth fruit? You must say** that **this is Nisan.** And further proof that the world was created in Nisan is that when the world was first created, the animals had to breed in order to fill the world, **and the period** beginning with Nisan **is a time when cattle, and beasts, and birds mate with one another, as it is stated: "The flocks are clothed in the meadows,** and the valleys are wrapped in grain; they shout for joy, they also sing" (Psalms 65:14).

Rabbi Yehoshua's proof runs in the opposite direction. *Mina'in she-be-Nissan nivra ha-olam?* — from where do we know the world was created in Nissan? *She-ne'emar*: *vatotzei ha-aretz deshe esev mazria zera ve-etz oseh peri* — and the earth brought forth grass, seed-bearing herbage, and a tree *making* fruit. Following the *Bach*'s *girsa*, *motzia deshaim* in this verse connotes the earth just beginning to push out grass — initial sprouting, not a full covering. And the critical distinction: it is not *etz peri*, a full fruit tree, but *etz oseh peri* — a tree in the process of producing fruit, not yet laden with it. *Hevei omer ze Nissan* — this is Nissan.

### Nissan Fertility

Rabbi Yehoshua adds a second dimension beyond the botanical. *Ve-oto ha-perek zman behema ve-chaya ve-of she-mizdavjin ze im ze* — that is the season when domesticated animals, wild animals, and birds mate with one another. The spring is the season of animal fertility. The *gemara* cites the verse *livshu charim ha-tzon* — the meadows are clothed with flocks — which the *gemara* used earlier in a different context to establish that the *rosh hashanah* for sheep and for *ma'aser behema* falls in *Elul* or *Tishrei*, precisely because their mating season is in Nissan. The connection between the imagery of creation and the fecundity of the natural world is most powerfully evoked by spring.

### Full Stature Creation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ נָמֵי, הָא כְּתִיב: ״עֵץ עוֹשֶׂה פְּרִי״! הָהוּא לִבְרָכָה לְדוֹרוֹת הוּא דִּכְתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to the opinion of **the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Eliezer, **isn't it written: "And tree yielding fruit,"** indicating that the world was created at a time when the trees were just beginning to form their fruit? The Gemara answers: **That** verse **is written as a blessing for** future **generations,** that then too they will form their fruit.

One might ask: how does Rabbi Eliezer respond to the verse *etz oseh peri*, which sounds like the tree is not yet full of fruit — fitting Nissan, not *Tishrei*? The *gemara* answers: *hahu li-vracha le-dorot hu de-chtiv* — that phrase was written not to describe the state of the trees at the moment of creation, but to convey the blessing for all future generations, namely that trees possess the capacity and nature to produce fruit. The word *oseh* indicates what the tree *does* — its essential character — not its momentary state. At the moment of creation itself, under Rabbi Eliezer's view, the trees were already filled with fruit. It was *Tishrei*.

## Full Stature Doctrine

### Maturity Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ נָמֵי, הָא כְּתִיב: ״עֵץ פְּרִי״? הָהוּא כִּדְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: כׇּל מַעֲשֵׂה בְרֵאשִׁית לְקוֹמָתָן נִבְרְאוּ, לְדַעְתָּן נִבְרְאוּ, לְצִבְיוֹנָן נִבְרְאוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְכֻלּוּ הַשָּׁמַיִם וְהָאָרֶץ וְכׇל צְבָאָם״, אַל תִּקְרֵי ״צְבָאָם״, אֶלָּא ״צִבְיוֹנָם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to ask: **And** according to the opinion of **the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Yehoshua, **isn't it written: "Fruit tree,"** indicating that the world was created in a season when the trees were already filled with their fruit? The Gemara answers: **That** verse may be understood **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: All** the **acts of Creation** were created **with their** full **stature,** immediately fit to bear fruit; **they were created with their** full mental **capacities; they were created with their** full **form. As it is stated: "And the heavens and the earth were finished, and all their host"** (Genesis 2:1). **Do not read** it as **"their host [*tzeva'am*]"; rather,** read it as **their form [*tzivyonam*],** which implies that the trees were created filled with ripe fruit.

The *gemara* now turns to Rabbi Yehoshua's response to the counter-challenge. Rabbi Yehoshua must explain the verse *etz peri* — a full fruit tree — which sounds like the tree is already mature and laden, consistent with *Tishrei*. The answer invokes a famous principle attributed to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *kol ma'aseh bereishit be-komasan nivre'u* — all creations of the six days were created already in their full stature. Adam and Chava were not created as one-day-old infants; the trees were not created as saplings. All things came into being fully formed. Therefore, *etz peri* does not indicate that the fruit was already present, but only that it was a mature, full-grown fruit tree — which it would be even in Nissan, when the fruit was just beginning to develop. The *etz peri* describes the tree's full physical stature; the *etz oseh peri* describes the fruit's early emergence.

### Consent Doctrine

The *gemara* continues: *leda'atan nivre'u* — they were created with their acquiescence, or will. This is a striking and philosophically provocative idea. God, as it were, asked the things of creation whether they wished to be created. There is an obvious circularity here — how can a tree consent to its own creation before it exists? — and the *gemara* does not resolve this directly. The image may point to some pre-existent potential or form, perhaps analogous to the Platonic notion of forms entering the physical world, or to the Kabbalistic idea of the will to create as itself a kind of primordial reality that precedes the act of creation. What is powerful about the idea is the sense that the natural world is not merely passive and inert, but participates in some sense in its own coming-into-being. The *gemara* elsewhere contains related images — the sun and moon arguing with God, the trees resisting God's original intention that the bark taste like the fruit — all suggesting a conception of nature as possessing something like will and interiority.

### Distinctive Form

The *gemara* derives this from the verse *vayechulu ha-shamayim ve-ha-aretz ve-chol tzeva'am* — the heavens and earth and all their hosts were completed — reading *tzeva'am* not as "their hosts" but as *tzevyonam*, their distinctive forms. *Al tikrei tzeva'am ela letzevyonam*.

How does this answer the question about *etz peri*? It establishes that *etz peri* means the tree was created at the full size and form of a fruit tree — that is, fully mature, with all its defining characteristics — without necessarily meaning it was at that moment laden with ripe fruit. Under Rabbi Yehoshua's reading, it was Nissan, the fruit was beginning to emerge (*etz oseh peri*), and the *etz peri* language signals only that the tree stood at full stature.

## Patriarchs Birth Tishrei

### Etanim Month

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן שֶׁבְּתִשְׁרִי נוֹלְדוּ אָבוֹת — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּקָּהֲלוּ אֶל הַמֶּלֶךְ שְׁלֹמֹה כׇּל אִישׁ יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּיֶרַח הָאֵיתָנִים בֶּחָג״, יֶרַח שֶׁנּוֹלְדוּ בּוֹ אֵיתָנֵי עוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Eliezer says: From where** is it derived **that in Tishrei the Patriarchs were born? As it is stated: "And all the men of Israel assembled themselves before King Solomon at the feast in the month of the mighty [*eitanim*],** which is the seventh month" (I Kings 8:2), i.e., Tishrei. What is the meaning of the phrase: The month of the mighty? It is **the month in which the mighty ones of the world,** i.e., the Patriarchs, **were born.**

The *gemara* now addresses the second major item in the dispute: the birth of the *avot*. Rabbi Eliezer says the forefathers were born in *Tishrei*. *Mina'in she-be-Tishrei noldu avot?* — from where? *She-ne'emar*: *vayikahalu el ha-Melech Shlomo kol ish Yisrael be-yerach ha-Etanim ba-chag* — all of Israel gathered to King Solomon in the *yerach ha-Etanim*, in the month of *Etanim*, on the festival. This verse, from the books of *Nevi'im*, refers to the dedication of the *Beit HaMikdash* and the month of *Sukkot* — hence *ba-chag*. The month is not called by its Babylonian name (*Tishrei*, which enters Hebrew usage only in the books of the exile and the post-exilic period, such as *Esther*, *Ezra*, and *Nehemiah*) but by an older Hebrew name: *Etanim*.

*Yerach she-noldu bo etanei olam* — the month in which the strong ones of the world were born, meaning the *avot*.

### Etanim Strength

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַשְׁמַע דְּהַאי ״אֵיתָן״ לִישָּׁנָא דְּתַקִּיפֵי הוּא — כְּדִכְתִיב: ״אֵיתָן מוֹשָׁבֶךָ״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״שִׁמְעוּ הָרִים אֶת רִיב ה׳ וְהָאֵיתָנִים מוֹסְדֵי אָרֶץ״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״קוֹל דּוֹדִי הִנֵּה זֶה בָּא מְדַלֵּג עַל הֶהָרִים מְקַפֵּץ עַל הַגְּבָעוֹת״. ״מְדַלֵּג עַל הֶהָרִים״ — בִּזְכוּת אָבוֹת. ״מְקַפֵּץ עַל הַגְּבָעוֹת״ — בִּזְכוּת אִמָּהוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where** may it **be inferred that** the term ***eitan* denotes mighty? As it is written: "Strong [*eitan*] is Your dwelling place,** and You put Your nest in a rock" (Numbers 24:21). **And it says: "Hear, O mountains, the Lord's controversy, and you strong [*eitanim*] foundations of the earth"** (Micah 6:2), which is a call to the Patriarchs. **And it says: "The voice of my beloved; behold, he comes leaping upon the mountains, skipping upon the hills"** (Song of Songs 2:8): **"Leaping upon the mountains"** means that the redemption will arrive early **in the merit of the Patriarchs,** who are called mountains, and **"skipping upon the hills"** means that it will come **in the merit of the Matriarchs.**

*Mai mashma de-hai etan lishna de-takifei hu?* — from where do we derive that the word *etan* connotes strength? The *gemara* cites two verses. First: *etan moshavakha ve-sim ba-sela kinecha* — your dwelling place is strong and secure. Second: *shimu harim et riv Hashem ve-ha-etanim mosedei aretz* — hear, O mountains, the contention of God, and the *etanim*, the foundations of the earth. In this verse, *etanim* appears in parallel with *harim* — mountains — and is associated with the idea of foundations. The *etanim* are thus both strong and foundational.

### Mountains Fathers

The identification of *etanim* with *harim* (mountains) opens the door to a further *midrashic* connection. The *gemara* cites the verse from *Shir Ha-Shirim*: *kol dodi hinei zeh ba medaleg al he-harim mekapetz al ha-geva'ot* — the voice of my beloved, behold he comes, leaping over the mountains, bounding over the hills. In the *midrashic* reading, this describes the coming of redemption: *medaleg al he-harim bi-zchut avot* — leaping over the mountains by the merit of the *avot*, who are identified with mountains; *mekapetz al ha-geva'ot bi-zchut imahot* — bounding over the hills by the merit of the *imahot*, who are associated with valleys. One *midrash* is being established by another: since we know from the second verse that *etanim* parallels *harim*, and since from *Shir Ha-Shirim* we learn that *harim* refers to the *avot*, the month of *Etanim* is identified as the month in which the forefathers were born.

There is something worth dwelling on in the imagery itself. The association of mountains with fathers and valleys or hills with mothers is not arbitrary. Beyond the physical symbolism of protrusion and indentation, there is the deeper image of fertility: valleys are where things are planted and where growth happens. The gendered imagery of land — Mother Earth, the earth as feminine vessel — runs through the agricultural imagination of *Chazal*. The mountains stand firm, the valleys receive and nurture.

This also opens onto a larger question about the two models of creation. The autumn model, associated with *Tishrei*, evokes a world already fully formed, mature, and ready. It is also the beginning of the agricultural cycle, the time when human beings must begin their work — planting, initiating, participating in the earth's productivity. The spring model evokes a world in the process of becoming, bursting forth naturally. One might argue that *Tishrei*, with its fully formed world and the beginning of the planting season, suggests a model in which human creativity and partnership with the divine are central: the world is given, and you must now till it and bring forth its fruits. *Nissan*, by contrast, suggests nature's own spontaneous outpouring, with the human being more the beneficiary than the initiator.

## Patriarchs Birth Nissan

### Ziv Blossoming

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן שֶׁבְּנִיסָן נוֹלְדוּ אָבוֹת — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי בִשְׁמוֹנִים שָׁנָה וְאַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת שָׁנָה לְצֵאת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מֵאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם בַּשָּׁנָה הָרְבִיעִית בְּחֹדֶשׁ זִיו״ — בְּיֶרַח שֶׁנּוֹלְדוּ בּוֹ זִיוְתָנֵי עוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua says: From where** is it derived **that in Nisan the Patriarchs were born? As it is stated: "And it came to pass in the four hundred and eightieth year after the children of Israel were come out of the land of Egypt, in the fourth year, in the month of Ziv"** (I Kings 6:1). This means **in the month in which the radiant ones [*zivtanei*] of the world,** the Patriarchs, **were born.**

Rabbi Yehoshua's parallel proof for the birth of the *avot* in Nissan draws on a verse from the building of the *Beit HaMikdash*: *vayehi bi-shmonim shanah ve-arba me'ot shanah le-tzet bnei Yisrael me-eretz Mitzrayim ba-shanah ha-revi'it be-chodesh Ziv* — and it was in the four hundred and eightieth year after the departure of Israel from Egypt, in the fourth year, in the month of *Ziv*. The next words in the verse — *hu ha-chodesh ha-sheni* — make explicit that *chodesh Ziv* is the second month, i.e., *Iyar*.

The argument is: *be-yerach she-noldu bo zivtanei olam* — in the month in which the luminaries of the world were born, meaning the *avot*. *Rashi* acknowledges a difficulty here: the verse explicitly identifies this as the second month, which is *Iyar*, not *Nissan*. *Rashi* offers two approaches. The first is that by the time we reach the second month, the *avot* had already been born (in *Nissan*), making this a somewhat strained reading. The second is more elegant: the term *chodesh Ziv* refers not strictly to *Iyar* but to the season of *Tkufat Nissan* — the spring season — which extends into *Iyar*. The name *Ziv* captures the radiance of spring, not a specific month. One must acknowledge, however, that the verse is explicit in identifying this as the second month, which creates some tension for Rabbi Yehoshua's proof.

### Blossoming Bracha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב: ״בְּיֶרַח הָאֵיתָנִים״! הָתָם, דְּתַקִּיפֵי בְּמִצְוֹת. וְאִידַּךְ נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב: ״בְּחֹדֶשׁ זִיו״? הַהוּא, דְּאִית בֵּיהּ זִיוָא לְאִילָנֵי. דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הַאי מַאן דִּנְפַק בְּיוֹמֵי נִיסָן וְחָזֵי אִילָנֵי דִּמְלַבְלְבִי, אוֹמֵר: בָּרוּךְ שֶׁלֹּא חִיסֵּר מֵעוֹלָמוֹ כְּלוּם, וּבָרָא בּוֹ בְּרִיּוֹת טוֹבוֹת וְאִילָנוֹת טוֹבוֹת לְהִתְנָאוֹת בָּהֶן בְּנֵי אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Yehoshua, **isn't it written: "In the month of the mighty,"** which indicates that the Patriarchs were born in Tishrei? The Gemara answers: **There,** it means **that** the month **is mighty in mitzvot,** due to the many Festivals that occur in Tishrei. The Gemara asks further: **And** according to **the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Eliezer, **isn't it written: "In the month of Ziv"?** The Gemara answers: Ziv is not an allusion to the Patriarchs. Rather, it means that Nisan is the month **in which there is radiance [*ziv*] for the trees. As Rav Yehuda said: One who goes out during the days of Nisan and sees trees that are blossoming recites: Blessed…Who has withheld nothing from His world and has created in it beautiful creatures and beautiful trees for human beings to enjoy.**

The *gemara* now addresses what the other side does with the opposing verse. Rabbi Yehoshua's camp must explain *yerach ha-Etanim*, which Rabbi Eliezer read as pointing to the birth of the *avot* in *Tishrei*. The answer: *ha-tam de-takifei be-mitzvot* — it is called *Etanim* because that period is strong in *mitzvot*. *Rashi* lists them concisely: *shofar, Yom Kippurim, sukkah, lulav, aravah, nisuch ha-mayim*. The month of *Tishrei* is dense with *mitzvot*, and so it is called *Etanim* — the strong month — but this is no indication that the *avot* were born then.

Symmetrically, Rabbi Eliezer must explain *chodesh Ziv* if he holds the *avot* were born in *Tishrei*. The answer: *hahu de-it beih ziva le-ilanei* — it is the month that has a radiance and beauty about the trees, when the trees are blossoming. That is what *Ziv* means — the glow of spring blossoms — not a reference to the birth of luminaries.

This leads to *Rav Yehuda*'s famous teaching: *ha-i man de-nafik be-yomei Nissan ve-chazei ilanei de-melavlevei* — one who goes out during *Nissan* and sees trees beginning to blossom recites: *Baruch she-lo chisar me-olamo klum u-bara vo briyot tovot ve-ilanot tovot le-hanot ba-hen bnei adam* — Blessed is the One who has not left His world lacking anything, and has created good creatures and good trees for human beings to derive pleasure from them — even from their visual beauty, not only from their fruit. This *bracha* is recited once a year upon seeing blossoming trees in spring, and *Rav Yehuda* cites it precisely to illuminate what *Ziv* — radiance — means in the context of *Iyar*.

### Death Parallels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר בְּנִיסָן נוֹלְדוּ — בְּנִיסָן מֵתוּ. מַאן דְּאָמַר בְּתִשְׁרִי נוֹלְדוּ — בְּתִשְׁרִי מֵתוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֲלֵיהֶם בֶּן מֵאָה וְעֶשְׂרִים שָׁנָה אָנֹכִי הַיּוֹם״, שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״הַיּוֹם״, וּמָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״הַיּוֹם״ — הַיּוֹם מָלְאוּ יָמַי וּשְׁנוֹתַי. לְלַמֶּדְךָ שֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא יוֹשֵׁב וּמְמַלֵּא שְׁנוֹתֵיהֶם שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים מִיּוֹם לְיוֹם מֵחֹדֶשׁ לְחֹדֶשׁ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֶת מִסְפַּר יָמֶיךָ אֲמַלֵּא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **The one who said** that **in Nisan** the Patriarchs **were born** also holds that **in Nisan they died. The one who says** that **in Tishrei they were born** also holds that **in Tishrei they died, as it is stated** about Moses on the day of his death: **"And he said to them: I am one hundred and twenty years old today"** (Deuteronomy 31:2). **As** there is **no** need for **the verse to state "today,"** since it is clear that Moses was speaking on that day, **what** is the meaning when **the verse states "today"?** It is to teach that Moses was speaking precisely, as if to say: **Today my days and years are** exactly **filled** and completed. This comes **to teach you that the Holy One, Blessed be He, sits and fills the years of the righteous from day to day** and **from month to month, as it is stated: "The number of your days I will fulfill"** (Exodus 23:26). Similarly, the Patriarchs merited that their years be fulfilled to the day, and so they died on the same date they were born.

The *gemara* immediately presses: *ve-akati be-shita yarchei mi ka yalda?* — but can one give birth in only six months? The *gemara* answers: *tanya, ota shanah me'uberet hayta* — it was a leap year. With the intercalation of an extra *Adar*, the period from *Sukkot* to Pesach extends to seven months, which makes the birth viable.

## Yitzchak Pesach Birth

### Sarah Promise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּפֶסַח נוֹלַד יִצְחָק, מְנָלַן — כְּדִכְתִיב: ״לַמּוֹעֵד אָשׁוּב אֵלֶיךָ״. אֵימַת קָאֵי? אִילֵּימָא בְּפֶסַח וְקָאָמַר לֵיהּ בַּעֲצֶרֶת — בְּחַמְשִׁין יוֹמִין מִי קָא יָלְדָה? אֶלָּא דְּקָאֵי בַּעֲצֶרֶת וְקָאָמַר לֵיהּ בְּתִשְׁרִי — אַכַּתִּי בְּחַמְשָׁה יַרְחֵי מִי קָא יָלְדָה? אֶלָּא דְּקָאֵי בְּחַג וְקָאָמַר לַהּ בְּנִיסָן.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the *baraita*: **On Passover Isaac was born.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? **As it is written** that the angel who informed Sarah that she would bear a son told Abraham: **"At the appointed time [*mo'ed*] I will return to you,** at this season, and Sarah shall have a son" (Genesis 18:14). This is understood to mean: At the time of the next Festival [*mo'ed*]. **When did** the angel say this? **If we say** that it was **on Passover and he said to him** that Sarah would have a son **on *Shavuot*, can** a woman **give birth after** only **fifty days? Rather,** say **that it was *Shavuot* and he said** that she would give birth on the Festival that occurs **in** the month of **Tishrei,** i.e., *Sukkot*. But **still, can she give birth after** only **five months? Rather,** you must say **that it was *Sukkot*, and he spoke about** the Festival that occurs **in** the month of **Nisan,** i.e., Passover.

On the birth of Yitzchak on Pesach, both Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua agree: *be-Pesach nolad Yitzchak*. The source is the verse *la-mo'ed ashuv eleicha* — I will return to you at the appointed time — spoken by the *malachim* to Sarah. The *gemara* reads *mo'ed* as *Yom Tov*: the next *Yom Tov* you will have a child.

But when exactly was this promise made? *Eimat kai?* — at what point? *Ileima be-Pesach* — if it was Pesach, *ve-ka'amar lah be-Atzeret* — and they told her she'd give birth by *Shavuot* — *be-chamishim yomin mi ka yalda?* — how can one give birth in fifty days? *Ela de-ka'ei be-Atzeret ve-ka'amar lah be-Tishrei* — perhaps it was *Shavuot* and they said she'd give birth by *Sukkot* — *be-chamisha yarchei mi ka yalda?* — one cannot give birth in five months. *Ela de-ka'ei be-Chag ve-ka'amar lah be-Nissan* — it must have been *Sukkot*, and they promised her by Nissan. That is at least six months — the minimum for a viable birth. This establishes that Yitzchak was born in Nissan, specifically on Pesach.

### Leap Year Need

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַכַּתִּי, בְּשִׁיתָּא יַרְחֵי מִי קָא יָלְדָה? תָּנָא: אוֹתָהּ שָׁנָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת הָיְתָה. סוֹף סוֹף, כִּי מַדְלֵי מָר יוֹמֵי טוּמְאָה — בָּצְרִי לְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: But **still, can** a woman **give birth after** only **six months?** The Gemara answers: A Sage **taught** in a *baraita*: **That year was a leap year,** in which an additional month of Adar was added before Nisan, and a woman can indeed give birth after seven months. The Gemara raises another question: **Ultimately, if one deducts** Sarah's **days of ritual impurity,** as when the angel spoke Sarah had not yet conceived, and there is a tradition that on that day she began menstruating, as is alluded to in the verse: "After I am grown old, shall I have pleasure" (Genesis 18:12), **there are less** than seven months.

The *gemara* immediately presses: *ve-akati be-shita yarchei mi ka yalda?* — but can one give birth in only six months? Six months alone is still insufficient. The *gemara* answers: *tanya, ota shanah me'uberet hayta* — it was a leap year. With the intercalation of an extra *Adar*, the period from *Sukkot* to Pesach extends to seven months, which makes the birth viable.

### Impurity Calculation

Even with a leap year, a further complication arises: *ki madli mar yomei tum'ah batzrei lehu* — if you subtract the days during which Sarah was *tameh* and unable to conceive, the window shrinks to less than seven full months. *Rashi* on the *Torah* notes that the very day the *malachim* arrived, *oto yom pirsa Sarah niddah* — Sarah became a *niddah* on that day, which is alluded to in the verse *acharei beloti hayta li edna*. It also explains why Avraham did not serve the *malachim* the dough that Sarah prepared — because she had become a *niddah* while preparing it, rendering the food *tamei*. If she became a *niddah* upon the angels' arrival, she could not conceive immediately, and the effective window is shortened by at least a week or two.

### Seventh Month Birth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא: אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר יוֹלֶדֶת לְתִשְׁעָה — אֵינָהּ יוֹלֶדֶת לִמְקוּטָּעִין, יוֹלֶדֶת לְשִׁבְעָה — יוֹלֶדֶת לִמְקוּטָּעִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי לִתְקוּפוֹת הַיָּמִים״, מִיעוּט תְּקוּפוֹת — שְׁתַּיִם, וּמִיעוּט יָמִים — שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra said: Even according to the one who said** that if a woman **gives birth** to a viable baby **in** her **ninth** month, **she cannot give birth prematurely,** and if she does not complete nine full months' gestation the baby will not survive, nevertheless, **if** a woman **gives birth in** her **seventh** month, **she may give birth early,** before the seventh month is complete. **As it is stated** about the birth of Samuel: **"And it came to pass after cycles of days** that Hannah conceived and bore a son" (I Samuel 1:20), which is understood as follows: **The minimum of "cycles,"** seasons of three months, **is two, and the minimum of "days" is two.** Consequently, it is possible for a woman to give birth after a pregnancy of six months and two days.

The resolution comes from *Mar Zutra*: *afilu man d'amar yoledet le-tish'ah eina yoledet le-ktu'im* — even the opinion that holds that a full-term pregnancy lasts nine months maintains that those nine months must be complete months; but *yoledet le-shiv'ah yoledet le-mikuta'im* — when a woman gives birth in the seventh month, the birth can occur even partway into the seventh month. The *gemara* supports this from the verse *vayehi li-tkufat ha-yamim* regarding Chanah — *mi'ut tkufot shtayim u-mi'ut yamim shnayim* — the minimum of *tkufot* is two (i.e., two seasons, or six months) and the minimum of days is two, so Chanah gave birth just two days into the seventh season. Thus a birth just at the beginning of the seventh month is *halakhically* recognized.

Applying this to Yitzchak: with a leap year, the period from *Sukkot* to Pesach is seven months. Even subtracting a week or two for Sarah's *niddah* period, the birth falls just at the opening of the seventh month — and such a birth is valid. This is how the *gemara* establishes that Yitzchak was born on Pesach.

It bears noting that all of this is consistent with the prior *gemara*, which assumes the *malachim* visited in *Tishrei*. This would align naturally with the next line: *be-Rosh Hashanah nifkeda Sarah* — Sarah was remembered on Rosh Hashanah, also in *Tishrei*.

## Three Women Remembered

### Rosh Hashanah Zichron

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה נִפְקְדָה שָׂרָה רָחֵל וְחַנָּה. מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אָתְיָא ״פְּקִידָה״ ״פְּקִידָה״, אָתְיָא ״זְכִירָה״ ״זְכִירָה״. כְּתִיב בְּרָחֵל: ״וַיִּזְכּוֹר אֱלֹהִים אֶת רָחֵל״, וּכְתִיב בְּחַנָּה: ״וַיִּזְכְּרֶהָ ה׳״, וְאָתְיָא ״זְכִירָה״ ״זְכִירָה״ מֵרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״שַׁבָּתוֹן זִכְרוֹן תְּרוּעָה״.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the *baraita*: **On Rosh HaShana, Sarah, Rachel, and Hannah were revisited** by God and conceived children. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? **Rabbi Elazar said:** This **is derived** by means of a verbal analogy between one instance of the term **revisiting [*pekida*]** and another instance of the term **revisiting,** and by means of a verbal analogy between one instance of the term **remembering [*zekhira*]** and another instance of the word **remembering. It is written about Rachel: "And God remembered Rachel"** (Genesis 30:22), **and it is written about Hannah: "And the Lord remembered her"** (I Samuel 1:19). **And** the meaning of these instances of the term **remembering** is **derived** from another instance of the term **remembering, with regard to Rosh HaShana, as it is written: "A solemn rest, memorial proclaimed with the blast** of a *shofar*" (Leviticus 23:24). From here it is derived that Rachel and Hannah were remembered by God on Rosh HaShana.

The *gemara* turns to the principle that Sarah, Rachel, and Chanah were all remembered on Rosh Hashanah. The underlying theme is that Rosh Hashanah is the day of *zikaron* — remembrance — and *pekida* — visitation, or divine intervention. These are not merely abstract categories; they are the central theological characterization of the day. *Shabbaton zichron teru'ah* — a day of rest, a memorial of the shofar blast — is the verse that anchors Rosh Hashanah as the day of divine remembrance.

### Rachel Chana

The *gemara* establishes the Rosh Hashanah connection through a *gezeira shava* — a textual analogy — using two parallel chains of derivation. The word *zechira* (remembrance) appears in connection with Rachel — *vayizkor Elokim et Rachel vayiftach et rachmah*, God remembered Rachel and opened her womb — and in connection with Chanah — *vayizkerah Hashem*, God remembered her. Since Rosh Hashanah is described as a day of *zichron*, *atya zchira zchira* — the same term connects these divine acts of remembrance to Rosh Hashanah.

### Sarah Pekida

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״פְּקִידָה״ ״פְּקִידָה״, כְּתִיב בְּחַנָּה: ״כִּי פָקַד ה׳ אֶת חַנָּה״, וּכְתִיב בְּשָׂרָה: ״וַה׳ פָּקַד אֶת שָׂרָה״.
>
> **Translation:** And the meaning of one instance of the term **revisiting** is derived from another instance of the term **revisiting.** It is written about Hannah: "And the Lord revisited Hannah" (I Samuel 2:21), and it is written about Sarah: "And the Lord revisited Sarah" (Genesis 21:1). From here it is derived that just as Hannah was revisited on Rosh HaShana, so too, Sarah was revisited on Rosh HaShana.

The connection to Sarah employs a parallel *gezeira shava* using the word *pekida*. The verse by Chanah says *ki pakad Hashem et Chanah* — God *visited* Chanah. The verse by Sarah says *va-Hashem pakad et Sarah* — God visited Sarah. Since Chanah's *pekida* has been linked to Rosh Hashanah through *zchira*, Sarah's *pekida* is linked to Rosh Hashanah through *pekida*.

### Timeline Tension

This creates an apparent tension with what the *gemara* established moments earlier. The *gemara* there placed the visit of the *malachim* to Avraham and Sarah during *Sukkot*, with Yitzchak born on Pesach the following year. But now the *gemara* says Sarah was remembered on *Rosh Hashanah* — two weeks before *Sukkot*. How do the two fit together?

One resolution — which accords better with the verse *la-mo'ed ashuv eleicha* — would read the timeline as follows: the *malachim* visited on Pesach (as *Rashi* hints, noting that Lot baked *matzah* for them when they departed to Sedom, suggesting it was Pesach night). They announced a child would come *la-mo'ed* — at the next *mo'ed*, i.e., the next *Yom Tov*. Six months later, on Rosh Hashanah, God remembered Sarah (*be-Rosh Hashanah nifkeda*), and seven to seven-and-a-half months later — in a leap year — Yitzchak was born on Pesach. This reading integrates the two *gemarot* more smoothly, and it is the approach *Tosafot* labors to work through. However, the present *gemara* seems to assume the visitation occurred in *Tishrei*, which complicates the picture. The two traditions sit in tension, and *Tosafot* devotes considerable effort to resolving the difficulty.

## Joseph Liberation

### Joseph Forgot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה יָצָא יוֹסֵף מִבֵּית הָאֲסוּרִין, מְנָלַן — דִּכְתִיב: ״תִּקְעוּ בַחֹדֶשׁ שׁוֹפָר בַּכֵּסֶא לְיוֹם חַגֵּנוּ. כִּי חֹק לְיִשְׂרָאֵל הוּא וְגוֹ׳. עֵדוּת בִּיהוֹסֵף שָׂמוֹ בְּצֵאתוֹ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** It was further taught in the *baraita*: **On Rosh HaShana Joseph came out of prison.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? **As it is written: "Sound a *shofar* at the New Moon, at the covered time of our Festival day. For this is a statute for Israel,** a judgment of the God of Jacob" (Psalms 81:4–5). This is a reference to Rosh HaShana, the only Festival that occurs at the time of the New Moon, when the moon is covered and cannot be seen. And immediately afterward it is written: **"This He ordained in Joseph for testimony, when he went out** over the land of Egypt" (Psalms 81:6), implying that Joseph's release from prison took place on Rosh HaShana.

*Be-Rosh Hashanah yatza Yosef mi-beit ha-asurim* — Yosef was freed from prison on Rosh Hashanah. The thematic connection to Rosh Hashanah is profound: the story of Yosef's imprisonment is essentially a story of *forgetting* — the *sar ha-mashkim* (chief cupbearer) forgot Yosef after he promised to remember him: *ki im zechartani itach*, and *velo zachar sar ha-mashkim et Yosef*. The redemption from this forgetting comes through divine *zichron* — the same divine memory that is the central theme of Rosh Hashanah.

### Tekua Shofar

The textual source is the *pasuk* from *Tehillim*: *tiku va-chodesh shofar ba-keseh le-yom chageinu ki chok le-Yisrael hu mishpat le-Elohei Yaakov* — blow the shofar on the month, on the concealed day of our festival, for it is a statute for Israel, a judgment of the God of Yaakov. This verse, as established earlier in the *masechta*, refers to Rosh Hashanah. The *pasuk* continues: *edut bi-Yehosef samo be-tzeito al eretz Mitzrayim* — a testimony was set upon Yosef when he went out over the land of Egypt. The juxtaposition of the Rosh Hashanah *pasuk* with Yosef's elevation over Egypt demonstrates that Yosef was freed and elevated on Rosh Hashanah.

One additional note: the *daf* at this point in the text has an unusual appearance — a *hagah* (marginal gloss) that pushes down the entire layout of the page, sitting atop the *Tosafot* rather than below it. This gloss represents a later commentator's attempt to resolve the question of when exactly the *malachim* visited — whether at Rosh Hashanah, Sukkot, or Pesach — a question that *Tosafot* also engages. It is perhaps unique in *Shas* for a *hagah* to reshape the page layout so dramatically.

## Labor Cessation

### Work Stopped

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה בָּטְלָה עֲבוֹדָה מֵאֲבוֹתֵינוּ בְּמִצְרַיִם — כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וְהוֹצֵאתִי אֶתְכֶם מִתַּחַת סִבְלוֹת מִצְרַיִם״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״הֲסִירוֹתִי מִסֵּבֶל שִׁכְמוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** It was also taught in the *baraita*: **On Rosh HaShana our forefathers' slavery in Egypt ceased.** From where is this known? **It is written here: "And I will bring you out from under the burdens of Egypt"** (Exodus 6:6); **and it is written there,** with regard to Joseph: **"I removed his shoulder from the burden"** (Psalms 81:7). From here it is derived by verbal analogy between the two instances of the word "burden" that just as Joseph was released from prison in Tishrei, so too, the slavery of our forefathers in Egypt ended in Tishrei.

The *gemara* continues with another point on which all agree: *be-Rosh Hashanah batlah avodah me-avoteinu be-Mitzrayim* — the forced labor ceased on Rosh Hashanah. The actual redemption came later, in Nissan, but the *bitul avodah* — the cessation of the oppressive work — began on Rosh Hashanah. This parallels, as noted earlier, the structure of the *yovel*, where the *shofar* of *Yom Kippur* announces freedom but the transition unfolds over time.

The textual proof employs a *gezeira shava* on the word *sevel* (burden): *ve-hotzeisi eschem mi-tachat sivlot Mitzrayim* — I will bring you out from under the burdens of Egypt — is linked to *ha-sirotiv mi-sevel shichmo* — I removed the burden from his shoulder — from the *pasuk* of *tiku va-chodesh shofar*, which is a Rosh Hashanah verse. The same root *s-v-l* appears in both, connecting the removal of Egypt's burden to Rosh Hashanah.

### Leil Shimurim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנִיסָן נִגְאֲלוּ — כִּדְאִיתָא. בְּתִשְׁרִי עֲתִידִין לִיגָּאֵל — אָתְיָא ״שׁוֹפָר״ ״שׁוֹפָר״. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״תִּקְעוּ בַחֹדֶשׁ שׁוֹפָר״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״בְּיוֹם הַהוּא יִתָּקַע בְּשׁוֹפָר גָּדוֹל״.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the *baraita*: **In Nisan** our forefathers **were redeemed** from Egypt, **as it is** explicitly stated in the Torah. The *baraita* continues: **In Tishrei in the future** the Jewish people **will be redeemed** in the final redemption. **This is derived** by means of a verbal analogy between one instance of the word ***shofar*** and another instance of the word ***shofar*. It is written here,** with regard to Rosh HaShana: **"Sound a *shofar* at the New Moon"** (Psalms 81:4), **and it is written there,** with regard to the final redemption: "And it shall come to pass **on that day, that a great *shofar* shall be blown"** (Isaiah 27:13).

Now the *gemara* returns to the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua on the future redemption, and this is where the two positions crystallize most sharply. *Be-Nissan nigalu* — they were redeemed in Nissan — and *be-Tishrei atidin lehigael* — in *Tishrei* will the future redemption come — according to Rabbi Eliezer. The proof: *atya shofar shofar* — a *gezeira shava* on the word *shofar*. *Tiku va-chodesh shofar* refers to Rosh Hashanah; *ba-yom ha-hu yitaka be-shofar gadol* refers to the great shofar of Messianic redemption. Just as the *yovel* shofar, blown on *Yom Kippur* of *Tishrei*, represents the announcement of freedom, so too will the great shofar of the future redemption sound in *Tishrei*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: בְּנִיסָן נִגְאֲלוּ, בְּנִיסָן עֲתִידִין לִיגָּאֵל — מְנָלַן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״לֵיל שִׁמּוּרִים״ — לַיִל הַמְשׁוּמָּר וּבָא מִשֵּׁשֶׁת יְמֵי בְּרֵאשִׁית.
>
> **Translation:** It was also taught in the *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehoshua says: In Nisan** our forefathers **were redeemed** from Egypt; **and in Nisan in the future** the Jewish people **will be redeemed** in the final redemption. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that the final redemption will be in Nisan? **The verse states: "It is a night of watching** for the Lord for bringing them out from the land of Egypt; this is the Lord's night of watching, for all the children of Israel throughout their generations" (Exodus 12:42). This teaches that the night of Passover is **a night that has been continuously watched,** i.e., set aside for the purpose of redemption, **from the six days of Creation,** and it will continue to be so until the final redemption.

Rabbi Yehoshua responds: *be-Nissan nigalu u-be-Nissan atidin lehigael* — in Nissan they were redeemed, and in Nissan will they be redeemed in the future. The proof: *amar kra leil shimurim* — the verse says *leil shimurim* (Shemot 12:42). How does Rabbi Yehoshua read this? *Leil ha-meshumar u-va mi-sheshet yemei bereishit* — a night that has been guarded and awaited since the six days of creation. This Pesach night was designated from the very beginning of the world as the night of redemption — both the first redemption and the future one.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ: לַיְלָה הַמְּשׁוּמָּר וּבָא מִן הַמַּזִּיקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how does **the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Eliezer, understand this verse? He derives from it that this is **a night that is continuously watched** and protected **from demons,** meaning that demons have no power on the first night of Passover.

The one who says *Tishrei* reads *leil shimurim* differently: *laila ha-meshumar min ha-mazikin* — a night guarded from harmful forces, alluding to the protection of the firstborn during *Makas Bechoros*, when *lo yiten ha-mashchit lavo el bateichem lingof* — the destroyer was not permitted to enter the Israelite homes.

### Nissan Tishrei Theology

It is worth pausing over this debate, because the question of whether the future redemption will come in Nissan or in *Tishrei* is not merely calendrical — it carries genuine theological weight. The argument for Nissan is intuitive and almost irresistible: if the first redemption came in Nissan, and Nissan is marked as *rishon hu lachem le-chodshei ha-shanah* — first among the months — and the entire month carries the character of *geulah*, then the future redemption should be a continuation and culmination of that original *geulah*. The imagery of Nissan is powerful: the movement from the dead of winter to the blossoming of spring, from darkness to light, from slavery to freedom. It is natural, organic, and cyclically satisfying.

The position that the future redemption will come in *Tishrei* is harder to grasp. *Tishrei* is the time of divine judgment, of God's memory and intervention in the world — *Rosh Hashanah* and *Yom Kippur*. One might argue that in *Tishrei*, the world does not simply blossom of its own accord; rather, it is human beings who must initiate, plant, and partner with the divine. The beginning of the agricultural cycle is the beginning of human creative responsibility. The future redemption, on this view, will not simply be a repeat of the original *geulah* — where *Bnei Yisrael* were, as it were, carried out passively — but will be one in which greater human partnership and initiative are required. This is an intellectually provocative thought, even if the *gemara* does not spell it out explicitly. The *shofar shofar* connection at least anchors it liturgically in the existing structure of Rosh Hashanah.

## Mabul Flood Dating

### Yehoshua Mabul Date

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָזְדוּ לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ, דְּתַנְיָא: ״בִּשְׁנַת שֵׁשׁ מֵאוֹת שָׁנָה לְחַיֵּי נֹחַ בַּחֹדֶשׁ הַשֵּׁנִי בְּשִׁבְעָה עָשָׂר יוֹם לַחֹדֶשׁ״, רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם שִׁבְעָה עָשָׂר בְּאִיָּיר הָיָה, יוֹם שֶׁמַּזַּל כִּימָה שׁוֹקֵעַ בַּיּוֹם וּמַעְיָנוֹת מִתְמַעֲטִין. וּמִתּוֹךְ שֶׁשִּׁינּוּ מַעֲשֵׂיהֶן, שִׁינָּה הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עֲלֵיהֶם מַעֲשֵׂה בְרֵאשִׁית, וְהֶעֱלָה מַזַּל כִּימָה בַּיּוֹם, וְנָטַל שְׁנֵי כּוֹכָבִים מִכִּימָה וְהֵבִיא מַבּוּל לָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua **follow their** lines of **reasoning,** as they disagreed about this same issue in another context as well. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **"In the six hundredth year of Noah's life, in the second month, on the seventeenth day of the month,** on that day were all the fountains of the great deep broken open, and the windows of heaven were opened" (Genesis 7:11). **Rabbi Yehoshua says: That day was the seventeenth of Iyyar,** the second month of the year counting from Nisan, which is **the day that the constellation of *Kima* sets during the day and** the season that **the springs diminish** with the increased heat. **But because** the people of the generation of the flood **changed their actions** for the worse, **the Holy One, Blessed be He, changed for them the acts of Creation, and** instead of *Kima* setting, **He caused the constellation of *Kima* to rise during the day and He removed two stars from *Kima*, and** in this way **He brought a flood to the world.**

The *gemara* now applies the same *Tishrei*-versus-Nissan dispute to the dating of the *mabul*. The verse says the flood began in the second month, on the seventeenth day. *Ve-azdu le-ta'amaihu* — they each follow their own reasoning. Rabbi Yehoshua says: *oso yom Yud-Zayin be-Iyar haya* — that day was the 17th of *Iyar*, because he counts from Nissan, making *Iyar* the second month.

What is significant about this date? *Yom she-mazal Kima shokeia ba-yom* — it is the day when the constellation *Kima* is setting during the day. *Rashi* identifies *Kima* with the Pleiades. *Rashi* here also provides a full list of the twelve zodiacal constellations in their Hebrew names: *Taleh* (Aries), *Shor* (Taurus), *Teomim* (Gemini), *Sartan* (Cancer), *Aryeh* (Leo), *Betula* (Virgo), *Moznayim* (Libra), *Akrav* (Scorpio), *Keshet* (Sagittarius), *Gedi* (Capricorn), *Deli* (Aquarius), *Dagim* (Pisces). The mnemonic is: *Hatshaf Av Maakt-Gadad*.

The *Kima* is in the setting phase during the day in *Iyar*, because by then the cycle of constellations has moved on from where it was at the start of Nissan. *U-mayanos mitma'atim* — the underground wellsprings are diminishing in spring, as the winter rains and snowmelt taper off. This is the natural state of affairs in *Iyar*. Therefore, *u-mitoch she-shinu ma'aseihem* — because the generation of the *mabul* perverted their ways — *shina ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu aleihem ma'aseh bereishit* — God altered the natural order against them. *Ve-he'ela mazal Kima ba-yom* — God made *Kima* rise during the day, in the opposite of its normal pattern — *ve-natal shenei kochavim mi-Kima ve-heivi Mabul la-olam* — and God took two stars from *Kima* and brought the flood upon the world. The stars of *Kima* functioned as plugs in the great heavenly storehouse of water; removing them opened the floodgates.

### Zodiac Signs

The constellations rotate through the sky in a twelve-part cycle. In Nissan, Aries (*Taleh*) is in the ascendancy as the day begins, and each subsequent constellation takes over in roughly two-hour intervals. Six months later, in *Tishrei*, the cycle is inverted — what was at the top in Nissan is now at the bottom, and vice versa. This rotating structure is the framework for understanding both the astronomical claims about *Kima* and the calculation of the *tekufot* (seasonal reckonings) — to which the *gemara* will turn shortly.

### Eliezer Mabul Date

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם שִׁבְעָה עָשָׂר בְּמַרְחֶשְׁוָן הָיָה, יוֹם שֶׁמַּזַּל כִּימָה עוֹלֶה בַּיּוֹם וּמַעְיָנוֹת מִתְגַּבְּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer** disagrees and **says: That day was the seventeenth of Marḥeshvan,** the second month counting from Tishrei, which is **the day that the constellation of *Kima* rises during the day and** the season that **the springs increase.**

Rabbi Eliezer holds that *oso yom Yud-Zayin be-Marcheshvan haya* — that day was the 17th of *Marcheshvan*, the second month counting from *Tishrei*. On his calendar, *Kima* is rising — *ola* — during the day in *Marcheshvan*, which is actually consistent with the natural pattern of that month. In *Marcheshvan*, the underground wellsprings are increasing — *mayanot mitgabrim* — as the winter rains begin. So the question the *gemara* will raise is: if this is already the natural state of affairs in *Marcheshvan*, what exactly did God *change* (*shina*) to bring the flood?

## Mabul Flood Dating (II)

### Boiling Water Sin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״שֵׁנִי״. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, מַאי ״שֵׁנִי״? שֵׁנִי לְדִין. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, הַיְינוּ דְּשִׁינָּה. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, מַאי שִׁינָּה? כִּדְרַב חִסְדָּא. דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בְּרוֹתְחִין קִלְקְלוּ וּבְרוֹתְחִין נִידּוֹנוּ. בְּרוֹתְחִין קִלְקְלוּ — בַּעֲבֵירָה, וּבְרוֹתְחִין נִידּוֹנוּ — כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וַיָּשֹׁכּוּ הַמָּיִם״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״וַחֲמַת הַמֶּלֶךְ שָׁכָכָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to Rabbi Yehoshua,** who holds that the flood began in the month of Iyyar, **this is as it is written:** "In the **second** month," which is referring to the month of Iyyar, the second month from Nisan. **But according to Rabbi Eliezer,** who holds that the flood began in the month of Marḥeshvan, **what is** the meaning of "the **second** month"? The Gemara answers: It means **second to** the month that includes the day of **judgment,** which is the month of Tishrei. The Gemara asks further: **Granted, according to Rabbi Yehoshua,** who holds that the flood began in the month of Iyyar, **this is** what it means that **He changed** the acts of Creation with a flood, as rain does not usually fall in Iyyar. **But according to Rabbi Eliezer,** who holds that the flood began in Marḥeshvan, **what did He change?** The Gemara answers: Even according to Rabbi Eliezer a change was made, **in accordance with** the statement **of Rav Ḥisda, as Rav Ḥisda said: They sinned with boiling** heat, **and they were punished with boiling** heat; **they sinned with the boiling** heat **of the sin** of forbidden sexual relations, **and they were punished with the boiling** heat of scalding waters. This is derived from a verbal analogy. **It is written here,** with regard to the flood: **"And the waters abated"** (Genesis 8:1), **and it is written elsewhere,** with regard to King Ahasuerus: **"And the heated** anger **of the king abated"** (Esther 7:10), which implies that the word "abated" means cooled. This indicates that at first the waters of the flood had been scalding hot.

The answer to that question comes from Rav Chisda: *be-rotchin kilkelu be-rotchin nidonu* — they sinned with boiling liquid and were punished with boiling liquid. The sin refers to sexual immorality — the *aveira* associated in *Chazal*'s reading with the generation of the *mabul*, involving unnatural unions. The punishment was that the floodwaters were not merely cold water but boiling hot. This is the *shina* — the alteration of the natural order — according to Rabbi Eliezer: not that water came in the wrong season, but that the water itself was supernaturally hot.

The proof for the hot water: *ktiv hacha va-yashoku ha-mayim* — the waters *abated*, using the root *sh-k-k*; *ve-chtiv hatam ve-chamat ha-melech shachachah* — and the king's fury (*chamat*) abated, the same root. Since *chemah* connotes fiery rage and heat, the shared root implies that the floodwaters, like a king's fury, required *cooling down* — demonstrating that they had been boiling hot.

## Calendar Resolution

### Jewish Counting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חַכְמֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מוֹנִין לַמַּבּוּל כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, וְלַתְּקוּפָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. חַכְמֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם מוֹנִין אַף לַמַּבּוּל כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **The Jewish Sages count** the years **from** Creation and **the flood in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** from Tishrei, and they calculate **the cycles** of the sun and the moon **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua,** from Nisan. **The sages of the** gentile **nations of the world,** on the other hand, **count both** the years **from** Creation and **the flood in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua,** from Nisan.

The *gemara* presents a surprising resolution in the form of a *baraita*: *chachmei Yisrael monin la-mabul ke-Rabbi Eliezer u-le-tekufa ke-Rabbi Yehoshua* — the Jewish sages count the *mabul* dates following Rabbi Eliezer (i.e., from *Tishrei*, making the second month *Marcheshvan*), but they reckon the *tekufot* (seasonal divisions of the year) following Rabbi Yehoshua (i.e., from Nissan). This is not consistent.

*Chachmei Umot Ha-olam monim af hem ke-Rabbi Yehoshua* — the sages of the nations are consistent: they count both the *mabul* dates and the *tekufot* from Nissan, following Rabbi Yehoshua throughout.

### Tekufa Reference

Both *Rashi* and *Tosafot* explain the significance of how one counts the *tekufot*: the seasonal reckoning takes the moment of creation as its point of reference. The *tekufa* calculation works backward from a fixed starting point, which must be the beginning of the world. Therefore, whichever month one uses as the reference point for the *tekufot* implicitly reveals which month one believes the world was created in. If the *chachmei Yisrael* reckon the *tekufot* from Nissan, they are, in effect, ruling that the world was created in Nissan — *be-Nissan nivra ha-olam* — even while they apply the *Tishrei* calendar to the *mabul* narrative in the *Torah*.

### Conclusion Ambiguity

What then is the final ruling? The *gemara* leaves the question unresolved in a striking way. The entire tradition of *tekufa* calculation — the backbone of the Jewish calendar's seasonal reckoning — implicitly assumes the world was created in Nissan. Yet so much of the liturgy of Rosh Hashanah assumes *be-Tishrei nivra ha-olam*: *zeh ha-yom haras olam*, *zeh ha-yom techilat ma'asecha* — this day is the birthday of the world, this day is the beginning of Your works.

The result is that both traditions — Nissan as the month of natural creation and *Tishrei* as the month of providential creation, judgment, and human partnership — remain alive simultaneously in Jewish consciousness and practice. The tension is not resolved; it is inhabited. The agricultural calendar begins in *Tishrei*; the redemptive calendar begins in Nissan. The world was created in *Tishrei* for purposes of divine judgment and human accountability; it was created in Nissan for purposes of natural birth and cosmic renewal. Both things are true, and the *gemara* on 11b ends, characteristically, by leaving us to hold both.
