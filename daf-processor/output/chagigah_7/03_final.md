# Chagigah 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav Yochanan vs Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם, אֵלּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם שִׁיעוּר: הַפֵּאָה, וְהַבִּכּוּרִים, וְהָרֵאָיוֹן, וּגְמִילוּת חֲסָדִים, וְתַלְמוּד תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in a mishna there (Pe'a 1:1): These are the mitzvot that have no measure: **Produce in the corner of the field** that must be left unharvested, which is given to the poor [***pe'a***]; **and the first fruits,** which are brought to the Temple; **and the appearance** in the Temple on the pilgrim Festivals; **and acts of loving-kindness; and Torah study.**

### Re'iyat Panim/Korban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כִּסְבוּרִין אָנוּ לוֹמַר, הָרֵאָיוֹן אֵין לוֹ שִׁיעוּר — לְמַעְלָה, אֲבָל יֵשׁ לוֹ שִׁיעוּר — לְמַטָּה, עַד שֶׁבָּא רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא בְּרַבִּי, וְלִימֵּד: הָרֵאָיוֹן אֵין לוֹ שִׁיעוּר לֹא לְמַעְלָה וְלֹא לְמַטָּה. אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: הָרְאִיָּיה מָעָה כֶּסֶף, וְהַחֲגִיגָה שְׁתֵּי כֶסֶף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: We thought to say that the** mitzva of **appearance** in the Temple **does not have a maximum measure, but it does have a minimum measure; until Rabbi Oshaya the Distinguished came and taught: The** mitzva of **appearance has neither a maximum nor a minimum measure. However, the Rabbis say:** The burnt-offering of **appearance** must be worth one **silver *ma'a* and the Festival peace**-offering **two silver** coins.

The seventh daf of Chagigah opens by resuming a debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish, one that arose from an apparent tension in the sources. The *mishnah* in Pe'ah had stated that *re'ayon* — the act of appearing before God in the Temple — has no *shiur*, no prescribed measure. Although the *mishnah* in our tractate had set a minimum for the *olat re'iyah* of either two *ma'ah* of silver or one *ma'ah*, the Gemara clarifies that those minimums are only rabbinic. The deeper question is whether there is instead a maximum, or a limitation on the number of times one may appear.

### Reish Lakish's Stringency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הָרֵאָיוֹן? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: רְאִיַּית פָּנִים בַּעֲזָרָה, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: רְאִיַּית פָּנִים בְּקׇרְבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** § **What is the** mitzva of **appearance** to which this mishna is referring? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** It is the mitzva of **the appearance of** one's **face in** the Temple **courtyard.** One may appear in the Temple as often as he likes over the course of a Festival. **And Reish Lakish said:** It is **the appearance of** one's **face** in the Temple **with an offering.** There is no measure in this regard, as every time one appears in the Temple during a Festival he must bring an offering.

The debate is framed as follows: Rabbi Yochanan says there is no *shiur* for *re'iyat panim* — the act of appearing in the *azarah* — while Reish Lakish says there is no *shiur* for the *korbanot*. At first glance, Reish Lakish's formulation sounds more permissive: bring as many *korbanot* as you wish. But the Gemara reads it as actually more demanding. What Reish Lakish means is that yes, you may appear in the *Beit HaMikdash* as often as you like throughout the *regel*, but every single time you appear, you are obligated to bring a *korban*. This follows from the verse *lo yera'u fanai reikam* — do not appear before Me empty-handed. Each appearance, on Reish Lakish's view, triggers its own obligation.

### Rav Yochanan's Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעִיקָּר הָרֶגֶל, כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דִּרְאִיַּית פָּנִים בְּקׇרְבָּן. כִּי פְּלִיגִי בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הָרֶגֶל: כׇּל הֵיכָא דַּאֲתָא וְאַיְיתִי — כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דִּמְקַבְּלִינַן מִינֵּיהּ, כִּי פְּלִיגִי דַּאֲתָא וְלָא אַיְיתִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **On the main** i.e., on the first, day of **the pilgrimage Festival, everyone agrees that the appearance of** one's **face** must be accompanied **by an offering. When they disagree,** it is with regard **to the rest of the days of the pilgrimage Festival,** i.e., whether or not one who appears must sacrifice an offering. Furthermore, **anywhere,** i.e., in any case **that one comes and brings** an offering, **everyone agrees that we accept it from him. When they disagree,** it is **when he comes and does not bring** an offering. Is this man obligated to bring an offering or not?

Rabbi Yochanan agrees that *lo yera'u fanai reikam* imposes a real obligation, but limits it to the first appearance — the first day of *Yom Tov*. That initial appearance must be accompanied by a *korban*. Subsequent appearances throughout the *regel*, including on *Chol HaMoed*, carry no such requirement. One might ask interesting hypothetical questions even within Rabbi Yochanan's framework — for instance, if a person's very first appearance was empty-handed and he then appeared a second time on the first day, has he already violated the prohibition, and is he now exempt or still obligated? These questions are not resolved by the Gemara, but they underscore the boundaries of Rabbi Yochanan's position: the fundamental demand is one *korban* at the outset.

### Beraita Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: ״וְלֹא יֵרָאוּ פָנַי רֵיקָם״ — בִּזְבָחִים. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בִּזְבָחִים, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בְּעוֹפוֹת וּמְנָחוֹת? וְדִין הוּא: נֶאֶמְרָה חֲגִיגָה לַהֶדְיוֹט, וְנֶאֶמְרָה רְאִיָּיה לַגָּבוֹהַּ. מָה חֲגִיגָה הָאֲמוּרָה לַהֶדְיוֹט — זְבָחִים, אַף רְאִיָּיה הָאֲמוּרָה לַגָּבוֹהַּ — זְבָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish **raised an objection to** the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan from a *baraita*: **"And none shall appear before Me empty-handed."** This teaches that Festival pilgrims must appear **with animal offerings.** Do **you say** that they must appear **with animal offerings, or** perhaps **it is only with bird**-offerings **or meal**-offerings? **And it is** possible to determine the answer **by logical derivation:** It **is stated** that one must bring **a Festival peace**-offering **for a commoner** to eat, **and** it **is stated** that one must bring a burnt-offering of **appearance,** which is **for God. Just as the Festival peace**-offering **that is stated for a commoner** is referring to **animal offerings, so too,** the burnt-offering of **appearance that is stated** to be sacrificed wholly **for God** is referring to **animal offerings.**

The Gemara now cites a *beraita*: *Eitavei* — Rabbi Yose be-Rabbi Yehuda teaches that Israel is commanded to ascend to Jerusalem on the three *regalim* — Pesach, Shavuot, and Sukkot. The *beraita* then states that they may not appear *chatza'im* — "in halves" — based on the verse *kol zechurecha*, "all your males," and they may not appear empty-handed, based on *velo yera'u fanai reikam*. This is brought as a challenge, apparently indicating that every time one appears, a *korban* is required — supporting Reish Lakish's stringent position.

### Ikar HaRegel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סָבַר: רְאִיַּית פָּנִים בָּעֲזָרָה, דְּכׇל אֵימַת דְּאָתֵי לָא צָרִיךְ לְאֵתוֹיֵי, רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: רְאִיַּית פָּנִים בְּקׇרְבָּן, דְּכׇל אֵימַת דְּאָתֵי צָרִיךְ לְאֵתוֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains **that Rabbi Yoḥanan holds** that the mitzva is merely **the appearance of** one's **face in the** Temple **courtyard.** Therefore, one **doesn't need to bring** an offering **whenever he comes** to the Temple. **Reish Lakish said** that the mitzva is **the appearance of** one's **face with an offering,** which means that **whenever one comes he must bring** an offering with him.

Rabbi Yochanan responds: *be-ikar haregel* — the obligation to appear with a *korban* applies on the *ikar haregel*, the primary and first day of the festival. The verse's prohibition against appearing empty-handed is directed at that initial appearance. On *Chol HaMoed*, and presumably even for later appearances on the same first day after one has already fulfilled the obligation once, no *korban* is required.

### Yera'eh Yera'eh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: ״יֵרָאֶה״ ״יִרְאֶה״ — מָה אֲנִי בְּחִנָּם, אַף אַתֶּם בְּחִנָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to** the opinion of **Reish Lakish** from a *baraita*: The verse states: "All your males shall appear." Although the verse as written can be read: **"Will see [*yireh*],"** it is actually read: **"Shall be seen [*yera'e*]."** The *baraita* derives that **just as I,** God, come to see you **for free,** as He has no obligation to bring an offering when He comes to see us in the Temple, **so too, you** may come to see Me in the Temple **for free,** i.e., there is no requirement to bring an offering.

The Gemara then records that Rabbi Yochanan challenged Reish Lakish with the wordplay of *yera'eh yera'eh* — the see-and-be-seen reading of the relevant verse. This dual reading recurs throughout the tractate. Without the Masoretic vocalization, the verse *yera'eh kol zechurecha et pnei* most naturally reads as "all your males shall see the face of God," not "shall be seen." Even in the *Nevi'im*, the phrase *ki tavo'u lera'ot panai* — "when you come to see My face" — resists being rendered in the passive. The verse is vocalized in the passive to avoid gross anthropomorphism, but the alternative reading is clearly present in the text. This gives the Gemara license to invoke both directions of the verb.

### Free Seeing Argument

The argument presented is strikingly audacious: *ma ani bechinam, af atem bechinam* — just as I, God, see you without charge, so too you see Me without charge. The implication is that there is a basis for appearing before God in the *Mikdash* without bringing any *korban* at all — reading precisely against the plain meaning of *lo yera'u fanai reikam*, which commands that one not appear empty-handed.

### Final Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: כֹּל הֵיכָא דַּאֲתָא וְלָא אַיְיתִי — דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּעָיֵיל וּמִתְחֲזֵי וְנָפֵיק, כִּי פְּלִיגִי דַּאֲתָא וְאַיְיתִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** with regard to **anywhere that one comes** to the Temple courtyard after the initial time on the first day of the Festival **and he does not bring** an offering, **everyone agrees that one enters, appears, and exits** without having to bring an offering. **When they disagree** it is in a case **where one comes and brings** an offering.

The Gemara resolves the dispute. Given this final *beraita* that Rabbi Yochanan invoked, Reish Lakish is forced to concede that it is possible to appear in the *Mikdash* without a *korban*. The conclusion is: *kol heicha de'ata velo ayeiti* — if one comes to the *Mikdash* without a *korban* in hand, *kulei alma lo pligi de'ayeil umitchazi venafik* — everyone agrees one may enter, appear, and depart. The debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish is therefore not about whether one may appear without a *korban* on subsequent visits — on that, all agree one may — but rather about bringing multiple *korbanot* in the context of *olat re'iyah*, as we shall see.

## Gemara's Methodology

### Paradox Explained

This resolution creates an immediate difficulty. The preceding *amud* was filled with Reish Lakish challenging Rabbi Yochanan with various *braitot* that seemed to require a *korban* at every appearance, and Rabbi Yochanan responding to each challenge. But now the Gemara concludes that Reish Lakish himself agrees with Rabbi Yochanan's basic position — that appearing without a *korban* on subsequent occasions is permitted. If Reish Lakish ultimately agreed, what was the purpose of all those challenges? Was Reish Lakish asking questions that, given his own final position, he had no real reason to ask?

### Gemara as Reconstruction

This is not an isolated phenomenon. *Tosafot*, in other places in the Talmud, note that according to certain final conclusions of the Gemara, the entire preceding conversation "never happened." This should not be read as a claim that the disputants misremembered, or that the tradition is unreliable. Rather, it reflects something important about the nature of Talmudic discourse: the Gemara is very often *reconstructing* a conversation, not reporting one. It knows that Rabbi Yochanan held one position and Reish Lakish held another. From that starting point, the Gemara asks: what would each of them do with this source? How would each respond to that challenge? To explore these questions, the Gemara frames the discussion as if the two Amoraim were directly challenging one another — but in reality, it is the Gemara itself that is constructing the dialogue and standing in for each disputant in turn.

### Understanding the Device

The key is awareness of the device. When one learns that the Gemara sometimes interjects its own editorial commentary within what appears to be an original *meimra*, students sometimes feel misled: "It sounds like the Amora is saying this — how can you tell me the Gemara inserted its own words?" One only feels deceived if one was unaware that this is the method being employed. Once one understands the device, there is no misrepresentation. The original debate is real: Rabbi Yochanan said *re'iyat panim* has no limit, Reish Lakish said *re'iyat korban* has no limit. Everything that follows is the Gemara's attempt to reconstruct precisely what each position entailed and how each sage would have responded to the available sources. Even when the Gemara narrates this reconstruction in the voices of the Amoraim themselves, a reader who understands the methodology is not being deceived.

### Dynamic Transmission

There is also a philosophical dimension worth noting. This reconstructive method reflects a dynamic quality intrinsic to the transmission of *Torah she-be'al peh*. Even today, when faced with a *machloket* between, say, the Rambam and the Ra'avad, we might ask: what would the Ra'avad say to this source? What would the Rambam say to that argument? We frame such questions with the words "he would probably say..." whereas the Gemara actually constructs the exchange as a living dialogue, as if the disputants are still arguing and we are the vehicle for the continuation of their debate. There is a power to this — the sense that the argument remains alive and that each generation inherits not only the conclusions but the ongoing conversation itself. The Gemara confers authority on this reconstruction retroactively, through the dynamic of human thought and imagination operating within the tradition.

## Multiple Korbanos Debate

### One Korban Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דְּאָמַר: רְאִיַּית פָּנִים בָּעֲזָרָה — רְאִיַּית פָּנִים הוּא דְּאֵין לָהּ שִׁיעוּר, הָא לְקׇרְבָּן — יֵשׁ לָהּ שִׁיעוּר. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: רְאִיַּית פָּנִים — בְּקׇרְבָּן, דַּאֲפִילּוּ קׇרְבָּן נָמֵי אֵין לוֹ שִׁיעוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rabbi Yoḥanan, who says** that the appearance mentioned in the mishna is referring to **the appearance of** one's **face in the** Temple **courtyard,** maintains that **it is** this **appearance** itself **that has no** maximum **measure,** i.e., one may appear as often as he wishes without an offering. However, **there is** a maximum **measure,** i.e., a limited number **of offerings,** that one may bring; only one burnt-offering of appearance per Festival. **And Reish Lakish said: The appearance of** one's **face** means **with an offering, as even offerings have no** maximum **measure.** One may bring as many offerings during the Festival as he wishes.

Having established that all agree one may appear without a *korban* on subsequent visits during the *regel*, the Gemara now identifies the genuine point of dispute. *Ki pligi* — where do they actually disagree? The case is: *de-ati ve-aisi* — one came and wished to bring a *korban*, specifically a second *korban* after having already brought his first. On this, Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish diverge.

Rabbi Yochanan's position is that *re'iyat panim de-ein lah shiur* — the number of appearances has no limit, one may come to the *Beit HaMikdash* as often as one wishes. But *le-korban yesh lo shiur* — there is a limit to the number of *korbanot* one may bring. Specifically, one may bring the *olat re'iyah* only once. This sounds strange at first: since when is there ever a ceiling on how many *korbanot* one may bring?

### Bal Tosif Issue

*Tosafot* frames Rabbi Yochanan's limitation as touching on *bal tosif* — the prohibition against adding to the Torah's commandments. If a person is simply bringing a generic *olah* at any time, there is no problem. But if one wishes to bring it on the first day of *Yom Tov* specifically in the context of *re'iyat panim* — as an expression of *oleh le-regel*, of appearing before God — then the Torah prescribed one *korban* for that act of appearing. To bring additional *korbanot* in that same context of *olat re'iyah* is to add to what it means to appear, and that carries a dimension of *bal tosif*. To bring a *korban* simply as a *korban*, without framing it as part of the *re'iyah* obligation, is always permitted; but to multiply *korbanot* specifically as expressions of the *re'iyah* itself is what Rabbi Yochanan would restrict.

### Multiple Korbanos OK

Reish Lakish disagrees: *re'iyat panim le-korban de-afillu korban nami ein lo shiur* — even the *korban* in the context of appearing has no limit. One must appear once with a *korban*, and after that, any further appearances with additional *korbanot* are entirely welcome — *be my guest*. This reading also fits better with the literal words of *ein lo shiur*, which suggest an absence of any upper limit. Indeed, the Rambam rules in accordance with Reish Lakish here, even though the general rule is that the *halacha* does not follow Reish Lakish in his disputes with Rabbi Yochanan.

### Hokeir Raglecha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: ״הֹקַר רַגְלְךָ מִבֵּית רֵעֶךָ״. הָתָם בְּחַטָּאוֹת וַאֲשָׁמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **raised an objection to** the opinion of Reish Lakish: **"Let your foot be seldom in your neighbor's house,** lest he be sated with you, and hate you" (Proverbs 25:17). It can be inferred from this verse that one should not bring extra gifts, i.e., offerings, to God. The Gemara responds: **There,** the verse is referring **to sin**-offerings **and guilt**-offerings, i.e., it is better not to sin in the first place.

Rabbi Yochanan then raises a challenge against Reish Lakish from the verse *hokeir raglecha mi-beit re'echa* — make your steps precious, do not be overly frequent in your friend's house. This verse implies some degree of restraint in visiting God's house. If we have just agreed that appearing without a *korban* is permitted, then the restraint must refer to something else — namely, the bringing of *korbanot*. This seems to support Rabbi Yochanan's position that one should not bring multiple *korbanot* as *olat re'iyah*.

### Sin vs Free-Will

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְרַבִּי לֵוִי. דְּרַבִּי לֵוִי רָמֵי, כְּתִיב: ״הֹקַר רַגְלְךָ מִבֵּית רֵעֶךָ״, וּכְתִיב: ״אָבוֹא בֵיתְךָ בְעוֹלוֹת״! לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּחַטָּאוֹת וַאֲשָׁמוֹת, כָּאן בְּעוֹלוֹת וּשְׁלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** This explanation is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Levi, as Rabbi Levi raised a contradiction** between two verses. **It is written: "Let your foot be seldom in your neighbor's house," and it is written: "I will come into Your house with burnt**-offerings, I will perform for You my vows" (Psalms 66:13). Rabbi Levi explained: This is **not difficult; here,** the verse is referring **to sin-**offerings **and guilt**-offerings, **and there,** it is referring **to burnt**-offerings **and peace**-offerings, concerning which one may bring as many as he likes.

The Gemara rejects this reading of the verse. *Hasam be-chataot ve-ashamot* — the verse is speaking about *sin offerings*, not about burnt offerings or peace offerings. The lesson of *hokeir raglecha* is: do not sin so frequently that you constantly need to come to the *Beit HaMikdash* to bring sin offerings. This is consistent with the teaching of Rabbi Levi, who resolved an apparent contradiction between two verses. One verse says *hokeir raglecha mi-beit re'echa* — be sparing in your presence at your friend's house — while another says *avo veitecha be-olot* — I will come to Your house with burnt offerings. Rabbi Levi resolves: *kan be-chataot ve-ashamot, kan be-olot ve-shlamim* — the first verse concerns sin offerings, the second concerns burnt offerings and peace offerings. Bringing *olot* and *shlamim* in abundance is encouraged, not restricted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: ״הֹקַר רַגְלְךָ מִבֵּית רֵעֶךָ״ — בְּחַטָּאוֹת וַאֲשָׁמוֹת הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בְּחַטָּאוֹת וַאֲשָׁמוֹת, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בְּעוֹלוֹת וּשְׁלָמִים? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״אָבוֹא בֵיתְךָ בְעוֹלוֹת אֲשַׁלֵּם לְךָ נְדָרָי״ — הֲרֵי עוֹלוֹת וּשְׁלָמִים אָמוּר, הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״הֹקַר רַגְלְךָ מִבֵּית רֵעֶךָ״ — בְּחַטָּאוֹת וַאֲשָׁמוֹת הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **That** opinion **is also taught** in a *baraita*: **"Let your foot be seldom in your neighbor's house." The verse is speaking of sin**-offerings **and guilt**-offerings. Do **you say** it is referring **to sin**-offerings **and guilt**-offerings, **or** perhaps **it is only** referring **to burnt**-offerings **and peace**-offerings? **When it says: "I will come into Your house with burnt**-offerings, **I will perform for You my vows," burnt**-offerings **and peace**-offerings **are mentioned,** as these are more pleasant to sacrifice to God. **How** then **do I uphold** the meaning of the verse: **"Let your foot be seldom in your neighbor's house"? The verse is speaking of sin**-offerings **and guilt**-offerings.

A *beraita* taught similarly: *hokeir raglecha mi-beit re'echa be-chataot ve-ashamot haketuv medabeir* — it is sin offerings the verse is discussing. One might have thought that even *olot ve-shlamim* are subject to the restraint. But since a separate verse, *avo veitecha be-olot ashalem lecha nedarai*, explicitly mentions burnt offerings and vows — the latter being the source for *shlamim* — the verse of *hokeir raglecha* is confined entirely to the realm of *chataot ve-ashamot*. The resolution is as elegant as it is striking: the imagery of *beit re'echa*, "your friend's house," is a beautiful way of understanding the relationship between Israel and God — and one's freedom to come often with gifts of *olot* and *shlamim* is entirely undiminished.

## Half Cannot Appear

### Rav Yosef's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין נִרְאִין חֲצָאִין כּוּ׳. סָבַר רַב יוֹסֵף לְמֵימַר: מַאן דְּאִית לֵיהּ עֲשָׂרָה בָּנִים, לָא לִיסְּקוּ הָאִידָּנָא חַמְשָׁה וּלְמָחָר חַמְשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** § Earlier the *baraita* taught: The people **may not appear partially,** as the verse states: "All your males" (Exodus 23:17). **Rav Yosef thought to say: One who has ten sons** should **not let five ascend now and** the other **five tomorrow.** Rather, they must all ascend to the Temple together.

The *beraita* cited earlier included the ruling *ve-ein nire'in chatza'im* — they may not appear "in halves." Rav Yosef initially proposed a reading of this phrase: if a man has twenty sons, he should not bring ten of them on the first day of *Yom Tov* and the other ten on the following day of *Chol HaMoed*. All of them should come on the first day. The "halves" refers to splitting one's household across different days of the festival.

### Abaye's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: פְּשִׁיטָא, הֵי מִינַּיְיהוּ מְשַׁוֵּית לְהוּ פּוֹשְׁעִים, וְהֵי מִינַּיְיהוּ מְשַׁוֵּית לְהוּ זְרִיזִין!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him:** This **is obvious; which of** his sons would **he render sinners,** who are too lazy to ascend, **and which of them** would **he render vigilant,** eager to perform the mitzva at the first opportunity?

Abaye objects immediately: *peshita* — this is obvious! One needs no verse to teach such a thing. If you bring five sons today and five tomorrow, those five who will come tomorrow are *peshuim* — negligent — for failing to appear on the primary day, and only those who came today are *charutzim* — prompt and diligent. No verse is needed to tell us not to deliberately make half of one's family negligent. Clearly, the *ikar haregel* — the first day — is when all must appear, and this follows from simple logic without any scriptural derivation.

### Excluding the Unfit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא קְרָא לְמַאי אֲתָא? לִכְדַאֲחֵרִים. דְּתַנְיָא, אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: הַמְקַמֵּץ, וְהַמְצָרֵף נְחֹשֶׁת, וְהַבּוּרְסִי — פְּטוּרִין מִן הָרְאִיָּיה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כׇּל זְכוּרְךָ״, מִי שֶׁיָּכוֹל לַעֲלוֹת עִם כׇּל זְכוּרְךָ. יָצְאוּ אֵלּוּ, שֶׁאֵין יְכוֹלִין לַעֲלוֹת עִם כׇּל זְכוּרְךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, for what** purpose **does** the **verse:** "All your males," **come?** It comes to teach **that which *Aḥerim*** taught. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: ***Aḥerim* say** that **a scrimper,** one who gathers dog feces to give them to tanners for the purpose of tanning hides; **a melder of copper,** who purifies copper from dross; **and a tanner** of hides **are** all **exempt from** the mitzva of **appearance,** as their occupation inflicts upon them an unpleasant odor. **As it is stated: "All your males,"** which indicates that only **one who is able to ascend with all your males** is obligated, **excluding these. As** people avoid their company, **they cannot ascend with all your males.**

The verse must therefore be coming to teach something else. The Gemara directs us to the teaching cited in the name of *Achereim* — a *beraita* we encountered earlier: the *mekametz* (one who works with dung), the one who smelts copper, and the *bursi* (tanner) — all of whom produce strong and offensive odors — are exempt from *re'iyah*. The source is the verse *kol zechurecha* — "all your males." The *beraita* interprets this to mean only those who could appear together as part of the full community: *mi sheyachol la'alot im kol zechurecha* — one who can ascend with the whole community. Those whose condition would require them to ascend separately, forming their own subgroup apart from the community, are excluded entirely: *yatz'u elu she-ein yecholim la'alot im kol zechurecha*. This is the true meaning of *ein nire'in chatza'im* — not that half a group may appear on one day and half on another, but that those who cannot be part of the unified whole are excluded from appearing altogether.

### Rashi's View

Rashi's interpretation is worth noting, not only for its content but for a remarkable personal observation he makes. Rashi explains *ein nire'in chatza'im* as referring to those who can only come in a small subgroup — *chabura mu'etet* — separate from the rest of the community, since they cannot join the whole. But what is striking is the way Rashi frames his interpretation: *kach nire'eh be-einai* — "this is how it seems to me," he writes, and then adds: *ve-chaverai meforshim* — "but my colleagues explain it differently." It is unusual for Rashi to speak of his colleagues as holding a distinct interpretation. One rarely thinks of Rashi as having peers in the ordinary sense. The comment is a rare and quietly humanizing window into the world of the Rashi we know primarily as an authoritative voice speaking alone from the margins of the page.

## Olot BaMoed

### Obligatory vs Optional

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עוֹלוֹת בַּמּוֹעֵד בָּאוֹת מִן הַחוּלִּין, וְהַשְּׁלָמִים מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר. יוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁל פֶּסַח, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: מִן הַחוּלִּין, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Burnt**-offerings that one sacrifices **on** the intermediate days of **the Festival** must **come from non-sacred** property, not from sacred property such as second-tithe money. **But the peace-**offerings may be brought **from the** second **tithe,** i.e., from money with which one redeemed second tithe, which is subsequently used to purchase food in Jerusalem. With regard to the Festival peace-offering sacrificed on **the first day of the festival of Passover, Beit Shammai say:** It must come **from non-sacred** property, **and Beit Hillel say:** It may be brought even **from the** second **tithe.**

The Gemara now turns to the second *mishnah* on the daf, which addresses the *korbanot* brought during *Yom Tov* and *Chol HaMoed* in more general terms, particularly the question of which sources of funds may be used to fulfill each obligation. The governing principle is *dvarim shebechovah einam ba'im ela min hachullin* — obligatory offerings may only come from non-sanctified funds. One cannot "double-dip," satisfying a divine obligation with funds that have already been designated or sanctified for another purpose.

### Three Korban Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יִשְׂרָאֵל יוֹצְאִין יְדֵי חוֹבָתָם בִּנְדָרִים וּנְדָבוֹת וּבְמַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה. וְהַכֹּהֲנִים בְּחַטָּאוֹת וַאֲשָׁמוֹת וּבִבְכוֹר, וּבְחָזֶה וָשׁוֹק. אֲבָל לֹא בְּעוֹפוֹת, וְלֹא בִּמְנָחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** In general, **Israelites fulfill their obligation** to eat peace-offerings of rejoicing **with** their voluntary **vows and gift** offerings donated during the year and sacrificed on the Festival; **and** likewise **with animal tithes. And the priests** fulfill their obligation of rejoicing **with** the meat of **sin**-offerings **and guilt**-offerings **and with firstborn** offerings, as the priests receive a portion of these, **and with the breast and thigh** of peace-offerings, to which they are also entitled. **However,** they do **not** fulfill their obligation **with birds,** e.g., a bird sacrificed as a sin-offering, **nor with meal**-offerings, as only the eating of meat constitutes rejoicing.

The *mishnah* addresses three distinct categories of *korbanot* brought during the festival. Understanding the *mishnah* requires recognizing that these three are conceptually distinct and governed by different rules regarding sources of funds.

The first is the *olat re'iyah* — the burnt offering brought when appearing before God in the Temple. The second is the *shalmei chagigah* — the peace offering brought to celebrate the *chag* itself, fulfilling the verse *vechagosem oto chag*. The third is the *shalmei simcha* — the peace offering brought to fulfill the obligation of rejoicing, *ve-samachta be-chagecha*, which the Rabbis understood to be fulfilled through the eating of meat brought as a *korban*.

The *mishnah* states that *olot ba-mo'ed ba'ot min ha-chullin* — *olot* brought on *Chol HaMoed* must come from unsanctified funds — and that *shlamim* may come from *ma'aser* funds. The *mishnah* then introduces a dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel regarding the *shalmei chagigah* on the first day of *Yom Tov* of Pesach.

To understand what *ma'aser* money means in this context: two out of every three years in the *ma'aser* cycle, the second tithe — *ma'aser sheni* — must be brought up to Jerusalem and spent there on food. The Torah permits its use for a wide variety of purchases, and using it to bring a *shelamim korban* and eat its meat is an excellent and natural use of those funds. The purpose of *ma'aser sheni* is precisely to enable a heightened religious encounter in Jerusalem — *ve-samachta lifnei Hashem* — and what better fulfillment than eating the meat of a *shelamim* brought in the *Beit HaMikdash*? So the question becomes: may the *ma'aser sheni* funds satisfy not just the general desire to eat meat in Jerusalem but the specific obligation of *shalmei chagigah* or *shalmei simcha*?

This connects to a broader principle: a pre-existing obligation in one domain cannot be discharged through a resource already obligated in another domain. The *ma'aser* funds carry their own mandate — to be brought to Jerusalem and eaten there. One cannot simultaneously use them to satisfy the independently imposed obligation to bring a *shalmei chagigah* or *olat re'iyah*. Or, put differently: if one has vowed to bring a *shelamim* as a personal undertaking and then seeks to count that same *shelamim* toward the divinely imposed obligation to bring a *shalmei simcha*, this conflation of one obligation with another is what the principle *dvarim shebechovah min hachullin* is designed to prevent.

### Olat Re'iyah Rules

The *olat re'iyah* must come from *chullin* — unsanctified funds — regardless of when it is brought, whether on *Yom Tov* or on *Chol HaMoed*. This is non-negotiable, as it is a full-fledged *davar shebechovah*.

### Shalmei Simcha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אֶלָּא: עוֹלוֹת — בַּמּוֹעֵד הוּא דְּבָאוֹת מִן הַחוּלִּין, הָא בְּיוֹם טוֹב — מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר? אַמַּאי, דָּבָר שֶׁבְּחוֹבָה, הִיא! וְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁבְּחוֹבָה, אֵינוֹ בָּא אֶלָּא מִן הַחוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: When the mishna states that **burnt-**offerings sacrificed **during the** intermediate days of **the Festival may come from non-sacred** property, this leads to the surprising conclusion that **on the Festival** they may even come **from the** second **tithe.** The Gemara asks: **Why** should this be the case? The burnt-offering **is an obligatory matter, and** there is a principle that **any obligatory matter** with regard to offerings **may come only from non-sacred** property that was consecrated expressly for this purpose alone, not from property that was previously consecrated for another purpose.

The *shalmei simcha*, by contrast, *ba'im af min hama'aser* — may come even from *ma'aser* funds. The reason, as the Gemara spells out, is that the *mitzvah* of *simcha* is not fundamentally an obligation to bring a *korban*. It is an obligation to experience joy, which the Rabbis understood to be achieved through eating the meat of a *shelamim*. Since the *korban* itself is not the object of the obligation — only the eating of its meat is — the fact that the animal was brought from funds already sanctified or already part of another obligation is not disqualifying. The *simcha* is achieved through the eating, and the eating can be accomplished regardless of the origin of the animal.

## Shalmei Chagigah (II)

### Dense Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּעוֹלוֹת בַּמּוֹעֵד בָּאוֹת, בְּיוֹם טוֹב אֵינָן בָּאוֹת, כְּמַאן — כְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי?!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that **this teaches us that burnt-**offerings **may come,** i.e., be sacrificed, **on** the intermediate days of **the Festival,** whereas **on the Festival they may not come** at all, **in accordance with whose** opinion is this mishna? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Shammai.**

The *mishnah* is notably dense, communicating through implication rather than explicit statement. The reference to *shlamim* coming from *ma'aser* is a cryptic reference specifically to *shalmei simcha*, not to all *shlamim*. The debate of Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel about the first day of *Yom Tov* of Pesach is a reference to the *shalmei chagigah*, not to *shalmei simcha*, and certainly not to the *olat re'iyah*. Reading the *mishnah* correctly requires knowing what type of offering is in view at each juncture — knowledge the *mishnah* presupposes rather than supplies. The Gemara will slowly unpack each component.

---

## Three Korbanot Framework

### Rejoicing Through Meat

To understand why *shalmei simcha* may be brought from already-sanctified funds, the conceptual distinction between the three *korbanot* must be clear. *Shalmei simcha* satisfies the obligation of *ve-samachta be-chagecha* — rejoicing on your festival. The Rabbis established that *simcha* for men is achieved through eating the meat of a *shelamim*. Crucially, the obligation is not to bring a *korban* but to eat meat that happens to be *korban* meat. It is the eating and the joy that constitute the fulfillment.

### Simcha Flexibility

Because the *shalmei simcha* obligation is fundamentally about the experience of eating sacred meat — and not about the act of bringing a *korban* — there is latitude in the source of the funds. If one had already vowed a *shelamim*, or if one's *ma'aser sheni* funds are being used to purchase meat in Jerusalem, those can serve the *mitzvah* of *simcha*. Similarly, the *mishnah* later states that *bnei Yisrael* can fulfill their *simcha* obligation through vows and free-will offerings, through *ma'aser behema*, and through other meat to which they have access — including the portions of *shelamim* that the *kohanim* receive, the firstborn animals brought as *korbanot*, and other sacred meats. The common thread is: any *korban* meat one eats in Jerusalem contributes to the fulfillment of *simcha*, regardless of the original purpose for which that *korban* was brought.

### Pure Gift Concept

The *olat re'iyah* operates on entirely different logic. It is a pure gift to God upon appearing before Him. The verse *lo yera'u fanai reikam* — do not appear before Me empty-handed — establishes that the act of appearing in the *Mikdash* must be accompanied by a tangible gift: an *olah* that goes entirely to God, consumed on the altar, with nothing returning to the one who brought it. The analogy suggests itself: it is like bringing a gift to a host when visiting — something given entirely for the host's benefit, not shared. Because this is a genuine and complete obligation to bring a *korban* in the fullest sense, it must come from *chullin* — unsanctified, personally owned funds. There is no room for double-counting.

### Celebration Offering

The *shalmei chagigah* occupies an intermediate position. It fulfills the verse *ve-chagosem oto chag* — you shall celebrate it as a festival — which requires the bringing of a *shelamim*. Unlike the *olat re'iyah*, the *shalmei chagigah* is eaten by the one who brings it; unlike the *shalmei simcha*, the obligation is specifically to bring a *korban* and not merely to eat meat. This dual character — both a genuine obligation to bring a *korban* and an offering whose meat is enjoyed — places the *shalmei chagigah* somewhere between the other two. It is this intermediate quality that makes the question of fund-mixing more nuanced, and that generates the debate between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel.

The contrast might be illustrated as follows. The *olat re'iyah* is what you give your host when you arrive — entirely for them. The *shalmei simcha* is the dessert you bring to be shared at the table. The *shalmei chagigah* is something in between: both a gift in the context of appearing and a shared celebration — both offering and feast.

## Shalmei Chagigah (II)

### Yom Tov Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: מְבִיאִין שְׁלָמִים וְאֵין סוֹמְכִין עֲלֵיהֶן, אֲבָל לֹא עוֹלוֹת. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מְבִיאִין שְׁלָמִים וְעוֹלוֹת, וְסוֹמְכִין עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna (*Beitza* 19a) that **Beit Shammai say: One brings peace-**offerings on a Festival, **but one does not place** his hands **on them,** as placing one's hands on an animal on a Festival is prohibited by rabbinic law. **However,** one may **not** sacrifice **burnt-**offerings at all, as they are not eaten, and slaughtering is permitted on a Festival only for human needs. **And Beit Hillel say: One brings** both **peace-**offerings **and burnt-**offerings, **and one does place** his hands **on them.** It is unlikely that an anonymous mishna would follow the opinion of Beit Shammai, whose rulings are not accepted as *halakha*.

The Gemara now turns to the specific question of why the *mishnah* specifies *olot* on *Chol HaMoed* as coming from *chullin*. Surely the same rule would apply on *Yom Tov* — why single out *Chol HaMoed*? The Gemara proposes that the *mishnah* may actually be making a different point: not that the source of funds is different on *Chol HaMoed*, but that non-obligatory *olot* may only be brought on *Chol HaMoed* at all — not on *Yom Tov* itself.

This is a striking result. The entire preceding *amud* assumed that the *olat re'iyah* is brought on the first day of *Yom Tov* — indeed, that this is its primary time. Yet here the Gemara entertains the possibility that even the *olat re'iyah* cannot be brought on *Yom Tov*. This turns out to be the position of Beit Shammai. The *mishnah* in *Beitza* records: Beit Shammai say one may bring *shelamim* on *Yom Tov* — since one will eat the meat, it falls within the category of *ochel nefesh* — but one may not perform *semichah* on them, since pressing one's hands on the animal is not necessary for food preparation. *Aval lo olot* — but one certainly may not bring an *olah* on *Yom Tov*, since there is no eating benefit whatsoever. Beit Hillel say one may bring both *shelamim* and *olot* on *Yom Tov*, and one may also perform *semichah*.

If this debate is about a generic *olah*, the stakes are clear enough. But the Gemara's implication here is more radical: according to Beit Shammai, even one's obligatory *olat re'iyah* cannot be brought on *Yom Tov* — it may only be brought on *Chol HaMoed*. This stands against everything the discussion has assumed. The Gemara, however, is unwilling to read the *mishnah* in our tractate as going like Beit Shammai and therefore rejects this interpretation.

### Chisurei Mechsara

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: עוֹלוֹת נְדָרִים וּנְדָבוֹת — בַּמּוֹעֵד בָּאוֹת, בְּיוֹם טוֹב — אֵינָן בָּאוֹת. וְעוֹלַת רְאִיָּיה בָּאָה אֲפִילּוּ בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וּכְשֶׁהִיא בָּאָה — אֵינָהּ בָּאָה אֶלָּא מִן הַחוּלִּין. וְשַׁלְמֵי שִׂמְחָה בָּאִין אַף מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר. וַחֲגִיגַת יוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁל פֶּסַח, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: מִן הַחוּלִּין, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This mishna **is incomplete, and this** is what **it is teaching: Burnt-**offerings brought as a fulfillment of one's **vow or as gift** offerings **may come on** the intermediate days of **the Festival.** However, **on the Festival** itself **they may not come. And the burnt-**offering **of appearance comes even on the Festival, but when it comes, it may come only from non-sacred** property, as it is obligatory. **And peace-**offerings **of rejoicing may come even from the** second **tithe. And** with regard to **the Festival peace-**offering brought on **the first day of Passover, Beit Shammai say:** It may come only **from non-sacred** property, **and Beit Hillel say:** It may come even **from the** second **tithe.**

*Chisurei mechsara* — the *mishnah* is incomplete and must be read with implied words added. The *mishnah*'s statement that *olot* on *Chol HaMoed* come from *chullin* does not mean that *olot* in general may only be brought on *Chol HaMoed*. Rather, the *mishnah* must be understood as distinguishing between two types of *olot*: free-will *olot* of vows and donations, which may only be brought on *Chol HaMoed*, and the *olat re'iyah*, which comes even on *Yom Tov* — and which, when brought, must come specifically from *chullin*.

### Olat Re'iyah Principle

The reconstructed teaching is: *olot nedarim u-nedavot ba-mo'ed ba'ot, be-Yom Tov einam ba'ot* — free-will *olot* come on *Chol HaMoed* but not on *Yom Tov* itself. And *olat re'iyah ba'ah afilu be-Yom Tov* — the *olat re'iyah* comes even on *Yom Tov*, and when it is brought, *eina ba'ah ela min hachullin* — it may only come from unsanctified funds. This is a difficult reading of the *mishnah*, which literally says *olot bamo'ed min hachullin*, suggesting that it is the *Chol HaMoed olot* that must come from *chullin*. The Gemara must work hard to reconstruct the text so that it is the *olat re'iyah* — brought on *Yom Tov* — that is subject to the *chullin* requirement, while the non-obligatory *olot* brought on *Chol HaMoed* are not constrained in the same way. The reading requires considerable *chisurei mechsara*, but it is the only reading the Gemara is willing to accept, given that the alternative would cast the *mishnah* as following the minority position of Beit Shammai.

### Pesach Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: עוֹלוֹת, נְדָרִים וּנְדָבוֹת — בַּמּוֹעֵד בָּאוֹת, בְּיוֹם טוֹב — אֵינָן בָּאוֹת. וְעוֹלַת רְאִיָּיה בָּאָה אֲפִילּוּ בְּיוֹם טוֹב. וּכְשֶׁהִיא בָּאָה — אֵינָהּ בָּאָה אֶלָּא מִן הַחוּלִּין. וְשַׁלְמֵי שִׂמְחָה בָּאִין אַף מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר. וַחֲגִיגַת יוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁל פֶּסַח, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: מִן הַחוּלִּין, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **That** opinion **is also taught** in a *baraita*: **Burnt-**offerings brought as a fulfillment of one's **vow or** as **gift** offerings **may come on** the intermediate days of **the Festival.** However, **on** the Festival **itself they may not come. And the burnt**-offering **of appearance comes even on the Festival, but when it comes it may come only from non-sacred** property. **And peace-**offerings **of rejoicing may come even from the** second **tithe. And** with regard to **the Festival peace-**offering brought on **the first day of the festival of Passover, Beit Shammai say:** It may come only **from non-sacred** property, **and Beit Hillel say:** It may come even **from the** second **tithe.**

The *mishnah* specifies the *chagigah* of the first day of Pesach rather than simply *chagigat Yom Tov* in general. The Gemara asks: why the specificity? Rav Ashi explains: the *mishnah* is distinguishing the *chagigah* of the fifteenth of Nisan from the *chagigat arba'ah asar* — the *chagigah* of the fourteenth. There is a *shelamim* that was brought together with the *korban Pesach*, called the *chagigat arba'ah asar* or more explicitly *chagigat arba'ah asar ha-ba'ah im ha-Pesach*. This is a well-known institution: since the Pesach must be eaten *al hasova* — when one is full and sated, not in a ravenous state — a companion *shelamim* was brought alongside the Pesach to fill the participants first. There is also a concern about breaking the bones of the Pesach, and the companion offering helps fill the group before the Pesach is consumed. This offering is called a *chagigah* but is not a *chagigah* in the strict sense — it is not brought on the *chag* itself but on the fourteenth, the eve of the festival.

### Arba'ah Asar Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא חֲגִיגַת יוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁל פֶּסַח? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, חֲגִיגַת חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר — אִין, חֲגִיגַת אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **the Festival peace-**offering brought on **the first day of the festival of Passover? Rav Ashi said: This** emphasis on the specific date **teaches us** that for **the Festival peace-**offering **of the fifteenth,** i.e., the pilgrimage Festival offerings brought on the first day of Passover, **yes,** this restriction applies; however, for **the Festival peace-**offering **of the fourteenth, no,** this limitation is not in force. If the Paschal lamb was too small for a group, they would bring an additional peace-offering so that the Paschal lamb could be eaten when one was full. This offering was called the Festival peace-offering of the fourteenth of Nisan. Since this offering is not obligatory, everyone agrees that it may be purchased with second-tithe money.

The *chagigat arba'ah asar* is not a strict biblical obligation. It is a mechanism for ensuring the Pesach is eaten in the appropriate manner — without urgency and without bone-breaking — and if the group is small enough that each participant will have a generous portion, the *chagigat arba'ah asar* is not needed at all. Because it is not a firm obligation, it may be brought from *ma'aser* funds and other already-sanctified sources. When the *mishnah* specifies the *chagigah* of the first day of Pesach as the one that is subject to the Beit Shammai/Beit Hillel debate — with Beit Shammai requiring *chullin* and Beit Hillel permitting *ma'aser* — it does so to distinguish this genuine *chagigah* from the *chagigat arba'ah asar*, which carries no such restriction. The same rule would apply to the *chagigah* of Sukkot and the *chagigah* of Shavuot; Pesach is mentioned specifically to draw the contrast with the fourteenth.

## Mixing Funds Debate

### Why No Pre-Sanctified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּעוֹלוֹת בַּמּוֹעֵד בָּאוֹת, בְּיוֹם טוֹב אֵינָן בָּאוֹת, כְּמַאן — כְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי?!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that **this teaches us that burnt-**offerings **may come,** i.e., be sacrificed, **on** the intermediate days of **the Festival,** whereas **on the Festival they may not come** at all, **in accordance with whose** opinion is this mishna? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Shammai.**

The Gemara returns to the question that drove the entire discussion: why may one not use pre-sanctified or already-obligated funds to fulfill a *davar shebechovah*? The intuition might seem to run the other way — if one has already sanctified money for a *korban*, surely that sanctification would make it *more* appropriate for fulfilling an obligation, not less. The proper way to frame the prohibition, as the Gemara makes clear, is not simply about "sanctified versus unsanctified" funds in the abstract, but about double-counting obligations. If one has accepted upon oneself a personal obligation to bring a *shelamim*, and later wishes to use that same *shelamim* to discharge the divinely imposed *mitzvah* of *shalmei simcha*, this is inappropriate — one obligation cannot ride piggyback on another. Similarly, *ma'aser sheni* funds carry their own mandate — to be brought to Jerusalem and enjoyed there — and they cannot simultaneously discharge a separate, distinct divine obligation. The underlying principle is that each obligation demands its own dedicated fulfillment.

### Ulla's Tofel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר, בֵּית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר. אַמַּאי? דָּבָר שֶׁבְּחוֹבָה הוּא, וְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁבְּחוֹבָה אֵינוֹ בָּא אֶלָּא מִן הַחוּלִּין? אָמַר עוּלָּא: בְּטוֹפֵל.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Master said** in the *baraita* that **Beit Hillel say:** Festival peace-offerings may be brought even **from the** second **tithe.** The Gemara asks: **Why? It is an obligatory matter, and any obligatory matter may come only from non-sacred** property. **Ulla said:** This is referring to a case **where** one **combines,** i.e., the offering is purchased with a combination of second-tithe funds and non-sacred money. The Gemara adds that *amora'im* dispute the details of this *halakha*.

Ulla addresses the question of how *beit Hillel*'s leniency can be reconciled with this principle. If the *shalmei chagigah* is an obligatory *korban*, why does Beit Hillel permit it to be brought from *ma'aser*? Ulla explains: *be-tofel* — the leniency applies to an *add-on*. The base *chagigah* obligation is discharged with one's own unsanctified funds. If one then wishes to bring additional animals — because one has a large family and wants more meat — one may supplement using *ma'aser* funds. The original obligation is satisfied through *chullin*; what follows is the *tofel*, the supplement, which may draw on *ma'aser*.

### Chizkiyah's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר, טוֹפְלִין בְּהֵמָה לִבְהֵמָה, וְאֵין טוֹפְלִין מָעוֹת לְמָעוֹת. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: טוֹפְלִין מָעוֹת לְמָעוֹת, וְאֵין טוֹפְלִין בְּהֵמָה לִבְהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ḥizkiya said: One may combine** one **animal with** another **animal.** If one has a large household for which one animal will not suffice, he brings one animal for the Festival offering from non-sacred money and he may combine funds of second tithe to purchase a second animal. Since he has fulfilled his obligation of the Festival offering by bringing one animal from non-sacred money, he may bring the others from second tithe or animal tithes. **But one may not combine money with** other **money,** i.e., one may not combine second-tithe funds with non-sacred money toward the purchase of one animal for the Festival offering. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One** may **combine money with** other **money, but one may not combine** one **animal with** another **animal.**

Chizkiyah states: *toflin behema le-vehema* — one may add animal to animal, bringing the first from *chullin* and then additional animals from *ma'aser*. But *ein toflin ma'ot le-ma'ot* — one may not mix funds. One cannot take thirty *zuz* of *chullin* money and forty *zuz* of *ma'aser* money and purchase a single animal with the combined sum, because then no single animal stands as an unambiguous fulfillment of the base obligation from *chullin*. The first animal must be purchased entirely from one's own unsanctified funds; subsequent animals may come from *ma'aser*.

### Rav Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan takes the opposite position: *toflin ma'ot le-ma'ot* — one may mix funds for a single purchase — but *ein toflin behema le-vehema* — one may not bring a second, entirely separate animal as a *chagigah* from *ma'aser*. According to Rabbi Yochanan, as long as each animal has *some* of one's own *chullin* money in it, it qualifies as part of the *chagigah* obligation. It is somewhat puzzling why one would need to frame additional animals as part of the *chagigah* at all — after one has fulfilled the base obligation, any additional animals could simply be designated as *shalmei simcha* and brought without restriction. Why must the additional animals be framed as *chagigah* for there to be a problem in the first place? This remains unclear from the Gemara's discussion.

### Tannaitic Sources

Both positions find support in tannaitic sources. The Gemara cites *braitot* in support of each view.

### Missat Yadecha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּחִזְקִיָּה: ״מִסַּת״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁאָדָם מֵבִיא חוֹבָתוֹ מִן הַחוּלִּין, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: יוֹם רִאשׁוֹן מִן הַחוּלִּין, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֲכִילָה רִאשׁוֹנָה מִן הַחוּלִּין, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Ḥizkiya: "After the measure** of the gift offering of your hand" (Deuteronomy 16:10). This **teaches that a person brings his obligatory** offering **from non-sacred** property. Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel disagree with regard to this issue, as **Beit Shammai say:** On **the first day** one may bring only **from non-sacred** property. **From this** point **forward** one may buy the animal **from the** second-**tithe** money. **And Beit Hillel say: The first eating** must be **from non-sacred** property. **From this** point **forward** one may bring **from the** second-**tithe** money. The phrase: The first eating, means the first animal that one eats, which is in accordance with Ḥizkiya's opinion.

The relevant verse is *missat yadecha* — the free giving of your hand — which appears in the context of *ve-asita chag Shavuot la-Adonai Elohecha*. The word *missat* is understood to indicate something that flows from one's own resources, one's own freely given funds. *Melamed she-adam meivi chovato min hachullin* — this teaches that one's base obligation must come from one's own unsanctified funds. But whence the permission to supplement with *ma'aser* or other funds? The verse continues: *ka'asher yevarechecha Adonai Elohecha* — as God has blessed you. This is understood to include everything with which God has blessed you, including *ma'aser* funds and the produce of the land. Thus the base obligation comes from *chullin*, and the supplement may come from the full range of divine blessing.

The *beraita* in support of Chizkiyah reads: *missat — melamed she-adam meivi chovato min hachullin. Beit Shammai omrim: yom rishon min hachullin, mikan ve-eilach min hama'aser* — the first day's *chagigah* must come from *chullin*; from then onward it may come from *ma'aser*. *U-Veit Hillel omrim: achilah rishonah min hachullin* — the first eating, meaning the first animal, comes from *chullin*, and subsequent animals may come from *ma'aser*. This supports Chizkiyah's view that the distinction is between the first animal and subsequent animals, not between mixed and unmixed funds.

### Ma'aser Behema

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּשְׁאָר כׇּל יְמוֹת הַפֶּסַח, אָדָם יוֹצֵא יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בְּמַעֲשַׂר בְּהֵמָה. בְּיוֹם טוֹב מַאי טַעְמָא לָא?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* concludes: All this applies on the first day of the Festival. **But** with regard to **all the other days of Passover,** on which one is obligated to bring peace-offerings of rejoicing, **a person fulfills his obligation** even **with animal tithes,** and certainly with funds of second tithe. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason that one does not** fulfill his obligation with animal tithes **on the Festival?**

The *mishnah* also mentions that throughout the rest of Pesach, one may fulfill the *simcha* obligation even through *ma'aser behema* — the animal tithe, whereby one out of every ten newborn animals is brought as a *korban* and its meat eaten. The Gemara asks: if *ma'aser behema* may be used on the later days of Pesach, why may it not be used on the first day of *Yom Tov*?

### Sikra Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְעַשּׂוֹרֵי בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וְאִי אֶפְשָׁר לְעַשֵּׂר בְּיוֹם טוֹב מִשּׁוּם סְקַרְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** There is a concern that if one requires additional animals, **perhaps he may come to separate tithes on the Festival** itself, **and one may not separate tithes on the Festival due to the red dye [*sikrata*].** Every tenth animal is marked with red dye, and as it is prohibited to dye on a Festival due to the labor of writing, one may not separate tithes on a Festival.

Rav Ashi answers: *dilma ati le-asuri be-Yom Tov* — one might come to *take* the *ma'aser behema* tithe on *Yom Tov* itself, and that is forbidden. The process of tithing animals requires marking the tenth animal with a stick dipped in paint — *sikra* — to identify it as the *ma'aser*. This act of marking constitutes *tzoveia* — dyeing — and possibly *kotev* — writing — both of which are prohibited on *Yom Tov*. One might note that the ordinary reason *trumot u-ma'asrot* cannot be taken on *Yom Tov* — that doing so *metakken* the produce, rendering it edible — does not apply to *ma'aser behema*, since the untithed animals are not *tevel* and are not forbidden prior to the tithe being taken. The specific prohibition on taking *ma'aser behema* on *Yom Tov* therefore rests on this separate concern of *sikra*, the painting of the animal.

The daf concludes with a final linguistic observation:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַשְׁמַע דְּהַאי מִסַּת לִישָּׁנָא דְחוּלִּין הוּא? דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיָּשֶׂם הַמֶּלֶךְ אֲחַשְׁוֵרוֹשׁ מַס עַל הָאָרֶץ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where may it be inferred that this: "After the measure [*missat*],"** mentioned in the verse, **is a term for non-sacred** property? The Gemara answers: **As it is written: "And the king Ahasuerus laid a tribute [*mas*] upon the land"** (Esther 10:1), which is clearly referring to a tribute of non-sacred property.

The word *misat* in the verse — *mai mashma dehai misat lishna de-chullin hu* — carries the connotation of one's own personal funds, like an assessment or tax drawn from one's own income. The proof is from *Esther*: *vayasem hamelech Achashverosh mas al ha'aretz* — King Achashverosh imposed a *mas*, a tax, on the land — which is a levy drawn from personal wealth. The word *misat yadecha* similarly indicates that the base obligation of the *chagigah* must come from the pilgrim's own personal, unsanctified resources.
