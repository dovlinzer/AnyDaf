# Hullin 91 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Gid Hanasheh: Both or One...

### Mishna vs. Rabbi Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּיָרֵךְ שֶׁל יָמִין וּבְיָרֵךְ שֶׁל שְׂמֹאל. מַתְנִיתִין לָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ נוֹהֵג אֶלָּא בְּאַחַת, וְהַדַּעַת מַכְרַעַת אֶת שֶׁל יָמִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that the prohibition of the sciatic nerve applies **to** the **thigh of the right** leg **and to** the **thigh of the left** leg. The Gemara says: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The prohibition of the sciatic nerve **applies only to** the sciatic nerve **in one** of the animal's thighs, **and logic dictates** that it is **the right** thigh.

We begin on 91b, picking up the discussion of whether the *gid hanasheh* applies to both thighs or only one. Our mishna rules that it applies to both, and this is the normative *halacha*. The Gemara opens by noting that our mishna does not follow Rabbi Yehuda, as a *braita* teaches: Rabbi Yehuda says the prohibition applies only to one of the thighs, and *hada'at machra'at* — the reasoning inclines, or the Torah's reasoning indicates — that it is the right one. It is worth noting in passing that the phrase *da'at machra'at* is one of the early appearances of the concept later associated with *da'at Torah*, though the meaning here is quite different: it simply means that if one examines the Torah carefully, the evidence points to the right side.

### Certainty vs. Doubt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מִיפְשָׁט פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וּמַאי דַּעַת? דַּעַת תּוֹרָה, אוֹ דִלְמָא סַפּוֹקֵי מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ, וּמַאי דַּעַת? דַּעַת נוֹטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Is it **obvious to Rabbi Yehuda** that the prohibition applies only to the sciatic nerve in the right thigh, **and** accordingly, **what** does he mean when he says that **logic** dictates? He means **the logic of the Torah. Or perhaps he is uncertain** as to whether it applies only to the right thigh or only to the left, **and** accordingly, **what** does he mean when he says that **logic** dictates? He means that **logic inclines** one to believe that the prohibition applies to the right thigh.

This leads to the *ibaya*: is it *pshita* to Rabbi Yehuda — does he know with certainty which thigh is prohibited? Or does he merely say *sfukei m'safka lei* — that he genuinely doubts whether it is the right or the left, and the phrase "the reasoning inclines" means only that logic leans in one direction without definitive evidence? This question has real *halachic* consequences, as we will see.

### Korban Pesach Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הָעֲצָמוֹת וְהַגִּידִים וְהַנּוֹתָר יִשָּׂרְפוּ לְשִׁשָּׁה עָשָׂר. וְהָוֵינַן בַּהּ: הָנֵי גִּידֵי מַאי עֲבִידְתַּיְיהוּ? אִי גִּידֵי בָשָׂר – לֵיכְלִינְהוּ, וְאִי דְּאִיַּיתּוּר – הַיְינוּ נוֹתָר! אֶלָּא גִּידֵי צַוָּאר, אִי לָאו בָּשָׂר נִינְהוּ – לִישְׁדִּינְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution based upon the following mishna (*Pesaḥim* 83a): **The bones** of the Paschal offering that contain edible marrow but cannot be eaten because it is prohibited to break the bones of the Paschal offering; **and the sinews; and the leftover** meat **should** all **be burned on the sixteenth** of Nisan, not on the fifteenth, the first day of Passover. **And we discussed it: What are the circumstances** in which **these sinews** must be burned? **If we say** they are **sinews of meat, let one eat them.** Why are they burned? **And if** they are sinews **that were left over** and not eaten, **that is** the case of **leftover** meat; why does the mishna list sinews separately? **Rather,** the mishna is referring to **sinews of the neck,** which are different from other sinews and are therefore mentioned separately. But **if they are not meat,** why do they require burning? **Let one** simply **discard them** like other waste.

The Gemara attempts to resolve this from a *braita* concerning the *korban pesach*: the bones, sinews, and any *notar* — leftover meat — must be burned on the sixteenth of Nisan. Burning on the fifteenth is impossible because one may not burn *kodashim* on *yom tov*, even *pasul kodashim*, so one waits until after *yom tov*.

The Gemara then asks: what is the *chiddush* of mentioning the sinews? If they are edible sinews, one should simply eat them — why burn them? And if they happen to be left over, they are already *notar* — the verse has already covered them. The Gemara concludes that the reference must be to inedible sinews, such as those in the neck. But then another problem arises: if they are not meat at all — if they are physically inedible — they carry no obligation of eating in the context of the *korban*, and therefore they are not *notar*. They should simply be discarded; why must they be burned?

### Rav Chisda's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לְגִיד הַנָּשֶׁה, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר: אֵינוֹ נוֹהֵג אֶלָּא בְּאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥisda said:** The mishna's mention of sinews **is necessary only** in order to teach the *halakha* **of the sciatic nerve,** and **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda, who said:** The prohibition of the sciatic nerve **applies only to** the sciatic nerve in **one** of the animal's thighs, and not to both.

Rav Chisda resolves this: the *chiddush* refers specifically to the *gid hanasheh* according to Rabbi Yehuda. In this case, the sinew is not inedible for physical reasons but because it is halachically prohibited. Now the Gemara from the previous daf is relevant: something prohibited by law does not carry the *mitzvah* of eating within the *korban* context, and therefore it should not be *notar* either — just discard it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא סַפּוֹקֵי מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ – שַׁפִּיר, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ מִיפְשָׁט פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ דְּהֶיתֵּירָא – לֵיכְלֵיהּ, דְּאִיסּוּרָא – לִשְׁדְּיֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains how this resolves the dilemma: **Granted if you say** that Rabbi Yehuda **is uncertain** as to whether it is the sciatic nerve of the right or the left thigh, it works out **well.** Since it is uncertain which thigh may be eaten, one may not eat either of them, and one must burn each of them on the sixteenth due to the possibility that it was the permitted one and now has the status of leftover meat. **But if you say** that it is **obvious to** Rabbi Yehuda that the sciatic nerve of only the right thigh is forbidden, **let him eat** the sciatic nerve **of the permitted** left thigh, and **let him throw** away only the sciatic nerve **of the forbidden** right thigh. Neither one should be burned.

But here the Gemara explains: *if* Rabbi Yehuda holds that there is genuine doubt about which thigh is the *gid hanasheh*, the case becomes clear. You cannot eat either *gid* because one of them is definitively the *gid hanasheh* — though you do not know which one. That is precisely the situation: one is the quarter, so to speak, and the other is the *gid*. You cannot eat the right or the left because one of them is certainly prohibited. But you must also burn them, because the one that is not the *gid hanasheh* was supposed to be eaten, and since you do not know which that is, you must burn both rather than eat either. This creates the unusual scenario of passively transgressing the prohibition of *lo totiru* — leaving meat over from the *korban* until morning — rather than actively transgressing the prohibition of *gid hanasheh*. The principle applies: it is preferable to transgress passively than actively.

This scenario makes sense only if Rabbi Yehuda harbors genuine doubt. If he *knew* which side was prohibited, there would be no difficulty: eat the permitted one, discard the prohibited one. There would be no grounds for burning anything.

### Avika's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אִיקָא בַּר חֲנִינָא: לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ מִיפְשָׁט פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּשֶׁהוּכְּרוּ וּלְבַסּוֹף נִתְעָרְבוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Rav Ika bar Ḥanina said: Actually I** could **say to you** that it is **obvious to** Rabbi Yehuda that the sciatic nerve of only the right thigh is forbidden. Nevertheless, **here we are dealing with** a case **where** the sciatic nerves **were identified** and removed, **but ultimately** the sciatic nerves **became intermingled** and one cannot tell which is from the right thigh and which is from the left thigh. Consequently, they must both be left over until the next day and then burned.

Avika bar Chanina offers a different resolution. Perhaps Rabbi Yehuda does in fact know which side is the *gid hanasheh* — it is definitively the right. The *braita* about burning, however, is dealing with a case where you originally knew which was the right thigh and which was the left, but then they became mixed up. In such a scenario, even if Rabbi Yehuda knows with certainty that it is the right *gid* that is prohibited, since the two *gidim* are now indistinguishable, you cannot eat either — and since one of them was obligated to be eaten, you burn both. This resolution preserves the view that Rabbi Yehuda has no doubt about which side is prohibited, while still explaining why burning is required.

## Why Burn the Gid?

### Rav Ashi: The Fat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לְשׁוּמְנוֹ, דְּתַנְיָא: שׁוּמְנוֹ מוּתָּר, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל קְדוֹשִׁים נָהֲגוּ בּוֹ אִיסּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** The mishna's ruling that the sinews must be burned **is necessary only** with regard to the **fat** around the sciatic nerve, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The **fat** around the sciatic nerve **is permitted** by Torah law, **but the Jewish people are holy** and **treated it as forbidden.** Since it is permitted by Torah law, it has the status of sacrificial meat and may not be simply discarded. Nevertheless, since the Jewish people treat it as forbidden, they do not eat it even from the Paschal offering, despite the mitzva to eat that offering in its entirety. Therefore, it is left until after the time when the meat may be eaten and it is burned as leftover sacrificial meat.

We now turn to the top of 91a for two alternative explanations that have nothing to do with Rabbi Yehuda's position. Rav Ashi says: *lo nitzrecha ela leshumno* — the *chiddush* of burning the sinew of the *korban pesach* concerns not the *gid hanasheh* itself but the fat surrounding it. The term used here is *shuman* rather than *chelev*, because the fat around the *gid hanasheh* is not forbidden *chelev*; at most it is rabbinically restricted. A *braita* supports this: *shumno mutar* — the fat around the *gid* is biblically permitted — but *Yisrael kedoshim nahagu bo issur* — because the Jewish people are holy, they have adopted the practice of refraining from it.

The *chiddush* is therefore as follows: you did not eat this fat because of widespread *minhag*, but since the Torah permits it biblically, it legally remains *notar* — meat that was supposed to be eaten — and it must therefore be burned rather than discarded. This case raises a striking observation: a *minhag* is causing a person to passively transgress a biblical prohibition of *notar*. The Gemara itself does not raise this as a difficulty and simply takes for granted that the *minhag* stands and the consequence is burning rather than eating. Nevertheless, the tension is genuinely remarkable, particularly since one could construct a scenario where someone has not yet eaten a *kezayit* of the *korban pesach*, and the only remaining meat is this rabbinically restricted fat — in which case the question of whether to eat it to fulfill the *mitzvah* becomes exceedingly pointed.

### Ravina: Two Sinews

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לְכִדְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, שְׁנֵי גִידִין הֵן: פְּנִימִי סָמוּךְ לְעֶצֶם – אָסוּר, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו; חִיצוֹן סָמוּךְ לְבָשָׂר – אָסוּר, וְאֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said:** The mishna's statement **is necessary only** with regard to the outer nerve, and it is **in accordance with that which Rav Yehuda** said that **Shmuel said. As Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: There are two nerves** included in the prohibition of the sciatic nerve. The **inner** nerve that is **next to the bone is forbidden** by Torah law, **and one is liable** to be flogged **for** eating **it.** The **outer** nerve that is **next to the flesh is forbidden** by rabbinic law, **and** therefore **one is not liable** to be flogged **for** eating **it.** In the case of a Paschal offering, since the outer nerve is permitted by Torah law, it assumes the status of leftover sacrificial meat when it is not eaten.

Ravina offers yet another answer. He cites Rabbi Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *shnei gidim hem* — there are actually two sinews running through the thigh. The inner one, *pnimi samuch l'etzem* — close to the thigh bone — is the biblical *gid hanasheh*, and one who eats it is *chayav*, liable for the biblical transgression. The outer one, *samuch l'basar* — closer to the fleshy part of the thigh on its outer side — is only rabbinically prohibited, and one is not biblically liable for eating it.

Looking at a diagram of the thigh's anatomy, this distinction becomes clearer: the inner sinew running alongside the thigh bone for the full length of the thigh is the biblical *gid hanasheh*; the outer sinew — known as the *gid hachitzon* — which runs only partway down is the rabbinic one. According to Ravina, the *braita* about burning the sinew of the *korban pesach* concerns this outer, rabbinic sinew. Since the Torah biblically permits it — one is merely rabbinically forbidden — it remains legally obligated to be eaten and therefore qualifies as *notar*, requiring burning rather than simple disposal. The rabbis said not to eat it, so you did not; but since the Torah says you could have eaten it, it is *notar* and must be burned.

This again raises the question of a *minhag* — or here a rabbinic enactment — causing a passive biblical transgression of *notar*, and even the possibility of someone who has not yet fulfilled his *kezayit* of *korban pesach* being left with only this outer sinew. The Gemara does not address these questions but proceeds to the next stage of the discussion.

## Which Side? Right or Left...

### Eating Both Thighs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אָכַל מִזֶּה כְּזַיִת וּמִזֶּה כְּזַיִת – סוֹפֵג שְׁמוֹנִים, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ סוֹפֵג אֶלָּא אַרְבָּעִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another resolution to the dilemma about Rabbi Yehuda's statement, from a mishna (96a): If **one ate an olive-bulk from this** sciatic nerve of the right leg **and an olive-bulk from that** sciatic nerve of the left leg, **he incurs eighty** lashes. **Rabbi Yehuda says: He incurs only forty** lashes.

We return to the core question: does Rabbi Yehuda know which side is the prohibited *gid*, or does he not? The Gemara cites another source that was encountered just a few days earlier. If one ate a *kezayit* of the *gid* from the right thigh and a *kezayit* from the left thigh, the Rabbis say he receives eighty lashes — two sets, one for each transgression — assuming he was warned separately for each. Rabbi Yehuda says he receives only forty lashes.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא מִיפְשָׁט פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ – שַׁפִּיר, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ סַפּוֹקֵי מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ – הָוְיָא לַהּ הַתְרָאַת סָפֵק, וְשָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּאָמַר: הַתְרָאַת סָפֵק לֹא שְׁמָהּ הַתְרָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if you say** it is **obvious to** Rabbi Yehuda that the sciatic nerve of the right thigh is the one forbidden by Torah law, it is **well. But if you say he is uncertain,** why would he hold that one incurs any lashes? When the individual is forewarned not to partake of each sciatic nerve, which is necessary in order to be liable for incurring lashes, **it is an uncertain forewarning, and we have heard** about **Rabbi Yehuda that he said: An uncertain forewarning is not characterized as forewarning.**

The Gemara now examines the logic. If Rabbi Yehuda knows which side is prohibited — and we will see it is the right — then the ruling is straightforward: you transgressed on the right and receive one set of lashes; you did not transgress on the left and receive no lashes there. That is perfectly coherent.

But if Rabbi Yehuda genuinely doubts which side is prohibited, a different problem arises. How can you receive even one set of lashes? Before eating each *gid*, the warning (*hatra'ah*) would have been doubtful — *hatra'at safek* — because at the moment of warning, you did not know whether this particular act would constitute a transgression. Rabbi Yehuda himself, as established from a *braita*, holds that *hatra'at safek lo shemah hatra'ah* — a doubtful warning does not count as warning. The *braita* discusses a man who does not know which of two men is his father, strikes one and then the other, curses one and then the other. Rabbi Yehuda rules that if done sequentially, he is exempt, because even though by the end we know he has certainly struck his father, before each individual act the warning could not be unequivocal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: הִכָּה אֶת זֶה, וְחָזַר וְהִכָּה אֶת זֶה, קִלֵּל אֶת זֶה, וְחָזַר וְקִלֵּל אֶת זֶה, הִכָּה שְׁנֵיהֶם בְּבַת אַחַת, אוֹ שֶׁקִּלֵּל שְׁנֵיהֶם בְּבַת אַחַת – חַיָּיב. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּבַת אַחַת – חַיָּיב, בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If one is uncertain which of two men is his father and he **struck this** one **and then struck that** one, or **cursed this** one **and then cursed that** one, or **struck both of them simultaneously, or cursed both of them simultaneously, he is liable** to receive the death penalty, as he certainly struck or cursed his father. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** If he struck or cursed both of them **simultaneously he is liable,** provided he was forewarned that his action will certainly render him liable to receive the death penalty. But if he struck or cursed them **one after the other he is exempt.** Apparently, Rabbi Yehuda is of the opinion that one is not punished after uncertain forewarning; since in this case it is impossible to determine which of them is the father, inevitably each forewarning is uncertain. Similarly, if Rabbi Yehuda is uncertain which sciatic nerve is forbidden by Torah law, he should hold that since the forewarning before eating either sciatic nerve is uncertain, one who consumes both should be exempt.

This would mean that if Rabbi Yehuda truly doubts which *gid* is prohibited, no lashes could be administered at all — not even forty. The ruling of forty lashes, then, is difficult to explain on the assumption of doubt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי תַּנָּא סָבַר לַהּ כְּאִידַּךְ תַּנָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר: הַתְרָאַת סָפֵק שְׁמָהּ הַתְרָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This *tanna*** of that mishna, on 96a, **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **another *tanna*** with regard to the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda, who said** that Rabbi Yehuda holds that **an uncertain forewarning is characterized as forewarning.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״לֹא תוֹתִירוּ מִמֶּנּוּ עַד בֹּקֶר וְגוֹ׳״ – בָּא הַכָּתוּב לִיתֵּן עֲשֵׂה אַחַר לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה, לוֹמַר שֶׁאֵין לוֹקִין עָלָיו, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the Paschal offering: **"And you shall not leave any of it until morning;** but that which remains of it until morning you shall burn with fire" (Exodus 12:10). **The verse comes to provide a positive mitzva** to burn the leftover meat **after** it has taught **a prohibition** against leaving it over, **to say that one is not flogged for** violation **of** the prohibition; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.** Rabbi Yehuda holds that one is not flogged for any transgression that can be rectified by the performance of a positive mitzva.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: לֹא מִן הַשֵּׁם הוּא זֶה, אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה לָאו שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה, וְכׇל לָאו שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה – אֵין לוֹקִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ya'akov says: This,** the *halakha* that one is not flogged, **is not for** that **reason. Rather,** it is **because** leaving over sacrificial meat **is a prohibition that does not involve an action,** as one violates the prohibition through failure to take action, **and** concerning **any prohibition that does not involve an action, one is not flogged for** its violation. The forewarning one could receive for this transgression is an uncertain one, because witnesses who forewarn the individual cannot be certain until daybreak that he will leave it over. Nevertheless, this *baraita* indicates that if not for the fact that leaving over sacrificial meat can be rectified by a positive mitzva, Rabbi Yehuda would hold that one is flogged for leaving over sacrificial meat.

The Gemara resolves this tension: *hai tanna savar lah ke'idach tanna deRabbi Yehuda* — the *tanna* who recorded the ruling of forty lashes holds by a different version of Rabbi Yehuda's position, one that says *hatra'at safek shemah hatra'ah* — even doubtful warning suffices, as long as by the end of the act we know a transgression occurred. The *braita* on *notar* supports this: the verse says do not leave over; if you do, burn it. The Torah gave a positive commandment after the negative, signaling that there are no lashes for the transgression — Rabbi Yehuda's view — because the positive *mitzvah* of burning provides a remedy. Rabbi Yaakov disagrees and says the reason for exemption from lashes is simply that it is a *lav she'ein bo ma'aseh* — a passive transgression, which never carries lashes. Notably, neither gives *hatra'at safek* as his reason, suggesting that in this context Rabbi Yehuda does not object to doubtful warning as grounds to exempt from lashes. Accordingly, the ruling of forty lashes can coexist with Rabbi Yehuda's doubt about which side is prohibited.

### Two Animals Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אָכַל שְׁנֵי גִידִין מִשְּׁתֵּי יְרֵכוֹת מִשְּׁתֵּי בְהֵמוֹת – סוֹפֵג שְׁמוֹנִים; רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ סוֹפֵג אֶלָּא אַרְבָּעִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution of the uncertainty with regard to the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda from a *baraita*: If **one ate two** sciatic **nerves from two thighs of two** different **animals, he incurs eighty** lashes. **Rabbi Yehuda says: He incurs only forty** lashes.

The Gemara attempts one more proof, and this one is quite elegant. The source states: if one ate two *gidei hanasheh* from two thighs from two different animals — meaning the same *side* from two separate animals — the Rabbis say eighty lashes and Rabbi Yehuda says forty. The Gemara observes: why did the source need to specify two different animals? Why not simply present the scenario of the right and left from the same animal? The only reason to insist on two animals is if it matters which side we are discussing — because if both sides were equally prohibited, any two *gidim* would suffice for the scenario, including right and left from the same animal. The fact that the source took care to specify two animals of the same side demonstrates that this scenario was constructed specifically to satisfy Rabbi Yehuda's position that only one particular side is prohibited, and to ensure that both *gidim* in the scenario are indisputably forbidden even according to him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּקָאָמַר מִשְׁתֵּי יְרֵכוֹת מִשְּׁתֵּי בְהֵמוֹת, פְּשִׁיטָא דְּתַרְוַיְיהוּ לְאִיסּוּרָא, וּלְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אִיצְטְרִיךְ. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִיפְשָׁט פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **From** the fact that the first *tanna* **said** that he was referring to **two thighs of two** different **animals,** it is **obvious** that he meant they were both from the right thigh, because if one were from the right thigh and one were from the left thigh it would not matter if they were from two different animals or from the same animal. Consequently, **both** sciatic nerves are definitely **forbidden** by Torah law, and according to the first *tanna* one incurs eighty lashes for eating them. **And** it **was necessary** to teach that **according to Rabbi Yehuda** one incurs only forty lashes, as will be explained. **Conclude from** this *baraita* that it is **obvious to** Rabbi Yehuda that it is the sciatic nerve of the right thigh that is forbidden by Torah law. The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from** it that this is Rabbi Yehuda's opinion.

This proves, even before analyzing the ruling, that Rabbi Yehuda knows which side is the prohibited one. The scenario *had* to be drawn this way to make it work within his framework.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ, אַמַּאי סוֹפֵג אַרְבָּעִים וְתוּ לָא? לִילְקֵי שְׁמוֹנִים! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן דְּלֵית בּוֹ כְּזַיִת, דְּתַנְיָא: אֲכָלוֹ וְאֵין בּוֹ כְּזַיִת – חַיָּיב, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בּוֹ כְּזַיִת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara seeks to clarify the *baraita*: **But if it** is **obvious to** Rabbi Yehuda that the sciatic nerve from the right thigh is prohibited by Torah law, **why** does he **incur forty** lashes **and nothing more? Let him be flogged eighty** times. The Gemara answers: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where** the volume of one of the sciatic nerves **is not an olive-bulk,** and Rabbi Yehuda holds that in such a case one is not flogged for its consumption. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If **one ate** the entire sciatic nerve **and** its volume **is not an olive-bulk,** he is nevertheless **liable** to be flogged, because he ate a complete, natural unit of forbidden food. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** He is not liable **unless it has** a volume of at least **an olive-bulk.**

Now, if Rabbi Yehuda knows the prohibited side and both of these *gidim* are from that side, why does he give only forty lashes? One might expect eighty. The Gemara answers: the case involves two *gidim* each of which lacks a *kezayit* individually. The *braita* records a debate: the Rabbis hold that eating an entire *gid*, even if it lacks a *kezayit*, constitutes a full transgression — this is the principle of *beriah*, that eating a whole entity creates liability regardless of volume, since the prohibition is against eating *the* sinew, not a quantity of it. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: one is only liable if a *kezayit* is present. In this scenario, each *gid* lacks a *kezayit*, but together they contain a *kezayit*. According to the Rabbis, eating two whole *gidim* is two transgressions — eighty lashes. According to Rabbi Yehuda, since neither individually contained a *kezayit*, there is effectively only one combined transgression — forty lashes. The debate about right and left is deliberately bracketed, and what is isolated here is the separate dispute about the quantity required.

We have now established that Rabbi Yehuda knows which side is the prohibited one. The remaining question is: which side, and why?

### Rabah: Textual Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה: אָמַר קְרָא ״הַיָּרֵךְ״ – הַמְיוּמֶּנֶת שֶׁבַּיָּרֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** that Rabbi Yehuda holds that only the sciatic nerve of the right thigh is forbidden by Torah law? **Rava said** that it is because the **verse states:** "Therefore the children of Israel eat not the sciatic nerve that is upon the spoon of **the thigh"** (Genesis 32:33). The definite article indicates that this is referring to **the most important thigh.**

Rabah derives it from the verse itself. The Torah uses the phrase *asher al kaf hayarech* — that which is on the *kaf* of the thigh — with the definite article ה (*hei*): *ha-yarech*, *the* thigh, not simply *a* thigh. The use of the definite article singles out one particular thigh, and the assumption is that we attribute primacy to the right side, which is called *hamyumenet shebaYarech* — the more important or distinguished of the thighs. Thus the right *gid* is the prohibited one.

### Rabbis' Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הַהוּא דְּפָשֵׁיט אִיסּוּרֵיהּ בְּכוּלֵּיהּ יָרֵךְ, לְאַפּוֹקֵי חִיצוֹן דְּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis,** who hold that the sciatic nerves of both the right and left thighs are forbidden by Torah law, explain the definite article as teaching that the prohibition applies to **the one whose prohibition spreads through the entire thigh,** i.e., the inner nerve. This serves **to exclude** the **outer** nerve, **which is not** prohibited by Torah law.

The Rabbis, who hold that both thighs are prohibited, explain the definite article differently. They read *ha-yarech* not as singling out one side but as identifying which of the two sinews within each thigh is the biblical *gid hanasheh* — namely, the one that extends (*pashit*) across the full length of the thigh, *lihafukei chitzon d'lo* — to the exclusion of the outer sinew that does not traverse the entire thigh. Thus the verse is describing the nature of the sinew rather than specifying a particular side.

### Wrestling Narrative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמַר: אָמַר קְרָא ״בְּהֵאָבְקוֹ עִמּוֹ״ – כְּאָדָם שֶׁחוֹבֵק אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ, וְיָדוֹ מַגַּעַת לְכַף יְמִינוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said** that Rabbi Yehuda holds that the sciatic nerve of the right thigh is forbidden because **the verse states:** "And when he saw that he could not prevail against him, he touched the spoon of his thigh; and the spoon of Jacob's thigh was strained, **as he wrestled with him"** (Genesis 32:26). The angel grappled with Jacob **like a man who hugs another** in order to throw him to the ground, **and his hand reaches to the spoon of the right** thigh **of the other.**

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi offers a different proof for the right side, deriving it directly from the narrative of the wrestling match. The Torah says *behe'avko imo* — in his grappling with him. The word suggests, in the manner of one who embraces another (*chovek*), that the angel was grasping Jacob from behind, with his arm going around Jacob's right side. As Rashi explains: when you reach your arm around someone in a wrestling grip, your right arm reaches your opponent's right side. Therefore, the *gid* that was displaced was on Jacob's right thigh. The fact that the wrestling match lasted the entire night and the displacement apparently occurred toward its conclusion makes the reconstruction of their physical positions somewhat speculative, but that is the reasoning the Gemara presents.

### Non-Jew Narrative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: כְּגוֹי נִדְמָה לוֹ, דְּאָמַר מָר: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנִּטְפַּל לוֹ גּוֹי בַּדֶּרֶךְ – טוֹפְלוֹ לִימִינוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says:** The angel **appeared to him as a gentile, as the Master said: A Jew who is joined by a gentile on the road** and continues his travels with him should **position** the gentile **to his right,** close to one's dominant hand. This allows the Jew to defend himself against any potential attack. Since Jacob followed this practice, it was therefore Jacob's right thigh that the angel touched.

Rabbi Yishmael bar Nachmani offers another approach: *ke'oved kochavim nidmeh lo* — the angel appeared to Jacob in the guise of a non-Jew, perhaps representing Esav. There is a ruling from elsewhere (*Avodah Zarah*) that if a Jew is walking with a non-Jew on the road, he should position the non-Jew on his right — *toflo leyamino* — so that the non-Jew cannot easily draw a sword with his dominant right hand and strike. This was a precaution given the real dangers of life under Roman occupation. Since the angel appeared on Jacob's right, he had access to Jacob's right thigh — which is why the right *gid* was affected.

This is a striking derivation: we are determining which *gid* was displaced by reconstructing how two people would have stood at the outset of the encounter, even though the actual displacement occurred at the very end of a night-long fight.

### Torah Sage Narrative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר אַחָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא בַּר עוּלָּא אָמַר: כְּתַלְמִיד חָכָם נִדְמָה לוֹ, דְּאָמַר מָר: הַמְהַלֵּךְ לִימִין רַבּוֹ – הֲרֵי זֶה בּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shmuel bar Aḥa said before Rav Pappa in the name of Rava bar Ulla** that the angel **appeared to** Jacob **as a Torah scholar,** and therefore Jacob positioned the angel on his right side, **as the Master said: One who walks to the right of his teacher is an ignoramus,** in that he does not know how to act with good manners. Consequently, it was Jacob's right thigh that the angel touched.

An alternative tradition: Rav Shmuel bar Acha in the name of Rava bar Ulla says the angel appeared to Jacob *ketalmid chacham nidmeh lo* — as a Torah sage. There is a principle that one who walks to the right of his teacher is an *am ha'aretz*, a boor. Out of deference, Jacob would have positioned himself to the angel's left, placing the angel on the right. This again placed Jacob's right thigh within reach of the angel.

Once more, as with the previous account, this describes the initial positioning and does not easily explain what occurred at the climactic moment of the fight — but these are the Torah sage's terms of analysis.

### Both Sides Moved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, מֵאֲחוֹרֵיהּ אֲתָא, וְנַשְּׁיֵיהּ בְּתַרְוַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis,** who hold that the sciatic nerves of both thighs are forbidden, understand that the angel **came from behind** Jacob **and hit him on both** thighs.

The Rabbis, who hold that both thighs are prohibited, explain the narrative differently: *me'achorei ata ve'nashyei* — the angel came from behind Jacob and attacked from the rear, displacing both *gidim* simultaneously. *Ve'nashyei betarvaihu* — he caused both sides to move. This is what underlies the Rabbis' ruling that both thighs are prohibited.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הַאי ״בְּהֵאָבְקוֹ עִמּוֹ״ מַאי דָּרְשִׁי בֵּיהּ? מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לִכְאִידַּךְ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהֶעֱלוּ אֲבַק מַרְגְּלוֹתָם עַד כִּסֵּא הַכָּבוֹד, כְּתִיב הָכָא ״בְּהֵאָבְקוֹ עִמּוֹ״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם ״וְעָנָן אֲבַק רַגְלָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the Rabbis, what do they derive** from **this** phrase: **"As he wrestled with him"** (Genesis 32:26)? The Gemara answers: They **require it for the other** interpretation **of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said:** This **teaches that the dust [*avak*] from their feet ascended to the throne of glory. It is written here: "As he wrestled [*behe'avko*] with him," and it is written there** in a description of how God will punish the wicked: "The Lord, in the whirlwind and in the storm is His way, **and the clouds are the dust of His feet"** (Nahum 1:3).

The Gemara then asks: what do the Rabbis do with the verse *behe'avko imo*, which Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi used to derive the right side? They use it differently: *melamed she'alu avak margelotam ad kisei ha'kavod* — it teaches that the dust (*avak*) raised by their feet during the struggle rose all the way to the heavenly throne. The verse is linked to another verse describing God's throne, *ve'anan avak raglav* — a cloud of dust beneath His feet. For the Rabbis, the word *be'he'avko* relates not to grasping but to the cloud of dust, signaling the cosmic significance of the struggle. Jacob and Esav, in the broader rabbinic framework, represent the perennial religious and historical confrontation between Israel and Rome — or later, Christianity — and perhaps the image of dust rising to the heavenly throne captures the eternal, transcendent nature of that struggle.

## Etymology & Sources

### Nasheh: Moved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: לָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ גִּיד הַנָּשֶׁה? שֶׁנָּשָׁה מִמְּקוֹמוֹ וְעָלָה, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״נָשְׁתָה גְבוּרָתָם הָיוּ לְנָשִׁים״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites another statement of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi concerning the sciatic nerve: **And Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: Why is its name called sciatic nerve [*gid hanashe*]?** It is **because** the sciatic nerve **left [*nasha*] its place and rose. And similarly** the verse **says:** "The mighty men of Babylon have ceased to fight, they remain in their strongholds; **their might has left [*nashata*], they are become as women"** (Jeremiah 51:30).

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi asks: why is the sinew called the *gid hanasheh*? What does the word *nasheh* mean? He answers: *shenasha mimekomo* — it moved from its place, it was displaced. The word *nasheh* denotes removal or movement, as evidenced by a verse in another context: *nashta gevuratam hayu lenashim* — their strength has been removed, they became like women. The name *gid hanasheh*, then, memorializes the fact that the sinew was dislodged in the course of Jacob's struggle.

### Jacob's Legacy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״דָּבָר שָׁלַח בְּיַעֲקֹב וְנָפַל בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל״? ״דָּבָר שָׁלַח בְּיַעֲקֹב״ – זֶה גִּיד הַנָּשֶׁה, ״וְנָפַל בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל״ – שֶׁפָּשַׁט אִיסּוּרוֹ בְּכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues with other expositions pertaining to the sciatic nerve. **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said: What** is the meaning of that **which is written:** "The Lord **sent a word to Jacob, and it has fallen upon Israel"** (Isaiah 9:7)? **"He sent a word to Jacob"; this** is a reference to **the sciatic nerve. "And it has fallen upon Israel";** this teaches **that its prohibition has** been **extended to the entire Jewish people.**

Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Chanina connects the *gid hanasheh* to a verse in Yeshayahu: *davar shalach Adonai beYaakov venafal beYisrael* — a matter God sent through Jacob, and it fell among the Jewish people. The *davar* — the matter or thing — sent through Jacob is the *gid hanasheh*, the only prohibition that we have specifically inherited through Jacob, apart from *davening maariv* according to another Gemara. The second half of the verse, *venafal beYisrael*, describes how this practice spread and became universal among the entire Jewish people, originating with Jacob himself but becoming the obligation of all Israel.

### Joseph & Brothers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״וּטְבוֹחַ טֶבַח וְהָכֵן״? פְּרַע לָהֶן בֵּית הַשְּׁחִיטָה, ״וְהָכֵן״ – טוֹל גִּיד הַנָּשֶׁה בִּפְנֵיהֶם, כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר גִּיד הַנָּשֶׁה נֶאֱסָר לִבְנֵי נֹחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina,** also **said: What** is the meaning of that **which is written:** "And when Joseph saw Benjamin with them, he said to the steward of his house: Bring the men into the house, **and slaughter the animals, and prepare** the meat; for the men shall dine with me at noon" (Genesis 43:16)? Joseph commanded his steward: **Expose the place of the slaughter** on the neck of the animal **to them** so that the brothers will know that it is being slaughtered correctly. **"And prepare"** teaches that Joseph instructed the steward to **remove the sciatic nerve in their presence** so that the brothers would know that it had been fully removed. The Gemara comments that this opinion is **according to the one who said** that **the sciatic nerve was forbidden to** the children of Jacob even before the Torah was given, when they still had the status of **descendants of Noah.**

Rav Yosef bar Rabbi Chanina expounds another verse: when Joseph instructed his household manager to slaughter an animal and prepare the meal for his brothers, the verse says *tevo'ach tevach ve-hachen* — slaughter and prepare. The *drash* reads *para lahen beit ha-shechita* — expose to them the place of slaughtering so they can inspect and confirm the *shechita* was performed properly. And *ve-hachen* — prepare the meat: *tol gid hanasheh bifneihem* — remove the *gid hanasheh* in their presence. Why specifically in their presence? Because, as we learned in the first *mishna*, one cannot simply trust a butcher to remove the *gid hanasheh*; it must be done visibly. This reading fits with the opinion that the sons of Jacob were already obligated in the prohibition of *gid hanasheh* — *gid hanasheh ne'esar livnei Noach* — meaning not all of humanity, but specifically Jacob's sons, before they formally became the Jewish people at *Matan Torah*.

One could ask how it helps that the slaughter was performed by Egyptians, since *shechitat nochri* is generally invalid. Perhaps the answer is that since everyone present — slaughterer and eaters alike — was at that historical moment a non-Jew in terms of formal *halachic* status, the considerations differ. In any case, the picture conjured by this *drash* is extraordinary: Joseph, concealing his identity, nevertheless ensuring that his brothers can verify every aspect of the *kashrut* of the meal he sets before them.

## Jacob's Righteousness

### Small Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּוָּתֵר יַעֲקֹב לְבַדּוֹ״, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּיֵּיר עַל פַּכִּין קְטַנִּים, מִכָּאן לַצַּדִּיקִים שֶׁחָבִיב עֲלֵיהֶם מָמוֹנָם יוֹתֵר מִגּוּפָם, וְכׇל כָּךְ לָמָּה? לְפִי שֶׁאֵין פּוֹשְׁטִין יְדֵיהֶן בְּגָזֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the verse of Jacob wrestling with the angel. The verse states: **"And Jacob was left alone;** and a man wrestled with him until the breaking of the day" (Genesis 32:25). **Rabbi Elazar says:** The reason Jacob remained alone was **that he remained** to collect some **small pitchers** that had been left behind. **From here** it is derived **that the possessions of the righteous are dearer to them than their bodies. And why** do they care **so much** about their possessions? It is **because they do not stretch out their hands to** partake of **stolen property.**

The Gemara now turns to a series of *aggadic* passages about Jacob, spending the remainder of the daf on the narrative surrounding his wrestling match with the angel. The Torah says *vayivater Yaakov levado* — Jacob was left alone. Why was he alone? Rabbi Elazar teaches: *she-nishtayer al pachim ketanim* — he had gone back to retrieve some small, inexpensive pottery vessels that had been forgotten when the household crossed. The *chiddush* is formulated thus: *mikan le-tzaddikim she-chaviv aleihem mamonam yoter mi-gufam* — from here we learn that the righteous care for their property more than their own bodies, in that Jacob was willing to remain alone and exposed to danger in order to retrieve some trivial vessels. Why such care over small things? *She-ein poshtim yedeihem be-gezel* — because they never reach out their hands to take what is not theirs. Those who respect property — their own and others' — treat every object with conscientiousness. The person who says "it's only a little thing, leave it behind" may not be the same person who is careful about the line between his and another's.

This is worth pausing over, because there is a conventional reading of the verse *u-vchol me'odecha* — with all your possessions — that treats excessive attachment to property as somehow secondary to other virtues. Yet here, the *tzaddik*'s care for even small possessions is presented as a mark of his character, not a failing.

### Not Alone at Night

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֵּאָבֵק אִישׁ עִמּוֹ עַד עֲלוֹת הַשָּׁחַר״, אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִכָּאן לְתַלְמִיד חָכָם שֶׁלֹּא יֵצֵא יְחִידִי בַּלַּיְלָה. רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר כָּהֲנָא אָמַר מֵהָכָא:
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And a man wrestled with him until the breaking of the day." Rabbi Yitzḥak says: From here** it is derived **that a Torah scholar should not go out** of his house **alone at night,** as Jacob went out alone at night and was injured. **Rabbi Abba bar Kahana said** that the source for this instruction is **from here:**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הִנֵּה הוּא זֹרֶה אֶת גֹּרֶן הַשְּׂעוֹרִים״.
>
> **Translation:** "And now is there not Boaz our kinsman, whose maidens you were with? **Behold, he winnows barley tonight in the threshing floor**…and it shall be, when he lies down, that you shall mark the place where he shall lie" (Ruth 3:2–3). This teaches that the reason Boaz did not return home from the threshing floor was that a Torah scholar should not go out alone at night.

Rabbi Abba bar Kahana derives from the same episode — Jacob left alone as the angel arrived — that a Torah sage should not go out alone at night. *Tosafot* notes that there is also the concern of *mazikim*, harmful spiritual forces that attack Torah sages in particular; in any event, night carries greater danger, whether from people or from supernatural forces, and the angel's insistence on leaving at daybreak is itself a sign that the night domain operates differently. Three additional proof-texts are adduced: Naomi knew Boaz would remain in the field overnight rather than walk home alone; Abraham rose early in the morning before setting out; Jacob's brothers were sent to check on the flocks during daylight; and the verse *vayizrach lo ha-shemesh* — the sun shone upon him — tells us that the fight with the angel only ended at sunrise, meaning the entire episode was a nighttime event.

### Folded Geography

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּד צַלִּי, בָּעֵי לְמֶיהְדַּר, אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: צַדִּיק זֶה בָּא לְבֵית מְלוֹנִי, וְיִפָּטֵר בְּלֹא לִינָה? מִיָּד, ״בָּא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **When he** had finished **praying** and **he wanted to return** to Haran, **the Holy One, Blessed be He, said: This righteous man came to my lodging place and he will depart without remaining overnight? Immediately, the sun set** before its proper time so that Jacob would stay overnight in that place.

There is a narrative about how Jacob came to sleep at this particular place. The Torah says *vayeitzei Yaakov mi-Beer Sheva vayelech Charana* — Jacob left Beer Sheva and went toward Charan — and then says *vayifga ba-makom* — he encountered the place. The word *vayifga* sounds like an unexpected encounter. The Gemara explains: Jacob actually did arrive in Charan, and then realized he had passed by the place where his forefathers had prayed — identified with *Har ha-Bayit* in Jerusalem, since the location of the ladder (*sha'ar ha-shamayim*, the gateway of heaven) is understood by the Rabbis to be none other than the Temple Mount — and had not stopped to pray there. He said: *efshar avarti al makom she-hitpalelu avotai va-ani lo* — how could I have passed the very place where my fathers prayed and not done so myself? He turned to go back, and *kafetzah lei are'a* — the land leaped up to meet him. He encountered the place through *kefitzat haderech*, the miraculous folding of distance, and was suddenly back at the holy site. The word *vayifga* thus carries both the meaning of an unexpected encounter and, as the Rabbis elsewhere note, the meaning of prayer.

The apparent contradiction between the narrative happening both at *Beit El Luz* and at Jerusalem is resolved by this same folding: when Jacob turned back toward Luz, the land of Israel folded beneath him so that *Har ha-Bayit* came to meet him at Luz. Geography itself became fluid. This also connects thematically to what will come later on the daf — the land folded under Jacob as a promise that it would be easy to conquer.

### Sun Set & Rise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב ״וַיִּקַּח מֵאַבְנֵי הַמָּקוֹם״, וּכְתִיב ״וַיִּקַּח אֶת הָאֶבֶן״. אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנִּתְקַבְּצוּ כׇּל אוֹתָן אֲבָנִים לְמָקוֹם אֶחָד, וְכׇל אַחַת וְאַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת: ״עָלַי יַנִּיחַ צַדִּיק זֶה רֹאשׁוֹ״. תָּנָא: וְכוּלָּן נִבְלְעוּ בְּאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another exposition of Rabbi Yitzḥak to explain an apparent contradiction between two verses pertaining to this incident. **It is written: "And he took of the stones of the place,** and placed them under his head, and lay down in that place to sleep" (Genesis 28:11). **And it is written:** "And Jacob rose up early in the morning, **and he took the stone** that he had placed under his head, and set it up for a pillar, and poured oil upon the top of it" (Genesis 28:18). The first verse indicates that Jacob took several stones, whereas the latter verse indicates that he took only one stone. **Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** This **teaches that all those stones gathered to one place and each one said:** Let **this righteous man place his head upon me.** And it was **taught: And all of them were absorbed into one** large rock.

Once Jacob prayed at the holy site, he wanted to leave and continue to Charan. But God said: *tzaddik zeh ba le-veit meloni ve-yipatr be-lo linah* — this righteous person came to My inn, My resting place, and he would depart without spending the night? So God caused the sun to set early, compelling Jacob to stay and sleep. This is the meaning of *ki va ha-shemesh* — the sun came, it set unexpectedly — creating the conditions for the dream of the ladder.

Now, back to the verse *vayizrach lo ha-shemesh* at the end of the fight: Rabbi Yitzchak explains that the sun which had set early for Jacob now rose early for Jacob — *shemesh ha-ba'ah ba'avuro zarcha ba'avuro*, the sun that had been summoned to set on his account now rose on his account. Having borrowed time from him earlier, it now repaid the debt. The sunrise ended the wrestling match. These two episodes — the sun setting early at Beit El and rising early after Penuel — form a pair, and God is the author of both.

A question arises: if Jacob arrived in Charan before setting out back toward Jerusalem, how does this interact with the tradition that Jacob *instituted* *davening maariv*, the evening prayer? The verse *ki va ha-shemesh* is cited in another context as the source for *maariv*. If the sun set miraculously early and Jacob prayed, does that constitute the institution of *maariv*? The *pashut peshat* of the Torah never mentions prayer here; the tradition of prayer is from the *midrash*, and the full details of the interplay between these traditions remain for another discussion.

### Gathering Stones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיַּחֲלֹם וְהִנֵּה סֻלָּם מֻצָּב אַרְצָה״, תָּנָא: כַּמָּה רׇחְבּוֹ שֶׁל סוּלָּם? שְׁמוֹנַת אֲלָפִים פַּרְסָאוֹת, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהִנֵּה מַלְאֲכֵי אֱלֹהִים עוֹלִים וְיוֹרְדִים בּוֹ״, ״עוֹלִים״ – שְׁנַיִם, ״וְיוֹרְדִים״ – שְׁנַיִם, וְכִי פָגְעוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי הָווּ לְהוּ אַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expounds other verses pertaining to the same incident. The verse states: **"And he dreamed, and behold a ladder set up on the earth,** and the top of it reached to heaven; and behold the angels of God ascending and descending on it" (Genesis 28:12). It was **taught: How wide was the ladder?** It was **eight thousand parasangs [*parsaot*], as it is written: "And behold the angels of God ascending and descending on it."** The word **"ascending [*olim*],"** written in plural, indicates that there were **two** angels ascending simultaneously. Likewise, the term **"and descending [*veyordim*],"** also in the plural, indicates that **two** angels were descending simultaneously. **And when they met one another they were** a total of **four** in one place, so the ladder must have been wide enough to accommodate four angels.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ בְּמַלְאָךְ ״וּגְוִיָּתוֹ כְתַרְשִׁישׁ״, וּגְמִירִי דְּתַרְשִׁישׁ תְּרֵי אַלְפֵי פַּרְסֵי הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is written** in a verse **with regard to an angel: "His body was like Tarshish"** (Daniel 10:6). **And** it **is learned** as a tradition **that** the city of **Tarshish was two thousand parasangs.** Consequently, in order to accommodate four angels, the ladder must have been eight thousands parasangs wide.

## Ladder & Throne

### Ladder Width

The Gemara asks: how wide was the ladder? *Shemonat alafim parsa'os* — eight thousand parsangs. The calculation proceeds as follows. The Torah says *malachei Elokim olim ve-yordim bo* — angels of God were ascending and descending upon it. *Olim* is plural: at least two angels were ascending. *Yordim* is plural: at least two were descending. Since these were different angels going up and coming down, and since they would have to pass each other at some point, at the moment of passing there would be four angels on the ladder simultaneously. And from another verse, a single angel's physical span is described as *k'Tarshish* — like Tarshish — and it is received tradition that Tarshish is 2,000 parsangs wide. Four angels at 2,000 parsangs each gives 8,000 parsangs.

### Jacob's Heavenly Image

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: עוֹלִין וּמִסְתַּכְּלִין בִּדְיוֹקְנוֹ שֶׁל מַעְלָה, וְיוֹרְדִין וּמִסְתַּכְּלִין בִּדְיוֹקְנוֹ שֶׁל מַטָּה. בְּעוֹ לְסַכּוֹנֵיהּ, מִיָּד ״וְהִנֵּה ה׳ נִצָּב עָלָיו״. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: אִלְמָלֵא מִקְרָא כָּתוּב אִי אֶפְשָׁר לְאׇמְרוֹ, כְּאָדָם שֶׁמֵּנִיף עַל בְּנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It was **taught** that the angels were **ascending and gazing at the image of [*bidyokeno*]** Jacob **above,** engraved on the Throne of Glory, **and descending and gazing at his image below.** The angels subsequently became jealous of Jacob, and **wanted to endanger** his life. **Immediately** Jacob received divine protection, as the verse states: **"And behold, the Lord stood over him"** (Genesis 28:13). **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says: Were it not written** in a **verse it would be impossible to utter it,** in deference to God, since it describes God as standing over Jacob to protect him from the angels **like a man who waves** a fan **over his son** to cool him down.

The angels would ascend the ladder and look at the image above — the *diyukan shel ma'alah* — the image on the heavenly throne. In rabbinic tradition, the face of the man among the four faces on the divine throne is the face of Jacob. The word *diyukan* derives from the Greek for icon, meaning image or likeness. Then the angels would descend and see Jacob sleeping below — *ve'yordim u-mistaklin be-diyukno shel mata* — and recognize that it was the same face. Realizing that a human being bore the image inscribed on the heavenly throne, they were seized with jealousy and wanted to harm Jacob — *ba'u le-sak'nah* — to injure him.

Immediately, *ve-hinei Hashem nitzav alav* — God stood over Jacob. Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish notes: *ilmalei mikra kasuv iy efshar le'omro* — were it not written in the verse, it would be impossible to say such a thing. What does it mean for God to stand over someone protectively? *Ke-adam she-meif al bino* — like a person fanning over his child. The image is of a parent standing over a sleeping child, fanning away insects or the heat, hovering in attentive care. The *Maharsha* suggests it could specifically mean fanning away flies. The point is not the specific motion but the intimacy of the care: had the verse not written it, such language applied to God would be unthinkable.

### Land Folded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הָאָרֶץ אֲשֶׁר אַתָּה שֹׁכֵב עָלֶיהָ וְגוֹ׳״, מַאי רְבוּתֵיהּ? אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁקִּפְּלָהּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְכׇל אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל וְהִנִּיחָה תַּחַת יַעֲקֹב אָבִינוּ, שֶׁתְּהֵא נוֹחָה לִיכָּבֵשׁ לְבָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains another verse from Jacob's dream. "And behold, the Lord stood over him and said: I am the Lord, the God of Abraham your father and the God of Isaac. **The land upon which you lie,** to you will I give it, and to your seed" (Genesis 28:13). The Gemara asks: **What is the greatness** of this promise, i.e., why is it expressed in this way despite the fact that in a literal sense Jacob was lying on a very small amount of land? **Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** This **teaches that the Holy One, Blessed be He, folded** up **the entirety of Eretz Yisrael and placed it under Jacob, our patriarch, so that it would be easy for his children to conquer.**

*Ha-aretz asher atah shochev aleha* — the land upon which you are sleeping, I will give to you. The plain meaning seems to promise only the small plot on which Jacob lay. But Rabbi Yitzchak teaches: *melamed she-kipel Hakadosh Baruch Hu kol Eretz Yisrael ve-hinichah tachat Yaakov Avinu* — God folded the entire land of Israel and placed it beneath Jacob, *she-tinoach le-hikaivesh le-vanav* — to show that it would be easily conquered by his descendants. The promise was not merely of territory; the folding of the land beneath him was a tangible sign that the conquest would be, in some sense, as natural as sleeping upon it.

## Angel's Turn to Sing

### Final Opportunity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֹּאמֶר שַׁלְּחֵנִי כִּי עָלָה הַשָּׁחַר״, אָמַר לוֹ: גַּנָּב אַתָּה אוֹ קוּבְיוּסְטוּס אַתָּה שֶׁמִּתְיָירֵא מִן הַשַּׁחַר? אָמַר לוֹ: מַלְאָךְ אֲנִי, וּמִיּוֹם שֶׁנִּבְרֵאתִי לֹא הִגִּיעַ זְמַנִּי לוֹמַר שִׁירָה עַד עַכְשָׁיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the verses that describe Jacob wrestling with the angel. **"And he said: Let me go, for the dawn has risen.** And he said: I will not let you go until you bless me" (Genesis 32:27). Jacob **said to** the angel: **Are you a thief, or are you a gambler [*kuveyustus*], who is afraid of dawn?** The angel **said to him: I am an angel, and from the day I was created my time to recite a song** before God **has not arrived, until now.** Now I must ascend so that I can sing songs of praise to God.

Returning to the conclusion of the wrestling match: the angel says to Jacob *shalcheni ki alah ha-shachar* — send me free, for the dawn is rising. Jacob responds with a challenge: what kind of creature are you, afraid of daylight? You must be a burglar or — and here *Tosafot* in another tractate notes — a card player, someone who does his questionable work under cover of night. The angel replies: *malach ani* — I am an angel — and explains his urgency: *u-miyom she-nivreiti lo higi'a zemani lomar shirah ad achshav* — from the day I was created, I had never yet had my turn to sing praise to God until this very moment. He must go now because his opportunity has finally arrived.

### Angels' Rotations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַב חֲנַנְאֵל אָמַר רַב, דְּאָמַר רַב חֲנַנְאֵל אָמַר רַב: שָׁלֹשׁ כִּתּוֹת שֶׁל מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת אוֹמְרוֹת שִׁירָה בְּכׇל יוֹם, אַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת ״קָדוֹשׁ״, וְאַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת ״קָדוֹשׁ״, וְאַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת ״קָדוֹשׁ ה׳ צְבָאוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: This **supports** the opinion **of Rav Ḥananel** when he related what **Rav said. As Rav Ḥananel said** that **Rav said: Three groups of ministering angels recite a song every day** from the verse "Holy, holy, holy is the Lord"; **one says: "Holy," and** another **one says: "Holy," and** another **one says: "Holy is the Lord of hosts;** the whole earth is full of His glory" (Isaiah 6:3).

This statement is supported by Rav Chanana citing Rav: *shalosh kitot shel malachei ha-shareit omrot shirah bechol yom* — three groups of heavenly angels take turns singing praise every day, constantly rotating. Each group gets its moment. The angel could not delay; it was finally his turn after an eternity of waiting.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: חֲבִיבִין יִשְׂרָאֵל לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא יוֹתֵר מִמַּלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת, שֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל אוֹמְרִים שִׁירָה בְּכׇל שָׁעָה, וּמַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת אֵין אוֹמְרִים שִׁירָה אֶלָּא פַּעַם אַחַת בַּיּוֹם, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ פַּעַם אַחַת בְּשַׁבָּת, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ פַּעַם אַחַת בְּחוֹדֶשׁ, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ פַּעַם אַחַת בַּשָּׁנָה, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ פַּעַם אַחַת בְּשָׁבוּעַ, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ פַּעַם אַחַת בְּיוֹבֵל, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ פַּעַם אַחַת בָּעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from the following *baraita*: **The Jewish people are more dear to the Holy One, Blessed be He, than the ministering angels, as the Jewish people** may **recite a song** of praise to God **at any time, but ministering angels recite a song** of praise **only one time per day. And some say** that the ministering angels recite a song of praise **one time per week. And some say** that they recite a song of praise **one time per month. And some say** that they recite a song of praise **one time per year. And some say** that they recite a song of praise **one time in** every **seven years. And some say** that they recite a song of praise **one time per Jubilee. And some say** that they recite a song of praise **one time in** the entire history of **the world.**

Israel, by contrast, is told: *Yisrael omrim shirah bechol sha'ah* — Israel may sing to God at any hour. We pray three times a day; we are not confined to a single assigned moment. How rare is the angel's opportunity? The Gemara offers a range of opinions: once a day, once a week, once a month, once a year, once every seven years, once every fifty years, and — most dramatically — once in an entire cosmic lifetime, *pa'am achat be-olam*. This last view fits the angel's urgency best: he has waited since creation itself for this single moment of *shirah*, and he cannot miss it.

The most natural reading of these opinions is that each *individual* angel receives an opportunity at one of these intervals — not that *all* angels collectively sing only that rarely, which would be a peculiar way to describe a theology of ongoing angelic praise. The theme reinforces the contrast: any individual Jew can praise God at any moment, whereas any individual angel must wait for his assigned turn across vast stretches of time.

### Israel vs. Angels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיִשְׂרָאֵל מַזְכִּירִין אֶת הַשֵּׁם אַחַר שְׁתֵּי תֵּיבוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל ה׳ וְגוֹ׳״, וּמַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת אֵין מַזְכִּירִין אֶת הַשֵּׁם אֶלָּא לְאַחַר שָׁלֹשׁ תֵּיבוֹת, כְּדִכְתִיב: ״קָדוֹשׁ קָדוֹשׁ קָדוֹשׁ ה׳ צְבָאוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** furthermore, **the Jewish people mention the name** of God **after two words, as it is stated: "Hear, Israel: The Lord** our God, the Lord is one" (Deuteronomy 6:4). **But the ministering angels mention the name** of God **only after three words, as it is written:** "And one called unto another, and said: **"Holy, holy, holy is the Lord of hosts;** the whole earth is full of His glory" (Isaiah 6:3).

Beyond the frequency of *shirah*, there is another dimension in which Israel surpasses the angels. *Ve-Yisrael mazkirin et Hashem achar shtei teivot* — Israel mentions the name of God after two words: *Shema Yisrael — Hashem*. The angels, by contrast, *ein mazkirin et Hashem ela achar gimel teivot* — do not mention God's name until after three words: *Kadosh Kadosh Kadosh — Hashem Tzeva'ot*. Moreover, Israel does not even begin its *shirah* above until Israel has first sung from below: *be-ron yachad kochvei voker* — with the rejoicing of the morning stars (understood as Israel), and only then *va-yari'u kol bnei Elohim* — do all the heavenly beings shout out. The angels wait for Israel to lead.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת אוֹמְרִים שִׁירָה לְמַעְלָה, עַד שֶׁיֹּאמְרוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל לְמַטָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּרׇן יַחַד כּוֹכְבֵי בֹקֶר״, וַהֲדַר ״וַיָּרִיעוּ כׇּל בְּנֵי אֱלֹהִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **And the ministering angels do not recite** their **song above until the Jewish people recite** their song **below,** on earth, **as it is stated: "When the morning stars sang together"** (Job 38:7), referring to the Jewish people, who are compared to stars; **and** only **then** does the verse state: **"And all the sons of God shouted for joy,"** which is a reference to the angels. This *baraita* teaches that the angels mention the name of God only after three words, i.e., after saying the word "holy" three times, whereas according to what Rav Ḥananel stated that Rav said, the third group of angels says the word "holy" once and then immediately mentions the name of God.

### Three Kadoshes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת ״קָדוֹשׁ״, וְאַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת ״קָדוֹשׁ קָדוֹשׁ״, וְאַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת ״קָדוֹשׁ קָדוֹשׁ קָדוֹשׁ ה׳ צְבָאוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara emends Rav Ḥananel's statement citing Rav: **Rather,** Rav said that **one** group of ministering angels **says: "Holy," and** another **one says: "Holy, holy," and** another **one says: "Holy, holy, holy is the Lord of hosts."** The Gemara challenges the statement of the *baraita* that the angels mention the name of God only after three words: **But there is** the verse: "Then a spirit lifted me up, and I heard behind me the voice of a great rushing: **Blessed be** the glory of the Lord from His place" (Ezekiel 3:12). In this praise, "Blessed be the glory of the Lord," the word "Lord" appears as the third Hebrew word, apparently uttered by the ministering angels.

This last point, however, creates an apparent difficulty. The Gemara described three groups of angels: one says *kadosh*, one says *kadosh*, and one says *Kadosh Hashem Tzeva'ot*. The third group mentions God's name after only one word — not three. How is this consistent with the rule that angels mention God's name only after three words?

The Gemara resolves this: the division into three groups does not mean each group says one *kadosh* independently. Rather, the groups build upon one another: the first group says *kadosh*, the second says *kadosh kadosh*, and the third — having inherited what the previous two said — says *kadosh kadosh kadosh Hashem Tzeva'ot*, completing the full proclamation. The third group thus pronounces all three *kadoshes* and only then reaches the name of God. This preserves the rule: even the last group of angels says three words before mentioning God's name. Israel, reciting *Shema Yisrael Hashem*, mentions God's name after just two words — and in that small asymmetry, the Gemara finds the superiority of Israel over the angels.
