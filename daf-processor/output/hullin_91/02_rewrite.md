# Hullin 91 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Gid Hanasheh: Both or On…

### Mishna vs. Rabbi Yehuda

We begin on 91b, picking up the discussion of whether the *gid hanasheh* applies to both thighs or only one. Our mishna rules that it applies to both, and this is the normative *halacha*. The Gemara opens by noting that our mishna does not follow Rabbi Yehuda, as a *braita* teaches: Rabbi Yehuda says the prohibition applies only to one of the thighs, and *hada'at machra'at* — the reasoning inclines, or the Torah's reasoning indicates — that it is the right one. It is worth noting in passing that the phrase *da'at machra'at* is one of the early appearances of the concept later associated with *da'at Torah*, though the meaning here is quite different: it simply means that if one examines the Torah carefully, the evidence points to the right side.

### Certainty vs. Doubt

This leads to the *ibaya*: is it *pshita* to Rabbi Yehuda — does he know with certainty which thigh is prohibited? Or does he merely say *sfukei m'sapka lei* — that he genuinely doubts whether it is the right or the left, and the phrase "the reasoning inclines" means only that logic leans in one direction without definitive evidence? This question has real *halachic* consequences, as we will see.

### Korban Pesach Proof

The Gemara attempts to resolve this from a *braita* concerning the *korban pesach*: the bones, sinews, and any *notar* — leftover meat — must be burned on the sixteenth of Nisan. Burning on the fifteenth is impossible because one may not burn *kodashim* on *yom tov*, even *pasul kodashim*, so one waits until after *yom tov*.

The Gemara then asks: what is the *chiddush* of mentioning the sinews? If they are edible sinews, one should simply eat them — why burn them? And if they happen to be left over, they are already *notar* — the verse has already covered them. The Gemara concludes that the reference must be to inedible sinews, such as those in the neck. But then another problem arises: if they are not meat at all — if they are physically inedible — they carry no obligation of eating in the context of the *korban*, and therefore they are not *notar*. They should simply be discarded; why must they be burned?

### Rav Chisda's Answer

Rav Chisda resolves this: the *chiddush* refers specifically to the *gid hanasheh* according to Rabbi Yehuda. In this case, the sinew is not inedible for physical reasons but because it is halachically prohibited. Now the Gemara from the previous daf is relevant: something prohibited by law does not carry the *mitzvah* of eating within the *korban* context, and therefore it should not be *notar* either — just discard it.

But here the Gemara explains: *if* Rabbi Yehuda holds that there is genuine doubt about which thigh is the *gid hanasheh*, the case becomes clear. You cannot eat either *gid* because one of them is definitively the *gid hanasheh* — though you do not know which one. That is precisely the situation: one is the quarter, so to speak, and the other is the *gid*. You cannot eat the right or the left because one of them is certainly prohibited. But you must also burn them, because the one that is not the *gid hanasheh* was supposed to be eaten, and since you do not know which that is, you must burn both rather than eat either. This creates the unusual scenario of passively transgressing the prohibition of *lo totiru* — leaving meat over from the *korban* until morning — rather than actively transgressing the prohibition of *gid hanasheh*. The principle applies: it is preferable to transgress passively than actively.

This scenario makes sense only if Rabbi Yehuda harbors genuine doubt. If he *knew* which side was prohibited, there would be no difficulty: eat the permitted one, discard the prohibited one. There would be no grounds for burning anything.

### Avika's Resolution

Avika bar Chanina offers a different resolution. Perhaps Rabbi Yehuda does in fact know which side is the *gid hanasheh* — it is definitively the right. The *braita* about burning, however, is dealing with a case where you originally knew which was the right thigh and which was the left, but then they became mixed up. In such a scenario, even if Rabbi Yehuda knows with certainty that it is the right *gid* that is prohibited, since the two *gidim* are now indistinguishable, you cannot eat either — and since one of them was obligated to be eaten, you burn both. This resolution preserves the view that Rabbi Yehuda has no doubt about which side is prohibited, while still explaining why burning is required.

## Why Burn the Gid?

### Rav Ashi: The Fat

We now turn to the top of 91a for two alternative explanations that have nothing to do with Rabbi Yehuda's position. Rav Ashi says: *lo nitzrecha ela leshumno* — the *chiddush* of burning the sinew of the *korban pesach* concerns not the *gid hanasheh* itself but the fat surrounding it. The term used here is *shuman* rather than *chelev*, because the fat around the *gid hanasheh* is not forbidden *chelev*; at most it is rabbinically restricted. A *braita* supports this: *shumno mutar* — the fat around the *gid* is biblically permitted — but *Yisrael kedoshim nahagu bo issur* — because the Jewish people are holy, they have adopted the practice of refraining from it.

The *chiddush* is therefore as follows: you did not eat this fat because of widespread *minhag*, but since the Torah permits it biblically, it legally remains *notar* — meat that was supposed to be eaten — and it must therefore be burned rather than discarded. This case raises a striking observation: a *minhag* is causing a person to passively transgress a biblical prohibition of *notar*. The Gemara itself does not raise this as a difficulty and simply takes for granted that the *minhag* stands and the consequence is burning rather than eating. Nevertheless, the tension is genuinely remarkable, particularly since one could construct a scenario where someone has not yet eaten a *kezayit* of the *korban pesach*, and the only remaining meat is this rabbinically restricted fat — in which case the question of whether to eat it to fulfill the *mitzvah* becomes exceedingly pointed.

### Ravina: Two Sinews

Ravina offers yet another answer. He cites Rabbi Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *shnei gidim hem* — there are actually two sinews running through the thigh. The inner one, *pnimi samuch l'etzem* — close to the thigh bone — is the biblical *gid hanasheh*, and one who eats it is *chayav*, liable for the biblical transgression. The outer one, *samuch l'basar* — closer to the fleshy part of the thigh on its outer side — is only rabbinically prohibited, and one is not biblically liable for eating it.

Looking at a diagram of the thigh's anatomy, this distinction becomes clearer: the inner sinew running alongside the thigh bone for the full length of the thigh is the biblical *gid hanasheh*; the outer sinew — known as the *gid hachitzon* — which runs only partway down is the rabbinic one. According to Ravina, the *braita* about burning the sinew of the *korban pesach* concerns this outer, rabbinic sinew. Since the Torah biblically permits it — one is merely rabbinically forbidden — it remains legally obligated to be eaten and therefore qualifies as *notar*, requiring burning rather than simple disposal. The rabbis said not to eat it, so you did not; but since the Torah says you could have eaten it, it is *notar* and must be burned.

This again raises the question of a *minhag* — or here a rabbinic enactment — causing a passive biblical transgression of *notar*, and even the possibility of someone who has not yet fulfilled his *kezayit* of *korban pesach* being left with only this outer sinew. The Gemara does not address these questions but proceeds to the next stage of the discussion.

## Which Side? Right or Bot…

### Eating Both Thighs

We return to the core question: does Rabbi Yehuda know which side is the prohibited *gid*, or does he not? The Gemara cites another source that was encountered just a few days earlier. If one ate a *kezayit* of the *gid* from the right thigh and a *kezayit* from the left thigh, the Rabbis say he receives eighty lashes — two sets, one for each transgression — assuming he was warned separately for each. Rabbi Yehuda says he receives only forty lashes, one set.

The Gemara now examines the logic. If Rabbi Yehuda knows which side is prohibited — and we will see it is the right — then the ruling is straightforward: you transgressed on the right and receive one set of lashes; you did not transgress on the left and receive no lashes there. That is perfectly coherent.

But if Rabbi Yehuda genuinely doubts which side is prohibited, a different problem arises. How can you receive even one set of lashes? Before eating each *gid*, the warning (*hatra'ah*) would have been doubtful — *hatra'at safek* — because at the moment of warning, you did not know whether this particular act would constitute a transgression. Rabbi Yehuda himself, as established from a *braita*, holds that *hatra'at safek lo shemah hatra'ah* — a doubtful warning does not count as warning. The *braita* discusses a man who does not know which of two men is his father, strikes one and then the other, curses one and then the other. Rabbi Yehuda rules that if done sequentially, he is exempt, because even though by the end we know he has certainly struck his father, before each individual act the warning could not be unequivocal.

This would mean that if Rabbi Yehuda truly doubts which *gid* is prohibited, no lashes could be administered at all — not even forty. The ruling of forty lashes, then, is difficult to explain on the assumption of doubt.

The Gemara resolves this tension: *hai tanna savar lah ke'idach tanna deRabbi Yehuda* — the *tanna* who recorded the ruling of forty lashes holds by a different version of Rabbi Yehuda's position, one that says *hatra'at safek shemah hatra'ah* — even doubtful warning suffices, as long as by the end of the act we know a transgression occurred. The *braita* on *notar* supports this: the verse says do not leave over; if you do, burn it. The Torah gave a positive commandment after the negative, signaling that there are no lashes for the transgression — Rabbi Yehuda's view — because the positive *mitzvah* of burning provides a remedy. Rabbi Yaakov disagrees and says the reason for exemption from lashes is simply that it is a *lav she'ein bo ma'aseh* — a passive transgression, which never carries lashes. Notably, neither gives *hatra'at safek* as his reason, suggesting that in this context Rabbi Yehuda does not object to doubtful warning as grounds to exempt from lashes. Accordingly, the ruling of forty lashes can coexist with Rabbi Yehuda's doubt about which side is prohibited.

### Two Animals Scenario

The Gemara attempts one more proof, and this one is quite elegant. The source states: if one ate two *gidei hanasheh* from two thighs from two different animals — meaning the same *side* from two separate animals — the Rabbis say eighty lashes and Rabbi Yehuda says forty. The Gemara observes: why did the source need to specify two different animals? Why not simply present the scenario of the right and left from the same animal? The only reason to insist on two animals is if it matters which side we are discussing — because if both sides were equally prohibited, any two *gidim* would suffice for the scenario, including right and left from the same animal. The fact that the source took care to specify two animals of the same side demonstrates that this scenario was constructed specifically to satisfy Rabbi Yehuda's position that only one particular side is prohibited, and to ensure that both *gidim* in the scenario are indisputably forbidden even according to him.

This proves, even before analyzing the ruling, that Rabbi Yehuda knows which side is the prohibited one. The scenario *had* to be drawn this way to make it work within his framework.

Now, if Rabbi Yehuda knows the prohibited side and both of these *gidim* are from that side, why does he give only forty lashes? One might expect eighty. The Gemara answers: the case involves two *gidim* each of which lacks a *kezayit* individually. The *braita* records a debate: the Rabbis hold that eating an entire *gid*, even if it lacks a *kezayit*, constitutes a full transgression — this is the principle of *beriah*, that eating a whole entity creates liability regardless of volume, since the prohibition is against eating *the* sinew, not a quantity of it. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: one is only liable if a *kezayit* is present. In this scenario, each *gid* lacks a *kezayit*, but together they contain a *kezayit*. According to the Rabbis, eating two whole *gidim* is two transgressions — eighty lashes. According to Rabbi Yehuda, since neither individually contained a *kezayit*, there is effectively only one combined transgression — forty lashes. The debate about right and left is deliberately bracketed, and what is isolated here is the separate dispute about the quantity required.

We have now established that Rabbi Yehuda knows which side is the prohibited one. The remaining question is: which side, and why?

### Rabah: Textual Proof

Rabah derives it from the verse itself. The Torah uses the phrase *asher al kaf hayarech* — that which is on the *kaf* of the thigh — with the definite article ה (*hei*): *ha-yarech*, *the* thigh, not simply *a* thigh. The use of the definite article singles out one particular thigh, and the assumption is that we attribute primacy to the right side, which is called *hamyumenet shebaYarech* — the more important or distinguished of the thighs. Thus the right *gid* is the prohibited one.

### Rabbis' Explanation

The Rabbis, who hold that both thighs are prohibited, explain the definite article differently. They read *ha-yarech* not as singling out one side but as identifying which of the two sinews within each thigh is the biblical *gid hanasheh* — namely, the one that extends (*pashit*) across the full length of the thigh, *lihafukei chitzon d'lo* — to the exclusion of the outer sinew that does not traverse the entire thigh. Thus the verse is describing the nature of the sinew rather than specifying a particular side.

### Wrestling Narrative

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi offers a different proof for the right side, deriving it directly from the narrative of the wrestling match. The Torah says *behe'avko imo* — in his grappling with him. The word suggests, in the manner of one who embraces another (*chovek*), that the angel was grasping Jacob from behind, with his arm going around Jacob's right side. As Rashi explains: when you reach your arm around someone in a wrestling grip, your right arm reaches your opponent's right side. Therefore, the *gid* that was displaced was on Jacob's right thigh. The fact that the wrestling match lasted the entire night and the displacement apparently occurred toward its conclusion makes the reconstruction of their physical positions somewhat speculative, but that is the reasoning the Gemara presents.

### Non-Jew Narrative

Rabbi Yishmael bar Nachmani offers another approach: *ke'oved kochavim nidmeh lo* — the angel appeared to Jacob in the guise of a non-Jew, perhaps representing Esav. There is a ruling from elsewhere (*Avodah Zarah*) that if a Jew is walking with a non-Jew on the road, he should position the non-Jew on his right — *toflo leyamino* — so that the non-Jew cannot easily draw a sword with his dominant right hand and strike. This was a precaution given the real dangers of life under Roman occupation. Since the angel appeared on Jacob's right, he had access to Jacob's right thigh — which is why the right *gid* was affected.

This is a striking derivation: we are determining which *gid* was displaced by reconstructing how two people would have stood at the outset of the encounter, even though the actual displacement occurred at the very end of a night-long fight.

### Torah Sage Narrative

An alternative tradition: Rav Shmuel bar Acha in the name of Rava bar Ulla says the angel appeared to Jacob *ketalmid chacham nidmeh lo* — as a Torah sage. There is a principle that one who walks to the right of his teacher is an *am ha'aretz*, a boor. Out of deference, Jacob would have positioned himself to the angel's left, placing the angel on the right. This again placed Jacob's right thigh within reach of the angel.

Once more, as with the previous account, this describes the initial positioning and does not easily explain what occurred at the climactic moment of the fight — but these are the Torah sage's terms of analysis.

### Both Sides Moved

The Rabbis, who hold that both thighs are prohibited, explain the narrative differently: *me'achorei ata ve'nashyei* — the angel came from behind Jacob and attacked from the rear, displacing both *gidim* simultaneously. *Ve'nashyei betarvaihu* — he caused both sides to move. This is what underlies the Rabbis' ruling that both thighs are prohibited.

The Gemara then asks: what do the Rabbis do with the verse *behe'avko imo*, which Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi used to derive the right side? They use it differently: *melamed she'alu avak margelotam ad kisei ha'kavod* — it teaches that the dust (*avak*) raised by their feet during the struggle rose all the way to the heavenly throne. The verse is linked to another verse describing God's throne, *ve'anan avak raglav* — a cloud of dust beneath His feet. For the Rabbis, the word *be'he'avko* relates not to grasping but to the cloud of dust, signaling the cosmic significance of the struggle. Jacob and Esav, in the broader rabbinic framework, represent the perennial religious and historical confrontation between Israel and Rome — or later, Christianity — and perhaps the image of dust rising to the heavenly throne captures the eternal, transcendent nature of that struggle.

## Etymology & Sources

### Nasheh: Moved

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi asks: why is the sinew called the *gid hanasheh*? What does the word *nasheh* mean? He answers: *shenasha mimekomo* — it moved from its place, it was displaced. The word *nasheh* denotes removal or movement, as evidenced by a verse in another context: *nashta gevuratam hayu lenashim* — their strength has been removed, they became like women. The name *gid hanasheh*, then, memorializes the fact that the sinew was dislodged in the course of Jacob's struggle.

### Jacob's Legacy

Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Chanina connects the *gid hanasheh* to a verse in Yeshayahu: *davar shalach Adonai beYaakov venafal beYisrael* — a matter God sent through Jacob, and it fell among the Jewish people. The *davar* — the matter or thing — sent through Jacob is the *gid hanasheh*, the only prohibition that we have specifically inherited through Jacob, apart from *davening maariv* according to another Gemara. The second half of the verse, *venafal beYisrael*, describes how this practice spread and became universal among the entire Jewish people, originating with Jacob himself but becoming the obligation of all Israel.

### Joseph & Brothers

Rav Yosef bar Rabbi Chanina expounds another verse: when Joseph instructed his household manager to slaughter an animal and prepare the meal for his brothers, the verse says *tevo'ach tevach ve-hachen* — slaughter and prepare. The *drash* reads *para lahen beit ha-shechita* — expose to them the place of slaughtering so they can inspect and confirm the *shechita* was performed properly. And *ve-hachen* — prepare the meat: *tol gid hanasheh bifneihem* — remove the *gid hanasheh* in their presence. Why specifically in their presence? Because, as we learned in the first *mishna*, one cannot simply trust a butcher to remove the *gid hanasheh*; it must be done visibly. This reading fits with the opinion that the sons of Jacob were already obligated in the prohibition of *gid hanasheh* — *gid hanasheh ne'esar livnei Noach* — meaning not all of humanity, but specifically Jacob's sons, before they formally became the Jewish people at *Matan Torah*.

One could ask how it helps that the slaughter was performed by Egyptians, since *shechitat nochri* is generally invalid. Perhaps the answer is that since everyone present — slaughterer and eaters alike — was at that historical moment a non-Jew in terms of formal *halachic* status, the considerations differ. In any case, the picture conjured by this *drash* is extraordinary: Joseph, concealing his identity, nevertheless ensuring that his brothers can verify every aspect of the *kashrut* of the meal he sets before them.

## Jacob's Righteousness

### Small Vessels

The Gemara now turns to a series of *aggadic* passages about Jacob, spending the remainder of the daf on the narrative surrounding his wrestling match with the angel. The Torah says *vayivater Yaakov levado* — Jacob was left alone. Why was he alone? Rabbi Elazar teaches: *she-nishtayer al pachim ketanim* — he had gone back to retrieve some small, inexpensive pottery vessels that had been forgotten when the household crossed. The *chiddush* is formulated thus: *mikan le-tzaddikim she-chaviv aleihem mamonam yoter mi-gufam* — from here we learn that the righteous care for their property more than their own bodies, in that Jacob was willing to remain alone and exposed to danger in order to retrieve some trivial vessels. Why such care over small things? *She-ein poshtim yedeihem be-gezel* — because they never reach out their hands to take what is not theirs. Those who respect property — their own and others' — treat every object with conscientiousness. The person who says "it's only a little thing, leave it behind" may not be the same person who is careful about the line between his and another's.

This is worth pausing over, because there is a conventional reading of the verse *u-vchol me'odecha* — with all your possessions — that treats excessive attachment to property as somehow secondary to other virtues. Yet here, the *tzaddik*'s care for even small possessions is presented as a mark of his character, not a failing.

### Not Alone at Night

Rabbi Abba bar Kahana derives from the same episode — Jacob left alone as the angel arrived — that a Torah sage should not go out alone at night. *Tosafot* notes that there is also the concern of *mazikim*, harmful spiritual forces that attack Torah sages in particular; in any event, night carries greater danger, whether from people or from supernatural forces, and the angel's insistence on leaving at daybreak is itself a sign that the night domain operates differently. Three additional proof-texts are adduced: Naomi knew Boaz would remain in the field overnight rather than walk home alone; Abraham rose early in the morning before setting out; Jacob's brothers were sent to check on the flocks during daylight; and the verse *vayizrach lo ha-shemesh* — the sun shone upon him — tells us that the fight with the angel only ended at sunrise, meaning the entire episode was a nighttime event.

### Folded Geography

There is a narrative about how Jacob came to sleep at this particular place. The Torah says *vayeitzei Yaakov mi-Beer Sheva vayelech Charana* — Jacob left Beer Sheva and went toward Charan — and then says *vayifga ba-makom* — he encountered the place. The word *vayifga* sounds like an unexpected encounter. The Gemara explains: Jacob actually did arrive in Charan, and then realized he had passed by the place where his forefathers had prayed — identified with *Har ha-Bayit* in Jerusalem, since the location of the ladder (*sha'ar ha-shamayim*, the gateway of heaven) is understood by the Rabbis to be none other than the Temple Mount — and had not stopped to pray there. He said: *efshar avarti al makom she-hitpalelu avotai va-ani lo* — how could I have passed the very place where my fathers prayed and not done so myself? He turned to go back, and *kafetzah lei are'a* — the land leaped up to meet him. He encountered the place through *kefitzat haderech*, the miraculous folding of distance, and was suddenly back at the holy site. The word *vayifga* thus carries both the meaning of an unexpected encounter and, as the Rabbis elsewhere note, the meaning of prayer.

The apparent contradiction between the narrative happening both at *Beit El Luz* and at Jerusalem is resolved by this same folding: when Jacob turned back toward Luz, the land of Israel folded beneath him so that *Har ha-Bayit* came to meet him at Luz. Geography itself became fluid. This also connects thematically to what will come later on the daf — the land folded under Jacob as a promise that it would be easy to conquer.

### Sun Set & Rise

Once Jacob prayed at the holy site, he wanted to leave and continue to Charan. But God said: *tzaddik zeh ba le-veit meloni ve-yipatr be-lo linah* — this righteous person came to My inn, My resting place, and he would depart without spending the night? So God caused the sun to set early, compelling Jacob to stay and sleep. This is the meaning of *ki va ha-shemesh* — the sun came, it set unexpectedly — creating the conditions for the dream of the ladder.

Now, back to the verse *vayizrach lo ha-shemesh* at the end of the fight: Rabbi Yitzchak explains that the sun which had set early for Jacob now rose early for Jacob — *shemesh ha-ba'ah ba'avuro zarcha ba'avuro*, the sun that had been summoned to set on his account now rose on his account. Having borrowed time from him earlier, it now repaid the debt. The sunrise ended the wrestling match. These two episodes — the sun setting early at Beit El and rising early after Penuel — form a pair, and God is the author of both.

A question arises: if Jacob arrived in Charan before setting out back toward Jerusalem, how does this interact with the tradition that Jacob *instituted* *davening maariv*, the evening prayer? The verse *ki va ha-shemesh* is cited in another context as the source for *maariv*. If the sun set miraculously early and Jacob prayed, does that constitute the institution of *maariv*? The *pashut peshat* of the Torah never mentions prayer here; the tradition of prayer is from the *midrash*, and the full details of the interplay between these traditions remain for another discussion.

### Gathering Stones

The Torah says Jacob took from *the stones of the place* — plural — and placed *the stone* under his head — singular. Rabbi Yitzchak teaches: *melamed she-niskabetzu kol osan avanim le-makom echad* — all those stones gathered themselves into one place. *Ve-chol achas ve-achas omeret: alai yanich ha-tzaddik ha-zeh rosho* — each stone declared that the righteous one should rest his head upon it. Because they all competed for the honor, *tana ve-chulan nivlu be-achas* — they were all absorbed into a single stone. That is how the many became one: they merged in shared devotion.

## Ladder & Throne

### Ladder Width

The Gemara asks: how wide was the ladder? *Shemonat alafim parsa'os* — eight thousand parsangs. The calculation proceeds as follows. The Torah says *malachei Elokim olim ve-yordim bo* — angels of God were ascending and descending upon it. *Olim* is plural: at least two angels were ascending. *Yordim* is plural: at least two were descending. Since these were different angels going up and coming down, and since they would have to pass each other at some point, at the moment of passing there would be four angels on the ladder simultaneously. And from another verse, a single angel's physical span is described as *k'Tarshish* — like Tarshish — and it is received tradition that Tarshish is 2,000 parsangs wide. Four angels at 2,000 parsangs each gives 8,000 parsangs.

### Jacob's Heavenly Image

The angels would ascend the ladder and look at the image above — the *diyukan shel ma'alah* — the image on the heavenly throne. In rabbinic tradition, the face of the man among the four faces on the divine throne is the face of Jacob. The word *diyukan* derives from the Greek for icon, meaning image or likeness. Then the angels would descend and see Jacob sleeping below — *ve-yordim u-mistaklin be-diyukno shel mata* — and recognize that it was the same face. Realizing that a human being bore the image inscribed on the heavenly throne, they were seized with jealousy and wanted to harm Jacob — *ba'u le-sak'nah* — to injure him.

### God's Protection

Immediately, *ve-hinei Hashem nitzav alav* — God stood over Jacob. Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish notes: *ilmalei mikra kasuv iy efshar le'omro* — were it not written in the verse, it would be impossible to say such a thing. What does it mean for God to stand over someone protectively? *Ke-adam she-meif al bino* — like a person fanning over his child. The image is of a parent standing over a sleeping child, fanning away insects or the heat, hovering in attentive care. The *Maharsha* suggests it could specifically mean fanning away flies. The point is not the specific motion but the intimacy of the care: had the verse not written it, such language applied to God would be unthinkable.

### Land Folded

*Ha-aretz asher atah shochev aleha* — the land upon which you are sleeping, I will give to you. The plain meaning seems to promise only the small plot on which Jacob lay. But Rabbi Yitzchak teaches: *melamed she-kipel Hakadosh Baruch Hu kol Eretz Yisrael ve-hinichah tachat Yaakov Avinu* — God folded the entire land of Israel and placed it beneath Jacob, *she-tinoach le-hikaivesh le-vanav* — to show that it would be easily conquered by his descendants. The promise was not merely of territory; the folding of the land beneath him was a tangible sign that the conquest would be, in some sense, as natural as sleeping upon it.

## Angel's Turn to Sing

### Final Opportunity

Returning to the conclusion of the wrestling match: the angel says to Jacob *shalcheni ki alah ha-shachar* — send me free, for the dawn is rising. Jacob responds with a challenge: what kind of creature are you, afraid of daylight? You must be a burglar or — and here *Tosafot* in another tractate notes — a card player, someone who does his questionable work under cover of night. The angel replies: *malach ani* — I am an angel — and explains his urgency: *u-miyom she-nivreiti lo higi'a zemani lomar shirah ad achshav* — from the day I was created, I had never yet had my turn to sing praise to God until this very moment. He must go now because his opportunity has finally arrived.

### Angels' Rotations

This statement is supported by Rav Chanana citing Rav: *shalosh kitot shel malachei ha-shareit omrot shirah bechol yom* — three groups of heavenly angels take turns singing praise every day, constantly rotating. Each group gets its moment. The angel could not delay; it was finally his turn after an eternity of waiting.

Israel, by contrast, is told: *Yisrael omrim shirah bechol sha'ah* — Israel may sing to God at any hour. We pray three times a day; we are not confined to a single assigned moment. How rare is the angel's opportunity? The Gemara offers a range of opinions: once a day, once a week, once a month, once a year, once every seven years, once every fifty years, and — most dramatically — once in an entire cosmic lifetime, *pa'am achat be-olam*. This last view fits the angel's urgency best: he has waited since creation itself for this single moment of *shirah*, and he cannot miss it.

The most natural reading of these opinions is that each *individual* angel receives an opportunity at one of these intervals — not that *all* angels collectively sing only that rarely, which would be a peculiar way to describe a theology of ongoing angelic praise. The theme reinforces the contrast: any individual Jew can praise God at any moment, whereas any individual angel must wait for his assigned turn across vast stretches of time.

### Israel vs. Angels

Beyond the frequency of *shirah*, there is another dimension in which Israel surpasses the angels. *Ve-Yisrael mazkirin et Hashem achar shtei teivot* — Israel mentions the name of God after two words: *Shema Yisrael — Hashem*. The angels, by contrast, *ein mazkirin et Hashem ela achar gimel teivot* — do not mention God's name until after three words: *Kadosh Kadosh Kadosh — Hashem Tzeva'ot*. Moreover, Israel does not even begin its *shirah* above until Israel has first sung from below: *be-ron yachad kochvei voker* — with the rejoicing of the morning stars (understood as Israel), and only then *va-yari'u kol bnei Elohim* — do all the heavenly beings shout out. The angels wait for Israel to lead.

### Three Kadoshes

This last point, however, creates an apparent difficulty. The Gemara described three groups of angels: one says *kadosh*, one says *kadosh*, and one says *Kadosh Hashem Tzeva'ot*. The third group mentions God's name after only one word — not three. How is this consistent with the rule that angels mention God's name only after three words?

The Gemara resolves this: the division into three groups does not mean each group says one *kadosh* independently. Rather, the groups build upon one another: the first group says *kadosh*, the second says *kadosh kadosh*, and the third — having inherited what the previous two said — says *kadosh kadosh kadosh Hashem Tzeva'ot*, completing the full proclamation. The third group thus pronounces all three *kadoshes* and only then reaches the name of God. This preserves the rule: even the last group of angels says three words before mentioning God's name. Israel, reciting *Shema Yisrael Hashem*, mentions God's name after just two words — and in that small asymmetry, the Gemara finds the superiority of Israel over the angels.
