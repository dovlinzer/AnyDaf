# Gittin 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lost Get

### Mishnah Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמֵּבִיא גֵּט וְאָבַד הֵימֶנּוּ; מְצָאוֹ לְאַלְתַּר – כָּשֵׁר, וְאִם לָאו – פָּסוּל. מְצָאוֹ בַּחֲפִיסָה אוֹ בִּדְלוֹסְקָמָא, אִם מַכִּירוֹ – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to an agent **who brings a bill of divorce and it** was **lost from him,** if he **finds it immediately** then the bill of divorce is **valid. But if not,** then it is **invalid,** as it is possible that the bill of divorce that he found is not the same one that he lost, and this second bill of divorce belongs to someone else whose name and wife's name are identical to the names of the husband and wife in the lost bill of divorce. However, if **he found it in a *ḥafisa* or in a *deluskema*** that he knows is his, or **if he recognizes** the actual bill of divorce, then it is **valid.**

The Mishnah at the top of the page opens with the case of *hamavi get ve-avda heimenu*: a *shaliach* who is transporting a *get* to deliver to the wife loses it along the way. The question is what he may do when he subsequently recovers it.

### Valid & Invalid

The Mishnah establishes a straightforward principle: *matza'o le-altar*, if he finds the *get* immediately — he dropped it, turned around, saw it, picked it up, and proceeded to deliver it — then it is *kasher*. *Ve-im lav pasul*: if he did not find it right away, it is invalid. The concern underlying this ruling is that after any meaningful interval, one cannot be confident that the recovered document is in fact the same *get* that was lost. However far-fetched it may seem, it is possible that another couple in the same city shares the husband's name, the wife's name, and the place name, and that what the *shaliach* has recovered is a different *get* altogether — one belonging to a different couple with identical names. It is on account of this worry that a *get* found only after some delay cannot be delivered.

### Recognition Markers

The Mishnah provides an exception: *matza'o be-chafisah o be-deluskama* — if the *get* is recovered inside a recognizable sack or purse. *Im makiro kasher*: if he recognizes it, it is valid. Rashi explains that this operates in two distinct ways. Either the *shaliach* recognizes the container itself — he lost his wallet, found his wallet, and the *get* was inside — or, even without a recognized container, he is able to identify the *get* itself through physical markers. What is required is not merely that he knows the names written within it, but that he can identify the document through some concrete physical characteristic. Recognition of the container is of course implicit in either case.

## Bava Metzia Cases

### No Return Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּרְמִינְהוּ: מָצָא גִּיטֵּי נָשִׁים וְשִׁחְרוּרֵי עֲבָדִים, דְּיָיתֵיקֵי, מַתָּנוֹת וְשׁוֹבָרִין, הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יַחֲזִיר – שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר כְּתוּבִין הָיוּ, וְנִמְלַךְ עֲלֵיהֶן שֶׁלֹּא לִיתְּנָן. הָא אָמַר ״תְּנו״ּ – נוֹתְנִין; וַאֲפִילּוּ לִזְמַן מְרוּבֶּה!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that if a bill of divorce was lost before being received by the woman it is invalid unless it was found immediately. **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a mishna (*Bava Metzia* 18a): If **one found bills of divorce, or** bills **of manumission, wills [*dayetikei*],** deeds of **gifts, or receipts, this** finder **should not return** these items to the one who is presumed to have lost them, **as I say** it is possible that **they were written and** then the writer **reconsidered about them** and decided **that he would not give them.** One could infer from this mishna as follows: **But** if the writer **said: Give** these found documents to the intended recipient, **one gives** them, **and** this is true **even** if **a long time** passed since they were lost, and there is no concern that perhaps this document belongs to a different couple entirely; it is concerned only with which party the document fell from. Our Mishnah, by contrast, worries that the recovered document may not be the same *get* at all.

The Gemara now introduces a challenge from a Mishnah elsewhere. The teaching there reads: *matza gittei nashim ve-shichrurei avadim, diyateiki matanot ve-shovrin, harei zeh lo yachazir* — if one finds a woman's *get*, a writ of freedom for a slave, a deathbed bequest, a gift document, or a receipt, one may not return it to either party. The concern is expressed as *be-din omer*: one can argue that the document was written but perhaps the underlying transaction was never completed — the money was never lent, the *get* was never delivered. In the case of a loan document, returning it to the party written as the lender could prejudice the borrower if the money was never in fact advanced. Since one cannot determine from which party the document fell, it is held by *beis din* and returned to neither. In the case of a *get*, returning it to the wife risks allowing her to remarry if it was never actually delivered, while returning it to the husband would deprive her of evidence that she had received it. The document therefore remains with *beis din*.

### Writer's Approval

The Gemara infers that this prohibition is conditional: *ha amar tnu notnim* — implicitly, if the party who originally caused the document to be written were to say "give it," it would be given. In the case of a *get*, if the husband says to deliver it to the wife, one complies. The reasoning is *mimah nafshach*: whichever party the document fell from, the husband's instruction resolves the ambiguity. If it fell from the wife, she is now recovering what is hers; if it fell from the husband, he is now directing that it be given. *Ve-afilu zman merubeh* — the implication is that even after a considerable time has elapsed, once the doubt about delivery is removed, one would proceed. This, however, appears to conflict directly with our Mishnah, which invalidates a *get* found after any meaningful delay. The Mishnah in Bava Metzia is apparently unconcerned about the possibility that the recovered document might belong to a different couple entirely; it is concerned only with which party the document fell from. Our Mishnah, by contrast, worries that the recovered document may not be the same *get* at all.

### Document Identity

The apparent contradiction thus resolves into a distinction between two different concerns. The Mishnah in Bava Metzia addresses *ownership* — to which party does this document belong, and can we be sure it was delivered? It takes for granted that the document found is the same document that was lost. Our Mishnah raises an entirely different concern: *identity* — is this even the same *get*, or might it be a document from an entirely different couple who happen to share the same names? These are two separate problems, and the Gemara now turns to resolving why our Mishnah raises the identity concern while the Mishnah in Bava Metzia does not.

## Rava's Resolution

### High Traffic Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁהַשַּׁיָּירוֹת מְצוּיוֹת, כָּאן בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין הַשַּׁיָּירוֹת מְצוּיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said:** This is **not difficult. Here,** in the mishna that rules that the bill of divorce cannot be used unless it was found immediately, it is stated **with regard to a place where caravans are** frequently **found,** and there is a concern that the found bill of divorce belongs to someone else with the identical name. **There,** in the mishna in tractate *Bava Metzia*, it is stated **with regard to a place where caravans are not** frequently **found,** so one may return the document if he knows that the writer did not reconsider.

Rava resolves the tension as follows: *kan bemakom she-shayarot metsuyot, kan bemakom she-ein shayarot metsuyot* — the cases differ based on whether or not the location in question is a high-traffic area frequented by caravans. Our Mishnah's concern that the recovered *get* might belong to a different couple is, on its face, a remarkably remote possibility — that another man in the same city shares the husband's name, another woman shares the wife's name, and their *get* happened to be lost in the same vicinity. Rava explains that our Mishnah entertains this concern only in a setting with heavy foot traffic and caravan passage. If the *shaliach* loses the *get* in a bustling crossroads, the worry becomes at least conceivable. The Mishnah in Bava Metzia, which is unconcerned with document identity, addresses a case where there is no such heavy traffic — the question there is simply which party the document fell from, not whether it came from an entirely different couple.

### Duplicate Couples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁהַשַּׁיָּירוֹת מְצוּיוֹת – וְהוּא שֶׁהוּחְזְקוּ שְׁנֵי יוֹסֵף בֶּן שִׁמְעוֹן בְּעִיר אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And even in a place where caravans are** frequently **found,** there is not always a concern that the bill of divorce may belong to another man with an identical name, **but** this concern **is** only **where it has been established** that there are **two** men named, e.g., **Yosef ben Shimon, in** that **one town.**

The Gemara immediately adds a further limitation: *ve-afilu bemakom she-shayarot metsuyot, ve-hu she-huchzeku shenei Yosef ben Shimon be-ir achat* — even in a high-traffic area, the identity concern arises only where it is actually known that two couples bearing the same names exist in the same city. The concern, even in a busy location, must be grounded in a concrete known fact — that a second couple with identical names is present — not in mere theoretical possibility.

This two-part requirement emerges not only from Rava's stated position but is also demonstrated by the case of a *get* found in the *beis din* of Rav Huna. A *shaliach* had come to deliver a *get*, lost it, and wished to reclaim it. The *get* bore the name of a town, *Shiyurei*, situated on the river *Rakhis*, and named a man divorcing a woman. Rav Huna asked: ought one to be concerned that there is another city called *Shiyurei* — one we do not know of — where another couple bears the same names? Rav Hisda directed Rabbah, then a student of Rav Huna, to investigate relevant precedents in time for Rav Huna's deliberations that evening. Rabbah went and found the teaching in Bava Metzia: *kol ma'aseh beis din harei zeh yachazir* — any document that has been authenticated by *beis din* may be returned, since *beis din* authentication occurs only after delivery has been confirmed. From this, Rabbah inferred that one is not concerned about other cities of the same name harboring couples with the same names.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, קַשְׁיָא דְּרַבָּה אַדְּרַבָּה: דְּהָהוּא גִּיטָּא דְּאִישְׁתְּכַח בֵּי דִינָא דְּרַב הוּנָא, וַהֲוָה כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ ״בִּשְׁוִירֵי מָתָא דְּעַל רָכִיס נַהֲרָא״, וְאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: חוֹשְׁשִׁין לִשְׁנֵי שְׁוִירֵי; וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא לְרַבָּה: פּוֹק וְעַיֵּין בָּהּ, דִּלְאוּרְתָּא בָּעֵי לַהּ מִינָּךְ רַב הוּנָא. נְפַק, דַּק וְאַשְׁכַּח – דִּתְנַן: כׇּל מַעֲשֵׂה בֵּית דִּין – הֲרֵי זֶה יַחְזִיר. וְהָא בֵּי דִינָא דְּרַב הוּנָא, דְּכִמְקוֹם שֶׁהַשַּׁיָּירוֹת מְצוּיוֹת דָּמֵי, וְקָא פָּשֵׁיט יַחְזִיר; אַלְמָא, אִי הוּחְזְקוּ שְׁנֵי יוֹסֵף בֶּן שִׁמְעוֹן בְּעִיר אַחַת – אִין, וְאִי לָא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **As, if you do not say so,** that this concern is taken into account only in a place where it is known that there are two people with the same name, then there is a **difficulty** presented in the form of a contradiction between this statement **of Rabba and** another statement **of Rabba. As** there was **a certain bill of divorce that was found,** i.e., brought, in **the court of Rav Huna, and** the name of the place that **was written in it** was: **In Sheviri the city, which is on the Rakhis River. And Rav Huna said: One is concerned about** the possibility of the existence of **two** cities called **Sheviri,** and it is possible that this bill of divorce belongs to another man with an identical name. And Rav Ḥisda said to Rabba,** who was then a student: **Go out and examine this** *halakha*, **as Rav Huna** will **ask you about it at night.** Rabba **went out, examined** it, **and discovered** a relevant source. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Bava Metzia* 20a): **One should return all court enactments,** i.e., promissory notes that have been authenticated by the court, to their owner, and there is no concern that perhaps there are two towns with the identical name and that the promissory note belongs to someone else. The Gemara explains: **But isn't the court of Rav Huna comparable to a place where caravans are** frequently **found,** as there were always many people present there? **And** yet Rabba **resolved** the question and ruled that **one should return** the document to the owner, which appears to contradict his earlier ruling that one should not return a document found in a place where caravans are frequently found. **Apparently** he holds that **if it is established** that there are **two** people named **Yosef ben Shimon in one town,** then **yes,** there is a concern and the document should not be returned, **but if not,** there is **no** concern.

The Gemara observes that Rav Huna's *beis din* was itself a high-traffic venue — all documents passed through there, and it was precisely the sort of place where lost documents might turn up. Yet Rabbah was willing to infer from the Bava Metzia Mishnah that even in such a setting one returns the document, and apparently was not worried about an unknown city of the same name with a similarly named couple. This proves that Rabbah requires both criteria — high traffic *and* known duplicate couples in the same city — before entertaining the concern that a recovered document might be a different *get* entirely. High traffic alone is insufficient.

### Flax Marketplace Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲבַד רַבָּה עוֹבָדָא בְּהָהוּא גִּיטָּא, דְּאִישְׁתְּכַח בֵּי כִיתָּנָא בְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא, כִּשְׁמַעְתֵּיהּ. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי בְּדוּכְתָּא הֵיכָא דְּתָרוּ כִּיתָּנָא, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּהוּחְזְקוּ – דְּלָא שְׁכִיחָן שְׁיָירָתָא; וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי בְּדוּכְתָּא דִּמְזַבְּנִי כִּיתָּנָא, וְהוּא שֶׁלֹּא הוּחְזְקוּ, וּשְׁכִיחָן שַׁיָּירוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rabba performed an action,** i.e., issued a practical ruling, **with regard to a certain bill of divorce that was found in the flax house in** the city of **Pumbedita, in accordance with his** ruling of ***halakha*.** As to the details of this incident, **there are** those **who say** that this was **in the place where** people **soaked flax, and although it was established** that there were two people with the same name living in the city mentioned in the bill of divorce, he ruled this way since it was a place **where caravans are not** frequently **found. And there are** those **who say** that this occurred **in a place where** people **sold flax, and it was not established** that two people with the same name lived in the city where the bill of divorce was written, **and** this occurred in a place where **caravans are** frequently **found.**

This two-pronged analysis is borne out by a practical ruling Rava issued. A *get* was found in the place of flax in Pumbedita, and Rava returned it to the *shaliach* following his own ruling, dismissing the concern that it might belong to a different couple. Two versions of the case are reported. According to some, *beduchta heicha d'tavu kitna* — it was a place where flax was processed, an industrial location outside the marketplace. Even if it were known that two couples with identical names both hailed from that town, there are no caravans there; the traffic criterion is not met, and so the concern does not arise. According to others, the case involved a place where flax was *sold* — a marketplace with genuine caravan traffic — yet it was not known that two couples with the same names existed in that city. Again, one criterion is absent. Either way, Rava returns the *get*, demonstrating that his position requires *both* high traffic and known duplicate couples before one withholds the document.

## Rav Zeira's View

### Mishnah vs Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי זֵירָא רָמֵי מַתְנִיתִין אַבָּרַיְיתָא, וּמְשַׁנֵּי. תְּנַן: הַמֵּבִיא גֵּט וְאָבַד הֵימֶנּוּ, אִם מְצָאוֹ לְאַלְתַּר – כָּשֵׁר, וְאִם לָאו – פָּסוּל. וּרְמִינְהוּ: מָצָא גֵּט אִשָּׁה בַּשּׁוּק, בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַבַּעַל מוֹדֶה – יַחְזִיר לְאִשָּׁה, אֵין הַבַּעַל מוֹדֶה – לֹא יַחְזִיר לֹא לָזֶה וְלֹא לָזֶה. הָא בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַבַּעַל מוֹדֶה מִיהַת – יַחְזִיר לָאִשָּׁה, וַאֲפִילּוּ לִזְמַן מְרוּבֶּה! וּמְשַׁנֵּי: כָּאן בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁהַשַּׁיָּירוֹת מְצוּיוֹת, כָּאן בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין הַשַּׁיָּירוֹת מְצוּיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Concerning this issue, the Gemara relates that **Rabbi Zeira raises a contradiction** between **the mishna and a *baraita* and** then **answers** it: **We learned** in the mishna: With regard to an agent **who brings a bill of divorce and it** was **lost from him, if he finds it immediately** then the bill of divorce is **valid. And if not,** it is **invalid. And** he **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita* that states: If one **found a woman's bill of divorce in the marketplace,** then **when the husband admits** that he wrote it and gave it, the finder should **return** it **to the woman.** If **the husband does not admit** to this, then he should **not return** it, **not to this** man **and not to this** woman. One can infer from here: **But** **when the husband admits** that he wrote it, **in any event** he should **return** it **to the woman, and** by omission this appears to be the *halakha* **even if a long time** has passed since the bill of divorce was lost. **And** Rabbi Zeira **answers: Here,** in the mishna where it states that he should not return it, it is stated **with regard to a place where caravans are** frequently **found; there,** in the *baraita* where it states that he should return it, it is stated **with regard to a place where caravans are not** frequently **found.**

Rav Zeira now undertakes essentially the same exercise as Rava, but frames the contradiction differently. Rather than contrasting our Mishnah with the Mishnah in Bava Metzia, he sets our Mishnah against a *baraita* in Bava Metzia. The Mishnah is restated: *hameivi get ve-avado heimenu, matza'o le-altar kasher, ve-im lav pasul* — found immediately, valid; found after a delay, invalid. This is contrasted with the *baraita*.

### Husband Agreement

The *baraita* states explicitly: *matza get isha be-shuk, bi-zman she-ha-ba'al modeh yachzir le-isha* — if a woman's *get* is found in the marketplace, and the husband acknowledges it is hers, one returns it to the woman. *Ein ha-ba'al modeh lo yachzir lo le-zeh ve-lo le-zeh* — if the husband does not acknowledge it, one returns it to neither party. And critically, the *baraita* adds: *ve-afilu zman merubeh* — even after a long time has elapsed, once the husband concedes the *get* is hers, it is returned. The *baraita* is thus entirely unconcerned with the possibility that the document might belong to a different couple, even when considerable time has passed. This is the same tension Rava addressed, and Rav Zeira resolves it with the same solution: our Mishnah is the case of high traffic, the *baraita* addresses a case without such traffic.

Some report that Rav Zeira requires both criteria, just as Rava does — high traffic *plus* known duplicate couples — and holds that only when both conditions are met does one not return the *get*. Others say Rav Zeira may be stricter than Rava: once the location has heavy traffic, even without confirmed knowledge of a second couple bearing the same names, Rav Zeira may deem the concern sufficient to invalidate the *get*. On that reading, high traffic alone would be enough to worry that somewhere a similarly named couple exists, even if one has no concrete knowledge of them. This position, admittedly far-fetched in its scope, would then *pliga deRava* — stand in opposition to Rava's ruling.

### Implicit Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי וְהוּא שֶׁהוּחְזְקוּ הוּא דְּלָא לַיהְדַּר, וְהַיְינוּ דְּרַבָּה; וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא הוּחְזְקוּ לָא לַיהְדַּר, וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבָּה. בִּשְׁלָמָא דְּרַבָּה לָא אָמַר כְּרַבִּי זֵירָא, מַתְנִיתִין אַלִּימָא לֵיהּ לְאַקְשׁוֹיֵי; אֶלָּא רַבִּי זֵירָא, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּרַבָּה? אָמַר לָךְ, מִי קָתָנֵי: אִם אָמַר ״תְּנוּ״ – נוֹתְנִין, וַאֲפִילּוּ לִזְמַן מְרוּבֶּה? דִּלְמָא אִם אָמַר ״תְּנוּ״ נוֹתְנִין כִּדְקַיְימָא לַן – לְאַלְתַּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara compares the rulings of Rabba and Rabbi Zeira. **There are** those **who say** with regard to Rabbi Zeira's statement that he should not return it in a place where caravans are frequently found: **And this is** the case **when it is established** that there are two people in the town with the identical name. In **that** case, Rabbi Zeira holds **that it should not be returned, and this is** the same ruling as that **of Rabba. And there are** those **who say:** In a place where caravans are frequently found, **even though it is not established** that there are two people with identical names, **it should not be returned, and** he **disagrees with** the ruling **of Rabba.** The Gemara clarifies: **Granted that Rabba did not say** a discourse **like** that of **Rabbi Zeira** and raise a contradiction from the *baraita*, as **he** holds that it is **a stronger** challenge **to raise a difficulty** from **the mishna** in tractate *Bava Metzia* because the Mishna, redacted by Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, employs more precise language than *baraitot*. **But what is the reason** that **Rabbi Zeira did not say** a discourse **like** that of **Rabba** and raise a contradiction from the mishna in tractate *Bava Metzia*? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Zeira could have **said to you: Does** the mishna **teach** that **if he said: Give** the found document to the intended recipient, **one gives** it, **and** this is so **even** if **a long time** passed? This was only an inference from the mishna. **Perhaps** the mishna should be interpreted differently, so as to teach: **If he said: Give** it, then **one gives** it, but this is only **as we maintain** in the mishna, when it is found **immediately,** not if a long time has passed.

The Gemara asks a pointed question about methodology: given that Rava accomplished the same reconciliation using two Mishnayot — which are more authoritative sources — why did Rav Zeira instead compare our Mishnah with a *baraita*? The answer is that Rav Zeira found the Mishnah in Bava Metzia inadequate as a proof text. *Mi katani im ha-ba'al modeh yachzir ve-afilu le-zman merubeh?* — does the Mishnah there actually state explicitly that when the husband agrees, one returns the *get* even after a long time? It does not. That inference is implicit. And since it is only implicit, one might argue that the Mishnah's permission to return the document when the husband agrees is limited to cases of immediate recovery. The Mishnah may simply mean: yes, under the right conditions — including immediacy — you return it if the husband agrees. Only the *baraita* states explicitly that one returns the *get* when the husband agrees *even after considerable time*. When a teaching states a rule explicitly without qualification, it communicates that the rule applies broadly; had immediacy been a required condition, the *baraita* would have said so. Therefore, Rav Zeira found the *baraita* a sharper proof of the genuine contradiction, even at the cost of using a less authoritative source.

## Stricter Approaches

### Witness Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אָמַר, כְּגוֹן דְּקָאָמְרִי עֵדִים: מֵעוֹלָם לֹא חָתַמְנוּ אֶלָּא עַל גֵּט אֶחָד שֶׁל יוֹסֵף בֶּן שִׁמְעוֹן. אִי הָכִי, מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא לֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא אִיתְרְמִי שְׁמָא כִּשְׁמָא וְעֵדִים כְּעֵדִים קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers an alternative resolution to the contradiction between the mishna here, on the one hand, and the mishna in tractate *Bava Metzia* and the *baraita*, on the other hand. **Rabbi Yirmeya said:** It is possible to resolve the contradiction in a different way: The latter permit one to return a lost bill of divorce only in a case **where** the **witnesses** who signed the bill of divorce **say: We have never signed a bill of divorce of Yosef ben Shimon other than** this **one,** in which case there is no concern that the bill of divorce belongs to someone else. The Gemara asks: **If that is so, what** is the purpose **of stating** that one returns the bill of divorce? Since it clearly belongs to her, there is no question that it must be returned to her. The Gemara answers: It is necessary **lest you say** that **one should be concerned** that **perhaps it happened** that another bill of divorce was written in which **the names** of the husband and the wife **are identical to the names** of the husband and wife of the second bill of divorce, **and** the names of the **witnesses** on that bill of divorce **are identical to** the names of the **witnesses** on this bill of divorce, when in fact they are different witnesses. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that this is not a concern.

Rabbi Yirmiya takes a substantially stricter position. Rather than reading our Mishnah as the exceptional case warranting concern about document identity, he inverts the framing: the case in Bava Metzia where the document is returned is itself the exceptional situation, and the rule is one of pervasive concern about document identity. Under Rabbi Yirmiya's reading, a *get* may be returned only when the witnesses signed on it testify: *me'olam lo chasmanu ela al get echad she-Yosef ben Shimon* — we have never in our lives signed on any other *get* bearing this husband's and wife's names. Only that affirmative declaration suffices to remove all doubt that the document is the unique *get* it purports to be.

### Same-Name Witnesses

The Gemara raises an objection: *mahu d'teima leichosh shemma isromi shma ke-shma ve-edim ke-edim* — ought one not to worry that even the witnesses could by coincidence share their names with witnesses on a different *get*? Perhaps another couple bears the same husband's and wife's names, their *get* is signed by witnesses who happen to share the names of these witnesses, and we have only displaced the problem one level. The Gemara dismisses this: one does not entertain that degree of concern. The witnesses' testimony that they have never signed another such *get* is taken as decisive; the possibility that both the parties *and* the witnesses are coincidentally replicated is too remote to take seriously.

### Siman Muvhak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר, כְּגוֹן דְּקָאָמַר: נֶקֶב יֵשׁ בּוֹ בְּצַד אוֹת פְּלוֹנִית, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ סִימָן מוּבְהָק. וְדַוְקָא בְּצַד אוֹת פְּלוֹנִית, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ סִימָן מוּבְהָק, אֲבָל: נֶקֶב בְּעָלְמָא, לָא;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests an alternative resolution to the contradiction. **Rav Ashi said:** When do the mishna in tractate *Bava Metzia* and the *baraita* rule that one should return the bill of divorce? It is in a case **where** the one who lost it **says: There is a hole in** the bill of divorce, **next to such and such a letter, as this is a clear-cut distinguishing mark for him.** The Gemara comments: **And** Rav Ashi permits the returning of such a bill of divorce **specifically** when one says that the hole is **next to such and such a letter, as that is a clear-cut distinguishing mark for him. But** if he said **only** that it had **a hole** without mentioning its precise location, then one should **not** return the bill of divorce, as that is not considered a clear-cut distinguishing mark.

Rav Ashi offers yet another approach. He essentially applies the principle embedded in the end of our Mishnah to the case in Bava Metzia. For Rav Ashi, concern about document identity is the general rule, and one returns a *get* only when the *shaliach* is able to identify the specific document through a *siman muvhak* — an absolute, unambiguous identifying mark. The example given is: *nekev yesh bo be-tzad os ploni* — there is a hole directly beside a specific named letter. Such a marker is uniquely identifying and admits of no reasonable doubt.

### Siman Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ סִימָנִין אִי דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא אִי דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: Rav Ashi **is uncertain** with regard to **whether** the obligation to return a lost item to its owner on the basis of **distinguishing marks** is **by Torah law** or **if** it is **by rabbinic law.** Therefore, in the case of a bill of divorce, he holds that one may rely only on a clear-cut distinguishing mark, as everyone agrees that the requirement to return a lost item to its owner on the basis of a clear-cut distinguishing mark is by Torah law.

The Gemara draws a sharp distinction between a *siman muvhak* and an ordinary *siman*. A *siman* is an identifying marker that increases the likelihood that an object is the one claimed — for example, noting that a wallet had a hole in it somewhere near the top. While this certainly makes it more probable that the wallet is the claimant's, it is not conclusive: other documents may also have holes, and the description is insufficiently precise. A *siman muvhak*, by contrast, is an absolute identifier that removes all reasonable doubt — a hole beside a *specific* letter on a *specific* line, for instance. In the context of returning lost objects generally, even an ordinary *siman* suffices to return an *aveidah*. This practice, however, may be only a rabbinic accommodation; at the Torah level, one might require a *siman muvhak* to establish ownership conclusively. For a *get*, this distinction is critical. Even if *simanim* of the ordinary kind are sufficient to return a wallet — whether because *simanim* are *d'oraita* or because rabbinic law permits it for monetary objects — they may be insufficient when the validity of a divorce hangs in the balance. Rav Ashi's uncertainty about whether *simanim* are *d'oraita* or only *d'rabbanan* leads him to require the more exacting standard: only a *siman muvhak* permits returning a *get*.

## Siman vs Recognition

### Two Options

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אִירְכַס לֵיהּ גִּיטָּא בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא, אֲמַר: אִי סִימָנָא – אִית לִי בְּגַוֵּיהּ, אִי טְבִיעוּת – עֵינָא אִית לִי בְּגַוֵּיהּ. אַהְדְּרוּהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ. אֲמַר: לָא יָדַעְנָא אִי מִשּׁוּם סִימָנָא אַהְדְּרוּהּ – וְקָסָבְרִי סִימָנִים דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, אִי מִשּׁוּם טְבִיעוּת עֵינָא – וְדַוְקָא צוּרְבָּא מִדְּרַבָּנַן, אֲבָל אִינָשׁ בְּעָלְמָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos this discussion the Gemara relates an incident: **Rabba bar bar Ḥana lost the bill of divorce** that he was transmitting, when he was **in the study hall. He said: If** they request **a distinguishing mark, I have** one **for it. If** it depends on **visual recognition, I have** methods of recognition **for it. They returned** the bill of divorce **to him. He said** afterward: **I do not know if they returned it due to the distinguishing mark** that I supplied, **and they hold that distinguishing marks** are used to return lost items **by Torah law,** or **if it was due to** my **visual recognition, and** it is **specifically Torah scholars [*tzurva miderabbanan*]** like me who are relied upon when they say that they recognize an item, **but an ordinary man** would **not** be relied upon to recognize the item and have it returned to him.

Rabbah Bar Bar Channah lost a *get* in the *beis midrash*. When the document was found, he declared that he could identify it in either of two ways: *i simanei it li bagah* — he could provide an identifying mark, some specific physical characteristic he could describe and that could be verified — or, alternatively, *i tvi'at eina it li bagah* — he could simply look at the document and recognize it through *tvi'at eina*, the impression of the eye. This mode of recognition is familiar from ordinary experience: one may be entirely certain that a garment is one's own without being able to articulate which specific feature makes it recognizable, because the knowledge arises from a gestalt of subtle accumulated cues. In Bava Metzia, the Gemara establishes that a *talmid chacham* may recover a lost object through *tvi'at eina*, because his reliability is trusted; extending this privilege to ordinary people would of course create an open-ended mechanism for fraudulent claims.

### Uncertain Grounds

The document was returned to Rabbah Bar Bar Channah. However, he himself expressed uncertainty: *lo yadana im mishum simana hadaruhu* — he did not know on what basis it had been returned to him. Was it because he offered a *siman*, and the *beis din* deemed that sufficient — thus implying a view that *simanim d'oraita* and adequate even for a *get*? Or was it *mishum tvi'at eina* — because he had asserted personal recognition?

### Torah Scholars Only

The uncertainty carries practical *halachic* weight. If the *get* was returned on the basis of *tvi'at eina*, then this procedure is available exclusively to a *talmid chacham* — *ve-davka tzurba me-rabbanan, aval inish de-alma lo* — but not to an ordinary person. If, however, the *get* was returned because the ordinary *siman* he offered was deemed sufficient, then an important and broader principle follows: even without a *siman muvhak*, a *siman* of the standard kind suffices to return a *get* — a ruling that would apply to any *shaliach*, not only to a scholar. The inability to determine which of these two grounds motivated the ruling leaves both the scope of *tvi'at eina* and the sufficiency of ordinary *simanim* for a *get* unresolved.

## Immediacy Measures

### Caravan Duration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם לָאו – פָּסוּל: תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵיזֶהוּ שֶׁלֹּא לְאַלְתַּר? רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: שֶׁשָּׁהָה כְּדֵי שֶׁתַּעֲבוֹר שְׁיָירָא וְתִשְׁרֶה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא אָדָם עוֹמֵד וְרוֹאֶה שֶׁלֹּא עָבַר שָׁם אָדָם. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: שֶׁלֹּא שָׁהָה אָדָם שָׁם. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: כְּדֵי לִכְתּוֹב אֶת הַגֵּט. רַבִּי יִצְחָק אוֹמֵר: כְּדֵי לִקְרוֹתוֹ. אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: כְּדֵי לְכוֹתְבוֹ וְלִקְרוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if one found the bill of divorce immediately, it is valid, **but if not,** then it is **invalid. The Sages taught: What is** considered **not immediately? Rabbi Natan says:** It is **when** there was **a delay equivalent** to the amount of time it would take **for a caravan to pass** by **and camp** there. **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says:** There is no fixed amount of time; rather, it is within the category of immediately **as** long as **there will be a person** that **stands and sees that no** other **person passed there. And some say** that he said: It is as long **as no person stopped there. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** It is within the category of immediately if there was not a delay **equivalent** to the amount of time it would take **to write the bill of divorce. Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** It is equivalent not to the amount of time needed write the bill of divorce, but **equivalent** to the amount of time it would take **to read it. Others say:** It is **equivalent** to the amount of time it would take **to write it and to read it.**

Having established the framework of immediate versus non-immediate recovery, the Gemara presents a *baraita* in which Tannaim debate exactly what constitutes *she-lo le-altar* — a non-immediate interval. Rabbi Natan defines it functionally, within Rava's framework of caravans: *kedei she-tavor shayara ve-tishreh* — the amount of time it would take for a caravan to pass by and to spend some time encamped in the area. Once that interval has elapsed, the concern that the document might have arrived from an entirely different group is deemed relevant.

### Witness Requirement

Rabbi Shimon ben Eleazar does not define the interval by a fixed duration at all. Rather, *kedei she-yehei adam omed ve-ro'eh she-lo avar sham adam* — one must have a person who was standing watch and can testify that no one passed through the area between the moment the *get* was dropped and the moment it was recovered. This standard is inherently more demanding, since even a small number of passersby would disqualify the recovery absent such testimony.

### Tarrying Standard

Others offer an intermediate position: *she-lo shaha adam sham* — what matters is not whether anyone merely passed by, but whether anyone actually tarried in the area. If people walked through without stopping, the concern is not triggered; once someone stopped and remained, one worries that the document might have fallen from that person.

### Writing/Reading Time

Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi offers a time-based standard using a unit drawn from the subject matter itself: *kedei likhtov et ha-get* — the time required to write a *get*. There is no logical nexus between writing time and the risk of document confusion; Rabbi simply chose a time unit familiar to the context. Rabbi Yitzchak shortens this considerably: *kedei likroso* — the time it takes merely to read a *get*, a substantially briefer interval. Yet others combine the two: the time to write *and* read it.

## Simanim Evidence

### Marks Testify

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ שָׁהָה, וְיֵשׁ בּוֹ סִימָנִין – מְעִידִים עָלָיו; דְּאָמְרִי: נֶקֶב יֵשׁ בּוֹ בְּצַד אוֹת פְּלוֹנִית. וְאֵין מְעִידִין עַל סִימָנֵי הַגּוּף; דְּאָמְרִי: אָרוֹךְ וְגוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And even** if there was **a delay and** the bill of divorce **has distinguishing marks on it,** the marks **attest to it** and it is considered a valid bill of divorce. This is the *halakha* where the distinguishing marks are clear-cut, e.g., **when they say: It has a hole next to such and such a letter. And one may not testify with regard to distinguishing marks of the** physical description of the bill of divorce **itself,** e.g., **where they say:** This bill of divorce is **long or short,** as these are not considered distinguishing marks.

The Gemara now turns to the role of identifying marks in recovering a *get* after what would otherwise be a disqualifying delay. The principle stated is: *va-afilu sha'ah, ve-yesh bo simanim, me'idim alav* — even if considerable time has elapsed, if the *get* bears identifying marks, those marks testify on its behalf. This restates and reinforces what the Gemara concluded in the analysis of Rav Ashi's position: where a sufficient identifier is present, the lapse of time does not render the document invalid.

### Hole Mark

The example of a qualifying *siman muvhak* is given: *nekev yesh bo be-tzad os ploni* — there is a hole positioned directly beside a specific named letter. The precision of this description — a particular hole in relation to a particular letter — is what elevates it to the level of an absolute identifier. It is not merely suggestive; it is conclusive.

### Insufficient Marks

By contrast, physical characteristics of the document as an object — *simanei ha-guf*, as it were — are insufficient: *ein me'idim al simanei ha-guf, d'amri aroch ve-gutz* — that the parchment is long or short, wide or narrow, dark or light in color. These are generic physical attributes that many documents may share and that cannot serve as identifying marks. They constitute at most an ordinary *siman*, not a *siman muvhak*, and as established earlier, an ordinary *siman* is inadequate to permit delivery of a *get*. The same principle that applies to the *baraita*'s analysis here parallels precisely the conclusion already reached in the immediately preceding sugya.

## Accepted Standard

### Tarrying Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְצָאוֹ קָשׁוּר בְּכִיס, בְּאַרְנָקִי וּבְטַבַּעַת, אוֹ שֶׁמְּצָאוֹ בֵּין כֵּלָיו; אֲפִילּוּ לִזְמַן מְרוּבֶּה – כָּשֵׁר. אִיתְּמַר: רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, הֲלָכָה: שֶׁלֹּא שָׁהָה אָדָם שָׁם. רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל, הֲלָכָה: שֶׁלֹּא עָבַר אָדָם שָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where one found a bill of divorce **tied** up **in a pouch** or **in a purse [*arnaki*], or** encircled **in a ring,** and he recognizes the document, **or** in a case **where he found it among his utensils** in his house, then **even** if **a long time** passed, the bill of divorce is **valid.** **It was stated** that the *amora'im* disagreed with regard to the *halakha* in this dispute: **Rav Yehuda** says that **Shmuel says:** The ***halakha*** is that the bill of divorce is valid as long **as no person stopped there. Rabba bar bar Ḥana** says that **Rav Yitzḥak bar Shmuel says:** The ***halakha*** is that the bill of divorce is valid as long **as no** other **person passed there.**

The Gemara records two practical rulings on the question of non-immediacy. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *halacha she-lo shaha adam sham* — the accepted standard is that non-immediacy is constituted when someone actually *tarried* in the area. Mere passage is insufficient to trigger the concern; it is only lingering that creates the worry that the document might have fallen from another person.

### No Passerby

Rabbah bar bar Chana said in the name of Rav Yitzchak bar Shmuel: *halacha she-lo avar adam sham* — the accepted ruling is stricter: the interval is non-immediate as soon as even a single person has *passed through*, not merely tarried. One might ask why these authorities stated the *positions* rather than simply ruling in favor of one of the named Tannaim above. The Gemara explains: *mishum d'aphlei* — because there were variant traditions about which Tanna held which view, it was cleaner to state the operative rule directly rather than risk confusion by citing a name associated with a disputed attribution.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מָר הֲלָכָה כְּמָר, וּמָר הֲלָכָה כְּמָר! מִשּׁוּם דְּאָפְכִי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Why is it necessary to state the actual opinion? **Let** this **Sage,** Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel, **say** that the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of this **Sage,** the opinion cited as: Some say; **and** let this **Sage,** Rabba bar bar Ḥana citing Rav Yitzḥak bar Shmuel, say that the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of this **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar. The Gemara answers: **Because** there are those **who reverse** the opinions of the *tanna'im*, they needed to state the opinions explicitly so that there would be no mistake with regard to the *halakha*.

## Wallet Definitions

### Chafisa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְצָאוֹ בַּחֲפִיסָה אוֹ בִּדְלוֹסְקָמָא: מַאי ״חֲפִיסָה״? אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: חֵמֶת קְטַנָּה. מַאי ״דְּלוֹסְקָמָא״? טְלִיקָא דְסָבֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if one **found** the bill of divorce **in a *ḥafisa* or in a *deluskema*,** then it is valid. The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the word ***ḥafisa*? Rabba bar bar Ḥana says:** It is **a small flask. What** is the meaning of ***deluskema*?** It is **a purse [*telika*] of the elderly.**

The Mishnah's mention of a *chafisah* and a *deluskama* as containers whose presence permits identification of the *get* invites a brief lexical clarification. What is a *chafisah*? Rabbah bar bar Chana explains: *cheima k'tana* — a small flask.

### Deluskama

And what is a *deluskama*? *Talika d'savi* — a purse carried by elderly people. In both cases, the point is the same: if the *get* is found within an object that itself can be recognized and identified as belonging to the *shaliach*, or if the document itself bears a *siman muvhak*, the combination of markers is sufficient to establish that this is indeed the same *get* that was lost. Without such markers — whether the container is unrecognized, the *siman* falls short of *siman muvhak*, or the claimant cannot offer *tvi'at eina* at the level the law requires — the document may not be delivered if found after any meaningful delay, given the concern, narrow as it may be, that it represents an entirely different *get* from a differently named couple who happen to share every relevant name.
