# Nazir 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishna: Multiple Acts

### Opening remarks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נָזִיר שֶׁהָיָה שׁוֹתֶה יַיִן כׇּל הַיּוֹם — אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״אַל תִּשְׁתֶּה״ ״אַל תִּשְׁתֶּה״, וְהוּא שׁוֹתֶה — חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת. הָיָה מְגַלֵּחַ כׇּל הַיּוֹם — אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״אַל תְּגַלֵּחַ״ ״אַל תְּגַלֵּחַ״, וְהוּא מְגַלֵּחַ — חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת. הָיָה מִטַּמֵּא לְמֵתִים כׇּל הַיּוֹם — אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״אַל תִּטַּמֵּא״ ״אַל תִּטַּמֵּא״, וְהוּא מִטַּמֵּא — חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A nazirite who was drinking wine all day is liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes. If people **said to him** during the course of the day: **Do not drink, do not drink, and** nevertheless **he** continues to **drink, he is liable for each and every** time he was warned. If a nazirite **kept shaving all day, he is liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes. If **they said to him: Do not shave, do not shave, and he shaves, he is liable for each and every** time he was warned. If he **became ritually impure from a corpse** many times **all day, he is liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes. If **they said to him: Do not become impure, do not become impure, and he** continues to **become impure, he is liable for each and every** time he was warned.

Daf 42 opens the Gemara's treatment of the third prohibition of *nazir*: becoming *tamei l'meitim*. Having addressed the prohibitions of wine and shaving, the sugya now turns to *tumas meis*, and as we shall see, the discussion carries consequences far beyond the laws of *nezirus* — extending to the prohibitions of *kehunah* in every generation.

### Single violation in day

This formulation itself raises a conceptual question worth noting. Some *Rishonim* suggest that the *nazir* may indeed be *chayav lishmayim* for each individual act of drinking, each one constituting a separate prohibition in some sense, yet he receives only one set of lashes because there was only one *hatra'ah*. The question is whether we conceptualize the day's drinking as multiple discrete acts for which the structure of *malkus* simply cannot be applied without separate warnings, or whether the entire episode is viewed as one continuous act of transgression.

### Act definition

This distinction matters because there is something genuinely puzzling here. On the one hand, drinking wine throughout the day could be seen as a process — an ongoing single act of violation. Getting a haircut, too, unfolds over time and could be conceptualized as one extended prohibition. But *tumas meis* is different in character: becoming *tamei* is not obviously a process. Each contact with a corpse seems, almost by definition, to be a discrete act. This observation cuts in two directions, as the Gemara will explore.

### Role of warning

The Mishna resolves the question of separate *malkus* through the mechanism of *hatra'ah*. If they said to him *al tishteh, al tishteh* — warning him before each act of drinking — and he drank each time, then *chayav al kol achat v'achat*: he is liable for each separately. The warning functions to define and divide what might otherwise be treated as a single continuous violation into separate acts, each carrying its own liability. Crucially, this appears to apply even if the individual drinkings occurred within a span that might otherwise be halachically combined as a single act of drinking. Conversely, even acts that are clearly separate — say, drinking in the morning and again days later — do not generate separate *malkus* without a separate *hatra'ah*.

The parallel case of shaving follows the same structure: *hayah megaleyach kol hayom eino chayav ela achat*, but with separate warnings before each hair, *chayav al kol achat v'achat*. And the same applies, at least formally, to *tumas meis*. The Mishna presents the three cases as parallel. Yet as the Gemara will demonstrate, the *tumah* case carries a crucial complication that the wine and hair cases do not share.

## Wine, Hair, Tumah

### Three parallel cases

The Mishna presents three parallel cases: drinking wine all day, cutting hair all day, and becoming *tamei l'meitim* all day. In each case, absent separate warnings, the *nazir* receives only one set of lashes; with separate warnings before each act, he receives a separate set for each. On the surface, these three cases are symmetric.

### Tumah vs. process

Yet a closer look reveals that *tumah* differs fundamentally from the other two categories. When a *nazir* drinks more wine, more of the prohibited substance enters his body — he has done something additional. When he cuts another hair, he has physically removed something additional from his head. In both cases, there is an incremental act with a concrete result. But when a person who is already *tamei* touches another dead body, what has he accomplished? His status was already *tamei*. The prohibition, as the Gemara will make clear, is *lo yitama* — a prohibition framed in terms of achieving a state of *tumah*. If the state was already present, the question arises whether a further contact constitutes a separate prohibited act at all.

### Repeat impurity question

This generates the central question that will drive the entire sugya: once a *nazir* (or *kohen*) is already *tamei*, does touching another dead body on the same day constitute an additional violation? The answer to this question has enormous practical consequences, not only for *nezirus* but for the laws of *kehunah* in our time. The *Rishonim* developed the position that all *kohanim* nowadays are presumed *tamei meis* — having buried a relative at some point and been unable to purify through the *efer ha-parah*. If a *kohen* who is already *tamei* does not transgress by further contact with the dead, the halachic implications are significant. This is the question to which the Gemara now turns.

One further nuance deserves mention before entering the Gemara. If a person touches a dead body on Sunday and then touches another dead body on Monday, there is a real-world effect: his *parah adumah* purification timeline has been reset. The ashes would have been sprinkled on him Tuesday had he not touched again on Monday; now he must wait anew. But if he touches a second dead body on Sunday — the same day — his *tumah* level has not meaningfully changed, and no practical consequence follows. The Gemara will build on this distinction.

## Rava: Tumah status

### Two prohibitions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיתְּמַר, אָמַר רַבָּה אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מִקְרָא מָלֵא דִּבֵּר הַכָּתוּב: ״לֹא יִטַּמָּא״. כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״לֹא יָבֹא״ — לְהַזְהִירוֹ עַל הַטּוּמְאָה, לְהַזְהִירוֹ עַל הַבִּיאָה. אֲבָל טוּמְאָה וְטוּמְאָה — לֹא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: A dispute among *amora'im* **was stated. Rabba said** that **Rav Huna said: The Torah stated** a *halakha* involving a nazirite in **a categorical verse: "He shall not become impure** for his father, or for his mother, for his brother, or for his sister, when they die" (Numbers 6:7). This includes all manners of contracting impurity imparted by a corpse, whether ritual impurity imparted by contact, by carrying, or in a tent, i.e., a corpse under the same roof. **When** the Torah **states: "He shall not come** near to a dead body" (Numbers 6:6), it serves **to warn him with regard to** contracting **impurity** from a corpse in any manner, as above, and **to warn him with regard to entering** an enclosure with a corpse, which is a unique prohibition applicable to a nazirite that is added by the phrase "He shall not come near to a dead body," and he is liable separately for each. **However,** with regard to one contracting **impurity** from a corpse **and** again contracting **impurity** from a corpse, the verse does **not** warn him, and he is liable to receive only one set of lashes.

The Gemara introduces a dispute through the formulation *amar Rava amar Rav Huna: mikra malei diber ha-katuv* — the Torah speaks fully, meaning that two distinct prohibitions are stated explicitly. The relevant verses for the *kohen* and *nazir* include both *lo yitama* and *lo yavo al kol nafshot meis*. The *lo yitama* verse prohibits the state of becoming *tamei*; the *lo yavo* verse prohibits the act of entering under the same roof as a corpse. The Gemara's point is that these are conceptually distinct *lavin*: a *nazir* who enters a house containing a corpse, having been warned separately about each prohibition, transgresses both and receives two sets of lashes. Touching a corpse without entering a house would yield only one.

### State vs. act

Rava explains the structure: *l'hazhir al ha-tuma l'hazhir al ha-bia* — one prohibition targets the state of *tumah*, the other targets the act of entry. If a *nazir* was warned about both before entering a house with a corpse, and he entered, he transgresses *lo yitama* and *lo yavo* simultaneously — two *lavin*, two sets of lashes for one physical act. This is because there is no way to enter such a house without violating both. The two prohibitions are conceptually distinguishable even when they are practically inseparable.

### Rava: No repeat lashes

> But the critical ruling of Rava concerns the case of *tumah* following *tumah*: *aval tuma v'tuma lo*. If a *nazir* is already *tamei* and he touches another dead body — whether simultaneously with the first contact or after removing his hand and touching again — Rava holds that he does not receive an additional set of lashes. The reasoning follows from the framing of *lo yitama*: the prohibition concerns achieving the state of *tumah*. If the state was already present, the subsequent contact has not changed the halachic reality. However actively he may have touched a second corpse, he has not become *tamei* in any new sense.

## Rav Yosef: Repeat acts

### Yosef: separate acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: הָאֱלֹהִים! אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: אֲפִילּוּ טוּמְאָה וְטוּמְאָה. דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: נָזִיר שֶׁהָיָה עוֹמֵד בְּבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת, וְהוֹשִׁיטוּ לוֹ מֵתוֹ וּמֵת אַחֵר וְנָגַע בּוֹ — חַיָּיב. אַמַּאי? הָא מִיטַּמֵּא וְקָאֵים! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: אֲפִילּוּ טוּמְאָה וְטוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yosef says** in the form of an oath: **By God! Rav Huna** actually **says** that he is separately liable **even** for contracting **impurity** from a corpse **and** again contracting **impurity** from a corpse, not only if he entered an enclosure with a corpse. **As Rav Huna says:** With regard to **a nazirite who was standing in a cemetery,** who is already ritually impure, **and they extended his corpse,** i.e., the corpse of his relative, **to him, and** similarly if they extended **a different corpse** to him **and he touched it,** he is **liable.** But **why** is he liable; he **has already become impure and is standing** in his state of impurity? **Rather, isn't it** correct to **conclude from** this that **Rav Huna said** he is separately liable **even** for contracting **impurity** from a corpse **and** again contracting **impurity** from a corpse?

Rav Yosef disagrees sharply, invoking an exclamation — *ha Elokim!* — before citing Rav Huna's actual ruling: *afilu tumah v'tumah*, even *tumah* that follows *tumah*, incurs separate lashes. This applies not merely when a *nazir* removes his hand from the first corpse and then touches a second, but even while he remains in contact with the first body. For Rav Yosef, each distinct act of touching — accompanied by a separate warning — constitutes a separately punishable transgression, regardless of the actor's ongoing *tumah* status. The prohibition is not only about the resulting state; it is also, in some sense, about the act itself. Just as a separate warning before cutting another hair generates separate *malkus*, so too a separate warning before touching another corpse should generate separate *malkus*, even if the person is already *tamei*.

An analogy illustrates the point: imagine a person in the process of cutting hair from one side of his head, and while still engaged in that act, he is warned before cutting hair from another section and proceeds. One might well say that those are separate acts generating separate *malkus*. Rav Yosef reasons similarly about *tumah*.

### Cemetery case

Rav Huna's concrete formulation of this principle is presented: *nazir she-hayah omed b'veit ha-kevarot v'hoshitu lo met acher v'naga bo, chayav* — a *nazir* standing in the cemetery, already *tamei*, who is handed another corpse and touches it, is nevertheless liable for a separate set of lashes. Note that by a *nazir*, unlike a *kohen*, even touching an immediate relative is prohibited — a *nazir* has the stringency of a *kohen gadol* in this regard. The case of the relative is mentioned only because it describes a common scenario; the halachic point is the same regardless of who the deceased is.

For Rav Yosef, the *nazir* in the cemetery who is already *tamei* and touches an additional corpse has performed a new, separately punishable act. The fact that his *tumah* status did not change is irrelevant to his liability for the act itself.

### Sources conflict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: כֹּהֵן שֶׁהָיָה לוֹ מֵת מוּנָּח עַל כְּתֵיפוֹ, וְהוֹשִׁיטוּ לוֹ מֵתוֹ וּמֵת אַחֵר וְנָגַע בּוֹ, יָכוֹל יְהֵא חַיָּיב — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְלֹא יְחַלֵּל״, בְּמִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְחוּלָּל, יָצָא זֶה שֶׁהוּא מְחוּלָּל וְעוֹמֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to** Rav Yosef from a *baraita*: With regard to **a priest who had a corpse placed on his shoulder, and they extended his corpse,** i.e., the corpse of his relative, **to him, and** similarly if they extended **a different corpse** to him **and he touched it,** one **might** have thought that **he should be liable** even for this contact. Therefore, **the verse states,** with regard to the prohibition against a High Priest becoming impure: **"And he shall not profane** the Sanctuary of his God" (Leviticus 21:12). This teaches that the prohibition of impurity applies **to one who is not** yet **profaned, excluding this one who is** already **profaned and standing** in that state of ritual impurity.

The Gemara now presents sources that appear to cut in opposite directions. *Abiaye* challenges Rav Yosef from a *braita*: a *kohen* who had a corpse on his shoulder — already *tamei* — and was handed another corpse and touched it, is not liable, because *lo yechalel* applies only *bemi she-eino mechuhlal*, to one whose status has not yet been violated. One who is *mechuhal v'omed*, already in a state of violated status, is excluded. This appears to support Rava's position against Rav Yosef.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְתִיקְשֵׁי לָךְ מַתְנִיתִין, דִּתְנַן: הָיָה מִיטַּמֵּא לְמֵתִים כׇּל הַיּוֹם — אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. אָמְרוּ לוֹ ״אַל תִּטַּמֵּא״ ״אַל תִּטַּמֵּא״ — חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת. וְאַמַּאי? הָא מִיטַּמֵּא וְקָאֵים!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef **said to him: But** if, as you claim, one is not liable for contracting one impurity after another, **the mishna should pose a difficulty for you. As we learned** in the mishna: If a nazirite **became ritually impure from corpses** many times **all day, he is liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes. **If they said to him: Do not become impure, do not become impure,** and he continues to become impure, **he is liable for each and every** time he was warned. **But why** should this be so? **He has already become impure and is standing** in his state of impurity.

Rav Yosef responds: *v'tifshut lach matnitin* — the Mishna supports me. The Mishna states explicitly that a *nazir* who became *tamei l'meitim* all day, with separate warnings before each contact, is *chayav al kol achat v'achat*. And the Gemara presses: *v'amai? Ha-mitamei ve-ka'ei!* Why should he be liable? He is already *tamei*! This is Rav Yosef's proof that even while already *tamei*, separate acts with separate warnings incur separate liability.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא קַשְׁיָא אַהֲדָדֵי! לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — בְּחִיבּוּרִין. כָּאן — שֶׁלֹּא בְּחִיבּוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the mishna and *baraita* pose **a difficulty for each other.** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult,** as **here** the *baraita* is referring **to a concurrent** contact with impurity, i.e., when he touched the second corpse he was still in contact with the first, so he is not liable for the second impurity. Conversely, **there** the mishna is referring **to** impurity that was **not a concurrent** contact. He touched the second corpse only after he had separated himself from the first, and therefore he is liable for each impurity.

The result is a direct contradiction between sources: the *braita* supports Rava, the Mishna supports Rav Yosef. *Ella kashya hadadei* — they stand in tension with each other.

## Active vs. removed

### Av hatumah levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְטוּמְאָה בְּחִיבּוּרִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא? הָא אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: לֹא אָמְרוּ טוּמְאָה בְּחִיבּוּרִין אֶלָּא לִתְרוּמָה וְקָדָשִׁים, אֲבָל לְנָזִיר וְעוֹשֵׂה פֶסַח — לָא. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, מַאי שְׁנָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** this *halakha*, that a nazirite is exempt from being flogged for a second contact with a corpse in a case of **concurrent impurity,** does it apply **by Torah law? Didn't Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef say** that **Rabbi Yannai said: They stated** the principle of **concurrent impurity only with regard to** partaking of ***teruma* and consecrated** foods, i.e., that one who touches a person who is in contact with a corpse is ritually impure with impurity imparted by a corpse for seven days. **However, with regard to a nazirite,** i.e., the question of whether a nazirite is considered ritually impure and has to bring offerings due to this contact, **and** with regard to **one who performs** the ritual of **the Paschal offering,** this *halakha* does **not** apply. **And if you say** that this *halakha* applies **by Torah law, what is different** between the case of *teruma* and the case of a nazirite?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּאן, בְּחִיבּוּרֵי אָדָם בְּאָדָם. כָּאן, בְּחִיבּוּרֵי אָדָם בְּמֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that there are two different types of concurrent impurity. **Here,** where there is a difference between *teruma* and a nazirite, it is referring to **concurrent** contact of one **person with** another **person.** If one touched another while the other was in contact with a corpse, the impurity of the first is by rabbinic law. By contrast, **there** it is referring **to concurrent** contact of **a person with a corpse.** One who is touching a corpse is considered linked to impurity by Torah law with regard to his second contact with a corpse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל טוּמְאָה וְטוּמְאָה לָא, דְּהָא מִיטַּמֵּא וְקָאֵים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara summarizes: The fact that one who is touching a corpse is not liable for contact with a second corpse leads to Rabba's aforementioned ruling: **However,** with regard to contracting **impurity** from a corpse **and** again contracting **impurity** from a corpse, i.e., if a person contracted impurity imparted by a corpse and then touched another corpse while still in contact with the first corpse, he is **not** liable for the second impurity, **as he has already become impure and is standing** in his state of ritual impurity.

The Gemara's resolution hinges on a distinction in *tumah* levels. To understand it, some background is necessary. A corpse has the status of *avi avot ha-tumah* — the source of sources of impurity. Normally, an *av ha-tumah* (such as a *zav* or *niddah*) conveys seven-day *tumah* to objects but only one-day *tumah* to persons who touch it (who become *rishon*). By a *meis*, the *tumah* is more severe: the person who touches it becomes an *av ha-tumah* himself — a seven-day *tamei* requiring *parah adumah* purification.

### Tumah bechiburin

This leads to the concept of *tumah bechiburin*: chain impurity while actively in physical contact. If person A is touching the corpse, and person B is touching person A while A remains in contact with the corpse, then person B, by virtue of this daisy-chain of contact, also attains a status of seven-day *tumah* as though he himself were touching the corpse. This elevation of B's status — from what would ordinarily be one-day *tumah* to seven-day *tumah* — is called *tumah bechiburin*.

Crucially, this elevated status exists only *while the contact is maintained*. The moment person A removes his hand from the corpse, person B reverts to his ordinary *rishon* status. The resolution now becomes clear: *lo kashya — kan bechiburin, kan she-lo bechiburin*. When the *braita* says the *kohen* is not liable for touching a second corpse while already holding the first, that is the case *bechiburin* — he is actively in contact with the first body, his *tumah* level is at its maximum, and touching another body changes nothing about his status. When the Mishna says he *is* liable for separate contacts with separate warnings, that is the case *she-lo bechiburin* — he has removed his hand, his *tumah* level has dropped slightly (from something approaching *avi avot* back to *av*), and touching again represents an actual change in his status. Hence in that case, separate *malkus* applies.

### Goses proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְטוּמְאָה בְּחִיבּוּרִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא? הָא אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: לֹא אָמְרוּ טוּמְאָה בְּחִיבּוּרִין אֶלָּא לִתְרוּמָה וְקָדָשִׁים, אֲבָל לְנָזִיר וְעוֹשֵׂה פֶסַח — לָא. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, מַאי שְׁנָא? כָּאן, בְּחִיבּוּרֵי אָדָם בְּאָדָם. כָּאן, בְּחִיבּוּרֵי אָדָם בְּמֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** this *halakha*, that a nazirite is exempt from being flogged for a second contact with a corpse in a case of **concurrent impurity,** does it apply **by Torah law? Didn't Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef say** that **Rabbi Yannai said: They stated** the principle of **concurrent impurity only with regard to** partaking of ***teruma* and consecrated** foods, i.e., that one who touches a person who is in contact with a corpse is ritually impure with impurity imparted by a corpse for seven days. **However, with regard to a nazirite,** i.e., the question of whether a nazirite is considered ritually impure and has to bring offerings due to this contact, **and** with regard to **one who performs** the ritual of **the Paschal offering,** this *halakha* does **not** apply. **And if you say** that this *halakha* applies **by Torah law, what is different** between the case of *teruma* and the case of a nazirite? The Gemara explains that there are two different types of concurrent impurity. **Here,** where there is a difference between *teruma* and a nazirite, it is referring to **concurrent** contact of one **person with** another **person.** If one touched another while the other was in contact with a corpse, the impurity of the first is by rabbinic law. By contrast, **there** it is referring **to concurrent** contact of **a person with a corpse.** One who is touching a corpse is considered linked to impurity by Torah law with regard to his second contact with a corpse.

The Gemara immediately asks: *v'tumah bechiburin d'Oraita?* Is the elevated status during *bechiburin* a biblical phenomenon? If so, the resolution above works cleanly. But there is a proof that it may be only rabbinic. The Gemara cites a ruling: *lo amru tumah bechiburin ela l'terumah v'kodashim, aval l'nazir v'oseh pesach lo*. The elevated seven-day status that person B acquires by touching A while A touches the corpse applies for purposes of *terumah* and *kodashim*, but not for *nezirut* or *korban pesach*. If B is a *nazir* or needs to bring a *korban pesach*, we tell him he is only a *rishon* — one-day *tamei* — and he need not restart his *nezirut* or forfeit his *pesach*.

This concession strongly implies that the elevated *bechiburin* status is not biblical, since we are willing to set it aside in high-stakes situations. If it were *d'Oraita*, we could not do so. The Gemara presses: *ve'amarta d'Oraita — mai shena?* If you say it is biblical, why distinguish? Clearly it is not fully *d'Oraita*.

The Gemara's response: *kan bechiburei adam b'adam, kan bechiburei adam b'meis*. There is a difference between the case of person B touching person A who touches the corpse (*chiburei adam b'adam*) and the case of person A directly touching the corpse (*chiburei adam b'meis*). Person A's seven-day status when directly touching the corpse is *d'Oraita*. Person B's derived seven-day status, one step removed, is *d'Rabbanan* — and therefore we set it aside for *nazir* and *pesach*. It is only the third person in the chain (person C touching person B who touches person A who touches the corpse) whose elevated status is purely *d'Rabbanan*. This is the reading of *Tosafot*; the *Rambam* reads the Gemara's conclusion differently, maintaining that even person B's elevated status is ultimately *d'Rabbanan*, though he nonetheless agrees that touching another corpse while actively in contact with the first does not incur additional *malkus*.

### Resolution: contact vs.…

To summarize the resolution as Rava frames it: during active contact with a corpse, the person's *tumah* level is at its maximum — essentially elevated to a status approaching *avi avot* for purposes of biblical law. Touching another corpse at that moment does not change his status and therefore does not trigger additional liability. But once he removes his hand, his *tumah* level diminishes (he is now an *av* rather than the elevated status of *bechiburin*), and subsequently touching another corpse represents a genuine change — a new act of *tumah* in the *d'Oraita* sense — for which he incurs a separate set of lashes with a separate warning.

This, according to the simple reading of the Gemara, is how Rava and Rav Yosef divide: Rava says that after removing his hand and touching again, he *is* liable; while actively touching he is not. Rav Yosef says he is liable even while actively touching.

## Lo yavo analysis

### Gemara's question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טוּמְאָה וּבִיאָה נָמֵי, הָא מִיטַּמֵּא וְקָאֵי! אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כָּאן בְּבַיִת, כָּאן בְּשָׂדֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, with regard to **impurity and entering** one should say **likewise,** that when he enters an enclosure containing a corpse when he is in contact with a corpse, **he has already become impure and is standing** in his impurity. Why should he be flogged again for entering the enclosure? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** In the case of impurity and entering one must again distinguish between two instances: **Here,** where Rav Huna said that one will be liable for both contractions of impurity, it is referring to one who was pure and who went **into a house** that contained a corpse, rendering him liable twice. The entering the house and the ritual impurity imparted by a corpse in a tent, i.e., to that which is under the same roof, occurred simultaneously, and therefore he is liable twice, once for contracting ritual impurity and once for violating the particular prohibition against a nazirite entering an enclosure with a corpse in it. Whereas **there,** where he is liable only once, it is referring to one who was **in a field.** In other words, if a nazirite touched a corpse in a field and subsequently entered an enclosure with a corpse in it while he was still in contact with the first corpse, he is not liable separately for that entering, as he was already ritually impure.

The Gemara now raises a sharp question about the parallel between *tumah* and *bi'ah* (entering a house containing a corpse). We established that according to Rava, a *nazir* who is actively touching one dead body and then touches another is not liable — because his *tumah* level was already at its maximum and touching again does not change it. The Gemara asks: *tumah u-vi'ah nami — ha-mitamei ve-ka'ei?* By the same logic, why should a *nazir* who is carrying a corpse on his shoulder and then walks into a house be liable for *lo yavo*? He is already *tamei*! If the rationale for exempting the person who touches twice is that his *tumah* status has not changed, should not the same rationale exempt him when he walks into a house while already actively *tamei*?

*Tosafot* frames the question carefully: the case parallel to *tumah-tumah* for the *bi'ah* category is a person who has a corpse on his shoulder and then enters a house containing another corpse. He was already *tamei* from the corpse on his shoulder, and now encounters *bi'ah* — why should he be liable for *lo yavo* when he has not changed his *tumah* status?

One might think the answer lies in the distinction between the two *lavin*: *lo yitama* is framed as a prohibition of achieving a status, whereas *lo yavo* is framed as a prohibition of performing an act of entering. But the Gemara does not rest with that answer; it probes more carefully.

### Hand vs. body

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּבַיִת נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּאַעֵיל יְדֵיהּ — אִיסְתָּאַב, כִּי עָיֵיל כּוּלֵּי, הַאי טָמֵא הוּא! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: צֵירַף יָדוֹ, מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה — אִיכָּא, מִשּׁוּם בִּיאָה — לֵיכָּא. וְצֵירַף גּוּפוֹ — טוּמְאָה וּבִיאָה בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי קָאָתוּ. הָא אִי אֶפְשָׁר דְּלָא עָיֵיל חוֹטְמוֹ בְּרֵישָׁא, וְנָחֵית לֵיהּ טוּמְאָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to one who enters **into a house too,** why should he be liable twice? Since one typically enters a place with his hands before his body, **once he inserts his hand he** immediately **becomes ritually impure.** This means that **when all** the rest **of him enters, this** person **is** already **impure.** **Rather, Rabbi Elazar said:** If **he inserted his hand** into the house first, **there is** liability **due to** contracting **ritual impurity;** however, **there is no** liability **due to entering** the enclosure. **But** if **he joined his body** and his hands, i.e., all of him entered at once, contraction of **impurity and entering** the enclosure **occur simultaneously,** and in that case he is liable twice. The Gemara objects: **It is impossible** that **his nose would not enter first, and** once it does, **impurity would descend to it** and thereby to this person immediately, before the rest of his body entered the house.

Rabbi Yochanan's first approach cites Rabbi Elazar: *kan ba-bayit, kan ba-sadeh*. But the Gemara objects to Rabbi Elazar's analysis. *Tosafot* reads this as follows. When we said that entering a house generates two sets of lashes — one for *lo yitama* and one for *lo yavo* — we meant a case where the person was not already *tamei* through active contact; both prohibitions struck simultaneously as he entered. The analogous case for *tumah-tumah* in the field is touching two corpses at the same instant — which yields only one set of lashes. And conversely, if a person is actively carrying a corpse on his shoulder and then enters a house containing another corpse, he would indeed not be liable for a second set, because his *tumah* was already present and active.

But the Gemara presses further: *be-vayit nami* — even by the house, once any part of a person's body enters the space under the roof, he becomes *tamei* from the corpse inside. And the prohibition of *lo yavo* is not complete until the majority of his body has entered. So the moment his hand or foot crosses the threshold, he is *tamei*, and by the time his whole body is inside — which is when *lo yavo* is transgressed — he is already in an active state of *tumah*. By Rava's logic, this should exempt him from liability for *lo yavo*! Why does he get two sets of lashes?

### Box case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: הִכְנִיס יָדוֹ, מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה — אִיכָּא, מִשּׁוּם בִּיאָה — לֵיכָּא. הִכְנִיס גּוּפוֹ — טוּמְאָה וּבִיאָה בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי קָא אָתְיָין. וְהָא אִי אֶפְשָׁר דְּלָא עָיֵיל אֶצְבְּעָתָא דְכַרְעֵיהּ בְּרֵישָׁא וְנָחֵת לְהוּ טוּמְאָה! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּכְנַס בְּשִׁידָּה תֵּיבָה וּמִגְדָּל, וּבָא חֲבֵירוֹ וּפָרַע עָלָיו אֶת הַמַּעֲזִיבָה, דְּטוּמְאָה וּבִיאָה בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי קָאָתְיָין. מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: כְּגוֹן דְּעָיֵיל כְּשֶׁהוּא גּוֹסֵס, וּנְפַק נִשְׁמְתֵיהּ אַדְּיָתֵיב. דְּטוּמְאָה וּבִיאָה בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי קָאָתְיָין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** If **he entered** with only **his hand there is** liability **due to** contracting **ritual impurity,** but **there is no** liability **due to entering** an enclosure with a corpse, as he cannot be considered inside the house. If **he entered** with **his body** by standing upright so that his head would not enter first, contraction of **impurity and entering** the enclosure **occur simultaneously.** The Gemara asks: **But** even so, **it is impossible** that **his toes would not enter first, and** once they do, **impurity would** thereby **descend to them,** causing him to become impure before his entire body enters the house. **Rather, Rav Pappa said:** We are dealing with a case **where one entered** into a house **in a chest, a box, or a cabinet,** which are not susceptible to ritual impurity and which protect their contents from impurity when they can hold more than forty *se'a*, **and another came and opened the cover** of the vessel **from over him.** In **that** case contracting **impurity and entering** the enclosure **occur simultaneously. Mar bar Rav Ashi said:** It is referring to a case **where one entered** the house **when** someone there **was dying, and** the latter's **soul departed when** he **was sitting** there. In **that** case too, contracting **impurity and entering** the enclosure with a corpse **occur simultaneously.** Since there was no corpse in the enclosure when he entered, he is considered to have entered an enclosure with a corpse at the moment the person died.

Rabbi Elazar offers one resolution: *tzireif yadav* — if the person entered with his hand pressed flat against his body, and *tzireif gufo* — the entire body entered at once as a single unit, then *tumah u-vi'ah be-hadi hadadi ka'atyan* — the *tumah* and the act of *bi'ah* arrive simultaneously. In this case, neither precedes the other, and both *lavin* are transgressed together.

The Gemara objects: *i efshar d'lo ayikhu khotmo be-reisha* — it is impossible for the nose not to enter first! As soon as the nose crosses the threshold, *tumah* has been contracted, and the act of *bi'ah* is not yet complete. So there is always a tiny interval between the *tumah* and the *bi'ah*, and by the time *lo yavo* is transgressed, the person is already actively *tamei*.

*Tosafot* notes that in their reading, entering backwards would be *she-lo ke-darko* — an abnormal mode of entry — and *lo yavo* requires normal entry to be transgressed, so walking backwards would not solve the problem halachically.

Rav Pappa then offers the celebrated case: *kegon she-nichnas be-shida, teiva u-migdal* — a person who climbed into a box or trunk, and a friend then ripped off the roof. In this case, when the roof is removed, the corpse's *tumah* is exposed simultaneously throughout the space, and the person inside is considered to have "entered" the space of the corpse at the same instant he contracts *tumah*. Thus *tumah u-vi'ah be-hadi hadadi ka'atyan* — they truly arrive together. *Tosafot* notes that for this case to generate *malkus*, the person inside must have been warned and must have been actively participating in breaking out — if his friend acted unilaterally, the person inside cannot be blamed.

### Goses case

Mar bar Rav Ashi presents another scenario in which the two violations can arrive simultaneously: *kegon de-ayel ke-she-hu goses* — the person entered the room while the occupant was *goses*, on the threshold of death, and the soul departed while he was remaining there. Since a *goses* does not yet transmit *tumah* — *tumah* requires the actual departure of the soul — when the person entered the room, he was not yet *tamei*. But the moment death arrived, both the *tumah* from the newly-created *meis* and the violation of *bi'ah* (by remaining present under the same roof) came simultaneously. This case requires, of course, that the *hatra'ah* be meaningful — hence the use of a *goses*, where death is anticipated imminently, lending substance to the warning.

## Goses impurity

### Lehachalo dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לְהֵחַלּוֹ״, עַד שָׁעָה שֶׁיָּמוּת. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: ״בְּמוֹתָם יִטַּמָּא״, עַד שֶׁיָּמוּת.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the ritual impurity of a corpse, **the Sages taught:** The Torah states concerning a priest's exposure to ritual impurity imparted by a corpse: "He shall not become impure, a chief among his people, **to profane himself"** (Leviticus 21:4), from which it is derived that the prohibition does not apply **until the time that** the person with whom he comes into contact **dies.** A priest does not become impure or profane his priesthood at any earlier stage. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says** that the verse stated with regard to a nazirite: "He shall not become impure for his father, or for his mother, for his brother, or for his sister, when they die" (Numbers 6:7), from which one can infer that **when they die,** one **contracts ritual impurity** from them, i.e., not **until** the other person actually **dies.**

A *Braita* is now cited: *lo yitama ba'al be'amav le-hachalo* — a *kohen* does not violate *until the person dies*, *ad she-yamut*. This is the view of the first *Tanna*: the violation is triggered only at death, not before. *Rebbi* cites *bemotam* — in their death — from the verse concerning the *nazir*, and derives the same conclusion: only at actual death does the prohibition take effect.

### Goses included

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַשְׁמָעוּת דּוֹרְשִׁין אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: גּוֹסֵס אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִ״לְּהֵחַלּוֹ״ — אֲפִילּוּ גּוֹסֵס, לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״בְּמוֹתָם״ — עַד שֶׁיָּמוּת אִין, גּוֹסֵס לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the difference **between** these two derivations? They apparently state the same *halakha* from different verses. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: The interpretation of the meaning** of the verse **is** the difference **between them.** There is no practical difference between them; rather, they derive the *halakha* from different verses. **Reish Lakish said:** The difference **between them** is with regard to **a dying person: According to the one who says** that one derives the *halakha* from "He shall not become impure, a chief among his people, **to profane himself," even a dying person** is included in the prohibition of impurity. **According to the one who says** that it is derived from **"when they die," once he dies, yes** there is impurity, whereas **a dying person, no,** he does not impart impurity.

Reish Lakish argues that there *is* a difference between the two *darshanim*: for the one who derives the rule from *le-hachalo*, even a *goses* is included in the prohibition. The phrase *le-hachalo* — "to desecrate" — is future-oriented, suggesting that putting oneself in a situation that will result in desecration is itself prohibited. It may also carry the sense of entering a situation of *chillul* even before death has occurred. For the one who derives it from *bemotam*, however, the rule is stricter in its semantic limitation: *ad she-yamut* — until he actually dies. The *goses* is not yet a *meis* and does not yet convey *tumah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִ״לְּהֵחַלּוֹ״, הָכְתִיב ״בְּמוֹתָם״! מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַבִּי. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: בְּמוֹתָם אֵינוֹ מִטַּמֵּא, אֲבָל מִטַּמֵּא בְּנִגְעָתָם וּבְזִיבָתָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to Reish Lakish's opinion: **And according to the one who says** that it is derived from **"to profane himself," isn't it written: "When they die"?** What does he derive from that verse? The Gemara answers: **He requires** that verse **for that which** was taught **by Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says** that the verse stresses "when they die," to teach: In a case **when they die he may not become impure; however, he may become impure from their leprosy or from their gonorrhea-like discharge.** A nazirite is prohibited from contracting ritual impurity only if it is from a corpse.

Rav Yochanan disputes Reish Lakish's reading, arguing that there is no practical difference between the two derivations: both exclude the *goses* from triggering *tumah*, and the difference is only in which verse provides the source.

### Death requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״בְּמוֹתָם״, הָא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְהַאי סְבָרָא! אִם כֵּן, לֵימָא קְרָא ״בְּמוֹת״, מַאי ״בְּמוֹתָם״ — שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי. וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״בְּמוֹתָם״, הָכְתִיב ״לְהֵחַלּוֹ״! ״לְהֵחַלּוֹ״ לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא, בְּמִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְחוּלָּל, יָצָא זֶה שֶׁמְּחוּלָּל וְעוֹמֵד. מֵיתִיבִי: אָדָם אֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא (אֶלָּא) עַד שֶׁתֵּצֵא נַפְשׁוֹ. וַאֲפִילּוּ מְגוּיָּיד וַאֲפִילּוּ גּוֹסֵס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks the reverse question: **And according to the one who says** that the source for the earliest time of the impurity of a corpse is the verse **"when they die," isn't it written: "To profane himself"?** What does he derive from that verse? The Gemara answers: **"To profane himself" comes for this** purpose, that the prohibition against becoming impure apply only **to one who is not profaned, excluding one who is** already **profaned.** There is no prohibition against a ritually impure priest becoming impure from a corpse. The Gemara **raises an objection** from a mishna (*Oholot* 1:6): **A person renders** others **impure only when his soul departs** from **him, even if he has severe lacerations [*meguyyad*], and even if** he **is dying.**

The Gemara then cites a *braita* that appears to resolve the matter: *adam eino mitamei ella ad she-teitzei nafsho, va'afilu meguyad va'afilu goses* — a person does not cause *tumah* until the soul departs, even if he has been critically wounded or is in his death throes. The *goses* is simply not yet a *meis* for purposes of *tumah*. This appears to refute any reading of *le-hachalo* that would include an actual *tumah* violation for the *goses*.

### Lehachalo vs tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִ״לְּהֵחַלּוֹ״ הָא קָתָנֵי דְּאֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא! לְעִנְיַן טַמּוֹיֵי — עַד דְּנָפְקָא נַפְשֵׁיהּ, לְעִנְיַן אִתַּחוֹלֵי — הָא אִיתַּחִיל.
>
> **Translation:** But according to the one who says** that the commencement of the impurity of a corpse is derived **from "to profane himself,"** this *baraita* is difficult, as it **teaches** that a dying person **does not impart impurity.** The Gemara answers: **With regard to imparting impurity,** he does not impart impurity **until his soul departs, but with regard to profaning** the sanctity of the priesthood, a priest **is profaned** by a dying person.

The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction. *Le-inyan tum'ei* — for the purpose of *tumah* transmission — yes, the person must actually die. But *le-inyan achlolei* — for the purpose of violating the prohibition of *chillul* of one's sacred status — *ha itchallei*: that violation begins when one enters the space of a *goses*. The two categories are distinct: the act of *tumah* and the act of *chillul* can be triggered at different moments.

*Tosafot* notes the uncertainty about how we *pasken* in practice. The *Shulchan Aruch* rules that a *goses* does not transmit *tumah* until the soul departs, but nonetheless rules that it is *assur* for a *kohen* to enter a house containing a *goses* — while noting that some permit it, and the *Rema* adds that it is good to be stringent. The precise level of this prohibition — whether *d'Oraita* or *d'Rabbanan*, and on which model — remains ambiguous in the *poskim*.

## Kohanim practice

### Tosafot: no repeat

*Tosafot* draws a direct practical conclusion from the Gemara's resolution. Even according to Rava's more lenient position — that one who is already *tamei* does not incur additional *malkus* for touching another corpse while actively in contact — this applies only during that active contact. Once a *kohen* has finished burying his relative and is no longer directly touching the body, he has reverted to a standard *av ha-tumah* status. Any further contact with the dead — touching another body, walking under a tree that encompasses a grave — constitutes a new and separately prohibited act. The *kohen* may not say: "I am already *tamei*, so I may as well visit other graves in the cemetery."

### Exit problem

This creates an immediate practical problem: how does a *kohen* exit a cemetery after burying a relative without violating further prohibitions? Walking out through a cemetery filled with graves, trees, and overhead obstructions creates endless opportunities for *ohel*-based *tumah*. The technical exemption of *bechiburin* — being actively in contact with one body — ceases the moment burial is complete.

### Edge burial

*Tosafot* records having heard from great authorities that this is precisely why it was the practice to bury the dead at the edge of the cemetery — so that the *kohen* who buried his relative could exit immediately without passing over additional graves or through areas that would constitute new violations. This is not merely a custom of convenience but a halachic accommodation to the constraints established by the Gemara's analysis of *tumah bechiburin* and its limits.

## Ra'avad reinterprets

### Rav Yosef: active only

The Ra'avad, in his glosses to the *Rambam*'s *Hilchos Nezirus*, announces: *amar Avraham — iyanti bi-shmu'ah be-Maseches Nazir*, I have carefully examined this sugya, and what I find there is not what everyone has assumed. The Ra'avad offers a revolutionary reinterpretation: the position that a *nazir* is exempt from additional lashes only *while actively touching* — the *bechiburin* exemption — is not the lenient position of Rava. It is the *strict* position of Rav Yosef. Rav Yosef, who said that even *tumah v'tumah* incurs separate lashes, only meant that this applies after one removes his hand and touches again. While actively in contact, even Rav Yosef concedes that no additional violation occurs.

### Rava: never repeat

According to the Ra'avad, Rava's position is the genuinely lenient one: Rava holds that even after removing one's hand and touching again — even many days later, when the *bechiburin* factor is long gone — no additional *malkus* is incurred. Because the person is already *tamei*, the prohibition of *lo yitama* simply does not apply to him again, regardless of how much time has passed or how many additional contacts he makes. The focus of *lo yitama* on *status* means that once the status is present, it cannot be re-violated — full stop.

### Ra'avad ruling

The Ra'avad then draws his halachic conclusion: *ve-khivan de-kaima lan Rava ve-Rav Yosef hilchesa ke-Rava* — since we rule like Rava against Rav Yosef, the ruling is that *tumah v'tumah, afilu peirish ve-chazar ve-naga, patur*. Even if a person removed his hand, waited, and touched again — he is exempt from *malkus*. He then applies this to the contemporary situation: *ve-ha-kohanim bi-zman ha-zeh temei mes hein* — *kohanim* in our time are all *tamei meis* — *ve-od ein aleihem chiyuv tumah* — and therefore they bear no further obligation of *tumah* prohibition with respect to additional contacts with the dead. *Ve-ha-mechayyev otam alav le-havi re'ayah* — whoever wishes to hold them liable bears the burden of proof.

### Modern application

The Ra'avad's position has obvious and significant implications. A word of caution is appropriate: some authorities read the Ra'avad as establishing *patur* — exemption from *malkus* — rather than *muttar* — full permission. On this reading, there may remain an *issur d'Oraita* or at least an *issur asei* even if lashes cannot be administered; the Ra'avad may only be saying that the person cannot be *mekhayyev* for lashes, not that the act is entirely permitted.

Nevertheless, the Ra'avad's reading has been invoked in discussions of whether a *kohen* may become a physician and thereby come into regular contact with deceased patients. The question of *kohen* doctors involves many additional considerations — questions of *pikuach nefesh*, of the nature of the prohibition in the absence of the *parah adumah* today, and of other grounds for leniency — but the Ra'avad's reinterpretation of this sugya remains a significant factor in that broader halachic conversation.
