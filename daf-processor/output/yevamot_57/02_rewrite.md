# Yevamot 57 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Forbidden Marriage S (II)

### Daf Overview

The present *daf* takes up from the bottom of 56b, at the *mishnah* there. The preceding *gemaras* addressed significant questions: the halachic definition of the act of *bi'ah*, and the status of a *kohen*'s wife who is raped and thereby forbidden to her husband — a *halacha* with profound and tragic consequences. The sugya now turns to the next *mishnah*, which continues the theme of personal status as it intersects with *kehuna*.

### Mishnah Introduction

The *mishnah* opens by enumerating cases of forbidden relationships involving *kohanim*: an *almanah* with a *kohen gadol*, or a *gerushah* or *chalutzah* with a *kohen hedyot* — all situations we know are forbidden at the stage of *nisuin*, when the couple has actually gone under the *chuppah* and consummated the marriage. Through that forbidden act of *bi'ah*, the woman not only transgresses a negative prohibition but also becomes a *chalalah*.

Why does *chalalah* status matter if she is already forbidden to a *kohen*? For an *almanah* forbidden to a *kohen gadol*, the consequence is that she becomes lost even to a *kohen hedyot*. And for a *gerushah* or *chalutzah* with a *kohen hedyot*, it matters in a different way: if she was a *bat kohen*, she would ordinarily be entitled to return to her father's household and continue eating *terumah* after her husband's death — provided she has no children and meets the other conditions. But if she has become a *chalalah*, she is disqualified even from eating *terumah* from her father's household, just as a male *kohen* who is a *chalal* loses his *kedushas kehunah*.

## Erusin vs Nisuin

### Rebbi Meir Position

All of the above consequences attach after the act of *bi'ah*. But the *mishnah* raises the case of *erusin* alone — *erusin* with a *kohen* in circumstances where the eventual *nisuin* would constitute an *issur*. The *tanna kamma* of the *mishnah*, whom the *gemara* will identify as Rebbi Meir, rules: *min ha-erusin lo yokhelu bi-trumah* — even during *erusin*, she may not eat *terumah*. Since *erusin* is but one step removed from *nisuin*, and there is no purpose to *erusin* unless one intends to proceed to *chuppah* and *nisuin*, Rebbi Meir treats the situation as though *bi'ah* is already imminent. She is therefore regarded as quasi-*chalalah* — at least rabbinically — and she cannot eat *terumah*.

## Forbidden Marriage S (II)

### Chalalah Status

Before proceeding to the opposing positions, the case requires clarification. There is a debate among the *rishonim* — reflecting a disagreement between Rashi and Tosafot — as to whether the *mishnah* speaks of a *yisraelit* married to a *kohen*, or of a *bat kohen* married to a *kohen*. If the subject is a *yisraelit*, one confronts a double difficulty: normally, a *yisraelit* cannot eat *terumah* until *nisuin*, not merely *erusin*, and furthermore, this is an *erusin* leading toward an *issur bi'ah*. It is difficult to see how anyone could permit her to eat *terumah* in such circumstances. Rashi nevertheless defends that reading, though we will not develop it here.

The more straightforward approach — that of Tosafot — reads the case as one involving a *bat kohen* married to a *kohen*. She is already entitled to eat *terumah* by virtue of her father's household. The question, therefore, is not what entitles her to eat, but whether something now disqualifies her. Does the *erusin* to a *kohen* — when it would eventually ripen into a forbidden *nisuin* — strip her of her existing entitlement to eat *terumah*?

## Erusin vs Nisuin (II)

### R. Eliezer & Shimon

Rebbi Eliezer and Rebbi Shimon disagree with Rebbi Meir and rule *makhshirin* — they permit her to eat. Their reasoning is that until *nisuin* actually occurs, until *bi'ah* actually takes place, she has not become a *chalalah*. She was eating *terumah* by virtue of her father, and that entitlement continues. The *kiddushin* is not *nisuin*; there has been no *chuppah*, no *bi'ah*, and therefore no disqualification.

### Bat Kohen Case

The *gemara* then addresses the transitions in this woman's status across the different stages of the relationship. The positions of Rebbi Eliezer and Rebbi Shimon hold that she may continue eating by virtue of her father. Rebbi Meir holds she cannot. The husband's *kohen* status, it bears noting, is not at issue — *terumah* entitlements are not a punishment; the question concerns how her own status has been affected.

### Position Analysis

Proceeding from *erusin* to *nisuin* clarifies matters further. *Nis'almanah o nisgareshah min hanisuin pesulah min haterumah* — if she becomes an *almanah* or *gerushah* after *nisuin*, she is fully disqualified. Once they have actually gone under the *chuppah* and consummated the marriage, she truly becomes a *chalalah*. Even if the husband dies the next moment, she remains a *chalalah* and cannot eat *terumah*. This is the universal position; everyone agrees to it.

## Gittin Status

### Post-Nisuin Chalalah

Once the relationship has reached the stage of *nisuin* — actual *chuppah* and *bi'ah* — the woman's disqualification as a *chalalah* is complete and permanent. The *gemara* will later discuss what happens in the case of *chuppah* without *bi'ah*, but in the standard case of *nisuin* with consummation, the *chalalah* status is irrevocable. Even subsequent death or divorce of the *kohen* does not restore her.

### Erusin Only Reversal

However, *min ha-erusin kesherot* — if the *kohen* dies during *erusin* alone, before *nisuin* has taken place, the women are *kesherot*, their status is restored. Presumably this holds even according to Rebbi Meir, who disqualifies her during *erusin*. His reasoning was that *kiddushin* places her on the cusp of forbidden *bi'ah* — but if that *bi'ah* never materializes, the anticipated disqualification never crystallizes, and she reverts to her prior acceptable status.

## Kal Vachomer Debate

### Meir's Argument

A *baraita* records Rebbi Meir's *kal vachomer* argument. He reasons: *u'mah kiddushei reshut ein ma'achilin, kiddushei averah lo kol sheken?* — if *kiddushei reshut*, the case of a *bat kohen* who marries a *yisrael*, removes her from her father's household and disqualifies her from eating *terumah*, then *kiddushei averah* — a betrothal leading toward a forbidden *bi'ah* — should certainly do so. When a *bat kohen* marries a *yisrael*, she is immediately pulled out of her father's category; she cannot eat *terumah* as a result of those *kiddushin*. How much more so, argues Rebbi Meir, should *kiddushin* to a *kohen* in a context of *issur* pull her out.

### Refutation Logic

The response to Rebbi Meir's *kal vachomer* is striking. The refutation is: *im amarta b'kiddushei reshut sheken ein lo lehachil b'makom acher* — in the case of *kiddushei reshut*, the *yisrael* cannot enable anyone to eat *terumah* on his account under any circumstances. *Tomar b'kiddushei averah sheken yesh lo lehachil b'makom acher?* — but in the case of *kiddushei averah*, this *kohen*, if he were married to another woman permissibly, she would eat *terumah* on his account. Being married to a *kohen* — even in a context of *issur* — is categorically different from being married to a *yisrael*.

### Middle Zone

The logic of the refutation reflects a subtle and counterintuitive principle: although this marriage, if consummated, would produce a full *issur* and make her a *chalalah*, the fact that she is betrothed to a *kohen* rather than a *yisrael* keeps her within the *kehuna* realm during *erusin*. She has not yet had *bi'ah*, and so has not yet become a *chalalah*; and she has not married a *yisrael*, so she has not been pulled out of her father's household. She exists in a middle zone — within the *kohen* realm but not yet disqualified by *bi'ah*.

The underlying structure is this: normally, *erusin* — even to a *yisrael* — is enough to pull a *bat kohen* away from her father's household, even though *nisuin* has not yet occurred. She does not yet eat on her husband's account, but she can no longer eat on her father's account either. And yet, Rebbi Eliezer and Rebbi Shimon maintain that being betrothed to a *kohen* — however forbidden the ultimate *nisuin* would be — keeps her within the *terumah* realm. Marriage to a *kohen*, even a forbidden marriage, is better than marriage to a *yisrael* for purposes of eating *terumah*. The *chalalah* status has not occurred, and she remains, during this middle zone, able to eat *terumah* by virtue of her father.

## Ptzua Daka Case

### Oshaya's Position

The *gemara* then extends this framework. Rebbi Elazar reports in the name of Rebbi Oshaya: if a *kohen* who is a *ptzua daka* — one with crushed testicles, forbidden to marry — betroths a *bat kohen* (reading the *girsa* with Tosafot, not *bat yisrael* as Rashi had it), the same three-way *machloket* between Rebbi Meir, Rebbi Eliezer, and Rebbi Shimon applies. The *ptzua daka kohen*, if the relationship were to become *nisuin* and were consummated, would make her a *chalalah*. The parallel to our *mishnah*'s case is exact: the problem is not her status but his, yet the consequences of consummation are identical.

### Problem Distinction

The *gemara* immediately interrogates this parallelism. Perhaps Rebbi Eliezer and Rebbi Shimon said what they said in the original case only because *yesh lo lehachil b'makom acher* — in that case, the *kohen* is the sort of person whose wife, in a permissible marriage, would eat *terumah* on his account. That quality — the capacity of this type of *kohen* to cause someone to eat *terumah* through marriage — is what keeps her within the *terumah* realm even during the forbidden *erusin*. But a *ptzua daka* is one who, by definition, cannot legally have a wife who eats *terumah* on his account. Is he not therefore equivalent to a *yisrael* from this perspective? And if marriage to a *yisrael* removes her from her father's *terumah* category, should not *erusin* to a *ptzua daka kohen* do the same?

### Support Criterion

The *gemara* then asks: but can we not say *yesh lo lehachil* even for a *ptzua daka kohen*? Perhaps there exists a woman he could marry such that she would eat *terumah* on his account — specifically, a *bat gerim*. The question of whether a *ptzua daka kohen* marrying a *bat gerim* can enable her to eat *terumah* is, as the *gemara* records, precisely the question that Rebbi Yochanan posed to Rebbi Oshaya — and to which Rebbi Oshaya gave no answer. Therefore, Rebbi Oshaya's own assumption that the case of a *ptzua daka kohen* maps cleanly onto the three-way *machloket* is itself unresolved: we do not know whether this *kohen* is *yesh lo lehachil b'makom acher*, because we do not know the answer to Rebbi Yochanan's unanswered question.

## Abaye vs Rava

### Abaye Position

The *gemara* now records a dispute between Abaye and Rava. The specific scenario, as Rashi explains it, is a *kohen* who married a woman while he was still fully fit, and subsequently became a *ptzua daka*. He has not had *bi'ah* with her since becoming a *ptzua daka*. The question is whether she continues to eat *terumah* on his account.

### Rashi Explanation

Rashi's explanation of the case is that the *nisuin* was entirely valid at the time it was performed — the *kohen* was not yet a *ptzua daka* — and she therefore became a full *eshet kohen* and was eating *terumah*. Now that he has become a *ptzua daka*, if he were to have *bi'ah* with her he would make her a *chalalah*. But as long as he has not done so, she has not become a *chalalah*, and the marriage remains valid because it was contracted when he was still permitted. Abaye therefore rules: *ho'il u'machilah belo bi'ah* — he can cause her to eat even without having now had *bi'ah* with her. The relationship is legitimate and she continues to eat.

### Continuity Proof

Abaye's proof: one cannot simply say that what happened earlier dictates ongoing status. The *gemara* puts it this way — *v'i lo tema hachi*, if you say that an earlier state alone justifies the continuation, then a *bat yisrael* who was married to a *kohen* and the *kohen* died should continue to eat *terumah* on the logic that she was already eating it. Obviously we do not say that. This demonstrates that ongoing eating is not justified merely by prior status. There must therefore be something currently active in the relationship between a *bat kohen* and her *ptzua daka* husband that supports her eating — and Abaye identifies that as the fact that the *kinyan* of *ishut* between them remains intact.

### Rava Response

Rava responds by distinguishing the two cases. When the *kohen* dies, the relationship is severed entirely — *paka kinyano*, his bond lapses — and that is why she stops eating. Here, by contrast, the *kohen* has become a *ptzua daka* but has not died; the relationship, the *kinyan*, has not been severed — *lo paka kinyano*. She therefore continues to eat not merely on the basis of the past but because the ongoing *kinyan* remains in force. Abaye's rejoinder to Rava is that one should derive the law about the *kinyan* of *ishut* from another case of *kinyan* of *ishut*, not from the *kinyan* of slaves — *kinyan d'ishut mikinyan d'ishut yalfinan v'lo yalfinan kinyan d'ishut mikinyan d'avadim* — since Rava had proposed that the *ptzua daka kohen*'s *avadim* eating *terumah* demonstrates that he is *yesh lo lehachil b'makom acher*. Rava's response is that in the case where the *kohen* married before becoming a *ptzua daka*, she is eating on the basis of the continuation of the earlier relationship, and this does not demonstrate that a *ptzua daka* can newly enable his wife to eat.

## Bat Gerim Question

### Unresolved Question

Against this background the *gemara* returns to the question Rebbi Yochanan posed: *ptzua daka kohen she-nasa bat gerim mahu she-yachilenah bi-trumah?* — a *kohen* who is a *ptzua daka* and who married a *bat gerim* in a straightforward way (she is not herself a *giyoret*, and there is no *issur* from the *ptzua daka* side if she is indeed permitted to him) — can he enable her to eat *terumah*? This question remained unresolved.

### Oshaya's Silence

Rebbi Oshaya gave Rebbi Yochanan no answer. Sometime afterward, another great scholar — the *gemara* suggests this was Resh Lakish — came and posed a different question, and Rebbi Oshaya resolved it. This contrast prompted a rebuke from Rebbi Yehuda Nesia.

### Rebuke & Explanation

Rebbi Yehuda Nesia — the grandson of Rebbi Yehuda HaNasi, not Rebbi Yehuda HaNasi himself — said to Rebbi Oshaya: is Rebbi Yochanan not also a great person? Why did you answer Resh Lakish and not Rebbi Yochanan? Rebbi Oshaya responded: *de-ka bei minai milta de-lav pshita li* — Rebbi Yochanan asked me something I did not know the answer to. My silence was not a slight on his stature; it was because the question has no clear resolution. The *gemara* now examines why.

## Gerim Status Analysis

### Yehuda Position

The difficulty lies in what position one takes on several intersecting questions. The *gemara* frames it by examining the two major opinions — those of Rebbi Yehuda and Rebbi Yossi — and then a third, that of Rebbi Eliezer ben Yaakov.

The *gemara* begins: *ei Rebbi Yehuda bein bi-kdushateih kaei bein be-lav bi-kdushateih kaei lo achlah* — according to Rebbi Yehuda, whether the *ptzua daka kohen* retains his *kedushas kehunah* (meaning, whether the marriage restrictions of *kehunah* still apply to him) or not, the woman cannot eat.

There are two sub-questions embedded in the phrase *bi-kdushateih kaei*: first, whether a *kohen* who is a *ptzua daka* retains the *issurim* that govern whom a *kohen* may marry; and second, the *machloket* between Rebbi Yehuda and Rebbi Yossi regarding whether descendants of *gerim* — *kehal gerim* — are themselves still considered *kahal* for purposes of the restriction of *ptzua daka*.

If the *ptzua daka kohen* retains his *kedushas kehunah* in full, then one cannot solve the problem by having him marry a *bat ger*, because Rebbi Yehuda rules *bat ger ke-vat chalal zachar* — the daughter of a male *ger* is like the daughter of a *chalal*, and a *kohen* may not marry her, just as he may not marry a *gerushah* or *chalalah*. This is not the *halacha* as we rule, and one can only note what enormous implications such a ruling would have — a *kohen* would have to investigate any trace of a *ger* in a woman's lineage, through the father's line as well as the mother's, for all generations. But according to Rebbi Yehuda, it is so.

### Bat Ger Status

Thus, if the *kohen*'s *kedushas kehunah* remains operative, there is no one this *ptzua daka kohen* can marry who would be permitted by the *kohen* rules and yet would be permitted by the *ptzua daka* rules — at least not among the natural candidates.

### Yossi Position

Rebbi Yossi takes the opposite position: *bein bi-kdushateih kaei bein be-lav bi-kdushateih kaei achla* — either way she can eat. His reasoning on both flanks is the mirror image of Rebbi Yehuda's. As to the *kehuna* restriction: *ger she-nitgayer bito keshera li-kehuna* — a convert's daughter is fully *keshera* to marry a *kohen*. A *kohen* is forbidden to a *giyoret* herself, but her children, born after conversion, have no such restriction. And as to the *ptzua daka* restriction: *kehal gerim lo ikrei kahal* — the descendants of *gerim* are not considered *kahal Hashem* for purposes of the *ptzua daka* prohibition. A *ptzua daka* may marry into the second generation of converts.

### Status Paradox

The paradox Rebbi Yossi's position generates is notable. He holds that a *bat giyoret* is sufficiently a full Jew that a *kohen* may marry her — no residual *ger* status clings to her in that context. Yet simultaneously, she retains enough of a non-native status that she falls outside *kehal Hashem* and may marry a *ptzua daka*. Rebbi Yehuda's position produces the mirror-image paradox: a *bat ger* is still enough of a *ger* that a *kohen* cannot marry her — but she is enough of a *yisrael* that a *ptzua daka* cannot marry her either. Both *tanna'im* end up in an internally asymmetric position, though each in the opposite direction. According to Rebbi Yossi, the question posed by Rebbi Yochanan poses no difficulty — either way she can eat. According to Rebbi Yehuda, either way she cannot. The question is therefore answerable according to both — which only deepens the puzzle of why Rebbi Oshaya said he had no answer.

## Eliezer b. Yaakov

### Conditional Status

The resolution lies with the position of Rebbi Eliezer ben Yaakov. He occupies a middle position: *isha bat gerim lo tinase li-kehuna ad she-tei imah mi-Yisrael* — a woman of *gerim* descent may not marry a *kohen* unless her mother is a *yisraelit*. In other words, Rebbi Eliezer ben Yaakov distinguishes between the *bat ger* — whose father is a *ger* but mother is a *yisraelit* — and the *bat giyoret*, whose mother is a convert. The former may marry a *kohen*; the latter may not.

### Implication Question

Now, if Rebbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's rule is that a *yisraelit* mother enables the daughter to marry a *kohen*, the question becomes: what does that *yisraelit* mother add? Does it add *kashrut* in the sense of *kashrus li-kehunah* — fitness to marry a *kohen* — but nothing else? Or does it add *kedushah* — a higher level of *kehal Hashem* status — which would simultaneously make her *assur* to a *ptzua daka*? According to Rebbi Yehuda and Rebbi Yossi, the two categories (permitted to *kohen* / forbidden to *ptzua daka*) always move in tandem: what is forbidden to one is permitted to the other. But perhaps Rebbi Eliezer ben Yaakov contemplates a third category: a woman who, by virtue of her *yisraelit* mother, has been elevated to *kashrus* for *kehunah* — but not so elevated in *kedushah* as to become *assur* to a *ptzua daka*. This is the question Rebbi Oshaya could not resolve.

### Baraita Proof

The *gemara* then adduces a *baraita* brought by Rav Aha bar Katinah from the south. The *baraita* asks: *minayin le-ptzua daka kohen she-nasa bat gerim she-ma'achilah bi-trumah?* — how do we know that a *ptzua daka kohen* who married a *bat gerim* may enable her to eat *terumah*? It derives this from the verse *ve-kohen ki yikneh nefesh kinyan kaspo hi yokhal bo*. The *gemara* immediately asks: according to whose opinion is this *baraita* needed? According to Rebbi Yehuda — it contradicts his position, since he holds she cannot eat. According to Rebbi Yossi — no verse is needed, since either way she may eat. It must therefore be according to Rebbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, whose position left open the question of the *ptzua daka*'s case. And from the *baraita* we learn: *kashira hi ve-tosaf bah acher* — she is *kashira*, more fit to marry a *kohen*, but no additional *kedushah* is added to place her in the *kehal Hashem* category. Therefore the *ptzua daka kohen* can marry her and enable her to eat *terumah*.

## Eliezer b. Yaakov (II)

### Kashrah vs Kedushah

The upshot, then, is a three-way taxonomy. According to Rebbi Yehuda and Rebbi Yossi, the two axes — permitted to *kohen* / forbidden to *ptzua daka* — are perfectly correlated. What is *assur* on one side is *muttar* on the other. But Rebbi Eliezer ben Yaakov introduces a conceptual split: *kashrut* for *kehunah* and *kedushah* for the *ptzua daka* restriction are not identical measures. The *yisraelit* mother adds *kashrut* to the daughter — making her eligible to marry a *kohen* — without adding enough *kedushah* to remove her from the set of women a *ptzua daka* may marry. The *baraita* confirms this: the *ptzua daka kohen* who married a *bat gerim* (with *yisraelit* mother) enables her to eat *terumah*. *Shema mina.*

## Chuppah Pesulot

### Rav vs Shmuel

The *gemara* now turns to a new dispute. Rav holds *yesh chuppah le-pesulot* — there is a meaningful act of *chuppah* even for *pesulot*, women in forbidden marriages. Shmuel holds *ein chuppah le-pesulot* — the act of *chuppah* has no halachic significance in a forbidden relationship.

The question being asked requires some framing. There is a well-known debate among the *rishonim* about *chuppas niddah* — whether a *chuppah* performed when the woman is a *niddah* is halachically valid for purposes of *nisuin*, *sheva berachot*, and so on. Sefardim, following the Rambam, hold one may not; Ashkenazim hold one may. But that debate concerns whether *chuppah* in such circumstances is meaningful enough to constitute *nisuin*. The present dispute is effectively the reverse: given a situation where the parties are forbidden to ever consummate the marriage — an *almanah* to a *kohen gadol*, a *gerushah* to a *kohen hedyot*, a *ptzua daka* with a woman, and so on — does the act of *chuppah* without *bi'ah* carry any significance? Specifically: does it operate as though they have had *bi'ah*, so as to make her a *chalalah* and disqualify her from eating *terumah* from her father?

This is a ratcheted version of Rebbi Meir's *mishnah* position: Rebbi Meir held that mere *kiddushin* operates quasi-like *bi'ah*. Now we ask — even according to those who rejected Rebbi Meir, what about *chuppah* itself, which is more advanced than *erusin* but still without actual *bi'ah*?

### Rav Agrees Exception

Shmuel notes that Rav — whom Shmuel refers to as *Abba* (Tosafot notes, against Rashi's reading of this as an honorific, that *Abba* was simply Rav's actual name, as the *Aruch* explains) — agrees with him in one specific case: *betinoket p'chuta mi-bas shalosh shanim ve-yom echad* — a girl under three years old and one day. *Ho'il ve-ein lah bi'ah ein lah chuppah* — since an act of *bi'ah* with such a girl has no halachic standing, an act of *chuppah* similarly has no standing. Even Rav concedes this exception.

### Sex Possible Rule

The corollary is also spelled out: once a girl is three years old and one day — the threshold at which *bi'ah* has halachic effect — the act of *chuppah* does carry significance.

### Consequences List

Rava confirms this from the *mishnah*: *bat shalosh shanim ve-yom echad mitkadeshet be-vi'ah* — *kiddushei bi'ah* takes effect from this age. *Ve-im ba aleha yavam kana'ah* — an act of *yibbum* is effective at this age. *Ve-chayavin aleha mishum eshet ish* — if she was betrothed and someone has *bi'ah* with her, the laws of *eshet ish* apply. *U-metamah et bo'alah* — if she is a *niddah*, her *tumah* transfers to the man who has *bi'ah* with her, rendering the bedding beneath and above subject to *mishkav* and *moshav tumah*. *Niset le-kohen ochelet bi-trumah* — if she is married to a *kohen* in the context of valid *nisuin*, she eats *terumah*, because *nisuin* is only meaningful in a context where *bi'ah* is halachically possible. *Ba aleha echad mi-kol ha-pesulim paslah* — if one of the *pesulim* has *bi'ah* with her, he disqualifies her, rendering her a *chalalah*.

### Final Conclusion

The *baraita* draws the conclusion: *bat shalosh shanim ve-yom echad hu de-mipsala be-vi'ah mipsala be-chuppah* — it is at the age of three years and one day that *bi'ah* disqualifies her, and *chuppah* likewise disqualifies her at that age. *Ha-p'chuta mi-bas shalosh shanim ve-yom echad de-la mipsala be-vi'ah lo mipsala nami be-chuppah* — below that threshold, since *bi'ah* itself would not disqualify her, *chuppah* similarly does not disqualify her. *Shema mina.* The principle is clear: *chuppah* for *pesulot* carries halachic weight — it operates as *bi'ah* for purposes of *chalalah* status — but only where *bi'ah* itself would be halachically significant, i.e., where the girl is at least three years and one day old.
