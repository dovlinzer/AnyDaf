# Eruvin 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Courtyard Lechi Case

### Small Courtyard Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי הִיא, דְּאָמַר: בָּעִינַן שְׁנֵי פַּסִּין. דְּתַנְיָא: חָצֵר, נִיתֶּרֶת בְּפַס אֶחָד, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: בִּשְׁנֵי פַּסִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who said** that in order to permit carrying in a courtyard that was breached, **we require two** upright **boards,** one on either side of the breach. **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: If **a courtyard** was breached and opens into the public domain, and the width of the breach does not exceed ten cubits, it **becomes permitted** to carry there, even **with** only **one** upright **board** remaining on one side of the breach. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** It is permitted only **with two** upright **boards** remaining, one on each side of the breach.

We begin at the very top of 10a. The *Gemara* had been engaged in a lengthy discussion about whether a *lechi* that is visible from the outside of a *chatzer* or *mavoi*, but flush on the inside, possesses halachic validity. One of the challenges against this position had been the classic case — discussed over the preceding days — of a small *chatzer* that opens into a larger one. In that case, the small *chatzer* is considered fully exposed and carrying within it is therefore prohibited. The question was: why is the small *chatzer* considered fully exposed? Why doesn't the fact that its walls extend beyond the boundary of the smaller space count as *lechis* visible from the outside?

The *Gemara*'s earlier answer was that the walls run flush all the way in, leaving nothing actually extending beyond. The counter-question then arose: since the small *chatzer* was said to be ten *amot* wide and the large *chatzer* eleven *amot* wide, there is a differential of one *amah* — three *tefachim* on each side. Should we not apply *lavud* and treat those extensions as joined? The *Gemara* responded that the division is not even: under the analysis of Rav Zeira and Ravina, there are two *tefachim* on one side and four on the other, so *lavud* can be invoked only on the two-*tefach* side, not the four-*tefach* side. The *Gemara* then noted that even one side should suffice, since ordinarily a single *lechi* is enough for a *chatzer* — and this brings us to where we pick up now.

### Rabbi's Two-Plank Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מַאי? אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא: נִרְאֶה מִבַּחוּץ וְשָׁוֶה מִבִּפְנִים — אֵינוֹ נִידּוֹן מִשּׁוּם לֶחִי, וְרַבִּי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, וּדְרַבִּי זֵירָא וּדְרָבִינָא לֵיתָא — מִשּׁוּם הָכִי קְטַנָּה בְּעֶשֶׂר וּגְדוֹלָה בְּאַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה, מִשּׁוּם דְּרַבִּי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this entire explanation: **What is this** comparison? **Granted, if you say** that the legal status of a side post that is **visible from the outside but** appears to be **even** with the wall **from the inside is not considered** like that of **a side post; and** that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei** that a side post or an upright board in a courtyard must be at least three handbreadths wide; **and that** the explanations of the mishna offered earlier by **Rabbi Zeira and Ravina are not** accepted; **that is why** there is significance to the fact that the **small** courtyard is **ten** cubits wide and the **large one is eleven** cubits wide. It is **due to** the fact that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei.** Since Rabbi Yosei holds that a side post must be three handbreadths wide, we require that the two upright boards together measure six handbreadths, i.e., one cubit, which is the minimal difference in size between the two courtyards.

The *Gemara* answers: *Rebbi hi*, this is the position of *Rebbi*. *De-amar beinon shenei passin* — he holds that you need two planks. This rule does not apply to a *mavoi* in the standard sense, but it does apply to a *chatzer*. By way of background: the fundamental distinction between a *mavoi* and a *chatzer* is largely geometric — a space that is longer than it is wide functions as a *mavoi*, while one that is wider than it is long functions as a *chatzer*. A *mavoi* is rectified with a single *lechi*; a *chatzer*, according to *Rebbi*, requires two planks, one on each side of its exposed face. We will examine the details of this distinction in future learning, but for now the operative point is clear: since we are dealing with a *chatzer*, *Rebbi* requires a plank on *each* side, and therefore a *lechi* on only one side is insufficient.

This is supported by a *Baraita*: *chatzer niteret be-pas achat* — a *chatzer* may be rectified with one plank positioned at the point exposed to the *reshut ha-rabbim* — *ve-Rebbi omer bishnei passin* — but *Rebbi* says it requires two planks. We will return to the comparison between *mavoi* and *chatzer* in coming days; for now, this is our answer to the previous difficulty.

The *Gemara* then asks: *hai mai* — what kind of answer is that? *Ei amrit bishlama nira mi-bachutz ve-shaveh mi-bifnim einu nidon mishum lechi* — if we accept the original assumption that something visible from the outside but flush on the inside does *not* have the status of a *lechi*, then the whole structure of the case — one *chatzer* opening into another — makes perfect sense. Moreover, *ve-Rebbi savar lah ke-Rabbi Yose* — we can also say that *Rebbi* holds like *Rabbi Yose*, who maintains that each plank must be at least three *tefachim* wide. On this reading, *ve-de-Rabbi Zeira ve-de-Ravina lessa* — we set aside the special analysis of Rabbi Zeira and Ravina, which posited walls extending inward creating an asymmetric four-and-two division, and instead understand the case straightforwardly.

Now, why does *Rebbi* holding like *Rabbi Yose* matter? The larger *chatzer* is permitted precisely because the opening does not exceed ten *amot* and there is wall projecting inward on each side. If *Rebbi* requires two planks for a *chatzer* to be rectified, and *Rabbi Yose* requires each such plank to be at least three *tefachim* wide, then the large *chatzer* must be exactly one *amah* larger than the small one — three *tefachim* on each side, and three *tefachim* equals one *amah*. This explains precisely why the case was formulated as ten and eleven *amot*.

### Dimensions Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ: נִרְאֶה מִבַּחוּץ וְשָׁוֶה מִבִּפְנִים — נִידּוֹן מִשּׁוּם לֶחִי, וּדְרַבִּי זֵירָא וּדְרָבִינָא אִיתָא, וְרַבִּי לָא סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי — גְּדוֹלָה בְּאַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** **However, if you say** that the legal status of a side post that is **visible from the outside but** appears to be **even** with the wall **from the inside is considered** like that of **a side post; and** that **Rabbi Zeira's and Ravina's** explanations **are** accepted as *halakha*; and that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **does not hold in accordance with** the opinion **Rabbi Yosei, why do I** need to explain that the **large** courtyard measures **eleven** cubits?

The *Gemara* now raises a sharp objection. *Hai mai* — on the alternative reading, where something visible only from the outside does count as a *lechi*, the case cannot be explained elegantly. Under that approach, we had to invoke Rav Zeira and Ravina's special scenario — walls extending inward — and then explain away *lavud* by positing an uneven two-and-four distribution. But then the precise dimensions of the case become puzzling. Why was it necessary to state that the large *chatzer* was exactly eleven *amot*, one *amah* more than the small one?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמָּה נַפְשָׁךְ: אִי לְמִשְׁרְיַיהּ לִגְדוֹלָה קָאָתֵי — בְּעֶשֶׂר וּשְׁנֵי טְפָחִים סַגִּיא. וְאִי לְמֵיסְרַהּ לִקְטַנָּה קָאָתֵי — לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן דְּמַפְלְגִי טוּבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Whichever way** you look at it, there is a difficulty: **If** the *baraita* **is coming to permit** one to carry in **the large** courtyard, then a width **of ten** cubits **and two handbreadths suffices.** These two handbreadths can be considered the upright boards that render the courtyard fit for one to carry within it. **And if it is coming to** teach a novel *halakha* according to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and **prohibit** one to carry in **the small** courtyard, **it should teach us** a case where the walls of the two courtyards are much **farther removed** from each other, rather than a case where they are only one cubit apart. Therefore, the second explanation cannot be accepted.

*Gedolah be-achas esrei lamah li* — why does it have to be eleven specifically? The *Gemara* offers two possibilities. If the point is to establish the minimum size at which the large *chatzer* becomes halachically sound, then *be-eser ve-shnei tefachim sagi* — ten *amot* and two *tefachim* would suffice, since on the alternative reading *Rebbi* does not hold like *Rabbi Yose* and does not require a full three *tefachim* per plank. The two-*tefach* extension on one side would be enough.

Conversely, if the point of the case is to explain why the small *chatzer* is problematic — that we cannot invoke *lavud* because one side is four *tefachim*, which exceeds the three-*tefach* threshold — then why give an example where the differential is so narrow? *Le-katan ke-hai, le-shmin d'mipok tuvei* — if you wanted to demonstrate that the small *chatzer* is impermissible, give a case where the large *chatzer* is much bigger, twenty *amot* say, so that no one would think to apply *lavud* at all. The very fact that the case was formulated as ten and eleven — a minimal, surgically precise differential — makes no sense unless the reason for the one-*amah* difference is that each plank must be exactly three *tefachim*, consistent with *Rebbi* holding like *Rabbi Yose*.

### Visibility Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: נִרְאֶה מִבַּחוּץ וְשָׁוֶה מִבִּפְנִים — אֵינוֹ נִידּוֹן מִשּׁוּם לֶחִי. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, can we not conclude from** the *baraita* that a side post that is **visible from the outside but** appears to be **even** with the wall **from the inside is not considered** to have the legal status of **a side post?** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **conclude from this.**

*Ella lav shema mina* — we must conclude: *nira mi-bachutz ve-shaveh mi-bifnim einu nidon mishum lechi*. If something is visible from the outside but flush on the inside, it does not count as a *lechi*. *Shema mina* — this is a sound proof.

To summarize the arc of the argument: the case of the two *chatzerot* had been presented as a potential challenge to the validity of an outside-visible-only *lechi*. The *Gemara* spent considerable effort over previous sessions trying to construct a special scenario — walls extending inward, uneven *lavud* — that would explain why the small *chatzer* was exposed without conceding the general principle. But now the *Gemara* pulls the rug out: if that elaborate special scenario were true, the case would not have been formulated with the precise dimensions of ten and eleven *amot*. The specificity of those numbers only makes sense on the straightforward reading — that outside-visibility does not count as a *lechi*, and the one-*amah* difference reflects *Rabbi Yose*'s three-*tefachim* requirement, which *Rebbi* accepts. *Shema mina*.

## Lechi Mavoi Extension

### Rav Yosef's Doubt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לָא שְׁמִיעַ לִי הָא שְׁמַעְתְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: I did not hear this *halakha*** of Rabba bar Rav Huna from my teachers. Rav Yosef had become ill and forgotten his learning, which is why he could not recall the *halakha* that a side post that is visible from the outside is considered to have the legal status of a side post.

At this point, *Rav Yosef* says: *lo shamia leh hai shemaitah* — I never heard this teaching, referring to the position of *Rav Huna* that had been cited in the preceding discussion, to the effect that a *lechi* visible only from the outside still counts. This statement reflects the well-known historical circumstance: *Rav Yosef* suffered an illness that caused him to forget his learning, and he relied on his students to remind him of what he had previously taught.

### Abaye's Counterargument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אַתְּ אֲמַרְתְּ נִיהֲלַן, וְאַהָא אֲמַרְתְּ נִיהֲלַן — דְּאָמַר רָמֵי בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: לֶחִי הַמּוֹשֵׁךְ עִם דׇּפְנוֹ שֶׁל מָבוֹי, פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת נִידּוֹן מִשּׁוּם לֶחִי, וּמִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ עִם חוּדּוֹ הַפְּנִימִי. אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת נִידּוֹן מִשּׁוּם מָבוֹי, וְאָסוּר לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בְּכוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** His student **Abaye said to him: You** yourself **told us** this *halakha*, **and** it was **with regard to this** that **you told** it to **us. As Rami bar Abba said** that **Rav Huna said:** With regard to **a side post that extends along the wall of an alleyway** and beyond, in which case it appears from the inside to be a continuation of the wall but due to its narrow width it is clearly visible as a side post from the outside, if that side post is **less than four cubits** long **it is considered** to have the legal status **of a side post. And one may use** the alleyway **up to the inner edge** of the side post. However, if the side post itself extends **four cubits,** the alleyway has no side post and **it is considered to** have the legal status **of an alleyway, and it is prohibited to utilize the entire** alleyway.

*Amar lei Abaye* — *Abaye*, who was *Rav Yosef*'s primary student, responded: *at amart niah lan* — you yourself told us this. *Ve-ha amart niah lan* — and specifically, you told it to us in the context of the following teaching. *De-amar Rami bar Abba amar Rav Huna* — in the name of *Rav Huna*, another ruling: *lechi ha-moshech im dofno shel mavoi*.

### Four-Amah Threshold

The case is as follows. Imagine the thickness of a *mavoi* wall. If a *lechi* extends out from that wall — not into the *mavoi* itself but along the exterior, parallel to the wall — then from within the *mavoi* it appears to be nothing more than a continuation of the wall itself. From the outside of the *mavoi*, however, it is clearly visible as a separate protrusion. This is a textbook example of something *nira mi-bachutz ve-shaveh mi-bifnim* — visible from outside, flush from inside.

What is the *halacha*? *Pachot me-arba amot nidon mishum lechi* — if this extension is less than four *amot* long, it counts as a *lechi*. *U-mishtamesh be-chudo ha-pnimi* — and the usable area of the *mavoi* extends only up to the inner edge of this extension, not beyond it, meaning one may not use the space directly opposite the *lechi* — a matter that had been established in earlier sessions. *Arba amot nidon mishum mavoi* — once the extension reaches four *amot* or more, it is no longer classified as a *lechi*; it becomes, in effect, a wall of a *mavoi* in its own right. *Ve-asur le-ishtamesh bekulan* — and now one may not use the entire *mavoi*, because the *mavoi* is left without any effective *lechi* closing it off. The extension has grown too large to serve as a *lechi* and instead absorbs the identity of a wall, leaving the *mavoi* open.

### Three Inferences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַתְּ אֲמַרְתְּ לַן עֲלַהּ: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תְּלָת. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: בֵּין לְחָיַיִן אָסוּר. וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: מֶשֶׁךְ מָבוֹי בְּאַרְבַּע. וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: נִרְאֶה מִבַּחוּץ וְשָׁוֶה מִבִּפְנִים נִידּוֹן מִשּׁוּם לֶחִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And you said to us about this: Learn from this** statement **three** *halakhot* with regard to *eiruvin*. **Learn from it** that in the area **between the side posts it is prohibited** to carry, as Rav Huna rules that one may use the alleyway only up to the inner edge of the side post. **And learn from it** that the minimal **length of an alleyway is four** cubits. **And learn from it** that a side post that is **visible from the outside but** appears to be **even** with the wall of the alleyway **from the inside is considered** to have the legal status **of a side post.**

*Shema mina telat* — from this teaching, three *halachot* may be inferred. First, *shema mina bein lechayim asur* — the space directly opposite the *lechi* is prohibited for use; one may only carry up to the inner edge of the *lechi*. This resolves a debate that had occupied the *Gemara* a day or two earlier. Second, *shema mina meshech mavoi be-arba* — the minimum length of a *mavoi* is four *amot*, since it is precisely at four *amot* that this extension stops functioning as a *lechi* and starts functioning as a *mavoi* wall. Third, and most critically for the present discussion, *shema mina nira mi-bachutz ve-shaveh mi-bifnim nidon mishum lechi* — something visible from the outside but flush on the inside counts as a *lechi*, since that is precisely the character of the extension in this case, and it was nonetheless given full *lechi* status.

The first two inferences align with positions that had already been established in earlier *sugyas*. It is the third inference that carries the most weight here.

### Ruling Confirmation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא: נִרְאֶה מִבַּחוּץ וְשָׁוֶה מִבִּפְנִים — נִידּוֹן מִשּׁוּם לֶחִי. תְּיוּבְתָּא וְהִלְכְתָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **The *halakha*** is that a side post that is **visible from the outside but** appears to be **even** with the wall **from the inside is considered** to have the legal status **of a side post.** The Gemara asks: It is possible that there is **a conclusive refutation** of this opinion, **and** it is also **the *halakha*?** This opinion was refuted earlier. Can the *halakha* then be decided in accordance with it?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין, מִשּׁוּם דְּתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא כְּווֹתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Yes,** it can **because Rabbi Ḥiyya taught** a *baraita* **in accordance with it.** Although the deductive analysis of the statements of other *tanna'im* led to different conclusions, the *halakha* relies on Rabbi Ḥiyya's explicit statement.

*Ve-hilcheta* — the *halacha* is: *nira mi-bachutz ve-shaveh mi-bifnim nidon mishum lechi*. It does count as a *lechi*.

The *Gemara* immediately registers the tension: *tiuvta ve-hilcheta*? We just demonstrated a contradiction — from the case of the two *chatzerot* — against this very position, and now we rule in accordance with it? *In* — yes, *mishum de-tani Rabbi Chiyya kevataih* — because although the two-*chatzer* case seems to contradict this ruling, there was an explicit *Baraita* taught by *Rabbi Chiyya* that directly supports it, stating that whether a *lechi* is visible on the outside and not the inside, or on the inside and not the outside, it is halachically valid. There is evidence in both directions. The weight of *Rabbi Chiyya*'s explicit teaching tips the scales, and so we rule accordingly.

This is a conceptually striking conclusion. One might have assumed that the entire purpose of a *lechi* — or a *korah* — is to serve as a *heker*, a recognizable marker, for those *inside* the space, reminding them not to carry beyond the boundary. If the marker is only visible from the outside, does it not fail at its fundamental purpose? The *Gemara*'s ruling suggests either that the *lechi* functions more as a formal partition than as a practical reminder, and therefore inside-visibility is not essential; or that *Chazal*, in formalizing the requirement, were satisfied with any form of recognition — even from the outside — as sufficient. The *halacha* stands: outside-visibility is enough. As a practical consequence, those living in the small *chatzer* of the original case would not be able to carry therein under the ruling of the *braita* — but they are not considered to be in a completely open domain, because the extension does constitute a *lechi*.

## Wide Opening Solutions

### Ten-Amah Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָרָחָב מֵעֶשֶׂר יְמַעֵט. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, תָּנָא: וְהָרָחָב מֵעֶשֶׂר יְמַעֵט, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְמַעֵט. וְעַד כַּמָּה?
>
> **Translation:** The opening mishna states: If the entrance to an alleyway is **wider than ten** cubits, **one must diminish** its width. **Abaye said** that a Sage **taught** in the *Tosefta*: If the entrance to an alleyway **is wider than ten** cubits, **one must diminish** its width. **Rabbi Yehuda says: He need not diminish it.** The question arises: **Until what** width does Rabbi Yehuda still permit carrying in the alleyway?

Having completed the discussion of the *korah* and *lechi* — their heights, the spaces below and opposite them, and related questions — the *Gemara* now turns to the second clause of the *Mishna*: if the opening of a *mavoi* is too wide, it must be reduced. *Ve-rachav me-eser yima'et* — if the opening exceeds ten *amot*, you must make it smaller before a *lechi* or *korah* will be effective.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבַר רַב אַחַי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף לְמֵימַר: עַד שְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה וּשְׁלִישׁ. וְקַל וָחוֹמֶר מִפַּסֵּי בִירָאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Initially, **Rav Aḥai thought to say before Rav Yosef: Up to thirteen and a third cubits. And** he derived this figure through **an *a fortiori*** argument **from** upright **boards surrounding a well.** Rabbi Yehuda maintains that if one placed upright boards up to thirteen and a third cubits apart from one another, he may consider the partitioned area around the well as a private domain and therefore carry within it.

*Amar Abaye: tanu rabbanan* — *Abaye* cites a *Baraita*. *Ve-rachav me-eser yima'et* — wider than ten, you reduce. *Rabbi Yehuda omer eino tzarich lema'et* — *Rabbi Yehuda* disagrees and holds that no reduction is required, even for openings wider than ten *amot*. Just as *Rabbi Yehuda* had ruled earlier that the *korah* need not be lowered even when it exceeds twenty *amot* in height, so too here he allows a wider opening without reduction.

*Ve-ad kama* — to what maximum width does *Rabbi Yehuda* permit this? *Savar Rav Acha kamei de-Rav Yosef lemeimar* — Rav Acha proposed before *Rav Yosef* that the limit should be *ad shelosh esreh amah u-shlish* — thirteen and a third *amot* — derived by a *kal va-chomer* from the laws of *pasi biraot*.

### Pasi Biraot Wells

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה פַּסֵּי בִירָאוֹת שֶׁהִתַּרְתָּה בָּהֶן פָּרוּץ מְרוּבֶּה עַל הָעוֹמֵד — לֹא הִתַּרְתָּה בָּהֶן יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה וּשְׁלִישׁ, מָבוֹי שֶׁלֹּא הִתַּרְתָּה בּוֹ פָּרוּץ מְרוּבֶּה עַל הָעוֹמֵד, אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא תַּתִּיר בּוֹ יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה וּשְׁלִישׁ?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Aḥai explains: **Just as** in the case of upright **boards surrounding a well, where you permitted** carrying, even though the boards form a partition where the **breached** segment **is greater than the standing** segment, **you did not permit** carrying **within them** if the gap between the boards is **more than thirteen and a third cubits** wide; in the case of **an alleyway, where you did not permit** carrying if the **breached** segment of its walls **is greater than the standing** segment, **is it not right that you will not permit** carrying **within it** if there is a gap **more than thirteen and a third cubits** wide?

The *pasi biraot* are the special partition-walls constructed around public wells. The scenario is as follows: a well in a public space is four by four *amot* and ten *tefachim* deep, making its interior a *reshut ha-yachid*. Drawing water from it while standing in a *reshut ha-rabbim* would constitute carrying between domains. The rabbinic solution is to erect corner-posts — small L-shaped walls at the four corners — that formally enclose the space around the well. The resulting enclosure looks like four walls with enormous gaps; indeed, there is far more open space than closed space, which ordinarily renders an enclosure invalid. Nevertheless, because the four corners are in place, the entire enclosed area is treated as a *reshut ha-yachid*, allowing people to draw and use the water within it. This is a special *heter*, quite different from the standard rules of *mechitza*. The maximum size of this enclosure, as the *Gemara* will explain, is thirteen and a third *amot* — large enough to accommodate four cows entering and four cows exiting simultaneously, which appears to have been a practical measure of the required access.

The proposed *kal va-chomer*: *pasi biraot*, where we are already extremely lenient in allowing more open space than closed space, nevertheless impose a maximum opening size of thirteen and a third *amot*. A *mavoi*, where we apply the ordinary stricter rules requiring more closed space than open, should certainly not permit openings exceeding thirteen and a third *amot* — for if even the lenient case caps at that figure, the stricter case should not exceed it.

### Analogy Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִיא הַנּוֹתֶנֶת: פַּסֵּי בִירָאוֹת שֶׁהִתַּרְתָּה בָּהֶן פָּרוּץ מְרוּבֶּה עַל הָעוֹמֵד — לֹא תַּתִּיר בָּהֶן יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה וּשְׁלִישׁ, מָבוֹי שֶׁלֹּא הִתַּרְתָּה בּוֹ פָּרוּץ מְרוּבֶּה עַל הָעוֹמֵד — תַּתִּיר בּוֹ יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמּוֹת וּשְׁלִישׁ!
>
> **Translation:** **But that** reasoning **provides** support for a contrary conclusion as well. Just as in the case of upright **boards surrounding a well, where you permitted** carrying **within them,** even though the boards form a partition where the **breached** segment **is greater than the standing** segment, **you will not** extend the leniency and **permit** carrying **within them,** when the gap between the boards is **more than thirteen and a third cubits;** in **an alleyway, where you** were stringent and **did not permit** carrying when the **breached** segment **is greater than the standing** segment, in a case where most of the walls are standing, **you will** certainly **permit** carrying, even when the gap is **more than thirteen and a third cubits.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי לְאִידַּךְ גִּיסָא: פַּסֵּי בִירָאוֹת דְּאַקֵּילְתְּ בְּהוּ חַד קוּלָּא — אַקֵּיל בְּהוּ קוּלָּא אַחֲרִינָא, מָבוֹי כְּלָל כְּלָל לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** one may argue **to the contrary.** One should be more stringent in the case of an alleyway. In the case of upright **boards surrounding a well, with regard to which you were lenient** and issued **one leniency, be lenient and** issue **another leniency** and maintain that a gap of up to thirteen and a third cubits still be considered an entrance. However, in the case of **an alleyway,** you should **not** be lenient **at all.** Therefore, there is no way to determine Rabbi Yehuda's opinion with regard to the width of an alleyway entrance.

The *Gemara* rejects this *kal va-chomer*, and does so from two opposing directions. First, one could argue the reverse: *pasi biraot* is so lenient in allowing *parutz merubah al ha-omed* — more open than closed — that we must draw the line somewhere, and so we cap it at thirteen and a third. But a *mavoi*, where we are generally strict and require *omed merubah al ha-parutz*, perhaps enjoys more latitude precisely at the dimension of the opening, since we have already been strict in other respects. Thus the opening of a *mavoi* might be permitted to exceed thirteen and a third *amot*.

Alternatively, the opposite inference could be drawn: precisely because *pasi biraot* is so lenient in one dimension, it can afford to permit thirteen and a third. But a *mavoi*, where no such leniencies exist, might be held to a stricter standard than ten *amot*, and thirteen and a third might actually be too generous. *Klal klal lo* — perhaps for a *mavoi* we allow nothing beyond ten.

In short, the *pasi biraot* is a fundamentally different legal construct, and no inference — in either direction — can be drawn from it to the case of a *mavoi*. The *Gemara* therefore leaves open the question of how far *Rabbi Yehuda* would extend his leniency in the case of the *mavoi*. The *Chachamim*'s position remains clear: ten *amot* is the maximum permissible opening width.

## Lengthwise Division

### Post Solution Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי לֵוִי: מָבוֹי שֶׁהוּא רָחָב עֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה, נוֹעַץ קָנֶה בְּאֶמְצָעִיתוֹ, וְדַיּוֹ. הוּא תָּנֵי לַהּ וְהוּא אָמַר לַהּ: דְּאֵין הֲלָכָה כְּאוֹתָהּ מִשְׁנָה. אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּלֵוִי: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּאוֹתָהּ מִשְׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Levi taught** a *baraita* with regard to reducing the width of an alleyway in order to render it fit for one to carry within it. If **an alleyway is twenty cubits wide, one may stick a reed in** the **center** of its entrance **and** that is **sufficient** to create two separate alleyways, each ten cubits wide. **He taught** this *baraita*, **and he said** about **it that the *halakha* is not in accordance with that teaching,** as the insertion of a reed is not effective in reducing the width. **Some say** that **Shmuel said in the name of Levi: The *halakha* is not in accordance with that teaching.**

The *Gemara* now turns to practical solutions for a *mavoi* whose opening is too wide. Reducing a twenty-*amah* opening to ten is straightforward in principle — but in practice, one may wish to keep the opening as wide as possible. *Tani Levi* — Levi taught: *mavoi she-rachav esrim amah* — if a *mavoi* is twenty *amot* wide — *no'etz kanna be-emtzaito ve-dayo* — simply plant a post in the middle and you are done. The post divides the twenty-*amah* opening into two ten-*amah* openings, each of which can then be fitted with a *korah*, and the problem is solved.

However, the *Gemara* reports that Levi himself — or in another version, *Shmuel* in Levi's name — retracted this: *ein halacha ke-osa mishna*. We do not rule like that *Baraita*. The reason is the principle the *Gemara* will articulate: *atya avira di-hai gisa ve-di-hai gisa u-mevatli* — the air space on this side and that side comes and renders the post inconsequential. A lone post standing in a twenty-*amah* opening, with ten *amot* of open air on each side, cannot be said to divide anything. It simply disappears into the surrounding void.

### Four-Amah Plank

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֶלָּא הֵיכִי עָבֵיד? אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּלֵוִי: עוֹשֶׂה פַּס גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה בְּמֶשֶׁךְ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, וּמַעֲמִידוֹ לְאׇרְכּוֹ שֶׁל מָבוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Rather, how should one act** in order to render an alleyway of that sort fit for one to carry within it? **Shmuel said in the name of Levi:** **One prepares a board ten** handbreadths **high with a length of four cubits and stands it lengthwise** down the middle **of the alleyway,** and thereby forms two small alleyways at the entrance to the alleyway, neither of which is more than ten cubits wide.

What then is the solution? *Amar Shmuel mishme d'Levi* — Levi's accepted answer: *oseh pas gavoha asarah be-meshach arba amot u-mamido le-orkho shel mavoi* — make a plank ten *tefachim* tall and four *amot* long, and stand it *lengthwise* into the *mavoi*. 

This is the ingenious part. Rather than placing something across the opening, perpendicular to the direction of entry, you insert a plank running *parallel* to the direction of the *mavoi*, extending four *amot* inward from the opening. The result is not a wall across the opening but a dividing partition along the length. Recall that we established earlier that four *amot* is the minimum length of a *mavoi*. An extension of four *amot* therefore does not function as a *lechi* — it is too long — but rather as a *mavoi* wall in its own right. By placing this four-*amah* plank lengthwise, you have in effect created two separate *mavois*, each ten *amot* wide, sitting side by side. Each may then be independently fitted with a *korah*.

### Air Space Negation

Why does this work where the post did not? Because we are no longer trying to make something function *across* the opening, where it would be overwhelmed by the surrounding air space on both sides. Instead, we are creating a partition *along* the length, defining one *mavoi* on each side of the plank. The plank is not a *lechi* subject to being negated by flanking air space; it is a wall. The air space on its sides does not negate it because it is not positioned transversely.

*Tosafot* notes that according to one view, a *tzuras ha-pesach* — a symbolic doorframe — would be needed at the back end, connecting the plank to the rear wall, in order to fully close off each *mavoi* from the other. However, the *Gemara* itself does not state this requirement, and *Tosafot* also entertains the possibility that it is unnecessary — that once the first four *amot* of the *mavoi* are divided by the plank, the two sides are sufficiently defined as separate *mavois*, and the fact that they are joined at the rear is inconsequential.

## Gap-and-Plank System

### 15-Amah Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי כִּדְרַב יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: מָבוֹי שֶׁהוּא רָחָב חֲמֵשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה — מַרְחִיק שְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת וְעוֹשֶׂה פַּס שָׁלֹשׁ אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** one can act **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Yehuda,** as **Rav Yehuda said:** If **an alleyway is fifteen cubits wide,** how does one reduce its width? **He distances** himself **two cubits** from one of the walls of the alleyway **and prepares a board three cubits** wide, thereby leaving an opening of only ten cubits.

*De-amar Rav Yehuda* — the *Gemara* presents an alternative solution, one that is more intuitive though technically more involved. *Mavoi she-rachav chamesh esreh amah* — if the *mavoi* is fifteen *amot* wide: *marchik shtei amot ve-oseh pas shalosh amot* — leave a two-*amah* gap and erect a three-*amah* plank. This closes off a five-*amah* section and leaves ten *amot* open — which is within the permissible limit.

The logic: you need to close off five *amot* of the fifteen-*amah* opening. One could simply erect a five-*amah* plank, but *Rav Yehuda* offers a more accessible solution. Within the five-*amah* section you wish to treat as closed, you leave a two-*amah* gap and erect a three-*amah* plank. Looking at that five-*amah* section, the majority — three out of five — is standing wall. The principle of *omed merubah al ha-parutz* — the standing portion is greater than the open portion — is satisfied, and the entire five-*amah* section is considered as if it were fully walled. The remaining ten *amot* constitute the permissible opening.

### Negation Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי? יַעֲשֶׂה פַּס אַמָּה וּמֶחֱצָה, וְיַרְחִיק שְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת, וְיַעֲשֶׂה פַּס אַמָּה וּמֶחֱצָה. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: עוֹמֵד מְרוּבֶּה עַל הַפָּרוּץ מִשְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹת לָא הָוֵי עוֹמֵד!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And why** must one reduce the width in this manner? **One** could also **prepare a board a cubit and a half** wide, **and distance** himself **two cubits, and** then **prepare** another board **a cubit and a half** wide, leaving the alleyway with an opening of only ten cubits. Apparently, one may **conclude from** the fact that Rav Yehuda did not suggest **this** possibility that if the **standing** segment of a wall is **greater than the breached** segment only when one combines the standing segments **from two directions,** i.e., both sides of the breach, it **is not considered** as though the **standing** segment were greater.

The *Gemara* sharpens the analysis by asking why *Rav Yehuda* did not suggest what might seem like an even more elegant arrangement. Rather than a two-*amah* gap and a three-*amah* plank, why not divide the three *amot* of wall across both sides of the gap? Specifically: a plank of one and a half *amot*, then a two-*amah* gap, then another plank of one and a half *amot*. The result is the same total: three *amot* of wall, two *amot* of gap, covering five *amot* — and the wall on each side is spread more evenly. Why is this not offered as an option?

Here the *Gemara* introduces its second operative principle: *atya avira di-hai gisa ve-avira di-hai gisa u-mevatli* — the air from one side and the air from the other side comes and cancels out what is in the middle. This principle operates at the level of individual segments rather than the totality of the opening. Even if the overall count of wall versus gap satisfies *omed merubah al ha-parutz*, a particular wall segment may be rendered halachically void if there is too much open air on both of its sides relative to its own size.

In the case of a one-and-a-half-*amah* plank flanked by a two-*amah* gap on one side and ten *amot* of open *mavoi* space on the other, the disproportion between the air space and the plank is severe enough to negate the plank entirely. This is not a matter of whether the total wall beats the total gap; it is a micro-analysis of each segment individually. By contrast, when the plank is three *amot* with only a two-*amah* gap on one side and the open *mavoi* on the other, the three-*amah* plank is robust enough that the flanking air space does not render it void.

The distinction between these two principles is essential: *omed merubah al ha-parutz* asks whether, across the entire section one is trying to close, more is walled than open. The air-negation principle asks whether any individual segment of wall is overwhelmed by the open space on both of its sides. Both conditions must be satisfied independently.

### Alternating Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ הָוֵי עוֹמֵד, וְשָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דְּאָתֵי אַוֵּירָא דְּהַאי גִּיסָא וְאַוֵּירָא דְּהַאי גִּיסָא וּמְבַטֵּל לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **Actually, I would say to you** that ordinarily **it is considered** as **standing** even when one must combine the standing segments on the two sides of the breach. **However, it is different here, as the air,** i.e., the two cubit opening, **of this** one **side** of the far board **and the air,** i.e., the ten cubit opening, **of this** other **side** of the board **come** together **and negate it.** Therefore, in this case, the board that is farther from the wall cannot serve to close off the alleyway.

The *Gemara* pushes further. Why not use alternating small walls: a one-*amah* plank, a one-*amah* gap, a one-*amah* plank, a one-*amah* gap, a one-*amah* plank — five *amot* covered, with three *amot* of wall and two *amot* of gap? Here the air flanking each individual one-*amah* plank is not larger than the plank itself — one *amah* of gap on each side of a one-*amah* plank — so the negation principle should not apply.

The *Gemara* raises the possibility that from this case we might infer *omed ke-parutz assur* — that when the standing wall is merely equal to the gap (one against one), rather than greater, it is insufficient. But the *Gemara* immediately deflects: *le-olam eima lach mutar* — actually, equal is sufficient. The problem with this arrangement is again the negation principle: even though each local one-*amah* plank has only a one-*amah* gap immediately flanking it, the plank is bordered on its far side by the ten-*amah* open space of the *mavoi*. That extended open air combines with the one-*amah* gap on the near side to overwhelm the one-*amah* plank and negate it. The negation principle is what disqualifies this arrangement, not any failure of *omed merubah al ha-parutz*.

### Spread-Out Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיַרְחִיק אַמָּה וְיַעֲשֶׂה פַּס אַמָּה וּמֶחֱצָה, וְיַרְחִיק אַמָּה וְיַעֲשֶׂה פַּס אַמָּה וּמֶחֱצָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And** one could **distance** himself **one cubit** from the wall, **and prepare a board** of **a cubit and a half, and distance** himself another **cubit, and prepare** another **board** of **a cubit and a half.** In this manner, one could diminish the width of the entrance of the alleyway to ten cubits.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין הָכִי נָמֵי, וְכוּלֵּי הַאי לָא אַטְרְחוּהּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Yes, it is indeed so;** this would work equally as well. **But the Sages did not burden him this much,** requiring him to prepare two boards where one suffices.

The *Gemara* then proposes: *na'avid pas amah u-mechtza, veyarchik amah, veya'aseh pas amah u-mechtza* — make a plank of one and a half *amot*, then a gap of one *amah*, then another plank of one and a half *amot*. This covers five *amot* with three *amot* of wall and two *amot* of gap. Each one-and-a-half-*amah* plank is flanked by a one-*amah* gap on one side and the ten-*amah* open *mavoi* space on the other. But the one-and-a-half-*amah* plank is larger than the one-*amah* gap — so the question is whether it survives the negation principle.

*Innahu hachi nami* — the *Gemara* affirms that this arrangement is indeed valid. One and a half is greater than one; the gap does not overwhelm the plank. *Ve-chulei hai lo atrachu rabbanan* — but *Chazal* did not impose this more burdensome configuration on people. They allowed the simpler solution of a single three-*amah* plank with a two-*amah* gap. The two-plank arrangement is valid if one wishes to use it, but it is not required.

## Side-Door Concern

### Abandonment Fear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא שָׁבֵיק פִּיתְחָא רַבָּה, וְעָיֵיל בְּפִיתְחָא זוּטָא! אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר מַתְנָה: חֲזָקָה אֵין אָדָם מַנִּיחַ פֶּתַח גָּדוֹל וְנִכְנָס בְּפֶתַח קָטָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a new issue: **But let us be concerned lest one abandon** use of **the larger entrance,** which is ten cubits wide, **and** begin to **enter** the alleyway **through the smaller entrance,** which has a width of two cubits. This would negate the larger opening's status as an entrance and render the alleyway unfit for one to carry within it, as it would no longer have an entrance with a side post. **Rav Adda bar Mattana said:** The **presumption** is that **a person does not abandon a larger entrance and enter** instead **through a smaller entrance.**

The *Gemara* now raises a conceptually different concern. Suppose one has erected the three-*amah* plank with a two-*amah* gap, and placed the *korah* over the ten-*amah* main opening. The permissibility of the *mavoi* depends on the *korah* marking the entry point. But what if people begin to use the two-*amah* gap as their habitual exit? If the residents abandon the main door and pass instead through the side gap — which has no *korah* — then the gap becomes the effective entrance of the *mavoi*, and the *korah* over the main opening no longer marks the actual point of entry. The concern is that practical human behavior might undermine the halachic construct.

### Strong Presumption

*Amar Rav Adda bar Matna*: *chazaka ein adam meniach petach gadol ve-nichnas be-petach katan* — there is a strong presumption (*chazaka*) that a person will not abandon a large door in favor of a small one. People naturally use the larger, more convenient opening. There is no realistic concern that the residents will habitually choose the narrow two-*amah* gap over the spacious ten-*amah* main entrance.

### Not a Shortcut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא מִדְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי וּדְרַבֵּי אַסִּי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **And** in **what** way is this **different** from the opinion **of Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi,** who maintain that in the case of an alleyway that is breached on its side wall close to its entrance, if the breach is large enough for one to enter through it, carrying in the alleyway is prohibited? There, too, such a breach should not be problematic, as a person does not abandon a larger entrance to enter through a smaller one.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם קָא מְמַעֵט בְּהִילּוּכָא. הָכָא לָא קָא מְמַעֵט בְּהִילּוּכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** in the case of Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi, the smaller entrance **reduces** his **walking** distance. If one approaches the alleyway from the side, the smaller entrance provides a shortcut, and therefore one might enter through it as well. However, **here,** in the case of the two entrances one two cubits and one ten cubits, **it does not reduce** his **walking** distance, as both openings are situated at the front of the alleyway.

The *Gemara* compares this to a case discussed in an earlier session, involving a *mavoi* whose side wall was breached. There, *Rav Huna* and *Rav Assi* were concerned that people might exit through the breach in the side wall under certain circumstances, thereby compromising the *mavoi*'s status. Why should we not be similarly concerned here?

The *Gemara* distinguishes: *hasam ka-mamit locha, hacha lo ka-mamit locha* — there, the side opening creates a shortcut; here, it does not. When there is a breach in a side wall, exiting through it allows one to bypass the front of the *mavoi* entirely — it is a genuinely more direct route in certain directions, and people may well be drawn to use it habitually. Here, by contrast, the two-*amah* gap is on the same face as the main opening. Exiting through the gap rather than through the main door offers no directional advantage whatsoever — it is simply a smaller version of the same exit. No one would consistently prefer a narrow gap to a wide door when both lead in precisely the same direction. The *chazaka* therefore holds, and the concern is dismissed.

## Toilet Seat Debate

### Toilet Seat Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: עוֹר הָעַסְלָא וְחָלָל שֶׁלּוֹ — מִצְטָרְפִין בְּטֶפַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the issue of the standing segment that is greater than the breached segment. **We learned** in the *Tosefta* **there,** in tractate *Kelim*: **The leather** covering **of a stool** [***asla***] **and its hole join** together **to** complete **a handbreadth** with regard to ritual impurity imparted by a tent over a corpse. Any person, vessel, or food that is beneath a covering that is at least a handbreadth in size together with a portion of a corpse of at least an olive-bulk becomes ritually impure with impurity imparted by a corpse. The *baraita* teaches that the leather covering of a stool and its hole combine to complete the measure of a handbreadth.

The *Gemara* returns, in a different halachic context, to the question of how air space on both sides of a partition affects its halachic status. *Tanan hasam be-Oholot* — a teaching from the tractate dealing with *tum'at meit*: *or ha-atzla* — a leather toilet seat. This was a piece of leather, approximately one *tefach* by one *tefach*, placed over the seat opening of a toilet, with a hole in the center through which waste passed. If this leather seat is suspended above a corpse, or a *ke-zayis* of a corpse, does it function as a barrier that blocks the spread of *tumah* upward, or is the hole too significant to be disregarded?

The principle at work: an object of one *tefach* by one *tefach* can block *tumah* from spreading through it. But if the hole in the center is large enough, the leather around it may not constitute a full *tefach* by *tefach* barrier. The question is: when can the leather surrounding the hole and the hole itself combine — *mitztarfin* — to be considered a full *tefach*, and when does the hole prevent this combination?

### Rav Dimi's Measure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״עוֹר הָעַסְלָא״? אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עוֹר כִּיסּוּי שֶׁל בֵּית הַכִּסֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the leather** covering **of a stool** referred to in the *Tosefta*? **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rav Yoḥanan said: The leather covering of a bathroom.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה? כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר: אֶצְבָּעַיִים מִכָּאן, וְאֶצְבָּעַיִים מִכָּאן, וְאֶצְבָּעַיִים רֶיוַח בָּאֶמְצַע. כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין, אָמַר: אֶצְבַּע וּמֶחֱצָה מִכָּאן, וְאֶצְבַּע וּמֶחֱצָה מִכָּאן, וְאֶצְבַּע רֶיוַח בָּאֶמְצַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how large** can the hole be and still combine with the leather covering to complete the handbreadth? **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said: Two fingers** of leather **from here,** on one side, **and two fingers** of leather **from here,** on the other side, **and a space of two fingers** for the hole **in the middle.** However, **when Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said: A finger and a half** of leather **from here, and a finger and a half** on the leather **from here, and a space of** a single **finger** for the hole **in the middle.**

*Ki ata Rav Dimi* — when Rav Dimi came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he said: *etzba'im mikan ve-etzba'im mikan ve-etzba revach be-emtza* — two finger-widths on this side, two on that side, and two finger-widths of gap in the middle. Since a *tefach* measures six finger-widths by the pinky-based scale, and two plus two plus two equals six, the entire piece is exactly one *tefach*, divided equally.

*Ki ata Ravin amar* — when Ravin came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he reported differently: *etzba u-mechtza mikan ve-etzba u-mechtza mikan ve-etzba revach be-emtza* — one and a half finger-widths on each side and one finger-width in the middle. Since a *tefach* also measures four thumb-widths, and one and a half plus one and a half plus one equals four, this arrangement equals one *tefach* by the thumb-based scale.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב דִּימִי: מִי פְּלִיגִיתוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא, הָא — בְּרַבְרְבָתָא, הָא — בְּזוּטְרָתָא, וְלָא פְּלִיגִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Dimi: Do** the two of **you,** yourself and Ravin, **disagree** in principle? Rav Dimi **said to him: No,** rather **this,** Ravin's statement, **is referring to the large** finger, i.e., the thumb, and **this,** my own statement, **is referring to the small** finger, the pinkie, **and we do not disagree.** Both were describing one handbreadth, which equals the width of four thumbs or six pinkies.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָאיֵי, פְּלִיגִיתוּ, וּבְעוֹמֵד מְרוּבֶּה עַל הַפָּרוּץ מִשְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹת פְּלִיגִיתוּ. לְדִידָךְ הָוֵי עוֹמֵד מִשְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹת, לְרָבִין מֵרוּחַ אַחַת — הָוֵי עוֹמֵד, מִשְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹת — לָא הָוֵי עוֹמֵד.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to him:** This is **not so** [***la'ei***]. **You disagree, and you disagree with regard to** the *halakha* in a case where the **standing** segment of a wall is **greater than the breached** segment only when one combines the standing segments **from two directions,** i.e., both sides of the breached segment. **According to you,** this wall **is considered as standing,** even when one must combine the standing segments **from two directions. According to Ravin,** if the standing segment **on one side** of the breach is greater, the wall **is considered as standing;** however, if the standing segment is greater only after combining the standing segments **from the two directions,** it **is not considered as standing.**

*Amar lei Abaye* — Abaye challenged *Rav Dimi*: *mi peligi*? Do you and *Ravin* actually disagree? *Rav Dimi* initially responded: *lo* — no. One is measuring by pinky-widths, the other by thumb-widths; both describe a *tefach*, the units simply differ. *Ha be-ravrvata, ha be-zutrata* — one uses the larger unit, the other the smaller.

*Abaye* pressed: *pela'a* — really? You are arguing, *u-va-omed u-va-parutz mi-shetei ruchos palgiton* — and specifically about whether closed space from both sides can be *combined* to exceed the gap. This is not a dispute about units of measurement; it is a dispute about the underlying principle.

### Combining Sides Debate

Abaye's insight is as follows. Both scholars describe a *tefach*-sized piece of leather with a hole of some size in the center. The question is what proportions are necessary for the leather to be considered a halachically effective barrier rather than a perforated non-entity. The critical variable is the relationship between each side of leather and the central gap. Rav Dimi says each side is equal to the gap — two against two. Ravin says each side must be larger than the gap — one and a half against one. Abaye argues that this difference reflects a principled disagreement: can you *combine* the leather on both sides to determine that the combined wall exceeds the hole, or must you look at each side independently?

If combining is permitted, then two plus two equals four, which exceeds the two-finger gap — and the leather is valid even with equal-sized sides. If you must evaluate each side independently, then two equal to two is insufficient — you need each side to individually exceed the gap — and Ravin's arrangement of one and a half against one is necessary.

### Dimi Clarifies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתְּךָ לָא פְּלִיגִיתוּ, לְרָבִין הָכִי אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְמֵימַר: אֶצְבַּע וּשְׁלִישׁ מִכָּאן, וְאֶצְבַּע וּשְׁלִישׁ מִכָּאן, וְאֶצְבַּע וּשְׁלִישׁ רֶיוַח בָּאֶמְצַע.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye continues: **For if it should enter your mind** to say that **you do not disagree,** but simply refer to the same measures by different names, **to** express his opinion, **Ravin should have said as follows: A finger and a third** of leather **from here, and a finger and a third** of leather **from here, and** a space of **a finger and a third** for the hole **in the middle.** In this case, there would still be a handbreadth in total, but each side of leather alone would not be larger than the space in the middle. The fact that Ravin presented a case where the hole in the middle is smaller than the width of the leather on either side indicates that his dispute with Rav Dimi is a fundamental one.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מַאי — פְּלִיגִינַן? לְדִידִי, הָכִי אִיבְּעִי לִי לְמֵימַר: אֶצְבַּע וּשְׁנֵי שְׁלִישִׁים מִכָּאן, וְאֶצְבַּע וּשְׁנֵי שְׁלִישִׁים מִכָּאן, וְאֶצְבָּעַיִם וּשְׁנֵי שְׁלִישִׁים רֶיוַח בָּאֶמְצַע.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Dimi responds: **Rather, what** do you wish to say, that **we disagree?** If so, **to** express the opinion attributed to **me, I should have said as follows: A finger and two thirds** of leather **from here, and a finger and two thirds** of leather **from here, and a space of two fingers and two thirds in the middle.** This would provide a more striking case where, despite the fact that the breach is much greater than the standing segments on either of its sides, the two standing segments combine together so that the standing segments are considered greater than the breached segment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר דִּפְלִגִינַן — בִּפָרוּץ כְּעוֹמֵד פְּלִגִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, if there is** room **to say that we disagree,** our dispute relates to a different point, and **we argue** in the case where **the breached** segment **is** exactly **equal to the standing** segment on each side. According to Ravin, it is considered breached; while according to Rav Dimi, it is considered standing.

Rav Dimi responds to Abaye's challenge: if I truly held that combining from both sides suffices, I should have given a more striking example — *etzba u-shnei shlishim mikan ve-etzba u-shnei shlishim mikan ve-etzba ushnei shlishim revach be-emtza* — one and two-thirds on each side against a gap that is larger than either side alone, with both sides together exceeding it. That would have been the dramatic statement of the combining principle. Instead I gave an example where each side is equal to the gap — which proves that I am not relying on combining at all.

*Ella ee ika le-meimar de-pliginon, be-parutz ke-omed pligin* — if there is a genuine disagreement between us, it is not about combining versus not combining. Both of us, *Rav Dimi* clarifies, hold that you may not combine the two sides; you must evaluate each side independently. The disagreement is about a different question: must each side individually be *greater than* the gap, or is it sufficient that each side be *equal to* the gap? Ravin holds that each side must be greater — hence one and a half against one. *Rav Dimi* holds that equality suffices — hence two against two.

This maps directly onto the question that arose in the context of *mavoi* partitions earlier in the *daf*: the dispute over *omed ke-parutz* — whether a standing wall that is merely equal in size to the open gap is sufficient, or whether the standing must exceed the opening. The leather toilet-seat case and the *mavoi* partition case thus illuminate the same underlying principle, applied in two very different halachic domains. It is worth noting, as *Tosafot* observes, that the way we define issues of *mechitza* for *eruvin* purposes is not necessarily identical to the way we define whether a barrier is sufficient to contain or block *tumah* — the two legal frameworks overlap but are not coextensive.
