# Bekhorot 55 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ma'aser Behemah Intro

### Ma'aser Behemah Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה מִצְטָרֵף כִּמְלֹא רֶגֶל בְּהֵמָה רוֹעָה, וְכַמָּה הִיא רֶגֶל בְּהֵמָה רוֹעָה? שִׁשָּׁה עָשָׂר מִיל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Animals subject to the obligation of **animal tithe join together** if the distance between them is no greater **than the distance that a grazing animal** can **walk** and still be tended by one shepherd. **And how much is the distance that a grazing animal walks?** It is **sixteen *mil*.**

The daf opens with the *mishnah* near the bottom of 54b, returning to the topic of *ma'aser behemah*. The subject arises here because *ma'aser behemah* shares important characteristics with *bechor*: both may be eaten with a *mum* outside the *beit hamikdash*. As noted in the preceding discussion, *ma'aser behemah* resembles other forms of *ma'aser* in that each year's obligation is counted separately — one may not draw from one year's animals to satisfy another year's obligation, nor from one species to satisfy the obligation of another.

### Ten Animals Requirement

One significant way in which *ma'aser behemah* diverges from the general rules of *ma'aser* is that sheep and goats are considered a single species — a ruling quite unlike what applies to grain tithes. The *mishnah* now addresses a threshold question: an owner must have ten newly born animals within a given year to be obligated. Fewer than ten and no *ma'aser* is taken. This raises the further question of which animals count together as "one flock." If a person owns sheep in two distant locations, do they combine toward the count of ten?

## 16 Mil Distance Rule

### Shepherd Walking Distance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר שֵׁילָא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״עוֹד תַּעֲבֹרְנָה הַצֹּאן עַל יְדֵי מוֹנֶה״, וְקִים לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן דְּשִׁיתְּסַר מִיל קָא שָׁלְטָא בֵּיהּ עֵינָא דְּרוֹעֶה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that animals join together for the purposes of tithing if the distance between them is no greater than the distance a shepherd can walk and still tend them. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? What is the source that this is the maximum distance at which they can be tithed together? **Rabba bar Sheila said that the verse states:** "So says the Lord of hosts: Yet again shall there be in this place, which is desolate, without man and without animal, and in all its cities, a habitation of shepherds causing their flocks to lie down. In the cities of the hill country, in the cities of the lowland, and in the cities of the south, and in the land of Benjamin, and in the places about Jerusalem, and in the cities of Judah, **the flock shall again pass under the hands of him who counts them,** says the Lord" (Jeremiah 33:12–13). It is derived from here that animals that can pass under the hands of one shepherd are called one flock and can be counted together for the animal tithe. **And the Sages have** an accepted **tradition that the eye of the shepherd can see** up to a distance of **sixteen *mil*.**

The *mishnah* establishes the principle: *ma'aser behemah mitztaref kimlo regel behemah ro'eh* — the animals combine within the full extent of the grazing range. The *mishnah* then specifies: *shesh esrei mil*, sixteen *mil*. A *mil* is 2,000 *amot*, approximately one kilometer, making sixteen *mil* roughly sixteen kilometers, or about twelve miles. Any flocks located within that distance of one another are considered a single flock for purposes of the *ma'aser* count.

### Rashi's Two Explanations

Rashi offers two explanations for why sixteen *mil* is the operative measure. The first, which is easier to envision, is that this is the maximum distance a shepherd would take his flock out from the city to graze in a single day. Why this should determine whether two distant flocks are considered one is somewhat less clear on its face, but it does make intuitive sense as a practical matter — a shepherd might graze some sheep at one edge and others at another edge of this range.

The second explanation, which aligns more closely with the *gemara*'s reasoning, is that sixteen *mil* is the distance over which a shepherd can visually survey his scattered flock. If the animals are spread across flat terrain, or the shepherd stands at an elevation, he can see this far. Within that visual radius, all the animals are considered to be under his watch and thus constitute one flock. This explanation is admittedly harder to picture — allowing sheep to scatter sixteen kilometers while walking them is difficult to imagine — which is presumably why Rashi offered the first explanation at all. Nevertheless, both interpretations appear in Rashi's commentary on the *mishnah*.

### 30+ Mil Apart Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ בֵּין אֵלּוּ לְאֵלּוּ שְׁלֹשִׁים וּשְׁנַיִם מִיל — אֵין מִצְטָרְפִין. הָיוּ לוֹ בָּאֶמְצַע — מֵבִיא וּמְעַשְּׂרָן בָּאֶמְצַע.
>
> **Translation:** If the distance **between these** animals **and those** animals **was thirty-two *mil* they do not join together.** If **he** also **had** animals **in the middle** of that distance of thirty-two *mil*, **he brings** all three flocks to a pen **and tithes them in the middle.**

If flocks are separated by thirty-two *mil* or more, they do not combine — *ein mitztarfin*. The *gemara* will note that the *mishnah* mentions thirty-two *mil* specifically in order to introduce the ruling of the next clause: *hayu lo be'emtza* — if there are sheep located in the middle of the thirty-two *mil*, they bridge the gap. Since each outer flock is then within sixteen *mil* of the middle flock, all are considered one. Rashi clarifies that this does not mean the animals are physically brought to the midpoint; rather, the presence of sheep in the middle conceptually connects the two outer flocks.

## Deriving the 16 Mil

### Pasuk Source

The *gemara* asks: *mina hanei milei* — what is the source that flocks must be considered one unit at all? Perhaps wherever a person owns animals, even spread across the world, they simply combine for purposes of *ma'aser*? Rava bar Shila answers from the verse: *o ta'avorna hatzzon al yedei moneh* — the sheep shall pass by through a counter. This echoes the foundational verse about *ma'aser behemah*, *kol asher ya'avor tachas hashevet* — all that passes under the rod — which describes the act of counting the tenth animal. The word *ta'avor* evokes this *pasuk*; the *moneh*, the counter, is performing this act of counting for *ma'aser behemah*. The verse implies that the animals must be positioned such that they could be counted by one person — that is, they must form one observable flock.

### Shepherd's Sight Range

The *gemara* then explains: *ve-kaimu lehu Rabbanan d'shitser mil k'shiur d'eina d'ro'eh* — the Rabbis determined that sixteen *mil* is the measure of a shepherd's visual range. This is why Rashi's second explanation tracks the *gemara* so closely: the verse speaks of a *moneh* who counts, and the *gemara* understands this to mean that the animals must all be within the field of vision of a single counter. Within sixteen kilometers — under ideal conditions, on clear terrain — a shepherd atop a rise can survey his scattered flock. Only then are they "one flock" for *ma'aser* purposes. This rule is not merely a stringency but also a significant leniency: without it, one might have thought that every animal one owns anywhere in the world must be physically gathered before the obligation of *ma'aser* is calculated.

## Rav vs Shmuel Debate

### Rav's 5-10-5 Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה? אָמַר רַב: חָמֵשׁ מִכָּאן וְחָמֵשׁ מִכָּאן וְחָמֵשׁ מֵאֶמְצַע, דְּהָנֵי חָמֵשׁ חַזְיָא לְהָכָא וְחַזְיָא לְהָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how many** animals must he have on the two sides and in the middle in order to tithe them as one group? **Rav says: Five from here and five from there and five in the middle.** The reason is **that these five** in the middle **are fit** to combine **with** the animals **here and are** likewise **fit** to combine **with** the animals **there,** to amount to a total of ten, to which the obligation of animal tithe applies. For this reason all three flocks are considered as one flock.

The *gemara* now turns to the question of how exactly the middle flock combines the two outer flocks. Rav rules: *chamesh mikan ve-chamesh mikan ve-chamesh be'emtza* — five on the left, five on the right, five in the middle. The combining works only if any two adjacent groups together reach ten. Five on the left combined with five in the middle equals ten; five in the middle combined with five on the right equals ten. The middle connects the outer flocks because it forms a valid unit with each of them.

If the arrangement does not allow any two adjacent groups to reach ten, they do not combine across that gap. So for example, five on the left, three in the middle, and five on the right: neither left-plus-middle nor right-plus-middle reaches ten, and the groups do not combine across that gap. On the other hand, if you had seven on one side and three in the middle and five on the other, the seven and three combine to make ten — those two groups are one flock — but the five on the far side, being only three *mil* from the middle group, also falls within the middle's range. Whether or not those five join depends on whether the middle-plus-right also totals ten. Rashi notes that if a group falls short of ten and never becomes part of a valid *ma'aser* obligation, it is as if those animals were born alone — no *ma'aser* is taken from them at all. This matters beyond the immediate case, because animals that were once part of a valid *ma'aser* obligation carry that status forward if more animals are born before the year ends, whereas animals that were never part of the count do not.

### Shmuel's One Sheep

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ חָמֵשׁ מִכָּאן וְחָמֵשׁ מִכָּאן וְאֶחָד בָּאֶמְצַע, חָזֵינַן לְרוֹעֶה כְּמַאן דְּקָאֵי הָכָא, וְקָרֵינַן בֵּיהּ ״מוֹנֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel says: Even** if there are **five from here and five from there and** only **one in the middle** they may be tithed together. The reason is that **we see the shepherd as one who stands here** in the middle **and we apply** the verse: **"Who counts them"** (Jeremiah 33:13), to him. Since he can see and count both sides from his vantage point in the middle, they are all considered one flock.

Shmuel disagrees: *afilu chamesh mikan ve-chamesh mikan ve-echad be'emtza* — even if there is only one sheep in the middle, the two outer groups of five combine through it. One need not be able to reach ten by looking at any two adjacent groups; rather, the single sheep in the middle is treated as if the shepherd himself were standing there. The *gemara* explains Shmuel's reasoning: *chazinan le'ro'eh k'man de'kai hacha* — we regard the shepherd as if he were positioned wherever there is even one sheep. Since the shepherd could be with the middle animal, *karinan bei moneh* — we read into him the role of counter — who can see sixteen *mil* in each direction. This conceptually draws all the flocks into his single field of vision. The shepherd, in Shmuel's view, is the unifying figure, not any arithmetic sum at any particular adjacent pair.

### Villages Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הָיָה לוֹ חָמֵשׁ בִּכְפַר חֲנַנְיָה וְחָמֵשׁ בִּכְפַר עוֹתְנַי — אֵין מִצְטָרְפוֹת עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא לוֹ אַחַת בְּצִיפּוֹרִי. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Rav from a *baraita*: If **one had five** sheep **in the village of** Ḥ**ananya and five** sheep **in the village of Otnai,** which is a distance of thirty-two *mil* from the village of Ḥananya, **they do not combine, unless he** also **has one** sheep between them, **in Tzippori.** This is apparently **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rav** that there must be at least five sheep in the middle.

A *baraita* is then cited to challenge one of these positions: if a person had five sheep in the village of Kfar Chananya and five in the village of Kfar Otni — presumed to be more than sixteen *mil* apart — they do not combine. But if he had one sheep in Tzippori — located midway between them — that single sheep suffices to combine the two groups of five. The *baraita* explicitly says *achat* — one — is enough. This seems to be a clear refutation of Rav, whose rule requires that any two adjacent groups sum to ten.

### Defense of Rav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּרְגְּמַהּ שְׁמוּאֵל אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַב: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיוּ תֵּשַׁע מִכָּאן וְתֵשַׁע מִכָּאן וְאַחַת בָּאֶמְצַע, דְּהַהִיא חַזְיָא לְהָכָא וְחַזְיָא לְהָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel interpreted** the *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav.** The *baraita* does not mean he had five sheep on either side, but rather it is referring to a case **where he had nine** sheep **from here and nine** sheep **from there and one in the middle.** This case is different, **as that** one sheep in the middle **is fit** to combine **with** the animals **here and is fit** to combine **with** the animals **there,** to constitute a total of ten animals on either side, to which the obligation applies.

Shmuel, however, defends Rav's position — and the *gemara* notes with admiration that Shmuel argues *aliba de-Rav*, in defense of his disputant rather than crowing over the difficulty. Shmuel explains: the *baraita*'s statement that one sheep in Tzippori suffices must be read against a different background scenario. Although the *baraita* opens by describing five and five in the outer villages, when it then says one in Tzippori is enough, it means: when there are in fact nine on one side and nine on the other. In that case, one in the middle gives you nine-plus-one equals ten in each direction, and the combination is complete. This is a stretch — the *baraita*'s language does not naturally yield this reading — but it is Shmuel's proposed reconciliation on Rav's behalf.

## Shepherd Location Factors

### Shepherd's Presence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: וְלִשְׁמוּאֵל, אֲפִילּוּ רוֹעֶה מְצָרְפָן, וַאֲפִילּוּ כִּלְיוֹ שֶׁל רוֹעֶה מְצָרְפָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says: And according to** the opinion of **Shmuel** that one animal in the middle is sufficient to combine the two groups on either side of it, **even** if **the shepherd** himself is in the middle without any sheep, he **combines** the two flocks on either side. **And even** if only **the implements of the shepherd** are in the middle, they **combine** the two flocks on either side into one flock. Since the shepherd must go there to collect his implements it is considered as though he is already there and therefore the two flocks are combined.

Rav Papa now raises a question that extends Shmuel's logic. If the combining principle is rooted in the shepherd's presence — the idea that the shepherd is conceptually positioned wherever there is a sheep — then perhaps the shepherd's actual presence, independent of any sheep, could also serve as a combining factor. This raises the question: at what precise moment is his presence assessed? Since *ma'aser behemah* does not operate with one single fixed moment of obligation, "where the shepherd happens to be on a given day" is not a clean answer.

### House Location Theory

Rashi resolves this by reframing the question. The more natural scenario the *gemara* has in mind is not a shepherd who happens to be standing at a midpoint on a particular day, but rather the practical reality of an owner who keeps flocks in two distant locations — say, one in Yafo and one in Yerushalayim — with a shepherd whose permanent home is situated at the midpoint. A shepherd who lives equidistant from two flocks naturally serves both. For Shmuel, whose principle is that the shepherd's conceptual presence is what unifies a flock, the location of the shepherd's house functions as the combining factor: *afilu ro'eh metzarfan* — even the shepherd combines them — meaning where his house is situated midway between the two flocks.

### Equipment & Animals

The *gemara* then extends the principle further: *ve-afilu kelav shel ro'eh metzarfan* — even if the shepherd merely keeps his supplies or equipment in a middle village, that too suffices to combine the flocks. Rashi explains this as referring to his working tools stored at an intermediate location, which signals his functional presence there even without his physical residence.

### Sheepdog Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב אָשֵׁי: כַּלְבָּא דְּרוֹעֶה מַאי? כֵּיוָן דְּקָרֵי לֵיהּ וְאָתֵי לָא מִצְטָרְפִי, אוֹ דִלְמָא זִימְנִין דְּלָא אָתֵי, וּמִצְטָרֵךְ אִיהוּ לְמֵיזַל וְאֵתֹיֵיהּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi raises a dilemma: What** is the *halakha* in a case where **the dog of the shepherd** is in the middle? Does it combine the two flocks into one or not? **Since** the shepherd can **call** the dog **and it comes,** perhaps it does **not combine** the two flocks, as the shepherd himself has no need to go to the middle, so the dog is not like the implements of the shepherd. **Or perhaps,** because **sometimes** the dog **does not come** when the shepherd calls it, **and** in such cases the shepherd **himself must go and bring it,** it does combine the two flocks. The Gemara states that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Rav Ashi then poses a further question: *kalba de-ro'eh mai?* — what about the shepherd's dog? If the sheepdog is stationed at the midpoint between two flocks, does it combine them? The *gemara* identifies the tension: *keivan de-kari lei ve-ati lei metzarfan* — since when the shepherd calls the dog it comes to him, one might say the dog's presence is really the shepherd's presence, because the dog is always oriented toward and responsive to the shepherd. But *o dilma zimnin de-la ati ve-metzarfan* — perhaps sometimes the dog does not come when called, and the shepherd must go to retrieve it, in which case the dog's location is more like the shepherd's location than the shepherd's tool. The *gemara* leaves this question unresolved: *teiku*.

It is worth noting that the whole framework here is primarily a *kula*, a leniency. Without the verse establishing that flocks must be conceptually one in order to combine, one might have assumed that mere ownership obligates a person in *ma'aser behemah* regardless of where the animals are located. The entire discussion of distance, middling flocks, the shepherd's house, equipment, and dog is therefore about defining the outer parameters of what makes a flock "one" — with the legal benefit flowing to the owner who might otherwise face an impossible practical burden.

## Jordan as Boundary

### Rabbi Meir's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: הַיַּרְדֵּן מַפְסִיק לְמַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Meir says: The Jordan** River **divides** between animals on two sides of the river with regard **to animal tithe,** even if the distance between them is minimal.

The *mishnah* adds a ruling from Rabbi Meir: *HaYarden mafsik le-ma'aser behemah* — the Jordan River constitutes a dividing line for *ma'aser behemah*. Even if flocks on opposite banks are physically close to one another — well within sixteen *mil* — they do not combine if separated by the Jordan. Whether this is because of a physical barrier or a conceptual-regional distinction is precisely what the *gemara* will debate.

### Bridge Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין שָׁם גֶּשֶׁר, אֲבָל יֵשׁ שָׁם גֶּשֶׁר — גֶּשֶׁר מְצָרְפָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami says: They taught** that the Jordan River serves as a partition **only when there is no bridge there, but** if **there is a bridge there, the bridge combines** the two flocks into one for the purposes of tithing.

Rabbi Ami qualifies: *lo shanu ela she-ein sham gesher* — this rule applies only where there is no bridge. *Aval yesh sham gesher, gesher metzarefan* — but if there is a bridge, the flocks combine. Rabbi Ami's reasoning is practical: the question of whether flocks form one unit comes down to whether they can realistically be brought together, and a bridge makes that possible. One might ask whether the bridge's distance from the flock matters — if the bridge is far out of the way, does it still count? The *gemara* does not resolve this here, but the question is fair: a bridge forty *mil* away may be technically available without being practically meaningful.

### Countries Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא מְיקָרְבָן הוּא. מֵיתִיבִי: הָיוּ לוֹ בִּשְׁנֵי עֶבְרֵי הַיַּרְדֵּן אֵילָךְ וְאֵילָךְ, אוֹ בִּשְׁנֵי אַבְטִילָאוֹת כְּגוֹן שֶׁל נָמֵר וְנָמוֹרִי — אֵין מִצְטָרְפִין, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר חוּצָה לָאָרֶץ וָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **Apparently,** the Jordan River serves as a partition **because** the flock on one side **is not close** and is unable to join the flock on the other side due to the river between them. The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: If **one had** sheep **on both sides of the Jordan** River, **here and there; or** if the flocks were **in two counties [*avtilaot*], e.g., Namer and Namori,** even if they were under the control of the same ruler, the flocks **do not combine,** even if there is no river between them and they are less than thirty-two *mil* apart. **And needless to say,** if one flock is **outside of Eretz** Yisrael **and** the other is inside **Eretz** Yisrael, they do not combine.

The *gemara* then cites a *baraita*: if a person had flocks *bi-shnei aftaliyot* — in two different provinces — even under a single overarching kingdom, *ein mitztarfin* — they do not combine. Rashi glosses *aftaliyot* with the Latin-derived *acontradas*, perhaps best rendered as provinces or administrative districts. The example given is places like Namer and Nemori, which Rashi describes as separated by as little as one *mil* but belonging to different provinces. The *baraita* adds: how much more so if one flock is in *Eretz Yisrael* and another in *chutz la'aretz*, which are halakhically the most distinct categories possible. This ruling seems difficult to reconcile with Rabbi Ami's bridge-based approach. If it is only about physical accessibility, then two flocks a single *mil* apart in neighboring provinces should certainly combine — one could easily walk between them. The *gemara* makes this point explicit.

## Regional Boundaries

### Physical vs Regional

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא חוּצָה לָאָרֶץ וָאָרֶץ כִּמְקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ גֶּשֶׁר דָּמֵי, וְקָתָנֵי: לֹא מִצְטָרְפִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies its objection: **But** the partition between **outside of Eretz** Yisrael **and** inside **Eretz** Yisrael is **like a place that has a bridge,** as the two areas are not necessarily separated by water. **And** yet the *baraita* **teaches** that they **do not combine.**

The *gemara* presses the contradiction: if the Jordan only divides flocks because it presents a physical obstacle, then the case of two provinces — where one can walk directly from one to the other without any river or barrier — should present no difficulty, and yet the *baraita* rules that they do not combine. *Ve-katani lo mitztarfin* — they still do not combine. This proves that the principle is not about physical accessibility but about regional identity. Once flocks are located in conceptually distinct regions, they are treated as separate flocks regardless of the physical ease of passage between them.

### Jordan in Joshua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַיְינוּ טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהַיַּרְדֵּן יִגְבֹּל אֹתוֹ לִפְאַת קֵדְמָה״, הַכָּתוּב עֲשָׂאוֹ גְּבוּל בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara retracts its previous explanation. **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Meir: As the verse states: "And the Jordan was to be its border on the east side.** This was the inheritance of the children of Benjamin, by its borders round about, according to their families" (Joshua 18:20). Here, **the verse renders** the Jordan River as **a border in and of itself.**

Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba citing Rabbi Yochanan therefore provides the real reason for Rabbi Meir's ruling: *de-amar kra, ve-haYarden gevulo lif'at kedma* — a verse in Yehoshua, in the context of the division of the land among the tribes, states that the Jordan shall be its boundary to the east. *Hakatuv asao gevul bifnei atzmo* — the Torah has designated the Jordan as a boundary in its own right. This is not merely a geographic description; it is a halakhic designation that the Jordan marks the edge of one defined region and the beginning of another.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, ״וְתָאַר הַגְּבוּל״ ״וְעָלָה הַגְּבוּל״, הָכִי נָמֵי הַכָּתוּב עֲשָׂאוֹ גְּבוּל בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: But **if that is so,** should one say that the demarcation of the land allotted to each tribe has the status of a border? Consider the verse: **"And the border was drawn** and turned about on the west side southward, from the mountain that lies before Beit Horon southward; and its goings out were at Kiriath Ba'al, which is Kiriath Jearim, a city of the children of Judah; this was the west side" (Joshua 18:14). And consider the verse: **"And the border went up** to the side of Jericho on the north, and went up through the hill country westward; and its goings out were at the wilderness of Beit Aven" (Joshua 18:12). Here **also, the verse** apparently **renders** each of these places **a border by itself.** If so, flocks on either side should not combine for purposes of tithing.

The *gemara* anticipates an objection: if every tribal boundary in Yehoshua constitutes a halakhic dividing line, then flocks on opposite sides of any tribal boundary should not combine either. The *gemara* answers with a verse from Devarim: *zot tihyeh lachem ha'aretz ligvuloteha saviv* — this shall be your land, defined by its surrounding boundaries — implying that all of *Eretz Yisrael* is one unit. The tribal divisions are internal; the Torah treats *Eretz Yisrael* as a single country within its outer borders. *Kula Eretz Yisrael gevul echad* — all of *Eretz Yisrael* is one boundary.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״זֹאת תִּהְיֶה לָכֶם הָאָרֶץ לִגְבֻלֹתֶיהָ סָבִיב״, כּוּלַּהּ אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל גְּבוּל אֶחָד הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** with regard to the demarcation of each inheritance, it **is different, as the verse states:** "And the border shall go down to the Jordan, and its goings out shall be at the Dead Sea; **this shall be your land according to its borders round about"** (Numbers 34:12). This teaches that **all of Eretz Yisrael is** considered to be within **one border,** notwithstanding the demarcations of each inheritance within.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, יַרְדֵּן נָמֵי! ״אֶרֶץ״ — וְלֹא יַרְדֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a further difficulty: **If so, the Jordan** River **also** should not be considered a border with regard to animal tithes. The Gemara explains that the verse states: "This shall be your **land,"** which is referring to those parts of land that are connected, **but not** to areas separated by the **Jordan** River, which is not land but water.

This brings into focus a distinction that runs through the entire discussion: *Ever HaYarden* — Transjordan — has *kedushah* and was part of the inheritance of two and a half tribes, yet it does not fall within the biblical description of *Eretz Yisrael* proper. When the Torah gives the borders of *Eretz Yisrael* in Devarim, *Ever HaYarden* is not included within those lines. Within *Eretz Yisrael* itself, therefore, no internal division separates flocks. But *Ever HaYarden*, despite its sanctity, is considered a distinct region. The *gemara* then notes the logical implication of the verse: *eretz*, the land — and not the Jordan itself. The Jordan River is not part of the land.

The connection between the *avtaliyot* ruling and the *Ever HaYarden* ruling involves two partially overlapping but distinct principles. One principle is political reality: different provinces under different administrations define different regions. Another principle is what might be called *Torah*-defined reality: the biblical designation of *Eretz Yisrael* and *Ever HaYarden* as distinct territories is itself sufficient to make them different regions, regardless of political arrangements. Both principles can be operative simultaneously, and when they converge — as with *Eretz Yisrael* and *Ever HaYarden* — the result is especially clear.

## Ever HaYarden Status

### Kedusha Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא — הַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי ״יַרְדֵּן״, אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי אַמֵּי — לִיתְנִינְהוּ לְכוּלְּהוּ נְהָרוֹת! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba,** who maintains that the ruling of Rabbi Meir is a Torah edict, **this** is the reason **that the** *tanna* **teaches: The Jordan** River divides between animals on two sides of the river with regard to animal tithe. **But according to Rabbi Ami,** who holds that the Jordan River serves as a partition because the animals are unable to cross from one side to the other, **let** Rabbi Meir **teach** his principle **with regard to all rivers** that cannot be crossed. They should all divide flocks with regard to animal tithe. The Gemara comments: This is **difficult.**

The *gemara* establishes that *Ever HaYarden* occupies an intermediate status. It has *kedushat Eretz Yisrael* in certain respects — including obligations of *terumot u'ma'asrot* — and it formed part of the tribal inheritance. Yet it is not *Eretz Yisrael* proper. It is called *Ever HaYarden* precisely because it stands on the far side of the Jordan boundary. For the purposes of *ma'aser behemah*, this distinction alone — not any physical barrier — renders the two sides of the Jordan separate regions that do not combine.

### Bikkurim Example

One practical illustration of the distinction: *bikurim* — the first fruits offering — may only be brought from *Eretz Yisrael* proper, not from *Ever HaYarden*. This is not because *Ever HaYarden* lacks sanctity, but because the obligation of *bikurim* is tied specifically to *Eretz Yisrael* within its biblical borders. Similarly, for *ma'aser behemah*, flocks in *Ever HaYarden* and flocks in *Eretz Yisrael* proper are in different regions and do not combine — even though both regions carry obligations of *terumot u'ma'asrot*, and even though *Ever HaYarden* was halakhically part of Israel's inheritance.

## Tannaim Debate

### Beteira Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי: ״כִּי אַתֶּם עֹבְרִים אֶת הַיַּרְדֵּן אַרְצָה כְּנָעַן״, ״אַרְצָה״ — אֶרֶץ כְּנַעַן, וְלֹא הַיַּרְדֵּן אֶרֶץ כְּנַעַן, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that the question of whether or not the Jordan River is part of Eretz Yisrael is **the subject of** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is taught in a *baraita*: The verse states: "Speak to the children of Israel, and say to them: **When you pass over the Jordan into the land of Canaan"** (Numbers 35:10). The term **"into the land"** indicates that **the land of Canaan** is considered part of Eretz Yisrael, **but the Jordan** River itself **is not** considered part of **the land of Canaan;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira.**

The *gemara* cites a tannaitic dispute that may reflect this very question. The verse in Bamidbar reads: *ki atem ovrim et haYarden artza Kena'an* — for you are crossing the Jordan into the land of Canaan. Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira derives from this: *eretz Kena'an* — the land of Canaan — and not the Jordan, and by extension not *Ever HaYarden* either. The Jordan and the far bank are categorically distinct from the land of Canaan.

### Yochai Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי אוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״מֵעֵבֶר לְיַרְדֵּן יְרֵחוֹ קֵדְמָה מִזְרָחָה״, מָה יְרֵחוֹ אֶרֶץ כְּנַעַן, אַף יַרְדֵּן אֶרֶץ כְּנַעַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says:** The verse **states** with regard to the portions of the tribes of Reuben, Gad, and half of Manasseh: "The two tribes and the half tribe have received their inheritance **beyond the Jordan at Jericho eastward, toward the sunrise"** (Numbers 34:15). **Just as Jericho** is part of **the land of Canaan, so too, the Jordan** River is part of **the land of Canaan.**

Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai reads differently. The verse elsewhere speaks of *me'ever l'Yarden Yericho kedma mizracha* — from across the Jordan of Jericho, eastward. The phrase *Yarden Yericho* — the Jordan of Jericho — connects the Jordan to Jericho. Just as Jericho is part of *Eretz Kena'an*, so too the Jordan and its environs are part of *Eretz Kena'an*. The *gemara* understands this tannaitic dispute as bearing on the question of whether *Eretz Yisrael* and *Ever HaYarden* are considered one region or two for purposes of *ma'aser behemah*. Note, however, that this is not the same debate as the earlier dispute between Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba. That earlier debate was about whether regions matter at all, or only physical accessibility. This tannaitic debate presupposes that regions matter and asks instead how to define the regional boundary — is *Ever HaYarden* within or outside *Eretz Kena'an*?

## Ever HaYarden Status (II)

### Kaftor VaPerach

The *gemara*'s discussion of *Eretz Yisrael* and *Ever HaYarden* calls to mind a distinction developed at length by the author of *Kaftor VaPerach*, the comprehensive work on *Eretz Yisrael* composed by a student of the Rosh in the thirteenth century. That work — remarkable for its breadth at a time before printing, requiring enormous copyist labor — dedicates a central chapter to the halakhic implications of *kedushat Eretz Yisrael*. The author draws a distinction between *shem Eretz Yisrael* — what is called the Land of Israel in the sense of the inherited homeland promised to the Patriarchs — and the narrower halakhic *kedushah* of *Eretz Yisrael* that governs obligations like *terumot u'ma'asrot*.

This distinction illuminates the debate here. One might argue that after the destruction of the *beit hamikdash*, if *kedushat Eretz Yisrael* is no longer operative, then living in *Eretz Yisrael* carries no more significance than living elsewhere. But *Kaftor VaPerach* rejects this: the land's special relationship with the Jewish people — the value of living there, of being buried there — is rooted in its identity as the homeland, not in the halakhic *kedushah* that governs agricultural tithes. By the same logic, *Ever HaYarden* may carry halakhic *kedushah* in the limited sense of *terumot u'ma'asrot* while still not being *Eretz Yisrael* in the fuller, inherited sense. Someone who wished to be buried in *Eretz Yisrael* would not be satisfied with burial in *Ever HaYarden*, even though the latter carries some form of sanctity.

## Jordan Definition

### Jericho Southward

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין ״יַרְדֵּן״ אֶלָּא מִבֵּית יְרֵחוֹ וּלְמַטָּה. לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אִילֵּימָא לְנוֹדֵר — הַלֵּךְ אַחַר לְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם, וְכֹל הֵיכָא דְּקָרוּ לֵיהּ ״יַרְדֵּן״ אִיתְּסַר לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא לְמַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The river is called **Jordan only from Beit Jericho and below,** i.e., to the south. The Gemara asks: **With regard to what *halakha*** is this statement relevant? **If we say** it is relevant **for one who takes a vow,** e.g., that water from the Jordan River is forbidden to him, this cannot be so, as there is a principle with regard to vows that one must **follow** the ordinary **language of people.** The meaning of a vow is interpreted in accordance with the manner in which the words are used in common speech. **And** therefore, **anywhere that** most people **call** the river by the name **Jordan, it is prohibited for him** to drink from it, regardless of whether it is north or south of Beit Jericho. **Rather,** the Gemara explains that Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement is relevant **for animal tithe.**

Rabba bar bar Chana citing Rabbi Yochanan states: *ein Yarden ela miBeit Yericho u'lemata* — only from Yericho southward is halakhically considered "the Jordan" for purposes of dividing flocks. The waters north of Yericho, though physically the same river, do not constitute the halakhic Jordan for this purpose.

The *gemara* asks what the practical application of this ruling might be. One suggestion: a person who takes a vow not to drink from the waters of the Jordan — could he drink from the river north of Yericho? The *gemara* rejects this application: for vows, we follow *lashon bnei adam* — common usage. Whatever people call the Jordan is forbidden to one who vows against it, and people call the whole river the Jordan. The ruling must therefore apply to *ma'aser behemah*: only the stretch of river from Yericho southward constitutes the dividing boundary for flocks.

This is somewhat puzzling. The reasoning given throughout for why the Jordan divides flocks is that the Torah designates it as the eastern boundary of *Eretz Yisrael*. Yet the biblical boundary between *Eretz Yisrael* and *Ever HaYarden* runs along the entire length of the Jordan — north of Yericho as well. If the dividing logic is conceptual-regional rather than physical, it is not immediately clear why the halakhic Jordan for *ma'aser* purposes should be limited to the southern stretch.

### Jericho Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: יַרְדֵּן יוֹצֵא מִמְּעָרַת פַּמְיָיס, וּמְהַלֵּךְ בְּיַמָּהּ שֶׁל סִיבְכִי, וּבְיַמָּהּ שֶׁל טְבֶרְיָא, וּבְיַמָּהּ שֶׁל סְדוֹם, וְהוֹלֵךְ וְנוֹפֵל לַיָּם הַגָּדוֹל, וְאֵין ״יַרְדֵּן״ אֶלָּא מִבֵּית יְרִיחוֹ וּלְמַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** According to the opinion of Rabbi Meir the Jordan River acts as a partition only south of Beit Jericho, but north of that point it is not a partition. The Gemara notes: **That is also taught** in a *baraita*: The **Jordan** River **issues forth from the Cave of Pamyas and flows via the Sea of Sivkhi,** i.e., Lake Hula, **and via the Sea of Tiberias,** i.e., the Sea of Galilee, **and via the Sea of Sodom,** i.e., the Dead Sea, **and continues and falls** down **to the Great Sea. But it is** called **Jordan only from Beit Jericho and below,** i.e., to the south.

The *gemara* supports the ruling with the same verse cited earlier: *me'ever l'Yarden Yericho* — the Jordan of Yericho. The Torah connects the Jordan specifically to Yericho, implying that what is called the Jordan in its halakhic sense is only the stretch adjacent to Yericho. The *baraita* confirms the ruling: the Jordan emerges from the cave of Pamyas in the north, flows through the Sea of Sivchi, the Sea of Tiberias (the *Kinneret*), and the Sea of Sdom (the Dead Sea). Yet despite this extended course, the statement *ve-ein Yarden ela miBeit Yericho u'lemata* — only from Yericho southward is it the Jordan. The tension between this geographic description and the halakhic limiting of the Jordan's dividing function to its southern reaches remains somewhat unresolved in the *gemara*.

## Rivers Etymology

### Jordan Etymology

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ ״יַרְדֵּן״? שֶׁיּוֹרֵד מִדָּן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אַתּוּן מֵהָתָם מַתְנִיתוּ לַהּ, אֲנַן מֵהָכָא מַתְנֵינַן לַהּ, ״וַיִּקְרְאוּ (לוֹ) לְלֶשֶׁם דָּן בְּשֵׁם דָּן אֲבִיהֶם״. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: לֶשֶׁם זוֹ פַּמְיָיס, וְתַנְיָא: יוֹצֵא יַרְדֵּן מִמְּעָרַת פַּמְיָיס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Why is** the river **called Jordan? Because it descends [*yored*] from** the city of **Dan. Rabbi Abba said to Rav Ashi: You learned** that the Jordan River emerges from the territory of Dan **from there,** i.e., from its name. **We learn it from here:** "And the border of the children of Dan went out from them; and the children of Dan went up and fought against Leshem, and took it, and smote it with the edge of the sword, and possessed it, and dwelt there, **and called Leshem: Dan, after the name of Dan their father"** (Joshua 19:47). **And Rabbi Yitzḥak says** that **this Leshem** is a city that was known in the talmudic period as **Pamyas. And it is taught** in a *baraita* that **the Jordan** River **emerges from the Cave of Pamyas.**

Having discussed the Jordan at length, the *gemara* offers an *aggadic* digression on the etymology of rivers. Rabbi Yochanan asks: *lamah nikra shemo Yarden?* — why is it called the Jordan? The answer: *she-yarad miDan* — because it descends from Dan. The tribe of Dan was situated in the far north; the river's name encodes its northern origin and downward course. Rav Abba tells Rav Ashi that in Bavel they derive this from the wordplay on *Yarden* itself, while in *Eretz Yisrael* the source is a verse: *vayi-kre'u le-Leshem Dan be-shem Dan avihem* — they called Leshem "Dan" after the name of their father Dan. Rabbi Yitzchak teaches that Leshem is Pamyas, and since the *baraita* states that the Jordan emerges from the cave of Pamyas, it follows that the Jordan comes from Dan. The chain of reasoning: Leshem equals Pamyas, Pamyas is the Jordan's source, therefore the Jordan originates in Dan.

### Prat as Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: זַכְרוּתֵיהּ דְּיַרְדְּנָא מִמְּעָרַת פַּמְיָיס, הֵיכָא דְּאָמַר: ״לָא שָׁתֵינָא מַיִם מִמְּעָרַת פַּמְיָיס״ — אִיתְּסַר לֵיהּ בְּכוּלֵּיהּ יַרְדְּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana says: The source of the Jordan** River is **from the Cave of Pamyas.** Therefore, in a case **where** one **says: I will not drink water from the Cave of Pamyas, it is prohibited for him** to drink water **from the entire Jordan** River.

Rav Kahana introduces the concept of *zachruta* — essence or quintessence. The *zachrutei di-Yardena* — the essence of the Jordan — is the cave of Pamyas, its source. Accordingly, one who vows not to drink from the waters of Pamyas is forbidden from the entire Jordan, since all of its waters originate there. The *gemara* then applies the same concept elsewhere: the *zachruta de-dama* — the quintessence of blood — is the liver (*kavda*), as Rabbi Yitzchak rules that a dissolved liver conveys impurity like blood itself, being concentrated as it is. And *zachruta de-maya* — the quintessence of all waters — is the Euphrates (*Prat*). Rav Yehuda citing Rav: one who vows not to drink from the waters of the Euphrates is forbidden from all waters in the world, since all waters ultimately originate from the Euphrates. The precise formulation matters: if one says "I will not drink from the waters of the Euphrates," that only forbids water taken directly from the Euphrates. But if one says "I will not drink from waters that came from the Euphrates" — since all waters trace back to it — nothing is permitted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זַכְרוּתָא דִּדְמָא — כַּבְדָּא, כִּדְרַבִּי יִצְחָק, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: כָּבֵד שֶׁנִּימּוֹחַ — מְטַמֵּא בִּרְבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Kahana also states: **The source of blood is the liver.** The halakhic ramification of this observation is **in accordance with** a statement **of Rabbi Yitzḥak, as Rabbi Yitzḥak says: A liver that dissolved,** i.e., a decomposed liver from a corpse, **imparts** ritual **impurity** if it has the volume **of a quarter-***log*, which is the minimum amount of blood that imparts ritual impurity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זַכְרוּתָא דְּמַיָּא — פְּרָת, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: הַנּוֹדֵר מִמֵּימֵי פְּרָת — אָסוּר בְּכׇל מֵימוֹת שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Kahana also states: **The source of** all **the water** in the world is the **Euphrates** River. The halakhic ramification of this is in accordance with a statement of Rav Yehuda, **as Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** With regard to **one who takes a vow** rendering **the waters of the Euphrates** River forbidden to him, **it is prohibited** for him to drink **from any water in the world.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵימָא דְּאָמַר ״לָא שָׁתֵינָא מִמֵּימֵי דִּפְרָת״ — מֵי פְרָת הוּא דְּלָא שָׁתֵינָא, הָא מִנַּהֲרָא אַחֲרִינָא שָׁתֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** in which it is prohibited to drink from any water in the world? **If we say** this is a case **where he says: I will not drink from the water of the Euphrates** River, **it is** only **the waters of the Euphrates** River **that he may not drink,** whereas **he may drink** water **from another river,** since one follows the ordinary language of people.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, דְּאָמַר: ״לָא שָׁתֵינָא מִמַּיִם דְּאָתוּ מִפְּרָת״, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כׇּל הַנְּהָרוֹת לְמַטָּה מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה נְהָרוֹת, וּשְׁלֹשָׁה נְהָרוֹת לְמַטָּה מִפְּרָת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is referring to a case **where he says: I will not drink from** any **water that comes from the Euphrates** River. It is prohibited for him to drink any water at all, **as Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: All the rivers are below,** i.e., they receive their waters **from, three rivers:** The Pishon, the Gihon, and the Tigris (see Genesis 2:11–14). **And** these **three rivers are below** and receive their waters **from the Euphrates** River.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאִיכָּא עֵינָתָא דְּמִידַּלְּיָין! אָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: הָנְהוּ סוּלָּמֵי דִּפְרָת נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **But there are springs that are higher** in the mountains than the Euphrates; how can their water come from the Euphrates? **Rav Mesharshiyya said: These are ladders of the Euphrates,** i.e., the waters of the Euphrates seep through the ground and are drawn upward to emerge from these springs.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״וְהַנָּהָר הָרְבִיעִי הוּא פְרָת״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it written:** "And a river went out of Eden to water the garden; and from there it was separated, and became four heads…And the name of the third river is Tigris; that is the one that goes toward the east of Ashur. **And the fourth river is the Euphrates"** (Genesis 2:10, 14)? This indicates that the Euphrates, which is mentioned last, is the least of the four rivers, not the source of the other three.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: הוּא פְּרָת דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said, and some say** it was **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov** who said: **It is the Euphrates that** the verse mentions **initially** as the river that went out of Eden, which divided into all the other rivers. After the other three branched out from it, the Euphrates continued to flow.

### Yuval vs Prat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: ״יוּבַל״ שְׁמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָיָה כְּעֵץ שָׁתוּל עַל מַיִם וְעַל יוּבַל יְשַׁלַּח שׇׁרָשָׁיו״, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ ״פְּרָת״? שֶׁמֵּימָיו פָּרִים וְרָבִים.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir says: Yuval is the name of** the Euphrates River where it emerges from Eden, **as it is stated: "For he shall be as a tree planted by the waters, and that spreads out its roots by the river [*yuval*],** and shall not see when heat comes, but its foliage shall be luxuriant; and shall not be anxious in the year of drought, neither shall cease from yielding fruit" (Jeremiah 17:8). **And why is it named Euphrates [*perat*]? Because its waters are fruitful [*parim*] and multiply** without the need for rainfall.

A *baraita* cites Rabbi Meir: the Euphrates' true original name is Yuval, as derived from the verse *ve-al Yuval yishlach sharashav* — by the Yuval shall the tree send down its roots. The *gemara* understands this verse as referring to the tree of *Gan Eden* watered by the original river, and thus Yuval is the proper name of the Euphrates. Why then is it called Prat? That is a descriptive name: *shemeimav parin ve-rabin* — because its waters are fruitful and multiply, constantly increasing. Prat is not a proper name but a characterization of the river's nature, namely its tendency to rise and swell.

## Mikvah Rainwater

### Shmuel's Father's Practi…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לִשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: נַהְרָא מִכֵּיפֵיהּ מִיבְּרִיךְ. וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַב, דְּאָמַר רַב אַמֵּי אָמַר רַב: מִיטְרָא בְּמַעְרְבָא סָהֲדָא רַבָּה פְּרָת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds that this **supports** the opinion **of Shmuel, as Shmuel says: The river is blessed from its riverbed;** the additional water in the river is not from rainfall but rather from subterranean sources. **And** this statement **disagrees** with the opinion **of Rav, as Rav Ami says** that **Rav says:** When **rain** falls **in the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **the great witness** attesting to that fact is **the Euphrates,** as the water flow in the Euphrates increases after the rainy season.

The observation that the Euphrates *parin ve-rabin* — increases — is cited as supporting the position of Shmuel, who holds that *nahara meikeifei mivrach* — when a river swells, it swells from its bedrock source, from natural underground springs rather than from rainfall. This carries an important halakhic application. The *gemara* relates: *avuha de-Shmuel* — Shmuel's father — would prepare *mikva'ot* for his daughters in *Nissan*, and mats in *Tishrei*. Shmuel lived in Bavel; his daughters needed to immerse in a valid *mikvah*. In *Tishrei*, at the end of summer before the rains began, the Euphrates was safe to use as a *mikvah*, but mats were required — either to avoid mud that might constitute a *chatzitzah*, or for privacy. In *Nissan*, however, after the long rainy season, a separate *mikvah* had to be constructed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל עָבֵיד לְהוּ לִבְנָאתֵיהּ מִקְוֶה בְּיוֹמֵי נִיסָן, וּמַפָּצֵי בְּיוֹמֵי תִשְׁרֵי. מִקְוֶה בְּיוֹמֵי נִיסָן — סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַב, דְּאָמַר רַב אַמֵּי אָמַר רַב: מִיטְרָא בְּמַעְרְבָא סָהֲדָא רַבָּה פְּרָת, שֶׁמָּא יִרְבּוּ נוֹטְפִין עַל הַזּוֹחֲלִין, וְהָווּ לְהוּ מֵי גְשָׁמִים רוּבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Shmuel's father fashioned a ritual bath for his daughters during the days of Nisan, and** placed **mats** in the Euphrates River **during the days of Tishrei** when his daughters immersed in the river. He fashioned **a ritual bath during the days of Nisan,** because **he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav, as Rav Ami says** that **Rav says:** When **rain** falls **in the West, the great witness** attesting to that fact is **the Euphrates.** Shmuel's father was concerned that **perhaps** the rainwater and snow water **that fell** and accumulated in the river **would exceed** the amount of naturally **flowing** spring water that originated in the river's sources, **and the** flowing **rainwater would be the majority.** Rainwater purifies only when it is collected; it does not purify when it is flowing. Therefore, Shmuel's father fashioned ritual baths of standing water for his daughters.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַפָּצֵי בְּיוֹמֵי תִשְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** he placed **mats during the days of Tishrei,** when his daughters immersed in the river itself, to give them privacy. During Tishrei the waters of the Euphrates do not come from rainwater; rather, the waters are from the river itself, and therefore immersion is valid even when that water is flowing.

The reason: a *mikvah* assembled from collected rainwater must be stationary — it cannot be flowing. A natural river, fed by underground springs, is permitted even as flowing water. But if the majority of a river's water consists of rainwater — *mei geshamim* — it is treated halakhically as a *mikvah* of rainwater, and flowing water of that character cannot be used for immersion. Shmuel's father was concerned that after the heavy rains of winter, the Euphrates contained more rainwater than natural spring water — *shema yirbu ha-notfim al ha-zochalim*, the dripping (rain) exceeds the flowing (natural water). If so, *vehavu lehu mei geshamim rubba* — the rainwater would constitute the majority, and immersion in the flowing river would be invalid. Hence the need for a separate *mikvah* in spring.

### Shmuel's Strictness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דִּידֵיהּ אַדִּידֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין הַמַּיִם נִטְהָרִים בְּזוֹחֲלִין אֶלָּא פְּרָת בְּיוֹמֵי תִשְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** this statement **of** Shmuel's, that the additional water in the Euphrates is not from rainfall but from subterranean sources, **disagrees** with another ruling that he **himself** issued. **As Shmuel says: Waters purify when flowing,** i.e., when they are not collected into a ritual bath, **only** in the **Euphrates during the days of Tishrei.** Since rain does not fall in the summer, only after the summer, in Tishrei, is it clear that the water one is immersing in is in fact river water.

The *gemara* notes that Shmuel's father's practice aligns with Rav's position that rainfall increases the Euphrates, and contradicts Shmuel's own stated principle that rivers swell from their bedrock source. Shmuel himself was if anything stricter still: *ein ha-mayim metaherin be-zochalin ela Prat beyomei Tishrei* — flowing water of the Euphrates can be used for immersion only in *Tishrei*. For the rest of the year, Shmuel treated the river as potentially compromised by rainwater. This contradicts his own stated position that rivers increase from underground, and the *gemara* marks it as *peliga didei adidei* — Shmuel arguing against himself. The practical upshot is significant for the laws of *mikva'ot*: anyone seeking to immerse in a river must assess the season and rainfall levels, since a preponderance of rainwater in a flowing river disqualifies it as a *mikvah*.

### Rashi's Mayim Chayim

The shiur closes with a striking position unique to Rashi here. The accepted interpretation — followed by Tosafot and adopted by Rashi in other contexts — is that the problem with rainwater in a flowing river is the flowing itself: a *mikvah* assembled from rainwater must be stationary, so flowing rainwater-majority water is invalid. Rashi in this passage, however, offers a more radical reading. Since women today are treated as potential *zavot* — and a *zavah* requires *mayim chayim*, naturally flowing spring water, for her immersion — if the majority of the river is rainwater, it no longer qualifies as *mayim chayim* in the requisite sense, and immersion is invalid not merely because it is flowing but because it is not the right kind of water entirely. This position was raised by certain *Geonim* and was comprehensively rejected by the *Rishonim*. Had it been accepted, the practical consequences would have been severe: virtually all existing *mikva'ot* would have been disqualified, and immersion would have required either the ocean or a dug well drawing from underground water. Interestingly, many medieval European *mikva'ot* were in fact dug wells, drawing on underground water that would qualify as *mayim chayim* — making the position not entirely implausible in that context. Nevertheless, the *halacha* does not follow it, and the standard *mikva'ot* built today rest on the firmly established ruling that rejects Rashi's reading here.
