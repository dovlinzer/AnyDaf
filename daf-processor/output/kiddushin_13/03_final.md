# Kiddushin 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hahu Gavra - Mat Case

### Mat Marriage Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּקַדֵּישׁ בְּצִיפְּתָא דְאָסָא, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: וְהָא לֵית בַּהּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה! אֲמַר לְהוּ: תִּיקְדּוֹשׁ בְּאַרְבַּע זוּזֵי דְּאִית בַּהּ. שְׁקַלְתַּהּ וְאִישְׁתִּיקָא.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain man who betrothed a woman with a mat of myrtle branches. People who were present said to him: But it is not worth one *peruta*. He said to them: Let her be betrothed with the four dinars that are wrapped in the mat. The woman took the mat and was silent.**

The Gemara opens with the case of *hahu gavra* — a man who betrothed a woman using a mat woven from reeds of *hadas*, myrtle. The incident is located approximately ten lines from the bottom of 12b, where the Gemara has been presenting a series of real-life cases centered primarily on whether the object used for *kiddushin* was worth a *shaveh prutah*. (The broader context is a series of such stories, following a separate point about a *kiddushin* performed in the marketplace, for which Rav would administer lashes on account of its lack of appropriate seriousness.)

In this case, bystanders objected to the man that the mat was not worth a *shaveh prutah* — a reminder that while a *prutah* today may be worth roughly two cents, in a pre-industrial economy where everything was handmade, even a small coin carried real purchasing power, and a simple mat might genuinely have fallen beneath that threshold.

### Hidden Zuzim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָוֵה שְׁתִיקוּתָא דִּלְאַחַר מַתַּן מָעוֹת, וְכֹל שְׁתִיקוּתָא דִּלְאַחַר מַתַּן מָעוֹת, לָאו כְּלוּם הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: This is silence after the money is given, and any silence after the money is given is nothing.**

The man responded to the objection by saying: *tikadesh b'arba zuzei d'ita bah* — let her be betrothed with the four *zuzei* hidden inside the mat. A *zuz* is a *dinar*, worth nearly two hundred times a *prutah*, so four *zuzei* represent a sum far in excess of the minimum. The complication, however, is that she had already taken possession of the mat before he made this announcement. She therefore took the mat without knowing that it contained his money. When he declared that the four *zuzei* should serve as *kesef kiddushin*, she simply said nothing — *shekaltah v'ishtika*, she held onto the mat and was silent. The question is whether that silence, following his declaration, constitutes an act of acceptance sufficient to effect *kiddushin*.

Rava rules: *havi shetikah d'le'achar matan maot*, this is a case of silence after the money has already been transferred, *v'chol shetikah d'le'achar matan maot lav klum hu* — and any such silence is of no legal consequence. Rava draws a sharp distinction between two types of conduct: actively reaching out and accepting an object when someone says *harei at mekudeshet li b'davar zeh* is a positive act of acceptance; merely failing to extract the coins from the mat and return them is an absence of rejection, not an affirmative acceptance. The two are categorically different. If the money is already in one's possession without any conscious act of receiving it, the failure to throw it back does not rise to the level of a *kinyan*.

### Rava's Source in the Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ, דְּתַנְיָא: אָמַר לָהּ: כִּנְסִי סֶלַע זוֹ בְּפִקָּדוֹן, וְחָזַר וְאָמַר לָהּ: הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בּוֹ. בִּשְׁעַת מַתַּן מָעוֹת – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, לְאַחַר מַתַּן מָעוֹת, רָצְתָה – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, לֹא רָצְתָה – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: From where do I state this opinion? As it is taught in a *baraita* that in a case where a man said to a woman: Take this *sela* as a deposit, and he subsequently went back and said to her: Be betrothed to me with it, if he said this at the time the money was given, she is betrothed. In a case where he said this after the money was given, then if she wanted to be betrothed in this manner, she is betrothed. If she did not want it, she is not betrothed.**

Rava then provides the source for this ruling from a *braita*. The case in the *braita* is: a man says to a woman, *kinsi sela zu b'pikadon* — take this *sela* (worth approximately 800 *prutot*) as a *pikadon*, to watch on my behalf. She accepts and holds it as a *shomer*. He then turns to her and says, *hitkadshi li bo* — be betrothed to me with it. The *braita* rules: if he said this *b'sha'at matan maot*, at the very moment he handed over the *sela*, she is *mekudeshet* if she agrees. *Le'achar matan maot*, if he said it after the money had already been handed over, *ratzta mekudeshet, lo ratzta eina mekudeshet* — if she agrees, she is betrothed; if she does not agree, she is not.

### Parsing the Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״רָצְתָה״ וּמַאי ״לֹא רָצְתָה״? אִילֵימָא ״רָצְתָה״ – דְּאָמְרָה: ״אִין״, לֹא רָצְתָה דְּאָמְרָה: ״לָא״, מִכְּלָל דְּרֵישָׁא, כִּי אָמְרָה ״לָא״ נָמֵי הָווּ קִידּוּשִׁין? אַמַּאי, וְהָא קָאָמְרָה ״לָא״! אֶלָּא לָאו: ״רָצְתָה״ – דְּאָמְרָה: ״אִין״, לֹא רָצְתָה – דְּאִישְׁתִּיקָה מִשְׁתָּקה. וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ שְׁתִיקָה דִּלְאַחַר מַתַּן מָעוֹת – וְלֹא כְּלוּם הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: **What is the meaning of: She wanted, and what is the meaning of: She did not want? If we say that: She wanted, means that she said yes, she wishes to be betrothed, and: She did not want, means that she explicitly said no, one can learn by inference that in the first clause of the *baraita*, when he spoke as he gave her the money and no difference is suggested between her wanting or not wanting, that if she said: No, it would also be a betrothal. But why should that be the case? After all, she said: No. Rather, is it not to be explained in this manner, i.e., that the phrase: She wanted, means she said yes, and: She did not want, means that she remained silent. And one can learn from this that silence after the money is given is nothing.** Her silence is not interpreted as an agreement to the betrothal.

The Gemara then presses: *mai ratzta, mai lo ratzta* — what precisely do *ratzta* and *lo ratzta* mean here? If *ratzta* means she explicitly said yes, and *lo ratzta* means she explicitly said no, then we would be left with an excluded middle: what about silence? But this cannot be the correct reading, because if explicit refusal were the only disqualifying scenario, then even in the first clause — where he speaks *b'sha'at matan maot* — an explicit refusal should also prevent *kiddushin*, and the *braita* would be teaching nothing new for the *le'achar matan maot* case. The contrast between the two clauses only makes sense if what works in one case and fails in the other is precisely silence. Therefore: *ratzta* must mean she actively said yes, and *lo ratzta* — the case where the *kiddushin* fails — must mean she was silent. This in turn demonstrates that *shetikah d'le'achar matan maot* is not a valid form of acceptance.

A practical application of this principle arises even under the *chuppah*: if the groom places the ring on the bride's finger and only then recites *harei at mekudeshet li b'davar zeh*, one could raise the question of whether this constitutes *shetikah d'le'achar matan maot*. The case under the *chuppah* is arguably different, since the entire proceeding is framed as *kiddushin* from the outset, but the question illustrates the real-world stakes of the principle.

## Pikadon vs. Kiddushin

### Rav Huna Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָשׁוּ בָּהּ בְּפוּם נַהֲרָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם בְּתוֹרַת פִּקָּדוֹן יַהֲבִינְהוּ נִיהֲלַהּ, סָבְרָה: אִי שָׁדֵינָא לְהוּ וּמִיתַּבְרִי – מְחַיַּיבְנָא בְּהוּ, הָכָא בְּתוֹרַת קִדּוּשִׁין יַהֲבִינְהוּ נִיהֲלַהּ, וְאִי אִיתָא דְּלָא נִיחָא לַהּ – לִישְׁדִּינְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **In the city of Pum Nahara they raised a difficulty against Rava's proof, in the name of Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua: Is it comparable? There, in the *baraita*, he gave her the money as a deposit but they are his. She therefore thinks: If I throw them and they break I will be obligated for them, as would anyone who destroys another person's property. Consequently, her silence is interpreted as consent only to continue in her role of holding it as a deposit. By contrast, here he gives them to her as betrothal money so that they would belong to her. And if it is so, that she is not amenable to being betrothed, let her throw them away.**

The Gemara records that in the academy of Pumnara, a challenge to Rava's ruling was raised in the name of Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua: *mi dami* — is this truly comparable? The *pikadon* case and the mat case are structurally different. In the *pikadon* case, *hatam betorat pikadon yahvinhu nihala* — the money was originally given to her in the capacity of a deposit; she became a *shomer* over it. When the man later proposes to use it as *kesef kiddushin*, her silence cannot be interpreted as acceptance, because she is already under an obligation to hold the object. The Gemara elaborates: *savra* — she reasons to herself that if she throws it back and it breaks or is lost, *mechayavna behu*, she will be liable. A *shomer* cannot simply discard what she is guarding. Her continued possession of the object therefore reflects her duty as a *shomer*, not any acceptance of *kiddushin*.

### Gemara Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּרֵיךְ רַב אַחַאי: אַטּוּ כּוּלְּהוּ נְשֵׁי דִּינָא גְּמִירִי? הָכִי נָמֵי סַבְרַהּ: אִי שָׁדֵינָא לְהוּ וּמִיתַּבְרִי מִיתְחַיַּיבְנָא בְּאַחְרָיוּתַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥai refutes this argument: Is that to say that all women are learned in *halakha*? In this case also, she thinks: If I throw them and they break I will be responsible for them.** Therefore, I will not throw them. One cannot deduce from the fact that she does not throw the coins that she accepted them as betrothal money.

The Gemara distinguishes: *hacha betorat kiddushin yahvinhu nihala* — in the mat case, the original transfer was framed entirely within the context of *kiddushin*. He gave her the mat saying *harei at mekudeshet li*, and she agreed to receive it. The only deficiency was that the mat itself lacked a *shaveh prutah* — but there were four *zuzim* hidden inside it. In this context, silence is quite reasonably interpreted as accepting those *zuzim* as *kesef kiddushin*, because *ve'ei sevira d'la nicha lah* — if she truly did not wish to be betrothed with the four *zuzim*, *lishdayinhu* — she should throw them back. She is not a *shomer* here; she has no obligation to retain the object. If she keeps it in silence, that silence reasonably signals acceptance.

Rav Acha then objects: *atu kuli neshei dina g'miri* — do you assume that all women know *halacha*? Even in the *kiddushin* context, one could argue that she retains the mat not because she is accepting the *kiddushin* but because she fears liability: *ei shadina lehu umitvri, mechaivna be'achrayusa* — if I throw it back and it breaks, I will have to pay. This fear — that returning something might create liability — is not a uniquely legal concern; it is the kind of intuitive worry anyone might have. Accordingly, silence here too cannot unambiguously be read as acceptance of *kesef kiddushin*.

### Ravina Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחָה רַב אַחָא בַּר רַב לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבִינָא: כְּהַאי גַּוְונָא מַאי? שְׁלַח לֵיהּ: אֲנַן לָא שְׁמִיעַ לַן הָא דְּרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, אַתּוּן דִּשְׁמִיעַ לְכוּ – חוּשׁוּ לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Rav, sent this question to Ravina: In a case like this, what is the *halakha*? Ravina sent the following response to him: We did not hear this statement of Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua. Therefore, we do not need to take it into consideration. You, who heard it, should be concerned about a possible betrothal.**

Rav Acha bar Rav sent the question to Ravina: in such a case, what is the *halacha*? Ravina replied: *ana lo shmiah lan ha de'Rav Huna breih de'Rav Yehoshua* — we have not received the tradition of Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua that draws this distinction between the *pikadon* case and the *kiddushin* case. Those who have not received this tradition presumably treat both cases identically, and would rule that silence is not acceptance. *Atun de'shmiah lechu chushu lah* — but you, who have received this tradition and who draw the distinction, must be concerned, and must treat the silence in the *kiddushin*-framed case as a possible *safek kiddushin*.

The question of why this ruling should vary by locale is interesting: Rav Huna bar Rav Yehoshua did not issue a binding *psak halacha*; he raised a cogent logical basis for distinguishing between the cases. And yet, evidently, having a *tradition* that a distinction is well-founded is not quite the same as having independently heard the argument — it represents something between a received *psak* and a purely personal analysis. The result is that in the locale that received this tradition, a *safek kiddushin* is the operative ruling.

## Varshachi Case

### Man Grabs Bundle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא אִיתְּתָא דַּהֲוָה קָא מְזַבְּנָה וַורְשְׁכֵי, אֲתָא הָהוּא גַּבְרָא חֲטַף וַורְשְׁכָא מִינַּהּ, אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: הַבֵהּ נִיהֲלִי! אֲמַר לַהּ: אִי יָהֵיבְנָא לִיךְ מִיקַּדְּשַׁתְּ לִי? שְׁקַלְתֵּיהּ וְאִישְׁתִּיקָה. וְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: יְכֵולָה לְמֵימַר: אִין, שְׁקַלִי, וְדִידִי שְׁקַלִי.
>
> **Translation:** **There was a certain woman who was selling belts. A certain man came and snatched a belt [*varshekha*] from her. She said to him: Give it to me. He said to her: If I give it to you will you be betrothed to me? She took it and was silent, and Rav Naḥman said: She could say: Yes, I took it, but I took my property.**

The Gemara presents a new case: *hahu itata de'hava ka'mezabna varshachi* — there was a woman who was selling *varshachi*. Rashi describes this as a bundle of linen, though other commentators offer gems or a type of head covering; the precise identity of the merchandise is secondary. A man came, *chataf varshacha minah* — grabbed one of the bundles from her. She said to him: *hav nihali* — give it back to me, it is mine. He replied: *ei yahivna lach mekudeshet li* — if I give it back to you, will you be betrothed to me with it? *Shakaltia ve'ishtaka* — she took it back and was silent. He handed it to her upon this conditional declaration, and she accepted it without a word.

### Rav Nachman Ruling

Rav Nachman rules that she can say *in shakli ve'didi shakli* — yes, I took it, but I took back what was mine. Even though he said *harei at mekudeshet li* and physically handed it to her, and even though she accepted it without objecting, that acceptance does not constitute *kiddushin* — she was simply retrieving her own property.

### Rava Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: קִידְּשָׁה בְּגָזֵל וּבְחָמָס, וּבִגְנֵיבָה, אוֹ שֶׁחָטַף סֶלַע מִיָּדָהּ וְקִדְּשָׁהּ – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת! הָתָם בִּדְשַׁדֵּיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised an objection to the opinion of Rav Naḥman from a *baraita*: If a man betrothed a woman with property that was taken by robbery; or with property taken by a forced transaction, or with property that was taken by theft; or if he grabbed a *sela* from her hand and betrothed her with it, she is betrothed. If so, why does Rav Naḥman say here that when he took it from her and betrothed her she is not betrothed? Rav Naḥman answered: There, it is referring to a case where he arranged to marry her, and therefore if she remained silent she certainly agreed to the betrothal.**

Rava challenges Rav Nachman from a *braita*: *kidsah be'gezel u'be'chamas u'be'gneivah o she'chataf sela mi'yadah ve'kidsha mekudeshet* — if he betrothed a woman with money he stole, or with *chamas* (taking an object by force of payment that the owner never agreed to accept, a form of *gezel*), or with *gneivah*, or if he grabbed a *sela* from her hand and betrothed her with it — she is *mekudeshet*. This creates an apparent difficulty: how can a man betroth a woman using money he does not own? And of the two scenarios — stolen from someone else, or grabbed from her — the latter seems more obviously problematic.

The Gemara navigates the first scenario through the principle of *yeush koneh*: once the original owner despairs of recovering stolen property, the thief acquires legal title, though he remains obligated to reimburse the owner. However, when the object was stolen directly from the woman standing right in front of him, she has obviously not despaired — *mi ya'eshet*? The Gemara ultimately inclines toward the view that *yeush* is not *koneh*, so *gezel* from third parties would not work, and the entire *braita* is understood as referring exclusively to the case where the object was stolen from her. The mechanism by which this works is that her silence — when he says *harei at mekudeshet li* and returns the object — constitutes an implicit waiver of her ownership, allowing him to hold it for a moment as his own in order to be *mekadesh* her with it.

This is directly in tension with Rav Nachman's ruling that she can claim *in shakli ve'didi shakli*. How can the *braita* say she is *mekudeshet* if she could always claim she was merely taking back her own?

### Sheduch Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא תֵּימְרָא דְּשָׁנֵי לַן בֵּין שַׁדֵּיךְ וּדְלָא שַׁדֵּיךְ, דְּתַנְיָא: אָמַר לָהּ: ״כִּנְסִי סֶלַע זוֹ שֶׁאֲנִי חַיָּיב לִיכִי״ וְחָזַר וְאָמַר לָהּ: ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בּוֹ״, בִּשְׁעַת מַתַּן מָעוֹת, רָצְתָה – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, לֹא רָצְתָה – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. לְאַחַר מַתַּן מָעוֹת, אֲפִילּוּ רָצְתָה – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where do you say that there is a difference between a case where he had previously arranged to marry her and where he had not arranged to do so? As it is taught in a *baraita* that with regard to a man who said to a woman: Take this *sela* that I owe you, and he went back and said to her: Be betrothed to me with it, if he said this at the time the money was given and she wanted it, she is betrothed; if she did not want it, she is not betrothed. If he issued his statement after the money was given, then even if she wanted it, she is not betrothed.**

The Gemara resolves the contradiction: *hatam be'deshadich* — in the *braita* there was a *sheduch*, an engagement period, prior to the incident. When the parties have been formally betrothed in the *shidduchin* sense and have been planning to marry, the contextual backdrop leads us to read her silence as genuine acceptance. She is implicitly permitting him to hold the object so that he can use it to be *mekadesh* her with it. By contrast, if a stranger in the marketplace suddenly grabs a woman's merchandise and attempts this maneuver, there is no such contextual basis for reading her silence as anything other than retrieving her own property.

The Gemara also asks: *u'mena teimara de'shani lan bein shadich le'lo shadich* — from where do we know this distinction matters? The answer comes from a *braita* concerning debt repayment, which will be analyzed in the next section.

## Debt Money Case

### Debt Payment Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״רָצְתָה״ וּמַאי ״לֹא רָצְתָה״? אִילֵימָא ״רָצְתָה״ – דְּאָמְרָה אִין, לֹא רָצְתָה – דְּאָמְרָה לָא, הָא אִישְׁתִּיקָה – הָווּ קִידּוּשִׁין. וְנִתְנֵי ״מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת״ סְתָם, כִּי הָתָם! אֶלָּא: ״רָצְתָה״ – דְּאָמְרָה ״אִין״, ״לֹא רָצְתָה״ – דְּאִישְׁתִּיקָה, וְקָא תָנֵי דְּאֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. מַאי טַעְמָא? יְכֵולָה לְמֵימַר: אִין, שְׁקַלִי, וְדִידִי שְׁקַלִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman clarifies: **What is the meaning of: She wanted, and what is the meaning of: She did not want? If we say that: She wanted, means that she said yes, and: She did not want, means that she explicitly said no, it can be inferred that if she was silent it is a valid betrothal. But if so, let the *baraita* teach simply that she is betrothed, without specification, as it did there in the previous case, with regard to one who said: Take this *sela* as a deposit. It is obvious that if she explicitly said no, she is not betrothed. Rather, the phrase: She wanted, means she said yes, and: She did not want, means that she remained silent. And in this case of silence the *baraita* taught that she is not betrothed. What is the reason that she is not betrothed if she remained silent? It is that she can say: Yes I took it, but I took my own property and did not intend to become betrothed.**

The *braita* states: a man tells a woman, *kinsi sela zu she'ani chayav lichi* — take this *sela* that I owe you, here is repayment of my debt. He then immediately says, *hitkadshi bo* — now be betrothed to me with it. The *braita* rules: *b'sha'at matan maot*, if he said this at the moment of handing over the money, *ratzta mekudeshet, lo ratzta eina mekudeshet* — if she agrees, she is betrothed; if not, she is not. *Le'achar matan maot*, if he said it after the money was already in her hand, *afilu ratzta eina mekudeshet* — even if she says yes, it does not work.

The second ruling — that even explicit agreement fails once the money has passed — requires explanation. The reason is that the moment the debt is paid, the money becomes fully hers. He is therefore asking her to be betrothed using her own money. One cannot effect *kiddushin* with the recipient's own property.

The Gemara again asks: *mai ratzta, mai lo ratzta* — what do these terms mean in the first clause, where the timing is *b'sha'at matan maot*? Following the same logical structure applied earlier in the *pikadon braita*: if *ratzta* means an explicit yes and *lo ratzta* means an explicit no, then silence would remain ambiguous, and the *braita* should simply have said *mekudeshet* without qualification. The necessity of specifying *ratzta* and *lo ratzta* as alternatives implies that *lo ratzta* covers the silent case — she is not betrothed if she says nothing — while *ratzta* means she actively and explicitly said yes. This confirms the earlier ruling: when money is handed over in a non-*kiddushin* context, an explicit acceptance is required for *kiddushin* to take effect.

Why does silence fail here even in the *b'sha'at matan maot* case? Because she is receiving money that is owed to her in any event. Unlike a gratuitous gift where the only plausible reason for accepting would be *kiddushin*, here she has an independent reason to take the money — it is a debt repayment. And unlike the case of stealing, where the item at least is handed back to her in a transactional moment framed as *kiddushin*, a debt creates an even more natural ground for taking the money without any acceptance of the *kiddushin* condition: *yechola le'meimar in shakli ve'didi shakli* — I simply took what was owed to me.

Indeed, the debt case is in certain respects an even stronger instance of this principle than the *gezel* case, because a debt need not be repaid at this specific moment; he could pay it later. When he chooses this particular moment to pay and simultaneously declares *harei at mekudeshet li*, there is no necessary connection between the two acts from her perspective.

This creates a sharp difficulty: if silence fails even in the relatively ambiguous debt case — where one might have thought the context could reasonably suggest she accepted the *kiddushin* framing — then all the more so should silence fail in the *gezel* case. Yet the *braita* rules that in the *gezel* case she *is* betrothed! The Gemara resolves this, as noted above, through the *sheduch* distinction: *ha de'shadich, ha de'lo shadich* — the *braita* where *kiddushin* takes effect from stolen property is the case where there was a prior engagement period. In that setting, silence is read as acceptance. The debt *braita*, by contrast, and the marketplace grabbing case of Rav Nachman, involve no such prior context, and silence therefore does not constitute agreement.

The upshot of the entire discussion is two distinct principles about silence. The first — *shetikah d'le'achar matan maot* — addresses the case where money was already in the woman's possession before the declaration of *kiddushin*, and rules that her failure to return it is not an act of acceptance. The second principle addresses the case where money is handed over simultaneously with or just before a *kiddushin* declaration, but the money is being given for an independent reason (debt repayment, restoration of stolen goods): here too silence is not acceptance, because she may simply be taking what is already hers. The exception in both cases is a prior *sheduch* context, which provides the background for reading her conduct as consent.

## Rav Assi Teachings

### Rav Assi Deathbed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַסִּי, עֲיַילוּ רַבָּנַן לְנַקּוֹטִינְהוּ לִשְׁמַעְתָּתֵיהּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ הַהוּא מֵרַבָּנַן וְרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב שְׁמֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי מָנִי: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאֵין אִשָּׁה נִקְנֵית בְּפָחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה – כָּךְ אֵין קַרְקַע נִקְנֵית בְּפָחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rabbi Asi died, the Sages assembled to collect his statements, i.e., they wished to review his rulings of *halakha* lest they be forgotten. A certain member of the Sages, and Rabbi Ya'akov was his name, said to them: This is what Rabbi Asi said that Rabbi Mani said: Just as a woman cannot be acquired by less than the value of one *peruta*, so too land cannot be acquired by less than the value of one *peruta*.**

The Gemara transitions with the notice: *ki nach nafsheih de'Rav Assi, ailu rabanan le'liktinhu le'shmateih* — when Rav Assi died, the rabbis gathered to collect his teachings, his *shmateih*, lest they be forgotten. One of the rabbis — Rav Yaakov by name — offered a tradition in the name of Rav Assi citing Rav Mani. What follows is a cluster of teachings, some original to Rav Assi and some transmitted by him in the names of earlier authorities.

Rav Assi said in the name of Rav Mani: *keshem she'ein isha nikneit be'pachot mi'shaveh pruta, kach ein karka nikneit be'pachot mi'shaveh pruta* — just as a woman cannot be acquired in *kiddushin* for less than a *shaveh prutah*, so too land cannot be acquired by *kesef* for less than a *shaveh prutah*. Tosafot notes the irony that this comparison learns *karka* from *isha*, whereas the usual direction of inference in the Talmud runs the other way. The teaching may be intended as a parallel rather than a formal legal derivation.

### Woman & Land Value

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: וְהָתַנְיָא: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין אִשָּׁה נִקְנֵית בְּפָחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה – קַרְקַע נִקְנֵית בְּפָחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה! אֲמַר לְהוּ: כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא – בַּחֲלִיפִין, דְּתַנְיָא: קוֹנִין בִּכְלִי, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **They said to him: But isn't it taught in a *baraita*: Although a woman cannot be acquired by less than the value of one *peruta*, land can be acquired by less than the value of one *peruta*? Rabbi Ya'akov said to them: When that *baraita* is taught to the effect that land can be acquired by less than the value of one *peruta* but a woman cannot, it was with regard to acquisition through symbolic exchange, i.e., a legal act of acquisition formalizing the transfer of ownership of an article. As it is taught in a *baraita*: One can acquire land and movable items through a symbolic exchange by giving a vessel, even though it is not worth one *peruta*.**

A *braita* challenges: *af al pi she'ein isha nikneit be'pachot mi'shaveh pruta, karka nikneit be'pachot mi'shaveh pruta* — even though a woman requires a *shaveh prutah*, land can be acquired for less. The response: *ki tanya hahi be'chalipin* — that *braita* is discussing acquisition through *chalipin*, such as a *kinyan sudar*, not through *kesef*. The *braita* itself supports this: *konin be'kli ve'af al pi she'ein bo shaveh pruta* — one effects *chalipin* with an object even if it is not worth a *prutah*. The rule is therefore: *kesef* for *karka* requires a minimum of a *shaveh prutah*, just as *kesef kiddushin* does; *chalipin* for *karka* does not. An interesting asymmetry remains, however: *chalipin* works for *karka* but not for *isha* — a point addressed in an earlier *sugya*.

### Moral Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר יָתְבִי וְקָאָמְרִי, הָא דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ בְּטִיב גִּיטִּין וְקִדּוּשִׁין לֹא יְהֵא לוֹ עֵסֶק עִמָּהֶן. אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְקָשִׁים לְעוֹלָם יוֹתֵר מִדּוֹר הַמַּבּוּל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אָלֹה וְכַחֵשׁ וְרָצֹחַ וְגָנֹב וְנָאֹף פָּרָצוּ וְדָמִים בְּדָמִים נָגָעוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **They then sat and said additional statements in the name of Rabbi Asi. One statement concerned that which Rav Yehuda says that Shmuel says: Anyone who does not know the nature of bills of divorce and betrothals should have no dealings in them, as his ignorance might lead people to commit serious sins. In this context, Rabbi Asi says that Rabbi Yoḥanan says: And those who deal with these issues without proper knowledge are worse for the world than the generation of the flood, as it is stated: "Swearing and lying, and killing, and stealing, and committing adultery. They break all bounds, and blood touches blood"** (Hosea 4:2).

The gathering of rabbis then produced another tradition: *chad amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel* — one of the rabbis cited the well-known dictum of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel that *kol she'eino yode'a betiv gittin ve'kiddushin lo yehei esek imahem* — whoever does not understand the intricacies of *gittin* and *kiddushin* should have nothing to do with them. On this, Rav Assi said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *dekashim le'olam yoter mi'dor ha'mabul* — those who blunder in this area cause harm to the world worse than the generation of the flood.

### Understanding the Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַשְׁמַע? כְּדִמְתַרְגֵּם רַב יוֹסֵף: מוֹלְדִין בְּנִין מִנְּשֵׁי חַבְרֵיהוֹן חוֹבִין עַל חוֹבִין מוֹסִיפִין, וּכְתִיב ״עַל כֵּן תֶּאֱבַל הָאָרֶץ וְאֻמְלַל כׇּל יוֹשֵׁב בָּהּ בְּחַיַּת הַשָּׂדֶה וּבְעוֹף הַשָּׁמָיִם וְגַם דְּגֵי הַיָּם יֵאָסֵפוּ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where may it be inferred that this verse is referring to individuals of this kind and that they are worse for the world than the generation of the flood? The Gemara explains: As Rav Yosef translated the phrase "and committing adultery, they break all bounds, and blood touches blood," as: They give birth to children through the wives of others; they add sins upon sins.** **And it is written in the next verse: "Therefore the land mourns and everyone who dwells there languishes, with the beasts of the field and the fowls of heaven, and the fishes of the sea are also taken away"** (Hosea 4:3).

The verse from Hoshea is cited: *alo ve'chachesh ve'ratzoach ve'ganov, ve'na'of, paretzu ve'damim be'damim naga'u* — swearing, denying, murder, burglary, and adultery; they burst forth and blood touches blood. Rav Yosef interprets the critical phrase *paretzu*: *molidin benin minshei chavreihon chovin al chovin mosifin* — they father children from their neighbors' wives, *ve'chovin al chovin mosifin* — and add iniquity upon iniquity. The connection to incorrect *psak* in *kiddushin* is that such errors lead to situations of unknowing adultery, which in turn produces illegitimate lineages, which compounds the transgression.

### Worse than the Flood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ בְּדוֹר הַמַּבּוּל לֹא נִגְזְרָה גְּזֵירָה עַל דָּגִים שֶׁבַּיָּם. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִכֹּל אֲשֶׁר בֶּחָרָבָה מֵתוּ״ – וְלֹא דָּגִים שֶׁבַּיָּם. וְאִילּוּ הָכָא, אֲפִילּוּ דָּגִים שֶׁבַּיָּם.
>
> **Translation:** This indicates that those who cause illegitimate children to be born lead to such a great tragedy that even the fish of the sea will die, **whereas in the generation of the flood the decree was not imposed upon the fish in the sea, as it is stated: "Whatever was on dry land died"** (Genesis 7:22). The explicit mention of dry land indicates that **the fish in the sea did not die. But here, in the punishment described in the verses in Hosea, even the fish in the sea** died.

The verse continues: *al ken te'evel ha'aretz ve'umlal kol yoshev bah be'chayat ha'sadeh u've'of ha'shamayim ve'gam degei ha'yam ye'asefu* — even the fish of the sea will be gathered in destruction. The Gemara notes: *ve'ilu be'dor ha'mabul lo nigzar gezeira al dagim she'ba'yam* — but in the flood, the fish were spared, as the verse says *mikol asher be'charava metu* — only those on dry land perished, not the fish. Here, even the fish are destroyed — so this is worse than the flood.

### Objections and Responses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא עַד דְּעָבֵיד לְכוּלְּהוּ! לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי מִפְּנֵי אָלָה אָבְלָה הָאָרֶץ״. וְאֵימָא: אָלָה לְחוּד, וְהָנָךְ לְחוּדֵיהוֹן! מִי כְּתִיב ״וּפָרָצוּ״? ״פָּרָצוּ״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara anticipates two objections. First: **But one can say that this punishment is not administered until they perform all of them, i.e., only if they commit all of the sins mentioned in the verse, including taking false oaths, committing murder, and stealing. The Gemara answers: It should not enter your mind to say this, as it is written: "For because of swearing the land mourns"** (Jeremiah 23:10). This verse teaches that on account of taking false oaths alone the world can be destroyed, from which it may be inferred that each these acts on its own is enough to warrant destruction. The Gemara further asks: **But one can say that taking a false oath is distinct, as it involves the desecration of God's name, and these other sins are distinct, i.e., they are less severe offenses that entail a less serious punishment.** Therefore, it is possible that this great tragedy will result only if one performs all of them. The Gemara explains: **Is it written: And they break all bounds, with the conjunction: And, which would indicate that all the sins are included as one unit? Rather, it is written: "They break all bounds,"** from which it may be inferred that this punishment is given separately for each one of these sins.

The Gemara notes the implication: *alma kasavar shibuda lav d'Oraisa* — Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel holds that liens arising from obligations are not biblically mandated. If they were, the Torah-imposed obligation to bring an *olah* would automatically create a lien on her estate, obligating the heirs to discharge the debt to the *Beis HaMikdash* even without prior designation. The fact that he limits the obligation to a case where she already designated the animal shows that without a specific act creating a concrete obligation, no lien exists.

### Korban Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדוּר יָתְבִי וְקָאָמְרִי: הָא דִּתְנַן: הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁהֵבִיאָה חַטָּאתָהּ וּמֵתָה – יָבִיאוּ יוֹרְשִׁין עוֹלָתָהּ. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: וְהוּא שֶׁהִפְרִישַׁתָּה מֵחַיִּים. אֲבָל לֹא הִפְרִישַׁתָּה מֵחַיִּים – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **They then sat and said additional statements in the name of Rav Asi, one of which concerned that which we learned in a mishna (*Kinnim* 2:5): With regard to a woman after childbirth who brought her sin-offering for her ritual purification and died, the heirs bring her burnt-offering to fulfill her remaining sacrificial obligations. Rav Yehuda says that Shmuel says: And this is the *halakha* only when the woman herself had already separated, i.e., designated, the animal for the burnt-offering in her lifetime. But if she had not separated it in her lifetime, no, the heirs are not required to bring a burnt-offering on her behalf.**

A third teaching from the gathering: *ha'ishah she'heiviah chatatah u'meisah, yavi'u yorshim olasah* — a woman who was obligated to bring a *chatas* and an *olah* (as with a woman after childbirth), brought her *chatas* and then died before bringing her *olah*; the heirs may bring her *olah*. This is possible because while a *chatas* cannot be offered on behalf of a deceased owner, an *olah* can.

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *vehu she'hifrisha michayim* — this is only the case if she had already sanctified and designated the animal for the *olah* during her lifetime. *Aval lo hifrisha michayim, lo* — if she died before designating the animal, the heirs have no obligation.

### Shibuda Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא קָסָבַר שִׁיעְבּוּדָא לָאו דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא. אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אַף עַל גַּב שֶׁלֹּא הִפְרִישַׁתָּה מֵחַיִּים. אַלְמָא קָסָבַר: שִׁיעְבּוּדָא הָוֵה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא. וְהָא פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ חֲדָא זִימְנָא, דְּרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מִלְוָה עַל פֶּה – אֵינָהּ גּוֹבָה מִן הַיּוֹרְשִׁין וְלֹא מִן הַלָּקוֹחוֹת. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מִלְוָה עַל פֶּה – גּוֹבָה בֵּין מִן הַיּוֹרְשִׁין, בֵּין מִן הַלָּקוֹחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **Apparently, Shmuel maintains that the property of a debtor is not liened by Torah law.** In other words, one does not say that because she was required to bring a burnt-offering there is a lien on her property and the debt must be paid even if she did not set aside the animal before her death. **Rabbi Asi says that Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This is the *halakha* even though she did not separate the burnt-offering in her lifetime. Apparently, Rabbi Yoḥanan maintains that the property of a debtor is liened by Torah law.** The Gemara asks: **But these Sages already disagreed about this issue once; why would they engage in the same dispute with regard to multiple applications?** **As Rav and Shmuel both say: With regard to a loan by oral agreement, i.e., without a document that places a lien on the land, if the debtor dies, the creditor cannot collect the loan from the heirs or from those who purchased the land. This is the *halakha* even if it is clear that there was a loan and the debtors do not dispute this fact. And Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish both say: With regard to a loan by oral agreement, the creditor can collect from the heirs and from those who purchased from the debtor. This shows that those same Sages already disagreed as to whether a lien on a property exists by Torah law in the absence of a document.**

Rav Assi then cites Rabbi Yochanan in direct disagreement: *af al pi shelo hifrisha michayim*, even if she did not designate the animal during her lifetime, the heirs must bring the *olah*. *Alma kasavar shibuda havei d'Oraisa* — Rabbi Yochanan holds that obligations do automatically create liens on property from Torah law.

One might wonder whether this dispute is limited to *korbanot*, where the *Beis HaMikdash* has a uniquely strong monetary claim. But the Gemara notes that this debate is already implicit in a broader dispute between Rav and Shmuel on one side and Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish on the other. *De'Rav u'Shmuel amri tarvaihu: malveh al peh eino goveh min ha'yorshim ve'lo min ha'lokchot* — Rav and Shmuel both hold that an oral loan, unrecorded in a *shtar*, cannot be collected from heirs or from purchasers of the debtor's property. *Rabbi Yochanan ve'Reish Lakish amri tarvaihu: malveh al peh goveh min ha'yorshim u'min ha'lokchot* — Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish hold that an oral loan can be collected from both heirs and purchasers.

This maps directly onto the *shibuda* question: does any obligation, by its very nature, create automatic liens on property, or is the lien only created through the formal act of executing a *shtar*?

### Why Both Formulations Matter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אִיתְּמַר בְּהָא – בְּהָךְ קָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא מִלְוָה כְּתוּבָה בַּתּוֹרָה הִיא, אֲבָל בְּהָךְ – אֵימָא מוֹדֶה לְהוּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וּלְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בְּהָא – בְּהָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּמִלְוָה כְּתוּבָה בַּתּוֹרָה כִּכְתוּבָה בִּשְׁטָר דָּמְיָא, אֲבָל בְּהָךְ – אֵימָא מוֹדֶה לֵיהּ לִשְׁמוּאֵל, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is **necessary to teach both cases as, if it were stated only about this case of a loan, one might say: It is only with regard to this situation that Shmuel says there is no lien on property, because it is a loan that is not written in the Torah. This loan is merely a private agreement between two individuals. But with regard to this obligation of a woman to bring a burnt-offering, one might say that he concedes to Rabbi Yoḥanan and to Reish Lakish. Since this woman's obligation to bring a burnt-offering is written in the Torah, perhaps it creates a lien on her property despite the fact that there is no document to that effect. And conversely, had the *amora'im* taught us about their dispute only in this case of the burnt-offering of a woman following childbirth, one might say that Rabbi Yoḥanan says that the Torah imposes a lien only in this case of the burnt-offering, as a loan written in the Torah is comparable to one inscribed in a document. But with regard to this private loan, one might say that he concedes to Shmuel that the lien does not apply by Torah law. Therefore, it is necessary to state the dispute in both cases.**

The Gemara then explains why both formulations of the debate — the *korban* case and the *malveh al peh* case — are necessary and cannot be collapsed into one. If we only had the *malveh al peh* case and Shmuel ruled there are no liens, one could argue that a voluntary loan does not carry Torah-mandated liens precisely because the obligation is self-assumed rather than divinely imposed. *Aval beha* — but in the *korban* case, where the Torah itself imposes the obligation, perhaps Shmuel would concede that such obligations do create liens, and perhaps he would agree with Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish. Conversely, if we only had the *korban* case and Rabbi Yochanan ruled there are liens, one might argue that a Torah-imposed obligation is uniquely powerful — as the Gemara puts it, *milve ha'ketuvah ba'Torah ki'ketuvah be'shtar damya*, a debt inscribed in the Torah is comparable to one inscribed in a *shtar* — and that even Rabbi Yochanan would concede that a mere oral loan lacks such a basis. *Tzricha* — both formulations are necessary to establish the full scope of each position.

The practical question of *how* we arrive at the ruling that an oral loan is collectible from heirs but not from purchasers also requires clarification. One theoretical framework holds that *shibuda d'Oraisa* is in force for all obligations, but the protection of *lokchot* — innocent purchasers who had no way of knowing of the debt — overrides it as a rabbinic enactment. A separate framework holds that fundamentally no debt carries automatic liens, but a *shtar* creates an implicit granting of lien rights to the creditor. These two frameworks have different implications: on the first view, the *shtar*-less debt does carry Torah-level liens that are rabinically suspended for *lokchot* but not for *yorshim*; on the second, the protection of *yorshim* is less obvious.

### Rav Pappa Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הִילְכְתָא, מִלְוָה עַל פֶּה – גּוֹבֶה מִן הַיּוֹרְשִׁין, וְאֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה מִן הַלָּקוֹחוֹת. גּוֹבֶה מִן הַיּוֹרְשִׁין – שִׁיעְבּוּדָא דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, וְאֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה מִן הַלָּקוֹחוֹת – דְּלֵית לֵיהּ קָלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said that the *halakha* is as follows: With regard to a loan by oral agreement, the creditor can collect from the heirs but he cannot collect from those who purchased from the debtor. The reason he can collect from the heirs is that the accepted opinion is that the property of a debtor is liened by Torah law. But he cannot collect from those who purchased from the debtor, because a loan by oral agreement does not generate publicity,** i.e., people will not have heard about it. Therefore, they are not careful to avoid buying property from the debtor. These purchasers should not suffer due to a lien that was not well known.

Rav Pappa rules: *hilcheta malveh al peh goveh min ha'yorshim ve'eino goveh min ha'lokchot* — an oral loan can be collected from heirs but not from purchasers. The reasoning follows: *goveh min ha'yorshim* — *shibuda d'Oraisa* is in force, confirming that obligations create automatic Torah-level liens. *Ve'eino goveh min ha'lokchot* — but against purchasers it cannot be collected, *de'leis lei kala*, because since the loan was not recorded and publicly known, we protect the innocent purchasers who had no notice. The *yorshim*, by contrast, are not third-party purchasers who paid money in reliance on the debtor's unencumbered estate; they simply receive what their ancestor had, subject to his existing obligations.

## Woman's Release

### Get & Death Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקוֹנָה אֶת עַצְמָהּ בְּגֵט וּבְמִיתַת הַבַּעַל. בִּשְׁלָמָא גֵּט, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְכָתַב לָהּ סֵפֶר כְּרִיתֻת״.
>
> **Translation:** **The mishna teaches: And a woman acquires herself through a bill of divorce or through the death of the husband. The Gemara asks: Granted, this is the *halakha* with regard to a bill of divorce, as it is written explicitly in the Torah: "And he writes for her a scroll of severance,** and gives it in her hand, and sends her out of his house; and she departs out of his house and she goes and becomes another man's wife" (Deuteronomy 24:1–2). This indicates that a bill of divorce enables a woman to marry whomever she wishes after the divorce.**

The Gemara now transitions to the next *mishnah*: *ve'konah et atzmah be'get u'be'misat ha'baal* — a woman acquires her own freedom through a *get* and through the death of her husband. The discussion of *kiddushin* naturally gives way to the question of how *kiddushin* is dissolved.

*Bishlama get* — that a *get* releases her is clear, as the Torah explicitly commands *ve'kasav lah sefer keritut*, a bill of severance. But the husband's death — *ela misat ha'baal, menalan* — where does the Torah ever explicitly state that she is released through his death?

### Arayot Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִיתַת הַבַּעַל מְנָלַן? סְבָרָא הוּא: הוּא אַסְרַהּ וְהוּא שָׁרְתַהּ. וְהָא עֲרָיוֹת, דְּאָסַר לְהוּ וְלָא שָׁרֵי לְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **But from where do we derive that the death of the husband also enables a woman to remarry? The Gemara answers: This is based on logical reasoning: He, the husband, rendered her forbidden to every man, and he has permitted her. Since the husband is no longer alive, there is no one who renders her forbidden. The Gemara asks: But consider the status of those with whom relations are forbidden, as he renders her forbidden to them, and yet he does not permit her to them when he dies. For example, one may not marry one's father's wife even after the father dies.**

The Gemara first suggests a *sevara*, a logical inference: *hu asrah ve'hu sharya* — he made her forbidden (by marrying her), so when he dies he makes her permissible. The Gemara rejects this immediately: *de'arayot de'asru lehu ve'lo shari lehu* — the logic does not hold, because by many of the *arayot*, a woman becomes forbidden to a specific person through a relationship that no longer exists, yet the prohibition persists even after the death of the party who created the forbidden connection. The clearest example is a father's wife: she becomes forbidden to the son because of her marriage to the father. Yet even after the father dies, the prohibition does not lift. So death does not automatically dissolve forbidden relationships, and we cannot simply assume that a wife is released upon her husband's death.

### Yevamah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִדְּאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא יְבָמָה שֶׁאֵין לָהּ בָּנִים – אֲסוּרָה, הָא יֵשׁ לָהּ בָּנִים – מוּתֶּרֶת. וְדִילְמָא: אֵין לָהּ בָּנִים – אֲסוּרָה לְעָלְמָא וְשַׁרְיָא לְיָבָם, וְיֵשׁ לָהּ בָּנִים – לְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא נָמֵי אֲסוּרָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, from the fact that the Merciful One states that a *yevama* who does not have children from her deceased husband is prohibited from marrying anyone besides the *yavam*, it may be inferred that a widow who has children from her deceased husband is permitted to remarry. The Gemara asks: But perhaps one should infer the following from that *halakha*: A woman who does not have children from her deceased husband is forbidden to everyone and permitted to the *yavam*, and a woman who has children from her deceased husband is forbidden to everyone also.**

The Gemara attempts a derivation from the laws of *yibbum*: the Torah states that a *yevamah she'ein lah banim asurah* — a childless widow is forbidden to all other men because of her obligation to the *yavam*, the deceased husband's brother. This implies *ha yesh lah banim mutteret* — implicitly, if she has children and is exempt from *yibbum*, she is permissible. The Gemara pushes back: *vedilma* — perhaps even if she has children, she remains forbidden to the world at large; the only thing the childless case tells us is that she is also *forbidden* to non-*yavam* men, while the case with children merely removes the *yavam*'s claim, leaving the general question of her status unresolved. The inference therefore fails.

### Widow & Kohen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, מִדְּאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא אַלְמָנָה לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל – אֲסוּרָה, הָא לְכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט שַׁרְיָא. וְדִילְמָא לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל בְּלָאו, לְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא בַּעֲשֵׂה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, the *halakha* that the death of the husband permits a woman to marry another man is derived from the fact that the Merciful One states that a widow is forbidden to a High Priest. This indicates that it is permitted for her to marry a common priest, and the same certainly applies to a non-priest. The Gemara asks: But perhaps one can say that she is forbidden to a High Priest by a prohibition, whereas she is forbidden to everyone else only by dint of a positive mitzva.**

The Gemara tries yet another approach: the Torah forbids a *kohen gadol* from marrying a widow. The implication is *ha le'kohen hedyot sharya* — to an ordinary *kohen* and to the rest of the world she is permissible. Again the Gemara rejects the inference: *de'dilma l'kohen gadol be'lav* — perhaps the *kohen gadol* violates a specific negative commandment, *ve'le'chulei alma be'aseh* — while for everybody else there is merely an *issur aseh* prohibiting her from remarrying. The discussion of whether and how we can definitively derive the permissibility of a widow after her husband's death will continue in the next session.
