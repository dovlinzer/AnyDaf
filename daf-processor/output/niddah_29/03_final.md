# Niddah 29 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Birth Status Determinati…

### Head vs. Majority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצָא מְחוּתָּךְ אוֹ מְסוֹרָס וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אֲפִילּוּ הָרֹאשׁ עִמָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that if the fetus **emerged in pieces, or** if it emerged **reversed,** i.e., feetfirst rather than headfirst; when most of its limbs emerge, its status is like that of a child born, with regard to the impurity of a woman after childbirth. **Rabbi Elazar says: Even** if **the head is among** the limbs that emerged, provided that the majority of the limbs did not yet emerge, the fetus is not considered born.

The present daf opens with a continuation of the *mishnah*'s discussion of when a fetus is considered halachically born. This question carries practical implications across several areas of *halacha*: the *tumah* of childbirth, the determination of when the day-count for a *bris* begins, and questions of *bechor* status. The *mishnah* had established two basic rules: in a normal birth, the child is considered born once the majority of the head — identified as the forehead — has emerged. In a case where the child is born dismembered or as a breech presentation, however, the head carries no special significance, and the determining criterion is the emergence of the majority of the body.

### Eliezer vs. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין הָרֹאשׁ עִמָּהֶן, אֲבָל הָרֹאשׁ עִמָּהֶן — הָרֹאשׁ פּוֹטֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: They taught** in the mishna that the woman is not impure unless most of the fetus's limbs emerged **only** in a case **where the head is not among** the limbs that emerged; **but** if **the head is among them, the head exempts** the woman's future offspring from the obligation of primogeniture, as the fetus is considered born.

The *gemara* turns to the precise language of the *mishnah*: *yatza mechutach u-mesuras* — "if it emerged dismembered or reversed." Rabbi Eliezer reads this straightforwardly: even if the head emerges in a dismembered birth, the head has no independent significance, and one must wait for the majority of the body. This is intuitive — in a dismembered birth, the head is detached from the rest of the body and cannot symbolically or practically represent the whole child.

Rabbi Yochanan, however, reads the *mishnah* more narrowly. He maintains that the *mishnah*'s ruling requiring the majority of the body applies only when the head has not yet emerged — whether because the birth is a breech presentation or because the child is dismembered and the head happened not to emerge first. But whenever the head does emerge, regardless of circumstances, Rabbi Yochanan holds that *harei hu ke-yalud* — it is considered as though the child has been born. In his view, the emergence of the head is always the decisive criterion.

### Shmuel's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בְּדִשְׁמוּאֵל קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין הָרֹאשׁ פּוֹטֵר בִּנְפָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Shall we say** that these Sages **disagree with regard to** the opinion **of Shmuel? As Shmuel said** that if a woman is pregnant with twins, and **the head** of one of the fetuses emerges and then disappears back into the womb, this **does not exempt** the other fetus from the obligation of primogeniture should it be born first. Shmuel says this specifically **in** a case of **non-viable newborns,** i.e., where the fetus whose head emerged was a non-viable newborn and the one that was eventually born first is a viable offspring. But if both are viable offspring, the emergence of the head is considered birth. The suggestion is that Rabbi Elazar agrees with the opinion of Shmuel, whereas Rabbi Yoḥanan disagrees with the opinion of Shmuel and maintains that the emergence of the head is considered birth even in the case of a non-viable newborn.

The *gemara* then introduces Shmuel's position: *ein ha-rosh poter bi-nefalim* — the head does not constitute a birth determination in the case of fetuses, meaning stillbirths. According to Shmuel, the head counts as signifying birth only when the child is a live birth. Even a whole, intact stillbirth does not acquire *yalud* status through the emergence of the head alone. The *gemara* therefore asks: are Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yochanan debating the position of Shmuel?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּשָׁלֵם — דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי, כִּי פְּלִיגִי בִּמְחוּתָּךְ, דְּמָר סָבַר: בְּשָׁלֵם הוּא דְּקָחָשֵׁיב, בִּמְחוּתָּךְ לָא קָחָשֵׁיב, וּמָר סָבַר: בַּמְחוּתָּךְ נָמֵי חָשֵׁיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion, as it is possible that **in** the case of **a whole** non-viable newborn, **everyone,** i.e., Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Yoḥanan, **agrees** that the emergence of the head is considered a birth, contrary to the opinion of Shmuel. **When they disagree,** it is **in** the case of a fetus that emerged **in pieces. As** one **Sage,** Rabbi Elazar, **holds** that **it is** specifically **in** the case of **a whole** non-viable newborn **that** the emergence of the head **is considered** birth, whereas **with regard to** a fetus that emerged **in pieces** it **is not considered** birth; **and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **holds** that **in** the case of a fetus that emerged **in pieces as well,** the emergence of the head **is considered** birth.

The *gemara* responds with a notable answer. *Bishlama de-chulei alma lo peligi* — the case of a whole but stillborn child is actually not the subject of their dispute. Indeed, contrary to what one might expect, the *gemara* suggests that everyone would disagree with Shmuel in that case: when the child is intact and the head emerges, even if the child is not alive, all would agree the birth is considered to have occurred. The debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yochanan is specifically about the dismembered case.

### Three Birth Scenarios

This analysis yields three distinct positions arranged along a spectrum. Shmuel occupies one extreme, holding that the head matters only in a live, intact birth. Rabbi Eliezer occupies the middle position: in an intact stillbirth the head counts, but in a dismembered birth it does not. Rabbi Yochanan occupies the opposite extreme: even in a dismembered birth, the emergence of the head is sufficient to establish *yalud* status. The *mishnah*'s case of the dismembered birth requiring the majority of the body is, according to Rabbi Yochanan, limited to the scenario where the head has not yet emerged at all.

### Independent Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא: טַעְמָא דְּיָצָא מְחוּתָּךְ אוֹ מְסוֹרָס, הָא כְּתִקְנוֹ — הָרֹאשׁ פּוֹטֵר. תַּרְוַיְיהוּ לֵית לְהוּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין הָרֹאשׁ פּוֹטֵר בִּנְפָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites **another version** of this discussion. **The reason** it is necessary for most of the limbs of the fetus to emerge for it to be considered born is **that it emerged in pieces, or** that it emerged **reversed,** i.e., feet first. It may be inferred from here that if it emerged **in its proper manner,** headfirst and whole, it is considered born, even according to Rabbi Elazar. Therefore, if the woman is pregnant with twins, the emergence of **the head exempts** the other twin from the obligations of primogeniture. Evidently, **both** Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Elazar **do not hold** in accordance with the opinion **of Shmuel, as Shmuel said** that the emergence of **the head** of one of the twins **does not exempt** the other **in** a case of **non-viable newborns.**

The *gemara* now presents an alternative tradition. *Ika dematni la leha shemata be-apei nafshah* — some transmit this debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yochanan not as a dispute about reading the *mishnah*, but as a freestanding *halachic* dispute. In this version, Rabbi Eliezer says *ein ha-rosh ke-rov evarim* — the head does not count as equivalent to the majority of the limbs — and Rabbi Yochanan says it does.

Since the *mishnah* clearly and unanimously rules that the head determines birth in the case of a normal live delivery, this standalone debate cannot possibly concern a live birth. It must therefore be addressing a miscarriage or stillbirth. In this reading, Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yochanan are debating the precise case Shmuel addresses: when a woman miscarries and the fetus is whole but not living, does the emergence of the head constitute a halachic birth? Here the positions are inverted from what the first reading suggested — Rabbi Eliezer holds the head does not count, Rabbi Yochanan holds that it does — and both are now engaged with Shmuel's exact question.

### Rav Papa Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כְּתַנָּאֵי, יָצָא מְחוּתָּךְ אוֹ מְסוֹרָס — מִשֶּׁיֵּצֵא רוּבּוֹ הֲרֵי הוּא כְּיָלוּד. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: מִשֶּׁיֵּצֵא כְּתִקְנוֹ. מַאי קָאָמַר?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says:** This dispute between Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Yoḥanan is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is stated in a *baraita*: If the fetus **emerged in pieces or reversed, when most of its** limbs **emerge** its status **is like** that of a child **born. Rabbi Yosei says:** Its status is like that of a child born **when it emerges in its proper manner.** The Gemara asks: **What is** Rabbi Yosei **saying?** His statement apparently indicates that if a fetus emerges with its feet first, then even after most of its limbs emerge it is not considered born.

Rav Papa introduces a parallel dispute among *tanna'im* to shed light on the debate. The *mishnah* states that for a dismembered or breech birth, *misheyatza rubo harei hu ke-yalud* — once the majority has emerged it is considered born. Rabbi Yosi adds: *misheyatza ke-tikno* — only when it emerges normally. This statement is initially difficult to parse: surely no one would rule that a breech birth is never considered born regardless of how much of the body emerges.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: יָצָא מְחוּתָּךְ וּמְסוֹרָס — מִשֶּׁיֵּצֵא רוּבּוֹ הֲרֵי הוּא כְּיָלוּד, הָא כְּתִקְנוֹ — הָרֹאשׁ פּוֹטֵר. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: מִשֶּׁיֵּצֵא רוּבּוֹ כְּתִקְנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says** that **this** is what the *baraita* **is saying:** If the fetus **emerged in pieces or reversed, when most of its** limbs **emerge** its status **is like** that of a child **born; but** by inference, if the fetus emerged **in its proper manner,** then the emergence of **the head exempts** its twin, should it eventually be born first, from the obligations of primogeniture. And **Rabbi Yosei says:** The status of a fetus that emerged in pieces is like that of a child born **when most of its** limbs **emerge,** provided that it emerged **in its proper manner,** i.e., both requirements are necessary. The suggestion is that the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan is in accordance with the ruling of the first *tanna*, that even if a fetus emerges in pieces, once its head emerges it is considered born; whereas the opinion of Rabbi Elazar follows the ruling of Rabbi Yosei that even if such a fetus emerges headfirst, it is not considered born.

Rav Papa therefore explains Rabbi Yosi's intent. Reading *mechutach u-mesuras* as "dismembered and reversed" — that is, a conjunctive description of a single compound case rather than two separate cases — the *mishnah*'s majority rule applies only to a birth that is both dismembered and reversed, meaning the head has not emerged. The implicit ruling of the first position is that if the dismembered child emerges in the normal head-first order, *ha-rosh poter* — the head alone suffices. Rabbi Yosi then responds that the head is determinative only *ke-tikno le-chaim* — when the child emerges naturally and is alive. Anything short of a natural live birth renders the head insufficient.

### Rav Zvid Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב זְבִיד: מִכְּלָל דְּבִמְסוֹרָס, רוּבּוֹ נָמֵי לֹא פּוֹטֵר? הָא קַיְימָא לַן דְּרוּבּוֹ כְּכוּלּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid objects to** Rav Pappa's interpretation of the *baraita*: Since Rabbi Yosei issued his statement that both requirements are necessary, i.e., a majority of limbs and emergence in the proper manner, with regard to the two cases mentioned by the first *tanna*, **by inference in** the other case, **where** the fetus emerged **reversed, even** when **most of its** limbs emerge **it does not exempt** its twin from the obligations of primogeniture. But this is difficult, as **we maintain that the majority of** an item is considered **like all** of it. Accordingly, when most of the fetus's limbs emerge it should be considered born even if it came out feetfirst.

Rav Zvid objects to the framing of this debate. The way Rav Papa has read it implies that when a breech birth produces a majority of the body, it would not count. But this is untenable — *rubo ke-kulo*, the majority is always treated as the whole, and no authority disputes that the majority of a body counts as born regardless of orientation. The formulation cannot mean that a reversed birth with a majority emerged is not considered born.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב זְבִיד, הָכִי קָאָמַר: יָצָא מְחוּתָּךְ וּמְסוֹרָס — מִשֶּׁיֵּצֵא רוּבּוֹ הֲרֵי זֶה כְּיָלוּד, הָא כְּתִקְנוֹ — הָרֹאשׁ פּוֹטֵר. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: מִשֶּׁיֵּצֵא כְּתִקְנוֹ לְחַיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Zevid says** that **this** is what the *baraita* **is saying:** If the fetus **emerged in pieces and reversed, when most of its** limbs **emerge** its status **is like** that of a child **born; but** by inference, if the fetus emerged **in its proper manner** then the emergence of **the head exempts** its twin from the obligations of primogeniture, even if it was born in pieces. **Rabbi Yosei says:** The fetus is considered born once its head emerges only in a case **when it emerges in its proper manner alive;** if it emerges in pieces, the fetus is considered born only when most of its limbs emerge.

Rav Zvid therefore recasts the debate with greater precision. The *mishnah*'s case of *mechutach u-mesuras* — both dismembered and reversed — teaches that when these two conditions combine and the head has not emerged, you go by the majority. The implicit ruling is that if even a dismembered child emerges in the normal head-first order, *ha-rosh poter*, the head exempts. This is the position of Rabbi Yochanan. Rabbi Yosi then counters: *misheyatza ke-tikno le-chaim* — the head matters only when the child comes out naturally and is alive. This aligns Rabbi Yosi's position closely with Shmuel: only a live, natural birth triggers the rule that the head determines the moment of birth.

### Baraita Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: יָצָא מְחוּתָּךְ (אוֹ) מְסוֹרָס — מִשֶּׁיֵּצֵא רוּבּוֹ הֲרֵי זֶה כְּיָלוּד, הָא כְּתִקְנוֹ — הָרֹאשׁ פּוֹטֵר. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: מִשֶּׁיֵּצֵא כְּתִקְנוֹ לְחַיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **This** explanation **is also taught** explicitly in another *baraita*: If the fetus **emerged in pieces** and **reversed,** then **when most of its** limbs **emerge** its status **is like** that of a child **born; but** if the fetus emerged **in its proper manner,** the emergence of **the head exempts** its twin. **Rabbi Yosei says:** The fetus is considered born once its head emerges only in a case **where it emerges in its proper manner alive.**

A *baraita* confirms this reading. It states that in a case of a dismembered and reversed birth, *misheyatza rubo harei zeh ke-yalud* — once the majority has emerged it is born. The implicit corollary: if it emerged in normal order even though dismembered, *ha-rosh poter*, the head determines birth. Rabbi Yosi adds in the *baraita*: *misheyatza ke-tikno le-chaim* — only when the child emerges naturally and alive does the head serve as the determining criterion. Anything less — stillbirth, dismemberment — and you revert to the majority standard.

### Head Majority Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵיזֶהוּ כְּתִקְנוֹ לְחַיִּים? מִשֶּׁיֵּצֵא רוֹב רֹאשׁוֹ. וְאֵיזֶהוּ רוֹב רֹאשׁוֹ? רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: מִשֶּׁיֵּצְאוּ צְדָעָיו. אַבָּא חָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: (מִשֶּׁיֵּצֵא) [מִשֶּׁתֵּצֵא] פַּדַּחְתּוֹ. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: מִשֶּׁיֵּרָאוּ קַרְנֵי רֹאשׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And what is** the exact stage when a fetus that emerged **in its proper manner alive** is considered born? **When most of its head emerges. And what is** the stage when **most of its head** emerges? **Rabbi Yosei says: When its temples emerge. Abba Ḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua: When its forehead emerges. And some say: When the corners of its head,** i.e., the projection of the head above the neck, **are visible.**

Having established when the head is the relevant criterion, the *gemara* now asks what exactly constitutes the majority of the head. Three opinions are given. Rabbi Yosi says *misheyatza tzida* — when the temples have emerged. *Abba Chanan* in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua says *misheyatza padachata* — when the forehead has come out, which as Rashi notes corresponds to the primary front surface of the head and effectively represents the majority of the face; this is the ruling implicit in the *mishnah* itself. A third view says *misheyatza karnei rosho* — when the "horns" of the head have emerged, which Rashi identifies as the upper and rear slope of the skull, approximately what is meant in medical terminology by crowning. This position appears to represent a threshold somewhat lower than the forehead — a smaller proportion of the head — and is notably more stringent in requiring less to have emerged before birth is considered to have occurred. The ruling for practical *halacha*, particularly for *brit milah*, follows the *stam mishnah*, which identifies the forehead as the critical threshold.

## Unknown Miscarriage (II)

### Mishnah Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' הַמַּפֶּלֶת וְאֵין יָדוּעַ מַהוּ — תֵּשֵׁב לְזָכָר וְלִנְקֵבָה. אֵין יָדוּעַ אִם וָלָד הָיָה אִם לָאו — תֵּשֵׁב לְזָכָר וְלִנְקֵבָה וּלְנִדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of a woman **who discharges and it is not known what is** the fetus's sex, she **shall observe** the strictures of a woman who gave birth both **to a male and to a female.** She is impure for fourteen days, like a woman who gave birth to a female, and any blood the woman sees only until forty days after birth, not eighty days thereafter, is pure, like a woman who gave birth to a male. If **it is unknown whether it was** a male or female **offspring** or **whether** it was **not** an offspring at all, **she shall observe** the period of impurity **for** a woman who gave birth to **a male and for** a woman who gave birth to **a female; and for** any blood that she sees, she observes the *halakhot* of **a menstruating woman.** Since it is possible that what she discharged was not an offspring at all, any blood she sees might be due to menstruation, not childbirth.

The *mishnah* now turns to cases where the status of the miscarriage cannot be established through inspection. The first scenario presented is a woman who miscarries but cannot determine whether the fetus was male or female. In such a case, she observes *teshev le-zachar ule-nekevah* — she must adopt the stringencies of both possibilities. She counts fourteen days of *tumah* as required for a female birth, but her *taharah* days end at day forty as required for a male birth, since she cannot assume the longer *tohar* period of a female.

The *mishnah* then escalates to a harder case: the woman cannot determine whether what she expelled was a halachic fetus at all — *eini yoda'at im velad hayah im lav*. She may not have had the opportunity to inspect the discharge, or what she saw gave her no clear indication of fetal status. In this situation, the *mishnah* rules *teshev le-zachar ule-nekevah ule-niddah* — she must observe the stringencies of three possibilities simultaneously. She treats herself as possibly a *yoledet nekevah*, granting herself fourteen days of *tumah*, but she cannot rely on any *taharah* days following that period, because she must also reckon with the possibility that this was not a birth at all, in which case she is simply a *niddah* with no *yemei tohar*. The mention of *zachar* in this formulation might seem superfluous — since the possibility that she is simply a *niddah* already eliminates any reliance on *taharah* days — and the *gemara* will indeed raise this question.

### R. Yehoshua b. Levi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ' אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: עָבְרָה נָהָר וְהִפִּילָה — מְבִיאָה קׇרְבָּן וְנֶאֱכָל, הַלֵּךְ אַחַר רוֹב נָשִׁים, וְרוֹב נָשִׁים וָלָד מְעַלְּיָא יָלְדָן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** With regard to a pregnant woman who **passed across a river and she discharged** her fetus into the river, and she does not know whether the fetus was fully formed, **she brings** the **offering** of a woman after childbirth, which is a burnt offering and a sin offering. **And** the sin offering, which is a bird, **is eaten** after the nape of its neck has been pinched, in the manner of a regular bird sin offering. This is the *halakha* despite the uncertainty that this fetus might not have been fully formed, in which case the woman would not be obligated to bring this offering, and a bird that is not an offering may not be eaten if its nape was pinched. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi explains the reasoning behind this ruling: One must **follow the majority of** pregnant **women, and most** pregnant **women give birth to full-fledged offspring.**

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi addresses the scenario where a pregnant woman miscarries in circumstances that prevent inspection — his paradigm case is a woman who crosses a river and miscarries into it. He rules that she brings a *korban* as a *yoledet*, and moreover that the *korban* is eaten — meaning we treat her as certainly having had a valid halachic birth. His basis is *halacha ke-rov nashim* — we rule according to the majority of women, and the majority of pregnant women, when they miscarry or give birth, produce a fetus that qualifies halachically as a valid birth. Therefore, even without inspection, we do not entertain the possibility that this was a non-fetal discharge.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: אֵין יָדוּעַ אִם וָלָד הָיָה, תֵּשֵׁב לְזָכָר וְלִנְקֵבָה וּלְנִדָּה. אַמַּאי תֵּשֵׁב לְנִדָּה? לֵימָא: הַלֵּךְ אַחַר רוֹב נָשִׁים, וְרוֹב נָשִׁים וָלָד מְעַלְּיָא יָלְדָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this claim. **We learned** in the mishna: If **it is unknown whether** what the woman discharged **was** a male or female **offspring** or whether it was not an offspring at all, **she shall observe** the period of impurity **for** a woman who gave birth to **a male and for** a woman who gave birth to **a female; and for** any blood that she sees, she observes the *halakhot* of **a menstruating woman.** The Gemara asks: According to the statement of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, **why must** the woman **observe** the *halakhot* of **a menstruating woman? Let us say** that one must **follow the majority of women, and the majority of women give birth to full-fledged offspring.** If so, she should observe a period of purity, during which any blood she sees is pure, after her period of impurity, like all women after childbirth.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין בְּשֶׁלֹּא הוּחְזְקָה עוּבָּרָה, וְכִי קָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי — כְּשֶׁהוּחְזְקָה עוּבָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The *halakha* in **the mishna** is referring **to** a case **where** the woman **had not been presumed** to be **pregnant** prior to her miscarriage; **and when Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says** that a woman who discharges is presumed to have discharged an offspring, he was speaking of a case **where** the woman **had been presumed** to be **pregnant** prior to her miscarriage.

The question immediately arises: how does this square with the *mishnah*, which explicitly entertains the possibility that the discharge was not a valid fetus, ruling *teshev le-niddah*? The *gemara* resolves this by distinguishing the cases. The *mishnah* addresses a situation where the woman's pregnancy was not definitively established — *beshelo huchzeka ubra*. Perhaps something emerged from her body, but there was no firm prior knowledge of an established pregnancy. In such a case, we cannot invoke *rov nashim*, because we are not even certain she was pregnant. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's ruling applies specifically *ke-shehuchzeka ubra* — when her pregnant status was known and established. In that case, we rely on the majority and do not worry about the possibility of a non-fetal discharge.

### Animal Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בְּהֵמָה שֶׁיָּצְתָה מְלֵאָה, וּבָאָה רֵיקָנִית — הַבָּא אַחֲרָיו בְּכוֹר מִסָּפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a difficulty with regard to the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi from a *baraita*: There is the case of a kosher **animal** that had not yet given birth, **which went** to the pasture **full,** i.e., pregnant, **and came** back the same day **empty,** i.e., with no live offspring.It clearly discharged, but it is unknown whether or not it discharged an offspring, which would exempt the animal's subsequent offspring from the status of a firstborn. In this case, the offspring **that comes after it** is **a firstborn** of **uncertain** status.

The *gemara* tests Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's principle with a parallel case involving animals. A pregnant animal — *behema sheyatza melei'a uvah reikanis* — leaves while pregnant and returns no longer pregnant, without the owner having witnessed the birth or miscarriage and without a live offspring having appeared. The question is whether the next offspring born to this animal is a *bechor*. The ruling is that it is a *bechor mi-safek* — a doubtful firstborn — because we cannot be certain that what previously emerged was a halachic birth. This appears to contradict Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi directly: the animal's pregnancy was established, yet we do not simply apply *rov behemot* to assume it gave birth to a valid fetus.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי? הַלֵּךְ אַחַר רוֹב בְּהֵמוֹת, וְרוֹב בְּהֵמוֹת וָלָד מְעַלְּיָא יָלְדָן, וְהַאי פָּשׁוּט הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But why** is this the *halakha*? According to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, one should **follow** the reality among **the majority of animals, and the majority of animals give birth to full-fledged offspring. And** if so, **this** subsequent offspring of the animal **is a regular** offspring, i.e., it is definitely not a firstborn.

### Ravina's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר: רוֹב בְּהֵמוֹת יוֹלְדוֹת דָּבָר הַפּוֹטֵר מִבְּכוֹרָה, וּמִעוּטָן יוֹלְדוֹת דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ פּוֹטֵר מִבְּכוֹרָה, וְכׇל הַיּוֹלְדוֹת מְטַנְּפוֹת, וְזוֹ הוֹאִיל וְלֹא טִנְּפָה — אִתְּרַע לֵהּ רוּבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina says** in response: In this case, the *halakha* does not follow the majority of animals, **because it can be said** that **the majority of animals give birth to an item that exempts** the animal's subsequent offspring **from firstborn** status, **but a minority of** animals **give birth to an item that does not exempt** the animal's subsequent offspring **from firstborn** status, i.e., an item that is not a full-fledged offspring. **And** an additional factor is that **all** animals **that give birth** to full-fledged offspring **discharge turbid liquids** one day before giving birth, **and since this** animal **did not discharge turbid liquids** before going to the pasture, **the** effect of the **majority is undermined.** Consequently, the status of the animal's birth is uncertain, and for this reason its subsequent offspring is considered a firstborn of uncertain status.

Ravina resolves the apparent contradiction. It is true that the majority of pregnant animals give birth or miscarry in a manner that halachically constitutes a birth and would therefore exempt the subsequent offspring from *bechor* status. However, there is a minority of cases where what is discharged does not constitute a halachic fetus — such as a fluid-filled placenta without a recognizable embryo. Normally we would not be concerned about such a minority. But here a specific indicator undermines the application of the majority: *dechol hayoldot mitnapfot* — any animal that carries a true fetus and is about to give birth emits a characteristic odor beforehand, and *vezo ho'il velo tinpa* — this animal displayed no such smell. The absence of this biological sign *itra lei ruba* — weakens the presumption of the majority, and we therefore treat the status of the subsequent offspring as doubtful.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי כׇּל הַיּוֹלְדוֹת מְטַנְּפוֹת, הָא מִדְּלָא מְטַנְּפָה בְּכוֹר מְעַלְּיָא הוּא! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: רוֹב יוֹלְדוֹת מְטַנְּפוֹת, וְזוֹ הוֹאִיל וְלֹא טִנְּפָה — אִתְּרַע לֵהּ רוּבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If all** animals **that give birth** to full-fledged offspring **discharge turbid liquids** before giving birth, then it should be concluded **from** the fact **that this** animal **did not discharge turbid liquids** that it did not discharge a full-fledged offspring, and therefore the animal's subsequent offspring **is a proper firstborn,** not a firstborn of uncertain status. **Rather,** one should **say** that Ravina meant the following: **The majority of** animals that **give birth discharge turbid liquids** beforehand, **and** therefore, **since this** animal **did not discharge turbid liquids, the** effect of the **majority is undermined.**

Tosafot add the necessary clarification: this ruling applies only when the animal was not away from its owner's purview long enough to have produced that smell and then miscarried without the owner's knowledge. If the animal had been absent for an extended period, one would simply assume the smell occurred and was missed. The case is therefore a tightly constrained one where the absence of the pre-birth indicator is a meaningful and observable datum.

## Unknown Miscarriage (II)

### Woman in Error Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַבִּין אָמַר: מֵתִיב רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא טוֹעָה, וְלָא יָדַעְנָא מַאי תְּיוּבְתָּא. מַאי הִיא? דְּתַנְיָא:
>
> **Translation:** **When Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said: Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, raises an objection** to the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi from the *halakha* of **a forgetful woman; but I do not know what the objection is.** The Gemara asks: This *halakha* of a forgetful woman, which forms the basis of the objection, **what is it?** The Gemara explains: **As it is taught** in a *baraita*:

*Ki ata Ravin amar* — when Ravin came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he reported that Rabbi Yose the son of Rabbi Chanina had challenged Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's principle from a *baraita* concerning a *to'eh* — a woman who is in error regarding her cycle and her status. Ravin then adds, pointedly, *velo yadana mai tiyuvteih* — and I do not even understand what the challenge was. He does not believe it constituted a valid refutation. The *gemara* proceeds to examine this *baraita* in detail, which concerns a woman who departed while pregnant and returned no longer pregnant, without any knowledge of when the miscarriage occurred. The case is brought as a potential challenge to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi — specifically, the *gemara* will argue that the difficulty in this case might imply we cannot assume the miscarriage was a valid halachic birth — and Ravin's point is that this implication is mistaken.

## Unknown Miscarriage (II)

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִשָּׁה שֶׁיָּצְתָה מְלֵאָה וּבָאָה רֵיקָנִית, וְהֵבִיאָה לְפָנֵינוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה שָׁבוּעִין טְהוֹרִין, וַעֲשָׂרָה שְׁבוּעוֹת אֶחָד טָמֵא וְאֶחָד טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **A woman left** home for an extended period of time while she was **full,** i.e., pregnant, **and came** back when she was **empty,** as she discharged the fetus, but it is unknown exactly when and what she discharged; **and she spent three weeks in our presence** during which she was **pure,** i.e., she did not experience any bleeding, **and** for the following **ten weeks** she alternated between **one impure** week, in which she experienced bleeding every day, **and one pure** week, in which she did not experience bleeding at all.

The *baraita* describes a woman who left while visibly pregnant and returned in a state that is no longer pregnant, without any knowledge of when the miscarriage occurred. She presents herself to *beit din* asking: what is my status, when do I go to the *mikvah*, and when may I resume relations with my husband? The case is complicated by the fact that she does not know the date of the miscarriage — it could have been that very day, or it could have been weeks prior. Over the following period she is observed to be clean for three full weeks, then bleeding every other week for ten weeks.

### Worst-Case Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת לְאוּר שְׁלֹשִׁים וְחָמֵשׁ, וּמַטְבִּילִין אוֹתָהּ תִּשְׁעִים וְחָמֵשׁ טְבִילוֹת, דִּבְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: שְׁלֹשִׁים וְחָמֵשׁ. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: דַּיָּהּ לַטְּבִילָה שֶׁתְּהֵא בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** In such a case, the woman **may engage in intercourse** with her husband at the end of the fifth week following her return, **on the eve of the thirty-fifth** day, after which she is again prohibited from engaging in intercourse with her husband. **And we require her to immerse** in a ritual bath a total of **ninety-five immersions.** This is **the statement of Beit Shammai. And Beit Hillel say** that she is required to immerse **thirty-five** times. **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says:** She is required to immerse only once, as **it is sufficient for the immersion to be at the end** of the period when she is required to immerse every day. The entire *baraita* will be explained below.

To analyze when she may resume relations with her husband, the *gemara* begins by identifying the worst-case scenario in terms of her *tumah* status. The most stringent possible reading is that she gave birth at this very moment, that the child was female — imposing two full weeks of *tumah* — and that she was also a *yoledet be-zova*, meaning that prior to giving birth she had been a *zava*, bleeding for three days outside her normal cycle without accompanying pain. A *zava gedolah* requires seven clean days before she becomes *tehorah*. The intersection of these two statuses creates a complex layering: she needs two weeks of *tumah* for the female birth, then must count seven clean days for the *zava* status. The question of whether *yemei leida she-eina ro'ah bahem* — days of childbirth *tumah* during which she does not bleed — can count toward the seven clean days of the *zava* is a matter of dispute; the *gemara* proceeds on the assumption that they do not.

### Tevilah Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא שָׁבוּעַ רִאשׁוֹן לֹא מְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת, אֵימַר: יוֹלֶדֶת זָכָר הִיא; שָׁבוּעַ שֵׁנִי, אֵימַר: יוֹלֶדֶת נְקֵבָה הִיא;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the objection from the *baraita* to the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: **Granted** in the **first week** following her arrival the woman **may not engage in intercourse** with her husband, despite the fact that she did not experience bleeding during that week, as one can **say** that perhaps **she is a woman who gave birth to a male** just before her arrival, which would mean that the first week is her seven-day period of impurity. Likewise, it is clear that the woman may not engage in intercourse with her husband during the **second week** after her arrival, as one can **say** that **she is a woman who gave birth to a female,** and consequently her period of impurity is two weeks long.

A further complicating principle is *tevilah bi-zmanah mitzvah* — it is a *mitzvah* to immerse at the earliest possible permitted moment, not merely to wait until one is certainly *tahor* and go to the *mikvah* then. This principle, disputed between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, drives the *gemara* to calculate not just when she is certainly *tahor*, but every individual day on which she might possibly first become *tahor* — because on each such day she is obligated to immerse. She cannot be permitted to her husband even after immersing on one of these days, since she may not yet be actually *tahor* — but according to Beit Shammai she must immerse nonetheless. This generates an enormous number of required immersions across the full period.

### Structured Scenario

The constructed scenario has the woman presenting herself having not bled at all for three clean weeks, then bleeding the entirety of every other week for a further ten weeks, alternating one week of bleeding with one week clean. The *gemara* determines through analysis that the sole night on which she may actually have relations with her husband in this entire period is the night before day thirty-five. The entire preceding period involves either *tumah* of some kind or unresolvable doubt, and the weeks that follow, as will be established, are complicated by *zava* status and the possibility that seven clean days have been disrupted by nighttime bleeding.

### Days 40 & 80

The *gemara* introduces day forty and day eighty as critical temporal boundaries. Day forty marks the end of the *yemei tohar* — the period of *taharah* blood — for the birth of a male child, while day eighty marks the same endpoint for a female. Since this woman does not know when her birth occurred, she cannot know when her own day forty or day eighty falls. The *gemara* constructs the scenario by assuming the birth occurred on the day she first presented herself, placing day forty and day eighty within the observable ten-week alternating period that follows.

### Weeks 1-2 Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁבוּעַ שְׁלִישִׁי, אֵימַר: יוֹלֶדֶת נְקֵבָה בְּזוֹב הִיא;
>
> **Translation:** It is also clear why she may not engage in intercourse during the **third week,** as one can **say** that perhaps **she is a woman who gave birth to a female as a *zava*,** i.e., a woman who experienced a discharge of uterine blood after her menstrual period for three days without the pain of labor, and therefore assumed the status of a *zava* before giving birth. If so, she must count seven clean days after her two-week period of impurity, and subsequently immerse.

The *gemara* explains why she cannot have relations during either of the first two weeks. The first week falls within the possible *tumah* period of a *yoledet zachar* — if she gave birth to a male, she is *tamei* for seven days. The second week falls within the possible *tumah* period of a *yoledet nekevah* — if she gave birth to a female, she remains *tamei* through the end of the second week. Therefore, even though she is not actively bleeding during these two weeks, she is presumed *tamei* due to the *tumah* of childbirth itself.

### Single Permitted Night

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא שָׁבוּעַ רְבִיעִי, אַף עַל גַּב דְּקָא חָזְיָא דָּם — תְּשַׁמֵּשׁ, דְּהָא דָּם טָהוֹר הוּא. לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אָזְלִינַן בָּתַר רוּבָּא?
>
> **Translation:** **But** in the **fourth week, even though** the woman **sees blood** during that period, **let her engage in intercourse** with her husband, **as it is pure blood.** At this stage the woman's period of purity is certainly underway. Why does the *baraita* state that she may not engage in intercourse with her husband in this week? Is it **not due to** the concern **that** she might have discharged an item that was not a full-fledged offspring? Evidently, although most pregnant women give birth to full-fledged offspring, **we do not follow the majority,** which contradicts the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi.

The *gemara* establishes that the sole moment in this entire constructed scenario where marital relations are permitted is the night before day thirty-five. The analysis that leads to this conclusion proceeds as follows. Week three, during which she observes seven clean days, could serve as the seven clean days required of a *zava*. Were this the case, one might expect that she could immerse at the conclusion of week three and resume relations. However, the *gemara* will show that this is precluded — due to nighttime bleeding at the start of week four — and that week four is itself problematic because any one of those days might have been the last day of the forty-day *taharah* period, making the blood of that week's end the first genuine *niddah* blood, extending her *niddah* period all the way to the Friday of week five. The last possible moment at which her *niddah* period could begin in week four is the final day of that week — *Shabbat* — in which case she completes her seven *niddah* days on the Friday of week five, and may immerse and have relations on the night before day thirty-five.

### 95 Immersions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּלֵילָוָתָא, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ, דְּאָמְרִי: טְבוּלַת יוֹם אָרוֹךְ בָּעֵי טְבִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** She is also required to immerse **on** every **night** of the third week, according to Beit Shammai. **Beit Shammai** conform **to their** line of **reasoning, as they say** in a mishna (71b) that a woman who finished her period of purity after childbirth, referred to as **a woman who immersed that long day** and is waiting for her purification process to be completed, **requires immersion** at the end of this period. The woman must therefore immerse on every night of the third week, in case that night is the end of her period of purity, for a total of thirty-five immersions by the end of the week. She must continue to immerse every night until her period of purity is certainly over, no matter when she gave birth, i.e., until the eightieth day since her arrival.

Beit Shammai, applying the principle of *tevilah bi-zmanah mitzvah*, holds that on every day that could conceivably be the precise moment she first becomes *tahor*, she must immerse. Across the full ten-week alternating period, the *gemara* identifies ninety-five such days — each representing one possible configuration of birth timing, *tumah* status, and *zava* count that could make that particular day the earliest permissible moment of *taharah*. Because some of those days are moments of possible *niddah* or *yoledet* status requiring nighttime immersion, while others are moments of possible *zava* status requiring daytime immersion, the ninety-five immersions include a combination of morning and evening immersions across the ten weeks.

### Final Immersion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: דַּיָּהּ לַטְּבִילָה שֶׁתְּהֵא בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says:** She is required to immerse only once, as **it is sufficient for the immersion to be at the end** of the period when she is required to immerse every day. The entire *baraita* will be explained below.

Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Yehuda dispute the entire framework of Beit Shammai. Their view is expressed in the principle *dayah la-tevilah she-tehei ba-acharonah* — it is sufficient for her to immerse only at the final immersion, meaning only when she is certainly and definitively *tahor*, without any lingering doubt. There is no obligation to immerse at each possible earlier moment of *taharah*; she waits until the one immersion she can perform with full confidence and performs that single immersion. According to this view, the elaborate counting of ninety-five separate immersion opportunities is entirely unnecessary.

### Week 3 Zava Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* בִּשְׁלָמָא שָׁבוּעַ רִאשׁוֹן לֹא מְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת, אֵימַר: יוֹלֶדֶת זָכָר הִיא; שָׁבוּעַ שֵׁנִי, אֵימַר: יוֹלֶדֶת נְקֵבָה הִיא;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the objection from the *baraita* to the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: **Granted** in the **first week** following her arrival the woman **may not engage in intercourse** with her husband, despite the fact that she did not experience bleeding during that week, as one can **say** that perhaps **she is a woman who gave birth to a male** just before her arrival, which would mean that the first week is her seven-day period of impurity. Likewise, it is clear that the woman may not engage in intercourse with her husband during the **second week** after her arrival, as one can **say** that **she is a woman who gave birth to a female,** and consequently her period of impurity is two weeks long.

The *gemara* examines why week three cannot simply allow relations. Having established that weeks one and two are off-limits due to *tumah* of childbirth, one might assume that week three — which is clean — serves as the seven clean days for a *zava*, and that at the end of week three she is *tahor* and may resume relations. The *gemara* addresses this by raising the possibility that she is a *yoledet nekevah* and also a *zava*, requiring that after the two weeks of *tumah* for the female birth she count seven clean days before immersing. If those seven clean days can only begin after the *tumah* period ends, they would run across week three.

### Yemei Leida Phrase

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּחָזְיָא לְיוֹם אַרְבָּעִים וְחָמֵשׁ, וּמַטְבִּילִין אוֹתָהּ תִּשְׁעִים וְחָמֵשׁ טְבִילוֹת... יוֹמָא קַמָּא דְּאָתְיָא לְקַמַּן לַיטְבְּלַהּ, דִּילְמָא שׁוֹמֶרֶת יוֹם כְּנֶגֶד יוֹם הִיא! בְּזָבָה גְּדוֹלָה קָמַיְירֵי, בְּזָבָה קְטַנָּה — לָא קָמַיְירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** [The Gemara's discussions continuing through the analysis of immersion requirements and conditions]

A critical phrase governs this analysis: *yemei leida she-eina ro'ah bahem einam olim le-sfirat zivasah* — days of childbirth *tumah* during which she does not see blood do not count toward the seven clean days required of a *zava*. The significance of this ruling is that the two weeks of *tumah* for a female birth, even if she is not actively bleeding during them, cannot be credited toward her seven clean days as a *zava*. Therefore, the seven clean days must be counted only after the two *tumah* weeks conclude — which means they fall in week three — and only at the end of week three is she potentially in a position to immerse and have relations.

### Night Bleeding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּאָמַר: ״אָסוּר לַעֲשׂוֹת כֵּן״, שֶׁמָּא תָּבֹא לִידֵי סָפֵק. לְאוּרְתָּא תְּשַׁמֵּשׁ? כְּשֶׁרָאֲתָה בָּעֶרֶב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The *halakha* in this *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who said** that **it is prohibited** for a woman **to do so,** i.e., to engage in intercourse on the seventh day of her *ziva* after immersing in a ritual bath, **lest she come to** a case of **uncertainty.** If she engages in intercourse on that seventh day after immersion, and subsequently experiences bleeding on that same day, it retroactively nullifies her entire seven clean days, and it turns out that she engaged in intercourse while she was impure. The Gemara continues to ask: Even so, **let her engage in intercourse that night,** after having completed seven clean days. The Gemara answers: The *baraita* is referring to a case **when** the woman **saw** blood **in the evening,** i.e., her fourth week of experiencing bleeding started in the nighttime.

Even granting that week three serves as the seven clean days of a *zava*, the *gemara* shows that she cannot actually have relations at the conclusion of that week. The reason is that the bleeding of week four begins at night — specifically, on *Shabbat* night at the transition from week three to week four. A *zava gedolah* completes her seven clean days and may immerse on the seventh day during the day, based on the principle of *miktzat ha-yom ke-kulo*. The *gemara* raises whether she could immerse on the seventh day — *Shabbat* — and have relations then. Rabbi Shimon rules that this is prohibited, because going to the *mikvah* on the seventh day and having relations creates a situation of potential retroactive transgression: if she bleeds later that same day, the *miktzat ha-yom ke-kulo* principle is undermined and the immersion is retroactively voided, meaning she would have had relations while still in a state of *tumah*. Therefore she may not have relations on the day of her seventh clean day. And since the bleeding begins *Shabbat* night, she has no opportunity to have relations *Shabbat* night either.

### Week 4 Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא שָׁבוּעַ רְבִיעִי, אַף עַל גַּב דְּקָא חָזְיָא דָּם — תְּשַׁמֵּשׁ, דְּהָא דָּם טָהוֹר הוּא. לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אָזְלִינַן בָּתַר רוּבָּא?
>
> **Translation:** **But** in the **fourth week, even though** the woman **sees blood** during that period, **let her engage in intercourse** with her husband, **as it is pure blood.** At this stage the woman's period of purity is certainly underway. Why does the *baraita* state that she may not engage in intercourse with her husband in this week? Is it **not due to** the concern **that** she might have discharged an item that was not a full-fledged offspring? Evidently, although most pregnant women give birth to full-fledged offspring, **we do not follow the majority,** which contradicts the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi.

Even were one to suggest that the blood of week four is *dam tohar* — ritually pure blood that falls within the *yemei tohar* following a valid birth — and therefore she should be permitted to have relations during that week without concern for the bleeding, the *gemara* rejects this reasoning. The problem is that we do not know when the *yemei tohar* began. Any one of the days of week four could be the last day of the forty-day period — meaning the blood on that day, or the day before, or earlier in the week, might be the first genuine *niddah* blood rather than *dam tohar*. We therefore cannot assume on any particular day of week four that we are still within the *yemei tohar*; the worst case scenario must always be entertained.

### Ravin's Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי ״לָא יָדַעְנָא מַאי תְּיוּבְתָּא״? אֵימַר: הִרְחִיקָה לֵידָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Rather,** as this is apparently a valid objection, **what** is the reason that Ravin said: **I do not know what the objection** to the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi **is?** The Gemara explains: Perhaps it is in fact assumed that the woman gave birth to a full-fledged offspring, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, and the reason she is not considered pure in the fourth week after her arrival is that one can **say** that **her birth was distant,** i.e., she gave birth a long time before her arrival, and therefore her period of purity had already ended before the fourth week.

This is precisely the point Ravin was making when he said he did not understand Rabbi Yose ben Rabbi Chanina's challenge to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi. The difficulty with week four is not that we are uncertain whether the miscarriage was a valid halachic birth. We may fully assume — following Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi — that the birth was valid and that she has *yemei tohar*. The problem is simply that we do not know when those *yemei tohar* begin. *Eimar hirchika leidatah* — perhaps the birth occurred long ago, well before she presented herself, in which case by the time we reach week four the forty days have already elapsed and the blood is ordinary *niddah* blood. The uncertainty is temporal, not categorical, and therefore this case provides no challenge to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's principle.

### Week 3 Saturday

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעֶשְׂרִים וְחַד תְּשַׁמֵּשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further inquires: As explained above, the reason it is prohibited for the woman to engage in intercourse during the third week is that she might have given birth just before arriving and had been a *zava* at the time, and therefore after observing the two weeks of impurity of a woman after childbirth, she must observe an additional seven days of impurity as a *zava*. A *zava* may undergo her purification process on the seventh day of her impurity, following which she is permitted to engage in intercourse. Accordingly, **let** the woman **engage in intercourse on** the **twenty-first** day after her arrival.

The *gemara* revisits the question of whether any moment in week three allows relations. On *Shabbat* day — the seventh day of the seven clean days — she could in principle immerse in the daytime as a *zava* completing her count. The question is whether she may then have relations on *Shabbat* day itself. Rabbi Shimon's ruling, as described above, is that relations on the seventh day are prohibited because of the risk of retroactive violation if bleeding occurs later that day. And since the bleeding of week four begins *Shabbat* night — at the very transition out of week three — she had no window to have relations *Shabbat* night either. Week three therefore offers no permissible moment.

### Week 1 Immersions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁבוּעַ קַמָּא מַטְבִּילִין אוֹתָהּ בְּלֵילָוָתָא, אֵימַר יוֹלֶדֶת זָכָר הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** During **the first week she** is required **to immerse at night,** every night of the week. The reason is that as the date of her childbirth and the sex of the offspring are unknown, one can **say** that **she is a woman who gave birth to a male.** If so, the seven days of impurity might have ended on any night of the first week, and therefore she must immerse in a ritual bath on each night.

The *gemara* now accounts for the ninety-five immersions required by Beit Shammai. In week one, she immerses seven times — once each night. The reasoning is that any one of the seven nights of week one could be the first night following the end of the *tumah* period of a *yoledet zachar*. If she gave birth seven days before she presented herself, then day seven of her *tumah* as a *yoledet zachar* falls on the first night of this week; if she gave birth six days before, it falls on the second night; and so on through each night of the week. Since on each of those nights she could be crossing from *tumah* to a possible state of *taharah*, she must immerse each night. These are nighttime immersions because *yoledet* and *niddah* status, like that of a *zava*, requires nighttime immersion when the *tumah* period ends in the evening.

### Week 2 Immersions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁבוּעַ שֵׁנִי, מַטְבִּילִין אוֹתָהּ בְּלֵילָוָתָא, אֵימַר: יוֹלֶדֶת נְקֵבָה הִיא. (בִּימָמָא) [בִּימָמֵי], אֵימַר: יוֹלֶדֶת זָכָר בְּזוֹב הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** During the **second week, she** is required **to immerse at night,** every night, as one can **say** that **she is a woman who gave birth to a female,** and therefore it is possible that her fourteen-day period of impurity ended on any of the nights of the second week. She must also immerse every day of the second week **in the daytime,** as one can **say** that **she is a woman who gave birth to a male as a *zava*.** If so, it is possible that her seven-day period of impurity as a woman after childbirth ended on any given day during the first week, after which she must start to count seven clean days as a *zava*. These seven days necessarily culminate during the second week, and the *halakha* is that a woman must immerse in a ritual bath on the morning when her seven clean days of *ziva* conclude.

In week two, she must immerse fourteen times — seven times at night and seven times in the morning. The seven nighttime immersions correspond to the possibility that she gave birth to a female and any of the seven nights of week two marks the conclusion of the fourteen-day *tumah* period of a *yoledet nekevah*. The seven morning immersions correspond to the possibility that she gave birth to a male and was also a *zava*, such that the seven days of week one served as her first clean days following the cessation of *zova* bleeding, and any morning of week two could be the seventh day of her clean days on which she immerses as a *zava*. This accounts for twenty-one immersions across the first two weeks.

### Week 3 Immersions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁבוּעַ שְׁלִישִׁי מַטְבִּילִין לַהּ (בִּימָמָא) [בִּימָמֵי], אֵימַר: יוֹלֶדֶת נְקֵבָה בְּזוֹב הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** During the **third week, she** is required **to immerse** every day **in the daytime,** as one can **say** that **she is a woman who gave birth to a female as a *zava*,** and consequently her fourteen-day period of impurity as a woman after childbirth ended during the second week. Therefore, the woman's seven clean days as a *zava* might end on any day of the third week, and she is obligated to immerse on that morning.

In week three, she immerses seven times in the morning — on each of the seven days of that clean week. These correspond to the possibility that she gave birth to a female and was also a *zava*, such that the fourteen days of *tumah* as a *yoledet nekevah* precluded counting the clean days of weeks one and two toward her seven clean days, and week three is the first valid week for counting. Any one of the seven days of week three could therefore be the seventh day of her seven clean days as a *zava*, requiring a daytime immersion on each. This brings the running total to twenty-eight, with further weeks — and the remaining sixty-seven immersions — to be accounted for in the continuation of the sugya.
