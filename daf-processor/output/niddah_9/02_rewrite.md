# Niddah 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Four Women Categories

### Never Menstruated

The Gemara on daf 9 opens by addressing the four categories of women who are granted *dayah sha'atah* — that is, if they discover blood, their *tumah* is reckoned only from the moment of discovery, without retroactive concern for the preceding twenty-four hours. The assumption underlying this dispensation is that women in these categories are not normally in a state of menstruation, so any bleeding they discover is presumed to have occurred just then. The first such category is a young woman who has never had her period. The Mishnah describes her as *kol shelo ra'asa* — one who has never seen menstrual blood.

### Hymenal & Birth Bleeding

The Gemara cites a *baraita*: if a woman married and bled as a result of the marriage — that is, hymenal bleeding — or if she gave birth and bled from the process of childbirth, she is still called a *besula*. The *baraita* notes that it is physiologically possible for a woman to conceive with the very first ovulation of her life, having never previously menstruated, and then to carry a child to term and give birth. In such a case, although she has experienced both hymenal and birth bleeding, she has still never bled from menstruation. The *baraita* concludes: *adayin hi nikra la besula* — she is still called a virgin in this context.

### Damim vs. Besulim

The critical clarification follows. When the Mishnah calls her a *besula*, it means *besulas damim* — a virgin of blood, one who has never bled menstrually — and not *besulas besulim*, a virgin in the physical or anatomical sense. The category is entirely about the history of menstrual bleeding, not about the status of the hymen or sexual history. A woman who has had intercourse or given birth but has never menstruated remains a *besulas damim* and is entitled to *dayah sha'atah*.

### Three Virgin Types

The Gemara then raises a difficulty. Rav Kahana cites a *Tosefta* which states that there are three things called *besula* — virgin — without qualification: *besulas adam*, *besulas karka*, and *besulas shikma*. If the Mishnah's *besulas damim* represents a fourth type of *besula*, why does the *Tosefta* enumerate only three?

### Besulas Adam

*Besulas adam* refers to a woman who has never had sexual intercourse: *kol zman shelo nivala* — as long as she has never been intimate. The practical consequences are twofold. First, a *Kohen Gadol* is required to marry a *besula*, and the relevant definition is a woman who has never had sexual relations. Second, in the laws of *kesubah* treated throughout Masechet Kesuvos, a woman assumed to be a virgin receives a *kesubah* of 200 *zuz*, whereas one who is known not to be a virgin receives only 100 *zuz* — and there, again, the operative definition is one of sexual history. It bears noting that this definition — one who has never had intercourse — is not identical to the anatomical question of whether the hymen is intact. The Gemara here defines *besulas adam* as a woman who has never had sex, which raises interesting questions about cases such as *mukat etz*, though the Gemara does not pursue them here.

### Besulas Karka

*Besulas karka* is virgin earth: *kol zman shelo ne'evda* — earth that has never been worked or plowed. Two legal contexts give this concept *nafka mina*. First, the law of *egla arufa*, the calf whose neck is broken in the valley when a murder victim is found and the killer is unknown, requires that the procedure take place in a *vadi asher lo uvad bo*, a valley never previously worked — that is, virgin earth. Admittedly, the Torah itself supplies the definition explicitly (*asher lo uvad bo*), so the term *besulas karka* does not add new information there; still, the concept has recognized standing in *halacha*. Second, for commercial transactions (*mikach u'mimkar*): if a seller represents land as virgin earth and it later proves otherwise, the buyer may void the sale, since he did not receive what was promised.

The *baraita* elaborates: *besulas karka* is earth that produces clumps (*regavim*) when dug rather than loose soil. If pottery shards are found embedded in the earth, the presumption is that humans worked it previously. Rock, however, is still considered *besulas karka*, even if the seller promised virgin earth and the plot turns out to contain solid rock — that is a separate question about misrepresentation, but the rock itself has never been worked.

### Besulas Shikma

*Besulas shikma* is a virgin sycamore tree — one that has never been cut: *kol zman shelo niktatza*. The practical interest here too is commercial: if one purchases a tree represented as a virgin sycamore, it must never have been cut, since cutting the tree causes it to regrow thicker and harder, yielding more productivity over time, and a buyer naturally wants to begin that process himself. Additionally, there is a *nafka mina* for *shvi'is* (the sabbatical year): the Mishnah states that one may not cut a virgin sycamore on *shvi'is* because doing so constitutes working it in the prohibited manner. If the tree has already been cut before, however, there is more room to permit certain actions to prevent loss, which would not be considered actual *avoda* (agricultural labor) in the full sense.

### Virgin Qualifier

Returning to the difficulty: since the *Tosefta* enumerates only three things called *besula*, how does our Mishnah introduce *besulas damim* as a fourth? Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves the question as follows: the *Tosefta* lists things called *besula* without any qualifying word — *shelo ra'asa*, no modifier necessary. But *besulas damim* — a virgin of blood, one who has never menstruated — requires a qualifier (*shem levai*) to convey its meaning. The *Tosefta*'s three are things that carry the meaning of "virgin" in unmodified form. Something that requires modification to communicate its particular sense of virginity is in a different category and was never included in the *Tosefta*'s list.

### Virgin Negation

Rav Sheshes son of Rav Idi offers an alternative answer. The *Tosefta*'s three types of *besula* share a defining feature: their virginity is negated through an act — through sexual intercourse, through working the earth, through cutting the tree. *Besulas damim*, by contrast, is terminated not by any deliberate action but by a natural process: the woman's body simply begins to menstruate. This is a categorically different kind of virginity, not the sort the *Tosefta* intended to enumerate.

### Virgin Irreversible

Rav Chanina son of Rav Ika offers a third answer: the *Tosefta* lists things whose virgin status, once lost, can never be restored. A woman who has had intercourse cannot become a *besulas adam* again. Earth that has been plowed is no longer virgin earth. A tree that has been cut is no longer a virgin sycamore. But a woman who has had her period might, later in life, enter a state where she no longer bleeds — postmenopause. In some sense, one might say she has returned to a condition resembling her original state. Since *besulas damim* can in this way be approximated again later, it is not of the same type as the three irreversible virginities listed by the *Tosefta*.

### Virgin Valued

Ravina offers a fourth answer: the *Tosefta* lists things that buyers and parties to a transaction *want* and *seek*. People desire to marry a virgin woman. People want to purchase virgin earth. People want to buy a virgin sycamore. *Besulas damim*, however — a woman who has never menstruated — is not something that is particularly sought after. Therefore it was not included in the *Tosefta*'s enumeration of desirable virginities.

### Blood & Fertility

The Gemara challenges this last answer. Is it really true that no one cares whether a woman has yet menstruated? A *baraita* is cited: Rabbi Chiya says, just as yeast (*se'or*) is beneficial for dough, so too abundant menstrual blood is beneficial for a woman. And it is taught in a *baraita* in the name of Rabbi Meir: *kol ishah shedameha merubin baneha merubin* — any woman whose menstrual flow is abundant will have many children. The Gemara's underlying medical assumption is that menstrual blood constitutes the "red matter" contributed by the mother to the formation of the embryo — the father's semen provides the white matter (skin, teeth, and so on), while the mother's blood provides the red matter (blood, organs, and so on). On this model, abundant menstrual blood correlates with greater reproductive capacity. If this is a known fact, one might think that men would very much care whether a woman is a *besulas damim* — a woman who has never bled — since that could correlate with a thinner endometrial lining and therefore with fewer or less healthy children.

### Virgin Definition

The Gemara therefore revises Ravina's formulation. The *Tosefta*'s three types of *besula* are not merely things that people care about, but things that people actively *desire* and *seek*: *midi dekafid vesalyi bizvina* — something that people care about and that buyers actually want to acquire. By contrast, *besulas damim* is not something people want. Men do not seek to marry a woman who has never menstruated; quite the contrary, the fact that a woman has a robust menstrual history is considered desirable. A *besulas damim* is a kind of virgin that is not in demand, and therefore she falls outside the *Tosefta*'s enumeration — which covers only those virginities that are valued and sought after.

## Pregnant Woman

### Pregnant Bleeds

The Gemara moves to the second category of women who receive *dayah sha'atah*: a pregnant woman. She is considered *mesuleket damim* — removed from menstruation — and therefore, if she sees blood, she is not retroactively *tamei* for the preceding twenty-four hours.

### Three Months

The Mishnah's formulation is *me'uberet misheyivada ubarah* — a pregnant woman from the time her pregnancy is recognizable. The Gemara asks: how far along must the pregnancy be to be considered *hakarat u'bara* — recognized? Sumkhos says in the name of Rabbi Meir: three months.

### Tamar Proof

The Gemara notes that there is no explicit scriptural *proof* for the three-month threshold, only a *zecher la'davar* — a scriptural hint. The verse describes Tamar's situation: *vayehi k'mishlosh chadashim* — and it was after three months that her pregnancy became apparent and she was brought forward to be punished. This indicates that three months is the standard for a pregnancy to become visible and recognized.

### Seven vs Nine

The Gemara immediately questions why this is called merely a *zecher* (hint) rather than a full *rayah* (proof). The answer: since some women carry to term at nine months and some at seven, the relevant stage of visibility might vary. Perhaps for a woman who will give birth at seven months, the one-third point of her pregnancy — approximately two and a third months — is when the pregnancy becomes apparent. It is therefore not a clean universal proof that three months is the threshold for all women, which is why the Gemara calls it only a hint.

### Miscarriage Case

The Gemara now presents a more complex case. A woman was known to be pregnant and she bled. Because she is pregnant, she is considered *mesuleket damim*, so she receives *dayah sha'atah* — retroactive *tumah* is not applied. Afterward, however, the pregnancy was unsuccessful: she gave birth to a *ruach* (literally "wind" — a non-viable fetus or a false pregnancy) or to something that was *she'eino shel kayama*, not viable. The question is: now that we know the pregnancy was unsuccessful, does that retroactively undermine her status? When she bled weeks earlier, she was presumably already pregnant — but since it never produced a viable birth, perhaps we should now consider that the pregnancy did not carry the relevant *halachic* weight, and she should be retroactively *tamei* for the twenty-four hours preceding that earlier bleeding.

The Gemara poses this question by noting a related *halacha* from a prior discussion: a woman who experienced birth pangs (*kashta*) for two days, bled throughout, and on the third day gave birth to something non-viable — the ruling is *harei zo yoledes b'zav*, she is a *zavah*. The reason is that the birth pangs and the labor can only attribute the bleeding to the birth if the birth was ultimately successful (*leida ma'alya*). If the birth was unsuccessful, the bleeding cannot be attributed to the birthing process, and therefore she becomes a *zavah*. This implies that when a pregnancy ends unsuccessfully, the *halachic* reality of pregnancy is retroactively undermined — which would seem to apply equally to our question.

### Negate Status?

The Gemara frames the tension sharply: if you say that even an unsuccessful birth is still considered a *leida* (birth) in the *halachic* sense — *kol she'samuch l'leida rachmana tahara* — then the bleeding surrounding it should not make her a *zavah*. But the ruling is that it does make her a *zavah*, which demonstrates that when a pregnancy ends unsuccessfully, the *halachic* status of pregnancy is not operative. If so, the same logic should apply to *dayah sha'atah*: retroactively, she should be *tamei* for the twenty-four hours before she bled, since her pregnancy turned out not to be halachically meaningful.

### Rabbinic Leniency

Rav Papi answers: *hanei milei miderabanan*. The twenty-four-hour retroactive *tumah* is only rabbinic in origin, not biblical. Since the entire institution of retroactive *tumah* is rabbinic, the Gemara is reluctant to apply it with full rigor, especially in cases where it would result in a kind of double retroactivity. The Tosafot explains that the reason retroactive *tumah* is treated leniently even among rabbinic rules is that it is a category of *tumah* one cannot actively protect against — a woman bleeds and only afterward discovers that the preceding hours are now implicated. Since there was no opportunity for prevention, the Rabbis are more inclined to find leniencies. In our case, once the pregnancy turns out to have been unsuccessful, to then go back further and declare her retroactively *tamei* for the period before the bleeding would be a double retroactive application — and that the Rabbis would not do.

### Pregnancy Reality

Rav Papa offers a sharper answer that dissolves the comparison entirely. The reason a pregnant woman does not bleed — and therefore is considered *mesuleket damim* — is not a formal *halachic* designation that depends on the outcome of the pregnancy. Rather, it reflects a physiological reality: *rosha kaved aleiha ve-eivarim kvedim aleiha* — her head feels heavy, her body feels heavy. The Gemara observed that pregnant women do not menstruate and attributed this to the specific physical symptoms they experience: a heaviness of head and limbs. That physiological reality does not change based on whether the pregnancy will ultimately succeed. The woman's body, while pregnant, is in that state — and that is what causes the absence of menstruation.

The question of whether the bleeding before childbirth makes a woman a *zavah*, by contrast, is a separate *halachic* determination: can the blood be *attributed* to the child? That attribution depends on whether there was actually a viable child. These are two different questions. One asks about the physiological cause of the absence of menstruation during pregnancy (which does not depend on the outcome), and the other asks about the legal attribution of bleeding to a specific birth event (which does depend on outcome). Rav Papa's conclusion: *hacha nami rosha ve-eivarim kvedim aleiha* — even when the pregnancy ends unsuccessfully, the physical reality that governed her body during pregnancy was real and genuine, and her status as *mesuleket damim* while pregnant is therefore unaffected.

### Early Pregnancy

Having resolved the question of unsuccessful pregnancies, the Gemara turns to a different and highly practical question. Rav Yirmiyah asked Rav Zeira: what is the status of a woman who bled and then, shortly thereafter, her pregnancy became visible — meaning she was clearly pregnant when she bled, but the pregnancy had not yet reached the three-month threshold of *hakarat u'bara*? Is she considered *mesuleket damim* even during the early months of pregnancy, before the three-month mark? Or is her *dayah sha'atah* status only operative once the pregnancy is recognizable?

The question has two dimensions. On one hand, she was objectively pregnant — the biological reality of pregnancy was present. On the other hand, the Gemara explicitly ties the status of *mesuleket damim* to a recognizable pregnancy (*misheyivada ubarah*), and the Tosafot emphasizes that the Gemara's question assumes the relevant threshold is indeed near that three-month point. The two possibilities the Gemara raises: perhaps since at the moment she bled the pregnancy was not yet visible, she is *tamei* retroactively (*mitamya*); or perhaps since the pregnancy became visible very shortly thereafter, it is obvious she was already in that state and she is not *tamei* retroactively (*lo mitamya*).

### Three-Month Threshold

Rav Zeira answers the question by referring back to the physiological reasoning already established. The reason a pregnant woman is *mesuleket damim* is *ella mishum deroshya kavid alei ve-eivarya kveidin alei* — because her head feels heavy and her body feels heavy. Before the three-month point, that physiological reality has not yet set in. Women do not generally begin experiencing the characteristic heaviness and physical symptoms of pregnancy until they are further along. Therefore, a woman who bleeds in the first trimester, before her pregnancy has reached the stage where these symptoms appear, is not yet considered *mesuleket damim* — the underlying condition that causes the exemption does not yet exist, even though she is technically pregnant.

### Modern Tests

This ruling has direct implications today. Pregnancy tests can now confirm pregnancy well before the three-month mark. The question arises: does a woman who tests positive and knows she is pregnant immediately acquire the status of *mesuleket damim*, or must she wait until three months? The Gemara's answer, as stated, is explicitly tied to the physiological state of feeling heavy — not merely to the knowledge of being pregnant. Modern medicine understands that the underlying hormonal changes of pregnancy begin immediately at conception, which is precisely what suppresses menstruation — not the feeling of heaviness as such. This raises a genuine *halacha* and science question: should we rule that *mesuleket damim* status begins from the moment a pregnancy is confirmed by test, since we now understand the true mechanism? There is discussion among the *poskim* on this point. Rav Moshe reportedly has a *teshuva* addressing the use of pregnancy tests to establish this status without waiting for three months. It is worth noting that accepting such a ruling would constitute a significant *chiddush*, because the Gemara explicitly grounds the *halacha* in a physiological description — *rosha kaved, eivarim kvedim* — and to rule on the basis of our scientific understanding rather than the Gemara's description is a substantive step in the application of science to *halacha*.

## Period Schedule Pregnancy

### Expected Day

The Gemara now introduces a related question from an elder (*saba*) who posed it to Rabbi Yochanan: *higgia vesat bimei iburah* — the time a pregnant woman would normally expect her period has arrived. Must she be concerned? In the laws of *niddah* as they apply today, the day a woman expects her *vesset* (established menstrual cycle) is a day on which the couple must separate, since having relations at the moment the period begins would mean she becomes a *niddah* mid-intercourse. The question is whether pregnancy exempts a woman from this concern — whether the fact that she is pregnant and is *mesuleket damim* overrides the obligation to be concerned about her *vesset* arriving.

To frame the question properly: imagine a woman whose *vesset* is calendar-based rather than interval-based — for example, she always sees on *Rosh Chodesh*. If she becomes pregnant, there will come a *Rosh Chodesh* during the pregnancy when she would normally expect her period. Must she and her husband separate that day? This scenario requires some ingenuity to construct within the Gemara's parameters, since three full periods without bleeding would ordinarily undermine a *vesset* — but one can imagine a *vesset* that occurs every other month, or some other configuration. In any event, the *halachic* question stands independently of the specific scenario: does pregnancy status override *vesset* concern?

### Vesset Biblical

The question is posed *aliba d'man d'amar vestot d'oraita* — according to the view that the obligation to anticipate one's *vesset* is biblical, not merely rabbinic. On this view, not only must a woman separate on the day she expects her period, but if that day passes and she has not checked herself, she is presumed *tamei* unless she can demonstrate otherwise. The *vesset* is so reliable a predictor, according to this position, that its arrival creates a presumption of *tumah* that must be actively rebutted. Given that background, the question is: does the state of being pregnant constitute sufficient counter-evidence to rebut that presumption without checking — or does the biblical-level force of the *vesset* require an actual *bdikah* (examination) even for a pregnant woman?

### Fear Withhold

Rabbi Yochanan answers by citing a proof from a Mishnah. Rabbi Meir rules: if a woman was hiding in a cave — the assumed scenario being flight from Roman persecution — and her *vesset* time arrived and she did not check herself, she is *tehorah*. She need not assume she became *niddah*. The reason: *shecharadah mesaleket et hadamim* — fear causes the blood to be withheld. Although the Gemara (and apparently Rabbi Meir) believed that extreme fear or stress could suppress menstruation, the key *halachic* inference is this: the reason Rabbi Meir permits her to assume *tehorah* is specifically because of the fear. This implies that absent the fear, a woman in whom the *vesset* time has passed without checking would be presumed *tamei*. The *vesset*, in other words, does carry biblical-level presumptive force.

And yet Rabbi Meir rules that fear is sufficient to override that presumption entirely — she does not even need to check. This is striking: even a biblical-level *vesset* prediction can be completely overridden by a counter-condition (fear) that makes it physiologically implausible that she would bleed. The Gemara concludes that pregnancy functions analogously.

### Pregnancy Vesset

The Gemara pauses to consider how this analogy works. One might wonder: since we know a pregnant woman is not going to bleed — or at least the Gemara presumes this — why is the *vesset* concern relevant at all? The answer seems to be that the Gemara did not understand pregnancy as creating an absolute hormonal certainty of no menstruation. Rather, the Gemara understood pregnancy as introducing a set of physiological conditions — heaviness of head and limbs — that tend to suppress bleeding, much as fear does. On this model, pregnancy is not categorically different from fear; both are conditions that interfere with the menstrual process, and the question is how strongly either can override the *vesset* prediction.

### Pregnancy Override

The Gemara concludes: just as the reality of fear is sufficient to completely override even a *vesset* that carries biblical presumptive force, so too the reality of pregnancy — *dameha mesulakin* — is sufficient to override the *vesset* concern. A pregnant woman does not need to check herself on the day she would have expected her period, and she may assume she is *tehorah* without doing so. The practical contemporary application is clear: a pregnant woman whose *vesset* day arrives is not required to separate from her husband on that day, even according to the stringent view that *vestot* are *d'oraita*.

## Nursing Woman

### Twenty-Four Months

The Gemara now addresses the third category of *mesuleket damim*: a nursing woman. As established in the Mishnah, she enjoys *dayah sha'atah* status for as long as she nurses, and this extends through the twenty-four-month standard period of nursing after childbirth. If she sees blood during this time, retroactive *tumah* for the preceding twenty-four hours is not applied.

### Child Death

The *baraita* introduced by the Gemara addresses a tragedy: *menika shemeis b'nah* — a nursing woman whose child dies within the twenty-four months. In such a case, *harei hi kechol hanashim* — she reverts to the status of all other women. She is no longer *mesuleket damim*, and if she subsequently bleeds, she is *tamei* for the preceding twenty-four hours or back to the last time she checked. This is because the status of a nursing woman, as the Gemara understands it, derives from the reality of nursing — and when the nursing ceases, so does the status.

### Reality Nursing

The *baraita* continues: *ulifichach im haysa menikaso v'holeches arba v'chamesh shanim* — and conversely, if she continued nursing for four or five years, she retains *dayah sha'atah* status for that entire extended period. This is the view of Rabbi Meir: the *halacha* follows the reality of nursing, not a fixed time period. If nursing stops early, the status stops. If nursing continues beyond twenty-four months, the status continues.

### Twenty-Four Fixed

Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Yossi, and Rabbi Shimon disagree. They hold that the status of *mesuleket damim* for a nursing woman is determined by the passage of twenty-four months from birth — *kol esrim v'arba chodesh* — regardless of whether she is actually nursing. If the child died but she is within twenty-four months of birth, she retains *dayah sha'atah* status. If twenty-four months have passed but she continues to nurse, she loses the status. The time period, not the physiological reality of nursing, is the determining factor.

### Blood to Milk

The Gemara then articulates what it takes to be the underlying dispute. It says *kishetimtza lomar* — "when you want to say," a phrase indicating that this is the Gemara's analysis rather than an explicit statement of the *Tannaim*: Rabbi Meir holds *dam ne'ekar vena'aseh chalav* — the blood that would otherwise become menstrual blood is transformed into milk. On this view, lactation is literally the conversion of menstrual blood into another substance. As long as the body is producing milk, the blood is being diverted into that production and will not manifest as menstrual blood. The moment nursing stops — whether because the child has died or been weaned — the blood is no longer being converted and menstruation can resume. This is why Rabbi Meir holds that the reality of nursing, and only the reality of nursing, determines the status.

### Body Recovery

The other *Tannaim* hold a different medical model: *eivareha mitparkin ve'ein nafsha chozeret aleiha ad esrim v'arba chodesh* — after childbirth, a woman's body feels disjointed and weakened, and it does not return to its normal strength for twenty-four months. On this view, the absence of menstruation after childbirth has nothing to do with nursing per se; it is a consequence of the physical trauma of birth and the body's extended recovery process. This recovery takes twenty-four months regardless of whether the woman nurses. Therefore, the relevant measure is the passage of twenty-four months from birth, not the continuation of nursing.

### Extended Case

The Gemara asks: given each view, why did each side need to specify the extended case (*lefichach*)? According to Rabbi Meir, it is obvious that if nursing continues for four or five years, the status continues — that follows directly from his principle. The Gemara answers: Rabbi Meir stated the *lefichach* in order to respond to Rabbi Yossi's position of twenty-four months, making explicit that he disagrees and that any duration of nursing determines the status. Similarly, one might ask why Rabbi Yossi needed to state that if nursing extends beyond twenty-four months the status ends — surely that follows from his principle. The Gemara answers: one might have thought Rabbi Yossi holds that *either* condition is sufficient — either twenty-four months from birth *or* active nursing — and that both together, or either independently, suffice to grant *dayah sha'atah* status. Therefore Rabbi Yossi had to make explicit that he goes solely by twenty-four months, and that extended nursing beyond that period adds nothing.

### Sources Both

A *baraita* is then cited that confirms this reading of the dispute explicitly: *dam ne'ekar vena'aseh chalav divrei Rabbi Meir* — the blood congeals and turns into milk, the words of Rabbi Meir. *Rabbi Yose omer: eivareha mitparkin ve'ein nafsha chozeret aleiha ad esrim v'arba chodesh* — Rabbi Yossi says her body is disjointed and does not recover for twenty-four months.

### Job Verse

Rabbi Ila'a then asks: what is Rabbi Meir's scriptural source for the idea that blood transforms into milk? He cites the verse from Iyov: *mi yiten tahor mitamei lo echad* — "Who will give something pure from something impure? Is it not the One?" — meaning God. Something *tahor* emerges from something *tamei*. Rabbi Meir interprets this as a reference to milk (*tahor*) emerging from menstrual blood (*tamei*).

### Semen Parallel

The Rabbis interpret the same verse differently. Rabbi Yochanan says: *zu shichvat zera shehi tme'ah v'adam notzar mimena tahor* — this refers to semen, which is itself *tamei*, and yet a human being — who is *tahor* — is formed from it. The verse speaks to the broader phenomenon that the generative substances of human reproduction, both the semen and the menstrual blood, are *tamei*, yet they produce something *tahor*. Rabbi Elazar offers a third interpretation: the verse alludes to the waters of *parah adumah*, which are called *mei niddah* in the Torah. These waters present the classic paradox of *parah adumah*: those who are involved in the purification process — the one who sprinkles and the one who is sprinkled upon — become *tahor*, while those who merely touch or carry the waters become *tamei*. *Tahor* emerges from *tamei*.

The Gemara's analysis of this final interpretation involves a careful parsing of the verses. One verse states that *umazzeh mei haniddah yechabbes begadav* — the one who sprinkles must wash his clothes, indicating he is *tamei*. Another verse addresses the one who *touches* the water. The Gemara concludes that both the one who sprinkles and the one who touches are *tamei*, and indeed the sprinkler (*hamazzeh*) is *tamei* to a greater degree than the one who merely touches (*hanogea*), since the sprinkler's clothes are also *tamei* while the one who touches does not have that stringency. How then does one say that something *tahor* emerges from something *tamei*? The Gemara reinterprets *mazzeh* in the verse not as the active sprinkler but as the one who *carries* the water (*noseh*). And one is not *tamei* for merely carrying the water unless one carries at least the minimum amount required for sprinkling. The Tosafot notes the further question: this works well according to the view that there is a minimum measure (*shi'ur*) for *haza'ah* (sprinkling); according to the view that no minimum measure is required for *haza'ah* to be effective, what can be said? The Gemara answers: even according to that view, the requirement of a minimum measure applies not to the amount that strikes the person being purified, but to the amount in the vessel from which the *hyssop* is dipped — *beinah shi'ur* — which is measured as enough to dip the tops of the hyssop branches and successfully sprinkle. The Mishnah elsewhere specifies this minimum: *kedei sheyitbol roshei givolim veyazah* — enough water to dip the hyssop tips and sprinkle. This paradox — that the very substance that causes *tumah* to those outside the purification process causes *tahara* to those within it — is what Shlomo had in mind when he said *amarti echkama vehia rechoka mimenni*: "I thought I would become wise, and realized it is beyond me." *Parah adumah* is the classic *chok*, the paradigmatic law that defies rational comprehension.

## Postmenopausal

### Three Cycles

The fourth and final category of *mesuleket damim* is the *zekeinah* — the postmenopausal woman. She is defined as one who has had three regular cycles pass (*shalosh onot*) without bleeding, and this must occur *samuch leziknata* — at a time proximate to her old age.

### Peers Perceive

The Gemara asks what constitutes *samuch leziknata*. Rav Yehuda defines it: *kol shechaveroteha omrot aleiha zekeinah hi* — any woman whom her peers describe as old, as a *zekeinah*.

### Called Imma

Rabbi Shimon offers a different formulation: *kol shekorein lah imma imma ve'eina bosha* — anyone who is called *imma imma* (mother, or in this context, grandmother) and is not embarrassed by it. Some versions of the text have only a single *imma* rather than the double form.

### Embarrassment

The Gemara records a further refinement from Rav Zeira and Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak. One of them says the criterion is *kol she'eina makpedes* — she does not object or protest. The other says *kol she'eina bousha* — she is not embarrassed. What distinguishes these two? The Gemara explains: the case of *bousha ve'eina makpedes* — she is inwardly embarrassed but does not verbally object. On one reading, the psychological experience of embarrassment comes later in this sequence than the willingness to register a formal protest; one might feel inward discomfort before she is ready to openly object. Or alternatively, *makpedes* refers to a verbal protest while *bousha* is a deeper, more personal emotional reaction. In either reading, the case of a woman who feels embarrassed but says nothing is the distinguishing scenario between the two opinions.

Tosafot notes that this formulation seems strange: why would a woman's subjective emotional response determine her *halachic* status? Tosafot therefore explains that the criterion is not truly about the individual woman's psychology, but about what a normal woman of her apparent age would or would not be embarrassed about. The perception by others — and the typical emotional response of women at that stage of life — serves as a social indicator of her apparent age.

The absence of a fixed chronological age threshold is also striking. One might have expected the Gemara to say "a woman of fifty" or "a woman of sixty." The Gemara instead anchors the definition to social perception and appearance. This presumably reflects an understanding that the onset of menopause varies significantly among individuals and does not correspond to a single age.

### Estrogen Link

Interestingly, there is a scientific correlation underlying the Gemara's formulation. The question one might ask is: why should the appearance of old age correlate with the cessation of menstruation? The answer is that the depletion of estrogen causes both phenomena simultaneously. It is estrogen that maintains both the menstrual cycle and many of the physical qualities associated with a youthful appearance. As estrogen levels decline, both menopause and the visible signs of aging progress together. The causation runs from estrogen depletion to both consequences — not from one to the other — but the *correlation* between the two is real and well-grounded in physiology. The Gemara, in other words, was entirely correct not to set a fixed chronological age, and instead to point to the appearance of aging as the relevant indicator — since that appearance is driven by the same underlying biological process that causes menopause.

### Cycle Length

How long is three *onot* (cycles)? Resh Lakish says in the name of Rabbi Yehuda Nesia: *ona beinonit shloshim yom* — the average cycle is thirty days. Therefore three cycles constitute ninety days. Rava says in the name of Rav Chisda: twenty days. The Gemara explains that these opinions do not actually dispute one another. One authority is counting the full cycle including the seven days of *tumah* (during which she is a *niddah*) plus the following twenty-three days until the next bleeding — totaling thirty. The other is counting only the twenty-three days of *tahara*, excluding the seven days of *tumah*. Some textual versions have twenty-three rather than twenty, which would correspond exactly to this calculation. In any case, everyone agrees that the standard menstrual cycle in the Gemara's reckoning is thirty days — and therefore a *zekeinah* who has gone three cycles, ninety days, without bleeding, and who appears old enough that her peers would call her a *zekeinah* or call her *imma*, is considered postmenopausal and *mesuleket damim* going forward.
