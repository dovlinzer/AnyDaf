# Hullin 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Trachea/Esophagus Status

### Treifa vs Neveilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְקוּבַת הַוֶּושֶׁט וּפְסוּקַת הַגַּרְגֶּרֶת!
>
> **Translation:** An animal that has **a perforated gullet,** where the perforation goes through the wall of the gullet, **or** one with **a severed windpipe.** According to this mishna, the severed windpipe renders the animal a *tereifa* and not an unslaughtered carcass.

The daf opens in the middle of a discussion on 32b concerning the fundamental distinction between *treifa* and *neveilah*. A *neveilah* is an animal that died without the benefit of *shechita* — including cases where the *shechita* was ruined in the process — whereas a *treifa* is an animal that received a valid *shechita* but suffered some form of internal or external injury. The *gemara* has been troubled by the fact that among the listed *treifos* are animals with holes in the esophagus or cut tracheas, because it assumes — and will ultimately conclude — that once either of those *simanim* is damaged, no valid *shechita* can ever be performed on that animal. If that is so, how can such a condition produce a *treifa* rather than a *neveilah*?

### Cut Trachea Problem


The problem runs as follows: if a cut trachea or esophagus renders any subsequent *shechita* meaningless, then an animal presenting with that condition can never receive a valid *shechita* at all. It should therefore always be classified as a *neveilah* — yet the *mishnah* describes it as a *treifa*.

### Cut Incorporated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: כָּאן – שֶׁשָּׁחַט בִּמְקוֹם חֲתָךְ, כָּאן – שֶׁשָּׁחַט שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹם חֲתָךְ. שָׁחַט בִּמְקוֹם חֲתָךְ – נִפְסְלָה בִּשְׁחִיטָה הִיא, שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹם חֲתָךְ – כִּי דָּבָר אַחֵר גָּרַם לָהּ לִיפָּסֵל דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish** resolved the apparent contradiction between the mishna here and the mishna on 42a, and **said: Here,** the *tanna* is referring to a case **where one cut** the gullet **in** the same **place** as the initial **cut** in the windpipe. **There,** on 42a, the *tanna* is referring to a case **where he cut** the gullet **not in** the same **place** as the initial **cut** in the windpipe. The reason for the distinction is that if **one cut** the gullet **in** the same **place** as the initial **cut** in the windpipe, it is an animal that **was rendered unfit during its slaughter** and it assumes the status of an unslaughtered carcass. By contrast, if he cut the gullet **not in** the same **place** as the initial **cut** in the windpipe it is **like** a case where **another matter caused** the animal **to become unfit.** Therefore, cutting the gullet renders it a *tereifa* and prevents it from imparting ritual impurity.

The most satisfying answer reached at the end of the previous session was that the distinction depends on whether the *shechita* incorporated the cut. If there was a pre-existing cut in the trachea or esophagus and the *shochet* cut at a different location, the *shechita* is valid and the pre-existing cut constitutes an external defect — rendering the animal a *treifa*. If, however, the *shochet* cut precisely at the location of that pre-existing cut, the damaged portion became incorporated into the act of *shechita* itself and invalidated it, producing a *neveilah* rather than a *treifa*.

### Shimon Lakish Lung

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: שָׁחַט אֶת הַקָּנֶה וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִיקְּבָה הָרֵיאָה כְּשֵׁרָה! אַלְמָא כְּמַאן דְּמַנְּחָא בְּדִיקּוּלָא דָּמְיָא, הָכָא נָמֵי כְּמַאן דְּמַנְּחָא בְּדִיקּוּלָא דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish say this,** that slaughter can be effective to prevent impurity after the windpipe was severed? **But doesn't Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish say:** If one **cut the windpipe and the lung was perforated thereafter** before he cut the gullet, the animal is **fit** for consumption? **Apparently,** after cutting the windpipe, the status of the lungs, whose existence is dependent upon the windpipe, **is like** that of an item **that is placed in a basket;** they are irrelevant to the determination of the animal's status. **Here too,** when the windpipe is severed at the beginning of the slaughter the windpipe should be considered **like** an item **that is placed in a basket** in the sense that the animal is considered to have only one *siman*, the gullet, and cutting of one *siman* does not affect the animal's status.

The *gemara* now challenges this framework by citing a ruling of Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish. The text states: *umi amar Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish hachi* — did Reish Lakish truly say that it matters whether the *shechita* was performed at the site of the cut or elsewhere? Against this, the *gemara* cites another statement of Reish Lakish: *shachat et ha-kaneh ve-achar kach nikva ha-reia, kesheira* — if one *shechted* the trachea and afterward the lung developed a hole, the animal is *kesheira*. This raises a further question that the *gemara* finds compelling: what is the law if an animal develops a *treifa* condition between the cutting of the first *siman* and the second? One might think the onset of *shechita* renders subsequent injury irrelevant, or one might argue that since the *shechita* is not yet complete, the animal remains susceptible to becoming a *treifa* mid-process.

In Reish Lakish's case, the situation is even more specific: once the trachea is cut, there is no longer any physiological relevance to the lung. Even if we were to ask this question about injury between the two *simanim* as a general matter, the lung seems obviously irrelevant once the trachea has been severed — the two form a single functional system. Reish Lakish therefore rules that a hole appearing in the lung after the trachea has been *shechted* does not render the animal a *treifa*.

### Lung Irrelevant


The *gemara* draws out the principle underlying Reish Lakish's ruling: *keman de-mancha be-dikula damya* — once the trachea is cut, the entire apparatus of trachea and lung is considered as if it has been placed in a basket, meaning it is no longer part of the living animal. A hole in the lung at that point is irrelevant because that organ has already been rendered as though removed.

### Organ Rendered Removed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן קוֹדֶם חֲזָרָה, כָּאן לְאַחַר חֲזָרָה, וּמִשְׁנָה לֹא זָזָה מִמְּקוֹמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The apparent contradiction is **not difficult. There** (42a), where it is stated that an animal with a severed windpipe is a *tereifa*, the mishna presents the opinion of Rabbi Akiva **before retraction** of his opinion. **Here,** the mishna presents the opinion of Rabbi Akiva **after retraction** of his opinion. **And** even though Rabbi Akiva retracted his opinion, **a mishna does not move from its place.** Once this version of the mishna was learned in the study hall, it remained valuable, even though it is no longer current.

The *gemara* then applies this principle back to the original problem. If a cut trachea causes the entire trachea-lung system to be treated as if removed — *hacha nami keman de-mancha be-dikula damya* — then, by the same logic, a pre-existing cut to the trachea or esophagus renders that organ as if it no longer exists as part of the animal. And if the organ is treated as nonexistent, how can one perform a *shechita* on it at all? A *shechita* requires cutting a living, functional organ. This brings the *gemara* full circle to the original difficulty: the very legal fiction that saves the animal from *treifa* status when the lung is punctured post-*shechita* is the same legal fiction that makes it impossible to do a valid *shechita* on an already-cut trachea or esophagus.

### Mishna Not Changed


The *gemara* concedes the force of this logic and offers a resolution through Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *lo kashya, kan kodem chazara, kan le-achar chazara, u-mishna lo zazah mi-mekomah* — it is not difficult; one source reflects Rabbi Akiva's position before his retraction, the other reflects his position after, and the *mishnah* was not removed from its place.

To understand this: the *mishnah* that classifies a cut trachea or esophagus as a *treifa* was taught during the period when Rabbi Akiva held that a ruined *shechita* still produces a *treifa* rather than a *neveilah*. Once Rabbi Akiva retracted that position and acknowledged that a defective *shechita* yields a *neveilah*, the logical consequence follows: an animal with a cut trachea or esophagus can never receive a valid *shechita*, and so it must be a *neveilah*. The *mishnah* text, however, was not revised after the retraction — *mishna lo zazah mi-mekomah*, a standing principle that once a *mishnah* was taught, its text is preserved even if the operative ruling later changed.

### Final: Neveilah


The *gemara*'s conclusion is striking: once the trachea or esophagus has been cut to the degree that constitutes a *treifa* condition, that organ is treated as if it no longer exists within the living animal. A *shechita* cannot be validly performed on it, and the animal therefore becomes a *neveilah*. This is the actual *halacha* according to the *gemara*'s final position.

One might ask: could there not be a case where an animal has a cut trachea and yet receives a valid *shechita* that yields *treifa* rather than *neveilah*? In the case of birds, where *shechita* requires only one *siman*, the answer may be yes — if the trachea is cut but the *shechita* is performed on the esophagus, or vice versa, the valid *siman* is still intact and operable, the cut *siman* is effectively treated as nonexistent, and so one might still achieve a *treifa* rather than a *neveilah*. With large animals, however, both *simanim* must be cut, and once one is treated as nonexistent, the *shechita* can never be completed validly — making *neveilah* the inevitable result.

## Treifa Mid-Shechita

### Rava: Lung Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: שָׁחַט אֶת הַקָּנֶה וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִיקְּבָה הָרֵיאָה – כְּשֵׁרָה. אָמַר רָבָא: לָא אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אֶלָּא בְּרֵיאָה, הוֹאִיל וְחַיֵּי רֵיאָה תְּלוּיִן בַּקָּנֶה, אֲבָל בִּבְנֵי מֵעַיִים – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses **the** matter **itself** that was cited previously. **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says:** If one **cut the windpipe and the lung was perforated thereafter** before he cut the gullet, the animal is **fit** for consumption. **Rava said: Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish states** this principle **only with regard to a lung, since the function of the lung is dependent upon the windpipe.** Once the windpipe is severed it is as though the lungs were removed from the animal. **But with regard to innards** that were perforated after the windpipe was cut but before the slaughter was completed, **no,** the animal becomes a *tereifa*, because the function of the innards is not dependent on the windpipe.

The *gemara* now examines Reish Lakish's ruling in its own right — *gufa* — independent of its use in the earlier discussion. Reish Lakish stated: *shachat et ha-kaneh ve-achar kach nikva ha-reia* — if one *shechted* the trachea and afterward the lung developed a hole, the animal is *kesheira*. Rava qualifies this ruling significantly: *lo amar Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish hachi ela be-reia* — Reish Lakish made this statement only with respect to the lung. Why? *ho'il ve-chayei reia teluya ba-kaneh* — because the life of the lung is dependent on the trachea. Once the trachea is severed, the entire trachea-lung unit is rendered moot, and a hole in the lung is therefore irrelevant. But as for the other internal organs — *aval bi-vnei me'ayim, lo* — an injury to the intestines or other organs during the *shechita* process can still render the animal a *treifa*. So long as the animal has not been fully *shechted*, it can still become a *treifa* through injury to organs not already rendered irrelevant by the act of *shechita*.

### Zeira Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא: מֵאַחַר שֶׁנּוֹלְדוּ בָּהּ סִימָנֵי טְרֵפָה הִתַּרְתָּ, מָה לִי בְּרֵיאָה, מָה לִי בִּבְנֵי מֵעַיִים!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira objects to** Rava's distinction. **Since** when **signs of being a *tereifa* developed** in the animal after the slaughter began, **you permitted** the animal and deemed the slaughter valid, **what** difference is there **to me** if the perforation was **in** the **lung,** and **what** difference is there **to me** if the perforation was **in** the **innards?** In either case, the animal should not be deemed a *tereifa* if the signs developed once the slaughter began.

Rabbi Zeira challenged Rava's distinction. His objection: once we have accepted that some degree of *shechita* has been performed and the animal is being killed, why should it matter which organ is injured? Either the onset of the *shechita* process immunizes the animal from acquiring *treifa* status — in which case no injury to any organ should matter — or it does not, and then we should not be selective about which organ we track. If Rabbi Zeira's position were accepted, it would mean that once the process of *shechita* begins, no subsequent injury to any organ can render the animal a *treifa*.

### Zeira Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲדַר בֵּיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא, דְּבָעֵי רַבִּי זֵירָא: נִיקְּבוּ בְּנֵי מֵעַיִים בֵּין סִימָן לְסִימָן, מַהוּ? מִי מִצְטָרֵף סִימָן רִאשׁוֹן לְסִימָן שֵׁנִי לְטַהֲרָהּ מִידֵי נְבֵלָה, אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** **But Rabbi Zeira retracted** his objection, **as Rabbi Zeira raises a dilemma:** If the **innards were perforated between** the cutting of the first ***siman* and** the second ***siman* what is** the *halakha*? **Does the first *siman* join** together **with the second *siman* to purify** the animal **from** the impurity of **an unslaughtered carcass or not?**

Rabbi Zeira, however, reversed himself — *va-chazar bo Rabbi Zeira*. The question is which position he reversed: the initial challenge or what follows. Following Rashi's reading, he reversed his first claim — namely, that onset of *shechita* always immunizes against *treifa*. The evidence of his reversal comes from a question Rabbi Zeira himself posed.

### Combine Simanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי מִצְטָרֵף סִימָן רִאשׁוֹן לְסִימָן שֵׁנִי לְטַהֲרָהּ מִידֵי נְבֵלָה, אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies this dilemma: **Does the first *siman* join** together **with the second *siman* to purify the animal from** the impurity of **an unslaughtered carcass or not?** In both cases the dilemma is: Does the cutting of the first *siman*, which serves the dual purpose of being a component of permitting consumption and preventing impurity of the animal, join together with the cutting of the second *siman*, which serves only the purpose of preventing impurity, in order to constitute a single act of slaughter and thereby prevent the animal from assuming the impurity of an unslaughtered carcass? Or perhaps because the cutting of each *siman* is performed for a different purpose they do not join together?

Rabbi Zeira asked: *nikvu bnei me'ayim bein siman le-siman mahu* — if the internal organs developed a hole between the cutting of the first *siman* and the second, what is the law? The very framing of this question assumes that the animal has indeed become a *treifa* — this assumption is itself the evidence of Rabbi Zeira's reversal, since he is no longer claiming that injury during *shechita* is categorically irrelevant. His question concerns something else entirely: perhaps the animal's status should be worse than *treifa*, and it should be classified as a *neveilah*.

The reasoning behind this question is subtle. At the moment the first *siman* was cut, the act of *shechita* was intended to make the animal fully permitted. Once the animal became a *treifa* mid-process, the second half of the *shechita* can no longer serve that function — all it can accomplish is to prevent the animal from being classified as a *neveilah*, which carries the additional consequence of *tumah*. Since the two halves of the *shechita* serve different *halachic* functions — the first oriented toward full permission, the second (post-*treifa*) oriented only toward avoiding *neveilah* status — perhaps they cannot be combined into a single unified act of *shechita*. And if they cannot combine, the *shechita* is incomplete, and the animal is a *neveilah*.

### Fetus Limb Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמְרִינַן: לָאו הַיְינוּ דְּבָעֵי אִילְפָא: הוֹצִיא עוּבָּר אֶת יָדוֹ בֵּין סִימָן לְסִימָן, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **And we say: Isn't this** the **dilemma that Ilfa raises: If a fetus extended its foreleg** outside the womb while the mother was being slaughtered, **between** the cutting of the first ***siman*,** the windpipe, **and the second *siman*,** the gullet, thereby causing the foreleg to have the status of a *tereifa*, **what is** the *halakha*?

The *gemara* notes that Rabbi Zeira's question closely parallels a question posed by Ilfa: *hotzi ubar yado bein siman le-siman* — a fetus extended a limb from the womb between the two *simanim* of the mother's *shechita*. It is an established *halacha* that *shechita* of the mother applies to the fetus in the womb, and that if a limb protrudes before the *shechita*, that limb is considered a *treifa* — as derived from a verse that will be discussed in a later *perek*. The question is: if the limb protruded between the two *simanim*, is that limb at least protected from *neveilah* status by virtue of the *shechita*, or have the two halves failed to combine into a valid act?

### Injury = Treifa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כָּאן לָא אִיבַּעְיָא לַן, אֶלָּא לְטַהֲרָהּ מִידֵי נְבֵלָה, אֲבָל בַּאֲכִילָה – אֲסוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** In any event, **we raise the dilemma only** in order **to purify** the foreleg **from** the impurity of **an unslaughtered carcass. But with regard to eating** the slaughtered animal, all agree that it is **forbidden,** as even Rabbi Zeira concedes that the animal is a *tereifa* and retracts his objection to the distinction that Rava proposed between the lungs and the innards.

The *gemara* explicitly draws the conclusion from both questions: *ad kan lo ibayei lan ela le-tahara midei neveilah, aval be-achila asura* — the only question under consideration is whether the animal or limb can be rescued from *neveilah* status; it is assumed in both cases that the injury during *shechita* renders the animal or limb a *treifa* and forbidden to eat. This confirms that Rabbi Zeira had indeed reversed himself and accepted that injury between the two *simanim* produces *treifa* status.

### Acha: No Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר רַב לְרָבִינָא: דִּלְמָא לְעוֹלָם לָא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ, וְרַבִּי זֵירָא לִדְבָרָיו דְּרָבָא קָאָמַר, וְלֵיהּ לָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Rav said to Ravina: Perhaps** Rabbi Zeira **actually did not retract** his opinion, as even initially he held that there is no distinction between lungs and innards. If either is perforated after one *siman* was cut, the animal is a *tereifa*. **And Rabbi Zeira stated** his objection to the distinction of Rava **in accordance with the statement of Rava, but** he **himself does not hold accordingly.**

Rav Acha bar Rav offered a different reading to Ravina: *dilma le-olam lo hadar bei — d'Rabbi Zeira le-divrei d'Rava ka'amar* — perhaps Rabbi Zeira never reversed himself at all. Rather, Rabbi Zeira was simply arguing within Rava's own framework. That is: Rabbi Zeira personally maintains that once *shechita* begins, no injury can produce *treifa* status for any organ. But arguing on Rava's own terms, he asks: if you, Rava, hold that most organs can still become *treifa* mid-*shechita*, then consider this hypothetical — can the two halves of the *shechita* combine to at least prevent *neveilah* status? The question is posed within Rava's position, not as Rabbi Zeira's own settled view.

### Unresolved


The question of whether an animal can become a *treifa* during the process of *shechita* — and precisely how to rule in such a case — remains unresolved. Rabbi Zeira's own position is itself uncertain: it is unclear whether he reversed himself, and if so, in which direction. Rashi and Tosafot debate which side of the argument constitutes his reversal. And even setting aside Rabbi Zeira, we do not know definitively how the *halacha* rules on this question.

## Jews vs Non-Jews

### Shechita Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִדְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ, מְזַמְּנִין יִשְׂרָאֵל עַל בְּנֵי מֵעַיִין, וְאֵין מְזַמְּנִין גּוֹי עַל בְּנֵי מֵעַיִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its analysis of the statement of Reish Lakish, who said that after the windpipe is cut, the lung is considered as though it was placed in a basket, and if it is perforated before the slaughter is completed, the animal does not become a *tereifa*. **Rav Aḥa bar Yaakov said: Learn from** the statement **of Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish** that **one may invite Jews** to eat the **innards** of an animal that was slaughtered, **but one may not invite gentiles** to eat the **innards** of an animal that was slaughtered, because they are forbidden to gentiles.

From the principle of Reish Lakish — that once the trachea is cut, the entire trachea-lung system is considered as if placed in a basket — the *gemara* infers a practical ruling with far-reaching implications for the distinction between Jewish and non-Jewish law. *shema mina mi-de-rabbi shimon ben lakish*: we derive from Reish Lakish's principle the following. Jews may invite their friends to eat the inner organs of a *shechted* animal while it is still alive; non-Jews may not be so invited.

### Organs Removed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? יִשְׂרָאֵל דְּבִשְׁחִיטָה תַּלְיָא מִילְּתָא, כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא שְׁחִיטָה מְעַלַּיְיתָא – אִישְׁתְּרִי לְהוּ; גּוֹיִם דְּבִנְחִירָה סַגִּי לְהוּ וּבְמִיתָה תַּלְיָא מִילְּתָא, הָנֵי – כְּאֵבֶר מִן הַחַי דָּמוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason?** For **Jews the matter** of rendering the meat of the animal fit for consumption **is dependent upon** the performance of a valid act of **slaughter. Once there is full-fledged slaughter** and both *simanim* are cut, the innards **are permitted to them** even if the animal is convulsing. But with regard to **gentiles, for whom stabbing is sufficient** and slaughter is not required, the innards are permitted only after the animal is completely dead, since **the matter** of rendering the meat of the animal fit for consumption **is dependent upon** its **death.** Therefore, if the animal is still convulsing, **these** innards, which are considered to be outside the body after the cutting of the two *simanim*, are **considered like a limb from a living** animal and it is forbidden for gentiles to eat them.

The reason with respect to Jews: *yisrael de-vi-shechita talya milta* — for a Jew, the entire status of the animal turns on *shechita*. *keivan de-ika shechita malya hi, sharya lehu* — since there was a valid *shechita*, the meat is permitted, even while the animal still lives.

For non-Jews the situation is different: *ovdei kochavim de-ve-nechira sagya lehu* — non-Jews have no concept of *shechita*; even a simple stabbing suffices to permit meat for them. *u-ve-mita talya milta* — for non-Jews, the permissibility of the meat depends on death. Since the animal has not yet died when the inner organs are served, and since the *gemara* applies Reish Lakish's legal fiction so literally as to treat the organs as already physically removed at the moment the first *siman* is cut, those organs are considered *eiver min ha-chai* from the perspective of a non-Jew. For a Jew, the act of *shechita* justifies treating the removal as part of a sanctioned process; for a non-Jew, the animal was still alive when these organs were, in a legal sense, removed, and they therefore carry the status of a limb severed from a living animal.

This is admittedly a striking application of the legal fiction. The *gemara* acknowledges its strangeness: the animal was not physically disassembled, and yet the *gemara* takes the metaphor of *keman de-mancha be-dikula damya* — as if placed in a basket — with full literalness, treating it as actual *eiver min ha-chai* for non-Jewish law. The more natural case where a genuine distinction arises between Jew and non-Jew is where the animal was fully *shechted* and then a piece of meat was physically removed while the animal was still alive: for a Jew, the *shechita* was complete and the removal is not *eiver min ha-chai*; for a non-Jew, the animal was still breathing and therefore it is. That straightforward case is worth keeping in mind as the *gemara* continues.

### Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הֲוָה יָתֵיבְנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב, וּבְעַי דְּאֵימָא לֵיהּ, מִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דִּלְיִשְׂרָאֵל שְׁרֵי וּלְגוֹיִם אָסוּר? וְלָא אֲמַרִי לֵיהּ, דְּאָמֵינָא: הָא טַעְמָא קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: I was sitting before Rav Aḥa bar Yaakov and I sought to say to him** that his statement is difficult: **Is there anything that is permitted for a Jew but prohibited for a gentile? But I did not say** that **to him, as I said** to myself: **Didn't he say a reason** for his ruling? Therefore, there is no reason to ask the question.

This leads to a classic *halachic* principle invoked by Rav Pappa: *mi ika midi de-le-Yisrael shari u-le-ovad kochavim assur* — can there exist something that is permitted to a Jew but forbidden to a non-Jew? The *gemara* in *sanhedrin* establishes as a general principle that the *mitzvos* given to Jews are additions to the pre-existing obligations of the *bnei noach*, not replacements for them. Since Jewish law was layered atop universal law, it should never produce a leniency unavailable to non-Jews. One way of framing the principle is that as human beings, Jews remain subject to the seven Noahide commandments — and nothing in Torah law can be more permissible for Jews than what the Noahide framework permits for all of humanity.

### Acha & Pappa


Rav Pappa relates that when sitting before Rav Acha bar Yaakov, who stated the ruling distinguishing Jews and non-Jews with respect to the inner organs, he wanted to raise this objection — but ultimately did not. His reason for restraint: *hai ta'ama ka'amar* — Rav Acha bar Yaakov offered a principled rationale. The leniency for Jews does not represent a case of Jewish law being simply more permissive; rather, it is a logical consequence of a stringency. Jews require *shechita*, and without it the meat is entirely forbidden. The concept of *shechita* introduces a framework in which the act of *shechita* governs the meat's status, independently of the animal's moment of death. Whatever leniency flows from that framework is a derivative consequence of a stricter regime, not an arbitrary privilege.

### Baraita: Both Permit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא דְּלָא כְּרַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: הָרוֹצֶה לֶאֱכוֹל מִבְּהֵמָה קוֹדֶם שֶׁתֵּצֵא נַפְשָׁהּ – חוֹתֵךְ כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר מִבֵּית הַשְּׁחִיטָה, וּמוֹלְחוֹ יָפֶה יָפֶה, וּמְדִיחוֹ יָפֶה יָפֶה, וּמַמְתִּין לָהּ עַד שֶׁתֵּצֵא נַפְשָׁהּ, וְאוֹכְלוֹ, אֶחָד גּוֹי וְאֶחָד יִשְׂרָאֵל מוּתָּרִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **It is taught** in a *baraita* **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Aḥa bar Yaakov: One who seeks to eat from** the meat of **an animal before its soul departs may cut an olive-bulk of meat from the area of the slaughter,** the neck, **and salt it very well,** i.e., more than is normally required, **and rinse it very well** in water to remove the salt and the blood, **and wait until the** animal's **soul departs, and eat it.** It is **permitted** for **both a gentile and a Jew to** eat **it.** Contrary to the statement of Rav Aḥa bar Yaakov, there is no distinction between Jew and gentile.

Nonetheless, the *gemara* cites a *baraita* that appears to directly contradict Rav Acha bar Yaakov's position: *harotze le-echol mi-behemah kodem she-teitzei nafshah* — one who wishes to eat from an animal before it dies. The *baraita* instructs: cut a *kezayis* of meat from the place of the *shechita* — the neck, where the flesh is exposed — while the animal is still alive. Then salt it thoroughly, rinse it thoroughly to remove the blood, and wait for the animal to die before eating it. The reason one must wait, as Rashi explains, is not because of *eiver min ha-chai*, but because of a separate prohibition derived from *lo tochlu al ha-dam* — one may not eat while the animal is still living. But the meat is definitively not *eiver min ha-chai*, since the animal had been *shechted*, and once the animal dies it may be eaten immediately. The *baraita* then states explicitly: *echad ovdei kochavim ve-echad Yisrael mutarim bo* — both non-Jews and Jews are permitted in this.

### Lenient Application


This *baraita* clearly contradicts Rav Acha bar Yaakov, who held that non-Jews would be forbidden because such meat constitutes *eiver min ha-chai* for them. The *baraita* applies the principle that nothing can be more permissible to Jews than to non-Jews — but in a direction that is entirely surprising. When this principle is typically invoked, it leads to a stringent result: if something is forbidden to non-Jews, it should be forbidden to Jews as well. Here, the *baraita* resolves the apparent tension in the opposite direction: since the *torah*'s introduction of *shechita* renders this meat permissible for Jews even while the animal lives, that same leniency must extend to non-Jews. It would be anomalous for Jewish law to produce a category of food that is simultaneously permitted to Jews and forbidden to non-Jews, so the *baraita* declares it permitted to both. This is a genuinely unusual application of the principle — lenient rather than strict — and the *gemara* provides no explicit account of why the principle was applied in this direction rather than the other.

### Health Benefit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין, דְּאָמַר רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַשְׁיָאן: הָרוֹצֶה שֶׁיַּבְרִיא, חוֹתֵךְ כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר מִבֵּית שְׁחִיטָתָהּ שֶׁל בְּהֵמָה, וּמוֹלְחוֹ יָפֶה יָפֶה, וּמְדִיחוֹ יָפֶה יָפֶה, וּמַמְתִּין לָהּ עַד שֶׁתֵּצֵא נַפְשָׁהּ, אֶחָד גּוֹי וְאֶחָד יִשְׂרָאֵל מוּתָּרִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** This *baraita* **supports** the statement of **Rav Idi bar Avin, as Rav Idi bar Avin says** that **Rav Yitzḥak bar Ashyan says: One who seeks to recuperate** from an illness should **cut an olive-bulk of meat from the area of slaughter,** i.e., the neck, **of an animal, and salt it very well, and rinse it very well, and wait until the** animal's **soul departs,** and eat it. It is **permitted** for **both a gentile and a Jew to** eat **it.**

The *gemara* then cites a statement of Rav Idi bar Avin in the name of Rav Yitzchak bar Ashian, which attends to the practical motivation: *ha-rotzeh she-yavri* — one who wishes to become strong — should cut a *kezayis* of meat from the place of *shechita* while the animal still has its life force. Salt it, rinse it, wait for the animal to die, and then eat it. The same ruling follows: *echad ovdei kochavim ve-echad Yisrael mutar be-eilu* — once again, both Jews and non-Jews are permitted. This statement supports the *baraita* and reinforces the lenient application of the principle, while offering the additional detail that the practice was understood to carry a health benefit — the vitality of the meat being attributed to its proximity to the animal's still-living state.

## Meat/Blood/Tumah

### Mishna Meat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹחֵט בְּהֵמָה חַיָּה וָעוֹף וְלֹא יָצָא מֵהֶן דָּם – כְּשֵׁרִים, וְנֶאֱכָלִין בְּיָדַיִם מְסוֹאָבוֹת, לְפִי שֶׁלֹּא הוּכְשְׁרוּ בַּדָּם. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: הוּכְשְׁרוּ בַּשְּׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who slaughters a domesticated animal, an undomesticated animal, or a bird, and blood did not emerge from them** during the slaughter, all of these are **permitted** for consumption **and** do not require the ritual washing of the hands as **they may be eaten with ritually impure [*mesoavot*] hands, because they were not rendered susceptible** to ritual impurity through contact **with blood,** which is one of the seven liquids that render food susceptible to impurity. **Rabbi Shimon says: They were rendered susceptible** to ritual impurity **by means of** the **slaughter** itself.

The *gemara* now transitions to a new *mishnah* — and a word of warning to the reader: the remainder of this daf and the two that follow are substantially occupied with questions of *tumah ve-taharah*. The *mishnah* states: *ha-shochet behemah chayah va-of ve-lo yatza meihen dam, ksherim* — one who *shechts* an animal, domesticated or wild, or a bird, and no blood issues from them: the *shechita* is valid. This is because *shechita* does not require severing the blood vessels; one need only cut the *simanim*. *ve-ne'echalin be-yadayim meso'avot* — and such meat may be eaten with *tamei* hands. The reasoning: since no blood contacted the meat, it was never rendered susceptible to *tumah* through a liquid. Food that has not been made susceptible (*huchshar*) cannot contract *tumah*, and hands that are *tamei* therefore pose no risk.

### Liquid Required


The *mishnah* provides its own rationale: *lefi she-lo huchshru ba-dam* — because they were not made susceptible through blood.

### Shimon: Shechita


Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *huchshru bi-shchita* — the act of *shechita* itself renders the meat susceptible to *tumah*. His reasoning is that *shechita* transforms a living animal into food; the very act that confers the status of food upon the meat should simultaneously render it susceptible, without need for contact with liquid. For something like fruit, which is naturally food, susceptibility requires human intention and contact with liquid — an indication that the item has come under human control. Here, however, a deliberate act — *shechita* — has already accomplished that transformation, and no additional liquid contact should be required.

### Proof: Chulin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ טַעְמָא דְּלֹא יָצָא מֵהֶן דָּם, הָא יָצָא מֵהֶן דָּם – אֵין נֶאֱכָלִים בְּיָדַיִם מְסוֹאָבוֹת. אַמַּאי? יָדַיִם שְׁנִיּוֹת הֵן, וְאֵין שֵׁנִי עוֹשֶׂה שְׁלִישִׁי בְּחוּלִּין!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The **reason** that they may be eaten with ritually impure hands is **that blood did not emerge from** the animals or birds during the slaughter; **but** if **blood emerged from them** during slaughter, **they** may **not be eaten with ritually impure hands.** The Gemara asks: **Why** not? Ordinary **hands are** impure with **second-degree** ritual impurity **and** an item of **second-degree** impurity **cannot impart third-degree** impurity to **non-sacred** items with which it comes into contact.

The *gemara* immediately addresses a threshold question: how do we know this *mishnah* is discussing *chulin* and not sacrifices? The question is not merely theoretical; if the *mishnah* addressed sacrifices, the analysis about hands making food *tamei* would look entirely different, for several reasons. The *gemara* offers several proofs from within the *mishnah* itself.

First: *de-katani chaya* — the *mishnah* mentions a *chaya*, a wild animal. There is no *korban* that consists of a *chaya*; sacrifices come exclusively from domesticated animals. Second: if dealing with sacrifices, how could the *mishnah* posit a case where no blood issued forth? *hu atzmo le-dam hu tzarich* — a *korban* requires blood; the entire purpose of *shechita* for a sacrifice is to obtain blood for the *zerikat ha-dam*. A scenario where the *shechita* produced no blood would render the *korban* invalid on entirely different grounds. Third: if dealing with sacrifices, the blood of *kodshim* does not render food susceptible to *tumah* in the first place — as Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, derived from the verse *al ha-aretz tishpachenu ka-mayim*: blood that is poured out like water (i.e., not caught for sacrificial use) functions like water and makes food susceptible; blood that must be caught for sacrifice does not. So blood of *kodshim* would not have made the meat susceptible regardless. Fourth: if discussing *kodshim*, even without blood the meat would be susceptible through *chibbas ha-kodesh* — the sanctity inherent in items used in the *beit ha-mikdash* renders them susceptible to *tumah* without any liquid contact. The *mishnah*'s implication that absence of blood means the meat is not susceptible would be false in the world of *kodshim*.

All four proofs confirm we are discussing *chulin*. But once we establish that context, the *gemara* raises its central difficulty.

### Hands: Sheni

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּמִמַּאי דִּבְחוּלִּין עָסְקִינַן? דְּקָתָנֵי חַיָּה, דְּאִילּוּ קָדָשִׁים – חַיָּה בְּקָדָשִׁים מִי אִיכָּא? וְתוּ, אִי בְּקָדָשִׁים, כִּי לֹא יָצָא מֵהֶן דָּם – כְּשֵׁרָה? הוּא עַצְמוֹ לַדָּם הוּא צָרִיךְ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **From where** is it ascertained **that we are dealing** in the mishna **with non-sacred** food, and not with the slaughter of an offering? That is clear, **as** the *tanna* **teaches** in the list of those slaughtered: **An undomesticated animal. As, if** the *tanna* is referring to the slaughter of **sacrificial** animals and birds, **is there** any **undomesticated animal** included **in** the framework of **sacrificial** animals? **And furthermore, if** the *tanna* is referring **to sacrificial** animals, **when no blood emerges from them are** the offerings **valid?** The offering **itself requires blood,** as it is only through the presenting of the blood upon the altar that the offering is accepted.

*Yadayim shniyos hen* — hands are *sheni*, second-degree impurity. The rabbinic enactment requiring handwashing before bread was instituted because *kohanim* needed to wash before eating *terumah*; as a *gezeira*, the Rabbis declared that unwashed hands carry the status of *sheini le-tumah*. A *sheini* can render *terumah* a *shlishi* and can affect *kodshim*, but it cannot affect *chulin* — *ein sheini oseh shlishi be-chulin*. So even if the meat were susceptible to *tumah*, why should it matter? *tamei* hands, which are a *sheini*, cannot make non-sanctified food *tamei*. The *mishnah* seems to be addressing a non-problem.

### Ma'aser Sheni

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ, אִי בְּקָדָשִׁים, כִּי יָצָא מֵהֶן דָּם מִי מַכְשִׁיר? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִנַּיִן לְדַם קָדָשִׁים שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַכְשִׁיר? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עַל הָאָרֶץ תִּשְׁפְּכֶנּוּ כַּמָּיִם״, דָּם שֶׁנִּשְׁפָּךְ כַּמַּיִם מַכְשִׁיר, שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִשְׁפָּךְ כַּמַּיִם אֵינוֹ מַכְשִׁיר!
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, if** the *tanna* is referring to **sacrificial** animals, **when blood emerges from them, does it render** them **susceptible** to ritual impurity? **But doesn't Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: From where** is it derived **that the blood of sacrificial** animals **does not render** food **susceptible** to ritual impurity? It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated:** "You shall not eat it; **you shall pour it upon the earth like water"** (Deuteronomy 12:24). **Blood** of a non-sacred animal, **which is poured like water** when it is slaughtered, **renders** food **susceptible** to ritual impurity. By contrast, blood of a sacrificial animal, **which is not poured like water** but is presented on the altar, **does not render** food **susceptible** to ritual impurity.

Rav Nachman, citing Rabbah bar Avuha, offers the first resolution: *hacha be-chulin she-nilkchu be-kesef ma'aser askinan* — we are discussing *chulin* purchased with *ma'aser sheni* money, and the ruling does not follow Rabbi Meir's position. The force of this answer is that meat bought with *ma'aser sheni* money acquires the sanctity of *ma'aser sheni*, which occupies an intermediate position between ordinary *chulin* and *terumah*. The question is whether *ma'aser sheni* can become *tamei* at the level of *shlishi*. The *gemara* cites the debate: *kol ha-ta'on bi'as mayim mi-divrei soferim, metamei et ha-kodesh u-fosel et ha-terumah u-mutar be-chulin u-ve-ma'aser* — this is Rabbi Meir's position, that anything requiring immersion by rabbinic law is a *sheini*, which renders *kodshim tamei* and invalidates *terumah*, but is permitted with *chulin* and also with *ma'aser*. The Sages, however, say *osrin be-ma'aser* — they forbid *ma'aser sheni* to be treated like *chulin* in this regard, treating it as susceptible to *shlishi* status like *terumah*. So if the meat has the sanctity of *ma'aser sheni*, and if we follow the Sages against Rabbi Meir, *tamei* hands (as *sheini*) can make the meat a *shlishi* — and it becomes halachically significant whether the meat was made susceptible through liquid contact.

### Shimi: Eating Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַטָּעוּן בִּיאַת מַיִם מִדִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים מְטַמֵּא אֶת הַקֹּדֶשׁ, וּפוֹסֵל אֶת הַתְּרוּמָה, וּמוּתָּר בַּחוּלִּין וּבַמַּעֲשֵׂר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹסְרִים בַּמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **anything that by rabbinic law requires entry** into **water,** i.e., either immersion or ritual washing of the hands, although it is pure by Torah law, it is accorded second-degree impurity. Therefore, such an item **renders sacrificial** food **impure,** meaning that the sacrificial food becomes impure and transmits impurity to other sacrificial food, **and disqualifies *teruma*,** meaning that it renders the *teruma* itself impure, but not to the extent that the *teruma* can render other *teruma* impure. **And** it **is permitted for non-sacred** food **and for** second-**tithe** produce to come in contact with such an item, and no impurity is thereby transmitted. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis prohibit** items that require entry into water from coming in contact **with** second-**tithe** produce, as they hold that the produce is thereby rendered impure. According to the Rabbis, the status of second-tithe produce is more stringent than that of non-sacred food, and second-tithe produce assumes third-degree impurity upon contact with an item of second-degree impurity, which is in accordance with the opinion of the mishna.

Rav Shimi bar Ashi challenged this resolution. His objection: the debate between Rabbi Meir and the Sages concerns *ma'aser sheni* only with respect to *eating* it while *tamei*, not with respect to *touching* it while *tamei*. The Rabbis applied their stringency about *ma'aser sheni* to the act of eating, not to physical contact. And what the *mishnah* appears to be addressing — the concern that *tamei* hands touching the meat would render it problematic — is a matter of touch, not eating. The *gemara* supports this reading with a subtle inference from the *mishnah*'s language: *ne'echalin be-yadayim meso'avot* uses the passive voice, suggesting that someone with *tamei* hands is feeding the food to another person. This implies the concern is about the *tamei* hand touching the meat and transmitting *tumah* to it — a problem of *negia* (touching), not *achila* (eating). But the debate between the Sages and Rabbi Meir, even granting the Sages' stringency, applies only to eating *ma'aser sheni* while *tamei* — not to touching it. The *mishnah* therefore cannot be resolved by appealing to *ma'aser sheni*.

### Hands Rishon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי: מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, אֶלָּא בַּאֲכִילַת מַעֲשֵׂר, אֲבָל בִּנְגִיעָה דְּמַעֲשֵׂר וַאֲכִילָה דְחוּלִּין – לָא פְּלִיגִי. וְהָא נְגִיעָה הִיא, מִדְּקָתָנֵי נֶאֱכָלִין בְּיָדַיִם מְסוֹאָבוֹת, מִי לָא עָסְקִינַן דְּקָא סָפֵי לֵיהּ חַבְרֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shimi bar Ashi objects to** this interpretation of that mishna. **From where** is it clear that it is contact that the Rabbis prohibit? **Perhaps the Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Meir only with regard to** a person with second-degree impurity **partaking of** second-**tithe** produce. **But with regard to contact** of an individual with second-degree impurity with second-**tithe** produce **or** his **eating non-sacred** food, **they do not disagree. And this** case in the mishna **is** a case involving **contact** with the flesh of the slaughtered animal, **from** the fact **that it teaches: They may be eaten with ritually impure hands,** in the passive form, and not: One may eat them with impure hands. **Are we not dealing** with a case where **another** with impure hands **fed him,** but the one eating it was ritually pure and did not touch it? Therefore, the case in the mishna here, which indicates that it is forbidden for one with impure hands to touch the flesh if it has come in contact with the blood, cannot be referring to an animal purchased in Jerusalem with second-tithe money. The reason is that in that case, the Rabbis concede that contact with second-tithe produce, even produce rendered susceptible to ritual impurity by the blood, is permitted for one whose hands are impure with second-degree ritual impurity.

Rav Pappa offers a second answer: *hacha be-yadayim techilot askinan* — we are discussing hands that have become *techilot*, i.e., *rishon le-tumah*. Under normal circumstances, unwashed hands are treated as *sheini*, which is why they cannot affect *chulin*. But if the hands have come into contact with a primary source of *tumah* under rabbinic law — specifically in the case that follows — they can be elevated to *rishon* status. A *rishon* can make *chulin* into a *sheini*, at which point the food is itself *tamei* and cannot be passed to others without concern. In such a case, whether the meat was made susceptible by liquid contact becomes highly relevant: if susceptible, the *tamei* hands (as *rishon*) would render the meat a *sheini*; if not susceptible, the hands have no effect.

## Hands Tzara'at

### Shimon Elazar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הָכָא בְּיָדַיִם תְּחִלּוֹת עָסְקִינַן, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: אֵין יָדַיִם תְּחִלּוֹת לַחוּלִּין, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: יָדַיִם תְּחִלּוֹת לַחוּלִּין, וּשְׁנִיּוֹת לַתְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather Rav Pappa said: Here,** in the mishna, **we are dealing with hands** that are impure with **first-degree** ritual impurity, which render even non-sacred food impure. **And** the mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: In those cases where **hands** have **first-degree** impurity, it is **not** that they render **non-sacred** food impure; rather, it means that they impart to *teruma* and sacrificial food second-degree impurity rather than third-degree impurity. **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says in the name of Rabbi Meir:** In those cases where **hands** have **first-degree** impurity, it means that they render **non-sacred** food impure. **And** in those cases where hands have **second-degree** impurity it means that they invalidate ***teruma*** and impart to it third-degree impurity.

The *gemara* identifies the ruling of *yadayim techilot* with the position of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar: *ein yadayim techilot le-chulin; Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar omer mishum Rabbi Meir: yadayim techilot le-chulin ve-sheniyot le-terumah* — the standard position is that hands cannot become a *rishon* vis-à-vis *chulin*. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, in the name of Rabbi Meir, maintains that there are scenarios in which hands become a *rishon*, and in those scenarios they have an impact even on *chulin*. The *mishnah*, according to this answer, reflects Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's position.

### Akiva vs Sages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִיכָּא יָדַיִם תְּחִלּוֹת? אִין, דְּתַנְיָא: הִכְנִיס יָדָיו לְבַיִת הַמְנוּגָּע – יָדָיו תְּחִלּוֹת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יָדָיו שְׁנִיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And are there** cases where **hands** assume **first-degree** impurity? The Gemara answers: **Yes, as it is taught** in a mishna (*Yadayim* 3:1): If **one inserts his hands into a leprous house** (see Leviticus 14:33–53) **his hands** assume **first-degree** impurity as though his entire body entered the house; this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. And the Rabbis say: His hands** assume **second-degree** impurity.

The specific case in which hands can become a *rishon* is established from a *mishnah*: *hichnis yado le-veit ha-menuga, yadav techilot* — if one inserted one's hands into a house afflicted with *tzara'at*, one's hands are *rishon le-tumah*. This is the ruling of Rabbi Akiva. The Sages disagree and hold that the hands are only *sheini*.

### Hands Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא בִּיאָה בְּמִקְצָת לֹא שְׁמָהּ בִּיאָה, וְהָכָא בִּגְזֵירָה יָדָיו אַטּוּ גּוּפוֹ קָא מִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **Everyone** agrees **that** in principle, **partial entry** into a leprous house **is not characterized** as **entry** in terms of rendering one who enters impure. Therefore, one who inserted his hands into the house is not impure by Torah law. **And here,** it is **with regard to** a rabbinic **decree** that renders his hands impure that **they disagree:** The Sages issued a decree that if one inserted his hands into a leprous house, **his hands** are impure **due to** the impurity by Torah law that one assumes when he enters the house with his entire **body.** The objective of the decree is to prevent him from entering the leprous house.

The *gemara* explains the basis of this dispute. All agree that *biah be-miktzat lo shemah biah* — entering with only part of one's body does not constitute the full act of entering the house. If it did, the person's entire body would be a *rishon*. So the *tumah* of the hands in this case is unambiguously rabbinic. The dispute concerns which rabbinic model applies. Rabbi Akiva holds *yadayim ke-gufo shavinhu Rabbanan* — the Rabbis treated the hands as equivalent to the body; since a body entering the house of *tzara'at* would be a *rishon*, the hands, standing in for the body, are likewise made a *rishon*. The Sages hold *yadayim ke-yadayim de-alma shavinhu Rabbanan* — the Rabbis modeled the hands' *tumah* on the standard rabbinic category of hand *tumah*, which is always *sheini*, not *rishon*. The entire rabbinic institution of hand *tumah* was built around the model of *sheini*, and there is no precedent for rabbinic hand *tumah* at the level of *rishon*.

### Relying Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר סָבַר: יָדָיו – כְּגוּפוֹ שַׁוִּינְהוּ רַבָּנַן, וּמָר סָבַר: יָדַיִם – כְּיָדַיִם דְּעָלְמָא שַׁוִּינְהוּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** One **Sage,** Rabbi Akiva, **holds** that when **the Sages** issued the decree they determined that the status of **his hands** inserted into the house is **like** the status of **his body** that enters the house, first-degree impurity. **And** one **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds** that **the Sages rendered** the status of **hands** inserted into the house **like** the status of **hands in general,** with regard to which they issued a decree of second-degree impurity, even if they had been washed.

The *gemara* therefore establishes that the *mishnah* relies on Rabbi Akiva's position: in a case where one's hands were inserted into a *beit ha-menuga*, the hands are *rishon*, they can render *chulin* meat *tamei* upon contact, and whether the meat was susceptible to *tumah* therefore becomes legally significant.

### Akiva Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלוֹקְמַהּ כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: יָדָיו תְּחִלּוֹת הָוְיָין? דִּלְמָא כִּי קָאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי בִּתְרוּמָה וְקָדָשִׁים, דַּחֲמִירִי, אֲבָל לְחוּלִּין – שְׁנִיּוֹת הָוְיָין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** why did Rav Pappa interpret the mishna in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar? **Let** Rav Pappa **interpret** the mishna **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who says:** If one inserts his hands into a leprous house, **his hands** assume **first-degree** impurity. The Gemara answers: **Perhaps when Rabbi Akiva says** that one's hands assume first-degree impurity, **that statement** applies only **in** cases of ***teruma* and sacrificial** food, **which are stringent. But** with regard **to non-sacred** food, perhaps hands **are** impure only with **second-degree** impurity.

The *gemara* raises a possible limitation: perhaps Rabbi Akiva's ruling that the hands become *rishon* was intended to apply only in the domain of *terumah* and *kodshim* — where the stakes are higher and greater stringency is appropriate — but not for *chulin*, where his ruling would have no practical application and he might have conceded that even if *rishon*, the hands carry no consequence.

### Elazar: Chulin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיהֶוְיָין נָמֵי שְׁנִיּוֹת, דְּהָא שָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּאָמַר: שֵׁנִי עוֹשֶׂה שְׁלִישִׁי בְּחוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **And let** hands **also be** impure only with **second-degree** impurity and the mishna could still be in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva. **As we heard that Rabbi Akiva says:** An item of **second-degree** impurity **imparts third-degree** impurity to **non-sacred** items.

This is precisely why the *gemara* identifies the *mishnah* with Rabbi Elazar son of Rabbi Shimon rather than with Rabbi Akiva alone. Rabbi Elazar son of Rabbi Shimon explicitly ruled that in scenarios where hands become *rishon*, that status has consequences even for *chulin*. This removes any doubt: the *mishnah* reflects a position — that of Rabbi Elazar son of Rabbi Shimon — which holds that *rishon* hands can make *chulin* meat *tamei*, and it is therefore entirely meaningful that the *mishnah* instructs one not to be concerned about eating such meat with *tamei* hands when no blood has been shed, because without susceptibility, even *rishon* hands have no effect.
