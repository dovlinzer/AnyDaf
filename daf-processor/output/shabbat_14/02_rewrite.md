# Shabbat 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chananya's Attic & 18 Th…

### New Mishna Context

The daf opens with a new *mishna*, which begins: *v'elu min hahalachot she'amru ba'aliyat Chananya ben Chizkiyah ben Garon she'alu l'vakro* — these are from the *halachot* that were stated in the upper story of Chananya ben Chizkiyah ben Garon, when they came to visit him. The *mishna* records that *nimnu v'rabu Beit Shammai al Beit Hillel* — Beit Shammai outnumbered Beit Hillel, and finding themselves with a majority, they seized the opportunity to pass legislation that had long been stalled. The *mishna* signals that everything we have just been learning — the tailor's pin, the scribe's feather, a *zav* and *zavah* not eating together, not sitting down to the barber before *mincha* time — all of these belonged to the *yud-chet davar*, the eighteen things decided in Chananya ben Garon's attic.

### Abaye's Phrasing Question

The *gemara* opens with a linguistic question. *Amar lei Abaye l'Rav Yosef: elu tnan o v'elu tnan?* — Did the *mishna* say *elu* (these following things) or *v'elu* (and these, connecting to what came before)? The difference is significant: *v'elu* would mean that the subjects of the preceding *mishnayot* are themselves among the *halachot* of the eighteen things, while *elu* would introduce a fresh, self-contained list. The *gemara* resolves this from a *braita*: *ein polin l'or haner v'ein korin l'or haner v'elu min hahalachot she'amru ba'aliyat Chananya ben Chizkiyah ben Garon* — the *braita* ends there, with no continuation that could cause ambiguity about direction. *Shma mina v'elu tnan* — we conclude the reading is *v'elu*, connecting backward to earlier material.

It is worth noting that the prohibition against reading by candlelight was clearly an ancient *takanah*. Rabbi Yishmael, who was himself relatively early, recounted that he once nearly tipped a candle while reading *l'or haner* and acknowledged that the sages had been right to forbid it. So certainly the carrying of a quill or pin, reading *l'or haner*, and a *zav* and *zavah* eating together were all from the eighteen things. Whether the prohibition against sitting down before *mincha* reaches back that far is less clear.

### Braita Proof

The *gemara*'s conclusion is that the *mishna* uses *v'elu* — linking the preceding *mishnayot* to the eighteen things. It is not assumed, however, that this encompasses everything in all three prior *mishnayot*. The phrase *min hahalachot* — "from" the *halachot* — indicates a selection, not an exhaustive enumeration. The eighteen things are not fully laid out in a single place; the *gemara* will eventually complete the list.

### Rambam's Listing

The *rambam* takes the more expansive view, attempting to extend the list all the way back to the very first *mishna* — the *ani* and the *baal habayit*, each with two actions that become four inside and four outside, adding up to eight. He then counts approximately five items from the prohibitions before *mincha* time (barber, bathhouse, and related matters), and another five from the things one may not carry on the eve of *Shabbat* and the prohibition of reading *l'or haner*, arriving at eighteen. This comprehensive reading is generally not the accepted position; the standard understanding is that the *mishna* beginning with carrying, and perhaps the *mishna* about sitting before *mincha*, are among the eighteen, but the list in the *mishna* is a partial one.

### Megillat Taanit

The *gemara* pauses for a significant historical digression: *mi katav Megillat Taanit?* — who wrote *Megillat Taanit*? This is an ancient text, unusual in that it was committed to writing even in a predominantly oral culture — perhaps because it consists largely of dates, which are difficult to memorize. It records the days on which fasting and delivering a *hesped* are forbidden, marking minor historical celebrations that were significant at the time even if we no longer observe them. Notably, *Chanukah* in its earliest Rabbinic formulation appears in *Megillat Taanit*, which describes what happened on those days and why one may not fast.

The *gemara* answers: *Chananya ben Chizkiya v'si'ato* — Chananya ben Chizkiya and his colleagues — *she'hayu mechabevim et ha'tsarot* — who held the afflictions dear. On its surface, this suggests a religious orientation centered on fasting and suffering, people who were always looking for an opportunity to fast and therefore needed a calendar of days on which fasting was forbidden. Rashi, however, reads *mechabevim et ha'tsarot* differently: they were *mechabev* the days on which their *tsarah* was imminent and God had saved them. On Rashi's reading, *Megillat Taanit* is a record of those minor miracles and celebratory moments, and its purpose is to mark the days when something good happened — hence the prohibition on fasting.

The tension in these two readings is real. Rashi must somewhat strain against the plain language, which speaks of the *tsar* itself rather than the salvation from it. *Rabban Gamliel* adds: *af anu mechabevim et ha'tsarot* — we too hold the afflictions dear. *Aval ma na'aseh she'im banu lichtov ein anu maspikin* — but if we were to write them all down, we could not manage it; there are simply too many. On the plain reading, this means that suffering under Roman oppression was so constant and pervasive that one could not document all of it. On Rashi's reading, we are saved so frequently — every single day from some threat — that we could not list all the days of divine intervention: *ein anu maspikin la'asot yom tov be'chol yom*.

The *gemara* then adds two further expressions: *davar acher ein shoteh nifga* — a *shoteh* does not feel when he is being struck; *ve'davar acher ein basar hamet margish be'izmel* — dead flesh does not feel the piercing of an awl. Rashi explains these as metaphors for unawareness: *kach kama nissim ba'im lanu v'ein anu makirim ba'hem* — so too, many miracles come to us and we do not even recognize them. But if one reads the passage according to its plain sense — that it is the affliction itself being counted — then the metaphors work equally well in the other direction: after constant suffering, one becomes inured to it, like dead flesh that no longer registers the blade.

At this point the *gemara* raises a factual challenge: is it really true that dead flesh feels nothing? *Ha'amar Rav Yitzchak: kasheh rimah lamet kemachat bivsar hachai* — Rav Yitzchak taught that a maggot's consumption of a corpse is as painful to the deceased's spirit as a needle piercing living flesh, as it is written: *ach besaro alav yichav v'nafsho alav te'eval* — his flesh will ache and his soul will mourn over him. The *gemara* resolves this: *eima ein basar hamet shebachayim margish be'izmel* — what the saying means is that a portion of dead flesh within a living person does not feel the awl. That is the nature of a callus: it is dead skin, with no nerve endings, and piercing it causes no pain.

### Yechezkel Reconciliation

Having discussed who wrote *Megillat Taanit*, the *gemara* records a second major achievement of Chananya ben Chizkiyah. *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav: beram zachur oto ish letov, v'Chananya ben Chizkiyah shemo, she'ilmala hu nignaz Sefer Yechezkel* — that person is remembered for good, and his name is Chananya ben Chizkiyah, for were it not for him, the Book of Yechezkel would have had to be hidden away — that is, excluded from the canon, treated essentially as a *sefer chitzoni*. Why? *She'hayu devarav sotrim divrei Torah* — because at the *pshat* level, portions of Yechezkel appear to contradict the Torah. Rashi cites one example: Yechezkel implies that *kohanim* in particular should not eat *neveilah*, which sounds as though non-*kohanim* may — a formulation foreign to the Torah. Beyond that, Yechezkel's description of the *Beit HaMikdash* differs markedly from the Torah's, and various *halachot* embedded in its *psukim* seem to conflict with established law.

*Mah asah?* — what did Chananya do? *He'elu lo gimmel meot garvei shemen* — they brought up 300 jugs of oil, *v'yashav ba'aliyah v'darshan* — and he and presumably a group of *rabbanim* sat in the upper room and worked through every apparent contradiction, finding ways to reconcile Yechezkel with the Torah. Rashi notes that the oil was needed to keep them working late into the night; they essentially locked themselves in for an extended period until all the problems were resolved.

This gives us a portrait of Chananya ben Garon as a figure deeply engaged with the scriptural and textual foundations of Jewish life: he reconciled Yechezkel with the Torah, he authored *Megillat Taanit* together with his colleagues, and his attic served as a kind of gathering place for significant *rabbinic* deliberation. The *rambam* notes, not unreasonably, that it was likely during one of these very meetings — perhaps the one concerning Yechezkel, or the one about *Megillat Taanit* — that the event of our *mishna* took place: finding themselves assembled and with Beit Shammai holding a majority, they took the opportunity to also resolve the eighteen outstanding matters.

## Tumah Classifications

### Nine Categories Listed

The *mishna* we now turn to — drawn from *Masechet Zavim* — lists nine categories of things that render a person or object a *sheni l'tumah*, meaning second-degree *tumah*, with the practical consequence of disqualifying *trumah* (*poslot et ha-trumah*): a *sheni l'tumah* that contacts *trumah* renders it a *shlishi*, which is *pasul* and may not be consumed. Most of these nine categories are among the eighteen things decreed in Chananya's attic, as the *gemara* will clarify.

The first two items are: *ha-ochel ochel rishon* — one who eats food that is a *rishon l'tumah* (food that directly touched an *av ha-tumah* such as a *sheretz*) — and *ha-ochel ochel sheni* — one who eats food that is a *sheni l'tumah* (food that touched a vessel that touched an *av ha-tumah*). In both cases the person becomes a *sheni*. This is notable: *de'oraita*, a person can only become a *rishon* by touching an *av ha-tumah* directly; the rabbis extended *tumah* status to a person who merely eats food of a lower degree. The third item is *ha-shoteh mashkin t'meim* — one who drinks *tamei* liquid, either *rishon* or *sheni* (liquid essentially auto-upgrades, as we will see). In all three cases, the person becomes a *sheni*.

The fourth category is *ha-ba rosho v'rubo b'mayim she'uvim* — one who immerses his head and most of his body in drawn water, i.e., water that was collected in a vessel rather than gathered naturally. The fifth is *v'tahor she'naflu al rosho v'rubo shlosha lugin mayim she'uvim* — a *tahor* person upon whom three *lugin* of drawn water fell, i.e., who took a shower. In both cases the person becomes a *sheni*.

The sixth and seventh items are *ha-sefer* and *ha-yadayim*: *sifrei kodesh* — holy scriptures, at minimum a *sefer Torah* — are themselves a *sheni* and convey *tumah* to *trumah*; and *yadayim* — hands — are always treated as a *sheni*, regardless of the rest of the body's status. The eighth item is *ha-tevul yom* — one who has immersed in a *mikvah* that day but before sunset — who is a *sheni* and disqualifies *trumah*. However, the *gemara* will note that *tevul yom* is *de'oraita* (*u'va ha-shemesh v'taher*) and should be removed from the count of the eighteen rabbinic decrees, even though it remains in the *mishna*'s enumeration. The ninth is *ha-ochlin v'ha-keilim she'nitme'u b'mashkin* — foods and vessels that became *tamei* through liquid contact, which we will unpack further below.

The *gemara* notes that a great deal of the eighteen things concerned *tumah v'taharah*, reflecting how central these concerns were to religious life even after the *churban*.

### R. Eliezer vs. R. Yehosh…

The *gemara* asks: *man tana ochel ochel rishon v'ochel ochel sheni mifsil pasli t'muyei lo mtamyei?* — who is the *tanna* who holds that eating a *rishon* or *sheni* renders you *pasul* but not *tamei* in the full sense — meaning you become a *sheni*, not a *rishon*?

*Amar Rabbah Bar Bar Channah amar Rabbi Yehoshua hi* — it is the position of Rabbi Yehoshua. The *gemara* cites a *mishna* from which this is drawn: *Rabbi Eliezer omer — ha-ochel ochel rishon rishon, ve-ochel ochel sheni sheni* — if you eat a *rishon*, you become a *rishon*; if you eat a *sheni*, you become a *sheni*. According to Rabbi Eliezer, you become what you ate. He adds: *ochel shlishi shlishi* — and if you ate food that is a *shlishi* (which can only be *trumah*, since *chullin* does not reach that degree), you become a *shlishi*, relevant only for *kodesh*.

Rabbi Yehoshua, by contrast, holds: *ha-ochel ochel rishon ve-ochel ochel sheni sheni* — whether you ate a *rishon* or a *sheni*, you become a *sheni*. The act of eating *tamei* food produces a single result: *sheni* status. As for eating a *shlishi*: *sheni le-kodesh ve-ein sheni le-trumah, be-chullin she-na'asu al taharat trumah* — you become a *sheni* with respect to *kodesh* but not with respect to *trumah*, and even this applies only when the *shlishi* in question came from *chullin* that was treated with the stringency of *trumah* (*chullin she-na'asu al taharat trumah*).

This last point requires elaboration. There is a practice of eating one's *chullin* in a state of *taharah*. But there is a further level: treating one's *chullin* as if it were *trumah*, being so scrupulous about it that one avoids contact with a *sheni*. The question is whether such *chullin*, once treated with *trumah*-level care, can also become *tamei* like *trumah* — i.e., can it become a *shlishi*? Rabbi Yehoshua concedes that if one has adopted this practice and the food became a *shlishi*, eating it produces *kodesh*-level *tumah*. The reason he concedes specifically in this case, and not for actual *trumah* that became a *shlishi*, is that since the food is ultimately only *chullin*, we are not confident the person guarded it as carefully as he claims, and we suspect its *tumah* may be higher than a *shlishi*. Once we are concerned it may be worse than declared, we treat it more stringently.

The operative holding for our *mishna* — that eating a *rishon* or *sheni* makes you a *sheni* regardless of the degree consumed — reflects the position of Rabbi Yehoshua.

### Eating Foods Reasoning

Having established whose view it is, the *gemara* turns to the rationale. *Ochel ochel rishon ve-ochel ochel sheni: mai ta'ama gazru beh rabbanan?* — why did the rabbis decree that eating a *rishon* or *sheni* renders you *tamei*?

The concern is practical and vivid. A *kohen* may be eating food that is a *rishon* or *sheni* — permissible food, since it is not *trumah* — and at the same table there is a cup of *trumah* wine. Having eaten the *tamei* food, he might absentmindedly wash it down with the *trumah* wine. While the *tamei* food is in his mouth, it is still a *rishon* or *sheni*, and the *trumah* wine entering his mouth at the same moment becomes *tamei*. This is the scenario the decree is designed to prevent. By telling the *kohen* that eating *tamei* food makes him a *sheni*, the rabbis ensure he will not sit down to such a meal while *trumah* is also on the table.

It is worth noting that there is an earlier and distinct *gezeirah* discussed by *Tosafot* — called *pesul geviyah*, the *pesul* of the body — which addresses what happens when a large quantity of *tamei* food has been swallowed and now sits in the stomach alongside ingested *trumah*. That decree operates with a larger *shi'ur* and addresses a somewhat different scenario. The decree we are discussing here is later, more encompassing, and more stringent: even a small amount of *tamei* food eaten renders one a *sheni* and unable to touch *trumah*, owing to the concern about washing it down.

There is also the question of what minimum *shi'ur* of food is required to trigger this status. *Tosafot* assumes the threshold is a *kebeitzah*.

### Drinking Liquids Ruling

The parallel case: *shoteh mashkin t'meim: mai ta'ama gazru behu rabbanan?* — why did the rabbis decree that drinking *tamei* liquid makes you a *sheni*? The scenario is the mirror image: you are drinking *tamei* wine, and your bread happens to be *trumah*. Without the decree, you would reason that you are *tahor*, the bread is *trumah* and *tahor*, all is well — and then you would take a bite of the *trumah* bread while the *tamei* wine is still in your mouth, rendering the *trumah tamei*. By making the consumption of *tamei* liquid produce *sheni* status, the rabbis ensure you will not combine them at the same meal.

The *gemara* then asks: *hainu hach* — is this not the same as the previous decree? Why enumerate them separately? The answer is: *sh'chichi v'ha lo sh'chichi* — the two scenarios are not equally common. It is far more likely that one would eat food and wash it down with a drink than that one would have liquid in the mouth while inserting food. The latter is physically awkward — the liquid tends to go down before one can bring the food in. So the decree about food causing *sheni* status is grounded in a common occurrence; the decree about liquid is extended to the less common case for the sake of symmetry and completeness, even though it would rarely arise in practice.

## Drawn Water Immersion

### Cave Water Origin

The next *gezeirah* concerns drawn water. *Haba rosho v'rubo b'mayim she'uvim: mai ta'ama gazru behu rabbanan?* — why did the rabbis decree that immersing oneself in drawn water renders one *tamei*?

*Amar Rav Bibi amar Rav Assi: she-vat'chilah hayu tovlin b'mei me'arah mechunasin* — originally, people who needed to immerse — presumably *kohanim* eating *trumah*, and possibly those obligated to immerse after a *keri* before saying *shema* or davening — would do so in cave water, naturally collected rainwater. Such water, though *halachically* valid, was typically stagnant and malodorous. *Ve-hayu notnin aleihem mayim she'uvim* — and so, naturally, they would rinse themselves with drawn water afterwards, to clean off the unpleasant residue of the *mikvah*.

*Hitchilu ve-asum keva* — but this practice became fixed and routine. People began treating the rinse as an integral part of the purification process. *Gazru aleihem tumah* — the rabbis therefore declared that this drawn-water rinse would render them *tamei*.

### Preventing Misunderstand…

Why exactly did the fixity of the practice necessitate a decree? The *gemara* records a dispute between Abaye and Rava on this point. *Amar Abaye: she-hayu omrim lo elu metaharim ela elu ve-elu metaharim* — Abaye says the problem was that people began saying: it is not the *mikvah* alone that purifies; it is the *mikvah* together with the rinse. This is a theological misunderstanding of what constitutes purification, and the rabbis could not allow it to stand.

Rava challenges this: *mai nafka minei? Ha katavlu behanach* — so what? At least they are going to the *mikvah*. If they think both are necessary, fine — they are still immersing in the *mikvah*. Rava therefore offers a sharper formulation: *she-hayu omrim lo elu metaharim ela elu metaharim* — they were saying not that both purify but that it is specifically the drawn-water rinse that purifies, not the *mikvah* at all. Since clean water produces a more obviously pleasant result, people began attributing the *taharah* to the rinse rather than the *mikvah*. At that point, the concern was that they might stop going to the *mikvah* altogether. Hence the decree: the drawn-water rinse makes you *tamei*, ensuring no one will view it as the operative act of purification.

The scope of this decree in practice raises interesting questions. Rashi reads the decree as applying specifically in the immediate aftermath of the *mikvah* immersion. The practical implication for women immersing in the *mikvah* has been discussed among the *poskim*: some have held that women should wait until they return home to shower, precisely because of this concern. The *Gra*, however, dismissed the stringency, arguing that the only consequence of taking a shower immediately after the *mikvah* is that one becomes a *sheni l'tumah* — and since we are all *tamei meis* in any case, such a concern is irrelevant. There is no prohibition involved; it is simply that drawn water produces a low-level *tumah* that is only consequential in a world where *tumah* is operationally significant.

### General Shower Gezeirah

There is, however, a second and broader *gezeirah* as well. The *mishna*'s next item is *v'tahor she'naflu al rosho v'rubo shlosha lugin mayim she'uvim* — a *tahor* person upon whom three *lugin* of drawn water fell, i.e., who took a shower entirely apart from any connection to a *mikvah* immersion. This person also becomes a *sheni*.

The *gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama gazru behu rabbanan tumah? De-lo ha lo kayma ha* — because if we only declared *tumah* for the drawn-water rinse immediately after the *mikvah*, no one would take the first decree seriously. To make the original decree credible and effective, it had to be generalized: drawn water creates *tumah* whenever it covers the majority of a person's body, regardless of whether a *mikvah* was involved. One can appreciate the practical weight of this: in a world with Roman bathhouse culture and widespread bathing, such a decree had real consequences for those who were *makpid* on eating *chullin be-taharah*. Though one imagines the answer lay in bathing one limb at a time to avoid covering the majority of the body, the broader implications of maintaining bodily purity under this regime are considerable.

## Holy Texts Tumah

### Sifrei Kodesh Tumah Reas…

The *mishna* then lists *ha-sefer* — holy scriptures — as conveying *tumah*. This is, at first glance, a striking proposition: the holiest objects in Jewish religious life are declared *tamei*. One might observe that although *tumah* and *kedushah* are in a sense antithetical, both represent a kind of metaphysical status that surrounds an object and generates real-world obligations. In a world where *tumah* was a lived reality, having *sifrei kodesh* generate *tumah* upon contact gave their holiness a tangible, halachic dimension — touching a *sefer Torah* changed one's metaphysical state and prohibited the eating of *trumah*.

The reason the *gemara* gives is concrete and historical: *she-vat'chilah hayu matzni'in et ha-ochlin de-trumah etzel Sefer Torah* — originally, people would store their *trumah* food together with the *sefer Torah*, reasoning that both are holy and belong together. *Ve-amru: hai kodesh ve-hai kodesh* — both are *kodesh*, why not keep them in the same place? The problem was *pesida*: the rats would come for the *trumah* and end up gnawing at the *sifrei Torah*. To remedy this, the rabbis declared *sifrei kodesh* to be *tamei* — meaning that *trumah* stored near them would be rendered *pasul* — thereby eliminating any incentive to store *trumah* alongside holy books.

### Hands Always Sheni

The final item in this cluster of the *mishna*'s nine categories: *ha-yadayim* — hands are always treated as a *sheni l'tumah*. The reason: *mipnei she-hayadayim askaniyot hen* — hands are constantly busy. One never knows where one's hands have been; they scratch, touch, fidget, and handle objects continuously and often unconsciously. Therefore, regardless of the rest of the body's *taharah* status, hands are presumed to be a *sheni* at all times. This is also, it is worth emphasizing, a radical rabbinic innovation in its own right: *de'oraita*, the *tumah* system treats a person as a unit — either entirely *tamei* or entirely *tahor*. The notion that a specific part of the body (the hands) can carry *tumah* independently of the rest is a purely rabbinic construction.

This has practical implications for daily life. Since bread evokes the context of *kohanim* eating *trumah*, and since hands are always a *sheni*, the rabbinic institution of washing hands before eating bread derives from this framework: *kohanim* would always need to wash before eating *trumah*, and the practice was extended as a reminder of that context.

### Direct Handling Conseque…

The *mishna*'s listing of *ha-sefer* as conveying *tumah* refers initially to the *sefer Torah* itself being a *sheni* that disqualifies *trumah* upon direct contact. But the *gemara* adds a related ruling: *af yadayim haba'ot machmat sefer poslot et ha-trumah* — hands that have touched a *sefer* also become a *sheni* and disqualify *trumah*. One might ask: since hands are always a *sheni* anyway, why does this need to be stated? The answer is that if a person had immersed in the *mikvah*, was extremely careful with his hands, kept his eye on them and they touched nothing — those hands would be *tahor*. The decree states that even then, touching a *sefer Torah* renders the hands a *sheni*.

The *gemara* explains this through a teaching attributed to Rav Parnach in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *ha-ocheiz sefer Torah arom nikvar arom* — one who holds a *sefer Torah* naked (*arom*) will be buried naked. The *gemara* immediately asks: *arom salka daitach?* — literally buried without shrouds? *Ela amar Rabbi Zeira: arom belo mitzvah* — rather, it means he will be buried bereft of that *mitzvah*: the *mitzvah* he performed while handling the *sefer Torah* directly — whether it was *hagbah*, *gelilah*, an *aliyah*, or study — will not be credited to him. *Belo otah mitzvah*, the *gemara* clarifies: not all his *mitzvot*, but specifically the reward for that act.

The underlying principle is the reverence owed to a *sefer Torah*: it must always be handled through an intermediary. The *yad*, the *tallit* used during *gelilah*, the mantle — all of these exist to prevent direct contact between the hand and the *klaf*. The way the rabbis encoded this reverence in the system of *tumah v'taharah* was by making direct contact with the *klaf* cause the hands to become a *sheni*, giving the obligation practical, halachic teeth. The consequence of violating this — loss of the *mitzvah's* reward — is stated in measured terms; even the *gemara* did not treat it as a catastrophic forfeiture of all *mitzvot*.

An interesting question arises regarding the scope of this decree: does *ha-sefer* refer only to a *sefer Torah*, or to all *sifrei kodesh*? *Tosafot* raises this explicitly, pointing out that in *Masechet Yadayim*, the canonical status of a book is expressed through the idiom of *metamei et ha-yadayim* — whether it renders hands *tamei*. This was precisely the framework for the debate over whether *Shir HaShirim* belongs in the canon, a question Rabbi Akiva answered with characteristic force: the holiness of *Shir HaShirim* is beyond question, *kodesh kodashim*. The same framework governed the canonization of *Yechezkel*. This suggests the *tumah* of *sifrei kodesh* applies broadly to all canonical books, though the *gemara* centers its discussion on the *sefer Torah*.

### Gezeirah Priority Debate

The *gemara* then asks a precise sequencing question: which *gezeirah* came first — the decree that hands touching a *sefer* become *tamei*, or the general decree that all hands are always a *sheni*?

*Eileima hach gazru bereisha* — if the general decree (all hands are always *tamei*) came first, then *hach lamah li?* — why would you need to separately decree that hands touching a *sefer* are *tamei*? That is already encompassed. *Ela hach gazru bereisha* — rather, the first decree was specifically about hands that touch a *sefer*. *Ve-hadar gazru bechulhu yadayim* — and afterward the rabbis extended it: hands at all times are a *sheni*. Once the extension was made, the original targeted decree became somewhat redundant, though there remains a *nafka minah*: if someone immerses, watches his hands carefully and they touch nothing, then immediately touches a *sefer Torah*, those hands are a *sheni* — a status they would not otherwise have had. But for practical purposes, once hands are always treated as a *sheni*, the prior decree is largely absorbed.

## Liquid Contamination

### Food Liquid Contamination

The ninth category in the *mishna*: *ha-ochlin v'ha-keilim she-nitme'u b'mashkin* — foods and vessels that became *tamei* through liquid. The *gemara* asks: *be-mashkin de-mai* — through what kind of liquid?

If it means liquid that touched a *sheretz* — an *av ha-tumah* — then the liquid is a *rishon* and the food it touches is a *sheni*, and this requires no special *de-rabbanan*: it is the basic biblical mechanism by which *tumah* propagates. *De-chtiv: ve-chol mashke asher yishateh be-chol keli yitma* — the Torah itself establishes that liquid that enters a vessel becomes *tamei*, and from there the chain of *rishon* and *sheni* is straightforward.

The decree the *gemara* is describing must therefore refer to a different case: *ela be-mashkin ha-ba'im machmat yadayim* — liquid that was touched by hands. Hands are a *sheni*. A *sheni* touching *chullin* liquid should, strictly speaking, accomplish nothing — a *sheni* can only affect *trumah*, not ordinary food or its liquids. Yet the *gezeirah* declares that when a *sheni* (or *rishon*) touches liquid, the liquid is upgraded to a *rishon*. This is the principle identified by *Tosafot*: *kol ha-posel et ha-trumah metamei et ha-mashkin lihyot techilah* — anything that can disqualify *trumah* (i.e., a *sheni* or higher) also renders liquid a *rishon*. The rationale is that liquid becomes *tamei* more easily than food does. Food requires *hechsher* — contact with liquid — before it can receive *tumah* at all; liquid, by contrast, is inherently receptive and is sometimes itself a source of *tumah* (as in the case of a *zav*'s fluids). Given liquid's heightened susceptibility, the rabbis were maximally stringent: any contact with a *sheni* or higher upgrades it to a *rishon*. The chain then continues: the *rishon* liquid touches food, rendering the food a *sheni*.

### Sheni-to-Rishon Upgrade

The principle just described — that a *sheni* touching liquid upgrades it to a *rishon* — is a dramatic rabbinic extension, and the *gemara* attributes it to a decree modeled on the case of liquids that originate from a *zav* (*gezeirah mishum mashkin ha-ba'im machmat sheretz*). The reasoning proceeds in two tiers. First, since the *de'oraita* rule already establishes that certain liquids — specifically the bodily fluids of a *zav*, *zavah*, or *niddah* — are an *av ha-tumah* in their own right (*de-chtiv: ve-chi yarok ha-zav ba-tahor*), and therefore capable of rendering a person or vessel a *rishon*, the rabbis saw fit to extend analogous stringency to other liquids more broadly. The result is a graduated structure: an *av* touching liquid renders it an *av*; a *rishon* or *sheni* touching liquid renders it a *rishon*. Liquid never merely stays at the level it receives from contact — it is always either at its own *de'oraita* peak or upgraded to *rishon* by the *gezeirah*.

This is striking precisely because it is counterintuitive: one would expect *tumah* to diminish as it propagates downward, not to be selectively amplified at the step involving liquid. The amplification reflects the rabbis' view that liquid is a particularly potent vehicle of *tumah* and demands exceptional stringency.

### Vessel Liquid Contact

The ninth category also includes *ha-keilim she-nitme'u b'mashkin* — vessels that became *tamei* through liquid. Vessels, like persons, can only be a *rishon de'oraita*. So if a vessel is becoming *tamei* through contact with liquid, the liquid must be treated, in this context, as an *av ha-tumah* — the only level that can render a vessel *tamei* at all.

The *gemara* again asks: what liquid? *Eileima be-mashkin de-zav* — the fluids of a *zav* or *zavah* or *niddah*? That is *de'oraita*, as established above. *Ella be-mashkin machmat sheretz* — rather, it must be liquid that touched a *sheretz*, and the *gezeirah* is *mishum mashke de-zav*: because some liquids *are* an *av* (those of a *zav*), the rabbis applied analogous treatment to other liquids in certain contexts, such that when liquid that has touched an *av* contacts a vessel, the vessel becomes a *rishon*. The vessel is thereby rendered capable of propagating *tumah* to *trumah*.

## Hands Gezeirah Attributi…

### Attribution Question

Having catalogued the content of the eighteen things, the *gemara* raises a question about their attribution: *tamidei Shammai v'Hillel gazru* — were these decrees issued by the students of Shammai and Hillel? Or was it Shammai and Hillel themselves?

### Earlier Tannaitic Gezerot

The *gemara* cites a *braita* that places certain earlier *gezerot* before the era of Shammai and Hillel altogether. *Yosi ben Yoezer ish Tzeredah v'Yosi ben Yochanan ish Yerushalayim* — familiar names from the first *zugot* in *Pirkei Avot* — issued two decrees: that land outside of Israel is *tamei* (since burial practices among non-Jews could not be relied upon, and one might unknowingly walk over a grave), and that glassware (*klei zechochit*) is susceptible to *tumah* even though the Torah's *de'oraita* system of *tumah* for vessels does not include glass. *Shimon ben Shatach* then instituted the *kesuvah* as an outstanding debt rather than money placed in escrow — a reform designed to make divorce economically consequential and thus a genuine deterrent — and also decreed *tumah* on metal vessels.

And then: *Shammai v'Hillel gazru tumah al ha-yadayim* — Shammai and Hillel themselves issued the decree about hands. So the *gemara* has a *braita* that attributes the *gezeirah* about hands specifically to Shammai and Hillel personally, not to their respective schools.

### Shammai-Hillel Role

The *gemara* presses further. One might try to resolve the tension by reading "Shammai and Hillel" in the *braita* as shorthand for their schools and disciples. But Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel establishes a critical point: *shmoneh asar davar gazru u-ve-shmoneh asar nechelku* — the eighteen things were originally matters of ongoing debate, and on this one occasion Beit Shammai's numbers prevailed and the matters were resolved. The eighteen things, in other words, were not uncontested rabbinic legislation; they were *machloket* items on which Beit Shammai's position ultimately won the day. And the significance of this: *v'ilu Hillel v'Shammai lo nechelku ela b'gimel mekomos* — Hillel and Shammai themselves personally disagreed on only three matters, as Rav Huna teaches, and these eighteen were not among the three.

### Hands Paradox

This creates a genuine difficulty. If the *gezeirah* about *yadayim* was made by Shammai and Hillel personally — as the *braita* states — then it predates the eighteen things, which were matters debated by their *schools*. But we are also told that the hands *gezeirah* is among the eighteen things, which are characterized as items that were *in dispute* between the schools before being resolved. How can something Shammai and Hillel personally agreed upon — it was never among their three personal disputes — also be among the eighteen things that were debated between their schools?

This paradox is left unresolved at the end of the *amud*, and the *gemara* will take it up on the following daf.
