# Yevamot 94 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eid Echad Foundation

### Single Witness Yibum

The daf opens in the midst of a question that has been developing across several folios: can the principles governing *eid echad* — the credibility extended to a single witness — be applied to the domain of *yibum*? The standard scenario for *eid echad* is familiar: a single witness testifies that a woman's husband has died, and on the basis of that testimony she is permitted to remarry. The question now is whether the same credibility operates when, instead of marrying a stranger, the woman is to perform *yibum* with her brother-in-law.

Why might this case differ? Because the stranger is someone toward whom she has no particular prior relationship or incentive to cut corners, whereas the brother-in-law is a man who has been part of the family. She knows him. Perhaps she is even more fond of him than of her husband. The concern, then, is whether that familiarity might lead her to do inadequate investigation — to accept thin evidence of her husband's death because the result would be a marriage she actually desires.

### Two Explanations

The Gemara frames the question by articulating two alternative explanations for why we believe an *eid echad* in the first place. The first is *milta d'avida l'igluyei* — the principle that matters of life and death naturally come to light. A man is either alive or dead; that fact will eventually be known. Because the truth will surface on its own, the single witness has no motive to lie, and that alone is sufficient to render his testimony credible. Under this reading, her own investigation is irrelevant, and whether she wants the outcome or not makes no difference to the reliability of the *eid*.

The second explanation is *dayka u'minsva* — that the woman herself investigates carefully before acting on the testimony. It is her own due diligence that bolsters the single witness's credibility, providing an independent check on his report. Under this reading, the reliability of the *eid echad* is not self-contained but is intertwined with the quality of her investigation. And if her investigation might be compromised by self-interest — whether through love of the brother-in-law or, as we will see, through hatred of him — then the credibility of the *eid echad* is correspondingly weakened.

### Tosafot's Concern

Tosafot raises a pointed difficulty. If the Rishonim had all along assumed that the credibility of *eid echad* rests on her investigation, how can the Gemara now present these two explanations as genuine alternatives? Tosafot resolves this by distinguishing between degrees of reliance on her investigation: one can say the earlier discussions assumed *some* role for her investigation without committing to its being the *primary* or *exclusive* basis. Tosafot therefore reads the Gemara as positing that her investigation contributes meaningfully but need not bear the full weight alone. This is an important resolution, though one must acknowledge that the Gemara's own framing presents the two explanations as a genuine opposition.

## Husband vs Brother

### Overwritten Testimony

The Gemara had previously sought proof that *eid echad* is believed even for purposes of *yibum* from a case in the Mishnah where testimony about the order of deaths was subsequently reversed. The Mishnah discusses a scenario in which a woman is told that her husband died first and her son died afterward — an order in which she is *not* obligated in *yibum*, since her son was alive when her husband died and thus there is no surviving brother-in-law without offspring. She then remarries. Subsequently she is told *chiluf hayu ha-devarim* — the facts were reversed — that the son died first and then the husband, meaning she *was* obligated in *yibum*. The Mishnah rules that she must leave her new husband and that her children are *mamzerim*.

The question the Gemara raises is: why does the second report overturn the first? If both consisted of two witnesses each, there is no obvious reason to prefer the second over the first. The Gemara therefore infers that the first report must have been a single witness and the second consisted of two, and the two outweighed the one. This would demonstrate that in principle an *eid echad* can be credited with testifying about the order of deaths — effectively permitting her to escape the obligation of *yibum*.

### Critical Question

Now a further question is posed: the student body asks Rav Sheshet that the real question is not whether we believe an *eid echad* that the *husband* is dead and permits *yibum* — even the woman herself is believed to say her husband died, as a Mishnah teaches: *ha'isha she'amra met ba'ali tinasei*, a woman who says her husband died may remarry; and *met ba'ali tisyabem*, she may perform *yibum* on the strength of her own word. If she is believed in all this, certainly an *eid echad* is believed.

The real question is *lemishra yevama le'alma* — whether one can believe an *eid echad* in order to permit the *yevama* to marry someone outside the family altogether, thereby releasing her entirely from the *yibum* obligation.

### Woman's Testimony

There are two obvious scenarios in which this question arises. One: the husband is already known to be dead, there is a *yavam* waiting, and a single witness reports that the *yavam* has died in a foreign country — may she now remarry freely? The second scenario emerges from the Mishnah itself: the testimony about the order of deaths, which, if believed, releases her from *yibum* entirely.

The woman's own testimony provides the baseline. She is believed to say *met ba'ali* — her husband died — and on that basis she may remarry or perform *yibum*. But she is *not* believed to say *met yivami she'inasei* — my *yavam* has died so that I may marry another man. Nor is she believed to say *meta achoti* — my sister died so that I may marry her husband. These cases differ from testimony about the husband's death, and the parallel question follows naturally: if she herself is not believed in these cases, does the same limitation apply to an *eid echad*?

## Incentive Problem

### Hatred vs Love

Tosafot provides a penetrating analysis of why the concern about improper investigation is more acute when the woman hates the *yavam* than when she loves him. The Gemara had already raised the possibility that if she loves the brother-in-law she might not investigate sufficiently because she wants to marry him. But that concern was the original question — and we had in some sense decided that her own credibility regarding the husband's death was not undermined even then. Why should hatred be worse?

Tosafot explains with a principle both psychologically astute and Talmudically grounded: *shegedola sinas hasone me'ahavas ha'ohev* — the hatred of an enemy is a more powerful force than the love of one who loves. Those motivated by strong negative feeling act with greater urgency and force than those motivated by positive attachment. The hater is more likely to neglect due diligence than the lover. Tosafot adds that it is even more common for a woman to hate her *yavam* than to love him, as is stated elsewhere in the tractate.

### Trapped Marriage

Tosafot presses further and articulates what is perhaps the more decisive point: the consequences are asymmetric. If she loves the *yavam* and yet fails to investigate properly, the worst outcome is that *yibum* takes place — she winds up married to a man she favored. But she might not have been particularly invested, because perhaps he will do *chalitzah* instead, or perhaps she would have been equally happy marrying any number of other men. The upside is modest. By contrast, if she hates the *yavam*, the worst-case scenario is catastrophic: she is trapped in a marriage to a man she despises. She has no legal recourse, and if *yibum* is performed she cannot unilaterally escape it. *Einah yecholah linatzel mimenu linasei le'acher* — she cannot escape him to marry another. Her only alternative is perpetual *iggun*, never receiving *chalitzah*, never being free to move on.

The asymmetry is stark. Not marrying the man you love still leaves you free. Being married to the man you hate leaves you with no options. That is the most powerful of all the negative forces we have catalogued, and it is precisely why the Gemara is more troubled by the case of hatred than by the case of love.

## Rav Sheshet

### Woman Cannot Testify

Rav Sheshet responds to the students' formulation of the question by saying *tenisuah* — we have already learned this in the Mishnah. He cites the Mishnah: *amru lah meis ba'alaich ve'achar kach meis binaiach* — they said to her that her husband died and afterward her son died. She remarried. Then they said *chiluf hayu ha-devarim* — the order was reversed. The Mishnah rules *teitzei* — she must leave — *ve'havlad rishon ve'acharon mamzer* — and the children from her first and last relationships are *mamzerim*. This is the view of Rabbi Akiva, who holds that even a violation of an *issur lav* can produce a *mamzer*.

The force of Rav Sheshet's point is the following: the Mishnah overturns the first report in favor of the second. But why? If both consisted of two witnesses, there is no obvious basis for preferring one over the other. Furthermore, if both had equal standing, the child's status should be *safeik mamzer* — a doubtful *mamzer* — not a definite one. But the Mishnah does not say *safeik mamzer*; a later clause of the same Mishnah carefully distinguishes which child is a *mamzer* and which is not, demonstrating that the Mishnah is being quite precise. A *safeik mamzer* would not be called simply *mamzer*. Therefore, the Mishnah must be working with the assumption that the second report definitively outweighs the first — which can only be because the first was an *eid echad* and the second was two witnesses.

### Two vs One Witness

The Gemara rebuts Rav Sheshet. It says *le'olam trei ve-trei* — perhaps the Mishnah is discussing two witnesses against two witnesses throughout, and that does not preclude it being a valid proof of anything about *eid echad*.

### Eidei Hazama

The Gemara continues: according to Rav Acha bar Minyumi, the reason we credit the second set of witnesses over the first is that the second set are not merely offering contrary factual testimony, but are *eidei hazama* — witnesses who testify that the first witnesses were not present at the events they claimed to have witnessed, which is the formal Talmudic mechanism for invalidating testimony entirely. On this reading, the second witnesses first disqualified the first witnesses as *eidei hazama* and then provided their own account of the reversed order.

The Mishnah, however, does not say anything about *eidei hazama*; it says *chiluf hayu ha-devarim* — the facts were reversed. The Gemara is reading the *eidei hazama* dynamic into the Mishnah. This reading is possible, and if it is adopted, then the Mishnah cannot serve as proof that we believe a single witness at all: the Mishnah might be describing a scenario where two witnesses trumped two others for the specific reason of *eidei hazama*, not because one of the original reports was an *eid echad*. The hoped-for proof therefore collapses.

## Agunah Principle

### Trapped Status

The Gemara now draws on a broader principle to frame when *eid echad* is believed at all: the rabbinic leniency of *eid echad* is specifically extended in cases where a person's status is trapped and in need of relief. The clearest such case is the *agunah* — a woman whose husband may or may not be alive and who cannot remarry until his death is established.

### Man Not Trapped

With this principle in view, the Gemara examines the parallel Mishnah's cases about what a man is not believed to testify. The Mishnah states: a man is not believed to say that his brother died so that he may perform *yibum* with his brother's wife, nor is he believed to say that his wife died so that he may marry her sister. The Gemara asks: should we infer from this that while the man is not believed, an *eid echad* would be believed?

The answer is no — because the principle of *mishum igguna akeilu bah rabbanan* applies only where someone is genuinely trapped. A man is never truly trapped in the way a woman is. Even before Rabbeinu Gershom's enactment, a man could marry additional wives; he is never locked into an inescapable marital limbo. The worst a man faces is that there is a particular woman he cannot marry — but he is free. A woman, by contrast, may be completely unable to remarry and has no recourse. Therefore, the entire framework of leniency for *eid echad* is targeted at her situation, not his. One cannot infer from a Mishnah dealing with both a man and a woman anything definitive about whether an *eid echad* would be believed in cases where no one is truly trapped. The Mishnah's purpose is simply to state that she — and he — are not believed; it carries no implication about a third-party *eid echad*.

The Gemara adds that the reason the Mishnah even needed to state this about her — given that according to Rabbi Akiva, if she were lying the child would be a *mamzer* and she herself would face all the severe consequences catalogued in earlier Mishnayos — is precisely that we might have thought: *chaysa al kilkula d'zar'ah u'dayka* — she would be scrupulously careful because she would never risk producing a *mamzer*. She would not even risk not doing due diligence. Therefore, the Mishnah teaches us that even so, we do not believe her, because the force of hatred toward the *yavam* is powerful enough to override even the fear of producing a *mamzer*-status child. That is how deeply the Gemara takes that psychological dynamic.

### Kal Vachomer

Rava cuts through the accumulated complexity with a *kal vachomer*. If we believe an *eid echad* to permit an *issur karet* — the grave transgression of a married woman remarrying, or performing *yibum* with a *yavam* — how can we fail to believe an *eid echad* for the comparatively lighter *issur lav* of a *yevama* marrying someone outside the family before receiving *chalitzah*? Surely if we extend credibility for the heavier matter, the lighter follows automatically.

## Woman's Credibility

### Objection to Rava

One of the rabbis responds to Rava's *kal vachomer* with a pointed counter-argument: look at the woman herself as a proof. She herself is believed regarding the *issur karet* — the testimony that her husband died, which permits her to remarry or perform *yibum*, both of which involve *issur karet*. Yet she is *not* believed to say that the *yavam* died, even though that involves only an *issur lav*. So it is demonstrably not the case that lighter stringency automatically follows from heavier — we see a case where she is believed for the heavier but not the lighter.

### No Due Diligence

Why is she not believed about the *yavam*'s death? *De-kivan de-zimnin de-sanya lei, lo daika u'minsva* — because there are cases in which she hates the *yavam* and will not perform due diligence. She may not consciously lie, but she will accept insufficient evidence, she will not push hard enough to confirm that he is truly dead, because she desperately wants to escape the prospect of marrying him. That motivational failure renders her testimony unreliable — not because she is dishonest but because she may be self-deceived or insufficiently rigorous.

### Witness Bolstered

The implication for *eid echad* then follows directly. If the credibility of a single witness rests in part on *dayka u'minsva* — on her investigation bolstering his testimony — then in a case where we cannot trust her investigation, the *eid echad* loses that bolstering as well. We cannot believe the *eid echad* about the *yavam*'s death for the same reason we cannot believe her: because her investigation, which props up his testimony, may have been compromised by the powerful force of hatred.

The Gemara does not formally resolve whether we do or do not believe the *eid echad* in this case. The argument hangs on how deeply the credibility of the *eid* depends on her own reliability. What the *Shulchan Aruch* (*Even Ha-Ezer* 158, section 3) does rule is: *neeman eid echad lehahid leyevama shemet baalah umityabemet al piv, o shemet yevamah shetinasei lashuk* — a single witness is believed to testify that the *yevama*'s husband died and she performs *yibum* on his word, or that the *yavam* died and she may marry another man. So the practical ruling follows Rava leniently — we do believe the *eid echad* even for releasing her from *yibum*. But the Gemara leaves the theoretical underpinning unresolved, and it touches on the core question that has run through this entire chapter: is the *eid echad* believed because of his own inherent reliability, or because of the investigation she performs?

## Mishna Cases

### Husband Reappears

The Gemara now transitions to the Mishnah. The final case of the preceding Mishnah involved a woman who, based on testimony that her husband had died, performed *kiddushin* — betrothal — with another man, and then the husband reappeared alive. Since she had only done *kiddushin* and not yet *nisuin* — had not yet begun living with the second man — she simply returns to her husband. There is no harm and no foul. Furthermore, if the second man nonetheless gives her a *get*, that *get* does not render her a *grushah* — a divorcee — for purposes of marrying a *Kohen*, because he was never truly her husband.

### Grushah Drasha

The Mishnah attributes a *drasha* on this point to Rabbi Elazar ben Mattia, who derived it from the verse *ve-ishah grushah me-ishah* — a woman divorced from *her husband*. Since this man never achieved the status of her husband, the *get* he gives her carries no force and does not make her a *grushah*.

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav observes that Rabbi Elazar could have derived a much more illuminating ruling — a pearl — from that verse, and instead used it for something relatively obvious — a piece of pottery. The pearl would have been: *afilu lo nitgarsha ela me-ishah* — even if she was divorced only from her husband, meaning even a *get* that says "you are divorced from me but remain forbidden to everyone else," which is not a valid *get* that permits remarriage — even such a document renders her *pesulah le-kehuna*, invalid for marriage to a *Kohen*. This is the concept of *reiach ha-get ha-posel bi-kehuna* — the "scent" of a *get* that disqualifies for *kehuna* even without fully releasing from the marriage. That is a genuine *chiddush*. By contrast, using the verse to teach that a *get* from someone who was never truly a husband has no effect is, to put it plainly, not much of a teaching.

### Sister Marriage

The next Mishnah introduces a new scenario. A man's wife goes to a distant country. Others come and tell him she has died. He then marries her sister. Subsequently his wife returns, alive. The Mishnah rules that the wife is *muteret lachazor lo* — permitted to return to him. The marriage to the sister is treated as entirely without consequence: the sister walks away without needing a *get* from him, he is *mutar bi-krovot shniyah* — permitted to the sister's relatives — and *ha-shniyah muteret bi-krovav* — the sister is permitted to his relatives. It is as though the marriage to the sister never existed.

Why is this so different from the parallel case where the woman remarries based on false testimony about her husband's death? The answer lies in how the situation appears to onlookers. When a woman marries a second man while her husband may still be alive, people might reasonably think: perhaps the husband gave her a *get*; perhaps the marriage was dissolved legitimately. The second marriage therefore appears plausible and carries real legal weight — it must be undone with a *get* and produces all the negative consequences. But when a man marries his wife's sister and it turns out his wife is alive, everyone understands immediately that even if he had divorced his wife, he could not have been validly married to the sister while his wife was alive. There is no scenario, however one arranges the facts, in which this marriage could have been legitimate. Therefore it is not treated as a marriage at all, requires no *get*, and carries no consequences.

The Gemara adds that this holds *even* if he is *mutar* to remain with the wife — it is the woman whose legal status is affected by certain forbidden sexual relations, not the man. His sleeping with the sister does not alter his wife's status, because she was not a party to what occurred.

### Wife Actually Dies

A further case: suppose the man was told his wife had died, married the sister, and then — after this had already occurred — the wife actually does die. He is now *mutar bi-shniyah* — permitted to remain with the sister. Whatever occurred in the interim, the eventual death of the first wife removes all barriers. Furthermore, the Mishnah specifies: if it subsequently becomes clear that the wife was in fact still alive when he married the sister, *havlad rishon mamzer* — the child born during the period the first wife was alive is a *mamzer* — *ve'ha'acharon eino mamzer* — but the child born after the first wife genuinely died is not.

### Rabbi Yosi

Rabbi Yosi states a general principle: *kol she'posel al yedei acherim posel al yedei atzmo* — whoever disqualifies through others disqualifies through himself; *ve'chol she'ein posel al yedei acherim eino posel al yedei atzmo* — and whoever does not disqualify through others does not disqualify through himself. This statement, as the Gemara will clarify, requires considerable unpacking.

## Wife and Brother

### Both Dead Reports

The Gemara extends the discussion to an additional scenario. Suppose both the wife and the brother-in-law go to a distant country and witnesses report that both of them have died. To use the names introduced in the shiur: Reuven is married to Rachel, and Baruch is married to Leah. Rachel and Baruch go overseas. Reports come back that both have died. Now Reuven, believing Rachel is dead, is free to marry Leah — his wife's sister is no longer forbidden now that the wife has died, and Leah, believing Baruch is dead, is free to marry Reuven. They marry.

Then both Rachel and Baruch return alive. What are the consequences?

### Irony Unveiled

From Leah's perspective, she received testimony that her husband Baruch was dead, remarried Reuven, and it turned out the testimony was false. All the devastating consequences of the earlier Mishnah fall upon her: she must leave both men — *tetzei mizeh umizeh* — she receives *gettin* from both, she is forbidden to both, her children's statuses are affected. She bears the full weight.

But what of Rachel? One might think: if we say it *looked like* Reuven was married to Leah — seriously enough that Leah suffers all these consequences — then perhaps it *also* looks like Reuven was married to another woman, which should affect Rachel's ability to return to him. Yet the Gemara rules: *afilu hachi, eshet giso asira agiso, ishto sharya* — even so, the brother-in-law's wife is forbidden to the brother-in-law, but his own wife remains permitted to him. The two consequences do not run in parallel. *Ve'lo amrinan Rachel u'mitoch shenesra eshet giso agiso te'aser ishto alav* — we do not say that because Leah is now forbidden to Baruch, therefore Rachel should be forbidden to Reuven.

The irony is considerable. The Gemara acknowledges it directly: the very fact that the marriage looked real enough to heap consequences on Leah does not, by some form of symmetry, impose consequences on Rachel. The Gemara explains this partly on the grounds that it is the woman's status that is affected by forbidden relationships — Rachel was not involved — and partly on the grounds that with Rachel alive and known to have been alive the whole time, no observer could plausibly conclude that the marriage to Leah was ever legitimate. There is no scenario that makes it work, so it is not treated as a marriage with respect to Rachel, even as it is treated as consequential enough with respect to Leah to impose all the negative ramifications upon her.

### Marriage Reality

The proof that the marriage was real enough in some sense is that Leah requires a *get* from both Reuven and from Baruch — she is bound to divorce proceedings with two husbands. Why does she need a *get* from Reuven? Because it looked like they were married. Yet this same "it looked like they were married" reasoning does not extend to forbid Rachel to Reuven. This asymmetry — which the Gemara accepts without fully resolving the apparent tension — reflects the broader principle that a man's forbidden relationship does not alter his wife's personal status, which is determined by her own actions, not his.

## Rabbi Akiva

### Wife Consequence

The Gemara now raises a challenge from Rabbi Akiva's position. If the Mishnah says Leah needs a *get* from Reuven — and it does — then according to Rabbi Akiva, shouldn't that trigger consequences for Rachel as well? Once Leah needs a *get*, she is legally treated, at least nominally, as Reuven's divorcee. And if she is his divorcee, then Rachel — her sister — becomes his divorcee's sister, which is a category of *arayot* that a *Kohen* may not marry, and which, if treated seriously, could make Rachel forbidden to Reuven entirely.

This presents a real problem for the Mishnah's ruling that Rachel remains permitted. If Rabbi Akiva is the author of the Mishnah — or if we are ruling according to Rabbi Akiva — how does the Mishnah conclude that Rachel is *muteret*?

### Baraita Proof

The Gemara brings a *baraita* to resolve this. The *baraita* states: *kol elu she-ba-Torah ein tzerichos mimennu get* — all the forbidden relationships enumerated in the Torah: if a man began living with his sister, his father's wife, or any of the *arayot*, no *get* is required to separate them, because the relationship is understood by all to be legally meaningless. The one exception is: *chutz me-eshet ish she-niset al pi beit din* — a married woman who remarried based on testimony accepted by a *beit din*. That case, as we have seen, looks enough like a legitimate marriage — because it is conceivable the husband had given her a *get* — that it requires a *get* to formally undo it.

### Akiva Adds Cases

Rabbi Akiva then adds two further cases where a *get* is required: *af eshet ach ve-achot isha* — even the brother's wife and the wife's sister. What makes these two different from blood-relation *arayot*, and why would Rabbi Akiva demand a *get* in these cases?

The answer lies in two features unique to these two relationships. First, they are not blood relatives — there is no automatic universal recognition that such a relationship is forbidden by nature. Second — and more importantly — both of these relationships have circumstances under which they become entirely permitted: the brother's wife becomes permitted through *yibum* if the brother died childless; the wife's sister becomes permitted after the wife dies. The existence of a legitimate pathway to this marriage means that observers might actually believe the marriage was permissible, and therefore it looks real enough to require a formal *get*.

All other *arayot* are forever forbidden without exception — there is no scenario in which marrying one's mother or one's sister becomes permitted. Therefore no observer will mistake such a union for a legitimate marriage. But with brother's wife and wife's sister, one can imagine a legitimate pathway, and therefore the illicit union takes on the appearance of a real marriage, requiring a *get* to dissolve.

## Limiting Akiva

### Only Kiddushin

The Gemara immediately qualifies Rabbi Akiva's position significantly. Rav Yehuda, citing Rav Chiya bar Yosef citing Rav, asks: what exactly is the case in which Rabbi Akiva says the brother's wife needs a *get*? The answer: *kegon she-kiddesh achiv et ha-isha* — it is specifically where the brother had done only *kiddushin* with the woman, without *nisuin* — and then went to a distant country, and the other man heard he had died and married the brother's wife.

### Tnai Logic

Why does the stage of the first marriage matter? Because *de-amri inshi* — people will say — *tenai hava lei be-kiddushin* — perhaps the brother's *kiddushin* was subject to a stipulation that was never fulfilled, meaning the *kiddushin* never actually took effect. And therefore, *shappir nasa* — the second man genuinely and legitimately married her. Once people believe the second marriage was valid, it looks real, and it requires a *get* to undo.

This logic applies precisely because *kiddushin* without *nisuin* is subject to the possibility of conditions: it is known and understood that betrothal can be contingent on stipulations that may fail to be met. Therefore, the mere fact of *kiddushin* does not definitively settle the matter in the eyes of the public.

### Sister Kiddushin

The same reasoning governs the case of the wife's sister. If the man had done only *kiddushin* with his first wife Rachel and not *nisuin*, and she went abroad and he heard she had died and he married Leah, people might say: *tenai hava lei be-kiddushin* — the *kiddushin* to Rachel was conditional and never took effect — *ve-shappir nasiv* — and therefore the marriage to Leah was perfectly valid. Because the marriage to Leah thus appears real, it requires a *get*, and once it requires a *get*, the sister of his apparent divorcee becomes forbidden to him.

### After Nisuin

But after *nisuin*, even Rabbi Akiva agrees that no *get* is required. The principle *ein tnai be-nisuin* — stipulations do not apply after *nisuin* has taken place — means that no one will entertain the possibility that the first marriage fell through. Once a couple has lived together as husband and wife, the marriage is treated as complete and unconditional. Therefore, no observer will think the second marriage was legitimate, it will not be treated as a real marriage, and no *get* is required.

The practical outcome is significant. If a man had *nisuin* with his first wife — as is the standard case — and she goes overseas and is reported dead and he marries her sister, and then the first wife returns, no *get* is needed to separate him from the sister. The marriage to the sister is simply dissolved, without formal proceedings, without lingering consequences for status. Rachel remains permitted to Reuven, and Leah walks away as though the marriage never was — because, in the eyes of anyone who knows that *nisuin* had taken place, it effectively never was.
