# Rosh Hashanah 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Layla v'Yom Min HaChodesh

### Requirement Statement

The *gemara* returns to a principle stated earlier: *tzarich sheyehei layla veyom min hachodesh* — both the night and the day of *Rosh Chodesh* must belong to the new month. Before unpacking the precise halachic implications, the *gemara* asks *minalan* — what is the source for this requirement?

### Yochanan & Reish Lakish

Two *amoraim* offer independent verse-based derivations. Rabbi Yochanan cites the phrase *me'erev ad erev* — "from evening to evening" — which establishes that a halachic day runs from one evening to the next, encompassing both night and the following day as a single unit. Reish Lakish draws from the verse governing the eating of *matzos* for seven days, which specifies *ad yom ha'echad ve'esrim lachodesh ba'erev* — until the twenty-first at night — again demonstrating that a day extends from the preceding evening up to, but not including, the following night. In both cases the underlying point is simply that a halachic day consists of night followed by day.

### Abaye & Rava Debate

*Mai beinaihu* — what practical difference exists between these two sources, given that both arrive at the same conclusion? Abaye answers that the difference is purely a matter of *mashmach dorshin* — which verse one derives the principle from — with no halachic consequence. Rava disagrees: *chatzos laila ika beinaihu* — the practical difference concerns *chatzos*, midnight. The verse from the *matzah* context presupposes that the obligation to eat *matzah* on the first night extends until midnight, meaning the first night of *Pesach* is in effect divided at *chatzos*. This division will bear on the question of whether the entire preceding night of *Rosh Chodesh* must belong to the new month, or whether only the portion from *chatzos* onward need do so.

### Rashi's Explanation

What does the requirement that both night and day belong to the new month actually mean in practice? Rashi understands this as a rabbinic concern, and accordingly treats the cited verses as mere *asmachtos*. The underlying issue, according to Rashi, is one of appearances: if the old moon was visible on a given night, *Beis Din* may not turn around and declare the following day *Rosh Chodesh*. As the *gemara* established earlier, at least twenty-four hours — and in practice closer to thirty-six — must elapse between the last sliver of the old moon and the appearance of the new one. If witnesses saw the old moon the night before and *Beis Din* were to declare the very next day *Rosh Chodesh*, it would be patently obvious to the entire community that the calendar was being manipulated. People who had seen the old moon with their own eyes would recognize the declaration as a fraud.

According to Rashi, then, the question of *chatzos* amounts to the following: if the old moon was seen in the first half of the night — before *chatzos* — is it sufficiently removed in time that *Beis Din* could declare the following day *Rosh Chodesh* without it being conspicuously obvious? Or must the old moon have been unseen even after *chatzos* before such a declaration becomes acceptable? This is the halachic dispute between the two sources as Rashi reads it.

### Rabbeinu Chananel

Rabbeinu Chananel offers a substantially different reading, one that accounts more naturally for why the *gemara* invokes biblical verses at all. In his view, the requirement is not primarily about appearances but about an objective astronomical condition: the entire day declared as *Rosh Chodesh* must fall after the *molad*, the moment of lunar conjunction. The *molad* is the instant the moon moves, even infinitesimally, off the precise alignment with the sun and earth — the astronomical new moon, invisible to the eye but real as an astronomical event.

### Molad & Visibility Gap

The *gemara* has been operating throughout this passage with the assumption that the new moon becomes visible approximately six hours after the *molad*. In practice, as is noted, the gap is closer to sixteen to eighteen hours — but the *gemara*'s working figure is six. This gap matters enormously for Rabbeinu Chananel's reading.

Consider the normal case: witnesses testify that they saw the new moon on Monday night at, say, 7:00 PM. They come before *Beis Din* the following morning, still within the halachic day of Monday. *Beis Din* declares Monday *Rosh Chodesh*. If the moon became visible at 7:00 PM Monday evening, then the *molad* — occurring at least six hours earlier — took place at approximately 1:00 PM on Sunday. This means that all of Monday, beginning at nightfall Sunday, falls well after the *molad*. The entire day of *Rosh Chodesh* is thus *min hachodesh* — from the new month.

But now consider a different scenario. Suppose the moon, by the end of a long month, has traveled far enough behind the sun that it becomes visible in the early morning — say, 7:00 AM on Monday. Witnesses see it at dawn, testify that day, and *Beis Din* declares Monday *Rosh Chodesh*. If the moon was visible at 7:00 AM, the *molad* occurred approximately six hours earlier, around 1:00 AM Monday. But that means Monday night — from nightfall Sunday until 1:00 AM — was still part of the old moon's phase, pre-*molad*. Part of Monday belonged to the old month. According to Rabbeinu Chananel, this is precisely what the requirement *layla veyom min hachodesh* precludes: you cannot declare Monday *Rosh Chodesh* unless the entire day, including the night with which it began, falls after the *molad*.

There is of course a perfectly valid version of even the early-morning scenario. If the *molad* occurred at, say, 5:00 PM on Sunday — too soon after conjunction for visibility — and the moon only became sufficiently separated by 7:00 AM Monday, then all of Monday (which began at Sunday nightfall, after 5:00 PM Sunday) is post-*molad*, and the declaration is valid.

*Tosafot* disagrees with Rabbeinu Chananel's framework as a matter of practical *halacha*, holding that what matters is when the moon was visible, not when the *molad* occurred. But Rabbeinu Chananel's reading does have the advantage of explaining why the *gemara* reaches for biblical verses — it is making a substantive legal point, not merely a rabbinic ruling about appearances.

### Chatzos & Matzah

Under Rabbeinu Chananel's reading, the debate about *chatzos* takes on a different character as well. The question becomes: must the entirety of Monday night — from nightfall through dawn — be post-*molad*, or is it sufficient that the portion of Monday night from *chatzos* onward falls after the *molad*? The connection to *matzah* is structural: since the obligation to eat *matzah* on the first night of *Pesach* runs until *chatzos*, the night is in a sense legally divided at that point. The verse therefore might imply that if the post-*chatzos* portion of the night is from the new month — i.e., the *molad* occurred before *chatzos* — that suffices, even though the earlier part of the night was technically pre-*molad*. This is Rabbeinu Chananel's understanding of how *chatzos* enters the analysis.

It should be noted that in practice, *Rosh Chodesh* was always declared during the day in question, often only after witnesses arrived and testified. If they saw the moon the night before, they would travel and arrive the following morning; if they saw it at dawn, they would testify later that same day. In either case, the declaration was always retrospective, applying to a day that was already underway. This is one reason why, to this day, one does not repeat *Shemoneh Esrei* if *Yaaleh V'yavo* was forgotten on the first night of *Rosh Chodesh* — because the formal *kiddush hachodesh* was never accepted at night, and the status of that first night was always uncertain until testimony was received.

### Fixed Calendar Practice

One might ask: given that we now have a fixed calendar, what relevance does this discussion of the *molad* retain? The answer is that the fixed calendar itself was constructed with precisely this gap in mind. When the *molad* is announced in synagogue — one is stating that the astronomical conjunction occurs at a particular hour — *Rosh Chodesh* is then the following day. Now one understands why: even according to the *gemara*'s more conservative estimate, it takes at least six hours after the *molad* for the moon to become visible. In practice it is closer to eighteen hours, and so the calendar is structured such that *Rosh Chodesh* falls the day after the *molad* is announced. It is also worth noting that the *molad* is announced in Jerusalem time — a reflection of the fact that *kiddush hachodesh* was always anchored in *Eretz Yisrael*, and we preserve that orientation even in the fixed calendar.

## Sefeika DeYoma in Bavel

### Rav Nachman Principle

The *gemara* now shifts from astronomy to the practical challenge facing communities in *Bavel* — distant lands that received no timely word from *Eretz Yisrael* about when *Rosh Chodesh* had been declared. Rabbi Zeira cites Rav Nachman: *kol sefeika lekamei shadinan* — any doubt about when *Rosh Chodesh* falls is resolved by pushing forward, not backward. That is, the concern is always that the previous month may have been thirty days rather than twenty-nine, which would shift *Rosh Chodesh* — and therefore the fifteenth, the day of the *Yom Tov* — one day later than assumed. The practical result is that communities observe both the fifteenth and the sixteenth as *Yom Tov*, out of uncertainty. The months that matter most in this context are *Nissan* and *Tishrei*, and the months immediately preceding them — *Adar* and *Elul* — are treated as standard twenty-nine-day months.

### Discovery Principle

One might ask: why is the concern exclusively about the month being longer than expected — making *Rosh Chodesh* a day later — and not also about the possibility that the month was shorter, making *Rosh Chodesh* a day earlier? In that case, should we not also keep the fourteenth as *Yom Tov*? The *gemara* addresses this directly: *trei yarchei chasiri kala it lehu* — if two consecutive short months occurred, the word would get out. Rashi clarifies that this does not refer to *Elul* and *Av* as the consecutive pair — by *Rosh Hashanah* there would not necessarily have been enough time to learn that *Elul* was a short month. Rather, the relevant pair is *Av* and *Tammuz*. In a normal calendar pattern, *Tammuz* is twenty-nine days and *Av* is thirty. If *Av* were also only twenty-nine, that would mean two consecutive short months, and that would have become known by the time *Elul* arrived. Since no such news came, one can safely assume *Av* was thirty days, and there is therefore no reason to fear that *Rosh Chodesh* fell a day earlier than assumed.

### Two-Day Observance

The practical upshot is that communities in *Bavel* observe both the fifteenth and sixteenth as *Yom Tov* — *chamiser veshitser avdinan* — due to the uncertainty of which day is truly the fifteenth. They do not, however, observe the fourteenth, because that would require assuming two consecutive short months, and such a scenario would have become known.

### Levi's Silence

The *gemara* now presents a remarkable story. Levi, who had come from *Eretz Yisrael*, found himself in *Bavel* on the eleventh of *Tishrei* — the day after *Yom Kippur* as reckoned in *Bavel*. Observing the Babylonian Jews cheerfully cooking and eating, he quipped: *bassim tavshila de-Bavla'ei beyoma rabba de-Ma'arava* — the pots of the Babylonians are smelling very good today, on the great day of *Eretz Yisrael*, i.e., *Yom Kippur*. *Elul* had been declared thirty days in *Eretz Yisrael*, which pushed *Rosh Hashanah* — and consequently *Yom Kippur* — one day later than the Babylonians had assumed.

Those present immediately asked Levi to testify formally — if he knew it was *Yom Kippur* in *Eretz Yisrael*, why not declare it and bring them to stop eating? Levi refused: *lo shamati mipi beis din mekudash* — I did not personally hear *Beis Din* declare *mekudash*. Rashi explains: the *halacha* requires that messengers depart only after explicitly hearing *Beis Din*'s formal declaration. Even in a case where the previous month passed its thirtieth day without witnesses, making it logically certain that the following day is *Rosh Chodesh*, the messengers do not depart until *Beis Din* formally accepts testimony the next day and pronounces *mekudash*. Levi had not witnessed that declaration and therefore declined to offer formal testimony.

### Tosafot's Decentralizati…

*Tosafot* finds this extraordinarily difficult. Regardless of technicalities about hearing *mekudash*, Levi knew — or knew with overwhelming probability — that today was *Yom Kippur* in *Eretz Yisrael* and that the Babylonians were eating in violation of one of the most severe prohibitions in the Torah. How could he remain silent on a technicality?

*Tosafot* first attempts to create a small measure of genuine uncertainty. Levi had departed from the vicinity of *Beis Din* approximately twenty minutes before nightfall on the thirtieth of *Elul*. While it was practically inconceivable that witnesses could have arrived, been examined, and had their testimony accepted in those final minutes, it was not logically impossible. Perhaps, in theory, the process was concluded in moments — *chick-chak* — and the thirtieth became *Rosh Chodesh* after all. *Tosafot* acknowledges this is barely a possibility, but uses it to argue that Levi's certainty was not absolute.

Yet even this concession leaves the problem largely intact. If one is ninety-nine-point-nine percent certain that the Babylonians are eating on *Yom Kippur*, how does a technical procedural scruple justify silence? Here *Tosafot* makes a genuinely startling move. He invokes the principle that *Beis Din*'s calendar determinations are binding even when in error — even when the witnesses were mistaken, even when the declaration was made intentionally contrary to reality. The declaration is what creates the sacred day, not the underlying astronomical event. *Tosafot* then extends this logic: the Babylonian community had collectively identified a particular day as *Rosh Hashanah* and consequently another as *Yom Kippur*. That communal identification, even without a formal *Beis Din* declaration, was binding for them. The result is that there could be a valid *Yom Kippur* in *Eretz Yisrael* on one day and a valid *Yom Kippur* in *Bavel* on a different day, with both being halachically legitimate.

This is a striking position. It projects, at least theoretically, a paradigm of decentralized calendar authority — two communities, each with a binding calendar, neither invalidating the other. *Tosafot* is careful to note that the historical Jewish community never actually tolerated two permanently divergent calendars; the battles of the *geonic* period over calendar authority — ultimately resolved in favor of *Bavel* — make clear how intolerable that situation was recognized to be. But *Tosafot* uses this framework to explain why Levi's silence was not, in fact, halachically indefensible: the Babylonians' day was their binding *Yom Kippur*, and Levi's testimony could not retroactively undo that.

## Messenger Routes & Zones

### Rav Yochanan Ruling

The *gemara* records that Rav Yochanan issued a public proclamation: *kol heicha d'matu shluchei Nissan v'lo matu shluchei Tishrei l'avdu trei yomei* — wherever the messengers of *Nissan* reached but the messengers of *Tishrei* did not, the communities there must keep two days, as a *gzeira* lest *Nissan* lead to leniency regarding *Tishrei*.

### Geographic Zones

To understand this ruling, one must appreciate the geographic reality. *Yerushalayim* is the center. Messengers depart after *Rosh Chodesh* to inform surrounding communities of when the month was declared. For *Nissan*, they leave on the first of the month and can travel for approximately twelve days before *Pesach* on the fourteenth — two *Shabbatot* during which they cannot travel are subtracted from the fourteen available days. For *Tishrei*, they can only begin on the second of the month (not on *Rosh Hashanah* itself), lose two *Shabbatot* and *Yom Kippur* to travel restrictions, and thus reach only a ten-day journey's radius before *Succos* on the fifteenth.

The result is a set of concentric zones. Communities close to *Eretz Yisrael* — within the ten-day radius — receive messengers for both *Nissan* and *Tishrei*, know the exact date of both *Pesach* and *Succos*, and keep one day. Communities beyond the twelve-day radius receive messengers for neither and must keep two days for both. But there is an intermediate zone: communities between ten and twelve days' journey receive the *Nissan* messengers but not those of *Tishrei*. They know the correct date of *Rosh Hashanah* and therefore *Pesach*, but do not receive timely word for *Succos*.

### Uniformity Requirement

For these intermediate communities, Rav Yochanan ruled that they must nonetheless keep two days of *Pesach* as well — *gzeira Nissan atu Tishrei*. The concern is one of internal consistency: if they keep only one day of *Pesach* (since they know the date), they will likewise keep only one day of *Succos*, even though for *Succos* they genuinely do not know. A community cannot have a split practice — one day for *Pesach* and two for *Succos* — without the distinction becoming blurred over time. Uniformity requires that the same rule apply to both festivals.

### Aibo & Chiyya Story

The *gemara* brings a narrative that illustrates both the ruling and the difficulty of implementing it. Rabbi Aibo bar Nagri and Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba, both students of Rav Yochanan, happened to visit a community that fell precisely in the intermediate zone — reached by *Nissan* messengers but not those of *Tishrei*. They observed the community keeping only one day of *Pesach*, and they said nothing. Word reached Rav Yochanan. He was displeased: *lav amina l'chu* — did I not tell you? In this zone, two days of *Pesach* are required, on account of *gzeira Nissan atu Tishrei*. The story echoes the earlier tale of Levi's silence — a pattern of rabbinic figures declining to intervene in calendar practice — but here the concern is entirely rabbinic in character, about maintaining uniform observance across the two major festival seasons.

## Two-Day Yom Kippur

### Rava's Practice

The *gemara* records that Rava *hava ragil d'hava yateiv b'taanita trei yomei* — Rava was accustomed to fasting two days of *Yom Kippur*. As Levi's story illustrated, one could never be absolutely certain in *Bavel* that *Elul* had indeed been twenty-nine days. If *Elul* was thirty days, the day one took to be the tenth of *Tishrei* was actually the ninth, and the real *Yom Kippur* fell the following day. Since Rava could not be certain on which of the two days *Yom Kippur* actually fell, he fasted both.

### Rava Vindicated

*Zimna chada ishtakach k'vateih* — on one occasion Rava turned out to be correct. *Elul* that year was indeed thirty days, and the community had misidentified *Yom Kippur*. Rava cited this vindication as confirmation of his practice.

### Rabbeinu Chananel

Rabbeinu Chananel, however, enters an important qualification. Even granting that the practice was understandable, it was not an *obligation* and should not be mistaken for one. The principle stated elsewhere in the *gemara* — *d'miyemot Ezra v'eilach lo matzinu Elul meubar*, that since the days of Ezra *Elul* has never been thirty days — reflects the standard expectation. Rava's fasting was *milta yeteira* — an extra stringency, something beyond the letter of the law. *Rabbeinu Chananel* writes explicitly: *lo chovah hu alav* — it was not incumbent upon him. And if someone undertook such a stringency, Rabbeinu Chananel notes, it was not something deserving of particular praise — one should not congratulate oneself for going beyond what is required when the underlying concern is not a genuine one under normal circumstances.

### Rav Nachman Story

The *gemara* then presents a story that vividly captures why the prospect of two consecutive days of *Yom Kippur* was so daunting. Rav Nachman fasted throughout *Yom Kippur*. At *Ne'ilah* — at the very close of the day — a man arrived and informed him: *lemachar yoma rabba b'maarava* — tomorrow is *Yom Kippur* in *Eretz Yisrael*. *Elul* had been thirty days; rav nachman had fasted on the ninth of *Tishrei*, and the true *Yom Kippur* was the following day.

### Nachman's Curse

Rav Nachman asked the man where he was coming from. The man answered: from *Damharia*. Rav Nachman, playing on the name, replied: *dam tehei acharitao* — blood should be the end of you. Rashi explains that this reflects the tremendous anguish of being told, at the very conclusion of an exhausting fast, that one must fast again tomorrow. The *gemara* also has Rav Nachman quote: *kalim hayu rodfenu* — swift were our pursuers. The bitter irony being: you came so quickly with this news. Had you arrived a few days later, it would have been too late to matter. As it stands, Rav Nachman faced the physical and spiritual challenge of two consecutive days of fasting.

## Tekufas Teves & Leap Year

### Solstice Rule

Rav Huna bar Avin sent a ruling to Rava: *kad chazit de-mashka tekufas Teves ad shis-asar be-Nissan* — if you observe that *tekufas Teves*, the winter solstice season, extends into the sixteenth of *Nissan*, then *abrah le-hahi shata* — declare a leap year for that year. The concern is straightforward: *Pesach* must fall in the spring. If the winter season has not yet given way to spring by the fifteenth of *Nissan*, then *Pesach* — which falls on the fifteenth — is occurring in winter, not spring. A leap month must be added so that the following year's *Nissan* falls properly in the spring season. Rav Huna adds: *v'lo tachosh lahem* — do not be concerned that such a declaration is somehow improper, for there are adequate grounds to do so.

### Verse Basis

The biblical anchor for this requirement is the verse *shamor et chodesh ha-aviv* — guard the month of spring. The word *chodesh* refers to the lunar month, the moon's renewal; the word *aviv* refers to the solar spring season. The verse thus enjoins that the lunar month of *Nissan* must coincide with the solar season of spring — *she-tehe be-chodesh Nissan*, that the spring should fall within the month of *Nissan*.

### Rashi's Explanation

One might ask: if the spring needs only to fall within *Nissan*, perhaps it suffices for spring to begin by the fifteenth, i.e., by *Pesach* itself. And if spring only begins on the sixteenth — one day late — why not simply add a day to one of the preceding months rather than an entire leap month? Rashi addresses both questions. On the first: the word *chodesh* in the verse implies *chadesh* — newness, waxing. The moon is still growing, still renewing itself, only through the fourteenth of the month. From the fifteenth onward it begins to wane. Therefore, for the month to be a *chodesh* in the full sense — a waxing, renewing month — the spring must begin by the fourteenth, not the fifteenth. On the second: since the spring must begin by the fourteenth rather than the fifteenth, a single-day adjustment to a preceding month would be insufficient. The gap requires a full thirty-day leap month — *ibur hashana* — to bring *Nissan* back into proper alignment with the solar spring.

### Adjustment Limits

This leads to a further question: if one could see in advance that *Pesach* would fall a day or two late, why not preemptively extend one or two of the preceding months by a day? The answer involves limits on how much imbalance is permissible in the calendar. A normal year has six thirty-day months and six twenty-nine-day months. One can extend this to seven thirty-day months and five twenty-nine-day months. Whether one may go as far as eight and four is a matter of dispute — some permit it, others do not, and according to some readings even eight and four is forbidden. If one has already used up the permissible single-day adjustments and cannot make a double adjustment, there is no alternative to declaring a full leap year.

## Moon Phases at Sea

### Seafarer's Marker

Rav Nachman addressed a practical challenge facing those who traveled at sea: *atun delo yadisu bikviya diyarcha* — you will be on the ocean and will have no way of knowing when *Beis Din* declared *Rosh Chodesh*. Without knowing the exact date, how will you know when the fourteenth of *Nissan* arrives and it is time to destroy *chametz*? Rav Nachman provided a lunar marker: *ki chazisu seira demashlim leyoma* — when you see that the moon "completes the day," i.e., sets precisely as the sun rises, then *biar* u *chamira* — destroy your *chametz*. That moment signals that you are at the midpoint of the month, around the fifteenth of *Nissan*.

### Astronomical Marker

The *gemara* then clarifies the astronomy behind this marker. At the middle of the month, the sun and moon are diametrically opposite one another, with the earth between them. This means that at sunset, the full moon rises on the opposite horizon; it then remains visible throughout the entire night; and at sunrise, it sets. The moon literally "completes" the day — its light fills the night from end to end, setting just as the sun comes up. This is the phenomenon that marks the fifteenth of the month.

### Land vs. Sea

The *gemara* then raises a difficulty. If this phenomenon marks the fifteenth, yet *chametz* must be destroyed on the fourteenth, why did Rav Nachman give this marker to seafarers? The *gemara* resolves this: *ledidu demaglu lehu alma* — for those on land, where the horizon is clear and visibility is enhanced, this phenomenon is actually observable on the fourteenth. The geometry of visibility from land versus sea creates a slight difference in how the positions of sun and moon appear relative to the horizon. For those at sea, the marker occurs on the fifteenth; for those on land with a clear, open horizon, effectively on the fourteenth. The *gemara* does not fully resolve the precise optical explanation, and the details of how enhanced horizon visibility shifts the apparent timing remain somewhat unclear.

## Shabbat Override

### Nissan & Tishrei

The *mishna* states: *al shenei chadashim mechalelin et haShabbos* — for two months, witnesses may violate *Shabbos* in order to testify about the new moon. Those two months are *Nissan* and *Tishrei*. For all other months, while witnesses might in principle come forward, the urgency that would justify overriding *Shabbos* applies only to these two.

### Festival Dependency

The reason is that *Nissan* and *Tishrei* are the hinge upon which all the *Yamim Tovim* turn. The *mishna* notes: *shebahen shluchim yotzim leSurya uvehen metaknin et hamoadot* — for these months the messengers go out to the surrounding lands, and the entire festival calendar is organized around them. *Pesach* depends on the correct dating of *Nissan*; *Rosh Hashanah*, *Yom Kippur*, and *Succos* all depend on *Tishrei*. For these months, the stakes of accurate testimony are high enough to override *Shabbos*.

### Formal Declaration

The *gemara* notes an apparent contradiction. The *mishna* says messengers go out for *Nissan* and *Tishrei*, implying not for other months. Yet an earlier *mishna* states that messengers go out for all six months. Abaye resolves this: for all months, *shluchim yotzim me'urta* — messengers can depart already at nightfall. If the preceding month was thirty days and no *Rosh Chodesh* was declared on the thirtieth, by nightfall one knows that the following day is *Rosh Chodesh*, and messengers may depart. But for *Nissan* and *Tishrei* — *ad sheyishme'u mipi Beis Din mekudash* — even when it is certain that today is *Rosh Chodesh*, messengers do not depart until they have explicitly heard the formal declaration of *mekudash* from *Beis Din*. This level of procedural rigor reflects the singular importance of these months.

### Verse Derivation

The *gemara* then asks: what is the scriptural basis for overriding *Shabbos* to testify about the new moon? The verse states: *eleh moadei Hashem asher tikre'u otam bemo'adam* — these are the appointed times of God, which you shall proclaim in their proper time. The imperative to proclaim the festivals at their correct time overrides *Shabbos* — *al keri'atam atah mechalel* — it is the act of declaring *Rosh Chodesh* itself that may override *Shabbos*. However, *v'ei atah mechalel al kiyuman* — one does not override *Shabbos* merely to ensure that the message reaches distant communities, i.e., the sending of messengers does not itself override *Shabbos*.

### Ben Zakkai's Takana

The *mishna* then records that when the *Beis Hamikdash* stood, witnesses would violate *Shabbos* to testify for all twelve months — *mechalelin af al kulam mipnei takanat hakorban* — because each month's *korban mussaf* depended on knowing the correct date. After the *Beis Hamikdash* was destroyed, Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai ruled that *Shabbos* may be violated for testimony only regarding *Nissan* and *Tishrei*: *vechi yesh korban?* — is there still a *korban*? Without the *Beis Hamikdash*, the weight that had attached to every month's testimony no longer exists, and only the two months with direct festival significance continue to justify the override.

### Post-Mikdash Reality

This *takana* of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai is often classified among his *takannot zekher lamikdash* — enactments made in memory of the Temple. But it is worth understanding his legislative program more precisely. Many of his rulings, including this one, are not so much attempts to preserve pre-Destruction practice as they are honest acknowledgments that the world has fundamentally changed. The earlier *mishna* on this daf noted that Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai also ruled that witnesses may be accepted even after *mincha* time — a leniency made possible precisely because there is no longer a *korban* whose preparation depends on immediate action. Similarly here: by restricting the *Shabbos* override to *Nissan* and *Tishrei*, he is not memorializing the *Mikdash* but responding to the new post-*Mikdash* reality in which only the festival calendar remains as a pressing halachic concern. The *takannot* of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai are thus better understood as legislation for a new era than as nostalgia for the one that was lost.
