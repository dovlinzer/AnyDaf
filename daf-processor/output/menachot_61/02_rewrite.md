# Menachot 61 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hagasha Overview

### Opening & Context

The daf opens in the middle of 61b, approximately fifteen lines before the lines widen, at the word *kemach*. We are in the midst of a series of *drashot* exploring how we know that a wide range of *menachot* require *hagasha* — the act of touching the *mincha* to the *mizbe'ach*. The *Torah* states this requirement explicitly in the opening chapters of Vayikra with respect to the standard voluntary *mincha*: *vihikrivah el hakohen vihigishah el hamizbe'ach* — the *kohen* brings it close to the *mizbe'ach* and touches it there. The *drashot* under discussion seek to extend that requirement beyond the *menachot* explicitly mentioned in those passages to encompass virtually every *mincha*.

### Major Exceptions

Two major exceptions emerge from this discussion. The first is *minchat nesachim*, the flour-offering that accompanies an animal *korban*: it does not come on its own account but is brought along as a companion to the animal offering. The second is *minchat kohanim*, which generates a dispute. Rabbi Shimon holds that *hagasha* applies only to a *mincha* from which *kmitza* is performed, while Rabbi Yehuda maintains that the criterion is simply that some portion of the *mincha* is placed on the *mizbe'ach*. Under Rabbi Yehuda's view, any *mincha* from which part goes onto the *mizbe'ach* is subject to *hagasha*.

### Conceptual Framework

It is useful to think of *hagasha* conceptually — not as a strict legal equivalence, but as a functional analogue to *zrikat hadam* in animal *korbanot*. The *mincha* does not become fully sanctified through the touching; rather, the burning on the *mizbe'ach* is what validates the offering. Nevertheless, the parallel is suggestive: just as an animal *korban* involves *zrikat hadam* followed by burning the *eimurim* on the *mizbe'ach*, a *mincha* involves touching it to the *mizbe'ach* followed by burning the *kometz*. This parallel also explains why *hagasha* would be required only when some portion of the *mincha* itself will ultimately be placed on the *mizbe'ach*. If nothing from the *mincha* itself is going onto the fire — as in the case of *shtei halechem*, where only the *levonah* and the *kevasim* go on the *mizbe'ach*, or *lechem hapanim*, where again only the *levonah* is burned — then there is no *hagasha* performed on the *mincha* either.

## Vehiveisa Derasha

### Sotah Inclusion

We now pick up with Rabbi Yehuda's derivation. He learns from the word *vehiveisa* — "you shall bring this *mincha*" — to include *minchat sotah* in the requirement of *hagasha*. This stands in contrast to Rabbi Shimon, who derived the inclusion of *minchat sotah* from the word *vihikrivah*. Rabbi Yehuda's derivation is grounded in the verbal parallel: the same root *havi'ah* appears in the context of *sotah* — *vihivei es korbanah aleha* — establishing a linguistic connection that justifies applying *hagasha* there as well.

### Omer Logic

Rabbi Yehuda goes further, however, and argues that *minchat ha'omer* does not require a separate *pasuk* at all. It can be derived through logical inference alone. The reasoning is a *kal vachomer*: if *minchat chotei*, which lacks *shemen*, *levonah*, and *tnufa*, and is the most reduced form of *mincha* in every respect, nevertheless requires *hagasha* — then *minchat ha'omer*, which does require waving and does have *shemen* and *levonah*, certainly should require *hagasha*. The argument seems airtight.

### Shimon's Challenge

Yet the *Gemara* immediately challenges this inference. One could object: what is distinctive about *minchat chotei* that might explain why it gets *hagasha* despite its reduced status? It comes from wheat flour, whereas the *omer* comes from barley. Perhaps the wheat-flour nature of *minchat chotei* is the reason it receives *hagasha*, and the *omer*, being barley, would not. The *Gemara* responds: *minchat sotah* can serve as a counter-proof — it too is barley, and yet it requires *hagasha*. But then one could argue in the other direction: *minchat sotah* comes to clarify sin (*livarer avon*), which gives it a heightened significance that might explain its *hagasha*. Interestingly, the reasoning here shifts from technical ritual requirements to the functional role of the offering. Rabbi Shimon then says that the *kal vachomer* can still be constructed using the common denominator — *hatzad hashaveh shebahen* — that both *minchat chotei* and *minchat sotah* share: both require *kmitza* and both require *hagasha*.

### Frequency Debate

Rabbi Yehuda responds that from the common denominator one can include the *omer* as well, since it too requires *kmitza*. The *Gemara* then entertains Rabbi Shimon's potential challenge that *minchat chotei* and *minchat sotah* are common (*metzuyan*) in a way the *omer* is not: they can occur frequently across *klal Yisrael* depending on circumstances, whereas the *omer* comes only once a year. Rabbi Yehuda's counter is sharp: *adarabba* — the *omer* is actually *more* frequent in a reliable sense. These other offerings might never occur in a given year; the *omer* is guaranteed every single year. So the frequency argument actually favors the *omer*. In any case, this is largely an academic debate: everyone agrees that *minchat ha'omer* does in fact require *hagasha*. The disagreement is only whether a *pasuk* was needed to establish this or whether it follows from logic.

The *Gemara* then moves on to a secondary question about what else is learned from the word *vehiveisa*. One possibility is that it teaches the range of *menachot* subject to *hagasha*. Another possibility raised is that it extends the types of *mincha* a private individual may bring as a *nedava* — perhaps allowing a barley-flour *mincha* in parallel to the barley *omer* that the *tzibbur* brings obligatorily. The *Torah*'s use of the word *eleh* — "these" — forecloses that possibility, limiting voluntary *menachot* to the five types explicitly enumerated. The passage also addresses the case where someone declares generically "I will bring a *mincha*": the *pasuk*'s phrase *me'eleh* indicates that he may bring any one of the five, or all five if he wishes.

## Hagasha Parameters

### Gezeirah Shavah

We now turn to a more systematic analysis of which *menachot* require *hagasha* and which do not, approaching this through a *gezeirah shavah* and a process of inclusion and exclusion. The *braita* takes up this analysis under the formulation of Rabbi Shimon. The word *es hamincha* — "the *mincha*" — includes the word *es*, which serves to extend the requirement of *hagasha* to additional *menachot* beyond those explicitly mentioned, such as the *menachot* of non-Jews and of women. Here the *Gemara* notes — and this is curious — that there is no technical *halachic* distinction between a woman's *mincha* and a man's *mincha* in any other respect. The parallel distinction for women would arise with *tnufa*, not with *hagasha*. The *Shita Mekubetzet* even suggests that the phrase referring to non-Jews and women may be a later gloss, and that the original text may have simply said *lerabos she'ar menachot*.

### Three Criteria

The *braita* then asks: why do we include other *menachot* but exclude *shtei halechem* and *lechem hapanim*? The answer given is *sheyesh meihem le'ishim* — because with all the included *menachot*, some portion of the *mincha* itself goes on the *mizbe'ach* fire. This is Rabbi Shimon's first criterion. His second criterion is *ba'ot biglal atzman* — they come on their own account, not as accompaniments to something else. His third criterion — and this is the distinctive one — is *yesh meihem la'kohanim*: the remainder must be eaten by the *kohanim*. This means that a *mincha* that is entirely burned on the *mizbe'ach*, with nothing left for the *kohanim*, also does not receive *hagasha* according to Rabbi Shimon. In summary, Rabbi Shimon holds that to require *hagasha*, a *mincha* must (1) come on its own account, (2) have some portion burned on the *mizbe'ach*, and (3) have the remainder eaten by *kohanim*.

### Exclusion Logic

The exclusion of *shtei halechem* and *lechem hapanim* follows directly from the first criterion. By *shtei halechem*, it is the blood of the *kevasim* that is thrown on the *mizbe'ach* and the lambs themselves that are burned — no part of the *mincha* itself is placed directly on the *mizbe'ach* fire. By *lechem hapanim*, it is the *levonah* that is burned on the *mizbe'ach*, not the bread itself. Since the *mincha* itself does not go onto the fire in either case, there is no *hagasha*.

One might question this reasoning: if *hagasha* functions as a quasi-*zrikat hadam* and the *mincha* is being given to God through the *levonah* burning, why not touch the *mincha* itself to the *mizbe'ach* as well? This raises a deeper question about what *hagasha* accomplishes. There are two distinct conceptual possibilities: either *hagasha* is understood as an act within the *hakravah* itself — a quasi-*zrikat hadam* that precedes the burning — or it is understood as a final *hachsharah*, a sanctification that qualifies the *mincha* to be offered on the *mizbe'ach*. Under the second understanding, since none of the *mincha* itself is going on the *mizbe'ach*, there is nothing to sanctify for that purpose, and *hagasha* is therefore inapplicable.

### Nesachim Exception

Turning to *minchat nesachim*: here the *mincha* is entirely burned on the *mizbe'ach*, so the first exclusionary criterion (*ein meihem le'ishim*) does not apply. However, it fails the second criterion — *she'einah ba'ah biglal atzmah* — it does not come on its own account. The conceptual point is that when one brings a *minchat nesachim*, the focus of the *hakravah* is the animal *korban*; the *mincha* is not the object of the offering but rather an accompaniment. One is being *makriv* the animal, and the *mincha* is burned on the *mizbe'ach* as part of what follows. Since the *mincha* is not itself the focus of the *hakravah*, it does not receive *hagasha*.

### Kohanim Debate

This brings us to the case that generates the real debate: *minchat kohanim* and *minchat kohen mashiach*. These *menachot* come on their own account (*ba'ot biglal atzman*) and are entirely burned on the *mizbe'ach*. Why, then, do they not require *hagasha*?

### Vehigishah Drasha

The *Torah* uses the word *vehigishah* — with a *mapik heh* — which is read as limiting: "only it, and not something else." This *drasha* is the textual basis for excluding *minchat kohanim*. But the *Gemara* immediately notes: this word is the very word teaching that *hagasha* is required at all — how can it simultaneously serve to limit the requirement? The answer is that the *Torah* could have written *vehigish*; the additional *heh* in *vehigishah* indicates both the requirement and the exclusion of something else. What remains to be answered is why specifically *minchat kohanim* is what gets excluded. Rabbi Shimon's answer is his third criterion: *she'ein meihem la'kohanim* — none of the *minchat kohanim* is eaten by *kohanim*, since it is entirely burned. Therefore it lacks the defining characteristic of the standard *mincha*, which is partially burned and partially eaten by *kohanim*.

It must be acknowledged that this third criterion is conceptually difficult. Whether one understands *hagasha* as a quasi-*zrikat hadam* or as a final *hachsharah*, it is hard to see why the division of the remainder between the *mizbe'ach* and the *kohanim* should be a prerequisite. A *mincha* entirely burned on the *mizbe'ach* would seem even more fully dedicated to God. Nevertheless, this is Rabbi Shimon's position, and the *Torah*'s limiting language of *vehigishah* provides the textual hook.

## Hagasha Technical (III)

### Kmitza Act

Having concluded the *drashot* on *hagasha*, the *Gemara* turns briefly to the end of the *pasuk* — the instruction that the *kohen* shall lift the *kometz* — to derive a technical detail about *kmitza*. One might have thought the *kohen* could use a vessel to gather the *kometz*. The *Torah* forecloses this by stating *be-kumtzo* — in his own hand. A *gezeirah shavah* from the word *haramah* used in Parashat Tzav, where it also speaks of lifting *be-kumtzo*, confirms that in both contexts the lifting must be done with the bare hand of the *kohen*, not with a vessel.

To summarize what *hagasha* requires: the *mincha* must come on its own account, some portion of it must be burned on the *mizbe'ach*, and — according to Rabbi Shimon — the remainder must be designated for *kohanim*. Rabbi Yehuda disputes the third criterion.

## Tnufa List

### Metzora Oil

Having completed the analysis of *hagasha*, the *Gemara* now moves to *tnufa* — waving. The *Mishnah* had indicated that some offerings require *hagasha*, some require *tnufa*, and some require both. A standard *mincha* does not require *tnufa*; conversely, most of what requires *tnufa* is not a *mincha* at all. The *Mishnah* now lists the things requiring *tnufa* without *hagasha*.

The first item is the *log shemen* of a *metzora* together with his *asham*. The purification process of the *metzora* involves bringing an *asham* and a *log* of oil on the eighth day of his purification. The relevant *pesukim* in the *parasha* of *metzora* describe the procedure: *ve'es log hashemen vehanif otam tnufa lifnei Hashem* — you wave them as a wave-offering before God. This is done while the lamb is still alive. Following the waving, the lamb is slaughtered and its blood is applied to the *metzora*'s ear, thumb, and toe, as is some of the oil.

Conceptually, the waving here appears to serve a designating function — setting apart both the *asham* and the *log* as the specific objects that will effect the *tahara* of the *metzora*. The *asham* is brought as a *korban* in the standard sense; its blood is *nizrak* and contributes to the purification. The *log* of oil, while not brought as a *korban* in the same technical sense, accompanies the *asham* and is waved together with it.

A practical question arises: how does one physically wave a live lamb together with a *log* of oil simultaneously? The *Gemara* will address this directly. The *b'dieved* position is that waving them separately also satisfies the requirement, but the *lechatchila* requirement is to wave them together. One might imagine that the *log* of oil, held in a vessel, could somehow be held together with the lamb while both arms are used. The *Gemara* will note that if each was waved separately, the obligation is nonetheless fulfilled.

### Bikkurim

The next item on the list is *bikkurim*, according to Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov. The *Torah* in Ki Tavo describes the *bikkurim* procedure: the *kohen* takes the basket from the owner's hand (*v'lakach hakohen et hatene miyadecha*) and places it before the *mizbe'ach* (*vehinicho lifnei mizbe'ach Hashem Elokecha*). The owner then recites the declaration (*Arami oved avi* etc.) and at the end the *Torah* says again *vehinnachto lifnei Hashem Elokecha* — you shall leave it before God — and bows down. Nowhere does the *Torah* explicitly say to wave the *bikkurim*. Yet Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov holds that *bikkurim* require *tnufa*, and his position is referenced by the *Mishnah* here.

It is worth noting that the *Mishnah*'s formulation — *kidvrei Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov*, "according to" Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov — reflects the fact that this was a known dispute: Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov's position had already been stated elsewhere, and the *Mishnah* here simply alludes to it. This may reflect something about the composition of such lists in *Mishnaic* literature, where composite thematic groupings sometimes incorporate cross-references to positions already recorded.

### Shlomim Parts

The third item is *imurei shalmei yachid* — the fats together with the breast (*chazeh*) and thigh (*shok*) of an individual's *shelamim*. This requirement is explicit in the *Torah*: *et chazeh hatnufah ve'et shok hatrumah al ishei hachalavim yavi'u l'hanif tnufah lifnei Hashem* — the breast and thigh are brought together with the fats and waved as a wave-offering before God. After the waving, the fats go on the *mizbe'ach* and the *kohanim* receive the breast and thigh.

The conceptual significance of the waving here is worth exploring. The question is how to characterize the relationship between the *kohanim*'s receiving the *chazeh* and *shok* and the act of *hakravah*. Is the *kohen*'s portion viewed as compensation for service (*chelef avodatam*), or is it conceived as given to God who then grants it to the *kohanim* — *mishulchan gavo'ah kazchu*? The act of waving the *chazeh* and *shok* together with the *chalavim* that will go on the *mizbe'ach* suggests the latter understanding: everything is bundled together as one presentation to God, with the fire receiving the fats and the *kohanim* receiving the remainder. This makes the *kohanim*'s eating more like the eating of the *mizbe'ach* — a giving to God — rather than a straightforward payment. It is notable that this waving applies specifically to *shelamim* and not to *olah* or other freewill offerings.

### Gender & Status

The *Mishnah* states: *echad anashim ve'echad nashim* — whether men or women, the *tnufa* of the *shelamim* applies equally. This is significant because *tnufa* involves a physical act with the *korban*, and we know from elsewhere that women do not perform *semicha*. The mention of women here is therefore not redundant but clarifies that this physical act is equally incumbent. The *Mishnah* then adds: *be-Yisrael velo b'acherim* — only for Jews and not for others. The straightforward reading is that the *korban* of a non-Jew does not require *tnufa*, even though we saw earlier that a non-Jew's *mincha* does require *hagasha*. The *Gemara*, however, will read this phrase differently, as we will see.

### Lechem & Lambs

The *Mishnah* then lists *shtei halechem* and *shnei kivsei Atzeret* — the two loaves and the two lambs of *Shavuot* — waved together. This is explicit in the *Torah*. There is no *hagasha* by *shtei halechem*, as we established: no part of the *mincha* itself is placed on the *mizbe'ach* directly.

### Physical Procedure

The *Mishnah* describes the procedure: the two loaves are placed on top of the two lambs while the lambs are alive. The *kohen* places his two hands underneath and waves the entire assemblage — forward and back, forward and back, and up and down. The *Torah*'s language *asher hunaf va'asher huram* — what was waved and what was lifted — establishes both the lateral and vertical motions. The connection to the waving of *lulav* is direct and significant: the same motions of forward, back, and up and down characterize both. The *Gemara* will make this connection explicit and tie these wavings to agricultural symbolism — prayers for wind and rain, for a bountiful year. The *omer*, *shtei halechem*, and *bikkurim* form a natural grouping of wavings tied to the agricultural calendar, with the *lulav* waving at *Sukkot* completing the cycle.

The practical mechanics of placing bread on a live lamb and waving them together present obvious challenges. The *Gemara* will note that *b'dieved* waving them separately also works.

## Hagasha Technical (III)

### East vs West

The *Mishnah* concludes this section by distinguishing the locations of the two rituals: *tnufa hayeta bamizrach* — waving could take place in the east, anywhere within the *Azarah* from the moment one enters. *Hagasha bema'arav* — the touching to the *mizbe'ach* was performed in the west. The reason is the requirement of *lifnei Hashem*, understood as proximity to the *Heichal* and *Kodesh Kodashim*, combined with the requirement of *pnei hamizbe'ach* — the face of the *mizbe'ach*, which is the southern side. Together these two requirements converge on the southwest corner of the *mizbe'ach*. If both *tnufa* and *hagasha* are required — as with the *omer* and *minchat kna'ot* — the *tnufa* is performed first, and then the *hagasha*.

The *Mishnah* confirms: *minchat ha'omer* and *minchat kna'ot* require both *hagasha* and *tnufa*, since they are *menachot* from which some portion is burned on the *mizbe'ach*, and the *Torah* explicitly prescribes waving for both. *Lechem hapanim* and *minchat nesachim* require neither *hagasha* nor *tnufa* — *lechem hapanim* because none of the bread itself goes on the *mizbe'ach*, and *minchat nesachim* because it does not come on its own account.

## Three-Part Pattern

### Summary Framework

Rabbi Shimon offers an elegant summary observation: there are three categories of offerings, and across these three categories, three *mitzvot* apply — *semicha*, *tnufa* while alive, and *tnufa* after slaughter — but each category receives only two of the three. No single category receives all three.

### Shlomim Pattern

The *shelamim* of an individual (*zivchei shalmei yachid*) receives *semicha* while alive, and *tnufa* after slaughter (of the *chazeh*, *shok*, and *chalavim*). It does not receive *tnufa* while alive.

### Tzibbur Pattern

The *shelamim* of the *tzibbur* — the two lambs of *Shavuot* (*zivchei shalmei tzibbur*) — receive *tnufa* both while alive and after slaughter. They do not receive *semicha*, because *semicha* is not performed on communal sacrifices.

### Asham Pattern

The *asham metzora* receives *semicha* (because it is a personal offering) and *tnufa* while alive (waved together with the *log* of oil before slaughter). It does not receive *tnufa* after slaughter. The three categories thus each receive a different pair from among the three *mitzvot*, forming a clean and instructive pattern.

## Metzora Waving

### Joint Waving

A *braita* now elaborates on the waving of the *metzora*'s *asham* and *log*. The *pasuk* states *vehikriv oto le'asham ve'es log hashemen vehanif otam tnufa* — the word *otam*, "them," in the plural, teaches that they are waved together, *ke'echad*, as one.

### Separate Waving

Yet the *braita* also derives that waving them separately — each one individually — nonetheless fulfills the obligation *b'dieved*. This is derived from the word *vehenif* alone, understood as if it stood independently before *otam*: even a single *tnufa* on one of them at a time is valid after the fact. This is a striking *chiddush*. One might have thought that the entire purpose of the joint waving was to link the *log* to the *asham*, designating them together as the instruments of the *metzora*'s *tahara*. If so, waving them separately would entirely defeat the purpose. Nevertheless, the *braita* rules that separate waving is valid *b'dieved*.

### Singular Tnufa

Finally, the *braita* excludes the possibility that one should wave them individually and then again together. The word *tnufa* is in the singular (*tnufa*, not *tnufot*), indicating one act of waving, not multiple.

## Hagasha Technical (III)…

### Yesod Requirement

The *Gemara* now establishes that *hagasha* requires a *yesod* — the foundation of the *mizbe'ach*. The *Gemara* connects this requirement to the model of *zrikat hadam*: *zrikat hadam* can only be performed at a corner of the *mizbe'ach* that has a *yesod* beneath it. That the *hagashat mincha* similarly requires a *yesod* underscores the conceptual identification of *hagasha* with *zrikat hadam*. Just as *zrikat hadam* is the act of presenting the blood to the *mizbe'ach* in a way that requires the *yesod*, so too *hagashat mincha* — as the presentation of the *mincha* to the *mizbe'ach* — requires the *yesod*. This strengthens the understanding of *hagasha* as the *mincha*'s functional analogue to *zrikat hadam*.

---

## Lifnei Hashem

### Lifnei Hashem?

The *Gemara* now examines the phrase *lifnei Hashem* in the context of *tnufa*. Given that *hagasha* is performed in the west — at the southwest corner — one might have assumed that *lifnei Hashem* necessarily means "to the west." Yet the *Mishnah* has stated that *tnufa* can be performed in the east. How then does *lifnei Hashem* apply to *tnufa* in the east?

### Chatat & Yesod

The *Gemara* resolves this by distinguishing the case of a *mincha* that is also called a *chatat*. In that case, the requirement of *yesod* drives the location to the southwest, since the southeast corner of the *mizbe'ach* had no *yesod*. Given the requirement of *pnei hamizbe'ach* (southern face) and the need for a *yesod*, the location must be the southwest corner. The *Gemara* is thus asserting something striking: *lifnei Hashem* does not by itself mean "west." It means within the *Azarah*. The sole reason *hagasha* is performed in the southwest is the *yesod* requirement — not because *lifnei Hashem* inherently mandates a westward location. For *tnufa*, where no *yesod* is required, *lifnei Hashem* is satisfied anywhere in the *Azarah*, including the east.

### Tosafot View

Tosafot is troubled by this conclusion. Numerous other contexts in the *Gemara* associate *lifnei Hashem* specifically with the west — with proximity to the *Kodesh* and *Kodesh Kodashim*. Tosafot does not have a fully satisfying resolution. Rashi offers an alternative explanation: *lifnei Hashem* in the context of *tnufa* is stated to establish that the waving must occur within the *Mikdash* — one might otherwise have thought that some of the accompanying activities could take place outside. The phrase does not specify any particular location within the *Azarah*; it simply requires that the *tnufa* occur there. On this reading, *lifnei Hashem* functions as a jurisdictional requirement, not a geographic one.

## Bikkurim Waving

### Elazar Identification

The *Gemara* now asks: who is Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov, and what is the source of his ruling that *bikkurim* require *tnufa*? A *braita* is cited: Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov states that *bikkurim* require *tnufa*. The *Gemara* then asks for the derivation.

### Yad-Yad Link

The derivation is a *gezeirah shavah* of *yad-yad* connecting *bikkurim* to *shelamim*. By *bikkurim*, the *Torah* writes *v'lakach hakohen et hatene miyadecha* — the *kohen* shall take the basket from your hand. By *shelamim*, the *Torah* writes *yadav tevi'ena* — his hands shall bring the offering. The shared root *yad* (hand) connects the two contexts, and since the *shelamim* context involves waving, so too does the *bikkurim* context.

### Hand Position

From this *gezeirah shavah*, additional details are derived. *Mah kan kohen af lehalan kohen* — just as by *bikkurim* the *kohen* is a central figure (he takes it from the owner's hand), so too in the waving of *shelamim* the *kohen* is involved. Conversely, *umah lehalan ba'alim af kan ba'alim* — just as by *shelamim* the owner brings the *chazeh* and *shok*, so too the waving of *bikkurim* involves the owner. The practical implementation: *kohen maniach yado tachat yedei ba'alim umenif* — the *kohen* places his hands beneath the owner's hands and they wave together. The positioning of the *kohen*'s hands beneath the owner's suggests that the owner is the primary waver, with the *kohen* supporting — which fits the model that these are the owner's *bikkurim*, his personal presentation to God.

Tosafot raises the concern of *chatzitza* — if the *kohen*'s hands are literally placed beneath and touching the owner's hands, might that constitute an interposition? Tosafot suggests an alternative physical arrangement: perhaps the owner's hands hold the top of the basket while the *kohen*'s hands support the bottom. However, this reading is challenged by the *Yerushalmi*'s discussion of the parallel case of *minchat sotah*, where the *Yerushalmi* grapples explicitly with the concern of physical contact between the *kohen* and the woman whose hands are being placed beneath his during *tnufa* — a concern that presupposes literal hand-to-hand contact. That discussion also explains why the *Gemara* might have difficulty with a woman performing *tnufa* if the hands are touching.

### Yehuda's View

The *Gemara* then raises the question: why attribute our *Mishnah*'s position on *bikkurim* to Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov? Rabbi Yehuda also holds that *bikkurim* require *tnufa*, deriving this from the word *vehinnachto* — "you shall place it." Rabbi Yehuda argues that since the *Torah* had already stated *vehinicho lifnei hamizbe'ach*, the second occurrence of *vehinnachto* cannot simply mean placement — it must mean something else, namely *tnufa*. This interpretation links the waving to the later stage of the *bikkurim* procedure, after the declaration of *Arami oved avi*, suggesting the waving occurs at that point.

### Preference Reasoning

The *Gemara* offers two reasons to prefer attributing the *Mishnah* to Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov rather than to Rabbi Yehuda. Rava states: *ho'il u'patach bo katuv techila* — since Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov's derivation comes from the earlier part of the *pasuk* (*v'lakach hakohen et hatene miyadecha*), it is preferable. This is not merely a question of sequence: the earlier *pasuk* also implies that the waving occurs at the beginning of the *bikkurim* presentation, before the declaration, rather than at the end — a substantive difference in practice. Rav Nachman bar Yitzhak adds a second reason: *ho'il v'rav gavra* — Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov's authority is particularly weighty. The principle that *mishnaso kav venaki* — his *mishnah* is measured and pure — means that we rule in accordance with Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov wherever he appears, making it natural to attribute the *Mishnah*'s ruling to him.
