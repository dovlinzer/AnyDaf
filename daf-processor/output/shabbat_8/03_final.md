# Shabbat 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav Chisda's 4×4 Rule

### Four-by-Four Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי כִּדְרַב חִסְדָּא
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: In the private domain, everyone agrees** that the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Ḥisda,** i.e., that the private domain is considered one entity filled from the ground to the sky.

The daf opens with the words *amar Abaye*, picking up directly from the discussion that had unfolded on 7b. The issue at hand was Rav Chisda's statement revisiting the question of whether an object must be placed on a space of four by four *tefachim* in order for that placement to constitute a valid *hanacha*, or whether the object must be taken from such a space in order for the removal to constitute a valid *akira*. After the extended treatment of what constitutes *reshut hayachid*, *reshut harabim*, and so on, Rav Chisda had ruled that if someone threw an object from *reshut harabim* and it landed on top of a flagpole standing in *reshut hayachid*, the person is *chayav* — even though the top of the pole does not measure four by four. This seems to indicate that, at least for *reshut hayachid* and at least for *hanacha*, Rav Chisda does not require a four-by-four surface.

### Tosafot: Extended Walls

One way of understanding Rav Chisda's ruling is the explanation offered by Tosafot, which builds on an idea encountered a few days earlier: when one has a roof over a *reshut hayachid*, we conceptually treat the enclosed space as filled, as though everything within it rests on the ground. Tosafot extends this logic even without a roof. Since we imagine the walls of a *reshut hayachid* as extending upward indefinitely, any object coming to rest within that vertical column is considered to be within the *reshut hayachid* — and therefore does not require a four-by-four surface beneath it.

### Rabbi on Ziz Protrusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכָא — בְּאִילָן הָעוֹמֵד בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד וְנוֹפוֹ נוֹטֶה לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְזָרַק וְנָח אַנּוֹפוֹ, דְּרַבִּי סָבַר אָמְרִינַן "שְׁדִי נוֹפוֹ בָּתַר עִיקָּרוֹ". וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי לָא אָמְרִינַן "שְׁדִי נוֹפוֹ בָּתַר עִיקָּרוֹ".
>
> **Translation:** **However, here** this *baraita* is referring to a special case involving **a tree standing in the private domain and its boughs lean into the public domain, and** one **threw** an object from the public domain **and it rested** upon **the boughs** of the tree. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds** that **we say: Cast its boughs after its trunk.** The tree's branches are considered an extension of its trunk, therefore the entire tree is considered a private domain, and one who throws onto it is liable. **And the** Rabbis **hold** that **we do not say: Cast its boughs after its trunk,** and therefore the boughs themselves are not considered to be a private domain, but rather an exempt domain, and one who throws atop them from the public domain is not liable.

The Gemara seeks to connect Rav Chisda's ruling to a statement by Rabbi (*Rebbe*) that appeared in an earlier discussion. Three lines from the bottom of 7b, the Gemara asks: *lema Rav Chisda d'amar keRebbe?* — perhaps Rav Chisda, who does not require a four-by-four surface (at least in *reshut hayachid*), is ruling in accordance with Rabbi's position. The *baraita* cited reads: *zarak venach al gabi ziz kolshehu* — if someone threw an object and it landed on a tiny protrusion (assumed to be a protrusion within a *reshut hayachid*), Rabbi rules the person is *chayav*, while the *chachamim* rule the person is exempt. The parenthetical implication follows: *alman lo ba'inan makom arba al arba* — you do not require a space of four by four, at least in *reshut hayachid*. This appears to be precisely Rav Chisda's point.

### Abaye's Concession

Abaye responds with a striking concession: the ruling is not about *reshut hayachid* at all in the sense Rav Chisda was discussing. Rather, when it comes to *reshut hayachid*, everyone agrees with Rav Chisda, including the *chachamim* who dispute Rabbi in that *baraita*. This is a significant shift, given that the entire *sugya* of earlier in the week was preoccupied with who could possibly hold that a four-by-four surface is not required. Here Abaye asserts that at least regarding *hanacha* in *reshut hayachid*, no four-by-four is required — and this is universally accepted. The debate in the *baraita*, then, concerns something else entirely, as Abaye goes on to explain.

## Tree Branch Query

### Trunk & Branch Setup

The case involves a tree whose trunk stands in *reshut hayachid* but whose branch extends out into *reshut harabim*. The scenario is: someone in *reshut harabim* throws an object and it lands on one of the branches. The object has come to rest on a branch that is physically located in *reshut harabim*, yet the trunk anchoring the entire tree is rooted in *reshut hayachid*.

### Rabbi vs. Rabbanan

The dispute between Rabbi and the Rabbanan in the *baraita* turns on precisely this question: *deRebbe savar amrinan shadin nofo batar ikaro* — Rabbi holds that we throw the branch after the trunk, meaning the branch is legally treated as part of the domain in which the trunk stands. *V'Rabbanan savri lo amrinan shadin nofo batar ikaro* — the Rabbanan hold that we do not apply this principle.

### Object Location Debate

What is at stake is the legal location of an object resting on the overhanging branch. If the branch is considered part of *reshut harabim* — which is where it physically sits — then the object is in *reshut harabim*. If, however, we follow Rabbi and treat the branch as part of *reshut hayachid* by virtue of its connection to the trunk, then the object is in *reshut hayachid*. This is the simplest and most natural reading of Abaye's reformulation, and it is in fact a general question that appears across multiple tractates: when a tree's trunk is in one domain and its branches extend into another, where is an object resting on those branches considered to be? A vivid parallel case — someone fleeing to a *city of refuge* who climbs onto a branch of a tree whose trunk stands within the city — raises exactly the same conceptual problem.

### Liability Consequences

The practical consequences for *hilchot Shabbat* are as follows. If the branch is in *reshut harabim* and the object on it is not resting on a four-by-four surface, the person is exempt — because in *reshut harabim*, a four-by-four surface is required. If, however, we follow Rabbi and treat the branch as part of *reshut hayachid* by virtue of the trunk's location, then the throw constituted carrying from *reshut harabim* into *reshut hayachid*, and no four-by-four is required for *hanacha* in *reshut hayachid* — making the person *chayav*. Alternatively, if the throw covered only two *amot*, not four: if the branch is *reshut harabim*, there is no violation at all; if the branch is considered *reshut hayachid*, the person has carried an object from *reshut harabim* into *reshut hayachid* and is *chayav*. The domain classification of the branch thus determines liability independently of the four-by-four question.

### Cities of Refuge Analogy

This same legal question — whether an object or person on an overhanging branch is considered to be in the domain of the trunk or in the domain of the branch's physical location — is addressed in several other contexts in the Talmud. The *arei miklat* case is particularly vivid: if someone fleeing a *goel hadam* climbs onto a branch that extends outside the city of refuge, while the tree's trunk stands within it, the question of whether that person is protected by the city's sanctuary turns on precisely this point.

### Rashi's Explanation

Rashi offers a different reading that, as noted, does not fit well with the language of the Gemara. According to Rashi, the question is not one of domain classification at all. Rather, the branch is not four by four, and the question is whether its connection to the trunk lends it sufficient significance — as though it were four by four. Rashi effectively reduces the dispute to a variation on the four-by-four question itself. However, this reading sits poorly with the Gemara's emphasis on the trunk being in *reshut hayachid* and the branch extending into *reshut harabim*, and with the formulation *shadin nofo batar ikaro*, which speaks of following the branch toward the root — not of borrowing significance from the root. The simple sense of the Gemara is clearly as presented above.

## Beehive Domain Rules

### Beehive Parameters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: זָרַק כַּוֶּורֶת לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, גְּבוֹהָה עֲשָׂרָה וְאֵינָהּ רְחָבָה שִׁשָּׁה — חַיָּיב. רְחָבָה שִׁשָּׁה — פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: One who threw a round** reed **barrel into the public domain,** and the barrel is **ten** handbreadths **high and** its diameter **is not six** handbreadths **wide, is liable.** Since its diameter is less than six handbreadths, its area can fit the area of four handbreadths squared. Therefore, this barrel is considered an object, and if he threw it from the private domain to the public domain he is liable. However, if the diameter of the barrel was **six** handbreadths **wide, he is exempt.** Since the area of the barrel is greater than the area of four handbreadths squared, it is considered an independent private domain, and he did not perform an act of throwing an object from one domain to another domain.

Abaye introduces a new case: *zarak kaveret li-reshut ha-rabim* — you threw a beehive into *reshut harabim*. The beehive is ten *tefachim* high but not six *tefachim* wide. In that case, *chayav*. If it is six *tefachim* wide, *patur*. The beehive is a vessel shaped like a cylinder. The question is whether it constitutes a *reshut hayachid*.

### Geometry & Diameter

A *reshut hayachid* must measure four *tefachim* by four *tefachim* — not in one direction, but as a square area. For a circular object, the relevant question is what diameter is needed to contain a four-by-four square. By basic geometry, the diagonal of a four-by-four square equals four times the square root of two, which is approximately 5.6. Abaye rounds this to six, as Rashi notes. So: if the beehive is less than approximately 5.6 *tefachim* in diameter, the interior does not contain the requisite four-by-four area, and it does not qualify as a *reshut hayachid*. If it reaches or exceeds that diameter, the hollow interior — bounded by walls and standing ten *tefachim* high — constitutes a *reshut hayachid*.

### Rashi: Objects vs. Domains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ אֵינָהּ רְחָבָה שִׁשָּׁה — פָּטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? — אִי אֶפְשָׁר לִקְרוּמִיּוֹת שֶׁל קָנֶה שֶׁלֹּא יַעֲלוּ לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Even if it was not six** handbreadths **wide he is exempt. What is the reason** for this? He is exempt because **it is impossible that the ends of the reeds** protruding from the weave of the barrel **will not extend above ten** handbreadths. Consequently, the entire barrel never entered the public domain, as part of it remains in a non-liable place, i.e., ten handbreadths off the ground of the public domain.

Rashi explains the ruling as follows: the Torah obligates one for carrying *objects*, not for carrying *domains*. You carry within a domain; you do not carry a domain itself. If the beehive meets the parameters of a *reshut hayachid* — ten *tefachim* high and four by four in area — it is classified as a domain rather than an object, and one is therefore not *chayav* for throwing it. Once it is smaller than those dimensions, it is simply an object, and carrying it across four *amot* in *reshut harabim* constitutes a full violation.

### Tosafot: Domain Transfer

Tosafot offers a slightly different explanation. The exemption, says Tosafot, is not because the beehive-as-domain is categorically different from an object. Rather, when the beehive lands in *reshut harabim*, it defines its own interior as a *reshut hayachid*. This means the thrower carried from *reshut hayachid* and deposited the object into what, upon landing, became a new *reshut hayachid*. One is not *chayav* for carrying from *reshut hayachid* to *reshut hayachid*. If the beehive is smaller — not meeting the dimensional threshold — then when it lands, its interior does not constitute a *reshut hayachid*, the object has been deposited in *reshut harabim*, and the person is *chayav*.

### Rava's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּפָאָהּ עַל פִּיהָ, שִׁבְעָה וּמַשֶּׁהוּ — חַיָּיב. שִׁבְעָה וּמֶחֱצָה — פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** If **he turned** the barrel that is less than six handbreadths wide **over on its mouth,** i.e., if he threw it with its mouth facing down, even if the barrel was only **seven** handbreadths **and a bit** high, **he is** still **liable,** as the legal status of this barrel is equivalent to that of any other object that lands there. However, if the height of this barrel was **seven and a half** handbreadths, he is **exempt.** Within three handbreadths of the ground, the principle of *lavud* takes effect: An object within three handbreadths of the ground has the legal status of being connected to the ground. The sides of the barrel extend to the ground and then it is considered as if the barrel already touched the ground of the public domain, even though it is actually still three handbreadths away, while its upper part remains an exempt domain. It is as if this was a barrel higher than ten handbreadths.

Rava introduces a further consideration: *afilu eina rechava vav patur* — even if the beehive is not six *tefachim* wide, the person may be exempt. Why? *Ee efshar lekromei shel kaneh shelo yaalu lemala measura* — it is impossible for a beehive made of reeds not to have some small twigs or protrusions that extend above ten *tefachim*. Since *reshut harabim* extends only up to ten *tefachim*, any portion of an object above that height is outside *reshut harabim*. As established earlier in the tractate, if part of an object rests in one domain while another part is in a different domain, the carrier is exempt. Abaye presumably regards those small twigs as insignificant, but Rava maintains that even minor protrusions suffice to take the object outside the parameters of *reshut harabim* entirely.

This principle has a striking implication: virtually any object of significant height, when placed in *reshut harabim*, will have part of itself extending above ten *tefachim* and will thus fall outside *reshut harabim*. One would, on Rava's view, essentially never be *chayav* for carrying a tall object in *reshut harabim*.

### Lavud & Height Math

The Gemara then turns to a variant of the case: *kafa al piha* — the mouth of the beehive is facing downward. In this configuration, the threshold shifts from ten *tefachim* to seven: *shiva umashehu chayav, shiva umechetza patur*.

The reason involves the principle of *lavud*. When an open end of a structure is within three *tefachim* of the ground, we conceptually treat it as though it is already connected to the ground. When the mouth of the beehive faces downward, the open walls are oriented toward the ground, and we can therefore apply *lavud* to extend those walls to meet the ground — treating the object as if it has already come to rest. If the beehive is just over seven *tefachim* tall — say, 7.01 *tefachim* — then when it descends to within three *tefachim* of the ground (that is, when there is 2.99 *tefachim* remaining), *lavud* kicks in and the walls are conceptually extended. The object is treated as having landed, and its total effective height is 7.01 + 2.99 = 10 *tefachim* exactly, still fully within *reshut harabim*. The person is *chayav*.

But if the beehive is 7.5 *tefachim* tall, when it descends to within three *tefachim* of the ground, *lavud* treats it as already landed — yet 0.5 *tefachim* of the object still extends above ten *tefachim*, placing part of the object outside *reshut harabim*. The person is therefore *patur*. Remarkably, even as the beehive continues falling and eventually the full object is below ten *tefachim*, Rashi maintains that since at the moment of the conceptual *hanacha* (via *lavud*) the object was not fully within *reshut harabim*, the person remains *patur*.

It should be noted that *lavud* applies here precisely because the walls — open side facing downward — can be conceptually extended toward the ground. When the flat base faces downward, one cannot extend a floor via *lavud* in the same manner; *lavud* applies to the continuation of walls, not floors. That is why the threshold changes depending on the orientation of the beehive.

### Rav Ashi on Vessel Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ שִׁבְעָה וּמֶחֱצָה — חַיָּיב. מַאי טַעְמָא? — מְחִיצּוֹת לְתוֹכָן עֲשׂוּיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Even** if the height of the barrel was **seven and a half** handbreadths, **he is liable,** as the sides of the barrel are not considered to be higher than they are in reality. **What is the reason** for this? The reason is because **partitions are made** exclusively **for the inside** of the barrel. The sides of the barrel play no role beyond the barrel itself, and therefore there is no room to extend the sides by means of the principle of *lavud*. Therefore, if the barrel itself is not higher than ten handbreadths, it is merely an object.

Rav Ashi disputes this entire analysis: *afilu sheva umachetza chayav*. Even if the beehive is seven and a half *tefachim* tall and the mouth faces down, the person is still *chayav*. His reasoning: *mechitzos letochan asuyos* — the walls of a vessel are made to serve the contents inside, not to function as proper partitions. Just as we do not extend the floor of a base-down beehive via *lavud* because a floor does not function as a wall, so too we do not apply *lavud* to the walls of a beehive even when the mouth faces downward, because those walls exist to contain the vessel's contents, not to serve as the structural *mechitzot* of a domain. Since they are vessel walls and not functional *reshut*-walls, *lavud* does not apply to them, and the object is treated as having landed only when it physically touches the ground — at which point it is fully within ten *tefachim* and the person is *chayav*.

## Usage & Domain Status

### Nine Tefachim Pillar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: עַמּוּד תִּשְׁעָה בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְרַבִּים מְכַתְּפִין עָלָיו, וְזָרַק וְנָח עַל גַּבָּיו — חַיָּיב. מַאי טַעְמָא? — פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה מִדְרָס דָּרְסִי לֵיהּ רַבִּים. מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה וְעַד תִּשְׁעָה — לָא מִדְרָס דָּרְסִי לֵיהּ וְלָא כַּתּוֹפֵי מְכַתְּפִי. תִּשְׁעָה וַדַּאי מְכַתְּפִין עִילָּוֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said: A pillar** that is **nine** handbreadths high, standing **in the public domain, and many** people adjust the burden on their **shoulders upon it, and one threw** an object from the private domain **and it rested atop** the pillar, **he is liable. What is the reason** for this? It is based on this principle: Anything protruding from the public domain: If it is **less than three** handbreadths off the ground, and the **multitudes step on it,** it is considered to be part of the ground. If it is **from three to nine** handbreadths, **they,** the multitudes, **neither step on it nor** adjust the burden on their **shoulders** on it, and it is not considered part of the public domain. However, a protrusion **nine** handbreadths high, **certainly** the multitudes adjust the burden on their **shoulders on it.** Since the multitudes utilize it, it is considered a public domain, despite its height.

The Gemara introduces a new case from Ulla: a pillar nine *tefachim* high in *reshut harabim*, upon which the masses regularly arrange and rearrange their loads (*verabim mekatfin aleha*). If someone throws an object four *amot* and it lands on top of that pillar, the person is *chayav*. What makes the top of a nine-*tefachim* pillar part of *reshut harabim*?

### Height-Based Usage Tiers

The Gemara articulates a framework of height-based usage tiers. Below three *tefachim*: people walk on it — unless it is something like a thornbush or excrement that people naturally avoid, anything within three *tefachim* of the ground is functionally walked upon and is considered structurally part of the ground and thus part of *reshut harabim*. From three to nine *tefachim*: the space is too high to be walked on but too low for convenient use — this range is a kind of dead zone, neither incorporated into the ground nor used for placing items. At nine *tefachim*: just the right height for people to set down and rearrange loads on their shoulders — a convenient working surface, like a changing table in a public space. Because the masses use it this way, it has the status of *reshut harabim* even though it is not structurally part of the ground. Things in the three-to-nine-*tefachim* range, by contrast, will be either a *karmelit* (if four by four) or a *makom patur* (if less than four by four).

The critical principle here is that usage — not structural connection to the ground — is the primary definitional criterion for *reshut harabim*. Even airspace that is not physically connected to the ground can carry the status of *reshut harabim* if it is genuinely used by the public in the manner characteristic of *reshut harabim*.

### Hole in Ground Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: גּוּמָּא מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְכֵן בְּגוּמָּא. רָבָא אָמַר: בְּגוּמָּא לָא. מַאי טַעְמָא? — תַּשְׁמִישׁ עַל יְדֵי הַדְּחָק לָא שְׁמֵיהּ תַּשְׁמִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Based on Ulla's statement, **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: A hole** in the ground of the public domain, which is several handbreadths deep, **what is** its legal status? Is it also considered, in accordance with Ulla's principle, part of the public domain? In general, with regard to the *halakhot* of Shabbat, there is no distinction between an area elevated above its surroundings and an area depressed below its surroundings. Rav Yosef **said to him: And the same** is true **in a hole;** these *halakhot* apply. **Rava said: In a hole,** these *halakhot* do **not** apply. **What is the reason** for this? Since **use under duress is not considered use,** and the use of a pit even if it is nine handbreadths deep is inconvenient, and it is not comparable to a pillar of the same height.

Abaye asks Rav Yosef: *guma mai?* — what about a hole in the ground? Yesterday's discussion had addressed holes in walls; now the question is whether holes in the ground are similarly considered part of *reshut harabim* if people use them. A hole in the wall can be imagined as a convenient platform for placing objects; a hole in the ground is considerably harder to use conveniently. The question maps directly onto Abaye and Rava's debate from the previous day about holes in walls. There, Abaye held that even less-than-convenient use suffices to make something part of *reshut harabim*, while Rava held that the standard is higher. Abaye's question about holes in the ground reflects his more permissive position; Rava's higher threshold would presumably exclude such holes. Tosafot and Rashi disagree about the parameters of the hole under discussion — Rashi speaks of a hole nine *tefachim* deep (by analogy to the pillar), while Tosafot argues that for holes in the ground, shallower holes are more accessible and therefore more eligible for *reshut harabim* status.

### Basket as Domain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר מַתְנָא לְרָבָא: הָיְתָה קוּפָּתוֹ מוּנַּחַת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים גְּבוֹהָה עֲשָׂרָה וּרְחָבָה אַרְבָּעָה — אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין לֹא מִתּוֹכָהּ לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְלֹא מֵרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים לְתוֹכָהּ. פָּחוֹת מִכֵּן — מְטַלְטְלִין. וְכֵן בְּגוּמָּא. מַאי לָאו אַסֵּיפָא: לָא, אַרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Mattana raised an objection to Rava's** opinion from that which was taught in a *baraita*: **One whose basket was placed in the public domain** and it was **ten** handbreadths **high and four wide, one may neither move** an object **from it to the public domain nor from the public domain to it,** since its legal status is that of a private domain. If it were **less than that** height, **one may carry** from it to the public domain and vice versa. The *baraita* adds: **And the same** is true **for a hole. Is this** statement **not** referring **to the latter** clause of the *baraita*: One may carry from a pit which is less than ten handbreadths deep to the public domain? This supports the opinion of Rav Yosef, that a hole is subsumed within the public domain. Rava rejected this: This statement is **not** referring to the latter clause of the *baraita*, but rather **to the first** clause of the *baraita*: It is like a basket in that one may not carry from a hole ten handbreadths deep to the public domain because it is a full-fledged private domain. However, no conclusion may be drawn with regard to a hole less than ten handbreadths deep.

Rav Ada bar Matna challenges Rava with a further case: *haytah kuppa munachat birshus harabim, gevohah asarah u-revachah arba'ah* — a basket in *reshut harabim*, standing ten *tefachim* high and four *tefachim* wide. Because it is ten *tefachim* high and has a four-by-four interior, one would imagine the walls of the basket extending conceptually to the ground, making the interior a *reshut hayachid*. The ruling: *ein mataltelin lo mitochah lereshut harabim velo mershus harabim letochah* — one may not carry from the basket into *reshut harabim* or from *reshut harabim* into the basket. If it is smaller than those dimensions — less than ten high or less than four by four — it is simply a basket sitting in *reshut harabim*, and one may carry to and from it freely. Parallel reasoning is then applied to a hole: *ve-chein begummah*, and the same applies to a hole in the ground.

### Basket Resolution

The Gemara then asks whether the ruling on holes is a proof for Abaye's position. The suggestion is that if a small hole (less than ten *tefachim* deep and four by four wide) is treated as *reshut harabim*, that supports Abaye's view that holes count as *reshut harabim*. Rava responds that the ruling is not about the smaller hole at all — the comparison to the basket was only about the case that matches *reshut hayachid* dimensions (ten deep and four by four). For holes that don't meet those parameters, the Gemara is simply not asserting *reshut harabim* status; they may well be a *karmelit* or *makom patur* rather than *reshut harabim*. Rava thus deflects the challenge without conceding his position.

### Objects Don't = Domains

Tosafot raises the implicit question here: if a small basket or beehive could be classified as a *karmelit* when it doesn't meet *reshut hayachid* dimensions, then why did we say earlier (in the beehive case) that the person is simply *chayav* because it is an object — and not treat it as a *karmelit*? The answer is a significant principle: objects are not reclassified as *reshuyot* — not as a *karmelit*, not as anything else — unless they meet the full dimensional criteria for a genuine domain. A *karmelit* is itself a rabbinic category, not a biblical *reshut*, and we do not impose even that rabbinic domain-status on an ordinary object simply because it happens to have some enclosed space. Only when an object meets the full dimensions of *reshut hayachid* (ten *tefachim* high and four by four wide) do we treat it as a domain at all.

## Eruv in Pit

### Eruv Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְכַּוֵּון לִשְׁבּוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְהִנִּיחַ עֵירוּבוֹ בְּבוֹר לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב. לְמַטָּה מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — אֵין עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב.
>
> **Translation:** One who **intended to establish his Shabbat** abode **in the public domain** at a specific site must place food sufficient for two meals for that site to be considered his legal residence. **And** if **he placed** the food used for **his *eiruv* in a pit above ten handbreadths,** i.e., less than ten handbreadths below ground level, **his *eiruv* is an *eiruv*.** If he placed the *eiruv* **below ten handbreadths** from ground level**, his *eiruv* is not an *eiruv*.** Because the pit is a private domain and he may not carry the *eiruv* from that private domain to a public domain, where he has established his residence, the *eiruv* is invalid.

The next *sugya* introduces a scenario from the laws of *eruv techumin*. One is not permitted to travel more than 2,000 *amot* beyond city limits on *Shabbat*. However, if before *Shabbat* one deposits food at the edge of the permitted boundary, that food establishes one's *Shabbat* starting point there, effectively allowing travel 2,000 *amot* in the opposite direction as well. For this to work, the food must be accessible — in theory, were the person standing at that spot, they could reach and eat it. The case: *hamaniach eruvo bebor* — the person placed the *eruv* food inside a pit.

### Accessibility Problem

A problem arises immediately. If the pit is a *reshut hayachid*, the food is in *reshut hayachid*, while the person is standing in *reshut harabim*. In theory, the person cannot access the food without transgressing. Since the *eruv* requires that the food be accessible and edible at the moment *Shabbat* begins, this inaccessibility would seem to invalidate the *eruv*.

### Pit Depth Criterion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִילֵּימָא בְּבוֹר דְּאִית בֵּיהּ עֲשָׂרָה, וּ״לְמַעְלָה״ — דְּדַלִּאי וְאוֹתְבֵיהּ, וּ״לְמַטָּה״ דְּתַתִּאי וְאוֹתְבֵיהּ, מָה לִי לְמַעְלָה וּמָה לִי לְמַטָּה. הוּא בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד, וְעֵירוּבוֹ בְּמָקוֹם אַחֵר הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara seeks to clarify the details of this case. **What are the** exact **circumstances? If you say** that the *baraita* is referring **to a pit that has ten** handbreadths in depth and the phrase: And he placed it **above** ten handbreadths, means **that he raised** the *eiruv* **and placed it** within ten handbreadths of ground level, and the phrase: **Below** ten handbreadths, means **that he lowered** the *eiruv* **and placed it** ten handbreadths or more below ground level, **what** difference does it make **to me** if the *eiruv* is **above** ten handbreadths **and what** difference does it make **to me** if it is **below** ten handbreadths? In any case, the pit is a private domain, and the principle states that the private domain extends from its lowest point to the sky. There is no difference whether the *eiruv* was placed higher or lower. In any case, **he is in one place,** in the public domain, **and his *eiruv* is in another place,** in the private domain. Since he cannot take the *eiruv* out of the pit, his *eiruv* is not an *eiruv*.

The Gemara explains that the ruling depends on the depth of the pit. If the pit is less than ten *tefachim* deep, it is not a *reshut hayachid* — the *eruv* works. If it is deeper than ten *tefachim*, it is a *reshut hayachid* — the *eruv* does not work. The Gemara then asks: what does it mean to say the food is "above" or "below" ten *tefachim*? If the pit is always ten *tefachim* deep (and thus always a *reshut hayachid*), then whether the food is placed near the top or near the bottom of the pit makes no difference — it is still in *reshut hayachid* either way, and the person cannot access it. *Ma li lemala ma li lemata* — what does it matter if the food is high or low? *Hu bemakom echad veeruvo bemakom acher* — the person is in one domain and the food in another, regardless of food placement. The Gemara therefore concludes that "above" and "below" refer to the depth of the pit itself, not the placement of the food within it.

### Beraita Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו בְּבוֹר דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ עֲשָׂרָה, וְקָתָנֵי עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב. אַלְמָא תַּשְׁמִישׁ עַל יְדֵי הַדְּחָק שְׁמֵיהּ תַּשְׁמִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** is the *baraita* **not** referring to a pit **that does not have in it** a depth of **ten** handbreadths? And the *baraita* should be understood as follows: If he placed his *eiruv* below ten handbreadths, refers to a pit whose lowest point is ten handbreadths or more below ground level. If he placed his *eiruv* above ten handbreadths, refers to a pit that is less than ten handbreadths deep and is not a private domain. **And,** with regard to that case**, it was taught** that **his *eiruv* is an *eiruv*. Consequently, usage under duress** in a pit that is less than ten handbreadths deep **is considered usage,** and a pit of that kind is a full-fledged part of the public domain.

This reading seems to be confirmed by a *baraita*. If the pit is less than ten *tefachim*, the *eruv* works — implying that the food, though in a pit, is accessible. But this raises a problem for Rava's position: even a pit less than ten *tefachim* would, on Rava's view, be a *karmelit* — and carrying from a *karmelit* to *reshut harabim* is rabbinically forbidden. So how can the *eruv* be valid if the food is in a *karmelit* and the person is in *reshut harabim*?

### Rava's Drabanan Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זִמְנִין מְשַׁנֵּי לֵיהּ הוּא וְעֵירוּבוֹ בְּכַרְמְלִית, וְאַמַּאי קָרֵי לַהּ ״רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים״ — לְפִי שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד. וְזִמְנִין מְשַׁנֵּי לֵיהּ, הוּא בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְעֵירוּבוֹ בְּכַרְמְלִית. וְרַבִּי הִיא, דְּאָמַר כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁהוּא מִשּׁוּם שְׁבוּת, לֹא גָּזְרוּ עָלָיו בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Rava suggested various responses to this objection. **At times he would answer him** that it is referring to a case where both **he and his *eiruv* are in a *karmelit*,** i.e., that he intended to establish residence in a *karmelit* and placed his *eiruv* there. The pit is less than ten handbreadths deep, and consequently, both he and his *eiruv* are in the same domain. **And why does** the *baraita* **call** his place of residence **the public domain? Because it is not the private domain. And at times he would answer him** that it is referring to a case where **he was,** indeed, **in the public domain and his *eiruv* was in a *karmelit*,** as a pit that is not ten handbreadths deep is not part of the public domain, rather it is a *karmelit*. With regard to the question, how can this be considered a legitimate *eiruv* as it is forbidden to carry from a *karmelit* to a public domain as well, this *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi**, who said: Anything that** is prohibited on Shabbat and its prohibition is not by Torah law, rather it **is due to a rabbinic decree [*shevut*],** the Sages **did not issue the decree** to apply **during twilight,** which is neither definitive day nor definitive night. Consequently, at the time that the *eiruv* was placed in the *karmelit* it was permissible for him to carry it to the public domain. Since an *eiruv* takes effect even if it is fit for use just one moment during twilight on Shabbat eve, his *eiruv* is effective.

Rava responds by invoking the principle cited by Rabbi: *kol davar she-hu mishum shevut lo gazru alav bein ha-shmashot* — rabbinic prohibitions (*shevut*) were not extended to apply during the period of *bein hashmashot* (the twilight interval between sunset and nightfall). The food must be accessible only during *bein hashmashot*, when *Shabbat* takes effect. If the only obstacle to accessing the food is a rabbinic prohibition (carrying from a *karmelit* to *reshut harabim*), that prohibition does not apply during *bein hashmashot*. Therefore the food is considered legally accessible, and the *eruv* is valid — even if the pit is a *karmelit* rather than *reshut harabim*.

This principle has wide-ranging implications. Applying it broadly, one might think that any rabbinic prohibition could be set aside during *bein hashmashot*. The *poskim* limit the application with two qualifications: first, the act must serve some legitimate *tzorech mitzvah*, not be a casual violation; second, one must not have already accepted *Shabbat* (through candle-lighting or *kabbalat Shabbat*), since that acceptance defines the moment as *Shabbat* regardless of the astronomical time. The principle can nonetheless be practically significant — for instance, for someone stranded outside their home as *Shabbat* begins.

### Rava Reasserts Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא תֵּימָא דַּחוֹיֵי קָא מְדַחֵינָא לָךְ, אֶלָּא דַּוְקָא קָאָמֵינָא לָךְ. דִּתְנַן: אִם הָיָה רְקָק מַיִם וּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים מְהַלֶּכֶת בּוֹ, הַזּוֹרֵק לְתוֹכָהּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — חַיָּיב. וְכַמָּה הוּא רְקָק מַיִם — פָּחוֹת מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים. וּרְקָק מַיִם שֶׁרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים מְהַלֶּכֶת בּוֹ, הַזּוֹרֵק לְתוֹכוֹ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rava said to Rav Adda bar Mattana: **Do not say that I am just putting you off** with these answers. **Rather,** what **I am saying to you** is **accurate.** The opinion that usage under duress is not considered usage is a bona fide opinion and the suggested answers are appropriate explanations of that *baraita*. **As we learned** in a mishna: **If there was a swamp and the public domain passes through it, one who throws** an object **into it** at a distance of **four cubits is liable** just like anyone who carried four cubits in the public domain. **And how** deep **is** this **swamp?** It is **less than ten handbreadths.** The mishna adds: **And** with regard to **a swamp that the public domain passes through it, one who throws four cubits into** the swamp **is liable.**

The Gemara makes clear that Rava is not merely offering a deflection. *Ve-lo teima d'chuyei kamidachina lach* — do not think he is just brushing aside the challenge. Rava reaffirms that his position is substantive: the hole is a *karmelit*, not *reshut harabim*, and the *eruv* works not because the pit is *reshut harabim* (as Abaye would have it) but because the rabbinic prohibition on carrying from a *karmelit* is suspended during *bein hashmashot* under Rabbi's ruling. The *baraita* is therefore not a proof for Abaye.

## Usage Hierarchy

### Puddle Passage Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא ״רְקָק״ ״רְקָק״ תְּרֵי זִימְנֵי, חַד בְּימוֹת הַחַמָּה וְחַד בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים. וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בְּימוֹת הַחַמָּה, דַּעֲבִידִי אִינָשֵׁי לְקָרוֹרֵי נַפְשַׁיְהוּ, אֲבָל בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים — אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים, אַגַּב דְּמִטְּנִיף מִקְּרֵי וְנָחֵית, אֲבָל בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה — לָא. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The difficulty concerning the repetition of the same topic with virtually identical words is clear, and therefore: **Granted,** it is possible to explain, that **swamp swamp was repeated twice; one** case **is** referring to **the summer, and one** case **is** referring to **the rainy season. And it is necessary** to emphasize that this ruling is in effect both in the summer and in the winter. **As, had** the mishna **told us** this *halakha* only **in the summer,** we would have said that since **people commonly** pass through the swamp **to cool themselves,** it is considered part of the public domain. **However, in the rainy season I would have said** it is **not** part of the public domain. And conversely, **had** the mishna **told us** this *halakha* only **in the rainy season,** I would have said that **since he is filthy** anyway, **it happens that he** is not cautious and **enters** into the swamp. **However, in the summer,** when he is not dirty with mud, I would have said that it is **not** part of the public domain. Therefore, **it was necessary** for the mishna to repeat swamp twice, to teach us that this *halakha* applies at all times.

Rava now marshals a *baraita* from the laws of puddles to ground his position systematically. *Haya rekek mayim u'reshut harabim mahalachet bo* — if there is a puddle of water and the public pathway runs through it, *hazorek l'tocha arba amot chayav* — one who throws four *amot* through it is *chayav*. People walk through the puddle, so it retains *reshut harabim* status. The *baraita* repeats this formulation twice — once for a puddle in the summer and once in the rainy season. Each iteration is necessary: in summer, children may splash through puddles for enjoyment, so one might think the puddle counts because it is pleasurable to walk through. Perhaps in winter, when people avoid puddles, it would be different. Conversely, in winter, people walk through puddles because they are already dirty and indifferent, so perhaps in summer, when they would rather stay dry, the puddle would be avoided. *Tzricha* — both cases must be stated, and the upshot of both is the same: people walk through puddles in any season, and the puddle remains part of *reshut harabim*.

### Walking vs. Using

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הִילּוּךְ תְּרֵי זִימְנֵי לְמָה לִי? אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ הִילּוּךְ עַל יְדֵי הַדְּחָק — שְׁמֵיהּ הִילּוּךְ, תַּשְׁמִישׁ עַל יְדֵי הַדְּחָק — לָא שְׁמֵיהּ תַּשְׁמִישׁ. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **However, why do I need** the mishna to state **twice** that the public domain **passes** through that swamp? **Rather, shouldn't one conclude from this** that **passage**, even when it is **under duress,** and not free and easy, **is considered passage,** but **usage under duress is not considered usage?** It was necessary to emphasize that the public domain actually passes through it. If the multitudes do not pass through it and it was only used under duress, it would not have been considered a public domain. The Gemara concludes: Indeed **conclude from this.**

Rava draws a principled distinction from the double formulation. Why does the *baraita* twice emphasize that the *reshut harabim goes through* the puddle? Rava reads this as a deliberate statement: *hiluch al yedei hadachak shmei hiluch* — walking with some degree of difficulty is still called walking and counts as sufficient use to establish *reshut harabim* status. *Tashmish al yedei hadachak lo shmei tashmish* — but use of a non-walking kind, if accomplished only with difficulty, is not considered genuine use for purposes of *reshut harabim*.

### Primary Function Priority

The implication is that the primary function of *reshut harabim* is passage — movement, travel, walking. Those portions of the public domain that serve this primary function retain their *reshut harabim* status even when using them involves some inconvenience (walking through a puddle, stepping over an obstacle). But secondary uses — placing objects, storing goods — must be genuinely convenient to qualify. A pillar at exactly the right height for shoulder-rearranging counts because it is very easy to use. Holes in walls or holes in the ground, where reaching in and retrieving objects requires awkward effort, do not meet the threshold for a secondary use.

### Abaye-Rava Contrast

Abaye does not draw this distinction. For Abaye, any use — whether walking or otherwise — that is carried out, even somewhat inconveniently, suffices to give something *reshut harabim* status. Rava, by contrast, creates a two-tier system: walking uses are treated more leniently (even *hiluch al yedei hadachak* counts), while non-walking uses must be readily convenient. The articulation of this distinction is the conceptual contribution of this *sugya* to the broader discussion of domain definitions.

## Bundle in Acts

### Bundle Throwing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הַאי זִירְזָא דְּקָנֵי, רְמָא וְזַקְפֵיהּ רְמָא וְזַקְפֵיהּ — לָא מִיחַיַּיב עַד דְּעָקַר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Somewhat related to the case of the barrel discussed earlier which was a case of moving an object without liability, the Gemara cites that **Rav Yehuda said: That bundle of reeds that he stood upright and threw down, stood upright and threw down repeatedly, he is not liable** for carrying it four cubits in the public domain **until he lifts it** off the ground. As long as he did not lift it from the ground, even though he moved it a long way, he did not perform the acts of lifting and placing which are prohibited by Torah law, as at least one part of the bundle always remained on the ground.

Rav Yehuda presents a case: *hai zarza de'kanei* — a bundle of reeds. If one throws the bundle and stands it up in *reshut harabim* (*rama v'zakfeih*), one does not transgress.

### Separate Acts Doctrine

The reason: *lo mechayeiv ad d'akar leih* — one is not liable until one picks the bundle up as a whole. If the bundle is, say, five *amot* tall, one can carry it through *reshut harabim* without becoming liable — by moving one end out of the original four-*amot* zone, allowing it to come to rest, and then moving the other end. Each stage is a separate act. The first act is not *mechayeiv* because part of the bundle remains within the four-*amot* zone. The second act is a new act, and the first act has already been completed and its *melacha* concluded. The acts are distinct and therefore non-cumulative for purposes of *chiyyuv*.

### Carrying Tall Objects

The practical application is immediately evident: a very tall object can, in principle, be moved through *reshut harabim* in increments, each step less than four *amot*, with the whole object coming to rest between steps. Each individual act falls short of the threshold for a *de'oraita* violation, even though the net result is that the object has traveled a great distance.

### Baby Carriage Issue

Tosafot raises a significant practical question: what about a baby carriage? One never picks it up; one merely rolls it. Two separate questions arise. The first, addressed in a *Gemara* in *Ketubot*, is whether lifting is required for *akira*: the answer is no — dragging an object out of its place constitutes *akira* even without lifting. The second question, which Tosafot raises here, is whether continuous rolling (without any moment of rest) is more like the bundle case (separate acts, each under four *amot*) or more like continuous dragging. Because the baby carriage rolls without stopping, Tosafot argues this is a single continuous act — not a series of separate acts. The object started here and ended there, and the movement never broke.

### Dragging vs. Separate Acts

The contrast with dragging (*kovel*) clarifies the principle. When one drags an object continuously out of its original place — even without lifting — that constitutes *akira*, and the continuous motion through four *amot* in *reshut harabim* makes one *chayav*. The baby carriage, rolled continuously, is similarly one unbroken act. The bundle case is different: each segment of movement ends with the bundle at rest, constituting a genuine break between acts. Tosafot notes, but does not fully resolve, the apparent parallel to the case where someone stops to switch an object from one shoulder to the other — which the Gemara elsewhere rules does not count as a break. The precise line between a meaningful rest and a momentary pause for readjustment remains an open question.

## Ledge as Free Zone

### Ledge Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: אָדָם עוֹמֵד עַל הָאִסְקוּפָּה, נוֹטֵל מִבַּעַל הַבַּיִת וְנוֹתֵן לוֹ, נוֹטֵל מֵעָנִי וְנוֹתֵן לוֹ. הַאי אִסְקוּפָּה מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said: A person standing on the threshold may take** an object **from the homeowner** standing in the private domain **and may give** an object **to him.** Similarly, while standing there, **he may take** an object **from a poor person** standing in the public domain **and may give** an object **to him** because there is no element of prohibition or liability in carrying in and carrying out in an exempt domain on Shabbat. The Gemara asks: **This threshold, what** is it; to what type of threshold is it referring? Different thresholds have different halakhic status.

The Gemara turns to the *mishnah*'s case of the *iskofah* — a ledge or threshold projecting from a *reshut hayachid* out over *reshut harabim*, which does not measure four *tefachim* by four *tefachim*. A person can stand on this ledge on *Shabbat*.

### Ledge Function

The *mishnah* describes the ledge as a transfer point. A person standing there can take an object from the householder inside the *reshut hayachid* and give an object to the householder; alternatively, he can take an object from a poor person standing in the *reshut harabim* and give an object to the poor person. In other words, the ledge is used as an intermediary between the public and private domains.

### What Is the Ledge?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילֵּימָא אִסְקוּפַּת רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — נוֹטֵל מִבַּעַל הַבַּיִת? הָא מַפֵּיק מֵרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים! וְאֶלָּא אִסְקוּפַּת רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד — נוֹטֵל מִן הֶעָנִי? הָא קָא מְעַיֵּיל מֵרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים לִרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד! אֶלָּא אִסְקוּפַּת כַּרְמְלִית — נוֹטֵל וְנוֹתֵן לְכַתְּחִלָּה, סוֹף סוֹף אִיסּוּרָא מִיהָא אִיתָא!
>
> **Translation:** **If you say** that it is referring to **a threshold** that is **the public domain,** i.e., the threshold of an alleyway that is fewer than three handbreadths off the ground and is not covered, and the post that demarcates the parameters of the alleyway is situated between the public domain and the alleyway, how can the *Tosefta* say that **he may take** an object **from the homeowner? Isn't he carrying out from the private domain to the public domain?** **Rather,** say that the *Tosefta* is referring to **a threshold** that is **the private domain,** in a case where it is covered, or it is situated between the post that demarcates the parameters of the alleyway and the private domain, or it is ten handbreadths high and its area is at least four by four handbreadths. How then can the *Tosefta* say that **he may take** an object **from a poor person? Isn't he carrying in from the public domain to the private domain?** **Rather,** say that the *Tosefta* is referring to **a threshold** that is a ***karmelit*,** i.e., it is not ten handbreadths high and it is four by four handbreadths; how can the *Tosefta* say that **he may take and give** even ***ab initio*? Ultimately,** in this case, **there is nevertheless a prohibition.** Even though a *karmelit* does not engender liability by Torah law, carrying from it is prohibited by rabbinic law and is certainly not permitted *ab initio*.

The Gemara systematically eliminates possible domain classifications. If the ledge were part of *reshut harabim*, then taking something from the householder and placing it on the ledge would constitute transferring from *reshut hayachid* to *reshut harabim* — a biblical violation. If the ledge were part of *reshut hayachid*, then taking something from the poor person in *reshut harabim* and placing it on the ledge would constitute transferring from *reshut harabim* to *reshut hayachid* — also a biblical violation. Could it be a *karmelit*? Even so, carrying between a *karmelit* and the other domains is rabbinically forbidden, and the *mishnah* permits these transfers *lechatchilah* — explicitly and unreservedly. A *karmelit* would still carry a rabbinic prohibition. *Sof sof issura miha ika* — there would still be some prohibition.

### Makom Patur Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִסְקוּפָּה מְקוֹם פָּטוּר בְּעָלְמָא הוּא — כְּגוֹן דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ אַרְבָּעָה עַל אַרְבָּעָה. וְכִי הָא דְּכִי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מָקוֹם שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ אַרְבָּעָה עַל אַרְבָּעָה טְפָחִים — מוּתָּר לִבְנֵי רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד וְלִבְנֵי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים לְכַתֵּף עָלָיו, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יַחֲלִיפוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** say that the *Tosefta* is referring to **a threshold** that is **merely an exempt domain,** and therefore there is no prohibition at all. In what circumstances is it an exempt domain? **In a case where it does not have** an area of **four by four** handbreadths, and it is therefore not considered a domain with regard to liability on Shabbat. **And** that *halakha* is **similar to that** statement made **when Rav Dimi** came from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia and he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: A place that does not have** an area of **four by four handbreadths** and is set apart, **it is permissible for** both **the people of the private domain and for the people of the public domain to** adjust the burden on their **shoulders upon it** on Shabbat, **as long as they do not exchange** objects between them from one domain to the other domain.

The Gemara therefore concludes: *ella iskoppa makom p'tur b'alma hu* — the ledge is a *makom patur*, a fourth type of space, a free zone. The term *patur* here does not carry the usual implication of biblical exemption with rabbinic prohibition remaining; rather, *makom patur* is truly free — like free parking. Objects may be placed there from either domain, and taken from there into either domain, without any prohibition whatsoever.

### Makom Patur Criteria

The operative criterion is dimensional: *kegon d'leis bei arba al arba tefachim* — the space must be less than four *tefachim* by four *tefachim*. Any space of four by four *tefachim* has sufficient area to constitute a *reshut*, whether *reshut hayachid* (if ten *tefachim* high or enclosed) or *karmelit* (if in public space but not meeting full *reshut harabim* criteria). A space smaller than four by four *tefachim* is legally insignificant as a domain — just as an object must rest on a four-by-four surface to constitute *hanacha*, so a space must measure at least four by four to register as a *reshut*. Below that threshold, the space is a non-domain, and domain-based restrictions simply do not apply to it.

The one exception: if a space less than four by four is located within *reshut harabim* and is used by the public — like the nine-*tefachim* pillar discussed earlier — it is incorporated into *reshut harabim* by virtue of that use. Otherwise, if not used by the *rabim* and not otherwise enclosed within *reshut hayachid*, a sub-four-by-four space is *makom patur*.

### Dimi on Non-Domain Spaces

The *mishnah* also permits exchanges on the ledge in a specific manner. The closing rule of the *mishnah* states that a person may take from the householder in *reshut hayachid* and give to the poor person in *reshut harabim*. This is permissible because each movement is independent: taking from private domain to the free zone, then taking from the free zone to public domain — two separate acts, neither of which violates the prohibition of transferring between domains. The principle extends to all places without four-by-four measure: they may be freely used for placing objects by both the people of *reshut hayachid* and the people of *reshut harabim*, provided they are not used as a corridor for transferring between the two major domains. The *makom patur* is a neutral zone, not a transit point. Using it as an intermediate stop for a transfer between the two major domains would effectively accomplish what direct transfer would accomplish, and that remains prohibited.
