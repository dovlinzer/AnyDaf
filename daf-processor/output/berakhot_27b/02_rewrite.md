# Berakhot 27b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Prayer vs. Sacrifice

### Shemoneh Esrei & Avodah

The daf opens at the very bottom of 27a, but before proceeding, an observation from the preceding discussion is worth noting. The question arises whether the fact that *shemoneh esrei* opens with the word *avodah* — in the blessing *retzei* — constitutes an emphasis on *avodah* as such. This is a genuinely excellent point, and it connects to something even more striking that deserves attention: the words that were later added as a preface to *shemoneh esrei*, *hashem sefatai tiftach ufi yagid tehilatecha*, appear in *Tehillim* in a context where the very next *pasuk* reads *ki lo tachpotz olah va'zevach* — that God does not desire burnt offerings and sacrifices, but rather the words of our mouths and the intentions of our hearts. The preface to *shemoneh esrei* thus stands in exact contrast to sacrificial worship: *tefillah* is what God truly desires.

### Tefillah as Substitute

A second observation reinforces this point. The one *beracha* in *shemoneh esrei* that is ostensibly about *avodah* — *retzei hashem elokeinu be'amcha yisrael ubitfilatam* — never explicitly mentions sacrifices at all. It says *ubitfilatam*, "and with their prayer." It speaks of *vahashev ha'avodah*, returning the service, but the language remains abstract.

### Ishei Yisrael

Even the phrase *ve'ishei yisrael* — the most direct allusion to sacrifices within *retzei* — must be one of the most indirect and abstract ways of referring to sacrificial offerings in the entire liturgy. The deliberate absence of explicit sacrificial language from the very *beracha* that is supposed to correspond to *avodah* is striking. One is left to ask: how much are sacrifices really in *shemoneh esrei*, even where one would most expect them to be?

### Tannaim Debate

The broader picture that emerges from this discussion is that there are *tannaim* who view *korbanot* as the ideal and *tefillah* as a substitute or replacement, and there are other *tannaim* who feel strongly that *tefillot* are more desired by God than *korbanot*. These debates intensified in the period following the *churban habayit*, as the rabbinic world grappled with how to understand prayer in relation to a sacrificial system that no longer existed.

### Empty Ritual

However, it is important to note what the *pshat* of *Tanach* itself seems to be saying. The classic prophetic criticism of Israel's sacrificial practice is not that God would prefer verbal prayer in place of sacrifice. Rather, it is that God does not want empty sacrifice — sacrifice without sincerity, sacrifice divorced from ethical conduct. The prophetic ideal, in other words, is not *tefillah* instead of *korbanot*, but rather the vessel of sacrifice filled with genuine devotion and reflected in all aspects of human behavior. That said, this framing is still consistent with the view that verbal articulation can serve as a legitimate substitute — but with the caveat that *davening* itself means nothing if a person's behavior outside the synagogue does not reflect what he was doing inside it.

### Biblical Tefillah

It is equally important to observe that *tefillah* was never absent from Jewish life even when the *beit hamikdash* stood. From *avraham yitzchak ve'ya'akov* through *david hamelech*, who composed *tehillim* — which many scholars understand as essentially the first *siddur*, whether recited alongside the sacrificial service or by those distant from the *beit hamikdash* — the human need and experience of *tefillah* was always present. It is quite possible that *korbanot* and *tefillah* were never seen as simply two alternative modes of the same thing, but rather as qualitatively different, each serving its own distinct function: *korbanot* for *kappara* and certain forms of *avodah*, *tefillah* for personal connection and petition.

### Rambam's Position

The *rambam* in *Moreh Nevuchim* takes the view that *korbanot* were a concession to human nature rather than the ideal form of worship. But even within his legal writings, the *rambam*'s treatment of *tefillah* is also more reserved than one might expect — a matter for another time. One practical note before moving on: since the entire debate about *tefillah keneged korbanot* concerns the standard daily prayers, *musafim* fall outside that discussion entirely, which is precisely why the practice is to include the explicit mention of the *korban* in *mussaf*. Everybody agrees, as the *gemara* established previously, that *musaf* is *keneged korban*, and *musaf* is the one place in the liturgy where we explicitly name the sacrifice.

## Davening Behind Rebbe

### Davening Practice

The *gemara* now turns to the very last line of 27a. The *gemara* asks: *rabbi yirmeyah heichi avid hachi* — how did Rabbi Yirmeyah do what he did? The context is that Rabbi Yirmeyah had been *davening* behind Rav.

### L'olam al Yitpalel

The difficulty is immediate: Rav Yehudah teaches in the name of Rav — the very same Rav behind whom Rabbi Yirmeyah *davened* — that *l'olam al yitpalel adam lo keneged rabbo velo achorei rabbo*: a person should never pray beside his *rebbe* (which implies equality) nor behind his *rebbe* (which is problematic either because it appears disrespectful to face the back of one's teacher, or because it can look as though one is bowing down toward him). The irony is sharp: the very *rebbe* behind whom Rabbi Yirmeyah *davened* was the one who taught that one should not do so.

### Rabbi Eliezer

The *gemara* adds a *baraita*: Rabbi Eliezer says that one who *davens* behind his *rebbe*, and several other related behaviors, causes the *shechina* to depart from Israel.

### Five Violations

The *baraita* enumerates the violations: *hamitpalel achorei rabbo*, *vehanotan shalom l'rabbo*, *vehamachzir shalom l'rabbo*, *vecholek al yeshivato shel rabbo*, and *ha'omer davar shelo shama mipi rabbo* — one who *davens* behind his *rebbe*; one who greets his *rebbe* (without the appropriate honorific); one who returns a greeting to his *rebbe* (same concern); one who disagrees with the *halachic* positions of his *rebbe*; and one who says a teaching he did not hear directly from his *rebbe*.

Regarding *noten shalom l'rabbo*: Rashi and *tosafot* explain that the problem is not the greeting itself but the failure to use the honorific — saying *shalom aleichem* as if to a peer rather than as *shalom aleichem rabbi u'mori*. The *maharsha* notes that in another context in the *gemara*, a slave is not permitted to greet his master with *shalom aleichem* at all, given the extreme gulf in status; but Rashi and *tosafot* did not apply that principle here, understanding it as specific to that situation of master and slave. Here the violation is the manner of address, not the greeting itself.

### Shechina Departs

All five of these violations share a common consequence: *gorem lishchina shetistalek miyisrael* — they cause the *shechina* to depart from Israel.

### Talmid Chaver

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction regarding Rabbi Yirmeyah: *shani rabbi yirmeyah bar abba d'talmid chaver hava* — Rabbi Yirmeyah bar Abba was different because he was a *talmid chaver*, a student-colleague. He revered Rav as his teacher, but he occupied the status of a younger colleague, and therefore it was appropriate — or at least acceptable — for him to *daven* in closer proximity.

### Havdalah Question

### Second-Person Address

The proof that Rabbi Yirmeyah held this *talmid chaver* status comes from the way he addressed Rav. He asked Rav directly: *mi badalta* — "have you separated from *melacha*?" — meaning, having *davened* *maariv* early, did you thereby stop performing *melacha*? And Rav answered: *badilna* — "yes, I have." The proof of colleague status is the second-person form of address. A student speaking to a *rebbe* would have said *mi badil mar* — "has the master separated?" — using the third person out of deference. The fact that Rabbi Yirmeyah used the second person demonstrates that his relationship with Rav was that of a colleague, not merely a subordinate student.

## Conflicting Testimonies

### Eliezer Ideology

Returning briefly to one of the violations enumerated in the *baraita* — *ha'omer davar shelo shama mipi rabbo*, the prohibition against saying something one did not hear directly from one's *rebbe* — the *maharsha* correctly notes that this seems puzzling. Throughout *shas*, *amoraim* are constantly innovating, developing new ideas, saying things they never heard directly from their teachers. The most pointed answer is that this represents a distinctly *Rabbi Eliezer* position. Rabbi Eliezer the *tanna* famously took pride in the fact that he never said anything he did not hear from his *rebbe*. From what we know of Rabbi Eliezer — from his reputation as a *tanna* connected to the *Shammaite* tradition, and from other stories about him — it is clear that his approach to *torah she'ba'al peh* was one of pure transmission. *Torah she'ba'al peh* for Rabbi Eliezer was not about creativity or innovation but about receiving from your *rebbe* what your *rebbe* received from his *rebbe*, all the way back to *Moshe*. This stands in stark contrast to the approach of Rabbi Akiva — who was himself among Rabbi Eliezer's students — and who embodied the opposite principle, *doresh tagim*, finding new meaning even in the crowns of letters. Rabbi Akiva was, in this very matter, *cholek* on his *rebbe* about the prohibition against being *cholek* on one's *rebbe*, and he said things that he could not possibly have heard directly from his teacher.

## Maariv Early & Melacha

### Melacha Separation

Having established that Rabbi Yirmeyah's question to Rav concerned whether *davening* *maariv* early creates a separation from *melacha*, the *gemara* now takes up that question directly: *umi badil* — is it really true that one who *davens* *maariv* early thereby separates from *melacha* and may no longer perform labor?

### Rabbi's Bath Story

The *gemara* raises a challenge from a statement of Rav Avina: *pa'am achat hitpalel rebbi shel shabbos b'erev shabbat* — once, Rebbi *davened* the Shabbos *maariv* when it was still Friday. Afterwards, he went into the bathhouse, taught a chapter of study, and it was still not yet dark. This would seem to prove that *davening* *maariv* early does not create an immediate prohibition against *melacha* — or at least against bathhouse use — since Rebbi did both after *davening*.

### Bathhouse Laws

Rava explains: what Rebbi did in the bathhouse was to go in for steam — *l'hazia*, to perspire — not to immerse in hot water. The rabbinic prohibition on Shabbos is against immersing in water that was heated on Shabbos, and at a secondary level, even in water heated before Shabbos, out of concern that one might come to heat water on Shabbos itself. But Rebbi's visit to the bathhouse predated the enactment of that second layer of the *gezeirah*. So in fact Rebbi was treating it as Shabbos — he just did so at a time when this particular activity was still permitted even on Shabbos.

### Before Gezeirah

Rava's resolution thus shows that the story does not prove one can perform *melacha* after *davening* early. Rebbi was indeed conducting himself as if Shabbos had arrived; it was only the bathhouse prohibition that had not yet reached its later stringency.

### Early Acceptance

The underlying *hava amina* of the *gemara* is now clarified: the question was whether *davening* *maariv* of Shabbos early, while it is still Friday, means one has implicitly accepted Shabbos early — *kabbalat shabbos* — such that one may no longer perform *melacha*. This is similar to what we see on *motzaei shabbos*, where one can *daven maariv* of a weekday early (following the opinion of Rav Yehudah) even while *melacha* is still prohibited. Just as on *motzaei shabbos* the *tefillah* is separated from the practical permission to do *melacha*, perhaps the same logic operates on *erev shabbos*.

### Mistake Case

The *gemara* then brings a case of Abaye, who permitted Rav Dimi bar Livai to perform a certain *melacha* involving baskets — some kind of smoking or preparation process — after he had *davened maariv*. But the *gemara* immediately notes: *ta'uta havai* — this was a mistake. Rav Dimi had *davened* thinking it was already nightfall; the sky was dark from clouds, and he believed Shabbos was over. When the clouds parted and the sun reappeared, it became clear he had *davened* prematurely. The *melacha* permission was predicated on this being an error: he had not intended to *daven* early, he thought it was actually the proper time.

### Community Mistake

The *gemara* then challenges even this: *ve'ta'uta mi hadar* — does a mistake necessarily get undone? Perhaps even a mistaken early *davening* creates a binding *kabbalat shabbos*. And Rav Avidan is cited: *pa'am achat nitkashru shamayim be'avim* — once, the sky was darkened by clouds, the entire community believed it was already dark, they entered the synagogue and *davened* the Saturday night *tefillah* on Shabbos afternoon. Then the clouds dispersed and the sun shone. They came and asked Rebbi — and Rebbi ruled: *ho'il ve'hitpalelu hitpalelu*, since they *davened*, they have *davened*. They were *yotzei*.

### Mistake Counts?

Now the *gemara*'s logic here requires attention. The original question was not whether a mistaken early *davening* counts as *tefillah* — that is what Rebbi ruled upon — but whether it constitutes a *kabbalat shabbos*. The *gemara* seems to assume that if such a *davening* counts as *tefillah*, it also counts as an implicit *kabbalah*. This is a surprising equivalence. One might argue that even if the *davening* is valid, it only establishes *tefillah* if it is genuinely treated as an early *davening*; if a person thought it was already the right time, perhaps the *davening* is valid as *tefillah* but not as a *kabbalat shabbos* — since no *kabbalah* was intended at all. And yet the *gemara* does not reject this assumption in its answer.

## Intentional Early Daveni…

### Kabbalat Shabbat

The *gemara* resolves the *tzibbur* case differently from an individual: *shani tzibbur d'lo matrachi'nan lehu* — a community is different; we do not burden them to go back and *daven* again. If the entire *minyan* erred and *davened* early, the *tefillah* stands, and when Shabbos ends they are not required to repeat *maariv*. An individual, by contrast, must have specifically intended to *daven* early for it to count as an intentional early *davening*.

### Rav's Practice

Rav Chiya bar Avin reports: *rav tzalay shel shabbos be'erev shabbos* — Rav *davened* the Shabbos *tefillah* on Friday. This is the paradigm case of deliberate early *davening*.

### Rabbi Yoshiya's Case

And Rabbi Yoshiya would *daven* the *motzaei shabbos tefillah* while it was still Shabbos afternoon.

### Tzibbur vs Yachid

These two cases together — intentional early *maariv* of Shabbos on Friday, and intentional early *maariv* of *motzaei shabbos* on Shabbos — frame the discussion of what exactly such *davening* accomplishes. For the *tzibbur*, a mistake suffices; for an individual, intention is required.

### Rambam's View

The *gemara*'s assumption throughout this passage is that when one *davens* *maariv* of Shabbos early, one has implicitly accepted Shabbos — *kabbalat shabbos* — and the concept of *tosefet shabbos* underlies the entire discussion. This concept appears elsewhere in the *gemara* in connection with *tosefet yom hakippurim*, derived from a *pasuk*, and is then extended to Shabbos. According to many *rishonim*, the extension to Shabbos may be only *derabanan*, but it is present nonetheless.

The *rambam*, interestingly, never mentions *kabbalat shabbos* in this sense. He does speak of *tosefet yom hakippurim* — adding time at the beginning and end of *yom kippur* — but when it comes to early Shabbos *davening*, the *rambam*'s explanation is simply that since *tefilat maariv* is *reshut*, one may *daven* it a little before its proper time. He does not frame this as an implicit *kabbalat shabbos* that transforms the halachic status of the moment. And correspondingly, one will not find in the *rambam* a statement that *davening maariv* early on *erev shabbos* creates a prohibition on *melacha*.

### Kiddush Question

The *gemara* asks the natural next question: if one *davens* Shabbos *maariv* early on Friday, can one then go home and make *kiddush*? *Omer kiddusha al hakos o eino omer kiddusha al hakos?*

### Rambam Kiddush

The *gemara* answers with a ruling from Rav Nachman in the name of Shmuel: *mitpalel adam shel shabbos be'erev shabbos ve'omer kiddusha al hakos* — one may *daven* Shabbos *tefillah* on Friday and make *kiddush* on a cup. And the ruling is: *hilchata kavatei*, this is how we rule.

For the *rambam*, this too does not require the concept of *tosefet shabbos*. When describing the *mitzvah* of *kiddush ve'havdalah*, the *rambam* speaks of designating Shabbos at its beginning and at its end — a little before the beginning, a little after the end. The idea is to mark the holy day, to designate it; and one can make *kiddush* in anticipation of Shabbos that is about to arrive, without the moment of *kiddush* itself creating the onset of Shabbos. Shabbos, for the *rambam*, begins at sunset. The *mitzvah* of *kiddush* can be fulfilled a bit beforehand as a designation and anticipation.

## Early Havdalah

### Kiddush on Cup

As noted, Rav Nachman rules that one who *davens* Shabbos on Friday afternoon may make *kiddush* on a cup, and this is the accepted *halacha*.

## Intentional Early Dave…

### Anticipation vs Reality

One might ask: when one says *mekadesh hashabbos* in the *beracha* while it is still *chol*, what exactly is being said? According to the *rambam*'s framework, one is not declaring that Shabbos has arrived; rather, one is saying these words in anticipation, acknowledging that Shabbos is about to come. Similarly, on the *motzaei shabbos* side, saying *havdalah bechonen hada'as* before Shabbos has technically ended is a parallel kind of anticipation — a looking forward or looking beyond — and does not represent a declaration that Shabbos is already over.

## Early Havdalah…

### Havdalah Timing

The *gemara* turns to the parallel question on *motzaei shabbos*: if one *davens* *maariv* of *motzaei shabbos* while it is still Shabbos afternoon, can one also make *havdalah* on a cup? *Omer havdalah al hakos o eino omer havdalah al hakos?* The *gemara* assumes that if one is *davening motzaei shabbos*, that means one includes *havdalah* in *shemoneh esrei*. But going further and making *havdalah* on a cup — separating the sacred from the ordinary through the formal ritual — when Shabbos has not yet concluded, is a significantly more striking act. It seems to truly declare that Shabbos is already over.

The *gemara* rules: from Rav Yehudah in the name of Shmuel — *m'toei shel motzaei shabbos ve'omer havdalah al hakos* — one who *davens motzaei shabbos* early may make *havdalah* on a cup. And this is the accepted ruling.

### Tzorech Mitzvah

*Tosafot*, however, adds an important condition. *Tosafot* says: *yesh lomar d'hacha mairi sheyesh lo tzorech mitzvah la'asos b'motzaei shabbos* — this ruling applies specifically where there is a *tzorech mitzvah*, a need related to a *mitzvah*, such as needing to travel to perform a circumcision and lacking wine for *havdalah* if one waits. *Tosafot* adds: *d'vadai tzarich l'hosif min hachol al hakodesh d'oraita* — because one is obligated to add from the weekday onto the holy day (*tosefet shabbos*), making *havdalah* before Shabbos is technically over seems to undercut that obligation. Therefore, *tosafot* requires some form of genuine need — a *tzorech mitzvah* or at least a *shas hadechak* — before one may do this. Critically, *tosafot* makes clear: *u'pshita d'lo matzi l'ma'avad melacha ad chasheicha* — obviously one cannot perform *melacha* until nightfall regardless of having *davened* and made *havdalah* early.

### Summer Schedule

A practical example makes this concrete: in Northern Europe in the summer, Shabbos can end very late. One may *daven maariv*, make *havdalah*, and go to sleep — while still not performing *melacha* until *tzeis hakochavim*. *Tosafot* in fact deals extensively with such situations at the very opening of this *masechet*, since where they lived, the days were long and the demands of the church calendar and civic life meant that nighttime *davening* was often conducted while it was still light outside.

### Tosefet Concern

Beyond the practical issue, *tosafot* expresses a deeper discomfort: there seems to be something more troubling about making *havdalah* — formally declaring the end of Shabbos's holiness — when Shabbos has not yet ended, compared to simply *davening maariv* early. Even if *halachically* permitted in cases of need, the act of *havdalah* carries a weight that makes one hesitate, even when the *din* allows it.

## Conflicting Testimonie…

### Transmission Chain

The *gemara* now presents a striking chain of transmission. Rav Zeira says in the name of Rav Assi, who said it in the name of Rav Elazar, who said it in the name of Rav Chanina, who said it in the name of Rav: *betzad amud zeh hitpalel Rabbi Yishmael b'Rabbi Yosi shel shabbos b'erev shabbos* — right by this pillar, Rabbi Yishmael son of Rabbi Yosi *davened* the Shabbos *tefillah* on Friday.

### Date Palm

Then *Ula* arrives and corrects the entire account. First: it was not beside a pillar — it was *betzad timra*, beside a date palm.

### Identity Corrections

Second: it was not Rabbi Yishmael son of Rabbi Yosi — it was Rabbi Elazar son of Rabbi Yosi. And third — most significantly — it was not *shel shabbos be'erev shabbos*, *davening* Shabbos *tefillah* on Friday. It was the opposite: *shel motzaei shabbos be'shabbos*, *davening* the *motzaei shabbos tefillah* while it was still Shabbos.

### Mesorah Issues

Of course, it is possible that both stories are true — two different incidents, one involving each practice. But the deeper point here is about the nature of *mesorah* itself. Ula corrects every single detail: the location, the person's identity, and the direction of the early *davening*. If these details, transmitted through a chain of five *amoraim*, could all be incorrect, the transmission is clearly imperfect. This connects directly to what was said earlier about Rabbi Eliezer's ideology of pure, unbroken transmission — the idea that *torah she'ba'al peh* passes perfectly from *rebbe* to student, unchanged. The *gemara* here gently but unmistakably illustrates that even in careful chains of transmission, details can be garbled. In this way, the *gemara* softens what would otherwise be an impossibly rigid standard: one cannot always say only what one heard directly from one's *rebbe*, precisely because the chain is never as clean as the ideal demands.

## Early Havdalah…

### Daven vs Melacha

One important practical distinction to carry forward from all of the above: *davening maariv* early — whether of Shabbos on Friday or of *motzaei shabbos* on Shabbos — does not by itself end or begin Shabbos in the sense of permitting or prohibiting *melacha*. One may *daven* early and even make *kiddush* or *havdalah* accordingly, but *melacha* is prohibited until Shabbos actually ends at nightfall, and Shabbos does not begin (in the sense of the *melacha* prohibition) until sunset — or, for those who accept *tosefet shabbos*, from the moment one explicitly accepts it upon oneself.

## Maariv Obligation

### No Fixed Time

The *gemara* now takes up the *mishna*'s statement: *tefilat ha'erev ein lah keva* — the evening prayer has no fixed time.

### Reshut Status

The *gemara* asks: what does *ein lah keva* mean? If it means one may *daven* the entire night — *kol halaila* — then the *mishna* should simply say so. The additional phrase, *ein lah keva*, implies something more: it signals that the evening prayer has a *reshut* character, that it is in some sense optional rather than obligatory.

### Gamliel vs Yehoshua

Rav Yehudah says in the name of Shmuel: *tefilat arvit* — Rabban Gamliel says it is a *chova*, an obligation; Rabbi Yehoshua says it is a *reshut*.

### Abaye vs Rava

Among the *amoraim*: Abaye rules *halacha kedivrei ha'omer chova*, like the one who says it is an obligation. Rava rules *halacha kedivrei ha'omer reshut*, like the one who says it is optional — though with significant qualification, as discussed below.

### Tosafot Reading

*Tosafot* argues that *tefilat arvit reshut* does not mean absolutely optional — that one simply need not *daven* if one does not feel like it. Rather, *reshut* here means that relative to other *halachic* obligations, *maariv* can be set aside in certain circumstances. It is an obligation, but one with a lower priority that can be overridden by competing obligations. This is how *tosafot* understands *reshut* in this context, and it is sometimes how *tosafot* uses the term elsewhere in the *gemara* as well.

### Rambam's Position

The *rambam* and others read the history differently: *tefilat arvit* began as a genuine *reshut* — a voluntary practice — but was then accepted by the community as an obligation through universal practice. This raises the question of whether the original *reshut* status retains any practical relevance now that the community has accepted it as *chova*, but this is not a question the *gemara* resolves here.

Whatever the precise meaning, it is clear from the *gemara* that *maariv* is treated as an obligation in all of the preceding discussions — every analysis of whether one is *yotzei* if one *daven*s early, whether one must *daven* twice *shacharit* to make up a missed *maariv*, all of these assume a binding obligation.

## Student Inquiry

### First Question

The *gemara* now brings a *baraita*: *maaseh be'talmid echad she'ba lifnei Rabbi Yehoshua* — a certain student came before Rabbi Yehoshua and asked: *tefilat arvit reshut o chova* — is the evening prayer *reshut* or *chova*?

### Yehoshua's Answer

Rabbi Yehoshua answered: *reshut* — it is optional.

### Second Question

The student then went and posed the same question to Rabban Gamliel. The act of seeking a second opinion after having already received a ruling is itself not necessarily problematic; one might genuinely wish to hear multiple perspectives on a contested issue.

### Gamliel's Answer

Rabban Gamliel answered: *chova* — it is an obligation.

### Contradiction Exposed

The student then said to Rabban Gamliel: but Rabbi Yehoshua told me it is *reshut*. At that point Rabban Gamliel said: wait — *hamten* — until the *ba'alei terisin*, the shield-bearers, the *talmidim*, enter the *beit hamidrash*. The image is deliberately militaristic: *torah* study as combat, scholars as warriors. Wait until everyone is assembled, and then you will see.

### Public Testimony

When all had entered, the student rose publicly and asked the question again. Rabban Gamliel immediately declared: *chova*. Then he turned to the assembled *chachamim*: *klum yesh adam shecholek badavar zeh* — does anyone disagree? In the very framing of the question, Rabban Gamliel has already expressed his opinion first — before the other voices have a chance to speak — and has made any dissent effectively impossible. Rabbi Yehoshua was cornered: *amar lei Rabban Gamliel — velo mishmacha amru li reshut?* And did I not hear that you said it was *reshut*? The order was then issued: *Yehoshua, amod al raglecha* — stand on your feet, and let people testify against you publicly.

## Yehoshua's Shaming

### Standing Punishment

Rabbi Yehoshua stood on his feet. And Rabban Gamliel continued to sit and expound — to lead the *shiur* — while Rabbi Yehoshua was made to stand throughout.

### People Protest

The assembled *talmidim* began to murmur. They told Chutzpit the *torgeman* — the one who served as the voice, the amplifier of the teaching — to stop. They effectively turned off Rabban Gamliel's mic. (The term *amod*, "stop," is the same word used when Rabbi Yehoshua was told to stand — a striking verbal parallel noted by the students themselves.)

### Affliction Complaint

Their complaint: *ad kama nitztar ve'neizil* — how long will Rabban Gamliel go on afflicting us? What kind of culture is this?

### Prior Incidents

They recalled prior incidents. In *Rosh Hashana* the previous year, there had been a famous dispute over the testimony for the new moon. Rabban Gamliel ruled that the testimony was valid and asserted his calendar against Rabbi Yehoshua's calculation. To enforce his authority, Rabban Gamliel required Rabbi Yehoshua to appear before him on what Rabbi Yehoshua believed was his *Yom Kippur* — with his staff and his money — compelling him to violate his own *Yom Kippur* as an act of submission to Rabban Gamliel's authority. The story appears in *Masechet Rosh Hashana*. Similarly, the case involving *bechorot* — which appears in *Masechet Bechorot* — involved another incident where Rabban Gamliel turned on Rabbi Yehoshua: *amod al raglecha*, stand on your feet.

### Other Cases

The references to *Rosh Hashana* and *bechorot* may refer both to the subject matter of those disputes and to the *masechtot* in which the stories appear.

### Removal Decision

The students reached a decision: Rabban Gamliel must be removed. *Ta ve'na'avreih* — let us go and depose him. The question then was: who should replace him?

---

## New Leadership

### Yehoshua Rejected

The proposal to appoint Rabbi Yehoshua was rejected: *ba'al ma'aseh hu* — he is a party to the dispute. To place Rabbi Yehoshua in Rabban Gamliel's position would be too partisan, and Rashi adds that it would inflict too great a pain on Rabban Gamliel himself.

### Akiva Rejected

Rabbi Akiva was proposed — a figure of immense *talmudic* stature — but also rejected. The concern: *dilma anish leh de'leit leh zechut avot* — perhaps Rabban Gamliel, still wielding political power as the *nasi*, would move against Rabbi Akiva, who lacked *yichus*, the family pedigree and genealogical protection that comes with descent from powerful houses. Rashi explains this very practically: Rabban Gamliel, descended from the patriarchal house of *Hillel*, had connections and authority that he could deploy against someone without comparable lineage. Rabbi Akiva's later marriage into wealth was real, but it did not provide the same protection as illustrious ancestry.

### Elazar Chosen

The choice fell on Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah. He was *chacham* — wise. He was *ashir* — wealthy, which matters in terms of practical power and the ability to sustain political obligations, including financial dealings with the house of Caesar. And he was *asiri le'Ezra* — the tenth generation from Ezra, granting him *yichus* that Rabban Gamliel could not easily assail. Ezra is a particularly resonant model: he was both *Ezra ha-sofer*, the *rabbinic* archetype as it were, and a political leader — a dual identity that mirrors the role of the *nasi* itself.

### Qualifications

The qualifications were thus: wisdom sufficient to answer any *Torah* challenge, wealth sufficient to engage the imperial court, and ancestral merit sufficient to protect him from retribution. *De'i makish leh mefarek leh* — if challenged on his Torah knowledge, he could handle it.

### Wife's Advice

Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah went and consulted his wife. She cautioned him: *sofam ma'avirin lach* — eventually they will depose you. Things will settle down, and your tenure will be temporary.

### One Day Worth It

Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah responded with a well-known saying: *lishamesh inish yoma chad bekassa demokre ulemachar lispari* — let a person use a beautiful glass for even one day, even if it breaks the next. The honor of the position, even if fleeting, is worth it. With that, he accepted.

---

## White Hair Miracle

### Youth Problem

Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah was eighteen years old at the time. For someone so young to assume authority over a *beit midrash* of *talmidei chachamim*, he would need to project an appearance of age and gravitas. His wife had already noted this: *leis lach chivvusa* — you don't have white hair.

### Eighteen Rows

A miracle occurred: *isracheish lei nissa ve'hadru lei tamsrei darei chivvusa* — and eighteen rows of white hair appeared on his head.

### Seventy-Year-Old

This is the background for the famous phrase cited from a *mishna* earlier in this *masechet*: *harei ani keven shiv'im shana*, "I am like a man of seventy years" — not *ben shiv'im*, not actually seventy, but *keven shiv'im*, like seventy. He was not actually seventy; he simply looked that old due to the miraculous appearance of the white hair.

### Open Doors

On the day Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah took office, *silkuhu leshomeir hapetach* — they removed the guard who stood at the door of the *beit hamidrash*. The door was thrown open.

### Gamliel's Policy

Under Rabban Gamliel, a strict admissions policy had been in force: *kol talmid she'ein tocho kevaro lo yikaneis leveit hamidrash* — any student whose inside does not match his outside, who is not as sincere as he appears, may not enter the *beit hamidrash*. This was not purely about power — there was also a genuine concern for authenticity, for ensuring that only truly sincere students filled the study hall. But the effect was exclusivity, and the policy was in Rabban Gamliel's hands to define and enforce.

### Benches Added

On the day of the change, many rows of benches were added. A dispute is cited in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: Abba Yosef ben Dosai and the *rabbanan* disagreed — one says 400 rows were added, the other says 700. The numbers may be somewhat exaggerated, but the point is the dramatic expansion of the study hall.

---

## Gamliel's Despair

### Torah Prevented

Rabban Gamliel became troubled. He thought: *dilma chas veshalom maniyati Torah miYisrael* — perhaps, God forbid, my policy held back Torah from Israel. If the *beit hamidrash* could accommodate hundreds more students than had ever been admitted under his watch, then he had been preventing the spread of Torah all along.

### Dream Vision

In a dream, he was shown white casks filled with black ash — vessels that appeared pure and clean on the outside but were filled with worthless refuse on the inside. This vision seemed to validate his original policy: see, the students he had been keeping out were indeed hollow — their exteriors promising but their interiors empty.

### Dream Consolation

The *gemara* immediately corrects this reading: *v'lo hi* — it was not so. The dream was sent *leyituvei da'atei* — to give him comfort, to make him feel better. It was not the truth.

### True Reality

The reality is that Rabban Gamliel's policy was wrong. The *gemara* clearly takes sides: the exclusive admissions policy did harm to Torah study.

### Eduyot Taught

On that very day — *bo bayom nishneis* — the entire *Masechet Eduyot* was taught. The tractate of testimonies, which brings together the voices of witnesses and authorities from across the rabbinic spectrum to clarify disputed law, was constructed on this very day of open doors and open debate.

---

## Bo Bayom Meaning

### Bayom Reference

The *gemara* clarifies: wherever a *mishna* uses the phrase *bo bayom* — on that day — *hahu yoma hava*, it refers to this day: the day Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah was appointed and the doors of the *beit hamidrash* were opened.

### Eduyot Purpose

*Masechet Eduyot* is structured around the collection of testimonies — not just from the established elite, but from any authoritative voice that had something to contribute. The point of *eduyot* is to gather all available facts, all reliable testimony, so that the truth of *halakha* can emerge. Such a project was only possible with an open-door policy.

### All Resolved

*V'lo hayta halacha shehaysa teluya beveis hamidrash shelo peirushuha* — there was no *halacha* that had been pending and unresolved that was not clarified on that day. The sheer diversity of voices, the range of knowledge and experience in the room, enabled what no exclusive circle could accomplish: complete resolution of every outstanding question.

### Gamliel Stays

*V'af Rabban Gamliel lo mana atzmo mi-beit hamidrash afilu sha'ah achas* — even Rabban Gamliel, now deposed, did not keep himself from the *beit hamidrash* for even one hour. His love of *Torah* was genuine and was greater than his pride.

### Diversity Resolves

The deeper lesson, which Devorah Steinmetz's article illuminates, is that the harm of Rabban Gamliel's policy was not merely that fewer students were admitted. Torah itself was diminished. When Rabban Gamliel feared he had prevented Torah from Israel, he may have meant something even more profound: by silencing dissent, by enforcing a single authoritative voice, he was not only restricting access to *Torah* — he was impairing *Torah* itself, because *Torah* requires the clash of diverse opinions in order to arrive at clarity.

### Silence vs Voices

The contrast being drawn throughout this entire narrative is between silence and voice, between exclusion and inclusion, between a culture in which only one opinion is heard and one in which every qualified voice can speak. The lesson of *Masechet Eduyot* being taught on this day is that it is precisely the diversity of testimony and opinion — not its suppression — that enables unresolved questions to be settled.

## Amoni Convert

### Convert's Petition

The *gemara* cites a *baraita* from *Masechet Yadayim* with the note *bo bayom* — on that same day. Yehuda, an *Amoni* convert, came before the assembled *rabbis* in the *beit hamidrash* and asked: *mah ani lavo bakahal* — what is my status regarding entry into the community? May I marry a Jewish woman?

### Gamliel Forbids

Rabban Gamliel ruled: *asur ata lavo bakahal* — you are forbidden. Entirely consistent with his broader tendency toward exclusivity, Rabban Gamliel applied the most restrictive reading.

### Yehoshua Permits

Rabbi Yehoshua ruled: *mutar ata lavo bakahal* — you are permitted.

### Biblical Citation

Rabban Gamliel cited the explicit *pasuk*: *lo yavo Amoni u'Moavi bikehal Hashem* — an *Amoni* and *Moavi* shall not enter the assembly of God. The Torah's prohibition appears unambiguous.

### Sancheriv Argument

Rabbi Yehoshua responded: *vechi Amon u'Moav bimkoman hein yoshvin* — do you think that *Amon* and *Moav* still sit in their original places? *Kvar ala Sancheiriv u'bilbel et kol ha'umim* — Sancheriv, king of Assyria, already arose and mixed up all the nations, transferring populations across the known world. The people who now call themselves *Amoni* or *Moavi* bear no reliable genealogical connection to the biblical *Amon* and *Moav*. Just as the Egypt of Cleopatra bears no ethnic continuity to the Egypt of the *Torah*, the identifiable national categories of the ancient world have been obliterated.

### Majority Rule

*De'chol de'parish me'ruba parish* — anyone who has separated from the mass of nations is presumed to come from the majority. And the majority of humanity is not *Amoni*, not *Moavi*, not *Amalek*, and not any of the nations subject to biblical exclusion. Therefore, one who comes forward today and cannot establish specific problematic lineage is presumed to belong to the permissible majority.

### Gamliel Persists

Rabban Gamliel pushed back: *v'lo kvar neemar — v'acharei chen ashiv shevut benei Amon neum Hashem* — but has not the prophecy already been stated that God will restore the captivity of the people of *Amon*? And this restoration has already occurred. The people of *Amon* have been restored to their homeland and can once again be identified.

### Israel First

Rabbi Yehoshua answered with another *pasuk*: *v'shavti et shevut ami Yisrael* — and I will restore the captivity of My people Israel. That restoration has not yet occurred. The prophetic framework in which nations are restored to their identifiable boundaries is conditional: it happens when Israel is restored first. Since Israel has not yet been restored, the condition has not been met, and the nations — including *Amon* — remain intermixed and unidentifiable.

### Convert Admitted

*Miyad hitiru lavo bikahal* — they immediately permitted him entry into the community.

---

## Leadership Contrast

### Exclusion vs Inclusion

The parallel between the *Amoni* case and the broader narrative of this *sugya* is unmistakable. Rabban Gamliel excludes; Rabbi Yehoshua includes. The pattern holds in every domain: who enters the *beit hamidrash*, who enters the community of Israel.

### Debate Styles

But there is a second, more subtle contrast. In the earlier episodes of this narrative, Rabban Gamliel did not merely hold one opinion — he used institutional power to silence the opposing opinion. Here, in this same *beit hamidrash* on the day of changed leadership, the dynamic is completely different.

### Honest Argument

Rabban Gamliel raises a genuine *halachic* counter — *v'lo kvar neemar*, has not the *pasuk* already stated — and Rabbi Yehoshua responds with a *halachic* counter of his own. They are actually debating. No one is forced to stand; no one's mic is turned off. Rabban Gamliel does not silence Rabbi Yehoshua. Even while holding the opposite position from Rabbi Yehoshua, Rabban Gamliel is now participating in the culture of honest *halachic* argument that Rabbi Yehoshua represents.

### Inside Outside

This echoes the central theme that runs through the entire narrative: the difference between inside and outside. Rabban Gamliel's *tocho kevaro* policy — admitting only those whose inside matches their outside — was oriented toward sincerity. But in practice it led him to judge by externals, to enforce conformity, and to suppress the genuine interior life of disagreement and debate that *Torah* requires.

---

## Reconciliation

### Gamliel Reconciles

Rabban Gamliel decided to seek reconciliation with Rabbi Yehoshua. *Azil u'fayseia l'Rabbi Yehoshua* — he went to make peace.

### House Inspection

When he arrived at Rabbi Yehoshua's home, *chazinhu l'ashita d'veitei d'mshachran* — he saw the walls of the house were black, covered in soot.

### Blacksmith Deduction

Rabban Gamliel said to him: *mikotlei beitcha ata nikar she-pachami ata* — from the walls of your house it is evident that you are a blacksmith. Rabbi Yehoshua worked with coal or iron to earn his living, and the blackened walls of his home bore witness to it.

### Yehoshua's Rebuke

Rabbi Yehoshua responded: *oy lo l'dor she'ata parnaso* — woe to the generation whose leader you are.

### Scholar's Suffering

He continued: *she'i ata yodeia b'tzaran shel talmidei chachamim* — you do not know the suffering of the *talmidei chachamim*. The irony is pointed: earlier in the narrative the people complained that Rabban Gamliel was *metzareira* them, causing them *tzaar*. Now Rabbi Yehoshua tells Rabban Gamliel that he does not know their *tzaar* — their lived pain, their daily hardship.

### Livelihood Concern

*Bameh hem mitparnesim u'vameh hem nizonim* — how we make our living, how we put food on our tables. Rabban Gamliel, wealthy and politically powerful, had never had to earn a living through manual labor. He did not understand the reality of the scholars who sat in his *beit hamidrash*.

### Gamliel Submits

Rabban Gamliel said: *na'aniti lach, mechol li* — I submit to you, forgive me. Rabbi Yehoshua, however, did not immediately respond.

### Father's Honor

Rabban Gamliel then added: *aseh bishvil kevod aba* — do it for the honor of my father. That tipped the balance, and Rabbi Yehoshua accepted.

---

## Shared Leadership

### Announcement

The question then arose: who would go and tell the *rabbis* that the reconciliation had occurred and that perhaps Rabban Gamliel might return to some role?

### Launderer Messenger

A certain launderer volunteered: *ana azilna* — I will go.

### Position Message

Rabbi Yehoshua sent a message to the *beit midrash* through the launderer: *man d'lavish mada yilbash mada* — the one who wears the cloak of authority should continue to wear it.

### Authority Principle

*Man d'la lavish mada* — the one who has never worn that cloak should not tell the one who wears it to take it off so that he can put it on.

### Door Locking

Rabbi Akiva, concerned about what this message meant, said to the *rabbis*: *truku galei* — lock the doors, lest the servants of Rabban Gamliel come and oppress the *rabbis* further. Rabbi Akiva thought the launderer might be a proxy for Rabban Gamliel's forces trying to reassert power.

### Personal Announcement

Rabbi Yehoshua, realizing the message had not been properly conveyed, went in person to the *beit hamidrash* and knocked on the door.

### Sprinkler Parable

He then conveyed his meaning through a parable: the *mazeh ben mazeh* — the sprinkler who is the son of a sprinkler (a reference to the one who applies the ashes of the *parah adumah* for purification) should continue to sprinkle. But *she'eino lo mazeh velo ben mazeh* — one who has never been a sprinkler and is not the son of a sprinkler — should he say to the one who has always sprinkled: your waters are mere cave water and your ashes are like cooking ash? The message: it is not fitting for someone who never held the position of authority to come and tell the one who always held it that his authority is illegitimate.

### Akiva's Apology

Rabbi Akiva understood and said: *kelum asinu ela bishvil kevodecha* — we acted only for your honor, Rabbi Yehoshua. And then: *lemachar ani ve'ata nashkim lepitcho* — tomorrow you and I will go early to the door of Rabban Gamliel, in the Roman custom of the morning visit to one's patron, as a formal expression of acceptance of his authority.

### Rotation System

The question remained: what to do about Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah? *Gimiri ma'alin bakodesh ve'ein moridin* — we have a principle that one raises in the realm of sanctity but does not lower. One who has been elevated to a position of authority cannot simply be demoted. The solution: *lidrosh Rabban Gamliel tlata shabta ve'Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah chada shabta* — Rabban Gamliel would lead for three weeks out of every four, and Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah for one. The hierarchy was clear, but Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah's role was institutionalized. And this is the background for the phrase found elsewhere in the *gemara*: *shabbat shel mi haytah — Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah hava*, "whose week was it? It was Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah's."

### Institutionalized

In the end, a genuine institutional change had occurred. Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah's wife had been right that things would revert — Rabban Gamliel did resume his primary role. But the world was not exactly the same as before. Multiple voices were now institutionalized. The rotation was real. And the culture of the *beit hamidrash* had been altered.

## Inside Outside Theme

### Chiastic Structure

The narrative of this entire *sugya* is beautifully constructed. Jeffrey Rubenstein's work demonstrates that it has a perfect chiastic structure: every element that builds toward the crisis finds its mirror image as the narrative resolves. The escalation and the de-escalation are architecturally parallel.

### Student Bookends

The *talmid* who asks the question about *maariv* at the beginning of the story and the *talmid* identified at the end — who was Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai — form the outer frame of the chiasm, bookending the entire episode.

### Tocho Kevaro

The central theme throughout is *tocho kevaro* — the inside-outside question. This is not merely a phrase in Rabban Gamliel's admission policy; it is the animating concern of the entire narrative. Does the inside match the outside? Is what is presented on the surface a true reflection of what lies beneath?

### External Judgement

The great irony, which the *gemara* traces with remarkable care, is that Rabban Gamliel — the man most insistent on aligning inside and outside — is himself repeatedly misled by externals. At the beginning of the story, he saw Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah as too young; the miracle of the white hair had to occur before Rabban Gamliel's own standard could be met visually. At the end, he came to Rabbi Yehoshua's house and saw the blackened walls, and immediately concluded: you are a blacksmith. He judged by the surface of the walls rather than by the inner reality of what it meant to be a *talmid chacham* supporting himself through manual labor.

### Sincerity Paradox

The paradox is sharp: Rabban Gamliel's passion for sincerity, for ensuring that inside matches outside, led him not to deeper understanding but to a systematic misreading of reality. His very commitment to the external signs of inner truth caused him to mistake those signs at every turn.

### Diversity Necessary

The broader implication is one that runs through the entire *sugya*: diversity of voices and debate are not obstacles to *Torah* clarity — they are necessary conditions for it. A single authoritative voice, however sincere and however learned, cannot by itself resolve what an open assembly of many voices can clarify. This is the lesson of *Masechet Eduyot* being taught *bo bayom*. And it is the lesson that Rabban Gamliel came to understand only after experiencing what he had prevented.

### Wikipedia Analogy

It is tempting to compare this to the debate between encyclopedic authority and open-source knowledge. When Wikipedia began, the assumption was that expert-authored, editorially controlled works like *Encyclopaedia Britannica* would always be superior. And yet the open, corrective, multi-voiced process of Wikipedia has proved remarkably effective — not despite the diversity of contributors but because of it. One must not push the analogy too far, but the underlying principle is the same: the process of mutual correction among many voices produces results that the exclusive authority of the few cannot match.

## Ammon Moav Shvi'it

### Sabbatical Status

The *gemara* now transitions to a *mishna* from another tractate: *Ammon u'Moav ma hen b'shvi'it* — what is the status of *Ammon* and *Moav* in the sabbatical year? The question is whether these neighboring lands are governed by the laws of *shvi'it* that apply within the Land of Israel, or by different rules.

### Tithe Differences

To understand the question, it is necessary to review the background of *ma'aser* in various contexts. Different tithes apply in different years: *ma'aser sheni*, the second tithe, which is brought to and consumed in *Yerushalayim*, applies in certain years; *ma'aser ani*, the poor man's tithe, applies in other years. Outside of the Land of Israel, the *shvi'it* restriction on agricultural labor does not apply, but other laws of *ma'aser* may.

### Shvi'it Rules

Within the land, the sabbatical year transforms the agricultural system entirely: the land is left fallow, its produce is *hefker* — ownerless — and the ordinary *ma'aser* obligations do not apply.

### Poor's Tithe

The rabbinic ruling in the *mishna* is that in the lands of *Ammon* and *Moav*, *ma'aser ani* should be given in the seventh year. This means treating those lands, in a particular respect, as if the seventh year were a regular year subject to the ordinary obligation to support the poor through *ma'aser*.

### Sustenance Need

The reason: in the sabbatical year, the Land of Israel produces no food through normal agriculture. The poor within Israel have nothing to eat. The communities of *Ammon* and *Moav*, whose lands are not subject to *shvi'it*, continue to produce crops. By requiring *ma'aser ani* from those lands specifically in the seventh year, the *rabbis* ensured that the poor would have sustenance even when the Land of Israel itself could not provide it.
