# Avodah Zarah 63 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Perek 5 Intro

### Perek Structure

Today's daf is 63, and with it we enter the fifth and final *perek* of *Masechet Avodah Zarah*. This represents a significant shift from what came before. We remain in the domain of *yayin nesech* and *stam yeinam*, but the angle of analysis changes considerably.

### Last vs. This Perek

The fourth *perek* was concerned primarily with the mechanics of how wine becomes *stam yeinam*: what stage of production qualifies a liquid as wine, and what types of physical contact — direct or indirect, intentional or incidental, deliberate in one context but performed for an entirely different purpose — render it forbidden. The *sugyot* of that chapter were often structured around vivid stories: an apple fell into a barrel of wine, someone plunged a hand in to retrieve it, bystanders physically restrained the arm from moving, and from there the *gemara* extracted its principles. The cases themselves were not technically complicated, but the theoretical questions they generated — about intent, about the underlying principles — produced enormous *Tosafot* that addressed not only the conceptual issues but also the practical applications relevant in their own time.

### Mishtaker Principle

This fifth *perek* opens with a different focus: the principle of *mishtaker be'issurei hana'ah* — being paid a salary for work performed in connection with objects that are forbidden in benefit. The opening *mishnah* addresses the case of a Jewish worker hired by a non-Jew to work with *yayin nesech*: the wages received are themselves forbidden in benefit. This concern will anchor the early discussions of the chapter before the *masechet* returns, toward its conclusion, to the *kashrut*-oriented themes more characteristic of the earlier chapters — questions of *ta'arovet*, mixtures, and wine that becomes commingled with other liquids.

## Payment Issue

### Gemara's Question

The *gemara* opens by interrogating the theoretical basis for this prohibition on wages. The *mishnah* states that payment for work with *yayin nesech* is forbidden in benefit — but why? Is this simply a consequence of the general principle governing *issurei hana'ah*?

### Issurei Hana'ah

This question has direct practical relevance, and it is one that arises with some regularity. Consider the question of whether a Jew may work as a waiter in a non-kosher restaurant that serves *basar bechalav* — a category that, unlike ordinary non-kosher meat, is genuinely *assur be-hana'ah*, forbidden in benefit. Is serving such food at a table, or working at a soup kitchen where *basar bechalav* is distributed, considered deriving benefit from a forbidden object, since one is being paid to interact with it?

The *gemara*'s answer is that as a general rule, this is not a problem under the category of *issurei hana'ah*. One is not deriving benefit from the *basar bechalav* itself — one is being paid to serve it. The *gemara* goes further: even if someone actually sells an item that is *assur be-hana'ah* and receives money for it — thereby transgressing through the act of sale, since receiving payment constitutes deriving benefit — the money itself does not carry a forbidden status, and continuing to use that money is not considered ongoing benefit from the prohibited object. Whether one may *lechatchilah* retain and use that money is a question that *Tosafot* engages with, but *bedi'avad* it is not problematic. If that is true even where money was directly exchanged for a forbidden object, it is certainly true that a worker being paid a salary for serving *basar bechalav* is not deriving benefit from the object itself.

### Issurei vs. Tofes

So if the general principle of *issurei hana'ah* does not account for the prohibition on wages for *yayin nesech*, perhaps the explanation lies in the concept of *tofes et damav* — the idea that in certain categories of prohibition, the forbidden status transfers to money received in exchange. By *yayin nesech* and *avodah zarah*, if one sells the prohibited object and receives payment, that money is itself forbidden in benefit, and the forbidden status continues to extend to whatever is purchased with it. The same principle applies to *shevi'it* produce. So here we have *issur hana'ah* — which applies to categories like *yayin nesech* and *basar bechalav* — and separately, *tofes et damav* — which applies to categories like *yayin nesech*, *avodah zarah*, and *shevi'it*.

But *tofes et damav* also cannot explain the prohibition on wages for *yayin nesech*. The *gemara* demonstrates this from *shevi'it*: if one is paid a salary to work *with* *shevi'it* produce, those wages do not themselves acquire *kedushat shevi'it*. The rule of *tofes et damav* applies only to money paid *for* the object itself — the object is exchanged for money and the status transfers through that exchange. A worker's salary for labor involving *shevi'it* is not money paid for *shevi'it*; it is payment for services. Therefore the *shevi'it* status does not transfer to it.

### Avodah Zarah Chumra

Thus, from the perspective of *issurei hana'ah*, payment for service should be permitted; and from the perspective of *tofes et damav*, salary for work is not the same as money paid for the object itself. Neither principle independently forbids taking wages for work with *yayin nesech*. The *gemara*'s conclusion is that the prohibition is a *chumra de'avoda zara* — a special stringency unique to the domain of *avodah zarah* — not derived from either of the two general principles. It is important to understand this precisely, because knowing that neither principle applies independently is itself practically significant.

### Liquor Store Question

This clarification matters for real-world questions such as whether one may work in a liquor store that carries non-kosher wine. Owning such a store is obviously a separate and more severe issue — there one is directly profiting from *yayin nesech* in ways that implicate both principles. But whether an employee who merely stocks shelves falls under the prohibition of *mishtaker be'issurei hana'ah* is a genuinely interesting question, one that the *mishnah* may well speak to. The *gemara*'s analysis of the underlying principles, and the conclusion that the prohibition in this domain is driven by the special *chumra* of *avodah zarah* rather than by either general principle, is essential background for reasoning through such cases. The final ruling, in any event, is that this *chumra* extends to *stam yeinam* as well: wages for work with *stam yeinam* are equally forbidden.

## Shvi'is Case

### Oranges Story

With the theoretical framework established, the *gemara* now turns to an illustrative case. The household of Rav Yanai (*debei Rav Yanai*) would borrow *peiros shevi'it* — fruit carrying the sanctity of the Sabbatical year — from poor people (*aniyim*). The likely motivation was charitable: the poor had gathered this produce from ownerless fields during the *shemittah* year, and as the time of *bi'ur* approached — after which the produce could no longer be kept and consumed — it became practically useful for a wealthy household to take the produce, use it promptly, and replace it with ordinary produce after *shemittah* ended. The arrangement allowed the poor to preserve the practical value of their *shevi'it* fruits beyond the *bi'ur* deadline.

A number of incidental complications surround this arrangement — there are questions of *ribbis*, since borrowing consumable goods and repaying in kind carries the risk of forbidden interest if values fluctuate; and separately, one might wonder whether *shemittah* at year's end cancels the loan entirely, although perhaps a *pruzbul* was in place. These questions are real but peripheral to the main discussion, which is this: when the borrowed fruit was repaid in the eighth year with ordinary non-*shevi'it* produce, were the workers who received that produce obligated to treat it as *peiros shevi'it*?

### Rabbi Yochanan

The people in Rav Yanai's household thought there might be a problem: perhaps the repaid fruit should be considered *chalifei shevi'it* — produce received in exchange for *shevi'it* — onto which the *kedushah* of *shevi'it* transfers. They brought the question to Rabbi Yochanan, and he replied: *ya'ot hen ovdin* — they are acting entirely properly, and there is no *kedushah* on the repaid fruit.

### Etnan Analogy

Rabbi Yochanan supports this ruling by analogy to the laws of *etnan* — payment given to a prostitute for her services, which the Torah forbids from being brought as a sacrifice in the *Beit HaMikdash*. From the laws of *etnan*, Rabbi Yochanan demonstrates a principle: the status of a prohibited object transfers to an exchanged item only when both objects are present simultaneously and a genuine exchange occurs between them. The *kedushah* of *shevi'it* produce, or the *issur* of *etnan*, extends to something received in exchange — but only if the original object and the replacement object exist together at the moment of exchange, so that the status can be understood as passing from one to the other. When the original fruit has long since been consumed and the replacement is delivered in the eighth year simply to repay a debt, there is nothing from which the *kedushah* can extend. The repaid fruit is merely the satisfaction of an outstanding obligation; it is not an exchange.

## Etnan Sugya

### Braisa Setup

The *gemara* now examines the laws of *etnan* directly. A *braisa* states: if the man gave the animal to the woman but had not yet had intercourse with her (*natan lah v'lo ba aleha*), or if he had intercourse but had not yet given her the animal (*ba aleha v'lo natan lah*) — in both cases the animal is permitted as a sacrifice (*etnanah mutar*) and does not carry the prohibited status of *etnan*.

### Logical Challenge

The *gemara* immediately challenges both halves of this *braisa*. The first case — giving the animal before the act — is obvious: *keivan d'lo ba aleha, matana b'alma hu d'yahav lah* — since the act has not yet occurred, the giving is simply a gift, with no connection to the services. There is nothing to give it the status of *etnan*. The second case, however, is incoherent: if he had intercourse but gave her nothing, *ha lo yahav lah midi* — he gave her nothing at all! There is no object that could be an *etnan*, permitted or otherwise. The question *etnanah mutar* is meaningless if nothing was ever given.

### Resolution

The *gemara* therefore reinterprets the *braisa*. It does not mean that both events happened but not the other — it means they did not happen simultaneously. The *braisa* must be read as: first he gave the animal and then later they had intercourse; or first they had intercourse and then later he gave her the animal. In both cases — payment followed by act, or act followed by payment — the *etnan* is permitted.

### Strictness

This is Rabbi Yochanan's ruling, and it is, as the *gemara* acknowledges, a striking position. Only if the giving of the animal and the act of intercourse occurred simultaneously — if the animal was in her possession as payment at the precise moment the services were rendered — does it acquire the status of *etnan*. If the payment came first and was finalized before the act, it does not retroactively become *etnan*. If the act came first and the payment followed, it does not acquire the status even though it is clearly being given as payment for services already rendered. One would naturally assume that payment delivered after the fact is precisely what defines *etnan* — yet Rabbi Yochanan says otherwise. The *etnan* status requires that the object be present, possessed, and defined as payment at the very moment the act is occurring.

### Sanctification Q

This leads to a question posed by Rav Hoshaia. The *gemara* had been discussing a scenario in which the man says to the woman: you will own this animal once the act is complete, but if you need it beforehand, you may take possession of it now (*lehavei gabaich ad sha'at bi'a, v'i itzterich lach kni me'achshav*). Under those terms, Rabbi Elazar explains, if she went ahead and brought the animal as a sacrifice before the act took place, the animal would not be *etnan* — since she sacrificed it while still fully owning it as her own property, before the act had occurred, and so the act did not define the animal's status.

Rav Hoshaia asks: what if, in that same scenario, she did not bring it as a sacrifice — she only *sanctified* it (*hikdishaso*), consecrating it to *hekdesh*, without yet offering it on the altar? What is the *halacha*?

One might argue that *amiraso lagavoah kimesiraso lahedyot* — when one consecrates an object to God, it is as though one has physically handed it over to another person; the *Beit HaMikdash* takes possession. On this reasoning, the moment she sanctified the animal, it was as though she had brought it as a sacrifice — she had exercised her early-possession right, the animal now fully belonged to *hekdesh*, and when the act of intercourse later occurred, the animal was no longer in the category of her property or payment. The act, therefore, would not have defined its status as *etnan*.

Alternatively, one might say: *hashta miha kai v'isa be'einayah* — right now, at the moment they have intercourse, the animal is still physically present. Even though she sanctified it, she has not yet actually used it or removed it from the scene; it remains present when the act is taking place, and therefore the act still defines it as *etnan*.

The *gemara* suggests that one could resolve Rav Hoshaia's question from the language Rabbi Elazar used — he specified *hikrivasah*, that she *brought* it as a sacrifice, which *davka* implies the animal was entirely out of the picture. If *hikdashasah* — merely sanctifying it — were equally sufficient, Rabbi Elazar would have stated that case rather than the more extreme one. This implies that sanctification alone does not save it from *etnan* status, since the animal remains physically present.

The *gemara* deflects this inference: it says that Rav Hoshaia's question is actually about Rabbi Elazar's own intent. Perhaps Rabbi Elazar himself was uncertain about the sanctification case, and therefore leaped to the clearer case of sacrifice — not because sanctification is obviously insufficient, but because he himself did not know the answer. The *gemara* concludes: *teiku*.

## Defend Yochanan

### Contradiction

The *gemara* now raises a direct contradiction to Rabbi Yochanan's ruling. A *braisa* explicitly states: if the act of intercourse occurred first and the payment was given afterward — even up to three years later — the *etnan* is forbidden (*afilu mikan ad shalosh shanim, etnan assur*). This is precisely the case that Rabbi Yochanan declared permitted.

### Nachman's Resolution

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves the contradiction by distinguishing between two scenarios, based on a *braisa* that employs two formulations: *hivali bi-bitla zeh* — "have intercourse with me in exchange for this specific animal" — versus *hivali bitla stam* — a general promise of payment without identifying a particular animal.

When a specific animal is designated at the moment of the act, the animal, though not yet in her possession, is already *identified* as the payment for that act. It is present, it is earmarked, and the act defines its status at that moment. Therefore, even if the physical transfer of the animal occurs three years later, the *etnan* status attaches — because the connection between the act and this particular animal was established at the moment of the act itself.

When no specific animal was identified at the time of the act — when the promise was general and no particular object was pointed to — then no object was present or earmarked at the moment of the act. Whatever is subsequently given in payment is not defined by that act; it is simply an outstanding debt being discharged. This is the case Rabbi Yochanan permitted.

### Non-Jewish Prostitute

The *gemara* presses further: even in the case where a specific animal is designated, it has not actually been given to her — she has not taken possession. *Ha mechusar meshicha* — it still requires *meshichah*, the act of taking physical possession, to effect transfer. So how does the designation of a specific animal give it *etnan* status?

The *gemara* answers: the case involves a non-Jewish prostitute. A non-Jew acquires movable property not through *meshichah* but through payment of money. Here, the giving of the services functions as the payment — it is the act of intercourse that constitutes the consideration. Therefore, at the precise moment the services are rendered, she acquires ownership of the designated animal. Possession transfers at the very instant of the act, and so the animal is simultaneously in her possession and defined as payment — exactly satisfying Rabbi Yochanan's requirement.

### Apoteke Lien

The *gemara* offers a second resolution for those who would apply this to a Jewish prostitute, where *kinyan chatzer* might be invoked if the animal happened to be in her yard. But the *braisa* speaks of delivery occurring three years later, which seems to preclude the animal having been in her yard at the time of the act.

The *gemara* answers with the concept of *apotiki*: the man declared this specific animal to be the *apotiki* — the lien — for his obligation. He said to her: if I bring you the money between now and a certain date, keep it; but if I fail to pay, take the animal in satisfaction of the debt. Under such an arrangement, although the animal was not physically delivered until years later, she held a legal property interest — a lien — in that specific animal from the moment the obligation arose. At the time of the act, this lien already existed with respect to a specifically identified object. It is therefore as though she had rights in that animal during the act itself, and it acquires the status of *etnan*, even though the physical transfer did not occur for three years.

The concept is noted in passing to have a cognate in modern languages — the word *apoteken* in certain languages carries the sense of a mortgage or pledge, likely from Greek or Latin.

The conclusion of this section is that Rabbi Yochanan's position is coherent but requires careful reading of the *braisot*: the *etnan* prohibition applies only when a specific object is present and owned — or subject to an existing lien — at the moment the act takes place, not when the payment is simply an outstanding debt to be discharged later.

## Workers' Food

### Rav Sheshet Q

The *gemara* now shifts to a related question raised by Rav Sheshet, which directly tests the principle that the *etnan* and *shevi'it* cases have been establishing. If an employer gives money to his donkey-drivers and workers to buy their own lunch (*lekhu ve-ikhlu be-dinar zeh*), does he bear any halachic responsibility for what they choose to purchase?

### Prohibited Foods

Three categories of concern are enumerated. First, *shevi'it*: the workers might take the money, go to a seller who is doing commerce in *shemittah* produce unlawfully, and purchase it — drawing the employer into a transaction involving *kedushat shevi'it*. Second, *terumos u'ma'asros*: if the workers are *amei ha'aretz* who purchase fruit from which tithes have not been properly separated, and the employer's money is what funded that purchase. Third, *yayin nesech*: if the workers are non-Jews and they purchase *yayin nesech* with the employer's funds.

### Rashi's Read

The question, as Rashi frames it, is not primarily about *lifnei iver* — not merely whether the employer is enabling others to sin by providing funds that might be spent on prohibited goods. The concern is sharper: is it as though the employer himself has *fed* his workers those prohibited foods? The *gemara* in *Sukkot* is cited by Rashi: one may not give *d'mei peiros shevi'it* — money in the place of *shevi'it* produce — to an *am ha'aretz* beyond what is needed for three meals, because he will not observe the laws of *shevi'it*. And with respect to *terumos u'ma'asros*, Rashi notes explicitly: since these workers are *amei ha'aretz* who will buy *eino me'ushar*, the employer is, on this framing, effectively feeding them untithed produce. Similarly for *yayin nesech*: since the employer has a legal obligation to provide his workers with meals, if we view his money as having directly purchased the food they ate, he has satisfied that financial obligation with *yayin nesech* and thereby derived benefit from it.

### Separate Transaction

The *gemara* answers that in the straightforward case — the employer gives the workers money in advance, and they go and make their own purchases — the employer bears no responsibility. The workers' choices are their own. We do not re-frame the transaction as the employer having purchased and fed them that food. Since the employer did not designate which specific goods the money would buy, the employer's funds are simply fungible money, and any subsequent purchase is a separate transaction initiated by the workers themselves.

### I Will Pay

However, the second case is different. If the employer says to his workers: *tze'u ve-ichlu va-ani pore'ah* — go out and eat, and I will pay the bill — then the employer *is* the one directly paying for whatever food they consume. In that case he is concerned about *shevi'it*, *ma'aser*, and *yayin nesech*, because he is now directly funding a specific food transaction, and we do treat it as though he fed them that food.

---

## Running Tab

### Chisda's View

Rav Chisda introduces a refinement. The distinction between the permissible and problematic case is not simply whether the employer said "here is the money" versus "go eat and I will pay." Rather, it turns on whether the storekeeper has a regular credit relationship with the employer (*chanvani ha-makifo*). If the storekeeper habitually extends credit to this employer, then the moment the storekeeper gives the workers their food, the employer becomes immediately obligated in the money to the storekeeper — *dimishtabed lei*. The debt is created instantaneously, not after a bill is rendered. In that case, it is as though the employer paid at the moment the food was given, and we regard him as having purchased and fed them that food.

### No Tab

By contrast, if there is no such ongoing credit relationship between this employer and this storekeeper, the bill merely comes later as a separate act, and the transaction is not regarded as having been direct on the employer's part.

### Raba's Resolution

Raba, however, rejects Rav Chisda's distinction. Whether or not there is a running-tab relationship, the employer always owes the storekeeper money once the storekeeper has given food to his workers at his direction. As the *gemara* demonstrates from a ruling elsewhere: if one says to another, *tein maneh li-ploni veyiknu kol nechasai lach* — give a hundred *zuz* to so-and-so and my entire estate shall transfer to you — this works as a valid transaction, on the logic that payment made on one's behalf is as if one received it directly. So too here: when the storekeeper gives food to the workers on the employer's behalf, it is as if the employer himself received the food and gave it to them, and he simultaneously becomes obligated to pay the storekeeper. This obligation exists regardless of whether there is a running tab.

### Outstanding Debt

Raba therefore rules that the distinction has nothing to do with the nature of the credit relationship. What matters is something different entirely: whether a specific sum of money has been pre-identified or designated. If the employer has not pre-designated a particular *dinar* or specific funds as the payment for this food, then what exists is simply an *outstanding debt* — an unspecified obligation to pay whatever the bill comes to, with no particular money identified as the consideration. An outstanding debt, where no specific object or amount has been earmarked, does not function like a simultaneous exchange. It is merely a financial obligation to be discharged later, and it does not render the employer's position equivalent to that of someone who directly purchased and distributed the food.

---

## Running Tab…

### Pre-payment Problem

The only case in which the employer would truly be regarded as having directly fed his workers the prohibited food is if he had pre-paid the storekeeper — given him specific funds in advance — and the storekeeper then distributed food to the workers out of those pre-paid funds. In that scenario, the prohibited food was directly purchased with money the employer already handed over, and there is no gap or separation between the employer's payment and the workers' receipt of the food.

### Text Reconciliation

The *braisa* that presents the problematic case — *tze'u ve-ichlu va-ani pore'ah* — must therefore be understood as describing either this pre-payment scenario, or the case in which the employer physically took the food from the storekeeper and handed it directly to the workers. The phrase *va-ani pore'ah* on this reading is reconciled by Rav Ashi as *kegon she-natal ve-natan be-yad* — the employer received the food from the storekeeper and placed it in the workers' hands himself. The language is somewhat strained, and the *gemara* acknowledges the difficulty — other *amoraim* proposed emending the text of the *braisa* to read *va-ani mchashev* (I will settle the account) or *telu ve-ichlu* (take it and eat) — but the underlying principle is clear: the employer is liable only if the money or the prohibited food was directly in his hands at the moment of the transfer.

## Practical Result

### Oranges Applied

This ruling now feeds directly back into the case of Rav Yanai's workers and the *shevi'it* oranges. When the fruit borrowed during *shemittah* was repaid in the eighth year, the repayment was simply the discharge of an outstanding debt. There was no pre-designated sum or specific object identified at the time of the original loan as the consideration. The original *shevi'it* fruit had long since been consumed; nothing was present at the moment of repayment to which the *kedushah* could transfer. The repaid fruit is therefore ordinary produce, free of *kedushat shevi'it*.

### Direct Principle

The broader principle is now in sharp relief: a transaction implicates an employer — or gives a prohibited status to money or produce — only when the money or the specific object is identified and present at the moment of the prohibited transfer. Where the connection between payment and prohibited item is established only after the fact, through the discharge of an outstanding and unspecified obligation, the two acts are treated as separate, and no status transfers between them.

## Breaking Barrels

### Breaking Q

The *gemara* closes the daf with a brief but pointed question. Rav Nachman, Ulla, Avimi bar Pappa, and Rav Chiya bar Ami were sitting together when they raised the following: if someone is hired to break barrels that had contained *yayin nesech* — not to transport the wine or work with it in any way, but simply to smash the empty barrels afterward — what is the *halacha*? Is this considered *mishtaker be'issurei hana'ah*, being paid for work in connection with a forbidden item?

The question has a slightly paradoxical edge: one might say that the worker *wants* the barrels to persist until his task begins, since without them there is no work and no pay. In that sense, he has a vested interest in the continued existence of objects associated with *yayin nesech* — similar to the perverse logic of someone who would want their *chametz* to remain a bit longer so they can enjoy the *mitzvah* of burning it. Does this interest in the barrel's persistence constitute a forbidden connection to the prohibited item?

### Nachman Ruling

Rav Nachman answers with characteristic brevity and warmth: *yishbor ve-tavo alav berachah* — let him break them and be blessed. His work is part of the process of eliminating and destroying objects associated with *yayin nesech*. We do not apply the strictures of *mishtaker be'issurei hana'ah* to someone whose labor contributes to the removal of the prohibited item from the world. The *gemara* will continue from this point in the following session.
