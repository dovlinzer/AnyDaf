# Avodah Zarah 61 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wine of Non-Jews

### Overview & Transition

Daf 61 concludes the *perek*. Most of the preceding discussion has concerned different types of touch — how a non-Jew's contact with Jewish-owned wine renders it prohibited — presented through a series of individual cases without a fully systematic treatment. This final *mishna*, however, introduces a distinct situation: wine that is owned by, or under the auspices of, a non-Jew. Whereas earlier cases addressed Jewish-owned wine and the circumstances under which non-Jewish contact creates a problem, here the starting point is the non-Jew's ownership itself. Whether this constitutes a conceptually distinct legal category, or merely a heightened version of the same concern, will be one of the central questions of the *daf*.

### Metaher & Tumah

The *mishna* opens with the phrase *hametaher yeino shel ovdei kochavim* — one who comes to *metaher*, to purify, the wine of non-Jews. The choice of the word *metaher* is striking and worth dwelling on. *Metaher* implies the existence of *tumah*, impurity, and it is no accident that the conceptual vocabulary of *tumah v'taharah* has become the dominant framework for thinking about this entire subject. The analogy is structurally apt: in the laws of *tumah*, an impure object communicates its impurity through touch, contaminating and invalidating whatever it contacts. With wine, the parallel is precise — Jewish wine is touched by a non-Jew and thereby rendered invalid. This was made explicit at the end of the preceding *daf*, where the *Gemara* drew a direct analogy between a *zav* and a non-Jew: just as a *zav* transmits *tumah* through various forms of direct and indirect contact, so too a non-Jew renders wine *tamei* through analogous modes of contact. The conceptual borrowings run even deeper — questions about whether a poured liquid constitutes one unit or two, whether touching the side of a barrel through an opening counts as contact, all of these are drawn from the world of *tumah v'taharah*.

### Non-Jews as Tamei

But this goes beyond legal analogy. The Rabbis explicitly treated non-Jews as *tamei*, categorizing them as *zavim* and *niddos* in certain respects. In a world where *tumah* and *tahara* no longer carry the practical weight they once did — when *kodshim* and *taharos* are no longer the daily preoccupation they were in the Tannaitic period — the one domain where the *tumah* of non-Jews continues to find expression is precisely in their contact with wine. The psychological and religious experience of wine contamination, then, is framed not primarily through the lens of *avoda zara*, but through the older and more visceral categories of *tumah v'taharah*. The non-Jew's touch is experienced as contamination in a quasi-ritual sense, and the discourse of *metaher* — purification — reflects how deeply that framework has been internalized.

## Case 1: Locked House

### Setup & Storage

The *mishna* then describes the first case: *v'nosno birshuso* — after processing the wine entirely on behalf of the non-Jew (who was kept away from contact throughout), the Jew places the filled barrels into the non-Jew's house. The non-Jew owns the wine; the Jew has processed it and is now storing it in the non-Jew's domain. The concern is that the non-Jew, feeling comfortable in his own home with his own wine, might quietly perform a libation to *avoda zara*. The *mishna* asks: under what conditions need we fear this, and under what conditions may we rely on available safeguards?

### Tosafot's Reading

*Tosafot*'s reading — which is the more cogent interpretation, and which corresponds to what was ultimately *Rashi*'s own final version before he raised difficulties with it — is that the case presupposes the Jew holds *maftei'ach v'chosam*, a lock and seal: the Jew has the key to the house and has affixed a seal to the door or barrels such that any unauthorized entry would be detectable. Given this, the non-Jew cannot simply walk in unnoticed; he would have to visibly break the seal or pick the lock, actions that expose him to scrutiny.

### Public Domain Effect

The *mishna* then adds that the house must be *patuach lirshus harabbim* — open to the public domain — and located in a city containing both Jews and non-Jews. The presence of Jewish passersby in the public street means that a non-Jew seen furtively attempting to access a locked and sealed house would appear suspicious. He knows this, and the deterrent effect is sufficient.

### Permission Conditions

Accordingly, in such a city — one with a mixed Jewish and non-Jewish population — the wine is *mutar*. But *b'ir she'kulo ovdei kochavim*, in an entirely non-Jewish city, it is *assur*. Non-Jewish bystanders would not provide the same deterrent: they would understand the context, see no reason to intervene, and might simply ignore the owner entering what is, after all, technically his own property. In such a case, a Jewish presence is required.

### Supervision Methods

The *mishna* clarifies, however, that the required supervision need not be continuous. *Ad she'yeishev umeshamer* — until you have a Jew sitting and watching — does not mean a Jew must be stationed there at all times. *Af al pi she'hu yotzei v'nichnas*, even if the supervisor comes and goes periodically, it is *mutar*. This is the mode of oversight known as *yotzei v'nichnas*, a form of *hashgachah* discussed at the very opening of *Masechet Chullin*: if a responsible party makes surprise visits at unpredictable intervals, that is sufficient to keep behavior honest. The non-Jew never knows when someone might appear, and this uncertainty functions as an effective deterrent.

## Wine of Non-Jews (II)

### Misleading Language

One might pause at the *mishna*'s opening phrase and note that *metaher* is actually misleading. The case described involves wine that has not yet been rendered *tamei* — the Jew processed the grapes himself, keeping the non-Jew away throughout. There is nothing to purify; the goal is *preventing* contamination from occurring in the first place. Once a non-Jew has actually handled wine and rendered it *stam yeinam*, there is no mechanism for *tahara* after the fact. The *mishna*'s language implies that the Jew is restoring something that had been lost, when in fact he is preserving something that was never compromised. What is implicit, perhaps, is that the only viable approach is to keep the non-Jew away during production — and the *mishna* then turns to the separate question of how to maintain that status during storage. Still, the phrasing is exaggerated, and the point stands.

## Case 2: Receipt

### Receipt Transfer

The second case presented by the *mishna* is *hametaher yeino shel ovdei kochavim v'nosno birshuso* — again, the wine is in the non-Jew's domain — but this time the Jew holds a receipt. The non-Jew has written *hitkabalti mimcha maot*, "I have received money from you," which functions as an acknowledgment that the wine now effectively belongs to the Jew, even if the actual payment has not yet been made. The receipt may be written in advance of the Jew actually paying, with the understanding that the Jew will sell the wine, collect the proceeds, and pay the non-Jew. But because the non-Jew has formally acknowledged Jewish ownership, his psychological relationship to the wine changes — he recognizes it as the Jew's property and is less inclined to treat it as his own to dispose of as he wishes. In this case, *mimcha maot mutar* — it is permissible.

### Beis She'an Case

The contrast case makes the principle clear. *Aval im rotze Yisrael lehotzi v'eino manicho ad sheyiten lo et meotav* — if the non-Jew has not truly relinquished control, and demands payment before allowing the Jew to remove the wine, then the non-Jew retains effective dominion. The Jew cannot exercise independent control over what is nominally his wine. *Ze haya ma'ase b'Veis She'an ve'asru* — this situation arose in Beis She'an and the Rabbis forbade it. The non-Jew's insistence on prior payment signals that he considers the wine still within his sphere of control, and given that it remains in his house with no lock-and-seal arrangement, the concern that he will take license with it is very real.

## Case 1: Locked House (II)

### Rashi vs. Tosafot

*Rashi*'s reading of the *mishna* differs substantially from *Tosafot*'s, and the difference reveals a fundamental conceptual disagreement. *Rashi* conflates the two cases of the *mishna* — the locked house and the receipt — treating them as cumulative requirements. According to this reading, both are necessary: the house must be accessible to a *reshus harabbim* where Jews might observe suspicious activity, *and* the non-Jew must have issued a receipt acknowledging the Jew's ownership. The underlying logic, which *Tosafot* makes explicit in citing *Rashi*, is the principle *havei k'yeino shel Yisrael* — it must become, in effect, the Jew's wine. For *Rashi*, and apparently for the *Rambam* as well, there is a categorical distinction: wine that belongs to a non-Jew is *stam yeinam* and presumptively problematic; wine that belongs to a Jew requires an actual event of contact before we begin worrying about the specific circumstances. These are not merely different points on a continuum of likelihood — they are different legal categories. The way to make non-Jewish wine permissible, on this reading, is to render it *not* non-Jewish wine: to establish Jewish ownership firmly enough that it falls into the other category.

*Tosafot* explicitly rejects this framework. In the first case of the *mishna*, the Jew has not paid and the non-Jew is the complete owner — yet *Tosafot* reads this as permissible with adequate safeguards. For *Tosafot*, there is ultimately one category: the question is always what is the likelihood that the non-Jew has touched the wine? When the non-Jew owns the wine and the house, that likelihood is substantially higher, and accordingly more robust safeguards are required. But the difference is quantitative, not categorical. As one might put it in contemporary terms: *Rashi* and the *Rambam* see a distinction between a Jew who has taken title to wine versus one who merely holds it on consignment with ownership remaining in the non-Jew; *Tosafot* sees only a spectrum of risk. This debate — conceptual categories versus likelihood analysis — is what structures much of the subsequent *Gemara*.

## Gemara Analysis

### Traveling Merchants

The *Gemara* challenges the *mishna*'s ruling that an all-non-Jewish city renders the wine *assur*. Even there, the *Gemara* asks, *v'ha'ika rochlin hamachzirin ba'ayarot* — there are Jewish spice merchants and peddlers who travel from town to town and periodically pass through. On *Tosafot*'s reading, one might wonder: what does it matter that a complete stranger passes by? Such a person would not know the context, would not recognize that the non-Jew is doing something suspicious with the locked house. *Tosafot*'s answer is precisely the point: the house is locked and sealed, so any non-Jew seen fiddling with the lock would appear furtive and criminal regardless of who is watching. Any Jew passing through who notices this will recognize that something is amiss. Even the occasional passage of Jewish merchants through an otherwise non-Jewish city is therefore sufficient to deter the non-Jew from attempting unauthorized entry — because looking like a thief in front of even one knowledgeable observer is too great a risk.

### Walled Cities

*Shmuel* addresses the case where even this deterrent is absent. *B'ir she'yesh la dlasayim u'vriach* — a walled city with controlled entry points, sealed at night, where strangers are either excluded or immediately identifiable — in such a city, no outsider would pass unnoticed, and the non-Jew knows it. The protection of anonymity-in-a-crowd is unavailable, and this changes the calculus. Even the occasional passage of Jewish merchants becomes unreliable as a deterrent when the city controls access so tightly that the non-Jew can account for every person present.

### Substitute Surveillance

*Rav Yosef* introduces several alternative forms of constructive surveillance that function equivalently to a *reshus harabbim*. *V'chalon k'reshus harabbim dami* — a window with a sightline from a Jewish neighbor's house is treated like a public domain; a neighbor who can observe the entrance to the storage house provides equivalent deterrence. *V'ashpah k'reshus harabbim dami* — similarly, a junkyard adjacent to the house creates a form of surveillance: people periodically come to forage or deposit garbage, and their random presence introduces enough uncertainty to deter illicit access. *V'dikla k'reshus harabbim dami* — a palm tree near the house is equivalent, since one who climbs to harvest dates gains an elevated sightline over the surrounding area, and a non-Jew contemplating unauthorized entry knows this.

### Barren Palm Tree

This last case generates a dispute. What if the top of the palm tree has been cut off and it no longer bears dates — *psik reisha* of its productive function? Is the non-Jew still deterred by its presence? *Rav Acha* and *Ravina* disagree. The one who forbids the wine reasons that without dates, no one has any reason to climb the palm tree, so the non-Jew has no cause to fear observation from above. The one who permits it counters: *zimnin d'avda leh beheima v'salik l'iyunei basrah* — sometimes a person loses an animal and climbs a tall tree to survey the neighborhood and locate it. A palm tree, even a barren one, affords an excellent vantage point, and the possibility that someone might ascend for this reason is sufficient to introduce deterrence. The underlying principle, as *Tosafot* emphasizes throughout, is that even a slight possibility of being observed — any credible chance of being seen and recognized as behaving suspiciously — is enough to deter the non-Jew from attempting to access a locked and sealed house.

## Baraita Cases

### Jew Owns Both

A *baraita* lays out four cases systematically. The first: a Jew purchases or rents a house in a courtyard owned by non-Jews and fills it with wine — wine that belongs to the Jew, in a house belonging to the Jew, but situated within a non-Jewish courtyard. If a Jew also lives in that courtyard, it is *mutar*, even without a *mafteach v'chosam*. The contrast with the *mishna* is instructive: in the *mishna*, the non-Jew owns the house and the wine, and a locked seal is essential because the owner feels entitled to enter freely. Here, where the Jew owns both house and wine, the non-Jew has no sense of entitlement toward the property — but his ease of physical access through the shared courtyard remains a concern. The presence of a Jewish resident in that courtyard, who would notice any suspicious activity, is sufficient without additional locks.

### Different Courtyard

If no Jews live in that courtyard but Jews reside in a neighboring courtyard, it remains *mutar*, but only *im hu she'hamifteach v'chosam b'yado* — only if the house is locked and sealed and the key is held by the Jew. Physical distance reduces the deterrent value of neighboring Jewish presence, and the lock-and-seal arrangement compensates.

### Non-Jew Owns Both

The third case: the wine belongs to the non-Jew and is stored in the non-Jew's house, but a Jew lives in that same courtyard. This is *mutar*, but here the *baraita* reinstates the requirement of *mifteach v'chosam*. The non-Jew's ownership of both the wine and the house means he feels fully at liberty to enter — Jewish proximity alone is not sufficient to override this sense of entitlement. The lock and seal are necessary in conjunction with the Jewish presence.

### Yochanan Lenient

*Rabbi Yochanan* intervenes and instructs the *tanna* to amend this ruling: *tani af al pi she'ein mifteach v'chosam b'yado mutar* — even without a lock and seal, if a Jew is living in the courtyard, it is permissible. *Rabbi Yochanan*'s reasoning is that a Jew actually residing in the courtyard creates a constant, organic presence that is more intimidating than a merely physical barrier. The non-Jew knows that his neighbor is there day and night, can step outside at any moment, and would notice immediately if anything were amiss. This ongoing presence provides a more effective deterrent than a lock that a determined person might eventually overcome.

### Jew Elsewhere

The fourth case: the non-Jew owns the wine and the house, but no Jew lives in that courtyard — the Jew is in a different courtyard entirely. Here, according to the *baraita*, even a lock and seal is insufficient: *divrei Rabbi Meir*. *V'chachamim osrim* — the *Chachamim* also rule it is forbidden — *ad sheyeih shomer yoshev umishammer*, unless a guard is present and watching, *o ad sheyavo memuneh haval kitin*, or a designated overseer who comes at appointed times.

## Case 4 Debate

### Rabbi Meir Strict

In the fourth case — non-Jew's wine, non-Jew's house, Jew in a different courtyard — *Rabbi Meir* holds that even a lock and seal on the door does not suffice. The non-Jew is the complete owner of both wine and house, feels total entitlement, faces no nearby Jewish presence, and could act with relative impunity. The mere physical barrier of a lock is not enough to overcome the cumulative weight of these factors.

### Chachamim View

The *Chachamim* similarly forbid the wine in this configuration unless either a guard is permanently stationed there or an overseer makes periodic visits. At first glance, this appears puzzling: the *Chachamim* seem to be responding to a lenient position, but *Rabbi Meir* himself has already declared it forbidden. The *Gemara* therefore asks precisely which case the *Chachamim* are addressing.

The *Gemara* rules out the possibility that they are responding to the fourth case (non-Jewish wine, non-Jewish house, Jew in a different courtyard), because the *tanna kamma* — including *Rabbi Meir* — has already forbidden that outright. The *Gemara* also rules out the third case (same courtyard), because *Rabbi Yochanan* has ruled so leniently there — not even requiring a lock and seal — that it would be strange to place the *Chachamim*'s strict requirement of a permanent guard in response to it. The *Gemara* therefore locates the *Chachamim*'s statement as addressing the second half of the first case: the Jew owns the wine and the house, but is living in a neighboring courtyard rather than the same one. The *tanna kamma* had ruled this permissible with a lock and seal; the *Chachamim* insist that even this is insufficient if there is no *reshus harabbim* passing by, since the only person positioned to notice anything is in the other courtyard and cannot actually see what is happening.

### Inspection Types

The *Gemara* then addresses the *Chachamim*'s alternative of a *memuneh haval kitin* — an overseer who comes at appointed times. This is puzzling in a different direction: fixed inspection times are arguably worse than nothing, since the non-Jew simply behaves properly during those predictable intervals and acts freely at all other times. The *Gemara* therefore adjusts the reading: *ella ad sheyavo memuneh she'eino ba l'kitin* — the requirement is an overseer who comes *without* fixed appointments, making surprise inspections. It is the very unpredictability of the visits that maintains the deterrent effect. This directly parallels the *yotzei v'nichnas* principle established in the *mishna* and discussed at the opening of *Chullin*: it is not the quantity of oversight but its unpredictability that keeps behavior honest.

## Shimon ben Elazar

### Two Readings

*Rabban Shimon ben Elazar* concludes the *mishna* with the statement: *reshus ovdei kochavim achas hi* — the domain of non-Jews is one. The *Gemara* immediately notes that this is ambiguous: *le-hakel o le-hachmir* — is he ruling leniently or strictly? *Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Ze'iri* says he is being lenient; *Rav Nachman* in the name of *Ze'iri* says he is being strict.

### Lenient: Own Domain

On the lenient reading (*Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Ze'iri*), the *tanna kamma* is the strict one. The *tanna kamma* holds that when the non-Jew's wine is stored in *any* non-Jewish domain — even a different non-Jew's house — the same precautions apply as when it is in the owner's own domain, because *v'chaishinan legomlin*: we are concerned about reciprocity. The non-Jew who owns the wine might approach the non-Jew who is housing it and say, in effect: allow me access to this wine for my libation needs, and when the situation is reversed, I will do the same for you. This is not necessarily an explicit arrangement, but a general laissez-faire attitude among non-Jews toward one another's religious needs. *Rabban Shimon ben Elazar* disagrees and is lenient: *b'mah devarim amurim bireshuso* — the precautions apply only when the wine is in the owner's own domain. *Aval bireshus oved kochavim acher mutar* — if it is in a different non-Jew's domain, it is permissible, *v'lo chaishinan legomlin* — and we are not concerned about reciprocal favors. The phrase *reshus ovdei kochavim achas hi* is then read as a question the *tanna kamma* might pose to *Rabban Shimon ben Elazar*: "Do you really treat all non-Jewish domains as identical?" And *Rabban Shimon ben Elazar* responds: no, I do not.

### Strict: Reciprocity

On the strict reading (*Rav Nachman* in the name of *Ze'iri*), the roles are reversed. The *tanna kamma* is lenient: precautions are required only *bireshuto*, in the owner's own domain; if the wine is stored with a different non-Jew, it is *mutar*, since the non-Jew who is responsible for it has no personal stake in the wine and would not casually compromise his custodial role. *Rabban Shimon ben Elazar* is strict: *kol reshus oved kochavim achas hi* — all non-Jewish domains are treated as one because of mutual cover — *mipnei harama'in*, on account of those who are devious. Non-Jews, he holds, will accommodate one another's religious practices regardless of ownership boundaries, and we therefore cannot distinguish between the owner's domain and any other non-Jewish domain.

### Tanya Support

A *braita* provides explicit support for the strict reading of *Rabban Shimon ben Elazar*: *amar Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, kol reshus oved kochavim achas hi mipnei harama'in* — his statement is declarative, not interrogative, and he explicitly grounds it in concern about deceptive reciprocity. The *halacha*, however, follows the lenient position — the *tanna kamma* on the strict reading, which limits concern to the owner's own domain. This is the appropriate conclusion not only because it produces a more lenient outcome, but because it reflects a more measured assessment of non-Jews rather than a conspiratorial one. When one enters into a business relationship with a non-Jew and entrusts him with wine, the concern is realistic when all factors point toward the owner's comfort and entitlement in his own space; it does not extend to a general assumption that all non-Jews collude to subvert Jewish interests.

## Gomlin Analysis

### Parzik Rufla Case

The *Gemara* offers a practical case that tests the limits of the *gomlin* principle. In the house of *Parzik Rufla* — *rufla* denoting a figure of high status, a second-in-command to the king — a Jew had deposited wine with a non-Jewish sharecropper (*arisa*) who worked *Parzik Rufla*'s land. This is a case of wine stored in a different non-Jew's domain: not the domain of the owner (*Parzik Rufla*) himself, but of someone subservient to him.

### Equals Reciprocity

The Rabbis sitting before *Rava* initially proposed that the concern of *gomlin* — reciprocal favors — applies only when the two non-Jews are social equals: I do you a favor, you do me a favor. That arrangement presupposes a relationship of mutual capacity and mutual need. But a lowly *arisa* could never be in a position to ask *Parzik Rufla* to watch wine on his behalf; the social gulf is too vast. Therefore, there is no realistic prospect of reciprocal accommodation, and the wine should be permissible.

### Rava Reversal

*Rava* counters with the opposite argument. Even granting that we would not normally worry about *gomlin* in an unequal relationship, the very inequality here creates a different and arguably greater problem: *kivan d'mirsas minei, machpia leh zechuseihu* — precisely because the *arisa* is subservient to and intimidated by *Parzik Rufla*, he will cover up for him. The sharecropper will not resist if *Parzik Rufla* arrives and demands access to the wine; he will look the other way, perhaps even actively facilitate it, to avoid antagonizing his powerful superior. Where equals might weigh the risk of being seen compromising their custodial role, a subservient person simply defers to the superior. The situation is therefore more dangerous, not less.

## Practical Applicatio (II)

### Wine Barrels Case

The *Gemara* closes the *sugya* with a practical case: in a certain city, Jewish wine was stored in barrels in the street. A non-Jew was found standing among those barrels. The question is whether we suspect he touched the wine and must therefore forbid it.

### Caught Criterion

*Rava* formulates the decisive criterion: *nitpas alav ke-ganav* — would he be caught like a thief? If the city is one where a non-Jew found tampering with barrels not belonging to him would face legal consequences — would be seized and held accountable — then the wine is *mutar*. Under those conditions, we may presume he did not touch the wine, because the risk of detection and punishment is too great. *V'ei lo assur* — but if it is the kind of place where such behavior carries no consequence, the wine is forbidden, because he has little to fear from having been found there. This criterion is directly analogous to the cases discussed throughout the *perek*: the controlling question is always how much liberty the non-Jew believes he has and how much risk he perceives in acting on it. The locked house, the seal, the Jewish passersby, the window sightline, the surprise inspections — all of these are instruments that increase the perceived risk and thereby remove the inclination to act. Where no such deterrents exist, we cannot rely on his restraint. And this, once again, confirms *Tosafot*'s reading: there is no categorical difference between Jewish-owned and non-Jewish-owned wine as a matter of fundamental legal status. In every case, the analysis comes down to the likelihood of unauthorized contact, assessed through the realistic conditions of deterrence and exposure.

## Conclusion

### Rules Summary

Very uncharacteristically, *Rashi* — or more precisely, *Rashi*'s students, as the text makes explicit after the *hadran* — appended a section at the end of the *perek* attempting to systematize what the *Gemara* itself left scattered across numerous individual cases. The paragraph is introduced as *maskana de-milta* — the bottom line — and represents a rare attempt at codification by a commentator who was not himself a codifier. That this was felt necessary is itself testimony to how unsystematic the *perek* was: the Talmud presented a vast range of cases involving different modes of contact without clearly articulating the organizing principles.

### Intent & Touch

The summary works through the relevant variables. *Maga oved kochavim kivan d'nitkaven liga she-shichshach, af al pi she-lo nizak, assur b'hana'ah* — if a non-Jew intended to touch the wine and moved his hand around among it, even without making a poured libation, the wine is forbidden even in benefit. *She-lo b'chavana, kegon she-lo hikkir bo she-hu yayin* — if he touched it without intent, not even knowing it was wine, it is *mutar b'hana'ah*, permissible in benefit. The analysis then proceeds through the full range of cases: direct touch and indirect contact, *kocho* (force applied indirectly), *kavanah* versus its absence, and whether in each case the wine is *mutar b'hana'ah* or *mutar b'shtiyah* — permissible in benefit only or also for drinking.

### Rashi's Ruling

The paragraph concludes with the note that *kach higgia ha-Rav u-pasak halacha le-ma'aseh* — "this is what the master [*Rashi*] arrived at and ruled as practical *halacha*." *Rashi*'s conclusions on these matters are not identical to those of all the *Rishonim*, but the attempt itself is significant: it acknowledges that the *perek*, for all its richness, did not leave the reader with a clear practical guide, and that someone had to do the work of integration.

## Practical Applicatio (II)

### Transport Distinction

*Tosafot* addresses one final practical question that the foregoing analysis raises: if a non-Jew is transporting Jewish wine from one city to another for sale, why is that not analogous to the case of a non-Jew left alone with wine and no deterrent to contact? *Tosafot*'s answer draws on the fundamental principle of perceived risk. In the *mishna*'s cases, the non-Jew exerted no effort to access the wine; he is simply present near it, and the question is whether he will take the opportunity to touch it for religious purposes. In that situation, there is little countervailing deterrence from consequences — he has nothing material to lose. But a non-Jew transporting wine for profit has a strong economic stake in the cargo's integrity. If he were to tamper with the wine and be caught, he would lose his commission and his livelihood, and face potential legal liability. That fear of consequences is itself a sufficient deterrent, and *Tosafot* rules that we are not concerned about contact in such a case. The principle is the same as *Rava*'s criterion of *nitpas alav ke-ganav* — it is the fear of being caught and suffering consequences that determines whether we may trust the non-Jew's restraint. With this ruling, the *perek* draws to its close.
