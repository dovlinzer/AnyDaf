# Yevamot 107 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mi'un Overview

### Perek Intro

Today's daf opens a new *perek* — *Perek Beit Shammai* — which, despite its name, is not exclusively focused on *Beit Shammai* but rather opens with a series of debates between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel*, returning us to the world of *yibum*.

### Mi'un Definition

The *perek* centers on the institution of *mi'un* — refusal by a minor girl. The background is as follows: a girl whose father has died may be married off by her mother and brothers. Even though the marriage is of rabbinic force only, and even though she actually assents to it (*mi-da'atah*), she retains the right to be *mema'enet* — to refuse her husband — until she reaches adulthood. The critical and defining feature of *mi'un* is its retroactive effect: unlike a *get*, which dissolves a marriage that existed, *mi'un* renders the marriage as though it never took place at all. She is considered to have never been married. This distinction carries significant practical implications, not least for matters of *kehunah* status.

### Marriage Stages

The *perek* raises questions about precisely at which stages of the marriage process *mi'un* may or may not be exercised, and what its implications are for *yibum* when a husband dies and a brother-in-law enters the picture.

---

## Arusot/Nesuot Debate

### Shammai Position

The *Mishnah* opens: *Beit Shammai omrim, ein mema'anin ela arusot* — only girls who are still in the *arusah* stage, the stage of betrothal, may perform *mi'un*. Once the girl has become a *nesuah* — once she has begun actually living with her husband and the marriage has become a lived reality — *Beit Shammai* holds that *mi'un* is no longer available to her. The marriage has reached a qualitatively different level when it has moved beyond the purely legal and technical into the genuinely lived.

### Hillel Position

*Beit Hillel* disagrees: *arusot u-nesuot* — at either stage, whether she is an *arusah* or a *nesuah*, she retains the right to perform *mi'un*. The *Mishnah* continues with additional disputes. *Beit Shammai* holds that *mi'un* is effective only against the husband (*b'ba'al*) but not against the *yabam*. *Beit Hillel* holds it applies *be-ba'al u-ve-yabam* — against both the husband and the brother-in-law. *Beit Shammai* further requires that the *mi'un* be performed in the presence of the husband and before a *Beit Din*; *Beit Hillel* permits it even not in his presence and even outside of a formal court setting.

The *Mishnah* then notes that *Beit Hillel* observes that a girl may be *mema'enet* as a minor even four or five times — her mother and brothers remarry her, she refuses again, and the cycle repeats. While this is stated as a legal observation, *Beit Shammai* responds with a sharp principle.

### Hefker Concern

*Beit Shammai* declares: *ein benot Yisrael hefker* — daughters of Israel must not be treated as ownerless or unprotected. Even if the mechanism is technically her choice, allowing a girl to be serially married off and to repeatedly refuse reduces her to an object passed from one man to the next in a manner fundamentally at odds with her dignity and the protections she deserves. Therefore *Beit Shammai* rules: she may perform *mi'un* with the first husband, but thereafter she must wait until she reaches adulthood — *mamtenet ad shetagdil* — and only then marry again. The concern of *lo yehei Bnot Yisrael hefker* translates into a legal restriction: we prevent the second rabbinic marriage from being eligible for *mi'un* at all.

### Discussion

A closer look reveals that this injunction is directed not purely at her but at the institution itself. While the girl assents to each marriage — the *Gemara* will later make clear that *kiddushei ketanah* by mother and brothers requires her *da'at* — the concern of *lo yehei Bnot Yisrael hefker* operates at the structural level. The rabbis are not simply protecting her freedom; they are preventing a pattern that, however voluntary in form, compromises her standing. The *Mishnah*'s curious insertion of the word *tema'en* a second time — even in the instruction to wait until adulthood — is something the *Gemara* will address.

---

## Ein Tenai Principle

### Stipulations Fail

The *Gemara* now turns to explain *Beit Shammai*'s position. *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel*: *mai ta'ama de-Beit Shammai? Lefi she-ein tenai be-nisu'in* — because there are no effective stipulations at the stage of *nisu'in*. The underlying assumption is that in a standard marriage, while stipulations (*tenaim*) might theoretically function at the stage of *kiddushin*, once the couple reaches *nisu'in* such conditions become inoperative. And therefore, in a normal marriage, there would be no mechanism by which the marriage could be retroactively dissolved after *nisu'in* through an external stipulation.

### Tnai vs Ta'ut

This principle requires some precision. *Ein tenai be-nisu'in* is not an absolute statement that stipulations are legally impossible at the *nisu'in* stage. The *Gemara* elsewhere explains that the reason *tenaim* are considered waived at *nisu'in* is *ein adam oseh beilato beilat zenut* — a man does not want his acts of marital intimacy to be conditional, such that if the condition is not met, the sex retroactively becomes extramarital. Therefore, as a general presumption, any stipulation he might have made is considered voluntarily waived once the couple begins living together. This is distinct from *kiddushei ta'ut* — a case of fundamental error — which can retroactively nullify even a *nisu'in*-stage marriage, since it is not an external stipulation but a basic mistake about the nature of the transaction itself.

### Public Misconception

Given this background, *Beit Shammai*'s concern becomes clear: if we allow a *nesuah* to perform *mi'un* and simply walk away without a *get*, observers who see a couple who have been living together for years suddenly separate will assume there must have been a *tenai* — a stipulation — that was activated. And from that they will draw the dangerous and false inference that *tenaim* are operative at the stage of *nisu'in*. The consequence would be that people would believe they can construct conditional marriages that dissolve retroactively even in full adult marriages, leading to the erosion of the entire institution of *nisu'in*. This concern for public misconception — *ati l-meimar yesh tenai be-nisu'in* — is the driving force behind *Beit Shammai*'s position according to this explanation.

This principle of *ein tenai benisuin* became historically significant: it is the very title of a famous *sefer* compiled from the rabbinic responsa that opposed the mid-nineteenth-century proposal by French rabbis to use conditional marriage as a solution to the *agunah* problem.

### Tosafot Caveat

*Tosafot* here raises a pointed difficulty. The same *Shmuel* who formulates the reasoning of *Beit Shammai* as based on *ein tenai benisuin* appears elsewhere — in the context of *Perek HaMadir* — to hold that if a man betrothed a woman with a condition and then cohabited with her without repeating the condition (*kidshah al tenai ve-khansah stam*), she does not require a *get*, a ruling that *Abaye* and *Rava* explain according to *Beit Shammai*'s own reasoning of *ein adam oseh beilato beilat zenut*. But this implies that even according to *Beit Shammai*, in *Shmuel*'s view, *yesh tenai benisuin* under certain circumstances. *Tosafot* works through the resolution, and the essential point is that *ein tenai benisuin* is not an absolute legal impossibility — it is a presumptive rule that may yield under specific conditions, and the principle is less categorical than the polemical use of its name might suggest.

## When Mi'un Ceases

### Chuppah Stage

The *Gemara* probes the precise boundary of *Beit Shammai*'s position. Since *Beit Shammai* says only an *arusah* can perform *mi'un*, this means anyone who is no longer an *arusah* cannot — even if she has not yet become a full *nesuah*. *Rashi* highlights the logical asymmetry: *Beit Shammai* does not say "a *nesuah* cannot perform *mi'un*" — they say "only an *arusah* can." These are not equivalent. The excluded middle matters. Someone who is no longer an *arusah* but not yet a *nesuah* is also excluded.

Who occupies this intermediate status? The *Gemara* considers the case of *nichnesah lechuppah velo niv'alah* — a girl who has entered the *chuppah* but has not yet had sexual relations. She is beyond *erusin* but not yet fully *nesuah* in the sense of consummated cohabitation. Perhaps *mi'un* should work here, since the reasoning that *tenaim* fail at *nisu'in* is grounded in the concern about *beilat zenut*, and there has been no *beilah* yet?

### Chuppat Issura

The *Gemara* responds: *lefi she-ein tenai be-chuppah* — stipulations do not work even at the *chuppah* stage. The reasoning is that just as a man would not want his act of *beilah* to be retroactively recontextualized as *beilat zenut*, so too he would not want his *chuppah* — which is entirely suggestive of and oriented toward marital intimacy and cohabitation — to be retroactively rendered *chuppat issura*, a *chuppah* in a non-marital context. The *chuppah* is not merely a ceremony; it represents the crossing of the threshold into marital life, and one would not want it to have taken place under conditions that would render it as though the couple were never married. Therefore, *lo nicha lei d'teihavei chuppat issura* — he would never have allowed his *chuppah* to stand on conditional terms, meaning any *tenai* is considered waived there as well. Consequently, since there is no *tenai* operative at *chuppah*, there is also no *mi'un* after *chuppah*.

### Transfer to Agents

The *Gemara* pushes further. Even before *chuppah*, there is a stage at which the girl has left the domain of her father — or in this case her mother and brothers — and been handed over to the husband's representatives for escort to his home: *masar av li-shluchei ha-ba'al*. At this point she is no longer a pure *arusah*, but she has not yet entered *chuppah*. Here there should be no concern of *beilat zenut* or *chuppat issura* — nothing intimate has occurred or been initiated. If *tenai* could work here, why not *mi'un*?

### No Distinction

The *Gemara* answers with the principle of *lo plug rabanan* — the rabbis did not draw fine distinctions between these intermediate stages. One might be right that, technically, *tenai* could be operative in this transitional state between *erusin* and *chuppah*. But once the rabbis established that *mi'un* does not apply once a girl is no longer an *arusah*, they did not distinguish between the subtle gradations between *arusah* and *nesuah*. All of those intermediate stages are treated as connected to the concerns of *nisu'in*, and allowing *mi'un* in any of them would create precisely the false impression of *tenai be-nisu'in* that *Beit Shammai* sought to prevent.

## Bei'at Zenut Logic

### Husband Consent

*Rabba* and *Rav Yosef* offer an alternative explanation for *Beit Shammai*'s position that moves away from the concern about public misconception and engages instead with the reality of the marital relationship itself. Their reasoning: *ta'ama de-Beit Shammai lefi she-ein adam oseh beilato beilat zenut* — a man will not allow his acts of marital intimacy to be categorized as fornication.

### Sex Sanctity

The concern here is practical and relational. If we tell a man that even after *nisu'in* this young girl can at any moment perform *mi'un* and walk away — retroactively declaring that she was never his wife — then he will be unwilling to consummate the marriage at all. He does not want to be in the position of having had sexual relations with a girl who is not and never was his wife. The very integrity of the marital relationship demands that it not be subject to retroactive nullification through *mi'un*. This is not a concern about what observers will think; it is a concern about the husband's own experience of the relationship and his unwillingness to participate if its legitimacy is perpetually in abeyance.

The *Gemara* again applies this reasoning through the stages of *chuppah* and the transfer to the husband's agents, yielding the same *lo plug rabanan* conclusion — once the rabbis established the rule, they did not subdivide it by stage.

### Institution Context

*Beit Hillel* responds: *keivan d'ika kiddushin v'ketubah, lo atu l-meimar d'biato biat zenut* — since everything that happened took place within the framework of *kiddushin* and *ketubah*, no one will categorize the marital relations as *beilat zenut*. Even if *mi'un* is later performed and retroactively the marriage is dissolved, that does not recast the nature of the sex that occurred. It took place within a rabbinic institution, preceded by an act of *kiddushin* and a *ketubah*. It was not two people taking a hotel room — it was a structured, legally recognized framework. And therefore, even after *mi'un*, the sex that occurred within that framework will never be redefined as extramarital.

### Kehunah Status

This point carries significant implications. If retroactively the marriage never existed, one might ask: is the girl considered to have had sex outside of marriage, with all the *halachic* consequences that might entail? For instance, some *rishonim* hold that *zonah* status for a *kohen* applies to any woman who has had sex outside of marriage. The answer here is no — even though legally, in retrospect, there was no marriage, the sex that occurred within the rabbinic institution of *kiddushei ketanah* will never carry the stigma of extramarital relations.

This also bears on the broader question raised by the *Rambam*, who holds that any sex outside of marriage constitutes a *lav de-oraita* of *lo tihyeh kedeishah*. One might ask: how did the rabbis ever permit rabbinic marriages that are not *de-oraita* marriages, if all such sex is technically extramarital according to the *Rambam*? The answer is precisely this — even a rabbinic marriage, taking place within the legal and social institution of marriage with its formal acts of *kiddushin* and *ketubah*, redefines the nature of the sex. It is no longer *zenut* because the institution within which it occurs transforms its character.

This line of reasoning has been extended in modern discussions of *agunah* solutions. If *ein tenai benisuin* is grounded in *ein adam oseh beilato beilat zenut*, some contemporary *poskim* have asked whether a couple who remain in a secular legal marriage — even if the *halachic* bond is contested — would similarly not be considered to have engaged in *beilat zenut*, since they remain within an institutional framework of marriage. The analogy to *kiddushei ketanah* with subsequent *mi'un* is real, though extending it further — to a couple cohabiting without any legal framework whatsoever — raises distinct concerns.

## Financial Incentives

### Strip-Mining Property

*Rav Pappa* offers an entirely different rationale for both *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel*, anchored not in marital intimacy or public perception but in financial considerations. Both sides, he says, are reasoning from the same economic concern — *nichsei milug*, the wife's property over which the husband holds usufruct rights (*peiri*) — but reaching opposite conclusions.

*Beit Shammai*'s concern: *shama d'achil lehu minei, d'sof sof l-mafka ka-atya* — if the girl can perform *mi'un* at any time and walk away with her property, the husband will reason that his hold on the property is temporary and precarious. He will therefore strip-mine it — cut down the trees, extract what he can immediately — rather than tend to it, invest in it, and maintain it over time. The threat of *mi'un* thus creates a perverse incentive for destructive short-term exploitation of the wife's estate.

### Investment Incentive

*Beit Hillel* inverts the argument entirely: *adaraba* — quite the contrary. *Kivan d'amart tema'en, ashvochei mashvach lehu* — because she *can* perform *mi'un*, the husband will take excellent care of the property. He knows that if he mismanages it, her relatives will advise her to leave him — *yatz'u lah kroveha* — and she will take her property back. The threat of *mi'un* thus functions as a disciplining mechanism, incentivizing the husband to be a responsible steward of her estate.

### Wedding Expenses

*Rava* adds yet another dimension to *Beit Shammai*'s reasoning: *she-ein adam tore'ach be-seudah u-mafsidah* — a man will not go to the trouble and expense of a wedding celebration if he knows it may retroactively be rendered meaningless. The concern is not merely financial in the abstract; it points to the social reality that the progression from *kiddushin* to *nisu'in* involves real investment — the wedding feast, the communal celebration — and a husband unwilling to commit will avoid making that investment. Since the rabbis created *kiddushei ketanah* as an institution serving genuine economic and social needs of the time, they wanted it to actually progress to *nisu'in*; creating conditions that discourage the move to *nisu'in* undermines the entire institution.

### Celebration Value

*Tosafot* responds to *Rava*'s concern with a telling observation: *tara-vaihu nicha lehu* — both parties actually *want* the wedding celebration, even if it may later be retroactively dissolved. Why? *Kedei le-guzlei kalah kayetzei ishah* — people desire the social standing that comes with being publicly married. Even a young girl benefits from having had a wedding, from having this status in the community, regardless of what eventually happens. The desire for the celebration exists independently of the permanence of the marriage.

## Mi'un & Yibum

### Shammai: Ba'al Only

The *Mishnah* states: *Beit Shammai omrim b'ba'al* — she can perform *mi'un* only against her husband, not the *yabam*. The scenario is readily imagined: the girl was given in this rabbinic *kiddushei ketanah*, her husband died before she performed *mi'un*, and now she stands before the brother-in-law with the *mitzvah* of *yibum* or *chalitzah*. Can the *mi'un* she failed to perform against her husband now be exercised against the brother-in-law? *Beit Shammai* says no; *Beit Hillel* says yes — *be-ba'al u-ve-yabam*.

### Oshaya: Ma'amar Only

The *Gemara* cites *Rabbi Oshaya*'s interpretation: *mema'enet le-ma'amaro v'einah mema'enet le-zikato* — she may perform *mi'un* in response to his *ma'amar*, but not in response to the *zikah*. To understand this, recall what *ma'amar* is: it is an act by the brother-in-law in which he formally gives her *kiddushin* — *harei at mekudeshet li be-taba'at zo* — a ring and declaration. From the outside it resembles normal *kiddushin*, and the reason it does not constitute full *de-oraita* binding *kiddushin* is only because she is a *yevamah* whose relationship is governed by *yibum* or *chalitzah*. The rabbis encouraged this step of *ma'amar* before actual *yibum*.

So the scenario *Rabbi Oshaya* envisions is this: the husband died, the brother-in-law performed *ma'amar* with this minor girl — entering into what amounts to a new act of *kiddushei ketanah* — and now she wishes to perform *mi'un*. Her *mi'un* can reverse the *ma'amar*, undoing that new relationship. But it gets her only back to square one: she still has a *zikah* to the brother-in-law inherited from her deceased husband, and that *zikah* requires *chalitzah*. The *mi'un* cannot uproot the *zikah* — *einah mema'enet le-zikato*.

### Rav Chisda Distinction

*Rav Chisda* explains the reasoning behind *Rabbi Oshaya*'s distinction: *dim-da'atah matzi akra* — the *ma'amar*, like *kiddushei ketanah* itself when performed by mother and brothers, requires her willing acceptance. Since she entered into this relationship with her own *da'at*, she is empowered to uproot it through *mi'un*. The *zikah*, by contrast, is *de'al korchah* — it was imposed on her. She did not willingly enter into a relationship with the brother-in-law; the bond arose automatically through the death of her husband and the operation of the laws of *yibum*. Since she never willingly entered into it, she cannot unilaterally unilaterally undo it. *Lo matzi akra.*

### Bi'ah Challenge

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this reasoning. The brother-in-law can also perform *bi'ah* with the *ketanah*, and when he does so, because she is a minor, the *bi'ah* does not create full *de-oraita yibum* — it has the same rabbinic status as a *ma'amar*. Moreover, she may be *oker* — she may undo it through *mi'un*. But *Rav Chisda*'s reasoning was that *mi'un* applies where she willingly entered the relationship. Yet in the case of *bi'ah*, she did not necessarily do so willingly in the relevant sense. The *Gemara* therefore must refine the principle: it is not precisely about her *da'at* in entering the relationship; rather, the distinction is that the *ma'amar* and *bi'as ketanah* represent new acts undertaken by the brother-in-law — new relationships entered into with her as a *ketanah* — and for those, because they mirror *kiddushei ketanah*, the rabbis granted her the power of *mi'un*. The *zikah*, by contrast, flowed to her from her original husband ראובן — it is not a new relationship initiated with the brother-in-law but something inherited through the first marriage. That inherited bond cannot be severed through *mi'un*, since the proper moment to sever the bond to ראובן was while ראובן was alive.

## Ulla's Position

### Ulla Allows Zikah

*Ulla* takes a far more expansive position: *mena'enet af le-zikato* — she may indeed perform *mi'un* even to sever the *zikah*. On *Ulla*'s view, the *mi'un* she performs vis-à-vis the brother-in-law actually uproots the original marriage — *nisu'ei kammai ka-akra* — she is retroactively undoing the first marriage to ראובן, and therefore she is entirely free, without need of *chalitzah*.

### Tzarat Ervah

*Rava* challenges *Ulla* from the *baraita*: *kol she-yecholah le-ma'en ve-lo me'anah, tzarasah choletzet ve-lo mityabbemet* — any woman who could have performed *mi'un* and did not is a woman whose co-wife (*tzarah*) performs *chalitzah* with the brother-in-law and not *yibum*. The scenario: the deceased husband was also married to a second woman who happens to be the brother-in-law's daughter — a case of *tzarat ervah*. If the first wife (*mema'enet*) could truly perform *mi'un* and make the entire first marriage disappear, why not instruct her to do so, thereby freeing the brother-in-law to perform *yibum* with the co-wife rather than merely *chalitzah*? The fact that the *halacha* does not permit this implies that *mi'un* cannot fully erase the status that crystallized at the moment of the husband's death.

### Rami's Source

*Rami bar Yechezkel* provides a source that clarifies the underlying principle: *mi'un be-ba'al muteret le-aviv; bi-yabam asurah le-aviv* — if a girl performs *mi'un* against her husband (ראובן), she is permitted to marry his father (יעקב), since the marriage is retroactively dissolved. But if she performs *mi'un* against the brother-in-law (שמעון), she remains forbidden to his father (יעקב). What accounts for the difference? In both cases *mi'un* was performed. The answer lies in what transpired at the moment of ראובן's death.

---

## Death Instantiates

### Moment of Death

The principle the *Gemara* derives is: *bi-sh'at nefilah nirit ke-khallato* — at the moment of the husband's death, she is already viewed as his daughter-in-law in a meaningful sense, because that moment immediately generates demands: something must be done about her relationship with שמעון. That demand — that she must either do *yibum*, *chalitzah*, or *mi'un* — concretizes her status as ראובן's wife in a way that cannot be fully undone retroactively.

### Tzarat Ervah Effect

The consequence: *hacha nami bi-sh'at nefilah nirit ke-tzarat bito* — when the husband died, דבורה's status as *tzarat ervah* was already instantiated. Even if the first wife later performs *mi'un* and retroactively dissolves the marriage, that act of dissolution does not fully remove the *tzarat ervah* status that was created at the moment of death. The rabbinic prohibition on *yibum* with the co-wife therefore remains.

### Retroactive Limits

The general principle is therefore this: retroactive dissolution through *mi'un* does not undo the statuses that were created at the moment of the husband's death. At that moment, before any *mi'un* was performed, the world looked a certain way — she was his wife, there was a need for the brother-in-law to act, certain prohibitions crystallized. A subsequent *mi'un* can address the ongoing relationship and prevent further obligations, but it cannot reach back and erase what became real at that critical moment. This is why *mi'un* against the *yabam* leaves her forbidden to his father: her status as daughter-in-law to יעקב became a reality the moment ראובן died, and no retroactive act can fully dissolve it.

## Multiple Brothers

### Rav: All Disqualified

*Rav* rules: *mi'un b'zeh asurah le-zeh* — if she performs *mi'un* against one brother (שמעון), she is thereby forbidden to the other brothers (לוי, etc.) as well. The *mi'un* operates on the *zikah* as a whole.

### Get Analogy

*Rav*'s reasoning draws on the analogy of *baalas ha-get*: just as if the brother-in-law gives her a *get* — which in the *yibum* context dissolves his rabbinic *zikah* — she becomes forbidden to all the brothers, so too *mi'un* against one has the effect of foreclosing the *yibum* option with all of them. *Lav kivan d'it'asrah le-chad, it'asrah le-kulhu?* — once she becomes forbidden to one through this mechanism, she becomes forbidden to all.

### Shmuel: Individual

*Shmuel* disagrees: *mi'un b'zeh muteres le-zeh* — even after performing *mi'un* against שמעון, she may still enter into *yibum* with לוי. The key distinction *Shmuel* draws is the identity of the actor. In the case of *baalas ha-get*, it is the man whose act changes her status, and his act can be said to represent or implicate all the brothers. But in *mi'un*, it is she who is acting. And she is not saying something general about all men — she is saying something specific: *lo ravine bech, lo tzevine bech* — I do not want *you*, שמעון, specifically. *Ha becha u-ve'acharina* — as for שמעון, I reject him; as for לוי, she may well be interested. The act of *mi'un* is directed at a specific individual, and it changes only her relationship with that individual, not her status in the world at large.

### Her Action Only

This distinction — that in *mi'un* it is *hee d'ka avdah beih*, she is the one acting upon him — is not merely technical. It reflects a genuine conceptual difference in how *mi'un* functions. Normally, the legal acts that define a woman's status — *get*, *chalitzah*, and so on — are acts performed upon her or for her. *Mi'un* is unique in that she is the agent, and her agency is directed at a specific person. *Shmuel* therefore holds that it affects only that specific person.

### Rav Ashi: Collective

*Rav Ashi* goes further: *mi'un b'zeh muteres afilu lo* — if she performs *mi'un* against שמעון while לוי and other brothers remain, the *mi'un* is entirely meaningless, and she can even subsequently perform *yibum* with שמעון himself. The reason will emerge from the *Gemara*'s final analysis.

## All-or-Nothing

### Yochanan

When *Ravin* returned from Eretz Yisrael, he transmitted in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: *mi'un b'zeh muteres le-achim* — if she performs *mi'un* against one brother, she remains permitted to the other brothers. This accords with *Shmuel*'s view that *mi'un* is individual and directed.

### Abaye's ID

*Abaye* identifies who among the previously cited authorities would disagree with *Rabbi Yochanan*: it is *Rav*, because *Rav* holds that *mi'un b'zeh asurah le-zeh* — refused of one, forbidden to all. *Rav*'s position that *mi'un* operates wholesale on the relationship to all brothers is directly in conflict with *Rabbi Yochanan*'s ruling that she remains permitted to the other brothers.

### Rava's ID

*Rava* identifies a different dissenter: *Rabbi Oshaya*. *Rabbi Oshaya* holds that there is no *mi'un* operative against the *zikah* at all — *mi'un* can only address a new *ma'amar* or *bi'as ketanah* entered into by the brother-in-law, not the underlying *zikah* inherited from the first husband. *Rabbi Yochanan*'s ruling, read carefully, implies that *mi'un* against one brother would have been meaningful for the *zikah* to that brother while preserving the *zikah* to the others — precisely what *Rabbi Oshaya* denies.

### Indivisible Zikah

*Rav Ashi*'s position now becomes clear. The *Gemara* articulates the principle: *ein mi'un le-chatzi zikah* — one cannot sever half a *zikah*. The *zikah* that the brothers hold is not a collection of independent relationships, each severable on its own. It is a single *zikah* inherited from the original husband ראובן, transmitted collectively to all the brothers as a unit. If she wishes to use *mi'un* to sever the *zikah*, she must sever the whole of it — directing her refusal at all brothers collectively. Attempting to sever the *zikah* to one brother while leaving it intact as to the others is an attempt at *mi'un le-chatzi zikah*, and that is legally incoherent. The partial *mi'un* accomplishes nothing — *muteres afilu lo*, she can even subsequently perform *yibum* with the very brother she attempted to refuse, because the refusal had no legal effect.

### Conclusion

The full range of positions thus emerges. *Rabbi Oshaya* holds that *mi'un* never touches the *zikah* at all — it can only address the new relationship created by the brother-in-law's *ma'amar* or *bi'as ketanah*. *Rav* holds that *mi'un* does operate on the *zikah* but, analogous to the *get* case, operates wholesale — refuse one brother and all are foreclosed. *Shmuel* holds that *mi'un* operates individually — she can refuse one brother and remain available to others, because her act is specifically directed. And *Rav Ashi*, as the *Gemara* finally clarifies, holds that the *zikah* is indivisible — it comes as a single inherited unit from the first husband — and therefore any *mi'un* must address the whole *zikah* or it is void. She must either reject all the brothers collectively or her *mi'un* is meaningless. The *Gemara* continues into the next *daf*.
